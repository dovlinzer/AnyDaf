# Bava Batra 127 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumtum Birth Status

### Rav Ami: Tumtum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: טוּמְטוּם שֶׁנִּקְרַע וְנִמְצָא זָכָר, אֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם; דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה הַבֵּן הַבְּכוֹר לַשְּׂנִיאָה״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בֵּן מִשְּׁעַת הֲוָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami says:** In the case of **one whose sexual organs are indeterminate [*tumtum*]** and **whose** skin **became perforated** so that his genitals were exposed **and** he **was found** to be **a male,** he **does not take a double** portion of his father's estate. **As the verse states: "And if the firstborn son was [*vehaya*] hers that was hated"** (Deuteronomy 21:15), which is interpreted to mean that he is not considered a firstborn **unless he is** recognized as **a son,** i.e., male, **from the moment of** his coming into **being [*havaya*],** i.e., his birth.

The *mishna* on 127a shifts from the laws of the double portion itself to the question of identifying who qualifies as the firstborn son, and it is this question that drives the *gemara's* ensuing discussion. Rav Ami opens with the ruling that a *tumtum* — a child whose genitalia have not descended or whose genitalia are concealed by a membrane at birth, such that gender cannot be determined — does not receive the double portion of the firstborn even if, after birth, the child is revealed to be male. The reason is that the verse states *vehaya haben habechor* — "and it shall be that the firstborn son" — meaning that the child must be recognizable as a *ben* from the very moment of birth. Since this child was not identifiable as a son at birth, the *halacha* of *bechor* does not attach. The *gemara* applies this same principle to the case of *ben sorer umoreh* and similarly rules that a second boy born in such a state would not affect the size of the firstborn's double portion.

### Rav Shizvi: Brit Milah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁיזְבִי אָמַר: אַף אֵינוֹ נִימּוֹל לִשְׁמֹנָה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אִשָּׁה כִּי תַזְרִיעַ וְיָלְדָה זָכָר״, ״וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא זָכָר מִשְּׁעַת לֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi says:** A *tumtum* who was found to be male **is also not circumcised on** the **eighth** day, if his eighth day occurs on Shabbat, although the mitzva of circumcision on the eighth day generally overrides Shabbat prohibtions. **As the verse states: "If a woman bears seed and gives birth to a male** then she shall be unclean seven days; as in the days of the impurity of her sickness shall she be unclean. **And on the eighth day** the flesh of his foreskin **shall be circumcised"** (Leviticus 12:2–3), which is interpreted to mean that he is not circumcised on the eighth day, in the event that it occurs on Shabbat, **unless he is** recognized as **a male from the moment of** his **birth.**

The *gemara* then records a parallel ruling by Rav Shizvi. Reading from the top of 127a: *Rav Shizvi amar — af eino nimal lishmona* — even if a baby is born in the state of a *tumtum* and is subsequently identified as male before the eighth day, the *brit milah* is not performed on the eighth day in a manner that overrides *Shabbat*. The source is the verse *isha ki tazria veyalda zachar… uvayom hashmini yimol besar orlato* — "if a woman gives forth seed and gives birth to a male… on the eighth day you shall circumcise the flesh of his foreskin." The phrase *yalda zachar* requires, as the *gemara* phrases it, *ad sheyehe zachar mishat leida* — the child must be male from the moment of birth. Since this condition was not met for the *tumtum*, the special weight of the eighth day — the weight that ordinarily overrides *Shabbat* — does not apply.

### Rav Sharvia: Tumat Leida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא אָמַר: אַף אֵין אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אִשָּׁה כִּי תַזְרִיעַ וְיָלְדָה זָכָר וְטָמְאָה שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא זָכָר מִשְּׁעַת לֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sherevya says: His mother is also not** rendered **ritually impure** due to his **birth, as the verse states: "If a woman bears seed and gives birth to a male, then she shall be unclean seven days,"** which is interpreted to mean that she is not rendered impure **unless he is** recognized as **a male from the moment of** his **birth.**

Rav Sharvia then draws from the same verse and the same *drasha* — *ad sheyehe zachar mishat leida* — but with a different halachic consequence: *af ein imo tme'a leida* — the mother of such a child has no *tumat leida*, the impurity that accompanies childbirth. The verse *isha ki tazria veyalda zachar vetame'a shivat yamim* — "if a woman gives birth to a male she is impure for seven days" — appears even before the laws of *brit milah* and applies only when the child is identifiable as male from the moment of birth. Here, since neither male nor female status was apparent at birth, the mother does not fall into either *parasha* of *tumah* — neither the *parasha* of a male child nor that of a female child. She would presumably remain subject to *derabbanan* obligations, and there is a point worth noting: this ruling is in some respects an even greater *chidush* than the case of an *androgynos*, since here the child may have been fully male in a biological sense — simply unknown.

## Childbirth Impurity Doubt

### Meseivei Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמַּפֶּלֶת טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס – תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלִנְקֵיבָה. תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Nidda* 28a): A woman **who miscarries a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite [*ve'androginos*] observes** the strictures of a woman who gave birth **to a male and to a female.** Since it is uncertain whether the fetus is male or female, the woman must observe the *halakhot* of ritual impurity according to both possibilities. This appears to be **a conclusive refutation** of the statement **of Rav Sherevya** that a woman who gives birth to a *tumtum* is not rendered impure at all. The Gemara affirms: This is **a conclusive refutation.**

At this point the *gemara* raises a challenge. *Meseivei* — the *gemara* asks from a *baraita*: *hamapelet tumtum ve'androgynos* — if a woman gives birth to a *tumtum* or an *androgynos*, since it is unknown whether the child is male or female, the *halacha* is *teshvei lezachar ulenekeiva* — she must observe the stringencies of both. This stands in apparent contradiction to Rav Sharvia's ruling, since the *baraita* presupposes that even where gender is indeterminate the mother is indeed subject to *tumat leida* — she is not excluded from the category entirely. The *gemara* therefore declares *tiyufta d'Rav Shavya tiyufta* — a complete refutation of Rav Sharvia's position.

### Seven & Forty Days

The content of the stringency *teshvei lezachar ulenekeiva* is as follows. For a male child, the mother is *tme'ah* for the first seven days, and thereafter — through day forty — any bleeding she experiences has the status of *dam tohar* and does not render her impure. For a female child, she is *tme'ah* for the first fourteen days, and thereafter — through day eighty — any bleeding is likewise *dam tohar*. When the child's gender is unknown, both stringencies apply simultaneously. Practically: she is treated as having given birth to a female for purposes of the initial period of *tumah*, meaning she is *tme'ah* for fourteen days; and she is treated as having given birth to a male for purposes of *dam tohar*, meaning the special dispensation expires at day forty rather than day eighty. There is no *halacha*, the *baraita* implies, that renders her entirely exempt from *tumat leida* on account of the ambiguity.

### Both Stringencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב שֵׁיזְבִי? תַּנָּא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, וּלְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say it is** also **a conclusive refutation** of the statement **of Rav Sheizevi** with regard to circumcision, as the *halakha* of circumsicion is stated together with the *halakha* of ritual impurity? The Gemara answers: The ***tanna*** of the mishna in tractate *Nidda* **is uncertain** whether the birth discussed in the verse includes that of a *tumtum*, **and** therefore he rules **stringently,** that she should observe the *halakhot* of ritual impurity for both possibilities. Rav Sheizevi's ruling can follow the same logic: The infant should not be circumcised on Shabbat, as it is uncertain whether the mitzva of his circumcision overrides the prohibitions of Shabbat.

The *gemara* then asks: *leima teihavei tiyufta d'Rav Shizvi* — should this same *baraita* also refute Rav Shizvi? Rav Shizvi had applied the very same *drasha* from *yalda zachar* — only now to the context of *brit milah* and its capacity to override *Shabbat*. Once it is established that the verse does not exclude a *tumtum* from *tumat leida*, it would seem that the verse similarly cannot exclude a *tumtum* from the laws of *brit milah*, and the *brit milah* of such a child should override *Shabbat* once he is identified as male before the eighth day.

The *gemara* responds: *tanna sapokei mesapka leih* — the *tanna* who authored the *baraita* was himself uncertain how to read the verse. Does *yalda zachar* exclude a child who was a *tumtum* at birth, or does it not? The *tanna* resolves his uncertainty through stringency in each domain. Regarding *tumah*, since the uncertainty exists, he does not exclude the *tumtum* from *tumat leida*, and the mother must observe both stringencies — fourteen days and the end of forty days — because perhaps the verse does not exclude such a child. Regarding *brit milah* and *Shabbat*, the uncertainty cuts the other way: perhaps the verse *does* exclude a *tumtum*, and therefore we cannot have *brit milah* override *Shabbat* for such a child. Both positions can thus be maintained simultaneously — the mother has *tumat leida* and the *brit milah* does not override *Shabbat* — because we apply stringency in both directions under the same uncertainty.

### Ei Hachi Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלִנְקֵבָה וּלְנִדָּה״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that the *tanna* of the mishna in tractate *Nidda* is uncertain whether the birth discussed in the verse includes that of a *tumtum*, the mishna **should have** stated that the woman **observes** the strictures of a woman who gave birth **to** both **a male and to a female, and** also **as a menstruating woman.** If it is uncertain whether the *halakhot* of ritual purity after birth pertain to a woman who gives birth to a *tumtum* at all, she should observe the *halakhot* of ritual impurity for any blood that emerges in the time period following the birth, as it should have the status of the blood of a menstruating woman. The Gemara concludes: This poses **a difficulty.**

The *gemara*, however, presses further: *ei hachi* — if that is correct, and the possibility genuinely exists that the verse excludes a *tumtum* entirely from the category of *tumat leida*, then there is a third possibility the *baraita* has neglected to consider. If the *tumtum* is excluded from the verse, the mother would not benefit from any *dam tohar* at all. She would be subject to *niddah* laws in the normal sense, without the special dispensation that accompanies childbirth. The *baraita* should therefore have said not merely *teshvei lezachar ulenekeivah* but *teshvei lezachar ulenekeivah uleniddah* — she must also observe the stringency that no *dam tohar* applies whatsoever. Since the *baraita* does not include this third possibility, it implies that a *tumtum* is definitely not excluded from the category — it is within the category of *tumat leida*, and the only question is whether the child is male or female. The *gemara* acknowledges this as *kashya*, a genuine difficulty, but treats it as insufficient to constitute a full refutation — *tiyufta* — of the resolution offered above. The upshot is that both rulings — *tumat leida* applies and *Shabbat* is not overridden — can stand, though the *gemara* concedes the tension.

## Verse Uncertainty

### Reconciliation

The resolution just offered extends to the positions of both Rav Shizvi and Rav Sharvia. The phrase *veyalda zachar* generates two distinct *halachot*: *vetame'a shivat yamim* — the mother's seven days of impurity — and *uvayom hashmini yimol* — circumcision on the eighth day. Whether this phrase excludes a *tumtum* from its ambit is itself uncertain. In the domain of *tumah* the *tanna* applies the uncertainty leniently toward the mother's obligation, including the *tumtum* within the category and requiring her to observe the combined stringencies of fourteen days and forty days. In the domain of *brit milah* on *Shabbat* the *tanna* applies the uncertainty in the direction of *chumra*, leaving open the possibility that the *tumtum* is excluded and therefore not permitting the *brit milah* to override *Shabbat*. Both Rav Shizvi and Rav Sharvia can each be maintained, each drawing on one branch of this bifurcated analysis.

## Ben vs. Zachar

### Sex & Gender

Before the *gemara* moves on, it is worth pausing on a distinction that illuminates the whole discussion. The *gemara* had no difficulty with the original ruling that a *tumtum* is excluded from the *halacha* of *vehaya hebben habechor* — the double portion — or from *ben sorer umoreh*. Yet the analysis of *tumat leida* and *brit milah* treats the *tumtum* as potentially included. What accounts for the difference?

The answer may lie in the distinction between the words *ben* and *zachar*. The verse concerning *bechor* uses the word *ben* — a relational and social category, closer to what one might call gender. The verse concerning *brit milah* and *tumat leida* uses the word *zachar* — a biological category, closer to what one might call biological sex. A *tumtum* is a *zachar* from birth in the biological sense; it is simply not yet known. But the category *ben* — son, boy, as a recognized social identity — has not yet been conferred. The *gemara's* language is telling: *ad sheyehei ben mishas leidah* — until he is a *ben* from the moment of birth. The statement is not merely that it was unknown whether he was a *ben*; it is that, in the relevant sense, he was not yet a *ben*, because the social and halachic identity of *ben* had not yet been established. He is a *zachar* — you simply do not know it yet — but he is not yet a *ben*. This distinction between biological sex and socially-constituted gender status helps explain why exclusion from *ben sorer umoreh* and *bechor* coheres with potential inclusion in the laws of *zachar* for purposes of *brit milah* and *tumat leida*.

## Firstborn Knowledge

### Baraita: Ben & Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי: ״בֵּן״ – וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם, ״בְּכוֹר״ – וְלֹא סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Ami** that a *tumtum* firstborn does not receive a double portion of the inheritance. The *baraita* states: From the phrase "the firstborn son" (Deuteronomy 21:15) it is derived that only **a son** receives a double portion, **but not a *tumtum*,** and only a definite **firstborn** receives a double portion, **but not** one about whom it is **uncertain** if he is a firstborn.

The *gemara* proceeds: *amar Rava tani k'vasi d'Rabbi Ami* — Rava affirms that a *baraita* supports Rav Ami's ruling. The *baraita* reads: *ben* — the verse says *vehaya haben habechor lisnia* — *ben velo tumtum*, a son and not a *tumtum*; and *velo safek*, not where there is doubt about who the *bechor* is. The first clause, *ben velo tumtum*, straightforwardly supports Rav Ami. The second clause, *velo safek*, invites analysis: what case of doubt is it coming to exclude?

### Two Boys Cave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא ״בֵּן״ וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם – כִּדְרַבִּי אַמֵּי. אֶלָּא ״בְּכוֹר״ וְלֹא סָפֵק – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מַאי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּדָרֵשׁ רָבָא, דְּדָרֵשׁ רָבָא: שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁיָּלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים בְּמַחֲבֵא, כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the *halakha* that **a son** receives a double portion **but a *tumtum*** does **not** is understandable, as it **is in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Ami, but what** case **does** the *halakha* that only a definite **firstborn** receives a double portion **but not** one about whom it is **uncertain** if he is a firstborn serve **to exclude?** Why would one about whom it is uncertain if he is a firstborn receive a double portion? The Gemara answers: It serves **to exclude that which Rava taught, as Rava taught** that if **two wives** of the same husband **gave birth to two males in hiding,** so that it is unknown which son was born first, and the husband subsequently had other sons, **each** of the two possible firstborns **writes an authorization to the other.** Since their brothers can claim against each of them individually that he is not the firstborn and does not deserve a double portion, each writes the other an authorization to collect his portion, so that they can jointly claim the additional portion in any event.

The *gemara* explains: *lafukei mai dedarash Rava* — to exclude what Rava himself had taught. Rava had ruled regarding a case in which a man had two wives who gave birth to two boys *bame'ara* — in a cave, while hiding, presumably from non-Jews, in total darkness. The mothers did not hold onto their children, the boys became mixed up, and it is not known which boy is the firstborn. The relevant doubt is not about identifying the child's sex — both are known to be male — but about which one was born first.

Rava's solution was *kotvin harsha'a ze laze* — they write a power of attorney from one to the other. One of the boys is entitled to the *chelek bechor*, the double portion; neither boy knows whether he is the one. The boy we will call Yosef writes Reuven a power of attorney: if I am the *bechor*, I hereby authorize you to collect on my behalf. Reuven then goes to the other brothers and says: I am entitled to collect a double portion — either as the *bechor* myself, or as Yosef's agent if he is the *bechor*. Either way, he collects. The result is that between Reuven and Yosef, three portions are collected, which is correct since one of them is definitively the *bechor*.

### Harsha'a Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא, וְהָא שְׁלַח רָבִין: דָּבָר זֶה שָׁאַלְתִּי לְכׇל רַבּוֹתַי וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לִי דָּבָר, בְּרַם כָּךְ אָמְרוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הוּכְּרוּ, וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ – כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה. לֹא הוּכְּרוּ – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** subsequently **said to Rava: But didn't Ravin send** a letter from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, stating: **I asked all my teachers** about **this matter and they did not tell me anything; but this** is what **they said in the name of Rabbi Yannai:** If the two sons **were** initially **recognized,** i.e., it was known which one of them was the firstborn, **and** they **were ultimately mixed,** and now the firstborn cannot be identified, **each writes an authorization to the other.** If **they were not** initially **recognized, each does not write an authorization to the other.**

At this point, Rav Papa challenges Rava, citing a message that Ravin had sent from Eretz Yisrael: *davar ze sha'alti lechol rabotai velo amru li davar — beram kach amru mishum Rabbi Yannai* — I posed this question to all my teachers and they gave me no answer of their own, but they related the following in the name of Rabbi Yannai. The answer depends on the precise circumstances of the case.

If *hukru ulevasof nitarvu* — the firstborn child was initially identified, and only later the boys became mixed up — then *kotvin harsha'a ze laze*, the power of attorney solution works. The firstborn's entitlement to the double portion was established; the problem is only one of subsequent confusion. That is a *safek* in the classic sense: there is an entitlement, we simply do not know who holds it, and the *harsha'a* solution is appropriate.

But if *lo hukru* — the children were never identified from the moment of birth, not even for a single moment — then *ein kotvin harsha'a ze laze*. They cannot write a power of attorney. This is the meaning of the *baraita*'s phrase *velo safek*, interpreted through the lens of *velo tumtum*: just as a *tumtum* loses his status of *bechor* because the identity of *ben* was not established at the moment of birth, so too if the identity of *bechor* was never established — if from the very first moment after birth no one identified this child as the firstborn — then the status of *bechor* never attached, and there is nothing to collect.

A natural question arises: what if the identity was not known at birth but was discovered later — through some subsequent means, including, in a modern context, DNA testing? The analogy to the *tumtum* case suggests the answer is the same. It does not matter if the identity is clarified later; what matters is whether it was established at the first moment.

This also sets up the question of *ne'emanut* — whose testimony is trusted to establish the identity of the *bechor*. The father, as will be seen, has special standing here, and a question naturally arises: what if the child's identity was known to the father alone — not to the community, not to the mother — but only to him? The full treatment of that question follows in the *gemara's* subsequent discussion.

## Hukru vs. Lo Hukru

### Rava Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אוֹקִי רָבָא אָמוֹרָא עֲלֵיהּ, וּדְרַשׁ: דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַרְתִּי לָכֶם – טָעוּת הֵן בְּיָדִי, בְּרַם כָּךְ אָמְרוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ – כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לְזֶה, לֹא הוּכְּרוּ – אֵין כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava then established an *amora*** to repeat his lesson to the masses aloud **and taught: The statements that I said to you are a mistake on my** part. **But this** is what **they said in the name of Rabbi Yannai:** If the two sons **were** initially **recognized and were ultimately mixed, each writes an authorization to the other.** If **they were not** initially **recognized, each does not write an authorization to the other.**

*Hadar oki Rava amora alei* — Rava then publicly stood up an *amora* before him — that is, he employed the figure who would relay his words aloud to the assembled crowd — and declared: *devarim she'amarti lachem ta'ut hen beyadi* — what I told you previously was mistaken. His earlier ruling that the *harsha'a* solution is always available was wrong. *Beram kach amru mishum Rabbi Yannai* — here is what is said in the name of Rabbi Yannai.

### Yannai Distinction

Rabbi Yannai's distinction is clear: *hukru levasof nitarvu, kotvin harsha'a ze laze* — if the *bechor* was originally identified and subsequently confused, the *harsha'a* works. *Lo hukru, ein kotvin harsha'a ze laze* — if the identity was never established from birth, they cannot write a power of attorney.

One might further wonder whether a case falls between these two poles — one known to the father but to no one else, or one identified after the fact. The case of the *tumtum* suggests that whatever subsequent discovery occurs, it is the moment of birth that is determinative. Just as the *tumtum* who is later revealed to be male does not thereby acquire the status of *bechor* retroactively, so too the boy whose identity as firstborn was never established at birth does not acquire that status through later evidence. The *baraita*'s language *velo safek* thus functions to exclude precisely this category — a doubt so fundamental that the status of *bechor* was never constituted in the first place.

## Father's Testimony

### Akra Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי אַקְרָא דְאַגְמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ: הָיוּ מוּחְזָקִים בָּזֶה – שֶׁהוּא בְּכוֹר, וְאָמַר אָבִיו עַל אַחֵר ״בְּכוֹר הוּא״, מַהוּ? שְׁלַח לְהוּ: כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The residents of Akra De'Agma sent** the following inquiry **to Shmuel: Teach us, our master:** If the court **had a presumption concerning this** son, **that he is a firstborn, and his father says concerning another** son of his: **He is** his **firstborn, what is** the *halakha*? Shmuel **sent to them** in response: Both of these sons **write an authorization, each to the other.**

*Shalchu lei benei Akra De-Agma le-Shmuel* — the people of Akra De-Agma sent the following question to Shmuel: *yelamdenu rabenu* — let our master teach us. *Hayu muchzakin be-zeh shehu bechor* — everyone had assumed that a certain individual was the *bechor*. *Ve'amar aviv al acher shehu bechor* — and the father declared that a different son — previously unknown, perhaps born to a wife in a distant place — is the *bechor*. *Mahu* — what is the law?

*Shalchu lehu: kotvin harsha'a ze laze* — Shmuel replied that they write a power of attorney. He treated this as a case of *safek*: the *chazakah* establishes one boy as *bechor*, the father's testimony establishes the other. Rather than resolving the tension, Shmuel treats them as two competing claims of equal weight, yielding a genuine doubt, and the *harsha'a* solution is applied.

### Yehuda vs. Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, לִישְׁלַח לְהוּ כְּרַבָּנַן; אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, לִישְׁלַח לְהוּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה! מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִי כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this ruling: **Whichever way you** look at it, Shmuel's opinion is difficult. **If he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** with regard to this issue, **he should have sent them** a response **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and **if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, he should have sent them** a response **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Shmuel's response is not in accordance with either tannaitic opinion. The Gemara answers: **He is uncertain whether** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda or in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** Therefore he sent them a response that incorporates both opinions.

The *gemara* objects. This is not a genuine *safek*. There are two positions — that of Rabbi Yehuda and that of the *Rabbanan* — and under each position the matter is fully resolved. The *gemara* formulates the objection: *ei ke-Rabbanan sevira lei, lislachu ke-Rabbanan* — if Shmuel holds like the *Rabbanan*, he should have ruled accordingly: the *chazakah* governs, the boy presumed to be the *bechor* retains that status, and the father's contrary testimony is disregarded. *Ei ke-Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei, lislachu ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — if he holds like Rabbi Yehuda, he should have ruled that the father is believed even against the *chazakah*, and the boy the father identifies is the *bechor*.

The *gemara* resolves: *mistapka lei* — Shmuel was himself uncertain which ruling to follow. Because he could not decide whether the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda or the *Rabbanan*, and each position yields a clear but different answer, Shmuel treated the legal conclusion as a *safek* — not a factual doubt but a jurisprudential one — and therefore applied the *harsha'a* solution as a practical resolution. This is structurally comparable to contemporary *poskim* who, when unable to decide between two authoritative positions on a question of personal status such as surrogate motherhood, advise a *geirus misafek* rather than committing to a definitive ruling.

## Father's Credibility

### Yehuda: Believed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״יַכִּיר״, יַכִּירֶנּוּ לַאֲחֵרִים. מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נֶאֱמָן אָדָם לוֹמַר: ״זֶה בְּנִי בְּכוֹר״. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁנֶּאֱמָן אָדָם לוֹמַר ״זֶה בְּנִי בְּכוֹר״, כָּךְ נֶאֱמָן אָדָם לוֹמַר ״זֶה בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה״ וְ״זֶה בֶּן חֲלוּצָה״. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is** the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Expounding the verse: **"He shall acknowledge** the firstborn" (Deuteronomy 21:17), the Sages said: **He shall acknowledge him to others.** In other words, a father is deemed credible to tell others that this is his firstborn. **From here, Rabbi Yehuda says** that **a man is deemed credible to say: This** is **my firstborn son,** even if he has sons presumed to be older. **And just as a man is deemed credible to say: This** is **my firstborn son, so too, a man** who is a priest **is deemed credible to say** about his son: **This** is **a son of a divorced woman, or: This** is **a son of a *yevama* who has performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*],** in which cases he is disqualified from the priesthood due to his flawed lineage [*ḥalal*]. **And the Rabbis say: He is not deemed credible.**

The *gemara* now turns to the substance of the debate. The relevant verse is *ki et habechor ben hasenua yakkir* — "for the firstborn, the son of the hated wife, he shall recognize." The word *yakkir* — he shall recognize, or he shall make known — is the textual anchor for the father's special credibility. *Mikan amar Rabbi Yehuda: ne'eman adam lomar zeh bni bechor* — from here Rabbi Yehuda derives that a person is believed to identify his son as the firstborn, and this testimony carries special halachic weight beyond that of a single ordinary witness.

Furthermore, says the *baraita*, *ukshem shene'eman adam lomar zeh bni bechor, kach ne'eman adam lomar zeh ben gerusha v'zeh ben chalutza* — just as a person is believed to identify his firstborn son, so too is he believed to say about a son of his that his mother was a *gerusha* — a divorcee — or a *chalutza* — a woman who underwent *chalitza* — thereby declaring that his son, if he is a *Kohen*, is in fact a *chalal*, disqualified from the priesthood. The leap from a monetary ruling about the *bechor's* double portion to a declaration affecting personal status is striking, and the *gemara* will address the basis for it.

The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, say *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed. But this requires clarification: do the *Rabbanan* deny the father's credibility entirely?

## Father's Testimony (Continued)

### Yakkir Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״יַכִּיר״. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, ״יַכִּיר״ לְמָה לִי? בִּצְרִיךְ הֶיכֵּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rava: Granted,** according **to Rabbi Yehuda, this is** the reason **that it is written: "He shall acknowledge;"** he derives from these words that a father is deemed credible to attest to the identity of his sons. **But** according **to the Rabbis, why do I** need the term **"he shall acknowledge"?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary for a case **when he requires identification,** i.e., when there is no presumption as to the identity of the firstborn. In such a situation the verse teaches that the father is deemed credible. The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yehuda's opinion only in a case where another son is presumed to be the firstborn.

*Amar lei Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak le-Rava: bishlama le-Rabbi Yehuda haynu dechtiv yakkir* — for Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the father is believed even against a *chazakah*, it is understandable why the Torah needed to write *yakkir*. The father is given special power to establish the identity of the *bechor* that goes beyond what his single testimony would ordinarily accomplish. *Ela le-Rabbanan yakkir lama li?* — but according to the *Rabbanan*, who limit the father's credibility, what is the function of the word *yakkir*?

The *gemara* answers: *betzarich hikkira* — it applies where the *bechor* needs to be recognized, meaning in a case of genuine doubt with no preexisting *chazakah*. According to this, everyone — Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan* alike — agrees that the father is believed in a neutral case where there is no *chazakah*. The dispute is limited to a case where the father's testimony goes against an established presumption. Rabbi Yehuda says the father is believed even there; the *Rabbanan* say he is not.

## Father's Credibility (Continued)

### Migu Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא, לְמִיתְּבָא לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם? לֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא אַחֵר – אִילּוּ בָּעֵי לְמִיתְּבָא לֵיהּ בְּמַתָּנָה, מִי לָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ?! לָא צְרִיכָא, בִּנְכָסִים שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ לְאַחַר מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to the Rabbis, **with regard to what *halakha*** is it necessary for the verse to teach that a father's identification of his firstborn is deemed credible? If it is **with regard to giving him a double** portion, it is superfluous. **Let** the son **be only** like **another** person, i.e., one who is not an heir; **if** the father **wants to give him** a double portion of his estate **as a gift, can he not give** it to **him?** Since the father can give a double portion to the firstborn without having to testify that he is his firstborn, it cannot be that the verse is teaching us that he is deemed credible with regard to the *halakha* that a firstborn receives a double portion. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary with regard to property that came into** the father's possession **afterward,** after he testified that his son is his firstborn. He could not have given him this property as a gift, as at the time of his testimony, the property did not yet belong to him, and one cannot transfer ownership of an item that one does not own. Therefore, it is necessary for the verse to teach that the father's testimony is deemed credible so that the son can receive a double portion of the father's future property as well.

*Ela amar Rav Ami — vetzaricha hacha, lemai hilcheta?* — but then one must ask: according to the *Rabbanan*, in what cases does the *Torah's* special grant of credibility actually matter? If there is no *chazakah*, the father is believed anyway — but why would the father need extraordinary credibility even in a neutral case? Let the *Torah* simply believe him on the basis of *migo*: since the father could have simply given this son the extra portion as a gift, he should be believed when he declares him the *bechor*, because he could have accomplished the same end through other means.

The *gemara* answers: *lo, tzericha binechasim shenafalu lo le'achar mikan* — the *Torah's* special grant of credibility is necessary for property the father acquires after making the declaration. The argument from *migo* is compelling for the property the father holds at the moment of the declaration — had he wished this son to receive the extra portion from his current estate, he could simply have given it as a gift. But *migo* only extends as far as the father's capacity to act at the time of the statement. If the father later acquires new assets — the *gemara* frames it as property that *falls to him* subsequently — the earlier *migo* carries no weight regarding those new assets, since at the time the father made his declaration he could not have gifted what he did not yet have.

Consider a concrete illustration. At the moment the father declares "this is my *bechor*," suppose he holds a certain sum and has four children. His declaration is believed to award the *bechor* a double portion of what he currently has, because he could have accomplished this by gift. But if he subsequently inherits or earns a large additional sum and then dies, the question is whether the earlier declaration governs that additional property. If the declaration's credibility rested solely on *migo*, the answer is no — the *migo* only had the weight of what he could have gifted at the moment he spoke. The *Torah's* grant of special *ne'emanut* to the father is therefore essential: it means the declaration establishes the status of *bechor* in a way that governs all subsequent property, not merely property he held at the moment of speaking.

### Future Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם, ״יַכִּיר״ לְמָה לִי? בִּנְכָסִים שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹסֵס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a person can transfer an entity that has not** yet **come into the world,** which would mean that one can give away his property even before it enters his possession, **why do I** need the term **"he shall acknowledge"?** The term is necessary for a case **where property came into** the father's possession **when he was moribund,** at which point he cannot transfer ownership of any of his property to others. It is with regard to such property that the verse states: "He shall acknowledge," to teach that the son who the father states is the firstborn will receive a double portion even of that property.

The *gemara* then asks whether this problem is resolved by the opinion of Rav Meir, who holds that *adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam* — a person can transfer ownership of something not yet in the world. If the father could declare right now "I am gifting this son a double share of everything I will ever acquire," the *migo* would extend to all future property as well. The *gemara* responds: *binechasim shenafalu k'shehu goses* — property that comes to the father while he is on his deathbed and is not of sound mind. Even according to Rav Meir, the capacity to transfer something acquired in the future requires that when the property actually arrives, the person is in a position to make the transfer. If when the asset is received the person is dying and incapacitated, the earlier general declaration of transfer does not take effect. The *Torah's* grant of credibility to the father therefore remains necessary even according to Rav Meir — specifically for assets that arrive under such circumstances.

### Tosafot Logic

Tosafot raise a further question. The *baraita* equates the father's credibility regarding *bechor* with his credibility regarding *ben gerusha* or *ben chalutza* — declarations that affect personal status, not merely monetary entitlement. How does the *migo* logic — which is grounded in the father's ability to redistribute monetary shares — justify credibility about personal status matters entirely? The transfer from one domain to the other requires explanation.

Tosafot offer a striking answer. Suppose a father says of a newborn: "this is my *bechor*." The *Torah* believes him. But consider what this implies about an older child already in the household — a child born to the father's wife twenty years earlier. The only way for the newborn to be the *bechor* — if the older child was also born to the same wife — is if the older child is not truly the father's son. And in a world without adoption, the only way a child born to the wife would not be the father's son is through adultery. Therefore, Tosafot argue, implicit in the *Torah's* crediting the father's identification of the *bechor* is the recognition that he may simultaneously be declaring the older child a *mamzer*. Since the *Torah* believes him in a context that carries this implicit consequence for personal status, it must be that the father's credibility extends to personal status declarations as well — at least according to Rabbi Yehuda, who believes the father even against a *chazakah*. The *Rabbanan*, who confine the father's credibility to neutral cases without a prior *chazakah*, would not necessarily agree, since for them the father's special credibility is far more limited in scope. Tosafot offer an alternative explanation as well — that the analogy is looser, simply an extension of the principle that if the *Torah* believed the father about one aspect of his son's status, it believed him about other aspects too — but this first analysis is the more compelling of the two.

## Competing Presumptions

### One Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיוּ מוּחְזָקִים בּוֹ שֶׁהוּא בְּכוֹר, וְאָמַר אָבִיו עַל אַחֵר שֶׁהוּא בְּכוֹר – נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:3): If the court **had a presumption concerning** a son **that he is a firstborn, and his father said concerning another** son of his **that he is** his **firstborn,** the father's statement is **deemed credible.**

*Tanu Rabbanan* — the *gemara* turns to a *baraita*. *Hayu muchzakin bo shehu bechor, ve'amar aviv al acher shehu bechor — ne'eman* — people assumed that a certain person was the *bechor*, and the father said that a different son is the *bechor*; the father is believed. This sounds like Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Negative Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ מוּחְזָקִים בּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּכוֹר, וְאָמַר אָבִיו: בְּכוֹר הוּא – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** If the court **had a presumption concerning** a son **that he is not a firstborn, and his father said** that **he is** his **firstborn,** the father's statement is **not deemed credible.**

*Hayu muchzakin bo she'eino bechor, ve'amar aviv bechor hu — eino ne'eman* — people assumed that a certain person was not the *bechor*, and the father said that he is the *bechor*; the father is not believed. This sounds like the *Rabbanan's* position.

### Baraita Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְסֵיפָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asserts that as the two *halakhot* of the *baraita* seem to contradict each other, it must be that **the first clause** represents the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and the latter clause** represents the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction within the *baraita*: *reisha Rabbi Yehuda vesiepa Rabbanan* — the first clause reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view, the second reflects the *Rabbanan's* view. The *baraita* is not a single unified opinion.

It is worth noting, however, that there may be a way to read both clauses consistently even within the *Rabbanan's* framework. In the first case, the father is identifying one son as *bechor* and thereby displacing another specific person who had been presumed to hold that status — he is, in effect, taking the double portion away from an identified individual. In the second case, society merely assumed that this son was not the *bechor*; there is no specific other person who had been affirmatively presumed to be the *bechor*. Perhaps even the *Rabbanan* would agree that the father is believed in the second scenario, because he is not actively displacing anyone's established status. The *gemara*, however, simply reads the *baraita* as divided between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan*.

## Son vs. Slave

### Son to Slave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָמַר ״בְּנִי הוּא״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״עַבְדִּי הוּא״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. ״עַבְדִּי הוּא״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״בְּנִי הוּא״ – נֶאֱמָן, דִּמְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ כְּעַבְדָּא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that if one **says** about another: **He is my son, and then says: He is my slave,** his latter statement is **not deemed credible.** It cannot disqualify him from marrying a Jewish woman or exclude him from his inheritance. Conversely, if he first says: **He is my slave, and then says: He is my son,** his latter statement is **deemed credible, as** in his first statement he presumably **was saying: He serves me as a slave** does.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan: amar beni hu, vechazar ve'amar avdi hu — eino ne'eman* — if a person arrives with a young man and declares "this is my son," and subsequently says "no, he is really my slave," he is not believed in his second statement. The rationale is straightforward: one does not refer to one's slave as one's son. Having made the first declaration, the reversal carries no credibility.

### Slave to Son

The reverse case follows: *avdi hu, vechazar ve'amar beni hu — ne'eman* — if he first says "this is my slave" and then says "he is my son," he is believed in the second statement. The *gemara* explains: *de'meshamesh li ke'avda kamina* — when he called him his slave, he meant that this son attends to him in a manner resembling a slave's service. It is linguistically plausible to refer to a devoted son with such language; it is not plausible to refer to a slave as one's son.

### Tax Collector

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחִילּוּפֵיהּ אַבֵּית הַמֶּכֶס – הָיָה עוֹבֵר עַל בֵּית הַמֶּכֶס וְאָמַר ״בְּנִי הוּא״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״עַבְדִּי הוּא״ – נֶאֱמָן. אָמַר ״עַבְדִּי הוּא״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״בְּנִי הוּא״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the opposite** *halakha* applies **to** one **at the tax house:** If one **is passing the customs house and says** about a certain person: **He is my son, and then** later **says: He is my slave,** his latter statement is **deemed credible,** as he presumably says that the other is his son only to evade paying a tax for possessing a slave. Conversely, if **he says** at the customs house: **He is my slave, and then** later **says: He is my son,** his latter statement is **not deemed credible,** as one would not unnecessarily render himself liable to pay a tax.

An important qualification applies, however, when the context involves a tax collection house — *beit hamachas*. Slaves constituted property on which taxes were owed; sons did not. If a person was passing through the customs house and declared "this is my son" — thereby avoiding the tax — and then subsequently claimed "he is really my slave," he is believed in the second statement. The initial declaration of sonship was plausibly motivated by the desire to evade taxation, and therefore the subsequent correction toward "slave" is credible. Conversely, *amar avdi hu, vechazar ve'amar beni hu — eino ne'eman* — if at the customs house he first said "this is my slave" and then claimed "he is my son," he is not believed. There is no reason to have declared someone your slave if he were not; the reversal is implausible.

### Service Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיָה מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ כְּבֵן, וּבָא וְאָמַר ״בְּנִי הוּא״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״עַבְדִּי הוּא״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. הָיָה מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ כְּעֶבֶד, וּבָא וְאָמַר ״עַבְדִּי הוּא״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״בְּנִי הוּא״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If one **was serving** another person **like a son** serves his father, **and** the person being served **came and said: He is my son, and then said: He is my slave,** his latter statement is **not deemed credible.** Additionally, if one **was serving** another person **like a slave** serves his master, **and** the one being served **came and said: He is my slave, and then said: He is my son,** his latter statement is **not deemed credible.** This contradicts Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that if he first says that he is his slave and then says that he is his son, his latter statement is deemed credible.

A *baraita* then refines this: *haya meshamesho keven — eino ne'eman* — if the young man was attending to the man in the manner of a son, and the man declared "this is my son" and subsequently said "this is my slave," he is not believed. This is consistent with what was said earlier.

*Haya meshamesho ke'eved — eino ne'eman* — if he was attending in the manner of a slave, and the man first declared "this is my slave" and then said "this is my son," he is likewise not believed. This appears to contradict the earlier ruling, which held that saying "slave" first and then "son" is believed on the grounds that "slave" may have been intended as a description of the son's devoted service.

### Dollar Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָתָם, דְּקָארוּ לֵיהּ: ״עַבְדָּא מְצַר מְאָה״. מַאי ״מְצַר מְאָה״? מְצַר עַבְדָּא מְאָה זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: There,** in Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, it is a case where he is presumed to be a slave **because** everyone **calls him: A slave** on whose **border [*metzar*]** are **one hundred.** Since everyone is referring to him in this manner, the person he served cannot call into question his status as a slave by saying that he is his son. The Gemara asks: **What** does it mean that on his **border [*metzar*]** are **one hundred?** The Gemara answers: **One hundred dinars are adjacent to [*metzar*]** this **slave.** In other words, he is worth one hundred dinars.

The resolution is offered by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *hatam dekaru lei avda dematzar me'ah* — the case in the *baraita* where the second declaration of sonship is not believed is specifically where the initial declaration of "slave" was accompanied by a dollar valuation — identifying this person as a slave worth one hundred *zuzei*. When a person not only calls someone his slave but also attaches a monetary value, he has gone beyond a loose manner of speaking and has formally commodified this individual as property. Under those circumstances it is not credible that the declaration was merely figurative, and the subsequent claim of sonship is therefore not believed. Where no such valuation was attached, the initial word "slave" remains ambiguous enough that the second declaration of sonship can be credited.

The deeper discomfort underlying this entire sugya — namely, that a person is simply believed to declare another human being his slave — is itself a striking feature of these rulings that warrants further study.

## Oath & Property

### Theft Counterclaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא, הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״עַבְדִּי גָּנַבְתָּ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״. ״מָה טִיבוֹ אֶצְלְךָ?״ ״אַתָּה מְכַרְתּוֹ לִי; אַתָּה נְתַתּוֹ לִי בְּמַתָּנָה. רְצוֹנְךָ הִשָּׁבַע וְטוֹל?״ וְנִשְׁבַּע – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Abba sent** several rulings from Eretz Yisrael **to Rav Yosef bar Ḥama,** including the following: In the case of **one who says to another: You stole my slave, and he says** in response: **I did not steal** him, and when the first inquires: If so, **what is the nature of his** presence **in your** possession, the latter responds: **You sold him to me, or: You gave him to me as a gift,** if he then adds: Although I owe you nothing, as the slave is mine, if it is **your desire, take an oath** that he is yours **and take** him; **and** the claimant **takes an oath** that the slave is his, the one in possession of the slave **cannot retract** his offer, although he was not obligated to make the offer.

*Shelach lei Rav Abba le-Rav Yosef bar Chama* — Rav Abba sent a query to Rav Yosef bar Chama. If one person says to another "you stole my slave," and the other responds "I did not steal him — you sold him to me" or "you gave him to me as a gift," the matter requires resolution.

### Slave Chazaka

Because slaves are self-mobile — they move themselves — the principle of *chazakah* that whatever is found under a person's control belongs to that person does not straightforwardly apply to slaves. A slave found in someone's yard may simply have walked there. For possession to constitute a *chazakah* in the case of a slave, either the slave must be too young to walk and is found in the person's care, or the slave has been present at the person's home for three consecutive years — which recalls the earlier discussions in the *masechta* about three years of possession establishing *chazakah*. The case here is presumably one of those two scenarios, so that the person's possession of the slave does function as a presumption of ownership. Even so, the plaintiff insists the slave was stolen.

### Oath Solution

The person who holds the slave says: you are so insistent, *sheva v'tol* — take an oath and take him. *V'nishba* — the plaintiff takes the oath. *Eino yachol lachzor bo* — the holder of the slave cannot then retract and say he did not intend to be bound by this offer. Having extended it and having had the other party accept and take the oath, the commitment is final and the slave must be surrendered.

### Precedent Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא, אָמַר לוֹ: ״נֶאֱמָן עָלַי אַבָּא״; ״נֶאֱמָן עָלַי אָבִיךָ״; ״נֶאֱמָנִין עָלַי שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָקָר״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rabbi Abba **teaching us? We** already **learned** in a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 24a) that if one of the litigants **said to** the other: My **father is trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** or: **Your father is trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** or: These **three cattle herders** who are not experts in *halakha* **are trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** and all of the individuals this litigant mentioned are legally disqualified from serving as judges, **Rabbi Meir says** that the one who stated this **can retract** his statement, **and the Rabbis say** that **he cannot retract** it, and he is obligated to have them serve as judges if the other litigant so desires. Since this *halakha* is already stated in the mishna, what novel *halakha* did Rabbi Abba teach?

The *gemara* questions whether this ruling is novel. Is it not already established that when parties to a dispute agree to modify the rules of adjudication — for example, agreeing that individuals normally disqualified from serving as judges, such as the other party's father or three itinerant cattlemen, may serve — they are bound by that agreement? The precedent *amar lo ne'eman alai aba, ne'eman alai avicha, ne'emanim alai shlosha ro'ei bakar* already establishes that agreed-upon modifications to court procedure are binding. The modification of oath terms in the present case should follow the same principle.

### Chachamim Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דִּבְ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ מַחְלוֹקֶת, וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara in tractate *Sanhedrin* records a dispute between *amora'im* as to the circumstances of the case of that mishna, with one *amora* holding that the dispute pertains only to a case where the one accepting the disqualified judge will need to forgive a debt if the judge rules in accordance with his opponent, while another *amora* holds that the dispute pertains even to a case where he will need to pay the other litigant if the judge rules in accordance with his opponent. By stating his ruling in a case where the litigant will have to give the slave to the other, Rabbi Abba **teaches us this, that the dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is even **in** a case where the litigant said: **I will give you** what you claim if that is the ruling of these judges, and the Rabbis hold that if he subsequently wishes to retract his suggestion, he cannot do so. **And** Rabbi Abba also teaches that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* responds that the case here is teaching two things. First, that the *halacha* follows the *Chachamim* against Rav Meir on this question — that such agreements are binding and one cannot retract. And second, and more specifically, the case involves the most expansive application of this principle: not merely an agreement that an otherwise invalid judge's ruling will release the other party from an obligation (*atol*), but an agreement that it will affirmatively compel the other party to pay or surrender property (*etein lecha*). There is a position that such an agreement only works to prevent someone from having to give up what they hold, but not to compel an active transfer. Here the *gemara* rules that even an agreement structured as *etein lecha* — one that will result in someone being compelled to hand over assets or a slave — is binding. We rule like the *Chachamim* that the agreement cannot be retracted, and that this holds even in the more stringent case.
