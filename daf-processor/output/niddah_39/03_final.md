# Niddah 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fourth Perek & Eleven Da…

### Fourth Perek Context

We begin today at *daf* 39, picking up on the *mishna* found at the very last line of 38. The third *perek* was devoted entirely to *yoledet*. The fourth *perek*, *Bnos Kusim*, opened with women of various statuses — *Tzadukim*, *Kutim*, and a series of special cases — before doubling back to *yoledet* and the discussion of *dam koshi*. It is not entirely obvious what principle of organization governs this *perek*, though some have suggested it is unified by the theme of *dam ziva*, since to some degree every case implicates the status of a *zav* as well. Whatever the theory, the *mishna* before us now brings us back to the foundational *halachos* of *niddah* and away from the more specialized discussions of *yoledet* and *dam koshi*.

### Kol Achas Asar Yom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם בְּחֶזְקַת טׇהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** For **all** the **eleven days** of *ziva* that follow the seven days of menstruation, a woman **has the presumptive status of ritual purity,** as it is unusual for her to experience bleeding on these days.

The *mishna* opens: *kol achas asar yom bechezkas tahara* — for the entire eleven days following *niddah*, a woman is presumed to be in a state of purity. Which eleven days are these? They are the eleven days after *niddah* during which, if a woman bleeds, she would be a *zava* rather than a *niddah*. The underlying logic is that the minimum interval of a normal menstrual cycle is nineteen days. Accordingly, anything less — days eight through eighteen from the previous onset of bleeding — is considered unusual, non-standard, and not menstrual blood. Because these days are not expected to be days of bleeding, she is assumed to be *tehora* during them.

### Eleven Days Logic

What precisely does it mean that she is presumed *tehora* during those eleven days? The *Gemara* will address this shortly, because we may recall the *mishna* earlier — around *daf* 12 — which stated that every woman, without exception, is required to check for *taharos* at regular intervals throughout the year, except perhaps when she is *niddah*, when such checking is irrelevant. Even women who are *mesulakos damim* — whose flow is suspended, such as a pregnant woman, a nursing woman, or a woman past menopause — still carry an obligation to check. So in what way are the eleven days of *zivah* a state of *bechezkas tahara* that excuses her from checking? This question will drive the *Gemara*'s discussion.

## Checking Obligations

### Yashva Lo Velo Badka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָשְׁבָה לָהּ וְלֹא בָּדְקָה, שָׁגְגָה, נֶאֶנְסָה, הֵזִידָה וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If a woman **sat and did not examine** herself every morning and evening to determine whether she emitted blood and is impure, it makes no difference whether she failed to examine herself **unwittingly** or due to **circumstances beyond her control,** or even if she acted **intentionally and did not examine** herself; she remains **ritually pure.** She is rendered impure only if she examined herself and was found to have emitted blood.

The *mishna* continues: *yashva lo velo badka* — if a woman who was supposed to check did not do so. This refers to any woman who failed to perform the twice-daily check that was obligatory in a world concerned with *tuma ve-tahara*. Whether she was negligent (*shogega*), compelled (*ones*), or deliberately chose not to (*hizida*), the result is the same: *tehora*. She is presumed pure regardless.

### Higia Zman Vesta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ שְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ, וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ טְמֵאָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה בְּמַחֲבֵא, וְהִגִּיעַ שְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ, וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ טְהוֹרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲרָדָה מְסַלֶּקֶת אֶת הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **the time of her** menstrual **cycle arrived,** when she is required to examine herself, **and she did not examine** herself, **that** woman **is ritually impure,** as it is typical for a woman to discharge blood at that time. **Rabbi Meir says:** If a woman **was in hiding** from danger, **and the time of her** menstrual **cycle arrived and she did not examine** herself on that day, **that** woman **is pure, because fear drives away blood.** There is therefore no concern that she might have emitted blood.

However, the *mishna* introduces a different case: *higia zman vesta velo badka* — the time of her expected period arrived and she did not check. This situation carries a different weight. That day she is assumed to have seen blood unless she can demonstrate otherwise. Accordingly, *harei zu tmeia* — she must consider herself *tmeia*. This principle is the foundational basis for the practice that women check on the day they anticipate their period. The twice-daily checking discussed earlier, which is an obligation within a system of *tuma ve-tahara*, does not carry consequences if left undone; it does not shift her presumptive status. But the day of the *veset* — the expected onset — does. If she fails to check on that day, she is presumed to have bled. This distinction is critical for contemporary practice: the obligation to check at the anticipated time of one's period, and the consequence of not having done so, remains operative today.

### Rabbi Meir - Fear & Hidi…

*Rabbi Meir* introduces an exception: *im haysa bemachvo* — if she was in hiding, fearing for her life, and the time of her *veset* arrived without her having checked, *harei zu tehora* — she is considered pure. The reason given: *mipnei shecharada mesalekes es hadamim* — fear causes the bleeding to be suspended. This overrides the normal assumption that a woman who fails to check at the time of her expected period is presumed *tmeia*.

### Zav & Zavah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל יְמֵי הַזָּב וְהַזָּבָה, וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בְּחֶזְקַת טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to the seven clean **days of the *zav* and the *zava*, and** with regard to a woman who **observes** a clean **day for a day** she experiences a discharge during her days of *ziva*, if she fails to examine herself on those days, **these** women **have a presumptive status of ritual impurity,** as they already experienced a discharge.

The *mishna* closes with another category: *aval yemei hazav vehazava veshomeret yom keneged yom* — when it comes to the days of the *zav*, the *zavah*, and the *shomeres yom keneged yom*. If a person has become a *zav*, or a woman has bled during the eleven days and become a *zavah gedolah*, or she bled once and is a *zavah ketanah* — *shomeres yom keneged yom* — needing to observe a clean day, then *harei elu bechezkas tumah*. Because of the concern that the flow may continue, if they fail to check, they are assumed to have had continued bleeding. This anticipates the requirement of checking during the *shiva nekiim* — the seven clean days a woman must count after becoming a *zavah*. The checking during that period carries a different quality: failure to check during the clean days results in a presumption of continued *tumah*.

So in sum, the *mishna* delineates three tiers of checking. First, the standard twice-daily check, which is operative in a world of *tuma ve-tahara* but which, if omitted, does not alter one's status (*b'diavad tehora*); this we no longer practice. Second, checking at the time of the expected *veset*, which if omitted results in a presumption of *tumah*; this we do observe today. Third, checking during the clean days following *zivah* bleeding, which likewise carries a presumption of *tumah* if omitted.

## Lemai Hilchata

### Rav Yehuda - No Checking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה. וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: "יָשְׁבָה וְלֹא בָּדְקָה" — מִכְּלָל דִּלְכַתְּחִלָּה בָּעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that throughout the eleven days of *ziva* that follow the seven days of menstruation, a woman has the presumptive status of ritual purity. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this stated? **Rav Yehuda says:** This serves **to say that she does not require an examination** during these days. The Gemara objects: **But from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches:** If **she sat and did not examine** herself she remains ritually pure, **it can be inferred that she requires examination *ab initio*.**

The *Gemara* now asks: *l'mai hilchata* — for what practical purpose does the *mishna* state that the eleven days are *bechezkas tahara*? *Rav Yehuda* answers: to teach us *she'eina tzricha bedika* — that a woman does not need to check during those days. This seems reasonable on its face.

### Contradiction: Daily Che…

But the *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty. The *seifa* of the *mishna* — the latter portion — states *yashva velo badka tehora*, implying by implication (*miklal d'lechatchila ba'ei bedika*) that she was in fact required to check beforehand. If the opening line is telling us she is exempt from checking during the eleven days, why does the *mishna* right afterward speak of a woman who failed to check when she was supposed to — as if those are the very same eleven days?

### Seifa Atan L'Yemei Nida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לִימֵי נִדָּה, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל אַחַד עָשָׂר בְּחֶזְקַת טׇהֳרָה, וְלָא בָּעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה, אֲבָל בִּימֵי נִדָּתָהּ — בָּעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה. יָשְׁבָה וְלֹא בָּדְקָה, שָׁגְגָה, נֶאֶנְסָה, הֵזִידָה וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In **the latter clause we arrive at** the case of a woman who is in the **days of menstruation,** not in the days of *ziva*. **And this** is what the mishna **is saying:** For **all** the **eleven days** of *ziva* that follow the days of menstruation, a woman **has the presumptive status of ritual purity and she does not require examination. But during the days of her menstruation she requires examination.** Nevertheless, if **she sat and did not examine** herself, whether **unwittingly** or due to **circumstances beyond her control,** or even if she acted **intentionally and did not examine** herself, she remains **ritually pure.**

The *Gemara* resolves this by reading the *mishna* as addressing two separate *halachos*. The *seifa* is not a continuation of the opening statement about the eleven days; rather, *seifa atan l'yemei niddah* — the latter portion transitions to a different law, concerning the days of *niddah*. Here is how the *mishna* should be read: *kol yod-alef yom bechezkas tahara v'lo ba'ei bedika* — the eleven days are presumed pure and no checking is required. That is the first *halacha*. Separately, the rest of the *mishna* addresses the other days of the month when a woman could become a *niddah*: during those days she is required to check, but *b'diavad*, if she did not check — whether deliberately, inadvertently, or under compulsion — she remains *tehora*. The exception is when the time of her *veset* arrives: then failure to check results in a presumption of *tumah*.

This is an elegant reading of the *mishna*, though it creates a significant challenge. It now appears that during the eleven days — uniquely — a woman is exempt from the twice-daily checking obligation, while during the rest of the month she remains obligated, even if the failure to check carries no retroactive consequence. This seems to invert what we might have expected, because we have seen that women who are *mesulakos damim* — pregnant, nursing, or pre-menopausal — are still required to check precisely during those periods when they are not expected to bleed. *Tosafos* addresses this tension and arrives at a somewhat counterintuitive conclusion: the temporary states of *mesulekos damim* — pregnancy, nursing, pre-menopause — occur within a woman's general years of active menstrual life, and therefore the checking obligation persists even during those intervals. But the eleven days between *niddah* and *niddah* are structurally different: they are never a time when menstrual bleeding is anticipated, as a matter of the basic calendar of *tumah*. Therefore, unlike the temporary states of *mesulekos damim*, the eleven days are categorically exempt. One might add that the post-menopausal woman presents a difficulty for this analysis since her state is permanent — *Tosafos* grapples with that case as well — but the framework is as described.

## Rabbi Meir's Position

### Yotzi Ve-Lo Yachzir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ וֶסֶת אֲסוּרָה לְשַׁמֵּשׁ. הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּימֵי נִדָּתָהּ, אֲבָל בִּימֵי זִיבָתָהּ בְּחֶזְקַת טׇהֳרָה קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** a different answer: The first clause of the mishna **is necessary only for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said:** With regard to **a woman who does not have** a fixed menstrual **cycle,** she is **forbidden to engage in intercourse,** lest she emit blood during intercourse. The mishna is teaching that **this statement** applies only **during the days of her menstruation, but during the days of her *ziva*** even Rabbi Meir concedes that she **stands in** her **presumptive status of purity** and may engage in intercourse with her husband.

*Rav Hisda* now offers an alternative: the teaching that the eleven days are *bechezkas tahara* is relevant *le-Rabbi Meir*. *Rabbi Meir* had ruled that a woman who lacks a fixed period (*she'ein lah veset*) is forbidden to have relations with her husband, because any day could potentially be the onset of her period — *asurah le-shamesh*. The *bechezkas tahara* of the eleven days comes to limit this prohibition. *Hani milei bimei niddatah* — Rabbi Meir's concern applies to the days when she could become a *niddah*. But *bimei zivatah bechezkas tahara kaima* — during the eleven days of potential *zivah*, she stands in a presumption of purity. The logic is compelling: even *Rabbi Meir*, who fears that the next period may come at any time, must concede that there is a minimum spacing between cycles. Days eight through eighteen cannot be the next period. Therefore, during those eleven days, a woman without a fixed *veset* is permitted to her husband.

### Danger of Transgression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: יוֹצִיא וְלֹא יַחְזִיר עוֹלָמִית? דִּלְמָא אָתְיָא לְקַלְקוֹלֵאּ בִּימֵי נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that even according to Rabbi Meir there are days in which a woman who does not have a fixed menstrual cycle is permitted to engage in sexual intercourse with her husband, **why did Rabbi Meir say** that her husband **must divorce** her **and he may never take** her **back,** even if she eventually develops a fixed menstrual cycle? Let them engage in intercourse during the eleven days of *ziva*. The Gemara answers: He must divorce her **lest** the matter **lead to failure during the days of menstruation,** i.e., in case they come to engage in intercourse during the days of menstruation, when she might experience regular menstrual bleeding.

The *Gemara* then asks: if the eleven days are permissible even according to *Rabbi Meir*, why did *Rabbi Meir* rule *yotzi velo yachzir* — that a husband must divorce a wife who lacks a fixed period and may never remarry her? Could he not remain married and simply confine marital relations to days eight through eighteen? The *Gemara* answers: *dilma atei le-akulei bimei niddah* — we are concerned that the *yetzer hara* will prevail. Once a husband becomes accustomed to the limited window of days eight through eighteen, we fear he will overstep and have relations on day nineteen, then day twenty, reasoning that she has not yet bled and has no fixed period, so what difference does it make. Since we cannot rely on him to maintain the restriction, *Rabbi Meir* requires divorce. *Tosafos* raises the question: if divorce is ultimately required, what is the practical relevance of permitting the eleven days? *Tosafos* answers that the permission applies during the period before the couple has gotten around to divorcing — the eleven days are permitted in that interval.

### Kula Rabbi Meir Hi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, רֵישָׁא לָאו רַבִּי מֵאִיר! כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם לֹא הָיְתָה בְּמַחֲבֵא וְהִגִּיעַ שְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — טְמֵאָה, שֶׁרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה בְּמַחֲבֵא וְהִגִּיעַ שְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — טְהוֹרָה, שֶׁחֲרָדָה מְסַלֶּקֶת אֶת הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But **from** the fact that **the last clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** it can be inferred that **the first clause** is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** The Gemara explains that **the entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and this** is what it **is saying: If she was not in hiding and the time of her** menstrual **cycle arrived and she did not examine** herself, she is **impure, as Rabbi Meir says:** If a woman **was in hiding and the time of her** menstrual **cycle arrived and she did not examine** herself, she is **pure, as fear drives away blood.**

The *Gemara* then addresses a structural problem with the *mishna*. Because *Rabbi Meir*'s name appears only toward the end of the *mishna* — introducing the *machvo* case about hiding from oppressors — one might infer that the earlier portion of the *mishna* is not *Rabbi Meir*'s opinion. If *Rabbi Meir* is first introduced at the end, then *reisha lav Rabbi Meir* — the opening is a different authority. The *Gemara* rejects this: *kula Rabbi Meir hi* — the entire *mishna* is *Rabbi Meir*. The opening, which establishes eleven days of *bechezkas tahara* and the rule about a woman without a *veset*, is *Rabbi Meir*. His statement about hiding is simply a qualification within his own position: in a normal case, if the time of the *veset* arrived and she did not check, she is *tmeia*; but if she was in hiding, she is *tehora* because fear suspends the bleeding.

Reading the *mishna* this way requires inserting implicit language. The *Gemara* reconstructs it as follows. *Kol yod-alef yom bechezkas tahara u-shraya le-ba'alah* — during the eleven days she is presumed pure and is permitted to her husband. *U-vimei niddatah asurah* — but during the rest of the days when she might become a *niddah*, she is forbidden. *Bameh devarim amurim* — to whom does this apply? *Be-ishah she-ein lah veset* — to a woman without a fixed period. But *aval yesh lah veset* — a woman who does have a fixed period is permitted to her husband at any time that she is *tehora*, without the constraint of the eleven-day window. She does, however, retain an obligation to check on a regular basis. If she did not check, she is *tehora b'diavad*, but once the time of her *veset* arrives and she fails to check, she is *tmeia*. The first line of the *mishna* therefore addresses the woman without a fixed period; the remainder addresses the woman with one. The *Gemara* notes that this reading requires treating the seifa as belonging to *Rabbi Meir* as well — the entire *mishna* is *Rabbi Meir*, with the hiding case as an internal qualification.

One observation worth noting: the *Gemara* operates on the assumption that a fixed *veset* is the standard condition for a woman. In contemporary practice, most women do not have a fully fixed *veset kavua* in the *halachic* sense, which may relate to how *vesotos* are taught and to how attuned we are to the patterns of our own bodies. But the *Gemara* treats *veset kavua* as thoroughly normative.

## Purpose Answers

### Metame'ah Me-Et Le-Et

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** a different explanation of the first clause of the mishna: The mishna is coming **to say that** if a woman experiences bleeding during the eleven days of *ziva*, as she previously had the presumptive status of purity **she does not impart impurity** retroactively **for** a twenty-four-hour **period** to any ritually pure items she touched. It is assumed that she did not emit any blood before this emission.

*Rava* now offers a third answer to *l'mai hilchata*. The relevance of the eleven days being *bechezkas tahara* is *lomar she-einah metame'ah me-et le-et* — to teach that a woman who bleeds during this period does not carry retroactive concern twenty-four hours back. This is a very elegant answer. Recall that *mesulakos damim* — women whose flow is suspended, such as pregnant or nursing women — do not need to be concerned retroactively about the twenty-four hours before they discovered the bleeding. This is precisely the practical meaning of *mesulakos damim*. The eleven days, like those periods, represent a time when a woman is not expected to bleed. Therefore, *Rava* suggests, when she does bleed during those days, she likewise need not extend retroactive concern back twenty-four hours. This would align the eleven days neatly with what we already know about *mesulakos damim*.

### Braita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַנִּדָּה, וְהַזָּבָה, וְהַשּׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם, וְהַיּוֹלֶדֶת — כּוּלָּן מְטַמְּאוֹת מֵעֵת לְעֵת. תְּיוּבְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **a menstruating woman, and a *zava*, and a woman who observes a day for a day, and a woman who gave birth, they all impart impurity** retroactively **for** a twenty-four-hour **period.** A woman who observes a day for a day is one who experiences bleeding for one or two days during her days of *ziva*, and the *baraita* teaches that even such a woman imparts impurity retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period. If so, this is **a conclusive refutation** of Rava's explanation.

The *Gemara* challenges this from a *braita*: *ha-niddah ve-ha-zavah ve-ha-shomeres yom keneged yom ve-ha-yoledet, kulam metame'os me-et le-et* — a *niddah*, a *zavah*, a *shomeres yom keneged yom*, and a *yoledet* all carry retroactive concern for twenty-four hours. So it is explicit that a woman who bleeds during the eleven days and becomes a *zavah* is *chosheshes* twenty-four hours back. This is a direct refutation of *Rava*'s answer, and the *Gemara* accepts it as such — *tiyuvta*. The third answer is rejected.

This outcome produces a striking irony that *Tosafos* takes pains to underscore. According to the first answer — *Rav Yehuda*'s — a woman during the eleven days does not need to check. Yet now we have established that if she bleeds during those days she does have retroactive concern twenty-four hours back. This is precisely the inverse of the pregnant woman: the pregnant woman must check twice daily, yet if she bleeds she does not carry retroactive concern. The woman during the eleven days need not check at all, yet if she bleeds she does carry retroactive concern. It is a genuinely counterintuitive asymmetry, but the *Gemara* leaves it in place.

### Eina Kova'as Vest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי זִיבָתָהּ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי הָא שְׁמַעְתְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya says** another explanation of the first clause of the mishna in the name of **Shmuel:** The mishna is coming **to say that** a woman **does not fix** a menstrual **cycle for herself during the days of her *ziva*.** In other words, a sighting during these days does not combine with sightings during the previous two periods of *ziva* to establish a fixed menstrual cycle. The Gemara relates that this statement was recited before **Rav Yosef,** who **said: I did not hear this *halakha*** from Shmuel.

The fourth answer is offered by *Rav Huna bar Chiya* in the name of *Shmuel*: *lo basi lomar lach she'eina kova'as veset besoch yemei zivasah* — the relevance of the eleven days is that a woman cannot establish a fixed *veset* during those days. This answer flows directly from the conceptual logic of *zivah*: if the reason bleeding during days eight through eighteen constitutes *zivah* rather than *niddah* is precisely because that blood cannot be menstrual, then by definition a cycle shorter than nineteen days cannot be recognized as a normal menstrual period. A woman who bleeds every eighteen days, or every fifteen days, cannot have that pattern recognized as a *veset kavua*, because the very structure of *zivah* denies menstrual status to blood occurring within those days.

## Veset Kavua

### Fixed Period Definition

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing to understand why *veset kavua* matters so much. *Rashi* captures the essential point with characteristic brevity. The two principal *nafka minas* of having a *veset kavua* are the following.

### Veset Stringencies

First, there is a stringency: once a woman has established a fixed period, that day remains a day of concern even if her pattern shifts. Should a woman who normally sees on day 30 begin seeing on day 25 for two months running, she cannot yet abandon concern for day 30. A *veset kavua* is not dislodged until it has been reversed three consecutive times. Fewer than three reversals leave the original day still operative, so she must check both on day 25 — based on recent patterns — and on day 30, until the old *veset* has been clearly negated.

Second, and this follows from the general rule about the day of the *veset*: if the day of her *veset kavua* arrives and she does not check, she is presumed *tmeia*. The *veset kavua* transforms that day into a day of heightened concern with real presumptive consequences for failure to check.

There is, of course, a significant leniency on the other side. *Rabbi Meir* held that a woman without any *veset* at all cannot remain married to her husband. A *veset kavua* removes that radical concern: she is permitted to her husband throughout the month except on the days of concern themselves. So while the *veset kavua* heightens concern on its specific day, it simultaneously resolves the broader anxiety about unpredictable bleeding. The importance of establishing a *veset kavua* therefore cuts in both directions. As regards the fourth answer, its halachic implication is clear: any cycle of fewer than nineteen days cannot attain the status of *veset kavua*.

### Lo Shmi'a Li

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי הָא שְׁמַעְתְּתָא.

*Rav Yosef*, who was a student of *Rav Yehuda*, who in turn was a prominent student of *Shmuel*, responds to the fourth answer by saying: *lo shmi'a li ha shema'ata* — I never heard this teaching. The ruling is being transmitted in the name of *Shmuel*, yet *Rav Yosef*, who was trained in *Shmuel*'s tradition through *Rav Yehuda*, has no recollection of it. This is not an uncommon situation for *Rav Yosef*, who suffered an illness that caused him to forget much of his learning. His *lo shmi'a li* is therefore not simply a denial but an implicit challenge to the tradition's authenticity: had it genuinely come from *Shmuel*, he argues, he would have encountered it.

## Veset Kavua (II)

### Day 15 to Day 20

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַתְּ אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן, וְאַהָא אֲמַרְתְּ לַן: הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר (יוֹם), וְשִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין לְשַׁמֵּשׁ. שִׁינְּתָה פַּעֲמַיִם לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** But **you** yourself **told us** this *halakha*, **and** it was **with regard to that** mishna **you told** it to **us,** as we learned in a mishna (63b): If the woman **was accustomed to see** an emission of blood on the **fifteenth day,** so that this was her fixed menstrual cycle, **and she deviated** from her cycle **to** see an emission on the **twentieth day,** then on both **this** day, the fifteenth, **and that** day, the twentieth, it is **prohibited** for her to engage in sexual intercourse due to the concern that she might have an emission on either day. If **she deviated** from her cycle **twice, to** see an emission on the **twentieth day,** then on both **this** day **and that** day it is **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse. If she deviates a third time to see on the twentieth she has established for herself a new fixed menstrual cycle.

*Abaye* responds to this challenge by invoking a *mishna* that *Rav Yosef* himself had taught them. The *mishna* presents the following case: a woman who regularly saw on day 15 (*haisah lemudah lihayos ro'ah yom tes-vav*) and switched to seeing on day 20 (*veshinsah leyom chaf*) — both days are forbidden (*ze vaze asurin*). One month later, she again sees on day 20 — still both days are forbidden. The rule is that a *veset kavua* must be reversed three consecutive times before the old day is no longer a concern. Accordingly, until she has seen on day 20 three times in a row without seeing on day 15, she remains obligated to be concerned about both days.

## Counting Methods (II)

### Abaye's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרְתְּ לַן עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לָא שְׁנָא אֶלָּא חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לִטְבִילָתָהּ, שֶׁהֵן עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁנַיִם לִרְאִיָּתָהּ, דְּהָתָם בִּימֵי נִדָּתָהּ קָאֵי לַהּ, אֲבָל חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לִרְאִיָּתָהּ, דְּבִימֵי זִיבָתָהּ קָאֵי — לָא קָבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And you said to us with regard to this** mishna that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to a woman who normally experiences bleeding **fifteen** days **from her immersion, which are twenty-two** days **from her sighting** of menstrual blood. This means **that there, she stands in her days of menstruation. But** if she normally experiences bleeding **fifteen** days **from her sighting** of menstrual blood, so **that she stands in her days of *ziva*,** she **has not fixed** a menstrual cycle, and the previous cycle is uprooted even if she deviates from it only once.

*Abaye* presses further: you, *Rav Yosef*, taught us this *mishna* and explained it to us in the name of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel*. You therefore did in fact transmit *Shmuel*'s position to us, even if you no longer recall having done so.

### Tes-Vav Litvilatah

The specific teaching that *Abaye* attributes to *Rav Yosef* in *Shmuel*'s name is: *lo shanu ela tes-vav litvilasah* — the day 15 in the *mishna* does not mean day 15 from the last time she bled, but day 15 from her *tevilah*, from when she immersed in the *mikvah*. This distinction is critical. A woman immersed at the *mikvah* after the night of the seventh day following the onset of bleeding. Day 15 from the *mikvah* is therefore day 22 from the last time she bled (*shehein chaf-beis lir'iyasah*), which falls squarely within the *niddah* period, not the *zivah* period. Conversely, day 15 from the actual onset of bleeding (*tes-vav lir'iyasah*) would place her in the middle of the *zivah* days — and for that reason, *lo kav'ah* — no fixed *veset* can be established for that day. This is precisely the ruling of *Shmuel* that *Rav Yosef* had claimed never to have heard: bleeding during the *zivah* period cannot constitute a *veset kavua*. *Abaye* demonstrates that *Rav Yosef* not only knew it but transmitted it himself.

## Rav Papa's Question

### Michush Mai Denichush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה מִדִּסְקַרְתָּא, מִקְבָּע לָא קָבְעָה, מֵיחָשׁ מַהוּ דְּנֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the ruling that a woman's menstrual cycle cannot be fixed during her days of *ziva*, **Rav Pappa said: I said** this ***halakha* before Rav Yehuda of Diskarta,** and I asked him for a clarification of the following matter: Granted, **she does not fix** a menstrual cycle during the days of *ziva*, and there is no need for three deviations to uproot her cycle; rather, it is uprooted by even one deviation. But **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **we should be concerned** that **she** might experience bleeding? In other words, if she normally experiences bleeding on a particular day during her days of *ziva*, must she avoid engaging in sexual intercourse with her husband on that day out of concern that she might emit blood?

*Rav Papa* reports that he presented this teaching before *Rav Yehuda of Diskarta* and posed the following question: granted that a woman cannot establish a *veset kavua* during the *zivah* days (*mikva lo kav'ah*), must she nevertheless be concerned about those days (*michush mai denichush lah*)? That is: even if the *zivah* period cannot produce a fixed *veset* with all the weight that entails, is it at least enough to require refraining from relations on a day within the *zivah* period on which she has been repeatedly bleeding? This is the distinction between a *veset kavua* — with its full halachic implications — and a *veset einah kavua*, a non-fixed anticipatory concern that still requires caution on any day that recent history suggests may be the onset of bleeding. *Rav Yehuda of Diskarta* was silent and offered no answer.

### Count From Actual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתִּיק וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵי. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: "נֶחְזֵי אֲנַן", הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה לְיוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר וְשִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda of Diskarta **was silent and did not say anything to** Rav Pappa. Therefore, **Rav Pappa said: Let us see** and try to resolve this **ourselves.** The mishna cited above states: If the woman **was accustomed to see** an emission of blood **on the fifteenth day, and she deviated** from her cycle **to** see an emission on the **twentieth day,** then on both **this** day **and that** day it is **prohibited** for her to engage in sexual intercourse.

*Rav Papa* then attempted to resolve the question on his own from the same *mishna* discussed above. He reasoned as follows. The *mishna* speaks of a woman who regularly saw on day 22 from her *mikvah* (which the *Gemara* has identified as the corrected reading of "day 15" in the *mishna*), and who then switched to day 27. In the following month, both day 22 and day 27 are forbidden. Now, day 22 in the next month — if counted not from the last time she actually bled (day 27) but from when she *would have* bled based on the old pattern (day 22) — falls within the *zivah* period. And yet the *mishna* says she must be concerned about it. This, *Rav Papa* argued, demonstrates that even within the *zivah* period there is at least a *choshesh* — a concern sufficient to require restraint — even absent a formal *veset kavua*. Moreover, his reading assumed that the counting of "day 22" is done from the virtual prior day 22, not from the actual last bleeding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לָא שְׁנָא אֶלָּא חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לִטְבִילָתָהּ, שֶׁהֵן עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁנַיִם לִרְאִיָּיתָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to this mishna, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to a woman who normally experiences bleeding **fifteen** days **from her immersion, which are twenty-two** days **from her sighting** of menstrual blood, which means that there, she stands in her days of menstruation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְשִׁבְעָה; דְּכִי הָדְרִי וְאָתוּ עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּי — קָיְימָא לַהּ בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי זִיבָתָהּ, וְקָתָנֵי: זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין — אַלְמָא דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** when the mishna states that **she deviated** from her cycle and experienced bleeding on the twentieth day, it means she experienced bleeding twenty days from her immersion, i.e., **twenty-seven days from** her previous sighting, not twenty-two. This means **that when twenty-two days again elapse** from when she usually experiences bleeding, **she stands within** what is now the eleven **days of her *ziva*. And** the mishna **teaches** that both **this,** the twenty-second day, **and that,** the twenty-seventh day, **are prohibited,** despite the fact that the twenty-second day now stands during her days of *ziva*. **Evidently, we are concerned** for an emission of blood during the days of *ziva* if she is accustomed to experiencing bleeding on that day.

*Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua* objected: who says we count from the virtual date? Perhaps we count day 22 from the actual last time she bled, which was day 27. Twenty-two days from day 27 places her concern on day 49 — firmly within the *niddah* period, not the *zivah* period. There goes the proof. And it is furthermore more logical to count from actual bleeding, not from what should have happened.

### Hen Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָסָבַר רַב פָּפָּא: עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין — מֵעֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין מָנֵינַן, נִדָּה וּפִתְחָהּ — מֵעֶשְׂרִין וְשִׁבְעָה מָנֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And Rav Pappa holds** that **we count twenty-two** days of her menstrual cycle **from twenty-two** days, i.e., from when she usually begins to menstruate, whereas **we count the beginning** of the days of **menstruation from** day **twenty-seven,** when she actually experiences bleeding. Consequently, the twenty-second day of her normal menstrual cycle falls during the days of *ziva*, according to the actual day of menstruation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין נָמֵי מֵעֶשְׂרִין וְשִׁבְעָה מָנֵינַן, דְּכִי הָדְרִי וְאָתוּ עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין — קָיְימָא לַהּ בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי נִדּוּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa: From where** do you know this is the correct reckoning of her days? **Perhaps one also counts** those **twenty-two** days **from** day **twenty-seven,** such **that when twenty-two days again arrive** from day twenty-seven, **she stands within her days of menstruation.** Accordingly, there is no proof from the mishna with regard to a sighting during the days of *ziva*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא; דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, הַאי תַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא דְּרָמְיָא יוֹמָא וְכָבְשָׁה יוֹמָא, וְרָמְיָא יוֹמָא וְכָבְשָׁה יוֹמָא, וְכָבְשָׁה תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי וְרָמְיָא חַד יוֹמָא,
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, adds: **And so too, it is reasonable** that the twenty-two days are counted from when she actually experiences bleeding. **As if you do not say so,** then consider the case of **this chicken that** normally **lays** an egg on one **day and withholds** an egg the next **day, and lays** an egg on the third **day and withholds** an egg on the fourth **day. And** the chicken deviated from its routine, so that after laying eggs on the first day and third day, it **withheld** an egg for **two days and** then **laid** an egg on **one day,** i.e., on the sixth day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָדְרָה נָקְטָה — כְּדִלְקַמֵּיהּ נָקְטָה, אוֹ כִּדְמֵעִיקָּרָא נָקְטָה? עַל כׇּרְחָךְ כְּדִלְקַמֵּיהּ נָקְטָה.
>
> **Translation:** When this chicken **again takes hold** of its previous routine and starts laying an egg on one day and withholding an egg on the next, does **it take hold of** the order of the routine **ahead of it,** i.e., will it withhold an egg on the next day, **or** does **it take hold** of its routine **as it** was **from the outset,** so that it will lay an egg on the seventh day, as if there had been no deviation? **Perforce it takes hold of** the order of the routine **ahead of it.** Likewise, a woman who deviated from her normal menstrual cycle counts the days of her cycle according to the order of the cycle ahead of her, i.e., from the time that she experiences bleeding.

To demonstrate that we count from actual events, the *Gemara* introduces a striking analogy. Consider a hen that lays an egg every other day. Suppose she lays on days one, three, five, seven — odd days. Then, at some point, she goes two days without laying: she was expected to lay on day seven, but she withheld on both day six and day seven. Then on day eight she lays. When will she lay next — on day nine or day ten? Clearly on day ten: we do not say she will lay on day nine because that is nine days from the last expected laying of day seven. She laid most recently on day eight, and we count from there. The cycle resets from actual occurrence, not from expectation. *Ki hadra nakta l'tamei nakta* — when she returns to laying, she does so based on what she most recently did. So too with a woman's *veset*: we count from the last actual bleeding, not from when she theoretically should have bled.

## Reish Lakish & R. Yochan…

### Reish Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי זִיבָתָהּ, וְאֵין אִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי נִדּוּתָהּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי נִדּוּתָהּ — הֵיכִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: **But** if so, a question arises with regard to **that which Reish Lakish said: A woman fixes** a menstrual **cycle for herself during the days of her *ziva*, but a woman does not fix** a menstrual **cycle for herself during the days of her menstruation,** i.e., when she is already a menstruating woman. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A woman fixes** a menstrual **cycle for herself during the days of her menstruation.** One could ask: **What are the circumstances** of this dispute? Rabbi Yoḥanan cannot be referring to a case where all her sightings occurred while she was a menstruating woman, as everyone agrees that a woman's menstrual cycle is not fixed in such a situation (see 11a).

The *Gemara* now introduces a debate between *Reish Lakish* and *Rabbi Yochanan* that bears on the question of *veset* during the *zivah* period. *Reish Lakish* holds that a woman can establish a *veset* during her *zivah* days — this directly contradicts what *Shmuel* had said and what the *Gemara* has been assuming — but she cannot establish a *veset* during the *niddah* period itself. By "during the *niddah* period itself" he means not simply during the seven days following onset of bleeding, but during the *niddah* days proper: if she bled on day one and became a *niddah*, can a separate bleeding on day five of that same *niddah* period — when she had stopped and restarted — count toward establishing a *veset*? *Reish Lakish* says no: it is all a continuation of the same *niddah* episode.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and holds that a woman can even establish a *veset* during her *niddah* period.

### Rosh Chodesh Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו כְּגוֹן דַּחֲזַאי רֵישׁ יַרְחָא, וְחַמְשָׁא בְּיַרְחָא, וְרֵישׁ יַרְחָא, וְחַמְשָׁא בְּיַרְחָא, וְהַשְׁתָּא חֲזַאי בְּחַמְשָׁא בְּיַרְחָא, וּבְרֵישׁ יַרְחָא לָא חֲזַאי,
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **is it not** referring to a case **where she saw** blood on the **first of the month; and** then again on **the fifth of** that same **month,** when she was a menstruating woman; **and** subsequently she saw blood on the **first of the** next **month and** then again on **the fifth of** that **month; and now** in the third month she **saw** blood **on the fifth of the month but on the first of the month she did not see** blood?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָאָמַר: אִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי נִדּוּתָהּ, אַלְמָא מֵרֵישׁ יַרְחָא מָנֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa concludes: **And** it is with regard to this case that Rabbi Yoḥanan **says: A woman fixes** a menstrual **cycle for herself during the days of her menstruation.** Although she was not actually a menstruating woman before she experienced bleeding on the fifth day of the third month, this is nevertheless considered a sighting during her days of menstruation. **Evidently, one counts** her menstrual cycle **from the first of the month,** despite the fact that she did not actually experience bleeding. Likewise, with regard to the case involving twenty-two days, one counts from when she generally experiences bleeding, not from the day she emitted blood in practice.

The *Gemara* asks for the practical case of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position. The example offered: she saw on *Rosh Chodesh*, on *Rosh Chodesh*, and then on the 25th of the following month and on *Rosh Chodesh*. So she has two instances of *Rosh Chodesh* and three instances of the fifth, with the third fifth coming after the bleeding that began five days earlier on the 25th. According to *Rabbi Yochanan*, if we can use that day five — which falls during the *niddah* period — as a valid data point, then she now has three fives, which suffices to establish a *veset kavua* for the fifth of the month. And since she has seen only twice on *Rosh Chodesh*, not three times, the one instance cancels and she no longer needs to be concerned about *Rosh Chodesh*. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position thus permits using a bleeding that occurs within the *niddah* period to help constitute a *veset*.

### Tosefet Hi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָסָבַר רַב פָּפָּא: עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין — מֵעֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין מָנֵינַן, נִדָּה וּפִתְחָהּ — מֵעֶשְׂרִין וְשִׁבְעָה מָנֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And Rav Pappa holds** that **we count twenty-two** days of her menstrual cycle **from twenty-two** days, i.e., from when she usually begins to menstruate, whereas **we count the beginning** of the days of **menstruation from** day **twenty-seven,** when she actually experiences bleeding. Consequently, the twenty-second day of her normal menstrual cycle falls during the days of *ziva*, according to the actual day of menstruation.

*Rav Papa* attempted to use this case to prove his earlier point — that we can count from a virtual day rather than from an actual one. His reasoning was that in the *Rabbi Yochanan* case, the fifth of the third month is used even though there was no independent day-one that month; we treated day five as if there had been. The *Gemara* rejects this reading, clarifying what *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position actually is: *hachi amar Rabbi Yochanan* — the case is not one where we imagined a day one. Rather, she bled on *Rosh Chodesh*, *Rosh Chodesh*, the 25th of the month, and then *Rosh Chodesh* again. Given that she has seen on *Rosh Chodesh* twice previously, we are willing to treat the bleeding that began on the 25th as *tosefet* — a precursor or extension — to the *Rosh Chodesh* bleeding, and use the day-one onset as a valid data point for the pattern. The last *Rosh Chodesh* is usable because of the prior pattern, not because we imagined bleeding that did not occur. This is counting from actual bleeding, not from virtual expectation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן דַּחֲזַאי רֵישׁ יַרְחָא, וְרֵישׁ יַרְחָא, וְעֶשְׂרִין וְחַמְשָׁה בְּיַרְחָא, וְרֵישׁ יַרְחָא, דְּאָמְרִינַן דָּמֵי יַתִּירִי הוּא דְּאִתּוֹסַפוּ בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, **said to** Rav Pappa: These are **not** the circumstances of the dispute. Rather, **this** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The dispute is referring to a case **where she saw** blood on the **first of the month; and** then again on the **first of the** next **month, and** then again on **the twenty-fifth** day **of** that **month; and** again on the **first of the** next **month,** which occurs during her days of menstruation. Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that although she is in her days of menstruation, this sighting on the first of the third month serves to fix her menstrual cycle, **as we say** with regard to the sighting on the twenty-fifth day of the previous month that **it is extra blood that gathered inside her.** Therefore, it does not negate her regular cycle.

### Ravin's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין וְכֹל נָחוֹתֵי יַמָּא, אַמְרוּהָ כְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And likewise, when Ravin and all** the **sea-farers came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia and transmitted statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan, **they said** this statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua.**

When *Ravin* and those who traveled from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel came, they reported that *Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua* had stated the same principle: we count from when she actually bled, not from when she theoretically should have bled. This confirms the rejection of *Rav Papa*'s proof and upholds the hen analogy as the operative logic.

## Three Possibilities

### Is Veset Kavua

We are thus left without a definitive answer to *Rav Papa*'s original question. The first possibility is that a cycle of fewer than nineteen days — one that falls within the *zivah* days — can in fact establish a *veset kavua* with full halachic weight. This is the position of *Reish Lakish*.

### Must Be Concerned

The second possibility is that such a cycle cannot constitute a *veset kavua*, but there remains at least a *chosheish* — a concern — sufficient to require restraint on those days. This was what *Rav Papa* sought to establish, but could not demonstrate from the *mishna*.

### No Concern

The third possibility is that there is no concern whatsoever; a cycle of fewer than nineteen days is simply irrelevant for *halachic* purposes and requires neither a presumption of fixed period nor even a precautionary restraint. The *Gemara* leaves all three possibilities unresolved. And given the contemporary confluence of *niddah* and *zivah* law into a single unified practice, the theoretical distinction between the *niddah* period and the *zivah* period is largely without practical consequence in any case — which likely renders the question moot for applied *halacha*, even before we reach a determination.
