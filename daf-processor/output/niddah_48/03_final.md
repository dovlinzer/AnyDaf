# Niddah 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stages of Development

### Na'arah & Bogeret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' בַּת עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיאָה שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — תָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁהִיא בַּת עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה, וְהִיא אַיְילוֹנִית — לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A girl twelve years and one day old who grew two pubic hairs is classified as a young woman. Six months later, she becomes a grown woman. But a woman who is **twenty years old who did not grow two** pubic **hairs** and was never classified as a young woman **shall bring proof that she is twenty years old, and** from that point forward **she** assumes the status of **a sexually underdeveloped woman [*ailonit*],** who is incapable of bearing children. If she married and her husband died childless, **she neither performs *ḥalitza* nor does she enter into levirate marriage,** as the mitzva of levirate marriage applies only to a woman capable of conceiving a child. An *ailonit* is excluded from that mitzva.

The *daf* we take up today is 48b, picking up in the middle of an extended discussion about the stages of physical and halakhic development in both boys and girls. The preceding *mishnayos* of this *perek* addressed the binding force of vows made by a minor approaching adulthood — the *perek* known as *Mufla Smuch l'Ish* — but the *mishnayos* that concern us now pivot to the critical question of when a girl formally transitions between halakhic stages of maturity. A girl who is twelve years and a day old and has produced two pubic hairs is a *na'arah*; when she proceeds to the next stage of physical development associated with puberty, she becomes a *bogeret*. The *Mishnah* went into considerable detail delineating the precise physical signs marking that later transition, and we will return to those signs as the chapter unfolds. Other *Gemaras* suggest that the transition is simply six months after the two pubic hairs appear following the twelfth birthday, but the *Mishnah* here treats the physical signs themselves as independently significant.

### Ailonit Definition

The final *mishnah* of the *perek* addresses what happens when normal physical development does not occur. If a woman is twenty years old and still has not produced two pubic hairs — *bat esrim shana shelo hevia shtei se'arot* — she is, for all her chronological age and emotional maturity, halakhically still a minor, because it is physical development, not the passage of time alone, that determines her status. If she can establish that she has reached twenty years of age without this sign, *tavi ra'ya she'hi bat esrim shana v'hi aylonit*, she is declared an *aylonit*. The *Gemara* offers an etymology connecting the term to *ayal*, a ram, suggesting a kind of male characteristic; in any case, what is clear is that she has not developed as a female normally would. The consequence is *lo choletzet v'lo mityabemet*: she does not perform *chalitza* and is not subject to *yibum*, because the obligation of *yibum* presupposes the capacity for childbearing, and an *aylonit* is presumed sterile. Though the *Mishnah* does not make this explicit, what emerges from other contexts — particularly *Yevamot* — is that she is also now considered an adult, a sterile adult who never passed through puberty. The *Mishnah*'s stated focus is on her sterility and its implications for *yibum*, not on the broader question of adulthood, but the latter follows as a necessary corollary.

### Saris Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יָבִיאוּ רְאָיָה שֶׁהוּא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה, וְהוּא סָרִיס, לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a man who is **twenty years old who did not grow two** pubic **hairs, they shall bring proof that he is twenty years old and he** assumes the status of **a sexually underdeveloped man [*saris*],** who is excluded from the mitzva of levirate marriage. Therefore, if his married brother dies childless, **he neither performs *ḥalitza* nor enters into levirate marriage** with his *yevama*.

The parallel case for a male: *ben esrim shana shelo hevi shtei se'arot*, a young man of twenty years who has not produced two pubic hairs. Once he can establish his age — *yavi ra'ya she'hu ben esrim shana v'hu saris* — he is declared a *saris*, a eunuch in the halakhic sense, meaning he is sterile. He likewise does not perform *chalitza* and is not obligated in *yibum* — *lo choletz v'lo meyabem* — because the entire institution of *yibum* is premised on the ability of the surviving brother to provide children for the deceased brother's wife. The *Mishnah* here too emphasizes the sterility and its implications for *yibum*; it does not explicitly declare him an adult, a point to which we will return.

### Sterility & Marriage

One might ask whether a marriage to a person later discovered to be an *aylonit* would be annulled as a *mekach ta'ut*, a transaction made in error. This is explicitly discussed in *Ketubot*, though the precise scope of that discussion requires care: the question is whether the *ketubah* obligation is voided — on the grounds that no reasonable husband would have accepted that liability under these circumstances — or whether the *kiddushin* itself is *batel*. The two positions may reflect a debate between *Rashi* and *Tosafot*. The *Mishnah*'s ruling that the *saris* does not perform *yibum* is understood by *Tosafot* to apply even to a minor, since *yibum* is inherently bound up with physical capacity; the accent of the *Mishnah* is thus squarely on fertility status, not on the age-of-majority determination.

## Physical Maturity Signs

### Nine vs. Twenty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' וּרְמִינְהִי: אֶחָד לִי בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, וְאֶחָד לִי בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a sexually underdeveloped man does not enter into levirate marriage with the widow of his childless brother. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another mishna (*Yevamot* 96b): A boy who is **nine years and one day old,** who has not developed two hairs, **and** a man who is **twenty years old who has not grown two hairs, are one and the same to me** with regard to levirate marriage, in that if they engaged in intercourse with the widow of their childless brother, this levirate marriage is partially effective, to the extent that this woman requires both a bill of divorce and *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* now raises a pointed challenge. A *baraita* teaches that there is the same *halakha* for a nine-year-old boy and a day and a twenty-year-old boy who has not produced two pubic hairs — *echad ben tesha shanim v'yom echad v'echad ben esrim shelo hevi shtei se'arot*. A nine-year-old and a day is assumed capable of a sexual act but is not yet an adult. The *baraita* places the twenty-year-old who lacks pubic hairs in precisely the same category: the act of *yibum* he performs is binding in the way an act of intercourse by a minor is binding — it obligates a *get* and *miun* to dissolve — but he is not yet considered a full adult. This appears to contradict our *Mishnah*, which states that at twenty years of age without pubic hairs, he is a *saris* and therefore past the threshold at which *yibum* applies at all. If he is truly a *saris* by our *Mishnah*'s logic, his act of *yibum* should carry no force whatsoever.

### Rav Shmuel's Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק, אָמַר רַב: וְהוּא שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בּוֹ סִימָנֵי סָרִיס. אָמַר רָבָא: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״וְהוּא סָרִיס״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak says** that **Rav says** in explanation of the ruling of the mishna here: **And this** *halakha* applies only in a case **where he developed** physical **signs of a sexually underdeveloped man** (see *Yevamot* 80b) by the age of twenty. By contrast, the mishna in *Yevamot* is referring to one who did not develop signs of a sexually underdeveloped man. **Rava said:** The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: And he is a sexually underdeveloped man,** which indicates that he had already developed physical signs of such a condition. The Gemara concludes: **Conclude from it** that this is the correct interpretation of the mishna.

The resolution is offered by Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak in the name of Rav: *v'hu shenoldu bo simanei saris* — the *Mishnah*'s ruling that a twenty-year-old without pubic hairs is definitively a *saris* applies only when he also displays additional physical indicators of sterility. The *Gemara* in *Yevamot* elaborates what those indicators are; *Rashi* here mentions that they involve the character of one's perspiration, the distribution of hair elsewhere on the body, or unusually smooth skin — in short, any of the systemic signs that puberty has simply not engaged. If all that is absent is the pubic hair but no such additional signs are present, we do not yet seal his identity as a *saris* at age twenty; we wait longer before making that halakhic determination. This reading, moreover, is supported by the language of the *Mishnah* itself — *dikta nami*, as Rav Adda points out — since the *Mishnah* says *v'hu saris*, underscoring that the ruling applies specifically where he presents as a *saris* with corroborating evidence.

### Age Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי לֹא נוֹלְדוּ לוֹ סִימָנֵי סָרִיס, עַד כַּמָּה? תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: עַד רוֹב שְׁנוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the *halakha* itself: **And** in a case **where he does not develop the signs of a sexually underdeveloped man, until what** age is he considered a minor? **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: Until most of his years** have passed, i.e., until he reaches the age of thirty-five, halfway to seventy, which is the standard length of a person's life.

The question then becomes: if he lacks additional signs of sterility, how long do we wait before declaring him a *saris*? The answer given by Rabbi Chiya is: *ad rov shnosav*, until a majority of his years have elapsed. Drawing on the verse in *Tehillim* — *yemei shenosenu shivim shana*, the days of our years are seventy — the majority of seventy is thirty-five. So a man who enters his thirty-sixth year and still has no pubic hairs and no secondary indicators of being a *saris* is at that point declared a *saris*. The broader *halakhic* implication — which is not made explicit in this *Gemara* but is developed from other sources — is quite striking: throughout this entire period, as long as the pubic hairs have not appeared, the person remains a halakhic minor. A man who is twenty, thirty, or thirty-four years old and lacks this physical development is treated as a *katan* for purposes of contracts and obligations generally, not merely for the specific laws of *yibum*. The threshold of twenty (with additional signs) or thirty-five (without them) is thus not merely a fertility determination; it is the point at which he transitions to adult status despite the absence of normal development.

### Rabbi Chiya's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אָתוּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, אִי כְּחִישׁ — אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״אַבְרְיוּהּ״. אִי בָּרִיא — אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״אַכְחֲשׁוּהּ״, דְּהָנֵי סִימָנִים זִימְנִין דְּאָתוּ מֵחֲמַת כְּחִישׁוּתָא, זִימְנִין דְּאָתוּ מֵחֲמַת בְּרִיאוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When** people **would come before Rabbi Ḥiyya** to inquire about someone who had reached the age of puberty but had not yet developed the physical signs of maturity, **if** the person in question was **thin, he** would **say to them: Go** and **fatten him** up before we decide on his status. **If** he was **fat,** Rabbi Ḥiyya would **say to them: Go** and **make him thin. As these signs** indicating puberty **sometimes come due to thinness** and **sometimes they come due to fatness.** It is therefore possible that after his bodily shape is properly adjusted this individual will develop the signs indicating puberty and will not have the status of a sexually underdeveloped man.

When cases of this sort came before Rabbi Chiya, his practice was notably pragmatic. If the individual was thin and undernourished, *i kachish ihyu binsei*, Rabbi Chiya would prescribe that they be fattened up and brought to health — *amar lehu avruyei*. If they were healthy and well-fed, *i bari*, he would recommend reducing their diet — *amar lehu achshusha*. His reasoning: *de'hanei simanim zimnin de'asu machmas kachishusa zimnin de'asu machmas bri'usa* — these signs of pubic development sometimes come when a person gains weight and sometimes when they lose it, depending on the individual's constitution. Rabbi Chiya thus sought first to address the medical situation before issuing a ruling on the person's status.

## Me'et Le'et Doctrine

### Rava's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הִלְכְתָא בְּכוּלֵּי פִּרְקָא — מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: The *halakha* in** this **entire chapter** with regard to all of the places where an age is mentioned in years is that even when the phrase: And one day, is not explicitly noted, they are all calculated **from** the **time** of year of birth **until** that same **time** of year in the age specified.

Rava now offers a sweeping statement: *hilkhata b'khula pirka me'et le'et* — the law throughout this entire chapter follows the principle of *me'et le'et*, from time to time. Normally, the phrase *me'et le'et* is encountered in discussions about measuring periods of days, where the question is whether we use full halakhic calendar days or precise twenty-four-hour units. By *bris milah*, for example, we count the eighth day by the calendar: a child born on Sunday afternoon can have his *bris* on the following Sunday morning, without waiting a full eight times twenty-four hours. *Me'et le'et* would mean going by the precise birth hour. Here, however, we are measuring years, not hours, and the issue is different.

### Me'et Le'et Meaning

In the context of years and the ages of maturity discussed in this chapter, the question is not whether to count by calendar days or precise hours but whether it is sufficient to be *within* a given year or whether one must have fully completed that year and entered the next. Thus if a boy was born on the seventh of Adar, the question is whether it suffices that it is now the seventh of Adar thirteen years later — meaning he has entered his fourteenth year — or whether he must have completed the entire thirteenth year and actually begun the fourteenth.

### Rashi vs. Others

According to *Rashi*, *me'et le'et* here is being used in an unusual sense relative to its more common application. The typical usage contrasts counting by halakhic calendar units with counting by precise hours; the calendar-based counting allows a partial day at the beginning or end to count as a full day, while *me'et le'et* demands the full measure. Here, however, Rava applies the term to years, using it to insist that one must complete the full count of years — twelve full years, then be thirteen and a day; thirteen full years, then be fourteen and a day; twenty full years, then be twenty and a day — rather than treating the onset of the year as sufficient. This is a somewhat unusual extension of the term, but it is how the *Gemara* and *Rashi* understand Rava's ruling.

One might ask whether this principle is related to *miktzat hayom k'kulo*, the rule that a portion of a day counts as a whole day. There is indeed a relationship: *miktzat hayom k'kulo* operates within the normal mode of counting by halakhic days, where a small portion at the end counts as a complete unit. *Me'et le'et* in the sense used here, by contrast, insists on the full period and does not allow the partial unit at the beginning to serve as the whole.

### Ula's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוּלָּא אָמַר: דִּתְנַן תְּנַן, וּדְלָא תְּנַן לָא תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ulla said:** With regard to cases **where we learned** in the mishna a quantity of years including the phrase: And one day, **we learned** that the reference is to full years; **and** with regard to cases **where we did not learn** this phrase, i.e., where a quantity of years is mentioned in the mishna without the phrase: And one day, **we did not learn** it, and part of the final year is equivalent to a whole year.

Ula challenges Rava's position from the language of the *Mishnah* itself. Throughout the earlier *mishnayos* in this chapter, the ages are stated with the addition of *v'yom echad* — "and a day": twelve and a day, thirteen and a day, nine and a day. Our *Mishnah*, however, states simply "twenty years" without adding "and a day." Ula argues: *tenan tenan, d'lo tenan lo tenan* — where the *Mishnah* says it, the rule applies; where the *Mishnah* omits it, the rule does not. According to Ula, the deliberate omission of "and a day" from the twenty-year threshold signals that entering the twentieth year is sufficient; one need not complete twenty full years and begin the twenty-first. The difficulty this creates for Rav, who maintains that *me'et le'et* applies universally to all these ages, is that Rav must explain why the *Mishnah* omitted the phrase "and a day" if twenty and a day is indeed required.

## 30-Day Standard

### 30-Day Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תָּנֵי: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן כִּיפָּר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, שְׁנַת עֶשְׂרִים שֶׁיָּצְאוּ מִמֶּנָּה שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — הֲרֵי הִיא כִּשְׁנַת עֶשְׂרִים לְכׇל דְּבָרֶיהָ, וְכֵן הוֹרָה רַבִּי בְּלוֹד: שְׁנַת שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה שֶׁיָּצְאוּ מִמֶּנָּה שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — הֲרֵי הִיא כִּשְׁנַת שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה לְכׇל דְּבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer** with regard to the *halakhot* of a sexually underdeveloped man and a sexually underdeveloped woman: **The twentieth year, of which thirty days have passed,** i.e., from the age of nineteen and thirty days, **is** considered **like the twentieth year in all regards; and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **similarly issued** a practical **ruling** of *halakha* **in** the city of **Lod,** that **the eighteenth year of which thirty days have passed is** considered **like the eighteenth year in all regards.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita*: Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer — *shnat esrim she-yatze'u mimenu shloshim yom harei hi k'shnat esrim l'khol devarav* — the twentieth year, once thirty days have gone out of it, counts as the twentieth year for all purposes. Similarly, Rebbe taught in Lod: the eighteenth year, once thirty days have gone out of it — *harei hi k'shnat shmoneh esre l'khol devara* — counts as the eighteenth year. The divergence between twenty and eighteen simply reflects the *machlokes* we already saw in the *Mishnah*: *Beit Hillel* sets the threshold at twenty, *Beit Shammai* at eighteen, and accordingly Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar records twenty while Rebbe records eighteen. That part of the tension is easily resolved.

### Sheyatzu Interpretation

The more substantive question is what *sheyatze'u mimenu shloshim yom* means. The straightforward reading is that thirty days have elapsed since the beginning of the twentieth year — one is thirty days into the twentieth year. This makes sense by analogy to the principle that thirty days in a year can count as a full year, just as one day can count as a full month: a year is composed of months, so thirty days — a month — can stand for the year as a whole. The *Rambam*, however, reads the phrase differently: *sheyatze'u* means the thirty days have *gone out* of the year in the sense that only thirty days remain — one is eleven months into the twentieth year, with thirty days left before completing it. Either way, the operative principle is that one does not need to complete the full year and enter the twenty-first; some portion of the twentieth year suffices, whether that portion is counted from the beginning or reckoned toward the end.

This creates an obvious difficulty for Rav, who holds that *me'et le'et* requires entering the twenty-first year. According to the *braita* of Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar, one month into the twentieth year appears to be sufficient — which directly contradicts the requirement of *me'et le'et* as Rav understands it.

## Me'et Le'et Sources

### Sacrifices Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּקֳּדָשִׁים, שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים שֶׁבִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this matter **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** and Rav maintains in accordance with the opinion that full years are required. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Full years are required with regard to the period of one **year stated with regard to sacrificial** animals, e.g., "a lamb in its first year" (Leviticus 12:6); the one **year stated with regard to houses of walled cities,** during which one can redeem a house he has sold in a walled city (see Leviticus 25:29); and the **two years** stated **with regard to an ancestral field,** during which one cannot yet redeem an ancestral field he has sold (see Leviticus 25:15).

The *Gemara* broadens the discussion by examining how *me'et le'et* operates in other areas of *halakha*, as these serve as the evidentiary basis for the *braita*'s ruling. Regarding sacrifices, the verse states *kevesh ben shnato* — a lamb within its first year. The derivation, as stated by Abba bar Yaakov, is *shnato shelo* — its own year, not the year of the general calendar: *v'lo shana sheminyan ha'olam*. The animal's age is measured from its birthday, not from *Rosh Hashanah*. An animal born before *Rosh Hashanah* does not become "second year" simply because the new calendar year has arrived; we track its individual birth year.

### Land Sales Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים שֶׁבְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי, וְכֵן שֶׁבַּבֵּן וְשֶׁבַּבַּת — כּוּלָּן מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The **six years** stated **with regard to a Hebrew slave** (see Exodus 21:2) **and similarly** the years **of a son and of a daughter,** as will be explained, **all of** these are years **from** the **time** of the first year **until** that same **time** of year in the year specified, i.e., these periods are units of whole years instead of expiring on predetermined dates, as at the end of the calendar year. This supports the opinion of Rav that the years mentioned with regard to a sexually underdeveloped man or woman are full years.

Similarly in the laws of houses in walled cities, the verse states *ad tom shnat mimkaro* — until the end of the year of its sale. The derivation is *mimkaro shelo* — the year runs from the moment of the sale itself, not from the onset of the calendar year. If you sell a house in a walled city and wish to redeem it, the one-year window is measured from the date of sale, not from *Rosh Hashanah*. Likewise, for a field of inheritance — *sdeh achuza* — the two years before the right of redemption becomes available are measured from the date of sale. The verse says *u'vemispar shnei tvu'ot yimkar lach*, reckoning by years of grain, and the *Gemara* notes that it is theoretically possible to harvest three cycles of grain in two full years if the sale occurred at the right moment in the agricultural calendar; this underscores that we measure by full years from the event, not by calendar divisions or by number of harvests.

### Slave Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנַת הָאֲמוּרָה בַּקֳּדָשִׁים מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, אָמַר קְרָא: ״כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ״, שְׁנָתוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא שָׁנָה שֶׁל מִנְיַן עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the one **year stated with regard to sacrificial** animals is calculated by whole years and not by calendar years? **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said** that **the verse states: "A lamb in its first year"** (Leviticus 12:6). Since the verse does not state: A one-year-old lamb, it means **a year** based on calculation of **its** life, **and not a year of the universal count,** i.e., the calendar year.

For the Hebrew slave, the verse states *shesh shanim ya'avod u'va-shvi'it* — six years he shall work, and in the seventh he shall go free. The apparent problem is that if "the seventh" means the seventh calendrical year — i.e., once *Rosh Hashanah* of the seventh year has arrived — he might technically be in his seventh year even though only five and a fraction years have elapsed since his purchase. The ruling of *me'et le'et* here means: he works for six full years counted from the date of his acquisition, and he goes free in his seventh year counted from that same date — regardless of when the calendar year turns.

### Sons & Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבַּבֵּן וְשֶׁבַּבַּת, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: לְעִנְיַן עֲרָכִין, וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: לְפִרְקִין דְּ״יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* mentions the years **of a son and of a daughter** among those calculated as full years. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this stated? **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: With regard to valuations,** i.e., the age of a valuated male or female is calculated in whole years from the date of his birth, not by calendar years. **And Rav Yosef says:** The *halakha* is stated **with regard to** the matters taught in **our** fifth **chapter** in tractate *Nidda*, which is named after its opening words: ***Yotze Dofen*,** meaning an offspring born by caesarean section. In other words, when a mishna in this chapter mentions years, it means full years, even when it does not state this explicitly.

The most ambiguous phrase in the *braita* is *v'khein sheben v'khein shebas* — and similarly for sons and daughters. What halakhic domain does this refer to? Here the *Gemara* presents two positions. Rav Giddal in the name of Rav says: *le'inyan arakhin* — it refers to the laws of *erchin*, valuations. When one vows to pay the *erekh* of another person, the Torah provides a schedule of values keyed to the person's age. The determination of age — whether someone is under five, under twenty, or within various other brackets — follows *me'et le'et*: one does not enter the next age bracket simply by virtue of the calendar year, but only by completing the full number of years from one's own birthday. Rav Yosef, by contrast, says the *braita* refers to *le'pirkin d'Yotzei Dofen* — to our very chapter. For all the ages enumerated in this chapter — twelve and a day, thirteen and a day, twenty years — the requirement of *me'et le'et* means one must have completed the full number of years and entered the next year. This, Rav Yosef argues, is what supports Rav's ruling that our *Mishnah*'s "twenty years" means twenty and a day.

## 30-Day Standard (II)

### Rav's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי פְּלִיגַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, הוּא אָמַר חֲדָא, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא חֲדָא, וְלָא פְּלִיגִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Do you** and Rav **disagree** with regard to this matter? In other words, as you interpreted the mention of a son and a daughter differently, does each of you reject the halakhic application of the other? Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **No, he said one** matter, **and I said one** different matter, **but we do not disagree.**

Rav's resolution of the apparent contradiction between *me'et le'et* and the *braita* of Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar becomes clear: the *braita*'s phrase *v'khein sheben v'khein shebas* can sustain both readings — it applies both to *erchin* and to the chapter of *Yotzei Dofen* — and Rav himself explicitly ruled, *hilkhata*, that the entire chapter of *Yotzei Dofen* including the ages of twenty and eighteen follows *me'et le'et*, meaning one must enter the twenty-first or nineteenth year respectively.

## Me'et Le'et Sources (II)

### Abaye's Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ פְּלִיגִי, מַאן דְּאָמַר לַעֲרָכִין לָא אָמַר לְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, וְהָאָמַר רַב: הִלְכְתָא בְּכוּלֵּה פִּרְקִין מֵעֵת לְעֵת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And this, too, stands to reason, as if it enters your mind** that they **disagree** with regard to this matter, then **the one who said** full years are required **for** determining **valuations** does **not say** that full years are necessary **for** the *halakhot* of this chapter, ***Yotze Dofen*. But doesn't Rav say,** like Rav Yosef, that **the *halakha* in** this **entire chapter** with regard to all of the places where an age is mentioned in years is that even when the phrase: And one day, is not explicitly noted, they are all calculated **from** the **time** of year of birth **until** that same **time** of year in the age specified, not by calendar years?

Abaye steps in to clarify that Rav Giddal and Rav Yosef are not actually disputing one another: *hu amar hada v'ana amina hada v'lo paligna* — one says it applies to *erchin*, the other says it applies to *Yotzei Dofen*, and they do not contradict each other. The *braita* is broad enough to support both applications. The proof that they must not be arguing is this: if Rav Giddal genuinely held that *me'et le'et* applies only to *erchin* and not to *Yotzei Dofen*, then the entire evidentiary foundation for Rav's ruling on the chapter of *Yotzei Dofen* would collapse — and that cannot be, since Rav himself stated *hilkhata* explicitly for this chapter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לַעֲרָכִין, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר לְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן? דּוּמְיָא דְּהָנָךְ: מָה הָנָךְ דִּכְתִיבָן, אַף הָנֵי נָמֵי דִּכְתִיבָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is correct, then **according to** Rav, who is **the one who said** that full years are required **for** determining **valuations, what is the reason** that **he does not say** in his interpretation of the *baraita* that full years are required **for** the *halakhot* of ***Yotze Dofen*?** The Gemara answers: Rav maintains that the years of a son and of a daughter mentioned in the *baraita* are **similar to those** other cases mentioned in the *baraita*: **Just as those** numbers of years **are written** in the Torah, **so too these** years of a son and of a daughter are referring to matters **where** the years **are written** in the Torah, i.e., the years of valuations, unlike the topics discussed in *Yotze Dofen*, where the years are not mentioned explicitly in the Torah.

Furthermore, the specific language of the *braita* — *ben u'vat*, sons and daughters — is actually the decisive clue that it refers to *Yotzei Dofen* and not solely to *erchin*. The Torah's *parasha* of *erchin* uses the terminology of *zakhar* and *nekeva*, male and female; it is throughout the chapter of *Yotzei Dofen* that the language of *ben* and *bat* appears — *bat esrim shana*, *ben shlosh esrei shana*. The *braita*'s choice of words thus anchors it in this chapter. Those who read it as referring to *erchin* respond that the other cases in the *braita* — sacrifices, house sales, field redemptions, the Hebrew slave — are all grounded in explicit *pesukim* in the Torah, and *erchin* is likewise an explicit *parasha* in the Torah, whereas the age thresholds for *saris* and *aylonit* are not explicitly stated in the Torah. The rebuttal: if the phrase were intended for *erchin*, it would have used the Torah's own terminology of *zakhar* and *nekeva*, not the chapter's language of *ben* and *bat*.

## Established Halakha

### Rav Yitzchak's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן כִּיפָּר, שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar, who said** his ruling **in the name of Rabbi Eliezer,** that a man nineteen years and thirty days of age who has not developed signs of puberty is deemed a sexually underdeveloped man.

Rav Yitzchak bar Nachmani rules explicitly: *halacha k'Rabbi Yose ben Kippar she'amar mishum Rabbi Eliezer* — the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar, who says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer that thirty days into the twentieth year is sufficient.

### Rav Zeira's Quest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֶזְכֶּה וְאֶיסַּק וְאַגְמַר לִשְׁמַעְתָּא מִפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּמָרַהּ. כִּי סְלֵיק, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָמְרַתְּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן כִּיפָּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Zeira said:** May it be God's will that **I merit to ascend** to Eretz Yisrael, **and** that **I learn** this ***halakha* from the mouth of its Master,** Rabbi Elazar. **When** Rabbi Zeira eventually **ascended** to Eretz Yisrael **he** indeed **found Rabbi Elazar** and **said to him: Did you say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar?**

Rav Zeira, upon hearing this ruling attributed to Rav Elazar, expressed his aspiration: *ezkeh v'eisak v'egmar l'shmaisa mipumei d'marei* — may I merit to go up to *Eretz Yisrael* and learn this teaching directly from the one who taught it. Rav Elazar was in *Eretz Yisrael*, and Rav Zeira wished to verify the ruling at its source. When he did make his way there and found Rav Elazar, he asked directly: have you really said that the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar, that one month into the twentieth year truly suffices?

### Rav Elazar's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִסְתַּבְּרָא אֲמַרִי, מִדְּכוּלֵּיהּ פִּירְקִין תָּנֵי ״יוֹם אֶחָד״, וְהָכָא לָא קָתָנֵי — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּוָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar **said to him: I said** that it **stands to reason** that the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion. He elaborated: **From** the fact **that** throughout **the entire chapter** the Mishna **teaches:** And **one day, but here,** in the last mishna, **it does not teach** this phrase, one can **conclude from it** that it **stands to reason** that there is a reason for this discrepancy, **in accordance with the opinion of** Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar.

Rav Elazar's response was illuminating: *mistavra amri* — I said it makes sense that we rule this way. His reasoning was straightforward: throughout the entire chapter, every age is stated with the phrase *v'yom echad*, "and a day" — three and a day, nine and a day, twelve and a day, thirteen and a day. Our *Mishnah* conspicuously omits that phrase at the twenty-year threshold. This omission is not accidental; it signals that twenty and a day — i.e., entry into the twenty-first year — is not required. The *Mishnah* means somewhere within the twentieth year. And since Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar specifies thirty days into that year, it makes sense to adopt that as the operative standard. *Shma mina mistavra k'vasai* — the plain meaning of our *Mishnah* supports ruling like him.

### Chapter Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן.
>
> **Translation:**

*Hadran lach Yotzei Dofen* — we have reached the end of the chapter of *Yotzei Dofen*. It is worth pausing to note that the central concern of this section — the relationship between physical development and halakhic adulthood — is not fully elaborated within this *Gemara* itself. What emerges most explicitly is the bearing on *yibum* and sterility. That a person lacking pubic hairs remains a *katan* for general halakhic purposes — contracts, obligations, and the full range of halakhic responsibilities — up to age twenty with additional signs, or age thirty-five without them, is established in other contexts. The hint here comes from the *baraita* that places the twenty-year-old without pubic hairs in the same category as a nine-year-old, though one might argue that this comparison is particular to the laws of *yibum*, which are uniquely tied to physical and sexual maturity. Nevertheless, the established *halakha* in the codes is that physical maturity is a genuine criterion of halakhic adulthood — not a parallel track but the determining one.

## Adulthood & Transmission

### Physical Maturity

The discussion of adulthood as established through physical rather than chronological criteria raises a broader reflection. The halakhic system consistently treats the signs of puberty — specifically pubic hair — as the operative marker of full legal status. A person could be twenty, or for that matter thirty-four, years old and remain a *katan* if those physical signs have not appeared. Only once we are prepared to say that the absence of development is itself an established fact — either because additional signs of sterility are present at twenty, or because the person has reached thirty-five without any development — do we declare the transition to adult status. This is striking: the system decouples chronological age from legal maturity in a more thoroughgoing way than one might initially expect.

### Transmission Issues

The narrative of Rav Zeira's journey is, as one of the participants observed, quietly subversive. What was transmitted from Rav Elazar was the statement that *halakha* follows Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar. When Rav Zeira arrived and put the question directly to Rav Elazar, the answer was not "yes, that is the *halakha*" but rather "I said it makes sense to rule this way" — *mistavra amri*. The transmission had been slightly garbled; the distinction between a reasoned *psak* and an authoritative received ruling had been collapsed in transit. The *Gemara* preserves both the mistransmitted version and the correction, a practice that recurs elsewhere in the *Gemara* and raises the question of how reliable oral chains of transmission can be.

### Debates & Tradition

This naturally opens onto a larger issue: how do we account for the debates that run throughout the *Gemara* if the Oral Torah is a received tradition? Two schools of thought address this. One, grounded in the *mishnah* in *Eduyot* — *mishe'amdu talmidei Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel shelo shimshu kol tzorchan* — attributes the proliferation of dispute to students who were insufficiently immersed in their teachers' *Torah*: forgetfulness and imprecision of transmission led to divergent conclusions, and that is why the age of Shammai and Hillel saw disputes multiply as they had not before. The *Rambam*, however, rejected this account with some force, precisely because the Karaites weaponized it against Rabbanite tradition: if debates arise from garbled transmission, the whole enterprise is suspect. The *Rambam* therefore insisted that direct transmission — what we call *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — represents an extremely small fraction of *Torah she-be'al peh*, perhaps a fraction of a percent. The overwhelming bulk of the Oral Torah is derived by human intellectual reasoning from the written text. Because it is derived reasoning, disagreement among its practitioners is entirely expected, and the debates do not impugn the reliability of the tradition.

### Hilchata Meaning

This brings us to an interesting observation about the term *hilkhata* itself. When the *Gemara* states *hilkhata* X, what precisely does it mean? It might mean a conclusion arrived at by weighing the evidence and making a reasoned judgment — in which case it is structurally similar to Rav Elazar saying *mistavra amri*, "it makes sense to rule this way." But the two formulations are apparently distinct: Rav Zeira asked whether Rav Elazar had said *halacha k'Rabbi Yossi ben Kipar*, and Rav Elazar's clarification that he had said it *makes sense* rather than declaring it an authoritative *hilkhata* implies that these carry different force. One possibility is that *hilkhata* sometimes reflects not a fresh reasoned judgment but an established consensus of practice — what the *Gemara* in *Sanhedrin* refers to as *sugya d'alma*, the way people customarily act. In that reading, *hilkhata* is a report of settled practice rather than a ruling derived from evidence, and the contrast with *mistavra* is meaningful: one is an empirical report of how the community has proceeded, the other is a judgment about what the sources support.

## Upper vs. Lower Signs

### Upper Without Lower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' בָּא סִימָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּא הָעֶלְיוֹן — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַבֶּמֶת. בָּא הָעֶלְיוֹן עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּא הַתַּחְתּוֹן, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **the lower sign** of puberty, two pubic hairs, **appeared** in a young woman **before the upper** sign, development of the breasts, **appeared,** then she is an adult, as the pubic hairs are an unequivocal sign. Therefore, if her childless husband died and she came before her husband's brother [*yavam*] for levirate marriage, **she either performs the ritual through which a *yavam* frees a *yevama* of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*] or enters into levirate marriage** with her husband's brother. If **the upper** sign indicating puberty **appeared before the lower** sign **appeared,** i.e., the two pubic hairs are not visible, **although that** order of development is apparently **impossible, Rabbi Meir says:** In fact, it is possible for the breasts to develop before the growth of two pubic hairs, and the concern is that the two hairs did not grow and fall out but rather they never grew in the first place, which would mean that she remains a minor. Therefore, if her childless husband dies, **she neither performs *ḥalitza* nor does she enter into levirate marriage** with her husband's brother.

We turn now to the opening *mishnah* of the new chapter, which continues the discussion of physical maturity. The *mishnah* establishes an important distinction between the lower sign (*siman hatachton*) — pubic hair — and the upper sign (*siman ha'elyon*) — breast development. The case that concerns us most closely is: *ba siman ha'elyon ad shelo ba hatachton* — she has the upper signs of development without the lower ones, without pubic hair.

### Rabbi Meir's Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַבֶּמֶת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אֶפְשָׁר לַתַּחְתּוֹן לָבֹא עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּא הָעֶלְיוֹן, אֲבָל אִי אֶפְשָׁר לָעֶלְיוֹן לָבֹא עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּא הַתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** She has reached majority, and therefore if her childless husband dies, **she either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage** with her husband's brother. That is **due to** the fact **that** the Sages **said:** It is **possible for the lower** sign of puberty **to appear before the upper** sign **appears; but** it is **impossible for the upper** sign **to appear before the lower** sign **appears.**

Before reaching that case, the *mishnah* first establishes the unambiguous one: *ba siman hatachton* — if she has the pubic hair — everyone agrees she is an adult, capable of *chalitza* and *yibum*. The contested case is when she has the upper development without the lower. The *mishnah* states: *af al pi she'ee efshar* — even though it is impossible. Rabbi Meir rules *lo choletzet v'lo mityabemet* — she is still a minor, cannot perform *chalitza*, is not subject to *yibum*. The *Chakhamim* rule *ochelet o mityabemet* — she is an adult. Their reasoning: *ee efshar le'elyon lavo ad shelo ba hatachton* — it is impossible to have the upper development without the lower having preceded it. Therefore, if she presents with the upper signs, we conclude with certainty that she had the lower signs at some prior point, even if they are not currently visible.

### Tanner Stages

The Tanner stages of puberty development, which can be readily examined in medical literature, offer a striking parallel to this *mishnah*. The staged progression of both pubic development and breast development is described with considerable specificity, and the observation that certain thresholds of breast development tend to coincide with or follow the onset of pubic hair maps closely onto the *mishnah*'s claim that significant upper development does not occur without prior lower development. The *Chakhamim*'s position — that the upper is an indicator guaranteeing the lower has occurred — reflects a clinical observation about the sequencing of these developmental stages.

### Impossible Phrase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר. וַהֲלֹא בָּא! ״בָּא״ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, ״אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר״ לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis disagree with regard to the *halakha* when the upper sign of puberty appears before the lower sign. The mishna stated about that case: **Although that** order of development is **impossible.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** the mishna explicitly states that it is referring to a situation where the upper sign **did appear** before the lower one. The Gemara explains: When the mishna states that it **did appear,** that is **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who maintains that such an occurrence is possible, whereas when the mishna teaches: **Although that** order of development is **impossible,** it is referring **to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who contend that it is not possible.

The *Gemara* asks: why does the *mishnah* need the phrase *af al pi she'ee efshar*, "even though it is impossible"? Why not simply state the *halakha* — that if she has the upper without the lower, Rabbi Meir says she is a minor and the *Chakhamim* say she is an adult — and let us infer the reasoning? The answer is that without the phrase, we might have understood the dispute very differently. We would have thought that Rabbi Meir is not claiming the upper can occur without the lower in an absolute sense; rather, he is noting that there is a minority of women — say, five percent — in whom the upper development precedes the lower. Rabbi Meir, consistent with his general approach of being concerned even for minority cases (*chaish l'miuta*), holds that we cannot treat her as an adult without the direct evidence of pubic hair, because she might be of this minority. The *Chakhamim*, who do not concern themselves with minority cases (*la chaishi l'miuta*), say that the overwhelming majority of women have the lower before the upper, so the presence of upper development is sufficient evidence of prior lower development.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִתְנֵי: ״בָּא הָעֶלְיוֹן — רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַבֶּמֶת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַבֶּמֶת״, וַאֲנָא יָדַעְנָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** why is it necessary at all to mention the phrase: Although that order of development is impossible. Instead, let the mishna simply **teach:** If **the upper** sign of puberty **appeared, Rabbi Meir says: She neither performs *ḥalitza* nor does she enter into levirate marriage** with her husband's brother; **and the Rabbis say: She either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage** with her husband's brother. **And I would know** that the ruling of the Rabbis is **due to** the fact **that it is impossible** for the upper sign of puberty to appear before the lower sign.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָא תְּנָא ״אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: רוֹב נָשִׁים תַּחְתּוֹן אָתֵי בְּרֵישָׁא, וּמִיעוּט עֶלְיוֹן אָתֵי בְּרֵישָׁא; וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא, וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּלָא חָיְישִׁי לְמִיעוּטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If** the mishna **had not taught: Although that** order of development is **impossible, I would say** that with regard to **most women the lower** sign **appears first,** before the upper sign; **but** in **a minority of women, the upper** sign **appears first. And Rabbi Meir,** who rules that she is a minor who may not perform either *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage, conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, that one must be concerned for the minority.** In other words, Rabbi Meir takes into account the minority of women in whom the upper sign appears first, and therefore he considers her to be a minor. **And the Rabbis** likewise conform to their standard line of **reasoning that one need not be concerned for the minority,** and therefore they consider the young woman to have reached majority.

If this were the dispute, a critical implication would follow: the debate would be confined to cases where no examination has been conducted. But if an examination were carried out and pubic hair was definitively not found, the *Chakhamim* might then concede to Rabbi Meir — because now there is direct evidence placing her in the minority, and even the *Chakhamim* do not claim she could not be in that minority, only that we do not presume it. The phrase *af al pi she'ee efshar* forecloses that reading: the *Chakhamim* are not saying it is improbable — they are saying it is *impossible*. Therefore, even if a physical examination is conducted and no pubic hair is found, the conclusion is not that she is a minor but that the hairs were present and subsequently fell out. The halakhic ruling follows our knowledge of what is biologically possible, not what direct observation at a given moment reveals.

## Order of Development

### Yechezkel Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״שָׁדַיִם נָכוֹנוּ וּשְׂעָרֵךְ צִמֵּחַ״, אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, אִיפְּכָא מִבָּעֵי לֵיהּ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁ״שָּׁדַיִם נָכוֹנוּ״ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁ״שְּׂעָרֵךְ צִמֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who holds that it is possible for the upper sign to precede the lower, **that** accounts for that verse **which is written** with regard to a maturing young woman: "And you increased and grew up, and you came to excellent beauty; **your breasts were fashioned, and your hair was grown"** (Ezekiel 16:7). This verse mentions the upper sign before the lower sign. **But according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** the verse **should have** said the **reverse,** first stating "your hair has grown," and then stating "your breasts were fashioned." The Gemara answers that **this** is what the verse **is saying: Since your breasts were fashioned, it is known that your hair was** already **grown.**

The *Gemara* now brings a verse from *Yechezkel* to bear on the dispute. In a passage using female development as a metaphor for Israel's readiness for its covenantal relationship — and elsewhere as a metaphor for Israel's faithlessness — the verse states: *shadayim nakhonu u-se'arekh tzimach*, your breasts were set and your hair had sprouted forth. The order in the verse — breasts first, then hair — apparently supports Rabbi Meir's position that breast development can precede pubic hair, since that is the sequence the verse describes.

### Sages' Reading

The *Chakhamim* read the verse differently: *keivan she-shadayim nakhonu, yadu'a she-se'arekh tzimach* — once the breasts had set, it was already known that the hair had sprouted. The verse is not describing a temporal sequence in which the breasts came first; it is stating that the presence of the breast development was proof that the hair had already come. The verse mentions breasts first because it is the visible sign, but it presupposes the hair as the prior and underlying development.

### Rashi's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״בַּעְשׂוֹת מִמִּצְרַיִם דַּדַּיִךְ לְמַעַן שְׁדֵי נְעוּרָיִךְ״, אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, אִיפְּכָא מִבָּעֵי לֵיהּ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁבָּאוּ דַּדַּיִךְ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁבָּאוּ נְעוּרַיִךְ, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי ״שְׁדֵי״? כּוּלֵּהּ בְּדַדֵּי כְּתִיב, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** this accounts for **that which is written: "When they from Egypt bruised your breasts for the sprouting forth of your young womanhood"** (Ezekiel 23:21). This verse, which compares the Jewish people to a promiscuous girl, describes how, in her childhood, Egyptian men would play with her by pressing her breasts in order to engage in licentious sexual intercourse with her when she became a young woman, i.e., once the pubic hairs that indicate puberty developed. This indicates that it is possible for the upper sign to appear before the lower sign. **But according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** the verse **should have** said the **reverse.** The Gemara similarly answers that **this** is what the verse **is saying: Since your breasts appeared, it is known that** the pubic hairs, which are the sign of **your young womanhood, have** already **appeared.** Or **if you wish, say** instead: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **The sprouting forth of [*shedei*]** your young womanhood? It is not speaking of the lower sign, but rather of the upper sign, i.e., the **entire** verse **is written** in reference **to the breasts,** *shadayim* in Hebrew. **And this** is what **the Holy One, Blessed be He, is saying to the Jewish people:**

*Rashi* offers a somewhat different analysis. The verse, on *Rashi*'s reading, simply describes the sequence as it presents to the observer — first the breasts become visible, then the hair is noted — without implying anything about which necessarily precedes the other in a biological sense. This reading keeps the verse from being decisive proof for either position and focuses the dispute on the halakhic inference from one sign to the other, not on the biological sequence per se.

### Pubic Hair Definitive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא אַתַּחְתּוֹן סָמְכִינַן, מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **In any event, everyone,** both Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis, agrees that **we rely on the lower** sign. **From where do we** derive that we rely exclusively on the lower sign and do not require the appearance of the upper sign as well?

Underlying the entire discussion is an agreed premise: *de-khulei alma miha tata samkhinan* — everyone agrees that the operative *siman* is the lower one, the pubic hair. The entire question is only whether the upper development can serve as evidence *of* the lower. This universal agreement that pubic hair is the determinative sign is established from a *gezeira shava* or parallel inference from the verses concerning the equality of men and women in Torah law, as the next section addresses directly.

## Gender Equality Principle

### Torah Equality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אָמַר קְרָא ״אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה כִּי יַעֲשׂוּ מִכׇּל חַטֹּאת הָאָדָם״, הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ לְכׇל עֳונָשִׁין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. מָה אִישׁ בְּסִימָן אֶחָד, אַף אִשָּׁה בְּסִימָן אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, and likewise** a Sage of **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The verse states: "A man or woman, when they commit any of the sins of men"** (Numbers 5:6). **The Torah** thereby **rendered a woman equal to a man with regard to all punishments of the Torah. Just as a man** is considered to have reached majority based **on one sign,** i.e., the lower sign of two pubic hairs, **so too, a woman** reaches majority based **on one sign,** the lower sign.

The derivation is introduced by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, and similarly taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: the verse states *ish o isha ki ya'asu mi'kol chatot ha'adam* — a man or a woman who commits any of the sins of a person. The verse deliberately equates man and woman: *hishva ha-katuv isha le-ish le-khol onshim she-ba-Torah* — the Torah places woman on equal footing with man for all the punishments and prohibitions of the Torah. From this principle of equality, the *Gemara* derives a parallel rule for the signs of maturity.

### One Sign Each

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: אוֹ הַאי אוֹ הַאי! כְּאִישׁ — מָה אִישׁ תַּחְתּוֹן וְלֹא עֶלְיוֹן, אַף אִשָּׁה תַּחְתּוֹן וְלֹא עֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** one can **say** that although a woman's status, like that of a man, depends on one sign, in the case of a woman she reaches majority **either** through **this,** the lower sign, **or that,** the upper sign. From where is it derived that it must be specifically the lower sign? The Gemara answers: This is based on the same comparison of men and women. Her *halakha* is **like** that of **a man: Just as a man** reaches majority based on the **lower** sign **and not** the **upper** sign, as this is not applicable to him, **so too, a woman** reaches majority based on the **lower** sign **and not** the **upper** sign.

*Ma ish be-siman echad af isha be-siman echad* — just as a man's halakhic adulthood is determined by one sign, so too a woman's. The logic is that for males, pubic hair is the only significant physical indicator of puberty that is both clear and appropriately timed to mark the onset of adult legal status; the secondary characteristics, while real, are not discrete enough to serve as the operative *siman*. Therefore, the Torah's equation of man and woman implies that one sign governs each.

### Yavne Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק, כָּךְ הָיוּ מְפָרְשִׁין בְּיַבְנֶה, וְאָמְרוּ: כֵּיוָן שֶׁבָּא תַּחְתּוֹן — שׁוּב אֵין מַשְׁגִּיחִין עַל עֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this** *halakha*, that the lower sign is sufficient by itself for a woman, **is also taught** in a *baraita*. **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Tzadok, said: This** is **how** the Sages **would explain it in** the study hall of **Yavne. They said: Once** the **lower** sign has **appeared, we no longer concern ourselves with** the appearance of the **upper** sign in order to establish a woman's status, as she is considered to have reached her majority by virtue of the lower sign.

Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok transmits a ruling that was taught in *Yavne*: *kol she-ba-tachton shuv ein mashgichin al ha-elyon* — whenever the lower sign is present, we no longer concern ourselves with the upper. Once the pubic hair has appeared, the breast development adds nothing halakhically; the lower sign is conclusive in and of itself. Presumably, the inverse implication — that in the absence of the lower sign, the upper sign requires analysis — is exactly what the preceding *mishnah* addresses.

## Sociological Observations

### City Girls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בְּנוֹת כְּרַכִּים — תַּחְתּוֹן מְמַהֵר לָבֹא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְגִילוֹת בְּמֶרְחֲצָאוֹת; בְּנוֹת כְּפָרִים — עֶלְיוֹן מְמַהֵר לָבֹא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁטּוֹחֲנוֹת בְּרֵחַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to the appearance of signs indicating puberty in young women, that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In the case of young **women** who reside in **cities,** the **lower** sign **appears** more **quickly** than the upper sign, **because they frequent the bathhouses,** which stimulates the growth of the hair. By contrast, in the case of young **women** who reside in **villages,** the **upper** sign **appears** more **quickly** than the lower sign, **because they grind with mills,** which develops their breasts.

A *baraita* introduced by Rabban Gamliel turns to a striking set of sociological observations about variations in developmental patterns across different populations of girls. *Benot krachim* — city girls — *tachton memaher lavo*, the lower sign, the pubic hair, develops earlier. The reason offered is *mipnei she-ragilot be-merchatza'ot* — because they regularly use bathhouses, which apparently stimulates the body and accelerates the development of pubic hair.

### Village Girls

*Benot kfarim* — village girls — *elyon memaher lavo*, the upper development, the breast development, comes earlier. The reason: *mipnei she-tochanot be-reichayim* — they grind with the millstone, which develops the upper body. The physical labor of grinding grain apparently accelerates breast development relative to pubic development. These are, to be clear, observations and speculations rather than established halakhic conclusions; there is no halakhic ruling that turns on which population a given girl belongs to. Whether the observations accurately capture any real phenomenon, or whether they reflect received folk wisdom, the *Gemara* does not adjudicate.

### Wealth Disparities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בְּנוֹת עֲשִׁירִים — צַד יָמִין מְמַהֵר לָבֹא, שֶׁנִּישּׁוֹף בְּאֶפִּקְרִיסוּתָן. בָּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים — צַד שְׂמֹאל מְמַהֵר לָבֹא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשּׁוֹאֲבוֹת כַּדֵּי מַיִם עֲלֵיהֶן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנּוֹשְׂאִין אֲחֵיהֶן עַל גִּסְסֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** There are differences in the rate of development of the breasts. In the case of **the daughters of the wealthy,** the growth of the breast on the **right side arrives** more **quickly** than that of the left side, **as** it **rubs against their upper body cloaks,** which are worn by the wealthy. By contrast, in the case of **the daughters of the poor,** the growth of the breast on the **left side arrives** more **quickly** than that of the right side, **because they draw jugs of water on** that side. **And if you wish, say** instead that the left breast develops more quickly **because they carry their** younger **brothers on their** left **sides.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar offers a further and more granular observation: among *benot ashirim*, daughters of wealthy families, *tzad yamin memaher lavo* — the right side develops first, because *she-noshfot afkistoteihen* — they wear some kind of jewelry or garment on their right side and the contact stimulates development on that side. Among *benot aniyim*, daughters of poor families, *tzad smol memaher lavo* — the left side develops first, because *mipnei she-sho'avot kadei mayim aleihen* — they carry water jugs on their shoulders, and for whatever reason they carry these on the left, or alternatively because they carry their younger siblings on that side — *mipnei she-nose'ot acheihen al gisisiehen*. This observation has no halakhic implication whatsoever, which, as one participant noted, makes it in some ways the more striking passage: the attention to the physical development of young girls here is entirely speculative and observational rather than in service of any legal ruling.

### Left Before Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: צַד שְׂמֹאל קוֹדֵם לְצַד יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The growth of the breast on the **left side** arrives **before** that of the **right side.**

The *Gemara* closes this section with a general statement: *tzad smol kodem le-tzad yamin* — as a rule, the left side develops before the right.

### Rare Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אֲחִי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא קָדַם צַד שְׂמֹאל לְצַד יָמִין, חוּץ מֵאַחַת שֶׁהָיְתָה בִּשְׁכוּנָתִי, שֶׁקָּדַם צַד שְׂמֹאל לְצַד יָמִין, וְחָזַר לְאֵיתָנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina, son of the brother of Rabbi Yehoshua, says:** It **never** occurred that the growth of the breast on the **left side** arrived **before** that of the **right side, except for** the case of **one** young woman **who was in my neighborhood, as** the growth of the breast on her **left side** arrived **before** that of the **right side, and** later the right breast **returned to its normal condition** and developed normally.

Rabbi Chanina ben achi Rabbi Yehoshua demurs: *me'olam lo kadam tzad smol le-tzad yamin* — in my experience, I have never seen the left side develop before the right — *chutz me'achat she-hayta bi-shkunati* — except for one girl who was in my neighborhood, *she-kadam tzad smol le-tzad yamin*, in whom the left side did develop first. But, the text adds, *ve-chazra ve-tenava* — presumably in the end the right side caught up and the normal pattern reasserted itself. On that note of careful, empirical exception-making, the *daf* concludes.
