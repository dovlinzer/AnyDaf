# Kiddushin 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Witnesses Debate

### One vs. Two Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דִּתְנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּמְגוֹרָה שֶׁל דִּסְקִים בְּיַבְנֶה שֶׁהָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת בְּחֶזְקַת שְׁלֵימָה, וּמָדְדוּ וְנִמְצֵאת חֲסֵירָה – כׇּל טְהָרוֹת שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל גַּבָּהּ הָיָה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן מְטַהֵר, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְטַמֵּא. אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מִקְוֶה זֶה בְּחֶזְקַת שָׁלֵם הוּא עוֹמֵד, מִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְחַסְּרוֹ – אַל תְּחַסְּרֶנּוּ מִסָּפֵק. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אָדָם זֶה בְּחֶזְקַת טָמֵא הוּא עוֹמֵד, מִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְטַהֲרוֹ – אַל תְּטַהֲרֶנּוּ מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do I say** my claim that one does not rely on a lone witness in matters of forbidden relations? **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said: An incident** occurred **with regard to** a water **reservoir of Diskim in Yavne, which had the presumptive status of** being **complete,** i.e., they thought it contained forty *se'a*, the requisite amount for a ritual bath, **and they measured it** after a time **and it was found to be deficient,** as it contained less than that amount. With regard to **all** immersions of **ritual purification performed in** the reservoir before it was measured, **Rabbi Tarfon would render** them **ritually pure, and Rabbi Akiva** would **render** them **ritually impure.** The two Sages discussed the matter. **Rabbi Tarfon said: This ritual bath retained the presumptive status of** being **whole** throughout this period, and **you are coming to** declare it **deficient** in the past **out of uncertainty. Do not deem it deficient out of uncertainty. Rabbi Akiva said** in response: **This person** who immersed himself in that ritual bath **retained the presumptive status of** being **ritually impure** before he immersed. **You are coming to purify him out of uncertainty. Do not deem him ritually pure out of uncertainty.**

The sugya opens with the dispute between Abaye and Rava concerning the principle of *ein davar she-be'erva pahot mi-shenayim* — that matters of personal status require no fewer than two witnesses. The question is not merely about the formal act of *kiddushin* or *get*, where two witnesses are needed to constitute the act itself, but about the evidentiary standard required to establish that such an act occurred, or to determine personal status more broadly. Rava holds that two witnesses are required both to effectuate and to prove matters of personal status. Abaye disagrees, maintaining that one uncontested witness suffices for proof even in such matters.

The stakes of this dispute extend well beyond the narrow domain of marriage and divorce. The same standard of proof applies, for instance, to questions of whether a woman was taken captive — which would render her forbidden to her *kohen* husband — or whether a woman committed adultery, which would render her forbidden to her husband entirely.

### Mikvah Case Source

Rava introduces his proof from a *mishnah* (cited also in the *Tosefta*): *Amar Rabbi Shimon* — there was an incident involving a *megurah*, a cistern or body of water, in a place called Diskim (or, as Rashi notes, this may be a personal name rather than a place), situated in Yavneh. This *mikvah* had long stood with a *chezkat shelemah* — a presumption of having sufficient water. When it was eventually measured, it was found to be lacking the requisite forty *se'ah*.

The question that arises is: what is the status of all the purifications performed using this *mikvah* since the last time it was confirmed to be complete? The *mikvah* did not become deficient the very day after the prior measurement. So when did it lose its valid status, and what are the consequences for everything done in the interim?

### Mikvah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מָשָׁל לְעוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, שֶׁעֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מָשָׁל לְעוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בַּעַל מוּם, שֶׁעֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: אַתָּה דִּימִּיתוֹ לְבַעַל מוּם, וַאֲנִי דִּמִּיתִיו לְבֶן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ לְבֶן חֲלוּצָה, נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה. אִי דּוֹמֶה לְבֶן גְּרוּשָׁה וּלְבֶן חֲלוּצָה – נְדוּנֶנּוּ כְּבֶן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ כְּבֶן חֲלוּצָה, אִם דּוֹמֶה לְבַעַל מוּם – נְדוּנֶנּוּ כְּבַעַל מוּם. הִתְחִיל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לָדוּן: מִקְוֶה פְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד, וּבַעַל מוּם פְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בִּשְׁנַיִם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: מִקְוֶה פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, בַּעַל מוּם פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ מֵאֲחֵרִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: עֲקִיבָא! כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמְּךָ כְּפוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said** in response: There is **a parable** that illustrates this. A priest was **standing and sacrificing** offerings **on the altar, and it became known that he is the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*.** The *halakha* is **that his** earlier **service** before this discovery remains **valid. Rabbi Akiva said:** A more accurate **parable** is that of a priest who was **standing and sacrificing on the altar, and it became known that he is blemished.** In this case, the *halakha* is **that his** earlier **service** is **disqualified.** **Rabbi Tarfon said in response: You compared** the case of a ritual bath found to be deficient **to** that of **a blemished** priest, **whereas I compared it to** the case of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*. Let us see to which** case **it is similar. If** this case **is similar to** that of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*, let us treat it like** the case of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*; if it is similar to** the case of **a blemished** priest, **let us treat it like** that of **a blemished** priest. **Rabbi Akiva began to analyze** the matter: **The disqualification of a ritual bath** is **by** the testimony of **an individual,** as witnesses are not required to establish that a ritual bath is deficient, **and** likewise **the disqualification of a blemished** priest with regard to performing the Temple service **is** by the testimony of **an individual. And do not let** the *halakha* of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* prove** otherwise, **as his disqualification is by** the testimony of **two** witnesses. Two witnesses are required to testify about one's mother to disqualify him from performing the Temple service; one is insufficient. **Alternatively,** one can say: **The disqualification of a ritual bath is due to** the bath **itself,** and similarly, **the disqualification of a blemished** priest is **due to** the priest **himself. And do not let** the *halakha* of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* prove** otherwise, **as his disqualification is due to others,** i.e., through his mother. **Rabbi Tarfon said to him: Akiva, anyone who separates from you, it is as though he has separated from life** itself. Rabbi Tarfon was impressed by Rabbi Akiva's explanation and accepted it. This concludes the *baraita*.

Two *tanna'im* debate the question. Rabbi Tarfon rules leniently: *mikvah zeh be-hezkat shalem hu omed* — the *mikvah* stands with a presumption of being complete. He invokes the principle of *chazakah*: since the *mikvah* had a recognized valid status, one cannot retroactively undermine that status based on mere doubt about when it became deficient. The doubt does not override the *status quo* of the *mikvah* being *shalem*. This is the foundational logic of *chazakah*: we assume the prior status persists until we have definitive evidence that it changed, which means we push the change to the last possible moment — when the measurement was actually taken.

Rabbi Akiva, however, rules stringently. He too invokes *chazakah*, but from the opposite direction: *adam zeh be-hezkat tame hu omed* — the person who immersed had a *status quo* of being *tameh* before entering the *mikvah*. Just as one cannot arbitrarily strip away the *mikvah's* status based on doubt, one equally cannot strip away the person's prior *tumah* status based on doubt. The fundamental question thus becomes a collision of two competing *chazakot*: the *chazakah* of the *mikvah* as valid versus the *chazakah* of the person as *tameh*.

Rabbi Tarfon then introduces a *mashal*: consider a *kohen* who was standing and offering *korbanot*, and it subsequently came to light that his mother had been divorced before marrying his father, making him a *ben grushah* and hence a *challal*. In that case, his *avodah* performed prior to the discovery is declared *ksherah* — valid. Now, admittedly, once we have this information, we know retroactively that he was a *challal* all along. Logically, one might expect his past *avodah* to be invalidated. Yet the *halacha* is that it is not, and the *gemara* will derive this from a *pasuk*. Rabbi Tarfon is invoking this case analogically, perhaps also gesturing toward the practical and societal consequences of the alternative — if a *kohen* at age seventy discovered he was a *ben grushah*, are we to retroactively recall and invalidate everything he performed over the preceding decades? There is reason, policy and otherwise, to affirm the prior status.

### Comparative Arguments

Rabbi Akiva responds with a counter-analogy: consider a *kohen* who was offering *korbanot* and was subsequently discovered to have a *mum* — a bodily blemish. In that case, his *avodah* is *pesulah* — retroactively invalid. This is striking, because intuitively one might have thought the opposite: being a *ben grushah* or *ben chalutzah* is a more fundamental deficiency of status — it goes to the heart of one's *kehuna* — whereas a *ba'al mum* retains his *kedushat kehuna* in every other respect. Yet the *halacha* distinguishes sharply: the *ba'al mum* whose *avodah* is retroactively disqualified, and the *ben grushah* whose *avodah* is retroactively valid.

Rabbi Akiva's point is structural: these two cases generate different legal consequences, so one must ask which case the *mikvah* scenario more closely resembles. And he argues that the *mikvah* case resembles the *ba'al mum* case: just as by a *ba'al mum* a single witness suffices to establish the disqualification, similarly, the deficiency of the *mikvah* can be established by one person — the deficiency is, so to speak, *pesulah bi-gufo*, present in the body of the thing itself. By contrast, the disqualification of a *ben grushah* or *ben chalutzah* — *psulo me-acherim* — derives from others, namely the conduct of the parents, and requires two witnesses.

Rabbi Akiva then formalizes the comparison: *mikvah pasul bi-yachid u-va'al mum pasul bi-yachid* — the deficiency of the *mikvah* is established by one person and the *ba'al mum* is established by one person. But the *ben grushah* and *ben chalutzah* — *psulo bi-shnayim* — their disqualification requires two witnesses. This is precisely the reason the *gemara* introduced this entire passage: to prove that matters of *erva*, of personal status, require two witnesses. He additionally offers a second line of comparison: *mikvah pesulah be-gufah, ba'al mum pasul be-gufo* — the problem is inherent in the body of the *mikvah* or the person, whereas *ben grushah u-ven chalutzah* — *psulo me-acherim* — the problem originates externally, from what others did.

Rabbi Tarfon, unable to counter this reasoning, concedes with characteristic rhetorical flourish: *kol ha-poreish mimcha ke-poreish min ha-chayim* — anyone who separates from you separates from life itself.

### Contested Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקָא מַכְחֵishׁ לֵיהּ, מִי מְהֵימַן? אֶלָּא דְּשָׁתֵיק, וְדִכְוָתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה דְּשָׁתֵיק, וְקָתָנֵי: מִקְוֶה פְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד וּבַעַל מוּם פְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בִּשְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the topic at hand: **What are the circumstances** with regard to **this blemished** priest **whose disqualification is by** means of **an individual** witness? **If** the priest **denies his** claim, maintaining that he is not blemished, **is** a lone witness **deemed credible? Rather,** it must be **that** the priest remains **silent** and therefore is considered to have admitted to the accusation. **And similarly with regard to the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*,** it must also be referring **to** one who is **silent, and** yet the *baraita* **teaches: The disqualification of a ritual bath** is **by** the testimony of **an individual, and** likewise **the disqualification of a blemished** priest with regard to performing the Temple service **is** by the testimony of **an individual. And do not let** the *halakha* of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* prove** otherwise, **as his disqualification is by** the testimony of **two** witnesses. This indicates that if the person under scrutiny himself remains silent, one witness is insufficient to disqualify him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם דְּקָא מַכְחֵishׁ לֵיהּ, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: ׳אַמַּאי מְהֵימַן׳ – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁלַח אַחְוִי. וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי: מִקְוֶה פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ וּבַעַל מוּם פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ מֵאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** could have **said** in response to this proof: **Actually,** both cases deal **with** an individual **who denies** the witness's testimony, **and that which you said: Why is** the witness **deemed credible** when he says that this priest is blemished, it is referring to a case **where** the witness **said to him: Remove** your clothes and **show** us that you are not blemished. Since this is a matter that can be investigated, the witness is deemed credible, because if he were lying the priest could prove it. **And this** explanation **is** consistent with **that which the *baraita* teaches: The disqualification of a ritual bath is due to** the bath **itself,** and similarly, **the disqualification of a blemished** priest is **due to** the priest **himself,** as he himself can be examined. **And do not let** the *halakha* of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* prove** otherwise, **as his disqualification is due to others.**

The *gemara* then turns to interrogate the precise meaning of the *ba'al mum* case. It asks: *hai ba'al mum she-pesulah bi-yachid heichi dami?* — What exactly is the case where one witness suffices for a *ba'al mum*? If the *kohen* himself is contesting the claim — denying he has a blemish — would we really believe a single witness against him? Surely not. So presumably, says Rava, the case is where the *kohen* is not contesting the testimony — *de-shatik* — and one uncontested witness is believed. And yet, even in that scenario, by a *ben grushah* and *ben chalutzah* two witnesses are still required. This is Rava's proof: even uncontested testimony of a single witness is insufficient for personal status matters; two are always required.

Abaye responds directly — and this is precisely the counterargument that presents itself naturally. Abaye says: *le'olam de-ka machish lei* — actually, we are dealing with a contested case. The *kohen* is denying it. But why, then, do we believe the single witness? Because the witness can say to the *kohen*: *shlach achvei* — strip and show us. If you deny having a blemish, demonstrate it. The single witness is believed even over the *kohen's* objection because the claim is objectively verifiable — the *ba'al mum* is *pesulah be-gufo*, the deficiency is on the body itself, and the *kohen's* refusal to demonstrate substantiates the witness's account.

This is precisely why the comparison to *ben grushah* and *ben chalutzah* breaks down for Abaye. In that case, if the *kohen* contests a single witness's claim that he is a *ben grushah*, there is nothing to show, no demonstration possible. The status derives entirely from a historical fact about the parents — *psulo me-acherim* — and so a contested single witness cannot be believed, because there is no objective verification to demand. Hence, for contested testimony about personal status where there is nothing to demonstrate, two witnesses are required. But for Abaye, this is the reason for the two-witness requirement in the *ben grushah* case, not a general rule that personal status always requires two witnesses.

### Personal Status Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה דַּעֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה, מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיְתָה לּוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ אַחֲרָיו״ – בֵּין זֶרַע כָּשֵׁר וּבֵין זֶרַע פָּסוּל. אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״בָּרֵךְ ה׳ חֵילוֹ וּפֹעַל יָדָיו תִּרְצֶה״ – אֲפִילּוּ חוּלִּין שֶׁבּוֹ תִּרְצֶה. רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״וּבָאתָ אֶל הַכֹּהֵן אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם״, וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁאָדָם הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּיָמָיו? אֶלָּא זֶה כָּשֵׁר וְנִתְחַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the *halakha* of the *baraita*: **And from where do we** derive **that the service of the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* is valid** after the fact? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: As the verse states** with regard to Pinehas the priest: **"And it shall be for him and his seed after him** the covenant of an everlasting priesthood" (Numbers 25:13), which includes **both fit seed and unfit seed.** This teaches that the service of a priest is valid after the fact even if he was disqualified. **Shmuel's father said** that the proof text is **from here:** It states in the blessing of the tribe of Levi: **"Bless, Lord, his substance [*ḥeilo*], and accept the work of his hands"** (Deuteronomy 33:11). The word *ḥeilo* is expounded as including **even** the service of **his profane ones [*ḥullin*],** which God will **accept** after the fact. **Rabbi Yannai said:** The source is **from here,** a verse stated with regard to first fruits: **"And you shall come to the priest who shall be in those days"** (Deuteronomy 26:3). **But can it enter your mind that a person can come to a priest who is not** alive **in his days?** What then is the meaning of the phrase "in those days"? **Rather, this** is referring to **a fit** priest **who** later **became** established as **a *ḥalal*.** All his previous days of service are considered those of a fit priest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעַל מוּם דַּעֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לָכֵן אֱמֹר הִנְנִי נֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת בְּרִיתִי שָׁלוֹם״ – כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׁלֵם וְלֹא כְּשֶׁהוּא חָסֵר. וְהָא ״שָׁלוֹם״ כְּתִיב! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וָיו דְּשָׁלוֹם קְטִיעָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the *baraita*. **From where do we** derive **that the service of a blemished** priest **is** retroactively **invalid? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: As the verse states** with regard to Pinehas: **"Wherefore say: Behold, I give to him My covenant of peace [*shalom*]"** (Numbers 25:12), which means that he receives the covenant **when he is whole [*shalem*], but not when he is** blemished and **lacking** a limb. The Gemara comments: **But *shalom* is written,** rather than *shalem*. **Rav Naḥman says:** The letter ***vav*** in the word ***shalom* is severed.** According to tradition, this letter is written with a break in it, and therefore the word can be read as though the *vav* were missing.

The *gemara* does not definitively resolve the dispute at this juncture. Whether the *ba'al mum* case was contested or uncontested remains open — for if the *mum* is internal, there is nothing to demonstrate in any event, making it structurally more similar to an uncontested case, which would restore Rava's proof. The *gemara* then briefly addresses the underlying *halachic* sources for the distinction between *ben grushah* and *ba'al mum*. How do we know a *kohen* discovered to be a *ben grushah* does not have his past *avodah* invalidated? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel derives it from the *pasuk* concerning Pinchas: *ve-haytah lo u-le-zaro acharav* — the covenant of *kehuna* extends to his descendants, *bein zera kasher bein zera pasul*, whether the offspring is fit or disqualified. The father of Shmuel derives it from the verse *bareich Hashem cheilo* — bless his strength — reading *cheilo* as referring even to *chullin she-bo*, those whose sanctity may be impaired, and *u-po'al yadav tirtzeh* as indicating acceptance of his service through the moment of discovery.

Rabbi Yochanan offers yet another source: *u-vata el ha-kohen asher yihyeh ba-yamim ha-hem* — you shall come to the *kohen* who will be in those days. This teaches that even if it turns out the *kohen* before whom you stood was later discovered to be unfit, the interaction was valid — *zeh kasher ve-nit-chalal*, he was fit and became disqualified thereafter.

Regarding the *ba'al mum*, Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel derives from the continuation of the Pinchas passage: *lachen emor hinni noten lo et briti shalom* — the covenant of *shalom* applies only *ke-shehu shalem*, when he is whole and unblemished, *ve-lo ke-shehu chaser*, not when he is lacking. The word *shalom* in the *pasuk* is written with a broken *vav* — a *vav keti'a* — as Rav Nachman notes, a scribal tradition attested in *Sifrei Torah*, with various traditions as to the precise form. This textual irregularity is the basis for reading *shalom* as *shalem*, and thereby limiting the grant of validity to an unblemished *kohen*.

On the conceptual level, this distinction remains genuinely puzzling. A *challal* has no *kedushat kehuna* at all — he is exempt from all its prohibitions and obligations — while a *ba'al mum* retains his sanctity fully, merely being unable to perform the *avodah*. Yet it is the *challal* whose prior *avodah* is retroactively valid and the *ba'al mum* whose prior *avodah* is retroactively disqualified. One possible line of reasoning, parallel to a point the *gemara* makes in another context, is that the *ba'al mum's* disqualification was present and objectively discoverable — it was there to be seen — whereas the *challal's* disqualification was a matter of hidden historical fact. That which was discoverable and observable carries a greater legal charge. But this remains difficult.

The practical ruling follows Rava — we generally rule *ein davar she-be'erva pahot mi-shenayim* even as a standard of proof. This has significant implications beyond invalidating personal status: a woman seeking to prove she is divorced, or a convert seeking to prove the validity of his conversion, faces the same evidentiary threshold. These remain live questions wherever status must be affirmatively established.

## Genealogical Status

### Child Follows Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר. וְאֵיזֶה זוֹ – זוֹ כֹּהֶנֶת, לְוִיָּה, וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית שֶׁנִּשְּׂאוּ לְכֹהֵן, לֵוִי, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a principle with regard to the *halakhot* of lineage: **Any case where there is betrothal,** i.e., where the betrothal takes effect, **and** the marriage involves **no transgression** by Torah law, the lineage of **the offspring follows the male,** his father. **And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? For example, **this** is the case with regard to **the daughter of a priest;** or **the daughter of a Levite; or the daughter of an Israelite, who married a priest, a Levite, or an Israelite.** In all these cases the child's lineage is established by his father's family.

The *mishnah* opens with a tripartite framework governing *yichus* — genealogical status — based on the nature of the union between the parents.

The first principle: *kol makom she-yesh kiddushin ve-ein aveira, ha-velad holech achar ha-zachar* — wherever *kiddushin* is binding and there is no transgression, the child's status follows the father. The *mishnah* illustrates this with the most basic case: whether the mother is a *kohenet*, a *leviyah*, or a *yisraelit*, and the father is a *kohen*, *levi*, or *yisrael*, the child inherits the father's tribal and priestly status. A *kohen* father produces a *kohen* child regardless of the mother's background. This is the standard, unexceptional case of a valid and permitted marriage.

### Child Follows Disqualified Party

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְיֵשׁ עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם. וְאֵיזֶה זוֹ – זוֹ אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר וּלְנָתִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And any case where there is** a valid **betrothal and** yet **there is a transgression, the offspring follows the flawed** parent. **And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? For example, **this** is the case of **a widow** who is married **to a High Priest,** or **a divorced woman or a *ḥalutza*** who is married **to a common priest,** or **a *mamzeret* or a Gibeonite woman** who is married **to an Israelite,** or **an Israelite woman** who is married **to a *mamzer* or to a Gibeonite.** In these situations the child inherits the status of the blemished parent.

The second principle: *kol makom she-yesh kiddushin ve-yesh aveira, ha-velad holech achar ha-pagum* — wherever *kiddushin* is binding but the union involves transgression, the child follows the disqualified party. The examples given are the forbidden unions of *kohanim*: an *almanah* with a *kohen gadol*, or a *grushah* or *chalutzah* with a *kohen hedyot*. The child born of such a union does not himself become a *kohen gadol* or a divorcée — rather, the child becomes a *challal*, stripped of the full sanctity of *kehuna*, which is the operative consequence of a forbidden *kohen* union. The conceptual principle is that the stigma of the forbidden relationship attaches to the offspring, regardless of whether the disqualified party is the father or the mother.

### Mamzer Status

The third principle extends this logic to *mamzer* status: if a woman who is a *mamzeret* marries a *yisrael*, or a *yisrael* woman marries a *mamzer*, the child is a *mamzer*. The *kiddushin* in such unions is binding — since the prohibition is an *isur lav* and not an *isur karet* or punishable by death — but the child inherits the disqualified status of whichever parent bears it. This applies symmetrically regardless of whether the *mamzer* is the father or the mother.

### Matrilineal Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ עָלָיו קִידּוּשִׁין, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהּ עַל אֲחֵרִים קִידּוּשִׁין – הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. וְאֵיזֶה זֶה – זֶה הַבָּא עַל אַחַת מִכׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. וְכׇל מִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ לֹא עָלָיו וְלֹא עַל אֲחֵרִים קִידּוּשִׁין – הַוָּלָד כְּמוֹתָהּ. וְאֵיזֶה זֶה – זֶה וְלַד שִׁפְחָה וְנׇכְרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in **any** case **where** a woman **cannot** join in **betrothal with** a particular man, as the betrothal does not take effect, **but she can** join in **betrothal with others,** i.e., the woman is considered a member of the Jewish people and can marry other Jews, in these cases **the offspring is a *mamzer*. And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? **This is one who engages in intercourse with any one of those with whom relations are forbidden that are** written **in the Torah. And** in **any** case **where** a woman **cannot** join in **betrothal with him or with others, the offspring is like her.** He is not considered his father's son at all, but has the same status as his mother. **And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? **This is the offspring of** a Canaanite **maidservant or a gentile woman,** as her child is a slave or a gentile like her. If he converts, he is not a *mamzer*.

The *mishnah* then turns to two remaining categories defined by the woman's status. First: *kol mi she-ein lah alav kiddushin aval yesh lah al acherim kiddushin* — a woman with whom *kiddushin* is not binding for this particular man, but who can nonetheless contract valid marriage with others — *ha-velad mamzer*. This is the category of *arayot*, the forbidden incestuous and adulterous relations enumerated in the Torah. For instance, a married woman's union with someone other than her husband produces a *mamzer*, because *kiddushin* cannot take effect with the adulterer, but she remains capable of valid marriage with her husband.

Finally: *kol mi she-ein lah lo alav ve-lo al acherim kiddushin* — a woman who cannot contract valid marriage with anyone — *ha-velad kemotah*, the child follows the mother's status and is not a *mamzer*. The *mishnah* identifies this category as the *vlad shifcha ve-nochrit*, the child of a non-Jewish woman or a slave woman. Since no Jew can contract *kiddushin* with a non-Jewish woman or a slave, and since these women cannot marry any Jew, the child follows the mother entirely — and is either a non-Jew or a slave, not a *mamzer*. This is the *mishnah's* expression of matrilineal descent: where no Jewish *kiddushin* can attach to the mother in any circumstances, the child's status is defined entirely by her.

## Rule vs. Exception

### Challenge to Completeness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּלָלָא הוּא דְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that **any case where there is betrothal** and the marriage involves no transgression, the lineage of the offspring follows the male. **Rabbi Shimon said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Is** it an established **principle** that **any case where there is betrothal and there is no transgression the offspring** invariably **follows the male? But**

The *gemara* opens with Rabbi Shimon challenging Rabbi Yochanan: *klala hu?* — Is this general principle truly comprehensive? Does the rule *kol makom she-yesh kiddushin ve-ein aveira ha-velad holech achar ha-zachar* genuinely account for all cases?

### Convert & Mamzeret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא מַמְזֶרֶת, דְּיֵשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם, דְּתַנְיָא: גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא מַמְזֶרֶת – הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא? מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: גֵּר לֹא יִשָּׂא מַמְזֶרֶת, וְיֵשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְיֵשׁ עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם.
>
> **Translation:** there is the case of **a convert who married a *mamzeret*, where there is** a valid **betrothal and there is no transgression,** as they are permitted to marry each other, and yet **the offspring follows the flawed** lineage and is a *mamzer*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a convert who married a *mamzeret*, the offspring is a *mamzer*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Shimon: **Do you maintain** that **the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei?** Not so; **the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: A convert may not marry a *mamzeret*, and** therefore in this case **there is betrothal and there is a transgression,** which is why **the offspring follows the flawed** lineage.

Rabbi Shimon immediately identifies a problem. *Harei ger she-nasa mamzeret* — consider a convert who married a *mamzeret*. According to one opinion, a *mamzeret* is permitted to marry a *ger*, making the *kiddushin* binding with no transgression. Yet even in that case, the child is a *mamzer* — *ha-velad holech achar ha-pagum*. Here we have a case of valid *kiddushin* with no *aveira*, yet the child does not follow the father; the child follows the stigmatized party. The *mishnah's* general rule is therefore violated.

A *baraita* confirms: *ger she-nasa mamzeret — ha-velad mamzer*. This is the position of Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yochanan's response is that our *mishnah* need not be Rabbi Yosi's view. *Matnitin Rabbi Yehuda hi* — perhaps the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a *ger* is in fact *forbidden* to marry a *mamzeret* — *ger lo yisa mamzeret*. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, the union involves transgression — *yesh kiddushin ve-yesh aveira* — and so the child following the *pagum* fits squarely within the *mishnah's* second rule. The apparent exception dissolves once we attribute the *mishnah* to Rabbi Yehuda.

### Klal Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנְיַיהּ! תְּנָא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let** the mishna **teach** this case as one of its examples. The Gemara answers: The mishna **taught** the principle: **Any case** where there is betrothal and a transgression, **to include** this kind of case. There is a guiding principle of the Gemara's interpretation of the Mishna that a mishna does not include extraneous phrases. Every apparently superfluous phrase in the context of a principle serves to include or exclude a certain case from that principle. This is the basis of the discussion of the Gemara here and below.

But the *mishnah* does not list the *ger* and *mamzeret* case explicitly under the second rule, even according to Rabbi Yehuda. Why not? The *gemara* answers: *tanna kol makom le-atuyei* — the phrase *kol makom* functions as a *klal*, a general statement broad enough to include unlisted cases within the scope of the rule. The *mishnah* says *kol makom she-yesh kiddushin ve-yesh aveira* — wherever there is *kiddushin* and transgression — and that formulation is deliberately expansive, encompassing the *ger u-mamzeret* case without explicit mention.

Alternatively, even if the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Yosi's view, the case of *ger u-mamzeret* would be an exception to the first rule, handled by the word *ve-eizo zu* in the *mishnah's* phrasing, as we shall see.

The *gemara* then raises a further unlisted case: *vaharei challal she-nasa bat Yisrael* — a *challal* who married a *bat Yisrael*. This union involves no transgression — a *challal* is not forbidden to a non-*kohen* woman — and *kiddushin* is fully binding. The child follows the father and is a *challal*. This is a straightforward application of the first rule, yet it is absent from the *mishnah's* list. Similarly, a *Yisrael* who married a *challala* — again, no transgression, binding *kiddushin*, and the child follows the father and is *kasher*. Why were these not listed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנְיַיהּ בְּהֶדְיָא! מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מִתְּנֵי לֵהּ. הֵיכִי נִיתְנֵי? כֹּהֶנֶת וּלְוִיָּה וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית וַחֲלָלָה שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן לֵוִי וְיִשְׂרָאֵל – וַחֲלָלָה לְכֹהֵן מִי חַזְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the *tanna* of the mishna **teach explicitly** this example of an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*. The Gemara answers: He did not do so **because he cannot teach it,** i.e., the *tanna* cannot mention this *halakha* in brief as part of the list. The Gemara clarifies its answer: **How can** the *tanna* **teach** it? He cannot state: **A daughter of a priest; and a daughter of a Levite; and a daughter of an Israelite; and a *ḥalala*, who married a priest, a Levite, or an Israelite,** as, **is a *ḥalala* fit for a priest?** This marriage involves a transgression. Consequently, the sentence of the mishna cannot be constructed so as to include a *ḥalala*.

The *gemara* answers: *de-lo pasika lei* — the case cannot be stated cleanly given the *mishnah's* framing. If one tried to extend the list to include *challala*, one would write something like: *kohenet, leviyah, Yisraelit, ve-challala she-niset le-kohen, levi, ve-Yisrael* — but then one is implicitly including the possibility of a *challala* marrying a *kohen*, which is in fact *assur*. The case does not lend itself to the tidy phrasing the *mishnah* prefers, and so it was left out despite being a genuine application of the rule. The *klal* of *kol makom* is understood to encompass it nonetheless.

### Eizehu Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, וּתְנָא ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי. ״וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ וְתוּ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** a different answer: **Actually,** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and** it **taught** the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, **to exclude** cases of betrothal without a transgression other than those listed in the mishna. The offspring follows the lineage of the father only in those cases specified by the mishna. The Gemara asks: **But** does the list that follows the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, include all applicable cases? **And** are there **no more?**

Now the *gemara* examines the function of *eizehu* — the word *ve-eizo zu* that introduces the specific lists in the *mishnah*. Initially, there is an assumption that *ve-eizo zu* is exhaustive — that it confines the application of the rule to precisely those cases enumerated. If so, any unlisted application would be a problem for the *mishnah's* completeness. Jenna's observation here is apt: at this stage, the *gemara* is working with the premise that *ve-eizo zu* is limiting, before ultimately arriving at the resolution that *ve-eizo zu* serves a different function.

The *gemara* develops the position that *ve-eizo zu* functions not only to define the scope of the rule but to flag exceptions — cases that fall outside the expected application of the rule. By saying "and which cases are these?" and then providing a list, the *mishnah* implicitly acknowledges that the list does not exhaust all possible applications; there are others not mentioned that nonetheless fall under the *klal*. At the same time, the specificity of the list implies that there are scenarios where one might have expected the rule to apply but where it does not. The word *eizehu* thus does double duty: it confirms that the rule applies to the listed cases, and it signals that certain unlisted cases are exceptions.

The overall framework that emerges is this: anything that is an application of the rule not explicitly listed is included under *kol makom*; anything that is an exception to the rule is signaled by *ve-eizo zu*. The two phrases together provide the *mishnah* with the flexibility to be both a general rule and a practically accurate guide.

### Israelite & Challala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֲלָלָה, דְּיֵשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר! תְּנָא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּרֵישָׁא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** there is the case of **an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*, where there is betrothal and there is no transgression,** and **the offspring follows the male,** and yet this case is not mentioned in the mishna. The Gemara responds: The mishna **taught** the principle: **Any case** where there is betrothal and no transgression, **in the first clause, to include** this situation.

Returning to the unlisted cases of *challal* and *Yisrael* — the *gemara* confirms that *tanna kol makom de-reisha le-atuyei*, the *kol makom* of the opening clause serves to include *Yisrael she-nasa challala* and *challal she-nasa bat Yisrael* as applications of the first rule, even though they were not explicitly enumerated. This is the *gemara's* formal resolution for those cases.

### Framing Difficulty

The *gemara* returns to the question of why the *challal* case was not listed explicitly, even though it is a genuine application of the rule. The answer, as established, is that including *challala* in the list of women would require writing a formulation that also implies a *challala* could marry a *kohen* — *ve-challala le-kohen lo chazya* — which she cannot. The *mishnah* cannot phrase it in a single clean sentence without creating a false implication, and so it leaves the *challal* case to be derived from *kol makom*.

### Egyptian Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִצְרִי שֵׁנִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה – בְּנָהּ שְׁלִישִׁי הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But there is** also the *halakha* **of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, as Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **a second-generation Egyptian man,** i.e., the son of an Egyptian convert, **who married a first-generation Egyptian woman,** a woman who herself converted, **her son is** considered **a third-generation** Egyptian who may marry a Jew of unflawed lineage. This is an example of a betrothal without a transgression where the offspring follows the father.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּרֵישָׁא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי. וּלְרַב דִּימִי דְּאָמַר שֵׁנִי הָוֵי, תְּנָא ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **taught** the principle: **Any case** where there is betrothal and no transgression, **in the first clause, to include** this example. The Gemara adds: **And** according **to** the opinion of **Rav Dimi, who said** that this son **is a second-generation** Egyptian, the mishna **taught:** In **which** case **is this** applicable, at its beginning, with regard to betrothal that does not involve a transgression, **to exclude** this case. Rav Dimi maintains that this son may not marry a Jew of unflawed lineage.

The *gemara* identifies another unlisted case: *Mitzri she-nasa Mitzrit* — an Egyptian convert who married a female Egyptian convert. The *halacha* is that an Egyptian convert may enter the community of Israel (*kahal Hashem*) only from the third generation: *dor shelishi yavo lahem bi-kehal Hashem*. The question is how generations are counted when the parents are of different generational standing within the Egyptian convert lineage.

Rabba bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan states: *Mitzri sheni she-nasa Mitzrit rishona — bena shelishi havei* — if a second-generation Egyptian convert married a first-generation Egyptian female convert, the child is a third-generation, and consequently permitted. Generations are counted by the father. This is another application of the rule that the child follows the father — *yesh kiddushin, ein aveira, ha-velad holech achar ha-zachar* — and it is included in the *klal* of *kol makom*.

### Non-Jewish Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא, דְּכִי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בָּאוּמּוֹת – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר. נִתְגַּיְּירוּ, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But there is** the following case, which apparently contradicts the principle of the mishna: **As when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael **he** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: With regard to the nations,** i.e., if members of two different nations married when they were gentiles, **follow the male** to determine the status of their child. This is the case whether he is a regular gentile, who may marry a Jew of unflawed lineage as soon as he converts, or whether he is an Ammonite or an Egyptian, who may not marry a Jewish woman of unflawed lineage. In either case the child's status follows that of the father. If these members of two different nations **converted, follow the flawed** lineage **of the two.**

Ravin, citing Rabbi Yochanan, introduces a principle regarding non-Jews: *be-umot halech achar ha-zachar* — among the nations, the tribal identity of a child follows the father. If a man from one non-Jewish nation has a child with a woman from another nation, the child's nationality is determined by the father. This too is an unlisted application of the rule that status follows the male, and the *gemara* includes it under *kol makom de-reisha le-atuyei*.

### Converted Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna **taught:** In **which** case **is this** applicable, in the first clause, **to exclude** this case.

The same teaching of Ravin continues: *nitgayeru — halech achar ha-pagum she-be-shneihem* — if they converted, the child follows whichever parent bears the more disqualifying status. For instance, if an *Edomi* convert married a *Mitzri* convert — where the *Edomi* is restricted for two generations and the *Mitzri* for three — the child follows the more stringent status. This is a case of *yesh kiddushin, ein aveira* — two converts, permitted to marry one another — yet the child does not follow the father; it follows the *pagum*. This is an exception to the first rule, and the *gemara* includes it under the category of *eizehu* as an unlisted exception.

### Klal & Eizehu Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּרֵישָׁא – לְאֵתוֹיֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֲלָלָה, וּדְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּרַב דִּימִי וְרָבִין! ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּסֵיפָא – לְאֵתוֹיֵי גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא מַמְזֶרֶת. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּרֵישָׁא – כְּדַאֲמַרַן, ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ – כְּדַאֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּסֵיפָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to an earlier point: **What is this** claim, that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei? **Granted, if you say that the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** one can explain that the phrase: **Any case, of the first clause,** serves **to include an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*,** as the child follows the father in that case too, **and** also to include the ruling of **Rabba bar bar Ḥana** that the son of a second-generation Egyptian who married a first-generation Egyptian woman is a third-generation Egyptian. Furthermore, the expression: In **which** case **is this** applicable, serves **to exclude** the ruling of **Rav Dimi** that her son is a second-generation Egyptian, **and** the statement of **Ravin** citing Rabbi Yoḥanan. And the phrase: **Any case, of the latter clause,** which is referring to betrothal with a transgression, serves **to include a convert who married a *mamzeret*. But if you say** that the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** then granted the phrase: **Any case** where there is betrothal and no transgression, **of the first clause, is as we said,** i.e., it includes an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*; and the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, **is as we said,** i.e., to the exclusion of Ravin or Rav Dimi, and the case of a convert who married a *mamzeret*. **But what** does the phrase: **Any case, of the latter clause,** serve **to add?**

The *gemara* now consolidates its analysis. According to Rabbi Yehuda — the likely author of the *mishnah* — the *ger u-mamzeret* case is an *aveira*, so it fits the second rule via the *kol makom* of the *seifa*. The *challal* and *Yisrael* cases are applications of the first rule also included in *kol makom*. The exceptions — the converted nations following the *pagum* despite no *aveira*, and possibly the *Mitzri* generation count depending on the ruling — are flagged by *eizehu*.

According to Rabbi Yosi, the *ger u-mamzeret* case is *muttar* and thus an exception to the first rule, included in *eizehu*. The converted nations' cases are likewise exceptions included there. But then the *kol makom* of the *seifa* — the general rule for when there is *kiddushin* and *aveira* — seems to have nothing left to include that is not already listed. The *gemara* resolves this by noting: *ayidi de-tana reisha eizo zu, tani seifa eizo zu* — since the *mishnah* uses *eizehu* in the first clause, it maintains the same structural parallel in the second clause, even if the second *eizehu* has no independent work to do in limiting or flagging exceptions there. Similarly, the *kol makom* of the *seifa* mirrors the *kol makom* of the *reisha* for structural reasons, even if — according to Rabbi Yosi — no additional cases need to be drawn in by the *seifa's kol makom*. The *mishnah's* symmetrical structure accounts for the repeated usage of both phrases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ דְּסֵיפָא לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא: אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״, תְּנָא סֵיפָא ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״. הָכִי נָמֵי אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״, תְּנָא נָמֵי סֵיפָא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning,** the same question applies **to Rabbi Yehuda** as well: **Why do I** need the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, **of the latter clause? Rather,** you must say: **Since** the mishna **taught** its **first clause** using the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, **it** also **taught** its **latter clause** using the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, merely for the sake of stylistic consistency. **So too** here, with regard to Rabbi Yosei's opinion, one can say: **Since** the mishna **taught** the **first clause** with the expression: **Any case, it** also **taught** its **latter clause** with: **Any case.**

### Non-Jewish Tribal Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בָּאוּמּוֹת הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר״? כִּדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְאֶחָד מִן הָאוּמּוֹת שֶׁבָּא עַל הַכְּנַעֲנִית וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן שֶׁאַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לִקְנוֹתוֹ בְּעֶבֶד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְגַם מִבְּנֵי הַתּוֹשָׁבִים הַגָּרִים עִמָּכֶם מֵהֶם תִּקְנוּ״. יָכוֹל אַף עֶבֶד שֶׁבָּא עַל שִׁפְחָה מִן הָאוּמּוֹת וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן שֶׁאַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לִקְנוֹתוֹ בְּעֶבֶד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר הוֹלִידוּ בְּאַרְצְכֶם״ – מִן הַנּוֹלָדִין בְּאַרְצְכֶם, וְלֹא מִן הַגָּרִים בְּאַרְצְכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **What** is the meaning of: **With regard to the nations, follow the male? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived with regard **to one** member **of the nations** outside Eretz Yisrael **who engaged in intercourse with a Canaanite woman and fathered a son** from her, **that you are permitted to purchase** the son **as a slave,** and he is not considered a member of the Canaanite nations, who are not allowed to live in Eretz Yisrael? **The verse states: "And also of the children of the residents who sojourn with you, of them you may buy"** (Leviticus 25:45), which indicates that there is a way in which one may purchase slaves from the inhabitants of Canaan. One **might** have thought that **even** with regard to a Canaanite **slave who engaged in intercourse with a maidservant from the** other **nations and fathered a son, that you are permitted to buy** the son **as a slave.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Which they have begotten in your land"** (Leviticus 25:45). This means that one may purchase **from those** who are merely **begotten in your land,** whose fathers are not from the Canaanite nations, **but not from those who reside in your land,** i.e., whose fathers are from the seven Canaanite nations.

The *gemara* now examines the case of non-Jewish tribal identity in detail, returning to the teaching of Ravin. *Mai be-umot halech achar ha-zachar?* — What is the precise case where among non-Jews the child's tribal identity follows the father?

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *minayin le-echad min ha-umot she-ba al Kena'anit ve-holid ben she-ata rashai liknoto be-eved?* — From where do we know that if a man from the nations (not one of the seven Canaanite nations) fathered a child with a *Kena'anit* woman, that child may be purchased as a slave and is not subject to the prohibition of destruction that applies to the seven nations? The *talmud lomar*: *ve-gam mi-benei ha-toshavim ha-garim imachem, me-hem tiknu* — from those who dwell as sojourners in your land, you may purchase. The *baraita* understands the distinction between "sojourners" and "native" to be defined by the father. If the father is from outside *Kena'an*, the child is not a *Kena'ani*, even if the mother is.

Conversely: if a *Kena'ani* man fathered a child with a non-*Kena'anit* woman — *yachol af eved she-ba al shifcha min ha-umot ve-holid ben she-ata rashai liknoto be-eved?* — perhaps that child is also purchasable as a non-*Kena'ani*? The *talmud lomar*: *asher holidu be-artzechem* — those born in your land, meaning those born into a foreign land whose father identifies them as belonging elsewhere. *Min ha-noladim be-artzechem — aval lo min ha-garim be-artzechem* — you may purchase those born here among sojourners, but not those who genuinely dwell in the land as part of the *Kena'ani* nations. And who belongs to the *Kena'ani* nations? Those whose father is *Kena'ani* — the child follows the father.

### Converted Nations Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נִתְגַּיְּירוּ הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם״ – בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּמִצְרִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא עַמּוֹנִית, מַאי פָּגוּם אִיכָּא, ״עַמּוֹנִי״ – וְלֹא עַמּוֹנִית! אֶלָּא בְּעַמּוֹנִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא מִצְרִית, וְאִי זָכָר הָוֵי – הַאי שִׁדְיֵהּ אַבָּתְרֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ, וְאִם נְקֵבָה הָוְיָא – שִׁדְיַהּ אַבָּתְרַהּ דִּידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the second clause of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement: If **they converted, follow the flawed** lineage **of the two. To which** case does this refer? **If we say** that it is referring **to an Egyptian man who married an Ammonite woman, what flawed** lineage **is there** here? The lineage of an Ammonite woman who converts is not flawed at all, as the Sages expounded that the verse: "An Ammonite…shall not enter into the assembly of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:4) is referring to **an Ammonite man but not an Ammonite woman,** which means that she can marry a Jew of unflawed lineage. **Rather,** it must be referring **to an Ammonite man who married an Egyptian woman, and** in that case **if** the child **is a male, cast him after** his father, and render him permanently prohibited from entering the congregation as an Ammonite male. **And if** the child born to them **is a female, cast her after** the mother, so that she is considered a second-generation Egyptian, who may not marry a Jew of unflawed lineage.

The discussion of what happens when such individuals convert — for instance, an *Edomi* convert and a *Moavi* convert, or an *Ammoni* convert, each of whom carries different *halachic* consequences regarding entry into the Jewish community — will continue in the next *shiur*.
