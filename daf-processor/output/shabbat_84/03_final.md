# Shabbat 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boats & Tumah

### Intro: Boats & Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנַּיִן לִסְפִינָה שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דֶּרֶךְ אֳנִיָּה בְלֶב יָם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This is another mishna that digresses from the central topic of this tractate. It, too, is based on an allusion from the Bible. **From where** is it derived **that the ship is ritually pure,** in the sense that it cannot become impure? **As it is stated: "The way of a ship in the midst of the sea"** (Proverbs 30:19).

The *daf* begins at the very bottom of 83b. The immediate topic under discussion is that a boat is not *mekabel tumah*. The *mishnah* learns this from a *pasuk* — *derech oniyah b'lev yam* — comparing the boat to the ocean, as all *mishnayot* in this *perek* do. Chananya, however, derives the exclusion from a more general principle.

### Chananya's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פְּשִׁיטָא, אֳנִיָּה בְּלֶב יָם הִיא! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כְּיָם: מָה יָם טָהוֹר — אַף סְפִינָה טְהוֹרָה. תַּנְיָא, חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: נִלְמְדֶנָּה מִשַּׂק, מָה שַׂק מִיטַּלְטֵל מָלֵא וְרֵיקָן — אַף כֹּל מִיטַּלְטֵל מָלֵא וְרֵיקָן. לְאַפּוֹקֵי סְפִינָה, דְּאֵינָהּ מִיטַּלְטֶלֶת מְלֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The allusion in the mishna requires clarification. The verse appears to state the **obvious.** Of course **a ship is in the midst of the sea.** Rather, **this** verse **teaches us** an allusion that the legal status of a boat is **like** that of **the sea. Just as the sea is ritually pure** and cannot become impure, **so too, a boat is ritually pure** and cannot become impure. **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Ḥananya says:** This *halakha* **is derived from** the *halakha* of **a sack,** as the impurity of wooden vessels is likened to the impurity of a sack. **Just as a sack,** which can become ritually impure, **is carried** both **full and empty, so too, any** object that **is carried** both **full and empty** can become ritually impure. This is **to exclude a ship,** which **is not carried** on land **full and empty,** as due to its weight it cannot be carried full.

According to Chananya, something made out of wood is only *tamei* if it is able — like a *sak* — to be carried both empty and full. A boat ordinarily cannot satisfy this condition, and is therefore excluded. Crucially, as the *gemara* established, this is not a categorical exclusion of all boats; it applies only to those objects that fail to meet the criteria. The *rabbanan*, as Tosafot points out, do not actually disagree with Chananya's underlying principle. They simply maintain that there is an additional, categorical exclusion of boats independent of that principle. The debate, then, is really about scope, not the principle itself.

### Jordan Boat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי סְפִינַת הַיַּרְדֵּן. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֳנִיָּה בְּלֶב יָם הִיא — הָא נָמֵי אֳנִיָּה בְּלֶב יָם הִיא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִיטַּלְטֶלֶת מָלֵא וְרֵיקָן — הָא נָמֵי מִיטַּלְטֶלֶת מְלֵאָה וְרֵיקָנִית. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ סְפִינַת הַיַּרְדֵּן טְמֵאָה — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁטּוֹעֲנִים אוֹתָהּ בַּיַּבָּשָׁה, וּמוֹרִידִין אוֹתָהּ לַמַּיִם. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם אַל יִמְנַע אָדָם אֶת עַצְמוֹ מִבֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וַאֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת, שֶׁהֲרֵי כַּמָּה שָׁנִים נִשְׁנֵית מִשְׁנָה זוֹ בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְלֹא נִתְגַּלָּה טַעְמָהּ עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא וּפֵירְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** there is a difference between them regarding a **Jordan ship,** which is a small boat used on the Jordan River. **According to the one who said** that it is derived from the verse: **"A ship in the midst of the sea," this is also a ship in the midst of the sea.** A river is pure like the sea and the boat will remain pure. **According to the one who said** that it is derived from the *halakha* of a sack, and in order to become impure it must be **carried full and empty, this is also carried** both **full and empty,** and can become ritually impure. **As Rabbi Ḥanina ben Akavya said: For what** reason did **they say that a Jordan ship can become impure? Because they load it on dry land** and carry it on land **and** then **lower it into the water.** It is carried on land when full. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: One should never prevent himself from** attending **the study hall for even one moment, as this mishna** which states that a Jordan ship can become ritually impure **was taught for several years in the study hall, but its reason was not revealed until Rabbi Ḥanina ben Akavya came and explained it.**

Two implications follow from Chananya's formulation. First, a boat that *can* in fact be carried — such as a *sfinat hayarden*, a small Jordan River vessel or canoe that is portaged across dry land — would, according to Chananya, be *tamei*, since it satisfies the empty-and-full carriability requirement. Second, an object made from non-wood material, such as pottery, also falls outside Chananya's criterion, which is specifically formulated for wooden objects. Neither exclusion is categorical for Chananya; both depend on whether the object satisfies the relevant conditions.

## Value of Torah Study

### Rav's Teaching

The *gemara* then records a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *leolam al yimna adam et atzmo mibeit hamidrash* — a person should never withhold himself from the study hall, even for a single hour. The reasoning offered is striking in its choice of example.

### Hidden Reasoning

*Sheharei kamah shanim nishtantah mishnah zu b'beit hamidrash v'lo nitgalah ta'amah* — for many years, the ruling that the *sfinat hayarden* can become *tamei* was taught in the study hall, yet its reasoning was never revealed, until Rabbi Chanina ben Akavya came along and explained it: the *sfinat hayarden* is *tamei* because it is carried on dry land, and therefore satisfies the condition of being carryable both empty and full.

One might reasonably ask: of all the examples in the Talmud, why was this particular *mishnah* chosen to illustrate the importance of constant attendance in the *beit midrash*? Part of the answer may be that if one were following the *mishnah* and treating the boat's exclusion as categorical, the inclusion of the *sfinat hayarden* would be inexplicable — one might have resolved the difficulty by drawing a distinction between ocean vessels and river vessels without ever arriving at the correct underlying principle. The point is that familiar material can suddenly yield new insight; one never knows when a brilliant clarification will emerge, and it would be a loss to have missed it.

### Rabbi Yonatan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: לְעוֹלָם אַל יִמְנַע אָדָם אֶת עַצְמוֹ מִבֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וּמִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אָדָם כִּי יָמוּת בְּאֹהֶל״ — אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה תְּהֵא עוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֵין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁמֵּמִית עַצְמוֹ עָלֶיהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אָדָם כִּי יָמוּת בְּאֹהֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** Following Rav's statement, the Gemara cites that which **Rabbi Yonatan said: One should never prevent himself from** attending **the study hall or from** engaging in **matters of Torah, even at the moment of death, as it is stated: "This is the Torah: A person who dies in a tent"** (Numbers 19:14). That is an allusion to the fact that **even at the moment of death, one should engage in** the study of **Torah. Reish Lakish said: Matters of Torah only endure in a person who kills himself over** the Torah, one who is ready to devote all his efforts to it, **as it is stated: "This is the Torah: A person who dies in a tent,"** meaning that the Torah is only attained by one who kills himself in its tent.

The teaching of Rav is then contrasted with that of Rabbi Yonatan, who states: *leolam al yimna adam et atzmo mibeit hamidrash umidivrei Torah* — a person should never withhold himself not only from the study hall, but from words of Torah altogether. *Veafilu b'sha'at mitah* — even at the moment of death. The *pasuk* adduced is *zot haTorah adam ki yamut b'ohel* — this is the Torah: a person who dies in the tent, meaning one who is *osek baTorah* even at the final moment of life.

The difference between the two teachings is fundamental. Rav's statement is essentially pragmatic: be present in the *beit midrash* because you might miss a crucial insight. The gain is the acquisition of knowledge. Rabbi Yonatan's teaching is not about what one will learn or retain. He is speaking about the value of the act itself — of cleaving to Torah at every moment for its own sake, not because of any result. Even when one is moments from death, with no prospect of applying or transmitting what one studies, that engagement remains intrinsically meaningful.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish brings these two perspectives together in a synthesis: *ein divrei Torah mitkaymin ela bemi shemamit atzmo aleha* — words of Torah endure only in one who kills himself over them, in the sense of total self-sacrifice and devotion. The *pasuk* is again *zot haTorah adam ki yamut b'ohel*: Torah is established in a person only if they are willing to die in the tent of Torah, to give everything for it.

Rav's teaching emphasized the outcome — attend the study hall because important ideas will be revealed there. Rabbi Yonatan's teaching elevated the act itself — study Torah always, even to the last moment, purely for the sake of it. Reish Lakish fuses the two: if you approach Torah with that total self-sacrificing attitude, driven not by anticipated results but by passion for Torah itself, that is precisely the disposition that best establishes Torah within you and yields the greatest results. The approach of self-sacrifice produces the outcome of genuine Torah retention.

## Rava & Carriability

### Rava on Oxen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַחֲנַנְיָא טִילְטוּל עַל יְדֵי שְׁוָורִים שְׁמֵיהּ טִילְטוּל? אִין דִּתְנַן: שָׁלֹשׁ עֲגָלוֹת הֵן. עֲשׂוּיָה כְּקָתֶידְרָא — טְמֵאָה מִדְרָס. כְּמִטָּה — טְמֵאָה טְמֵא מֵת. שֶׁל אֲבָנִים — טְהוֹרָה מִכְּלוּם. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְאִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית קִבּוּל רִמּוֹנִים, טְמֵאָה טְמֵא מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according **to Ḥananya,** who holds that a boat carried both full and empty can become ritually impure, is **carrying by oxen considered carrying?** He answered his own question. **Yes,** as **we learned** in a mishna: In terms of the *halakhot* of ritual purity and impurity, **there are three** distinct types of **wagons:** A wagon built **like a chair,** meaning closed on the sides, can become **ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **treading.** Since it is designated for sitting, it becomes impure if a *zav* sits on it, even if he does not touch it. A wagon built **like a bed** can become **ritually impure** with **impurity** imparted by **a corpse.** It contracts all types of impurity, except for impurity imparted by the treading of a *zav*. A wagon made **of stone,** whose bottom is netting, remains **ritually pure** and does not become impure **from any** type of impurity. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And if** in the stone wagon **there is** a **receptacle for pomegranates,** i.e., the holes are not large enough for a pomegranate to fall through, it is considered a utensil and it can become **impure with impurity** imparted by **a corpse.** Even though a stone wagon is not carried full, but is pulled by oxen, it can become ritually impure. Apparently, carrying by oxen is considered carrying.

Returning to the *sfinat hayarden*, the *gemara* records Rava's formulation at the top of 84a: *ul'Chananya, taltula yedei shevarim shmai taltula* — according to Chananya, being carried by oxen also qualifies as carriability. The question is not whether human beings specifically do the carrying, but whether the object is capable of being moved by some external agent. Tosafot notes that carriability by the sea itself would not count — because there the object is stationary while the ground moves beneath it, which is fundamentally different from the case where the ground is stable and the object is moved by animals or people.

### Wagon Mishnah

The *gemara* now cites a *mishnah*: *shalosh agalot hein* — there are three types of wagons, each with a different *tumah* status.

### Covered Wagon

The first type is a wagon *asuya k'katedra* — made like a *katedra*, a covered vehicle designed for passengers to ride in, akin to a stagecoach. This becomes *tamei midras*, because it is specifically made for human seating or reclining. When a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah* sits or reclines in it, the wagon becomes an *av hatumah* of *midras* even without direct touching. The defining criterion is that the object is made for the purpose of bearing a person's weight.

### Flatbed Wagon

The second type is a wagon *k'mitah* — like a bed used for transporting goods, not for seating people. Rashi explains this as essentially a flatbed wagon, a vehicle for loading cargo rather than for carrying passengers. Such a wagon cannot become *tamei midras*, because it is not designed for sitting. However, it can become *tamei tumat met* — it remains susceptible to all other forms of *tumah*, since it is still a functional vessel used for carrying objects.

### Stone Wagon

The third type is a wagon used to carry stones. Rashi explains that these wagons were built with a crisscross of wooden slats forming the floor, with large gaps between them, making the base effectively a grid with holes larger than a pomegranate. A wooden vessel with a hole larger than a pomegranate loses its status as a receptacle (*beit kibbul*) and is therefore completely *tahor* — *tehorah miklum*. Even though stones or boulders rest in it and it functionally carries them, the absence of an effective receptacle surface removes it from the category of vessels susceptible to *tumah* altogether.

This raises a sharp question: if even the stone wagon, when its holes are not larger than a pomegranate, can be *tamei* — the *gemara* cites Rabbi Yochanan: *v'im yesh bah beit kibbul rimonim tmei'im tmei meis* — then how can such a wagon become *tamei* at all? It carries boulders; no person could carry it when loaded. What makes it a vessel with carriability?

### Oxen & Tumah

This is precisely the point Rava's principle addresses. The answer is that the stone wagon is pulled by oxen or horses, and that is sufficient. One might have thought that only human carriability counts, or that something which transports massive loads and cannot be moved by a person is simply not classified as a carriable vessel. The *gemara* establishes otherwise: *taltula yedei shevarim shmai taltula* — carriability by oxen is genuine carriability, and the stone wagon therefore falls within the *tumah* framework, provided it has a section with a *beit kibbul* — a surface that can effectively hold a pomegranate without it rolling away or falling through.

The discussion of *beit kibbul* is refined further: what counts as a *beit kibbul* in a partially-holed surface? The standard is not that the entire floor be solid, but that some contiguous section of the surface effectively retain an object. If even one portion of the crisscross floor has openings smaller than a pomegranate such that a pomegranate placed there would be held in place and not fall through, that section constitutes a *beit kibbul*, and the wagon is susceptible to *tumah*. If no such section exists — if every part of the floor has gaps larger than a pomegranate — then there is no *beit kibbul*, and the wagon is completely *tahor*.

## Three Chest Types

### Side-Opening Chest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלֹשׁ תֵּיבוֹת הֵן: תֵּיבָה שֶׁפִּתְחָהּ מִצִּדָּהּ — טְמֵאָה מִדְרָס. מִלְּמַעְלָה — טְמֵאָה טְמֵא מֵת. וְהַבָּאָה בְּמִדָּה, טְהוֹרָה מִכְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** By association, the Gemara now cites the second part of the mishna: With regard to laws of impurity, **there are three** types of **chests: A chest that opens from the side,** on which one can sit or lie, because it can be used for sitting, it can become **ritually impure** with **impurity** imparted by **treading** if a *zav* sits on it. Even if one needs to open the chest, a person can keep sitting on it. A chest that opens **from the top** does not become ritually impure with impurity imparted by treading because it cannot be opened with somebody on it. However, it can become impure **with impurity** imparted by **a corpse. And** a chest **that comes** in a very large **size,** and can hold more than forty *se'a*, remains **ritually pure** and does not become impure **from any** type of impurity.

The *gemara* now quotes the continuation of the same *mishnah*: *shalosh tevot hen* — there are three types of chests. The first is a chest whose opening is on the side — *teva shepitchah mitzidah*. Such a chest is *tamei midras*. The reasoning is that when the access point is on the side, the top of the chest remains usable as a seat without interfering with the chest's primary function. A person can sit on top of it while its contents remain accessible from the side. Because it is designed in a way that supports dual use — storage and seating — a *zav* or *zavah* who sits on it renders it *tamei midras*.

### Top-Opening Chest

The second type opens from the top. Here, sitting on the lid interferes with accessing the contents, so the chest cannot be said to be designed for use as a seat. It therefore cannot become *tamei midras*. It does, however, remain susceptible to *tumat met* and all other forms of *tumah*, since it is a functional vessel.

### Huge Chest

The third type is a chest that is enormous in size — *vehaba bemidah*. Rashi notes uncertainty about the precise phrase but understands it to refer to an extraordinarily large chest. Such a chest is completely *tahor — tehorah miklum* — because when filled it could not be carried, and thus falls outside the category of vessels susceptible to *tumah*. This follows the same carriability principle that governs the stone wagon: an object that cannot be moved, even by oxen, when full fails the fundamental test for wooden vessel *tumah*.

## Pottery & Midras

### Pottery Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִדְרַס כְּלִי חֶרֶס טָהוֹר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַסְּפִינָה. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב זְבִיד, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִדְרַס כְּלִי חֶרֶס — טָהוֹר, וּמַגָּעוֹ טָמֵא, וּסְפִינָה שֶׁל חֶרֶס טְמֵאָה כַּחֲנַנְיָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַסְּפִינָה טְהוֹרָה, כְּתַנָּא דִידַן. מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מַאי ״אַף״? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִדְרַס כְּלִי חֶרֶס — טָהוֹר, וּמַגָּעוֹ טָמֵא. וְשֶׁל עֵץ, בֵּין מִדְרָסוֹ וּבֵין מַגָּעוֹ — טָמֵא, וּסְפִינַת הַיַּרְדֵּן — טְהוֹרָה כְּתַנָּא דִידַן. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַסְּפִינָה טְמֵאָה, כַּחֲנַנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to impurity imparted by **treading, an earthenware vessel is ritually pure.** If a *zav* sits on an earthenware vessel and does not touch the inside of it, it does not become impure. **Rabbi Yosei says:** That is **even** the status of **a ship.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *baraita* **saying? Rav Zevid said** that the *baraita* **is saying the following:** According to the first *tanna*, with regard to impurity imparted by **treading, an earthenware vessel is ritually pure; however,** if the *zav* **touches** the vessel it becomes **impure. And an earthenware ship** can become **impure** with impurity imparted by the treading of a *zav*, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥananya. Rabbi Yosei says: Even a boat is ritually pure, in accordance with** the opinion of the ***tanna*** of **our** mishna. **Rav Pappa strongly objects to this** explanation: If so, **what** is the meaning of the word **even** employed by Rabbi Yosei, indicating that he is adding to the opinion of the first *tanna* of the *baraita*? According to the above explanation, the first *tanna* says that a boat can become ritually impure and Rabbi Yosei says that it is pure. Rabbi Yosei is not adding to the previous opinion but disagreeing with it. **Rather, Rav Pappa said** that the *baraita* **is saying the following:** With regard to impurity imparted by **treading, an earthenware vessel is ritually pure, and** with regard to **its contact** with a source of ritual impurity, **it is impure. And** as far as **a wooden vessel** is concerned, with regard to **both its** impurity imparted by **treading and its contact** with a source of ritual impurity, it is **impure. And a Jordan ship is ritually pure in accordance with** the opinion of the ***tanna*** of **our** mishna. **Rabbi Yosei says: Even the boat is impure** like other wooden vessels, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥananya.**

The *gemara* now introduces a separate *baraita*: *midras klei cheres tahor* — pottery vessels cannot become *tamei midras*. Even a pottery object specifically designated and used as a seat — say, a pottery stool — is categorically excluded from the *midras* category of *tumah*.

### Rav Zevid

Rav Zevid reads this ruling precisely: pottery vessels are excluded from *midras*, but not from other *tumot*. Implicitly — and the *baraita* is understood to state this explicitly — *umaga'o tamei*: if a *zav* touches the inside of a pottery vessel, it certainly becomes *tamei* through that contact. The exclusion of pottery is limited specifically to the *midras* category.

### Pottery Boat Debate

This creates an ambiguity regarding boats. The *baraita* mentions that Rabbi Yosi adds *af asfinot* — "even boats." But what does this addition mean, and in what context? The *gemara* poses the question *mai kamar* — who was talking about boats to begin with?

Rav Zevid explains the *baraita* as follows: pottery vessels are excluded from *midras* but remain susceptible to other *tumot*, and this applies equally to pottery boats — *usefina shel cheres tmei'a keChananya* — a pottery boat would be *tamei* like other pottery vessels (by touch, not by *midras*). Rabbi Yosi then disputes this: *af hasfina tehora* — even a boat is entirely *tahor*, because boats are categorically excluded regardless of material, following the majority view that the boat exclusion is categorical rather than Chananya's material-based framework.

However, Rav Pappa raises a difficulty with Rav Zevid's reading. The *af* — "even" — in Rabbi Yosi's formulation is awkward. The *tanna kamma* is saying that pottery things are not *midras* but are *tamei* by other routes; what is Rabbi Yosi adding with "even boats are *tahor*"? In relation to what *tumah* are boats being declared *tahor*? The *tanna kamma* was speaking of boats being *tamei* — not of anything being *tahor*. The *af* implies that boats are *tahor* in addition to something else being *tahor*, but nothing *tahor* has been mentioned.

### Wooden vs Boat

Rav Pappa therefore offers an alternative reading that reverses the structure. *Midras klei cheres tahor* — pottery cannot be *midras*. *Umaga'o tamei* — but it is *tamei* by other means. *Veshel etz* — and for wooden vessels, *bein midraso bein maga'o tamei* — whether as a seat or by touch, a wooden vessel is susceptible to all *tumot*, including *midras*. Then *usfinat hayarden tehora* — and the *sfinat hayarden* is *tahor*. Now Rabbi Yosi's response works: *af hasfina tmei'a keChananya* — even a boat is *tamei*, like Chananya holds, because if it can be carried, it is not categorically excluded.

Under this reading, the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yosi is a reprise of the Chananya-versus-*rabbanan* debate, framed now around the *sfinat hayarden* specifically. The *tanna kamma* holds the categorical boat exclusion; Rabbi Yosi follows Chananya's material-and-carriability framework. This connects back to the two *nafka mina* cases established earlier — pottery boats versus Jordan boats — as the two points where the debate between Chananya and the *chachamim* has practical consequence.

## Mikveh & Midras

### Chizkiya's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְרַס כְּלִי חֶרֶס מְנָלַן דְּטָהוֹר? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּמִשְׁכָּבוֹ״, מַקִּישׁ מִשְׁכָּבוֹ לוֹ: מָה הוּא אִית לֵיהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה — אַף מִשְׁכָּבוֹ נָמֵי אִית לֵיהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions what was stated: **And from where do we** derive that with regard to impurity imparted by **treading, an earthenware vessel is ritually pure? Ḥizkiya said:** It is **as the verse states: "And whoever touches his bed** shall wash his clothes, and bathe himself in water, and be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 15:5). The verse **juxtaposes his bed to himself. Just as he has** the possibility of **purification** through immersion **in a ritual bath, so too, his bed** is referring to a vessel that **has** the possibility of **purification in a ritual bath.** Since an impure earthenware vessel cannot be purified in a ritual bath, unlike other vessels, it does not become ritually impure when a *zav* lies on it.

Having established that pottery cannot become *midras*, the *gemara* asks: *u'midras klei cheres minalan d'tahor* — how do we know that a pottery vessel cannot become *tamei midras*? Chizkiya derives this from the *pasuk*: *v'ish asher yiga b'mishkavo* — a person who touches the *mishkav* of a *zav* becomes *tamei*. The Torah says *mishkavo* — his *mishkav*, his seat. The word *mishkavo* creates a comparison between the *mishkav* and the *zav* himself: *makish mishkavo lo*.

### Mishkav Connection

The comparison yields a principle: just as the *zav* himself has *tahara b'mikveh* — he can be purified through immersion in a *mikveh* — so too his *mishkav* can only be something that has the potential for *tahara b'mikveh*. An object that cannot be taken to a *mikveh* to be purified cannot serve as a *mishkav* for a *zav*. The reasoning has an internal logic beyond mere exegesis: the *zav*'s personal *tumah* status is temporary — it ends when he goes to the *mikveh* after his purification process. It would be incongruous for the object he sits on to acquire a *tumah* that is more permanent, more intractable, than his own. Pottery vessels, which cannot be purified in a *mikveh* — the Torah's remedy for pottery *tumah* is destruction and refashioning, not immersion — are therefore categorically excluded from becoming a *mishkav*.

A parallel derivation appears from the house of Rabbi Yishmael: *k'mishkav nidatah yihyeh lah* — referring to a *zavah*, the *pasuk* uses the language "like the *mishkav* of her *niddah*-state it shall be for her," again connecting the *mishkav* to her own purification capacity. *Mah hi it lah tahara b'mikveh af mishkava nami it lah tahara b'mikveh* — since she can be purified in a *mikveh*, only objects that can similarly be taken to a *mikveh* qualify as her *mishkav*. This excludes *klei cheres*, pottery vessels, for which *mikveh* immersion provides no *tahara*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״כְּמִשְׁכַּב נִדָּתָהּ יִהְיֶה לָּהּ״, מַקִּישׁ מִשְׁכָּבָהּ לָהּ: מָה הִיא אִית לַהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה — אַף מִשְׁכָּבָהּ נָמֵי אִית לַהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי כְּלִי חֶרֶס דְּלֵית לֵיהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה. מֵתִיב רַבִּי אִילְעָא: מַפָּץ בְּמֵת מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that the verse states: "Every bed on which she lies all the days of her *zava* emission **shall be for her like the bed of her menstrual period"** (Leviticus 15:26). The verse **juxtaposes her bed to herself: Just as she has** the possibility of **purification in a ritual bath, so too, her bed** is referring to a vessel that **has** the possibility of **purification in a ritual bath.** This is **to the exclusion of an earthenware vessel, which does not have** the possibility of **purification in a ritual bath. Rabbi Ila strongly objects** to this from what we learned: **From where** is it derived that **a** reed **mat** becomes ritually impure **from** contact with **a corpse?**

Tosafot notes a technical precision: a *zav* is actually required to immerse in *mayim chayim* — living water such as a spring or natural well — not simply a collected-rainwater *mikveh*. The *gemara*'s language of *tahara b'mikveh* is therefore somewhat imprecise. Nevertheless, the principle is clear: any capacity for ritual immersion purification suffices; it is only complete inability to be purified through immersion that disqualifies an object from the *mishkav* category.

## Mat & Midras

### Rabbi Ila's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה פַּכִּין קְטַנִּים שֶׁטְּהוֹרִין בְּזָב — טְמֵאִים בְּמֵת, מַפָּץ שֶׁטָּמֵא בְּזָב, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא טָמֵא בְּמֵת?! וְאַמַּאי, הָא לֵית לֵיהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם הוֹאִיל וְאִיכָּא בְּמִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is** derived through **an** *a fortiori* **inference:** We know that small vessels do not become ritually impure through the impurity of a *zav* because they are not designated for sitting, and are too small for the *zav* to insert his finger into their airspace. **If small** earthenware **pitchers** remain **pure** and are not susceptible **to** the impurity of **a *zav*,** but they do **become ritually impure from** contact with **a corpse; is it not logical that a** reed **mat,** which contracts **impurity from a *zav*, will** become **ritually impure from** contact with **a corpse? And why** should the reed mat become impure? **Isn't it** true that **it does not have** the possibility of **purification in a ritual bath? Rabbi Ḥanina said to him: There,** in the case of the mat, **it is different because there is** purification **in** other vessels **of its kind,** i.e., other wooden vessels that are made from materials that grow from the earth can be purified in a ritual bath.

This principle — that *mishkav* status requires the capacity for *mikveh* purification — appears to generate a difficulty. Rabbi Ila raises the challenge: *mapatz d'met minayin* — from where do we know that a mat (*mapatz*) can become *tamei tumat met*? The *baraita* he cites takes as its starting point that a mat *can* be *tamei midras* — since it is made for lying or sitting upon and a *zav* can rest on it — and then reasons from a *kal vachomer* that if small pottery vessels (*pachim ktanim*) are *tahor b'zav* yet *tamei* by a dead body, then certainly a mat which *is tamei b'zav* should become *tamei* by a dead body as well.

This *kal vachomer* establishes an important structural principle: objects that qualify as *midras* — as surfaces for the *zav* — gain entry into the broader *tumah* system through that status. Even if a mat has no *beit kibbul* and would be turned away at the "front door" of ordinary wooden vessel *tumah* for lack of a receptacle, its *midras* status opens a "back door" through which it can also become *tamei tumat met*. The *tumah* of *midras* thus functions as a gateway to other forms of *tumah* even for objects that would not otherwise qualify.

Rabbi Ila's implicit challenge, however, is this: if a mat cannot be purified in a *mikveh* — either because it is a wooden object without a *beit kibbul* and the Torah never addressed its *tahara*, or (as Tosafot argues) because it is made of hemp, a material that the Torah never subjected to *tumah* in the first place and therefore never provided a *mikveh* remedy for — then by the same logic used to exclude pottery, a mat should also be unable to become *mishkav* and thus unable to be *tamei midras*.

## Mikveh & Midras (II)

### Back Door Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַחֲמָנָא לִיצְלַן מֵהַאי דַּעְתָּא: אַדְּרַבָּה, רַחֲמָנָא לִיצְלַן מִדַּעְתָּא דִידָךְ. וְטַעְמָא מַאי — תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי. כְּתִיב: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּמִשְׁכָּבוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְכׇל הַמִּשְׁכָּב אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכַּב עָלָיו הַזָּב יִטְמָא״. הָא כֵּיצַד? יֵשׁ בְּמִינוֹ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה. אֵין בְּמִינוֹ, מַקִּישׁ מִשְׁכָּבוֹ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ila **said to** Rabbi Ḥanina: **May the all-Merciful save us from this opinion.** Rabbi Ḥanina responded: **On the contrary, may the all-Merciful save us from your opinion. And what is the reason** that this is relevant? What is the significance of the fact that other vessels of its kind can be purified in a ritual bath if the vessel itself cannot be purified in a ritual bath? It is because **two verses are written.** In one verse **it is written: "And whoever touches his bed** shall wash his clothes, and bathe himself in water, and be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 15:5). The verse juxtaposes his bed to himself. Just as he has the possibility of purification through immersion in a ritual bath, so too, his bed has the same legal status as he does; in order for a bed on which a *zav* lies to be subject to the impurity imparted by lying, it must be immersible in a ritual bath. This teaches that his bed has the same legal status as he does; in order for a bed on which a *zav* lies to be subject to the impurity imparted by lying, it must be immersible in a ritual bath. **And** in another verse **it is written: "Every bed on which the *zav* lies shall be impure;** and every vessel on which he sits shall be impure" (Leviticus 15:4). This verse includes all beds on which a *zav* might lie, even one that cannot be purified in a ritual bath. **How** can **these** two verses be reconciled? If **there is** purification **in** other vessels **of its kind, even though it** itself **does not have purification in a ritual bath,** it is subject to the impurity imparted by lying. However, if **there is no** purification **in** other vessels **of its kind,** the verse **juxtaposes his bed to himself.** Any vessel that is not like him in the sense that it cannot be purified in a ritual bath, is not subject to impurity imparted by lying.

Before turning to the resolution, it is worth appreciating the structural insight in the *gemara*'s discussion of the *pachim ktanim* mentioned in the *baraita*. These "little vessels" are tiny pottery containers — like the miniature perfume bottles one might see in a museum collection — so small that a *zav* cannot insert even his smallest finger inside them. Since pottery becomes *tamei* only through contact with the interior, and a *zav* cannot physically touch the inside of such a vessel, these small pottery items are *tahor b'zav* — the *zav* cannot render them *tamei*. Rashi considers and rejects the idea that touching with body hair would suffice, reasoning that hair-contact works only because it is treated as physical contact, and contact that can never reach the interior at all simply does not apply. Similarly, *hesset* — moving the vessel — works as a form of constructive contact, but again only where actual contact would otherwise be possible; for objects whose interior can never be reached, even moving them does not suffice.

Tosafot raises what the *gemara* leaves unanswered: could not a *zav* spit into such a vessel, since the saliva of a *zav* is itself an *av hatumah*? Tosafot does not resolve this, but the implicit answer is that *tumah* from spittle is *tumah* from an *av hatumah* secondary to the *zav*, not *tumah* directly from the *zav* himself — a separate mechanism.

How, then, do these little vessels become *tamei* at all? Through a *met* — a corpse. A dead body conveys *tumah* through shared airspace (*ohel*), and the sealed-off interior of these tiny pottery vessels is reached by that airspace regardless of the size of the opening. Thus these *pachim ktanim* are *tehorim b'zav utmei b'met* — *tahor* with respect to a *zav*, *tamei* with respect to a corpse. They demonstrate that susceptibility to the *tumah* of a corpse can exceed susceptibility to the *tumah* of a *zav*. Once this is established, the *kal vachomer* regarding the mat becomes cogent: if even objects *tahor* by a *zav* can be *tamei* by a *met*, how much more so an object that *is tamei* by a *zav* — namely, the mat used as *midras* — should be *tamei* by a *met* as well.

## Mat & Midras (II)

### Rabbi Chanina's Answer

Tosafot notes a technical precision: a *zav* is actually required to immerse in *mayim chayim* — living water such as a spring or natural well — not simply a collected-rainwater *mikveh*. The *gemara*'s language of *tahara b'mikveh* is therefore somewhat imprecise. Nevertheless, the principle is clear: any capacity for ritual immersion purification suffices; it is only complete inability to be purified through immersion that disqualifies an object from the *mishkav* category.

The challenge remains: how can a mat be *tamei midras* given that it lacks *tahara b'mikveh*? Rabbi Chanina answers: *shani hatam de'ika be'minei* — it is different there, because there exist objects within the same general category that do have *tahara b'mikveh*. Wooden vessels with a *beit kibbul* are taken to a *mikveh*; or, as Tosafot maintains, even wooden vessels without a *beit kibbul* can in principle be taken to a *mikveh* if they became *tamei*. Either way, the mat — whether understood as a non-receptacle wooden object or as a hemp object broadly in the class of plant-derived materials — belongs to a category within which *mikveh* purification is a recognized remedy. This categorical proximity is sufficient to include it as *mishkav*.

Pottery, by contrast, is never brought to a *mikveh*. No pottery vessel, regardless of shape, size, or function, can be purified through immersion. It is categorically and absolutely outside the *mikveh* system. That absolute exclusion is what disqualifies pottery from *mishkav* status. A mat, for all the complications of its specific case, belongs to a family of materials that are part of the *mikveh* world.

Rabbi Ila's rejoinder is sharp: *rachmana litzlan me'hai da'ata* — God should save us from such an opinion! If this particular mat cannot actually be brought to a *mikveh*, what difference does it make that some other object in a vaguely similar category can be? The mat is *tamei* with no exit; it should not be *mishkav*. Rabbi Chanina responds in kind: *rachmana litzlan mi'da'ata didach* — God should save us from *your* opinion. The same exchange appears in Bava Kamma and Ketubot, apparently between the same *amora'im*.

### Competing Verses

The justification for Rabbi Chanina's position is then grounded in a reconciliation of two competing *pesukim*. One *pasuk* — *v'ish asher yiga b'mishkavo* — uses the possessive *mishkavo*, connecting the *mishkav* to the *zav* and requiring that it be like him, capable of *tahara b'mikveh*. A second *pasuk* — *v'chol hamishkav asher yishkav alav hazav yitma* — says simply "any *mishkav* upon which the *zav* lies shall be *tamei*," without the possessive connection, implying a broader category not restricted to objects exactly like him.

*Ha'keitzad* — how do we reconcile the two? The resolution: *yesh be'mino af al pi de'leit lei tahara b'mikveh* — if the object is within the category of things that have *tahara b'mikveh* somewhere in their family, even if this particular instance does not, the broader *pasuk* applies and it can be *mishkav*. *Ein be'mino* — when it is not even in the category, like pottery, the restrictive *pasuk* applies: *mishkavo lo*, it must be like him in being capable of *mikveh* purification. The two *pesukim* thus carve out two distinct regimes: within the *mikveh*-eligible category, the broad *pasuk* expands *mishkav* coverage even to specific objects that cannot themselves be immersed; outside that category entirely, the possessive *pasuk* excludes them.

## Rava's Sealing

### Sealed Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִדְרַס כְּלִי חֶרֶס טָהוֹר מֵהָכָא: ״וְכֹל כְּלִי פָתוּחַ אֲשֶׁר אֵין צָמִיד פָּתִיל עָלָיו״, הָא יֵשׁ צְמִיד פָּתִיל עָלָיו — טָהוֹר הוּא. מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּיַחֲדִינְהוּ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא טָהוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The fact that with regard to impurity imparted by **treading, an earthenware vessel is ritually pure** is derived **from here,** as it is stated: **"And any open vessel that does not have a sealed cover on it** becomes impure" (Numbers 19:15). By inference, if **there is a sealed cover on it, it is pure. Are we not dealing** even with a case **where one designated** the vessel **for** use by **his wife,** when she has the status of a **menstruating woman? And** even so, **the Torah states** that it is **ritually pure?** Apparently, an earthenware vessel with a sealed cover is not subject to impurity from any source.

Rava offers an entirely different derivation for the exclusion of pottery from *midras*. The *pasuk* regarding *tumah* in a house containing a dead body states: *v'chol kli patuach asher ein tzamid patil alav* — any open vessel that does not have a sealed cover over it will become *tamei*, because the *tumah* travels through the airspace and enters the pottery vessel's interior. The corollary — *ha yesh tzamid patil alav, tahor hu* — if the pottery vessel does have a sealed cover, it remains *tahor*.

### Proof via Seal

Rava's argument proceeds as follows: suppose one designated a pottery vessel specifically for use as a seat — set it aside for a *niddah* to sit on, in the manner of a dedicated *mishkav*. Such a vessel should, were it capable of being *mishkav*, be susceptible to *tumah*. An object susceptible to *tumah* cannot serve as a *chatzitzah*, a barrier that blocks *tumah* from entering. Accordingly, a sealed pottery vessel designated for *mishkav* use should not block *tumah* — it should be susceptible to the *tumah* of the *met* in the same room regardless of its seal. Yet the Torah states categorically that a sealed pottery vessel is *tahor*. The only way to reconcile this is to conclude that pottery vessels simply cannot be *mishkav* — they cannot acquire *tumah* from the outside, which is precisely what *mishkav*-*tumah* involves. A sealed pottery vessel is always *tahor* because pottery *tumah* operates exclusively through the interior, and the seal protects the interior completely. This demonstrates that pottery has no *mishkav* category at all.

## Arugah Mishnah

### Bed Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנַּיִן לַעֲרוּגָה שֶׁהִיא שִׁשָּׁה עַל שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים שֶׁזּוֹרְעִין בְּתוֹכָהּ חֲמִשָּׁה זֵרְעוֹנִין, אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת הָעֲרוּגָה וְאַחַת בָּאֶמְצַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי כָאָרֶץ תּוֹצִיא צִמְחָהּ וּכְגַנָּה זֵרוּעֶיהָ תַצְמִיחַ״ — ״זַרְעָהּ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״זֵרוּעֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Gemara continues to discuss an additional *halakha* based on a biblical allusion. **From where** is it derived **that** in **a garden bed that is six by six handbreadths, that one may plant five** different types of **seeds in it?** He may do so without violating the prohibition of sowing a mixture of diverse kinds of seeds in the following manner. One sows **four** types of plants **on** each of **the four sides of the garden bed and one in the middle.** There is an allusion to this in the text, **as it is stated: "For as the earth brings forth its growth, and as a garden causes its seeds to grow,** so will the Lord God cause justice and praise to spring forth before all the nations" (Isaiah 61:11). **Its seed,** in the singular, **is not stated; rather, its seeds,** written in the plural. Apparently, it is possible that several seeds may be planted in a small garden.

The *gemara* now transitions from *taharot* to *zera'im* — a sweeping shift across the landscape of *shas*. The *mishnah* under discussion asks: *minayin le-arugah she-hi shisha al shisha tefachim* — how do we know that a garden bed (*arugah*) measuring six by six *tefachim* may contain five different species of seeds? *V'zorea betochah chamesh zerounin* — four varieties planted at the four sides of the bed, and one in the center: *arba b'arba ruchot ha-arugah v'achat b'emtza*.

### Scripture Derivation

The derivation comes from the *pasuk* in Isaiah: *ki ka'aretz totzi tzimchah uk'ginah zeirueha tatzmiach*. Rav Yehuda breaks the *pasuk* into five components: *totzi* — "it brings forth" — is one; *tzimchah* — "its sprout" — is two; *zeirueha* — "its seeds," a plural — is two more, for a total of four; and *tatzmiach* — "it causes to sprout" — adds a fifth. Together these five lexical elements in the *pasuk* support the *mishnah*'s ruling that five species may coexist in a single *arugah*.

### Rav Yehuda's Breakdown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״כִּי כָאָרֶץ תּוֹצִיא צִמְחָהּ״. ״תּוֹצִיא״ — חַד, ״צִמְחָהּ״ — חַד, הֲרֵי תְּרֵי. ״זֵרוּעֶיהָ״ — תְּרֵי, הָא אַרְבְּעָה. ״תַּצְמִיחַ״ — חַד, הָא חַמְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions this allusion: **From where is it inferred** that the verse refers to five types of seeds? **Rav Yehuda said** that it is derived as follows: **"For as the earth brings forth its growth"** indicates five types of seeds because **"brings forth"** represents **one** and **"its vegetation"** represents **one,** and **that** totals **two. "Its seeds,"** written in the plural, represents at least **two,** and **that** totals **four. "Cause to grow" is one** more. This verse includes terms connoting planting and seeds in a single garden bed that total **five** species of seeds.

### Rashi's Principle

Rashi makes a critical point that frames the entire discussion: by *kilayim*, what matters is not whether the different plants are drawing nutrients from the same soil or even growing in close physical proximity. The defining criterion of *kilayim* is visual separation. Rashi's proof is practical: one may plant different species right up to either side of a fence — the plants are an inch apart, the roots mingle — yet there is no *kilayim* violation, because the fence provides visual separation. The plants look distinct and separated, and that is what the prohibition targets.

### Separation Criteria

Applying this principle to the *arugah*: the four border rows work because adjacent rows run in different directions. One row runs north-to-south, the adjacent row runs east-to-west. Even with only a tiny gap between them, the visual effect of different directions makes them appear as distinct plantings. This directional difference is sufficient for the visual separation *kilayim* requires. The species in the center of the bed, however, are not saved by directional difference — they share the same general orientation as adjacent rows — and must instead be separated by three *tefachim* in all directions. Since the bed is six by six *tefachim*, the center planting achieves that three-*tefach* buffer on each side, at least in broad terms.

### Rashi: Rabbinic Kilayim

One final and significant point: Rashi maintains that all of the foregoing — *kilayim* of non-vine species — is rabbinic in nature. According to Rashi, the only biblical *kilayim* prohibition applicable to planting is *kilayim haKerem*, the prohibition against mixed planting involving the vine. When there is no vine involved, the prohibition against mixing seeds is rabbinic. Tosafot sharply disagrees, arguing that *kilay zra'im* — the mixing of non-vine seeds — is unambiguously biblical. Tosafot concedes that the *gemarot* assign greater weight to *kilayim haKerem* in one specific respect: that the produce of *kilay haKerem* is forbidden for benefit, a stringency that may not apply to *kilay zra'im*. But the prohibition of planting itself, Tosafot maintains, applies biblically to seeds as well as vines. Rashi's position — that non-vine *kilayim* is entirely rabbinic — is thus a distinctive and striking view that will require further exploration as the *gemara* continues.
