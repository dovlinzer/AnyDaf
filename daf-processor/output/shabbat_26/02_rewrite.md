# Shabbat 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzari Oil Debate

### Rava's Aroma Concern

The daf opens at the very bottom of 25b, picking up a dispute regarding the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who rules that one may not use *tzari* — a balsam-derived aromatic oil — for Shabbat lamp lighting. Rava explains the reason: *mitoch she-reicho nodef*, because the oil has an exceptionally powerful aroma. The concern is a form of *gezeirah shema yistapek mimenu* — we are afraid that someone, overcome by the fragrance, will dip their bread into the oil, thereby either extinguishing the lamp directly or causing it to burn for a shorter duration. That indirect extinguishing is *gram kibbuy*, and it is for this reason that we do not permit the use of *tzari*.

### Abaye's Fire Risk

Abaye, at the top of 26a, challenges Rava's rationale. He asks: why frame the prohibition around the pleasant aroma when there is a far more concrete concern — the fact that it *af*, it floats? Apparently, burning *tzari* causes sparks or flaming particles to float upward and adhere to walls and ceilings, with the real danger of setting the entire building on fire. Rashi notes this as the operative concern. Tosafot raises a sharp objection: if that were the true reason, we should prohibit the use of *tzari* even on weekdays, since the fire hazard is equally present then. Tosafot's own resolution is that on Shabbat, the additional problem is that one would be compelled to extinguish the resulting fire, thereby violating *Shabbos*. Nevertheless, the fire-safety concern itself has broader application.

### Double Concern

The *gemara* reconciles the two positions by invoking the principle of *chada ve-od ka-amar* — one reason and another: the prohibition rests on both grounds simultaneously. First, *mipnei shehu af* — because it floats and can ignite surrounding surfaces, a concern that may apply even during the week. Second, *gezeirah shema yistapek mimenu* — because the aroma is so alluring that one might come to dip bread into it. Both concerns reinforce each other and together underlie the prohibition.

### Story: Balsam Fire

To drive home the extreme flammability of balsam oil, the *gemara* relates a cautionary anecdote. A mother-in-law who despised her daughter-in-law advised the younger woman to anoint herself with *mishcha de-afarsimon*, this fragrant balsam oil. After she did so, the mother-in-law sent her to light a lamp. The young woman went to kindle it, the flame caught hold of her, and she was consumed. The story is clearly not presented for emulation, but it powerfully illustrates just how volatile this oil is — and why the rabbis took precautions surrounding its use.

## Tevel & Terumah

### Prohibited Materials

The *gemara* transitions to a *baraita*: *ein madlikin be-tevel tamei be-chol* — one may not use *tevel tamei*, untithed grain that is also ritually impure, even on a weekday. And *ein tzarich lomar b'Shabbat* — certainly not on Shabbat. Similarly, *ein madlikin b'neft b'lavan be-chol* — one may not use white naphtha as lamp fuel even on a weekday, and certainly not on Shabbat. These prohibitions, the *gemara* notes, are clearly not limited to Shabbat concerns alone.

### Tevel vs Terumah

The prohibition on white naphtha is readily explained by the same concern raised earlier: *mipnei shehu af*, it floats and is dangerously flammable. But the prohibition on *tevel tamei* is more puzzling. We learned on the previous daf that *terumah temei'ah* — impure priestly tithe — may be used for lamp fuel, because one may derive benefit from it during the process of its destruction. Since *tevel* is essentially grain that still has *terumah* within it waiting to be separated, why should it be worse than *terumah temei'ah* itself?

### Separation Requirement

The *gemara* answers from a *pasuk*: *va-ani hinei natati lecha et mishmeres terumosai* — "I have given you the guarding of my *terumos*," in the plural. As was discussed previously, the verse speaks of two *terumos* — one *tehorah* and one *temei'ah*. From the *tehorah* we learn that it must be protected and kept separate; from the *temei'ah* we learn it must be burned, and yet one may benefit in the course of that burning. But then comes the central derivation: just as *terumah tehorah* may only be utilized from the moment of its separation — a *kohen* cannot eat *tevel* on the grounds that it contains *terumah* he is entitled to eat, because that entitlement only begins once the *terumah* has actually been lifted off — so too *terumah temei'ah*. The benefit one is permitted during its destruction applies only *mish'as harama v'eilach*, from the moment of separation onward. While the oil still sits within the *tevel* in an undifferentiated state, it is off-limits.

### Benefit in Destruction

This ruling raises a deeper question that Tosafot addresses directly. The argument seems to imply that *tevel* itself is *assur b'hana'ah* — forbidden in benefit — since we needed a special *pasuk* to permit benefit from *terumah temei'ah*, and yet here we learn that even that permission does not extend back to cover *tevel*. But Tosafot clarifies that *tevel* is not *assur b'hana'ah* in the classic sense. One could, for example, use *tevel* as bedding without transgression. What is forbidden is specifically the *hana'ah* that comes through the process of destroying it — one may not burn *tevel* as fuel or feed it to animals in a destructive way. Interestingly, then, *terumah temei'ah* and *tevel* emerge as something like mirror images of one another: *terumah temei'ah* is generally *assur b'hana'ah*, with one narrow exception permitting benefit during destruction; *tevel* is generally permitted in benefit, with one narrow exception forbidding benefit that arises in the course of destroying it.

## Shimon ben Elazar

### Tzari Source

The *gemara* returns with a *gufa* — returning to address the body of the ruling of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. He prohibited *tzari* for the reasons already discussed. But Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar also made an independent statement clarifying what *tzari* actually is: *tzari eino ela saraf mei'atzei hakataf* — *tzari* is the sap that exudes from the wood of the balsam tree. This is the same substance mentioned in the *ketoret* passage recited in *davening*. Some understand this identification not merely as botanical information but as the operative reason for the prohibition: sap, as distinct from oil, does not burn smoothly and does not draw well after the wick. Tosafot, however, notes that if this were the *pshat*, there would be no need for the other reasons — the aroma concern and the flammability concern — and it is unclear why the *gemara* would have constructed those explanations at all. In any case, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar establishes that *tzari* is a tree-derived sap, not a conventional oil.

## Lighting Oil Positions

### Yishmael Position

The *gemara* now surveys a range of *tannaitic* opinions on which oils may and may not be used for Shabbat lighting, revealing a clear progressive tendency to restrict. Rabbi Yishmael rules: *kol hayotzei min ha-etz ein madlikin bo* — anything derived from a tree, meaning not from fruit but from the wood or other non-fruit parts of the plant, may not be used for lighting. The presumed reason is that such substances do not burn cleanly and do not draw properly after the wick.

### Yishmael b. Broka

Rabbi Yishmael ben Broka goes further still: *ein madlikin ela beyotzei min hapri* — one may only use something that comes from fruit. This excludes not only tree-derived saps but also, for example, fish oil. Sesame oil and olive oil, deriving from fruits of their respective plants, would remain permitted, but animal products and saps would not.

### Tarfon Position

Rabbi Tarfon takes the most restrictive position: *ein madlikin ela beshemen zayit bilvad* — only olive oil, and nothing else. The progression from no restriction to some restriction to fruit-only to olive-oil-only represents a steady escalation, with each opinion narrowing the range of acceptable materials.

### Yochanan Response

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka rises to respond to this trend. His argument is essentially one of practical leadership: *mah ya'asu anshei Bavel she-ein lahem ela shemen shumshumin* — what are the people of Babylonia to do, who have only sesame oil? *U-mah ya'asu anshei Madai* — what of the people of Media, who have only nut oil? *U-mah ya'asu anshei Alexandria* — what of Alexandria, where only gourd oil is available? *U-mah ya'asu anshei Kapotkia* — and what of Cappadocia, where they have neither this nor that, but only *neft*, naphtha? One may be personally stringent, but a rabbinic leader bears responsibility for *kol Klal Yisrael*, and a ruling that leaves entire communities unable to fulfill *hadlakat nerot Shabbat* is untenable. Rabbi Yochanan therefore concludes: *ein lecha ela mah shemanu chachamim ein madlikin* — the only materials that are forbidden are those enumerated by the early sages in their established list.

This is in fact the *stam* position of the Mishnah itself, which after listing prohibited wicks and oils turns to note that the *chachamim* permit all the oils — sesame, nut, gourd, fish, *paku'ot*, *itran*, and *neft* — precisely the same oils defended by Rabbi Yochanan. The successive generations of *tannaim* attempting to add prohibitions are pushing against an earlier, more permissive consensus, and Rabbi Tarfon's stringency, however personally compelling, cannot be sustained as a communal ruling.

### Shezuri Clarification

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri similarly affirms the permissibility of gourd oil and *neft* — again, materials explicitly permitted in the Mishnah by the *chachamim* — moving in the direction of expansion rather than restriction.

### Sumchos Position

Sumchos states: *kol hayotzei min habasar ein madlikin bo ela beshemen dagim* — anything that derives from animal flesh may not be used for lighting, with the sole exception of fish oil. This effectively permits fish oil while excluding rendered animal fat, which had already been disallowed by the earlier Mishnaic list.

### Oil Consensus

The *gemara* immediately questions whether Sumchos adds anything new: *Sumchus hainu Tanna Kamma* — is this not simply the same as the *tanna kamma*? The prohibition on *chelev*, liquefied fat, was already established by the earlier sages, so what does Sumchos contribute? The *gemara* resolves that the practical difference involves the ruling of Rav Bruna in the name of Rav — namely, the question of whether mixing a permissible oil with a non-permissible animal product renders the mixture acceptable. The two opinions would differ on that point, though the *gemara* notes *v'lo mesaimei* — we cannot determine which opinion goes which way. What emerges overall is a picture of *tannaim* pulling in two directions: some toward expansion of prohibitions, others — culminating in the broad ruling of the *chachamim* — affirming a wide range of permissible oils for communities across the diaspora.

## Shimon ben Elazar…

### Plant Materials Tumah

The *gemara* returns to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and presents a *baraita* that contains two rulings together: *kol hayotzei min ha-etz ein bo mishum shalosh al shalosh u-masachin bo chutz mi-pishtan* — anything derived from a tree carries no *tumah* of three-by-three, and it may be used as *schach*, with the exception of linen.

The first ruling requires some unpacking. In the laws of garment *tumah*, there are two relevant sizes: three by three *etzba'ot* — finger-widths — which is a very small swath, perhaps three inches by three inches, significant only to someone poor enough to use it as a patch; and three by three *tefachim* — handbreadths — which is a substantially larger piece, perhaps nine inches by nine inches or more, something that even a wealthy person would find a use for and that could function as an actual garment. Here Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says that materials derived from plants — *Rashi* interprets this to include canvas, cotton, and similar plant-derived fibers — do not become *tamei* even at the smaller size of three by three fingers. These are not classic garment materials. The classic materials are wool and linen, *tzemar u-fishtim*, and only those carry the full range of garment *tumah*.

### Plant Materials Schach

The second ruling follows naturally. For *schach* to be valid, two conditions must be met: the material must grow from the ground, and it must not be *mekabel tumah* — susceptible to becoming *tamei*. Plant-derived materials satisfy both criteria. They grow from the ground, and as we have just established, they do not become *tamei* as garments. Therefore they are entirely valid for use as *schach*.

The one explicit exception is linen, *pishtan*. Linen does derive from a plant, but it is itself a paradigmatic garment material — it is the *pishtan* of the Torah's repeated formulation *beged tzemar o beged pishtim*. Because linen functions as a classic cloth, it is considered *mekabel tumah* in the full sense, and therefore it may not be used as *schach*.

## Garment Tumah Laws

### Wool & Linen Only

Abaye now notes that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* are expressing the same fundamental position: *amru davar echad* — they said the same thing. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling, just cited, establishes that plant-derived materials are not subject to garment *tumah*. The *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* arrives at the same principle from a different direction.

The *baraita* of the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* reasons as follows: the Torah speaks of *begadim* — garments — in many contexts without qualification. *Rashi* lists numerous examples: garments that become *tamei* through contact with a dead rodent, through a corpse, through a seminal emission, and of course in the context of *tzitzit*. In all these places the Torah simply says *beged* without specifying material. One might therefore assume that any material used for clothing qualifies. But in one location — the laws of *tzara'at* — the Torah specifies: *beged tzemar o beged pishtim*, a garment of wool or a garment of linen. Rather than reading this as a limitation applicable only to *tzara'at*, the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* reads it as the Torah's definitive paradigm: whenever the Torah says *beged*, it means something made of wool or linen. *Mah halan tzemar u-fishtim af kol tzemar u-fishtim* — just as there it means wool and linen, so everywhere it means wool and linen.

So according to Abaye, both Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* hold that if a material is not wool or linen, it simply does not qualify as a garment for any *halachic* purpose — regardless of size.

Rava disagrees with Abaye's reading. He argues that the two positions are not identical: *shlosha al shlosha bishe'ar begadim ika beineihu* — they differ with respect to a large piece, three by three *tefachim*, made from non-classic material. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, Rava contends, only said that the *small* size — three by three fingers — does not become *tamei* when made from non-wool and non-linen materials. This implies that a large piece of three by three *tefachim* of such material might well count as a garment. The *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael*, by contrast, excludes all non-wool and non-linen material absolutely, at every size.

### Minimum Size

With the positions framed, the *gemara* asks the foundational question: how do we know that even a small swath — three by three finger-widths — of wool or linen becomes *tamei*? The Torah mentions *beged* without specifying a minimum size. The *baraita* answers from the phrase *v'habeged* — "and the garment" — the *vav* of inclusion suggesting that even something less than a full garment, even a fragment used as a patch, is included.

But the *gemara* presses: perhaps *v'habeged* comes to expand the size, not reduce it. Perhaps it includes three by three *tefachim* of non-classic material, rather than three by three fingers of classic material. The *gemara* responds that the phrase *beged tzemar o beged pishtim* already establishes clearly that the material must be wool or linen — there is no room to use *v'habeged* to expand material. The expansion of *v'habeged* must therefore operate along a different axis: it extends downward in size, reaching even the small swath of three by three fingers that only a poor person would find worth keeping.

But why is the larger size, three by three *tefachim*, not already derived from a *kal v'chomer*? The *gemara* explains: the Torah by *tzara'at* introduces the extraordinary rule that even unformed warp thread and unfinished woof thread — *shesi v'erev* — become *tamei*, even though they have not yet been woven into any garment at all. This is unique to *tzara'at* and does not apply to other forms of *tumah*. From this we can construct a *kal v'chomer*: if unworked threads with no garment form whatsoever can become *tamei*, then certainly a piece that has already been woven into a cloth large enough for anyone — rich or poor — to find use for should become *tamei*. Three by three *tefachim* is precisely such a piece: it can serve as a scarf, a head-covering, or any number of small garments, and a wealthy person would not discard it. Therefore, three by three *tefachim* of wool or linen becoming *tamei* requires no *pasuk*; it follows from a *kal v'chomer* from *shesi v'erev*.

### Larger Sizes

The *gemara* then confirms: three by three fingers, however, cannot be derived by *kal v'chomer*. A swath that small is only valued by the poor as a patch; a wealthy person throws it away. It lacks the universal significance needed to sustain the inference from *shesi v'erev*, which — whatever its limitations — at least consists of material capable of being woven into something of real value. Therefore the *gemara* says explicitly: *ta'ama de-chtav kra, ha lo chtav kra lo gamrinan b'kal v'chomer* — the reason we know three by three fingers is only because the Torah wrote *v'habeged*. Had that phrase not appeared, we could not have derived it by logical inference.

Now the *gemara* asks the reverse question. The Torah writes *beged tzemar o beged pishtim* twice — once as *beged tzemar u-fishtim* and once as *beveged tzemar o beveged pishtim*. Two distinct expressions of wool and linen. *Terei miutei ktivi* — there are two exclusions written. One comes to exclude non-classic materials at the small size of three by three fingers, and one comes to exclude them even at the larger size of three by three *tefachim*. Together, these two exclusions seal off any route to including non-wool and non-linen garments, however large.

All of this analysis, the *gemara* notes, operates by the laws of *tzara'at* first. Once established there, the principles are extended to other areas of *tumah* as well — though the mechanism of that extension becomes the next debate.

### Material Debate

The *gemara* now turns to the broader structural question that follows from Rava's reading of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. Rava maintains that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* do in fact differ: the latter holds that non-classic materials never count, while the former holds that at a larger size — three by three *tefachim* — they do count. For Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, where does this inclusion of non-classic material at larger sizes come from? The *gemara* answers: *nafka me'o beged* — from the phrase *o beged*, "or a garment," which appears in the *tzara'at* passage and which is read as an inclusive extension, reaching beyond the paradigm to embrace other types of material when the size is sufficient.

It should be noted that this debate has significant implications well beyond the realm of *tumah*. One prominent application is *tzitzit*: is a full garment made of cotton obligated in *tzitzit* biblically? This is a major *machlokes* in the *gemara*, among the *rishonim*, and in how we *pasken*. That question turns on precisely this issue — whether non-wool, non-linen garments count as *begadim* when they are of full garment size, which is exactly what Rava and Abaye are debating in their respective readings of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.

### Abaye Position

Abaye rejects Rava's reading. According to Abaye, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar agrees with the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* that non-classic materials do not count regardless of size. On this reading, what does Abaye do with the phrase *o beged*? He explains: *mibayei lei le-rabot shalosh al shalosh b'tzemer u-fishtim de-mitamei b'shratzim*. We have established everything we need to know from *tzara'at* in its own right — wool and linen count, even small sizes, based on *v'habeged* and the *kal v'chomer* from *shesi v'erev*. But all of that was derived within *tzara'at*. How do we know that this same definition of garment extends to *all* other forms of *tumah* — to the *tumah* of *shratzim* (dead rodents), for example? That is what *o beged* teaches: the *tzara'at* definition of garment is the universal definition of garment across the Torah. The phrase does not expand the materials; it applies the size-and-material framework of *tzara'at* to every other context of *tumah*.

### Rava Position

Rava's counter-reading is that the *tzara'at* garment laws automatically serve as a paradigm for all other areas of *tumah* without needing a special phrase for that purpose: *galei Rachmana gabei negaim v'hu hadin le-shratzim* — the Torah reveals it by *nega'im* and the same rule applies to *shratzim* by inference.

Abaye objects: *ika le-mifrach mah le-negaim she-ken shesi v'erev metamei bahem* — you cannot draw a straightforward inference from *nega'im* to other areas of *tumah*, because *nega'im* is a stricter category. One of the very derivations we used within *nega'im* — the *kal v'chomer* about three by three *tefachim* — relied on the fact that *shesi v'erev* becomes *tamei* in *nega'im*, which is true nowhere else. Since *nega'im* has this distinctive stringency, we cannot assume its rules apply elsewhere without a dedicated verse. *O beged*, therefore, is needed to make that extension.

Rava responds: if *nega'im* were genuinely stricter such that we could not apply it elsewhere, then the Torah should have written the rule by *shratzim* — the more lenient category — and allowed us to derive *nega'im* from there. The fact that the Torah wrote it by *nega'im* and not by *shratzim* tells us that *nega'im* is not the stricter case, and we can in fact derive the other areas from it. *O beged*, then, is not needed for extension; it is free to teach the inclusion of non-classic materials at larger sizes.

Abaye replies that the Torah could not have written it by *shratzim* and derived *nega'im* from there either — because *shratzim* has its own stringency: *shratzim* cause *tumah* even with a piece the size of a lentil, *adasha*, which is smaller than any relevant garment threshold. Each category has its own distinctive stringencies, and therefore each requires its own explicit verse. Neither *shratzim* nor *nega'im* could have served as the source for the other. Consequently, according to Abaye: *o beged* extends the *tzara'at* framework to all other *tumah* contexts, and it leaves no room to include non-classic materials even at larger sizes. The conclusion is absolute: only wool and linen count as garments.

To summarize the final position of each authority: Rabbi Yishmael (the *tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael*) holds clearly that only wool and linen are *begadim* for any *halachic* purpose, regardless of size. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is more cryptic. Abaye reads him as agreeing with Rabbi Yishmael: nothing but wool and linen counts. Rava reads him as drawing a distinction: a small swath of three by three fingers counts only if it is wool or linen, but a large piece of three by three *tefachim* counts even from non-classic plant materials, because *o beged* includes such material at significant size. This debate, with its implications for *tzitzit* and other areas of *halacha*, will continue on the following daf.
