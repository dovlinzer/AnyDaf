# Avodah Zarah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Abaye: Marvach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַשְׁאִילָן וְלִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן כּוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְהַשְׁאִילָן — דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְהוּ, אֲבָל לִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן — מַעוֹטֵי קָא מְמַעֵט לְהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּזֵרָה לִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן אַטּוּ לְהַשְׁאִילָן. רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that it is prohibited **to lend them items and to borrow items from them** during the three days preceding their festivals. The Gemara asks: **Granted, it is prohibited to lend the items to them, as this causes them to have a profit. But why is it prohibited to borrow items from them** during this period? Doesn't this serve to **reduce for them the property they possess during the festival?** **Abaye said:** The Sages issued **a decree that it is prohibited to borrow items from them due to the concern that he might come to lend items to them.** **Rava said: All of it,** lending and borrowing, **is because the gentile might go and give thanks** to his idol, as he will be pleased that the Jew was forced to borrow items from him.

The daf picks up at the bottom of 6b, where the Gemara has been surveying the various categories of forbidden business interactions with non-Jews. Abaye holds that the primary concern animating these prohibitions is *marvach*—transactions that give the non-Jew greater resources and increase. Lending money or objects to a non-Jew falls squarely within this concern. What about the reverse direction, such as borrowing from them or compelling them to pay up a debt? Those too are forbidden, but as a *gezeira*: one direction is prohibited in order to prevent the other. The core problem, for Abaye, is anything that enlarges the non-Jew's holdings.

### Rava: Azol uModeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַלְווֹתָם וְלִלְווֹת מֵהֶן. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְהַלְווֹתָם — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְהוּ, אֶלָּא לִלְווֹת מֵהֶן — אַמַּאי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּזֵרָה לִלְווֹת מֵהֶן אַטּוּ לְהַלְווֹתָם. רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that it is prohibited **to lend money to them or to borrow money from them. Granted, it is prohibited to lend money to them, as this causes them to have a profit. But if one wants to borrow money from them, why is it prohibited?** **Abaye said:** The Sages issued **a decree that it is prohibited to borrow money from them, due to the concern that he might come to lend money to them.** **Rava said: All of it,** lending and borrowing money, is prohibited for the same reason, **as** in either situation the gentile **will go and give thanks** to his object of idol worship.

Rava takes a different view. For him, both directions of transaction are forbidden for the same reason: the concern of *azol umodeh*, that as a result of the interaction the non-Jew will go and give thanks to his deity. Rashi sharpened this concern by pointing to the verse *lo yishamah al picha*—that expressions of thanksgiving to a pagan god should not occur through your agency. A third and more concrete concern running through the entire *mishnah* is *lifnei iver*, the prohibition of providing a non-Jew the objects of his worship.

## Debt Collection Debate

### Yehuda: Debt Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפוֹרְעָן וְלִפְרוֹעַ מֵהֶן כּוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְפוֹרְעָן — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְהוּ, אֶלָּא לִפְרוֹעַ מֵהֶן — מַעוֹטֵי מְמַעֵט לְהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּזִירָה לִפְרוֹעַ מֵהֶן אַטּוּ לְפוֹרְעָן. רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also teaches that it is prohibited **to repay debts owed to them and to collect payment of their debts. Once again, the Gemara asks: Granted, it is prohibited to repay debts owed to them, as giving them the money at this time causes them to have a profit. But why is it prohibited to collect payment of their debts?** Doesn't this serve to **reduce their fortune?** **Abaye said:** The Sages issued **a decree that it is prohibited to collect debts from them, due to the concern that he might come to repay their debts.** **Rava said: All of it,** repaying and collecting debts, is prohibited for the same reason, **as** in either situation the gentile might **go and give thanks** to his idol for having had sufficient funds to pay his debts.

With this framework in place, the Gemara turns to a specific case. Rabbi Yehuda permits collecting a debt from a non-Jew even on the eve of his holiday, reasoning that compelling repayment causes the non-Jew distress rather than joy, and therefore does not implicate the concern of *simcha*. The *Chachamim* disagree: even though the non-Jew suffers an immediate loss, in the long run he will be relieved that the debt is no longer hanging over him, and that eventual satisfaction constitutes the prohibited joy.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן כּוּ׳. וְלֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה ״אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמֵּיצֵר עַכְשָׁיו שָׂמֵחַ הוּא לְאַחַר זְמַן״?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says: One may collect the repayment of debts from them,** because this causes the gentile distress. The Gemara asks: **And doesn't Rabbi Yehuda accept the principle that even though he is distressed now, he will be happy afterward?**

The Gemara then presses Rabbi Yehuda: does he not accept the principle that even if something causes immediate pain, one looks at the larger picture and recognizes it will produce joy in the end? The Gemara locates evidence that Rabbi Yehuda does accept precisely this macro perspective—but in an entirely different context.

### Chol HaMoed Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִשָּׁה לֹא תָּסוּד בַּמּוֹעֵד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּיוּוּל הוּא לָהּ. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּסִיד שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְקַפְּלוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד, שֶׁטּוֹפַלְתּוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּצֵירָה עַכְשָׁיו, שְׂמֵחָה הִיא לְאַחַר זְמַן!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Yehuda says: A woman may not apply lime to her skin during the intermediate days of the Festival in order to remove bodily hair and soften her skin, because this temporarily disfigures her.** **And Rabbi Yehuda concedes with regard to lime that she can peel off during the intermediate days of the Festival that she may apply it on the intermediate days of the Festival, as even though she is distressed now, as the lime renders her unattractive, she will be happy afterward,** when the lime is removed and she becomes more attractive. It is evident from this *baraita* that Rabbi Yehuda does take into account the joy that will be experienced at a later time with regard to permitting an action now.

The parallel case comes from the laws of *Chol HaMoed*. A *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehuda holds a woman may not apply a lime plaster to her face on *Chol HaMoed*, because it is a *nival*—a de-beautifying—for her during the festival, and looking unkempt is not appropriate for the spirit of *Chol HaMoed*. Yet the Gemara then notes that Rabbi Yehuda concedes in the case of a plaster that can be peeled off (*kaflo*) during *Chol HaMoed* itself: although right now while it is on her face she looks unpleasant and that immediate condition is not fitting for the festival, once she peels it off she will look attractive, and *af al pi shemetzar achshav sameach hi le'achar zman*—even though it causes distress now, she will be happy afterward. The macro perspective thus governs, and the use of such a plaster is permitted.

The logic maps onto the debt collection question: just as Rabbi Yehuda looks past the present discomfort to the later joy in the *Chol HaMoed* case, why does he not do the same for the non-Jew who has just had his debt collected and will, in the long run, feel relieved?

### Rashi's Distinction

Rashi addresses this question by observing that everything permitted on *Chol HaMoed* involves activities that cause some momentary effort or inconvenience—*shechita*, meal preparation, and similar tasks—but ultimately serve the purpose of *simcha* during the festival itself. The macro logic is built into the structure of *Chol HaMoed*: you look past the present exertion because the entire enterprise is oriented toward joy within the festival period. Rashi's language is *kol mah sheheitiru la'asot bemoed metzar be'oto sha'ah...veyihyeh mishmetzar simcha le'achar zman shebetoch hamoed*—whatever they permitted to do on *Chol HaMoed* causes some distress at that moment, but leads to joy later within the festival itself. The analogy breaks down in the case of debt collection because the non-Jew's ultimate relief will not necessarily materialize within the days of his actual holiday; the timing does not align.

### Rav Nachman's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הַנַּח לְהִלְכוֹת מוֹעֵד, דְּכוּלְּהוּ מֵיצֵר עַכְשָׁיו, (שְׂמֵחָה) [שָׂמַח] לְאַחַר זְמַן. רָבִינָא אָמַר: גּוֹי לְעִנְיַן פֵּרָעוֹן לְעוֹלָם מֵיצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says in response: Leave aside the *halakhot* of the intermediate days of a Festival.** These cannot be compared to other cases, **as** with regard to **all the labors permitted on a Festival this is the reason for the leniency: Although he is distressed by performing them now, as they involve effort and trouble, he will be happy afterward** on the Festival itself that he has performed them, when he enjoys the benefits of the labor he has performed. Due to the joy they will bring him on the Festival, these labors are permitted. **Ravina said** that there is a different answer: Rabbi Yehuda maintains that **with regard to repaying a debt a gentile is always distressed,** even after the fact. But in general, Rabbi Yehuda does take into account the joy that will be experienced at a later time.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a more structural explanation. On *Chol HaMoed* the macro perspective is the default framework universally applied—*hanei dechol hamoed dekulei alma meitzari achshav sameichi le'achar zman*—everything permitted during the festival follows that logic. But the laws governing transactions with non-Jews around their holidays may operate from a fundamentally different starting point, one that looks at the immediate and the micro rather than the larger picture.

### Ravina's Pessimism

Ravina takes yet another approach, one that is frankly pessimistic about the non-Jewish disposition. He argues that non-Jews harbor a persistent and abiding resentment—*eimat yeteirah leolam meitzar*—such that even long-term they will never genuinely feel satisfaction at having had the debt paid off. They will always have wished they had found some way to escape the obligation. On this reading, Rabbi Yehuda's position is not that he rejects the macro principle; it is that he simply does not believe the long-term *simcha* will ever materialize in this case.

## Yehoshua ben Korcha

### Documented vs. Oral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: מִלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר אֵין נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן, מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמַצִּיל מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa,** as it states that one may not collect payment from a gentile during the three days preceding their festivals, without differentiating between various cases. **As it is taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** In the case of **a loan with a promissory note, one may not collect payment from** gentiles before their festivals, as one can demand repayment of the debt by presenting the promissory note in his possession at a later stage. By contrast, in the case of **a loan by oral agreement, one may collect payment from them, because he is considered as one who salvages money from them,** since he has no promissory note and cannot be sure that the gentile will repay the loan at another time.

The Gemara now notes that the *mishnah*'s position—which prohibits collecting even debts before the holiday—does not accord with the view of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha. A *baraita* records his distinction: *milveh b'shtar ein nifrain meihen, milveh al peh nifrain meihen mipnei shehu k'matzil mi'yadam*—a documented loan (*milveh b'shtar*) may not be collected before their holiday, but an undocumented oral loan (*milveh al peh*) may be collected, because in that case it is as though one is rescuing one's assets from their hands. Without documentation the creditor is always at risk: the debtor could simply deny the debt. Collecting while one has the chance is therefore an act of financial self-preservation.

### Halacha k'Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב יוֹסֵף אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא, וְיָתֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Yosef sat behind Rabbi Abba in the study hall, and Rabbi Abba sat before Rav Huna,** as a student before his teacher. **And** Rav Huna **sat and said the following statements: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa, and the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.**

Rav Yehuda was sitting behind Rav Yabba, who was sitting before Rav Huna. Rav Huna declared: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha*—we rule like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha that collection of an undocumented debt is permitted. *Ve'halacha k'Rav Yehuda*—we also rule like Rav Yehuda in the dyer case, to be discussed below.

An additional factor supporting the ruling regarding undocumented debts is *meiti u'masur*—the ease with which the debtor could simply deny or escape the obligation. Where that risk is present, not only is there less *simcha* for the non-Jew in being compelled to pay, but the creditor's need to protect himself takes on added weight. The concern of *eivah*—hatred generated by Jewish non-engagement with economic obligations—is also implicitly in the background as a permitting factor.

## Dyer Liability Case

### Dyer Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — דְּתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹתֵן צֶמֶר לַצַבָּע לִצְבּוֹעַ לוֹ אָדוֹם וּצְבָעוֹ שָׁחוֹר, שָׁחוֹר וּצְבָעוֹ אָדוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** As for the statement that the *halakha* is **in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda,** this is **as it is taught in a mishna:** In the case of **one who gives wool to a dyer to dye it red for him and instead he dyed it black, or one who gives wool to a dyer to dye it black and instead he dyed it red,**

The Gemara now turns to the case of the dyer, which is not directly related to the laws of *avodah zarah* but is brought here because Rav Huna declared it in the same sitting. The *mishnah* in Bava Kamma presents the case of *tzavar l'tzava*—one who gives wool to a dyer with instructions to dye it a particular color, and the dyer dyes it the wrong color.

### Meir: Gazlan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי צַמְרוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הַשֶּׁבַח יָתֵר עַל הַיְּצִיאָה — נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַיְּצִיאָה, וְאִם הַיְּצִיאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח — נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says:** The dyer **gives the owner of the wool the value of his wool.** Since the dyer deviated from the owner's wishes, he is considered akin to a robber who acquires the stolen item by changing it. Therefore, like a robber he keeps the changed item and pays the owner its original value. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The dyer does not acquire the wool; rather, the owner of the wool must reimburse the dyer for his expenses, without losing out himself. **If the value of the enhancement, i.e., the enhanced value of the wool, exceeds the dyer's expenses,** the owner of the wool **gives the dyer the expenses. And if the expenses exceed the enhancement, he gives him the value of the enhancement.**

Rabbi Meir holds that because the dyer changed the object contrary to the owner's instructions, he has effectively taken wrongful possession: he is a *gazlan*. As a *gazlan* he acquires the wool and must reimburse the owner the full value of the wool as it was before dyeing.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. The dyer acted without intent to steal; he was simply working under instructions he failed to follow. He therefore does not become a *gazlan*, and the wool remains the property of the owner—now dyed the wrong color. Because the dyer failed to follow the owner's instructions, the owner holds the upper hand. He does not owe the contracted fee. Instead: *im ha'shevach yeteir al ha'yetzia notein lo et ha'yetzia*—if the improvement in the wool's value exceeds the dyer's expenses, the owner pays only the expenses. *Ve'im ha'yetzia yeteirah al ha'shevach notein lo et ha'shevach*—if the expenses exceed the improvement, the owner pays only the improvement. The owner pays the lesser of the two, and the dyer bears the loss.

To give a concrete example: if the contracted fee was one hundred, but the dyer's actual expenses (including labor, stripped of profit) came to fifty, and the wool's value increased by eighty, the owner pays fifty. If the wool's value increased by only thirty despite the dyer's expenses of fifty, the owner pays thirty. The *shinui*—the unauthorized change—forfeits the dyer's right to his contractual fee and leaves him with only the lesser of cost or benefit.

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְאַפֵּיהּ. בִּשְׁלָמָא הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: יָחִיד וְרַבִּים הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּים, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הֲלָכָה כְּיָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef turned his face away** to demonstrate his displeasure with Rav Huna's comment. The Gemara explains why Rav Yosef was unhappy: **Granted, his ruling that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa was necessary,** as it might **enter your mind to say that because this is a dispute between an individual and the many,** the ***halakha* should be in accordance with the opinion of the many,** not in accordance with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa. Rav Huna therefore **teaches us** that in this case the ***halakha* is in accordance with** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa despite the fact that he is **an individual.**

When Rav Yosef heard Rav Huna declare *ve'halacha k'Rav Yehuda*, he turned his face around in surprise. He understood why Rav Huna needed to rule explicitly like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: that was a minority opinion against the *mishnah*, a *daat yachid*, and one might otherwise assume *yachid verabbim halacha kerabbim*—when an individual opinion stands against the majority, the halacha follows the majority. So there it was necessary to announce the ruling explicitly. But why was it necessary to state that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda in the dyer case? That should be self-evident.

### Stam Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְמָה לִי? פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּמַחְלוֹקֶת וְאַחַר כָּךְ סְתָם — הִלְכְתָא כִּסְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **But why do I need the statement that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda?** It is **obvious that this is the case, as** there is a well-known principle **that** whenever there is **a dispute in a mishna and afterward** one opinion is presented as the ruling of **an unattributed** mishna, i.e., without attribution to a particular Sage or that the ruling is subject to debate, **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion presented in the **unattributed** mishna.

The reason it should be obvious, Rav Yosef continues, is the principle of *machlokes ve'achar kach stam halacha kestam*: when a dispute appears in the *mishnah* and is followed by an anonymous, unattributed *mishnah* that sides with one position, the *halacha* follows the anonymous ruling. Here, the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda appears in Bava Kamma, but Bava Metzia contains an anonymous *mishnah* stating *kol hameshane yado al hatachtona*—whoever deviates from the terms of an agreement is in the disadvantaged position—and *kol hachozer bo yado al hatachtona*—whoever backs out of an agreement is similarly disadvantaged. That *stam mishnah* clearly encodes Rabbi Yehuda's principle: the party who deviated does not collect the contracted price but only the lesser of expenses or improvement. Since Bava Metzia follows Bava Kamma, this is a case of debate followed by a later anonymous ruling, and the *halacha* should follow the *stam* without any need for explicit declaration.

### Mishna Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּבָבָא קַמָּא, וּסְתָם בְּבָבָא מְצִיעָא, דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַמְשַׁנֶּה — יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, וְכׇּל הַחוֹזֵר בּוֹ — יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that here the ruling of the unattributed mishna appears after the dispute, as the **dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir appears in** tractate ***Bava Kamma*, and** the **unattributed mishna appears in *Bava Metzia*,** which is the next tractate in the order of the Mishna. **As we learned in a mishna:** **Whoever changes the terms accepted by both parties is at a disadvantage, and whoever reneges on an agreement is at a disadvantage.** This statement is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that a craftsman who deviates from his assignment is at a disadvantage, as he receives only the expense or the enhancement, whichever is worth less.

Rav Yosef raises this question, but the answer given is that Rav Huna would invoke the principle of *ein seder laMishnah*—the *mishnayot* do not necessarily appear in the order in which they were composed or taught. The fact that the dispute appears in Bava Kamma and the *stam* in Bava Metzia does not necessarily mean the *stam* postdates the dispute. Perhaps the *stam* was composed first and the explicit debate was formulated later; perhaps Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi placed them in this sequence for organizational reasons that do not reflect the intended hierarchical relationship. If the order of composition is uncertain, one cannot derive from the position of the *stam* that it was meant to resolve the earlier debate.

### Within vs. Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין סֵדֶר לַמִּשְׁנָה, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר סְתָם תְּנָא בְּרֵישָׁא, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת. אִי הָכִי, כֹּל מַחְלוֹקֶת וְאַחַר כָּךְ סְתָם — לֵימָא אֵין סֵדֶר לַמִּשְׁנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why did Rav Huna feel it necessary to state explicitly that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda? It was necessary because Rav Huna holds that the Mishna is not sequential,** and therefore it is not clear that the mishna in *Bava Kamma* precedes the mishna in *Bava Metzia*. Consequently, **it can be said that in fact this is a case of an unattributed mishna that is taught first, and afterward** appears the **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir. The Gemara challenges: **If that is so, that the Mishna is not sequential, then in every case of a dispute** that is **afterward followed by an unattributed mishna, let us say** that **the Mishna is not sequential.**

The Gemara presses: if one can never infer anything from the order of *mishnayot*, the entire principle of *machlokes ve'achar kach stam* would collapse. Rav Huna clarifies: *ein seder laMishnah* applies only across separate *masechtos*, not within one *masechta*. Within a single tractate, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi organized the material with enough intention that the sequence is meaningful, and a *stam mishnah* appearing after a dispute within the same tractate does signal that we rule like the *stam*. But between two separate tractates, the organizational logic does not carry the same implication.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא? כִּי לָא אָמְרִינַן אֵין סֵדֶר — בַּחֲדָא מַסֶּכְתָּא, בִּתְרֵי מַסְּכָתֵי אָמְרִינַן. וְרַב יוֹסֵף: כּוּלָּהּ נְזִיקִין חֲדָא מַסֶּכְתָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And Rav Huna? How would he respond to this claim? He would say: When do we not say that the Mishna is non-sequential? The Mishna is considered sequential when both *mishnayot* appear in one tractate, but when they are in two different tractates, we do say that the Mishna is not sequential,** and it is unclear which one was taught last. Therefore, in this case, as each mishna is found in a different tractate, one in *Bava Kamma* and the other in *Bava Metzia*, one cannot say for certain which was taught first. **And how would Rav Yosef respond?** He would say: **All of** tractate ***Nezikin*, i.e., *Bava Kamma*, *Bava Metzia*, and *Bava Batra*, is** considered **one tractate,** and therefore its internal order of *mishnayot* is sequential.

Rav Yosef responds: Bava Kamma, Bava Metzia, and Bava Basra are not truly three separate *masechtos*—*kulan Nezikin chada masechta hu*. They constitute one large thirty-chapter tractate that was divided into three for practical reasons. If the debate appears in Bava Kamma and the *stam* appears in Bava Metzia, they are within one masechta for these purposes, and the sequence should be meaningful. On this view, there was indeed no need to state the ruling explicitly. But on the other view—that Bava Kamma and Bava Metzia are considered distinct tractates for the purposes of sequence—the order carries no implication, and Rav Huna's explicit declaration was necessary.

### Halacha Pesuka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי לַהּ גַּבֵּי הִלְכָתָא פְּסִיקָתָא: כׇּל הַמְשַׁנֶּה — יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, וְכׇּל הַחוֹזֵר בּוֹ — יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say instead that Rav Yosef maintained that it was not necessary to say that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, because his ruling is taught amid** other **decided *halakhot*: Whoever changes the terms accepted by both parties is at a disadvantage, and whoever reneges on an agreement is at a disadvantage,** i.e., this statement is unrelated to the subject matter of the chapter in which it appears. Consequently, it is evidently the accepted *halakha* and therefore Rav Huna's statement was unnecessary.

The Gemara offers a second resolution, independent of the question of masechta order. Even if one concedes that Bava Kamma and Bava Metzia are separate tractates and the sequence carries no implication, it should still have been obvious that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda—because of the way the *stam mishnah* in Bava Metzia is framed. *Mishum de'katani legabbei hilchata pesikta*: the ruling is presented as *halacha pesuka*, an apodictic legal principle stated in tight, absolute terms. *Kol hameshane yado al hatachtona, kol hachozer bo yado al hatachtona*: this is not a case study where the *mishnah* works through a particular scenario and attributes an answer to a named authority. It is framed as a broad *klal*, a general principle stated in the terse declarative language of binding law. When the *mishnah* speaks that way, it signals consensus and finality.

Rashi and Tosafot understand this point somewhat differently. Rashi notes that the principle *kol hameshane yado al hatachtona* was not the primary topic of the *mishnah* in Bava Metzia in which it appears—it was, so to speak, brought in from outside the immediate subject matter, which itself signals that it is being stated as a standalone *halacha psuka* rather than as a ruling tied to a specific context. Tosafot, by contrast, focuses on the language itself: *midekatni kol hameshane hu lashon halacha psuka*—the formulation *kol hameshane*, stating the rule as an all-encompassing principle, is itself the hallmark of *halacha psuka*. The framing as a *klal* rather than as a case-bound ruling is what makes explicit the binding character of the position.

## Yehoshua ben Korcha…

### Tosafot's Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יֹאמַר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״הֲנִרְאֶה שֶׁתַּעֲמוֹד עִמִּי לָעֶרֶב״. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״הֲנִרְאֶה שֶׁתַּעֲמוֹד עִמִּי לָעֶרֶב״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A person may not say to another on Shabbat: Does it seem that you will join me this evening?** This is prohibited, as the speaker is hinting that he would like to hire him for labor after the conclusion of Shabbat. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: A person may say to another on Shabbat: Does it seem that you will join me this evening?** In this case, he is not asking him explicitly.

Returning to the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha on debt collection: the *halacha* as stated in the Gemara permits collection of an oral, undocumented debt (*milveh al peh*) before a non-Jewish holiday but forbids collection of a documented debt (*milveh b'shtar*). Tosafot, however, extends this leniency significantly. Tosafot reasons that the Gemara's distinction between documented and undocumented debts was predicated on a situation where Jews had sufficient leverage—perhaps some degree of governmental control—such that a documented debt was secure and the creditor could afford to wait. In the reality Tosafot faced, where non-Jews controlled the government and enforcement mechanisms, a documented debt was no more secure than an oral one: the creditor was always at risk of losing the money. Tosafot therefore rules that even a *milveh b'shtar* may be collected before the non-Jewish holiday in such circumstances, because the imperative to protect one's assets applies regardless of documentation. Tosafot also invokes the *Yerushalmi* in support of this position. It is difficult to argue that Tosafot's reality was so fundamentally different from the Gemara's, but the ruling reflects a broader pattern in Tosafot of defending established practice and protecting Jewish economic interests.

## Conflicting Rulings

### No Forum Shopping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּשְׁאָל לְחָכָם וְטִימֵּא — לֹא יִשָּׁאֵל לְחָכָם וִיטַהֵר, לְחָכָם וְאָסַר — לֹא יִשָּׁאֵל לְחָכָם וְיַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: In the case of one who asks a question of a Sage** with regard to an issue of ritual impurity **and the Sage rules that the item is impure, he may not ask the same question of another Sage and have him rule that it is pure.** Similarly, in the case of one who asks a Sage a halakhic question **and he deems it forbidden, he may not ask the question of another Sage and have him deem it permitted.**

The Gemara pivots to a *baraita* on a different topic, brought here because it involves another ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha. The *baraita* states: *hanishal lachacham vetimei lo yishal lachacham veyitaher*—if someone asked a sage for a ruling and was told the object is *tamei* (or forbidden), he may not go ask another sage and seek a ruling of *tahor* (or permitted).

This prohibition raises a fundamental question about the nature of halakhic rulings. One way to understand it is through the prism of metaphysical reality: when an authorized sage issues a ruling, it creates a halakhic fact. To say something is *assur* is to make it *assur*, within the range of legitimate interpretation. A second ruling from another sage would be negating something that has already been concretized. On this reading, the problem is not merely procedural.

Alternatively, the concern may be primarily one of *kvod chacham*: you asked me for a ruling; going to someone else signals that you do not respect my judgment. One *nafka mina* between these two framings is the case where the first sage ruled leniently—*mutar*. If the first ruling creates a metaphysical reality, no such *issur* was created by a lenient ruling, and consulting someone else seems unproblematic. But if the concern is *kvod chacham*, the disrespect occurs regardless of whether the first ruling was stringent or lenient. Another question is whether, even if someone goes ahead and asks a second sage, they remain bound by the first ruling—or whether the second ruling can override it.

### Tosafot's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּשְׁאָל לְחָכָם וְטִימֵּא — לֹא יִשָּׁאֵל לְחָכָם וִיטַהֵר, לְחָכָם וְאָסַר — לֹא יִשָּׁאֵל לְחָכָם וְיַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: In the case of one who asks a question of a Sage and the Sage rules that the item is impure, he may not ask the same question of another Sage and have him rule that it is pure. Similarly, in the case of one who asks a Sage a halakhic question and he deems it forbidden, he may not ask the question of another Sage and have him deem it permitted.**

Tosafot raises a pointed objection to the prohibition on seeking additional opinions. The entire enterprise of Tosafot as a school of learning is premised on hearing multiple voices, probing arguments, and allowing truth to emerge from the collision of perspectives. Why should a questioner be forbidden to consult multiple sages? *Ve-yidonu zeh im zeh*—let them argue with one another. *Ulai yeshivuhu midvaro*—perhaps if you received a ruling from one sage and believed it to be mistaken, going to others and raising the question would prompt a discussion that would lead the first sage to reconsider if his position was indeed wrong. The value of multiple voices is precisely that no single authority is beyond correction.

### Disclosure Exception

Tosafot resolves the tension by distinguishing between two types of follow-up consultation. If someone receives a ruling and then goes to another sage without disclosing that they have already been ruled upon—simply hoping for a different answer—that is the prohibited forum shopping. But if someone discloses to the second sage: "I already asked Rav X and he ruled Y, but I have doubts about the ruling," then the second consultation is legitimate. The practical effect of that disclosure is to draw the two sages into a conversation. The second sage, upon hearing what the first ruled, will engage with that ruling—perhaps call the first sage, raise the question directly, and allow genuine halakhic debate to occur. The goal is not to obtain a personally favorable ruling but to clarify the *halacha* through dialogue. Obtaining a different ruling for purely personal purposes is distinct from reopening the question for the sake of truth.

### Shabbat Invitations

Rabbah bar bar Chanah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha*.

### Multiple Sages

The second case is the *baraita* on forum shopping discussed above: *hanishal lachacham vetimei lo yishal lachacham veyitaher*. This is again attributed in context to the block of rulings following Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's positions.

## Multiple Simultaneous

### Default: Greater/Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם, אֶחָד מְטַמֵּא וְאֶחָד מְטַהֵר, אֶחָד אוֹסֵר וְאֶחָד מַתִּיר, אִם הָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶם גָּדוֹל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ בְּחָכְמָה וּבְמִנְיָן — הַלֵּךְ אַחֲרָיו, וְאִם לָאו — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַמַּחְמִיר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשֶׁל תּוֹרָה הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַמַּחְמִיר, בְּשֶׁל סוֹפְרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַמֵּיקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** In a situation where **there were two Sages sitting together and one deems an item impure and the other one deems it pure,** or if **one deems it prohibited and the other one deems it permitted,** the questioner should proceed as follows: **If one of the Sages was superior to the other in wisdom and in number,** one should **follow his ruling, and if not,** he should **follow the one who rules stringently. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** If the uncertainty exists **with regard to a Torah law, follow the one who rules stringently;** if it exists **with regard to a rabbinic law, follow the one who rules leniently.**

The Gemara addresses what happens when two sages are asked simultaneously and give contradictory rulings. Unlike forum shopping, here there was no sequential consultation—both opinions arrived at once. *Im hayah echad meihem gadol mechaveiro bechochma uveminyan*—if one of the two sages is greater in wisdom and in number, *halacha k'moto*, follow the greater sage. If it is not clear who is greater, *halacha achar hamachmir*: default to the stringent opinion.

### Torah vs. Rabbinic

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha qualifies this: *be'shel Torah halech achar hamachmir, be'shel soferim halech achar hameikel*. In matters of Torah law, follow the stringent opinion; in matters of Rabbinic law, follow the lenient opinion. This maps onto the fundamental principle *safek de'Oraita l'chumra, safek de'rabbanan l'kula*. In its most familiar application, that principle concerns *safek metzius*—doubt about the facts. One does not know whether a piece of meat is *chazir*; that is a biblical question and one must be stringent. One does not know whether an object is *muktzah*; that is a Rabbinic question and one may be lenient. Similarly with doubtful cases of *milchig* and *fleishig*. But Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's ruling extends this principle to a different domain: not doubt about facts but a genuine halakhic dispute between two contemporary authorities. Even where the debate is not about facts but about the law itself, if the matter is Rabbinic, follow the lenient opinion.

The significance of this ruling is difficult to overstate. Much of the practice of *psak* as it has developed through the *rishonim* and later *poskim*—including the extensive rulings of Rav Ovadia Yosef—rests on this principle: when authorities dispute a Rabbinic question, one may rely on the lenient opinion. Without this ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, every halakhic disagreement would require defaulting to the stringent position.

### Rambam's Caveat

The Rambam frames this entire discussion with an important qualification. His language in the fifth chapter of *Mamrim* is: *im ein atta yodea lehachan haddin noteh*—if *you yourself* do not know which way the law inclines. Rambam's point is that a *posek* does not begin by counting up opinions on each side and applying the stringency-leniency default. The rules about following the greater sage, or defaulting to the lenient opinion in Rabbinic matters, apply only when the *posek* has genuinely worked through the sources and cannot determine on his own assessment which position is correct. If he can determine it, he should rule accordingly.

This reflects a fundamental question about the role of a *posek*: is his task to tabulate the positions of earlier authorities—counting how many stand on each side, weighing their relative stature—and then apply the mechanical default rules? Or is it to engage with the original sources, assess which position seems to best reflect the underlying principles of *halacha*, and only fall back on the counting and default rules when that assessment yields no clear answer? Rambam clearly envisions the latter.

### Gadol Mechaveiro

The phrase *gadol mechaveiro bechochma uveminyan* requires interpretation. *Chochma*—greater in wisdom—is relatively straightforward. But what does *minyan*—number—mean when we are speaking of a single sage rather than a court? The standard explanation of the *rishonim* is that it refers to the number of *talmidim*: how many disciples one has, how far one's teaching reaches. One might also interpret it as measuring the breadth of one's community—how many people turn to this sage for *psak*. The distinction matters: *talmidim* in the strict sense means those who have learned directly under the sage, whereas the scope of a *posek*'s constituency extends to all who follow his rulings, whether or not they sat in his presence.

This also raises the question of how to tabulate when the sages are not contemporaries but historical figures. A ruling of the Rambam and a ruling of an obscure *rishon* are not equal votes. How does one weigh them? And if Tosafot adopts a position and subsequent Ashkenazi *poskim* uniformly follow it, does that count as one vote or many? If they are simply repeating and deferring to Tosafot without independent assessment, their agreement may not add cumulative weight in the way that genuinely independent concurrences would.

### Sources/Versions

Tosafot raises an additional application of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's principle, this time to a question internal to the Gemara itself. When the Gemara presents two versions of a position using the formula *ika de'amrei*—"there are those who say"—which version governs? Rashi's approach was to apply the *beshel Torah hamachmir / beshel soferim hameikel* rule: in Torah matters follow the stricter of the two versions; in Rabbinic matters follow the later version, which tends toward leniency. The *Riva* (Rabad's school) disagreed: *ika de'amrei* introduces an alternative version that is secondary and supplementary to the first, and the first version (*lashon kamma*) always controls. Rabbeinu Tam took yet a third position: treat the two versions as a genuine dispute and apply *de'Oraita lechumra, de'rabbanan lekula* straightforwardly—Torah matters go to the stricter version, Rabbinic matters to the more lenient one. The range of opinion on this question illustrates how wide the application of these principles extends.

## Trust & Repentance

### Reversion Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁחָזְרוּ בָּהֶן — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן עוֹלָמִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָזְרוּ בָּהֶן בְּמַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן, בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: עָשׂוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶם בְּמַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן,
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: And all of the people who are not deemed credible due to sins that they performed, even when they retract and repent from their evil ways, society never accepts them;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If **they retract their ways in private, society does not accept them,** but if they repent **in public [*befarheseya*],** society **accepts them. There are those who say** that there is another version of this discussion: If **they performed their sinful matters in private,** then when they repent society **accepts them.**

The Gemara presents a *baraita* concerning those who reverted—*chazru bahen*. The discussion concerns individuals who had undertaken to observe the laws of *tumah v'taharah* and *trumos u'ma'asros* at the level of a *chaver*—someone trusted in these matters—but then slipped back into the less careful practices of an *am ha'aretz*. They now seek to be accepted again into the community of those trusted with such obligations. May they be re-admitted? The *baraita* records a three-way dispute.

### Meir: Never

Rabbi Meir rules absolutely: *ain mikablin osam olamis*—we never accept them back. Having demonstrated that they cannot sustain their commitment, they have lost the community's trust permanently.

### Yehuda: Nuanced

Rabbi Yehuda introduces a nuance. *Chazru bahen b'matmonyos ain mikablin osam, b'farhesya mikablin osan*—if they reverted in private while maintaining the appearance of observance in public, we do not accept them back. That combination—carefully preserving a public reputation for scrupulousness while privately abandoning the practice—is the behavior of someone trying to deceive the community, and such a person cannot be trusted. But if they reverted openly and publicly, without any attempt to conceal their backsliding, they were not trying to deceive anyone. They simply found the commitment beyond their capacity to maintain. Such a person, if they come back and wish to try again, may be accepted.

### Tosafot: Scenarios

The *ika de'amrei* version of the *baraita* inverts the variables in an illuminating way. On this reading, *asu divreihem b'matmonyos*—if the first time around they were known to be scrupulous even in private, careful in their observance even when no one was watching—then even after they slipped, we accept them back. Because we have positive evidence from their prior private behavior that their commitment was genuine and not merely performative, we give them the benefit of the doubt and re-admit them. But if their earlier observance was *b'farhesya* only—we only ever saw them keeping the standard in public, never in private—then we have no basis to know whether their commitment was ever real or merely for show, and after they slipped we cannot accept them back.

Tosafot's reading thus maps both versions of the dispute onto a common underlying question: is there sufficient evidence to believe that the person's initial commitment was authentic, such that their failure to sustain it was a matter of weakness rather than deception?

### Always Accept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁחָזְרוּ בָּהֶן — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן עוֹלָמִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָזְרוּ בָּהֶן בְּמַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן, בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation (concluding segment):** The Sages then present a third position. We cannot find this exact text in the Sefaria materials provided, but following the sequence of the *baraita*, it continues with Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda ben Korcha's position. According to the version structure in the materials, the third position states: **Ben kach u'ben kach mikablin**—**in either case, we accept them back.**

Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda ben Korcha rule simply: *ben kach u'ben kach mikablin*—in either case, we accept them back. It does not matter how they reverted, whether publicly or privately, or what we knew of their private behavior the first time around. The door must always remain open. The biblical verse *shuvu banim shovavim*—"return, wayward children"—is invoked as the foundation: the call to return is unconditional.

### Halacha: Accept

Avodah Zarah 7a provides the continuation in context, showing that the *baraita* concludes with Rabbi Shimon's lenient position being established as the law. The statement would be: **Rabbi Yitzchak ish Parcha in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha k'oto hazug*—the law follows that pair, Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda ben Korcha. We accept penitents back.**

Rabbi Yitzchak ish Parcha in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha k'oto hazug*—the law follows that pair, Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda ben Korcha. We accept penitents back.

### Modern Issues

The tension in this ruling is real and pressing in contemporary application. The *halacha* establishes an open-arms policy for those who return, grounded in the theology of *teshuvah*: the call to return is unconditional, and we believe in the capacity for genuine change. But it is crucial to note what this ruling does and does not establish. *Teshuvah* is about forgiveness—ultimately a relationship between the penitent and God, who knows what is in one's heart. Trust is a separate matter entirely. Trust must be earned through demonstrated behavior over time, and the standards for restoring it depend heavily on what is at stake.

A case involving sloppiness in observance of *tumah v'taharah*—serious as those laws are—presents different considerations than a case involving someone who deliberately committed fraud for financial gain, or more gravely still, someone who caused direct harm to others. In contexts involving abuse—and particularly sexual abuse—even a genuine expression of *teshuvah*, accompanied by sincere acknowledgment of wrongdoing, does not automatically translate into restored communal trust, especially when the safety of others is at risk. It is worth observing that most who seek reinstatement in such cases do not offer anything resembling genuine *teshuvah*: "mistakes were made" is not *teshuvah*, nor is an apology unaccompanied by serious efforts at restitution and seeking forgiveness from those harmed. But even a full *teshuvah* process does not settle the question of risk. What is permissible—what the principle of *ben kach u'ben kach mikablin* means in practice—depends on context, on what is being entrusted to the returning individual, and on a sober assessment of the circumstances.

## Three Days Before

### Before/After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים״ — הֵן וְאֵידֵיהֶן, אוֹ דִלְמָא הֵן בְּלֹא אֵידֵיהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before the Sages: When the mishna teaches that it is prohibited to conduct business with gentiles on the three days before their festival, do the three days include them, i.e., the days preceding the festival and their festival itself, in which case the prohibition applies only to the festival and the two preceding days?** **Or perhaps it is referring to them without their festival,** i.e., the prohibition applies to three full days before the festival.

The Gemara now records a dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and the *Chachamim* about the temporal scope of the prohibition. Rabbi Yishmael holds that business dealings with non-Jews are forbidden three days before *and* three days after their holiday—*shelosha lifneihem u-shelosha achareihem assur*. The *Chachamim* rule: *lifneihem assur, achareihem mutar*—only before is forbidden, after is permissible.

### Christians/Sunday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה לִפְנֵיהֶם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה לְאַחֲרֵיהֶן אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof from a mishna (7b). **Come and hear, as Rabbi Yishmael says:** On the **three days before the festivals of gentiles and** the **three days after them,** these actions are **prohibited.**

Rav Tachlifa bar Avdimi citing Shmuel applies Rabbi Yishmael's position to Christians specifically. The original text reads *l'divrei Rabbi Yishmael, Notzri l'olam assur*: according to Rabbi Yishmael, doing business with a Christian is always forbidden, because their holiday is Sunday—and three days before Sunday combined with three days after leads to a prohibition that wraps around the entire week. This Gemara carries significant halakhic weight on two points. First, it implicitly treats Christianity as *avodah zarah*—a position that the Meiri, who was reluctant to so characterize it, sought to deflect by reading *Notzri* as a reference to the people of Nevuchadnezzar rather than to Christians. Second, it establishes that a regular weekly holiday is considered a sufficiently significant observance to trigger these prohibitions.

The *Chachamim*'s ruling that only before is forbidden, and after is permissible, then appears to simply reiterate the position already stated. The Gemara objects: *chachamim hainu tana kama*—isn't this identical to the first anonymous opinion? The repetition creates an opening to investigate what substantive disagreement might lie between the first formulation and the *Chachamim*'s restatement.

### Distinguishing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הֵן וְאֵידֵיהֶן, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל יוֹם אֵידֵיהֶן חַשֵּׁיב לְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא וְחַשֵּׁיב לְהוּ לְבַסּוֹף?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement. **If it enters your mind that the three days include them and their festival,** this would mean that **Rabbi Yishmael counts the day of their festival twice, as he counts it initially,** as part of the first set of three days, **and** he also **counts it at the end,** along with the second set of three days. Clearly, the three days do not include the day of the festival itself.

The Gemara proceeds to propose four possible distinctions, each of which introduces a separate halakhic question.

### Including Holiday

The first distinction: perhaps they disagree about whether the count of "three days" includes the holiday day itself or begins from the day before it. *Tana kama savar hen velo edeihen*—the first view holds that three days means three days not counting the holiday, for a total of four forbidden days. The later *Chachamim*, who state simply *lifneihem assur*, perhaps mean three including the holiday—two full days plus the holiday.

### Proceeds After

The second distinction: they may be debating the status of proceeds from business conducted during the forbidden period before the holiday. The first *tana* may hold that while the act of doing business is prohibited, its proceeds after the fact are permitted. The *Chachamim*, phrasing the prohibition as *assur* without qualification, signal that even the proceeds are forbidden—*nasava venasana assur* in the unqualified sense.

### Shmuel: In/Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר אַבְדִּימִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נוֹצְרִים לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לְעוֹלָם אָסוּר, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״הֵן וְאֵידֵיהֶן״, הָאִיכָּא אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה דְּשָׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come and hear a proof from that which Rav Taḥlifa bar Avdimi says that Shmuel says: According to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael, it is always prohibited to engage in business with Christians,** as their festival takes place every Sunday. Since the three days preceding and following their festival are included in the prohibition, one cannot engage in business with them any day of the week. **And if it enters your mind that the three days of the mishna include them and their festival,** i.e., only the two days preceding and following the festival are included in the prohibition, then according to Rabbi Yishmael **there are still Wednesday and Thursday,** on which it is **permitted** to engage in business with Christians.

The third distinction introduces Shmuel's ruling: *bagola ein assur ella yom edam*—outside the land of Israel, the only day forbidden for business is the holiday day itself. There is no prohibition of the three days before in the diaspora. On this reading, the dispute between the two formulations in the *baraita* may be whether to accept Shmuel's geographic distinction. The first *tana* accepts Shmuel's ruling—three days before applies only in Israel, and in the diaspora only the day itself is forbidden. The *Chachamim*'s broader phrasing, stating the prohibition without geographic qualification, implies that the prohibition of three days before applies equally in Israel and outside it. This third distinction is largely an occasion to introduce Shmuel's position into the discussion.

### Shmuel's Logic

Shmuel's ruling is striking—indeed, the Gemara's application of it invites scrutiny. Why should the prohibition be stricter in Israel than in the diaspora? Rashi suggests two lines of reasoning. One is that in Israel both Jews and non-Jews are more scrupulous about their respective religious obligations, making the concern about facilitating pagan worship more acute. The other is that in the diaspora the imperative to maintain good relations with the surrounding non-Jewish population is greater, warranting some relaxation of the restrictions. The second rationale is somewhat puzzling: one might think that living under Roman rule in Israel would make the concern about Jewish-gentile relations no less pressing than living among the Babylonians. Rashi's reasoning on this point has something of a forced quality. What is not in doubt is the practical import of Shmuel's ruling: it gave the *rishonim* considerable latitude to relax these restrictions for Jews living outside Israel, on the basis that the full scope of the prohibition was never intended to apply in the diaspora.

It is also worth noting that Shmuel himself was a Babylonian *amora*, ruling from outside Israel—which gives his ruling something of an autobiographical dimension.

### Nachum HaMada

The fourth distinction introduces Nachum HaMada. A *baraita* records his view: *eino assur ella yom echad lifnei edeihen*—the prohibition applies only one day before the holiday, not three. The *Chachamim* in the *mishnah*, who state only *lifneihem assur* without specifying three days, may be holding like Nachum HaMada: one day before is sufficient. The first *tana*, who explicitly says three days, rejects Nachum HaMada's leniency. The Gemara's use of these four distinctions allows it to introduce two significant minority positions—Shmuel's geographic distinction and Nachum HaMada's temporal leniency—into the halakhic conversation, setting up further discussion to follow.
