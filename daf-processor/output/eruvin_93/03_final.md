# Eruvin 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Large & Small Yards

### Two-Directional Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָצֵר גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה לִקְטַנָּה — גְּדוֹלָה מוּתֶּרֶת וּקְטַנָּה אֲסוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּפִתְחָהּ שֶׁל גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a large courtyard that was breached into a small** one, it is **permitted** for the residents of **the large** courtyard to carry, but it is **prohibited** for the residents of **the small one** to do so. That is **because** in that case, the legal status of the breach **is like** that of **the entrance of the large** courtyard.

Today's daf is 93, and we pick up at the bottom of 92b with an important discussion that carries ramifications well beyond the immediate topic of *hilchos Shabbos*. The central configuration under discussion involves a large courtyard (A) and a small courtyard (B), where B is entirely open to A but A is not entirely open to B. This asymmetry gives rise to two distinct legal consequences, which the *gemara* treats together but which are worth distinguishing analytically.

### Disassociation & Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּפָנִים בַּקְּטַנָּה — מוּתָּר לִזְרוֹעַ אֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה. אִשָּׁה בַּגְּדוֹלָה וָגֵט בַּקְּטַנָּה — מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת. אִשָּׁה בַּקְּטַנָּה וְגֵט בַּגְּדוֹלָה — אֵינָהּ מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת. צִבּוּר בַּגְּדוֹלָה וּשְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר בַּקְּטַנָּה — יוֹצְאִין יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן. צִיבּוּר בַּקְּטַנָּה וּשְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר בַּגְּדוֹלָה — אֵין יוֹצְאִין יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן. תִּשְׁעָה בַּגְּדוֹלָה וְיָחִיד בַּקְּטַנָּה — מִצְטָרְפִין. תִּשְׁעָה בַּקְּטַנָּה וְאֶחָד בַּגְּדוֹלָה — אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. צוֹאָה בַּגְּדוֹלָה — אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע בַּקְּטַנָּה. צוֹאָה בַּקְּטַנָּה — מוּתָּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע בַּגְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** If there are **vines in the small** courtyard, **it is permitted to sow** other crops **in the large one** *ab initio*, even if they are not planted four cubits away from the vines, because the vines are not considered to be located in the larger courtyard, and therefore there is no prohibition whatsoever. **A wife, who owned both courtyards, was standing in the large** courtyard, **and** her husband threw her **a bill of divorce** into **the small** courtyard, **she is divorced.** Her presence in the larger courtyard extends to the smaller one, and she is therefore considered to be standing in the small courtyard. If, however, **the wife was in the small** courtyard **and the bill of divorce** was thrown into **the large one, she is not divorced.** Likewise, with regard to communal prayer, if the **congregation** was **in the large** courtyard, **and the prayer leader** was **in the small one, they fulfill their obligation** through his prayer, as the congregation is considered to be in the small one as well. However, if **the congregation** was **in the small** courtyard, **and the prayer leader** was **in the large one, they do not fulfill their obligation.** The same principle applies to a prayer quorum: If there were **nine** men **in the large** courtyard **and one** man **in the small one, they join** together to form the necessary quorum of ten, as the small courtyard is subsumed within the large one, and the individual is considered to be in the large courtyard. However, if there were **nine** men **in the small** courtyard **and one in the large one, they do not join** together. Furthermore, if there was **excrement in the large** courtyard, **it is prohibited to recite *Shema* in the small one,** as the excrement is considered to be in the small courtyard as well, and it is prohibited to recite *Shema* in the presence of excrement. If, however, there was **excrement in the small** courtyard, **it is permitted to recite *Shema* in the large one.**

The first consequence concerns the ability to disassociate. Because B is completely open to A, there is no way for B to divorce itself from what is happening in A. The reverse, however, is not true: A can disassociate itself from B. A concrete illustration of this appears in the preceding discussion about reciting *shema* in proximity to excrement. If one is reciting *shema* in A and excrement is located in B, B need not be considered relevant to A — A sees B as a partially separate space, accessible only through a distinct opening. But if one is reciting *shema* in B and excrement is in A, B cannot disassociate from A, because from B's perspective the two spaces form one continuous expanse. This is the same logic underlying the *mishna* itself: if one is not permitted to carry in A and finds oneself in B, one cannot carry in B either, precisely because B is *parutz bimlo'o* — completely breached into A.

### Primary & Adjunct

The second consequence concerns the relationship of primary and adjunct space. B is an adjunct to A, but A is not an adjunct to B. This has practical implications for the laws of *minyan*. If the majority of a *minyan* is in A and the *shliach tzibbur* is in B, that arrangement is valid, because B as the adjunct space follows A as the primary space. However, the reverse does not work: if the *minyan* is in B and the *shliach tzibbur* is in A, the arrangement fails. The secondary element must be in the adjunct space, not the primary one. A cannot be pulled after B; only B can be pulled after A.

### Perceptual Perspective

A useful way to understand these two principles as a unified whole is to consider the perspective of a person standing in each yard. If you are standing in A and look around, you see your own space with a smaller yard off to one side — something clearly distinct, requiring you to pass through a doorway to reach it. The walls of B are visible; B registers as a separate room. But if you are standing in B and look around, you see no interior walls demarcating a boundary. The far wall of A is simply the far wall of your visual field, and the entire expanse appears as one undivided space. This is precisely why, from B, one cannot disassociate and why A functions as the encompassing primary domain.

## Wall Paradox

### Vineyard & Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן, מָצִינוּ מְחִיצָה לְאִיסּוּר. שֶׁאִילְמָלֵי אֵין מְחִיצָה, מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְזוֹרֵעַ, וְאִילּוּ הַשְׁתָּא אֲסוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to them: If so, we have found a partition that** causes **prohibition.** According to these principles, the existence of a partition renders sowing crops prohibited; in the absence of a partition sowing the crops would have been permitted due to their distance from the vines. Ostensibly, this is a counterintuitive conclusion. **As, were there no partition** at all, it would be sufficient to **distance** oneself **four cubits** from the vine **and sow** the crop, **whereas now** that the area is divided into two courtyards by means of a partition, **it is prohibited** to sow the crop in the entire small courtyard.

Abaye, who was seated among three senior colleagues during this discussion, posed a sharp challenge: *im kein matzinu mechitza le-issur* — if so, we find that a *mechitza* creates a prohibition. The context for this observation draws on the laws of *kilayim* in vineyards. The standard *halakha* is that if one has a row of grapevines and wishes to plant wheat nearby, it suffices to distance oneself four *amot* from the vines. Now consider what happens when a wall is added around the vineyard: from the perspective of B, the enclosed vineyard is treated as a *kerem*, extending into B's space. The prohibition of *lo tizra karemcha kilayim* — do not sow your vineyard with mixed species — now applies within B. Even if one is more than four *amot* from the vines, if the wall has effectively incorporated the space, planting anything there becomes forbidden.

### Mechitza Le-Issur

Abaye's challenge is thus: the presence of walls should, if anything, demarcate and separate. Yet what we are being told is that by configuring B as an adjunct to the *kerem*, the walls make things *more* forbidden, not less. Is it possible that a *mechitza* — something that should create separation — actually produces greater association and, consequently, a greater *issur*?

### Rav Zeira Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְלֹא מָצִינוּ מְחִיצָה לְאִיסּוּר?! וְהָא תְּנַן: חָצֵר גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה לִקְטַנָּה — גְּדוֹלָה מוּתֶּרֶת וּקְטַנָּה אֲסוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּפִתְחָהּ שֶׁל גְּדוֹלָה. וְאִילּוּ הִשְׁוָה אֶת גִּיפּוּפֶיהָ גְּדוֹלָה נָמֵי אֲסוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Abaye: And didn't we find a partition that** causes **prohibition? But didn't we learn** in the mishna: With regard to **a large courtyard that was breached into a small** one, it is **permitted** for the residents of **the large** courtyard to carry, but it is **prohibited** for the residents of **the small one** to do so. That is **because** in that case, the legal status of the breach **is like** that of **the entrance of the large** courtyard. **And if he were to even its protrusions** by constructing partitions in the larger courtyard so that the large courtyard no longer protruded beyond the smaller one, carrying in **the large** courtyard would **also be prohibited,** as it would now be completely breached into the smaller courtyard. Apparently, in this case, construction of additional partitions causes prohibition.

Rav Zeira responded to Abaye: *velo matzinu mechitza le-issur?* — are you really claiming we find no such precedent? He cited our very *mishna*: *chatzer gedola shenifretzah liketana, gedola muteres ketana asura* — the large courtyard that is breached into the small one leaves the large permitted and the small forbidden. And furthermore, *v'ilu hishveh et kipifeha gedola nami asura* — if one levels the overhang of the small yard, creating a new wall that effectively extends the boundary, the large yard also becomes forbidden. Here, adding a *mechitza* made something that was formerly permitted into something forbidden. This is a case of *mechitza le-issur*, and it is familiar *halakha*.

## Wall Removal Cases

### Mikveh & Roof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָתָם סִילּוּק מְחִיצּוֹת הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְלֹא מָצִינוּ מְחִיצָה לְאִיסּוּר? וְהָא אִתְּמַר: סִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי אַכְסַדְרָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ פַּצִּימִין — כְּשֵׁירָה. וְאִילּוּ הִשְׁוָה פַּצִּימֶיהָ — פְּסוּלָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְדִידִי כְּשֵׁירָה, לְדִידָךְ סִילּוּק מְחִיצּוֹת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rabbi Zeira: The two cases are not comparable, as **there,** adding partitions in order to even the protrusions is not considered establishment of partitions. On the contrary, **it is** effectively **the removal of partitions.** These partitions are designed to negate the original partitions of the courtyard. **Rava said to Abaye: And didn't we find a partition that** causes **prohibition? But wasn't it stated:** If one **placed roofing on top of a portico that has doorposts,** i.e., a portico with two parallel walls that are valid for a *sukka*, as well as posts in the corners supporting the portico and protruding like doorposts, which are considered as sealing the other two sides of the portico, it is a **valid** *sukka*. However, **if he evened the doorposts** by constructing walls adjacent to the existing walls, obscuring the posts so that they do not protrude, the *sukka* is **invalid.** This teaching indicates that the creation of a partition can cause prohibition. **Abaye said to him: In my opinion,** with regard to that case of a portico, the *sukka* is **valid.** However, even **according to your opinion,** this **is** another instance of **the removal of partitions.** Evening the doorposts does not render the *sukka* invalid through the establishment of new partitions, but because it negates the original partitions of the *sukka*.

Abaye, however, was not satisfied. He noted that in Rav Zeira's example, the act of adding a wall did not simply add a wall — it removed other walls. The new partition effectively eliminated the existing walls that had defined A as its own distinct space. By adding this wall, those defining walls were rendered irrelevant, causing A to collapse into the same status as B. So what looks like an addition of a wall is really a removal of walls, and it is the *siluk mechitzot* — the removal of those walls — that creates the prohibition, not the mere act of building a new one.

This same response applies to the next attempted counterexample, brought by Rabba bar bar Chana, involving a house half of which has a roof and half of which does not. If grapevines are planted under the roofed section and one wishes to plant seeds under the open section, the roofline serves as a type of *mechitza* — a demarcating edge akin to *pi tikra yored v'sotem*, the conceptual principle that the edge of a roof descends and closes the space. If one then extends the roof to cover the entire house, the division disappears and planting seeds anywhere in the house becomes forbidden. Once again, Abaye responds: this is not a case of adding a wall and thereby making things worse — it is a case of adding a roof that effectively removes the wall created by the partial roofline. The answer is the same: *hasam siluk mechitzot hu*.

### Distinguish Removal

Abaye's sustained point across all these exchanges is precise: every counterexample offered involves a scenario where building something new has the effect of dismantling an existing *mechitza*. None of these examples, therefore, address his original question, which concerns a *mechitza* that is purely additive — one that does not eliminate any prior partition but that nonetheless produces greater association rather than greater separation.

### Sukka & Posts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְלֹא מָצִינוּ מְחִיצָה לְאִיסּוּר? וְהָתַנְיָא: בַּיִת שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ מְקוֹרֶה וְחֶצְיוֹ אֵינוֹ מְקוֹרֶה, גְּפָנִים כָּאן — מוּתָּר לִזְרוֹעַ כָּאן. וְאִילּוּ הִשְׁוָה אֶת קֵרוּיוֹ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan said to Abaye: And didn't we find** that **a partition** causes **prohibition? But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a house, half of which is roofed and half unroofed,** if there are **vines here,** under the roofed section of the house, **it is permitted to sow** crops **there,** in the open section. The reason is that it is as though the edge of the roof descends to the ground and forms a partition between the two sections of the house. **And if he evened its roofing,** by extending the roof to cover the entire house, it would be **prohibited** to sow other crops there. It is evident that the very placement of a partition, in this case a roof, causes prohibition.

Rava then offered another attempted counterexample, drawn from the laws of *sukka* (79a). The case involves placing *s'chach* on top of a portico (*achsadra*) that has *patzimim* — a type of structural shoulder or partial wall extending inward at the corners, creating what functions as a partial third wall. Such a *sukka* is *keshera*, because two full walls and a minimal third wall suffice for *sukka* validity. However, if one levels the *patzimim* — by adding a wall between them — the *sukka* becomes *pesulah*. Adding a wall made the *sukka* invalid.

### Pi Tikra Yored

Abaye responds on two levels. First, for his own position, the case is not a question at all: *lididi keshera* — according to me, it is already *keshera* even after the modification, because I hold *pi tikra yored v'sotem*, that the edge of the roof descends and closes the space, providing the needed third wall regardless of what is done to the *patzimim*. Second, addressing the position of those who do hold the *sukka* becomes *pesulah*: that is once again a case of *siluk mechitzot*. By adding the new wall between the *patzimim*, one has effectively removed the *patzimim* themselves as halakhically significant walls. The addition caused the disappearance of prior valid walls. This is the same dynamic as all the earlier cases, and therefore it still does not constitute a genuine proof that a *mechitza* can create prohibition purely through addition.

## Vineyard Solution

### Mechitzot Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי בְּיַד רַב שְׁמַעְיָה בַּר זְעֵירָא: וְלֹא מָצִינוּ מְחִיצָה לְאִיסּוּר? וְהָתַנְיָא: יֵשׁ בִּמְחִיצוֹת הַכֶּרֶם לְהָקֵל וּלְהַחֲמִיר, כֵּיצַד?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava sent** a different proof to **Abaye by means of Rav Shemaya bar Ze'eira,** with regard to the same issue. **And didn't we find a partition** causes **prohibition? But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **There is** an element **in the partitions of a vineyard** that causes **leniency** with regard to diverse kinds of seeds **and** an element that causes **stringency. How so?**

The *gemara* then turns to a *braita* sent to Abaye by Rava through the intermediary of Shmaya bar Zeira. The *braita* teaches: *yesh b'mechitzos hakerem lehakeil ulehachmir* — there are ways in which the walls of a vineyard can be both lenient and stringent.

### Leniency: Opposite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֶּרֶם הַנָּטוּעַ עַד עִיקַּר מְחִיצָה — זוֹרֵעַ מֵעִיקַּר מְחִיצָה וְאֵילָךְ. שֶׁאִילּוּ אֵין שָׁם מְחִיצָה מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְזוֹרֵעַ, וְזֶה הוּא מְחִיצּוֹת הַכֶּרֶם לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a vineyard that is planted until the** very **base of a partition, one sows** crops **from the base of** the other side of **the partition onward.** This is a leniency, **as were there no partition there,** he would be required to **distance** himself **four cubits** from the last vine **and** only then **sow** there. **And this is** an element in **partitions of a vineyard** that causes **leniency.**

The leniency is as follows. If a vineyard is planted right up to the edge of a wall — *kerem hanatu'a ad ikkar mechitza* — one may plant other species beginning immediately on the other side of the wall, *zorei'a mei'ikkar mechitza v'eilaich*. Without the wall, one would have been required to distance four *amot* from the vines. The wall effectively substitutes for that distance, allowing planting right against the wall's opposite face. This is *mechitzos hakerem lehakeil*.

### Stringency: Same Side

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַחֲמִיר כֵּיצַד? הָיָה מָשׁוּךְ מִן הַכּוֹתֶל אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה — לֹא יָבִיא זֶרַע לְשָׁם, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵי אֵין מְחִיצָה מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְזוֹרֵעַ, וְזוֹהִי מְחִיצוֹת הַכֶּרֶם לְהַחְמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for an element in partitions that causes **stringency, how so?** If the vineyard **was distanced eleven cubits from a wall, one may not bring** the **seeds** of other crops **there,** between the vineyard and the wall, and sow that area. This is a stringency, **as were there no partition,** it would suffice to **distance** himself **four cubits** from the last vine, **and sow** there. **This is** an element of **partitions in a vineyard** that causes **stringency,** a clear situation of a partition that causes prohibition.

The stringency is the inverse. If there is open space between the vineyard and a wall, and that space is only eleven *amot*, one may not plant anything there — *lo yehei zorei'a l'sham*. Without the wall, four *amot* of distance from the vines would have been sufficient. With the wall present and one on the same side as the vineyard, the requirement is now twelve *amot* rather than four. This is *mechitzos hakerem lehachmir*, and this, says Rava, is the clear precedent for Abaye's concern: a *mechitza* that, rather than separating, requires far greater distance and is therefore stringent.

To complete the picture: the *mishna* in *Kilayim* records that a *karachas hakerem* — a bald spot in the middle of a vineyard, surrounded by vines on all sides — must be at least sixteen *amot* wide according to Beis Hillel (Beis Shammai requires twenty-four) for it to be treated as an independent space in which one may plant. A *machol hakerem* — the band of space between the vines and the surrounding wall — must be at least twelve *amot* wide according to Beis Hillel (Beis Shammai requires sixteen). In both cases, the surrounding vines or walls pull the space into the orbit of the vineyard, requiring a minimum threshold before the space is considered independent. Once that threshold is met, one must still maintain four *amot* from the vines themselves — *kedei avodaso*, the working space needed to tend the vine — and may plant only in the remainder.

### Workspace Forfeit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, אוֹתְבַן מִמַּתְנִיתִין, דִּתְנַן: קָרַחַת הַכֶּרֶם, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה. מְחוֹל הַכֶּרֶם, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא קָרַחַת הַכֶּרֶם? כֶּרֶם שֶׁחָרַב אֶמְצָעִיתוֹ. אִם אֵין שָׁם שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה — לֹא יָבִיא זֶרַע לְשָׁם, הָיוּ שָׁם שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה — נוֹתֵן לוֹ כְּדֵי עֲבוֹדָתוֹ, וְזוֹרֵעַ אֶת הַמּוֹתָר. אֵי זוֹ הִיא מְחוֹל הַכֶּרֶם? בֵּין הַכֶּרֶם לַגָּדֵר. שֶׁאִם אֵין שָׁם שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה — לֹא יָבִיא זֶרַע לְשָׁם, הָיוּ שָׁם שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה — נוֹתֵן לוֹ כְּדֵי עֲבוֹדָתוֹ, וְזוֹרֵעַ אֶת הַמּוֹתָר. אֶלָּא — הָתָם לָאו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, דְּכֹל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לְגַבֵּי כֶרֶם עֲבוֹדַת הַכֶּרֶם. לְגַבֵּי גָדֵר, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מִזְדַּרְעָן — אַפְקוֹרֵי מַפְקַר לְהוּ. דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי, אִי אִיכָּא אַרְבַּע — חֲשִׁיבָן, וְאִי לָא — לָא חֲשִׁיבָן.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him: And according to your reasoning** that this presents a difficulty, **raise an objection against our** opinion **from a mishna,** rather than a less authoritative *baraita*, **as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **a clearing in a vineyard, Beit Shammai say:** Its measure is **twenty-four cubits, and Beit Hillel say: Sixteen cubits.** With regard to **the perimeter of a vineyard, Beit Shammai say: Sixteen cubits, and Beit Hillel say: Twelve cubits.** The mishna explains: **And what is a clearing in a vineyard?** It is referring to **a vineyard whose middle** section **was laid bare** of vines. If **there are not sixteen cubits** across in the clearing, **one may not bring** foreign **seeds** and sow them **there,** due to the Torah prohibition against sowing other crops in a vineyard (Deuteronomy 22:9). If **there were sixteen cubits** across in the clearing, **one provides** the vineyard with **its** requisite **work area,** i.e., four cubits along either side of the vines are left unsown to facilitate cultivation of the vines, **and he sows the rest** of the cleared area with foreign crops. The mishna continues: **What is the perimeter of a vineyard?** It is the vacant area **between the vineyard and the fence** surrounding it. If **there are not twelve cubits** in that area, **one may not bring** foreign **seeds** and sow them **there.** If **there are twelve cubits** in that area, **he provides** the vineyard with **its** requisite **work area,** four cubits, **and he sows the rest.** However, were the vineyard not surrounded by a fence, all he would need to do is distance himself four cubits from the last vine. It is clear from this *halakha* that the partition causes prohibition. **Rather,** the objection was not raised from there because **there, isn't this the reason** that the partition is not considered to cause prohibition? It is **because the entire** area of **four cubits alongside a vineyard is** considered **the vineyard's work area,** and is therefore an actual part of it. Likewise, with regard to the four cubits **alongside the fence** surrounding the vineyard, **since they cannot** easily **be sown** due to the wall, **he renounces ownership over** the area. With regard to the space **in between, if it is four** cubits, **it is** deemed **significant** in its own right, **and if not, it is not significant** and is nullified relative to the rest, and it is prohibited to sow there. A similar reasoning applies to the *baraita*. The stringency is not due to the fact that the partition causes prohibition, but because the partition impedes cultivation of the vineyard.

The *gemara*, however, ultimately concludes that this is not the reason the wall creates a stringency. Abaye responds: *ela hasam lav hainu taama* — the reason there is not what you think. The wall does not pull the space together in some abstract halakhic sense. Rather, the explanation is practical: the four *amot* immediately adjacent to the wall are, in effect, already forfeit. Since one cannot plant against the wall — both because the roots would undermine the wall's foundation and because a plow cannot operate in that narrow strip — those first four *amot* along the wall are treated as if one has already renounced ownership of them: *afkurei mafkir lehu*. They are simply unavailable. Therefore, for the remaining space between the vine and the wall to be considered independent — significant enough to plant in — it must be four *amot* on its own, beyond the already-forfeited strip. That yields a total of twelve *amot*: four forfeit at the wall, four of *kedei avodaso* at the vine, and four of independent space in the middle. This is not because the wall integrated the space into the vineyard; it is because the wall practically eliminated a portion of the available space. Abaye insists that a *mechitza* pulling disparate spaces together into a single unified domain — without any such practical mechanism — remains conceptually troubling.

## Caravans & Space

### Three Overhang Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁלֹשָׁה קַרְפֵּיפוֹת זֶה בְּצַד זֶה, וּשְׁנַיִם הַחִיצוֹנִים מְגוּפָּפִים, וְהָאֶמְצָעִי אֵינוֹ מְגוּפָּף, וְיָחִיד בָּזֶה וְיָחִיד בָּזֶה — נַעֲשֶׂה כִּשְׁיָירָא, וְנוֹתְנִין לָהֶן כׇּל צוֹרְכָּן וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** If there are **three enclosures alongside one another, and the two outer** ones **protrude,** i.e., they are wider than the middle one, so that there are partitions on both sides of the breach between them and the middle enclosure, **and the middle one does not protrude,** and there are no partitions between it and the outer enclosures, as it is totally breached, **and there is one** person **in this** one **and one** person **in that** one and yet another person in the third enclosure, the people in the enclosures are considered as though they are all living in one large enclosure. Consequently, the legal status of the group **becomes like** that of **a caravan, and one provides them** with **all** the space **that they require.** In other words, they may use the entire enclosure even if it is very large, just as there are no limits to the size of the enclosed area in which members of a caravan may carry.

Rav Yehuda now applies the framework of primary and adjunct space to a different context, drawing on the *halakha* of *eruv* boundaries for encamped groups. The scenario involves three spaces — A, B, and C — arranged side by side, where the two outer ones (A and C) have overhangs and the middle one (B) does not. There is one person in each space. The question concerns whether these three individuals qualify as a *shayara* — a caravan — which would permit them to enclose a space larger than *beis sasayim* using even minimal (*lavud*-based) wall structures.

The ruling is that they do qualify: *na'asah keshayara v'nosnim lahem kol tzorchan*. From B's perspective, A and C are entirely open to it; all three individuals are effectively present in B. Since three people constitute a *shayara*, B's enclosure can be as large as needed. According to Rashi's reading, it is specifically B's space that may be unlimited, since all three are treated as residents there.

### Opposite Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶמְצָעִי מְגוּפָּף וּשְׁנַיִם הַחִיצוֹנִים אֵינָן מְגוּפָּפִין, וְיָחִיד בָּזֶה וְיָחִיד זֶה [וְיָחִיד בָּזֶה] — אֵין נוֹתְנִין לָהֶם אֶלָּא בֵּית שֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** However, if the **middle** enclosure **protruded, and the two outer ones did not protrude,** i.e., they were narrower than the middle enclosure, so that their entire width was breached into it, **and there is one** person **in this** one **and one** person **in that** one and yet another in the third, **one provides them only** an area of **six *beit se'a*,** in accordance with the *halakha* of individuals in a field, who may enclose an area of only two *beit se'a* per person. As the middle enclosure is larger than the two outer ones, it determines their status, in accordance with the principle stated above, not the other way around. Consequently, the person in the middle enclosure is regarded as though he established residence in only one of the outer enclosures, constituting a group of no more than two, which does not have the legal status of a caravan.

The reverse configuration changes matters considerably. Here B (the middle space) has the overhang and is the larger space, while A and C (the outer spaces) are smaller and lack overhangs. Again there is one person in each. In this arrangement, B sees A as an adjunct and C as an adjunct, but A and C each see only one person associated with themselves — the single person in B can be said to extend toward A, but from C's perspective that same person also extends toward C. No single space accumulates three individuals. The ruling is *ein nosnim lahem ela beis shesh* — they are given a combined total of six *se'ah*, which is simply three times the standard individual allotment of *beis sasayim*, one for each person. No *shayara* status is achieved.

### Ambiguous Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אֶחָד בָּזֶה וְאֶחָד בָּזֶה וּשְׁנַיִם בָּאֶמְצָעִי, מַהוּ? אִי לְהָכָא נָפְקִי — תְּלָתָא הָווּ, וְאִי לְהָכָא נָפְקִי — תְּלָתָא הָווּ. אוֹ דִילְמָא: חַד לְהָכָא נָפֵיק, וְחַד לְהָכָא נָפֵיק. וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר חַד לְהָכָא נָפֵיק וְחַד לְהָכָא נָפֵיק, שְׁנַיִם בָּזֶה וּשְׁנַיִם בָּזֶה וְאֶחָד בְּאֶמְצָעִי מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי, אִי לְהָכָא נָפֵיק — תְּלָתָא הָווּ, וְאִי לְהָכָא נָפֵיק — תְּלָתָא הָווּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא: אֵימַר לְהָכָא נָפֵיק, וְאֵימַר לְהָכָא נָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** Based on these assumptions, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If there is **one** person **in this** outer enclosure, **and one person in the other** outer enclosure, **and two** people **in the middle** enclosure, **what is** the *halakha*? Is the ruling that **if** the pair **exit to here,** one of the outer enclosures, **they are three** people in one place, **and if they exit to there,** the other outer enclosure, **they are three** people in one place, and three people are considered a caravan and provided with all the space they require, as stated above? **Or perhaps,** as **one may exit to here and the other may exit to there,** in which case there would be no more than two people in each enclosure, they are provided with only two *beit se'a* per person. **And if you say** that **one may exit to here and one may exit to there,** if there were **two** people **in this** outer enclosure, **and two** people **in that** outer enclosure, **and one** person **in the middle** enclosure, **what** is the *halakha*? Is the ruling that **here, certainly, if he exits to here they are three, and if he exits to there they are** likewise **three,** and consequently they should be provided with all the space they require in any case? **Or perhaps,** in this case too there is uncertainty, as **say** that **he** might **exit to here, and say** that **he** might **exit to there.** As the direction in which he will leave his enclosure is undetermined, they should be provided with only two *beit se'a* each.

The *gemara* then raises a series of uncertain configurations. The first: what if there are two people in B and one in each of A and C, using the second (opposite) configuration? One might reason that from A's perspective, both people in B are associated with A, yielding three people — *shayara* status. From C's perspective the same logic would apply. Or perhaps one person from B is associated with A and the other with C, leaving each side with only two — no *shayara*. A further scenario: two in A, two in C, and one in B. From A's perspective, the single B-resident joins them for three; from C's perspective the same. Or perhaps the one person in B can go in only one direction, and since we do not know which, neither A nor C can count on having three.

### Leniency Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא בַּעְיַין לְקוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** These dilemmas were essentially left unresolved, **but** the ***halakha* is** that **these dilemmas** are decided **leniently, and** they are provided with all the space they require in these cases.

The *gemara* resolves all these open questions with a single ruling: *v'hilchasa ba'ayan lekula* — the *halakha* for all the unresolved cases is lenient. This is consistent with the general posture of *eruvin* toward permissibility in doubtful cases.

## Mound & Wall

### Five & Five Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: גִּידּוּד חֲמִשָּׁה וּמְחִיצָה חֲמִשָּׁה — אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא אוֹ כּוּלּוֹ בְּגִידּוּד אוֹ כּוּלּוֹ בִּמְחִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** **An embankment,** a height disparity between two surfaces **of five** handbreadths, **and** an additional **partition of five** handbreadths **do not join** together to form a partition of ten handbreadths, the minimum height for a partition to enclose a private domain. It is regarded as a partition of ten handbreadths **only if** the barrier **is** composed **entirely of the embankment or if it is** composed **entirely of a partition.**

Rav Chisda raises a new question directly relevant to the laws of *reshus hayachid*: if there is a *giddur* — a raised platform or mound — of five *tefachim*, and atop it stands a wall of five *tefachim*, do the two combine to constitute a valid enclosure? A full *reshus hayachid* requires a height of ten *tefachim*. If the entire elevation were in the ground itself, or entirely in a wall, the answer would be straightforward. But here, five comes from the raised ground and five from a wall built upon it.

### Tosafot Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ וְעֶלְיוֹנָה גְּבוֹהָה מִן הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אוֹ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ גִּידּוּד חֲמִשָּׁה וּמְחִיצָה חֲמִשָּׁה — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If there were **two courtyards, one above the other, and** the **upper one was ten handbreadths higher than the lower one, or if it had an embankment of five** handbreadths **and a partition** of **five** handbreadths, the two courtyards are considered separate domains **and they establish two *eiruvin*,** one for each courtyard, **and they do not establish one *eiruv*.** If the height disparity was **less than** ten handbreadths, the two areas are considered a single domain, and **they establish one *eiruv* and they do not establish two *eiruvin*.** 

Tosafot raises a searching difficulty with this scenario. The *gemara* suggests that such a combination might function as a *mechitza* from one side but not from the other: a person standing on the far side would see a wall of ten and would treat the space as *reshus hayachid*, while a person on the near side — standing at ground level looking at a five-*tefach* mound with a five-*tefach* wall atop it — might not. Tosafot asks whether it is coherent to say that something is a *reshus hayachid* in only one direction. He notes that if this were true, throwing an object from the far side into the enclosure would be *chayav*, while throwing from the enclosure outward would be *patur* — a striking asymmetry that seems halakhically untenable.

### Two Yards Brayta

This implies that the five-and-five configuration constitutes a genuine separation between the two courtyards. Conversely, if the wall is lower — *pachot mikan* — the ruling reverses: *me'arvin echad v'ein me'arvin shnayim*, they must make one unified *eruv* and cannot make two.

The problem this *braita* poses is immediate. Rav Chisda's initial ruling is that five-and-five do *not* combine. If so, from the upper courtyard's perspective there is no genuine wall of ten separating it from the lower courtyard — in which case why can the two not simply make one unified *eruv*? And yet the *braita* says they must make two separate ones, which implies a genuine separation exists.

## Weak Answer

### Lower Perspective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר (רַב): מוֹדֶה רַב חִסְדָּא בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה, הוֹאִיל וְרוֹאֶה פְּנֵי עֲשָׂרָה. אִי הָכִי: תַּחְתּוֹנָה — תְּעָרֵב שְׁנַיִם וְלֹא תְּעָרֵב אֶחָד, עֶלְיוֹנָה — לֹא תְּעָרֵב לֹא אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: Rav Ḥisda concedes** that an embankment and a partition combine **with regard to the lower** courtyard, **since it faces a wall of ten,** i.e., there is a full partition of ten handbreadths before its residents. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** according to this reasoning, the residents of the **lower** courtyard, from whose perspective there is a valid partition, should **establish two *eiruvin*,** i.e., an independent *eiruv*, **and do not establish one *eiruv*** together with the upper courtyard, while the residents of **the upper** courtyard **establish neither one *eiruv* nor two.** The residents of the upper courtyard neither establish an *eiruv* on their own, as it is breached into the lower one, nor can they establish an *eiruv* together with the lower courtyard, because the latter is separated from it.

Rava addresses the difficulty: *modeh Rav Chisda betachtonah haoveret al pnei asara* — Rav Chisda concedes that with respect to the lower courtyard, which looks up at what appears to be a full ten-*tefach* wall, a genuine separation exists. From the lower courtyard's vantage point, the combined height of mound and wall presents a complete barrier. The lower courtyard is therefore genuinely separated and must make its own independent *eruv*.

### Upper Dilemma

The difficulty, however, is with the upper courtyard. From above, the wall is only five *tefachim*, which — on Rav Chisda's reading — does not suffice to create a separation. The upper courtyard therefore cannot make its own separate *eruv*, since from its perspective no genuine wall divides it from the lower courtyard. Nor can it make a unified *eruv* with the lower courtyard, because from the lower courtyard's perspective a full separation does exist. The upper courtyard is entirely stuck — unable to make either a separate or a joint *eruv*: *lo tearav lo echad velo shnayim*.

### Special Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה עֶלְיוֹנָה מְגוּפֶּפֶת עַד עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the **upper** courtyard had full-fledged ten-handbreadth-high walls that **protruded** on both sides of the section of the partition that was merely five handbreadths high, a protrusion that extended **up to ten cubits.** In this case, the upper courtyard is properly enclosed by a partition ten handbreadths high, while the section that is only five handbreadths high is deemed an entrance. Consequently, even the residents of the upper courtyard can establish an *eiruv* on their own. But the two courtyards cannot be merged by a single *eiruv*, because the lower courtyard is enclosed by a partition from which there is no entrance to the upper courtyard.

To resolve this, Rabbah bar Ulla proposes a constrained reading of the *braita*: *kegon shehayta elyonah megufefet ad eser amot* — we are dealing with a special case in which the upper courtyard is itself enclosed on its other sides by walls that reach the full ten *tefachim*. In such a case, the upper courtyard has an independent halakhic enclosure by virtue of those other walls, allowing it to make its own separate *eruv* regardless of the ambiguous status of the shared boundary. The five-and-five wall that faces the lower courtyard need not itself constitute a full *mechitza*, because other walls of the upper courtyard supply the necessary enclosure independently.

### Below Five Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, אִי בָּעֲיָא — חַד תְּעָרֵב, אִי בָּעֲיָא — תְּרֵי תְּעָרֵב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, say the latter clause** of that same *baraita*: If the height disparity was **less than** ten handbreadths, **they** are considered a single domain, and the residents therefore **establish one *eiruv*, but they do not establish two *eiruvin*.** According to the explanation suggested above, that there is a partition ten handbreadths high between the courtyards with an entrance of sorts between them, **if they wish, they establish one *eiruv*,** and **if they wish, they establish two *eiruvin*.** That is the *halakha* in a case of two courtyards with an entrance between them.

The second clause of the *braita* — that when the wall drops below five, the two must make a single *eruv* — is explained as follows. Once the wall falls below five *tefachim*, the total height from the lower courtyard's perspective also drops below ten. The lower courtyard is now *parutz bemilo'o* — completely open to the upper courtyard — and can no longer maintain an independent *eruv*. Both must therefore join together. The upper courtyard, meanwhile, retains the option to separate itself by means of its other walls, but the lower courtyard loses its independent standing entirely. This is a labored and technically constrained explanation of the *braita*, and its complexity signals that the *gemara* is not fully satisfied with it.

## Mareimar Solution

### Five Amot Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַשׁ מָרִימָר: גִּידּוּד חֲמִשָּׁה וּמְחִיצָה חֲמִשָּׁה — מִצְטָרְפִין. אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּנֵי מָר מִידֵּה בִּמְחִיצָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. וְהִלְכְתָא: גִּידּוּד חֲמִשָּׁה וּמְחִיצָה חֲמִשָּׁה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Mareimar taught: An embankment of five** handbreadths **and an** additional **partition of five** handbreadths above it **combine** to form a partition of ten handbreadths. **Ravina met Rav Aḥa, son of Rava,** and **said to him:** Has **the Master taught anything with regard to** this **partition,** whether it is effective or not? **He said to him: No.** The Gemara concludes: The ***halakha*** is that **an embankment of five** handbreadths **and a partition of five** handbreadths **combine** to form an effective partition of ten handbreadths.

The resolution comes from Mareimar, who rules directly and cleanly: *gidud chamisha u'mechitza chamisha mitztarfin* — a mound of five and a wall of five do combine to form a valid *mechitza*. This position cuts through all the difficulties generated by the weak answer, because it accepts the plain reading of the *braita*: the five-and-five scenario genuinely constitutes a full partition on both sides, not merely from one direction.

### Ravina Confirms

Ravina encountered Rav Acha brei de-Rava and asked whether he had heard any teaching on the question of *mechitza* in this context — specifically, whether there was an alternative ruling on the five-and-five combination. The answer was no: *amar lei lo*. No competing position on this question was in circulation.

### Final Ruling

The *halakha* is therefore stated explicitly: *v'hilchesa gidud chamisha u'mechitza chamisha mitztarfin* — a raised mound of five *tefachim* combined with a wall of five *tefachim* atop it does form a complete *mechitza*, constituting a valid enclosure of *reshus hayachid* on both sides. The asymmetric reading — *reshus hayachid* from one direction but not the other — is rejected. The simple sense of the *braita*, which treats the five-and-five combination as producing a genuine separation requiring two separate *eruvin*, is affirmed as the *halakha*.

## Shabbat Changes

### Wall Falls Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: דָּיוֹרִין הַבָּאִים בְּשַׁבָּת, מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּאָסְרוּ? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, תָּא שְׁמַע: חָצֵר גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה לִקְטַנָּה — הַגְּדוֹלָה מוּתֶּרֶת וְהַקְּטַנָּה אֲסוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּפִתְחָהּ שֶׁל גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hoshaya raised a dilemma:** What is the ruling with regard to **residents who arrive on Shabbat,** i.e., who join the residents of a courtyard on Shabbat, e.g., if the wall between two courtyards collapsed on Shabbat so that new residents arrive in one courtyard from the other. Had these people arrived before Shabbat they would have rendered it prohibited for the residents to carry in the courtyard unless they participated with the original residents in their *eiruv*. **Do** these residents **render it prohibited** for the original residents to carry in the courtyard, even if they arrive on Shabbat itself? **Rav Ḥisda said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from the mishna: With regard to **a large courtyard that was breached into a small** courtyard, it is **permitted** for the residents of **the large** courtyard to carry, but it is **prohibited** for the residents of **the small** one to do so. It is permitted to carry in the large courtyard **because** the breach **is** regarded **like the entrance of the large** courtyard. Apparently, even if the breach occurred on Shabbat, it is prohibited for the residents of the small courtyard to carry.

Rav Hoshaya raises an independent question: *diyorin habaim b'Shabbos* — what is the law when the configuration of a courtyard changes on *Shabbos* itself, so that parties who were previously considered separate become, in effect, combined? The question is specifically whether such a mid-*Shabbos* change renders carrying forbidden where it had previously been permitted.

Rav Chisda brings a proof from our *mishna*: *chatzer gedolah shenifretzah liketanah, hagedolah muteres vehaketanah asurah* — the large courtyard that is breached into the small one; the large is permitted and the small is forbidden. He reads *shenifretzah* — "that was breached" — as referring to a wall that fell during *Shabbos*. On this reading, B was permitted when *Shabbos* began because the wall was intact, but once the wall fell, B became forbidden. This would demonstrate that changes occurring during *Shabbos* can retroactively alter the *halakhic* status of a space.

### Raba Interprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: אֵימַר מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם נִפְרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: Say** that the mishna is dealing with a case where **it was breached while it was still day,** i.e., on Friday. However, there is no prohibition if the breach occurred on Shabbat itself.

Raba challenges this reading. He suggests that *shenifretzah* does not necessarily imply the wall fell during *Shabbos*: *eimar mibod yom nifretzah* — one may equally read it as the wall having already fallen before *Shabbos* began. In that case, the *mishna* describes a static situation present at the onset of *Shabbos* and says nothing at all about mid-*Shabbos* changes.

### Abaye Insists

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לָא תֵּימָא מָר ״אֵימַר״, אֶלָּא וַדַּאי מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם נִפְרְצָה. דְּהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר, בְּעַי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא וּבְעַי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב יְהוּדָה: עֵירַב דֶּרֶךְ הַפֶּתַח וְנִסְתַּם הַפֶּתַח, עֵירַב דֶּרֶךְ חַלּוֹן וְנִסְתַּם הַחַלּוֹן, מַהוּ? וְאָמַר לִי: שַׁבָּת, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הוּתְּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: The Master should not state: Say,** indicating that it is possible to explain the mishna in this manner. **Rather,** the mishna is **certainly** referring to a case where the courtyard **was breached while it was still day. As Master,** you **are the one who said: I raised a dilemma before Rav Huna, and I raised a dilemma before Rav Yehuda:** If one **established an *eiruv*** to join one courtyard to another **via** a certain **opening, and that opening was sealed** on Shabbat, or if one established an *eiruv* **via** a certain **window, and that window was sealed** on Shabbat, **what is** the *halakha*? May one continue to rely on this *eiruv* and carry from one courtyard to the other via other entrances? **And he said to me: Once** it **was permitted** to carry from courtyard to courtyard at the onset of **Shabbat, it was permitted** and remains so until the conclusion of Shabbat. According to this principle, if a breach that adds residents occurs on Shabbat, the breach does not render prohibited activities that were permitted when Shabbat began.

Abaye, however, insists on a definitive reading: *ela vaday mibod yom nifretzah* — it must mean the wall fell before *Shabbos*, not during *Shabbos*. And critically, this is not merely a textual preference. Abaye reminds Raba that the principle at stake is Raba's own established position — one that Raba himself had applied in an earlier context when he directed Abaye to query both Rav Huna and Rav Yehudah about the following case: if an *eruv* was established through an opening — *eruv derech hapetach* — and that opening was subsequently sealed on *Shabbos*, or an *eruv* was established through a window that was then sealed during *Shabbos*, what is the *halakha*?

### Shabbat Haturah

The response that came back — attributed in the text with some ambiguity of singular and plural — was: *Shabbos keivan shehutar hutar* — since *Shabbos* began in a state of permissibility, it remains permitted. Whatever configuration was in place at the onset of *Shabbos* governs the entire day. A change that occurs *during* *Shabbos* does not alter the status that was established at *Shabbos'* beginning. Abaye presses this principle: if the wall fell before *Shabbos*, then B's status was already fixed as forbidden when *Shabbos* started, and the *mishna* simply describes that. But if the wall fell during *Shabbos*, the principle of *Shabbos keivan shehutar hutar* would apply — and B, which began *Shabbos* in a permitted state, should remain permitted throughout. The *mishna* therefore cannot be describing a mid-*Shabbos* collapse, for if it were, B should still be permitted. This is where the daf concludes.
