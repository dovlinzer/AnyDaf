# Ketubot 40b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 50 Shekel Fine

### 50 Shekel Coverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: חֲמִשִּׁים סְלָעִים אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מִכֹּל מִילֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that **the Merciful One said** that the payment is **fifty *sela* from all** these **matters,** i.e., the fine, degradation, humiliation, and pain.

The *gemara* opens with a probing question on the *mishna*'s framework of payments for rape. Having established that the rapist owes *boshet* and *pegam* in addition to the fifty *shekel* fine, the *gemara* asks: how do we know that the fifty *shekel* does not simply cover everything — shame, depreciation, and all associated injuries? Perhaps *rachmana* said fifty *zuzi* for the whole of it, *mi-kol milei*.

### Virgin vs. Non-Virgin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא יֹאמְרוּ: בָּעַל בַּת מְלָכִים חֲמִשִּׁים, בָּעַל בַּת הֶדְיוֹטוֹת חֲמִשִּׁים?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** That cannot be, as **they will say:** If **one who engaged in forced intercourse with a daughter of kings** pays a sum of **fifty** *sela*, does **one who engaged in forced intercourse with the daughter of commoners [*hedyotot*]** also pay **fifty** *sela*?

Rabbi Zeira attempts to resolve this from logic. *Bas melachim chamishim, bas hedyotos chamishim?* Could it be that the daughter of a king and the daughter of a commoner each receive the same fifty *shekel*, with no adjustment whatsoever for the vastly different degree of shame and injury inflicted? The inequity seems self-evident.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד נָמֵי, יֹאמְרוּ: עֶבֶד נוֹקֵב מַרְגָּלִיּוֹת — שְׁלֹשִׁים, עֶבֶד עוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה מַחַט — שְׁלֹשִׁים?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** This is not a decisive argument, as **if so, with regard to a** Canaanite **slave** killed by an ox, the Torah says that the owner of the ox pays the master of the slave thirty *sela*. There too, **they will say:** For **a slave who pierces** precious **pearls [*margaliyyot*],** a valuable skill, the fine is **thirty** *sela*, and for **a slave who performs** the **needlecraft** of a tailor, a common, less valuable skill, is the fine also **thirty** *sela*?

Abaye, however, pushes back: *i hachi, gabei eved nami.* The same apparent injustice exists in the case of an ox that gores a slave, where the Torah fixes the payment at thirty *shekel* regardless of the slave's earning capacity. An *eved nokeiv margoliyos* — a slave capable of boring pearls and commanding enormous productive value — receives the same fixed payment as a slave fit only to sew with a needle. Yet there the Torah plainly set a fixed amount. So too here, perhaps the Torah simply ordained fifty *shekel* as a fixed sum with no regard for individual variation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִילּוּ בָּאוּ עָלֶיהָ שְׁנַיִם, אֶחָד כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, יֹאמְרוּ: בָּעַל שְׁלֵימָה — חֲמִשִּׁים, בָּעַל פְּגוּמָה — חֲמִשִּׁים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Zeira said** a different proof: **Had two** men **engaged in forced intercourse with her, one vaginal intercourse, and one anal intercourse, they will say:** If one who **engaged in forced intercourse** with **an untainted** virgin pays **fifty** *sela*, does one who **engaged in forced intercourse** with her when she is **tainted,** i.e., after she has engaged in anal intercourse, also pay **fifty** *sela*?

Rabbi Zeira then reformulates the logical argument on different grounds. Consider the following scenario: two men rape the same woman in sequence — *echad ke-darkah ve-echad she-lo ke-darkah*. Tosafot clarifies that the first assailant penetrates vaginally and the second anally — the ordering matters because the second perpetrator also owes the fifty *shekel*, which is possible only if she still retains the halakhic status of *besulah*. Since anal intercourse does not rupture the hymen, the woman is still halakhically considered a virgin for the purpose of the fifty *shekel* fine, and the second rapist pays as well. Now, the *gemara* asks: *yomru ba al sheleimah chamishim, ba al pegumah chamishim?* Will people not object that the man who raped an intact woman pays the same as the man who raped one who had already been violated? That seems patently unreasonable. Surely, since her physical state has changed between the two acts, the payment ought to differ — and this demonstrates that the fifty *shekel* cannot be meant to cover everything including *boshet* and *pegam*, for those would indeed differ.

### Slave Market Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד נָמֵי, יֹאמְרוּ: עֶבֶד בָּרִיא — שְׁלֹשִׁים, עֶבֶד מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין — שְׁלֹשִׁים?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** This too is no proof, as **if so, with regard to a slave as well, they will say** if one whose ox gored **a healthy slave** pays **thirty** *sela*, does one whose ox gored **a slave afflicted with boils** also pay **thirty** *sela*?

Abaye again answers with the slave analogy: *yomar eved bari shloshim, eved mukeh shchin shloshim* — a healthy, able-bodied slave and one covered with boils both receive the same thirty-*shekel* payment when gored by an ox. Physical differences between the two slaves do not alter the fixed sum. So the Torah's use of a fixed amount does not by itself prove that individual variation in *boshet* and *pegam* is separately compensated.

### Verse Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִינָּהּ״, הָנֵי תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִינָּהּ, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** a different proof. **The verse says:** "Fifty shekels of silver…**because he tormented her"** (Deuteronomy 22:29); **these** fifty *sela* are the fine paid **because he tormented her.** From which it **may be inferred that there are** the additional payments of **humiliation and degradation** beyond that sum mentioned in the verse.

The *gemara* therefore concludes that neither logical argument suffices on its own, and turns instead to explicit *pesukim*. Abaye derives it from the verse *tachat asher ina* — the fifty *shekel* is payment specifically for the act of coercive intercourse itself. The very formulation *tachat asher ina* implies that this payment is for the oppression, the act of rape — *miklal de'ika boshet u'pgam*, which tells us by implication that there are additional payments for the associated shame and depreciation that lie outside the scope of the fifty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָתַן הָאִישׁ הַשּׁוֹכֵב עִמָּהּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה חֲמִשִּׁים כָּסֶף״. הֲנָאַת שְׁכִיבָה חֲמִשִּׁים, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** an alternative proof. **The verse says: "And the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty shekels of silver"** (Deuteronomy 22:29). For **the pleasure of lying** with her he pays **fifty** *sela*; from which it **may be inferred that there are** the additional payments of **humiliation and degradation,** beyond payment of the fine.

Rava derives the same principle from a different verse: *v'natan ha'ish hashocheiv imah l'avi ha-na'ara chamishim kasef* — the man who lay with her shall pay fifty *kesef* to the father. By combining the act of lying with her and the payment, the verse indicates that *hana'at shchiva chamishim* — the fifty covers the pleasure of the sexual act. *Miklal de'ika boshet u'pgam*: the embarrassment and depreciation are separate injuries not subsumed under this payment. It is worth noting that the framing of this as *hana'at shchiva* — the pleasure of intercourse — is somewhat jarring from the victim's perspective, since this is manifestly a case of rape. The *gemara* is, however, simply using the verse's language to delineate the precise scope of the fixed payment.

## Payment Recipients

### Woman or Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְדִידַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that these additional payments are paid **to her?**

Having established that *boshet* and *pegam* are paid in addition to the fifty *shekel*, the *gemara* turns to a further question: to whom do these additional payments go? The Torah explicitly directs the fifty *shekel* to the father, but the *boshet* and *pegam* were inferred implicitly. Perhaps they belong to the woman herself. *Ve'eima l'didah* — maybe those payments go directly to her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּנְעוּרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״, כׇּל שֶׁבַח נְעוּרֶיהָ — לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **the verse says:** "Between a father and his daughter, **being in her young womanhood, in her father's house"** (Numbers 30:17), from which it is derived that **all profits** of **her young womanhood** go **to her father.**

Tosafot notes the most immediate counter-argument: if *boshet u'pgam* were the woman's payments, then in a case of *pitui* — consensual seduction — there would be no such payment, since a consenting woman has waived her right to compensation for shame and depreciation. Yet the *halacha* is that *boshet u'pgam* are owed even in *pitui*, demonstrating that these payments belong to the father. Even where the daughter consents, the father has not consented, and the obligation persists. Still, the *gemara* seeks a verse basis for this conclusion.

### Youth Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂה הַבַּת לְאָבִיהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִי יִמְכּוֹר אִישׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ לְאָמָה. מָה אָמָה, מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְרַבָּהּ — אַף בַּת, מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However,** with regard to **that** which **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: From where** is it derived **that the handiwork of the daughter** goes **to her father?** It is **as it is stated: "And if a man sells his daughter as a maidservant"** (Exodus 21:7), juxtaposing his daughter to a maidservant: **Just as a maidservant, her handiwork** belongs **to her master,** as she was sold for that purpose; **so too a daughter, her handiwork** goes **to her father.**

The *gemara* invokes a verse from the laws of vow annulment: *binureha beit aviha* — while she is in her youth in her father's house. From this, a general principle is proposed: *kol shevach neurim l'aviha* — all benefits deriving from her youth belong to the father. If so, any monetary claim arising from her status as a young woman in her father's household would revert to him, including *boshet u'pgam*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִ״בִּנְעוּרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״! אֶלָּא, הַהִיא בַּהֲפָרַת נְדָרִים הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** Why do I** need this complicated proof? **Let him derive it** directly from the verse **"Being in her young womanhood, in her father's house." Rather,** this cannot be derived from that verse because **that** verse **is written with regard to the nullification of vows.** A young woman is subject to her father's authority with regard to her vows, which he has the right to nullify. The matter of monetary rights is not addressed in that verse.

The *gemara* then cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *minayin she'maaseh bat l'aviha?* — from where do we know that a daughter's earnings belong to her father? The answer is derived from the verse *vechi yimkor ish et bito le'amah* — that a man may sell his daughter as a maidservant. When she is sold, her earnings transfer to the new master; this teaches that when she is not sold, her earnings belong to the father.

### Types of Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִינֵּיהּ, מָמוֹנָא מֵאִיסּוּרָא לָא יָלְפִינַן. וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִקְּנָסָא, מָמוֹנָא מִקְּנָסָא לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say: Let us derive** monetary matters **from** vows, i.e., just as she is subject to her father's authority with regard to vows the same is true with regard to monetary matters, **we do not derive monetary** matters **from ritual** matters. **And if you say: Let us derive** that her father receives payment **from** the *halakha* of **a fine,** i.e., just as the fine is paid to her father, as explicitly stated in the Torah, so too, other payments are also paid to her father, **we do not derive monetary** matters **from fines.**

The *gemara* challenges the broad reading of *binureha beit aviha*: if that verse established a sweeping principle that the father receives all benefits from his daughter, there would have been no need for the separate derivation regarding her earnings from the verse about selling her. The fact that a distinct source is required for her earnings shows there is no general umbrella principle. Why not? *ממונא מאיסורא לא ילפינן* — rights pertaining to *issur*, such as the annulment of vows, do not automatically translate into monetary rights. The verse *binureha beit aviha* speaks only to the father's right to annul vows. The verse of *vechi yimkor* speaks to his right to her earnings. Neither covers the third category: payments arising from a physical injury done to her person.

As Tosafot observes, this is genuinely a distinct category. One can understand that a daughter's labor earnings go to her father, and separately that he may dissolve her vows. But when it comes to compensation for a bodily violation, the argument that she personally suffered and should personally receive payment is not without force. The *gemara* acknowledges this and declines to conflate the categories.

### Father's Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּאָבִיהָ הָוֵי, דְּאִי בָּעֵי מָסַר לַהּ לִמְנֻוּוֹל וּמוּכֵּה שְׁחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains that **it is reasonable that** the payments of humiliation and degradation are paid **to her father, as if he wished** to do so he could **give her** hand in marriage **to a repulsive** man **or one afflicted with boils,** thereby humiliating her. Since her humiliation is under his control, payment for her humiliation is similarly his.

*Ve-chi teima nilaf mi-qenasa* — perhaps we should derive from the fact that the fifty *shekel* goes to the father that *boshet u'pgam* go to him as well? No: *mamona mi-qenasa lo yalpinan* — a fine is treated as an exceptional payment and cannot serve as a paradigm for regular monetary compensation. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *mistavra de'aviha havei* — there is a reasoned basis to assign these payments to the father. *Ve'i bayit mosrah le-menuval u-le-mukeh shechin* — the father holds power over these matters because he controls who she marries. The *pegam*, the loss of virginity, is something he has the authority to effectuate by marrying her off. The *boshet*, the shame, is likewise something over which he has practical control — he can choose to marry her to a person of low social standing, which would itself constitute a form of degradation. Because he has this control over her status and honor, the payments for their impairment logically flow to him.

One might observe that this reasoning has its tensions. The shame in question is presumably suffered by the woman herself, not primarily by the father. If she is the one shamed, why does she not receive the payment directly, especially in a case of consensual seduction where she willingly incurred the shame? The *gemara*'s position seems to be that even in *pitui*, where the woman consented, she is nonetheless considered to have suffered *boshet* — and yet the payment goes to the father by virtue of his structural authority over these matters. The question of *tza'ar* — pain and suffering — remains somewhat unresolved in this framework, since the father's analogous control over her physical suffering is harder to establish.

## Slave Market Valuation

### Virgin vs. Non-Virgin Slave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּגָם, רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא שִׁפְחָה נִמְכֶּרֶת. הֵיכִי שָׁיְימִינַן לַהּ? אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן בֵּין שִׁפְחָה בְּתוּלָה לְשִׁפְחָה בְּעוּלָה לְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: How is her **degradation** assessed? **One considers her as though she were a maidservant sold** in the marketplace, and assesses how much she would have been worth beforehand and how much she would be worth currently. The Gemara asks: **How do we assess her** value? **Shmuel's father said: One estimates** the difference **between how much a person is willing to give** to purchase **a virgin maidservant and** how much he is willing to give **to** purchase **a non-virgin maidservant to serve him.**

Having established that *pegam* is paid as a separate monetary compensation to the father, the *gemara* asks how its amount is determined. The *mishna* already stated that the evaluation is done as though she were a slave woman being sold. The *gemara* asks: *heichi shammenan lah?* — how exactly is she evaluated? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *omdim kamma adam rotze liten bein shifcha betula le-shifcha be'ula lishamesho* — we estimate how much more a person would pay for a virgin maidservant versus a non-virgin one in order to have her serve him.

### Slave Marriage Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ? אֶלָּא בֵּין שִׁפְחָה בְּעוּלָה לְשִׁפְחָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּעוּלָה, לְהַשִּׂיאָה לְעַבְדּוֹ. וּלְעַבְדּוֹ מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ? בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְרַבּוֹ קוֹרַת רוּחַ הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **a non-virgin maidservant to serve him;** if he purchases her for service, **what difference is there to him** whether or not she is a virgin? **Rather,** the difference **between a maidservant who engaged in intercourse and a maidservant who did not engage in intercourse** is with regard to how much one is willing **to marry her to his slave.** The Gemara further asks: **And** with regard to marrying her **to his slave, what difference is there to him** whether or not she is a virgin? The Gemara answers: It is **with regard to a slave from whom his master has** a sense of **satisfaction,** and he seeks a virgin for the slave in order to reciprocate. The difference between the price that the master is willing to pay for each of the maidservants is the degradation that the offender pays the victim.

The *gemara* immediately objects: *shifcha be'ula lishamesho mai nafka lei minei?* If the slave woman is being purchased simply to work in the household, what difference does her virginity make to the buyer? The *gemara* therefore clarifies: the correct formulation is *bein shifcha be'ula le-shifcha she'eina be'ula le-hasi'ah le'avdo* — the buyer is purchasing her with the intention of marrying her off to his slave. In that context, the slave himself cares whether his wife is a virgin, and the master is willing to pay more to acquire a virgin for the slave's benefit. The *gemara* refines this further: specifically this applies to a slave for whom the master has affection — *be'eved she-yesh lo le-rabbo korat ruach heimenu* — and therefore wishes to do the slave a favor.

### Tosafot's Critique

Tosafot raises a significant difficulty with this entire evaluative model. By the standard method of assessing damages through the slave market, there is at least a rough correspondence between the slave's market value and the actual economic loss. If a slave capable of highly skilled work is injured and loses that capacity, the depreciation in his market price would reflect the capitalized value of his lost earning potential. In that sense, the slave market serves as a reasonable proxy for actual loss.

But in the case of *pegam* for a violated woman, the correspondence breaks down. The relevant question is not how much more a slave master would pay to acquire a virgin for his slave's marriage. It is rather what the woman herself has lost by no longer being a virgin — which may include the ability to marry into a family of high social standing, a loss that could be far greater than any difference captured by the slave market price. Tosafot phrases this sharply: *teimah demai shuma hi zo vechi isha chashuva mi-mishpacha gedolah einah nifsedet be-pigmah ella shaveh shifcha ha-niset le'eved?* Is a woman of distinguished lineage not losing far more than merely the slave-market differential between a virgin and non-virgin maidservant?

Tosafot's own resolution is that the evaluation must be carried out with reference to a slave woman *of the type that she is* — *kol isha shamin le-fi mah she-hi be-inyan zeh*. In other words, one would ask how much more a buyer would pay for a virgin of this particular standing and background versus a non-virgin of the same standing, in order to marry her off to a slave. Even so, Tosafot clearly acknowledges the difficulty: it remains the case that the measure is still tied to a slave marriage context and may not fully capture the actual loss to the woman. The *gemara* identifies the problem but the solution is not entirely satisfying.

## Meir vs. Sages

### Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מֶכֶר — אֵין קְנָס. וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קְנָס — אֵין מֶכֶר. קְטַנָּה — יֵשׁ לָהּ מֶכֶר, וְאֵין לָהּ קְנָס. נַעֲרָה — יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס, וְאֵין לָהּ מֶכֶר. הַבּוֹגֶרֶת — אֵין לָהּ לֹא מֶכֶר וְלֹא קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any place where there is sale** by a father of his minor daughter as a Hebrew maidservant, **there is no fine** if she is raped. **And any place where there is a fine,** when a young woman is raped; **there is no sale** by the father. The Gemara specifies: **A minor is subject to sale** by her father, **and she is not entitled to a fine** if she is raped. **A young woman is entitled to a fine** if she is raped **and is not subject to sale. A grown woman is neither subject to sale nor entitled to a fine.**

The *gemara* now returns to a principle that was cited earlier: *kol makom she-yesh mecher ein kenas, ve-kol makom she-yesh kenas ein mecher* — wherever there is the possibility of sale, there is no fine, and wherever there is a fine, there is no sale. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. The operative distinction concerns the father's authority over his daughter at different stages of her development. A *ketana*, a minor girl, can be sold by her father as a maidservant — this is the *mecher*. Once she becomes a *na'ara* at the onset of puberty, she can no longer be sold, but it is at this stage that the fifty *shekel* fine for rape or seduction applies. And once she becomes a *bogeret* — fully mature at twelve and a half — neither applies. Thus for Rabbi Meir, the categories are mutually exclusive: *ketana* has *mecher* but no *kenas*; *na'ara* has *kenas* but no *mecher*; *bogeret* has neither.

### Sages' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס בִּמְקוֹם מֶכֶר. דְּתַנְיָא: קְטַנָּה מִבַּת יוֹם אֶחָד וְעַד שֶׁתָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יֵשׁ לָהּ מֶכֶר וְאֵין לָהּ קְנָס. מִשֶּׁתָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת עַד שֶׁתִּיבְגַּר — יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס וְאֵין לָהּ מֶכֶר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מֶכֶר — אֵין קְנָס, וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קְנָס — אֵין מֶכֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: קְטַנָּה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד וְעַד שֶׁתִּיבְגַּר — יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This** *halakha* in the mishna is **the statement of Rabbi Meir, but the Rabbis say: She is entitled to a fine** even **where** there is **a sale, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A minor** girl, **from one day old until she grows two** pubic **hairs, is subject to sale and is not entitled to a fine. From when she grows two** pubic **hairs** and becomes a young woman **until she matures** into a grown woman, **she is entitled to a fine and she is not subject to sale;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir, as Rabbi Meir would state** the principle: **Any place where there is a sale there is no fine, and any place where there is a fine there is no sale. And the Rabbis say: A minor** girl **from the age of three years and one day until she matures** into a grown woman **is entitled to a fine.**

The Sages disagree. A *braita* records their view: *ketana mi-bat shalosh shanim ve-yom echad ve-ad she-tivager yesh lah kenas* — a minor girl from three years and one day until she becomes a *bogeret* has a *kenas*. The *kenas* and *mecher* therefore overlap. From three years and one day onward, intercourse with a minor is legally significant as an act of sex, and therefore the fine applies. Yet the father can still sell her during the years she is a *ketana*. The Sages thus hold that *kenas* can coexist with *mecher* for the period from age three to the onset of puberty.

### Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלוֹ תִּהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה״ — בִּמְהַוָּה עַצְמָהּ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir?** It is as **the verse says** with regard to a rapist: "And the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty *sela*, **and to him she shall be as a wife"** (Deuteronomy 22:29). **The verse is speaking with regard to one** halakhically competent to **marry** by **herself,** which means she must be a young woman.

Rav Chisda asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Meir?* — what is Rabbi Meir's textual basis? The verse by rape states *v'lo tiyeh le'ishah* — she shall become his wife. The use of a verb form that implies her own participation in the betrothal — since a *na'ara* can herself accept *kesef kiddushin* — indicates that the Torah is speaking of a *na'ara* specifically, one who is herself an active party to marriage. A *ketana* cannot independently effectuate her own marriage; only the father acts on her behalf. The Torah's framing therefore implies that the *kenas* applies only to a *na'ara*.

### Missing Letter Hei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״נַעֲרָ״, אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** And** what is the rationale for the opinion of **the Rabbis? Reish Lakish said** that **the verse says "young woman [*na'ara*]";** however, although the term is pronounced *na'ara*, it is written as *na'ar*, without the letter *heh*, and **even a minor girl is indicated** by that term.

Reish Lakish offers the Sages' response. The word *na'ara* in the passage concerning rape is written in the Torah without the letter *hei* — defectively spelled. This defective spelling suggests something less than a full *na'ara* is intended: *afilu ketana be-mashma* — even a minor is included. The absence of the *hei* signals that the halakhic scope of the verse is broader than the standard meaning of *na'ara*.

### Motzi Shem Ra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתְנוּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה״, ״נַעֲרָה״ מָלֵא דִּיבֵּר הַכָּתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish said: **One who slanders a minor girl,** falsely claiming that she was not a virgin on the wedding night, **is exempt** from paying the fine, **as it is stated: "And they shall give them to the father of the young woman [*na'ara*]"** (Deuteronomy 22:19). **The verse spoke of *na'ara*,** not only pronounced but written **in full,** indicating that this payment is in effect only with regard to a young woman, not a minor girl.

A further teaching of Reish Lakish appears in this context and sharpens the analysis. Reish Lakish holds that *ha-motzi shem ra a-ketana patur* — the laws of *motzi shem ra*, the husband who falsely claims his wife was not a virgin, do not apply if the wife was a *ketana* at the time of marriage. This is true even according to the Sages who hold that a *ketana* is subject to the rape *kenas*. The basis for this distinction is that by *motzi shem ra*, the Torah writes *na'ara* spelled fully — *na'arah malei diber ha-katuv* — with the *hei*, indicating that only a genuine *na'ara* and not a *ketana* is meant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״נַעֲרָה״, הָא לָאו הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְאִם אֱמֶת הָיָה הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה לֹא נִמְצְאוּ בְתוּלִים לַנַּעֲרָה. וְהוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הַנַּעֲרָה אֶל פֶּתַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ וּסְקָלוּהָ״, וּקְטַנָּה לָאו בַּת עוֹנָשִׁין הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava** strongly **objects to this: The reason is that the Merciful One writes "*na'ara*"** with a *heh*. Is that to say that **if that were not the case I would have said that** this *halakha* applies **even** to **a minor girl?** How is that possible? **But isn't it written: "And if this matter was true, that the hymen of this young woman was not found intact; then they shall remove the young woman to the entrance of her father's house and stone her"** (Deuteronomy 22:20–21). **And** this verse clearly refers to a young woman old enough to be punished, as **a minor is not subject to punishment.**

Rav Adda bar Ahava raises an objection: is it really necessary to derive the adult status of the woman in *motzi shem ra* from the full spelling of *na'arah*? The Torah itself describes the possibility of her being executed for adultery — *v'im emet hayah ha-davar ha-zeh lo nimtze'u vetulim la-na'arah ve-hotzi'u et ha-na'arah el petach beit aviha u-sekoluha*. A minor is not subject to capital punishment. The very content of the passage establishes she must be an adult; no inference from spelling is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כָּאן נַעֲרָה, הָא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נַעֲרָ״ — אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, here,** with regard to the payment of a slanderer, the verse speaks of **a *na'ara*,** written with a *heh*, and a minor is excluded. This is a paradigm from which **it may be inferred** that in any place **that it is stated "*na'ara*"** without a *heh*, **even a minor girl is indicated.**

The *gemara* accepts this challenge and reframes the analysis. We do not derive that *motzi shem ra* requires an adult woman from the full spelling of *na'arah*. Rather, we know she is an adult from the content of the passage. Once we have independently established her adult status there, the full spelling of *na'arah* with the *hei* can serve as a contrast: *ella hacha na'arah, ha kol makom she-ne'emar naar, afilu ketana be-mashma* — where we know it is an adult, the full spelling marks that explicitly; where *na'ara* appears without the *hei*, the implication extends even to a minor. This is how the Sages derive that the rape *kenas* applies even to a *ketana*.

## Admission & Fines

### Confession Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר: ״פִּתִּיתִי אֶת בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — מְשַׁלֵּם בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says: I seduced the daughter of so-and-so, pays** compensation for **humiliation and degradation** based **on his own** admission, **but does not pay** the **fine.**

The *mishna* now turns to the distinction between fines and monetary compensation in the context of self-incrimination. *Ha-omer pitisi et bito shel ploni* — someone who says, I seduced this man's daughter: *meshalem boshet u'pgam al pi atzmo* — he pays for shame and depreciation on the basis of his own admission, *ve'ein meshalem knas* — but he does not pay the fine.

### Fine vs. Compensation

The principle at work is fundamental: fines are punishments imposed by the court and can only be established through external testimony. An individual's self-incrimination does not generate an obligation to pay a *knas*. Indeed, it goes further — in some configurations, a prior admission can even exempt a person from a fine later, even if witnesses subsequently appear. But at a minimum, an admission alone is never sufficient to obligate payment of a fine. Monetary compensation, by contrast, operates on normal evidentiary principles: if you admit you caused someone a loss, you owe them for it. *Boshet u'pgam* are monetary payments — *mamon* — not punitive fines, and therefore they are payable on self-admission.

### Theft Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: ״גָּנַבְתִּי״ — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **one who says: I stole, pays the principal,** the value of the stolen goods, **based on his own** admission, **but does not pay** the **double payment and** the **payment four and five** times the principal for the slaughter or sale of the sheep or ox that he stole.

The *mishna* extends the principle to other cases. *Ha-omer ganavti* — one who confesses to theft: *meshalem et ha-keren al pi atzmo* — pays the principal on his own admission, *ve'ein meshalem tashlumei khefel ve-tashlumei arba'a va-chamisha* — but does not pay the double payment or the fourfold and fivefold payments. Those are fines, and fines require external witnesses.

### Exceeding Damages Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַמְשַׁלֵּם יָתֵר עַל מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק — אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle: Anyone who pays more than what he damaged,** the payments are fines and therefore **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission.

How do we identify a payment as a fine rather than compensation? The *mishna* states the rule: *kol ha-meshalem yater al mah she-hizik eino meshalem al pi atzmo* — any payment that can exceed the actual damages is not payable on self-admission. If a payment can ever be higher than the real loss, it cannot be pure compensation; it must be a punitive surcharge, i.e., a fine. The thirty *shekel* for an ox that gores a slave illustrates this: since some slaves are worth less than thirty *shekel*, there are scenarios where the payment exceeds the actual loss. Therefore the thirty *shekel* is a *knas* and cannot be imposed on self-admission alone. It is important to note the precise formulation: the *mishna* does not say that a fixed payment is automatically a fine. A fixed payment that always fell below or at actual damages could simply be a cap on compensation. It is specifically the possibility of *exceeding* actual damages that marks a payment as a fine.

## Seduction vs. Rape

### Rape Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלִיתְנֵי ״אָנַסְתִּי״! לָא מִבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִבַּעְיָא ״אָנַסְתִּי״, דְּלָא קָא פָּגֵים לַהּ, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, אֲבָל ״פִּתִּיתִי״, דְּקָא פָּגֵים לַהּ, אֵימָא: לָא מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And let** the *tanna* **teach** this *halakha* with regard to one who said: **I raped** the daughter of so-and-so. Why did the mishna cite the case of seduction? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **is speaking** employing the style: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** for the mishna to cite the case of one who says: **I raped** her, **where he does not tarnish her** reputation and merely incriminates himself, as it is obvious that **he pays** compensation for **humiliation and degradation** based **on his own** admission.

The *gemara* asks: why does the *mishna* use the case of *pitui*, seduction, to illustrate the rule of admission? Why not use *ones*, rape? *Lo mivaiya anasti* — it is self-evident in the rape case: *de-lo kapagim lah* — he is not impugning her reputation. He admits to having committed a forcible act against her. In that case it is obvious that he would pay *boshet u'pgam* on his own admission, since his confession does not spread any ill repute about the woman — the wrongdoing is unambiguously his.

### Seduction Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל ״פִּתִּיתִי״, דְּקָא פָּגֵים לַהּ, אֵימָא: לָא מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** in the case of one who says: **I seduced** her, **where he tarnishes her** reputation as he testifies that she willingly engaged in relations with him, and he is not deemed credible to do so, **say** that **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that even in the case of seduction he pays compensation for humiliation and degradation based on his own admission.

The seduction case, however, is more instructive. When he says he seduced her, he is also saying something about her — *de-kapagim lah* — he is spreading a damaging report about this woman's conduct. This raises the question of whether we should accept his admission at all, since doing so would in effect validate a claim that tarnishes her reputation.

### Rebbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אַף בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ — לֹא כׇּל הֵימֶנּוּ שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Even** the payments of **humiliation and degradation, he does not pay** them based **on his own** admission as **it is not within his power to tarnish** the reputation of **the daughter of so-and-so** based merely on his confession.

A *tanna* disagrees with the *mishna*'s ruling. A *braita* records that Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *af boshet u'pgam eino meshalem al pi atzmo* — even the monetary payments of *boshet u'pgam* are not payable on self-admission in the seduction case. *Lo kol heimenu she-yifgom bito shel ploni* — we do not empower him to impugn this man's daughter by accepting money on the basis of his self-serving confession. Accepting the money validates his account and thereby does harm to her reputation.

### Family Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: נִיחָא לַהּ לְדִידַהּ, מַאי? דִּלְמָא לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְאָבִיהָ. נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְאָבִיהָ, מַאי? דִּלְמָא לָא נִיחָא לְהוּ לִבְנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה. נִיחָא לְהוּ לִבְנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה, מַאי? אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלֵיכָּא חַד בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם דְּלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** According to Rabbi Shimon, if **she herself is amenable** to his claim, and admits that his version of the events is accurate, **what** is the *halakha*? Is he exempt from payments of humiliation and degradation in that case as well? Abaye responded: **Perhaps her father is not amenable** to his daughter's reputation being tarnished. We therefore do not rely on his statement even in this case. Rav Pappa continued: If **her father is** also **amenable** to his claim, **what** is the *halakha*? Abaye responded: **Perhaps her** other **family members are not amenable,** as the reputation of the entire family would be tarnished. Rav Pappa asked: If the **family members** too are **amenable, what** is the *halakha*? Abaye answered: Even if all the local relatives are amenable, **it is impossible that there** will **not** be at least **one** relative **in a country overseas who is not amenable** to his claim.

This raises a broader question about the scope of this concern. Even if the immediate parties — the daughter and the father — are willing to accept the money, perhaps the extended family would be dishonored by having the matter adjudicated in this way. The *gemara* considers progressively whether we protect the family's interest even when the woman consents, even when the father consents, and even when the family at large appears to consent. Ultimately the *gemara* suggests that we cannot be certain there is no relative somewhere who would object — *ee efshar de-leka chad be-medinas ha-yam de-lo nicha lei* — and therefore the court's responsibility to protect innocent parties from reputational harm may override the willingness of the immediate parties to proceed.

### Tosafot Distinction

Tosafot draws an important distinction within this discussion. *Hainu de-mishum de-kodam tev'u ha-av techilah, ein tzarich lomar lav kol heimenu* — if the father or the daughter was the one who initiated the lawsuit, that changes matters entirely. When the complainant themselves comes to court and asserts that the seduction occurred, they are the ones placing the matter before the court, not merely acquiescing to someone else's claim. In that case, even according to Rabbi Shimon's approach, his admission would presumably result in payment, since the reputational damage is now coming from the aggrieved party themselves rather than from the alleged perpetrator.

## Half Damages Debate

### Rav Pappa View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא, רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא — מָמוֹנָא. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא — קְנָסָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the payment of **half the damage** that the owner of an innocuous ox, which was not yet witnessed goring a person or an ox three times, must pay to the owner of the ox that he gored. **Rav Pappa said: Half the damage** is considered a payment of **money,** compensation for the damage caused. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said** that **half the damage** is considered payment of a **fine.**

The *gemara* now takes up a fundamental dispute about the nature of the half-damages payment (*palga nizka*) owed by the owner of a *shor tam*, a non-habitual gorer. Rav Pappa holds that *palga nizka mamona* — the half-damages payment is monetary compensation, not a fine. His reasoning: *stam shevarim lav be-chezkat shimur kaimi* — one cannot assume that ordinary oxen are naturally self-restrained. An owner has a genuine obligation to watch even a tame ox. *U-be-din hu de-meshalem kulei* — strictly speaking, if his ox gores someone, he ought to pay full damages, because he is negligent in not having watched his animal. The Torah, however, was lenient — *rachmana hu de-chayis alei, de-achatti lo ya'id torei* — since the ox had not yet been forewarned and established as habitual, the Torah showed compassion and required only half. The half is thus a reduced compensation: the owner was barely over the line of negligence, so he pays half rather than the full amount he would otherwise owe.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא מָמוֹנָא, קָסָבַר: סְתָם שְׁוָורִים לָאו בְּחֶזְקַת שִׁימּוּר קָיְימִי. וּבְדִין הוּא דִּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוּלֵּיהּ, וְרַחֲמָנָא הוּא דְּחָיֵיס עִלָּוֵיהּ, דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא אִיַּעַד תּוֹרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **Rav Pappa said: Half the damage** is considered a payment of **money,** as **he maintains: Standard oxen do not exist in the presumptive status of safety,** and therefore are likely to cause damage. **And by right,** the owner **should pay the entire** damage caused by his animal, **and it is the Merciful One that has compassion on him, as his ox is not yet forewarned** until it has gored a person or an animal three times.

### Rav Huna View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא קְנָסָא, קָסָבַר: סְתָם שְׁוָורִים בְּחֶזְקַת שִׁימּוּר קָיְימִי. וּבְדִין הוּא דְּלָא לִישַׁלֵּם כְּלָל, וְרַחֲמָנָא הוּא דְּקַנְסֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִינְטְרֵיהּ לְתוֹרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said** that **half the damage** is payment of a **fine,** as **he maintains: Standard oxen exist in the presumptive status of safety,** and are not dangerous. **And by right,** the owner **should not pay at all,** as the ox goring could not have been anticipated, and therefore the owner bears no responsibility. **And it is the Merciful One that penalized him so that he would guard his ox.** The sum that he pays is a fine.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua disagrees: *palga nizka knasa* — the half-damages is a fine. His reasoning is the reverse: *stam shevarim be-chezkat shimur kaimi* — before an ox becomes habitual, one may assume it will not gore, and therefore the owner bears no genuine obligation to watch it against such an eventuality. *U-be-din hu de-lo leshalem klal* — strictly, the owner should owe nothing, since he was not negligent. Yet the Torah nonetheless imposed payment — *rachmana hu de-kensei, ki heichi de-ninterei le-toreih* — as a deterrent fine to encourage owners to take precautions. The two positions thus argue about which side of the negligence threshold the *tam* owner falls on: Rav Pappa says barely above, making the half a partial compensation; Rav Huna says barely below, making the half a punitive fine.

### Sharing Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימָן: הִיזִּיק, מָה, וְהֵמִית, כְּלָל. תְּנַן: הַנִּיזָּק וְהַמַּזִּיק בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for the proofs cited with regard to this dispute: **Damaged; what; and killed; principle. We learned** in a mishna that if an innocuous ox gored and killed another's ox, both **the damaged and the damager** share **in the payments.**

The *gemara* cites a *mishna* from Bava Kamma: *ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik ba-tashlumin* — the injured party and the injuring party share in the payment, a formulation suggesting that the *nizak* bears part of the loss. This language, it is argued, fits naturally with the view that half-damages is compensation — the *nizak* is entitled to full damages but effectively absorbs half the loss. If half-damages is a fine, however, the *nizak* is not really entitled to anything in principle, since the *mazik* was not negligent. How then can one speak of the *nizak* sharing in the cost?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִפְחַת נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This *halakha* is **necessary only for the degradation of the carcass.**

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that the language of *ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik ba-tashlumin* is taught for the *halacha* of *pechat neveila* — the depreciation of the carcass. After the goring, the *nizak* takes possession of the dead animal. If the carcass depreciates in value between the moment of death and the time of recovery, that loss falls entirely on the *nizak*; the owner of the goring ox bears no share of that depreciation. The *mishna*'s language that both parties share in the payment refers specifically to this aspect: the *nizak* bears the full risk of carcass depreciation, which is why one can say he is included in the loss. This explanation sidesteps the question of whether the half-damages itself reflects compensation or fine logic.

### Mishna Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד, שֶׁהַתָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק מִגּוּפוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה. וְלָא קָתָנֵי שֶׁהַתָּם אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues. **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from a *baraita*: **What is** the difference **between an innocuous and a forewarned** ox? The difference is **that** the owner of **an innocuous** ox **pays half** of the **damage from its body.** Compensation for the damage may be collected only from the body of the ox that gored another ox. If the ox that gored another ox is worth less than half the damage, e.g., if an inexpensive ox killed an expensive one, the injured party receives less than half the damage. **And** the owner of **a forewarned** ox **pays the entire damage from the** owner's **property,** and the value of the ox that gored another ox has no effect on the payment. The *tanna* **did not teach** an additional difference that the owner of **an innocuous** ox **does not pay** based **on his own** admission **and** the owner of **a forewarned** ox **pays** based **on his own** admission.

A further challenge is raised from a *mishna* in Bava Kamma that enumerates the differences between a *shor tam* and a *shor muad*. The *mishna* lists that a *tam* pays half damages collected only from the body of the goring animal, while a *muad* pays full damages from the best of the owner's property. Notably, the *mishna* does not list as a difference that the *tam*'s payment is a *knas* and therefore cannot be collected on self-admission while the *muad*'s payment can. This omission suggests that the two are parallel in kind — both are monetary compensation — which would support Rav Pappa's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תְּנָא וְשַׁיַּיר: מַאי שַׁיֵּיר דְּהַאי שַׁיַּיר! שַׁיַּיר חֲצִי כוֹפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this claim: This *baraita* is no proof, as the *tanna* **taught** some cases **and omitted** others and did not list all the differences between innocuous and forewarned oxen. The Gemara asks: **What** else **did he omit that he omitted this?** The failure to include an item in a list can be deemed insignificant only if it is one of at least two omissions. If there is only one omission, apparently it was omitted advisedly. The Gemara replies: **He omitted** the *halakha* of the **half ransom** as well.

The *gemara* responds: *tanna ve-shayer* — the *mishna*'s list is not exhaustive. The *gemara* asks, however, *mai shayer de-hai shayer* — if you say something was left off, you must identify what else was omitted, since a list does not simply leave out one item. The *gemara* answers that the *mishna* also omitted the question of *chatzi kofer*: when a *shor tam* kills a person, the question of whether half *kofer* is owed is itself a matter of dispute, with Rabbi Yose ha-Gelili holding that a *tam* does pay *chatzi kofer*. Since that distinction is also absent from the *mishna*'s list, the list is plainly incomplete, and the absence of the fine-versus-compensation distinction cannot be taken as proof that the distinction does not exist.
