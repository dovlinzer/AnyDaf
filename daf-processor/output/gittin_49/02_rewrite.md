# Gittin 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tikkun Olam Framework

### Three Categories

Today's daf, 49, opens a new *perek* — *mipnei tikkun olam* — though it draws us into a realm that feels far closer to *Choshen Mishpat* than to the laws of *gittin* we have been studying. The governing question is: which types of creditors and claimants may collect from which grade of real estate? The *mishnah* establishes a three-way division. A *nizak* — one whose property or person has been damaged — collects from the *idit*, the top-grade, grade A real estate of the *mazik*. A creditor who has lent money collects from grade B property. And a woman collecting her *ketubbah* collects from grade C. Each of these carries its own *tikkun olam* logic, and it is precisely that logic which the *Gemara* will now unpack.

The most immediately striking case is the first: why should a *nizak* collect from the *mazik*'s very best property? The stated rationale is deterrence — by forcing the *mazik* to part with the highest-quality land, we incentivize people to be more careful and less negligent about inflicting harm. But the *Gemara* immediately notes that this does not seem to be a rabbinic *tikkun olam* at all: the *Torah* itself states *meitav sadehu u-meitav karmo yeshalem* — he shall pay from the best of his field and the best of his vineyard. So what exactly are the rabbis adding?

### Ishmael vs. Akiva

The answer depends on a foundational dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. According to Rabbi Yishmael, the *Torah* verse, read at face value, means that the *nizak* collects from the best of *his own* fields — that is, the highest-quality property belonging to the *damagee*, not the *damager*. The rabbinic *tikkun olam* then adds a further layer: the *nizak* is entitled to collect from the best of the *mazik*'s property. According to Rabbi Akiva, however, the *Torah* already mandates collection from the best of the *mazik*'s property — so there is no *tikkun olam* for the case of *nezek* at all. The *mishnah*'s *tikkun olam* language, on Rabbi Akiva's reading, applies only to the other categories in the *mishnah*.

### Property Equivalence

The *Gemara* then asks: what precisely does it mean that the *nizak* collects from "the best of his fields"? An initial reading — that damage to grade C property of the *damagee* would be compensated by that same *damagee*'s grade A property — is quickly rejected as absurd. The *Gemara* therefore presents the answer of Rav Acha bar Yaakov: *kigon she-hayesa idit de-nizak k-ziboris de-mazik* — the case under discussion is one where the finest property of the *damagee* is equivalent in quality to the worst property of the *mazik*. In other words, suppose the *damagee* is a person whose best holdings barely qualify as grade C, while the *mazik* owns grade A, grade B, and grade C property. Which of the *mazik*'s lands may the *nizak* collect from?

To make the arithmetic vivid: suppose the damage equals one acre of grade C land, and each ascending grade is worth twice the previous. If the *nizak* is limited to collecting based on the value of his own best property — grade C — he would receive the equivalent in the *mazik*'s grade C land, namely one full acre. But if he may collect from the *mazik*'s grade A property, he receives a quarter of an acre of superior land, equivalent in value to his full acre of grade C. This is the heart of the dispute. According to Rabbi Yishmael, we evaluate what the *nizak* is entitled to based on his own best property — so if his best is grade C, he collects the equivalent from the *mazik*'s grade C. The *tikkun olam* then adds the entitlement to collect from the *mazik*'s best. According to Rabbi Akiva, the *Torah* already grants the *nizak* the right to collect from the *mazik*'s finest holdings, and this is the more powerful disincentive: the prospect of losing prime real estate for a moment of negligence.

It is worth noting that at the end of the day — once the rabbinic *tikkun olam* is in place — everyone agrees that the *nizak* is entitled to collect from the *mazik*'s best property. The dispute is whether that entitlement is *de-oraita* or rabbinic. And lest one wonder why the quality of land matters when the total value is the same, the practical answer is well known: a house or field in a desirable location has an official market price but may be far more liquid and valuable in practice than the equivalent nominal value in a less desirable holding. It makes a significant difference what grade of land one is actually receiving.

## Ishmael's View

### Gezerah Shavah

The *Gemara* now lays out the textual basis for each position. Rabbi Yishmael derives his reading through a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *sadeh* — "field" — appears twice in the relevant passage. The first time it describes the field that was consumed — plainly the *nizak*'s field, since it is the one that was grazed upon. The second time, in *meitav sadehu yeshalem*, it says "the best of the field he shall pay." Since the *sadeh* mentioned below follows the *sadeh* mentioned above — and the upper usage clearly refers to the *damagee*'s field — Rabbi Yishmael reads the lower usage identically: the best of the *damagee*'s field. According to this reading, the verse establishes that collection is measured against the *nizak*'s highest-quality property, and the rabbinic *tikkun olam* upgrades that to the *mazik*'s best.

---

## Akiva's View

### Simple Reading

Rabbi Akiva's position is based on a simple reading of the verse's syntax. *Meitav sadehu yeshalem* — "he shall pay the best of his field." The subject who is paying is the *mazik*, and so the possessive *his* refers to the *mazik*. The verse is grammatically straightforward: the one paying compensates from the best of what belongs to him, the *damager*. Rabbi Akiva does not require a *gezeirah shavah* — the plain sense of the pronouns in the verse already points to the *mazik*'s property.

---

## Ishmael's View…

### Grade Mismatch

How does Rabbi Yishmael then explain the second half of the verse — *meitav sadehu yeshalem* — which Rabbi Akiva reads as referring to the *mazik*'s field? Rabbi Yishmael assigns different work to the *gezeirah shavah* and to the plain sense of the verse respectively: *hani gezeirah shavah ve-hani kra*. The *gezeirah shavah* establishes the baseline rule — the *nizak* collects based on the grade of his own best property. The plain sense of *meitav sadehu* then addresses a distinct scenario: what happens when the *mazik*'s holdings do not include the grade of property that the *nizak* is entitled to collect?

For example, suppose the *nizak*'s best property is grade B, and the *nizak* is therefore entitled to collect grade B from the *mazik*. But the *mazik* owns only grade A and grade C — he has no grade B at all. Must the *nizak* settle for the *mazik*'s grade C? Rabbi Yishmael says no: in that case the *nizak* may collect from the next higher grade available, the *mazik*'s grade A. We cannot force the *nizak* to accept less than what he is entitled to merely because the *mazik* happens not to own the precise grade. This is the function of *meitav sadehu* — "the best of his field" — understood as the *mazik*'s field: when the *mazik* lacks the appropriate grade, the *nizak* may upgrade and collect the closest superior quality the *mazik* possesses.

So on Rabbi Yishmael's account, the framework is: you begin with the *nizak*'s best property as the measure of entitlement; but if the *mazik* lacks that grade, the *nizak* may reach up to the next grade available from the *mazik*'s holdings. What does *tikkun olam* add? In Rabbi Yishmael's scheme, the rabbinic layer is precisely the general rule that the *nizak* collects from the *mazik*'s best — not merely from whatever grade of the *mazik*'s land happens to correspond to the *nizak*'s best. Rabbi Akiva, of course, maintains that this entitlement to collect the *mazik*'s absolute best is already *de-oraita*, and is thus clearly a more powerful disincentive against negligence.

---

## Akiva's View…

### Hekdesh Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Akiva adds a further element to his position: *lo ba ha-katuv ela ligbos la-nizikin min ha-idit, ve-kal va-chomer la-hekdesh* — the verse comes to establish that victims of damage collect from grade A, and *a fortiori* so does *hekdesh*. This *kal va-chomer* requires careful unpacking, and the *Gemara* works through several possibilities.

The initial question is: what does it mean that *hekdesh* should collect grade A as a *kal va-chomer*? In what capacity is *hekdesh* collecting? The first possibility considered is: our ox gored an ox belonging to *hekdesh*. But the *Gemara* immediately raises the verse *ki yigach shor ish et shor re'ehu* — the ox-goring liability applies when a normal person's ox gores *his fellow's* ox. *Hekdesh*'s ox is not the ox of "his fellow" in the relevant sense: *shor re'ehu amar Rachmana ve-lo shor shel hekdesh*. The verse exempts the case where *hekdesh* is either party, so there would be no obligation to pay at all.

The second possibility is a standard monetary obligation to *hekdesh* — for instance, someone who declared *harei alai maneh le-vedek ha-bayit*, pledging a hundred *maneh* to the Temple treasury. In that case the *gizbar* — the Temple treasurer — comes to collect, and the *Gemara* entertains the idea that he could seize grade A property. But this faces a different objection: *lo yehei ella ba'al chov, u-va'al chov dino be-veinonit* — a simple creditor collects grade B, not grade A. Why should *hekdesh* as a creditor fare better? A *kal va-chomer* does not elevate the conclusion above what has been demonstrated; it establishes that the same rule applies, not a superior one. And we have no independent basis to grant *hekdesh* grade A status when collecting a debt.

Moreover, even this *kal va-chomer* could be challenged: *mah le-va'al chov she-ken yafeh kocho be-nizikin, tomar be-hekdesh she-ken hora kocho be-nizikin* — one can refute the analogy by noting that a normal person is in a stronger position regarding damage claims, while *hekdesh* is in a *weaker* position, since *hekdesh*'s ox goring a layperson's ox generates no liability. Since *hekdesh* is disadvantaged in the domain of damages, there is no valid *kal va-chomer* to give it superior collection rights.

At this point the *Gemara* returns to the original scenario — an ox goring *hekdesh*'s ox — and says: *Rabbi Akiva savar lah ke-Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya*, Rabbi Akiva holds in accordance with Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's position on that very question.

## Hekdesh Status

### Hekdesh's Ox

A *baraita* records: *shor shel hekdesh she-nagach le-shor shel hedyot patur* — if *hekdesh*'s ox gores a layperson's ox, there is no liability. *Ve-shel hedyot she-nagach le-shor shel hekdesh, bein tam bein mu'ad meshalem nezek shalem* — but if a layperson's ox gores an ox belonging to *hekdesh*, one pays the full amount regardless of whether the ox has previously gored or not, rather than the half-payment that ordinarily applies for a first-time *tam*. As one participant aptly noted: the house always wins.

### Shimon's View

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya reads the verse *ki yigach shor ish et shor re'ehu* asymmetrically. When *hekdesh* takes the role of the *mazik*, it pays nothing — it falls outside the verse's scope. But when *hekdesh* takes the role of the *nizak*, the layperson pays in full, because the exemption from full payment that applies during the first three gorings is precisely the exemption for the case where *shor re'ehu* — a fellow person's ox — is the *mazik*. If the ox gored *hekdesh*'s property, that exemption no longer applies. *Hekdesh* benefits in both directions: it pays nothing when it is the damager, and it collects in full when it is the damagee. With this framework in place, Rabbi Akiva's *kal va-chomer* becomes intelligible: since *hekdesh* is granted such extraordinary privilege in the law of ox-goring — collecting full damages even from a first-time *tam* — it stands to reason that when *hekdesh* collects, it is entitled to collect from the best property, grade A, just as a normal *nizak*.

### Normal vs. Hekdesh

The *Gemara* then raises a further question about the structure of the dispute. Given that Rabbi Akiva makes his point about *hekdesh* at the end of his position, one might wonder: perhaps Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael are not debating the normal case of *nizak* and *mazik* at all. Perhaps the entire dispute between them concerns only the *hekdesh* question — Rabbi Akiva holding like Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya (that *hekdesh* collects in damage cases), and Rabbi Yishmael holding like the *rabbanan* (that *hekdesh* does not collect in damage cases). On this reading, the two *tannaim* would agree about the normal case, and their disagreement would be confined to whether *hekdesh* has collection rights.

The *Gemara* rejects this interpretation on two grounds. First, if Rabbi Akiva agreed with Rabbi Yishmael on the normal case, his language — *lo ba ha-katuv ela ligbos la-nizikin min ha-idit* — would make little sense, since he would not be expanding the *nizak*'s rights beyond what Rabbi Yishmael already grants. Second, *Rav Ashi* cites an explicit *baraita* that settles the matter: *meitav sadehu shel nizak u-meitav karmo shel nizak divrei Rabbi Yishmael; Rabbi Akiva omer meitav sadehu shel mazik u-meitav karmo shel mazik*. The debate is explicit and unambiguous — Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva are disagreeing about the normal case of *nizak* and *mazik*, not merely about *hekdesh*.

## Rav Ashi's Resolution

### Torah vs. Rabbinic

Now that the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is clearly established, a question remains about the *mishnah* itself. Rav Ashi proposes: *le-olam okimna ke-Rabbi Akiva* — we can read the *mishnah* as going according to Rabbi Akiva throughout. But if Rabbi Akiva already holds that the *Torah* mandates collection from the *mazik*'s best property, why does the *mishnah* describe this as a *tikkun olam*? Rav Ashi answers: *de-Rabbi Shimon hi, de-darash ta'ama dikra*. What the *mishnah* is saying is not that this is a rabbinic enactment layered on top of *Torah* law; rather, it is explicating the *rationale* behind the *Torah*'s own rule. The *mishnah*'s invocation of *tikkun olam* is functioning as a statement of purpose, not as a marker of rabbinic legislation.

### Ta'am HaKra

This invokes the distinct *talmudic* principle associated with Rabbi Shimon: *doresh ta'ama dikra* — he derives legal consequences from his understanding of the reason behind a verse. The key insight from *Tosafot* is that Rabbi Shimon's method is not merely a philosophical exercise in explaining *ta'amei ha-mitzvot*. Rather, he uses the identified rationale to determine the *parameters* and *applications* of the law. *Tosafot* illustrates this with two examples: the prohibition against taking a widow's garment as collateral (*lo tachbol beged almanah*), and the restriction on the king multiplying wives. In the garment case, for instance, Rabbi Shimon arrives at a strikingly unexpected *ta'am*: the concern is not economic oppression of the poor widow but rather the appearance of sexual impropriety, since returning a poor person's garment every evening would require the creditor to visit her home nightly. However counterintuitive, Rabbi Shimon uses this rationale to define who is and is not covered by the prohibition.

### Deterrence Rationale

Applied to our case: *mipnei mah amru ha-nezikin shamin lahem be-idit, mipnei ha-gazlanim u-mipnei ha-chamsanim* — why did they say that damage cases are assessed with grade A property? Because of thieves and *chamsanim*, those who coerce others into selling property against their will. The deterrent logic runs as follows: a person contemplating theft or coercive acquisition should say to himself, *lamah ani gozel, lamah ani chomes — le-machar beit din yordin le-nechasai ve-notlim sadeh na'ah sheli* — "Why should I steal or seize property? Tomorrow the court will come and take my finest field."

This points to a real lacuna in pure compensation logic. If all a *gazlan* need do is return what he took, the disincentive is minimal — he simply gives back the equivalent. The *Torah* does impose double payment on a *ganav* (one who steals covertly) but only equivalent repayment on a *gazlan* (one who seizes openly). Where, then, is the disincentive for open seizure? The answer is that if the *gazlan* cannot pay in cash, he will be made to hand over his best land. That is a genuine deterrent.

The *baraita* adds: *ve-samchu al mah she-katuv ba-Torah meitav sadehu meitav karmo yeshalem* — and the rabbis relied on what is written in the *Torah*. This phrase is itself revealing. The *Torah* explicitly invokes *meitav* only in the case of *shen va-regel* — an animal grazing in another's field. It is not stated explicitly for other categories of damage. What motivated *Chazal* to extend this rule across all damage cases? The *tikkun olam* rationale: once we understand that the *Torah*'s purpose in mandating grade A collection is deterrence, we can reason that if the *Torah* imposes this penalty even for *negligence* — letting one's animal wander onto another's land — certainly the same principle should apply in cases of willful damage or coercive seizure. The *gezeirah shavah* provides the technical mechanism; the *tikkun olam* rationale provides the conceptual justification for extending it broadly. This is Rabbi Shimon *doresh ta'ama dikra* in action: using the identified reason to expand the rule's application beyond its narrow textual context.

## Creditor Protection

### Predatory Lending

Having explained the grade A rule for *nezikin*, Rabbi Shimon proceeds to the second tier of the *mishnah*: *mipnei mah amru be-chov be-veinonit* — why did the rabbis say that a creditor collects grade B? The concern here is predatory lending: *kedei she-lo yireh adam le-chavero sadeh na'ah ve-dirah na'ah ve-yomar ekpotz ve-alvenu kedei she-agvenu be-chovi* — a person should not look at his neighbor's beautiful field and fine house and think, "Let me lend him money, he will default, and I will acquire his property through debt collection." Allowing collection from grade A would invite this sort of predatory calculation. Therefore, the rabbis limited the creditor to grade B.

### Lending Incentives

The *Gemara* immediately asks: if the concern is that lending will be used as a vehicle for seizing the borrower's best property, why not go further and limit the creditor to grade C? The answer is equally direct: *im ken atah no'el delet bifnei lovin* — if you do that, you shut the door before borrowers. The system requires that lending remain viable. This is not a trivial concern in the context of *Torah* law: the prohibition on interest, the *shemitah*-based annulment of loans every seven years, and the general uncertainty of recollection all make lending a poor financial proposition already. We cannot disincentivize it further. The constraint that a creditor may collect grade B but not grade A balances two competing social goods: protecting borrowers from predatory lenders on one side, and protecting the institution of lending on the other. It is worth recalling that the *Gemara* earlier in *Gittin* devoted an entire extended discussion to *pruzbul* — the mechanism Hillel instituted specifically to prevent the social harm caused when lending dried up in the years before *shemitah*. The grade B rule is part of the same ongoing effort to keep credit accessible.

## Ketubah Collection

### Marriage Incentive

The third category in the *mishnah* is *ketubbat isha bi-ziborit* — a woman collects her *ketubbah* from grade C property. This is the view of Rabbi Yehuda; Rabbi Meir holds she collects grade B. Rabbi Shimon, consistent with his practice of identifying the rationale behind rulings, explains: *mipnei mah amru ketubbat isha bi-ziborit* — why did they say the *ketubbah* is collected from grade C? *She-yoter mi-mah she-ish rotzeh liso isha rotzah lihinase'a* — because a woman's desire to marry exceeds a man's. To put it more precisely: men must be incentivized to assume the financial obligations that marriage entails under rabbinic law. The *ketubbah* is an enormous innovation protecting the woman — it provides financial security against divorce and widowhood, and imposes ongoing financial obligations during the marriage. But precisely because it represents a significant liability for the husband, we must make its terms bearable enough that men will be willing to marry. Limiting *ketubbah* collection to grade C land achieves this: the husband can say to himself that even in the worst case, the debt will be settled from his least valuable holdings.

### Divorce Asymmetry

The *baraita* then offers a second rationale — *davar acher* — which is considerably more puzzling: *isha yotzah li-rtzonah ve-shelo li-rtzonah, ve-ish eino motzi ela li-rtsono* — a woman can be divorced whether she wills it or not, while a man only divorces willingly. The *Gemara* immediately asks *mai davar acher* — how is this relevant to the question of *ziborit*? If anything, the fact that a woman can be divorced against her will would seem to argue for *greater* protection, not lesser.

The *Gemara* constructs the following *hava amina*: since we are limiting the *ketubbah* to grade C out of concern for the husband's interests, one might wonder — if we are so solicitous of the husband — why not go further and say that when *he* is the one being divorced (or more precisely, when *she* is the cause of the divorce), he should receive a *ketubbah* from *her*? The *Gemara*'s response is that this is essentially absurd: *efshar de-mashli lah be-gitta* — it is entirely within his power to withhold a *get*. He is always the one who decides whether the marriage ends. He cannot be in the position of the divorce recipient; that entire framework is inapplicable to him. The asymmetry of divorce rights is not a reason to award him a *ketubbah* — it is a reminder that the system as structured places control of the divorce in his hands alone.

What the *davar acher* seems to be gesturing toward is the observation that the woman's financial protection through the *ketubbah* — paid by the husband — is inherently tied to the reality that *he* controls the exit from the marriage. Because she cannot initiate divorce, and because all the financial burden of the *ketubbah* falls on him regardless of who is responsible for the breakdown of the marriage, the rabbis chose not to make that burden so heavy as to be crushing. The grade C limitation reflects a deliberate calibration, not a derogation of the woman's rights. The bottom line, as the *Gemara* affirms, is that the woman never becomes the one to incur debt as a result of divorce — the obligation runs from husband to wife, and the question is only how burdensome to make it.

### Orphans Limitation

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman then offers a restrictive reading of the entire discussion: *lo amru ela mi-yatmei* — the ruling that *ketubbah* is collected from grade C applies only when she is collecting from orphans, meaning after the husband has died and his estate has passed to his children. If she is collecting directly from the husband himself — in the case of divorce, while he is still alive — she collects grade B, like any ordinary creditor. This makes intuitive sense: why should a woman have fewer rights than a standard creditor when collecting from a living husband?

### Grade B Direct

The *Gemara* challenges this reading: if the *mishnah* is discussing *only* collection from orphans, then why does it single out *ketubbat isha* as collecting grade C? There is already a general rule — *ein nifra'in mi-nichsei yetomim ela min ha-ziborit* — one may only collect from orphans' property using grade C. That rule would already cover the *ketubbah*. Why state it separately? The *Gemara* therefore considers the reverse: the grade B versus grade C debate about *ketubbah* is specifically about collection from the husband himself, and the *mishnah* is telling us that even here, the *ketubbah* is grade C (Rabbi Yehuda) or grade B (Rabbi Meir).

The *Gemara* then offers a further possibility: *le-olam mi-yatmei* — the debate really is about orphans — and the novelty of specifying *ketubbat isha* is this: one might have thought that because of *mishum china* — for the sake of the woman's social dignity — the rabbis would elevate the *ketubbah*'s collection rights even from orphans to grade B. *Ka mashma lan* — the *mishnah* therefore teaches us that they did not. Note that *Rashi* and *Tosafot* differ about the meaning of *mishum china*. *Rashi* reads it as incentivizing the woman — making the *ketubbah* attractive enough that women will want to marry. *Tosafot* offers a more indirect reading: if the court consistently forces women to collect from the worst property, their social status declines, which paradoxically disincentivizes men from wanting to marry women of lower social standing. According to *Tosafot*, *mishum china* is ultimately about maintaining the woman's social status high enough that men will want to marry her — but not so high that men are financially crushed.

Rava and Abaye each bring further proofs aimed at resolving whether the grade levels described in the *mishnah* concern collection from the debtor himself or from his orphaned heirs. Abaye's proof is the most decisive in its structure: the *mishnah* lists all three categories — *nezikin* at grade A, creditor at grade B, *ketubbah* at grade C — in a single list. If all three concerned collection from orphans, then there would be no reason to distinguish between them, since the rule of grade C from orphans would flatten the distinction entirely. Clearly the *mishnah* is describing collection from a living debtor. And if the creditor and the *nizak* are collecting from the living debtor at grade B and grade A respectively, then *ketubbat isha* must also be discussing collection from the living husband — not from orphans.

## Guarantor Case

### Guarantor Setup

At this point Rav Acha bar Yaakov proposes a sophisticated resolution that avoids the force of Abaye's proof. The *mishnah*, he suggests, is discussing a case that is neither the debtor himself nor his orphaned children — it concerns a guarantor: *kegon she-na'asa arev le-nizkei bno u-le-va'al chov bno u-le-ketubbat kalato* — someone who agreed to stand as guarantor for the damage obligations of his son, for his son's debt, and for his son's *ketubbah* to his daughter-in-law. Now all three categories of claimants come to collect not from the son himself but from the guarantor.

For the *nizak* collecting from the guarantor, the rate is grade A — the same as if he were collecting from the son directly. For the creditor, grade B. But for the *ketubbah*, only grade C. The *mishnah* thereby establishes distinct collection rights for each category, applicable even to a guarantor, and explains why the *ketubbah* specifically drops to grade C even when the husband is in principle alive and a guarantor is being pursued.

### Lifetime Collection

Why are the rules for *nezikin* and for debt unchanged when moving from the debtor to the guarantor? The *Gemara* explains: *nezikin u-va'al chov de-mechayim gavu* — damages and debts are normally collected while the debtor is alive. Their collection is, so to speak, a *mechayim* transaction. When one then turns to the guarantor, one collects under the same conditions that would have applied against the debtor — as a lifetime collection, at grade A or grade B respectively.

### Post-Mortem Collection

The *ketubbah*, however, is different: *ketubbat isha de-le'achar mita gavya* — a *ketubbah* is normally collected after the husband's death. (Divorce exists, of course, but death is arguably the more common and more natural trigger for *ketubbah* collection, particularly in the context of this discussion.) When one goes to the guarantor and that collection is treated as if it occurs in the mode of a standard *ketubbah* collection — which is a post-mortem collection — then the applicable standard is the one that governs post-mortem collection: *mi-yatmei*, from orphans, at grade C.

*Rashi* in fact sharpens this considerably by suggesting the *Gemara* is discussing a case where the son has already died. In that scenario: for the *nizak* and the creditor, they were entitled to collect from the son *during his lifetime*, and their entitlement does not diminish merely because he has since died and they are now pursuing the guarantor. The grade A and grade B rights travel with them. But for the *ketubbah*, the wife's entitlement arose precisely because the husband died — her cause of collection is the death itself. That being so, even when she goes to the guarantor, she is collecting in a *le'achar mita* mode, which means grade C.

The result is elegant: the case is neither the debtor himself nor his orphaned children, but a guarantor — a genuinely distinct category — and the three-way division of grade A, grade B, and grade C all apply simultaneously and coherently to a single set of claimants approaching the same guarantor.

### Areiv vs. Kablan

The *Gemara* raises a final technical objection: *ve-teipok lei de-areiv de-ketubbah lo mishtabed* — a standard guarantor (*areiv*) does not become obligated for a *ketubbah*, because no money changes hands in the formation of the *ketubbah* obligation, and the guarantor therefore cannot truly be said to have assumed it. The *Gemara* answers: *be-kablan* — the case concerns a *kablan*, a higher-order form of guarantor in which money does effectively change hands and a genuine assumption of obligation takes place. The *Gemara* then notes that this answer works according to the opinion that a *kablan* incurs obligation even where the borrower has no assets of his own — the *kablan* takes on a primary obligation rather than merely a backstop guarantee. The *sugya* concludes at this point, with the resolution of the *mishnah*'s collection hierarchy through the innovative construction of the guarantor case.
