# Bava Batra 136 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matnat Shchiv Mei'ra Bas…

### Opening & Orientation

The daf under discussion is 136, though the material opens on 135b, at the *mishnah* that transitions the tractate into the terrain of *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* — the deathbed bequest — and its written counterpart.

### Oral Deathbed Bequest

*Matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* is the oral deathbed bequest, a special *takana* established by the Rabbis whereby a dying person's verbal declaration of his wishes takes effect at death. Because it is oral, it requires witnesses to establish that it was actually said — but no formal document or act of acquisition is necessary for its validity.

### Diatiki & Meshichah

The *mishnah* shifts focus to the written form of the deathbed bequest, known as a *diatiki* — a Greek term for a written will. The critical distinction is that whereas an oral *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* takes effect the moment the declaration is made (subject to proof), a written will has no binding force until it is physically handed over — until *meshichah*, delivery to the recipient.

### Mishnah: Binding Rules

The *mishnah* illustrates this with a striking case: if a person dies and the written will is found bound to his thigh — a gesture redolent of the ancient Near Eastern oath (recall *sim na yadecha tachat yireichi* in the context of Avraham) — the will is nonetheless not yet legally binding. There is no question that he wrote it; the binding to the thigh establishes authorship beyond doubt. Yet legal force only attaches once the document has been handed over. Once he has transferred it to another person — whether that recipient is an heir or not an heir — *devarav kayamim*, it is valid and binding. The *gemara* will add that this holds even if no specific name appears in the document: a blank-check-style will transferred to the recipient is effective.

### Diatiki vs. Shtar

The *diatiki* must not be confused with a *shtar matana*, an ordinary gift document. A *shtar matana* takes effect immediately upon delivery and operates through the standard mechanisms of property transfer — the document itself effects the *kinyan* of the land by containing language such as *sadi netuna lecha* or *sadi mechura lecha*, which expresses the donor's present intent to convey. The *diatiki*, by contrast, is an expression of the person's last will and testament; it takes effect only at death. The *gemara* makes this distinction precise: a *shtar matana* says *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* — from today and completed at death — whereas the *diatiki*'s defining language is, as the *beraita* will explain, *da tehei lemeikam ulehayos*, "this shall be established and stand" — the classic formula of a last will and testament.

## Diatiki Definition & Mat…

### Diatiki Definition

The *tana rabbanan* offers a formal definition: what is a *diatiki*? Any document in which the language *da tehei lemeikam ulehayos* — "this shall stand and be established" — appears. This is the *gemara*'s characteristic etymological derivation of the Greek legal term through Aramaic phrasing. It is, in essence, the declaration: this is my last will and testament.

### Matnat Bari Structure

The *gemara* then raises the obvious question: what is a gift document? How do we distinguish a *shtar* that is a will from one that is a *shtar matana*? The *gemara* notes that land is acquired through *kesef*, *shtar*, or *chazakah*. For *shtar*, all that is required is that the document contain the operative words — *sadi netuna lecha* or *sadi mechura lecha* — specifying the field, together with delivery of the document. That is a *matana*. The *gemara* then asks: is it exclusively a document that says *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* that qualifies as a *matana*? What about a simple present-tense declaration, *me'achshav*? Of course that is also a gift. The formulation *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* therefore cannot be the general definition of a *matana*.

### Abaye's Explanation

*Ela amar Abaye*: here is what the *beraita* actually means. *Eizehu matnas bari she-hi ke-matnas sh'chiv mei'ra* — what is a gift given by a healthy person (*matnas bari*) that operates not through the special *takana* of *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* but nonetheless approximates its effect? The answer is a gift structured as *me-hayom u-le'achar misa*. Not everyone has the luxury of waiting until they are on their deathbed to arrange their affairs; people who are young and healthy may wish to set up testamentary-like arrangements in advance. The mechanism of *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* is unavailable to them. So they employ a *matnas bari* that achieves a similar result.

### Me-hayom Structure

How does *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* approximate *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra*? The full phrase means: the title (*gufa*) is transferred today, but the *peiros* — the income, the usufruct, the benefit — passes to the recipient only after the donor's death. The next *mishnah* will spell out the practical consequences: since the title passes today, the donor cannot sell the principal; since the donor retains the *peiros* for his lifetime, the recipient cannot yet fully benefit from the property. The donor effectively says: I want you to own this land, but I want to continue drawing the income from it until I die. This is structurally similar to *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra*, where the recipient really only gets full benefit after the donor's death. The choice to transfer title now — thereby surrendering the ability to sell the principal — is admittedly an unusual one. One might ask: why not simply arrange for the acquisition to take effect a moment before death? That would preserve full flexibility in the interim. Yet the *gemara* treats the *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* structure as the classic vehicle, and the following *mishnah* will explore why.

### Retraction Rights

*Tosafos* addresses whether a *matnas bari* structured as *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* can be retracted. The *Rashbam* holds that since the gift is made while healthy, and the *me-hayom* transfers title at the moment of writing, the donor cannot subsequently retract it — this is unlike a *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra*, which can always be superseded by a later will. The *Rashbam* acknowledges, however, that certain other *gemaras* imply that such a gift *can* be retracted, and *Tosafos* raises this tension explicitly.

*Rabbeinu Tam* resolves the tension differently. He argues that when the *gemara* speaks of a *matnas bari she-hi ke-matnas sh'chiv mei'ra*, it refers to a case where the donor explicitly wrote: *me-hayom im lo achzor bi ad le'achar misa* — "from today, provided I do not retract it until after my death." The donor expressly reserves the right of retraction. According to *Rabbeinu Tam*, this explicit reservation is what makes the *matnas bari* truly approximate a *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra*, since wills by their nature are always revocable until death. Without such an explicit clause, a simple *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* would, per the *Rashbam*, be irrevocable. The *Rashbam*'s position and *Rabbeinu Tam*'s position thus represent two distinct readings: the *Rashbam* understands the *matnas bari* as irrevocable absent a specific retraction clause, while *Rabbeinu Tam* holds that the cases under discussion in the *gemara* are specifically those in which retraction was explicitly built in. It is also reasonable to suppose that if the document itself were headed with the notation *matnas bari she-hi ke-matnas sh'chiv mei'ra*, that context alone might suffice to import the right of retraction.

## Writing & Delivery Rule

### Shichiv Mei'ra Order

Rava bar Rav Huna was sitting in the portico (*achsadra*) of the academy of Rav, and taught in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *shchiv mei'ra she-amar kitvu u-tnu mana le-ploni u-met, ein kotvin ve-nosnin* — if a person on his deathbed said "write a document and give a hundred *maneh* to so-and-so," and then died before the document could be written and delivered, one does not write the document and deliver it after his death.

The practical issue is this: a verbal declaration of *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* — simply saying "give my property to Reuven" — would be fully effective. But this dying man did not simply make a verbal bequest. He said *write it and give it*. Does this instruction transform the bequest into a verbal *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* — effective immediately upon utterance — or does it constitute a directive that the *written* document shall be the vehicle of the gift, in which case delivery of the document is required? If the document is the vehicle, and the man died before it could be written and delivered, the bequest fails entirely: there is no *shtar achar misa*.

### Verbal vs. Written

The concern is captured in the phrase *shema lo gamar le-haknoto ela bi-shtar* — perhaps he only intended to effect the transfer through the written document, not through the verbal declaration alone. And since a *shtar* cannot take effect after death (*ein shtar achar misa*), if that is indeed what he intended, the recipient gets nothing. The case is thus a *safek*: did the verbal instruction constitute a present-tense effective bequest, or was the *shtar* meant to be the sole vehicle? Because of this doubt, and because the doubt favors non-transfer, we do not write and deliver the document posthumously.

### Rebbi Eliezer Warning

Upon hearing this teaching of Rava bar Rav Huna in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, Rebbi Eliezer turned to those present and said *izdaharu bah* — be careful about this *halacha*, pay close attention to it. One might instinctively assume that since the dying man already declared his wishes verbally, the verbal bequest is effective and the *shtar* is merely supplementary documentation. Rebbi Eliezer warns that this is not the case: when the instruction is specifically to write a *shtar*, we must entertain the possibility that the *shtar* was intended as the sole operative mechanism.

There is a dispute about the attribution of this exchange. One version has Rava bar Rav Huna teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, with Rebbi Eliezer urging attention to the ruling. Rav Shizbi offers an alternative version: it was Rebbi Eliezer who originated the ruling, and Rabbi Yochanan who said *izdaharu bah* — affirming the teaching of his student.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak rules that Rav Shizbi's version is more plausible. The logic: if Rebbi Eliezer was the one who formulated the ruling and Rabbi Yochanan endorsed it by saying *izdaharu bah*, that is entirely natural — a master lending the weight of his authority to his student's teaching. But if Rabbi Yochanan was the author of the ruling, what need would there be for Rebbi Eliezer to say *izdaharu bah* in relation to his own master? A student affirming his teacher's position in a manner implying the teacher needed endorsement is itself problematic — it carries an echo of the *kibbud av va'eim* concern that you do not publicly second your father's opinion in a way that implies he required your validation.

The attribution is further clinched by a transmission from Ravin, who reported in the name of Rebbi Abbahu that Rebbi Eliezer sent the following ruling to the diaspora (*le-golah*, to Bavel) in the name of *rabbeinu* — which in this context refers to Rav. Rebbi Eliezer was a student of both Rabbi Yochanan and Rav, and sent the ruling to Bavel in Rav's name: *shchiv mei'ra she-amar kitvu u-tnu mana le-ploni u-met, ein kotvin ve-nosnin, shema lo gamar lehaknoso ela be-shtar, ve-ein shtar achar misa*. Rabbi Yochanan then added *tibadek* — one must examine the circumstances.

### Rav Yochanan Qualification

What does *tibadek* mean? Rabbi Yochanan is not disagreeing with the ruling but qualifying it: the outcome depends on context. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael he explained the qualification.

### Later Will Override

Rav Dimi delivered two teachings. The first, while relevant to the broader topic of wills, is not directly about the *kitvu u-tnu* case: *deyateki mevateles deyateki* — a later will overrides an earlier one. This is the standard rule of testamentary succession. The *gemara* will later discuss whether a will executed with a formal *kinyan* can nonetheless be overridden by a later will; *Shmuel* rules that even in such a case the later will prevails, since the entire context of *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* preserves the right of revision until death.

### Roiin Assessment

Rav Dimi then addresses the *kitvu u-tnu* case directly. *Roiin* — one assesses the circumstances. *Im ke-miyafes es kocho* — if from the context it is clear that the man wanted the document written as additional strength (*miyafes es kocho*), meaning he obviously intended his verbal declaration as the operative bequest and wanted the document merely as corroborating evidence, then *kotvin* — one may write the document even after death. *Ve-im lav* — if it is not clear that this was his intent, and the ambiguity remains whether he intended the *shtar* to be the sole vehicle, *ein kotvin* — one may not write it.

The challenge with this framework is that it offers no objective test; every case requires judgment. The *gemara* therefore seeks clearer criteria. Rebbi Abba bar Memel raises an apparent contradiction from a *beraita* about a *bari* — a healthy person — who says "write and give a hundred *maneh* to so-and-so" and then dies: one does not write it. The implication (*ha*) is that by contrast, in the case of a *shchiv mei'ra*, one always *would* write it — suggesting that by a *shchiv mei'ra* we invariably assume the verbal bequest was operative. However, Rebbi Abba bar Memel himself resolves this: the implication only applies *be-miyafes es kocho*, when there are contextual indicators that the document was intended as supplementary. The *beraita* does not collapse the distinction entirely; it merely suggests that for a *shchiv mei'ra* the presumption more readily tilts toward *miyafes es kocho* than for a *bari*.

### Rav Chisda Kinyan

The *gemara* then asks: *heichi dami miyafes es kocho* — what specific language in the document indicates that the document was intended as *miyafes es kocho*? The answer is the formulation articulated by Rav Chisda: *u-kanina minei mosif a-matanata da* — "and we effected a *kinyan* on him in addition to this gift." When the *shtar* contains language explicitly framing the *kinyan* or the document as an addition (*mosif*) to the underlying bequest, this demonstrates that the verbal declaration was the primary vehicle and the document is supplementary. This is analogous to a case where the dying man said *af kitvu ve-chatumu ve-havu lei* — "and also write and sign and give it to him." The word *af* — "also," "in addition" — is the marker that the written document is secondary.

Conversely, if the *shtar* or the *kinyan* is framed as the primary mechanism rather than as supplementary, there is a risk that it displaces the *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra* mechanism entirely and subjects the transfer to the ordinary rules of *kinyanim*, with all their attendant limitations. A *kinyan sudar* may seem like "more is better," but if it signals that the donor wanted the *kinyan* to be the operative vehicle rather than his verbal declaration, then its failure before death means the transfer fails. The key question in every case is therefore: did the donor want the *shtar* or *kinyan* in *addition to* the verbal bequest, or *instead of* it?

### Final Ruling

The *gemara* concludes with the ruling: *itmar, Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: halacha, kotvin ve-nosnin* — in the relevant case (where it is *miyafes es kocho*), one does indeed write and deliver the document after death. So too Rabba amar Rav Nachman: *halacha, kotvin*. The *Rashbam* cautions against reading this as a wholesale abandonment of the *miyafes es kocho* framework developed above. Rather, the ruling applies specifically in the case where the context clearly indicates that the written document was intended as supplementary evidence to the operative verbal *matnat sh'chiv mei'ra*.

## Father-to-Son Transfer

### Mishnah Text

The *mishnah* states: *ha-kosev nechasav le-vanav tzarich she-yichtov me-hayom u-le'achar misa* — one who writes his property over to his sons must write the formula *me-hayom u-le'achar misa*, "from today and after my death." This is the position of Rebbi Yehuda.

### Rashbam Context

The *Rashbam* situates this *mishnah* not necessarily in the context of a will, but in a common practical scenario: a healthy man (*bari*) who is about to remarry and wishes to protect his existing assets for the children of his first marriage. If he remarries, the children from a potential second marriage would share in the inheritance of his current property. Moreover, his new wife would have *sheibud* — a lien — on his property for her *kesubah*. To forestall this, he transfers title to his current children now, retaining only the *peiros* for his lifetime. The transfer protects the principal from future heirs and from the new wife's claims, while still allowing him the benefit of the property during his life.

### Rebbi Yossi

Rebbi Yossi disagrees with Rebbi Yehuda: *eino tzarich* — the explicit words *me-hayom* are not required. If the document says *le-achar misa*, that suffices. We will see shortly that this is because Rebbi Yossi holds that *zmano shel shtar mochi'ach alav* — the date in the document itself testifies that the transfer is meant to take effect as of the date of writing.

### Rights Restrictions

The *mishnah* then lays out the rights and restrictions that follow from this structure. The father cannot sell the principal because the property is *kesuvim le-ven* — the title is already written to the son. The son equally cannot sell, because the property is *be-reshus ha-av* — under the father's control and benefit. Note the asymmetry in the *mishnah*'s language: the father's inability is framed in terms of ownership (*kesuvim le-ven*), while the son's inability is framed in terms of control (*birshus ha-av*). The father has the *peiros*; the son has the title. Neither can alienate what belongs to the other.

### Father Selling

If the father sells: what he is selling is only his right to the *peiros*, which is all he owns. He can sell the income stream — the right to benefit from the land until his death — and such a sale is valid. A buyer acquires the *peiros* for the duration of the father's life: *mechurin ad she-yamut* — sold until he dies. Once the father dies, the buyer's right to the income ceases, and full ownership consolidates in the son.

### Son Selling

If the son sells his interest — the title — the buyer acquires nothing he can immediately use. *Ein la-lokei'ach bahem klum ad she-yamut ha-av* — the buyer has no benefit in the property until the father dies. Once the father dies, the buyer acquires full ownership along with the right to the *peiros*. The son has sold everything he had: a present title with deferred access, which becomes fully operational upon the father's death. Whether the buyer can further alienate this deferred interest will be addressed in the *gemara*.

## Me-hayom Effectiveness

### Formula Mechanics

The *gemara* opens with the question: *v'chi kasav me-hayom u-le'achar misa, mai havay?* — when one writes *me-hayom u-le'achar misa*, how does this formula actually function? What makes it effective?

### Get Comparison

The *gemara* raises a challenge from *Gittin*: the *mishnah* there rules that a *get* given with the formula *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* is a *get ve-eino get* — it is uncertain whether it is a valid bill of divorce or not. If the husband dies, the wife performs *chalitza* (since perhaps she was not divorced and is therefore obligated), but cannot perform *yibum* (since perhaps she was divorced and remarriage with the brother would be *arayos*). She is a *safek megureshes*. If the formula is ambiguous in the case of a *get*, why is it unambiguous — and fully effective — in the case of a property transfer?

The *gemara* answers: *hasam mesapka lan ei tenai havi ei chazara havi* — there, in the case of a *get*, we are genuinely uncertain how to read the formula. Does it mean: "it should take effect today in the event that I die" (*tenai* — a condition)? Or does it mean: "it takes effect today" but then the husband is retracting himself by saying "no, only after I die"? Either reading is grammatically possible, and the second reading would be problematic since a *get* cannot take partial effect today and complete itself after death — a *get* must be fully valid at the moment of delivery or not at all.

In the case of a monetary transfer, however, *hacha hachi ka-amar lei* — the formula yields a perfectly coherent and direct reading: *gufa kani me-hayom* — the title you acquire today; *peira le-achar misa* — the income-right transfers after death. Title and *peiros* are two distinct property interests that can be allocated across time. This parsing works cleanly in *dinei mammonos* in a way it cannot in *dinei gittin*.

### Rav Yosef Position

Rav Yosef now takes up Rebbi Yossi's position and declares: *ein tzarich* — the explicit words *me-hayom* are not even necessary. Even if the document merely says *le-achar misa*, we interpret it as though it said *me-hayom u-le'achar misa*. The date in the *shtar* renders *me-hayom* implicit.

### Rabba Anecdote

At this point the *gemara* relates an anecdote that both contextualizes and confirms the ruling. Rabba bar Avuha fell ill. Rav Huna and Rav Nachman came to visit him. Rav Huna said to Rav Nachman: go ask him whether we rule like Rav Yosef or not. The context of deathbed visits and the topic of wills and bequests lends the scene an added resonance — one that may not be entirely coincidental. Rav Nachman replied: I do not even understand the reasoning behind Rav Yosef's position; how can I ask him how we rule? Rav Huna responded: you ask about the ruling; I will tell you the reasoning. Rav Nachman asked Rabba bar Avuha, who replied: *hachi amar Rav* — this is what Rav taught: *halacha ke-Rav Yosef* — we rule like Rav Yosef. They departed, and Rav Huna proceeded to explain the reasoning.

### Rav Yosef's Reason

*De-amar zmano shel shtar mochi'ach alav* — because the date of the document testifies on its own behalf. If the document's entire operative effect was meant to be *le-achar misa*, there would be no reason to date it; the date would be superfluous. The very fact that the document bears a date implies that something is meant to take effect as of that date. The date is therefore an implicit declaration of *me-hayom*. This reasoning applies specifically in the context of property transfers: the date carries this implication because property documents are routinely dated to reflect when rights begin to accrue.

A parallel application arises by *get*: Rebbi Yossi holds that if a *get* contains the phrase *im masi* ("if I die") and the *get* is dated, the date functions as an implicit *me-hayom*, making the *get* effective from the date of writing in the event of the husband's death. Whether we rule the same way for *get* as for property documents involves additional considerations, but the present *gemara* establishes clearly that for property transfers, *halacha ke-Rav Yosef*.

### Beraita Support

The *gemara* notes that a *beraita* independently supports this position: *Rebbi Yossi omer eino tzarich mipnei she-zmano shel shtar mochi'ach alav* — Rebbi Yossi says it is not necessary to write *me-hayom*, because the date of the document testifies on its own behalf. The *beraita* thus gives the explicit rationale, confirming both the ruling and its underlying logic. The *halacha* follows Rav Yosef, and consequently follows Rebbi Yossi.

## Kinyan in Shtar

### Hakna Question

Having established that we rule like Rebbi Yossi — that a dated document saying *le-achar misa* is treated as though it said *me-hayom u-le'achar misa* — Rava poses a further question to Rav Nachman: *be-hakna mahu?* What is the law when the *shtar* contains a *kinyan* — when the document records that a formal act of acquisition (*kinyan sudar*) was performed? Does the presence of the *kinyan* in the *shtar* itself supply the equivalent of *me-hayom*, even without the explicit phrase and without reliance on the date?

The question is significant. Rebbi Yossi and Rebbi Yehuda disagree about whether a bare dated *shtar* saying *le-achar misa* is treated as *me-hayom*. But perhaps even Rebbi Yehuda would concede that a *shtar* recording an explicit *kinyan* necessarily implies *me-hayom*: if you did a *kinyan* today, you must have intended the transfer to be operative today.

### Kinyan Not Required

Rav Nachman's initial response: *be-hakna ein tzarich* — when there is a *kinyan* recorded in the *shtar*, one does not need the explicit phrase *me-hayom*. The *kinyan* itself implies present-tense intent to transfer.

### Pappa's Distinction

Rav Pappa, however, introduces a nuance: *ika akna'ata de-tzarich ve-ika akna'ata de-lo tzarich* — there is a formulation of *kinyan* language that requires the explicit *me-hayom* declaration and a formulation that does not.

### Order of Language

The distinction turns on the order of the *kinyan* language in the document. If the *shtar* says *akneiyei ve-kanina minei* — "he instructed us to effect a *kinyan* for [the recipient], and we effected a *kinyan*" — then *lo tzarich*, no explicit *me-hayom* is needed. If however it says *kanina minei ve-akneiyei* — "we effected a *kinyan*, and he had instructed us to" — then *tzarich*, the explicit declaration is still required.

### Rashbam: Language Order

The *Rashbam* explains the distinction as follows. When the *shtar* begins with *akneiyei* — he told us to effect a *kinyan* — and then adds *kanina minei* — and we did — the second clause appears somewhat redundant, since the witnesses' signatures already attest to the *kinyan* having been done. This redundancy is read as carrying extra weight: it signals that the *kinyan* was done with an intent to take effect immediately, *me-hayom*. The double language represents an extra degree of conviction. Conversely, when the *shtar* begins with *kanina minei* — we did a *kinyan* — and then explains *akneiyei* — because he instructed us — the second clause is explanatory rather than emphatic. It answers *why* the *kinyan* was done, not adding any additional commitment. This formulation therefore does not carry the extra implication of *me-hayom*.

### Rav Chanina Challenge

Rav Chanina miSura challenges this analysis sharply: *mi ika midi de-anan lo yadeinan ve-safra yadi?* — is there really a distinction that we here in the *beis medrash* are unaware of, which the scribes understand? The implication of the distinction is that scribes consciously chose one formulation over the other to signal different legal intents. Can it be that this craft-level knowledge resided with the scribes but was unknown to the sages?

### Scribal Confirmation

The *gemara* reports that messengers were sent to the scribes of Abaye and to the scribes of Rava to ask whether there was any meaningful distinction between the two formulations or whether it was purely stylistic. Both sets of scribes confirmed: *yadi* — yes, they knew the difference. The formulations were not interchangeable; each carried a distinct legal implication. This is a striking moment: the *gemara* acknowledges that there is specialized craft knowledge belonging to professional scribes that is not necessarily possessed by the scholars of the *beis medrash*. Just as a patent judge need not be a technical expert in the underlying field, the *amora'im* do not always know the precise conventions of the scribal profession. The scribes' confirmation vindicates Rav Pappa's distinction.

### Huna's Synthesis

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua offers a synthesis: *bein akneiyei ve-kanina minei bein kanina minei ve-akneiyei, lo tzarich* — in fact, either order of the *kinyan* language renders explicit *me-hayom* unnecessary. Whenever a *kinyan* is recorded in the *shtar*, in whatever formulation, the *shtar* is treated as *me-hayom*, and there is no debate.

### Debate Scope

If so, where does the debate of Rebbi Yehuda and Rebbi Yossi actually apply? *U-veduchran pisgamei de-havi be-anpana pligi* — their debate applies to a *shtar* that is framed as *beduchran pisgamei*, a record of things the witnesses have seen and heard. In this mode, the witnesses are not recording that they themselves performed a *kinyan* on behalf of the recipient; they are simply testifying that they witnessed Reuven transferring his field to Shimon *le-achar misa*. When no *kinyan* by the witnesses is recorded in the document, only their testimony to an event, the debate applies: Rebbi Yehuda requires explicit *me-hayom*; Rebbi Yossi holds that the date suffices.

### Final Clarification

Rav Kahana reports that he presented this analysis before Rav Zevid miNehardea, and that there were variant traditions about exactly how the teaching was formulated. The version that Rav Kahana's academy taught runs as follows, in the name of Rava citing Rav Nachman: *be-aknoi, ein tzarich* — whenever there is a *kinyan* recorded in the *shtar*, regardless of formulation, explicit *me-hayom* is unnecessary and the document is always treated as conveying *me-hayom*. *Be-duchran pisgamei de-havu be-anpana* — in the case of a bare witness-record without an explicit *kinyan* performed by the witnesses themselves — *pligi*: that is where Rebbi Yehuda and Rebbi Yossi debate, and where the question of whether the document's date suffices as an implicit *me-hayom* determines the outcome.
