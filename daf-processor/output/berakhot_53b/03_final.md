# Berakhot 53b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Borei Meorei Ha'esh

### Deriving Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְבָרְכִין עַל הַנֵּר עַד שֶׁיֵּאוֹתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And one does not recite the blessing over the candle until he derives benefit** from its light.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא ״יֵאוֹתוּ״ יֵאוֹתוּ מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כׇּל שֶׁאִילּוּ עוֹמֵד בְּקָרוֹב וּמִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּרִיחוּק מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says that Rav says: Derives benefit does not mean that the one reciting the blessing must actually derive benefit from the light of the candle. Rather, as long as if one were to stand close to the candle he could utilize its light, if he sees it he may recite a blessing over it, even if he is now standing at a distance.**

The final *amud* of the eighth *perek* opens with a striking *halacha* that most people, in practice, misunderstand. The *mishna* states: *ein mevarchin al ha-ner ad she'ye'otu l'oro* — one does not recite the blessing over the flame until one derives benefit from it. The reason for this sequencing is rooted in the nature of the blessing itself. The *bracha* on the *ner* during *havdalah* is not a *birkat ha-nehenin*, a blessing for pleasure derived — the proof being that one does not recite it at other times during the week. Rather, it is a *birkat ha-shevach*, an acknowledgment and praise, recognizing that fire may now be used, that the creative week begins again, and — according to the *midrash* — that on *Motzaei Shabbos* Adam *Ha-rishon* himself first created fire. Because it is a *birkat ha-shevach*, one performs it *after* the experience, not before. One thus first looks at the flame — at one's fingers, at the light — and only then recites the *bracha*, not the reverse.

### Proximity vs. Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּרִיחוּק מָקוֹם שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ashi also said: We learned** that one may recite the blessing **even at a distance.**

The *Gemara* immediately opens the question of how literally to understand the requirement of deriving benefit. *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* says: *lo ye'otu ye'otu mamash* — it does not mean one must actually derive benefit in a literal sense. Rather: *kol she'illu omed bikrov umishtamesh l'oro* — it suffices that the flame is of sufficient quality that were someone standing close to it, they could use it — and moreover, one may recite the *bracha* even from a distance (*brichuk makom*), as long as the flame possesses that quality. *Rav Ashi* similarly rules that even standing far away one may recite the blessing, provided the flame meets that standard. Two distinct principles thus emerge: first, one need not actually use the light; and second, one need not even be positioned close enough to use it.

### Hidden/Unseen Flame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיְתָה לוֹ נֵר טְמוּנָה בְּחֵיקוֹ, אוֹ בְּפַנָּס, אוֹ שֶׁרָאָה שַׁלְהֶבֶת וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ, אוֹ נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ וְלֹא רָאָה שַׁלְהֶבֶת — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶה שַׁלְהֶבֶת וְיִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *Tosefta*: **One who had a candle hidden in his lap** or placed **inside an** opaque **lamp, or if he saw a flame and did not utilize its light, or if he utilized its light and did not see a flame, may not recite a blessing until he** both **sees the flame and utilizes its light.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ וְלֹא רָאָה שַׁלְהֶבֶת מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — דְּקַיְימָא בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית. אֶלָּא רָאָה שַׁלְהֶבֶת וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? לָאו דִּמְרַחֲקָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first clarifies the content of the *Tosefta* itself: **Granted,** a case where one **utilizes its light and did not see a flame, can be found where** the flame **is situated** around **a corner,** illuminating the area but hidden from his view. But **how can** a case where one **saw a flame and did not utilize its light be found? Is it not** referring to a case **where one is distant?** Apparently, one must actually utilize the flame; merely having the potential to utilize it is not sufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, כְּגוֹן דְּעָמְיָא וְאָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No.** This refers to **a case where** the flame is **gradually dimming.** One sees the flame, but is unable to utilize its light.

The *Gemara* then challenges this reading from a *mishna*. The *mishna* states: if one had a light hidden in one's chest — perhaps a lamp or lantern — or saw a flame but did not use it, or used it without seeing it, one may not recite the *bracha* until one both sees the flame *and* uses it. The *Gemara* concedes that it is straightforward to imagine using a flame without seeing it — the flame is around a corner, so one receives light without a direct view. But what would it mean to see the flame and yet not use it? The *Gemara* initially suggests this refers to being too far away — but immediately withdraws that reading, since distance, on the view just established, should not disqualify. Instead, the *Gemara* resolves that the case of "seeing without using" refers to a flame that is sputtering out: one still sees a flicker of flame, but it is so weak that no one, not even someone standing immediately beside it, could derive any use from it. This is the only case excluded — not distance, but deficiency in the quality of the flame itself.

### Smoldering Embers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גֶּחָלִים לוֹחֲשׁוֹת — מְבָרְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן, אוֹמְמוֹת — אֵין מְבָרְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי לוֹחֲשׁוֹת? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל שֶׁאִילּוּ מַכְנִיס לְתוֹכָן קֵיסָם וְדוֹלֶקֶת מֵאֵילֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may recite a blessing over smoldering coals** just as he does over a candle; however, **over dimming [*omemot*]** coals, **one may not recite a blessing. What are the circumstances of smoldering coals? Rav Ḥisda said:** Smoldering coals are **any** coals **that, if one places a wood chip among them, it ignites on its own** without fanning the flame.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״אוֹמְמוֹת״ אוּ ״עוֹמְמוֹת״? תָּא שְׁמַע דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: ״אֲרָזִים לֹא עֲמָמֻהוּ בְּגַן אֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the wording of the *baraita*, the Gemara **raises a dilemma:** Does the *baraita* say ***omemot*** beginning with an *alef*, or ***omemot*** beginning with an *ayin*? **Come and hear** a resolution, **as Rav Ḥisda bar Avdimi said:** The correct version is *omemot* beginning with an *ayin*, as it is stated: **"The cedars in the garden of God could not dim it** [***amamuhu***]**"** (Ezekiel 31:8).

The *Gemara* proceeds with a *baraita*: *gechalim lochashot* — smoldering embers that are still active, whispering — one may recite a *bracha* over them. *Omimot* — embers in the process of extinguishing — one may not, since they are no longer strong enough to constitute a usable flame. The *Gemara* then asks: what precisely constitutes *lochashot*? *Rav Chisda* answers: any ember over which, if one were to insert a wood chip, it would catch and light on its own. If it would require stirring first to cause something else to catch fire, it does not meet the standard. The *Gemara* also notes a textual question about whether the word *omimot* is spelled with an *alef* or an *ayin*, and resolves the orthography from a biblical verse — *arazim lo amamuhu be-gan Elohim* — confirming that the correct spelling is with an *ayin*.

### Flame Quality Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: ״יֵאוֹתוּ״ מַמָּשׁ. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר עוּלָּא: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּכִּיר בֵּין אִיסָּר לְפוּנְדְּיוֹן. חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּכִּיר בֵּין מְלוּזְמָא שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא לִמְלוּזְמָא שֶׁל צִפּוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the question whether or not one must actually benefit from the flame's light in order to recite a blessing, **Rava said:** When the mishna said **benefit,** it meant that he must **actually** derive benefit from the light. The Gemara asks: **And how** adjacent must one be in order to be considered to have derived benefit from the flame? **Ulla said: So that he can distinguish between an *issar* and a *pundeyon*,** two coins of the period. **Ḥizkiya said: So that he can distinguish between a weight** used in **Tiberias and a weight** used in **Tzippori,** which were slightly different.

*Rava*, it should be noted, takes a stricter position: *ya'osu mamash* — one must actually derive benefit. Yet even *Rava*'s formulation is not taken with full literalness; *Rashi* explains that *mamash* means *omed bisamuch*, standing close enough to benefit — not that the act of benefit must be performed. The *Gemara* then asks how strong the flame must be, and records two opinions. *Ulla* says: strong enough to distinguish between an *issar* and a *pundiyon*, two different coins — one might compare this to distinguishing a quarter from a nickel by the flame's light alone. *Chizkiyah* says: strong enough to distinguish between the *meluzma* coin of Tiberias and the *meluzma* coin of Tzippori — essentially, to tell apart similar coins minted in different cities by the quality of the flame. The common denominator is a flame of genuine and usable luminescence.

### Examples: Rav Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה מְבָרֵךְ אַדְּבֵי אַדָּא דַּיָּילָא, רָבָא מְבָרֵךְ אַדְּבֵי גּוּרְיָא בַּר חָמָא. אַבָּיֵי מְבָרֵךְ אַדְּבֵי בַּר אֲבוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that the *amora'im* conducted themselves in accordance with their above-stated opinions. At the conclusion of Shabbat, **Rav Yehuda** would **recite a blessing over** the light **of the house of Adda, the servant,** which was far from his house. **Rava** would **recite a blessing over** the light **of the house of Gurya bar Ḥama,** which was adjacent to his house. **Abaye** would **recite a blessing over** the light **of the house of bar Avuh.**

The *Gemara* then offers three historical examples of *amoraim* making the *bracha* on flames at a distance. *Rav Yehuda* would recite the *bracha* on a flame visible in the house of Ada Dayyala, a neighbor, even though *Rav Yehuda* himself was not close enough to personally benefit from it — consistent with his own ruling that distance is not disqualifying. *Rava* recited the *bracha* on a flame in the house of Gurya bar Chama; *Rashi* notes that this neighbor lived close enough that *Rava* could, had he chosen, have used the light — consistent with *Rava*'s stricter position that one must at least be in a position to benefit. And *Abaye* recited the *bracha* on a flame he saw in the house of Bar Avuha. In all these cases, the underlying principle is the same: the flame must be of sufficient quality, and one may bless it even from a distance, at least according to all the positions prior to *Rava*.

## No Search for Candle

### Core Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֵין מְחַזְּרִין עַל הָאוּר כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְחַזְּרִים עַל הַמִּצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said** a general halakhic principle: **One need not seek out light** at the conclusion of Shabbat **in the manner that one seeks out** other **mitzvot.** If no flame is available over which to recite a blessing, it does not prevent one from reciting *havdala*.

The *Gemara* next records a ruling of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* that is, practically speaking, equally important and equally unfamiliar to most people: *ein machzirin al ha-or kederach she-machzirin al ha-mitzvah* — one does not go searching for a flame for *havdalah* the way one goes out of one's way to fulfill a *mitzvah*. If one has wine for *kiddush* and does not have it readily available, one buys it, one knocks on a neighbor's door, one finds an alternative. If one lacks a candle for *havdalah*, the ruling is simply to proceed without it. One does not go to the neighbor. One does not make a special effort. If a flame happens to be available, one blesses it; if not, one omits that component of *havdalah* entirely.

### Rav Zeira Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה מְהַדַּרְנָא, כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, אֲנָא נָמֵי לָא מְהַדַּרְנָא, אֶלָּא אִי מִקְּלַע לִי מִמֵּילָא — מְבָרֵיכְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Zeira said: Initially I would seek out** light, **once I heard this** *halakha* that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, I too do not seek** out light. **However, if** a candle **happens** to become available **to me, I recite a blessing** over it.

*Rav Zeira* relates his personal practice as an illustration: *me-reisha hava mihadarna* — originally, he would search around for a candle for *havdalah*. But once he heard this ruling of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav*, he adopted it entirely: *ana nami lo mihadarna* — he too stopped searching. *Ella im mikla li mi-meila mevarachina* — only if a flame happened to come his way would he recite the *bracha*. This is a striking posture for *havdalah*, a ceremony that has in later tradition become highly ritualized with dedicated candles, ornate holders, and careful preparation.

### Spices/Components

The natural next question is what applies to *besamim*, the spices. The *Gemara* draws the conclusion *kol she-ken besamim* — all the more so for spices. The reasoning is instructive. The flame at *havdalah* at least marks something significant: the distinction between *kodesh* and *chol*, the first act of creation in the new week. The *besamim*, by contrast, serve only to restore the person — to compensate, as it were, for the loss of the *neshama yeseira*, the additional soul of *Shabbos*. If one need not search even for the more symbolically significant element, certainly one need not search for the more personal one. The *halacha* is therefore clear: if one lacks *besamim* or a candle, one recites *havdalah* without those components and does not go out of the way to procure them.

One might ask whether this ruling connects to the broader category of *birkat ha-shevach* — a category of blessing not triggered by the fulfillment of a commandment but by encountering something noteworthy. Just as one does not go searching for blossoming trees in order to recite *birkat ha-ilanot*, there is a sense in which the candle and spices of *havdalah*, not being the primary *mitzvah* — which began as part of *Shemoneh Esrei* and even when moved to *havdalah al ha-kos* did not inherently include these components — retain a character that does not generate an obligation to seek them out.

## Benching Location

### Mishna Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁאָכַל וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב זְבִיד וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב דִּימִי בַּר אַבָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשָׁכַח, אֲבָל בְּמֵזִיד, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל — יַחְזוֹר לִמְקוֹמוֹ וִיבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Our mishna cited a dispute regarding **one who ate** and forgot and did not recite a blessing; Beit Shammai say: He returns to the place where he ate and recites the blessing. Beit Hillel say: That is unnecessary. He recites the blessing at the place where he remembered. **Rav Zevid said and some say Rav Dimi bar Abba** said: This **dispute is** only with regard to a case **where one forgot** to recite the blessing, **but** if he did so **intentionally, everyone agrees that he must return to** the **place** where **he** ate **and recite a blessing.**

The *Gemara* now shifts entirely to the laws of *birkat ha-mazon*. The *mishna* records the following dispute: if one ate and forgot to recite *birkat ha-mazon* and subsequently left the place where one ate, *Beit Shammai* holds that one must return to that place in order to bench; *Beit Hillel* holds that one does not. This rule applies specifically to bread, because bread establishes the concept of *keviyas makom* — a fixed place where one's meal is set and over which the obligation of *birkat ha-mazon* becomes anchored.

### Gemara Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, ״וְשָׁכַח״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This is obvious. We learned** in the mishna: **And forgot,** not if he did so intentionally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְתֵימָא הוּא הַדִּין אֲפִילּוּ בְּמֵזִיד, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״וְשָׁכַח״ — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחָן דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי. קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that **the same is true,** that Beit Hillel permit one to recite a blessing without returning to the place where he ate, **even** in a case where he **willfully** did not recite a blessing, **and that which was taught: And forgot, is to convey the far-reaching** nature **of** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** who require him to return to the place where he ate even if he forgot, Rav Zevid **teaches us** that there is no disagreement in that case.

The *Gemara*, through *Rav Zevid* (and some say *Rav Dimi bar Abba*), clarifies the scope of the dispute. The debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerns only the case of *shoche'ach* — one who forgot to bench before leaving. But in a case of *mezid* — one who knowingly left without benching — everyone agrees that one must return to bench. The *Gemara* then asks: is this not obvious? It might have been thought that *Beit Hillel*'s leniency extends even to the *mezid* case, and the *mishna*'s mention of forgetting was merely to highlight the full stringency of *Beit Shammai*. The *Gemara* therefore clarifies: no, the dispute is specifically limited to the case of *shogeg*, and in the case of *mezid* even *Beit Hillel* concedes.

### Tower/Wallet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: לְדִבְרֵיכֶם, מִי שֶׁאָכַל בְּרֹאשׁ הַבִּירָה וְשָׁכַח וְיָרַד וְלֹא בֵּרַךְ, יַחְזוֹר לְרֹאשׁ הַבִּירָה וִיבָרֵךְ?! אָמְרוּ לָהֶן בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵית הִלֵּל: לְדִבְרֵיכֶם מִי שֶׁשָּׁכַח אַרְנָקִי בְּרֹאשׁ הַבִּירָה לֹא יַעֲלֶה וְיִטְלֶנָּה? לִכְבוֹד עַצְמוֹ הוּא עוֹלֶה, לִכְבוֹד שָׁמַיִם לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: According to your statement, one who ate atop** the Temple Mount, **God's chosen place of residence, and forgot and descended without reciting a blessing, must he return to the top of** the Temple Mount, **God's chosen place of residence, to recite a blessing? Beit Shammai said to Beit Hillel:** Why not? **And according to your statement, one who forgot his purse atop** the Temple Mount, **God's chosen place of residence, would he not ascend to retrieve it? If one ascends in deference to his own** needs, **all the more so** he should ascend **in deference to Heaven.**

A *baraita* records the rhetorical exchange between the two schools. *Beit Hillel* challenges *Beit Shammai*: according to your position, imagine someone who ate at the top of a tower, forgot to bench, and has since descended. Do you require him to climb all the way back up? This seems an unreasonable demand. *Beit Shammai* responds pointedly: according to your position, if that same person had forgotten his wallet at the top of the tower, would he not climb back up to retrieve it? If he would ascend for his own financial interest, how much more so — *kol she-ken* — should he ascend *likhvod shamayim*, for the honor of Heaven, to bench in the place where he ate.

### Honor of Heaven

The force of *Beit Shammai*'s argument is that the place where one ate is not merely a convenience but the *halachically* correct location for *birkat ha-mazon*. Returning to that location is an expression of *kavod shamayim* — a level of seriousness about one's obligations to God that should exceed one's seriousness about one's personal financial affairs.

### Two Students

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ תְּרֵי תַּלְמִידֵי, חַד עֲבַד בְּשׁוֹגֵג כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי וְאַשְׁכַּח אַרְנְקָא דְּדַהֲבָא, וְחַד עֲבַד בְּמֵזִיד כְּבֵית הִלֵּל וְאַכְלֵיהּ אַרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There were **these two students** who ate and did not recite a blessing. **One of them did so unwittingly,** and, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** returned to where he ate, **and found a purse of gold. One of them did so intentionally,** and, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** albeit in circumstances where they agree with Beit Shammai, did not return **and a lion ate him.**

The *Gemara* then recounts two aggadic stories about students, each providing a pointed illustration. One student, in a case of forgetting (*shogeg*), went beyond the letter of the law and returned to the place where he had eaten to bench, conducting himself in accordance with *Beit Shammai*. When he returned, he found *arnaki d'dahava* — a wallet filled with gold coins. The deliberate echo of the tower-and-wallet analogy is unmistakable: he went back for God's honor, and found personal wealth waiting for him. The second student, who had left intentionally (*mezid*) and did not return to bench — a position that even *Beit Hillel* does not sanction — was eaten by a lion. The eating imagery is recursive: he was careless about where he had eaten, and was consumed in return.

It is worth noting that earlier in the *masechta*, the *Gemara* criticized someone for acting in accordance with *Beit Shammai* against the ruling. Here, by contrast, the *Gemara* presents *Beit Shammai*'s stringency as clearly praiseworthy. *Tosafot* makes this explicit: even *Beit Hillel* does not say that going back is wrong — only that it is not required. Going beyond the *halacha* and returning, as *Beit Shammai* demands, is evidently the ideal. The general ruling throughout the *perek* follows *Beit Hillel*, but the aggadah makes clear that *Beit Shammai*'s position represents the aspirational standard.

## Benching Location…

### Gold Pigeon Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה הֲוָה קָאָזֵל בְּשַׁיַּירְתָּא, אֲכַל וְאִשְׁתְּלִי וְלָא בָּרֵיךְ. אֲמַר: הֵיכִי אַעֲבֵיד? אִי אָמֵינָא לְהוּ ״אִנְּשַׁאי לְבָרֵךְ״, אָמְרוּ לִי: בָּרֵיךְ, כׇּל הֵיכָא דִּמְבָרְכַתְּ — לְרַחֲמָנָא מְבָרְכַתְּ. מוּטָב דְּאָמֵינָא לְהוּ: אִנְּשַׁאי יוֹנָה דְּדַהֲבָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִנְטַרוּ לִי, דְּאִנְּשַׁאי יוֹנָה דְּדַהֲבָא. אָזֵיל וּבָרֵיךְ וְאַשְׁכַּח יוֹנָה דְּדַהֲבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana was once traveling with a caravan. He ate and forgot and did not recite a blessing. He said** to himself: **What shall I do? If I say to them: I forgot to recite a blessing, they** will **say to me to recite a blessing** here, as **wherever you recite a blessing, you recite a blessing to God.** It is **better that I say to them: I forgot a golden dove.** Then they will wait for me while I retrieve it. **He said to them: Wait for me, as I forgot a golden dove. He went and recited a blessing and found a golden dove.**

The *Gemara* continues with a related story. *Rabbah bar bar Chana* was traveling with a caravan when he ate, forgot to bench, and realized that the caravan was about to move on. He calculated that if he told his companions that he had forgotten to bench, they would simply tell him: *berich kol heicha d'mevarchet* — bless wherever you are, God is everywhere, why do you need to go back? He knew that argument would prevail and he would not be permitted to return. So instead he told them he had lost a gold pigeon — *yona d'dahava* — and asked them to wait while he retrieved it. He returned, benched, and when he arrived back at the spot, he indeed found a gold pigeon.

### Dove Symbolism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא יוֹנָה? דִּמְתִילִי כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְיוֹנָה. דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה נֶחְפָּה בַכֶּסֶף וְאֶבְרוֹתֶיהָ בִּירַקְרַק חָרוּץ״ — מָה יוֹנָה אֵינָהּ נִיצּוֹלֶת אֶלָּא בִּכְנָפֶיהָ, אַף יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינָן נִיצּוֹלִין אֶלָּא בְּמִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about a **dove,** that he specifically said that that was the object that he forgot? The Gemara answers: **Because the community of Israel is likened to a dove, as it is written: "The wings of a dove, covered in silver, and its pinions with the shimmer of gold"** (Psalms 68:14). The Gemara explains the parable: **Just as a dove is saved** from its enemies **only by its wings, so too, Israel is saved only by** the merit of the **mitzvot.**

The *Gemara* then asks why specifically a pigeon. The answer offered is that *keneset Yisrael* is compared to a dove, as the verse states: *kanfei yona nechpa ba-kesef ve-evroteha bi-yerakrak charutz* — the wings of a dove covered with silver, and its pinions with gleaming gold. Just as a dove is saved by its wings — it escapes danger by flight — so too the Jewish people are saved by their *mitzvot*: *af Yisrael einam nitzolim ella be-mitzvot*. *Rabbah bar bar Chana*, by being *machmir* and insisting on benching in the proper place, was saved — and rewarded — through this act of *mitzvah* observance.

One might note that the story raises a separate question: *Rabbah bar bar Chana* told his companions something untrue and also caused them delay and inconvenience for something that was not *halachically* required of him. Without entering into the full discussion of truthfulness and permissible departures from it in the *poskim*, it is fair to observe that the *Gemara* presents the story approvingly, focusing entirely on his *chumra* in the performance of the *mitzvah* rather than on the means he employed.

## Time Limit Digestion

### Until When Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד אֵימָתַי הוּא וְכוּ׳. כַּמָּה שִׁיעוּר עִכּוּל? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָעֵב. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיִּצְמָא מֵחֲמַת אֲכִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And until when does he** recite the blessing? Until the food is digested in his intestines. The Gemara asks: **What is the duration of digestion? Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** As **long as he is not** yet **hungry** again. **And Reish Lakish said: As long as he is thirsty due to his eating.**

The *Gemara* raises a further practical question regarding *birkat ha-mazon*: *ad eimatai* — until when, after eating, may one still recite *birkat ha-mazon*? The answer the *Gemara* gives is: until the food is digested. But what is the measure of digestion?

### Until No Hunger

*Rabbi Yochanan* says: *kol zman she-eino ra'ev* — as long as one is not yet hungry again. On this view, the window for benching could extend for several hours depending on the size and richness of the meal, since one does not return to hunger immediately after a substantial meal.

### Until Thirsty

*Resh Lakish* takes a different position: *kol zman she-yitzma machmat achilato* — as long as one is still thirsty as a result of having eaten. This is a notably shorter window. The assumption seems to be that once one has drunk enough to quench the thirst generated by eating, the meal has, in a physiological sense, passed. This would in many cases impose a significantly tighter time constraint than *Rabbi Yochanan*'s formulation.

### Four Mil Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא לְמָר זוּטְרָא וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב יֵימַר בַּר שֵׁיזְבִי לְמָר זוּטְרָא: מִי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב אַמֵּי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כַּמָּה שִׁיעוּר עִכּוּל? — כְּדֵי לְהַלֵּךְ אַרְבַּע מִילִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar bar Shelamya said to Mar Zutra, and some say** that it was **Rav Yeimar bar Sheizevi** who said to **Mar Zutra: Did Reish Lakish say that? Didn't Rav Ami say** that **Reish Lakish said: What is the duration of digestion? As long as** it takes **to walk four *mil*?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בַּאֲכִילָה מְרוּבָּה, כָּאן בַּאֲכִילָה מוּעֶטֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** where Reish Lakish said the duration is as long as it takes to walk four *mil,* is **in** a case where he ate **a sizable meal, here,** where Reish Lakish said the duration is as long as he remains thirsty, is **in** a case where he ate **a meager meal.**

A challenge is then raised: does not *Rav Ami* in the name of *Resh Lakish* state that the measure of digestion (*shiur ikul*) is the time it takes to walk four *mil* — that is, 72 minutes, on the widely accepted standard of 18 minutes per *mil*? How does this square with *Resh Lakish*'s thirst-based criterion, which could be much less? The *Gemara* resolves: *la kashya* — there is no contradiction. The difference turns on the size of the meal: a large meal (*achila meruba*) and a small meal (*achila mu'etet*) follow different tracks. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* debate which standard applies to which case, but in any event the operative ruling follows *Rabbi Yochanan* — one may bench as long as one is not yet hungry again — with the four-*mil* measure of 72 minutes serving as the general practical benchmark. In practice, the common custom is not to bench more than 72 minutes after the conclusion of eating, though questions remain about whether a sufficiently filling meal might in principle extend that window.

## Saying Amen

### Full Blessing Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לָהֶן יַיִן וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna a tannaitic dispute with regard to a case where **wine came before** the diners after the meal, and we also learned in the mishna that one answers amen after a Jew recites a blessing even if he did not hear the entire blessing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא שָׁמַע כּוּלָּהּ בְּרָכָה עוֹנֶה? וְכִי לָא שָׁמַע הֵיכִי נָפֵיק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that** if **a Jew** recites a blessing, **even though one did not hear the entire blessing, he responds** amen? **If he did not hear** the entire blessing, **how did he fulfill** his obligation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִיָּיא בַּר רַב: בְּשֶׁלֹּא אָכַל עִמָּהֶן, וְכֵן אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ בְּשֶׁלֹּא אָכַל עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥiyya bar Rav said:** This is not a case where one seeks to fulfill his obligation by responding amen; rather, it is a case **where he did not eat with them** yet still wishes to answer amen to their blessing. **And so Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** It is a case **where he did not eat with them.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to a different but related topic: the laws of responding *amen*. The relevant passage concerns saying *amen* after a *Yisrael* whose *bracha* one did not hear in its entirety — in contrast to a *Kuti*, after whom one does not answer *amen* at all, because one cannot be certain to whom he directed his blessing. The *Gemara*'s formulation implies that after a *Yisrael* one may answer *amen* even without having heard the entire blessing. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: if one did not hear the complete *bracha*, how could one possibly be *yotzei*? *Chiyya bar Rav* resolves the difficulty: the case concerns one who did not eat together with the group and therefore has no obligation to be *yotzei* through the *bracha* in the first place. The *amen* here is purely a social response — an act of acknowledgment and participation — not a mechanism for fulfilling one's own obligation.

### Amen vs. Yotzei

This distinction is essential and has wide practical ramifications. Saying *amen* and being *yotzei* are two separate functions. When one is attempting to fulfill one's own obligation through another person's recitation — as is common with *kiddush* on Friday night, where family members rely on the *ba'al ha-bayit* — one must hear every single word of the *bracha*. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules accordingly. In practice this is frequently compromised: people begin singing along with the *bracha*, or speak, or fail to attend carefully enough. Missing a substantive word in the *bracha* through which one is being *yotzei* is a genuine *halachic* problem, and one that demands real care, particularly on *Shabbos* night.

## Saying Amen…

### Rambam View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ: בְּרִי, חֲטוֹף וּבָרֵיךְ. וְכֵן אֲמַר רַב הוּנָא לְרַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: חֲטוֹף וּבָרֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav said to his son, Ḥiyya: My son, seize** the opportunity **and recite a blessing** quickly. **And similarly Rav Huna said to his son, Rabba, seize** the opportunity **and recite a blessing.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּמְבָרֵךְ עֲדִיף מִמַּאן דְּעָנֵי ״אָמֵן״? וְהָתַנְיָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גָּדוֹל הָעוֹנֶה ״אָמֵן״ יוֹתֵר מִן הַמְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that one who recites a blessing is preferable to one who answers amen? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** The reward of **the one who answers amen is greater than** the reward of **the one who recites the blessing?**

The *Rambam* takes a position on this question that requires careful attention. He writes: *kol ha-oneh amen harei zeh kemotzi bracha mipiv dami* — whoever says *amen* is considered as though he himself uttered the *bracha*, since the word *amen* encapsulates and ratifies the entire blessing. He further writes that whoever hears a *bracha* from beginning to end, even without saying *amen*, is considered as though he responded *amen*. The structure of the *Rambam*'s formulation implies that the very act of saying *amen* stands in for hearing the entire *bracha* — as though *amen* is a complete substitute.

### Halakhic Practice

The *Kessef Mishneh* — who is the *Beis Yosef* — acknowledges that this is the straightforward reading of the *Rambam*, but rules that the *halacha* cannot be understood this way in light of the *Gemara* just cited. The *Gemara* itself raised the objection: if one did not hear the whole *bracha*, how can one be *yotzei* even by saying *amen*? The answer given was that the case involves one who has no obligation to be *yotzei* in the first place. This implies that saying *amen* alone, without having heard the complete *bracha*, does not discharge the obligation. Despite the *Rambam*'s apparent position, the *halacha* follows the stricter reading.

### Practical Issues

The practical implications are considerable. When one is being *yotzei* through another's recitation, hearing every word is not a matter of etiquette or attentiveness — it is the core *halachic* requirement. This is particularly challenging with *kiddush*, where the *tefach* of people singing along, the noise of the assembled group, or individual inattention can result in missing words. Missing the key language of the *bracha* — the essential formula that constitutes the blessing one is obligated to fulfill — creates a real question as to whether one has been *yotzei* at all. It is something that requires active and conscientious attention.

## Respondent vs. Blesser

### Rabbi Yossi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּמְבָרֵךְ עֲדִיף מִמַּאן דְּעָנֵי ״אָמֵן״? וְהָתַנְיָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גָּדוֹל הָעוֹנֶה ״אָמֵן״ יוֹתֵר מִן הַמְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that one who recites a blessing is preferable to one who answers amen? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** The reward of **the one who answers amen is greater than** the reward of **the one who recites the blessing?**

The *Gemara* records a *tannaitic* dispute on a related question: between the one who recites the blessing and the one who responds *amen*, who is greater? *Rabbi Yossi* holds: *gadol ha-oneh amen yoter min ha-mevarech* — the one who responds *amen* is greater than the one who recites the blessing.

### Soldiers/Warriors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי נְהוֹרַאי: הַשָּׁמַיִם, כֵּן הוּא. תִּדַּע — שֶׁהֲרֵי גּוּלְיָירִין יוֹרְדִין וּמִתְגָּרִים בַּמִּלְחָמָה, וְגִבּוֹרִים יוֹרְדִין וּמְנַצְּחִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Nehorai said to him: By Heavens,** an oath in the name of God, **it is so. Know** that this is true, **as the military assistants [*gulyarin*] descend to the** battlefield **and initiate the war** and **the mighty descend and prevail.** The amen that follows a blessing is compared to the mighty who join the war after the assistants, illustrating that answering amen is more significant than reciting the initial blessing.

*Rabbi Nehorai* explains *Rabbi Yossi*'s position with an analogy. *Goyarin* — infantrymen — go down into battle first, engaging the enemy in the initial clash. Then the *giborim* — the mighty ones — come down and achieve the actual victory. The one who initiates is not necessarily the one who wins. The person who says *amen*, arriving after the *bracha*, provides the act of affirmation that crowns and completes the whole. The *bracha* without the *amen* is the charge without the conquest. This concept resonates with an insight that the importance of *followers* is often undervalued: a leader with no one following is simply an eccentric. The first follower who signals that the leader is worth following performs an act of social and communal power comparable to the leader's own. When one says *amen*, one declares to all present that this blessing is real, acknowledged, and shared — a communal act of recognition with genuine weight. Indeed, this line — *gadol ha-oneh amen* — is reportedly inscribed in large letters on the walls of the *Altneuschul* in Prague, almost certainly to discourage talking during *davening*.

### Both Equivalent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד הַמְבָרֵךְ וְאֶחָד הָעוֹנֶה ״אָמֵן״ בַּמַּשְׁמָע, אֶלָּא שֶׁמְּמַהֲרִין לַמְבָרֵךְ יוֹתֵר מִן הָעוֹנֶה אָמֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is subject to **a tannaitic dispute, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Both the one who recites a blessing and the one who answers** amen **are included** among those who "stand up and bless" (Nehemiah 9:5), but **they hurry to** reward, i.e., **the one who recites the blessing, more than** they hurry to reward, i.e., **the one who answers amen.**

A *baraita* then offers a resolution: *echad ha-mevarech ve-echad ha-oneh amen ba-mashma* — both the one who blesses and the one who says *amen* are equivalent, *ella she-memaharim la-mevarech yoter min ha-oneh* — except that Heaven rewards the one who recites the blessing more swiftly than the one who responds. This is a *machloket tannaim*, with one view privileging the respondent and another treating them as practically equal while giving a marginal priority to the blesser in terms of heavenly reward.

### Grab Leadership

It is in this context that the practical advice of *Rav* is situated. *Rav* said to his son *Chiyya*: *chatof u'varich* — grab and bless, meaning: seize the opportunity to lead a blessing for a group rather than merely responding. Similarly, *Rav Huna* told his son *Rabbah*: *chatof u'varich*. This advice does not contradict the equal — or slightly superior — status of the *oneh amen*. The question of whether it is better for each individual to recite their own blessing versus have one person lead and others respond is a separate matter, governed by the principle of *berov am hadrat melech* — the glory of God is enhanced when a multitude participates together. The consensus is that having one *mevarech* with the assembled responding *amen* is preferable to breaking up and each reciting independently. But within that structure, *Rav* counsels his son to be the one leading rather than merely responding, in light of the marginal priority the *baraita* accords the *mevarech*.

## Children/Context

### School Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל מֵרַב: מַהוּ לַעֲנוֹת ״אָמֵן״ אַחַר תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַחַר הַכֹּל עוֹנִין אָמֵן, חוּץ מִתִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן, הוֹאִיל וּלְהִתְלַמֵּד עֲשׂוּיִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel raised a dilemma before Rav: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **answering amen after** the blessings of **schoolchildren?** Rav **said to him: One answers amen following everyone** whom we hear recite a blessing, **except for schoolchildren, as they** recite blessings merely in order **to learn** them, not as expressions of thanksgiving.

The *Gemara* records that *Shmuel* asked *Rav* the following question: *mahu la'anot amen achar tinokot shel beit raban* — what is the rule about saying *amen* after children in school who are practicing reciting *brachot*? These children are not reciting *brachot* in a context of obligation or fulfillment; they are practicing. Does the formulation of a *bracha*, absent the context of genuine obligation, warrant a response of *amen*?

### Practice Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּדְלָא עִידָּן מִפְטְרַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל בְּעִידָּן מִפְטְרַיְיהוּ — עוֹנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **This applies** specifically **at a time when they are not fulfilling their obligation** with the recitation of the blessing, but are simply learning. **However, at a time when they are fulfilling their obligation** through the recitation of a blessing, **one answers** amen after their blessing.

*Rav* answered: *achar ha-kol onim amen chutz mi-tinokot shel beit raban* — one says *amen* after everyone except these children in the school context, *ho'il u-le-lamed asuyin* — since they are reciting solely for the purpose of learning and practice. A *bracha* without genuine context is not a *bracha* in the full sense, and does not generate the response of *amen*.

However, this exclusion is not absolute. The *Gemara* qualifies: *ve-hani mili bi-d'lo idan maftirayhu* — this applies only when it is not the time of their *maftir*. *Rashi* explains this to mean the time when children were given the *haftarah* to read, as was the historical practice of assigning the reading of the *haftarah* to minors. At that moment, the *bracha* is no longer practice — it is genuine performance in context, and *amen* is appropriate. The *Ra'avad* offers a broader reading: the qualification applies whenever the children are being *poter* themselves, i.e., reciting a *bracha* in genuine fulfillment of their own obligation — such as before eating or before taking a *lulav*. In those cases, even if the reciter is a child, the *bracha* has genuine meaning and warrants the response of *amen*.

## Oil/Water Barriers

### Oil Prevention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שֶׁמֶן מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַבְּרָכָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי זִילַאי. רַבִּי זִיוַאי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב. רַב אַחָא אוֹמֵר: שֶׁמֶן טוֹב מְעַכֵּב. רַבִּי זוּהֲמַאי אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּזֹוהָם פָּסוּל לַעֲבוֹדָה, כָּךְ יָדַיִם מְזוֹהָמוֹת פְּסוּלוֹת לִבְרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one does not have **oil** to spread on and cleanse his hands after eating, this **prevents** him from reciting **the** Grace after Meals **blessing;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Zilai. Rabbi Zivai says:** Lack of that **oil does not prevent one** from reciting Grace after Meals. **Rav Aḥa says:** Lack of **fine oil prevents one** from reciting Grace after Meals. One must wait until he rubs oil on his hands. **Rav Zuhamai says: Just as one who is filthy is unfit for Temple service, so too are filthy hands unfit for** reciting **the** Grace after Meals **blessing.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the topic of *mayim acharonim*, picking up from the end of the *mishna*. A debate is recorded regarding whether having oil on one's hands prevents the recitation of *birkat ha-mazon*. *Reb Zilai* rules: *shemen me'akeiv et ha-bracha* — oil on the hands is a barrier to the blessing. One must clean the hands first.

### Zivai: No Block

*Rav Zivai* disagrees: *eino me'akeiv* — oil on the hands does not prevent the blessing. There is no impediment.

### Acha: Aromatic

*Rav Acha* takes an intermediate position: *shemen tov me'akeiv* — specifically aromatic, fragrant oil is the barrier. *Rashi* explains that if it is one's practice to use scented oil and one's hands are coated in it, one must wash before benching. Ordinary oil, however, does not present the same concern.

### Zuhmai: Smell

*Rav Zuhmai* — whose very name, the *Gemara* notes, seems related to the word *zuhama*, meaning stench — offers the broadest formulation: *keshem she-kohen mezuham pasul la-avodah* — just as a *kohen* who is *mezuham*, physically malodorous or blemished in certain ways, is disqualified from the Temple service — *kach yadayim mezuhamot pesulot la-bracha* — so too, foul-smelling hands disqualify one from reciting the blessing. The analogy to the *kohen* frames the recitation of *birkat ha-mazon* as a form of sacred service that demands bodily cleanliness as a precondition.

### Tosefta Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אֲנָא לָא זִילַאי וְלָא זִיוַאי וְלָא זוּהֲמַאי יָדַעְנָא אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתָא יָדַעְנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״וְהִתְקַדִּשְׁתֶּם״ אֵלּוּ מַיִם רִאשׁוֹנִים, ״וִהְיִיתֶם קְדֹשִׁים״ אֵלּוּ מַיִם אַחֲרוֹנִים, ״כִּי קָדוֹשׁ״ — זֶה שֶׁמֶן, ״אֲנִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ — זוֹ בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** of this: **I do not know of Zilai or Zivai or Zuhamai; rather, I know a *baraita*, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, and some say** that it was **taught in a *baraita*:** It is stated: "And you shall sanctify yourselves, and you shall be holy, for holy am I, the Lord your God" (Leviticus 20:26). With regard to this verse, the Sages said: **And you shall sanctify yourselves, these are the first waters** with which one washes his hands before the meal; **and you shall be holy, these are the final waters; for holy, this is oil** which one spreads on his hands; **am I, the Lord your God, this is** the Grace after Meals **blessing.**

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* demurs from citing these three authorities. He declares: *ana lo Zilai v'lo Zivai v'lo Zuhmai yadana* — I do not know these figures and cannot rely on their rulings alone. Instead, he grounds his ruling in a *baraita* (or, according to some versions, a tradition in the name of *Rav*): *ve-hiskadishtem vi-hyisem kedoshim* — "you shall sanctify yourselves and you shall be holy."

### Three Sanctifications

The *baraita* proceeds to parse this verse into a sequence of four sacred acts corresponding to the meal. *Ve-hiskadishtem* — "sanctify yourselves" — refers to *mayim rishonim*, the washing of hands before eating. *Vi-hyisem kedoshim* — "and you shall be holy" — refers to *mayim acharonim*, the washing of hands after eating. *Ki kadosh* — "for holy" — refers to the oil, the cleansing that follows. *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* — "I am the Lord your God" — refers to the *bracha*, the blessing of God's name. Three forms of sanctification through water and oil create the conditions of holiness in which God's name is then invoked. The equation of physical cleanliness with *kedusha* — holiness — is thus not merely metaphorical but structurally embedded in the sequence of the blessing.

### Mayim Acharonim

This *drasha* provides yet another source for the institution of *mayim acharonim*. *Tosafot*, notably, rules that in their time *mayim acharonim* was not obligatory, because the practical concerns that generated the original requirement no longer applied. Other *gemarot* ground *mayim acharonim* in the concern about *melach sdomis* — Sodomite salt, which posed a danger to the eyes if transferred from the hands to the face. The present *drasha*, by contrast, frames *mayim acharonim* more in ritual and sanctity terms than in health terms, with the washing representing a preparatory purification before the invocation of God's name.

One might observe that the rationale of *Tosafot* for not requiring *mayim acharonim* in Northern Europe is interesting when viewed against the history of eating utensils. If one no longer handled food directly with one's hands — because of the spread of forks and cutlery — then neither the health concern of *melach sdomis* nor the practical need for post-meal hand-cleansing would apply. The difficulty is that *Tosafot* appears to formulate this leniency in the twelfth century, while cutlery seems to have spread through Northern Europe in the thirteenth century. It is worth noting, meanwhile, that *mayim acharonim* remains widespread among *Sefardic* communities, where, at least historically, food was more commonly handled by hand. In any case, the *drasha* here — connecting hand-washing before and after the meal to the language of *kiddush yadayim*, sanctification of the hands, which appears in *tannaitic* literature as the technical term for priestly hand-washing — suggests a further layer of meaning in which the act of eating and blessing is understood as a form of sacred service, with all the attendant requirements of physical and ritual preparation that such service demands.
