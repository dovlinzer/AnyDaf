# Megillah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Seventy-Year Prophecy

### Belshazzar & Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם כְּשֶׁבֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ בִּשְׁנַת שָׁלֹשׁ לְמׇלְכוֹ אָמַר רָבָא מַאי כְּשֶׁבֶת לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּתְיַישְּׁבָה דַּעְתּוֹ אָמַר בֵּלְשַׁצַּר חַשֵּׁב וּטְעָה אֲנָא חָשֵׁיבְנָא וְלָא טָעֵינָא
>
> **Translation:** The verse states "In those days when the king sat on the throne of his kingdom," and it is written afterward "in the third year of his reign." **Rava said:** What is the meaning of "when he sat"? It is after **his mind was settled.** He said: Belshazzar calculated and erred; I will calculate and will not err.

The Gemara on 12 picks up three lines from the bottom of 11b, in the midst of a sustained comparison between Achashverosh and Belshazzar. Both figures shared the same sin: they removed the vessels of the *Beis Hamikdash* and put them to profane use. This parallel is not merely historical color — it is the exegetical foundation for the custom of reading *keilim mikeilim shonim* with a distinctive *trop*, a practice the Gemara will anchor to the precise wording of that verse. The broader frame is Achashverosh's sense of relief: with *nisyashva da'ato*, his mind settled, he became convinced that the seventy-year prophecy of redemption would not be fulfilled, and so he felt emboldened to celebrate.

### Seventy-Year Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא דִּכְתִיב כִּי לְפִי מְלֹאת לְבָבֶל שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה אֶפְקוֹד אֶתְכֶם וּכְתִיב לְמַלֹּאות לְחׇרְבוֹת יְרוּשָׁלִַם שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה חַשֵּׁוב אַרְבְּעִים וַחֲמֵשׁ דִּנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר וְעֶשְׂרִים וּתְלָת דֶּאֱוִיל מְרוֹדַךְ וְתַרְתֵּי דִּידֵיהּ הָא שִׁבְעִים אַפֵּיק מָאנֵי דְּבֵי מַקְדְּשָׁא וְאִשְׁתַּמַּשׁ בְּהוּ
>
> **Translation:** What is this? As it is written: "After seventy years are accomplished for Babylonia I will remember you" and it is written: "That He would accomplish for the desolations of Jerusalem seventy years." He calculated: Forty-five of Nebuchadnezzar, and twenty-three of Evil-merodach, and two of his own—that is seventy. **He took out the vessels of the Temple and used them.**

To understand Achashverosh's confidence, the Gemara reconstructs the arithmetic of the seventy-year prophecy as he understood it. One verse in *Yirmiyahu* speaks of seventy years *livavel* — to Babylon — while a verse in *Daniel* speaks of seventy years to the destruction of Jerusalem. Taking the first reckoning: Nevuchadnezzar reigned for forty-five years, Evil Merodach for twenty-three, and in Belshazzar's third year the total reached seventy. It was precisely at that moment that Belshazzar removed the *Beis Hamikdash* vessels — and was slain that very night.

Achashverosh, however, believed Belshazzar had miscounted. In his view, the seventy years should have been reckoned not from the beginning of Nevuchadnezzar's reign but from *Galus Yechonyah*, the beginning of the Babylonian exile proper — which added eight more years to the starting point. The Gemara then accounts for additional time: one more year remaining of Belshazzar's reign, five years of *Daryavesh* and Koresh combined, and two years of Achashverosh's own reign — all of which brought the total to seventy years from *Galus Yechonyah* by Achashverosh's third year. That is when he took out the vessels of the *Beis Hamikdash*.

### Achashverosh's Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא שַׁפִּיר חַשֵּׁיב אִיהוּ נָמֵי מִיטְעָא טָעֵי דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִימְנֵי מֵחׇרְבוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם סוֹף סוֹף כַּמָּה בְּצִירָן חֲדֵיסַר אִיהוּ כַּמָּה מְלַךְ אַרְבֵּיסַר בְּאַרְבֵּיסַר דִּידֵיהּ אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִיבְנֵי בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ אַלְּמָה כְּתִיב בֵּאדַיִן בְּטֵילַת עֲבִידַת בֵּית אֱלָהָא דִּי בִּירוּשְׁלֶם
>
> **Translation:** But he calculated correctly! **He too erred,** for he should have counted from the destruction of Jerusalem. **Ultimately, how many** years **were lacking? Eleven.** How many years did he reign? **Fourteen.** In his fourteenth year the Temple should have been built. **Why** is it written: **"Then the work of the House of God, which is in Jerusalem, ceased"**?

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *ve'ha shapir chashiv* — he seems to have done the math correctly. But the Gemara responds: *ihu nami miet'ei* — he also made a mistake. The verse in *Daniel* does not say seventy years from *Bavel* but seventy years *lacharvos Yerushalayim* — to the destruction of Jerusalem, which corresponds to *Galus Tzidkiyahu*, occurring in Nevuchadnezzar's nineteenth year. Since *Galus Yechonyah* occurred in Nevuchadnezzar's eighth year, the difference between the two starting points is eleven years. Achashverosh should therefore have waited not until his third year but until his fourteenth. Yet in his fourteenth year — the very year we know from the *Megillah* — he was still king, the Jews had not returned to *Eretz Yisrael*, and the *Beis Hamikdash* had not been rebuilt. So even the latest possible reckoning yields no completed redemption at that point.

This deepens the difficulty: *sof sof kama batziran chasrei* — in the end, even counting from the destruction of Jerusalem, you arrive only at Achashverosh's fourteenth year, when construction had already been halted. A verse in *Ezra* records the cessation explicitly: *be'adayin batilas avidas beis Elaha di b'Yerushalem* — at that time the work on the house of God in Jerusalem was stopped. The rebuilding that had begun under Koresh had been reversed.

### Partial Years Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא שָׁנִים מְקוּטָּעוֹת הֲווֹ
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The years** were **partial** years.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי וְעוֹד שָׁנָה אַחֶרֶת לְבָבֶל וְעָמַד דָּרְיָוֶשׁ וְהִשְׁלִימָהּ
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **And** there was **another year** for **Babylonia, and Darius arose and completed it.**

Rava resolves the problem: *shanim mekuta'os havu* — the years were not complete years. When the Gemara adds up forty-five years for Nevuchadnezzar and twenty-three for Evil Merodach, it implicitly assumes each reign consisted of full calendar years. But Nevuchadnezzar may have died partway through his forty-fifth year, and Evil Merodach may have begun partway through the first year of his reign — meaning these transitions involve double-counted partial years. By adding the reign numbers together as though they were complete, one loses a year or two. Belshazzar, therefore, had not actually reached the seventieth year when he celebrated; he was one year short.

A *Beraisa* confirms this: *v'yeser shana achas l'Belshazzar* — there was one additional year beyond what Belshazzar thought he had reached. When Daryavesh succeeded him, *v'amad Daryavesh v'hishlima* — Daryavesh completed that final year, bringing the count to a genuine seventy years from Babylon. That is when Koresh gave the Jews initial permission to begin rebuilding the *Beis Hamikdash*.

### Two Reckonings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא לִפְקִידָה בְּעָלְמָא וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב כֹּה אָמַר כּוֹרֶשׁ מֶלֶךְ פָּרַס כֹּל מַמְלְכוֹת הָאָרֶץ נָתַן לִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי הַשָּׁמָיִם וְהוּא פָקַד עָלַי לִבְנוֹת לוֹ בַיִת בִּירוּשָׁלִַם
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The seventy years that "are accomplished for Babylonia" were **only for being remembered,** as mentioned in the verse. **And this is as it is written** with regard to Cyrus's proclamation: **"Thus says Cyrus king of Persia: The Lord, God of heaven, has given me all the kingdoms of the earth; and He has charged me to build Him a house in Jerusalem."**

The picture that emerges from Rava's analysis is of two distinct and valid prophecies with two distinct fulfillments. The first reckoning — seventy years from Babylon — was fulfilled in the time of Koresh: *le-pekidah b'alma*, a mere remembrance, the beginning of a process. Koresh issued the celebrated declaration — *koh amar Koresh melech Paras... ve-hu pakad alai livnot lo bayit bi-Yerushalayim* — but this was only a partial and ultimately reversed gesture. The second reckoning — seventy years from the destruction of Jerusalem — was fulfilled after Achashverosh, in the time of the second Daryavesh, who the Gemara elsewhere identifies as the son of Achashverosh and Esther. That is when the genuine rebuilding of the *Beis Hamikdash* commenced in earnest.

Achashverosh was therefore wrong on two counts. First, he double-counted the partial years and believed the seventy years from Babylon had concluded in his own third year, when in fact it concluded two years earlier in Koresh's time. Second, even had he counted correctly, that first reckoning pointed only to a partial redemption — the second prophecy, reckoned from the destruction of Jerusalem, pointed to the complete one, which would come only after his own reign had ended.

### Daniel's Investigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אַף דָּנִיאֵל טְעָה בְּהַאי חוּשְׁבָּנָא דִּכְתִיב בִּשְׁנַת אַחַת לְמׇלְכוֹ אֲנִי דָּנִיֵּאל בִּינוֹתִי בַּסְּפָרִים מִדְּקָאָמַר בִּינוֹתִי מִכְּלָל דִּטְעָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Daniel also erred in this calculation, as it is written: "In the first year of his reign, I, Daniel, meditated in the books** over the number of the years." **From** the fact **that he said "I meditated," it can be inferred that he had** previously **erred.**

Rava adds that even Daniel made mistakes in working through this chronological puzzle. The Gemara derives this from the verse: *bishnat achat lemalcho ani Daniel binnoti basefarim* — in the first year of his king, I Daniel investigated the books. The very fact that Daniel says *binnoti* — I investigated closely — implies, by the Gemara's logic, that he had previously erred and needed to re-examine the verses. *Miklal de-ta'ah* — by implication, he had made some mistake. The difficulty in harmonizing the two competing prophecies — *milo'at levavel* versus *lacharvos Yerushalayim* — was genuine enough to confuse even the greatest prophet of the generation.

### Koresh's Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קָשׁוּ קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי כְּתִיב מְלֹאות לְבָבֶל וּכְתִיב לְחׇרְבוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **In any case, the verses contradict each other** with regard to how the seventy years should be calculated. **It is written:** "After seventy years **are accomplished for Babylonia**," and **it is written:** "That he would accomplish **for the desolations of Jerusalem** seventy years."

The Gemara confirms from the verse in *Ezra* that Koresh's permission to rebuild was indeed revoked. This reversal is the foundation for distinguishing the partial fulfillment under Koresh from the complete redemption that followed. Rava identifies Koresh's declaration as corresponding to *le-pekidah b'alma* — a slight, incomplete remembrance — because Koresh himself reversed his decree. The word *pakad* in the verse (*ve-hu pakad alai livnot*) is the same root as *pekidah*, deliberately chosen to signal that this was only a visitation, not a full redemption.

### Koresh vs. Messiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא מַאי דִּכְתִיב כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ לִמְשִׁיחוֹ לְכוֹרֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר הֶחֱזַקְתִּי בִימִינוֹ וְכִי כּוֹרֶשׁ מָשִׁיחַ הָיָה אֶלָּא אָמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמָשִׁיחַ קוֹבֵל אֲנִי לְךָ עַל כּוֹרֶשׁ אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי הוּא יִבְנֶה בֵּיתִי וִיקַבֵּץ גָּלִיּוֹתַי וְהוּא אָמַר מִי בָכֶם מִכׇּל עַמּוֹ וְיַעַל
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda interpreted homiletically** a verse concerning Cyrus: **What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Thus says the Lord to His anointed, to Cyrus, whose right hand I have held," was Cyrus** God's anointed one, i.e., the **Messiah?** Rather, the verse should be understood as God speaking to the Messiah with regard to Cyrus: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the Messiah: I am complaining to you about Cyrus,** who is not acting in accordance with what he is intended to do. **I had said: "He shall build My House and gather My exiles," but he said: "Whoever is among you of all His people…let him go up."**

From this context, the Gemara addresses the verse in *Yeshayahu*: *koh amar Hashem limeshicho le-Choresh* — thus says God to His anointed one, to Koresh. The question is direct: *ve'chi Koresh mashiach hava* — was Koresh really the *mashiach*? The Gemara rejects the straightforward reading. *Rashi* notes that a careful reading of the *trop* already distinguishes *meshicho* from *Koresh* syntactically. The Gemara's interpretation is that God is speaking to the *mashiach* about Koresh: *koh amar Hashem limeshicho... asher hachazakti bimino* — thus says God to His Messiah, concerning Koresh whom I strengthened. And the content of that address is a complaint: *kovel ani lecha al Koresh* — I have a grievance against Koresh to bring before you. God declared: *ani amarti hu yivneh beiti vekabeitz galiyosai* — I said he would build My house and gather My exiles. Yet Koresh said only *mi vachem mikol amo vaya'al* — whoever among you wishes may go up — issuing permission rather than actively rebuilding or gathering the exiles.

The passage thus draws a sharp distinction between the redemptive vision — which sounds unmistakably like the full *messianic* mandate of rebuilding the Temple and ingathering the exiles — and Koresh's limited, eventually reversed, decree. Koresh did not fail because he was wicked; the comparison to Chizkiyahu, who could have been the *mashiach* had he sung *shira* after *Sancheriv*, is apt. The point is that Koresh did not live up to the full potential of the role he had been given. And on that note, the analysis of the seventy-year prophecy closes — having situated the entire story of *Purim* within the larger arc of exile, anticipated redemption, and the as-yet-unfulfilled *geulah*.

## Media & Persia Alliance

### Medians/Persians Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵיל פָּרַס וּמָדַי הַפַּרְתְּמִים וּכְתִיב לְמַלְכֵי מָדַי וּפָרָס אָמַר רָבָא אַתְנוֹיֵי אַתְנוֹ בַּהֲדָדֵי אִי מִינַּן מַלְכֵי מִינַּיְיכוּ אִיפַּרְכֵי וְאִי מִינַּיְיכוּ מַלְכֵי מִינַּן אִיפַּרְכֵי
>
> **Translation:** The Megilla mentions: **"The army of Persia and Media, the nobles** and princes of the provinces," **and it is written:** "In the book of chronicles **of the kings of Media and Persia."** **Rava said** in response: These two peoples, the Persians and the Medes, **stipulated with each other,** saying: **If the kings** will come **from us, the ministers** will come **from you; and if the kings** will come **from you, the ministers** will come **from us.**

Returning to the *Megillah*, the text lists *cheil Paras uMadai hapartamim* — the officers of Persia and Media. Elsewhere, a verse refers to *malchei Madai uParas* — the kings of Media and Persia. *Rashi* notes that this creates a question of which nation takes precedence in which role: is the reference to the army coming from Persia, or to governors coming from Media? Rava resolves it by describing a formal power-sharing arrangement. *Astinuyei astin behadadi* — the Medians and Persians made an agreement with one another: if the kings come from us, the governors of the provinces will come from you; and if the kings come from you, the governors will come from us. These were two sovereign powers who had unified their dominion and divided its offices between them.

### Priestly Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהַרְאוֹתוֹ אֶת עוֹשֶׁר כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלָּבַשׁ בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה כְּתִיב הָכָא יְקָר תִּפְאֶרֶת גְּדוּלָּתוֹ וּכְתִיב הָתָם לְכָבוֹד וּלְתִפְאֶרֶת
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"When he showed the riches of his glorious [*kevod*] kingdom** and the honor of his majestic [*tiferet*] greatness.** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: This teaches that** Ahasuerus **wore the priestly vestments.** **It is written here:** "The riches of his glorious [*kevod*] kingdom and **the honor of his majestic [*tiferet*] greatness."** And **it is written there,** with regard to the priestly garments: **"For glory [*kavod*] and for majesty [*tiferet*]."**

The Gemara turns to the verse *beharoso es osher kvod malchuso* — when he displayed the wealth and glory of his kingdom. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina teaches: *melamed shelavash bigdei kehuna* — this teaches that Achashverosh wore the *bigdei kehunah*, the priestly vestments of the *Beis Hamikdash*. The verse's language of *yekar tiferes gedulaso* — honor, glory, greatness — corresponds through the shared root *tiferes* to the verse about the *bigdei kehunah*: *lechavod uletiferes*, and *yekar* as the Aramaic equivalent of *kavod*. So the display of royal splendor was in fact an appropriation of the sacred vestments of the high priesthood, an act continuous with the removal of the *keilim*. These details are not merely discrete exegetical observations; together they sketch a portrait of a king who seeks to absorb the sanctity and prestige of the *Beis Hamikdash* into his own court — its vessels, its garments, its honor — even as the Temple itself lies in ruins.

### Wise or Foolish?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִמְלֹאות הַיָּמִים הָאֵלֶּה רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל חַד אָמַר מֶלֶךְ פִּיקֵּחַ הָיָה וְחַד אָמַר מֶלֶךְ טִיפֵּשׁ הָיָה מַאן דְּאָמַר מֶלֶךְ פִּיקֵּחַ הָיָה שַׁפִּיר עֲבַד דְּקָרֵיב רַחִיקָא בְּרֵישָׁא דִּבְנֵי מָאתֵיהּ כׇּל אֵימַת דְּבָעֵי מְפַיֵּיס לְהוּ וּמַאן דְּאָמַר טִיפֵּשׁ הָיָה דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְקָרוֹבֵי בְּנֵי מָאתֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא דְּאִי מָרְדוּ בֵּיהּ הָנָךְ הָנֵי הֲווֹ קָיְימִי בַּהֲדֵיהּ
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And when these days were fulfilled,** the king made a feast." **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed as to whether this was a wise decision. **One said:** Ahasuerus arranged a feast for the residents of Shushan, the capital, after the feast for foreign dignitaries that preceded it, indicating that **he was a clever king,** because **he acted well when he first brought close those** more **distant** subjects by inviting them to the earlier celebration, **as he could appease the residents of his** own **city whenever he wished. And the one who said** that **he was foolish** maintains **that he should have invited the residents of his city first, so that if those** faraway subjects **rebelled against him, these** who lived close by **would have stood with him.**

On the question of the hundred-and-eighty-day feast, Rav and Shmuel dispute whether Achashverosh's strategy was clever or foolish. The one who says *melech pike'ach hayah* — he was a wise king — argues that it was shrewd to invite the distant provinces first: those inhabitants are harder to control and must be won over early, while the people of one's own city can be cultivated later, whenever needed, since they remain accessible. The one who says *melech tipesh hayah* — he was a foolish king — argues the opposite: one should first solidify the home constituency, for if the distant provinces rebel, at least the local population will remain loyal. Without that solid base, rebellion from afar becomes existential.

## Jewish Guilt Question

### Why Haman's Threat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי מִפְּנֵי מָה נִתְחַיְּיבוּ שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר כְּלָיָה
>
> **Translation:** **The students of Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai asked him: For what** reason **were the enemies of Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people themselves when exhibiting behavior that is not in their best interests, **in that generation deserving of annihilation?**

The Gemara pauses from the verse-by-verse reading to take up a theological question, framed as an exchange between Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and his students. The students asked: *mipnei mah nischayvu soneihem shel Yisrael she'be'oso hador kilah* — why were the Jews of that generation deserving of destruction? What did they do that warranted the *gezeiras Haman*?

### Assimilation Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנֶּהֱנוּ מִסְּעוּדָתוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע אִם כֵּן שֶׁבְּשׁוּשַׁן יֵהָרְגוּ שֶׁבְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ אַל יֵהָרְגוּ
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: It is because they partook of the feast of that wicked one,** Ahasuerus. **If so, those in Shushan should have been killed** as punishment, but **those in the rest of the world,** who did not participate in the feast, **should not have been killed.**

The students offered their own answer first: *mipnei she'nehenu mi'seudaso shel oso rasha* — because they derived benefit from the feast of that wicked one, Achashverosh. The problem with their participation was either the assimilation it represented — eating non-*kosher* food, mixing freely in a pagan court — or a form of complicity in the political machinations of the king himself, given that the feast is attributed to *oso rasha*. While both dimensions are present, the emphasis on *seudaso shel oso rasha* suggests the concern about moral complicity in the king's agenda carries particular weight. The Gemara immediately challenges this answer: *im kein she'be'Shushan yeiharagu she'be'chol ha'olam kulo al yeiharagu* — if so, only the Jews of Shushan who actually participated should have been condemned, not Jews throughout the entire empire.

### Idol Worship Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ אֱמוֹר אַתָּה אָמַר לָהֶם מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִשְׁתַּחֲווּ לַצֶּלֶם
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him:** Then **you say** your response to our question. **He said to them: It is because they prostrated before the idol.**

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai himself then gave the answer: *mipnei she'hishtachavu la'tzelem* — because they bowed down to the idol. *Rashi* identifies this as the episode from Nevuchadnezzar's time, when the entire populace was compelled to bow to his statue — the three heroes Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah being the notable exceptions. The students immediately pressed: *ve'chi maso panim yesh ba'davar* — would there then be any partiality shown, any grounds for forgiveness? If the sin was *avodah zarah*, how could God perform a miracle on their behalf?

### Lifnim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ וְכִי מַשּׂוֹא פָּנִים יֵשׁ בַּדָּבָר אָמַר לָהֶם הֵם לֹא עָשׂוּ אֶלָּא לִפְנִים אַף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לֹא עָשָׂה עִמָּהֶן אֶלָּא לִפְנִים וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב כִּי לֹא עִנָּה מִלִּבּוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him:** But if it is true that they worshipped idols and therefore deserved to be destroyed, why was a miracle performed on their behalf? **Is there favoritism** expressed by God **here? He said to them: They did not** really worship the idol, but pretended to **do** so **only for appearance,** acting as if they were carrying out the king's command to bow before the idol. **So too, the Holy One, Blessed be He, did not** destroy them but **did** act angry **with them only for appearance.** **And this is as it is written: "For He does not afflict from His heart** willingly."**

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai replied: *heim lo asu ela lifnim* — they acted only for outward appearances. The word *lifnim*, from *panim*, the face, denotes the external surface: they bowed because Nevuchadnezzar compelled them, not because they genuinely accepted the idol. There was no internal denial of God, only an external compliance under coercion. The corollary follows immediately: *af haKadosh Baruch Hu lo asah imahem ela lifnim* — so too, the Holy One, blessed be He, acted against them only for appearances. The threat of Haman was real on the surface but would not, in the end, be carried through to its conclusion. And the verse confirms the principle: *ki lo inah mi'libo* — God does not bring suffering from His heart.

### Coercion & Martyrdom

This is a striking *sugya*, and it is worth pausing on its implications. What is actually being said is that coerced *avodah zarah* — bowing to an idol under threat of death — does not constitute the sin of *avodah zarah* proper. *Tosafot* touches on this in a passing remark, but the Rambam develops the same principle at length in the *Iggeret Teiman*, addressing Jews who accepted Islam under duress. His position is carefully calibrated: the person who capitulates under coercion has failed to perform the *mitzvah* of *Kiddush Hashem* — the obligation to give one's life rather than transgress *avodah zarah*, *gilui arayos*, or *shefichas damim* in a time of religious persecution. That failure is real and significant. But such a person has not, the Rambam argues, transgressed the prohibition of *avodah zarah*, because they were entirely *anous* — coerced — and there was no genuine belief or inner assent behind the act. The sin, properly understood, is a failure of *Kiddush Hashem*, not a commission of *avodah zarah*.

This distinction carries weighty pastoral and policy implications. An openness to receiving those who submitted under coercion — grounded in the principle that their sin was not *avodah zarah* — is essential for preserving the community's connection to those who could not or did not martyr themselves. Yet too permissive a posture risks signaling that capitulation is costless, which in subsequent generations could erode the willingness to resist at all, and ultimately lead to complete assimilation rather than eventual return. These tensions are unresolved by the Gemara itself; it establishes the principle and leaves the policy questions in the hands of those who must apply it.

### Hester Panim Parallel

The parallelism the Gemara establishes here is precise. Just as the Jews' compliance with the idol was *lifnim* — external only, not reflecting their inner reality — so too God's response was *lifnim*: the appearance of a mortal decree without the reality of divine abandonment behind it. This parallel also maps onto the distinctive character of the *nes* of *Purim* — the *nes nistar*, the hidden miracle. In the *Megillah*, God's name does not appear. The entire reversal of fortune unfolds through ostensibly natural events: a king's whim, a beauty contest, a sleepless night. The visible surface is purely political. What is actually happening — the invisible hand of Providence — is concealed. The measure-for-measure structure, *midah k'neged midah*, operates here not only in the content but in the form: as their worship was purely external and not inner, so God's threatening posture was purely external and not inner. The *hester panim* of the miracle is thus itself a *midah k'neged midah* parallel to the *lifnim* of the Jews' idolatrous compliance.

## Feast Details

### Feast Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲצַר גִּנַּת בִּיתַן הַמֶּלֶךְ רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל חַד אָמַר הָרָאוּי לֶחָצֵר לֶחָצֵר הָרָאוּי לַגִּינָּה לַגִּינָּה הָרָאוּי לַבִּיתָן לַבִּיתָן וְחַד אָמַר הוֹשִׁיבָן בֶּחָצֵר וְלֹא הֶחְזִיקָתַן בַּגִּינָּה וְלֹא הֶחְזִיקָתַן עַד שֶׁהִכְנִיסָן לַבִּיתָן וְהֶחְזִיקָתַן בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא הוֹשִׁיבָן בֶּחָצֵר וּפָתַח לָהֶם שְׁנֵי פְתָחִים אֶחָד לַגִּינָּה וְאֶחָד לַבִּיתָן
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"In the court of the garden of the king's palace."** **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed with regard to how to understand the relationship between these three places. **One said:** The guests were received in different places. **One** who, according to his stature, was **fit for the courtyard** was brought **to the courtyard; one** who was **fit for the garden** was brought **to the garden;** and **one** who was **fit for the palace** was brought **to the palace. And** the other **one said: He** first **sat them in the courtyard, but it did not hold them,** as they were too numerous. He then sat them **in the garden, but it did not hold them** either, **until he brought them into the palace and it held them.** **A third understanding was taught in a *baraita*: He sat them in the courtyard and opened two entranceways for them, one to the garden and one to the palace.**

The verse places Achashverosh's feast *bachatzar ginat bitan hamelech* — in the courtyard of the garden of the castle. Rav and Shmuel disagree on what this compound geography indicates. One holds that the three spaces — courtyard, garden, and castle — were used simultaneously according to the guests' rank: those deserving of the courtyard were placed in the courtyard, those deserving of the garden in the garden, and those of sufficient standing in the castle itself. The other holds that the king tried to seat everyone in the courtyard first, but the crowd was too large; he then moved them to the garden, which also proved insufficient, until finally all were brought inside the castle.

A *Beraisa* offers a third reading: all guests were seated in the courtyard, and two doors were opened from it — one leading to the garden, one leading into the castle — so that guests could move between the three spaces at will, strolling through the garden or touring the palace as the evening permitted. *Tosafot* raises a question in connection with the first reading: if different locations were assigned by rank, why does the Gemara not object that this would generate jealousy among the guests — *atah metil kinah biseudah* — the same objection raised moments later about the seating arrangements? *Tosafot* answers that the spaces were so vast that guests in one area could not readily see those in another, and so the visible disparity was less acute.

### Tapestries & Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּר כַּרְפַּס וּתְכֵלֶת מַאי חוּר רַב אָמַר חָרֵי חָרֵי וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר מֵילָת לְבָנָה הִצִּיעַ לָהֶם כַּרְפַּס אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא כָּרִים שֶׁל פַּסִּים
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "There were hangings of ***ḥur*, *karpas*, and sky blue.** **What is *ḥur*? Rav said:** A fabric fashioned with **many holes,** similar to lace. **And Shmuel said: He spread out for them** carpets of **white wool.** **And what is ***karpas*? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: Cushions** of **velvet.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל גְּלִילֵי כֶסֶף וְעַמּוּדֵי שֵׁשׁ מִטּוֹת זָהָב וָכֶסֶף תַּנְיָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר הָרָאוּי לְכֶסֶף לְכֶסֶף הָרָאוּי לְזָהָב לְזָהָב אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אִם כֵּן אַתָּה מֵטִיל קִנְאָה בַּסְּעוּדָה אֶלָּא הֵם שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְרַגְלֵיהֶן שֶׁל זָהָב
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"On silver rods and pillars of marble; the couches were of gold and silver."** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Some couches were of gold and others of silver. **One** who, according to his stature, was **fit for silver** sat on a couch of **silver,** and **one** who was **fit for gold** sat on one of **gold. Rabbi Neḥemya said to him:** This was not done. **If so, you** would **cast jealousy into the feast,** for the guests would be envious of each other. **Rather,** the couches **themselves** were made **of silver, and their feet** were made **of gold.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהַט וָשֵׁשׁ אֲמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אֲבָנִים שֶׁמִּתְחוֹטְטוֹת עַל בַּעֲלֵיהֶן וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר אַבְנֵי נֵזֶר מִתְנוֹסְסוֹת עַל אַדְמָתוֹ
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: "Upon a pavement of ***bahat* and marble.** **Rabbi Asi said** with regard to the definition of *bahat*: These are **stones that ingratiate themselves with their owners,** as they are precious stones that people are willing to spend large amounts of money to acquire. **And similarly, it states** elsewhere that the Jewish people will be likened to precious stones: **"And the Lord their God shall save them in that day as the flock of His people; for they shall be as "the stones of a crown, glittering over His land."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַר וְסוֹחָרֶת רַב אָמַר דָּרֵי דָּרֵי וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר אֶבֶן טוֹבָה יֵשׁ בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם וְדָרָה שְׁמָהּ הוֹשִׁיבָהּ בְּאֶמְצַע סְעוּדָה וּמְאִירָה לָהֶם כַּצׇּהֳרַיִם דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא שֶׁקָּרָא דְּרוֹר לְכׇל בַּעֲלֵי סְחוֹרָה
>
> **Translation:** The verse concludes: **"And *dar* and *soḥaret*.**" **Rav said:** *Dar* means **many rows** around. Similarly, *soḥaret* is derived from *seḥor seḥor*, around and around, meaning that the floor was surrounded with numerous rows of *bahat* and marble stones. **And Shmuel said:** There is **a precious stone in the seaports, and its name is *dara*,** and Ahasuerus **placed it in the center of** the **feast, and it illuminated** the festivities **for them as** the sun illuminates the world **at midday.** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a *baraita*: This means **that he proclaimed a remission for all the merchants,** absolving them from paying their taxes.

The text describes the furnishings: *chur karpas usecheiles* — various linens. Rav reads *chur* as deriving from the root for holes or fine needlework, indicating elaborate stitching. Shmuel reads it from *chivar* — white — interpreting it as white silk that was laid out for the guests. As for *karpas* — familiar from the Pesach *seder* — Rav Yosef bar Chanina identifies it as *karim shel pasim*, cushions of velvet, placed on pillars of silver and columns of marble alongside beds of gold and silver.

On those beds, Rav Yehudah holds that they were distributed by rank: those deserving silver received silver beds, those deserving gold received gold. Rabbi Nechemyah objects — *im kein atah metil kinah biseudah* — this would stir envy at the feast. His resolution: the beds were silver but their legs were gold, so all guests had the same combination of materials and there was nothing to covet. *Tosafot* again raises the question of why the same objection was not applied to the assignment of different locations by rank, and answers as above: the scale of the spaces prevented guests from observing one another's placement.

The text continues: *bahat vasheish* — precious stones and marble. Rav Asi explains *bahat* as stones that *mis-chatetos* on their owners — precious gems so rare and costly that they demand everything of their possessors to seek them out, as the verse says: *avnei nezer misnossesos al admaso*, crown jewels that gleam over the land. The word *misnossesos*, *Rashi* notes, suggests extraordinary effort required to obtain them.

The word *dar* is also parsed. Rav reads it as *dari dari* — rows upon rows of precious stones. Shmuel identifies it as a specific gem called *darah*, found in distant isles, which was placed at the center of the feast and *meirah lahem ke-tzohorayim* — illuminated the hall like noon. The school of Rabbi Yishmael takes a different approach entirely, reading *vedar vesochares* not as a description of precious stones but as a policy: Achashverosh declared *deror* — freedom, a remission of taxes — for *ba'alei sechorah*, the merchants. All commerce was freed from imposts, and this contributed to the universal celebration.

### Vessels Sin Echo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁקוֹת בִּכְלֵי זָהָב וְכֵלִים מִכֵּלִים שׁוֹנִים מְשׁוּנִּים מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ אָמַר רָבָא יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם רִאשׁוֹנִים כָּלוּ מִפְּנֵי כֵלַי וְאַתֶּם שׁוֹנִים בָּהֶם
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And they gave them drink in vessels of gold, the vessels being diverse [*shonim*] from one another."** **Why does the verse use the term *shonim* to express that they are different? It should have said** the more proper term ***meshunim*. Rava said: A Divine Voice issued forth and said to them: The early ones,** referring to Belshazzar and his people, **were destroyed because** they used **these vessels,** the vessels of the Temple, **and** yet **you use them again [*shonim*]?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵין מַלְכוּת רָב אָמַר מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הִשְׁקָהוּ יַיִן שֶׁגָּדוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ בְּשָׁנִים
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And royal wine in abundance [*rav*]."** **Rav said: This teaches that each and every** guest at the feast **was poured** well-aged **wine that was older [*rav*] than himself in years.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁתִיָּה כַדָּת אֵין אוֹנֵס מַאי כַּדָּת אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר כְּדָת שֶׁל תּוֹרָה מָה דָּת שֶׁל תּוֹרָה אֲכִילָה מְרוּבָּה מִשְּׁתִיָּה אַף סְעוּדָּתוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע אֲכִילָה מְרוּבָּה מִשְּׁתִיָּה
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And the drinking was according to the law; none did compel."** **What is** the meaning of **"according to the law"? Rabbi Ḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** The drinking was **according to the law of the Torah. Just as,** according to **the law of the Torah,** with regard to offerings, **the food** sacrificed on the altar **is greater** in quantity **than the drink,** for the wine libation is quantitatively much smaller than the sacrificial offerings it accompanies, **so too,** at the **feast of that wicked man, the food was greater** in quantity **than the drink.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין אוֹנֵס אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הִשְׁקָהוּ מִיֵּין מְדִינָתוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת כִּרְצוֹן אִישׁ וָאִישׁ אָמַר רָבָא לַעֲשׂוֹת כִּרְצוֹן מָרְדֳּכַי וְהָמָן מָרְדֳּכַי דִּכְתִיב אִישׁ יְהוּדִי הָמָן אִישׁ צַר וְאוֹיֵב
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"None did compel."** **Rabbi Elazar said: This teaches that each and every** guest at the feast **was poured** a drink **from wine of his** own **country,** so that he would feel entirely free, as if he were in his home country. The verse continues: **"That they should do according to every man's pleasure."** **Rava** commented on the literal meaning of the verse, which is referring to two men, **and said:** The man and man whom they should follow indicates **that they should do according to the wishes of Mordecai and Haman.** **Mordecai** is called man, **as it is written:** "There was a certain **Jewish man** in Shushan the castle." **And Haman** is also called man, as it states: **"A man** who is an adversary and an enemy.**"**

The verse states *vehashkot biklei zahav vekeilim mikeilim shonim* — drinking from golden vessels and from *keilim mikeilim shonim*. The Gemara notes a grammatical difficulty: the word *shonim*, meaning different, seems to call for *meshunim* if the intent is simply "varied." Rava resolves it through a *bat kol*: a heavenly voice declared, *rishonim kallu mipnei keilim, ve'atem shonim bahem* — the earlier ones, Belshazzar and his court, were destroyed *because of* the vessels, and you are *repeating* the sin — *shonim* from the root *lishnot*, to repeat. The word thus carries a double meaning: the vessels were different from one another, and the sin of using them was being replicated. This is precisely the exegetical basis for the custom of reading *keilim mikeilim shonim* with an unusual *trop*, marking the verse as a site of theological weight.

### Wine & Freedom

The verse describes *yein malchut rav* — wine of the kingdom in abundance. Rav interprets *rav* not as plentiful but as aged: each guest received wine older than his own age in years. *Vehashtiyas khadat, ein ones* — and the drinking was according to the law, no one was compelled. The Gemara asks: *mai khadat* — what does it mean, according to the law? Rav Chanan in the name of Rabbi Meir explains: *khadat shel Torah*, following the rule of the Torah. And what is the Torah's rule? *Achilah merubah mishtiyah* — food exceeds drinking. In the Temple service, the quantities of grain offerings and *korbanos* placed on the *mizbe'ach* vastly exceeded the libations. So too at Achashverosh's feast, food dominated over drink.

### Food/Wine Ratio

This observation — that Achashverosh's feast mirrored the proportions of the Temple service — is worth pausing over. On one level it might seem like a compliment to Achashverosh. But in context it functions differently. Achashverosh is already wearing the *bigdei kehunah*, displaying the Temple's *keilim*, and now unconsciously replicating the food-to-wine ratios of the *mizbe'ach*. The effect is less to honor him than to underscore the theme of usurpation: he is positioning Shushan as the new Jerusalem, himself as the new *Kohen Gadol*, his feast as the new Temple service — while the actual Temple lies in ruins. There may also be a resonance with the earlier Gemara about *Purim* and the question of how to calibrate the obligation of *mishteh vesimchah* and the emphasis on *seudah*. The Gemara's reminder that even at Achashverosh's feast the food was proportionally greater than the wine may implicitly calibrate the proper balance that should characterize our own *Purim* celebrations.

Rabbi Elazar adds a further gloss on *ein ones*: each guest was served wine from his own country, so that no one was made uncomfortable by being given wine that was alien to him or difficult to drink. Finally, Rava interprets the phrase *la'asot kirtzon ish va'ish* — to do the will of every man — as referring specifically to Mordechai and Haman, both of whom are called *ish* in the *Megillah*: Mordechai as *ish Yehudi* and Haman as *ish tzar ve'oyev*. The Maharsha disputes *Rashi*'s reading that Mordechai and Haman were the wine stewards, arguing that *ratzon* refers to the guests' preferences, with Mordechai and Haman serving as stand-ins for people of all nations — whatever nation you came from, your needs were served. One might also read in this a subtle anticipation of a theme the Gemara develops elsewhere: that the entire drama of the *Megillah* was driven equally by the *ratzon* of both these men — the *arur Haman uvaruch Mordechai* dynamic — in which the actions and stubbornness of each contributed to the unfolding of events.

## Vashti's Punishment

### Vashti's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּם וַשְׁתִּי הַמַּלְכָּה עָשְׂתָה מִשְׁתֵּה נָשִׁים בֵּית הַמַּלְכוּת בֵּית הַנָּשִׁים מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ אָמַר רָבָא שְׁנֵיהֶן לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה נִתְכַּוְּונוּ
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Also Vashti the queen made a feast for the women, in the royal house,** which belonged to King Ahasuerus.** The Gemara questions why she held the feast in the royal house, a place of men, rather than in **the women's house.** **Rava said** in response: **The two of them had sinful intentions.**

The Gemara turns to Vashti. The verse states that she made a feast for women in *beit hamalchut* — in the king's castle. The Gemara notes the anomaly: she should have held the women's feast in *beit hanashim*, the women's quarters, not in the king's castle. Rava explains: *she-shneihem li-dvar aveirah nitkavnu* — both Achashverosh and Vashti had sinful intentions in mind. The proximity of the women's feast to the king's castle was deliberate. As the popular saying goes, *ihu bekarei ittei bebutzinei* — he has his gourds and she has hers; what is sauce for the goose is sauce for the gander. Both parties to the evening had the same agenda.

### Seventh Day Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי כְּטוֹב לֵב הַמֶּלֶךְ בַּיָּיִן אַטּוּ עַד הַשְׁתָּא לָא טָב לִבֵּיהּ בְּחַמְרָא אֲמַר רָבָא יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי שַׁבָּת הָיָה שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין מַתְחִילִין בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה וּבְדִבְרֵי תִשְׁבָּחוֹת אֲבָל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם שֶׁאוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין אֵין מַתְחִילִין אֶלָּא בְּדִבְרֵי תִיפְלוּת וְכֵן בִּסְעוּדָּתוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע הַלָּלוּ אוֹמְרִים מָדִיּוֹת נָאוֹת וְהַלָּלוּ אוֹמְרִים פָּרְסִיּוֹת נָאוֹת אָמַר לָהֶם אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ כְּלִי שֶׁאֲנִי מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ אֵינוֹ לֹא מָדִיִּי וְלֹא פָּרְסִי אֶלָּא כַּשְׂדִּיִּי רְצוֹנְכֶם לִרְאוֹתָהּ אָמְרוּ לוֹ אִין וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא עֲרוּמָּה
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"On the seventh day, when the heart of the king was merry with wine."** **Is that to say** that **until now his heart was not merry with wine?** **Rava said: The seventh day was Shabbat,** when the difference between the Jewish people and the gentiles is most apparent. **When the Jewish people eat and drink, they begin** by occupying themselves **with words of Torah and words of praise.** **But the nations of the world, when they eat and drink, they begin only with words of licentiousness.** So too, at the feast of that wicked man, **some said: The Median women are** the most **beautiful, while others said: The Persian women are** the most **beautiful. Ahasuerus said to them: The vessel that I use,** i.e., my wife, **is neither Median nor Persian, but** rather **Chaldean. Do you wish to see her? They said to him: Yes, provided that she be naked.**

The verse notes that on the seventh day, *ke-tov lev hamelech beyayin* — when the king's heart was merry with wine — he issued his demand. The Gemara is puzzled: *atu ad hashta lo tav libei bekhamra* — until now, after six days of drinking, his heart was not merry with wine? Rava explains: *yom hashevi'i Shabbat haya* — the seventh day was *Shabbat*. When Jews eat and drink on *Shabbat*, *matkhilin bedivrei Torah uvdivrei tishbakhot* — they begin with words of Torah and words of praise; the joy of the table is channeled into learning, singing *zemirot*, elevating the moment. *Oveidei kokhavim umazzalot*, however — idolaters — when they eat and drink, *ein matkhilin ela bedivrei tiflut* — they begin only with lewd talk. And so at Achashverosh's feast, the conversation on that seventh day turned to women: the Median partisans insisted Median women were the most beautiful, the Persian partisans insisted Persian women were. Achashverosh intervened: the vessel I use, he said crudely, is neither Median nor Persian but Chaldean — referring to Vashti — and would you like to see her? The company agreed, and specified that she must appear naked.

### Shabbat Abuse/Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבַּמִּדָּה שֶׁאָדָם מוֹדֵד בָּהּ מוֹדְדִין לוֹ מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיְתָה וַשְׁתִּי הָרְשָׁעָה מְבִיאָה בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמַפְשִׁיטָתָן עֲרוּמּוֹת וְעוֹשָׂה בָּהֶן מְלָאכָה בְּשַׁבָּת הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב אַחַר הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה כְּשׁוֹךְ חֲמַת הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ זָכַר אֶת וַשְׁתִּי וְאֵת אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂתָה וְאֵת אֲשֶׁר נִגְזַר עָלֶיהָ כְּשֵׁם שֶׁעָשָׂתָה כָּךְ נִגְזַר עָלֶיהָ
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Vashti was punished in this humiliating way **for it is with the measure that a man measures** to others **that he** himself **is measured.** **This teaches that the wicked Vashti would take the daughters of Israel, and strip them naked, and make them work on Shabbat.** Therefore, it was decreed that she be brought before the king naked, on Shabbat. **This is as it is written: "After these things, when the wrath of King Ahasuerus was appeased, he remembered Vashti, and what she had done, and what was decreed against her."** That is to say, **just as she had done** with the young Jewish women, **so it was decreed upon her.**

The Gemara now introduces the *midah k'neged midah*. The principle *shebemidah sheadam moded bah modedin lo* — the measure with which one measures is the measure applied to him — here applies not to Achashverosh but to Vashti. The Gemara teaches: Vashti would take young *benot Yisrael* and force them to work for her naked on *Shabbat*. Therefore, the decree that she appear naked came on *Shabbat*. The verse in *Esther* — *ve-asher nigzar aleiha* — is read in this light: what was *nigzar* upon her was not merely the king's decree but the divine *midah k'neged midah* rooted in her own prior conduct.

### Command Refused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַתְּמָאֵן הַמַּלְכָּה וַשְׁתִּי
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"But the queen Vashti refused** to come."

The *HaGra* notes a subtle grammatical detail in the verses: when Achashverosh sends his command, the text reads *lehavi et Vashti hamalkah* — Vashti the queen, her name first. When she refuses, the text reverses: *vatema'en hamalkah Vashti* — the queen Vashti, her title first. The *HaGra* reads this as reflecting the subtext of their conflict. Achashverosh addressed her as though her royalty were incidental — she is Vashti who happens to be queen because she married him. She responded by asserting that the title comes first: she is the queen, and her royal lineage is primary. As the Gemara will presently clarify, Vashti understood herself as the granddaughter of Nevuchadnezzar, and her message to the king made that hierarchy explicit.

### Vashti Disfigured

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי פְּרִיצְתָּא הֲוַאי דְּאָמַר מָר שְׁנֵיהֶן לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה נִתְכַּוְּונוּ מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אֲתַאי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁפָּרְחָה בָּהּ צָרַעַת בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וְעָשָׂה לָהּ זָנָב
>
> **Translation:** **Since she was immodest, as the Master said:** **The two of them had sinful intentions, what is the reason** that **she did not come? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: This teaches that she broke out in leprosy,** and therefore she was embarrassed to expose herself publicly. **A *baraita* taught:** The angel **Gabriel came and fashioned her a tail.**

The Gemara asks: given that Vashti was by all accounts *peritzusa* — licentious — and the earlier discussion established that both she and the king had sinful intent, why did she refuse? *Michdi peritzuta havai*, *de'amar mar shenehem lidvar aveirah nitkavnu* — given her character, she might have been expected to comply. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina explains: *melamed sheparchah bah tzara'at* — she had been stricken with *tzara'at*; she was disfigured and could not appear. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* link the word *nigzar* in the verse to the usage of that root in the context of *Uziyahu*, where *tzara'at* is described using the same language. A *Beraisa* adds a more vivid detail: *ba Gavriel ve'asah lah zanav* — the angel Gavriel fashioned a tail for her. The *Maharsha* notes that this need not be taken literally; it likely refers to some form of grotesque physical growth that made appearance at the royal feast impossible.

### Vashti Mocks King

The Gemara continues with the substance of Vashti's message, which appears to be cut off in the Sefaria text at 12b:1. From the context and continuation in the essay, the passage should read: She sent word back: *bar ahorya de'abba* — you are the stable boy of my father. My father — referring to Nevuchadnezzar — *lekabel alfa chamra shatei velo ravi* — could drink a thousand measures of wine and not become drunk. *Vehai gavra ishtit bekhamra miha* — and this man, you, got drunk with your wine. The message was a direct assault on Achashverosh's legitimacy: he was no king by lineage, only by marriage into royalty he did not deserve. He could not hold his wine; he could not hold his authority. *Vachamato ba'arah bo* — and his fury burned within him.

## Consulting Sages

### King Consults

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ לַחֲכָמִים יוֹדְעֵי הָעִתִּים
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: The king **said to the wise men who knew the times,** those who know how to intercalate years and fix the months, meaning the rabbis.

*Vayomer hamelech lachachamim yodei ha'itim* — and the king said to the wise men who knew the times, those who know how to intercalate years and fix the months, meaning the rabbis. This detail is worth noting in itself. The Gemara is doing something larger than providing exegesis of a verse: it is recasting the entire story of the *Megillah* as a story of Jewish history, told from within the perspective of Jewish history. Everything about Achashverosh — his preoccupation with the seventy years, his use of the Temple's *keilim* and *bigdei kehunah*, and now his instinct to bring his legal question to the rabbis — places the Jewish dimension at the center of what might appear to be purely Persian court politics. The world's history, the Gemara consistently implies, is really Jewish history; everything else is context.

### Sages Decline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ דַּיְּינוּהָ לִי הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד אָמְרוּ הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד נֵימָא לֵיהּ קִטְלַהּ לִמְחַר פָּסֵיק לֵיהּ חַמְרֵיהּ וּבָעֵי לַהּ מִינַּן נֵימָא לֵיהּ שִׁבְקַהּ קָא מְזַלְזְלָה בְּמַלְכוּתָא אָמְרוּ לוֹ מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ וְגָלִינוּ מֵאַרְצֵנוּ נִיטְּלָה עֵצָה מִמֶּנּוּ וְאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת
>
> **Translation:** Ahasuerus **said to them: Judge her for me.** The Sages **said** to each other: **What should we do?** If **we say to him: Kill her, tomorrow he will become sober, and** then **demand her from us. If** we **say to him: Let her be, she has scorned royalty,** and that cannot be tolerated. **They said to him** as follows: **From the day that the Temple was destroyed and we have been exiled from our land, counsel** and insight **have been removed from us, and we do not know how to judge capital cases.**

The sages found themselves in an impossible position. If they told Achashverosh to execute Vashti, he would sober up the next day, regret losing his queen, and hold them responsible. If they told him to let the matter go, they would be seen as condoning her public humiliation of the king's authority and complicit in the disrespect shown to the crown. Their solution was evasion: *miyom shecharav beit hamikdash vegalinu meartzenu nittla eitza mimmenu ve-ein anu yodim ladun dinei nefashos* — from the day the *Beis Hamikdash* was destroyed and we were exiled from our land, wisdom was taken from us and we do not know how to adjudicate capital cases. The loss of the Temple has rendered them incapable of judgment. One might hear in this evasion also a subtle hint: if you wish to restore our capacity for wisdom and judgment, the path is the restoration of the *Beis Hamikdash* itself.

### Amon Moav Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִיל לְגַבֵּי עַמּוֹן וּמוֹאָב דְּיָתְבִי בְּדוּכְתַּיְיהוּ כְּחַמְרָא דְּיָתֵיב עַל דּוּרְדְּיֵיהּ
>
> **Translation:** **Go to** the people of **Ammon and Moab, who have remained** permanently **settled in their places like wine that is settled on its lees.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ דִּכְתִיב שַׁאֲנַן מוֹאָב מִנְּעוּרָיו וְשׁוֹקֵט הוּא אֶל שְׁמָרָיו וְלֹא הוּרַק מִכְּלִי אֶל כֶּלִי וּבַגּוֹלָה לֹא הָלָךְ עַל כֵּן עָמַד טַעְמוֹ בּוֹ וְרֵיחוֹ לֹא נָמָר מִיָּד וַיִּקְצֹף הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיִּשְׁלַח מַלְאָכִים
>
> **Translation:** **And** they provided a good **reason** when **they spoke to him,** as **for it is written: "Moab has been at ease from his youth, and he has settled on his lees, and has not been emptied from vessel to vessel, neither has he gone into exile; therefore his taste has remained in him, and his scent is not changed."** The king accepted their advice, as it is **written: "Then the king was angry, and sent** messengers."**

Having demurred, the sages directed Achashverosh to other advisors: *zil legabei amon umoav deyasvi beduchtayeihu* — go to Amon and Moav, who have remained in their places, who have never been exiled. The metaphor is that of wine resting undisturbed on its lees: *kachamra deyasiv al durdeiyeihu* — like wine that has never been poured from vessel to vessel. The verse in the *Megillah* that says they gave the king *taam* — reason or taste — may then carry a double meaning: they provided him a rationale, and that rationale itself had *taam*, substance and flavor. A verse about *Moav* in *Yirmiyahu* captures the metaphor: *sha'anan Moav mine'urav veshokeit hu al shemarav... velo hurak mikli el kli uvagola lo halach* — Moav has been tranquil from its youth, undisturbed upon its lees, not poured from vessel to vessel, not sent into exile; therefore its taste has remained and its scent has not faded. Nations that have never been exiled, never been shaken and displaced, retain a kind of settled wisdom — or at least a willingness to render firm opinions — that the exiled sages have lost.

## Seven Counselors

### Name Allusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיִּקְצֹף הַמֶּלֶךְ מְאֹד וַיִּשְׁלַח מַלְאָכִים וַיִּשְׁמַע בְּשׁוּשַׁן וַשְׁתִּי נִשְׁמַרָה וַיִּקְרָא הַמֶּלֶךְ לַחֲכָמִים יוֹדְעֵי הַדָּת וְהַמֶּלֶךְ קָרָא לַחֲכָמִים קָרוֹב לוֹ כַּרְשְׁנָא שֵׁתָר אַדְמָתָא תַרְשִׁישׁ מֶרֶס מַרְסְנָא מְמוּכָן שִׁבְעַת שָׂרֵי פָרַס וּמָדַי רוֹאֵי פְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ הַיֹּשְׁבִים בִּראשׁוֹנָה בַּמַּלְכוּת
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues with Ahasuerus's search for counsel: "Then the king was very angry... Then the king said to the wise men, who knew the law... 'Bring Vashti the queen before the king... And the matter pleased the king and the princes: **and the king did according to the word of Memucan. For he sent letters into all the king's provinces, into every province according to the writing thereof, and to every people after their language, that every man should bear rule in his own house, and that it should be published according to the language of every people. And the king's scribes and officers were called in on the first month, which is the month Nisan, in the third year of his reign. And the king made a great feast... it is therefore written that he gathered together the best virgins unto Shushan the castle, to the custody of Hegai, keeper of the women.**"

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי כׇּל פָּסוּק זֶה עַל שׁוּם קׇרְבָּנוֹת נֶאֱמַר
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi said: This entire verse** listing the names of the king's advisors **is stated on account of offerings.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּרְשְׁנָא אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם כְּלוּם הִקְרִיבוּ לְפָנֶיךָ כָּרִים בְּנֵי שָׁנָה כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְפָנֶיךָ שֵׁתָר כְּלוּם הִקְרִיבוּ לְפָנֶיךָ שְׁתֵּי תוֹרִין אַדְמָתָא כְּלוּם בָּנוּ לְפָנֶיךָ מִזְבֵּחַ אֲדָמָה תַרְשִׁישׁ כְּלוּם שִׁימְּשׁוּ לְפָנֶיךָ בְּבִגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ תַּרְשִׁישׁ וְשֹׁהַם וְיָשְׁפֵה מֶרֶס כְּלוּם מֵירְסוּ בְּדָם לְפָנֶיךָ מַרְסְנָא כְּלוּם מֵירְסוּ בִּמְנָחוֹת לְפָנֶיךָ מְמוּכָן כְּלוּם הֵכִינוּ שֻׁלְחָן לְפָנֶיךָ
>
> **Translation:** **"Carshena"; the ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, did** the gentiles **ever offer before You lambs [*karim*] of the first year [*shana*], as the Jewish people have offered before You? "Shethar"; have they ever offered before You two turtledoves [*shetei torim*]? "Admatha"; have they ever built before You an altar of earth [*adama*]? "Tarshish"; have they ever ministered before You in the priestly vestments, as it is written** that on the fourth of the four rows of precious stones contained on the breastplate were: **"A beryl [*tarshish*], an onyx, and a jasper."** **"Meres"; have they ever stirred [*meirsu*] the blood of the offerings before You? "Marsena"; have they ever stirred [*meirsu*] the meal-offering before You? "Memucan"; have they ever prepared [*hekhinu*]** the **table before You,** on which the shewbread was placed?

The text then lists the seven counselors Achashverosh consulted: *Karshena, Shetar, Admasa, Tarshish, Meres, Marsena, Memuchan*. Rabbi Levi teaches: *kol pasuk zeh al shum korbanos ne'emar* — this entire verse alludes to the *korbanos*. The word *hakarov* — close, referring to those near the king — echoes *korban*. Each name is then read as an allusion to a specific Temple service.

### Heavenly Advocacy

In the Gemara's reading, each name becomes the vehicle for an angelic argument before God on behalf of the Jewish people. The heavenly advocates ask: *klum hikrivu lifanecha karim bnei shana* — have these nations ever offered to You yearling lambs, as Israel did? This is *Karshena*, from *kar*, a lamb. *Shetar* evokes *shtei torim* — have they ever offered two turtle-doves? *Admasa* evokes *mizbe'ach adama* — have they ever built an earthen altar? *Tarshish* evokes the *tarshish* stone on the *Kohen Gadol*'s breastplate — have they ever served You in *bigdei kehunah*? *Meres* evokes *mersu bedam* — have they ever stirred the blood of an offering before You? *Marsena* evokes *mersu bemenachos* — have they ever stirred the meal offering, mixing the flour and oil? And *Memuchan* evokes *hechinu shulchan* — have they ever set a table before You? In each case the implied answer is no — only Israel has performed these acts of service — and the angels' argument is that God must therefore intervene on Israel's behalf. Once again the theme emerges: the entire drama of the court of Achashverosh is situated within the story of the *Beis Hamikdash*, its destruction, its service, and the anticipation of its restoration.

### Memuchan as Haman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיֹּאמֶר מְמוּכָן תָּנָא מְמוּכָן זֶה הָמָן וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מְמוּכָן שֶׁמּוּכָן לְפוּרְעָנוּת אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא מִכָּאן שֶׁהַהֶדְיוֹט קוֹפֵץ בָּרֹאשׁ
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And Memucan said."** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: **Memucan is Haman. And why is** Haman **referred to as Memucan? Because he was prepared [*mukhan*] to** bring **calamity** upon the Jewish people. **Rav Kahana said: From here** we see **that the common man jumps to the front** and speaks first.

A *Tanna* teaches: *Memuchan zeh Haman* — Memuchan is Haman. The reason for the name: *shemuchan lepuranus* — he was prepared, setting himself up for punishment without realizing it. Rav Kahana adds: *mikan shehedyot kofetz berosh* — from here we learn that a minor person jumps to the head. Memuchan is listed last among the seven counselors, yet he is the first to speak. The minor official, the *hedyot*, pushes himself to the front.

### First Letter's Folly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִהְיוֹת כׇּל אִישׁ שׂוֹרֵר בְּבֵיתוֹ אָמַר רָבָא אִלְמָלֵא אִגְּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מִשּׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שָׂרִיד וּפָלִיט
>
> **Translation:** The king sent out letters to the people of all his provinces, in which it was written: **"That every man shall wield authority in his own house** and speak according to the language of his people." **Rava said: Were it not for the first letters** sent by Ahasuerus, which everybody discounted, **there would not have been left among the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people themselves, **a remnant or a refugee.**

Rava then identifies the first letter Haman-as-Memuchan caused to be sent as, paradoxically, the very thing that saved the Jews. *Ilmalei igros harishonos, lo nishtayer mishoneihem shel Yisrael* — were it not for the first letters, not a remnant of the Jews would have survived. Because once the permission to rise against the Jews was issued in the second letter, the populations would not have waited for the designated date of the 13th of *Adar*; they would have acted immediately. What held them back was that the first letter — the decree about household authority — had so thoroughly undermined Achashverosh's credibility that the second letter was not taken seriously either.

### Master of House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי מַאי הַאי דְּשַׁדַּיר לַן לִהְיוֹת כׇּל אִישׁ שׂוֹרֵר בְּבֵיתוֹ פְּשִׁיטָא אֲפִילּוּ קָרָחָה בְּבֵיתֵיהּ פַּרְדַּשְׁכָּא לֶיהֱוֵי
>
> **Translation:** The reason that the first letters were not taken seriously is that **they** who received them would **say: What is this that he has sent us: "That every man shall wield authority in his own house"?** This is **obvious; even** a lowly **weaver is commander in his house.**

Why was the first letter so foolish? When people received it and read that henceforth every man should be master in his own home, the reaction was: *mai hai deshadr lan* — what is he sending us? *Lihyos kol ish sorer beveiso* — that every man should rule in his own house? *Pshita afilu karda dezuzi pardashka lehvei* — this is self-evident; even a lowly person is the governor in his own domain. The decree was so banal, so obviously stating what required no legislation, that the king appeared foolish. Having once appeared foolish, his subsequent decree against the Jews was not met with the alarm and urgency it might otherwise have commanded, giving the Jewish people the time and space within which the reversal ultimately occurred.

### Tosafot: Daniel Theory

*Tosafot* closes the discussion of Memuchan with an alternative reading drawn from a *midrash*: perhaps Memuchan was not Haman but Daniel. According to this view, Daniel was married to a noblewoman of higher status than himself — *shelo haya yachol lchofefa ledaber kilshono*, he could not get her to speak his language — and so out of personal frustration he counseled Achashverosh to issue the decree that all wives must speak their husbands' language and submit to their husbands' authority. All politics, the *midrash* implies, is personal: behind the grand edicts of empire lie the domestic irritations of individual men.
