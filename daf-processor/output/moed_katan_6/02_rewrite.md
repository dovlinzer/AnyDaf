# Moed Katan 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Graves & Safekot

### Safekot Overview

The daf opens in the middle of 6b, picking up a discussion of the obligation to mark graves and related *halachot* on *chol hamoed*. The Gemara introduces the category of *safekot* — doubtful situations that require marking — on the principle that things that are obvious and visible to everyone need not be marked. Only cases of genuine uncertainty require a marker.

### Skhochos & Praot

The Gemara enumerates two such doubtful categories. *Skhochos* are trees that overhang and spread out in such a way that one might assume a body was buried beneath them, but where uncertainty remains: Is there actually a body there? Beneath which branch precisely does the *tumah* lie? Similarly, *praot* are rocks that protrude outward from a wall — *avanim pru'ot hayotzot min hageder* — which can communicate *tumah*, but only if something is buried in their vicinity, and it is not always clear under which space that might be.

### Beit HaPras

The Gemara then turns to *beit hapras*. As the Mishnah elsewhere states — *hachoresh et hakever harei hu oseh beit hapras* — if someone plows over a grave, the entire surrounding area becomes a *beit hapras*, because small fragments of bone may have scattered throughout the field. Since there is no certainty where any particular piece lies, the concern is rabbinic, but rabbinically the entire field is off-limits, and one who enters becomes *tamei*.

How large is a *beit hapras*? The Gemara teaches *melo ma'anah mei'ah amah* — the full length of a plow's path, one hundred *amot* (roughly 150 feet), by the width of the plow itself.

### HaPras Ohel

The Gemara raises a sharp question: the general rule is that marking is required only when *tumah* is communicated *b'ohel* — when one passes over or stands beneath something that constitutes a tent-like covering over the *tumah*. This is because *tumah* that sits openly on the ground is visible and people will avoid it themselves. But *beit hapras* involves only scattered bones lying on the surface — bones too small to see easily but also too small to generate *tumah b'ohel*. So why does *beit hapras* require a marker at all?

### Blown Path

The Gemara cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *menafei'ach adam beit hapras v'holeich* — a person may blow the ground directly in front of him and proceed step by step, clearing the surface of any scattered fragments before walking over them. This confirms that the concern of *beit hapras* is entirely about what lies on the surface, not what is buried underground, and hence not a concern of *tumah b'ohel* in the classical sense.

### Trampled Path

Rav Yehuda bar Ammi in the name of Ulla adds: *beit hapras shenidash tahor* — a *beit hapras* that has been trampled by foot traffic is considered *tahor*, because the fragments would have been scattered or pressed underfoot by repeated passage. This again confirms that the concern is entirely surface-level.

One might still ask why the Gemara demands a marker at all, given that the principle is to mark only *tumah b'ohel*. The most natural answer is that unlike ordinary large objects — which one can see and avoid — tiny bone fragments are not visible, and one cannot assume they will be noticed. However, the Gemara had already established elsewhere that even fragments as small as a barley grain (*se'orah*) need not be marked unless they generate *tumah b'ohel*, which complicates this explanation. The question, then, of why *beit hapras* requires marking at all is not fully resolved in the sugya.

Rav Papa ultimately resolves the difficulty by distinguishing between two types of *beit hapras*: one in which a known grave was plowed over (*sadeh shenechrash bah kever*), where the concern is surface-level scattered bone, and one in which a grave was lost in the field (*sadeh she'avad bah kever*), where the concern is an intact buried grave — a genuine case of *tumah b'ohel*. It is only the latter type that requires marking.

## Three HaPras Types

### Mishna Types

With this distinction in hand, the Gemara introduces a Mishnah listing three categories that bear the designation *beit hapras*: *sadeh shene'evad bah kever* — a field in which a grave has been lost; *sadeh shenechrash bah kever* — a field in which a grave has been plowed over; and *sdei vochin* — a field of crying. The Gra notes that the original reading of the third category may have been *kuchin*, referring to the burial niches (*kuchim*) used in the rock-cut tombs of *Eretz Yisrael*, but our standard text reads *sdei vochin*.

### Field of Crying

What is *sdei vochin*, a field of crying? Rav Yehoshua bar Abba in the name of Ulla explains: *sadeh she'maftirin bah meisim* — it is the field in which the dead are taken leave of. As Rabbeinu Gershom explains, when a body was being transported from one city to another for burial, at a certain field the delegation from the city of origin would hand the deceased over to those who would carry it forward to its burial place. Everyone would stop, final *hespedim* would be delivered, and the mourners would weep — hence *sdei vochin*, the field of crying. The field was designated for this handoff, and the concern is that something may have been lost there.

### Yei'ush Ba'alim

Why should this field be considered a *beit hapras*? The Gemara asks *v'ta'ama mai*, and Avimi answers: *mishum yei'ush ba'alim nag'u bah* — because of the owners' despair. Perhaps during the handoff a downpour forced everyone to take shelter, and in the confusion the body — or a piece of it — was abandoned and forgotten. Rabbeinu Gershom prefers a less dramatic reading: perhaps a small fragment of the *meis* fell off and was not retrieved. One participant suggested a plausible scenario: in the ancient practice of reinterment — where a body was buried for a year and then the bones collected and placed in an ossuary — it is possible that during the transfer some bone fragment fell and went unnoticed. This is admittedly speculative, but it captures the spirit of the concern.

## Marked Fields

### Ambiguous Marking

The Gemara now addresses a practical problem. You find a field with a marker (*sadeh metzuyenet*) but you do not know what type of *beit hapras* it is. This matters practically: if it is the type where a grave was plowed over, you may blow a path for yourself and walk through; if it is the type where a grave is lost, the concern is a buried *tumah* generating *tumah b'ohel*, and no such workaround is available. The marker alone does not tell you which type you are dealing with.

### Trees & Type

The Gemara cites a *braita*: *yesh bah ilanos, biyadu'a shenechrash bah kever* — if the field has trees, you know it is the plowed-grave type; *ein bah ilanos, biyadu'a she'avad bah kever* — if there are no trees, you know it is the lost-grave type.

Why should the presence of trees matter? Rabbeinu Gershom's explanation is that trees require plowing to plant, and so a field with trees must have been plowed, generating the concern of *nechrash*. But this is somewhat loose reasoning, since plowing for planting does not necessarily mean the grave itself was plowed over.

Tosafot offers a sharper answer, grounding it in a *Tosefta*: a field in which a grave is lost may not be planted at all — *einah nita'at kol neta*. This prohibition may exist either to mark the loss of the grave or to prevent further plowing that might make the grave even harder to locate. Given this rule, the inference becomes precise: if trees are present, the field must have been planted while the grave was still known and locatable, and only later was the grave accidentally plowed over, transforming it into the *nechrash* type. If no trees are present, the field is following the *halacha* — the grave is lost and the prohibition against planting is being observed.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a qualification: *ad sheyeishem zaken o talmid* — you cannot rely on this inference without an elder or a rabbinic student present, because not everyone is expert enough to draw the right conclusion from the evidence, and not every situation fits neatly into this framework.

The Gemara then records a striking statement of Abaye: *shma mina, tzurva merabanan d'isa b'masa, kol mili d'masa alei rami* — we learn from here that if there is a student of the rabbis in a city, all the dealings of the city are his responsibility. The inference is gently ironic: it is precisely the expertise of the Torah scholar that makes him the one who must pay attention to everything happening around him.

### Transformation

Rav Papa resolves a deeper tension in the sugya. If only the lost-grave type of *beit hapras* requires a marker, how can the *braita* assume you might encounter a marked field that turns out to be the plowed-grave type? The answer: the marking was originally placed because the grave was lost. Subsequently, the field was plowed over. That act of plowing transformed a field of type A — lost grave — into type B — plowed grave. Hence it is possible to encounter a marked field of the plowed-grave type: it was marked when it was still of the lost-grave type, and the marker remained after the transformation.

This answer raises its own difficulty. The plowed-grave type is in principle more lenient — the concern is only surface-level, and you can blow a path through. But if the field started as a lost grave, how can we assume the plowing fully resolved the original, more serious concern? Does one know that the buried grave was entirely fragmented and surfaced by the plowing? The Gemara does not fully unpack this, and it remains somewhat difficult.

### Interior/Exterior

The Gemara presses the evidentiary value of trees further. If there are no trees and we conclude the grave is lost, one might object: perhaps the trees are on the interior of the field and the grave is on the exterior, beyond the trees — so the area of trees would be unaffected by the prohibition against planting in a lost-grave field. The Gemara answers by invoking a principle stated by Ulla in another context: *b'omdin al hagvulin* — we are speaking of trees situated right at the border, specifically the border with a public domain (*reshut harabbim*). Since no one buries in a public domain, any grave must lie within the field, inside the tree line, and the trees' presence therefore speaks directly to the nature of the field's concern.

### Interspersed Trees

The Gemara then raises the converse problem: even if there are trees, perhaps the *tumah* is in an interior section of the field that has no trees, and the trees surround it without covering it — leaving a hidden patch where a lost grave could lie undetected. The Gemara answers: *b'mesubachin* — the trees are interspersed throughout the entire field, spread uniformly, leaving no section without trees. There is therefore no place in the field where a lost grave could exist without violating the prohibition on planting.

An alternative resolution is added: *ein marchikin tziyun mimkom tumah shelo l'hafsid Eretz Yisrael* — the marker is not distanced from the place of *tumah* so as not to cause unnecessary loss of land in *Eretz Yisrael*. The marker is placed right at the boundary of the *tumah*, not far from it. Therefore, if the markers are at the edges of the field, we may assume the *tumah* reaches exactly to the edge — there is no reason to suspect a pocket of *tumah* somewhere deeper inside that the marker does not reflect.

## Markers & Stones

### One Stone

The Gemara now discusses the specifics of markers themselves. Rav Yehuda teaches: if you find a single marked stone — one bearing whitewash — *tachteha tamei*, directly beneath it is *tamei*. The marker does not demarcate a broad surrounding area; it marks only the space directly under the stone itself. Rabbeinu Gershom explains why this does not conflict with the principle that a marker should be placed before the *tumah* rather than on top of it: because the stone is three-dimensional and elevated, it is visible from a distance, giving adequate warning before one steps directly over it.

### Two Stones

What if there are two marked stones with a gap between them? *Im yesh sid benehem benehem tamei* — if there is plaster between them, the space between them is *tamei* as well. *V'im ein sid benehem benehem tahor* — if there is no plaster, only the two stones themselves are *tamei*, not the space between them. The plaster functions as an explicit marking of the intervening area, extending the *tumah* designation into that space.

### Plaster Spillage

A contradiction emerges from a *braita*: it states that if the area between two marked stones is *plowed*, the space between them is *tahor*, and if not plowed, it is *tamei* — making no mention of plaster. How do we reconcile the two sources?

Rav Papa resolves this: the case in the *braita* involves plaster that spills over from the top of the stone and drips down its side onto the adjacent ground — an ambiguous situation. You cannot immediately tell whether the plaster between the stones was placed there intentionally to mark the intervening space, or whether it is merely a spill from the marking of the stone itself. In this ambiguous case, you look to whether the ground between the stones has been plowed. If it was plowed — *de'eima mechamat choresh hu de'ichsu* — you reason that the plaster that appears to have reached into the intervening space was actually displaced there by the plow's action, not placed intentionally, and therefore the space between them is *tahor*. If it was not plowed, and the plaster has spread somewhat beyond the stone, you assume it was intentionally applied to mark the intervening area, and the space is *tamei*. Counterintuitive as this reasoning may seem at first, that is the Gemara's resolution — though the Tosafot approach the underlying situation somewhat differently, reflecting genuine uncertainty about the physical scenario being described.

### Field Boundaries

Rabbi Assi extends the analysis to field boundaries. If one boundary of a field is marked, only that boundary strip is *tamei*; the rest of the field remains *tahor*. Two marked boundaries: same result — only they themselves are *tamei*. Three marked boundaries: still only those strips themselves, not the interior. But if all four boundaries of the field are marked, the logic inverts: the four markers themselves are *tahor*, and the entire field between them is *tamei* — *vechol hasadeh kullah tme'ah*.

The rationale is the principle already cited: *ein marchikin tziyun mimkom tumah shelo l'hafsid Eretz Yisrael* — you do not distance the marker from the *tumah* in order not to cause needless loss of productive land. When all four borders are marked, it becomes clear that the markers are placed at the perimeter precisely to indicate that the *tumah* lies within — in which case the interior is *tamei* and the markers stand just outside it. With fewer than four, each marked stone is understood to be marking only its own immediate location, and no inference can be drawn about the interior.

The lecturer notes that this entire discussion leaves certain questions imperfectly resolved — among them, how a field that began as the more serious *avad bakever* type can fully transition to the more lenient *nechrash* type, with all the original concerns considered resolved. This tension remains in the background as the daf moves forward.

## Chol HaMoed Work

### Public Works

The Gemara now reaches the final section of the Mishnah listing activities permitted on *chol hamoed* for the public welfare. After grave-marking, the next item is *yotsin af al hakilayim* — messengers are sent out to inspect fields for *kilayim*, mixed species growing together. This activity falls under the broader heading of *tzorchei rabbim*, communal needs, alongside other activities mentioned in the same context: repairing roads, fixing public pathways, measuring *mikvaot* (which may have been damaged or muddied during the rainy season), marking graves that may have had their markers washed away, and attending to all communal needs generally.

### Kilayim Timing

The Gemara raises an obvious challenge: but these messengers were already sent out a month earlier, on the 15th of *Adar*, at which point one also makes the announcement about *shekalim* contributions. Why would you need to send messengers out again on *chol hamoed*?

The Gemara notes that this question does not arise for most items on the list: roads get damaged again, *mikvaot* can deteriorate again, and markers can wash away — it is entirely natural that these tasks must be repeated. But *kilayim* is different. Once you have uprooted all the mixed species growing in a field, the problem is solved. People are not planting new crops at this stage of the season. So why specifically repeat the *kilayim* inspection on *chol hamoed*?

The answer given is: *chad amar kan bevachir v'kan be'afil* — one opinion holds that there are early-sprouting and late-sprouting varieties. The 15th of *Adar* takes care of the early growth; *chol hamoed*, which falls approximately a month later, catches the late sprouters before they have spread. The other opinion holds: *kan bizra'im v'kan beyerakot* — the first inspection addresses grain crops, and the second addresses vegetables, which sprout later. Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds that the 15th of *Adar* is only a default date: *lo shanu ela she'ein nitzan nikar* — this timing applies only when sprouting is not yet evident; if crops are visibly sprouting earlier in a given year, the inspection goes out earlier accordingly.

### Cheap Labor

Even granting that a second *kilayim* inspection is warranted, why specifically on *chol hamoed*? Rav Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Yochanan offers a striking answer: *mishum sechar pe'ulah de'mozli gabei* — because labor is cheap on *chol hamoed*. Since everyone is off work — work being restricted on *chol hamoed* — laborers are available and willing to work for less. The communal authorities take advantage of this window to hire workers at reduced cost. That this work is itself permitted on *chol hamoed* is a separate matter — it qualifies either as a *tzorech mitzvah* or falls under the special allowances for work of significant communal need.

### Communal Funds

Rav Zvid (or according to some, Rav Mesharshaya) infers from this that the wages paid to these inspectors come from *trumas halishka* — the communal temple treasury. The logic is clean: if the payment came from fining the field-owners whose *kilayim* was found, why would anyone care about keeping the cost down? Let them pay whatever the going rate is — it is their penalty. The fact that the Gemara is deliberately seeking cheap labor must mean the funds are communal, drawn from the *shekalim* collected annually, and the community has an interest in conserving those funds. The broader question of how *trumas halishka* — a sanctified fund — may be used for non-sanctified communal purposes is connected to the principle that the *beit din* may stipulate the conditions under which such funds are deployed, as discussed at length when studying *Masechet Shekalim*.

### Kilayim Penalties

The Gemara then cites a tradition about the proportion of foreign seed in a mixture that triggers the *kilayim* concern. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak states: *kol se'ah sheyesh bah rova* — any *se'ah* of grain containing a *rova* of a *kav* of a different seed species (a ratio of approximately one to twenty-four, just over four percent) triggers the obligation to reduce the mixture before planting. Below that threshold, one need not discard the entire batch; one simply dilutes the proportion.

A *braita* records the historical escalation of enforcement measures. Initially, the inspectors would uproot the *kilayim* and throw it in front of the field-owners' animals. The owners were delighted: their fields were weeded for free and their livestock were fed. The inspectors then changed tactics and threw the uprooted material on public roads instead, removing the benefit to the owners — but the owners remained pleased, as their fields were still weeded at no cost. Finally the authorities enacted a definitive penalty: *hiskinu she'yihyu mafkirin kol hasadeh kullah* — the entire field would be confiscated. Only this measure created a real deterrent and eliminated the perverse incentive structure that had made the earlier approaches counterproductive.

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between the *braita* describing outright confiscation and the earlier tradition of simple reduction (*mema'et*): these reflect two different eras — before the enactment of confiscation and after it.

## Watering Debate

### R. Elazar Loss

The Mishnah now turns to the next topic: watering fields on *chol hamoed*. Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov rules: *moshkin et hamayim me'ilan le'ilan* — you may channel water from tree to tree; however, *u'vilvad she'lo yashkeh et kol hasadeh* — you may not water the entire field. The assumption is that the non-tree portion of the field is a *beit haba'al*, a rain-watered field that does not require irrigation. Watering it would not prevent a loss and is therefore impermissible on *chol hamoed*.

### Unwatered Seeds

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov adds: *zra'im she'lo shatu lifnei hamoed lo yashkeim bamoed* — seeds that were not watered before the *moed* may not be watered on the *moed*. The reasoning is counterintuitive: one might assume that seeds that haven't been watered are in greater need of water. But the inference runs the other way: if they haven't been watered until now, we assume they are not in regular need of irrigation. Watering them on *chol hamoed* would not be preventing a loss but creating an additional benefit, and that is not sufficient justification. Only crops that are regularly irrigated — and whose watering schedule the *moed* happens to fall within — may continue to be watered.

### Chachamim Lenient

The *Chachamim* disagree and permit both cases. This is a surprising leniency: either they read the facts differently and consider these situations to involve actual potential loss (*hefsed*), or — more likely — they adopt the position associated with Rabbi Meir, which appears elsewhere in the daf, that one is permitted to perform agricultural work on *chol hamoed* even to prevent a loss of potential profit (*revach*), not only to prevent an actual loss. The *chachamim* in this Mishnah appear to represent that broader view.

### Dried Field

Rav Yehuda rules: *haita sadeh metunenet, mutar* — if it is a field that was naturally moist but has since dried out, watering is permitted. This is because the drying constitutes a genuine *hefsed*, and even if the field was ordinarily a *beit haba'al* that did not require irrigation, its present condition places it clearly in the category of potential loss. A *braita* confirms this, adding the explicit formulation: the prohibition against watering on *chol hamoed* applies only to seeds that were not watered before the *moed* — i.e., those not in regular need of water — but seeds that were regularly watered before the *moed* may continue to be watered on it. A field that has dried out, even if ordinarily self-sufficient, is also permitted.

Conversely, *ein mashkin sadeh gerid bamoed* — you may not water a chronically dry field on *chol hamoed*. Either watering such a field does no lasting good, or the field is simply accustomed to surviving on minimal moisture, and watering it would be an augmentation of profit rather than prevention of loss. Here too the *Chachamim* permit both this and the previous case — again reflecting the more permissive view that profit-prevention is sufficient justification.

### Garden Watering

Ravina draws an inference from the *Chachamim*'s ruling: *shma mina*, we can learn from this that *hai tarbitza, shari l'tarbutzei be'cholo shel mo'ada* — it is permissible to water a vegetable garden on *chol hamoed*. His reasoning: what justification do the *Chachamim* offer for permitting the chronically dry field (*sadi gerid*)? *De'afala mashvei lei charfa* — by watering it, you cause late-growing produce to become early-growing, advancing the harvest. That is a profit-motivation, not a loss-prevention. And yet the *Chachamim* allow it. The same reasoning applies to watering a garden: even if the garden will eventually produce without intervention, watering it now accelerates growth, and that acceleration — *afala mashvei lei charfa* — is sufficient justification under the *Chachamim*'s view.

This reading represents a significant *heter* on *chol hamoed*, effectively ruling like Rabbi Meir that *revach* alone justifies agricultural work. The *Rishonim*, however, are deeply divided on how to read this passage. Some *Rishonim* read Ravina's statement as a *psak* in favor of the *Chachamim*. Others read the Gemara's continuation as a challenge to Ravina's inference, with the question *sadi gerid mai ta'ama lo* — why isn't the dry field forbidden? — functioning as a refutation that drives the analysis back to the stricter position of Rabbi Yehuda, who requires actual *hefsed*. There are also significant *girsa* disputes in this passage. The question of whether the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Meir here is thus genuinely contested among the *Rishonim*.

### Shvi'it Water

The Gemara introduces a *braita* that further complicates the picture: *marbitzin sdei lavan b'shvi'it* — you may water a wheat field on *shvi'it*, *aval lo b'moed* — but not on *chol hamoed*. Strikingly, the Gemara permits this on *shvi'it* but not on *chol hamoed* — a counterintuitive ordering, since *shvi'it* is a biblical prohibition while the restrictions of *chol hamoed* are rabbinic. One explanation offered is that agricultural work prohibited on *shvi'it* refers specifically to the acts enumerated in the *pesukim* (plowing, harvesting, and the like), and watering is not among them, making any prohibition only rabbinic. Another source suggests *shvi'it* observance today is itself only rabbinic. In either case, light watering — *marbitzin* may refer to sprinkling rather than full irrigation — is permitted. Yet since this appears to be a case of profit rather than loss, its permissibility on *shvi'it* remains somewhat puzzling, and the Gemara does not fully explain the asymmetry.

A second *braita* then appears to contradict the first: *marbitzin bein b'moed bein b'shvi'it* — you may do it on both *chol hamoed* and *shvi'it*. Rav Huna resolves the contradiction: the first *braita* follows Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, who restricts *chol hamoed* activity to cases of genuine loss; the second follows the *Rabbanan* — i.e., the *Chachamim*, i.e., Rabbi Meir — who permit it even for profit. Both positions agree on *shvi'it*.

### Erev Shvi'it

A final *braita* adds nuance to the *shvi'it* discussion: *marbitzin sdei lavan erev shvi'it kedei sheyetze'u yerakot b'shvi'it* — you may water a wheat field on the eve of *shvi'it* specifically so that vegetables will grow during *shvi'it* itself. And beyond that — *ella she'marbitzin sdei lavan b'shvi'it kedei sheyetze'u yerakot l'motzei shvi'it* — you may even water during *shvi'it* itself if the purpose is to enable growth after *shvi'it* ends. This at least provides some rationale for the leniency: the watering during *shvi'it* is oriented toward post-*shvi'it* benefit, not toward producing *shvi'it* crops for consumption. The tension with the laws of *tosefet shvi'it* — the prohibition of certain agricultural activities in the months preceding *shvi'it* — remains in the background, mitigated perhaps by the light nature of *marbitzin* (sprinkling rather than full irrigation). The Gemara leaves these tensions partially unpacked.

## Pest Control

### Trapping

The Mishnah continues with the next item of permitted *chol hamoed* activity: *tzadin et ha'ishut v'et ha'achbarim mis'dei ha'ilan umis'dei halavan k'darko* — you may trap *ishut* (to be identified momentarily) and *achbarim* (rats) from an orchard or from a grain field in the normal manner, both on *chol hamoed* and on *shvi'it*. The justification is clear: this is a straightforward case of preventing *hefsed*, as these creatures actively destroy crops.

Tosafot raises the question of why *shvi'it* even needed to be mentioned — trapping animals is not agricultural work; it is simply protecting a field. He suggests that in order to set the traps, one may need to move earth or displace rocks, which could constitute prohibited activity on *shvi'it*. An analogous tension was encountered earlier in the daf concerning the digging of irrigation channels on *shvi'it*, where the Gemara questioned what the concern could be if one is merely moving water rather than working the soil. This parallel is worth noting, though the Gemara does not draw it explicitly here.

A second opinion in the Mishnah — attributed in some manuscripts to Rabbi Yehuda rather than to the *Chachamim*, restoring consistency with Rabbi Yehuda's role elsewhere as the stricter voice — permits normal trapping from an orchard (*k'darko*), since that is a more serious case of *hefsed*, but requires a *shinuy* (procedural modification) when trapping from a wheat field, because the concern there is somewhat less pressing. Even so, the trapping is not forbidden; the *shinuy* is a concession to the principle of minimizing non-essential work on *chol hamoed* when a full *hefsed* is not at stake.

The Mishnah also mentions *umakpizin et hapirtza b'moed* — you may seal a breach in a fence on *chol hamoed*, and on *shvi'it* you may even build a fence initially. The latter carries a modest *chidush*: one of the concerns on *shvi'it* is not merely the prohibition against working the soil, but the prohibition against acts that express ownership and dominion over one's land and crops. Building a fence could be read as such an assertion of ownership. Nevertheless, since the purpose is protection against pests and intruders — a genuine *hefsed* concern — it is permitted.

### Ishut Mole

The Gemara asks: *mai ishut* — what exactly is this creature? Rav Yehuda the son of Biri answers: *she'ein lah einayim* — it is a creature without eyes. This is understood to be a mole.

Rava bar Yishmael (or according to some, Rabbi Yeimar bar Shmaya) provides the scriptural basis: *k'mo shavlul temas yahalech* — like a snail (*shavlul*) that melts away as it moves; *nefel ashat bal chazu shamesh* — the *ashat* (the *ishut*) falls without having seen the sun. The *ishut* is thus a creature that has never seen sunlight — confirming the identification as a mole.

### Anthills

The Gemara adds another pest control measure: *umacharivin chorei nemalim* — you may also destroy ant tunnels. The *braita* asks: *keitzad macharivin* — how is this done in practice?

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel explains: *meivi afar mechur zeh v'noten letoch chur zeh, v'chonkin zeh et zeh* — you bring soil from one ant colony and introduce it into the tunnels of another, and they strangle one another. Rabbeinu Gershom understands this as the foreign smell of the introduced soil that disorients or suffocates the colony. One might also imagine that the introduction of alien soil brings rival ants into the colony, triggering intercolony aggression — but Rabbeinu Gershom's reading focuses on the smell.

Rav Yeimar bar Shalamia in the name of Abaye stipulates the conditions under which this technique works: *v'hu d'ka'i b'trei evrei nahara* — the two colonies must be on opposite sides of a river; *v'hu d'lika gishra* — and there must be no bridge; *v'hu d'lika gamla* — no elevated walkway; *v'hu d'lika metzra* — not even a single plank crossing. Only if the two colonies are genuinely isolated from one another — with no means of natural contact — will the introduction of foreign soil succeed in disrupting the colony. And even then, the colonies must be separated by at least a *parsa* in distance. Only under these conditions does the method work to destroy the anthills.
