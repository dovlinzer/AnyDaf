# Pesachim 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

### to'eh b'dvar mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק אַקִּילְעָא דְּרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק, וְיָתְבִי וְקָא אָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: נִתְחַלֵּף לוֹ שַׁפּוּד שֶׁל נוֹתָר בְּשַׁפּוּד שֶׁל צָלִי, וַאֲכָלוֹ — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak were sitting on the porch of Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak, and they sat and said: Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: If one** unwittingly **confused a spit of leftover** sacrificial meat, which it is prohibited to eat and which makes one liable to receive the punishment of *karet* when one eats it intentionally, **with a spit of roasted** sacrificial meat, which is a mitzva to eat, **and he ate it, he is liable** to bring a sin-offering. Although he intended to do a mitzva, since he unwittingly transgressed the prohibition against eating leftover sacrificial meat, he must bring a sin-offering.

We begin on 72b, in the midst of the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning one who is *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* — who errs while in the course of performing a *mitzvah*, particularly one that carries a license to override Shabbat, such as *brit milah* or the *Korban Pesach*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that under the right circumstances — an honest mistake, properly understood — the person is *patur*. The Gemara now picks up with a scene: Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak were sitting on the porch of Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak, and they were discussing the following statements.

### Notar & Niddah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה בָּעַל — חַיָּיב. יְבִמְתּוֹ נִדָּה בָּעַל — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** they further reported that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **one** unwittingly **engaged in sexual intercourse with his wife while she was menstruating, he is liable** to bring a sin-offering. But if **he** unwittingly **engaged in sexual intercourse with his sister-in-law who was waiting** to become his wife through **levirate marriage while she was menstruating, he is exempt,** because the act of intercourse itself is a mitzva. A man whose brother died without children is obliged by Torah law to marry his deceased brother's widow and "come to her" (Deuteronomy 25:5), so that even if he mistakenly transgressed while attempting to fulfill the mitzva, he is exempt from bringing a sin-offering.

Reish Lakish says: if the spits became confused — the spit bearing *notar*, leftover sacrificial meat whose willful consumption incurs *kares*, was mixed up with the spit bearing properly roasted *korban* meat that one is obligated to eat — and one ate from the wrong spit, one is *chayav*. This is not a case involving Shabbat; it is a case of attempting to perform a *mitzvah* and, through a factual error, committing an *aveira* that would ordinarily require a *korban* if done *b'shogeg*.

Rabbi Yochanan adds a related case: if a man attempts to have marital relations with his wife — a *mitzvah* — and it turns out she is a *niddah*, he is *chayav*. However, if a man performs *yibum* with his *yevama* — his widowed sister-in-law, where the act of *yibum* is itself a *mitzvah* — and she too turns out to be a *niddah* at that moment, he is *patur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בְּהָהִיא דְּמִחַיַּיב, דְּלֹא עָשָׂה מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to determine whether Rabbi Yoḥanan agrees or disagrees with Reish Lakish's ruling: **Some say** that **all the more so in the** first case, where one unwittingly ate a spit of leftover sacrificial meat, Rabbi Yoḥanan would **deem** him **liable** to bring a sin-offering, **for he did not** actually **perform a mitzva** when he ate the meat, even though he intended to do so. This stands in contrast to the second case, where the person unwittingly engaged in sexual intercourse with his wife while she was menstruating, where he at least performed a small mitzva, as will be explained shortly.

The Gemara then considers how these cases relate to one another. One version says: *kol shekein b'hi mechayev* — all the more so Rabbi Yochanan would hold the one who ate *notar* is *chayav*, since by eating *notar* there is no *mitzvah* element at all. When the husband had relations with his wife, at least he also fulfilled the *mitzvah* of marital intimacy, even though he transgressed the prohibition of *niddah*; there is a dual dimension to the act. But eating *notar* yields nothing but the *aveira*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִית דְּאָמְרִי, בְּהַהִיא — פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָתָם הוּא דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְשַׁיּוֹלֵי. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְשַׁיּוֹלֵי — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Others say** that according to Rabbi Yoḥanan **in that** case where one ate leftover sacrificial meat, **he is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering. **What is the reason? There,** where he had sexual intercourse with his wife while she was menstruating, he is liable, **for he should have asked** her if she was menstruating, and because he failed to do so he is liable to bring a sin-offering. **But here, where** he ate a spit of leftover sacrificial meat, **he did not have** anyone **to ask,** and so he is **not** liable to bring a sin-offering.

Another version inverts this: by the *notar* case, he is actually *patur*, because there he could not have asked the spit whether it was *notar*. The liability in the *niddah* case of his wife flows precisely from the fact that he *could* have asked her. This reading recognizes the personhood of the wife — she is someone he could and should have consulted. *Aval hacha*, where inquiry was impossible, there is greater grounds for exemption.

What remains somewhat open is exactly how these cases map onto Rabbi Yehoshua's framework. Presumably the discussion takes place within Rabbi Yehoshua's position, which does allow for exemption in certain cases. Yet here, liability is affirmed in most instances — either because the person could have asked, as in the wife case, or perhaps because, as in the *notar* case, the person did not ultimately perform a *mitzvah* at all. The Gemara does not fully unpack why the *notar* case yields liability vis-à-vis Rabbi Yehoshua; it can be surmised that accomplishing a *mitzvah* in the end is a relevant factor that has been somewhat underplayed here but is more explicit elsewhere.

### Yevama Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי שְׁנָא יְבִמְתּוֹ, דְּקָא עָבֵיד מִצְוָה? אִשְׁתּוֹ נָמֵי קָא עָבֵיד מִצְוָה! בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ מְעוּבֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is different** about one who unwittingly engaged in sexual intercourse with **his sister-in-law,** in that he is exempt from bringing a sin-offering? Is it **that he performed a mitzva,** i.e., the mitzva of levirate marriage? If so, then **also** in the case where he unwittingly engaged in sexual intercourse with **his wife** while she was menstruating, **he performed a mitzva,** for he occupied himself in the fulfillment of the mitzva of procreation. The Gemara answers that we are dealing here with a case **where his wife is pregnant,** such that intercourse cannot lead to procreation.

The Gemara then asks: Rabbi Yochanan, what is the difference? By the *yevama* he performed a *mitzvah* — but by his wife he was also attempting, and at least arguably accomplishing, a *mitzvah*. Marital relations is a *mitzvah* for husband and wife. So why is one *chayav* and the other *patur*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיכָּא שִׂמְחַת עוֹנָה! שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת עוֹנָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another question: Nevertheless, **there is** the mitzva of the **enjoyment of conjugal rights.** One of a husband's marital obligations is to engage in sexual intercourse with his wife at regular intervals (see Exodus 21:10), and this is considered a mitzva. The Gemara answers that we are talking about a case **where it is not the time of her conjugal rights.**

The Gemara's initial response is *me'uberet* — the wife is pregnant. Since pregnancy precludes *pru u'revu*, perhaps marital relations in that context carry no *mitzvah*. The Gemara immediately rejects this: *ve'ika simchat onatah* — the *mitzvah* of marital intimacy is not confined to procreative contexts. There is a *mitzvah* of giving one's wife pleasure, *simcha*, through regular conjugal relations, independent of whether children will result.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְשַׂמֵּחַ אִשְׁתּוֹ בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה! סָמוּךְ לְוִוסְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: Even so, **didn't Rava say** that **a man is obligated to please his wife through a mitzva?** That is to say, he must engage in sexual intercourse with her when she so desires, even if it is not the time of her conjugal rights. The Gemara answers that we are dealing with a case where it was **near her expected date** of menstruation, when sexual relations are prohibited due to a concern that the woman may already be menstruating or that she may begin to menstruate during the sexual act.

The Gemara then proposes: *she'lo bish'at onatah* — it was not the time of her scheduled *onah*. There is a regularity, discussed in Ketubot, based on the husband's occupation, and the *mitzvah* applies at those scheduled intervals. Outside of them, perhaps it is not a *mitzvah*. To this the Gemara raises Rava's statement: *chayav adam lesameach ishto bidvar mitzvah* — a man is obligated to give his wife pleasure even outside the fixed schedule, whenever it would be welcome and pleasurable to her. So there seems to be a *mitzvah* at virtually any time.

## Marital Mitzvah

### Pregnant Wife

This passage in the Gemara is striking in its own right, quite apart from the primary question of *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah*. The Gemara moves through a progression of views on when marital relations constitute a *mitzvah*: first, only when procreation is possible; then, at scheduled intervals; and finally, from Rava's statement, at essentially any time it would be pleasurable to the wife. This is a significant broadening of the concept.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava's ruling — that a man is obligated to give his wife pleasure, even outside the formal *onah* schedule, as a *mitzvah* — represents the Gemara's most expansive position on this question. The Gemara's apparent refusal to accept that marital relations are ever *not* a *mitzvah* (at least whenever they would be welcome) carries weight. Rashi, drawing on another Gemara, qualifies this somewhat: the *mitzvah* applies when she is signaling her interest. The Gemara here, however, may not have wanted to rely on that qualification — perhaps because it did not wish to assume that the absence of such signals implies unwillingness, which would approach coercion. A middle position is conceivable: she is not overtly initiating, but nor would she decline. The simple reading of this Gemara suggests that whenever relations would be welcomed or appreciated, there is a *mitzvah*. This Gemara thus stands as a significant source on the scope of *onah* as a *mitzvah*.

### Samuch Levistah

Given this, when would it not be a *mitzvah*? The Gemara answers: *samuch levistah* — when it is close to the time of her expected period. Relations are prohibited at that time, since she may become a *niddah* during the act itself. Whether the prohibition is *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan* is a debate among the *Rishonim*, but the point is that proximity to the expected onset renders relations not only non-obligatory but actively prohibited. Therefore, in that specific context, there is no *mitzvah* — and the husband is *chayav* because he was not performing a *mitzvah* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ יְבִמְתּוֹ נָמֵי! יְבִמְתּוֹ בְּזִיז מִינַּהּ, אִשְׁתּוֹ לָא בְּזִיז מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if they engaged in sexual intercourse near the woman's expected date of menstruation when he should have refrained from doing so, then **even** in the case where he had sexual intercourse with **his sister-in-law** and she turned out to be menstruating, he should **also** be liable, as the mitzva of levirate marriage does not apply at that time. The Gemara explains that with regard to **his sister-in-law, he is** still **shy [*bazeiz*]** in front **of her** and uncomfortable asking her whether she is close to her expected menstruation date, whereas with regard to **his wife, he is not shy** in front **of her,** and so he should have asked her.

The Gemara then asks: if so, the same should apply to the *yevama* — she too would presumably be *samuch levistah* at the relevant moment, so he was not performing a *mitzvah* with her either. Why then is he *patur* in that case?

### Embarrassment Factor

The Gemara answers: *yevamto baziz mina* — with his *yevama*, he is embarrassed. They have never been intimate before. He is not comfortable asking her whether she is approaching her period, even as they are about to have their first act of *yibum* together. *Ishto lo baziz mina* — with his wife, by contrast, he has no such embarrassment, and he should have asked.

The Gemara thus shifts from what initially appeared to be a substantive distinction — that *yibum* is a *mitzvah* while relations with a *niddah* wife are not — to a procedural one: the determinative factor is whether it was reasonable to expect him to inquire. In the case of the *yevama*, inquiry was not reasonably expected; in the case of his wife, it was. One might observe that the Gemara completely leaves aside the question of the woman's own liability — whether the *yevama* herself was *meizid*, *shogeg*, or *ones* — focusing entirely, as is its consistent practice, on the man.

The larger point emerging from all of this is that these cases — not Shabbat cases, but cases of attempting a *mitzvah* and committing an *aveira* through error — result in liability in most configurations, except where inquiry was genuinely impracticable. The Gemara has also not unpacked why Reish Lakish holds the *notar* case to be *chayav* within Rabbi Yehoshua's framework; one plausible answer is that there the person did not wind up performing a *mitzvah* in any meaningful sense, and that is what Rabbi Yehoshua requires for exemption. But the Gemara does not articulate this explicitly here.

## Rabbi Yehoshua Position

### Following Which Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כְּמַאן? אִילֵּימָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְשָׁכַח וְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַלּוּלָב לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוֹצִיא בִּרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan, in accordance with whose** opinion did he rule with regard to one who erred while engaged in a mitzva? **If you say** he ruled **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as we learned** in a mishna: **Rabbi Yosei says:** If **the first Festival day of the festival** of *Sukkot* **occurs on Shabbat, and he forgot and carried** his ***lulav* out into the public domain** and continued to transport it there, unwittingly performing a prohibited labor, **he is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering. That is **because he carried it out with permission,** that is to say, he was acting with the intention of fulfilling a mitzva.

The Gemara now asks: *Rabbi Yochanan k'man* — who exactly is Rabbi Yochanan following? He applies the principle of *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* to the *yevama* case, which lacks urgency. The Gemara considers whether he follows Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Yehoshua of the *Mishnah* regarding the *Korban Pesach*, Rabbi Yehoshua of the *brit milah* cases, or Rabbi Yehoshua of *trumah*. Each possibility is examined.

### Urgency Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּזְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל.
>
> **Translation:** There is room to differentiate between Rabbi Yosei's ruling and that of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Perhaps it is different there, as he is pressed for time.** Since by Torah law the mitzva of *lulav* applies only one day a year, he is anxious to fulfill the mitzva and therefore does not realize that he is violating a Torah prohibition. This is unlike the case of levirate marriage, which need not be performed at any specific time and about which he is less pressured.

If Rabbi Yochanan follows Rabbi Yosi — as we learn in a *Mishnah*: if the first day of *Sukkot* falls on Shabbat and one forgot and carried the *lulav* into the *reshut harabbim* while attempting to perform the *mitzvah*, he is *patur mipnei she'hotzi birshut*, because he carried with license, trying to fulfill a *mitzvah*. The Gemara objects: *dilma shani hatam zmano bahu* — perhaps that case is distinct because of the urgency. If you want to perform the *mitzvah* of *lulav* on the first day of Sukkot, you must do it today. That urgency — *zmano bahu* — provides grounds for exemption that do not apply to *yibum*, which can be performed today or next week.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דִּזְבָחִים. דִּילְמָא הָתָם נָמֵי זְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one might say that Rabbi Yoḥanan issued his ruling in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua** in our mishna **with regard to offerings,** for according to Rabbi Yehoshua, one who unwittingly slaughtered another offering on Shabbat for the purpose of a Paschal offering, and the animal was fit to be brought as a Paschal offering, is exempt from bringing a sin-offering. Here too, however, there is room to differentiate. **Perhaps there also** we can say that Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him because **he is pressed for time.**

The same objection applies to Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the *Mishnah* of our tractate: when one makes a mistake while bringing the *Korban Pesach*, there too there is urgency — *zmano bahu* — the *korban* must be brought today. And similarly for the *brit milah* cases discussed yesterday: the circumcision must be performed on its designated day. All of these cases of exemption share the feature of temporal pressure. Rabbi Yochanan's application of the principle to *yibum*, where no such urgency exists, therefore represents a *chidush*, an extension beyond what was previously established.

### Trumah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דְּתִינוֹקוֹת. הָתָם נָמֵי זְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one might say that Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua with regard to babies,** one that should have been circumcised on Shabbat eve and the other on Shabbat, and the circumciser forgot and circumcised the one that should have been circumcised on Shabbat eve on Shabbat. In that case the circumciser is exempt from bringing a sin-offering. But once again there is room to differentiate: Perhaps **there too,** Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him because **he is pressed for time.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דִּתְרוּמָה, דִּתְנַן: הָיָה אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we should say that Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua with regard to *teruma*, as we learned** in a mishna: If a priest **was eating *teruma*, and it became known that he was the son of a divorced woman or a woman who underwent *ḥalitza*** and he is therefore disqualified from the priesthood and may not eat *teruma*, **Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **liable** to pay **the** value of the **principal and** an additional **fifth,** like any non-priest who unwittingly ate *teruma*, whereas **Rabbi Yehoshua exempts** him. In this case, one who thought he was a priest and therefore fulfilling a mitzva when he ate the *teruma* was actually committing a transgression, but nevertheless, Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him from the ordinary penalty. On the face of it, this is similar to Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling.

The Gemara then considers Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the case of *trumah*: *detnan*, as we learned in a *Mishnah*, someone was eating *trumah* and in the middle of the meal was informed that he is, in fact, a *ben grushah* or *ben chalutzah* — the son of a *kohen* who married a divorcée or a woman released from *levirate* marriage — and therefore lacks the sanctity of a *kohen* and is a *challal*. Rabbi Eliezer holds him *chayav* to pay the *keren v'chomesh* — principal and an additional fifth — for having eaten *trumah* as a non-*kohen*. Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him.

Now, eating *trumah* carries no urgency tied to a particular day. There is no *zmano bahu* here — a *kohen* may eat *trumah* on any day. Yet Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him. This seems to be exactly the kind of precedent Rabbi Yochanan needs for extending the exemption to the non-urgent *yevama* case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָ כִּדְרַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: בִּתְרוּמָה בְּעֶרֶב הַפֶּסַח, דִּזְמַנָּהּ בָּהוּל.
>
> **Translation:** This comparison can, however, be rejected, for **perhaps** that case should be explained **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Beivai bar Abaye. For Rav Beivai bar Abaye said:** The mishna is talking about a disqualified priest who ate ***teruma*** which is leaven **on the eve of Passover, whose time is pressing,** because if he does not eat it quickly he will have to burn it.

The Gemara pushes back: *dilma k'Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye* — perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the *trumah* case was specifically where the *trumah* was eaten on *Erev Pesach*, and on that day there *was* urgency: the *trumah* bread was about to become *chametz* and forbidden, so it had to be consumed that day. On any other day, he would be *chayav*. The urgency factor persists even here.

### Trumah Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: שָׁאנֵי תְּרוּמָה דְּאִיקְּרִי עֲבוֹדָה, וַעֲבוֹדָה רַחֲמָנָא אַכְשַׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** there may be another reason to differentiate: Eating ***teruma* is different, because it is called** sacred **service, and the Merciful One validates service** performed by someone of priestly descent even if he is not fit for the priesthood.

Alternatively, the Gemara offers a distinct rationale for the *trumah* ruling that has nothing to do with urgency: *shani trumah d'ikrei avodah* — eating *trumah* is categorized as an act of *avodah*, divine service. *Avodah rachmana achsher* — and where a *kohen* performed *avodah* without knowing he was disqualified, the Torah itself validates that *avodah* retroactively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה — כׇּל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת כּוּלָּן שֶׁהִקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ פְּסוּלִין, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מַכְשִׁיר. וְאָמְרִינַן, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּרֵךְ ה׳ חֵילוֹ וּפֹעַל יָדָיו תִּרְצֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: If a priest **was standing and sacrificing offerings** on the altar, **and it became known that he was the son of a divorced woman or the son of a woman who underwent *ḥalitza*, all the offerings that he offered on the altar** until that time **are disqualified,** because only service performed by a priest deemed fit is acceptable. **And Rabbi Yehoshua validates** them. **And we said: What is the reason of Rabbi Yehoshua? As it is written** with regard to the tribe of Levi: **"Bless, Lord, his substance and accept the work of his hands"** (Deuteronomy 33:11), which teaches that after the fact, God accepts the work of a priest's hands, even if it becomes clear that he was actually disqualified at the time he performed the service.

This is demonstrated from a *Mishnah*: if a *kohen* was standing at the altar bringing a *korban* and it was then revealed that he is a *ben grushah* or *ben chalutzah*, Rabbi Yehoshua holds that all the *korbanot* he brought throughout his life as a *kohen* are *kasher*. The Scriptural basis, as derived by the Gemara, is the verse *barech Hashem cheilo u'fo'al yadav tirtzeh* — "bless, O Lord, his valor, and accept the work of his hands" (Devarim 33:11), read in conjunction with the preceding verse about the *kohen* bringing incense and *korbanot*. The *drashah* understands this as divine approbation of the *kohen*'s service performed in good faith, even if he was retroactively discovered to be disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְרוּמָה הֵיכָא אִיקְּרִי עֲבוֹדָה? דְּתַנְיָא, מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן שֶׁלֹּא בָּא אֶמֶשׁ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. לְשַׁחֲרִית מְצָאוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא בָּאתָ אֶמֶשׁ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ? אָמַר לוֹ: עֲבוֹדָה עָבַדְתִּי. אָמַר לוֹ: כׇּל דְּבָרֶיךָ אֵינָן אֶלָּא דִּבְרֵי תֵימָה, וְכִי עֲבוֹדָה בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה מִנַּיִן? אָמַר לוֹ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions what was stated above: **And where is *teruma* called service?** The Gemara explains: **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving Rabbi Tarfon, who did not come in the evening to the study hall. In the morning, Rabban Gamliel found him** and **said to him: For what** reason **did you not come in the evening to the study hall? He said to him: I was performing** sacred **service. He said to him: All your words are astonishing; from where** do we have **service in this time** after the destruction of the Temple? **He said to him: It says** in Scripture:

The source for treating *trumah* as *avodah* is established through a *Tosefta*-style teaching involving Rabbi Tarfon, who once told Rabban Gamliel that he had not come to the study hall the previous night because he had been performing *avodah* — priestly service. Rabban Gamliel was puzzled: *vechi avodah bizman hazeh minayin* — how is there temple service nowadays? Rabbi Tarfon replied by citing the verse *avodat matanah eten et kehunatchem*, in the context of *trumah*, which uses the language of service and declares that those who draw near improperly shall die. From this the *Gemara* derives a *hekesh*: eating *trumah* in the provinces is analogous to performing *avodah* in the Temple. Therefore, just as a *kohen* who served unknowingly while disqualified is not required to bring a *korban* — indeed, his *avodah* stands — so too a *kohen* who ate *trumah* while unknowingly disqualified is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֲבוֹדַת מַתָּנָה אֶתֵּן אֶת כְּהֻנַּתְכֶם וְהַזָּר הַקָּרֵב יוּמָת״, עָשׂוּ אֲכִילַת תְּרוּמָה בִּגְבוּלִין כַּעֲבוֹדַת בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **"I have given you the priesthood as a service of gift; and the stranger that comes near shall be put to death"** (Numbers 18:7). This verse is found in the context of the priestly gifts, including *teruma*, and comes to teach us that **they made the eating of *teruma* in the outlying areas,** i.e., outside the Temple, **like the service of the Temple.**

The Gemara's conclusion, then, is that Rabbi Yochanan's extension of the *patur* principle to the *yevama* — a non-urgent case — represents a genuine *chidush* relative to the established *Tannaitic* precedents. All the prior cases either involved urgency (*zmano bahu*) or a special status of *avodah* applicable only to *trumah*. Rabbi Yochanan has gone a step further.

## Productive Acts

### Tiken Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא לִבְרֵיהּ: כִּי אָזְלַתְּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי זְרִיקָא, בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר ״מְקַלְקֵל בְּחַבּוּרָה — פָּטוּר״, ״שְׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לְאוֹכְלָיו — חַיָּיב״? מָה תִּיקֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana said to his son: When you go before Rabbi Zerika, ask him: According to the opinion that says that one who inflicts a destructive wound is exempt,** i.e., that one who causes a wound on Shabbat that has no constructive effect but rather is purely destructive in nature has not performed a prohibited labor and is therefore exempt from bringing a sin-offering, how are we to understand the mishna's ruling that **one who slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **for those who cannot eat it is liable?** Since the slaughter is invalid, he should be seen as having wounded the animal in a way that brings no benefit and is simply destructive. **What has he improved** through the slaughter that he should be liable for having performed a prohibited labor?

The background principle: on Shabbat, *kol hamikalkelin peturin* — any act that is destructive rather than constructive carries no liability. The *chiyuv* for a *melachah* requires a productive act. This creates a difficulty for the cases in the *Mishnah*: if one slaughters a *Korban Pesach* incorrectly — *shelo lishmah*, for the wrong owners, or in other disqualifying circumstances — the result is a *pasul korban*, a piece of meat that must be burned. In what sense was the *shechitah* a productive, constructive act?

Rav Huna bar Chinena posed this question to his son to bring before Rav Zreika, working from the premise that *makalkel bechabura patur* — that wounding, like any other *melachah*, is only culpable when productive. If you slaughter an animal *shelo lishmah* on Shabbat, what did you *tiken* — what did you fix or improve?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיקֵּן אִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He has improved** it in that **if** the sacrificial parts of the offering **ascended** to the top of the altar, **they do not descend.** The *halakha* is that if the sacrificial parts of a disqualified offering are inadvertently brought up to the top of the altar, they need not be removed and they may be burned on the altar. Thus, the slaughter had some constructive effect.

The Gemara answers: *tiken im alu lo yerdu* — you produced at least this benefit: a *pasul korban*, even though invalid, if its fats were placed on the *mizbe'ach* would not be removed. The act of *shechitah*, even invalid, creates a minimal legal improvement in the animal's status. That small positive consequence is sufficient to categorize the act as constructive and impose liability.

### Treifa Beseter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטוֹ וְנִמְצָא בַּעַל מוּם חַיָּיב, מָה תִּיקֵּן? תִּיקֵּן בְּדוּקִּין שֶׁבָּעַיִן, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר אִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: We learned in the mishna that if one **slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **and it was found to have a blemish, he is liable** to bring a sin-offering. Here too, it may be asked: **What has he improved** through the slaughter, so that he should be liable? The Gemara answers: **He has improved** it if the blemish was small, e.g., if it was **on the** animal's **eyelid, and in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said** that in the case of an offering with such a small blemish, **if** its sacrificial parts **ascended** to the top of the altar, **they do not descend** from it, because it is not a disgrace to the altar for the sacrificial parts of such an offering to be burned on it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטוֹ וְנִמְצָא טְרֵיפָה בַּסֵּתֶר — פָּטוּר. הָא בְּגָלוּי — חַיָּיב. מָה תִּיקֵּן? תִּיקֵּן לְהוֹצִיא מִידֵי נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the next clause of our mishna that if one **slaughtered** the Paschal lamb **and it was found to have a hidden condition that would cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], he is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering. The mishna's wording indicates that **if** the animal's condition **is visible,** its owner **is liable.** It may be asked: **What has he improved** by slaughtering an animal with a such a condition? The Gemara answers: **He has improved** it in that he **removed it from** the category of **an animal carcass [*neveila*],** i.e., an animal that died of natural causes or as the result of an improperly carried out act of ritual slaughter. Had the animal died on its own it would have been treated as a *neveila*, which is a primary source of ritual impurity, rendering those who touch or carry it ritually impure. Proper slaughter of the animal prevents it from falling into that category and imparting ritual impurity.

The *Mishnah* then addresses the case where the animal was slaughtered and was subsequently found to be a *treifa* with an internal defect — *treifa beseter*, a hidden *treifa*. In that case, one is *patur*. The implication of the *Mishnah*, as the Gemara reads it, is that had the *treifa* been evident externally — *begaluy* — one would be *chayav*. The Gemara asks: *mai tiken*? What was improved? A *treifa* slaughtered as a *korban* would have to be removed from the altar.

The Gemara answers: *tiken lehotzi midei gezeila* — the *shechitah* accomplished something. Had the animal died without *shechitah*, it would have been a *neveilah* — ritually impure and possibly subject to the prohibition of *gezeila* (theft) if taken by others. By performing *shechitah*, even of a *treifa*, one removed it from the status of *neveilah*. That is a sufficient productive dimension to establish liability on Shabbat.

### Avodah Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: הָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט חַטָּאת בַּשַּׁבָּת בַּחוּץ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ שָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת, מָה תִּיקֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina strongly objects to this:** With regard to **that which was taught** elsewhere in a *baraita*, that **one who** unwittingly **slaughters a sin-offering on Shabbat outside** the Temple **for** the sake of **idolatry is liable to** bring **three sin-offerings for it:** For desecrating Shabbat, for slaughtering an offering outside the Temple, and for practicing idolatry; here too, the question may be raised: **What has he improved** by slaughtering the animal? Here we cannot answer that he removed it from the category of an animal carcass and prevented it from becoming a primary source of ritual impurity, because any animal that was used as an idolatrous offering imparts ritual impurity. Therefore, it would seem that the slaughter served no constructive purpose.

Rav Avina then raises a more challenging case from a *Braita*: one who slaughtered a *chatas* on Shabbat, outside the Temple, and for *avodah zarah* — three separate violations, each *chayav kares* if done willfully, each *chayav* a *chatas* if done *beshogeg*. The Gemara thus says one would bring three sin offerings. But Rav Avina's question is: *mai tiken*? What constructive act was performed?

The answer of *lehotzi midei neveilah* does not apply here. Something slaughtered for *avodah zarah* is treated as *tamei*, indeed bearing *tumah b'ohel* according to the Gemara in Shabbat — a severity beyond that of ordinary *neveilah*. The *shechitah* did not remove the animal from *tumah*; it replaced one severe status with another, arguably worse one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַוִּירָא: שֶׁמּוֹצִיאוֹ מִידֵי אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avira said:** Even here he has improved it in that **he removed it from** the category of **limbs from a living creature.** Even a gentile is liable if he eats meat taken from a living animal, but once the animal is slaughtered there is no longer any liability. Accordingly, even this act of slaughter has achieved a productive result.

The Gemara answers through Rav Avira: *shemotzi'o midei eiver min hachai* — the *shechitah* at least removed the prohibition of *eiver min hachai*, the limb of a living animal. Before *shechitah*, anyone consuming a limb of that animal — certainly a *ben Noach* — would have violated *eiver min hachai*. After *shechitah*, that specific prohibition no longer applies. Even if what remains is *neveilah*, *tamei*, and *tikroves avodah zarah*, it is no longer *eiver min hachai*. This minimal improvement suffices to render the act productive and impose *chiyuv*.

Notably, Rashi points out explicitly that this benefit is relevant to the *ben Noach*. This raises an interesting implication: a *ben Noach* is prohibited in *eiver min hachai*, yet apparently is not prohibited in *tikroves avodah zarah* — since if trading one for the other provided no benefit, the answer would fail. Although a *ben Noach* is certainly warned against *avodah zarah* itself, perhaps the status of *tikroves avodah zarah* — the offering of *avodah zarah* — does not carry the same prohibition for a *ben Noach*.

Tosafot also notes that the *tumah* status of *tikroves avodah zarah* that the Gemara here treats as *d'Oraita* appears elsewhere to be *d'Rabbanan*, and addresses that tension.

## Akirah Debate

### Asham to Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטוֹ וְנוֹדַע וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּיתַּק לִרְעִיָּה, וּשְׁחָטוֹ סְתָם — כָּשֵׁר לָעוֹלָה. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: לָא בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that if **one slaughtered** a Paschal lamb on Shabbat **and** afterward **it became known** that the owners had died, he is exempt from bringing a sin-offering, because he slaughtered with permission. **Rav Huna said that Rav said:** Regarding **a guilt-offering that was consigned to grazing:** If the owner of a guilt-offering dies or achieves atonement through a different guilt-offering, the animal is sent out to graze in the field until it develops a blemish, at which point it can be sold. The money from the sale is used to purchase a burnt-offering. **And** if, before it developed a blemish, someone **slaughtered it without specifying** its purpose, **it is valid as a burnt-offering.** The Gemara concludes that Rav **apparently holds that it does not require uprooting.** There is no need for an explicit declaration in order to change the status of the offering; even if it is slaughtered without its purpose specified it is valid.

Rav Huna reports in the name of Rav: if you have an *asham* — a guilt offering — that was set out to *reiyah* (pasture), because the owners had died and the *halacha* is to let it graze until it acquires a *mum*, at which point it is redeemed and the money used for a communal *olah* — and before acquiring a *mum* you slaughtered it *stam*, without specifying its purpose, it is *kasher l'olah*. It automatically counts as an *olah*, since that was its intended destination all along. The money, and therefore the identity, was already heading toward an *olah*.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי כִּי לֹא נִיתַּק נָמֵי! גְּזֵירָה לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אַטּוּ לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** even **when it has not** yet **been consigned** to grazing, it should **also** be valid, for any guilt-offering whose owner has achieved atonement through a different offering is presumably going to be brought as a free-will burnt-offering. The Gemara answers: This invalidation stems from a rabbinic **decree** with regard to a guilt-offering **after** its owner achieved **atonement** with a different offering **due to** concern about a guilt-offering **before** its owner achieved **atonement** with a different offering. Before the owner achieves atonement the animal is certainly considered a guilt-offering; it is only after the owner achieves atonement that the offering becomes valid for use as a burnt-offering, and then by strict *halakha* it is immediately valid for that purpose, even before the animal develops a blemish.

From this, the Gemara infers: *almah kasavar lo ba'ei akirah* — Rav holds that once a *korban* can no longer be used for its original purpose and is in the pipeline toward another use, it requires no active redefinition. It is automatically transformed.

The Gemara immediately challenges: if that is so — *ki lo nitak nami* — why confine this ruling to after the animal was put out to pasture? The moment the owners die, the only possible destination for this animal is an *olah*. The *halacha* of *lo ba'ei akirah* should apply even before it was put out to pasture. The Gemara answers: *gezeirah l'achar kaparah atu lifnei kaparah* — it is a rabbinic enactment. Biblically, the same would apply even before *reiyah*. But rabbinically, if we permit using the animal as an *olah* immediately — before the visible, public act of setting it out to pasture — people who see an *asham* become an *olah* will think that *korbanot* can simply be switched at will. The act of *reiyah* serves as a visible marker signaling that this is a special category case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא, דִּתְנַן: אָשָׁם שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלָיו אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלָיו — יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָמוּת. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: יִמָּכֵר וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do you say** that this is the case? **As we learned** elsewhere in a mishna: **A guilt-offering whose owner has died or whose owner has achieved atonement** through a different guilt-offering **grazes until it becomes unfit,** whereupon **it is sold and its money is used for a** communal **free-will burnt-offering. Rabbi Eliezer says:** This guilt-offering is made to **die** on its own. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** When it develops a blemish, **it is sold, and he brings a burnt-offering** for himself **with its money.**

The Gemara then attempts to prove this ruling from another *Mishnah*: an *asham* whose owners died or who were atoned for through another animal — the *Mishnah* says it grazes until it acquires a *mum*, is sold, and the money goes into the donation box for communal *olot*. Rabbi Eliezer holds it must die, treating it like a *chatas*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds it is sold and the money used for a personal *olah*. The Gemara notes: *bidamav in, aval gufo lo* — only the money may be used for an *olah*, not the animal itself directly. Why not use the animal directly? The Gemara applies the same rationale: *d'gazar l'achar kaparah atu lifnei kaparah*. Tosafot finds this proof somewhat circular — the explanation simply restates the same principle without independently demonstrating it — but the point is affirmed: Rav's position of *lo ba'ei akirah* is supported, with the *reiyah* requirement for the *asham* being only a rabbinic fence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדָמָיו אִין, אֲבָל גּוּפוֹ לָא. דְּגָזַר לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אַטּוּ לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This indicates that according to Rabbi Yehoshua, as soon as the owner achieves atonement, his animal loses its status as a guilt-offering and it becomes a burnt-offering. Nevertheless, even he says that the animal that is bought **with its money** may **indeed** be brought as a burnt-offering, **but** the guilt-offering **itself** must **not** be sacrificed as a burnt-offering. Undoubtedly, his reason must be that the Sages issued **a decree** with regard to a guilt-offering **after** its owner achieved **atonement** with a different offering, **due to** concern about a guilt-offering **before** its owner achieved **atonement** with a different offering. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is correct.

### Pesach Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב הוּנָא: שְׁחָטוֹ וְנוֹדַע שֶׁמָּשְׁכוּ בְּעָלִים אֶת יָדָם וְכוּ׳,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raised an objection to Rav Huna** with regard to his opinion about uprooting the status of an offering from what we learned in our mishna: If **one slaughtered** a Paschal lamb on Shabbat **and** afterward **it became known that the owners had withdrawn** from it and registered for a different one, or that they had died or become ritually impure, he is exempt from bringing a sin-offering, because he slaughtered with permission.

Rav Chisda challenges Rav Huna: the *Mishnah* says that if the owners withdrew from the *Pesach* and this was discovered after *shechitah*, the *Pesach* is *pasul*, and a *Braita* teaches it must be burned immediately. If Rav is correct that no active redefinition is needed — that the moment the owners withdrew, the *Pesach* automatically became a *shelamim* — then why is it *pasul* at all? It is a *kasher shelamim*. The worst one could say is that it was slaughtered after the *tamid shel bein ha-arbayim* — somewhat out of sequence — but that would not be an intrinsic *psul*. One would simply wait overnight and burn it. It would not be burned immediately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: בַּחוֹל כִּי הַאי גַוְנָא יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה, הַאי פֶּסַח הוּא, וְכֵיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ בְּעָלִים — הָוֵה לֵיהּ פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to this** mishna that **on a weekday, in a case like this,** where it turns out that there is no one to eat the Paschal offering, **it should be burned immediately. Granted, if you say** that an offering that has no owner **requires uprooting** from its previous status in order for its status to change and in the absence of explicit uprooting it retains its original status, here too it can be argued that **this is** still **a Paschal offering. And since it has no owners, its disqualification is in** the body of the offering **itself,** because it was sacrificed for no purpose, and **therefore it should be burned immediately.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה — מֵרֵישָׁא הָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁלָמִים, פְּסוּלוֹ מִשּׁוּם מַאי — מִשּׁוּם דָּבָר אַחֵר, דְּקָא שָׁחֵיט לֵיהּ אַחַר תָּמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם. עִיבּוּר צוּרָה בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that an offering such as this **does not require uprooting,** but rather its original status is automatically void upon the death of its owner, and **it was a peace-offering from the beginning,** as a Paschal offering whose status has been revoked is considered a peace-offering, **due to what** then **is its disqualification? Due to something else,** i.e., **that he slaughtered it after the daily afternoon offering,** which is the proper time to slaughter the Paschal lamb. But a peace-offering that is slaughtered then is disqualified. In that case, however, **it should require** that it be left overnight until **its form decays,** thus attaining the status of leftover sacrificial meat, and only then should it be burned.

The Gemara's initial response is to suggest reversing Rav's ruling: perhaps Rav's formulation should not be read as *shachato stam kasher l'olah* — slaughtered it without designation and it is automatically an *olah* — but rather *shachato l'shem olah kasher* — it is valid only if one actively slaughtered it for an *olah*. Under this reading, Rav holds *ba'ei akirah*, that active redefinition is required. That would explain the *Mishnah*: since the owners withdrew and no one actively redefined it as a *shelamim*, it remains a *Pesach pasul*.

## Akirah Resolution

### Rabbi Chiya Bar Gamda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ — יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד. בַּדָּם וּבַבְּעָלִים — תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה!
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **This is the principle: Any** offering **whose disqualification is in** the body of the offering **itself should be burned immediately** and without delay. But if the disqualification is **in the blood** of the offering or **in the owners,** the meat must be kept overnight, so that **its form** is allowed to **decay,** and only then **should it be taken out to the place of burning.** Thus, the *baraita* that says that a Paschal lamb that was slaughtered on a weekday, and afterward it became known that the owners had died, should be burned immediately proves, against the opinion of Rav Huna, that an offering that has no owners still requires explicit uprooting from its previous status for its status to change.

The Gemara now introduces Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda, who formulated a position that preserves the original reading of Rav — *lo ba'ei akirah* — while resolving the difficulty from the *Mishnah*. His view emerged as a consensus from a group: *nizreka mipi chabura*. The distinction is as follows. When does a *Pesach* require active redefinition to become a *shelamim*? Only when the *Pesach* still has a viable future as a *Pesach* — specifically, when the owners were *temei meit* and are therefore in line for a *Pesach sheini*. In that case, the *korban* has not lost its *Pesach* trajectory; it might yet be used as a *Pesach* on the 14th of Iyyar. Such an animal must be actively redefined to become a *shelamim*; it will not transform automatically.

But when the owners have simply withdrawn entirely — they have no future claim on any *Pesach* — the animal has no remaining *Pesach* pipeline to remain in. In that case, *lo ba'ei akirah*: it automatically becomes a *shelamim*, with no need for active redefinition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמָּדָא, דְּאָמַר: נִזְרְקָה מִפִּי חֲבוּרָה וְאָמְרִי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ בְּעָלִים טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים וְנִדְחִין לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: A difficulty arises **according to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda, who** dealt with the question whether a Paschal lamb requires uprooting and **said** that **it was thrown out from the group** of scholars who were studying the issue, **and they** all **said** as follows: Uprooting is required in a case **where the owners** of the offering **were ritually impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse** during the first *Pesaḥ* **and they were pushed off to the second *Pesaḥ*,** for in that case, they presumably want to sacrifice this animal as their Paschal offering on the second *Pesaḥ*, and therefore its status does not change unless it is explicitly uprooted.

This maps neatly onto Rav's original ruling. The *asham* set out to pasture is likewise not in any remaining *asham* pipeline — there is no residual use for it as an *asham* — and it therefore becomes an *olah* automatically. Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda's formulation vindicates that original position. The principle is: *ba'ei akirah* only when the *korban* remains in its original pipeline; *lo ba'ei akirah* when that pipeline is closed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי הוּא דְּבָעֵי עֲקִירָה, הָא בְּעָלְמָא לָא בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה. מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from this that **it is** only **this** offering that **requires** explicit **uprooting,** because it is reasonable to assume that its owners still intend to use it for its original purpose, **but in general it does not require uprooting.** According to this opinion, **what is there to say,** as it would seem that our mishna indicates that explicit uprooting is necessary?

The Gemara then restates the principle cleanly: *hayinu d'ba'ei akirah, hu d'ba'ei akirah; ha b'alma lo ba'ei akirah* — the type of *Pesach* that requires *akirah* is specifically that type; in a general case it does not.

The question then remains: how does this resolve the difficulty from the *Mishnah* regarding the *Pesach* whose owners withdrew? According to *lo ba'ei akirah*, it should automatically have become a *shelamim*. The Gemara notes that Rav himself cannot use the *nireh venidcha* answer — that it was once fit and then rejected — because Rav holds *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*: living animals cannot be permanently rejected. This is a resonant principle: the possibility of change inheres in living things; they cannot be labeled and discarded as irrevocably invalidated.

### Nireh Venidcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִפְרִישׁוֹ קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת, וּמֵתוּ בְּעָלִים אַחַר חֲצוֹת, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ נִרְאֶה וְנִדְחֶה. וְכׇל הַנִּרְאֶה וְנִדְחֶה — שׁוּב אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וְנִרְאֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: With what are we dealing here** in our mishna? We are dealing with a case **where they designated** the animal to be sacrificed as their Paschal offering **before midday,** and midday came and it was firmly established as their Paschal offering, **and the owners died after midday, such that** the offering **was** first considered **fit and** then **rejected:** It was originally fit to be sacrificed as either a Paschal offering or a peace-offering, and then it was rejected as a peace-offering when it was firmly established as a Paschal offering, and rejected as a Paschal offering when its owners died. And the principle is that **anything that was** first **fit and** afterward **rejected does not return to being fit.** The offering is therefore disqualified and burned immediately, as it can never be brought as a Paschal offering or as a peace-offering.

A further resolution is offered by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: the case in the *Mishnah* is specifically where the owners separated the animal before *chatzos* and then died after *chatzos*. This is significant because *chatzos* is the operative moment for the *Korban Pesach*. At *chatzos*, the animal was fully valid — the owners were alive and enrolled. Then it became unfit. This constitutes *nireh venidcha*: it was once fit (*nireh*) at the critical moment, and then was pushed away (*nidcha*). The principle *kol ha-nireh venidcha, shuv eino chozer venireh* — once something was fit and was rejected, it cannot be reinstated — applies here. The *Pesach* cannot revert to become a *shelamim*, because it has the stigma of having been valid and then rejected. This is why the *Mishnah* treats it as an intrinsically invalid *Pesach pasul*, burned immediately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא אֶלָּא לְרַב, הָא אָמַר רַב: בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים אֵינָם נִדְחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this answer: **Is this reason** necessary **for anyone but Rav,** who holds that explicit uprooting is not required? **But Rav** himself **said** that **living creatures cannot be** permanently **rejected.** The *halakha* of rejection applies only to animals that were already slaughtered, but living creatures cannot be permanently rejected from their sanctified status or eligibility for a mitzva.

But the Gemara immediately notes: this answer works only within Rav's framework on the condition that Rav does not hold *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*. Since Rav does hold that living animals cannot be permanently rejected, the *nireh venidcha* principle does not help him. The question therefore remains for Rav.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: וְכֵן הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֲחֵרִים לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח — פָּסוּל, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita* that adds to the mishna the detail that on a weekday the disqualified offering is immediately burned? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said: And similarly,** if **one slaughtered another** offering, such as a peace-offering, **for the purpose of a Paschal offering,** it is **disqualified.** If so, **its disqualification is in** the body of the offering **itself,** and so it should be burned immediately.

Rav Pappa proposes: *ha mani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *keivan sheshochet leshem Pesach pasul* — slaughtering another *korban* in the name of a *Pesach* is intrinsically invalid. Apply this here: once the owners withdrew, the animal automatically became a *shelamim* per *lo ba'ei akirah*. The *shochet*, not knowing this, continued slaughtering it as a *Pesach*. He was therefore slaughtering a *shelamim* for the purposes of a *Pesach*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, *hashochet acheirim leshem Pesach pasul* — slaughtering another *korban* for the purposes of a *Pesach* is *pasul* with an intrinsic *psul* — and that is why it must be burned immediately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, חַטָּאת נָמֵי מִחַיַּיב, דְּהָא לֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר טוֹעֶה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה פָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, he would deem him liable also to bring a sin-offering** for having slaughtered an invalid offering on Shabbat, **for Rabbi Eliezer does not accept** the position that **one who errs in regard to a mitzva is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering. This explanation must, therefore, be rejected.

This is an elegant solution, but the Gemara objects: *ve'i Rabbi Eliezer hi, chatas nami mechayev* — if the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer, why does the end of the *Mishnah* hold that one who slaughtered the *Pesach* without knowing the owners had withdrawn is *patur*? Rabbi Eliezer does not accept *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* as grounds for exemption. He should hold one *chayav* a *chatas*. This is the very debate we began with.

One could respond that the *patur* at the end of the *Mishnah* rests on *ones* rather than on *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* — how was the *shochet* to know the owners had withdrawn? Rashi makes this point. But the Gemara apparently understands that *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* is at least a contributing factor in the exemption, and Rabbi Eliezer would not recognize it. The Rabbi Eliezer solution therefore cannot accommodate the *Mishnah* in its entirety.

### Yosef Ben Choni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, תַּרְגְּמָא רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב סַלָּא חֲסִידָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: הָא מַנִּי יוֹסֵף בֶּן חוֹנַאי הִיא. דִּתְנַן, יוֹסֵף בֶּן חוֹנַאי אוֹמֵר: הַנִּשְׁחָטִים לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּלְשֵׁם חַטָּאת — פְּסוּלִים, אַלְמָא: פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ הִיא, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד, וּבִפְטוֹרֵי — סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef, son of Rav Salla the Ḥasid, explained before Rav Pappa** as follows: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Yosef ben Ḥonai. As we learned** in a mishna that **Yosef ben Ḥonai says:** Other offerings **that are slaughtered for the purpose of a Paschal offering or for the purpose of a sin-offering are disqualified,** as he agrees with Rabbi Eliezer in this regard. **It is apparent** that **its disqualification is in** the body of the offering **itself and therefore it should be burned immediately. But with regard to exemptions** from sin-offerings in cases of unintentional desecration of Shabbat, **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** that one who errs in regard to a mitzva is exempt.

The resolution comes through Rav Yosef the son of Rav Sala Chasida, before Rav Pappa: *ha mani Yosef ben Choni hi* — the *Mishnah* follows Yosef ben Choni, a *tanna* whose opinion we seldom encounter. Yosef ben Choni says: *hanishchatim leshem Pesach uleshem chatas pesulin* — things slaughtered for the purpose of a *Pesach* or a *chatas* are *pasul*. Just as a *Pesach* or *chatas* slaughtered for another *korban* is invalid, so too other *korbanot* slaughtered for the purposes of a *Pesach* or *chatas* are invalid.

This is substantially the same ruling as Rabbi Eliezer's on this point, but without the broader position of Rabbi Eliezer on *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah*. The *Mishnah* can therefore hold that the *shelamim leshem Pesach* is intrinsically *pasul* — *pasul b'gufo* — burned immediately, while separately holding, on the question of the *shochet*'s liability, like Rabbi Yehoshua: *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah patur*. *Alma psulah b'gufo hi, umishum hachi yisaref miyad* — it is intrinsically invalid, and therefore burned immediately. *Uvi'pturei, savar lah k'Rabbi Yehoshua* — and regarding exemption from a *korban*, it follows Rabbi Yehoshua. This is a fully coherent and internally consistent reading of the *Mishnah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּי מֵעִיקָּרָא: יוֹסֵף בֶּן חוֹנַאי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, it is clear** that it is **as he answered at the beginning,** that our mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Yosef ben Ḥonai.** Therefore, on weekdays the offering is burned immediately, and on Shabbat, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, one does not become liable to bring a sin-offering.

### Investigation Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ שְׁהוּת בְּיוֹם לֵידַע אִם מָשְׁכוּ בְּעָלִים אֶת יְדֵיהֶם אוֹ שֶׁמֵּתוּ אוֹ שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ — חַיָּיב, וּתְעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיוֹצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different answer to this question: **Rav said** his ruling with regard to a guilt-offering **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: If** one slaughtering the Paschal offering on Shabbat still **has time in the day to clarify whether the owners withdrew or died or became ritually impure, he is liable** to bring a sin-offering for having slaughtered on Shabbat, **and** the meat must be kept overnight so that **its form** be allowed to **decay, and** then **it should be taken out to the place of burning. What is the reason** that its form must be allowed to decay? **Is it not because** he holds that **it does not require uprooting?** And for that reason the disqualification is not inherent in the offering, and so it must be left overnight to attain the status of leftover sacrificial meat before being burned.

Rav Ashi offers a final answer: *Rav de'amar k'Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*. This tanna adds a dimension of investigation as a factor in liability and exemption. He says: if there was time during the day to investigate and discover whether the owners had withdrawn, or whether they had died, or whether they had become *tamei*, then one is *chayav* — the failure to investigate is actionable. If there was no such time, one is *patur*.

This tanna also holds — and this is what is relevant for the *akirah* question — *ute'ubar tzurah vetatze leveit hasereifah*: you wait for its appearance to change overnight before burning it. This indicates that according to him, the *Pesach* whose owners withdrew is not intrinsically *pasul* as a *Pesach*. It is a *shelamim* automatically, it is simply *pasul* because it was brought after the *tamid*, and therefore one waits for it to become *notar* overnight before burning. This is entirely consistent with *lo ba'ei akirah* — automatic redefinition, without intrinsic *psul*.

### Burning Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָ מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר לַהּ כְּתַנָּא דְּבֵי רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ פִּיגּוּל נָמֵי בָּעֵי עִיבּוּר צוּרָה. דְּיָלֵיף ״עָוֹן״ ״עָוֹן״ מִנּוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **From where** is this known to be correct? **Perhaps** it requires uprooting and the disqualification is inherent. And the fact that he requires decay of form **is because he agrees with the *tanna* of the school of Rabba bar Avuh, who said** that **even *piggul*,** an offering disqualified by improper intent, which is considered an inherent disqualification, **also requires decay of form, for he derived** this requirement by way of a verbal analogy between the word **"iniquity"** (Leviticus 7:18) stated in relation to an offering disqualified by improper intent and the word **"iniquity"** (Leviticus 19:7) stated **with regard to leftover** sacrificial meat.

The Gemara asks: how do we know this reflects *lo ba'ei akirah* rather than reflecting the position of the school of Rabba bar Avuha, who holds that *even pigul* and all other invalid *korbanot* require waiting overnight before burning — derived through a *gezeirah shavah* of *avon avon* from *notar*? Under that position, even an intrinsically *pasul Pesach* would require waiting overnight, and no inference about automatic *shelamim*-status could be drawn.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, נִטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָא וַדַּאי בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמָּדָא: נִזְרְקָה מִפִּי חֲבוּרָה, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ בְּעָלִים טְמֵאֵי מֵת וְנִדְחוּ לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **For if you do not say so,** i.e., if the *baraita* was taught in accordance with this opinion, then in a case in which **the owners became ritually impure, what is there to say?** In that case **it certainly requires uprooting, for Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda said** that **it was thrown out from the group** of scholars who were studying the issue: Uprooting is required in a case **where the owners** of the offering **were ritually impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse** during the first *Pesaḥ* **and they were pushed off to the second *Pesaḥ*.** Since their offering is presumably set to be used on the second *Pesaḥ*, explicit change of the offering's status is required.

The Gemara answers: the school of Rabba bar Avuha cannot be the basis here, because among the cases where Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka requires waiting overnight is the case where the owners became *tamei*. But if the owners are *tamei*, the *Pesach* is in line for *Pesach sheini* — and as Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda established, that case certainly requires *akirah*. So that *Pesach* is *pasul* intrinsically as a *Pesach* (without *akirah* it remains a *Pesach pasul*). Why then would one wait overnight? Only if we say that our tanna follows *lo ba'ei akirah* for the withdrawal case, treating it as a *shelamim* with only an extrinsic *psul*, while for the *tamei* case, since it needs *akirah* and lacks it, it is also intrinsically *pasul* — yet both cases share the rule of waiting overnight. That consistency can only be explained by the school of Rabba bar Avuha's blanket rule that *all* invalid *korbanot* wait overnight. Which means our tanna might be following that rule rather than reflecting *lo ba'ei akirah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּי מֵעִיקָּרָא: יוֹסֵף בֶּן חוֹנַאי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, it is clear** that it is **as he answered at the beginning,** that our mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Yosef ben Ḥonai.** Therefore, on weekdays the offering is burned immediately, and on Shabbat, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, one does not become liable to bring a sin-offering.

The Gemara concludes: *ela mivarta k'deshanin meikara Yosef ben Choni hi* — the clearest answer remains the one already given. The *Mishnah* follows Yosef ben Choni regarding the intrinsic invalidity of *shelamim leshem Pesach*, and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding the exemption from a *korban* for the *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah*. The *akirah* debate itself remains a dispute among *tannaim*: some hold that a *korban* whose original purpose has lapsed must be actively redefined for another purpose (*ba'ei akirah*); others hold it transforms automatically (*lo ba'ei akirah*), with the *reiyah* requirement for the *asham* being only a rabbinic fence and not a substantive condition.
