# Pesachim 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

### to'eh b'dvar mitzvah

We begin on 72b, in the midst of the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning one who is *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* — who errs while in the course of performing a *mitzvah*, particularly one that carries a license to override Shabbat, such as *brit milah* or the *Korban Pesach*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that under the right circumstances — an honest mistake, properly understood — the person is *patur*. The Gemara now picks up with a scene: Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak were sitting on the porch of Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak, and they were discussing the following statements.

### Notar & Niddah

Reish Lakish says: if the spits became confused — the spit bearing *notar*, leftover sacrificial meat whose willful consumption incurs *kares*, was mixed up with the spit bearing properly roasted *korban* meat that one is obligated to eat — and one ate from the wrong spit, one is *chayav*. This is not a case involving Shabbat; it is a case of attempting to perform a *mitzvah* and, through a factual error, committing an *aveira* that would ordinarily require a *korban* if done *b'shogeg*.

Rabbi Yochanan adds a related case: if a man attempts to have marital relations with his wife — a *mitzvah* — and it turns out she is a *niddah*, he is *chayav*. However, if a man performs *yibum* with his *yevama* — his widowed sister-in-law, where the act of *yibum* is itself a *mitzvah* — and she too turns out to be a *niddah* at that moment, he is *patur*.

The Gemara then considers how these cases relate to one another. One version says: *kol shekein b'hi mechayev* — all the more so Rabbi Yochanan would hold the one who ate *notar* is *chayav*, since by eating *notar* there is no *mitzvah* element at all. When the husband had relations with his wife, at least he also fulfilled the *mitzvah* of marital intimacy, even though he transgressed the prohibition of *niddah*; there is a dual dimension to the act. But eating *notar* yields nothing but the *aveira*.

Another version inverts this: by the *notar* case, he is actually *patur*, because there he could not have asked the spit whether it was *notar*. The liability in the *niddah* case of his wife flows precisely from the fact that he *could* have asked her. This reading recognizes the personhood of the wife — she is someone he could and should have consulted. *Aval hacha*, where inquiry was impossible, there is greater grounds for exemption.

What remains somewhat open is exactly how these cases map onto Rabbi Yehoshua's framework. Presumably the discussion takes place within Rabbi Yehoshua's position, which does allow for exemption in certain cases. Yet here, liability is affirmed in most instances — either because the person could have asked, as in the wife case, or perhaps because, as in the *notar* case, the person did not ultimately perform a *mitzvah* at all. The Gemara does not fully unpack why the *notar* case yields liability vis-à-vis Rabbi Yehoshua; it can be surmised that accomplishing a *mitzvah* in the end is a relevant factor that has been somewhat underplayed here but is more explicit elsewhere.

### Yevama Exception

The Gemara then asks: Rabbi Yochanan, what is the difference? By the *yevama* he performed a *mitzvah* — but by his wife he was also attempting, and at least arguably accomplishing, a *mitzvah*. Marital relations is a *mitzvah* for husband and wife. So why is one *chayav* and the other *patur*?

The Gemara's initial response is *me'uberet* — the wife is pregnant. Since pregnancy precludes *pru u'revu*, perhaps marital relations in that context carry no *mitzvah*. The Gemara immediately rejects this: *ve'ika simchat onatah* — the *mitzvah* of marital intimacy is not confined to procreative contexts. There is a *mitzvah* of giving one's wife pleasure, *simcha*, through regular conjugal relations, independent of whether children will result.

The Gemara then proposes: *she'lo bish'at onatah* — it was not the time of her scheduled *onah*. There is a regularity, discussed in Ketubot, based on the husband's occupation, and the *mitzvah* applies at those scheduled intervals. Outside of them, perhaps it is not a *mitzvah*. To this the Gemara raises Rava's statement: *chayav adam lesameach ishto bidvar mitzvah* — a man is obligated to give his wife pleasure even outside the fixed schedule, whenever it would be welcome and pleasurable to her. So there seems to be a *mitzvah* at virtually any time.

## Marital Mitzvah

### Pregnant Wife

This passage in the Gemara is striking in its own right, quite apart from the primary question of *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah*. The Gemara moves through a progression of views on when marital relations constitute a *mitzvah*: first, only when procreation is possible; then, at scheduled intervals; and finally, from Rava's statement, at essentially any time it would be pleasurable to the wife. This is a significant broadening of the concept.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava's ruling — that a man is obligated to give his wife pleasure, even outside the formal *onah* schedule, as a *mitzvah* — represents the Gemara's most expansive position on this question. The Gemara's apparent refusal to accept that marital relations are ever *not* a *mitzvah* (at least whenever they would be welcome) carries weight. Rashi, drawing on another Gemara, qualifies this somewhat: the *mitzvah* applies when she is signaling her interest. The Gemara here, however, may not have wanted to rely on that qualification — perhaps because it did not wish to assume that the absence of such signals implies unwillingness, which would approach coercion. A middle position is conceivable: she is not overtly initiating, but nor would she decline. The simple reading of this Gemara suggests that whenever relations would be welcomed or appreciated, there is a *mitzvah*. This Gemara thus stands as a significant source on the scope of *onah* as a *mitzvah*.

### Samuch Levistah

Given this, when would it not be a *mitzvah*? The Gemara answers: *samuch levistah* — when it is close to the time of her expected period. Relations are prohibited at that time, since she may become a *niddah* during the act itself. Whether the prohibition is *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan* is a debate among the *Rishonim*, but the point is that proximity to the expected onset renders relations not only non-obligatory but actively prohibited. Therefore, in that specific context, there is no *mitzvah* — and the husband is *chayav* because he was not performing a *mitzvah* at all.

The Gemara then asks: if so, the same should apply to the *yevama* — she too would presumably be *samuch levistah* at the relevant moment, so he was not performing a *mitzvah* with her either. Why then is he *patur* in that case?

### Embarrassment Factor

The Gemara answers: *yevamto baziz mina* — with his *yevama*, he is embarrassed. They have never been intimate before. He is not comfortable asking her whether she is approaching her period, even as they are about to have their first act of *yibum* together. *Ishto lo baziz mina* — with his wife, by contrast, he has no such embarrassment, and he should have asked.

The Gemara thus shifts from what initially appeared to be a substantive distinction — that *yibum* is a *mitzvah* while relations with a *niddah* wife are not — to a procedural one: the determinative factor is whether it was reasonable to expect him to inquire. In the case of the *yevama*, inquiry was not reasonably expected; in the case of his wife, it was. One might observe that the Gemara completely leaves aside the question of the woman's own liability — whether the *yevama* herself was *meizid*, *shogeg*, or *ones* — focusing entirely, as is its consistent practice, on the man.

The larger point emerging from all of this is that these cases — not Shabbat cases, but cases of attempting a *mitzvah* and committing an *aveira* through error — result in liability in most configurations, except where inquiry was genuinely impracticable. The Gemara has also not unpacked why Reish Lakish holds the *notar* case to be *chayav* within Rabbi Yehoshua's framework; one plausible answer is that there the person did not wind up performing a *mitzvah* in any meaningful sense, and that is what Rabbi Yehoshua requires for exemption. But the Gemara does not articulate this explicitly here.

## Rabbi Yehoshua Position

### Following Which Opinion

The Gemara now asks: *Rabbi Yochanan k'man* — who exactly is Rabbi Yochanan following? He applies the principle of *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* to the *yevama* case, which lacks urgency. The Gemara considers whether he follows Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Yehoshua of the *Mishnah* regarding the *Korban Pesach*, Rabbi Yehoshua of the *brit milah* cases, or Rabbi Yehoshua of *trumah*. Each possibility is examined.

### Urgency Cases

If Rabbi Yochanan follows Rabbi Yosi — as we learn in a *Mishnah*: if the first day of *Sukkot* falls on Shabbat and one forgot and carried the *lulav* into the *reshut harabbim* while attempting to perform the *mitzvah*, he is *patur mipnei she'hotzi birshut*, because he carried with license, trying to fulfill a *mitzvah*. The Gemara objects: *dilma shani hatam zmano bahu* — perhaps that case is distinct because of the urgency. If you want to perform the *mitzvah* of *lulav* on the first day of Sukkot, you must do it today. That urgency — *zmano bahu* — provides grounds for exemption that do not apply to *yibum*, which can be performed today or next week.

The same objection applies to Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the *Mishnah* of our tractate: when one makes a mistake while bringing the *Korban Pesach*, there too there is urgency — *zmano bahu* — the *korban* must be brought today. And similarly for the *brit milah* cases discussed yesterday: the circumcision must be performed on its designated day. All of these cases of exemption share the feature of temporal pressure. Rabbi Yochanan's application of the principle to *yibum*, where no such urgency exists, therefore represents a *chidush*, an extension beyond what was previously established.

### Trumah Exception

The Gemara then considers Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the case of *trumah*: *detnan*, as we learned in a *Mishnah*, someone was eating *trumah* and in the middle of the meal was informed that he is, in fact, a *ben grushah* or *ben chalutzah* — the son of a *kohen* who married a divorcée or a woman released from *levirate* marriage — and therefore lacks the sanctity of a *kohen* and is a *challal*. Rabbi Eliezer holds him *chayav* to pay the *keren v'chomesh* — principal and an additional fifth — for having eaten *trumah* as a non-*kohen*. Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him.

Now, eating *trumah* carries no urgency tied to a particular day. There is no *zmano bahu* here — a *kohen* may eat *trumah* on any day. Yet Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him. This seems to be exactly the kind of precedent Rabbi Yochanan needs for extending the exemption to the non-urgent *yevama* case.

The Gemara pushes back: *dilma k'Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye* — perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in the *trumah* case was specifically where the *trumah* was eaten on *Erev Pesach*, and on that day there *was* urgency: the *trumah* bread was about to become *chametz* and forbidden, so it had to be consumed that day. On any other day, he would be *chayav*. The urgency factor persists even here.

### Trumah Avodah

Alternatively, the Gemara offers a distinct rationale for the *trumah* ruling that has nothing to do with urgency: *shani trumah d'ikrei avodah* — eating *trumah* is categorized as an act of *avodah*, divine service. *Avodah rachmana achsher* — and where a *kohen* performed *avodah* without knowing he was disqualified, the Torah itself validates that *avodah* retroactively.

This is demonstrated from a *Mishnah*: if a *kohen* was standing at the altar bringing a *korban* and it was then revealed that he is a *ben grushah* or *ben chalutzah*, Rabbi Yehoshua holds that all the *korbanot* he brought throughout his life as a *kohen* are *kasher*. The Scriptural basis, as derived by the Gemara, is the verse *barech Hashem cheilo u'fo'al yadav tirtzeh* — "bless, O Lord, his valor, and accept the work of his hands" (Devarim 33:11), read in conjunction with the preceding verse about the *kohen* bringing incense and *korbanot*. The *drashah* understands this as divine approbation of the *kohen*'s service performed in good faith, even if he was retroactively discovered to be disqualified.

The source for treating *trumah* as *avodah* is established through a *Tosefta*-style teaching involving Rabbi Tarfon, who once told Rabban Gamliel that he had not come to the study hall the previous night because he had been performing *avodah* — priestly service. Rabban Gamliel was puzzled: *vechi avodah bizman hazeh minayin* — how is there temple service nowadays? Rabbi Tarfon replied by citing the verse *avodat matanah eten et kehunatchem*, in the context of *trumah*, which uses the language of service and declares that those who draw near improperly shall die. From this the *Gemara* derives a *hekesh*: eating *trumah* in the provinces is analogous to performing *avodah* in the Temple. Therefore, just as a *kohen* who served unknowingly while disqualified is not required to bring a *korban* — indeed, his *avodah* stands — so too a *kohen* who ate *trumah* while unknowingly disqualified is exempt.

The Gemara's conclusion, then, is that Rabbi Yochanan's extension of the *patur* principle to the *yevama* — a non-urgent case — represents a genuine *chidush* relative to the established *Tannaitic* precedents. All the prior cases either involved urgency (*zmano bahu*) or a special status of *avodah* applicable only to *trumah*. Rabbi Yochanan has gone a step further.

## Productive Acts

### Tiken Principle

The Gemara now transitions to 73a, to the *Mishnah*'s cases of *shechitah* performed on Shabbat for the *Korban Pesach* in various invalid configurations, and the question of why each results in liability — specifically, why each constitutes a productive *melachah* on Shabbat.

The background principle: on Shabbat, *kol hamikalkelin peturin* — any act that is destructive rather than constructive carries no liability. The *chiyuv* for a *melachah* requires a productive act. This creates a difficulty for the cases in the *Mishnah*: if one slaughters a *Korban Pesach* incorrectly — *shelo lishmah*, for the wrong owners, or in other disqualifying circumstances — the result is a *pasul korban*, a piece of meat that must be burned. In what sense was the *shechitah* a productive, constructive act?

Rav Huna bar Chinena posed this question to his son to bring before Rav Zreika, working from the premise that *makalkel bechabura patur* — that wounding, like any other *melachah*, is only culpable when productive. If you slaughter an animal *shelo lishmah* on Shabbat, what did you *tiken* — what did you fix or improve?

The Gemara answers: *tiken im alu lo yerdu* — you produced at least this benefit: a *pasul korban*, even though invalid, if its fats were placed on the *mizbe'ach* would not be removed. The act of *shechitah*, even invalid, creates a minimal legal improvement in the animal's status. That small positive consequence is sufficient to categorize the act as constructive and impose liability.

### Treifa Beseter

The *Mishnah* then addresses the case where the animal was slaughtered and was subsequently found to be a *treifa* with an internal defect — *treifa beseter*, a hidden *treifa*. In that case, one is *patur*. The implication of the *Mishnah*, as the Gemara reads it, is that had the *treifa* been evident externally — *begaluy* — one would be *chayav*. The Gemara asks: *mai tiken*? What was improved? A *treifa* slaughtered as a *korban* would have to be removed from the altar.

The Gemara answers: *tiken lehotzi midei gezeila* — the *shechitah* accomplished something. Had the animal died without *shechitah*, it would have been a *neveilah* — ritually impure and possibly subject to the prohibition of *gezeila* (theft) if taken by others. By performing *shechitah*, even of a *treifa*, one removed it from the status of *neveilah*. That is a sufficient productive dimension to establish liability on Shabbat.

### Avodah Zarah

Rav Avina then raises a more challenging case from a *Braita*: one who slaughtered a *chatas* on Shabbat, outside the Temple, and for *avodah zarah* — three separate violations, each *chayav kares* if done willfully, each *chayav* a *chatas* if done *beshogeg*. The Gemara thus says one would bring three sin offerings. But Rav Avina's question is: *mai tiken*? What constructive act was performed?

The answer of *lehotzi midei neveilah* does not apply here. Something slaughtered for *avodah zarah* is treated as *tamei*, indeed bearing *tumah b'ohel* according to the Gemara in Shabbat — a severity beyond that of ordinary *neveilah*. The *shechitah* did not remove the animal from *tumah*; it replaced one severe status with another, arguably worse one.

The Gemara answers through Rav Avira: *shemotzi'o midei eiver min hachai* — the *shechitah* at least removed the prohibition of *eiver min hachai*, the limb of a living animal. Before *shechitah*, anyone consuming a limb of that animal — certainly a *ben Noach* — would have violated *eiver min hachai*. After *shechitah*, that specific prohibition no longer applies. Even if what remains is *neveilah*, *tamei*, and *tikroves avodah zarah*, it is no longer *eiver min hachai*. This minimal improvement suffices to render the act productive and impose *chiyuv*.

Notably, Rashi points out explicitly that this benefit is relevant to the *ben Noach*. This raises an interesting implication: a *ben Noach* is prohibited in *eiver min hachai*, yet apparently is not prohibited in *tikroves avodah zarah* — since if trading one for the other provided no benefit, the answer would fail. Although a *ben Noach* is certainly warned against *avodah zarah* itself, perhaps the status of *tikroves avodah zarah* — the offering of *avodah zarah* — does not carry the same prohibition for a *ben Noach*.

Tosafot also notes that the *tumah* status of *tikroves avodah zarah* that the Gemara here treats as *d'Oraita* appears elsewhere to be *d'Rabbanan*, and addresses that tension.

## Akirah Debate

### Asham to Olah

Having addressed the Shabbat liability questions, the Gemara returns to one final matter arising from the last case of the *Mishnah*: you slaughtered the *Pesach* and it was discovered that the owners had already withdrawn. You are *patur* because you were *onus* — you could not have known. This opens a broader discussion about *akirah* — the question of whether a *korban* that can no longer be used for its original purpose requires an active redefinition to become valid for another purpose, or whether it is automatically redefined.

Rav Huna reports in the name of Rav: if you have an *asham* — a guilt offering — that was set out to *reiyah* (pasture), because the owners had died and the *halacha* is to let it graze until it acquires a *mum*, at which point it is redeemed and the money used for a communal *olah* — and before acquiring a *mum* you slaughtered it *stam*, without specifying its purpose, it is *kasher l'olah*. It automatically counts as an *olah*, since that was its intended destination all along. The money, and therefore the identity, was already heading toward an *olah*.

### Rav's Position

From this, the Gemara infers: *almah kasavar lo ba'ei akirah* — Rav holds that once a *korban* can no longer be used for its original purpose and is in the pipeline toward another use, it requires no active redefinition. It is automatically transformed.

The Gemara immediately challenges: if that is so — *ki lo nitak nami* — why confine this ruling to after the animal was put out to pasture? The moment the owners die, the only possible destination for this animal is an *olah*. The *halacha* of *lo ba'ei akirah* should apply even before it was put out to pasture. The Gemara answers: *gezeirah l'achar kaparah atu lifnei kaparah* — it is a rabbinic enactment. Biblically, the same would apply even before *reiyah*. But rabbinically, if we permit using the animal as an *olah* immediately — before the visible, public act of setting it out to pasture — people who see an *asham* become an *olah* will think that *korbanot* can simply be switched at will. The act of *reiyah* serves as a visible marker signaling that this is a special category case.

The Gemara then attempts to prove this ruling from another *Mishnah*: an *asham* whose owners died or who were atoned for through another animal — the *Mishnah* says it grazes until it acquires a *mum*, is sold, and the money goes into the donation box for communal *olot*. Rabbi Eliezer holds it must die, treating it like a *chatas*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds it is sold and the money used for a personal *olah*. The Gemara notes: *bidamav in, aval gufo lo* — only the money may be used for an *olah*, not the animal itself directly. Why not use the animal directly? The Gemara applies the same rationale: *d'gazar l'achar kaparah atu lifnei kaparah*. Tosafot finds this proof somewhat circular — the explanation simply restates the same principle without independently demonstrating it — but the point is affirmed: Rav's position of *lo ba'ei akirah* is supported, with the *reiyah* requirement being a rabbinic fence.

### Pesach Challenge

Rav Chisda challenges Rav Huna: the *Mishnah* says that if the owners withdrew from the *Pesach* and this was discovered after *shechitah*, the *Pesach* is *pasul*, and a *Braita* teaches it must be burned immediately. If Rav is correct that no active redefinition is needed — that the moment the owners withdrew, the *Pesach* automatically became a *shelamim* — then why is it *pasul* at all? It is a *kasher shelamim*. The worst one could say is that it was slaughtered after the *tamid shel bein ha-arbayim* — somewhat out of sequence — but that would not be an intrinsic *psul*. One would simply wait overnight and burn it. It would not be burned immediately.

The Gemara's initial response is to suggest reversing Rav's ruling: perhaps Rav's formulation should not be read as *shachato stam kasher l'olah* — slaughtered it without designation and it is automatically an *olah* — but rather *shachato l'shem olah kasher* — it is valid only if one actively slaughtered it for an *olah*. Under this reading, Rav holds *ba'ei akirah*, that active redefinition is required. That would explain the *Mishnah*: since the owners withdrew and no one actively redefined it as a *shelamim*, it remains a *Pesach pasul*.

## Akirah Resolution

### Rabbi Chiya Bar Gamda

The Gemara now introduces Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda, who formulated a position that preserves the original reading of Rav — *lo ba'ei akirah* — while resolving the difficulty from the *Mishnah*. His view emerged as a consensus from a group: *nizreka mipi chabura*. The distinction is as follows. When does a *Pesach* require active redefinition to become a *shelamim*? Only when the *Pesach* still has a viable future as a *Pesach* — specifically, when the owners were *temei meit* and are therefore in line for a *Pesach sheini*. In that case, the *korban* has not lost its *Pesach* trajectory; it might yet be used as a *Pesach* on the 14th of Iyyar. Such an animal must be actively redefined to become a *shelamim*; it will not transform automatically.

But when the owners have simply withdrawn entirely — they have no future claim on any *Pesach* — the animal has no remaining *Pesach* pipeline to remain in. In that case, *lo ba'ei akirah*: it automatically becomes a *shelamim*, with no need for active redefinition.

This maps neatly onto Rav's original ruling. The *asham* set out to pasture is likewise not in any remaining *asham* pipeline — there is no residual use for it as an *asham* — and it therefore becomes an *olah* automatically. Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda's formulation vindicates that original position. The principle is: *ba'ei akirah* only when the *korban* remains in its original pipeline; *lo ba'ei akirah* when that pipeline is closed.

The Gemara then restates the principle cleanly: *hayinu d'ba'ei akirah, hu d'ba'ei akirah; ha b'alma lo ba'ei akirah* — the type of *Pesach* that requires *akirah* is specifically that type; in a general case it does not.

The question then remains: how does this resolve the difficulty from the *Mishnah* regarding the *Pesach* whose owners withdrew? According to *lo ba'ei akirah*, it should automatically have become a *shelamim*. The Gemara notes that Rav himself cannot use the *nireh venidcha* answer — that it was once fit and then rejected — because Rav holds *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*: living animals cannot be permanently rejected. This is a resonant principle: the possibility of change inheres in living things; they cannot be labeled and discarded as irrevocably invalidated.

### Nireh Venidcha

A further resolution is offered by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: the case in the *Mishnah* is specifically where the owners separated the animal before *chatzos* and then died after *chatzos*. This is significant because *chatzos* is the operative moment for the *Korban Pesach*. At *chatzos*, the animal was fully valid — the owners were alive and enrolled. Then it became unfit. This constitutes *nireh venidcha*: it was once fit (*nireh*) at the critical moment, and then was pushed away (*nidcha*). The principle *kol ha-nireh venidcha, shuv eino chozer venireh* — once something was fit and was rejected, it cannot be reinstated — applies here. The *Pesach* cannot revert to become a *shelamim*, because it has the stigma of having been valid and then rejected. This is why the *Mishnah* treats it as an intrinsically invalid *Pesach pasul*, burned immediately.

But the Gemara immediately notes: this answer works only within Rav's framework on the condition that Rav does not hold *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*. Since Rav does hold that living animals cannot be permanently rejected, the *nireh venidcha* principle does not help him. The question therefore remains for Rav.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

Rav Pappa proposes: *ha mani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *keivan sheshochet leshem Pesach pasul* — slaughtering another *korban* in the name of a *Pesach* is intrinsically invalid. Apply this here: once the owners withdrew, the animal automatically became a *shelamim* per *lo ba'ei akirah*. The *shochet*, not knowing this, continued slaughtering it as a *Pesach*. He was therefore slaughtering a *shelamim* for the purposes of a *Pesach*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, *hashochet acheirim leshem Pesach pasul* — slaughtering another *korban* for the purposes of a *Pesach* is *pasul* with an intrinsic *psul* — and that is why it must be burned immediately.

This is an elegant solution, but the Gemara objects: *ve'i Rabbi Eliezer hi, chatas nami mechayev* — if the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer, why does the end of the *Mishnah* hold that one who slaughtered the *Pesach* without knowing the owners had withdrawn is *patur*? Rabbi Eliezer does not accept *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* as grounds for exemption. He should hold one *chayav* a *chatas*. This is the very debate we began with.

One could respond that the *patur* at the end of the *Mishnah* rests on *ones* rather than on *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* — how was the *shochet* to know the owners had withdrawn? Rashi makes this point. But the Gemara apparently understands that *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah* is at least a contributing factor in the exemption, and Rabbi Eliezer would not recognize it. The Rabbi Eliezer solution therefore cannot accommodate the *Mishnah* in its entirety.

### Yosef Ben Choni

The resolution comes through Rav Yosef the son of Rav Sala Chasida, before Rav Pappa: *ha mani Yosef ben Choni hi* — the *Mishnah* follows Yosef ben Choni, a *tanna* whose opinion we seldom encounter. Yosef ben Choni says: *hanishchatim leshem Pesach uleshem chatas pesulin* — things slaughtered for the purpose of a *Pesach* or a *chatas* are *pasul*. Just as a *Pesach* or *chatas* slaughtered for another *korban* is invalid, so too other *korbanot* slaughtered for the purposes of a *Pesach* or *chatas* are invalid.

This is substantially the same ruling as Rabbi Eliezer's on this point, but without the broader position of Rabbi Eliezer on *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah*. The *Mishnah* can therefore hold that the *shelamim leshem Pesach* is intrinsically *pasul* — *pasul b'gufo* — burned immediately, while separately holding, on the question of the *shochet*'s liability, like Rabbi Yehoshua: *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah patur*. *Alma psulah b'gufo hi, umishum hachi yisaref miyad* — it is intrinsically invalid, and therefore burned immediately. *Uvi'pturei, savar lah k'Rabbi Yehoshua* — and regarding exemption from a *korban*, it follows Rabbi Yehoshua. This is a fully coherent and internally consistent reading of the *Mishnah*.

### Investigation Factor

Rav Ashi offers a final answer: *Rav de'amar k'Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*. This tanna adds a dimension of investigation as a factor in liability and exemption. He says: if there was time during the day to investigate and discover whether the owners had withdrawn, or whether they had died, or whether they had become *tamei*, then one is *chayav* — the failure to investigate is actionable. If there was no such time, one is *patur*.

This tanna also holds — and this is what is relevant for the *akirah* question — *ute'ubar tzurah vetatze leveit hasereifah*: you wait for its appearance to change overnight before burning it. This indicates that according to him, the *Pesach* whose owners withdrew is not intrinsically *pasul* as a *Pesach*. It is a *shelamim* automatically, it is simply *pasul* because it was brought after the *tamid*, and therefore one waits for it to become *notar* overnight before burning. This is entirely consistent with *lo ba'ei akirah* — automatic redefinition, without intrinsic *psul*.

### Burning Timeline

The Gemara asks: how do we know this reflects *lo ba'ei akirah* rather than reflecting the position of the school of Rabba bar Avuha, who holds that *even pigul* and all other invalid *korbanot* require waiting overnight before burning — derived through a *gezeirah shavah* of *avon avon* from *notar*? Under that position, even an intrinsically *pasul Pesach* would require waiting overnight, and no inference about automatic *shelamim*-status could be drawn.

The Gemara answers: the school of Rabba bar Avuha cannot be the basis here, because among the cases where Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka requires waiting overnight is the case where the owners became *tamei*. But if the owners are *tamei*, the *Pesach* is in line for *Pesach sheini* — and as Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda established, that case certainly requires *akirah*. So that *Pesach* is *pasul* intrinsically as a *Pesach* (without *akirah* it remains a *Pesach pasul*). Why then would one wait overnight? Only if we say that our tanna follows *lo ba'ei akirah* for the withdrawal case, treating it as a *shelamim* with only an extrinsic *psul*, while for the *tamei* case, since it needs *akirah* and lacks it, it is also intrinsically *pasul* — yet both cases share the rule of waiting overnight. That consistency can only be explained by the school of Rabba bar Avuha's blanket rule that *all* invalid *korbanot* wait overnight. Which means our tanna might be following that rule rather than reflecting *lo ba'ei akirah*.

The Gemara concludes: *ela mivarta k'deshanin meikara Yosef ben Choni hi* — the clearest answer remains the one already given. The *Mishnah* follows Yosef ben Choni regarding the intrinsic invalidity of *shelamim leshem Pesach*, and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding the exemption from a *korban* for the *to'eh b'dvar mitzvah*. The *akirah* debate itself remains a dispute among *tannaim*: some hold that a *korban* whose original purpose has lapsed must be actively redefined for another purpose (*ba'ei akirah*); others hold it transforms automatically (*lo ba'ei akirah*), with the *reiyah* requirement for the *asham* being only a rabbinic fence and not a substantive condition.
