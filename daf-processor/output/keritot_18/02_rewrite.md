# Keritot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Asham Talui Basics

### Definition

The central question of this chapter, and indeed of much of the surrounding *sugyot*, concerns the nature of the doubt required to obligate one in an *asham talui*. The *asham talui* is brought when a person ate something believing it was permitted (*heter*), and subsequently discovered that he might have committed a sin. Had he known with certainty that he had sinned — assuming the act carried the penalty of *kares* — he would bring a *chatat*. But since he knows only that he might have sinned, he brings an *asham talui*, which serves to protect him and atone provisionally until the matter can be resolved.

### Knowledge Requirement

As both Rashi and Tosafot note, this *korban* applies only when the person genuinely believed at the time that what he was doing was permitted. If at the time of the act he was already aware that the matter was in doubt (*safeik*) and proceeded anyway, he does not bring an *asham talui*. Rashi characterizes such a person as *mezid*, which is admittedly a somewhat harsh framing, since he did not willfully transgress a known prohibition — he simply knew it was a *safeik* and acted regardless. Tosafot prefers the formulation *shev mi-yediyata*: ordinarily, *shogeg* means that had the person known the relevant facts he would not have acted; here, however, he did know the facts — namely that there was a doubt — and acted anyway. Such a person cannot bring a *korban*.

### One or Two Pieces

With that framework in place, the *Gemara* now turns to the question of what actually constitutes the relevant doubt. The classic case presented in the *Mishnah* involves a person who had before him a piece of food that might have been *chelev* (forbidden fat) or *shuman* (permitted fat). The underlying debate — which has until this point been framed in terms of the reading of a *pasuk*, specifically whether to read the word as *mitzvot* (plural) or *mitzvas* (singular) — is whether one needs *shtei chatichot* (two pieces), one of which was definitely *chelev* and the other definitely *shuman*, with the uncertainty lying in which one was eaten, or whether *chaticha achat* (one piece) of uncertain status suffices. The *Gemara* has until now traced this debate through the textual sources; it now turns to examine the underlying *sevarot* — the logical rationales — that animate each position.

## Efshar Levarer Reason

### Rav's Position

The *Gemara* picks up at the bottom of 17b, where Rabbi Chiya bar Abba states the rule: if two pieces were before you — one definitely *chelev* and one definitely *shuman* — and you ate one without knowing which, you are *chayav* in an *asham talui*. But if there was only a single piece of uncertain status and you ate it, *patur* — you are exempt. This is Rav's position: *shtei chatichot* obligates, *chaticha achat* does not.

### Efshar Levarer Logic

Rabbi Zeira now provides a rationale for Rav's position that goes beyond the textual derivation. *Kasavar*, he explains: *shtei chatichot efshar levarer issuro, chaticha achat ee efshar levarer issuro* — with two pieces, it is possible to clarify the nature of the prohibition; with one piece, it is not. The logic is that the *asham talui* is not merely a one-time atonement for the present moment; it serves as a provisional measure, protecting the person until it can be determined whether he truly needs to bring a *chatat*. If the matter could in principle be clarified — because the other piece still exists and can be tested — then the *asham talui* has a functional role to play in the larger process of eventual resolution. If, by contrast, there was only one piece and its status can never be determined, there is no such framework of provisional atonement awaiting clarification.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Cases

Rabbi Yirmiya now challenges Rabbi Zeira's explanation with a series of cases in which an *asham talui* is brought even though the matter can never be clarified — and indeed, there was never *shtei chatichot* to begin with. These cases, which were also examined in the previous *daf*, all bear the name of Rabbi Eliezer. In each instance, Rabbi Zeira's response is consistent: these cases follow the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that even *chaticha achat* suffices, and this does not undermine the position of our *Mishnah*.

### Koy Case

The first case: Rabbi Eliezer rules that one is *chayav* an *asham talui* for eating the *chelev* of a *koy*, the animal of indeterminate species-status. If the *koy* is classified as a *behemah* (domesticated animal), its fat is forbidden; if it is classified as a *chayah* (wild animal), its fat is permitted. The doubt is inherent to the nature of the creature — it can never be definitively resolved. There are not two pieces, and there is no possibility of ever clarifying the prohibition. Yet Rabbi Eliezer obligates an *asham talui*. The *Gemara* answers: this is Rabbi Eliezer's position, which does not require *efshar levarer*. Rav holds otherwise.

### Yibum Case

The second case: a woman's husband died and she underwent *yibum* (levirate marriage) with his brother, and subsequently a child is born after seven months. The doubt is whether the child belongs to the first husband — in which case the *yibum* union was obligatory and the subsequent relationship is permitted — or to the second husband, in which case the *yibum* union was not a *mitzvah*, and their relationship constitutes *arayot* (a forbidden union). The child is in any case *kasher*, since either way there is no element of *mamzerut*. Yet the couple brings an *asham talui*, even though before the era of genetic testing — and even in principle — it may never be determined which husband fathered the child. Again, no *shtei chatichot*, no *efshar levarer*, and yet an *asham talui* is required according to Rabbi Eliezer. The *Gemara* gives the same answer.

### Nidda Case

The third case: a husband and wife are intimate, and afterward she is found to have *niddah* blood. If the blood was found immediately upon her wiping, the assumption is that she was a *niddah* during the act of intercourse, they are both *tamei*, and they bring a *korban*. If the blood was found only after some time, the status is *temeah mi-safeik* — doubtfully *tamei* — and a *braita* teaches that they are *chayavin be-asham talui*. But here too, there was never an established *issur* clearly in front of them, there is no second piece to test, and the question of whether she was a *niddah* at the time of relations can never be resolved. The *Gemara* once again answers: *ha mani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — that is Rabbi Eliezer's view.

With all three cases answered, the *Gemara* has established the following: there is a legitimate tannaic position — that of Rabbi Eliezer — which requires neither *shtei chatichot* nor *efshar levarer*. But that is not the position of our *Mishnah*, which requires two pieces, and one rationale for that requirement is *efshar levarer*.

## Psuk vs. Efshar Levarer

### Distinction Between Reas…

The *Gemara* now clarifies a line that was passed over briefly above but is essential to the analysis: *mai ika bein ta'ama de-Rava le-ta'ama de-Rabbi Zeira* — what practical difference is there between Rava's reason, which is rooted in the formal textual requirement of two (*psuk* or *gziras ha-kasuv*), and Rabbi Zeira's reason, which is based on the functional logic of *efshar levarer*?

### Half-Kezayit Scenario

The *Gemara* answers: *ika beinayhu kezayis u-mechtzah* — they differ in the case of one piece that is a *kezayit* and another that is a half-*kezayit*, one being *chelev* and one *shuman*, and you ate one without knowing which. According to Rava, who requires the formal criterion of two full halachic units, a half-*kezayit* does not qualify — only one of the two pieces represents a complete act of eating, and therefore the formal requirement of two is not satisfied. The person is *patur*. According to Rabbi Zeira, however, who grounds the requirement in the ability to test, the fact that a half-*kezayit* remains is sufficient: we could examine it and determine which piece the person ate. The test remains possible, and therefore *efshar levarer* is satisfied even with a half-*kezayit* as the remaining piece. The practical difference between a formal textual requirement and a functional rationale thus produces divergent outcomes in this scenario.

It is worth noting — as the discussion suggests — that in our era, with the availability of DNA testing and other scientific methods, questions of paternity that were previously irresolvable fall under a different framework if the criterion is *efshar levarer*. The ability to clarify becomes, in principle, relevant again.

## Ikvei Assur Reason

### Ikvei Assur Logic

The *Gemara* now introduces a third explanation for Rav's position, transmitted by Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha in the name of Rav. The statement of the ruling itself is identical — *shtei chatichot chayav, chaticha achat patur* — but the underlying rationale is now articulated differently: *kasavar shtei chatichot ikva issura, chaticha achat lo kava issura* — with two pieces, the *issur* has been established; with one piece, it has not.

This rationale is the most intuitively compelling of the three and the most widely cited. When two pieces were before you — one definitively *chelev* and one *shuman* — you know, looking back at your act, that there was a concrete *issur* present in the situation. Whatever you ate, there was unquestionably something forbidden in front of you. The only question is whether you chose the path of *issur* or the path of *heter*. The objective reality of a *cheiv* sin was established in the environment. By contrast, when there was only one piece of uncertain status, it is possible — indeed, perhaps likely — that the piece was entirely *heter* all along. You were merely in doubt about it. But if the piece was always *shuman*, there never was a concrete *issur* before you; the entire episode was *heter* and you only worried unnecessarily. In that case, there was no objective sinful situation; there was only subjective uncertainty.

### Conceptual Significance

This distinction points to a significant conceptual dimension. The *asham talui* can be understood in one of two ways. If it is purely a protective measure — something that covers a person until he can determine whether he truly needs a *chatat* — then perhaps the mere possibility of sin, even without any established concrete *issur*, should suffice. But if the *asham talui* is understood as something more quasi-substantive, as reflecting an inherently problematic quality in the act itself, then having eaten in the presence of a concrete *issur* — even if one happened to have eaten the *heter* piece — is already an inherently problematic act. The *ikvei assur* approach suggests that to trigger an *asham talui*, the objective reality of an *issur* must have been present, not merely a subjective doubt about what something was.

There is something almost philosophically significant about this approach: it is not that you might have sinned that obligates you; it is that you conducted yourself in a situation where something genuinely *assur* was present. That kind of act has an inherent weight even if it turns out you ate the right piece.

### Non-Jew Ate First

The *Gemara* now asks what practical difference there is between the *ikvei assur* rationale and the *efshar levarer* rationale. The answer: *ika beinayhu*, they differ in the following scenario — two pieces were before you, one *chelev* and one *shuman*; a non-Jew came and ate the first piece; a Jew then came and ate the second piece.

According to Rava's formal requirement of two, the Jew ate when only one piece was before him — *patur*. According to Rabbi Zeira's *efshar levarer* rationale, at the time the Jew ate, the other piece was no longer present — there was nothing left to test — *patur* as well. But according to Rav Nachman's *ikvei assur* rationale, the *issur* had been present — at one point there was definitely a *chelev* piece in the mix — and therefore the Jew who ate the second piece should be *chayav*, since the *issur* was established even if no longer physically present when he ate. This creates a scenario where *ikvei assur*, taken to its logical conclusion, produces *chiyuv* even when only one piece remains at the time of the act — because what matters is whether an *issur* was ever established in the situation, not whether it is currently present.

This is a striking result and extends the *ikvei assur* logic somewhat beyond what might have been initially expected. One might have thought that *ikvei assur* simply means there must be an actual *issur* before you at the moment of your act. But the *Gemara's* scenario — in which the non-Jew ate the first piece before the Jew acted — suggests that what matters is whether an *issur* was ever objectively part of the situation, even if it is gone by the time of the act. Tosafot discusses this point at length in relation to other cases, including *bein ha-shmashot* of *Shabbos* — where the *issur* had already been established (it was *Shabbos*) and the only question is whether it has ended yet — which might also satisfy *ikvei assur*, though there are distinctions to be drawn.

### Rava's Challenges

Rava now challenges Rav Nachman with the same three cases that Rabbi Yirmiya raised against Rabbi Zeira — the *koy*, the *yibum*, and the *niddah* — each of which involves an *asham talui* obligation without *ikvei assur*. In each case, Rav Nachman gives the same answer: *ha mani Rabbi Eliezer hi*. These represent Rabbi Eliezer's view that does not require *ikvei assur*.

The *koy* case is straightforward: the *koy* by its very nature never had an established status, so there is no *ikvei assur* at all. The *yibum* case is more complex. One might try to argue that the *ikvei assur* was established here as well — after all, the woman was forbidden to her brother-in-law while her husband was alive, so her *issur* status was in some sense already established and the question is merely whether it changed. But the *Gemara* does not make that argument, and the *yibum* case is treated as lacking *ikvei assur*. The *niddah* case is even clearer in the opposite direction: the woman began as a *non-niddah* and the question is whether she became *tamei* during the act. Since she started in a state of *heter*, the *issur* was never established beforehand, and thus there is no *ikvei assur*.

After Rav Nachman gave his responses and left, Rava reflected: *mai ta'ama lo amarei lei d'ha mani Rabbi Meir hi* — why did I not also say that this could reflect Rabbi Meir's position? For Rabbi Eliezer is not the only *tanna* who does not require *ikvei assur*.

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *Gemara* introduces a *baraita*: one who slaughters an *asham talui* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* (*shechutei chutz*) — is he *chayav* for the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*? Rabbi Meir holds he is *chayav*; the *Chachamim* hold he is exempt.

Rashi explains this debate as being about a case where the *asham talui* was brought for a situation that lacked *ikvei assur* — such as the *koy* case — and the question is whether such an offering still carries the legal status of a *korban*. Rabbi Meir holds it does; the *Chachamim* hold that without *ikvei assur*, the obligation was never established and therefore the animal lacks the full status of a *kodesh* that would trigger *shechutei chutz*.

Rabbeinu Gershom offers a more creative and elegant reading. He suggests that the debate concerns the situation of one who slaughtered his *asham talui* outside — which creates its own *safeik*: if the underlying sin was real, the animal was a genuine *korban* and slaughtering it outside was *shechutei chutz*; if the underlying sin did not occur, the animal was perhaps not a genuine *korban*. The question of whether one sinned with *shechutei chutz* is thus itself a *safeik*. Rabbi Meir holds that for this *safeik* one brings — notably — an *asham talui*, since one might have done *shechutei chutz*. The *Chachamim* say *patur*, because this is not a case of *ikvei assur*. According to Rabbeinu Gershom, therefore, Rabbi Meir's position demonstrates that he does not require *ikvei assur* to be *chayav* in an *asham talui*.

Rava reflects that he could have made this point to Rav Nachman — not merely attributing the dissenting view to Rabbi Eliezer, but noting that Rabbi Meir independently holds the same position. The question of why he did not say so in the moment is left unresolved.

The *Gemara* then asks: why not simply say, as he had been doing, that it is Rabbi Eliezer? The answer is: *ha kamashma lan d'Rabbi Meir b'shitat Rabbi Eliezer kai* — the point is that even Rabbi Meir, a separate *tanna*, stands in the same approach as Rabbi Eliezer. This expands the scope of the dissenting view.

To summarize where we have arrived: there are now three rationales for the requirement of *shtei chatichot* — the *psuk* (formal textual requirement), *efshar levarer* (ability to clarify), and *ikvei assur* (establishment of a concrete prohibition) — and a minority view, held by Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Meir, that requires none of these.

## One vs. Two Pieces Debate

### Two Pieces Required

The *Gemara* now moves to a new statement: *chaticha safeik shel chelev safeik shel shuman, achalah, plugta de-Rabbi Eliezer ve-Chachamim* — a single piece of uncertain status, if eaten, is the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim*. This seems to simply restate what has been discussed. Tosafot raises a subtlety here: the *Rabbi Eliezer* of this passage may not be the same *Rabbi Eliezer* as the one cited earlier; the earlier cases involved *Rabbi Elazar* (a different figure), while here it is *Rabbi Eliezer*. The distinction matters for understanding whose position is being represented.

### Rabbi Eliezer Alternative

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai irya ki achalah* — why specifically say if you ate it? According to Rabbi Eliezer, *afilu lo achalah nami* — even if you did not eat anything at all, you could bring an *asham talui*. Why? Because of a *mishnah* that records Rabbi Eliezer's remarkable ruling: *misnadeiv adam asham talui bechol yom* — a person may voluntarily bring an *asham talui* every single day. Since every day one might have inadvertently committed some sin, and every day the verse applies — *ve-hu rachum yechaper avon* — one may bring an *asham talui* simply out of general spiritual concern, without any specific concrete basis for suspecting that a sin occurred. This is, in its own way, a striking *halacha* — it gives institutional sanction to a kind of conscientious self-examination and preemptive atonement.

The consequence for the current discussion is: if Rabbi Eliezer allows an *asham talui* even for a wholly abstract, generic doubt, he clearly does not require *shtei chatichot*. But by the same token, the inference about what *obligates* him is complicated: the *nedava* (voluntary) dimension of Rabbi Eliezer's position tells us what one may do, not necessarily what one is required to do. So attributing to Rabbi Eliezer an *obligatory* *asham talui* for *chaticha achat* based solely on this *mishnah* is uncertain.

### Bava ben Buta Reference

Rav Ashi explains that this passage concerns Rabbi Eliezer *aliba de-Bava ben Buta* — in accordance with Bava ben Buta's approach. A *mishnah* records that Bava ben Buta would say he would have brought an *asham talui* even the morning after *Yom Kippur*, so eager was he to have ongoing atonement. His colleagues, however, told him: wait until you have actually entered a realm of doubt. Do not bring an *asham talui* the morning after *Yom Kippur* when everything has just been atoned for. The point is that even within Rabbi Eliezer's broader view, there must be at least some minimal basis — some *smidgen* of doubt — to justify bringing the *asham talui*, even as a *nedava*.

### Obligation vs. Optional

The question thus becomes: when Rabbi Eliezer says you may bring an *asham talui* even for a generic, unspecified doubt, does that also mean you are *obligated* when there is something more concretely problematic but still only *chaticha achat*? Logically, these positions should travel together — if *asham talui* does not require *shtei chatichot* even to do it *as a nedava*, then it seems unlikely that the formal *chiyuv* would require the stricter threshold of *shtei chatichot*. The *Gemara's* resolution is not entirely clear-cut, but the most comfortable reading of the conclusion appears to be: if something concretely problematic occurred, one is *chayav*; for purely abstract, unspecified general doubt — like the morning after *Yom Kippur* — one may bring it optionally as a *nedava*. This framework allows for a hybrid: one can simultaneously hold that *shtei chatichot* is required for a formal *chiyuv* (the position of the *Mishnah*) while still preserving Rabbi Eliezer's category of an optional *asham talui* for lesser degrees of doubt.

## Practical Scenarios

### Two Present Scenario

The *Gemara* now presents a *Tanna de-vei Rebbi* with a series of concrete scenarios, all built around the case of two pieces — one *chelev* and one *shuman* — in front of a person. The first case is the standard one: a Jew ate the first piece, and the standard *asham talui* obligation applies because there were two pieces at the time of his act.

### Non-Jew First, Jew Second

The reverse scenario: a non-Jew ate the first piece, and then a Jew ate the second. In this case, *patur* — the Jew is exempt. Why? Because at the time the Jew performed his act, only one piece remained. There was no *shtei chatichot* at the moment of his action, no *efshar levarer*, and — according to the standard view — no *ikvei assur* properly operative at that moment (even though it had existed earlier). This scenario, notably, appears to contradict the *ikvei assur* framework as discussed earlier, since the *issur* was in principle already present at some point. The *Gemara* does not revisit that tension here, apparently treating this *baraita* as following a different tannaic opinion. *Rabbi* (Rebbi), however, holds that the Jew in this scenario is *chayav*, because according to his view, even *chaticha achat* suffices.

### Mixed Willful/Unwitting

If the person ate the first piece *be-shogeg* (inadvertently) and then the second *be-mezid* (deliberately, knowing it was a *safeik*): for the first piece — when there were two and he ate *be-shogeg* — he is *chayav* an *asham talui*. For the second piece — when there was only one remaining and he ate knowing it was a *safeik* — he is exempt on both counts: both because there was only one piece, and because eating with knowledge of the doubt (*mezid*) disqualifies from *asham talui*. *Rabbi* again holds the Jew is *chayav* because of his lenient position on *chaticha achat*.

If the order is reversed — the first piece *be-mezid* and the second *be-shogeg*: for the first piece, though there were two, the *mezid* quality disqualifies. For the second piece, though it was *be-shogeg*, there was only one piece left. *Patur* in this case. *Rabbi* says *chayav* for the second.

### Both Unwitting

If both pieces were eaten *be-shogeg* — and this now introduces a second person — *sheneihem chayavin*, both are *chayav*. When the first person ate, there were two pieces and he was *shogeg*: clearly *chayav* an *asham talui*. But when the second person ate, there was only one piece. He should logically be *patur*. Why then is he also *chayav*?

### Secondary Eater Issue

The *Gemara* explains: *ha-sheni lo min ha-din* — the second person is not obligated in the strict legal sense. But if we say the second person is *patur*, the implication is that he did not eat the *chelev*, which means the first person did — and that in turn would make the first person *chayav* in a *chatat*, not merely an *asham talui*. One does not wish to create a situation where the second person's exemption functions as an implicit verdict that the first person must bring a *chatat*.

### Stipulation Solution

Rav Ashi resolves this: we instruct the second person to bring an *asham talui* and make a *tenai* — a stipulation. He declares: if the first person ate the *shuman*, then I ate the *chelev*, and this *asham talui* should serve as atonement for my having eaten it. And if not — if the first person ate the *chelev* — then let this offering function as a generic voluntary *asham talui* in the spirit of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that one may bring it as a *nedava* at any time. This elegant solution draws on the *nedava* dimension of Rabbi Eliezer's position while operating within the framework of *shtei chatichot* for obligatory offerings. The two positions — requiring *shtei chatichot* for a *chiyuv* and allowing *asham talui* as a *nedava* without it — are thus compatible and can coexist within a single legal strategy.

## Multiple Asamim Question

### Rabbi's Position

The *Gemara* now turns to a *Tanna de-vei Rebbi* addressing how many *asham taluim* one must bring when acts of possible sin are separated by intervening awareness. The scenario: a person ate doubtful *chelev*, then became aware he might have sinned, then forgot and ate again, then became aware again, and so on repeatedly. Rebbi (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) rules: just as with a *chatat* — where becoming definitively aware that one sinned between acts obligates separate *chataot* for each — so too with *asham talui*, becoming aware in between that one might have sinned obligates a separate *asham talui* for each act. The same *yedi'ah* that divides for *chatat* divides for *asham talui*.

### One Asham View

Against this, Rabbi Yossi, Rabbi Yehudah, Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Shimon hold: *ein meivi ela asham talui echad* — even if there were multiple acts of possible sin separated by awareness, only one *asham talui* is brought. Their proof-text is the redundant phrasing *al shigagato asher shagag* — for the error that he erred — which the *Gemara* reads as teaching that even many *shigagot* of the same type require only one *korban*.

### Guilt vs. Objective Sin

This position reflects a profound conceptual distinction. A *chatat* addresses the objective reality of sin: something must be cleansed, and each separate sinful act requires its own cleansing. This is why a *tinok she-nishba* — a child raised in captivity who violated *Shabbos* without any awareness — still brings a *chatat*. The obligation is not about personal guilt; it is about the objective fact that a sin occurred. An *asham*, by contrast — and an *asham talui* in particular — is rooted in the person's own guilt. The very language of the *pasuk* speaks to this: *asham hu, ashom asham la-Hashem* — the vocabulary of *ashmah*, of personal culpability and guilt. And because the *asham talui* focuses on the person rather than on the objective sin-event, multiple instances of the same category of possible sin constitute a single undivided condition of guilt, not multiple discrete events each requiring separate atonement.

This is a conceptually elegant position: if I repeatedly ate something that might have been *chelev*, my being at fault is a unified personal state, not a series of discrete sinful acts. The minority view here thus isolates the *asham talui* as an expression of personal moral accountability rather than objective sin-reality. The *halacha*, however, follows Rebbi and the *Mishnah*: an *asham talui* is treated like a *chatat*, and when awareness intervenes between acts, multiple *ashamot* are required.

## Asham vs. Chatat Awarene…

### Learning Chatat from Ash…

Rabbi Zeira now offers a striking reinterpretation of Rebbi's ruling. When Rebbi said that just as definite awareness divides for *chatat* so does doubtful awareness divide for *asham talui*, Rabbi Zeira reads the comparison in the opposite direction: *kan shana Rabbi yedi'ot safeik mechalekot le-chataot* — here Rebbi was not drawing on *chatat* to explain *asham talui*, but rather teaching us something about *chatat* itself by way of *asham talui*. That is: just as doubtful awareness (*yedi'at safeik*) suffices to divide for *asham talui*, so too does *yedi'at safeik* divide for *chatat* — even if a person becomes aware only that he might have sinned, and subsequently continues sinning, then later discovers he definitely did, the acts are divided and he must bring multiple *chataot*.

### Rava's Counterargument

Rava disagrees: *ein yedi'at safeik mechaleket le-chataot* — doubtful awareness does not divide for *chatat*. Only definite awareness does. He reads Rebbi's comparison as an analogy, not a direct application: just as definite awareness creates multiple *chataot*, by analogy doubtful awareness creates multiple *ashamot* — but the *type* of awareness required differs between the two *korbanot*.

### Abaye's Response

Abaye challenges Rava: do you really maintain that doubtful awareness does not divide for *chatat*? If so, a person who experienced only *yedi'at safeik* between acts and later discovered he had definitely sinned would bring only one *chatat*. But then why would he bring multiple *asham taluim*? Abaye's logic is that the comparison between *asham talui* and *chatat* cannot work by analogy — it must be a direct application of the same rule. If the rule for *chatat* is that only definite awareness divides, then you cannot analogically apply it to *asham talui*, because *asham talui* never involves definite awareness. Therefore, the rule must operate identically: *yedi'at safeik* must be sufficient to divide — for both.

### Baraita Evidence

In support, Abaye cites a general principle from a *baraita*: *kol she-challukin be-chatat, challukin be-ashamos* — whatever divides for *sin offerings* divides for *asamot* as well. This demonstrates that the dividing mechanism must be the same for both, and therefore whatever divides for *asham talui* — namely *yedi'at safeik* — must also divide for *chatat*.

## Yom Kippur Mechanism

### Yom Kippur Atonement

The discussion now incorporates *Yom Kippur*. There is a principle that *Yom Kippur* atones for doubtful sins and thereby relieves a person of the obligation to bring an *asham talui* — *Yom Kippur* functions in place of an *asham talui*. At the same time, for a person who is definitively *chayav* a *chatat*, the mere passage of *Yom Kippur* does not cancel the *chatat* obligation. With this in mind, the *Gemara* explores whether *Yom Kippur* can serve as a dividing force analogous to *yedi'at safeik*.

### Pre/Post Yom Kippur Acts

Two background principles are important here. First, when we say awareness divides acts for *chatat* or *asham talui*, one interpretation is psychological — the person consciously recalled the possible sin, which restarts the process. Another interpretation is objective: at the moment of awareness, a *chiyuv* of *asham talui* is created for what came before; subsequent acts are therefore separate from the prior obligation. The *Gemara* elsewhere notes that even the passage of days (*yamim she-beinataim*) can constitute a form of *yedi'ah*, which is difficult to explain psychologically but makes sense if it is about objective *chiyuv*. Second, if *Yom Kippur* functions structurally like an *asham talui*, dissolving the doubtful-sin obligation for what preceded it, then on the objective-*chiyuv* view, it should divide acts — acts before *Yom Kippur* were addressed by one *chiyuv*, acts after begin a new chapter.

## Asham vs. Chatat Aware…

### Yom Kippur Challenge

Rava bar Chanan challenges Abaye directly: according to you, who holds that *yedi'at safeik* divides for *chataot*, consider the following — one ate a *kezayis* of *chelev* before *Yom Kippur* and a *kezayis* of *chelev* after *Yom Kippur*, all in a single *he'elem* (uninterrupted state of forgetfulness), without any awareness in between. Since *Yom Kippur* functions structurally like an *asham talui* — and if *yedi'at safeik* divides for *chataot* — should he then bring two *chataot* because *Yom Kippur* intervened? This seems deeply counterintuitive and perhaps absurd. The person never had any awareness at all; he ate in a single continuous state of forgetting. Are we really going to say that the calendar fact of *Yom Kippur* passing divides his undifferentiated forgetful eating into two separate *chatat* obligations?

## Yom Kippur Mechanism…

### Awareness Requirement

Abaye defends his position by challenging the premise. He asks: *u-man leima lan de-Yom HaKippurim mechaper al d'lo mityada lei* — who says *Yom Kippur* atones for someone who does not even know he is obligated? Perhaps when the principle states that *Yom Kippur* functions like an *asham talui*, it is limited to someone who is aware of his possible obligation. In the scenario under discussion, the person is in a continuous state of *he'elem* — he has no awareness — and perhaps *Yom Kippur* does not operate as an *asham talui* for such a person at all.

### Yoma Mishna

Rava rejects this defense. The *Mishnah* in Yoma that enumerates what *Yom Kippur* atones for explicitly states *hoda ve-lo hoda* — whether the matter is known to him or not. *Yom Kippur* atones for sins the person does not even know about. One cannot therefore restrict *Yom Kippur's* atoning function to cases of awareness.

### Eating/Drinking Same Day

Rava bar Chanan sharpens the challenge further with a different formulation. A *mishnah* rules: if one ate and drank on *Yom Kippur* in a single state of forgetfulness, he brings only one *chatat*, because eating and drinking both violate the same prohibition of affliction. Now between eating and drinking there must necessarily have been some brief interval of time — and since every moment of *Yom Kippur* functions as an *asham talui* for whatever doubt existed in that moment, if *yedi'at safeik* divides for *chataot*, then that brief interval of *Yom Kippur* should divide the eating and the drinking into two separate *chatat* obligations. Yet the *Mishnah* says only one. This is a powerful concrete challenge from an authoritative *mishnaic* source.

### Unresolved Tension

Abaye attempts a further defense: *u-man leima lan de-chol sha'ata ve-sha'ata mechaper* — who says every individual moment of *Yom Kippur* functions as a separate *asham talui*? Perhaps the entire day constitutes a single act of atonement, operative as a whole from the previous evening through nightfall, not moment by moment. Therefore that brief interval between the eating and the drinking does not constitute an intervening *asham talui*-equivalent that would divide the acts.

Rava bar Chanan responds sharply — the *Gemara* records the phrase *tarda*, which Rashi glosses as something close to *shote*, meaning a naive or obtuse claim — and cites a *baraita*: if on *Yom Kippur* itself a person committed a possible sin, even if it occurred at *im chasheicha*, right at the moment before nightfall, he is *patur* — because *kol ha-yom mechaper*, every part of the day atones. This *baraita* explicitly states that every moment of *Yom Kippur* functions as an atonement mechanism. The defense is therefore untenable.

### Position Attribution

The *Gemara* arrives at an unresolved position on whether *yedi'at safeik* divides for *chataot*. The final suggestion offered is: *teimar Rabbi Zeira le-Rabbi* — attribute Rabbi Zeira's reading to Rebbi; it is Rebbi who holds that *yedi'at safeik* divides for *chataot*, as an extension of his ruling that multiple *asham taluim* are required when *yedi'at safeik* intervenes. The challenges from the *Mishnah* about eating and drinking on *Yom Kippur* and the broader questions raised — these all apply to *Rebbi's* view. *Ha Rabbanan hi* — the rabbis who dispute Rebbi, who hold that only one *asham talui* is required regardless of intervening doubtful awareness, do not face these difficulties, because their entire framework does not treat *yedi'at safeik* as a dividing force.

The sugya thus closes with the tension unresolved and the debate formally assigned to the positions of Rebbi and the *Rabbanan*. The discussion will continue after *Yom Tov*.
