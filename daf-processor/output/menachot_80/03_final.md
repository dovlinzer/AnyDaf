# Menachot 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Todah & Derivatives

### Mishnah Overview

Daf 80 of Menachot continues the tractate's engagement with the todah offering, a korban that carries a distinctive bread requirement: forty loaves accompanying the sacrifice, comprising thirty matzah and ten chametz. Because the todah is thus intimately bound up with a minchah-type obligation, it finds its natural place in Menachot. The sugya opens by examining which animals, though possessing the kedushah of a todah, are nonetheless exempt from that bread requirement — and it proceeds to develop a series of increasingly nuanced principles about the relationship between the todah and its accompanying lechem.

### Baraita Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְלַד תּוֹדָה וּתְמוּרָתָהּ, הִפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָה וְאָבְדָה וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ – אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה״ – הַתּוֹדָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, וְלֹא וְלָדָהּ וְלֹא חִילּוּפָהּ וְלֹא תְּמוּרָתָהּ טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the offspring** of an animal designated as **a thanks offering, or** an animal that is **its substitute;** or in a case where **one separated** an animal as **a thanks offering and it was lost and he separated another in its stead,** and the first animal was then found, in all three cases, the second animal, i.e., the offspring, the substitute, or the replacement, is sacrificed, but it **does not require** the bringing of accompanying **loaves.** This is **as it is stated:** "If he sacrifices it for a thanks offering, **then he shall sacrifice with the thanks offering** unleavened cakes mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil, and cakes mingled with oil, of fine flour poached" (Leviticus 7:12). The verse indicates that **the** initial **thanks offering requires loaves, but neither its offspring, nor its replacement, nor its substitute requires loaves.**

The Mishnah at the bottom of 79b presents three cases in which an animal bearing the kedushah of a todah does not require bread. The first is the vlad todah: since a female animal may serve as a todah, its offspring inherits a todah kedushah — yet it is not the todah itself. The second is the temurah: the Torah prohibits exchanging a sanctified animal, but if one attempts to do so, the second animal acquires kedushah even as the first retains it. The third case is הפריש תודה ואבדה והפריש אחרת תחתיה — one designated a todah, it was lost, and so a second was sanctified in its stead; the first was then found, leaving both animals standing. One of them will serve as the todah proper; the other bears only a derived kedushah.

In all these cases, the Mishnah rules einah te'unah lechem, as the verse states, וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה — the todah requires bread, but not its child, not its substitute, and not its temurah. Only the todah itself carries the bread obligation.

### Textual Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַהוּ אוֹמֵר ״תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיב״? ״אִם עַל תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיב״ – מִנַּיִן לְמַפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת – מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה יַקְרִיב וְלַחְמָהּ עִמָּהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַתּוֹדָה יַקְרִיב״. יָכוֹל שֶׁתְּהֵא שְׁנִיָּה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ״ – אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם. אַחַר שֶׁרִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב וּמִיעֵט, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת וְלָדוֹת וַחֲלִיפוֹת וּתְמוּרוֹת לְהַקְרָבָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִם עַל תּוֹדָה״. יָכוֹל יְהוּ טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה״ – תּוֹדָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, וְלֹא וְלָדָהּ וְלֹא חִילּוּפָהּ וְלֹא תְּמוּרָתָהּ טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **What is it** that the verse teaches when it **states: He sacrifices for a thanks offering?** The verse states: **If he sacrifices for a thanks offering. From where** is it derived that **with regard to one** who **separated** an animal as **his thanks offering and it was lost and he separated another in its stead, and the first animal was** then **found, and** now **they both stand** fit to be sacrificed, **from where** is it derived **that he may sacrifice whichever** one **of them he wishes, and its loaves** are brought along **with it? The verse states** that **he shall sacrifice the thanks offering,** i.e., as long as he sacrifices some thanks offering. One **might** have thought **that the second** animal also **requires loaves** to be brought with it. Therefore, **the verse states: "He sacrifices it,"** indicating that only **one** thanks offering requires loaves, **but not two. Since the verse included** the second animal as fit for sacrifice **and excluded** it from the requirement to bring loaves with it, **from where** is it derived that the verse also **includes** a thanks offering's **offspring, replacements, and substitutes** as fit **for sacrifice?** From the fact that **the verse states: "If for a thanks offering,"** one **might** have thought that **they would require loaves** to be brought with them. Therefore, **the verse states: "Then he shall sacrifice with the thanks offering,"** indicating that the initial **thanks offering** itself **requires loaves, but neither its offspring, nor its replacement, nor its substitute requires loaves.**

A baraita elaborates the derivation. The verse reads: זֹאת תּוֹרַת זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים אִם עַל תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת מַצּוֹת בְּלוּלוֹת בַּשֶּׁמֶן רְקִיקֵי מַצּוֹת מְשׁוּחִים בַּשֶּׁמֶן. The baraita asks: if one separated a todah, it was lost, a second was sanctified in its stead, and then the first was found — leaving both animals present — how do we know that either one may be brought to fulfill the obligation, and that whichever is brought may be accompanied by its bread? The answer is derived from the phrase אִם עַל תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ — whichever is the todah, either may be brought.

One might then suppose that the second animal — the one not brought — also requires bread. The baraita therefore derives from יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ, "he shall bring it" — one, not two. Only one todah receives the bread; the other does not.

### Kapara Condition

The baraita now turns to a sharper reading of the pasuk. The phrase אִם עַל תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ contains the word al, which had initially been read together with toda as a unified phrase. Parsed separately, however, im al toda — "if upon a toda," that is, something that stands over or derives from a toda — the word al signals that even derivative animals, those not directly sanctified to serve as the toda itself, are included in the category of animals that may be brought as an offering.

### Obligatory vs. Free-will

Having established through im al toda that derivative animals — the vlad and the temurah — may nonetheless be brought as offerings, the baraita considers whether they should also require bread. One might argue that since they have not been displaced by another animal competing for the same obligation, perhaps they should carry the full bread requirement. The verse responds with the phrase וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת מַצּוֹת, where al zevach hatodah limits the bread obligation to the animal actually serving as the todah itself. Derivative animals may be offered but do not require bread. The principle is thus: im al toda expands the category of animals eligible to be brought; al zevach hatodah limits the bread requirement to the todah proper. The baraita concludes: תּוֹדָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם וְלֹא וְלָדָהּ וְלֹא חֲלִיפָתָהּ וְלֹא תְּמוּרָתָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם.

## Bread & Kapara

### Todah Nedava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח רַב חֲנִינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה – טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the offspring, substitute, and replacement of a thanks offering, the Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥanina sent** a letter from Eretz Yisrael to the Sages in Babylonia containing the following statement **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The mishna **taught** that the offspring, replacement, and substitute do not require loaves **only** in a case where they were sacrificed **after** the owner achieved **atonement,** i.e., where the initial thanks offering was already sacrificed with its loaves; **but** if they were sacrificed **before** he achieved **atonement, they require loaves.**

Three lines from the bottom of 79b, the Gemara introduces a statement transmitted by Rav Chanina in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אֲבָל לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם. The ruling that bread is not required applies only after kapara has been achieved — that is, after the sacrifice has been legitimately offered and the obligation fulfilled. Before kapara, the animal would require bread. The Gemara then devotes considerable effort to unpacking what case this distinction is meant to address.

### Vlad Nedava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי בַּהּ רַב עַמְרָם: אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַחֲלִיפֵי תּוֹדַת חוֹבָה – אִי לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה, תְּנֵינָא! אִי לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה, תְּנֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram discussed this** *halakha* sent in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan and asked: **To which** *halakha* in the mishna is this referring? **If we say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring **to the replacement for an obligatory thanks offering,** then there is no novelty in his statement. **If** his intention is to teach that where the thanks offering was found **before** the owner achieved **atonement** with its replacement, the replacement requires loaves, **we** already **learned** this in the *baraita*, as it states: He may sacrifice whichever one of them he wishes, and its loaves are brought along with it. And **if** his intention is to teach that where the thanks offering was found **after** the owner achieved **atonement** with the replacement, the thanks offering does not require loaves, this too **we** already **learned** in the *baraita*, as it teaches that only the first offering requires loaves.

Rabbi Abin challenges Rabbi Yochanan's formulation by asking on which case the before/after kapara distinction actually applies — a necessary question, since different cases yield different results.

### Vlad Chova

The first candidate is chalufei todah chova: one who had a personal obligation — either through a vow (harei alai) or through the circumstances that Chazal identified as giving rise to a todah obligation, such as crossing a desert or a sea — designated an animal as a todah, it was lost, and a substitute was sanctified. This is precisely the case analyzed in the baraita at the daf's opening. But as Rabbi Abin notes, the distinction between lifnei kapara and le'achar kapara is already explicit there: before either animal has been brought, both are lifnei kapara and whichever is offered gets the bread; once one has been offered and the obligation is satisfied, the other is le'achar kapara and receives no bread. Since the baraita already teaches this, Rabbi Yochanan's statement would be adding nothing. אִי לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה תְּנִינָא, אִי לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה תְּנִינָא — both sides of the distinction were already taught.

### Shevach Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַחֲלִיפֵי תּוֹדָה נְדָבָה, בֵּין לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה בֵּין לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם – מַרְבֶּה בְּתוֹדוֹת הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring not to the replacement for an obligatory thanks offering, but **to the replacement for a voluntary thanks offering.** This too is difficult, because **whether** the initial thanks offering was found **before** the owner achieved **atonement** with the replacement **or after** he achieved **atonement,** both offerings **require loaves,** as the owner **is** considered one who **increases thanks offerings.** That is, since he was not required to bring a replacement for the offering, if he brings another offering it is considered an additional voluntary offering, and a voluntary thanks offering requires loaves.

The Gemara then proposes that Rabbi Yochanan may be speaking of chalufei todah nedava — a free-will todah with no underlying personal obligation. In this case, if the todah is lost and a substitute is sanctified, the person has incurred no obligation; the first animal was sanctified as a voluntary offering. The Gemara concludes, however, that this cannot be Rabbi Yochanan's case either, because here בֵּין לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה בֵּין לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם — both before and after the first todah has been offered, the second also requires bread. Since there is no obligation being discharged, each animal is simply another todah in its own right. מַרְבֶּה בְּתוֹדוֹת הוּא — one is simply bringing additional todot.

### Abaye's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַוְּלַד תּוֹדָה חוֹבָה – לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה טָעוּן לֶחֶם, לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אֵין טָעוּן לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring **to the offspring of an obligatory thanks offering.** Rabbi Yoḥanan teaches that if the offspring was sacrificed **before** the owner achieved **atonement, it requires loaves,** but if it was sacrificed **after** he achieved **atonement, it does not require loaves.**

The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yochanan must be speaking of vlad todah chova — the offspring of an obligatory todah. In this case, there is a personal obligation, the todah gives birth, and the question is whether the offspring may be used to discharge the obligation and whether it requires bread.

The ruling: לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה טָעוּן לֶחֶם, לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אֵין טָעוּן לֶחֶם. Before the mother has been offered — lifnei kapara — the vlad requires bread. After the mother has been offered — le'achar kapara — the vlad does not. The Gemara notes that one might have assumed the opposite: since the vlad is always a derivative animal, it should never require bread, regardless of whether the mother has been offered. The question then is why it would ever require bread at all.

### Chilufei Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּקָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ. הָוֵי בַּהּ נָמֵי אַבָּיֵי כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to this explanation, **what is** Rav Ḥanina **teaching us** by sending this letter? He teaches us **that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds: A person achieves atonement with the enhancement of consecrated** property, even though it is not the initial consecrated property. If one sacrifices the offspring of an obligatory thanks-offering before the thanks-offering itself, he achieves atonement with the offspring, even though it is an enhancement of the consecrated thanks-offering. Accordingly, if he offers it before its mother, it requires loaves. The Gemara notes: **Abaye also discusses** the letter sent by Rav Ḥanina **in this way** and reaches conclusions similar to those of Rav Amram.

The answer lies in Rabbi Yochanan's foundational principle: דְּקָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּשֶׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ — a person can achieve atonement through the increase of hekdesh. When a sanctified animal gives birth, the offspring represents a natural increase, a shevach hekdesh. Rabbi Yochanan holds that in the case of an obligatory offering, this natural increase has the power to satisfy the owner's obligation — the offspring can serve, in effect, as the obligatory sacrifice itself. It is, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, akin to the principle of ubar yerech imo, the fetus as an extension of the mother: whether the animal became pregnant before or after it was sanctified (as Tosafot clarifies), the offspring carries sufficient connection to the original sanctity to discharge the obligation.

Consequently, if one brings the vlad of an obligatory todah before the mother has been offered, that vlad is functioning as the todah itself — it is satisfying the personal obligation — and it therefore requires bread. However, once the mother has been brought and the obligation discharged, the vlad is no more than a derivative animal with todah kedushah; it is le'achar kapara and receives no bread. This is the precise distinction Rabbi Yochanan is drawing.

### Chatas vs. Todah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חִילּוּפֵי תוֹדָה נְדָבָה, בֵּין לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה בֵּין לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה – טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, מַרְבֶּה בְּתוֹדוֹת הוּא. וְלַד תּוֹדָה נְדָבָה, בֵּין לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה בֵּין לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה – אֵין טָעוּן לֶחֶם, מוֹתַר דְּתוֹדָה הוּא. וְולַד תּוֹדָה חוֹבָה, לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה – טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם, לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה – אֵין טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the conclusions of Rav Amram and Abaye, it **was also stated: Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one achieves atonement with **the replacement for a voluntary thanks offering, whether** the thanks offering was found **before** he achieved **atonement or after** he achieved **atonement,** the replacement **requires loaves,** as the owner **is** considered one who **increases thanks offerings. The offspring of a voluntary thanks offering, whether** it was sacrificed **before** he achieved **atonement or after** he achieved **atonement, does not require loaves,** as offspring **is** considered **a leftover of the thanks offering. And** with regard to **the offspring of an obligatory thanks offering,** if it was sacrificed **before** he achieved **atonement, it requires loaves,** but if it was sacrificed **after** he achieved **atonement, it does not require loaves.**

Abaye independently investigated which case Rabbi Yochanan's statement was addressing and arrived at the same conclusion: the reference is to vlad todah chova. This is confirmed by a parallel citation — אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — which explicitly states the relevant case. Chalufei todah nedava, the substitute for a free-will todah, requires bread both before and after — מַרְבֶּה בְּתוֹדוֹת הוּא, each is simply an additional todah. But vlad todah chova: לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם.

## Shmuel's Principle

### Five Chatot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל שֶׁבְּחַטָּאת מֵתָה – בְּתוֹדָה אֵין טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, כֹּל שֶׁבְּחַטָּאת רוֹעָה – בַּתּוֹדָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional *halakhot* with regard to the loaves of a thanks offering that was lost and another animal was taken as its replacement: **Shmuel says:** In **any** situation **in which a sin offering** would be placed in isolation for it **to die,** if that same situation occurs **with a thanks offering, it does not require loaves.** And in **any** situation **in which a sin offering** would be placed in the field to **graze** until it develops a blemish, if that same situation occurs **with a thanks offering, it requires loaves.**

Shmuel draws a direct correspondence between the laws of the chatas and the todah: any scenario in which a chatas animal must be left to die has its parallel in a todah scenario where the animal does not require bread; and any scenario in which a chatas animal is left to graze until it develops a blemish and is then redeemed has its parallel in a todah scenario where the animal does require bread.

### Rav Amram's Q

The five types of chatas that must die are encoded in the acronym ViTiMNA. Vav stands for vlad chatas — the offspring of a chatas, which cannot be brought for the sin and must be left to die. Tet stands for temuras chatas — the temurah of a chatas, which likewise cannot be used for the sin and must die. Mem stands for mesu ba'aleha — cases where the owners have died, rendering the offering purposeless with respect to the sin. Nun stands for nevda — the animal became lost, a substitute was sanctified and used, and the original was then found; one was brought and the other must die. Ayin stands for avar shnato — the animal has passed its first year and is therefore no longer eligible as a chatas.

Of these five, the ayin — avar shnato — is simply not relevant to the todah, since a todah has no such age requirement. The remaining four categories — vlad, temurah, nevda (the niskaper ba'acher case), and mesu ba'aleha — in principle map onto todah scenarios where bread would not be required.

### Rebbe vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב עַמְרָם: מַהוּ אוֹמֵר ״הַתּוֹדָה יַקְרִיב״? מִנַּיִן לְמַפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת – מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה יַקְרִיב וְלַחְמָהּ עִמָּהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַתּוֹדָה יַקְרִיב״. יָכוֹל תְּהֵא שְׁנִיָּה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ״ – אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram raises an objection** from the *baraita* cited on 79b: **What is it** that the verse teaches when it **states: He sacrifices for a thanks offering? From where** is it derived that **one who separated** an animal as **his thanks offering and it was lost and he separated another in its stead, and the first animal was** then **found, and** now **they both stand** fit to be sacrificed, **from where** is it derived **that he may sacrifice whichever** one **of them he wishes, and its loaves** are brought along **with it? The verse states: He sacrifices for a thanks offering.** One **might** have thought **that the second** animal also **requires loaves** to be brought with it. **The verse states: "He sacrifices it,"** indicating that only **one** thanks offering requires loaves, **but not two.**

Rav Amram raises an objection against Shmuel by citing the baraita with which the daf opens: the case of הִפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָתוֹ וְאָבְדָה וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, where one brings whichever animal he wishes and the second receives no bread. The baraita appears to confirm Shmuel's correspondence: the second todah gets no bread, just as by the parallel chatas the second would die.

The objection, however, rests on a closer examination. By a chatas in that same scenario, there is actually a dispute. The baraita regarding chatas — הִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו וְנִמְצְאָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וַהֲרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם עוֹמְדִין — records a dispute. Rebbe says: you bring whichever you wish and the second must die. But the case in question is where both animals are found simultaneously, before either has been offered.

### Shmuel & R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי חַטָּאת כִּי הַאי גַּוְנָא רוֹעָה, דִּתְנַן: הִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת – מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן וּשְׁנִיָּה תָּמוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין חַטָּאת מֵתָה אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים, הָא קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים – רוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Amram continues: **Whereas with regard to a sin offering in a case like this,** the Rabbis hold that the animal **is** placed in the field to **graze, as we learned** in a mishna (*Temura* 22b): If **one separated his sin offering and it was lost, and he separated another** animal **in its stead, and the first** sin offering **was found, and both** animals **stand** fit for sacrifice, then he **achieves atonement with one of them and the second** animal **shall** be left to **die;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: A sin offering** is left to **die only** if it **was found after its owner achieved atonement.** It may be inferred from the opinion of the Rabbis **that** if it is found **before the owner achieved atonement,** it is placed in the field to **graze.**

The Chachamim disagree with Rebbe. The Chachamim hold that a chatas dies only when it was found after the owners were already atoned through the second animal — אֵין חַטָּאת מֵתָה אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים. If the first was found before either was offered — kodem sheniskapru — then since both were still potentially usable, the unused one does not die; rather, it grazes. According to the Chachamim, then, the case of niskaper ba'acher where both animals are simultaneously present is a case of ro'eh, not mesa. Yet by a todah the second animal does not get bread. This would violate Shmuel's principle: a case of ro'eh by chatas should correspond to bread by todah, yet here by todah there is no bread.

### Shmuel's Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה – מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Shmuel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said:** An animal separated as a sin offering that was **lost at the time of the separation** of its replacement, even if it was found before the replacement was sacrificed, is left to **die.** The principle stated by Shmuel is therefore correct, since in every situation in which a sin offering is left to die, a thanks offering does not require loaves.

The Gemara resolves that שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ — Shmuel holds like Rebbe. Shmuel does not distinguish between whether the first animal was found before or after the second was offered; in any scenario where niskaper ba'acher occurs, the unused chatas dies. This aligns perfectly with our case: whether both todot are standing before you simultaneously or one is found afterward, the one not used for the obligation does not get bread, and the corresponding chatas would die.

### Mesu Ba'aleha Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רוֹעָה, לְרַבִּי הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כִּדְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הִפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת, מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תִּרְעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that even in such a case the second sin offering is left to die, **how can you find** a case where the sin offering is placed in the field to **graze according to** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? The Gemara responds: It can be found in a case **like that of Rabbi Oshaya, as Rabbi Oshaya says:** If **one separated two sin offerings,** one to achieve atonement for his sin and the other **as a guarantee** in case the first one is lost, **he may achieve atonement with whichever of them he wishes, and the second shall** be placed in the field to **graze.**

If Shmuel holds like Rebbe, a new question arises: what scenario would Rebbe recognize as a case of ro'eh, where the unused chatas grazes rather than dies? If Rebbe says that any case of niskaper ba'acher — in any configuration — results in the unused animal dying, it is difficult to find a chatas case that he would classify as ro'eh at all.

The Gemara answers with the scenario of Rav Oshaya: הִפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תִּרְעֶה. Here, from the very outset, one separates two animals explicitly as backups for each other, with the intention that either may fulfill the obligation. Neither was lost; neither was designated as a replacement for a lost animal. Both were sanctified simultaneously as joint candidates for the obligation. In this case, the unused animal grazes, not dies — because, unlike the niskaper ba'acher scenario where one animal was temporarily unavailable, here both were always fully present and designated from the start.

However, the Gemara notes that in the todah parallel to this scenario — where two todot were separated together from the outset — the second still does not receive bread. The verse הַתּוֹדָה teaches that whatever the configuration, if two animals are competing to fill the same obligatory slot, only one receives bread. Shmuel's principle breaks down here: this is a case where the chatas grazes, yet the todah still does not get bread.

### Rabbi Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא גַּבֵּי תוֹדָה כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא אֵין טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, אֶלָּא שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת מֵתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But in a case like this** involving **a thanks offering,** the animal **does not require loaves.** Therefore, if Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that in such a case the sin offering is placed in the field to graze, then Shmuel cannot hold in accordance with his opinion, since according to Shmuel, in circumstances where a sin offering would be left to graze, a thanks offering in the same circumstances would require loaves. **Rather, Shmuel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said:** There are **five sin offerings** that are left to **die;** one is a sin offering that was lost and found before its replacement was sacrificed, and another is the case where a sin offering was separated as a guarantee.

The Gemara therefore concludes that שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ — Shmuel holds like Rabbi Shimon, who maintains that in all five chatas cases, without exception, the animal dies. Rabbi Shimon would apply the ruling of mesa to any scenario of niskaper ba'acher, including Rav Oshaya's case. Under this approach, there is no case of ro'eh for Shmuel, and his first principle — that wherever a chatas dies, the todah does not get bread — maps cleanly onto the Mishnah and baraita without contradiction.

But this raises a different problem: if Rabbi Shimon recognizes no ro'eh case at all, why did Shmuel state the second half of his principle — that wherever a chatas grazes, the todah does get bread? The Gemara answers: שְׁמוּאֵל נַמִּי חֲדָא קָאָמַר — Shmuel was in fact only stating the first part of his statement. He said only כָּל שֶׁבְּחַטָּאת מֵתָה בְּתוֹדָה אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם. Yet even this formulation seems to be teaching nothing new, since the Mishnah and baraita already cover the relevant cases. The Gemara therefore explains: לְאַפּוּקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — the purpose of Shmuel's statement is to reject Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yochanan holds that a person can achieve kapara through shevach hekdesh and therefore that the vlad of an obligatory todah, brought before the mother, requires bread. The vav of ViTiMNA stands for vlad chatas — since the vlad of a chatas must die, its counterpart in a todah does not get bread. Shmuel thereby rejects Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: דְּקָא מַשְׁמַע לָן דְּלָא — the vlad todah does not get bread even lifnei kapara.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רוֹעָה, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** it cannot be that Shmuel holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, since **Rabbi Shimon does not hold** that there are **any** circumstances in which a sin offering is placed in the field to **graze.** Accordingly, if Shmuel holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, how can he say: And in any situation in which a sin offering would be placed in the field to graze until it develops a blemish, if that same situation occurs with a thanks offering, it requires loaves?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי חֲדָא קָאָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁבְּחַטָּאת מֵתָה – בְּתוֹדָה אֵין טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: In fact, **Shmuel also stated** only **one** principle: In **any** situation **in which a sin offering** is placed in isolation for it **to die,** if that same situation occurs **with a thanks offering, it does not require loaves.** He did not state the second principle. The Gemara asks: **What is** Shmuel **teaching us?** The Gemara responds: Shmuel teaches us his statement **to exclude** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said: A person achieves atonement with the enhancement of consecrated** property. According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, the offspring of an obligatory thanks offering requires loaves if it is sacrificed before its mother. Shmuel **teaches us that** one does **not** achieve atonement with the enhancement of consecrated property, and the offspring of a thanks offering does not require loaves, which corresponds to the fact that the offspring of a sin offering is left to die.

### Owner-Possessed

Strikingly, the Gemara does not explore the case of mesu ba'aleha as it applies to the todah — that is, where the owners have died, a scenario in which a chatas must die. On the face of it, this presents a potential challenge to Shmuel's principle: why should the death of the owners prevent a todah from requiring bread? A todah is not inherently tied to an act of sin; there exists such a thing as a todas nedavah, a free-will todah entirely uncoupled from personal circumstance. It would seem logical that even if the owners died, the todah should still carry its bread requirement.

The Yerushalmi, in fact, takes exactly this position — that a todah whose owners died does require bread — which would constitute a direct contradiction to Shmuel's parallel. This question is not addressed anywhere in the Bavli, and it is notable that no Rishonim appear to have raised it explicitly. One commentator on the Yerushalmi does ask the question, but the silence in the Bavli remains striking.

## Todah & Bread

### Rava's Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: זוֹ תּוֹדָה וְזוֹ לַחְמָהּ, אָבַד הַלֶּחֶם – מֵבִיא לֶחֶם אַחֵר, אָבְדָה תּוֹדָה – אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא תּוֹדָה אַחֶרֶת. מַאי טַעְמָא? לֶחֶם לִגְלַל תּוֹדָה, וְאֵין תּוֹדָה לִגְלַל לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the loaves of a thanks offering, **Rabbi Abba says:** If one volunteered to bring a thanks offering, and said: **This** animal **is a thanks offering and this** flour **is** designated for **its loaves,** then if **the loaves were lost, he brings other loaves.** If **the thanks offering was lost, he does not bring another thanks offering,** and the loaves are not sacrificed. **What is the reason** for this? **The loaves are** brought **on account of the thanks offering;** therefore, if there is no thanks offering, there are no loaves. **But the thanks offering is not** brought **on account of the loaves;** consequently, if the loaves were lost, the thanks offering is still sacrificed, and one brings different loaves.

Rabbi Abba introduces a principle that clarifies the nature of the relationship between the todah and its bread: זוֹ תּוֹדָה וְזוֹ לַחְמָהּ — if one separates out an animal and declares it a todah, then separately designates bread or flour for the accompanying lechem. אָבַד הַלֶּחֶם מֵבִיא לֶחֶם אַחֵר — if the bread is lost, one may bring substitute bread for the todah. But אָבְדָה תוֹדָה אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא תוֹדָה אַחֶרֶת — if the todah itself is lost, one may not bring another todah for the purpose of using this bread. The originally designated bread cannot simply be transferred to a new todah.

### Rav Kahana

The reasoning is: לֶחֶם לְגַבֵּי תוֹדָה וְאֵין תוֹדָה לְגַבֵּי לֶחֶם. The bread is an appurtenance of the todah; the todah is the possessor and the bread is the possessed. Substitute bread may be brought to serve the todah, because it is the todah that stands as the primary entity and may receive new bread. But if the todah is lost, the designated bread has lost its anchor; it is linked specifically to that todah and cannot be redirected to serve a different one.

### Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לְתוֹדָתוֹ וְנִתּוֹתְרוּ – מֵבִיא בָּהֶן לֶחֶם. לְלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה וְנִתּוֹתְרוּ – אֵין מֵבִיא בָּהֶן תּוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **And** with regard to the distinctions between a thanks offering and its accompanying loaves, **Rava says:** In the case of one who **separated money for his thanks offering** and some of the money **remained** after he purchased the offering, **he brings,** i.e., purchases, **with** the remaining money **loaves** to accompany the thanks offering. If he separated money **for the loaves of the thanks offering and** some of the money **remained** after he purchased the loaves, **he may not bring a thanks offering with** the remaining money.

Rava applies the same principle to money. If one sets aside funds for his todah and there is leftover money — מֵבִיא בָּהֶן לֶחֶם — the surplus may be used to purchase the accompanying bread, since the bread is encompassed within the todah designation. If, however, one sets aside funds specifically for the bread of his todah and there is leftover money — אֵין מֵבִיא בָּהֶן תּוֹדָה — that surplus may not be used to purchase the todah animal. Money designated for bread cannot be redirected to the todah itself.

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִנַּיִן לְלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ ״תּוֹדָה״? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת מַצּוֹת״. אִי הָכִי, אִיפְּכָא נָמֵי! לֶחֶם אִיקְּרִי ״תּוֹדָה״, תּוֹדָה לָא אִיקְּרַי ״לֶחֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? If we say** that it is **due to** the statement **of Rav Kahana,** that is difficult. **As Rav Kahana said: From where** is it derived that **the loaves of a thanks offering are** themselves **called a thanks offering?** It is derived from **that** which **is stated** in the verse: **"Then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanks offering loaves"** (Leviticus 7:12). The juxtaposition of the words "thanks offering" and "loaves" indicates that the loaves are themselves called a thanks offering. Therefore, one who separated money for a thanks offering may use that money for the loaves as well. The Gemara explains the difficulty: **If that is so, the opposite** should **also** be the *halakha*, i.e., the thanks offering is called loaves, and it should therefore be permitted to use the money remaining from the loaves for the thanks offering. The Gemara rejects this: **The loaves are called a thanks offering,** but **the thanks offering is not called loaves.**

Rav Kahana grounds this asymmetry in a verse: וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת מַצּוֹת. Reading הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת מַצּוֹת — the challot matzot are themselves part of the todah — Rav Kahana derives that the bread is subsumed under the name and category of the todah. Hence, money designated for the todah includes money for the bread, since the bread is a component of the todah.

### Rava's Analysis

The Gemara resolves this with a precise formulation: לֶחֶם אִיקְרֵיא תוֹדָה, תּוֹדָה לָא אִיקְרֵיא לֶחֶם. The bread may be called todah — it is part of the todah — but the todah is not called bread. Money designated for the todah encompasses everything that goes by the name todah, including the bread. But money designated for bread encompasses only what goes by the name lechem, and the animal does not fall within that category. The bread is an extension of the animal, not the reverse.

## Triple Todot

### First Offered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הִפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְחָזַר וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְאָבְדָה, וְחָזַר וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְנִמְצְאוּ הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת, וַהֲרֵי שְׁלׇשְׁתָּן עוֹמְדוֹת. נִתְכַּפֵּר בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה – שְׁנִיָּה אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, שְׁלִישִׁית טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rava says:** If **one separated** an animal as **his thanks offering and it was lost, and he again separated another in its stead, and it** too **was lost, and he again separated another in its stead, and the first** two animals **were** then **found, and the three of them stand** fit to be sacrificed, the *halakha* is as follows: If **he achieved atonement with the first** animal, **the second does not require loaves,** as it is the replacement for the first, and the first was sacrificed with its loaves. But **the third requires loaves,** because it is the replacement for the second, which does not require loaves.

Rava now presents an elaborate scenario. A person has an obligatory todah. The first animal is designated and then lost; a second animal is designated in its stead. The second is then also lost; a third animal is designated in the stead of the second. At this point, the first and second animals are both found, and all three stand together: number one, number two (which had been a substitute for number one), and number three (which had been a substitute for number two). The question is: whichever of the three is used to fulfill the obligation, how do the remaining two fare regarding the bread requirement?

### Third Offered

If one uses number one to fulfill the obligation, number two does not receive bread. Number two had been designated as a substitute for number one; they were both competing for the same obligatory slot. Since number one was brought and filled that slot, number two is not the actual todah, and does not get bread. Number three, however, does get bread. Number three had been designated as a substitute for number two. But with the benefit of hindsight, number two was never going to be the obligatory todah — its place was taken by number one. Number three, as a substitute for a non-obligatory animal, is therefore simply an additional todah being brought — מַרְבֶּה בְּתוֹדוֹת הוּא — and as such it receives bread. Notably, this means a derivative animal receives bread while the animal for which it was substituting does not — a striking result.

### Middle Offered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּפֵּר בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית – שְׁנִיָּה אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, רִאשׁוֹנָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם;
>
> **Translation:** If **he achieved atonement with the third,** then **the second does not require loaves,** because its replacement, i.e., the third, was sacrificed with loaves, but **the first requires loaves,** because its replacement, i.e., the second, will be sacrificed without loaves.

Conversely, if one uses number three to fulfill the obligation, number two again does not receive bread — this time because number two and number three were both competing for the same slot, and number three won out. Number one, however, does receive bread. Number one was the original todah, standing entirely on its own, not competing with any other animal for the obligation at the time it was designated. It receives bread in its own right.

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאֶמְצָעִית – שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֵין טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם;
>
> **Translation:** If he achieved atonement **with the middle,** i.e., the second animal, **both of them,** i.e., the first and the third, **do not require loaves,**

If one uses number two — the middle animal — then שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֵינָן טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם, neither number one nor number three receives bread. Number two was competing with number one for the first obligatory slot, and it was also the animal for which number three was designated as a substitute, placing two and three in competition for the second slot. Since number two fills the obligation, both number one (the other competitor for the obligation) and number three (whose principal, number two, turned out to be the actual todah) are left without bread.

### Abaye Simpler

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן – שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֵין טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם, כּוּלְּהוּ חֲלִיפִין דַּהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Even if he achieved atonement with** any **one of them,** the other **two do not require loaves,** as **they are all replacements for one another.**

Abaye offers a simpler and more sweeping analysis. אֲפִלּוּ נִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֵינָן טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם — regardless of which of the three animals is used, the remaining two never receive bread. כּוּלְּהוּ חֲלִיפֵי דַּהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ — all three are, in effect, substitutes for one another. Even though number three was designated as a substitute for number two and not directly for number one, the entire chain of substitution was directed toward satisfying the same single obligation. All three were competing, at some level, for the same box. Whichever animal enters that box, the other two are not the actual todah per se and therefore receive no bread.
