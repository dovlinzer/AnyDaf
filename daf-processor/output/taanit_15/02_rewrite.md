# Taanit 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek Seder Ta'aniyot

### Ch. Overview

With the opening of the new *perek*, the focus of the tractate shifts markedly. Whereas the previous chapter concerned itself primarily with the mechanics of fasting — the structure of the three, three, and seven *ta'aniyot tzibbur* — this chapter turns to what might be called the liturgical and communal architecture of the fast day. The operative verse, *kar'u tzom kidshu atzara*, calls not only for a fast but for a day of gathering, *tefillot*, and communal self-assessment. That is the animating concern of *Seder Ta'aniyot*: not merely that one fasts, but how the entire order of the day is organized as a communal experience.

### Mishna Structure

The printers of the Talmud made the unusual decision to gather all the *mishnayot* of this *perek* into a single extended first *mishna*. What follows, then, is less a single discrete teaching than a compendium — essentially all six or seven *mishnayot* of the chapter stitched together. The *mishna* opens with the question *Seder Ta'aniyot keitzad* — "What is the order of the fast days?" — a formulation that recalls the *Pesach Seder*: not what we do, but in what sequence, according to what agenda, do we do it.

## Ark & Ashes

### Teiva & Eifer

The first element of the order is *motzi'in et hateiva* — they bring out the ark to *rechovah shel ir*, the public square of the city. The *rechov* here is not a side street but the central public gathering place. The *Gemara* will later clarify, through a *beraita*, that this ritual belongs to the final seven fasts rather than the earlier ones, but the *mishna* itself does not specify; it describes the order of the day without identifying to which tier of fasts it applies.

Once the ark is brought out, the community places *eifer mikleh* — ashes of something burnt — upon it. One might ask why the *mishna* specifies *eifer mikleh* rather than simply *eifer*. *Rashi* addresses this directly: *eifer mamash velo afar* — we mean actual ashes, not dust. The reason precision is required is that the word *eifer* on its own can equally denote dust, as the verse *me'afar sereifat hachatat* demonstrates, where *afar* clearly refers to ashes. Conversely, *afar* can be called *eifer*, as in *Avraham*'s declaration *v'anochi afar va'eifer*. Because the two terms bleed into one another, the modifier *mikleh* — of something burnt — is necessary to establish unambiguously that actual fire-produced ashes are intended.

### Leadership Mourning

The ashes are placed not only upon the ark but *u'verosh hanasi u'verosh av beit din* — upon the head of the *nasi* and the head of the *av beit din*. In the context of *Eretz Yisrael* this would mean figures like *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* as the political head, alongside the *av beit din* as the rabbinic head — the model of dual leadership represented by the *zugot* who preceded *Hillel* and *Shammai*, in which one served as *nasi* and the other as *av beit din*.

A natural question arises: does this happen only in the city where these offices are actually located, or does it occur everywhere? The *mishna* does not resolve this explicitly. It seems most reasonable to understand it as describing whoever occupies the analogous roles of political and religious leadership in each locale — the *nasi* and *av beit din* functioning as designations of role-type rather than of specific individuals. The placement of ashes on these figures carries a clear message: communal crisis is ultimately a matter of leadership responsibility. If things are going wrong in the community, the process of *me'aynin b'milei d'mata* — assessing what the community has done wrong — places the burden squarely on those at the top.

Why ashes on the *aron* itself? One reading is purely functional and psychological: having the symbol prominently in front of the assembled community, facing the ark, drives home the weight of the moment. But there may be a deeper resonance. *Chazal*'s principle of *imo anochi b'tzara* — God too suffers when Israel suffers — suggests that the ashes on the ark may symbolize God's participation in the mourning. If the relationship between the community and God has been weakened, the distancing is felt on both sides. The ashes on the *aron* may be a way of expressing precisely this: we are all in mourning, God included.

### Individual Ashes

*V'chol echad v'echad noten b'rosho* — every individual also places ashes on his own head. The practice of placing ashes on the head as a sign of mourning has parallels elsewhere: some communities maintain the *minhag* of a *chatan* placing ashes on his forehead at a wedding, evoking *na'aleh Yerushalayim al rosh simchatenu*. The symbolic resonance here is the mourning of the community as a whole — not just the leadership, not just the ark, but every person present. We are all experiencing this moment of rupture and distance. The burnt quality of the ashes — *Rashi*'s insistence on *eifer mamash* — reinforces the sense of destruction and tragedy, evoking what has been lost and what is at stake.

The communal mourning is oriented toward a specific grief: not an abstract misfortune, but the weakening of the relationship with God. If the process ultimately reaches the stage of *k'vnei adam hanizufim laMakom*, the community is in full mourning over that distance. Even at this earlier stage, that horizon is present and felt. The fasting, the ashes, the gathering — all are means of translating that interior grief into a communal posture that can become the basis for *teshuvah* and renewed connection.

### Outdoor Ritual

The decision to bring the *teiva* outdoors — rather than remaining in the *shul* — carries its own significance. Practically, the public square may simply be large enough to hold the entire community in a way that an interior space cannot. The outdoor setting also has the quality of *parhesia*, a public declaration that heightens the seriousness of what is being done.

But there is yet another dimension worth considering. There is a tradition that the *Torah* should not remain secluded in the *beit knesset* or *beit midrash* — that it ought to be brought into the wider life of the community. Read in that light, one element of what may have gone wrong, prompting the fast in the first place, is that the *Torah* has not been integrated into public life as deeply as it should be. Bringing the *teiva* out into the square may thus be not merely a logistical or dramatic choice, but a symbolic statement: the *Torah* belongs here, in the midst of the people, and its displacement into a purely private or institutional domain is itself part of what we are mourning.

## Hazaken's Drasha

### Words of Kibushan

*Hazakein shebahem* — the elder among them, who need not be the *nasi* or the *av beit din* but may simply be the appropriate figure for this role, perhaps a skilled *darshan* — stands before the assembled community and delivers *divrei kivushin*. *Rashi* traces the word to the pressing of olives: words that press, that humble, that stir people toward honest self-reflection.

The substance of the *drasha* turns on a pointed reading of the *Yonah* narrative. *Lo ne'emar beanshei Ninveh vayar elohim et sakam ve'et ta'anitam* — it does not say that God saw the sackcloth and fasting of the Ninevites. *Ella vayar elohim et ma'aseihem ki shavu midarkam hara'ah* — it says God saw their deeds, that they had turned from their evil way. The lesson is explicit: the external practices are not the point. God responds to what people actually do, to the change in behavior, not to the performance of ritual acts.

### Rend Your Heart

The *drasha* continues with a verse from the prophets: *vekiru levavchem ve'al bigdeichem* — rend your hearts, not your garments. Whatever is being done outwardly must ultimately express a real interior rupture, a genuine *teshuvah*.

One might ask why the *mishna* introduces the second quotation with the phrase *uvakabala hu omer* when both *Yonah* and *Yoel* belong to the *Trei Asar* and are therefore from the same prophetic corpus. The likely explanation is structural: the first quotation is embedded in a rhetorical contrast (*lo ne'emar...ella*) that flows naturally into the *pasuk* itself. To insert *uvakabala hu omer* there would disrupt the rhetorical rhythm. The second quotation, by contrast, needs a transitional phrase — "and it further says" — and that is precisely what *uvakabala* provides.

There is also a view that *kabala* is used specifically when *pesukim* from *Nach* function as direct *mussar* — explicit moral instruction — rather than as narrative from which a lesson is drawn. The *Yonah* passage is narrative; the verse *vekiru levavchem* is directly admonitory. Whether or not that distinction accounts for the terminology here, it is worth noting.

One further observation deserves attention. The *hazakein* speaks of sackcloth, fasting, and rending of garments — yet the community has, at this point in the order, only been fasting. Sackcloth never becomes an obligatory *halachic* practice, despite its pervasive presence throughout *Tanakh*. Rending of garments is similarly not part of the formal ritual. The exhortation not to rend garments but to rend the heart is addressed, in a sense, to a community that was not actually rending garments to begin with — which makes the contrast all the more pointed.

### Teshuva Focus

It is worth pausing to note that this *drasha* of the *hazakein* represents essentially the only place in the entire *masechet* where *teshuvah* is explicitly raised as a central theme. That is striking. One might be tempted to conclude that *teshuvah* is therefore the whole point of *ta'anit tzibbur*, but the weight of that conclusion should be measured carefully. *Teshuvah* is certainly one element, and here it is foregrounded, but the larger context of the *mishna* — the *tefillot*, the communal gathering, the reaching out to God — suggests a broader range of concerns. This *drasha* is the one moment where the internal dimension is explicitly named.

## Prayer Leader

### Zakein Veragil

With the *drasha* concluded, the community rises for *tefillah* — *amdu bitfilah*. Whether this is *shacharit* or *mincha* is not entirely clear from the *mishna*. It is worth recalling that the earlier *Gemara* described the first half of the fast day as devoted to *milei demata* — communal assessment — with the latter half devoted to *tefillah*, which might suggest *mincha*. On the other hand, in the *Gemara*'s conception of *shacharit*, the service consisted essentially of *birchos krias shema* and *shemoneh esrei*, without *pesukei dezimrah* or *birchos hashachar* as part of the synagogue liturgy. It is therefore not impossible that the *drasha* immediately preceded *shemoneh esrei* of *shacharit*. The question remains open.

For this *tefillah*, the community appoints a *shliach tzibbur* who is *moridim lifnei hateivah zakein veragil* — elderly and accustomed to leading prayer, someone who knows how to *daven* without error and who can bring genuine feeling to the role. The elderly prayer leader carries natural authority and the perspective that comes with years and experience.

### Qualifications

The *mishna* specifies two additional qualities. First, *vayeish lo banim* — he should have children. The significance here is the development of a capacity for genuine compassion: someone who knows what it means to care deeply for others and to feel responsibility for their wellbeing is better positioned to represent the *tzibbur* in its moment of need and to intercede on their behalf.

Second, *uveiso reikam* — his house should be empty. *Rashi*'s understanding is that this refers to a lack of material possessions, so that the *shliach tzibbur* approaches God from a place of genuine humility rather than self-sufficiency. The *Gemara* will offer an alternative explanation. The overarching purpose of these qualities is captured in the *mishna*'s concluding phrase: *kedei sheyehei libo shaleim bitfilah* — so that his heart should be whole and fully present in the prayer.

### Rent vs. Whole

This formulation creates a striking contrast with the *drasha* that immediately preceded it. The *hazakein* quoted *vekiru levavchem* — rend your hearts. The *shliach tzibbur* is chosen so that *libo shaleim* — his heart is whole. Is the heart rent or whole?

The tension may be productive rather than contradictory. One reads the rent heart as the work of *teshuvah* and self-examination — cutting through the armor of habit and self-justification to arrive at genuine feeling. The whole heart then describes the mode of one who, having done that work, turns fully toward God in prayer. The two states correspond to two distinct moments in the spiritual process: first the rupture of honest self-reckoning, then the wholeness of complete devotion. Both the *hazakein* and the *shliach tzibbur* are *zekeinim*, but their roles call forth different qualities of heart.

## 24 Brachot

### Six Additions

The *shliach tzibbur* leads the *chazarat hashatz* — the repetition of *shemoneh esrei* — not in the usual form of eighteen (or nineteen) *brachot* but with six additional ones: *u'mosif aleihem od sheish*. The eighteen standard *brachot* serve as the base; these six are added in the repetition only. They are inserted in the same place that *Anenu* is inserted on ordinary fast days — between *goel* and *rofei*. If *Anenu* represents the brief fast-day supplement, these six *brachot* represent the full elaboration for when the situation has reached its most serious stage.

### Zichronot/Shofrot

The first two of the six additions are *zichronot* and *shofrot* — immediately evoking *Rosh Hashanah* and the *musaf* liturgy of that day. The connection is not coincidental. The *Gemara* earlier in the tractate had already noted that *ta'aniyot* and *Rosh Hashanah* share structural parallels in their *brachot*, and the *mishna* in *Rosh Hashanah* had discussed the *shofar* and *chatzotzrot* in juxtaposition with these very themes.

*Zichronot*, with the *chatima* of *zocher habrit* — God, remember the covenant — speaks directly to the anxiety of distance. We are asking God to recall the bond that ties us together, the relationship that must not be abandoned even now. *Shofrot*, in our familiar *nusach* of *shomei'a kol teruat amo Yisrael b'rachamim*, frames the *shofar* blast as Israel's attempt to reach God and asks God to listen. Since the *mishna* will describe actual *shofar* blowing as part of this service, the liturgical content and the ritual act reinforce each other: the *tefillah* speaks of the *shofar* and the *shofar* sounds during the *tefillah*. *Rashi* notes that these *brachot* would include the full complement of *pesukim* drawn from *zichronot* and *shofrot*, giving them the full textual weight of their *Rosh Hashanah* counterparts.

The remaining four *brachot* are constituted from *prakim b'Tehillim*. The third addition opens with *el Hashem batzarata li karati vaya'aneini* — I called to God in my distress and He answered me. The fourth is *essa einai el heharim* — I lift my eyes to the mountains; whence comes my help? The fifth is *mima'amakim keraticha Hashem* — from the depths I have called to You. The sixth is *tefillah l'ani ki ya'atof* — a prayer of the afflicted man when he grows faint, as he pours out his speech before God.

The thematic unity of these four *prakim* is unmistakable: in each, a person in distress turns entirely toward God and calls out for help. Notably, none of these *pesukim* speaks to the theme of *teshuvah* or the acknowledgment of sin. They speak of *tefillah*, of calling out, of God's capacity to hear and answer. This observation will prove relevant to the dispute that follows.

### Rabbi Yehuda

*Rabbi Yehuda omer lo haya tzarich lomar zichronot v'shofrot* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *zichronot* and *shofrot* should not be recited. He may have felt that these *brachot* were too evocative of *Rosh Hashanah* and inappropriate in this different liturgical context. In their place, Rabbi Yehuda substitutes different texts.

### Psalmic Texts

The substitutions Rabbi Yehuda proposes are: *ra'av ki yihyeh ba'aretz* from *Melachim* — the passage in *Shlomo*'s dedication prayer for the *Beit HaMikdash* — and the passage beginning *asher hayah devar Hashem el Yirmiyahu al divrei habatzarot*, the word of God to *Yirmiyahu* concerning the drought.

To understand why Rabbi Yehuda prefers these texts, it is worth examining their content. The *Melachim* passage recounts *Shlomo*'s prayer at the dedication of the *Beit HaMikdash*, in which he anticipates every form of national crisis — enemy defeat, drought, famine, pestilence — and asks God to hear the prayers that will be directed toward this house. The verses immediately before the explicit mention of *ra'av* describe people who sin, turn to God, acknowledge their wrongdoing, and ask for forgiveness. The dominant language throughout is: because of *cheit*, the people will do *teshuvah*, and therefore God will respond. The final lines affirm that God will hear, forgive, and act — but only because the people have turned from their wrong path.

The *Yirmiyahu* passage similarly opens with the desolation caused by drought (*avlah Yehudah*, *lo hayah geshem ba'aretz*) and then arrives, in *pasuk zayin*, at *im avoneinu anu vanu* — if our sins have testified against us. It follows with *ki rabu meshuvoteinu lecha chatanu* — we have multiplied our turnings-away, we have sinned against You. The cry to God — *mikveh Yisrael Hashem moshio be'et tzarah*, why are you like a stranger to us, why are you like one who cannot save? — is embedded in a context saturated with the acknowledgment of sin.

This stands in sharp contrast to the four *prakim b'Tehillim* endorsed by the *tanna kamma*, which speak exclusively of calling out and being answered, without any element of sin or *teshuvah*. The difference illuminates what is at stake in the dispute. Rabbi Yehuda's position seems to be that what must be foregrounded in this liturgical moment is precisely the acknowledgment of sin and the call to *teshuvah*. The *Rosh Hashanah* theme may not be the wrong mode — it is not entirely inappropriate — but it may not be the most urgent priority. What needs to open and drive these *brachot* is the theme of turning away from sin and returning to God.

### Dispute Analysis

There may be a second dimension to Rabbi Yehuda's position. Recall the earlier discussion in the tractate about whether *matri'in* refers to blowing the *shofar* or to reciting *Anenu*. The possibility arises that Rabbi Yehuda's objection is also, in part, to the emphasis on ritual — particularly the *shofar* — at the expense of the more demanding interior work of *tefillah* and *teshuvah*. Ritual acts like *shofar* blowing have genuine power: they focus attention, stir feeling, and motivate. But they also carry the risk of creating a sense of having discharged one's obligation through external performance. If there is no *shofar* ritual to point to, the community must ask harder questions — have we actually prayed enough, have we actually turned?

It seems likely, then, that two distinct concerns animate Rabbi Yehuda's position: first, that the *Rosh Hashanah* framing be replaced with a framework more directly centered on sin and *teshuvah*; and second, that the emphasis on ritual observance not be permitted to substitute for the deeper interior reckoning that constitutes genuine return.

## Mi She'ana Formulas

### Abraham Formula

After the six *brachot* are recited, each concludes with a *chatima* — a closing formula of the blessing. *V'omer chotmeihen*: and he says their closing lines. The first does not belong to the six new additions but to the standard *goel Yisrael* that precedes them. *Al harishona hu omer: mi she'ana et Avraham Avinu behar haMoriah hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem goel Yisrael.*

This formula — "He who answered Abraham our father at *Har haMoriah* should answer you and hear the sound of your cry today" — is inserted immediately before the standard *chatima* of *goel Yisrael*, functioning precisely as an interpolation that frames and enriches the *chatima* without replacing it. The principle that the last line before a *chatima* shapes the meaning of the *chatima* itself is operative here: by saying the *mi she'ana l'Avraham* formula right before the seal, the *bracha* of *goel Yisrael* now resonates with the plea for direct divine response in a moment of acute need.

This is not only the earliest structural instance of this form of prayer but also the liturgical ancestor of the *selichot* service as we know it today. The *mi she'ana* formulas of *selichot* derive from precisely this *mishna*.

The *chatima* of *goel Yisrael* here is the standard one. Both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda agree on the *chatimot*; the dispute was only about which *pesukim* frame the body of the *brachot*. From the *chatimot* onward, there is no *machloket*.

### Sea Formula

*Al hashniyah hu omer: mi she'ana l'avoteinu al yam suf hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma kol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem zocher hanishkachot.* The one who answered the ancestors at the *Yam Suf* should answer you. The *chatima* is *zocher hanishkachot* — who remembers what has been forgotten.

*Rashi* explains the thematic connection: Israel was in Egypt for many years, seemingly forgotten for generations, to the point that they despaired of redemption — *nitya'ashu min hage'ulah*. Then God remembered them. The *bracha* of *zichronot* and its *chatima* of *zocher hanishkachot* tie precisely into the moment of the *Yam Suf*: what seemed like total abandonment became the moment of the most dramatic remembrance. This is a particularly tight and elegant thematic connection.

### Gilgal Formula

*Al hashlishit hu omer: mi she'ana l'Yehoshua b'Gilgal hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem shome'a teruah.* The one who answered *Yehoshua* at *Gilgal* — which *Rashi* confirms refers to the battle of *Yericho* — should answer you. The *chatima* is *shome'a teruah*.

Here everything aligns cleanly. *Yehoshua* was answered at *Yericho* through the *shofarot* — the walls fell to the blast of the *shofar*. The *bracha* is *shofrot*, the theme is *shofar*, the *mi she'ana* is the paradigmatic instance of *shofar*-led military deliverance, and the *chatima* of *shome'a teruah* seals it all together. This is the most internally consistent of all the formulas.

### Samuel Formula

*Al harevi'it hu omer: mi she'ana li'Shmuel baMitzpah hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem shome'a tze'akah.* The one who answered *Shmuel* at *Mitzpah* should answer you. The *chatima* is *shome'a tze'akah*.

The corresponding *perek* is *el Hashem batzarata li karati vaya'aneini*. The connection between *Shmuel* and *tze'akah* is supported by *Rashi*, who notes that the text by *Shmuel* uses the language of *vayitz'ak el Hashem* — and he cried out to God. The *chatima* of *shome'a tze'akah* thus reflects the specific scriptural language associated with *Shmuel*'s intercession.

### Elijah Formula

*Al hachamishit hu omer: mi she'ana l'Eliyahu b'Har Karmel hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem shome'a tefillah.* The one who answered *Eliyahu* at *Har Karmel* should answer you. The *chatima* is *shome'a tefillah*.

The corresponding *perek* is *essa einai el heharim* — I lift my eyes to the mountains. *Rashi* connects *Eliyahu* to this by observing *keneged asiri harrim* — it corresponds to the theme of mountains, and *Eliyahu ne'eneh b'Har HaCarmel me'ein asiri harrim*. The *har* of *Har Karmel* ties into the *harim* of the *perek*. As for the *chatima* of *shome'a tefillah*, *Rashi* connects it to the text by *Eliyahu* that uses the language of *aneni* — answer me — which he glosses as *zo tefillah*, this is *tefillah*. The connection is present but less tightly woven than in some of the other cases.

### Jonah Formula

*Al hashishit hu omer: mi she'ana et Yonah mib'ei hadagah hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem ha'oneh b'eit tzarah.* The one who answered *Yonah* from the belly of the fish should answer you. The *chatima* is *ha'oneh b'eit tzarah*.

The corresponding *perek* is *mima'amakim keraticha Hashem* — from the depths I have called to You. The tie between *mib'ei hadagah* and *mima'amakim* is vivid and direct: the belly of the fish is the paradigmatic depth. *Rashi* links the *chatima* of *ha'oneh b'eit tzarah* to the language in *Yonah* of *karasi mitzara li* — I called from my distress — confirming the thematic resonance. *Yonah* was preeminently in an *eit tzarah*, and the *chatima* makes that explicit.

### David/Solomon

*Al hashvi'it hu omer: mi she'ana et David u'Shlomo b'no b'Yerushalayim hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh. Baruch atah Hashem hamerachem al ha'aretz.* The one who answered *David* and his son *Shlomo* in *Yerushalayim* should answer you. The *chatima* is *hamerachem al ha'aretz*.

The corresponding *perek* is *tefillah l'ani ki ya'atof* — a prayer of the poor man when he grows faint. The *chatima* of *merachem al ha'aretz* requires explanation. When were *David* and *Shlomo* answered in *Yerushalayim*? The *Gemara* explains: *David* in connection with a famine — *vayehi ra'av bimei David shalosh shanim shana achar shana* — a famine that lasted three consecutive years. *Shlomo* in connection with his bringing the *aron* into the *kodesh hakodashim* at the dedication of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The former is directly relevant to the crisis at hand; the latter evokes the ultimate moment of closeness between God and Israel in *Yerushalayim*.

### Rashi Analysis

*Rashi* explains the *chatima* of *merachem al ha'aretz* as follows: *she'hein hitpalelu al Eretz Yisrael u'tefillah l'ani ki ya'atof lahania dochak geshamim* — because *David* and *Shlomo* prayed for the land of Israel, and the *perek* of *tefillah l'ani* speaks of *be'atzar geshamim*, the withholding of rain. This makes the final *bracha* the most explicitly connected to the actual crisis motivating the fast: the fear of famine, the lack of rain, the land under threat. *Rashi*'s broader comment — *v'chen kulam l'fi inyan hamizmorim* — indicates that the thematic connections between each *mi she'ana* figure, the *perek* assigned to that *bracha*, and the *chatima* are generally intended to cohere, even if some instances are more tightly woven than others.

Stepping back, a pattern emerges across all seven formulas. The *chatimot* move from the generic to the specific. The earlier ones — *goel Yisrael*, *zocher hanishkachot*, *shome'a teruah* — are broad invocations. The later ones become more particular, and the final *chatima* of *merachem al ha'aretz* arrives at the precise, concrete concern: the land, the rain, the famine. This structure reinforces the observation made throughout this *perek*: the dominant theme of these prayers is God's responsiveness to those who call out to Him in distress. The concern about sin and *teshuvah*, however theologically important, is proportionally a smaller thread. The overwhelming weight of the liturgy here is: we call, God answers.

## Maaseh Account

### Shofar Blowing

The *mishna* now records a historical incident. *Maaseh bi'yemei Rebbi Chalafta ve'Rebbi Chananya ben Teradyon she'avar echad lifnei hateivah* — it happened in the days of *Rebbi Chalafta* and *Rebbi Chananya ben Teradyon* that someone came before the ark to lead the service. After he concluded the full text of the first *bracha* — *ve'gamar et haberachah kula* — the community did not respond *amen*. Instead, an announcer called out *tik'u kohanim*, and the *kohanim* blew. Then the *mi she'ana* formula was recited — *mi she'ana et Avraham Avinu behar haMoriah, hu ya'aneh etchem veyishma bekol tza'akatchem hayom hazeh* — and then, separately, came the call *hari'u bnei Aharon hari'u*, and they sounded a *teruah*.

What was deviant about this practice? Several elements: the community did not say *amen* after the *chatima*; the *mi she'ana* formula was separated from the *bracha* and placed after the *chatima* rather than integrated before it; and the blowing was structured so as to give the *mi she'ana* the weight of a separate announcement accompanied by *teki'ot* and *teru'ot*, rather than being embedded within the *bracha*. The effect was something like twelve distinct acts — *brachot*, separate *mi she'ana* formulas, and interleaved *teki'ot* and *teru'ot* — rather than the unified structure the *mishna* prescribes.

### Rabbinic Limit

*Uksheba hadavar eitzel chachamim amru: lo hayu nohagim ken ela besha'ar mizrach uvehar habayit* — when the matter came before the sages, they ruled that this practice was done only at the eastern gate or on the Temple Mount, not outside the precincts of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

What precisely was being censured? The *Gemara* will clarify that the *teki'ot* and *teru'ot* themselves were not the problem — blowing the *shofar* together with the twenty-four *brachot* was standard practice even outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. What the sages objected to was the failure to answer *amen* and, possibly, the structural displacement of the *mi she'ana* formula outside the *bracha* itself. It is, however, quite possible to read the *mishna* differently: a straightforward reading of the text suggests that the blowing itself — the prominent role of the *kohanim* with the *chatzotzrot* or *shofar* — was the practice restricted to the Temple precinct. That reading cannot be dismissed. The *Gemara*'s resolution will address this, but the *mishna* as written leaves the question genuinely open.

## Fasting Levels

### First 3 Fasts

The *mishna* now turns to how the priests in the *Beit HaMikdash* would participate in the communal fasts. The connection to what precedes is not accidental: the *mishna* has just concluded with a reference to *beit hamikdash*, and the previous *bracha* centered on *Shlomo b'Yerushalayim*. The entire *perek* has maintained a thread of connection to the *mikdash*, and now that connection is made explicit in terms of who fasts and to what degree.

According to *Rabbi Yehoshua*: during the first three fasts, *anshei mishmar* — the division of priests on call for that week — would fast but not complete the fast. *Anshei beit av* — those assigned to the service on that specific day — would not fast at all. The logic is clear: their *avodah* took priority, and those actively engaged in the Temple service could not be expected to weaken themselves unnecessarily.

### Middle 3 Fasts

During the middle three fasts, the obligations escalate by one degree. *Anshei mishmar* would now fast and complete the fast — a full-day fast. *Anshei beit av* would fast but not complete it, breaking their fast at some point in the afternoon to sustain themselves for the *avodah*.

### Last 7 Fasts

During the last seven fasts, *elu v'elu mit'anim umashlimim* — both groups would fast completely through the day. This represents the maximal level of communal participation, in which even those performing active Temple service are included in the full fast. This corresponds to the moment when the *teiva* is brought out, the ashes are placed, and the full twenty-four *brachot* are recited. Everything escalates together. Even inside the *mikdash*, where the *kohanim* are engaged in the service that is itself an expression of closeness to God, the community-wide fast is total.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

The scheme above reflects the position of *Rabbi Yehoshua*. *Vachachamim omrim* — the sages hold a somewhat less severe position, in which each tier is reduced by one level of stringency. According to the sages: during the first three fasts, *elu v'elu lo hayu mit'anim klal* — neither group would fast at all in the *Beit HaMikdash*. During the middle three fasts, *anshei mishmar* would fast but not complete it, while *anshei beit av* would not fast at all. During the last seven fasts, *anshei mishmar* would fast and complete the fast, but *anshei beit av mit'anim v'lo mashlimim* — those actively working in the *mikdash* would fast but not complete it, even at the most severe stage.

The underlying question in the dispute between Rabbi Yehoshua and the sages is how to weigh the demands of the communal fast against the priority of the Temple service. Rabbi Yehoshua is more willing to impose fasting on those actively engaged in the *avodah*; the sages are more protective of the *avodah*'s integrity and maintain a distinction even at the highest level of communal urgency.
