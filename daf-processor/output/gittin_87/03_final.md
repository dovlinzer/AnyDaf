# Gittin 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Edei Mesira Krati

### Intro: Edei Mesira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר: אַף בִּשְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Even if** there are no signatures of witnesses on the document, but rather he handed it to her in the presence of two witnesses, it is a valid bill of divorce. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce.** But **when I said this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said** that the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Elazar **with regard to** monetary **documents as well.**

The daf opens in the middle of 86b, where the *mishnah* has been discussing three types of *gittin* that are *pasul* — those lacking full *eidus*. The cases involved either the husband's own handwriting, a single *eid*, or various combinations thereof. That discussion proceeded according to Rabbi Meir's position that *edei chatima krati* — the witnesses who sign the document are the essential ones — and raised questions about what counts as valid signatures. At the close of the *mishnah*, however, Rabbi Eliezer articulates a fundamentally different position: a *get* requires no signatures at all. As long as it was delivered in front of witnesses, it is valid *l'chatchila*. And Rabbi Eliezer extends this principle beyond *gittin* — even a *shtar chov*, a loan document, if given in front of witnesses without signatures, creates liens on encumbered property. His principle is *edei mesira krati*: the witnesses who observe the transfer are the essential ones, and their *eidus* effectively combines with the physical document to constitute it as a document of testimony.

### Gittin vs. Shtarot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב סָבַר – בִּשְׁטָרוֹת לָא?! הָא קָתָנֵי: וְגוֹבָה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים! רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר תַּרְתֵּי אָמַר; וְרַב סָבַר כְּווֹתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does Rav hold** that **with regard to** monetary **documents** the *halakha* is **not** in accordance with Rabbi Elazar? **Doesn't** Rabbi Elazar **teach** in the mishna that on the basis of this bill of divorce a woman **can collect** the amount written in her marriage contract even **from liened property?** Apparently, Rabbi Elazar's ruling has monetary ramifications as well. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Elazar made two statements,** i.e., his ruling was both with regard to divorce and with regard to monetary documents. **And Rav holds in accordance with his** opinion **with regard to one** issue, i.e., divorce, **and disagrees with his** opinion **with regard to one** issue, monetary documents.

One might have expected the *halacha* to follow Rabbi Eliezer more readily in monetary matters than in *gittin*. After all, *dinei mamonot* is a more flexible area of law — *hefker beis din hefker* and similar principles give the rabbis wider latitude — whereas *gittin* involves personal status, which typically demands stricter standards. Yet the *gemara* will demonstrate the opposite, and for a compelling reason. A *get* is an instrument that, once it meets its technical requirements, effects a change in status. A *shtar*, by contrast, creates liens partly because it constitutes a matter of public record — buyers of property need to know that liens may exist against what they are purchasing. There is therefore a pragmatic reason to demand more objectively accessible documentation in monetary matters than in *gittin*, where the instrument's function is more purely technical.

### Halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין. וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ רֵיחַ הַגֵּט אֵין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce. And Rabbi Yannai says:** This **does not have even the trace of a bill of divorce.**

The *gemara* opens with Rav Yehuda citing Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer begittin* — we rule like Rabbi Eliezer for *gittin*, that witnesses of delivery without signatures suffice. When Rav Yehuda transmitted this ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel extended it: *af b'shtarot* — even by monetary documents. The *gemara* then asks whether Rav himself disagreed with Shmuel on this point, since the *mishnah* mentions collection from *nechasim meshubadim*, which implies that Rabbi Eliezer's position creates binding liens even in monetary matters. The *gemara* resolves this: Rabbi Eliezer himself said both — both for *gittin* and for *shtarot*. Nobody disputes what Rabbi Eliezer held. The question is only how we rule. Rav rules like him for *gittin* but not for *shtarot*; Shmuel rules like him in both domains.

### Rabbi Yannai's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: לְרַבָּנַן – אֲפִילּוּ רֵיחַ הַגֵּט אֵין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Yannai not hold** in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar** at all, as opposed to the other *amora'im*? The Gemara answers: Rather, **this is what** Rabbi Yannai **is saying: According to the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Elazar, this bill of divorce **does not have even the trace of a bill of divorce.**

The *gemara* then cites Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer begittin*. Rabbi Yannai, however, says: *afilu reiach haget ein bo* — a *get* without signatures does not even have the whiff of a *get*. A *get* lacking any *reiach haget* is so completely invalid that it does not even create the usual complications for *kehunah* that a questionably valid *get* would generate.

The *gemara* immediately objects: does Rabbi Yannai not hold like Rabbi Eliezer? The answer is that of course he does. What Rabbi Yannai is saying is that *for the rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer — and that *rabbanan* is no small figure, being Rabbi Meir — the position is not merely that such a *get* is *pasul*, but that it is diametrically and categorically invalid. If you believe that a *shtar* is fundamentally a signed document, then a document delivered in front of witnesses but bearing no signatures is not a defective *get* — it is simply not a *get* at all. The analogy is apt: if someone handed you an unsigned check and told you two people had watched the transaction, you would rightly say that is not a check. That is Rabbi Meir's position — and Rabbi Yannai is articulating it faithfully, while personally ruling like Rabbi Eliezer.

The *gemara* thus presents an extreme polarity: Rabbi Eliezer validates such a *get* entirely, while those who oppose him regard it as having no legal status whatsoever.

### Amoraic Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ רֵיחַ הַגֵּט אֵין בּוֹ. לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: לְרַבָּנַן – אֲפִילּוּ רֵיחַ הַגֵּט אֵין בּוֹ. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא לְמָרִי בַּר מָר, בְּעִי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין, אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה? אַדְּהָכִי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר אַבָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין. וְרַבּוֹתֵינוּ הַבְּקִיאִים בִּדְבַר הֲלָכָה, מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין – דְּאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: וַחֲבֵרֵינוּ הַבְּקִיאִים בִּדְבַר הֲלָכָה, וְתַלְמִידֵי רַבֵּינוּ, מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין – דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** that **Reish Lakish says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This **does not have even the trace of a bill of divorce.** The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan does not hold** in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar** at all. The Gemara answers: Rather, **this is what** Rabbi Yoḥanan **is saying: According to the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Elazar, this **does not have even the trace of a bill of divorce.** **Rabbi Abba bar Zavda sent** the following request **to Marei bar Mar: Ask Rav Huna** whether the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce or** whether the ***halakha* is not** in accordance with his opinion. **Meanwhile,** before this request reached him, **Rav Huna died. Rabba, son of** Rav Huna, **said to** Marei bar Mar: **My father said in the name of Rava like this:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce. And our teachers who are well-versed in matters of *halakha*** also **said in the name of our teacher** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce, as Rav Ḥama bar Gurya,** referred to as our teachers, **says** that our teacher **Rav says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce.** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this statement: **And our colleagues who are well versed in matters of *halakha* and the disciples of our teacher said in the name of our teacher** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce, as Rav Ḥisda,** Rabba bar Rav Huna's colleague, **said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya,** his teacher, **said** that **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce.** **And similarly, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael he said that **Rabbi Elazar,** the *amora*, **says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce.**

The *gemara* proceeds to accumulate a striking series of rulings all affirming *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer begittin*. Rabbi Yochanan bar Rabbi Yannai cites Reish Lakish: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer begittin*. When Rabbi Yochanan's own name appears immediately after with the statement *afilu reiach haget ein bo*, the *gemara* again clarifies in the same way: Rabbi Yochanan is articulating the position of the *rabbanan* who oppose Rabbi Eliezer, not his own ruling.

The *gemara* then relates that Rabbi Abba bar Zavdi sent a message to Mari bar Mar: ask Rav Huna whether we rule like Rabbi Eliezer for *gittin* or not. Before the question could be put to him, Rav Huna died. His son Rava then reported: this is what my father said — *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer begittin*. Some transmit this in the name of Rav directly. Additionally, *raboseinu habeki'im b'dvar halacha* — the rabbis who are expert in matters of law, identified as Rav Chama bar Guria citing Rav — rule the same way. And some say that not just those experts but the students of Rav, *mishum Rabbeinu*, transmitted the same ruling. Finally, when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported that Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer begittin*.

The cumulative effect is remarkable. The *gemara* is thoroughly insistent that this is the unanimous ruling of the *Amoraim* — that we rule like Rabbi Eliezer for *gittin* — while simultaneously emphasizing, through the careful limitation *begittin*, that this ruling does not extend to monetary *shtarot*.

## Mixed-Up Gittin

### Setup: Two Identical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנַיִם שֶׁשָּׁלְחוּ שְׁנֵי גִיטִּין שָׁוִין, וְנִתְעָרְבוּ; נוֹתֵן שְׁנֵיהֶם לָזוֹ, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם לָזוֹ. לְפִיכָךְ, אָבַד אֶחָד מֵהֶן – הֲרֵי הַשֵּׁנִי בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a case of **two** men **who sent** their wives **two identical bills of divorce** with an agent, as both their names and their wives' names are identical, **and** the two bills of divorce **were mixed up,** the agent **should hand both** bills of divorce **to this** wife **and both** of them **to that** wife, so that each wife definitely receives her bill of divorce, although it is unclear which one is hers. **Therefore,** if **one of** the bills of divorce **was lost** before it was given to both women, **the other is void,** because it is unknown which bill of divorce was meant for which woman.

The *gemara* turns to a new *mishnah*: *shnayim she'shaltchu shnei gittin shavin* — two men sent two *gittin* that are identical, because by coincidence both husbands and both wives happened to share the same names. This raises a preliminary question not addressed by the *mishnah* directly — can a *get* be valid if it is not *mochiach mitocho*, not self-evidently identifiable as referring to one particular couple? If there are multiple men in a city named Reuven ben Yaakov married to women named Sarah bat Rachel, can the document serve as valid written testimony? This question bears more acutely on Rabbi Meir's position, where the document itself must function as written *eidus*, but the *mishnah* sets this aside and assumes the *gittin* are otherwise valid. The scenario is simply that both husbands gave their identical *gittin* to a single *shaliach*, who was to deliver them to the respective wives — and the *shaliach* mixed them up and no longer knows which belongs to which.

The *mishnah*'s solution is elegant: *notnin shnayhem l'zu v'shnayhem l'zu* — give both *gittin* to one wife and then both to the other. Each wife thereby receives the *get* that was written for her, even if neither she nor the *shaliach* knows which one it is. However, if one of the *gittin* is lost — *avad echad meihem, harei hasheni batel* — the remaining *get* cannot be used. Giving it to each wife individually would render each one a *safek gerushah*, a woman of uncertain divorced status, which is the worst possible outcome, and so the remaining document is simply invalidated.

## Five Husbands, One G (II)

### One Date, Multiple Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁכָּתְבוּ כְּלָל בְּתוֹךְ הַגֵּט – ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מְגָרֵשׁ פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי פְּלוֹנִית״, וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה; כּוּלָּן כְּשֵׁרִין, וְיִנָּתֵן לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת. הָיָה כּוֹתֵב טוֹפֶס לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה; אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים נִיקְרִין עִמּוֹ – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **five** husbands **who wrote a general wording in the bill of divorce,** i.e., who wrote one common bill of divorce for their wives with a single formula, writing that **so-and-so divorces** his wife **so-and-so, and so-and-so** divorces **so-and-so, and the witnesses** signed **below,** in this case **all** of these bills of divorce that were combined into one bill **are valid; and** the bill **must be handed to each and every** wife individually, so they will all be divorced by it. If the scribe **was writing** a separate **formula** in the bill of divorce **for each and every** couple, **and the witnesses** signed **below,** the formula **with which the witnesses'** signatures **are read is valid.** In other words, the formula directly underneath which they signed is valid, and the others are not valid.

The *mishnah* then introduces a distinct case: *chamisha shekatvu klal b'toch haget* — five husbands who incorporated all their names within a single *get*, listing each man and his wife: *ish ploni megaresh plonit, v'ploni plonit*, and so on, with the witnesses signing below. The ruling is *kulam kesherim* — they are all valid — and the *get* is given to each wife in turn.

However, if *haya chosev tofes l'chol echad v'echad* — if there was a separate text for each individual — and the witnesses sign below, then only the one most proximate to the witnesses is valid: *es she'ha'eidim niktamim imo kasher*. The question that will occupy the *gemara* is: what exactly distinguishes a single unified statement, for which the witnesses' signatures validate the whole, from multiple separate statements, for which the signatures apply only to the bottommost?

## Mixed-Up Gittin (II)

### Why Problematic?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּאִי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי – הָא לָא יָדְעִי בְּהֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ קָא מִגָּרְשָׁה. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – אֵימָא דְּבָעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כְּתִיבָה לִשְׁמָהּ, נְתִינָה לִשְׁמָהּ מִי בָּעֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that the two bills of divorce that were mixed up should be handed to both wives? **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** It **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as had** it **been** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, since he says** that **witnesses of the transmission** of the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce, handing both bills of divorce to both wives would not enable the divorce of each woman to take effect. This is because the witnesses **do not know** at the time of the transmission **with which one of** the bills of divorce **she is being divorced.** **Abaye said: You** can **even say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. Say that** although **Rabbi Elazar requires the writing** of the bill of divorce to be **for her sake,** i.e., with the specific woman and man for whom it is designated in mind, **does he require giving** it to her **for her sake?** Although the transmission of the bill of divorce is what causes it to take effect, it need not be done with the designated woman in mind. Therefore, the divorce here is valid, as the bill of divorce was definitely given to the wife for whom it was intended.

The *gemara* asks: *man tana* — according to which *tanna* is the case of the two mixed-up *gittin* a problem? Rabbi Yirmeyah answers: *d'lo k'Rabbi Eliezer* — it is not according to Rabbi Eliezer. This is initially surprising, because one might have expected the opposite: if the *get* is ambiguous as to which person it refers to, that seems more problematic under Rabbi Meir, for whom the document must constitute clear written *eidus*. Yet the *gemara* argues that it is specifically Rabbi Eliezer's position that creates the difficulty.

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *edei mesira krati* — the witnesses who observe the delivery are the essential ones; their testimony combines with the physical document and makes it an instrument of *eidus*. For this to work, there is a logical requirement: the witnesses must be able to know which document is doing the divorcing. If two identical *gittin* are handed over together and neither the witnesses nor anyone else knows which one was written for this particular woman, how can the witnesses' testimony be said to combine with *this* document? The witnesses observed a delivery, but they cannot attest that *this* document is the one that was written for *this* woman. Their *eidus* would need to attach specifically to a document that they can identify as the operative one.

*Tosafot* articulates this precisely: the witnesses did not know that *this* document — as opposed to the other one — was the one written for this woman. And since Rabbi Eliezer's entire mechanism requires that the witnessing of the delivery combine with the specific document that was designated for her, the uncertainty about which document is which is fatal to his framework. For Rabbi Meir, by contrast, the witnesses signed the document at the time of writing, when everything was clear. The signing was done *lishmah*, with the correct parties in mind, producing a complete and valid instrument. Whatever happens at the time of delivery — even if the *shaliach* is uncertain about which *get* to hand over — is irrelevant to the document's validity, because the *eidus* is already embedded in it.

*Rashi* approaches this from the verse *v'chatav lah sefer keritut* — for Rabbi Meir, the word *v'chatav* refers to the signing, which was performed *lishmah* and completed the instrument. For Rabbi Eliezer, the *v'natanah b'yadah* — the delivery — also bears *halachic* weight, and one might argue it too must be *lishmah*. But the deeper logic is structural: if it is the act of witnessed delivery that constitutes the document as testimony, then the witnesses' awareness of what they are witnessing — specifically, *which* document is being activated — is an essential component of that act.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye rejects this analysis: *afilu teima Rabbi Eliezer* — the *mishnah* can be understood even according to Rabbi Eliezer. *Ketivah lishmah, netinah lishmah mi bei?* — writing must be *lishmah*, but does delivery require *lishmah* as a separate condition? Abaye maintains that the requirement is simply that it be delivered *as a get* — not, say, as a *shtar chov* — and the witnesses' uncertainty as to which document was written for this particular woman does not undermine their testimony about the delivery itself. The document was written correctly and *lishmah* at the time of writing; the fact that the witnesses at delivery cannot distinguish between two identical *gittin* does not invalidate either one.

## Five Husbands, One G (II)

### Yochanan: Zman Echad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁכָּתְבוּ כְּלָל כּוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי כְּלָל, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי טוֹפֶס? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן – זֶהוּ כְּלָל. זְמַן לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד – זֶהוּ טוֹפֶס.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna: With regard to **five** husbands **who wrote a general wording** in a bill of divorce, all of them can divorce their wives with it. But if a separate formula was written for each, only the last one is valid. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of** the case of the **general wording,** and **what are the circumstances of** the case of the separate **formula** that was written for each couple? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If there is **one date** and one formula written **for all of them,** such as: On such-and-such a date so-and-so divorced so-and-so, and so-and-so divorced so-and-so, **that is a general wording.** If a separate **date** and formula was written **for each and every one** of them, **that is** considered a separate **formula.**

The *gemara* now returns to the five-husbands *mishnah* and asks *heichi dami klal* — what constitutes a unified statement for which the witnesses validate all five *gittin*? And *heichi dami tofes* — what constitutes separate statements for which the witnesses apply only to the last?

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *zman echad l'kullan zehu klal* — a single date for all of them makes it one unified statement. *Zman l'chol echad v'echad zehu tofes* — a separate date for each makes them individual statements. On Rabbi Yochanan's view, the key variable is the date. If the entire document bears one date at the outset — "on the first of the week, I, Reuven ben Yaakov, divorce my wife... and I, Yaakov ben Yitzchak, divorce my wife..." — the shared date pulls the entire document together as one continuous statement, and the witnesses at the bottom validate everything. But if each section bears its own date — even if all the dates are identical — the repetition of the date marker makes each section self-contained, and the witnesses apply only to the bottommost statement.

The underlying logic is that a separate date makes a statement *self-referential* and therefore independent. Without its own date, a statement must look back to the single date at the top, and that shared reference point unifies the whole document. With its own date, each statement stands alone.

### Lakish: Klal Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ זְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן נָמֵי הָוֵי טוֹפֶס, אֶלָּא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי כְּלָל? דְּכָתֵב ״אָנוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי גֵּירַשְׁנוּ נְשׁוֹתֵינוּ פְּלוֹנִית וּפְלוֹנִית״.
>
> **Translation:** **Even** if **one date** was written **for all of them it is still** considered a separate **formula** if the divorce of each couple is mentioned separately. **Rather, what are the circumstances of** the case of **a general wording?** It is a case **where one wrote: We, so-and-so and so-and-so, have** hereby **divorced our wives so-and-so and so-and-so.**

Reish Lakish goes further: *afilu zman echad l'kulam nami avei tofes* — even with a single date for all, the result is still multiple separate statements. On Reish Lakish's view, the mere sequence of individual declarations — even connected by conjunctions — does not by itself constitute one unified statement. Each declaration "I divorce my wife" is an independent legal act; stringing them together with *vav*s does not make them one.

What then constitutes the *klal* — the unified statement — according to Reish Lakish? *D'kasvu anu ploni u'ploni gerashnu nishoseinu plonit u'plonit* — you would need to begin with a collective declaration: "We, Reuven and Shimon and Levi and Yehuda, divorce our wives..." That opening collective statement — *anu* — is what transforms the document into a single unified act, allowing the witnesses' signatures at the bottom to validate everything that follows.

But there is an immediate difficulty: if everything is so tightly bound into one statement that the witnesses validate the whole, doesn't that mean one *get* is divorcing multiple women? And the Torah says *v'chatav lah* — *lah v'lo l'chavertah* — a *get* must be for *her*, not for her and her friend simultaneously. The *gemara* resolves this for Reish Lakish: after the collective opening, you then write out individual statements — *ploni geiraish plonit, v'ploni geiraish plonit* — spelling out each husband's divorce separately. The collective opening creates the unity that allows the witnesses to cover everything, while the subsequent individual statements preserve the separateness of each *get* for each woman.

### Klal vs. Tofes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר זְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן – זֶהוּ כְּלָל, לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא כִּי (חָתְמוּ) [חֲתִימִי] סָהֲדִי, אַבָּתְרָא הוּא דַּחֲתִימִי! מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: עֵדִים חֲתוּמִין עַל שְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם בְּגֵט – פָּסוּל, חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא עַל שְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם חָתְמוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba objects to this: According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says** that when there is **one date** written **for all of them that is a general wording, we should be concerned** that **perhaps when the witnesses signed** the bill of divorce **they** intended to **sign for** only **the last** couple. **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:9) that if **witnesses are signed on a greeting** that was written **in a bill of divorce,** i.e., after the formulation of the bill of divorce the scribe extended greetings to someone and the witnesses signed underneath, it is **invalid,** as **we are concerned** that **perhaps they signed** the **greeting** and not the bill of divorce?

Rav Abba challenges Rabbi Yochanan's position. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the conjunction — the *vav* connecting the individual declarations under a single date — is sufficient to make everything one statement. But why should that be so? Perhaps the witnesses signed only on the final, most proximate statement. The *gemara* supports this concern by citing a *baraita*: *edim she-chotmin al she'elat shalom b'get pasul* — if witnesses sign at the bottom of a *get* that concludes with a social pleasantry ("and please send my regards to so-and-so"), the *get* is invalid, because we suspect the witnesses may have signed only on that closing pleasantry rather than on the *get* itself.

This principle establishes that witnesses' signatures are presumed to apply only to what is most immediately proximate to them. If an interpolated phrase — even a formulaic one — can sever the witnesses from the *get*, then surely separate individual declarations should do the same.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ: אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״שַׁאֲלוּ״ – פָּסוּל, ״וְשַׁאֲלוּ״ – כָּשֵׁר; הָכָא נָמֵי – דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it stated with regard to** this *baraita* that **Rabbi Abbahu says:** It **was explained to me by Rabbi Yoḥanan** that if the wording of the greeting is: **Ask** about the well-being of so-and-so, it is **invalid,** as perhaps the witnesses intended to witness only the greeting. But if the wording is: **And ask** about the well-being of so-and-so, with the conjunctive prefix *vav*, thereby making it appear as a continuation of the previous matter, it is **valid,** as the witnesses presumably signed the bill of divorce as well. **Here too,** it should be explained that this is a bill of divorce **in which it is written: So-and-so** has divorced his wife so-and-so, **and so-and-so** has divorced so-and-so, **and so-and-so** has divorced so-and-so, as the conjunctive prefix indicates continuation.

Rav Abba then clarifies that he had the position of Rabbi Yochanan explained to him directly. The case of *she'alat shalom* is *pasul* because the final line reads "and ask after the welfare of so-and-so" — a separate sentence that the witnesses could plausibly have been signing. But if the closing reads "and you should ask" — *v'sha'alu* — then it is *kasher*, because the conjunction makes it part of the same sentence as the *get* itself. Similarly, for Rabbi Yochanan, individual declarations joined by *vav* — "and I, Yaakov, divorce my wife... and I, Shimon, divorce my wife..." — are one run-on statement. The *ands* make it all one sentence, and the witnesses at the bottom validate the whole.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר זְמַן לְכׇל אֶחָד – זֶהוּ טוֹפֶס; מַאי אִירְיָא מִשּׁוּם טוֹפֶס? וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ נִכְתַּב בַּיּוֹם וְנֶחְתַּם בַּלַּיְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another challenge: **And furthermore, according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says** that when there is a separate **date** written **for each** couple, **that is** considered a separate **formula, why** is it rendered invalid **specifically due to** it being considered a separate **formula? Derive** that it is invalid **because it is** like a bill of divorce that **was written during the day and signed at night,** which is invalid because it was signed on a different calendar date than it was written. Here too, since a different date was written for each couple, and the witnesses signed only after the last formula, they clearly signed only on that date, and with regard to the previous couples it is therefore an invalid bill of divorce.

However, the *gemara* raises a further difficulty with Rabbi Yochanan's position. If the declarations are joined by *vav* but each bears its own date — even the identical date — does the redundant dating create a problem? The *gemara* observes that having a repeated date entry is even more self-contained than simply having a new sentence, because the date marker signals a fresh, independent statement. Moreover, if different dates were used, the earlier sections would be invalid on entirely separate grounds: a *get* signed on a different day from the one on which it was dated is *pasul*, because it was effectively signed on a day other than its stated date. The *gemara* resolves this by noting that in Rabbi Yochanan's case, all the dates are the same — the same day is simply repeated for each line. The repetition itself, even of the same date, is what signals that each is a self-contained statement — which is all the more reason it would function as a *tofes* even within Rabbi Yochanan's framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבָּא, בְּחַד בְּשַׁבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Kashisha, son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: We say the following** explanation of the case **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** It is a case **where it is written in** the respective formulations: **On Sunday, on Sunday.** In other words, the date written in each bill of divorce is the same, as they were all written on the same date.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמַר: זְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן נָמֵי טוֹפֶס הָוֵי; וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי כְּלָל – דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ הָכִי: ״אָנוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי גֵּירַשְׁנוּ נְשׁוֹתֵינוּ פְּלוֹנִית וּפְלוֹנִית״; נִמְצְאוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים מִתְגָּרְשׁוֹת בְּגֵט אֶחָד, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״וְכָתַב לָהּ״ – וְלֹא לָהּ וְלַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** According **to Reish Lakish, who says** that if the divorce of each couple is mentioned separately, then even if **one date** was written **for all of them it is still** considered a separate **formula, and** he explains that **what are the circumstances of** the case of **a general wording;** a case **where the following** wording **is written in** the bill of divorce: **We, so-and-so and so-and-so, have** hereby **divorced our wives so-and-so and so-and-so.** Accordingly, **two women are found being divorced with one bill of divorce.** All of these women are divorced with the same bill of divorce that contains a list of several couples. **And the Torah said: "And he writes her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), **and** it is derived from this phrase that he **cannot** write it both **for her and for another** woman. Rather, each woman requires a separate bill of divorce.

Ravina asks Rav Ashi: according to Reish Lakish, who says that even a single date is insufficient to create a *klal* — and that what is needed is the collective opening statement — what would a *klal* look like in practice? The *gemara* answers: *anu ploni u'ploni gerashnu nishoseinu plonit u'plonit* — the collective "we" followed by all the names and their wives. But this immediately generates the problem already noted: *nimtze'u shtei nashim misgarshos b'get echad* — two women are being divorced by one *get*, which violates *v'chatav lah v'lo l'chavertah*. Hence Reish Lakish's requirement that the collective opening be followed by individual statements for each husband.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּהֲדַר כְּתַב: ״פְּלוֹנִי גֵּירַשׁ פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי גֵּירַשׁ פְּלוֹנִית״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is valid **because he then wrote** after the general formulation: **So-and-so has divorced so-and-so, and so-and-so has divorced so-and-so.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַכּוֹתֵב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לִשְׁנֵי עֲבָדָיו – קָנוּ, וּמְשַׁחְרְרִין זֶה אֶת זֶה? וְלָאו אוֹקֵימְנָא בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת?!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: And** in **what** way **is** this case **different from that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who writes** a document granting **all of his property to his two slaves, they** both **acquire** the property **and** must **emancipate each other?** Since the slaves are included in the property, by acquiring the property together they each have ownership over half of the other slave. Consequently, they must emancipate each other. Since the *halakhot* of emancipation of slaves are compared to the *halakhot* of divorce, it can be proven from here that one bill of divorce can be written for two wives. The Gemara answers: **But didn't we establish** that that *baraita* is referring **to** a case where the master wrote **two documents,** one for each slave, stating that he is giving him all of his property, and he gave them to them simultaneously? Certainly each slave needs his own bill of emancipation.

The *gemara* considers whether this is analogous to the case of a master who gives all his property to two slaves, which would free both of them, since each owns half of the other as part of the property. Might that case demonstrate that one document can effect a legal change for two people? The *gemara* dismisses this: *v'okimna bishnei shtarot* — that case was long since explained as involving two separate documents, one given to each slave. One document for one slave and another for the other; the combined effect is joint ownership and mutual freedom. One *shtar* for two people remains impermissible. The conclusion stands: you cannot have one *get* for two women, and the collective statement in Reish Lakish's model must be accompanied by individual declarations.

### Baraita Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁכָּתְבוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַגֵּט: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מְגָרֵשׁ פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי פְּלוֹנִית״, וּזְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה – כּוּלָּן כְּשֵׁרִים, וְתִנָּתֵן לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת. זְמַן לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה – אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים נִקְרָאִים עִמּוֹ, כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** and similarly it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Reish Lakish.** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** as follows: With regard to **five** men **who wrote in the bill of divorce: So-and-so** hereby **divorces so-and-so, and so-and-so** divorces **so-and-so, and so-and-so** divorces **so-and-so, and** wrote **one date for all of them, and the witnesses** signed **below,** in this case **all of them are valid; and** this document **must be handed to each and every one** of the women mentioned in it. Nevertheless, if a separate **date** is written **for each and every one and the witnesses** signed **below,** the bill of divorce **with which the witnesses'** signatures **are read is valid.**

The *gemara* confirms both positions with *tannaic* support. In favor of Rabbi Yochanan: if five men write *ish ploni megaresh plonit v'ploni plonit* — each name connected by *vav* with a single date — and the witnesses sign below, *kulam kesherim*, and the *get* is given to each wife. If each section has its own date, the witnesses apply only to the final section. This is precisely Rabbi Yochanan's framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ רֶיוַח בֵּינֵיהֶם – פָּסוּל, וְאִם לָאו – כָּשֵׁר; שֶׁאֵין זְמַן מַפְסִיקָן. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁכָּתְבוּ כְּלָל בְּתוֹךְ הַגֵּט – ״אָנוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי גֵּירַשְׁנוּ נְשׁוֹתֵינוּ פְּלוֹנִית וּפְלוֹנִית, פְּלוֹנִי גֵּירַשׁ פְּלוֹנִית וּפְלוֹנִי גֵּירַשׁ פְּלוֹנִית״; וּזְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה – כּוּלָּן כְּשֵׁרִין, וְתִנָּתֵן לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת. זְמַן לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, וְרֶיוַח לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה – אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים נִקְרָאִין עִמּוֹ, כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: If there is space between them it is **invalid,** as the witnesses' signatures appear to be referring only to the last couple. **But if** there is **not** a space it is **valid, as** the **date** written for each couple **does not separate** between **them.** Rather, they are still considered the same bill of divorce with regard to the witnesses' signatures. It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Reish Lakish:** With regard to **five** men **who wrote a general wording in the bill of divorce: We, so-and-so and so-and-so, have** hereby **divorced our wives so-and-so and so-and-so; so-and-so has divorced so-and-so, and so-and-so has divorced so-and-so, and** they wrote **one date for all of them, and the witnesses** signed **below,** in this case **all of them are valid; and it must be handed to each and every** woman. If a separate **date** was written **for each and every one, and** there is **a space for each and every one, and the witnesses** signed **below,** only **the one with which the witnesses'** signatures **are read is valid;** the others are not.

In favor of Reish Lakish: if five men write the collective *anu ploni u'ploni gerashnu nishoseinu*, followed by the individual *ploni geiraish plonit*, with a single date and witnesses below — *kulam kesherim*. If each individual section has its own date and its own line-space, the witnesses apply only to the final section. This is precisely Reish Lakish's framework.

### ben Beteira: Revach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רֶיוַח בֵּינֵיהֶם – פָּסוּל, שֶׁהַזְּמַן מַפְסִיקָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: Even if there is no space between them,** the upper ones are **invalid, as the date separates them.**

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira then weighs in with a position more lenient than anything encountered so far. He says: *im yesh revach beneihem pasul, v'im lav kasher* — if there is a blank line-space between the sections, they are *pasul* as separate units; if there is no such space, they are *kasher* as one unit. Crucially: *she'ein zman mafsikan* — a separate date is not sufficient to create separation. Only a physical gap in the text — a blank line — constitutes a break.

On Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's view, the natural reading of a continuous text is that it is all one document, and a separate date-entry alone does not sever that continuity. One needs an actual physical interruption — a *revach* — to signal that what follows is a distinct and independent statement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – מַאי אִירְיָא זְמַן לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד? הָא אָמַר: זְמַן אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן נָמֵי טוֹפֶס הָוֵי! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא עָרְבִינְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא; אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּעָרְבִינְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, אִי פָּלֵיג לְהוּ זְמַן – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Reish Lakish, why** is the *baraita* referring **specifically** to a case in which a separate **date** was written **for each and every one? Didn't he say** that if **one date** was written **for all of them it is still** considered a separate **formula?** The Gemara answers: **This matter** applies **where he did not merge them at the outset** but instead wrote the divorce of each couple separately. **But here, he merged them at the outset,** writing: So-and-so and so-and-so have hereby divorced their wives so-and-so and so-and-so. Therefore, **if** the **date separates them** they **are** considered to be separate bills of divorce, and **if not,** then they are **not** considered separate bills of divorce.

The *gemara* then raises a sharp internal question against Reish Lakish's supporting *baraita*: Reish Lakish requires a collective statement to create a unified *klal*. But if that collective statement is what makes the whole thing one, why does the *baraita* then say that you can break it back into separate units by means of separate dates and line-spaces? If you needed the collective to unify them, the simple absence of the collective should be sufficient to produce separate units — why the need for dates and *revach* as well?

The *gemara* answers: the two cases are distinct. Where no collective statement was ever made, the individual declarations are simply multiple separate units from the outset, and no further mechanism is needed to establish their separateness. But where a collective statement *was* made at the outset — *anu ploni u'ploni gerashnu* — that collective creates a presumption of unity. To overcome that presumption and re-establish the separateness of the individual sections, something affirmative is needed: *mipalgu zman u-shita* — a separate date-entry and a line-space for each section. The combined force of these two elements can overcome the unifying effect of the initial collective statement. Without both, the collective statement continues to bind the whole document together.

## Parallel Columns

### Setup: Hebrew, Greek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנֵי גִיטִּין שֶׁכְּתָבָן זֶה בְּצַד זֶה, וּשְׁנַיִם עֵדִים עִבְרִים בָּאִים מִתַּחַת זֶה לְתַחַת זֶה, וּשְׁנַיִם עֵדִים יְוָנִים בָּאִים מִתַּחַת זֶה לְתַחַת זֶה – אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים נִקְרָאִים עִמּוֹ, כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **two bills of divorce that** a scribe **wrote** on the same paper **one next to the other, and** the signatures of **two Hebrew witnesses,** i.e., witnesses who signed in Hebrew from right to left, **extend from underneath this** bill of divorce on the right **to underneath that** bill of divorce on the left, **and** the signatures of **two Greek witnesses,** i.e., who signed in Greek from left to right, **extend from underneath that** bill of divorce on the left **to underneath this** bill of divorce on the right, the bill of divorce **with which the** names of the **first** two **witnesses are read [*nikra'in*] is valid.** The other bill of divorce is invalid, as it is not considered signed by these witnesses.

The final *mishnah* of the daf introduces another scenario: *shnei gittin shek'tavam zeh b'tzad zeh* — two *gittin* written side by side in parallel columns on one piece of paper. Below them are four signatures in total: two Hebrew witnesses and two Greek witnesses. The question is which signatures belong to which *get*.

### Language & Direction

The key to the *mishnah*'s ruling lies in the direction in which each script is written. Hebrew runs from right to left, so a Hebrew signature that begins at the right will be aligned with — and is presumed to belong to — the right-hand *get*. Greek runs from left to right, so a Greek signature that begins at the left will be aligned with and presumed to belong to the left-hand *get*. If the two Hebrew signatures appear directly beneath the two *gittin* — spanning both columns — and the two Greek signatures appear below them, the Greek witnesses are two lines removed from the *get* on the right. In that configuration, only the right-hand *get* is valid: the Hebrew witnesses are proximate to it, and the Greek witnesses, though displaced, are aligned with and proximate to the right-hand column as well by virtue of where they begin. The *mishnah*'s underlying presumption is that signatures must be immediately proximate — directly beneath — the *get* they are validating.

### Alternating Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְוָנִי, וְעֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְוָנִי, בָּאִים מִתַּחַת זֶה לְתַחַת זֶה – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **one witness** signed in **Hebrew** from right to left, **and one witness** signed beneath him in **Greek** from left to right, **and** underneath that signature **one witness** signed in **Hebrew, and** beneath him **one witness** signed in **Greek,** with the signatures **extending from underneath this** bill of divorce **to underneath that** bill of divorce, **both** bills of divorce **are invalid.**

The *mishnah* then presents the case where the signatures alternate: one Hebrew witness, one Greek witness, one Hebrew witness, one Greek witness. In this arrangement, *sheneihem pesulim* — both *gittin* are invalid. No two signatures for the same *get* are proximate to one another, and there is a *hefsek* — an interruption — between any pairing. The *mishnah* thus assumes that valid *eidus* on a *get* requires the two witnesses' signatures to be proximate both to the document and to each other.

### Proximity Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלִיתַּכְשַׁר הַאי בִּ״רְאוּבֵן״, וְהַאי בְּ״בֶן יַעֲקֹב עֵד״, דְּהָא תְּנַן: ״בֶּן אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי עֵד״ – כָּשֵׁר! דִּכְתִיב: ״רְאוּבֵן בֶּן״ אַקַּמָּא, וְ״יַעֲקֹב עֵד״ אַבָּתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is stated in the first case of the mishna that the bill of divorce under which the names of the first two witnesses appear is valid and the other is not, even though the signatures extend to that bill of divorce as well. The Gemara says: **But let this** bill of divorce **be validated by** the name **Reuven** alone, **and** let **that** bill of divorce be validated **by** the continuation: **Son of Ya'akov, witness,** which can be considered a separate signature. **Didn't we learn** in a subsequent mishna (87b) that a signature that reads: **Son of so-and-so, witness, is valid?** The Gemara answers: It is a case **where** the words: **Reuven, son of, are written on the** side of the **first** bill of divorce, **and** the words: **Ya'akov, witness,** are written **on the** side of the **latter** bill of divorce, indicating that it is one signature.

The *gemara* asks a pointed question about the case of two Hebrew witnesses followed by two Greek witnesses: why can't we simply let both sets of witnesses validate both *gittin*? After all, we know from a prior ruling that a *get* written in one language with witnesses who signed in another is valid — language itself is not a barrier. If so, why not say the Hebrew witnesses validate the right *get* and the Greek witnesses, even though displaced by two lines, validate the left *get*?

The *gemara* cites Chizkiyah to address this: *mil'o b'krovim kasher* — if the gap between a *get* and its witnesses was filled in with additional text, even signatures of relatives (who are *pasul* as witnesses but whose names in writing fill the space), the *get* is valid. Chizkiyah's ruling establishes that proximity is about continuity: a blank gap breaks the connection between document and witness; filled space — even with legally useless text — maintains it. But if filled space suffices to maintain connection, why can't the Hebrew signatures in the middle, which are themselves writing between the top *get* and the bottom Greek signatures, serve as the filling that keeps the Greek signatures proximate?

### Chizkiyah: Fill Gaps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, כֵּיוָן דְּמוּפְלָג בִּשְׁנֵי שִׁיטִין – לָא; וְהָאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מִלְּאָהוּ בִּקְרוֹבִים – כָּשֵׁר! הָא תָּנֵי זְעֵירִי: שְׁנֵיהֶן כְּשֵׁרִין. וְתַנָּא דִּידַן – דִּלְמָא גּוּנְדָּלִית חֲתֻים, וְכוּלְּהוּ אַחַד הוּא דַּחֲתִימִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say that since** the signatures of the witnesses of the second bill of divorce **are two lines away** from the bill of divorce itself, it is **not** valid, as that is the *halakha* with regard to a document that has a gap between the text and the signatures, **but didn't Ḥizkiyya say** that if the gap was **filled,** even **with** the signatures of **relatives** who are disqualified from serving as witnesses, it is **valid?** The Gemara comments: **Ze'eiri** in fact **teaches** that **both of** the two bills of divorce **are valid,** not only the one beneath which the names of the first two witnesses appear. The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning of **the *tanna* of our** mishna, who does not agree with this ruling? The Gemara answers: He is concerned that **perhaps** the bottom two witnesses **signed in reverse [*gundalit*].** For example, if the top two signatures are in Hebrew, perhaps the witnesses who signed in Greek reversed the word order of their signatures, imitating the Hebrew style, **and** in actuality **all of the** witnesses **signed one** bill of divorce.

Chizkiyah's principle is that a gap is problematic because it visually and legally detaches the witnesses from the document they are meant to validate. Once the gap is filled — by any writing, even non-*kasher* signatures — the document runs continuously from beginning to witness, and the connection is maintained. The implication for our case is that the Hebrew signatures physically separating the *gittin* from the Greek signatures might constitute just such a fill — maintaining continuity rather than severing it.

### Ziri: Both Valid

*Ziri* has a variant text of the *mishnah* itself. According to Ziri, in the case of two Hebrew witnesses followed by two Greek witnesses, *shnayim kesherim* — both *gittin* are valid. On Ziri's reading, there is no requirement of strict proximity; each set of witnesses is associated with its respective *get* regardless of the gap, and both *gittin* are therefore fully valid.

### Our Tanna's View

Our *tanna*, however, maintains that only the proximate *get* is valid and not the other. The *gemara* explains our *tanna*'s concern: *dilma gundali chasam* — perhaps the signatures were written in an unusual orientation. *Rashi* explains *gundali* as referring to some strange or reversed manner of signing. The possibility is that even the Greek signatures, written in some aberrant fashion, may be oriented toward the right-hand *get* rather than the left — meaning all four signatures could be attaching to the same *get*, leaving the other without valid witnesses at all. Because we cannot rule out this possibility, we cannot confidently say which *get* has two valid witnesses and which does not, and therefore only the *get* that is certainly proximate to identifiable signatures is valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְוָנִי. וְלִיתַּכְשַׁר הַאי בְּעֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְוָנִי, וְהַאי בְּעֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְוָנִי – דְּהָא תְּנַן: עֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְוָנִי – כָּשֵׁר! הָא תָּנֵי זְעֵירִי: שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּשֵׁרִים. וְתַנָּא דִּידַן – דִּלְמָא גּוּנְדָּלִית חֲתֻים; וּתְלָתָא אַחַד, וְחַד אַחַד.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna that if **one witness** signed in **Hebrew and** then **one witness** signed in **Greek,** and then one more witness signed in Hebrew and one in Greek, both bills of divorce are invalid. The Gemara asks: **But let this** bill of divorce **be validated by one Hebrew witness and one Greek witness, and that** bill of divorce be validated **by** the other pair composed of **one Hebrew witness and one Greek witness. Didn't we learn** in the subsequent mishna that a bill of divorce that was signed by **one witness** in **Hebrew and one witness** in **Greek is valid?** The Gemara answers: **Ze'eiri** in fact **teaches** that **both of them are valid.** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning of **the *tanna* of our** mishna? The Gemara answers: He is concerned that **perhaps they signed in reverse,** i.e., perhaps one of the witnesses who signed in Greek reversed the word order of his signature, **and** in actuality he signed the other bill of divorce. Consequently, **three** witnesses signed **one** bill of divorce **and** only **one** witness signed the other **one.** Therefore, they are both rendered invalid.

The same logic applies to the alternating case. One might suggest that the first Hebrew witness and the first Greek witness together constitute two witnesses for the right *get*, and the second Hebrew and second Greek together constitute two witnesses for the left *get*. The *gemara* notes that Ziri again says *shnayim kesherim* — both are valid. Our *tanna*, however, again raises the concern that one of the signatures might be written in an unusual direction — *gundali* — such that instead of the alternating pattern yielding one witness per document, three signatures might be attaching to one *get* and only one to the other. Since we cannot determine which *get* has two valid witnesses and which has only one, neither can be definitively validated.

The fundamental principle underlying our *tanna*'s position, then, is that a signature must be unambiguously aligned with — visually, directionally, and proximately — the *get* it is meant to validate. Ambiguity in any of these dimensions, whether caused by physical gaps or uncertain orientation, is sufficient to undermine the validity of the *get*.
