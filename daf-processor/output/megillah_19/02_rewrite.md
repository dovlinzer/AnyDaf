# Megillah 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Megillah Writing Require…

### Prohibited Inks

The daf opens at the very end of 18b, completing the discussion of how the *megillah* must be written and read. The *gemara* establishes that if the *megillah* was written with *sama* — an arsenic-based mineral dye — it is invalid. Similarly prohibited are *sikreta*, a red pigment derived from lead; *komos*, a dye made from tree resin; *kankantom*; and *charta d'uskhafi*, which Rashi identifies as a black dye used for dyeing leather.

### Valid Parchment Materials

The *gemara* then turns to *diftera*, the type of leather one may not write on. *Diftera* is defined as leather that has been salted and treated with flour (*demaliach vekamiach*), but has not been treated with water, and — critically — has not been treated with *afatzim*, that is, gallnuts, which were also used as a basis for dye. Writing on plain paper (*niyar*), understood as a cloth-based writing surface, is likewise invalid. The *gemara* concludes: *ad she'tehei kesuva Ashuris* — until it is written in *Ashuris*, which is the Hebrew script familiar today, the Babylonian-influenced square script (named for *Ashur*), as distinct from the ancient Hebrew script. This requirement is supported by the verse in Esther, *kechsavam ukizmanam*, "according to their writing and their time," which reflects that this was the writing of the period.

### Ashuris Script & Ink

The requirement that the *megillah* be written *al hasefer bedyo* — on parchment with ink — is derived through a *gezeirah shavah*. The word for writing (*kesuva*) appears here in connection with Esther, and similarly in the book of Yirmiyahu, where Baruch the scribe describes his work: *va'ani koseiv al hasefer bedyo*, "and I write on the parchment with ink." The *gezeirah shavah* links the *megillah* to this earlier text, establishing that valid writing requires parchment and ink, in *Ashuris* script. What precisely constituted that ink — its ingredients — is a matter taken up by the *rishonim*.

### Tosafot on Ink Ingredien…

Tosafot raises a pressing difficulty. The *gemara* specifies that *diftera* — untreated with *afatzim* — is invalid as a writing surface. This implies that valid *klaf* must be treated with gallnuts. Yet, Tosafot asks, how do we write on our *kelafim* today for *sifrei Torah*, *tefillin*, and *megillos*, given that they are not treated with *afatzim*? Rabbeinu Tam answers that the plaster (*sid*) applied to contemporary *kelafim* serves the same function as the *afatzim* treatment. This is, by any measure, a radical move: the *gemara* specifies precise materials for the preparation of *klaf*, and Rabbeinu Tam is arguing that a functionally equivalent substitute is acceptable.

### Minhag vs. Gemara

What makes this striking is that Rabbeinu Tam was not innovating a new practice — he was justifying one already universally accepted. The widespread practice was already in place, and he came to explain how it could be considered valid. This reveals the extraordinary power of *minhag*: to reinterpret explicit *gemara* requirements on the basis of accepted practice requires considerable audacity. Yet that is precisely what was done.

In the same passage, Tosafot records that Rabbeinu Tam also wanted to go in the opposite direction on the question of ink. He argued that ink made from gallnuts (*afatzim*) does not constitute valid *dyo*, drawing support from a *braitah* in *Gittin* that discusses the permissibility of various writing substances, and concluding that the standard for rabbinic ink excludes gallnut-based ink. On that basis, he sought to invalidate *sifrei Torah* written with gallnut ink — a position the German communities (*ashkenazim*) pushed back on vigorously.

One detail that emerges in this discussion is the traditional requirement that ink be erasable, a tradition derived from the case of *sotah*, where the text is scraped into the water. There is a certain irony here: one might expect that a sacred text would demand the most permanent medium possible, yet the halakhic tradition points in the opposite direction.

Taken together, these two adjacent discussions in Tosafot are fascinating: in one case Rabbeinu Tam defers to practice and reinterprets the *gemara* to justify it; in another, he invokes the *gemara* against an established practice. The interplay between textual authority and accepted custom could not be illustrated more sharply.

## Location & Reading Dates

### Mishnah: City Types

The *mishnah* introduces a new issue: *ben ir shehalach lechrach uven krach shehalach le'ir* — a resident of an unwalled city who went to a walled city, or a resident of a walled city who went to an unwalled city. Since the fourteenth and fifteenth of Adar are read on different days depending on whether one's city is walled or unwalled, a traveler faces a question of identity: which day does he observe? The *mishnah* answers: *im atid lachzor limkomo korei kimkomo* — if he intends to return to his place of origin, he reads according to the schedule of his hometown, even if that differs from where he is at the time. *Ve'im lav korei imahem* — if he does not plan to return, he reads with the people of the city he is in.

The question of "return by when" is left open by the *mishnah* and will be addressed by the *gemara*.

### Rava: Night of 14th

Rava sharpens the rule considerably: *lo shanu ela she'atid lachzor beleil yod-dalet* — the principle that one reads like his place of origin applies only if he plans to return on the night of the fourteenth. If, however, he will not return until after daybreak of the fourteenth, then *korei imahem* — he reads with the people of the city he finds himself in, since he will be present during the daytime reading, which is the determinative act.

This requires some unpacking. The *mishnah* knows only of a daytime reading; it is only in the *gemara* that a nighttime reading is added. The daytime reading therefore remains the identifying marker. The critical question is: will you be in this city on the morning they read? If yes, you are considered a member of that city for *mikra megillah* purposes. If not, you remain identified with your city of origin.

To illustrate: a person from Jerusalem visiting Tel Aviv finds himself there on the night of the fourteenth. If he plans to remain through the morning, he reads with Tel Aviv on the fourteenth — at night and again in the morning. If he is from Tel Aviv and travels to Jerusalem before the fourteenth, arriving on the thirteenth: if he plans to be in Jerusalem on the morning of the fifteenth, when Jerusalem reads, he does not read on the fourteenth at all. He has become, for purposes of *megillah*, a citizen of Jerusalem.

Rava then asks: *mina amina lah* — what is the source for this rule? The verse in Esther states: *al ken ha-yehudim ha-perazim ha-yoshvim b'arei ha-perazot* — "therefore the Jews of the unwalled cities, who dwell in the unwalled cities." The redundancy is telling: we already know they are *perazim*; why specify that they *dwell* in the unwalled cities? *Peraz ben yomo nikra peraz* — a one-day resident of an unwalled city is already considered an unwalled-city person. A single day of presence on the determinative day suffices to establish one's identity for that year's *megillah* reading.

### One-Day Residents

One might then ask: this derivation establishes the rule for a *peraz*, an unwalled-city resident — but what about the reverse? If a one-day Tel Avivian is a Tel Avivian, is a one-day Jerusalemite a Jerusalemite? The *gemara* answers by *sevara* — logical symmetry: *iy d'peraz ben yomo korei peraz, mukaf ben yomo korei mukaf*. If residence for a single critical day establishes identity in one direction, it does so equally in the other direction. A person spending only that one critical morning in a walled city reads on the fifteenth.

### Village to City

Rava then addresses a distinct case: *ben kfar shehalach l'ir* — a villager who has traveled to a city. Here we are no longer discussing the fourteenth versus the fifteenth, but rather the earlier market-day reading permitted for villagers (on the eleventh, twelfth, or thirteenth, whenever Monday falls before Purim). The entire rationale for that leniency was that villagers come into the city on market day and are therefore available to hear the *megillah* read. The question is: if such a villager, having already read early on market day, now finds himself in the city again on the night of the fourteenth — does he read again?

### Market Day Reading

Rava rules: *bein kach uvein kach korei imahem* — regardless of whether he plans to return home before daybreak or stay through the morning, he reads with the city residents. Rashi explains: the case is a villager who already read two days earlier on market day and is now back in the city on the night of the fourteenth, intending to leave before dawn. Even so, he must read again.

Why? *K'vnei ha'ir ba'inan l'mikrei* — because in principle, even a villager's obligation is to read on the fourteenth, like city residents. *Ve'rabbanan hu d'akilu al ha-kfarim k'dei she-yispeku mayim u'mazon l'acheihem she-bakrachim* — the Rabbis provided an early-reading leniency specifically so that villagers could supply food and water to the larger communities without being burdened. That leniency applies only *hani mili ki isa b'duchtei* — when, come the actual fourteenth, he is not in the city. *Aval ki isa b'ir, k'vnei ha'ir ba'inan l'mikrei* — but if he is in fact in the city on the fourteenth, his prior reading was only a substitute, and now that the real thing is available to him, the substitute does not discharge him.

Tosafot finds this deeply troubling — can a person who is already *yotzei* be required to read again? Yet Tosafot ultimately accepts Rashi's reading, recognizing that the early reading was a conditional leniency: it counted only on the assumption that the person would not be present for the primary obligation. Since he now is present, the leniency is retroactively inapplicable.

### Braitot Reconciliation

Abaye challenges Rava from a *braitah* that states a *ben kerach* who travels to an unwalled city reads *k'shiuro* — according to his original place — in all cases. This would seem to conflict with the *mishnah*, which makes the determination depend on whether one plans to return. Abaye prefers not to say the *braitah* argues with the *mishnah*, and so proposes that the text of the *braitah* is corrupt: *kerach* (walled city) should read *kefar* (village). As emended, the *braitah* would say that a villager always reads according to his original place — meaning even if he finds himself in the city on the fourteenth, his prior reading stands, in contradiction to Rava.

The *gemara* responds sharply: *v'lav tirutza m'taratzat?* — once you have already emended the text to avoid one contradiction, you have surrendered the right to use it as a source for a new one. The *gemara* can simply re-emend *korei k'shiuro* to *korei imahem*, restoring agreement with Rava. Once textual manipulation has begun, the *braitah* can no longer serve as the basis for a *kushya*.

## Starting Point of Reading

### Four Opinions

The *mishnah* raises a second question entirely separate from the matter of traveling between cities: *me'heichan korei adam et ha'megillah* — from where does one begin reading the *megillah* in order to fulfill the obligation (*latzeis yedei chovato*)? Rabbi Meir says: the entire *megillah* (*kulah*). Rabbi Yehudah says: from *Ish Yehudi* — the beginning of Mordechai's story. Rabbi Yosei says: from *Achar HaDevarim HaEleh* — which is when Haman enters the narrative.

### Gezeira Shava Analysis

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai adds a fourth position, not in the *mishnah*: from *Balaila Hahu* — "on that night," the pivotal turning point when everything begins to reverse itself. One might understand this as capturing the *neis* itself: even if you miss the entire buildup, you have the story of the miracle, which is what is being commemorated.

Rabbi Yochanan observes: *v'chulam mikra echad darshu* — all four opinions derive from a single verse: *vatichtov Ester ha'malkah u'Mordechai ha'Yehudi et kol tokef* — "and Esther the queen and Mordechai the Jew wrote all the *tokef*," the power. The word *tokef* — power, or the substance of the matter — becomes the shared analytical key. Each opinion identifies a different center of gravity in the narrative and therefore a different starting point. According to the one who says the entire *megillah* (*kulah*): *tokfo shel Achashverosh* — the power of Achashverosh, which opens the story. According to the one who says from *Ish Yehudi*: *tokfo shel Mordechai* — the power of Mordechai. According to the one who says from *Achar HaDevarim Ha'Eleh*: *tokfo shel Haman* — the power of Haman. According to the one who says from *Balaila Hahu*: *tokfo shel neis* — the power of the miracle.

### Rabbah's Framework

Rabbah offers an additional interpretive framework, built around the verse *u'ma ra'u al kach v'uma higia aleihem* — "and what did they see about this, and what came upon them?" He reads this as three structural elements of the narrative: what motivated the central actor, what the underlying cause was, and what the result was. This is not merely a literary observation; it is a way of organizing and justifying each of the four starting points.

### Central Characters

For the one who says to read from the beginning (*kulah*): What did Achashverosh see that led him to use the vessels of the *Beit ha-Mikdash*? The cause (*al kach*) was that he calculated that seventy years had passed without the Jews being redeemed, and therefore concluded the prophecy of return would never be fulfilled. The result was that he killed Vashti — which, within this reading, frames the entire *megillah* in the context of the exile, the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, and the cosmic confrontation between Achashverosh's ambitions and the divine promise of redemption.

For the one who says from *Ish Yehudi*: What motivated Mordechai to be *ikni b'Haman* — to be jealous of, or antagonistic toward, Haman by refusing to bow? The cause was *d'shavei nafshei avodah zarah* — that Haman had made himself into an object of idolatrous worship, which explains why Mordechai could not simply bow and spare the Jewish people the subsequent catastrophe. The result was that the miracle occurred. This reading places Mordechai at the center, but does so in a way that also raises uncomfortable questions: to what extent did Mordechai's refusal bring the *tzaros* upon the people?

For the one who says from *Achar HaDevarim Ha'Eleh*: What led Haman to extend his hatred from Mordechai personally to all the Jews? The cause was *d'Mordechai lo yichra v'lo yishtachaveh* — that Mordechai would not bow. The result was that Haman and his sons were hanged. For the one who says from *Balaila Hahu*: What led Achashverosh to call for the book of chronicles to be read to him? The cause was that Esther had invited Haman to the party with Achashverosh. The result was the miracle.

### Hester Panim Theme

What emerges from Rabbah's framework is something more than narrative structure. The *gemara* is probing hidden motives — asking not merely what happened, but what drove people to act as they did, and how those hidden motivations connect to a concealed divine hand in history. The *megillah* is, famously, a text in which God is never mentioned explicitly. The *gemara*'s repeated focus on *u'ma ra'u al kach* — on causes that lie beneath the surface of the story — reflects precisely this quality. People's hidden motives correspond to God's hidden direction of events. The coherence that makes this a *megillah* and not merely a chronicle of coincidences lies in reading that hidden logic. This is the *hester panim* dimension of the *megillah*, and it explains why the *gemara* invests such effort in uncovering the motivations of Achashverosh, Mordechai, Haman, and Esther.

### Halacha: Entire Megillah

The *gemara* then rules: *halacha kedivrei ha'omer kulah* — the law follows the position that one must read the entire *megillah*. And even according to the one who says one need only begin from *Ish Yehudi*, *tzerichah she'tehei ketuva kulah* — the entire *megillah* must nonetheless be written. The object of the *mitzvah* requires a complete scroll even if one is not required to read every word aloud.

## Megillah: Sefer vs. Iger…

### Sefer and Igeret

The *gemara* introduces a principle of dual identity: *megillah nikret sefer venikret iggeret* — the *megillah* is called both a *sefer*, a book (like a *Sefer Torah*), and an *iggeret*, a letter. Being called a *sefer* imposes more stringent formal requirements; being called an *iggeret* relaxes certain standards. The interplay between these two designations governs a series of practical halakhic questions.

### Parchment Threads

One implication: *nikret sefer she'im tefarah bechutei pishtan pesulah* — because it is called a *sefer*, if the parchments are sewn together with flax thread it is invalid. A *Sefer Torah* must be sewn with *gidin* — sinews (catgut, from the intestines or sinews of sheep and goats) — and the same applies to the *megillah*. Yet *venikret iggeret she'im hitil bah sheloshah chutei gidin kesherah* — because it is also called an *iggeret*, a letter, even if only three threads of *gidin* are used, it is valid. A letter does not require the full formal sewing of a sacred book.

Rav Nachman adds: *uvilvad sheyehu meshulashim* — provided that these three threads are evenly distributed, dividing the parchment into thirds. There is a dispute among the *rishonim* as to the precise arrangement. The *Rif* holds that three holes produce two spans, each thread separating the scroll into portions. Rashi and Tosafot, citing the relevant *pesukim*, hold that the three holes and the threading pattern divide the parchment into four sections. In either reading, the key requirement is that the distribution be even across the length of the scroll.

### Megillah Within Scroll

Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel rules: *hakorei bamegillah haketuva bein haketuvim lo yatza* — one who reads from a *megillah* written as part of a larger scroll of *Tanakh* (i.e., a complete scroll containing all or many books of *Ketuvim*, with the *megillah* embedded within it) has not fulfilled the obligation. This is a striking ruling: the scroll in question is undoubtedly *sifrei kodesh*. The issue is not sanctity, but rather the *megillah*'s dual identity. As part of a continuous scroll, it may function perfectly well as *Sefer Tanakh*, but it does not exist as an independent *mitzvah* object. The *megillah* must stand alone.

This is illustrated by the story of Levi bar Shmuel, who was reading before Rav Yehudah from such an embedded *megillah*. Rav Yehudah asked whether it was acceptable, and was told: anyone who reads from a *megillah* written within a larger scroll of *Ketuvim* has not fulfilled the obligation.

### Parchment Distinction

Rava limits the ruling: *lo amran ela d'lo mechasar umyatar purta* — this applies only when the parchment of the *megillah* section is not at all distinguishable from the surrounding text in terms of width. *Aval mechasar umyatar purta, leit lan bah* — if the *megillah*'s parchment is even slightly wider or narrower than the rest, it is valid. The *megillah* need not be physically detached from the rest of the scroll, but it must be visually distinct — it must have its own identity, even if that identity is constituted by a slight difference in the width of the parchment on which it is written.

### Pirsumei Nisa

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan initially states the same rule — reading from an embedded *megillah* does not fulfill the obligation — and then, in the same breath, he qualifies it: *betzibbur shanu* — this was said only for a communal reading. For an individual reading (*beyachid*), it does not matter.

This distinction is theologically meaningful. Reading in *tzibbur* activates the requirement of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle. The *megillah* itself is alluded to in the text of Esther as *ha'iggeret hazot*, as a visible, tangible document through which the story is told. In a communal setting, that visual and public dimension becomes a formal requirement: the *megillah* must exist as a separate, identifiable object, one that can be seen and held up as the vehicle of the story. In private reading, the *pirsumei nisa* dimension is absent or reduced, and consequently the requirement of an independent scroll does not apply. Interestingly, the fact that one is reading in a *tzibbur* does not merely permit this heightened standard — it demands it. One cannot say "I fulfilled the private standard while in the community." The communal context creates its own obligations, and those obligations can only be satisfied on their own terms.

## Permitted Readers

### Excluded Categories

The *mishnah* states: *hakol k'sherim likrot et hamegillah chutz mecheiresh shoteh v'katan* — everyone is valid to read the *megillah* except a *cheiresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan*. Rabbi Yehudah dissents with respect to the *katan*, holding him valid. Notably, women are not excluded; they are obligated in *mikra megillah*. Rabbi Yehudah's allowance of a *katan* is surprising at first, since a *katan* is not fully obligated in *mitzvot*. The reasoning may be that since *megillah* is a rabbinic *mitzvah*, and a *katan* carries at least a rabbinic obligation, one rabbinic obligation might fulfill another.

### Cheresh Issue

The *gemara* asks: *man tanna cheresh* — which *tanna* holds that a *cheiresh* is excluded? It first clarifies who the *cheiresh* in question is. In rabbinic literature, *cheiresh* can denote someone who can neither hear nor speak, but if he cannot speak, he cannot read in any case, so there would be no need for a legal exclusion. The *cheiresh* here must therefore refer to someone who can speak but cannot hear — and that raises the question: why should the inability to hear disqualify him from reading?

### Rabbi Yose: Hearing

Rav Matna answers: *Rabbi Yose*. The *gemara* recalls the ruling discussed a few days earlier: *hakorei et shema velo hishmia l'ozno* — one who reads *shema* without making it audible to his own ears. Rabbi Yose holds: *lo yatza* — he has not fulfilled the obligation. Applied to the *megillah*: the issue is not personal status but the act itself. If the reader cannot hear his own words, the reading lacks some essential quality of *kri'ah*. And this deficiency presumably affects not only the reader himself but any listeners who rely on him — if the act of reading is defective at its root, no one is discharged through it.

### Rav Matna Position

The *gemara* then asks: *u'mimai de-Rabbi Yose hi bediavad nami lo* — how do we know this is Rabbi Yose holding that even *bediavad* a deaf person's reading is invalid? Perhaps the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehudah, who is understood (through the *gemara*'s assumptions) to disagree with Rabbi Yose on *shema*, and perhaps Rabbi Yehudah's exclusion of the *cheiresh* is only a *l'chatchila* matter — a preference, not an absolute disqualification.

### Multiple Katan Types

The *gemara* initially resists this reading by pointing out that the *cheiresh* is listed together with the *shoteh* and *katan*, who are excluded even *bediavad* as persons lacking full legal standing. By analogy, the *cheiresh* should be excluded *bediavad* as well, not merely *l'chatchila*. The *gemara* tries to escape this by saying *ha k'dita v'ha k'dita* — each exclusion stands on its own terms: *cheiresh l'chatchila*, *shoteh* and *katan bediavad*. But this is undermined by the *seifa* of the *mishnah*, which records Rabbi Yehudah's allowance of the *katan* — proving that the *reisha*'s exclusion of the *katan* is not Rabbi Yehudah. At this point the *gemara* proposes the most aggressive resolution: *chasuri mechasra v'hachi katani* — the *mishnah* is incomplete and must be read as follows: the exclusion of the *katan* in the *reisha* refers to a *katan shelo higia l'chinuch*, one who has not yet reached the age of *chinuch*, while the *katan* that Rabbi Yehudah permits is a *katan shehigia l'chinuch*, one who has. This allows the *reisha*'s exclusion to be read as both *l'chatchila* and *bediavad*, consistent with Rabbi Yehudah — and preserves the goal of reading the *mishnah* entirely as Rabbi Yehudah.

### Tosafot: Force to Yehuda

The acrobatics here are, as the *gemara* itself seems to feel, considerable. Why go to such lengths to make the *mishnah* consistent with Rabbi Yehudah? Tosafot explains: because we *pasken* like Rabbi Yehudah — that *bediavad* one fulfills the obligation even without hearing one's own words. Since we rule that way, we are motivated to ensure that the *stam mishnah* does not stand in contradiction to that ruling. The interpretive effort is driven by the need for halakhic coherence.

### Three Positions

At this point, three positions have crystallized, each tied to a different *tanna*. Rabbi Meir: *l'chatchila* it is acceptable not to hear one's own words — intent (*kavana*) is what counts, as he says regarding *shema*, *achar kavanas halev hein hein hadevarim*, the words follow the direction of the heart. Rabbi Yosei: even *bediavad*, if one did not hear one's own words, the act is invalid. Rabbi Yehudah: *bediavad* it is valid; *l'chatchila* one should hear. The *gemara* arrives at this three-way scheme in part through an additional *braitah* where Rabbi Yehudah transmits a ruling in the name of his teacher, Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: *hakorei shema tzorich she-yashmia l'ozno* — when one reads *shema* one is required to make it heard by one's own ears. The language *tzorich*, "requires," is understood as a *l'chatchila* formulation — meaning *bediavad* it is valid if not heard. This resolves the puzzle of a *braitah* permitting a deaf person to take *terumos u'maasros l'chatchila* (despite the *bracha* he makes that he cannot hear): that *braitah* follows Rabbi Meir, for whom even *l'chatchila* the hearing requirement is not operative.

### Types of Acts

Tosafot closes the discussion with a pointed question: why are *megillah* and *krias shema* treated as analogous in terms of the hearing requirement, while *birkas hamazon* seems to occupy different ground? Tosafot's instinct is that this makes sense, and offers the following analysis: *shema* and *megillah* are both acts of reciting or reading a fixed text in a formalized and ritualized manner. *Birkas hamazon*, though the Rabbis composed its text, is at its core an act of prayer and gratitude — not the recitation of a fixed *mikra*, a canonical scriptural passage. Acts of reading a fixed canonical text carry more rigorous formal requirements: they must be articulated, and arguably heard. Acts of prayer or blessing, even when textualized, retain a more interior, intentional character — which is why the *gemara* records that *birkas hamazon* thought in one's heart is *yotzei bediavad*, while for *shema* and *megillah* the requirement of audible recitation is more formally operative. And even within this category, one can note that *megillah* carries an added dimension: it is not merely recitation but *reading from a book*, a formal act with its own additional criteria — which may well distinguish it from *shema* in ways that remain to be fully explored.
