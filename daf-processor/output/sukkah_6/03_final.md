# Sukkah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ten Tefachim Review

### Three Sources for 10 Tefachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: שִׁיעוּרִין, חֲצִיצִין, וּמְחִיצִין — הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: The measures** in various areas of *halakha*, e.g., olive-bulk, dried fig-bulk, egg-bulk, **and** the various *halakhot* of **interpositions** that serve as a barrier between one's body and the water in a ritual bath and invalidate immersions, **and** the dimensions and nature of halakhic **partitions** are all ***halakhot*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.** They were not written in the Torah; rather, they were received in the framework of the Oral Law.

The *daf* opens by reviewing a discussion from the preceding *daf* concerning the minimum height of a *sukkah*: ten *tefachim*. By way of contrast, the maximum height of twenty *amot* was established through the Rabbis' application of functional principles — the ability to see the *sechach* and the ability of the *sechach* to cast shade — whereas the specific quantity of twenty itself was arrived at through the application of those principles rather than a concrete derivation. The number ten *tefachim*, however, rests on more firmly grounded sources. The *Gemara* offered three explanations. The first derives the number from the *Aron* together with the *Kaporet*: the *Aron* was nine *tefachim* and the *Kaporet* one, yielding ten. The second derives it from the *Keruvim* atop the *Aron*, whose wingspan over the base measured ten *tefachim*. The third explanation, which will serve as the jumping-off point for the present *daf*, is simply *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* — a direct tradition requiring no textual derivation.

### Shekhina & Anan'ei HaKavod

The connection between the *Aron* and the *Kaporet* and the laws of *sukkah* is not immediately obvious, and the *Gemara* acknowledges the difficulty. Rashi explains that ten *tefachim* near the ground constitutes the domain of the earth, and therefore a *mechitzah* — focusing here on the walls of the *sukkah* — must be ten *tefachim* in order to span the full height of that domain. On this reading, the rule is a general principle of *mechitzah* applicable across many domains of *halakha*. However, a far more evocative reading understands the ten *tefachim* as rooted in the idea of the *sukkah* as the *ananei haKavod* — the clouds of divine glory that hovered over the people in the wilderness. The divine presence manifested concretely in the *Mishkan*, hovering above the *Kaporet*, and that presence never descended below ten *tefachim*. The *sukkah* therefore must be at minimum ten *tefachim* because it cannot be lower than the domain in which God's presence dwells. On this reading, the imagery of *Sukkot* is not merely commemorative but reenactive: one inhabits a space that mirrors the immediate divine encounter.

### Yom Kippur Connection

This imagery acquires added force when one considers the temporal sequence of the calendar. *Yom Kippur* centered entirely on the elaborate ritual enabling the *Kohen Gadol* to enter the *Kodesh Kodashim* — the space of the *Aron*, the *Kaporet*, the *Keruvim*. Five days later, *Sukkot* begins, and the *Gemara* here envisions the *sukkah* as a kind of reenactment of precisely that encounter — a dwelling that echoes the *Kodesh Kodashim*, framed by the forgiveness of *Yom Kippur* and the divine revelation of *Matan Torah*. Whether one follows Rashi and Tosafot in reading the ten *tefachim* as a general rule of *mechitzah*, or whether one reads it as imagery unique to *sukkah*, the associative power of the passage is undeniable.

## Halakha L'Moshe MiSinai

### Three Categories Intro

The *Gemara* now proceeds to a statement of Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav: *shiurin, chatzitzin, u'mechitzin halakha l'Moshe miSinai* — the laws of minimum quantities, of obstructions for *mikvah*, and of *mechitzah* are all *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. The *Gemara* will take each of these three categories in turn.

### Shiurim Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיעוּרִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ! דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶרֶץ חִטָּה וּשְׂעוֹרָה וְגֶפֶן וּתְאֵנָה וְרִמּוֹן אֶרֶץ זֵית שֶׁמֶן וּדְבָשׁ״, וְאָמַר רַב חָנִין: כׇּל הַפָּסוּק הַזֶּה לְשִׁיעוּרִין נֶאֱמַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this assertion: Are **measures** a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai? **They are** written **in the Torah, as it is written: "A land of wheat, and barley, and vines, and figs, and pomegranates, a land of olive oil and honey"** (Deuteronomy 8:8), **and Rav Ḥanin said: This entire verse is stated for** the purpose of teaching **measures** with regard to different *halakhot* in the Torah.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges the characterization of *shiurim* as *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. Surely they are *de'oraita* — derived from a verse? The distinction here is significant: *de'oraita* means derived from a verse by textual interpretation, whereas *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* is a tradition transmitted from Sinai without being anchored in a verse. The *Gemara* thus raises the possibility that the *shiurim* are not purely traditional but are scripturally grounded.

### Seven Produce Verse

The verse adduced is from Deuteronomy (8:8): *eretz chittah u'se'orah gefen u'te'enah ve'rimon eretz zeis shemen u'devash* — a land of wheat and barley, grapevine and fig and pomegranate, a land of olive oil and honey. Rabbi Chanin teaches that this entire verse was stated for the purpose of *shiurim*: each of the seven species of the Land of Israel serves as the basis for a distinct halachic measurement. The produce of the Land is not merely catalogued as a natural blessing; it encodes a legal architecture.

### Wheat & Leprous House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִטָּה — לְבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע, דִּתְנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לְבַיִת הַמְּנוּגָּע וְכֵלָיו עַל כְּתֵפָיו, וְסַנְדָּלָיו וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּיָדוֹ — הוּא וָהֵן טְמֵאִין מִיָּד. הָיָה לָבוּשׁ כֵּלָיו וְסַנְדָּלָיו בְּרַגְלָיו וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּאֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו — הוּא טָמֵא מִיָּד, וְהֵן טְהוֹרִים עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶה בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס, פַּת חִטִּין וְלֹא פַּת שְׂעוֹרִין, מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכֵל בְּלִיפְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Wheat** was mentioned as the basis **for** calculating the time required for one to become ritually impure when entering **a house afflicted with leprosy, as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **one who enters a house afflicted with leprosy** of the house (see Leviticus, chapter 14), **and his clothes** are draped **over his shoulders, and his sandals and his rings are in his hands,** both **he and they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, **immediately** become **ritually impure.** However, if **he was dressed in his clothes, and his sandals were on his feet, and his rings were on his fingers, he immediately** becomes **ritually impure, but they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, **remain pure until he stays** in the house **long enough to eat half a loaf** of bread. This calculation is based on **wheat bread,** which takes less time to eat, **and not** on **barley bread,** and it relates to one who is **reclining and eating** it together **with relish** or a condiment, which hastens the eating. This is a Torah measurement connected specifically to wheat.

One might ask why clothing worn on the body enjoys a longer window before becoming *tamei* than clothing merely being held. The answer lies in the relevant verse's phrasing — the Torah conditions the *tumah* of worn garments on dwelling in the house long enough to constitute "eating" there, which the Rabbis interpret as the duration of an *achilat pras*. Garments held in the hand, by contrast, are treated as any other object and become *tamei* immediately upon entry.

### Barley & Bone Impurity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂעוֹרָה — דִּתְנַן: עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה — מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Barley** is also used as a basis for measurements, **as we learned** in a mishna: **A bone** from a corpse the size **of a** grain of **barley imparts** ritual **impurity through contact and by being carried,** but **it does not impart impurity** by means **of a tent,** i.e., if the bone was inside a house, it does not render all the articles in the house ritually impure.

Barley — *se'orah* — provides the measurement for human bone. The *Mishnah* teaches that a piece of bone the size of a barley kernel (*etzem k'se'orah*) conveys *tumah* through contact and carrying (*maga u'massa*), though not through *ohel* — tent-covering — for which a much more substantial portion of the corpse is required. The barley kernel thus establishes the minimum threshold below which bone causes no *tumah* at all.

### Wine & Surface Tension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֶּפֶן — כְּדֵי רְבִיעִית יַיִן לְנָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The halakhic measure determined by **a vine is the quantity of a quarter**-*log* **of wine for a nazirite.** A nazirite, for whom it is prohibited to drink wine, is liable to be flogged if he drinks that measure.

Wine — *yayin* — provides the measurement relevant to the *nazir*. A *nazir* does not transgress until he drinks a *revi'it* — a quarter-*log* — of wine, approximately three and a half ounces. This, however, raises a difficulty. Wheat and barley are used to measure *other* things: wheat measures the time-duration standard, barley the size of bone. Wine, by contrast, seems simply to be the thing being measured — we already know from the Torah that a *nazir* may not drink wine. What does wine add to the system of *shiurim*?

Rashi offers an elegant solution. A *nazir* transgresses not only by drinking wine but also by consuming the solids of the grape — the *chartzanim* and *zag*, the skins and pits. For those solids, the required quantity is, on at least one approach, a *revi'it* rather than a *kezayit*. But how does one measure a *revi'it* of solid material? The answer is liquid displacement: fill a container with liquid, submerge the solid, and measure the overflow. But what liquid should one use? Rashi argues that this matters because wine has a higher surface tension than water. To displace a *revi'it* through wine will require slightly more solid material than through water, because the greater surface tension resists displacement. Wine therefore functions as the liquid standard for displacement measurement — and that is the halachic relevance of its inclusion among the seven species. Tosafot proposes additional explanations, but Rashi's account is notably precise.

### Fig & Shabbat Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּאֵנָה — כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת לְהוֹצָאַת שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Fig** alludes to the measure of **a dried fig-bulk with regard to** the *halakhot* of **carrying out** on **Shabbat.** One is liable for carrying food fit for human consumption on Shabbat, provided that he carries a dried fig-bulk of that food.

Fig — *te'enah* — provides the standard for carrying on *Shabbat*. For all foodstuffs, the minimum quantity whose transfer from domain to domain constitutes a *Shabbat* violation is a *grogeres* — the volume of a dried fig.

### Pomegranate & Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִמּוֹן — דִּתְנַן: כָּל כְּלֵי בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים — שִׁיעוּרָן כְּרִמּוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Pomegranate** teaches the following measure, **as we learned** in a mishna: **All** ritually impure wooden **vessels belonging to** ordinary **homeowners** become pure through being broken, as broken vessels cannot contract or maintain ritual impurity. They are considered broken if they have holes **the size of pomegranates.**

Pomegranate — *rimon* — provides the standard for vessel integrity. The *Mishnah* teaches that for the vessels of a householder (*ba'al habayit*), the threshold at which a vessel is no longer considered a vessel for purposes of susceptibility to *tumah* is a hole the size of a pomegranate. The distinction between householder and merchant is significant: a merchant will discard a vessel with even the smallest hole because it can no longer be sold; a householder continues using a damaged vessel so long as it remains functional for *something* — liquids, then solids, then ever-larger produce. Only when the hole reaches the size of a pomegranate can even pomegranates no longer be stored inside, and the vessel is finally discarded.

### Olive as Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶרֶץ זֵית שֶׁמֶן (וּדְבָשׁ)״ — אֶרֶץ שֶׁכׇּל שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ כְּזֵיתִים. ״כָּל שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ״ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! הָא אִיכָּא הָנֵי דְּאָמְרִינַן! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שֶׁרוֹב שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ כְּזֵיתִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages interpreted: **"A land of olive oil and honey,"** as: **A land, all of whose measures are olive-bulks.** The Gemara poses a question: **Does it enter your mind** that it is a land **all of whose measures** are olive-bulks? **But aren't there those** measures **that we** just **mentioned** above, which are not olive-bulks? **Rather, say: A land, most of whose measures are olive-bulks,** as most measures relating to forbidden foods, e.g., fats, blood, *piggul*, leftover sacrificial flesh, ritually impure food, and the sciatic nerve, are olive-bulks, as are the measures for a corpse to transmit impurity in a tent and for an animal carcass to transmit impurity through contact.

Olive — *zayit* — is identified by the *Gemara* as the primary standard. The verse says *eretz zeis shemen*, a land of olive oil, and the *Gemara* derives: a land all of whose measurements are by the *kezayit*. The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this: surely not all measurements use the *kezayit*, given everything established above? The *Gemara* emends: *most* of the measurements are by the *kezayit*. This is indeed the standard halachic intuition. The *kezayit* is the default: the minimum for eating *matzah*, for all forbidden foods, for quantities of *neveilah* or *meis* that convey *tumah* — the olive-sized amount is the universal baseline.

### Honey & Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבָשׁ — כְּכוֹתֶבֶת הַגַּסָּה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Honey,** i.e., dates from which date honey is extracted, also determines a measure, as with regard **to** eating on **Yom Kippur,** one is liable only if he eats **a large date-bulk** of food.

Honey — *devash* — provides the standard for *Yom Kippur* fasting. As just learned in *Masechet Yoma*, the amount of food whose consumption violates the *Yom Kippur* fast is a *koseves hagassah* — a large, fat dried date. Honey here is understood broadly to include date honey, and the date itself supplies the measuring unit.

### Asmakhta Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ! וְתִסְבְּרָא? שִׁיעוּרִין מִי כְּתִיבִי? אֶלָּא הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently,** all **these** halakhic measurements **are** derived from this verse **in the Torah** and are not *halakhot* transmitted to Moses from Sinai. The Gemara refutes this argument: **And how can you understand** it in that manner that all these **measures are** explicitly **written** in the Torah with regard to each of the *halakhot* mentioned above? **Rather, they are *halakhot*** that were transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and the verse** cited **is mere support** for these *halakhot*, not a source.

Having mapped all seven species to their respective *shiurim*, the *Gemara* reasserts the verse as the source. But then it pauses: does this really make sense? The verse simply lists produce; there is no internal basis for the particular correspondences just drawn. How do we know to match wheat to the leprous house, barley to bone impurity, and so forth? There is no hint of this in the text itself. The *Gemara* concludes: *hilkhesa ninhu ve'kra asmakhta b'alma* — they are traditions, and the verse is merely an *asmakhta*, a supporting reference rather than a genuine derivation. The *shiurim* are genuinely *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*.

### Halakha vs. Derabbanan

A textual variant in the margin — citing *Berachot* 41b — reads instead *mi'derabbanan ve'kra asmakhta b'alma*: that the *shiurim* are rabbinic, and the verse is merely an *asmakhta*. This raises a fascinating conceptual question: can something classified as *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* also be described as *derabbanan*? The two categories seem in tension. One possibility is that the terms are occasionally used loosely — that *derabbanan* can sometimes describe anything not derived from a verse, including *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. Another, more suggestive possibility parallels what appeared in *Masechet Yoma*: that the Torah granted the Rabbis a certain latitude to define the operative quantities, and while those definitions ultimately govern Torah-level obligations, there was a degree of rabbinic discretion in their formulation. The issue is not cleanly resolved and will surface again below.

## Chatzitzin Laws

### Biblical Chatzitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצִיצִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ! דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָחַץ (אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ) בַּמַּיִם״, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר חוֹצֵץ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵין הַמַּיִם!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said earlier that Rav said that the *halakhot* governing **interpositions** that invalidate ritual immersion are *halakhot* transmitted to Moses from Sinai. The Gemara challenges this assertion: **These,** too, **are** written **in the Torah, as it is written: "And he shall bathe his flesh in the water"** (Leviticus 14:9), and the Sages derived **that nothing should interpose between his flesh and the water.** Apparently, the *halakhot* of interposition are derived from a verse in the Torah and not through oral tradition.

The second category — *chatzitzin*, obstructions to immersion — is also said to be *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. It should be noted that the *Gemara*'s discussion applies to all who immerse in a *mikvah*, not only women observing the laws of *niddah*. In the era of the *Gemara*, men who were *ba'alei keri* were equally subject to the obligation of immersion and the accompanying laws of *chatzitza*.

The *Gemara* challenges the characterization as *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*: is not *chatzitza* biblical, derived from the verse *ve-rachatz et besaro ba-mayim* — "he shall bathe his flesh in water"? From *besaro ba-mayim* — his flesh in water — the *Gemara* infers that nothing may intervene between the flesh and the water. This is a straightforward scriptural derivation, and the *halakha* of *chatzitza* would appear to be *de'oraita* rather than traditional.

### Hair Obstruction Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא — לִשְׂעָרוֹ, כִּדְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָא: נִימָא אַחַת קְשׁוּרָה — חוֹצֶצֶת, שָׁלֹשׁ — אֵינָן חוֹצְצוֹת, שְׁתַּיִם — אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses **comes** to teach, it is not with regard to an interposition on one's skin, which is indeed derived from verses in the Torah. Rather, it comes to teach that an interposition **in one's hair** invalidates the immersion, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, as Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: A single hair [*nima*] tied** in a knot **interposes** and invalidates the immersion. **Three** hairs tied together in a knot **do not interpose,** because three hairs cannot be tied so tightly that water cannot penetrate them. With regard to **two** hairs tied together in a knot, **I do not know** the *halakha*. This *halakha* with regard to hair is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

The *Gemara* responds: the scriptural verse establishes the basic rule, but the *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* comes to address the application of *chatzitza* to hair. This is the teaching of Rabba bar bar Chana: a single strand of hair that is knotted upon itself constitutes a *chatzitza*, because the tight knot prevents water from penetrating; three strands knotted together do not constitute a *chatzitza*, because a knot of three strands is looser and water does reach them; two strands — he does not know. The point of invoking Rabba bar bar Chana, however, is not to teach the precise threshold of one, two, or three strands, but simply to establish that *chatzitza* applies to hair at all — not only to the flesh.

### Et Particle Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂעָרוֹ נָמֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָחַץ אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּמַּיִם״, אֶת הַטָּפֵל לִבְשָׂרוֹ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — שְׂעָרוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: The *halakha* with regard to **one's hair is also** written **in the Torah,** as it is taught in a *baraita* with regard to that **which is written: "And he shall bathe [*et besaro*] his flesh in the water."** The superfluous word ***et*** comes to include **that which is subordinate to his flesh, and what is that?** That is **his hair.** The fact that, like the body, there can be no interposition between one's hair and the water is also derived from a verse.

But the *Gemara* now presses further: is it truly necessary to invoke *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* to include hair? One might derive it from the verse itself. The verse says *et besaro ba-mayim* — "his flesh." Why does the Torah write *et besaro* rather than simply *besaro*? The word *et*, following the method of Nachum Ish Gamzu, includes that which is *tafel l'vesaro* — attendant to, or connected with, the flesh. Hair is precisely that: attached to and part of the body. Just as *ve-ahavta et Hashem* — with the *et* — is read to include the *talmidei chachamim* who accompany love of God, so here *et besaro* includes the hair alongside the flesh. On this reading, no *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* is needed for hair, and the traditional rule must be directed elsewhere.

### Majority & Makpid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא, לְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, רוּבּוֹ וּמַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — חוֹצֵץ, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ. וְגָזְרוּ עַל רוּבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד מִשּׁוּם רוּבּוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד, וְעַל מִיעוּטוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד מִשּׁוּם רוּבּוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **comes** to teach, it is not with regard to an interposition in one's hair, which is indeed derived from a verse in the Torah. Rather, it comes to teach **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak said: By Torah law,** if there is an interposition between a person and the water, and it covers **the majority** of **his** body, **and he is particular about it** and wants the interposing substance removed, only then **is it** considered **an interposition** that invalidates immersion in a ritual bath. **However, if he is not particular about** that substance, **it is not** considered **an interposition.** The Sages, however, **issued a decree** that it is prohibited to immerse with a substance covering **the majority** of **one's** body with regard to **which he is not particular, due to** substances covering **the majority** of **one's** body with regard to **which he is particular. And,** they issued a decree that it is prohibited to immerse with a substance covering **the minority** of **his** body with regard to **which one is particular, due to** substances covering **the majority** of **his** body with regard to **which one is particular.**

The *Gemara* therefore redirects: the *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* comes to teach the principle stated by Rabbi Yitzchak. Rabbi Yitzchak teaches that biblically, a *chatzitza* invalidates immersion only under two concurrent conditions: it must cover the majority of the body (*rubo*), and the person must be *makpid* upon it — must object to it, not want it there. Both conditions must be present for biblical invalidation. The threshold is high: one would need to envision something like mud covering most of the body that the person has actively not cleaned off — and even then, only if there is active objection. If either condition is absent, the obstruction is not biblically invalidating.

### Gezeirah Extensions

From this biblical baseline, the Rabbis extended the prohibition by *gezeirah*. The first extension: majority without *makpid* (*rubo she-eino makpid*) is rabbinically prohibited, decreed on account of the biblical case of majority with *makpid* (*rubo ha-makpid*). The two cases share the characteristic of *rov* — majority coverage — and are therefore sufficiently similar to warrant a protective decree.

### Minority & Makpid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְזַר נָמֵי עַל מִיעוּטוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד מִשּׁוּם מִיעוּטוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד, אִי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם רוּבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: Then **let us also issue a decree** deeming substances covering **the minority** of **one's** body with regard to **which he is not particular** an interposition, **due to** substances covering **the minority** of **his** body with regard to **which he is particular, or alternatively, due to** substances covering **the majority** of **his** body with regard to **which he is not particular.**

The second extension, which is the operative concern in virtually all practical questions of *chatzitza* today, is: minority with *makpid* (*mi'uto ha-makpid*) is also rabbinically prohibited, decreed on account of the biblical case of majority with *makpid*. The two cases share the characteristic of *makpid* — active objection — and that shared characteristic alone is sufficient to trigger the decree. In practice, the obstruction is almost always on only a minority of the body, and the entire practical question reduces to defining what constitutes *makpid*.

### Makpid Philosophy

This question of *makpid* deserves careful conceptual attention. Technically, when something is on the body and one is not *makpid*, the water still does not touch that area of skin. Why should the absence of *makpid* remove the problem? Two competing conceptual frameworks present themselves. On the first, if one wishes the substance to remain — or at minimum, is indifferent to its presence — it is as if it has become integrated into the body and is therefore no longer a foreign obstruction. On the second framework, the prohibition of *chatzitza* is not that every centimeter of skin must be touched by water, but rather that there must be no *obstruction* present. Something one actively objects to is a foreign body and thus an obstruction; something to which one is indifferent is not designated as an obstruction in any meaningful sense, even if it physically blocks water.

The difference between these two frameworks is practically significant. If the conceptual requirement is *integration* into the body, then mere indifference is insufficient — one would need to actively *want* the substance present, to treat it as part of oneself. Only things like permanent stitches or medical implants might qualify. If, however, the framework is *non-designation as obstruction*, then simple indifference — having not thought about it, not caring one way or the other — suffices to render it non-problematic. The vast majority of practical *chatzitza* questions turn on precisely this distinction.

### Gezeirah L'Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא גּוּפָא גְּזֵירָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִגְזֹר גְּזֵירָה לִגְזֵירָה?!
>
> **Translation:** We do not issue that decree because the *halakha* that deems both an interposition covering the minority of his body about which one is particular and an interposition covering the majority of his body about which one is not particular an interposition **is itself a decree. Shall we** then **arise and issue** one **decree to** prevent violation of another **decree?** In any case, these details with regard to interpositions are neither written nor alluded to in the Torah; rather, they are *halakhot* transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

The *Gemara* raises an obvious question: having established the two rabbinic extensions, why not add a third — minority without *makpid* (*mi'ut she-eino makpid*) — since allowing it might lead to the problematic cases of minority with *makpid* or majority without *makpid*? The *Gemara* answers: that would be a *gezeirah l'gezeirah* — a decree to protect a decree — and the Rabbis do not extend their enactments to that degree.

A visual framework helps clarify this principle. Imagine a 2x2 grid with *rov*/*mi'ut* on one axis and *makpid*/*eino makpid* on the other. The cell of majority with *makpid* is the biblical prohibition. Any cell sharing one characteristic with that central case — majority without *makpid* (same row) or minority with *makpid* (same column) — is rabbinically prohibited by *gezeirah*. The cell of minority without *makpid*, however, differs from the biblical case in *both* dimensions simultaneously. To prohibit it would require a *gezeirah* protecting one of the two rabbinic extensions, not the biblical prohibition itself — that is, a decree layered upon a decree. This the Rabbis decline to do. Minority without *makpid* therefore remains permitted.

### Rashi on Devar Torah

Rashi answers: the phrase *devar Torah* — which we might expect to mean simply "by Torah law" — in this context specifically designates *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. The traditional rule is the initial quantification of *chatzitza* itself: the definition that the biblical threshold requires majority coverage combined with *makpid*. There was no verse that yielded this specific formulation; it comes from tradition. The Rabbis then built their *gezeirot* upon that traditional foundation.

The *Meiri*, however, records an alternative view: that what is being labeled *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* is in fact the rabbinic *gezeirah* of the two extensions. This is a striking claim — normally *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* describes a technical transmitted tradition, while a *gezeirah* is a classic form of rabbinic legislation. That the same rule could bear both designations underscores the ambiguity, already noted above in the context of *shiurim*, about whether *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* always means a fixed, mechanically transmitted norm or whether it can sometimes denote a domain in which the Rabbis were granted latitude to legislate with quasi-Sinaitic authority.

## Mechitzin Walls

### 10 Tefachim Wall Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחִיצִין — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר — מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai that the minimum height for **partitions** is ten handbreadths **is as we stated** earlier. The Gemara asks: **This** works out **well** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that there is no verse in the Torah from which this *halakha* can be derived, as he therefore concludes that it is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai. **However, according to Rabbi Meir,** who holds that all of the cubits in the Temple consist of six handbreadths and therefore the measure of ten handbreadths can be derived from verses in the Torah, **what is there to say?** What is the *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai with regard to partitions?

The *Gemara* now turns to the third category: *mechitzin* — walls. The relevant *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* in this context concerns the minimum height of a *mechitzah*, which is ten *tefachim*.

### Yehuda vs. Meir Debate

This prompts the *Gemara* to note: the claim that ten *tefachim* is *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* works well according to Rabbi Yehuda, who does not derive the number from the *Aron* or the *Keruvim* and therefore requires a separate tradition to establish it. But according to Rabbi Meir, who does derive ten *tefachim* from the *Aron* and the *Keruvim*, where is the *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* for *mechitzin*? The *Gemara*'s answer is that even according to Rabbi Meir, there are many additional *halakhot* of *mechitzah* that cannot be derived from any verse and must come from tradition.

### Magic Wall Concepts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא — לְגוּד, וְלָבוּד, וְדוֹפֶן עֲקוּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **When the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses **comes** to teach, it is with regard **to** other *halakhot* concerning partitions, e.g., the *halakhot* of **extending** [***gode*],** according to which an existing partition is extended upward or downward to complete the requisite measure; **and** the *halakhot* of **joining [*lavud*],** according to which two solid surfaces are joined if they are separated by a gap of less than three handbreadths; **and** the *halakhot* of **a curved wall** of a *sukka*. A *sukka* is fit even if there are up to four cubits of unfit roofing, provided that this roofing is adjacent to one of the walls of the *sukka*. In that case, the unfit roofing is considered a bent extension of the wall. These concepts are certainly not written in the Torah.

Specifically, the *Gemara* identifies *gud*, *lavud*, and *dofen akuma* as *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*: the principle that a wall can be conceptually extended upward or downward (*gud*); the principle that a gap of less than three *tefachim* is treated as closed (*lavud*); and the principle that a wall connected to invalid *sechach* can be imagined to bend inward (*dofen akuma*). These are the constructs that will recur throughout the tractate in discussions of borderline cases of *sukkah* construction, and none of them are derivable from any verse. Their authority rests entirely on transmitted tradition.

### Measurement Direction

An important question remains unresolved: when we speak of ten *tefachim* as the minimum valid height, is this measured internally — from the floor to the underside of the *sechach* — or externally? A related question is equally significant: when the height falls below ten *tefachim*, what exactly is invalidated — the walls, or the *sechach*? These two questions are linked to the different sources adduced for the ten-*tefachim* requirement. The derivation from the *Keruvim sokhekhim* — which frames the ten *tefachim* as the space canopied by the *sechach* — locates the problem in the *sechach* rather than the walls. The present *Gemara*, by classifying the height requirement under the category of *mechitzah*, seems to locate the problem in the walls. The tension between these framings reflects a deeper question about whether the defining characteristic of a *sukkah* is its *sechach* or its walls.

## Number of Walls

### Mishnah: 2+1 Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁתַּיִם כְּהִלְכָתָן, וּשְׁלִישִׁית אֲפִילּוּ טֶפַח. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ כְּהִלְכָתָן, וּרְבִיעִית אֲפִילּוּ טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: In order to construct a fit *sukka*, **two** of the walls must be walls **in the standard sense,** sealing the entire length and height of the *sukka*, **and the third** wall may be **even** one **handbreadth** long. **Rabbi Shimon says: Three** of the walls must be walls **in the standard sense, and the fourth** wall may be **even** one **handbreadth** long.

The *Gemara* now takes up a *baraita*: two walls must be complete (*ke-hilkhatan*), and the third wall need be only a *tefach*. Tomorrow's discussion will address the precise placement of that symbolic third wall. The present discussion concerns the source for these numbers.

### R. Shimon: 3+1 Walls

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: three walls must be complete, and only the fourth need be a *tefach*. The debate between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon, then, is whether the minimum valid *sukkah* requires two full walls and a symbolic third, or three full walls and a symbolic fourth.

### Written vs. Read Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do **they disagree? The Rabbis hold: The tradition** of the manner in which the verses in the Torah are written **is authoritative,** and one derives *halakhot* based on the spelling of the words. **And Rabbi Shimon holds: The vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative,** meaning that one derives *halakhot* based on the pronunciation of the words, although it diverges from the spelling.

The *Gemara* introduces the first approach to explaining this debate. The Rabbis hold *yesh em la-mesoret*: when there is a discrepancy between the written form of a word (*kesiv*) and its traditional pronunciation (*keri*), the written form governs halachic derivation. Rabbi Shimon holds *yesh em la-mikra*: the read, pronounced form governs. The word *em* — mother — is used here in the sense of the generative source from which derivations spring. Rashi clarifies, as this distinction is easily confused: *mesoret* refers to the way the text is written (*kesiv*), while *mikra* refers to the way it is read aloud (*keri*).

### Four Units Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת, ״בְּסֻכַּת״ ״בְּסֻכַּת״ ״בַּסֻּכּוֹת״ — הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to *sukka*, **the Rabbis hold: The tradition** of the verses **is authoritative,** as the word *basukkot* is written three times in the context of the mitzva of *sukka*. It is written twice in the verse: **"In *sukkot* [*basukkot*]** shall you reside seven days; all that are home-born in Israel shall reside **in *sukkot* [*basukkot*]"** (Leviticus 23:42). In both of these instances, the word in Hebrew is spelled without a *vav*, as are Hebrew words in the singular. And one time it is written with a *vav*, as are Hebrew words in the plural: "So that your future generations will know that I caused the children of Israel to reside **in *sukkot* [*basukkot*]"** (Leviticus 23:43). **There is** mention **here** of *sukka* **four** times, two singular plus one plural hinted at here in these verses.

The operative verses are the three Torah occurrences of the word *sukkot* in the context of dwelling: *ba-sukkot* (written without a *vav*, as singular *sukat*) appears twice, and *ba-sukkot* (written with a *vav*, plural) appears once. Under the *yesh em la-mesoret* principle, each occurrence contributes units according to its written form. The two occurrences written in the singular contribute one unit each; the one occurrence written in the plural contributes two. Total: four units.

### Rabbanan Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּל חַד לְגוּפֵיהּ, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תְּלָתָא. שְׁתַּיִם כְּהִלְכָתָן, וַאֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא וּגְרַעְתַּהּ לִשְׁלִישִׁית וְאוֹקֵמְתַּהּ אַטֶּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Subtract one to** teach the mitzva of *sukka* **itself, and three remain.** These three remaining *sukkot* teach that the *sukka* requires three walls; **two** of the three are walls **in the standard sense,** and **the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **comes and reduces the** dimension of the **third and establishes it as** one **handbreadth.** That tradition teaches that one wall need not be any longer than one handbreadth.

The Rabbis then apply the following logic: the first unit is required for the straightforward reading of the verse — simply, one must dwell in a *sukkah*. This accounts for one unit. Three units remain, yielding three walls. However, a *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* intervenes and *reduces* the third wall: it need not be a full wall but only a representative *tefach*. The result is two complete walls and one symbolic wall.

### R. Shimon Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא, ״בַּסֻּכּוֹת״ ״בַּסֻּכּוֹת״ ״בַּסֻּכּוֹת״ — הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ, דַּל חַד קְרָא לְגוּפֵיהּ, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ אַרְבַּע. שָׁלֹשׁ כְּהִלְכָתָן, אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא וּגְרַעְתַּהּ לִרְבִיעִית וְאוֹקֵמְתַּהּ אַטֶּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** On the other hand, **Rabbi Shimon holds: The vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative.** Therefore, although two of the instances are written without a *vav*, since they are all vocalized in the plural, ***basukkot*, *basukkot*, *basukkot*, there is** mention **here** of *sukka* **six times** in these two verses. **Subtract one verse to** teach the mitzva of the *sukka* **itself, and** two mentions of *basukkot*, which equal **four** *sukkot*, **remain** and teach that the *sukka* requires four walls. **Three** of the walls are walls **in the standard sense, and the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **comes and reduces the** dimension of the **fourth and establishes it as** one **handbreadth.**

Rabbi Shimon, operating under *yesh em la-mikra*, reads all three occurrences in the plural — *ba-sukkot*, *ba-sukkot*, *ba-sukkot* — since that is how they are pronounced. Each plural form contributes two units, yielding six. One plural unit — that is, two — is consumed by the basic command to dwell in a *sukkah*. Four units remain. A *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* reduces the fourth wall to a symbolic *tefach*. The result is three complete walls and one symbolic wall.

### Sechach Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: סְכָכָה בָּעֲיָא קְרָא, וּמָר סָבַר: סְכָכָה לָא בָּעֲיָא קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **the vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative, and here,** it is **with regard to this that they disagree: One Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that to derive **its roofing requires a verse;** therefore, only three of the original six *sukkot* remain from which walls can be derived. The *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai reduces the dimension of one of the three walls to one handbreadth. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that to derive **its roofing does not require a verse,** as the essence of *sukka* is its roofing. No additional source beyond the verse from which the mitzva of *sukka* is derived is required for the roofing. Therefore, walls are derived from four of the six *sukkot*: Three full-fledged walls and a fourth measuring one handbreadth.

The *Gemara* then offers a second approach: set aside the *mesoret/mikra* debate entirely. Assume everyone derives from the read form, which yields six units; discount two for the basic command, leaving four. Yet the Rabbis still arrive at only three full walls. The debate, on this reading, is whether *sechach* requires its own textual unit. One position holds that *sechach* does require a unit: among the four remaining units, one teaches the need for *sechach*, and three teach the need for three walls — of which the third is then reduced to a *tefach* by *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. The other position holds that *sechach* requires no textual unit, because the word *sukkah* itself is definitionally identified with *sechach* — as Rashi noted at the very opening of the tractate, *al shem ha-sechach keruyah sukkah*, the *sukkah* is named for its *sechach*. The moment the verse says *dwell in a sukkah*, it has already implied *sechach*, and all four remaining units are available to teach the walls.

This is not merely a technical counting exercise. The underlying question is whether *sechach* and the walls are equal components of a *sukkah*, each requiring independent scriptural derivation, or whether *sechach* constitutes the essential identity of the structure while the walls are a supplementary requirement. The mechanics of the number-counting encode a genuine conceptual dispute.

### Halakha Reduces/Adds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא — לְגָרֵעַ, וְיֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא — לְגָרֵעַ. וּמָר סָבַר: כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא — לְהוֹסִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **when the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses **comes** to teach, it is **to reduce** to one handbreadth the dimension of one of the three walls derived from the verses. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds: When the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses **comes** to teach, it is **to add** another wall to the three walls derived from the verses; however, the dimension of that fourth wall may be one handbreadth.

The third approach: assume everyone goes by the written form (*mesoret*), which yields four units; subtract one for the basic command, leaving three. All agree on three. The debate then is about the function of the *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. One view holds that the *halakha* comes to *reduce* — to say that three walls are required but the third need not be complete, only a *tefach*. The other view holds that the *halakha* comes to *add* — three walls are the starting point, but the tradition teaches an additional, fourth wall is required, albeit in a reduced form of a *tefach*. The *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* therefore either diminishes an apparent requirement or supplements it, and the number of walls follows accordingly.

### First Reference Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, כִּי אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא — לְגָרֵעַ, וְיֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת. וְהָכָא בְּדוֹרְשִׁין תְּחִילּוֹת קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: דּוֹרְשִׁין תְּחִילּוֹת, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין דּוֹרְשִׁין תְּחִילּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **when the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses **comes** to teach, it is **to reduce** the dimension of one of the three walls. And everyone agrees that **the tradition** of the verses **is authoritative,** and there are four mentions of *sukka* in the verse. **And here** it is **with regard to** whether **one derives** numbers for halakhic matters from the **first** mention of a term in the Torah that **they disagree.** When that total is derived from the number of instances a certain word appears in the Torah, there is a dispute whether the first instance is included in the tally, or whether the first instance is necessary to teach the mitzva itself and the number may be counted only from subsequent mentions. One **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **one derives** numbers from the **first** mention and therefore four walls derived from the verses. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **one does not derive** numbers from the **first** mention, and therefore only three walls are derived from the verses.

The fourth approach: assume everyone agrees that the *halakha* comes to reduce, and everyone uses the written form. This should yield three units, which reduce to two and a symbolic remnant — two complete walls. Yet the Rabbis require two full walls and a *tefach*, and Rabbi Shimon three full walls and a *tefach*. The remaining debate, then, is whether *dorishin techilos* — whether the first scriptural reference may itself be used for halachic derivation rather than being reserved solely for the *pshat* meaning of the verse. One view holds that the first reference may be counted and used, in which case one starts with four rather than three and arrives at three walls. The other holds that the first reference is off-limits — dedicated to the plain meaning of the verse — yielding only three usable units and thus two walls and a *tefach*.

### Protection from Elements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר: טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מֵהָכָא: ״וְסוּכָּה תִּהְיֶה לְצֵל יוֹמָם מֵחוֹרֶב וּלְמַחְסֶה וּלְמִסְתּוֹר מִזֶּרֶם וּמִמָּטָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana said** that the **rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon** is derived **from here: "And there shall be a *sukka* for shade in the daytime from the heat, and for refuge and cover from storm and from rain"** (Isaiah 4:6). A *sukka* without three full-fledged walls does not provide shelter nor serve as refuge.

The *Gemara* concludes with an independent tradition: Rav Masna cites the reason for Rabbi Shimon's position from a verse in Isaiah describing the messianic vision — *ve-sukkah tihyeh l'tzel yomam... u'lemachseh u'lemistor mi-zerem u'mimatar* — and it shall be a canopy for shade by day, a shelter and refuge from storm and rain. This is the very verse with which the tractate opened. The verse depicts a *sukkah* as providing not only shade but comprehensive protection from the elements: heat, storm, and rain. This protection requires walls — walls that surround and defend. If the *sukkah* must shield from the elements in all directions, the walls are not secondary appendages to the *sechach* but co-equal elements of the structure's protective function.

### Walls Equal Sechach

This is a striking conclusion. The walls, on Rabbi Shimon's reading, are equal in importance to the *sechach*. The *sukkah* is not defined solely by its canopy; it is defined by the totality of its enclosure. This has direct implications for why Rabbi Shimon requires more walls: if walls are functionally equivalent to *sechach* — each providing a dimension of protection — then the structure needs more of them. The *ananei haKavod* imagery, invoked at the beginning of this *daf*, surrounded the Israelites from all directions; a *sukkah* reenacting that experience must similarly enclose.

### Shade vs. Protection

This reading, however, stands in tension with the *Gemara* at the opening of the tractate. There, the same verse from Isaiah was invoked to establish the maximum height of twenty *amot* — and specifically, the first half of the verse, *ve-sukkah tihyeh l'tzel yomam*, "it shall be a canopy for shade by day," was cited to demonstrate that shade is the primary function of the *sukkah*. Indeed, those *Amoraim* argued that the *shade* of the *sechach* is so central to the *sukkah*'s identity that when the *sechach* is too high to cast meaningful shade, the *sukkah* is invalid. There, the latter portion of the verse — the protection from storm and rain — was essentially set aside.

Here, Rav Masna draws on precisely that latter portion to argue that walls are as important as *sechach*. As one student noted, the two usages read from opposite ends of the same verse, and the *Amoraim* involved are different. The Gra famously observed that the very shape of the letters of the word *sukkah* — *samech*, *vav*, *kaf*, *heh* — can be read as encoding the three standard wall configurations: one letter representing four walls, one representing three, and one representing two walls and a *tefach*. Whether or not one reads that into the letters, the *Gemara*'s discussion across these pages has circled repeatedly around the same question: is the *sukkah* defined by its shade above or its enclosure around? The answer, it seems, depends on which question one is asking.
