# Menachot 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Yehuda: Chadash vs. C…

### Mishna: Roasted Grain De…

The Mishna at the end of 67b records a debate regarding what occurred after the *omer* was brought: the marketplaces would fill with roasted grain. Rabbi Meir held that this was done *shelo birtzon chachamim* — not with the approval of the Sages — whereas Rabbi Yehuda maintained that it was done *birtzon chachamim*, with their full approval. The Gemara's concern driving this discussion is whether preparing and handling grain that is not yet permitted — because the *omer* has not yet been brought — raises a worry that one might accidentally come to eat it.

### Concern About Eating

The Gemara on 67b, five lines from the bottom, poses this question directly: *v'lo gazar Rabbi Yehuda dilma ati l'meichal minei* — was Rabbi Yehuda truly unconcerned that someone handling and roasting this grain, getting it ready to sell, might accidentally eat from it before the *omer* was brought?

### Chametz Handling

The Gemara then raises a contradiction — *u'reminhu* — from Rabbi Yehuda's own ruling elsewhere. Rabbi Yehuda rules that one must search for *chametz* on the night of the 14th, and if forgotten, in the morning, and if still forgotten, at the time of *bi'ur*. But once midday of *erev Pesach* arrives, one should no longer go searching for *chametz*, because handling *chametz* at that point creates a concern that one might accidentally eat from it. So Rabbi Yehuda is clearly concerned about inadvertently eating *chametz* when one is in the midst of handling it on *erev Pesach* — yet by *chadash* he apparently has no such concern. The Sages, by contrast, rule that even if one did not search on *erev Pesach*, one may still search on *Pesach* itself. So the contradiction runs in both directions: Rabbi Yehuda is strict about *chametz* handling but permissive about *chadash* handling, while the Rabbanan are permissive about *chametz* handling but strict about *chadash* handling.

### Abbaye's Answer

The Gemara at the top of 68a, attributed to Rabbah — or according to some, Rava — begins an initial resolution: *shani chadash*, *chadash* is different, *ho'il v'lo hitarta lo ela al yedei kituf zachur hu* — since the grain was permitted to be gathered only by hand, by plucking rather than cutting with a scythe, that fact keeps in people's minds that this grain has a special status and they will remember not to eat it accidentally.

This answer invites scrutiny on two fronts. First, it assumes that the very people who plucked the grain are also the ones roasting and selling it — in reality one might easily imagine a middleman who purchases the wheat and processes it without having been involved in the plucking at all. Second, and more pointedly: even if the same person was involved, this was presumably a day earlier. By the time one is roasting grain and preparing it for market, who retains in conscious awareness that yesterday the plucking was done in an unusual manner?

### Omer & Shhinui

To understand why plucking by hand was required at all, one must appreciate the nature of the *omer*'s uniqueness. The *omer* is defined by the Torah as *reshit ktzirchem* — the first of your cutting. This means that prior to the *omer* being brought, there is both a negative prohibition against eating *chadash* — *v'lechem v'kali v'karmel lo tochlu ad etzem hayom hazeh* — and a separate concern about harvesting. The concern about harvesting is not formulated as an independent negative prohibition, but rather as a structural requirement: one is not permitted to cut grain prior to the *omer* because the *omer* must constitute *reshit ktzirchem*. Cutting grain beforehand would impinge upon or compromise the *omer*'s status as the first cutting.

The question then arises: at what point does the prohibition on harvesting lift? Is it after the *omer* has been cut, or only after the *omer* has been brought? Our Gemara, though laconic on the point, seems to imply that until the *omer* is actually brought, no harvesting was permitted — only plucking by hand. The Rambam, however, rules that once the *omer* has been cut, one may begin harvesting one's own grain even before the *omer* is brought. The Kesef Mishneh disagrees, maintaining that the prohibition on harvesting persists until the *omer* is actually brought. The question is thus debated among the *Rishonim*.

What is clear from the Gemara's logic is that plucking by hand is not considered *katzir* at all — it does not constitute the act of harvesting — and therefore one may gather grain in that manner even prior to the *omer* being brought without triggering the prohibition of *reshit ktzirchem*. The *shinui* of using one's hands, then, is not merely a procedural modification; it actually removes the act from the category of *katzir* entirely.

One might also ask: could one harvest after the *omer* was cut but before it was brought? As noted, this is precisely what the Rambam and Kesef Mishneh dispute. Our Gemara implies that throughout this entire period — until the *omer* was brought — the only permitted mode of acquisition was by hand.

## Processing Stages

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye now presses the initial resolution. *Tinach katzira* — fine, at the moment of harvesting, the requirement to use one's hands rather than a scythe provides a *heker*, a reminder that this grain is still forbidden. One will not pop grain into one's mouth precisely because the unusual method of collection serves as an ongoing cue. But grain passes through many stages of handling after the initial gathering. *Techina v'herkeda* — grinding and sifting. *Mai it leimeimar?* What can be said about those stages? There is nothing inherently distinctive about grinding or sifting that would remind the handler that eating is still forbidden. Rabbi Yehuda's general concern that people might accidentally consume something forbidden while handling it should apply just as forcefully at these later stages.

### Shhinui Solutions

The Gemara responds: *ha lo kashya* — that is not difficult. One might require a *shinui* even at the processing stages. For grinding, one might be required to use a hand mill rather than a water mill — *techina bereichaya d'yada*. For sifting, one might sift on the back of the sieve rather than the inside — *herkeda agabei nafah*. This is a noteworthy answer, because in the case of the initial harvesting the *shinui* of using one's hands was not artificially imposed — it arose naturally from the prohibition against cutting *katzir* before the *omer*. Here, however, there is no independent reason to require a *shinui* in grinding or sifting; the idea is being introduced solely as a safeguard against accidental eating. And crucially, no such requirement was actually stated in the Mishna. The Mishna simply describes the practice of handling and selling the grain; it nowhere stipulates that processing must be done with a *shinui*. The difficulty remains, therefore, as to what actually prevented accidental eating at these later stages.

## Irrigated Fields

### Harvesting Permission

The Gemara now introduces the case of *beit hashalachin* — irrigated fields. Such fields, the Gemara notes, are actually permitted to be harvested even before the *omer* is brought. The source is a later Mishna: *kotzin beit hashalachin sheba'amakim* — one may harvest the irrigated fields in the valleys, though one may not pile the grain into sheaves — *aval lo godshim*.

### Why Not from Irrigated

The reason harvesting is permitted from these fields is that the *omer* is not brought from *beit hashalachin*. Because the *omer* does not come from irrigated fields, harvesting them does not impinge on the *omer*'s status as *reshit ktzirchem*. The prohibition on harvesting prior to the *omer* applies only to grain from which the *omer* could be brought; for grain that is categorically excluded from eligibility for the *omer*, no such restriction exists.

### Tosafot's Problem

This raises the difficulty that Tosafot grapples with. Even if an irrigated field is not the ideal source for the *omer*, presumably *b'di'eved* — after the fact — grain from such a field would still count. If so, should there not still be a concern about *reshit ktzirchem* if grain from an irrigated field is harvested before the *omer* is cut?

Tosafot offers two responses. The first is that since *lechatchila* one does not bring the *omer* from an irrigated field, that grain simply does not fall within the category defined by *reshit ktzirchem*, even if it would technically suffice *b'di'eved*. This is a striking approach: *reshit ktzirchem* is understood not as a universal prohibition on all grain but as one specifically defined by the grain that would ordinarily constitute the *omer*. In other words, the prohibition on pre-*omer* cutting applies only to grain that occupies the relevant halakhic space — grain from which the *omer* is normatively brought.

Tosafot's second answer addresses the specific case of the Mishna — irrigated fields in the valleys. Perhaps such grain is doubly disqualified — both irrigated and located in a valley — such that even *b'di'eved* it would not suffice for the *omer*. If so, there is no question of *reshit ktzirchem* at all.

This leads to a broader conceptual observation. The prohibition on pre-*omer* harvesting can be framed in two distinct ways. One framing places the concern on the side of the *omer* itself: the *omer* must be *reshit ktzirchem*, and therefore nothing may be cut before it lest the *omer* lose its status as the first cutting. The other framing places the concern differently: the bringing or cutting of the *omer* is a *matir*, a permitting act, and nothing may be cut prior to that permitting act taking effect. Under this second framing, one would say that only grain eligible to serve as the *omer* is subject to the pre-*omer* cutting prohibition. Grain ineligible for the *omer* — such as that from irrigated fields — was never included in the prohibition to begin with. It is this second framing that best accounts for the Gemara's conclusion: irrigated-field grain may be harvested before the *omer* is brought, yet it remains forbidden to eat.

A question worth raising in connection with Tosafot's analysis is whether there is any grain that, even *b'di'eved*, would not fulfill the *omer* obligation. Grain from outside of Israel certainly cannot — that is one clear class. But within Israel, the question of whether there exist categories of grain so far from the ideal as to be disqualified even after the fact adds a further dimension to this analysis.

### Chutz La'aretz Issues

This issue connects to the broader question of *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz* and the longstanding practice of not being careful about it. The Aruch Hashulchan attempts an approach along the following lines: just as the prohibition on harvesting is limited to grain from which the *omer* can be brought, so too the prohibition on eating *chadash* should be limited to such grain. Since the *omer* cannot come from grain outside of Israel, there would be no prohibition to eat *chadash* there either. The difficulty with this approach is that the Gemara never says so explicitly — and if it were true, there would never have been any opinion that *chadash* is forbidden outside of Israel. The Aruch Hashulchan is essentially attempting to justify a longstanding practice rather than derive the position from an authoritative textual source.

### Two-Crop Problem

The other dimension of the difficulty with *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz* is agricultural. In Israel, there was essentially one wheat season. The grain would just be beginning to ripen by the time of the *omer*, so at most a few days of waiting were involved — a modest inconvenience at worst. Outside of Israel, however, and particularly as one moves further from the equator, two distinct grain crops developed: a winter wheat and a spring wheat. The winter wheat would be harvested roughly around the time of *Rosh Hashana*, meaning that by the time *Pesach* arrived six months later, this grain had been sitting in storage the entire time — yet technically remained *chadash* with respect to the *omer*. That is a very different situation from what the Torah's system envisioned.

Some have suggested, along lines similar to the point raised regarding *beit hashalachin*, that perhaps the *omer* was intended only to be *matir* grain that ripens by the time of *Pesach*. Grain that was already harvested and stored months before *Pesach* might, under this reasoning, not require the *omer* as its *matir*. These are admittedly weak approaches, not grounded in any explicit Talmudic source, but they represent attempts to account for why outside of Israel the prohibition of *chadash* has not been treated with the same stringency. The broader theoretical question they raise — to what degree everything in this system ties back to the *omer* and its relationship to natural cycles and seasons — is a genuine one.

## Rava's Resolution

### Framing the Issue

Rava now steps back and reframes the discussion. The initial question was posed only as a contradiction within Rabbi Yehuda's position: Rabbi Yehuda is unconcerned about accidental eating of *chadash* while handling it, yet is concerned about accidental eating of *chametz* while handling it on *erev Pesach*. But Rava points out, with a note of challenge — *de-Rabbanan lo kashya?* — what about the Rabbanan? They face the mirror-image contradiction: they are permissive about handling *chametz* even on *Pesach* itself, yet concerned about handling *chadash* before the *omer* is brought. Both sides need resolution.

### Habitual Status

Rava then provides a unified answer. Rabbi Yehuda's position is not contradictory — *kide-shani-nan*, as already explained by Abaye. The key for Rabbi Yehuda is one's habitual relationship to the substance in question. Until now, *chadash* has been forbidden — that has been the default, the ongoing status of this grain throughout the person's experience. Even as one now handles it to prepare for sale, in one's mind this grain is still in the category of the forbidden. The psychological default keeps one from inadvertently eating it. *Chametz*, by contrast, has been permitted all year long right up until *erev Pesach*. One's habitual relationship to *chametz* is one of permission. Switching that mental association in the final hours is difficult, and therefore Rabbi Yehuda is concerned that one will slip and eat.

### Mindset & Concern

The Rabbanan reason differently. Their concern is not about habitual status but about mindset and purpose in the moment. If one is handling *chametz* for the express purpose of destroying it — on a search-and-destroy mission — one will not accidentally eat from it. The single-minded intent to eliminate the *chametz* is its own safeguard. One does not absent-mindedly consume something one is bent on getting rid of. Therefore the Rabbanan permit handling *chametz* even on *Pesach* itself. *Chadash*, however, is being handled for commercial purposes — one is preparing it to sell. In that context, where the grain is being processed as food destined for consumption by others, the handler might quite easily take a taste. The mindset of sale does not function as a safeguard the way the mindset of destruction does.

### Badilei Minei

One might ask why there is any concern at all about accidentally eating *chadash* or *chametz* — after all, we do not find any such concern raised about handling *treifa* or *nevela* or other non-kosher foods. The answer lies in the principle of *badilei minei*. Things that are inherently not kosher — foods one keeps categorically apart from — one will not accidentally consume. There is no psychological confusion about *chazir* or *nevela*: they are simply not in the category of food for the observant Jew. But *chametz* and *chadash* are grain — the same substance one eats throughout the year under normal circumstances. Only at a particular time does the prohibition attach. This makes the boundary psychologically porous in a way that applies to substances that are only conditionally forbidden, not categorically non-kosher. This same principle, it is worth noting, is what underlies the various rabbinic safeguards around meat and milk: one is not required to separate a Jew eating kosher food from a non-Jewish colleague eating non-kosher at the same table, but one does require separation between someone eating meat and a companion eating dairy. It is precisely because meat and milk are individually kosher, and only their combination creates the problem, that one might forget.

An additional point worth noting: *chadash* also carries the stringency of *davar she-yesh lo matirin* — it is a forbidden item that has a future permitting act that will render it allowed. This adds a further layer of halakhic severity to the prohibition, beyond what has been discussed.

## Processing Stages (II)

### Roasted Grain Heker

Rav Ashi now offers his own resolution to the contradiction within Rabbi Yehuda's position. He argues that Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned about accidental eating of *chadash* because *kemach kali tenan* — the Mishna specifies roasted grain. The fact that it is specifically roasted grain that is being sold constitutes a *heker*, a recognizable marker, that distinguishes this grain from ordinary grain. The unusual form of the product — roasted and not in a standard form — will keep in people's minds that this is something special and not yet permitted for eating.

### Rejection of Rav Ashi

The Gemara rejects Rav Ashi's explanation with an unusually sharp formulation: *ha d'Rav Ashi beruta hi* — or *beduta hi* — that statement of Rav Ashi is simply wrong, a joke. This phrase appears rarely in the Gemara and seems particularly associated with critiques of Rav Ashi's positions. *Tenach mi-mikali v'eilach* — fine, from the moment the grain is roasted, the distinctive form might remind people. But *ad kali mai ika lemeimar* — what about before the grain is roasted? During all the processing stages that precede the roasting — the plucking, grinding, and sifting — there is nothing about the appearance of the grain to remind anyone that it is off limits.

And if one were to say, *v'ki teima hachi nami al yedei kituf* — well, the *shinui* of plucking by hand rather than harvesting applies at that stage — the Gemara immediately counters: *Beis Hashalchin desharei limichzad mai ika lemeimar* — what about irrigated fields, where, as established, one is permitted to harvest normally without any *shinui* at all? There, the grain looks no different from any other harvested grain, nothing distinguishes it as forbidden, and yet Rabbi Yehuda is apparently unconcerned about accidental eating. Rav Ashi's approach cannot account for this case. The Gemara therefore dismisses Rav Ashi's explanation entirely, reverting to Rava's answer as the operative resolution.

## When Chadash Permitted

### Bringing the Omer

The next Mishna states: *mishakaraf HaOmer hutar chadash miyad* — from the moment the *omer* was brought, the new grain became immediately permissible.

### Midday Rule

For those who lived at a distance from the Beis Hamikdash and could not know precisely when the *omer* had been brought, there was a rule: *harichokim mutarim mechatzot hayom u'lehallan* — those in remote locations were permitted to eat the new grain from midday onward. The reasoning was that the court would never be negligent in bringing the *omer* — *mipnei she'yod'im she'ein Beis Din mitatzlim bo* — and therefore one could be certain that by midday it had definitively been brought.

### Post-Destruction

Once the Beis Hamikdash was destroyed, the question of when *chadash* becomes permissible becomes more complex, as there is no longer an *omer* to serve as the *matir*.

### Takana Takana

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai established that the day of waving — *yom hanaf*, the sixteenth of Nissan, the second day of *Pesach* — should remain entirely forbidden, so that *chadash* would only become permissible on the seventeenth. The name *yom hanaf* itself is noteworthy, reflecting the Torah's emphasis on waving the *omer* — *v'hanifchem es ha'omer* — which gave the day its popular designation. The apparent implication of this enactment is that biblically, without an *omer*, *chadash* would be permissible from the beginning of the sixteenth, and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai instituted a rabbinic extension requiring one to wait until the seventeenth.

### Biblical or Rabbinic

Rabbi Yehuda in the Mishna, however, disputes this framing. He holds that the restriction is not rabbinic at all: *v'halo min HaTorah hu assur* — it is biblically forbidden. The source is the verse *ad etzem hayom hazeh* — until the very essence of this day — which Rabbi Yehuda reads as meaning until the entire day has passed. Unless the *omer* is brought during that day to permit *chadash* earlier, the prohibition extends through the entire sixteenth by biblical law. The disagreement in the Mishna, then, is whether Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ruling was a *takana* — a rabbinic institution — or a *derasha* and *haskamat* of biblical law.

## Amoraic Dispute

### Sunrise Permits

Rav and Shmuel both rule: *bizman she-Beis HaMikdash kayam, omer matir* — when the Temple stands, it is the bringing of the *omer* that permits *chadash*. *Bizman she-ein Beis HaMikdash kayam, he'ir mizrach matir* — when there is no Temple, the rising of the sun — the beginning of the new day — renders *chadash* permissible.

### Two Verses

The textual basis for distinguishing between these two scenarios lies in what the Gemara describes as *trei krah* — two verses that seem to point in different directions. One verse states *ad havi'achem* — until you bring the *omer*. Another states *ad etzem hayom hazeh* — until this very day. The Gemara resolves the apparent tension: *kan bizman she-Beis HaMikdash kayam, kan bizman she-ein Beis HaMikdash kayam* — the first verse governs when the Temple stands, and the second governs when it does not. When the Temple exists, the *omer* is the operative *matir*; when it does not, the beginning of the day itself is the operative moment. This reading raises an interesting edge case left unresolved by the Gemara: what if, when the Temple stood, the *omer* was somehow not brought? Would *chadash* remain forbidden indefinitely? Or would the beginning of the day retroactively suffice? These questions are not addressed, but the principle is clear: Temple present, it is the *omer*; Temple absent, it is the beginning of the sixteenth.

### R. Yochanan View

Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish both rule differently: *afilu bizman she-Beis HaMikdash kayam, he'ir mizrach matir* — even when the Temple stands, the rising of the sun is what renders *chadash* permissible at a biblical level. This position has an internal logic to it. If one is going to say that without the Temple *chadash* is permitted from the beginning of the sixteenth, it is difficult to argue that the presence of the Temple makes matters more restrictive. The *omer* would therefore be understood not as a mechanism that creates the permission for *chadash*, but rather as a mechanism that hastens permission earlier than the beginning of the day. Technically, *chadash* is permitted once the sixteenth begins; the *omer*, when it is brought, effectively pulls forward the moment of permission to whatever time during the day it is offered. The implication of this position, as the Gemara acknowledges, is that the *omer* is somewhat superfluous as a *matir* with respect to the prohibition of eating *chadash* — its role is practical rather than essential, advancing the moment of permission rather than creating it.

The position that contrasts most sharply with Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is Rabbi Yehuda's in the Mishna. For Rabbi Yehuda, when there is no Temple, *chadash* remains forbidden until the end of the sixteenth — *ad v'ad b'chlal*, the verse *ad etzem hayom hazeh* is read inclusively, meaning until the day itself is fully complete. When the Temple stands, the *omer* permits *chadash* during the day. This means that the bringing of the *omer* is actually more effective than its absence: with the *omer*, one can eat from the time it is brought; without it, one must wait until the end of the day.

### Clarifying Takana

The Gemara then asks a pointed question: does Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai really disagree with Rabbi Yehuda? The Mishna states that he *hitkin* — enacted — that the day of waving should be entirely forbidden. Rabbi Yehuda then responds in the Mishna that the matter is biblical, not rabbinic. But, says the Gemara, *Rabbi Yehuda hu de-kata'i* — it is Rabbi Yehuda who misunderstood. He thought that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was speaking rabbinically — making a new decree. In fact, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai held exactly what Rabbi Yehuda holds: that the prohibition is biblical, that the sixteenth remains forbidden by Torah law until the end of the day. They do not disagree at all.

But the Mishna says *hitkin* — he enacted — which is the classic language of a rabbinic institution. The Gemara answers: *darash v'hitkin* — he expounded and established. The term *hitkin* here means that he clarified and publicized the correct law. Until the Temple's destruction, there had been no occasion to determine what the biblical rule was in the absence of an *omer*, since the *omer* was always brought. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai now declared the halacha: the prohibition is biblical and extends through the entire sixteenth. This was not legislation but exposition.

## Chutz La'aretz Practice

### Rav Pappa Practice

Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua — who were in *chutz la'aretz* and lived after the destruction of the Temple — *achlu chadash be'orta d'shitsar nogei shivsar*: they ate *chadash* on the night of the sixteenth leading into the seventeenth. They waited out the entirety of the sixteenth — consistent with Rabbi Yehuda and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai — and began eating as soon as the seventeenth arrived at nightfall.

### Rabbinic Status

This, however, immediately raises the question of *sfeika d'yoma*. In *chutz la'aretz*, one keeps an extra day of *yom tov* precisely because of doubt about which day is the true calendar day. If that doubt applies here, then the seventeenth of the Diaspora calendar might actually be the true sixteenth — meaning *chadash* should remain forbidden until the eighteenth. Yet Rav Pappa and Rav Huna ate on the night leading into the seventeenth. *Ksavri chadash be'chutz la'aretz de'rabbanan* — they held that *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz* is only a rabbinic prohibition.

### Sfeka D'yoma Excluded

Since *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz* is itself only a rabbinic matter, these Amoraim held that one does not pile upon a rabbinic prohibition the additional stringency of *sfeika d'yoma*. *U'lesfeika lo chaishinan* — we do not apply the doubt-about-the-day concern when the underlying prohibition is itself only rabbinic. The practical result: one is obligated to wait until the seventeenth, but the seventeenth means the seventeenth as reckoned by the calendar, without extending it to the eighteenth on account of *sfeika d'yoma*.

### Rav Ashi Academy

The rabbis of Rav Ashi's academy followed a different practice. *Achlu betzafra d'shivsar* — they did not eat at nightfall leading into the seventeenth; they waited until the morning of the seventeenth.

### Dispute Summary

Their reasoning: *ksavri chadash be'chutz la'aretz de'oraisa* — they held that *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz* is a biblical prohibition. If so, *sfeika d'yoma* should in principle apply, and one should wait until the eighteenth. Yet they ate in the morning of the seventeenth — not the eighteenth. The reasoning implicit in this practice is that they relied on the position of Rav and Shmuel — that without the Temple, *chadash* becomes permissible from the beginning of the sixteenth by biblical law. Accordingly, from a purely biblical standpoint, *chadash* would be permitted from the beginning of the sixteenth. It is only the rabbinic enactment — following Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ruling — that extends the prohibition through the sixteenth. Since that extension is rabbinic, *sfeika d'yoma* is not applied to it. By morning of the seventeenth, the day is so clearly underway that even accounting for any calendrical doubt, one is well past the sixteenth. The dispute between these two academic practices, then, mirrors the underlying dispute about the biblical status of *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz*: those who hold it is rabbinic eat at the beginning of the seventeenth night; those who hold it is biblical wait until the morning of the seventeenth.
