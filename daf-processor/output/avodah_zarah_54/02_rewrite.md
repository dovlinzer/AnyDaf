# Avodah Zarah 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mizbeach vs. Bimos

### Mizbeach/Bimos Intro

The daf opens by wrapping up the discussion from the bottom of 53b concerning the distinction between a *mizbeach* (altar) and a *bimos* (a stand upon which an idol is placed). The earlier debate had established competing opinions regarding how much physical damage must occur before each structure loses its status as an object of idolatrous use. One opinion held that even a slight chip suffices to render a *bimos* no longer functional for its purpose, whereas a *mizbeach* requires majority destruction; the opposing view reversed this hierarchy. The question that now requires resolution is a more basic one: by looking at these structures, how do we know which is which?

### Stone Composition

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a *bimos* is made of a single stone, whereas a *mizbeach* is made of many stones constructed together. The physical composition itself is the distinguishing criterion.

### Biblical Source

Chizkiyah then asks: what is the *pasuk* that supports both the claim that a *mizbeach* consists of many stones, and the principle that it must be majority destroyed before it is considered nullified? The verse he cites is: *"b'sumo kol avnei mizbeach k'avnei gir menupatzos, lo yakumu asherim v'chammanim"* — "when He places all the stones of the altar like shattered sandstone, the *asherim* and sun-images shall not rise up" (Isaiah 27:9). The verse uses the plural *avnei* (stones), confirming that a *mizbeach* is multi-stoned. And the logic continues: only when the altar is shattered like sandstone — that is, substantially destroyed — will it no longer be used for worship of the *asherim* and sun-images. By implication, so long as it is not majority shattered, it remains in active use.

## Ne'evad Animals

### Own vs. Friend

A new topic begins with a *baraita*: if one worships his own animal, he renders it a *ne'evad* — an object that has been worshipped — and it becomes forbidden for use on the *mizbeach*. However, if one worships his friend's animal, it does not become forbidden. The principle underlying this distinction is the rule that a person cannot render forbidden something that is not his own.

### Ne'evad Definition

The *Gemara* then raises a tension from another source. A different *baraita* defines a *ne'evad* — an animal whose worship disqualifies it from the *mizbeach* — as any animal that was worshipped, whether unintentionally or deliberately, whether under compulsion (*ones*) or willingly (*ratzon*). Rashi notes that the pairs *shogeg u'meizid* (negligent and intentional) and *ones v'ratzon* (forced and willing) largely describe similar circumstances, but the *Gemara* will now zero in on the word *ones*.

The *Gemara* asks: what is the case of *ones* described here? It initially reads the word as referring to the animal being taken against the owner's will — that is, someone seized his friend's animal and bowed down to it. Under this reading, the *baraita* would be teaching that even if the animal was worshipped in a manner that violated the owner's will, it still acquires the status of *ne'evad*. This would constitute a direct contradiction to the *baraita* that permits a friend's animal, since the rule of *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — one cannot render forbidden what is not his — should apply equally here.

### Ones/Forced

Rami bar Chama rejects this reading. He says that *ones* in the *baraita* does not refer to the animal being seized against the owner's will; rather, it refers to the worshipper himself being forced. Non-Jews put a proverbial gun to the worshipper's head and compelled him to bow to his own animal. The *chiddush* of the *baraita* is then that even though the worshipper acted under compulsion, the animal nevertheless acquires the status of *ne'evad* and is forbidden to the *mizbeach*.

This reading opens a rich conceptual discussion. The obligation of *yehareg v'lo ya'avor* — to surrender one's life rather than transgress — applies to *avodah zarah*. If a person was indeed forced at the threat of death, one might argue that the act of *avodah zarah* was not truly his, and therefore the animal should not be *ne'evad*. The *chiddush* of the *baraita*, on this reading, is that it is *ne'evad* regardless. It is also worth noting the related discussion in Sanhedrin regarding whether worshipping *avodah zarah* out of fear or love (*me'ahava u'miyira*), without genuine belief, constitutes a culpable act at all. Even amid all these nuances, the *baraita* rules the animal forbidden.

### Rami Bar Chama

The *Gemara* now develops this conceptual tension further. The classic question when someone is *ne'enas* (coerced) is whether we say: he did the act but is exempt, or whether we say it is as though he never did the act at all — that he was merely an instrument in the hands of the coercer. Rami bar Chama appears to hold that under compulsion the person is treated as though he never performed the act. That is why he needs to specifically reread *ones* in the *baraita* as referring to a case where the person was coerced — because on his own view, coercion would normally mean the animal is not *ne'evad* at all.

## Act vs. Exempt

### Rabbi Zeira

Rabbi Zeira challenges the framework head-on: *ones rachmana patrei* — an *ones*, one who acted under compulsion, is exempted by the Torah. The biblical source is the verse concerning the betrothed woman who was raped: *"u'l'na'ara lo ta'aseh davar"* — "do nothing to the young woman," for she bears no guilt. This establishes the general principle that one who is coerced bears no liability. Rabbi Zeira's challenge pushes toward the position that if you were coerced, you simply did not do the act — and therefore the animal cannot be *ne'evad*.

### Rava/Kiddush Hashem

Rava resolves the tension by drawing on the interplay between two *pesukim*. Originally, all scenarios — including *avodah zarah* — fell under the category of *"v'chai bahem"*, the principle that one should live by the commandments rather than die for them. Applying *v'chai bahem* to *avodah zarah* might suggest that if one's life is threatened, one should capitulate and bow to an idol. However, the Torah then adds the verse *"v'lo techalelu et shem kodshi"* — do not desecrate My holy name — from which we derive the obligation of *Kiddush Hashem* and the requirement to surrender one's life rather than commit *avodah zarah*, even under duress.

These two verses appear to conflict, and Rava resolves them by invoking the position of Rabbi Yishmael: *"ha b'tzina ha b'farhesia"* — one verse applies to private situations, the other to public. According to Rabbi Yishmael, the obligation of *yehareg v'lo ya'avor* with regard to *avodah zarah* applies specifically in public (*b'farhesia*). In private, one need not sacrifice one's life. Rava uses this distinction to explain when coercion constitutes "not having done the act" versus "having done the act but being exempt":

When one is compelled to worship *avodah zarah* in private — where, according to Rabbi Yishmael, the Torah itself permits capitulation — the coercion is so complete that it is as if the person never did the act. The animal would therefore not be *ne'evad*. But when the worship occurs in public, where *yehareg v'lo ya'avor* demands martyrdom, the person was *supposed* to choose death. He chose not to. Even though in the realm of *avodah zarah* itself he remains an *ones* — after all, the threat was real — he cannot say "I didn't do it," because the Torah told him to allow himself to be killed and he actively declined. In that case, he did the act (albeit under duress), and the animal becomes *ne'evad*.

This is a genuinely striking conceptual distinction: the difference between "I did not do it" and "I did it but I am exempt." The former erases the act entirely; the latter acknowledges the act while removing personal liability. When martyrdom is obligatory and the person capitulates, he must "own" the act — even if he is technically exempt as an *ones* in the worship itself — and the *ne'evad* status follows.

### Tosafot Defense

The obvious objection arises immediately: does Rava truly hold that *avodah zarah* in private does not require martyrdom? Such a position would seem untenable given the historical reality of Jewish martyrdom, not all of which occurred under conditions of publicity. Tosafot addresses this directly and emphatically: Rava himself holds that *avodah zarah* requires *yehareg v'lo ya'avor* in all circumstances, public or private. He is not personally endorsing Rabbi Yishmael's view; he is merely arguing that *even according to Rabbi Yishmael*, one can distinguish between the two modes of coercion. His demonstration is thus hypothetical — showing that the conceptual distinction between "did not do it" and "did it but exempt" is coherent and useful, regardless of where one ultimately draws the line on when martyrdom is required.

This point carries significant weight beyond the technical *halacha*. Tosafot's insistence that Rava requires martyrdom even in private reflects the reality of Jewish communities throughout the medieval period, where martyrdom *al Kiddush Hashem* occurred in circumstances that were not always public by any strict definition. It would be deeply problematic — historically and ideologically — to suggest that those who gave their lives in private were not obligated to do so. Tosafot draws proof from other *Gemaras* to establish that Rava's position is absolute: *avodah zarah* always requires *yehareg v'lo ya'avor*.

One additional distinction worth noting: the scenarios discussed in the *Gemara* typically involve a single coerced act — bowing once before an idol — after which the person returns to his life as an observant Jew. In the medieval context, Jews were often being compelled to convert entirely, which is categorically different. A complete renunciation of Jewish identity might plausibly constitute *yehareg v'lo ya'avor* even in private by any standard. Tosafot, however, does not draw this distinction — his proof stands on the universal scope of Rava's position.

## Persecution Context

### Persecution Baraita

The rabbis attempt to bring a *baraita* in support of Rava's position. The *baraita* addresses *bimusa'os* — platforms or altars of non-Jews — that were used during a period of religious persecution (*she'at hagezeirah*), when Jews were being compelled en masse to worship *avodah zarah*. The *baraita* rules: even after the persecution has passed and the *gezeirah* is nullified, those platforms remain forbidden. This seems to demonstrate that coerced worship can still render objects forbidden — supporting the position that an *ones* can create *ne'evad* status.

### Willing Apostates

Rava, surprisingly, rebuffs this attempted support. He says: *"eima Yisrael mumar havah u'falach lei b'ratzon"* — one might say that in a time of mass persecution, some Jews chose to worship willingly, not merely under the immediate threat of death. At such moments of existential crisis, some individuals effectively declared themselves no longer part of the Jewish community and participated voluntarily. Since the *baraita* does not specify that the worship was exclusively coerced, one cannot draw from it any proof about cases of pure *ones*.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi sharpens this point further. Rather than saying "perhaps" there were willing apostates among those worshipping, Rav Ashi says one can say with certainty: in any scenario of mass persecution, there will always be some Jews worshipping willingly. The *baraita* therefore cannot serve as proof for the case of pure *ones*, since the forbidden status of those platforms can be explained entirely by the voluntary worship of willing apostates.

## Property Control

### Chizkiyah Answer

Having established through the preceding discussion that *ones* in the original *baraita* means the worshipper was himself forced, the *Gemara* now returns to consider whether *ones* might originally have meant what we first thought: that the animal was seized against the will of its owner. Chizkiyah says: yes, that is precisely what the *baraita* means. The case is one where someone poured wine between the horns of the animal (*nisach l'avodas kochavim yayin al karneha*) as an act of idol worship — an actual physical action performed on the animal belonging to another.

### Ownership Principle

This reading preserves the rule of *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — one cannot render forbidden something that is not his — because that principle applies only when there is no physical act. If someone merely bows before another's animal or directs worship toward it, he has not done anything to the animal itself, and therefore cannot alter its status. But if he performs a physical act *upon* the animal — pouring wine upon it, for example — he has physically engaged with the object in a way that extends his effective control over it, and can thereby render it forbidden even though it belongs to another.

### Rav Adda Challenge

Rav Adda bar Ahava challenges Chizkiyah's case. Pouring wine upon an animal as an act of worship, he argues, makes the animal a *bimos* — a kind of stand or support used in the service of *avodah zarah* — but not a *ne'evad*. And an animal that functions as a *tashmish* (accessory or appurtenance) of *avodah zarah* is not forbidden to the *mizbeach* in the same way a *ne'evad* is. An animal cannot literally become an *avodah zarah* itself, but it can become a *ne'evad* — something worshipped as the object of worship. Using it as a vessel or platform for the libation makes it a *tashmish*, which is a distinct and lesser category. The *baraita* discusses *ne'evad*, and Chizkiyah's case does not fit.

### Action on Animal

Rav Adda bar Ahava then revises the formulation: the case must be one where wine was poured between the animal's horns in worship of the animal itself — not using the animal as a stand for something else, but directing the act of worship at the animal as the object of veneration. That indeed creates *ne'evad* status. The key revision is that the physical act (*ma'aseh*) must be directed at the animal itself as the object of worship, not merely employ the animal as a surface or support. With this refinement in place, the principle is established: performing a physical act upon another's animal in the course of worshipping it renders it *ne'evad*, even against the owner's will.

## Physical Acts Transfer

### Ulla Teaching

The *Gemara* now introduces a teaching brought by Ulla in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, which parallels and consolidates what has just been established. Even though one who bows down to another's animal — *mishtachaveh l'vehemas chaveiro* — does not render it forbidden (since *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* applies, and bowing involves no physical act upon the animal), there is an exception.

### Action Exception

If one performs a physical act upon the friend's animal in the course of worshipping it — *asah bah ma'aseh* — then that physical engagement gives the worshipper sufficient effective control over the object to render it forbidden to the *mizbeach*, even though it belongs to another.

### Rav Nachman

When this teaching was reported to Rav Nachman, he responded: *"puk ve'imru lei le'Ulla"* — go back and tell Ulla — *"kevar tirgemah Rav Huna lishmati'tich beVavel"* — that Rav Huna already taught this very principle in Bavel. Ulla had come from Eretz Yisrael bearing a significant teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, but Rav Nachman notes that Rav Huna had already established essentially the same rule in the Babylonian academies.

### Rav Huna Case

What had Rav Huna said? If another's animal was lying crouched (*revutzah*) before an idol — the animal having assumed that position of its own accord, without being pushed down — and one then took a knife and cut even one of the two *simanim* (the trachea or esophagus) as an act of sacrificial worship, that single cut suffices to render the animal forbidden, even though a full *shechitah* was not performed and the animal has not technically become a *zevach*.

The cases are not perfectly parallel. Rav Huna's case involves an animal being brought as a *tikrovet* (offering) to *avodah zarah* — a process that, when complete, would render it forbidden to all people, not merely to the *mizbeach*. The *ne'evad* cases discussed earlier concern an animal forbidden specifically for use on the *mizbeach* but not for ordinary lay consumption. Nevertheless, Tosafot explains that the operative principle is the same: a physical act performed upon another's object in the context of idolatrous worship transfers the forbidden status, even without ownership. The *chiddush* that any such act — even something less dramatic than slitting the throat — suffices to create this status is in some ways even more striking in the *ne'evad* context than in Rav Huna's *tikrovet* context, where the act of killing is itself an obvious and decisive engagement with the animal.

## Sources

### Kohanim Source

Having established that a physical act upon another's object can render it forbidden, the *Gemara* asks: from where do we derive this principle? It first considers the case of the *kohanim* of Beit Chonyo discussed two days earlier. Those priests who had served in Beit Chonyo — a breakaway sanctuary — were disqualified from serving in the *Beit HaMikdash*, even though they were not themselves forbidden objects.

### Rejected Source

This source is immediately rejected. The *kohanim* of Beit Chonyo are a completely different case: they are not an instance of one person changing the *halachic* status of an object belonging to another. Their disqualification is a *knas* — a rabbinic fine or penalty — not a substantive change of status. The principle operative there is entirely different and cannot teach us anything about the *ne'evad* question.

### Altar Stones

A better source presents itself: the stones of the *mizbeach* that were defiled when the Seleucid Greeks occupied the *Beit HaMikdash*. When the Hasmoneans retook the Temple and restored worship, they had to dispose of those stones — they could not be reused. But how had the Greeks rendered them forbidden? The Greeks did not own the *mizbeach*; *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* should have applied. It must be, then, that because the Greeks performed physical acts upon those stones in the context of their idolatrous worship, they were able to change the stones' status despite not owning them. This would confirm the principle under discussion.

### Ownerlessness

The *Gemara* deflects this attempted proof as well. Perhaps the case of the *mizbeach* stones is explained differently — *k'derav Pappa*, along the lines of what Rav Pappa taught elsewhere: the verse states *"u'va'u vah pritzim v'chilaluha"* — when violent men entered and desacralized it. This indicates that the desecration stripped the stones of their sanctified status and rendered them ownerless (*hefker*), after which the Greeks effectively took ownership. If so, the Greeks were, in a sense, making *their own* property forbidden — which is unproblematic. The principle that an outsider can forbid another's property through a physical act remains unproven from this case.

### Achaz Vessels

The *Gemara* turns to the top of 54b and identifies a stronger source: the vessels that King Achaz used for *avodah zarah*. The verse states: *"kol hakeilim asher hizniach haMelech Achaz bemalkhuto bema'alo"* — all the vessels that King Achaz had set aside during his reign, in his act of trespass. The verse continues: *"hikinu v'hikdashhnu"* — we prepared them and sanctified them. As was established earlier, this means the vessels were stored away (*ganzum*) because they had been rendered forbidden. But these were vessels of the *Beit HaMikdash* — the Temple's property, not Achaz's. Since *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo*, how could Achaz forbid them? The answer must be that because Achaz physically used them in the service of *avodah zarah*, he was able to render them forbidden even though they were not his. This is the operative precedent. Here too, as the *Gemara* acknowledges, the prohibition is one applicable to ordinary use (*tashmishei avodah zarah*) rather than strictly to the *mizbeach*, and the vessels are inanimate rather than animals — but the governing principle, that physical use of another's property in idolatrous worship can transfer forbidden status, is established.

## Ground/Money Status

### Natural Ground

Further teachings arrived from Eretz Yisrael bearing applications and extensions of these principles. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he taught in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: even though one who bows down to natural ground — *karka olam*, the ordinary ground of the earth — does not thereby render it forbidden (just as bowing before another's animal without a physical act fails to render it forbidden), there is an exception.

### Digging Exception

If one digs wells, cisterns, or caves in the ground in the context of worshipping it, one has performed a physical act that effectively changes the ground itself. That act renders the dug formation forbidden. Presumably, this applies to the specific portion of ground that was altered — the well or cave — not to the surrounding landscape. The principle is the same as with another's animal: absent a physical act, the ground (which, like another's animal, is not "yours" to control) cannot be rendered forbidden; but the physical act of digging constitutes sufficient engagement to create *issur*.

### Payment Exchange

When Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came from Eretz Yisrael, he taught another teaching of Rabbi Yochanan: even though bowing to an animal does not render it forbidden, if an animal was given as payment in exchange for an *avodah zarah* — if one sold an idol and received an animal in return — that animal acquires the forbidden status of the idol itself. The mechanism here is not *ne'evad* (the animal was not worshipped) nor *tashmish* (it was not used in the service of worship). Rather, it is the principle of *chalifin* — the status of the *avodah zarah* travels through the transaction and attaches to whatever was received in exchange. The animal is not itself *avodah zarah*, but having served as the direct payment for one, it inherits the same *issur hana'ah*.

### Money Paid

This naturally introduces the broader question of money paid in exchange for *avodah zarah*. Money used to purchase an idol, or received from its sale, is forbidden in *hana'ah* — a topic whose foundations were laid in the earlier *perakim* of the tractate. The question now arising is what happens when that money is used to purchase something else, and then that something is exchanged again, and again.

## Exchanges Debate

### Direct Payment Only

When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael he reported that this question is the subject of a *machloket* between Rabbi Yishmael berabbi Yosi and the Rabbis. One opinion holds: the *chalifin* — the initial direct payment received for the *avodah zarah* — is forbidden. But *chalifei chalifin* — what one buys with that payment — is permitted. The *issur* does not propagate beyond the first exchange.

### Infinite Extension

The other opinion holds: even *chalifei chalifin* — two or more degrees removed from the original *avodah zarah* — remains forbidden. The *issur* extends indefinitely through every subsequent transaction.

### Biblical Source

What is the reasoning of the opinion that extends the prohibition infinitely? The *pasuk* states: *"v'hayita cherem kamo hu"* — and you shall be *cherem*, under the ban, like it (Deuteronomy 7:26). The phrase *"kol she'ata mehaye mimenahu"* — anything that derives or emerges from it — is understood to include anything that comes about as a result of the *avodah zarah*, including successive exchanges. Since *kamo hu* is read expansively, the *issur* follows through every link in the chain.

### Kamo'hu Limit

What does the other opinion, which limits the *issur* to the first exchange, do with the phrase *kamo hu*? That opinion reads *kamo hu* restrictively: "like it" — meaning only that which is directly and immediately received in its stead, not further removes. The *issur* is like the original object in that the first exchange inherits its status, but only the first. And what does this opinion do with the need to exclude *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem* (other categories of *issur hana'ah*) from this chain-transfer principle? It holds that *kamo hu* does precisely that work — limiting the extension of transferred *issur* to *avodah zarah* specifically, and excluding *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem*. The proof: if a woman received money from the sale of *orlah* or *kilayei hakerem* as her *kesef kiddushin*, she would nonetheless be *mekudeshet* (betrothed), since that money does not itself carry *issur hana'ah* status. (Rashi notes that using such money for *kiddushin* would still be problematic because the person performing the *kiddushin* is deriving *hana'ah* — but that is a separate consideration from the money's intrinsic status.) The principle that transferred funds carry *issur hana'ah* applies to *avodah zarah* alone, not to other prohibitions.

## Shemitah Parallel

### Sanctity Transfer

The other opinion — the one that extends the *issur* of *avodah zarah* through all generations of exchange — must address the question of *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem* differently. It argues: I derive the exclusion of *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem* not from *kamo hu* at all, but from the hermeneutical principle of *shenei kesuvim haba'im k'echad*. The *Torah* teaches the principle of transferred *issur* in two separate contexts: *avodah zarah* (as just cited) and *shemitah* produce. The *pasuk* regarding *shemitah* states: *"ki yovel hi kodesh tihyeh lachem"* — it is *yovel* (Jubilee), it shall be holy to you — and from the analogy to *kodesh* (sanctified Temple property), we derive that just as sanctity transfers from a blemished *korban* to the money received in its redemption, so too the sanctity of *shemitah* produce transfers to the money received in exchange for it.

### Two Verses Rule

The principle of *shenei kesuvim haba'im k'echad ein melamdin* holds that when the Torah states the same *halacha* in two separate cases, those two cases are to be understood as exceptional — and we do not extrapolate from them to a general rule applicable elsewhere. If the Torah had stated this principle of transferred status only once, we might have used that one case as a paradigm for all prohibited objects, including *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem*. But since it states it twice — once for *avodah zarah* and once for *shemitah* — we conclude that these are the exceptions, and the default rule is that transferred status does *not* carry forward. Accordingly, *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem* do not pass their *issur* on to funds received in exchange for them, and no use of *kamo hu* is needed to establish this.

### Kedesh vs. Shemitah

But then *kamo hu* is left without a function in this opinion — it need not exclude *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem*, since *shenei kesuvim* already does that. And this opinion holds that *kamo hu* does not limit the transfers to the first generation either. What then does *kamo hu* teach? The *Gemara* explores the distinction between *kodesh* (Temple sanctification) and *shemitah* (Sabbatical-year sanctity). With *kodesh*, when a blemished animal is redeemed and the sanctity is transferred to the redemption money, the animal itself exits its sanctified status and becomes ordinary. One might analogize this to *shemitah*: perhaps when *shemitah* produce is sold, the sanctity transfers to the money and the produce itself becomes ordinary. The verse therefore adds *"tihyeh"* — *"b'havayasa teha"* — it shall remain in its original state. The *kedushah* never departs from the original *shemitah* produce; even as it passes forward to whatever is exchanged for it, the original retains its sanctity.

### Continuous Exchange

The *Gemara* then traces the mechanics of how *kedushas shevi'is* travels through a sequence of transactions. If one exchanged *shemitah* grain for meat, the grain retains its *kedushah* and the meat acquires it. If one then exchanged the meat for fish, the *kedushah* departs the meat and attaches to the fish. Fish exchanged for wine — the *kedushah* moves to the wine. Wine for oil — it moves to the oil. The operative rule is *acharon acharon nitpas b'shevi'is* — the last item in the chain carries the *kedushah* of *shemitah*, while the original produce remains permanently *assur*. With each successive exchange, the *kedushah* travels forward and attaches to the newest acquisition, while the original is never released.

This same traveling structure applies, according to the opinion of infinite extension, to the *issur* of *avodah zarah*: each successive exchange passes the *issur* forward, and the original object remains forbidden. The opinion that limits to the first exchange reads *kamo hu* as imposing precisely this limitation — and does not accept that *shenei kesuvim* alone suffices to exclude *orlah* and *kilayei hakerem*, making *kamo hu* necessary for that function instead.

## God Permits Idolatry

### Mishnah Question

The *Gemara* concludes this section of *Avodah Zarah* — for after this *mishnah* the remaining *Gemara* will be devoted to *yayin nesech* — with a philosophically significant exchange. The *mishnah* reports: the Jewish elders were asked in Rome — *"im ein retzono ba'avodas kochavim, lamah eino mevatlah?"* — if your God does not want idolatry, why does He not destroy it? The question is striking, particularly given the tractate's sustained engagement with the practical, legal, and social dimensions of living in a world suffused with *avodah zarah*. Beyond the legal questions, there is a theological one: why does God allow this to persist?

### Natural Objects

The elders responded: if people worshipped things the world had no need of, God would destroy them. But look at what is actually worshipped — the sun, the moon, the stars, the constellations. Should God destroy His world on account of fools? The point is that the objects of idolatrous veneration are often the very structures of the natural world, whose existence is essential. God will not undo creation because human beings have foolishly divinized it.

The philosophers respond: fine — but then why does God not at least destroy the statues and images, the things the world does not need? If those are an offense to Him, and their destruction would harm no one, why do they remain?

The elders' answer is subtle: even if God were to destroy the man-made idols while leaving the sun and moon and stars intact, the effect would be counterproductive. Idol worshippers would look at the surviving celestial bodies and conclude not that God is true, but that their own idols — the ones still standing, the heavenly ones — are real. Rather than *chillul Hashem*, their continued existence prevents an even greater *chillul*. Removing the statues in isolation would only deepen the pagan conviction.

### Natural Law

The *Gemara* then cites the parallel teaching of the rabbis (*tanu rabanan*), which presents the same philosophical question and initial answer but adds a deepened theological principle. After noting that God does not destroy the world on account of fools — *"ye'aved olamo mipnei hashotim?"* — the *Gemara* adds: *"ella olam keminhago noheg"* — rather, the world proceeds according to its natural course. This is more than a pragmatic explanation. It is a fundamental theological claim: God has constituted the world to operate by natural law, and does not intervene in the details, even when things occur that are contrary to His will. The world was designed to run on its own regularities. And: *"v'shotim shekilkelu atidin litein es hadin"* — those fools who corrupted and acted wickedly will ultimately be held accountable.

### Judgment

The principle of *olam keminhago noheg* thus contains two components: the world runs by its natural laws regardless of the moral quality of human action, and divine judgment is deferred but real. The two are not in tension — the deferral of punishment is itself part of how the world was constructed to operate.

### Theft Example

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a parable. If a person steals a measure of wheat and plants it in the ground, the moral logic might demand that the wheat not sprout — that the natural world register the moral wrong and refuse to cooperate. Yet the wheat grows. *"Ella olam keminhago noheg"* — the world follows its natural course. The thief will answer for his crime, but nature does not itself adjudicate human wrongdoing.

### Adultery Example

Another illustration: if a man engages in adultery, one might think nature ought not permit conception — that God would withhold His creative cooperation from an act of profound moral violation. Yet pregnancy can result. Again: *"olam keminhago noheg"* — and the wrongdoers will face judgment.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish adds a final, deeply poignant dimension to this theme. He teaches in God's name, as it were: *"lo dayan leresha'im she'osim tzalmi pumbi"* — it is not enough that the wicked treat the divine image — *tzelem Elokim*, the human being — with contempt, making it common and disrespected. But beyond that: *"ella shematrichin osi u'machtimin osi al korchi"* — they burden Me and force My seal upon something against My will. When human beings engage in adultery and conception results, God is, in a sense, compelled to participate — to animate that new life with a *tzelem Elokim*, a divine image. The world's natural laws, which God Himself set in motion, operate in a way that sometimes presses God, as it were, into reluctant participation in the consequences of human sin.

This is a remarkable closing teaching. It goes beyond the pragmatic explanation of why *avodah zarah* persists, or even beyond the philosophical principle of *olam keminhago noheg*. It expresses something of the pathos of the divine posture in a world of human freedom: God, having created a world that operates by its own laws and having granted human beings genuine moral agency, finds Himself drawn into the results of human choices — even choices that violate His will. The world He made, and the natural order He established, sometimes works against His own intentions, and He bears it.
