# Menachot 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Semicha Rules Overview

### Mishna Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל קׇרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּבּוּר אֵין בָּהֶם סְמִיכָה, חוּץ מִן הַפָּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת, וְשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף שָׂעִיר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: For **all communal offerings there is no** mitzva of **placing hands** on the head of the offering, **except for the bull that comes** to atone **for** a community-wide violation of **any** one **of the mitzvot** that was perpetrated due to an erroneous ruling of the Sanhedrin, where the judges of the Sanhedrin are required to place their hands upon its head (see Leviticus 4:13–21); **and the scapegoat** brought on Yom Kippur, upon which the High Priest places his hands (see Leviticus, chapter 16). **Rabbi Shimon says: Also** in the case of the **goat** that comes to atone for a community-wide perpetration of **idol worship** that occurred due to an erroneous ruling of the Sanhedrin, the judges of the Sanhedrin are required to place their hands upon its head (see Numbers 15:22–26).

The mishna that opens Menachot 92a begins: *kol korbanos hatzibur ain bahem semicha* — all communal sacrifices do not require *semicha*, the laying of hands upon the animal's head, with two exceptions: the *par he'elem davar shel tzibbur* and the *se'ir hamishtalach*. At first glance, this mishna seems out of place in Masechet Menachot, which is primarily concerned with flour offerings and their associated laws. The explanation lies in the structure of the surrounding mishnayot. The previous mishna addressed *kol korbanos hatzibur ve'yachid teunim nesachim* — which sacrifices require *nesachim*, the accompanying wine libation and flour-and-oil offering — and it drew a line between communal and individual offerings. Before that, another mishna discussed the measuring vessels used in the *mikdash*, which were also relevant to the flour and oil. This mishna, then, continues in the same *kol*-formulation style, even though it concerns animal sacrifices rather than *menachos* proper.

### Tzibbur vs. Yachid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל קׇרְבְּנוֹת הַיָּחִיד טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְהַפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **All offerings of an individual require placing hands, except for the firstborn** offering, **the** animal **tithe** offering, **and the Paschal offering.**

The structural logic of the mishna divides along the axis of *korban tzibbur* versus *korban yachid*. The communal sacrifices, as a rule, do not receive *semicha*. One can appreciate why: *semicha* signifies ownership and personal connection between the offerer and the sacrifice. By definition, a *korban tzibbur* belongs to the entire community, and it is simply not feasible to have all of *am yisrael* lay their hands upon it. Even if some representative body were to do so, this raises the question of whether that adequately captures the ownership relationship that *semicha* is meant to express. The *maamados* described in Masechet Taanis offer a partial model of communal representation, but the sense of individual ownership is attenuated in any communal sacrifice.

### Par He'elem Davar

The first exception is the *par he'elem davar shel tzibbur*, the special ox brought when the *beis din* issues an erroneous ruling and the community acts upon it in error. This is the subject of Masechet Horayos and is described in Parshat Vayikra. The relevant text reads:

וְסָמְכוּ זִקְנֵי הָעֵדָה אֶת יְדֵיהֶם עַל רֹאשׁ הַפָּר לִפְנֵי ה' וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַפָּר לִפְנֵי ה'

The elders of the congregation lay their hands upon the ox before God, and the ox is slaughtered. The explicit mention of *semicha* in this *pasuk* is what mandates it here. This exception is understandable: although the offering is technically communal, it is closely tied to the *beis din* — a small, defined body of individuals whose ruling caused the sin. The *sanhedrin* acts as the identifiable owners in a much tighter sense than the diffuse public behind ordinary communal offerings.

The mishna also records that Rabbi Shimon extends this framework: *af se'ir avodas kochavim* — even the special goat brought when the community commits *avodah zarah* following a mistaken ruling of the *beis din* requires *semicha*. This is rooted in Parshat Shelach, where the Torah describes a scenario in which the entire community errs regarding one of all the commandments — understood by *chazal* as a reference to *avodah zarah*, the sin that is *keneged* all the *mitzvos*. The verse prescribes both an ox and a goat as a *chatat*, yet does not mention *semicha* explicitly. Rabbi Shimon argues that this offering too should carry *semicha*.

### Yachid Exceptions

The second portion of the mishna addresses individual sacrifices: *kol korbanos hayachid teunim semicha* — all individual offerings require *semicha* — with three exceptions: *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *pesach*. These three are categorically different from ordinary individual offerings. The *pesach* is fundamentally oriented toward the experience of eating and the celebration of *yetzias mitzrayim*; the act of *hakrava* is in service of the eating rather than standing as an independent act of connection to God. Similarly, *bechor* and *ma'aser* function more as gifts transferred to the *kohen* via the sacrificial process than as direct expressions of the individual's personal devotion. If *semicha* is an expression of ownership and personal identification with the offering as a vehicle of connection to God — whether in atonement or in gift — then these three occupy a fundamentally different category. Just as they are treated differently with respect to *nesachim* based on their distinct character, so too they are excluded from the requirement of *semicha*.

### Yoresh Inherit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיּוֹרֵשׁ סוֹמֵךְ, וּמֵבִיא נְסָכִים, וּמֵימֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The mitzva of placing hands is performed by the owner of the offering. The mishna adds: **And** if the owner died, then **the heir** is regarded as the offering's owner and so he **places** his **hands** on the offering **and brings** the accompanying **libations. And** furthermore, **he can substitute** a non-sacred animal for it. Although it is prohibited to perform an act of substitution, if the owner of an offering does this, his attempt is successful to the extent that the non-sacred animal is thereby consecrated, even though the original offering also remains sacred.

The mishna then raises the case of the *yoresh*, the heir. If a father consecrated an animal and died before bringing it, and his son inherited it, is the son considered the *ba'al* — the owner — such that he may perform *semicha*? The mishna answers affirmatively: *hayoresh somech umevi nesachim umeimir*. The heir performs *semicha*, brings the required *nesachim*, and is even capable of effecting *temurah* — the legally prohibited yet halachically operative exchange of a consecrated animal, which transfers sanctity to the substitute. All three of these — *semicha*, *nesachim*, and *temurah* — share a grounding in ownership. The mishna's ruling that the heir may do all three reflects a consistent principle: inheritance creates full legal ownership, sufficient for all halachic purposes tied to the status of *ba'alim*.

### Se'ir Hamishtalach

The second exception in the communal category is the *se'ir hamishtalach*, the goat sent off to *azazel* on Yom Kippur. Here the Torah is explicit — Aharon lays both hands upon the head of the living goat, confesses the sins of all Israel, and sends it to the wilderness. This case is qualitatively different from ordinary *semicha*. The *se'ir hamishtalach* is not brought as an offering to God and is not about a *ba'al* expressing ownership. Rather, the act is one of transferring sin — placing the transgressions of the community symbolically onto the goat's head. The *semicha* here is not an act of ownership; it is an act of confession and transfer. That explains why it functions as an exception to the general rule: it is exceptional not merely in being permitted despite being a communal sacrifice, but because its *semicha* is of an entirely different nature.

## Baraita & Debate

### Baraita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל קׇרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּבּוּר אֵין בָּהֶן סְמִיכָה, חוּץ מִפַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת וּשְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֵין בָּהֶן סְמִיכָה, וְאֶת מִי אָבִיא תַּחְתֵּיהֶם – שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: For **all communal offerings there is no** mitzva of **placing hands, except for the bull that comes** to atone **for** a community-wide violation of **any** one **of the mitzvot, and** the **goats** that come to atone **for** a community-wide violation of the prohibition **of idol worship;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yehuda says: There is no** mitzva of **placing hands** with regard to the **goats** that come to atone **for idol worship. But** if this is the *halakha*, **which** offering **shall I bring in their place? The scapegoat.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that refines the positions recorded in the mishna. The *baraita* states: *kol korbanos hatzibur ein bahem semicha chutz mipar haba al kol hamitzvos ushirei avoda kochavim divrei Rabbi Shimon* — all communal sacrifices do not receive *semicha* except for the ox brought for all the commandments and the goats for *avodah zarah*, in the words of Rabbi Shimon. Two features of this *baraita* are immediately striking compared to the mishna. First, Rabbi Shimon here does not include the *se'ir hamishtalach* in his list of exceptions, even though the Torah explicitly mandates *semicha* there. Second, he instead includes the *se'irei avodas kochavim* as his second exception alongside the *par he'elem davar*.

### R. Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָא מְעַיֵּיל? אָמַר רָבִינָא: גְּמִירִי שְׁתֵּי סְמִיכוֹת בְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interrupts the citation of the *baraita* with a question: Why does Rabbi Yehuda search for an additional case? Is it **not possible not to insert** an additional case? **Ravina said:** It **is learned** as a tradition that there are **two** instances in which **placing hands** is required **for communal** offerings.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *se'irei avodas kochavim ain bahem semicha* — the goats for *avodah zarah* do not receive *semicha*. The *baraita* then records a curious move on Rabbi Yehuda's part: since he rejects the *se'irei avodas kochavim* as an exception, he requires a substitute for the second slot. Ravina explains this by citing a tradition — *gemiri shtei semichot batzibbur* — that there are exactly two cases in which the *tzibbur* performs *semicha*. Since the tradition fixes the number at two, and Rabbi Yehuda does not accept the *se'irei avodas kochavim*, he must find an alternative. That alternative, in his view, is the *se'ir hamishtalach* itself.

## Baraita & Debate (continued)

### R. Shimon Rejoins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וַהֲלֹא אֵין סְמִיכָה אֶלָּא בִּבְעָלִים, וְזֶה – אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו סוֹמְכִין בּוֹ! אָמַר לוֹ: אַף זֶה – אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו מִתְכַּפְּרִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Shimon said to** Rabbi Yehuda: How can you include the scapegoat as one of the two cases requiring the placing of hands? **Isn't it** the *halakha* that **placing hands** can be performed **only by** the offering's **owner,** i.e., the one who will achieve atonement through the sacrifice of the offering? **And** with regard to **this** offering, the scapegoat, it is **Aaron** the High Priest **or** whichever of **his sons** serves as High Priest who **places** his **hands on it,** and yet it is not he who achieves atonement through it. Rabbi Yehuda **said to him:** With regard to **this** offering **as well,** that *halakha* is fulfilled because **Aaron and his sons** are considered owners, as they also **achieve atonement through it** together with the rest of the community.

Rabbi Shimon responds to Rabbi Yehuda's inclusion of the *se'ir hamishtalach*: *vahalo ein semicha ella be'ba'alim, v'zeh Aharon uvanav somchim bo* — but surely *semicha* is only valid when performed by the owners themselves, and in this case it is Aharon and his sons who perform the *semicha*! Rabbi Shimon is not denying that Aharon lays his hands on the goat; he is arguing that that act is not *semicha be'ba'alim* — not the *semicha* of ownership. The entire discussion of who counts as a *ba'al* for purposes of *semicha*, including the ruling about the *yoresh*, presupposes that *semicha* must be performed by those who stand in an ownership relationship to the offering. The *se'ir hamishtalach* belongs to *am yisrael* — as the verse says, *ume'es adas bnei Yisrael yikach shnei se'irei izzim l'chatat* — two goats, for the community. Aharon performing *semicha* is therefore not the *semicha* of an owner; it is an act of placing sins on the goat's head. That is a fundamentally different category.

## Aharon Semicha Type

### Semicha Be'ba'alim


The conceptual crux of Rabbi Shimon's objection is the distinction between *semicha be'ba'alim* and other forms of hand-laying. Ordinary *semicha* is an act of ownership and identification: the *ba'al* presses both hands on the head of his offering, expressing that this animal represents him and goes in his stead. The prohibition against designating a *shaliach* to perform one's *semicha* flows directly from this: *semicha* is inherently personal and cannot be delegated. The act that Aharon performs on the *se'ir hamishtalach*, by contrast, is one of confession and transference of sin — *vehitvada alav et kol avonot bnei Yisrael*. That the physical gesture resembles ordinary *semicha* does not make it halakhically equivalent.

### Aharon Representative


Rabbi Yehuda counters with a pointed response: *af zeh Aharon uvanav miskapprim bo* — Aharon and his sons are themselves among those atoned for by the *se'ir hamishtalach*. Thus, even if the goat formally belongs to all of Israel, Aharon is not acting as a mere functionary or *shaliach*. He is among the *ba'alim* — one of the owners in the sense that he is one of those whose sins are being atoned for. He represents the community not merely as its agent but as one of its members, and that membership gives his act the character of *semicha be'ba'alim*.

### Yirmiya Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the dispute. **Rabbi Yirmeya said: And** Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda, who disagree as to whether the High Priest achieves atonement through the scapegoat, each **follow** their standard line of **reasoning.**

Rabbi Yirmiya observes that this dispute is not isolated: *v'azdu l'ta'amayhu* — both Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda are following their respective positions as known from elsewhere. The disagreement about whether Aharon's act on the *se'ir hamishtalach* constitutes *semicha be'ba'alim* is a downstream consequence of a deeper disagreement about the structure of atonement on Yom Kippur, which each *tanna* applies consistently across multiple contexts.

### Shevuot Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: "וְכִפֶּר אֶת מִקְדַּשׁ הַקֹּדֶשׁ" – זֶה לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים, "וְאֶת אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד" – זֶה הֵיכָל, "מִזְבֵּחַ" – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ, "יְכַפֵּר" – אֵלּוּ הָעֲזָרוֹת, "כֹּהֲנִים" – כְּמַשְׁמָעָן, "עַל כׇּל עַם הַקָּהָל" – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, "יְכַפֵּר" – אֵלּוּ הַלְוִיִּם.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: At the end of the passage delineating the Yom Kippur Temple service, the verse states: "He shall bring atonement upon the sanctum of the sacred, and upon the Tent of Meeting and the altar he shall effect atonement; and upon the priests and upon all the people, shall he bring atonement" (Leviticus 16:33). **"He shall bring atonement upon the sanctum of the sacred"; this** is referring to **the innermost sanctum,** i.e., the Holy of Holies. **"And upon the Tent of Meeting"; this** is referring to the **Sanctuary.** "And the **altar";** this is understood **in accordance with its plain meaning. "He shall effect atonement"; this** is referring to the Temple **courtyards.** "And upon the **priests";** this is understood **in accordance with its plain meaning.** "And **upon all the people"; these are the Israelites. "Shall he bring atonement"; this** is referring to **the Levites.**

A *baraita* from Masechet Shevuot is then cited to illuminate this deeper disagreement. The *baraita* interprets the sequence of verses at the end of the Yom Kippur service:

> וְכִפֶּר אֶת מִקְדַּשׁ הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְאֶת אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד וְאֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ יְכַפֵּר וְעַל הַכֹּהֲנִים וְעַל כָּל עַם הַקָּהָל יְכַפֵּר

The *baraita* parses each phrase: *mikdash hakodesh* — this is the *lifnai v'lifnim*, the inner sanctum; *ohel moed* — this is the *heichal*, where the blood was sprinkled on the *paroches*; *hamizbe'ach* — this is straightforwardly the golden altar. The initial phrase *vekipper* thus covers the atonement of the *mikdash* itself through the blood sprinklings. Then the verse continues: *yechapper al hakohanim v'al kol am hakahal yechapper*. This final *yechapper* refers to the *azaros* — the Temple courtyards, even where no blood sprinkling took place, which are also purified through the service. And then: *kohanim kemashma'an* — the *kohanim* are the *kohanim*; *kol am hakahal* — these are *yisrael*; and the additional *yechapper* explicitly includes the *levi'im*, who might otherwise have been subsumed within *yisrael*. The conclusion of this *baraita* according to Rabbi Yehuda is: *hushvu kulam l'kappara achat shemiskapprim b'se'ir hamishtalach* — all of them — *kohanim*, *yisrael*, and *levi'im* — are equated in receiving one atonement, namely the *se'ir hamishtalach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּשְׁווּ כּוּלָּן לְכַפָּרָה אַחַת, שֶׁמִּתְכַּפְּרִין בְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **All of them are equated with regard to** the fact that they are all atoned for through **one atonement,** i.e., **that they are atoned for by the scapegoat** for all transgressions other than the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

## R. Shimon Parallel

### Par-Se'ir Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁדַּם שָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים מְכַפֵּר עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – כָּךְ דַּם הַפָּר מְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו. כְּשֵׁם שֶׁוִּידּוּי שֶׁל שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ מְכַפֵּר עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת – כָּךְ וִידּוּי שֶׁל פָּר מְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Shimon says: Just as** the **blood of the goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary **atones for Israelites for** their **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **so too,** the **blood of the bull** of the High Priest, whose blood presentation is also performed inside the Sanctuary, **atones for the priests for** their **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods. And **just as** the **confession** made **over the scapegoat atones for Israelites for other transgressions, so too,** the **confession** made **over** the **bull atones for the priests for other transgressions.** It is apparent from the *baraita* that it is only according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda that the scapegoat atones for both Israelites and priests, and consequently the High Priest may be considered an owner with regard to the mitzva of placing hands.

Rabbi Shimon's position is structured around a precise and elegant parallelism between the ox and the goat of Yom Kippur. His formulation is: *keshem shedam se'ir hane'aseh bifnim mechapper al Yisrael al tumat mikdash v'kodashav, kach dam hapar mechapper al hakohanim al tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — just as the blood of the goat done on the inside atones for Israel for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, so the blood of the ox atones for the *kohanim* for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. The ox — the *par* on which Aharon performs *semicha* and recites *viduy* — is the exclusive property of the priestly atonement process, not a communal offering in the usual sense. The Torah's statement that Aharon brings atonement for himself and his household and all the congregation of Israel is understood as describing two separate tracks operating in parallel, not a single unified atonement.

### Viduy Division


The parallelism extends to *viduy* and the atonement for all other sins. Rabbi Shimon continues: *keshem sheviduy shel se'ir hamishtalach mechapper al Yisrael b'she'ar aveiros, kach viduy shel par mechapper al hakohanim b'she'ar aveiros* — just as the *viduy* recited over the *se'ir hamishtalach* atones for Israel for sins other than *tumat mikdash*, so the *viduy* recited over the *par* atones for the *kohanim* for their other sins. The logic maps neatly: the two goats together form a single *korban* for Israel — one goat's blood goes *bifnim* for *tumat mikdash*, the other is sent to *azazel* with the *viduy* for *she'ar aveiros*. The *par* serves the same dual function for the *kohanim*: its blood goes *bifnim* for their *tumat mikdash*, and the *viduy* accompanying it covers their other transgressions. There is no separate *korban* carrying the *kohanim's* sins in the *she'ar aveiros* category; their *par* does double duty.

This is not merely a technical claim about how atonement is allocated. It reflects a principled reading of the Torah's structure: *ume'es adas bnei Yisrael yikach shnei se'irei izzim l'chatat* — the two goats are taken from the community and are for the community, while the *par* is explicitly Aharon's. The *viduy* on the *se'ir hamishtalach* is therefore not Aharon's *viduy* as one of the atoned; it is the *kohen's* functional act performed on behalf of *yisrael's* offering. From Rabbi Shimon's standpoint, this disqualifies it as *semicha be'ba'alim*.

One might ask why Rabbi Shimon would insist on separating the *kohanim* from the rest of Israel even for *she'ar aveiros* — general sins seemingly unrelated to their priestly function. A possible way of understanding this is that in the Torah's conception, all human sin generates a kind of metaphysical *tumah* that penetrates the *mikdash* — *lo titam'u bahem* applies even to sins like *ma'achalos assuros* or *arayos*. The verse at the end of the Yom Kippur section says *hashochen itam betoch tum'osam* — God manages to dwell among Israel even amidst the *tumah* they generate, and the Yom Kippur service makes this possible. If that is so, then the *kohanim*, who are more centrally embedded in the *mikdash*, may generate a qualitatively more penetrating *tumah* through their sins. Their atonement therefore requires a more interior track — one that parallels the interior sprinkling of blood — even for general sins.

### Equality Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָא וַדַּאי הוּשְׁווּ? אִין, הוּשְׁווּ דִּבְנֵי כַּפָּרָה נִינְהוּ, מִיהוּ כֹּל חַד וְחַד מְכַפֵּר בִּדְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes Rabbi Shimon's opinion: **And according to Rabbi Shimon,** one can ask: **Weren't** both Israelites and priests **certainly equated** in the verse in Leviticus? In what way are they equated in the verse? The Gemara explains: Yes, according to his opinion **they are equated** in **that they are all subject to atonement** on Yom Kippur, **but each one** of the groups **achieves atonement in its own** way. The priests achieve atonement through the bull brought by the High Priest and his confession, while the Israelites and Levites achieve atonement through the confession over the scapegoat.

Having articulated this parallel structure, the *gemara* presses Rabbi Shimon: does the *pasuk* not explicitly equate *kohanim*, *yisrael*, and *levi'im* in a single atonement — *v'al hakohanim v'al kol am hakahal yechapper*? If the verse places them all in one category, how can Rabbi Shimon insist that their atonements come through different vehicles?

### Each Group Atoned


The resolution is concise and precise: *hushvu — dibnei kappara ninhu. Mihu, kol chad vechad mechapper bidnafshei* — yes, they are equated in the sense that all of them are atoned for. But the atonement of each comes through its own channel. The verse affirms that everyone receives *kappara*; it does not demand that the *kappara* come through a single mechanism. Rabbi Shimon reads the verse as guaranteeing the outcome — everyone is atoned — while leaving the structure of how each group achieves that atonement to be derived from elsewhere.

## Tumaa Comparison

### R. Yehuda Tumaa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, יִשְׂרָאֵל מְכַפְּרִי בְּדַם שָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים, וְכֹהֲנִים בְּפַר שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן. בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת, אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מְכַפְּרִי בְּוִידּוּי שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ. וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת נָמֵי, כֹּהֲנִים בְּוִידּוּי דְּפַר מִתְכַּפְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara summarizes: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** for the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **Israelites achieve atonement through** the presentation of the **blood of the goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary, **and** the **priests** achieve atonement **through** the presentation of the blood of the **bull of Aaron,** i.e., of the High Priest. And **for other transgressions,** both **these** Israelites **and those** priests **achieve atonement through** the **confession** made over the **scapegoat. But according to Rabbi Shimon, for other transgressions as well,** the **priests achieve atonement through** the **confession** made **over** the **bull** of the High Priest.

The *gemara* now sharpens the contrast between the two positions. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the differentiation between *kohanim* and *yisrael* applies only in the domain of *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. There, *yisrael* is atoned for through the blood of the goat done on the inside, while the *kohanim* are atoned for through the blood of the ox. In this one domain their paths diverge. But for *she'ar aveiros* — all other sins — *elu va'elu miskapprim beviduy se'ir hamishtalach*: both groups are equally atoned for through the *viduy* of the *se'ir hamishtalach*, with no differentiation.

This view has an internal attractiveness. When it comes to *tumat mikdash*, one can understand why the *kohanim* would require a distinct atonement: they are the ones who work in the *mikdash*, they are more deeply implicated in its *tumah*, and perhaps their culpability in this particular domain is categorically different. But for general sins, the egalitarian position seems more intuitive — we are all sinners before God, *kohen* and *yisrael* alike, and the communal atonement of the *se'ir hamishtalach* reaches everyone.

### R. Shimon All Cases


Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, maintains the separation across all categories. Even for *she'ar aveiros*, the *kohanim's* atonement comes through the *viduy* of the *par*, not through the *se'ir hamishtalach*. The two goats are entirely for Israel; the ox is entirely for the *kohanim*. This parallel structure is complete and admits of no exceptions.

### Source Confirmation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְקָתָנֵי: אֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶחָד כֹּהֲנִים וְאֶחָד כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, מָה בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֲנִים וּלְכֹהֵן מָשִׁיחַ? אֶלָּא שֶׁדַּם הַפָּר מְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** This understanding of their dispute is just **like that** which **is taught** in a mishna (*Shevuot* 2b): **Israelites and priests and** the **anointed priest,** i.e., the High Priest, achieve atonement from the scapegoat equally. **What** is the difference **between Israelites, priests, and** the **anointed priest?** It is **only that the bull** of the High Priest that he offers on Yom Kippur **atones for the priests for** their **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, whereas the Israelites achieve atonement for defiling caused by them through the goats that are sacrificed on Yom Kippur; **this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* confirms this disagreement from the *baraita* in Shevuot: *echad Yisrael v'echad kohanim v'echad kohen mashiach* — all receive atonement for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. The *baraita* then asks what distinguishes them: *ella she'dam hapar mechapper al hakohanim al tumat mikdash v'kodashav divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the only distinction, according to Rabbi Yehuda, is that the blood of the ox atones for the *kohanim* for *tumat mikdash*, while the blood of the goat does so for *yisrael*. Beyond that, they share equally in the atonement of the *se'ir hamishtalach*. Rabbi Shimon's position, as the *baraita* in Shevuot continues, extends the parallel to *she'ar aveiros* as well: *keshem shedam se'ir hane'aseh bifnim mechapper al Yisrael... kach dam hapar mechapper al hakohanim... keshem sheviduyo shel se'ir hamishtalach mechapper al Yisrael, kach viduyo shel par mechapper al hakohanim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁדַּם הַשָּׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים מְכַפֵּר עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל – כָּךְ דַּם הַפָּר מְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים. כְּשֵׁם שֶׁוִּידּוּיוֹ שֶׁל שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ מְכַפֵּר עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל – כָּךְ וִידּוּיוֹ שֶׁל פָּר מְכַפֵּר עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods, **just as** the **blood of the goat, whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary, **atones for Israelites, so** too, the **blood of the bull** of the High Priest, whose blood presentation is also performed inside the Sanctuary, **atones for the priests.** And for all other transgressions, **just as** the **confession** made **over the scapegoat atones for Israelites, so** too, the **confession** made **over** the **bull atones for the priests.**

This disagreement now clarifies why Rabbi Shimon cannot include the *se'ir hamishtalach* in his list of communal sacrifices that require *semicha be'ba'alim*. For Rabbi Shimon, Aharon's act on the *se'ir hamishtalach* is the act of a *kohen* performing a ritual function for Israel's offering — not the act of a *ba'al* connecting to his own sacrifice. Since the *se'ir hamishtalach* belongs entirely to *yisrael* and Aharon's atonement comes through the *par*, Aharon is not among the *ba'alim* of the *se'ir hamishtalach* for Rabbi Shimon's purposes, and his *semicha* on it cannot qualify as *semicha be'ba'alim*.

## Avodas Kochavim

### Yehuda Excludes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְסָמְכוּ זִקְנֵי הָעֵדָה אֶת יְדֵיהֶם עַל רֹאשׁ הַפָּר״ – פַּר טָעוּן סְמִיכָה, וְאֵין שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon disagree as to whether or not the goats that come for a community-wide violation of the prohibition against idol worship require the rite of placing hands. With regard to this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the bull that comes to atone for a community-wide violation of one of the mitzvot: **"And the elders of the congregation shall place their hands upon the head of the bull before the Lord"** (Leviticus 4:15). The verse specifies that this requirement applies to the bull to indicate that, with regard to offerings brought for community-wide transgressions, only the **bull requires placing hands, but** the **goats** brought **for idol worship do not require placing hands;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* now turns to the question of why Rabbi Yehuda excludes the *se'irei avodas kochavim* from *semicha*. A *baraita* is cited: *v'samchu ziknei ha'edah et yedeihem al rosh hapar* — this verse from Parshat Vayikra, describing the *semicha* on the ox of the standard *par he'elem davar shel tzibbur*, is read by Rabbi Yehuda as establishing that the ox specifically requires *semicha*: *par ta'un semiha v'ein se'irei avodas kochavim te'unim semicha divrei Rabbi Yehuda*. The explicit mention of *semicha* by the *par* implicitly excludes the goat that accompanies the sin-offering for communal *avodah zarah*, even though that goat is part of the same general category of *par he'elem davar*. When *beis din* errs on the matter of *avodah zarah*, the offering includes both an ox and a goat, but only the ox carries *semicha*.

### Shimon Chai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: פַּר טָעוּן סְמִיכָה בִּזְקֵנִים, וְאֵין שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה טָעוּן סְמִיכָה בִּזְקֵנִים, אֶלָּא בְּאַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Both offerings require placing hands. The verse specifies the requirement with regard to the **bull** because only it **requires** that **placing hands** be performed **by Elders** of the Sanhedrin, **but** the **goats** brought **for idol worship do not require** that **placing hands** be performed **by the Elders,** but **rather by Aaron,** i.e., the High Priest.

Rabbi Shimon's source for excluding the *se'irei avodas kochavim* from the *semicha* performed by Aharon — while simultaneously including them in a different kind of *semicha* — comes from a different *pasuk* entirely. The verse describing Aharon's act on Yom Kippur reads:

> וְסָמַךְ אַהֲרֹן אֶת שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו עַל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׂעִיר הַחַי וְהִתְוַדָּה עָלָיו אֶת כָּל עֲוֹנוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל

The phrase *al rosh hase'ir hachai* — on the head of the *living* goat — teaches, according to Rabbi Shimon, that it is specifically this living goat that receives this particular form of *semicha*: *chai ta'un semiha b'Aharon v'ein se'irei avodas kochavim te'unim semicha b'Aharon*. The *se'irei avodas kochavim* receive *semicha*, but not the *semicha* performed by Aharon — rather, they receive the *semicha* of the *zekeinim*, *semicha be'ba'alim*. This is the second exception Rabbi Shimon lists alongside the *par he'elem davar*.

### Sheshes Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״הַחַי״ – הַחַי טָעוּן סְמִיכָה, וְאֵין שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חַי״ טָעוּן סְמִיכָה בְּאַהֲרֹן,
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: With regard to the Yom Kippur scapegoat, the verse states: "And Aaron shall place both his hands upon the head of the live goat, and confess over him all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions, even all their sins" (Leviticus 16:21). The emphasis of **"the live** goat" indicates that with regard to communal offerings of goats, only **the live** goat, i.e., the scapegoat, **requires placing hands, but the goats** brought **for idol worship do not require placing hands;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** Both offerings require placing hands. The verse specifies the requirement with regard to the **live** goat to teach that it **requires** that **placing hands** be performed **by Aaron,** i.e., the High Priest,

At this point the *gemara* encounters an apparent contradiction. When responding to the *pasuk* of the *par he'elem davar*, Rabbi Shimon had said that the *se'irei avodas kochavim* receive *semicha* with the *kohanim*. But now, responding to the *pasuk* of the *se'ir hachai*, he says they receive it with the *zekeinim*. These two statements cannot both be correct. Rav Sheshes, before even addressing the contradiction, questions whether the first *baraita* is reliable at all: *v'tisbara deha kammaita metaretzta hi?* — Is the first *baraita* even internally coherent? Given that Rabbi Shimon consistently emphasizes *semicha be'ba'alim* and is skeptical of any *semicha* not performed by genuine owners, it would be strange for him to say that the *kohanim* perform *semicha* on the goat for *avodah zarah*. The *kohanim* are not the *ba'alim* of that offering in any obvious sense. The first *baraita* is therefore suspect on its own terms.

### Corrected Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וְתִסְבְּרָא דְּהָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא מְתָרַצְתָּא הִיא? הָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: סְמִיכָה בִּבְעָלִים בָּעֵינַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: And** how can **you understand that this first** *baraita* **is accurate,** in order to use it as the basis for a contradiction? **But didn't Rabbi Shimon say** that **we require** that **placing hands** be performed **by** the **owners,** i.e., those who will achieve atonement through the offering? The goat brought for idol worship is brought to atone for the Sanhedrin and the people, not for the High Priest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תָּרֵץ הָכִי: ״הַפָּר״ – פַּר טָעוּן סְמִיכָה, וְאֵין שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״הַחַי״ – חַי טָעוּן סְמִיכָה בְּאַהֲרֹן, וְאֵין שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה בְּאַהֲרֹן, אֶלָּא בִּזְקֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, answer** that the earlier *baraita* teaches **as follows:** The verse specifies the requirement of placing hands with regard to **the bull** brought for a community-wide transgression to indicate that only that **bull requires placing hands, but** the **goats** brought **for idol worship do not require placing hands;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** The verse specifies the requirement of placing hands with regard to **the live** goat, i.e., the scapegoat, to indicate that only that **live** goat **requires** that **placing hands** be performed **by Aaron,** i.e., the High Priest, **but the goats** brought for a community-wide perpetration of **idol worship do not require** that **placing hands** be performed **by Aaron. Rather,** they require that it be performed **by the Elders** of the Sanhedrin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָא אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בָּעוּ סְמִיכָה, וְאִי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ דְּלָא בָּעוּ סְמִיכָה – בְּאַהֲרֹן הוּא דִּשְׁמִיעַ לָךְ, וּמִיעוּטָא מֵ״הַחַי״ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And this is what Rabbi Shimon was saying to Rabbi Yehuda: Goats** brought **for idol worship require placing hands. And if you heard** a tradition **that they do not require placing hands, it is** only **with regard to** the fact that it is not to be performed by **Aaron,** i.e., the High Priest, **that you heard** that tradition, **and the exclusion** of the High Priest from having to place hands **is** derived **from** the term **"the live** goat."

The corrected reading of the two *baraisos* proceeds as follows. The first *baraita* records only Rabbi Yehuda's position: *par ta'un semicha v'ein se'irei avodas kochavim te'unim semicha divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the ox requires *semicha*, and the goats of *avodas kochavim* do not. Rabbi Shimon does not respond to the *par* at all in this context. Instead, in the second *baraita*, Rabbi Shimon responds from his own *pasuk* — *chai ta'un semicha b'Aharon v'ein se'irei avodas kochavim te'unim semicha b'Aharon ella b'zekenim* — the living goat gets *semicha* through Aharon, and the goats of *avodas kochavim* do not get the *semicha* of Aharon; they get the *semicha* of the elders. The *gemara* then articulates precisely what Rabbi Shimon is saying to Rabbi Yehuda: *se'irei avodas kochavim ba'u semicha* — the goats for *avodah zarah* do require *semicha*. If you have a tradition that they do not, that tradition is about the specific *semicha* of Aharon; it does not mean they receive no *semicha* at all. The *miyut* — the limiting teaching — derives from *hachai*, and what it limits is the *semicha* of Aharon, not the institution of *semicha* altogether. The goats of *avodas kochavim* receive *semicha be'ba'alim* performed by the *zekenim*, and that is precisely their *semicha* — a genuine *semicha* of owners, not a priestly act of transference.

## Nasi Offering Source

### Yehuda Nasi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לַן מִדְּתַנְיָא: ״וְסָמַךְ יָדוֹ עַל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׂעִיר״ – לְרַבּוֹת שְׂעִיר נַחְשׁוֹן לִסְמִיכָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לִסְמִיכָה, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל חַטָּאת שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמָהּ לִפְנִים טְעוּנָה סְמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **we derive** it **from that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When a king sins…he shall bring for his offering an unblemished male goat. **And he shall place his hand upon the head of the goat"** (Leviticus 4:22–24). The verse could have stated: Upon its head. The reason it adds "of the goat" is **to include** the **goat** brought as a sin offering by **Nahshon** (see Numbers 7:12–17) **in** the requirement of **placing hands** on the head of an offering. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** The term "of the goat" serves **to include** the **goats** brought as sin offerings for communal **idol worship in** the requirement of **placing hands** on the head of an offering, **as Rabbi Shimon would say: Any sin offering whose blood enters inside** the Sanctuary **requires placing hands.**

The *gemara* now asks: from where does Rabbi Shimon derive that the *se'irei avodas kochavim* require *semicha* in the first place? The derivation is traced to a *baraita* interpreting a *pasuk* from the section in Parshat Vayikra dealing with the *nasi* who sins:

> וְסָמַךְ אֶת יָדוֹ עַל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׂעִיר

The *nasi* who unintentionally violates one of God's commandments brings a male goat as a *chatat*, and he performs *semicha* on its head. The *baraita* presents the debate over what the phrase *al rosh hase'ir* — rather than the simpler *al rosho* — comes to include. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the additional specification of *hase'ir* serves to include the goat of *nachshon* — that is, the goats brought by the *nesi'im* at the *chanukas hamishkan*, the dedication of the Tabernacle. Each *nasi* brought, among other offerings, *se'ir izim echad l'chatat* — a goat as a *chatat*. The need for a *pasuk* here is non-trivial: the *chatat* brought by the *nesi'im* was *hora'as sha'ah*, a temporary exceptional ruling, since one ordinarily cannot voluntarily bring a *chatat*; a *chatat* is brought only for an inadvertent sin. Since this *chatat* was exceptional in its origin, one might have thought that *semicha* would not apply to it. The *pasuk* by the *nasi* comes to include even this exceptional case within the requirement of *semicha*.

### Shimon HaSe'ir


Rabbi Shimon reads the same phrase differently. He does not apply it to the *nesi'im's* offering at the *chanukas hamishkan*. Rather, he reads *al rosh hase'ir* as including the goats for *avodah zarah* — *lerabot se'irei avodas kochavim lismicha*. The definite article, signaling that *this* goat's head (not simply *its* head) is specified, points toward an additional goat in a different context that also requires *semicha*. For Rabbi Shimon, that additional goat is the *se'ir* of communal *avodah zarah*.

### Blood Inside Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמֵימְרָא ״שֶׁהָיָה״? סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the concluding statement of the *baraita*: **Why do I** need the *baraita* **to state: As** Rabbi Shimon **would** say? The statement would appear to explain that Rabbi Shimon's ruling is merely an expression of a principle that he held, while the *baraita* itself explains that Rabbi Shimon derived the matter from the repetition of the word "goat." The Gemara explains: The statement is not presenting the basis of his ruling but is **merely a mnemonic** to aid in remembering it.

Rabbi Shimon is recorded as having a general principle: *kol chatat shenichnas damah lifnim te'una semicha* — any *chatat* whose blood is brought inside the sanctuary requires *semicha*. This functions as a *siman*, a mnemonic, helping identify which offerings carry the requirement. The offerings whose blood enters the inner sanctuary include the *par he'elem davar shel tzibbur*, the *par* and *se'ir* of Yom Kippur, and the *se'irei avodas kochavim*. However, this rule is not a precise halakhic derivation; it is explicitly acknowledged to be *simana be'alma* — a memory device rather than the source of the law.

## Why Not Se'ir

### Why Avodas Kochavim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: שָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים! דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא, דִּמְכַפֵּר עַל עֲבֵירוֹת מִצְוָה יְדוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions why Rabbi Shimon expounds the word "goat" as referring to goats brought for idol worship: **But** why not **say** that the word "goat" includes the **goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary on Yom Kippur? The Gemara answers: It is more reasonable to include a goat offering that is **similar to** the offering mentioned in that verse, i.e., the **goat of a king, which atones for known transgressions of a mitzva.** A goat brought for idol worship is similar in this regard, whereas the goat brought on Yom Kippur atones specifically for transgressions of which the transgressor is unaware.

The *gemara* immediately presses the obvious question: if Rabbi Shimon is learning from the *pasuk* of the *nasi* that a certain additional goat requires *semicha*, why does he apply this to the *se'irei avodas kochavim* rather than to the *se'ir hane'aseh bifnim* — the goat whose blood is brought inside on Yom Kippur? That goat is a *korban tzibbur* with no *semicha*, and it too involves blood brought inside. Why not extend the derivation there?

### Specific Known Sin


The answer lies in the character of the *nasi's* offering. The *se'ir* of the *nasi* atones for a specific, known *aveirah* — the *nasi* sinned intentionally enough to recognize he did something wrong and now brings his *chatat* for it. The *se'irei avodas kochavim* similarly atone for a specific, identified sin — the community committed *avodah zarah* knowingly in the sense that the *beis din's* ruling was acted upon, and the nature of that sin is defined. The *se'ir* brought on Yom Kippur for *tumat mikdash*, by contrast, atones for a type of *tumah* that was committed unknowingly — the person was unaware that *tumah* had occurred. This puts it in a categorically different position from the *nasi's* offering, and the analogy therefore does not extend to it.

### Two Semichos Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבִינָא דְּאָמַר: גְּמִירִי שְׁתֵּי סְמִיכוֹת בְּצִיבּוּר, קְרָאֵי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions why Rabbi Shimon needs a verse at all: **And according to** the opinion of **Ravina, who said:** It **is learned** as a tradition that there are **two** instances in which **placing hands** is required **for communal** offerings, **why do I** need any **verses,** i.e., why do I need the word "goat," to include goats brought for idol worship in the requirement of placing hands? Since he holds that the priests do not achieve atonement through the scapegoat, perforce the goat brought to atone for idol worship must require placing hands, as otherwise there would not be two instances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ הִלְכְתָא, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The *halakha*** learned by tradition **was necessary, and** the exposition of **the verses was necessary** as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי מִקְּרָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **As, if** the *halakha* were to be derived only **from the verse, I would say** through an *a fortiori* inference that even **communal peace offerings,** i.e., the two lambs brought with the two loaves on the festival of *Shavuot*, require placing of the hands.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מֵהִלְכְתָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לָא יָדְעִינַן הֵי נִינְהוּ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא, דִּמְכַפֵּר עַל עֲבֵירוֹת מִצְוָה יְדוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** it were derived only **from the *halakha*** learned by tradition, without the exposition of the verse, **I would say** that **we do not know** exactly **which** offering other than the bull brought for a community-wide transgression requires placing hands. Therefore, the word "goat" **teaches us** that the second instance is a goat offering **similar to** the offering mentioned in that verse, specifically the **goat of a king, which atones for known transgressions of a mitzva,** i.e., a goat brought for idol worship.

The *gemara* then returns to Ravina's statement that there is a tradition of *shtei semichot b'tzibbur* — exactly two cases where the community performs *semicha*. Given this tradition, once Rabbi Shimon has the *par he'elem davar* as the first exception, he needs exactly one more. But without the limiting function of the *pasuk*, one might have argued for additional exceptions: the *se'ir hane'aseh bifnim* of Yom Kippur, or perhaps *zivchei shalmei tzibbur* — communal peace offerings — via a *kal vachomer* from individual *shelamim*. The tradition that there are only two cases therefore does real limiting work by capping the number. At the same time, the tradition alone would not tell us which goat fills the second slot.

### Pasuk Tradition


The *pasuk* and the tradition thus function together. The tradition tells us there are exactly two, preventing the inclusion of additional candidates. The *pasuk* from the *nasi's* offering — read through the lens of *dumya d'se'ir nasi d'mechapper al aveiras mitzva yedu'a* — tells us which specific goat fills the second slot: the *se'ir* for communal *avodah zarah*, not the *se'ir hane'aseh bifnim* of Yom Kippur and not *shalmei tzibbur*. The *gemara* concludes: without the tradition, we would know one more case is required but not which one; without the *pasuk*, we would know the number but have no basis for identifying the specific case. Together, they produce Rabbi Shimon's complete position.

## Conclusion Principles

### Semicha Nature

The *sugya* of Menachot 92a revolves around two interlocking conceptual questions. The first is the nature of *semicha* itself: what is it expressing, and who is qualified to perform it? The *sugya* consistently treats *semicha* as fundamentally an act of ownership and personal identification — the *ba'al* connects himself to his offering, declaring that this animal represents him. This is why the heir (*yoresh*) can perform *semicha*: he has genuinely inherited ownership. It is why a *shaliach* cannot: ownership cannot be delegated. And it is why ordinary *korbanot tzibbur* are exempt: the diffuse collective ownership of the community does not crystallize into the kind of identifiable personal ownership that *semicha* requires. The exceptional cases — the *par he'elem davar*, the *se'irei avodas kochavim* according to Rabbi Shimon — are exceptions precisely because the *beis din* or *zekenim* stand in a sufficiently defined ownership relationship to those specific offerings.

### Division Atonement

The second conceptual question is whether the atonement of *yom kippur* treats *kohanim* and *yisrael* as a single undifferentiated community or as two distinct groups each requiring its own track of atonement. Rabbi Yehuda takes the more egalitarian position: for *tumat mikdash*, their atonements diverge by necessity, but for general sins they converge in the *se'ir hamishtalach*. Aharon performing *semicha* on the *se'ir hamishtalach* is therefore *semicha be'ba'alim*, because he too is atoned through it. Rabbi Shimon draws a complete parallel: the two goats of Israel and the one ox of the *kohanim* each operate as fully independent atonement systems, covering both *tumat mikdash* and *she'ar aveiros* for their respective constituencies. Aharon's act on the *se'ir hamishtalach* is therefore a priestly function, not an owner's act. These two disagreements — the conceptual nature of *semicha* and the structure of Yom Kippur atonement — are thus not independent. As Rabbi Yirmiya observes, each *tanna* applies his position consistently, and the dispute about *semicha* on the *se'ir hamishtalach* is the direct and necessary consequence of their deeper disagreement about how the Yom Kippur service is structured.
