# Kiddushin 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yetzer Hara & Torah

### Yetzer Hara Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קָשֶׁה יֵצֶר הָרָע, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ יוֹצְרוֹ קְרָאוֹ רַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יֵצֶר לֵב הָאָדָם רַע מִנְּעֻרָיו״. אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק: יִצְרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מִתְחַדֵּשׁ עָלָיו בְּכׇל יוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רַק רַע כׇּל הַיּוֹם״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לֵוִי: יִצְרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מִתְגַּבֵּר עָלָיו בְּכׇל יוֹם וּמְבַקֵּשׁ הֲמִיתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צוֹפֶה רָשָׁע לַצַּדִּיק וּמְבַקֵּשׁ לַהֲמִיתוֹ״, וְאִלְמָלֵא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עוֹזְרוֹ אֵין יָכוֹל לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים לֹא יַעַזְבֶנּוּ בְיָדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** So **difficult is the evil inclination that even its Creator calls it evil, as it is stated: "For the inclination of a man's heart is evil from his youth"** (Genesis 8:21). **Rav Yitzḥak says: A person's** evil **inclination renews itself to him every day, as it is stated:** "And that every inclination of the thoughts in his heart was **only evil all day [*kol hayyom*]"** (Genesis 6:5). "*Kol hayyom*" can also be understood as: Every day. **And Rabbi Shimon ben Levi says: A person's inclination overpowers him every day, and seeks to kill him, as it is stated: "The wicked watches the righteous and seeks to slay him"** (Psalms 37:32). **And if not** for the fact that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, assists** each person in battling his evil inclination, **he could not** overcome **it, as it is stated: "The Lord will not leave him in his hand"** (Psalms 37:33).

The *daf* opens five lines down from where we left off, with a *tanu rabanan* that addresses the *yetzer hara* with striking directness. *Kashe yetzer hara she'afilu yotzro kara ra* — the *yetzer hara* is so formidable that even its Creator called it evil, as it is written: *ki yetzer lev ha'adam ra mine'urav*. This is worth pausing on. The entire opening of *Bereishit* is structured around *ki tov* — God surveys creation and pronounces it good. The one thing that receives the designation *ra* is what human beings carry within them. That inversion is deliberate and sobering.

Rabbi Yitzchak adds: *yitzro shel adam mitchadesh alav bechol yom* — the *yetzer hara* renews itself against a person every single day, as the verse says *rak ra kol hayom*, it is evil the whole day. And Rabbi Shimon ben Levi goes further still: *yitzro shel adam mitgaber alav bechol yom u'mevakesh hamito* — it grows stronger each day and seeks to destroy you, as it is written, *tzofeh rasha latzadik u'mevakesh lehamito*.

What emerges from these statements is not merely a description of natural sinful impulse but something more vivid and almost personified — a force with creativity, strategy, and malicious intent. This is not simply the *pshat* of a human being's natural lustful drives; the Gemara is drawing on imagery closer to that of a figure who, like a little devil on one's shoulder, is actively scheming to bring a person to ruin. The *yetzer hara*, in this framing, is relentlessly innovating against you, which means the response must be equally dynamic.

### Divine Help & Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּךְ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אָמַר לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: בָּנַי, בָּרָאתִי יֵצֶר הָרָע וּבָרָאתִי לוֹ תּוֹרָה תַּבְלִין. וְאִם אַתֶּם עוֹסְקִים בַּתּוֹרָה – אֵין אַתֶּם נִמְסָרִים בְּיָדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלוֹא אִם תֵּיטִיב שְׂאֵת״, וְאִם אֵין אַתֶּם עוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה – אַתֶּם נִמְסָרִים בְּיָדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַפֶּתַח חַטָּאת רֹבֵץ״, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁכׇּל מַשָּׂאוֹ וּמַתָּנוֹ בְּךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵלֶיךָ תְּשׁוּקָתוֹ״. וְאִם אַתָּה רוֹצֶה אַתָּה מוֹשֵׁל בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה תִּמְשׇׁל בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **So** too **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Israel: My children, I created an evil inclination,** which is the wound, **and I created Torah as its antidote. If you are engaged in Torah** study **you will not be given over into the hand** of the evil inclination, **as it is stated: "If you do well, shall it not be lifted up?"** (Genesis 4:7). One who engages in Torah study lifts himself above the evil inclination. **And if you do not engage in Torah** study, **you are given over to its power, as it is stated: "Sin crouches at the door"** (Genesis 4:7). **Moreover, all** of the evil inclination's **deliberations** will be **concerning you, as it is stated** in the same verse: **"And to you is its desire." And if you wish you shall rule over it, as it is stated** in the conclusion of the verse: **"But you may rule over it"** (Genesis 4:7).

The Gemara then states: *ilmalai Hakadosh Baruch Hu ozro, ein yachollo* — were God not there to assist, a person could not withstand the *yetzer hara*, as the verse says, *Elokim lo ya'azvenu veyado*, God will not abandon him to its hand. This is a striking and somewhat uncomfortable claim. It implies that on one's own, the deck is already stacked against you. There is no simple resort to free will as a sufficient counterbalance — without divine assistance, one is simply outmatched.

This raises a genuine conceptual tension. The Jewish conception typically holds that we possess both a *yetzer hara* and a *yetzer hatov*, and that the capacity for moral choice is genuinely ours. This is not original sin; one does not start out in a state of depravity requiring external salvation. Yet the Gemara insists that at some fundamental level, unaided human striving is insufficient. One way to read this less troublingly is through the principle the Gemara elsewhere articulates: *haba letaher mesay'in oto* — one who comes to purify himself is aided from Above. God does not do it for you; rather, once you exert your own free will and take the first steps on the right path, divine assistance arrives to *chazek*, to strengthen and sustain that effort. On this reading, the Gemara may be offering *chizuk* to someone who feels they are constantly failing — reminding them that the effort to do right, however imperfect, will be met and supported. The Torah itself, given just before this passage, is presented as the divine instrument: *sam tam*, a perfect elixir, the tool provided by God to combat the *yetzer hara*.

It is also worth noting, as one participant in the discussion observed, that there is a tendency in contemporary Orthodox discourse to focus almost entirely on human choice and responsibility, with relatively little explicit acknowledgment of the idea that God is present to assist and strengthen us. The prayers carry this message, but ordinary religious discourse often does not. The Gemara here offers a corrective, however challenging its metaphysics may be.

### Beit Midrash Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בְּנִי אִם פָּגַע בְּךָ מְנֻוּוֹל זֶה – מׇשְׁכֵהוּ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. אִם אֶבֶן הוּא – נִימּוֹחַ, וְאִם בַּרְזֶל הוּא – מִתְפּוֹצֵץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלוֹא כֹה דְבָרִי כָּאֵשׁ נְאֻם ה׳ וּכְפַטִּישׁ יְפֹצֵץ סָלַע״. אִם אֶבֶן הוּא – נִימּוֹחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״ וְאוֹמֵר: ״אֲבָנִים שָׁחֲקוּ מַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage from **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: My son, if this wretched one,** the evil inclination, **encounters you, pull it into the study hall,** i.e., go and study Torah. **If it is a stone** it will **melt, and if it is iron** it will **break, as it is stated** with regard to the Torah: **"Is not My word like fire, says the Lord, and like a hammer that breaks the rock in pieces?"** (Jeremiah 23:29). Just as a stone shatters a hammer, so too one can overcome his evil inclination, which is as strong as iron, through Torah study. With regard to the second part of the statement: **If it is a stone** it will **melt,** this is **as it is stated** with regard to the Torah: **"Ho, everyone who thirsts, come for water"** (Isaiah 55:1), **and it states: "The water wears the stones"** (Job 14:19), indicating that water is stronger than stone.

*Tana debei Rabbi Yishmael* offers an arrestingly direct prescription. *Beni, im pagah becha menuval zeh* — my son, if this abominable thing takes hold of you — *moshchehu leveit hamidrash*, drag it to the *beit midrash*. Make the *yetzer hara* your *chavruta*, as it were. *Im even hu nimoach* — if it is stone, it will dissolve; *veim barzel hu mitpotzetz* — if it is iron, it will shatter. The verse cited is *halo ko devarai ka'esh neum Hashem* — are not My words like fire — *uchefatish yifotzetz sela*, and like a hammer that shatters rock.

*Tosafot* notes a textual difficulty: the verse speaks of the hammer shattering, but the Gemara applies it to the metal being destroyed. *Tosafot* proposes reading it as *al yedei sela*, by means of the rock — or alternatively, separates the two images: the fire melts the metal, and the hammer shatters the stone. It is admittedly a difficult reading of the *pasuk*, but the homiletical point stands. A complementary verse is then cited — *hoy kol tzame lechu lamayim*, come all who are thirsty to water — and *avanim shachaku mayim*, stones are worn away by water, suggesting that sustained immersion in Torah will erode even the hardest resistance.

The circle thus closes: *talmud Torah* is not merely a religious obligation or a form of intellectual cultivation; it is the essential weapon in a daily existential battle. The *chidush* of the *yetzer hara* — its capacity for constant innovation and renewal — demands a corresponding *chidush* in Torah learning as its antidote.

## Father's Obligations

### Marry Off Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַשִּׂיאוֹ אִשָּׁה. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״קְחוּ נָשִׁים וְהוֹלִידוּ בָּנִים וּבָנוֹת וּקְחוּ לִבְנֵיכֶם נָשִׁים וְאֶת בְּנוֹתֵיכֶם תְּנוּ לַאֲנָשִׁים״. בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּנוֹ – בְּיָדוֹ, אֶלָּא בִּתּוֹ – בְּיָדוֹ הִיא? הָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ: נִיתֵּן לַהּ מִידֵּי וְ[נַ]לְבְּשַׁיהּ וּנְכַסְּיַיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דִּ[לְ]קִפְצוּ עֲלַהּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* (29a) teaches that a father is commanded **to marry** his son to **a woman.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this matter? **As it is written: "Take wives and bear sons and daughters, and take wives for your sons and give your daughters to men"** (Jeremiah 29:6). The Gemara analyzes this verse: **Granted** with regard to **his son,** this is **in his power,** i.e., he can instruct him to marry a woman, as a man is the active agent in a marriage. **But** with regard to **his daughter, is** this **in his power?** She must wait for a man to marry her. The Gemara answers: **This is what** Jeremiah was **saying to them** in the aforementioned verse: Her father should **give her something** for her dowry, **and he should dress and cover her** with suitable clothing **so that men will leap to** marry **her.**

The Gemara turns from the *aggadeta* of *yetzer hara* and *Torah* to the father's specific obligations toward his son — including, notably, the obligation to marry him off: *le'hashio isha*. The source (*manalan*) is the verse *kechu nashim veholidu banim uvanot, ukechu livneichem nashim, ve'et benoteichem tenu la'anashim* — take wives, have sons and daughters, take wives for your sons, and give your daughters to men.

What is immediately striking is that this *pasuk* appears to obligate the father toward both sons and daughters in the matter of marriage. The Gemara notes *bishlama beno beyado* — I can understand regarding the son, because in theory the father acts as the initiating party on his son's behalf. But then it says *ella bito beyado hi* — and his daughter is in his hand — which must rank as one of the more ironic statements in this entire *sugya*, given that we learned earlier that a father may actually marry off his daughter against her will, while he cannot do the same for his son. The *Gemara* answers: *leiten lah midai velilbasheih nechasaya, ki heichi dekaftzu alei inshi* — give her gifts, clothe her in ornaments, so that men will be attracted to her and wish to marry her. The father's obligation regarding his daughter, then, is to provide a suitable dowry and presentation, making her a desirable match.

The irony here is layered. Throughout this *masechta*, the father's obligations have been defined as applying to sons, not daughters. Yet here, with barely a pause, the Gemara reads the *pasuk* as extending to daughters as well, without flagging the tension. The deeper irony is that while the father legally has the power to contract his daughter's *kiddushin*, he cannot similarly contract his son's marriage — yet the framework of initiative and representation is mapped onto the son's case as if the father were the active party. As the Gemara elsewhere established, *ish mechazir achar aveidato* — it is the male who pursues; so the father, representing his son, can initiate inquiries and pursue a match, whereas representing his daughter, he can only make her attractive enough to receive such pursuit.

### Teaching Trade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלַמְּדוֹ אוּמָּנוּת. מְנָלַן? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״רְאֵה חַיִּים עִם אִשָּׁה אֲשֶׁר אָהַבְתָּ״. אִם ״אִשָּׁה״ מַמָּשׁ הִיא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁחַיָּיב לְהַשִּׂיאוֹ אִשָּׁה – כָּךְ חַיָּיב לְלַמְּדוֹ אוּמָּנוּת. אִם תּוֹרָה הִיא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁחַיָּיב לְלַמְּדוֹ תּוֹרָה – כָּךְ חַיָּיב לְלַמְּדוֹ אוּמָּנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* further states that a father is commanded **to teach** his son **a trade.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Ḥizkiyya said: As the verse states: "Enjoy life with the wife whom you love"** (Ecclesiastes 9:9). **If** this verse is interpreted literally, and **it** is referring to **an actual woman,** then one can derive as follows: **Just as** a father **is obligated to marry** his son **to a woman, so too, he is obligated to teach him a trade,** as indicated by the term: Life. **And if** the wife mentioned in this verse is allegorical, and **it is the Torah,** then one should explain the verse in the following manner: **Just as he is obligated to teach him Torah, so too, he is obligated to teach him a trade.**

The Gemara then addresses the obligation to teach a trade: *lelamdo omanut*. The source is *re'eh chayim im ishah asher ahavta* — see life with the woman that you love. The Gemara reads this on two levels: if *ishah* means a literal wife, then just as one is obligated to marry off one's son, so too must one teach him a livelihood. If *ishah* is a metaphor for Torah — Torah as the *ishah*, the other woman with whom a man lives his life, a motif Boyarin has explored in his analysis of both the militaristic and romantic metaphors for *talmud Torah* — then just as one must teach Torah, one must also teach a trade.

Rabbi Yehuda sharpens this: *kol she'eino melamdo omanut melamdo listut* — whoever does not teach his son a trade teaches him banditry, or as the Gemara immediately clarifies, *ke'ilu melamdo listut* — it is as if he teaches him banditry, because without a livelihood, what will the child do? The *Gemara* asks what Rabbi Yehuda adds beyond the *Tanna Kamma*, and answers: *de'agmarei iska* — the *Tanna Kamma* might have been satisfied with teaching the son commerce, buying and selling. Rabbi Yehuda insists on an actual *omanut*, a manual trade, because commerce depends on market conditions and may not always sustain a person, whereas a skilled trade — plumbing, tailoring, carpentry — will always find need.

That the value of a specific manual trade is not merely theoretical was brought into sharp relief in the discussion that followed. One member of the group noted that her relative survived the Shoah because his work as a tailor gave him skills the Nazi regime could use; another shared that his knowledge of plumbing was what kept him alive in the camps. *Kol she'eino melamdo omanut melamdo listut* takes on a dimension of literal life and death that no abstract analysis can match.

As for the formal status of these obligations — to marry off one's son, to teach a trade — the sources being cited are from *Navi*, not from explicit Torah commands, and one might question whether these even rise to the level of *mitzvah derabbanan* in a strict sense. They feel more like broad parental responsibilities subsumed under the general obligation to raise a child properly, akin in spirit to *chinuch* — training one's children in the *mitzvot* — but oriented toward life in the world rather than life as a Torah Jew.

### Swimming & Safety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים אַף לַהֲשִׁיטוֹ בַּנָּהָר. מַאי טַעְמָא? חַיּוּתֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* adds: **And some say** that a father is **also** obligated **to teach** his son **to swim in a river.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **It is** necessary for **his life,** i.e., this is potentially a lifesaving skill.

Some authorities add a further obligation: *af lehashito banahar* — to teach one's son to swim in the river. *Mai ta'ama? Chayutei hee* — because it is a matter of preserving life. The ability to swim is presented as a basic life-saving skill a parent is obligated to transmit. Indeed, as was noted in passing, some universities to this day will not grant a degree to a student who cannot swim — an echo of this ancient recognition that swimming is not a luxury but a survival competency.

## Filial Obligations

### Sons & Daughters Equal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מִצְוֹת הָאָב עַל הַבֵּן וְכוּ׳. מַאי כׇּל מִצְוֹת הָאָב עַל הַבֵּן? אִילֵּימָא כֹּל מִצְוָתָא דְּמִיחַיַּיב אַבָּא לְמִיעְבַּד לִבְרֵיהּ נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת – וְהָתַנְיָא: הָאָב חַיָּיב בִּבְנוֹ לְמוּלוֹ וְלִפְדוֹתוֹ. אָבִיו – אֵין, אִמּוֹ – לֹא! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל מִצְוֹת הָאָב הַמּוּטָּלֹת עַל הַבֵּן לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָבִיו, אֶחָד אֲנָשִׁים וְאֶחָד נָשִׁים חַיָּיבִין. תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ, אִשָּׁה מִנַּיִן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״תִּירָאוּ״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **all mitzvot of a father with regard to his son,** both men and women are obligated to perform them. The Gemara inquires: **What** is the meaning of the expression: **All mitzvot of a father with regard to his son? If we say** that this is referring to **all of the mitzvot that a father is required to perform for his son,** are **women obligated** in these? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A father is obligated with regard to his son to circumcise him, and to redeem him?** This indicates that **his father, yes,** he is obligated to do these, but **his mother, no,** she is not obligated to perform these mitzvot for her son. **Rav Yehuda said** that **this is what** the mishna **is saying:** With regard to **each mitzva** for **the father that is incumbent upon the son to perform for his father, both men and women are obligated** in them. The Gemara comments: **We** already **learned this, as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to the verse: **"A man** shall fear [*tira'u*] his mother and his father" (Leviticus 19:3). **I have** derived **only** that **a man** is obligated in this mitzva; **from where** do I derive that **a woman** is also obligated? **When it says** in the same verse: **"A man shall fear [*tira'u*] his mother and his father"** (Leviticus 19:3), employing the plural form of the verb, this indicates that **there are two** that are obligated **here,** both a man and a woman.

The Gemara now pivots decisively to the second major subject of the *amud*: the filial obligations of children toward parents — *kibbud av va'em*. The *mishnah* states *kol mitzvat ha-av al ha-ben* — all obligations of the father upon the son — and the Gemara initially asks what this means. If it refers to the obligations a father has toward his son, does it follow that women are equally obligated in those parental duties? That cannot be right, since we already established that a mother, unlike a father, is not obligated in *brit milah* or *pidyon hebben*.

Rav Yehuda resolves this: the *mishnah* must be read as *kol mitzvat ha-av ha-mutelet al ha-ben la'asot le-aviv* — all obligations that a child owes to a parent — and these fall equally on *echad anashim ve-echad nashim*, both sons and daughters. The *baraita* confirms this: *ish ish imo ve'aviv tira'u* — the plural *tira'u* implies that two people are addressed, both the man and the woman.

### Married Women Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ״? – אִישׁ סִיפֵּק בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת, אִשָּׁה אֵין סִיפֵּק בְּיָדָהּ לַעֲשׂוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְשׁוּת אֲחֵרִים עָלֶיהָ. אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב: נִתְגָּרְשָׁה, שְׁנֵיהֶם שָׁוִים.
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** that both of them are obligated, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Man"?** In the case of **a man,** it is **in his power to perform** this mitzva; whereas with regard to **a woman,** it is **not** always **in her power to perform** this mitzva, **because she is under the authority of another** person, i.e., her husband. As she is obligated to her husband to maintain her household, she is not always able to find time for her parents. **Rav Idi bar Avin says** that **Rav says:** Consequently, if a woman **is divorced,** then **both of them,** a daughter and a son, are **equal** with regard to honoring and fearing their father and mother.

The *baraita* then asks: if the plural *tira'u* includes both men and women, why does the verse emphasize *ish*, man? The answer: *ish she'yesh be-yado la'asot, isha she-ein be-yada la'asot* — a man generally has more capacity to act on behalf of his parents, whereas a married woman's primary obligations run to her husband's household. *Mipnei she'yesh reshut acherim aleha* — because others have authority over her. This does not exempt her from the obligation; it acknowledges that her opportunity to fulfill it is often constrained.

*Tosafot* makes a point worth emphasizing here: legally, a married woman's domestic duties to her husband are only *derabbanan*, not *de-oraita*. The *de-oraita* obligation itself does not subordinate a wife to household service. Yet even *Tosafot* acknowledges that as a practical matter, *einah metzuya etzel avia ela etzel ba'alah* — a married woman's home is her husband's home, not her father's, and her time and energy flow accordingly.

There is an irony here that was pointed out: while the Gemara frames men as having more *opportunity* to honor parents, lived experience frequently reverses this. It is typically the daughter — or the daughter-in-law — who bears the primary burden of caregiving for aging parents, whether her own or her husband's, even in contemporary society with its more egalitarian ideals. The Gemara's theoretical framework and social reality have long been in productive tension on this point.

Rav Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav clarifies that this asymmetry applies only to a married woman. If a woman is divorced — or never married — *shneihem shavin*, her obligation is equal to her brother's. The baseline, then, is genuine equality.

## Parents = God

### Honor Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נֶאֱמַר: ״כַּבֵּד אֶת אָבִיךָ וְאֶת אִמֶּךָ״, וְנֶאֱמַר: ״כַּבֵּד אֶת ה׳ מֵהוֹנֶךָ״, הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב כִּבּוּד אָב וָאֵם לִכְבוֹד הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that **it is stated: "Honor your father and your mother"** (Exodus 20:11), **and it is stated: "Honor the Lord with your wealth"** (Proverbs 3:9). In this manner, **the verse equates the honor of one's father and mother to the honor of the Omnipresent,** as the term "honor" is used in both cases.

The Gemara now presents a series of scriptural parallels that equate the obligations owed to parents with those owed to God. *Ne'emar kabeid et avicha ve'et imecha* — honor your father and mother — and *ne'emar kabeid et Hashem mehonecha* — honor God from your wealth. *Hishvah hakatuv kevod av va'eim likvod hamakom* — the Torah has equated honoring parents with honoring God.

### Reverence Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ אִמּוֹ וְאָבִיו תִּירָאוּ״, וְנֶאֱמַר: ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא וְאֹתוֹ תַעֲבֹד״, הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב מוֹרָאַת אָב וָאֵם לְמוֹרָאַת הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **it is stated: "A man shall fear his mother and his father"** (Leviticus 19:3), **and it is stated: "You shall fear the Lord your God and Him you shall serve"** (Deuteronomy 6:13). **The verse equates the fear of one's father and mother to the fear of the Omnipresent.**

Similarly: *ne'emar ish imo ve'aviv tira'u* — revere your mother and father — alongside *ve'et Hashem elokecha tira ve'oto ta'avod* — you shall revere God and serve Him. *Hishvah hakatuv morat av va'eim lemorat hamakom* — the reverence due to parents is equated with the reverence due to God.

### Cursing Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר: ״מְקַלֵּל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ מוֹת יוּמָת״, וְנֶאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי יְקַלֵּל אֱלֹהָיו וְנָשָׂא חֶטְאוֹ״, הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב בִּרְכַּת אָב וָאֵם לְבִרְכַּת הַמָּקוֹם. אֲבָל בְּהַכָּאָה וַדַּאי אִי אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **it is stated: "He who curses his father or his mother shall be put to death"** (Exodus 21:17), **and it is stated: "Whoever curses his God shall bear his sin"** (Leviticus 24:15). **The verse equates the blessing,** a euphemism for cursing, **of** one's **father and mother to the blessing of the Omnipresent. But with regard to striking,** i.e., with regard to the *halakha* that one who strikes his father or mother is liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment, **it is certainly not possible** to say the same concerning the Holy One, Blessed be He.

And: *ne'emar mekalel aviv ve'imo mot yumat* — one who curses his father or mother is put to death — alongside *ish ish ki yekalel elohav venasa chet'o*, cursing God being treated as a capital offense. *Hishvah hakatuv birchat av va'eim lebirchat hamakom* — the Gemara uses the euphemism *birchat* (blessing) for cursing in both cases, equating them. *Aval behaka'ah* — as for one who strikes a parent being put to death — there is no parallel, since striking God is simply impossible. *Vechein badin* — and it makes sense that parents are thus equated with God.

### Three Partners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּדִין, שֶׁשְּׁלָשְׁתָּן שׁוּתָּפִין בּוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוּתָּפִין הֵן בָּאָדָם: הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְאָבִיו, וְאִמּוֹ. בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָדָם מְכַבֵּד אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מַעֲלֶה אֲנִי עֲלֵיהֶם כְּאִילּוּ דַּרְתִּי בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְכִבְּדוּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And so** too, the equating of one's attitude toward his parents to his attitude toward God is **a logical derivation, as the three of them are partners in his** creation. As **the Sages taught: There are three partners in** the forming of **a person: The Holy One, Blessed be He,** who provides the soul, **and his father and his mother. When a person honors his father and mother, the Holy One, Blessed be He, says: I ascribe** credit **to them as if I dwelt between them and they honor Me** as well.

Why does this equation hold? *She-shelosha shutafin hein ba'adam* — because three partners participate in the creation of a human being: *Hakadosh Baruch Hu ve'aviv ve'imo*, God, father, and mother. And the Gemara adds: *bizman she'adam mechabeid et aviv ve'et imo, amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu, ma'ala ani aleichem ke'ilu darti beineichem vechibduni* — when a person honors his father and mother, God says: I regard it as if I dwelt among them and they honored Me.

## Motivation Debate

### Gratitude Model

The equation of honoring parents with honoring God is weighty, and it invites the question: what is the underlying rationale for this comparison? The intuitive reading is one of gratitude. Parents brought you into the world; they gave you everything. You owe them honor, and recognizing that debt models for you the even greater honor owed to God, who created the entire universe, created your parents, and is the ultimate source of everything you have received. On this reading, the connection moves from the immediate and human — the visceral sense of owing something to those who nurtured you — to the theological: a *kal vachomer* that your gratitude toward parents should extend all the more to God.

### Awe Model

Gerald Blidstein, in his careful study *Honor Thy Father and Mother*, argues for a different reading. He suggests that the comparison is not primarily about the ethics of gratitude — that model being rooted in human experience and then applied upward to the divine. Rather, Blidstein sees the connection as running along a distinctly religious axis: what governs the relationship to parents is *awe*. The same quality of *yirah* — the sense of utter otherness, of one who is at a higher level, of standing before a creator whose act exceeds anything you could fully comprehend — that characterizes a properly religious relationship with God is what should characterize the relationship with parents. It is not about giving back what you owe; it is about the *rimon* of standing before those who brought you into being, which mirrors the awe of standing before the Creator of all being.

On this alternative reading, the motivational structure is reversed. It is not that we learn from the parent-child relationship how to relate to God; rather, the religious sensibility of awe and reverence that we cultivate toward God is the very sensibility that should shape how we stand before our parents. Parents, as creators, partake of something of the divine creative act, and our response to them should draw on the same deep well. As one participant put it: the obligation to parents is not ethically driven out of a sense of debt, but motivated by a deep sense of awe and reverence — the same disposition that enables genuine *davening* and authentic engagement with *Hashem*.

## Practical Principles

### Action Over Feeling

Raising the equation of parents with God immediately prompts a question about cases where the equation feels strained — where parents were absent, neglectful, unkind, or worse. The Torah's response, as we will see worked out in the Gemara, is not to address internal states at all. The *Torah* does not command you how to feel about your parents. It commands how to act. Whether you feel respect or not, you behave in ways that demonstrate honor. Whether you regard your parents as deserving of it or not, you perform the actions that constitute *kavod* and *yirah*. This is a fundamental principle, and it stands in interesting contrast to many other relational obligations in Jewish law: here, the action itself is not sufficient either (as we will see at the end of the *amud*), but the baseline is action, not sentiment.

### Self-Care Priority

What about genuinely abusive parents — those who have caused real psychological or emotional harm? Here a critical principle applies, drawn from the broader category of *mitzvot bein adam l'chaveiro*: *chayecha v'chayei chavercha chayecha kodmin* — your life and your friend's life, your life comes first. The first person you are obligated to is yourself. Where maintaining a relationship with one's parents causes genuine harm to one's psychological well-being, the obligation to self-protection is prior. A person in such a situation is not merely *permitted* to maintain distance; they may be *required* to do so, because *kibbud av va'em* cannot override the primary obligation to protect oneself.

This is not a license for avoidance whenever relationships are difficult. *Kibbud av va'em* does demand real personal and financial extension. But where the threshold of genuine harm is crossed, self-care takes precedence — and that is a halachic principle, not merely a pastoral accommodation.

### No Love Command

One further observation deserves notice. The Torah commands honoring parents. It commands fearing and revering them. It prohibits cursing or striking them. What it conspicuously does not command is *love*. There is no *ve'ahavta* directed toward parents. Whether this reflects a recognition that love cannot be commanded, or that the relational framework here is one of awe and duty rather than affection, is itself a rich question — but the absence is notable and worth sitting with.

## Natural vs. Commanded

### Natural Tendencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם שֶׁבֵּן מְכַבֵּד אֶת אִמּוֹ יוֹתֵר מֵאָבִיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּשַׁדַּלְתּוֹ בִּדְבָרִים, לְפִיכָךְ הִקְדִּים הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כִּיבּוּד אָב לְכִיבּוּד אֵם. וְגָלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם שֶׁהַבֵּן מִתְיָירֵא מֵאָבִיו יוֹתֵר מֵאִמּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּלַמְּדוֹ תּוֹרָה, לְפִיכָךְ הִקְדִּים הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מוֹרָא הָאֵם לְמוֹרָא הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is revealed and known before the One Who spoke and the world came into being that a son honors his mother more than** he honors **his father, because** **she persuades him with** many **statements** of encouragement and does not treat him harshly. **Therefore,** in the mitzva of: "Honor your father and your mother" (Exodus 20:11), **the Holy One, Blessed be He, preceded** the mention of **the honor** due one's **father before** mentioning the **honor** due one's **mother.** The verse emphasizes the duty that does not come naturally. Similarly, **it is revealed and known before the One Who spoke and the world came into being that a son fears his father more than his mother, because** his father **teaches him Torah,** and consequently he is strict with him. **Therefore,** in the verse: "A man shall fear his mother and his father" (Leviticus 19:3), **the Holy One, Blessed be He, preceded** the mention of **fear of the mother before** the mention of **fear of the father.**

*Rebbi* teaches: *galui veyadua lifnei mi she'amar vehayah ha'olam she'ben mechabeid et imo yoseir mei'aviv* — it is revealed and known before the One who created the world that a son shows more honor to his mother than his father. Why? *Mipnei she'meshadelto bidvarim* — because she encourages him, nurtures him, draws him close with words. *Rashi* renders this with a sense of gentle persuasion, even seduction.

The underlying dynamic is clear: the mother is classically the nurturer, the father the authority figure. Toward the nurturer, the natural human response is warmth, care, and the desire to do well by her — that is *kavod*. Toward the authority figure, the natural response is the stepping back, the sense of distance, the slight apprehension — that is *yirah*.

### Torah's Reversal

Precisely because these tendencies are natural, the Torah intervenes to counterbalance them. *Ulefikach hikdim Hakadosh Baruch Hu kibbud av le'kibbud em* — therefore God placed *kavod av*, the honor of the father, before *kavod em* in the verse *kabeid et avicha ve'et imecha*. The command is directing you to extend to your father the honor that comes less naturally. Similarly: *galui veyadua lifnei mi she'amar vehayah ha'olam haben mityarei me'aviv yoter me'imo* — a son more naturally has reverence for his father, in part because *biglal shemelamdo Torah*, the father embodies the authority of Torah instruction. Therefore the verse *ish imo ve'aviv tira'u* places the mother first — to direct you to give your mother her due *yirah*, which comes less naturally.

This is a general principle that the *Sifrei* draws out explicitly: sometimes the command of *kibbud av va'em* requires you to do precisely what does not come naturally. That is, in part, why it requires a command at all. And it may also illuminate why the Torah does not command love — not because love is unimportant, but because in some relationships, love comes naturally and does not need legislation, while the more formal postures of honor and reverence may not.

## Causing Anguish

### God Withdraws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָדָם מְצַעֵר אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: יָפֶה עָשִׂיתִי שֶׁלֹּא דַּרְתִּי בֵּינֵיהֶם, שֶׁאִלְמָלֵי דַּרְתִּי בֵּינֵיהֶם, צִיעֲרוּנִי. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הָעוֹבֵר עֲבֵירָה בַּסֵּתֶר – כְּאִילּוּ דּוֹחֵק רַגְלֵי שְׁכִינָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הַשָּׁמַיִם כִּסְאִי וְהָאָרֶץ הֲדֹם רַגְלָי״.
>
> **Translation:** **A *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman: When a person causes his father and mother suffering, the Holy One, Blessed be He, says: I did well in not dwelling among them, for if I had dwelled among them they would have caused Me suffering** as well, as it were. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Anyone who transgresses in private, it is** considered **as though he is pushing away the feet of the Divine Presence,** i.e., he distances God from him, so to speak. **As it is stated: "So says the Lord: The heaven is My throne and the earth is My footstool"** (Isaiah 66:1). When someone sins in secret, he demonstrates that he thinks God is absent from that place, and it is as though he pushes His feet away from the earth.

The Gemara presents an inversion of the earlier promise. If honoring parents leads God to say *ke'ilu darti beineichem vechibduni* — it is as if I dwelt among them and they honored Me — then causing parents anguish produces the opposite. *Bizman she'adam mita'er et aviv ve'et imo, amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu: yafeh asiti shelo darti beineihem* — when a person causes anguish to his father and mother, God says: it is good that I did not dwell among them. *She'ilmalei darti beineihem, tzi'aruni* — for had I dwelt among them, they would have caused Me anguish. The implication cuts two ways: such behavior amounts to a rejection of the divine presence, and also signals that if this is how they treat their parents — those who stand in relation to God — then they would have treated God no differently.

This also raises a question about the precise contours of *kibbud av va'em*. The Gemara earlier cited the case of *Dama ben Netina* who would not disturb his sleeping father — clearly a form of not causing *tza'ar*. But what of adult children who make life choices their parents disapprove of? Is causing parental unhappiness through one's own legitimate choices a violation of *kibbud av va'em*? There is a meaningful distinction between directly doing something offensive or disrespectful to a parent and simply living one's life in ways the parent does not prefer. The former may implicate *tza'ar*; the latter generally does not.

### Secret Sins

The Gemara digresses — though the connection to divine presence makes it feel organic. *Kol ha'over aveirah beseter, ke'ilu dochek raglei Shechinah* — whoever commits a sin in private is as if he pushes God's feet out of the world. The verse: *hashamayim kis'i ve'ha'aretz hadom raglai* — the heavens are My throne and the earth My footstool. God's feet, as it were, rest on this earth; to sin in secret is to implicitly deny that God's presence extends here. One who sins publicly at least demonstrates, however perversely, that he knows he is being observed but does not care. One who sins in secret suggests that he believes God cannot see — that God has no presence in this private space — and thereby pushes *Shechinah* out of the world.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds: *assur le'adam sheyelech arba amot bekoma zekufa* — a person may not walk four *amot* with a fully upright posture, *melo kol ha'aretz kevodo* — for the entire earth is filled with God's glory. To stride with exaggerated erectness is to assert one's own primacy, to crowd out the awareness of divine presence.

### Head Covering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיְּהַלֵּךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בְּקוֹמָה זְקוּפָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מְלֹא כׇל הָאָרֶץ כְּבוֹדוֹ״. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לָא מְסַגֵּי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בְּגִילּוּי הָרֹאשׁ. אָמַר: שְׁכִינָה לְמַעְלָה מֵרֹאשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the same issue, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** It is **prohibited for a person to walk** even **four cubits with an upright posture,** which is considered an arrogant manner, **as it is stated: "The entire world is full of His glory"** (Isaiah 6:3). One who walks in an arrogant manner indicates a lack of regard for the glory and honor of God that is surrounding him, and thereby chases God from that place, as it were. The Gemara relates: **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, would not walk four cubits with an uncovered head. He said: The Divine Presence is above my head,** and I must act respectfully.

From this emerges the practice of Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *lo masgei arba amot begillui harosh* — he would not walk four *amot* with an uncovered head. *Amar: Shechinah lemalah meroshi* — he said, the *Shechinah* is above my head. This is the textual basis for the *kippah* — not a formal prohibition, but a *minhag* rooted in the continuous awareness of divine presence overhead.

Returning to the broader context: the Gemara has now constructed a world in which the presence of God is mediated, in part, through parents. The *Shechinah* dwells with those who honor their parents; it withdraws when they cause anguish. And the same sensibility that makes a person aware of *Shechinah* above their head — that constant consciousness of being in the presence of something greater — is precisely what *kibbud av va'em* is meant to cultivate toward one's parents.

## Competing Demands

### Father Precedence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאַל בֶּן אַלְמָנָה אַחַת אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אַבָּא אוֹמֵר: ״הַשְׁקֵינִי מַיִם״ וְאִימָּא אוֹמֶרֶת: ״הַשְׁקֵינִי מַיִם״, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶם קוֹדֵם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַנַּח כְּבוֹד אִמְּךָ וַעֲשֵׂה כְּבוֹד אָבִיךָ, שֶׁאַתָּה וְאִמְּךָ חַיָּיבִים בִּכְבוֹד אָבִיךָ. בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר לוֹ כָּךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, נִתְגָּרְשָׁה מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִבֵּין רִיסֵי עֵינֶיךָ נִיכָּר שֶׁבֶּן אַלְמָנָה אַתָּה. הַטֵּל לָהֶן מַיִם בְּסֵפֶל וְקַעְקֵעַ לָהֶן כְּתַרְנְגוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The son of one widow asked Rabbi Eliezer:** If my **father says** to me: **Give me water to drink, and** my **mother** also **says** to me: **Give me water to drink, which of them** should I honor **first?** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: Set** aside **the honor of your mother, and perform the honor of your father, as you and your mother are** both **obligated in the honor of your father. He came before Rabbi Yehoshua and** asked him the same question, and Rabbi Yehoshua **said this** same answer **to him.** The man **said to him: My teacher,** if one's mother **is divorced, what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: From your eyelashes,** which are filled with tears, **it is evident that you are the son of a widow,** and you have no father. Why, then, are you asking this question as though it were relevant for you? Consequently, Rabbi Yehoshua answered him sarcastically: **Pour water for them into a pitcher and squawk at them as** one does to summon **chickens.** In other words, if one's mother is divorced, the same honor is due to both parents, and neither takes precedence.

The Gemara now enters a halachic case study. A certain son of a widow approached Rabbi Eliezer: *aba amar hashkeini mayim, ve-ima omeret hashkeini mayim* — both my father and my mother are asking me to bring them water. *Eizeh mehem kodem* — which takes priority? Rabbi Eliezer answered: *anach kvod imcha va-asei kvod avicha* — set aside your mother's honor for the moment and attend to your father's honor. *She-atah ve-imcha chayavim bi-chvod avicha* — for both you and your mother are obligated in your father's honor.

This ruling reflects a principle that has been developing throughout the Gemara. While sons and daughters are fundamentally equally obligated in *kibbud av va'em*, and while *kavod* and *yirah* are equally owed to both parents, the case of competing simultaneous demands must be resolved when the parents are in a marital relationship. In a patriarchal household, the father is the head; the mother herself owes *kavod* to the father in certain respects. Just as a married daughter's obligations to her parents may yield to her obligations to her husband, so the mother's claim on the child's immediate service may yield to the father's, not because the mother is less important, but because the household hierarchy places the father's demands first when they directly conflict.

### Divorced Mother

The same questioner then approached Rabbi Yehoshua and posed the same scenario. After receiving the same answer, he pressed: *Rebbi, nitgarsha mahu* — what if my mother is divorced? In that case, she is no longer in a position of owing *kavod* to the father; the marital hierarchy that made the father's claim prior no longer operates. What then takes precedence?

### Harsh Response

Rabbi Yehoshua's response is arresting: *mi-bein risei eynecha nikar she-ben almana atah* — from between your eyelashes it is clear that you are the son of a widow. *Rashi* explains that his eyelashes had fallen out from crying — he was an orphan. Rabbi Yehoshua was rebuking him for asking a question framed in personal terms — *aba amar, ima omeret*, my father says, my mother says — when he had no living father. He was asking as if it were a real dilemma when it was not.

Rabbi Yehoshua then told him: *Hattel lahen mayim besefel vekakei lahen ketarnagolim* — pour water in a bowl and cluck at them like chickens, let them come drink from it. The image is, to say the least, jarring. Treating this as a mocking dismissal seems pastorally inexplicable when addressed to an orphaned child. Whatever Rabbi Yehoshua's precise intent, the halachic upshot is clear: where parents are divorced, there is genuinely no principled resolution to competing demands. There is no hierarchy that straightforwardly applies, and the child must exercise judgment — or, in a sense, the *halacha* simply does not give a clear answer.

This absence of resolution is itself significant. The area of *bein adam l'chaveiro* — and *kibbud av va'em* is paradigmatically such an area — does not lend itself to the kind of precise, context-independent ruling that governs *lulav* or *tefillin*. Human relationships are too complex, too contextual, too varied. The Gemara is, in part, teaching that moral judgment must fill the gaps that halachic specificity cannot reach. And this is one reason the Gemara here moves so readily between *aggadah* and *halacha* — because neither alone is sufficient to capture the full demand being made.

## Two Drashot

### Ulla's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ עוּלָּא רַבָּה אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״יוֹדוּךָ ה׳ כׇּל מַלְכֵי אָרֶץ כִּי שָׁמְעוּ אִמְרֵי פִיךָ״ – ״מַאֲמַר פִּיךָ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״אִמְרֵי פִיךָ״ – בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא ״אָנֹכִי״ וְ״לֹא יִהְיֶה לְךָ״, אֲמַרוּ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם: לִכְבוֹד עַצְמוֹ הוּא דּוֹרֵשׁ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר: ״כַּבֵּד אֶת אָבִיךָ וְאֶת אִמֶּךָ״ חָזְרוּ וְהוֹדוּ לְמַאֲמָרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla the Great interpreted** a verse **homiletically at the entrance to the house of the *Nasi*. What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "All the kings of the earth shall give You thanks, O Lord, for they have heard the words of Your mouth"** (Psalms 138:4)? It **is not stated: The word of Your mouth,** in the singular. **Rather,** the verse uses the expression: **"The words of Your mouth,"** in the plural. To what is this phrase referring? **When the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: "I am** the Lord your God" (Exodus 20:2), **and,** in the same verse: **"You shall have no** other gods before Me," **the nations of the world said: He teaches** this **for His own honor,** as both statements entail respect for God. **Once He said: "Honor your father and your mother"** (Exodus 20:11), **they returned and conceded** the truth **of the first statements,** which is why the verse uses the plural expression: "Words of Your mouth," i.e., all the words of God's mouth.

Ulla the Elder delivered a *drasha* at the gates of the house of the *Nasi*. The verse: *yoducha Hashem kol malchei aretz ki sham'u imrei picha* — all the kings of the earth will acknowledge You, God, because they heard the words of Your mouth. The plural *imrei picha*, words of Your mouth, rather than the singular *ma'amar*, is noted. When God declared *anochi* and *lo yihiyeh lecha* — I am the Lord your God and you shall have no other gods — *amru umot ha'olam: lechvod atzmo doresh*, the nations said: He demands this for His own honor; there is no universal moral principle at work, just self-aggrandizement. *Kevan she'amar kabeid et avicha ve'et imecha* — but when God then commanded honoring one's father and mother, *chazru v'hodu lema'amarot harishonot* — they came back and acknowledged the validity of the first commandments. For now they recognized that there is a genuine and universal moral principle underlying the obligation to give honor and recognition to those who bring you into the world and do for you. The divine demand for honor is of a piece with that same principle; it is not self-serving but deeply grounded.

### Rava's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״רֹאשׁ דְּבָרְךָ אֱמֶת״ – ״רֹאשׁ דְּבָרְךָ״ וְלֹא ״סוֹף דְּבָרְךָ״? אֶלָּא מִסּוֹף דְּבָרְךָ נִיכָּר שֶׁרֹאשׁ דְּבָרְךָ אֱמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This can be derived **from here: "The beginning of Your word is truth"** (Psalms 119:160). Is **the beginning of Your word** truth **but not the end of Your word? Rather, from the end of Your word it is apparent** to everyone **that the beginning of Your word is truth.**

Rava offers a parallel *drasha* from a different verse: *rosh devarcha emet* — the first of Your words is true. But is only the first true and not the last? Rather, *misof devarcha nikar she'rosh devarcha emet* — from the end of Your words it becomes clear that the first was true. When the *aseret hadibrot* conclude with honoring one's father and mother, it retroactively validates the opening claim *anochi Hashem* — for the same divine voice that demands honor of God also demands honor of parents, confirming that the framework is coherent and principled throughout.

## Dama ben Netina

### Lost Fortune

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב עוּלָּא: עַד הֵיכָן כִּיבּוּד אָב וָאֵם? אָמַר לָהֶם: צְאוּ וּרְאוּ מָה עָשָׂה נׇכְרִי אֶחָד בְּאַשְׁקְלוֹן, וְדָמָא בֶּן נְתִינָה שְׁמוֹ. פַּעַם אַחַת בִּקְּשׁוּ חֲכָמִים פְּרַקְמַטְיָא בְּשִׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא שָׂכָר, וְהָיָה מַפְתֵּחַ מוּנָּח תַּחַת מְרַאֲשׁוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָבִיו, וְלֹא צִיעֲרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rav Ulla: How far** must one go to fulfill the mitzva of **honoring one's father and mother?** Rav Ulla **said to them: Go and see what one gentile did in Ashkelon, and his name was Dama ben Netina. Once the Sages sought** to purchase **merchandise [*perakmatya*]** from him **for six hundred thousand** gold dinars' **profit, but the key** for the container in which the merchandise was kept **was placed under his father's head,** and he was sleeping at the time. **And** Dama ben Netina would **not disturb** his father by waking him, although he could have made a substantial profit.

In response to the question of how far *kibbud av va'em* extends, Ulla directs: *tze'u ur'u mah asah oved kochavim echad be-Ashkelon, ve-Dama ben Netina shemo* — go see what a certain non-Jew in Ashkelon did. His name was *Dama ben Netina*, and his story is one of the most famous in this entire *sugya*.

The Sages sought merchandise — precious stones for the *ephod* of the *Kohen Gadol* — for which they were prepared to pay six hundred thousand *dinarin* in profit to *Dama*. (Rav Kahana's version raises the figure to eight hundred thousand.) The key to his storeroom lay under the pillow beneath his sleeping father's head. *Ve-lo tzi'aro* — and he would not disturb him. He forfeited a fortune rather than interrupt his father's sleep.

This story made an impression far beyond its immediate lesson about *kibbud av va'em*. There is embedded within it a profound statement about the sanctity of another person's sleep — *gezelat sheina*, the theft of someone's sleep — as something close to inviolable. The Kabbalistic and *mussar* literature picks up on this dimension, and this story is in many ways its source.

The question of where the prohibition falls halachically is also interesting. Is refusing to disturb a sleeping parent a dimension of *kavod* — providing for his needs, including the need for undisturbed rest? Is it an expression of *mora* — reverence, which involves not treating the parent as an object to be instrumentalized for one's own purposes? Both framings are plausible, and the story does not resolve the question, though it makes the ideal vivid and unforgettable.

One might also wonder: what if the father had woken up and said, "You didn't rouse me for *six hundred thousand dinarin*?" The Gemara does not raise this, presumably because *Dama ben Netina* was not Jewish and his father was not obligated in *kibbud av va'em* toward *Dama*. But it is a reasonable question about the limits of deference.

The non-Jewish exemplar is itself significant. The message is at once universal and *kal vachomer*: if a Gentile without the obligation of *kibbud av va'em* as a commanded *mitzvah* can reach such heights, how much more is demanded of one who is obligated? Beyond the *kal vachomer*, there is a recognition that this value — the reverence and care owed to those who brought you into the world — is part of the moral fabric of humanity, accessible and recognizable across religious boundaries. And the Gemara's use of a non-Jew as the model for this Jewish practice carries its own implicit message about the universality of the underlying moral truth.

It is also worth noting what *Dama ben Netina* is providing: the precious stones for the *ephod*, the garment of the *Kohen Gadol*, central to the religious life of all *Klal Yisrael*. The non-Jew who models *kibbud av* is also the one who enables the religious service of the entire Jewish people. The convergence is striking.

### Divine Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשָׁנָה הָאַחֶרֶת נָתַן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שְׂכָרוֹ, שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה לוֹ פָּרָה אֲדֻמָּה בְּעֶדְרוֹ. נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ, אָמַר לָהֶם: יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי בָּכֶם שֶׁאִם אֲנִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִכֶּם כׇּל מָמוֹן שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם אַתֶּם נוֹתְנִים לִי, אֶלָּא אֵין אֲנִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִכֶּם אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן שֶׁהִפְסַדְתִּי בִּשְׁבִיל כְּבוֹד אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The next year the Holy One, Blessed be He, gave** Dama ben Netina **his reward, as a red heifer was born in his herd,** and the Jews needed it. When **the Sages of Israel came to him he said to them: I know,** concerning **you, that if I** were to **ask for all the money in the world you** would **give** it **to me. But I ask only that money that I lost due to** the **honor of Father.**

The story continues: *le-shanah acheret natan Hakadosh Baruch Hu secharo* — the following year, God provided his reward. *Shenoldah lo parah adumah be-edro* — a *parah adumah* was born in his flock. When the Sages of Israel came to him, *Dama* said: I know you would pay me any price in the world for this animal. *Ela* — but I ask only this: *oto mammon she-hifsadeti bishvil kibbud abba* — the same sum I lost in the honor of my father. Nothing more. His reward matched exactly what he had relinquished.

The *parah adumah* is itself charged with symbolism: it is the paradigmatic *chok*, the statute whose rationale is withheld. That the reward for an act of pure moral devotion — forfeiting enormous profit out of reverence for a sleeping father — comes through the most inscrutable of all *mitzvot* is perhaps not accidental.

### Commanded Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: וּמָה מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה – כָּךְ, מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: גָּדוֹל מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה מִמִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥanina says: And if** this is related about **one who is not commanded** by the Torah to honor his father, as Dama was a gentile, **and** nevertheless when **he performs** the mitzva he is given **this** great reward, **all the more so** is one rewarded who is **commanded** to fulfill a mitzva **and performs** it. **As Rabbi Ḥanina says: Greater** is one who **is commanded** to do a mitzva **and performs** it **than one who is not commanded** to do a mitzva **and performs** it.

The Gemara now cites Rabbi Chanina's principle, which contextualizes the entire story: *gadol metzuvah ve'oseh mishe'eino metzuvah ve'oseh* — one who is commanded and does is greater than one who is not commanded and does. This is the framework within which the exemplary behavior of *Dama ben Netina*, a non-Jew, must be understood. As remarkable as his conduct was, those who are commanded in *kibbud av va'em* and fulfill it are at a higher level — not because they are personally superior, but because the commanded act is more deeply desired and demanded by God.

*Rashi* explains that being commanded actually creates greater resistance — the external obligation generates a kind of counter-pressure that makes compliance harder — and therefore greater reward. A more general formulation: if the act is *commanded*, it reflects that God desires it more intensely, and therefore the person who performs it under obligation achieves something higher.

This principle opens onto a broader question that the story of *Dama ben Netina* uniquely illuminates: what happens to the moral vitality of an obligation once it is formalized as a religious command? The non-Jew, unburdened by the command, could reach those heights perhaps precisely because his motivation remained purely moral. For the Jew, the challenge is to maintain that moral force and inner drive even after the act has been overlaid with the structure of religious obligation. The story of *Dama ben Netina* keeps that moral ideal vivid and present, which may be precisely why it is told in this context.

## Rav Yosef Blind

### Initial Hope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאן דַּהֲוָה אָמַר לִי הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: סוֹמֵא פָּטוּר מִן הַמִּצְווֹת – עָבֵידְנָא יוֹמָא טָבָא לְרַבָּנַן, דְּהָא לָא מִיפְּקִידְנָא וְהָא עָבֵידְנָא. הַשְׁתָּא דְּשַׁמְעִיתַהּ לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: גָּדוֹל מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה יוֹתֵר מִמִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה, אַדְּרַבָּה: מַאן דְּאָמַר לִי דְּאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה עָבֵידְנָא יוֹמָא טָבָא לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef,** who was blind, **said: At first I would say:** If **someone would tell me** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: A blind person is exempt from** fulfilling **the mitzvot, I would make a festive day for the rabbis, as I am not commanded and yet I perform** the mitzvot. This means my reward is very great. **Now that I have heard that which Rabbi Ḥanina says: Greater** is one who **is commanded** to do a mitzva **and performs** it **than one who is not commanded** to do a mitzva **and performs** it, **on the contrary:** If **someone would tell me** that the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and a blind person is obligated in mitzvot, **I would make a festive day for the rabbis.**

The Gemara presents a remarkable personal disclosure from Rav Yosef, who was blind. He said: *meireisha hava amina* — at first I used to think — *man de-hava amar li halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda, de-amar suma patur min hamitzvot* — if someone were to tell me that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that a blind person is exempt from *mitzvot*, *avidna yoma tava lerabbanan* — I would make a *Yom Tov* for the Torah scholars. Why? *Dela mipakedna, ve'avidna* — because I am not commanded, and yet I perform the *mitzvot*; I thought that being an unconstrained volunteer would bring me *greater* reward than being an obligated performer.

### Reversal

*Ha de-shames leha de-amar Rabbi Chanina* — but now that I have heard Rabbi Chanina's ruling, that *gadol metzuvah ve'oseh yoter mishe'eino metzuvah ve'oseh*, the one who is commanded is greater — *vehashta adaraba* — it is now the opposite. Now Rav Yosef says: if someone were to tell me that *we do not rule like Rabbi Yehuda* — that I am indeed obligated — that is when I would make the *Yom Tov*, because being obligated means my reward would be greater. Rav Yosef's reversal captures with great poignancy how deeply the principle of *metzuvah ve'oseh* reorders one's understanding of religious merit.

## Mother's Assault

### Public Assault

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: פַּעַם אַחַת הָיָה לָבוּשׁ סִירְקוֹן שֶׁל זָהָב, וְהָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בֵּין גְּדוֹלֵי רוֹמִי. וּבָאתָה אִמּוֹ וּקְרָעַתּוּ מִמֶּנּוּ, וְטָפְחָה לוֹ עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, וְיָרְקָה לוֹ בְּפָנָיו, וְלֹא הִכְלִימָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: Once** Dama ben Netina **was wearing a fine cloak [*sirkon*] of gold, and was sitting among the nobles** of **Rome. And his mother came to him and tore** his garment **from him and smacked him on the head and spat in his face, and** yet **he did not embarrass her.**

The Gemara relates another incident involving *Dama ben Netina*. *Ki asa Rav Dimi amar* — when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael* he reported: once *Dama ben Netina* was wearing a golden embroidered *sirkon* — some kind of fine toga or garment — and was seated among the great men of Rome. *U-va'at immo* — his mother came. *Vekara'aso mimenu* — she tore it off him. *Vetapcha lo al rosho* — she struck him on the head. *Veyarka lo befanav* — and she spat in his face. *Velo hichlima* — and he did not embarrass her.

The contrast with the earlier story is instructive. In the case of the sleeping father, *Dama* demonstrated *kavod* — honor — by not disturbing him, by treating his father's repose as paramount even at enormous personal cost. Here, with his mother, he demonstrates *mora* — reverence and self-restraint — by absorbing a public humiliation without retaliation or shaming her. The Gemara noted earlier that a son more naturally shows *kavod* to his nurturing mother and *mora* to his authoritative father. In both these stories, *Dama ben Netina* performs the *less* natural act: honor toward the father, restraint and reverence toward the mother.

## Attitude Principle

### Feeding Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי אֲבִימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: יֵשׁ מַאֲכִיל לְאָבִיו פַּסְיוֹנֵי וְטוֹרְדוֹ מִן הָעוֹלָם, וְיֵשׁ מַטְחִינוֹ בָּרֵיחַיִם וּמְבִיאוֹ לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Avimi, son of Rabbi Abbahu, taught: There is** a type of son **who feeds his father pheasant [*pasyonei*] and** yet this behavior **causes him to be removed from the World,** i.e., the World-to-Come; **and there is** one **who makes him grind with a millstone,** which is difficult work, **and this action brings him to the life of the World-to-Come.**

The *amud* concludes with a teaching that synthesizes everything the Gemara has been building toward. *Tani Avi berei de-Rav Avo: yesh ma'achil le'aviv pasyoni vetordo min ha'olam* — there is one who feeds his father fattened pigeons — a genuine delicacy — and through doing so drives him from the world, from any share in the *olam ha-ba*. *Veyesh mat'chino bereichayim umevi'o lechayei ha'olam haba* — and there is one who causes his father to grind at a millstone and through this brings him to the life of the *olam haba*.

The *Yerushalmi* supplies the context. In the first case: a son feeds his father beautiful dishes day after day, and one day the father asks where such fine food comes from. The son replies: "What do you care, old man? Just chew and eat." In the second: the father is called to perform some compulsory service for the king — dangerous, degrading — and the son says: "You stay here and work the millstone; I'll go do the service for the king," thereby protecting his father at personal risk and cost.

### Respect Demonstration

The principle is clear and far-reaching. We have discussed, and will discuss further, the specific actions that constitute *kavod* and *yirah* — providing food and drink, not occupying a parent's seat, not contradicting them publicly, accompanying and serving them. But the Gemara here insists that performing those very actions is not sufficient to fulfill *kibbud av va'em*. It depends entirely on how they are done — what they communicate, what attitude they express.

You can provide the finest meals with complete contempt and disdain. That is not *kibbud av va'em*; it may in fact be a desecration of it. You can perform none of the prescribed acts and yet do the single greatest *kiyum* of *kibbud av va'em* through an act of self-sacrifice that places your parent's welfare above your own.

This is where *kibbud av va'em* diverges fundamentally from most areas of *halacha*. When we put on *tefillin*, the attitude with which we do so does not affect whether we have fulfilled the obligation. The precise physical act, performed correctly, is *yotzei*. Here, the same physical act can be fulfilled or violated depending entirely on what it expresses. *Kavod* is not a checklist; it is a posture, a communication, a demonstration. And that is why the constant movement in this *sugya* between *halacha* and *aggadah*, between the specific rulings and the moral stories, is not incidental. The aggadic dimension — the stories of *Dama ben Netina*, the Gemara's images of God withdrawing when parents are caused anguish, the equation of parents with God — is not ornamental. It is the very substance of what *kibbud av va'em* requires.
