# Bava Batra 128 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Six Rulings Structure

### Intro: Amoraim

The daf begins seven lines down on 128a with the words *shalach lei Rabbi Abba le-Rabbi Yosef bar Chama* — Rabbi Abba sent the following to Rabbi Yosef bar Chama. Rabbi Abba and Rabbi Yosef bar Chama are third-generation *amoraim*. Rabbi Abba was originally from Bavel but made *aliyah* to Eretz Yisrael, and from there he transmitted these rulings back to his colleague in Bavel.

### Overview

The next full daf consists of a series of six rulings sent by Rabbi Abba to Rabbi Yosef bar Chama. Most of them concern either *edut* — testimony — or the laws of inheriting property. At the end of the sequence, the *gemara* will rule on all six cases.

## Govin min Avadim

### Creditor vs. Slaves

The first ruling: *halacha, govin min ha-avadim* — the *halacha* is that one may collect from slaves — and Rav Nachman says *ein govin*, one may not collect. The *gemara* itself provides little context for these rulings, so the Rashbam is indispensable here. The Rashbam explains the case as follows: *baal chov she-meit, gova me-avdei yetomim* — a creditor whose debtor died may collect his debt from the slaves that the deceased bequeathed to his children. Rabbi Abba rules that the *baal chov* may indeed force the heirs to repay him from the inherited slaves.

### Rashbam & Yochanan

The Rashbam clarifies that Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and holds *ein govin* — one cannot be repaid from slaves. That concludes the first ruling.

## Edut: Generations

### Shelishi & Sheni

The second ruling concerns the laws of testimony and the degree of familial closeness that disqualifies a witness. Rabbi Abba rules: *shelishi be-sheni kasher* — a third-generation relative may testify in a case involving a second-generation relative on the other side. To illustrate: Reuven and Shimon are brothers. Reuven has a son, and that son has a son — Reuven's grandson. Shimon also has a son. The ruling is that Reuven's grandson, who stands in the *shelishi* (third-generation) position relative to the original fraternal connection, may testify in a case involving Shimon's son. In English terms, they would be described as first cousins once removed. This topic will be treated in greater depth in Masechet Sanhedrin.

### Rava's Expansion

Rava goes further and says *af be-rishon* — the *shelishi* (Reuven's grandson) may testify even against Shimon himself, who stands in the *rishon* position. The *shelishi* can testify against the *rishon*.

### Rav Ashi Rejected

Mar bar Rav Ashi goes further still, ruling *achsher be-abba de-abba* — that it is even permissible for a grandson to testify against his grandfather, or vice versa. The *gemara* immediately rules: *ve-leit hilcheta ke-Mar bar Rav Ashi* — the *halacha* does not follow Mar bar Rav Ashi.

### Rashbam vs. Rosh

The Rashbam reads the disqualification broadly: *de-vnei vanim u-vnei vnei vanim ad elef dorot lo ya'idu la-avoteihem* — no matter how many generations of direct descent one traces, those descendants can never testify one against another in the direct line. On this reading, no grandson of Reuven can ever testify against Reuven himself, regardless of how many generations separate them.

The Rosh, however, disagrees and holds that the rule is *davka shelishi* — it applies specifically to the third generation. A great-grandson — who stands in the *revi'i* position — can, according to the Rosh, testify against his great-grandfather, because once the relationship is *revi'i*, the testimony is *kasher*. The Rosh cites the Rif as holding the same view. This debate concerns the direct line of descent — whether a direct descendant may ever testify against a direct antecedent, the Rashbam holding never, and the Rosh holding that from the fourth generation onward it is permitted. Again, these questions will receive fuller treatment in Sanhedrin.

## Blind Witness Testimony

### Blind Witness Disqualified

The third ruling, found some thirteen lines into the *gemara*: *haya yodea lo be-edut karka ad shelo nistama ve-nistama pasul* — if a person knew the dimensions of someone's field before he went blind and subsequently went blind, he is *pasul le-edut*, disqualified from giving testimony.

### Shmuel Permits

Shmuel disagrees and holds *kasher efshar de-michvein metzarnaha* — he is in fact *kasher* to testify, because even though he is now blind, he can describe the boundaries of the field from memory, indicating, for instance, that it extends from a certain tree to a well-known rock. He need not physically point to or walk the boundaries. However, Shmuel adds *aval glima lo* — if the testimony concerns a cloak rather than a field, a person who was sighted when he observed the garment but has since gone blind cannot testify about it. The Rashbam explains that cloaks are too similar to one another to be adequately identified through description alone.

### Rav Sheshet: Garment/Gold

Rav Sheshet extends the permissive position further: *afilu glima efshar de-michvein midat orko u-midat rochbo* — even a cloak can be testified about by a formerly sighted, now blind witness, because he can describe its length and its width. However, Rav Sheshet agrees that a *niska* — which the Rashbam explains is a *chatichat kesef o zahav*, a piece of silver or gold — cannot be testified about by such a witness.

### Rav Papa: Weight

Rav Papa goes one step further still: *afilu niska efshar de-michvein midat mishkalotav* — even a piece of gold or silver can be testified about, because perhaps before he went blind the witness knew its precise weight, and that identification by weight suffices.

### Braita Refutation

At this point the *gemara* raises a *meitivei* — a challenge from a *braita* — that proceeds to reject all of these positions. The *braita* begins with two illustrative cases. First: a person knew testimony regarding someone before that person became his son-in-law, and then the person became his son-in-law — *pikeach ve-nitcharesh*, he had full hearing and speech but then became a deaf-mute — *pikeach ve-nistama*, he could see and then went blind — *shafui ve-nishtateh*, he was of sound mind and then lost his mind — in all these cases: *pasul*. If he was *kasher le-edut* when he first learned what he would be testifying about, but he is *pasul le-edut* at the time of giving testimony — whether due to a disqualifying relationship created through marriage or due to a condition such as blindness, deafness, or insanity — he is *pasul* and may not testify.

The *braita* then teaches the reverse: if he knew the testimony while *kosher*, subsequently became his son-in-law but then his daughter died, or he became a deaf-mute and then recovered, or went blind and then regained his sight, or lost his mind and then recovered — *kasher*. He is *kasher le-edut*.

The *braita* then formulates the general principle: *zeh haklal — kol she-techilato o sofo be-paslut pasul; techilato ve-sofo be-kashrut kasher*. This is the rule: if he was *pasul le-edut* either when he first acquired the knowledge or when he is called to give testimony, he is *pasul*. But if he was *kasher* at both the beginning and the end, whatever happened in between is irrelevant, and he is *kasher*.

The *gemara* concludes at the beginning of 128b: *tiyuvta de-chulhu tiyuvta* — this is indeed a refutation of all the preceding opinions. And it is a strong refutation. One is left to ask: how did all these *amoraim* — Shmuel, Rav Sheshet, Rav Papa — not know this *braita*? No satisfying answer has been found. Notably, the only one whose ruling is vindicated is Rabbi Abba himself, who ruled that if the witness knew the testimony before going blind and has since gone blind, he is *pasul* — which is precisely what the *braita* teaches. All the others who permitted testimony based on the blind witness's ability to indicate various *simanim* — identifying features — are refuted.

## Child Inheritor Claim

### Abaye's Interpretation

The fourth ruling, at the top of 128b: *ha-omer al tinok bein ha-banim ne'eman* — one who makes a declaration about a child among his children is believed; and Rabbi Yochanan says *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed. The ruling as stated is cryptic: what exactly did this person say about the child? The *gemara* asks *mai ka'amar* — what is he saying?

Abaye explains: *ha-omer al tinok bein ha-banim yeresh kol nechasai ne'eman ke-Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka ve-Rabbi Yochanan amar eino ne'eman ke-rabbanan*. The case is that a father singles out one child from among his children and declares that this child should inherit all of his property. According to Abaye, Rabbi Abba rules that this declaration is *ne'eman* — it is upheld — following the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, while Rabbi Yochanan rules *eino ne'eman*, following the position of the *rabbanan*.

### Yochanan b. Broka

This connects the ruling to a *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka and the *rabbanan* that appears in the *mishna* on 130a. That *mishna* states: *Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka omer: im amar al mi she-raui le-yorsho devarav kayamim; ve-al mi she-ein raui lo le-yorsho ein devarav kayamim* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka holds that if a father designated as his heir someone who would in any case stand to inherit him, his words take effect; but if he designated someone who would not otherwise be his heir, his words do not take effect.

The underlying *machloket* is whether a person can contradict — *soter* — the words of the Torah in how he distributes his estate. To illustrate: Reuven has three sons. According to Torah law, the eldest receives *pi shnayim* and the others divide the remainder equally. If Reuven says he wants his second son to inherit everything, the *rabbanan* say he cannot do this — he cannot be *soter divrei Torah*, he cannot set aside the Torah's inheritance laws. But Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka holds that if the person he is designating is someone who would in any case be in line to inherit him, the declaration is valid.

In the case of the *tinok*: according to Abaye's reading, the father has multiple children and points to one of them, declaring that this child should inherit everything. Rabbi Abba, following Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, says the declaration stands. Rabbi Yochanan, following the *rabbanan*, says it does not. The question of what makes this particular case difficult — given that all the children are already in line to inherit him — is addressed by the dynamic of singling out one child to receive everything to the exclusion of the others, which constitutes overriding the Torah's distribution scheme.

It is worth noting that a practical workaround exists: a father can give property as a gift during his lifetime, rather than structuring it as an inheritance. In that case he is not being *soter divrei Torah* at all, since the law of *matanah* — gift — during one's lifetime is not subject to the Torah's inheritance rules. That, however, is a distinct mechanism that sidesteps rather than resolves the underlying *machloket*.

### Rava Objects

Rava objects to Abaye's reading: *hai ne'eman ve-eino ne'eman* — if the ruling is about what Abaye claims, namely whether the designated child actually inherits, then the language used by Rabbi Abba is wrong. Rabbi Abba said *ne'eman* and *eino ne'eman* — believed and not believed. But if the question is whether the father's designation overrides the Torah's inheritance scheme, one would not use the language of belief; one would say *yeirash* or *lo yeirash* — he inherits or he does not inherit. The word *ne'eman* — believed — does not fit that case. Rava's objection is thus a linguistic one: the formulation of the ruling points to a different underlying case.

### Rava's Fix: Bechor

Rava therefore reinterprets the ruling: *ha-omer al tinok bein ha-banim bechori hu ne'eman ke-Rabbi Yehuda ve-Rabbi Yochanan amar eino ne'eman ke-rabbanan*. The case is not about designating a child to receive everything, but about a father who points to one child among his children and says: this child is my eldest son — *bechori hu*. Rabbi Abba rules that the father is believed, in accordance with the position of Rabbi Yehuda in the *machloket* already seen on the previous daf. Rabbi Yochanan rules *eino ne'eman*, in accordance with the *rabbanan* in that same *machloket* with Rabbi Yehuda. This reading makes the language of *ne'eman* and *eino ne'eman* entirely appropriate — the question is precisely whether the father's word is believed. With Rava's reinterpretation, the ruling coheres.

## Wife as Child

### Wife Inherits Equally

The fifth ruling: *ha-omer titol ishti ke-echad min ha-banim notelet ke-echad min ha-banim* — one who says "my wife shall receive as one of my children" — she receives as one of the children. If a man has five children and declares that his wife should inherit alongside them, his estate is divided not into six portions but into seven — the additional portion reflecting the *pi shnayim* that the *bechor* receives — with his wife counting as one additional heir.

### Rava's Limits

Rava comes to clarify the ruling on two points: *u-ve-nechasim shel achshav u-ve-vanim ha-ba'im le-achar mi-kan*. First, the declaration covers only the property he currently holds. Whatever additional property he acquires from this point until his death does not fall within the scope of the declaration and is distributed according to the standard rules. As the Rashbam explains, when he says *titol ishti ke-echad min ha-banim*, he is speaking about what he presently owns — he has no way of knowing what he will acquire in the future. Even according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that *adam makneh davar she-lo ba le-olam* — a person can transfer ownership of something he has not yet acquired — that applies only when he explicitly states that the gift is to include future acquisitions, which this father has not done.

Second, the number of children among whom the estate is divided is determined not by how many children he had when he made the declaration, but by how many children he has at the time of death. If he had five children when he made his statement but seven by the time he died, the estate is divided among seven plus the wife — eight shares — not among five plus the wife. The reason is that his statement was *titol ke-echad min ha-banim* — she should receive as one of whatever children I have — and the natural reading of *ha-banim* is the children who exist at the time of the division, not those alive at the time of the statement. It is thus notable that the two limits cut in opposite directions: the *property* is fixed at the time of the declaration, while the *number of heirs* is determined at death.

## Debt Admission

### Setup

The sixth and final ruling: a creditor produces a *shtar chov* — a loan document — against a debtor. The creditor says: I have not been repaid anything. The debtor says: I have paid back half. And witnesses then testify that he paid back the entire loan. The ruling is: *harei zeh nishba ve-goveh mechetza mi-nechasim benei chorin aval mi-meshubadin lo* — the debtor takes an oath and the creditor collects half from the debtor's unencumbered property, but not from encumbered or alienated property. The rationale for excluding encumbered property is that the owners of that property or those holding liens on it will say: *anan a-eidim samchinan* — we relied on the witnesses' testimony that the entire debt had been repaid, and we therefore purchased or accepted lien on this property under the assumption that the debtor had no outstanding obligations. They cannot therefore be made to bear the loss.

### Rashbam Explains

The Rashbam explains why the debtor must take an oath. The witnesses testified that the entire loan was repaid *after* the debtor had already admitted to paying back only half. The Rashbam writes: *ha-loveh she-para, nishba, ve-yipater mi-lishalem mechetza* — the borrower takes an oath that he paid back half and is thereby exempt from paying the portion he swore about. *U-mechetza yishalem lefi she-kevar hodah, de-hodat ba'al din shaveh yoteir me-ha-edutam* — he pays back the other half because his own prior admission weighs more heavily than the testimony of the witnesses. The Torah teaches that when a debtor is *modeh* — admits — that he still owes part of a debt, he must take an oath regarding the portion he claims to have paid and must pay back the rest. This applies when he has admitted partial liability; if he denied the entire debt, no oath would be required, and similarly if he admitted the full debt.

### BM Context

The ruling then introduces a further dimension: *va-afilu le-Rabbi Akiva de-amar meshiv aveidah havei* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds in a certain case that the debtor is like one who is *meshiv aveidah*, returning lost property, and therefore exempt from an oath, Rabbi Abba rules that in our case even Rabbi Akiva would agree that an oath is required.

This is a reference to the *machloket* between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar at the beginning of the second chapter of Bava Metzia, on approximately daf 3. The Rashbam summarizes the case there: *plugta de-Rabbi Akiva ve-Rabbi Shimon bar Elazar bi-techillat Shnayim Ochazin* — the dispute at the beginning of the chapter of *Shnayim Ochazin*. The case involves a *shtar* that records only that a loan of multiple coins was made — *sela'im o dinarim* — without specifying the amount. The creditor claims five; the debtor claims three. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that since the debtor has acknowledged part of the claim — *modeh miktzat ha-ta'anah* — he is like a *modeh miktzat* and must take an oath. Rabbi Akiva says he is *ke-meshiv aveidah* — like someone returning lost property — and does not have to take an oath. The reasoning: the word *sela'im* implies at minimum two coins. Since the debtor is claiming three — more than the minimum he could have claimed — he is in a sense volunteering an admission beyond what was strictly required. He is therefore believed for three without an oath, just as one who returns a lost object is not put under suspicion.

Rabbi Abba's ruling, however, is that even Rabbi Akiva would agree that in our case an oath is required: *hani milei heicha de-leika eidim* — Rabbi Akiva's principle applies only where there are no witnesses. It is the absence of witnesses that makes the debtor's voluntary admission look like *meshiv aveidah*. But: *aval heicha de-ika eidim, irtutei irtat* — when there are witnesses, the debtor is in a state of fear about what they will say, and his admission is therefore not a free and voluntary one. The Rashbam elaborates: the debtor knows witnesses exist and knows they intend to testify in a way that will confirm one side or the other, but he does not know which way they will go. Fearing they might confirm the creditor's full claim, he hedges by admitting to half — because if he claims only half, he knows he will at most owe half, rather than risk the witnesses confirming the full amount. Since his admission is driven by strategic calculation rather than honest disclosure, it does not carry the character of *meshiv aveidah*, and an oath is required.

### Rav Ashi Objects

Rav Ashi challenges this analysis from the opposite direction. Even Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — who in the Bava Metzia case said the debtor is *modeh miktzat* and therefore must take an oath — was speaking only *heicha de-leika sahadei de-kamsaya lei*, where there are no witnesses to corroborate what he says. But *aval heicha de-ika sahadei de-kamsaya lei, vadai meshiv aveidah havei* — when there are witnesses who confirm his account, then certainly he is like *meshiv aveidah* and should not have to take an oath.

### Mar bar Rav Ashi

Mar bar Rav Ashi's position, then, is that once witnesses come forward and say the debtor paid back the entire loan, the debtor is in the position of *meshiv aveidah* — he has, as it were, returned the lost property of the creditor's claim — and should therefore not be required to swear. He would still pay back whatever remains according to his own admission, but the oath would be dispensed with.

### Debate: Fear

This reasoning raises a genuine difficulty. The debtor's admission was made before the witnesses testified. At the time he spoke, he did not know which way the witnesses would go. According to the analysis developed earlier, his admission of paying back half was itself a product of fear — a strategic hedge in uncertain circumstances. Now the witnesses have come and testified in his favor. Does that retroactively change the nature of his earlier admission?

The deeper question is about the *assumption* we make regarding the debtor's state of mind. Was he hedging on the assumption that the witnesses would testify against him — in which case claiming half would limit his exposure — or was he hedging the other way, hoping the witnesses would confirm a full repayment while he admitted only to half as a kind of insurance? One might object that if he truly expected the witnesses to testify in his favor, he would simply have claimed to have paid everything — since the witnesses would then have confirmed him completely and he would owe nothing. The fact that he claimed only half suggests he was not confident of favorable testimony. This is why the fear of the witnesses is said to have been upon him.

Mar bar Rav Ashi's counter is not that the fear was absent, but that once the witnesses do testify in his favor, we should not compound the situation by requiring an oath that the witnesses' own testimony renders potentially false. Since we take *shevuot* with the utmost seriousness, forcing a debtor to swear that he paid only half — when witnesses have said he paid everything — risks imposing a false oath. We therefore exempt him from the *shevuah* and treat him as *meshiv aveidah* in that respect, while still holding him to his admission regarding the amount. It is a partial reliance on the witnesses: they are believed enough to spare him the oath, but not to override his own prior admission about the amount owed. This kind of bifurcated reliance on testimony does occur elsewhere in *halacha*, though it always carries a degree of tension.

## Final Ruling

### Zutra: Follow Abba

Mar Zutra expounded in the name of Rav Shimi bar Ashi: *hilcheta be-chol hani shema'ateta ki-de-shalach lei Rabbi Abba le-Rav Yosef bar Chama* — the *halacha* in all of these cases follows the rulings that Rabbi Abba sent to Rav Yosef bar Chama.

### Nachman Dispute

Ravina immediately raises a question to Rav Ashi: what about Rav Nachman? In the first ruling, Rav Nachman disagreed with Rabbi Abba — Rabbi Abba ruled *govin min ha-avadim* and Rav Nachman said *ein govin*. Ordinarily in such disputes the *halacha* follows Rav Nachman. What is the *halacha* there?

### Ashi: Clarify

Rav Ashi responds: *ein govin matninan lah ve-chein amar Rav Nachman* — the way the ruling was actually taught was *ein govin*, one may not collect from slaves. What Rabbi Abba actually sent was that the *halacha* is *ein govin*, and Rav Nachman agrees. The version that had *govin* was a misreading of the original ruling. Once corrected, there is in fact no dispute between Rabbi Abba and Rav Nachman in that case.

### Rava Clarifies

The *gemara* then asks: *ve-ella hilcheta le-apukei mai?* — if so, what is Mar Zutra's ruling actually coming to exclude? Since in most cases there was either no dissenting opinion or the *gemara* had already ruled, what is the point of declaring that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Abba?

One answer: *ee le-apukei mi-de-Rava mosifu* — it comes to clarify that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Abba and also Rava's extension in the case of testimony across generations. Rava had said *af be-rishon*, that the *shelishi* can testify even against the *rishon*. The *gemara* explains that Rava was not disagreeing with Rabbi Abba but rather clarifying his position: Rabbi Abba ruled *shelishi be-sheni kasher* in the context of countering someone who had explicitly said the *shelishi* may not testify against the *sheni*. Rava's addition — that the *shelishi* may testify even against the *rishon* — is thus a further clarification of Rabbi Abba's view and not a departure from it. Therefore the *halacha* follows both Rabbi Abba and Rava's expansion.

### Reject Ash: BnB

As for Mar bar Rav Ashi, who had ruled *achsher be-abba de-abba* — that a grandson can testify against his grandfather — the *gemara* notes that this position was already explicitly rejected earlier: *ha kevar afsik de-leit hilcheta ke-vatei*. The *gemara* already ruled that the *halacha* is not like Mar bar Rav Ashi. Mar Zutra's declaration need not address this point; it was settled.

### Blind: Resolved

Similarly, regarding the entire discussion about what a blind witness can and cannot testify about — Shmuel's permission for land, Rav Sheshet's extension to garments, Rav Papa's extension to pieces of gold — all of those opinions were already refuted by the *braita* in a *tiyuvta de-chulhu tiyuvta*. There is no need to issue an additional ruling on those cases; the *braita* resolved them.

### Not Like Yochanan

What the declaration *does* come to rule on is: *ela pukei mi-de-Rabbi Yochanan* — it comes to establish that the *halacha* is not like Rabbi Yochanan, who said in the fourth case that a father is *eino ne'eman* to designate his eldest son against the established *chazakah*. The *halacha* is in fact that the father is believed — *pasken* like Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Abba against Rabbi Yochanan and the *rabbanan* in the case of a father claiming which of his children is the *bechor*.

### Against Ashi: Oath

Finally, the declaration rules against Mar bar Rav Ashi's position in the sixth case — the question of whether the debtor must take a *shevuah* when he admits to paying half and witnesses say he paid everything. Mar bar Rav Ashi held that in such a case, because the witnesses confirm his partial payment at minimum, he is like *meshiv aveidah* and exempt from the oath. The *halacha* rejects this. When a debtor admits to paying half and witnesses testify that he paid the whole, he must nonetheless take a *shevuah* and pay back the remaining half from his unencumbered property. The *halacha* is like Rabbi Abba — the debtor is *modeh miktzat* and must swear — and not like Mar bar Rav Ashi who would exempt him from the oath.
