# Menachot 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ein Me'akvin Overview

### Theme & Context

The overarching theme of this section of Masechet Menachot is *ein me'akvin* — the principle that the absence of one element does not necessarily prevent or invalidate another. Having worked through the intricate relationship between the *kivsei atzeret* and the *shtei halechem*, the Mishnah now turns to a broader set of *korbanot* and examines the interrelationships among them.

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא אַ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָעֹלָה״ רִיבָּה סְמִיךְ לֵיהּ. מַתְנִי׳ הַתְּמִידִין אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַמּוּסָפִין, וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַתְּמִידִין, וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר – יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Failure to sacrifice **the daily offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of **the additional offerings, and** likewise, failure to sacrifice **the additional offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of **the daily offerings. And** failure to sacrifice some of **the additional offerings** on a day when more than one is sacrificed, e.g., if it was both Shabbat and the New Moon, **does not prevent** sacrifice of the **other** additional offerings. If the priests **did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, nevertheless, **they should sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering, as failure to sacrifice one daily offering does not prevent sacrifice of the other.

The Mishnah opens with two symmetrical rulings: *hatmidim ein me'akvin et hamusafim* — the *tamidim* do not hold back the *musafim* — and *velo hamusafim me'akvin et hatamid* — the *musafim* do not hold back the *tamid*. Concretely: if one has lambs sufficient only for the *tamidim* but not for the *musaf* of Shabbos, one may still go ahead and bring the *tamid*. Conversely, if one has *korbanot* for the *musaf* but lacks what is needed for the *tamid*, one may still bring the *musaf* even knowing the *tamid* cannot be brought.

### Musafim Interrelation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** And **the additional offerings** on a day when more than one is sacrificed **do not prevent** sacrifice of the **other** additional offerings.

The Mishnah continues: *velo hamusafim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the *musafim* do not hold each other back. The *musafim* are typically a large group: two cows, a ram, seven sheep, and so on. If one cannot bring the entire complement, one may still bring whatever portion is available. Moreover, on a day such as Shabbos Rosh Chodesh, when two separate *musaf* obligations apply, one does not render the other indispensable — each stands independently.

### Two Tamidim

The Mishnah adds: *lo hikrivu keves baboker yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* — if the morning lamb was not brought, the afternoon lamb may still be brought. The two *tamidim* are thus not *me'akev* one another. This is somewhat striking — one might have expected them, as a matched pair, to operate on an all-or-nothing basis. But the Mishnah treats them similarly to the *musafim*: bring what you can.

### R. Shimon: Tamidim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ אֲנוּסִין אוֹ שׁוֹגְגִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ מְזִידִין וְלֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: When** does this *halakha* apply? It applies **at a time when** the failure to sacrifice the daily morning offering was because **they were** prevented from sacrificing it due to **circumstances beyond their control or** they failed to sacrifice it **unwittingly. But if** the priests acted **intentionally and did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, **they should not sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering.

Rabbi Shimon qualifies this ruling sharply: *eimatai? bizman shehayu anusim o shogegin* — when is this true? Only when the failure to bring the morning *tamid* was beyond one's control, whether due to compulsion (*ones*) or inadvertence (*shogeg*). But *im hayu mezidin* — if it was a willful choice — *lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim*: they may not bring it in the afternoon.

This position of Rabbi Shimon connects to a stance he takes elsewhere in Masechet Menachot: if one must choose between bringing some of the *musafim* with their *nesachim* or bringing all of them without, Rabbi Shimon holds it is better to do fewer correctly than to do all of them improperly. A consistent thread runs through both rulings: do not make a deliberate choice to perform something in a half-measure. If the choice is willful, either do it properly — both *tamidim* — or do not do it at all. The relationship between the two *tamidim* is, in Rabbi Shimon's view, no less binding than the relationship between each animal and its *minchat nesachim*. If the failure was not a willful choice, *ones* or *shogeg*, the matter stands differently.

### R. Shimon: Musafim

Rabbi Shimon does not, however, apply this same restriction to the *musafim* generally. Nor does he quarrel with the Mishnah's ruling about the *musafim* holding each other back.

### Incense: Morning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטוֹרֶת בַּבּוֹקֶר – יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Incense was burned twice a day, half a measure in the morning and half a measure in the afternoon. If **they did not burn** the half-measure of **incense in the morning, they should burn** the half-measure **in the afternoon.**

The Mishnah continues with a parallel ruling regarding the *ketoret*: *lo hiktiru ketoret baboker yiktiru bein ha'arbayim* — if the morning *ketoret* was not burned, it may be burned in the afternoon. The *ketoret* was divided into two equal portions, half offered in the morning and half in the afternoon.

### R. Shimon: Incense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, שֶׁאֵין מְחַנְּכִים אֶת מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב אֶלָּא בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים, וְלֹא מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה אֶלָּא בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, וְלֹא אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָן אֶלָּא בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בְּשַׁבָּת, וְלֹא אֶת הַמְּנוֹרָה אֶלָּא בְּשִׁבְעָה נֵרוֹתֶיהָ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.** The reason for the difference between the daily offerings and the incense is **that** the daily service on a new **golden altar is initiated only with** the burning of **the incense of the spices** in the afternoon, at which time they would burn a full measure. **And the** daily service on a new **altar of the burnt offering,** on which the daily offerings were sacrificed, is initiated **only with the daily morning offering. And** use of a new **Table** was initiated **only with** the arrangement of **the shewbread on Shabbat, and** use of a new **Candelabrum** was initiated **only with** the kindling **of its seven lamps in the afternoon.**

Here Rabbi Shimon appears to accept the principle that the afternoon *ketoret* may stand alone. But he adds a significant qualification: *vekulaso hayata kreva bein ha'arbayim* — when bringing only the afternoon *ketoret*, one brings the full measure, not merely the half-portion that would ordinarily be offered in the afternoon. The reasoning will be developed in the Gemara. The difference between the *ketoret* and the *tamidim* in Rabbi Shimon's system — why he accepts the afternoon *ketoret* alone but not the afternoon *tamid* alone — will require analysis. Notably, whereas by the two *tamidim* the Mishnah emphasizes that they form a pair, by the *ketoret* Rabbi Shimon concedes that the afternoon can stand independently, even while insisting on the full measure.

### Altar Inauguration

The Mishnah then shifts to a related set of rulings about *chinuch* — the inauguration of the Sanctuary's vessels and altars. The golden altar (*mizbeach hazahav*) is inaugurated only through the burning of the *ketoret hasamim*. The outer altar (*mizbeach ha'olah*) is inaugurated only through the *tamid shel shachar*. The *shulchan* is inaugurated only through the *lechem hapanim* on Shabbos. The *menorah* is inaugurated only through the lighting of all seven lamps in the afternoon. Each vessel is inaugurated through the performance of its characteristic *avoda*.

The ruling connecting the outer altar specifically to the *tamid shel shachar* is not incidental to the earlier discussion. It provides implicit support for Rabbi Shimon's position: since the morning *tamid* is the *avoda* that gives the outer altar its foundational identity — it is through the morning *tamid* that the altar is inaugurated and that is why the altar is always called *mizbeach ha'olah* — one should not make a willful choice to skip it and rely on the afternoon alone. The Gemara will elaborate on all of this.

## Priority Question

### R. Chiya's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: צִיבּוּר שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן תְּמִידִין וּמוּסָפִין, אֵי זֶה מֵהֶן קוֹדֵם?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the daily offerings and the additional offerings each do not prevent fulfillment of the mitzva with the other. Concerning this, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** In the case of **a community that did not have** the resources to sacrifice both **the daily offerings and the additional offerings, which of them takes precedence** over the other?

Rav Chiya bar Avin posed a question to Rav Chisda: *tzibur she-ein lahem temidim u-musafim eizeh me-hem kodem?* — if a community lacks sufficient animals for both the *tamidim* and the *musafim*, which takes precedence?

### Same Day Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא תְּמִידִין דְּיוֹמֵיהּ וּמוּסָפִין דְּיוֹמֵיהּ – פְּשִׁיטָא תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ תָּדִיר וּמְקוּדָּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** to which Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin is referring? **If we say** that he is referring to a case where the choice is between **the daily offerings of that day and the additional offerings of that** same **day,** it is **obvious** that **the daily offerings are given preference, as** the sacrifice of the daily offerings **is** more **frequent** than the sacrifice of the additional offerings, which are sacrificed only on special occasions, **and** the daily offerings are also **sanctified.**

The Gemara immediately asks: what exactly is the case? If we are discussing a choice between the *tamidim* and the *musafim* of the same day — say, Shabbos, where one has only two lambs and must choose between using them for the two *tamidim* or for the *musaf* of Shabbos — the answer is straightforward.

### Tadir Principle

*Peshita temidim adifei* — obviously the *tamidim* take precedence. The principle at work is *tadir u-mekudash*. The *tamid* is more *tadir* — more frequent, brought every day — than the *musaf*, which is only brought on Shabbos. Furthermore, the *tamid* of Shabbos is no less *kadosh* than the *musaf* of Shabbos, so there is no counterweight on the side of sanctity. There is in fact an entire hierarchy in a chapter of Zevachim establishing that *kol hatadir me-chavero kodem et chavero* — whatever is more frequent takes precedence — and similarly *kol hamekudash me-chavero kodem et chavero* — whatever is of greater sanctity takes precedence. For the same-day case, the *tamid* wins on both counts.

### Tamidim Tomorrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, תְּמִידִין דְּלִמְחַר וּמוּסָפִין דְּהָאִידָּנָא – תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי, שֶׁכֵּן תָּדִיר, אוֹ דִלְמָא מוּסָפִין עֲדִיפִי, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ מְקוּדָּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin must be referring to a case where they have enough animals for the daily offerings of today and also for either **the daily offerings for tomorrow or the additional offerings for today.** In such a case, are **the daily offerings given preference since** their sacrifice is more **frequent? Or perhaps the additional offerings are given preference because they are** considered more **sanctified** because they are sacrificed on a holy day.

The genuinely difficult case is: *temidim de-machar u-musafim de-ha-idna* — tomorrow's *tamidim* versus today's *musafim*. Suppose the *Beis Hamikdash* has four lambs and knows with certainty that no additional animals can be obtained before tomorrow, given the requirement to check animals four days in advance. Even if someone arrived at the door with lambs tonight, they could not be used tomorrow. The question is: do you use two lambs for today's *musaf* and forgo tomorrow's *tamid*, or do you set aside two for tomorrow and forgo today's *musaf*?

### Planning Ahead

The Gemara frames two possible answers. *Temidim adifei she-ken tadir* — perhaps tomorrow's *tamidim* take priority because the *tamid* is the *tadir* par excellence; nothing better exemplifies frequency of obligation than the *tamid*. Or: *musafim adifei d'hava lei mekudash* — perhaps today's *musafim* take priority because they carry the quality of *mekudash*. Rashi offers two readings of *mekudash* here. The first is that a *korban* of a sacred day — Shabbos — carries implicitly greater *kedusha* than a *korban* of an ordinary weekday, so today's Shabbos *musaf* is of a higher order than tomorrow's weekday *tamid*. The second reading is more intriguing: *mekudash* here does not mean inherently holier, but rather that the *kedusha* is presenting itself now, today, and one should address what is before oneself rather than plan for tomorrow. In this reading, the question is not about the comparative sanctity of the two offerings but about a philosophy of obligation: does one prioritize the present moment or plan ahead?

This case is in certain respects unique, because the Gemara stipulates that tomorrow is genuinely foreclosed — one cannot rely on providence to resolve things. If there were any possibility of obtaining more animals in the interim, one might simply focus on today's obligations and see what the morning brings.

It is also worth noting that the principle of *kodem* — priority — as it appears in the chapter in Zevachim typically governs sequencing when all the offerings are present, not necessarily the choice to bring one at the expense of another. The Gemara here is indeed using *kodem* in the stronger sense: which offering has enough priority that one actively forfeits the other on its behalf.

### Mishnah Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: הַתְּמִידִין אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַמּוּסָפִין, וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַתְּמִידִין, וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin: The resolution to this dilemma can be derived from that which **you learned** in the mishna: Failure to sacrifice **the daily offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of **the additional offerings, and** likewise, failure to sacrifice **the additional offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of **the daily offerings. And** failure to sacrifice some of **the additional offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of the **other** additional offerings.

Rav Chisda responds: *tanituha* — we have already taught the answer in our Mishnah. The Mishnah states that the *tamidim* do not hold back the *musafim* and the *musafim* do not hold back the *tamidim*. What can this prove?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ, וּלְקַדֵּם, וְהָתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר קוֹדֵם לְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָרַךְ עָלֶיהָ הָעֹלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** to which the mishna is referring? **If we say** that it is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **has** all of the animals necessary for both offerings, **and** the only question is **in** terms of sacrificing one **before** the other, **isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that no** sacrifice **should precede** the sacrifice of the **daily offering of morning?** The *baraita* continues: It is derived from that which **the verse states:** "And the fire upon the altar shall be kept burning thereby, it shall not go out; and the priest shall kindle wood on it every morning; **and he shall lay the burnt offering in order on it,** and shall cause the fats of the peace offering to go up in smoke upon it" (Leviticus 6:5).

The Gemara asks: what is the case that the Mishnah is addressing? If it means that you have all the animals and the ruling concerns order — that if one performed them in reverse order it remains valid — that cannot be right, because a *baraita* teaches: *menayin shelo yehei davar kodem letamid shel shachar* — from where do we know that nothing may precede the morning *tamid*? The verse states *v'arach aleha ha'ola* — "arrange upon it the burnt offering" — and Rava derives from this that the *ola* goes first, meaning the *tamid*, and nothing precedes it. So if the Mishnah were speaking about order, it would be saying something already known to be impermissible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: ״הָעֹלָה״ – עוֹלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** in explanation of this derivation in the *baraita* that the term **"the burnt offering,"** with the definite article, is referring to **the first burnt offering** of the day, which is the daily offering. Consequently, one may not sacrifice the additional offerings before the daily offering.

Therefore, the Gemara concludes, the Mishnah must be speaking about a case where one does not have all the animals and must choose which to bring. And if it is the same-day choice, why does the Mishnah treat the *musafim* as equivalent — saying that the *tamidim* do not hold back the *musafim*? On the same day, it would obviously be wrong to choose the *musaf* over the *tamid*. The fact that the Mishnah treats them symmetrically — neither holds back the other — implies that the Mishnah is speaking about a choice between today's *musafim* and tomorrow's *tamidim*. And since the Mishnah says neither holds back the other, both choices are equally valid, implying that there is no mandatory precedence of tomorrow's *tamidim* over today's *musafim* or vice versa.

### Abaye's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ! וְאִי דְּיוֹמֵיהּ, אַמַּאי? תָּדִיר וּמְקוּדָּשׁ – תָּדִיר עָדִיף! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלִמְחַר, וְקָתָנֵי אֵין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, אַלְמָא כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית לְהוּ, וּלְקַדֵּם. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר קוֹדֵם – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the mishna is referring to a case **where he does not have** a sufficient number of animals for both offerings. **And if** it is referring to the offerings **of that day, why** does the mishna say that failure to sacrifice one does not prevent sacrifice of the other? Since the daily offering is more **frequent** and it is sanctified, **and** the additional offerings are **sanctified** but are less frequent than the daily offering, the **frequent** offering **is given preference.** Consequently, failure to sacrifice the daily offering should prevent the sacrifice of the additional offerings. **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where the Temple treasury does not have enough animals for both the additional offerings of today and the daily offering **of tomorrow, and** yet the mishna **teaches** that failure to sacrifice one does **not prevent** one from sacrificing **the other? Evidently, they are equal** and he may sacrifice whichever offering he chooses. This would resolve Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin's dilemma. **Abaye said to** Rav Ḥisda: That is not a valid resolution of the dilemma, as one can claim that **actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **where they have** enough animals for all the offerings, **and** the mishna is referring to the issue of sacrificing one **before** the other. **And** with regard to **that which** poses **a difficulty for you** based upon the *baraita* that states that **no** sacrifice **should precede** the sacrifice of the daily offering of the morning, that *baraita* **is merely** stating **the mitzva** *ab initio*.

Abaye rejects this reading. *Le'olam de-it lehu* — in truth, the Mishnah is speaking about a case where one has all the animals, and the ruling concerns what happens if they were brought out of order. The fact that *lechatchila* one must not bring anything before the *tamid* is, as Abaye explains, merely a *mitzva* — an ideal — and the Mishnah is ruling that *bedi'avad*, after the fact, it is valid even if the order was reversed. Therefore, Abaye concludes, the Mishnah says nothing at all about the *lechatchila* choice between today's *musafim* and tomorrow's *tamidim*. The Mishnah is a *bedi'avad* ruling, not a *lechatchila* directive, and cannot serve as proof for the priority question.

## Six Sheep Reserve

### Baraita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשִּׁשָּׁה טְלָאִים הַמְבוּקָּרִין בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים, כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וְלִשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin's dilemma based upon a mishna (*Arakhin* 13a): One maintains **no fewer than six lambs that** have been **inspected** and found to be unblemished **in the Chamber of the Lambs, sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana** that in some years occur adjacent to it.

The Gemara now introduces a new potential source: *ein pochtin me-shisha tela'im ha-mevukarim be-lishkat ha-tela'im* — there were never fewer than six inspected sheep in the chamber of lambs. The *Beis Hamikdash* maintained a reserve of sheep in a dedicated room, the *lishkat ha-tela'im*, that had been checked (*mevukarim*) four days in advance.

### Shabbos/RH Case

The Baraita specifies: *kedei le-Shabbos u-le-shnei yamim tovim shel Rosh Hashanah* — enough for Shabbos and the two days of Rosh Hashanah, the only two-day *Yom Tov* observed in Eretz Yisrael. The reasoning seems intuitive: when a run of days prevents one from going out to obtain animals on short notice, one must draw on reserves. Two lambs are needed for the *tamidim* of Shabbos and another two for each day of Rosh Hashanah — hence six. The striking implication is that the reserve is stocked for the *tamidim* alone, not for the *musafim*, which on Rosh Hashanah alone require seven lambs, two cows, and a ram. Why is the reserve focused exclusively on the *tamidim*?

### Challenge to Six

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ תְּמִידִין וּמוּסָפִין – טוּבָא הֲווֹ! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that** it is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **has** enough animals for all of the offerings that should be brought, six lambs are not enough, as **there are many** lambs sacrificed for **daily offerings and additional offerings** on those three days. In total, twenty-two lambs are required: Two each day for the daily offerings, two for the additional offering of Shabbat, seven for the additional offering of Rosh HaShana, and seven for the additional offering of the New Moon. **Rather, is it not that** the mishna is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **does not have** enough animals for all of the offerings, and it is teaching that the animals that he does have are utilized for the daily offerings of all three days rather than for the additional offering of Shabbat on the first of the three days? Consequently, one may **conclude from it** that the **daily offerings** of tomorrow **are given preference** over the additional offerings of today.

The Gemara, however, challenges whether six is even sufficient for the described scenario. *Heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the reserve is meant to supply both *tamidim* and *musafim* for Shabbos and Rosh Hashanah, *tuva havu* — that would require far more than six animals. The *musafim* of Rosh Hashanah alone run to many animals. So the reserve must be only for the *tamidim*, not for the full complement. *Ela lav de-leis lei* — it must be that one does not have enough animals for everything, and the reserve is designed so that when forced to choose, one ensures the *tamidim* are covered. This appears to prove that *tamidim adifei* — the *tamidim* take precedence.

Rashi sharpens the implication further. Suppose Shabbos falls immediately before the two days of Rosh Hashanah. On Shabbos itself one has six animals in reserve. If the intent were to cover both Shabbos and Rosh Hashanah fully, one would bring the *tamid* and *musaf* of Shabbos — using four animals — and have only two left for both days of Rosh Hashanah, which is insufficient. But if one deliberately brings only the two *tamidim* on Shabbos, forgoing the *musaf*, one preserves four for Rosh Hashanah's *tamidim*. So one is explicitly choosing tomorrow's (and the day after's) *tamidim* over today's *musaf*.

However, the Gemara pulls back: *lo le'olam de-islei* — in truth, one does have enough animals for everything. The reserve is not about choosing *tamidim* over *musafim* when resources run short.

### Ben Bag Bag

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית לֵיהּ, וְהָכִי קָא אָמַר: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשִּׁשָּׁה טְלָאִים הַמְבוּקָּרִין בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, וּמַנִּי? בֶּן בַּג בַּג הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, בֶּן בַּג בַּג אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְתָמִיד שֶׁטָּעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה לָכֶם לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת עַד אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם״. מָה לְהַלָּן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, אַף כָּאן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **No,** this is not a valid proof, as one can claim that **actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the Temple treasury **has** enough animals for all of the offerings of the three days, **and this is what** the mishna **is saying** when it mentions six lambs: One maintains for use as daily offerings **no fewer than six lambs that** have been **inspected** and found to be unblemished **in the Chamber of the Lambs four days prior to** their **slaughter.** Lambs sacrificed as other offerings do not have to be inspected four days prior to being slaughtered. **And** in accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **ben Bag Bag. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **ben Bag Bag says: From where** is it derived that **the daily offering requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter? The verse states** with regard to the daily offering: **"You shall observe [*tishmeru*] to sacrifice to Me in its due season"** (Numbers 28:2), **and elsewhere,** with regard to the Paschal offering, the verse **states:** "On the tenth day of this month they shall take for them every man a lamb…**and you shall keep it [*mishmeret*] until the fourteenth day** of this month" (Exodus 12:3–6). Consequently, it is derived by means of a verbal analogy that **just as** in the verse **there,** the Paschal offering **requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter,** from the tenth of the month to the fourteenth, **so too here,** the daily offering **requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter.**

The Gemara asks: whose view underlies this Baraita? The answer is Ben Bag Bag. *Ben Bag Bag omer: minayin letamid she-ta'un bikur arba'ah yamim kodem shechitah* — from where do we derive that the *tamid* requires inspection four days before slaughter?

Ben Bag Bag derives it through a *gezerah shavah* from the *Korban Pesach*. The Torah states regarding the *tamid*: *tishmru lehakriv li bemo'ado* — "you shall guard to bring it in its appointed time." Elsewhere, regarding the Pesach, it says: *vehayah lachem lemishmeres ad arba'ah asar yom* — "it shall be kept by you until the fourteenth day." The Pesach was set aside on the tenth, giving four days of inspection before slaughter on the fourteenth. Just as the Pesach requires four days of prior inspection, so too the *tamid* requires four days of prior inspection. Crucially, Ben Bag Bag limits this requirement to the *tamid* specifically — not to every *korban*. The *musafim* require no such advance checking.

### Why Check Tamidim

There is something genuinely striking about this pairing: the two offerings singled out for the rigorous requirement of four-day advance inspection are, at opposite extremes, the *Korban Pesach* — the singular event of the year, brought once — and the *tamid* — brought every single day. Nothing stands between these two poles. The common thread seems to be a message about degrees of care: the once-a-year event demands intensive preparation as a matter of course, but so does the daily offering, precisely because its very regularity invites negligence. The opening of Parashat Tzav and Rashi's comment there — *ziruz miyad uledorot*, that special urgency is required precisely for the daily tasks that Kohanim might take for granted — resonates with this theme.

### Mnemonic Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָנֵי שִׁשָּׁה? שִׁבְעָה הָווּ, דְּהָא אִיכָּא דְּצַפְרָא דִּתְלָתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא! וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תְּמָנְיָא הָווּ, דְּהָאִיכָּא דְּפַנְיָא דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דִּלְבָתַר דְּאַקְרֵיב קָאָמַר. מִכׇּל מָקוֹם שִׁבְעָה הָווּ! אֶלָּא, תַּנָּא בְּעָלְמָא קָאֵי, וּמַאי כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וּשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה? סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a discussion pertaining to the mishna cited earlier. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** With regard to **these six** inspected lambs that must always be kept in the Chamber of Lambs, there ought to **be seven, as there is** a need for another lamb for the daily offering **of the morning of the third** day **of the week,** since it cannot be inspected on Shabbat or the subsequent festival days of Rosh HaShana, and therefore should be inspected and kept beforehand. Rav Ashi responded to Ravina: **According to your reasoning** the correct number is not seven, but rather **it is eight; as there is** also the daily offering **of the afternoon of Shabbat eve** that should also be counted. Ravina replied: **That** is **not difficult,** because the mishna **is saying that after he sacrificed** the daily offering of the afternoon there must be six inspected lambs. The Gemara asks: **In any case,** the question stands: Why does the mishna say six lambs when, in fact the number required **is seven?** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the *tanna* is** speaking **generally,** referring to how many inspected lambs are required throughout the year. **And what** did he mean by his statement: **Sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana?** It is **merely** intended as **a mnemonic,** to help one remember that there must be enough lambs for the daily offering of three days.

The Gemara now resolves the earlier challenge. The reference to Shabbos and Rosh Hashanah as the case that motivates the reserve of six is not a literal specification of the scenario but a *simana be'alma* — a mnemonic device. The number six corresponds to the *tamidim* for those three days, and that serves as a convenient way to remember it, but the actual operative rule is simply that the *Beis Hamikdash* always maintained a standing reserve of six inspected lambs in the *lishkat ha-tela'im*, ready to be drawn upon at any time. The reserve was not specifically calibrated for a Shabbos-Rosh Hashanah emergency. The evidence for this reading is linguistic: the Baraita says *kedei le-Shabbos* — "the equivalent of Shabbos" — not *le-Shabbos*, "for Shabbos." The phrasing suggests a numerical mnemonic rather than a literal purpose.

This resolution also addresses a structural problem: if the reserve were literally calibrated for Shabbos followed by two days of Rosh Hashanah, one would need not six but seven lambs, because the morning *tamid* of the day following Rosh Hashanah would also need to be available, and by then no opportunity to replenish and inspect new animals would yet have arisen. The Gemara acknowledges this — *mikkol makom shiva havu* — and it is this unresolvable arithmetic that ultimately confirms that the Baraita was never literally speaking about that scenario in the first place.

### Rotation System

What, then, is the logic of the reserve? Rashi and the *Shittah Mekubetzet* explain that the six sheep represent a rotating queue. Every day, two new sheep are introduced into the *lishka* and two are drawn out for use. Since each sheep must be inspected four days before it can be used, the reserve maintains enough inspected animals to bridge the four-day gap between intake and availability. The system is continuous: animals enter at one end of the queue, are inspected over the course of four days, and are drawn from the other end when ready.

The *Shittah Mekubetzet* works through the logic in detail. If one begins bringing *korbanot* on a Wednesday, one starts inspecting animals the preceding Shabbos, giving Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday — four days. Two animals begin inspection on Shabbos (for Wednesday), two more on Sunday (for Thursday), and two more on Monday (for Friday), yielding eight animals in the *lishka* before the first day of *hakravah* begins. As the process continues, one draws two from the bottom of the queue each morning and adds two to the top each afternoon, maintaining the cycle.

The question of checking or procuring animals on Shabbos itself is worth noting. The *Tosafot* suggest that the *Beis Hamikdash* need not have performed the checking itself; suppliers were in the business of preparing and inspecting lambs for sale to the *Beis Hamikdash*, and the *Beis Hamikdash* could purchase from such suppliers who certified that the animals had been checked for four days. The limitation was not commercial activity per se but the practical impossibility of reaching suppliers in the middle of the night when the morning *tamid* had to be offered at first light.

### Six Explained

Why, given all this, does the number six appear — rather than eight, which would seem to be the logically required minimum? The *Shittah Mekubetzet* offers an explanation: at one particular point in the rotation, after two animals have been drawn out for the day's *tamid* but before the two replacement animals have been introduced that same day, the *lishka* holds exactly six. The ruling that there be "no fewer than six" refers to this interim count — six after the day's draw and before replenishment. Strictly speaking, eight would be needed to maintain a fully continuous supply, but the rule captures the count at the momentary low point in the cycle. It is a somewhat compressed formulation, though the *Shittah Mekubetzet* demonstrates that the logic holds.

The ultimate conclusion of all this is that the Baraita about the six sheep cannot answer the original question of whether *tamidim* take precedence over *musafim*. The reserve exists because the *tamid* requires an advance preparation that the *musafim* do not — not because the *tamid* is prioritized when resources are scarce. The two concerns are distinct, and the question posed by Rav Chiya bar Avin remains unresolved.

## Tamidim Debate

### Afternoon Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבֹּקֶר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the mishna **is incomplete, and this is** what **it is teaching:** If **they did not sacrifice a lamb** for the daily offering **in the morning, they should not sacrifice** a lamb in **the afternoon. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the service of a new **altar had not** yet **been initiated,** since it must first be initiated with the daily offering of the morning. **But** if the service of **the altar had** already **been initiated,** then **they should sacrifice** the lamb of the daily offering in **the afternoon** even though they did not sacrifice the lamb of the morning offering.

The Gemara now turns to a closer analysis of the Mishnah's ruling that if the morning lamb was not brought, it may be brought in the afternoon. Taken at face value, this is what the Mishnah permits. But the Gemara, working through the positions of the *chachamim* and Rabbi Shimon, introduces important qualifications that were not visible in the Mishnah alone.

### Chachamim: Inauguration

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between the Mishnah's permissive ruling and the end of the Mishnah's statement about inauguration through a reading it introduces as *chisorei michsra* — the Mishnah is missing contextual material. Even the *chachamim*, who generally permit the afternoon *tamid* when the morning was not brought, agree that this applies only *aval nitchanech hamizbe'ach* — once the altar has already been inaugurated. Before inauguration, one may not start with the afternoon lamb; the inauguration of the outer altar requires specifically the *tamid shel shachar*. The end of the Mishnah — *ain mechanchen et hamizbe'ach hachitzon ela be-tamid shel shachar* — reflects this shared premise. The *chachamim* concede that the morning *tamid* carries foundational weight. Their disagreement with Rabbi Shimon is only about how far that weight extends.

### R. Shimon Restricts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ אֲנוּסִין אוֹ שׁוֹגְגִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ מְזִידִין – לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבֹּקֶר, לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: When** does this *halakha* apply? It applies **at a time when** the failure to sacrifice the daily morning offering was because **they were** prevented from sacrificing it due to **circumstances beyond their control or** they failed to sacrifice it **unwittingly. But if** the priests acted **intentionally and did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, **they should not sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering.

Rabbi Shimon, as noted in the Mishnah, goes further. Even after inauguration, if the failure to bring the morning *tamid* was willful, the afternoon *tamid* may not be brought — *lo hikrivu keves baboker, lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim*. Only in cases of *ones* or *shogeg* does he permit the afternoon alone.

### Second Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאֵת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַשֵּׁנִי תַּעֲשֶׂה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – שֵׁנִי בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, וְלֹא רִאשׁוֹן בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, that if the lamb of the daily offering of the morning was not sacrificed and the service of a new altar had not yet been initiated, then the lamb of the afternoon is also not sacrificed? The Gemara explains that it is derived **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: "The one lamb you shall offer in the morning; **and the second lamb you shall offer in the afternoon"** (Exodus 29:39). This verse teaches that **the second** lamb of the daily offering is sacrificed **in the afternoon, but** if it is **the first** one to be sacrificed, it may **not** be sacrificed **in the afternoon.** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the service of a new **altar had not** yet **been initiated,** since it must first be initiated with the offering of the morning. When the verse refers to the first or second offering, it means the first or second offering ever sacrificed on the altar. **But** if the service of **the altar had** already **been initiated,** then **even** if it is **the first** to be sacrificed that day, it should be sacrificed **in the afternoon.**

The Gemara now brings a *baraita* that grounds this entire discussion in the scriptural text. The verse states: *et hakeves ha'echad ta'aseh baboker ve'et hakeves hasheni ta'aseh bein ha'arbayim* — "the first lamb you shall do in the morning and the second lamb you shall do in the afternoon." The *baraita* derives: *sheni bein ha'arbayim velo rishon bein ha'arbayim* — the second is in the afternoon, not the first. The afternoon lamb must be the *second*, meaning it must follow a morning lamb that was already brought. If the afternoon lamb would be the first offering brought, it is inappropriate.

This, however, applies with the qualification already noted: *bameh devarim amurim shelo nitchanech hamizbe'ach* — when is this restriction operative? When the altar has not yet been inaugurated. There the verse's pairing is mandatory. But *aval nitchanech hamizbe'ach, afilu rishon bein ha'arbayim* — once inaugurated, even if the afternoon lamb is the first and only offering of the day, it is acceptable.

### Post-Inauguration

This ruling has roots in the way the *Torah* introduces the *tamid* in Parashat Tetzaveh (Shemos 29:38–46), which reads: *vezeh asher ta'aseh al hamizbe'ach, kevasim bnei shanah shnayim layom tamid... et hakeves ha'echad ta'aseh baboker ve'et hakeves hasheni ta'aseh bein ha'arbayim*. The passage is both an instruction for regular daily practice and an account of how the altar first begins to function. The *chachamim* read the pairing requirement as pertaining to the inauguration context, not to every subsequent day. The identification of the altar as *mizbeach ha'olah* — the altar of the burnt offering — and the description of the *tamid* as *ha'asuya behar sinai lere'ach nicho'ach* (as found in the *tamid* passage in Pinchas 28:6) reinforces the sense that the morning *tamid* is the foundational act through which the altar achieves its identity. The *chachamim* locate that requirement in the act of inauguration; Rabbi Shimon holds it extends to every willful choice thereafter.

Once the altar has been inaugurated, the *chachamim* hold that the afternoon *tamid* may be brought even without the morning one. The morning *tamid* retains its primacy as the anchor of the altar's identity, but that primacy expresses itself through the inauguration, not through a perpetual requirement of pairing on every subsequent day.

### Kohanim Negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי כֹהֲנִים חָטְאוּ, מִזְבֵּחַ בָּטֵל?! אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ הֵן, אֲבָל אֲחֵרִים יַקְרִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does it make sense that **because the priests sinned** by intentionally failing to sacrifice the morning daily offering, **the altar** should be entirely **idle? Rava said** that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: They,** the priests who deliberately failed to sacrifice the morning daily offering, **should not sacrifice** the afternoon daily offering; **but other** priests **should sacrifice** it.

One might ask: if Rabbi Shimon's restriction on willful omission of the morning *tamid* is meant to protect the integrity of the service, does it not punish the *mizbe'ach* for the negligence of the *kohanim*? If the *kohanim* were derelict in the morning, why should the *mizbe'ach* go without a *tamid* altogether for that day?

Rabbah resolves this: *lo hikrivu heim aval acherim yakrivu* — the ruling does not mean that no *tamid* is brought at all. It means that *those* negligent *kohanim* are not entitled to bring it in the afternoon. Other *kohanim* may step in and bring it. The system of the *tamid* continues; what is forfeited is the right of the derelict *kohanim* to perform the afternoon service. The *mizbe'ach* does not suffer; the negligent parties do.

## Incense Debate

### Afternoon Incense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטֹרֶת בַּבֹּקֶר – יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** If **they did not burn** the half-measure of **incense in the morning, they should burn** the half-measure **in the afternoon.**

The Mishnah rules that if the morning *ketoret* was not burned, it may be burned in the afternoon. This is a permissive ruling without restriction — at least from the *chachamim's* perspective, and apparently from Rabbi Shimon's as well, with the caveat about the full measure. The question is: why is the *ketoret* treated more leniently than the *tamid*? Rabbi Shimon disallows the afternoon *tamid* in a case of *mezid* but seems to permit the afternoon *ketoret*. What distinguishes them?

### Rarity Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטֹרֶת בַּבֹּקֶר, יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא, וּמְעַתְּרָא, חֲבִיבָא לְהוּ וְלָא פָּשְׁעִי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if the priests acted intentionally and **did not burn** the **incense in the morning,** even those same priests **may burn** it in the **afternoon.** The reason for this is **that since** burning the incense **is uncommon and causes** those who do so to become **wealthy,** it is **dear to the** priests, **and they will not be negligent** in the performance of this rite.

The Gemara explains: *decheivan delo shechichi u-me'atra* — since the *ketoret* is not a common opportunity and it makes one wealthy. The *ketoret* was brought by a single *kohen*, chosen by lot, and the opportunity was offered only once in a lifetime — a *kohen* who had already performed the *ketoret* was excluded from future lots. This rarity made the service extraordinarily precious to those who served. Thirteen *kohanim* might be involved in bringing the *tamid* up to the *mizbe'ach*; the *ketoret* belonged to one.

### Incense Reward

*U-me'atra* — and it made one rich. There was a tradition that burning the *ketoret* brought material prosperity. This confluence of rarity and reward meant that *kohanim* who were privileged to perform it took extraordinary care. The motivation to be present and diligent was essentially self-reinforcing.

### Rabbinic Concern

The Gemara's treatment of this distinction also subtly reframes Rabbi Shimon's position. Throughout the Mishnah, Rabbi Shimon speaks in terms of *shogeg* versus *mezid*. But once the Gemara understands his restriction as rabbinic in character — a *gezeirah* meant to guard against carelessness — rather than a principled ruling that the *tamid* intrinsically cannot be valid without the morning component, the analysis shifts. The Gemara now speaks not of *mezid* but of *poshe'a* — negligent. The restriction is a rabbinic protection against *peshi'ah*, dereliction. Where *peshi'ah* is structurally implausible — as with the *ketoret*, where the rarity and reward make negligence virtually inconceivable — the protection is simply unnecessary and therefore not imposed.

*Chaviva lehu velo pash'i* — it is beloved to them and they will not be negligent. This is the Gemara's explanation for why no parallel *gezeirah* was enacted for the *ketoret*: the motivation structure itself serves as the protection.

### R. Shimon: Full

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.**

Rabbi Shimon rules that when the afternoon *ketoret* is brought alone, the full measure is brought — not merely the half-portion that would ordinarily constitute the afternoon offering. Why this should be so is taken up in the following discussion.

## Incense Debate Continued

### Full Amount Why

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, שֶׁאֵין מְחַנְּכִים אֶת מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב אֶלָּא בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.** The reason for the difference between the daily offerings and the incense is **that** the daily service on a new **golden altar is initiated only with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the afternoon,** at which time they would burn a full measure.

Rabbi Shimon's ruling that the full measure of *ketoret* is brought in the afternoon connects, as the Gemara will now show, to his view about the *chinuch* of the *mizbeach hazahav*.

---

## Golden Altar

### Afternoon Inauguration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The service of a new golden altar is initiated **with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning?** The Gemara answers: The question of whether the incense of the morning or the afternoon initiates the service of a new golden altar **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.**

Rabbi Shimon adds: *she'ein mechanchen et mizbeach hazahav ela biketoret samim shel bein ha'arbayim* — the golden altar is inaugurated only through the *ketoret* of the afternoon, not the morning. This phrase, *shel bein ha'arbayim*, does not appear in the Mishnah as we have it; the Gemara reads it as part of Rabbi Shimon's position. If the afternoon *ketoret* is the more fundamental act for the golden altar — the one through which the altar achieves its inaugural status — then it makes sense both that the afternoon alone is acceptable to Rabbi Shimon and that the full measure should be brought when only the afternoon offering can be made.

The Gemara immediately notes a *baraita* that contradicts Rabbi Shimon: *vehatanya biketoret samim shel shachar* — the *chinuch* of the golden altar is through the morning *ketoret*, not the afternoon. So there is a *tanaic* dispute about which *ketoret* — morning or afternoon — is the more foundational, and correspondingly which one effects the *chinuch* of the golden altar.

### Abaye's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּבֹּקֶר בַּבֹּקֶר בְּהֵיטִיבוֹ אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת יַקְטִירֶנָּה״. אִי לָאו דַּעֲבַד הַדְלָקָה מֵאוּרְתָּא, הֲטָבָה בְּצַפְרָא מֵהֵיכָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It **stands to reason** that the *halakha* should be **in accordance with the one who says** that it was initiated **with the incense of the spices of the afternoon, as it is written** with regard to the golden altar: "And Aaron shall burn thereon incense of sweet spices; **every morning, when he dresses the lamps, he shall burn it.** And when Aaron lights the lamps at dusk, he shall burn it, a perpetual incense before the Lord throughout your generations" (Exodus 30:7–8). The fact that ashes are removed from the lamps of the Candelabrum in the morning indicates that the lamps had been lit previously, since **if** the priest had **not performed the lighting** of the lamps the previous **evening, from where** would the ashes be **removed in the morning?** This proves that the Candelabrum must have been lit for the first time in the evening. Since the verse states: "When Aaron lights the lamps at dusk, he shall burn it, a perpetual incense before the Lord," it must be that the incense was burned for the first time in the evening.

Abaye rules in accordance with the view that the afternoon *ketoret* is the more primary: *mistabra keman de'amar biketoret samim shebein ha'arbayim* — it is more reasonable to say that the *chinuch* is through the afternoon *ketoret*.

Abaye's reasoning is as follows. The verse states: *baboker baboker beheitivo et haneirot yaktirenna* — every morning, when one tends the lamps of the *menorah*, one burns the *ketoret*. The *ketoret* and the *menorah* are thus paired: the morning *ketoret* accompanies the morning cleaning of the *menorah*, and the afternoon *ketoret* accompanies the afternoon lighting of the *menorah*. Now, the morning cleaning of the *menorah* — *heitiv* — can only take place because the *menorah* was lit the previous evening. The lighting of the *menorah* is the starting point of the *menorah*'s cycle; the cleaning is the secondary act. If the *ketoret* is linked to the *menorah*, then the starting point of the *ketoret*'s cycle is the afternoon/evening, when the *menorah* is first lit. The morning *ketoret*, by this logic, is the secondary act. Therefore, the afternoon *ketoret* — paired with the initial lighting of the *menorah* — is the more primary, and it is through this more primary act that the *chinuch* of the golden altar is effected.

The argument, as presented, is suggestive rather than fully compelling, and the Gemara does not press it further. But the underlying intuition is clear: the *ketoret* derives its primary orientation from the *menorah*, and the *menorah*'s primary moment is the evening lighting, making the afternoon/evening *ketoret* the anchor of the cycle.

### Parallel View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר, גָּמַר מִמִּזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה: מָה לְהַלָּן בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר – אַף כָּאן בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that the service of a new golden altar is initiated **with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning,** from where is this *halakha* derived? The Gemara answers: He **derives** it **from** the initiation of **the altar of the burnt offering. Just as there,** the service of a new altar of the burnt offering is initiated **by** means of the **daily offering of the morning** rather than the afternoon, **so too here,** the service of a new golden altar is initiated **by** means of the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning.**

The opposing view — that the morning *ketoret* effects the *chinuch* — draws by analogy from the outer altar. Just as the outer altar is inaugurated through the morning *tamid*, so the golden altar is inaugurated through the morning *ketoret*. Both are altars; both operate on a morning-afternoon cycle; the morning is the starting point for the outer altar and should be the starting point for the golden altar as well. *Mah lahalan betamid shel shachar af kan biketoret samim shel shachar*.

### Altar Placement

The deeper question underlying this dispute concerns how the golden altar is to be categorized. Is it essentially a parallel to the outer altar — both are altars, both operate on a morning-afternoon cycle, and the morning is therefore primary for both? Or does the golden altar belong to a different conceptual category, aligned with the other vessels of the *heichal* — the *menorah* and the *shulchan* — whose primary cycle runs from evening to morning? If the golden altar is fundamentally aligned with the *menorah*, then its primary moment is the afternoon and evening; if it is fundamentally aligned with the outer altar, its primary moment is the morning.

Rabbi Shimon holds that the afternoon is primary for the golden altar, and from this flows his ruling that the afternoon *ketoret* alone can stand independently, that the full measure is brought when only the afternoon is offered, and that the *chinuch* of the golden altar is effected through the afternoon *ketoret* specifically. The *chachamim* who hold the morning *ketoret* effects the *chinuch* implicitly align the golden altar with the outer altar, where the morning is the foundational moment.
