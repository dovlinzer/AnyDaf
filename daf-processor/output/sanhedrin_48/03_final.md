# Sanhedrin 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Context & Overview

### Previous Daf Review

Sanhedrin 48 picks up at the bottom of 47b, at the line beginning *itmar*, ten lines from the bottom of the page. The daf arrives in the middle of a sustained treatment of *misat beit din* — execution by rabbinic court — and the rules governing what happens afterward. The prohibition of *halanas hameis*, leaving the body overnight, which applies to one who has been hanged, opens a broader conversation about the general obligation of burial. That discussion branches into the question of the halakhic basis for *kevurah* in the first place, which then generates its own set of *nafka minos*.

### Purpose of Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת לַהֲרוּגֵי בֵּית דִּין? הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת, כֵּיוָן דְּשֶׁלֹּא בְּדִין קָא מִיקַּטְלִי, הָוְיָא לְהוּ כַּפָּרָה. הֲרוּגֵי בֵּית דִּין, כֵּיוָן דִּבְדִין קָא מִיקַּטְלִי, לָא הָוֵי לְהוּ כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: How do **those** referred to in the verse in Psalms who were **killed by** a heathen **government compare to those** inhabitants of an idolatrous city who were **killed by** a Jewish **court? Those who were killed by** a heathen **government, since they were killed unjustly, achieve atonement** through their deaths, even without repentance. But **those who were killed by** a Jewish **court, since they were killed justly** for their sins, **do not achieve atonement** unless they repent.

One central question is whether the purpose of *kevurah* is primarily to achieve *kaparah* — atonement — for the deceased, or whether it is driven by the concern that leaving a body unburied constitutes *bizayon*, a degradation and disgrace. The Gemara draws practical consequences from this distinction.

### Aveilus & Kaparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲבֵילוּת מֵאֵימָתַי קָא מַתְחֲלָא? מִסְּתִימַת הַגּוֹלֵל. כַּפָּרָה מֵאֵימָתַי קָא הָוְיָא? מִכִּי חָזוּ צַעֲרָא דְקִבְרָא פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** **When does** the obligation of **mourning** a deceased relative **commence?** It begins **from** the time of **the sealing of** the grave with **the grave cover.** And **when is atonement** achieved? Atonement is achieved **when** the deceased begins to **see** and experience **a bit of the anguish of the grave.**

Connected to this is the question of timing: when precisely does *aveilus* begin, and when does the *kaparah* take effect? The discussion from the previous daf establishes that *aveilus* begins at *stimas hagolel* — Rashi understands this as the closing of the coffin lid, while Tosafos understands it as the placement of the headstone, where there is one.

### Issurei Hana'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִבְרֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲווֹ שָׁקְלִי מִינֵּיהּ עַפְרָא לְאִישָּׁתָא בַּת יוֹמָא. אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל. אֲמַר לְהוּ: יָאוּת עָבְדִין, קַרְקַע עוֹלָם הוּא, וְקַרְקַע עוֹלָם אֵינָהּ נֶאֱסֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that people **would take dirt from the grave of Rav** as a cure **for a one-day fever.** A number of people **came and told Shmuel** about this practice, thinking that perhaps the dirt should be forbidden, as one may not derive benefit from a corpse. Shmuel **said to them:** They **are acting properly, as** the dirt in the grave **is natural ground, and natural ground does not become forbidden** in any situation.

The Gemara then moved into the question of *issurei hana'ah* — the prohibition on deriving benefit from a corpse. A corpse is itself forbidden in *hana'ah*, and the Gemara addressed a case involving soil taken from the burial site of Rav. Shmuel ruled that such soil is permissible, since it is *karka olam* — the ground itself — as distinct from something that was *talush u'levsof chibro*, originally detached and subsequently reattached to the ground, which, when placed there for the corpse, is conceptually connected to it and thus forbidden. This leads directly into the central question of the current daf: at what stage does something become connected to the corpse and thereby forbidden in *hana'ah*? This is the question of *hazmanah milsa* — whether designation alone, prior to actual use, suffices to confer a halakhic status.

## Hazmanah Milsa

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הָאוֹרֵג בֶּגֶד לַמֵּת, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אָסוּר, וְרָבָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed about the *halakha* in the following case: With regard to **one who weaves a garment for one who** has **died,** what is the *halakha*? **Abaye says:** It is **prohibited** to derive benefit from the garment, even if it was never actually used as a shroud. **And Rava says:** It is **permitted** to derive benefit from the garment.

The Gemara opens with a case: *ha'oreg beged l'meis* — one who is weaving a garment to serve as a shroud for a corpse (and it is crucial that the person is already dead). Does the act of weaving for the sake of the deceased render the garment already connected to and forbidden on account of the corpse, or must it await actual use? Abaye says it is forbidden; Rava says it is permitted.

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר אָסוּר – הַזְמָנָה מִלְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says** that one is **prohibited** from deriving benefit from the garment, since he maintains that mere **designation** of an item **is** a significant **matter,** i.e., all the relevant *halakhot* of an item already apply once an item is designated for a specific purpose, whether or not it has been used for that purpose.

Abaye rules *asur*, explaining his position with the phrase *hazmanah milsa* — designation is halakhically meaningful. It should be noted that Abaye is not speaking of mere verbal intent; the designation must come through an action, in this case the act of weaving. Nevertheless, the point is that preparing something, even before it is put to use, is sufficient to confer its halakhic status.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר מוּתָּר – הַזְמָנָה לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** that the garment is **permitted,** as he maintains that mere **designation is nothing,** and therefore the garment becomes prohibited only once it is actually used as a shroud.

Rava rules *mutar* and holds *hazmanah lav milsa* — designation without actual use is of no halakhic consequence. The status attaches only when the item is actually employed for its designated purpose.

### Models Compared

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּאַבָּיֵי? גָּמַר ״שָׁם״ ״שָׁם״ מֵעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה. מָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה בְּהַזְמָנָה מִיתַּסְרָא, הַאי נָמֵי בְּהַזְמָנָה מִיתַּסְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reasoning** behind the opinion **of Abaye? He derives** a verbal analogy between the word **"there"** written in the context of a grave, **from** the word **"there"** written in the context of **a heifer whose neck is broken.** **Just as** it **becomes prohibited** to derive benefit from **a heifer whose neck is broken through** mere **designation,** i.e., as soon as the animal is designated as such, **here too,** it **becomes prohibited** to derive benefit from the items relating to a dead person **through** mere **designation.**

The Gemara then probes the reasoning behind each position. *Mai ta'ama d'Abaye?* — What is Abaye's source? He derives it through a *gezerah shavah* of *sham sham* from *eglah arufah*. By the *eglah arufah* — the calf brought to the wadi in the case of an unsolved murder — the Torah uses the word *sham*, "there," indicating that the calf is left in the wadi. The same word *sham* appears in connection with death, in the verse *vatamas sham Miriam*. From the *eglah arufah* the rule emerges that the calf becomes forbidden in *hana'ah* already when it descends into the *nachal eitan*, the hard wadi, even before its neck is broken. As Rashi explains: *yeridata lenachal eitan hi osartah* — the mere descent into the wadi, which is the act of designation, suffices to forbid it. So too, by analogy, when something is designated for the dead, the designation itself is sufficient. Whether this applies as a general principle across all areas of *halakha* or specifically to matters involving the dead remains an open question at this stage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא גָּמַר ״שָׁם״ ״שָׁם״ מֵעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּהַזְמָנָה לָא מִיתַּסְרָא, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי בְּהַזְמָנָה לָא מִיתַּסְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava derives** a verbal analogy between the word **"there"** written in the context of a grave **from** the word **"there"** written in the context of objects of **idol worship.** **Just as** it **does not become prohibited** to derive benefit from objects of **idol worship through** mere **designation,** but only through actual ritual worship, **so too, here** it **does not become prohibited** to derive benefit from items relating to a deceased person **through** mere **designation.**

Rava, for his part, also employs the *gezerah shavah* of *sham sham*, but draws his analogy from *avodah zarah*. The verse by *avodah zarah* uses the term *asher avdu sham hagoyim*, and from this comes the principle that there is *ein hekdesh l'avodah zarah* — one cannot formally consecrate something to an idol through designation alone. If one declares that a house or an ox is donated to *avodah zarah*, the declaration confers no status; the object is not forbidden until it is actually put to use with the idol. Correspondingly, the corpse is forbidden in *hana'ah*, and something becomes connected to it — becoming an appurtenance of it — only when it is actually employed in connection with it, not when it is merely designated.

### Rava's Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא גָּמַר מֵעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה? אָמַר לָךְ: מְשַׁמְּשִׁין מִמְּשַׁמְּשִׁין גָּמְרִינַן, לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה דְּהִיא גּוּפַהּ קְדוֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rava, what is the reason** that **he does not derive** a verbal analogy **from a heifer whose neck is broken?** The Gemara answers: Rava could have **said to you:** **We derive** the *halakha* governing **the accessories** relating to a dead person, e.g., a shroud, **from** the *halakha* governing the **accessories** used in idol worship, e.g., the animal sacrificed to the idol. This is **to the exclusion of a heifer whose neck is broken, which is itself consecrated** for the ceremony taking place in the valley and is not an accessory used in that ceremony.

The Gemara now asks why Rava does not also learn from *eglah arufah*, and why Abaye does not learn from *avodah zarah*. Rava answers: *mishamesh mi'meshamesh gamrinan* — the relevant analogy is between one appurtenance and another appurtenance. Both the items designated for *avodah zarah* and the garments made for a corpse share the structural characteristic of being appurtenances of a forbidden object, rather than being that primary forbidden object itself. The question in both cases is: when does an accessory acquire the status of connection to the main forbidden thing? The answer, from *avodah zarah*, is: only when it is actually used together with it. This is not comparable to the *eglah arufah*, where one is making the thing itself forbidden — not an appurtenance of it. When a primary object is being forbidden, that may occur at an earlier stage, just as an object actually designated to be an idol itself becomes forbidden immediately upon designation. But for the status of an appurtenance, actual use is required.

### Abaye's Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא גָּמַר מֵעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? אָמַר לָךְ: מִידֵּי דְּאוֹרְחֵיהּ מִמִּידֵּי דְּאוֹרְחֵיהּ גָּמְרִינַן, לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּלָאו אוֹרְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Abaye, what is the reason** that **he did not derive** the *halakha* by way of a verbal analogy **from** the *halakha* governing accessories used in **idol worship?** The Gemara answers: Abaye could have **said to you** that **we learn** the *halakha* governing **something that** involves a proper **way** of conduct, i.e., burying the dead, **from** the *halakha* governing **something that** involves a proper **way** of conduct, i.e., the rite relating to the heifer whose neck is broken, as these are both mitzvot by Torah law. This is **to the exclusion of** objects of **idol worship, which do not** involve a proper **way** of conduct.

Abaye responds: *midi d'orcha mi'midi d'orcha* — one should compare matters of the same normative character. The *eglah arufah* and the shroud for a corpse are both things carried out within the ordinary halakhic framework, operating within the realm of things people are supposed to be doing — or at least things that fall within the normal universe of *halakhic* obligation and practice. When one acts within that framework, designating something for its purpose suffices to confer status, since the *hazmanah* flows from a commanded or at least normatively appropriate act. *Avodah zarah*, by contrast, is something nobody should be doing — not Jews, and indeed not non-Jews either. One does not give earlier or fuller effect to designations that should never have been made in the first place. Hence the analogy from *avodah zarah* is rejected; *eglah arufah*, which operates within the halakhic order, is the appropriate model.

The Gemara also establishes that the phrase *hazmanah milsa* and *hazmanah lav milsa* is a general formulation, not limited to cases involving the dead. Even though the *gezerah shavah* was anchored in the context of *meis*, what is found in that context becomes the general rule throughout *halakha*: either, following Abaye, designation suffices to confer status across the board, or, following Rava, only actual use does.

## Rav Chisda's Test

### Kerchief & Sefer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כִּפָּה שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא מִדְרָס וּנְתָנַתּוּ לְסֵפֶר – טָהוֹר מִן הַמִּדְרָס, אֲבָל טָמֵא מַגַּע מִדְרָס. אֵימָא: נְתָנַתּוּ וּכְרָכַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against Rava from a *baraita* discussing ritual impurity that is contracted by treading: If **a scarf is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **treading,** e.g., after a *zav* sat or lay down upon it, **and** its owner then **designated it** as a cover **for** a Torah **scroll,** it is **pure of** the more severe level of ritual impurity, that which is imparted by **treading,** even without immersion, because it is no longer an object intended to be lied on or sat upon. **But** it is still **impure** with the less severe ritual impurity, that which is contracted **by touching that which is treaded upon.** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Say** that the *baraita* should read as follows: The owner of the scarf **designated it** as a cover for a Torah scroll **and wrapped it** around the scroll, so that there was not only designation, but an action as well.

The Gemara raises a challenge from a case involving a *kippa she'tamei midras* — a headscarf that has contracted *tumah* of *midras*, the type of impurity generated when a *zav* or *zava* lies on or sits on something susceptible to that form of *tumah*. Because such a headscarf can double as a pillow, it is susceptible to *midras tumah*. Now if one then redesignates this scarf to serve as a wrapping for a *sefer Torah* or any other book — a use that is wholly incompatible with the function of sitting or lying upon — the scarf becomes *tahor min hamidras*. It can no longer be a *mishkav* or *moshav* since it is no longer used for that purpose. However, it remains *tamei maga midras* — impure through contact with a *midras* — and is thus a *rishon l'tumah*. The mechanism of that residual *tumah* is subtle: at the moment of its transition from *midras* to *sefer* covering, it ceases to be a *midras* object itself, but in that same instant, as an ordinary vessel, it has touched something that was a *midras* — namely itself in its prior state — and thereby contracts one degree of *tumah*. The relevant point is that the redesignation alone, apparently without any actual use as a *sefer* wrapping, changes the object's status. This seems to constitute proof that *hazmanah milsa*, supporting Abaye.

There are, however, two difficulties with this reading. First, even Abaye was speaking of a *hazmanah* effected through an action — weaving the garment — not through bare verbal declaration. Second, the general principle in the laws of vessels holds that *kol hakelim yordim lidei tumasan b'machshava v'ein olim midei tumasan ela b'shinui ma'aseh* — vessels acquire *tumah* through *machshava* (thought or intent), but they are not purified from their *tumah* except through an actual physical change to the object. These two difficulties may be mutually resolving. Tosafos suggests that the phrase *nasnu l'sefer* — "and he designated it for the *sefer*" — implies that some physical change was made to render the scarf suitable for use as a *sefer* covering, even though it has not yet actually been used. Thus the case demonstrates that designation through an action is sufficient to change status even absent actual use — a proof for Abaye, and, importantly, the principle appears to extend beyond the specific context of the dead.

### Rav Chisda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי נְתָנַתּוּ וּכְרָכַתּוּ? כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַאי סוּדָרָא דְּאַזְמְנֵיהּ לְמֵיצַר בֵּיהּ תְּפִילִּין, וְצַר בֵּיהּ תְּפִילִּין – אָסוּר לְמֵיצַר בֵּיהּ פְּשִׁיטֵי. אַזְמְנֵיהּ וְלָא צַר בֵּיהּ, צַר בֵּיהּ וְלָא אַזְמְנֵיהּ – שְׁרֵי לְמֵיצַר בֵּיהּ פְּשִׁיטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If there was an action, **why do I** need for the *baraita* to say that the owner **designated it and wrapped it?** Wouldn't wrapping alone have sufficed? The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda says:** With regard to **this cloth that one designated for** the purpose of **bundling phylacteries in it,** if he already **bundled phylacteries in it,** then **it is forbidden to bundle coins in it,** as it was designated and used as an accessory of a sacred object. But if **he** merely **designated it** for that purpose **but did not** yet **bundle** phylacteries **in it,** or if **he bundled** phylacteries **in it but did not** previously **designate it** for bundling phylacteries, then **it is permitted to bundle coins in it.** Consequently, in the case of the scarf, it was necessary both to designate it as a cover for the Torah scroll and to actually wrap it around the scroll.

Rava, however, contests this reading. He insists that *nasnu v'karcho* — "he designated it and wrapped the *Sefer Torah* with it" — means that both steps occurred: the designation and the actual wrapping. The Mishnah mentions the designation specifically because, as Rav Chisda teaches, both designation and use are necessary.

### Both Required

The *sugya* continues to explore this principle through the lens of Rav Chisda's teaching: both *hazmanah* (intentional designation through action) and *tzira* (actual use) are required for an object to acquire the status of being connected to a sacred purpose.

### Designation Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ולְאַבָּיֵי דְּאָמַר: הַזְמָנָה מִילְּתָא הִיא, אַזְמְנֵיהּ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא צַר בֵּיהּ. צַר בֵּיהּ: אִי אַזְמְנֵיהּ – אִין, אִי לָא אַזְמְנֵיהּ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to Abaye, who said** that **designation is** a significant **matter,** the *halakha* is as follows: If **he designated** the cloth for the purpose of bundling phylacteries in it, **even if he did not bundle** phylacteries **in it,** it is prohibited to bundle money in it. In a case where **he** already **bundled** phylacteries **in it, if he designated** the cloth for bundling phylacteries, **yes,** it is prohibited to bundle money in it; but **if he did not designate it** for bundling phylacteries, bundling money in the cloth is **not** prohibited.

Abaye, who holds *hazmanah milsa*, does not accept Rav Chisda's requirement of dual conditions. For Abaye, what matters is the designation. *Azmanei af al gav de'lo tzar bei* — designating it, even without ever wrapping *tefillin* in it, suffices to give it the status of *tashmishei kedushah*. Actual use is neither needed in addition to designation nor does it accomplish anything by itself. *Tzar bei ei azmanei* — if one used it: did one designate it? If yes, it has the status; if not, it does not. The designation is the sole operative factor. The result is that for Abaye the only question is whether a genuine *hazmanah* was performed; for Rava, the only configuration that confers status is designation followed by actual use.

## Graves & Structures

### Tombstone Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁבְּנָאוֹ לְשֵׁם חַי – מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה. הוֹסִיף בּוֹ דִּימוֹס אֶחָד לְשֵׁם מֵת – אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דִּרְמָא בֵּיהּ מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof against Rava: With regard to **a monument** that was built as a memorial **for the sake of** an individual while he was still **alive,** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it, as it is like any other construction; but if even a single **row** of stones **was added to it for the sake of a corpse,** one is **prohibited from** deriving **benefit** from it, apparently even before the deceased is buried there. This seems to indicate that mere designation is a significant matter. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where the corpse was placed** under the monument, so that it became forbidden not through mere designation, but by way of an action.

The Gemara brings another test case. *Nefesh she'banao le'shem chai* — if someone constructs a structure, a kind of tombstone or funerary monument, over a grave site intended for someone still alive. Since that person is still living, there is as yet no corpse with which the structure could be connected. Accordingly, *mutar b'hana'ah* — benefit may be derived from it, because the object to which it would be connected does not yet exist.

### Addition After Death

After this structure is built and while it remains permissible — the person then dies, and at that point someone adds *dimos echad l'shem meis*, a single row of bricks with the corpse now in existence — that addition renders the entire structure forbidden in *hana'ah*. This seems to support Abaye: the act of adding bricks, which constitutes designation through action, combined with the presence of a corpse, suffices even before anything is fully "used" in the ordinary sense.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא הוֹסִיף? כִּי לֹא הוֹסִיף נָמֵי! לָא צְרִיכָא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּפַנְּיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, why** does the *baraita* **specifically** mention that a row of stones **was added?** If the deceased was buried under the monument, one should be prohibited from deriving benefit from it **even when** a row of stones **was not added,** as the entire monument has been used for the dead. The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** for the *baraita* to state that a row of stones was added in order to teach us that **even if** the corpse **was** later **removed** from the grave, the monument remains forbidden since it had been designated for a corpse.

The Gemara deflects by establishing that the case concerns a body already interred: *hacha b'mai askinan de'ramya bei meis* — we are dealing with a situation where the corpse has actually been placed in the grave. This invites a further objection: if the body is already in the grave, why does the addition of the new row of bricks matter at all? Why should the original tombstone not already be forbidden simply by virtue of the buried body? The answer is that the case involves the body having subsequently been removed — *af al gav de'panya*. This now maps precisely onto the structure of the *tefillin* case. The tombstone was used alongside a corpse — the body was in the grave — but it had never been genuinely designated for that purpose, since at the time of original construction the intended occupant was still alive. Absent designation, even use creates only a transient status: the object is forbidden while in use but reverts to its original permissible state once the corpse is removed, just as the kerchief used ad hoc to wrap *tefillin* remains eligible for use as a money pouch. If, however, the row of bricks is added after the person's death — constituting a genuine designation at a time when there is a corpse — then the full status attaches, and the structure remains forbidden even if the body is later removed.

### Reversing Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אִם הָיָה מַכִּירוֹ, חוֹלְצוֹ וּמוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rafram bar Pappa says** that **Rav Ḥisda says: If** the corpse was later removed from the grave and **one recognizes** the row of stones that had been added to the monument in the dead man's honor, he may **remove** that row of stones, **and** it is **permitted** to derive benefit from the rest of the construction.

Rav Pappa bar Pappa in the name of Rav Chisda adds a further teaching: *im hayah mekiro chotzo mutar* — if one can identify the specific bricks that were added by way of designation, one may remove them, and in doing so reverse the act of designation. With the designation undone, the structure reverts to its prior permissible status. This reinforces that the operative mechanism is the act of designation through a physical action — laying the bricks — and that undoing that act undoes the designation.

## Father's Grave

### Father's Cave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַחוֹצֵב קֶבֶר לְאָבִיו, וְהָלַךְ וּקְבָרוֹ בְּקֶבֶר אַחֵר – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִקָּבֵר בּוֹ עוֹלָמִית. הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** yet another proof against Rava from a *baraita*: If **one hewed a grave for his** deceased **father** in one place, **and** then **went and buried him in a different place,** the son **may never be buried in** the grave that he had dug. This indicates that mere designation is a significant matter. The Gemara answers: **There,** the son may not be buried in the grave that he had dug **because** that would detract from **the honor** that he must show **his father,** not because the designation is significant with regard to the status of a grave in general.

The Gemara brings yet another case: *ha'chotzev kever l'aviv* — one who has hewn a cave for his father's burial. He carves it out, but ultimately the father is interred elsewhere. The ruling is *lo yikaver bo olamis* — no one may ever be buried in that cave. The Gemara's initial assumption is that this prohibition stems from *issur hana'ah*, and is a function of *hazmanah milsa* — the designation of the cave for the father is sufficient to connect it to the deceased and to make burial of another therein a form of forbidden *hana'ah*.

### Hazmanah vs. Honor

This initial reading is puzzling on multiple grounds. The cave is *karka olam*, ground, and the Gemara itself noted earlier that ground is generally not subject to *issur hana'ah*. Moreover, even if we were to say it is forbidden, why would using the cave for a different corpse constitute *hana'ah* forbidden to the living? Burying anyone in it is serving the dead, not the living. And if it is designated for the dead in general, any corpse should be eligible. The Gemara resolves both of these tensions by rejecting the *hazmanah milsa* reading: *hatam mishum kevod aviv* — the prohibition here is not about *issur hana'ah* at all, but about the personal honor owed to one's father. It is socially and morally disrespectful to use a grave carved specifically for one's father for a different person. The restriction is particular to the relationship; if the cave had been carved for Reuven and Reuven was ultimately buried elsewhere, Shimon could certainly be interred there.

### Stones Quarried

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר, אַף הַחוֹצֵב אֲבָנִים לְאָבִיו וְהָלַךְ וּקְבָרוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִקָּבֵר בָּהֶן עוֹלָמִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So, too, it is reasonable** that the son may not be buried in the grave that he had dug only because of the honor due to his father, **as the latter clause** of the same *baraita* **teaches: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even** if **one hewed stones for his father's** grave **and** then **went and buried him in a different place,** the son himself **may never be buried** in a tomb constructed **from those** stones.

The Gemara adds that this reading is supported by the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the continuation of that Mishnah: *af ha'chotzev avanim l'aviv* — even one who quarries stones intended to build a burial structure for his father, and the father is ultimately interred elsewhere, those stones may not be used for another corpse. This extension makes perfect sense if the reason is *kevod ha'av* — respect for one's father — since even preparing stones in honor of a parent creates a social and moral restriction on using those materials for another. The father's honor attaches to anything expressly prepared in his memory.

### Honor Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד אָבִיו – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם הַזְמָנָה: טְוִוי לַאֲרִיגָה מִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, if you say** that the son may not be buried in the grave that he dug for his father **because of the honor** that must be shown **his father,** it is **well** understood why he can also not be buried in tomb constructed from stones that he hewed for his father's grave. **But if you say** that it is **because of designation,** the son should be permitted to be buried in such a tomb, as **is there anyone who says** that **spinning** yarn in order **to weave** it into cloth for shrouds is regarded as designating an item for a dead person, resulting in a prohibition against deriving benefit from the yarn? In such a case there is certainly no prohibition, and the case of hewing stones for a tomb is analogous to a case of spinning yarn in order to weave it into cloth.

If, by contrast, the prohibition were a function of *hazmanah milsa* and *issur hana'ah*, this extension would be impossible to sustain. Abaye's position holds that designation through action — *hazmanah* — confers status, but this requires that the designated object already be in a state ready for use. A finished garment designated as a shroud is in a ready state; quarried stones awaiting construction are the equivalent of thread before it is woven into a garment. No one would argue that spinning thread gives it the status of a burial garment. Hence Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling cannot be grounded in *hazmanah milsa* and must be understood purely in terms of *kevod ha'mais* and *kevod ha'av*. The prohibition is one of dignity and honor, not of *issurei hana'ah*.

## New Grave Case

### Used vs. Unused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קֶבֶר חָדָשׁ מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה. הֵטִיל בּוֹ נֵפֶל – אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. הֵטִיל – אִין, לֹא הֵטִיל – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof in support of Rava from a *baraita*: With regard to **a fresh grave** that was not designated for a particular individual, one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it. But if even **a non-viable newborn** that died **was cast into it,** one is **prohibited from** deriving **benefit** from the grave. It may be inferred from here that if a non-viable newborn **was cast** into the grave, **yes,** one is prohibited from deriving benefit from the grave, but if it **was not cast** into the grave, one is **not** prohibited from deriving benefit from the grave, indicating that mere designation is insignificant.

The Gemara brings a further source: *kever chadash mutar b'hana'ah* — a new grave is permissible in benefit. The assumption here is that "new" means it was dug for a corpse but has not yet been used. *Nitna bo nefel assur b'hana'ah* — once even a *nefel*, a miscarried fetus, has been placed in the grave, it becomes forbidden. The bracketed issue of *karka olam* and how this applies to ground is noted but set aside.

### Supports Rava

On its plain reading, this passage appears to be a strong proof for Rava. The grave was dug — it was designated for a corpse — and yet it remains permitted until a body is actually placed in it. The operative factor is *heitil*, placing the body in, not the prior designation: *heitil in, lo heitil* — only when one has placed [the body], and not before.

### Fetus Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הַדִּין דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הֵטִיל, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר: אֵין לִנְפָלִים תְּפִיסַת הַקֶּבֶר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this inference: **The same is true even if** a non-viable newborn **was not cast** into the grave; one is still prohibited from deriving benefit from the grave, merely by virtue of the designation. Why, then, does the *baraita* say that a non-viable newborn was cast into the grave? This serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who says: A non-viable newborn does not take possession of its grave** to render it forbidden even once it is buried there. To counter this opinion, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this opinion is not accepted, and a non-viable newborn is treated like any other corpse.

The Gemara rejects this straightforward reading by offering an alternative. What the teaching actually comes to establish is not the general rule of *hazmanah lav milsa*, but rather to controvert the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that a *nefel* — a miscarried fetus — does not *tofes* the grave, does not affix the status of a used burial site to it. The baraita is saying that even a fetus does create that status, against Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. On Abaye's reading, *kever chadash* does not mean a grave dug but not yet used; it means a grave prepared for someone still living, which is why it is permissible — not because the grave has not yet been used, but because there is no corpse with which it can be connected. Once it is designated for a dead person, even a fetus, it is forbidden. The Gemara acknowledges that this reading of *kever chadash* is strained but maintains it in order to preserve Abaye's position.

## Money Collection

### General vs. Specific

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מוֹתַר הַמֵּתִים – לְמֵתִים, מוֹתַר הַמֵּת – לְיוֹרְשָׁיו. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? שֶׁגָּבוּ מֵחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof in support of Rava's opinion from a mishna (*Shekalim* 2:5): The money **left over** from the money that was collected for burying **the dead** must be allocated **to** the burial of other **dead** people. The money **left over** from the money that was collected for burying a particular **dead person** is given **to his heirs.** Evidently, money that had been designated for the sake of a dead person but was not actually used for burying him does not become forbidden, and his heirs may use it. The Gemara rejects this proof: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with money **that had been collected** for the deceased **during his lifetime.** It was thought that a certain individual was soon to die, and money was collected to cover the costs of his burial. Since at the time the money was collected the person was alive, the money had not been designated for the sake of a dead person. Therefore, one is permitted to derive benefit from it, and it is given to the heirs of the deceased.

The Gemara introduces a distinction from a Mishnah in tractate Shekalim: *mosar hamaisim lamaisim* — if funds are collected generally for burial costs of the poor, leftover funds remain available for future burial needs. *Mosar hamais l'yorshav* — if funds are collected specifically for a named individual who has died, any surplus after covering the burial expenses goes to the heirs. The question is whether the designation of money for a specific corpse confers on those funds a status connected to the deceased that would preclude their ordinary use — and the ruling that the heirs receive the surplus suggests that it does not.

### Braisa Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא תָּנֵי הָכִי, דִּתְנַן: מוֹתַר הַמֵּתִים – לְמֵתִים, מוֹתַר הַמֵּת – לְיוֹרְשָׁיו. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: כֵּיצַד? גָּבוּ לְמֵתִים סְתָם – זֶהוּ מוֹתַר הַמֵּתִים לְמֵתִים, גָּבוּ לְמֵת זֶה – זֶהוּ מוֹתַר הַמֵּת לְיוֹרְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But we were not taught this, as we learned** in the mishna: The money **left over** from the money that was collected for burying **the dead** must be allocated **to** the burial of other **dead** people. The money **left over** from the money that was collected for burying a particular **dead** person is given **to his heirs. And it is taught with regard to this** mishna in a *baraita*: **How so?** If **they collected** money **for the** purpose of burying the **dead without specification, this is** the case about which they said that the money **left over** from the money that was collected for burying **the dead** must be allocated **to** the burial of other **dead** people. But if **they collected** money **for** the sake of **this** particular **dead** person, **this is** the case about which they said that the money **left over** from the money that was collected for burying a particular **dead** person is given **to his heirs.** This indicates that the mishna is discussing a case where the money was designated for one who had already died.

A *braisa* is cited to elaborate. *Gavu l'maisim stam* — if one collected for burial costs in general, without specifying any individual, the surplus is available for *maisim stam*, any burial need. *Gavu l'mais zeh* — if one collected specifically for this corpse, for Reuven after his death, that is a case of *mosar hamais l'yorshav*, and the surplus goes to the heirs. This shows that designating money for a particular corpse does not render it permanently connected to that corpse in the manner of *issurei hana'ah*. Tosafos notes that one might object that money is too fungible to serve as a test case for *hazmanah milsa*, since it is not yet in the state of the final object. Tosafos responds that precisely because money can be readily converted into whatever burial necessity is required, it is considered in a state of readiness — analogous to a completed garment rather than raw thread — and is a valid test case for the principle.

### Abaye Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ. אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הַזְמָנָה מִילְּתָא הִיא. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ – תָּפֵיס, דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ – לָא תָּפֵיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye** can **explain** the entire mishna **according to his** line of **reasoning, and Rava,** too, can **explain** the entire mishna **according to his** line of **reasoning.** The Gemara explains: **Abaye** can **explain** the entire mishna **according to his** line of **reasoning,** as follows: **According to everyone,** i.e., the first *tanna*, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Natan, mere **designation is** a significant **matter.** The **first *tanna* holds** that the sum **that** it **is fitting to** spend for the burial of the particular dead person **is encompassed** by the prohibition, and the sum **that** it **is not fitting to** spend **is not encompassed** by the prohibition. Therefore, whatever money is not required for his burial is given to his heirs, as it was never included in the prohibition.

Abaye maintains that all positions in this dispute are compatible with *hazmanah milsa* — all agree that designation confers meaningful status. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that *dechazu letaphis* — only the funds that were actually required by the burial are retroactively considered designated. The surplus, which in hindsight was never needed, is understood to have never been included in the *hazmanah*. This is a principle akin to *breira*: the designation is clarified retroactively by what was actually necessary. Rabbi Meir holds that since at the time of collection the donors assumed everything collected would be needed, perhaps all the funds were fully designated for the *mais* — *tafi*, it designated — or perhaps not — *lo tafi* — since retroactively not all of it was used. His uncertainty on this point leads to the ruling of *lo yiga bahen ad sheyavo Eliyahu*. Rabbi Nosson holds unambiguously that yes, since the money was collected for this *mais* while the collection was taking place, it is fully designated — *peshita lei devadai taphis* — and must therefore be applied to the benefit of that *mais*.

### Rava Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הַזְמָנָה לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: כִּי בָּזוּ לֵיהּ, אַחוֹלֵי מַחֵיל זִילוּתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי יוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava,** too, can **explain** the mishna **according to his** line of **reasoning,** as follows: **According to everyone,** i.e., the first *tanna*, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Natan, mere **designation is nothing,** and so the money collected for the burial of a particular dead person does not become forbidden. And the disagreement is about an entirely different manner. The **first *tanna* holds** that even though the heirs of the deceased **disgraced him** by collecting money for his burial, the deceased still **forgoes his disgrace for** the benefit of his **heirs,** and therefore the leftover money is given to them.

Rava explains the same dispute entirely within his own framework: *lechulei alma hazmanah lav milsa hi* — all agree that designation is of no halakhic consequence. The entire debate, therefore, has nothing to do with *issurei hana'ah* and everything to do with *kevod hamais* — the honor of the deceased. It would be disrespectful for money collected in a person's memory, for his funeral, to be taken home by his heirs and spent on their own needs. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that the deceased would have wanted his heirs to benefit and would forgive the indignity — *machil zilutei gabei yorshim*. Rabbi Meir is uncertain whether the deceased would forgive that disrespect or not — *mesafke lei machil o lo machil* — and so rules that no one should touch the funds. Rabbi Nosson holds that the deceased would certainly not forgive such indignity — *peshita lei delo machil* — and therefore the funds must be spent on something honorable for him, namely the grave monument or sprinkling.

## Parents' Grief

### Prevention Duty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָיוּ אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ מְזָרְקִין בּוֹ כֵּלִים, מִצְוָה עַל הָאֲחֵרִים לְהַצִּילָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof in support of Rava's opinion from a *baraita*: **If the father and the mother of** the deceased **were throwing garments onto** their late son's bier so that they would be buried along with him, it is **a mitzva for the others** present **to save** those garments from being lost. This indicates that mere designation is insignificant, as were it significant, one would be prohibited to derive benefit from the garments as soon as the parents cast them onto the bier.

The Gemara brings a final test case in this series: *hayu aviv ve'imo mizarkin bo kelim* — the mother and father of a deceased child, overwhelmed with grief, are throwing their belongings — clothing, jewelry, and various objects — into the coffin or onto the body. The ruling is *mitzvah aleichem lehatzilan* — it is obligatory for bystanders to prevent these items from entering the coffin, to rescue them. The reason is practical and compassionate: the parents, in their immediate anguish, will later be grateful for the intervention once they have recovered from the initial shock of grief. Rashi clarifies that *lehatzilan* refers to removing objects that the parents have already thrown into the coffin before they are buried together with the corpse.

### Fevered State

The question is what this teaches about *hazmanah milsa*. If the items become forbidden only once they actually come into contact with the body and are buried with it, then *hazmanah lav milsa* and Rava is supported. One should be able to retrieve them at any point before actual interment, since no status has yet attached.

Abaye's response to this proof is *hatam mishum merirayhu* — in that situation the parents are acting out of a state of *merirut*, profound bitterness and anguish. Their casting of items into the coffin does not constitute a genuine, reflective *hazmanah*. It is an impulsive act of grief, not a considered designation. Therefore it carries no halakhic weight as *hazmanah*, even for Abaye.

### Gezera Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם מִשּׁוּם מְרָרַיְיהוּ. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא נָגְעוּ בַּמִּטָּה, אֲבָל נָגְעוּ בַּמִּטָּה – אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** the parents threw the garments onto their son's bier **because of their bitterness** over his death, but they did not really mean to designate the garments to be buried with him. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** how can one understand **that which is taught with regard to** this *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is stated with regard to a case **where** the garments **did not** yet **touch the bier** before they were saved. **But** if **they** already **touched the bier, they are forbidden.** If no importance is ascribed to what is done out of bitterness, why should the garments that touched the bier be forbidden?

The Gemara, however, questions whether Abaye's answer is sufficient to explain the teaching of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who says: *bameh devarim amurim shelo nag'u bamittah — aval nag'u bamittah assurin* — when is it that we can rescue these items? Only if they have not yet touched the funeral bier. Once they have touched the bier, they are forbidden. This means that for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the combination of the parents' intent and contact with the bier is sufficient to confer forbidden status — regardless of the fevered state of the parents. If their designation is meaningless due to *merirut*, the touching of the bier should also be irrelevant. Evidently, for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, even a fevered *hazmanah* carries some weight when combined with physical contact. And for the *Tanna Kamma*, even touching the bier is not enough to forbid the items, which aligns with Rava.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ עוּלָּא: בְּמִטָּה הַנִּקְבֶּרֶת עִמּוֹ, דְּמִחַלְּפִי בְּתַכְרִיכִי הַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla interpreted** the *baraita* as referring to a case where the garments were thrown **onto a bier that was to be buried** together **with** the son, and it is possible **that** those garments might **be mistaken for shrouds of the deceased.** The Sages decreed that it is prohibited to derive benefit from such garments, lest people mistakenly think that just as they are permitted, so too, actual shrouds are permitted. There is no proof from here that mere designation is significant and that articles designated for the dead are permanently forbidden.

The Gemara resolves this through Ulla's teaching: *targemah Ulla bemittah hanikberet imo* — the case of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concerns a bier that is actually going to be buried together with the deceased. The issue is not *hazmanah milsa* at all; the ruling is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic protective decree — based on the concern that if one were permitted to retrieve items thrown onto the bier while the bier itself is going to be interred, people would infer that the actual *tachrichin*, the burial shrouds, may also be retrieved and removed from the dead. Permitting the removal of items from the bier would thus create a dangerous precedent regarding the shrouds themselves. The *Tanna Kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel therefore disagree not about *hazmanah milsa* but about the scope of this protective decree.

## Tefillin & Sefer

### Ad Hoc Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כִּיס שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ לְהַנִּיחַ בּוֹ תְּפִילִּין – אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחַ בּוֹ מָעוֹת. הִנִּיחַ בּוֹ תְּפִילִּין – יַנִּיחַ בּוֹ מָעוֹת. אֵימָא: עֲשָׂאוֹ וְהִנִּיחַ בּוֹ תְּפִילִּין – אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחַ בּוֹ מָעוֹת, כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof in support of Abaye's opinion from a *baraita*: If **one fashioned a pouch in which to put phylacteries, it is prohibited to put money in it.** If **he put phylacteries in it,** but he did not designate it beforehand for that purpose, **he may** still **put money in it.** The ruling of the first clause indicates that designation is significant. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Say** that the *baraita* should be understood as follows: If **one fashioned** a pouch for phylacteries **and** then **put the phylacteries in it, it is prohibited to put money in it.** This is **in accordance with** the ruling **of Rav Ḥisda,** that if one designated a cloth for the purpose of bundling phylacteries in it and then he wrapped the phylacteries in it, he may no longer use the cloth to hold money.

The Gemara brings a further teaching: *kis she'asao lehaniach bo tefillin, assur lehaniach bo ma'ot* — a bag that one has made to hold *tefillin* may not be used as a money pouch. However, *heniach bo tefillin, yaniach bo ma'ot* — if one has merely placed *tefillin* in it ad hoc, without designating it as one's *tefillin* bag, one may use it for money afterward. This appears to be an explicit statement that designation alone — making the bag *for tefillin* — suffices to give it the status of *tashmishei kedushah*, while ad hoc use without designation does not. This would be a clear proof for Abaye.

### Both Required

The Gemara deflects: *eima asao veheniach bo tefillin assur lehaniach bo ma'ot k'derav Chisda* — the teaching should be read as: he designated it *and* placed his *tefillin* in it, and only then is it forbidden as a money pouch, following Rav Chisda's requirement of both designation and use. The designation mentioned in the text is not sufficient on its own; the text is compressed, and the actual use is implied. This is admittedly not the plain meaning of the text, but it is how Rava reads it.

### Craftsman Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אָמַר לְאוּמָּן ״עֲשֵׂה לִי תִּיק שֶׁל סֵפֶר״ אוֹ ״נַרְתִּיק שֶׁל תְּפִילִּין״ – עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן קוֹדֶשׁ, מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן חוֹל. נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן קוֹדֶשׁ – אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another *baraita* that supports the opinion of Rava: If **one said to a craftsman: Fashion me a case [*tik*] for** a Torah **scroll, or:** Fashion for me **a bag [*nartik*] for phylacteries, as long as he has not** yet **used them** for **a sacred** purpose, it is **permitted** for him **to use them** for **a mundane** purpose. But if **he** already **used them** for **a sacred** purpose, it is **prohibited** for him **to use them** for **a mundane** purpose. This indicates that mere designation is nothing until the item is actually used for the purpose for which it had been designated.

The Gemara then brings an explicit and unambiguous teaching: *amar le'uman aseh li tik shel sefer o nartik shel tefillin* — if one instructs a craftsman to make a covering for a *Sefer Torah* or a case for *tefillin*. *Ad shelo nishtamesh bahen kodesh, mutar lehishtamesh bahen chol* — before one has actually used them for the holy objects, one may use them for mundane purposes. *Nishtamesh bahen kodesh, assur lehishtamesh bahen chol* — once one has used them for the holy purpose, they become *tashmishei kedushah* and may no longer be used for mundane purposes. This is an explicit ruling like Rava: designation and commission alone are insufficient; actual use for the sacred object is required.

### Tannaic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: צִיפָּן זָהָב אוֹ שֶׁטָּלָה עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹר שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – פְּסוּלוֹת. עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – כְּשֵׁירוֹת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא עִיבְּדָן לִשְׁמָן. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה פְּסוּלוֹת, עַד שֶׁיְּעַבְּדוֹ לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The matter of whether or not mere designation is significant **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one took phylacteries and **coated them with gold or patched them** with the **hide of a non-kosher animal, they are unfit.** But if one patched them with the **hide of a kosher animal** then **they are fit, and** this is so **even though he did not prepare them,** i.e., the hides, **for their sake,** i.e., for the sake of their use in a mitzva. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even** if he patched them with the **hide of a kosher animal they are unfit,** and they remain unfit **until he prepares them for their sake.** Preparing the hide for the sake of the mitzva is analogous to designating it for that purpose. The first *tanna* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who disagree as to whether the hide must be prepared for the sake of the mitzva, differ as to whether designation is significant.

Faced with these conflicting indications from various *braisos* and Mishnaic sources, the Gemara concludes: *tannai hi* — it is a dispute among Tannaim. The evidence from earlier suggested Tannaim who hold that *hazmanah* alone, or *hazmanah* through action without actual use, suffices; the final teaching cited clearly requires actual use. Rashi explains the final *baraisa* about tanning leather *lishma* — for the sake of *tefillin* — in light of *hazmanah lav milsa*: since designation does not confer status, preparing the leather without *ibud lishma* is irrelevant, and only actual proper preparation for the sake of *tefillin* matters. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that the leather of a kosher animal is invalid for *tefillin* unless the tanning process was done *lishma*, for that purpose. Tosafos, in the name of Rabbeinu Tam, explains the linkage in reverse: Rava, who holds *hazmanah lav milsa*, corresponds to the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that *ibud lishma* is required, since designation alone is insufficient and a more substantive act of preparation is needed. Abaye, who holds *hazmanah milsa*, would not require *ibud lishma*, since the designation through the act of preparation gives the leather its status. The Gemara acknowledges that the connection between the *lishma* requirement for leather and the *hazmanah* debate is not straightforward — in particular, even Rava requires both *hazmanah* and use to confer status, so one could argue that even for Rava one should need *ibud lishma* plus actual use — but the Gemara draws the conclusion that at minimum there is a *machloket Tannaim* underlying the debate of Abaye and Rava.

## Final Ruling

### Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַשׁ מָרִימָר: הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mareimar taught** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Abaye** that mere designation is significant. **And the Rabbis say** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that mere designation is nothing. The Gemara concludes that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava** that mere designation is nothing.

The *sugya* concludes with a statement of the practical *halakha*. *Darash Mareimar hilkhata k'vatei de'Abaye* — Mareimar ruled publicly that the *halakha* follows Abaye. *Ve'rabbanan amri hilkhata k'vatei de'Rava* — the Rabbis say the *halakha* is in accordance with Rava.

### Accepted Law

*Ve'hilkhata k'vatei de'Rava* — and the accepted *halakha* is like Rava. This is not one of the well-known exceptional cases — the *ya'al kegam* — in which we rule against Rava. The practical consequence is that *hazmanah lav milsa*: designation alone does not confer status. What is required is both *hazmanah* — a genuine, intentional designation through action — and actual use. Only the combination of both conditions causes an object to acquire its new halakhic status.

## Execution & Property

### Property Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת – נִכְסֵיהֶן לַמֶּלֶךְ, הֲרוּגֵי בֵּית דִּין – נִכְסֵיהֶן לַיּוֹרְשִׁין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת נִכְסֵיהֶן לַיּוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **those executed by** a Jewish **king** for crimes that they committed against him, **their property** belongs **to the king.** As for **those executed by the court** for a capital transgression, **their property** belongs **to** their **heirs. Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** with regard to **those executed by** a Jewish **king, their property** belongs **to** their **heirs.**

With the conclusion of the *sugya* on *hazmanah milsa*, the Gemara pivots back to the topic of court-ordered executions and the laws governing property. *Harugei malkhut nikhseihem l'melekh* — the property of those executed by the king reverts to the king, presumably in the context of *mored b'malkhut*, rebellion against the crown. *Harugei beit din nikhseihem l'yorshim* — those executed by the court leave their property to their heirs. This latter principle is unremarkable: regardless of the circumstances of death, the estate passes to heirs in the ordinary course. The *chiddush* is the claim that property is seized by the king in the case of a royal execution.

### Dissent

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *af harugei malkhut nikhseihem l'yorshim* — even those executed by the king leave their property to the heirs. Rabbi Yehuda challenges the premise that any government body acquires rights to the property of an executed person.

### Navot Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״הִנֵּה בְּכֶרֶם נָבוֹת אֲשֶׁר יָרַד שָׁם לְרִשְׁתּוֹ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Yehuda: But isn't** it **already stated:** "Arise, go down to meet Ahab, king of Israel, who is in Samaria; **behold he is in the vineyard of Naboth, where he is gone down there to inherit it"** (I Kings 21:18)? The wording of the verse indicates that Ahab went down there by right, proving that the property of those executed by the king legally belongs to the king.

The Rabbis cite a proof against Rabbi Yehuda from the story of Navot: *hinei b'cherem Navot asher yarad sham l'rishto* — "Behold, he is in the vineyard of Navot, where he went down to take possession of it." The word *l'rishto* — "to inherit it" — suggests that Achav acquired Navot's vineyard through a recognized mechanism of inheritance following Navot's death at the king's behest, implying that the property of one executed by the king passes to the king.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: בֶּן אֲחִי אָבִיו הָיָה, וְרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ הָיָה. וַהֲלֹא הַרְבֵּה בָּנִים הָיוּ לוֹ? אָמַר לָהֶן: אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בָּנָיו הָרַג, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם לֹא אֶת דְּמֵי נָבוֹת וְאֶת דְּמֵי בָנָיו רָאִיתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda **said to them:** Ahab **was** Naboth's cousin, the **son of his paternal uncle, and** therefore **he was fit to inherit** from **him.** Accordingly, he took possession of the property in his capacity as an heir, and not as the king. They said to him: **But Naboth had many sons.** Why, then, did they not inherit from him? Rabbi Yehuda **said to them:** Ahab **executed** Naboth **and** also **his sons, as it is stated: "I have seen** yesterday **the blood of Naboth and the blood of his sons"** (II Kings 9:26). The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis** counter this claim? The Gemara answers: In their opinion, **that** verse is referring to **the sons who would have issued from him** had Naboth not been executed. Ahab was held accountable for the blood of Naboth and for the blood of his unborn children.

Rabbi Yehuda responds: *ben achei aviv hayah v'raui l'yorsho hayah* — Ahab was Navoth's cousin, and the vineyard came to him through ordinary family inheritance, not through any royal prerogative over the property of the executed. The Gemara objects: but Navoth had many sons; why would it pass to a cousin? Rabbi Yehuda answers: *oso v'es banav harag* — Achav had Navoth and all of his sons killed, removing them from the line of succession. With the direct heirs eliminated, Achav as a collateral relative inherited the vineyard in the normal course of inheritance law. It was not royal confiscation; it was the result of a systematic and horrific elimination of all closer heirs.

### Blasphemy Charge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר נִכְסֵיהֶן לַמֶּלֶךְ, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״בֵּרַךְ נָבוֹת אֱלֹהִים וָמֶלֶךְ״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר נִכְסֵיהֶן לַיּוֹרְשִׁין, לְמָה לִי ״וָמֶלֶךְ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted, according to the one who says** that **the property of** those executed by the king belongs **to the king, that is** the reason **that it is written** that Jezebel arranged for witnesses to falsely testify that **"Naboth cursed God and the king"** (I Kings 21:13). Since Naboth cursed the king, Ahab could execute him and seize his property. **But according to the one who says** that **the property of** those executed by the king belongs **to** their **heirs, why do I** need the testimony that Naboth cursed the **king?** It would have sufficed for the witnesses to testify that he cursed God, in which case he would have been executed by the court, and Ahab would have taken possession of the vineyard as his heir.

The Gemara observes that the position holding *nikhsei l'melekh* gains particular support from the specific accusations fabricated against Navot: *beirach Navot Elohim vaMelekh* — that he blasphemed both God and the king. If the king's property claim over the executed requires that the charge be *mored b'malkhut* — rebellion against the king — this explains why the false witnesses were instructed to accuse Navot of blaspheming the king: to establish the legal predicate for royal confiscation of the vineyard.

Rabbi Yehuda, however, is confronted with a question: even on his view, if the property would have gone to Achav in any event through inheritance, why did they need to fabricate the charge of blaspheming the king? And more fundamentally, why include the charge of blaspheming God at all? This charge was entirely unnecessary for any legal purpose related to property acquisition. The Gemara answers: *l'afushei ritcha* — to inflame the judges' anger. Adding the charge of blasphemy against God was designed to make the accusation maximally outrageous and ensure that the judges — who would otherwise need to be manipulated into convicting an innocent man — would be so enraged that they would convict without scrutiny. The same reasoning applies for Rabbi Yehuda's position: even without needing the legal framework of *mored b'malkhut* for property purposes, the false charge against the king was added alongside the charge against God purely to inflame the court. The *sugyah* breaks off at this point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, אֱלֹהִים לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לְאַפּוֹשֵׁי רִיתְחָא. הָכִי נָמֵי, לְאַפּוֹשֵׁי רִיתְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **And according to your reasoning,** that the witnesses testified that Naboth cursed the king so that Ahab could execute him and seize his property, **why do I** need the additional testimony that Naboth cursed **God? Rather,** you must say that the witnesses were instructed to testify that Naboth cursed both God and the king in order **to increase the anger** of the judges by accusing him of a second offense. **So too,** it can be argued that according to Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning, the witnesses testified that Naboth also cursed the king in order **to increase the anger** of the judges. No proof can be brought from here that the property of those executed by the king belongs to the king.
