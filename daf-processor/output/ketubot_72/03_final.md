# Ketubot 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father's House Vow

### Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא תֵּלֵךְ לְבֵית אָבִיהָ, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא עִמָּהּ בָּעִיר — חוֹדֶשׁ אֶחָד יְקַיֵּים, שְׁנַיִם יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁהוּא בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת — רֶגֶל אֶחָד יְקַיֵּים, שְׁלֹשָׁה — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who vows** and obligates **his wife not to go to her father's house, when** her father **is with her in the** same **city,** if the vow is to be in effect up to **one month, he may maintain** her as his wife. If the vow is for **two** months, **he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract. And when** her father **is in a different city,** if the vow is to be in effect until at most **one pilgrim Festival,** i.e., until the next pilgrim Festival, **he may maintain** her as his wife. Although the wife often visits her parents during the Festival, she is capable of refraining one time. For **three** Festivals, however, **he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract.**

The *mishnah* on 71b introduces a case in which a man takes a vow preventing his wife from visiting her father's house. The mechanism by which he does so is worth pausing on: since a husband cannot directly obligate his wife through a vow, the operative structure is a conditional arrangement — something along the lines of "the pleasure of intimacy with you is forbidden to me if you go to your father's house." The effect is that the condition makes it impossible for her to go without triggering the vow. The scenario has an obvious domestic resonance: whether he simply does not wish to visit his in-laws, or feels that his wife has not fully acclimated to her new home, the *mishnah* treats this as a recognizable, if problematic, marital situation.

### Same City Rule

The *mishnah* distinguishes between cases based on where the wife's parents live. If the father's house is in the same city — where more frequent visits would be the natural expectation — then *chodesh echad yakayem, shnayim yotzi*: a vow lasting one month is tolerable and the marriage may continue, but beyond that he must divorce her. The question of whether the vow itself specifies "one month" or is indefinite with the *beit din* determining how long to allow it to stand is left open, but the practical threshold is clear.

### Different City Rule

When the parents live in a different city, the *mishnah* measures tolerance in *regalim* — pilgrimage festivals. *Regel echad yakayem, shelosha yotzi v'yiten ktubah*: up to one *regel* is sustainable; three consecutive *regalim* during which she cannot visit her father is unreasonable, and he must divorce her and pay her *ktubah*. This immediately raises the obvious question: what about two *regalim*?

### Internal Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא גּוּפַהּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ רֶגֶל אֶחָד יְקַיֵּים. הָא שְׁנַיִם — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. הָא שְׁנַיִם — יְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Concerning the first clause in the mishna, the Gemara asks: **This** mishna **itself is difficult: You said** on one hand that if the vow will be in effect for **one pilgrim Festival he may maintain** her as his wife, from which it may be deduced **that** if he forbade her from going to her father's house for **two** Festivals, **he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract.** But **say the latter clause:** For **three** Festivals **he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract,** from which it may be deduced **that** if the vow will be in effect for **two** Festivals, **he may maintain** her as his wife. Thus, the inferences from the first and latter clauses are contradictory.

The *gemara* presses on the internal tension: the opening clause implies that two *regalim* is the point of intolerability (since one is tolerable, *ha shnayim yotzi*), yet the closing clause implies that two is still manageable (*ha shnayim yakayem*, since three is the threshold). These cannot both be right.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְכֹהֶנֶת, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּרְדוּפָה, כָּאן בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ רְדוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In **the latter clause, we have come to** a case concerning **the wife of a priest,** with regard to whom more time is allowed before a divorce is required, since her husband may not remarry her afterward. **And** this **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who distinguished in the previous mishna between the wives of an Israelite and those of a priest. **Rabba bar Ulla said:** It is **not difficult,** and can be explained in a different way: **Here,** in the first clause, it is referring **to** a woman who is **eager** and enthusiastic to return regularly to her father's home, and if her husband prohibits her from doing so for more than one Festival it will cause her significant distress; while **there,** in the latter clause, it is referring **to** a woman **who is not eager.** Consequently, he must divorce her only if the vow will last for three Festivals.

Abaye resolves this by distinguishing between two types of wives: *birdufa* — a woman who is prone to frequent visits, who constantly wants to return to her parents' home — and *she'eina redufa*, one who visits only occasionally. For the woman who constantly runs to her parents, two *regalim* is already intolerable; for the woman who visits only occasionally, the threshold is three. The distinction carries an additional layer of social reality. Given how young these women often were when they married, one can understand both why a husband might grow frustrated with a wife who never seems settled in her new home, and why the restriction on her visits is correspondingly more oppressive for such a woman.

Abaye's colleague Rava bar Ulla offers an alternative resolution: the difference between two and three *regalim* corresponds to a distinction drawn by Rabbi Yehuda between a woman married to a *yisrael* and one married to a *kohen*. Since divorce from a *kohen* is irreversible — a *kohen* may not remarry a divorcée — the rabbis are more reluctant to compel divorce in that case and therefore extend the tolerance to three *regalim*.

### Midrash Interlude

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָז הָיִיתִי בְעֵינָיו כְּמוֹצְאֵת שָׁלוֹם״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּכַלָּה שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת שְׁלֵמָה בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ, וּרְדוּפָה לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַגִּיד שִׁבְחָהּ בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Once the Gemara has mentioned the idea of a woman who is eager to return to her father's house, it discusses another context where a similar idea is mentioned. Concerning the verse **"Then I was in his eyes as one that found peace"** (Song of Songs 8:10), **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The meaning is: **Like a bride who is considered perfect in her father-in-law's house, and is eager to go and relate her praise in her father's house,** to tell how many complimentary things were said about her by her husband's family.

At this point the *gemara* introduces a *midrashic* interlude citing the verse from *Shir HaShirim*: *az hayisi b'einav k'motzet shalom* — "then I was in his eyes like one who found favor." Rabbi Yochanan interprets this as describing a bride who is at peace in her father-in-law's household — meaning her husband's home — and yet still feels drawn to return and share her happiness with her family: *redufa leilech lehagid shivchah b'veit aviha*. This may be setting up a contrast with the previous case: not every wife who wishes to visit her parents is doing so because she has failed to settle into married life. A woman can be genuinely content with her husband and still feel a strong, legitimate pull toward her parents. The *gemara* here acknowledges that longing as real and natural.

## Ish vs. Ba'al

### Hosea Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא נְאוּם ה׳ תִּקְרְאִי אִישִׁי וְלֹא תִקְרְאִי לִי עוֹד בַּעְלִי״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּכַלָּה בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ, וְלֹא כְּכַלָּה בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, concerning the verse **"And it shall be on that day, says the Lord, that you will call Me: My Husband [*Ishi*], and you will no longer call Me: My Master [*Ba'ali*]"** (Hosea 2:18), **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The meaning is: **Like a bride in her father-in-law's house** after she has already lived with her husband, whom she is consequently not ashamed to call her marriage partner, **and not like a** betrothed **bride** still **in her father's house,** who simply refers to her groom as: My master.

From this *midrashic* passage the *gemara* transitions to a verse in Hosea: *v'haya bayom hahu ne'um Hashem tikre'i ishi v'lo tikre'i li od ba'ali* — "On that day, says God, you will call Me 'my man' and you will no longer call Me 'my master.'" The plain meaning of the verse's conclusion, *vahasiroti et shem hab'alim mipihah* — "I will remove the name of the *ba'alim* from your mouth" — refers to the elimination of idolatrous associations, since the *ba'alim* were Canaanite deities. The prophetic vision is of a purified worship of God, free from any echo of idolatry.

### Ba'al Mastery

But the verse also says something about the nature of marriage. The word *ba'al* carries a double valence: it means master, and it is also the term for sexual possession — *bo'alaich osaich*, "He who possesses you made you." The relationship structured around *ba'al* is one of male dominance in which even sexuality can be an expression of mastery. *Ish* and *ishah*, by contrast, suggest equality — man and woman — in the same register.

### Feminist Reading

The verse's call to say *ishi* rather than *ba'ali* speaks directly to how one conceptualizes the marital relationship. Contemporary readers attuned to questions of gender equality find in this verse a powerful expression of partnership — *ish* and *ishah* standing on equal footing rather than the asymmetry of master and wife. The contrast is not merely linguistic but structural: saying "my man" rather than "my master" reflects an entirely different understanding of what marriage is.

### Rashi on Union

Rabbi Yochanan articulates the verse's application as follows: *kekallah b'veit chamia v'lo kekallah b'veit aviha* — like a bride who is at home in her husband's household, not one who is perpetually drawn back to her father's house. The connection to *ishi* rather than *ba'ali* is not entirely transparent, but Rashi clarifies: the language of *ishut* and *nisu'in* evokes the model in *Bereishit* of *al kein ya'azov ish et aviv v'et imo v'davak b'ishto* — a man leaves his father and mother and cleaves to his wife, the two becoming one unit. *Ba'al* may connote mastery and sexual possession, but it does not capture that idea of genuine union. Rashi adds: the bride who has already married feels at ease with her husband and is no longer embarrassed before him. That ease, that sense of being genuinely at home, is what *ish* and *ishah* point toward.

## Weddings & Funerals

### Mishnah Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא תֵּלֵךְ לְבֵית הָאֵבֶל אוֹ לְבֵית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנּוֹעֵל בְּפָנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, **one who vows** and obligates **his wife not to go to a house of mourning** to console the mourners, **or to a house of feasting** for a wedding, **must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract.** Why is this so? **Because** it is as if he were **locking** a door **in front of her.**

The *mishnah* continues: *hamadir ishto shelo telech l'veit ha'evel o l'veit hamishte* — a man who vows to prevent his wife from attending a house of mourning or a house of festivity, which is understood to mean a wedding. In either case, *yotzi v'yiten ktubah* — he must divorce her and pay her *ktubah*. No grace period is specified, presumably because the occasion of a funeral is unpredictable: one never knows when it will arise, and the vow is therefore immediately intolerable. The *mishnah* provides the reason: *mipnei sheno'el bifaneha* — he is locking the door in her face, cutting her off from normal social and communal life.

### Locking Door Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא נוֹעֵל בְּפָנֶיהָ, אֶלָּא לְבֵית הָאֵבֶל מַאי נוֹעֵל בְּפָנֶיהָ אִיכָּא? תָּנָא: לְמָחָר הִיא מֵתָה וְאֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה סוֹפְדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **there is** effectively an act of **locking** a door **in front of her** by withholding from her any possibility of rejoicing, **but** when he forbids her from going **to a house of mourning, what locking** of a door **in front of her is there? He taught: In the future she** too **will die, and no person will eulogize her** or take care of her, just as she did not do so for others.

The *gemara* accepts this reasoning without difficulty regarding the wedding: clearly, preventing a woman from attending celebrations closes off a dimension of normal social pleasure. But the *gemara* presses on the case of the house of mourning: *mai ne'ul bifaneha ika* — what "locking of the door" is involved in preventing her from attending a *beit ha'evel*? Going to a house of mourning is not a pleasure; in what sense is she being deprived?

### Reciprocal Obligation

A *braita* responds: *lemachar hi meita v'ein kol beryah sofedatah* — tomorrow she will die and no one will eulogize her. There is a reciprocal social fabric involved in attending funerals. The woman who is prevented from being present for others will find, when her own time comes, that no one comes for her. And some say the consequence is even starker: not merely that no one will eulogize her, but that no one will want to bury her. The harm, in other words, is not only spiritual but concretely social: it damages her standing in the community and severs the bonds of mutual obligation on which communal life depends.

### Rabbi Meir Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״טוֹב לָלֶכֶת אֶל בֵּית אֵבֶל מִלֶּכֶת אֶל בֵּית מִשְׁתֶּה בַּאֲשֶׁר הוּא סוֹף כׇּל הָאָדָם וְהַחַי יִתֵּן אֶל לִבּוֹ״, מַאי ״וְהַחַי יִתֵּן אֶל לִבּוֹ״? דְּבָרִים שֶׁל מִיתָה: דְּ[יִ]סְפֹּד — יִסְפְּדוּנֵיהּ, דְּ[יִ]קְבַּר — יִקְבְּרוּנֵיהּ, דִּידַל — יְדַלּוּנֵיהּ, דִּ[י]לַוֵּאי — יְלַוּוֹנֵיהּ, דְּ[יִ]טְעֹן — יִטְעֲנוּנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir used to say: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "It is better to go to a house of mourning than to go to a house of feasting, since that is the end of all men, and the living will take it to heart"** (Ecclesiastes 7:2)? **What** does **"and the living will take it to heart"** mean? It means that they will take **matters** relating **to death** to heart, realizing that they too will eventually die. He **who eulogizes** others, people **will eulogize him;** he **who buries** someone, people **will bury him;** he **who lifts** others to bring them to burial, people **will** similarly **lift** him to bring him to burial; he **who escorts** others out for burial, people **will** similarly **escort him;** he **who carries** others, others **will carry him.**

A *braita* then cites Rabbi Meir interpreting the verse in *Kohelet*: *tov lalechet el beit ha'evel milalechet el beit hamishte ba'asher hu sof kol ha'adam v'hachai yitein el libo* — "Better to go to the house of mourning than to the house of rejoicing, for that is the end of all people, and the living should take it to heart." What should one take to heart while at the house of mourning?

### Funeral Consequences

Rabbi Meir spells out the reciprocal calculus in stark terms: *desapad yesafdunihu* — one who eulogizes will be eulogized; *dekavar yikvrunihu* — one who buries will be buried; *de'adli yadlunihu* — one who wails will be wailed over; *delavay yalvunuhu* — one who accompanies a coffin will be accompanied; *detaan yiteanunihu* — one who serves as a pallbearer will have pallbearers at his own burial. The deeper meaning of *v'hachai yitein el libo* is not merely memento mori — though certainly the verse means that too — but an instruction about the obligations of communal solidarity. Attending to the dead is not sentiment; it is the fabric of mutual responsibility without which no community can function.

## Legitimate Exception

### Exception Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה טוֹעֵן מִשּׁוּם דָּבָר אַחֵר — רַשַּׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he claimed** he forbade her **due to something else,** meaning he is concerned about inappropriate conduct there, **he is permitted** to do so.

The *mishnah* includes an important qualification: *v'im haya toen mishum davar acher rashay* — if he has another legitimate reason for the vow, it is permissible. This is a striking move: *chazal* are not simply adjudicating tolerability but making a more nuanced judgment about whether the harm to the wife is justified by competing considerations. The implication is that sometimes restrictions that would otherwise be intolerable can become acceptable when there is genuine cause.

### Promiscuity Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מִשּׁוּם בְּנֵי אָדָם פְּרוּצִין שֶׁמְּצוּיִין שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** meant by **something else? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** He claims he did so **due to promiscuous individuals that are** commonly **found there,** and he does not want his wife to be among them.

Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel identifies what counts as a legitimate other reason: *mishum benei adam perutzim she-metzuyim sham* — because there are people of promiscuous or morally loose character present at those gatherings. The husband's concern is not that he suspects his wife of anything specific, but that he objects to the environment and the company to which she would be exposed. Even granting that concern, the question remains: how much weight does his claim carry?

### Rav Ashi Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּאִיתַּחְזַק, אֲבָל לָא אִיתַּחְזַק — לֹא כָּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We said** that he may forbid her **only** with regard to a case **where a presumption has been** established that promiscuous people frequent this location, **but** if **no** such **presumption has been** established, **it is not in his power** to say he is concerned about it.

Rav Ashi sets a threshold: *lo amran ela de'itchazek* — this is only valid if the presence of such people at those gatherings has been objectively established. *Aval lo itchazek lo kol kemineih* — if it has not been established, his claim carries no weight. He cannot simply assert that the crowd is morally compromised; there must be some external, objective basis for that claim. This standard is important precisely because it prevents a jealous or controlling husband from manufacturing a pretext to isolate his wife. The distinction between tolerability and justifiability is thus not simply a matter of his subjective feeling, but is subject to some degree of communal verification.

## Silence & Absurd Vows

### Secrets Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתֹּאמְרִי לִפְלוֹנִי מַה שֶּׁאָמַרְתָּ לִי״, אוֹ: ״מַה שֶּׁאָמַרְתִּי לָךְ״, אוֹ שֶׁתְּהֵא מְמַלְּאָה וּמְעָרָה לָאַשְׁפָּה — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he said to her:** The vow will be void **on condition that you tell** so-and-so what you told me, or what I told you, or** on condition that **she fill** something up **and pour** it **into the refuse, he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract.**

The *mishnah* lists two further cases requiring divorce with *ktubah*. The first: *al menat shetomri l'ploni mah she'amart li o mah she'amarti lach* — a vow conditioned on her revealing to a third party either what she said to her husband or what he told her in private. Whatever she said to him privately, she must now disclose to someone else, or else remain bound by the vow's consequences. This too requires divorce.

### Shame Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתֵימָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דְּבָרִים שֶׁל קָלוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let her say it.** Why shouldn't she simply comply with his wishes? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** It is referring to **degrading matters,** meaning intimate conversations between husband and wife, which she is ashamed to relate in the presence of others.

Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel explains: *devarim shel kalon* — these are shameful matters, things that ought not to be disclosed. At minimum, the disclosure brings shame upon the one who originally spoke those words. Private marital communication has an inherent dignity that is violated when one spouse is compelled to broadcast it.

### Meaningless Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ שֶׁתְּהֵא מְמַלְּאָה וּמְעָרָה לְאַשְׁפָּה. וְתִיעְבֵּיד! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שֶׁתְּמַלֵּא וְנוֹפֶצֶת. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תַּנָּא: שֶׁתְּמַלֵּא עֲשָׂרָה כַּדֵּי מַיִם וּתְעָרֶה לְאַשְׁפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated: **Or** he said the vow will be void on condition **that she fill** something up **and pour** it **into the refuse.** The Gemara asks: **And let her do it. Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The mishna's intention is **that** he demanded that **she fill** herself up **and** then **shake** herself out. This is a euphemistic way of saying that the husband wants her to take measures to prevent herself from becoming pregnant, and she is permitted to protest this. **It was taught in a *baraita*:** The case is **that** he told her **to fill** up **ten jugs of water and pour** them **into the refuse,** a task that involves pointless effort and appears foolish.

The second case: *o shetehei memale'ah um'arah l'ashpah* — or that she should fill things and spill them into the *ashpah*, the dunghill. This too requires *yotzi v'yiten ktubah*. But what does it mean? The plain reading suggests some absurd, repetitive labor. However, the *gemara*'s understanding is more specific.

### Shmuel Interpretation

Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel explains: *shetimaleh v'nofetzet* — the filling and spilling refers to intercourse: the husband ejaculates, her body is "filled," and she then washes out the semen, preventing pregnancy. This is the *gemara*'s primary reading: the husband is compelling his wife to ensure she cannot conceive. Even if a woman is not technically obligated in the commandment of *pru u'rvu*, she possesses the right to have children. Forcing her to prevent her own pregnancy — especially if she desires children — is an intolerable invasion. The phrase *memale'ah um'arah* is thus understood as a euphemistic description of this practice.

A *beraita*, however, presents a different version: *shetimaleh asarah kadei mayim v'saara l'ashpah* — fill ten jugs of water and pour them out into the dunghill. Pure, pointless labor. No preventative function, just meaningless repetition.

### Foolish Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. אֶלָּא לְמַתְנִיתָא מַאי נָפְקָא לַהּ מִינַּהּ? תִּיעְבֵּיד! אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאִיתָ כְּשׁוֹטֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted** that **according to Shmuel,** who explains that the mishna is referring to a case where the husband insists that she not become pregnant, **due to that** reason **he must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract. But according to the *baraita*,** which explains that he simply wants her to engage in pointless work, **what difference does it make to her? Let her do it. Rabba bar bar Ḥanna said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Because she** would **appear insane** if she were to perform pointless actions, she may therefore demand a divorce.

Against this second reading, the *gemara* asks: *v'sa'avid* — so let her do it. What is so catastrophic about having to perform useless work? The first reading is clearly worse because of its impact on the possibility of pregnancy. But the second — filling and spilling water — seems merely tedious. Rabbah bar bar Chanah citing Rabbi Yochanan answers: *mipnei shenira'it k'shotah* — because it makes her appear to be a crazy woman. The public dimension matters: she is made to look foolish in the eyes of the community, and this is sufficient grounds for divorce.

### Domestic Control

It is worth noting what the *gemara* does not quite say but what the phenomenon clearly implies. The pattern of compelling a wife to perform absurd, meaningless tasks — completely at the husband's whim, simply because he demands it — is a recognizable structure of domestic control. Those familiar with patterns of marital abuse will recognize that hyper-controlling behavior of this kind — making a spouse perform arbitrary, demeaning acts — is not merely a question of appearances. It reflects a dynamic of domination that often precedes or accompanies other forms of abuse. The *gemara*'s reasoning focuses on the public aspect, *nira'it k'shotah*, but the underlying reality it addresses is something more troubling.

## Borrowing & Lending

### Vessel Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁאַל וְשֶׁלֹּא תַּשְׁאִיל נָפָה וּכְבָרָה וְרֵיחַיִם וְתַנּוּר — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאָהּ שֵׁם רַע בִּשְׁכֵינוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: One who vows** and obligates **his wife not to borrow or not to lend** utensils that people generally lend, such as **a sifter, or a sieve, or a mill, or an oven, must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract, since** by making such rules **he causes her to develop a bad reputation among her neighbors,** who will suspect her of stinginess or haughtiness.

Rav Kahana introduces a further case: *hamadir ishto shelo tishal v'shelo tash'il* — a man who makes a vow (or, as *Tosafot* notes, perhaps here a direct vow may function) preventing his wife from borrowing certain vessels or from lending them out. The vessels specified are a *nafa* (sieve), a *kivra* (a different type of sieve), a *reikayim* (millstone), and a *tanur* (oven) — all portable household implements of the kind regularly exchanged between neighbors. This too requires *yotzi v'yiten ktubah*.

### Bad Reputation

The reason: *mishum she-mashi shem ra bishcheinosha* — because it gives her a bad reputation among her neighbors. A woman who never lends the cup of flour, who never lets anyone use her sieve, is seen as unfriendly, selfish, closed off from the normal give-and-take of communal life. This social standing matters, and the husband who creates that situation through his vow is the one at fault.

### Wife's Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַמַּדִּיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁאַל וְשֶׁלֹּא תַּשְׁאִיל נָפָה וּכְבָרָה רֵיחַיִם וְתַנּוּר — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאָהּ שֵׁם רַע בִּשְׁכֵינוֹתֶיהָ. וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁנָּדְרָה שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁאַל וְשֶׁלֹּא תַּשְׁאִיל נָפָה וּכְבָרָה וְרֵיחַיִם וְתַנּוּר, וְשֶׁלֹּא תֶּאֱרוֹג בְּגָדִים נָאִים לְבָנָיו — תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאָתוֹ שֵׁם רַע בִּשְׁכֵינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who vows** and obligates **his wife not to borrow or not to lend a sifter, or a sieve, or a mill, or an oven, must divorce** her **and give** her the payment of her **marriage contract, since he causes her to develop a bad reputation among her neighbors. And similarly,** if it is **she who vowed not to borrow or not to lend a sifter, or a sieve, or a mill, or an oven, or that she will not weave nice garments for his children, she** may be **divorced without** payment of her **marriage contract.** This too is **because she causes him to develop a bad reputation among his neighbors,** as they will link her behavior to him and think that he instructed her to act this way.

A *braita* teaches the same principle in the mirror case: *hamadir ishto shelo tash'il v'shelo tishal* — even preventing her from borrowing creates a bad reputation. One might think that never asking neighbors for anything is neutral or even virtuous — not imposing on others. But the *braita* rejects this: borrowing is itself part of community life. The woman who never asks her neighbors for anything is perceived as distant, and that too damages her standing.

### Clothing Vow

The *braita* extends this further: *v'shelo ta'arog begadim na'im livaneha* — if the wife herself vowed not to weave fine clothes for his children from a previous marriage. Weaving fine clothes for stepchildren is not among the wife's formal legal obligations. Yet the *braita* rules *teitzei shelo b'ketuva* — she leaves without her *ktubah*, because *mipnei she-mashi'aso shem ra bishcheinav* — she has given him a bad reputation among his neighbors. People ask: why don't your children have nice new clothes for *Yom Tov*? How comes your wife won't weave for them? His social standing suffers because of what his wife is — or is not — doing.

### Shared Standing

The broader principle is clear: community standing is not simply an individual matter. A husband's reputation and a wife's reputation are intertwined through their shared social relations. This is captured well in the vision of *eishet chayil* — *noda bashe'arim ba'alah*, her husband is known at the gates — where the wife's behavior reflects on and shapes the husband's standing in the community. Reputation, the ability to borrow and lend, the quality of one's participation in the routines of communal exchange: all of these are part of what makes a marriage viable or not. And it is worth noting in this context that the parallel from *darchei shalom* applies here as well: the same *braita* that records that we give *tzedakah* to the poor of non-Jews also records that we collect *tzedakah* from non-Jews. The reason: *mipnei darchei shalom*. The relationship of giving and receiving is itself constitutive of community. A woman — or man — who is cut off from that reciprocity is cut off from something essential.

## Transgressions Moshe

### Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ יוֹצְאוֹת שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה — הָעוֹבֶרֶת עַל דָּת מֹשֶׁה וִיהוּדִית. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא דָּת מֹשֶׁה? מַאֲכִילָתוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעוּשָּׂר, וּמְשַׁמַּשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה, וְלֹא קוֹצָה לָהּ חַלָּה, וְנוֹדֶרֶת וְאֵינָהּ מְקַיֶּימֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these** are examples of women who may be **divorced without** payment of their **marriage contract:** A woman **who violates** the **precepts of Moses,** i.e., *halakha*, **or** the precepts of **Jewish** women, i.e., custom. The Mishna explains: **And who is** categorized as a woman who violates the **precepts of Moses?** This includes cases such as when **she feeds him** food **that has not been tithed, or she engages in sexual intercourse with him** while she has the legal status of **a menstruating woman, or she does not separate a portion of dough to be given to a priest [*ḥalla*], or she vows and does not fulfill** her vows.

The *mishnah* now pivots significantly. Until this point, virtually all the cases we have examined involved the husband as the source of the problem — it was *yotzi v'yiten ktubah*, he must divorce her and pay her *ktubah*, he was at fault. Here the *mishnah* shifts: *v'eilu yotzot shelo b'ktubah* — these are the women who leave without a *ktubah*. The emphasis falls on her behavior. She is transgressing — and because she is at fault, she forfeits her *ktubah*. That said, one should note that *yotzot* itself raises the same interpretive question we have seen throughout: does the *beit din* compel the divorce, or does it merely determine that if a divorce happens, she is not entitled to *ktubah*?

The categories of transgression are: *da'overet al dat Moshe v'Yehudit* — she is transgressing the Law of Moshe or the practices of Jewish women. "Law of Moshe" refers to biblical law; "Jewish women" refers to accepted norms of conduct among Jewish women. Importantly, the *mishnah* is not saying that any transgression — smoking on *Shabbat*, say, or some sin that is entirely between her and God — constitutes grounds for divorce without *ktubah*. The *mishnah* is quite specific, and the *gemara* will clarify why: what is at issue is transgression that directly impacts the husband and makes the marriage untenable.

### Food Without Tithes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאֲכִילָתוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעוּשָּׂר, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּיָדַע — נִפְרוֹשׁ. אִי דְּלָא יָדַע — מְנָא יָדַע? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ ״פְּלוֹנִי כֹּהֵן תִּיקֵּן לִי אֶת הַכְּרִי״, וְאָזֵיל שַׁיְילֵיהּ, וְאִשְׁתְּכַח שִׁיקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna stated: **She feeds him** food **that has not been tithed.** The Gemara attempts to clarify: **What are the circumstances** of the case under discussion? **If he knows** that the food is untithed, **he should abstain** and not eat it. And **if he does not know** that the food is untithed, then **how does he know** that she in fact fed him such food, so that he can divorce her? The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary when she tells him: So-and-so** the **priest rectified the pile** of grain **for me** by tithing it, **and he** then **went and asked** the priest whether he did so, **and it was found** to be **a lie.** It is therefore clear that she did not tithe the food before she served it to him.

The first transgression under *dat Moshe*: *ma'achilato she'eino me'ushur* — she feeds him food that has not had *terumot* and *ma'aserot* properly separated from it, rendering it *tevel*, which is prohibited for consumption. This directly causes him to transgress, not merely a minor prohibition but a serious one — indeed, the class of transgressions listed here all carry severe consequences, many of them *karet*.

It is worth pausing on why the *mishnah* focuses on *tevel* rather than, say, feeding him *chazir*. At one level this reflects practical reality: feeding one's husband obviously non-kosher food was presumably rare and easily prevented, whereas the question of whether *terumot* and *ma'aserot* had been taken — especially from produce that passed through the household in various stages — was a live and common concern in the *mishnaic* period. The category of *am ha'aretz* — the ordinary Jew who was not scrupulous about *ma'aserot* — was a real social phenomenon. Moreover, the *mishnah* places this under *dat Moshe*, which raises a question noted by the students: much of this could be rabbinic obligation depending on the period. Yet the *mishnah* treats it as *dat Moshe*.

### Knowledge Problem

The *gemara* presses immediately: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? *Ei yada, nifrosh* — if he knows she is feeding him *tevel*, then don't eat it. The situation seems entirely within his control. *Ei lo yada, mena yada* — and if he doesn't know, how does he ever find out? If he trusts her, he trusts her, and if the transgression never surfaces, how does it become grounds for divorce?

### Indirect Evidence

The *gemara* answers: *lo tzricha d'amra leih ploni Kohen tiken li et hakari* — the case is one where she told him that such-and-such a *Kohen* came and helped her separate *terumot* and *ma'aserot* from the pile of produce. *V'azal v'shaleih v'ishtakach shikra* — he went and asked, and it emerged that she had been lying. He trusted her — that is the foundation of the relationship — and evidence surfaced through a third party that her assurance was false. The transgression was committed and the lie compounded it.

### Niddah Relations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁמַּשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּיָדַע בָּהּ — נִפְרוֹשׁ, אִי דְּלָא יָדַע — נִסְמוֹךְ עִילָּוַהּ. דְּאָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר כָּהֲנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מִנַּיִן לְנִדָּה שֶׁסּוֹפֶרֶת לְעַצְמָהּ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְסָפְרָה לָּהּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״, ״לָהּ״ — לְעַצְמָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **Or she engages in sexual intercourse with him** while she has the status of **a menstruating woman.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If he knows about her** that she is a menstruating woman, **he should abstain.** And **if he does not know,** then **he should rely on her. Because Rav Ḥinnana bar Kahana said** that **Shmuel said: From where** is it derived **that a menstruating woman can count** the days **for herself,** and that she is trusted to testify that she did so? **As it is stated: "Then she shall count to herself seven days"** (Leviticus 15:28). **"To herself"** means **by herself,** and she may be trusted that she did so. If so, why can't the husband trust his wife that she is not a menstruating woman?

The second transgression: *u'meshamshashu niddah* — she has sexual relations with him while she is a *niddah*. Again the *gemara* asks: if he knows she is a *niddah*, he should simply refrain. If he doesn't know, how does it emerge?

### Reliance Counting

The *gemara* begins from an established principle: Rav Chinana bar Kahana states that a man may rely on his wife in these matters. Shmuel derives this from the verse *v'safra lah shivat yamim* — she counts for herself — meaning the *niddah* herself determines her count and her own purity status. The husband trusts her completely; this is the normative expectation. How, then, could the husband ever come to know that she was lying?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: פְּלוֹנִי חָכָם טִיהֵר לִי אֶת הַדָּם, וַאֲזַל שַׁיְילֵיהּ, וְאִשְׁתְּכַח שִׁיקְרָא. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הוּחְזְקָה נִדָּה בִּשְׁכֵינוֹתֶיהָ — בַּעְלָהּ לוֹקֶה עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary when she tells him: So-and-so** the **Sage purified the blood for me** by ruling that it did not qualify as menstrual blood, **and he went and asked him, and it was found** that her claim was **a lie. And if you wish, say** instead that this is **similar to that which Rav Yehuda** said, **as Rav Yehuda stated:** If **she is known by her neighbors to be a menstruating woman, her husband is flogged if** he has relations with **her, due to** the prohibition against cohabiting with **a menstruating woman.** In this case, she was known by her neighbors to be a menstruating woman, but she had not told her husband. She then engaged in sexual intercourse with him, and he subsequently discovered her status from her neighbors.

The case is one where she told him she had asked a *chacham* about a blood stain and he had ruled it permissible — *ploni chacham tihar li et hadam*. The husband then went and asked that same *chacham*, and discovered that she had never brought such a question. The lie surfaced through a third party, exactly as in the *tevel* case. A variant offered by the *gemara*: the case of *huchzeka niddah bishcheinosha* — her *niddah* status had been established through her neighbors. In the absence of modern conveniences, a woman who was *niddah* would wear distinctive clothing to protect her regular garments from soiling. If neighbors observed her wearing *niddah* clothing, that was sufficient indirect evidence of her status, such that if she then had relations with her husband — inferred from the pattern of those clothes being worn before and after — the husband could even receive lashes on the basis of that indirect evidence. The broader point is that external evidence can establish what the husband, relying on her word, did not know.

This *gemara* occasions a notable *Tosafot* excursus. The *tosafot* observes that the counting described here is technically associated not with the *niddah* but with the *zavah*, who counts seven clean days. Since contemporary women treat themselves as both *niddah* and *zavah* and count seven clean days accordingly, the *tosafot* asks: why do we not recite a *bracha* on this counting, just as we recite a *bracha* on *sefiras ha'omer*? The answer offered is that a *bracha* presupposes a stable, completable act of counting. *Sefiras ha'omer* runs for forty-nine days without interruption. The *zavah*'s counting, by contrast, is liable to restart at any point if bleeding resumes during those seven days — meaning any individual day's count may turn out to have been invalidated. This makes it structurally different from the *omer*, which proceeds to completion. Most *poskim* resolve the *tosafot*'s question differently, reasoning that the seven-day count is not a formal act of counting at all but simply a process of noting the passage of time; it is not a *mitzvah* in the same sense as *sefiras ha'omer* and therefore does not carry a *bracha*.

### Challah Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא קוֹצָה לָהּ חַלָּה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּיָדַע — נִפְרוֹשׁ, אִי דְּלָא יָדַע — מְנָא יָדַע? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: פְּלוֹנִי גַּבָּל תִּיקֵּן לִי אֶת הָעִיסָּה, וְאָזֵיל שַׁיְילֵיהּ, וְאִשְׁתְּכַח שִׁיקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **Or she does not separate *ḥalla*.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If he knows** that she did not separate *ḥalla*, **he should abstain. If he does not know,** then **how does he know** about it afterward in order to divorce her? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary when she tells him: So-and-so** the **kneader rectified the dough for me** by separating *ḥalla*, **and he went and asked him, and it was found** that her claim was **a lie.**

The third transgression under *dat Moshe*: *v'lo kotza la challah* — she fails to separate *challah* from the dough. The *gemara* applies the same analysis: if he knew, he should not eat it; if he did not know, the case is one where she told him she had brought the dough to a kneader — *ploni gaval tiken li et ha'isa* — who had separated *challah* for her. He went and asked, and found she was lying.

### Unfulfilled Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹדֶרֶת וְאֵינָהּ מְקַיֶּימֶת. דְּאָמַר מָר: בַּעֲוֹן נְדָרִים בָּנִים מֵתִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל תִּתֵּן אֶת פִּיךָ לַחֲטִיא אֶת בְּשָׂרֶךָ וגו״. וְאֵיזוֹ הֵן מַעֲשֵׂה יָדָיו שֶׁל אָדָם — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר בָּנָיו וּבְנוֹתָיו. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״לַשָּׁוְא הִכֵּיתִי אֶת בְּנֵיכֶם״, ״לַשָּׁוְא״ — עַל עִסְקֵי שָׁוְא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna also stated: **Or she vows and does not fulfill** her vows. The Gemara clarifies the reason for this, as it is different from the other cases in the mishna, where she causes her husband to violate a prohibition. In this case it is only she who violates a prohibition. **As the Master said: Due to** the sin of unfulfilled **vows, children die, as it is stated:** "It is better not to vow than to vow and not pay. **Do not allow your mouth to bring your flesh to sin…why should the Lord become angry at your voice and destroy the work of your hands?" (Ecclesiastes 5:4–5). **And what is the work of a person's hands? You must say** it is referring to **his sons and his daughters. Rav Naḥman said:** A proof to the above idea may be brought **from here: "In vain I smote your children"** (Jeremiah 2:30). The phrase **"in vain"** means: **For matters** caused by **vain** words, meaning that you took a vow and did not fulfill it.

The fourth transgression: *v'noderet v'eina mekayemet* — she makes vows and does not fulfill them. This one is less immediately obvious in its connection to the marriage. The first three transgressions directly cause the husband to eat forbidden food or engage in forbidden relations. What does her failure to fulfill her own vows have to do with him?

### Children's Death

The *gemara* explains via a teaching of the Sages: *ba'avon nedarim banim metim* — for the sin of unfulfilled vows, children die. The source is the verse *al titein et picha lachati et b'sarecha* — do not let your mouth cause your flesh to sin — with the end of the verse: *v'chibbel et ma'asei yadecha*. The "work of your hands" is understood as *banav u'vnotav* — one's sons and daughters. Rav Nachman adds a parallel from Jeremiah: *lashav hikeiti et b'neichem* — for nothing have I smitten your children — where *lashav* is understood as relating to *shav*, unfulfilled vows. Whatever one's theological response to the idea that children bear consequences for their parents' failures, this is clearly the understanding that drives the halachic concern. The transgression affects not only the woman but the household, and the children are at risk. That is why unfulfilled vows are placed alongside the other three transgressions: in each case, the wife's conduct creates a consequence that falls upon the husband and the family.

### Meir Provocation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַיּוֹדֵעַ בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁנּוֹדֶרֶת וְאֵינָהּ מְקַיֶּימֶת — יַחְזוֹר וְיַדִּירֶנָּה. יַדִּירֶנָּה?! בְּמַאי מְתַקֵּן לַהּ? אֶלָּא: יַחְזוֹר וְיַקְנִיטֶנָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּדּוֹר בְּפָנָיו וְיָפֵר לָהּ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין אָדָם דָּר עִם נָחָשׁ בִּכְפִיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir used to say: Anyone who knows concerning his wife that she vows and does not fulfill** her vows **should return and vow** to obligate **her.** The Gemara wonders: **He should vow** and obligate **her? How will he rectify** it **for her** by doing this? **Rather,** the intention is **he should return and provoke her, so that she will vow in his presence and he can** then **nullify** it **for her. They said to him:** This solution is not effective, because **a person does not reside in a basket [*kefifa*],** i.e., in close quarters, **with a snake,** since this is extremely dangerous. Similarly, he cannot constantly prevent her from taking vows, so it would be preferable that he divorce her.

A *braita* cites Rabbi Meir: *kol hayodeia b'ishto shenoderet v'eina mekayemet* — a man who knows his wife takes vows and does not fulfill them. Rabbi Meir's advice: get her to take vows in your presence and then annul them. The *gemara* asks — *yadirna b'mai matkein lah* — how would making a vow on her help? The answer is: *ela yachzir v'yaknitena* — provoke her, make her angry, so that she will take a vow in front of him, and then he can be *mefer* it, annul it. Several problems with this approach are immediately apparent. Even if he manages to provoke her into taking a vow in his presence, what about the vows she takes when he is not there? Moreover, even if he is *mefer* at the end of the day, what happens to the period between when the vow was taken and when he annulled it — could she have transgressed in the interim? And whether the husband must actually hear the vow in order to annul it is itself a question.

### Snake Basket

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַיּוֹדֵעַ בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹצֶה לוֹ חַלָּה — יַחְזוֹר וְיַפְרִישׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין אָדָם דָּר עִם נָחָשׁ בִּכְפִיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* similar to the previous one that **Rabbi Yehuda used to say: Anyone who knows concerning his wife that she does not separate *ḥalla* for him should go back and separate** it **after she** is finished. **They said to him:** This solution is not effective, since **a person does not reside in a basket with a snake.**

The response to Rabbi Meir's suggestion — and apparently also to the parallel suggestion about *challah* — is decisive: *ein adam dar im nachash b'chfifah* — a person cannot be expected to live with a snake in a basket. The point is not technical; it is existential. Even if there is a mechanism by which the problem can theoretically be managed — don't eat it, provoke her to take vows in front of you — it is not a viable response. The constant vigilance required, the ongoing need to be on guard against one's own spouse, is itself the problem. Rabbi Yehuda similarly says regarding *challah*: go back and separate it yourself. The answer is the same: *ein adam dar im nachash b'chfifah*.

The image here is powerful, and its applications extend beyond this *gemara*. The *Rambam* famously invokes this very principle — *ein adam dar im nachash b'chfifah* — in the context of a wife who declares that she finds her husband physically repugnant and cannot bear intimacy with him. Even though she is the one making the claim, and even though she therefore forfeits her *ktubah*, the *Rambam* holds that the husband must be compelled to give a *get* — because she too cannot be forced to live with a snake. One cannot require a person to cohabit with someone she finds impossible to be with.

## Transgressions Jewish

### Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹהִי דָּת יְהוּדִית? יוֹצְאָה וְרֹאשָׁהּ פָּרוּעַ, וְטוֹוֶה בְּשׁוּק, וּמְדַבֶּרֶת עִם כָּל אָדָם. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְקַלֶּלֶת יוֹלְדָיו בְּפָנָיו. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף הַקּוֹלָנִית. וְאֵיזוֹהִי קוֹלָנִית? לִכְשֶׁהִיא מְדַבֶּרֶת בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ וּשְׁכֵינֶיהָ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And who is** considered a woman who violates the **precepts of Jewish** women? One who, for example, **goes out** of her house, **and her head,** i.e., her hair, is **uncovered; or** she **spins** wool **in the** public **marketplace; or** she **speaks with every man** she encounters. **Abba Shaul says: Also one who curses his,** i.e., her husband's, **parents in his presence. Rabbi Tarfon says: Also a loud woman. And who is** defined as **a loud woman? When she speaks inside her house and her neighbors hear her voice.**

The *mishnah* now enumerates the transgressions that fall under *dat Yehudit* — the practices of Jewish women — as opposed to *dat Moshe*, biblical law. Four examples are given. Before the *gemara* addresses each one, it is worth noting what the category of *dat Yehudit* represents. These are not biblical prohibitions; they are norms of conduct observed among Jewish women. The concern, as will become apparent, is less about her sinning per se and more about the social and relational signals that certain behaviors send — particularly signals that could attract the attention of other men or compromise the exclusivity and dignity of the marriage.

### Uncovered Head

The first example of *dat Yehudit*: *yotzah v'roshah parua* — going out in public with her head uncovered. The term *parua* connotes more than simply bare; it suggests disheveled, loose, free-flowing. This is the behavior that the *mishnah* categorizes as a violation of Jewish women's practice, making her liable to lose her *ktubah* in divorce.

### Sotah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֹאשָׁהּ פָּרוּעַ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּפָרַע אֶת רֹאשׁ הָאִשָּׁה״, וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אַזְהָרָה לִבְנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא יֵצְאוּ בִּפְרוּעַ רֹאשׁ! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא —
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **And who is** considered a woman who violates the **precepts of Jewish** women? One who **goes out and her head is uncovered.** The Gemara asks: The prohibition against a woman going out with **her head uncovered** is not merely a custom of Jewish women. Rather, **it is by Torah** law, **as it is written** with regard to a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful: **"And he shall uncover the head of the woman"** (Numbers 5:18). And **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** From here there is a **warning to Jewish women not to go out with an uncovered head,** since if the Torah states that a woman suspected of adultery must have her head uncovered, this indicates that a married woman must generally cover her head. The Gemara explains: **By Torah** law,

The *gemara* immediately presses: if *roshah parua* is *dat Yehudit* — a norm, not a biblical law — why is it sourced in the *Torah*? The verse cited is *u'phara rosh ha'isha* from the *sotah* passage in *Bamidbar*, where the *kohen* uncovers the suspected adulteress's head. The school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from here *azhara livnot Yisrael shelo yetzu b'farua rosh d'Oraita* — a prohibition for Jewish women against going out with an uncovered head, which is of biblical force.

### Norm vs. Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* קַלְתָּהּ שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, דָּת יְהוּדִית — אֲפִילּוּ קַלְתָּהּ נָמֵי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** if she covers her head with **her basket** [***kilta***], **it seems well** and is sufficient. But by **precepts of Jewish** women, i.e., custom, **even** if her head is covered by **her basket** this **is also prohibited;** she requires a substantial head covering.

Rashi, however, reads this differently. Rather than deriving a prohibition from the *sotah* ritual, Rashi understands the inference as follows: *mi-klal de-chtiv u'phara, mi-klal de-hahi sha'ata lav perua'h hi* — the fact that the *kohen* uncovers her head implies that, at that moment, it was covered. From this we can infer the norm: *ein derech b'not Yisrael latzeit b'farua rosh* — it was simply not the practice of Jewish women to go out with their heads uncovered. This is not a prohibition; it is a recorded norm. Rashi adds *v'chein ikar* — and this is the more accurate reading. A significant number of *rishonim* and *poskim* follow this position, treating the *Torah*'s reference as no more than *asmachta* or a record of existing practice, rather than a binding *d'Oraita* prohibition.

### Law vs. Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קַלְתָּהּ, אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם פְּרוּעַ רֹאשׁ. הָוֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: הֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא בְּשׁוּק — דָּת יְהוּדִית הִיא! וְאֶלָּא בֶּחָצֵר — אִם כֵּן לֹא הִנַּחְתָּ בַּת לְאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ שֶׁיּוֹשֶׁבֶת תַּחַת בַּעְלָהּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב כָּהֲנָא: מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר וְדֶרֶךְ מָבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If she covers her head with **her basket, there is no** violation of the prohibition against having **an uncovered head. Rabbi Zeira discussed it: Where** is the woman that Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to? **If we say** he means that she appears this way **in the marketplace, this is** a violation of **precepts of Jewish** women, as explained previously. **And** if you say **rather** that he means she appears this way **in** her own **courtyard, if so, you have not allowed** any **daughter of our father Abraham to remain with her husband,** since most women are not careful to cover their heads completely inside their own courtyards. **Abaye said, and some say that Rav Kahana** said: Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to when she walks **from** one **courtyard to** another **courtyard or via an alleyway.** Although these places are not considered public areas, strangers may still be present in them.

The *gemara* resolves the question of category by distinguishing between the biblical requirement and the *dat Yehudit* demand. Biblically — *d'Oraita* — a *kalta*, a simple basket-like head covering, is sufficient. The practice of Jewish women, *dat Yehudit*, demands more: *afilu kalta nami assur* — even a basket is insufficient. The exact content of this additional requirement is not fully specified — whether it concerns how much of the head is covered, the opacity of the covering, or some other quality — but the principle is clear: there are two thresholds, biblical and normative, and they differ.

### Context Question

Rabbi Zeira then asks a clarifying question: *heicha*? Where is the context in which this minimal covering — the *kalta* — satisfies requirements? The *gemara* has just established that in the marketplace, *dat Yehudit* demands more than a *kalta*, so it cannot be referring to the public marketplace. Perhaps it refers to her own courtyard? But that introduces a problem.

### Abaye Answer

Abaye (and some say Rav Kahana) answers: *me-chatzer le-chatzer derech mavoi* — the minimal *kalta* is required and sufficient when moving from one courtyard to another by way of the *mavoi*, the alleyway. This is the intermediate zone between the privacy of one's own space and the full publicness of the marketplace.

### Physical Layout

To understand this answer, one must picture the physical layout of the ancient Jewish residential neighborhood. Each household had its own *chatzer* — a courtyard — opening off the main house. Multiple *chatzeirot* opened into a shared, semi-communal space, and those in turn opened into the *mavoi*, the alleyway, which connected to the broader *shuk*, the marketplace. So the *chatzer* itself, while not fully private — other houses open onto it, and other men may be present — is not a genuinely public space either. The *mavoi* is more public, the *shuk* most public of all.

### Public Space Focus

The conclusion the *gemara* reaches is important: in her own *chatzer*, even with other people around, a woman need not cover her head at all. The absence of any head covering becomes a problem only as one moves into more genuinely public space. And in the marketplace, a simple *kalta* is not enough — *dat Yehudit* requires a fuller covering. The intermediate case — crossing briefly through the *mavoi* between courtyards — requires at least the *kalta*.

This emphasis on public space as the operative context has significant implications for contemporary discussions of *kisui rosh*. The concern is not hair as *ervah* in the manner of the *gemara* in *Berachot* — *se'ar b'isha ervah* — which appears to treat exposed hair as categorically problematic. Here the concern is contextual and tied to norms of visibility to strangers. If the governing principle is about public presentation and signals sent to unknown men, then the relevant questions become: what counts as public space, what counts as an accepted norm, and how those norms can evolve. These are live questions for *poskim* across traditions, and different communities have reached very different conclusions depending on which *gemara* they emphasize and how they understand the category of *dat Yehudit*.

### Knitting Arms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְטוֹוֶה בַּשּׁוֹק. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמַרְאָה זְרוֹעוֹתֶיהָ לִבְנֵי אָדָם. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: בְּטוֹוֶה וְרַד כְּנֶגֶד פָּנֶיהָ, וּמְדַבֶּרֶת עִם כׇּל אָדָם. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בִּמְשַׂחֶקֶת עִם בַּחוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **And** the mishna stated that a woman violates Jewish custom if she **spins** wool **in the marketplace. Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** This means **that she reveals her arms to people** by raising her sleeves as she spins. **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Avimi** said: It is referring to **when she spins** with **a red** [***vered***] thread **opposite her face** to highlight her beauty, which entails an element of promiscuity. The mishna also stated another violation of Jewish custom: **Or she speaks with every man** she encounters. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** This means **that she flirts with young men.**

The second example: *v'tova ba-shuk* — she knits in the marketplace. Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel explains: *b'mar'eh zero'oteha livnei adam* — the problem is that knitting exposes her arms to men in public. The motion of knitting draws attention to the arms and makes them visible in a way that is considered immodest. It is striking that this particular behavior — which might seem innocuous in other cultural contexts — is listed as grounds for divorce without *ktubah*.

### Red Thread

Rav Chisda in the name of Avimi offers an alternative explanation: *bitova b'vrad k'neged paneha* — the issue is that she knits with a red thread positioned in front of her face. *Tosafot* reads this literally: a red thread (*vered*) catches the eye, draws attention to her face, and causes men to look at her. Rashi reads *paneha* as a euphemism, suggesting that the thread hangs down in front of her pubic area, attracting attention in a more directly suggestive way. Either reading points to the same underlying concern: the knitting creates an occasion for the eyes of strange men to rest on her in a way that the husband finds unacceptable.

### Flirtation

The third example: *u'medaberet im kol adam* — she speaks with every man. On its face this might simply mean conversing with strangers. But Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel specifies: *b'misacheket im bachurim* — she is joking around, being overly familiar, flirting with young men. The problem is not occasional conversation but a pattern of actively seeking flirtatious interaction with men.

### Joking Young Men

The application is not limited to an explicitly sexual or romantic overture; it describes a woman who is *misacheket*, playful and familiar, in a way that signals an inappropriate openness to male attention. *Al tarbeh sicha im ha-isha* from *Pirkei Avot* may similarly be understood in this light — not a prohibition on conversation per se, but on the kind of excessive, flirtatious familiarity described here.

### Arab Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: זִימְנָא חֲדָא הֲוָה קָאָזֵילְנָא בָּתְרֵיהּ דְּרַב עוּקְבָא, חֲזִיתֵיהּ לְהַהִיא עַרְבָיָא דַּהֲוָה יָתְבָה קָא שָׁדְיָא פִּילְכַּהּ, וְטוֹוֶה וְרַד כְּנֶגֶד פָּנֶיהָ. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזֵיתִינַן, פְּסַיקְתֵּיהּ לְפִילְכַּהּ שְׁדֵיתֵיהּ. אֲמַרָה לִי: עוּלָם, הַב לִי פֶּלֶךְ. אָמַר בַּהּ רַב עוּקְבָא מִילְּתָא. מַאי אָמַר בָּהּ? רָבִינָא אָמַר: ״טוֹוָה בַּשּׁוּק״ אָמַר בַּהּ. רַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: ״מְדַבֶּרֶת עִם כׇּל אָדָם״ אָמַר בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥanna said: One time I was walking behind Rav Ukva. I saw an Arab woman who was sitting, casting her spindle, and spinning a red** thread **opposite her face. Once she saw us, she tore the spindle** from the thread **and threw it down. She said to me: Young man, give me** the **spindle. Rav Ukva made a comment about her,** noting that she provided an example of one of the types of promiscuity mentioned in the mishna. The Gemara asks: **What did he say about her?** Which one of the cases in the mishna did he mention? **Ravina said: He said about her** that she was an example of a woman who licentiously **spins in the marketplace. The Rabbis said: He said about her** that she was an example of a woman who licentiously **speaks with every man.**

A vivid illustrative anecdote: Rabbah bar bar Chanah citing Rabbi Yochanan recounts that he was once walking behind Rav Ukva when he noticed an Arab woman sitting and throwing a spindle while weaving, with the thread positioned in front of her — whether in front of her face or lower, per the dispute above. When she noticed them approaching, she deliberately snapped the thread and threw the spindle down in front of him — the gesture of dropping an object for a man to retrieve, a classic invitation to interaction. She then said to him: *hav li pilcha* — young man, give me back my spindle. The flirtatious intent was transparent. Rav Ukva applied to her a ruling from our *mishnah*. The *gemara* debates which case: Ravina says Rav Ukva was citing *tova ba-shuk* — she was knitting in the marketplace in the problematic manner. The Rabbis say he cited *medaberet im kol adam* — she was proactively engaging strange men. On the second reading, *medaberet im kol adam* is understood not as casual conversation but as actively seeking flirtatious contact.

### Cursing Parents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְקַלֶּלֶת יוֹלְדָיו בְּפָנָיו. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בִּמְקַלֶּלֶת יוֹלְידָיו בִּפְנֵי מוֹלָידָיו. וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״אֶפְרַיִם וּמְנַשֶּׁה כִּרְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן יִהְיוּ לִי״. אָמַר רַבָּה: דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ ״נֵיכְלֵיהּ אַרְיָא לְסָבָא בְּאַפֵּי בְּרֵיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **Abba Shaul says: Also** a woman **who curses** her husband's **parents in his presence** violates the precepts of Jewish women. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** Even **when she curses his parents in the presence of his children** and not in his presence she is considered one who violates Jewish custom. **And your mnemonic is "Ephraim and Manasseh will be to me like Reuben and Simeon"** (Genesis 48:5), which teaches that grandchildren have the status of children. Cursing one's husband's parents in front of his children is tantamount to doing so in front of the husband himself. **Rabba said:** An example is **that she said in the presence of** her husband's **son: May a lion devour** your **grandfather.**

Abba Shaul adds a fourth category that stands apart from the theme of female modesty and male attention: *af ha-m'kalelet yeladav b'fanav* — even a woman who curses her husband's parents in his presence. Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel extends this: *b'm'kalelet yeladav bifnei molidav* — she curses his children in the presence of their grandparents. The mnemonic offered is *Efraim u'Menashe k'Reuven v'Shimon yihyu li* — grandsons are like sons — so insulting a grandfather in the presence of the grandchildren is equivalent to insulting a father before his children. The example given is the wife who says to her children, *neichlei arya l'sabba* — "let a lion eat your grandfather." Whether said directly to the husband or in the presence of their children, this is placed in the category of conduct that makes the marriage untenable.

### Loud Voice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף הַקּוֹלָנִית. מַאי ״קוֹלָנִית״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמַשְׁמַעַת קוֹלָהּ עַל עִסְקֵי תַּשְׁמִישׁ. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: בִּמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת בְּחָצֵר זוֹ וְנִשְׁמַע קוֹלָהּ בְּחָצֵר אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **Rabbi Tarfon says: Also a loud woman.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the definition of **a loud woman? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** She is considered loud **when she raises her voice about matters relating to intercourse,** i.e., she quarrels and fights with her husband about it loudly enough that the neighbors overhear, causing him embarrassment. **It was taught in a *baraita*: When she engages in intercourse in this courtyard** and she screams from pain, **and** therefore **her voice is heard in another courtyard.**

Rabbi Tarfon adds: *af ha-kolanit* — also the *kolanit*, the woman with the excessively loud voice. The *mishnah* then provides a definition: *likshehi medaberet b'toch beisah u'shcheneha shom'in kolah* — when she speaks inside her house and her neighbors can hear her voice. The plain reading is that she argues so loudly that the neighbors overhear their quarrels and private conversations — both embarrassing and invasive.

### Loud Sexual

Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel, however, interprets this very differently: *b'mashma'at kolah al iskei tashmish* — her voice can be heard regarding matters of sexual relations. The issue is not domestic arguments but sounds during intercourse that carry beyond the walls of the bedroom.

### Rashi Pleasure

Rashi offers two readings of this phenomenon: either she cries out because sex is painful to her — a genuinely sad interpretation — or she is expressing pleasure loudly. Either way, the *gemara* treats this as a matter of privacy that, when violated, damages the husband's sense of dignity.

### Defect Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְנִיתְנְיַיהּ גַּבֵּי מוּמִין בְּמַתְנִיתִין! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, then **this should be taught** together **with** the **blemishes in the mishna** at the end of the chapter, where it lists cases of women who may be divorced without payment of their marriage contract due to a physical blemish, as opposed to the mishna here, which discusses immodest conduct. **Rather, it is clear as we initially answered,** that a loud woman is so defined due to immodest behavior.

The *gemara* raises an objection to this second reading: if the issue with the *kolanit* is that she cannot control the sounds she makes during intimacy, then this seems like an intrinsic physical characteristic — a *mum*, a defect — rather than a behavioral choice. It should therefore belong in the category of *mumim* addressed in a separate *mishnah*, not among the behavioral transgressions listed here.

### Controllable Behavior

The *gemara* resolves this by preferring the first interpretation: *ela mechvarta kedishanin me'ikara* — the correct reading is the earlier one, that *kolanit* refers to a woman who speaks so loudly inside her house that the neighbors can hear her conversations. This is controllable behavior, a matter of how one conducts oneself, and therefore appropriately categorized among behavioral transgressions of *dat Yehudit* rather than among physical defects. The *mishnah* is about choices and conduct — things the wife can and should control — and the *kolanit* belongs there only if her loudness is understood as a behavioral pattern rather than an involuntary physical trait.
