# Pesachim 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kezayis vs. Kibeitzah

### Returning for Chametz

Today's daf is 50, and we wrap up the third *perek* before beginning the fourth. We continue from the bottom of 49b, picking up seven lines from the bottom at the two dots, beginning with the word *she'yatza*.

The preceding Mishna had established two parallel scenarios in which a person might need to turn back and destroy something: the case of *chametz* left at home, and the case of meat from a *korban shelamim* taken out of Jerusalem. With respect to *chametz*, the principle is clear — one must destroy *chametz* before *Pesach*. Where turning back is not feasible, either because one is occupied with a *mitzvah* and lacks time, or because one is rushing to save lives (in which case no calculation is made at all), then *bitul* is performed instead. As noted in the previous session, *bitul* appears only once in all the *Mishnayos* of Masechet Pesachim — as a last resort. It was only in the Amoraitic period that the *Gemara* ruled *ha'bodek tzarich she'yevatel*, expanding *bitul* into a standard backup practice, which then raised the question of why *biyur* remains necessary at all. In the Mishnah, however, *bitul* was the fallback when *biyur* was impossible, reflecting the primary focus on physically destroying *chametz*.

The parallel case of the *shelamim* meat involves a *korban* that becomes *pasul* the moment it is taken beyond the walls of Jerusalem. The Rabbis ruled that one must return it and burn it in Jerusalem or in the Beit HaMikdash. Tosafos notes that had it been *kodshei kodashim*, such as a *chatas*, the biblical requirement would be to burn it specifically in the Beit HaMikdash; since it is *kodshei kalim*, the obligation is rabbinic, and the Rabbis accordingly ruled that if one has already passed the point from which Jerusalem is no longer visible, one need not turn back. But if Jerusalem can still be seen, one must return and burn the meat there.

### Mishna Debate

The Mishna then addresses the threshold question: how much *chametz* or *korban* meat must remain for the obligation to turn back to apply? This is where the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda enters. Rabbi Meir holds that one returns only for a *kibeitzah* of either substance, while Rabbi Yehuda holds that even a *kezayis* obligates return.

### Reversal Question

The *Gemara* notes the apparent implication of these positions: Rabbi Meir considers a *kibeitzah* the significant minimum, while Rabbi Yehuda considers even a *kezayis* significant. This seems internally consistent — until the *Gemara* raises a contradiction from the Mishna concerning *zimun*. There, the question is the minimum amount of bread required for a *zimun* obligation: the first opinion holds a *kezayis*, which is presumably Rabbi Meir's view, while Rabbi Yehuda states a *kibeitzah* is required. Thus in our Mishna, Rabbi Meir requires a *kibeitzah* and Rabbi Yehuda a *kezayis*, but in the *zimun* Mishna their positions appear precisely reversed. How is this to be explained?

(It is worth noting the curious use of the word *ad* — "until" — in the *zimun* Mishna, which implies a downward direction rather than a minimum threshold. The Vilna Gaon apparently offers a brilliant reading of this language involving the size of a loaf divided among the *mezamnim*, though the precise details of his reading are not at hand.)

### Texts Switched

Rabbi Yochanan's response is *muchlefet hashitah*: one of the texts has the names of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda switched, and the apparent contradiction is simply a scribal transposition.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye, however, resists this approach: *l'olam lo teipoch* — do not reverse the texts. The contradiction is only apparent because the two debates operate on entirely different grounds. *Hasam bikra pligi* — there, in the *zimun* Mishna, they debate based on a scriptural verse. *Hacha bisvara pligi* — here, in our Mishna, they debate based on a different principle altogether.

### Rabbi Meir's Ketubim

In the *zimun* debate, Rabbi Meir draws on the verse *v'achalta v'savata u'veirachta* (Devarim 8:10). He reads *v'achalta* as referring to the act of eating and *v'savata* as referring to drinking — not literal satiation — so that the act triggering the obligation of *Birkat Hamazon* is *achila*, and the standard measure of *achila* is a *kezayis*. Tosafos notes this reflects the principle of *achila k'moshehi*, that eating by definition is measured in *kazayis*.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Sevia

Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, reads the verse in its plain sense — *pshat*: *achila she'yesh bo sevia*, an eating that brings about satiation. The act that triggers *Birkat Hamazon* must be the kind of eating that satiates, and that minimum is a *kibeitzah*. It admittedly does not sound like a very large amount to produce satiation, which would imply that most people would rarely be biblically obligated.

### Tosafos Asmachta

Tosafos clarifies that at the biblical level both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree that true satiation is required. The measures of *kezayis* and *kibeitzah* are not strictly biblical thresholds; rather, they function as *asmachta* — rabbinic standards that are anchored to scripture, as Rashi explains elsewhere in a reference to Perek Kama of Mi She'meiso. At the rabbinic level, when a more objective and accessible minimum is needed, Rabbi Meir holds one *kezayis* is sufficient as a symbolic act of eating, while Rabbi Yehuda insists on a *kibeitzah* as at least symbolically evocative of satiation.

### Hazarah Significance

In our Mishna, the debate concerns not *Birkat Hamazon* but *hazarah* — when one is obligated to return and destroy. The two Tannaim apply different frameworks for determining what amount renders an object significant enough to warrant the effort of returning.

### Rabbi Meir Tumah

Rabbi Meir holds *hazartu k'tum'atu* — the threshold for return mirrors the threshold for *tumah*. The minimum measure for transmitting *tumah* is a *kibeitzah*. Whether *kibeitzah* is the minimum for an object to *receive* *tumah* or the minimum to *transmit* it is itself a matter of debate, but in either case it is the measure associated with the significance of food as a substance. Therefore, one returns for a *kibeitzah*. This applies equally to the *chametz* case and the *korban* case: even though the *korban* meat did not become *tamei* but merely *pasul* by leaving Jerusalem, the relevant framework is still the significance of *okhel* as food, and that significance is measured by *kibeitzah*. It is admittedly not immediately transparent why *tumah* thresholds apply to the *chametz* case, but the underlying logic seems to be that *kibeitzah* represents when an object attains substantial status as food.

### Rabbi Yehuda Issurah

Rabbi Yehuda's position is more directly intuitive: *k'issuro hazartu k'issuro* — the threshold for return mirrors the threshold for prohibition. The minimum amount of *chametz* one transgresses by eating is a *kezayis* — though *chametz* carries an *issur* for any amount (*kol shehu*), the standard *shiur* of significance is a *kezayis*. Likewise, the meat of a *pasul korban* becomes forbidden in the measure of a *kezayis*. Therefore one must return for a *kezayis*.

### Object vs. Act

One way of framing this debate: Rabbi Meir focuses on the significance of the *object itself* — at what point is this food considered a meaningful object? That is determined by *kibeitzah*, the threshold at which food acquires or transmits *tumah*. Rabbi Yehuda focuses on the significance of a problematic *act* one could perform with it — at what amount does eating it constitute a genuine violation? That is determined by *kezayis*. Rabbi Meir thus asks: when is this considered a meaningful *amount of food*? Rabbi Yehuda asks: when does this amount pose a meaningful *risk of transgression*?

### Rabbi Natan

A *braisa* adds a third opinion: Rabbi Natan says the threshold is *shtei beitzim* — two eggs' worth. One returns for neither a *kezayis* nor a *kibeitzah* but only for two *beitzim*. The *Gemara* records simply that the Sages did not agree with him, and the source of this measure is left unexplained.

## Aggada Future World

### Light Yikaros

Having concluded the analytical discussion of *hazarah*, the *Gemara* transitions into an extensive *aggadic* section. Rashi notes this connects thematically to the references to Jerusalem, and it also serves as a fitting close to the first three *prakim* of the *masechta*, which form a unified thematic unit focused on the laws of *chametz*. The verse from Zechariah (14:6–7) provides the opening: *v'haya bayom hahu lo yihiye or yikaros v'kipaon* — on that day there will no longer be the light of *yikaros* and *kipaon*. The precise translation of these two words is not obvious, and the *Gemara* proceeds to offer competing interpretations.

### Rabbi Elazar Light

Rabbi Elazar reads the verse as a statement about relative weight and weightlessness. *Yikaros* — from the root *yakar*, "heavy" or "precious" — refers to the light that is precious and weighty in this world, meaning the strong, brilliant sunlight we currently value highly. *Kipaon* — from an Aramaic word meaning to float — describes that same light as it will be in the world to come: featherweight, trivial, inconsequential by comparison. The divine light of *olam haba* will be so overwhelming that the light of this world, for which we now have such regard, will seem like nothing at all. The metaphor of heavy and light runs throughout this passage in a striking way.

### Negaim Oholos

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different reading. The *yikaros* — the weighty and precious things of this world — are the tractates of *Negaim* and *Oholos*, universally regarded as the most challenging portions of the entire *Shas*. When Rabbi Akiva was once drawn toward *aggadata*, the retort directed at him was: *Akiva, mah lecha etzel agadata? Kelach lecha etzel Negaim v'Oholos* — go occupy yourself with *Negaim* and *Oholos*, implying they were his true domain. These tractates are *yikaros* — enormously weighty — in this world. But in the world to come they will be *kappuyin* — lightweight, accessible to all. The level of Torah knowledge in the world to come will be so exalted that even schoolchildren will know *Negaim* and *Oholos*.

### Weighty People

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi applies the verse to people: the *yikaros* of this world — those given great honor and prestige — will be *kappuyin* in the world to come, reduced to nothing. This refers to the powerful and wealthy who are not righteous, who command great respect in this world but will have no standing in the next.

### Rav Yosef Vision

This teaching is illustrated by the famous story of Rav Yosef, the son of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. Rav Yosef became gravely ill — *chalash* — and *isnagid*: he fainted or, in the more dramatic reading, his vital signs briefly ceased and he was brought back. When he revived, his father asked: *mai chazit?* — what did you see? The assumption was that the brief brush with death had afforded him a vision of the world to come, much as is reported by people today who describe such experiences.

### Olam Hafuch

Rav Yosef answered: *olam hafuch ra'iti* — I saw a topsy-turvy world. *Elyonim l'mata v'tachtonim l'ma'ala* — those who are at the top in this world are at the bottom in the next, and those who are at the bottom here are elevated there. The powerful, the prestigious, those who command status in this world — there they are at the bottom. The quiet *tzaddik*, perhaps one of the *lamed-vavniks*, who receives no recognition here — in the world to come, such a person is elevated.

### Father Response

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's response is striking: *olam barur ra'ita* — you saw the *clear* world, the world as it truly is. It is *this* world that is upside down. The world to come sets things right. He then asks the natural question: *heichi hasam?* — what is the status of Torah sages like us there? The answer: *ki heichi d'isinnan hacha hachi isinnan hatam* — the same way we are here, we are there. The Rabbis, who are given respect in this world, also have that respect in the world to come.

### Talmudo B'Yado

Rav Yosef further reports: *v'shamati shehayu omrim, ashrei mi sheba l'khan v'talmudo b'yado* — blessed is the one who arrives there with his learning in hand. Entering *olam haba* with a rich store of Torah knowledge is a most distinguished entrance. This is notable because the earlier focus was on the *tachtonim* — the *tzaddikim* and *chasidim* outside the rabbinic class who are elevated in the next world — yet the narrative consistently returns to Torah scholarship as a distinct and prized form of merit, reflecting an ongoing counterpoint: while recognizing the tremendous value of personal righteousness and piety, the *Gemara* is careful not to minimize the extraordinary standing of *talmidei chachamim*.

### Harugei Malchut

A second report: *harugei malchut* — those martyred by the Roman government, killed for their faith — occupy the highest *madrega* of all. *Ein adam yachol la'amod bimechitzatam* — no one else can stand in their section.

### Harugei Lod

The *Gemara* immediately asks: who are these *harugei malchut*? If one says it means Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues, the great sages persecuted in the Hadrianic persecutions — would Rabbi Akiva require martyrdom to achieve this standing? His stature as a Torah sage would alone place him at the summit of *olam haba*. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ella harugei Lod* — it refers to those killed in Lod. The Talmud elsewhere records that when the Roman government sought to hold the entire city of Lod accountable for a killing, two individuals came forward and accepted blame upon themselves to save the entire city. Such selfless martyrdom — sacrificing oneself to redeem an entire community — places those individuals at a level where no one else can stand alongside them.

This raises a broader question that the discussion implicitly gestures toward: why does the *Gemara* limit *harugei malchut* at this highest level to so specific a group rather than extending the category to all who died rather than renounce their faith? The very phrase *ein adam yachol la'amod bimechitzatam* implies a select, almost singular subset. And there is something telling in how the *Gemara* consistently situates the rabbinic voice as observing all of this from a slight distance — noting who achieves the highest standing, reaffirming its own stable position, but treating these extremes of selfless martyrdom and unknown piety as objects of admiration from the outside.

### Metzilot Hasus

The *Gemara* proceeds to the verse later in Zechariah 14: *bayom hahu yihiyeh al metzilot hasus kodesh l'Hashem* — on that day the words *kodesh l'Hashem* will be inscribed on the *metzilot hasus*, the ornaments or bells of horses. What is the meaning of *metzilot hasus*?

### Jerusalem Expansion

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — and this, says Rashi, is the thematic connection to the preceding *aggadata* about Jerusalem — understands *metzilot* through the root *tzel*, shade. The verse describes Jerusalem's future expansion: the city will grow until it reaches as far as a horse can run while still casting its shade, meaning for half a day, approximately six hours. The city will be so vast as to span the distance a horse travels in half a day, which by rough reckoning could be thirty miles or more. Jerusalem today, while considerably larger than in the time of the *Gemara*, does not yet approach such dimensions, though the *Gemara*'s contemporaries would have found even present-day Jerusalem fantastically large.

### Horse Ornaments

Rabbi Eliezer reads *metzilot* as the ornamental decorations — beads and adornments — hung between horses' eyes. *Kol metzilot shetola'im lasus bein einav yihiyeh kodesh l'Hashem* — all such ornaments will be consecrated to God. Everyone will donate their horses' decorations to the Temple.

### War Booty

Rabbi Yochanan reads the verse as referring to the booty of war: *kol biza shebozin Yisrael ad shehasus ratz umatzil* — all the booty that Israel captures in the time it takes a horse to run and cast its shade, half a day, presumably during the war of *Gog u'Magog*, will be consecrated to God.

### Verses Resolved

The *Gemara* then examines how the interpretations connect to the continuation of the verse: *v'haya hasirot b'veit Hashem k'mizrakim lifnei hamizbeach* — even the pots in the House of God will be like the sacred vessels used for casting blood by the altar, meaning everything will be made of purest gold. This continuation fits naturally with Rabbi Yochanan's reading: if all war booty is consecrated, there will be immense wealth, and even ordinary vessels will be fashioned from gold. But for the other two interpretations — whether about the expansion of Jerusalem's boundaries or the donation of horse ornaments, neither of which implies enormous wealth — how does the next verse follow? The *Gemara* answers: *milta achrita ka'amar* — it is simply an additional, separate point. Another element of the messianic era is that Israel will be very wealthy, will donate generously, and even the pots in the Temple will be of gold.

Similarly, the verse concludes *v'lo yihiye Kna'ani od b'veit Hashem Tzeva'ot* — there will no longer be a *Kna'ani* in the House of God. According to Rabbi Yochanan's reading about booty, *Kna'ani* is understood as a trader, meaning there will no longer be any commercial activity in the precincts of the Temple. In the Temple era, there was significant transactional activity around the Beit HaMikdash — people purchasing animals for *korbanot*, money-changers, merchants — which was always viewed with a certain unease, famously evoked by Jesus's expulsion of the money-changers. In the messianic era, with such abundance, none of that will be necessary.

### Kena'ani Trader

The *Gemara* establishes this reading of *Kna'ani* as trader from elsewhere: the verse in Bereishit states *vayar sham Yehuda bat ish Kna'ani u-shmo Shua* — Yehuda saw there the daughter of a *Kna'ani* man. The *Gemara* asks: could this literally mean a Canaanite woman? *Efshar?* Abraham had charged his servant not to take a wife for Yitzchak from the inhabitants of Canaan; Yitzchak charged Yaakov *al tikach isha mibnot Kna'an* — do not marry a Canaanite woman. Would Yehuda then go and marry one? *V'Yehuda azil v'nasiv?* Rather, says Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, *bat gavra tagra* — the daughter of a *trader*. Support for this reading comes from Hoshea: *Kna'an beyado mozney mirma* — in his hand are deceitful scales — which explicitly associates the *Kna'ani* with commerce.

### No Kena'ani

For the other two interpretations of *metzilot hasus*, the *Gemara* reads *v'lo yihiye Kna'ani* differently. Rabbi Yirmiyah says: *ein kan ani* — there will be no poor person there. Either way, the verse speaks of a future era of prosperity in which neither commerce nor poverty will mar the sacred space. The word *Kna'ani* thus supports two distinct readings: a trader, or a person in material need, and both are taken to describe a reality that will be absent in the messianic era.

### God's Name Echad

The *Gemara* turns to the theological climax of the passage: *vehaya Hashem l'melech al kol ha'aretz; bayom hahu yiheyeh Hashem Echad u'shmo Echad* — on that day God will be One and His name will be One. The *Gemara* immediately asks: is God not One now? God's unity is eternal. One straightforward reading would be that all peoples will come to recognize God as One — but the *Gemara* presses further, looking specifically at the Jewish experience of God.

Rabbi Acha bar Chanina explains: it is not that God will become One — He is always One — but rather that our *experience* of God will become unified. In this world, when good things happen one recites *Baruch Hatov ve'hametiv*; when bad things happen one recites *Baruch Dayan ha'emet*. These are two different *brachot*, reflecting two very different modes of encountering God: God as the source of good, and God as the true Judge whose decrees, though just, are experienced as painful. In the world to come, *kulo Hatov ve'hametiv* — there will only be *Baruch Hatov ve'hametiv*. No one will ever need to say *Baruch Dayan ha'emet*. God will be *Echad* in the sense that our experience of God will be undivided — only goodness, only blessing, no longer the duality of joy and grief that characterizes life in this world.

### Experience God

This raises a further difficulty: if the point is about our *experience* of God rather than some objective theological change, why not simply say that all peoples will recognize God as One? The *Gemara* seems to be addressing something more internal, more specifically Jewish — a change in how the Jewish people encounters the divine. Even granting this, the question of why the verse needed to say *Echad* to express this experiential unity is not fully resolved.

### Name Pronunciation

The *Gemara* then turns to the second half: *u'shmo Echad* — His name will be One. Is God's name not one now? Rabbi Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: in this world, God's name is written one way — *yud-heh* (the Tetragrammaton) — but read another way — *alef-dalet* (*Adonai*). The written form and the pronounced form are different, introducing a kind of disunity into even how we refer to God. In the world to come, *nichtav be'yud-heh ve'nikra be'yud-heh* — God's name will be both written and read the same way. The barrier between the true name and the spoken name will dissolve, reflecting a closeness and directness of relationship with the divine that is not available in this world.

There is further support for this hiddenness being intentional in our current state: the verse *zeh shemi le'olam* ("this is My name forever") is written *le'alem* — without a *vav* — which can be read as *le'alem*, meaning "to conceal." The name is to be hidden. *Veze zichri ledor dor* — the way I am *remembered* and evoked across generations is not through my true name, but through the substitute name *alef-dalet*. The two halves of the verse thus complement each other: the true name (*yud-heh*) is *ze shemi*, hidden *le'alem*; the way of evoking God in this world is *ze zichri*, the pronounced name *alef-dalet*. The imperfection of this world is manifest even in how we name God — we experience divinity through a filter, unable in this state to encounter it directly.

### Rava Pronunciation

Rava once thought to teach the proper pronunciation of the divine name publicly, at his regular lecture.

### Name Hidden

An old man in the audience immediately cautioned him: *le'alem ktiv* — it is written to be concealed. The very spelling of the verse instructs that the name must remain hidden. This is not merely a practice but something close to a divine directive embedded in the text itself, and even in discussing it publicly one must observe a limit. With that, the *Gemara* concludes with *Hadran alach Eilu Overin*, marking the end of the third *perek*.

## Perek III-IV Transition

### Masechta Structure

With the conclusion of *Hadran alach Eilu Overin*, the third *perek* closes. As noted, the first three *prakim* of Masechet Pesachim form a coherent unit entirely devoted to the laws of *chametz* — how to find it, ensure that dough does not become *chametz*, how to dispose of it, and all related concerns. The final *perek* of the *masechta* addresses the *leil seder*, and the middle section is devoted to *korbanot*. The fourth *perek*, which we now begin, is something of a standalone unit: it opens with certain practices of *Erev Pesach* that are not *chametz*-related, and then develops into an extensive treatment of *minhagim* and the binding nature of local custom — a topic that becomes genuinely fascinating. The conclusion of the first three *prakim* with an extended *aggadic* passage is, in this light, particularly fitting.

## Work Erev Pesach

### Mishna Work

The fourth *perek* opens: *makom shenahagu la'asot melacha b'arvei pesachim ad chatzot osim* — in a place where the custom is to perform *melacha* on the eve of *Pesach* until midday, one may do so. Implicit in the Mishna is that from midday onward, no one does *melacha* on *Erev Pesach* — the *korban pesach* is slaughtered in the afternoon, and everyone must be ready. The question the Mishna addresses is whether *melacha* is permitted in the *morning* hours. The answer varies by community. *Makom shenahagu shelo la'asot ein osim* — where the custom is not to work, one does not work.

This is significant on two levels. First, it powerfully recognizes the binding nature of *minhag*, which raises a serious question — since when does *minhag* carry the force of an obligation? Second, it implicitly embraces the principle of *eilu va'eilu* — the possibility of legitimately different practices coexisting in different communities without one being simply right and the other wrong.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi explains the rationale for the stricter custom: *kedei shelo yehei tarud bemelacha veyishkach* — so that one not become absorbed in work and forget the things that must be done. What must be done on *Erev Pesach*? *Biyur chametz*, the final disposal of *chametz*; *shechitat hapesach*, the slaughtering of the *korban pesach* after midday; and *tikkun matzah*, the preparation of *matzot* for the *leil seder*. And underlying all of this is a further principle: *demitzvah litro'ach mibe'od yom kedei lehaseiv maher* — it is a *mitzvah* to complete one's preparations before nightfall so as to begin the *Seder* as early as possible. This desire to start early, driven partly by the famous practice of *chalukat matzah be'leil pesachim bishvil tinokot shelo yishanu* — distributing *matzah* at the Seder to keep the children awake — is evidently ancient. The concern about children falling asleep before the Seder was just as real then as now.

### Travel Stringency

The Mishna then addresses the case of one who travels between communities with differing practices: *hahoilech mimakom she'osim lemakom she'ein osim o mimakom she'ein osim lemakom she'osim* — whether going from a place that permits work to one that does not, or vice versa. The ruling is *notnin alav chumrei makom sheyatza misham v'chumrei makom shehalach lesham* — one adopts the stringencies of both the place one left and the place one is going. One receives the worst of both worlds. This principle recurs prominently in the context of the second day of *Yom Tov* for travelers and will be well familiar from that discussion.

### Avoid Machlokes

The Mishna adds: *v'al yishaneh adam mipnei hamachloket* — a person should not change his practice in a way that will cause controversy. The Mishna thus holds two apparently competing values in tension: on the one hand, it embraces legitimate diversity between communities; on the other hand, it insists that within a given community, one should conform to local practice to avoid *machloket* and preserve *shalom*.

The natural question is whether these values can always be harmonized. When someone arrives from a community that does not permit work and enters one that permits it, is that person *violating* the local convention by refraining from work? Not necessarily — the permissive practice *allows* work; it does not *require* it. Refraining from work does not openly contradict local practice, and therefore there is no *machloket*. This distinguishes *Erev Pesach* from cases like wearing *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed*, where different practices are visibly incompatible: one either wears *tefillin* or does not, and the difference is immediately apparent to everyone around. In a more heterogeneous community — which, it is worth noting, was not the reality in the time of *Chazal* — the very norm of uniformity may be inherently inapplicable.

### Shevi'it Produce

The Mishna presents a parallel case involving *shevi'it* produce: *hamolich peirot shevi'it mimakom shekalu lemakom shelo kalu* — one who transports *shemita* produce from a place where it is no longer available in the fields to a place where it still is, or vice versa. The law of *biur* — the requirement to destroy *shevi'it* produce once it is no longer accessible to animals in the wild — depends on local availability. When produce is still available to animals outdoors, one need not destroy what one has indoors; when it is no longer so available, one must destroy it. In both directions of travel, the traveler must apply the more stringent standard: *chayav leva'er* — he is obligated to destroy the produce.

### Rabbi Yehuda Shevi'it

Rabbi Yehuda offers his own position, saying *tzei vehavei lecha af ata* — "go out and bring even for yourself," a somewhat enigmatic phrase. The *Gemara* will need to clarify what Rabbi Yehuda means by this.

## Mai Irya Erev Pesach

### Why Pesach

The *Gemara* opens with a question: *mai irya arvei pesachim?* — why specify *Erev Pesach* in particular? The prohibition on doing *melacha* in the latter part of the day is not unique to *Erev Pesach*; it applies to other *erev* occasions as well. The question seems to press on the idea that since there is evidently a broader principle at work, what *chiddush* is the Mishna introducing by specifying *Erev Pesach*?

### Braisa Mincha

A *braisa* establishes the general principle: *ha'oseh melacha b'arvei shabbatot v'yamim tovim min hamincha ulemala* — one who performs *melacha* on *Erev Shabbat* or *Erev Yom Tov* from *Mincha* onwards — Rashi clarifies this means *Mincha Ketana*, i.e., from approximately 3:30 in the afternoon — *eino ro'eh siman beracha* — will never see a sign of blessing in that work.

### Threshold Difference

The *Gemara* now delineates why *Erev Pesach* is genuinely distinct. There are two differences. First, the *Erev Shabbat* prohibition begins only at *Mincha Ketana* — 3:30 in the afternoon — while the *Erev Pesach* prohibition begins at midday (*chatzot*), a substantially earlier point in the day. Second, the consequence differs: on *Erev Shabbat*, working after *Mincha* means one *eino ro'eh siman beracha* — one will see no blessing in the work, but it is not technically prohibited. On *Erev Pesach*, the situation is categorically different.

This *siman beracha* teaching deserves more attention than it typically receives. How many people are aware that performing *melacha* after 3:30 on *Erev Shabbat* will deprive them of blessing in that work? Rav Soloveitchik once observed that while America had produced genuinely *shomer Shabbat* Jews, it had not produced *shomer Erev Shabbat* Jews — people who devoted *Erev Shabbat* itself to the preparations and spirit of *Shabbat*, an idea deeply connected to the insight that the experience of any sacred time is inseparable from how one prepares for it. Practically, of course, not everyone has the latitude to leave work at 3:30 on a Friday afternoon, but the principle remains largely unacknowledged.

### Noon Cherem

On *Erev Pesach* the stakes are higher. *Hacha shamutei nami meshamtinan lei* — here, one who does *melacha* after midday would actually be subject to *cherem*, to excommunication. This is not merely a loss of blessing; it is an actual violation. The *Gemara* thus establishes that *Erev Pesach* is a genuine *chiddush* compared to the general *erev* prohibition, differing both in when the restriction begins and in the severity of the consequence for violating it. Given this, the question of how seriously the *issur melacha* of *Erev Pesach* afternoon is observed in practice is, to say the least, pointed.

## Zariz Shafel Rewards

### Four Types

A *braisa* is cited presenting a taxonomy of four character types defined by the combination of diligence or laziness with their resulting gain or loss: the *zariz veniskar* (diligent and rewarded), the *zariz venifsad* (diligent and harmed), the *shafel veniskar* (lazy and rewarded), and the *shafel venifsad* (lazy and harmed). All four cases are framed around the practice of *Erev Shabbat*.

### Zariz Niskar

The *zariz veniskar* — the diligent person who gains — is one who works productively throughout the week but refrains from work on *Erev Shabbat*. Such a person is rewarded for their industry while properly honoring the approach of *Shabbat*.

### Zariz Nifsad

The *zariz venifsad* — the diligent person who loses — is one who works hard throughout the entire week including *Erev Shabbat*. In the end, that overreach will come back to harm them; the failure to stop before *Shabbat* undoes the blessing of the week's labor.

### Shafel Niskar

The *shafel veniskar* — the lazy person who gains — is one who does no work at all during the week, nor on *Erev Shabbat*. Through sheer idleness, they accidentally achieve the proper observance of *Erev Shabbat*. Like the proverbial stopped clock that is right twice a day: by not working at all, they succeed in not working on *Erev Shabbat*.

### Shafel Nifsad

The *shafel venifsad* — the lazy person who loses — is one who does nothing the entire week, but then specifically chooses to work on *Erev Shabbat*. This is the worst outcome: all the idleness of the week brings no benefit, and the one time action is taken, it is taken at the wrong moment.

### Mechuza Women

Rava applies this framework with characteristic sharpness to the women of Mechuza. Even though they do not work on *Erev Shabbat* — not out of any religious motivation, but purely out of their pampered, indulgent lifestyle, since they do not work any day of the week either — *afillu hachi shafel veniskar karinan lehu*: even so, they are classified as *shafel veniskar*. They receive the reward of not working on *Erev Shabbat*, regardless of the reason. Rava's critique of the overly cosmopolitan ways of Mechuza is subtly embedded even in the compliment.

### Kindness Height

Rava then notes a contradiction between two verses. One verse states *ki gadol ad shamayim chasdecha* — God's kindness reaches up to the heavens. Another states *ki gadol me'al shamayim chasdecha* — God's kindness reaches *above* the heavens. Which is it?

### Lishma Intent

The resolution: *kan be'osim lishma vekan be'osim shelo lishma* — one verse describes those who perform *mitzvot lishma*, for the right reason, and the other describes those who perform them *shelo lishma*, for the wrong reason. When one acts *lishma*, God's kindness extends above the heavens; when one acts *shelo lishma*, it extends only to the heavens — still a vast and remarkable reward, but less than the highest. This connects back to Rav Yehuda's famous teaching in the name of Rav: *le'olam ya'asok adam be-Torah u-mitzvot af al pi shelo lishma, she-mitoch shelo lishma ba lishma* — one should always engage in Torah and *mitzvot* even if not for the right reason, because from acting *shelo lishma* one will eventually come to *lishma*. Even doing the right thing for the wrong reason carries great reward — and may ultimately lead to doing it for the right reason.

## Work Blessing Teachings

### Wife Millstone

A *braisa*: *ha-metzapeh li-sechar ishto ve-reichayim eino roeh siman beracha le-olam* — one who hopes to profit from the earnings of his wife or from a millstone will never see a sign of blessing. Rashi explains that the wife's earnings refer to her use of *matkolta* — a type of measuring scale — which she rents out, apparently a low-yield enterprise. Tosafos suggests it refers to a woman doing knitting or weaving in public, which poses other concerns.

### Work vs. Rent

With respect to the millstone: *agarta* — if one is simply renting it out, that apparently yields no blessing. But *aval avda u-mezavna* — if one actually produces and then sells, that is another matter entirely: *ishtabuchi mishtabach ba kera* — Scripture itself praises this. The connection is to *Eshet Chayil*.

### Eshet Chayil

The verse cited is *sadin asta va-timkor... de-chagor natna la-Kena'ani* — she made linen garments and sold them, she gave a girdle to the trader. The *Eshet Chayil* passage in Mishlei, as noted here and as we will encounter again shortly, uses *Kna'ani* in the sense of a merchant or trader, consistent with what the *Gemara* established earlier. The point of the contrast is the difference between active production and selling (which is blessed) versus passive rental of tools or equipment (which is not).

### Large Volume

Another *braisa*: *ha-mistacher be-kanim u-ve-kankanim eino roeh siman beracha le-olam* — one who deals in rods or large vessels will never see a sign of blessing. The reason: *keivan de-nafish ap-chazeihu* — because their bulk is very large, *shalta beihu eina* — the *ayin hara* takes hold. The sheer visibility of large quantities of merchandise attracts the evil eye. The recommendation is therefore not to be in a business of highly visible, voluminous goods.

### Market Vendors

*Tagrei simta* — market stall vendors; *megadlei beheima daka* — those who raise small animals, i.e., sheep, known for grazing in fields not their own, which constantly drew complaint; *kotzei ilanos tovos* — those who cut down beautiful trees for timber, an act that drew the ire of onlookers; *ve-nosnin eineihem be-chelek yafe* — and those who always grab the nicer portion — all of these will never see a sign of blessing.

### Nicer Portion

The common thread is public grievance: *de-tohu inshi* — people are astounded and upset. The market vendors are constantly exposed to the public; the shepherds were notorious for their animals grazing where they should not; those who destroy beautiful trees provoke the outcry of passersby; those who always claim the best portion for themselves generate resentment. All such activities draw negative attention and, with it, the absence of divine blessing.

### Four Prutos

Another *braisa*: *arba prutos ein bahem siman beracha le-olam* — four types of payment, associated with small earnings, will never yield a sign of blessing: *schar kosvin*, the fees paid to scribes; *schar meturgamin*, the pay of translators or the *meturgeman* who repeated the *rav*'s words loudly for the audience; *schar yesomim*, fees earned from managing orphans' estates; and *ma'ot ha-ba'ot mi-medinat ha-yam*, returns on overseas investments.

### Scribes Translators

The case of the translators is understandable: *mishum demechzei k'sechar Shabbat* — it looks like payment for work performed on *Shabbat*, which is inherently problematic even when technically permitted. The case of orphans' money: *lav bnei mechila ninhu* — minors cannot grant forgiveness; if one misuses their funds, even unwittingly, there is no pardon available, as there would be with an adult who might be *mochel*. Overseas investments: *mishum delo kol yoma misrachesh nisa* — one cannot rely on a miracle every day; ships sank regularly in those waters, and building an enterprise on such unpredictable risk will not yield lasting blessing.

### Why Scribes Specifically

The case of scribes requires more explanation: why should writing *Sifrei Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot* not be a well-rewarded enterprise? The answer comes from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *chaf daled taaniyot yashvu Anshei Knesses Ha-Gedolah al kosvei Sfarim Tfillin u-Mezuzos* — the Men of the Great Assembly sat in twenty-four fasts, directed at God, for the scribes who write sacred texts.

### Scribes Profitability

What were they praying for? *Shelo yisasheru* — that the scribes should never become wealthy. And why? *Sheilmalei misaashrin ein kosvin* — because if writing these texts were too profitable an enterprise, the scribes would stop writing. If the work became so lucrative that it attracted people who would then move on to more profitable ventures, or if the cost became prohibitive, the supply of *Sifrei Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot* would dry up. It was therefore a kind of divine decree, emerging from the prayers of the greatest sages, that this work remain modestly compensated — affordable and constantly available.

### Sacred Text Sellers

The *braisa* extends the category: *kosvei Sfarim Tfillin u-Mezuzos, hein ve-tagreihen ve-tagrei tagreihen* — the scribes themselves, their wholesalers, and their retailers. And *kol ha-oskim bimelechet Shamayim* — all who occupy themselves in this heavenly work, with *laasuyei mochrei Techeiles* — including those who sell *techeiles*, the sacred blue dye used for *tzitzit*. All of these, like the scribes, will never see a sign of blessing — not because they are doing something wrong, but because the enterprise is, by divine design, kept from becoming too profitable.

### Lishma Exception

The *braisa* concludes with a redemptive qualification: *ve-im oskin lishma, ro'im* — if they engage in this work *leshem Shamayim*, for the sake of Heaven rather than for profit, then they *will* see a sign of blessing. The reason there is no *siman beracha* for those who do it for the money is precisely so they will not stop producing these sacred objects. But one who does it *lishma* — whose motivation is not financial gain but genuine devotion to making Torah, *tefillin*, *mezuzot*, and *techeiles* available — such a person will, even if they become successful, continue to do it. For them, prosperity need not undermine the work, and so for them the divine concern that inspired the twenty-four fasts does not apply. With this observation — that *lishma* transforms not only the spiritual value of an act but even its material outcomes — the fourth *perek* begins.
