# Sotah 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meshauach Milchama

### Hebrew Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁמְּדַבֵּר אֶל הָעָם — בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ הָיָה מְדַבֵּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה כְּקָרׇבְכֶם אֶל הַמִּלְחָמָה וְנִגַּשׁ הַכֹּהֵן״ — זֶה כֹּהֵן מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה, ״וְדִבֶּר אֶל הָעָם״ — בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The priest who was **anointed for war, at the time that he** would **speak to the nation, he would speak** to them **in the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, **as it is stated: "And it shall be, when you draw near to the battle, that the priest shall approach** and speak to the people" (Deuteronomy 20:2). **This** priest identified in the verse is **the priest anointed for war,** the priest who is inaugurated specifically to serve this function. **"And speak to the people";** he addresses them **in the sacred tongue,** Hebrew.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְדִבֶּר״, וּלְהַלָּן אוֹמֵר ״מֹשֶׁה יְדַבֵּר וְהָאֱלֹהִים יַעֲנֶנּוּ בְקוֹל״, מָה לְהַלָּן בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ — אַף כָּאן בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara clarifies an ambiguous point in the mishna: **What is it saying** when the mishna derives from the verse that the priest must address the people in Hebrew? The Gemara answers: **This is what** the mishna **is saying:** The ruling is derived from a verbal analogy, **as it is stated** here: **"And speak"** (Deuteronomy 20:2), **and there,** concerning the giving of the Torah, **it is stated: "Moses spoke and God answered him by a voice"** (Exodus 19:19). **Just as there,** the voice spoke **in the sacred tongue, so too here,** the priest speaks **in the sacred tongue.**

The derivation proceeds from the verse *v'hayah k'korovchem el hamilchama v'nigash ha-kohen v'diber el ha-am* — when you draw close to battle, the *kohen* shall draw near and speak to the people. The *Gemara* then links this *dibbur* to another instance of the same root elsewhere, in the context of the giving of the Torah: *Moshe yedaber veha-Elokim ya'anenu be-kol* — and just as that speech was in *lashon ha-kodesh*, so too this one. The *Gemara* acknowledges that there are many instances of *dibber* throughout the Torah, and *Tosafot* engages with this directly. The *Yerushalmi* formulation runs as follows: why does the word *v'dibber* alone establish Hebrew? After all, *keri'at shema* also uses the related form *ve-dibarta bam*, yet it may be recited in any language. The *Yerushalmi* answers that what is decisive is the combination of *dibber* and *amirah* together — *v'diber ha-kohen v'amar* — and then proceeds to distinguish the case of *viduy ma'aser*, which also contains a form of *amirah* yet may be recited in any language. Rabbi Chagai then offers the alternative derivation through a *gezeirah shavah* of *hagashah*: *v'nigshu ha-kohanim* corresponds to another instance of *hagashah* spoken in *lashon ha-kodesh*, and therefore here too the language must be Hebrew.

The underlying logic may be that there is a spiritual power to the use of the sacred tongue, particularly when one is invoking divine protection. In any case, the requirement stands.

### Brothers vs Enemies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאָמַר אֲלֵיהֶם שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳ עַל אוֹיְבֵיכֶם״ — וְלֹא עַל אֲחֵיכֶם, לֹא יְהוּדָה עַל שִׁמְעוֹן וְלָא שִׁמְעוֹן עַל בִּנְיָמִין. שֶׁאִם תִּפְּלוּ בְּיָדָם יְרַחֲמוּ עֲלֵיכֶם, כְּמָה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּקֻמוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר נִקְּבוּ בְשֵׁמוֹת וַיַּחֲזִיקוּ בַשִּׁבְיָה וְכׇל מַעֲרֻמֵּיהֶם הִלְבִּישׁוּ מִן הַשָּׁלָל וַיַּלְבִּישׁוּם וַיַּנְעִלוּם וַיַּאֲכִלוּם וַיַּשְׁקוּם וַיְסֻכוּם וַיְנַהֲלוּם בַּחֲמֹרִים לְכׇל כּוֹשֵׁל וַיְבִיאוּם יְרֵחוֹ עִיר הַתְּמָרִים אֵצֶל אֲחֵיהֶם וַיָּשׁוּבוּ שֹׁמְרוֹן וְגוֹ׳״. עַל אוֹיְבֵיכֶם אַתֶּם הוֹלְכִים, שֶׁאִם תִּפְּלוּ בְּיָדָם אֵין מְרַחֲמִין עֲלֵיכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah dictates the priest's address: **"And** he **shall say to them: Hear Israel,** you draw near today to battle against your enemies; let not your heart faint; fear not, nor be alarmed, and do not be terrified of them" (Deuteronomy 20:3). The priest expounds: **"Against your enemies" and not against your brothers.** This is **not** a war of the tribe of **Judah against Simon and not Simon against Benjamin,** such **that if you fall into their hands** your brothers **will have mercy on you, as it is stated** with regard to a war between Judah and Israel: **"And the men that have been mentioned by name rose up, and took the captives, and with the spoil clothed all that were naked among them, and arrayed them, and shod them, and gave them to eat and to drink, and anointed them, and carried all the feeble of them upon donkeys, and brought them to Jericho, the city of palm trees, unto their brethren; then they returned to Samaria"** (II Chronicles 28:15). Rather, **you are marching** to war **against your enemies, and if you fall into their hands, they** will **not have mercy on you.**

The *meshiach milchama* begins his address with the words *Shema Yisrael — atem k'revim ha-yom el ha-milchama al oyveichem*: you are drawing close today against your enemies. The emphasis on *al oyveichem* — against your *enemies* — is deliberate. He continues: not against your brothers, not Yehudah against Shimon or Shimon against Binyamin. This is a distinction that carries real weight, because internecine conflict did occur during the pre-monarchic period and even afterward. The reasoning given is *she-im tiplu b'yadam yerachamu aleichem* — if you were to fall in the hands of your brothers, they would have compassion on you. By contrast, these are your enemies, and if you fall in their hands they will have no mercy. So the address begins by clarifying the stakes: this is not a civil war with its implicit safety net; this is war against those who will show no quarter.

### Pekach & Oded

The proof-text for the brotherly compassion argument comes from the incident of *Pekach ben Remalyahu*, as summarized by *Rashi* at the end of the *perek*. *Pekach*, king of the northern kingdom of *Yisrael*, waged war against *Achaz* of *Yehudah* and took many captives from *Bnei Yehudah*. A prophet named *Oded* went out before the returning army of *Yisrael* and declared: God was angry at *Bnei Yehudah* and therefore delivered them into your hands — but you have smitten them without restraint, and now you intend to enslave them? *Hashivu et ha-shivya* — return the captives.

The *pasuk* then relates: *va-yakumu ha-anashim asher nikre'u vi-shemot va-yachaziku ba-shivya* — those who were designated by name took hold of the captives, clothed the naked from the booty, shod them, fed them, gave them drink, anointed them, placed the ones who could not walk on donkeys, and brought them to *Yericho*, city of palm trees, to their brethren — and then returned to *Shomron*. This is what happens when you fall among brothers. The *meshiach milchama* invokes this episode to sharpen the contrast: your current enemy will not treat you this way.

### Courage Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל יֵרַךְ לְבַבְכֶם אַל תִּירְאוּ וְאַל תַּחְפְּזוּ וְגוֹ׳״. ״אַל יֵרַךְ לְבַבְכֶם״ — מִפְּנֵי צׇהֳלַת סוּסִים וְצִחְצוּחַ חֲרָבוֹת, ״אַל תִּירְאוּ״ — מִפְּנֵי הַגָּפַת תְּרִיסִין וְשִׁפְעַת הַקַּלְגַּסִּין, ״אַל תַּחְפְּזוּ״ — מִקּוֹל קְרָנוֹת, ״אַל תַּעַרְצוּ״ — מִפְּנֵי קוֹל צְווֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The priest continues: **"Let not your heart faint; fear not, nor be alarmed,** and do not be terrified of them" (Deuteronomy 20:3). **"Let not your heart faint" due to the neighing of horses and the sharpening of** the enemy's **swords. "Fear not" due to the knocking of shields [*terisin*] and the noise of** their **boots [*calgassin*]. "Nor be alarmed" by the sound of trumpets. "Do not be terrified" due to the sound of shouts.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם הַהֹלֵךְ עִמָּכֶם״. הֵם בָּאִין בְּנִצְחוֹנוֹ שֶׁל בָּשָׂר וָדָם, וְאַתֶּם בָּאִים בְּנִצְחוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם. פְּלִשְׁתִּים בָּאוּ בְּנִצְחוֹנוֹ שֶׁל גׇּלְיָת, מָה הָיָה סוֹפוֹ — לְסוֹף נָפַל בַּחֶרֶב וְנָפְלוּ עִמּוֹ. בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן בָּאוּ בְּנִצְחוֹנוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹבַךְ, מָה הָיָה סוֹפוֹ — לְסוֹף נָפַל בַּחֶרֶב וְנָפְלוּ עִמּוֹ. וְאַתֶּם אִי אַתֶּם כֵּן, ״כִּי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם הַהֹלֵךְ עִמָּכֶם לְהִלָּחֵם לָכֶם וְגוֹ׳״ — זֶה מַחֲנֵה הָאָרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The priest explains why the soldiers need not be terrified. **"For the Lord your God is He that goes with you,** to fight for you against your enemies, to save you" (Deuteronomy 20:4). Remember that **they come** to war **championed by flesh and blood, and you are coming championed by the Omnipresent. The Philistines came championed by Goliath. What was his end? In the end, he fell by the sword, and they fell with him** (see I Samuel, chapter 17). **The Ammonites came championed by Shobach. What was his end? In the end, he fell by the sword, and they fell with him** (see II Samuel, chapter 10). **But** as for **you, you are not so,** reliant upon the strength of mortals: **"For the Lord your God is He that goes with you, to fight for you** against your enemies, to save you"; **this** verse is referring to the **camp of the Ark** of the Covenant that accompanies them out to war.

Having made the soldiers aware of exactly what they face, the *meshiach milchama* turns to fortifying their resolve. *Al yerach levavchem v'al tira'u v'al tachpezu v'al ta'artzu* — do not let your heart grow faint, do not fear, do not tremble, do not be terrified. He then enumerates the specific psychological weapons the enemy will deploy: the braying of the horses, the clashing of swords, the clamoring of shields — *Rashi* explains that armies would deliberately bang their shields together to spread fear among the opposing side — the hordes of enemy soldiers, and the blasting of horns. Each of these is a form of psychological warfare, and the address names them explicitly so the soldiers know what to expect.

The cure for all of it is theological: *ki Hashem Elokeichem holech imachem* — God is going with you. The address continues: *hem ba'im be-nitzchono shel basar va-dam* — they come with the strength of flesh and blood, *ve-atem ba'im be-nitzchono shel Makom* — and you come with the strength of God. Two examples are then marshaled. The Philistines came with the valor of Goliath — *mah hayah sofo?* — what was his end? *U-lesof nafal be-cherev* — he fell by the sword, and his people fell with him. The Ammonites came with the valor of *Shovach* — what was his end? He too fell by the sword, and his nation with him. But you are not like them, for God goes with you to fight for you, you come with the *aron*, and you will be victorious.

What is striking about this formulation is that the address does not minimize the threat. The soldier is told precisely how terrible his enemy is, and precisely how little mercy he can expect. The courage comes not from illusion but from faith. The *meshiach milchama* strengthens hearts not by misrepresenting the danger, but by orienting the army toward the source of genuine power.

### Kohen Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנִגַּשׁ הַכֹּהֵן וְדִבֶּר אֶל הָעָם״. יָכוֹל כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְדִבְּרוּ הַשֹּׁטְרִים״, מָה שׁוֹטְרִים בִּמְמוּנֶּה, אַף כֹּהֵן בִּמְמוּנֶּה. וְאֵימָא כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל? דּוּמְיָא דְּשׁוֹטֵר, מָה שׁוֹטֵר שֶׁיֵּשׁ מְמוּנֶּה עַל גַּבָּיו — אַף כֹּהֵן שֶׁיֵּשׁ מְמוּנֶּה עַל גַּבָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "The priest shall approach and speak to the people"** (Deuteronomy 20:2). One **might** have thought that **any priest who would want** to address the people may assume this role. To counter this idea, **the verse states: "And the officers shall speak"** (Deuteronomy 20:5). **Just as the officers** described are those who have been **appointed** to discharge their responsibilities, **so too, the priest** described is one who has been **appointed** for this role. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, why not **say** that **the High Priest** should deliver this address, as he is also appointed? The Gemara replies: The appointed priest must be **similar to** an appointed **officer. Just as an officer** is one **who has** someone else, i.e., a judge, with greater authority **appointed above him, so too, the priest** described must be one **who has** someone else, i.e., the High Priest, **appointed above him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל נָמֵי, הָאִיכָּא מֶלֶךְ עַל גַּבָּיו! בַּעֲבוֹדָתוֹ קָאָמַר. וְאֵימָא סְגָן! סְגָן לָאו מְמוּנֶּה הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכּוֹהֲנִים: לָמָּה סְגָן מְמוּנֶּה — שֶׁאִם אֵירַע בּוֹ פְּסוּל בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, נִכְנָס וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ תַּחְתָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this answer: **The High Priest also** meets this qualification, as **there is the king above him,** and therefore, the High Priest should deliver the address. The Gemara clarifies: The caveat that one must have someone appointed above him **is saying** someone above him **in his function.** That there is a king is irrelevant to the station of the High Priest, who ranks highest in the priesthood. The Gemara suggests: **Then say** it may be **the deputy** High Priest, who is second to the High Priest; he should deliver the address. The Gemara answers: **The deputy is not an appointed** office, as this position has no particular function other than being a ready substitute for the High Priest. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy** High **Priest, says: To what** end is **the deputy appointed?** It is merely for the possibility **that if some disqualification befalls the High Priest,** the deputy **steps in and serves in his stead.** However, the deputy has no specific role of his own.

The *Gemara* now asks: *yachol kol kohen she-yirtzeh* — might any *kohen* who wishes step forward and deliver this address? After all, the verse does not specify a particular appointment. The *Gemara* answers from a *kal va-chomer* or analogy to the *shotrim*, the officers who speak afterward (*mi ha-ish ha-yarei ve-rach ha-levav — yeilech ve-yashuv le-veito* and so on): just as the *shotrim* hold formally appointed positions — presumably appointed by the *shofetim*, the judges they serve — so too the *kohen* who speaks must be someone specifically appointed to that role. *Mah shotrim bi-memunah — af kohen bi-memunah*.

One might then suggest that it must be the *kohen gadol*. But this is rejected by analogy to the *shoter* as well: just as the *shoter* is appointed yet has someone above him in the hierarchy, so the *kohen* here must be someone appointed yet with a superior over him. The *kohen gadol* does have the king above him in certain respects — as we have seen, the king hands the *Sefer Torah* to the *kohen gadol* at *Hakhel* — but within the realm of *avodah*, of the Temple service itself, no one outranks the *kohen gadol*. The comparison to a *shoter* who has a *shofet* over him therefore does not fit the *kohen gadol* in that domain. The *meshiach milchama* must thus be someone appointed to this specific task yet subordinate to the *kohen gadol*.

### Sgan HaKohanim

The natural candidate would then seem to be the *sgan* — the deputy or vice *kohen gadol*. But the *Gemara* rejects this too: *sgan lav memunah hu* — the *sgan* does not hold the kind of appointment that qualifies here. The *Gemara* supports this from a *baraita*: Rabbi Chanina, who was himself the *sgan ha-kohanim*, states that the sole purpose of the *sgan*'s appointment is *she-im yei'ara bo psul be-kohen gadol — nichnas u-meshamesh tachtav* — if the *kohen gadol* becomes disqualified or dies, the *sgan* enters and serves in his place. He is the vice president. He has no independent mandate beyond standing ready to replace the *kohen gadol* when necessary.

*Tosafot* raises an interesting difficulty here. A *baraita* in *Sanhedrin* states that when the *kohen gadol* was receiving mourners, *ha-sgan u-meshiach milchama omed bi-ymino* — the *sgan* and the *meshiach milchama* would stand to his right. This implies that the *sgan* does play some active role alongside the *kohen gadol*, rather than simply waiting for him to die. *Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between rabbinic and biblical appointment. The *sgan* was certainly given certain tasks by his fellow *kohanim* — there is a *mishnah* that describes the *memuneh* giving instructions, and there are various roles assigned. But these were internal arrangements made by the *kohanim* among themselves, similar to how they might appoint a *markal* (overseer) or a *gizbar* (treasurer). What the *sgan* lacks is a *biblically recognized status* of appointment that would make him the *kohen* who speaks before battle. His only Torah-level appointment is to be ready to replace the *kohen gadol*; everything else is institutional arrangement, not a recognized *minui* from the Torah itself. The *meshiach milchama* must therefore be a distinct appointment — someone designated specifically for this function.

## David & Goliath

### Shema Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאָמַר אֲלֵיהֶם שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״. מַאי שְׁנָא ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי, אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא קִיַּימְתֶּם אֶלָּא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית — אִי אַתֶּם נִמְסָרִין בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Torah says about the priest anointed for war: **"And he shall say to them: Hear Israel"** (Deuteronomy 20:3). The Gemara asks: **What is different** in this setting that necessitates the usage of the phrase: **"Hear Israel"? Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the Jewish people: Even if you have not fulfilled** any mitzva **except reciting *Shema* of the morning and the evening, you** will **not be delivered into the hands** of your enemies. As an allusion to this promise, the priest's address borrows the phrase "Hear Israel," a phrase most familiar from the beginning of the recitation of *Shema*.

The *Gemara* pauses on the opening words of the *meshiach milchama*'s battlefield address — *Shema Yisrael* — and asks: *mai shna Shema Yisrael?* Why specifically this formulation, so resonant with the declaration of God's unity? Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai explains: God says to *Yisrael* — even if you have fulfilled nothing other than *keri'at shema* of the morning and evening, you will not be delivered into their hands. The *zechus* of reciting *Shema Yisrael* twice daily is sufficient protection. This is a notably accessible guarantee — not dependent on extraordinary merit, but on the most basic and universal Jewish practice.

## Two Addresses

### Timing & Content

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל יֵרַךְ לְבַבְכֶם אַל תִּירְאוּ כּוּ׳״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פַּעֲמַיִם מְדַבֵּר עִמָּהוּ, אַחַת בַּסְּפָר, וְאַחַת בַּמִּלְחָמָה. בַּסְּפָר מַה הוּא אוֹמֵר —
>
> **Translation:** With respect to the mishna's comments about the verse: **"Let not your heart faint; fear not,** nor be alarmed, and do not be terrified of them" (Deuteronomy 20:3), **the Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 7:18): The priest **speaks with them twice, one** time when they are gathered for war **at the border, and one** time when they are **on the battlefield** itself. When they are **at the border, what does he say?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁמְעוּ דִּבְרֵי מַעַרְכֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה, וְחִזְרוּ. בַּמִּלְחָמָה מָה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אַל יֵרַךְ לְבַבְכֶם אַל תִּירְאוּ וְאַל תַּחְפְּזוּ וְאַל תַּעַרְצוּ״. כְּנֶגֶד אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם עוֹשִׂין: מְגִיפִין, וּמְרִיעִין, צוֹוְחִין, וְרוֹמְסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Hear my words, the regulations of war,** and consider who is fit to participate in the battle. **And return** home, all of you who are exempt from combat. **What does he say on the battlefield? "Let not your heart faint; fear not, nor be alarmed, and do not be terrified of them"** (Deuteronomy 20:3). These four cautions **correspond** to **four actions done by the nations of the world:** They **clash** their weapons, **and** they **blast** horns, they **shout, and** they **trample** heavily with their horses to frighten their adversaries.

The *Gemara* now establishes that the *kohen* spoke to the people not once but twice. *Pa'amayim medaber imahem* — he would address them on two separate occasions. The first was *ba-sfar* — at the border city, before the army had actually left the land of *Yisrael* proper. The second was *ba-milchama* — at the battlefield itself, immediately before engagement. At the border, the content was essentially administrative: *shimu divrei ma'archei milchama ve-chazru* — hear what the officers are telling you about who is eligible to return, and return accordingly. That is, the *shotrim* would there enumerate the categories of those who should go home — one who has built a house and not dedicated it, betrothed a woman and not yet married her, planted a vineyard and not redeemed it, and the one who is fearful and soft-hearted.

The address at the battlefield itself was the courage speech: *al yerach levavchem al tira'u v'al tachpezu v'al ta'artzu*. This sequence — first sending home those who should not be there, then inspiring those who remain — is logically the reverse of the order in which the verses appear in the Torah. The Torah presents the encouragement of the *kohen* before the *shotrim* announce the exemption categories. The *Gemara* does not comment directly on this discrepancy but it is evident that the practical sequence makes more sense: eliminate the fearful first, and deliver the courage speech to those who are actually going into battle.

### Four Tactics

The four expressions of fear — *al yerach*, *al tira'u*, *al tachpezu*, *al ta'artzu* — correspond to four specific things that enemy armies would do in order to break the morale of the opposing force. They would clang their shields (*magifim*); they would blast the horns (*meri'im*); they would let out war cries (*tzovchim*); and they would trample forward with their armies (*romsim*). Each phrase in the *kohen*'s address is a counter to one of these tactics, reassuring the soldiers against each specific form of psychological warfare.

## David & Goliath…

### Goliath Brazenness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִשְׁתִּים בָּאוּ בְּנִצְחוֹנוֹ שֶׁל גׇּלְיָת כּוּ׳. גׇּלְיָת, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁעָמַד בְּגִילּוּי פָּנִים לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּרוּ לָכֶם אִישׁ וְיֵרֵד אֵלָי״, וְאֵין ״אִישׁ״ אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה״. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הֲרֵינִי מַפִּילוֹ עַל יַד בֶּן אִישׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדָוִד בֶּן אִישׁ אֶפְרָתִי הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna recorded the particulars of the priest's address: **The Philistines came championed by Goliath.** The Gemara describes the battle between David and Goliath. What is implied by the name **Goliath? Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The verse indicates **that he stood before the Holy One, Blessed be He, with brazenness [*gilui panim*], as it is stated: "Choose yourselves a man [*ish*], and let him come down to me"** (I Samuel 17:8), and **man [*ish*]** is referring to **none other** than **the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "The Lord is a man [*ish*] of war"** (Exodus 15:3). **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I will hereby fell him by the son of a man [*ben ish*], as it is stated: "Now David was the son of that man [*ben ish*] of Ephrath"** (I Samuel 17:12).

Rabbi Yochanan offers a *drasha* on the name *Golyat* itself: *she-amad be-giluy panim* — he stood with brazenness before God. The source is his challenge: *baru lachem ish v'yered elai* — choose for yourselves a man and let him come down against me. The *Gemara* reads this as a direct affront to the divine, since *v'ein ish ela Ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — the word *ish* in certain contexts refers specifically to God, as in the verse *Hashem ish milchama*, God is a man of war. Goliath was therefore not merely challenging the army of Israel; he was effectively saying: send down your God and I will fight Him. God's response: *hareini mapilo al yedei ben ish* — I will bring him down through the hands of a *ben ish*, a son of a man, a mere youth — as in the verse *ve-David ben ish Ephrati*, David son of a man of Ephrat. The wordplay is pointed: Goliath invoked *ish* as a challenge to God, and it is through a *ben ish* that he is undone.

### Self-Trapping Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת לְכָדוֹ פִּיו לְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע. אֶחָד: ״בְּרוּ לָכֶם אִישׁ וְיֵרֵד אֵלָי״, וְאִידַּךְ: ״אִם יוּכַל לְהִלָּחֵם אִתִּי וְהִכָּנִי וְגוֹ׳״. וְאִידַּךְ, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ לְדָוִד: ״הֲכֶלֶב אָנֹכִי כִּי אַתָּה בָא אֵלַי בַּמַּקְלוֹת״. דָּוִד נָמֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״אַתָּה בָּא אֵלַי בְּחֶרֶב וּבַחֲנִית וּבְכִידוֹן״! הֲדַר אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְאָנֹכִי בָא אֵלֶיךָ בְּשֵׁם ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אֱלֹהֵי (יִשְׂרָאֵל) מַעַרְכוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר חֵרַפְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Meir: In three instances, his** own **mouth ensnared that wicked one** and unwittingly foretold his own downfall. **One** time he said: **"Choose yourselves a man, and let him come down to me,"** describing himself at the bottom. **And another** time, he said: **"If he is able to fight with me and kill me** then will we be your servants; but if I prevail against him, and kill him, then shall you be our servants, and serve us" (I Samuel 17:9). There, he supposed that his opponent would defeat him, before supposing that he, Goliath, would be victorious. Finally, **the other** time was **when he said to David** (I Samuel 17:43): **"Am I a dog, that you come to me with staves?"** The Gemara asks: But didn't David also speak in this manner? **David also said to him: "You come to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with a javelin"** (I Samuel 17:45). The Gemara answers: David **then said to him,** immediately afterward: **"But I come to you in the name of the Lord of hosts, the God of the armies of Israel, whom you have taunted"** (I Samuel 17:45).

Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Meir identifies three specific moments where Goliath's own words sealed his fate. First, *baru lachem ish v'yered elai* — the word *v'yered*, let him come down, implies a descent upon Goliath, a conquest. Second, *im yuchal le-hilachem iti ve-hikani* — if he will be able to fight me and smite me; even phrased conditionally, Goliath articulated his own defeat. Third, his taunt to David — *ha-kelev anochi ki ata ba elai be-maklot* — am I a dog that you come to me with sticks? — ultimately described how he would be beaten, like a dog.

One might ask: David also makes a forceful declaration — *ata ba elai be-cherev u-ve-chanit u-ve-kidon* — you come to me with sword and spear and javelin — does this not similarly describe David being attacked? The *Gemara* answers no, because David immediately continues: *ve-anochi ba eleicha be-shem Hashem Tzeva'ot elokei ma'archot Yisrael* — but I am coming against you in the name of God. David's statement is not a prediction of his own defeat; it is precisely the declaration the *meshiach milchama* would make — we come in the name of God. The contrast between their respective sources of power is the entire point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּגַּשׁ הַפְּלִשְׁתִּי הַשְׁכֵּם וְהַעֲרֵב״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּדֵי לְבַטְּלָן מִקְּרִיאַת שְׁמַע שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית. ״וַיִּתְיַצַּב אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּנֶגֶד אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם שֶׁנִּתְּנָה בָּהֶן תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse says: **"And the Philistine drew near morning and evening"** (I Samuel 17:16). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He did this specifically **in order to prevent them from** completing **the recitation of *Shema*** in the required times of **morning and evening. "And** Goliath **presented himself forty days"** (I Samuel 17:16). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** These days **correspond** to **the forty days over which the Torah was given,** as he wanted to do away with it.

The connection to the earlier theme is explicit: Goliath would come out *ha-shakem ve-ha'arev* — early in the morning and in the evening — precisely at the times of *keri'at shema shacharit ve-arvit*, in order to prevent Israel from reciting *Shema*. This echoes Rabbi Yochanan's earlier teaching: the *zechus* of *Shema* is what protects in battle, and Goliath knew exactly what to undermine.

### Beinayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּצֵא אִישׁ הַבֵּינַיִם מִמַּחֲנוֹת פְּלִשְׁתִּים וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי ״בֵּינַיִם״? אָמַר רַב: שֶׁמְּבוּנֶּה מִכׇּל מוּם. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בֵּינוֹנִי שֶׁבְּאֶחָיו. דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמַר: שֶׁהוּא עָשׂוּי כְּבִנְיַן. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בַּר מְאָה פָּפֵי וַחֲדָא נָאנָאי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse introduces Goliath: **"And a champion [*ish habeinayim*] went out from the camp of the Philistines,** named Goliath" (I Samuel 17:4). The Gemara asks: **What** is indicated by the term ***beinayim*? Rav said:** The word is related to the root *beit*, *nun*, *heh*, meaning build, and means **that he is built [*muvneh*]** perfectly and free **of any blemish. And Shmuel said:** The word is related to the word *bein*, meaning between, and means that he was **the middle [*beinoni*] among his brothers.** A Sage **from the school of Rabbi Sheila said:** The word is related to the root *beit*, *nun*, *heh*, meaning build, and means **that he** was **made** strong **as a building [*binyan*]. Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The word is related to the word *bein*, meaning between, and means that he was born from among many, as follows: He was **the son of one hundred fathers [*pappi*] and one dog [*nanai*],** as his mother engaged in sexual intercourse with one hundred men and a dog, and he was fathered from among them.

The *pasuk* describes Goliath as *ish ha-beinayim* — a man of the *beinayim*. The *Gemara* explores several interpretations of this term. Rav says *she-mevuneh mi-kol mum* — he was perfectly constructed, without any blemish or defect. Shmuel says *beinoni she-b'achav* — he was merely the middling one among his brothers; if you think Goliath was large, you should see his older brothers. The school of Rabbi Shila says *she-hu asuy ke-vinyan* — he was built like a building, massive and solid. And Rabbi Yochanan says *bar me'ah papi ve-chada nani* — he had a hundred fathers and one mother.

*Rashi* elaborates on this last reading: *ben ha-ta'arovet* — a son of mixture — because many men came upon his mother in a single night and she conceived from one of them. One is the biological father and all the others are adulterers. *Rashi* adds that *nani* is the word for father in Persian. The *drasha* on *beinayim* — from the root *bein*, between — thus suggests he emerged from *bein anashim harbei*, from among many men. *Rav Yosef* adds in the same vein: *she-ha-kol dashim et imo ke-gat* — everyone trampled his mother like a *gat*, a winepress. The word *me'aros* in the phrase *mime'aros Plishtim* is read *ma'archos*, and *she-ha-kol he'eru be-imo* — everyone performed the act of *giluy arayot* with his mother. The portrait being drawn is of a man of illegitimate and sordid origins, despite his fearsome appearance.

### Harpa/Arpa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״הָרָפָה״, וּכְתִיב ״עׇרְפָּה״, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל חַד אָמַר: ״הָרָפָה״ שְׁמָהּ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״עׇרְפָּה״ — שֶׁהַכֹּל עוֹרְפִין אוֹתָהּ מֵאַחֲרֶיהָ, וְחַד אָמַר: ״עׇרְפָּה״ שְׁמָהּ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״הָרָפָה״ — שֶׁהַכֹּל דָּשִׁין אוֹתָהּ כְּהָרִיפוֹת, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַתִּקַּח הָאִשָּׁה וְתִפְרֹשׁ הַמָּסָךְ עַל פְּנֵי הַבְּאֵר וַתִּשְׁטַח עָלָיו הָרִפוֹת״. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״אִם תִּכְתּוֹשׁ אֶת הָאֱוִיל בַּמַּכְתֵּשׁ בְּתוֹךְ הָרִיפוֹת בַּעֱלִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is written** that Goliath's mother was: **"Harafa"** (II Samuel 21:16), **and** in another place **it is written: "Orpah"** (Ruth 1:4), and the Gemara will soon explain that this was the same woman. **Rav and Shmuel** engaged in a dispute concerning this matter. **One** of them **said: Her name was Harafa, and why is she called** by the **name Orpah?** It is **because everyone came at her from behind [*orfin*] her,** i.e., sodomized her. **And one** of them **said: Her name was Orpah, and why is she called** by the **name Harafa?** It is **because everyone threshed her like groats [*harifot*],** i.e., engaged in sexual intercourse with her, **and so it says** that this word means groats: **"And the woman took and spread the covering over the well's mouth, and strewed groats [*harifot*] thereon"** (II Samuel 17:19). **And if you wish,** you can **say from here: "Though you should crush a fool in a mortar with a pestle among groats [*harifot*],** yet will not his foolishness depart from him" (Proverbs 27:22).

A later *pasuk* in *Sefer Shmuel* refers to four sons born *le-Harpa be-Gat* — to *Harpa* in *Gat*. These four, who fell in battle against David and his servants, appear to be Goliath's brothers, and *Harpa* is their mother. Yet elsewhere her name appears as *Arpa*. This *Arpa* is, of course, known from the story of *Rut* — she is the sister-in-law of *Rut* who kissed her mother-in-law *Na'omi* and turned back, rather than accompanying her to the land of *Yisrael*. The *Gemara* now draws the connection between the two.

*Rav u-Shmuel* debate the two names. One holds that her name was *Harpa*, and she was called *Arpa* because *she-ha-kol orfin otah me-achoreiha* — everyone would approach her from behind, the word *oref* denoting the back of the neck. The other holds that her name was *Arpa*, and she was called *Harpa* because *she-ha-kol dashim otah ka-arifot* — everyone trampled her like *harifot*, the crushed or milled grain. Two *pesukim* are adduced in support of the *harifot* reading: the woman who spread a cloth over the pit and scattered *arifot* upon it as a trap (*va-tishpach aleha ha-arifot*), and the verse *im tichtosh et ha-evil ba-machtesh be-toch ha-arifot* — if the foolish one is ground up in the mortar among the grain. Both images carry the sense of something being violently broken down. The point in all of these interpretations is consistent: the mother of these giants was a woman of profound immorality, and her sons' enormous physical power is in inverse proportion to the legitimacy of their birth.

### Four Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת אַרְבַּעַת אֵלֶּה יֻלְּדוּ לְהָרָפָה בְּגַת וַיִּפְּלוּ בְיַד דָּוִד וּבְיַד עֲבָדָיו״. מַאי נִינְהוּ? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: סַף וּמָדוֹן גׇּלְיָת וְיִשְׁבִּי בְּנוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of the battle of David and Goliath. **"These four were born to Harafa in Gath; and they fell by the hand of David, and by the hand of his servants"** (II Samuel 21:22). The Gemara asks: **What** are the names of the four siblings mentioned here? **Rav Ḥisda said:** They are **Saph, and Madon, Goliath, and Ishbi in Nob** (see II Samuel 21:16–20).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּפְּלוּ בְיַד דָּוִד וּבְיַד עֲבָדָיו״, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתִּשַּׁק עׇרְפָּה לַחֲמוֹתָהּ וְרוּת דָּבְקָה בָּהּ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: יָבוֹאוּ בְּנֵי הַנְּשׁוּקָה, וְיִפְּלוּ בְּיַד בְּנֵי הַדְּבוּקָה. דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: בִּשְׂכַר אַרְבַּע דְּמָעוֹת שֶׁהוֹרִידָה עׇרְפָּה עַל חֲמוֹתָהּ — זָכְתָה וְיָצְאוּ מִמֶּנָּה אַרְבָּעָה גִּבּוֹרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתִּשֶּׂנָה קוֹלָן וַתִּבְכֶּינָה עוֹד״,
>
> **Translation:** It says: **"And they fell into the hands of David and his servants."** Why? It is because of the acts of their forebears, **as it is written: "And Orpah kissed her mother-in-law, and Ruth cleaved to her"** (Ruth 1:14). **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: The children of the one who kissed,** referring to the four giants descended from Orpah, **will come and fall into the hand of the children of the one who cleaved,** referring to David, who was descended from Ruth. **Rava taught: As a reward for the four tears that Orpah shed** in sadness **over her mother-in-law, she merited four mighty warriors descended from her, as it is stated: "And they lifted up their voice and wept again"** (Ruth 1:14).

*Rav Chisda* identifies the four sons born to *Harpa* in *Gat*: *Saph u-Madon Golyat ve-Yishbi ve-Nov* — names that appear in the text of the *pesukim* themselves. Each fell *be-yad David u-ve-yad avadav* — at the hands of David and his servants in separate engagements. The connection to *Rut* is now drawn explicitly: *de-kasiv va-tishak Arpa la-chamotah ve-Rut davkah bah* — *Orpah* kissed her mother-in-law and *Rut* cleaved to her. Rabbi Yitzchak states in the name of God, as it were: *yavo benei ha-neshukah* — let the children of the one who gave the goodbye kiss come forth, *ve-yippelu be-yad benei ha-devukah* — and let them fall at the hands of the children of the one who cleaved. The symmetry is striking and purposeful: the choice *Orpah* made on the road to *Yisrael* — the kiss of farewell — determined the fate of her descendants.

*Rava* adds a dimension of nuance to *Orpah*'s story. *Be-schar arba d'maot she-horida Orpah al chamotah* — in the reward of the four tears that *Orpah* shed for her mother-in-law, *zachta ve-yatza mimenah arba'ah gibborim* — she merited that four mighty warriors emerged from her. The four tears are derived from the verse *va-tisenah kolam va-tivkena od* — *Rashi* understands this as weeping from both eyes, making two, doubled by the word *od* to four. *Orpah* was not simply a wicked person; she wept genuinely for *Na'omi* and accompanied her a significant distance before turning back. Her failing was not cruelty but a lesser degree of self-sacrifice than *Rut*. And so she received a reward — four sons of extraordinary physical power — even as those sons ultimately fell at the hands of David, the descendant of *Rut*, *Orpah*'s counterpart.

### Praising Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״חֵץ חֲנִיתוֹ״, וְקָרֵינַן ״עֵץ חֲנִיתוֹ״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֲדַיִן לֹא הִגִּיעָנוּ לַחֲצִי שִׁבְחוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע. מִכָּאן שֶׁאָסוּר לְסַפֵּר בְּשִׁבְחָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים. וְלָא לִפְתַּח בֵּיהּ כְּלָל! לְאוֹדוֹעֵי שְׁבָחֵיהּ דְּדָוִד.
>
> **Translation:** **It is written** about Goliath: "And **the half [*ḥetz*] of his spear** was like a weaver's beam" (I Samuel 17:7), **and we read,** according to the Masoretic tradition: "And **the shaft [*etz*] of his spear." Rabbi Elazar says:** The written version of the text demonstrates that **we have not yet reached half [*ḥetzi*] of the praise of that wicked** man. Only half of his spear was as long as a weaver's beam, but the Masoretic reading offers a less impressive description. It is learned **from here that it is prohibited to relate the praise of wicked people.** The Gemara asks: If so, **then** the verse **should not begin** by praising **him at all.** The Gemara answers: It was necessary in this case in order **to relate the praise of David,** who defeated Goliath.

The *Gemara* notes a textual detail about Goliath's description: *k'siv chetz chanito ve-karinan etz chanito* — the written form of the text says *chetz*, the arrow of his spear, while the read form (*keri*) says *etz*, the wood of his spear. Rabbi Elazar draws the following conclusion from the sparse description of Goliath's might: *adayin lo higanu le-chatzi shevacho shel oto rasha* — we have not even reached half of the praise due to that wicked one. The Torah's description, impressive as it is, captures less than half of his actual power and stature.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: if that is so, why describe him at all? *She-asur le-saper be-shivchen shel resha'im* — it is forbidden to enumerate the praises of the wicked. The answer: *lo dumi shavkei d'dami* — you cannot omit it entirely, because you must describe Goliath's greatness in order to convey the magnitude of David's achievement. The praise of Goliath is not for Goliath's sake; it is for David's. Without knowing what David overcame, one cannot fully appreciate the victory.

## Shovach Army

### Shovach Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן בָּאוּ בְּנִצְחוֹנוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹבַךְ כּוּ׳״. כְּתִיב ״שׁוֹבַךְ״, וּכְתִיב ״שׁוֹפַךְ״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: ״שׁוֹפַךְ״ שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ ״שׁוֹבַךְ״ — שֶׁעָשׂוּי כְּשׁוֹבָךְ. וְחַד אָמַר: שׁוֹבַךְ שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ ״שׁוֹפַךְ״ — שֶׁכׇּל הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתוֹ נִשְׁפָּךְ לְפָנָיו כְּקִיתוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § According to the mishna, the priest would say: **The Ammonites came championed by Shobach** (see II Samuel, chapter 10). In one account, his name **is written: "Shobach"** (II Samuel 10:18), **and** in another place **it is written: "Shophach"** (I Chronicles 19:18). **Rav and Shmuel** engaged in a dispute concerning this matter. **One** of them **said: His name was Shophach, and why is he called** by the **name Shobach?** It is **because he was built like a dovecote [*shovakh*],** as he was exceptionally tall. **And one** of them **said: His name was Shobach, and why is he called** by the **name Shophach?** It is **because anyone who would see him** would become terrified and his courage would be **spilled [*nishpakh*] before him like** water from **a jug.**

The *Gemara* returns to the motif introduced earlier in the *meshiach milchama*'s address — *Bnei Amon ba-u be-nitzchono shel Shovach* — the Ammonites came to battle relying on the valor of *Shovach*, their general, whose story is recounted in *Sefer Shmuel*. However, in *Divrei ha-Yamim* the same figure is called *Shofach*. The *Gemara* notes: *k'siv Shovach u-ch'siv Shofach* — both names appear in the text.

*Rav u-Shmuel* debate his true name. One holds that his name was *Shofach*, and he was called *Shovach* because *she-asui ke-shovach* — he was built like a pigeon coop, extraordinarily tall and imposing. The other holds his name was *Shovach*, and he was called *Shofach* because *she-kol ha-ro'eh oto nishpach le-fanav ke-kiton* — everyone who saw him would spill out before him like a pitcher, perhaps losing control of their bowels or simply dissolving in fear before him.

### Ashpato Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַשְׁפָּתוֹ כְּקֶבֶר פָּתוּחַ כֻּלָּם גִּבּוֹרִים״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, חַד אָמַר: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁזּוֹרְקִין חֵץ עוֹשִׂין אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת שֶׁל חֲלָלִים, וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר שֶׁאוּמָּנִין בַּקְּרָב — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֻּלָּם גִּבּוֹרִים״. וְחַד אָמַר: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין צוֹרְכֵיהֶן עוֹשִׂין אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת שֶׁל זֶבֶל, וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחוֹלֵי מֵעַיִם הֵם — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֻּלָּם גִּבּוֹרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a dispute concerning the enemy forces of Nebuchadnezzar. The prophet states: **"Their quiver [*ashpato*] is an open sepulcher, they are all mighty men"** (Jeremiah 5:16). **Rav and Shmuel, and some say Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi,** engaged in a dispute concerning the implication of the verse. **One** of them **said: When they shoot an arrow, they produce heaps** and **heaps [*ashpatot ashpatot*] of corpses. And lest you say that** they are **skilled in** the arts of **battle** but they are not particularly strong, **the verse states: "They are all mighty men." And one** of them **said: When they perform their needs,** i.e., relieve themselves, **they produce heaps** and **heaps [*ashpatot ashpatot*] of excrement,** which indicates they eat heartily, like mighty men. **And lest you say it is because they are ill** in their **intestines, the verse states: "They are all mighty men"** and are not ill.

The *Gemara* then turns to a *pasuk* about the armies of *Nevuchadnezzar*: *ashpato ke-kever patuach kulam gibborim* — his quiver is like an open grave; they are all mighty men. The word *ashpato* can mean a quiver for arrows, but it can also mean a garbage heap or *ashpah*. *Rav u-Shmuel* — or in another version, *Rav Ami ve-Rav Asi* — debate the meaning. One holds that *ashpato* refers to the quiver of arrows: *b'sha'ah she-zorkim chetz osim ashpah ashpah shel chalalim* — when they shoot their arrows they make mound upon mound of corpses, each mound like an *ashpah*, a garbage heap. One might then think that this merely reflects their skill at archery rather than their personal might — therefore *Talmud lomar kulam gibborim*, the verse concludes that they are all personally mighty men as well.

### Excrement View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַאי מַאן דִּנְפִישׁ זִיבְלֵיהּ, חוֹלִי מֵעַיִם הוּא. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לִיטְרַח בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said: Learn from** this exchange that if there is **one whose excrement is abundant, he is ill** in his **intestines.** The Gemara asks: **What difference is there** whether or not he is ill in his intestines? The Gemara answers: It is **so that** one who suffers these symptoms **will tend to himself** medically.

The other opinion reads *ashpato* differently. *B'sha'ah she-osim tzorcheihem osim ashpah ashpah shel zevel* — when they relieve themselves, they make mound upon mound of excrement, suggesting by their sheer physical size the enormous quantities they discharge. This too is a form of praising their might, if an unusual one. Here again the verse's conclusion is necessary: *kulam gibborim* — they are indeed mighty, not merely large in an unhealthy way.

*Rav Mari* draws a practical medical inference from this discussion: *shema minah — hai man de-nafish zivlei cholei me'ayim hu* — one who produces excessive excrement is to be presumed to have intestinal illness. The *nafka mina* is *le-itpuchi be-nafshei* — the person should attend to his own welfare and seek medical attention. *Rashi* explains this as: *le-vakesh alav refu'ot* — to go seek healing.

## Worry & Health

### Managing Worry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דְּאָגָה בְלֶב אִישׁ יַשְׁחֶנָּה״, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי חַד אָמַר: יַשִּׂחֶנָּה מִדַּעְתּוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר: יְשִׂיחֶנָּה לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar vein, one is urged to relieve his distress. The verse states: **"If there is a care in the heart of a man, let him bend it [*yashḥena*]"** (Proverbs 12:25). **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** dispute the verse's meaning. **One said: He should force it [*yasḥena*] out of his mind.** He should remove his worries from his thoughts. **And one said:** It means **he should tell [*yesiḥena*]** his troubles **to others,** which will relieve his anxiety.

The *Gemara* cites the verse *da'agah be-lev ish yashchenah* — if there is worry in a man's heart, he should do something with it (*yashchenah* or *yassichennah*, depending on how one reads the vowels). *Rav Asi* introduces the discussion and notes two competing readings of the operative word, both using a *sin* — *yassichennah*.

### Therapy Methods

The two readings yield two opposing therapeutic strategies. One says *yassichennah mi-da'ato* — push it out of his mind, distract himself from the worry, deal with problems by redirecting his attention elsewhere. The other says *yassichennah la-acherim* — speak about it to others, share the burden of the concern. The *Gemara* here reflects a genuine debate about how human beings should manage anxiety: suppression and distraction on one side, disclosure and conversation on the other. The *Gemara* presents both as legitimate readings of the verse without definitively resolving between them — though the pairing with the discussion of intestinal health in the preceding lines creates a suggestive context, implying that unexpressed worry has a way of manifesting physically.
