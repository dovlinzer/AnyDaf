# Yevamot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mother Omission Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּפְלִיגִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן, וְקָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** This is a case concerning **which Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis disagree, and** yet it **is taught.** If this *halakha* is taught, evidently this mishna does deal with matters subject to dispute.

We pick up at the bottom of 9a, and the subject is the question that was posed to *Rabbi*: why does the *mishnah* enumerate fifteen women and not sixteen, having omitted the case of *imo* — one's mother? The scenario that makes this question possible is as follows. Reuven is the son of Yaakov's marriage to Devorah, while Yosef is the son of Yaakov and Rachel, who had relations with Yaakov outside of marriage. Since Rachel was never married to Yaakov, she is not technically *eshet aviv* — the father's wife — from Reuven's perspective. Reuven could therefore marry Rachel, and if he did, Yosef would find himself in a *yibum* situation with his own mother upon Reuven's death.

### Anusat Aviv Debate

The entire force of this question hinges on the case of *anusat aviv* — a woman with whom one's father had sexual relations but whom he never married. Is she forbidden to the son as well? This is precisely the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan*. Rabbi Yehuda forbids *anusat aviv* to the son, while the *Rabbanan* permit her. Accordingly, whether Reuven could ever have married Rachel in the first place is itself a matter of debate.

### Mishnah & Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנוֹלַד וּלְבַסּוֹף יִיבֵּם — לָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, in a case where he was born **and at the end,** i.e., afterward, a second older brother **entered into levirate marriage** with the *yevama* and subsequently died as well, and the same woman again came before that same younger brother, **Rabbi Shimon did not disagree** with the opinion of the Rabbis here either.

*Rabbi*'s response to Levi was sharp: it seems to me you have no brain in your head. The basic explanation offered for why the *mishnah* does not list *imo* is that the *mishnah* confines itself to cases that are not subject to dispute — *plugta lo ka-mairi* — and the permissibility of marrying *anusat aviv* is precisely such a dispute. With that, the *gemara* moves on to probe this claim more deeply.

## Eshet Achiv Debate

### Case Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: חָלוּק הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַף בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** In fact, Rabbi Shimon disagreed only with regard to a different case, when there were two brothers, one of whom died, and the second took the *yevama* in levirate marriage. If a third brother was subsequently born and afterward the second brother died, Rabbi Shimon maintains that the youngest brother may enter into levirate marriage with this woman because she was never forbidden to him, as what occurred prior to his birth is of no relevance.

The *gemara* challenges the premise that the *mishnah* avoids matters of dispute by pointing to the last line of 9a: *v'harei eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — what about the case of a brother's wife when the surviving brother was not yet born at the time of the marriage? This case does appear in the *mishnah* as one of the fifteen, yet it is, as the *gemara* notes, itself the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan*.

### Birth Order Scenario

To understand the case: Reuven marries Rivka. Reuven then dies. Shimon — who was alive contemporaneously with Reuven — performs *yibum* with Rivka and marries her. Subsequently, Levi is born. Now Levi has a potential *mitzvat yibum* with Rivka if Shimon were to die, because she is *eshet achiv* — Shimon's wife. But Rivka was also Reuven's wife, and with respect to Reuven's marriage, Levi never had a *mitzvat yibum* at all, since he was not yet alive.

### Sister-in-Law Status

The rule of *ki yeshvu achim yachdav* — the brothers must have been alive simultaneously — means that if a brother was not yet born when the deceased brother was alive, there is no *mitzvat yibum* with respect to that first marriage. So when Levi is born, how does he relate to Rivka? Since *yibum* had not yet occurred, he relates to her solely as the wife of his dead brother Reuven — *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — with no *mitzvat yibum* and with a full status of *ervah*. At that moment she is forbidden to him as a dead brother's wife. Then, when Shimon subsequently performs *yibum*, she remains an *ervah* to Levi — because that status had already crystallized before the *yibum* occurred.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon disagrees in a specific scenario: if Levi was born *after* Shimon had already performed *yibum* with Rivka. In that case, when Levi comes into the world, Rivka's status in his eyes is that of a living brother's wife — Shimon's wife — with whom he has a *mitzvat yibum*. He has no basis to relate to her as Reuven's wife with respect to whom he was never obligated. In that scenario, Rabbi Shimon holds that Rivka is not an *ervah* to Levi, and Levi would actually be obligated to perform *yibum* with her upon Shimon's death.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתּוֹתַב רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: This statement of **Rabbi Oshaya was conclusively refuted.**

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: the *mishnah*'s inclusion of *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* is not problematic even given Rabbi Shimon's position, because there exists a scenario upon which all agree — namely, the case where Levi was born *before* Shimon performed *yibum*. In that scenario, even Rabbi Shimon concedes that Rivka is an *ervah* to Levi. Thus the *mishnah* can list this case and remain on universally agreed ground.

The *gemara* then cites a *baraita*: *be-nolad u-lesof yibem lo pligi Rabbi Shimon* — where Levi was born first and Shimon performed *yibum* afterward, Rabbi Shimon does not disagree that she is an *ervah*. However, Rabbi Oshia is then cited as saying that Rabbi Shimon in fact disagrees even in that scenario, maintaining she is never an *ervah*. The *gemara* dismisses this: *hi tiyufta d'Rabbi Oshia* — Rabbi Oshia was contradicted. That dispute will be taken up in a later *sugya*. For now, the operative conclusion is that even according to Rabbi Shimon, there is always a scenario of *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* upon which everyone agrees she is an *ervah*.

## Bekulan Principle

### Fifteen Apply Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: בְּכוּלָּן אֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה — מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה, וְהָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה — מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה, וַאֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** With this in mind, the Gemara states its objection: **And didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav said, and likewise Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** in a *baraita*: With regard **to all of those** fifteen women enumerated in the mishna, it is possible to find a scenario in which **I** would **call them: The** woman **who is forbidden to this** brother **is permitted to that** brother, **and the one who is forbidden to this** brother **is permitted to that** brother. **And** consequently, **her sister who is her sister-in-law performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.**

We now arrive at a striking and intricate passage. Rabbi Yehuda said in the name of Rav, and Rabbi Chiyya taught similarly: *be-khulan ani kore bahem ha-asura la-zeh muteret la-zeh ve-asura la-zeh muteret la-zeh* — in all fifteen of the forbidden relationships, I can apply the rule that she is forbidden to this one and permitted to that one, and forbidden to that one and permitted to this one. And further, the woman who is *achoto she-hi yevimto* — his sister who is also his sister-in-law — may perform *chalitzah* or *yibum*.

### Four Brothers Case

To understand what this means, we must look ahead to the *mishnah* in *perek arbaah achim*, which deals with four brothers. Suppose Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah, who are sisters. Levi and Yehuda are the other two brothers. Now Reuven and Shimon both die. Why can't Levi simply perform *yibum* with Rachel and Yehuda with Leah? The problem is *zikah* — a quasi-spousal bond that attaches before *yibum* occurs. Levi has a *zikah* to both Rachel and Leah, as does Yehuda. If Levi were to perform *yibum* with Rachel, he would be actualizing a marital bond with *achot zikukato* — the sister of his quasi-wife Leah — which is equivalent to marrying one's wife's sister. The same problem applies in reverse. Accordingly, the *halachah* is that both women require *chalitzah*, and neither can have *yibum* performed with her.

### Asymmetry Principle

However, the *mishnah* introduces a crucial qualification. If Rachel happens to be an *ervah* to Levi — say, his aunt, his daughter, or some other forbidden relationship — but is not an *ervah* to Yehuda, then the dynamic changes entirely. Levi has no *zikah* to Rachel, since she is his *ervah*. That means Leah is not *achot zikukato* for Levi — he has no quasi-spousal bond to her sister. Levi is therefore free to perform *yibum* with Leah. Symmetrically, since Levi will perform *yibum* with Leah, Yehuda has no *zikah* problem with Rachel, and Yehuda may perform *yibum* with Rachel. This is the rule of *ha-asurah la-zeh muteret la-zeh*: she who is forbidden to one is permitted to the other. This is the *bekulan* principle — the claim that for each of the fifteen forbidden relationships, one can construct a scenario in which that forbidden relationship creates precisely this asymmetry, freeing the other brother to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *yibum*.

### Bekulan Explored

The elegance of *bekulan* is that it must work for all fifteen cases. For each *ervah* on the list, one must find a scenario where that *ervah* is forbidden to one brother but not the other, and moreover, where she and her co-yevamah are sisters — since that is the structural requirement for the case to generate the asymmetry described above. The straightforward part of the exercise is establishing that the woman is an *ervah* to one brother and not the other. The complicating factor is the additional requirement that the two women must be sisters to one another, and yet the brother who is forbidden to one sister must still be permitted to perform *yibum* with the other. Working through all fifteen cases is precisely what the extended Rashi on this passage accomplishes. Rather than exhaustively reproducing that analysis, we will trace through one illustrative example.

### Bito Example

Take the case of *bito* — one's daughter. Can Rachel be Levi's daughter? Certainly. Suppose Levi and his wife Devorah have a daughter named Rachel. Can Rachel marry Reuven? Yes — a man may marry his niece. And is Rachel an *ervah* to Yehuda? No — she is his niece, permitted to him. So this case works at the first level: Rachel is forbidden to her own father Levi but permitted to Yehuda.

### Daughter Complication

However, there is a further requirement: Leah must be Rachel's sister, and Levi must still be permitted to perform *yibum* with Leah. This is where the complications arise. If Leah is also a daughter of Devorah, then Devorah's daughter is his wife's daughter — *bat ishto* — which triggers the prohibition of *isha u-vitah*, a woman and her daughter. So Levi cannot do *yibum* with Leah if Devorah was his wife.

The only way to have Rachel and Leah be sisters through Devorah while preserving Levi's ability to do *yibum* with Leah is if Levi was never married to Devorah. If their relationship was non-marital — *onnes* in the broad sense — then Leah, as Devorah's daughter, is not Levi's *bat ishto* in the halachically operative sense. The prohibition of *isha u-vitah* applies to a woman one was *married* to; since Levi was never married to Devorah, Leah is not his wife's daughter. Thus Rachel, born of Levi and Devorah, is his daughter and therefore his *ervah*, while Leah, born of some other father and Devorah, is his niece's half-sister — with no forbidden relationship between them — and he may perform *yibum* with her. This scenario works, but only through a non-marital union.

## Rav Yehuda Limits

### Chamoto Onward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה מְתַרְגֵּם מֵחֲמוֹתוֹ וְאֵילָךְ. אֲבָל שִׁיתָּא בָּבֵי דְּרֵישָׁא — לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda explains** that this is not true of all of the women listed in the mishna. Only **from** the case of **his mother-in-law and onward,** until the end of the list, is it possible for one sister to be a forbidden relation to one brother and the other sister not to be forbidden at all. **However, in the first six cases, no,** this option does not apply. **What is the reason** for this?

Rav Yehuda *metargem* — he interpreted the scope of *bekulan* — and limited it: *me-chamato va-eilach*, from *chamato* — mother-in-law — downward. He holds that only the latter categories of the fifteen should be included in the *bekulan* claim. The first six variations involving daughter and her descendants should not be included.

### Onnes vs. Nisuin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּבִתּוֹ בְּאוּנְסִין מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ, בְּנִשּׂוּאִין לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ. בְּנִשּׂוּאִין קָמַיְירֵי, בְּאוּנְסִין לָא קָא מַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Since,** for example, in the case of **one's daughter,** although it is possible that one of the sisters is the daughter of one of the brothers, her mother is prohibited to all of the brothers as their brother's wife, and therefore she could not possibly have a daughter from another brother. This case is possible only if one brother raped a woman who subsequently gave birth to his daughter. Since the prohibition with regard to a brother's wife applies only in the case of marriage, this same woman is not forbidden to a different brother. **In** the case of **rape you** can **find** this scenario, but **in** the case of **marriage you cannot find** it. However, the mishna cannot be interpreted as referring to a case of this kind, as **it deals with marriage** and **it does not deal with rape.**

The reason, as the previous analysis of *bito* illustrated, is that the case of a daughter can only be constructed through a non-marital union — *onnes* — not through *nisuin*, lawful marriage. As demonstrated, the only way to have Rachel and Leah be sisters while Levi can still do *yibum* with Leah is if Levi was never married to their mother. Since the case requires extra-marital relations to even be possible, and since the *bekulan* principle should speak to ordinary marital scenarios, Rav Yehuda holds that *b'nisuin ka-mairi, b'onnes lo ka-mairi* — we are discussing cases arising from marriage, not from non-marital union. Tosafot notes that the very phrase *be-khulan ani kore bahem* — "in all of them I can apply" — implies cases that commonly occur. On that basis, the first six categories are excluded from *bekulan* by Rav Yehuda.

## Abaye Disagrees

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי מְתַרְגֵּם אַף בִּתּוֹ מֵאֲנוּסָתוֹ — כֵּיוָן דְּאַשְׁכּוֹחֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אִי בָּעֲיָא מִן הָאוּנְסִין תִּהְוֵי, [אִי בָּעֲיָא מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין תִּהְוֵי]. אֲבָל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ — לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye explains** differently, as he would include in this scenario **even his daughter from the woman he raped.** Why would he include this case? **Since you can find** this possibility, **if you wish it can be by rape, and if you wish it can be by marriage,** i.e., regardless of the details, this situation can occur. **However,** the last case, that of **a wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist,** he would **not** include. **What is the reason** for this?

Abaye *metargem* differently. He holds that *af bito me-anusato* — even the case of a daughter arising from a non-marital union — should be included in *bekulan*. His reasoning is that the scenario is possible regardless: *ei ba'it teima mi-nisuin tehei, ei ba'it teima me-anusin tehei* — whether we construct it through marriage or through extra-marital union, the principle can be applied. Since the case is achievable, it belongs in the list.

### Eshet Achiv Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דִּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הוּא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, לְרַבָּנַן לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — בִּפְלוּגְתָּא לָא קָא מַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Since it is** only **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon that you find this** potential scenario, as he permits a *yevama* to her *yavam* in a case where he was born after she first entered levirate marriage, it is therefore possible that one sister might be forbidden to one brother if he was born before his brother took her in levirate marriage. Likewise, another sister might be forbidden to another brother for the same reason, although the first woman would be permitted to him, as in his lifetime she was never the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist. **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** however, **you cannot find this** scenario, and the mishna **does not deal with disputes** but only with those cases that are accepted by all opinions.

However, Abaye draws his own exclusion: *aval eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — the case of a brother's wife when the surviving brother was not yet born — cannot be included in *bekulan*. This case, as we noted earlier, requires two additional brothers in the scenario to operate in parallel. Specifically, for the required asymmetry to work, one would need Rachel to be an *ervah* to Levi as *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo*, while simultaneously, the parallel case — Leah being *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* to Yehuda — would need to hold as well, and each would need to be permitted to the other brother.

### Rabbi Shimon Only

The problem, Abaye argues, is structural. *Ki-van de-l'Rabbi Shimon hu de-mishkachat lah, le-Rabbanan lo mishkachat lah, be-plugta lo ka-mairi* — the scenario can only be constructed according to Rabbi Shimon's position, not according to the *Rabbanan*. According to the *Rabbanan*, *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* is simply an *ervah* in all cases. So for the scenario to work in parallel — Rachel forbidden to Levi and permitted to Yehuda, Leah forbidden to Yehuda and permitted to Levi — one cannot have both operate symmetrically. The reason is age: if Levi is older, one can construct the case where Leah is *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* to Yehuda — who was born after Yissachar died — while Levi, being older, was alive contemporaneously with Yissachar and thus has a *mitzvat yibum* with Leah. But then one cannot run the parallel scenario with Rachel, because if Levi is older, Rachel cannot be *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* to Levi while simultaneously not being the same to Yehuda who is younger. The asymmetry cannot be made to work in both directions simultaneously according to the *Rabbanan*. Since including this case in *bekulan* would require relying on Rabbi Shimon's position — which is a disputed view — and the *mishnah* should not be read as depending on matters of *plugta*, Abaye excludes *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* from *bekulan*.

## Rav Safra Includes

### Safra's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב סָפְרָא מְתַרְגֵּם אַף אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּשִׁיתָּא אַחִים, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rav Safra explains** that the possibility of one brother permitted only to one woman and another brother likewise permitted only to one woman exists **even** in the case of **a wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, and you** can **find this** scenario **in** a case involving **six brothers, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon.**

Rav Safra *metargem* still differently. He holds that even *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* can be included in *bekulan* — *af eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo meshkachat lah* — though only *ve-aliba de-Rabbi Shimon*, according to Rabbi Shimon's position. He is willing to include a case that works only within one opinion.

### Mnemonic Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסִימָנָיךְ: מֵת, נוֹלַד, וְיִבֵּם. מֵת, נוֹלַד, וְיִבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And your mnemonic** to remember how this might occur is as follows: **Died, was born, entered into levirate marriage, died, was born, entered into levirate marriage.** In other words, there were several brothers, and one, Reuven, **died,** before another, Yissakhar, **was born, and** another brother, Shimon, **entered into levirate marriage** with the deceased's wife, and there was another brother, Levi, who **died** as well, and yet another brother, Zevulun, **was born, and** a sixth brother, Yehuda, **entered into levirate marriage** with the deceased's wife.

To demonstrate how this works, the *gemara* offers a mnemonic: *met, noseh, ve-yibem, met, noseh, ve-yibem* — died, married, and performed *yibum*, died, married, and performed *yibum*. This sequence of events is the key to constructing the scenario.

### Six Brothers Setup

The scenario requires six brothers: Reuven, Shimon, Levi, Yehuda, Yissachar, and Zevulun. Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah respectively. Levi and Yehuda are the brothers who will ultimately perform *yibum*. Yissachar and Zevulun will be born into the picture at critical junctures.

### Birth-Death Sequence

Here is the sequence: Reuven dies. At this point, Yissachar is born. Then Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel. Now, according to Rabbi Shimon, what is Rachel's status to Yissachar at the moment he comes into the world, *before* Levi performs *yibum*? She is *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — Reuven's wife, with whom Yissachar had no *mitzvat yibum* since he was not alive. Once Levi then performs *yibum*, she remains an *ervah* to Yissachar, because that status had already crystallized when he was born.

Then Shimon dies. Now Zevulun is born. Levi performs *yibum* with Leah. When Zevulun comes into the world, what is Leah's status? At the moment of his birth, before *yibum*, Leah is *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* to him — she is Shimon's wife, with whom Zevulun had no *mitzvat yibum* since he was not alive. She is therefore an *ervah* to Zevulun, just as Rachel is an *ervah* to Yissachar.

Now what is Rachel's status to Zevulun? By the time Zevulun is born, Levi has already performed *yibum* with Rachel. According to Rabbi Shimon, when a brother is born *after yibum* has already been completed, he relates to the woman as a living brother's actual wife — and therefore has a *mitzvat yibum* obligation to her. Rachel is therefore *mutar* to Zevulun.

### Rabbi Shimon Logic

This is the crucial asymmetry: Rachel is *assur* to Yissachar — because he was born before *yibum* occurred — and *mutar* to Zevulun — because he was born after *yibum* occurred. Leah, correspondingly, is *assur* to Zevulun for the same structural reason and *mutar* to Yissachar. According to the *Rabbanan*, this cannot be made to work in parallel, because the younger brother will always find that any *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* is *assur* to him by virtue of being younger — he cannot simultaneously have a case where it is *mutar* to him. But according to Rabbi Shimon, the timing of *yibum* relative to birth — not merely birth order — determines the status, and this allows the asymmetry to be constructed symmetrically on both sides. Thus Rav Safra maintains that *bekulan* can include *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo*, according to Rabbi Shimon.

## Mother Q Resolved

### Core Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי הָנֵי כְּלָלֵי לֵית לֵיהּ. רַב אַדָּא קַרְחִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: לְעוֹלָם אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי הָנֵי כְּלָלֵי, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִמּוֹ אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו — בַּחֲדָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, בְּתַרְתֵּי לָא קָמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **does not hold** in accordance with **these general statements** applied by Rabbi Ḥiyya, and therefore he maintains that no issue subject to dispute was taught in the mishna. **Rav Adda Karḥina,** who was sitting **before Rav Kahana, said in the name of Rava: Actually,** one can explain that **Rabbi does hold** in accordance with **these general statements** laid out by Rabbi Ḥiyya. **And this is what** he **said** to Levi: In the case of **his mother** who had been **raped by his father, you find this** case **with** only **one** of the women; however, **you cannot find it with two.**

We now return to the original question with which we began. If *bekulan* can include matters of dispute — specifically the *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* scenario, which works only according to Rabbi Shimon — then why did *Rabbi* exclude *imo* from the fifteen? If the *mishnah* is willing to deal with *plugta*, why not include the case of mother, which is also a *plugta* (between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan* regarding *anusat aviv*)?

### Rav Ada's Answer

Rav Ada Karchina, in the presence of Rav Kahana, said in the name of Rava: *le-olam itei de-Rabbi hani klali* — in truth, *Rabbi* can hold that the entire *mishnah* applies to the *bekulan* scenario, and that *bekulan* includes matters of dispute. And nevertheless, *imo* could not be listed. Why? Because *imo anusat aviv be-chada mishkachat lah, be-tarti lo ka-mishkachat lah* — the case of mother who is *anusat aviv* satisfies only one of the two structural requirements of the *bekulan* scenario, but not both simultaneously.

### Mother Impossibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי יַעֲקֹב שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת אָנַס, אֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה — לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **If Ya'akov,** the father, **raped two sisters** who gave birth to two sons, and he had two other sons who married these raped sisters and died, in this scenario **you find** a case of **her sister who is her sister-in-law,** as there is a *yevama* who comes before one of the brothers who is a forbidden relative and also a sister and a sister-in-law of another *yevama*. However, **you cannot find** the principle of: **She who is forbidden to this one is permitted to that one.**

Recall the requirements. For *bekulan* to work, we need: (1) that the woman is an *ervah* to one brother but not the other — *ha-asurah la-zeh muteret la-zeh* — and (2) that the two women involved are sisters — *achoto she-hi yevimto*. The case of mother can satisfy (1) alone or (2) alone, but not both at once. Here is why.

For Rachel to be Levi's mother in the required scenario, Yaakov and Rachel must have had extramarital relations, producing Levi. Reuven, from Yaakov's marriage to Leah, then marries Rachel — since she is neither his mother nor his father's lawful wife, only *anusat aviv*, which is the disputed case. This makes Levi liable for *yibum* with his own mother should Reuven die. That is the *imo* scenario. Now, for *bekulan* to work, Leah must be Rachel's sister, and Yehuda — Levi's brother — must be permitted to do *yibum* with Leah.

### Sisters Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי שְׁתֵּי נׇכְרִיּוֹת אָנַס, הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the father **raped two unrelated women,** who gave birth to two sons and then married two other sons of the same father who subsequently died, **you find** the case of: **She who is forbidden to this one is permitted to that one,** as each of the two raped women comes before one of the brothers for levirate marriage while she is forbidden to the other as his mother. However, **you do not find** a situation of: **Her sister who is her sister-in-law.** Therefore, the case of one's mother who was raped by one's father cannot be included in the list of the mishna, as the characteristics stated by Rabbi Ḥiyya do not apply to this case.

But here is the fatal complication. Rachel and Leah, in this scenario, are sisters. And Yaakov had extramarital relations with Rachel. But Leah is Rachel's sister — she is Levi's mother's sister, *achot imo*. The prohibition of *achot imo* — one's maternal aunt — applies universally, without dispute. Levi cannot do *yibum* with Leah. So the moment you have Rachel and Leah be sisters, Leah becomes forbidden to Levi as *achot imo*, and the *bekulan* structure collapses: you cannot have Levi do *yibum* with one and Yehuda do *yibum* with the other in the required symmetric fashion.

What if Rachel and Leah are not sisters — if Yaakov had relations with two unrelated women? Then you have (1): Rachel is an *ervah* to Levi as his mother, while Leah is not. But you have lost (2): the two women are no longer sisters, and *achoto she-hi yevimto* does not apply. *Ve-hayu shtei nochriyot anusos* — if they are unrelated women, then *ha-asurah la-zeh u-muteret la-zeh mishkachat lah* — the forbidden-to-one / permitted-to-the-other condition holds. But *achot she-hi yevimto lo mishkachat lah* — you do not have them as sisters.

### Mother Excluded

In short: if the two women are sisters, Levi is forbidden to Leah as *achot imo*, and the structure fails. If they are not sisters, the *bekulan* scenario does not apply. Therefore, the case of *imo* as *anusat aviv* cannot be incorporated into *bekulan* at all, not because it involves *plugta*, but because it structurally cannot satisfy both conditions of *bekulan* simultaneously. This is why *Rabbi* did not list *imo* in the fifteen — not to avoid disputes, but because *imo* simply does not fit the *bekulan* framework that the list is designed to support.

## Next Mishnah Link

### Six Severe Arayot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי הָנֵי כְּלָלֵי, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא קָמַיְירֵי, וּמַאי ״כִּמְדוּמֶּה אֲנִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מוֹחַ בְּקׇדְקֳדוֹ״ דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ —
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ashi stated** a different explanation: **Actually, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **does not hold** in accordance with **these general statements** of Rabbi Ḥiyya, **and** the mishna **does deal with** *halakhot* that are in **dispute.** According to his opinion, **what is** the meaning of the phrase: **It seems to me that he has no brain in his head, that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Levi?

At this point, Rav Ashi offers a different and — in the *gemara*'s presentation — strikingly simpler explanation. The next *mishnah* states: *shesh arayot chamurot me-eilu* — there are six forbidden relationships more severe than those of the fifteen. The severity consists in the fact that these six women *cannot* be married to a brother — *mipnei she-hen nesuot la-acherim* — because they are ineligible for *yibum* altogether, being permanently forbidden to any brother. As a result, *tzaroseihen mutarot* — their co-wives are exempt from *yibum* and *chalitzah* as well, since there is never a valid *yibum* bond to begin with.

### Mother in Next List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא דָּיְיקַתְּ מַתְנִיתִין? דְּמַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא [דְּאָסַר בַּאֲנוּסַת אָבִיו].
>
> **Translation:** According to Rav Ashi, **this is what** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said** to Levi: **What is the reason** that **you were not precise** in your interpretation of **the mishna? As the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who prohibited** the case **of a woman** who was **raped by one's father.**

Who are these six? The first is *imo* — one's mother. This is the key to Rav Ashi's argument. The very fact that the next *mishnah* lists *imo* as a woman who can *never* be married to a brother tells us something critical about the *mishnah*'s position. If the *mishnah* were following the *Rabbanan*, who permit *anusat aviv*, then a mother who was never married to the father *could* potentially be married to a brother — since *anusat aviv* is *mutar* according to them. There would be no basis to place *imo* on the list of those who can never be married to a brother.

### Why Mother Severe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי: שֵׁשׁ עֲרָיוֹת חֲמוּרוֹת מֵאֵלּוּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן נְשׂוּאוֹת לַאֲחֵרִים צָרוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרוֹת: אִמּוֹ, וְאֵשֶׁת אָבִיו, וַאֲחוֹת אָבִיו. מַאי אִמּוֹ? אִילֵּימָא נְשׂוּאַת אָבִיו — הַיְינוּ אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו!
>
> **Translation:** **As it teaches** in the next mishna: **Six** women **with whom relations are forbidden are more severe than these** enumerated in the previous mishna, **because they** may **marry** only **others** and can never be married to any of the brothers, and therefore **their rival wives are permitted.** These women include: **His mother, his father's wife, and his father's sister.** Rav Ashi infers: **What is** the meaning of: **His mother? If we say** that this mother is **married to his father,** then **this is the same** case as: **His father's wife,** which would mean that the mishna lists only five cases, not six.

But the *mishnah* does place *imo* on that list. This implies that the *mishnah* holds like Rabbi Yehuda, who forbids *anusat aviv* — and therefore a mother who was *anusat aviv* can never be married to a brother, since she too is forbidden as *anusat aviv*. Thus *imo* does not appear in the first list of fifteen because she is on the next list: she belongs among those who can never be married to a brother, not among those who — as the *yevamot* scenario requires — sometimes can be.

### Rabbi's Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו — אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו. וְקָתָנֵי: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן נְשׂוּאוֹת לַאֲחֵרִים: לַאֲחֵרִים אִין, לָאַחִין לָא. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאִית לֵיהּ הַאי סְבָרָא — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָסַר בַּאֲנוּסַת אָבִיו. מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא תָּנֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to **a woman raped by his father? And it is taught** in the mishna that the *halakhot* of levirate marriage do not apply to them **because they may marry** only **others,** which indicates: **Others, yes; the brothers, no.** Since each of the brothers are sons of the same father, each one is prohibited from marrying the mother of one of the other brothers, as although she is not his mother, she is nevertheless a woman raped by his father, whom one may not marry. **Who did you hear who holds** in accordance with **this conclusion** that a woman raped by one's father is prohibited to all his sons? It is **Rabbi Yehuda who prohibits a woman raped by his father. Due to that** reason the case of a woman raped by one's father **was not taught** in the first mishna.

This is the explanation underlying *Rabbi*'s rebuke of Levi. The question of why *imo* is absent from the fifteen should have been answered by simply reading the next *mishnah*. It is apparent from there that the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda, and that *imo* — as *anusat aviv* — is categorically forbidden to any brother. Levi's failure to notice this is what prompted the pointed remark that he appears to lack a brain in his head. And indeed, Rav Ashi observes, whatever complexity was generated by the earlier discussion — about *plugta*, *bekulan*, and the structural impossibility of *imo* satisfying both conditions — all of that could have been set aside. The simple answer was always available from the next *mishnah*.

## Ravina Challenge

### Be-di-avad Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — דְּאִי עֲבַר וּנְסֵיב? ״דְּאִי״ לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda you** can **find this** possibility that a woman raped by a father comes before the son for levirate marriage. **What if one** of the brothers **transgressed and married** a woman raped by his father? Even Rabbi Yehuda concedes that this is a violation of a regular prohibition that does not incur *karet*, and therefore the marriage is valid. Consequently, if this brother died childless, this woman would come before her son for levirate marriage. The Gemara rejects this proof: The *tanna* **does not teach** cases of **what if,** i.e., the mishna does not cite an example that could result only from a transgression.

Ravina, however, presses Rav Ashi. He argues: *Rabbi Yehuda nami mishkachat lah* — even according to Rabbi Yehuda, one can find a scenario in which a mother is married to a brother, making *imo* a potential candidate for the first list of fifteen after all. The first case Ravina proposes is *be-di-avad*. Even Rabbi Yehuda, who forbids *anusat aviv*, does not classify it as *issur karet*. And when a prohibited marriage does not rise to the level of *karet*, the *kiddushin* is nonetheless binding *be-di-avad*. Therefore, if a brother went ahead and married *anusat aviv* — transgressing Rabbi Yehuda's ruling — the marriage would be valid after the fact. In such a case, *imo* would technically be a brother's wife, and upon his death there would be a *yibum* scenario involving one's own mother.

### Le-chatchila Focus

This is immediately dismissed. *De-i lo katani* — we do not include cases of transgression in the list. The *mishnah* speaks *le-chatchila*, of situations that arise through proper, permitted conduct. A scenario that can only occur because someone violated the *halachah* is not the kind of case the *mishnah* enumerates.

### Adultery Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא, בְּלָא ״דְּאִי״ נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ: יַעֲקֹב אָנַס כַּלָּתוֹ וְהוֹלִיד מִמֶּנָּה בֵּן, וּמֵת [רְאוּבֵן] בְּלֹא בָּנִים. וּנְפַלָה לַהּ קַמֵּי בְּרַהּ, וּמִיגּוֹ דְּאִיהִי אֲסִירָה — צָרָתָהּ נָמֵי אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: Even without** a hypothetical: **What if, you** can **find** an example of a woman raped by a father who is not forbidden to the son in marriage. How so? **Ya'akov,** the father, **raped his daughter-in-law, from whom he fathered a son. And** the son, **Reuven,** the husband of the daughter-in-law, **died childless. And** as Ya'akov had two sons, the deceased Reuven and a son by Reuven's wife whom Ya'akov had raped, Reuven's wife **would** then **come before her son** for levirate marriage. **And since she is forbidden** to all the brothers, due to the *ḥalitza* performed by the deceased *yavam*, **her rival wife is also forbidden.**

Ravina then offers a more sophisticated scenario — one that does not require any transgression on the part of the brother. The case: Yaakov commits adultery with Reuven's wife, Rachel, and she bears a son.

### Yosef Birth

Specifically: Reuven marries Rachel. They have no children together. Yaakov — Reuven's father — sleeps with Rachel, and she conceives a son, Yosef. Yosef is therefore Reuven's paternal brother (they share the same father, Yaakov). Reuven did nothing wrong; he married Rachel properly. Yaakov's conduct is another matter entirely, but that is not what concerns us here.

### Yibum with Mother

Now Reuven dies, leaving Rachel childless. Rachel is Reuven's wife, and Yosef is Reuven's brother. Therefore Rachel falls before Yosef for *yibum*. But Rachel is also Yosef's mother. This is a *yibum* scenario involving one's own mother — and it arose not from any transgression by the brother, but from an adulterous act by the father. The original marriage — Reuven to Rachel — was entirely proper. So *imo* could in theory be listed in the fifteen even according to Rabbi Yehuda, without requiring any *be-di-avad* transgression by the brother.

### Heter vs. Isur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאַחְוָתָה דְּהֶתֵּירָא קָמַיְירֵי, בְּאַחְוָתָה דְּאִיסּוּרָא לָא קָמַיְירֵי. וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן, בַּדְקַהּ לֵוִי בְּמַתְנִיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **said to** Rav Ashi: The mishna **deals with** cases of **legitimate brothers,** whereas **it does not deal with cases of illegitimate brothers.** The offspring of a man who rapes his daughter-in-law is a *mamzer*, and cases of this kind are not discussed in the mishna. The Gemara comments: **Nevertheless,** despite Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's criticism, **Levi established this** additional possibility of a woman raped by one's father **in his *mishnayot*.**

Rav Kahana responds to this: *be-achvaysa de-hetera ka-mairi, be-achvaysa de-isura lo ka-mairi* — we speak of relationships that arose through a permitted union, not through a forbidden one. Yosef is Reuven's brother only because Yaakov committed adultery — an act of *isur*. The brotherly relationship itself is a product of a forbidden union. Furthermore, Yosef would be a *mamzer* — the offspring of an adulterous relationship — which raises its own complications with respect to *yibum*. The *mishnah*'s framework presupposes that the family relationships under discussion arose through ordinary, permitted means. A case of *imo* that can only be constructed through an adulterous act is not the type of case the *mishnah* addresses.

## Levi's List

### Levi's Insertion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי לֵוִי: אִמּוֹ, פְּעָמִים פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ, וּפְעָמִים אֵינָהּ פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ. כֵּיצַד? הָיְתָה אִמּוֹ נְשׂוּאַת אָבִיו, וְנִשֵּׂאת לְאָחִיו מֵאָבִיו, וּמֵת — זוֹ הִיא אִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵין פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Levi taught** in his *baraita*: With regard to the case of **his mother, at times she exempts her rival wife, and at** other **times she does not exempt her rival wife**. **How so?** If **his mother was married to his father and** after his father's death she illicitly **married** her son's **paternal brother, who** then **died, this is** a case of: **His mother who does not exempt her rival wife.**

Despite *Rabbi*'s dismissal — and the multiple layers of explanation we have now traversed — Levi was not content to leave the question unanswered by simply noting that *imo* belongs to the next *mishnah*. Instead, *badka Levi be-matnisin* — Levi inserted the case of *imo* directly into his own version of the *mishnah*, expanding the list from fifteen to sixteen.

### Married Mother

Levi's formulation states: *imo pe'amim poteret tzaratah, pe'amim einah poteret tzaratah* — one's mother sometimes exempts her co-wife and sometimes does not. He then distinguishes two scenarios. First: *hayetah imo nesuah aviv* — if his mother was his father's lawful wife. In that case, the brother can never validly marry her — it is *eshet aviv*, a category of *issur karet* — and any purported marriage would be halachically void. Therefore if a brother contracted such a marriage and died, there is no *yibum* bond and no effect on the co-wife.

### Anusat Aviv Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אִמּוֹ אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו, וְנִשֵּׂאת לְאָחִיו מֵאָבִיו, וּמֵת — זוֹ הִיא אִמּוֹ שֶׁפּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If, however, **his mother was a woman** who was **raped by his father and** subsequently **married his paternal brother, and** the brother **died, this is** a case of **his mother who exempts her rival wife.** According to the opinion of the Rabbis this marriage is permitted, and even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda the marriage is valid despite the transgression.

Second: *hayetah imo anusat aviv* — if his mother was never married to his father. A brother could validly marry her according to the *Rabbanan* who permit *anusat aviv*. If that brother then married her and died, there would be a genuine *yibum* obligation — including all the consequences for the co-wife. This is the case where *imo* does belong on the list, at least according to the *Rabbanan*.

### Sixteenth Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁנוּ חֲכָמִים בְּמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ ״חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה״, יֵשׁ לָנוּ לְהוֹסִיף שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה כְּגוֹן זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And although the Sages taught in the mishna: Fifteen** women, **we must add a sixteenth** case, **for example,** the situation described above.

Levi therefore concludes that while the *Rabbanan* established fifteen cases, *yesh lanu le-hosif shesh esrei ke-gon zu* — there is a sixteenth case possible, of this type. Levi's underlying point is that rather than accepting the explanation that the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda and therefore categorically excludes *imo*, he preferred to flag the case of *imo as anusat aviv* as a potential addition to the list. The list of fifteen was ancient and authoritative — the very fact that the *Amoraim* debated why it contains fifteen and not sixteen confirms that this number had long been established. Levi's response was not to dispute that established count but to annotate it: the number is fifteen, the ancient tradition is clear, and yet there exists a sixteenth case of this kind worth noting. It is that annotation which Levi incorporated into his own recension of the *mishnah*.
