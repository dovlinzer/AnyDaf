# Menachot 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parshiyot Setumot

### Rav Huna's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: חֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא דְּכָרֵיךְ לַהּ מֵ״אֶחָד״ כְּלַפֵּי ״שְׁמַע״, וְעוֹשֶׂה פָּרָשִׁיּוֹתֶיהָ סְתוּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥelbo said: I saw Rav Huna wrap** a written *mezuza* **from** the word ***eḥad* to** the word ***shema*,** i.e., rolling it from left to right, as the first verse written in a *mezuza* is: "Listen [*Shema*], O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one [*eḥad*]" (Deuteronomy 6:4). **And** he **prepared** the two **passages of** the *mezuza* in the **closed** manner, i.e., starting the second passage (Deuteronomy 11:13–21) on the same line that he finished writing the first passage (Deuteronomy 6:4–9).

The Gemara on 36b records the following teaching: Rabbi Chelbo reports that he observed Rav Huna rolling his *mezuzah* from *Echad* towards *Shema* — that is, scrolling it up from left to right so that when one opens it, one begins at the beginning. Rabbi Chelbo further notes that Rav Huna made its *parshiyot setumot* — he arranged the two sections, *Shema* and *Ve-hayah im shamoa*, with only a closed, partial break between them.

### Petuchot/Setumot

To appreciate the significance of this practice, one must understand the distinction between *petuchot* and *setumot*. In a traditional *Chumash* — such as the classic *Chumash Vistretz* — this is indicated visually by the letters *peh* and *samech*, which Artscroll renders graphically. *Peh* stands for *patuach* and *samech* for *setum*. Despite the *samech* being rarer, *setum* actually represents the smaller break. *Setum* means closed: a line of text ends mid-line, a span of white space follows, and then the next section begins on the same line toward the end — a paragraph-level break but not a full section break. *Patuach*, by contrast, means open: the text stops mid-line, white space fills the remainder of that line, and the next section begins on the following line — a larger, section-level break.

*Tosafot* cites the *Yerushalmi* and other opinions suggesting an even larger variant of *patuach*: the text ends mid-line, the rest of the line is left blank, and then an entire additional blank line follows before the next section begins — equivalent to a double line-feed. There is no established *mesorah* for this form in the Torah, though apparently some Dead Sea Scroll texts do employ it where a conceptually larger section break is warranted. The fundamental distinction, then, is that *patuach* is the larger break and *setum* is the smaller one.

### Shema in Torah

The question of how the *mezuzah* sections relate to one another depends on how they actually appear in the Torah text itself. When one examines the Torah, one finds that the *Shema* — *Devarim* 6:4–9 — concludes with *u-visharecha*, after which there is only a *setum*, a minor break, before *Ve-hayah ki yeviacha*. Similarly, *Ve-hayah im shamoa* (*Devarim* 11:13–21) is also *setum* with respect to the parasha that precedes it. The implication is that the *Shema* flows into what follows it with a measure of continuity, and *Ve-hayah im shamoa* is similarly continuous with what precedes it. If one were placing these two *parshiyot* side by side in a *mezuzah* and wished to reflect their character as they appear in the Torah, the natural choice would be *setumot* — indicating a measure of continuity or modest separation — rather than *petuchot*. That is precisely what Rav Huna did.

## Rabbi Meir/Shimon

### Rabbi Meir Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הָיָה כּוֹתְבָהּ עַל דּוּכְסוּסְטוֹס כְּמִין דַּף, וְעוֹשֶׂה רֶיוַח מִלְּמַעְלָה וְרֶיוַח מִלְּמַטָּה, וְעוֹשֶׂה פָּרָשִׁיּוֹתֶיהָ פְּתוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said: Rabbi Meir would write** a *mezuza* **on *dokhsostos*,** the inner layer of animal hide, not on parchment, which is from the outer layer, and he would prepare it **like a column** of a Torah scroll, i.e., long and narrow. **And he would make a space above and a space below** the text **and would prepare the passages of** the *mezuza* in the **open** manner, i.e., he would begin the second passage on the line following the end of the first passage.

The Gemara then introduces a challenge from the following *beraita*. Rabbi Shimon Elazar reports that Rabbi Meir — one of the most celebrated scribes in the Talmud, who actually practiced this craft professionally — would write the *mezuzah* on *duchsostos*. We will address precisely what *duchsostos* is below, but it is a specific type of parchment derived from a particular layer of the animal hide. Rabbi Shimon Elazar further records that Rabbi Meir would write it *k'min daf* — in the format of a page, meaning, as Rashi notes, *arucha u-ketzara*, a narrow column rather than a wide format. Rabbi Meir would also make a *revach* — a margin — above and below. And crucially: *ve-oseh parshiyoteha petuchot* — he made the *parshiyot petuchot*, with a full, open break between the two sections.

### Shimon's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: רַבִּי, מָה טַעַם? אָמַר לִי: הוֹאִיל וְאֵין סְמוּכוֹת מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **I said to him: My teacher,** for **what reason** do you prepare the passages in the open manner, when in a Torah scroll those same passages are written in the closed manner? **He said to me: Since** the passages **are not adjacent** to one another **in the Torah,** as the first passage is Deuteronomy 6:4–9 and the second is Deuteronomy 11:13–21, I prepare them as open passages.

Rabbi Shimon Elazar then describes his own exchange with Rabbi Meir. He says: *Rebbi, mah ta'am* — why do you write the *parshiyot petuchot*, given that they appear *setumot* in the Torah? The implicit argument is that the natural presentation, as the sections appear in the Torah itself, would suggest making the break *setum* rather than *patuach*.

### Not Juxtaposed

Rabbi Meir answered: *ho'il ve-ein smuchot min ha-Torah* — since in the Torah the two *parshiyot* are not actually juxtaposed with one another. The *Shema* and *Ve-hayah im shamoa* appear in entirely different places in the Torah, separated by intervening text. The quality of *setum* reflects a sense of visual and textual continuity between adjacent sections of a single text. Since these two *parshiyot* do not actually appear next to one another in the Torah, the *setumot* designation is inapplicable — there is no actual continuity to reflect. Therefore, Rabbi Meir chose to write them *petuchot*.

One might ask whether Rabbi Meir means that *petuchot* is the only valid option, or merely that *petuchot* is permissible given the absence of actual juxtaposition in the Torah. The Gemara will address this question shortly.

## Margins/Rav Ruling

### Ruling: Margins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל, אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, מַאי לָאו אַפְּתוּחוֹת? לָא, אַרֶיוַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. What, is it not** correct that Rav stated this **with regard to** Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's opinion that one prepares the passages in the **open** manner? This would present a difficulty to the opinion of Rav Huna, Rav's student, who wrote them in the closed manner. The Gemara answers: **No;** he meant that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar **with regard to** the **space** that one must leave above and below the text.

The Gemara records: *Amar Rabbi Chananel amar Rav, halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon Elazar* — we rule like Rabbi Shimon Elazar. This is notable, since the discussion was structured as a story about Rabbi Meir's scribal practice, yet the Gemara understands it as a halachic dispute and issues a ruling accordingly. The implication would seem to be that we rule in favor of *petuchot* over *setumot*, in apparent contradiction to Rav Huna's practice of *setumot* described at the outset.

### Clarification

The Gemara immediately asks: *mai lav a-petuchot* — does this ruling not refer to the *petuchot* question? And it answers: *lo, a-revach* — no, perhaps the ruling refers specifically to the matter of margins, not to *petuchot* versus *setumot*. Since Rabbi Meir is recorded as making margins above and below the text, and this practice is not self-evidently required, the ruling *like Rabbi Shimon Elazar* who cited Rabbi Meir may simply mean that margins are halachically necessary — not that we are endorsing *petuchot* over *setumot*.

## Resolution

### Scrolls Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה רֶיוַח? אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יַעֲקֹב: כִּמְלֵא אַטְבָּא דְּסִיפְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much space** must one leave? **Rav Menashya bar Ya'akov says, and some say** it is **Rav Shmuel bar Ya'akov** who **says:** The space **of a full scribe's clip [*atba*],** with which the sheets of parchment are held.

This is a genuine *chiddush*, because the historical evidence suggests that margins were not standard practice. The Dead Sea Scroll *tefillin* fragments — a transcription and near-visual reproduction of which was distributed for examination — show that when scribes of that period wrote *tefillin*, they left no margins whatsoever. Because *tefillin* and *mezuzah* parchments were typically byproducts of the larger sheets prepared for *Sifrei Torah* — the scraps and off-cuts from the edges of those larger sheets — they were small pieces of parchment onto which text was squeezed as efficiently as possible. Given that neither *tefillin* nor *mezuzah* are generally opened and read, aesthetic concerns of *hiddur* were less pressing. The ruling that one *does* require a margin for a *mezuzah* is therefore a meaningful halachic statement. And it is this requirement — the *revach* — that constitutes the content of the ruling like Rabbi Shimon Elazar, not the *patuach*/*setum* question.

## Margins/Rav Ruling…

### Margin Width

The Gemara then asks: *ve-kamah revach* — how wide must the margin be? Rav Menashya bar Yaakov, and some say Rav Shmuel bar Yaakov, answers: *ke-melo itba de-safrei* — like the width of an *itba de-safrei*. Rashi explains that an *itba de-safrei* was a thin implement — something like a paperclip or narrow holder used to clip pages together and prevent them from folding. The margin required for a *mezuzah* is therefore minimal — nothing like the three or four fingers required for a *Sefer Torah*. Since the *mezuzah* is typically not viewed once installed, the aesthetic requirements are calibrated accordingly, and only a narrow, thin margin is mandated.

## Practice/Minhag

### Abaye Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: וְאַתְּ לָא תִּסְבְּרָא דְּכִי אָמַר רַב אַרֶיוַח, וְהָא רַב אִית לֵיהּ מִנְהֲגָא, וְהָאִידָּנָא נְהוּג עָלְמָא בִּסְתוּמוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: And you, do you not hold that when Rav said** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar he was referring **to** the **space,** not the manner of writing the passages? **But Rav is of** the opinion that an established **custom** must be observed, **and nowadays the general custom** is to write the passages of the *mezuza* **in** the **closed** manner.

At this point Abaye raises a question to Rav Yosef: *ha lav tishparta* — do you not hold that when Rav said we rule like Rabbi Shimon Elazar, we must be speaking about the margins and not the *patuach*/*setum* question? For how could Rav be ruling in favor of *petuchot* when established practice clearly runs in the other direction? *Ve-ha Rav it lei minhaga* — Rav, after all, holds that *minhag* governs.

### Minhag Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבָּה, אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, אָמַר רַב: אִם יָבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וְיֹאמַר חוֹלְצִין בְּמִנְעָל – שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, אֵין חוֹלְצִין בְּסַנְדָּל – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, שֶׁכְּבָר נָהֲגוּ הָעָם בְּסַנְדָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides the source that according to Rav one must observe established customs. *Ḥalitza* is the ritual that frees the widow of a childless man from the obligation to enter into levirate marriage with her late husband's brother. This ceremony involves the widow removing her brother-in-law's sandal from his foot. Rabba spoke of the importance of observing customs in that context, **as Rabba says** that **Rav Kahana says** that **Rav says: If Elijah comes and says** that **one performs *ḥalitza* with a shoe,** the Sages **listen to him.** But if he says that **one may not perform *ḥalitza* with a sandal, they do not listen to him, as the people are already accustomed** to performing *ḥalitza* **with a sandal.**

This phrase — *Rav it lei minhaga*, that Rav follows *minhag* — requires careful unpacking, because it involves a central question in the theory of *psak halacha*: how much weight does established communal practice carry relative to canonical textual sources?

One must distinguish between two related but distinct concepts. The Hebrew word *minhag* can refer either to a custom — a non-mandated activity that communities have adopted over time — or to a *practice*, meaning what the people have actually been doing. The critical halachic question is whether *psak halacha* is determined primarily by what the canonical sources say, or significantly by what established practice has been.

Haym Soloveitchik, in his essay "Rupture and Reconstruction," explores this tension at length. We have become, in modernity, a thoroughly text-based tradition — and we are therefore sometimes startled to encounter the idea that accepted practice might override or reframe what a *gemara* seems to say. But this was not always the dominant orientation. In traditional societies, the living tradition — what was actually handed down and practiced — was primary. Indeed, Jacob Katz, the pioneering historian of *halacha*, observes that when *Tosafot* sought to refine or adjust halachic norms based on the written *Gemara*, they were perceived by contemporaries as the innovators, even the reformers. "Our practice has always been thus — you want to change it because of something written in a book somewhere?" In this light, what we read as *Tosafot*'s accommodation of practice to text was actually, from the traditional perspective, a revolutionary move toward textualism. *Tosafot* does maintain a dialectic — they are prepared at times to bring practice under the scrutiny of the text — but the older model gave practice primary weight.

This dynamic is visible in the contrast between the *Mishnah Berurah* and the *Arukh HaShulchan*. The *Mishnah Berurah* proceeds from the *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* and issues rulings accordingly, at times explicitly correcting what the community is doing. The *Arukh HaShulchan*, by contrast, frequently invokes *minhag Yisrael Torah hi* or *minhag Yisrael kedoshim*, and sees it as a *mitzvah* to find ways of sustaining established communal practice — reinterpreting sources to align with what communities have been doing, and sometimes simply stating that despite the *machloket* in the literature, the *minhag* in *Lita* has always been such-and-such, and therefore that is what we do. Part of this difference in orientation reflects not only differing intellectual approaches but differing institutional positions: *halacha* has traditionally been *paskened* by rabbis embedded in communities, not by those operating within the *yeshiva* context — a distinction with significant implications for how *psak* functions today.

With this in mind, returning to our Gemara: the practice at the time was that *mezuzot* were written with *setumot* — the smaller, closed break — rather than *petuchot*. Given that Rav follows the weight of established practice, how could one suppose that Rav issued a ruling in favor of *petuchot*? The answer is that he did not. When Rav said we rule like Rabbi Shimon Elazar, he must have been speaking about the *revach* — the margin — not about overriding the established practice of writing *setumot*.

### Eliyahu/Chalitzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, אָמַר רַב: אִם יָבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וְיֹאמַר אֵין חוֹלְצִין בְּמִנְעָל – שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, אֵין חוֹלְצִין בְּסַנְדָּל – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, שֶׁכְּבָר נָהֲגוּ הָעָם בְּסַנְדָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another version of Rav's statement: **And Rav Yosef says** that **Rav Kahana says** that **Rav says: If Elijah comes and says** that **one may not perform *ḥalitza* with a shoe,** the Sages **listen to him;** if he says that **one may not perform *ḥalitza* with a sandal, they do not listen to him, as the people are already accustomed** to performing *ḥalitza* **with a sandal.**

The Gemara now brings the source that establishes Rav's principle regarding *minhag*. Rabbah said in the name of Rav Kahana, who said in the name of Rav: *Im yavo Eliyahu veyomar, choltzin be-min'al, shomein lo* — if Eliyahu HaNavi were to come and declare that *chalitzah* may be performed with a *min'al* (a closed shoe, as opposed to an open sandal), we would listen to him. But *ein choltzin be-sandal, ein shomein lo* — if Eliyahu were to say that a *sandal* is invalid for *chalitzah*, we would not listen to him. Why? *She-kvar nahagu ha-am be-sandal* — because the people have long established the practice of using a *sandal* for *chalitzah*, and that practice cannot be overridden.

This is a striking *gemara*. One should note, however, that the principle is stated with respect to Eliyahu HaNavi, whose halachic authority is itself not straightforward. The principle that *ein navi rasha'i lechadesh davar* — a prophet has no standing to introduce new halachic rulings — applies to Eliyahu as to any prophet. His weight in halachic discourse may be no greater than that of any rabbi, and perhaps less. Invoking Eliyahu, therefore, does not necessarily imply that established practice would override a weighty, unambiguous canonical source. What the Gemara does establish is that Rav assigns meaningful halachic weight to well-established communal practice — enough to resist even prophetic instruction. There is also a parallel formulation in the *Yerushalmi* — *minhag mevattel halacha* — that goes even further, and was occasionally cited by the *Geonim*, though how far that principle extends remains its own question.

### Ambiguous/Established

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? מִנְעָל לְכַתְּחִילָּה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַרֶיוַח? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And we say,** when discussing these versions of his statement: **What** is the difference **between** these two versions of his statement? The **difference is** whether one may use **a shoe *ab initio*.** In any case, according to both statements Rav maintains that a custom must be observed, and the custom in this case is to write the passages in a closed manner. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that when Rav says that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar he was speaking **of** the **space,** not the manner of preparing the passages? The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that this is correct.

The Gemara then records a second version of the teaching from Rav Yosef: Rav Kahana said in the name of Rav — if Eliyahu were to declare that a *min'al* is *invalid* for *chalitzah*, we would listen to him; but if he declared that a *sandal* is invalid, we would not, again because of established practice.

The two versions differ: the first says we would listen to Eliyahu *permitting* the *min'al*; the second says we would listen to Eliyahu *prohibiting* the *min'al*. The Gemara asks: *mai beinayhu* — what is the practical difference between these two versions? The answer is: *min'al le-chatchila ika beinayhu* — the question of whether the *min'al* is currently acceptable in the first place. The version that says we would follow Eliyahu in permitting the *min'al* assumes the *min'al* is currently in a state of limited or ambiguous use — not established practice, not clearly prohibited, but not commonly employed. We would accept prophetic guidance to normalize it. The version that says we would follow Eliyahu in prohibiting the *min'al* assumes the *min'al* is currently somewhat acceptable, at least in practice, but since it is not firmly established, we would accept guidance that it should be discontinued. In either case, what both versions share — and what is the operative point for our discussion — is that neither version would follow Eliyahu if he sought to invalidate the *sandal*, because the use of the *sandal* is thoroughly established. The distinction, then, is between an ambiguous practice with some currency but no consensus, which remains susceptible to authoritative guidance, and a well-established practice, which does not.

### Ashkenazi/Sephardi

Chaim Soloveitchik has observed that this tension between text and practice played out historically along geographic and communal lines. *Ashkenazi* communities tended to assign greater weight to established practice, and for understandable reasons: these were cloistered, insular communities that had maintained their traditions through enormous sacrifice, including the martyrdom of the Crusades. For such communities, one looks at the practices of one's community and presumes they are correct — the *frumkeit* of the people is not in question. *Sefardi* communities, by contrast, were more integrated into the surrounding culture, more socially variegated, and in moments of extremity — such as forced conversion — more prone to internal deviation. When *Sefardi Rishonim* looked at their communities' practices, they were less confident that every deviation from the canonical text reflected a legitimate tradition rather than laxity. This difference in communal self-perception directly shaped the methodology of *psak*: whether one approaches an established practice with deference, seeking to validate it from the sources, or with scrutiny, evaluating it against the text.

## Resolution…

### Proof Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מִצְוָה לַעֲשׂוֹתָן סְתוּמוֹת, וְאִי עַבְדִינְהוּ פְּתוּחוֹת – שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, וּמַאי פְּתוּחוֹת דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר – אַף פְּתוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It is **a mitzva** *ab initio* **to prepare** the passages of a *mezuza* in the **closed** manner, **but if one prepared them** in the **open** manner, it is **permitted** to use the *mezuza*. **And what is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar saying** when he says that Rabbi Meir would prepare the passages in the **open** manner? He means that one may prepare them **even** in the **open** manner.

Returning to the main thread: the Gemara says *ela lav, shema mina* — this must be a proof that when Rav ruled like Rabbi Meir, it was specifically about the *revach* (the margin), not about *petuchot*, since the established practice is *setumot* and Rav would not issue a ruling against established practice. *Shema mina* — this is accepted.

The Gemara then seeks to support the view that even Rabbi Meir would concede the validity of *setumot*. It cites a *beraita*: *Sefer Torah she-bala u-tefillin she-balu* — if a *Sefer Torah* has worn out and *tefillin* have worn out with age, can one reuse the parchment? The *beraita* rules: *ein osim mehem mezuza* — one may not use such parchment to make a *mezuzah* — whether by writing new text upon it or by cutting out the *Shema* and *Ve-hayah im shamoa* and placing them in a *mezuzah*. The reason is *lefi she-ein moridin mi-kedusha chamura li-kedusha kala* — because one may not lower an object from a higher *kedusha* to a lower one. A *Sefer Torah* and *tefillin* possess a higher *kedusha* than a *mezuzah*, and therefore one may not repurpose their parchment for a *mezuzah*.

### Rav Natan Ruling

Rav Natan bar Yitzchak states: *mitzvah la-asotan setumot* — the proper practice, *lechatchila*, is to write the *parshiyot* of the *mezuzah setumot*, reflecting how each *parsha* appears individually in the Torah. *Ve-im avdinan petuchot, shapir dami* — but if one wrote them *petuchot*, as Rabbi Meir chose to do, this is also acceptable. Since the two *parshiyot* are not actually juxtaposed in the Torah, there is latitude in either direction. *Setumot* is preferable because it reflects the independent character of each *parsha* as it appears in its own context in the Torah; *petuchot* is valid because, in the *mezuzah*, these sections do not appear next to one another in the way that would demand *setum* continuity.

### Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ, כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁבָּלָה וּתְפִילִּין שֶׁבָּלוּ – אֵין עוֹשִׂין מֵהֶן מְזוּזָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מוֹרִידִין מִקְּדוּשָּׁה חֲמוּרָה לִקְדוּשָּׁה קַלָּה. הָא מוֹרִידִין – עוֹשִׂין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that a *baraita* **supports his** opinion: **Similarly,** just as one may not convert phylacteries of the head into phylacteries of the arm, with regard to **a Torah scroll that became worn and** parchment of **phylacteries that became worn, one may not fashion them into a *mezuza*** by excising the relevant passages, despite the fact that the Torah passages of a *mezuza* appear in them. This is prohibited **because one does not reduce** the sanctity of an item **from** a level of **greater sanctity,** that of a Torah scroll or phylacteries, **to** a level of **lesser sanctity,** that of a *mezuza*. The Gemara infers from this *baraita*: If it were permitted to **reduce** the sanctity of an item from a level of greater sanctity to a level of lesser sanctity, one could **fashion** a *mezuza* from a Torah scroll.

The Gemara then clarifies the meaning of Rabbi Shimon Elazar's quotation of Rabbi Meir: *u-mai petuchot di-kamar Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar? De-kamar af petuchot* — when Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar cited Rabbi Meir's practice of *petuchot*, he meant that Rabbi Meir holds that even *petuchot* is acceptable. This is not quite the same as Rabbi Meir's own *lechatchila* preference for *petuchot*, since we now establish *setumot* as the *lechatchila* and *petuchot* as the *bediavad*. But the practical conclusion holds: the two positions are not in fundamental contradiction. The halacha is that *lechatchila* one writes *setumot*, but *bediavad* *petuchot* is valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? הָכָא סְתוּמוֹת וְהָכָא פְּתוּחוֹת! דִּלְמָא לְהַשְׁלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: But **why** is that the *halakha*, when **here,** in a Torah scroll, the passages are prepared in the **closed** manner, **but there,** in a *mezuza*, the passages are prepared in the **open** manner? Evidently, it is permitted to write a *mezuza* with the passages prepared in the closed manner. The Gemara refutes this proof: **Perhaps** one should infer from the *baraita* that were it not for the fact that it is prohibited to reduce the sanctity of an item from a level of greater sanctity to a level of lesser sanctity, one would be allowed **to complete** a line or two of a *mezuza* by sewing to it those lines from a Torah scroll or parchment of phylacteries that became worn, but one may not fashion an entire *mezuza* from a sheet of a Torah scroll or parchment of phylacteries, as the passages in a Torah scroll and phylacteries are prepared in the closed manner.

The Gemara attempts to further support the view that even Rabbi Meir accepts *setumot* from the *beraita* regarding recycled *Sefer Torah* parchment. The argument is as follows: if not for the problem of *moridin* — lowering from higher to lower *kedusha* — one could in principle use *Sefer Torah* parchment for a *mezuzah*. Yet in the *Sefer Torah* the relevant sections appear *setumot*, while in the *mezuzah* Rabbi Meir writes them *petuchot*. If this transfer were permissible, it would presumably be in the *setum* format in which they appear in the Torah. This implies that even Rabbi Meir would agree that *setumot* is a valid way to write the *mezuzah*. The Gemara, however, deflects this proof: *lo, dilma le-hashlim* — perhaps the *beraita* is speaking only of a case where one is supplementing an incomplete *mezuzah* — adding, say, the *Shema* from a *Sefer Torah* to a *mezuzah* that already has the *Ve-hayah im shamoa*, or vice versa. In that case, since only one *parsha* is being added rather than both being juxtaposed, no inference about the *setumot*/*petuchot* question can be drawn. The proof remains inconclusive, but the ruling stands as stated.

### Tosafot Variants

*Tosafot* at the top of the page offers an illuminating discussion of what *petuchot* and *setumot* actually look like in practice, reflecting the range of opinions and the lack of universal standardization. *Tosafot* cites Rashi's view and then adds: *ve-anu nohagim lehani'ach chalak me'at bi-tchilat shita ve-achar kach matchil Ve-hayah im shamoa ve-shema gam ze karui stuma* — our practice is to leave a small blank at the beginning of the line and then begin *Ve-hayah im shamoa*, and perhaps this too is called *setuma*. This is, in effect, what we would call indenting — beginning the new *parsha* not at the very start of the line but a few spaces in — which creates a visible but modest break.

*Rabbeinu Tam*, by contrast, explains *patucha* as the case where a *parsha* ends in the middle of a line and the remainder of the line is left blank — the measure being approximately the space of the word *le-mishpechoteihem*. If that space is not left, the result is something different, and he goes on to distinguish multiple variants depending on the size of the gap and where it falls. One variant that particularly stands out is leaving not just the remainder of the line blank but an entire additional blank line — a double line-feed — which apparently appears in some Dead Sea Scroll texts at points of major conceptual transition. The Gemara does not spell out these visual formats with precision, and *Tosafot*'s discussion makes clear that the exact visual rendering of *setum* and *patuach* was not universally standardized, even among the *Rishonim*.

## Klaf/Duchsostos

### Halacha Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי – תְּפִילִּין עַל הַקְּלָף, וּמְזוּזָה עַל דּוּכְסוּסְטוֹס; קְלָף בִּמְקוֹם בָּשָׂר, דּוּכְסוּסְטוֹס בִּמְקוֹם שֵׂעָר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: The *baraita* indicates **that** if it were permitted to **reduce** the sanctity of an item from a level of greater sanctity to a level of lesser sanctity, one could **make** a *mezuza* from phylacteries. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that it is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai** that the passages of **phylacteries** are written **on parchment,** the outer layer of an animal's hide, **and** the passages of **a *mezuza*** are written **on *dokhsostos*,** the inner layer, and when writing on **parchment,** one writes **on the side of** the hide that faced the **flesh;** when writing on ***dokhsostos*,** one writes **on the side of** the hide on which there was **hair?**

The Gemara now turns to a related question: on what type of parchment must *tefillin* and *mezuzah* be written? The discussion is grounded in a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*: *tefillin al ha-klaf u-mezuzah al duchsostos* — *tefillin* are written on *klaf* and *mezuzah* on *duchsostos*.

### Leather Parts

What are *klaf* and *duchsostos*? The Gemara explains: *klaf be-makom basar, duchsostos be-makom se'ar* — *klaf* is the surface of the skin that faced the flesh of the animal, and *duchsostos* is the surface that faced the hair.

To understand this practically: the leather taken from an animal has a certain thickness. That thickness is split into two layers. One layer is the inner layer — the side that was in contact with the animal's flesh; the other is the outer layer — the side that was in contact with the hair. *Klaf*, used for *tefillin*, is the inner layer, and one writes on the surface of the *klaf* that faced the flesh — the *makom basar*. *Duchsostos*, used for *mezuzah*, is the outer layer, and one writes on the surface that faced the hair — the *makom se'ar*. The terminology *duchsostos* is Greek in origin.

### Recycling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמִצְוָה. וְהָתַנְיָא: שִׁינָּה פָּסוּל בִּתְפִילִּין!
>
> **Translation:** How, then, can one use the other side of the hide for a *mezuza*? The Gemara answers that this requirement of *dokhsostos* for a *mezuza* is stated **as a mitzva,** but it is not indispensable. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one **changed** between parchment and *dokhsostos*, the item is **unfit?**

This specification raises the question of recycling parchment. The *beraita* already cited establishes: *ein osim mehem mezuza* — one may not use worn *Sefer Torah* or *tefillin* parchment to make a *mezuzah*. On what basis?

Two distinct grounds are relevant. The first is the rule of *moridin*: one may not lower an object from higher *kedusha* to lower *kedusha*. The second is the parchment specification just stated: *tefillin* require *klaf* and *mezuzah* requires *duchsostos*, and one cannot repurpose parchment designated for one across the *klaf*/*duchsostos* divide.

### Moridin

One would like to cut out the *Shema* from a worn *Sefer Torah* and place it in a *mezuzah*, or take the *Shema* from *tefillin* parchment and reuse it. This is prohibited by *lefi she-ein moridin mi-kedusha chamura li-kedusha kala* — one does not lower from a higher *kedusha* to a lower one. A *Sefer Torah* and *tefillin* possess a higher *kedusha* than a *mezuzah*. By contrast, the reverse direction — taking parchment from a *mezuzah* and using it for *tefillin* or a *Sefer Torah* — would be an elevation in *kedusha* and is in principle permissible, following the principle of *ma'alin ba-kodesh ve-lo moridin*.

### Flexibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: שִׁינָּה בֵּין בָּזֶה וּבֵין בְּזֶה פָּסוּל! אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בִּתְפִילִּין, וְהָא דְּכַתְבִינְהוּ אַקְּלָף בִּמְקוֹם שֵׂעָר, וְהָא דְּכַתְבִינְהוּ אַדּוּכְסוּסְטוֹס בִּמְקוֹם בָּשָׂר. הִילְכְתָא: תְּפִילִּין לָא בָּעֵי שִׂרְטוּט, וּמְזוּזָה בָּעֲיָא שִׂירְטוּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one **changed whether in this** manner **or in that** manner, it is **unfit?** The Gemara explains that this *baraita* does not mean that one changed either in the case of phylacteries or a *mezuza*. Rather, both **this** manner **and that** manner are referring **to phylacteries, and this** case is **where one wrote them on parchment** but **on the side of** the hide on which there was **hair,** not on the side that faced the flesh, **and that** case is **where one wrote them on *dokhsostos* on the side** that faced the **flesh.** In both of these situations the phylacteries are unfit, but a *mezuza* that one wrote on parchment is fit. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that the parchment of **phylacteries does not require scoring, but** the parchment of **a *mezuza* requires scoring.**

The Gemara then discusses the degree of flexibility available by *mezuzah* relative to *tefillin* with respect to parchment type. A *beraita* states that *shina*, changing the parchment specification, renders the object *pasul*. The Gemara clarifies that this stringency applies specifically to *tefillin*: the *klaf* for *tefillin* must be precisely the designated type, and any variation — whether using *klaf be-makom se'ar* instead of *klaf be-makom basar*, or using *duchsostos be-makom basar* instead of the correct side — invalidates the *tefillin*. By *mezuzah*, however, there is greater latitude: the range of parchments that may be used is broader. Were it not for the *moridin* prohibition, one could theoretically use *tefillin* parchment for a *mezuzah*, since the *mezuzah* tolerates a wider range of parchment types. The *tefillin* parchment specification, by contrast, admits no flexibility — any deviation from the precise *klaf be-makom basar* is *pasul*.

The upshot is that while the *mezuzah* has a specified parchment in its *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, it is somewhat more flexible in what it accepts than the highly precise *klaf* requirement of *tefillin*. This asymmetry is itself notable: in certain formal requirements, such as *ke-sidran* and the parchment specification, the *mezuzah* and *tefillin* differ in degree of stringency, and not always in the direction one might expect.
