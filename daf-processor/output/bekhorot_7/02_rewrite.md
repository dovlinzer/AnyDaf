# Bekhorot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Camel Repetition

### Non-Kosher Principle

The *sugya* opens by returning to a foundational principle that will occupy the *gemara* for several days: *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei* — products and offspring of non-kosher animals are themselves non-kosher, while those of kosher animals are kosher. This principle encompasses not only the offspring of non-kosher animals but also their products, including milk. The milk of a camel, for instance, is non-kosher. Additionally, according to Rabbi Shimon's distinctive position, even the offspring of a kosher animal that *looks* like a non-kosher one is non-kosher.

### Ella Me'ata Challenge

One of the derivations for this principle rested on the fact that the Torah repeats the word *gamal* — once in Vayikra and once in Devarim — teaching not only that the camel itself is non-kosher, but extending that status to the camel's milk or, according to Rabbi Shimon, to any offspring of a cow that resembles a camel. The *gemara* now challenges this reliance on repetition. If the doubling of *gamal* teaches something new, then the repetition of *shafan*, *arnevet*, and *chazir* — the hare, the rabbit, and the pig — should similarly generate new derivations. Yet no such *drashot* are offered for those animals.

### Shasua and Ra'ah

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a *baraita* that explains why the lists of kosher and non-kosher species were repeated in Devarim at all. The mammals were repeated on account of the *shasua*, an unusual animal with a double spine that needed to be included among the non-kosher species. The birds were repeated because one passage uses the term *da'ah* and the other uses *ra'ah*, which the *gemara* understands as two distinct species — hence the repetition was necessary to capture both. By this logic, the repetition of *gamal gamal* is likewise explained as part of the general re-statement of the list, not as a basis for a new *drasha*.

### Drasha Rule

The *gemara* responds with a general rule of interpretation: *kol heikha de'ika lemidrash darshinan* — wherever a *drasha* is possible, we make a *drasha*. When there is something to derive, we take advantage of the repetition. Only where no *drasha* presents itself do we attribute the doubling to the need to include a new species or clarify a new term. Therefore, since a meaningful *drasha* can be extracted from *gamal gamal*, we apply it, without demanding that every other repeated animal name yield a parallel derivation.

## Rabbi Shimon View

### Sheep/Goat Offspring

The *gemara* turns to the *baraita* on the *mishna*'s ruling: a sheep that gives birth to something resembling a goat, or a goat that gives birth to something resembling a sheep, is exempt from *bechorah* — firstborn status. However, if the offspring has some characteristics resembling the mother's species, it is obligated. This is the *stam* position of the *mishna*.

### Head & Majority

Rabbi Shimon raises the threshold considerably. He holds that even partial resemblance to the mother is insufficient for the purposes of *bechorah*; the offspring is obligated only if its head and the majority of its body resemble the mother — *ad she'yihiye rosho ve'rubo domeh le'imo*. The head is a primary marker of identity, and the majority of the body must correspondingly reflect the mother's species. Minor traits belonging to a different species do not disqualify, but the predominant appearance must align with the mother. This standard is stated explicitly by Rabbi Shimon in the context of *bechorah*, but the *gemara* now examines whether it applies equally to the question of *kashrut*.

### Appearance & Kashrut

The *gemara* asks: for the offspring of a cow that looks like a camel, how much must it resemble its kosher mother in order to be permitted for eating according to Rabbi Shimon? Must it satisfy the same *rosho ve'rubo* requirement, or does something less — some minimal characteristic resembling the mother — suffice?

This question turns on an important asymmetry. Regarding *bechorah*, the defining category is positive: the animal must look like an ox, like a sheep. The Torah says *ach bechor shor* — however the firstborn of an ox — emphasizing *ad she'yehei shor u'bechoro shor*, that the firstborn must fully resemble the correct species. Given this framing, Rabbi Shimon naturally demands that the majority of the offspring appear like the mother. But by the laws of non-kosher animals, the Torah is not defining what an animal must look like to be *kosher*; rather, it is defining what makes it *non-kosher* — if the offspring of a cow looks like a camel, it is forbidden. If so, one might reason that the animal need not fully resemble a camel to be forbidden, and conversely, even a few characteristics resembling the cow might be enough to save it. The question, in other words, is whether the categories operate symmetrically.

### Baraita Inference

The *gemara* brings a *baraita* to resolve the question: a kosher animal that gives birth to something resembling a non-kosher species is *assur ba'achilah* — forbidden to eat. However, if its head and majority resemble the mother, it is *chayav be'vechorah* — obligated in *bechorah*. The *gemara* initially reasons that since the *baraita* moves directly from forbidding the non-kosher-looking offspring to requiring *rosho ve'rubo* for *bechorah*, the same standard must implicitly govern *achilah* as well. *Shema mina* — from here one may infer that Rabbi Shimon requires the head and majority even for eating.

### Leapfrog Logic

The *gemara* immediately counters this inference. Perhaps the opposite is true. The *baraita* entirely *skipped over* the question of eating and jumped to *bechorah* — *deika nami d'ka shavik le'achilah ve'ka kamlei a'bechorah*. This "leapfrogging" may itself be meaningful: the *baraita* had something specific to say about *bechorah* and said nothing about eating, which suggests that for eating the standard is more lenient. Even some characteristics resembling the mother suffice, and no special statement was required.

### Resolution

The *gemara* resolves the ambiguity. In truth, one could say that Rabbi Shimon requires *rosho ve'rubo* for eating as well, and the reason the *baraita* focused on *bechorah* is that it needed to correct a possible misconception. Given the verse *ach bechor shor* — which emphasizes *ad she'yehei shor u'bechoro shor* — one might have thought that even the head and majority is insufficient for *bechorah*, that the animal must be entirely and completely of the right species. The *baraita* therefore teaches *ka mashma lan* — it comes to inform us that head and majority is indeed enough for *bechorah*. It was not teaching us anything about eating by omission; it was simply addressing a specific concern about firstborn status. Accordingly, the question of what Rabbi Shimon requires for *achilah* remains formally unresolved by this *baraita*.

## One Characteristic

### Siman Echad

Another *baraita* is brought: from the verse *et zeh lo tochlu mimaleh ha-gerah u-mimafrizei ha-parsa* — this you shall not eat, from those that regurgitate their cud and split their hooves — we derive *zeh atah ochel aval atah ochel ha-ba be-siman echad*: you may not eat these non-kosher animals, but you *may* eat something that looks somewhat like them yet retains one characteristic (*siman echad*) like its mother. This operates entirely within Rabbi Shimon's framework — it is the question of how much kosher appearance is required to redeem an otherwise non-kosher-looking offspring.

### Physical Characteristic

One might read *siman echad* as referring specifically to a kosher characteristic — split hooves or cud-chewing — since the context is the verse about those very traits. Tosafot, however, is explicit that this reading would be a mistake. The discussion throughout has been about general physical characteristics, not specifically the kosher *simanim*, and Tosafot instructs us not to be misled by the context into thinking that only a kosher trait counts. *Siman echad* means one physical characteristic resembling the mother — any characteristic. This of course remains the position within Rabbi Shimon's camp; according to the *chachamim*, whose view is the *stam mishna*, even an offspring that looks entirely like a camel is kosher if its mother was a cow, and this entire discussion of degrees of resemblance only arises according to Rabbi Shimon.

A practical application of Rabbi Shimon's position — though he is not the accepted *halacha* — arises with respect to laboratory-created animals. Where an animal is engineered to look like a non-kosher species, the question of whether appearance alone can determine its status becomes genuinely pressing, and Rabbi Shimon's framework provides the relevant conceptual tools.

### Kosher Parents

The *baraita* continues: *ve-eizeh zeh ha-ba be-siman echad? zeh tamei ha-nolad min ha-tahor ve-iburo min ha-tahor* — what constitutes a case of one *siman*? It is a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother and conceived by a kosher father. So even with two fully kosher parents, the offspring may look non-kosher, and as long as one characteristic resembles the mother, it is permitted. This represents the *siman echad* threshold within Rabbi Shimon's position. But the *baraita* then raises whether this leniency could extend even to cases where the father was a non-kosher animal, bringing us to the question of paternity.

## Paternity Debate

### Rabbi Yehoshua

The *baraita* continues with a formal debate between Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Eliezer on the requirements for an offspring to be considered of a kosher species. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that both parents must be of a kosher species. He operates within Rabbi Shimon's general framework — appearance matters — but adds that even if the offspring has one characteristic like the mother, it is only kosher if the father was also a kosher animal.

### Seh Doubled

Rabbi Yehoshua's derivation relies on the doubled term in the verse: *seh kesavim ve-seh izim* — a lamb of sheep and a lamb of goats — using *seh* twice. He reads this as requiring that both the father be a ram (*aviv keves*) and the mother be a ewe (*imo kivsah*). Only then does the offspring qualify as a kosher species in the full sense; if the father belongs to a non-kosher species, the offspring is automatically non-kosher regardless of how it looks.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees. His opening argument is that the verse cannot be coming to permit something that is already obviously permissible. If both parents are kosher, the offspring is self-evidently permissible — there is no need for a verse to tell us that.

### Non-Kosher Father

What, then, does the verse come to permit? *Ela le'hosif al ha-mutar* — to add to the category of what is permitted. Specifically, it permits the case of *tamei ha-nolad min ha-tahor ve-iburo min ha-tamei* — an offspring that looks non-kosher, born of a kosher mother but fathered by a non-kosher animal. In such a case, one might have assumed that the genetic contribution of a non-kosher father renders the offspring non-kosher regardless of the mother. The verse, by emphasizing *seh* — any lamb of sheep, any lamb of goats, *mikol makom* — teaches that the mother's status alone determines the offspring's kosher status, and even a non-kosher father does not disqualify it.

The *gemara* then makes explicit that all of this operates within Rabbi Shimon's position: *kari lei tamei ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the *baraita* describes the offspring as non-kosher, meaning it accepts Rabbi Shimon's principle that appearance matters. Within that framework, Rabbi Yehoshua holds that even one *siman* like the mother saves the offspring. The *gemara* then notes, however, that the *gemara*'s conclusion about Rabbi Yehoshua does not settle what Rabbi Shimon himself would hold. *Hai tanna savar lah ke-vassai be-chada u-falig alei be-chada* — this *tanna*, Rabbi Yehoshua, agrees with Rabbi Shimon on one point and disagrees with him on another. Rabbi Yehoshua accepts that appearance matters, but his position that *siman echad* suffices cannot be attributed to Rabbi Shimon himself, who might require *rosho ve'rubo* even for eating. It should be noted, as a student rightly observed, that Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua are earlier authorities — teachers of Rabbi Akiva, whose student Rabbi Shimon was. The language of the *gemara* that frames their positions as diverging from Rabbi Shimon should therefore be understood as meaning they hold the same general approach but not that they learned it from him; the logical relationship runs in the other direction.

## Cross-Breeding

### No Interbreeding

A new challenge to the entire framework emerges. The *baraita* posited a scenario in which a kosher animal and a non-kosher animal produce offspring together. But is such a scenario biologically possible?

### No Cross-Species

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states a general principle: *le'olam ein misaberet* — it is simply not possible for a kosher animal to become pregnant by a non-kosher animal, or vice versa. Moreover, large animals (*gassa*) — cattle, camels, horses — cannot crossbreed with small animals (*dakka*) — sheep and goats. And domesticated animals (*behema*) cannot crossbreed with non-domesticated wild animals (*chaya*), in either direction.

### Koy Exception

The one acknowledged exception, notes Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, involves the dispute surrounding the *koy*. According to those who hold that the *koy* is a hybrid of a *chaya* and a *behema*, crossbreeding between those categories must be possible. But outside of that disputed case, such crossbreeding does not occur, which raises the question: how can the *baraita* posit a non-kosher father mating with a kosher mother?

### Kolut Animal

Rabbi Yirmiyah resolves the difficulty: the "non-kosher father" in the *baraita* is not truly a non-kosher species at all. It is a *kolut ben parah* — an offspring of a cow that itself looks like a non-kosher animal, having non-split hooves or otherwise appearing non-kosher. Because it is genetically a cow, it can mate with another cow — there is no interspecies breeding. But because it looks like a non-kosher animal, it is *halachically* non-kosher. So the *baraita*'s "non-kosher father" is biologically a cow that is *halachically* non-kosher. It can sire offspring with a kosher cow, and we then ask: what is the status of that offspring?

### Appearance Matters

This resolution carries a significant implication: even Rabbi Eliezer, who seemed to be dismissing the relevance of appearance and attending only to genetics and the mother's status, must in fact agree that fully resembling a non-kosher animal renders the offspring non-kosher. The scenario he discusses is only coherent if he accepts this much — otherwise there would be no "non-kosher father" to speak of. The *gemara* concludes: *de-ihu be-divrei Rabbi Shimon ka'amar* — Rabbi Eliezer is operating within Rabbi Shimon's framework, accepting that appearance matters. What he and Rabbi Yehoshua disagree about is the degree of appearance required and the role of paternity. The same conclusion holds: *hai tanna savar lah ke-vassai be-chada u-falig alei be-chada* — agreement on the general principle of appearance, disagreement on the specifics, and neither position can simply be attributed to Rabbi Shimon himself.

## Dual Causation

### Both Causes Matter

The *gemara* now draws out the underlying logical structure of the Rabbi Eliezer–Rabbi Yehoshua debate. The concept at stake is *zeh ve-zeh gorem* — dual causation, where both the mother and the father contribute to producing the offspring. Rabbi Eliezer appears to hold *zeh ve-zeh gorem mutar*: even when one of the two causes is problematic, the offspring is permitted, as long as the mother is kosher. Rabbi Yehoshua appears to hold *zeh ve-zeh gorem assur*: if one cause is forbidden, the result is forbidden, and even a kosher mother cannot overcome a non-kosher father.

### Mother Only

This framing is, however, immediately complicated. The *gemara* notes *ve-ha-ipcha shameinan lehu* — we actually know them to hold the reverse positions in a different context. That context is *velad tereifah* — the offspring of a *tereifah*. Here the mother is the problematic party (she is *tereifah*) and the father is healthy. Can this offspring be brought as a sacrifice? The question is not about its own *kashrut* for eating — a newborn animal has its own independent status and is not itself *tereifah* merely because its mother was — but about whether it can be brought as a *korban*. If the mother was sanctified and is a *tereifah*, the offspring's sanctity derives from the mother, and a *tereifah* cannot be brought on the altar. So its derived sanctity is compromised.

### Both Parents Required

In this context of sacrificial status, Rabbi Eliezer says *lo yikarev le-gabei mizbe'ach* — it may not be brought on the altar — because the mother's problematic status disqualifies the offspring. Rabbi Yehoshua says *yikarev* — it may be brought — because the father was not *tereifah* and that is sufficient. So here Rabbi Eliezer takes the stringent position that one problematic cause contaminates the whole, and Rabbi Yehoshua takes the lenient position that one good cause suffices. This is precisely the *reverse* of what their positions on paternity in the kosher-species debate implied.

### Position Reversal

The *gemara* resolves this apparent reversal. Each authority has a general position and an exception driven by a textual consideration.

Rabbi Eliezer's general position is actually that *zeh ve-zeh gorem assur* — when one cause is problematic, the result is forbidden. This is consistent with his ruling on *velad tereifah*: the *tereifah* mother contaminates. But in the case of kosher species and the non-kosher father, he makes an exception. How so? Because the verse could simply have written *kesavim ve-izim* — sheep and goats. Why does it say *seh kesavim ve-seh izim*, using *seh* twice? *Shema mina* — from here we learn *seh mikol makom*: any offspring qualifies as a *seh* regardless of the father's species, as long as the mother is a kosher animal. The doubled *seh* teaches that the father's non-kosher status does not disqualify the offspring in this context.

Rabbi Yehoshua, by contrast, holds generally that *zeh ve-zeh gorem mutar* — one good cause is sufficient. This is consistent with his ruling on *velad tereifah*: the kosher father saves the offspring. But in the case of kosher species, he makes an exception. How so? Because if one good cause were always enough, the verse would not have needed the plural forms *kesavim ve-izim*. Why write "of the sheep" and "of the goats" in the plural if a single parent's kosher status suffices? *Shema mina* — from here we learn that both parents must be kosher: *ad de'avi keves ve-imo kivsah*, until the father is a ram and the mother is a ewe.

The *gemara* thus establishes that neither authority has an absolute rule. Both have a default position and a textually-driven exception in the opposite direction. Tosafot raises the question of why the *gemara* framed this entire discussion in the abstract terminology of *zeh ve-zeh gorem* rather than the more natural and conventional framework of *chosheshim le-zera ha-av o lo* — whether we attribute legal significance to the father's genetic contribution. The answer offered is that there is a distinction between those two frameworks, though in practice the discussion here substantially overlaps with that of *zera ha-av*.

## Shimon Standard

### Shimon Explicit

The *gemara* now resolves the remaining open question — what Rabbi Shimon himself would require for *achilah* — with an explicit *baraita*. The *baraita* states: *gamal gamal shtei pe'amim* — the Torah says *gamal* twice: one to teach that a camel born from a camel is non-kosher, and one to teach that even a camel-looking offspring born from a cow is non-kosher, following Rabbi Shimon's position. The *baraita* then states: *ve-im rosho ve-rubo domeh le-imo mutar ba-achilah* — if the head and majority resemble the mother, it is permitted for eating.

### Both Contexts

The implication is unambiguous: *shema mina le-achilah nami ba'ei Rabbi Shimon rosh ve-rubo* — for eating too, Rabbi Shimon requires the head and majority to resemble the mother. A single characteristic does not suffice under Rabbi Shimon's standard. *Shema mina*. The question of how this interacts with the debate about the father's role remains open; the *baraita* speaks to appearance requirements but does not spell out Rabbi Shimon's position on paternity.

## Animal Products

### Donkey Urine

The *gemara* returns to the broader principle of *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei* and raises a question about donkey urine. Donkey urine was in fact consumed for medicinal purposes — the *gemara* later notes it was considered beneficial for jaundice (*yerakona*) — and the question is therefore practical: is it considered a product of the non-kosher animal and therefore forbidden, or is it merely water passing through?

### Clear Urine

The *gemara* first explains why the question was not raised about the urine of horses or camels: *de-lo achiri* — that urine is clear and essentially odorless, resembling plain water. It is not transformed by the animal; water enters and water exits — *maya ul, maya nafak*. Because it is not distinguishable from the liquid that entered the animal, it is not considered to have been produced by the animal, in the same way that a fish swallowed by a non-kosher fish is not thereby rendered non-kosher, since it was merely ingested and not transformed. The urine of horses and camels is *not like milk* — the milk of a camel is non-kosher precisely because it *is* a product of the animal, something produced by and transformed within the animal's body.

### Foul Urine

Donkey urine, however, is different — *deachiri*, cloudy and foul-smelling, in some respects resembling milk in the sense that it has been altered by its passage through the animal's body. The question is whether this transformation is sufficient to classify it as a true product of the animal: *miguffei kamimtzi mimtzatzi* — is it produced from the body? Or even here, is it essentially water in and water out, with the cloudiness and smell being no more than *havla de-bisra* — the vapor of the flesh, the animal's odors absorbed by the liquid without genuinely transforming it?

### Sheshet Answer

Rav Sheshet answers by citing the *mishna*: *ha-yotzei min ha-tamei tamei ve-ha-yotzei min ha-tahor tahor* — whatever comes out of a non-kosher animal is non-kosher. The *mishna* says *min ha-tamei* — "from the non-kosher" — not merely "of the non-kosher," which subtly suggests it is referring to something that is a product *of* the animal rather than something that *is* the animal itself. And donkey urine too is *mina de-tamei hu* — it is of the species of the non-kosher, a product thereof. The answer, on this reading, is that once something is categorized as a product of the animal, the question of the degree of transformation is already answered.

### Drunk by People

The *gemara* records an alternative framing of the question. According to this version, the question about horses and camels was not set aside because their urine is clear and unproduced, but simply because *de-lo shatu inshi* — normal people do not drink it, making the question practically moot. If no one drinks it, the permissibility or forbiddenness is irrelevant. Donkey urine, however, *de-shatu inshi* — people actually drink it, specifically for *yerakona*. This reframing is significant: it does not concede the theoretical point about water in, water out for horse and camel urine. It simply bypasses the question where it is moot, implying a broader definition of what counts as a product.

### Urine Forbidden

Rav Sheshet's answer applies equally under this second framing: *ha-yotzei min ha-tamei tamei* — the principle is comprehensive, and donkey urine, which people actually consume, falls within it. It is forbidden.

## Bee Honey

### Honey Permitted

The *gemara* poses a challenge — *meitivei* — from a *baraita*: why is bee honey permitted? Bees are non-kosher insects, and if the principle is that whatever comes from a non-kosher creature is non-kosher, bee honey should be forbidden.

### Stored Nectar

The *baraita* itself answers: *mipnei she-machnisot otan le-gufan ve-ein mematzot otan mi-gufan* — because bees bring the nectar into their bodies but do not produce the honey from their bodies. That is, honey is not extracted from the bee; it is something the bee collects externally and processes in ways that do not constitute internal production.

### Storage vs. Transform

This leads to a careful examination of how bee honey is actually made. The nectar is drawn from flowers and stored in a separate, non-digestive stomach — essentially a carrying vessel rather than a digestive organ. The bees return to the hive, transfer the nectar mouth-to-mouth among themselves, add an enzyme, and deposit it on the walls of the hive, where it evaporates and becomes honey. Crucially, the transformation into honey does not take place *inside* the bee's body; whatever enzymatic contribution the bee makes occurs before or as the material is expelled. The bee does not digest and re-produce the honey the way a cow produces milk. On this account, honey is not a product of the bee in the sense relevant to the prohibition; the bee is more like a vehicle of collection and transport, and the transformation occurs externally.

This analysis has implications for donkey urine as well. If "produced by an animal" requires genuine internal transformation, then the comparison between honey and urine becomes pointed. Honey is not truly produced from the bee's body; urine, by contrast, might be considered transformed by the body. The *gemara* leverages this comparison to refine what we mean by *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei*.

### Torah Exception

Rabbi Yaakov, however, takes a fundamentally different approach. He holds that bee honey *is* a genuine product of a non-kosher creature and would, by rights, be forbidden. It is permitted only because the Torah granted a specific exception. *Duvsha Rachmana shareya* — the Merciful One permitted it. The reason he maintains this is that, given the principle of *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei*, honey should be no better than donkey urine; and since we have established that donkey urine is forbidden, honey ought to be forbidden as well — unless the Torah explicitly permitted it.

### Sole Exception

The derivation Rabbi Yaakov uses comes from the verse: *ach ze tochlu mi-kol sheretz ha-of* — this you shall eat from all the flying *sheretz*. The verse distinguishes between what you may and may not eat among winged crawling things. The *drasha* is: *sheretz of tamei ata ochel* — you may not eat non-kosher flying insects, but *aval ata ochel ma she-of tamei mashritz* — you may eat a specific product of non-kosher flying insects. What is that product? *Ve-eizeh ze? Ze dvash devorim* — bee honey, and bee honey alone. The Torah has given a single, identified exception, and nothing more.

### Bee Honey Only

The *gemara* asks: might the same permission extend to *dvash ha-gizim ve-ha-tzirim* — the honey of hornets and wasps? Rabbi Yaakov rules *no*. The reasoning: *marbe ani dvash devorim she-ein lahem shem livai* — I permit bee honey because "honey" without qualification means bee honey. It needs no further identifier; it is the primary referent of the word. But *dvash ha-gizim* and *dvash ha-tzirim* — hornet honey or wasp honey — carry qualifying names, *shem livai*, indicating that they are secondary, non-standard products. When the Torah grants an exception for the product of non-kosher flying insects, it means the default product, the one that needs no qualifier. Everything else remains under the general prohibition.

### All Honey OK

The opposing view is recorded in a *baraita*: *dvash agazin ve-ha-tzirim tahor u-mutar ba-achilah* — the honey of these other insects is *tahor* and permitted for eating. This follows the alternative framework: honey is not considered internally produced by the bee, and the same logic applies to honey of hornets and wasps. On this view, there is no special Torah exception for bee honey alone — all honey of this type is permitted because none of it constitutes a true product of the insect's body.

### Transformation Key

The disagreement therefore hinges on the underlying conceptual question: is honey-making a true internal transformation that should render the product non-kosher under *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei*? If it is, then honey is fundamentally forbidden and only saved by a Torah exception — in which case that exception is limited to bee honey alone. If it is not a true internal transformation, then honey was never subject to the prohibition to begin with, and there is no reason to distinguish between honey produced by bees, hornets, or wasps.

## Placenta Status

### Donkey Stones

The *gemara* then takes up a puzzling phenomenon: *hani beini de-chamarta* — certain egg-like objects expelled from the wombs of female donkeys. The rabbis initially assumed these were a form of eggs and therefore forbidden. Given the broader question of what counts as a product of a non-kosher animal, identifying these objects correctly is essential.

### Testicles/Semen

Two explanations are offered. Rashi explains that the rabbis initially thought these objects were *beitzim* — testicles — that had somehow been torn off a male animal during mating and entered the female, subsequently being expelled. Under this reading, they would be forbidden as a form of *eiver min ha-chai* — a limb separated from a living animal — not because they were produced by the donkey, but because they were an external animal part that entered the female.

Rav Safra, however, offers a different identification: *zar'a de-ayala hu de-azil batar ayalta* — these objects are dried semen from a *ayal*, some large kosher deer, that had been pursuing a female deer (*ayalta*). Unable to mate with it — *ve-aidi de-rachmah tzar lo mizdakeka*, because it had a narrow womb or was not receptive — the animal turned to a female donkey (*yachmorta*) to copulate with instead. The result was dried semen that eventually was expelled from the donkey's body. On this reading, what appeared to be "eggs" is actually congealed semen from a kosher animal, and is therefore permitted.

A student correctly noted that *yachmur* — the *yachmorta*'s mate — appears in the Torah's list of kosher animals in Parashat Re'eh, meaning both candidate animals in Rav Safra's scenario are in fact kosher species. Whether the objects are permitted or forbidden thus turns on which interpretation is correct: are they severed parts of a living animal, rendering them *assur*, or are they the externally derived semen of a kosher animal, rendering them *mutar*?

### Placenta Refuse

The *gemara* then turns to a related but distinct case. Rav Huna rules: *shilya keneged panav shel chamor mutar* — the placenta of a donkey is permitted. A supporting *baraita* is cited: the placenta of a human, *bein chai bein met* — regardless of whether the mother and child are alive or dead — does not carry the *tumah* of a corpse (*tumat met*). This indicates that the placenta is not considered an integral part of the body of either mother or child.

### Refuse Definition

The reason given by the *gemara* for the placenta's permitted status is: *pirsha be-alma hu* — it is merely refuse. It is not a product of the animal in any meaningful sense; it is biological waste, and therefore the principle of *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei* does not apply to it.

This raises an important conceptual question left hanging at the end of the *sugya*: why is the placenta considered *pirsha* — refuse — while donkey urine is considered a product and forbidden? Both are biological material expelled from the animal's body. The *gemara* hints that what disqualifies something from being a true "product" may be a combination of factors: whether it is transformed by the animal's body, whether normal people regard it as food or use it as such, and whether it can be classified as mere waste expelled by the body rather than something the body generates. The placenta opens yet another sub-category in this taxonomy — neither ingested-and-expelled like the fish inside a fish, nor produced-and-transformed like milk, but something closer to biological refuse that never had the character of a discrete product at all. This question — how to map the precise borders of *yotzei min ha-tamei tamei* — continues into the *mishna* to follow.
