# Eruvin 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Migdal & Key Review

### Tanna Kamma vs Eliezer

We pick up at the very top of 35a, though it is worth reviewing the ground covered previously, as the conclusion of the prior session was somewhat rushed. The *mishna* on our *daf* addresses a situation where one places an *eiruv* inside a *migdal* — a wooden wardrobe or chest — and then loses the key. The *tanna kamma* rules that the *eiruv* remains valid, while Rabbi Eliezer rules that it does not. The underlying question is straightforward: if you lack the key, how can you access the *eiruv* on *Shabbos*? Presumably one cannot simply break the wardrobe open, though we will see that this assumption itself becomes the subject of debate.

The *gemara* initially explored a special case — a *migdal* constructed from *liveini* bricks laid without cement — where, according to one opinion, dismantling the structure would not constitute a violation. However, this leniency was applicable only on *Yom Tov*, not *Shabbos*, and so the *gemara* proposed that the *mishna*'s case is confined to *Yom Tov*. That was the first answer.

### Yom Tov Exception

Within that framework, the *gemara* quoted an additional *braisa* presenting a position of Rabbi Eliezer that introduces a further distinction: the validity of the *eiruv* depends on where the key was lost. Even when Rabbi Eliezer rules that the *eiruv* is invalid, that ruling applies only when there is no realistic possibility of retrieving the key. If there is a genuine possibility of finding the key and bringing it to the *eiruv*, Rabbi Eliezer would concede that the *eiruv* is valid — even if the key has not yet been found.

The operative question is therefore the location of the loss. If the key was lost in the city, and the intervening spaces between the finder and the wardrobe consist entirely of *reshuyos hayachid*, then it would theoretically be possible to retrieve the key and carry it through those domains. In that case, Rabbi Eliezer would validate the *eiruv*. If, however, the key was lost in the *sadeh* — the open field — retrieval is practically impossible, and Rabbi Eliezer would invalidate it. The *gemara* notes that the introduction of the carrying question indicates the case is being discussed in the context of *Shabbos*, not *Yom Tov*, since carrying is only a concern on *Shabbos*.

The *chidush* of this *braisa* is remarkable: Rabbi Eliezer does not require that the key actually be found — only that finding it is a realistic possibility. The mere theoretical accessibility of the key, at the moment *Shabbos* begins, is sufficient to validate the *eiruv*.

### Key Location Logic

The *braisa* as quoted by the *gemara* frames the question not in terms of where the key was lost, but in terms of where it was found — specifically, found on *Shabbos* itself. Rashi explains that the finding must have occurred on *Shabbos* because if the key had been found before *Shabbos* began, the scenario of *Shabbos* commencing without the key would not apply. One might then ask: why should finding the key on *Shabbos* matter, if the validity of the *eiruv* is determined by conditions at the moment *Shabbos* begins?

The answer, which the *rishonim* appear to accept without lengthy discussion, is that the location where the key is found on *Shabbos* is simply evidence of where it was located when *Shabbos* began. If found in the city, it was in the city on *erev Shabbos*; if found in the *sadeh*, it was in the *sadeh* on *erev Shabbos*. One might also suggest, in the spirit of *yesh bereirah*, that the act of finding the key retroactively confirms that it was findable all along — and since this is a rabbinic matter, *yesh bereirah* would apply. Regardless, the *gemara*'s emphasis is squarely on the question of where the key was lost at the onset of *Shabbos*, and the finding is simply probative of that fact.

### Three Positions

Reading the *mishna* and *braisa* together yields three distinct positions. The *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* rules that the *eiruv* is valid even when there is no possibility of accessing the key — but this, according to the *gemara*'s first answer, is limited to the special case of a brick-constructed wardrobe on *Yom Tov*, where dismantling is permissible. In the general case — a wooden wardrobe, on *Shabbos* — the *tanna kamma* would also concede that the *eiruv* is invalid.

Rabbi Eliezer, who in the *mishna* appears to be the strict opinion, turns out on examination to be more lenient than the general position of the *chachamim*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even setting aside the special case of bricks and *Yom Tov*, the *eiruv* is valid on *Shabbos* as long as the key was lost within the city, since it could theoretically have been retrieved via the interconnected *reshuyos hayachid* — relying on the principle, attributed to Rabbi Shimon, that rooftops, courtyards, and enclosed fields all share a single *reshus hayachid* status for objects that spent *Shabbos* within them. Thus one could carry the key from domain to domain without transgression.

The *rabbanan*, by contrast, are unmoved by the theoretical possibility of finding and retrieving the key. Even if the key was lost in the city, they do not share Rabbi Eliezer's view that the various *reshuyos hayachid* constitute a unified domain for this purpose, and they therefore rule the *eiruv* invalid in all cases other than the special brick-and-*Yom Tov* scenario.

## Kli vs Ohel Debate

### Ohel Status

Rabbah and Rav Yosef propose an entirely different approach to the *mishna*, cutting through the complexity of the first answer. They argue: *hacha b'migdal shel etz askinan* — the *mishna* is speaking of an ordinary wooden wardrobe, not a brick structure. The reason the *tanna kamma* considers the *eiruv* accessible has nothing to do with *Yom Tov* or the special properties of unbonded bricks. Rather, it turns on the fundamental question of what halakhic category the wardrobe belongs to: is it a *kli* — a vessel — or an *ohel* — a structure?

If the wardrobe has the status of a *kli*, then the principle that there is no *binyan b'keilim* — no prohibition of building that applies to vessels — would mean that there is correspondingly no *stirah b'keilim*, no prohibition of destroying vessels either. One could therefore break open the wardrobe with a hatchet, just as the *gemara* in *Shabbos* permits breaking a wine barrel to access the wine inside. Destroying the wardrobe would not constitute the *melacha* of *soter*, and the *eiruv* would accordingly be considered accessible.

### Destruction of Vessels

The *tanna kamma* holds precisely this view: the wardrobe is a *kli*, and one may break it open. The second opinion — that of Rabbi Eliezer — holds that the wardrobe is not a *kli* but an *ohel*. A sufficiently large wardrobe, one that functions like a freestanding structure with a roof, takes on the character of a tent or small building. The roof is halakhically significant: creating a roofed enclosure is the paradigm of *boneh*, even when the structure is not permanent. If the wardrobe has *ohel* status, then breaking it open would constitute *soter*, and the *eiruv* would be inaccessible — hence Rabbi Eliezer's stringency.

It is worth noting in this context that Tosafot takes a more expansive view of *soter b'keilim*, arguing that even genuine vessels can be subject to *soter* under certain conditions. Tosafot therefore would say that even a smaller wardrobe — one that would not qualify as an *ohel* — could not simply be smashed open on *Shabbos*, since destroying a complete, fully formed vessel may itself constitute *soter*. This has implications for the familiar question of opening packaging on *Shabbos*: the wine barrel case involves an object designed to be opened, whereas a full-fledged *kli* that is not intended for destruction might stand differently. According to Tosafot, it would then follow that the *gemara*'s case necessarily involves a wardrobe bound with a string — but more on that shortly.

### Tum'ah Connection

Having articulated the *kli*-versus-*ohel* dispute, the *gemara* suggests that this disagreement between Rabbah and Rav Yosef maps onto an earlier *tannaitic* dispute. The *mishna* in *Maseches Zavim* teaches that a *zav* — whose mode of transmitting *tum'ah* includes moving objects even without direct contact — who strikes a *shida*, *teiva*, or *migdal*, renders them *tamei*. The *tanna kamma* rules them *tamei*; Rabbi Nechemiah and Rabbi Shimon rule them *tahor*.

The *gemara* suggests that their dispute may hinge on the same question: if the *migdal* is a *kli*, it is susceptible to *tum'ah* through the *zav*'s action; if it is an *ohel*, it is not. This would make the *tanna'im*'s debate in *Zavim* a direct parallel to Rabbah and Rav Yosef's dispute in our *sugya*.

### Tremor Debate

Abaye, however, rejects this parallel. He adduces a *braisa* that makes clear that the *ohel* status of the *migdal* is irrelevant to the *tum'ah* question. The *braisa* states: if an *ohel* was actually moved — *nisa* — by the *zav*, it becomes *tamei*; if a *kli* was not actually moved — *eino nisa* — it remains *tahor*. The classification as *ohel* or *kli* does not determine the outcome; what matters is whether genuine displacement occurred. Furthermore, the *mishna* in *Zavim* itself states in its closing clause that Rabbi Nechemiah and Rabbi Shimon concede that if the object was actually moved — *nisutin* — it would be *tamei*. So there is no dispute about the principle.

What, then, is their dispute? Abaye explains: *ve-hacha be-re'adah machmat kocho askinan* — they are debating a case where the *zav* strikes the wardrobe and it shakes in place, vibrating without being displaced. The rule of *nisa machmat kocho* — moved through direct force — renders it *tamei*; *machmat re'adah* — moved through vibration — renders it *tahor*. Everyone agrees on this rule. The debate is whether causing an object to shake in place without displacing it constitutes *hesset*, genuine movement, or not. One opinion holds that shaking counts as movement; the other holds it does not. In any event, Abaye has shown that the *kli*-versus-*ohel* distinction is not what the *tanna'im* in *Zavim* are arguing about.

As Rashi frames it, the practical upshot for our *eiruv* question is as follows: if the wardrobe is small enough to be a genuine *kli* — below the threshold of *arbaim se'ah* volume — then everyone agrees one can break it, and the *eiruv* is accessible. If it is large enough to fall into a non-*kli* category — above *arbaim se'ah* — then everyone agrees it cannot be broken. The middle ground that generates debate must lie elsewhere.

## String & Knife Issue

### String Binding

Both Abaye and Rava resolve the problem together. The *mishna* must be discussing a case of *migdal kashur b'matna* — a wardrobe bound with a string. Rashi's understanding is that we are dealing with a large wardrobe that is too big to qualify as a *kli* and therefore cannot be broken open without transgressing *soter*. That rules out simply smashing it. However, the wardrobe is secured not only by a lock but also by a rope or string wrapped around it. The key to the lock may be missing, but the string is accessible. Could one simply cut the string and open the wardrobe that way, without touching the lock?

The answer requires a knife, and here the entire dispute of the *mishna* resolves itself into a *muktzah* question: may one use a knife on *Shabbos* to cut a rope?

### Rabbi Yosei Lenient

The *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* rules the *eiruv* valid because he holds like Rabbi Yosei, *de-amar kol ha-kelim nitlin b'Shabbos* — that all vessels may be handled on *Shabbos* except for the great saw and the plow-peg. In other words, any ordinary vessel may be used even for purposes beyond its primary function, without concern for *muktzah*. On this view, one may take a kitchen knife and use it to cut the rope binding the wardrobe. The *eiruv* is therefore considered accessible, and it is valid.

### Rabbi Nechemiah Strict

Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, holds like Rabbi Nechemiah, *de-amar afilu tallis ve-afilu tarvad ein nitlin ella le-tzorech tashmishan* — that even a cloak or a ladle may only be handled for their own specific, intended purpose. A kitchen knife is intended for cutting food; using it to cut a rope is outside its designated function, and therefore the knife is *muktzah* for that purpose. Since one cannot use the knife, the rope cannot be cut, the wardrobe cannot be opened, and the *eiruv* is inaccessible.

It is striking that what appears in the *mishna* as a fundamental dispute about the validity of an *eiruv* in a locked wardrobe resolves, on this reading, into a narrow and technical *muktzah* question: whether Rabbi Yosei or Rabbi Nechemiah's position on handling vessels governs.

## Nitgalgal & Destruction

### Rolling Away

The next *mishna* addresses what happens when the *eiruv* itself is removed from its intended location before *Shabbos* begins. *Nitgalgal chutz la-techum* — if it rolled outside the *techum*, it started *Shabbos* beyond the two-thousand-*amah* boundary that the *eiruv* was meant to extend, and it can no longer serve its function. The *mishna* proceeds to enumerate additional cases: *nafal alav gal* — rubble fell on top of it; *nisraf* — it was burned; or, if it was *terumah*, it became *tamei*. The rule is: if any of these occurred while it was still *b'od yom* — on Friday before *Shabbos* — the *eiruv* is invalid. If they occurred only after nightfall — *mishe-chashechah* — it is valid, since at the moment of *Shabbos* the *eiruv* was in proper order.

Rava adds a qualification to the *nitgalgal* case: *lo shanu ela she-nitgalgel chutz le-arba amot* — the rule of invalidity applies only when the *eiruv* rolled more than four *amot* beyond the two-thousand-*amah* mark. Within four *amot* it remains valid, since the place where one deposits an *eiruv* is treated halakhically like one's home, and within four *amot* of that location is considered to share that space. This is the source cited earlier in the *masechta* for the four-*amah* rule.

The most consequential case is the *safek* — doubt. If one does not know whether the disqualifying event occurred before or after *Shabbos* began, the *mishna* records a dispute. Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah rule *harei zeh chammar gamal* — it is like being both a donkey-driver and a camel-leader simultaneously. The donkey-driver walks behind and pushes the animal forward; the camel-leader walks in front and pulls the animal forward. If one is somehow positioned between a donkey and a camel moving in opposite directions, one is pulled both ways at once and can go nowhere. The idiom captures the halakhic predicament precisely.

If the *eiruv* was valid, the person acquired a *techum* extending two thousand *amot* to the north of where he placed it, but he forfeited the corresponding two thousand *amot* to the south of his starting point. If the *eiruv* was invalid, he never acquired the extended northern range but retains the full two thousand *amot* in every direction from where he spent *Shabbos*. Not knowing which, he cannot travel the full four thousand *amot* north — because maybe the *eiruv* was valid, in which case he gets only two thousand from the *eiruv*'s location — and he cannot travel freely to the south — because maybe the *eiruv* was valid, in which case he surrendered his southward range. He is left with only two thousand *amot* to the north of his starting point, the minimum guaranteed to him in either scenario.

### Building Falls

Concerning the case of *nafal alav gal* — where rubble falls onto the *eiruv* — the *gemara* initially assumes that the case is one where the stones are merely *muktzah*, a rabbinic prohibition, and could in principle be removed. If so, one might expect the *mishna*'s ruling that the *eiruv* is inaccessible to conflict with the opinion of Rabbi — Rebbe — who holds that rabbinic prohibitions (*shevut*) were not in force *bein ha-shemashos*, during twilight. If the *eiruv* was already covered as *Shabbos* began, but the only impediment is a rabbinic *muktzah*, should Rabbi not consider it still accessible?

The *gemara* resolves this: the *mishna* could accord with Rabbi, but the case must involve a situation where a shovel or hoe is required to uncover the *eiruv* — and that would constitute a biblical violation. The *rishonim*, including the Ritva, explain that digging through rubble with implements could constitute either *boneh* — if it improves the floor of a structure — or *choresh* — plowing, if it disrupts and prepares ground in a field context. Either way, the prohibition is biblical, not merely rabbinic.

The *gemara* then explains why each scenario in the *mishna* is necessary. If the *mishna* had only stated *nitgalgal*, one might think the invalidity follows only because the *eiruv* is no longer physically present at the right location — but *nafal alav gal* would be different since the *eiruv* remains in place, merely covered. Conversely, if only the *nafal alav gal* case were given, one might say it fails because the *eiruv* is hidden and invisible — but for *nitgalgal* one might think it valid, since one can see where it went and a wind could theoretically blow it back. By stating both cases, the *mishna* teaches that any form of inaccessibility — regardless of the specific mechanism — disqualifies the *eiruv*.

### Burned & Impure

The *mishna* then gives two further cases: *nisraf* — the *eiruv* was burned — and *terumah nitme'et* — it was *terumah* that became *tamei*. Why are these cases necessary once the previous ones have established the principle? The *gemara* answers at the top of 35b: *tana nisraf le-hodia kocho de-Rabbi Yossi* — the case of burning is cited to reveal the full extent of Rabbi Yossi's leniency. Rabbi Yossi holds that when the *safek* arises, one may assume the *eiruv* was valid. One might have thought this assumption is plausible only when the *eiruv* still exists in some form — perhaps it deteriorated or was partially destroyed — and one can extend the presumption of its prior valid status. When the *eiruv* has been completely destroyed by fire, can one still invoke the prior good status? Rabbi Yossi says yes — even in that extreme case.

The case of *terumah nitme'et* teaches the opposite lesson: *le-hodia kocho de-Rabbi Meir* — to reveal the full extent of Rabbi Meir's stringency. Here the *eiruv* is physically present, entirely intact, unchanged in form. The only question is a halakhic one: did its status change before or after *Shabbos*? One might expect that since nothing physical has changed, even Rabbi Meir would allow reliance on the prior good status. He does not. Even in this most minimal scenario, where the *eiruv* is present before one's eyes and only its halakhic status is in doubt, Rabbi Meir rules that the *safek* leaves one in the *chammar gamal* predicament.

## Safek Eiruv

### Chammar Gamal

With those boundaries established, the *mishna* turns to the core dispute about *safek eiruv* — a doubtful *eiruv*. The *chammar gamal* image captures the halakhic paralysis with vivid precision. A person who left his *eiruv* at point B, two thousand *amot* north of where he will spend *Shabbos*, intended to extend his *techum* northward to four thousand *amot*. If the *eiruv* was valid, he effectively started *Shabbos* at point B, giving him two thousand *amot* north of B but surrendering free movement south of his actual sleeping location. If it was invalid, he started at his own location and has two thousand *amot* in every direction.

Not knowing which scenario applies, he cannot commit to either. He cannot walk the full four thousand *amot* north — because if the *eiruv* failed, he has only two thousand from his starting point. He cannot walk freely to the south — because if the *eiruv* succeeded, he forfeited that range. He is left with the bare two thousand *amot* north of himself, the minimum guaranteed under either outcome. That is the chammar gamal.

### Meir & Yehudah

Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah adopt the stringent position. When the status of the *eiruv* is unknown, they do not allow any reliance on its prior good standing. The person is left in the *chammar gamal* position: restricted to two thousand *amot* north, and cut off from his full southern range. There is no basis, in their view, for defaulting to the favorable assumption that the *eiruv* remained intact when *Shabbos* began.

### Yosei & Shimon

Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Shimon take the lenient position: *safek eiruv kasher* — a doubtful *eiruv* is valid. Since the entire institution of *eiruv* is rabbinic, a doubt is resolved leniently. Moreover, the *gemara* will develop the point that in addition to the general principle of leniency in rabbinic doubts, there is a *chazakah* at work: since the *eiruv* was properly in place before *Shabbos*, one may presume that it remained in that valid state when *Shabbos* began. Rabbi Yossi bolsters this further by citing the testimony of Aftolmus in the name of five elders that a doubtful *eiruv* is indeed *kasher* — an ancient tradition confirming the lenient position.

## Meir Consistency Q

### Tum'ah Comparison

The *gemara* now poses a sharp challenge against Rabbi Meir. His stringency in the *safek eiruv* case seems surprising given that the *gemara* assumes *techumin* might be rabbinic — and Rabbi Meir is generally lenient on rabbinic doubts. The *gemara* supports this from a well-known *mishna* concerning *tum'ah*: someone who was *tamei* went to immerse in a *mikvah*, and is uncertain whether he immersed, or uncertain whether the *mikvah* contained the requisite *arbaim se'ah*, or chose between two *mikvaos* one of which was *kasher* and one of which was not, without knowing which he entered — all of these constitute a *safek* regarding his *taharah*. When the underlying *tum'ah* is biblical in severity, the rule is *sefeko tamei*: he remains presumed *tamei*, since he began in that status and cannot confirm its removal. But when the underlying *tum'ah* is a rabbinic one — such as eating *tamei* food, drinking *tamei* liquids, or immersing in drawn water (*mayim she'uvin*) — the ruling is *sefeko tahor*. On a rabbinic *tum'ah*, the *safek* is resolved leniently.

This is presumed to reflect the opinion of Rabbi Meir, since the *stam mishna* is attributed to him. And it raises an obvious difficulty: if Rabbi Meir is lenient on doubts in rabbinic *tum'ah*, why is he strict on a *safek eiruv*, which is likewise rabbinic?

### Bird Flies Measure

A further challenge arises from a *mishna* regarding the measurement of *techumin* when the path crosses a mountain. The standard approach is to measure as the bird flies, treating the terrain as flat. The *gemara* describes this as *yachol le-havli'o* — one can "swallow" the mountain using fifty-*amah* measuring strings: one measurer stands on one side, another on the other, the string is held taut at the appropriate horizontal level, the position is marked, and measurement continues. When the mountain is wider than fifty *amot* — *eino yachol le-havli'o* — a more complex method is employed, *mekadrin beharim*, which still attempts to approximate horizontal measurement using a four-*amah* string held at chest and foot level.

Cited in the name of Rabbi Meir — specifically transmitted by Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai in the name of Rabbi Meir — is the ruling that one does indeed pierce through the mountain with measurements as the bird flies even in the *eino yachol le-havli'o* case. This is significant because Rav Nachman citing Rabba bar Abbuha rules that *ein mekadrin* — one does not measure by piercing through — *lo be-arei miklat* and *lo be-eglah arufah*. For *arei miklat*, the cities of refuge, the 2000-*amah* buffer zone is measured along the actual ground, not as the bird flies; similarly, *eglah arufah* requires measuring which city is closest by walking distance, not aerial distance. The *gemara* infers from these cases a principle: biblical measurements are made as the dog walks, not as the bird flies. If *techumin* were biblical, one would not measure them as the bird flies.

### Techumin D'Rabbanan

The *gemara* therefore concludes: since the ruling attributed to Rabbi Meir permits measuring *techumin* as the bird flies, Rabbi Meir must hold that *techumin* are *d'rabbanan*. But then why is he strict in the *mishna* about *safek eiruv*?

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ha didei ha de-rabbeih* — the leniency is his teacher's view, not Rabbi Meir's own. The language of the ruling makes this clear: Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai does not cite the bird-flies measurement as Rabbi Meir's personal position, but rather says *shamati she-makkilin* — "I heard a tradition that one is lenient." Rabbi Meir is transmitting what he received, not endorsing it as his own *pesak*. The *gemara* notes that the precise phrasing — *shamati* — supports this inference. Rabbi Meir personally holds that *techumin* are *d'oraisa*, which is why he is strict by *safek eiruv*, and he would not permit measuring as the bird flies for a biblical *halacha*.

## Meir Strictness

### Started Bad

With that reconciliation in place, the *gemara* launches a second challenge against Rabbi Meir: a *d'oraisa* versus *d'oraisa* inconsistency. The *gemara* cites a *mishna*: someone who touched a person at night without knowing whether that person was alive or dead at the time of contact — *eino yodeia im chai im meit* — and then discovered in the morning that the person had died sometime during the night. Rabbi Meir rules *tahor*; the *chachamim* rule *tamei*, reasoning *shekol tumos ke-sha'at metziatam* — one assesses *tum'ah* statuses as of the moment of their discovery. Since the person is now found to be dead, the presumption runs backward, treating the corpse as having already been dead at the time of contact. But Rabbi Meir is lenient, even though touching a corpse is a biblical *tum'ah*. This appears to contradict his stringency in the *mikvah* case.

The distinction is crucial. In the *mikvah* case, the person *began* in a state of *tum'ah*. His initial status was bad. The doubt is whether that bad status was removed; and when one cannot confirm removal, the rule is to remain with the prior bad status. The logic is unimpeachable: you were *tamei*, you don't know if you became *tahor*, remain *tamei*.

### Two Chazakos

The touching case is structurally different. The person began the night in a state of *taharah* — he was *tahor* before he reached out in the dark. Moreover, he himself began as *tahor*. So there are two *chazakos* of good status: the person being touched was alive and the one doing the touching was *tahor*. When there are two *chazakos* operating in the direction of leniency, Rabbi Meir is willing to be lenient even in a *d'oraisa* case. He resolves the doubt in favor of those two presumptive statuses and rules *tahor*.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, disagree with this approach and instead apply the principle of *ke-sha'at metziatam* — assessing as of the time of discovery — which overrides the prior good status.

### Witnesses Weaken

How does this framework apply to the *safek eiruv* case, where the *eiruv* also *began* in a good state? Rava and Rav Yosef propose that the case in the *mishna* involves two contradictory sets of witnesses. One set testifies that the *eiruv* was disqualified before *Shabbos* began; another set testifies that it was disqualified only after nightfall. Each set of witnesses undermines the other, leaving the question unresolved. But crucially, the very existence of witnesses testifying to the bad outcome — even if contradicted — erodes the *chazakah* of the *eiruv*'s initial good status. The prior good status is weakened not merely by uncertainty but by affirmative testimony to its failure. The *chazakah* is undermined, leaving Rabbi Meir unable to rely on it, and so he rules stringently.

### Two Chazakos Rule

Rava offers a cleaner formulation of the entire principle. *Hasam trei chazakei le-kula, ve-hacha chada chazakah le-kula* — in the touching case there are two *chazakos* supporting leniency; in the *eiruv* case there is only one. In the touching case: first, the person being touched started the night alive; second, the person doing the touching started the night *tahor*. Two independent presumptive statuses, both pointing toward leniency — and Rabbi Meir allows them to prevail even over a *d'oraisa* doubt. In the *eiruv* case there is only one *chazakah*: that the *eiruv* was in good order before *Shabbos*. One *chazakah* alone is insufficient for Rabbi Meir when the stakes are *d'oraisa*, and he rules stringently.

This distinction between a single *chazakah* and a double *chazakah* as the determining factor in Rabbi Meir's approach to *safek d'oraisa* represents one of the deeper conceptual tensions in the *sugya*, and the discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
