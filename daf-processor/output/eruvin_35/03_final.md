# Eruvin 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Migdal & Key Review

### Tanna Kamma vs Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוֹ בְּמִגְדָּל וְאָבַד הַמַּפְתֵּחַ — הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהַמַּפְתֵּחַ בִּמְקוֹמוֹ — אֵינוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one put** the *eiruv* **in a cupboard** and locked it, **and the key was lost,** so that he is now unable to open the cupboard and access the *eiruv*, **it is** nonetheless a valid ***eiruv*. Rabbi Eliezer says: If he does not know that the key is in its place, it is not** a valid ***eiruv*.**

We pick up at the very top of 35a, though it is worth reviewing the ground covered previously, as the conclusion of the prior session was somewhat rushed. The *mishna* on our *daf* addresses a situation where one places an *eiruv* inside a *migdal* — a wooden wardrobe or chest — and then loses the key. The *tanna kamma* rules that the *eiruv* remains valid, while Rabbi Eliezer rules that it does not. The underlying question is straightforward: if you lack the key, how can you access the *eiruv* on *Shabbos*? Presumably one cannot simply break the wardrobe open, though we will see that this assumption itself becomes the subject of debate.

### Yom Tov Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: נְתָנוֹ בְּמִגְדָּל, וְנָעַל בְּפָנָיו וְאָבַד הַמַּפְתֵּחַ — הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּיוֹם טוֹב, אֲבָל בְּשַׁבָּת — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. נִמְצָא הַמַּפְתֵּחַ, בֵּין בָּעִיר בֵּין בַּשָּׂדֶה — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בָּעִיר — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, בַּשָּׂדֶה — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and it teaches the following:** If **one placed** the *eiruv* **in a cupboard and locked it, and the key is lost, it is** nonetheless a valid ***eiruv*. In what** case **is this statement said? On a Festival; however, on Shabbat, his *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*.** If **the key is found, whether in a city or in a field, his *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*. Rabbi Eliezer** disagrees and **says:** If it is found **in a city, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*;** but if it is found **in a field, his *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*.**

The *gemara* initially explored a special case — a *migdal* constructed from *liveini* bricks laid without cement — where, according to one opinion, dismantling the structure would not constitute a violation. However, this leniency was applicable only on *Yom Tov*, not *Shabbos*, and so the *gemara* proposed that the *mishna*'s case is confined to *Yom Tov*. That was the first answer.

Within that framework, the *gemara* quoted an additional *braisa* presenting a position of Rabbi Eliezer that introduces a further distinction: the validity of the *eiruv* depends on where the key was lost. Even when Rabbi Eliezer rules that the *eiruv* is invalid, that ruling applies only when there is no realistic possibility of retrieving the key. If there is a genuine possibility of finding the key and bringing it to the *eiruv*, Rabbi Eliezer would concede that the *eiruv* is valid — even if the key has not yet been found.

The operative question is therefore the location of the loss. If the key was lost in the city, and the intervening spaces between the finder and the wardrobe consist entirely of *reshuyos hayachid*, then it would theoretically be possible to retrieve the key and carry it through those domains. In that case, Rabbi Eliezer would validate the *eiruv*. If, however, the key was lost in the *sadeh* — the open field — retrieval is practically impossible, and Rabbi Eliezer would invalidate it. The *gemara* notes that the introduction of the carrying question indicates the case is being discussed in the context of *Shabbos*, not *Yom Tov*, since carrying is only a concern on *Shabbos*.

The *chidush* of this *braisa* is remarkable: Rabbi Eliezer does not require that the key actually be found — only that finding it is a realistic possibility. The mere theoretical accessibility of the key, at the moment *Shabbos* begins, is sufficient to validate the *eiruv*.

### Key Location Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעִיר — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: אֶחָד גַּגּוֹת וְאֶחָד חֲצֵירוֹת וְאֶחָד קַרְפֵּיפוֹת — רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בְּתוֹכָן. בַּשָּׂדֶה אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב — כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now explains the difference: If the key is found **in a city, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said: Roofs, courtyards, and enclosed fields [*karpeifot*] are all one domain** with regard **to utensils that began Shabbat in them.** Accordingly, a utensil that was left on a roof at the beginning of Shabbat may be carried into a courtyard or a *karpef*. It is possible to transfer anything located in a city from one place to another in a similar manner. If, however, the key is found **in a field, his *eiruv* is not a** valid ***eiruv*, in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** due to the prohibition to carry in a *karmelit*. Although carrying there is merely a *shevut*, the *eiruv* is not valid.

The *braisa* as quoted by the *gemara* frames the question not in terms of where the key was lost, but in terms of where it was found — specifically, found on *Shabbos* itself. Rashi explains that the finding must have occurred on *Shabbos* because if the key had been found before *Shabbos* began, the scenario of *Shabbos* commencing without the key would not apply. One might then ask: why should finding the key on *Shabbos* matter, if the validity of the *eiruv* is determined by conditions at the moment *Shabbos* begins?

The answer, which the *rishonim* appear to accept without lengthy discussion, is that the location where the key is found on *Shabbos* is simply evidence of where it was located when *Shabbos* began. If found in the city, it was in the city on *erev Shabbos*; if found in the *sadeh*, it was in the *sadeh* on *erev Shabbos*. One might also suggest, in the spirit of *yesh bereirah*, that the act of finding the key retroactively confirms that it was findable all along — and since this is a rabbinic matter, *yesh bereirah* would apply. Regardless, the *gemara*'s emphasis is squarely on the question of where the key was lost at the onset of *Shabbos*, and the finding is simply probative of that fact.

### Three Positions

Reading the *mishna* and *braisa* together yields three distinct positions. The *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* rules that the *eiruv* is valid even when there is no possibility of accessing the key — but this, according to the *gemara*'s first answer, is limited to the special case of a brick-constructed wardrobe on *Yom Tov*, where dismantling is permissible. In the general case — a wooden wardrobe, on *Shabbos* — the *tanna kamma* would also concede that the *eiruv* is invalid.

Rabbi Eliezer, who in the *mishna* appears to be the strict opinion, turns out on examination to be more lenient than the general position of the *chachamim*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even setting aside the special case of bricks and *Yom Tov*, the *eiruv* is valid on *Shabbos* as long as the key was lost within the city, since it could theoretically have been retrieved via the interconnected *reshuyos hayachid* — relying on the principle, attributed to Rabbi Shimon, that rooftops, courtyards, and enclosed fields all share a single *reshus hayachid* status for objects that spent *Shabbos* within them. Thus one could carry the key from domain to domain without transgression.

The *rabbanan*, by contrast, are unmoved by the theoretical possibility of finding and retrieving the key. Even if the key was lost in the city, they do not share Rabbi Eliezer's view that the various *reshuyos hayachid* constitute a unified domain for this purpose, and they therefore rule the *eiruv* invalid in all cases other than the special brick-and-*Yom Tov* scenario.

## Kli vs Ohel Debate

### Ohel Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הָכָא בְּמִגְדָּל שֶׁל עֵץ עָסְקִינַן, דְּמָר סָבַר: כְּלִי הוּא, וְאֵין בִּנְיָן בְּכֵלִים וְאֵין סְתִירָה בְּכֵלִים. וּמָר סָבַר: אֹהֶל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The discussion above constitutes one understanding of the mishna. **Rabba and Rav Yosef,** however, **both said: Here, we are dealing with a wooden cupboard,** and the *tanna'im* disagree with regard to the following point: **The** first, anonymous **Sage,** who rules that the *eiruv* is valid, **holds** that the cupboard **is a utensil, and** that **there is no** prohibited labor of **building utensils, and** similarly, **there is no dismantling of utensils.** Since dismantling a utensil is not included in the prohibited labor of dismantling, one may make a hole in the cupboard in order to access the food used for the *eiruv*. **And** the other **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, who invalidates the *eiruv*, **holds** that the cupboard **is a tent.** A wooden implement of such a large size is no longer classified as a utensil; rather, it is considered a building, and therefore it is subject to the prohibitions against building and dismantling on Shabbat.

Rabbah and Rav Yosef propose an entirely different approach to the *mishna*, cutting through the complexity of the first answer. They argue: *hacha b'migdal shel etz askinan* — the *mishna* is speaking of an ordinary wooden wardrobe, not a brick structure. The reason the *tanna kamma* considers the *eiruv* accessible has nothing to do with *Yom Tov* or the special properties of unbonded bricks. Rather, it turns on the fundamental question of what halakhic category the wardrobe belongs to: is it a *kli* — a vessel — or an *ohel* — a structure?

If the wardrobe has the status of a *kli*, then the principle that there is no *binyan b'keilim* — no prohibition of building that applies to vessels — would mean that there is correspondingly no *stirah b'keilim*, no prohibition of destroying vessels either. One could therefore break open the wardrobe with a hatchet, just as the *gemara* in *Shabbos* permits breaking a wine barrel to access the wine inside. Destroying the wardrobe would not constitute the *melacha* of *soter*, and the *eiruv* would accordingly be considered accessible.

### Destruction of Vessels

The *tanna kamma* holds precisely this view: the wardrobe is a *kli*, and one may break it open. The second opinion — that of Rabbi Eliezer — holds that the wardrobe is not a *kli* but an *ohel*. A sufficiently large wardrobe, one that functions like a freestanding structure with a roof, takes on the character of a tent or small building. The roof is halakhically significant: creating a roofed enclosure is the paradigm of *boneh*, even when the structure is not permanent. If the wardrobe has *ohel* status, then breaking it open would constitute *soter*, and the *eiruv* would be inaccessible — hence Rabbi Eliezer's stringency.

It is worth noting in this context that Tosafot takes a more expansive view of *soter b'keilim*, arguing that even genuine vessels can be subject to *soter* under certain conditions. Tosafot therefore would say that even a smaller wardrobe — one that would not qualify as an *ohel* — could not simply be smashed open on *Shabbos*, since destroying a complete, fully formed vessel may itself constitute *soter*. This has implications for the familiar question of opening packaging on *Shabbos*: the wine barrel case involves an object designed to be opened, whereas a full-fledged *kli* that is not intended for destruction might stand differently. According to Tosafot, it would then follow that the *gemara*'s case necessarily involves a wardrobe bound with a string — but more on that shortly.

### Tum'ah Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְהָנֵי תַנָּאֵי, דִּתְנַן: הִקִּישׁ עַל גַּבֵּי שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל — טְמֵאִין. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֲרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** their dispute is parallel to **the dispute between these *tanna'im*, as we learned** in a mishna with regard to the ritual impurity of a *zav*: One of the unique laws of a *zav* is that he imparts impurity to an object simply by moving it, even if he does not touch it directly. If a *zav* **knocked on a carriage, crate, or cupboard,** even if he did not actually come into direct contact with them, **they are** nonetheless ritually **impure** because he caused them to move when he struck them. **Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Shimon** disagree and **render** them **pure.**

Having articulated the *kli*-versus-*ohel* dispute, the *gemara* suggests that this disagreement between Rabbah and Rav Yosef maps onto an earlier *tannaitic* dispute. The *mishna* in *Maseches Zavim* teaches that a *zav* — whose mode of transmitting *tum'ah* includes moving objects even without direct contact — who strikes a *shida*, *teiva*, or *migdal*, renders them *tamei*. The *tanna kamma* rules them *tamei*; Rabbi Nechemiah and Rabbi Shimon rule them *tahor*.

The *gemara* suggests that their dispute may hinge on the same question: if the *migdal* is a *kli*, it is susceptible to *tum'ah* through the *zav*'s action; if it is an *ohel*, it is not. This would make the *tanna'im*'s debate in *Zavim* a direct parallel to Rabbah and Rav Yosef's dispute in our *sugya*.

### Tremor Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְתִיסְבְּרָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֹהֶל וְנִיסָּט — טָמֵא, כְּלִי וְאֵינוֹ נִיסָּט — טָהוֹר. וְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְאִם הָיוּ נִיסּוֹטִין — טְמֵאִים, זֶה הַכְּלָל: נִיסָּט מֵחֲמַת כֹּחוֹ — טָמֵא, מֵחֲמַת רְעָדָה — טָהוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in refutation of this proof: **And** do **you think** that this is a reasonable explanation of the mishna? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If a *zav* shook a real **tent, and it moved, it is** ritually **impure;** and if he shook **a utensil and it did not move, it is** ritually **pure?** This indicates that the critical factor is not whether the article is classified as a tent or as a utensil but whether or not it actually moves when shaken. Furthermore, **it was taught in the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **And if they moved, they are** ritually **impure,** and **this is the principle:** If a utensil or a tent **moved due to the** direct **force** of the *zav*, **it is** ritually **impure.** But if it moved **due to vibrations,** e.g., if the *zav* knocked on the floor or on the platform upon which the object is located, and the vibrations of the floor or the platform caused the object to move, **it is** ritually **pure,** as it was not moved by the direct force of the *zav*. Once again, the determining factor is not the object's classification as a tent or a utensil but whether it was actually moved by the *zav*.

Abaye, however, rejects this parallel. He adduces a *braisa* that makes clear that the *ohel* status of the *migdal* is irrelevant to the *tum'ah* question. The *braisa* states: if an *ohel* was actually moved — *nisa* — by the *zav*, it becomes *tamei*; if a *kli* was not actually moved — *eino nisa* — it remains *tahor*. The classification as *ohel* or *kli* does not determine the outcome; what matters is whether genuine displacement occurred. Furthermore, the *mishna* in *Zavim* itself states in its closing clause that Rabbi Nechemiah and Rabbi Shimon concede that if the object was actually moved — *nisutin* — it would be *tamei*. So there is no dispute about the principle.

What, then, is their dispute? Abaye explains: *ve-hacha be-re'adah machmat kocho askinan* — they are debating a case where the *zav* strikes the wardrobe and it shakes in place, vibrating without being displaced. The rule of *nisa machmat kocho* — moved through direct force — renders it *tamei*; *machmat re'adah* — moved through vibration — renders it *tahor*. Everyone agrees on this rule. The debate is whether causing an object to shake in place without displacing it constitutes *hesset*, genuine movement, or not. One opinion holds that shaking counts as movement; the other holds it does not. In any event, Abaye has shown that the *kli*-versus-*ohel* distinction is not what the *tanna'im* in *Zavim* are arguing about.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הֶיסֵּט מֵחֲמַת כֹּחוֹ — טָמֵא, מֵחֲמַת רְעָדָה — טָהוֹר. וְהָכָא בִּרְעָדָה מֵחֲמַת כֹּחוֹ עָסְקִינַן, וּבְהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר — הָוֵי הֶיסֵּט, וּמָר סָבַר — לָא הָוֵי הֶיסֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the dispute between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Shimon should be understood as follows: **All** agree that **movement due to** the direct **force** of the *zav* causes the object to become ritually **impure,** whether it is a tent or a utensil. Conversely, if the movement was **due to vibrations** of the floor or base, **it is** ritually **pure. And here, we are dealing with** a case where the object **vibrated because of** the direct **force** of the *zav*, i.e., where he banged upon the object itself, causing it to vibrate but not to move. **And** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to the following** point: **The** first **Sage holds** that this, too, **is** considered **movement; and the** other **Sage,** Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that vibration **is not** considered **movement.** Therefore, Abaye rejects Rabbah and Rav Yosef's proof for their explanation of the mishna.

As Rashi frames it, the practical upshot for our *eiruv* question is as follows: if the wardrobe is small enough to be a genuine *kli* — below the threshold of *arbaim se'ah* volume — then everyone agrees one can break it, and the *eiruv* is accessible. If it is large enough to fall into a non-*kli* category — above *arbaim se'ah* — then everyone agrees it cannot be broken. The middle ground that generates debate must lie elsewhere.

## String & Knife Issue

### String Binding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְנִיתִין בְּמַאי מוֹקְמִינַן לַהּ? אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בְּמַנְעוּל וּקְטִיר בְּמִתְנָא עָסְקִינַן, וּבָעֵי סַכִּינָא לְמִיפְסְקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore proceeds to ask: If so, **how is the mishna** with regard to *eiruv* **to be interpreted?** The Gemara answers: **Abaye and Rava both said: We are dealing** here **with a lock** that is **tied with a leather strap, and a knife is required to cut it** if there is no key.

Both Abaye and Rava resolve the problem together. The *mishna* must be discussing a case of *migdal kashur b'matna* — a wardrobe bound with a string. Rashi's understanding is that we are dealing with a large wardrobe that is too big to qualify as a *kli* and therefore cannot be broken open without transgressing *soter*. That rules out simply smashing it. However, the wardrobe is secured not only by a lock but also by a rope or string wrapped around it. The key to the lock may be missing, but the string is accessible. Could one simply cut the string and open the wardrobe that way, without touching the lock?

The answer requires a knife, and here the entire dispute of the *mishna* resolves itself into a *muktzah* question: may one use a knife on *Shabbos* to cut a rope?

### Rabbi Yosei Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: כָּל הַכֵּלִים נִיטָּלִין בְּשַׁבָּת, חוּץ מִמַּסָּר הַגָּדוֹל וְיָתֵד שֶׁל מַחֲרֵישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The** anonymous **first *tanna* holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said: All utensils may be moved on Shabbat, except for a large saw and the blade of a plow.** Consequently, one may take a knife, cut the strap, and remove his *eiruv* from the cupboard.

The *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* rules the *eiruv* valid because he holds like Rabbi Yosei, *de-amar kol ha-kelim nitlin b'Shabbos* — that all vessels may be handled on *Shabbos* except for the great saw and the plow-peg. In other words, any ordinary vessel may be used even for purposes beyond its primary function, without concern for *muktzah*. On this view, one may take a kitchen knife and use it to cut the rope binding the wardrobe. The *eiruv* is therefore considered accessible, and it is valid.

### Rabbi Nechemiah Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ טַלִּית אֲפִילּוּ תַּרְווֹד — אֵין נִיטָּלִין אֶלָּא לְצוֹרֶךְ תַּשְׁמִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, who said: Even a cloak,** and **even a spoon,** which are certainly used only for activities permitted on Shabbat, **may be moved** on Shabbat **only for the purpose of their** ordinary **use.** The same applies to a knife, which may be moved only in order to cut food, but not for any other purpose. Consequently, one cannot cut the strap around the lock of the cupboard, and therefore his *eiruv* is invalid unless the key is located in town and he can transport it via courtyards.

Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, holds like Rabbi Nechemiah, *de-amar afilu tallis ve-afilu tarvad ein nitlin ella le-tzorech tashmishan* — that even a cloak or a ladle may only be handled for their own specific, intended purpose. A kitchen knife is intended for cutting food; using it to cut a rope is outside its designated function, and therefore the knife is *muktzah* for that purpose. Since one cannot use the knife, the rope cannot be cut, the wardrobe cannot be opened, and the *eiruv* is inaccessible.

It is striking that what appears in the *mishna* as a fundamental dispute about the validity of an *eiruv* in a locked wardrobe resolves, on this reading, into a narrow and technical *muktzah* question: whether Rabbi Yosei or Rabbi Nechemiah's position on handling vessels governs.

## Nitgalgal & Destruction

### Rolling Away

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִתְגַּלְגֵּל חוּץ לַתְּחוּם, נָפַל עָלָיו גַּל אוֹ נִשְׂרַף, תְּרוּמָה וְנִטְמֵאת, מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — אֵינוֹ עֵירוּב. מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה — הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one's *eiruv* **rolled beyond the** Shabbat **limit,** and he no longer has access to his *eiruv* since he may not go beyond his limit, or if **a pile** of stones **fell on it, or** if **it was burnt,** or if the *eiruv* was ***teruma* and it became** ritually **impure;** if any of these occurrences took place **while it was still day,** prior to the onset of Shabbat, **it is not** a valid ***eiruv*,** since one did not have an *eiruv* at twilight, which is the time one's Shabbat residence is established. However, if any of these occurred **after dark,** when it was already Shabbat, **it is** a valid ***eiruv*,** as it was intact and accessible at the time one's Shabbat residence is determined.

The next *mishna* addresses what happens when the *eiruv* itself is removed from its intended location before *Shabbos* begins. *Nitgalgal chutz la-techum* — if it rolled outside the *techum*, it started *Shabbos* beyond the two-thousand-*amah* boundary that the *eiruv* was meant to extend, and it can no longer serve its function. The *mishna* proceeds to enumerate additional cases: *nafal alav gal* — rubble fell on top of it; *nisraf* — it was burned; or, if it was *terumah*, it became *tamei*. The rule is: if any of these occurred while it was still *b'od yom* — on Friday before *Shabbos* — the *eiruv* is invalid. If they occurred only after nightfall — *mishe-chashechah* — it is valid, since at the moment of *Shabbos* the *eiruv* was in proper order.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם סָפֵק, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי זֶה חַמָּר גַּמָּל.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the matter is **in doubt,** i.e., if he does not know when one of the aforementioned incidents occurred, **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda say: This** person **is** in the position of both **a donkey driver,** who must prod the animal from behind, **and a camel driver,** who must lead the animal from the front, i.e., he is a person who is pulled in two opposite directions. Due to the uncertainty concerning his Shabbat border, he must act stringently, as though his resting place were both in his town and at the location where he placed the *eiruv*. He must restrict his Shabbat movement to those areas that are within two thousand cubits of both locations.

Rava adds a qualification to the *nitgalgal* case: *lo shanu ela she-nitgalgel chutz le-arba amot* — the rule of invalidity applies only when the *eiruv* rolled more than four *amot* beyond the two-thousand-*amah* mark. Within four *amot* it remains valid, since the place where one deposits an *eiruv* is treated halakhically like one's home, and within four *amot* of that location is considered to share that space. This is the source cited earlier in the *masechta* for the four-*amah* rule.

The most consequential case is the *safek* — doubt. If one does not know whether the disqualifying event occurred before or after *Shabbos* began, the *mishna* records a dispute. Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah rule *harei zeh chammar gamal* — it is like being both a donkey-driver and a camel-leader simultaneously. The donkey-driver walks behind and pushes the animal forward; the camel-leader walks in front and pulls the animal forward. If one is somehow positioned between a donkey and a camel moving in opposite directions, one is pulled both ways at once and can go nowhere. The idiom captures the halakhic predicament precisely.

If the *eiruv* was valid, the person acquired a *techum* extending two thousand *amot* to the north of where he placed it, but he forfeited the corresponding two thousand *amot* to the south of his starting point. If the *eiruv* was invalid, he never acquired the extended northern range but retains the full two thousand *amot* in every direction from where he spent *Shabbos*. Not knowing which, he cannot travel the full four thousand *amot* north — because maybe the *eiruv* was valid, in which case he gets only two thousand from the *eiruv*'s location — and he cannot travel freely to the south — because maybe the *eiruv* was valid, in which case he surrendered his southward range. He is left with only two thousand *amot* to the north of his starting point, the minimum guaranteed to him in either scenario.

### Building Falls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ נִתְגַּלְגֵּל חוּץ לַתְּחוּם. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְגַּלְגֵּל חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, אֲבָל לְתוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — הַנּוֹתֵן עֵירוּבוֹ יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna: If one's *eiruv* **rolled beyond the** Shabbat **limit** prior to the onset of Shabbat, it is not a valid *eiruv*. **Rava said: They only taught** this in a case **where** one established his *eiruv* at the edge of his town's Shabbat limit and the *eiruv* **rolled more than four cubits** outside that limit; **however,** if it remained **within four cubits** of the Shabbat limit, it is a valid *eiruv*. The principle is that **one who places his *eiruv*** in a particular location **has four cubits** around it, since he has established his Shabbat residence there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל עָלָיו גַּל וְכוּ׳. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דְּאִי בָּעֵי מָצֵי שָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If **a pile** of stones **fell on** the *eiruv* prior to the onset of Shabbat, it is not a valid *eiruv*. **It** might **enter your mind** to say that the mishna is referring to a case **where if one wanted he could take** the *eiruv*, i.e., where it is physically possible to clear the stones and remove the *eiruv* from underneath them. The only reason he cannot do so is because of the rabbinic prohibition to handle items that are set-aside, such as stones, on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי, הָאָמַר: כָּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת — לֹא גָּזְרוּ עָלָיו בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As, if** you say that **it is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, there is a difficulty: **Didn't he say** that with regard to **anything prohibited due to rabbinic decree, they did not issue the decree to apply during twilight?** The prohibition to handle items that are set-aside is also a rabbinic decree, and therefore, according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, since the *eiruv* was accessible at twilight, it should be valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא כְּרַבִּי, לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּבָעֵי מָרָא וַחֲצִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Even if you say** that the mishna **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, we can say that this ruling **was necessary only** in a case **where a hoe or a spade would be required** in order to remove the *eiruv* from under the stones, i.e., one would have to dig, which is a Shabbat labor prohibited by Torah law, not only by rabbinic decree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי. דְּאִי תְּנָא ״נִתְגַּלְגֵּל״ — מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיתָא גַּבֵּיהּ. אֲבָל נָפַל עָלָיו גַּל, דְּאִיתֵיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ — אֵימָא לֶיהֱוֵי עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** both rulings, the ruling concerning an *eiruv* that rolled beyond the Shabbat limit and the ruling concerning an *eiruv* that became buried under a pile of rocks, **are necessary. As, if** the mishna had only **taught** the case of the *eiruv* that **rolled** away, we might have said that the *eiruv* is invalid **because it is not near him, but** if **a pile** of rocks **fell on** the *eiruv*, since **it is near him,** you might **say that it should be** a valid ***eiruv*,** as one does not actually have to eat the *eiruv*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא ״נָפַל עָלָיו גַּל״ — מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיכַּסֵּי. אֲבָל נִתְגַּלְגֵּל, זִימְנִין דְּאָתֵי זִיקָא וּמַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ — אֵימָא לֶיהֱוֵי עֵירוּב צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** the mishna had only **taught** the case where **a pile** of rocks **fell on** the *eiruv*, we might have said that the *eiruv* is invalid **because it is covered, but** in the case where it **rolled** away, since **sometimes a wind comes and brings it** back, **you might say that it should be a** valid *eiruv*. Therefore, it was necessary to teach both cases.

The *gemara* initially assumes that the case is one where the stones are merely *muktzah*, a rabbinic prohibition, and could in principle be removed. If so, one might expect the *mishna*'s ruling that the *eiruv* is inaccessible to conflict with the opinion of Rabbi — Rebbe — who holds that rabbinic prohibitions (*shevut*) were not in force *bein ha-shemashos*, during twilight. If the *eiruv* was already covered as *Shabbos* began, but the only impediment is a rabbinic *muktzah*, should Rabbi not consider it still accessible?

The *gemara* resolves this: the *mishna* could accord with Rabbi, but the case must involve a situation where a shovel or hoe is required to uncover the *eiruv* — and that would constitute a biblical violation. The *rishonim*, including the Ritva, explain that digging through rubble with implements could constitute either *boneh* — if it improves the floor of a structure — or *choresh* — plowing, if it disrupts and prepares ground in a field context. Either way, the prohibition is biblical, not merely rabbinic.

The *gemara* then explains why each scenario in the *mishna* is necessary. If the *mishna* had only stated *nitgalgal*, one might think the invalidity follows only because the *eiruv* is no longer physically present at the right location — but *nafal alav gal* would be different since the *eiruv* remains in place, merely covered. Conversely, if only the *nafal alav gal* case were given, one might say it fails because the *eiruv* is hidden and invisible — but for *nitgalgal* one might think it valid, since one can see where it went and a wind could theoretically blow it back. By stating both cases, the *mishna* teaches that any form of inaccessibility — regardless of the specific mechanism — disqualifies the *eiruv*.

### Burned & Impure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ נִשְׂרַף, תְּרוּמָה וְנִטְמֵאת. לְמָה לִי? תְּנָא נִשְׂרַף — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. תְּנָא תְּרוּמָה וְנִטְמֵאת — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further states: **Or** if the *eiruv* **was burnt,** or if the *eiruv* was ***teruma* that became** ritually **impure** before Shabbat, it is not a valid *eiruv*. The Gemara asks: **Why do I need** to teach these two cases? The essential point of both cases is the same: The *eiruv* is no longer fit to be eaten. The Gemara answers: The mishna **taught** the case where the *eiruv* **was burnt** **to convey the far-reaching nature of Rabbi Yosei's** statement, as he is lenient in a case of uncertainty whether the *eiruv* was burnt the previous day or only after nightfall, even though the *eiruv* is now entirely destroyed. Additionally, the mishna **taught** the case of ***teruma* that became** ritually **impure to convey the far-reaching nature of Rabbi Meir's** statement, as he is stringent even though the *teruma* itself is still present, and there is only an uncertainty about when it became impure.

## Safek Eiruv

### Chammar Gamal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: סְפֵק עֵירוּב כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַבְטוּלְמוֹס הֵעִיד מִשּׁוּם חֲמִשָּׁה זְקֵנִים עַל סְפֵק עֵירוּב שֶׁכָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon** disagree and **say: An *eiruv*** whose validity is **in doubt is** nevertheless **valid. Rav Yosei said:** The Sage **Avtolemos testified in the name of five Elders that an *eiruv*** whose validity is **in doubt is valid.**

With those boundaries established, the *mishna* turns to the core dispute about *safek eiruv* — a doubtful *eiruv*. The *chammar gamal* image captures the halakhic paralysis with vivid precision. A person who left his *eiruv* at point B, two thousand *amot* north of where he will spend *Shabbos*, intended to extend his *techum* northward to four thousand *amot*. If the *eiruv* was valid, he effectively started *Shabbos* at point B, giving him two thousand *amot* north of B but surrendering free movement south of his actual sleeping location. If it was invalid, he started at his own location and has two thousand *amot* in every direction.

Not knowing which scenario applies, he cannot commit to either. He cannot walk the full four thousand *amot* north — because if the *eiruv* failed, he has only two thousand from his starting point. He cannot walk freely to the south — because if the *eiruv* succeeded, he forfeited that range. He is left with the bare two thousand *amot* north of himself, the minimum guaranteed under either outcome. That is the chammar gamal.

### Meir & Yehudah

Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah adopt the stringent position. When the status of the *eiruv* is unknown, they do not allow any reliance on its prior good standing. The person is left in the *chammar gamal* position: restricted to two thousand *amot* north, and cut off from his full southern range. There is no basis, in their view, for defaulting to the favorable assumption that the *eiruv* remained intact when *Shabbos* began.

### Yosei & Shimon

Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Shimon take the lenient position: *safek eiruv kasher* — a doubtful *eiruv* is valid. Since the entire institution of *eiruv* is rabbinic, a doubt is resolved leniently. Moreover, the *gemara* will develop the point that in addition to the general principle of leniency in rabbinic doubts, there is a *chazakah* at work: since the *eiruv* was properly in place before *Shabbos*, one may presume that it remained in that valid state when *Shabbos* began. Rabbi Yossi bolsters this further by citing the testimony of Aftolmus in the name of five elders that a doubtful *eiruv* is indeed *kasher* — an ancient tradition confirming the lenient position.

## Meir Consistency Q

### Tum'ah Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר סְפֵיקָא לְחוּמְרָא? וְהָתְנַן: טָמֵא שֶׁיָּרַד לִטְבּוֹל, סָפֵק טָבַל סָפֵק לֹא טָבַל; וַאֲפִילּוּ טָבַל — סָפֵק טָבַל בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה, סָפֵק לֹא טָבַל בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה; וְכֵן שְׁנֵי מִקְווֹאוֹת — בְּאַחַת יֵשׁ בָּהּ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה, וּבְאַחַת אֵין בָּהּ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה; וְטָבַל בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן טָבַל — סְפֵיקוֹ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the mishna's ruling itself: **Does Rabbi Meir** really **hold** that in cases of **doubt** one must be **stringent? Didn't we learn** the following in a mishna: If a ritually **impure** person **descended to immerse** in a ritual bath, and there is **doubt** whether he actually **immersed or he did not immerse; and even if he** certainly **immersed,** there is **doubt** whether he **immersed in** a ritual bath containing **forty *se'a*** of water, the minimal amount of water necessary for the ritual bath to be valid, **or he did not immerse in forty *se'a*; and similarly,** if there are **two** adjacent **ritual baths, one** of which **has forty *se'a*** of water **in it** and is therefore valid, **and one** of which **does not have forty *se'a*** of water **in it, and he immersed in one of them, but he does not know in which of them he immersed;** in each of these cases, owing to **one's doubt, he** remains ritually **impure?**

The *gemara* now poses a sharp challenge against Rabbi Meir. His stringency in the *safek eiruv* case seems surprising given that the *gemara* assumes *techumin* might be rabbinic — and Rabbi Meir is generally lenient on rabbinic doubts. The *gemara* supports this from a well-known *mishna* concerning *tum'ah*: someone who was *tamei* went to immerse in a *mikvah*, and is uncertain whether he immersed, or uncertain whether the *mikvah* contained the requisite *arbaim se'ah*, or chose between two *mikvaos* one of which was *kasher* and one of which was not, without knowing which he entered — all of these constitute a *safek* regarding his *taharah*. When the underlying *tum'ah* is biblical in severity, the rule is *sefeko tamei*: he remains presumed *tamei*, since he began in that status and cannot confirm its removal. But when the underlying *tum'ah* is a rabbinic one — such as eating *tamei* food, drinking *tamei* liquids, or immersing in drawn water (*mayim she'uvin*) — the ruling is *sefeko tahor*. On a rabbinic *tum'ah*, the *safek* is resolved leniently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה. אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאָה קַלָּה, כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל אוֹכָלִין טְמֵאִין וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין טְמֵאִין, וְהַבָּא רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בְּמַיִם שְׁאוּבִין, אוֹ שֶׁנָּפְלוּ עַל רֹאשׁוֹ וְעַל רוּבּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין מַיִם שְׁאוּבִין, וְיָרַד לִטְבּוֹל; סָפֵק טָבַל סָפֵק לֹא טָבַל; וַאֲפִילּוּ טָבַל — סָפֵק טָבַל בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה, סָפֵק לֹא טָבַל בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה; וְכֵן שְׁנֵי מִקְווֹאוֹת — בְּאַחַת יֵשׁ בָּהּ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה, וְאַחַת אֵין בָּהּ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה; וְטָבַל בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן טָבַל — סְפֵיקוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **In what case is this statement,** which maintains that in cases of doubt one is considered impure, **said?** It is said **with regard to severe** forms of ritual **impurity,** i.e., those imparted by a primary source of ritual impurity. **However, with regard to lenient** forms of ritual **impurity** imposed only by rabbinic decree, **such as one who ate** half a half-loaf of **impure foods; and** similarly, **one who drank impure liquids; and one whose head and most of his body came under drawn water,** as opposed to spring water or rainwater, in which case the Sages decreed that person to be ritually impure; **or if three *log* of drawn water fell on one's head and most of his body,** in which case the Sages also decreed that person to be impure; **and** if in any of these cases **one descended to immerse** himself in a ritual bath to purify himself of the rabbinically decreed impurity, and there is **doubt** whether he actually **immersed or he did not immerse; and even if he** certainly **immersed,** there is **doubt** whether he **immersed in forty *se'a*** of water **or he did not immerse in forty *se'a*; and similarly,** if there were **two ritual baths, one** of which **has forty *se'a*** of water **in it and one** of which **does not have forty *se'a*** of water **in it, and he immersed in one of them, but he does not know in which of them he immersed;** in all of these cases, owing to **his doubt, he is** ritually **pure.**

This is presumed to reflect the opinion of Rabbi Meir, since the *stam mishna* is attributed to him. And it raises an obvious difficulty: if Rabbi Meir is lenient on doubts in rabbinic *tum'ah*, why is he strict on a *safek eiruv*, which is likewise rabbinic?

### Bird Flies Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: תְּחוּמִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Meir holds** that the prohibitions relating to Shabbat **limits are** prohibited **by Torah law,** and therefore the uncertainties in the mishna involve a Torah prohibition, with regard to which one may not be lenient.

A further challenge arises from a *mishna* regarding the measurement of *techumin* when the path crosses a mountain. The standard approach is to measure as the bird flies, treating the terrain as flat. The *gemara* describes this as *yachol le-havli'o* — one can "swallow" the mountain using fifty-*amah* measuring strings: one measurer stands on one side, another on the other, the string is held taut at the appropriate horizontal level, the position is marked, and measurement continues. When the mountain is wider than fifty *amot* — *eino yachol le-havli'o* — a more complex method is employed, *mekadrin beharim*, which still attempts to approximate horizontal measurement using a four-*amah* string held at chest and foot level.

Cited in the name of Rabbi Meir — specifically transmitted by Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai in the name of Rabbi Meir — is the ruling that one does indeed pierce through the mountain with measurements as the bird flies even in the *eino yachol le-havli'o* case. This is significant because Rav Nachman citing Rabba bar Abbuha rules that *ein mekadrin* — one does not measure by piercing through — *lo be-arei miklat* and *lo be-eglah arufah*. For *arei miklat*, the cities of refuge, the 2000-*amah* buffer zone is measured along the actual ground, not as the bird flies; similarly, *eglah arufah* requires measuring which city is closest by walking distance, not aerial distance. The *gemara* infers from these cases a principle: biblical measurements are made as the dog walks, not as the bird flies. If *techumin* were biblical, one would not measure them as the bird flies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר תְּחוּמִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא?! וְהָא תְּנַן: אִם אֵין יָכוֹל לְהַבְלִיעוֹ, בְּזוֹ אָמַר רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בַּר יַנַּאי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁמְּקַדְּרִין בֶּהָרִים. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ תְּחוּמִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — מִי מְקַדְּרִין? וְהָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֵין מְקַדְּרִין לֹא בְּעָרֵי מִקְלָט, וְלֹא בְּעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does Rabbi Meir** really **hold** that the prohibitions relating to Shabbat **limits are** prohibited **by Torah law? Didn't we learn** in a mishna: When taking measurements related to Shabbat boundaries, if a fifty-cubit rope is held at either end by two people, the distance between them is deemed to be fifty cubits, even if the distance on the ground is greater, owing to inclines and depressions? If there is a hill or incline between them that **cannot be swallowed** by the fifty-cubit measuring rope, so that the usual mode of measurement cannot be used, **in this** situation, **Rabbi Dostai said in the name of Rabbi Meir: I heard that we pierce mountains,** i.e., we measure the distance as if there were a hole from one side of the hill to the other, so that in effect we measure only the horizontal distance and ignore the differences in elevation. **And if it should enter your mind** to say that the prohibitions relating to Shabbat **limits are** prohibited **by Torah law, would** it be permitted to **pierce the mountains? Didn't Rav Naḥman say that Rabba bar Avuh said: We may not pierce** mountains **when** measuring the boundaries of **cities of refuge nor** when measuring which city is closest to a corpse and is therefore obligated to perform the rite of the **heifer whose neck is broken, because those** laws **are from the Torah;** therefore, a more stringent policy is used to measure the distances precisely?

The *gemara* therefore concludes: since the ruling attributed to Rabbi Meir permits measuring *techumin* as the bird flies, Rabbi Meir must hold that *techumin* are *d'rabbanan*. But then why is he strict in the *mishna* about *safek eiruv*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דְרַבֵּיהּ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״בְּזוֹ אָמַר רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בַּר יַנַּאי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁמְּקַדְּרִין בֶּהָרִים״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as there is no contradiction between the two statements. **This** statement, according to which Shabbat limits are by Torah law, **is his; that** statement, in which he is lenient, **is his teacher's.** The language of the mishna **is also precise** according to this explanation, **as we learned: In this** case, **Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai said in the name of Rabbi Meir: I have heard that we pierce mountains.** This formulation indicates that Rabbi Meir did not state his own opinion. Rather, he transmitted a ruling that he had heard from his teacher, even though he did not agree with it himself. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from this** that this resolution is correct.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ha didei ha de-rabbeih* — the leniency is his teacher's view, not Rabbi Meir's own. The language of the ruling makes this clear: Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai does not cite the bird-flies measurement as Rabbi Meir's personal position, but rather says *shamati* — "I heard a tradition that one is lenient." Rabbi Meir is transmitting what he received, not endorsing it as his own *pesak*. The *gemara* notes that the precise phrasing — *shamati* — supports this inference. Rabbi Meir personally holds that *techumin* are *d'oraisa*, which is why he is strict by *safek eiruv*, and he would not permit measuring as the bird flies for a biblical *halacha*.

## Meir Strictness

### Started Bad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דִּתְנַן: נָגַע בְּאֶחָד בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אִם חַי אִם מֵת, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמְצָאוֹ מֵת. רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְטַהֵר וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִין, שֶׁכׇּל הַטֻּמְאוֹת כִּשְׁעַת מְצִיאָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: There is still room **to raise a contradiction** between one ruling with regard to **Torah law and** another ruling with regard to **Torah law, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. As we learned** in a mishna: If one **touched one** other person **at night, and he does not know whether** the person he touched was **alive or dead, and the following day he arose and found him dead,** and he is in doubt as to whether or not he contracted ritual impurity as a result of having come into contact with a corpse, **Rabbi Meir renders him** ritually **pure.** It is assumed that the deceased was still alive until the point that it is known with certainty that he is dead. **And the Rabbis render him** ritually **impure because** it is assumed that **all** ritually **impure** items had already been in the same state as they were **at the time they were discovered.** Just as the deceased was found dead in the morning, so too, it may be presumed that he was dead when he was touched in the middle of the night. Therefore, Rabbi Meir is lenient even with respect to an uncertainty relating to a Torah law, and he holds that a person is presumed to be alive until it is known with certainty that he died. Why, then, is he stringent concerning doubt as to whether the *eiruv* had already become impure on the previous day or only after nightfall? Here too, one should assume that the *eiruv* is ritually pure until he knows with certainty that it became defiled, and so the *eiruv* should be valid, even if Shabbat limits are considered Torah law.

With that reconciliation in place, the *gemara* launches a second challenge against Rabbi Meir: a *d'oraisa* versus *d'oraisa* inconsistency. The *gemara* cites a *mishna*: someone who touched a person at night without knowing whether that person was alive or dead at the time of contact — *eino yodeia im chai im meit* — and then discovered in the morning that the person had died sometime during the night. Rabbi Meir rules *tahor*; the *chachamim* rule *tamei*, reasoning *shekol tumos ke-sha'at metziatam* — one assesses *tum'ah* statuses as of the moment of their discovery. Since the person is now found to be dead, the presumption runs backward, treating the corpse as having already been dead at the time of contact. But Rabbi Meir is lenient, even though touching a corpse is a biblical *tum'ah*. This appears to contradict his stringency in the *mikvah* case.

### Two Chazakos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ שֶׁהָיָה עָלֶיהָ שֶׁרֶץ כָּל בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת. אִי הָכִי, בְּהָא לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי סְפֵק עֵירוּב כָּשֵׁר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yirmeya said: The mishna** is referring to a case **where a creeping animal** that imparts ritual impurity **was on** the *teruma* that was used to establish the *eiruv* **for the entire twilight** period. The Gemara asks: **If so, in that case, would Rabbi Yosei say** that **an *eiruv*** whose validity is **in doubt is valid?** There is no uncertainty in this case.

The distinction is crucial. In the *mikvah* case, the person *began* in a state of *tum'ah*. His initial status was bad. The doubt is whether that bad status was removed; and when one cannot confirm removal, the rule is to remain with the prior bad status. The logic is unimpeachable: you were *tamei*, you don't know if you became *tahor*, remain *tamei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הָכָא בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים עָסְקִינַן, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם נִטְמָאָה, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was **Rabba and Rav Yosef who both said:** The doubt here does not result from the facts of the case themselves, but from conflicting testimonies and an inability to decide between them. **Here, we are dealing with two sets of witnesses, one** of which **says:** The *teruma* **became impure while it was still day,** before the onset of Shabbat; **and one** of which **says:** The *teruma* became impure only **after nightfall.**

The touching case is structurally different. The person began the night in a state of *taharah* — he was *tahor* before he reached out in the dark. Moreover, he himself began as *tahor*. So there are two *chazakos* of good status: the person being touched was alive and the one doing the touching was *tahor*. When there are two *chazakos* operating in the direction of leniency, Rabbi Meir is willing to be lenient even in a *d'oraisa* case. He resolves the doubt in favor of those two presumptive statuses and rules *tahor*.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, disagree with this approach and instead apply the principle of *ke-sha'at metziatam* — assessing as of the time of discovery — which overrides the prior good status.

### Witnesses Weaken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָתָם תְּרֵי חֶזְקֵי לְקוּלָּא, וְהָכָא חֲדָא חֲזָקָה לְקוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** That is not the way to resolve the apparent contradiction between the two rulings; rather, there is a difference between the cases with regard to the ritual impurity itself: **There,** with regard to touching a person who was later found dead, there are **two presumptions** supporting **leniency,** whereas **here,** with respect to the *teruma* being used for an *eiruv*, there is only **one presumption** supporting **leniency.** How so? With regard to one who touched another person who was later found to be dead, there are two presumptions of purity: Firstly, the person who was found dead was previously alive, and the presumption is that he remained in that state until we know with certainty that he was dead. Secondly, the one who touched that person was previously pure, and he remains in that presumptive state until we know with certainty that he became impure. Therefore, Rabbi Meir had adequate reason to be lenient. However, with regard to *teruma*, only one presumption exists, that the *teruma* was previously pure and presumably remained in that state until proven otherwise. Since there is no additional presumption, Rabbi Meir ruled stringently.

How does this framework apply to the *safek eiruv* case, where the *eiruv* also *began* in a good state? Rava and Rav Yosef propose that the case in the *mishna* involves two contradictory sets of witnesses. One set testifies that the *eiruv* was disqualified before *Shabbos* began; another set testifies that it was disqualified only after nightfall. Each set of witnesses undermines the other, leaving the question unresolved. But crucially, the very existence of witnesses testifying to the bad outcome — even if contradicted — erodes the *chazakah* of the *eiruv*'s initial good status. The prior good status is weakened not merely by uncertainty but by affirmative testimony to its failure. The *chazakah* is undermined, leaving Rabbi Meir unable to rely on it, and so he rules stringently.

### Two Chazakos Rule

Rava offers a cleaner formulation of the entire principle. *Hasam trei chazakei le-kula, ve-hacha chada chazakah le-kula* — in the touching case there are two *chazakos* supporting leniency; in the *eiruv* case there is only one. In the touching case: first, the person being touched started the night alive; second, the person doing the touching started the night *tahor*. Two independent presumptive statuses, both pointing toward leniency — and Rabbi Meir allows them to prevail even over a *d'oraisa* doubt. In the *eiruv* case there is only one *chazakah*: that the *eiruv* was in good order before *Shabbos*. One *chazakah* alone is insufficient for Rabbi Meir when the stakes are *d'oraisa*, and he rules stringently.

This distinction between a single *chazakah* and a double *chazakah* as the determining factor in Rabbi Meir's approach to *safek d'oraisa* represents one of the deeper conceptual tensions in the *sugya*, and the discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
