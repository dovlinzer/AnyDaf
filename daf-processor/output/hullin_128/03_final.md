# Hullin 128 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dangling Limb Debate

### Intro & Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁחֲטָה הַבְּהֵמָה – הוּכְשְׁרוּ בְּדָמֶיהָ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **the animal was slaughtered,** although this act of slaughter does not render it permitted for consumption by a Jew (see 73b), the limb and the flesh **were** thereby **rendered susceptible** to impurity by coming in contact **with the blood of** the slaughtered animal, as blood is one of the seven liquids; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Shimon says: They were not rendered susceptible** to impurity through the animal's own blood; they are rendered susceptible only once they have been wet with another liquid.

The sugya on 128a opens by revisiting a theme that has recurred throughout this *perek*: the dangling limb — a piece of meat or a limb hanging loosely from a living animal. The halachic background established earlier in the *maseches* is as follows. While the animal is alive, the dangling limb has the status of food and can become *tamei*, even though it is technically neither fully part of the animal nor an *ever min hachai*. If the animal is *shechted*, the limb cannot be eaten — we learned this from the principle of *basar basadeh treifah* — yet it is not classified as *neveilah*; it retains the status of food and remains susceptible to *tumah*. The *mishna* thus presents the following question: does the act of *shechita* render this dangling limb susceptible to *tumah*? Does the *shechita* or its blood constitute *hechsher* — that which makes food susceptible to become *tamei* through contact with liquid?

By way of background: ordinarily, both the act of *shechita* and the blood that flows from it make the meat of a properly slaughtered animal susceptible to *tumah*. The question is whether that mechanism extends to this dangling limb. Rabbi Meir holds that it does; Rabbi Shimon holds that it does not. The *Gemara* now asks: *be-mai kamifligi* — what precisely is the conceptual point of dispute?

### Rabba's Yad Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בִּבְהֵמָה נַעֲשֵׂית יָד לְאֵבֶר קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר אֵין בְּהֵמָה נַעֲשֵׂית יָד לְאֵבֶר, וּמָר סָבַר בְּהֵמָה נַעֲשֵׂית יָד לְאֵבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** The mishna is discussing a case where the blood of the slaughtered animal came into contact with the body of the animal but not with the partially severed limb. The *tanna'im* agree that if an appendage that constitutes a handle is rendered susceptible to impurity, the food to which it is attached is also rendered susceptible. But **they disagree with regard to** whether **an animal constitutes a handle for** its **limb.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **an animal does not constitute a handle for** its **limb,** and therefore the limb is not rendered susceptible to contract impurity along with the body of the animal. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **an animal constitutes a handle for** its **limb,** and therefore the limb is rendered susceptible along with the body of the animal.

Rabba frames the debate in terms of *yad* — the concept of a "handle." From the beginning of this *perek* we have been dealing with the notion that a bone attached to a piece of meat can serve as a *yad*, a conduit, for transmitting *tumah* to the meat. There was also a question raised earlier as to whether the concept of *yad* functions not only for transmitting *tumah* but also for *hechsher* — for making food susceptible through contact with liquid.

Rabba assumes that the concept of *yad* does apply to *hechsher*, and frames the debate accordingly. The question he raises is: can something vastly larger and more significant serve as a *yad* for something tiny and insignificant? Specifically: the blood of *shechita* contacts the cow's body. Can the enormous cow serve as a *yad* — a handle — for the tiny dangling limb, such that it is as though the blood touched the limb directly, thereby making the limb susceptible? One holds that *ein behemah na'asis yad le-ever* — the cow cannot serve as a *yad* for its own dangling limb — and therefore the limb is not rendered susceptible. The other holds *behemah na'asis yad le-ever* — even this asymmetric relationship qualifies as a *yad*, and the limb is thereby rendered susceptible.

### Abaye's Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּאוֹחֵז בְּקָטָן וְאֵין גָּדוֹל עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: אוֹחֵז בְּקָטָן וְאֵין גָּדוֹל עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ – הֲרֵי הוּא כָּמוֹהוּ, וּמָר סָבַר: אֵינוֹ כָּמוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** a different explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon: The mishna is discussing a case where the blood of the slaughtered animal came into contact with the body of the animal but not with the partially severed limb, and both *tanna'im* agree that an animal does not constitute a handle for its limb. But they also agree that if the liquid comes into contact with only part of the food it renders the entire item susceptible to impurity. Therefore, if the partially severed limb is considered part of the animal it is rendered susceptible to impurity along with the animal. Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon **disagree with regard to** whether the limb is considered part of the animal, and generally speaking, with regard to any case where a small part of an item is hanging off the larger part such that if one **grasps** and lifts the **small** part the **large** part **does not ascend with it.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that although if one **grasps** and lifts the **small** part the **large** part **does not ascend with it,** the small part **is** still considered one and **the same** with the large part. Therefore, a partially severed limb is rendered susceptible to impurity along with the body of the animal. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that the small part **is not** considered one and **the same** with the large part in such a case, and therefore the partially severed limb is not rendered susceptible to impurity along with the body of the animal.

Abaye offers an alternative framing that bypasses the *yad* concept entirely. He focuses instead on whether the limb and the cow constitute a single halachic unit. The key observation is asymmetric: if one were to lift the entire cow, the dangling limb would come along with it; but if one were to lift only by the dangling limb, the cow would not come along — it would simply break off. One position holds that even in a case where *ocheiz be-katan ve-ein gadol oleh imo* — you grasp the small part and the large part does not rise with it — the two pieces are nonetheless considered one unit. Under this view, there is no need for the *yad* concept at all; the liquid that contacts the cow contacts the whole cow, dangling limb included. The opposing position holds *eino kamosho* — this asymmetry disqualifies them from being considered a single unit — and therefore the *yad* question is moot; they are simply two separate entities.

### Yochanan's Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר, בְּאוֹחֵז בְּקָטָן וְאֵין גָּדוֹל עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רָמֵי דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִי אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אוֹחֵז בְּקָטָן וְאֵין גָּדוֹל עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ הֲרֵי הוּא כָּמוֹהוּ? וּרְמִינְהוּ: אוֹכֶל שֶׁנִּפְרַס וּמְעוֹרֶה בְּמִקְצָת, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם אוֹחֵז בַּקָּטָן וְגָדוֹל עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ – הֲרֵי הוּא כָּמוֹהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינוֹ כָּמוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan also holds** in accordance with the explanation of Abaye that the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** the status of a small part of an item that is hanging off the larger part such that one **grasps** the **small** part **and** the **large** part **does not ascend with it. As Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a contradiction** between one statement **of Rabbi Meir and** another statement **of Rabbi Meir: Did Rabbi Meir** actually **say** that even in a case where one **grasps** the **small** part of an item **and** the **large part does not ascend with it,** the small part **is** still considered one and **the same** with the large part? One can **raise a contradiction** to this statement from a mishna (*Tevul Yom* 3:1): With regard to a piece of **food that was sliced** from a larger piece of food **and** remains **partially connected** to the larger piece, the entire item is considered one and the same with regard to impurity. If one who was previously ritually impure and immersed that day and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed touched either piece of the item, the entire item becomes impure. **Rabbi Meir says: If** when one **grasps the small** piece, the **large** piece **ascends with it,** it **is** considered one and **the same; but if** it does **not** ascend with it, it **is not** considered one and **the same.**

Rabbi Yochanan understands the debate in terms identical to Abaye's framing — the question of whether a small part's inability to pull the large part along means they are not a single unit. But he then raises an internal contradiction within Rabbi Meir's positions. In our *mishna*, Rabbi Meir holds that the *shechita*'s blood renders the dangling limb susceptible, which on Abaye's reading means Rabbi Meir considers them one unit even when the small piece cannot pull the large. Yet a *mishna* elsewhere (at the beginning of 108a) addresses an analogous case: a piece of bread in which one portion has separated and hangs connected by only a thin point of attachment. The same asymmetry applies — lift by the large piece and the small comes along; lift by the small piece and it breaks off. In that *mishna*, Rabbi Meir states *im ocheiz be-katan ve-gadol oleh imo harei hu kamosho* — it is one unit *only if* lifting the small piece brings the large piece along with it; *ve-im lav eino kamosho* — otherwise they are two separate units. In other words, in that *mishna* Rabbi Meir rules that the large will not follow the small means they are *not* one unit — the exact reverse of what he apparently holds in our *mishna*.

Rabbi Yochanan's response is *muchlefet ha-shita* — the positions should be reversed — though this is more a framing of the problem than a resolution. It should be noted that *Tosafot* observes that the text of that *mishna* as we have it actually has Rabbi Meir holding the reverse of what Rabbi Yochanan's version had, making the contradiction a non-issue according to our reading. Nevertheless, as Rabbi Yochanan had the text, Rabbi Meir appears to contradict himself.

### Tvul Yom Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּילְמָא שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין טְבוּל יוֹם לִשְׁאָר טוּמְאוֹת. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד טְבוּל יוֹם וְאֶחָד שְׁאָר טוּמְאוֹת. וְדִילְמָא, לְרַבִּי לָא שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ, וּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי מוּחְלֶפֶת הַשִּׁיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the contradiction between the two statements of Rabbi Meir, the Gemara asks: **What is the difficulty? Perhaps Rabbi Meir distinguishes between** the impurity of **one who immersed that day and other types of impurity.** The impurity of one who immersed that day is a more lenient type of impurity, because he has already immersed and need only wait until the end of the day in order to consume sacrificial offerings. Therefore, there is reason to be more lenient when he touches a partially severed piece of food and to rule that the entire food item does not become impure. The Gemara answers: One cannot make such a distinction between different types of impurity because it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** With regard to **both** the impurity of **one who immersed that day and other types of impurity,** the *halakhot* of contact are the same; whatever is considered contact that transmits impurity with regard to one type of impurity transmits impurity for all types of impurity. The Gemara asks: **But perhaps Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **does not distinguish** between the impurity of one who immersed that day and other types of impurity **and Rabbi Meir does distinguish** in such a manner. Therefore, there is no contradiction between the statements of Rabbi Meir. **Rabbi Yoshiya said: This is what Rabbi Yoḥanan** meant to **say: According to the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who does not distinguish between the impurity of one who immersed that day and other types of impurity, there is a contradiction between the two statements of Rabbi Meir, and **the** attribution of the **opinions** in the mishna in tractate *Tevul Yom* **is reversed.**

The *Gemara* proposes a resolution: perhaps Rabbi Meir distinguishes between a *tvul yom* and other *tumot*. A *tvul yom* is a person who has immersed in a *mikvah* that day and will become *tahor* that evening; in the interim, he is a second-degree *tamei* who can render *teruma* into *shlishi*. The *mishna* about the bread involves precisely a *tvul yom* touching *teruma*. Perhaps Rabbi Meir considers this a weaker *tumah* and is therefore more lenient in the bread case, treating the two pieces as separate units. In the case of the dangling limb, where the *tumah* in question may be more severe, Rabbi Meir might be stricter and treat them as one unit.

At this point it is worth flagging a question that arises about the nature of *tvul yom tumah*. *Rashi* characterizes it as rabbinic in nature. The *Gilyon HaShas* — Rabbi Akiva Eiger — cites this and expresses astonishment: *ha tvul yom hu tumah d'oraisa b'posel terumah b'negi'ah* — the *tumah* of a *tvul yom* with respect to disqualifying *teruma* is biblical, not merely rabbinic. He notes that *Rashi* should have said the *tumah* is weak (*klisha*), not that it is rabbinic, and leaves the matter as *tzarich iyun gadol*. The proper framing, then, is not that we are being lenient where the law is only rabbinic, but rather that by other *tumot* we are stricter — the default halacha treats the pieces as two separate units, which applies when the *tumah* is merely the weaker (though still biblical) *tvul yom* level; when confronted with a more severe *tumah*, Rabbi Meir applies a stricter standard and considers them one unit.

The *Gemara* then challenges this resolution by citing a *braisa* in which Rebbi states that there is no distinction between *tvul yom* and other *tumot* with respect to this question of unity. The *Gemara* responds: perhaps that is Rebbi's view, but Rabbi Meir may well distinguish between them. Therefore, Rabbi Yochanan's position is refined by Rabbi Yoshiya: *according to Rebbi* — who insists there is no distinction — the contradiction within Rabbi Meir's positions stands, and the *shita* must be reversed.

## Yad Tumah/Hechsher

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בְּיֵשׁ יָד לְטוּמְאָה, וְאֵין יָד לְהֶכְשֵׁר, קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר יֵשׁ יָד לְטוּמְאָה וְאֵין יָד לְהֶכְשֵׁר, וּמָר סָבַר יֵשׁ יָד לְטוּמְאָה וּלְהֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon: The mishna is discussing a case where the blood from the slaughter came into contact with the body of the animal but not with the partially severed limb, and both *tanna'im* agree that an animal constitutes a handle for its limb. **They disagree with regard to** the principle that **there is** a status of **a handle,** i.e., a handle is considered part of the item itself, **with regard to** transmitting **impurity** to the attached food, **but there is no** status of **a handle with regard to rendering** the attached food **susceptible** to impurity, as in that regard the handle is considered a separate item. One **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **there is** a status of **a handle with regard to** transmitting **impurity, but there is no** status of **a handle with regard to rendering** the attached food **susceptible** to impurity. Therefore, although the body of the animal constitutes a handle vis-à-vis the limb, it does not render the limb susceptible to impurity. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **there is** a status of **a handle** both **with regard to** transmitting **impurity and with regard to rendering** the attached food susceptible to impurity. Therefore, the body of the animal renders the limb susceptible to impurity.

Rava frames the debate differently again. He proposes that the entire argument concerns whether *yad* applies not only to the transmission of *tumah* but also to *hechsher*. All parties would agree that the cow can serve as a *yad* for the dangling limb in the context of *tumah* — touching something *tamei* to the cow would convey *tumah* to the dangling limb. The question is whether *yad* also extends to *hechsher*: does liquid contacting the cow count as liquid contacting the limb, so as to make the limb susceptible? One holds *yesh yad l'tumah ve-ein yad l'hechsher* — the *yad* concept conveys *tumah* but does not convey the capacity for *hechsher*. The other holds *yesh yad l'tumah u'l'hechsher* — *yad* functions equally for both. This is a striking *Gemara* in that a single disputed *mishna* permits so many distinct and conceptually rich framings: whether a large body can be a *yad* for something smaller; whether something is one halachic unit or two when asymmetrically connected; whether *yad* extends from the domain of *tumah* to the domain of *hechsher*.

## Hechsher & Intent

### Why Intent Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּהֶכְשֵׁר קוֹדֶם מַחְשָׁבָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** a different explanation of the dispute: Both *tanna'im* hold that a handle renders the attached food susceptible to impurity. Yet, the mishna is discussing a case where the slaughter took place before the owner of the animal designated its meat for the consumption of a gentile. Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon **disagree with regard to** whether an item can be **rendered susceptible** to impurity **before intention,** i.e., before one intends to use it as food. Rabbi Shimon holds that since the animal came in contact with the blood of the slaughter before the owner intended to use it as food, it is not rendered susceptible to impurity. Rabbi Meir holds that the susceptibility to impurity takes effect such that when the owner considers it as food it will be susceptible to impurity.

Rav Papa offers yet another framing of the debate. He proposes that the *mishna* concerns *hechsher kodem machshava* — what happens when food comes into contact with liquid *before* there is any intention to eat it. Ordinarily, with food whose intended consumption can be taken for granted, the moment liquid contacts it the food becomes susceptible. But the dangling limb presents a problem: a Jew cannot eat it even after *shechita* (it remains prohibited as *basar basadeh treifah*). The only scenario in which anyone could eat it is if it is *shechted* and then given to a non-Jew. Moreover, had the animal died naturally, the limb would have become *ever min hachai*, forbidden even to a non-Jew. This limb, therefore, cannot be presumed to be intended as food. It only qualifies as food if someone has affirmatively decided to eat it or feed it to a non-Jew. What happens, then, if the blood of *shechita* contacted it — creating *hechsher* — before the owner ever formed that intention? Is that *hechsher* valid, or must the item first be defined as food (through intention) before *hechsher* can take effect?

### Rav Papa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כָּךְ הָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שׁוֹנֶה – חֵלֶב שְׁחוּטָה בַּכְּפָרִים צָרִיךְ מַחְשָׁבָה, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר, שֶׁכְּבָר הוּכְשַׁר בַּשְּׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** This dispute is also found in that **which we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Okatzin* 3:2): **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Rabbi Akiva would teach this** *halakha*: **Fat** forbidden in consumption for a Jew from an animal **slaughtered in the villages requires intention,** i.e., designation, for consumption, for it to become susceptible to impurity. This is because the population in such places is small and there is an abundance of meat, so people do not generally consume the fat. Consequently, unless the Jewish owner intends for a gentile to consume it, it is not considered food. **But** the fat **does not require** contact with a liquid in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity, **as it was already rendered susceptible by the** blood of **slaughter** even though it came into contact with the blood before the Jew designated it for consumption.

This question is formulated by Rav Papa as the subject of the dispute in the *mishna*, and the *Gemara* turns immediately to a *braisa* to provide context. The *Gemara* states: *amar Rav Yehuda kach haya Rabbi Akiva shoneh* — this is what Rabbi Akiva used to teach.

### Rabbi Akiva on Chelev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לְפָנָיו: לִמַּדְתָּנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, עוּלְשִׁין שֶׁלִּקְּטָן וְהִדִּיחָן לַבְּהֵמָה, וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן לָאָדָם – צְרִיכוֹת הֶכְשֵׁר שֵׁנִי. וְחָזַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לִהְיוֹת שׁוֹנֶה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda continues: **I said before him: You taught us, our teacher,** that in a case of **endives that one picked and rinsed** in water **for** the consumption of **an animal, and** one later **reconsidered** and decided to designate **them for human** consumption, the endives **need** to come into contact with liquid **a second** time in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity, as food designated for animal consumption does not contract impurity. Evidently, for a food item to become susceptible to impurity, its contact with liquid must occur after one designated it as food. **And Rabbi Akiva** then **retracted** his previous statement and **taught** the *halakha* **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

*Chelev shechuta bakfarim tzarich machshava ve-ein tzarich hechsher she-kevar huchshar bi-shechita.* The case involves *chelev* — forbidden fat — from a slaughtered animal in villages, where *chelev* is not normally consumed. *Rashi* suggests two possible reasons for this: either there is proportionally more meat per capita in villages and so the *chelev* is not in demand, or, conversely, *chelev* is a delicacy that only city-dwellers can afford. Either way, in the villages the *chelev* cannot be presumed intended for consumption. The only persons who would eat it would be non-Jews. Rabbi Akiva's original teaching was: the *shechita* renders the *chelev* susceptible (*huchshar bi-shechita*), and all that remains is forming the intention to eat it. That intention may come *after* the blood has already contacted the fat. In other words, the order — *hechsher* first, intention second — is acceptable.

### Ulshin Counterexample

Rabbi Yehuda responded to Rabbi Akiva: you have already taught us otherwise. He invoked the case of *ulshin* — a type of herb or bitter green (chicory, or perhaps endive) — that was plucked and rinsed for animal consumption, and only afterward was the owner's mind changed (*nimlach aleihem*) to use them for human consumption. In that case, Rabbi Akiva himself had taught that a second act of contact with liquid (*hechsher sheni*) is required, because the definition of "food" (i.e., human food) only came *after* the first contact with liquid. The *ulshin* teaches that *hechsher* requires the item already to be defined as food at the moment of contact; the intention cannot retroactively validate a prior *hechsher*. By the same logic, the *chelev* in the villages — which only becomes food once someone forms the intention to eat it — should also require that the intention precede the *hechsher*.

### Akiva Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר לַהּ כְּמֵעִיקָּרָא, וּמָר סָבַר לַהּ כַּחֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa concludes: Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon disagree in the same manner. One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds in accordance with** that which Rabbi Akiva taught **originally, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds in accordance with** that which Rabbi Akiva taught after his **retraction** of his original statement.

Rabbi Akiva accepted this argument and reversed his position to align with Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning. Both the pre-reversal and post-reversal positions are now on record, and the *Gemara* maps them onto the *mishna*: *mar savar lah ke-meikara* — one Tanna holds the pre-reversal position, that *hechsher* preceding intention is still valid — and that is Rabbi Meir. *U-mar savar lah ke-chazara* — the other holds the post-reversal position, that intention must precede *hechsher* — and that is Rabbi Shimon. Both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon were students of Rabbi Akiva, and there is nothing internally contradictory about Rabbi Meir maintaining the pre-reversal position: he may simply have judged it the more logically coherent of the two, or may not have been present when Rabbi Akiva retracted it.

## Shechita Timing

### Blood Splatter Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: בְּנִתְקַנֵּחַ הַדָּם בֵּין סִימָן לְסִימָן קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said** another explanation of the dispute: The mishna is discussing a case where the blood of slaughter came into contact with a partially severed limb, and the blood of slaughter is one of the liquids that render food susceptible to impurity. But slaughter is valid only if one cuts the two *simanim*, the windpipe and the gullet, or the majority of the two *simanim*, and the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** a case where **the blood** in question **was wiped off between** the cutting of the first ***siman* and** the second ***siman*.**

Rav Ashi bar Rav Ika offers yet another framing, one that is admittedly a substantial *ukimta* — a forced reading of the *mishna* — but which allows the introduction of an important conceptual question discussed elsewhere. The scenario he proposes: during the *shechita*, blood splattered from the partially cut trachea and landed on the dangling limb. Before the *shechita* was completed, that blood was wiped off. Then the *shechita* was finished. Is the blood that came out and contacted the limb *during* the *shechita* — before the act was concluded — considered blood of *shechita*, which would make the limb susceptible? Or is it merely blood of a wound, which would not?

The underlying question is: only blood of *shechita* creates *hechsher*; blood flowing from a wound does not. The blood in this case came out between the cutting of the trachea and the cutting of the esophagus — before the *shechita* was complete. For the main body of the cow, there is no problem, since the cow will be covered by blood at the conclusion. But the dangling limb received only this early splatter, which was subsequently wiped away.

### Continuous vs. End-Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף, וְהַאי דַּם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר אֵינָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה אֶלָּא לְסוֹף, וְהַאי דַּם מַכָּה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **slaughter is** defined **from** the **beginning to** the **end** of its performance, **and this** blood that splashed on the limb **is** considered **blood of slaughter. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **slaughter is** defined **only** as **the conclusion** of its performance, **and this** blood from the first *siman* **is** considered the **blood of a wound** and does not render the limb susceptible to impurity.

This framing imports the earlier debate: *yeshno li-shechita mi-techilah ve-ad sof* — *shechita* is a continuous process from its beginning to its end — versus *eino li-shechita ela be-sof* — *shechita* is only formally defined at its conclusion. According to the first view, the blood that emerges mid-process is already blood of *shechita*, and therefore would create *hechsher*. According to the second view, the blood that came out before completion is merely blood of a wound.

### Retroactive Definition

This raises a conceptual difficulty. If *shechita* is truly only defined at the end, one cannot mean that only the final moment of cutting constitutes the act — that would mean one is *shechting* an animal that is already a *treifah* (having had its trachea severed without that act yet qualifying as *shechita*). The resolution must be: *eino li-shechita ela be-sof* does not mean the act occurs only at the final instant, but rather that we only *label* it *shechita* retroactively. While one is in the process of cutting, the act is not yet formally designated as *shechita* — it is only upon successful completion that one looks back and says: that entire process was *shechita*. If the act is never completed, it was merely an act of injuring and killing an animal.

This retroactive labeling is precisely what the *wiping away* of the blood illuminates. If *shechita* were defined prospectively from the outset, then the blood that contacted the limb during the cutting would already be blood of *shechita* at that moment — and it would have created *hechsher* regardless of whether it was later wiped away. The wiping away would be irrelevant. The only reason wiping away matters is if *shechita* is defined only at completion, looking backward. At that point of retroactive labeling, the blood is already gone — it is no longer present to create *hechsher*. Hence the debate turns on whether we look forward or backward at the act of *shechita*.

## Shechita vs. Blood

### Rav Ashi's Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בִּשְׁחִיטָה מַכְשֶׁרֶת וְלֹא דָּם קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** another explanation of the dispute: The case is where the blood of slaughter came in contact with the partially severed limb, but Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon **disagree with regard to** the principle that the **slaughter** itself **renders** the limb **susceptible** to impurity, **and not** the **blood** of slaughter. Rabbi Shimon holds that the blood of slaughter is not one of the liquids that render food susceptible to impurity. But the slaughter itself renders the meat of the animal susceptible to impurity, because it prepares the meat for consumption. Therefore, since a partially severed limb is not prepared for consumption by the slaughter, as it remains forbidden, the slaughter does not render it susceptible to impurity. Rabbi Meir maintains that the blood of slaughter is one of the liquids that render food susceptible to impurity, and therefore, the limb is rendered susceptible.

Rav Ashi (in what the *Gemara* introduces as a separate formulation) frames the debate as follows: *be-shechita machsheret ve-lo dam kamifligi* — the argument concerns whether it is the act of *shechita* itself, without blood, that creates *hechsher*, or whether it is specifically the blood of *shechita*. This was itself a dispute discussed earlier in the *masechta*: one position held that the act of *shechita* alone — even if miraculously no blood flows — constitutes *hechsher*, and that conversely the *blood* of *shechita* does *not* independently create *hechsher*. Under that view, it is the act of *shechita* that transforms the animal into food, and that transformation constitutes the *hechsher*.

How does this play out for the dangling limb? The *shechita* did not transform the dangling limb into permitted food — even after *shechita*, the limb remains forbidden. Therefore, if the mechanism of *hechsher* is that *shechita* makes the meat fit for eating, that mechanism does not apply to the dangling limb at all. If, on the other hand, the mechanism is the *blood* of *shechita*, then the blood — which contacted the cow's body and can be attributed to the limb via *yad* or unity — would create *hechsher* for the limb as well. The debate in the *mishna*, on this reading, is precisely which mechanism operates: *shechita*-as-act-of-making-food, which excludes the dangling limb, or *blood*-of-*shechita*, which does not.

## Categorical Exclusions

### Living Cow as Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבָּה: בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ, מַהוּ שֶׁתֵּעָשֶׂה יָד לְאֵבֶר? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** §Earlier (127b), Rabba explained that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon disagree with regard to whether the body of an animal constitutes a handle for its limb. With regard to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who holds that it does constitute a handle, **Rabba raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if **an animal** with a partially severed limb came into contact with a source of impurity **during its lifetime?** In such a case, where the animal is not susceptible to contract impurity because it is alive but the partially severed limb is susceptible to contract impurity as food, does the animal **constitute a handle for** its **limb** and transmit the impurity to the limb? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Having presented six distinct framings of the *mishna*'s dispute, the *Gemara* now turns to a series of fascinating questions about the limits of the *yad* concept. The first question is posed by Rava: *behemah be-chayeha mah shete'aseh yad le-ever* — can a living, breathing cow serve as a *yad* for its dangling limb? In the debate about our *mishna*, the cow was a dead animal; the question was whether the large body could be a *yad* for the small dangling piece. But now consider the case where the animal is still alive. We already know that while the animal is alive the dangling limb is considered food and can become *tamei*. But if liquid fell not on the limb but on the living cow, can that living cow serve as a *yad* to communicate *hechsher* or *tumah* to the limb?

*Tosafot*, following *Rashi*, notes that we already accept that a *yad* need not itself be susceptible to *tumah* — a bone can serve as a *yad* for the meat it is attached to, even though the bone itself is not food. But the cases we are about to encounter involve things that are categorically excluded in a more fundamental way — not merely inedible, but belonging to categories that may be so ontologically removed from the world of *tumah* that they cannot function as *yad* at all. A living, breathing animal is one such category. The *Gemara* answers: *teiku* — the question stands unresolved.

### Connected Cucumber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ, קִישּׁוּת שֶׁנְּטָעָהּ בְּעָצִיץ וְהִגְדִּילָה וְיָצָאת חוּץ לֶעָצִיץ – טְהוֹרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי מָה טִיבָהּ לְטַהֵר? אֶלָּא הַטָּמֵא בְּטוּמְאָתוֹ, וְטָהוֹר בְּטׇהֳרָתוֹ. בָּעֵי אַבָּיֵי: מַהוּ שֶׁתֵּעָשֶׂה יָד לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara introduces a similar dilemma. **Abaye says:** The Sages **said** in the mishna (*Okatzin* 2:9): In the case of an impure **cucumber that one planted in** an unperforated **flowerpot,** such that the cucumber is considered detached from the ground and susceptible to impurity as food, **and** the cucumber **grew and went out beyond** the edge of **the flowerpot** such that part of the cucumber is overlying the ground, the entire cucumber is considered attached to the ground and therefore becomes **pure. **Rabbi Shimon says: What is the nature of** the impurity of the cucumber that it **is rendered pure** in such a case? **Rather, the** part of the cucumber that is inside the flowerpot and **impure** remains **in its** state of **impurity, and** the part of the cucumber that is outside the flowerpot and **pure** remains **in its** state of **purity.** Based on this mishna, **Abaye raises a dilemma** according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon: **What is** the *halakha* if the part of the cucumber outside of the flowerpot comes into contact with a source of impurity? Does the part of the cucumber outside the flowerpot **constitute a handle for its counterpart** inside the flowerpot and transmit impurity to it to it? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Abaye raises a parallel question rooted in a *mishna*. A cucumber planted in a sealed flower pot (an *atzitz* without a hole) is not considered *mechubar l'karka* — connected to the ground — and therefore the cucumber can become *tamei*. If the vine then grows beyond the edges of the pot and hangs over the ground, the portion suspended over the ground is considered *mechubar l'karka* and cannot become *tamei*; but the portion remaining within the pot retains the status of a detached item and can become *tamei*. The *Tanna Kamma* had held that the entire plant is *tahor* in such a case — presumably because the connected portion purifies the whole — but Rabbi Shimon rejects this: *ha-tamei be-tumato ve-ha-tahor be-taharato* — the part that can become *tamei* retains its susceptibility, and the part considered *mechubar* retains its *taharah*, independently.

Abaye's question: can the portion of the cucumber that is *mechubar l'karka* — which is categorically excluded from *tumah* by virtue of its connection to the ground — serve as a *yad* for the portion sitting in the pot? The two are physically and fully one organism; yet halachically one half is in the world of *tumah* and one half is outside it. Can the excluded half transmit *tumah* or *hechsher* to the susceptible half? The *Gemara* answers: *teiku*.

### Avodah Zarah Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַחֲצִי דַלַּעַת אֲסָרָהּ, בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מַהוּ שֶׁתֵּעָשֶׂה יָד לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another dilemma based on the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** The Sages **said** that **one who bows down to a half of a gourd,** worshipping it as a divinity, **renders** that half of the gourd **forbidden,** i.e., it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, because it was worshipped as an idol. The other half, though, is not forbidden. Based on this ruling, **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** According to Rabbi Shimon, an item that is forbidden due to idol worship is not susceptible to impurity as food (see 129a). **What is** the *halakha* if the forbidden part of the gourd comes into contact with a source of impurity? Does the forbidden part **constitute a handle for its** permitted **counterpart** and transmit impurity to it? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Yirmiya raises yet another case. The *mishna* teaches that one who bows down and worships half of a gourd has rendered that half *avodah zarah*. *Avodah zarah* is forbidden in benefit — it cannot be eaten, sold, or used in any way that generates benefit, and therefore it lacks the status of food. Something that is not food cannot become *tamei*. Can the half-gourd that is *avodah zarah* serve as a *yad* for the other half that is not *avodah zarah* and retains the status of food? This case differs from an inedible bone: a bone is simply not food by its nature, whereas this half-gourd is physically edible — it is categorically excluded only because it is forbidden in benefit. Does that categorical exclusion remove it from the *yad* function entirely? The *Gemara* answers: *teiku*.

### Fig Branch Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ יִחוּר שֶׁל תְּאֵנָה שֶׁנִּפְשַׁח וּמְעוֹרֶה בִּקְלִיפָּתָהּ – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִם יָכוֹל לִחְיוֹת – טָהוֹר, וְאִם לָאו – טָמֵא. בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּעָשֶׂה יָד לַחֲבֵירוֹ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a similar dilemma. **Rav Pappa says:** The Sages **said** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:8): In the case of **a branch of a fig** tree **that was** mainly **detached** from the tree **and** which remains **attached** only **to the bark of** the tree, **Rabbi Yehuda** holds that the figs on the branch are considered as if they are still attached to the tree. Therefore, he **deems** them **not susceptible to impurity. And the Rabbis say: If** one is **able** to reattach the branch to the tree such that the branch can continue **to live** and produce fruit, then it is considered as if it is attached to the tree and the fruit on it is **not susceptible to impurity. But if not,** then the fruit is **susceptible to impurity.** With regard to this mishna, **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** According to the Rabbis, who distinguish between a branch that can be reattached and one that cannot, **what is** the *halakha* in the case of a branch that is mainly detached from a tree but can be reattached, and a second branch grows out from that branch, and is mainly detached from it and cannot be reattached? In such a case, the branch that can be reattached to the tree is not susceptible to contracting impurity, but the branch attached to it is. If the branch that is not susceptible to contract impurity comes into contact with a source of impurity, does it **constitute a handle for the other** branch and transmit impurity to it? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Papa raises a case from a *mishna* about a fig tree. A branch has been broken but not fully severed — the bark still connects it to the tree. Rabbi Yehuda holds it is still considered attached to the tree and therefore *mechubar l'karka*, so the fruit on it cannot become *tamei*. The Rabbis distinguish: if the branch can still survive on the residual connection, it is considered attached and *tahor*; if not, it is considered severed and the fruit can become *tamei*, even though it remains physically attached by the bark. Rav Papa asks: can the tree — which is fully *mechubar l'karka* — serve as a *yad* for this halachically-severed branch? The *Gemara* answers: *teiku*.

### Cornerstone Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: אֶבֶן שֶׁבַּזָּוִית, כְּשֶׁהוּא חוֹלֵץ – חוֹלֵץ אֶת כּוּלָּהּ, וּכְשֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵץ – נוֹתֵץ אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: מַהוּ שֶׁתֵּעָשֶׂה יָד לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses another similar dilemma. **Rabbi Zeira says:** The Sages **said** in a mishna (*Nega'im* 13:2): In the case of a large **stone that** is situated **in the corner** of a wall shared by two houses, where the stone is visible from inside both houses, if a leprous mark appears in one of the houses on the stone, **when one extracts** the stone **he must extract the entire** stone, even the part of the stone that is part of the neighbor's wall. **But when one destroys** his house, after a reappearance of the leprous mark, **he must destroy** only the part of a stone that is in **his** house, **and leaves** the part of a stone **that belongs to his neighbor.** With regard to this mishna **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* in such a case with regard to the transmission of impurity from the leprous house to the adjacent house? Does half of the stone in the adjacent house **constitute a handle for the other** half and impart impurity such that one who enters the neighbor's house becomes impure just as one who enters the leprous house? Or perhaps there is no *halakha* of a handle with regard to the impurity of a leprous house. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Zeira poses the final case of this series, drawn from the laws of *tzara'at* of houses. A cornerstone sits precisely at the boundary between two adjacent houses. When the house reaches the earlier stage of *tumah* requiring removal of stones (*chalitzah*), the entire cornerstone must be removed. But when it reaches the most severe stage requiring *netitzah* — shattering — one need only shatter the half of the stone belonging to the afflicted house; the half in the neighbor's house, which is *tahor*, may be left in place. Thus at the *netitzah* stage the stone is simultaneously half *tamei* and half *tahor*. Rabbi Zeira asks: can the *tahor* half of the cornerstone serve as a *yad* for the *tamei* half, such that one who touches the *tahor* half would become *tamei* through it? The *Gemara* answers: *teiku*.

All of these cases share a common structure: the *yad* candidate is not merely non-food or inedible, but is categorically excluded from the world of *tumah* in some more fundamental way — whether because it is a living organism, connected to the ground, forbidden in benefit, or halachically *tahor*. Whether such categorical exclusions strip something of the capacity to serve as *yad* remains, in each instance, unresolved.

## Death & Status

### Death Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתָה הַבְּהֵמָה – הַבָּשָׂר צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר, הָאֵבֶר מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר נְבֵלָה – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If **the animal died** without slaughter, **the** hanging **flesh needs** to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity in order to become impure, as its halakhic status is that of flesh severed from a living animal, which is ritually pure and does not have the status of an unslaughtered carcass. **The** hanging **limb imparts impurity as a limb** severed **from a living** animal **but does not impart impurity as the limb of** an unslaughtered **carcass;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Shimon deems** the limb **ritually pure.**

The *mishna* now turns to a different scenario: the animal was not *shechted* but died naturally. Here the *mishna* invokes the principle of *misa oseh nipul* — death causes the limb to "fall." While the limb was dangling during the animal's life, it was food but was neither *neveilah* nor *ever min hachai*. If the animal died naturally, we treat the death as though the limb fell from the animal at the moment of death — that is, as though it separated from the animal while the animal was still in the process of dying. Consequently, the limb does not become *neveilah* (because it was no longer part of the animal when the animal died) but rather becomes *ever min hachai* — the status of a limb torn from a living animal.

### Meat vs. Limb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי לְאֵבֶר (שֶׁל) [מִן] הַנְּבֵלָה? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ בָּשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ (מֵאֵבֶר בְּהֵמָה), דְּאִילּוּ בָּשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מֵאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי – לֹא מְטַמֵּא, מֵאֵבֶר מִן הַנְּבֵלָה – מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** the impurity of **a limb from a living** animal **and** the impurity of **a limb from a carcass?** The Gemara answers: The difference **between them** is with regard to **flesh that separates from the limb of an animal, as flesh that separates from a limb** severed **from the living does not impart impurity,** just like flesh that separates directly from a living animal. By contrast, an olive-bulk of flesh that separates **from the limb of a carcass imparts impurity** like the flesh of a carcass.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ika bein ever min hachai le-ever shel neveilah* — what practical difference does it make whether we classify this dangling limb as *ever min hachai* or as *neveilah*? Both cause *tumah*. The answer: *ika beinaihu basar ha-porish mimenu* — the difference manifests when a piece of *meat* separates from this dangling limb after the animal has died. *Ever min hachai* means the *whole* limb — only the complete limb in its entirety conveys *tumah*. This is analogous to *basar min hachai*: if one tears a piece of flesh from a living animal, it is forbidden to eat but does not convey *tumah*, because *tumah* of *ever min hachai* requires the full intact limb, not mere flesh. A piece of meat that separates from an *ever min hachai* is not itself *tamei*. *Neveilah*, by contrast, causes *tumah* through the flesh itself — a piece of meat from a *neveilah* does convey *tumah*. Thus the practical consequence of the distinction: if meat falls from this dangling limb, it conveys *tumah* only if the limb's status is *neveilah*, not if it is *ever min hachai*.

### Pasuk Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי דִּמְטַמֵּא, מַאי קְרָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״. וְהַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִכְאִידַּךְ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ – מִקְצָת בְּהֵמָה מְטַמְּאָה, וּמִקְצָת בְּהֵמָה אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה, וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? זוֹ טְרֵפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ! אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״מִבְּהֵמָה״, מַאי ״מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the verse** from which it is derived **that a limb** severed **from the living imparts impurity? Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to the impurity of a carcass it is written: **"And if some animal [*min habehema*]** of which you may eat **dies,** he who touches its carcass shall be impure until evening" (Leviticus 11:39). The phrase "some animal [*min habehema*]," which also means: From an animal, is interpreted as indicating that a limb torn from an animal is included in the impurity discussed in the verse. The Gemara asks: How can Rav interpret this verse in such a manner? **This** verse **is necessary for** another *halakha* **that Rav Yehuda** said that **Rav said,** as **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*:** In the verse: **"And if some animal [*min habehema*]** of which you may eat **dies,** he who touches its carcass shall be impure until evening," the phrase "some animal [*min habehema*]" indicates that **some** dead **animals impart impurity** as a carcass, **and some** dead **animals do not impart impurity** as a carcass. **And which is that** animal that does not impart impurity as a carcass? **That is an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*] that one slaughtered.** Even though slaughter does not render the animal permitted to be eaten, it removes the animal from the category of a carcass with regard to impurity. The Gemara answers: **If so,** if the verse teaches only one of the two *halakhot*, **let the Merciful One write *mibehema*,** in one Hebrew word. **What** is the verse teaching when it states: **"*Min habehema*,"** with two Hebrew words? It is teaching that one should **conclude two** conclusions **from it.**

The *Gemara* then asks: *ever min hachai de-mitamei mai kera* — what is the biblical source for the *tumah* of *ever min hachai*? Rav Yehuda citing Rav derives it from the verse *ve-chi yamut min ha-behemah* — "and if one of the animals shall die." The *Gemara* reads *min ha-behemah* — "from the animal" — not as referring to the death of the entire animal but as indicating that even if only a *part* of the animal "dies" (i.e., is severed from it), that causes *tumah*. This same verse, however, was also used by Rav Yehuda citing Rav for a different teaching: *miktzas behemah metamei u-miktzas behemah eino metamei* — some dead animals convey *tumah* and some do not. A *treifah* that was *shechted* does not convey *tumah* of *neveilah* even though it may not be eaten, because the *shechita* removes the *neveilah* status. The word *min* — used as a separate prepositional word rather than a prefix — signals that both teachings can be derived from the same phrase: (1) not every carcass causes *tumah* (thereby excluding a *shechted treifah*), and (2) even something less than the full animal — a severed limb — causes *tumah*. Both rulings are contained in the single formulation *min ha-behemah*.

## Ever Definition

### Meat vs. Limb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהֵא בָּשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַי טָמֵא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ – מָה מִיתָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹשָׂה חֲלִיפִין, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה חֲלִיפִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** if this verse teaches that a limb from a living animal imparts impurity, one can **also** derive from it that **even flesh** from a living animal imparts impurity. The Gemara responds: **It should not enter your mind** to derive this, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **flesh that separates from a living** animal **is impure** like a carcass. Therefore, **the verse states: "And if some animal** of which you may eat **dies." Just as death does not generate a replacement,** i.e., life is not regenerated from the dead, **so too any** element of an animal **that** dies and **does not generate a replacement** assumes the impurity of a carcass. An animal does not replace a severed limb but it does replace severed flesh; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

Having established that *ever min hachai* conveys *tumah* while flesh alone (*basar min hachai*) does not, the *Gemara* now turns to the foundational question: how do we define what constitutes an *ever* for purposes of this *tumah*? The challenge it poses is direct: if *min ha-behemah* means "something from the animal died," why is it limited to a full limb? Perhaps even flesh alone that separates from a living animal should convey *tumah*? A *braisa* is invoked to address this.

### Yosei: Non-Replacement

The *braisa* opens: *yakhol yehei basar ha-porish min ha-chai tamei* — one might have thought that flesh separating from the living conveys *tumah*. Therefore the verse teaches *ve-chi yamut min ha-behemah* — the separated item must resemble death. *Mah mitah she-ein lah chalifin* — just as death does not replace itself, so too only something that does not replace itself. A limb that is severed from an animal will not regenerate. Flesh that is removed may heal; the wound fills in and the animal recovers. Therefore only what is irreplaceable — a full limb — counts. This is the position of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.

### Akiva: Animal Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״בְּהֵמָה״, מָה בְּהֵמָה – גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת, אַף כֹּל – גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״בְּהֵמָה״ מָה בְּהֵמָה – בָּשָׂר גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת, אַף כֹּל – בָּשָׂר גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** One can derive this *halakha* from the word **"animal"** in the verse. **Just as an animal** contains **sinews and bones, so too, any** element of an animal that contains **sinews and bones** imparts impurity. Therefore, a limb, which contains sinews and bones, imparts impurity, but flesh, which does not contain sinews and bones, does not impart impurity. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** One can derive this *halakha* from the word **"animal"** in the verse. **Just as an animal** contains **flesh, sinews, and bones, so too, any** element of an animal that contains **flesh, sinews, and bones** imparts impurity.

Rabbi Akiva derives from a different interpretive move. The verse says *min ha-behemah* — from the animal. Just as an animal (*behemah*) has not only flesh but also *gidim va-atzamot* — sinews and bones — so too only something that has sinews and bones qualifies as an *ever* for *tumah*. Mere flesh, which lacks bones and sinews, does not qualify.

### Rebbi: Adds Flesh

Rebbi agrees with the structural logic of Rabbi Akiva's derivation but extends the required elements: *mah behemah basar gidim va-atzamot* — an animal has flesh, sinews, and bones — *af kol basar gidim va-atzamot* — so too the qualifying *ever* must have all three. Flesh alone is insufficient, but so is a bone with sinews and no flesh. All three components are required.

### Arkuva Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ אַרְכוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** the opinions of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabbi Akiva?** The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them is** with regard to the **leg joint,** which contains sinews and bones but no flesh. According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi it does not impart impurity when it separates from a living animal, but according to Rabbi Akiva it does.

What then is the practical difference between Rabbi Akiva and Rebbi? The *Gemara* identifies the *arkuva* — the lower joint of the leg, apparently characterized by an extreme paucity of meat (*rashi* describes it as having a lack of flesh, with the hard, sinewy structure predominating). Such a bone-and-sinew structure without meaningful flesh would qualify as an *ever* under Rabbi Akiva's requirement of sinews and bones, but would *not* qualify under Rebbi's requirement that flesh be present alongside the bones and sinews.

### Organs Test Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כּוּלְיָא וְנִיב שְׂפָתַיִם אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** practical difference **is there between** the opinions of **Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? Rav Pappa said:** The practical difference **between them is** with regard to the **kidney and** the **upper lip** that separate from a living animal. According to Rabbi Yosei these parts of the animal impart impurity because the animal does not generate a replacement for them; according to Rabbi Akiva they do not impart impurity, because they do not contain bones.

The point of difference between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is identified by Rav Papa: *kulya ve-nispataim* — kidneys and lips. These are organs that naturally lack bones. Under Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's criterion — that the separated item not replace itself — a kidney or lip, once severed, does not regenerate: it qualifies as an *ever*. Under Rabbi Akiva's criterion — that the item resemble a *behemah* in having sinews and bones — organs that naturally lack bones would arguably not qualify. *Tosafot* suggests that the question may really concern something that naturally possesses bones but is encountered in a boneless state; either way, the simple reading of the *Gemara* is that organs without bones are the operative test case between these two views.

## Sheratzim Extension

### Sheratzim Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי גַּבֵּי שְׁרָצִים כְּהַאי גַּוְונָא: יָכוֹל בָּשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַשְּׁרָצִים יְהֵא טָמֵא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּמוֹתָם״ – מָה מִיתָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹשָׂה חֲלִיפִין, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹשָׂה חֲלִיפִין. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״שֶׁרֶץ״, מָה שֶׁרֶץ – גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת, אַף כֹּל – גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״שֶׁרֶץ״, מָה שֶׁרֶץ – בָּשָׂר גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת, אַף כֹּל – בָּשָׂר גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת. בֵּין רַבִּי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ אַרְכוּבָּה. בֵּין רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כּוּלְיָא וְנִיב שְׂפָתַיִם אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states a different *halakha* where these three *tanna'im* express the same opinions. **So too, it is taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to creeping animals like this case:** One **might** have thought that **flesh that separates from a creeping animal** during its lifetime **should be impure** like a creeping animal carcass. Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to creeping animal carcasses: "And upon whatever any of them falls **when they are dead** shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:32). The word "dead" teaches that **just as death does not generate a replacement, so too any** element of a creeping animal **that** dies and **does not generate a replacement** imparts the impurity of a creeping animal carcass, excluding flesh; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. **Rabbi Akiva says:** One can derive this *halakha* from the term **"creeping animal." Just as a creeping animal** contains **sinews and bones, so too any** element of a creeping animal that contains **sinews and bones** imparts impurity. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** One can derive this *halakha* from the term **"creeping animal." Just as a creeping animal** contains **flesh, sinews, and bones, so too, any** element of a creeping animal that contains **flesh, sinews, and bones** imparts impurity. The Gemara comments: The practical difference **between** the opinions of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabbi Akiva is** with regard to **the leg joint,** which contains sinews and bones but no flesh. **What** difference **is there between** the opinions of **Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? Rav Pappa said:** The practical difference **between them is** with regard to **the kidney and the upper lip,** which contain no bones and which are not regenerated by the creeping animal.

The *Gemara* then teaches the identical three-way debate in the context of *sheratzim* — reptiles and rodents that convey *tumah* according to the Torah. The same question arises: does meat alone that falls from a living *sheretz* convey *tumah*, or only something that qualifies as an *ever*? The same three positions are restated. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili derives from the word *be-motam* — "when they die" — the same logic of *mah mitah she-eino oseh chalifin*: only something that does not replace itself qualifies. Rabbi Akiva holds that it must resemble the *sheretz* itself by having sinews and bones. Rebbi adds the requirement of flesh. The *arkuva* distinction between Rebbi and Rabbi Akiva applies here as well, and Rav Papa's *kulya ve-nispataim* identifies the distinction between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.

### Both Pesukim Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהֵמָה – הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלֹא מְטַמֵּא מֵחַיִּים, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא מְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, אֲבָל שֶׁרֶץ דִּמְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה – אֵימָא לְטַמֵּא מֵחַיִּים. וְאִי אַשְׁמְועִינַן שֶׁרֶץ, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא – לֹא מְטַמֵּא מֵחַיִּים, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה, דִּמְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא – אֵימָא תְּטַמֵּא מֵחַיִּים, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is necessary** to teach this *halakha* both with regard to flesh that separates from a living animal and with regard to flesh that separates from a creeping animal. **As if** the *baraita* **had taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **an animal,** one might have said that **this is the reason that** flesh that separates **from a living** animal **does not impart impurity:** It is **because** an animal carcass **does not impart impurity in** the measure of **a lentil-bulk,** but rather only in the measure of an olive-bulk. **But** with regard to **a creeping animal, which imparts impurity** even **in** the measure of **a lentil-bulk, say** that flesh that separates **from** it while it is **living should impart impurity.** **And if** the *baraita* **had taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **a creeping animal,** one might have said that flesh that separates from a creeping animal does not impart impurity **because** a creeping animal **does not impart impurity via carrying,** and therefore flesh that separates from it **while it is living does not impart impurity. But** with regard to **an animal, which imparts impurity via carrying, say** that flesh that separates from it **while it is living should impart impurity.** Therefore, **it is necessary** to teach this *halakha* in both cases.

One might ask: is it necessary to state both laws — by *behemah* and by *sheratzim*? The *Gemara* demonstrates that each is needed, because each could have been argued to be more lenient by analogy to the other. Had only the *behemah* source been stated, one might have reasoned: the reason mere flesh of a *behemah* does not convey *tumah* is that *behemah* *tumah* has a stringency not shared by *sheratzim* — namely, that *behemah* does not convey *tumah* in the quantity of a lentil (*khadasha*) but only in the quantity of an olive (*kezayit*). Since *sheratzim* convey *tumah* in the smaller quantity of a lentil, one might think that *basar min hachai* from a *sheretz* would also convey *tumah*. Conversely, had only the *sheretz* source been stated, one might have said: *sheratzim* do not convey *tumah* through being carried (*massa*), and therefore we are not concerned that flesh alone conveys *tumah*. But a *behemah* does convey *tumah* through *massa* — its *tumah* is more powerful — and one might have concluded that flesh alone from a living *behemah* conveys *tumah*. Each source counteracts the potential inference from the other, and therefore both are needed. With this the *Gemara* concludes: by both *behemah* and *sheretz*, there is a *tumah* of *ever min hachai* and there is no *tumah* of *basar min hachai*.
