# Yoma 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chafina & Blood Service

### Chafina Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הוֹלָכָה בִּשְׂמֹאל, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: נָטַל אֶת הַמַּחְתָּה בִּימִינוֹ וְאֶת הַכַּף בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: What** is the *halakha* with regard to **carrying** the blood in one's **left** hand? Is this action valid, or is carrying, like receiving and sprinkling the blood, an act that must be performed with the right hand? Rav Sheshet **said to them: We** already **learned it;** there is an answer to this question from the mishna: **He took the coal pan in his right** hand **and the spoon in his left** hand. This proves that although the spoon is carried in the left hand to the place of the service, the rite is valid.

Daf 49 opens precisely at its first word, and the subject at hand is the significance of the various acts comprising the *chafina* and the subsequent placement of the *ketoret*. The process unfolds in three stages: taking the *ketoret* from its original container into the hands, transferring it into the ladle, and then conveying it — in this case, into the *kodesh kodashim*. These three stages correspond, to a meaningful degree, to three stages in the service of a standard *korban*: the *shechita*, the separation of the portions designated for the altar from the remainder, the placing of the blood into the vessels, and then the conveyance of the blood to the altar.

The *gemara* had raised various questions about whether the *chafina* itself must be performed with one's hands or whether a vessel could be used instead. Throughout the preceding *daf*, the *chafina* was treated as a significant ritual act — a necessary and substantive component of the service — and considerable discussion surrounded its precise parameters. More significantly, the *gemara* explored the question of what happens if *ketoret* spills between the moment of *chafina* and the moment it is deposited into the ladle: is that a problem analogous to blood spilling on the ground? Is the hand containing the *ketoret* comparable to the throat of the animal? This comparison is not merely rhetorical; it underscores the degree to which these *avodot* parallel the *avoda* of the blood, *hadam*.

The *Tosafot Yeshanim* and *Tosafot* noted two respects in which the parallel is imperfect. First, the placement of the *ketoret* into the vessel functions more as a means of conveyance than as an *avoda* in its own right. Second, the taking of the *ketoret* from its container does not replicate the act of separating what goes on the altar from what does not — a distinction that is structurally integral to *shechita* — and therefore the equivalence between the two processes is not self-evident. These considerations left open the question of the degree to which the rules governing the blood service could be imported into the *chafina* service, or vice versa.

### Avodah Status Debate

Nevertheless, the *gemara* ultimately does not reach a definitive resolution on this question, and it goes on to note that a thought of *pigul* can occur even during the act of *chafina* — a point that strongly implies the *chafina* is itself a full-fledged *avoda*. The *Rambam* draws precisely this conclusion: the *chafina* is an act of *avoda*. This conclusion having been tentatively reached, the *gemara* proceeds to explore whether requirements known to apply to the blood service should be imposed on the *chafina* as well, and conversely, whether a leniency found in the *chafina* might apply back to the blood.

### Holacha & Left Hand

The discussion then shifts from the *chafina* and the placement into the ladle to the conveyance — the *holacha* — into the *kodesh kodashim*. The *gemara* raises the question: can the *holacha* of the blood be performed with the left hand? The reasoning offered is that since the conveyance of the *ketoret* is done with the left hand, perhaps the *holacha* of blood may likewise be done with the left hand, assuming a genuine equivalence between the two. This was, in fact, the tentative conclusion with which the previous *daf* closed: perhaps the conveyance of blood could be performed with the left hand, just as the conveyance of *ketoret* is performed with the left hand.

## Baraita Challenge (II)

### Invalid Persons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: זָר, וְאוֹנֵן, שִׁיכּוֹר, וּבַעַל מוּם, בְּקַבָּלָה וּבְהוֹלָכָה וּבִזְרִיקָה — פָּסוּל. וְכֵן יוֹשֵׁב, וְכֵן שְׂמֹאל — פָּסוּל! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard **to receiving, carrying, or sprinkling** blood, if **a non-priest, a mourner** on his first day of mourning, **a drunk** priest, **and a blemished** priest, performed the rite, it is **disqualified. And likewise** if the priest was **sitting, and likewise** if he performed one of these rites with his **left** hand, it is **disqualified.** This statement contradicts the ruling of Rav Sheshet. The Gemara concludes: This is indeed **a conclusive refutation,** and Rav Sheshet's opinion is rejected.

It is at this point that the *gemara* introduces a *meitivei* challenge — an objection drawn from a *baraita*. The *baraita* states: *zar ve-onen, shikkor u-va'al mum* — a non-*kohen*, an *onen* (a *kohen* who has suffered the loss of an immediate relative on that very day), a *shikkor* (a *kohen* who is intoxicated — a matter of some dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* as to whether this applies only to wine or extends to other intoxicants as well), and a *ba'al mum* (a *kohen* with a physical blemish) — all of these individuals are forbidden from performing the *avoda*, and if they do perform it, the *avoda* is invalidated. These prohibitions derive from explicit *pesukim* and are extended by the *gemara* through interpretation. The *baraita* specifies that these individuals invalidate the service in three of the four classic *avodot*: *kabbala* (catching the blood), *holacha* (conveying the blood), and *zerika* (throwing the blood). Notably absent from this list is *shechita*, because *shechita* is *kasher be-zar* — valid even when performed by a non-*kohen*. Whether *shechita* is equally valid when performed by an *onen*, a *shikkor*, or a *ba'al mum* is a separate question left for another context.

One may observe that the *baraita*'s structure falls into two natural groups. The first — *zar*, *onen*, *shikkor*, *ba'al mum* — concerns the personal status of the individual, his standing *be-gavra*, so to speak. The second group — sitting and use of the left hand — concerns the manner in which the *avoda* is performed, not the status of the performer. This distinction has logical coherence and also reflects the mnemonic rhythms of oral tradition, where organizing material as a group of four followed by a group of three creates a certain cadence that facilitates memory.

### Left Hand Forbidden

The *baraita* then continues: *ve-chen yoshev* — and similarly if the *kohen* is seated — and *ve-chen smol* — and similarly if he uses the left hand — all of these invalidate the service. Crucially, this prohibition against the left hand applies not only to *kabbala* and *zerika*, but explicitly also to *holacha*, the very act of conveyance. The *gemara* therefore declares: *pasul* — all of this is invalid. And then: *tiyuvta* — this is an outright contradiction of what was argued yesterday, namely that *holacha* could be performed with the left hand.

### Rav Sheshet Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב שֵׁשֶׁת הוּא דְּאוֹתְבַהּ, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת לְאָמוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, בְּעִי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: הוֹלָכָה בְּזָר מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּשֵׁירָה, וּמִקְרָא מְסַיְּיעֵנִי: ״וַיִּשְׁחֲטוּ הַפָּסַח וַיִּזְרְקוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים מִיָּדָם וְהַלְוִיִּם מַפְשִׁיטִים״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't Rav Sheshet** the **one who objected** on the basis of this very *baraita*? **As Rav Sheshet said to the interpreter of Rav Ḥisda: Raise the** following **dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: What** is the *halakha* with regard to **carrying** the blood performed **by a non-priest? He said to him:** It is **valid, and a verse supports me: "And they slaughtered the Paschal offering and the priests sprinkled with their hand, and the Levites flayed"** (II Chronicles 35:11). This verse indicates that the priests took the blood from the hands of the Levites, from which it can be inferred that the Levites carried the blood from the place of slaughtering to the place of sprinkling.

The *gemara* now raises an acute difficulty. If Rav Sheshet was the one who had argued that *holacha* could be performed with the left hand based on the analogy to the *ketoret*, how could he not have known of this *baraita*? In fact, the *gemara* reveals that Rav Sheshet did know this *baraita* — for it was Rav Sheshet himself who in a different context used this same *baraita* to challenge a different position. This makes his earlier argument all the more puzzling.

## Non-Kohen Holacha

### Amoraim & Tannaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹתֵיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: זָר וְאוֹנֵן, שִׁיכּוֹר וּבַעַל מוּם, בְּקַבָּלָה וּבְהוֹלָכָה וּבִזְרִיקָה — פָּסוּל, וְכֵן יוֹשֵׁב וְכֵן שְׂמֹאל — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet objected** based on the aforementioned *baraita*: With regard **to receiving, carrying, or sprinkling** blood, if **a non-priest, a mourner** on his first day of mourning, **a drunk** priest, **and a blemished** priest, performed the rite, it is **disqualified.** This statement proves that carrying cannot be performed by a non-priest. Since Rav Sheshet himself cited this *baraita* in his objection, he was certainly familiar with it. How, then, could he issue a ruling in contradiction to the *baraita*?

The *gemara* explains how we know that Rav Sheshet was acquainted with this *baraita*: Rav Sheshet said to the *amora* of Rav Chisda — that is, to the one who served as Rav Chisda's personal announcer — the following question. Before continuing with the substance of the exchange, it is worth pausing to clarify a terminological ambiguity. The term *amora*, used in this context, does not refer to the great *amoraim* of the *gemara* — figures such as Rava, Abaye, Rav Sheshet, and Ravina — but rather to the individual who served as a kind of human loudspeaker for a leading sage. In the era before any form of amplification, a scholar would whisper his teaching to this *amora*, who would then announce it loudly to the assembled audience. Similarly, the term *tanna* in this context does not refer to the *tannaim* of the *mishnaic* era — figures such as Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon — but rather to those whose function was memorization and recitation of fixed oral texts.

The *Mishna*, it should be recalled, was not committed to writing in the time of Rebbi. What Rebbi accomplished was the fixing of a textual tradition — an oral textual tradition — by harmonizing and standardizing competing oral versions. Once the text was fixed, Rebbi would call in a group of *tannaim*, repeaters and memorizers — the word *tanna* derives from the root meaning "to repeat" — and transmit the text to them verbally. They would memorize it precisely and then travel to different cities and towns to disseminate it word for word. As Saul Lieberman observed, what was wanted in such a *tanna* was a strong memory and very little creativity, since any individual stylistic contribution would corrupt the tradition. The earliest writings of the *mishnaic* text likely date to the beginning of the Geonic period, though certain tannaitic texts such as the *Sifra* and *Megillat Ta'anit* may have been committed to writing earlier.

The *amora* as announcer — from the root meaning "to say" or "to declare" — served a parallel function in the lecture hall: not to memorize but to project. These were the lowercase *amora* and lowercase *tanna*, distinguished from the capital-letter *Amoraim* and *Tannaim* who are the subjects of the Talmudic tradition.

### Rav Sheshet's Query

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction in Rav Sheshet's own position — how he could have argued on the previous *daf* that *holacha* may be done with the left hand, while in a later exchange invoking a *baraita* that explicitly prohibits it. But first, we must follow the logic of Rav Sheshet's query to Rav Chisda's announcer. Rav Sheshet instructed Rav Chisda's *amora* to pose the following question to Rav Chisda: *holacha be-zar mahu* — what is the law regarding whether a non-*kohen* may perform the *holacha*? Just as a non-*kohen* may perform *shechita*, which is *kasher be-zar*, might he similarly perform *holacha*? This question is particularly apposite because *holacha* is characterized as a *melacha she-efshar levatla* — a type of act that can theoretically be dispensed with, since if the *shechita* is performed adjacent to the *mizbeach*, no conveyance is necessary at all.

### Non-Kohen Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִקְרָא מְסַיְּיעֵנִי: ״וַיִּשְׁחֲטוּ הַפָּסַח וַיִּזְרְקוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים מִיָּדָם וְהַלְוִיִּם מַפְשִׁיטִים״!
>
> **Translation:** and a verse supports me: **"And they slaughtered the Paschal offering and the priests sprinkled with their hand, and the Levites flayed"** (II Chronicles 35:11). This verse indicates that the priests took the blood from the hands of the Levites, from which it can be inferred that the Levites carried the blood from the place of slaughtering to the place of sprinkling.

The *amora* — whether after consulting Rav Chisda or perhaps responding immediately — answered: *keshera* — it is valid. And he added: *u-mikra mesayeini* — I have a verse that supports this position.

### Pesach Evidence

The supporting verse comes from *Divrei Hayamim*: *vayishchatu hapesach*, they — the owners — slaughtered the *pesach*; *vayizreku hakohanim miyadam*, the *kohanim* threw the blood from their hands — meaning from the hands of the owners — *vehaleviyim mafshitim*, and the *levi'im* flayed the animals. The inference is that the owners were holding the vessels containing the blood and handing them over for the *zerika*, which means the owners themselves performed the *holacha*. The sequence, as reconstructed, would be: the owner performs the *shechita*, the *kohen* performs the *kabbala* — since that is uncontested as requiring a *kohen* — and then gives the vessel back to the owner, who carries it to the *kohen* near the *mizbeach*, who performs the *zerika*. This intermediate carrying by the owner would constitute *holacha* performed by a *zar*.

### Zar Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹתֵיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: זָר וְאוֹנֵן, שִׁיכּוֹר וּבַעַל מוּם, בְּקַבָּלָה וּבְהוֹלָכָה וּבִזְרִיקָה — פָּסוּל, וְכֵן יוֹשֵׁב וְכֵן שְׂמֹאל — פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet objected** based on the aforementioned *baraita*: With regard **to receiving, carrying, or sprinkling** blood, if **a non-priest, a mourner** on his first day of mourning, **a drunk** priest, **and a blemished** priest, performed the rite, it is **disqualified.** This statement proves that carrying cannot be performed by a non-priest.

Rav Sheshet then challenged this reading by citing the very *baraita* under discussion: *zar ve-onen, shikkor u-va'al mum, be-kabbala u-ve-holacha u-ve-zerika pasul* — the *zar* cannot perform *holacha*. How, then, can the verse from *Divrei Hayamim* be taken as evidence that a *zar* may do so?

## Rav Sheshet's Retraction

### Rav Sheshet Learned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּשַׁמְעַהּ הֲדַר אוֹתְבַהּ. וְהָא רַב חִסְדָּא קְרָא קָאָמַר? דַּעֲבוּד מַעֲשֵׂה אִיצְטְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **After** Rav Sheshet **heard** the *baraita* that was cited against his opinion, **he objected** to the ruling of Rav Ḥisda from that same *baraita*. At first Rav Sheshet was unaware of the *baraita*, which is why he ruled against it, but when he learned it, he relied upon it to object to Rav Ḥisda's statement. The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Ḥisda cite a verse** in support of his opinion? How can a *baraita* contradict a verse? The Gemara answers: The verse does not mean that the Levites walked with the blood, but rather **that they acted like benches** and merely stood holding the bowls of blood in their hands.

The *gemara* now resolves the apparent contradiction in Rav Sheshet's own position — how he could have argued on the previous *daf* that *holacha* could be performed with the left hand, while in a later exchange invoking a *baraita* that explicitly prohibits it. The answer is chronological: *batar de-shama, hadar bei* — after he himself was challenged with this *baraita* and came to learn it, he subsequently retracted his earlier position. The order of events, then, was as follows. First, Rav Sheshet reasoned that *holacha* could be performed with the left hand, drawing on the analogy to the *ketoret*. He was then challenged with the *baraita* that explicitly prohibits *holacha* with the left hand. Once he learned that *baraita* and accepted its ruling, he was able to turn around and deploy the same *baraita* in a later context to challenge Rav Chisda's *amora* on the question of *holacha* by a *zar*.

The conclusion that emerges is that both positions argued for yesterday are rejected: *holacha* cannot be performed with the left hand, and *holacha* cannot be performed by a non-*kohen*. Although *holacha* is in principle less weighty than some other *avodot* — it is the sort of act that can sometimes be bypassed entirely — when it is in fact performed, its invalidity under these conditions stands.

The *Tosafot Yeshanim* address the residual difficulty directly: *ve-chen smol kashya, u-matnitin ein reaya* — the prohibition of the left hand is a difficulty, but the fact that it is used by the *ketoret* is not a proof to the contrary, because by the *ketoret* there is simply no alternative — *mishum de-lo efshar*. One hand must hold the fire-pan and the other must hold the ladle; the two-handed requirement inherent in *melo chofnav* means the left hand must be involved, and accordingly the left hand was given a special dispensation specifically for *Yom Kippur*. As the *gemara* in another context states, the left hand was specially validated for use on *Yom Kippur* in this regard.

One might further argue — and this returns to the broader theme running through the entire discussion — that the acts performed in connection with the *chafina* do not precisely parallel the *avodot* connected with the blood in the first place. The *chafina* is not the *shechita*, the placement into the vessel is not the casting of the blood, and perhaps the *holacha* of the *ketoret* is likewise not the *holacha* of the blood. The question of whether *pigul* can apply during *chafina* cuts in one direction; the question of whether spillage is a disqualifying problem cuts in another. The analogy is real but contested, and the *gemara* has not closed the issue entirely. Two possible grounds of distinction exist: first, the ongoing and unresolved question of whether these *chafina*-related acts carry identical legal weight to blood-related acts; and second, even granting that they are generally equivalent, the left-hand usage by the *ketoret* is forced by necessity and cannot serve as a precedent for elective left-hand use in the blood service.

## Maise Itzba

### Passive Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב חִסְדָּא קְרָא קָאָמַר? דַּעֲבוּד מַעֲשֵׂה אִיצְטְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Ḥisda cite a verse** in support of his opinion? How can a *baraita* contradict a verse? The Gemara answers: The verse does not mean that the Levites walked with the blood, but rather **that they acted like benches** and merely stood holding the bowls of blood in their hands.

The *gemara* returns to Rav Chisda's position, noting that it was based on an actual *pasuk* from *Divrei Hayamim*. The verse seemed to indicate that the owners — *zarim* — were performing the *holacha* of the blood. The *gemara* responds: *d'avid maise itztaba* — the *zar* was merely acting as a passive pedestal. The *zar* held the vessel, the *kohen* took it from him and performed the *zerika*, but this does not mean the *zar* conveyed the blood. Rather: the *kohen* performed the *kabbala*, then conveyed the vessel, handed it to the *zar* — who simply extended his hand and held it stationary, functioning as little more than a stand — and then the *kohen* performed the *zerika*. The *zar* did not perform any act; he was entirely passive, inanimate in his role. Because he performed no action, there is no *avoda* to invalidate.

### Shabbat Parallel

This concept of *maaseh istaba* — passive pedestal — appears in several *rishonim* in connection with the first *mishna* of *Shabbat*, where the *gemara* describes scenarios involving a poor person standing outside and an owner inside a house, exchanging an object. One scenario has the poor person take the bread from the owner's hand, thereby performing the full *melacha* of carrying from inside to outside. In that case, the poor person is liable. But the *rishonim* ask: what of the owner? If he simply held out his hand and the poor person took the object, the owner apparently bears no liability. Why not? Is he not at least a *mesayeia* — a passive facilitator — and subject to some level of concern on the grounds of *lifnei iver*? The answer given by the *rishonim* is precisely *maaseh istaba*: the owner was utterly passive, performing no act whatsoever, and an individual who is completely inert — acting as an inanimate object — cannot have any act attributed to him.

One student in the course of this discussion raised the challenge that a human being cannot truly be completely passive — even the act of holding something still requires some muscular engagement, some micro-movement. This is a sharp observation that goes to the heart of what it means to perform an *avoda* or a *melacha* through another's body, and it is a difficulty that the invocation of *maaseh istaba* does not fully dissolve.

## Chafina Parameters

### Another's Chafina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חָפַן חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנָתַן לְתוֹךְ חׇפְנָיו מַהוּ? ״מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו״ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא אִיכָּא! אוֹ דִילְמָא: ״וְלָקַח״ ״וְהֵבִיא״ בָּעֵינַן? וְהָא לֵיכָּא! תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of appropriate methods for taking handfuls of incense. **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* with regard to a case where **another** priest **scooped and placed** the incense **into the hands** of the High Priest? The Gemara clarifies the two sides of the question: Do **we require: "His full hands," and that is** fulfilled here, as in practice the High Priest has a handful of incense? **Or perhaps we require** that the High Priest must fulfill the mitzvot: **"And he shall take…and he shall bring"** (Leviticus 16:12), **and that is not** the case here, as the High Priest did not scoop and take the incense himself? This question was also left unanswered, and the Gemara concludes: **Let it stand** unresolved.

Rav Pappa poses a series of questions about the parameters of the *chafina*, turning now to the question of who must perform it. The verse states *ve-lakach melo ha-machta* or *u-melo chofnav*, specifying that the *ketoret* must fill his hands — but does it say that he personally must perform the act of scooping? Perhaps it suffices that his hands be full. This raises the question: *chafan chaveiro ve-nasan le-toch chofnav mahu* — what if one person performs the *chafina* and then pours the *ketoret* into the hands of the *Kohen Gadol*? On one hand, one might say: *melo chofnav ba'inan ve-ika* — we require that his hands be full, and they are full, and the act of *chafina* with hands was performed, even if not by his hands. On the other hand: *o dilma ve-lakach ve-heivi ba'inan* — perhaps the requirement of *ve-lakach*, "he shall take," applies to the *melo chofnav* as well, and the same person must perform both the taking and the bringing. Or at minimum, the taking must be done by the *Kohen Gadol* himself. The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.

### Kohen Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: חָפַן וָמֵת, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס אַחֵר בַּחֲפִינָתוֹ? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בֹּא וּרְאֵה שְׁאֵלַת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi raised a dilemma:** If the High Priest **scooped and died, what** is the *halakha* with regard to the possibility **that another** High Priest may replace him and **enter with his handful?** May the second priest enter the Holy of Holies with the incense that the first priest scooped, or must he start from the beginning of the process? **Rabbi Ḥanina said** to his students in excitement: **Come and see** that Sages from a later generation were able to ask a difficult question on par with **the question of the earlier** generations. Even I, Rabbi Ḥanina, asked this same question, which was posed by my elder, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asked a closely related question: *chafan u-met* — what if the *Kohen Gadol* performs the *chafina*, transfers the *ketoret* into the ladle, and then dies? *Mah she-yikanes acher be-chafinato* — can another *Kohen Gadol* enter and use the *chafina* performed by the first one? This is structurally analogous to the previous question — a different person continues the service — except that here both the first and second individuals are *Kohen Gadol*, and the question is specifically whether the same person must complete what he started.

### Chronology Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי קַשִּׁישׁ? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לִי הִתִּיר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא לִשְׁתּוֹת שַׁחֲלַיִים בְּשַׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this comment: **Is that to say that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** was **older** than Rabbi Ḥanina, which is why Rabbi Ḥanina referred to him as an early Sage? **But didn't Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say: Rabbi Ḥanina permitted me to drink cress juice on Shabbat** for medicinal purposes. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's deference to Rabbi Ḥanina shows that Rabbi Ḥanina was older than him.

At this juncture, before the *gemara* addresses the substance of the question, it pauses for a digression. Rabbi Chanina responded to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's question by saying: *bo u-re'ei she'elas ha-rishonim* — look at the question that the earlier ones asked. This phrasing implies that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was the younger of the two, asking about a question that the previous generation had already raised. But a contrary inference seems available: elsewhere, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said that it was Rabbi Chanina who permitted him to do a certain thing — the very fact that he sought permission from Rabbi Chanina implies that Rabbi Chanina was the senior authority. The *gemara* takes up this chronological puzzle before returning to the *halakhic* substance.

### Cress Juice Digress

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִשְׁתּוֹת, פְּשִׁיטָא? דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין אוֹכֵל אָדָם לִרְפוּאָה, וְכׇל הַמַּשְׁקִין שׁוֹתֶה!
>
> **Translation:** Since it has been raised, the Gemara addresses the issue of cress juice on Shabbat: Did Rabbi Ḥanina permit him **to drink** cress juice? It is **obvious** that it is permitted to drink this juice; why would it be prohibited? **As we learned** in a mishna: **All** types of **food** that healthy people eat **may be eaten by people** even **for medicinal** purposes, and one may likewise **drink all drinks** for medicinal purposes, as the Sages did not include these in their decree against taking medicine on Shabbat.

The specific permission Rabbi Chanina granted was: *hitir li lishtot shachlayim be-Shabbat* — Rabbi Chanina permitted me to drink cress juice on *Shabbat*. The *gemara* immediately objects: *pshita* — surely that is obvious. A *mishna* teaches that one may eat any food even for medicinal purposes, and one may drink any beverage for medicinal purposes, as long as it is in fact a food or a beverage rather than a medicine per se. If cress juice is a normal beverage, of course one may drink it. The *gemara* therefore clarifies: the question was not whether one may drink cress juice, but whether one may *grind* it — *lishchok* — and then drink it on *Shabbat*. The act of grinding raises a separate concern. The *gemara* then asks: under what circumstances? If there is genuine danger to life, of course it is permitted — *mishra shari*. If there is no danger, it is of course forbidden regardless. The *gemara* resolves: there was indeed danger. The question Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi posed to Rabbi Chanina was: does cress juice actually provide healing — *mi masya* — such that one could be *mechallel Shabbat* to prepare it? Or does it have no medical efficacy at all, in which case one cannot be *mechallel Shabbat* for its sake? And Rabbi Chanina permitted it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלָא: לִשְׁחוֹק וְלִשְׁתּוֹת שַׁחֲלַיִם בְּשַׁבָּת. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִיכָּא סַכַּנְתָּא — מִשְׁרָא שְׁרֵי. וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא סַכַּנְתָּא — מֵיסָר אֲסִיר! לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיכָּא סַכַּנְתָּא, וְהָכִי קָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: מִי מַסְּיָא, דְּנֵיחוּל עֲלַיְיהוּ שַׁבְּתָא, אוֹ לָא מַסְּיָא וְלָא נֵיחוּל עֲלַיְיהוּ שַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's** question was: Is it permitted **to grind and drink cress on Shabbat?** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** it involves a situation **where there is danger** to life, and this is the prescribed cure, **it is** certainly **permitted; and if** it is a case **where there is no danger,** it is **prohibited** as labor on Shabbat. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the question concerns a case **where there is** a life-threatening **danger, and this is the dilemma that he raised before him: Does** this drink **heal,** which would mean **that** it is appropriate **to violate Shabbat for it, or** does it **not heal, and** therefore one should **not violate Shabbat for it?**

The *gemara* then asks: why consult Rabbi Chanina specifically on this matter? The answer: *mishum de-baki be-refu'ot* — because Rabbi Chanina was knowledgeable in medicine. The evidence: Rabbi Chanina himself said that in all his years, no one ever came to ask him about a wound from a white mule who had survived — implying that he received many medical inquiries, and that wounds from white mules were universally fatal or permanently unhealing. The *gemara* challenges: but we do see people surviving such wounds. The response: *eima ve-chayat* — perhaps the intent is that the wound never fully healed, not that the person necessarily died. A further challenge: but we do see such wounds heal. The final response: *be-sumkan inu ve-chivan reish kara'aihu kami* — the particularly virulent cases refer to a mule that is reddish in body but has white legs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּבָקִי בִּרְפוּאוֹת הוּא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא שְׁאָלַנִי אָדָם עַל מַכַּת פִּרְדָּה לְבָנָה וְחָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if this was not a halakhic question but a medical one, **what is different** about this question that led him to ask it specifically of **Rabbi Ḥanina?** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asked Rabbi Ḥanina **because he is an expert in medicines, as Rabbi Ḥanina said: No man ever consulted me about a wound** inflicted by a **white mule and recovered.** This shows that people came to Rabbi Ḥanina for medical advice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּחַיֵּי! אֵימָא: וְחָיָית. וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּמִיתַּסִּי! בְּסוּמָּקָן אִינְהוּ וְחִיוָּרָן רֵישׁ כַּרְעַיְהוּ קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at this claim: **But we see that** people who are kicked by mules **do survive.** The Gemara answers that instead it should **say:** No man ever consulted me about a wound of this kind **and** the wound **survives,** i.e., the wound never heals. The Gemara challenges this statement as well: **But we see that** it **does heal.** The Gemara responds: **We say** that the wound will never heal only **when** the mules **are red and the tops of their legs are white.**

Having established that Rabbi Chanina was a physician, the *gemara* notes that the evidence for who was the elder sage is actually the opposite of what was initially assumed. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi went to Rabbi Chanina not because Rabbi Chanina was his superior in wisdom but because he needed accurate medical information. He called him *Rabbi* Chanina — using the honorific — but that establishes Rabbi Chanina's authority rather than his age relative to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. *Shma mina d'Rabbi Chanina kashish* — we see that Rabbi Chanina was the elder of the two. The meaning of *she'elas ha-rishonim*, then, must be reinterpreted: not that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was of the earlier generation, but rather that when Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asked his question, Rabbi Chanina said to him: "The question you are asking is the kind of question that the earlier generation already asked." It is a compliment to the depth of the question, not a chronological statement about the questioner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא קַשִּׁישׁ? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: שְׁאֵלָתָן כִּשְׁאֵילָה שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its previous question. **In any event,** one can **learn from this** discussion **that Rabbi Ḥanina** was **older** than Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. Why, then, does Rabbi Ḥanina refer to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi as a member of an earlier generation? **Rather,** we must explain that **this is** what **he said,** i.e., that Rabbi Ḥanina's statement should be understood as follows: **Their question is like the question of the early ones.** In other words, Rabbi Ḥanina meant that this question, posed by a member of a later generation, is as difficult as that of the early Sages.

### Back to Chafina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: ״בְּפַר״, וְלֹא בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Ḥanina** actually **say that** this question, of one High Priest using the incense scooped by another, was difficult to answer? **But didn't Rabbi Ḥanina say:** "With this Aaron shall come into the sacred place, **with a bull"** (Leviticus 16:3); this means that the High Priest must enter **with** the offering of **a bull and not with the blood of** the **bull?** In other words, the High Priest himself must slaughter his bull. Should a different priest slaughter the bull, receive its blood, and then die, the priest who replaces him may not enter the Holy of Holies with the blood of the bull slaughtered by his predecessor. Instead, he must bring a new bull, slaughter it, collect its blood, and take that blood inside.

Returning now to the substance of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's question — and the *gemara* anticipates an immediate difficulty. How could Rabbi Chanina have treated the question of whether another *Kohen Gadol* may use the *chafina* of the first as a legitimate *halakhic* question, when Rabbi Chanina himself held that *be-par ve-lo be-damo shel par* — the *Kohen Gadol* must begin his service with the live *par*, not with the blood of the *par*? If that is so, then as soon as the first *Kohen Gadol* dies, the next *Kohen Gadol* must go all the way back to the beginning — to the slaughter of the *par* itself. And since Rabbi Chanina also holds that *ketoret* done before the *shechita* of the *par* is invalid, the entire sequence would need to be repeated. The question of whether the second *Kohen Gadol* can use the first one's *chafina* would be moot: of course he must do everything over.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: קְטוֹרֶת שֶׁחֲפָנָהּ קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטַת הַפָּר לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם!
>
> **Translation:** And likewise, **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** If the priest **scooped the incense before the slaughtering of the bull, he did nothing,** as the handful of incense must be taken after the slaughter of the bull. If so, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Ḥanina, it is impossible for a priest to enter the Sanctuary with the handful taken by his fellow priest. The reason is that if the first priest died after the handful was taken, he certainly has not yet entered with the blood he collected from the bull. Consequently, his slaughter was not effective for the substitute priest, who must repeat the entire service from the slaughter onward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִדְּקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, הָא מִכְּלָל דְּקָסָבַר ״בְּפַר״, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר. וּלְמַאי דִּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ — שְׁאִילָתוֹ כִּשְׁאֵילַת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this is** what Rabbi Ḥanina **said: From** the fact that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **raised this dilemma,** it can be understood **by inference that he holds** that the verse **"with a bull"** means that the High Priest may enter the Holy of Holies **even with the blood of a bull.** This means that the second priest does not have to go back and slaughter a second bull. **And according to that which** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **maintains, his question is like the question of the earlier** generations. Although the question does not arise according to the opinion of Rabbi Ḥanina himself, according to the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, the question is indeed a difficult one.

The *gemara* therefore clarifies that Rabbi Chanina was not saying the question was legitimate from his own perspective. Rather, he was addressing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi on his own terms. The fact that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asked whether another *Kohen Gadol* can continue from the *chafina* reveals that he assumes *be-par va-afilu be-damo shel par* — that the next *Kohen Gadol* does not need to restart from the live animal, but can continue from the blood. Given that assumption, the question about the *chafina* is a genuine one: since you hold that the blood of the *par* suffices, you need not restart from the slaughter; but does the *chafina* of the deceased *Kohen Gadol* suffice for the one who succeeds him, or must he perform his own? *She'ilato ki-she'elat ha-rishonim* — your question, said Rabbi Chanina to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, is of the same type as those asked by the earlier generation.

The essential distinction between the two questions is this: the first question (Rav Pappa's) concerns whether any *chafina* performed by someone other than the *Kohen Gadol* can be used by him; the second (Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's) concedes that the *chafina* must be that of a *Kohen Gadol*, but asks whether it must be done by the *same* individual who completes the service. These are related but distinct questions.

## Chofen V'Chozer

### Rav Pappa's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי חוֹפֵן חוֹזֵר וְחוֹפֵן — חֲבֵירוֹ נִכְנָס בַּחֲפִינָתוֹ — דְּהָא מִקַּיְימָא חֲפִינָה. אִי אֵין חוֹפֵן וְחוֹזֵר וְחוֹפֵן — תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? What in fact is the ruling in a situation where the High Priest took a handful of incense and then died? May the newly appointed High Priest use the handful that has already been scooped, or does he require a new handful? **Rav Pappa said:** The resolution of this question depends on a different problem. **If** the High Priest **scoops** the handful when he takes the incense from the coal pan, and **again scoops** a handful in the Holy of Holies, this would mean that **another** priest may **enter with the handful** of the first High Priest, **as** the mitzva of **scooping** the handful has been **fulfilled.** However, **if** the High Priest does **not scoop and again scoop, let the dilemma be raised.**

Rav Pappa now addresses the question of whether the answer to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's question depends on whether *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* applies. The term *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* refers to whether the *Kohen Gadol*, upon entering the *kodesh kodashim*, must perform the *chafina* a second time — pouring the *ketoret* from the ladle back into his hands before releasing it onto the coals — or whether he may pour it directly from the ladle. Rav Pappa reasons as follows: if *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* applies — if one must indeed perform an additional *chafina* inside the *kodesh kodashim* — then another person can enter using the *chafina* of the first dead *Kohen Gadol*, because the critical *chafina* will in any case be performed again, this time by the one who actually completes the service. The act of *chafina* will be fulfilled, and it will be his own. But if there is no *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* — if the *ketoret* is poured straight from the ladle — then the question of whether another person may complete the service using the original *chafina* is a live and unresolved one.

### Rav Huna Reply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִי חוֹפֵן וְחוֹזֵר וְחוֹפֵן, לֹא יִכָּנֵס אַחֵר בַּחֲפִינָתוֹ — אִי אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁלֹּא יְחַסֵּר וְשֶׁלֹּא יוֹתִיר. וְאִי אֵין חוֹפֵן חוֹזֵר וְחוֹפֵן — תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa: On the contrary, if** the High Priest **scoops and again scoops, another** priest should **not** be permitted to **enter with his handful,** as **it is impossible** that the new handful **will be neither less nor more** than the amount of the first handful, which means the handful will not have been taken properly. **But** conversely, **if he does not scoop and again scoop, let the dilemma be raised,** as the mitzva of taking a handful has already been fulfilled by the first priest, and the second priest has merely to place the incense on the coals.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua responded to Rav Pappa with the opposite reasoning. If *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* applies — if there is a second *chafina* inside the *kodesh kodashim* — then in fact another *Kohen Gadol* cannot use the original *chafina*, because: *ee efshar she-lo yechsar ve-she-lo yotir* — it is impossible that the second *chafina* will be exactly the same size as the first. The measure of *melo chofnav* taken on the outside and the measure of *melo chofnav* performed inside the *kodesh kodashim* will inevitably differ, because different hands have different sizes, and because the physical act of re-scooping is not identical to the original act. If both *chafina* acts are part of the *avoda* and both must match, continuity between two different people cannot be maintained. But if there is no *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* — if the *ketoret* is poured straight from the ladle — then the question is genuinely open: the second *Kohen Gadol* is using the first's *melo chofnav* without performing his own, and the question is whether that is permissible.

### Interior Chafina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חוֹפֵן חוֹזֵר וְחוֹפֵן, אוֹ לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע: כָּךְ הָיְתָה מִידָּתָהּ. מַאי לָאו: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּדָּתָהּ מִבַּחוּץ כָּךְ מִדָּתָהּ מִבִּפְנִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the background to this problem. **As a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does the High Priest **scoop** a handful from the incense once **and again scoop a handful** a second time in the Holy of Holies, **or** does he **not** scoop a second time? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from the mishna: **This was the measure** of the spoon. **What, is it not** correct to infer from the mishna that **just as its measure** is **on the outside, so is its measure on the inside,** i.e., there is no need to scoop another handful, as this is its fixed measure that he pours onto the coal pan.

The *gemara* moves to resolve the question of *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* by citing a *baraita* that describes in vivid detail what the *Kohen Gadol* does when inside the *kodesh kodashim*. The *baraita* states: *ocheiz et ha-bazikh* — he takes the ladle (referred to here as *bazikh* rather than *kaf*, though the vessels are similar) — *be-rosh etzbe'otav* — at the tips of his fingers — *ve-yeish omrim be-shinav* — some say using his teeth — *u-ma'aleh be-godlo* — and he lifts it with his thumbs — *ad she-magia le-vein atzilei yadav* — until it reaches between his elbows. What this describes is a complex physical maneuver by which the long-handled ladle is repositioned so that the bowl of the ladle, held beneath the arm and pressed against the body, can be tilted or maneuvered such that the *ketoret* is transferred back into both hands. The use of one's teeth is mentioned as one method — which is somewhat remarkable given that the *gemara* elsewhere indicates that using one's teeth is inappropriate, though *Tosafot* acknowledge that in situations where there is no alternative, it may be unavoidable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דִּילְמָא שֶׁאִם רָצָה לַעֲשׂוֹת מִדָּה — עוֹשֶׂה, אִי נָמֵי: שֶׁלֹּא יְחַסֵּר וְשֶׁלֹּא יוֹתִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** that is not necessarily the correct interpretation of the mishna. **Perhaps** it means **that if he wants to measure** a precise **amount** for his handful, **he may measure** with a utensil for this purpose. **Alternatively,** it could mean **that** he may take **neither less nor more** than the measure he initially took. Consequently, there is no proof from the mishna with regard to whether the High Priest must scoop a second handful.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a *baraita*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? אוֹחֵז אֶת הַבָּזֵךְ בְּרֹאשׁ אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים בְּשִׁינָּיו, וּמַעֲלֶה בְּגוּדָלוֹ עַד שֶׁמַּגַּעַת לְבֵין אַצִּילֵי יָדָיו, וְחוֹזֵר וּמַחְזִירָהּ לְתוֹךְ חׇפְנָיו, וְצוֹבְרָהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא עֲשָׁנָהּ שׁוֹהֶה לָבוֹא. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מְפַזְּרָהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא עֲשָׁנָהּ מְמַהֶרֶת לָבוֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **How should** the High Priest **act** in the Holy of Holies, when he needs to place the incense on the coals by taking a handful from the spoon and placing it in his hands? After he places the coal pan on the ground, **he holds the** front of **the ladle,** i.e., the spoon of incense, **with his fingertips, and some say** he holds it **with his teeth.** At this stage the handle of the spoon rests between his arms. **And** he pushes it and **raises** it **up** slowly **with his thumb** toward his body **until it reaches between his elbows,** which he then uses to turn it over. **He then returns** the incense **into his palms,** after which he pours it from his hands into the coal pan. **And he heaps** the incense into a pile on the coals **so that its smoke rises slowly. And some say** he does the opposite, that **he scatters it so that its smoke rises quickly.**

### Incense Placement

Two opinions are recorded about how the *ketoret* is then placed upon the coals. The first opinion: *ve-tzavra* — he heaps it, placing the entire quantity in a mound — *ke-dei she-ashana shoha la-vo* — so that the smoke will rise slowly, giving the *Kohen Gadol* time to complete the act and exit before the cloud of smoke envelops him. The second opinion: *mifazra* — he scatters it across the coals — *ke-dei she-ashana memaheret la-vo* — so that the smoke rises quickly.

This debate is not merely technical. The *Kohen Gadol* is in the innermost sanctum, at the most intimate moment of the entire *Yom Kippur* service — an encounter of extraordinary weight and singularity. Does one set the incense in a mound, creating a delay before the smoke ascends, so as to withdraw before the encounter fully occurs — in effect, arranging the encounter but not remaining present for it? Or does one scatter it, causing the smoke to rise immediately, so as to be fully present at the moment when the cloud rises and the encounter is most immediate? This is a question of profound symbolic resonance, evoking the broader debate about whether one approaches the divine encounter or withdraws from it at the decisive moment.

### Avodah Hardship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ הִיא עֲבוֹדָה קָשָׁה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. זוֹ הִיא וְתוּ לָא? וְהָא אִיכָּא מְלִיקָה! וְהָא אִיכָּא קְמִיצָה! אֶלָּא: זוֹ הִיא עֲבוֹדָה קָשָׁה מֵעֲבוֹדוֹת קָשׁוֹת שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חוֹפֵן וְחוֹזֵר וְחוֹפֵן. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** taking of a handful of incense **is** the most **difficult** sacrificial **rite in the Temple.** The Gemara asks: **This one** is the hardest rite, **and no other? But there is pinching,** which is also considered extremely difficult; **and there is taking a handful** of a meal-offering, another complex rite. **Rather, this** taking of a handful of incense **is** one **of** the most **difficult rites in the Temple,** rather than the single most difficult one. In any event, you can **learn from this** that the High Priest **scoops** a handful **and again scoops.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that it is so.

After describing this intricate procedure, the *baraita* declares: *ve-zu hi avoda kasha she-be-Mikdash* — this is the most difficult *avoda* in the Temple. The *gemara* immediately responds: *zu hi ve-tu lo?* — is this really the hardest, and nothing else compares? Is there not also *melika* — the slicing of a bird's neck from behind with the fingernail — and *kemitza* — the particularly difficult smoothing of the *mincha* dough in the hand? The *gemara* therefore amends: *ela zu hi avoda kasha mi-me'ah avodot she-be-Mikdash* — rather, this is one of the hardest among the many *avodot* in the Temple.

What the *baraita* makes unmistakably clear, in any event, is that the procedure involves transferring the *ketoret* from the ladle back into the hands — the *chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* is not optional but required. The *Kohen Gadol* does not simply pour from the ladle directly onto the coals; he performs a second *chafina* inside the *kodesh kodashim* and releases the *ketoret* from his hands. The *gemara* concludes: *shma mina chofen ve-chozer ve-chofen* — we learn from this that indeed a second *chafina* is required inside the *kodesh kodashim*, and the incense must be released from the hands. *Shma mina* — this is established.

The remaining unresolved question is whether another person may use the *chafina* of one who has died — that is, whether the second *Kohen Gadol* who enters may rely on the *chafina* performed by the first. Given that he will in any case perform his own *chafina* inside the *kodesh kodashim*, as the *baraita* establishes, the question of whether he is the one who did the initial *chafina* on the outside may be secondary. But as Rav Huna's argument makes clear, if the two *chafina* acts must correspond in measure — and it is physically impossible that they will — then even this route is problematic.

## Shechita Succession (II)

### Live Animal Start

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שָׁחַט וָמֵת, מַה הוּא שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס אַחֵר בְּדָמוֹ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: ״בְּפַר״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר, אוֹ דִילְמָא: ״בְּפַר״ — וְלֹא בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a High Priest **slaughtered** the bull **and died, what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **another** High Priest may **enter** the Holy of Holies **with the blood** of the bull that his predecessor slaughtered? **Do we say** that the verse: "With this Aaron shall come into the sacred place, **with a bull"** (Leviticus 16:3) teaches that the priest must enter with the blood of a bull, but it need not necessarily be the blood of the bull he himself slaughtered, **and** in that case he may enter **even with the blood of a bull** slaughtered by someone else? **Or perhaps** the verse should be interpreted precisely: **"With a bull," and not with the blood of a bull** slaughtered by another?

The *gemara* turns to the parallel question regarding the blood service: *shachat u-meis* — what if the *Kohen Gadol* slaughtered the *par* and then died? Can another *Kohen Gadol* take over from the point of the blood, or must he return to the beginning and slaughter again?

### Two Positions

The fundamental question is formulated as: *mi amrinan be-par va-afilu be-damo shel par* — do we say the verse's reference to "the cow" (*be-par*) encompasses even the blood of the cow, meaning the blood serves as a valid stand-in for the animal itself? Or: *be-par ve-lo be-damo shel par* — "with the cow" means specifically the cow, and entry into the service must begin with the living animal, not merely its blood?

### Four-Way Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אוֹמֵר: ״בְּפַר״ — וְלֹא בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: ״בְּפַר״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר. רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: ״בְּפַר״ — וְלֹא בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר, רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: ״בְּפַר״ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages disputed this matter. **Rabbi Ḥanina says: "With a bull," and not with the blood of a bull,** which means that the newly appointed High Priest must slaughter another bull, as he can enter only with the blood of a bull he himself slaughtered. **And Reish Lakish said: "With a bull," and even with the blood of a bull.** Likewise, **Rabbi Ami said: "With a bull" and not with the blood of a bull; Rabbi Yitzḥak said: "With a bull," and even with the blood of a bull.**

Four *amoraim* weigh in. Rabbi Chanina holds *be-par ve-lo be-damo shel par* — one must begin with the live cow. Reish Lakish holds *be-par va-afilu be-damo shel par* — even the blood suffices as a starting point. Rabbi Ami holds *be-par ve-lo be-damo shel par*, in agreement with Rabbi Chanina. Rabbi Yitzchak holds *be-par va-afilu be-damo shel par*, in agreement with Reish Lakish.

### Blood Represents Life

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא: נִמְנִין וּמוֹשְׁכִין יְדֵיהֶן מִמֶּנּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּשָּׁחֵט, וְאִם אִיתָא — ״עַד שֶׁיִּזְרוֹק״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami raised an objection to Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa,** the smith: To join a group of people who arranged to partake together of a single Paschal offering, individuals may **register** as part of a group **and they** may **withdraw from it** and join another group **until** the offering **is slaughtered. And if it is so,** that the blood of an animal is considered part of the offering, this *tanna* who authored this statement **should have said** that they may withdraw **until** the blood **is sprinkled.** If, as you maintain, the blood of an offering is part of the offering itself, why can't a person register to or withdraw from a group of a Paschal offering until its blood is sprinkled?

Rabbi Ami then challenges Rabbi Yitzchak. If the blood of the *par* truly represents the animal itself — such that beginning with the blood is equivalent to beginning with the live cow — then by that same logic, one should be able to join a *Korban Pesach* group even after the *shechita*, as long as the blood has not yet been thrown. The *halakha* is that participants may join or withdraw from a *pesach* group only until the *shechita*; once the animal is slaughtered, the group is fixed. But if the blood represents the living animal, why should the *shechita* be the cutoff? The blood is still present and still represents the *korban*. The challenge is direct: *ve-i isa, ad she-yizarek mibei lei* — if what you say is correct, the rule should be that one may join until the blood is thrown, not until the *shechita*.

### Pesach Joining Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִהְיוֹת מִשֶּׂה״ — מֵחַיּוּתֵהּ דְּשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** He answered him: **It is different there, as it is written:** "And if the household is too small **for a lamb [*mehiyot miseh*],** he and his neighbor who is next to his house" (Exodus 12:4). The phrase "*mehiyot miseh*" is read as ***meiḥiyutei deseh*,** from the life of a lamb. In other words, one can withdraw from a group only as long as the lamb is alive. If so, its blood is not considered part of the Paschal lamb by a special decree of the Torah, which does not apply to Yom Kippur.

The *gemara* responds: *shani hasam, de-ksiv min ha-seh* — there it is different, because the verse writes *min ha-seh*, "from the sheep." The verse that speaks of reducing or enlarging the group of participants uses the term *seh* — from the being of the *seh*. This generates the *drasha*: *mi-chayusa de-seh* — from the life of the *seh*, the sheep being alive. The ability to add or remove participants is linked specifically to the period when the animal is alive, not to the period when the blood is present. The *pesach* rule, then, is an exception derived from its own verse, and does not refute the broader principle that blood represents the animal in other contexts.

## Blood Represents Ani (II)

### Seh-Seh Linkage

The resolution, then, is that the *pesach* rule is established through a *gezera shava* — a linkage between *seh* in the context of the *pesach* and *seh* in the context of reducing the group. Since the word *seh* recurs in both contexts, the *halakha* of joining the group is tied to the life of the *seh* specifically. This makes the *pesach* the exception rather than the disproof: the restriction to adding participants only while the animal is alive is a unique feature of *pesach* derived from its own verse, and it does not undermine the general principle that blood represents the living animal in other *avodot*.

## Shechita Succession (II)

### Peter Chamor Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב מָר זוּטְרָא: וְאֵין פּוֹדִין — לֹא בָּעֵגֶל וְלֹא בְּחַיָּה וְלֹא בִּשְׁחוּטָה וְלֹא בִּטְרֵיפָה וְלֹא בְּכִלְאַיִם וְלֹא בְּכוֹי, אֶלָּא בְּשֶׂה. שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּיָלֵיף ״שֶׂה״ ״שֶׂה״ מִפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra raised an objection: And one may not redeem** a male firstborn donkey **with a calf, nor with an undomesticated animal, nor with a slaughtered** lamb, **nor with an animal with a condition that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], nor with** the product of the prohibited **crossbreeding** of a lamb and a goat, **nor with a *koy*,** a kosher animal with characteristics of both a domesticated animal and a non-domesticated animal, **but with a lamb.** This proves that a slaughtered animal is not considered a lamb. The Gemara rejects this claim: **It is different there, as** that *tanna* **derives** a verbal analogy of **"lamb"** (Exodus 13:13) and **"lamb"** (Exodus 12:4) **from the Paschal offering:** Just as a slaughtered lamb cannot be used for a Paschal offering, the same applies to the case of a firstborn donkey.

Mar Zutra extends the discussion in a striking direction. The *mishna* states that a *peter chamor* — the firstborn of a donkey — cannot be redeemed with a calf, nor with a non-domesticated animal, nor with a slaughtered sheep, nor with a *treifa*, nor with a crossbreed, nor with a *koy*, but only with a *seh*. The question Mar Zutra poses is: why can it not be redeemed with a slaughtered sheep? If the blood of the sheep still represents the animal — if the blood of a slaughtered animal is halakhically equivalent to the living animal — then why should the dead sheep not serve for *pidyon peter chamor*?

The comparison might seem strained: in the case of the *par* on *Yom Kippur*, the blood is being brought in service of a *korban*, representing the life offered on the altar, and there is a compelling reason to say the blood stands in for the animal. But in the case of *pidyon peter chamor*, there is no *korban* being offered, and one might think the blood simply has no representational function at all. Nevertheless, the *gemara* does not immediately dismiss the comparison on those grounds.

## Blood Represents Ani (II)

### When Blood Represents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנִי הָתָם, דְּיָלִיף ״שֶׂה״ ״שֶׂה״ מִפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It is different there, as** the requirement of *seh* for *pidyon peter chamor* is derived by *gezera shava* from *pesach*, and just as by *pesach* the *seh* must be alive, so too by *pidyon peter chamor* the *seh* must be alive. The *seh-seh* linkage thus has double duty: it both establishes the restriction for the *pesach* joining rule and extends that requirement to *pidyon peter chamor*.

The *gemara* responds to Mar Zutra's challenge by invoking the same resolution used previously: *shani hasam, de-yalif seh seh mi-Pesach* — there it is different, because the requirement of *seh* for *pidyon peter chamor* is derived by *gezera shava* from *pesach*, and just as by *pesach* the *seh* must be alive, so too by *pidyon peter chamor* the *seh* must be alive. The *seh-seh* linkage thus has double duty: it both establishes the restriction for the *pesach* joining rule and extends that requirement to *pidyon peter chamor*.

What is particularly striking about this final discussion is its scope. The debate over whether the blood of the *par* represents the living animal — which one might expect to be a narrow question about the Yom Kippur *avoda* — is being applied as a *global* principle across the entire spectrum of *halakha*. The *gemara* entertains the possibility that if the blood represents the living animal for purposes of the *par* service, it might represent the living animal for the *pesach* joining rule, and even for the *pidyon* of a *peter chamor*. The *gezera shava* from *seh* to *seh* is what limits this expansion, but the very fact that the *gemara* considers the expansion at all reveals how fundamental and far-reaching the underlying conceptual question is. The discussion of whether a second *Kohen Gadol* can continue the service where the first left off is not merely a practical question about Yom Kippur procedure; it touches on the deepest question of what blood represents in the sacrificial system. This question will continue into the following *daf*.
