# Hullin 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Rov

### Chazakah Review

Daf 11 opens by moving from the principle of chazakah, which occupied the preceding days, to an equally fundamental principle: rov, going by the majority. The discussion of chazakah established that status quo creates a high threshold to prove that things have changed — you assume an object or situation retains its prior status until you can prove otherwise. Two dimensions of chazakah were examined: first, the basic rule that one must demonstrate that the knife used for shechitah was valid, that no chatzitzah was present, and so forth; and second, the question of itra chazakah — what happens when the chazakah is disrupted? When a nick is found in the knife, when a bad letter appears in a Sefer Torah, do you use the status quo to determine that the change occurred at the latest possible moment or not? The Gemara derived the principle of chazakah from the laws of tzara'at, where one assumes the size of a spot has remained constant.

The new topic, rov, is in many ways the more intuitive principle. It directs us to go by the likelihood of something having occurred, using that likelihood to guide behavior when the facts are not known with certainty. Both chazakah and rov are tools for navigating doubt — acknowledging that while we would always prefer to know the facts, life often demands that we act without certainty, and the halachic system provides principled direction for doing so.

### Mina Ha-Milta

At the top of 11a, the Gemara asks: mina ha-milta — where is this well-established principle derived from? The question is not whether rov is rabbinic in origin, since the Gemara will immediately derive it from psukim and apply it to biblical prohibitions; rather, it asks where in the Torah this broadly accepted principle — zil batar ruba, go by the majority — is grounded. The Gemara answers: dechtiv acharei rabim lehatot, the verse in Shemot that instructs us to incline after the majority.

### Acharei Rabim Limit

The Gemara immediately qualifies this answer. The verse acharei rabim lehatot provides a clear source only for ruba de-ita kaman — a majority that is present before us, such as tesha chanuyot, the case of nine stores, or the vote of a Sanhedrin. In those cases, the derivation is unproblematic. The real question — ki kamiba'ya lan — is whether we can rely on rov in the case of ruba de-leisa kaman, an abstract or statistical majority not physically present before us, such as the case of katan ve-ketanah in the context of yibbum. From where do we know that principle?

## Ita vs. Leisa Kaman

### Nine Stores Example

The distinction between ruba de-ita kaman and ruba de-leisa kaman is worth spelling out carefully. Ruba de-ita kaman means that the majority is actually present and identifiable before you. Consider a case of three pots of meat, two of which are kosher and one of which is neveilah. If one accidentally ladles from one of them and cannot recall which, the question of what to assume is grounded in a concrete, present set: two out of three are fine. One can attribute one's action to the majority because the items that constitute that majority are right there.

The classic Talmudic formulation of this is tesha chanuyot — nine kosher butcher stores and one non-kosher store. A piece of wrapped meat is found on the street, evidently dropped from one of the stores. Since nine out of ten are kosher, one may assume the meat is kosher. This is not merely an abstract 90% probability; it is a judgment made against a known, present set of possibilities. One is attributing the item to the majority of a set that is actually in front of one — that is ruba de-ita kaman.

### Leisa Kaman & Yibbum

Ruba de-leisa kaman, by contrast, involves general statistical probabilities not anchored in any present, identifiable set. The Gemara's example is the case of yibbum involving a katan or ketanah. When a man dies without children, the mitzvah of yibbum or chalitzah applies — but only if there is the possibility of children, since the entire purpose of yibbum is le-hakim shem achiv ha-met, to perpetuate the brother's name. If either party is sterile, the mitzvah does not apply, and their union would instead be a biblical prohibition.

If one of the parties is a minor, there is no way to determine whether they will grow up to be fertile or sterile. The question is whether one may proceed with yibbum based on the likelihood that the minor will turn out to be fertile. This is not a question about which item from a known set a given object belongs to; it is a pure question of general population probabilities. What percentage of minors grow up to be sterile? Presumably very few — perhaps one in a hundred or one in a thousand — but there is no present, identifiable set from which one is drawing this inference. That is ruba de-leisa kaman.

### Formal vs. Probability

One might ask why this distinction should matter at all. In both cases the question ultimately reduces to likelihood: what is more probable? The Gemara's insistence on the distinction, however, points toward something deeper. As was seen in the discussion of chazakah, halacha does not operate purely as a probability-calculating machine. Chazakah is a formal rule — assume status quo until you know otherwise — and does not claim to tell you what the actual likelihood is that something has changed. Similarly, the Gemara's treatment of rov suggests that it is not simply about percentages.

When dealing with ruba de-ita kaman, one can do something more than calculate probabilities: one can associate the item in question with one of two present, distinguishable groups — the majority or the minority. There is a formal act of attribution occurring. With ruba de-leisa kaman, all one has are abstract percentages, and the question is whether halacha permits acting on the basis of pure probability alone. The Gemara's framing implies that this is the harder case, and the rest of the passage is devoted to finding a source that proves ruba de-leisa kaman is also operative.

### Sanhedrin & Birur

The Gemara includes Sanhedrin voting within the category of ruba de-ita kaman, and this is a striking inclusion. When a court takes a vote and twelve judges say one thing and eleven say the other, the majority determines the ruling — this is the halacha of acharei rabim lehatot in its most direct application. But it is immediately apparent that this is categorically different from inferring where a piece of meat came from. When one says the majority of stores were kosher, there is at least a sense in which one is making a claim about the real-world origin of the meat. When a Sanhedrin votes, however, no one is claiming that the majority vote tells us anything about the actual facts of the case. The majority vote is a procedural mechanism for reaching a decision, not a truth-tracking device.

The Gemara's inclusion of Sanhedrin voting in the same discussion as the case of the nine stores is therefore highly revealing. It signals that the principle of rov is not solely about resolving factual uncertainty by identifying the most probable truth. Sometimes it is about providing rules for how one lives and acts within a space of unresolved doubt. This is the distinction the contemporary yeshiva world frames as birur versus hanhaga: is rov a clarification of the facts, or is it a guideline for action even when the facts remain unresolved? Chazakah is clearly a hanhaga — it does not tell you the facts, it tells you how to proceed. The question is whether rov functions similarly, or whether it actually purports to establish the facts.

The inclusion of Sanhedrin voting suggests that rov has at least a hanhaga dimension. When the court does not know the truth, the system provides a rule: go by the majority vote. By extension, when the source of a piece of meat is unknown, the system provides a parallel rule: associate it with the majority. In both cases, the underlying doubt may not be resolved, but there is a principled framework for action.

### Birur vs. Hanhaga

This leads to a nuanced theoretical point. One might argue that birur does not require absolute certainty, only that a sufficient evidentiary threshold has been crossed. On this reading, when one says the piece of meat probably came from the kosher stores, one is not claiming certainty — one is claiming that the threshold for treating the matter as established has been met. Whether one frames this as "we have established the facts sufficiently" or "we have a rule for acting in the absence of established facts" may turn on precisely how one defines the concept of birur and what level of certainty it requires.

The connection to ruba de-ita kaman is particularly suggestive here. The reason ruba de-ita kaman is conceptually more tractable is that it allows one to replicate, in a sense, the structure of the Sanhedrin vote — there are nine stores versus one store, and one can treat that as a formal majority determination the same way a court vote is a formal majority determination. This provides a procedural basis for action that does not depend solely on raw probability. Ruba de-leisa kaman, dealing only in abstract percentages, raises the harder question of whether pure probability is sufficient to ground a halachic determination.

## Proofs from Korbanos

### Olah Brain Proof

Having established the question, the Gemara proceeds to search for a source for ruba de-leisa kaman. Rabbi Elazar says: atya me-reisha shel olah — one learns it from the head of a korban olah. The Torah states venisach otah lintacheha — the olah is cut into its parts and burned on the mizbe'ach. The Gemara derives from the phrase otah lintacheha velo netacheha lintachim that while the animal is divided into its constituent parts, one does not cut those parts into sub-parts. The head is thus offered whole; it is one of the sections placed on the mizbe'ach without further subdivision.

The problem is that one of the known treifot is a hole in the krum shemoach, the membrane surrounding the brain. If the head is offered whole and one is not permitted to break it open, how can one ever verify that the animal being brought as a korban olah is not a treifah on account of a perforated brain membrane? The only explanation is that one relies on rov — the majority of animals do not have perforated brain membranes — and that this rov must function even though the items are not present before us in a formal ruba de-ita kaman sense. This would constitute proof for ruba de-leisa kaman.

### Olah Refuted

The Gemara immediately challenges this proof. Mimai — how do you know the brain is not actually inspected? Perhaps one does crack open the skull to examine the membrane — dilma de-palyei u-vadkei. And if the concern is the rule that the parts may not be cut into sub-parts, hani milei heicha de-chatchei legamrei — that would only apply if one were to fully separate the skull into two distinct pieces. Aval heicha de-liyeif, if one merely cracks it open while keeping the two sides connected at the bottom, leis lan bah, that may not violate the prohibition. The proof from the olah is thus rejected: perhaps the brain is routinely inspected before the head is placed on the mizbe'ach, and no reliance on rov is necessary.

### Pesach Bone Proof

Mar breih d'Ravina tries another proof: atya mishvirat etzem d'be-Pesach, from the prohibition against breaking bones of the korban Pesach. The Torah states ve-etzem lo tishberu bo — not a single bone of the Pesach offering may be broken. Since the skull is a bone, one can never crack it open to inspect the brain membrane. Yet the korban Pesach must be valid and not a treifah, and the animal is brought and consumed. The only explanation is that one relies on rov — again, ruba de-leisa kaman.

### Coal Refutation

The Gemara refutes this as well. Dilma de-manach gumra alei ve-kalyu lei u-vadik lei — perhaps one places a hot coal on the skull, allows the heat to burn through it, and then inspects the brain through the resulting opening. This would not constitute active bone-breaking, and indirect burning through a coal is not shvirat etzem. A Baraita is cited in support: hamichatech begidin vehasoref be-atzamot ein bo mishum shevirat etzem — one who cuts through sinews or burns through bones does not transgress the prohibition of breaking a bone. Thus the korban Pesach case also fails to prove that rov must be operative, since an alternative method of inspection is available.

### Alyah Spine Proof

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a proof from the alyah — the fat tail of the sheep that is burned as part of the korban. The Torah says chelbo ha-alyah temimah — the entire fat tail must be offered. The fat tail is removed from a point along the spine — specifically, as the Gemara explains, le-umat ha-atzeh, opposite the atzeh, meaning at the level of the kidneys, since makom she-haklayot yo-atzot, the kidneys are the seat of counsel and wisdom, so the fat tail is severed at the point where it connects to the kidneys. This means the fat tail that is offered includes a section of the spine within it.

One of the treifot is nifzak chut hashidra — a severed spinal cord. If the fat tail must be offered whole — temimah — how can one inspect the portion of the spine concealed within it? One has no way of checking for this treifah. The inevitable conclusion is that one relies on rov, on the majority of animals not having severed spinal cords. This would once again be ruba de-leisa kaman.

### Alyah Refuted

The Gemara raises two possible rebuttals. First, demitata pasik lei — perhaps one cuts the fat tail off below the point where the spine runs through it, so that the offered portion of the fat tail contains no spine at all. The Gemara dismisses this: the verse says le-umat ha-atzeh, at the level of the kidneys, which situates the cut at a point where the spine is present. Second, even granting that the spine is included, dilma de-fatach lei u-vadak lei — perhaps one does cut open the fat tail and inspect the spine, and ve-i mishum temimah, if the concern is the requirement that it be whole, heicha de-chatcheh le-gamrei, that would only apply if one cut it into two entirely separate pieces. Aval heicha de-laf, if one opens it while keeping it connected, leis lan bah, perhaps that does not violate temimah. The proof from the alyah is therefore also rejected.

### Eglah Arufah Proof

Rav Sheshet breih de-Rav Idi says: atya me-eglah arufah, one learns it from the calf whose neck is broken in the wadi when a murder victim is found and the killer is unknown. The verse describes the eglah arufah using the term ha-arufah, from which the Gemara derives keshei sheleimah tehei — the calf must remain whole. The ritual requires breaking its neck and leaving it untouched thereafter; it cannot be cut open or inspected.

Before proceeding, the Gemara establishes why the eglah arufah cannot be a treifah. Kaparah ketiv bah — the verse employs the language of kappara, atonement: kaper le-amcha Yisrael. The house of Rabbi Yannai teaches that since it is associated with kappara like kodashim, it is ke-kodashim, and just as sacrifices cannot be treifot, the eglah arufah cannot be a treifah. Yet if it must remain whole — sheleimah — there is no way to inspect its internal organs. How can one ever perform this ritual without concern that the animal was a treifah? The answer must be that one relies on rov.

Tosafot notes that one might ask why the Gemara does not raise the same rebuttal here as it did with the olah — namely, that one can crack it open without fully severing it. The Gemara apparently understands that the term sheleimah, as distinct from temimah used in the alyah case, means genuinely whole and untouched. Moreover, unlike the olah and the alyah, where the animal is actively cut up as part of the offering process, the eglah arufah is killed and then simply left — there is no further handling. This stronger emphasis on complete non-interference makes the proof more compelling, and the Gemara seems to accept it as a meaningful advance in the argument.

### Parah Adumah Proof

Rabba bar Shila brings a parallel proof from the parah adumah, the red heifer. The Torah states ve-shachat ve-saraf — it is slaughtered and then burned. The drasha is: mah shechitatah ke-shehee shleimah, just as at the moment of slaughter it is whole, af srefiatah ke-shehee shleimah, so too at the moment of burning it is whole. The same term shleimah appears here as in the eglah arufah, and the emphasis is similarly on the animal remaining completely untouched and unhandled other than the act of killing. No cutting, no internal inspection is possible.

The question then arises: how can one ever perform the parah adumah ritual without concern that the animal was a treifah? And who says a parah adumah cannot be a treifah? Chatat karyah rachmana — the Torah calls it a chatat, a sin offering. There are extensive sugyot in Shas debating the precise status of the parah adumah — it is performed outside the Beis Hamikdash, yet the blood is sprinkled toward it, and in many respects it is treated like a korban. But for present purposes, the Gemara grants that since it is called a chatat, it has sufficient korban-like status that it cannot be a treifah. Yet since it must be whole and untouched, there is no way to inspect it, and one must rely on rov — ruba de-leisa kaman.

## Rov in Capital Law

### Paternity & Rov

Rav Mari shifts the discussion away from korbanos and korban-adjacent laws entirely and brings a proof from capital law: atya mi-makeh aviv va-imo. The Torah mandates the death penalty for one who strikes his father or mother, understood as drawing blood. The immediate problem is: how do we know who the father is? V-leichosh dilma lav aviv hu — perhaps the man assumed to be the father is not actually the biological father. Are we prepared to execute someone for striking his presumed father when paternity cannot be established with certainty?

The Gemara answers: ela lav mishum de-amrinan zil batar ruba — we go by majority. V-rov beilos achar ha-ba'al — the majority of sexual acts that a wife engages in are presumed to be with her husband, and therefore the child is attributed to the husband. On this basis, the presumed father is treated as the legal father for purposes of this capital offense.

### Rov vs. Chazakah Nature

This proof raises a fundamental question. The Gemara's initial rebuttal is: dilma kegon she-haya aviv ve-imo chavushin be-veis ha-asurin — perhaps the Torah only permits prosecution in a case where the parents were held together in jail, such that the wife had no opportunity to commit adultery and paternity was certain? The Gemara dismisses this: afilu hachi, ein apitropus la-arayos — even in that case, there is no absolute guarantee, since when the sexual drive is involved, normal assumptions about behavior cannot be made with certainty. The Gemara invokes this as a general principle: one can never completely rule out the possibility of adultery based on circumstantial controls alone.

One might press further and ask whether rov beilos achar ha-ba'al is genuinely a rov at all. When the Gemara speaks of the likelihood that an animal is not a treifah, there is at least a theoretical empirical basis — one can observe many animals, determine what percentage have internal defects, and derive a percentage. But in the case of paternity, there is no analogous population study. The claim that most of a wife's sexual acts are with her husband is not derived from any statistical survey; it is a presumption about human behavior and social norms.

This is indeed better described as a chazakah — but a different kind of chazakah than the status quo chazakah discussed in earlier sugyot. The Gemara elsewhere uses the phrase chazakah ein adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo — there is a presumption that a person will not be so brazen as to explicitly deny a debt he owes. Similarly, rov beilos achar ha-ba'al is essentially a presumption of human nature: we presume that a married woman's sexual life is primarily with her husband, not because we have counted, but because that is the operative social and moral assumption. The Gemara itself uses the language of rov here, but as noted, this is the kind of rov that is grounded in and functions more like a chazakah of human nature. It is telling that this case is being included in a discussion of rov, since it blurs the line between the two categories.

### Murder Victim Treifah

Rav Kahana says: atya mi-horeg et ha-nefesh — one learns it from the execution of a murderer. The Torah mandates that a murderer be put to death. But v-leichosh dilma treifah hava — perhaps the person who was killed was a treifah, in which case the killer cannot be executed.

This requires some elaboration. Unlike the case of a terminally ill patient, where murder remains retzichah regardless of prognosis, the halacha treats the case of a treifah differently. If a person has an internal injury that renders him a treifah — meaning that due to this specific injury he will die — then killing such a person is not considered full murder in the halachic sense, and the killer cannot be executed. This principle applies bein biydei adam bein biydei shamayim, whether the injury was caused by human action or by natural causes. The question of how one acquires the status of a treifah in halachic terms is discussed extensively in the relevant sugyot.

The problem for our Gemara, then, is that one can never know whether the murder victim was a treifah. If there is any possibility he was, how can the murderer ever be executed? The answer must be ela lav mishum de-amrinan zil batar ruba — we go by majority, and we assume the victim was not a treifah.

### Autopsy & Kevod Hamet

The Gemara raises the possibility: v-chi teima, dibadkinan lei — perhaps we do perform an autopsy on the victim and determine whether he was a treifah? The Gemara responds: ha ka menavel — that would constitute a nivul, an indignity to the dead body, which is prohibited on account of kevod ha-met.

The Gemara immediately counters this objection: v-chi teima, mishum ibud neshamah d-hai — one should be willing to tolerate the indignity to the corpse in order to protect the life of the murderer. If the victim was indeed a treifah, the murderer should not be executed, and ninvalei — we would allow the autopsy in order to protect his life. This is a meaningful digression, as it touches on the question of what circumstances permit autopsy and override the concern for kevod ha-met.

However, even granting that an autopsy would be performed, it still cannot resolve the uncertainty. V-neichosh shema bi-mkom sayaf nekev havah — perhaps exactly where the sword pierced the victim's heart, there was already a pre-existing hole. If the victim was killed by a sword through the heart, an autopsy would show a perforated heart — but one cannot determine whether that perforation existed before the murder or was caused by the sword itself. There is no way to rule out the possibility that the victim was a treifah in precisely the location where he was killed. Thus, regardless of whether an autopsy is performed, one cannot achieve certainty, and one must rely on rov.

### Edim Zomemim Proof

Ravina says: atya me-eidim zomemin. The Torah states va-asitem lo ka-asher zamam — one does as the conspiring witnesses planned to do to the person they testified against. If two witnesses falsely testified that Reuven killed someone, intending to have Reuven executed, they are themselves executed. But v-leichosh dilma hai d-ashidu bei treifah havah — perhaps the person they were testifying against was himself a treifah. If they were only trying to have a treifah killed, their scheme, while wicked, was not aiming at the destruction of a full life in the halachic sense. Can one then execute them?

The answer must be ela lav mishum de-amrinan zil batar ruba — we assume the person they sought to have killed was not a treifah.

The Gemara raises the obvious rebuttal: d-nivdkei — let us inspect the person they were trying to kill. He is still alive; why not examine him and determine whether he is a treifah? The Gemara responds with a Baraita: lo haragu neheragin — the eidim zomemin are only executed if they did not succeed in having the person killed; if they did succeed, ein neheragin, they cannot themselves be killed. Since the person they sought to have killed is still alive, one might think an inspection is possible. But a live person cannot be cut open for an autopsy — so there is genuinely no way to determine whether the intended victim is or is not a treifah. One must therefore rely on rov.

### Shechitah Rov Proof

Rav Ashi returns the discussion to the opening topic of the masechet. Atya mi-shechitah atzmah — the proof comes from shechitah itself. The Torah states shechot ve-echol — slaughter and eat. V-leichosh shema bi-mkom nekev ka-shachit — perhaps right at the site of the cut, where one severed the trachea and esophagus, there was already a pre-existing hole. If so, the animal was a treifah before the shechitah was performed. The only reason one may eat the meat is ela lav mishum de-amrinan zil batar ruba — one goes by majority and assumes no pre-existing hole was present.

## Efshar Le-Varer

### Shimi's Challenge

Rav Ashi reports that he transmitted this entire discussion to Rav Kahana, who in turn transmitted it to Rav Shimi, and Rav Shimi responded with a fundamental challenge: dilma heicha de-efshar efshar, heicha de-lo efshar lo efshar — perhaps where checking is possible, one must check and cannot rely on rov; where checking is not possible, one may rely on rov.

This is the principle the Rishonim frame as efshar le-varer — can one use rov and chazakah as tools even when one has the ability to obtain more information? Rav Shimi is suggesting that all the proofs brought so far share a common feature: in each case, it was impossible to perform the relevant inspection, whether because of the laws of the korban, because the evidence had been destroyed, or because the facts were simply not ascertainable. Maybe the license to rely on rov is only granted when there is no alternative, and when one can check, one must check.

### Rabbi Meir & Miut

The Gemara responds with a powerful reductio. L-Rabbi Meir d-chaish le-miuta — Rabbi Meir holds that one must be concerned about the minority; he does not permit relying on rov and ignoring the miut at a rabbinic level. If Rav Shimi's principle is correct, then Rabbi Meir's position would extend further: even when checking is possible, one would have to check, and even then there are things that cannot be checked. Hacha nami d-lo achil bisra — would one conclude that Rabbi Meir simply never ate meat? Because there is always the possibility that the trachea had a pre-existing hole precisely at the site of shechitah, and there is no way to verify this after the fact.

V-chi teima, hacha nami — perhaps Rabbi Meir was indeed a vegetarian? The Gemara dismisses this: pesach ve-kodashim mai eika le-meimar — Rabbi Meir still lived under the obligation to eat korban Pesach and, in the time of the Beis Hamikdash, to participate in the eating of korbanos. He could not have been a vegetarian in any complete sense. Therefore, even Rabbi Meir would concede that where there is genuinely no alternative, one cannot remain in a state of perpetual abstention. This demonstrates that the distinction between possible and impossible checking cannot be the operative line.

### Rashi Halacha Misinai

The Gemara does not resolve this tension explicitly in the text, but Rashi, at the end of 11b and the beginning of 12a, provides the resolution. Looking at the first narrow column of Rashi on 12a: ella vadai — since one cannot prove from any of the evidence brought that rov applies even when checking is possible, one must instead say halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai — ha de-samchinan a-ruba afilu heicha de-efshar. There is a transmitted tradition that one may rely on rov even when the facts could theoretically be ascertained. Alternatively, Rashi adds, one returns to the verse acharei rabim lehatot — mashma bein ruba de-ita kaman bein ruba de-leisa kaman, and regardless of whether checking is possible, de-mai shna hai me-hai, there is no basis for distinguishing.

### Lungs Matzui Rule

Rashi then states the practical bottom line: de-ha milta samchinan — we rely on the fact that one goes by rov even when more information could be obtained, velo badkinan kol yud-chet trefos, and we do not check for all eighteen categories of treifot. The one exception is nekuvat ha-reia — the possibility of holes in the lung. Mishum de-shachi bei re-uta — because it is common, shachi'ach, to have problems in the lung. Hatam badkinan — there we check.

Rashi also addresses the case where inspection of the lung becomes impossible: ve-heicha de-ishtachach de-avda reia velo badak, if the lung was lost and one did not check it, michla achila, one may eat the meat, since even then one falls back on rov. The requirement to check applies only when checking is possible and the defect is shachi'ach; if inspection is no longer possible even for the lungs, rov suffices.

### Matzui vs. Eino Matzui

The Rishonim's distinction between miut ha-matzui and miut she-eino matzui — a likely minority possibility and an unlikely one — is not stated explicitly in the Gemara but provides the conceptual framework for the ruling. In practice, the threshold is generally understood to be around five percent: a defect that occurs in five percent or fewer cases is considered miut she-eino matzui; more than that is miut matzui.

The underlying logic of this distinction is illuminating. When a problem arises in fewer than five percent of cases, one might say that halacha itself defines this as vadai — certainty. The question is not how certain one must be in a psychological sense, but what threshold the halachic system sets for treating something as established. If ninety-five percent certainty counts as established fact, then a sub-five-percent minority does not even rise to the level of a recognized safek — doubt. On this understanding, when the miut is she-eino matzui, there is no doubt to address, and no further investigation is warranted. No reliance on rov is even needed, strictly speaking, because the category of doubt has not been triggered.

When the miut is matzui, by contrast, the doubt is genuine and acknowledged. Here one does have a safek that must be addressed, and if one can obtain more information, one must do so. This creates two distinct logical modes in which rov operates: in the case of miut she-eino matzui, rov functions by effectively dissolving the doubt — there is no recognized uncertainty. In the case of miut matzui, rov acknowledges the doubt and provides a basis for action despite it — but when the doubt is acknowledged and resolution is available, one is obligated to pursue it.

This is the final conceptual architecture of the sugya: one goes by rov in all cases, but the nature of that reliance differs depending on whether the minority possibility is matzui or not. And in the practical realm of hilchot treifot, the consequence is clear — one checks only the lungs, where problems are shachi'ach, and relies on rov for all other organs.
