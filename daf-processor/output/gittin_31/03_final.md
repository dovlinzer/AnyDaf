# Gittin 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lending to Kohen/Levi

### Unspecified Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאָמַר לְלֵוִי: ״מַעֲשֵׂר יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּיָדִי״, אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לִתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁבּוֹ. ״כּוֹר מַעֲשֵׂר יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ – חוֹשְׁשִׁין לִתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:2): With regard to **an Israelite who said to a Levite: There is tithe of yours in my possession** that I separated from my produce on your behalf, **one is not concerned about** the ***teruma* of the tithe that is in it,** i.e., the one-tenth of the tithe that is given to the priest and forbidden to both the Levite and the Israelite; rather it is assumed to be only first tithe. However, if he said to him: **There is a *kor* of tithe of yours in my possession,** then **one is concerned about** the ***teruma* of the tithe that is in it.**

Today's daf begins at *Gittin* 31b, picking up in the middle of the discussion concerning the institution of lending money to a *kohen*, *levi*, or *ani*, whereby the borrower repays through *terumot* and *ma'asrot*. Within that context, the *gemara* introduces a related *baraita*: if a *Yisrael* says to a *levi*, "*ma'aser yesh lecha b'yadi*" — "you have *ma'aser* in my possession" — *ein chosheshin l'trumat ma'aser shebo*, we need not be concerned that there is *trumat ma'aser* within that *ma'aser*. The *halakha* is that there is no such concern. By contrast, if he specified a measure — "*kor ma'aser yesh lecha b'yadi*," you have a *kor* (thirty *se'ah*) of *ma'aser* in my possession — then *chosheshin l'trumat ma'aser shebo*, we are concerned about the *trumat ma'aser*. The *gemara* immediately protests: *mai ka'amar*? What is this even saying? The *baraita* seems impenetrable as stated.

### Abaye's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאָמַר לְלֵוִי ״מַעֲשֵׂר יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּיָדִי, וְהֵילָךְ דָּמָיו״, אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא עֲשָׂאוֹ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר עַל מָקוֹם אַחֵר. ״כּוֹר מַעֲשֵׂר יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּיָדִי, וְהֵילָךְ דָּמָיו״, חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא עֲשָׂאוֹ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר עַל מָקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *baraita* **saying? Abaye said: This is what it is saying:** In the case of **an Israelite who said to a Levite: There is tithe of yours in my possession, and here is money** in exchange for **it,** then **one is not concerned** that **perhaps** the Levite **converted** the tithe that is now in the possession of the Israelite into ***teruma* of the tithe for** tithes that he has **elsewhere.** Since the Israelite did not specify how much tithe of the Levite's he had, the Levite would not know how much of his other tithe could be exempted from *teruma* of the tithe by converting this tithe into *teruma* of the tithe. But if an Israelite said to a Levite: **There is a *kor* of tithe of yours in my possession, and here is money** in exchange for **it,** then **one is concerned** that since the Levite knows the amount of the tithe, **perhaps he converted it** into the ***teruma* of the tithe for** tithes that he has **elsewhere.**

Abaye steps in with a clarification: *hachi ka'amar* — here is what the *baraita* actually means. A *Yisrael* says to a *levi*: "*ma'aser yesh lecha b'yadi v'heilach damav*" — "I have separated *ma'aser* in my home, I have you in mind, it is yours; now here is money, let me buy it back from you." This fits squarely within the context of the *mishnah*, where a *Yisrael* separates *ma'aser* and then repurchases it from the *levi* in order to keep the grain in his own possession.

### Tosafot on Timing

Given this reading, *Tosafot* explains the concern as follows. The *levi* has agreed in principle to sell the *ma'aser* back, but the sale does not become binding until he takes the money — and in this case, since the grain is already physically in the *Yisrael*'s house, taking possession is effectively accomplished the moment he receives the money. Before the money changes hands, however, the *levi* still owns the *ma'aser*. *Tosafot* therefore asks: are we concerned that in that window of time, before the sale is binding, the *levi* might have used the separated *ma'aser* to perform *havdalah* — declaring it to be *trumat ma'aser* on behalf of *other ma'aser* he has at home? We are not concerned, says the *baraita* in the unspecified case.

### Switcheroo Concern

The concern that *Tosafot* is addressing is the following. Suppose the *levi* has a thousand bushels of *ma'aser* at home, and the *Yisrael* has just given him one hundred bushels of *ma'aser* (in some halakhically recognized manner, perhaps via a third-party *kinyan* on his behalf). The *levi* could say: "I will now designate these hundred bushels as *trumat ma'aser* on my other nine hundred bushels." He then takes the money from the sale — and the *Yisrael* discovers that what he bought back is now entirely *terumah*: ninety percent cannot be eaten by a non-*kohen* at all. In the unspecified case, we are not concerned that this occurred, because the *levi* did not know the precise amount of *ma'aser* involved, and one cannot properly designate *trumat ma'aser* without knowing the quantity. If, however, the *Yisrael* stated an exact measure — *kor ma'aser* — and that is also how the *levi* came to know the precise amount, then indeed we must be concerned: he knew exactly how much he had, and could have performed this "switcheroo," converting the full amount to *trumat ma'aser al makom acher*, on other *ma'aser* elsewhere.

### Wickedness Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ בְּרַשִּׁיעֵי עָסְקִינַן, דְּשָׁקְלִי דְּמֵי וּמְשַׁוּוּ לֵיהּ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Abaye's explanation: **Is that to say** that **we are dealing with wicked** people, **who take money** in exchange for the tithe **and** afterward **convert it** into the ***teruma* of the tithe?** Once the Levite takes money in exchange for the tithe, it is no longer his to convert it into *teruma* of the tithe. Why would the *baraita* address the case of a Levite who acts in this fashion?

At this point the *gemara* objects sharply: *atu b'rishi'ei askinan*? Are we dealing with wicked people? *Deshaklu d'mei u'machlei lehu trumat ma'aser*? That they take money for the grain and yet turn it all into *trumat ma'aser* behind the buyer's back? If the *levi* did such a thing, the entire sale should be a *mekach ta'ut* — a transaction made in error — and void entirely. The *gemara* concludes that this reading, while it fits the language of the *baraita* elegantly, cannot be its true *pshat*.

## Deceased Levi Scenarios

### Rav Mesharshiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאָמַר לְבֶן לֵוִי ״מַעֲשֵׂר לְאָבִיךְ בְּיָדִי, הֵילָךְ דָּמָיו״, אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא עֲשָׂאוֹ אָבִיו תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר עַל מָקוֹם אַחֵר. ״כּוֹר מַעֲשֵׂר לְאָבִיךְ בְּיָדִי, וְהֵילָךְ דָּמָיו״, חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא עֲשָׂאוֹ אָבִיו תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר עַל מָקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Mesharshiyya, son of Rav Idi, said** that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** In the case of **an Israelite who said to the son of a Levite:** There is **tithe of your father's in my possession,** about which I had informed him while he was still alive; **here is money** in exchange for **it,** one is **not concerned** that **perhaps** before his death **his father converted it** into ***teruma* of the tithe for** tithes that he had **elsewhere,** and the son may accept the money. But if an Israelite said to the son of a Levite: There is **a *kor* of tithe of your father's in my possession, and here is money** in exchange for **it;** then one **is concerned** that **perhaps his father converted it** into ***teruma* of the tithe for** tithes that he has **elsewhere,** and the son may not accept the money.

The *gemara* therefore proposes an entirely different scenario, attributed to Rav Mesharshiya son of Rav Idi. The *baraita* now reads as follows: a *Yisrael* says to the son of a *levi*: "*ma'aser l'avicha b'yadi*" — "your father entrusted *ma'aser* to me before he passed away." The scenario has shifted: we are no longer dealing with a *levi* who might act improperly in the present, but with *ma'aser* that a now-deceased *levi* had left in someone else's possession. The *Yisrael* now wishes to purchase this *ma'aser* from the son. The question becomes: must we be concerned about what the *father* did to this *ma'aser* before he died?

### Trumat Ma'aser Question

The concern is whether the father, before his death, might have designated the entire sum of *ma'aser* as *trumat ma'aser al makom acher* — as *trumat ma'aser* on other *ma'aser* he possessed elsewhere. The *Yisrael* who wishes to buy the grain needs to know whether it is clean *ma'aser* (with only a tenth yet to be given to the *kohen*) or whether the whole amount has been converted to *trumat ma'aser*, leaving it entirely *terumah*. The *baraita* rules: if the amount was unspecified — "*ma'aser l'avicha b'yadi*" with no quantity stated — *ein chosheshin*, we need not be concerned. If a specific amount was named — "*kor ma'aser l'avicha b'yadi*" — then *chosheshin*, we are concerned.

There are in principle three possible states in which the father could have left the *ma'aser*: first, he did nothing at all, and it remains raw *ma'aser* awaiting *trumat ma'aser*; second, he removed the *trumat ma'aser* from within it, though we do not know where it ended up; and third, he converted the entire amount to *trumat ma'aser* on other grain elsewhere. The *gemara* treats the third scenario as the most extreme and the one animating the concern.

### Non-Standard Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי נֶחְשְׁדוּ חֲבֵרִים לִתְרוֹם שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַמּוּקָּף?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Mesharshiyya's explanation: Why is there a concern that the father may have converted it into *teruma* of the tithe for tithes that he had elsewhere? If one has produce that needs to have *teruma* or *teruma* of the tithe separated from it, and he wants to perform the separation from other produce, to exempt all the produce, the Sages established that the other produce must be situated nearby. **And are *ḥaverim*,** who are meticulous in their observance of mitzvot, especially the *halakhot* of *teruma* and tithes, **suspected of separating** *teruma* from produce **that is not situated near** the produce they seek to exempt?

The *gemara* then raises a pointed objection: *v'chi nechsh'du chaveirim litrom shelo min hamukaf*? Are we really to suspect scrupulous people — *chaveirim* — of separating *trumat ma'aser* in a manner that is not *min hamukaf*, not from grain that is all physically together in one place? The requirement that the grain being separated and the grain it serves as *terumah* for should be in the same physical space (*mukaf*) is the standard, normative procedure. Suspecting this *levi*, who was presumably a *chaver* scrupulous in his observance of *terumot u'ma'asrot*, of routinely designating grain in one location as *trumat ma'aser* for grain in an entirely different location seems unwarranted. The *gemara* accordingly dismisses this reading as well.

### Rav Ashi's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: בֶּן יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאָמַר לְלֵוִי ״כָּךְ אָמַר לִי אַבָּא: מַעֲשֵׂר לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ אוֹ ״מַעֲשֵׂר לְאָבִיךְ בְּיָדִי״ – חוֹשְׁשִׁין לִתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁבּוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא קִיץ לָא הֲוָה מְתַקֵּן לֵיהּ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. ״כּוֹר מַעֲשֵׂר לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ אוֹ ״כּוֹר מַעֲשֵׂר לְאָבִיךְ בְּיָדִי״ – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לִתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁבּוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּקִיץ תַּקּוֹנֵי תַּקְּנֵיהּ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: This is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** With regard to **the son of an Israelite who said to a Levite: This is what my father told me,** that there is **tithe of yours in my possession, or** that there is **tithe of your father's in my possession,** then one **is concerned about** the ***teruma* of the tithe that is in it** that presumably was never separated. **Since it is not a set** amount, **the homeowner would not have rendered** the tithe **fit for him** by separating the *teruma* of the tithe. But if the son of an Israelite said to a Levite: My father told me that there is **a *kor* of tithe of yours in my possession, or** that there is **a *kor* of tithe of your father's in my possession,** then one is **not concerned about** the ***teruma* of the tithe that is in it. Since it is a set** amount, **the homeowner** is assumed to have **rendered** the tithe **fit** by separating the *teruma* of the tithe.

Rav Ashi proposes a third interpretation. The *baraita* describes yet another scenario: a *ben Yisrael* reports to a *levi*: "*kach amar li Abba* — this is what my father told me before he died — '*ma'aser l'avicha b'yadi*,' that he had *ma'aser* in his possession belonging to your father." So here it is not the *Yisrael* himself reporting on grain entrusted to him, but the *Yisrael*'s son relaying what his deceased father said. Reuven says: my father Yaakov, before he died, separated *ma'aser* from his grain and designated it for you, the *levi*.

The question now is what Yaakov — the deceased *Yisrael* — might have done with that *ma'aser* before he died. Specifically, the concern here is not the *switcheroo* of *al makom acher*, but rather the opposite: did the father do the *levi* a favor and proactively separate the *trumat ma'aser* from within the *ma'aser*, so that the *levi* receives clean, ready-to-use *ma'aser*? Or does the *ma'aser* still contain its *trumat ma'aser* waiting to be taken?

### Articulation Effect

The *baraita* rules as follows: if the father said only "*ma'aser lecha b'yadi*" without specifying the amount, then *chosheshin l'trumat ma'aser shebo* — we are concerned that there is still *trumat ma'aser* within it, because since the amount was unspecified (*lo kayitz*), the father likely did not know the exact quantity and would not have taken the trouble of properly separating the *trumat ma'aser*. If, however, the father specified "*kor ma'aser lecha b'yadi*" — a precise measure — then *ein chosheshin l'trumat ma'aser shebo*: we need not be concerned, because since the amount was known and articulated, we assume the father fixed it up completely before he died.

### Father's Preparation

This reading, it should be noted, inverts the apparent direction of the original *baraita*. In the original text, the unspecified case triggered no concern and the specified case triggered concern; in Rav Ashi's reading, it is the specified case where we assume the father acted charitably on the *levi*'s behalf and there is no concern, while the unspecified case is where we remain concerned. As the *gemara* itself acknowledges, this does not map cleanly onto the literal language of the *baraita* — *adarabba* — but at least the underlying scenario is coherent. The operative principle is that just as we generally assume a *chaver* does not die and leave his grain in an unfixed state, so too if a *Yisrael* knew the exact amount of *ma'aser* he had designated for a particular *levi*, we presume he finished the job — that *tikkunei takniya baal habayis*, the householder fixed it up — separating the *trumat ma'aser* so the *levi* would receive clean grain, ready for use, what one might call *trumat ma'aser genummen*.

## Ba'al HaBayit Rights

### Question of Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת לִתְרוֹם תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר?! אִין, אַבָּא אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן גַּמְלָא הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, אַבָּא אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן גַּמְלָא אוֹמֵר: ״וְנֶחְשַׁב לָכֶם תְּרוּמַתְכֶם״ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Ashi's explanation: **And does the homeowner have permission to separate** the ***teruma* of the tithe** from the tithe of the Levite? The Levite is the one who is required to separate the *teruma* of the tithe and to give it to a priest. The Gemara answers: **Yes;** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Abba Elazar ben Gamla. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Abba Elazar ben Gamla says:** The verse states concerning the *teruma* of the tithe: **"And the gift that you set apart [*terumatkhem*] shall be reckoned to you,** as though it were the grain of the threshing floor" (Numbers 18:27).

Having established that the *ba'al habayit* is presumed to have separated *trumat ma'aser* from the *ma'aser* in his possession, the *gemara* pauses to ask a fundamental *halachic* question: *v'chi yesh lo reshut l'baal habayit litrom trumat ma'aser*? Since when does the *ba'al habayit* have the authority to separate *trumat ma'aser* from *ma'aser* that is not his? Once *ma'aser* has been separated, it belongs to the *levi* — or more broadly to *shevet Levi*. The *ba'al habayit* would normally need to be appointed as a *shaliach* to act on the *levi*'s behalf. The assumption in the previous discussion seemed to be that the *ba'al habayit* has an inherent entitlement: having separated the *ma'aser*, he simply finishes the job by separating the *trumat ma'aser*. But what is the basis for this?

### Abba Elazar View

The *gemara* answers: this is the position of Abba Elazar ben Gamla. As established in a *baraita*, Abba Elazar ben Gamla derives this authority from the *pasuk* concerning *trumat ma'aser*: *v'nechshav lachem trumatchem* — "and your *terumah* shall be considered for you."

### Scriptural Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁתֵּי תְּרוּמוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר: אַחַת תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה וְאַחַת תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר; כְּשֵׁם שֶׁתְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה נִיטֶּלֶת בְּאוֹמֶד וּבְמַחְשָׁבָה, כָּךְ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר נִיטֶּלֶת בְּאוֹמֶד וּבְמַחְשָׁבָה; וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת לִתְרוֹם תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, כָּךְ יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת לִתְרוֹם תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse speaks of two *terumot***; **one** is **standard *teruma* and one** is ***teruma* of the tithe.** Since they are mentioned together, they can be compared: **Just as standard *teruma* is taken by estimate,** as there is no requirement for the amount separated to be measured precisely, **and** it can be taken **by thought,** as one is not required to physically separate it before consuming the remaining produce, **so too, *teruma* of the tithe may be taken by estimate and by thought. And** this comparison also teaches that **just as the homeowner has permission,** i.e., the right, **to separate standard *teruma*, so too, the homeowner has permission to separate *teruma* of the tithe.**

The *pasuk* in full (Bamidbar 18:26–27) reads: the *levi'im* shall take a *ma'aser min hamaaser* — a tenth of the tenth — and this shall be *v'nechshav lachem trumatchem k'dagan min hagoren uch'mleiah min hayekev* — "your *terumah* shall be considered as grain from the threshing floor and fullness from the winepress." Rashi explains that the verse is drawing a comparison between the two *terumot*: the *trumat ma'aser* that the *levi* gives is treated identically to the *terumah gedolah* that the *Yisrael* takes from his grain. The *baraita* makes this explicit: *bishtei trumos hakatuv medaber*, the *pasuk* is speaking of two *terumot* simultaneously and equating them.

### Estimation Method

From this equation the *gemara* derives: *keshem sheterumah gedolah nitelet b'omed uvemachshavah*, just as regular *terumah* is separated by estimation and by thought alone, *kach trumat ma'aser nitelet b'omed uvemachshavah* — so too *trumat ma'aser* may be separated by estimation and thought. Since *terumah* has no fixed Torah-mandated amount (even a single kernel exempts the entire pile, with the Rabbinic standard being approximately two percent), one cannot be expected to give a precise measure and is encouraged to estimate, presumably rounding up rather than coming up short. *Tosafot* notes that we specifically encourage estimation for this reason, similar to the practice of a merchant who heaps a measure generously. Even though *trumat ma'aser* does have a fixed amount — one tenth of the *ma'aser* — the Torah's equation to *terumah gedolah* teaches that a guesstimated tenth suffices and need not be precisely measured. Furthermore, *bemachshavah*: the entire act of separation can be accomplished through thought alone, without any verbal declaration. The source for this is the word *v'nechshav* in the *pasuk* — the root *cheshbon*, calculation or thought — indicating that a purely mental act of designation suffices.

### Conclusion

From this equation the *baraita* proceeds: *ukeshem sheyesh reshut l'baal habayit litrom terumah gedolah*, just as the owner of the grain separates *terumah gedolah*, *kach yesh reshut l'baal habayit litrom trumat ma'aser* — so too he has the authority to separate *trumat ma'aser* from the *ma'aser* in his possession. Having separated the *ma'aser*, he is entitled to complete the process by separating the *trumat ma'aser* as well. One might ask whether this entitlement also grants him the right to determine which *kohen* receives the *trumat ma'aser*, or whether that right remains with the *levi* — but the *gemara* does not explore this question, and at minimum the *ba'al habayit*'s authority to perform the separation itself is established.

## Setting Aside Produce

### Designated Room

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּנִּיחַ פֵּירוֹת, לִהְיוֹת מַפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן תְּרוּמָה וּמַעַשְׂרוֹת; מָעוֹת, לִהְיוֹת מַפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי – מַפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהֵן קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who sets aside produce** with the understanding that **he will separate *terumot* and tithes with it,** so that when he has untithed produce he can render it fit by declaring that the *teruma* and tithes that must be separated will be from the produce that he had set aside for this purpose, or one who sets aside **money** with the understanding that **he will separate** and redeem **second tithe with it,** then he may later **separate** the *teruma* or tithe **with them, based on the presumption that** the produce or the money **are extant.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a new *mishnah*, which continues the overarching theme of the *masechet* — presuming that an agent remains alive and his actions valid — applied now to *terumot* and *ma'asrot*. The *mishnah* states: *hameniach peiros lihyos mafrish aleihen terumah uma'asrot* — one who sets aside produce in a specific location (say, a room) intending to use that stockpile as a source from which to separate *terumah* and *ma'asrot* over time. The practical scenario is this: a *Yisrael* fills a room with grain or fruit and maintains a running ledger as he processes batches from his fields. Each time he has new *tevel* grain and needs to separate *terumah*, he declares: "two more bushels in that room are *terumah*" — or *ma'aser* — until the entire room has been exhausted in designations. *Tosafot* notes that this arrangement is technically *shelo min hamukaf*, since the grain being designated and the grain it exempts are in different locations, and explains that the Rabbis permitted this specifically in cases of pressing need such as *erev Shabbat*, when one needs to prepare food quickly.

### Chazaka Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָבְדוּ – הֲרֵי זֶה חוֹשֵׁשׁ מֵעֵת לְעֵת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **If** he discovers that **they were lost,** then **he must be concerned** that the produce or money that he set aside was lost **from the time until the** same **time,** as will be explained in the Gemara, and he must separate *teruma* and tithes from the produce a second time; this is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar.**

*Mafrish aleihem bechezkas sheheim kayamim* — one may continue separating in the ordinary fashion with the presumption that the grain in the room still exists. There is no need to verify the continued existence of the stockpile every time one makes a designation; the *chazaka* that it was there permits the assumption that it remains.

### 24-Hour Window

There is no need to verify the continued existence of the stockpile every time one makes a designation; the *chazaka* that it was there permits the assumption that it remains. *Im avdu, harei zeh choshesh me'es le'es* — if he goes back and discovers the stockpile is gone — whether rotted, stolen, or lost — he must be concerned for twenty-four hours. That is, any designations made in the last twenty-four hours are retroactively void, since the grain may have been gone during that window. Designations made before that point, however, stand. The reasoning behind this specific number is not obvious: if no one ever checked on the room, why should we assume the grain must have been present until twenty-four hours before discovery rather than having been lost months earlier? The *gemara* will address this tension directly, but the underlying conceptual framework is one of competing *chazakot*: the *chazaka d'meikara* — the original status quo that the grain was there — and the *chazaka d'hashta* — the inference from the current state that things may always have been as they are now. At what point does the current status override the original presumption? The *mishnah*'s answer is twenty-four hours. *Tosafot* notes this is reminiscent of the *me'es le'es* principle in the laws of *nidda*, but stops short of explaining why the twenty-four-hour figure works here — it simply observes the parallel without resolving the underlying rationale.

### Wine Checking Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה פְּרָקִים בּוֹדְקִין אֶת הַיַּיִן – בְּקִידּוּם שֶׁל מוֹצָאֵי הַחַג, וּבְהוֹצָאַת סְמָדַר, וּבִשְׁעַת כְּנִיסַת מַיִם בְּבוֹסֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: One checks the wine** that is set aside to be used for separating *terumot* and tithes for other wine to see if it has turned to vinegar, which would render it unfit for this purpose, **at three times** during the year: **When the east wind** blows **at the conclusion of the festival** of *Sukkot*, **and when the** blossoms fall and the **grape buds emerge** and appear as small clusters, **and at the time** when **water enters** and fills **the unripe grape.** Since there is a change in the weather at these times, one should check to ensure that the wine has not turned to vinegar.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a further rule specifically for wine: *bishlosha perakim bodkin et hayayin* — one must check on wine designated in such a room at three specific times, because wine can turn to vinegar, and one cannot separate *terumah* from wine over vinegar. The three times are: when the east wind blows after *Sukkot* (specifically in a year where *Sukkot* falls after the fall equinox, as Rashi explains); when the grape blossoms (*smadar*) emerge; and when water begins to enter the unripe grapes (*boser*). These are moments when wine is particularly vulnerable to spoilage, and a *ba'al habayis* relying on a designated room of wine must verify at each of these junctures that his wine has not become vinegar.

## Me'es Le'es Debate

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי מֵעֵת לְעֵת? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מֵעֵת לְעֵת שֶׁל בְּדִיקָה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי:
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that if the produce or money was lost, then one must be concerned that it occurred from the time until the same time. The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by: **From the time until the** same **time? Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It means **from the time** it was **checked** and found to be lost, **until the** same **time** of day on the previous day. If one had used that produce to render fit other produce within the twenty-four hours before noticing that it had been lost, one must separate *teruma* and tithes for that produce again. **Rabbi Elazar ben Antigonus says in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yannai:**

The *gemara* now examines precisely what the *mishnah*'s phrase *me'es le'es* means. Does it look backward from the moment of discovery, or forward from the moment of deposit? Rabbi Yochanan holds: *me'es le'es she-bdika* — the twenty-four hours run backward from the time of inspection. You discovered the problem now; you must be concerned that this problematic state began up to twenty-four hours ago. Everything done in the last twenty-four hours is potentially void; anything before that is presumed valid.

### Rabbi Elazar ben Tanchum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעֵת לְעֵת שֶׁל הַנָּחָה.
>
> **Translation:** It means **from the time** that it was **set aside until the** same **time** the day after it was set aside. In other words, he must be concerned about all of the untithed produce that he had rendered fit after the first twenty-four hour period from when he set aside the produce or money had passed.

Rabbi Elazar ben Tanchum, citing Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Yanai, takes a far more extreme position: *me'es le'es shel hanacha* — the twenty-four hours run forward from the time of deposit. From the moment the stockpile was set aside, you may only assume with confidence that the first twenty-four hours were good. Once that initial window closes, you must treat everything as potentially problematic, up through the present moment of discovery.

### Language Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אִם אָבְדוּ – הֲרֵי זֶה חוֹשֵׁשׁ מֵעֵת לְעֵת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מֵעֵת לְעֵת שֶׁל בְּדִיקָה – שַׁפִּיר; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מֵעֵת לְעֵת שֶׁל הַנָּחָה – הַאי ״מֵעֵת לְעֵת״?! ״עַד מֵעֵת לְעֵת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **If** he discovers that **they were lost,** then **he must be concerned** that the produce or money that he set aside was lost **from the time until the** same **time. Granted, according to the one who said** that this means **from the time** it was **checked** and found to be lost **until the** same **time** of day on the previous day, it works out **well,** as the wine is presumed to have turned to vinegar from that moment. **But according to the one who said** that this means **from the time** that it was **set aside until the** same **time** the day after it was set aside, **this** expression: **From the time until the** same **time,** is imprecise, as the presumption is that the wine turned to vinegar dating back to the day after it had been set aside. Therefore, it **should have** said: One is concerned going back **until** then **from the time** it was set aside **to the** same **time** the following day, i.e., twenty-four hours after it had been set aside. The Gemara comments: The wording of the mishna is **difficult** according to that opinion.

The *gemara* tests both positions against the language of the *mishnah*: *im avdu harei zeh choshesh me'es le'es* — if lost, one is concerned for *me'es le'es*. The word *choshesh* — concerned — clearly refers to the problematic state. Under Rabbi Yochanan's reading, the phrase fits naturally: you are concerned about the past twenty-four hours. Under Rabbi Elazar ben Tanchum's reading, however, if the good window is only the first twenty-four hours of deposit, the *mishnah* should have said *ad me'es le'es* — "until twenty-four hours" — rather than simply *choshesh me'es le'es*. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — this is indeed difficult for Rabbi Elazar ben Tanchum's position. The plain reading of the *mishnah* clearly supports Rabbi Yochanan.

### Mikva Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: חֲלוּקִין עָלָיו חֲבֵירָיו עַל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – דִּתְנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁנִּמְדַּד וְנִמְצָא חָסֵר; כׇּל טְהָרוֹת שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל גַּבָּיו לְמַפְרֵעַ – בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that this is **the statement of** the *tanna* **Rabbi Elazar.** The *amora* **Rabbi Elazar says: Rabbi Elazar's colleagues disagree with him, as we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 2:2): In the case of **a ritual bath** that was known to have contained the requisite forty *se'a*, **which was** then **measured and found deficient** in its quantity of water, **all pure items that had been rendered** pure **in it, retroactive** to when the ritual bath was last measured, **whether** this ritual bath is found **in the private domain or in the public domain, are impure.**

The *mishnah* ends with *divrei Rabbi Elazar* (the *gemara* corrects the reading to Rabbi Elazar rather than Rabbi Eliezer). The *gemara* then fills in the implicit counterpoint: *chalukin alav chaveirav al Rabbi Elazar* — his colleagues dispute him. The proof comes from a *mishnah* in the laws of *mikva*: a *mikva* that was measured and found to be deficient — *nimdad v'nimtza chaser* — meaning it had been valid at some known past point and is now found lacking. The rule is: *kol taharos she-nasu al gabav l'mifrea*, all purifications performed in it retroactively — *bein b'reshut hayachid bein b'reshut harabbim* — whether in the private or public domain — are *teme'ot*, presumed invalid.

The logic here is crucial. Normally, in the public domain, a doubt about *tumah* is resolved leniently (*safek tumah b'reshut harabbim tahor*). But that rule applies when an object started as *tahor* and we are uncertain whether it became *tamei*. Here, the object — a vessel, a person — started as *tamei*. Its *chazaka* was *tumah*. It was brought to the *mikva* to achieve *tahara*, but now we discover that the *mikva* itself was in doubt. The *chazaka* of *tumah* has never been definitively broken. Therefore, even in the public domain, it remains *tamei*, and this is true retroactively for as long as the *mikva*'s validity was in question — without any twenty-four-hour limitation. The same logic, by extension, applies to the grain: the grain had a *chazaka* of being *tevel*. The householder's ongoing designations were meant to change its status. If the stockpile on which those designations depended was gone, the designations never took effect, and the grain retains its original *chazaka* of *tevel*.

### Chazaka Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא דַּחֲלוּקִין! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מַאי ״לְמַפְרֵעַ״ – מֵעֵת לְעֵת, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is obvious that** those who ascribe to the statement of that mishna **disagree** with Rabbi Elazar the *tanna*. What novel idea is Rabbi Elazar the *amora* teaching? The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that **what** the term **retroactive** is referring to is any item that was immersed and purified **from the time** it was found to be deficient **until the** same **time** the previous day, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar. Therefore, he **teaches us** that in fact they disagree and deem impure all items immersed from the time that the ritual bath had last been checked.

The *gemara* anticipates a possible reconciliation: one might have tried to read the *mikva mishnah*'s word *l'mifrea* ("retroactively") through the lens of our *mishnah* and claim that *l'mifrea* means only the past twenty-four hours — thus limiting the retroactive reach in the *mikva* case as well and harmonizing it with the twenty-four-hour rule. The *gemara* rejects this: Rabbi Elazar's position is that the grain case goes all the way back, not merely twenty-four hours. His colleagues therefore apply the same logic to the *mikva*, rendering all past *tevilot* invalid without limit. In the *mikva* case, everything is *tamei* all the way back to the last verified measurement; there is no twenty-four-hour buffer. The competing *chazakot* are resolved decisively in favor of the original status: when an object's *chazaka* was *tumah*, a cloud of doubt over the act meant to purify it leaves the *chazaka* intact, indefinitely.

## Eastern Wind Effects

### Wind Characteristics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה פְּרָקִים מוֹכְרִין אֶת הַתְּבוּאָה – לִפְנֵי הַזֶּרַע, וּבִשְׁעַת הַזֶּרַע, וּבִפְרוֹס הַפֶּסַח. וּבִשְׁלֹשָׁה פְּרָקִים מוֹכְרִין אֶת הַיַּיִן – בִּפְרוֹס הַפֶּסַח, וּבִפְרוֹס עֲצֶרֶת, וּבִפְרוֹס הַחַג. וְשֶׁמֶן – מֵעֲצֶרֶת וְאֵילָךְ. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָּא? אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב פָּפָּא: לְשׁוּתָּפִין. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, מַאי? אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל יוֹמָא פִּירְקֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **at three times** during the year **one sells the grain: Before** the time of **the sowing, and during the time of the sowing, and before the** festival of **Passover. And at three times** of the year **one sells the wine: Before the** festival of **Passover, and before** the festival of ***Shavuot*, and before the festival** of *Sukkot*. **And oil** is sold **from** the festival of ***Shavuot* and onward.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** was this stated? One may sell these products whenever he so desires. **Rava said, and some say** that it was **Rav Pappa** who said: This *halakha* was stated **with regard to partners,** as one partner may sell these products without the permission of the other only at these times of the year. At other times, he must first receive permission from his partner. **From this** point **forward,** i.e., after the latest of the three times has passed, **what** is the *halakha*? **Rava said: Every day is its time** to be sold.

Because Rabbi Yehuda's rule about checking wine invoked the east wind (*ruach kadim*) blowing after *Sukkot*, the *gemara* takes up an extended discussion of the nature and effects of this wind. The point of departure is a *pasuk* from the book of Yonah: *vayeman Elokim ruach kadim charishit* — God appointed an east wind, *charishit*. What does *charishit* mean? Rav Yehuda says: when this east wind blows, *osa telamim telamim bayam* — it carves furrows in the sea, like a plow (*choreish*) carving rows in the earth. So powerful is its force that it produces these wave-furrows in the water. Rava objects: if that is what *charishit* means, how does it fit the context of the *pasuk*, which continues *vatach hashemesh al rosho shel Yonah vayitalaf* — the sun beat upon Yonah's head and he fainted? The context speaks of heat, not of the raw power of a wind at sea.

### Four Winds Balance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא, אָמַר רַב: אַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת מְנַשְּׁבוֹת בְּכׇל יוֹם, וְרוּחַ צְפוֹנִית מְנַשֶּׁבֶת עִם כּוּלָּן; שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא כֵּן, אֵין הָעוֹלָם מִתְקַיֵּים אֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת. וְרוּחַ דְּרוֹמִית קָשָׁה מִכּוּלָּן, וְאִלְמָלֵא בֶּן נֵץ מַעֲמִידָהּ, מַחְרֶבֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מִפָּנֶיהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲמִבִּינָתְךָ יַאֲבֶר נֵץ יִפְרֹשׂ כְּנָפָיו לְתֵימָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: This is what Rav Ḥanan bar Rava says** that **Rav says: Four winds blow on each day, and the north wind blows** together **with each of** the other three; **as, if this were not so** and the northern wind did not blow, then **the world would not survive** for **even one hour. And the south wind is harsher than all of them, and were it not** for the angel called **Ben Netz,** who **stops it** from blowing even harder, then it would **destroy the entire world before it, as it is stated: "Does the hawk [*netz*] soar by your wisdom, and stretch her wings toward the south?"** (Job 39:26).

Before returning to resolve this, the *gemara* presents a tradition reported by Gneiva — who happened to pass by Rav Huna and Rav Chisda as they sat together. One of them suggested they rise for him as a great *talmid chacham*; the other demurred, noting that Gneiva was known for his contentious nature. While they deliberated, Gneiva approached them and, upon being told they were discussing winds, offered the following teaching in the name of Rav Chanan bar Rava citing Rav: *arba ruchot menashvot bechol yom* — four winds blow every day. *Ruach tzonit menashevet im kulam* — the northern wind blows in conjunction with all of them. *She'imalai kein ein ha'olam mitkaiyam afilu sha'ah achat* — were it not for the northern wind, the world could not endure even a single hour. Rashi explains that the northern wind has a tempering, balancing effect — neither too hot nor too cold — and without it, the other winds would be destructive.

The southern wind, however, is the most powerful and dangerous of them all: *veruach dromit kashah mikulam* — the southern wind is the harshest. Were it not for an angel called Netz, who stands in its path and holds it back, *machrevet kol ha'olam kulo mipaneha* — it would destroy the entire world, presumably through an excess of cold. The scriptural basis is *hamavinatcha ya'aver Netz yifros knafav l'teiman* — "from Your understanding does Netz spread its wings to the south" (Iyov 39:26). This angel spreads its wings southward to restrain the southern wind and protect the world from its destructive force.

### Destructive Effects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי כִּזְרוֹחַ הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וַיְמַן אֱלֹהִים רוּחַ קָדִים חֲרִישִׁית״ – מַאי ״חֲרִישִׁית״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁמְּנַשֶּׁבֶת, עוֹשֶׂה תְּלָמִים תְּלָמִים בַּיָּם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתַּךְ הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ עַל רֹאשׁ יוֹנָה וַיִּתְעַלָּף״?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁמְּנַשֶּׁבֶת, מְשַׁתֶּקֶת כׇּל הָרוּחוֹת מִפָּנֶיהָ. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר בְּגָדֶיךָ חַמִּים בְּהַשְׁקִט אֶרֶץ מִדָּרוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned the east wind, the Gemara quotes a related verse: **"And it came to pass, when the sun arose, that God prepared a vehement [*ḥarishit*] east wind"** (Jonah 4:8). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the word **"*ḥarishit*"? Rav Yehuda says:** It means a wind that plows [*ḥoreshet*], as **at the time that** the wind **blows, it forms many furrows in the sea,** as though it were plowing the sea. **Rabba said to him: If that is so,** then **that which is written** in the continuation of the verse: **"And the sun beat upon the head of Jonah and he fainted"** (Jonah 4:8), is difficult; it seems from the continuation of the verse that the wind was extremely hot, not strong. **Rather, Rabba says: At the time that it blows, it silences all the winds before it.** Therefore, it is called *ḥarishit*, because all other winds are silent [*maḥarishot*] before it. Since only this wind was blowing, it became very hot. **And this is as it is written: "You whose garments are hot, when the earth is still by reason of the south wind"** (Job 37:17).

Returning to the east wind and the context of Yonah, the *gemara* next reports a series of traditions about the damaging effects of the *ruach kadim*. Rava citing Rav states: *isha mapelet bo* — the east wind can cause a woman to miscarry. Shmuel says: *afilu margaliyot she'bayam markivot bo* — even the pearls in the sea rot because of it. And Rabbi Yochanan states: *afilu shichvat zera she'b'mei isha masrachet bo* — even semen within a woman's body spoils as a result of it, preventing conception.

### Scriptural Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדַחֲזָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יַעֲקֹב הֲוָה, אָזֵיל לְגַבֵּיהּ. גַּלִּי לִדְרָעֵיהּ, אֲמַר: שַׁדְיָא נָשֵׁיב! אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי אָמַר רַב: אִשָּׁה מַפֶּלֶת בּוֹ; וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מַרְגָּלִית שֶׁבַּיָּם מַרְקֶבֶת בּוֹ; רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁבִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה מַסְרַחַת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **When he saw that it was Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov, he** also **went to him.** Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov **uncovered his arm** due to the heat and **said:** The wind called **Shadya is blowing. Rava says** that **this is** what **Rav says** about this wind: **A woman miscarries in its** wake. **And Shmuel says: Even a pearl [*margalit*] that is in the sea decomposes in its** wake. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even the semen that is in a woman's womb putrefies in its** wake.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then notes that all three Amoraim derived their respective positions from the same single *pasuk*: *ki hu bein achim yafria yavo kadim ruach Hashem, mimidbar ya'aleh v'yeivosh m'koro v'yecharav mayano v'hu yishasseh otzar keli chemdah* — "For he will cause division between brothers; the east wind of God shall come from the desert; his wellspring shall dry up and his spring shall wither; it shall plunder the treasure house of every precious vessel" (Hoshea 13:15). Rava then completes the exegesis: *adei Sura hu dedaykei kraei* — these are the scholars of Sura who know how to read *pesukim* in precisely this way. *V'yeivosh m'koro* — "his wellspring shall dry up" — refers to the womb of the woman, source of life; this is the miscarriage. *V'yecharav mayano* — "his spring shall wither" — refers to *shichvat zera she'b'mei isha*, the semen in the woman's body. *V'hu yishasseh otzar keli chemdah* — "it shall plunder the treasure house of precious vessels" — refers to *margaliyot she'bayam*, the pearls of the sea.

### Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, וּשְׁלׇשְׁתָּן מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ: ״כִּי הוּא בֵּין אַחִים יַפְרִיא יָבוֹא קָדִים רוּחַ ה׳ מִמִּדְבָּר עוֹלֶה וְיֵבוֹשׁ מְקוֹרוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. ״יֵבוֹשׁ מְקוֹרוֹ״ – זוֹ מְקוֹרָהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה. ״וְיֶחֱרַב מַעְיָינוֹ״ – זֶה שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁבִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה. ״הוּא יִשְׁסֶה אוֹצַר כׇּל כְּלִי חֶמְדָּה״ – זוֹ מַרְגָּלִית שֶׁבַּיָּם. אָמַר רָבָא: עָדֵי סוּרָאָה הוּא, דְּדָיְיקִי קְרָאֵי! מַאי ״כִּי הוּא בֵּין אַחִים יַפְרִיא״? אָמַר רָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak,** who was present, **said** about their statements: **And the three of them derived** their statements **from one verse,** as it is stated: **"For though he be fruitful [*yafri*] among the reed-plants, an east wind shall come, the wind of the Lord coming up from the wilderness, and his spring shall become dry,** and his fountain shall be dried up; he shall spoil the treasure of all precious vessels" (Hosea 13:15). The statement that a woman miscarries in its wake is based on the phrase **"His spring shall become dry,"** as **this** is referring to **a woman's womb.** With regard to the phrase **"And his fountain shall be dried up," this** is referring to the **semen that is in a woman's womb.** And with regard to the phrase **"He shall spoil the treasure of all precious vessels," this** is referring to **a pearl in the sea. Rava says: This** Sage **is from Sura, as they are precise** with **verses.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **"Though he be fruitful [*yafri*] among the reed-plants"? Rava said: Even**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוּפְתָּא בְּקוֹפִינָא דְמָרָא – רָפְיָא. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ סִיכְּתָא בְּדַפְנָא – רָפְיָא. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ קַנְיָא בְּכוּפְתָּא – רָפְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **the handle in the hole [*kofina*] of the hoe [*mara*] becomes loose [*rafya*],** as he understands the word *yafri* to refer to separating connected items. Similarly, **Rav Yosef said: Even the peg** hammered **into the wall becomes loose. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: Even the reed** woven **into the basket becomes loose.**

The *gemara* finally returns to the opening of the *pasuk*: *ki hu bein achim yafria* — "for it will cause division between brothers; *yafria*." Rava interprets: *afilu shufsa bekufa demara rafya* — even the handle set into the head of an axe will be loosened and rotted by this east wind. The term *bein achim*, "between brothers," refers to the two sides of the axe-head through which the handle passes; and *yafria* is a rearrangement of the letters of *rafya* — weakening. Even that tightly fitted wood will be rotted away. Rav Yosef interprets the same verse differently: *afilu sachsa bedafna rafya* — even a peg driven into a wall will be loosened. And Rav Acha bar Yaakov: *afilu kanya bekufsa rafya* — even wicker woven into a basket will weaken and rot.

With that, we conclude our survey of the effects of the eastern wind, and complete *masechet Gittin*, chapter five — *hadran alach Kol HaGet*.
