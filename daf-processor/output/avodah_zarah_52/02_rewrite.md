# Avodah Zarah 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yishmael vs. Akiva

### From Accessories to Idols

Today's daf is 52, and we pick up at the bottom of 51b. The sugya marks a significant transition: having spent considerable time discussing the laws of *tashmishei avoda zara* — the accessories and vessels connected to idol worship — and questions of *issur hana'ah* and *tikrovet*, the Gemara now turns to the core question of when an actual idol becomes forbidden in benefit. The *stam* Mishna had established that the idol of a non-Jew becomes forbidden as soon as it is made, while the idol of a Jew becomes forbidden only once it has been worshipped. The Gemara will now unpack a debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva that turns this Mishna's ruling on its head — and does so in the form of mirror-image positions.

### Mishnah & Positions

The Gemara opens by asking: *Masnisin mani? Rabbi Akiva hee* — whose view does our Mishna represent? The answer is Rabbi Akiva. A *braisa* is then cited in which the two Tannaim stake out their respective positions. Both Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva agree on one foundational point regarding *tashmishei avoda zara*: the verse in Devarim, *abeid te'abdun es kol hamakomos asher avdu sham hagoyim... es eloheihem*, cannot be read as referring to the physical places of worship — for the same Torah states *eloheihem al heharim*, their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains themselves do not become forbidden. Rather, the verse must be redirected — *tenei inyan l'keilim* — to the vessels and implements used in the worship. And regarding those implements, everyone agrees: *she'ein asurin ad sheye'aveidu*, they are not forbidden until they are actually used in worship.

The dispute concerns what follows from this agreement. Rabbi Yishmael concludes that just as the *tashmishim* are only forbidden once used, so too the idol itself of a non-Jew: *avodas kochavim shel oveid kochavim einah asura ad shete'aveid*, it is not forbidden until it is worshipped. And as its inverse: *shel yisrael asura miyad*, the idol of a Jew is forbidden immediately. Rabbi Akiva — whose view is reflected in the Mishna — holds precisely the opposite: the idol of a non-Jew is forbidden immediately, and the idol of a Jew only once it is worshipped. Both Tannaim agree that Jew and non-Jew are mirror images of one another; they disagree only about which direction the mirror faces.

---

## Yishmael Position

### Vessels Agreement

The Gemara's first task is to unpack how Rabbi Yishmael infers from the law of *tashmishim* to the idol itself. The *braisa* established that the verse's reference to destroying places of worship is actually directed at the implements used there, since it is followed by *asher atem yorshim osam es eloheihem* — and their gods. The word *nishtamshu* points to actual use: *she'ein asurin ad sheye'aveidu*, they are not forbidden until they are used in worship.

### From Vessels to Idols

The Gemara asks: but we just established that this verse is about *keilim* — the implements. How does Rabbi Yishmael get from there to a ruling about the idol itself? The answer lies in the continuation of the verse: *asher atem yorshim osam es eloheihem*. The phrase connects the implements to the gods — *makish eloheihem l'keilim* — and therefore: *mah keilim ad sheye'avedu af eloheihem nami ad sheye'avedu*. Just as the implements are forbidden only once used in worship, so too the gods themselves are forbidden only once worshipped. That is Rabbi Yishmael's position regarding the idol of a non-Jew.

### Verbal Inference

Rabbi Akiva, however, rejects this verbal linkage. He reads *asher atem yorshim osam es eloheihem* as a new, independent clause — *es hifsik ha'inyan* — and therefore the reference to their gods does not carry the same limitation that applies to the implements. The *tashmishim* are forbidden only once used; the idol itself operates under a different rule. This is the crux of the disagreement.

---

## Akiva Position

### No Vessel Connection

With the verbal linkage between implements and idols rejected by Rabbi Akiva, the two Tannaim diverge. Rabbi Akiva will need his own source for why a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately — and Rabbi Yishmael will need his own source for why a Jew's idol is forbidden immediately. Neither can rely on the *hekesh* the other employs.

---

## Yishmael Position…

### Jew Immediate (Logical)

Since Rabbi Yishmael holds that a non-Jew's idol is only forbidden once worshipped, the Gemara asks where he derives the inverse ruling — that a Jew's idol is forbidden immediately. The first answer is *savara*: pure logical reasoning. If a non-Jew's idol requires actual worship to become forbidden, it stands to reason that a Jew's is the inverse.

The Gemara immediately challenges this. Perhaps a Jew's idol is never forbidden at all? The Gemara dismisses this: *hashta geniza ba'ya itzurei lo mitzra* — we already know from a verse that it must be buried; obviously it is forbidden. But then perhaps it operates just like a non-Jew's — only once worshipped? Here the Gemara turns to a verse: *ve'es chataschem asher asisem es ha'egel* — your sin that you made the golden calf. *Mishas asiya kam lei becheit*: the sin occurs from the moment of making. The Gemara anticipates the objection that this establishes only when the person transgresses, not when the object becomes forbidden — *itzurei lo mitzra*. Another verse is therefore adduced: *arur ha'ish asher ya'aseh pesel umasecha to'avas Hashem vesam basater*. The object is called *to'avas Hashem* — an abomination of God — from the moment it is made. And since the object itself bears this label, it is forbidden in *hana'ah* from that moment: *mishas asiya kai be'arur*.

The Gemara again anticipates Rabbi Akiva's counter: he would read *to'avas Hashem* not as a *halachic* declaration of forbidden status but as a religious-descriptive phrase — *davar hameivi lidei to'evah* — meaning the object is an abomination in the sense that it leads toward idol worship, not that it legally assumes the status of *issur hana'ah* at that stage.

---

## Akiva Position…

### Psilei Eloheihem

Where does Rabbi Akiva derive that a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately? Ulla states: from the verse *psilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh* — the carved images of their gods you shall burn in fire. The root *pesel* — to carve — indicates that *mi-she-psalo na'aseh eloheiha*: from the moment it is carved, it assumes the status of a god. Both the identification as a deity and the obligation to destroy it apply from the instant of carving. That is Rabbi Akiva's source for the immediate prohibition of a non-Jew's idol.

### Bitul Concept

Rabbi Yishmael, however, would read that same verse differently. Rav Yosef taught: *minayin l'oveid kochavim she-posel eloho* — where do we know that a non-Jew can invalidate his own idol? From this same verse: *psilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh*. The word *psilei* is read through the root *lifsol* — to invalidate — yielding the principle of *bittul avoda zara*: a non-Jew can *mevattel* his own idol. This is where Rabbi Yishmael locates the source for the *bittul* laws we will encounter in an upcoming Mishna.

The Gemara then notes that Rabbi Akiva derives the *bittul* principle from Shmuel's resolution of a seeming contradiction. One verse says *lo tachmod kesef ve-zahav aleihem* — do not desire the gold and silver on the idols — and another says *ve-lakachta lach* — take it for yourself. Shmuel reconciles them: *psalo l'eloho lo tachmod* — if the idol has been consecrated as a god, do not desire it; *psalo me-eloho ve-lakachta lach* — if he has invalidated it from being a god, then you may take it. Rabbi Akiva thus learns *bittul* from Shmuel's *drashah*, leaving him free to read *psilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh* as the source for the immediate prohibition.

### Vesam Basater

Having established that a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately according to Rabbi Akiva, the Gemara asks where Rabbi Akiva derives the opposite ruling for a Jew — that a Jew's idol is forbidden only once worshipped. Rav Yehudah cites the verse: *arur ha'ish asher ya'aseh pesel umasecha to'avas Hashem vesam basater* — cursed is the man who makes an idol and places it in hiding. Rabbi Akiva reads *vesam basater* as prescriptive and attached to the action of worship: *ad she-ya'aseh lah devarim she-ba-sater* — only once he actually performs those hidden acts of worship upon it does it assume its forbidden status.

Rabbi Yishmael would counter that he does not need this verse for that purpose. Instead, he reads *vesam basater* as the source for a different *din* — that the idol of a Jew requires *genizah*, burial. This is developed via Rav Chisda's teaching, to which we now turn.

---

## Genizah Issue

### Genizah Definition

The question of *genizah* has surfaced several times in the preceding discussion, and it is worth pausing to examine it carefully. The Gemara asks: *minayin l'avodat kochavim shel Yisrael shete'unah genizah* — from where do we know that a Jew's idol requires *genizah*? Two answers are given by the two positions. Rabbi Yishmael derives it from *vesam basater* itself — "place it in hiding" — which is read as a directive: bury it, do not benefit from it. Rav Chisda, whose teaching is used by Rabbi Akiva, derives it from the verse *lo tita lecha asherah kol etz etzel mizbeiach*: do not plant an *asherah* next to God's altar.

### Mizbeach Parallel

The derivation from the *mizbei'ach* proceeds as follows: *mah mizbeiach ta'un genizah* — just as an altar, when its stones become disqualified, requires *genizah* rather than simple disposal, *af asherah te'unah genizah* — so too an *asherah* requires *genizah*. The underlying assumption is that *mizbei'ach* stones are subject to *genizah* by virtue of their *kedushah* as *hekdesh*.

The other opinion, Rabbi Yishmael's, reads the same verse *lo tita lecha asherah* not for the *genizah* principle but for Reish Lakish's teaching: *kol hama'amid dayan she'eino hagun ke'ilu nota asherah be-Yisrael* — one who installs an unfit judge is as though he planted an *asherah* in Israel. The juxtaposition of *shoftim veshotrim titein lecha bechol she'arecha* with *lo tita lecha asherah* teaches that corrupting the justice system is analogous to idolatrous corruption of God's presence in society. Rav Ashi adds that where Torah sages are available and are bypassed for corrupt judges, it is as though the *asherah* were planted *etzel mizbeiach* — right next to the altar.

### Tosfos Reading

The word *genizah* itself, as applied to a Jew's idol, is conceptually striking. Tosfos addresses this directly, explaining that *genizah* in this context does not mean literal burial but rather *bi'ur belo hana'ah* — destruction without benefit, the way the golden calf was ground up and scattered. Tosfos cites *kekli shareit amrinan degnuzah af asherah te'unah genizah*, reading the term as functionally equivalent to the destruction mandated for a non-Jew's idol.

### Conceptual Problem

Even with Tosfos's reading, the conceptual tension here is worth noting. The objects one buries are generally *holy* objects — *hekdesh* that cannot be used but must be respectfully disposed of. To speak of *genizah* for an idol is disorienting. It would seem more intuitive that a Jew's idol — representing a far greater affront to God given the covenantal relationship — should require the most vigorous destruction, while a non-Jew's idol, perhaps, need only be neutralized. The inverted logic is puzzling.

One possible reading is that the language of destruction of idols sits uncomfortably close to the destruction of the people who worship them. In the case of the seven Canaanite nations, *avodat kochavim* and physical destruction of the people went together — and perhaps the Rabbis wished to avoid that violent association when speaking of Jewish idols, even while making them inaccessible. Yet one might equally argue the opposite: for an *ir nidachas* — an apostate city — total destruction is mandated. The point raised in the discussion, that the precedent of Yaakov burying the idols *tachas ha'elah* at Shechem may have informed this concept, is suggestive but leaves the deeper question open.

## Reshaped Vessels

### Tumah Question

Rav Hamnuna raises a question: *reitech kli la'avodat kochavim mahu* — if someone melted down and reshaped a metal vessel to use in idol worship, what is its status? The Gemara immediately clarifies the terms of the question. If the vessel is being reshaped as a *tashmish* for a non-Jew's idol, there is no question — everyone agrees that *tashmishei avoda zara* are not forbidden until actually used in worship, so the mere act of reshaping changes nothing. If the question concerns a *tashmish* for a Jew's idol according to Rabbi Akiva — who holds the idol of a Jew is forbidden only once worshipped — then again there is no question, since even the idol itself is not yet forbidden; how much more so the *tashmish*.

The Gemara narrows the question: it is concerning a *tashmish* for a Jew's idol according to the position that a Jew's idol is forbidden *immediately*. The question is then: does the *tashmish* of a Jewish idol follow the rule for *tashmishei avoda zara* generally — forbidden only once used — or does it follow the status of the idol it accompanies, which is forbidden from the moment it is made? This is a genuine *ibaya* that the Gemara defers.

But the Gemara then notes that if this were Rav Hamnuna's concern, he should simply have asked about a freshly made *tashmish* — why did he specifically raise the case of a reshaped vessel? This leads to the real question.

### Reshaping Principle

The Gemara explains: *Rav Hamnuna mishum tumah yeshana kamba'ei lei* — his question was about *tumah*, specifically the principle of *tumah yeshana*, old impurity. We know from the laws of *kelim* that a metal vessel which breaks becomes *tahor*, and if it is remelted and refashioned, it regains its *tumah* status — *yachzeru l'tumah yeshana*. This is rabbinic in nature. The question is whether the same applies to a vessel that had been used as a *tashmish* of *avoda zara*, which carries a rabbinic *tumah* — if it breaks and is reshaped, does that *tumah* return?

### Unresolved Issues

The Gemara articulates two nested questions: first, does the principle of returning *tumah yeshana* apply to *tumah d'rabbanan* at all, or only to biblical *tumah*? Second, even if *tumah d'rabbanan* generally does not return, would the *tumah* of *avoda zara* be treated more stringently — *mishum chumra d'avodat kochavim mashveiya rabanan k'tumah d'oraita* — and thus return upon reshaping? Both questions remain unresolved: *teiku*.

## Tikrovet Bitul

### Tikrovet Food Tumah

Rabbi Yochanan poses a question to Rabbi Yannai: *tikrovet avodat kochavim shel ochlin mahu* — if food was offered as *tikrovet* to an idol, and a non-Jew subsequently performs *bittul* on the idol, does the *bittul* remove the *tumah* of the food offering? We know that *bittul* removes the *issur* status of the idol itself and renders it *mutar be'hana'ah*. But *bittul* does not remove the *issur* of *tikrovet* — that principle was established earlier. So the question is whether *bittul* can at least remove the *tumah* that attached to the food by virtue of its use as *tikrovet*.

### Why Food

The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yochanan specifies food, not vessels. The reason is that for *keilim*, the question would be simpler: *keivan de'is lehu taharah b'mikvah tumah nami bitlah* — since vessels ordinarily have the capacity to be purified through immersion in a *mikvah*, their *tumah* status is inherently more transient. The connection between *issur* and *tumah* for a vessel is looser. But food cannot be immersed and purified; once it is *tamei*, that status is fixed. This makes the interaction between *bittul* and *tumah* genuinely uncertain for food.

Similarly, asking about the idol itself would also not pose a real question: *keivan d'issura batel tumah nami bitlah* — since the biblical *issur* is removed by *bittul*, the rabbinic *tumah* which depends on that *issur* obviously falls away as well. The *tikrovet* is the hard case: it combines food (whose *tumah* cannot be lifted by immersion) with an *issur* that *bittul* cannot remove.

### Two Sides

The tension, then, is this. On one side: since the *issur* of *tikrovet* does not go away — *keivan d'issura lo batel*, like the teaching of Rav Gidal — perhaps the *tumah* likewise persists. On the other side: the *issur* of *tikrovet* is a Torah-level *issur*, but the *tumah* is only rabbinic — and perhaps *bittul*, even if ineffective against the *d'oraita* prohibition, suffices to lift the rabbinic *tumah* overlay. *Teiku*: the Gemara leaves this unresolved as well.

## Beit Chonyo

### Question Posed

Rav Yosef ben Shmuel poses a question to Rebbi: *keilim she-shimshu bahen b'Beit Chonyo mahu she-ishtamshu bahen b'Beit Hamikdash* — vessels that were used in the Temple of Chonyo in Egypt: may they be repurposed for use in the Temple in Jerusalem? The background is that Chonyo was one of the sons of Shimon HaTzaddik who fled to Egypt and established a temple there. The question of what exactly was practiced there — whether it was genuinely prohibited *avoda zara* or an unauthorized *bamah* where God was worshipped outside the sanctioned Temple — is disputed.

The Gemara clarifies that the question is posed according to the view that *Beit Chonyo* was not a house of *avoda zara*. If it were, the answer would be obvious: vessels used for *avoda zara*, even after *bittul*, cannot be brought into the Temple — that was established earlier. The question is specifically about the ambiguous case where there was no *avoda zara* status, yet the vessels were used in an irregular cultic context.

### Mishnah Rule

The Gemara cites the Mishna: *kohanim she-shimshu b'Beit Chonyo lo yishamshu b'Mikdash she-b'Yerushalayim* — priests who served in *Beit Chonyo* may not serve in the Jerusalem Temple. The Mishna adds *v'eino tzarich lomar l'davar acher* — needless to say, if they were involved in actual *avoda zara*, they certainly cannot serve. But even absent *avoda zara*, the mere act of serving in *Beit Chonyo* disqualifies them. This establishes clearly that *Beit Chonyo* is not treated as *avoda zara*, yet service there carries consequences.

### Punishment Logic

The question therefore becomes: is the disqualification of the *kohanim* a fine levied on persons — *kansinhu rabbanan* — because they are *bnei da'ah*, beings of free will and intellect who made a conscious choice to serve there? If so, the *knas* would apply to them but not to the inanimate vessels, which had no say in where they were used. Or does the stigma attach equally to vessels, which are static and retain their association regardless of agency? There are competing intuitions here: the argument for applying the disqualification only to the *kohanim* is that punishment serves the goal of deterrence and censure directed at persons who chose wrongly. Yet one could equally argue that a person, being dynamic and capable of *teshuva*, should not be permanently defined by past choices — whereas a vessel, which is static, perhaps retains its association more rigidly.

### Rebbi Forbidden

Rebbi responds: *assurin hen* — they are forbidden. He adds, remarkably, *umikra haya b'yadeinu v'shachachnuha* — we had a verse from the Torah that would have demonstrated this, but I have forgotten it. This is a striking admission, all the more so because Rebbi never supplies the forgotten verse; instead, the discussion continues with Rav Yosei ben Shaul attempting to bring a proof, and Rebbi ultimately responding to that proof.

## Temple Stones

### Achaz Vessels

Rav Yosei ben Shaul challenges Rebbi by citing a verse: *kol ha-keilim asher hizniach hamelech Achaz b'malchuto b'ma'alo* — all the vessels that King Achaz had abandoned during his reign in his faithlessness — *hichanu v'hikdashnu*, we have prepared and sanctified them for use in the Temple. This is from the time of Chizkiyahu. Achaz had used Temple vessels for *avoda zara* — vessels that technically could not become *assur* because they belonged to the Temple and not to him — but which were nonetheless profaned. Yet here they appear to have been restored to Temple use through some ritual process. This, argues Rav Yosei ben Shaul, demonstrates that objects used in an inappropriate context, even if not technically forbidden, can be ritually restored and reused.

### Hichanu Hikdashnu

The verse's phrase *hichanu v'hikdashnu* is read by Rav Yosei ben Shaul as: *hichanu* — they were immersed in a *mikvah*, *hikdashnu* — and they were re-sanctified. That is, a ritual process was performed and the vessels were returned to active Temple service. This would serve as a precedent for *Beit Chonyo* vessels as well: perform a ritual, and they may be reused.

### Rebbi Counter

Rebbi responds: *baruch atah l'shamayim shehechzarta li aveidati* — blessed are you before Heaven, for you have returned my lost object! That was precisely the verse I had forgotten. But I read it the exact opposite of you. *Hichanu* does not mean they were immersed for reuse — *shegenazum*, they were put into *genizah*. *Hikdashnu* does not mean they were sanctified for renewed service — *she-hikdashnu acherim tachteiahem*, new vessels were sanctified in their place. The verse, according to Rebbi, proves that those vessels could not be reused and were buried, with replacements made.

### Northeast Chamber

The Gemara then attempts to bring a proof in support of Rebbi from the Mishna: *mizrachit tzonit* — the northeastern chamber of the *Ezrat Nashim* in the Temple. There the *Beit Chashmonai* stored the altar stones that had been defiled by the Greeks. Rav Sheshet explains: *sheshiktzum l'avoda zara* — they had been used for *avoda zara*. Nonetheless they were not destroyed — they were placed in *genizah*. Since the Greeks had no *halachic* power to render Temple property *assur* (it was not theirs to forbid), the stones were technically not prohibited, and yet they were buried rather than reused. This parallels the principle Rebbi applies to the *Beit Chonyo* vessels.

### Pritzim Principle

The Gemara raises a challenge: why couldn't the Hasmoneans simply have a non-Jew perform *bittul* on the stones (thereby removing the *avoda zara* status), break them, and use them freely? The answer lies in a verse cited by Rav Pappa: *u-va'u vah pritzim v'chilluluha* — violators entered and desecrated it. When the non-Jews seized the Temple, this very act of seizure — the fact that they were *pritzim*, violent trespassers who forced their way in — constituted the *chillul*, the desecration. As Rashi explains: *keivan she-nichnesu ovdei kochavim l'heikhal yatzu keilav l'chullin* — once idolaters entered the sanctuary, its vessels lost their sanctity. They became *chullin*. Once they lost their *kedushah*, they could subsequently become genuine *avoda zara* — at which point, even after *bittul*, they could not be brought into the Temple.

This is a *halachically* significant principle: the act of enemy seizure of the Temple is itself the act of desecration, not a subsequent act of *avoda zara*. The Gemara then raises the immediate practical implication: if the vessels lost their sanctity upon the enemy's entry, then after *bittul* they should be freely usable even for private purposes. Why does the Gemara struggle to find a way to use them?

---

## Desecration Broader

### Non-Sanctified Reuse

The Gemara works through the stones case. Once *bittul* is performed, breaking the stones is not possible because *lo tanif aleihem barzel* — iron may not be applied to altar stones. Smoothing them down is likewise prohibited. Yet the stones, in principle, are no longer *hekdesh* and are no longer *avoda zara* after *bittul*. Why then the *genizah*?

### Temple Metals

A parallel case is raised: Rabbi Yoshia had said they sought to forbid all gold and silver vessels in the world — *mishum kaspo v'zahavo shel Yerushalayim* — because of concern that the gold and silver of Jerusalem, the sanctified treasury of the Temple, had been seized by the Romans, melted down, and put into general circulation. If that gold was *hekdesh*, could one inadvertently use it? Abaye clarifies: the concern was not about all gold and silver globally, but specifically about the *dinar Hadriani* and *Turiani* coins whose faces had been worn away — *shayfa* — since they might have originally been Jerusalem coins. Whether the concern is that they were reminted from Temple gold, or that they originally bore a *Yerushalayim* seal whose image had been rubbed out, the worry was about unwitting use of *hekdesh* material.

### Coin Allowance

How was it ultimately permitted to use such coins? *Ad she-matzu lo mikra min ha-Torah she-mutar: u-va'u vah pritzim v'chilluluha* — they found the same verse: when the violators entered and desecrated it. The very act of the Roman conquest removed the sanctity from the Temple treasury. Once the enemy seized it, the gold and silver lost their *kedushah*, and could therefore circulate freely.

### Vessel vs. Coin

But this raises the question: if the *pritzim* verse de-sanctifies everything, why were the altar stones still subject to *genizah* rather than free reuse? The Gemara draws a critical distinction: *hatam lo ishtamshu bahu l'gavoah* — the gold and silver of the Temple treasury were not directly used in the divine service. They were there to fund the purchase of Temple objects; they were not *klei sharet*. *Hacha keivan d'ishtamish bo l'gavoah* — the altar stones, however, were directly used in the divine service. Even after the *kedushah* technically departed, *lav orcha l'ishtamushi b'hedyot* — it is simply not appropriate to use such objects for private, mundane purposes. This is the *genizah* principle in its fullest expression: not a *halachic* prohibition rooted in *issur*, but a posture of reverence that prevents certain objects from descending to ordinary use.

## Bitul Introduction

### Non-Jew Nullifies

The next Mishna begins: *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel chaveiro* — a non-Jew can nullify his own idol and even the idol of another. The second part of this ruling is remarkable: one non-Jew can walk up to another's idol — one he has no ownership over, over which he has exercised no will or intention in setting up — and remove its forbidden status as far as Jews are concerned. Note that the original worshipper presumably continues to regard the object as a god and may continue to worship it; if he does, it would presumably become forbidden again. But in the interval, if a Jew encounters it after *bittul*, it is permitted.

One might ask whether the *mevatel* non-Jew must share the same religious tradition as the idol's owner — could a Saturn worshipper nullify a Zeus worshipper's idol? The Gemara does not appear to draw any such distinction; all non-Jews are grouped together, and any non-Jew may perform *bittul* on any idol. The category is treated as unified.

### Idol vs. Vessels

The Mishna further teaches: *ham'vatel avodat kochavim m'vatel m'sham'sheha* — if a non-Jew performs *bittul* on the idol itself, the *bittul* automatically extends to all its accessories and implements as well, since they are entirely subordinate to the idol. Conversely, *bitel m'sham'sheha m'sham'sheha mutarin v'hi asurah* — if he performs *bittul* only on a particular accessory, that accessory becomes permitted but the idol remains forbidden. *Bittul* of the principal automatically covers the ancillary; *bittul* of the ancillary has no upward effect on the principal.

### Jew Cannot Nullify

The Mishna concludes: *v'Yisrael ein mevatel avodat kochavim shel oved kochavim* — a Jew cannot perform *bittul* on a non-Jew's idol. The reason is not stated in the Mishna but is evident from its practical implications: if a Jew could nullify non-Jewish idols, the entire apparatus of *issurei hana'ah* and destruction obligations would be trivially circumvented.

## Rebbi Opinion Change

### Shimon Question

The Gemara now records an exchange between Rebbi and his son Rabbi Shimon. Rebbi had been teaching the Mishna to Rabbi Shimon: *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel chaveiro* — the standard ruling. Rabbi Shimon responds: *Rebbi, shonita lanu b'yaldutcha* — but when you were younger, you taught us *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel Yisrael* — that a non-Jew can even nullify the idol of a Jew. Has Rebbi changed his position?

### Gemara Response

Before Rebbi can answer, the Gemara intervenes with astonishment: *d'Yisrael mi ka-mevatel?* — wait, can anyone ever have said that a non-Jew may nullify a Jewish idol? The verse says *vesam basater* — it is placed in hiding, permanently inaccessible. The idol of a Jew has an immovable status; *bittul* by a non-Jew should be structurally impossible.

### Joint Ownership

The resolution offered by Rav Hillel son of Rav Vallas turns on the specific scenario that Rebbi's earlier teaching addressed: the idol was jointly owned by a Jew and a non-Jew. In that case, the two stages of Rebbi's opinion are comprehensible.

### Young Rebbi

In his youth, Rebbi held: *Yisrael ada'ata d'akum palach* — when a Jew worships an idol, he does so under the influence and in the context of the non-Jew he is associated with. The idea is something like a *pintele Yid*: Jews are not genuinely committed to idol worship; they are drawn into it through proximity to non-Jewish practice. In this framework, the Jewish partner's attachment to the idol is never truly independent — it flows from, and is defined through, the non-Jewish partner's attachment. Therefore, when the non-Jew performs *bittul* on his own share of the idol, he simultaneously nullifies the Jewish dimension as well, because that dimension was never autonomous: *keivan d'akum mevatel d'nafshei Yisrael nami mevatla*.

### Old Rebbi

In his old age, Rebbi revised this position: *Yisrael ada'ata d'nafshei palach* — a Jew who worships idols does so on his own terms, with his own independent intention and commitment. The idol therefore has an independent status as the idol of a Jew, which cannot be dissolved simply by the non-Jewish partner performing *bittul* on his own share. *Ki ka mevatel avodat kochavim d'nafshei Yisrael lo batel* — when the non-Jew nullifies his portion, he nullifies only his own; the Jewish portion retains its separate, indelible character as the *avodat kochavim shel Yisrael*.
