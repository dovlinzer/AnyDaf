# Avodah Zarah 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yishmael vs. Akiva

### From Accessories to Idols

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְכֵלִים, מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל נׇכְרִי אֵינָהּ אֲסוּרָה אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁתֵּעָבֵד, וְשֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** Since the verse does not apply to places that were themselves worshipped, **apply it** to the **matter of vessels** that were used for idol worship. It is **from here** that the Sages **stated: A gentile's** object of **idol worship is not prohibited until it is worshipped, but a Jew's** object of idol worship is forbidden **immediately.**

Today's daf is 52, and we pick up at the bottom of 51b. The sugya marks a significant transition: having spent considerable time discussing the laws of *tashmishei avoda zara* — the accessories and vessels connected to idol worship — and questions of *issur hana'ah* and *tikrovet*, the Gemara now turns to the core question of when an actual idol becomes forbidden in benefit. The *stam* Mishna had established that the idol of a non-Jew becomes forbidden as soon as it is made, while the idol of a Jew becomes forbidden only once it has been worshipped. The Gemara will now unpack a debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva that turns this Mishna's ruling on its head — and does so in the form of mirror-image positions.

### Mishnah & Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בְּכֵלִים אוֹקֵימְנָא לַהּ! אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם יֹרְשִׁים אֹתָם אֶת אֱלֹהֵיהֶם״, מַקִּישׁ אֱלֹהֵיהֶם לְכֵלִים, מָה כֵּלִים עַד שֶׁיֵּעָבֵדוּ, אַף אֱלֹהֵיהֶם נָמֵי עַד שֶׁיֵּעָבֵדוּ. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּלָא מַקֵּישׁ, אָמַר לָךְ: ״אֶת״ הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions how the *halakha* with regard to an object of idol worship is derived from this verse. **But didn't we interpret this** verse as being stated **with regard to vessels** used in idolatrous worship, and not to an object of idol worship? The Gemara answers: **The verse states:** "You shall destroy all the places, where the nations **that you are to dispossess** served **their gods"** (Deuteronomy 12:2). The verse **juxtaposes "their gods" to** "the places," i.e., the **vessels** used to serve them. **Just as** the **vessels** are not forbidden **until they are** used for **worship, so too their gods,** the idols, are **also** not forbidden **until they are worshipped. And Rabbi Akiva, who does not** consider the terms **juxtaposed,** he could **say to you** that the word **"*et*,"** written in the verse before the term "their gods," **separates the matter** of their gods from the matter of the vessels.

The Gemara opens by asking: *Masnisin mani? Rabbi Akiva hee* — whose view does our Mishna represent? The answer is Rabbi Akiva. A *braisa* is then cited in which the two Tannaim stake out their respective positions. Both Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva agree on one foundational point regarding *tashmishei avoda zara*: the verse in Devarim, *abeid te'abdun es kol hamakomos asher avdu sham hagoyim... es eloheihem*, cannot be read as referring to the physical places of worship — for the same Torah states *eloheihem al heharim*, their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains themselves do not become forbidden. Rather, the verse must be redirected — *tenei inyan l'keilim* — to the vessels and implements used in the worship. And regarding those implements, everyone agrees: *she'ein asurin ad sheye'aveidu*, they are not forbidden until they are actually used in worship.

The dispute concerns what follows from this agreement. Rabbi Yishmael concludes that just as the *tashmishim* are only forbidden once used, so too the idol itself of a non-Jew: *avodas kochavim shel oveid kochavim einah asura ad shete'aveid*, it is not forbidden until it is worshipped. And as its inverse: *shel yisrael asura miyad*, the idol of a Jew is forbidden immediately. Rabbi Akiva — whose view is reflected in the Mishna — holds precisely the opposite: the idol of a non-Jew is forbidden immediately, and the idol of a Jew only once it is worshipped. Both Tannaim agree that Jew and non-Jew are mirror images of one another; they disagree only about which direction the mirror faces.

---

## Yishmael Position

### Vessels Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אַשְׁכְּחַן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל נׇכְרִי דְּאֵין אֲסוּרָה עַד שֶׁתֵּעָבֵד, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל דַּאֲסוּרָה מִיָּד מְנָא לֵיהּ? סְבָרָא הוּא, מִדְּנׇכְרִי עַד שֶׁתֵּעָבֵד, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אֲסוּרָה מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, we found** a source for the *halakha* **that a gentile's** object of **idol worship is not prohibited until it is worshipped. From where does he** derive **that a Jew's** object of idol worship is **prohibited immediately?** The Gemara answers: **It is** based on **logical reasoning. From** the fact that **a gentile's** idol is not forbidden **until it is worshipped,** it stands to reason that **a Jew's** idol is **prohibited immediately.**

The Gemara's first task is to unpack how Rabbi Yishmael infers from the law of *tashmishim* to the idol itself. The *braisa* established that the verse's reference to destroying places of worship is actually directed at the implements used there, since it is followed by *asher atem yorshim osam es eloheihem* — and their gods. The word *nishtamshu* points to actual use: *she'ein asurin ad sheye'aveidu*, they are not forbidden until they are used in worship.

### From Vessels to Idols

The Gemara asks: but we just established that this verse is about *keilim* — the implements. How does Rabbi Yishmael get from there to a ruling about the idol itself? The answer lies in the continuation of the verse: *asher atem yorshim osam es eloheihem*. The phrase connects the implements to the gods — *makish eloheihem l'keilim* — and therefore: *mah keilim ad sheye'avedu af eloheihem nami ad sheye'avedu*. Just as the implements are forbidden only once used in worship, so too the gods themselves are forbidden only once worshipped. That is Rabbi Yishmael's position regarding the idol of a non-Jew.

### Verbal Inference

Rabbi Akiva, however, rejects this verbal linkage. He reads *asher atem yorshim osam es eloheihem* as a new, independent clause — *es hifsik ha'inyan* — and therefore the reference to their gods does not carry the same limitation that applies to the implements. The *tashmishim* are forbidden only once used; the idol itself operates under a different rule. This is the crux of the disagreement.

---

## Akiva Position

### No Vessel Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּלָל וּכְלָל לָא! הַשְׁתָּא גְּנִיזָה בָּעֲיָא, אִיתְּסוֹרֵי לָא מִיתַּסְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why not **say** that **a Jew's** idol **is not** forbidden **at all?** The Gemara answers: **Now,** the status of a Jew's idol cannot be revoked and the idol **requires interment.** Is it possible that **it does not become prohibited?**

With the verbal linkage between implements and idols rejected by Rabbi Akiva, the two Tannaim diverge. Rabbi Akiva will need his own source for why a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately — and Rabbi Yishmael will need his own source for why a Jew's idol is forbidden immediately. Neither can rely on the *hekesh* the other employs.

---

## Yishmael Position…

### Jew Immediate (Logical)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא כִּדְנׇכְרִי! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאֶת חַטַּאתְכֶם אֲשֶׁר עֲשִׂיתֶם אֶת הָעֵגֶל״, מִשְּׁעַת עֲשִׂיָּיה קָם לֵיהּ בְּחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** one could **say** that a Jew's idol is forbidden only once it is worshipped, just **as a gentile's** idol is forbidden only once it is worshipped. The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "And I took your sin, the calf that you had made,** and I burned it with fire" (Deuteronomy 9:21), which indicates that **from** the **time of** its **making** its worshippers were **liable for** the **sin.**

Since Rabbi Yishmael holds that a non-Jew's idol is only forbidden once worshipped, the Gemara asks where he derives the inverse ruling — that a Jew's idol is forbidden immediately. The first answer is *savara*: pure logical reasoning. If a non-Jew's idol requires actual worship to become forbidden, it stands to reason that a Jew's is the inverse.

The Gemara immediately challenges this. Perhaps a Jew's idol is never forbidden at all? The Gemara dismisses this: *hashta geniza ba'ya itzurei lo mitzra* — we already know from a verse that it must be buried; obviously it is forbidden. But then perhaps it operates just like a non-Jew's — only once worshipped? Here the Gemara turns to a verse: *ve'es chataschem asher asisem es ha'egel* — your sin that you made the golden calf. *Mishas asiya kam lei becheit*: the sin occurs from the moment of making. The Gemara anticipates the objection that this establishes only when the person transgresses, not when the object becomes forbidden — *itzurei lo mitzra*. Another verse is therefore adduced: *arur ha'ish asher ya'aseh pesel umasecha to'avas Hashem vesam basater*. The object is called *to'avas Hashem* — an abomination of God — from the moment it is made. And since the object itself bears this label, it is forbidden in *hana'ah* from that moment: *mishas asiya kai be'arur*.

The Gemara again anticipates Rabbi Akiva's counter: he would read *to'avas Hashem* not as a *halachic* declaration of forbidden status but as a religious-descriptive phrase — *davar hameivi lidei to'evah* — meaning the object is an abomination in the sense that it leads toward idol worship, not that it legally assumes the status of *issur hana'ah* at that stage.

## Akiva Position…

### Psilei Eloheihem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״אָרוּר הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה פֶסֶל וּמַסֵּכָה״, מִשְּׁעַת עֲשִׂיָּיה קָם לֵיהּ בְּאָרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why not **say** that **this matter** applies only **with regard to** rendering **the man** who made the idol **liable for** the **sin,** but the object of idol worship **does not become prohibited** until it is worshipped? The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "Cursed be the man who shall make a graven or molten image,** an abomination to the Lord, the work of the hands of the craftsman, and shall set it up in a hidden place" (Deuteronomy 27:15), which indicates that **from** the **time of** its **making** the person who made the idol **is liable to be cursed.**

Where does Rabbi Akiva derive that a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately? Ulla states: from the verse *psilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh* — the carved images of their gods you shall burn in fire. The root *pesel* — to carve — indicates that *mi-she-psalo na'aseh eloheiha*: from the moment it is carved, it assumes the status of a god. Both the identification as a deity and the obligation to destroy it apply from the instant of carving. That is Rabbi Akiva's source for the immediate prohibition of a non-Jew's idol.

### Bitul Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? דָּבָר הַמֵּבִיא לִידֵי תּוֹעֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva,** who does not maintain that a Jew's idol is forbidden from the time that it is made, how does he interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Akiva explains that the term "an abomination" means **an object that leads to abomination** but itself is not considered an abomination before it is worshipped.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל נׇכְרִי דַּאֲסוּרָה מִיָּד מְנָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר עוּלָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״פְּסִילֵי אֱלֹהֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״, מִשֶּׁפִּסְּלוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה אֱלוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the opinion of Rabbi Akiva: **And** as for the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, from where does he** derive **that a gentile's** object of **idol worship** is **prohibited immediately? Ulla said: The verse states: "The graven images of their gods you shall burn with fire;** you shall not covet the silver or the gold that is on them and take it for yourself, lest you be snared thereby, for it is an abomination to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 7:25). The term "graven images" indicates that **from** the time **that** the gentile **engraves** and carves the stone into an idol **it becomes a god** and is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף, דְּתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: מִנַּיִן לְגוֹי שֶׁפּוֹסֵל אֱלוֹהוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פְּסִילֵי אֱלֹהֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yishmael, how does he interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael **requires that** verse **for that which Rav Yosef teaches** in a *baraita*, **as Rav Yosef teaches: From where** is it derived **that a gentile may revoke [*sheposel*]** the status of an object as **his god?** This is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "The graven images of [*pesilei*] their gods you shall burn with fire"** (Deuteronomy 7:25).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דִּשְׁמוּאֵל רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״לֹא תַחְמֹד כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב עֲלֵיהֶם״, וּכְתִיב ״וְלָקַחְתָּ לָךְ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? פִּסְּלוֹ לֶאֱלוֹהַּ — ״לֹא תַחְמֹד״, פְּסָלוֹ מֵאֱלוֹהַּ — ״וְלָקַחְתָּ לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Akiva, from where does he derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Akiva **derives it from** the interpretation **of Shmuel, as Shmuel raises a contradiction: It is written:** "The graven images of [*pesilei*] their gods you shall burn with fire; **you shall not covet the silver or the gold that is on them"** (Deuteronomy 7:25), **and** in the continuation of the verse **it is written: "And take it for yourself,"** indicating that one is permitted to take the silver and gold. **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? If the gentile **engraved** and carved the stone **as a god,** it is immediately rendered forbidden and the prohibition **"you shall not covet"** applies. If the gentile **revoked [*pesalo*]** the idol's status **as a god,** the continuation of the verse: **"And take it for yourself,"** applies.

Rabbi Yishmael, however, would read that same verse differently. Rav Yosef taught: *minayin l'oveid kochavim she-posel eloho* — where do we know that a non-Jew can invalidate his own idol? From this same verse: *psilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh*. The word *psilei* is read through the root *lifsol* — to invalidate — yielding the principle of *bittul avoda zara*: a non-Jew can *mevattel* his own idol. This is where Rabbi Yishmael locates the source for the *bittul* laws we will encounter in an upcoming Mishna.

The Gemara then notes that Rabbi Akiva derives the *bittul* principle from Shmuel's resolution of a seeming contradiction. One verse says *lo tachmod kesef ve-zahav aleihem* — do not desire the gold and silver on the idols — and another says *ve-lakachta lach* — take it for yourself. Shmuel reconciles them: *psalo l'eloho lo tachmod* — if the idol has been consecrated as a god, do not desire it; *psalo me-eloho ve-lakachta lach* — if he has invalidated it from being a god, then you may take it. Rabbi Akiva thus learns *bittul* from Shmuel's *drashah*, leaving him free to read *psilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh* as the source for the immediate prohibition.

### Vesam Basater

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אַשְׁכְּחַן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל נׇכְרִי דַּאֲסוּרָה מִיָּד, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל עַד שֶׁתֵּעָבֵד מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְשָׂם בַּסָּתֶר״, עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה לָהּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּסֵּתֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, we found** a source for the *halakha* **that a gentile's** object of **idol worship** is **prohibited immediately. From where do we** derive that **a Jew's** object of idol worship is not forbidden **until it is worshipped? Rav Yehuda said** that **the verse states:** "Cursed be the man who shall make a graven or molten image…**and shall set it up in a hidden place"** (Deuteronomy 27:15). This indicates that the idol is not rendered forbidden **until** the idolater **performs** in service of the idol those **matters,** i.e., rites, **that are** performed **in a hidden** place.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? הַהוּא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁטְּעוּנָה גְּנִיזָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׂם בַּסָּתֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yishmael, how does he interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael **requires that** verse **for that which Rabbi Yitzḥak** says, **as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that a Jew's** object of **idol worship requires interment?** This is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And shall set it up in a hidden place"** (Deuteronomy 27:15), which Rabbi Yitzḥak interprets as requiring one to inter the idol in a hidden place.

Having established that a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately according to Rabbi Akiva, the Gemara asks where Rabbi Akiva derives the opposite ruling for a Jew — that a Jew's idol is forbidden only once worshipped. Rav Yehudah cites the verse: *arur ha'ish asher ya'aseh pesel umasecha to'avas Hashem vesam basater* — cursed is the man who makes an idol and places it in hiding. Rabbi Akiva reads *vesam basater* as prescriptive and attached to the action of worship: *ad she-ya'aseh lah devarim she-ba-sater* — only once he actually performs those hidden acts of worship upon it does it assume its forbidden status.

Rabbi Yishmael would counter that he does not need this verse for that purpose. Instead, he reads *vesam basater* as the source for a different *din* — that the idol of a Jew requires *genizah*, burial. This is developed via Rav Chisda's teaching, to which we now turn.

---

## Genizah Issue

### Genizah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁטְּעוּנָה גְּנִיזָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תִטַּע לְךָ אֲשֵׁרָה כָּל עֵץ אֵצֶל מִזְבַּח״, מָה מִזְבֵּחַ טָעוּן גְּנִיזָה, אַף אֲשֵׁרָה טְעוּנָה גְּנִיזָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Akiva, from where does he derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Akiva **derives it from** that **which Rav Ḥisda** says that **Rav says, as Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived **that a Jew's** object of **idol worship requires interment?** This is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "You shall not plant for yourself an *ashera* of any kind of tree beside the altar** of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 16:21). The verse juxtaposes an *ashera*, a tree used as part of idolatrous rites, to the altar. This indicates that **just as** the **altar requires interment, so too an *ashera* requires interment.**

The question of *genizah* has surfaced several times in the preceding discussion, and it is worth pausing to examine it carefully. The Gemara asks: *minayin l'avodat kochavim shel Yisrael shete'unah genizah* — from where do we know that a Jew's idol requires *genizah*? Two answers are given by the two positions. Rabbi Yishmael derives it from *vesam basater* itself — "place it in hiding" — which is read as a directive: bury it, do not benefit from it. Rav Chisda, whose teaching is used by Rabbi Akiva, derives it from the verse *lo tita lecha asherah kol etz etzel mizbeiach*: do not plant an *asherah* next to God's altar.

### Mizbeach Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמַּעֲמִיד דַּיָּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן — כְּאִילּוּ נוֹטֵעַ אֲשֵׁרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שֹׁפְטִים וְשֹׁטְרִים תִּתֶּן לְךָ בְּכׇל שְׁעָרֶיךָ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״לֹא תִטַּע לְךָ אֲשֵׁרָה כָּל עֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yishmael, how does he interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael **requires that** verse **for that which Reish Lakish** says, **as Reish Lakish says: Anyone who appoints** over the community **a judge who is unfit** for the position, due to his lack of knowledge or wickedness, is considered **as though he plants an *ashera* among the Jewish people, as it is stated: "Judges and officers you shall make for yourself in all of your gates"** (Deuteronomy 16:18), **and juxtaposed to it** is the verse: **"You shall not plant for yourself an *ashera* of any kind of tree** beside the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 16:21).

The derivation from the *mizbei'ach* proceeds as follows: *mah mizbeiach ta'un genizah* — just as an altar, when its stones become disqualified, requires *genizah* rather than simple disposal, *af asherah te'unah genizah* — so too an *asherah* requires *genizah*. The underlying assumption is that *mizbei'ach* stones are subject to *genizah* by virtue of their *kedushah* as *hekdesh*.

The other opinion, Rabbi Yishmael's, reads the same verse *lo tita lecha asherah* not for the *genizah* principle but for Reish Lakish's teaching: *kol hama'amid dayan she'eino hagun ke'ilu nota asherah be-Yisrael* — one who installs an unfit judge is as though he planted an *asherah* in Israel. The juxtaposition of *shoftim veshotrim titein lecha bechol she'arecha* with *lo tita lecha asherah* teaches that corrupting the justice system is analogous to idolatrous corruption of God's presence in society. Rav Ashi adds that where Torah sages are available and are bypassed for corrupt judges, it is as though the *asherah* were planted *etzel mizbeiach* — right next to the altar.

### Tosfos Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וּבִמְקוֹם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, כְּאִילּוּ נְטָעוֹ אֵצֶל מִזְבֵּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵצֶל מִזְבַּח״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: And** if one appoints an unsuitable individual as a judge **in a place** where there are **Torah scholars,** it is **as though he planted** an *ashera* **next to** the **altar, as it is stated: "Beside the altar** of the Lord your God."

The word *genizah* itself, as applied to a Jew's idol, is conceptually striking. Tosfos addresses this directly, explaining that *genizah* in this context does not mean literal burial but rather *bi'ur belo hana'ah* — destruction without benefit, the way the golden calf was ground up and scattered. Tosfos cites *kekli shareit amrinan degnuzah af asherah te'unah genizah*, reading the term as functionally equivalent to the destruction mandated for a non-Jew's idol.

### Conceptual Problem

Even with Tosfos's reading, the conceptual tension here is worth noting. The objects one buries are generally *holy* objects — *hekdesh* that cannot be used but must be respectfully disposed of. To speak of *genizah* for an idol is disorienting. It would seem more intuitive that a Jew's idol — representing a far greater affront to God given the covenantal relationship — should require the most vigorous destruction, while a non-Jew's idol, perhaps, need only be neutralized. The inverted logic is puzzling.

One possible reading is that the language of destruction of idols sits uncomfortably close to the destruction of the people who worship them. In the case of the seven Canaanite nations, *avodat kochavim* and physical destruction of the people went together — and perhaps the Rabbis wished to avoid that violent association when speaking of Jewish idols, even while making them inaccessible. Yet one might equally argue the opposite: for an *ir nidachas* — an apostate city — total destruction is mandated. The point raised in the discussion, that the precedent of Yaakov burying the idols *tachas ha'elah* at Shechem may have informed this concept, is suggestive but leaves the deeper question open.

## Reshaped Vessels

### Tumah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב הַמְנוּנָא: רִיתֵּךְ כְּלִי לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, מַהוּ? עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּנׇכְרִי, בֵּין לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וּבֵין לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְשַׁמְּשֵׁי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הֵן, וּמְשַׁמְּשֵׁי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֵין אֲסוּרִין עַד שֶׁיֵּעָבֵדוּ! אֶלָּא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Hamnuna raises a dilemma:** If one **welded [*ritekh*]** a broken **vessel for idol worship, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara asks: With regard to **whose idol worship** does Rav Hamnuna raise the dilemma? **If we say** that Rav Hamnuna is referring to **a gentile's idol worship,** that is difficult, as **both according to Rabbi Yishmael and according to Rabbi Akiva** vessels used in idolatrous worship **are** considered **accessories of idol worship, and accessories of idol worship are not prohibited until they are** used for **worship. Rather,** Rav Hamnuna is referring to **a Jews' idol worship.**

Rav Hamnuna raises a question: *reitech kli la'avodat kochavim mahu* — if someone melted down and reshaped a metal vessel to use in idol worship, what is its status? The Gemara immediately clarifies the terms of the question. If the vessel is being reshaped as a *tashmish* for a non-Jew's idol, there is no question — everyone agrees that *tashmishei avoda zara* are not forbidden until actually used in worship, so the mere act of reshaping changes nothing. If the question concerns a *tashmish* for a Jew's idol according to Rabbi Akiva — who holds the idol of a Jew is forbidden only once worshipped — then again there is no question, since even the idol itself is not yet forbidden; how much more so the *tashmish*.

The Gemara narrows the question: it is concerning a *tashmish* for a Jew's idol according to the position that a Jew's idol is forbidden *immediately*. The question is then: does the *tashmish* of a Jewish idol follow the rule for *tashmishei avoda zara* generally — forbidden only once used — or does it follow the status of the idol it accompanies, which is forbidden from the moment it is made? This is a genuine *ibaya* that the Gemara defers.

### Reshaping Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַשְׁתָּא הִיא גּוּפַהּ לָא מִיתַּסְרָא עַד שֶׁתֵּעָבֵד, מְשַׁמְּשֶׁיהָ מִיבַּעְיָא? וְאֶלָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּאָמַר: ״אֲסוּרָה מִיָּד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion does Rav Hamnuna raise the dilemma? **If we say** that he raises the dilemma **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva,** this is difficult. **Now** that the idol **itself is not prohibited until it is worshipped, is** it **necessary** to state that **its accessories** are not forbidden until they are used for idol worship? **Rather,** perhaps Rav Hamnuna raises the dilemma **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that a Jew's idol is **prohibited immediately.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְשַׁמְּשִׁין? מְמַשְׁמְשִׁין גָּמְרִינַן, מָה הָתָם עַד שֶׁיֵּעָבֵדוּ, אַף הָכָא עַד שֶׁיֵּעָבֵדוּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא מִינַּהּ גָּמַר, מָה הִיא אֲסוּרָה מִיָּד, אַף מְשַׁמְּשֶׁיהָ אֲסוּרִין מִיָּד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests an explanation of the dilemma: **What** is the *halakha* with regard to a Jew's accessories of idol worship? Do **we learn** the *halakha* with regard to a Jew's **accessories from** the *halakha* with regard to a gentile's **accessories? Just as there,** in the case of a gentile's accessories of idol worship, they are not forbidden **until they are** used for idol **worship, so too here,** in the case of a Jew's accessories of idol worship, they are not forbidden **until they are** used for idol **worship. Or perhaps** the *halakha* **is learned from** the *halakha* with regard to a Jew's object of idol worship **itself. Just as** the idol **itself is prohibited immediately, so too its accessories are prohibited immediately.**

The Gemara explains: *Rav Hamnuna mishum tumah yeshana kamba'ei lei* — his question was about *tumah*, specifically the principle of *tumah yeshana*, old impurity. We know from the laws of *kelim* that a metal vessel which breaks becomes *tahor*, and if it is remelted and refashioned, it regains its *tumah* status — *yachzeru l'tumah yeshana*. This is rabbinic in nature. The question is whether the same applies to a vessel that had been used as a *tashmish* of *avoda zara*, which carries a rabbinic *tumah* — if it breaks and is reshaped, does that *tumah* return?

### Unresolved Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא דְּקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ רִיתֵּךְ כְּלִי? תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ עָשָׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: If that is the explanation of Rav Hamnuna's dilemma, **why does he specifically raise the dilemma** with regard to one who **welded** a broken **vessel? Let him raise the dilemma** with regard to one who **fashioned** a vessel for idolatrous worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הַמְנוּנָא מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה יְשָׁנָה קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: כְּלֵי מַתָּכוֹת, פְּשׁוּטֵיהֶן וּמְקַבְּלֵיהֶן טְמֵאִים, נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ טָהֵרוּ, חָזַר וַעֲשָׂאָן כֵּלִים — יַחְזְרוּ לְטוּמְאָה יְשָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Hamnuna** is referring to a vessel that was used for idol worship before it broke, and **he is raising the dilemma with regard to** the matter of **previous ritual impurity. As we learned** in a mishna (*Kelim* 11:1): With regard to **metal vessels,** both **their flat** vessels, which have no repositories, **and their receptacles,** vessels that have repositories, **are** all **impure** if they came into contact with a primary source of ritual impurity. If **they broke, they** thereby **became purified.** But if one **remade** the broken vessels into new **vessels, they reassume their previous impurity.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: כִּי הָדְרָא טוּמְאָה — הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְטוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲבָל טוּמְאָה דְּרַבָּנַן — לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ שְׁאָר טוּמְאוֹת דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** is **the dilemma** that Rav Hamnuna **is raising: When** the mishna teaches that a vessel that is remade **reassumes** its **impurity,** does **this matter** apply only **to impurity by Torah law, but** in the case of **impurity by rabbinic law,** such as the impurity of an object of idol worship, it does **not** apply? **Or perhaps** there **is no difference** between impurity by Torah law and impurity by rabbinic law. The Gemara asks: If that is the explanation of Rav Hamnuna's dilemma, why does he specifically discuss a vessel used for idol worship? **Let him raise the dilemma** with regard to any **other** type of **impurity by rabbinic law.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, טוּמְאָה דְּרַבָּנַן מִי הָדְרָא אוֹ לָא הָדְרָא? וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר לָא הָדְרָא, טוּמְאָה דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מִשּׁוּם חוּמְרָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מִי שַׁוְּיוּהָ רַבָּנַן כְּטוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אוֹ לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Hamnuna **is raising** two **dilemmas, one** of which stems **from** the **other. Is impurity by rabbinic law reassumed, or** is it **not reassumed? And if you say** that it is **not reassumed,** what is the *halakha* with regard to the **impurity of** an object of **idol worship? Did the Sages render** its status **like** that of **impurity by Torah law, due to** the **stringency of idol worship, or not?** The Gemara concludes: The dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara articulates two nested questions: first, does the principle of returning *tumah yeshana* apply to *tumah d'rabbanan* at all, or only to biblical *tumah*? Second, even if *tumah d'rabbanan* generally does not return, would the *tumah* of *avoda zara* be treated more stringently — *mishum chumra d'avodat kochavim mashveiya rabanan k'tumah d'oraita* — and thus return upon reshaping? Both questions remain unresolved: *teiku*.

## Tikrovet Bitul

### Tikrovet Food Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יַנַּאי: תִּקְרוֹבֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל אוֹכָלִים, מַהוּ? מִי מַהֲנֵיא לְהוּ בִּיטּוּל לְטַהוֹרִינְהוּ מִטּוּמְאָה, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised a dilemma to Rabbi Yannai:** With regard to **an offering** consisting **of food** brought in **idolatrous worship, what is** the *halakha*? **Is** the **revocation** of their status as an object of idol worship by a gentile **effective to purify them from** the **ritual impurity** of an offering brought in idolatrous worship **or** is it **not** effective?

Rabbi Yochanan poses a question to Rabbi Yannai: *tikrovet avodat kochavim shel ochlin mahu* — if food was offered as *tikrovet* to an idol, and a non-Jew subsequently performs *bittul* on the idol, does the *bittul* remove the *tumah* of the food offering? We know that *bittul* removes the *issur* status of the idol itself and renders it *mutar be'hana'ah*. But *bittul* does not remove the *issur* of *tikrovet* — that principle was established earlier. So the question is whether *bittul* can at least remove the *tumah* that attached to the food by virtue of its use as *tikrovet*.

### Why Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ כֵּלִים! כֵּלִים לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לְהוּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה — טוּמְאָה נָמֵי בָּטְלָה; כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ — אֳוכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him raise the dilemma** with regard to whether revocation of vessels' status purifies **vessels** used in idol worship from their impurity. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yoḥanan **does not raise the dilemma** with regard to **vessels, since they have** the ability to attain **purity** by being immersed **in a ritual bath,** and therefore their **impurity** can certainly **also be nullified. When he raises the dilemma,** it is only with regard to **food,** which cannot be purified in a ritual bath.

The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yochanan specifies food, not vessels. The reason is that for *keilim*, the question would be simpler: *keivan de'is lehu taharah b'mikvah tumah nami bitlah* — since vessels ordinarily have the capacity to be purified through immersion in a *mikvah*, their *tumah* status is inherently more transient. The connection between *issur* and *tumah* for a vessel is looser. But food cannot be immersed and purified; once it is *tamei*, that status is fixed. This makes the interaction between *bittul* and *tumah* genuinely uncertain for food.

Similarly, asking about the idol itself would also not pose a real question: *keivan d'issura batel tumah nami bitlah* — since the biblical *issur* is removed by *bittul*, the rabbinic *tumah* which depends on that *issur* obviously falls away as well. The *tikrovet* is the hard case: it combines food (whose *tumah* cannot be lifted by immersion) with an *issur* that *bittul* cannot remove.

### Two Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה גּוּפַהּ! עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה גּוּפַהּ לָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיסּוּרַהּ בָּטֵיל, טוּמְאָה נָמֵי בָּטְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let him raise the dilemma** with regard to whether revoking its status purifies the object of **idol worship itself** in a case where it consists of food. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yoḥanan **does not raise the dilemma** with regard to the object of **idol worship itself,** as, **since its prohibition is nullified,** its **impurity is also nullified.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ תִּקְרוֹבֶת לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל אוֹכְלִין, מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּאִיסּוּרֵיהּ לָא בָּטֵיל, כִּדְרַב גִּידֵּל, טוּמְאָה נָמֵי לָא בָּטְלָה, אוֹ דִלְמָא אִיסּוּר דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא בָּטֵיל, טוּמְאָה דְּרַבָּנַן בָּטֵיל? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** When he raises the dilemma,** it is only with regard to **an offering** consisting **of food** brought **in idolatrous worship. What** is the *halakha*? Does one say that **since its prohibition is not nullified, in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Giddel,** who teaches that the prohibition that takes effect with regard to offerings brought in idolatrous worship is never nullified, therefore the **impurity is also not nullified? Or perhaps** only their **prohibition,** which is **by Torah law, is not nullified,** but their **impurity,** which is **by rabbinic law, is nullified.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The tension, then, is this. On one side: since the *issur* of *tikrovet* does not go away — *keivan d'issura lo batel*, like the teaching of Rav Gidal — perhaps the *tumah* likewise persists. On the other side: the *issur* of *tikrovet* is a Torah-level *issur*, but the *tumah* is only rabbinic — and perhaps *bittul*, even if ineffective against the *d'oraita* prohibition, suffices to lift the rabbinic *tumah* overlay. *Teiku*: the Gemara leaves this unresolved as well.

## Beit Chonyo

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל מֵרַבִּי: כֵּלִים שֶׁשִּׁימְּשׁוּ בָּהֶן בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּשְׁתַּמְּשׁוּ בָּהֶן בְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul asked Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: With regard to **vessels that were used in the temple of Onias, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to using them in the Temple?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בֵּית חוֹנְיוֹ לָאו בֵּית עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הִיא, דִּתְנַן: כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁשִּׁימְּשׁוּ בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ לֹא יְשַׁמְּשׁוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And he raised this dilemma according to** the opinion of **the one who says** that **the temple of Onias was not a temple of idol worship. As we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 109a): **Priests who served in the temple of Onias may not serve in the Temple that is in Jerusalem, and needless to say,** those priests who served in a temple **of something else,** i.e., idol worship, may not serve in the Temple in Jerusalem. This distinction indicates that the temple of Onias was not a temple of idol worship. It was used for the service of God, but it violated the prohibition against sacrificing offerings outside of the Temple in Jerusalem.

The background is that Chonyo was one of the sons of Shimon HaTzaddik who fled to Egypt and established a temple there. The question of what exactly was practiced there — whether it was genuinely prohibited *avoda zara* or an unauthorized *bamah* where God was worshipped outside the sanctioned Temple — is disputed.

The Gemara clarifies that the question is posed according to the view that *Beit Chonyo* was not a house of *avoda zara*. If it were, the answer would be obvious: vessels used for *avoda zara*, even after *bittul*, cannot be brought into the Temple — that was established earlier. The question is specifically about the ambiguous case where there was no *avoda zara* status, yet the vessels were used in an irregular cultic context.

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּהֲנִים הוּא דְּקַנְסִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן, מִשּׁוּם דִּבְנֵי דֵעָה נִינְהוּ, אֲבָל כֵּלִים — לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: Does one say that **it is** only the **priests that the Sages penalized** by preventing them from serving in the Temple, **as they possess awareness** and are responsible for their actions, **but** they did **not** institute a similar decree with regard to **vessels,** as they are inanimate? **Or perhaps** there **is no difference,** and the decree also applies to the vessels.

The Mishna teaches: *kohanim she-shimshu b'Beit Chonyo lo yishamshu b'Mikdash she-b'Yerushalayim* — priests who served in *Beit Chonyo* may not serve in the Jerusalem Temple. The Mishna adds *v'eino tzarich lomar l'davar acher* — needless to say, if they were involved in actual *avoda zara*, they certainly cannot serve. But even absent *avoda zara*, the mere act of serving in *Beit Chonyo* disqualifies them. This establishes clearly that *Beit Chonyo* is not treated as *avoda zara*, yet service there carries consequences.

The question therefore becomes: is the disqualification of the *kohanim* a fine levied on persons — *kansinhu rabbanan* — because they are *bnei da'ah*, beings of free will and intellect who made a conscious choice to serve there? If so, the *knas* would apply to them but not to the inanimate vessels, which had no say in where they were used. Or does the stigma attach equally to vessels, which are static and retain their association regardless of agency? There are competing intuitions here: the argument for applying the disqualification only to the *kohanim* is that punishment serves the goal of deterrence and censure directed at persons who chose wrongly. Yet one could equally argue that a person, being dynamic and capable of *teshuva*, should not be permanently defined by past choices — whereas a vessel, which is static, perhaps retains its association more rigidly.

### Rebbi Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אֲסוּרִים הֵן, וּמִקְרָא הָיָה בְּיָדֵינוּ וּשְׁכַחְנוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul: The vessels that were used in the temple of Onias **are prohibited, and we possessed** knowledge of **a verse** from which this *halakha* was derived, **but we forgot** which verse **it** is.

Rebbi responds: *assurin hen* — they are forbidden. He adds, remarkably, *umikra haya b'yadeinu v'shachachnuha* — we had a verse from the Torah that would have demonstrated this, but I have forgotten it. This is a striking admission, all the more so because Rebbi never supplies the forgotten verse; instead, the discussion continues with Rav Yosei ben Shaul attempting to bring a proof, and Rebbi ultimately responding to that proof.

## Temple Stones

### Achaz Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״כׇּל הַכֵּלִים אֲשֶׁר הִזְנִיחַ הַמֶּלֶךְ אָחָז בְּמַלְכוּתוֹ בְּמַעֲלוֹ הֵכַנּוּ וְהִקְדָּשְׁנוּ״, מַאי לָאו ״הֵכַנּוּ״ — דְּאַטְבֵּלִינַּנְהוּ, ״הִקְדָּשְׁנוּ״ — דְּאַקְדֵּישִׁנַּנְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul **raised an objection to** this ruling from a verse referring to vessels of the Temple that Ahaz had used for idol worship: **"All the vessels, which King Ahaz in his reign did cast away when he acted treacherously, we have prepared and sanctified,** and, behold, they are before the altar of the Lord" (II Chronicles 29:19). **What, is it not** the case that the term **"we have prepared"** means **that we immersed them** in a ritual bath, and the term **"sanctified"** means **that we sanctified them** to be used again in the Temple? This would indicate that the vessels may be used in the Temple even though they were used in idolatrous worship.

Rav Yosei ben Shaul challenges Rebbi by citing a verse: *kol ha-keilim asher hizniach hamelech Achaz b'malchuto b'ma'alo* — all the vessels that King Achaz had abandoned during his reign in his faithlessness — *hichanu v'hikdashnu*, we have prepared and sanctified them for use in the Temple. This is from the time of Chizkiyahu. Achaz had used Temple vessels for *avoda zara* — vessels that technically could not become *assur* because they belonged to the Temple and not to him — but which were nonetheless profaned. Yet here they appear to have been restored to Temple use through some ritual process. This, argues Rav Yosei ben Shaul, demonstrates that objects used in an inappropriate context, even if not technically forbidden, can be ritually restored and reused.

### Hichanu Hikdashnu

The verse's phrase *hichanu v'hikdashnu* is read by Rav Yosei ben Shaul as: *hichanu* — they were immersed in a *mikvah*, *hikdashnu* — and they were re-sanctified. That is, a ritual process was performed and the vessels were returned to active Temple service. This would serve as a precedent for *Beit Chonyo* vessels as well: perform a ritual, and they may be reused.

### Rebbi Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה לַשָּׁמַיִם שֶׁהֶחְזַרְתָּ לִי אֲבֵדָתִי. ״הֵכַנּוּ״ — שֶׁגְּנַזְנוּם, וְ״הִקְדָּשְׁנוּ״ — שֶׁהִקְדַּשְׁנוּ אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul: **Blessed are you to Heaven, as you have returned my lost** verse **to me.** That verse is the forgotten source of the *halakha* that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi stated, and it should be interpreted as follows: **"We have prepared"** means **that we interred them; "and sanctified"** means **that we sanctified other** vessels **in their stead,** as the vessels used by Ahaz were forbidden, and the same applies to the vessels from the temple of Onias.

Rebbi responds: *baruch atah l'shamayim shehechzarta li aveidati* — blessed are you before Heaven, for you have returned my lost object! That was precisely the verse I had forgotten. But I read it the exact opposite of you. *Hichanu* does not mean they were immersed for reuse — *shegenazum*, they were put into *genizah*. *Hikdashnu* does not mean they were sanctified for renewed service — *she-hikdashnu acherim tachteiahem*, new vessels were sanctified in their place. The verse, according to Rebbi, proves that those vessels could not be reused and were buried, with replacements made.

### Northeast Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית — בָּהּ גָּנְזוּ בֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי אֶת אַבְנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁשִּׁקְּצוּ אַנְשֵׁי יָוָן, וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: שֶׁשִּׁקְּצוּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the mishna **supports** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion (*Middot* 1:6): The **northeast** chamber of the Chamber of the Hearth was the chamber **in which the Hasmoneans sequestered the altar stones that the people of Greece desecrated. And Rav Sheshet says:** This means **that** they **desecrated** the stones by using them **for idol worship.** This indicates that Temple vessels that were used in idolatrous worship can no longer be used and must be sequestered.

The Gemara then attempts to bring a proof in support of Rebbi from the Mishna: *mizrachit tzonit* — the northeastern chamber of the *Ezrat Nashim* in the Temple. There the *Beit Chashmonai* stored the altar stones that had been defiled by the Greeks. Rav Sheshet explains: *sheshiktzum l'avoda zara* — they had been used for *avoda zara*. Nonetheless they were not destroyed — they were placed in *genizah*. Since the Greeks had no *halachic* power to render Temple property *assur* (it was not theirs to forbid), the stones were technically not prohibited, and yet they were buried rather than reused. This parallels the principle Rebbi applies to the *Beit Chonyo* vessels.

### Pritzim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָתָם קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וּדְרַשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבָאוּ בָהּ פָּרִיצִים וְחִלְּלוּהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** That case of the altar's stones is different, because **there** a Sage **found a verse and interpreted it homiletically, as it is written:** "And they shall profane My secret place, **and robbers shall enter into it and profane it"** (Ezekiel 7:22). The verse indicates that when the gentiles entered the Temple and desecrated it, the altar was desacralized and acquired non-sacred status. Consequently, when the stones were subsequently used for idolatrous worship they were rendered forbidden even for non-sacred use.

The Gemara raises a challenge: why couldn't the Hasmoneans simply have a non-Jew perform *bittul* on the stones (thereby removing the *avoda zara* status), break them, and use them freely? The answer lies in a verse cited by Rav Pappa: *u-va'u vah pritzim v'chilluluha* — violators entered and desecrated it. When the non-Jews seized the Temple, this very act of seizure — the fact that they were *pritzim*, violent trespassers who forced their way in — constituted the *chillul*, the desecration. As Rashi explains: *keivan she-nichnesu ovdei kochavim l'heikhal yatzu keilav l'chullin* — once idolaters entered the sanctuary, its vessels lost their sanctity. They became *chullin*. Once they lost their *kedushah*, they could subsequently become genuine *avoda zara* — at which point, even after *bittul*, they could not be brought into the Temple.

This is a *halachically* significant principle: the act of enemy seizure of the Temple is itself the act of desecration, not a subsequent act of *avoda zara*. The Gemara then raises the immediate practical implication: if the vessels lost their sanctity upon the enemy's entry, then after *bittul* they should be freely usable even for private purposes. Why does the Gemara struggle to find a way to use them?

## Desecration Broader

### Non-Sanctified Reuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נִיתְבְּרִינְהוּ? ״אֲבָנִים שְׁלֵמוֹת״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא! נְנַסְּרִינְהוּ? ״לֹא תָנִיף עֲלֵיהֶם בַּרְזֶל״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why the altar's stones were sequestered: The Hasmoneans **said: What should we do** to revoke the stone's forbidden status? **Shall we break them?** That is not possible, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: "You shall build the altar of the Lord your God of **unhewn stones"** (Deuteronomy 27:6). **Shall we saw them** without breaking them? That is also not a viable option, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"You shall lift up no iron tool upon them"** (Deuteronomy 27:5). There was therefore no alternative to sequestering the stones.

The Gemara works through the stones case. Once *bittul* is performed, breaking the stones is not possible because *lo tanif aleihem barzel* — iron may not be applied to altar stones. Smoothing them down is likewise prohibited. Yet the stones, in principle, are no longer *hekdesh* and are no longer *avoda zara* after *bittul*. Why then the *genizah*?

### Temple Metals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? לִיתְבְּרִינְהוּ וְלִישְׁקְלִינְהוּ לְנַפְשַׁיְיהוּ! מִי לָא אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״בִּקְּשׁוּ לִגְנוֹז כׇּל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם מִשּׁוּם כַּסְפָּא וְדַהֲבָא שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״, וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: יְרוּשָׁלַיִם הָוְיָא רוּבָּא דְּעָלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** did the Hasmoneans have no alternative? **Let them break** the stones **and take them for themselves. Didn't Rav Oshaya say:** The Sages **wished to sequester all** of the **silver and gold in the world because of** the **silver and gold of Jerusalem,** most of which was consecrated to the Temple treasury and became mixed with other silver and gold. **And we discussed it** and asked: **Is Jerusalem the majority of the world** that all of the silver and gold of the world should be forbidden as perhaps it came from Jerusalem?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בִּקְּשׁוּ לִגְנוֹז דִּינָרָא הַדְרְיָיאנָא טוּרְיָינָא שִׁיפָא, מִפְּנֵי טִבְעָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, עַד שֶׁמָּצְאוּ לָהּ מִקְרָא מִן הַתּוֹרָה שֶׁהוּא מוּתָּר, ״וּבָאוּ בָהּ פָּרִיצִים וְחִלְּלוּהָ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** The Sages **wished to sequester all** of the **worn-out dinars of Hadrian and Trajan because of the coins of Jerusalem,** as it was known that these coins contained a large quantity of the Temple treasury's gold and silver. They did not permit the use of these coins **until they found a verse in the Torah** indicating **that it is permitted: "And robbers shall enter into it, and profane it"** (Ezekiel 7:22).

A parallel case is raised: Rabbi Yoshia had said they sought to forbid all gold and silver vessels in the world — *mishum kaspo v'zahavo shel Yerushalayim* — because of concern that the gold and silver of Jerusalem, the sanctified treasury of the Temple, had been seized by the Romans, melted down, and put into general circulation. If that gold was *hekdesh*, could one inadvertently use it? Abaye clarifies: the concern was not about all gold and silver globally, but specifically about the *dinar Hadriani* and *Turiani* coins whose faces had been worn away — *shayfa* — since they might have originally been Jerusalem coins. Whether the concern is that they were reminted from Temple gold, or that they originally bore a *Yerushalayim* seal whose image had been rubbed out, the worry was about unwitting use of *hekdesh* material.

How was it ultimately permitted to use such coins? *Ad she-matzu lo mikra min ha-Torah she-mutar: u-va'u vah pritzim v'chilluluha* — they found the same verse: when the violators entered and desecrated it. The very act of the Roman conquest removed the sanctity from the Temple treasury. Once the enemy seized it, the gold and silver lost their *kedushah*, and could therefore circulate freely.

### Vessel vs. Coin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, לָא אִשְׁתַּמַּשׁוּ בְּהוּ לְגָבוֹהַּ; הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִשְׁתַּמַּשׁ בְּהוּ לְגָבוֹהַּ — לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹשֵׁי בְּהוּ הֶדְיוֹטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The two cases are not comparable. **There,** the coins of Jerusalem **had not been used in** service of the **Most High;** they were only consecrated. **Here, since** the stones of the altar **had been used in** service of the **Most High,** it is **not proper conduct for an ordinary person to make use of them,** and therefore the Hasmoneans sequestered the stones.

But this raises the question: if the *pritzim* verse de-sanctifies everything, why were the altar stones still subject to *genizah* rather than free reuse? The Gemara draws a critical distinction: *hatam lo ishtamshu bahu l'gavoah* — the gold and silver of the Temple treasury were not directly used in the divine service. They were there to fund the purchase of Temple objects; they were not *klei sharet*. *Hacha keivan d'ishtamish bo l'gavoah* — the altar stones, however, were directly used in the divine service. Even after the *kedushah* technically departed, *lav orcha l'ishtamushi b'hedyot* — it is simply not appropriate to use such objects for private, mundane purposes. This is the *genizah* principle in its fullest expression: not a *halachic* prohibition rooted in *issur*, but a posture of reverence that prevents certain objects from descending to ordinary use.

## Bitul Introduction

### Non-Jew Nullifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נׇכְרִי מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלּוֹ, וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל נׇכְרִי. הַמְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — מְבַטֵּל מְשַׁמְּשֶׁיהָ, בִּיטֵּל מְשַׁמְּשֶׁיהָ — מְשַׁמְּשִׁין מוּתָּרִין, וְהִיא אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A gentile can revoke** the status of **his** object of **idol worship and** the status of the idol **of another** gentile, **but a Jew cannot revoke** the status of the object of **idol worship of a gentile. One who revokes** the status of an object of **idol worship** thereby **revokes** the status of **its accessories.** But if he **revokes** the status of **its accessories,** its **accessories** alone are rendered **permitted, but** the object of idol worship **itself** remains **prohibited.**

The next Mishna begins: *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel chaveiro* — a non-Jew can nullify his own idol and even the idol of another. The second part of this ruling is remarkable: one non-Jew can walk up to another's idol — one he has no ownership over, over which he has exercised no will or intention in setting up — and remove its forbidden status as far as Jews are concerned. Note that the original worshipper presumably continues to regard the object as a god and may continue to worship it; if he does, it would presumably become forbidden again. But in the interval, if a Jew encounters it after *bittul*, it is permitted.

One might ask whether the *mevatel* non-Jew must share the same religious tradition as the idol's owner — could a Saturn worshipper nullify a Zeus worshipper's idol? The Gemara does not appear to draw any such distinction; all non-Jews are grouped together, and any non-Jew may perform *bittul* on any idol. The category is treated as unified.

### Idol vs. Vessels

The Mishna further teaches: *ham'vatel avodat kochavim m'vatel m'sham'sheha* — if a non-Jew performs *bittul* on the idol itself, the *bittul* automatically extends to all its accessories and implements as well, since they are entirely subordinate to the idol. Conversely, *bitel m'sham'sheha m'sham'sheha mutarin v'hi asurah* — if he performs *bittul* only on a particular accessory, that accessory becomes permitted but the idol remains forbidden. *Bittul* of the principal automatically covers the ancillary; *bittul* of the ancillary has no upward effect on the principal.

### Jew Cannot Nullify

The Mishna concludes: *v'Yisrael ein mevatel avodat kochavim shel oved kochavim* — a Jew cannot perform *bittul* on a non-Jew's idol. The reason is not stated in the Mishna but is evident from its practical implications: if a Jew could nullify non-Jewish idols, the entire apparatus of *issurei hana'ah* and destruction obligations would be trivially circumvented.

## Rebbi Opinion Change

### Shimon Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי: נׇכְרִי מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, שָׁנִיתָ לָנוּ בְּיַלְדוּתֶךָ נׇכְרִי מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מִי קָא מְבַטְּלָהּ? וְהָא ״וְשָׂם בַּסָּתֶר״ כְּתִיב! אָמַר רַבִּי הִילֵּל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי וָולֶס: לֹא נִצְרְכָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the following version of the mishna: **A gentile can revoke** the status of **his** object of **idol worship and** the status of the idol **of another** gentile. Rabbi Shimon **said to him: My teacher, in your youth,** i.e., when you were younger, **you taught us** a different version of the mishna: **A gentile can revoke** the status of **his** object of **idol worship and** the status of the object of idol worship **of a Jew.** The Gemara asks: **Can** the status of **a Jew's** idol **be revoked? But isn't it written: "And shall set it up in a hidden place"** (Deuteronomy 27:15), from which the Sages derived that a Jew's idol requires interment? **Rabbi Hillel, son of Rabbi Volas, said: No,** this *halakha* is **necessary** in a case **where** the gentile **has partnership in** the idol, and therefore it is possible to say that its status can be revoked.

The Gemara now records an exchange between Rebbi and his son Rabbi Shimon. Rebbi had been teaching the Mishna to Rabbi Shimon: *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel chaveiro* — the standard ruling. Rabbi Shimon responds: *Rebbi, shonita lanu b'yaldutcha* — but when you were younger, you taught us *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo v'shel Yisrael* — that a non-Jew can even nullify the idol of a Jew. Has Rebbi changed his position?

### Gemara Response

Before Rebbi can answer, the Gemara intervenes with astonishment: *d'Yisrael mi ka-mevatel?* — wait, can anyone ever have said that a non-Jew may nullify a Jewish idol? The verse says *vesam basater* — it is placed in hiding, permanently inaccessible. The idol of a Jew has an immovable status; *bittul* by a non-Jew should be structurally impossible.

### Joint Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיַלְדוּתוֹ מַאי קָסָבַר, וּבְזִקְנוּתוֹ מַאי קָסָבַר? בְּיַלְדוּתוֹ סָבַר: יִשְׂרָאֵל אַדַּעְתָּא דְּנׇכְרִי פָּלַח, כֵּיוָן דְּנׇכְרִי מְבַטֵּל דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי מְבַטְּלָהּ. וּבְזִקְנוּתוֹ סָבַר: יִשְׂרָאֵל אַדַּעְתָּא דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ פָּלַח, כִּי מְבַטֵּל נׇכְרִי דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא בָּטֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **hold in his youth, and what did he hold in his old age?** The Gemara answers: **In his youth he held** that the **Jew worships** the idol **based on the intention of** the **gentile. Once the gentile revokes** the status of **his** share in the idol, the status of the share **of** the **Jew is also revoked. But in his old age** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **held** that the **Jew worships** the idol **based on his own intentions.** Therefore, **when** the **gentile revokes** the status of the object of idol worship, he revokes the status of only **his own** share, but the status of the share **of** the **Jew is not revoked.**

The resolution offered by Rav Hillel son of Rav Vallas turns on the specific scenario that Rebbi's earlier teaching addressed: the idol was jointly owned by a Jew and a non-Jew. In that case, the two stages of Rebbi's opinion are comprehensible.

### Young Rebbi

In his youth, Rebbi held: *Yisrael ada'ata d'akum palach* — when a Jew worships an idol, he does so under the influence and in the context of the non-Jew he is associated with. The idea is something like a *pintele Yid*: Jews are not genuinely committed to idol worship; they are drawn into it through proximity to non-Jewish practice. In this framework, the Jewish partner's attachment to the idol is never truly independent — it flows from, and is defined through, the non-Jewish partner's attachment. Therefore, when the non-Jew performs *bittul* on his own share of the idol, he simultaneously nullifies the Jewish dimension as well, because that dimension was never autonomous: *keivan d'akum mevatel d'nafshei Yisrael nami mevatla*.

### Old Rebbi

In his old age, Rebbi revised this position: *Yisrael ada'ata d'nafshei palach* — a Jew who worships idols does so on his own terms, with his own independent intention and commitment. The idol therefore has an independent status as the idol of a Jew, which cannot be dissolved simply by the non-Jewish partner performing *bittul* on his own share. *Ki ka mevatel avodat kochavim d'nafshei Yisrael lo batel* — when the non-Jew nullifies his portion, he nullifies only his own; the Jewish portion retains its separate, indelible character as the *avodat kochavim shel Yisrael*.
