# Nazir 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Shimshon

### Shimshon Status

The *gemara* on daf 4b picks up in the middle of a discussion about *Nazir Shimshon* and *Nazir Olam*. The background question concerns whether Shimshon accepted his *nezirut* himself, through a *neder*, or whether the *nezirut* was externally imposed. This is halachically significant because the mechanism for one person to declare himself a *Nazir Shimshon* — to transfer Shimshon's status onto oneself — depends on whether Shimshon's own *nezirut* originated through a vow. This was the subject of a *tannaitic* dispute reviewed previously.

### Angel's Declaration

The *gemara* now asks: *v'Shimshon lav nazir havae* — and was Shimshon not a *nazir*? The question seems strange at first glance, since nobody denied that he was a *nazir*. The debate was specifically about whether his *nezirut* came about through a *neder*. The *gemara* presses: *v'ha-ksiv* — does the verse not say *ki nezir elohim yihye hana'ar min habaten*, that the child would be a *nazir* to God from the womb? To this, the *gemara* responds: *hasam malach d'ka-amar* — there it was only the angel saying so, not Shimshon himself.

Fitting this into the preceding discussion, the objection would be: didn't he accept his *nezirut* through a *neder*? The angelic declaration would not demonstrate that Shimshon undertook his *nezirut* in the normal way. The answer is that it was only the angel's directive, not a formal vow. More broadly, though, one may read this passage as addressing a simpler version of Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Shimon said *lo amar klum shelo matzinu b'Shimshon sheyatza nezirut mipiv* — he never uttered a declaration of *nezirut*. This can be read in two ways: either he was a *nazir* but not through a vow, and therefore the status cannot be transferred; or, more radically, that according to Rabbi Shimon, Shimshon never formally held the halachic status of *nazir* at all. The *gemara*'s question and answer may be hinting at this second reading — the angelic announcement did not itself confer a halachic *nezirut*.

### Contact with Dead

The *gemara* continues: *u'minah d'itmei l'meisim* — how do we know that a *Nazir Shimshon* carries an allowance to become *tamei meis*? Shimshon certainly killed many people, but how do we know he directly touched them? The *gemara* first suggests a proof from the verse *lechi hachamor hikeisi elef ish* — with the jawbone of an ass I slew a thousand men. Since he was wielding that instrument at such close range, presumably he came into contact with the bodies. The *gemara* pushes back: *dilma giruy garu beih* — perhaps he threw it at them, never touching them directly. Questions of *tuma b'chiburin* — *tumah* transmitted through an intermediate object — are at play, but if he threw the jawbone as a projectile, no direct contact need be assumed.

The *gemara* then proposes a different verse: *vayach mehem shloshim ish vayikach es chalitzosam* — he smote thirty men and took their garments. If he removed their clothing after killing them, he must have touched them. The *gemara* responds: perhaps he stripped them first and then killed them. To this, the *gemara* answers *vayach vayikach ksiv* — the verse says "he smote and he took," in that order: he struck them first and took their garments afterward. Yet the *gemara* still resists: *v'dilma gosesin shavinahu* — perhaps he only rendered them mortally wounded but not yet dead when he took the garments, so that he still did not touch full corpses.

### Tradition on Contact

With all textual proofs exhausted, the *gemara* concludes: *ela fayn, gemara gemira lah* — this is simply a received tradition that Shimshon did in fact come into contact with some of those he killed. That closes the discussion of *Nazir Shimshon*.

## Nazir Olam

### Absalom Example

The *gemara* turns to the concept of *Nazir Olam* and asks: *v'nazir olam heicha ktiv* — where in Scripture is the idea of a *Nazir Olam* sourced? This refers not simply to someone who chooses indefinitely to remain a *nazir*, but to a formal status in which the *nezirut* is permanently constitutive — and which paradoxically permits periodic hair-cutting. A *braita* answers: *Rabbi omer: Avshalom Nazir Olam hayah* — just as *Nazir Shimshon* is derived from a narrative in *Tanach*, so too *Nazir Olam* is derived from the story of Avshalom.

### Absalom Narrative

The relevant verse is *vayehi miketz arba'im shanah* — and it came to pass at the end of forty years — at the point when Avshalom was plotting his rebellion against David. He says to the king: *elchah na va'ashalem et nedarai asher nadarti l'Hashem b'Chevron* — let me go and I will fulfill my vows that I made to God in Chevron. We also know that he would cut his hair *echad l'shneim asar chodesh* — once every twelve months, as Rabbi derives from the phrase *vayehi miketz yamim l'yamim* — and it was at the end of days upon days.

What is being read into these *pesukim* is that Avshalom's declaration "let me fulfill my vows" means he needed to bring the three *korbanot* that normally conclude a *nezirut*. For a *Nazir Olam*, these offerings do not terminate the *nezirut* — they open a halachic aperture allowing the *nazir* to cut his hair. Thus Avshalom would periodically bring these *korbanot* and trim his hair, yet his *nezirut* persisted permanently. The phrase *yamim l'yamim* is understood to indicate that he did this on a yearly basis.

It is worth noting an irony here that connects to the previous *sugya*. The discussion concluded with the case of a man who became a *nazir* for the purpose of eventually cutting his hair, and there was a contrast with the *mishnah* as Shmuel reads it — a person who grabs his hair and says "I will be beautiful, therefore I will be a *nazir*." The question of whether the *nazir*'s long hair represents beauty or social displacement is genuinely complex. The *pshat* of *pera* suggests the hair grows wild and unkempt, marking the *nazir* as outside normal society. Yet Avshalom's hair is described in *Tanach* precisely as something strikingly beautiful — which makes the *Nazir Olam* example an interesting counterpoint.

### Walled City Link

Rabbi derives that Avshalom's yearly hair-cutting cycle comes through a *gezeirah shavah* between the word *yamim* as it appears in the Avshalom narrative and the word *yamim* as it appears in the laws of houses in a walled city. The relevant *pasuk* there reads *yamim tihyeh ge'ulato* — his right of redemption shall be for *yamim* — and then subsequently, *ad melot lo shanah temimah* — until a full year has been completed. The *gemara* derives: *yamim* there means twelve months, a full year. Therefore *yamim l'yamim* by Avshalom also means a full year.

*V'yalif yamim yamim mibatei arei chomah, ma hasham yud-bet chodesh, af kan yud-bet chodesh* — just as *yamim* there means twelve months, so too here. This is how Rabbi concludes that a *Nazir Olam* cuts his hair once a year.

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge. Rabbi himself holds elsewhere that *yamim* in that very *pasuk* about the walled city means literally two days — *ein yamim pachus mishnayim* — so that the seller must wait at least two days before exercising his right of redemption. How then can Rabbi use *yamim* in that *pasuk* to mean a full year?

The *gemara* answers: *hai gezeirah shavah mishum koved gamir, uvishenei yamim leika koved* — the *gezeirah shavah* is established on account of the weight of the hair becoming burdensome, and the hair certainly does not become burdensome after two days. Since the *gezeirah shavah* for the *Nazir Olam* case is premised on the notion of hair growing heavy, the *yamim* of the *gezeirah shavah* must refer to a period long enough for hair to become burdensome — which brings us to the fuller reading of that *pasuk*, identifying *yamim* with a complete year.

### Qualified Yamim

Having established that Rabbi links *yamim* to a full year via *batei arei chomah*, the *gemara* considers whether Avshalom's *yamim* could be linked to some other verse that carries *yamim* with a different duration.

First: *ve'eima beis shana* — perhaps it means two years? There is a verse that reads *vayehi mikeitz shenasayim yamim* — and it came to pass at the end of two years of days — where *yamim* appears in the context of two years. The answer: *danin yamim she'ein imahem shanim miyamim she'ein imahem shanim* — we derive *yamim* (unqualified) from *yamim* (unqualified). The verse by Avshalom says *yamim l'yamim* with no attached qualifier, and the verse by *batei arei chomah* says *yamim tihyeh ge'ulato* also without a qualifier. But *shenasayim yamim* has the word *yamim* immediately juxtaposed with *shenasayim*, making it a qualified usage. One may not learn from a qualified *yamim* to an unqualified one. *Al yochiach zeh sheyeish imo shanim*.

Second: *ve'eima sheloshim yom* — perhaps thirty days? There is a verse that reads *ad chodesh yamim* — until a month of days — where *yamim* appears alongside the word for month. The same answer applies: *danin yamim she'ein imahem chodashim miyamim she'ein imahem chodashim* — *yamim* without a month-qualifier is learned from *yamim* without a month-qualifier. *Ve'al yochiach zeh sheyeish imo chodashim*.

Third: *ve'eima meihacha miyamim yamimah* — perhaps we should learn from the verse about the daughters of Israel who would go to commemorate the daughter of Yiftach, described as coming *miyamim yamimah*, four times a year. If they came four times a year, perhaps the unit of *yamim* represents a three-month interval? The *gemara* responds: *danin yamim miyamim ve'ein danin yamim miyamimah* — we derive *yamim* from *yamim*, not from *yamimah*, which is a slightly different morphological form of the word. A *gezeirah shavah* requires the closest available verbal match.

The *gemara* then asks: *umai nafka mina* — why should the morphological form matter in a *gezeirah shavah*? Does a *gezeirah shavah* require identical words? The *gemara* invokes the well-known *braita* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael regarding *tzara'at* in a house, where the *kohen* "returns" (*veshav*) and "comes" (*uva*) — two different words used to describe the same act — and the *gemara* uses them interchangeably: *zo hi shivah zo hi biah*. This seems to suggest that *gezeirah shavah* tolerates links of meaning even without identical words. The *gemara* answers: *hani mili heicha deleika dedami leih* — that principle applies only when there is no closer match available. *Aval heicha de'ika dedami leih, midedami leih yalpinan* — but where there is an exact verbal match, the *gezeirah shavah* must follow the more precise link. Since *yamim* unqualified is available as a match, we do not resort to *yamimah*.

A further reason not to learn from the Yiftach narrative: *mena yade'ah dechol tlata yarchi chad zimna* — who says the four annual visits were evenly spaced at three-month intervals? *Dilma arba zimnei beshata*, four times a year, but irregularly distributed — perhaps *arba yarchi chad zimna*, then *trei yarchi bechad zimna*, and so on. Since the visits need not have followed a fixed interval, one cannot extract any standard unit of time from *miyamim yamimah*.

A student raised the structural observation that the *braita* of Rabbi Yishmael — who is associated with the school of *dibra Torah k'lashon b'nei adam* and generally tends toward meaning over precise morphology — is being read here as if it were making a point about *gezeirah shavah* as a formal technique, when in fact that *braita* may not be using *gezeirah shavah* at all. Rabbi Yishmael's comparison of *shivah* and *biah* may simply be a recognition that two words are synonymous in context, not a *gezeirah shavah* in the technical sense. This touches on the broader question of what *gezeirah shavah* originally meant. There is an early *mishnah* in which Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel invoke a *gezeirah shavah* in the context of two rabbinic laws — possibly the *eiruv techumin* case — suggesting that in its earliest form, *gezeirah shavah* may have meant something more like a logical analogy between comparable concepts, rather than the formal technique of word-linking that automatically transfers an entire body of law from one domain to another.

The upshot of all of this is that Rabbi's derivation stands: Avshalom is understood to have been a *nazir*, periodically bringing his *korbanot* and trimming his hair on a yearly basis (*miyamim l'yamim* = once a year), and this is how Rabbi establishes that a *Nazir Olam* may trim his hair annually.

### Nehorai: 30 Days

Rabbi Nehorai disagrees and says *megale'ach achas lishloshim yom* — a *Nazir Olam* may cut his hair once every thirty days. This is a substantial allowance: patterned after the standard unit of *nezirut*, after one has completed an equivalent period, one may get a trim. The basis is *gabei kohanim* — derived from the laws of *kohanim*, who may not allow their hair to become *gadel pera*, wild growth. *Mishum d'ika koved* — after thirty days, hair is considered to have become heavy and burdensome. *Hacha nami ika koved* — the same applies here: once the hair reaches the thirty-day threshold, it is considered burdensome and the allowance to trim kicks in.

Tosafot notes the circularity in this reasoning: the only reason we know that the *kohen* prohibition of *gadel pera* means more than thirty days is that we learn it from the standard *nezirut*, where thirty days' hair growth is defined as wild. So the chain of reasoning goes: a standard *nazir* has thirty-day *nezirut*, during which the hair must be *gadel pera*; this means thirty days defines wild hair; a *kohen* may not have *gadel pera* hair, so thirty days is the threshold; and now we use that threshold to tell us when a *Nazir Olam* may trim. Tosafot suggests the reasoning may prefer *kohanim* as the intermediate reference rather than deriving *Nazir Olam* directly from ordinary *nezirut*, since it may be preferable not to derive a *Nazir Olam*'s law directly from the temporary *nazir* (*Nazir Sha'ah*).

One genuinely puzzling question that this raises: when a *Nazir Olam* receives his trim, must his hair still maintain the *pera* state? If he trims every thirty-one days — cutting off only the hair that grew after the previous trim — his hair would cycle in and out of the *pera* threshold. On day twenty-nine it would not yet qualify as *pera*; on day thirty or thirty-one it would; then he trims and returns below the threshold. Is the *Nazir Olam* then living most of his life with hair that does not qualify as *pera*? This seems strange, since one would expect the condition of *pera* to be a permanent characteristic of the *nazir*'s state, not merely a momentary threshold he crosses before trimming back down. The *gemara* does not resolve this explicitly.

### Yosi: Weekly

Rabbi Yosi is more radical still: *megale'ach me'erev Shabbos le'erev Shabbos* — a *Nazir Olam* may cut his hair every *erev Shabbos*. The reasoning is *shekein matzinu bivnei melachim shemegalchin me'erev Shabbos le'erev Shabbos* — this is the grooming standard of princes, who cut their hair weekly, and since Avshalom was a prince, he presumably maintained this standard.

This position is striking on multiple levels. First, if Avshalom trimmed his hair weekly, how did his hair become so famously long that it caught in the tree? One answer offered is that his weekly trim was minimal — perhaps only split ends — and the hair overall remained long. Second, and more fundamentally, if a *Nazir Olam* gets a weekly haircut, his hair may never truly reach the state of *pera* at all after the first month. He would look entirely presentable at all times — essentially indistinguishable from anyone else. This seems to suggest that the *Nazir Olam*'s outward appearance is qualitatively different from that of a standard *nazir*, which is itself quite strange.

The *gemara* asks *mai ika beinei v'sha'ar echav* — how would Avshalom look different from his non-*nazir* brothers, the other princes, who also cut their hair weekly? What is the practical *nafka mina* between Avshalom's regimen and theirs? Two answers are given. First, *yom tov shechal lihayos b'emtza Shabbas* — if a *yom tov* fell mid-week, the other princes could get haircuts for *kavod yom tov*, but Avshalom, being a *nazir*, could not cut mid-week. Second, *ur l'giluychi mitzafra d'ma'alei Shabata* — his brothers could cut their hair Friday morning, whereas Avshalom, who had to wait out the full permitted interval, could not cut until *panya*, just before sunset on Friday. Tosafot adds that this may also reflect the requirement that Avshalom could not cut without first bringing his *korbanot* — so the distinction between him and his brothers was visible in the timing, even if not in the length of his hair.

### Forty Years

The *gemara* raises a chronological difficulty: *hanei arbayim shana mai avidteihu* — what are these forty years counting from? David's entire reign lasted only thirty-seven years, so "forty years after" the beginning of David's kingship is impossible. Rav Nehorai says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: *l'ketz mem shana she'sha'alu lahem melech* — the forty years count from when the Jewish people first asked for a king from Shmuel. The rebellion of Avshalom against his father mirrors the original rebellion of *Bnei Yisrael* against divine sovereignty when they demanded a human king — an act that Shmuel regarded as a rejection of God's kingship. Forty years later, Avshalom rebels against his father's earthly kingship. A *tanna* adds: *osa shana she'sha'alu lahem melech hi shenas eser l'Shmuel HaRamasi* — the year of that request was the tenth year of Shmuel's career, a chronological anchor that explains how all the dates in the narrative align.

## Standard 30 Days

### Mishnah Text

The *mishnah* states simply: *stam nezirut sheloshim yom* — standard, unexplicated *nezirut* lasts thirty days. If a person says "I am a *nazir*" without specifying a duration, the assumed period is thirty days, after which he brings his *korbanot*. We return here to ordinary *nezirut*, setting aside the special cases of *Nazir Shimshon* and *Nazir Olam*.

### Gematria Method

The *gemara* asks: *mani milei* — what is the source for thirty days? Rav Matna derives it from the verse *kadosh yihiyeh, gadel pera se'ar rosho* — holy shall it be, the growing wild of his hair. The word *yihiyeh* in *gematria*: *yud* (10) + *heh* (5) + *yud* (10) + *heh* (5) = 30. *Yihiyeh be-gematria tlasin havu* — *yihiyeh* in *gematria* equals thirty.

It is worth noting that this is a case where the *gemara* deploys *gematria* in service of an actual *halacha*. It seems fairly clear, however — given that Bar Pada immediately offers an entirely different derivation — that both authorities likely began with a received tradition that *stam nezirut* is thirty days, and each then sought to connect that tradition back to the *pesukim*. When everyone assumes a law and the debate is only about which verse supports it, that pattern typically indicates a tradition in hand rather than a derivation in process. This dynamic recurs frequently in the *sugyot* of *Chazal*: sometimes they derive the law from the verse, and sometimes they start with the tradition and find the verse afterward.

### Pada Counts

Bar Pada offers a different derivation: *k'neged nazir neziru ha'amurim ba'torah lamed chasar achas* — count the occurrences of the words *nazir* and *neziru* in the Torah and you will find twenty-nine. Interesting: if one actually counts all occurrences of *nazir* and related forms in the Torah, the total comes to thirty. Bar Pada's position is that one occurrence must be excluded — either because it is needed to introduce the concept and cannot count, or because, as the *rishonim* suggest, the phrase *ki nezer elohav al rosho* uses *nezer* to mean a crown rather than *nezirut*, and therefore does not count toward the tally. This leaves twenty-nine — and that, as we shall see, leads Bar Pada to conclude that *stam nezirut* is in principle twenty-nine days, not thirty.

### Word Usage Debate

The *gemara* challenges: why does Rav Matna not use Bar Pada's counting method? Rav Matna replies: *hahu ledrasha* — each extra occurrence of the word *nazir* in the Torah is there to teach a specific law. As we have seen, for example, *miyayin veshechar yazir* with its doubled form teaches *le'esor yayin mitzvah keyayin reshut* — that even wine consumed in fulfillment of a *mitzvah* is prohibited to the *nazir*; and the doubled expression *ki yafuli lindor neder Nazir lehazir l'Hashem* teaches *melamed she'nezirut chala al nezirut* — that one *nezirut* can take effect while another is still running, hovering in abeyance until the first concludes. Since every occurrence is pressed into service for an independent law, Rav Matna cannot simply count them up as a calendar.

### Pada Defense

Bar Pada responds: *leit chad min'hon d'lav ledrasha* — there is not a single occurrence that does not teach something. His point is that even those occurrences used for counting are not simply superfluous; rather, their very presence is meaningful. Since it is impossible that all twenty-nine (or thirty) instances teach independent *drashot* — there are not that many independent laws of *nezirut* to be derived — at least some of them must be there for counting purposes. *Ela mide'hahu leminyana, kulhu nami leminyana* — since one is established as being for counting, all may be treated as counting toward the total. The ones that do teach specific laws serve a dual function: they teach the law and they count. The key principle is that once any of the occurrences cannot be assigned an independent *drasha*, it establishes that the function of enumeration is operative across all of them.

## Matna vs. Pada (III)

### Matna: 30 True

Having established the two methods of derivation, the *gemara* now draws out their substantive legal disagreement. For Rav Matna, the true duration of *stam nezirut* is thirty days. The *korbanot* ending the *nezirut* are brought on day thirty-one, after the thirty days are complete.

### Pada: 29 True

For Bar Pada, the true duration is twenty-nine days in principle. However, since the *korbanot* cannot be brought at night, and one reaches the end of day twenty-nine at nightfall, one must wait until day thirty to bring the *korbanot* in practice. So the *nezirut* ends functionally on day thirty — but this is merely a practical constraint, not the halachically defined duration.

The *gemara* now tests these positions against the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states *stam nezirut sheloshim yom* — thirty days. For Rav Matna this is straightforward. For Bar Pada it is a difficulty: he says twenty-nine. Bar Pada responds: *idi ve'idi yom sheloshim d'megaleach umeiti korbanotav nami* — in all cases, it is on day thirty that one shaves and brings *korbanot*, because one can never end it before day thirty even on the twenty-nine-day theory. Hence the *mishnah* says "thirty days" as the practical day of completion, even though the underlying duration is twenty-nine.

The *gemara* next examines the *mishnah*: *mi she'amar hareini Nazir megaleach yom sheloshim ve'echad* — one who simply says "I am a *nazir*" cuts his hair on day thirty-one. For Rav Matna this is straightforward: thirty-day duration, *korbanot* on day thirty-one. For Bar Pada it is a difficulty: he should be able to bring his *korbanot* on day thirty. Bar Pada responds: *eima seifa* — look at the end of that *mishnah*: *im gilach beyom sheloshim yatza* — if he cut on day thirty, he has fulfilled his obligation *bedi'avad*. Bar Pada can explain this: the *nezirut* was twenty-nine days, and day thirty is the proper day of completion. The *seifa* is actually in his favor. The *reisha*, which says to wait until day thirty-one *lechatchila*, Bar Pada explains as a precautionary measure: *na'aseh ke'omer shleimim* — we are concerned that one may have said "I am a *nazir* for a full month," in which case full thirty days are required. Since the difference between twenty-nine days and a month is not substantial in people's minds, we require everyone to wait until day thirty-one *lechatchila* to avoid confusion. In principle, for *stam nezirut*, day thirty is the correct and sufficient day.

### Matna Resolution

For Rav Matna, the *reisha* is straightforward — thirty days, bring *korbanot* on day thirty-one — but the *seifa* is the difficulty. How does day thirty work *bedi'avad* if the duration is thirty days? *Kasavar miktzas hayom kekulo* — Rav Matna holds the principle that part of a day counts as the whole day. One need not complete all of day thirty; having entered day thirty, the thirty-day requirement is satisfied, and the *korbanot* may be brought. The reason we nonetheless say *lechatchila* to wait until day thirty-one is that we prefer not to rely on *miktzat hayom kekulo* as a first resort. But *bedi'avad*, bringing the *korbanot* on day thirty itself is valid.

## Partial Day Princip (III)le

### Principle Overview

The principle of *miktzat hayom kekulo* — that part of a day counts as the whole — is familiar from other areas of *halacha*. In *aveilus*, the seven days of mourning: one rises at the beginning of the seventh day, with that partial day counting as the full seventh. Similarly, a *zavah* who counts seven clean days may immerse at the beginning of the seventh. Importantly, this principle does not always operate as a *lechatchila* standard; in some contexts, such as *niddah*, one must wait until the full end of the seventh day. Whether and when one may rely on *miktzat hayom kekulo* is itself context-dependent, which is part of why Rav Matna treats it as a *bedi'avad* resource rather than a first-choice option.

## Matna vs. Pada (III)

### Consecutive Cases

The *gemara* now examines back-to-back *neziriyot*. The *mishnah* states: *mi shenazar shtei neziriyot megaleach et harishona yom shloshim ve'echad vehashniya leyom shishim ve'echad* — one who takes on two consecutive *neziriyot* cuts for the first on day thirty-one and for the second on day sixty-one. The same questions arise. For Rav Matna: straightforward, thirty-day duration each, so day thirty-one ends the first and day sixty-one ends the second. For Bar Pada: why wait until day thirty-one for each? On his view the *nezirut* is twenty-nine days.

## Partial Day Princip (III)le…

### Full Days Exception

The *gemara* then examines the *mishnah*'s statement: *hareini nazir shloshim yom* — if one explicitly says "I am a *nazir* for thirty days" and then cuts on day thirty, *lo yatza* — one has not fulfilled the obligation. This holds for both opinions. For Bar Pada, who does not invoke *miktzat hayom kekulo*, this is simple: once one has specified full days, the full count must be satisfied. For Rav Matna, the question is sharper: why doesn't *miktzat hayom kekulo* apply here? The *gemara* answers: *be'omer shleimim* — because one said "full days." When one explicitly articulates a full thirty-day *nezirut*, the *miktzat hayom* principle is preempted; the statement of intent requires complete days, not a partial final day.

### Day 30 Hinge

In the case of back-to-back *neziriyot*, day thirty-one ends the first and begins the second. But if one went ahead and ended the first *nezirut* on day thirty — either through *miktzat hayom* (Rav Matna) or because it was a twenty-nine-day *nezirut* (Bar Pada) — then day thirty itself serves as the pivot: it ends the first and begins the second. Accordingly, the second *nezirut* started on day thirty, and adding thirty days (with the same *lechatchila* logic) brings one to day sixty for the second shaving. This is exactly what the *mishnah* says: *ve'im gilach et harishona leyom shloshim, megaleach et hashniya leyom shishim* — if one shaved for the first on day thirty, one shaves for the second on day sixty.

## Matna vs. Pada (III)…

### Pada Resolution

Returning to Bar Pada's resolution of the back-to-back case: the *seifa* of the *mishnah* — that shaving the first on day thirty means shaving the second on day sixty — works for Bar Pada because on his view the first *nezirut* was twenty-nine days, properly concluded on day thirty, and the second likewise. The *reisha*, which says to wait until day thirty-one and day sixty-one *lechatchila*, Bar Pada again explains as *be'omer shleimim* — or as a precautionary measure to account for cases in which one has articulated a full month, so that everyone is asked *lechatchila* to wait the extra day in order to avoid confusion between *stam nezirut* and an explicitly month-long *nezirut*.

## Partial Day Princip (III)le……

### Double-Dip Issue

For Rav Matna, the *seifa* of the back-to-back *mishnah* requires that *miktzat hayom kekulo* operate in two directions simultaneously on day thirty. On Rav Matna's view, the first *nezirut* was thirty days; ending it on day thirty requires invoking *miktzat hayom kekulo* — a portion of day thirty counts as the full thirtieth day of the first *nezirut*. But then day thirty also counts as day one of the second *nezirut*, which in turn requires invoking *miktzat hayom kekulo* in the other direction — that entering day thirty is treated as a full first day of the new *nezirut*. The *gemara* presses: can we truly double-dip in this way?

The *gemara* explains: *mahu detaima, hani mili le'inyan chada nezirut, aval lishtei nezirut lo* — one might have thought that *miktzat hayom kekulo* can be invoked for one *nezirut* but not to simultaneously serve two *neziriyot*. If the logic of the principle is that we treat the portion of day thirty as if it were the full final day of the first *nezirut*, then that day is, conceptually, fully consumed by the first. How can that same partial day also be treated as a fresh opening day of the second? The *gemara*'s resolution is that the *mishnah*'s explicit ruling — *yom shloshim oleh lekan ulekan*, day thirty counts in both directions — establishes this as a genuine *chiddush*: the same partial day can simultaneously close one *nezirut* and open another, applying *miktzat hayom kekulo* in both directions at once.
