# Bava Metzia 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatzi Sachar & Fees

### Machatzit Sachar Intro

The daf opens on a topic already introduced at the end of 68b: the arrangement known as *machatzit sachar*, in which one party entrusts goods or capital to another for investment, and they divide both profits and losses equally. What makes this arrangement legally fraught is that, because the recipient assumes liability for half the principal, that half is effectively a loan. If the recipient then performs his labor without receiving any compensation, the use of that capital constitutes *ribbit* — the forbidden payment of interest — since his labor is being extracted as a kind of implicit return on the loan. The solution is to ensure that the recipient receives a genuine fee for his efforts, independent of the profit-sharing arrangement. The question that runs through the entire opening section of 69a is what counts as an adequate fee.

### Sachar Katef & Babies

The *Tanna'im* debate this question in the context of a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan makom she'nahagu lehalot sachar katef lema'ot livehemah ma'alin* — in a place where there is a practice of paying a *sachar katef*, a "schlepping fee" for transporting animals, one must in fact pay that fee when entering a *machatzit sachar* arrangement involving livestock. The *sachar katef* — literally the fee for the shoulder, for physically carrying the animals, especially smaller ones — represents one component of the compensation owed to the sharecropper for his labor.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds: *shamin egel al imo* — one evaluates the calf together with its mother, and similarly a foal with its mother. The *Gemara* fills in the implication: even in a place where one would ordinarily pay the *sachar katef* for the young animals, if the young are being raised alongside their mothers, no additional schlepping fee is required for the offspring. The reasoning, following Rashi, is that when the young accompany the mother, the need for physical carrying is substantially reduced, since the young generally follow the mother on their own. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, holds that even when the young travel with the mother, the occasional need to carry them means the standard fee still applies.

### Dung as Fee

The *Gemara* then turns to ask the obvious question about Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel lo ba'ei sachar amalo umezono?* Does he not require that the recipient be paid for his effort and for the feeding of the animals? If he is not receiving a real fee, how does the arrangement avoid the problem of *ribbit*?

The answer: *ika glalim* — there is the dung. The person raising the animals is permitted to keep whatever dung the animals produce, and this counts as his fee. The *Gemara* then asks what the *tanna kamma* would say to this — why is dung insufficient? The answer: *glalim afkar me'afkar lehu* — the dung is ordinarily abandoned, ownerless. When someone allows another person to keep the dung of animals he is watching, he is not truly conferring anything of value, since he would not have troubled himself to collect the dung in any case. Therefore, according to the *tanna kamma*, this cannot count as a real fee.

This discussion, while ostensibly about the *sachar katef*, opens into a broader debate that connects to what was discussed earlier: whether a nominal, even trivial, benefit can serve as the required fee in a *machatzit sachar* arrangement, or whether a real, substantive payment is required. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel joins the positions attributed to Rebbe Yehuda — who holds that something as slight as allowing the other party to dip his bread in your vinegar is sufficient — and Rebbe Yose bar Rebbe Yehuda — who holds that allowing the sharecropper to keep the thin, watery remnant of the milk is enough — in permitting a nominal payment to discharge the obligation.

### Rav Nachman's Ruling

Rav Nachman issues a ruling: *halacha k'Rebbe Yehuda, ve'halacha k'Rebbe Yose bar Rebbe Yehuda, ve'halacha k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — we rule in accordance with each of these three positions. On its face, this sounds like a sweeping endorsement of the lenient approach, validating even trivial nominal amounts as sufficient fees.

## Rebbe Ilish Case

### Half/Half Problem

The *Gemara* now presents a case that tests the limits of the nominal-fee approach. A document emerged belonging to the sons of Rebbe Ilish — a *shtara* in their father's name, recording an arrangement of *palga be'agar palga be'hefsed*, half profit and half loss, with no mention of any fee. The problem is clear: under a pure half-and-half arrangement, half of the capital is effectively a loan to the recipient, and if he performs his labor without compensation, his labor itself becomes a form of *ribbit*. There is no dung here, no vinegar-dipping, nothing nominal — just a bare split with no fee.

Rava responds: *Rebbe Ilish gavra rabba hu, ve'issura l'inshi lo havi safi* — Rebbe Ilish was a great man; he would not have arranged for others to transgress a prohibition. The phrasing is deliberately ironic: the word *safi* means to feed, and the *issur* of *ribbit* falls on both the lender and the recipient. Rebbe Ilish would not have participated in an arrangement that placed either party in violation. Therefore, *mamma nafshach* — whichever way you look at it — this document cannot be what it appears to be on its surface.

### Disproportionate Splits

Rava spells out two arrangements that would legitimize what the *shtara* records. The first: *ee palga b'agar trei talti b'hefsed* — if you are to receive half the profit, then you must bear two-thirds of the loss. The logic is as follows. If a person bears two-thirds of the loss, that means two-thirds of the capital is his own money — it is effectively a loan, for which he bears total responsibility. But he is entitled to only two-thirds of the profit on the basis of that loan. When he receives half the profit instead, the extra sixth beyond his loan-based entitlement constitutes his fee for managing the investment. The disproportionality between his profit share and his loss share is precisely what generates the compensation that avoids *ribbit*.

The second arrangement inverts this: *ee palga b'hefsed trei talti b'agar* — if you bear half the loss, meaning half is the loan, then you should be entitled to two-thirds of the profit, the extra sixth again serving as the fee for your effort.

The discomfort in following Rashi's presentation here — who shifts the subject between the investor and the recipient in mid-analysis — is noted by Tosafot as well, which proposes a different *girsa* to clarify the language. But the underlying principle is consistent: any arrangement in which the profit-share exceeds the loss-share generates an implicit fee, and that is a valid structure.

### Rebbe Ilish Defense

This is exactly the point Rav Kahana raises when he reports having presented this analysis before Rav Zvid of Nehardea. Rav Zvid responded: perhaps Rebbe Ilish never used a disproportionate arrangement at all. Perhaps he simply relied on something trivial — some small incidental benefit — as Rebbe Yehuda and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permitted. And since Rav Nachman already ruled *halacha* in accordance with those positions, the *shtara* recording a pure half-and-half arrangement need not trouble us — Rebbe Ilish presumably relied on some nominal benefit as his fee.

### Nominal Amounts Rejected

The *Gemara* pushes back sharply: *lav hilkheta itmar, ela shita itmar* — Rav Nachman was not issuing a practical ruling. He was identifying a *shita*, a common theoretical position shared by three authorities. When a *posek* lines up three names who all hold the same view, the intent is typically to bracket and identify that position as a minority one that we do not follow — not to endorse it. If Rav Nachman had actually meant to rule leniently, he would have simply said *halacha k'Rebbe Yehuda*, since Rebbe Yehuda is the most lenient of the three and a ruling in his favor automatically encompasses the others.

The *Gemara* adds: *hakhi nami mistavra* — this interpretation is the more compelling one. And the *Gemara*'s conclusion is that nominal amounts are insufficient. A real fee is required.

Rabbeinu Tam dissents, arguing that we should at minimum rule in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, since the accepted principle is that wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears in the Mishnah, the *halacha* follows him. But Tosafot rejects this, citing the very next *Gemara* — in which Rava and Shmuel require either a *dinar* or the head of a calf — as proof that we do not rule like any of the three lenient positions, not even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The rule about Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is a general principle that does not apply in every case.

## Fee Certainty Debate

### Rav: Profit-Based Fee

The *Gemara* now records a dispute between Rav and Shmuel about what form the required fee must take. Rav says: *motar shelish b'sacharcha harei zeh mutar* — if you say to the recipient, "Your fee will be any profit we earn beyond a third," that arrangement is valid. The fee is real — it is a genuine entitlement — even though it is not guaranteed. If the investment earns more than a third profit, the excess belongs entirely to the recipient as his fee. If it does not, he receives no fee for that period.

### Shmuel: Fixed Fee

Shmuel disagrees: *matza mosa shlishi yeilech l'beiso reikam?* — if the investment fails to earn more than a third, should the recipient go home without any fee at all? That contingency is unacceptable. *Ela amar Shmuel kotzetz lo dinar* — Shmuel requires that a fixed *dinar* be promised to the recipient in advance, regardless of how the investment performs. Note that a *dinar*, while clearly more substantial than dung or milky water, is still far below what the recipient might earn as a *po'el*, a full hired laborer, so the exact level of required compensation remains an open question. But the structure is clear: for Shmuel, the fee must be certain and predetermined.

### Calf Head Case

The *Gemara* then addresses what appears to be a contradiction within Rav's own position. Rav himself elsewhere discusses a case where a person fattens a calf and receives the head of the calf as his fee — *reish igla l'pituma* — with the rest divided between the parties. The question is: does this not suggest that Rav himself requires a fixed, certain fee (the head), contradicting his earlier allowance of a contingent fee based on a profit threshold?

Rashi resolves the apparent contradiction by reading Rav's case as a compound arrangement: either the recipient earns more than a third profit, in which case the excess is his fee, or if that threshold is not reached, he is guaranteed the calf's head as a fallback. On this reading, Rav's case actually supports Shmuel's position that some guaranteed payment must exist.

But the *Gemara* offers an alternative: *lo, d'chamlei — ei mosa shlish ei reish igla l'pituma* — perhaps Rav's point is simply that either type of arrangement is independently valid. One could structure the deal as a contingent profit-based fee (per Rav's own ruling), or one could promise the recipient the calf's head as a fixed fee (per Shmuel). These are two separate permissible models, not a single compound deal. Rav's mention of the calf's head does not require a simultaneous profit-contingency fallback; it simply illustrates a valid fixed-fee alternative.

### Contingent Fee Rules

The *Gemara* then qualifies Rav's position: when does Rav permit a fee contingent on exceeding a profit threshold? *Kegon d'is lei behemah l'didei* — specifically in a case where the recipient already has his own animals and is raising them alongside the investor's animals. In this situation, the effort the recipient expends on behalf of the investor's animals is marginal — the work of feeding and herding many animals is roughly the same as feeding and herding fewer. As the *Gemara* puts it: *gvil l'tora gvil l'torei* — preparing fodder for one ox or for many oxen requires essentially the same effort. Because the incremental labor on the investor's behalf is so small, it is less problematic if the fee is uncertain, since the recipient is not truly performing a dedicated, uncompensated service. In such a case, a contingent fee suffices.

## Rav Elazar Story

### Original Terms

The *Gemara* now recounts an illustrative incident. Rav Elazar of Hagronya purchased an animal and gave it to his *arisa* — his sharecropper — under a *machatzit sachar* arrangement: the sharecropper would raise and fatten the animal, they would split profits and losses equally, and the fee for the sharecropper's labor would be the head of the animal. The head is not a large portion — it carries relatively little meat — but it has some value, and it was apparently a conventional form of fee in such arrangements.

### Wife's Advice

The sharecropper's wife, seeing that her husband was receiving the head as a kind of bonus on top of his share of the profit, reasoned that if he had been contributing his own capital rather than just his labor, he might receive something even better. She advised him: *ei mishtatefas bahadei, yahiv lach nami alyasa* — if you were to join Rav Elazar as an actual partner, investing your own money, he would surely give you the *alya* as well — the fat tail, which is a considerably more prized and substantial portion of the animal.

### Partnership Problem

Persuaded by his wife's logic, the sharecropper agreed to invest his own money and became a full equal partner with Rav Elazar. When they came to divide the animal, they split the *alya* equally — there was no bonus, as the wife had anticipated, since now they were true partners. And then, to make matters worse, when it came time to divide the head, Rav Elazar proposed splitting it as well. The sharecropper was taken aback: *hashta keme'ikara nami lo* — now that I have invested my own money, I am not even receiving what I used to get before?

### Labor & Ribbit

Rav Elazar explained: *ad hashta zuzei didi havu, ei lo hava yahivna lach tefei purta, meichzei keribis* — until now, all the capital was mine. If I had not given you a little extra — the head — it would have looked like *ribbit*, since you were performing labor on my behalf without receiving independent compensation. *Hashta shutafi anan* — but now we are partners. There is no longer any *ribbit* concern, because you are investing your own money and receiving your proportional share of the profits.

The sharecropper pressed further: even if there is no *ribbit* problem, I still performed all of the labor — where is my fee for the work I actually did? Rav Elazar's response, drawn from common social reality: *stam arisa lemarei ara kamshabed nafshei* — a sharecropper is effectively bound to the landowner. It is customary that a sharecropper performs various incidental services for the landowner beyond the strict terms of his agreement, including tasks like grazing the animals. The work he did in raising the animal falls within this broadly expected scope of the sharecropper's role.

The outcome is striking. In both scenarios — as a pure sharecropper and as a full partner — the sharecropper performed all the labor and received no additional compensation beyond his proportional share. In the first scenario, the *ribbit* concern forced Rav Elazar to pay the head as a fee; in the second, the *ribbit* concern dissolved, and with it the practical protection the sharecropper had enjoyed. By changing his status, the sharecropper inadvertently forfeited the very benefit the law had been generating on his behalf.

## Duration & Offspring

### Mishna Duration

The *Gemara* transitions to a new topic: the duration of the *machatzit sachar* arrangement and the obligations that arise regarding offspring. A *baraita* establishes the framework: *hasham behemah l'chavero ad matay chayav l'tapel bah* — when one entrusts an animal to another under an arrangement requiring assessment of its value, how long is the recipient obligated to tend to it?

### Animal Types

Sumchos teaches: for donkeys — *atonot* — the obligatory period is 18 months; for sheep — *gedorot* — it is 24 months. If the recipient seeks to dissolve the arrangement before the period expires, the other party may refuse: *chavero me'akev alav*.

The reason is that the effort invested and the profit realized are not symmetrically distributed across time: *eino domeh tipulah shel shanah zu l'tipulah shel shanah acheres* — the labor of one year does not resemble the labor of the other year. Early in the arrangement, effort may be high and profit low; later, the reverse may be true. Because effort and return are not temporally synchronized, a premature split would inevitably disadvantage one of the parties. Only at the conclusion of the full agreed period do effort and return balance out. This is similar to the dynamics of agriculture: the farmer who plows and plants expends most of his effort before the harvest, and it would be unfair to dissolve the partnership at planting time, when he has done most of the work but the fruits have not yet been realized.

### Rabbi Yosi View

A separate *baraita* addresses the care of offspring specifically: *hasham behemah l'chavero ad matay chayav l'tapel bivladot* — when animals produce young during the arrangement, how long must the recipient continue to tend to the offspring? The first opinion gives 30 days for small animals and 50 days for large ones. Rabbi Yosi extends this for small animals to 3 months — *mipnei shetipulah merubah* — because the labor involved in raising small animals is substantially higher than for large ones.

### New Arrangement

After that obligatory period expires, if the recipient continues to raise the offspring, a new arrangement implicitly takes hold. Before the birth of offspring, the entire capital was provided by the investor. But the offspring are born with a different ownership structure: half of them already belong to the recipient as his share of the profits. So if he continues raising the remainder — the investor's half — that is functionally identical to a new *machatzit sachar* arrangement on those animals. The recipient takes on half-profit and half-liability for those animals, just as in the original arrangement.

To make this concrete: imagine 100 offspring are born. Fifty belong entirely to the recipient as his profit share. The remaining fifty belong to the investor. If the recipient continues to tend to those fifty, the same rules that governed the original entrusted animals now govern this second generation: the recipient gets half the profit from those fifty and bears half the loss. As the *Gemara* formulates it: *notel mechtza shelo v'chatzi mechtza b'shel chaveiro* — he takes his own half (outright ownership of 50) plus half of the half that belongs to the other (that is, half the profit on the remaining 50).

### Unilateral Division

Rav Menashe bar Gadda, when the time came to divide the offspring, went ahead and did so on his own authority. He separated his fifty from the investor's fifty unilaterally. The case came before Abaye, who rejected this: *man palig lach?* — who authorized you to divide? Even though the division is conceptually clear, the physical separation of animals — determining which specific animals belong to which party — cannot be done unilaterally. It requires a *beit din*. Furthermore, Abaye adds, in a place where the custom is to continue raising the offspring together — *makom she'nahagu l'gadel yagdilu* — the recipient has no entitlement to the additional half-of-a-half profit formula at all; raising the offspring is simply assumed to be part of the ongoing arrangement.

## Partnership Split

### Money vs. Goods

A general principle now emerges about how jointly held assets may be dissolved. When partners hold cash, they may divide it unilaterally — money is, as Rav Nachman formulates it, *zuzei keman de'paligi damu*, already effectively divided, since one dollar is interchangeable with any other. Commodities, by contrast, require a *beit din* to oversee the division, since not all units of a commodity are equivalent.

### Samaritan Case 1

The principle is illustrated through two cases involving partners. In the first, one of the partners went ahead and removed his share of the cash from their joint funds without consulting the other. When the matter was brought before Rav Papa, he ruled in favor of the partner who had made the unilateral split: since cash is fungible, one party may take his share without requiring the other's agreement or judicial oversight.

### Wine Division

The following year, these same partners jointly purchased wine. The other partner, citing the precedent of the cash case, went ahead and unilaterally separated his portion of the wine. This time Rav Papa ruled against him: *man palig lach?* — no one authorized you to divide. When the partner complained that Rav Papa seemed to be ruling against him consistently, Rav Papa explained that he needed to spell out his reasoning explicitly, lest it appear arbitrary. The distinction is this: *zuzei nishkol tavi veshadei chasrei* — money is interchangeable; you can take good money and leave behind bad money, and there is no real difference. But *chamra kuli alma yad'ei de'ika devasim ve'ika delo basim* — everyone knows that some wine is sweeter and some is not, that there is variation in quality. One partner cannot be permitted to appropriate the better barrels unilaterally and leave the inferior ones for the other.

### Coin Grades

Rav Papa extends the principle with additional nuance. Rav Nachman's ruling that *zuzei keman de'paligi damu* applies only when the coins are truly homogeneous: *tavi vetavi, tikuli vetikuli* — all of good quality or all of equal weight. The *Gemara* illustrates this with something analogous to denominations: coins of higher face value are more convenient in some transactions, while lower denominations are more convenient in others. If a partnership holds a mix of denominations, one partner may not unilaterally take all the lower-denomination coins and leave the higher-denomination ones for the other, or vice versa, since the parties may have different practical needs. *Aval tavi vetikuli lo* — if the coins differ in quality or denomination, unilateral division is impermissible even for cash.

## Money Rental

### Rav Chama Case

The *Gemara* now addresses a creative attempt to circumvent the prohibition of *ribbit* by reframing a loan as a rental. Rav Chama would "rent out" his money — *mogar zuzei b'pshita* — charging a small daily fee, calling it a rental rather than interest. The *Gemara* reports that Rav Chama ultimately lost all his money, which is understood as a divine signal that his framing was incorrect and that this practice was in fact prohibited as *ribbit*.

### Tool vs. Money

The *Gemara* asks: what was Rav Chama's reasoning? *Mai shena mimara?* — how is renting money different from renting a hoe? If I can charge a fee for the use of a tool, why not for the use of money? The *Gemara* responds: *mara hadrei be'einei, ve'it lei pachtei* — a hoe is returned as the same object, and it depreciates through use; the rental fee compensates for both the loss of use during the rental period and the wear and tear on the object. Neither is true of money. *Zuzei lo hadrei be'einei* — money is not returned as the same object; the borrower keeps the original coins and returns equivalent ones. Conceptually, therefore, it is a loan, not a rental. And *lo it lei pachtei* — money does not depreciate through use, so the second justification for rental fees is also absent. Calling a loan a rental does not transform its legal character.

### True Rental

There are, however, genuine cases of money rental that are permitted. Tosafot points to the case in the *Tosefta* of a money changer who wishes to borrow coins for his apprentices to practice with — he intends to return the same coins and has no use for the money otherwise. Another legitimate case is where someone needs to display funds to establish creditworthiness or net worth and will never actually spend the money. In these cases, the money is truly being used as an object, returned as the same specific coins, and the arrangement is conceptually a rental rather than a loan. But Rav Chama's practice was none of these — it was lending money with a fee, and no rebranding changes that.

## Third-Party Fees

### Persuasion Fee

Rava now introduces a category of arrangements that are clearly permitted precisely because the *ribbit* flows through a third party rather than directly between borrower and lender. Rava rules: *shari lei le'inish lemeimar lei lechavrei, hei lach arba zuzei ve'ozli leploni zuzei* — it is permitted for one person to say to another, "Here are $4 — go lend Charlie $100." The $4 constitutes payment to the intermediary for his role in facilitating the loan. Charlie repays the lender only the $100, without interest. The lender profits from his $4 payment, but that payment comes from the investor or sponsor, not from Charlie. Since *lo asra Torah ela ribbis haba miloveh lemalveh* — the Torah only prohibits *ribbit* that flows from the borrower to the lender — this arrangement is entirely permissible.

One might wonder whether this principle would permit a fully intermediated structure where the borrower himself pays the intermediary who then pays the lender. Rava addresses this as well: *shari lei le'inish lemeimar lei lechavrei, shakil lach arba zuzei ve'imri lei leploni le'ozfan zuzei* — it is also permitted for the borrower to say to a third party, "Take $4 and go convince Michael to lend me $100." Here the $4 comes from the borrower, but it goes to a third party, not to the lender. *Sachar amira kashakil* — the third party is simply being paid for his service of persuasion, his *dibur*, his advocacy on the borrower's behalf. Since the lender receives nothing extra from the borrower, there is no *ribbit*.

### Abba Mar Case

This principle is illustrated by an incident involving Abba Mar, the son of Rav Papa. Abba Mar would regularly receive a ball of wax — *ugna dikira* — from the wax merchants as a gift, and he would encourage his father to continue lending money to those merchants. When the rabbis pointed out to Rav Papa that his son appeared to be receiving interest payments — benefits flowing from the borrowers to the lender's household — Rav Papa dismissed the concern.

### Interest Flow

*Lo asra Torah ela ribbis haba miloveh lemalveh* — the Torah forbids only *ribbit* that flows from the borrower to the lender. The wax is flowing from the borrowers to a third party, Abba Mar, not to Rav Papa himself. And in Rava's second formulation, the $4 flows from the borrower to a third-party persuader, not to the lender. In neither case does the prohibited flow — from *loveh* to *malveh* — actually occur.

One might still wonder whether sustained, ongoing payments of this kind could give rise to *avak ribbit*, a rabbinic extension of the interest prohibition. But the *Gemara* here appears satisfied that as long as the direct flow between borrower and lender is interrupted by a third party, the core prohibition is not triggered.
