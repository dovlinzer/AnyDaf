# Bava Batra 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Soured Wine & Vessels

### Wine Turns Vinegar

The *daf* opens with a *mishnah* at the bottom of 97b that continues the Gemara's extended discussion of sales involving defective or misrepresented goods, now focusing specifically on wine. The *mishnah* states: *ha-mocher yayin la-chaveiro ve-himtzi* — one who sells wine to a friend and it sours. *Eino chayav be-achrayuto* — the seller bears no responsibility. However, *ve-im yadua she-yeino machmitz* — if it was known that this seller's wine has a propensity for souring, then *harei zeh mekach taut* — it is a void sale. If it can be established through evidence that the souring was a consistent pattern characteristic of this seller's wine, and the buyer was unaware of this, the sale is nullified.

### Aromatic Wine Duration

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-amar lo yayin mevusam ani mocher lach* — if the seller explicitly characterized the wine as particularly aromatic, implying exceptional quality and staying power, then *chayav le-amid lo ad ha-Atzeret* — the seller is obligated to stand behind that representation until *Shavuot*. The Rashbam explains that after *Atzeret* the summer heat sets in and it is entirely expected that wine will sour; the seller therefore bears no responsibility for spoilage that occurs in that season. But if the wine sours before then, the seller must replace it.

### Old & Aged Wine

The *mishnah* also distinguishes between designations of age. *Yashan mishel eshtakad* — if the seller represents the wine as old, it must be at least from the prior year. *U-meyushan mishel gimmel shanim* — if described as aged, it must be from at least three years prior, which the Rashbam explains as meaning from two years ago, counting the current year as the first.

It is worth noting that this *mishnah* does not directly address several issues that were analyzed extensively in the preceding Gemara — whether the wine was sold *lemikpeh* (for cooking or extended use), or the significance of the first or last three days of storage. Those principles were discussed earlier; here the *mishnah* presents its cases more sparingly, and the *rishonim* work to integrate it with the principles already established. Taking the *mishnah* on its own terms: if nothing was specified about prolonged use, one might assume the buyer intended to consume the wine promptly, which would eliminate seller liability. Yet the *mishnah* does not specify how quickly the souring occurred, and that ambiguity is precisely what the Gemara addresses.

### Vessel Ownership Rule

The Gemara opens with a ruling by Rav Yose b'Rabbi Chanina: *lo shanu ela be-kankanim de-loke'ach* — the principle that the seller bears no liability applies only when the wine was transferred into the buyer's vessels. *Aval be-kankanim de-mocher* — but if the wine was sold in the seller's own vessels and the buyer took it in those vessels, then *amar lei ha-chamar chava kankanach chava* — the buyer may say back to the seller: it is your wine, it is your vessels, and it soured — this is clearly your responsibility, and it constitutes a *mekach taut*.

Strikingly, there is no timeframe specified here. The Rashbam explicitly states *afilu zman merubeh* — even after a long time, if it was in the seller's vessels, the buyer may still invoke this argument and declare the sale void. This appears to stand in tension with some of the earlier Gemara's analysis about the three-day window, and the Rashbam labors to reconcile the two passages.

### Lemikpeh Requirement

The Gemara then challenges Rav Yose b'Rabbi Chanina's framing. If the seller's vessels are what create liability, the seller could simply say: *lo ibai lach le-shahuyei* — why did you need to store it? I assumed you would drink it immediately, and if you had, it would have been fine. The Gemara therefore concludes: *tzricha de-amar lei le-mikphei* — the ruling necessarily presupposes that the buyer specified the wine was needed for *lemikpeh*, that is, for cooking wine or some other extended use, meaning it was understood from the outset that the wine needed to last.

This, however, raises a further objection: *u-mai dochkei de-Rav Yose b'Rabbi Chanina le-ukmei le-matnitin be-kankanim de-loke'ach ve-de-amar lei le-mikphei* — why was Rav Yose b'Rabbi Chanina compelled to read the *mishnah* in such a constricted way, requiring both conditions simultaneously — that the buyer's vessels were used *and* that *lemikpeh* was specified? Why not read the *mishnah* as a simpler case: the seller's vessels, no specification of extended use, and the seller is exempt because the buyer could simply have drunk it right away?

### Rava's Logic

Rava answers that the *mishnah* itself forced Rav Yose b'Rabbi Chanina's reading. The *mishnah* states that if it is known that this person's wine has a propensity for souring, it is a *mekach taut*. But if no extended use was specified, the seller could always deflect responsibility by saying: you should not have let it sit. Even in the case of known-souring wine, the seller could argue this. The fact that the *mishnah* treats the known-souring case as clearly a *mekach taut* — without any such deflection being entertained — demonstrates that the *mishnah*'s underlying context is one where extended use was assumed: *shema mina de-amar lei le-mikphei, shema mina*. It must be inferred from the *mishnah* itself that *lemikpeh* was specified. And if so, then the only way the seller avoids liability in the first clause of the *mishnah* is because the wine was transferred to the buyer's vessels — which is Rav Yose b'Rabbi Chanina's ruling.

### Mazal Doctrine

The Gemara notes that this entire analysis stands in tension with the position of Rav Chiya bar Yosef: *chamra mazla de-marei garim* — when wine sours, it is the *mazal* of whoever currently owns it that causes it. Once the wine passes into the buyer's possession, if it sours, that is the buyer's misfortune, not the seller's doing — unless there is something very concrete to pin on the seller, such as a known pattern of souring. According to Rav Chiya bar Yosef, even where *lemikpeh* was specified and even if the wine was in the seller's vessels, once ownership transferred to the buyer, the soured wine is the buyer's bad luck.

This is derived from a *pasuk*: *ve-af ki ha-yayin boged gever yahir* — just as wine rebels and turns to vinegar, so too does this happen as a *mida k'neged mida* to a person who takes on airs and misrepresents himself. The *mazal* of wine follows its owner, and the theological framing of the *pasuk* drives that point home.

### Ethics: Pride & Hypocrisy

The Gemara pursues this theme of *yuhra* — haughtiness and false pretense — into an ethical digression. Rav Mari teaches: *hai man de-yahir afilu inish beiteih lo mekabel* — one who puts on airs cannot be tolerated even by the members of his own household, which the Rashbam understands as referring to his wife. The verse states *gever yahir ve-lo yinaveh* — the haughty man will not dwell — and the Gemara reads this as meaning he cannot even be tolerated in his own home, where those closest to him can see through his pretense.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav extends this further: anyone who elevates himself by dressing like a *talmid chacham* — adopting the dress and markers associated with Torah sages — *ve-eino talmid chacham* — while not in fact being one — *ein machnisin oto bi-mechitzato shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — will not be admitted into God's presence in the world to come. The proof is derived through a *gezeira shava* between *ve-lo yinaveh* and *el neveh kodshecha* — to Your holy dwelling: one who misrepresents himself as a *talmid chacham* forfeits entry into God's sacred space. Crucially, the teaching is not that one must be a *talmid chacham* to merit such closeness — only that one must not falsely present oneself as something one is not.

## Business Arrangements

### Consignment to Retailer

Rava introduces a related case: *hai man de-zavin lei chavisah de-chamra le-chanva'ah ada'ata le-savrei* — one who sells a barrel of wine to a storekeeper with the understanding that the storekeeper will sell it gradually, retail, over time. If *ve-sakif apalga o asilsa* — the wine sours when only half or a third has been sold, *dina hu de-makabel lei minei* — the seller is obligated to take back the barrel and compensate the storekeeper for what remains.

The reason is that selling wine to a storekeeper under such conditions is analogous to the *lemikpeh* case: the seller is implicitly representing that the wine will remain sound for the duration of the expected selling period. The Rashbam reads this as a consignment arrangement where the wine never truly left the seller's ownership, making the obligation to absorb the loss straightforward. Tosafot, however, noting that the Gemara uses the language *de-zavin* — he sold — argues that a genuine sale occurred, but one where the seller's implicit warranty of durability survived the transfer of ownership, analogous to the *kankanim de-loke'ach* dynamic.

### Spigot Exception

This liability has limits. *Ve-lo amaran ela de-la shani be-berza* — this ruling applies only if the storekeeper did not change the spigot. If the storekeeper replaced the spigot, *aval shani be-berza lo* — that breaks the seller's liability. According to Tosafot's reading, this parallels the case of *kankanim de-loke'ach*: the storekeeper took an action that may itself have caused or contributed to the souring, and the seller cannot be held responsible for a deterioration that the buyer may have caused. According to the Rashbam's reading of consignment, the change of spigot would constitute *peshia* — negligence on the storekeeper's part.

### Market Day Limit

Similarly, *ve-lo amaran ela de-la mata yoma de-shuka* — the seller's liability also ends if the market day had already arrived. If a market day came during which all the wine could have been sold, the storekeeper had a full opportunity to liquidate the barrel. If it was not sold by that point, the seller can no longer be held responsible for subsequent spoilage.

### Iska Deal Structure

Rava introduces another case: *hai man de-kabbil chamra ada'ata de-mamti lei le-farvasa de-Zut Shafat* — someone who received wine on the understanding that he would bring it to a particular marketplace, Parvasa de-Zut Shafat, and sell it there. The Rashbam explains this as a classic *iska* arrangement: the originating party owns the wine and provides the capital, the other party acts as the traveling merchant. Structurally, half the wine is treated as a loan to the merchant — now in his ownership, which is why he shares equally in profit and loss on that half — while the other half remains the owner's wine, with the merchant acting as agent. To avoid a *ribis* problem, the owner also pays the merchant for his labor.

In this arrangement, the owner imposed a condition: the wine cannot be sold locally where prices are low, but must be transported to this other market where prices are higher. *Ve-ad mati hasam zil* — but by the time the merchant arrived at the distant market, the price of wine had fallen. *Dinu hu de-mikabel lei* — the law is that the loss falls entirely on the owner who imposed the condition. Since he prevented the merchant from selling the wine locally — even if local prices had risen, the merchant could have sold there — the restriction is what caused the loss, and the owner must absorb it.

### Price Loss Distinction

The Gemara then raises a question: *havi hala mai* — what if, instead of a price decline, the wine actually soured by the time it reached the distant market? Is the analysis the same?

This distinction matters: a price decline is a market fluctuation that might have occurred regardless of where the wine was sold, whereas souring is an intrinsic deterioration of the wine itself. The question is whether the owner who imposed the restriction bears sole responsibility for the spoilage, or whether spoilage is treated differently.

### Shared Spoilage Risk

Rav Hillel reported in the name of Rav Kahana: *hala lo* — in the case of vinegar, the ruling is different. Even though the owner imposed the restriction, if the wine sours that is not his responsibility alone. The loss of spoilage is shared between the two parties equally, just as profits and losses are shared in an *iska* arrangement generally. The fact that the owner imposed the condition does not transform every misfortune into his exclusive liability; wine souring is a different category from a loss directly caused by the restriction.

### Full Seller Liability

The Gemara then presents an alternative view: *ve-ika de-amri afilu hala nami mikabel* — some say that even in the case of spoilage, the owner bears full responsibility. *Ke-man? Ke-Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* — like the position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina. Recall that in the *mishnah*, the seller is exempt from liability only if the wine was transferred to the buyer's vessels; if it remained in the seller's vessels and was sold *lemikpeh*, the seller is responsible. Here too, the *iska* arrangement is analogous to a *lemikpeh* situation — the merchant is holding the wine for an extended period by the owner's design — and the imposition of a restriction on when and where to sell is analogous to the wine remaining in the owner's domain. Therefore, if it sours, the entire loss should fall on the owner, not be shared with the merchant. The two positions thus map cleanly onto the underlying dispute about whether vessel ownership or the *lemikpeh* designation is the operative factor.

## Building Dimensions

### Newlywed & Widow Homes

After concluding the wine discussion, the Gemara turns at the top of 98b to an entirely different subject: minimum dimensions for dwellings. The *mishnah* opens with two scenarios — *beis chasnus* and *beis almanus*: a newlywed's home built for a son, and a widow's house built for a daughter. The background to these scenarios reflects the social norms of the era. When a son married, he would typically bring his bride to live near his parents' home; his father might build a separate wing or small dwelling adjacent to the family property for the newlywed couple. When a daughter's husband died, she would return to her father's household, where a separate structure would be built for her as a widow.

The *mishnah*'s question is: if someone purchases a plot of land or contracts a builder for such a structure without specifying dimensions, what are the minimum dimensions presumed?

### Minimum Size Dispute

*Boneh arba amos al shesh, divrei Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva says such a home is four *amos* by six. Rabbi Yishmael objects: *refes bakar hi zo* — that is a stable, not a home fit for a newlywed couple.

### Stable Dimensions

Following Rabbi Yishmael's objection, the *mishnah* states that one who wishes to build a stable builds four *amos* by six. The Gemara asks who is making this statement — Rabbi Yishmael or Rabbi Akiva? Two readings are offered. On one reading, Rabbi Akiva himself concedes that four by six is the size of a stable, but argues that *pe'amim she-adam oseh diraso ki-refes bakar* — people sometimes make their homes as small as a stable, and that does not make such a dwelling unacceptable as a newlywed's house. On the other reading, it is Rabbi Yishmael saying that a stable is four by six, as proof that Rabbi Akiva's proposed minimum for a newlywed's home is inadequate; Rabbi Akiva might counter that a stable is actually even smaller and that four by six is therefore a passable minimum for a human dwelling.

### House Size Categories

Beyond the disputed minimum, the *mishnah* establishes progressive size categories. A small house (*bayis katan*) is six *amos* by eight. A large house (*gadol*) is eight *amos* by ten. A *tarkalin* — a banquet hall — is ten by ten. The Gemara clarifies that a *tarkalin* is a *kuvsa bei vardi*, a hall adorned with roses, a space for entertaining rather than for ordinary dwelling, and that its square shape (as opposed to rectangular) befits a banquet room. A *kantir* — twelve by twelve — is identified as a *tarbitza de-fadni*, something like the grand courtyard of a mansion.

### Height Formula

For all these structures, the *mishnah* establishes that *rumo ke-chatzi orko ve-chatzi rochvo* — the height of the ceiling must equal half the combined length and width. So a room eight *amos* by ten would require a ceiling height of nine *amos* (half of 18). A room twelve by twelve would require a ceiling of twelve *amos*. These would be considered quite high ceilings, especially for larger rooms.

### Heichal Proportion Proof

Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel cites the *Heichal* — the sanctuary of the *Beis HaMikdash* — as the proof source for this formula. The *Heichal* built by Solomon was twenty *amos* wide and forty *amos* long (sixty total, comprising the outer *Kodesh* and the inner *Kodesh HaKodashim*), with a ceiling height of thirty *amos* — precisely half of twenty plus forty.

The Gemara, however, questions the grammar of the *mishnah*: *re'aya la-davar man katani lah* — who is saying the phrase "proof of this matter"? If it is Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel, then the *mishnah* is set up as his supporting proof for the Tanna Kamma's height formula, and the text means: "What is the proof? Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel says — like the *Heichal*." On this reading, Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel is endorsing the formula.

### Attribution Debate

The alternative reading is that the Tanna Kamma himself says *re'aya la-davar* — there is a proof — but does not specify it, and Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel's subsequent mention of the *Heichal* is actually a challenge: *Atu kol alma ke-vinyan Heichal avdi?* — Does everyone build their homes to the same proportions as the *Heichal*? What kind of proof is this? The ordinary homeowner's standard cannot be derived from the grandeur of the Temple sanctuary. On this reading, Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel is not endorsing the height formula but questioning its source.

### Roof Beams Standard

A *baraisa* records an alternative standard: *Chachamim omrim rumo ke-korosav* — the Sages say the ceiling height is determined by the roof beams. The beams of a building were laid across the width of the structure, running parallel to the shorter dimension and resting in holes in the walls. According to this standard, the height should match the length of those beams, which corresponds to the width of the room — not half the combined width and length.

### Beams & Wall Thickness

The Gemara asks: if the beams run across the width, why not simply say the height equals the width? Two reasons are offered. First, *basisa me-ilai revach* — stone buildings of antiquity were built so that the walls tapered as they rose; the base was thicker and the top narrower, meaning the interior width measured at floor level was shorter than the width measured at ceiling level. Measuring by the beams captures the actual usable interior width at the point where the ceiling sits. Second, *mishum de-ika bei kavei* — the beams are not exactly equal to the interior width because they are inserted into holes carved into the walls on each side, making them slightly longer than the simple interior measurement. The phrase *rumo ke-korosav* precisely captures both of these nuances that the phrase "height equals width" would miss.

## Heichal Contradiction

### Verse Contradiction

The *daf* concludes with Rav Chanina's resolution of an apparent contradiction in the biblical descriptions of the *Heichal*. One verse describes Solomon's Temple as *shishim ama orko ve-esrim rachbo u-shloshim ama komaso* — sixty *amos* long, twenty *amos* wide, and thirty *amos* high. This is the basis of the height formula: thirty equals half of sixty plus twenty. However, a second verse, describing the *Dvir* — the *Kodesh HaKodashim* — states *esrim ama orech ve-esrim ama rochav ve-esrim ama komaso*: twenty by twenty by twenty. The *Kodesh HaKodashim* occupied twenty of the sixty *amos* in length, leaving forty *amos* for the outer *Kodesh*. The full structure is sixty by twenty, and the *Heichal* was thirty *amos* high. But the *Kodesh HaKodashim* alone should also have been thirty *amos* high — yet the verse says twenty. Where did the other ten *amos* of height go?

### Cherubim Height Measure

Rav Chanina answers: *ki kachashiv mi-sfas kruvim u-le-mala* — when the verse measures the twenty *amos* of height in the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, it is measuring not from the floor but from the top of the *keruvim*. Solomon built two golden *keruvim* standing on the floor of the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, each ten *amos* tall. From the top of the *keruvim* to the ceiling was twenty *amos*, giving a total floor-to-ceiling height of thirty — consistent with the first verse.

### Space Symmetry Principle

This immediately prompts the question: *mai ka mashma lan* — what principle is being conveyed by measuring from the tops of the *keruvim* rather than from the floor? The answer is found at the top of 98a: *lemata ke-lemala* — the space below mirrors the space above. *Ma le-mala ein meshamesh klum, af lemata ein meshamesh klum* — just as the twenty *amos* above the *keruvim* are entirely empty, unoccupied by any object, so too the ten *amos* below — occupied by the *keruvim* themselves and the *aron* — function in the same miraculous way as empty space. The *keruvim* and the *aron*, despite their physical presence, are understood to occupy no space at all. This is the famous Talmudic tradition that the *aron* did not take up measurable space in the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, and the Gemara will continue with this point on the following *daf*.
