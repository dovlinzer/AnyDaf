# Nazir 29b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father Making Son Nazir

### Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish

The *sugya* opens by asking whether the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — regarding whether a father's power to impose *nezirut* on his son is a special *halacha* particular to *nezirut*, or whether it derives from the rabbinic concept of *chinuch* — maps onto a parallel dispute among *Tannaim*. The *Gemara* introduces a *braita*: *ad masai madzir beno benazir* — until what age may a father make his son a *nazir*? Rabbi holds: until the son brings two pubic hairs, i.e., until he becomes a full adult at the age of thirteen. Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda holds: until he reaches *onat nedarim* — the stage at which a minor may make binding vows.

This concept of *onat nedarim* requires some elaboration. A child — whether boy or girl — reaches *onat nedarim* one year before the age of majority: a year before a girl's *bat mitzvah* and a year before a boy's *bar mitzvah*. At this point, provided the child understands that he or she is making a vow to God and has basic comprehension of the act, the vow is considered binding. This status is known as *mufla samuch l'ish* — the ability to enunciate a vow while not yet a full adult. Whether this category is *d'oraisa* or *derabbanan* is itself a subject of considerable debate, as we will see.

### Tannaim: 13 vs. 12

The *Gemara* asks whether this *Tannaitic* dispute mirrors the *Amoraic* one. The initial reading suggests that Rabbi's position — that the father retains power until the son becomes a full adult with two pubic hairs — is consistent with viewing the father's authority as a *halacha*, an intrinsic legal power over his minor son. Even if the son has already reached *onat nedarim* and can make his own vows to some degree, the father's power is conceived broadly: the son is still a minor in the overall legal sense, and the father retains dominion over him until the son becomes a full adult. Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda's position, by contrast — that the father's power ends already at *onat nedarim* — seems consistent with the *chinuch* framework: once the son has some independent standing in the domain of vows, the father's role as educator in that area becomes inapplicable.

### Halacha Debate: Mufla

The *Gemara* rejects this initial alignment, however. It offers a first alternative resolution: *d'chulei alma halacha hi b'nazir* — perhaps all agree that the father's power is a *halacha*, not merely *chinuch*. The debate concerns a different question entirely: what is the legal status of *mufla samuch l'ish*?

According to Rabbi, *mufla samuch l'ish* is *derabbanan*. The son's independent standing in vows at age twelve is only a rabbinic enactment. Since the father's authority to impose *nezirut* is a *halacha* — operating at the *d'oraisa* level — it overrides the son's merely rabbinic independence. Hence the father retains his power even past *onat nedarim*, all the way until the son is a full adult.

According to Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda, *mufla samuch l'ish* is *d'oraisa*. If the son possesses biblically recognized independence in the domain of vows, then even a *halacha* granting the father power over his son should not extend into an area where the son is biblically autonomous. Since *nezirut* is itself a form of *neder*, and the son is independently recognized by the *Torah* as a party to vows, the father cannot impose *nezirut* on him once he reaches *onat nedarim*. The dividing line is therefore not *chinuch* versus *halacha*, but rather *d'oraisa* versus *derabbanan* status of the son's independence.

A natural question arises: does the *Gemara* actually use the phrase *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, or merely *halacha*? In fact, the *Gemara* uses only the word *halacha*. The assumption that *halacha* implies *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* and therefore *d'oraisa* status is standard, but two qualifications deserve mention. First, there are well-known instances where *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* is used rhetorically rather than literally — the *Mishnah* in *Eduyos* speaks of *halacha* that Ammon and Moav tithe *ma'aser ani* in the seventh year, a ruling that is clearly *derabbanan*, yet the phrase is used. Second, some authorities, including the *Netziv*, suggest that *halacha* in certain contexts may simply denote an ancient tradition without carrying the full technical weight of *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* here clearly operates on the assumption that *halacha* functions at the *d'oraisa* level, since the entire dynamic depends on the father's authority being able to override the son's *derabbanan* independence — not his *d'oraisa* independence. What may further support treating it as *d'oraisa* here is that we are not dealing with a wholly new independent ruling, but rather with a parameter internal to the *d'oraisa* laws of *nezirut* themselves — a detail about the scope of the *Torah*'s framework already in place.

### Chinuch Framework

The *Gemara* then presents a second approach: *d'chulei alma k'dei l'chancho b'mitzvot* — perhaps all agree the father's authority is based on *chinuch*, and the entire institution is *derabbanan*. Moreover, *mufla samuch l'ish* is also *derabbanan* on all views. We therefore have two rabbinic forces in tension: the father's *chinuch* obligation on one side, and the son's rabbinic independence in vows on the other.

Rabbi holds that in this collision, we tip in favor of *chinuch*: *ati chinuch d'rabbanan v'dachi mufla samuch l'ish d'rabbanan*. The father's *chinuch* role prevails, and the son's limited rabbinic independence in vows does not terminate the father's power. This approach also helps explain why *chinuch* in the context of *nezirut* can override other principles — such as *chullin ba'azarah* and *shechitah l'of* — that are similarly *derabbanan*: we are already in a domain of rabbinic accommodation.

Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda holds the reverse: once the rabbis recognized the son's independent standing in the area of *nedarim*, that recognition itself removes him from the *chinuch* framework in this domain. *Chinuch* is predicated on the child being a non-agent in the relevant area. Once the child can act independently — even rabbinically — the father's *chinuch* role falls away.

The *Gemara* concludes that the *Tannaitic* dispute cannot be cleanly mapped onto a single underlying question. Either it is about the *d'oraisa* versus *derabbanan* status of *mufla samuch l'ish* within an overall *halacha* framework, or it is about which rabbinic principle prevails when two *derabbanan* institutions conflict. The lines do not divide neatly.

## Rabbi Chanina Case

### Chanina's Story

The *Gemara* introduces a *braita* containing a real-life illustration of this debate: *maaseh b'Rabbi Chanina* — there was a case involving Rabbi Chanina whose father had made him a *nazir*. He was brought before Rabban Gamliel, who wished to investigate the matter. Rabban Gamliel sought to determine whether the boy had already reached adult status when his father imposed the *nezirut* — because if the son had already been a full adult at that point, the father's act would have been ineffective. The question, as framed by the two opinions, was either whether the boy had two pubic hairs (Rabbi's position — the test for full adulthood), or whether he had already reached *onat nedarim* (Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda's position — the earlier threshold).

The young Chanina, demonstrating remarkable presence of mind, said to Rabban Gamliel: do not trouble yourself with the examination. If I was a minor when my father imposed *nezirut* on me, then I am a *nazir* on account of my father — *eheye bishvil aba*. And if I was already an adult, then I accept *nezirut* upon myself now regardless — *eheye bishvil atzmi*. Either way, I am a *nazir*.

Rabban Gamliel rose and kissed him on his head, and declared: *muvtach ani beze sheyoreh halacha beyisrael* — I am confident that this young man will one day teach *halacha* in Israel. And indeed, we are told, it was not long before that prediction came true.

### Mid-Period Majority

The *Gemara* initially poses what the *Rosh* characterizes as a somewhat throwaway question — noting that it is easy to understand Chanina's reasoning according to Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda, since reaching *onat nedarim* is that opinion's threshold, and Chanina's statement that he would accept *nezirut* himself if he had reached *onat nedarim* fits naturally. The harder question, according to Rabbi, is this: if the test is two pubic hairs and full adulthood, then when Chanina said "if I am an adult, I accept it for myself," he was presumably speaking of that same standard. But then the *Gemara* quickly moves to what is the real question.

The *Gemara* identifies three scenarios: the son was already an adult when his father made him a *nazir*; he became an adult after the thirty days of *nezirut* concluded; or he became an adult in the middle of the thirty-day period. The first two cases are straightforward. If he was already an adult at the outset, his acquiescence — whether silent or verbal — renders it his own *nezirut*, and it concludes after thirty days. If he became an adult only after the thirty days ended, the *nezirut* was entirely that of the father and it concludes naturally.

But the middle case is genuinely difficult. The *Tosefta* makes explicit what the *Gemara* assumes: if a son becomes a full adult in the middle of the *nezirut* his father imposed, becoming an adult in itself terminates whatever his father had begun — just as the *Mishnah* earlier discussed how the son's protest or the relatives' shaving terminates the *nezirut*. Reaching full legal adulthood functions as an automatic termination, even without any explicit act of protest. This creates a significant problem for Chanina's declaration.

### Termination Criteria

The *Gemara* now asks why the analysis differs depending on whether the dividing line is *shtei se'arot* (two pubic hairs, full adulthood) or *onat nedarim* (the ability to make vows). The answer turns on a fundamental distinction between these two thresholds.

Reaching *onat nedarim* grants the son a limited form of independence in the specific domain of vows. But it does not change his overall legal status. He is still a minor in every other sense. Consequently, while *onat nedarim* would be strong enough to prevent the father from imposing *nezirut* at the outset — since in the area of *nedarim* the son is now an independent actor — it is not strong enough to terminate a *nezirut* already underway. The father was fully empowered to impose *nezirut* before *onat nedarim* arrived. The fact that the son now has some limited independence in the realm of vows does not retroactively undo what was validly imposed. His fundamental legal status remains that of a minor.

By contrast, *shtei se'arot* — full adulthood — represents a complete transformation of legal status. The son is now a new legal person in every domain, not merely in one limited area. That complete transformation is powerful enough to terminate whatever the father had begun, even mid-period, without any explicit protest.

### Counting Periods

This asymmetry resolves the *Gemara*'s problem according to Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda. Under that opinion, reaching *onat nedarim* mid-period does not interrupt the *nezirut* at all. The thirty days run continuously from when the father imposed it. So Chanina's declaration — "either way I am a *nazir*" — works perfectly: whether the father's *nezirut* was valid from the start or not, thirty days from the outset suffices.

Under Rabbi's opinion, however, things are more complicated. If Chanina became an adult in the middle of the thirty days, the *nezirut* imposed by his father ended at that point. He would need to begin a fresh thirty-day count from the moment of adulthood, and bring his *korbanot* at the end of that period. So he cannot simply say "either way thirty days from the outset." He would need to account for the possibility that he became an adult partway through.

The *Gemara* resolves this: according to Rabbi, one would have to be more precise. If it became clear that Chanina became an adult mid-period, the appropriate approach would be to complete the father's thirty days, and then — if adulthood had not been reached at the very outset — accept a new *nezirut* from that moment forward, bringing *korbanot* only at the conclusion of an additional thirty days. In practical terms, since the precise moment of adulthood cannot always be established, one might need to accept *nezirut* and bring *korbanot* in a way that covers both possibilities. The *Gemara* leaves the precise resolution open but establishes that unlike Rabbi Yose b'Rebbi Yehuda's clean answer, Rabbi's position requires two separate counting periods depending on when adulthood was reached.

One might raise a comparison to *giur katan* — a minor who converts and upon reaching majority has the option to repudiate the conversion. There are genuine structural similarities: in both cases, a status was imposed on someone who was a minor, and full adulthood triggers a reassessment. Similarly, the *Minchas Chinuch* raises the celebrated question of a child who becomes a *bar mitzvah* in the middle of *sefiras ha'omer* — does the counting before adulthood count toward the *d'oraisa* obligation of counting fifty days, or was it entirely without legal meaning? The same logic applies here.

What emerges from this *sugya* is a subtle distinction: the threshold that prevents imposition going forward (*onat nedarim*) is not necessarily the same threshold that terminates an ongoing *nezirut* (*shtei se'arot*). Nor is a minor's right to protest — which can end the *nezirut* even without adulthood — identical to the automatic termination triggered by becoming an adult. Each operates by its own logic.

## Son Using Father's Funds

### Mishna Scenarios

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *Mishnah*, which addresses a distinct but related father-son question: may a son use funds that his father had designated for his own *nezirut* sacrifices? The *Mishnah* opens with the ruling that a son may do so — but a daughter may not. The details of how and when this applies are then spelled out.

The *Mishnah* distinguishes two scenarios. In the first, which Rabbi Yosi presents as the case where the mechanism does *not* apply, a son becomes a *nazir* after his father has already died, and declares: *hareini nazir al menas she'agale'ach al ma'os abba* — I accept *nezirut* on condition that I use my father's funds. In the second scenario, which Rabbi Yosi identifies as the case where it does apply: the son and the father were both *nezirim* simultaneously, the father set aside unspecified funds — *ma'os setumim* — for his own *nezirut*, and then the father died.

It should be noted that this is the *girsa* supported by the *braita* quoted shortly thereafter in the *Gemara*, which is the reverse of what appears in some printed versions of the *Mishnah*. Relying on that *braita*, where Rabbi Yosi's position is stated explicitly, we read the *Mishnah* as well with Rabbi Yosi holding that only the simultaneous case works — not the post-death acceptance.

What underlies the difference between these two scenarios? When father and son are *nezirim* at the same time, there is something that might be described as a family religious undertaking — a shared *nezirut* enterprise. In that context, when the father dies, the funds he designated flow to the son as a natural extension of what had already been a shared process. When the son only accepts *nezirut* after the father's death, he is an entirely independent actor, and the father's designated funds — which are *hekdesh*, consecrated property — cannot simply be redirected for someone else's *korbanot*.

There is also a first version of this mechanism, which in the *Mishnah* as initially read seems to be the case that works: the son stipulates from the outset that he will use the father's money. In that version, the logic is different. Unspecified funds — *ma'os setumim* — have a certain indeterminacy about their destination; they normally go to an *olah*. If the son at the moment of accepting his own *nezirut* declares that his *nezirut* is predicated on using those funds, he creates a link between his *nezirut* and those funds from the beginning.

### Halacha: Son Not Daughter

The *Gemara* asks for the rationale underlying the son's ability to use his father's funds. Rabbi Yochanan answers simply: *halacha hi b'Nazir* — it is a *halacha* specific to *nezirut*. There is no *pasuk*, no *chinuch* argument, no logical derivation. This is a traditional ruling. Notably, unlike other places where Rabbi Yochanan offers *halacha* as one possible explanation alongside others, here it appears to be the only available answer, and Reish Lakish offers no alternative.

The *Gemara* then asks: is it not obvious that only a son and not a daughter may use the father's funds? A son inherits his father; a daughter does not, except in the absence of sons. Since these are *hekdesh* funds, the ability to use them follows an inheritance-like logic, and of course a daughter inherits differently. The *Gemara* responds: *lo tzricha d'leis lei ela barta* — the *chiddush* is needed for the case where the father had only a daughter, who would in that case inherit all his other property. One might think that since the *halacha* is framed in terms of inheritance and she is the heir, she should be entitled. The *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — or the *halacha* itself — tells us this is not so. The right belongs specifically to a son, not to a daughter, even when she is the sole heir.

This is a striking ruling. The *halacha* is acknowledged to be somewhat arbitrary in its parameters — there is no broader principle that straightforwardly generates these details. That is precisely what makes it a *halacha*: it is an ancient tradition whose contours are simply received rather than derived.

### Braita Positions

The *Gemara* then raises the question: do the *rabbanan* actually disagree with Rabbi Yosi, and if so, on which point? As noted, Rabbi Yosi (working with the *girsa* supported by the *braita*) holds that the mechanism works when the son accepts *nezirut* after the father's death with explicit stipulation. The rabbis might agree with this and add that it also works when they were *nezirim* simultaneously — a broader position. Or the rabbis might hold the exact mirror image of Rabbi Yosi, saying it works only when they were *nezirim* simultaneously and not after death. Or there may be an even broader position that both scenarios work.

The *braita* is then cited: it presents Rabbi Yosi's position explicitly — the post-death stipulation works; simultaneous *nezirut* does not. It then records that Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Yehuda all hold that the simultaneous case also works — *zehu shemegalei'ach al maos aviv*. What remains unclear is whether these latter authorities also include the post-death case or limit themselves to the simultaneous scenario. The *Gemara* notes this ambiguity and does not fully resolve which cases the *rabbanan* include or exclude. What is visible is the range: Rabbi Yosi anchors one position; Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Yehuda take a broader view regarding simultaneous *nezirut*; and the exact scope of their disagreement with Rabbi Yosi remains partially open.

## Multiple Sons & Yerusha

### Two Sons Division

With the basic framework established, the *Gemara* turns to a series of questions probing how far the analogy to *yerusha* — inheritance law — governs the operation of this *halacha*. These questions are raised as *ba'yot* by *Rabbah* and *Rava*.

The first: what if the father left two sons, both of whom are *nezirim* and both of whom wish to use their father's designated funds? Do we say that this entire institution has nothing to do with *yerusha* — in which case it is simply first come, first served: *kol de-kadim gille'ach gille'ach*, whoever brings his *korbanot* first gets to use the funds? Or do we say that *yerushah gemiri lah* — this *halacha* is nonetheless shaped by the principles of inheritance — in which case each son receives half?

One might further ask: at what point must both sons be *nezirim* in order for both to assert a claim? If one son becomes a *nazir* twenty days into the other's *nezirut*, or after the first son's thirty days are already complete but before the first son has brought his *korbanot*, does the second son have standing to claim half? The *Gemara* does not explore these complications here, focusing on the simpler case where both sons are *nezirim* simultaneously. The question of principle remains: is this a pure *halacha* with no *yerusha* logic embedded in it, or does it import *yerusha* principles?

### Bechor Double Portion

*Rava* sharpens the question: what if one son is a *bekhor* — a firstborn — and the other is a *pashut*, an ordinary son? By the laws of *yerusha*, the *bekhor* receives a double portion: *pi shenayim*. Does that double portion apply here as well?

If we say *halakhah gemiri lah* — this is a pure *halacha* with no *yerusha* logic — then perhaps the sons divide equally, and the *bekhor* has no special claim. If we say *yerushah hi* — it follows *yerusha* principles — then the *bekhor* should receive twice what his brother receives of the father's *hekdesh* funds, just as he would receive a double portion of ordinary estate property.

But the *Gemara* then adds a further refinement, reflecting the analysis of the *Rosh*. Even if we generally apply *yerusha* principles, perhaps the *bekhor*'s double portion is a special case that does not extend to *hekdesh*. The *bekhor*'s entitlement to *pi shenayim* is derived from the verse *b'chol asher yimatzei lo* — from whatever is found, whatever is available to him. The word implies accessible, available assets. *Hekdesh* funds, however, are not "available" in the ordinary sense — they are pre-consecrated property. One might argue that the *bekhor*'s special double portion, grounded in this language of availability, simply does not apply to *hekdesh* funds, even if the general *yerusha* principle of division between sons does.

The *Rosh* argues that the phrase *u-ve-chullin hu de-it leh pi shenayim aval be-hekdesh lo* — that the *bekhor* gets a double portion from *chullin* but not from *hekdesh* — is not raising an entirely new question but rather clarifying the question about the *bekhor*. It is not, as *Tosafos* suggests, a matter of how the father verbally designated the funds (whether he said *nezirusi* versus *korbanos nezirusi*). The *Rosh* dismisses that reading as *pirush zar* — a strained explanation — since there is no meaningful legal difference between those formulations. Rather, the question is structural: even granting that *yerusha* principles govern the division of funds between sons, does the *bekhor*'s specific entitlement to *pi shenayim*, which is textually anchored in the concept of available assets, extend into the realm of *hekdesh*? That is the unresolved question.

### Nazir Types

The *Gemara* then asks a further question: what if father and son are different *types* of *nezirim*? The case: the father is a *nazir olam* — a lifelong *nazir* — and the son is a *nazir stam*, a standard thirty-day *nazir*. Or the reverse. Can funds designated for one type of *nezirut* be used by someone observing a different type?

A brief clarification: a *nazir olam* does not bring *korbanot* at the conclusion of a fixed term, since there is no fixed term. Rather, a *nazir olam* takes a haircut periodically — once every thirty days by some views, once a year by others — and brings *korbanot* at that time. So the question of designated funds is still relevant.

The question is whether the *halacha* allowing the son to use the father's funds was formulated specifically for standard *nezirut* — *stam nezirut* — or whether it extends to any combination of *nezirut* types. *Tosafos* further notes that one might even question whether this *halacha* applies when both are *nazir olam* — perhaps it was specifically calibrated for standard *nezirut* alone.

### Tamei/Tahor Transfer

Finally, *Rav Ashi* poses the most acute version of the question: what if the father's funds were not merely designated for a different *type* of *nezirut*, but were designated for a qualitatively different sacrificial purpose? Specifically, if the father had become *tamei* — ritually impure — and set aside funds for *korbanot* of *nezirut tumah* (which involve different animals, including birds), and the son's *nezirut* is *nezirut tahara* — pure *nezirut* — may the son use those funds? The *korbanot* of *nezirut tumah* and *nezirut tahara* are not merely different in magnitude or proportion; they are categorically distinct offerings. And the reverse: if the father's funds were for *nezirut tahara* and the son's *nezirut* is *tamei* — what then?

The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. The questions about two sons, the *bekhor*, the different types of *nezirut*, and the *tamei/tahor* transfer remain open, marking the close of the tractate's final discussion.
