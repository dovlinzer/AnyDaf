# Zevachim 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Camps Structure

### Ohel Moed Overview

The daf begins at the bottom of 116b, at the two dots, picking up the progression from before *ohel moed* to the period of *ohel moed* itself. The *ohel moed* represents the paradigmatic setup: *kodshei kodashim* are eaten within the space of the *chatzer* of the *ohel moed*, while *kodshei kalim* — essentially the *shelamim* — are eaten throughout all of *machaneh yisrael*. During this period, private *bamot* are prohibited.

### Kodshei Kalim Debate

The Gemara opens with the statement: *kodshim kalim ne'echalim bechol machaneh yisrael* — the lighter sacrifices may be eaten wherever the entire camp of Israel is situated, which in later generations translates to all of Jerusalem. Rav Huna then makes a striking remark: *bechol mekomos Yisrael* — in all places of Israel — and adds, *aval machaneh lo hava*, suggesting that there was no defined concept of *machaneh yisrael* even when the camp existed in the wilderness. This is a puzzling formulation.

Rav Nachman challenges Rav Huna: *u-machanos bamidbar lo hava?* — surely there were camps in the wilderness, and surely *machaneh yisrael* carried halakhic significance for *kodshei kalim*? He adduces a *braita*: the camps in the wilderness are the paradigm for the later structure of zones in Jerusalem. The *braita* then spells out the correspondence.

### Wilderness to Jerusalem

The *braita* establishes the three-camp parallel explicitly. From the beginning of Jerusalem until the Temple Mount (*Har ha-Bayis*) corresponds to *machaneh yisrael* — this is where *kodshei kalim* are eaten. From the Temple Mount until the Gate of Nikanor (*sha'ar nikanor*), the entrance into the *Azarah*, corresponds to *machaneh leviyah* — a buffer zone directly surrounding the Temple, even though the Levites did not literally inhabit it. From the Gate of Nikanor inward — where the *mizbei'ach* stands — corresponds to *machaneh shechinah*, paralleling the area within the curtains (*kla'im*) surrounding the *ohel moed* in the wilderness.

The Gemara then corrects Rav Huna's formulation: the point is not that *machaneh yisrael* was absent in the wilderness, but rather that it was not a fixed geographic location. Wherever the camp relocated, that place became *machaneh yisrael*. The Gemara asks: is this not obvious? The answer is that there is a genuine question about status in transit. When the cloud moved and the people began traveling, one who had begun eating *kodshei kalim* was now leaving the geographic coordinates of the original encampment — perhaps that constituted a violation of *yotzei*, taking the sacred food outside its permitted zone?

The *chiddush* is that even while in transit, if one remains within the camp, it retains the status of *machaneh yisrael*. The verse states: *v'nasa ohel moed* — even though the *ohel moed* is traveling, it remains *ohel moed*. Tosafos notes, however, that while *machaneh yisrael* retains its status in transit, *machaneh shechinah* does not, since once the structure is disassembled, the zone of the *ohel moed* itself is no longer operative — a person cannot meaningfully remain "within" *machaneh shechinah* as it moves. An interesting corollary question arises: could one bring a private *bamah* while in transit? The Gemara does not address this.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai adds that in the later Temple period there was an additional zone, the *cheil* — the wall surrounding *Ezrat Nashim* — which represents a level of sanctity higher than *Har ha-Bayis* but not yet *Ezrat Yisrael*. He notes that there was no specific punishment (*onesh*) for entering this zone while *tamei*, and it is generally understood that this higher level is of rabbinic status.

### Shilo Camps Question

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai then turns to the period of Shilo. Shilo was a precursor to Jerusalem — *bamot* were prohibited during that era, *kodshei kodashim* could only be consumed within the precincts of the *mishkan*, and *kodshim kalim* had defined zones. Yet Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai states that in Shilo there were only two camps, not three. The question immediately arises: which camp was absent?

The analysis suggests that *machaneh leviyah* is the most expendable from the perspective of *korbanot*. For sacrificial purposes, one needs *machaneh shechinah* to define where *kodshei kodashim* are consumed, and *machaneh yisrael* to define where *kodshim kalim* may be eaten. *Machaneh leviyah* might seem dispensable. Rabbah, however, argues the opposite: *mistavra de-machaneh leviyah havei* — it actually makes more sense that *machaneh leviyah* was present, and that *machaneh yisrael* was what was absent.

### Machaneh Leviyah Necessity

Rabbah's reasoning turns not on *korbanot* but on *tumah*. The Torah commands: *vayishalchu min ha-machaneh kol tzarua v'chol zav v'chol tamei lanefesh* — they shall be sent out of the camp. However, a *zav* and a *tamei meis* are permitted in *machaneh yisrael*; only a *metzora* is expelled from all three camps. The Torah further states *v'lo yetame'u et machanehem* — implying that each category of *tamei* person is sent out of a different number of camps: *ten machaneh lazeh u-machaneh lazeh* — give one camp to this one and a camp to that one. If there were no *machaneh leviyah*, then a *zav* and a *tamei meis* would only be expelled from *machaneh shechinah*, since they are permitted in *machaneh yisrael*. This would deprive them of a second camp to be sent out of, undermining the Torah's framework of graduated expulsion.

Rava presses back: if *machaneh yisrael* were absent instead, a *zav* and a *metzora* would both be expelled to the same zone — outside *machaneh leviyah* — which would violate the *metzora*'s status of *badad yeshev*, the principle that the *metzora* must dwell in a zone not designated for any other *tamei* person.

The Gemara's resolution: in Shilo, all three camps existed for *tumah* purposes, but only two of the three applied to the law of *miklat* — sanctuary. The statement that Shilo had only *shtei machanos* means it had only two camps for the purpose of providing refuge to an unintentional killer.

This gives rise to a larger observation: the primary halakhic significance of the camp structure, as the Torah presents it, may be the *tumah* framework — zones of permitted and prohibited entry for various categories of impurity — rather than the framework of where *korbanot* may be consumed. Indeed, immediately after establishing the camp structure in *Bamidbar*, the Torah commands the expulsion of *teme'im*, not the eating of sacrifices. The eating of *korbanot* within defined zones may operate on a distinct conceptual basis — perhaps connected to sight-lines or proximity to the *mikdash* rather than the formal camp designation.

## Cities of Refuge

### Miklat in Midbar

The Gemara now addresses a third dimension of *machaneh leviyah*: its function as a place of sanctuary (*miklat*) for an unintentional killer. The verse states: *va-samti lecha makom asher yanus shamah* — I will designate for you a place to which he shall flee. The word *lecha* — "for you" — addresses Moshe directly, and the *braita* explains: *mekoma* — your place, meaning where Moshe and the Levites dwelt, namely *machaneh leviyah*, would already function as a *miklat* during the wilderness period. Even before the establishment of the formal *arei miklat* across the Jordan, *machaneh leviyah* provided sanctuary. The verse's phrase *asher yanus shamah* — "that he shall flee there" — teaches that even in the wilderness this was operative.

This generates an important conceptual point: the Torah's institution of *arei miklat* may be built upon the paradigm of the space surrounding the *mikdash*. When *Chazal* teach that all forty-two cities of the Levites serve as *arei miklat* — not only the six designated cities — and that *machaneh leviyah* itself served as a *miklat*, this suggests that the *miklat* institution is fundamentally connected to sacred space, to the camp of the Levites surrounding the sanctuary.

### Ben Levi Refuge

A consequence follows: what happens when a *ben Levi* kills accidentally? Normally a killer flees to a different city of refuge. But the *ben Levi* already lives in a Levite city. The *baraita* teaches: *ben Levi she-harag goleh mi-pelekh le-felekh* — a Levite who kills goes into exile from one district to another. This reflects what happened in the wilderness, where there was only one *machaneh leviyah*: since there was nowhere else to go, the concept of exile within one's own territory must be valid. Hence: *ve-im galah le-pilko pilko kolto* — if he exiled himself within his own city, even that city absorbs him as a sanctuary.

### Internal Exile

This principle extends beyond the *ben Levi*. Anyone who already lives in an *ir miklat* and kills accidentally may go into exile within that same city, simply by relocating to a different neighborhood. One might assume this is only *bedi'avad* — a post-facto allowance when emigrating to another city is not feasible. Tosafos, however, raises the possibility that it could be *lechatchilah*, a legitimate primary option. The cost of this choice, though, is permanent: one who exiles within one's own city must forever remain barred from re-entering the neighborhood from which one fled. If one wished to retain freedom of movement throughout the city, one would need to uproot entirely and go to a different city. It is therefore an open question whether the two options — internal exile versus exile to another city — are halakhically equivalent alternatives, or whether external exile is preferred and internal exile is merely tolerated.

### Scriptural Source

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika provides the scriptural basis: *ki b'ir miklato yeshev* — he shall dwell in the city of his refuge. The phrase *ir shekaltato kvar* — the city that has already absorbed him — teaches that one who is already in the city can remain there even if it is his own city of origin. This is a remarkable *chiddush*: the verse anticipates and permits internal exile by specifying "the city that has [already] given you sanctuary."

The broader takeaway is that *machaneh leviyah* during the period of the *mishkan* served as the prototype for the *ir miklat* institution. The space around the Temple in Jerusalem presumably carried the same status, functioning as a sanctuary zone. In Shilo, however, this particular function was absent: even though *machaneh leviyah* existed for the purposes of *tumah*, it did not operate as a *miklat*. The standard *arei miklat* — the six designated cities and the other Levite cities — remained available; what was absent was the zone immediately surrounding the *mishkan* at Shilo serving as sanctuary space.

## Bamot Permitted

### Free-Will on Bamah

The Gemara now turns to the period of *heter bamot* — when private and central *bamot* were permitted — and asks what sacrifices could be offered in each setting. A *braita* states: *kol neder u-nedavah haya kareiv ba-bamah* — anything brought as a free-will offering, a *neder* or *nedavah*, could be offered on a *bamah*. Practically this means an *olah* and *shelamim*. A *chatat* or *asham* — obligatory sin offerings — could not be brought on a private *bamah*, since *she-ein neder u-nedavah ein kareiv ba-bamah*: if it is not a free-will offering it cannot be brought.

### Minchah & Nezirut

Two additional categories require separate analysis: *minchah* and *nezirut*. A *minchah* is entirely free-will in character, yet it is not a *zevach* — an animal sacrifice. Some of the relevant verses concerning *bamot* speak specifically of slaughtering (*vayizbechu et zivcheihem*), leading some to conclude that *minchot* were not permitted on a *bamah*. This exclusion has socioeconomic implications: the *minchah* and bird-offerings were the option for those who could not afford larger animals, so excluding them from the *bamah* effectively limited private *bamah* offerings to those of means.

Additionally, if one does permit *minchot* on a *bamah*, one encounters the question of *kodshei kodashim*: the remnants of a *minchah* may only be consumed within the zone of the *mikdash*, so permitting *minchah* on a private *bamah* generates difficult questions about where those remnants could be eaten.

Rabbi Meir holds that both *minchah* and *nezirut* could be brought on a *bamah*. The Nazirite vow begins as a free-will act; Rabbi Meir treats the resulting sacrifices accordingly. The Chachamim disagree, holding: *lo kareiv yachid ela olot u-shelamim bilvad* — an individual brings nothing but *olot* and *shelamim*.

### Rabbi Yehudah Gilgal

Rabbi Yehudah introduces a distinct category of analysis. He is not discussing the private *bamah* but the central *bamah* — the public sanctuary. He states: *kol she-tzibur v'yachid makrivin b'ohel moed she-bamidbar makrivin b'ohel moed she-bagilgal* — everything that both the community and the individual could bring at the *ohel moed* in the wilderness, they brought at the *ohel moed* at Gilgal. The central location at Gilgal functioned identically to the *mishkan* in the wilderness — every communal sacrifice, and every individual sacrifice, could be brought there. The sole difference between the wilderness period and the Gilgal period was the permissibility of private *bamot*; the central location itself was unrestricted. For his private *bamah*, Rabbi Yehudah agrees with the Chachamim: *lo hayah makriv aleha ella olah u-shelamim* — only *olah* and *shelamim*.

The implication of Rabbi Yehudah's position is that if an individual wished to bring a *korban chatat* or *asham*, he could simply go to Gilgal and bring it there.

### Chachamim Position

The Chachamim respond: *kol she-hatzibur makrivin b'ohel moed she-bamidbar makrivin b'ohel moed she-bagilgal* — whatever the community (*tzibur*) brought in the *ohel moed* in the wilderness, the community brings at Gilgal. But *kan u-kan lo karevu yachid ella olah u-shelamim bilvad* — in both settings, the private individual (*yachid*) is restricted to *olah* and *shelamim* alone. The Chachamim thus agree with Rabbi Yehudah regarding the *tzibur* — it brings everything at Gilgal — but they flatly disagree that the *yachid* at Gilgal may bring obligatory sacrifices. Even at the central location, an individual operates within the same restricted framework as he does on his private rooftop.

The practical consequences are acute. According to the Chachamim, a woman after childbirth (*yoledet*), a *zav*, or a *zava*, could never achieve *taharah* by bringing their required *korbanot* on a private *bamah*. They would be permanently barred from eating *korbanot*, including the *korban Pesach*, for the entire duration of their *tumah* — unless they made the journey to the central location. But according to the Chachamim, even if they arrived at Gilgal, they still could not bring their *chatat*, since individuals remain restricted even there. The result is that large categories of people could never achieve ritual purity during the entire period of *heter bamot* — an extraordinarily far-reaching consequence of this position.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon takes the most restrictive position of all, limiting even the *tzibur*: *af tzibur lo hikrivu ella pesachim ve-chovah ha-keva'ah lahem zman* — even the community brought only *pesachim* and sacrifices with a fixed time (*mussaf*, *tamid*, *Yom Kippur* offerings). Rashi mentions *par he'elam davar* as a potential communal exception, but Tosafos notes that Rabbi Shimon's ultimate position may be even broader, possibly excluding communal *chatot* as well. This fuller dimension of Rabbi Shimon's view will be examined further as the Gemara continues.

## Free-Will vs. Obligation

### Genre vs. Circumstance

With the debate about what could be brought on a private *bamah* established, the Gemara now probes the conceptual structure underlying it. The defining criterion for a private *bamah* is that the sacrifice must be a *neder* or *nedavah* — a free-will offering. The question is whether this criterion is defined by the *genre* of the sacrifice (*olah* and *shelamim* as opposed to *chatat* and *asham*) or by the *circumstances* of obligation (whether the person came to bring it voluntarily or under compulsion).

These two axes can diverge. An *olah* brought by a *yoledet* after childbirth is technically in the genre of *olah*, yet the circumstances are obligatory — she is required to bring it. Conversely, the *chatat* and *asham* of a *nazir* arise from circumstances that began as entirely free-will: no one compelled the person to accept *nezirut*. Yet the resulting *korbanot* are technically obligatory once *nezirut* is accepted. The question, then, is whether for the purposes of what is permissible on a *bamah*, we require: (a) the intersection — both the genre of *olah* and *shelamim* and free-will circumstances, (b) the union — either the right genre or free-will circumstances, or (c) only the genre, regardless of circumstances.

### Shmuel Chatat

Shmuel addresses the *nezirut* debate directly. He states: *machloket b'chatat v'asham* — the tannaitic dispute about *nezirut* concerns specifically the *chatat* and *asham* of the *nazir*. On this reading, the debate is whether free-will circumstances are sufficient to permit even a *chatat* and *asham* on a private *bamah*. Those who permit *nezirut* on a *bamah* would hold that since *nezirut* was accepted voluntarily, even the *chatat* and *asham* it generates may be brought — representing the "union" approach. Those who prohibit it hold that the *chatat* and *asham* are always barred regardless of circumstances. But according to Shmuel's initial formulation, all opinions would agree that the *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir* may be brought on a private *bamah*.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges this reading from a *braita*. The *braita* discusses the *chazeh* and *shok* (breast and thigh) of a *shelamim* brought on a *bamah*: these priestly gifts apply at a central *bamah gedolah* but not at a private *bamah ketanah*. The *braita* also mentions the bread portions of a *todah* offering in this context. Rava notes, however, that the *braita* conspicuously fails to mention the cooked foreleg (*zroa b'sheilah*) of the *shelamim* of a *nazir* — the priestly gift given from the Nazirite's peace-offering. If, as Shmuel suggests, all opinions agree that the *nazir* brings his *olah* and *shelamim* on a private *bamah*, the *braita* should have stated that this foreleg gift does not apply there. The silence is inexplicable unless one assumes that no opinion permits the *shelamim* of a *nazir* on a private *bamah* at all — rendering there nothing to discuss.

### Resolution

Faced with this challenge, the Gemara concludes that Shmuel's statement must be reformulated. Rather than *machloket b'chatat v'asham*, Shmuel actually said: *machloket b'olah u-shelamim* — the debate concerns the *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir*, while regarding the *chatat* and *asham*, *divrei hakol chulin v'lo karvi* — all agree they are entirely off-limits on a private *bamah*, regardless of the circumstances of their origin.

The resolution thus establishes a two-part requirement for a private *bamah*: the sacrifice must belong to the genre of *olah* and *shelamim* (not *chatat* and *asham*), and it must arise from free-will circumstances. The *chatat* and *asham* of a *nazir* fail the first test and are universally excluded. The *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir* pass the first test but face ambiguity on the second: does one consider the circumstances free-will (since *nezirut* was voluntarily accepted) or obligatory (since once accepted, the *korbanot* are required)? That is the tannaitic debate.

According to the Chachamim who prohibit bringing any sacrifices of a *nazir* on a private *bamah*, the *olah* and *shelamim* of the *nazir* fail the second test: although the *nezirut* began freely, the resulting *korbanot* are obligatory and cannot be considered *neder u-nedavah*. This explains the *braita*'s silence about the *zroa b'sheilah*: the *braita* follows the Chachamim, according to whom no *korban nazir* whatsoever would be brought on a private *bamah* — hence there is nothing to say about what happens to its gifts.

The *olah* of a *yoledet* similarly fails under this framework: it falls in the right genre but arises from fully obligatory circumstances, and would not be brought on a private *bamah* according to any opinion.

The woman who gives birth, it should be noted, retains the option of bringing her obligatory offerings at the central location. During the period of *heter bamot*, Gilgal served as that central sanctuary, and Rabbi Yehudah would permit her to bring her *chatat* there. According to the Chachamim, however, even that avenue is unavailable for a *yachid*.

## Yachid in Gilgal

### Chachamim Reasoning

The Gemara now turns to the reasoning of the Chachamim regarding the central *bamah* at Gilgal. Even when a *yachid* comes to the central location, why should he be restricted to *olah* and *shelamim*? The Chachamim derive this from the verse *ish ha-yashar be-einav ya'aseh* — each man shall do what is right in his own eyes. They read *ish* as referring to the individual (*yachid*), who *vadai likrov* — who surely wants to bring — but *chovot lo yikrov* — may not bring obligatory sacrifices. The period of *heter bamot* is characterized by a kind of individuated religious expression: the individual operates in a mode of personal, voluntary engagement with the divine. He may not simultaneously participate in the system of collective obligation. The community (*tzibur*) retains its obligatory structure and brings everything in Gilgal, but the private individual, wherever he offers — on his rooftop or at Gilgal — remains in the voluntary mode. The *ish ha-yashar* phrase applies to him in all settings.

### Rabbi Yehudah Counter

Rabbi Yehudah disputes this. He holds that *ki ksiv ha-yashar be-einav* — the phrase "what is right in his own eyes" — applies specifically to the context of private *bamot*, not to the central location. When one is operating in one's own private space, governed by one's own eyes and one's own discretion, one is limited to voluntary sacrifices. When one enters the public, communal space of the central sanctuary, one exits the *be-einav* mode and participates in the full obligatory structure.

### Meaning of Ish

The Gemara raises a difficulty with Rabbi Yehudah's position: does not the word *ish* itself — "a man" — imply that any individual is always restricted? Rabbi Yehudah responds: *ki ksiv ish le-hachshir et ha-zar* — the word *ish* in this context teaches something else entirely. When serving on a private *bamah*, any person (*ish*) may perform the *avodah*, even a non-priest (*zar*). The verse is not restricting what the individual may bring; it is teaching that the private *bamah* service does not require a *kohen*.

One might have thought that on a private *bamah*, worship reverted to the pre-*mikdash* model and required a *bechor* — a firstborn. The verse's use of *ish* teaches that a *zar* may serve, without any requirement for sanctified lineage.

### Conclusion

The Gemara concludes by noting an apparent redundancy: the Chachamim who appear in the later discussion — who agree with Rabbi Yehudah that the *tzibur* brings everything at Gilgal while the *yachid* is restricted to *olah* and *shelamim* — seem identical to the initial *tanna kamma*. Rav Pappa resolves this: *kivvan de-nesachim bamidbar beineihu* — the sub-debate between these two positions concerns whether an *olah* and *shelamim* brought by a *yachid* on a private *bamah* are accompanied by *nesachim* — the wine, flour, and oil libations. All agree that the individual brings *olah* and *shelamim*, but the *tanna kamma* and the later Chachamim diverge on whether the *nesachim* accompany them, a question rooted in what was done during the wilderness period.

In summary: on the private rooftop *bamah*, the undisputed minimum is *olah* and *shelamim*, with a debate about *minchah* and the *nezirut* offerings (now clarified as concerning the *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir*), and a further debate about *nesachim*. At Gilgal, the *tzibur* brings everything according to all, and the debate between Rabbi Yehudah and the Chachamim concerns whether the *yachid* at the central location may bring obligatory sacrifices as well. Rabbi Shimon's more restrictive position, limiting even the *tzibur*, remains to be examined in the continuation of the *sugya*.
