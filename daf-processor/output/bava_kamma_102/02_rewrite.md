# Bava Kamma 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kedushat Shvi'is

### Dyes & Wood

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion, already underway at the bottom of 101b, concerning *kedushat shvi'is* — the sanctity of the sabbatical year's produce. The initial assumption might have been that *shmita* holiness attaches only to food. The question now before the *Gemara* is whether it extends further: does it apply to items used for dyeing, or even to wood itself?

### Benefit & Consumption

The *Gemara*'s conclusion rests on an interpretive principle derived from the verse's use of *le'achla* — "for eating." The verse teaches that *shmita* sanctity applies to things that are *hana'aso u'viuro shaveh*: things from which one derives benefit at the very moment of their consumption. Dyes qualify under this standard. *Rashi* understands the benefit as occurring when the dye is absorbed into the garment; *Tosafot* locates it in the gradual wearing of the dyed garment. Either way, dyes meet the criterion.

Ordinary firewood, by contrast, does not qualify. Even when a branch grows during *shmita*, the benefit from burning wood comes only *after* it has ignited and begun to turn to coal — that is, *hana'as achar biuro*, benefit after consumption. The flame one benefits from is the ember stage, not the moment of burning, and therefore normal wood is excluded from *kedushat shvi'is*.

### Oily Wood Debate

At this point the *Gemara* raises a challenge. The exclusion of wood seems too broad. The verse says *yatzu eitzim* — wood is excluded — *she-hana'as achar biuran*, because its benefit comes after it is consumed. But is that universally true of all wood? The *Gemara* asks: *ve-ika eitzei d'mash'chan di-hana'aso u-viuro shaveh* — is there not oily wood whose benefit and consumption are simultaneous?

*Rashi* understands this as a reference to a particular species of oily wood that burns slowly and is used for light rather than for cooking — the light being produced precisely as the wood burns. *Tosafot* reads the question differently: it is not about a species of wood at all, but about the *manner* of use. If one uses wood to produce immediate heat for a certain kind of cooking — where the benefit comes from the fire itself rather than from the embers — then the benefit and the consumption are indeed simultaneous. On *Tosafot*'s reading, the same piece of wood might or might not carry *kedushat shvi'is* depending on the intended use.

### Rava's Resolution

*Rava* resolves the difficulty with a single principle: *stam eitzim l'hasakah hein omdim* — the default use of wood is for fuel, meaning for the kind of fire from which one benefits at the ember stage. Since that is the presumed purpose of wood, we apply that presumption even where the particular use happens to differ.

This resolution reads more naturally according to *Tosafot* than according to *Rashi*. If the question were about a distinct species of oily wood, it would be strange to say the species is "presumed" to be used in the standard way. But if the question is about intended use, then *Rava*'s answer is precise: the default intention determines the *shmita* status. If the default is that wood is used for ember-producing fires — which is *hana'as achar biuro* — then even wood used in an atypical manner never acquired *kedushat shvi'is* to begin with. The *Gemara* thus leaves open the theoretical possibility that wood whose *stam* use is *hana'aso u'viuro shaveh* might carry *kedushat shvi'is*, but confirms that for ordinary firewood the presumption forecloses this.

## Le'Achla Expansion

### Wine & Soaking

Having established the basic framework, the *Gemara* now turns to the underlying *tannaic* debate that informs this entire discussion: *ve-eitzim l'hasakah tannai hi* — the question of whether firewood has *kedushat shvi'is* is itself the subject of a *tannaic* dispute. To understand that dispute, the *Gemara* cites a *braitah*:

One may not soak flax in *shmita* wine, nor use *shmita* wine for laundering. This is the position of the Rabbis. Rabbi Yosei, however, permits both. What drives this disagreement?

The Rabbis derive their position from the word *le'achla* — for eating. The verse's phrase *le'achla* excludes *mishrah* (soaking) and *khivusah* (laundering): *le'ochla velo lemishrah, le'ochla velo lekhivusah*. The reason is that soaking and laundering are *hana'atan achar biuran* — the wine, once absorbed into the flax or garment, only produces its effect after some time has passed. *Rashi* notes that the soaking may continue for several days. The consumption thus precedes the benefit, and therefore these uses fall outside the scope of *le'achla*.

Rabbi Yosei, by contrast, invokes the word *lakhem* — "for you" — which the verse also contains. He reads *lakhem* as *lekhol tsorkheikhem*: you may use *shmita* produce for all your needs, not only for eating. How do the Rabbis respond? They accept that *lakhem* broadens *le'achla*, but insist that the expansion must still be *dumya d'le'ochla* — analogous to eating — meaning that *hana'aso u'viuro shaveh* must still be satisfied. Soaking and laundering fail that test.

### Common Use Standard

At this point the *Gemara* introduces what proves to be a third and broader position — that of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda accepts that *le'achla* alone is not the whole picture. But when the *Gemara* asks how Rabbi Yehuda can be so expansive given the word *le'achla*, his answer is that *le'achla* is needed for a different teaching found in a separate *braitah*: *le'ochla velo lemlugma* — one may not use *shmita* produce for a poultice or medicinal salve. That is what the word *le'achla* limits.

If *le'achla* is used to exclude poultices, how can it simultaneously exclude laundering? It cannot, Rabbi Yehuda would say, once *lakhem* has expanded the scope. The *braitah* then asks explicitly: why does the expansion through *lakhem* include laundering but exclude a poultice? The answer is *shaveh l'chol adam* — laundering is something that every person engages in, while a poultice is used only by those who are ill or have some special need. *Lakhem* expands *le'achla* to whatever is a universal, common human use. This principle will be recognized as parallel to the concept of *davar hashaveh lekhol nefesh* known from the laws of *Yom Tov*.

### Excluded Uses

A *braitah* then catalogs the items excluded even on Rabbi Yehuda's expanded view: *le'ochla velo lemlugma* — not for a poultice; not for heating in some special therapeutic sense; and not for sprinkling wine as a kind of air freshener (*ziluf*); and not for *apiktevizyon* — inducing vomiting, apparently as a form of purging. All of these are excluded because they are not *shaveh lekhol adam*: they serve only the sick, the finicky, or a narrow subset of people, and therefore do not qualify as the kind of common, universal use that *lakhem* was meant to authorize.

The *Gemara* then identifies this *braitah* — *le'ochla velo lemlugma* and its list of exclusions — as going like Rabbi Yosei (*k'Rabbi Yosei*), because if it were going like the Rabbis, it would also have excluded laundering and soaking. That it does not exclude those things shows it operates within Rabbi Yehuda's broader framework, where *shaveh l'chol adam* is the operative criterion.

### Firewood & Tannaim

This clarifies what the *Gemara* means when it frames the question of firewood as *tannai hi* — a *tannaic* dispute. The same verse — *le'achla* and *lakhem* — that governs what *shmita* produce *may be used for* also governs what *types of produce carry kedushat shvi'is* in the first place. As *Tosafot* explains, the two questions are two sides of the same coin.

According to the Rabbis, *shmita* sanctity attaches to anything that is *hana'aso u'viuro shaveh* — benefit simultaneous with consumption. This includes dyes but excludes ordinary firewood. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *shmita* sanctity attaches to anything that is *shaveh l'chol nefesh* — commonly used by all people. Since everyone uses firewood, firewood would carry *kedushat shvi'is* under Rabbi Yehuda's view. Whether firewood has *kedushat shvi'is* thus depends directly on this *tannaic* debate.

## Dye Mishna

### Wrong Dye Case

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah* in our daf. The case: a craftsman receives white wool to be dyed — but dyes it black; or receives black wool and dyes it red. He has applied the wrong dye, contrary to the owner's instructions. How is he liable?

### Shinuy Koneh

Rabbi Meir rules: *shinuy koneh*. The craftsman's unauthorized change in the object effects a transfer of ownership. The craftsman now owns the wool, and must pay the original owner the full value of the wool as it was before the dyeing.

### Yehuda's Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. In his view, a *shinuy* of this kind — where the craftsman was not trying to steal but simply made a mistake — does not transfer ownership. The wool still belongs to its original owner. However, since the craftsman erred and did not produce what was requested, the owner has the upper hand in determining compensation. The craftsman is entitled to the lesser of two amounts: either his material expenses (not his labor), or the amount by which the value of the wool increased due to the dyeing, whichever is smaller.

## Rav Huna Ruling

### Huna's Position

Rav Yosef was sitting behind Rav Abba, in front of Rav Huna, at a *shiur* that Rav Huna was delivering. Rav Huna declared two rulings: *halacha k'Rebbi Yehoshua ben Korcha*, we rule like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha in the case we will explain shortly; and *halacha k'Rebbi Yehuda*, we rule like Rabbi Yehuda in the *Mishnah* before us.

### Yosef Challenges

Rav Yosef turned his face away, signaling his disagreement. He accepted the ruling regarding Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha — that case involves a *da'as yachid*, a minority opinion, and it is genuinely necessary for Rav Huna to tell us that we rule even against the majority in that instance. But the ruling like Rabbi Yehuda in our *Mishnah* seemed to Rav Yosef unnecessary, and he challenged it: why state the obvious?

### Yehoshua ben Korcha

The *Gemara* first explains the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha. The relevant *braitah* concerns the prohibition on doing business with idol-worshippers in the three days before their holidays — including the prohibition on collecting repayment of loans. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha draws a distinction: if the loan is documented (*milveh beshtar*), one need not collect it now, as the lender can always prove the debt in court later. But if the loan is only oral (*milveh al peh*) and there is no evidentiary record, then the lender may collect even in those three days, because this is *k'matzil miyadam* — saving one's money from a situation of potential loss. This position is a *da'as yachid*, yet we rule accordingly — hence Rav Huna's declaration is useful there.

## Stam vs. Machlokes

### Stam Rule

Rav Yosef's objection to the ruling *k'Rebbi Yehuda* is based on the principle of *machlokes v'achareiha stam halacha k'stam*: when a *Mishnah* contains a named dispute, and that dispute is followed by an anonymous (*stam*) *Mishnah* that resolves the matter without naming the parties, we rule in accordance with the anonymous *Mishnah*. In our case, the *machlokes* between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda appears here in Bava Kamma, and it is followed by a *stam* in Bava Metzia: *kol hamishaneh yado al hatachtona* — whoever deviates from his instructions is in the weaker position. This *stam* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view. Therefore, the ruling *k'Rebbi Yehuda* is self-evident and required no declaration.

What is the *stam* in Bava Metzia? The *Mishnah* there teaches: *kol hamishaneh yado al hatachtona* — any craftsman or agent who deviates from instructions has the lower hand; *v'khol hachozer bo yado al hatachtona* — and whoever backs out of a contract has the lower hand. The first clause directly applies to our case and reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position. Hence, Rav Yosef argues, the *halacha* was already obvious.

### Order in Masechet

Rav Huna's defense is that one might have thought *ein seder la-Mishnah* — that there is no meaningful order to the *Mishnayos*, and one cannot infer that the *stam* in Bava Metzia was arranged *after* the dispute in Bava Kamma. If the order is not reliable, perhaps the *stam* is actually earlier and the dispute later — which would mean the later position is the dispute, not the anonymous ruling, and we would not apply the principle of *machlokes v'achareiha stam*.

Rav Yosef counters: if you genuinely believed there was no order at all, then the principle of *machlokes v'achareiha stam* could never be applied anywhere, because you could always claim the *stam* came first. The very existence of that rule implies that order is meaningful. The response must therefore be more nuanced.

### Nezikin Unity

Rav Huna refines his position. The principle of *ein seder la-Mishnah* applies only across separate *masechtos*. Within a single *masechet*, the arrangement by the editor — ultimately Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — is treated as deliberate and meaningful, and the principle of *machlokes v'achareiha stam* can be applied within that unit. However, across two different *masechtos*, one cannot assume a meaningful sequence.

Rav Yosef responds: *kula Nezikin chada masechta hi* — all of Nezikin is conceptually one *masechet*. The names Bava Kamma, Bava Metzia, and Bava Basra — "First Gate," "Middle Gate," and "Last Gate" — reflect only a practical division of a single thirty-chapter tractate, just as Masechet Keilim in *Taharos* is similarly divided. The three *Bavas* together form one organic whole, and therefore the ordering principle applies across them. The *stam* in Bava Metzia follows the *machlokes* in Bava Kamma within a single conceptual *masechet*, and *halacha k'stam*.

### Categorical Statements

An alternative justification is offered: even if one does not invoke the *stam* principle, the *Mishnah* in Bava Metzia is formulated as a categorical, universal statement — *kol hamishaneh... kol hachozer bo* — "whoever switches... whoever backs out." Categorical formulations of this type carry particular halachic weight and are treated as more than an ordinary *stam*. They represent accepted principles, not merely one opinion stated anonymously. Therefore, even apart from the question of sequence, the ruling like Rabbi Yehuda was already clear, and Rav Huna's declaration was superfluous.

## Wrong Grain Case

### Two Braitot

The *Gemara* now introduces a new case: *ha-nosein ma'os li-shelucho likach lo chitin ve-lakach mehem se'orim* — someone gives money to his agent to purchase wheat for him, and the agent instead purchases barley, or vice versa. The context here is not a personal purchase for household use but an investment transaction — the buyer intends to resell the grain. Two *braitot* are cited that appear to contradict one another.

### First Braitah

The first *braitah* rules: *im pichesu pichesu lo, ve-im hosiru hosiru lo* — if the grain decreased in value, the loss falls on the agent; if it increased in value, the gain belongs to the agent. On this view, the agent's deviation from instructions transfers full ownership to him. He must repay the principal to the one who sent him, but the grain — and all profit or loss from it — is entirely his. This mirrors the *shinuy koneh* principle: by violating his instructions, the agent acquires the purchase as his own.

### Second Braitah

The second *braitah* rules differently: *im pichesu pichesu lo, ve-im hosiru hosiru la-emtza* — if the grain lost value, the agent bears the loss alone; but if the grain gained value, the profit is split between them. This requires background to understand. The original arrangement, the *Gemara* implies, was a partnership of a common type: the sender contributed the capital, the agent contributed the effort, and both were to share profits and losses equally. When the agent violates his instructions, that arrangement is partially disturbed. He cannot make the *ba'al habayis* absorb his mistake, so any loss falls on the agent alone. But since it was ultimately the sender's money and the agent did execute a purchase on his behalf, the original profit-sharing agreement retains some force — any gain is still divided between them.

### Interest Loophole

This arrangement is striking and worth pausing over. The *Tosafot* themselves note that the second *braitah* could be exploited as a workaround for *ribbis* — the prohibition on charging interest. Consider: an investor gives a thousand dollars to an agent with impossibly strict instructions — say, to lend it out only if stored in an extraordinarily secure vault with gold-and-silver collateral. The investor and agent both understand the instructions will not be followed. The agent lends the money out, collects interest, and the investor then claims that since the agent violated the instructions, the result under the second *braitah* is that the investor's principal is guaranteed (any loss falls on the agent) while any profit is split with the investor. The investor thus enjoys a secured principal with participation in upside — effectively a loan with interest, structured through a nominally accidental *shinuy*. The second *braitah*'s ruling thus creates, at least in theory, a vehicle for circumventing the prohibition on interest.

## Yochanan Analysis

### Braitot Alignment

Rabbi Yochanan explains the apparent contradiction between the two *braitot* cleanly: *lo kashya — ha Rabbi Meir ve-ha Rabbi Yehuda*. The first *braitah* reflects Rabbi Meir; the second reflects Rabbi Yehuda.

### Meir: Violation

Rabbi Meir holds *shinuy koneh* — when an agent deviates from his instructions, the deviation effects a transfer of ownership. Here the *shinuy* is not a physical change in the object (as in the wool-dyeing case) but a violation of the *ba'al habayis*'s directions. Nevertheless, Rabbi Meir treats it as a *shinuy* sufficient to transfer ownership. The agent must repay the money, but the grain is his: all profit and all loss belong to him.

### Yehuda: No Transfer

Rabbi Yehuda holds that *shinuy eino koneh* — this kind of deviation does not transfer ownership. The grain remains, in some sense, the property of the *ba'al habayis*. Because the agent erred, he cannot impose any loss on the sender. But the grain's identity as the sender's property means the original profit-sharing arrangement persists for any gains.

## Personal vs. Trade

### Personal Use Question

The *Gemara* in *Eretz Yisrael* laughed at Rabbi Yochanan's explanation — noteworthy because Rabbi Yochanan himself was from *Eretz Yisrael*, and it is unusual for a scholar's home tradition to mock his own teaching. The challenge is directed specifically at his account of Rabbi Yehuda's position. According to Rabbi Yochanan, for Rabbi Yehuda, the agent's purchase of barley instead of wheat is a genuine *shinuy* — a real violation of the *ba'al habayis*'s will. And yet Rabbi Yehuda still maintains that the wheat or barley belongs to the *ba'al habayis*.

Rav Elazar formulates the objection more precisely: *dilma ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Meir ela be-midi de-chazi lei le-gufeh* — perhaps Rabbi Meir's principle of *shinuy koneh* applies only when the item in question is intended for personal use. In the wool case, the owner cared specifically whether the garment would be red or black; the particularity of the item mattered to him personally. In a commercial trade context — *l'schora* — where the sender simply wants product and cares only about profit, maybe Rabbi Meir would not apply *shinuy koneh* at all. If it is all just commodity, the deviation is not weighty enough to transfer ownership.

### Rav Elazar's Fix

If that is correct, then both *braitot* can be explained within Rabbi Meir's framework alone, and no appeal to a Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Meir dispute is necessary. *Ve-lo kashya — kan l'achila, kan l'schora*: the first *braitah* (full transfer) addresses a case where the agent was buying for personal use — the *ba'al habayis* wanted wheat for bread, specificity mattered, and a deviation is a genuine *shinuy* that transfers ownership to the agent. The second *braitah* (split profits) addresses a commercial trade context — the *ba'al habayis* saw it all as product and the deviation is not weighty enough to transfer ownership, so the sender retains ownership and shares in the gain, while the agent bears the loss for his error. Both cases go according to Rabbi Meir; the distinction is purely contextual.

## Agent Representation

### Acquisition Problem

At this point a deeper difficulty surfaces regarding the second *braitah* under Rabbi Yochanan's framework. Specifically, the *Gemara* in *Eretz Yisrael* challenges: *ve-chi mi hodi'a l'ba'al chitim she-yakneh chitim l'ba'al ma'ot* — who told the seller of the wheat that his wheat should be acquired by the one who sent the agent? In the second *braitah*, the *ba'al habayis* owns the grain — that is why he gets half the profit. But when the agent buys barley instead of wheat (or wheat instead of barley), he is acting against the explicit wishes of the sender. In what sense does the agent still represent the *ba'al habayis* at the moment of purchase? The seller sees only the agent in front of him and has no intention to transfer ownership to some third party. How then does the *ba'al habayis* come to own the grain?

*Rashi* sharpens the point: the difficulty is particularly acute for Rabbi Yochanan, who identifies this as a case of genuine *shinuy* — a real violation of the sender's will. Once the deviation rises to the level of a true *shinuy*, how can the agent still be viewed as operating in the sender's capacity? And if he is not, how does the wheat come to belong to the sender at all?

### Knowledge Required

Before an answer is offered, Rav Shmuel bar Sasrati raises a preliminary challenge: even when the agent does *exactly* what he was instructed to do, the seller does not know who the *ba'al habayis* is. The seller sees the agent and transacts with the agent. How does the *ba'al habayis* acquire the wheat even in the ordinary, compliant case?

### Zachin Principle

Rabbi Abbahu responds: the straightforward case poses no difficulty. *Shani chitin v'chitin di-shlichusei ka avid ve-chi ba'al habayis dami* — when the agent is performing his agency properly, he is simply an extension of the *ba'al habayis*; he is, as it were, the *ba'al habayis*'s hand. The seller need not know who the principal is. As long as the agent is acting within the scope of his authority, the transaction flows through him directly to the sender.

To demonstrate that an agent can effect acquisition for another party without the seller's explicit awareness, Rabbi Abbahu cites a *Mishnah*: when a person declares *hekdesh* — "all my property is consecrated" — his wife's clothing, his children's clothing, and sandals he has purchased for them are not included in the sanctification, even if not yet delivered. Similarly, if he paid a dyer to dye his wife's garment, that garment is excluded from *hekdesh*, even though the dyer had no idea he was dyeing the wife's garment specifically.

The dyer's ignorance of the intended recipient does not prevent the wife from acquiring the garment. The husband, in commissioning the dyeing, represents his wife by virtue of *zachin* — the principle that one may act to benefit another without that person's explicit appointment, since conferring a benefit on someone requires no formal authorization. The husband is acting as his wife's agent by operation of law. The seller or craftsman need not know. *V'chi yad isha dami* — the husband is like an extension of the wife's own hand. *Hacha nami shlichusei ka avid ve-chi yad ba'al habayis dami* — and so too with a compliant agent: he is an extension of the sender, and the wheat is acquired by the sender without the seller needing to know.

The difficulty, then, is specifically in the deviant case. When the agent's violation is so severe that Rabbi Meir would say *shinuy koneh* — when the agent is no longer acting within his mandate — how does the grain still come to the *ba'al habayis* under Rabbi Yehuda?

## Third Party Knowledge

### Abba Objects

Rabbi Abba challenges Rabbi Abbahu's proof. The *zachin* analogy — husband buying sandals for wife — does not demonstrate that the seller's knowledge is irrelevant in the way Rabbi Abbahu claims. Rabbi Abba proposes an alternative explanation of why the wife's clothing and the purchased sandals escape *hekdesh*: *kol hamakdish nechasav ein da'ato al ksus ishto uvanav* — when a person consecrates all his property, he simply does not have his wife's and children's clothing in mind. It is not that his wife owns those sandals through some mechanism of agency; it may be that he has not yet transferred them to her at all. The *hekdesh* does not capture those items merely because the husband did not intend to include them — not because they already belong to her. If that is the correct explanation, then no conclusion can be drawn about whether a seller's ignorance of the principal's identity affects acquisition.

### Tefillin Evidence

Rabbi Zeira challenges Rabbi Abba's proposed explanation. If *hekdesh* excludes the wife's items simply because the husband did not have them in mind, then subjective intention determines what is consecrated. But is that right? *Ve-chi da'ato shel adam al tefillav?* — does a person really intend to consecrate his *tefillin* when he says "all my property"? Presumably not. And yet we learn from a *Mishnah* that when a person consecrates all his property, the *tefillin* are included — the *beit din* assesses their value and requires the owner to redeem them so he may continue wearing them. The consecration of *tefillin* was presumably unintended, yet it stands. This shows that "all my property" is taken literally, regardless of subjective intent. If so, subjective intent cannot be what saves the wife's sandals from *hekdesh* — and therefore Rabbi Abba's explanation fails, and we are back to saying the wife must already own those sandals, which requires the *zachin* principle.

### Abaye Resists

Abaye resists this inference. He accepts that a person does have his *tefillin* in mind when consecrating all his property. The consecration of *tefillin* is intended: the person declaring *hekdesh* thinks, "I am performing a *mitzvah*, and my *tefillin* can go along with everything else." He does not exclude them because there is no social reason to do so. The wife's clothing, however, is different: *mishum eivah* — for fear of domestic conflict. No one wants their wife and children stripping their closets and donating their clothing to the Temple. Therefore, even when saying "all my property," a person specifically and deliberately excludes what belongs to or is destined for his wife and children. The distinction between *tefillin* and the wife's clothing is thus one of rational motivation: the former is consecrated with equanimity, the latter is excluded out of practical concern. Since the husband intends to exclude the wife's items, they need not have been transferred to her already. The *zachin* principle is not established by this case.

### Collateral Evidence

Rav Oshaya presses further with another variant of the *hekdesh* case. There are two scenarios in the *Mishnah*: one where the person himself consecrates his property (*makdish nechasav*), and one where he has incurred an *erech* obligation and the *beit din* seizes collateral (*memashkenin osan*). Rav Oshaya's point targets the second case. In the collateral-seizure scenario, the *beit din* acts against the debtor's will — this is not a voluntary act of dedication where the debtor's intentions matter. It is a compulsory legal proceeding. And yet even there, the wife's clothing and the sandals purchased for her escape seizure. This cannot be explained by the *mishum eivah* rationale — the *beit din* does not care about domestic tranquility. Therefore, the wife must already own those items, which requires the *zachin* principle, and Abaye's solution fails.

### Exception Doctrine

Rabbi Abba offers a final response. He concedes the point about involuntary seizure but reframes the entire category. The ruling that the wife's clothing and sandals escape both voluntary *hekdesh* and involuntary seizure is not a demonstration of a general principle of agency without the seller's knowledge. Rather, it reflects a specific rabbinic institution: *kol hamakdish nechasav na'aseh k'mi she-hikneh lahen ksus ishto uvanav me-ikara* — when a person is about to consecrate property (or, by extension, when *beit din* is about to seize collateral), it is treated *as if* he had already transferred his wife's and children's items to them before the consecration or seizure occurred. This is not a natural legal conclusion but a rabbinic enactment — *na'aseh k'mi*, treated as if — designed to preserve social stability. The *hekdesh* and *arachin* cases are therefore exceptional and cannot be used to prove any general principle about third-party acquisition without the seller's knowledge. As a general rule, the question of whether the seller must know he is transacting with an agent remains unanswered.

## Unresolved Issue

### Logical Problem

The *Gemara* has now established the difficulty clearly. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who reads the two *braitot* as a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, there is a genuine *shinuy* in the agent's conduct — he has violated the *ba'al habayis*'s explicit instructions. Yet Rabbi Yehuda maintains the grain belongs to the sender. The question stands: how does the *ba'al habayis* acquire ownership of grain purchased in violation of his instructions, if the agent is no longer acting as his representative and the seller has no intention of transferring the item to the sender?

### Seller Knowledge

The proposed resolution is practical rather than theoretical. If we assume that in such transactions the agent informs the seller that he is purchasing on behalf of a specific principal — "I am buying this for Mr. Jones" — then the seller is aware from the outset that the grain is intended for the *ba'al habayis*. The seller's intent is therefore to transfer the grain to the principal, not merely to the agent standing before him.

### Closure

If that is the case, then the agent's subsequent *shinuy* — his deviation from instructions — does not affect the seller's intent. The seller already directed the transaction toward the *ba'al habayis*. Whether the agent followed instructions or violated them is irrelevant to the question of whom the seller intended to sell to. The *ba'al habayis* acquires the grain because the seller intended him to have it, and that intention was formed at the moment of sale, independent of the agent's compliance or non-compliance with his mandate.

If, however, we assume the seller does not know who the principal is — that the agent transacts anonymously — then the *Gemara* remains unresolved. We understand why the compliant agent effects acquisition for the *ba'al habayis* (he is simply the sender's extension), but we do not understand how a deviant agent does so. The *Gemara* leaves this question open for now.
