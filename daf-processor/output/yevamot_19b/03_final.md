# Yevamot 19b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Pappa's Reading

### Rav Pappa Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּיִיבֵּם וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד — פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, בְּנוֹלַד וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִיבֵּם — לָא פְּלִיג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Rabbi Shimon disagreed** in the case where the second brother **performed levirate marriage and after that** the third brother **was born;** however, **where** the third brother **was born** after the death of the first brother **and after that** the second brother **performed levirate marriage** Rabbi Shimon **did not disagree.** He agreed that in this case she would be forbidden to the newly born brother as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.

The sugya opens in the wake of a sustained engagement with Rabbi Oshaya's interpretation of Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Oshaya had attempted to argue that Rabbi Shimon was unbothered by the concern of *eishet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* across all cases — a reading that proved conceptually difficult and straining against the *mishna*. We turn now to Rav Pappa, who offers the more measured and logically coherent position: Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the *rabbanim* only in a single, specific scenario — where Shimon performed *yibbum* with Rachel and only thereafter was Levi born. In that case, Rabbi Shimon maintains that once Rachel was already married to Shimon at the moment Levi entered the world, the connection to Reuven falls away and there is no problem. The *braita* confirms this reading, and the *gemara* notes that it simultaneously constitutes a refutation of Rabbi Oshaya's broader interpretation.

The *braita* begins: two brothers are alive at the same time, and one of them — Reuven — dies without a child. Shimon prepares to perform *ma'amar* with Rachel, his *yevama*, but does not complete it before another brother, Levi, is born — and then Shimon dies.

### No Ma'amar Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִים בְּעוֹלָם אֶחָד, וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶם בְּלֹא וָלָד, וְעָמַד הַשֵּׁנִי הַזֶּה לַעֲשׂוֹת מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לַעֲשׂוֹת בָּהּ מַאֲמָר עַד שֶׁנּוֹלַד לוֹ אָח, וּמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּשְׁנִיָּה — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If there were **two coexisting brothers, and one died childless, and the second was about to perform levirate betrothal with his *yevama* but did not manage to perform levirate betrothal before his brother was born, and** then the second brother **died,** then **the first** woman **goes out** and is free to remarry without *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage **due to** the fact that **she was the wife of a brother with whom** the third brother **did not coexist, and the second either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.** She was never the rival wife of the widow of the first brother.

This is Case A: Shimon did absolutely nothing with Rachel. He may have intended to perform *ma'amar*, but intention is legally irrelevant — what matters is whether the act was done. Rachel therefore remains in her original status as Reuven's widow, an *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* relative to Levi, who was born after Reuven's death. Since Shimon had no meaningful legal relationship with Rachel, her status does not impinge on Sarah's situation at all. The *braita*'s ruling is therefore: Rachel is exempt — she exits under the category of *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — and Sarah, Shimon's actual wife, falls before Levi for either *yibbum* or *chalitzah*. The two women are entirely unrelated in their legal status, and Levi may proceed with Sarah without restriction.

### Ma'amar Before Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד אָח, אוֹ שֶׁנּוֹלַד לוֹ אָח וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר, וּמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּשְׁנִיָּה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If the second brother **performed levirate betrothal with her and afterward his brother was born, or if his brother was born and then he performed the levirate betrothal, and then he died, the first goes out** and is free to remarry **as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, and the second,** the wife of the second brother, **must perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.** This is because, due to the levirate betrothal, she is considered by the Rabbis to be the rival wife of a wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.

Case B introduces the scenario where Shimon performed *ma'amar* with Rachel at some point before his death — whether before or after Levi's birth is, for this analysis, immaterial; what matters is that *ma'amar* was done while Shimon was still alive. In this case, Rachel still exits on the grounds of *eishet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — Shimon never consummated the marriage, so she remains an *erva*. But the *ma'amar* creates a quasi-relationship: Rachel now has some form of attachment to Shimon, which generates a quasi-*tzarat ervah* status for Sarah. This quasi-relationship is not equivalent to full *yibbum* — the *ma'amar* is a rabbinic institution, a form of *kiddushin* — but it is sufficient to prevent Levi from performing *yibbum* with Sarah. Since on the level of Torah obligation the *zikah* remains and *chalitzah* is still required, Levi must perform *chalitzah* with Sarah but may not perform *yibbum*. Rachel, meanwhile, simply walks away.

Here the *gemara* notes the curious language of the *braita*: *amad lo hispik* — "he stood to do and did not have the chance." If he did *ma'amar*, he did it; if not, he did not. Why introduce the narrative of intention and missed opportunity? The *gemara* explains: *amad* indicates that she had initially agreed, *lo hispik mida'atah* means that in the end she withdrew her consent — and then he performed *ma'amar* against her will, *be'al karchah*. This raises the fundamental question of whether *ma'amar* is to be understood through the rules of *kiddushin*, in which case consent is required, or through the rules of *yibbum*, in which case — since *yibbum* itself takes effect even *be'al karchah* — *ma'amar* might likewise.

### Yibbum Before Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּנָסָהּ וָמֵת, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד לוֹ אָח, אוֹ שֶׁנּוֹלַד לוֹ אָח וְאַחַר כָּךְ כְּנָסָהּ, וָמֵת — שְׁתֵּיהֶן פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּיבּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If the second brother **married** his deceased brother's wife **and** then **died** himself, **and afterward a brother was born, or if a brother was born and then he married her and died,** the two wives **are both exempt from *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage.** In this case, one was the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist and the other her rival wife.

Case C — actual *yibbum*, not merely *ma'amar* — is the case that drives the central dispute. Here Shimon married Rachel and Sarah both: he performed *yibbum* with Rachel and was married to Sarah. The *braita* presents two sub-scenarios: *yibbum* occurred, then Shimon died, then Levi was born; or Levi was born, then *yibbum* occurred, then Shimon died. In both of those orderings, the ruling of the *tanna kamma* is that both women are exempt — from both *chalitzah* and *yibbum*. In the first sub-ordering, Rachel is *eshet Shimon shelo haya be'olamo* to Levi; in the second, she is *eshet Reuven shelo haya be'olamo*. In either case she is an *erva* to Levi, and Sarah, as her *tzarah*, is likewise exempt. Rabbi Shimon, however, dissents in precisely the third scenario — introduced separately — where *yibbum* occurred, then Levi was born, and then Shimon died. In that case, when Levi entered the world, Rachel was already Shimon's wife. She never stood before Levi for even a moment in a prohibited status. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon rules that Levi may perform *yibbum* or *chalitzah* with whichever of the two women he chooses.

## Three Yibbum Scenarios

### Yibbum → Birth → Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּנָסָהּ וְנוֹלַד לוֹ אָח, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת — שְׁתֵּיהֶן פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּיבּוּם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וּבָא וּמְצָאָהּ בְּהֶיתֵּר, וְלֹא עָמְדָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּאִיסּוּר — מְיַיבֵּם לְאֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, אוֹ חוֹלֵץ לְאֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If he **married** his *yevama* **and** then **a brother was born, and then he died, both** the wife of the first deceased brother and the original wife of the *yavam* **are exempt from *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Shimon says: Since** the third brother **came and found her in a permitted state, and she was never for a moment prohibited to him,** as when he was born she was already the wife of the second brother, who was still alive, **he** therefore **takes whichever he wishes in levirate marriage, or performs *ḥalitza* with whichever he wishes.**

In the first of the three *yibbum* orderings, Shimon performs *yibbum* with Rachel, then dies, and only then is Levi born. When Levi enters the world, Rachel is *eshet Shimon shelo haya be'olamo* — the wife of a brother, Shimon, who predated his existence. Everyone agrees this is a problem.

### Birth → Yibbum → Death

In the second ordering, Levi is born first, and only then does Shimon perform *yibbum* with Rachel, and then dies. When Levi was born, Shimon had not yet altered Rachel's status; she still bore the identity of Reuven's widow, as *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* with respect to Reuven. Shimon's subsequent *yibbum* does not retroactively change the status that was already fixed at the moment of Levi's birth. Everyone also agrees that this is a problem.

### Yibbum → Birth → Death (…

The third scenario is the exception: Shimon performs *yibbum* with Rachel, then Levi is born, and then Shimon dies. When Levi enters the world, Rachel is Shimon's wife. She has never stood before him in any prohibited status — *lo amda alav sha'ah achat be'issur*. Rabbi Shimon therefore rules that Levi may perform *yibbum* or *chalitzah* with either woman.

### Gemara's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בָּבָא דְסֵיפָא לְמַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ? אִילֵּימָא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר קָתָנֵי לַהּ — מִכְּדִי לָא שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין יִיבֵּם וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד בֵּין נוֹלַד וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִיבֵּם?! לְעָרְבִינְהוּ וְלִתְנִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **The section of the latter clause** of the *baraita*, which refers to the case of a brother born after the levirate marriage, **according to whom is it taught? If we say it is taught for** the purpose of clarifying the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** it does not make sense, **since it makes no difference to Rabbi Meir whether the levirate marriage preceded the birth or the birth preceded the levirate marriage.** In his opinion under both circumstances she is the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist. And if this were in fact taught for the purpose of clarifying his opinion **it should have combined** the cases **and taught them** together.

The *gemara* then asks a pointed structural question: why does the *braita* treat the third scenario as a separate clause? If Rabbi Shimon disagreed with all three scenarios equally, as Rabbi Oshaya had maintained, the *braita* should have grouped all three together. The fact that the first two scenarios are presented jointly — and only the third is introduced as a distinct case over which Rabbi Shimon dissents — proves that Rabbi Shimon's disagreement is confined to that third scenario alone. *Shema mina*: Rabbi Shimon disputes the ruling only when *yibbum* preceded Levi's birth, not when Levi was born before *yibbum* was performed.

## Rachel's Status Change

### Status Continuity Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וּבְיִיבֵּם וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד — פְּלִיג, בְּנוֹלַד וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִיבֵּם — לָא פְּלִיג. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** that the latter segment was meant to clarify the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** since the different parts of the *baraita* enumerate different possibilities? **And** Rabbi Shimon **disagrees in** the case when the brother first **performed levirate marriage and afterward** his brother **was born,** but he **does not disagree in** the case where the younger brother **was born and afterward** the second brother performed **levirate marriage.** The Gemara summarizes: **Conclude from this** that Rabbi Shimon disagrees only here, as Rav Pappa explained.

A natural question arises: if Rachel was already married to Shimon through *yibbum*, why should she continue to carry any legal identity as Reuven's widow in relation to Levi? Is she not now fully Shimon's wife?

The conceptual crux is this: at the moment of Levi's birth, a legal status attaches to him regarding Rachel. If *yibbum* occurred before he was born, then when he arrived in the world, Rachel was Shimon's wife — and that is how he encounters her. But if he was born before *yibbum* occurred, then at the moment of his birth, Rachel was Reuven's childless widow — and that status, *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo*, is fixed with respect to him from that moment forward. The subsequent act of Shimon's *yibbum* does not undo what was already legally established. The status attaches to the woman relative to Levi at the time of his birth, and it does not dissolve simply because circumstances later change.

### Status Retention Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: עָמַד הַשֵּׁנִי לַעֲשׂוֹת מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לַעֲשׂוֹת מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ עַד שֶׁנּוֹלַד לוֹ אָח, וָמֵת — רִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּשְׁנִיָּה — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. מַאי ״עָמַד״, וּמַאי ״לֹא הִסְפִּיק״? אִי עֲבַד — עֲבַד, וְאִי לָא עֲבַד — לָא עֲבַד!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss the *baraita* itself. **The Master said: The second was about to perform levirate betrothal with his *yevama*, but did not manage to perform levirate betrothal with his *yevama* before his brother was born, and** then the second brother **died. The first** woman **goes out** and is free to remarry without *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage **due to** the fact that **she was the wife of a brother with whom** the third brother **did not coexist, and the second** woman **performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **Was about to, and what is** the meaning of: **Did not manage** to perform levirate betrothal? The important issue is not his intention but his actions. **If he did it, he did it; and if he did not do it, he did not do it.**

This principle receives further elaboration in the *gemara*. The *mishna* elsewhere states that after *yibbum*, *harei hi k'ishto l'chol davar* — she is fully like his wife in every respect. Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina explains: this means he may divorce her with a *get* and remarry her. Yet if she is still somehow to be considered Reuven's widow, *chalitzah* — not *get* — would have been required to dissolve any such bond. The *gemara* resolves the tension through the principle of *shadi heiteira a'heiteira v'shadi issura a'issura*: where the legal context is entirely permissible — as in the case of divorce and remarriage after *yibbum* — we apply the more lenient framing and treat her fully as Shimon's wife, without importing the complication of her prior status as Reuven's widow. Where, however, the question involves a potential *issur ervah* — namely, her status relative to Levi — that prior status continues to obtain.

One might ask: does this not mean that a woman carries two statuses simultaneously? The answer, as the *gemara* makes clear, is precisely yes — at least for the purposes of the relevant *issur*. Rachel never loses her identity as *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* relative to Levi, regardless of her subsequent marriage to Shimon. In the domain of *issur*, that earlier status persists.

## Ma'amar Institution

### Intent Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: ״עָמַד״ — מִדַּעְתָּהּ, וְ״לֹא הִסְפִּיק״ — מִדַּעְתָּהּ, אֶלָּא בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי. דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: קָנָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the correct interpretation is: **Was about to** means that he was about to perform levirate betrothal **with her consent. Did not manage** means that he did not manage to perform it **with her consent, but instead** did it **against her will.** Consequently, it is understood that this *baraita* **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who performs levirate betrothal with his *yevama* without her consent, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: He acquired** her and the betrothal is fully valid, like a consensual levirate betrothal with his *yevama*; **and the Rabbis say: He did not acquire** her.

Returning to Case A of the *braita*, the *gemara* questions the unusual language: *amad hasheni la'asot ma'amar... velo hispik*. If he did *ma'amar*, he did it; if not, he did not — why describe an intention that was not fulfilled? The *gemara* explains that the language conveys a specific scenario: initially, Rachel agreed to the *ma'amar*, and then, before it could be executed with her consent, she withdrew her agreement. At that point, Shimon performed the *ma'amar* *be'al karchah* — without her acquiescence. The question then becomes whether such a *ma'amar* is legally effective.

### Ma'amar = Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי — גָּמַר מִבִּיאָה דִיבָמָה: מָה בִּיאָה דִיבָמָה — בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ, אַף קִדּוּשִׁין דִּיבָמָה — בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ. וְרַבָּנַן גָּמְרִי מִקִּדּוּשִׁין דְּעָלְמָא: מָה קִדּוּשִׁין דְּעָלְמָא — מִדַּעְתָּהּ, אַף קִדּוּשִׁין דִּיבָמָה — מִדַּעְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's opinion? **He learned** this **from** the case of a *yavam* engaging in **intercourse with a *yevama*. Just as** even **non-consensual intercourse with the *yevama*** renders her his wife, as the matter does not require her consent, **so too, betrothal of a *yevama*** can be **non-consensual.** But **the Rabbis learned from** the *halakhot* of **betrothal in general; just as betrothal in general requires consent** by the woman, **so too, betrothal of a *yevama*** for purposes of levirate marriage **requires consent.**

The *ma'amar* is a rabbinic institution — Chazal required the *yavam* to perform an act of *kiddushin* before proceeding to *yibbum*. As the Rambam formulates it: by rabbinic enactment, the *yavam* may not consummate the *yibbum* until he has consecrated her in front of two witnesses with a *perutah* or its equivalent in value — and this is called *ma'amar*. It is thus structurally equivalent to a standard *kiddushin*: money, witnesses, and — at least according to the *chachamim* — her consent. The dispute between Rebbi and the *chachamim* turns on precisely this last requirement.

### Rebbi's Logic

Rebbi holds that *ma'amar* performed without her consent is nonetheless legally effective. His reasoning: *gamar mi-bi'ah de-yevamah* — he derives the rule by analogy to the act of *yibbum* itself. Just as *yibbum* takes halakhic effect even against her will — since the *zikah* already constitutes a kind of pre-existing quasi-bond — so too the *ma'amar* that precedes it takes effect even without her agreement.

### Chachamim Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר מִילֵּי דִיבָמָה מִמִּילֵּי דִיבָמָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, וּמָר סָבַר: מִילֵּי דְקִדּוּשִׁין מִמִּילֵּי דְקִדּוּשִׁין הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what** principle do Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis **disagree? One Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that halakhic **matters concerning *yevamot* must be inferred from matters concerning *yevamot*** and not from other areas of *halakha*. **And one Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that halakhic **matters concerning** levirate **betrothal must be inferred from matters concerning betrothal.**

The *chachamim* disagree and rule that a *ma'amar* performed without her consent is legally null: *lo asah klum*. Their reasoning: *gamri mi-kiddushin de-alma* — since Chazal instituted *ma'amar* as a form of *kiddushin*, it must follow the rules of ordinary *kiddushin*, which requires her consent. A woman cannot be consecrated against her will: *ein ishah miskadeshes ela l'rtzonah*.

### Ma'amar Classification

The underlying dispute, the *gemara* frames explicitly: one view holds that since *ma'amar* is a *yevamah* institution, it should be governed by *yevamah* rules — and *yibbum* works even *be'al karchah*. The other view holds that since what Chazal created is a form of *kiddushin*, it must be governed by *kiddushin* rules — and *kiddushin* requires her consent. Our *braita*, by treating a *ma'amar* performed without consent as ineffective, follows the position of the *chachamim* and not Rebbi.

## Ma'amar + Birth Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד לוֹ אָח, אוֹ שֶׁנּוֹלַד לוֹ אָח וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר, וָמֵת — רִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּשְׁנִיָּה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּיאָתָהּ אוֹ חֲלִיצָתָהּ שֶׁל אַחַת מֵהֶן פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies another segment of the *baraita*. It is taught: If the second brother **performed levirate betrothal with her, and afterward his brother was born, or if his brother was born and then he performed levirate betrothal and died, the first** woman **goes out** and is free to remarry **because** she is **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, and the second,** the wife of the second brother, **performs *ḥalitza* but does not enter into levirate marriage. Rabbi Shimon says: Intercourse or *ḥalitza* with one of them exempts her rival wife.**

Scenario B involves *ma'amar* performed by Shimon with Rachel — whether before or after Levi's birth is immaterial — followed by Shimon's death. Rachel exits under the banner of *eishet achiv shelo haya be'olamo*; she was never fully married to Shimon and remains an *erva*. But because of the *ma'amar*, a quasi-relationship exists between Rachel and Shimon, which creates a quasi-*tzarat ervah* with respect to Sarah. The *rabbanim* rule: Sarah may only perform *chalitzah*, not *yibbum*, with Levi.

### All or Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַהֵיָיא קָאֵי? אִילֵּימָא אַנּוֹלַד לוֹ אָח וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר — הָא אָמְרַתְּ בְּנוֹלַד וּלְבַסּוֹף יִיבֵּם לָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֶלָּא אַעָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד לוֹ אָח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To which** case is **Rabbi Shimon referring? If we say** that he is referring to the case when **his brother was born and then he performed levirate betrothal with her, didn't you** already **say** that **Rabbi Shimon did not dispute** the case where the brother **was born and then ultimately he performed a levirate marriage,** and she would be forbidden as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist. **Rather,** one must say that Rabbi Shimon disputed the case where **he performed levirate betrothal with her and afterward his brother was born.**

Rabbi Shimon's position here is distinctive. He holds that *ma'amar* is not a middle category that creates a quasi-relationship — it is either fully equivalent to *yibbum* or it is nothing at all. If *ma'amar* counts as *yibbum* and it was performed before Levi's birth, then when Levi arrives, Rachel is already Shimon's wife, and Levi may perform *yibbum* or *chalitzah* with either woman. If *ma'amar* counts as nothing, then Rachel is entirely out of the picture — she is *eshet Reuven shelo haya be'olamo* and completely irrelevant — and Sarah is fully available to Levi without any complication from Rachel. Either way, the focus should be on Sarah. Rabbi Shimon therefore rules: *bi'aso o chalitzaso shel achad meihen poteres tzarasah* — the *bi'ah* or *chalitzah* performed with one of them — specifically Sarah — exempts the other.

### Safek Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָלַץ לְבַעֲלַת מַאֲמָר לֹא נִפְטְרָה צָרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲוַאי צָרָה וַדַּאי, וּבַעֲלַת מַאֲמָר סָפֵק, וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** Later in the *baraita* it is taught: If the third brother **performed *ḥalitza* with** the wife of the first brother, **to whom** the second brother **performed levirate betrothal, her rival wife is not exempt.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason** for this? It is **because the rival wife**, the widow of the second brother, has a **definite** legal status that requires an act to free her to remarry, as she is the wife of a brother with whom he did coexist, **whereas** the widow of the first brother **with whom** the second brother **performed levirate betrothal** had only **an uncertain** legal status, as it is not clear if she is to be considered truly the wife of the second brother by means of the levirate betrothal or not. **And** the principle is that **an uncertainty does not override a certainty.** Therefore, even if the third brother performs *ḥalitza*, since the status of the first woman's obligation is uncertain, the status of the *ḥalitza* itself is uncertain, as it is possible that she did not require *ḥalitza* at all. Consequently, this *ḥalitza* is not sufficient to exempt her rival wife. This teaches that he must perform *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage with the woman who is definitely obligated, and then the other will be exempt.

But what if Levi were to direct his attention to Rachel — performing *chalitzah* with the *ba'alas ma'amar* rather than with Sarah? The *gemara* rules: *chalatz l'va'alas ma'amar lo nifterah tzarah* — *chalitzah* to the *ma'amar* woman does not free the *tzarah*. The reasoning: Sarah's status as Shimon's wife and her obligation to Levi is *vadai* — certain. Rachel's status, by contrast, is a *safek* — she is either completely Shimon's wife or completely irrelevant. *V'ein safeik motzi miyedei vaday* — a matter of doubt cannot override a certainty. Directing the act of *chalitzah* toward Rachel, whose very eligibility is uncertain, accomplishes nothing for Sarah, whose standing is clear. Therefore, whatever is done must be done with Sarah.

## Tzaras Ailonit

### Klal's Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בִּיבָמָה: כֹּל שֶׁהִיא אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה וְאִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Sages **stated a principle about a *yevama*: Whoever is** forbidden **by a prohibition of forbidden relations** to her *yavam* **neither performs *ḥalitza* nor enters into levirate marriage** and is completely exempt. If she is forbidden by **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva** or by **a prohibition** stemming from **sanctity,** as will be explained later, then since in these cases the obligation of levirate marriage is not fundamentally nullified **she performs *ḥalitza*** in order to become free of the levirate bond, **and** due to her prohibition **she does not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *mishna*'s concluding formulation — *klal amru* — is taken by the *gemara* as signaling that something additional is being included beyond the cases explicitly enumerated. The question is: what?

### Rav Pappa Excludes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא: לְאֵתוֹיֵי צָרַת אַיְלוֹנִית, וְכִדְרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: This **principle** stated in the mishna about *yevamot*, **what** other cases does it **add?** Since the entire list of cases involving a forbidden relation was already detailed in the first chapter, what is this mishna adding? **Rafram bar Pappa said: It comes to include the** case of a **rival wife of a sexually underdeveloped woman [*aylonit*],** who is incapable of bearing children. Not only does an *aylonit* herself not enter into levirate marriage, since she is unable to give birth, but her rival wife is exempt as well. **And** this is **like** the principle **of Rav Asi,** who said that the rival wife of an *aylonit* is forbidden because the *aylonit* herself remains prohibited to the *yavam* as the wife of his brother, as she was never rendered permitted by the obligation of levirate marriage. Therefore, her rival wife is the rival wife of someone prohibited as a forbidden relation.

Rav Pappa offers one reading: the *klal* comes to include the case of *tzaras ailonit* — the *tzarah* of a woman who is an *ailonit*, a woman physiologically incapable of bearing children. Since there is no *mitzvas yibbum* with an *ailonit* — the entire purpose of *yibbum* being to perpetuate the deceased brother's name through offspring — her *tzarah* is likewise excluded from *yibbum* and *chalitzah*. This follows the position of Rav Assi. Rav Pappa acknowledges, however, that this is not the accepted ruling.

### Alternative Interpretati…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כׇּל שֶׁאִיסּוּרָהּ אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה, הוּא דַּאֲסִירָא צָרָתַהּ, הָא לָא אִיסּוּרָהּ אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — צָרָתַהּ לָא אֲסִירָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי — אָמַר רַפְרָם: לְמַעוֹטֵי צָרַת אַיְלוֹנִית, וּדְלָא כְּרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that Rafram bar Pappa's statement was made in a different context. It was taught in the mishna: **Whoever is forbidden** to her *yavam* **by a prohibition of forbidden relations** is completely exempt, which implies that **it is** specifically in such a case **that her rival wife is forbidden. But** any case when one wife **is not forbidden by a prohibition of forbidden relations** but is instead forbidden for some other reason, then **her rival wife is not forbidden.** This case comes **to exclude what? Rafram said: It excludes the rival wife of an *aylonit*,** who requires levirate marriage or *ḥalitza* because the *aylonit* is not prohibited as a forbidden relation. **And** this statement is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Asi.**

A second reading of the *klal* goes in the opposite direction. According to this view, the *mishna* is specifying that the prohibition on the *tzarah* applies only where the woman herself is an *issur ervah* — *kol she-issurah issur ervah hi d'assira tzarasah*. The implication: if the woman is excluded not because she is an *erva* but only because there is no *mitzvas yibbum* — as in the case of an *ailonit* — then her *tzarah* is not forbidden. This is the operative ruling: an *ailonit* is excluded from *yibbum* not on account of *ervah* but because there is no *mitzvah* to perform, and therefore her *tzarah* remains entirely permitted. This reading of the *klal* thus excludes *tzaras ailonit* from the prohibition — the inverse of Rav Assi's position — and this is indeed how we rule in practice.

## Status Permanence

### Status Erasure Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: כְּנָסָהּ — הֲרֵי הִיא כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמְּגָרְשָׁה בְּגֵט, וּמַחְזִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** what about **that which we learn** in a mishna (38a): If **he took** his *yevama* **in marriage** as his wife, **then her** legal status is **that of his wife in every sense; and Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** This **teaches** **that he must divorce** his *yevama* **with a bill of divorce** and she does not also require *ḥalitza*. **And he may remarry her,** if he wishes, after the divorce; as the *halakha* is not ruled in accordance with the opinion that after he performs the mitzva she is once more forbidden to him as his brother's wife. Why?

The *gemara* turns to examine the broader principle at stake in the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *rabbanim* regarding Rachel's status after *yibbum*. Once Shimon has married Rachel through *yibbum*, does her prior identity as Reuven's widow simply disappear? The *rabbanim* maintain that it does not — at least for the purpose of determining Levi's relationship to her — and they derive this from a careful reading of the relevant *pasuk*: *v'lekachah lo l'isha v'yibmah* — the Torah states both that he takes her as a wife and that he performs *yibbum*. The double formulation indicates that even after she becomes Shimon's wife, her identity as a *yevamah* — connected to Reuven — continues to persist. *Adayin yibbum me'harishonim aleha.*

### Divorce Not Chalitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּם מִשְׁמַיִם הָכָא נָמֵי! הָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״. שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וּלְקָחָהּ לוֹ לְאִשָּׁה״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁלְּקָחָהּ — נַעֲשֵׂית כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר. אִי הָכִי הָכָא נָמֵי! הָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** There, too, let** the *halakha* **say** that the verse states: **"And consummate the levirate marriage [*veyibbema*],"** as explained above, meaning that **the first levirate bond is still upon her and she should** also **require *ḥalitza*.** It is different there, as the verse states: "And take her to him to be his wife"** (Deuteronomy 25:5) to teach that **once he has taken her,** her legal status is **that of his wife in every sense.** The Gemara objects: **If so, here too,** in the case of a brother born after the levirate marriage, according to the opinion of the Rabbis this same principle should apply. The Gemara answers that **The Merciful One states: "And consummate the levirate marriage [*veyibbema*],"** that is, even after the marriage she is still considered to be the wife of the deceased brother [*yevama*] with respect to any brothers who are born later.

This raises a difficulty: if Rachel perpetually retains some status as Reuven's widow, then when Shimon wishes to divorce her after *yibbum*, should he not be required to perform *chalitzah* rather than give a *get*? The *mishna* states explicitly that after *yibbum* she is fully like his wife in every sense — *k'ishto l'chol davar* — and Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina explains that this means he may divorce her with a *get* and subsequently remarry her. But if she remains Reuven's widow in any meaningful sense, *chalitzah* would seem to be the appropriate mechanism. The tension is palpable.

### Shadi Heiteira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ? מִסְתַּבְּרָא שְׁדִי הֶיתֵּירָא אַהֶיתֵּירָא, וּשְׁדִי אִיסּוּרָא אַאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did you see** to distinguish in this way and say that once she is married the levirate obligation is totally abrogated with regard to *ḥalitza*, but that she remains prohibited as the wife of a brother with whom one did not coexist with respect to any brothers born in the future? The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** to say: **Toss that which is permitted on that which is permitted, and toss that which is prohibited on that which is prohibited.** In other words, in cases where the woman becomes permitted to her *yavam* through levirate marriage, it stands to reason that this permitted state is absolute, but with regard to the prohibition against taking the wife of a brother with whom one did not coexist, it stands to reason that the verse comes to teach that she retains her prohibited status with respect to any brothers born in the future.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty through the principle of *shadi heiteira a'heiteira v'shadi issura a'issura*: apply permissive rulings to permissive contexts, and apply restrictive rulings to restrictive contexts. When the question involves an entirely permissible situation — Shimon married Rachel lawfully through *yibbum* and now wishes to divorce her in an equally permissible context — we apply the lenient framing and treat her fully as his wife, subject only to *get*. We do not introduce the additional complication of Reuven's prior claim into a context where no *issur* is at stake.

### Ba'ah M'Tzad Heiteira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: הוֹאִיל וּבָא וּמְצָאָהּ בְּהֶיתֵּר וְלֹא עָמְדָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּאִיסּוּר, אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אֲחוֹתוֹ מֵאִמּוֹ שֶׁנְּשָׂאָהּ אָחִיו מֵאָבִיו, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹלַד אָח, וָמֵת — תִּתְיַיבֵּם, הוֹאִיל וּבָא וּמְצָאָהּ בְּהֶיתֵּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But according to Rabbi Shimon, who said** that **since he came and found her in a permitted state** a brother born subsequently may perform levirate marriage **since she was never for a single moment prohibited to him; however, if** that is **so,** consider the case of **one's maternal** half **sister,** who **married his paternal** half **brother;** their marriage was fully permissible since the husband and wife were in no way related to each other. **And then his brother was born and** the married brother **died;** in that case, **let** the sister **enter into levirate marriage** with her newly born half brother for the same reason, i.e., **since he came and found her in a permitted state,** as when he was born she was already his brother's wife.

By contrast, when Levi was born after *yibbum* and the question is whether Rachel constitutes an *issur* to him, we apply the restrictive framing: her status as *eshet achiv* — connected to Reuven — remains operative, and she is an *erva* to Levi. This is the principle of *ba'ah m'tzad heiteira* that Rabbi Shimon invokes from the other direction: since when Levi was born she came to him entirely from a position of permission — she was already Shimon's wife — she never stood before him even for a moment in a prohibited status, and therefore there is no *issur* to sustain.

### Sister Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר אֲחוֹתוֹ לְהֵיכָן אֲזַל?! הָכָא נָמֵי: אִיסּוּר אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בָּעוֹלָם לְהֵיכָן אֲזַל? הַאי אִיסּוּרָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ הֶיתֵּירָא, הַאי אִיסּוּרָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֶיתֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this suggestion: How can you say that? What happened to **the prohibition against** marrying **his sister, to where did it go?** This widow is the maternal sister of the newly born brother and is therefore forbidden to him. The Gemara objects: If so, **here too,** one could have said: What happened to **the prohibition** against marrying the wife of **a brother with whom one did not coexist, to where did it go?** In this case as well, the prohibition against marrying the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist already applied from the first brother, so why does Rabbi Shimon see this as abrogated by marriage? The Gemara answers that the comparison is unsound. **This prohibition** against marrying one's sister **has no** case where it is **permitted** and so in this case is also not canceled, whereas **that prohibition** against marrying a brother's wife **has** a case where it is **permitted,** when the mitzva of levirate marriage applies to a second brother and is therefore removed completely before the third brother is born.

The *gemara* presses further: if it is true that once Rachel married Shimon her prior status as Reuven's widow is dissolved relative to Levi, then by the same logic, a sister who is also a *yevamah* should likewise lose her forbidden status as a sister once she marries Shimon through *yibbum* — and then when Levi is born, she should be fully permitted to him. Why not apply the same reasoning? The *gemara* answers decisively: *hai issur delait lei heiteira* — the prohibition of a sister is absolute and permanent; it has no mechanism for dissolution. *Hai issur de'it lei heiteira* — the prohibition of a dead brother's wife, by contrast, is inherently dissolvable; the entire *masechta Yevamot* is premised on the fact that under certain circumstances that *erva* status is overridden by *yibbum*. Because the *issur* of *eshet achiv* admits of a *heter*, once she marries Shimon we no longer look at her through the lens of that prior *issur*. But sisterhood, carrying no such *heter*, never dissolves — and the sister analogy therefore does not hold.

## Forbidden Women Rules

### Rule Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בִּיבָמָה: כֹּל שֶׁהִיא אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה וְאִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. אֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Sages **stated a principle about a *yevama*: Whoever is** forbidden **by a prohibition of forbidden relations** to her *yavam* **neither performs *ḥalitza* nor enters into levirate marriage** and is completely exempt. If she is forbidden by **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva** or by **a prohibition** stemming from **sanctity,** as will be explained later, then since in these cases the obligation of levirate marriage is not fundamentally nullified **she performs *ḥalitza*** in order to become free of the levirate bond, **and** due to her prohibition **she does not enter into levirate marriage.** The Sages stated another principle: If two sisters who had been married to two brothers who subsequently died happened before the third brother for levirate marriage, and one of those sisters is a close relation to this third brother and is therefore forbidden to him, she is exempt from levirate marriage. But the other, **her sister who is her *yevama*,** i.e., her sister-in-law, **performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage.** In this case, they are not ruled to be two sisters who happened before him simultaneously for levirate marriage, since one of them is prohibited to him as a forbidden relation, and therefore she never actually happened before him at all.

The *mishna* formulates a comprehensive principle: *kol shehi issur ervah lo choletzet velo mityabemet* — any woman who is a genuine *erva* neither performs *chalitzah* nor *yibbum*, and her *tzarah* likewise exits entirely. Three categories of lesser *issurim* are then distinguished: *issur mitzvah*, *issur kedushah*, and the case of a sister who is simultaneously a *yevamah*.

### Isur Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה — שְׁנִיּוֹת מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים. אִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה — אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְנָתִין וּמַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna explains: **A prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva** is referring to **secondary** forbidden **relationships,** which are prohibited **by rabbinic law.** The Sages prohibited marriage to certain women who were not forbidden by the Torah but were nevertheless deemed forbidden incestuous relations. **A prohibition** stemming from **sanctity** is referring to marriage of **a widow to a High Priest, a divorcée or a woman who has performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*] to a common priest, a daughter born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzeret*]** or a **Gibeonite woman to an Israelite, and** also **an Israelite woman to a Gibeonite or** to **a son born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*].**

*Issur mitzvah* refers to *shniyot mi-divrei soferim* — secondary *arayos* that are forbidden by rabbinic enactment. Because such a prohibition is *de-rabbanan* in character, and since *kiddushin* with a woman forbidden by a rabbinic prohibition is binding *bedi'avad*, there remains a *zikah* in a *yibbum* context and therefore an obligation of *chalitzah*. The woman thus performs *chalitzah* but not *yibbum*.

### Isur Kedushah

*Issur kedushah* refers to prohibitions arising from the laws of *kehunah*: an *almanah l'Kohen Gadol*, a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* to an ordinary *kohen*, or a *mamzeret* married to a *yisrael* — and vice versa, a daughter of Israel married to a *mamzer*. Since these are *lavin* rather than *arayot*, the *kiddushin* is binding *bedi'avad* and a *zikah* exists; *chalitzah* is therefore required, but *yibbum* is prohibited.

### Sister-Yevama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ. אֲחוֹתָהּ דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא דְּאִיסּוּר מִצְוָה, כֵּיוָן דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ, פָּגַע בַּאֲחוֹת זְקוּקָתוֹ! אֶלָּא אֲחוֹתָהּ דְּאִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **Her sister who is her *yevama*** performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage. The Gemara clarifies this: **Whose sister? If we say** it is the sister of one **who is** forbidden due to **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva, since by Torah** law **she is cast before him** for levirate marriage, then this would in fact simply be a case of two sisters who fell simultaneously before him, both requiring levirate marriage, since according to Torah law there is no prohibition against entering into levirate marriage with such a woman. If so, it turns out that **he encounters the sister of the woman with whom he has** a levirate **bond; but** that case has already been taught. **Rather,** it must refer to **the sister of** a woman who is forbidden to him by a **prohibition of forbidden relations,** and since he may not enter into levirate marriage with a forbidden relation, her sister is not considered to be the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond. Therefore, the sister may be taken in levirate marriage.

The case of *achotah shehi yevamtah* — a sister who is also a sister-in-law — arises when two brothers married two sisters. For example, Reuven married Rachel, Levi married Leah, and both Reuven and Levi died. Now Rachel and Leah stand together before a third brother, Zevulun. Since he has a quasi-*kiddushin* — a *zikah* — with both women simultaneously, consummating a relationship with one would constitute intimacy with *achos zikukaso*, the sister of his quasi-wife. He therefore cannot perform *yibbum* with either. However, if one of the two women is an *erva* to Zevulun — say, she is his daughter — then she exits the picture entirely, and he may perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum* with the other. The *mishna*'s ruling of *choletzet o mityabemet* thus applies where the one who is the *erva* frees the other from the problem of *achos zikukaso*.
