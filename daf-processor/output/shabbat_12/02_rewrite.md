# Shabbat 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Carrying & Karmelis

### Intro & Zav Case

We pick up three lines from the bottom of 11b. To briefly orient ourselves: we have finally arrived at the *mishnah* dealing with *erev shabbat* and the activities one may not perform *samuch l'chasheicha* — close to nightfall. The *mishnah* discusses, for example, a tailor going out holding a needle, or various craftsmen carrying the tools of their trade as Shabbat approaches.

### Samuch L'Chasheicha

It is worth noting that the earlier discussion of eating before *mincha* uses the phrase *samuch l'mincha*, whereas here the *mishnah* uses *samuch l'chasheicha* — much closer to the end of the day. The *Yerushalmi* actually focuses on this distinction, though for our purposes the key point is simply that *samuch l'chasheicha* refers to the final moments before Shabbat begins. The concern throughout this section of the *mishnah* is that these activities will inadvertently lead to *chillul shabbat* — whether by accidentally carrying in the public domain, or by adjusting a flame while reading by candlelight (*hataya*).

### Zav/Zavah in Mishna

The *mishnah* also includes a ruling that a *zav* should not eat together with a *zavah* — *shelo yochal im hazavah* — because it may lead to prohibited intimacy, which carries *kares*. This ruling has nothing to do with Shabbat, and its presence in a *mishnah* about *erev shabbat* understandably raises a question: what is it doing here?

### Goldberg's Gezeirah

Two answers are worth noting. The first comes from Avraham Goldberg, a scholar who wrote an academic commentary on *Mishnah Shabbat* approximately fifty years ago. His argument is more precise than a general claim that *gezeirot* are uncommon in the *Mishnah*. He distinguishes between two types of preventive legislation. In the *Mishnah*, what we find is: do not do A, because in the very process of doing A, B will result. The tailor should not go out *erev shabbat* holding a needle, because that carrying will turn into carrying on Shabbat. One should not read *l'or haneir*, because in the act of reading one will come to tip the lamp. The *zav* should not eat with the *zavah*, because one thing will lead to another in the course of that same encounter. This is categorically different from the *gezeirot* so common in the *Bavli*, where the concern is: if we permit you to do this today, next week you will go on to do something else entirely. Goldberg's point is that all the cases in the *mishnah* share this same quality — one action naturally evolving into another within a single event — and that is why the *zav* case was incorporated here, even though it has no direct connection to Shabbat.

### Safrai Theory

A second explanation comes from a recent scholarly edition of the *Mishnah* produced by the Safrai family — academics who situate the *Mishnah* within the historical and cultural reality of *Eretz Yisrael*. They raise the possibility that the preceding *mishnah* and this *mishnah* were never originally part of *Masechet Shabbat* at all. Perhaps there existed an independent collection of *gezeirot* of the form "do not do A, lest it lead to B" — do not go to the barber before *mincha* lest you forget *mincha*, do not do various things that will lead to *chillul shabbat*, do not have a *zav* eat with a *zavah* lest it lead to *hergel aveirah* — and this collection was subsequently embedded into the tractate. On this reading, the *mishnah* was not originally constructed as a Shabbat teaching; it was a thematic anthology of such causal *gezeirot* that was placed here editorially.

### Abaye v. Rava

With that background, we turn to the central concern of today's *daf*. The question is: when the Sages enact a prohibition because it might lead to carrying, do they extend that protection even when the concern is carrying in a *karmelit* — a semi-public domain that is only rabbinically prohibited? This became a dispute between Abaye and Rava. Abaye holds that yes, even a concern that one will come to carry in a *karmelit* — a *d'rabbanan* — is sufficient grounds for the *gezeirah*. We make a *gezeirah l'gezeirah* even to protect against a rabbinic prohibition. Rava disagrees: we do not extend the *gezeirah* to protect against a *reshus d'rabbanan*.

The *gemara* then turns to the *mishnah* to see whether it can prove either position. The *mishnah* mentions that a tailor should not go out with a needle stuck in his garment. The *gemara* assumes this is not the normal mode of carrying — and therefore the concern must be that it will lead to *d'rabbanan* carrying, which would support Abaye. But Rava responds: this is according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that for a craftsman, wearing the tools of his trade — even in a manner that ordinary people would not — constitutes a normal mode of carrying. So the *gezeirah* is still protecting against a *d'oraita*, not merely a *d'rabbanan*.

## Zav & Pad Case

### Two vs. Three Flows

The *gemara* now focuses on the case of the *zav* — a man who experiences a genital discharge rendering him *tamei*, likely something analogous to gonorrhea. The *mishnah* states that a *zav* may not go out on Shabbat with a pad to catch the discharge, and if he does, he has transgressed the prohibition of carrying. However, there are two conflicting formulations — one that makes him *chayav* and one that makes him *patur*. How do we reconcile them?

*Ela amar Rav Hamnuna: lo kashya. Kan b'zav ba'al shtei reiyot, kan b'zav ba'al shalosh reiyot.* Rav Hamnuna resolves the contradiction as follows. A *zav* who has experienced two flows is awaiting a third, because three flows is what obligates him to bring a *korban*. When he wears this pad in his undergarment on Shabbat, he is actively interested in capturing any additional discharge so that he can determine whether he has had a third flow. Since he has a real purpose in carrying the pad, even though he carries it in an unusual manner — within his undergarments rather than in his hands — this constitutes a genuine act of carrying, and he is *chayav*. What about a *zav* with three flows who is already obligated to bring a *korban*? There the *gemara* says he is *patur*, because he is no longer interested in capturing additional flow for that purpose.

### Exemption Problem

But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: even a *zav ba'al shalosh reiyot* should have a reason to capture the flow. *Zav ba'al shalosh nami mibaei leh l'sefira* — he needs seven clean days before his purification is complete, and if he has another discharge on a separate day, it will interrupt those seven days. So he too has an interest in knowing whether he discharged.

The *gemara* answers: *lo nitzrecha ela l'bo bayom* — the case is specifically on the very day he had his third flow. On that day, halachically speaking, an additional discharge makes no difference — he is already in the midst of his flow-day, so the count is unaffected. In such a case, he has no genuine interest in capturing the discharge, and he is therefore *patur*.

### Protecting Garments

The *gemara*, however, is not satisfied: *v'ha mibaei leh k'dei shelo yitanfu kelav* — even if he has no *halachic* need to capture the discharge, he still wants to keep his garments clean! He is wearing the pad to protect his clothing, and that is still a genuine purpose. Why should he be *patur*?

The *gemara* answers in the name of Rabbi Zeira: *hai tanna hu d'amar kol atzulei tinuf lo k'chashiv* — this opinion holds that preventing soiling is not considered a significant purpose. Now what exactly does that mean? One possibility is that since most people do not bother carrying something merely to keep their garments clean in this way, it resembles carrying in an abnormal fashion — like the quill in the ear — and is therefore treated as an irregular mode of carrying. Alternatively, and this becomes halachically significant as we will see, it may mean that an item worn purely to protect one's garments achieves the status of a garment itself. If something is worn only to protect clothing rather than the body, perhaps it is not truly "carried" at all.

This raises an immediate practical question: why is one permitted to wear a raincoat on Shabbat when there is no *eruv*? After all, a raincoat is not there to protect the wearer directly — it is there to keep the garment underneath dry. Perhaps it is permitted precisely on the logic that such protective clothing is categorized as clothing rather than as a carried object, and therefore its being moved from domain to domain is not prohibited carrying.

### Analogy Rejected

The *gemara* then draws a comparison to a ruling in the laws of *hechsher okhlin* — the capacity of liquids to render food susceptible to *tumah*. The case: someone turns a vessel upside down on a wall in the rain. *Im bishvil shetudach keara* — if the intention is to wash the vessel, then the rainwater that lands on it is *bechiyutan*, meaning the person desired that liquid, and it therefore has the capacity to render food susceptible to *tumah*. *Im bishvil shelo yelakeh hakotel* — if however the intention is only to protect the wall, then the person did not want the liquid, and it is *eino bechiyutan*.

The analogy is admittedly strange — one *halacha* concerns carrying on Shabbat, the other concerns the capacity of liquid to convey susceptibility to *tumah* — but the conceptual point being made is: just as merely protecting something does not render the liquid significant, so too merely protecting garments does not render the pad significant.

The *gemara* then rejects this analogy: *mi dami?* The cases are not parallel. *Hasam lo kaba'i lehu lehaney maya klal* — in the vessel-and-wall case, one is not interested in the liquid at all; the liquid is entirely incidental. But *hacha kaba'i leh lehai kis lekibuley d'ziva* — in the *zav* case, the person is genuinely interested in the pad itself, as a receptacle to catch the discharge. This is comparable to the continuation of that very *mishnah*: a kneading trough left out in the rain — liquid that spatters out or floats on top is *eino bechiyutan*, since it was not retained, but liquid that remains inside the trough is *bechiyutan*, because the vessel was meant to hold it. Similarly, the *zav*'s pad is genuinely being used as a receptacle. The analogy therefore fails to support the view that the pad is insignificant.

### Melacha She'Eina

Having exhausted alternative explanations, the *gemara* offers the definitive resolution: *ela Abaye v'Rava d'amrei tarvaihu, lo kashya — ha Rabbi Yehuda v'ha Rabbi Shimon.* Both Abaye and Rava agree that the apparent contradiction is resolved by recognizing that the two opinions in the *mishnah* reflect the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon regarding *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* — a *melacha* performed not for its intrinsic intended purpose.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that even if one performs a *melacha* without its classic intended purpose, one is *chayav*. Rabbi Shimon holds that one is *chayav* only when the *melacha* is performed for its intrinsic purpose. How does this apply to the *zav*? When carrying is performed to protect garments rather than to transport an object from one domain to another for the sake of placing it there, this is arguably not carrying for its intrinsic purpose — one is not interested in the object being in the destination domain per se; one simply wants to keep it with oneself. According to Rabbi Shimon, that constitutes *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*, and the *zav* is therefore *patur*.

### Rashi's Approach

Rashi's explanation of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* is worth noting separately, as it differs from the standard formulation. The conventional understanding is that *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* means performing a *melacha* not for its classic purpose — for example, trapping a scorpion not because one wants the scorpion, but to prevent it from biting. Rashi, however, offers a different framing: *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* describes a *melacha* that is performed in response to an undesirable circumstance — removing something bad rather than constructing something positive. The *zav*, who wishes he did not have this discharge and is using the pad purely to manage a condition he would rather not have, is in this sense not engaging in the *melekhet machshevet* — the purposive, constructive labor — that Shabbat prohibits at the *d'oraita* level. This is a distinctive approach that Rashi returns to in other contexts.

## Delousing & Candles

### Delousing Issue

Before resuming the broader discussion of *karmelit* and carrying, the *gemara* turns to another clause of the *mishnah*: *lo yefaleh kelav* — one should not delouse one's garments on Shabbat — and *lo yikra l'or haneir* — one should not read by candlelight. The *gemara* raises a question about how to read these two prohibitions. Is the prohibition against delousing independent — forbidden at any time on Shabbat because one might kill a louse — or is it specifically a prohibition against delousing by candlelight, parallel to the prohibition against reading by candlelight?

## Practical Cases

### Raincoats & Hats

The discussion of the *zav*'s pad has direct *halachic* relevance for several contemporary situations. The *rishonim* adopt the position that wearing something to protect one's body — even indirectly, as when a raincoat prevents one's garments from becoming wet and thereby clinging to the body — is considered normal clothing and raises no issue of carrying. The more difficult case is the plastic hat cover. If it is raining heavily and one covers one's hat with such a cover, the argument that protecting the hat is protecting one's head becomes strained, since the cover is not in direct contact with the body. One practice in response is to make a small pinhole in the hat cover before Shabbat, so that were it to rain, water would penetrate — thus allowing one to argue that even when covering the hat, one is protecting one's head from the water that would eventually come through.

### Sanitary Pads

The same analysis applies to a woman who wears a sanitary pad on Shabbat where there is no *eruv*. If the flow is significant and the pad is preventing discharge from reaching her body and causing discomfort, the pad functions as protective clothing for the body and raises no issue. The question becomes more complex in a case where the flow is minimal — say, near the end of her cycle — and the concern is only that it might soil her undergarment, not that it will reach her body. In that scenario, the analogy to the *zav* case is closer, and the *halachic* status of the pad is less clear.

### Hefsek Tahara Issue

Rav Moshe addresses two related *teshuvot* on these questions. In the first, he discusses a woman in the minimal-flow scenario described above. He concludes that he cannot readily find grounds to permit it, and suggests instead that she simply wear an extra layer of undergarments — though he acknowledges uncertainty about whether that fully resolves the concern if the discharge might pass through to the outer garment without reaching the body.

The second and more serious question concerns women who follow the *minhag* of wearing a *moch dachuk* — a tightly inserted pad — from before sunset on the day of *hefsek tahara* through *tzeis hakochavim*, as part of the transition from the period of *niddah* to the seven clean days. Rav Moshe notes that in this case the woman may not have been bleeding for some time, and the pad is not there to protect her body at all; it is there entirely for *halachic* checking purposes, analogous precisely to the *zav ba'al shtei reiyot* who wears the pad to capture and inspect the discharge. If she walks to shul on Friday night without an *eruv*, this appears to fall squarely into the *zav* case and ought to be prohibited.

One might have expected Rav Moshe to simply rule that she should not go out, or that the *minhag* of *moch dachuk* should not be observed on Friday night given that it is not *halachically* required — and indeed he does argue that since *moch dachuk* is only a *minhag*, there may be grounds to set it aside. But he also works toward a further argument: even if the *gemara* considers the *zav*'s mode of carrying — wearing the pad in the undergarment — to be the normal manner of carrying for someone in that condition, wearing something inserted inside the body itself might not constitute a normal *derech* of carrying. He attempts to reduce the concern to a *d'rabbanan* and then to argue further that the unusual mode of carrying renders it below the threshold of prohibition. He acknowledges, however, that this line of reasoning is not straightforward.

## Delousing & Candles…

### Killing Lice

Returning to the *mishnah*: *lo yefaleh kelav* — do not delouse your garments. The *gemara* poses the question: does this prohibition apply during the daytime generally, because one might kill a louse (*shema yahrog*), according to the position of Rabbi Eliezer? *D'tanya: amar Rabbi Eliezer, horeg kinah beShabbat ke'ilu horeg gamal* — one who kills a louse on Shabbat is as if he killed a camel. The vivid image makes the point: regardless of the creature's size, it is a living being, and the same *halacha* applies.

### Reading Candlelight

The prohibition against reading *l'or haneir* is stated separately: *shema yateh* — lest one tip the candle to get more light. The *gemara* asks whether these are two entirely separate *gezeirot* — one about killing lice (applicable at any time), and one about reading by candlelight — or whether the *l'or haneir* applies to both: do not delouse your garments *by candlelight* and do not read *by candlelight*, both being prohibited for the same reason.

### Chananya Attic

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that reads: *ein polin l'or haneir v'ein korin l'or haneir* — one may not delouse by candlelight and one may not read by candlelight. This *baraita* makes explicit that the delousing prohibition is specifically tied to candlelight and shares the same rationale as the reading prohibition — *shema yateh*, lest one tip the flame. The *gemara* concludes: *shema mina detarvayhu shema yateh* — we derive that both prohibitions share this single concern. Furthermore, this ruling is identified as one of the *halachot* taught in the attic of Chananya ben Chizkiya ben Guryon — the famous gathering of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel that produced a series of significant rulings, which we will encounter in the next *mishnah*.

## Practical Cases…

### Medicine & Objects

The discussion of the *zav* and *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* has significant practical implications for cases where a person needs to carry an object not because they want it transported to a specific location, but because they need it on their person at all times. Consider someone who must carry identification or medication — a nitroglycerin tablet for a cardiac condition, or an inhaler — where there is no *eruv*. The object is not being carried to a destination; it needs simply to remain with the person. This is structurally similar to the *zav* carrying the pad. The *gemara*'s analysis means that while such carrying may not be entirely *mutar*, it may be reduced from a *d'oraita* to a *d'rabbanan* — and that distinction is halachically critical in practice.

## Tefillin & Shabbat

### Tefillin Erev Shabbat

*Tanna d'bei Rabbi Yishmael*: a person may be wearing *tefillin* on *erev shabbat* right up until it becomes dark — *yotze adam bitefillin erev shabbos im chashecha*. We are not concerned that he will forget to remove them before Shabbat. The reasoning is rooted in the principle that Shabbat is *lav zman tefillin* — wearing *tefillin* on Shabbat is not considered a form of dress or adornment, and since Shabbat is not a time for *tefillin*, retaining them would constitute carrying rather than wearing.

### Mashmesh Principle

Why are we not afraid he will forget? *Keivan d'amar Rabbah bar Rav Huna: chayav adam lemashmesh bitefillin kol sha'ah v'sha'ah* — a person is obligated to constantly touch and feel his *tefillin*, maintaining active awareness that he is wearing them. This obligation of *mashmesh* ensures he will not forget.

### Tzitz Kal V'Chomer

The source for the *mashmesh* obligation is a *kal v'chomer* from the *tzitz* — the golden headplate worn by the *Kohen Gadol*. The *tzitz* bears only a single mention of God's name, yet the Torah states *v'hayah al mitzcho tamid* — it shall be on his forehead always — meaning the *Kohen Gadol* must maintain constant consciousness of it, *shelo yasach da'ato mimmenu*. *Tefillin*, by contrast, contain God's name many times over. How much more so, then, must one not allow one's attention to wander from them. Since this *mashmesh* requirement keeps a person continuously aware of his *tefillin*, *hilchach midkar dachir lehu* — he will remember they are on him and will remember to remove them before Shabbat.

## Checking Clothes

### Chananya's Teaching

*Tanya, Chananya omer: chayav adam lemashmesh bivigadav erev shabbat im chashecha* — one is obligated to check one's garments on *erev shabbat* before nightfall, lest one have forgotten that one is carrying something in a pocket and inadvertently carry it into the public domain on Shabbat. This differs from the earlier *mishnah*, which addressed cases where one *consciously* holds an object *erev shabbat*. Chananya's ruling addresses the case where one is entirely unaware — one may simply not know that something is in one's pocket.

### Halacha Rabta

*Amar Rav Yosef: hilchata rabta l'shabbat* — this is a major and important *halacha* for Shabbat: check your pockets before Shabbat.

### Tosafot Question

This ruling raises a noteworthy question, which *Tosafot* poses in connection with the *mishnah*: if a person carries something in his pocket without awareness, he never consciously performed an *akirah* — he never intentionally lifted the object. Without the requisite mental state, this cannot be *melekhet machshevet*, and it would never constitute a *d'oraita* violation. The prohibition against such carrying is therefore always *d'rabbanan* at most — and the instruction to check pockets is, on this analysis, a *gezeirah l'gezeirah*. The *Beit Yosef* raises the same concern. One answer is that we do rule like Abaye, who permits *gezeirah l'gezeirah* in the domain of carrying, and the importance of checking pockets falls within that framework. Alternatively, one might envision scenarios where the person discovers mid-Shabbat that he has his keys in his pocket, stops, starts walking again — and what began as unconscious carrying could shade into something more deliberate. In any event, while the debate of Abaye and Rava is real, the practical ruling appears to be that checking one's pockets is a significant obligation.

## Inspection & Light

### Distinguishing Clothes

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: afilu lehavchin bein bigdo lebigdei isha* — once the prohibition is established against any close inspection by candlelight, Rav Yehuda extends it further. Even going to one's closet on Shabbat and attempting to distinguish by candlelight which garments belong to the husband and which to the wife is prohibited. This implies that the garments would look sufficiently similar to require careful examination.

### City vs. Field

*Amar Rava: lo amran ela debnei Mechuza* — this ruling applies specifically to city-dwellers like those of Mechuza, where men's and women's clothing may resemble each other. *Aval debnei chaklita, meida yadei* — but for people who work the land, the distinction is obvious at a glance and no careful inspection is needed. Rashi explains that working people need clothing that allows physical mobility — narrower, more fitted garments — whereas men of the urban middle and upper classes can wear looser clothing similar in cut to women's garments. Within the city as well: even among city dwellers, the concern applies only to *zekanta* — older women, whose clothing may more closely resemble men's — but not to younger women, *diyaldos*, where the difference remains apparent.

### Public Delousing

*Ein polin birshus harabbim mipnei hakavod* — one should not delouse one's garments in the public domain even during the week, as this is considered disrespectful to those around. Similarly, Rabbi Yehuda (and some attribute it to Rabbi Nechemia) rules that one should not take an emetic in a public space — *ein osin apiktoizin birshus harabbim mipnei hakavod*.

## Killing Lice

### Molel V'Zorek

*Hamapleh kelav, molel vezorek, uvilvad shelo yaharog* — when delousing one's garments on Shabbat, one may roll the louse between the fingers and then discard it, provided one does not kill it. The concern about killing lice on Shabbat is the reason for this restriction.

### Abba Shaul

*Abba Shaul omer: molel vezorek, uvilvad shelo yimlol* — Abba Shaul is more stringent: one may not even roll the louse. According to Rashi, both positions regard killing as problematic, and the dispute is whether rolling falls within the protective fence. *Tosafot* offers a different reading: both might agree that killing lice is not itself the concern, but rolling is prohibited because permitting it might lead one to perform other actions that would kill insects whose killing is genuinely prohibited.

### Rav Huna Permits

*Amar Rav Huna: halacha hi molel vezorek vezehu kevodo va'afilu bechol* — Rav Huna rules that rolling and discarding is the proper practice, and indeed the respectful one — even during the week. Up to this point, the consensus appears to be that killing lice is at minimum *d'rabbanan*.

### Rava & Rav Sheshes

A contrary voice now emerges. *Rava maktel lehu* — Rava would kill them. *Rav Sheshes maktel lehu* — Rav Sheshes would kill them. Rashi reads this as referring to Shabbat. *Rava shadi lehu lekana demaya* — Rava would drop them into a bucket of water to drown them. *Rav Nachman* told his daughters, who were delousing the household's garments: *katulin* — kill them, *ve'ashmina li kala desanvatai* — let me hear the sound of my enemies dying. The halachic basis for these permissive positions is the *gemara*'s conviction that lice do not reproduce sexually but generate spontaneously from dust. Since they do not reproduce, there is no *melacha* of *nefesh* — no genuinely prohibited killing — in destroying them. This is indeed the *psak* in the *Shulchan Aruch*, which opens difficult questions about *halacha* and science, and whether anyone today could genuinely rule that killing lice is *mutar*.

### Shammai v. Hillel

*Tanya: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer: ein horgin et hamacholet beShabbat, divrei Beit Shammai. U'Beit Hillel mattirin* — we taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Beit Shammai that one may not kill lice on Shabbat, while Beit Hillel permits it.

The same *baraita* continues: *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* also quotes Rabban Gamliel as ruling that one may not arrange a betrothal for young daughters, nor hire a tutor to teach one's son, nor arrange an apprenticeship on Shabbat — *ein meshadchin et hatinokot le'ares, velo et hatinok lelamdo sefer, ul'lamdo omanut*. And further: *ein menachamin aveilim ve'ein mevakrin cholim beShabbat* — one may not comfort mourners or visit the sick on Shabbat. All of these are what Beit Shammai prohibits; Beit Hillel permits them all. The first three concern activities with a business or transactional character. However, since their ultimate purpose is *cheftzei shamayim* — serving religious and communal ends — Beit Hillel permits them, just as synagogues routinely make announcements about communal affairs on Shabbat on the same basis. Visiting the sick and comforting mourners are different in character — these are not business transactions — but Beit Shammai prohibits them as well, on the grounds that they disturb one's equanimity and draw one into distress on Shabbat. Beit Hillel, however, rules that the *bein adam lachaveiro* dimension of these acts takes precedence.

## Visiting Sick

### Shammai v. Hillel

*Tanu rabbanan: hanichnas levaker et hacholeh* — when one comes to visit the sick on Shabbat, one says: *Shabbat hi milizok u'refua kerova lavo* — it is Shabbat, and one is not to cry out; healing will come soon. This formula — familiar from the *mi sheberach* for the sick recited on Shabbat — is not merely a rhetorical permission to speak about the illness. Rather, it is a message to the patient: Shabbat is not the time for lengthy complaints about one's aches, poor service, or suffering. The visitor is there to offer presence and comfort, not to serve as an audience for grievances.

### Chanina Stringency

*Amar Rabbi Chanina: bekoshi hitiru lenachem aveilim ulevaker cholim beShabbat* — only with difficulty did the Sages permit comforting mourners and visiting the sick on Shabbat. This is a striking statement. There was genuine ambivalence about whether bringing the weight of illness and grief into one's Shabbat experience was appropriate. Ultimately, the *bein adam lachaveiro* obligations prevailed, and *bikur cholim* was permitted. *Nichum aveilim* on Shabbat remains more constrained — we do not pay formal *shiva* calls on Shabbat, both because the *avel* himself does not observe *shiva* publicly on Shabbat and because of the general hesitation about engaging with mourning on that day.

### Blessings for Sick

Three different formulations for what one says when visiting the sick are recorded. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: Hamakom yerachem alecha ve'al cholei Yisrael* — God should have compassion on you and on the sick of Israel. *Rabbi Yossi omer: Hamakom yerachem alecha betoch cholei Yisrael* — God should have compassion on you *among* the other sick of Israel, weaving the individual's illness into the collective. *Shevna Ish Yerushalayim*: upon entering, he would simply say *shalom*, and upon leaving he would say *Shabbat hi milizok u'refua kerova lavo verachamav merubim veshivto beshalom* — Shabbat prevents crying out; healing is near; His compassion is great; may your Shabbat be one of peace.

*Tosafot* raises an interesting question in connection with Rabbi Yehuda's blessing. Rabbi Yehuda appears elsewhere — in the context of *Rosh Hashanah* — to hold that one's judgment is written on *Rosh Hashanah* and sealed on *Yom Kippur*. If so, how does Rabbi Yehuda here encourage praying for a sick person's recovery on Shabbat throughout the year? If the judgment has already been sealed, what purpose does the prayer serve? *Tosafot* offers the answer that the *gemara* itself gives elsewhere: even if one's judgment was rendered at the beginning of the year, *tefillah* is always efficacious. This is indeed how we conduct ourselves — we treat *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur* as the time of *gmar din* and yet believe unequivocally that prayer at any moment can change the outcome.

### Aramaic Prayer

*Keman azla ha de'amar Rabbi Chanina: mi sheyesh lo choleh betoch beiso tzarich sheyiarvenu betoch cholei Yisrael* — when praying for a sick member of one's household, one should include that person within the broader prayer for all of Israel's sick, rather than focusing solely on one's own relative. This aligns with Rabbi Yossi's formulation of *betoch cholei Yisrael* — among the sick of Israel.

The *gemara* then records that *Rabbah bar Bar Channah* observed Rabbi Elazar visiting the sick and sometimes saying *Hamakom yifkedcha leshalom* — God should visit you in peace — and sometimes saying in Aramaic: *Rachamana yidkerach leshalem* — the Merciful One should remember you for peace. This occasions a challenge: *Rav Yehuda* ruled that one should never pray for one's needs in Aramaic — *le'olam al yishal adam tzerachav bilshon Arami* — and *Rabbi Yochanan* added that if one prays in Aramaic, the ministering angels will not attend to the prayer, since they do not engage with Aramaic.

This requires explanation. The restriction on Aramaic appears to be specific to that language — other vernaculars are discussed elsewhere (notably in *Mishnah Sotah*) as permissible for prayer. Aramaic may have been seen as a corruption of Hebrew — a Semitic tongue that is nonetheless not the sacred language — and therefore something the angels decline to convey upward. The *gemara*'s answer is: *shani choleh d'shchina imo* — a sick person is different, because the divine presence (*shechina*) rests directly with the one who is ill. When God is directly present, no angelic intermediary is needed, and therefore prayer in Aramaic is effective. This principle — that Aramaic prayer is permitted where God's presence is immediately manifest — also explains why *Yekum Purkan* is recited in Aramaic, but specifically only in the presence of a *minyan*, since the *gemara* holds that the *shechina* is present wherever ten people gather for prayer.

The *gemara* cites a source: *d'amar Abaye, v'amri lah amar Rabba: minayin she'shchina so'edet hacholeh? She'ne'emar: Hashem yis'adenu al eres devai* — how do we know that God supports the sick? From the verse: God will support him on the bed of languishing.

### Visiting Etiquette

*Hanichnas levaker et hacholeh lo yeshev gabei mita velo gabei kise, ela mi'tatef veyoshev lefanav* — one who visits the sick should not sit on the bed nor on a chair, but should wrap oneself and sit before the patient. The reason: *mipnei she'shechina lemala meroshotav shel choleh* — the divine presence rests above the head of the sick person. Therefore, one must not position oneself at a height above the patient's head, so as not to be elevated above the place where the *shechina* resides.

A second verse-based derivation: *amar Rava v'amri lah amar Rabbi: minayin she'hakadosh baruch hu zan et hacholeh?* — from where do we know that God sustains the sick person? Again from *Hashem yis'adenu al eres devai*, now reading *yis'adenu* not as support but in the sense of *se'udah* — sustenance. Sick people often lose their appetite and struggle to maintain adequate nutrition; this verse suggests that even when eating is reduced, divine presence provides sustenance.

## Reading Candles

### No Exceptions Rule

Returning to the prohibition against reading by candlelight: *amar Rava: afilu gavoah shtei komot* — Rava establishes that even if the candle is very high — two stories up — one may not read by its light. This reflects the principle of *lo plug*: when the Sages enact a restriction, they do not carve out exceptions for every set of circumstances, because doing so invites endless arguments about borderline cases. Even where the concern of *hataya* does not practically apply — because there is no way one could reach to tip such a high candle — the prohibition remains. *Va'afilu shtei mardao'ot* — even if the lamp is raised even higher, on two long poles — the rule still holds.

### Group Exception

However, *chad hu d'lo yikra* — it is an individual who may not read, *ha trei shapir dami* — but perhaps two people may. The *mishnah* uses the singular, and the *gemara* is inclined to read this as meaningful. The reasoning draws on a broader principle: certain rabbinic restrictions against forgetting do not apply when one is in a group, because someone will remind the others. This logic appears elsewhere — for example, in the prohibition against entering a bathhouse on Shabbat, where even with multiple towels one may not go alone, but with a large group the concern is reduced because mutual supervision operates.

### Torch Problem

*Amar Rav Huna: u'vemadura afilu asara bnei adam assur* — if it is a torch rather than an oil lamp, even ten people are forbidden. One might have supposed that a torch cannot be tipped and therefore the concern does not apply. But a torch can be adjusted, angled, moved toward oneself to get more light. More importantly, when ten people are reading by a single torch, each is individually occupied with his own text, separated in his own space — they do not constitute a mutual-reminder group but rather ten individuals, each independently exposed to the temptation of adjusting the flame.

This raises a contemporary question: does any of this apply to electric light? Nowadays, there is generally no concern about adjusting an electric bulb. However, a dimmer switch introduces a related issue: if the light is set low and one is tempted to raise it to read more comfortably, the analogy to *hataya* may be relevant.

### Wealthy Exception

*Amar Rava: im adam chashuv hu mutar* — Rava rules that a person of high social standing may read by candlelight. The reason: a *chashuv* person would never handle his own lamp; he always has an attendant do it for him. Since he never touches candles or lamps himself, there is no realistic concern that he will come to tip it. This is a striking distinction. On one hand, even if the lamp is placed ten stories high, one may not read by it — *lo plug*. Yet if one belongs to a class of people who never personally handles lamps, the prohibition simply does not apply. The *gemara* is apparently unconcerned about the social stratification implicit in this distinction — in a society where such class differences were simply a reality, applying different rules to different classes of people was unproblematic.

### Yishmael Story

*Metivei: lo yikra l'or haner shema yateh. Amar Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha: ani ekra velo ateh* — Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha declared: I will read; I will not come to tip it. He was either sufficiently vigilant about Shabbat or sufficiently *chashuv* that the concern did not seem to apply to him. *Pa'am achat kara u'vikeish l'hatot* — once, while reading, he was about to tip the candle. He caught himself and declared: *kamah gedolim divrei chachamim she'hayu omrim lo yikra l'or haner* — how great are the words of the Sages who said one may not read by candlelight. The parallel to the Solomonic story about multiplying wives — the very king who declared "they will not lead my heart astray," and whose heart was indeed led astray — is apt. No matter how great one believes oneself to be, one must take seriously what *Chazal* know about human nature.

*Rabbi Natan* reports an even more striking version: *kara vehittah* — he actually did tip it. He did not merely come close; he did it. He then wrote in his notebook: *ani Yishmael ben Elisha karisi vehitisi ner b'Shabbat, uchesheyi­baneh Beit Hamikdash avi chatas shmenah* — I, Yishmael ben Elisha, read and tipped a lamp on Shabbat; when the Temple is rebuilt, I will bring a fat *chatat*, for I was *mechalel shabbat b'shogeg*.

### Torah Engagement

Despite this powerful story, the *gemara* does not overturn the principle permitting a *chashuv* person to read by candlelight. *Amar Rabbi Abba: shani Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha, ho'il u'mashim atzmo al divrei Torah k'hedyot* — Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha was different: when he engaged in Torah, he forgot his own status. He immersed himself so completely that he lost track of who he was, and in that state of total absorption he reached to adjust the lamp just as any ordinary person would. For others who remain conscious of their *chashuv* status even while learning — who never forget themselves entirely in Torah — the concern does not arise, and the permission stands.

## Servants Checking

### Vessel Checking

The *gemara* now takes up the case of a *shamash* — a household attendant — checking glasses and dishes to ensure they are clean. One *baraita* permits the *shamash* to perform this inspection by candlelight; another *baraita* prohibits it. These are in apparent contradiction.

### Permanent v. Temp

*Lo kashya: kan b'shamash kavua, kan b'shamash she'eino kavua* — the two *baraitot* address different situations. One concerns a permanent, regular attendant; the other concerns a temporary hire. Rashi and *Tosafot* disagree about which way to read the distinction: according to Rashi, a temporary attendant, having no security in his position, does a careless job and therefore cannot be trusted to inspect properly. A regular attendant, invested in the household, is more careful. *Tosafot* reads it the other way. The essential point is that the *halacha* varies based on the attendant's relationship to the household.

### Oil v. Naphtha

*V'ibait eima, ha v'ha b'shamash kavua, v'lo kashya, ha d'mishcha ha d'nafta* — alternatively, both *baraitot* concern a permanent attendant, and the distinction is between oil and naphtha. Naphtha is a malodorous petroleum substance that no one wants to handle unnecessarily, and therefore a *shamash* using a naphtha lamp would not come to tip it toward himself or the vessels. Oil, by contrast, is something the attendant handles comfortably, and there is a realistic concern he might tip the lamp. Accordingly, checking by an oil lamp is prohibited while checking by a naphtha lamp is permitted.

### Rav Disagreement

*Iba'ya lehu: shamash she'eino kavua v'demishcha mai?* — what about a temporary attendant and an oil lamp? A permanent attendant using oil is prohibited. Is a temporary attendant using oil also prohibited, or does something about his temporary status change the calculus? *Amar Rav: halacha v'ein morin ken* — Rav rules that it is technically permissible (*halacha*), but one should not publicize this ruling (*ein morin ken*), lest it lead people to make errors. *Rav Yirmiya bar Abba* disagrees: *halacha u'morin ken* — one may and should publicize that it is permissible.

### Household Practices

The *gemara* closes with a story that illustrates this dispute in practice. Rav Yirmiya bar Abba — the same one who ruled that one may publicize the leniency — was once a guest at the home of Rav Assi on Shabbat. *Kam shamaya badkei lehu l'or shraga* — Rav Yirmiya's attendant got up and checked the vessels by candlelight. *Amrah leih deviteihu* — Rav Assi's wife said to him: you yourself do not allow our attendant to do this — why is your guest permitting his attendant to do so? *Amar lah: shavkeih, kerabbeih sevira leih* — he told her: leave him alone; he follows the ruling of his teacher. Rav Yirmiya, having ruled that the leniency may be publicized and applied, is acting in accordance with his own *psak*. We in this house are stringent, but he is not to be faulted for following his *rebbe*'s position.
