# Yevamot 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Yossi & Shmuel

### Mishnah: Rabbi Yossi

The daf picks up at the bottom of 95b. The *mishnah* there contains a somewhat cryptic statement attributed to Rabbi Yossi, to the effect that whoever causes a prohibition to fall upon others causes the same prohibition to fall upon himself, and conversely, if no prohibition falls upon others, none falls upon him either. The *gemara* has been explaining this in terms of a case of consistency: a man's wife and his brother-in-law both travel to a distant country (*medinat hayam*), both are reported dead, and it subsequently emerges that both are alive. The man had, in the interim, married his wife's sister — believing his wife to be dead — and his wife's sister had married the brother-in-law, believing her husband to be dead. Rabbi Yossi's principle demands consistency: the question of whether that intermediate marriage is perceived as having been binding or not must be applied uniformly to both parties. Either both will be *muttar* or both will be *assur*, depending on whether that marriage was understood by onlookers as having actually taken effect.

### Shmuel's Ruling

Continuing from this point, the *gemara* records: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, halacha k'Rabbi Yosei* — Rav Yehuda rules in the name of Shmuel that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yossi. However, as the *gemara* will immediately make clear, there are different versions and interpretations of Rabbi Yossi's position, so it is not immediately obvious which formulation Shmuel intends to validate.

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi explains that Shmuel's statement is meant to apply to the entirety of Rabbi Yossi's ruling. The concern animating Rabbi Yossi's principle, according to Rashi, is whether people would perceive the intermediate marriage as having been binding — the same concern that underlies other restrictions, such as prohibiting a woman who remarried after a mistaken report of her husband's death from returning to her first husband, lest people conclude that her first husband had divorced her and she had legitimately remarried. The critical variable, Rashi notes, is whether the original marriage between Reuven and Rachel had been consummated through full *nissuin* or only *kiddushin*. If Rachel and Reuven had only reached the stage of *kiddushin*, it is conceivable that people might have perceived Reuven's subsequent marriage to Leah as valid — reasoning, perhaps, that the *kiddushin* with Rachel had fallen through. If, however, they had completed *nissuin*, no one could plausibly have viewed his marriage to Leah as binding, since a fully married man cannot take his wife's sister. Only if it had been *kiddushin* with Leah would there be no problem whatsoever.

## Rav Yosef Challenges

### Rav Yosef's Challenge

Rav Yosef raises a sharp question on Shmuel's ruling: *Umi amar Shmuel hachi* — did Shmuel really say this? *D'Shmuel l'taamei* — for Shmuel himself is on record elsewhere with a seemingly contradictory position.

### Rav vs. Shmuel Debate

The *gemara* cites a well-known dispute: *Yevamah — Rav amar harei hi k'eishesh ish*, Rav holds that a *yevamah* — a woman awaiting *yibum* or *chalitza* — is to be treated like a married woman. *U'Shmuel amar einah k'eishesh ish*, Shmuel holds she is not treated like a married woman. The bare formulation of this debate leaves its context obscure, and the *gemara* now turns to Rav Huna's interpretation to explain what exactly is at stake.

### Rav Huna's Context

Rav Huna proposes the following as the context of the Rav-Shmuel dispute: a man's brother performed *kiddushin* with a woman and then traveled to a distant country (*medinat hayam*), whereupon it was reported that the brother had died. The surviving brother, believing he was performing *yibum*, proceeded to perform *kiddushin* with the widow — his *yevamah*. Then the brother turned out to be alive. This case differs from a standard case of a woman who remarries after a mistaken report of her husband's death. In that ordinary case, even in hindsight, observers might still believe the second marriage was valid — reasoning, for instance, that the first husband had sent a *get* from afar. Here, however, once the brother emerges alive, it becomes retroactively impossible for anyone to have regarded the second marriage as binding, since a man's sister-in-law is forbidden to him even after divorce so long as the brother lives. In hindsight, no one could have perceived that *kiddushin* as a genuine marriage.

### Kiddushin & Perception

Yet the complication, as Rav Huna's reading highlights, is that the original marriage between the brother and the woman was only *kiddushin* — not full *nissuin*. This creates a potential avenue for people to think that perhaps the first *kiddushin* had not taken full effect, and therefore the second *kiddushin* — performed by the surviving brother thinking he was doing *yibum* — might have been perceived as valid. Rav holds that this concern is live: since the original marriage was only *kiddushin*, people in hindsight might plausibly imagine it had been conditional or defective, making the brother's subsequent *kiddushin* appear binding. Hence the *yevamah* is treated like a married woman, with all the attendant stringencies. 

---

## Shmuel's Leniency

### Gemara Resolves

Shmuel, however, rules *einah k'eishesh ish* — even the fact that the first marriage was only *kiddushin* does not lead us to fear that people will perceive the second act as a genuine marriage. Once the first brother appears alive, it is obvious to all that the surviving brother could never have validly married her, regardless of what stage the first marriage had reached. The *gemara* now returns to Rav Yosef's challenge: if Shmuel is lenient even when the first marriage was only *kiddushin* in the case of the *yevamah* and her brother-in-law, how can Shmuel also rule like Rabbi Yossi, who appears to impose a stringency specifically when the first marriage was only *kiddushin*?

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye presses the question further: *Memai d'chi amar Shmuel halacha k'Rabbi Yosei, ad Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha ka'amar* — how do we know which version of Rabbi Yossi Shmuel intended to validate? Perhaps when Shmuel said *halacha k'Rabbi Yosei*, he meant to endorse only the interpretation of Rabbi Ami, not that of Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha. And even granting that he meant Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha's version, perhaps he was endorsing only Rabbi Yossi's lenient ruling — not the stringent case involving *kiddushin*. That is: perhaps Shmuel's declaration that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yossi was intended to convey that, just as the husband may return to his original wife Rachel, so too Leah may return to her original husband Barak — in all cases, *l'kula*. Perhaps Shmuel was never endorsing any stringency at all.

### Hindsight Principle

The underlying logic that the *gemara* is developing is essential to appreciate in full. The usual stringencies applied to a woman who remarries after a mistaken report of her husband's death — which function not merely as a punitive fine but as a consequence of the fact that her union with the second man genuinely appeared, to observers, to be a valid marriage — depend on a factual predicate: that people actually could have perceived the second marriage as binding. There are cases, however, in which that predicate fails. When it subsequently emerges that the first husband's brother is alive, or that the first husband's wife's sister is alive, no one in hindsight could conceivably have viewed the second union as a legitimate marriage. The bond of the *yevamah* relationship, or the prohibition of marrying one's wife's sister, is absolute and applies even post-divorce. In hindsight, then, it is impossible that anyone would have perceived the intermediate marriage as valid, and therefore the entire foundation for the stringencies collapses.

### Special Cases

This insight applies distinctly in the cases of wife's sister and husband's brother. In an ordinary case of a husband returning alive after being reported dead, observers could still in hindsight theorize that the husband had sent a *get* from *medinat hayam*, and so the second marriage still appears potentially legitimate even after the fact. But in the special cases at issue here — where it is the wife's sister or the brother who reappears alive — such a theory is impossible, since the prohibition on marrying one's wife's sister, or a woman to her husband's brother, applies even after divorce and even after *get*. Hindsight therefore conclusively forecloses any perception of validity. And the question on the table reduces to this: can we be lenient even when the first marriage was only *kiddushin*, since even then people would in hindsight realize that the second union could not have been binding? Shmuel, on this reading, answers yes — even *kiddushin* at the beginning does not give us reason to fear that the second marriage was perceived as valid, and leniency applies across the board.

## Alt. Interpretations

### Rav Huna's Reading

The *gemara* then considers whether Rav Huna's framing of the Rav-Shmuel debate is even the correct one. *Dilma leita l'd'Rav Huna klal* — perhaps Rav Huna's interpretation is entirely off the mark. Perhaps the debate between Rav and Shmuel over whether a *yevamah* is *k'eishesh ish* concerns something altogether different.

### Zina Case

One alternative: perhaps the debate concerns the ruling of Rav Hamnuna, who teaches that *shomeret yavam she'zinta asura l'yivama* — a woman awaiting *yibum* who engages in illicit relations (*zina*) with another man becomes forbidden to her brother-in-law. On this reading, Rav holds that a *yevamah* is sufficiently like a married woman that her *zina* disqualifies her from *yibum*, while Shmuel holds she is not treated as a married woman and therefore is not disqualified.

### Kiddushin & Yevama

A third possibility: the debate between Rav and Shmuel concerns whether *kiddushin* from another man can take effect with a *yevamah* while she still awaits *yibum*. Rav holds that her *zika* to the brother-in-law renders her sufficiently like a married woman that *kiddushin* from an outside man cannot bind her. Shmuel holds she is not treated as a married woman, and therefore *kiddushin* from another man would be effective.

### One Debate, Multiple Fra…

The *gemara* notes that Rav and Shmuel are recorded as having debated the *yevamah*'s status using various formulations on multiple occasions, and raises the question of why the same dispute would be articulated twice. The answer: *chada miklal chaverta itmar* — the debate was actually stated only once, and the second formulation was derived inferentially from the first. The two framings are not independent disputes but different expressions of a single underlying *machlokes*. The last two cases — whether *zina* disqualifies and whether outside *kiddushin* can bind — fit together especially naturally, since both ask how thoroughly the *yevamah*'s bond to her brother-in-law resembles the bond of marriage.

The larger *chiddush* emerging from the entire extended discussion is worth pausing to appreciate. While the question of whether a husband can return to his wife after he mistakenly married her sister is relatively straightforward, the more striking case is whether the woman herself — having remarried, even if only on account of a mistaken report of her husband's death — can return to her first husband. The *gemara* has been exploring the narrow set of circumstances in which hindsight removes the very foundation of the stringencies: cases of wife's sister or husband's brother, where the reappearance of the "dead" party conclusively demonstrates, for all observers, that the intermediate marriage never could have been valid.

## Sequential Sisters

### Three Wives Setup

The *mishnah* now presents a striking scenario. A man is told that his wife died; he marries her sister from their shared father (that is, a paternal half-sister of his first wife). He is then told that the second wife died; he marries her sister from their shared mother (a maternal half-sister of the second). And so on: each wife is reported dead, he marries the next, and ultimately all of them turn out to be alive. The women form a chain: wives one and two share a father but not a mother; wives two and three share a mother but not a father; and so the chain continues through a fifth wife.

### Ruling on Validity

The *mishnah* rules: he may remain married to wives one, three, and five; he is forbidden to wives two and four. The logic follows directly. Wife one is a valid marriage. When he married wife two — who is the paternal sister of wife one — his first wife being still alive meant the marriage to wife two was invalid: one may not marry one's wife's sister. Wife three, however, shares no prohibited relationship with wife one (she is related only to wife two, through their mother), so the marriage to wife three was valid. Wife four, the sister of wife three through their mother, was therefore an invalid marriage. Wife five, bearing no prohibited relationship to wife three, was valid.

### Yibum Consequences

Since wives one, three, and five are his legitimate wives, they and their *tzaros* (co-wives) interact normally for purposes of *yibum*. Should he die, an act of *yibum* or *chalitza* with one of the valid wives would exempt the co-wives as well. Wives two and four, however, are not his wives, and an act with them by a brother would carry no exempting power.

### First Wife Truly Dead

The *mishnah* adds a case: what if the report about the first wife's death was actually true — she genuinely died? In that case, his marriage to wife two was valid (his first wife being truly dead, the marriage to her sister was permitted). Wife three, then, is the sister of his living wife two and is therefore forbidden to him. The pattern inverts: he is married to wives two and four and forbidden to wives one (dead), three, and five. And correspondingly, valid acts of *yibum* with wives two and four would exempt their *tzaros*, while acts involving wives three and five would not.

## Minor & Yibum

### Nine-Year-Old Status

The *mishnah* now transitions back squarely to the laws of *yibum*, introducing the case of a surviving brother who is nine years old and one day. At this age, an act of sexual intercourse (*bi'ah*) is legally cognizable — it is treated as a genuine act of *bi'ah* — yet because the boy is not yet an adult, it cannot constitute a fully consummated act of *yibum*. The question is what legal consequences flow from such an act.

### Disqualification Power

The *mishnah* rules: *Ben tesha shanim veyom echad hu posel al yedei achim* — the minor's *bi'ah* with the *yevamah* disqualifies the adult brothers from performing *yibum* with her thereafter. This act is treated as analogous to *maamar* — one of the quasi-acts that, while not completing *yibum*, is nevertheless legally consequential enough to remove others from the picture. Conversely, *ve'achim poslim al yado* — if the adult brothers act first, whether by *bi'ah*, *chalitza*, *maamar*, or *get*, that act will disqualify the minor from later completing a full act of *yibum* when he comes of age. The *mishnah* then specifies the asymmetry: *ela hu posel techilla ve'achim poslim techilla ve'sof* — his acts disqualify only if he goes first, whereas the brothers' acts disqualify him whether they act first or second.

### Maamar Question

The *gemara* pauses on a question implicit in this ruling: does a minor actually have a legally cognizable *maamar* at all? The *mishnah*, having established that the minor's *bi'ah* functions like *maamar* of an adult, and having then suggested that even other acts by the minor (such as *maamar* itself) are meaningful if performed first — prompts the question of whether the *mishnah* has now overstated the minor's legal capacity.

---

## Bia vs. Maamar

### Baraita on Bia

A *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Oshaya poses a direct challenge. The *baraita* teaches: if an adult brother performed *maamar* with the *yevamah*, and thereafter a minor brother — nine years old and one day — had *bi'ah* with her, that *bi'ah* disqualifies her to the first (adult) brother, even though the minor came second. This appears to contradict the *mishnah*, which stated that the minor's acts are only effective when he acts first.

### Mishnah Reinterpreted

To resolve the contradiction, the *gemara* proposes a revised reading of the *mishnah*: *bi'ah paslah afilu besof* — the minor's act of *bi'ah* disqualifies even when it comes second. The *mishnah*'s statement that the minor only creates a problem at the beginning (*techilla*) must therefore be referring not to *bi'ah* but to lesser acts such as *maamar* — quasi-acts that are effective only if the minor acts first. *Maamar techilla posel, besof lo posel* — *maamar* by the minor disqualifies only if it precedes the brothers' acts; *bi'ah* by the minor disqualifies regardless of order.

This is admittedly a strained reading of the *mishnah*, which appears to use the example of *bi'ah* when saying the minor is effective only first. But the *gemara* accepts this reinterpretation in order to maintain consistency with the *baraita*.

### Gemara Probes: Does a Minor Even Have Maamar or Get?

Having now said that the minor's *bi'ah* is powerful even at the end, while *maamar* is only effective at the beginning, the *gemara* probes further: does a minor have a legally recognized *maamar* at all? And does he have a legally recognized *get*? This question challenges the newly expanded picture of the minor's legal capacities.

### Bia Always Works

A *baraita* addresses the matter directly: *ben tesha shanim veyom echad hu posel bedavar echad, ve'achim poslin al yado bearba'ah devarim* — the minor can disqualify the brothers with one act, while the brothers can disqualify him with four. *Hu posel al yedei achim bevia* — his disqualifying act is exclusively *bi'ah*. *Ve'achim poslin al yado bevia, be'maamar, beget, uvechalitza* — the brothers can disqualify him through any of the four acts: *bi'ah*, *maamar*, *get*, or *chalitza*. This *baraita* seems to say that the minor has only *bi'ah*, not *maamar* or *get*. The *gemara* responds: the *baraita* lists only those acts that are always effective — *bi'ah* for the minor is always effective regardless of order, and therefore it is listed as his single act. *Maamar*, which is only effective when it comes first and not when it comes later, is not listed — not because it is meaningless, but because it is not *pesika*, not uniformly reliable. The *gemara* has thus, through this chain of reasoning, arrived at a richer account of the minor's legal capacities than either the *mishnah* or the *baraita* alone would suggest: *bi'ah* is always effective; *maamar* (and potentially other acts) are effective when performed first.

## Minor's Four Acts

### Minor Has Get

Additional *amoraic* statements now confirm and extend this picture. Rav Yehuda rules in the name of Shmuel: *yesh lo get* — the minor does have a legally meaningful *get*.

### Minor Has Maamar

Similarly, Rav Tachlifa bar Avimi rules: *yesh lo maamar* — the minor has a legally meaningful *maamar* as well.

### Rabbi Meir's View

A *baraita* then records that these positions align with the view of Rabbi Meir: *yesh lo get, yesh lo maamar, divrei Rabbi Meir*. The *gemara* then probes Rabbi Meir's position more carefully. The *baraita* had earlier taught: *asu biat ben tesha k'maamar begadol* — the Sages equated the minor's act of *bi'ah* with the *maamar* of an adult. The analogy is precise: just as an adult's *maamar* does not fully consummate *yibum* but does disqualify the other brothers, so too the minor's *bi'ah* does not fully consummate *yibum* but does disqualify the other brothers. Rabbi Meir then adds: *asu chalitzat ben tesha k'get begadol* — the Sages equated the minor's *chalitza* with an adult's *get*. One might ask: if Rabbi Meir also holds that the minor has a *get* of his own, why would one need to compare his *chalitza* to an adult's *get*? Why not compare it to his own *get*? The implication seems to be that the minor's *get*, while extant, is weaker than an adult's *get* — and therefore the comparison is made to the adult's *get*, to convey that the minor's *chalitza* rises to the level of an adult's *get*, even if the minor's own *get* does not.

### Chalitza Equals Get

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua resolves this: *ait lei vezutar* — the minor does have a *get*, but it is weaker than an adult's *get*. The relevance of this distinction becomes clear in the context of Rabban Gamliel's ruling that a second *get* after a first *get* carries no legal effect (*ein get achar get*). According to Rabban Gamliel, this rule applies symmetrically — adult after adult, minor after minor — but an adult's *get* following a minor's *get* would be effective, since they are not equivalent. Conversely, among the Rabbis who hold that a second *get* after a first *get* is effective, this too applies symmetrically — adult after adult, minor after minor — but a minor's *get* after an adult's is not effective, since the minor's act is weaker.

### Acts Correspondence

The underlying logic can now be articulated cleanly. *Bi'ah* and *chalitza* are the two biblical acts pertaining to *yibum*; *maamar* and *get* are their rabbinic counterparts. For a minor, the biblical acts — when performed by the minor — carry the legal weight of the corresponding rabbinic acts performed by an adult. Hence, the minor's *bi'ah* is equated to an adult's *maamar*, and the minor's *chalitza* is equated to an adult's *get*. This is what it means to say that a minor's *chalitza* is like an adult's *get*: it is not comparing his *chalitza* to his own *get* (which would be weaker), but to the full strength of an adult's *get*. The logic preserves the parallel structure of biblical and rabbinic acts while accounting for the diminished legal capacity of the minor.

## Second Minor's Bia

### Second Minor Question

The *mishnah* now addresses a further case: if a first minor has *bi'ah* with the *yevamah*, and then a second minor — also nine years old and one day — likewise has *bi'ah* with her, does the second minor's act disqualify the first? The question is whether the logic that allows one minor's *bi'ah* to disqualify an adult brother (as established above) applies equally when both parties are minors.

### Self-Disqualification

A related case: the same minor who had *bi'ah* with the *yevamah* then also has *bi'ah* with her *tzarah* (co-wife). If his first act was quasi-effective (analogous to *maamar*), then his second act with the co-wife should, by the same logic, disqualify himself from completing *yibum* with the first — since one can build only one household (*bayit echad hu boneh*) and cannot do *yibum* with two wives. The *mishnah* indicates that in this case, *posel al yedei atzmo* — he disqualifies himself.

### Rabbi Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with both cases. His position, explained in the *baraita*, is: *im biah rishonah biah, biah sheniyah einah biah* — if the first act was a full, binding *bi'ah*, then the second act is simply not a *bi'ah* at all (since the matter is already settled). And *v'im biah rishonah einah biah, biah sheniyah nami einah biah* — if the first act was not a full, binding *bi'ah*, then the second act is likewise not a *bi'ah*. Rabbi Shimon rejects the intermediate, fifty-percent position entirely. For him, the minor's act is either one hundred percent effective or zero percent effective — there is no quasi-status.

### All-or-Nothing Logic

This all-or-nothing position has decisive implications. When two minors each perform *bi'ah*, or when the same minor performs *bi'ah* with both co-wives, Rabbi Shimon reasons that the second act cannot do anything the first act did not already do. If the first act was fully effective, it is already settled and the second act is irrelevant. If the first act was not effective, the second act is equally ineffective — one cannot accumulate two zero-percent acts into a meaningful result. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon rules that neither a second minor's *bi'ah* nor the same minor's *bi'ah* with the co-wife creates any additional disqualification.

The *gemara* notes: *matnitin de'lo k'Ben Azzai* — the *mishnah*'s ruling (against Rabbi Shimon) is not in accordance with Ben Azzai. Ben Azzai teaches that *maamar* after *maamar* by two different brothers (*yevamim*) is effective — the second brother's *maamar* disqualifies the first — but *maamar* after *maamar* by the same brother with two different *yevamot* is not effective: having done *maamar* with one co-wife does not disqualify him from the other. Translating to the present case: the minor's *bi'ah* with one wife followed by *bi'ah* with the co-wife should, according to Ben Azzai's logic, not disqualify him from the first wife. The *mishnah*'s ruling that he does disqualify himself therefore runs contrary to Ben Azzai's principle.

## Minor Dies & Yevama

### Complex Case Setup

The next *mishnah* presents a more intricate scenario. The minor — nine years old and one day — performs *bi'ah* with his *yevamah*. Thereafter, once he has grown to adulthood, he marries another woman. He then dies without having consummated *yibum* with the *yevamah* as an adult (he had only the act as a minor, which was equivalent to *maamar*, not full *yibum*).

### Zikat Shnei Yevamim

This creates the unusual situation of *zikat shnei yevamim* — a dual *zika* (brother-in-law connection) encumbering the first woman. The surviving brothers are bound to the *yevamah* through two separate obligations: the original biblical obligation arising from her first husband (say, Er), and a rabbinic obligation arising from the minor's quasi-*maamar*-level *bi'ah* (say, Onan). These two *zikot* are of different legal grades — one *d'oraisa*, one *d'rabbanan* — and they coexist because the minor's act, being only rabbinic in weight, never displaced the underlying biblical *zika* of the first husband. This situation would never arise when a full adult performs *yibum*, since a fully consummated act of *yibum* is entirely *d'oraisa* and completely absorbs and resolves the original *zika*. It is the uniquely intermediate character of the minor's *bi'ah* that creates this dual-*zika* situation.

The surviving brother (Shelah, in this illustration) therefore faces obligations to two women: the *yevamah* from the first husband (Tamar, from Er), carrying *zikat shnei yevamim*, and the second wife the minor married as an adult (say, Devorah), to whom he has a straightforward *d'oraisa yibum* obligation.

### First Woman Chalitza

The *mishnah* rules: *harishona choletzet v'lo mityabemet* — with respect to the first woman (who carries the dual *zika*), the surviving brother may only perform *chalitza*, not *yibum*. This is because her status is complicated by the dual obligation: she stands in the *yibum* relationship to him both through the biblical *zika* of her original husband and through the rabbinic *zika* of the minor. Performing *yibum* with a woman in this doubly encumbered state is not permissible; only *chalitza* will suffice. *Ve'shniya o choletzet o mityabemet* — with the second wife (who has a straightforward *yibum* obligation), the brother may choose either *yibum* or *chalitza*.

### Rabbi Shimon's Option

Rabbi Shimon, consistent with his all-or-nothing position, rules differently: *miyabem eizo sheyirtze, v'choletz lashniya* — he may perform *yibum* with whichever woman he chooses and then *chalitza* with the other. According to Rabbi Shimon, the minor's act is either fully effective or entirely null. If it was null, then Tamar stands only in the *zika* of Er, and there is no complicating dual encumbrance; she is no different from any other *yevamah*. If the minor's act was fully effective, then Tamar was fully Onan's wife, and the surviving brothers face two distinct and independent *yibum* obligations — one per wife — and must perform *yibum* with one and *chalitza* with the other, but there is no reason *yibum* cannot be done with either. The one thing Rabbi Shimon concedes is that *yibum* cannot be performed with both women, since it is possible that the minor's act was fully effective (meaning Onan had two full wives), and one may perform *yibum* with only one of them. Therefore he must do *yibum* with one and *chalitza* with the other — but he has free choice as to which.

## Adult Without Puberty

### Pubic Hair Requirement

The *mishnah* closes with a brief but significant ruling: *echad shehu ben tesha shanim veyom echad, v'echad shehu ben esrim shelo hevi shtei sa'arot* — both the nine-year-old and a twenty-year-old who has not yet developed two pubic hairs are governed by the same *halacha*. The physiological sign of adulthood — the presence of two pubic hairs — is the operative criterion, not chronological age alone. An individual who has reached twenty years of age but lacks this sign of physical maturity retains the same *halachic* status as a minor of nine years and one day, with all the attendant consequences for the laws of *yibum* discussed throughout this *daf*.
