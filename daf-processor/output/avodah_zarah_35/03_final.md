# Avodah Zarah 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Cheese Mishnah Review

### Context & Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שָׁאַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אֶת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָסְרוּ גְּבִינַת עַכּוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקֵיבַת נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The mishna teaches: Rabbi Yehuda said: Rabbi Yishmael asked Rabbi Yehoshua on the road, saying to him: For what reason did the Sages prohibit the cheese of non-Jews? He said to him: Because they curdle it with the stomach contents of an unslaughtered animal carcass.**

Daf 35 opens with an abundance of practically relevant material — non-Jewish cheese, milk, bread, and a range of other foods — but before engaging the *gemara* directly, it is necessary to review a *mishnah* from 29b, which the *gemara* on our daf references repeatedly. Without familiarity with that *mishnah*, the subsequent discussion would be difficult to follow.

### R. Yishmael vs R. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא קֵיבַת עוֹלָה חֲמוּרָה מִקֵּיבַת נְבֵלָה, וְהִנָּךְ קוֹהֲנִים שְׁדַאְתְּהוֹן יָפָה, אוֹכְלִין לָהּ סוֹרְפָהּ חַיָּה! וְהִנָּךְ קוֹהֲנִים שְׁדַאְתְּהוֹן לָא יָפַת, לָא מִיתְחַיְּיבִין עַל כׇּל עָלְמָא, וְעוֹד: שׁוֹתְקִין מִן הַמִּשְׁמָר וְאֵין בִּילּוּל, אִם הַנְּבֵלָה לָא אִתְּרַחִיץ בַּמַּיִם — הָא נִשְׁחַט הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: But is not the stomach of a burnt offering more stringent than the stomach of an animal carcass? And yet those priests whose desire is good eat it directly, roasting it alive. And those priests whose desire is not good are not liable in any respect. Moreover:** those priests are silent about the watch, there is no mixture, and if the animal carcass was not washed in water, it was slaughtered.

The *mishnah* records a conversation attributed to Rabbi Yehuda, in which Rabbi Yishmael questioned Rabbi Yehoshua while they were walking together: *mipnei mah asru gevinat ovdei kochavim* — why did the sages forbid non-Jewish cheese? Rabbi Yehoshua's initial answer was *mipnei shema'amidin otah bekeivat neveilah* — because non-Jews coagulate the cheese using stomach gunk from animals that were not slaughtered through proper *shechitah*, rendering the material *neveilah*.

### Olah vs Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, לָמָּה לֹא אֲסָרוּהָ בַּהֲנָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: If so, why didn't they prohibit it from deriving benefit?**

Rabbi Yishmael immediately challenges this reasoning. He notes that the stomach gunk of an *olah* sacrifice is even more stringent than that of a *neveilah*, and yet we know that a *kohen sheda'ato yafah* — a priest who does not mind — may eat it directly, *sorfah chayyah*, without any further preparation. Moreover, the rabbis ruled *ein nehenin velo mo'alin* — one neither derives benefit from it nor violates the laws of *me'ilah* — indicating that the stomach gunk of the *olah* does not carry a full Torah-level prohibition. The upshot of this exchange is that eating stomach gunk of a *neveilah* would not itself be the real concern, thereby undermining Rabbi Yehoshua's first explanation.

### Idol Worship Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקֵיבַת עֶגְלֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, לָמָּה לֹא אֲסָרוּהָ בַּהֲנָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: Because they curdle it with the stomach contents of calves dedicated to idol worship. He said to him: If so, why didn't they prohibit it from deriving benefit?**

Forced to offer an alternative, Rabbi Yehoshua provides a second reason: non-Jews coagulate cheese using stomach gunk from animals that were dedicated to idol worship — *bekeivat eglei avodah kochavim*. Rabbi Yishmael responds sharply: if that is the case, then such cheese should be forbidden not merely to eat but also as an *issur hana'ah* — forbidden to derive any benefit from at all — since that is the standard rule for objects of idol worship. Rabbi Yehoshua has no answer.

### Closure & Diversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשִּׂיאוֹ לְדָבָר אַחֵר. אָמַר לוֹ: כִּי תִקְרִי "כִּי טוֹבִים דֹּדֶיךָ מִיָּיִן", אוֹ "כִּי טוֹבוֹ דוֹדֶיךָ מִיָּיִן"? אָמַר לוֹ: כִּי טוֹבִים, דִּכְתִיב: "לְרֵיחַ שְׁמָנֶיךָ טוֹבִים".
>
> **Translation:** **He diverted him to another matter. He said to him: How do you read "For your love [*dodekha*] is better than wine" — in the masculine or the feminine? He said to him: In the masculine, for it is written: "For the fragrance of your oils is good."**

At this point, *vehisio ledavar acher* — he changes the subject entirely. Rabbi Yehoshua pivots to a question about *Shir HaShirim*: how do you read the verse, *ki tovim dodecha miyayin* — "for your loves are better than wine" — in the masculine form, *dodecha*, or the feminine, *dodaich*? Rabbi Yishmael answers that it should be read in the masculine, since the parallel phrase *lere'ach shemanecha tovim* — "for the fragrance of your oils is good" — uses the masculine form. The apparent randomness of this digression will be explained shortly.

## Peresh Rulings

### Stoned Ox vs Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָדְבוּי אָמַר רַב: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּפֶרֶשׁ שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל — מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, בְּפֶרֶשׁ עֶגְלֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥadevoi says that Rav says: One who betroths a woman with the excrement of an ox that is to be stoned, she is betrothed. One who betroths with the excrement of calves used for idol worship, she is not betrothed.**

The *gemara* now opens with a statement from Rav Achdavoy: one who betroths a woman using the *peresh* — the excrement or stomach gunk — of a *shor haniskal*, an ox that was stoned to death for having gored a person, the betrothal is valid (*mekudeshet*). However, one who betroths using the *peresh* of an animal used for idol worship — *eglei avodat kochavim* — the betrothal is not valid (*einah mekudeshet*). The *gemara* proceeds to explain the distinction between these two rulings in two different ways.

### Logical Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא: גַּבֵּי עֶגְלֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה נִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּנִפְחֵיהּ, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּנִפְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, propose logical reasoning: With regard to calves for idol worship, their additional girth is satisfactory for it, but with regard to an ox that is to be stoned, its additional girth is not satisfactory for it.**

The first approach is through *sevara*, logical reasoning. In the case of an animal dedicated to idol worship, it was in fact beneficial — *nichelei benafchei* — for the animal to have this gunk inside it, since a fatter, larger-looking animal was considered more impressive for purposes of idol worship. Because the gunk serves a purpose and benefits the animal's presentation, it is considered part and parcel of the prohibited animal, and is itself forbidden. By contrast, in the case of the *shor haniskal*, there is no benefit in having the gunk inside the ox — *lo nichelei benafchei* — and therefore it retains an independent status that is not subsumed under the prohibition of the animal.

### Textual Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא: כְּתִיב הָכָא: "לָא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה", וּכְתִיב הָתָם: "סָקוֹל יִסָּקֵל הַשּׁוֹר וְלֹא יֵאָכֵל אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ", בְּשָׂרוֹ אָסוּר, הָא פִּרְשׁוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, cite a verse: It is written here: "And there shall cleave naught of the dedicated thing to your hand," and it is written there: "The ox shall be stoned, and its flesh shall not be eaten." Its flesh is forbidden, but its excrement is permitted.**

The second approach is through *kra*, derivation from verses. Regarding idol worship, the verse states *lo tidbak beyadcha me'uma min hacherem* — nothing shall cling to your hand from that which is *cherem*, consecrated to idolatry. The word *me'uma*, "anything at all," implies by extension even the *peresh*. Regarding the *shor haniskal*, the verse states *sakol yisakel v'hashor lo ye'achel et besaro* — the ox shall be stoned and its flesh shall not be eaten. The verse specifies *besaro*, its flesh — which implies *ha pirsho mutar*, its excrement is permitted.

### Rava's Integration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תַּרְוַיְיהוּ תְּנַנְהִי, מִדְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין בְּקֵיבַת נְבֵילָה, וְקָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ: וַהֲלֹא קֵיבַת עוֹלָה חֲמוּרָה מִקֵּיבַת נְבֵילָה. מִכְּלָל דְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה שָׁרוּ פִּרְשַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We learned both of these from the mishna. From the fact that he said to him: Because they curdle it with the stomach of a carcass, and he responded to him: But is not the stomach of a burnt offering more stringent than the stomach of a carcass? By inference, the excrement of items from which benefit is prohibited is permitted.**

Rava makes the striking observation that both of these conclusions — *tarveihu teni hi* — can in fact be derived from the very *mishnah* we reviewed at the beginning. The first derivation: the *mishnah* records that Rabbi Yehoshua initially said the prohibition on cheese was because the cheese was made with stomach gunk from a *neveilah*, and Rabbi Yishmael responded that stomach gunk from an *olah* offering — which is itself an *issur hana'ah* — is nevertheless permitted for a *kohen* to eat. This exchange implies by inference that objects which are themselves forbidden for benefit (*isurei hana'ah*) nevertheless have their *peresh* permitted. The second derivation: when Rabbi Yehoshua gave his alternative answer — that the cheese is made with stomach gunk from idol-worshipped animals — and Rabbi Yishmael countered that it should therefore be *asur behana'ah*, this implies that the *peresh* of an idol-worshipped animal is indeed forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקֵיבַת עֶגְלֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְקָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן לָמָּה לֹא אֲסָרוּהָ בַּהֲנָאָה — מִכְּלָל דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֲסִיר פִּרְשַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And from the fact that he said to him: Because they curdle it with the stomach of calves of idol worship, and he responded to him: If so, why didn't they prohibit it from deriving benefit — by inference, the excrement of idol worship is prohibited.**

The *gemara* then raises an interesting question. Given Rabbi Yishmael's challenge — that idol-worshipped animal gunk should be forbidden for benefit — why could Rabbi Yehoshua not have simply responded that there is no *issur* at all, since *leita l'issura be'einei*, the prohibition is no longer intact? After all, we find in other contexts (the case of *muryas*, a fish brine to which wine or vinegar is added in small amounts) that *rabbanan* did not forbid it for benefit precisely because the prohibited substance had lost its independent identity by being absorbed.

### Cheese Form Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרָא בְּעֵינֵיהּ! דְּהָא מוּרְיָיס, לְרַבָּנַן דְּלָא אַסְרוּהּ בַּהֲנָאָה, מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרָא בְּעֵינֵיהּ? אָמְרִי: הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאוֹקֹמֵיה קָא מוֹקֵים, חֲשִׁיב לֵיהּ כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But let him respond to him due to the fact that there is no prohibited entity in it! After all, fish stew: according to the Rabbis, who did not prohibit it from deriving benefit, what is the reason? Is it not due to the fact that there is no prohibited entity in it? They said: Here, since the stomach curdles it, it is considered as if it contains a prohibited entity in it.**

The *gemara* answers that cheese is categorically different. Since the stomach gunk is the very substance that gives form and structure to the cheese — *keivan de'okmei ka mokim* — it has sufficient significance to be considered as though it remains fully present and intact. Therefore, Rabbi Yehoshua could not have deflected with that argument; the *issur* is treated as though it is still *be'einei*, still extant, by virtue of the coagulating function it performs.

## Shir HaShirim Diversion

### Oil Verse Chosen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, עֲרֵיבִים עָלַי דִּבְרֵי דוֹדֶיךָ יוֹתֵר מִיֵּינָהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came, he said: The congregation of Israel said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, the statements of Your beloved ones are more pleasant to me than the wine of Torah.**

We now return to the puzzle of why Rabbi Yehoshua specifically chose the verse *lere'ach shemanecha tovim* — "the fragrance of your oils is good" — as his subject-changing distraction. When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that *Knesset Yisrael* said before the Holy One: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, the words of your beloved ones — meaning the sages — are more beloved to me than the wine of Torah itself: *arevim alai divrei dodecha yoter mi-yeinah shel Torah*.

### Sages Over Torah

This is a remarkable statement. The words of the sages — the Oral Torah, the elaborations and interpretations of the rabbis — are described as more pleasant than the wine of the written Torah itself. It is the oral tradition that is being celebrated, its capacity to extend and illuminate, and the verse from *Shir HaShirim* becomes a vehicle for that celebration.

### Kiss Verse Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא הַאי קְרָא דְּשַׁיְילֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר אַמֵּי: מֵרֵישֵׁיהּ דִּקְרָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, "יִשָּׁקֵנִי מִנְּשִׁיקוֹת פִּיהוּ". אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָחִי, חֲשׁוֹק שִׂפְתוֹתֶיךָ זוֹ בָּזוֹ וְאַל תִּבָּהֵל לְהָשִׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** **What is different about this verse that he asked him? Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said, and some say Rabbi Shimon bar Ami said: From the beginning of the verse he said to him: "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth." He said to him: Yishmael, my brother, press your lips one to the other, and do not be so hasty to retort.**

One might ask: why was this particular verse chosen as the distraction, of all the verses in *Shir HaShirim*? Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi — and some say Rabbi Shimon bar Ami — answers that the choice was dictated not by this part of the verse but by its very opening: *yishakeni mi-neshikot pihu* — "let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth." Rabbi Yehoshua was using this verse to send a message to Rabbi Yishmael: *Yishmael achi, chashok siftotecha zo be-zo ve-al tivahel le-hashiv* — Yishmael, my brother, clamp your lips together and do not be so quick to respond. The citation of *Shir HaShirim* was a pointed, if poetic, request to stop asking so many questions.

## Decree & Secrecy

### New Decree Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר עוּלָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא: גְּזֵרָה חֲדָשָׁה הִיא, וְאֵין מְפַקְפְּקִין בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason? Ulla says, and some say Rav Shmuel bar Abba says: It was a new decree, and one does not scrutinize it.**

This raises a further question: why did Rabbi Yehoshua not simply say he had no answer, rather than changing the subject entirely? Ulla — and some say Rav Shmuel bar Abba — explains: *gezeirah chadashah hi ve-ein mefakpekin bah* — this was a new decree, and one does not interrogate new decrees. When a new law is issued, one does not publicly challenge or undermine it, even if a compelling logical objection could be raised.

### Snake Venom Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי גְּזֵירְתָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מִשּׁוּם נִיקּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **What was the decree? Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi says that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: It was due to puncturing.**

What, then, was the actual substance of this decree? Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *mishum nikur* — it was because of snake venom. The concern was that non-Jews were not careful about leaving their cheese exposed, and snakes could come and inject venom into it, rendering the cheese dangerous to eat. It is a striking reason, and the question naturally arises: if that was the reason, why was it not simply stated?

### Secrecy Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם נִיקּוּר! כִּדְעוּלָּא, דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: כִּי גָּזְרִי גְּזֵירְתָּא בְּמַעְרְבָא לָא מְגַלּוּ טַעְמָא עַד תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, דִּלְמָא אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּלָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ וְאָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But let him say to him: It is due to puncturing! In accordance with the statement of Ulla, as Ulla said: When they decreed a decree in the West, they did not reveal the reason until twelve months of the year, lest there be a person who does not agree with it and will come to treat it with contempt.**

The answer is provided by Ulla himself. When a new decree was issued in *Eretz Yisrael*, *lo megalu ta'ama ad treisar yarchei shata* — they did not reveal its reason for twelve months. The concern was that *dilma ika inish d'lo sevira leh*, perhaps there would be someone who disagreed with the reasoning, and he would come to treat the decree lightly — *atei l'zilzulei bah*. By keeping the reason secret for a full year, the practice would become established before anyone had the opportunity to argue against it. Only once people were already observing the law would the reason be disclosed. It is a telling insight into the politics of communal legislation.

## Yirmiya Challenge

### Aged Cheese Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְגַדֵּף בַּהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, יְבֵשָׁה תִּשְׁתְּרֵי, יָשָׁן תִּשְׁתְּרֵי! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: יָבֵשׁ מוּתָּר, אֵין מַנִּיחוֹ לְיַבֵּשׁ, יָשָׁן מוּתָּר, אֵין מַנִּיחוֹ לְיַשֵּׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmiya ridicules it: If so, dry cheese should be permitted, and aged cheese should be permitted! As Rabbi Ḥanina says: Dry is permitted, for venom does not allow it to dry; aged is permitted, for venom does not allow it to age.**

Rabbi Yirmiya is dismissive of the snake venom theory — the *gemara* says *megadef bah*, he mocks it. His challenge: if the concern is truly snake venom, then dried cheese should be permitted, and aged cheese should be permitted. Why? Because Rabbi Chanina rules in the context of *gilluy* — the broader laws of exposed liquids — that dried and aged substances are not subject to the snake venom concern: *yavesh mutar ein manichin l'yavesh, yashan mutar ein manicho l'yashan*. The venom does not allow substances to dry out or age; therefore, if a substance is dry or aged, one knows it could not contain active venom. Since non-Jewish cheese is not conditionally permitted when it is aged or dried, snake venom cannot be the actual reason for the prohibition.

### Milk Residue Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לָהּ בְּלֹא צִחְצוּחֵי חָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Because it is not possible for it without particles of milk.**

Rabbi Chanina therefore offers a different explanation: *lefi she'i efshar lah belo tzichtzuchei chalav* — because it is impossible to make cheese without leaving residual traces of milk in the final product. The concern is that the milk used by non-Jews may have included *treif* milk mixed in with otherwise permissible milk, and those residual traces of non-kosher milk remain present in the cheese.

### Stomach Lining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּעוֹר קֵיבַת נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: Because they curdle it with the skin of the stomach of an animal carcass.**

Shmuel offers yet another explanation: *mipnei shema'amidin otah b'or keivat neveilah* — because non-Jews coagulate the cheese not with the gunk itself but with an actual piece of the stomach lining of a *neveilah*, a piece of skin from the animal's stomach dropped into the milk to make it curdle. According to Shmuel, the gunk itself is not the problem — it is the stomach lining that is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קֵיבָה גּוּפָא שַׁרְיָא, וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָתְנַן: קֵיבַת הַגּוֹי וְשֶׁל נְבֵילָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה,
>
> **Translation:** **The stomach itself is permitted! And did Shmuel say this? But didn't we learn: The stomach of a gentile and of an animal carcass is prohibited.**

The implication of Shmuel's position is striking: *ha keiva gufa sharya* — the stomach gunk itself is permitted. But this creates a difficulty: a *mishnah* elsewhere states that *keivat oved kochavim v'shel neveilah harei zu asurah* — the stomach gunk of a non-Jew and of a *neveilah* is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ, אַטּוּ דְּגוֹי לָאו נְבֵלָה הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed it: Is not an animal of a gentile an animal carcass?**

An additional difficulty with that *mishnah* is noted: the phrasing is itself strange. What does it mean to distinguish between the gunk of a non-Jew and of a *neveilah* as though they were separate categories? *Atu d'oved kochavim lav neveilah hi* — is the animal of a non-Jew not itself a *neveilah*?

### Shmuel Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חֲדָא קָתָנֵי, קֵיבַת שְׁחִיטַת גּוֹי — נְבֵלָה אֲסוּרָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא. כָּאן קוֹדֶם חֲזָרָה, כָּאן לְאַחַר חֲזָרָה, וּמִשְׁנָה לֹא זָזָה מִמְּקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: It is teaching a single matter: The stomach of the slaughter of a gentile is like an animal carcass, forbidden. This is not difficult. Here, before the retraction; here, after the retraction. And a mishna does not move from its place.**

Shmuel resolves this: the *mishnah* is teaching that the slaughter of a non-Jew is to be treated as *neveilah* — they are in fact the same thing. There remains the contradiction between Shmuel's two apparent positions — one permitting the gunk, one forbidding it. The *gemara* resolves this: *kan kodem chazra, kan le'achar chazra* — one statement reflects Shmuel's view before he changed his mind, and the other reflects his view afterward. *Umishnah lo zazah mimkomah* — the *mishnah* was not rewritten to reflect the updated position, because that is simply not how we treat established texts. The earlier version remains in place as a historical artifact of what was once taught. The practical bottom line, according to Shmuel's final position, is that the stomach gunk is permitted, and it is the stomach lining itself that is the problem.

## Multiple Reasons

### Pig Fat Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מַלְכִּיָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּחְלִיקִין פָּנֶיהָ בְּשׁוּמַּן חֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Malkiyya says in the name of Rav Adda bar Ahava: Because they smooth its surface with pig fat.**

The *gemara* now accumulates a striking series of additional reasons offered by various *amoraim* for the prohibition on non-Jewish cheese — a relatively unusual phenomenon. Rav Malachya in the name of Rav Ada bar Ahava states: *mipnei shemachlikim paneha beshoman chazir* — because non-Jews grease the surface or sides of their cheese with pig fat.

### Vinegar Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּחוֹמֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Because they curdle it with vinegar.**

Rav Chisda offers a different reason: *mipnei shema'amidin otah bechomet* — because they use vinegar to coagulate the cheese. Since vinegar functions analogously to wine in halachic terms, this would render the cheese not merely forbidden to eat but also subject to the prohibition of benefit.

### Orla Sap Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בִּשְׂרַף הָעׇרְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: Because they curdle it with sap of orla.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers yet another reason: *mipnei shema'amidin otah b'saraf ha'orla* — because non-Jews coagulate their cheese using sap derived from a tree whose fruit is *orla*, meaning a fruit tree within its first three years of growth, from which the fruit is forbidden.

### Orla Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַמַּעֲמִיד בִּשְׂרַף הָעׇרְלָה — אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פֶּירִי. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֶלָּא בְּקִטְפָא דִּגְוָזָא, אֲבָל בְּקִטְפָא דְּפֵירָא מוֹדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **In accordance with whose opinion? In accordance with this tanna, as it is taught: Rabbi Eliezer says: One who curdles with sap of orla is prohibited because it is fruit. You may even say Rabbi Yehoshua, as far as here Rabbi Yehoshua does not disagree with Rabbi Eliezer except regarding sap of a branch, but regarding sap of a fruit he concedes.**

The *gemara* asks: whose opinion does this follow? It follows the position of Rabbi Eliezer in a *beraita*: *hamamid b'saraf ha'orla asur mipnei shehu peri* — one who coagulates cheese with sap from an *orla* tree is forbidden from the result, because that sap has the legal status of fruit. The *gemara* adds that one need not limit this to Rabbi Eliezer's view — even Rabbi Yehoshua could agree here. The only point of disagreement between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua in this area is regarding *kitpa d'goza*, the sap of a branch, which Rabbi Yehoshua holds is not considered fruit. But with regard to *kitpa d'peira* — sap that flows directly from the fruit itself — Rabbi Yehoshua agrees that it has the status of fruit and is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי בְּפֵירוּשׁ, שֶׁהַמַּעֲמִיד בִּשְׂרַף הֶעָלִין וּבִשְׂרַף הָעִיקָּרִין — מוּתָּר, בִּשְׂרַף הַפַּגִּין — אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פֶּירִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what we learned: Rabbi Yehoshua said: I heard explicitly that one who curdles with sap of the leaves and sap of the roots is permitted, with sap of young figs is prohibited, because it is fruit.**

Rabbi Yehoshua himself says explicitly: sap of the leaves or sap of the roots used in cheese-making is *mutar*, perfectly permitted; but sap from the *paggim* — the young figs — *asur*, because *mipnei shehu peri*, it is like the fruit itself.

### Tension Unresolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין לְרַב חִסְדָּא, בֵּין לְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, תִּתְּסַר בַּהֲנָאָה, קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **According to both Rav Ḥisda and Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, it should be prohibited from deriving benefit. This is difficult.**

However, a sharp difficulty now surfaces with the vinegar reason and the *orla* sap reason. Both Rav Chisda and Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak posit reasons that would logically render the cheese forbidden not merely to eat but also as an *issur hana'ah* — since vinegar has the status of wine, and *orla* is forbidden for benefit. Yet we know that non-Jewish cheese is in fact only forbidden to eat, not forbidden for benefit. The *gemara*'s response is simply *kashya* — it is a difficulty, and it is left unresolved. This unresolved tension suggests that none of these individual reasons is fully satisfactory as a standalone explanation, and that the prohibition may have originated as a broader decree that does not reduce cleanly to any single rationale.

## Torah Scholar Homily

### Balsam Oil Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "לְרֵיחַ שְׁמָנֶיךָ טוֹבִים", לְמָה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם דּוֹמֶה? לִצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטֹין: מְגוּלָּה — רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף, מְכוּסָּה — אֵין רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman, son of Rav Ḥisda, expounded: What is the meaning of that which is written: "Your ointments have a goodly fragrance"? To what is a Torah scholar comparable? To a flask of *pelaitin*: When exposed, its scent diffuses; when covered, its scent does not diffuse.**

Rav Nachman the son of Rav Chisda expounds: what is the meaning of the verse *lere'ach shemanecha tovim* — "the fragrance of your oils is good"? To what is a *talmid chacham* compared? To a covered plate of *paltin* — balsam oil. When the plate is uncovered, *reicha nodef* — its fragrance wafts and spreads, a beautiful and pleasant smell. When it is covered, *mekhusah ein reicha nodef* — the fragrance is contained and no one benefits from it. So too the Torah scholar: when he is out in public, teaching and sharing his wisdom, the fragrance of his knowledge spreads; when he is hidden away and not teaching, the world does not benefit.

### Revealed Secrets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁמְּכוּסִּין מִמֶּנּוּ מִתְגַּלִּין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And moreover, matters that are hidden from him are revealed to him.**

But the homily goes further: *v'lo od ela devarim she'mechusim mimenu mitgalim lo* — not only does his fragrance spread when he teaches, but the things that were hidden even from himself become revealed to him. Rashi explains: *mitgalim lo me-aleihen belo torach k'shehu melamdan* — they become revealed on their own, without labor, in the very act of teaching. The teacher discovers, as he teaches, depths he had not previously accessed.

### Alamot Wordplay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "עֲלָמוֹת אֲהֵבוּךָ", קְרִי בֵּיהּ "עֲלוּמוֹת"; וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמַּלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת אוֹהֲבוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "עֲלָמוֹת אֲהֵבוּךָ", קְרִי בֵּיהּ "עַל מָוֶת"; וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁנּוֹחֵל שְׁנֵי עוֹלָמוֹת, אֶחָד הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְאֶחָד הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "עֲלָמוֹת", קְרִי בֵּיהּ "עוֹלָמוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated: "Maidens love You," read as "hidden things." And moreover, the Angel of Death loves him, as it is stated: "Maidens [*alamot*] love You," read as "over death [*al mot*]." And moreover, he inherits two worlds, this world and the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Maidens," read as "worlds [*olamot*]."**

There now follows a wordplay on the verse *alamot ahevucha* — "young women love you." The *gemara* says: do not read *alamot*, young women, but rather *alumot*, hidden things. The hidden things will love you — that is, they will come to you, they will be revealed to you, as you engage in Torah. The homily continues: *v'lo od ela she'malach hamavet ohavo* — not only that, but the Angel of Death loves him. The *gemara* reads *alamot* as *al-mavet* — he who presides over death will love the Torah scholar, which is to say, the Angel of Death cannot touch him while he is engaged in Torah learning. And finally, *v'lo od ela she'nochel shnei olamot* — he inherits two worlds, *echad olam hazeh ve'echad olam haba*, this world and the world to come. The word *alamot* is now read as *olamot* — worlds. The Torah scholar inherits worlds.

## Food Mishnah

### Forbidden to Eat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם אֲסוּרִין וְאֵין אִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה: חָלָב שֶׁחֲלָבוֹ גּוֹי וְאֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל רוֹאֵהוּ, וְהַפַּת וְהַשֶּׁמֶן שֶׁלָּהֶן. רַבִּי וּבֵית דִּינוֹ הִתִּירוּ הַשֶּׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are items belonging to gentiles that are prohibited, but their prohibition is not that of an item from which benefit is prohibited: Milk that was milked by a gentile and a Jew did not see him, and their bread and oil. Rabbi and his court permitted the oil.**

The *mishnah* now shifts to a broader enumeration: *ve-eilu devarim shel ovdei kochavim asurin ve-ein isuran issur hana'ah* — these are things associated with non-Jews that are forbidden, but whose prohibition does not extend to *issur hana'ah*, forbidden benefit. One may not eat them but one may derive benefit from them.

### Non-Jewish Milk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁלָקוֹת, וּכְבָשִׁין שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לָתֵת לְתוֹכָן יַיִן וָחוֹמֶץ, וְטָרִית טְרוּפָה, וְצִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דָּגָה כִּלְבִּית שׁוֹטֶטֶת בּוֹ, וְהַחִילֵּק, וְקוֹרֶט שֶׁל חִלְתִּית, וּמֶלַח שְׂלָקוֹנְדִית — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין, וְאֵין אִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And boiled and pickled vegetables, whose manner of preparation involves adding wine and vinegar to them, and minced *tarit* fish, and brine that does not have a *kilbit* fish floating in it, and *ḥilak*, and a sliver of *ḥiltit*, and *salkondit* salt — these are prohibited, but their prohibition is not that of an item from which benefit is prohibited.**

The first item is *chalav she-chalavo oved kochavim ve-ein Yisrael ro'ehu* — milk milked by a non-Jew without Jewish supervision. This is the foundation of the well-known discussion around *chalav stam*. Rav Moshe Feinstein's *heter* — largely accepted in the Modern Orthodox community and in significant portions of the *Charedi* world in earlier generations — holds that government regulation of the dairy industry functions as a form of constructive supervision. Since the regulatory framework makes adulteration effectively impossible, it is *anan sahadei*, as if we are all present watching. Under this view, commercially produced milk is treated as *chalav Yisrael*. It is not that one abandons the requirement of *chalav Yisrael*; rather, one holds that the regulatory environment satisfies it.

### Bread & Oil

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-haphat ve-hashemen shelahem* — their bread and their oil. Regarding oil: *Rebbi u'veit dino hitiru ha-shemen* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his court permitted non-Jewish oil, reversing an earlier prohibition. The *mishnah* thus records a historical leniency regarding oil.

### Brined Foods

The *mishnah* then lists a series of additional forbidden foods: *shlakot u-kvashim she-darkan latet letochan yayin ve-chometz* — cooked vegetables and brined foods into which wine or vinegar are customarily added; *tarit trufah* — a kosher fish that has been so thoroughly mashed that one cannot verify whether non-kosher fish has been mixed in; *sir she-ein ba daga kalbit shotelet bo* — fish brine in which a *kalbit* fish is not floating, since the *kalbit* fish, according to Rashi, floats only in kosher fish brine and therefore serves as a sign of kashrut; *ha-chilek* — a fish that is itself permissible but resembles a non-kosher species; *kurt shel chiltit* — a measure of asafoetida, a pungent spice used in ancient cooking and known in contemporary Indian cuisine; and *melach salkundit*. All of these are forbidden to eat but permitted for benefit.

It is worth noting that the appearance of *chiltit* here connects to a broader halachic principle discussed elsewhere in *Avodah Zarah*: *devarim charifim*, sharp and pungent substances, have the capacity to awaken dormant tastes. Even a taste that would ordinarily be considered *pagum* or neutralized by time can be reactivated when cooked or cut with something as potent as *chiltit* — a principle that is later applied to onions, garlic, and similar sharp foods.

## Milk Concerns

### Switching Worry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ חָלָב לְמַאי נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? אִי מִשּׁוּם אִיחַלּוֹפֵי — טָהוֹר חִיוָּר, טָמֵא יָרוֹק!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Milk, with regard to what do we need to be concerned? If it is due to exchange, kosher is white, non-kosher is green!**

The *gemara* now probes the rationale behind the prohibition on non-Jewish milk. The challenge is direct: why worry? If the concern is *achlufi* — that a non-Jew switched out kosher milk for non-kosher milk — the answer seems obvious: *tahor chivver, tamei yarok* — kosher milk is white and non-kosher milk is a different color, greenish or yellowish. They look different. Switching would be immediately detectable.

### Cheese Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִשּׁוּם אִיעָרוֹבֵי — נֵיקוּם! דְּאָמַר מָר: חָלָב טָהוֹר עוֹמֵד, חָלָב טָמֵא אֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is due to mixture, let him curdle it, as the Master said: Kosher milk curdles, non-kosher milk does not curdle.**

If the concern is not outright switching but *eiruvei* — mixing a small amount of non-kosher milk into kosher milk — there is also an easy solution. One should simply attempt to make cheese from it, because *chalav tahor omed, chalav tamei eino omed* — kosher milk can be made into cheese, while non-kosher milk cannot. This is apparently a real physical phenomenon, having to do with the enzymatic composition of milk from non-kosher animals. If the milk coagulates properly, it is kosher; if not, there is a problem.

### Cheese Test Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּקָא בָעֵי לִגְבִינָה, הָכִי נָמֵי. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דְּקָא בָעֵי לֵיהּ לְכַמְכָּא. וְנִשְׁקוֹל מִינֵּיהּ קַלִּי וְנֵיקוּם! כֵּיוָן דִּבְטָהוֹר נָמֵי אִיכָּא נַסְיוּבֵי דְּלָא קָיְימִי, לֵיכָּא לְמֵיקַם עֲלַהּ דְּמִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If he wants to make cheese, yes, so too. What are we dealing with here? Where he wants to use it for *kamkha*. And let him take a bit of it and curdle it! Since there is also whey in kosher milk, which does not curdle, there is no way to establish the matter with regard to it.**

However, the *gemara* notes that the cheese test only solves the problem if one is using the milk to make cheese. What if one intends to use it for *chamcha* — a dish that uses raw, uncongealed milk? One might still propose that a small amount be taken aside and tested. But the *gemara* responds: *keivan d'v'tahor nami ika nisyubei d'lo kaima*, even with fully kosher milk there will be *nisyuvei* — whey — that does not coagulate into cheese. Non-kosher milk mixed in with kosher could blend with the whey, and one would mistake the adulterated portion for ordinary kosher whey, rendering the test unreliable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דְּקָבָעֵי לֵהּ לִגְבִינָה, אִיכָּא דְּקָאֵי בֵּינֵי אִטְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say instead: You may even say where he intends to make cheese, there is milk that remains between the crevices.**

Alternatively, even if one is making cheese, *ika d'ka bini atifei* — there will be milk remaining hidden within the holes of the cheese, much like the holes in Swiss cheese, meaning small pockets of non-kosher milk could be concealed within the finished product. The test therefore does not provide a reliable certification.

## Bread Story

### Admiration Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַפַּת. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פַּת לֹא הוּתְּרָה בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And bread. Rav Kahana says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Bread was not permitted by a court.**

The *mishnah* listed bread among the forbidden items. The *gemara* opens this discussion with an unusual statement. Rabbi Kahana says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *pat lo hutra beveit dino* — bread was never permitted by his court, and some versions read *beveiti*, in his house. The very fact that Rabbi Yochanan felt compelled to say this implies that someone held otherwise. The *gemara* confirms: *in* — yes, there is indeed such a view.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּסְבוּרִין הָעָם הִתִּיר רַבִּי הַפַּת, וְלָא הִיא, רַבִּי לֹא הִתִּיר אֶת הַפַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The people thought that Rabbi permitted bread, but it is not so; Rabbi did not permit bread.**

When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported the following story. Once Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi went out into the countryside, and a non-Jew brought before him *pat purnei* — bread baked in a large oven, *ma'afeh se'ah*. Rabbi exclaimed: *kama na'eh pat zu* — how beautiful is this bread! And then: *mah ra'u chachamim le'osrah* — what did the sages see that led them to forbid it? This is a striking statement. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, the *redactor* of the *Mishnah* and the preeminent rabbinic authority of his generation, is expressing apparent bewilderment at the prohibition.

### Intermarriage Fear

The *gemara* responds with a *kasha*: what do you mean, what did they see? The reason is well established — *mishum chatnut*, because of the concern that eating together leads to social intimacy which leads to *chatnut*, intermarriage.

### Jewish Baker Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, אָמַר: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, אֶלָּא אָמְרוּ: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַךְ רַבִּי לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד וְרָאָה פַּת דָּחוּק לַתַּלְמִידִים, אָמַר רַבִּי: אֵין כָּאן פַּלְטֵר? כִּסְבוּרִין הָעָם לוֹמַר פַּלְטֵר גּוֹי, וְהוּא לֹא אָמַר אֶלָּא פַּלְטֵר יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef, and some say Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, says: The incident did not occur this way. Rather, they said: Once Rabbi went to a certain place and saw bread scarce for the students. Rabbi said: Is there no baker here? The people thought to say: a gentile baker. But he said only: a Jewish baker.**

Rav Yosef — and some say Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda — disputes the story entirely. The incident did not happen this way. Rather, this is what actually occurred: Rabbi traveled to a certain place and observed that his students were *dachuk* — in real distress, without access to bread. He said: *ein kan palter* — is there no baker here? The people assumed he meant a non-Jewish baker, thus implying that even such bread would be acceptable under these circumstances. But Rabbi's intent was entirely otherwise.

### No Jewish Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּלְטֵר גּוֹי, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלֵיכָּא פַּלְטֵר יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל בִּמְקוֹם דְּאִיכָּא פַּלְטֵר יִשְׂרָאֵל — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥelbo said: Even according to the one who says a gentile baker, we said this only where there is no Jewish baker. But in a place where there is a Jewish baker, no.**

Rabbi Chelbo rules: even according to the interpretation that assumed Rabbi was asking about a non-Jewish baker, *lo amran ela delika palter Yisrael* — this leniency applies only where there is no Jewish bakery available. Where a Jewish baker exists, there is no dispensation to eat non-Jewish bread under any reading of the incident.

### City vs Country

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּלְטֵר גּוֹי, הָנֵי מִילֵּי בַּשָּׂדֶה, אֲבָל בָּעִיר — לָא, מִשּׁוּם חַתְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even according to the one who says a gentile baker, that statement applies in the field. But in the city, no, due to marriage.**

Rabbi Yochanan adds a further qualification: even the lenient reading of Rabbi's statement — that non-Jewish bread might be permissible in some circumstances — *hani milei basadeh*, applies only in the countryside. *Aval ba'ir lo* — but in a city, one may not eat non-Jewish bread, *mishum chatnut*, because the concern of social proximity leading to intermarriage is fully operative in an urban setting.

### Ibu Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְבוּ הֲוָה מְנַכֵּית וְאָכֵיל פַּת אַבֵּי מִצְרֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לָא תִּשְׁתַּעוּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ דְּאַיְבוּ, דְּקָאָכֵיל לַחְמָא דַּאֲרַמָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Aivu would bite and eat bread at the boundaries of the fields. Rava said, and some say Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Do not speak with Aivu, as he eats bread of Arameans.**

The *gemara* closes with a pointed anecdote. A certain Ibu would take and eat non-Jewish bread *abei metzrei* — along the borders of the fields, apparently reasoning that this qualified as the permitted countryside context. Rava — and some say Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — declared to the people: *lo tishte'u minei* — do not associate with Ibu, because *dek'achil lachma arma'ei*, he eats non-Jewish bread. It is a striking conclusion: not merely a theoretical ruling, but a social distancing from one who treats the prohibition lightly.
