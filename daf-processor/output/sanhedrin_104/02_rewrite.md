# Sanhedrin 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Extending Food Merit

### Michah Food Merit

The *daf* opens by returning to a question carried over from the previous discussion: why was Michah not included among those enumerated as having no portion in the world to come? The Gemara's answer was that Michah made his food available to passersby, and that merit shielded him from the list. This serves as the springboard for a broader teaching about the remarkable power of extending food to others.

### Distance/Bring Close

Rabbi Yochanan teaches in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma: *gedolah legima* — great is the tasting and extending of food. He then expands this teaching in his own name, enumerating four dimensions of food's power. First, *meracheket et hakrovim* — it can distance those who are close. Second, *umikarrevet et harichokim* — it can bring near those who are far. Third, *uma'alemet einayim min harisha'im* — it causes God, as it were, to turn a blind eye from the wickedness being committed. Fourth, *umashra Shechinah al nevi'ei habaal* — it even has the power to cause the *shechina* to rest upon a prophet of the *baal*, of *avodah zarah*, for the merit of one who extended food to him. And fifth, *sheshigagato olah zadon* — even negligence in failing to provide food is treated as though it were intentional wrongdoing.

### Ammon Moab

The first principle — that food distances those who were close — is demonstrated from Ammon and Moav. The explicit *pasuk* states: *al devar asher lo kidmu etchem balechem uvamayim* — because they did not greet you with bread and water. That simple failure to extend bread and water caused these two nations, who bordered Israel and were related to us through Lot, to be permanently distanced from the community of Israel.

### Yitro Merit

The second principle — that food brings near those who are far — is demonstrated from Yitro. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *bischar kir'an lo veyochal lechem* — in the reward of Yitro's daughters calling Moshe and giving him food, *zachu benei banav veyashvu belishkat hagazit* — his descendants ultimately merited to sit in the *Lishkat HaGazit*, the chamber of the *Sanhedrin* within the *Beis HaMikdash* itself. The *pasuk* from Divrei Hayamim identifies these descendants as the *sofrim* who dwelt in Yavetz — the Tiratim, Shimatim, and Suchatim — who are identified as *hakeinim*, and the *pasuk* in Shoftim confirms that *bnei Keini chotein Moshe* — the sons of the Keini, Moshe's father-in-law — went up with the children of Yehuda toward the desert of the south near Arad.

One might ask what precisely is meant by calling Ammon and Moav "close" and Yitro "far." One reading is geographical: Ammon and Moav bordered Israel while Yitro was geographically distant. Another reading would cast it in terms of family relation: Ammon and Moav were blood relatives descended from Lot, while Yitro was an outsider. Rashi, however, is uncomfortable with the second reading, since he holds that Yitro descended from the *benei Keturah* through Midyan — ultimately tracing back to Avraham. Rashi therefore reads the distinction as purely geographical.

### Divine Blindness

The third principle — that extending food causes God to overlook wickedness — is demonstrated from Michah himself. As we said, Michah's habit of extending food to those who passed his home is precisely the reason he was not included in the list of those without a portion in the world to come.

The fourth principle — that the *shechina* can rest upon a prophet of the *baal* in the merit of extending food — is drawn from the story of Iddo HaNavi. A false prophet, one who was in truth a prophet of the *baal*, deceived Iddo and brought him to his house and fed him. The *pasuk* then records: *vayehi devar Hashem el hanavi asher heshivo* — and the word of God came to that prophet, the very one who had brought Iddo back and hosted him. The power of extending food was sufficient to draw a direct communication from God even to one who was a prophet of idolatry.

### Yehonatan David

The fifth principle — that negligence in failing to provide food is counted as intentional sin — is taught by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav. *Ilmale hilvehu Yehonatan l'David shtei kikrot lechem* — had Yehonatan only given David two loaves of bread when David was fleeing from Shaul, *lo neherga Nov ir hakohanim* — the city of Nov, the city of the *kohanim*, would not have been massacred by Shaul. *V'lo nitrad Doeg Adomi* — and Doeg HaEdomite, who slandered David at that time, would not have been *nitrad*, excluded from *olam haba*, as a consequence of his sin. *V'lo neherag Shaul v'shloshet banav* — and Shaul and his three sons would not have been killed as a result of that cascade of events. All of this tragedy traces back to the negligence — not malice, mere negligence — of failing to provide food when it was needed.

## Kings Not Listed

### Achaz Yotam

Having concluded the discussion of Michah and the importance of extending food, the Gemara now turns to a series of evil kings in Israel's history who were not included in the list of those without a portion in the world to come, and asks why. *U'mipnei ma lo manu et Achaz* — why was Achaz not listed?

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba answers: *mipnei shemutal bein shenei tzaddikim bein Yotam l'Chizkiyahu* — because he was sandwiched between two righteous men. His father was Yotam and his son was Chizkiyahu. The lineage runs: Uziyahu, Yotam, Achaz, Chizkiyahu. Because of both his father and his son, he was not listed.

A prior question is worth noting: does being omitted from the list mean that he actually merits *olam haba*, or is it merely that *chazal* chose not to call him out explicitly? This distinction will recur throughout. When the Mishnah listed the three kings and four *hedyotot*, was that list exhaustive and definitive, or was it a deliberate enumeration that left others unmentioned for their own reasons? The question remains open, but it should be kept in mind as the discussion unfolds.

A separate reason is offered by Rav Yosef: *mipnei shehayah lo boshet panim mi'Yeshayahu* — because Achaz felt shame before Yeshayahu. When God told Yeshayahu to go meet Achaz by the upper pool, at the field of the *kovess* — the launderer — Achaz, upon seeing Yeshayahu, could not face him. Some say *d'chavshinehu l'apei* — he buried his face and passed by. Others say he placed a launderer's sprinkling implement over his face as a kind of mask and slipped away. Either way, he was overcome with embarrassment before the prophet. This reason sounds less like a technicality about whether to include him in a list and more like a genuine moral attainment — the capacity for shame — that may qualify him for a portion in the world to come.

### Son Father Merit

The Gemara asks about Amon — another notoriously wicked king who is also absent from the list. *Mipnei kevodo she-Yoshiyahu* — because of the honor of Yoshiyahu, who was Amon's son, and who was one of the most righteous kings in Israel's history.

This immediately raises a counterpoint: *Menashe nami lo nimnei* — by the same logic, Menashe should not have been listed either. Menashe is definitively on the list. And Menashe's father was Chizkiyahu. So if we protect a son because he had a righteous son, why not protect Menashe because he had a righteous father?

The Gemara responds with a fundamental principle: *bra mazakei aba aba lo mazakei bra* — a son brings merit to the father, but a father does not bring merit to the son. The logic is intuitive. A wicked person who failed to learn from his own righteous father cannot claim protection from that father's merit — if anything, the father's righteousness makes the son's wickedness more culpable. But a wicked person who nevertheless raised or fathered a righteous son — something of that goodness reflects back on the father and works in his favor.

This is reinforced by the *pasuk*: *ve'ein mi-yadi matzil* — no one can save from My hand — and the Gemara reads this to mean that *ein Avraham matzil et Yishmael ve'ein Yitzchak matzil et Esav*. Avraham's righteousness could not save Yishmael; Yitzchak's righteousness could not save Esav. A good father cannot protect a bad son.

With this principle established, the Gemara notes: *hashta de-atit le-hachi* — now that we have this principle, we can actually revisit Achaz as well. Rather than needing the reason of shame before Yeshayahu, we can say simply: *mishum kevodo shel Chizkiyahu* — because of the honor of Chizkiyahu, his son. The merit of the righteous son protected the father from being named on the list.

### Amon Yoshiyahu

Returning now to Amon: the answer that his righteous son Yoshiyahu shielded him from the list follows directly from the principle just established. The son's merit works retroactively in the father's favor. Amon was wicked, but Yoshiyahu was exceptional, and that is sufficient reason — at least — for his exclusion from explicit enumeration.

### Yehoyakim Skull

*Mipnei ma lo manu et Yehoyakim* — and why was Yehoyakim not included? The Gemara gives a striking answer, tied to a dramatic story. The answer is *mishum de-Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* — because of the case involving Rabbi Chiya bar Avin.

Rabbi Chiya bar Avin taught — and this Gemara appeared earlier in the tractate — that it was written on the skull of Yehoyakim: *zot ve-od acheret* — this, and one more. The *pasuk* had foretold that Yehoyakim would receive the burial of a donkey, meaning he would be cast out and left unburied. The inscription on his skull indicated that that curse had befallen him, and yet one more punishment was to come.

The grandfather of Rabbi Preida — identified as the same Rabbi Chiya — found this skull, cast aside at the gates of Jerusalem, which is precisely what the *pasuk* had said would happen. Written on it were the words *zot ve-od acheret*. He buried the skull, and it would not stay buried — it kept rising back to the surface. He buried it again, and again it would not remain. Understanding then that this was the skull of Yehoyakim — of whom it is written *kvurat chamor yikaver* and *sachov ve-hashlech*, that he would be cast away — he recognized that a proper burial was simply impossible for this man. Yet it was still the skull of a king, and *lo ikshar lezalzulei beih* — it was not proper to treat it with such degradation. So he wrapped it in silk and placed it in a box so that at least it would not be kicked around.

His wife saw the skull in the box. She assumed it must be a first wife whose memory he could not release — *dehalo ka menashe lah*, he simply could not forget her. Consumed by jealousy, she fired up the oven and burned the skull entirely. And that, says the Gemara, is what *zot ve-od acheret* meant: the first curse of unburied, dishonored remains, and the second curse of being completely incinerated. Because of this extreme and distinctive punishment that had already befallen him, Yehoyakim was not listed among those explicitly excluded from *olam haba*. Whether that omission means he genuinely has a portion there, or only that *chazal* chose not to list him, remains, as before, ambiguous.

## Chizkiyahu Sins

### Arrogance Sign

A *braisa* is introduced: *bishvil hatov be'einecha asiti* — because of the phrase "I did what was good in Your eyes," attributed to Chizkiyahu's prayer when he was deathly ill. By taking credit for his own righteousness in this way, Chizkiyahu displayed a form of arrogance. And that arrogance led, by the principle of *aveira goreres aveira*, to further sin.

Specifically, when Yeshayahu came to him with the divine promise that his prayer had been heard and he would live another fifteen years, Chizkiyahu demanded a sign. *Ma os* — what will be the sign? This is presented as *chutzpah*: he had just received an extraordinary reprieve, a prophetic promise of renewed life, and yet he demanded confirmation. Moreover, when given the choice of a sign — whether the sun should stand still for ten hours or move backward — he chose the backward movement because mere stillness was insufficiently dramatic. Arrogance upon arrogance.

### Babylon Exile

*Uvishvil ma os* — and because he demanded a sign, *nochrim achlu al shulchano* — foreigners ate at his table. The king of Babylonia sent emissaries when he heard that Chizkiyahu had been ill, and somehow — whether through the connection to the miraculous sign or through the arrogance that now flowed freely — Chizkiyahu welcomed them and hosted them. *Achlu al shulchano uvishvil she'nochrim achlu al shulchano garam galus le'vanav* — and because they ate at his table, it caused the exile of his children.

The specific sin that brought about this consequence was that Chizkiyahu showed these emissaries everything: his silver and gold, his fragrances, his fine oil, his treasuries. Rava asks: what is *Beis Nechoso* — the "treasure house" mentioned in the *pasuk*? One view is that he had his wife attend to the guests, which was not *tzanua* for the queen. Shmuel says it simply means his treasury, which fits the plain context. Rabbi Yochanan understands it as a reference to some kind of weapon. After this display, the prophecy came: his children would be taken into exile and become eunuchs in the palace of the king of Bavel. *Uvaneha asher yetzu mimecha asher toulid yikachu vehayu sarisim beheichal melech Bavel.*

### Non-Jews Home

The Gemara uses this episode as support for a broader teaching of Chizkiya the Amora: *kol hamizamen akum letoch beiso umeshamesh elav garam galus le'vanav* — anyone who invites a non-Jew into his home and attends to him — serving him in a way that demonstrates deference or subservience — brings about exile for his children. The *pasuk* about Chizkiyahu's children being taken captive is cited as proof.

The chain is therefore: arrogance in prayer led to demanding a sign, which led to receiving the Babylonian emissaries, which led to displaying the royal treasuries to them, which led to the prophecy of exile for his children. One sin generates the next — *aveira goreres aveira* — until the consequences extend across generations.

## Eicha Verses

### Vadad Alone

Now that the discussion has touched on the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* — the culmination of the evil kings' legacy — the Gemara pivots to a *derasha* on the opening verses of *Eicha*, the Book of Lamentations, proceeding through them in order.

First, a general observation: Rava in the name of Rabbi Yochanan asks *mipnei ma laku b'Eicha* — why were they punished with *Eicha*? The answer is that the *gematria* of *Eicha* is thirty-six, corresponding to the thirty-six *kritot* in the Torah — the gravest violations for which one incurs *karet* — which they had transgressed. A second question: *mipnei ma laku b'alef-bet* — why is *Eicha* structured as an *alef-bet* acrostic? Because they transgressed the Torah that was given in *alef-bet* — that is, in the full breadth and entirety of the Hebrew language. The *alef-bet* represents the complete scope of Torah from beginning to end, and their sin was correspondingly total.

Turning now to the opening phrase itself — *Eicha yashva vadad*, how does she sit alone — Rava in the name of Rabbi Yochanan notes that God had originally promised: *vayishkon Yisrael betach badad ein Yaakov el eretz dagan v'tirosh af shamav yaarfu tal* — Israel will dwell in security, alone, like the eye of Yaakov, in a land of grain and wine, its heavens dripping with dew. That *badad* was the *badad* of strength: self-sufficient, secure, not dependent on the nations. Now, *yashva badad* means something entirely different — alone and desolate, abandoned. *Achshav yehiye badad moshavam* — now their dwelling place is a different kind of aloneness, the aloneness of abandonment.

### City Widow

*Ha'ir rabati am haita k'almana* — the city that was so populous has become like a widow. Rava in the name of Rabbi Yochanan asks: what made Jerusalem so densely populated? The answer is that they would marry a younger woman to an older man and an older woman to a younger man. Rashi explains that this is not about legal minors but about younger and older in a relative sense: by matching someone of lesser fertility with someone of greater fertility, both couples were able to begin having children immediately, maximizing the population. Matching two very young people together, by Rashi's account, would have delayed reproduction.

Then Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav adds a crucial qualifier: *k'almana v'lo almana mamash* — like a widow but not literally a widow. A literal widow's husband is dead and will never return. Jerusalem is compared instead to *k'isha shehalach baala l'medinat hayam v'daato lachazor aleha* — a woman whose husband has gone overseas but intends to return. The relationship is not severed. God has not abandoned us forever; the return is anticipated.

### Ruler Vassal

*Rabati bagoyim sarati bamdinot hayta lamas* — she who was great among the nations, a prince among the provinces, has become a vassal. Rava in the name of Rabbi Yochanan notes: wherever the Jewish people would go, *nasim sarim l'adoneihem* — they would become princes to their very masters.

### Two Captives

A *baraisa* illustrates this: there was an episode involving two Jewish captives taken on Mount Carmel. Their captor was walking behind them. As they walked, one captive said to the other: that camel walking ahead of us is blind in one eye, it is carrying two skins — one of wine and one of oil — and the two people leading it are one Jew and one non-Jew.

The captor overheard this and demanded — perhaps with something between admiration and hostility toward this stiff-necked people — *me'ayin atem yode'im*, how could they possibly know all this? They explained each inference in turn. The camel ate only from one side of the road — the side it could see — revealing that it was blind in the other eye. They knew the skins contained wine and oil because wine drips and is absorbed into the ground, while oil drips and floats on the surface; they had observed both phenomena as they walked behind the camel. As for the men leading it: the non-Jew relieved himself directly on the road, while the Jew stepped to the side — *mipnei tzniyut*.

The captor ran after the caravan, confirmed every detail, and was so astonished that he kissed these two captives on their heads, brought them home, made a great feast for them, and danced before them. The masters had become servants; the captives had become the *sarim*. And he declared: *Baruch shebachar b'zaro shel Avraham v'natan lahem michochmato* — blessed is He who chose the seed of Avraham and gave to them from His wisdom — *u'v'chol makom she'hein holchin nasim sarim l'adoneihem* — wherever they go, they become princes to those who would be their masters. He released them, and they returned home in peace.

## Eicha Crying

### Spies Night

The Gemara moves to the next phrase in *Eicha*: *bachoh tivkeh balaylah* — she shall surely weep in the night. The double language of weeping — *bachoh tivkeh* — prompts the question: *shtei bchiyot hallalu lama*, why the double weeping? Rava in the name of Rabbi Yochanan answers: *echad al Mikdash Rishon v'echad al Mikdash Sheni* — one for the First *Beis HaMikdash* and one for the Second *Beis HaMikdash*. The lamentation in *Eicha* already anticipates both destructions.

Why specifically at night? *Al iskei laylah* — because of the original nighttime sin. The *pasuk* in Bamidbar records: *vatissa kol ha'edah vayitnu et kolam vayivku ha'am balaylah hahu* — the entire congregation lifted its voice and wept that night, the night of *chet hamiraglim*, the sin of the spies, when they despaired and wanted to return to Egypt. According to tradition, that night was the night of Tisha B'Av. God responded: *atem bchitem bchiya shel chinam v'ani ekba lachem bchiya l'dorot* — you have wept for nothing, and I will establish this night as a night of weeping for all future generations.

Three additional explanations are offered for the significance of *balaylah*. First, *shekol habocheh balaila kolo nishma* — when one cries at night, the voice carries further, since the world is quiet and there is no ambient noise. Second, *kochavim umazalot bochim imo* — the very stars and constellations seem to weep along, for at night one perceives the full cosmos, and the entire universe shares in the grief. Third, *hashomea kolo bocheh kenegdo* — the sound of nighttime crying is so clear and unobstructed that those who hear it are overwhelmed with sympathy and cry along.

This last point is illustrated by an episode involving Raban Gamliel. A woman in his neighborhood had lost her son and cried for him at night. Raban Gamliel heard her crying and could not help but cry along — *ad shenishru risei einav* — to the point that his very eyelashes fell out. The next morning his students noticed his reddened eyes and understood what had happened. Unwilling to see their master suffer so, they removed her from the neighborhood.

### Husband Youth

*V'dimasah al lochayah* — her tears upon her cheek. Rav in the name of Rabbi Yochanan interprets *lochayah* as suggesting moisture, the freshness of youth: the crying is *k'isha shebochah al baal ne'urim* — like a woman who weeps for the husband of her youth. The *pasuk* cited is *eli k'visulah chogeret sak al baal ne'urah* — a wail like that of a young woman girding herself in sackcloth for the husband of her youth.

### Enemies Head

*Hayu tzareha l'rosh* — her enemies became the head over her. Rav in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: *kol hameitzik l'Yisrael naasah rosh* — whoever oppresses Israel becomes a head, a leader among nations. This is a striking statement, and the Gemara itself seems aware that it requires careful handling: one would not want it read as an endorsement of oppressing the Jewish people. The general understanding is that God, out of a kind of respect for Israel, would not allow a weak or minor nation to subjugate them — only a truly mighty nation could be permitted to do so.

The proof-text offered is *ki lo muaf laasher mutzak lah* — there is no weariness to the one who afflicts her. This is read as a separate but related teaching: those who oppress Israel never seem to grow weary of it. The Gra notes that this *drash* on the *pasuk* does not actually prove the prior point about oppressors becoming great nations; rather it is a distinct observation — anti-Semites and those who persecute Israel throughout history never seem to exhaust themselves in doing so.

### Complaint Blessing

*Lo aleichem kol ovrei derech* — let it not befall you, all you who pass by. Rav in the name of Rabbi Yochanan derives from here: *mikan l'kuvlanah min haTorah* — from here we see the concept of making a complaint or lament in a way that also extends a blessing to those nearby. Rashi explains that when one bemoans one's own tragedies, one should add: but may this not befall you — *lo aleichem* — as a way of protecting the listener. The lament itself becomes an expression of care for others.

Rav Amram in the name of Rav adds another layer: *asuni ke'ovrei al dat* — my punishments have made me appear as though I transgress my own religion. The sufferings visited upon Jerusalem make it look, to outside observers, as though God has abandoned His people — as though they must have been the worst of transgressors.

### Jerusalem Sodom

This is then connected to the statement: *ve'ilu biSdom ketiv vaHashem himtir al Sdom* — when it comes to Sodom, God rained down fire and brimstone. *Ve'ilu biYerushalayim ketiv mimarom shalach esh be'atzmotai vayirdena* — and by Jerusalem it says God sent fire from on high into my very bones and crushed them. The same image of heavenly fire applied to Jerusalem as to Sodom implies that Jerusalem's sin was as great as Sodom's — or greater.

Indeed, the *pasuk* states explicitly: *vayigdal avon bat ami mechatat Sdom* — the sin of my people was greater than the sin of Sodom. This raises an obvious question: *vachi maso fanim yesh badavar* — is there favoritism here? If Jerusalem's sin was greater than Sodom's, why was Jerusalem not destroyed as utterly as Sodom?

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan answers: *midah yeteirah hayetah biYerushalayim shelo hayetah biSdom* — there was a distinctive quality in Jerusalem that was absent in Sodom. The *pasuk* about Sodom reads: *hineh zeh hayah avon Sdom achotech gaon sivat lechem veyad ani ve'evyon lo hechezikah* — Sodom had bread in abundance but did not support the poor and destitute. It was stingy and hardhearted.

By contrast, the *pasuk* about Jerusalem reads: *yedei nashim rachmaniyot bishlu yaldeihen* — the hands of compassionate women cooked their own children. Rashi offers two readings of this juxtaposition. The first — striking in its moral audacity — is that even in the act of cooking their own children in the extremity of the famine during the siege, the women of Jerusalem shared what they had with their neighbors, as the end of the *pasuk* indicates that they ate it together. Even amid unimaginable horror, the people of Jerusalem retained a measure of compassion for one another — and that quality distinguished them from Sodom. The second reading is darker: the comparison is not exculpatory but rather shows that Jerusalem's destruction was in some ways worse than Sodom's — Sodom was annihilated but had known wealth, while Jerusalem's destruction came through terrible privation and suffering. In that sense there was no favoritism; they suffered more, not less.

### Spies Mouth

The Gemara then notes: *sillah kol abbirai Hashem bekirbi* — God has crushed all my mighty ones within me — and interprets this *ke'adam she'omer lechavero nifsala matbeia zu* — like a person who says to his friend: this coin has been invalidated. A coin is invalidated when it is flattened and the image stamped upon it is destroyed. So too, God flattened and obliterated the mighty of Jerusalem.

Moving to *patzu alaich pihem* — they opened their mouths against you — the acrostic in *Eicha* reaches the letter *peh*. The Gemara notes a well-known anomaly: in *Eicha*, the *peh* precedes the *ayin* in the acrostic, whereas in standard Hebrew order *ayin* precedes *peh*. Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan explains: *bishvil meraglim she'amru befihem mah shelo ra'u be'eineihem* — because of the spies, who said with their *peh*, their mouth, what they had not seen with their *ayin*, their eye. Their mouth preceded their eye — they fabricated their report, giving voice to things they had never actually witnessed. The inversion of *peh* before *ayin* in *Eicha* is the literary memorial to that original inversion.

### Bread Judges

A final *pasuk* — not from *Eicha* but on the same theme — is cited: *ochlei ami achlu lechem Hashem lo kara'u* — those who devour my people eat bread, they do not call out to God. Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan derives: *kol haohel milachman shel Yisrael toeim ta'am lechem* — whoever eats from the bread of Israel truly tastes what good bread is. Rashi understands this as referring to the enemies who stole and took from Israel's food — when they took what was ours, they experienced the real taste of bread. And conversely, *she'eino ochel milachman shel Yisrael eino toeim ta'am lechem* — one who does not eat Jewish bread has never truly tasted bread. Why did it come to this — that our enemies were able to steal and consume our food? *Hashem lo kara'u* — because we did not call out to God.

Two opinions are offered for who precisely *Hashem lo kara'u* refers to. Rava says *elu hadayanim* — these were the judges, who failed to bring God's presence into their courts and did not administer true justice. Shmuel says *elu melamdei tinokot* — these were the schoolteachers, who were lax in their responsibility to have children read *pesukim*, diligent in their teaching. Both sins — the corruption of the courts and the neglect of Torah education — are identified as the underlying causes of the national catastrophe.

## List Authority

### Great Assembly

Having completed the *derasha* on *Eicha*, the Gemara returns to the foundational question of the entire discussion: who composed the list of those without a portion in *olam haba*? Rav Ashi answers: *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah mana'um* — it was the Men of the Great Assembly, at the beginning of the period of the Second *Beis HaMikdash*, who deliberated and determined who would be explicitly enumerated.

### Shlomo David

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav then relates that the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* sought to add one more name to the list. They did not even say the name directly — such was the respect they maintained — but it was Shlomo HaMelech, whose wives, according to the plain meaning of the *pesukim*, had led him into idolatry.

At that moment, *ba'ah dmut dyukno* — the image, the *dyukan*, the icon of his father David, appeared before them and prostrated itself at their feet. The *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* were unmoved. *Ba'ah esh min hashamayim velichecha esh besafseleihem* — fire came from heaven and singed their benches. They remained unmoved. Remarkably, the Gemara presents this almost as a parallel to the *lo bashamayim hi* principle — except that here the domain is not *halacha* but the determination of who merits *olam haba*.

### God Final Say

Then a *bat kol* emerged and spoke: *chazita ish mahir bimlachto lifnei melachim yityatzev bal yityatzev lifnei chashuchim* — have you seen a man diligent in his work? He will stand before kings; he will not stand before the darkened ones. The *bat kol* identified this verse as referring to Shlomo. He had built the *Beis HaMikdash* in seven years while his own palace took thirteen. His diligence in God's house was exceptional. Such a person *lifnei melachim yityatzev* — will stand before kings, meaning in *Gan Eden* — *bal yityatzev lifnei chashuchim* — not before the darkened ones, not in *Gehinnom*.

Still the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* did not relent. A second *bat kol* emerged with a final declaration: *hime'imach yeshalmenah* — is it from you that payment will come? *Ki ma'asta ki atah tivchar velo ani* — because you have made your determination: do you think you choose and not I? God declared: it is I who decide who receives *olam haba*.

This is a powerful and fitting conclusion. The *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* had the authority and wisdom to compose their list, and their deliberations were taken with the utmost seriousness — fire from heaven and two heavenly voices notwithstanding, they did not simply yield. But God's final word reasserts divine sovereignty: the human list is a human list. The eschatological fate of every individual — whether explicitly named or conspicuously omitted, whether included for reasons of honor, punishment, shame, or the merit of a righteous child — ultimately rests not with any assembly but with God alone. And this returns us, in the most clarifying way, to the question that has threaded through the entire discussion: what does it actually mean to be on the list, or left off it? The answer, in the end, is that the list reflects the wisdom and considered judgment of the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* — but *atah tivchar velo ani* is turned on its head. It is not they who choose. It is God.
