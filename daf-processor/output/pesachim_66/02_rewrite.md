# Pesachim 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach on Shabbat

### Intro & Debate

Daf 66 opens immediately after the *mishnah*, which had catalogued what aspects of *shabbat* may be overridden when the fourteenth of Nisan falls on *shabbat*. The *mishnah* had framed this primarily as a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer on one side, who took the radical position that virtually anything necessary to enable the *korban pesach* may be done even if it could have been prepared the day before, and Rabbi Yehoshua together with his student Rabbi Akiva on the other. At the end of the *mishnah*, Rabbi Akiva had cited the word *b'moado* — "in its appointed time" — as the scriptural basis for bringing the *korban pesach* on *shabbat* regardless of when the fourteenth falls.

The *gemara*, however, does not begin by analyzing that dispute. Instead it steps back to a more fundamental question: how do we know in the first place that *pesach* overrides *shabbat* at all? The answer emerges through a remarkable historical narrative.

### B'nei Beteira Forget

*Tanu rabbanan* — our Rabbis taught: *halacha zu nitalma mi-B'nei Beteira* — this *halacha* that *pesach* may be brought on *shabbat* was lost to *B'nei Beteira*. *B'nei Beteira* were the major authorities at the *Beit HaMikdash* at the time of Hillel. Rashi identifies them as *nesi'im*. They appear elsewhere in the tradition as well — most famously in the story involving Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai at Yavneh, where *shabbat* fell on Rosh Hashanah and *B'nei Beteira* argued that the *shofar* could only be blown on *shabbat* in the *Beit HaMikdash*, which no longer existed.

One might wonder how such a basic *halacha* could have been forgotten, given that *pesach* falls on *shabbat* on average once every seven years. It is possible that the leaders of that era found ways to manipulate the calendar to avoid the situation; in any case, the *gemara* states plainly that when the fourteenth fell on *shabbat* that year, they had simply forgotten — *shachachu v'lo yadu im pesach doche et hashabbat im lav* — whether *pesach* overrides *shabbat* or not.

They asked around: does anyone know whether *pesach* overrides *shabbat*? They were told: there is a certain man who has recently come up from Bavel — *Hillel HaBavli* — who spent time attending upon the two great leaders of the generation, Shemaya and Avtalyon, and he would know. The designation *Hillel HaBavli* is striking, since we do not normally know him by that name; Hillel's family was connected to the *nesi'im* and the *Reish Galuta*, yet the tradition here identifies him as coming from Bavel. They sent for him and summoned him.

They asked: do you know whether *pesach* overrides *shabbat*? His opening response is unforgettable: *v'chi pesach echad yesh lanu bashanah shedoche et hashabbat* — what, do we have only one *korban pesach* in a year that overrides *shabbat*? We have more than 200 sacrifices each year that override *shabbat*! The arithmetic is simple: two *tamid* offerings every day times roughly fifty *shabbatot* is one hundred, plus the *musaf* of *shabbat* — two additional animals — is another hundred. Add the *musaf* offerings of *yamim tovim* that fall on *shabbat* and you already exceed 200. The point is that the overriding of *shabbat* by fixed-time obligations is a thoroughly established principle in *avodah*.

### Hillel's Proof

Hillel then proceeded to lay out his formal proofs. He said: *ne'emar moado b'Pesach v'ne'emar moado b'tamid* — the word *mo'ado*, "in its appointed time," appears both by the *korban pesach* and by the *tamid* daily offering. Just as *mo'ado* by the *tamid* indicates that it overrides *shabbat*, so too *mo'ado* by the *pesach* indicates the same.

But there is an immediate difficulty: where do we know that *tamid* itself overrides *shabbat*? The word *mo'ado* appears there too, so if *mo'ado* alone established the override, we would not need to derive *pesach* from *tamid* — *pesach* has its own *mo'ado*. The *gemara* will address this in detail later, but for now, Hillel is presenting both a *gezerah shavah* — a verbal analogy linking the shared word — and, additionally, a *kal vachomer*: if the *tamid*, for which there is no punishment of *karet* if one fails to bring it, overrides *shabbat*, then certainly the *pesach*, for which neglect carries the penalty of *karet*, should override *shabbat*. This *kal vachomer* added independent logical weight beyond the verbal link.

### Hillel Becomes Nasi

The reaction was immediate: *miyad hoshivuhu b'rosh u'minuhu nasi aleihem* — they placed him at their head and appointed him *nasi* over them. He then spent the entire day expounding the laws of *pesach*, both to educate the people and to demonstrate his newly established authority.

Then, pointedly, he began to taunt them: *mi garam lachem she'e'eleh miBavel v'ehyeh nasi aleihem* — what caused this, that I, an outsider, had to come from Bavel to be your leader? *Atzlut shebachem* — your laziness. You did not do as I did; you did not devote yourself to attending upon and truly learning from the great masters of the generation, Shemaya and Avtalyon.

At that moment — and as the narrative makes clear, this is where the element of *comeuppance* enters — they turned to him with a practical question. *Rabbi*, they now called him, deferentially: what is the law if someone forgot to bring his slaughtering knife to the *Beit HaMikdash* before *shabbat*? Could he bring it on *shabbat* itself? This was, as the *gemara* will later connect, precisely the question debated by Rabbi Eliezer — whether preparatory acts for the *korban pesach* override *shabbat*.

Hillel's answer is arresting: *halacha zo shamati v'shachachti* — I heard this *halacha*, and I have forgotten it. There it is — the man who had just mocked the leaders for their failure to learn properly from Shemaya and Avtalyon now cannot remember what he himself heard from Shemaya and Avtalyon.

But Hillel did not leave them without guidance. *Hanach lahem l'Yisrael*, he said — leave it to the Jewish people. *Im einam nevi'im hem b'nei nevi'im hem* — if they are not themselves prophets, they are at least the descendants of prophets. They will have the religious intuition to work it out correctly.

The following day — which was the fourteenth itself, a *shabbat* — this is precisely what the people did. Those whose *pesach* was a sheep embedded the knife in the animal's wool; those whose *pesach* was a goat stuck it between the animal's horns. The animal would then walk itself to the *Beit HaMikdash* carrying the knife, without the person having performed the act of carrying. When Hillel saw what they were doing, he remembered the *halacha*: *kach mekublani mipi Shemaya v'Avtalyon* — this is exactly the tradition I received from Shemaya and Avtalyon.

There is something striking and beautiful about this episode. The *gemara* elsewhere employs the principle of *puk chazei mai ama davar* — if you do not know the *halacha*, go and see what the people are doing, and take your cue from established popular practice. But that principle relies on an existing practice already being observed. Here no one knew what to do; there was no established practice. Hillel was trusting not existing practice but the people's forward-looking religious intuition — *im einam nevi'im hem b'nei nevi'im hem*. That is a considerably stronger statement. There are *teshuvot* that invoke this phrase, though typically in the sense of "since they have been doing it, we may assume they are right" — a kind of retroactive validation of existing practice. The use of the phrase here, to mean "let us see what they will do and trust they will arrive at the truth," is rarer and more arresting.

One might also note how *B'nei Beteira* address Hillel here: *rebbi*. We do not normally encounter the title "Rabbi Hillel." The *zugot*, the pairs of leaders, were not called *rabbis* in the technical sense; the title had not yet come into that usage. When *B'nei Beteira* say *rebbi*, they mean it as a deferential honorific — "my master" — not as a formal designation.

## Knife on Shabbat

### Animal Carries Knife

The *gemara* now returns to examine the *halacha* of the knife more carefully. The *baraita* had described how the animal was made to carry the knife — embedded in the wool of the sheep or between the horns of the goat. But an immediate objection arises: *beha ka avid avodah b'kodashim* — by doing this, you are using your consecrated animal to perform labor for you, which is itself prohibited. The verse *lo ta'avod b'bechor shorecha* establishes that one may not perform labor with a consecrated animal.

In response, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* about Hillel's own practice: *miyamav lo ma'al adam b'olato* — never once did anyone have the opportunity to commit *me'ilah* through Hillel's *olah* offering. How was this possible? Because Hillel's practice was to bring his animal into the *azarah* while it was still *chullin* — unsanctified — and only to sanctify it at the very last possible moment. He would then perform *semicha*, lay his hands on the animal, and immediately *shochet* it. By sanctifying the animal only at the moment of *shechita*, there was never a window during which misuse could occur.

This explains the knife situation: if the owner had not yet sanctified his animal, having the animal carry the knife on *shabbat* would not involve using a consecrated animal for labor. But this immediately raises a new problem: *pesach v'shabbat heichi matzi makdish lei* — how could you sanctify your *korban pesach* on *shabbat* itself? A *mishnah* teaches explicitly that one may not perform acts of *hekdesh* — consecration — on *yom tov*, and certainly not on *shabbat*. The prohibition encompasses sanctifying, assigning monetary valuations (*ma'arich*), *hacharama*, and the separation of *terumah* and *ma'aser*.

The *gemara* resolves this: *hani mili b'chovah she'ein kavua lahem zman* — that prohibition applies to obligations that do not have a fixed time. But an obligation with a fixed time — *chovah she'kavua lahem zman* — such as the *korban pesach*, which must be brought specifically on the fourteenth, may be sanctified even on *shabbat* itself. This is derived from the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: *makdish adam et pischo b'shabbat* — a person may sanctify his *pesach* on *shabbat*, and similarly may sanctify his *chagigah* on *yom tov* itself.

## Gemara Analysis

### Gezerah Shavah Analysis

The *gemara* now steps back to analyze the structure of Hillel's argument. *Amar mar* — we return to the text of the *baraita*: *ne'emar moado b'Pesach v'ne'emar moado b'tamid* — it says *mo'ado* by *pesach* and it says *mo'ado* by *tamid*.

The *gemara* asks: where do we actually know that *tamid* overrides *shabbat*? If we say the source is simply the word *mo'ado* written in connection with the *tamid*, the same word appears in connection with *pesach* — so why would we need to derive *pesach* from *tamid* at all? The *gemara* concludes: *mo'ado lo mashma lei* — to the author of this derivation, i.e., Hillel, the word *mo'ado* by itself does not carry the implication of overriding *shabbat*. He required an independent source for *tamid*. And that source is the verse *olat Shabbat b'shabato al olat hatamid* — the *olah* of *shabbat* is brought alongside the regular *tamid*, which means the *tamid* is itself brought on *shabbat*. Since the verse describing *shabbat* uses the word *mo'ado*, and we know the *tamid* is brought on *shabbat*, we can make the *gezerah shavah* linking the *mo'ado* of *pesach* to the *mo'ado* of *tamid*.

The *gemara* then turns to the *kal vachomer*: if the *tamid*, which carries no punishment of *karet* for its neglect, overrides *shabbat*, then certainly the *pesach*, which carries *karet*, should override *shabbat*. The *gemara* immediately identifies a challenge to this *kal vachomer*: *mah l'tamid sheken tadir v'chalil* — the *tamid* has the additional factors of being offered daily (*tadir*) and being entirely consumed (*chalil*). Perhaps these factors are what enable it to override *shabbat*, and the *pesach*, which is brought only once a year and from which people eat the meat, should not be compared. The *kal vachomer* is therefore *paricha* — contradicted; it does not hold.

The *gemara* then asks: if Hillel had a valid *gezerah shavah* available, why did he first present the *kal vachomer* that turns out to be contradicted? *Kal vachomer amar lehu b'reisha* — he tried the *kal vachomer* first, and when they successfully challenged it, *hadar amar lehu gezerah shavah* — he then fell back on the *gezerah shavah*. But this raises the question: if he had the *gezerah shavah* all along, why lead with the *kal vachomer*?

The answer the *gemara* gives is: *ella l'didehu ka'amar lehu* — he was speaking for their benefit. A *gezerah shavah* is only valid if it is your received tradition; *she'ein adam dan gezerah shavah me'atzmo* — one may not independently construct a *gezerah shavah* without having received it as a *mesorah*. Clearly *B'nei Beteira* did not have this *gezerah shavah* as their tradition — if they had, the *halacha* would not have been lost to them in the first place. So Hillel first presented the *kal vachomer* because a *kal vachomer* is something *adam dan me'atzmo* — it may be derived independently through reason. He was giving them a proof they could have worked out for themselves. When they successfully rebutted it, he offered the *gezerah shavah* as his own tradition.

This leads to a striking observation: in the end, Hillel did not actually prove anything to *B'nei Beteira* by logical necessity. The *kal vachomer* was successfully refuted; the *gezerah shavah* is only as good as the tradition behind it, and they had no such tradition themselves. They were, in the end, simply accepting his word that he had received this tradition. Whether a *gezerah shavah* transmitted by one authority in one locale is universally binding is itself a debated question. The *Ramban* in fact argues that *gezerah shavah* was never a tradition about which specific words could be linked; the tradition was the *halacha* itself — that *pesach* is brought on *shabbat* — and the *gezerah shavah* was the way one could find a support or peg for it in the *pesukim*. This touches on a deeper question about the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh*, which the *gemara* will explore through a comparison with the *Yerushalmi*.

## Knife on Shabbat (II)

### Melacha Ba-Kodashim

Having resolved the question of how one sanctifies the *pesach* on *shabbat* itself, the *gemara* returns to the remaining *shabbat* problem with the knife. If the animal was unsanctified when it carried the knife, there is no violation of *melacha b'kodashim* — using a consecrated animal for labor. But there is still the prohibition of *mechamer* — using an animal to carry loads, as one would use a donkey (*chamor*). Having an animal carry something for you on *shabbat* is a form of prohibited *melacha*, even when the animal itself is not consecrated.

### Sanctification on Shabbat

As discussed, the resolution to the *hekdesh* problem — the prohibition against consecrating on *shabbat* — is that an obligation with a fixed time (*chovah she'kavua lahem zman*) such as the *korban pesach* may be sanctified on *shabbat* itself. Rabbi Yochanan states explicitly: *makdish adam et pischo b'shabbat* — a person may sanctify his *pesach* on *shabbat*, and similarly may sanctify his *chagigah* on *yom tov*.

### K'Le'Achar Yad

The remaining problem is *mechamer*. Here the *gemara* offers an important resolution: *mechamer k'le'achar yad* — the animal is carrying in an unusual manner. If one truly intended to use a sheep as a beast of burden, one would not embed the knife in its wool. If one wanted a goat to carry something, one would not attach it between its horns. The animal is performing the function of carrying, but doing so via a *shinuy* — a significant deviation from normal procedure. This reduces the act from a biblical prohibition to a rabbinic one.

But the *gemara* acknowledges that even a rabbinic prohibition remains: *issur d'Rabbanan miha ika*. And as our *mishnah* established — against the position of Rabbi Eliezer — one may not violate a rabbinic prohibition that is merely *hechsher* for the *korban pesach*, i.e., preparatory rather than the act of sacrifice itself. So how was this permitted?

Tosafot explains that two mitigating factors together made it permissible to override this *d'rabanan*. First, the act was done *k'le'achar yad* — in an unusual manner — which signals clearly that this is not normal procedure and renders the violation less severe. Second, it was not the person performing the carrying but the animal. Even though using an animal inappropriately is itself prohibited, the prohibition is less direct when the person is not personally the one performing the *melacha*. A *d'rabanan* that is indirect and performed in an unusual manner is more susceptible to being overridden for the sake of the *korban pesach* than a straightforward *d'rabanan* of the kind that our *mishnah* does not permit.

### People's Intuition

The *gemara* now ties this back to the narrative: *hainu d'ka ba'a minei* — this is precisely the question they were asking Hillel when they asked about the knife. The question was whether something that has a biblical *heter* — i.e., could in principle be done in a permitted way — but is rabbinically problematic in the manner being contemplated (*davar shevut omed l'fanav*) may be overridden in a *k'le'achar yad* fashion for the sake of the *mitzvah*. And Hillel's answer, as we saw: *halacha zo shamati v'shachachti* — I heard this and I have forgotten it. *Ela hanichu lahem l'Yisrael im einam nevi'im hem b'nei nevi'im hem* — leave it to Israel; if they are not prophets, they are descendants of prophets, and they will arrive at the truth through their own religious intuition.

## Gemara Analysis (II)

### Bavli vs. Yerushalmi

The *Yerushalmi* contains a parallel version of this entire story, but with significant differences that illuminate a much deeper dispute about the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh* itself. It is worth comparing the two carefully.

The *Yerushalmi* opens similarly: this *halacha* was lost to the elders of *Beteira* (*ziknei Beteira*, not *B'nei Beteira*). They are told there is a certain Babylonian — simply *Bavli echad*, not even named at first — who studied under Shemaya and Avtalyon, and perhaps there is hope to be had from him. They summon him, he gives the same dramatic opening about there being more than one *pesach* that overrides *shabbat*, and the elders respond approvingly: yes, indeed we said there might be hope from you.

But what follows is entirely different. The *Yerushalmi* explicitly states that Hillel then proceeded to prove his case *mehekesh umikal vachomer umigzeirah shavah* — from an analogy, a *kal vachomer*, and a *gezerah shavah*. And critically, the *Yerushalmi* identifies all three *before* presenting the content, deliberately foregrounding the hermeneutic tools being deployed. The *hekesh* — a conceptual analogy rather than a verbal link — runs as follows: just as the *tamid* is a *korban tzibur*, a communal offering, and the *pesach* is likewise a *korban tzibur*, they are analogous and the *tamid*'s override of *shabbat* applies equally to the *pesach*. Then comes the *kal vachomer* as in our *gemara*, and finally the *gezerah shavah* of *mo'ado mo'ado*.

Now notice the *Yerushalmi*'s response to all three arguments: *yesh lo teshuvah* — each one can be answered. The *hekesh* has a rebuttal; the *kal vachomer* has a rebuttal; and the *gezerah shavah* — *she'ein adam dan gezerah shavah me'atzmo* — you cannot construct that on your own. Every argument he offered, they dismissed.

In the *Yerushalmi*'s account, Hillel then spent the entire day — *kol hayom kulo* — not teaching *halachot* and holding court as the new *nasi*, but trying to convince them. *Lo kiblu mimenu* — they refused to accept anything he said. Finally, at the end of the day, he said: *yavo alay kach shamati miShemaya v'Avtalyon* — let it come upon me, this is what I received as a tradition from Shemaya and Avtalyon. The moment they heard this — *keivan shesham'u mimenu ken amdu u'minuhu nasi aleihem* — they immediately stood and appointed him *nasi*.

This is a completely different story. In the *Yerushalmi*, all the logical derivations, all the hermeneutics, meant nothing to them. What mattered was the *mesorah* — do you have this as a received tradition from an authoritative teacher? The moment he invoked the direct chain of transmission from Shemaya and Avtalyon, they accepted him entirely. The story ends the same way — with him mocking them and then forgetting the *halacha* about the knife, and being forced to say *kach shamati v'shachachti*. Even there, the resolution comes through the invocation of tradition.

This contrast between the *Bavli* and the *Yerushalmi* versions is not merely a narrative variation. It encodes a fundamental dispute about what *Torah she-be'al peh* is. In the *Yerushalmi*'s telling, *Torah she-be'al peh* is primarily about *mesorah* — the transmission of a received tradition from teacher to student, generation to generation. Logical proofs and hermeneutic derivations, however sophisticated, are not authoritative in themselves. What is authoritative is: who taught you, and can you trace your teaching back to an unbroken chain of transmission?

In the *Bavli*'s telling, by contrast, what impressed *B'nei Beteira* was Hillel's ability to derive the *halacha* through logic and hermeneutic principles. The *kal vachomer* alone ultimately failed, but the *gezerah shavah* was accepted, and the whole framework of logical derivation was treated as genuinely probative.

Hillel comes from Bavel. In Bavel, the tradition had always leaned more toward the creative, intellectually generative model of *Torah she-be'al peh* — the *yud-gimmel middot*, the hermeneutic principles, the derivation of *halacha* from first principles. The chain of direct transmission from teachers in *Eretz Yisrael* was more attenuated in Bavel; necessity itself drove a more derivation-based approach. *B'nei Beteira* represent the *Mikdash*-centered, tradition-bound model — this is how we have always done it, and we need to know that it is what was always done.

What Hillel brought to *Eretz Yisrael* was precisely the Babylonian *Torah she-be'al peh* — and this was the founding moment of the rabbinic leadership that would define the tradition going forward, from Hillel's own dynasty through Rabbi Akiva's school and ultimately to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the redaction of the *mishnah*. The whole subsequent rabbinic project — a *Torah she-be'al peh* grounded in derivation, interpretation, and the creative application of hermeneutic principles — has its symbolic origin in this encounter.

There is also a thread worth noting within the story itself that complicates a pure *mesorah*-only view: the story begins precisely because something was *forgotten*. The whole problem is that even received traditions can be lost. And in the resolution, Hillel says: trust the people's intuition. *Im einam nevi'im hem b'nei nevi'im hem*. They are not using the *yud-gimmel middot*; they are not applying *gezerah shavah*; and they do not have a tradition. Yet they arrive at the correct answer through religious intuition. This too is a form of arriving at truth that is neither pure derivation nor pure *mesorah* — and Hillel, the arch-proponent of the derivation model, endorses it.

There is an important *tosefta* in Sanhedrin that illuminates this further. It describes seven things Hillel expounded before *B'nei Beteira* — and the seven things are precisely his seven hermeneutic principles: *kal vachomer*, *gezerah shavah*, *binyan av mikatuv echad*, *binyan av mishnei ketuvim*, *kelal u'frat*, *prat u'chelal*, and *davar halamed me'inyano*. What he was expounding was not merely the *halacha* about *pesach* on *shabbat* — he was teaching them the very methodology by which *halacha* is derived. He was, in effect, presenting a manifesto for a new kind of *Torah she-be'al peh*.

### Seven Principles

The *tosefta* in Sanhedrin states: *shiv'ah devarim darash Hillel haZaken lifnei B'nei Beteira* — seven things Hillel the Elder expounded before *B'nei Beteira*. The seven are enumerated as: *kal vachomer*, *gezerah shavah*, *binyan av mikatuv echad*, *binyan av mishnei ketuvim*, *kelal u'frat*, *prat u'chelal*, *davar halamed me'inyano* — and also *kayyotzei bo mimakom acher*, a case similar to another case from elsewhere. That accounts for all seven.

The significance of the *tosefta*'s phrasing is the word *darash* — he was expounding, teaching them actively. What was he teaching them? Not just the specific *halacha* about *pesach* and *shabbat*, but the framework: here are the authorized principles of derivation by which *halacha* is legitimately determined. This is the deeper revolution that Hillel represented. *B'nei Beteira* operated in a world where authority meant transmission; Hillel was introducing — or formalizing and championing — a world in which authority could also mean disciplined, principled derivation.

The profound question this raises — whether *Torah she-be'al peh* is primarily a repository of transmitted laws or primarily an authorized interpretive process given to human beings — is one that runs throughout the entire *talmud*. The *Rambam* in his *Perush HaMishnayot* addresses this directly in response to the Karaite challenge: if everything was transmitted, how can there be debates on every page of the *talmud*? His answer is that what was transmitted directly — what we call *halacha l'Moshe m'Sinai* — is a tiny fraction of the tradition. The overwhelming majority was given to human beings to derive through the application of authorized principles; and because derivation by human beings is inherently not predetermined, different scholars can and do arrive at different conclusions. The debates are not a sign of confusion but of the system working as intended. The *Ramban*'s view on *gezerah shavah* — that it was never a tradition of which words to link, but rather a tradition of the *halacha* for which one then found textual support — represents a mediating position between pure derivation and pure *mesorah*.

Both of these positions are embedded in this story. The *Bavli*'s Hillel champions derivation; the *Yerushalmi*'s *B'nei Beteira* insist on *mesorah*. And the resolution — in both versions — ultimately involves both: Hillel had a tradition, which he was able to express and support through hermeneutic reasoning, and the people's intuition arrived at the same place. The episode becomes a founding narrative not just for a specific *halacha*, but for the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh* itself.

## Aggadic Stories

### Hillel's Pride Falls

After these passages, the *gemara* shifts into an *aggadic* register with a series of morally instructive narratives connected to the themes of pride and anger. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: anyone who conducts himself with haughtiness — if he is a wise person, his wisdom departs from him; if he is a prophet, his prophecy departs from him.

The proof that a wise man's wisdom departs from him on account of haughtiness is drawn directly from Hillel himself. We just saw: he mocked *B'nei Beteira* for their laziness, exalted himself for having attended upon Shemaya and Avtalyon, and was immediately made to say *halacha zo shamati v'shachachti* — I heard this and I forgot it. The very thing he boasted about — his superior learning from the great masters — was the thing he was then shown to have forgotten. The narrative works as a precise and pointed comeuppance.

### Moshe's Anger

*Reish Lakish amar*: anyone who gets angry — if he is wise, his wisdom departs from him; if he is a prophet, his prophecy departs from him. The proof that anger causes wisdom to depart comes from Moshe. The verse says *vayiktzof Moshe al pekudei hachayil* — Moshe became angry at the officers of the army when they returned from the war against Midian. After that anger, when there was a *halacha* to be transmitted — regarding the purification of vessels captured in war — it was Elazar the *kohen* who transmitted it, not Moshe: *vayomer Elazar hakohen el anshei hatzava... zot chukat hatorah asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* — this is the statute of the Torah that God commanded Moshe. The verse specifies it was commanded to Moshe, yet it was Elazar who transmitted it. The conclusion is that Moshe had forgotten it on account of his anger.

### Prophecy & Anger

The proof that anger causes prophecy to depart comes from Elisha. After expressing anger at Yehoram, Elisha said: *lulei p'nei Yehoshafat melech Yehudah ani nosei im abit elecha v'im ereka* — were it not for my regard for Yehoshafat king of Judah, I would not even look at you. He was venting his anger openly. Then immediately the verse says: *ve'ata k'chu li menagen* — and now bring me a musician — *vehaya kenagen hamenagen vatehi alav yad Hashem* — and it was only when the musician played that the spirit of God rested upon him again.

The contrast between these two examples is worth noting. The case of Elisha and music has a naturalistically comprehensible quality: extreme anger disturbs the inner equilibrium that prophecy requires; calming music restores it. This reads as something close to cause and effect in a psychological sense. The case of Hillel's haughtiness reads differently — more as divine *comeuppance*, a measure-for-measure correction.

The proof that anger caused prophecy to depart comes also from Devorah, who in her song said: *ad shekamti Devorah ad shekamti em b'Yisrael* — until I arose, Devorah, until I arose as a mother in Israel. This self-aggrandizement on her part led to her then having to push herself to prophecy: *uri uri Devorah uri uri dabri shir* — rouse yourself, Devorah, rouse yourself, speak your song — implying that prophetic inspiration did not come easily to her and she had to prod herself to attain it.

### Eliab's Story

*Amar Rav Mani bar Patish*: anyone who gets angry — even if greatness was decreed for him from heaven, he is brought down from it. This is derived from Eliab, the brother of David. The verse says *vayichar af Eliab beDavid* — Eliab became angry at David when David came to the battle camp before the confrontation with Goliath. Eliab accused David of abandoning his flock and coming out of idle curiosity or impudence: *ani yadati et zedonecha v'et roa levavecha ki lemaan re'ot hamilchama yaradta* — I know your willfulness and the evil of your heart; you came just to see the battle.

When Shmuel came to anoint the new king from among the sons of Yishai, God's response to each son he considered was: *lo beze bachar Hashem* — this is not the one God has chosen. But for Eliab specifically, the verse reads differently: *vayomer Hashem el Shmuel al tabet el marehu v'el geva komato ki me'astihu* — God said, do not look at his appearance or his height, for I have despised him. Not merely "not chosen" — *despised*.

The *gemara* reads this as follows: Eliab had indeed *been* chosen — *michlal dehavah rachim lei* — he had been selected for greatness by heaven, but was subsequently rejected. The anointing episode occurred before the scene of his anger at David, which the *gemara* acknowledges. Rashi explains that Eliab's anger in that scene was simply the latest expression of a deep-seated anger that had been there all along, and that it was this character trait that led to his rejection even prior to the Goliath episode. The language of the verse — *me'astihu*, I have despised him — echoes the language used of Saul's rejection, creating a deliberate parallel between two figures who were chosen and then removed on account of inner flaws.

## Pesach & Tumah

### Pesach Overrides Tumah

The *gemara* now pivots back to *halachic* analysis. We have established that *tamid* and *pesach* override *shabbat*. The next question is: how do we know they override *tumah*? The *gemara* reasons as follows: the same way we derived the override of *shabbat* for *pesach* from *tamid* — knowing that *tamid* overrides *shabbat* and applying that to *pesach* — we can reverse the reasoning for *tumah*: knowing that *pesach* overrides *tumah*, we can derive the same for *tamid*.

But this immediately invites the question: *pesach gufeih minlan* — where do we know that *pesach* itself overrides *tumah*? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the verse states *ish ish ki yiheyeh tameh la-nefesh* — if any individual is *tamei* due to contact with the dead, he is pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*. The *drasha* is: an individual is pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*, but the entire congregation is not pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*. If the majority of the congregation is *tamei*, they do not wait — *ella avdi be-tumah* — they perform the *korban pesach* while *tamei*. This is derived from the fact that the Torah provides an alternative (*Pesach Sheni*) only for an individual, signaling that no such alternative operates when it is the congregation as a whole.

### Reish Lakish's View

Reish Lakish challenges Rabbi Yochanan's derivation. Why read this *drasha* as a leniency for the congregation? One could equally read it as a stringency: an individual is pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*, but if the entire congregation is *tamei*, they have no recourse at all — neither *Pesach Rishon* nor *Pesach Sheni*. The derivation cuts in the opposite direction: the Torah's provision of an alternative is granted only to individuals, not to congregations — meaning the congregation is actually worse off, not better. Reish Lakish rejects the leniency reading as insufficiently grounded.

### Three Categories

Reish Lakish therefore offers an alternative source for the principle that *pesach* overrides *tumah*, and specifically derives from it an important limitation. The verse in Bemidbar states: *vishalchu min hamachaneh kol tzarua v'chol zav v'chol tamei la-nefesh* — send out of the camp all who are *tzarua*, all who are *zav*, and all who are *tamei* through contact with the dead. Why enumerate all three categories? The most severe are the *tzarua* and the *zav*, whose *tumah* arises intrinsically from their own bodies. The least severe is the *tamei meit*, whose *tumah* comes from an external source. Logic alone would have told us: if even the *tamei meit* — the least severe — must be sent away, certainly the *zav* and *metzora* must be as well. The explicit enumeration of all three must therefore be teaching something that could not have been derived by logic alone.

Reish Lakish's answer: the verse is telling us that there is a time of year when only the *zav* and *metzora* are sent out, but *tamei meitim* are not. That time is *pesach* celebrated in a state of *tumah* — *zeh pesach haba b'tumah*. The *halacha* that *pesach* overrides *tumah* applies only to *tamei meit* — the person rendered impure through contact with a corpse. It does not apply to those whose *tumah* is intrinsic, arising from their own bodies, such as the *zav* and the *metzora*. For the congregation's *tumah* to be overridden and the *korban pesach* brought, it is necessary that the *tumah* be *tumat meit* — externally contracted impurity — not the more severe, intrinsic impurities of *zivah* or *tzara'at*. For those, even a congregation must wait until they are able to bring the *korban* in purity.
