# Bava Kamma 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gezel Hager Status

### Korban Status Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: כֹּהֲנִים בְּגֶזֶל הַגֵּר – יוֹרְשִׁין הָווּ, אוֹ מְקַבְּלֵי מַתָּנוֹת הָווּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** What is the status of **priests with regard to** the restitution for **robbery of a convert? Are they** considered **heirs** of the convert **or are they recipients of gifts?**

The daf picks up on 111b, approximately twenty lines from the top, at the point of a new inquiry by Rava. Before the question itself can be understood, however, it is necessary to recall the broader context established in the preceding sugya. The Gemara has been discussing *gezel hager* — the case where someone steals from a *ger* who subsequently dies without heirs, swears falsely about it, and then wishes to make restitution. In an ordinary case of theft accompanied by a false oath, returning the principal is a prerequisite to bringing the *asham*, but the money itself does not carry any *korban*-like status. It is merely a condition that must be satisfied before the *asham* can be offered.

In *gezel hager*, however, the situation is fundamentally different. The money returned takes on a *korban*-like character in a series of striking ways: it must be returned during the day, it must be returned in full, and when the *kohanim* receive it they cannot pool it with other funds and divide it freely — each unit that was returned must be apportioned equally. The money, in other words, is treated as though it were a *korban* in its own right.

### Heirs vs Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּזַל חָמֵץ שֶׁעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח. אִי אָמְרַתְּ יוֹרְשִׁין הָווּ – הַיְינוּ הַאי דְּיָרְתִי מוֹרֵית. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מְקַבְּלֵי מַתָּנוֹת הָווּ – מַתָּנָה קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא דְּנִיתֵּיב לְהוּ, וְהָא לָא קָא יָהֵיב לְהוּ מִידֵּי – דְּעַפְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference?** The Gemara answers: The difference would be in a case **where one robbed** a convert of **leavened bread, and** then **Passover elapsed over it,** rendering it an item from which one is prohibited to derive benefit and therefore valueless. **If you say** that the priests **are heirs, this is what they inherit:** Only that which the robber **bequeaths** to them, and the priests receive the valueless leavened bread as is. **And if you say** that **they are recipients of gifts,** it is **a gift that the Merciful One is saying that** the robber **should give to them, and this** robber **is not giving them anything, for it is merely dust.** Therefore, the robber should have to pay the priests what the value of the bread had been at the time of the robbery.

Against this backdrop, Rava poses the following question: *kohanim b'gezel hager, yorshim havu o mikablei matanos havu* — when the *kohanim* receive the money returned in a case of *gezel hager*, do they receive it as heirs of the *ger*, or as recipients of a gift? The question is not about the monetary status of the *kohanim* in the abstract. As established in the preceding *mishnah*, if the person requiring *kapara* has died, the *kohanim* have no monetary claim to the money — the *yorshim* keep it, because its only function was to serve as part of *kapara*, and once there is no living person to atone for, that function lapses. The question therefore concerns only the case where the person is alive and actively giving the money to the *kohanim* as part of the *kapara* process: in what capacity do the *kohanim* receive it?

### Chametz Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְעֵירָא בָּעֵי הָכִי: אֲפִילּוּ אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר מְקַבְּלֵי מַתָּנָה הָווּ, הָא – לָא אִיבַּעְיָא לַן, דְּהַהִיא מַתָּנָה אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא דְּנִיתֵּיב לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zeira raises the dilemma like this: Even if you say** that **they are recipients of gifts, this** question, i.e., whether a robber of leavened bread over which Passover then elapsed fulfills the mitzva to return the stolen item even in this devalued state, **is not our dilemma,** as this is certainly a fulfillment of the obligation. **For this** stolen item is the **gift** with regard to which **the Merciful One states** in the Torah **that** the robber **should give** it **to** the priests.

The Gemara asks: *l'mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does it make? The proposed case is one where the *ger* had chametz stolen from him, and that chametz then *avar alav ha-Pesach* — Passover passed over it, rendering it halachically worthless. If we say the *kohanim* are *yorshim* — heirs — then the logic is that you give them exactly what you would have given back to the *ger* himself. Since you would have returned the worthless post-Pesach chametz to the *ger* (as that is what was stolen), you return the same thing to the *kohanim*, even though it has no value. But if we say the *kohanim* are *mikablei matanos* — recipients of a gift — then the Torah is directing you to give them something of real value, not merely the equivalent of what you took. On this reading, handing them the worthless chametz is no gift at all — *afra b'alma hu*, it is mere dust.

### Rav Zeira Resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי קָמִבַּעְיָא לַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ עֶשֶׂר בְּהֵמוֹת בְּגֶזֶל הַגֵּר. [מִי] מִחַיְּיבִי לְאַפְרוֹשֵׁי מִינַּיְיהוּ מַעֲשֵׂר, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Zeira continues: **Rather, when we have a dilemma** whether the priests are considered as heirs or as recipients of gifts, the practical difference arises in a case **where ten animals came into** the priest's possession **for** payment of **robbery of a convert.** The dilemma is: **Are they obligated to separate tithe from them, or not?**

Rav Zeira, however, objects to this proposed *nafka mina*. Even if you say the *kohanim* are gift-recipients, he argues, the question of the worthless chametz is not actually at issue. *Hi matana amar Rachmana d'neisivei lehu* — the gift that the Torah instructs you to give them is precisely what you would have given back to the *ger* himself. If post-Pesach chametz would have discharged your obligation to the *ger*, it likewise discharges your obligation to the *kohanim*. The question of *yorshim* versus *mikablei matanos* does not bear on what you give; it bears only on in what capacity they receive it.

### Maaser Behema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹרְשִׁין הָווּ – דְּאָמַר מָר: קָנוּ בִּתְפִיסַת הַבַּיִת – חַיָּיבִין; אוֹ דִלְמָא מְקַבְּלֵי מַתָּנוֹת הָווּ – וּתְנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ וְהַנִּיתָּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה – פָּטוּר מִמַּעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two possibilities: Perhaps **they are heirs,** in which case they will be obligated, **for the Master said** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 56b) that if heirs **acquired** animals **in** the jointly **held** property **of the estate,** i.e., the heirs jointly owned the animals as the inheritance had yet to be divided, **they are obligated** to separate tithes from animals born to those animals, and the same will apply to the priests. **Or perhaps they are recipients of gifts, and we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 55b): **One who purchases** an animal **or one who** has an animal **given to him as a gift** is **exempt from** the obligation to separate the **animal tithe,** and the same will apply to the priests. **What** is the *halakha* in this case?

The true *nafka mina*, Rav Zeira explains, involves *ma'aser beheima*. Imagine that through the mechanism of *gezel hager* a *kohen* receives ten sheep. If we say the *kohanim* are *yorshim* — a continuation of the *ger*'s ownership — then their receipt of these animals is analogous to inheriting them. Just as one who inherits animals is a continuation of the original owner and must separate *ma'aser beheima* if the original owner's obligation was still outstanding, so too the *kohen* would be obligated to separate *ma'aser beheima*. But if we say the *kohanim* are recipients of a new gift — *mekablei matanos* — then they are like someone who purchases animals or receives them as a gift, and the *mishnah* teaches explicitly that one who buys or receives animals as a gift is *patur mimaaser beheima*.

### Gezel Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה נִיתְּנוּ לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו, וְכוּלָּן נִיתְּנוּ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל, וּבְרִית מֶלַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ḥalla* 2:7–10): **Twenty-four priestly gifts were given to Aaron and to his sons, and all of them were given with** a derivation from **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization; and** with **a covenant of salt.**

The Gemara resolves the question by noting that the *beraita* calls the *kohanim*'s receipt a *matana* — a gift — just as the other priestly gifts are gifts. *Shma mina mekablei matanos havu* — we conclude that the *kohanim* receive the money in the capacity of gift-recipients, not as heirs. This makes sense in light of the broader principle: since the *kohanim* have no monetary claim to the money when *kapara* is unnecessary (as when the obligated party has died and the *yorshim* retain it), the *kohanim*'s right to it is purely functional — it flows from the *korban*-like character of the *kapara* process, not from any inheritance relationship with the *ger*.

## 24 Gifts Kehuna

### Klal Perat Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמְקַיְּימָן – כְּאִילּוּ מְקַיֵּים כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל וּבְרִית מֶלַח, כׇּל הָעוֹבֵר עֲלֵיהֶם – כְּאִילּוּ עוֹבֵר עַל כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל וּבְרִית מֶלַח.
>
> **Translation:** This serves to teach that **anyone who fulfills** the mitzva of giving the gifts of the priesthood is considered **as if he fulfills** the entire Torah, which is interpreted using the principle of **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization; and** as if he has brought all of the offerings, concerning which there is **a covenant of salt. And anyone who violates** the mitzva of giving the gifts of the priesthood is considered **as if he violates** the entire Torah, which is interpreted using the principle of **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization; and** as if he has not brought all of the offerings, concerning which there is **a covenant of salt.**

The *beraita* notes that all twenty-four were given through the hermeneutical framework of *klal u-perat u-klal* — a general statement, followed by a list of particulars, followed by another general statement. Rashi cites the relevant verse from Parashat Korach: *lechol kodshei bnei Yisrael lecha netatim lemoshcha* — for all the sanctities of Israel I have given them to you for greatness — followed by an enumeration of the gifts, and concluded with *bechol trumat hakodashim*, another general statement. These twenty-four gifts are included within that comprehensive framework.

The *beraita* adds that this covenant is characterized as *brit melach* — a covenant of salt, eternal and unbreakable. Anyone who fulfills the obligation of giving these gifts is regarded as though he has fulfilled the entire *klal u-perat u-klal* and honored the *brit melach*. Anyone who transgresses and fails to give even one of them is regarded as though he has violated the entire framework.

### Ten Mikdash Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: עֶשֶׂר בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאַרְבַּע בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְעֶשֶׂר בַּגְּבוּלִים. עֶשֶׂר בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ – חַטַּאת בְּהֵמָה, וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וְאָשָׁם וַדַּאי, וְאָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וְזִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִבּוּר, וְלוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע, וּמוֹתַר הָעוֹמֶר, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, וּשְׁיָרֵי מְנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And these are** the twenty-four gifts: There are **ten in the Temple, and four in Jerusalem, and ten in the boundaries** of Eretz Yisrael. The **ten** gifts that the priests consume only **in the Temple** are **an animal sin-offering; and a bird sin-offering; and a definite guilt-offering; and a provisional guilt-offering; and communal peace-offerings,** i.e., lambs offered on *Shavuot*; **and a *log* of oil** that accompanies the guilt-offering **of** a recovered **leper; and the surplus of the *omer*,** i.e., what remains of the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan; **and the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering of two loaves from the new wheat offered on *Shavuot*; **and the shewbread; and the leftovers of grain-offerings,** after the priests have offered the required handful.

The *beraita* divides the twenty-four gifts into three groups. The first group consists of ten gifts that apply within the *Beis Hamikdash*. These are: (1) *chatas behema* — the animal *chatas* offering, whose meat is eaten by the *kohanim*; (2) *chatas ha-of* — the bird *chatas*; (3) *asham vaday* — the *asham* brought for a definite sin, which includes *asham gezelos* (the case under discussion) as well as *me'ila* and other categories; (4) *asham taluy* — the *asham* brought for a doubtful sin, in cases where if the sin were confirmed one would bring a *chatas*; (5) *zivchei shalmei tzibur* — the communal *shelamim* offerings, most notably the two lambs brought on Shavuot, which unlike ordinary *shelamim* are *kodshei kodashim* and may be eaten only within the *Beis Hamikdash*; (6) *log shemen shel metzora* — the log of oil used in the purification *korban* of a *metzora*, which is likewise consumed only within the *azara*; (7) *mosar ha-omer* — the remainder of the *omer mincha*; (8) *shtei halechem* — the two loaves brought on Shavuot; (9) *lechem hapanim* — the showbread eaten weekly; and (10) *shiyarei menachos* — the portions left over from any *mincha* offering, which are *kodshei kodashim*.

### Four Jerusalem Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַרְבַּע בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם – הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְהַבִּיכּוּרִים, וְהַמּוּרָם מִן הַתּוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר, וְעוֹרוֹת קֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** the **four** gifts that the priests consume anywhere **in Jerusalem: The firstborn** of kosher animals; **and the first fruits; and the** portions **separated** for the priests **from the thanks-offering and the nazirite's ram; and hides of consecrated** animals.

The second group consists of four gifts associated with Yerushalayim, accessible to *kohanim* through their *korban*-related functions but not restricted to use within the *Mikdash* itself. These are: (1) *habechor* — the firstborn animal; (2) *bikurim* — the firstfruits; (3) the portions received from a *toda* offering and all *shelamim* — namely the *chazeh* and *shok*, the breast and thigh — which the *beraita* combines with the ram of the *nazir* under a single entry; and (4) *oros kodashim* — the hides of *korbanot*, including those of *kodshei kodashim* such as the *olah*.

The inclusion of the hides in this "Yerushalayim" category warrants attention. Rashi explains that the hides are not listed among the ten gifts that apply *bagevulin* — throughout the land — because they come to the *kohen* through the *korban* process, which takes place in the *Beis Hamikdash*. Yet they are also not listed among the ten gifts restricted to the *Mikdash*, because the definition of that category is items that become invalid if removed from the *kla'im* — the enclosure. The hides, once given to the *kohen*, may be taken anywhere; a *kohen* may fashion them into sandals and walk with them outside of Yerushalayim. Categorizing them as "Yerushalayim gifts" captures this intermediate status: they are given through the *Mikdash* process but are not restricted in use to that space.

### Ten Throughout Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַעֲשָׂרָה בַּגְּבוּלִין: תְּרוּמָה, וּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר, וְחַלָּה, וְרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז, וְהַמַּתָּנוֹת, וּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן, וּפִדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר, וּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, וּשְׂדֵה חֲרָמִים, וְגֶזֶל הַגֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And ten** gifts that the priests consume anywhere **in the boundaries** of Eretz Yisrael: ***Teruma*,** i.e., the portion of the produce designated for the priest; **and *teruma* of the tithe,** which the Levite separates from the tithe he receives and gives to a priest; **and *ḥalla*,** i.e., the portion of dough of the five main grains designated for the priest; **and the first of the sheared** wool; **and the gifts** of non-sacrificial, slaughtered animals, namely, the right foreleg, the cheeks, and the maw; **and** money given for the **redemption of** the firstborn **son; and** a sheep or goat given as **redemption of the firstborn donkey; and** a consecrated **ancestral field** the priests receive in the Jubilee Year; **and a dedicated field; and** payment for **robbery of a convert** who died without heirs.

The third group consists of ten gifts that apply *bagevulin* — throughout the land of Israel. These are: (1) *terumah* — the first portion of grain; (2) *trumas ma'aser* — the tenth of the tithe that a *Levi* receives, which he in turn gives to a *kohen*; (3) *challah* — the portion of dough separated before baking; (4) *reishis hagez* — the first of the fleece-shearings; (5) *hamatanos* — the *zeroah, l'chayayim v'keivah*, the foreleg, cheeks, and maw given to the *kohen* when an animal is slaughtered; (6) *pidyon haben* — the five *shekalim* given to redeem a firstborn son; (7) *pidyon peter chamor* — the sheep given to redeem a firstborn donkey; (8) *sdei achuza* — the ancestral field that is sanctified and not redeemed, which passes to the *kohanim*; (9) *sdei charamim* — a field sanctified through *cherem*; and (10) *gezel hager* — the money returned in the case of *gezel hager*, which goes to the *kohanim*.

The inclusion of *gezel hager* in the *bagevulin* category is striking, and Tosafos explicitly flags it as a problem. The *beraita* places *gezel hager* alongside gifts like *terumah* and *challah* that can be given to any *kohen* anywhere in Israel. Yet throughout the preceding discussion, the Gemara has treated the returned money as possessing *korban*-like qualities: it cannot be given at night, it cannot be given in installments, and it must be distributed equally within the *mishmar*. The *mishmar* framework itself implies a connection to the rotating service groups who function in the *Mikdash*. Why then is *gezel hager* categorized as a gift *bagevulin* rather than one requiring delivery in Yerushalayim?

Tosafos poses this question directly, asking why *gezel hager* is counted among the ten *bagevulin* gifts, given Rava's ruling that if the money was returned at night or only in part it is invalid — just as would be the case with an *asham*. By the same logic, if you gave it outside of the *Beis Hamikdash*, should it not likewise be invalid?

Rashi addresses the problem differently. In his view, *gezel hager* is called a *gvulin* gift because you may give it to the *kohen* of the *mishmar* even outside of Yerushalayim — provided, however, that it is given to the *kohen* whose *mishmar* is currently on duty. Rashi adds that if the *kohen* happens to be home rather than serving in Yerushalayim that week, receiving the money outside the city is still valid, because it is his *mishmar* that is serving. The remarkable implication, as Rashi reads it, is that while the money carries all the formal characteristics of a *korban* in terms of timing, wholeness, and equal division, the act of giving it need not occur within Yerushalayim, as long as it is directed to the currently serving *mishmar*. The question of precisely where the act of giving must take place thus remains, in the words of the discussion, unresolved in a fully satisfying way. It is genuinely strange that the money is treated as *korban*-like in all these respects and yet the requirement that characterizes virtually every other *korban* — that it be brought to Yerushalayim — is apparently not applied here.

## Abbaye Partial Kapara

### Abbaye Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן אֶת הַכֶּסֶף לְאַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר [וְכוּ׳]. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, כֶּסֶף מְכַפֵּר מֶחֱצָה. דְּאִי לָא מְכַפֵּר, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַהְדַּר לְיוֹרְשִׁין. מַאי טַעְמָא? אַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי לָא יְהַב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he **gave the money to the members of the priestly watch** and then died before they sacrificed his guilt-offering, the heirs cannot remove the money from the priests' possession. **Abaye said: Learn from** this mishna that **monetary restitution** for the robbery **atones** for **half** of the sin, **for if** it **does not atone** at all, and atonement is not achieved until the guilt-offering is sacrificed, **I would say** that in the case of robbery of a convert, if the guilt-offering is not brought the priest **returns** the money **to the** robber's **heirs. What is the reason** I would say this? Because **he did not give** the money **to** the priests **with this intention** of giving the money and not achieving atonement at all, and it would be a mistaken transaction.

The *mishnah* addresses a case where the *gazlan* gives the money to the *mishmar* and then dies. In light of what has been established — that the *kohanim*'s right to the money exists only when it functions as part of *kapara*, and that if the *gazlan* dies before giving it the *yorshim* retain it — one might think the *yorshim* could demand the money back: their father died, the *kapara* was never completed, and the money was given on the condition that it would serve as *kapara*. The *mishnah* rules that the *yorshim* cannot make this claim. From this ruling, Abbaye derives a significant conclusion: *shma mina kesef mechaper mechtza* — the money achieves partial atonement in its own right.

The logic of Abbaye's proof is as follows. If the money accomplished nothing independently — if *kapara* was achieved solely by the combined force of the money and the *asham*, with neither having effect on its own — then the *yorshim* would have a compelling argument: the money was given under the assumption that *kapara* would be achieved, that assumption proved false, and therefore the *kohanim* should return what was given to them on false pretenses. *Adaata de-hachi lo yahav lei* — had the *gazlan* known that the *kapara* would not be completed, he would not have given the money. The fact that the *mishnah* nonetheless rules against the *yorshim* proves, in Abbaye's view, that the money did accomplish something — it achieved *kapara* in part.

### Future Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ תִּיפּוֹק לְחוּלִּין – דְּאַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי לָא אַפְרְשַׁהּ! אָמְרִי: חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ, הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ דִּלְמִיתָה אָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** it would follow that **a sin-offering whose owners have died,** leaving no one to bring the offering, **should be transferred to non-sacred** status, **as** the robber **did not separate** the animal as an offering **with this intention** of its not being sacrificed to atone for his sin. The Sages **say** in response: In the case of **a sin-offering whose owners have died,** the Sages **learned this *halakha* through tradition that** the animal **is left to die.**

The Gemara, however, challenges the premise of Abbaye's argument. Abbaye assumes that when someone acts without explicitly articulating the conditions under which he does so, an unarticulated expectation — one that was never stated but that would have shaped behavior had it been anticipated — can retroactively define the transaction. The Gemara asks: is this really so? Can a person always say, *adaata de-hachi lo yahav lei* — had I known this would happen, I never would have done it — and use that unarticulated assumption to reclaim what was given?

This question has significant implications far beyond the immediate case. The extent to which an unarticulated *da'at* — a privately held assumption that was never expressed — can void a transaction is a question that arises in discussions of *nedarim*, *kiddushin*, and matters touching on *agunah*. The Gemara proceeds to test the proposition through a series of parallel cases.

### Kodashim Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אָשָׁם שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלָיו לִיפּוֹק לְחוּלִּין – דְּאַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי לָא אַפְרְשֵׁיהּ! אָשָׁם נָמֵי הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לָהּ – כֹּל שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת מֵתָה, בְּאָשָׁם רוֹעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, a guilt-offering whose owner has died should be transferred to non-sacred** status, **as** the owner **did not separate** the animal as an offering **with this intention** of it not being sacrificed to atone for his sin. The Gemara answers: With regard to **a guilt-offering,** the Sages **also learned this *halakha* through tradition: Any** occurrence **that,** if it occurs **with regard to a sin-offering** the animal is placed in isolation for it to **die,** if it occurs **with regard to a guilt-offering** the animal is left to **graze** until it develops a blemish precluding its use as an offering, at which point it can be redeemed.

The Gemara first considers the case of *chatas she-mesu be'aleha* — an animal that was sanctified as a *chatas* and then the owner died. The *halacha* is that such an animal must be left to die. Why, the Gemara asks, don't we say that the person never intended to sanctify the animal on the assumption that he would die? Had he anticipated that eventuality, he would never have separated it. If unarticulated conditions can retroactively void acts, the animal should revert to non-sacred status. The Gemara answers that the rule governing a *chatas* whose owner dies — that it goes to its death — is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, a tradition that overrides the theoretical argument. In principle the argument may have force, but the tradition governs.

The Gemara then raises the parallel case of an *asham* whose owner died. There the *halacha* is that the animal grazes until it becomes blemished, at which point it is redeemed, and the proceeds are used for an *olah*. Why not say there too that the owner never intended to sanctify it assuming he would die? The Gemara answers again: *asham nami hilchisa gemiri la* — this too is a *halacha* transmitted by tradition, rooted in the principle that whatever would require a *chatas* to be left to die requires an *asham* to graze.

### Yevama Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, יְבָמָה שֶׁנָּפְלָה לִפְנֵי מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין תִּיפּוֹק בְּלָא חֲלִיצָה, דְּאַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי לֹא קִדְּשָׁה עַצְמָהּ! הָתָם אֲנַן סָהֲדִי דְּמֵינָח נִיחָא לַהּ בְּכֹל דְּהוּ – כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: טָב לְמֵיתַב טַן דּוּ, מִלְּמֵיתַב אַרְמְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then in the case of **a woman whose husband died childless [*yevama*], who happened before** her late husband's brother who was **afflicted with boils** to enter levirate marriage with him, **should go out** free to marry **without** being required to perform **the ritual through which the *yavam* frees the *yevama* of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*]. For she did not betroth herself** to this man's deceased brother **with this intention** of having a levirate bond with a man afflicted with boils. The Gemara answers: **There, it is clear to us** **that it is satisfactory for her to** have **any** kind of marital arrangement, and even had she taken into consideration the possibility of entering a levirate bond with this *yavam* who is afflicted with boils she still would have accepted betrothal to her husband. This is **in accordance with** the statement of **Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish says** that women have a saying: **It is better to sit** as **two bodies [*tan du*],** i.e., to be married, **than to sit** lonely like **a widow.**

The Gemara then raises a case outside the realm of *kodashim* where the *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* argument is unavailable. *Yevama she-nafla lifnei mukeh shechin* — a woman whose husband dies and she falls into *yibum* before a brother-in-law who is afflicted with a repulsive physical condition. Ought she be permitted to walk away without even requiring *chalitza*? After all, *adaata de-hachi lo kidshta atzma* — had she known at the time of marriage that her husband would die and leave her bound to this brother-in-law, she never would have agreed to the marriage. If unarticulated assumptions can retroactively undo transactions, her *kiddushin* should be regarded as never having fully taken effect.

The Gemara rejects this argument on a different ground. *Anan sahadei d'minach nicha lah b'chol d'hu* — we know as a matter of fact that even had she been able to foresee the future, she still would have preferred to be married at the time, even knowing what lay ahead. As Reish Lakish states: *tav le-meitav tan du mi-le-meitav armelu* — it is better to dwell as two than to dwell as a widow. The threshold for claiming that a woman would have refused marriage even with full foreknowledge of what might come is very high. Without meeting that threshold, the unarticulated assumption cannot undo the *kiddushin*.

The relevance of this sugya to *agunah* discussions is apparent. The argument that "had I known my husband would withhold the *get* I never would have married him" encounters the same Talmudic response: the presumption is that a woman would still have chosen marriage, so the unarticulated condition cannot retroactively dissolve the *kiddushin*.

### Tosafos Unilateral

Tosafos draws a significant distinction in explaining why the Gemara's cases are not fully parallel to one another. Tosafos observes that the *kodashim* cases — sanctifying an animal as a *chatas* or *asham* — are unilateral acts. There is no other party to the transaction; the sanctification is accomplished by the individual alone. In bilateral transactions, *dvarim she-belev einam dvarim* — what is in one's heart and left unarticulated is legally irrelevant, because the other party to the transaction has rights that cannot be retroactively impaired by the first party's private assumptions. In a unilateral act, however, there may be greater latitude to allow an unarticulated condition to define the scope of the act.

The *yevama* case falls into this category as well, Tosafos explains, because the husband is already dead. The claim that the woman never would have entered the marriage under these circumstances is not impacting a living counterparty; the husband is gone, and the only practical effect is to free her from the *yibum* obligation to the brother-in-law. When there is no living party on the other side of the transaction, the argument from unarticulated assumption carries more weight than it would in a standard bilateral exchange.

### Context vs Condition

This analysis raises a further difficulty with the Gemara's challenge to Abbaye that deserves attention. All the cases the Gemara cites involve a completed transaction — sanctifying an animal as a *chatas*, entering into *kiddushin* — where some subsequent unforeseen development is being invoked to undo what was done. In those cases, the act was precisely what it was defined to be, and it is only some external future event that the party wishes to use to retroactively redefine their intent.

Abbaye's case, however, seems structurally different. The *gazlan* never gave the money as a simple gift. He gave it in the context of *kapara* — as a component of the atonement process. It was not a *stam matana*. If we conceive of it as similar to a down payment in a transaction that was never completed, then the question is not whether some unarticulated future assumption should undo an otherwise completed transaction. Rather, the money was given entirely within the context of *kapara*, and if *kapara* was never achieved, the very purpose for which it was given was never realized. Why the Gemara treats this as parallel to the *kodashim* and *yevama* cases — where the underlying act itself was complete and some external factor is being invoked retroactively — is not fully clear, and the parallel seems difficult to sustain.

## Order Money/Korban

### Normal Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן אֶת הַכֶּסֶף לִיהוֹיָרִיב וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָתַן אָשָׁם לִיהוֹיָרִיב וְכֶסֶף לִידַעְיָה – יַחֲזִיר כֶּסֶף אֵצֶל אָשָׁם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יַחֲזִיר אָשָׁם אֵצֶל כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the robber **gave the money to** the priestly watch of **Joiarib** and then gave the guilt-offering to the priestly watch of Jedaiah, the following priestly watch, to sacrifice on his behalf, he has fulfilled his obligation. By contrast, if he first gave the guilt-offering to the priestly watch of Joiarib and then gave the money to the priestly watch of Jedaiah, if the guilt-offering is extant, then members of the priestly watch of Jedaiah, who received the money, should sacrifice it. The Gemara quotes a *baraita* that records a dispute between *tanna'im* concerning this case. **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 10:18): If the robber **gave the guilt-offering to** the priestly watch of **Joiarib and** then gave **the money to** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah,** they **should return the money** to be **with the guilt-offering,** i.e., with the priestly watch of Joiarib; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say** the opposite: They **should return the guilt-offering** to be **with the money,** and the priestly watch of Jedaiah will sacrifice it.

The *mishnah* establishes the basic rule for the order in which the money and the *asham* must be given. The *gazlan* gives the money to one *mishmar* of *kohanim*, and the *asham* is then offered by that same *mishmar*. This is the normal sequence: money first, then *korban*, both to the same group.

### Reversed Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ אָשָׁם לִיהוֹיָרִיב בְּמִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ דִיהוֹיָרִיב, וְכֶסֶף לִידַעְיָה בְּמִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ דִידַעְיָה – זֶה זָכָה בְּשֶׁלּוֹ וְזֶה זָכָה בְּשֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this dispute? **If we say that he gave the guilt-offering to** the priestly watch of **Joiarib during the priestly watch of Joiarib and the money to** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah during the priestly watch of Jedaiah,** then **this** one **acquired that which belongs to it and that** one **acquired that which belongs to it.** Why would the court remove what was given lawfully to the priestly watch?

A *beraita*, however, addresses a case where the order is reversed. The *asham* was given to group one — Yehoyariv, the first *mishmar* — and the money was given to group two, Yedaya. What is to be done?

### Yehudah vs Hachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּיָהֵיב אָשָׁם לִיהוֹיָרִיב בְּמִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ דִיהוֹיָרִיב, וְכֶסֶף לִידַעְיָה בְּמִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ דִיהוֹיָרִיב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו מִשְׁמַרְתּ[וֹ] דִּידַעְיָה הִיא – לִידַעְיָה קָנְסִינַן לֵיהּ, הִלְכָּךְ יַחֲזִיר כֶּסֶף אֵצֶל אָשָׁם; וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין (הוּא) עֲבוּד בְּנֵי יְהוֹיָרִיב דְּקַיבִּלוּ אָשָׁם מִקַּמֵּי כֶסֶף, הִלְכָּךְ לְדִידְהוּ קָנְסִינַן לְהוּ, וְיַחְזוֹר אָשָׁם אֵצֶל כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he gave the guilt-offering to** the priestly watch of **Joiarib during the priestly watch of Joiarib and the money to** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah** also **during the priestly watch of Joiarib,** and the dispute is as follows: **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **since this is not the priestly watch of Jedaiah, we penalize Jedaiah; therefore,** the priestly watch of Jedaiah must **return the money** in their possession **to be with the guilt-offering** held by the Joiarib watch. **And the Rabbis hold** that **the members of** the priestly watch of **Joiarib acted unlawfully when they accepted the guilt-offering before** the robber paid **the money,** since payment for the robbery must be given before the guilt-offering can be offered. **Therefore, we penalize them, and the guilt-offering** in their possession **returns** to be **with the money** held by the Jedaiah watch.

Rabbi Yehudah rules: *yachzir kesef etzel asham* — the money should be returned to follow the *asham*. That is, group two should return the money to group one, so that the money and the *asham* are in the same *mishmar*'s hands. The *chachamim* rule the opposite: *yachzir asham etzel kesef* — the *asham* follows the money. Group one gives the *asham* to group two, and group two brings the *korban*.

### Rava Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אִם קָדְמוּ בְּנֵי יְהוֹיָרִיב וְהִקְרִיבוּ אֶת הָאָשָׁם – יַחְזוֹר וְיָבִיא אָשָׁם אַחֵר וְיַקְרִיבוּהוּ בְּנֵי יְדַעְיָה, וְזָכוּ הַלָּלוּ בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּיָדָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* on this topic (*Tosefta* 10:18): **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: According to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** that if during the Joiarib watch the robber gave the guilt-offering to the Joiarib watch and the money to the Jedaiah watch, the money should return to the priestly watch of Joiarib, **if the members of** the priestly watch of **Joiarib went first and sacrificed the guilt-offering** before receiving the money from the priestly watch of Jedaiah, then the robber **should go back and bring another guilt-offering, and the members of** the priestly watch of **Jedaiah,** who are already in possession of the money, **should offer it, and those** members of the priestly watch of Joiarib **acquired that** offering **that is in their possession.**

The *gemara* initially struggles to understand the scenario, since if the respective weeks of the two *mishmarot* have already passed, it is too late to transfer anything meaningfully. Rava clarifies: the case is where the *asham* was given to group one during its week, and the money was given to group two — also during group one's week, in advance of group two's turn. In that setting, the question of who holds what, and whose rights supersede, is still live.

According to Rabbi Yehudah, since it was not yet group two's week when they received the money, they are penalized: *le-Yedaya kensinan lah* — Yedaya must give the money back to group one. Group one will then hold both the money and the *asham*, and can bring the *korban* properly. According to the *rabbanan*, it is group one that acted wrongly: *shelo kedin avad benei Yehoyariv* — Yehoyariv should not have accepted the *asham* before the money was in hand. Because they accepted the *korban* before the money was paid, they are penalized, and the *asham* is given to group two to bring during its week.

### Group One Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָשָׁם פָּסוּל הוּא! אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say: For what** purpose is that offering **useful? It is a disqualified guilt-offering,** since it was sacrificed before the payment for the robbery was given, and had to be entirely burned on the altar. **Rava said:** The *baraita* is referring **to** acquiring **its hide,** which the members of the priestly watch of Joiarib keep.

A *beraita* adds detail to Rabbi Yehudah's position. If, while the money is being transferred back to group one, group one jumps ahead and brings the *asham* before receiving the money, the *asham* is invalid and the person must bring another *asham* — this time ensuring the money is paid first. The new *asham* would then be brought by group two, which now holds the money.

### Invalid Korban Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, אָמַר רַבִּי: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אִם קַיָּים אָשָׁם – יַחְזוֹר כֶּסֶף אֵצֶל אָשָׁם. פְּשִׁיטָא – הָכִי אִית לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: According to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda, if the guilt-offering is** still **extant,** i.e., if the priestly watch of Joiarib did not already sacrifice it, **the money** must **return** to be **with the guilt-offering.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious? This is** what Rabbi Yehuda **holds** explicitly; what novelty did Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi teach?

One might ask: if the *asham* that group one brought prematurely is invalid, what do they retain? Rava rules: *le'oro* — they receive the hide. The meat of the invalid *asham* may not be eaten, but the leather remains theirs.

This ruling prompts a challenge from Tosafos. Elsewhere the principle appears to be that the leather of a *korban pasul* cannot be used or distributed. Tosafos leaves this as a genuine difficulty. One possible resolution is that the *psul* here is of a qualitatively different kind. A standard *psul* — *pigul*, a blemish, improper *shechita* — is intrinsic to the animal itself. Here, the animal is entirely *kasher*; its only defect is that it was offered before the money was paid. It is a *psul dechutz legufo* — a deficiency external to the animal itself. In that sense it resembles a *korban shelo lishmo*, where the animal is *kasher* but the owners do not receive *kapara*. However, even this parallel is imperfect, since in a *korban shelo lishmo* the *kohanim* do eat the meat, whereas here they apparently do not. The *asham* brought before the money thus occupies an anomalous intermediate status: not invalidated intrinsically, yet not fully valid in its function — and so the *kohanim* receive the leather but not the meat. It is a strange and unresolved issue, as Tosafos acknowledges.

The deeper principle underlying the requirement that money precede *korban* is worth pausing on. It reflects the classic teaching familiar from Masechet Yoma: one cannot seek forgiveness from God before making right what was done to one's fellow. The *korban* to God cannot precede restitution to man.

### Expired Mishmarot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, אָמַר רַבִּי: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אִם קַיָּים אָשָׁם – יַחְזוֹר כֶּסֶף אֵצֶל אָשָׁם. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דִּנְפַק מִשְׁמַרְתָּם דְּהָנֵי וּדְהָנֵי, וְלָא תְּבַעוּ. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא אַחוֹלֵי גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא תָּבְעִי, לִהְדְּרוּ בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where** it happened **that the priestly watch of these and of those,** i.e., both Joiarib and Jedaiah, **exited** at the end of their Temple service, **and they did not demand** of the other the item in possession of the other watch. **Lest you say** that **they waived** their rights **in favor of each other,** so that the money stays in possession of the Jedaiah watch, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **teaches us that we say: Since** the Jedaiah watch **did not demand** the guilt-offering from the Joiarib watch after the latter exited, **let them go back to the first,** standard scenario, returning the money to Joiarib to be with the guilt-offering.

The Gemara then addresses a further complication. What if both groups' weeks have expired and neither group made any demands of the other — group one never demanded the money from group two, and group two never demanded the *asham* from group one?

One might think the parties have been implicitly *mochel* to one another: group two keeps the money, group one keeps the *asham*, and when group one's turn comes around again in the cycle they will bring the *asham* after the money has already been in group two's possession. But the Gemara rules otherwise: *keivan delo tavu lehadadi kam bereisha* — since neither side made demands, the default rule reasserts itself, and the money follows the *korban*. Group two must return the money to group one.

The precise practicalities of how this plays out across the twenty-four week cycle of *mishmarot* are not fully spelled out in the Gemara, and certain logistical questions — such as the age restrictions on *korban* animals and the mechanics of holding a sanctified animal across multiple *mishmar* rotations — are left open.

## Scripture Order

### Rava Scripture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהַמֵּבִיא גְּזֵילוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ [וְכוּ׳]. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רָבָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הָאָשָׁם הַמּוּשָׁב לַה׳ לַכֹּהֵן, מִלְּבַד אֵיל הַכִּפֻּרִים אֲשֶׁר יְכַפֶּר בּוֹ״ – מִכְּלָל דְּכֶסֶף בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **For one who brings his stolen item** to the priests **before he brings his guilt-offering** has fulfilled his obligation, but one who brings his guilt-offering before he brings his stolen item has not fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rava said:** This is **as the verse states:** "But if the man has no kinsman to whom restitution may be made for the guilt, **the restitution for guilt that is made shall be the Lord's, even the priest's; besides the ram of the atonement, whereby atonement shall be made for him"** (Numbers 5:8). Learn **by inference that the money** must be returned **first,** before the guilt-offering is brought.

The Gemara now turns to the scriptural basis for the rule that the money must precede the *asham*. Rava derives it from the verse in the *parasha* of *gezel hager*: *ha'asham hamushav la-Hashem la-kohen milvad eil ha-kippurim asher yechaper bo alav* — "the *asham* that is returned to God, to the *kohen*, in addition to the ram of atonement with which he shall be atoned." Rava reads *ha'asham* here as referring to the money — the principal that is returned — and the phrase *milvad eil ha-kippurim* ("in addition to the ram of atonement") as establishing that the money comes first, since it is mentioned prior to the ram.

### Objection Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״מִלְּבַד עֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר״ – הָכִי נָמֵי מִכְּלָל דְּמוּסָפִין בְּרֵישָׁא?
>
> **Translation:** Having understood that the inference is that the wording "besides the ram of the atonement" indicates that the ram must be brought after the money is given, **one of the Sages said to Rava: If that is so,** then in a different verse, which states concerning the additional offerings sacrificed on the first day of Passover: "You shall offer these **besides the burnt-offering of the morning,** which is for a continual burnt-offering" (Numbers 28:23), **so too** should one learn **by inference that the additional offerings** are brought **first,** before the morning burnt-offering?

One of the *rabbanan* challenges this reading. If *milvad* — "in addition to" — means that whatever follows must come second, then the verse describing the *musaf* offering, *milvad olat haboker asher le-olat hatamid* ("in addition to the morning *olah*"), would imply that the *musaf* comes before the morning *tamid*. But we know from a *beraita* that nothing precedes the morning *tamid*: *ve-arach aleha ha-ola* — the *olah* is the thing that inaugurates the altar each morning. The word *milvad* alone, therefore, cannot prove order.

### Future Tense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר קוֹדֵם לְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָרַךְ עָלֶיהָ הָעֹלָה״, וְאָמַר רָבָא: ״הָעוֹלָה״ – עוֹלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that no** sacrifice **shall precede the daily morning offering? The verse states:** "And the fire on the altar shall be kept burning on it, it shall not be extinguished; and the priest shall kindle wood upon it every morning, and **he shall prepare the burnt-offering upon it** and shall cause the fats of the peace-offerings to go up in smoke upon it" (Leviticus 6:5). **And Rava says: "The burnt-offering,"** with the definite article, is referring to **the first burnt-offering,** i.e., the daily morning offering, which is first both chronologically and in terms of importance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא מֵ״אֲשֶׁר יְכַפֵּר בּוֹ״ נָפְקָא לֵיהּ, וַעֲדַיִין לֹא כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** I do not hold that the *tanna* derives this *halakha* from the wording of: "Besides the ram of the atonement," but **I** hold that the *tanna* **derives it from** the phrase in the same verse: **"Whereby atonement shall be made for him"** (Numbers 5:8), which is written in the future tense, indicating that he will achieve atonement in the future by bringing the ram of atonement, **but** as of now, when he is giving payment for the robbery, the offering **did not yet atone** for his sin.

Rava responds that he does not derive the order from *milvad* alone. The decisive phrase is *asher yechaper bo* — "with which he *will* be atoned," in the future tense. The *korban* has not yet been brought at the time the verse speaks of returning the money. The verse thus presents the money as the present act and the ram as the future act — establishing that the money must come first.

### Learn from Meila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם הֵבִיא מְעִילָתוֹ וְלֹא הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ; אֲשָׁמוֹ וְלֹא הֵבִיא מְעִילָתוֹ – שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **he gave the principal to him** but did not yet give the additional one-fifth payment, his not giving it does not preclude sacrificing the offering. The Gemara now clarifies the source of this *halakha*. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* on the topic of the guilt-offering brought by one who misuses consecrated property, which is accompanied by repayment of the value of the item and an additional fifth of its value: **From where** is it derived **that if he brought his** payment for his benefit from **misuse** of consecrated property **but did not bring his guilt-offering,** or if he brought **his guilt-offering but did not bring his** payment for his benefit from **misuse** of consecrated property, **that he did not fulfill** his obligation?

The Gemara then draws a parallel to the laws of *me'ila*. In a case of *me'ila* — misappropriating sanctified property — the offender must likewise return the principal and a *chomesh* and bring an *asham*. How do we know that bringing the *asham* before paying the principal is ineffective? From the verse *be-eil ha-asham ve-nislach lo* — "with the ram of the *asham* he shall be forgiven" — which is read as meaning: the word *asham* in this verse refers to the money (the principal), and only together with the *eil* (the ram) does one achieve forgiveness. But how do we know the word *asham* in the *me'ila* verse means the principal rather than the *korban*? Because in the *gezel hager* verse, the word *asham* is unambiguously the money — the verse says "the *asham*... in addition to the ram," making clear that *asham* and *eil* are distinct. From this the Gemara derives that in the *me'ila* verse as well, *asham* denotes the principal.

### Chomesh Not Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאֵיל הָאָשָׁם וְנִסְלַח לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **The verse states: "With the ram of the guilt-offering [*ha'asham*], and he shall be forgiven"** (Leviticus 5:16). The *baraita* interprets the phrase: "The ram of the guilt-offering," to be referring to two entities: The ram, i.e., the offering, and the payment, represented by the words "the guilt-offering"; this teaches that forgiveness and atonement are achieved only after both the ram of the guilt-offering is sacrificed and the restitution of the monetary payment is given.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא מְעִילָתוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאֵיל הָאָשָׁם״ – הָאָשָׁם בִּכְבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And from where** is it derived **that if he brought his guilt-offering before bringing his** payment for his benefit from **misuse** of consecrated property **that he did not fulfill** his obligation? **The verse states: "With the ram of the guilt-offering,"** indicating that **the guilt-offering was already** brought.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאַיִל וְאָשָׁם מְעַכְּבִים, כָּךְ חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאֵיל הָאָשָׁם וְנִסְלַח לוֹ״ – אַיִל וְאָשָׁם מְעַכְּבִים בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְאֵין חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **just as the ram and guilt-offering,** i.e., payment, **preclude** his achieving atonement, **so too,** payment of the additional **one-fifth** of the value **precludes** his achieving it. Therefore, **the verse states: "With the ram of the guilt-offering, and he shall be forgiven,"** which teaches that **the ram and guilt-offering,** i.e., payment, **preclude** his achieving atonement **with regard to consecrated** property, **but** the payment of the additional **one-fifth does not preclude** his achieving it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיִלְמַד הֶקְדֵּשׁ מֵהֶדְיוֹט, וְהֶדְיוֹט מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And let** the *halakha* of misuse of **consecrated** property **be learned from** the *halakha* of **common** money, i.e., payment for robbery of a convert who died without heirs, **and** let the *halakha* of **common** money be learned **from** the *halakha* of misuse of **consecrated** property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶקְדֵּשׁ מֵהֶדְיוֹט – מָה ״אָשָׁם״ דְּהָתָם קֶרֶן, אַף ״אָשָׁם״ דְּהָכָא קֶרֶן. וְהֶדְיוֹט מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ – מָה הֶקְדֵּשׁ אֵין חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב, אַף הֶדְיוֹט נָמֵי אֵין חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** In what manner? The *halakha* of misuse of **consecrated** property can be learned **from** the *halakha* of **common** money as follows: **Just as** the word **"guilt" that is** written **there,** in the context of robbery of a convert, is referring to the **principal,** i.e., the payment itself, **so too,** the word **"guilt" that is** written **here,** in the context of misuse of consecrated property, is referring to the **principal. And** the *halakha* of **common** money can be learned **from** the *halakha* of misuse of **consecrated** property as follows: **Just as** with regard to the *halakha* of misuse of **consecrated** property the additional **one-fifth** payment **does not preclude** his achieving atonement, **so too,** with regard to the *halakha* of **common** money **as well,** the additional **one-fifth** payment **does not preclude** his achieving atonement.

Finally, the Gemara addresses whether the *chomesh* — the additional fifth — is *me-akev*, meaning whether failure to pay it prevents *kapara*. The *beraita* teaches from the verse *be-eil ha-asham ve-nislach lo* that only the *eil* (the ram) and the *asham* (the principal) are indispensable — not the *chomesh*. While the obligation to pay the *chomesh* exists, its non-payment does not block atonement.

This rule is then extended cross-textually: *ve-yilmed hekdesh me-hedyot ve-hedyot me-hekdesh* — *me'ila* and *gezel hager* illuminate each other. From *gezel hager* we learn how to read the word *asham* in the *me'ila* verse as referring to the principal. And from *me'ila* we learn that in *gezel hager* as well, the *chomesh* is not *me-akev* — the *chomesh*, while obligatory, does not stand between the offender and his *kapara*. Put another way: a monetary penalty (*knas*) is not *me-akev* one's *kapara*.

With this, the daf concludes the chapter *ha-gozel eitzim*, and the Gemara recites *Hadran*.

## Final Chapter Intro

### Back to Gezeilah

The final *perek* of Bava Kamma returns us to the foundational territory of the tractate. The preceding chapters explored a specialized corner of the law — the case of *gezel hager* with its unique *korban* and its unusual treatment of the returned money. The new *perek* takes us back to classic *gezeilah*, evoking in particular the beginning of the ninth *perek*, which focused heavily on *shinuy* — the transformation of stolen objects. Whereas the ninth *perek* concentrated on *shinuy ma'aseh*, the physical alteration of an object, this final *perek* will center on *shinuy reshut* — the change of domain or possession — and the circumstances under which a *gazlan* must return the object itself, must pay its value, or is released from obligation entirely.

### Thief Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל אֶת בָּנָיו, וְהִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. וְאִם הָיָה דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it to **his children, or** who **left** a stolen item **to them** and then died, the children **are exempt from paying** the victim of the robbery after their father's death. But **if** the stolen item **was something that serves** as a legal **guarantee** of a loan, the heirs **are obligated to pay.**

The *mishnah* opens with the following case: someone steals and feeds the stolen food to his children, and then dies. The children are *patur* — exempt from paying. This is immediately surprising. If the stolen object still exists (in consumed form, as nourishment to the children), why are they not obligated to make restitution? And the *mishnah* adds a further qualification: *ve-im haya davar sheyeish bo achrayut chayavin lishalem* — if it was something that carries a lien, such as land, they are obligated to pay. Why the distinction? The *mishnah* appears to require unpacking in two very different directions — both why the children are exempt in the ordinary case and why they are liable in the case of *achrayut*. Two distinct readings of the *mishnah* will emerge from the Gemara.

## Ye'ush Reshus

### Dual Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: גָּזַל וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים, וּבָא אַחֵר וַאֲכָלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ – רָצָה מִזֶּה גּוֹבֶה, רָצָה מִזֶּה גּוֹבֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל כַּמָּה דְּלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דְּמָרֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥisda says:** If one **robbed** another, **and the owners** of the stolen item **have not** yet **despaired** of retrieving it, **and another** person **came,** took it **from** the robber **and ate it,** if the owner **wishes he may collect from this one,** i.e., the first robber, and if **he wishes he may collect from that one,** i.e., the second robber. **What is the reason** that he may collect from whomever he chooses? It is because **as long as the owners did not despair** of retrieving it, **it remains the possession of its owner,** so that when the second robber stole it from the first, he was in fact stealing from the original owner. Nevertheless, since the first robber was already obligated to return the item, his obligation remains in force and the owner may demand payment from him if he wishes.

Rav Chisda states a general principle: if someone stole an object and the owners have not yet despaired of recovering it (*lo nisyashu habealim*), and then a second party comes and consumes the object, the original owner can collect from either party — *ratza mizeh goveh, ratza mizeh goveh*. The original *gazlan* is obviously liable. But so is the person who consumed the object, even though he received it from the *gazlan*. Both are answerable to the victim.

### Not Yet Despaired

The reason, Rav Chisda explains, is that *kol kamma d'lo nisyashu habealim, b'reshusei d'marei kai* — as long as the owners have not despaired, the stolen property remains legally within the owner's domain. When the second party consumed it, he was in effect taking and destroying property that still belonged to Joe. His liability to Joe therefore runs independently of the *gazlan*'s liability.

### Mishnah Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל אֶת בָּנָיו, וְהִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא! אָמַר לְךָ רַב חִסְדָּא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this opinion based on what **we learned** in the mishna: In the case of **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it **to his children, or** one who **left** a stolen item **to them** as an inheritance, the children **are exempt from paying** the victim of the robbery after their father's death. This appears to be **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda,** who holds that one who steals from a thief is obligated to pay the owner. The Gemara answers: **Rav Ḥisda** could have **said to you: When that** mishna **is taught,** it is addressing a case where it is **after** the owners had already **despaired** of retrieving the item, whereas Rav Ḥisda was referring to a case where the owners had not yet despaired.

The *gemara* then challenges this principle from the opening *mishnah*: the *gazlan* feeds his children and dies, and the children are exempt. If the property still belongs to the original owner at the moment the children consumed it — because *ye'ush* had not yet occurred — why are they not liable, just as the second party in Rav Chisda's case is liable? Rav Chisda resolves the contradiction: *ki tanya hi l'achar ye'ush* — the *mishnah* is speaking about a case that takes place *after ye'ush*. The owner has already despaired of recovering the property by the time the children consume it. That is why they are exempt.

### Weakened Ownership

Continuing with Rav Chisda's framework, the distinction between before and after *ye'ush* maps onto a broader principle. Before *ye'ush*, the owner's claim is intact and extends to whoever comes into contact with the stolen property. After *ye'ush*, the owner's legal grip on the object has weakened to the point that those who receive or consume it cannot be held liable as thieves or tortfeasors. The *mishnah* accordingly exempts the children because, by the time they consumed the food, the owner's ownership had already been attenuated through *ye'ush*. The concept of *shinuy reshut* — a change in possession — will be developed further as the *perek* continues, exploring precisely how much the owner's claim diminishes when *ye'ush* and changes of domain combine.
