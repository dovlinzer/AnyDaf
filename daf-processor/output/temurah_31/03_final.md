# Temurah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Offspring Forbidden Status

### Mishnah & Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָאֲסוּרִים לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ וַלְדוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרִין. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: וְלַד טְרֵפָה לֹא יִקְרַב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁירָה שֶׁיָּנְקָה מִן הַטְּרֵפָה — פְּסוּלָה מֵעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ טְרֵפָה — אֵין פּוֹדִין אוֹתָן, שֶׁאֵין פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **all** animals **whose** sacrifice **on the altar is prohibited,** sacrifice of **their offspring is permitted. Rabbi Eliezer says: The offspring of an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*] shall not be sacrificed on the altar. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says: A kosher** animal **that suckled from a *tereifa* is disqualified from** sacrifice **on the altar.** With regard to **all sacrificial** animals **that became *tereifot*, one may not redeem them** and render them non-sacred, **as** their consumption is forbidden and **one does not redeem sacrificial** animals **to feed them to dogs,** as this is considered a degradation of sacrificial animals.

The daf begins approximately eight lines from the bottom of 31, with the Mishnah's general principle: *kol ha'asurin legabei mizbe'ach veladoteihen mutarin* — anything that is forbidden to the altar, its offspring are nonetheless permissible. The earlier discussion had centered on the case of the *esnan*, but the Mishnah now generalizes the rule to encompass all categories of animals disqualified from the altar: the *rovei'a* (an animal that committed bestiality), the *nirva* (one on which bestiality was performed), the *ne'evad* (an animal that was worshipped), the *muktza*, and similar cases. Their offspring, the Mishnah teaches, remain permitted.

On this ruling, however, there is a *baraita* in which Rabbi Eliezer dissents — and his dissent is notably broader than his position in the Mishnah itself. Whereas the Mishnah records Rabbi Eliezer objecting only with respect to the offspring of a *treifa*, the *baraita* cites him as forbidding the offspring of all forbidden animals across the board: *Rabbi Eliezer oser*.

### Pregnant After Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁעִיבְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִרְבְּעוּ, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר ״עוּבָּר יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא״, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי ״לָאו יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא״, אֲבָל נִרְבְּעוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף עִיבְּרוּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** This **dispute** applies in a case **where** the animals **became pregnant and then were the objects of bestiality,** thereby becoming prohibited to sacrifice on the altar. **As Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **a fetus is** considered **the thigh of its mother** and regarded as part of the animal, and is consequently prohibited like its mother. **And the Rabbis hold** that a fetus **is not** considered **the thigh of its mother. But** if the animals **were the objects of bestiality and then became pregnant, everyone agrees** that **they are permitted.**

Rav Huna bar Chinena said in the name of Rav Nachman that the dispute in the *baraita* is specifically limited to a case where the animal first became pregnant and only afterwards was subjected to the act that rendered it forbidden — for example, it was worshipped or used in bestiality while already carrying a fetus. In that scenario, Rabbi Eliezer holds *ubar yerech imo* — the fetus is legally considered a limb of the mother — and therefore whatever prohibited status attached to the mother applies equally to the fetus. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold *lav yerech imo*: the fetus retains its independent legal identity, and an act done to the mother is not done to the fetus.

If, however, the forbidden act occurred first and the animal became pregnant only afterward — meaning the fetus came into existence after the mother had already acquired her disqualified status — then, according to Rav Huna bar Chinena's reading, all authorities would agree the offspring is permitted. The question there would be whether the mother's status can somehow transfer to a fetus that was not yet in existence when the problem occurred, and on that everyone concurs it cannot. This is a logically clean and intuitive framework for the debate.

### Forbidden After Pregnant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנִּרְבְּעוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף עִיבְּרוּ, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם אָסוּר, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** This **dispute** applies in a case **where** the animals **were the objects of bestiality and then became pregnant, as Rabbi Eliezer holds** that when both **this** permitted factor **and that** prohibited factor **cause** a certain situation, the resulting item **is prohibited.** Since the mother of this offspring is prohibited, the offspring is likewise prohibited, despite the fact that its father is permitted. **And the Rabbis hold** that when **this** permitted factor **and that** prohibited factor **cause** a result, it, i.e., the offspring, is **permitted** like its father.

Rava offers a more expansive reading. He holds that the *machlokes* in the *baraita* extends even to the case where the animal became pregnant *after* the forbidden status had already taken effect. This is the more challenging position, because it requires explaining why any status should transmit to a fetus conceived after the fact.

Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning in this reading is *zeh vezeh gorem assur*: this fetus was formed through the joint contribution of the mother and the father. The father was presumably not involved in any forbidden act. Yet since one of the contributing causes — the mother — carried a disqualified status, that status taints the offspring. This involves two distinct propositions. First, the fact that only one contributing cause was problematic is sufficient; the problem need not apply to both parents. Second, and more fundamentally, this is a type of *p'sul* that transfers at all to the offspring — which is itself a significant *chiddush* that the Gemara does not fully explain at this stage. The Rabbis counter with *zeh vezeh gorem mutar*: since the father was an entirely normal contributing factor, the single tainted cause is neutralized, and the offspring is permitted.

In Rava's reading, the reverse case — where the forbidden act occurred *while* the animal was already pregnant — would yield universal agreement that the fetus is forbidden, since this returns to Rava's own stated position elsewhere in the *daf*: *vlad nirvat assurah* — the offspring of an animal that was used in bestiality while pregnant is forbidden, because *hu ve'vlado nirvu*, the act was performed on the fetus as well, given that *ubar yerech imo*. Similarly, *vlad nogachas assur* — if the animal gored while pregnant, the fetus is equally implicated.

### Sanctification & Bizuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנִּרְבְּעוּ כְּשֶׁהֵן מוּקְדָּשִׁין, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר בִּיזּוּי מִילְּתָא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי לָא. אֲבָל נִרְבְּעוּ כְּשֶׁהֵן חוּלִּין, הוֹאִיל וְהִשְׁתַּנּוּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Some state another version** of the previous discussion: **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** This **dispute** applies in a case **where** the animals **were the object of bestiality when they** already had the status of **sacrificial** animals. **As Rabbi Eliezer holds** that the offspring are also prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar, as their sacrifice is considered a degradation of consecrated items, and **degradation** of consecrated items is **significant. And the Rabbis hold** that the offspring may be sacrificed, as they maintain that this is **not** considered a degradation of consecrated items. **But** in a case where the animals **were the object of bestiality when they were non-sacred,** and were subsequently consecrated, **since** their status **was changed** by their consecration, **everyone agrees** that **they are permitted.**

The second version of the dispute shifts the frame entirely. According to this *lishna achrina*, Rav Huna bar Chinena said in the name of Rav Nachman that the debate concerns a case where the forbidden act occurred while the animal was *already sanctified* — *shenirbe'u keshen mukdashin*. Rabbi Eliezer holds *bizuy milta*: when an animal has already been designated as a *korban* and then becomes subjected to these acts, that is a severe and flagrant offense — an aesthetic and religious stigma so pronounced that it brands the animal indelibly. The logic is that once something has been set apart for the altar and then acquires such a taint, the contamination is highly visible and therefore plausibly transmits even to the offspring. The Rabbis disagree: *lo*, it does not pass on; the status applies only to the mother and stops there.

The reverse, in this version, is the case where the forbidden act occurred before the animal was sanctified — *nirbe'u keshen chullin*. There, says Rav Nachman, everyone agrees the offspring is permitted, because *ho'il ve'ishtannu*, the offspring is something different, and a pre-sanctification disqualification that is less overtly offensive does not carry over.

Rava, once again, pushes the debate further. He cites Rav Nachman as holding that the dispute actually extends even to the case where the act occurred before sanctification — *keshen chullin*. Rabbi Eliezer, on this reading, holds *bizuy milta* even by *chullin*: the categories of *pesulei ha-mizbe'ach* are sufficiently weighty that once an animal acquires one, that status follows the offspring regardless. The Rabbis counter: *ho'il ve'ishtannu mutar* — the offspring is independent. And in the reverse case, where the act was done once the animal was already sanctified, all agree that the stigma is so blatant — *divrei hakol assur* — that it passes to the offspring.

This second version reframes the entire debate: it is not about *ubar yerech imo* or *zeh vezeh gorem* in the technical sense, but about an almost aesthetic-legal question of when a stigma is sufficiently pronounced that it propagates to the next generation. The Gemara at this point invokes the concept of *ubar yerech imo* as relevant primarily when the fetus was present at the time of the prohibited event. When the pregnancy came later, the deeper question is whether there is any principled basis for status transfer at all. The Gemara here anchors that principle in *bizuy milta*, though it acknowledges this reasoning is not fully worked out.

## Treifa Offspring

### Treifa Birth Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵפָה יָלְדָה — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה וּלְבַסּוֹף עִיבְּרָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵיפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁעִיבְּרָה וּלְבַסּוֹף נִטְרְפָה, וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי: דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר עוּבָּר יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי עוּבָּר לָאו יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that with regard to **the offspring of a *tereifa*,** Rabbi Eliezer prohibits it to be sacrificed on the altar, whereas the Rabbis permit it. The Gemara explains: There is a dispute as to whether a *tereifa* is capable of giving birth. **According to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* can give birth, you find** this dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis in a case **where** the animal first **became a *tereifa* and then became pregnant.** By contrast, **according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* cannot survive,** i.e., cannot give birth, **you find** this dispute in a case **where** the animal first **became pregnant and then became a *tereifa*. And this is** the point over which **they disagree, as Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **a fetus is** considered **the thigh of its mother,** and therefore just as its mother is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar as a *tereifa*, the same applies to its offspring. **And the Rabbis hold** that **a fetus is not** considered **the thigh of its mother,** and consequently there is no reason to prohibit it to be sacrificed on the altar.

The Mishnah specifically singles out the *treifa* as the category in which Rabbi Eliezer's dissent from the general rule is most explicit, and the Gemara now turns to analyze this case. The first question is how the scenario is even possible. According to the opinion *treifa yalda* — that a *treifa* animal can in fact bear offspring — there is no difficulty. The scenario is simply that the animal first became a *treifa* and afterward became pregnant. According to the opinion that a *treifa* cannot survive long enough to give birth — *treifa eina chaya* — the only viable scenario is the reverse: the animal was first pregnant and then became a *treifa*. These two scenarios map cleanly onto the two frameworks discussed above.

### Zeh VeZeh Gorem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם אָסוּר, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And this is** the matter over which **they disagree, as Rabbi Eliezer holds** that when **both this** permitted factor **and that** prohibited factor **cause** a certain situation, the resulting item **is prohibited.** Here too, as the mother of this offspring is prohibited, the offspring is likewise prohibited, despite the fact that its father is permitted. **And the Rabbis hold** that when **both this** permitted factor **and that** prohibited factor **cause** a situation, the resulting item is **permitted.** Therefore, the offspring in this case is permitted like its father.

Under the scenario where the *treifa* occurred first and then the animal became pregnant, the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis is once again one of *zeh vezeh gorem*. Rabbi Eliezer says *assur*: even though the fetus was formed through both the mother and the father, the mother's *treifa* status is a contributing cause and therefore taints the offspring. The Rabbis say *mutar*: the father's contribution neutralizes the taint.

This case differs from the earlier ones in an important respect. There, the *p'sul* was a disqualification from the altar — non-physical, relational, a function of what was done to the animal. Here, the *treifa* status is about the animal's fitness as food. The principle *hayotzei min ha-tamei tamei* — that which issues from a non-kosher source is itself non-kosher — is a known *halacha* in *kashrus*, and it applies directly here: milk from a *treifa* cow is itself *treifa*. So the intuition that the status should transfer is perhaps stronger here than in the earlier cases, and the only reason the Rabbis resist it is the *zeh vezeh gorem* consideration — the father was also a contributing cause. Rashi apparently holds that the debate is limited to *pesulei ha-mizbe'ach*, while Tosafot notes from the Gemara in Chullin that Rabbi Eliezer means the full *treifa* status passes on — the offspring would be forbidden for consumption as well.

Under the scenario where the animal was pregnant first and then became *treifa* — the only possible scenario according to *treifa eina chaya* — the debate reverts to *ubar yerech imo*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the fetus is part of the mother, and the mother's *treifa* status therefore applies to it. The Rabbis hold the fetus is independent.

### Treifa vs. Acts

Tosafot raises a pointed observation here. The concept of *ubar yerech imo* was developed to explain why an act done to the mother — being worshipped, being used in bestiality — could be considered done to the fetus as well, since the fetus is legally a limb of the mother. But becoming a *treifa* is not an *act* done to the animal in the same sense; it is a physical reality about its condition. The fetus does not share that physical defect. Even granting *ubar yerech imo*, there is no obvious mechanism by which the physical injury of *treifa* in the mother should render the fetus *treifa*, since the fetus is physically unaffected. Moreover, if *ubar yerech imo* were the operative principle here, it should apply equally whether the *treifa* occurred before or after pregnancy — since in either case the fetus would simply be part of the mother's body. Tosafot therefore emphasizes that applying *ubar yerech imo* to *treifa* is a genuine extension of the concept, not a straightforward application, and the more natural framing of the debate — even in the *treifa* case — concerns whether the mother's status transfers to the offspring at all, which is the *zeh vezeh gorem* question.

## Products & Status

### Treifa Egg Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּאֶפְרוֹחַ בֵּיצַת טְרֵפָה שֶׁאָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֶלָּא בִּוְלַד בְּהֵמָה, דְּמֵאַוֵּירָא קָא רָבְיָא, אֲבָל בֵּיצַת טְרֵפָה דְּמִגּוּפַהּ דְּתַרְנְגוֹלְתָּא קָא רָבְיָא — אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis. **Rav Huna says: The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Eliezer in** the case of **a chick** that emerges from **the egg of a *tereifa*** dove **that it is prohibited** to be sacrificed on the altar. **What is the reason** that the Rabbis rule stringently here? The Rabbis **disagree with Rabbi Eliezer** and claim that the offspring of a *tereifa* is permitted **only with regard to the offspring of an animal, which grows from** its own **space,** i.e., even when it is in its mother's womb it develops as an independent entity. **But** with regard to **an egg of a *tereifa*** hen, which **grows from the body of the hen, even the Rabbis concede** that the chick which emerges from this egg may not be sacrificed on the altar.

The Gemara reports that Rav Huna stated: *modim chachamim le-Rabbi Eliezer be-efroach beitzas treifa she-assur* — the Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer regarding the offspring of a *treifa* animal nonetheless agree with him when it comes to a chick that hatches from the egg of a *treifa* hen. This is a significant concession, and the Gemara immediately asks for the rationale.

It explains: *ad kan lo pligi rabbanan alei de-Rabbi Eliezer ella be-vlad behema*, the Rabbis dispute Rabbi Eliezer only regarding the live-born offspring of a mammal, *de-me'avira ka ravya* — because the offspring grows by means of air (i.e., within the womb in a relatively independent manner). But an egg of a *treifa* hen, *mi-gufa de-tarnigoles ka ravya* — grows directly from the very body of the hen itself — and even the Rabbis agree that in that case the chick is forbidden.

This is a natural transition point, because the underlying question throughout has been why the status of the mother should ever transmit to a product that issues from her. The Gemara here introduces a distinction: some products are so thoroughly embedded in and constituted by the mother's body that they carry her status automatically; others retain sufficient independence that the transfer does not occur.

### Rashi Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב הוּנָא: תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ, מְלֹא תַּרְוָד רִימָּה הַבָּאָה מֵאָדָם חַי — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַמֵּא, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַהֲרִין. עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ אֶלָּא בְּרִימָּה, דְּפִירְשָׁא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, אֲבָל בֵּיצָה דְּמִגּוּפַהּ דְּתַרְנְגוֹלֶת הוּא — אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Huna: That** which **is taught** in a *baraita* **supports your** opinion: With regard to **a full ladle [*tarvad*] of worms that come from a living person** who subsequently died, **Rabbi Eliezer** holds that it **transmits ritual impurity** in the same manner as a corpse, as a full ladle is the minimum amount of dust from the dead that imparts impurity; **and the Rabbis deem it pure.** Rava analyzes this *baraita*: **The Rabbis disagree with** Rabbi Eliezer **only with regard to worms, which are merely a secretion. But** with regard to **an egg, which is** part **of the hen's body, even the Rabbis concede** that in the case of a *tereifa*, the chick that emerges from the egg is prohibited to the altar.

Rashi offers two separate explanations for why a live-born animal fetus is less "part of" the mother than an egg. The first pertains to the gestation period. An egg developing within a hen is deeply embedded and physically intertwined within the hen's body in a way that the fetus in a mammalian womb is not. The fetus in the womb is enclosed within an amniotic sac with fluid around it, which gives it a degree of physical distinctness. The egg, by contrast, is as fully part of the hen's body as any organ. This explanation has the advantage of mapping well onto the language *mi-gufa de-tarnigoles ka ravya*.

Rashi's second explanation focuses on the growth that occurs after birth. When a calf is born, it is far from its final size — the growth it undergoes after birth is enormous relative to what it was at birth, demonstrating a significant independent development. A newly hatched chick, by contrast, is already quite close to the size of an adult bird. This greater post-birth growth in mammals, Rashi suggests, demonstrates a greater independence of the offspring from the mother's body. One might, however, find this counterintuitive: an organism that is more complete at the time it exits the mother might seem more, not less, intertwined with the mother. The first explanation therefore seems more compelling — the egg is wholly a product of the mother's body when it is laid, whereas the live-born fetus retains a certain physical separateness throughout gestation.

An alternative framing would be: when an egg is laid, it is a food byproduct of the mother — it exits as an inert object, not yet a living independent creature. Its development into a chick is a subsequent stage. At the moment of laying it is indistinguishable in legal character from the mother's milk; both are food products of a *treifa* animal. By contrast, the live-born calf exits as an independent living animal from the very first moment, which suggests independence. This logic is consistent with the principle *hayotzei min ha-tamei tamei* as it applies to food products — milk, eggs — without extending it to live animals.

### Maggots Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא — עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר עֲלַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן אֶלָּא בְּרִימָּה, דְּאִיקְּרִי אָדָם מֵחַיִּים ״רִימָּה״, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְתִקְוַת אֱנוֹשׁ רִמָּה וּבֶן אָדָם תּוֹלֵעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: No proof can be brought from here for the opinion of Rav Huna, as **on the contrary, the opposite is reasonable: Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with the Rabbis only with regard to worms,** as he maintains that worms are considered part of a person even if they emerged from him while he was still alive, **as a person is called a worm while** he is still **alive, as it is written: And the hope of man is a worm, and the son of man, that is a maggot** (see Job 25:6).

Rava says to Rav Huna: I have a *baraita* that supports your position. The *baraita* concerns *malei tarvad rima ha-ba me-adam chai* — a ladle's worth of maggots that emerge from the living skin of a person (arising from a rotting infection). Rabbi Eliezer holds that these are considered part of the person's skin and therefore convey ritual impurity as a limb of a corpse. The *chachamim* hold they do not. The Gemara says: *ad kan lo pligi rabbanan alei de-Rabbi Eliezer ella birima* — the Rabbis only dispute Rabbi Eliezer in this extreme case of maggots, because *de-pirsha be'alma hu* — they are waste-matter, not truly the body itself. But something that is genuinely part of the body of its source — such as an egg that forms within the hen — the Rabbis would agree with Rabbi Eliezer that its status is determined by the status of the source.

The analogy is admittedly loose. The maggots case is about whether living organisms growing from a person's skin are considered part of that person's body for purposes of *tumah*. The egg case is about whether a food product and eventual living organism carries the *kashrus* status of its source. The connecting logic is simply about the degree of physical integration with the source: the more extreme the independence (maggots as mere waste), the more the Rabbis are willing to say status does not transfer; the more the product is genuinely constituted by the source (the egg), the more even the Rabbis concede that status follows.

### Abaye Egg Independence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל גַּבֵּי בֵּיצָה, אֵימַת גָּדְלָה? לְכִי מַסְרְחָא, וְכִי אַסְרַחָא — עַפְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹדֶה! וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא בְּהֶדְיָא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לַחֲכָמִים בְּאֶפְרוֹחַ בֵּיצַת טְרֵפָה שֶׁמּוּתָּר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי תַּנְיָא — תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to** a chick that emerges from **an egg,** one can say: **When** does the chick **grow** out of and emerge from the egg? It does so **when** the egg **rots, and when** it **rots it is** considered **merely dust** and is no longer attributed to the hen that laid it. Therefore, despite the fact that the hen is a *tereifa*, **even Rabbi Eliezer** should **concede** that the chick that emerges from its egg is permitted to be sacrificed. **And furthermore, it is explicitly taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer concedes to the Rabbis with regard to the chick** that emerged from the **egg of a *tereifa*** animal **that it is permitted** to be sacrificed on the altar, which is not in accordance with the opinion of Rav Huna. Rava **said to** Abaye: **If this** *baraita* **is taught, it is taught,** and I cannot take issue with it.

Abaye responds to Rava with a pointed reversal: *adaraba, ipcha mistabra* — on the contrary, the opposite logic is more compelling. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer only says that the maggots carry the person's status because even a living human being is called *rima* — as the verse says, *be-tikvat enosh rima*, "the hope of man is maggots," and *u-ven adam tolei'a*, "the human being becomes a worm." Because human beings are characterized by this term from the very outset, the maggots that emerge from a living person are seen as naturally part of that person. But an egg, Abaye argues, is a different matter: *eimat gadla* — when does the chick actually grow? *Ki gadla ve-chi masrecha, afra be'alma hu* — it grows and develops only after it has been laid, and if it spoils before hatching it is mere dust. The development of the chick is an entirely post-birth phenomenon, occurring outside the mother's body, and therefore the chick that emerges is independent of the mother's *treifa* status.

This argument leads to a *baraita* that states explicitly: *modeh Rabbi Eliezer la-chachamim be-efroach beitzas treifa she-mutar* — Rabbi Eliezer himself agrees with the Sages that the chick that hatches from the egg of a *treifa* hen is permitted. Rava concedes: when there is a *baraita*, one cannot argue against it.

The practical *halacha* that emerges is therefore: the milk of a *treifa* cow and the eggs of a *treifa* hen are *treifa*, because they are direct bodily products of the mother. But the live-born calf of a *treifa* cow and the chick that hatches from the egg of a *treifa* hen are both permitted — the calf because it is an independent living creature, and the chick because its development as a living creature occurs entirely outside and after the mother.

## Nursing Treifa

### Extreme Feeding Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּמִפַּטְמָא מִינַּהּ, אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה הֶאֱכִילָהּ כַּרְשִׁינֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, הָכִי נָמֵי דַּאֲסִירָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? If we say that** it is because the kosher animal **was fattened from** the *tereifa* animal, **if that is so,** then in a case where its owner **fed it vetches of idolatry, so too,** should one say **that it is prohibited?** Feeding an animal vetches of idolatry renders it prohibited only if the animal has been set aside for idolatry; a regular animal is not prohibited by this action.

The Mishnah cites Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, who holds that a calf that nursed from a *treifa* mother is *pesulah me'al gabi ha-mizbe'ach* — disqualified for the altar. The implicit reasoning would seem to be that an animal whose growth and sustenance derived entirely from a non-kosher source acquires a stigma that renders it unfit as a *korban*. This is striking, because the preceding discussion established that even the live-born calf of a *treifa* is independent of the mother's status — yet here the Gemara entertains the idea that feeding alone can disqualify.

The Gemara limits this drastically. One might have thought Rabbi Chanina's principle is that any fattening by a *treifa* source disqualifies. But the Gemara rejects this reading: if the animal were fed grain dedicated to idolatrous purposes — *karshinei avodah zarah* — would we also say it becomes forbidden? The answer is no. Being fed from something forbidden does not by itself impart forbidden status to the animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, תָּנֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא טְרִיטָאָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֱנִיקָהּ חָלָב רוֹתֵחַ מִשַּׁחֲרִית לְשַׁחֲרִית, הוֹאִיל וִיכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד עָלֶיהָ מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Ḥanina Terita taught** the meaning of the mishna **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** This is referring to a case **where one gave** the kosher animal **to suckle** from the *tereifa* **warm,** i.e., fresh, **milk every morning. Since** the kosher animal **can maintain** its existence **based upon** this suckling **for** a twenty-four-hour **period,** until the next suckling, it survives only due to the *tereifa* animal. Consequently, the kosher animal is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar.

Rav Chanina Terita'ah taught before Rabbi Yochanan the precise limits of Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's ruling: the disqualification applies only in an extreme case — *she'henika chalav rotei'ach mi-shacharit le-shacharit* — where the calf nursed fresh, warm milk from its *treifa* mother every single morning, first thing in the morning, and that feeding could have sustained it for the entire day. In this scenario, the calf's entire growth and sustenance is traceable exclusively to the non-kosher source, and it is this totality of dependence — not mere incidental contact with forbidden food — that Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos considers sufficient to disqualify for the altar. This is not a matter of the calf being non-kosher for consumption; it remains permitted to eat. The disqualification is limited to *korban* purposes, and even there it is only in this most extreme case.

### Feeding Insufficient

The Gemara thus makes clear that the act of feeding, by itself, is an insufficient basis for disqualification. A cow fed from grain used for *avodah zarah* is not thereby rendered forbidden. The principle *hayotzei min ha-tamei tamei* applies to organic products that issue directly from the animal's body, not to substances that merely pass through the animal as food. Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's position, even as it survives limitation, remains a singular ruling confined to the most extreme form of nutritional dependence.

## Treifa Redemption

### No Treifa Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ טְרֵפָה — אֵין פּוֹדִין אוֹתָן, שֶׁאֵין פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״תִּזְבַּח״ — וְלֹא גִּיזָּה, ״וְאָכַלְתָּ״ — וְלֹא לִכְלָבֶיךָ, ״בָּשָׂר״ — וְלֹא חָלָב. מִכָּאן שֶׁאֵין פּוֹדִים אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: With regard to **all sacrificial** animals **that became *tereifa*, one may not redeem** them and render them non-sacred, as their consumption is prohibited, and one does not redeem sacrificial animals to feed them to dogs. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** with regard to the verse: "Notwithstanding you may slaughter and eat flesh within all your gates" (Deuteronomy 12:15). This verse, which is referring to disqualified consecrated animals that have been redeemed, is expounded as follows: **"You may slaughter," but** you may **not shear** their wool; **"and eat," but** you may **not** give **to your dogs** to eat; "and eat **flesh," but not** their **milk. From here** it is derived **that one does not redeem sacrificial** animals **to feed them to dogs.**

The Gemara now addresses a related principle: *v'chol ha-kodashim she-nitrfu*, if an animal was sanctified as a *korban* and then became a *treifa*, it cannot be redeemed. The reason is practical: redeeming a *korban* that has developed a *mum* is permitted only so that the owner — a Jew — can benefit from the meat. A *treifa* cannot be eaten by a Jew; it can at best be fed to a dog or given to a *nochri*. If the only permissible use is one that a Jew cannot personally enjoy, the redemption has no valid basis.

### Parashat Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: ״תִּזְבַּח וְאָכַלְתָּ״ — אֵין לְךָ בָּהֶן הֶיתֵּר אֶלָּא מִשְּׁעַת זְבִיחָה וְאֵילָךְ, קָסָבַר: פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who state** a different version: **"You may slaughter and eat,"** this teaches that **you have permission** to derive benefit **from them only from the time of slaughter and onward,** i.e., those benefits that come after slaughter, such as the consumption of its flesh, are permitted. But one may not derive benefit from their shearing or their milk, as these occur even when the animal is alive. It can be inferred from here that the *tanna* of this *baraita* **maintains** that **one may redeem sacrificial** animals **to feed them to dogs,** as this occurs after the slaughter.

The Gemara asks: from where do we derive this? A *baraita* expounds the verses dealing with the redemption of a blemished *korban*. The Torah says *tizbach* — you shall slaughter — *velo giza*, not shear it (meaning a redeemed animal may not be shorn). The Torah then says *ve'achalta* — you shall eat it — *velo le-chalbecha*, not for your dogs. And *basar* — meat — *velo chalav*, you may not milk it and benefit from the milk. The *derashah* emerges: the Torah permits only eating the meat, *ve'achalta* with an emphasis on *you* — a Jew — consuming it personally. Feeding it to dogs is not a legitimate basis for redemption.

A minority opinion challenges this: *tizbach ve'achalta* means only that the animal may not be used before slaughter — it cannot be shorn or milked while in its sanctified state — but once slaughtered, the meat may be used for any purpose whatsoever, including feeding dogs. According to this view, one may redeem a *korban* even when the only possible use of the meat is as dog food. This view thus conflicts with the Mishnah's ruling. The substantive question is therefore whether *ve'achalta* restricts not only the *what* but the *who* — whether it mandates that a Jew personally consume the meat, or merely that the meat be the form of benefit, to be used however one wishes after slaughter.

## Mizbe'ach vs. Bedek

### Temurah Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁאֵין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, וְיֵשׁ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁאֵין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ. שֶׁקׇּדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There are** elements that apply **to** animals **consecrated for the altar that do not** apply **to** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance, and there are** elements that apply **to** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance that do not** apply **to** animals **consecrated for the altar.** One element exclusive to animals consecrated for the altar is **that** animals **consecrated for the altar render** an animal exchanged for them **a substitute, and** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance do not render** an animal exchanged for them **a substitute.**

With the conclusion of *perek kol ha'asurin*, the Gemara moves into the final *perek* of the tractate: *yesh be-kodshei mizbe'ach*. This *perek* provides a structured comparative accounting of the laws that distinguish *kodshei mizbe'ach* — animals sanctified as sacrificial offerings — from *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* — property sanctified for the Temple treasury. The relevance to *Temurah* is direct: the entire tractate concerns the laws of *temurah*, which apply only to *korbanot* and not to *kodshei bedek ha-bayit*.

The Mishnah opens: *yesh be-kodshei mizbe'ach she'ein be-kodshei bedek ha-bayit* — there are aspects that apply to *kodshei mizbe'ach* and not to *kodshei bedek ha-bayit*. The first: *kodshei mizbe'ach osin temurah* — sanctified animals can effect a *temurah*; *kodshei bedek ha-bayit ein osin temurah* — treasury sanctifications cannot. This is the foundational principle that has governed the entire tractate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּ״וּכְלָלָא הוּא דְּכׇל קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה? וַהֲרֵי עוֹפוֹת דְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ הֵן, וּתְנַן: הַמְּנָחוֹת וְהָעוֹפוֹת אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה! כִּי קָתָנֵי — אַבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the statement of the mishna that an item consecrated for the altar renders an animal exchanged for it a substitute: **And is** it an established **principle that anything consecrated for the altar renders** an animal exchanged for it **a substitute? But** there are offerings brought from **birds,** i.e., doves and pigeons, **which are consecrated for the altar, and** yet they do not render an animal exchanged for them a substitute, as **we learned** in a mishna (*Temura* 13a): **Meal offerings and bird** offerings **do not render** an animal exchanged for them **a substitute.** The Gemara responds: **When** this *halakha* of the mishna **was taught,** it was taught only **with regard to animal** offerings.

The Gemara immediately questions whether this is truly a universal rule. Birds are also *kodshei mizbe'ach*, yet the Mishnah earlier taught *ha-minchos ve-ha-ofos ein osin temurah* — meal-offerings and birds cannot make a *temurah*, because the verse says *behemah be-behemah*, restricting *temurah* to livestock. Similarly, the offspring of a *korban* is *kodshei mizbe'ach* in derivation, yet *ein ha-vlad oseh temurah* — an offspring cannot make a *temurah*, because its sanctity is derivative, not primary. And the *temurah* itself cannot make a further *temurah* — *ein temurah osah temurah*.

The Gemara responds: when the Mishnah states the rule, it refers to *ikar zivchah* — the paradigmatic, primary consecration of a *korban*. Primary sanctifications of livestock for the altar make *temurah*; derivative sanctities — offspring, *temurah* itself — do not. And the rule for birds and *minchos* is simply that *temurah* requires *behemah*. The principle as stated in the Mishnah is therefore a reliable generalization about original, primary livestock *korbanot*.

### Pigul Notar Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ חַיָּיבִין (עָלָיו) [עֲלֵיהֶן] מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר, וְטָמֵא,
>
> **Translation:** In addition, if one slaughters an animal **consecrated for the altar** with the intention to eat it beyond its designated time, or if he ate the offering after its designated time, or if he ate the offering while ritually impure, **he is liable** to receive *karet* **for eating it due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, and** eating while **ritually impure,** respectively.

The second distinction: *kodshei mizbe'ach chayavin aleihem mishum pigul notar ve-tamei* — when a *korban* is offered with improper intent regarding time or location, it becomes *pigul*; when meat is left beyond its permitted time it becomes *notar*; when the meat becomes *tamei*, these all create severe prohibitions on consuming the sacrificial meat, some incurring *karet*. None of these categories apply to *kodshei bedek ha-bayit*. They are laws specific to the sacrificial act and the consumption of *korban* meat, not to mere consecration for the treasury.

### Post-Redemption Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן אָסוּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן, וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב. אֵין נוֹתְנִין מֵהֶן לָאוּמָּנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן — מָה שֶׁאֵין כֵּן בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** If animals consecrated for the altar became pregnant and then became blemished and gave birth after redemption, **their offspring and their milk are forbidden after their redemption. And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **liable** to receive *karet*. And the Temple treasurer **does not give compensation to craftsmen from** money designated for purchasing animals consecrated for the altar. And in all these instances, **that is not so with regard to** money **consecrated for Temple maintenance.**

The third distinction: *vladam ve-chalavam assur le'achar pidyonan* — even after a *kodshei mizbe'ach* animal has been redeemed following the development of a blemish, certain residual sanctity remains. One may not shear the animal, may not work it, and — as the Gemara has just discussed — may not benefit from its milk. The Mishnah adds that even the offspring carries residual restrictions if the animal was pregnant prior to redemption and gave birth afterward. All of this reflects the intrinsic, identity-based nature of *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach*, which is not fully dissolved by redemption.

By contrast, when *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* is redeemed, the *kedushah* transfers entirely to the money and the object is fully desanctified. The distinction flows from the nature of each form of sanctification: *kodshei mizbe'ach* is a substantive, personal identity of the animal as a *korban*, while *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* is essentially a matter of monetary value transferred to the treasury.

### Shechita Chutz

The fourth distinction: *ve-ha-shochet an bachutz chayav* — one who slaughters a *korban* outside the Temple is liable for the *issur karet* of *shechitat kodshim* outside the *Beit ha-Mikdash*. This prohibition has no application to treasury sanctifications, which were never designated for sacrificial slaughter.

Fifth: *ein notnim me-hem le-umanim be-skharam* — one may not pay Temple workers with animals sanctified as *korbanot*. The animal is designated for the altar alone and may not be redirected to pay for labor. The Gemara derives from the verse *va'asu li mikdash* — "make for Me a Temple," with the emphasis on *li*, that the funds of *bedek ha-bayit* may be used to pay workers. One might have thought that sanctified funds could only be used for physical objects incorporated into the Temple structure, not for wages, since wages leave no traceable sanctified object behind. The verse teaches otherwise: the monetary value of treasury sanctifications may be used to compensate labor.

## Bedek HaBayit Laws

### Stam Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, שֶׁסְּתַם הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת, הֶקְדֵּשׁ בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת חָל עַל הַכֹּל, וּמוֹעֲלִין בְּגִידּוּלֵיהֶן, וְאֵין בָּהֶן הֲנָאָה לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** elements that apply **to** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance** that do not apply to animals consecrated for the altar, in **that unspecified consecrations are** designated **for Temple maintenance; consecration for Temple maintenance takes effect on all** items; **and one** is liable to bring a guilt offering and pay an additional payment of one-fifth for **misuse** of consecrated property, not only for the items themselves, but **for their by-products,** e.g., milk of a consecrated animal or eggs of a consecrated chicken; **and there is no benefit for the owner** from items consecrated for Temple maintenance, in contrast to some animals consecrated for the altar, e.g., a peace offering, from which there is benefit for the owner.

The Mishnah now pivots to enumerate the unique features of *kodshei bedek ha-bayit*. The first: *she-stam hekdeshot le-vedek ha-bayit* — an unspecified consecration defaults to the Temple treasury. If a person simply says "I am sanctifying this" without specification, we interpret the declaration as a consecration for the treasury rather than as a *korban*.

The second distinctive feature: *hekdesh bedek ha-bayit chal al ha-kol* — treasury sanctification takes effect on everything. Unlike *kodshei mizbe'ach*, which requires a kosher, unblemished animal fit for the altar, treasury sanctification can rest on any object whatsoever: money, property, utensils, land, and even animals that could not serve as *korbanot*.

Third: *u-mo'alin be-giduleihen* — the prohibition of *me'ilah*, the misuse of sacred property, extends not only to the sanctified object itself but also to the products that grow from it. If one sanctifies a tree for the treasury and fruit grows on it, the fruit is also subject to *me'ilah*. The Gemara will develop how this differs from the treatment of products in *kodshei mizbe'ach*.

Fourth: *ve-ein bahem hana'ah la-ba'alim* — and there is no personal benefit for the owners, and according to some the *kohanim* as well. All benefit goes exclusively to the *Beit ha-Mikdash*. This contrasts sharply with *korbanot*, from which both the owners and the *kohanim* frequently benefit — eating *shelamim*, portions of *kodshei kodashim*, and so on. The nature of *bedek ha-bayit* sanctification is a total transfer of the object's value to the Temple with no residual personal claim.

## Stam Hekdesh Debate

### Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: סְתָם הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת, וְחָל עַל הַכֹּל. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דְּתַנְיָא: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו, וְהָיָה בָּהֶן בְּהֵמָה רְאוּיָה לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבוֹת — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי עוֹלוֹת, וּנְקֵבוֹת יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי שְׁלָמִים, וּדְמֵיהֶן יִפְּלוּ עִם שְׁאָר נְכָסִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses these *halakhot*: **The Master said** in the mishna that **unspecified consecrations** are designated **for Temple maintenance, and** consecration for Temple maintenance **takes effect** on **all** items. **Who is the *tanna*** whose ruling is stated in this mishna? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua.** This is **as it is taught** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 4:7): In the case of **one who consecrates his possessions** without specification, **and among** the consecrated property **there was an animal suitable** for sacrifice **upon the altar, male or female,** what should be done with it? **Rabbi Eliezer says: Males** that are suitable to be brought as a burnt offerings should **be sold for the needs of burnt offerings,** i.e., to individuals obligated to bring a burnt offering. **And females** should **be sold for the purpose of** being used for **peace offerings. And** the **money** received from **their** sale is **allocated with the rest of** his **property for Temple maintenance.**

The Gemara asks: whose view underlies the Mishnah's ruling that *stam hekdesh* goes to *bedek ha-bayit*? Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: it is not Rabbi Yehoshua — implying it follows Rabbi Eliezer. The Mishnah records their dispute: if one sanctifies all of one's property (*ha-makdish nechasav*) and among that property are animals fit for the altar — unblemished males and females — what happens?

Rabbi Eliezer holds that it all goes to the treasury. The male animals should be sold to those who need to bring an *olah*, and the female animals should be sold to those who need to bring *shelamim* — so that the animals eventually end up as *korbanot*, since we prefer that animals fit for the altar ultimately be used as such — but the proceeds of the sales go with all the other property to the treasury. The fundamental consecration, even of the animals, is for *bedek ha-bayit*.

### Yehoshua Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים הֵן עַצְמָן יִקְרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת, וּנְקֵבוֹת יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי שְׁלָמִים וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן עוֹלוֹת, וּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The **males** are not sold; rather, they **themselves** should **be sacrificed as burnt offerings, and** the **females** should **be sold for the purpose of** being used for **peace offerings** and they will be offered as such, **and** one should purchase and **bring burnt offerings with the money** received from **their** sale. **And the rest of the property,** which is unsuitable for sacrificial use, is allocated **for Temple maintenance.** Evidently, Rabbi Yehoshua maintains that unspecified consecrations are not necessarily designated for Temple maintenance.

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees. He holds that even when one uses an unspecified declaration, one would naturally intend animals to be consecrated as *korbanot*. Males should be brought themselves as *olot*, since *stam hekdesh* for an animal implies total divestiture — an *olah* is the *korban* that is fully consumed with no personal benefit retained. Females cannot themselves be brought as an *olah*, so they are sold to people who need *shelamim* — so they will be brought as a *korban* — and the money is used to purchase an *olah* in their stead. The rest of the property — the non-animal sanctifications — goes to *bedek ha-bayit*. Rabbi Yehoshua is thus prepared to *cholek et nidro*, to read the vow as having different meanings for different parts of the property.

### Males & Females

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַav אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַב: בְּעֵדֶר שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ זְכָרִים, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹדֶה, דְּלָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ וּמַקְדִּישׁ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** this explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, stated by Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan, **disagrees with** the explanation of **Rav Adda bar Ahava. As Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **Rav says: With regard to a herd that is entirely male, even Rabbi Eliezer concedes** that suitable animals are brought as burnt offerings, **as a person does not set** aside his ability to consecrate to the altar an animal fit to be **consecrated for the altar and** instead **consecrate** it **for Temple maintenance.** Even if the owner consecrated these animals without specification, it is presumed that he consecrated them for sacrifice upon the altar.

The Gemara now asks: in what circumstances is there actually a dispute? Rav Adda bar Ahava said in the name of Rav: if the entire flock consisted exclusively of male animals, even Rabbi Eliezer would agree that it should be read as consecration for the altar. The principle is *lo shevik inish kodshei mizbe'ach u-makdish le-vedek ha-bayit* — people do not abandon the more elevated form of sanctification (the altar) and consecrate for the treasury, when the altar option is fully available. If all the animals are male and could straightforwardly be brought as *olot*, Rabbi Eliezer concedes that this is what the person meant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּעֵדֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מֶחֱצָה זְכָרִים וּמֶחֱצָה נְקֵבוֹת, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אֵין חוֹלֵק אֶת נִדְרוֹ, וּמִדִּנְקֵבוֹת לָאו עוֹלוֹת — זְכָרִים נָמֵי לָאו עוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua **disagree only with regard to** a case where one consecrates **a herd that has one-half males and one-half females. And** their dispute is as follows: **Rabbi Eliezer holds** that one does **not divide his vow,** i.e., state a single vow that must be fulfilled in different ways, **and** therefore, **from** the fact **that** the **females are not** consecrated as **burnt offerings,** as burnt offerings must be male, it must be that **the males are also not** consecrated as **burnt offerings.**

Therefore, according to this first version, the dispute is limited to a flock that contains both males and females. There, the person cannot have meant the declaration as an *olah* for everything — the females cannot themselves be *olot* — and Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein adam cholek nidro*, one does not split one's vow. Since the females manifestly cannot be *olot*, Rabbi Eliezer reads the entire vow as *bedek ha-bayit*. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees: he is prepared to split the vow, and in any event even the females carry *kedushat olah*, so the vow can be read consistently as relating to *olah* — with the females redeemed and their proceeds used for an *olah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: חוֹלֵק אֶת נִדְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that a person **does divide his vow.** Accordingly, the males, which are fit to be brought as burnt offerings, are consecrated for the altar, whereas the females are sold to those who must bring peace offerings, and the money from their sale is used to purchase burnt offerings. According to this interpretation, Rabbi Eliezer concedes that one does not consecrate for Temple maintenance an animal fit for the altar. Consequently, the mishna is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer either.

### Mixed Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחְרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר רַv אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַav: בְּשֶׁלֹּא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא בְּהֵמָה, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹדֶה, דְּלָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ וּמַקְדֵּישׁ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ שְׁאָר נְכָסִים עִמָּהֶן, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אֵין אָדָם חוֹלֵק אֶת נִדְרוֹ, מִדִּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים לָא לְקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבַּח, בְּהֵמָה נָמֵי לָא לְקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: אָדָם חוֹלֵק אֶת נִדְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Some say another version** of the explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **Rav says: In** a case **where** one **consecrated** without specification **only** the type of **animal** that is suitable as an offering, **even Rabbi Eliezer concedes** that all the animals are consecrated for the altar. Accordingly, the males are brought as burnt offerings, and the females are sold to those who require peace offerings, and the money from their sale is used to purchase burnt offerings. The reason is **that a person does not set** aside his ability to consecrate for the altar an animal fit to be **consecrated for the altar and** instead **consecrate** it **for Temple maintenance.** **They disagree only** with regard to a case **where there is other property** that was consecrated **with** the animals. **And** their dispute is as follows: **Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **a person does not divide his vow.** Consequently, **from** the fact **that** the **other property is not consecrated for the altar,** as it is not fit for sacrifice, it must be that the **animals are also not consecrated for the altar. And Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that **a person divides his vow.** Accordingly, the animals are consecrated for the altar, while the other property is consecrated for Temple maintenance. According to this explanation as well, the mishna is not in accordance with the opinions of either Rabbi Yehoshua or Rabbi Eliezer.

According to the second version of Rav Adda bar Ahava's statement, the concession goes further: even if the person sanctified only animals — including males and females — Rabbi Eliezer would agree the animals are intended for the altar. Rabbi Eliezer would only rule for *bedek ha-bayit* when other property (non-animals) was sanctified alongside the animals. In that case, since the non-animals clearly cannot be *korbanot*, and since Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein adam cholek nidro*, the non-animal purpose (*bedek ha-bayit*) colors the reading of the entire declaration including the animals. Rabbi Yehoshua, again, is willing to split the vow: the animals go as *korbanot*, the rest goes to the treasury.

### Text Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְמֵיהֶן יִפְּלוּ עִם שְׁאָר נְכָסִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת — בִּשְׁלָמָא לְלִישָּׁנָא בָּתְרָא, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי: ״עִם שְׁאָר נְכָסִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת״. אֶלָּא לְלִישָּׁנָא קַמָּא, לִיתְנֵי ״יִפְּלוּ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת״! תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״וּדְמֵיהֶן יִפְּלוּ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to determine which version of the dispute is correct from the language of the Mishnah itself. The final line of the Mishnah in Rabbi Eliezer's formulation states that the proceeds of selling the animals *yippelu im she'ar nechasim le-vedek ha-bayit* — shall fall with the rest of the property into the treasury. The phrase "with the rest of the property" implies that there *is* other property besides the animals — which would support the second version, according to which the dispute concerns cases where non-animal property was also consecrated. According to the first version, however, which holds the dispute concerns only males-and-females flocks, there would be no "rest of the property" — and the Mishnah's phrase would be difficult. The Gemara resolves this by pointing to an alternative textual tradition that simply reads *yippelu le-vedek ha-bayit* without the phrase "with the rest of the property." With that reading, the first version is supported. **Some say another version** of the explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. The Gemara adds: **Some say another version** of the explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **Rav says: In** a case **where** one **consecrated** without specification **only** the type of **animal** that is suitable as an offering, **even Rabbi Eliezer concedes** that all the animals are consecrated for the altar.

The upshot is a nuanced picture of Rabbi Eliezer's position. At the maximum, he holds that even with a pure herd of consecrateable males, *stam hekdesh* means *bedek ha-bayit*. At a more moderate reading, he only insists on *bedek ha-bayit* when there are females in the flock — since the vow cannot be read consistently as an *olah* for everything — or, at the most limited reading, only when there are also non-animals consecrated alongside, forcing a single uniform reading as *bedek ha-bayit* across the board. Rabbi Yehoshua, in all versions, prioritizes reading animal consecrations as *korbanot* wherever possible, even at the cost of splitting the vow.
