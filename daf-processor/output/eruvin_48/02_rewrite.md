# Eruvin 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ownerless Objects & Tech…

### 2000 Amah Debate

Today's *daf* is 48, and we begin by revisiting the closing discussion of 47b, which was covered somewhat hastily at the end of the previous session. The *gemara* there had been framing a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the Rabbis concerning whether ownerless objects — *cheftzei hefker* — are subject to the 2,000-*amah* limit of *techum Shabbat*, or whether, having no owner, they may be carried without restriction.

### Non-Jewish Property

Closely related to this is the question of objects owned by non-Jews. Since non-Jews have no *techum* of their own, one might argue that their property likewise carries no *techum* restriction. The *gemara* considers a case in which objects are brought from beyond the *techum* — presumably on *Yom Tov* — and then potentially acquired by Jews within the *techum*. The very mention of acquisition on *Yom Tov* requires a brief clarification: when necessary for *simchat Yom Tov*, a purchase may be transacted without the actual transfer of money. Once that is established, the remaining question is whether the objects themselves are permitted to be moved — and that turns entirely on whether objects owned by a non-Jew are defined by a *techum*.

### Melacha by Non-Jew

Beyond the *techum* question, there is a further consideration: ordinarily, the *halacha* prohibits benefiting from *melacha* performed by a non-Jew on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* on a Jew's behalf. The case under discussion presumably sidesteps this concern by positing that the non-Jew brought the goods for the majority of the city's population, which was non-Jewish. When a non-Jew performs *melacha* for a predominantly non-Jewish population, a Jew may benefit incidentally from that activity.

### Walled vs. Open Cities

A further point arising from that case is that the objects, even if brought from beyond the *techum*, could be carried throughout the entire city. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* understand this to mean it was a walled city — analogous to the barn discussed in the first *mishnah* of the tractate, where the wall creates a defined enclosed space. Other *rishonim*, however, maintain that any location within the city is considered a single contiguous space regardless of walls. This dispute carries significant practical relevance: when an object arrives *mechutz latechum*, is its movement restricted to a walled enclosure, or does the entire city constitute one unified domain?

## Net Dividing Techum

### Rabbi Chiyya's Net

With that review complete, we turn to the passage that opens our *daf* — the last line of 47b: *Tani Rabbi Chiyya* — "Rabbi Chiyya taught." The case involves a net (*cherem*) stretched across a body of water situated precisely at the boundary between the *techum Shabbat* of two adjacent cities. Rabbi Chiyya rules that in order to draw and use the water on either side of the net, a *mechitza shel barzel* — an iron wall — would be required to separate the domains. The reasoning is that the water is considered communally associated with the inhabitants of each respective city; once the water crosses the net, it has passed beyond the city's *techum*. Accordingly, residents of city A cannot use the water on city B's side, and vice versa, unless an iron wall physically demarcates the boundary.

### R. Yose Laughs

*Mechayech alei Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina* — Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina laughed at this ruling. The *gemara* inquires: *mai ta'ama kamechayech* — what was the reason for his laughter? One possible reading is that Rabbi Yose holds like the Rabbis *lekula* — that *cheftzei hefker* have no *techum* restriction — and he was mocking Rabbi Chiyya for ruling stringently like Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri. But the *gemara* rejects this reading: the mere fact that one holds the lenient position is no grounds to ridicule one who holds the strict position. That remains a perfectly legitimate stance, and it is worth noting here that the principle *halacha kedivrei hameikel be-eruv* — that the law follows the lenient opinion in matters of *eruv* — was not stated as a universal consensus; the *gemara* clearly does not assume that every authority must accept it.

There is also a subtler difficulty worth pausing over. If the water is truly *hefker*, its *techum* should be measured from where it physically sits — 2,000 *amot* from the water itself — not 2,000 *amot* from where the city's residents are. The *gemara*'s framing, which ties the water's *techum* to the city's inhabitants, occupies a strange middle ground: technically ownerless, yet somehow defined by those who wish to use it. The *gemara* acknowledges this tension but nonetheless proceeds with the framework as given.

A second possible reason for the laughter is rejected as well. Perhaps Rabbi Yose was mocking the ruling because a *braita* teaches that *neharot hamoshchin u-mayanos hanovim* — flowing rivers and natural springs — are *keraglei kol adam*, available to everyone without restriction. If so, water should never be subject to a *techum* at all. But the *gemara* deflects this too: how do we know the body of water in question was flowing? Perhaps Rabbi Chiyya was speaking of a stationary (*mechunas*) body of water, which does *koneh shevita* and is subject to the *techum* restriction. A ruling about still water is perfectly reasonable, and there is nothing there to mock.

### Water Leniency

The *gemara* then identifies the true basis for Rabbi Yose's laughter. Even granting that there is a genuine *techum* problem, Rabbi Yose found it absurd to suppose that an iron wall would solve it. *Mishum dekalushei hikelu chachamim bemayim* — the Sages were broadly lenient regarding bodies of water in order to enable their use, and that general leniency ought to apply here. The classic illustration of this principle is the case of drawing water from the sea on *Shabbat* while aboard a boat. Since the boat is a *reshut hayachid* and the sea is a *karmelit*, one may not draw water directly over the side. The halachic solution is to construct a box-frame on the side of the boat before *Shabbat* — four walls with no floor — and lower the bucket through it. The *mechitza* of this frame is treated as if it descends to the bottom of the sea, thereby creating a *reshut hayachid* below, even though ordinarily a *mechitza teluyah* — a suspended partition that does not reach the ground — would not halachically constitute a valid *mechitza*.

This principle is made explicit through a related teaching: *Rabbi Tavla* once asked *Rav* whether a *mechitza teluyah* would be effective to permit use of a ruin on dry land. *Rav* replied: *ein mechitza teluyah materet ela bemayim* — a suspended partition is effective only over water. It is a specific leniency, *kalushei hikelu chachamim bemayim*, granted uniquely in the context of bodies of water. Accordingly, Rabbi Yose's point was that even if one grants Rabbi Chiyya's concern, the netting itself — already present in the water — should qualify under this leniency as a sufficient *mechitza*, rendering it no different in principle from the boat frame. The demand for an iron wall is therefore not a solution; it is beside the point.

### Netting Problem

The *gemara* presses the question further: why would an iron wall be any better than the netting already there? The water passes through the netting — *de'ayli behu maya* — so the netting does not physically stop anything. But the *gemara* responds: *shel barzel nami ayli behu maya* — an iron wall likewise does not stop the water from passing beneath it, assuming the wall does not extend to the very floor of the body of water. A standard halachic wall of ten *tefachim* presents the same problem regardless of material. 

One possible resolution is that Rabbi Chiyya was not actually proposing the iron wall as a practical remedy. Rather, *tzricha ve-ein lah takanah ka'amar* — he was using it as a rhetorical device, a hyperbolic way of expressing that the problem is insoluble: if you wanted a real fix, you would need an iron wall sunk all the way to the floor, and since no such thing exists, the water simply cannot be used across the boundary. This too is a reasonable reading. But Rabbi Yose's objection stands regardless: given the *gemara*'s established leniency for water, even an imperfect partition such as the netting should suffice, and there is no need for any iron wall at all.

### Boat Water Example

The boat example, already introduced to illustrate the principle of *mechitza teluyah* over water, bears emphasis. The reason the box-frame on the side of the boat works is not that it literally encloses a column of water — it manifestly does not go to the sea floor — but that the Sages treated it as though it did, as a specific dispensation to make bodies of water practically usable on *Shabbat*. It is precisely this dispensation that Rabbi Yose invokes: once you accept that the Sages created such a leniency, the netting across the river should benefit from the same logic.

## Four Amot Debate

### Mishnah Positions

We now return to the *mishnah* and the question of the four *amot* granted to a person who finds himself outside the *techum* — whether because he intentionally left, or because he fell asleep before *Shabbat* and had not *kana shevita*. The *mishnah* presents a range of views on how those four *amot* are measured. The *chachamim* rule that a person has four *amot*, but once he chooses a direction — how much he takes in front of him versus behind him — he is committed to that choice. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees, holding that the person is fixed at the center of a four-by-four box, with two *amot* in each direction. It is noteworthy that the *chachamim*'s position permits a degree of subjectivity unusual in *halacha*: one may choose to take all four *amot* in front, zero behind, or any combination, and that choice is binding.

### Box Dimensions

The *gemara* raises an apparent redundancy: Rabbi Yehuda's position — that one has four *amot* with the freedom to choose one's placement within them — seems identical to the *tanna kamma*, who also says four *amot*. *Amar Rava: shmona al shmona ika beinaihu* — Rava resolves the difficulty by pointing out that the *tanna kamma*'s statement of "four *amot*" follows the convention used throughout the tractate for measurements like 2,000 *amot*, which always means four *amot* in each direction. The *tanna kamma* therefore grants a box of eight by eight *amot*, with the person at the center. Rabbi Eliezer reduces this to a four-by-four box, also centered. Rabbi Yehuda accepts the four-by-four box but allows the person to choose where within it he stands.

*Tanya nami hachi* — a *braita* corroborates this: *yesh lo shmona al shmona — divrei Rabbi Meir*, explicitly stating that the person occupies the center of an eight-by-eight box, consistent with the *tanna kamma*.

*Rava* adds an important qualification: *machlokes lehalech* — the dispute between the eight-by-eight and four-by-four positions concerns movement. When it comes to *letalteil* — carrying in a *reshut harabim* — all agree that one may carry only four *amot* from where one picked up the object. Even Rabbi Meir, who grants eight *amot* for walking, would not permit carrying an object the full eight *amot* across the box.

### Biblical Source

The *gemara* asks: *ve-hani arba amot hecha ksiva* — where in the text are these four *amot* found? The answer comes from a *braita* citing the verse *shevu ish tachtav* — "let each person remain in his place" — which serves as the *asmachta* for the concept of *techum*. The phrase *tachtav*, "beneath him," is understood to denote the physical space a person occupies.

### Body Measurement

The *braita* elaborates: *gufo shalosh amot* — a person's body occupies three *amot* — plus one additional *amah* for a total of four. Rabbi Meir describes this extra *amah* as *kedei lifshot yadav ve-raglav* — room to stretch out one's arms and legs while lying down. Rabbi Yehuda agrees on the total of four *amot* but describes the extra *amah* differently: it is *kedei she-yitol chefetz mi-tachas margelosav u-maniach tachas merashosav* — sufficient space to take an object from beneath one's feet and place it beneath one's head. This evokes, perhaps intentionally, the imagery of *Yaakov* lying down with something beneath his head.

The *gemara* then asks *mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between these two formulations? The answer, according to *Rashi*, is that they disagree about whether the four *amot* are precisely measured (*metzumtzamot*) or allow for a slight additional margin, since the act of stretching may yield fractionally more. The *gemara* had already discussed how different measuring instruments used in the *Beit Hamikdash* reflected varying degrees of precision — whether the fingers are held tightly or loosely, for instance — and those distinctions carry over here.

### Amah Size

Regarding the physical dimensions involved: the most widely accepted measurements today are those of *Rav Chaim Na'eh*, whose analysis was uniquely rigorous and has been supported by archaeological evidence. Most significant among these are the *mikvaot* discovered at *Masada*. Given the extreme scarcity of water there, those *mikvaot* were presumed to be no larger than halachically necessary — and their dimensions corresponded precisely to the standard *mikvah* measurement of three *amot* by one *amah*, using *Rav Chaim Na'eh*'s figures. According to his measurements, one *amah* is approximately a foot and a half (a *tefach* being roughly 3.1–3.2 inches, or just over eighteen inches per *amah*). Some authorities are *machmir* and follow larger measurements, but *Rav Chaim Na'eh*'s figures are generally accepted as the most accurate baseline.

### Tosafot Height

This raises an obvious difficulty: if one *amah* is approximately a foot and a half, then *shalosh amot* — the stated height of a person's body — amounts to only four and a half feet. While people were somewhat shorter in antiquity, this figure is still puzzlingly low. *Tosafot*, in the context of the *gemara* in *Bava Basra* which requires a privacy wall (*mechitza* for *hezek re'iyah*) to be four *amot* tall, poses the question: if a person is only *shalosh amot*, why does the wall need to be four? *Tosafot*'s first answer is that the additional *amah* accounts for someone standing on their toes to peer over — *she-magbi'a akeivo ve-omed al etzbe'ot raglav* — "lifting one's heel and standing on one's toes." *Tosafot*'s more fundamental answer, however, is that *shalosh amot* refers to shoulder height, and the head adds approximately one more *amah*, bringing the total to four. This interpretation does create some tension with other sources — notably the *gemara*'s description of the *mikvah* dimensions and the assumption that a person submerged there would need to crouch — but it is generally accepted as the correct reading. Similarly, *Tosafot* notes that the *gemara* elsewhere states the length of the *kuchin* — the burial niches in catacomb walls — is four *amot*, which, including the head, confirms this understanding.

## Subjective Measurement

### Rav Mesharsheya

The *gemara* now introduces a question posed by *Rav Mesharsheya* to his son before the son went to study with *Rav Papa*: *arba amot she'amru — ba'amah didei yahavinan lei?* — do we measure a person's four *amot* using that individual's own *amah* (i.e., the length from his own elbow to the tip of his middle finger), or *ba'amah shel kodesh* — by the standard fixed *amah*, which was preserved like an authoritative reference measure in the *Beit Hamikdash*? The question has conceptual force: the four *amot* are derived from *tachtav* — your space — suggesting that the measurement is inherently personal and subjective.

### Og Problem

Rav Mesharsheya prepares his son with a counterargument for each possible answer. If *Rav Papa* says we use the standard *amah*, the rejoinder is: *Og Melech HaBashan matei lei* — what would you do with Og, King of Bashan? For an extraordinarily large person, four standard *amot* barely allows movement, defeating the entire purpose of granting space to allow a person to function. This functions as a *reductio ad absurdum*, but the point is genuine: if the idea is to give a person enough room to move, perhaps the measurement must scale with the person.

### Kelim Mishnah

Conversely, if *Rav Papa* says we use the individual's own *amah*, the son should ask: *mai ta'ama lo tanya gabei yesh ameru* — why, then, is this not listed among the measurements that go by the individual in the *mishnah* in *Kelim*? That *mishnah* enumerates several halachic quantities that are measured according to the specific person performing the act — such as the *kometz* taken by the *kohen* from the *mincha*, or *melo chofnav*, the double-handful of *ketoret* brought by the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. If the four *amot* were truly subjective, one would expect them to appear on that list. Their absence is at least a question that demands an answer.

### Rav Papa Response

When the son arrived before *Rav Papa* with these questions, *Rav Papa*'s initial reaction was: *iddaykinan kulei hai lo havei taninan* — if we were to spend all our time on such *diyukim*, we would never actually learn anything. Some explain this response as directed specifically at the method of the question: the concern that something was missing from a list is not a strong *diyuk*, since there are many reasons an item might be omitted from any given *mishnah*. One should not be overly troubled by a list's silence.

### Rav Papa Rules

Nevertheless, *Rav Papa* proceeds to rule: *le'olam be'amah didei yahavinan lei* — in fact, we do measure by each individual's own *amah*, which is a striking and relatively rare instance of halachic subjectivity. As for why the four *amot* do not appear in the *Kelim* list: *delo pesika lei* — the rule is not universally consistent, and therefore could not be included in a categorical list. Specifically, *mishum de'ika nanas be'eivarav* — there may be a person with disproportionately foreshortened limbs. The *gemara* does not mean a person of small stature generally; presumably a small person would simply receive fewer *amot* in proportion. Rather, it refers to a large-framed person whose arms are unusually short relative to their body, such that measuring by their personal *amah* would actually grant them *less* space than is appropriate. In that case, we would revert to the standard *amah shel kodesh*. Because the rule admits of this exception, it cannot be stated as an unqualified categorical principle — and that is why it was left off the *Kelim* list.

## Overlapping Zones

### Two People Overlap

The *mishnah* addresses the case of two people whose respective four-*amot* zones partially overlap. Each person may make use of the shared, overlapping space. However, the space at the far edge of each person's zone — the area that lies behind one person and is entirely outside the other's zone — remains inaccessible to the other. Neither person may even walk into that external space, let alone carry objects there.

### Three Courtyards

The *mishnah* compares this to a case of three courtyards arranged in a row, each opening into the next, with all three also opening into a *reshut harabim*. The opening to the *reshut harabim* is essential to the analogy: if the outer courtyards had no independent access to the public domain and could only be reached through each other, the domains would not be fully independent and the rules of *eruvin* would operate differently. Where each courtyard has its own opening to the *reshut harabim*, the middle courtyard (B) may make an *eruv* with A and independently make an *eruv* with C, just as the middle person's zone overlaps with both neighbors. In each pairing, the combined space is accessible to those members. But A and C remain entirely separate from one another.

### Confusion Concern

The Rabbis push back, however, and here the three-people analogy and the three-courtyard case diverge. The Rabbis accept the three-people arrangement — they do not dispute that each person may move within their respective combined zone — but they reject the analogous courtyard arrangement. Their stated reason: *avshu dayarin* — there are many inhabitants in courtyards, whereas in the three-person case there are not.

### Rashi Explanation

*Rashi* explains the Rabbis' reasoning as follows. When only three individuals are involved, the boundaries are manageable — each person can remember precisely where his zone ends and where he must stop. But in a courtyard setting, the number of people is large, the shared space is complex, and the lines between what belongs to AB and what belongs to BC are easily blurred. If residents of A are permitted to carry freely into B, someone will inevitably forget the limits and carry further, into C. It is precisely that concern — the inability of a large, loosely connected population to reliably track overlapping but non-unified domains — that leads the Rabbis to prohibit the arrangement entirely in the courtyard case. Notably, this is the inverse of the usual logic: we sometimes assume that many people will remind one another of the rules. Here, the concern is not one of individual forgetfulness but of structural confusion among many people moving through interconnected spaces with competing loyalties.

## Three Courtyard Eruv

### Unification Problem

This leads the *gemara* to examine why, if B makes an *eruv* with both A and C, the three courtyards cannot simply unify into one. If the *eruv* food of AB and the *eruv* food of BC are both placed in B, they are both located within a single courtyard — so should not B function as a single hub, effectively making A, B, and C one domain?

### Rav Yehuda Answer

*Rav Yehuda* resolves this: the case under discussion is one where the middle courtyard's *eruv* food was placed not in B, but in A and C respectively. That is, the AB *eruv* was deposited in A, and the BC *eruv* was deposited in C. Since the two *eruvin* reside in separate outer courtyards rather than together in B, there is no common locus to unify the three into one domain. A and C remain independent anchors, and B simply mediates between two distinct partnerships.

### Rav Sheshes Answer

*Rav Sheshes* offers a different explanation: even if both *eruvin* were placed within courtyard B, the problem persists — *kegon shenasnuho bishnei batim* — because they were placed in two separate houses within B. Let us say the AB *eruv* was placed in one house within B (belonging to one resident, call him Fred) and the BC *eruv* was placed in another house (belonging to a second resident, John). Fred's house and John's house are separate structures. The AB domain is effectively Fred's space, and the BC domain is John's space; the two do not merge into one.

### Two Houses Rule

To support this, the *gemara* cites a dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* regarding five people in a courtyard who collected their *eruv* food and placed it in two vessels. *Beit Shammai* rules the *eruv* invalid — all food must be in one vessel. *Beit Hillel* rules it valid. The *gemara* then clarifies that *Beit Hillel*'s leniency is limited: they validate two vessels only when both are in the same house. The logic of an *eruv* is to establish a single unified domain anchored in one location. *Bishnei keilim b'bayis echad* — two vessels in one house — can still achieve that conceptual unity. *Bishnei batim* — two vessels in two separate houses — cannot, even for *Beit Hillel*. Accordingly, *Rav Sheshes*'s case, where the *eruv* food of AB and BC sits in two different houses within B, fails to create a unified ABC domain even according to the lenient opinion.

### Shlichus Challenge

*Rav Aha brei d'Rav Avya* challenges both explanations before *Rav Ashi*. According to *Rav Yehuda*'s scenario, in which AB's *eruv* is in A and BC's *eruv* is in C: once A and B have made their *eruv*, they become one unified entity — call it the AB nation. When the residents of B then go on to make an *eruv* with C, they are acting not merely as residents of B but as agents (*shlichus*) of the entire AB unit. Should not that *shlichus* extend the AB unity to encompass C as well, making everything one domain? According to *Rav Sheshes*'s scenario, the challenge is equally pointed: if the *eruv* food of AB and BC are both in courtyard B — in Fred's house and John's house respectively — then it is as if there are two separate *reshuyot* operating within a single courtyard, with some residents of B affiliated with A and others with C. This should logically be treated like a case where one member of a courtyard failed to participate in the *eruv*, which invalidates the entire arrangement. Why, then, is the outcome not that nobody in B can carry at all?

## Rav Ashi Resolution

### Non-Participation

*Rav Ashi* resolves both difficulties. Against *Rav Yehuda*: the *shlichus* argument fails because A's non-participation in the BC *eruv* is itself expressive. *Galya da'atei debeni nicha lei u-veni lo nicha lei* — A's conduct reveals its intentions. A was willing to make an *eruv* with B, but explicitly sat out when B joined with C. That deliberate non-participation signals that A did not wish to unify with C. Consequently, when B exercised its *shlichus* in forming a partnership with C, it could not be said to represent A. The two *eruvin* therefore remain genuinely separate, and no single ABC domain emerges.

### Leniency Not Stringency

Against *Rav Sheshes*: *im amru diyurin lehakeil, yomru diyurin lehachmir?* — just because we apply a legal fiction for purposes of leniency does not mean we must apply the same fiction to generate stringency. The rule that AB's *eruv* treats everyone as if residing in Fred's house, and BC's *eruv* treats everyone as if residing in John's house, is a dispensation adopted *lehakeil* — to permit carrying — not a hard structural reality. In reality, every actual resident of courtyard B is participating in their respective *eruv*; no one has truly "sat out." We therefore have no basis to treat this as though some member of B failed to participate, which would be the ground for invalidating the arrangement entirely. The fiction of shared residence is invoked when it helps; it is not pressed into service to create a *chumra* where none exists in practice.

## Domain Serving Principle

### Rabbi Shimon

*Rav Yehuda* citing *Rav* now introduces the position of Rabbi Shimon, identifying it as the underlying framework of the entire preceding discussion. Rabbi Shimon holds that the middle courtyard B may indeed form an *eruv* with A and separately with C, and that within each pairing, movement and carrying are permitted. This is the position illustrated by the analogy to three people with overlapping four-*amot* zones, where Rabbi Shimon says to the Rabbis: just as you agree the three-person arrangement works, so too the three-courtyard arrangement should work.

### Sages Objection

The *chachamim* disagree with Rabbi Shimon on a principled basis, stated as a maxim: *reshut achas meshameshet lishtei reshuyot* — one domain may serve two — *aval lo shtei reshuyot meshameshos lereshut achas* — but two domains may not both serve one. The formulation is compact enough to generate entirely different readings, as reflected in the dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot*.

### Rashi Conceptual

*Rashi* understands the principle as conceptual in nature. The problem with allowing B to carry into both A and C is that B thereby reveals itself to be pulled in contradictory directions. B is simultaneously allied with A and allied with C — two distinct and unrelated domains — and this internal contradiction undermines B's own unity. If B carries into A, it is expressing that it is essentially part of A's domain; if it then carries into C, it is expressing affiliation with C. These two affiliations cannot coexist coherently. By contrast, A carrying into B, and C carrying into B, presents no contradiction: B simply functions as a shared space, with A and C each treating it as an extension of their own domain. That is coherent. But for B to carry in both directions is self-contradictory — *reshut achas*, B, cannot serve *shtei reshuyot*, both A and C, in the sense of actively carrying into both.

### Tosafot Practical

*Tosafot* reads the principle in the opposite direction and for entirely practical reasons. *Tosafot* does not frame this as a conceptual contradiction but as a concern about what will inevitably follow in practice. If A is permitted to carry into B, then someone from A will eventually carry further into C — the intermediate space of B offers no natural stopping point that people will reliably observe. Similarly, if C carries into B, the same slippage toward A will occur. *Tosafot* therefore rules that B may carry into A, and B may carry into C — taking items *away* from the problematic shared zone is not the concern — but A may not carry into B, and C may not carry into B, because that creates the risk of cascading confusion. This is precisely the inverse of *Rashi*'s ruling, and the disagreement is fundamental: *Rashi* sees the prohibition falling on B's outward carrying, while *Tosafot* sees it falling on A's and C's inward carrying.
