# Kiddushin 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gerim & Authority

### Ger in Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָּנֵיהּ דְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה גִּיּוֹרָא הֲוָה, וַהֲוָה קָא מִנְּצֵי אִיהוּ וְרַב בִּיבִי, מָר אָמַר: אֲנָא עָבֵידְנָא סְרָרוּתָא דְמָתָא, וּמַר אָמַר: אֲנָא עָבֵידְנָא סְרָרוּתָא דְמָתָא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף אֲמַר לְהוּ: תְּנֵינָא: ״שׂוֹם תָּשִׂים עָלֶיךָ מֶלֶךְ... מִקֶּרֶב אַחֶיךָ״, כׇּל מְשִׂימוֹת שֶׁאַתָּה מֵשִׂים – לֹא יִהְיֶה אֶלָּא מִקֶּרֶב אַחֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Adda bar Ahava's host was** the son of **a convert, and he and Rav Beivai were quarreling. One said: I** will **perform the service of the city,** i.e., I will be appointed to a position of authority, **and one said: I** will **perform the service of the city. They came before Rav Yosef** to decide between them. Rav Yosef **said to them: We learned: "You shall set him king over you,** whom the Lord your God shall choose; **one from among your brothers"** (Deuteronomy 17:15). The repetition of the verb "set" in the verse [*som tasim*] indicates: **All appointments that you appoint may be only from among your brothers.** Therefore, a convert may not serve in any official position.

The present daf begins at the top of 77a, though our shiur opened by returning briefly to yesterday's material, which carried weighty halachic implications regarding *gerim*. The central teaching from the previous daf concerned the prohibition against appointing a *ger* to a position of authority. The Gemara derives this from the verse *som tasim alecha melech mikerev achicha* — "you shall surely place upon yourself a king from among your brethren" — understanding *mikerev achicha* as excluding a *ger* from such a role. The analogy to the American constitutional requirement that a president be a "naturally born citizen" is apt, though even that phrase has proven surprisingly contested in modern legal discourse.

### Rav Moshe Serarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: וַאֲפִילּוּ אִמּוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִמּוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל מִקֶּרֶב אַחֶיךָ קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ. הִלְכָּךְ רַב בִּיבִי דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּא הוּא – לִיעַיֵּין בְּמִילֵּי דִשְׁמַיָּא, וּמָר – לִיעַיֵּין בְּמִילֵּי דְמָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to** Rav Yosef: **And** does this *halakha* apply **even** if the **mother** of the person in question is born **Jewish?** In other words, does this apply to one whose father is a convert? Rav Yosef **said to him:** If **his mother is** born **Jewish,** the words: **"From among your brothers" are said about him. Therefore,** now that it has been determined that this person's mother was born Jewish and that he is fit to serve a public role, **Rav Beivai, who is a great man** in Torah learning, **should oversee the matters of Heaven,** i.e., the public issues that involve the performance of mitzvot; **and the Master,** Rav Adda bar Ahava's host, **should oversee the** other **matters of the city.**

The Rambam extends this principle further, deriving from a *drasha* — apparently sourced in a lost *Midrash Halacha* — the phrase *melech velo malkah*, that the prohibition applies equally to women. This gives rise to contemporary discussions about women and *serarah*. The Young Israel's standing policy that neither women nor converts may serve as presidents of a synagogue congregation drew considerable attention when publicized, with the public finding the restriction on *gerim* more surprising than that on women — a telling reversal of contemporary sensibilities.

One of the most important responsa on this topic is a *teshuvah* of Rav Moshe Feinstein, addressing whether a *ger* may serve as a *rosh yeshiva*. Rav Moshe opens his responsum with a striking methodological framing: on the one hand, the Gemara prohibits a *ger* from holding positions of *serarah*; on the other hand, the Torah commands *ahavat hager* — love of the convert — and legislates *mishpat echad yihyeh lachem kager k'ezrach ha'aretz*, that one law shall apply equally to the native-born and the convert alike. Rav Moshe argues that this obligation to love the *ger* and treat him equally compels us to understand the *issur* of *serarah* as narrowly as possible. From that premise, he proceeds to argue that a *rosh yeshiva* does not constitute a position of *serarah* in the relevant halakhic sense. His broader principle is that positions people enter voluntarily — elected positions or those within institutions one chooses to join — are not the kind of imposed coercive authority the Gemara has in mind. The Gemara's concern is with authority imposed from above, not with roles entered into by mutual consent.

### Ger Identity Descent

A further observation from yesterday's Gemara bears directly on our current discussions of *yichus*. The Gemara had recorded a case in which the resolution was that the individual in question — referred to as a *ger* — could serve in a position of authority because *imo miyisrael havay*, his mother was Jewish. This raises an obvious and pointed question: if his mother was Jewish, why is he called a *ger* at all?

One possibility is that his father was a *ger*, and the Gemara earlier established that *ger* identity — at least for certain purposes, such as the *issur* involving *mamzer* — can extend through generations even among descendants of *gerim*. Perhaps the Gemara uses the term *ger* even for someone whose mother is *miyisrael* because his father was a *ger*, and that identity lingers in some form. A more radical possibility, however, is that the Gemara is using the term *ger* loosely, to denote someone whose parentage is not entirely of Jewish birth — not in the technical sense of one who underwent conversion, but in the broader sense of having some non-Jewish parentage. This reading would imply that even partial non-Jewish paternal lineage suffices to invoke certain *ger*-related concerns. There is indeed a position among the *Rishonim* — admittedly not the accepted *halacha* — that when the father is not Jewish but the mother is, the child actually requires conversion. We do not rule this way; matrilineal descent suffices. But the Gemara's use of the term *ger* for someone whose mother is Jewish remains genuinely puzzling and raises real questions about what *ger* identity means across generations.

### Rav Bibi Division

In the end, the Gemara resolved the case by dividing responsibilities: Rav Bibi was appointed to oversee *milei d'shmaya* — matters of a religious character — while the *ger* was given responsibility for *milei d'alma*, the more mundane civic affairs. There is a parallel Gemara that describes the proper internal division of household responsibilities along similar lines, with the wife governing the *milei d'alma* and the husband the *milei d'shmaya*. As the joke goes: some husbands have been waiting for decades for their wives to tell them what constitutes a *milei d'shmaya*.

## Yichus Registers

### Register Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף מִי שֶׁהָיָה וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? דָּיְיקִי וּמַחְתְּמִי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yosei says: Even** the descendants of **one who** had signed as a witness in the Old Court of Tzippori does not need to have their lineage investigated. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers: In that city, they would first **examine** witnesses and only afterward **have them sign.** Consequently, anyone who signed as a witness in Tzippori must certainly have been of unflawed lineage.

Picking up approximately ten lines from the bottom of 76b, the Gemara continues its discussion of *yichus* — at what point and in what circumstances lineage must be verified. Rabbi Yosei states that one need not investigate the lineage of anyone listed in the register of *arkhei hayeshana*. Rashi explains that *yeshana* is the name of a town near Tzippori. The register in question, according to Rashi, was one of judges rather than witnesses — though the functional point is the same: only individuals whose *yichus* had been verified would have been admitted to such a register. The Gemara explains the rationale with the phrase *dayka u'machtmi* — they were careful before accepting people as signatories, so anyone found in that register can be trusted to have already been cleared.

### David's Troops

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּחֲיָילוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית דָּוִד. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קְרָא? ״וְהִתְיַחְשָׂם בַּצָּבָא בַּמִּלְחָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus** says: Even the descendants of one who was written in the army list of the Jewish king does not have their lineage investigated. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The reference is to one who was written **in** the list of **the military troops of the House of David,** who were all of pure lineage. **Rav Yosef said: What is the verse** from which it is derived? The phrase is: **"Reckoned by lineage for service in war"** (I Chronicles 7:40).

Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus adds another category: *istratya shel melech*, someone listed in the king's military register, is also presumed to have verified lineage. The Gemara, in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel, specifies that this refers to *be'chaylot shel beit David* — the troops of King David. Service in David's army was restricted to those of distinguished *yichus*. This recalls a historical pattern in which military officership was a hereditary, aristocratic position, with the nobility occupying command roles by virtue of lineage.

### Veyityachsam

Rav Yosef identifies the scriptural basis: the verse *veyityachsam batzava bamilchama* — "and they were affiliated by lineage in the army, in the war." The very word *yichus* appears in this context, denoting the formal genealogical identification of soldiers in David's military.

### Merit Ancestors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא זְכוּתָן וּזְכוּת אֲבוֹתָם מְסַיַּיעְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** for this requirement that they be of unflawed lineage? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is **in order that their merit and the merit of their ancestors will help them** in battle.

The Gemara, in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav, explains the reason: *kedei shetehei zechutan vezechut avoteihem mesayatun* — so that both their own merit and the merit of their forefathers would come to their aid in battle. The underlying logic is that military success is not solely a function of physical prowess and tactical skill; divine assistance matters, and one who brings with him the accumulated *zechut* of righteous ancestors is a more potent asset on the battlefield.

### Non-Israelite Warriors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא צֶלֶק הָעַמּוֹנִי, מַאי לָאו דְּאָתֵי מֵעַמּוֹן? לָא, דְּיָתֵיב בְּעַמּוֹן. וְהָאִיכָּא אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי, מַאי לָאו דְּאָתֵי מֵחֵת? לָא, דְּיָתֵיב בְּחֵת. וְהָאִיכָּא אִתַּי הַגִּתִּי! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי דְּיָתֵיב בְּגַת, וְהָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִתַּי הַגִּיתִּי בָּא וּבִטְּלָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't there Zelek the Ammonite,** one of David's warriors (II Samuel 23:37); **what, is it not** indicated **that** he was a convert who **came from Ammon?** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** his name indicates only **that he dwelled in Ammon,** but he was born a Jew. The Gemara asks: **But isn't there Uriah the Hittite** (II Samuel 23:39); **what, is it not** indicated **that** he **came from Heth?** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** his name indicates only **that he dwelled in Heth. But isn't there Ittai the Gittite** (II Samuel 15:19)? **And if you would say** that **so too** his name indicates **that he dwelled in Gath** but was born a Jew, **but doesn't Rav Naḥman say,** to explain how David could make use of the crown of the idol of Ammon in apparent violation of the prohibition against deriving benefit from idolatry: **Ittai the Gittite came and nullified its** status of an idol. The *halakha* is that only a gentile can nullify an idol, by doing something degrading to it. This indicates that Ittai the Gittite must have been a gentile.

This raises an immediate difficulty from Tanach itself. Several of David's most celebrated warriors appear to be of non-Israelite descent. Tzelek is identified as *ha-Ammoni* — does this not indicate he descended from Ammon? The Gemara answers: no, it means he *dwelt* in Ammon. Similarly, Uriah is called *ha-Chiti* — the Gemara answers that this too denotes his place of residence, not his ethnic origin. But then there is Itai *ha-Gitti*, identified as from Gat. Should we say the same — that he merely lived there? The Gemara cannot, because Rav Nachman explicitly states that Itai *ha-Gitti* came and annulled the idol worship of a certain locality — a detail Rashi explains as indicating that he had himself been an idolater, establishing that he genuinely descended from Gat and was not originally Jewish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת יְלָדִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְדָוִד, וְכוּלָּם בְּנֵי יְפַת תּוֹאַר הָיוּ, וְכוּלָּם מִסְתַּפְּרִים קוֹמֵי, וּמְגַדְּלִים בְּלוֹרִית הָיוּ, וְכוּלָּם יוֹשְׁבִים בִּקְרוֹנִיּוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב וְהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִים בְּרָאשֵׁי גְיָיסוֹת, וְהֵן הֵן בַּעֲלֵי אֶגְרוֹפִים שֶׁל בֵּית דָּוִד! דְּאָזְלִי לְבַעוֹתֵי עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again questions the statement that all of the soldiers in David's army were of unflawed lineage. **And further, Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: David had four hundred youths** in his camp, **all sons of beautiful women,** i.e., born to women captured in war, who were therefore gentiles, **all of** whom **had their hair cut** in the ***komei*** style **or** who **grew** their hair in **a gentile hairstyle [*belorit*]** on the back of their heads, **and all of them sat in gold carts [*bikroniyyot*] and would march at the head of troops** in David's army; **and these very ones were the strong men of the House of David,** i.e., David would rely on their strength. This states that David's army included men of flawed lineage. The Gemara answers: These four hundred youths did not fight in the battles, but rather **they would go forth** in front of the troops **in order to frighten everyone.**

There is also the tradition that David had four hundred young men — not biological children, as Rashi clarifies, but soldiers in his retinue — all of whom were *benei yifat to'ar*, descendants of women taken captive in war. These men would cut their hair in styles associated with non-Jewish practice and sat in golden wagons, leading the armies into battle. The Gemara resolves the contradiction by limiting their role: *de-azli leva'utei alma* — they went to the battlefield not to fight but to strike fear into the enemy. The actual combatants were the soldiers of pure lineage; these men served as a kind of psychological vanguard. Whether this resolution is fully satisfying is another matter — the Gemara has its answer, even if the evidence from Tanach pulls in multiple directions.

## Bat Chalal Status

### Chalal Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּת חָלָל זָכָר – פְּסוּלָה מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה לְעוֹלָם. יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֲלָלָה – בִּתּוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה לַכְּהוּנָּה. חָלָל שֶׁנָּשָׂא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל – בִּתּוֹ פְּסוּלָה לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The daughter of a male *ḥalal* is unfit to** marry into **the priesthood forever.** In other words, all daughters of male descendants of a *ḥalal* are prohibited from marrying priests, as they have the status of *ḥalalot*. If there was **an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*, his daughter is fit to** marry into **the priesthood,** whereas if there was **a *ḥalal* who married a Jewish woman, his daughter is unfit to** marry into **the priesthood.**

We arrive now at the *Mishna* at the top of 77a. The subject is the status of a *chalal* and those descended from him. By way of introduction: the Torah explicitly prohibits a *kohen* from marrying certain categories of women. The verse states *isha zona vechalala lo yikachu ve-isha gerusha me-isha lo yikachu* — a *kohen* may not marry a *zonah*, a *chalala*, or a *gerusha*. A *gerusha* requires no elaboration. The definitions of *zonah* and *chalala*, however, demand careful examination.

A *chalala* is defined in two related ways. First, she is a woman born from a union that is forbidden specifically to a *kohen* — for example, the child of a *kohen* and a *gerusha*, or a *kohen* and a *chalala*, or a *kohen* and a *zonah*. Second, there is the question of whether the *chalal* or *chalala* status, once established, passes to subsequent generations through the *chalal* himself, even in cases where no forbidden act of intercourse has occurred. A *zonah*, for our purposes, is the product of a forbidden act of intercourse that is not unique to *kohanim* — such as an act of *arayot* — or the offspring of a union involving a *mamzer*; we will return to the *zonah* category in the Gemara.

### Chalal L'Olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי לְעוֹלָם? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִידֵּי דַּהֲוָה אַמִּצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי, מָה לְהַלָּן לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the meaning of the statement that the daughter of a *ḥalal* is unfit **forever?** The Gemara explains: It is necessary **lest you say** that the *halakha* should be **just as it is in** the case of **an Egyptian** convert **and an Edomite** convert, and that **just as there,** with regard to an Egyptian and an Edomite, their descendants are permitted to enter into the congregation **after three generations, so too here,** the daughter of a descendant of a *ḥalal* should **also** be allowed to marry into the priesthood **after three generations.** The mishna therefore **teaches us** that this prohibition is permanent.

The *Mishna* states: *bat chalal zachar pesulah min hakehunah le-olam* — the daughter of a male *chalal* is forever forbidden to the *kehunah*. The operative word is *le-olam* — forever, across generations. This requires explanation.

If a *chalal* has a child and it is a son, that son is likewise a *chalal*, yet his being a *chalal* in the *Yisrael* context carries no practical consequence on its own. He functions as a carrier — analogous, as it were, to a recessive genetic trait. The male line continues to carry the status without it manifesting. But when the line finally produces a daughter, she becomes a *chalala*, forbidden to a *kohen*. That is what *le-olam* means: however many generations of sons intervene, the status persists through them, waiting to manifest in the first daughter.

### L'Olam Mechanism

The mechanism is striking in its asymmetry: males carry the status but do not personally suffer its prohibition in any meaningful way (since being a *chalal* matters for a *Yisrael* mainly in the sense of not having *kedushat kehunah* to begin with); females receive the status and are forbidden to *kohanim*, but do not transmit it further. Males transmit without manifesting; females manifest without transmitting. Once the status reaches a daughter, it ends with her — she does not pass it on.

### Yisrael Chalala

The *Mishna* continues: *Yisrael shenasa chalala bito kesherah lakehunah* — if a *Yisrael* marries a *chalala*, the daughter is fit for *kehunah*. The status does not pass through the mother. Transmission is exclusively patrilineal. This is not a case of a forbidden act of intercourse — a *Yisrael* is entirely permitted to marry a *chalala* — and since the father has no *chalal* status, the daughter acquires none. The mother's status simply does not descend.

## Parallel Chalal Cases

### Rabbi Yehuda Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּת גֵּר זָכָר כְּבַת חָלָל זָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: The daughter of a male convert is like the daughter of a male *ḥalal*,** and she is also prohibited from marrying into the priesthood.

The *Mishna* now introduces a parallel set of laws concerning *gerim*. Rabbi Yehuda states: *bat ger zachar* — the daughter of a male *ger* who has a child with a *Yisraelit* woman — is like the daughter of a *chalal* in this respect: her status renders her forbidden to a *kohen*, like a *giyoret*. This is a striking ruling. A man who converted and subsequently married a fully Jewish woman has a daughter who, according to Rabbi Yehuda, cannot marry a *kohen*. The *ger* status, transmitted through the male line, gives the child a *giyoret* classification sufficient to disqualify her from *kehunah*. This is consistent, by the way, with the earlier Gemara where a person whose mother was Jewish was still referred to as a *ger* — apparently because the father was a *ger*, that identity can persist. And if a *kohen* had married this woman, he would need to divorce her.

### Rabbi Eliezer Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּשָׂא גִּיּוֹרֶת – בִּתּוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה לַכְּהוּנָּה, וְגֵר שֶׁנָּשָׂא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל – בִּתּוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה לַכְּהוּנָּה, אֲבָל גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא גִּיּוֹרֶת – בִּתּוֹ פְּסוּלָה לַכְּהוּנָּה. אֶחָד גֵּר וְאֶחָד עֲבָדִים מְשׁוּחְרָרִים אֲפִילּוּ עַד עֲשָׂרָה דּוֹרוֹת, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא אִמּוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov** disagrees and **says:** If there was **an Israelite who married a** female **convert, his daughter is fit to** marry into **the priesthood, and** similarly if there was **a convert who married a Jewish woman, his daughter is fit to** marry into **the priesthood. But** if there was a male **convert who married** a female **convert, his daughter is unfit to** marry into **the priesthood.** With regard to **both converts and emancipated** Canaanite **slaves,** their daughters are unfit to marry into the priesthood **even up to ten generations.** This *halakha* applies to the offspring **until his mother is** born **Jewish.**

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees. He holds that for the purposes of *kehunah*, the *ger* status does not pass down if either parent was born Jewish. A *Yisrael* who married a *giyoret* — the daughter is fit. A *ger* who married a *bat Yisrael* — the daughter is fit. The *ger* or *giyoret* status is not transmitted to the next generation unless *both* parents are *gerim*. The one case where it does pass down: *ger shenasa giyoret bito pesulah lakehunah* — the daughter of a *ger* and a *giyoret* is forbidden to a *kohen*.

It is worth noting here that the ordinary reason given for why a *giyoret* is forbidden to a *kohen* — concerns about her conduct prior to conversion — would obviously be irrelevant to the daughter of *gerim*. The application of the prohibition to second-generation descendants of *gerim* is therefore much more about a continuing identity status than about any behavioral concern, and the question of where precisely this is derived from the *psukim* becomes important.

### Imo Miyisrael

The Gemara adds a clarifying statement: *echad ger ve-echad avadim meshucharim afilu ad asarah dorot ad shetehei imo miyisrael* — whether a *ger* or a freed slave, even through ten generations, the disqualification persists *ad shetehei imo miyisrael* — until the mother is born Jewish. Here again the phrase *imo miyisrael* recurs, echoing the earlier discussion. There is a slight tension, however: the *Mishna* had indicated that either parent being born Jewish suffices to break the transmission — yet this formulation mentions only the mother, not the father. The asymmetry is not fully resolved here.

### Rabbi Yosei Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא גִּיּוֹרֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even** if there was a male **convert who married** a female **convert, his daughter is fit to** marry into **the priesthood.**

Rabbi Yosei takes the most lenient position: even the child of a *ger* and a *giyoret* is fit. The *ger* status, in his view, does not transmit at all — it applies only to the first-generation convert himself and does not descend to any subsequent generation under any circumstances.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Amitzri Adomi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֲלָלָה – בִּתּוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה לַכְּהוּנָּה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״וְלֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ בְּעַמָּיו״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״לֹא יִטַּמָּא בַּעַל בְּעַמָּיו״. מָה לְהַלָּן זְכָרִים וְלֹא נְקֵבוֹת, אַף כָּאן זְכָרִים וְלֹא נְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that in the case of **an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*,** his daughter is fit to marry into the priesthood. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon** that **it is stated here: "And he shall not profane his offspring among his people"** (Leviticus 21:15), **and it is stated there: "He shall not defile himself, being a chief man among his people"** (Leviticus 21:4): **Just as there** the prohibition against a priest contracting ritual impurity from a corpse applies to **males but not females; so too here,** with regard to the profanation of his offspring, where the term "among his people" is also employed, it is **males** who are rendered unfit and who render their daughters unfit to marry into the priesthood when they are *ḥalalim*, **but not females.**

The Gemara now asks: what does *le-olam* — the ruling that the *chalal* status persists indefinitely through the male line — come to teach? One might have thought the transmission of this status should be limited, analogous to the cases of *Mitzri* and *Adomi*, where the prohibition lapses after three generations. Perhaps the *chalal* status should similarly expire. The Gemara instructs us that this inference is incorrect — *le-olam* means precisely what it says, with no generational cutoff.

### Velo Yechalel

The Gemara then asks: from where is the transmission of the *chalal* status derived? Rabbi Yochanan cites Rabbi Shimon (or Rabbi Yishmael): the verse states *velo yechalel zaro be-amav* — "he shall not defile his offspring among his people." This verse appears in the context of the *Kohen Gadol* and the *almanah*, and it establishes the foundational principle that the offspring of a *kohen* who violates the marriage prohibitions becomes a *chalal*. From *velo yechalel zaro be-amav* we learn that the child of such a union is *chalal*.

### Tumah Gezeirah Shavah

The Gemara then introduces a *gezeirah shavah*: just as the *issur* of *tumah* for a *kohen* focuses on men — *velo yitama ba'al be-amav* uses the masculine — so too the transmission of *chalal* status focuses on men. The *chalal* status is carried through the male line. The same way the laws of *tumah* are stated in the masculine and emphasize the male, so too the *chalal* status passes down through the father.

### Zaro Both Genders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל תִּישְׁתְּרֵי! מִי כְּתִיב בְּנוֹ? ״זַרְעוֹ״ כְּתִיב, ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ בְּעַמָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that profanation applies only to males, **the daughter of a High Priest** and a widow **should be permitted** to marry into the priesthood, and there should be no status of a *ḥalala*. The Gemara rejects this: **Is it written** that **his son** is profaned? **It is written: "His offspring,"** which includes his daughter, as the verse reads: **"He shall not profane his offspring among his people."** Nevertheless, the daughter of his *ḥalala* daughter, who is already the third generation, is permitted as a result of the verbal analogy.

But does the word *zaro* — "his offspring" — in *velo yechalel zaro be-amav* not include daughters as well? The Gemara confronts a potential objection: if the emphasis is on men, perhaps the status only ever passes to sons and never to daughters. To this the Gemara responds: *mi ketiv beno? zaro ketiv* — the verse does not say "his son," it says "his offspring." *Zaro* encompasses both sons and daughters. The one who *receives* the status can be either a son or a daughter. That is what we learn from *zaro*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת בְּנוֹ תִּישְׁתְּרֵי! כְּתִיב ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ״, מַקִּישׁ זַרְעוֹ לוֹ, מָה הוּא – בִּתּוֹ פְּסוּלָה, אַף בְּנוֹ – בִּתּוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, that his granddaughter is permitted as a result of the verbal analogy, **the daughter of his son should** also **be permitted.** The Gemara answers: **It is written: "He shall not profane his offspring,"** by which the Torah **links his offspring to him: Just as** for **him, his daughter is unfit; so too** for **his son,** the son of a High Priest, **his daughter is unfit.**

A further question: once the status has been transmitted to a son, how do we know it continues to a second generation — specifically, does the son's daughter also receive the status? Perhaps *zaro* refers only to the immediate child. The Gemara responds by pointing to the possessive force of *zaro*: the offspring is likened to the father, and therefore *mahu bito pesulah af beno bito pesulah* — just as the father's daughter receives the *chalal* status, so too the son's daughter receives it, since the son, being a *chalal* himself, is now in the position of the father.

### Male Carriers Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָלָל שֶׁנָּשָׂא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל – בִּתּוֹ פְּסוּלָה. הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא: ״בַּת חָלָל זָכָר פְּסוּלָה מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה לְעוֹלָם״! אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּשָׂא חֲלָלָה, תְּנָא נָמֵי סֵיפָא חָלָל שֶׁנָּשָׂא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if there was **a *ḥalal* who married a Jewish woman, his daughter is unfit** to marry into the priesthood. The Gemara questions this ruling: **Wasn't this** already **taught in the first clause: The daughter of a male *ḥalal* is unfit to** marry into **the priesthood forever?** The Gemara answers: **Since it taught** in **the first clause** about **an Israelite who married a *ḥalala*, it also taught** in **the latter clause** about **a *ḥalal* who married a Jewish woman,** to present the complete ruling.

But then one might ask: if *zaro* includes daughters, why doesn't the daughter also transmit the status to her children? The Gemara answers: this is precisely what the *gezeirah shavah* comes to teach. The emphasis on the male, derived from *tumah*, establishes that while both sons and daughters *receive* the status from a *chalal* father, only males *transmit* it. The *gezeirah shavah* narrows the carrier role to men alone. The daughter receives the *chalala* status and is thereby forbidden to a *kohen*, but she does not pass it to her own children. The chain runs through sons indefinitely, manifesting in daughters but terminating there.

## Parallel Repetition

### Redundancy Challenge

The Gemara raises a question about the structure of the *Mishna* itself. The *Mishna* states in its opening clause *bat chalal zachar pesulah min hakehunah le-olam*, and then later states *chalal shenasa bat yisrael bito pesulah lakehunah*. But this third clause — that the daughter of a male *chalal* who married a *bat Yisrael* is forbidden — seems to repeat what was already established in the first clause. The *Mishna* has already told us that!

### Parallel Structure

The Gemara explains: *aidi d'tana reisha yisrael shenasa chalalah, tana nami seifa chalal shenasa bat yisrael* — since the *Mishna* needed to teach the second case, that a *Yisrael* who married a *chalala* has a fit daughter, it formulated a parallel clause with the reverse scenario: a *chalal* who married a *bat Yisrael* has a disqualified daughter. The parallel literary structure accounts for what would otherwise appear redundant. The *Mishna* wanted two clean parallel cases — *Yisrael* with *chalala* on one side, *chalal* with *bat Yisrael* on the other — even at the cost of apparent redundancy with the opening clause.

## Rabbi Dostai

### Mikveh Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בֶּן יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִקְוֵה טׇהֳרָה לַחֲלָלוֹת, כָּךְ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִקְוֵה טׇהֳרָה לַחֲלָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda says: Just as the sons of Israel** are **a ritual bath of purity for *ḥalalot*,** i.e., the daughter of a *ḥalala* who marries an Israelite does not transmit her status of a *ḥalala*, and their daughters may marry priests, **so the daughters of Israel are a ritual bath of purity for *ḥalalim*,** and their daughters may marry priests.

The Gemara now notes that the *Mishna*'s ruling stands in contrast to the opinion of Rabbi Dostai bar Rabbi Yehuda, cited in a *baraita*: *keshem shebnei Yisrael mikveh tahara l'chalalot, kach bnot Yisrael mikveh tahara l'chalalim* — just as Jewish men function as a *mikveh* of purification for *chalalot*, so Jewish women function as a *mikveh* of purification for *chalalim*. The *mikveh* imagery is admittedly more intuitive in the second half: the child grows within the woman, so she as the containing environment "purifies" the child that emerges. Applied to men, the image is less natural, but the halakhic point is symmetric.

### One Parent Kasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ בְּעַמָּיו״ – בְּעִם אֶחָד הוּא דְּמֵיחֵל, בִּשְׁנֵי עֲמָמִים אֵינוֹ מֵיחֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Rabbi Dostai, son of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara explains that **the verse states: "He shall not profane his offspring among his people"** (Leviticus 21:15), which teaches: **It is among one people that he profanes,** i.e., the offspring is a *ḥalal* only when he and his wife are both profaned, i.e., *ḥalalim*, but **among two peoples he does not profane.** If the mother is of a different people, i.e., not a *ḥalala*, the offspring is of unflawed lineage.

Rabbi Dostai's position is that it does not matter whether the *chalal* is the father or the mother — as long as one parent is not a *chalal*, the child is fit. If the father is a *Yisrael* and the mother is a *chalala*, the child is fit. If the father is a *chalal* and the mother is a *bat Yisrael*, the child is equally fit. The *Mishna*, however, does not rule this way; it holds that when the father is the *chalal*, the status passes to the child regardless of the mother's status.

## Woman Becomes Chalala

### Woman Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זַרְעוֹ, הִיא עַצְמָהּ, מִנַּיִן? אָמַרְתָּ: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, מָה זַרְעוֹ שֶׁלֹּא עָבַר עֲבֵירָה – מִתְחַלֵּל, הִיא שֶׁעָבְרָה עֲבֵירָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמִּתְחַלֶּלֶת?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states that a priest may not engage in intercourse with a woman who is forbidden to him, so that **"he shall not profane his offspring." I have** derived **only** that **his offspring** resulting from a union with a woman forbidden to him is profaned, i.e., has the status of a *ḥalal*; **from where** do I derive that **she herself, i.e.,** the woman who engaged in forbidden intercourse with the priest, is also disqualified from marrying into the priesthood? **You** can **say** it is an ***a fortiori*** inference: **If his offspring, who did not commit a transgression, is profaned, is it not logical that she, who did commit a transgression, is** similarly **profaned?**

The *baraita* raises a further question: the verse *velo yechalel zaro* speaks of *his offspring* — from this we know the child of the forbidden act becomes a *chalal*. But where do we learn that the woman herself, who participated in the forbidden act of intercourse with the *kohen*, also becomes a *chalala*?

### Kal V'Chomer

The Gemara proposes a *kal vachomer*: if the child — who committed no sin — becomes a *chalal*, then surely the woman who participated in the transgression should become a *chalala* as well.

### Kohen Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא, עַצְמוֹ יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁעָבַר עֲבֵירָה וְאֵין מִתְחַלֵּל! מָה לְהוּא, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין מִתְחַלֵּל בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, תֹּאמַר בְּהִיא, שֶׁמִּתְחַלֶּלֶת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **He,** the priest **himself, shall prove** otherwise, **as he committed a transgression but** he is **not profaned.** Although he may not serve in the Temple while he remains married to her, he regains his status of a fit priest once he divorces her. The Gemara rejects this: **What** is notable **about** the priest? He is notable **in that** he is a male, **and in no case is** a male priest **profaned** by engaging in forbidden intercourse. Will **you say** the same **with regard to her,** a woman, **who is disqualified** from marrying a priest **in all cases?** For example, if she engages in intercourse with a man of flawed lineage, she assumes the status of a *zona* and is permanently disqualified from marrying into the priesthood.

The Gemara immediately challenges this *kal vachomer* with a counterexample: let the *kohen* himself prove it. The *kohen* committed the forbidden act of intercourse and yet does not become a *chalal* as a result — his *kehunah* status is unaffected. If the active transgressor (the *kohen*) does not lose status, perhaps the prohibition does not attach to the guilty parties at all, only to the innocent child.

### Status Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר: אָמַר קְרָא ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ״ – לֹא יְחוּלַּל, זֶה שֶׁהָיָה כָּשֵׁר וְנִתְחַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is your wish to say** that this reasoning is faulty, there is a different proof: **The verse states: "He shall not profane his offspring,"** which means that the priest may not engage in intercourse with a woman who is forbidden to him so that someone **shall not become profaned.** The verse is referring to **this** woman **who was** at one time **fit and** then **became profaned.** In other words, the term profanation does not apply to his offspring, as they were never fit to begin with. Rather, it is referring to the woman with whom he engaged in intercourse, as, since she was initially fit to marry into the priesthood, she can be described as becoming profaned.

The Gemara responds by distinguishing the *kohen*'s situation from the woman's. A *kohen*, once born with the status of *kasher kohen*, can never lose his *kehunah* — his status is immutable in that direction. The woman, by contrast, has a halakhic status that is demonstrably changeable — a woman can begin life with a perfectly fit status and acquire a disqualifying one through subsequent events, as we see with *almanah*, *gerusha*, and *zonah*. Since we know that a woman's *kehunah*-related status can change, and since the child's becoming a *chalal* demonstrates that this act produces defilement, the *kal vachomer* holds: the woman too is defiled by the act and becomes a *chalala*.

## Chalala Definition

### Born Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזוֹ הִיא חֲלָלָה – כֹּל שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה מִן הַפְּסוּלִים. מַאי ״פְּסוּלִים״? אִילֵימָא פְּסוּלִים לוֹ, הֲרֵי מַחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ דִּפְסוּלָה לוֹ וּבָנֶיהָ כְּשֵׁרִים, דִּכְתִיב ״תּוֹעֵבָה הִיא״ – הִיא תּוֹעֵבָה, וְאֵין בָּנֶיהָ תּוֹעֲבִים!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: Who is a *ḥalala*?** The term is referring to **any** woman **born from** people of **flawed** lineage. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of people of **flawed** lineage? **If we say** it means she was born from someone **unfit for him,** i.e., a woman forbidden to this particular man due to a family relationship or for some other reason, **isn't there** the case of one who **remarries his divorcée** after she had been married to someone else in the interim; she is **unfit for him, and** yet **her children are fit. As it is written** with regard to this case: **"That is an abomination"** (Deuteronomy 24:4), and this is interpreted to mean: **That** marriage **is an abomination, but the children of** that marriage **are not an abomination** and are entirely fit.

The Gemara now addresses a new *baraita* dealing with the definition of *chalala*. *Tanu Rabbanan*: *eizo hi chalalah? kol she-noldah min hapsulim* — who is a *chalala*? Anyone born from the disqualified ones. The Gemara immediately asks what *psulim* means here.

### Kohen-Specific

If *psulim* means simply any woman forbidden to a particular *kohen* for whatever reason, the scope would be too broad. A man who divorced his wife, she subsequently married another man, and then was divorced from him — if the first husband, who was a *kohen*, took her back, that act is forbidden because she is now *pesulah lo*, but it is not a prohibition unique to *kohanim*. The prohibition of *machazir gerushato* applies to all Israelites alike.

### Machzir Grusha

Yet we know that the children of such a union are *kesherim* — from the verse *to'evah hi*: *hi to'evah*, the woman herself is forbidden, *ve-ein baneha to'avin*, but the prohibition does not extend to her children. This proves that a *chalala* is not simply the product of any forbidden sexual act involving a *kohen*, but specifically of those acts prohibited to *kohanim* as *kohanim*.

### Psul Kehuna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵיזוֹ הִיא חֲלָלָה – כֹּל שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה מִן פְּסוּל כְּהוּנָּה. נוֹלְדָה – אִין, לֹא נוֹלְדָה – לָא? הֲרֵי אַלְמָנָה, וּגְרוּשָׁה, זוֹנָה, דְּלֹא נוֹלְדָה, וְקָא הָוְיָא חֲלָלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: This is** what the Sages are **saying: Who is a *ḥalala*?** The term is referring to **any** woman **born from one unfit for the priesthood.** The Gemara questions this: This implies that one who was **born, yes,** she is a *ḥalala*, but one who was **not born** from one unfit for the priesthood is **not** a *ḥalala*. **Aren't there** the cases of **a widow, or a divorced woman,** or **a *zona*** who engaged in intercourse with a priest? They were **not born** from one who was unfit for the priesthood, **and** yet such a woman **is a *ḥalala*.**

Rav Yehuda therefore reformulates: *eizo hi chalalah? kol she-noldah mi-psul kehunah* — a *chalala* is anyone born from a woman who is specifically forbidden to a *kohen* by reason of her status vis-à-vis the *kehunah*. The disqualification must be one unique to *kohanim*, not a general prohibition that happens to apply to a *kohen* as well.

The Gemara raises an objection: if we define *chalala* as one who is *born* from such a union, what about the woman herself — the *almanah* or *gerusha* who has intercourse with a *kohen*? She is not born from anything; she becomes a *chalala* through the act. And we established above that she does become a *chalala*.

### Never Kasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵיזוֹ חֲלָלָה מוּזְכֶּרֶת שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר כְּלָל? כֹּל שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה מִן פְּסוּל כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: This is** what the Sages are **saying: Who is the *ḥalala* mentioned that did not have a moment of fitness at all,** but was unfit from birth? She is **any** woman **born from one unfit for the priesthood.**

Rava responds with a more precise formulation: *eizo hi chalalah muzkeret?* — what is the *chalala* explicitly mentioned in the *pasuk*? The verse *velo yechalel zaro be-amav* describes a child *she-lo haytah she'at hakosher klal* — one who was never *kasher* to begin with, who was born already disqualified. That is the *chalala* the Torah refers to directly: *kol she-noldah mi-psul kehunah* — anyone born from a union forbidden by the laws of *kehunah*.

### Basic Pshat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מוּזְכֶּרֶת״? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אָבִין: הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵיזוֹ הִיא חֲלָלָה שֶׁעִיקָּרָהּ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וְאֵין צְרִיכִין לְפָרֵשׁ מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים – כֹּל שֶׁנּוֹלְדָה מִן פְּסוּלֵי כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the term: **Mentioned?** Where was she mentioned? **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avin said: This is** what the Sages are **saying: Who is the *ḥalala* whose** prohibition **is rooted in the words of** a verse in **the Torah, and it is not necessary to clarify** her prohibition further **by the words of the Sages?** She is **any** woman **born from one unfit for the priesthood.** With regard to such a woman the verse explicitly states: "He shall not profane his offspring." By contrast, the *halakha* that a woman who engages in forbidden intercourse with a priest becomes a *ḥalala* is not explicit in the Torah, but is learned through an exposition of the Sages.

Rav Yitzchak bar Avin adds a final clarification: *eizo hi chalalah she-ikarah mi-divrei Torah?* — what is the *chalala* whose status is derived from the plain meaning of the Torah, without rabbinic interpretation? *Ve-ain tzerichah le-faresh mi-divrei sofrim* — it requires no supplementary rabbinic derivation. The answer is the same: anyone born from a woman who is specifically forbidden to a *kohen* by the laws of *kehunah*. The *pasuk* states explicitly — do not take an *almanah*, *gerusha*, *chalala*, or *zonah* — *velo yechalel zaro be-amav* — and if you do, the child will be a *chalal*. The plain *pshat* of the *pasuk* requires nothing beyond this.

## Forbidden Statuses

### Four Statuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַלְמָנָה אַלְמָנָה אַלְמָנָה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. גְּרוּשָׁה גְּרוּשָׁה גְּרוּשָׁה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If a High Priest engages in sexual intercourse with **a widow, a widow, a widow, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. Similarly, if a priest engages in intercourse with **a divorcée, a divorcée, a divorcée, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes.

The Gemara now addresses a new *baraita* dealing with the layering of multiple forbidden statuses. The case concerns a *kohen* who has intercourse with a woman who is simultaneously — or who becomes in sequence — an *almanah*, *gerusha*, *chalala*, and *zonah*. How many sets of lashes does he receive? And does the order in which she acquired these statuses matter?

### Three Widow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אַלְמָנָה אַלְמָנָה אַלְמָנָה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. הַאי אַלְמָנָה הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁבָּא עַל אַלְמְנַת רְאוּבֵן וְעַל אַלְמְנַת שִׁמְעוֹן וְעַל אַלְמְנַת לֵוִי, אַמַּאי אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify this *baraita*. **The Master said** in the *baraita*: If a High Priest engages in intercourse with **a widow, a widow, a widow, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of this widow? If we say that he engaged in sexual intercourse** with three widows: **With Reuven's widow, and with Shimon's widow, and with Levi's widow, why is he liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes?

The *baraita* begins with a simpler case: a woman who is a widow three times over — *almanah almanah almanah* — if a *kohen* has intercourse with her, he receives only one set of lashes. The Gemara works to identify what this case actually means. It cannot mean he had intercourse with three different widows in succession, for those are separate bodies and separate acts, yielding obvious multiple liability. And if he had intercourse with a single woman three times — did the interposing warnings between each act define them as separate transgressions? The precedent from the *nazir* who drank wine repeatedly is invoked: only if a separate warning preceded each act does each act constitute an independent transgression. The actual case of *almanah almanah almanah* turns out to be a single woman who was widowed by three consecutive husbands. She is triply an *almanah*, but she is one person and one act — so only one transgression is incurred.

### Separate Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִים, הֲרֵי שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Aren't they separate bodies,** i.e., three different people? **Aren't they labels [*shemot*]** of **separate [*muḥlakim*]** prohibitions, since each one is forbidden in her own right? It is therefore clear that he should receive lashes for each act.

The broader question is then: when a woman carries four distinct forbidden statuses simultaneously, can a single act of intercourse with a *kohen* generate liability for all four simultaneously? In principle, *issur chal al issur* — one forbidden status layering atop another — is a halakhic mechanism that can produce multiple liabilities from a single act. But this mechanism is not universally applicable.

### Issur Mosif

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שֶׁבָּא עַל אַלְמָנָה אַחַת שָׁלֹשׁ בִּיאוֹת. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ – פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אֶלָּא דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ אַכֹּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא – אַמַּאי אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת? וְהָתְנַן: נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״ ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״ וְהוּא שׁוֹתֶה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps it means **that he engaged in three acts of intercourse with one widow.** The Gemara analyzes this possibility: **What are the circumstances? If they did not forewarn him** between each act, it **is obvious** that **he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes, for one must be forewarned in order to be liable to receive lashes, and here he was forewarned only once for the three acts. There would be no need to state this *halakha*. **Rather,** perhaps the case is **that they forewarned him for each and every one** of his acts of intercourse. But if that were the case, **why is he liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nazir* 42a): **A nazirite who was drinking wine** in violation of his naziriteship **the entire day is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If **they said to him: Do not drink, do not drink,** i.e., he was forewarned several times, **and he** nevertheless **drinks, he is liable** to receive lashes **for each and every** time he was forewarned and proceeded to drink.

Rava establishes the governing principle: this *tanna* does not hold the general rule of *issur chal al issur* — that any forbidden status can be imposed upon a pre-existing one. However, the *tanna* does accept *issur mosif* — a status that *broadens* the scope of the prohibition beyond what the prior status covered can be layered on top of it. And this is why the order of the statuses matters.

### Order Broadens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁבָּא עַל אַלְמְנַת רְאוּבֵן שֶׁהָיְתָה אַלְמְנַת שִׁמְעוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה אַלְמְנַת לֵוִי. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא הֲרֵי שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִים – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן גּוּפִים מוּחְלָקִים בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** for a case **where he engaged in sexual intercourse** one time **with Reuven's widow, who was** previously **Shimon's widow, who was** previously **Levi's widow. Lest you say** they are **separate labels** of prohibitions and he should be liable to receive three sets of lashes, since she was widowed from three different people, the *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that **we require separate bodies** for him to receive separate punishments, **and** as that is **not** the case here, he is liable to receive only one set of lashes.

Working through the sequence: a woman who begins as an *almanah* is forbidden to a *Kohen Gadol* but permitted to a regular *kohen*. If she then becomes a *gerusha*, the scope of the prohibition expands — now she is forbidden to any *kohen*, not just the *Kohen Gadol*. Since this is an *issur mosif* — a broadening — the *gerusha* prohibition layers atop the *almanah* prohibition, and the *Kohen Gadol* who has intercourse with her is liable for both. She is still at this point permitted to eat *terumah*, assuming she is a *bat kohen*.

When she then becomes a *chalala* — perhaps because a *kohen* had intercourse with her — the scope broadens again: now she is forbidden from eating *terumah* as well, a new dimension of prohibition previously absent. This broadening allows the *chalala* status to layer atop the *gerusha* status, which had layered atop the *almanah* status. All three are now simultaneously operative.

### Zonah Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה וּגְרוּשָׁה וַחֲלָלָה זוֹנָה, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵם כַּסֵּדֶר – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. זִינְּתָה וְנִתְחַלְּלָה וְנִתְגָּרְשָׁה וְנִתְאַרְמְלָה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** If a High Priest engages in sexual intercourse with a woman who was **a widow, and** then was **a divorcée, and** then was **a *ḥalala*,** and then was **a *zona*, when** the changes to her status occurred **in** that **order,** that she was first widowed, then remarried and was divorced, and subsequently engaged in intercourse with a priest, thereby becoming a *ḥalala*, and then she engaged in intercourse with a gentile or a forbidden relative, thereby becoming a *zona*, the High Priest **is liable** to receive lashes **for each and every one** of these transgressions each time he engages in intercourse with her. By contrast, if she first **became a *zona*** by engaging in intercourse with a gentile or a forbidden relative, **and** then **became a *ḥalala*** by engaging in intercourse with a priest, **and** subsequently **she divorced,** remarried, **and was widowed,** a High Priest who now engages in intercourse with her **is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes.

The final stage — *zonah* — presents a difficulty. She is already fully forbidden to *kohanim* in every respect. In what sense does becoming a *zonah* broaden the prohibition further? Rav Chana bar Katina explains: *v'shem znut poseil b'Yisrael* — there is a form of *znut* that disqualifies a woman even in the context of her marriage to a *Yisrael*. Adultery renders a woman forbidden to her husband, a prohibition applicable to all of Israel, not just to *kohanim*. Although this particular woman's *zonah* status does not render her forbidden to anyone she was not already forbidden to, the conceptual scope of *znut* as a category extends to all Israel — and this broader conceptual reach qualifies it as an *issur mosif* sufficient to allow it to layer atop the prior statuses. Done in this precise order — *almanah*, *gerusha*, *chalala*, *zonah* — all four statuses are operative simultaneously, and a *kohen* who has intercourse with her incurs liability for each. In any other order, the broadening mechanism does not apply, and only a single transgression is incurred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא זוֹנָה – מַאי אִיסּוּר מוֹסִיף אִית בַּהּ? אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר רַב קַטִּינָא: הוֹאִיל וְשֵׁם זְנוּת פּוֹסֵל בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Yet, one could ask: **But** in the case of **a *zona*, what expanded prohibition is there with regard to her?** There is no additional prohibition with a *zona* beyond what is prohibited with regard to a *ḥalala*. **Rav Ḥana bar Rav Ketina said: Since the label of *zenut* disqualifies** her **in Israel,** it is viewed as an expanded prohibition. Although a *zona* as precisely defined in this context, i.e., a woman who engages in sexual intercourse with one who is unfit for her, is prohibited only to priests and does not add any prohibition beyond that of a *ḥalala*, the label of a *zona* in its broader sense, such as a married woman who commits adultery, does disqualify her to an Israelite, namely her husband. Therefore, there is a prohibition with regard to a *zona* that has a greater scope than the prohibition with regard to a *ḥalala*.
