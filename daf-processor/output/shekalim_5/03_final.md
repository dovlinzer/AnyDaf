# Shekalim 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Double Kolbonot

### Meir: Two Fees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן סֶלַע לִיטּוֹל שֶׁקֶל חַיָיב שְׁנֵי קוֹלָּבּוֹנוֹת. אָמַר רִבִּי לָעְזָר. דְרִבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דְרִבִּי מֵאִיר אָמַר. אֶחָד שֶׁקֶל שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן וְאֶחָד סֶלַע שֶׁהוּא נוֹטֵל. רַב אָמַר. דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא. אֶחָד שֶׁקֶל שֶׁהוּא [נוֹתֵן וְאֶחָד שֶׁקֶל שֶׁהוּא] נוֹטֵל וְאֶחָד לִדְבַר תּוֹרָה. עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב שְׁלֹשָׁה קוֹלָּבּוֹנוֹת אִינּוּן. אֲתַא רִבִּי יִרְמְיָה רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק בְּשֵׁם רַב. שְׁלֹשָׁה קוֹלָּבּוֹנוֹת אִינּוּן. אֶחָד שֶׁקֶל שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן וְאֶחָד שֶׁקֶל שֶׁהוּא נוֹטֵל וְאֶחָד לִדְבַר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** "He who gives a tetradrachma to receive back a *sheqel* is liable for two agios." Rebbi Eleazar said, it is Rebbi Meïr's, as Rebbi Meïr said, one for the *sheqel* which he gives and one for the tetradrachma which he takes. Rav said, it is everybody's opinion, one for the *sheqel* which he [gives, one for the *sheqel* which he] takes, and one for the words of the Torah. In Rav's opinion, are there three agios? There came Rebbi Jeremiah, Rebbi Samuel bar Rav Isaac in the name of Rav: There are three agios, one for the *sheqel* which he gives, one for the *sheqel* which he takes, and one for the words of the Torah.

We pick up in the middle of 4b, dealing with a ruling in the *mishnah* concerning Rabbi Meir. Even when someone pays a full *shekel* on behalf of himself and another person, Rabbi Meir insists on two *kolbonot* — two separate fees. The *gemara* establishes that Rabbi Meir's position is that even someone who pays a straight half-*shekel* owes a fee. This is grounded in the idea that the *shekel* of fire that God showed Moshe — the half-*shekel* — must be perfectly pure. Since every real-world, non-Platonic half-*shekel* is somewhat less than perfect, a rounding fee is required. That is Rabbi Meir's rationale.

The *mishnah* then continues to a new case: *natan sela ve'notal shekel* — you gave a *sela* and received back a *shekel*. The terminology here is confusing: in the *mishnah*'s usage, a *sela* is a biblical *shekel*, and a *shekel* is a half-*shekel*. In practical terms, you gave a whole *shekel* and received back a half-*shekel* as change. The *mishnah* rules *chayav sheni kolbonot* — you are obligated in two *kolbonot*. This seems counterintuitive. One might assume this is simply a normal transaction — you give a whole and receive back a half — and that only one fee should apply.

### Rounding vs. Transaction

Why, then, does the *mishnah* require two fees? There are in fact two distinct rationales operating simultaneously. One *kolbon* is the standard rounding fee that Rabbi Meir requires any time a half-*shekel* is paid — the fee for the slight impurity of every real-world coin relative to the Platonic ideal. The second *kolbon* is a transaction fee, a service charge for the act of exchange itself. These are conceptually separate obligations: one concerns the nature of the coin, the other concerns the mechanics of the transaction.

### Attribution & Tosefta

Rabbi Elazar states: *amar Rabbi Elazar Rabbi Meir hee* — that line of the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Meir's opinion. It might seem surprising that a line appearing in the body of the *stam mishnah* should be attributed to a single named authority rather than representing the consensus position. However, the *Tosefta* explicitly identifies it as *divrei Rabbi Meir*, confirming Rabbi Elazar's attribution. Sometimes what we encounter in the Yerushalmi — as in the Bavli — is a citation of the beginning of a passage where the proof is actually located at the end. So here, the demonstration is not merely inferential — not simply that since Rabbi Meir always requires a fee for a straight half-*shekel*, he would require two in this exchange scenario — but that the *Tosefta* explicitly states, regarding the case of giving a whole and receiving back a half, that two *kolbonot* are required, *divrei Rabbi Meir*, which is precisely what Rabbi Elazar is asserting.

A *baraita* is then cited — *de'tanya* — which essentially quotes our *mishnah* and notes that if one pays on behalf of two people, the standard position is *kolbon echad*, one fee. Rabbi Meir, however, says *sheni kolbonot*, two fees. This confirms that Rabbi Meir's view is that every half-*shekel* payment carries its own standard fee, so that a full *shekel* given for two people incurs two *kolbonot*.

### Three Fees Total

The *gemara* then introduces a further position attributed to Rav, which is really presenting Rabbi Meir's view in a more developed form. Rav states: *al date'ih d'Rav gimel kolbonot inun* — according to Rav, meaning according to Rabbi Meir, the exchange scenario would actually produce not two *kolbonot* but three. This is also transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah and Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak: *echad she'ata noten v'echad she'ata notel v'echad l'dvar Torah* — one for the act of giving the whole coin, one for the act of receiving back the half, and one as the standard biblical-derived fee that always accompanies any half-*shekel* payment.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, would explain the *mishnah*'s ruling of two *kolbonot* as follows: a straight half-*shekel* by itself carries no fee according to the *chachamim*, but when you give a whole and receive change, you pay one *kolbon* for each side of the exchange transaction — one for giving, one for receiving. Why that bilateral transaction generates two fees while a unilateral payment generates none is, frankly, not fully transparent, and the *mefarshim* do not appear to resolve it in a manner that fully satisfies. The candid acknowledgment here is that the logic remains somewhat elusive.

## Chachamim Position

### Chachamim: Two Fees

The *chachamim*'s position, as just outlined, holds that the two *kolbonot* required when giving a whole *shekel* and receiving a half back are both transaction fees — one assessed for giving too much, and one for receiving the change. There is no additional biblically-derived rounding fee in this framework, since the *chachamim* do not accept Rabbi Meir's premise that every half-*shekel* payment independently incurs a fee. The structural tension in the *chachamim*'s position — why a two-sided exchange generates two fees while a straightforward payment generates none — remains genuinely difficult to resolve.

### Bavli Layer Issue

This is an appropriate moment to address a broader textual issue that bears on our learning throughout Shekalim. The doubling of language we have observed in this passage — where formulations appear to be repeated in slightly varied form, and certain transitions feel more abrupt than the *stam* of the Bavli typically produces — is not simply a stylistic feature of the Yerushalmi. There is a more fundamental explanation, rooted in a specific phenomenon affecting the text of Yerushalmi Shekalim in particular.

It has been noted that in an exchange with Professor Seth Schwartz, he mentioned that the Yerushalmi Shekalim has been heavily influenced by the Bavli. This points to a significant matter of *mesorah* and textual transmission.

### Sussman Manuscript

The great Yerushalmi scholar Sussman — who authored the introduction to the new edition transcribing the Ktav Yad Leiden — conducted a study some thirty years ago in which he demonstrated that the Yerushalmi as it appears in our printed text has two distinct layers. There is the original Yerushalmi layer, and a second layer that was heavily influenced by the Bavli and became integrated into the text. The specific circumstances of this integration appear connected to the decision to include Shekalim — the one tractate of *Moed* without its own Bavli — within the printed Bavli editions alongside the Yerushalmi text. In that process, some additional version, gloss, or parallel material became grafted onto the original, producing the composite Bavli-influenced layer visible in our current Yerushalmi Shekalim. The new transcription of the Ktav Yad Leiden makes this two-layer structure clearly visible. When the text appears to duplicate itself, or when transitions seem jarring, readers should be aware that this phenomenon of textual layering is a documented feature of this particular tractate.

## Brothers & Estates

### Partners Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאַחִין וְהַשֻּׁתָּפִין שֶׁחַיָיבִין בַּקּוֹלָּבּוֹן וּפְטוּרִין בְּמַּעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. בְּשֶׁחִלְקוּ וְחָזְרוּ וְנִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ. שֶׁחַיָיבִין בְּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה וּפְטוּרִין מִן הַקּוֹלָּבּוֹן. בְּשֶׁלֹּא חִלְּקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Brothers or partners who are liable for the agio and not liable for animal tithe," if they distributed and then formed a partnership; "if they are liable for animal tithe and not liable for the agio," if they never distributed.

The *mishnah* turns to the case of *ha'achin ve'hashutafin* — the brothers and partners. Although the *Rambam* reads this as two distinct cases, the more natural reading treats it as a compound term, brothers who are also partners, that is, brother-partners. The *mishnah* states that those obligated in the *kolbon* are exempt from *ma'aser behemah*, and those obligated in *ma'aser behemah* are exempt from the *kolbon*.

The underlying logic is as follows. Obligation in the *kolbon* signals that you are treated as individuals rather than as a corporate entity. A standard partnership of multiple individuals is *chayav* in the *kolbon* — each individual who pays on behalf of others must pay a *kolbon* — but is exempt from *ma'aser behemah*, since *shutafut*, partnership, is exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. Conversely, exemption from the *kolbon* and obligation in *ma'aser behemah* reflects treatment as a corporate entity.

### Division & Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי לָעְזָר. וְהֵן שֶׁחִלְקוּ גְדָיִים כְּנֶגֶד תְּיָישִׁים וּתְיָישִׁים כְּנֶגֶד גְּדָיִים. אֲבָל אִם חִלְקוּ גְדָיִים כְּנֶגֶד גְּדָיִים וּתְיָישִׁים כְּנֶגֶד תְּיָישִׁים הוּא חֶלְקוֹ מִשָּׁעָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וַאֲפִילוּ חִלְקוּ גְדָיִים כְּנֶגֶד גְדָיִים וּתְיָישׁים כְּנֶגֶד תְּיישִׁים כִּלְקוּחוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar said, only if they counted lambs against rams and rams against lambs, but if they counted lambs against lambs and rams against rams, it was his part from the first hour. Rebbi Joḥanan said, even if they counted lambs against lambs and rams against rams, they are like buyers.

The operative case is *beshe'hilku ve'chazru ve'nishtattfu* — they divided the estate and then chose to re-enter partnership. Having divided, each person's share is now private property. They then partner as individuals. That is a normal partnership: *chayav* in the *kolbon* as a collection of individuals, and exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. The reverse — *beshe'lo hilku*, they never divided — represents the one case in *halachah* that recognizes something analogous to a corporation rather than a partnership. An undivided estate exists as a unified entity, not merely as a sum of individual partners. In that case, the estate is treated as one body: exempt from the *kolbon* as a single entity, but *chayav* in *ma'aser behemah*.

### Breira Conditions

Rabbi Elazar introduces a significant qualification: the rule that dividing and then reconstituting yields a partnership rather than a reconstituted estate applies only when the division was *not* along symmetric lines — for example, one brother took the kid goats (*gedayim*) and the other took the adult male goats (*tyashim*), or the reverse. But if the division was symmetric — *gedayim k'neged gedayim, tyashim k'neged tyashim*, each party taking half of each category — then *hu chelko mish'at rishonah*, that is the portion that was always coming to that person from the moment of inheritance. This is the principle known in the Bavli as *yesh breira*: does a subsequent division retroactively clarify what was always each party's portion?

The point is subtle. When brothers inherit an estate, we might conceptualize each as holding an undivided fifty percent of every item. When they then divide the estate, if one takes the house and the other takes the car, there has clearly been a transaction — a swap of respective undivided interests — and the division has created something new. But if they split every category exactly in half, so that each takes the half of each type that was always naturally coming to him, then one might argue that no real transaction occurred, and that the division simply made explicit what was already implicit. That retroactive clarification — that this was always my portion — is the core of *breira*.

This generates important practical consequences. First, regarding the reconstitution of the estate: if the initial split was genuinely transactional, then when the brothers subsequently reunite their holdings, they have created a new partnership, not reconstituted the old corporate estate. But if the split was a natural taking of predetermined halves, then when they re-pool the assets, one might argue they have simply reverted to the original entity.

Second, and more concretely, there is the *halachah* of *lakuach*: animals that were purchased, as opposed to raised from birth in one's possession, are exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. If we view the division of the estate as a transaction — essentially a swap of undivided interests — then the animals each brother received are considered *lakuach* and exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. If, however, we view the equal split as merely a realization of each party's pre-existing share with no transaction taking place, then the animals carry no *lakuach* status and remain *chayav* in *ma'aser behemah*.

The term *breira* in this context might seem an imprecise application of the concept as it appears in other discussions, where it means explicitly that a later act reveals what was always the status of the earlier designation. Here the underlying logic is similar: we can only say that no barter occurred and that each party naturally received his inherent portion if we can retroactively say that this portion was always his. That is a form of *breira*, even if less dramatic than its other applications.

### Yochanan Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּהִיא דְתַנִּינָן תַּמָּן. הַלָּקוּחַ וּשֶׁנִּיתַּן לוֹ מַתָּנָה פָּטוּר מִמַּעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** as we have stated there: "The buyer or recipient of a gift is not liable for animal tithe.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. Even in the case of a perfectly symmetric division — *gedayim k'neged gedayim, tyashim k'neged tyashim* — he holds *k'lakuach hen*: the animals are treated as purchased. Once you make any division, you have in effect exchanged your undivided interest in the full estate for a defined parcel of specific assets, and that exchange is a transaction. Therefore the animals you received are *lakuach* and exempt from *ma'aser behemah* regardless of whether the split was symmetric. As the *mishnah* in the relevant context teaches: *lakuach*, that which was purchased, or *she'nitan lo b'matanah*, that which was received as a gift, is exempt from *ma'aser behemah*.

The practical upshot, then, of the dispute between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yochanan is whether a symmetric division of an estate constitutes a transaction that confers *lakuach* status, and correspondingly whether a symmetric re-pooling of assets reconstitutes the original corporate estate or creates a new partnership.

## Estate & Shekel

### Scenarios Depend

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר. רִבִּי יִרְמְיָה בָעֵי. וְלָמָּה לֵי נָן אָמְרִין. פְּעָמִים שֶׁחַיָיבִין בָּזֶה וּבָזֶה וּפְעָמִים שֶׁהֵן פְטוּרִין מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה. הֵיךְ עֲבִידָא. חִלְקוּ אֶת הַנְּכָסִים וְאַחָר כָּךְ חִלְקוּ אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה. חַיָיבִין בָּזֶה וּבָזֶה. חִלְקוּ אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְאַחָר כָּךְ חִלְקוּ אֶת הַנְּכָסִים. פְטוּרִין מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥizkiah said that Rebbi Jeremiah asked: And why are we not saying that sometimes they are liable for both and sometimes they are not liable for either. How is this done? If they distributed the properties and afterwards distributed the animals they are liable for both. If they distributed the animals and afterwards distributed the properties they are not liable for either.

Rabbi Chiya in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah raises a penetrating question: why can we not construct scenarios in which the brothers are *chayav* in both the *kolbon* and *ma'aser behemah* simultaneously, or exempt from both simultaneously? The *mishnah* treats these as always inverse — if you are *chayav* in one, you are exempt from the other — but Rabbi Yirmiyah challenges this by pointing to cases where the asset division is mixed.

If they divided the general property and monetary assets of the estate but did not divide the animals (*chalku hanekhasim velo chalku behemah*), then from the perspective of the *shekel* obligation, where we look at the general assets, each person holds private property and they are individuals — *chayav* in the *kolbon*. But from the perspective of *ma'aser behemah*, the animals remain undivided corporate property — also *chayav*. The reverse case (*chalku behemah velo chalku hanekhasim*) yields the opposite: the animals, now divided or recombined as a partnership, are either *lakuach* or a new *shutfus* — exempt from *ma'aser behemah*. And the general assets, still undivided as estate property, mean the estate pays the *shekel* as a single entity — exempt from the *kolbon*. So both exempt. This produces the counterintuitive result that one can be *chayav* in both simultaneously, or exempt from both simultaneously, depending on which assets have been divided.

### Majority Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי מָנָא. הָדָא דְאַתּ אָמַר. בְּשֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה הַבְּהֵמָה רוֹב. אֲבָל אִם הָיְתָה הַבְּהֵמָה רוֹב הֵן הֵן עִיקַּר נְכָסָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Mana said, this holds only if the animals were not a majority, but if the animals were a majority they form the main property.

Rabbi Mana clarifies: this is only so when the animals do not constitute the majority of the estate's assets. But if the animals are the majority of the assets (*im hayta behemah rov*), then they define the character of the estate overall — *hen hen iqar nechasim* — and the entire estate is assessed by reference to the animals regardless of what happened to the other assets.

### Status Puzzle

This analysis raises a striking and genuinely puzzling observation. The *shekel* is not an asset-based tax. It is a personal obligation — every individual Jew owes a half-*shekel*. Why, then, should one's status as a *shekel*-payer be defined by the estate one happens to have inherited jointly with siblings? Imagine someone who has lived independently for twenty years, has no relationship with his brothers, and then inherits a joint estate. Why should his identity vis-à-vis the *shekel* — a personal religious obligation — now be filtered through the lens of this inherited estate? The *mishnah*'s framework seems to assume that once brothers are co-heirs of an undivided estate, their *shekel* status is jointly determined by that estate. This is far from self-evident, and the strangeness is compounded by the fact that the determination shifts depending on which portion of the estate is divided and which is not.

### Money Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי אָבּוּן אָמַר. רִבִּי שַׁמַּי בָעֵי. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעֲשִׂיתָן [כְּאָדָם אֶחָד] (כְּאֶחָד אָדָם) אֶצֶל מַעֲשֵׂר בְהֵמָה אַתְּ פוֹטְרוֹ מִן הַקּוֹלָּבוֹן. אָמַר לֵיהּ לֹא. שַׁנְיָיה הִיא שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן סֶלַע אַחַת שְׁלֵימָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abun said that Rebbi Shammai asked: Because you made them like one person for animal tithe, you made them not liable for agios? He said to him, no. There is a difference because he is giving a complete tetradrachma.

One proposed resolution is that the relevant question is not the brothers' abstract personal status but rather from which account the *shekel* is actually being paid. If the *shekel* is drawn from the estate's communal funds, then the estate is the payer and the determination follows the estate's corporate status. If each brother pays from his own private funds, he is clearly an individual and pays accordingly. This approach has considerable explanatory power and was helpfully observed in the course of discussion. However, it creates a further difficulty with Rabbi Mana's ruling: why should the fact that the animals are the majority of the estate affect anything, if what matters is simply the account from which the *shekel* payment is drawn? One is presumably not paying the *shekel* in livestock. The persistent awkwardness of this suggests that something more than a simple "source of funds" rule is at work — perhaps that the character of the estate, as reflected in its primary asset class, somehow does define the personal status of its heirs in ways that remain difficult to fully systematize.

The *gemara* then asks a further question in the name of Rabbi Avin citing Rabbi Shimi: just because they are like one person for purposes of *ma'aser behemah*, how does that exempt them from the *kolbon*? The *kolbon* relates to the personal *shekel* obligation, not to animal ownership. The answer offered is that the exemption from the *kolbon* is not really about being conceptually one person at all. Rather, it follows from a separate *halachah*: when one person pays a full *shekel* covering multiple individuals' obligations simultaneously, even if as a gift or on their behalf, they pay only one *kolbon*, not one per person. The estate, drawing from its communal funds, is doing exactly that — paying one full *shekel* for multiple members — and therefore is exempt from multiple *kolbonot*. This seems to answer the question but immediately generates a new one: if that is the reason, it has nothing to do with the estate structure at all. Anyone who pays a full *shekel* on behalf of multiple people would be exempt. Why, then, does the *mishnah* differentiate between an undivided estate and one that has been divided and reconstituted? If the brothers divide and then reform as a partnership and still pay as a group, they would likewise be giving one coin for multiple people. The *gemara* points this out, noting the apparent contradiction with the *mishnah*'s ruling that divided-and-reconstituted brothers are indeed *chayav* in the *kolbon*. The *gemara*'s attempt at resolution — citing a tradition about two brothers-in-law who inherited from their respective fathers-in-law — is itself difficult to parse and, by the admission of the analysis here, does not fully resolve the underlying tension. The *mishnah*'s framework connecting estate status to *shekel* identity remains genuinely challenging.

## Kolbon Destination

### Meir: To Shekalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאֵיכָן הָיוּ הַקּוֹלָּבּוֹנוֹת נוֹפְלִין. רִבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר. לַשְּׁקָלִים. רִבִּי לָעְזָר אוֹמֵר. לִנְדָבָה. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַשְּׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר. רִיקּוּעֵי זָהָב וְצִיפּוּי לְבֵית קוֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר. שׁולְחָנִין הָיוּ נוֹטְלִין אוֹתָן בִּשְׂכָרָן. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים. לְהוֹצָאַת דְּרָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** "Where were the agios credited to? Rebbi Meïr says, to the *sheqalim*. Rebbi Eleazar says, for voluntary offerings. Rebbi Simeon from Shezur says, gold sheeting, cover for the Holiest of Holies. Ben Azzai says, the money changers were taking them as their fees; but some say, for travel expenses."

The *mishnah* concludes this section by asking: *le'eichan hayu hakalbonot noplim* — to what use were the *kolbonot* put? The answer reveals much about how each authority understood the nature of the fee in the first place. Rabbi Meir says *l'shekalim* — the *kolbonot* were added directly to the *shekalim* funds. This is entirely consistent with Rabbi Meir's rationale that the *kolbon* serves to round out the *shekel* to its ideal measure. If its purpose is to complete the *shekel*, the natural destination is the general *shekalim* fund.

### Eliezer: To Nedavah

Rabbi Eliezer says *l'nedavah* — the funds go for *nedavah*, the communal free-will burnt offerings. These are the *olot* brought on the *mizbe'ach* during periods of inactivity to keep the altar continuously occupied. They are a type of communal *korban* but not among the standard obligatory communal sacrifices. The *kolbonot* thus support Temple activity in a related but distinct category.

### Shezuri & Azzai

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's opinion — *riku'ei zahav tzipui l'beit kodshei hakodashim*, that the funds were used for gold plating in the Holy of Holies — is striking and its rationale is not entirely transparent. It calls to mind the fate of Korach's fire pans, which were beaten into coverings for the *mizbe'ach*, but why the *kolbonot* specifically should go to gold plating in the *kodesh hakodashim* is not immediately obvious.

Ben Azzai, by contrast, offers the most intuitive answer given the transactional understanding of the *kolbon*: *shulchanim hayu notlim otan bischaran* — the money changers took the *kolbonot* as their fee. If the *kolbon* is fundamentally a money-changing service charge, paying the changers with it is perfectly fitting. And some authorities say the funds went *l'hotza'at drachim* — to cover the travel and operational expenses of the entire collection process, sending agents out to collect the half-*shekels* and transport them.

The underlying conceptual divide is thus made explicit by the destinations: Rabbi Meir, who understands the *kolbon* as a rounding fee to perfect the *shekel*, sends it back into the *shekalim* fund. The others, who understand it as a transaction or operating fee, direct it toward the costs of conducting the collection — either to the money changers directly, or to the broader logistical expenses of the system.

## Collection & Transport

### Currency Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: מְצָרְפִין שְׁקָלִים דַּרְכּוֹנוֹת מִפְּנֵי מַשּׂוֹי הַדֶּרֶךְ. כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהָיוּ שׁוֹפָרוֹת בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ כָּךּ הָיוּ בַּמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: One consolidates *sheqalim* to *darics* because of the difficulty of transport. Just as there were horns in the Temple, so were in the countryside.

The next *mishnah* opens with a practical *halachah* regarding the logistics of collection: *metzarfin shekalim ledarkonot* — one may exchange collected *shekels* into *darkonot*, which are gold coins (a term connected to the Greek or Persian monetary system). The rationale is *mipnei masa haderech* — to reduce the physical weight and burden of transporting the collected silver to the *Beit HaMikdash*. This is purely a logistical accommodation.

### Collection Horns

A further detail of the collection infrastructure is noted. Just as there were *shofarot* in the *Mikdash* itself — shaped like horns with a narrow slit at the top and a wide base, making it possible to deposit funds but impossible to extract them by hand — so too were such *shofarot* maintained in local communities throughout the country, *kach hayu shofarot b'medinah*. Whether *medinah* here refers specifically to Jerusalem or to the country at large is a matter of dispute among the commentators.

### Coin-Only Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיַעֲשׂוּ אוֹתָן מַרְגָּלִית. שֶׁמְּא תָזִיל הַמַּרְגָּלִית וְנִמְצָא הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ מַפְסִיד. כַּהִיא דְתַנִּינָן תַּמָּן. וְכוּלָּן נִפְדִּין בְּכֶסֶף וּבְשָׁוֶה כֶסֶף חוּץ מִן הַשְּׁקָלִים. [וְאֵין פּוֹדִים בְּכֵלִים]. אָמַר רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק. שֶׁמָּא יָזִילוּ הַכֵּלִים וְנִמְצָא הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ מַפְסִיד. וְהָכָא שֶׁמָּא תָזִיל הַמַּרְגָּלִית וְנִמְצָא הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ מַפְסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Should one exchange them for a pearl? Perhaps the pearl would lose in value and the Temple treasury would suffer damage. As we have stated there, "All can be redeemed by money or monies-worth except for *sheqalim*" [and one does not redeem by implements;] and Rebbi Samuel bar Rav Isaac said, perhaps the implements would lose in value and the Temple treasury would suffer damage. Also here, perhaps the pearl would lose in value and the Temple treasury would suffer damage.

The *gemara* raises a question: why exchange collected *shekels* specifically into gold coins? Why not convert them into diamonds or pearls for ease of transport? The answer is *shema tazil hamargaliyot venimtza hekdesh mafsid* — lest the value of precious stones decline, causing *hekdesh* to suffer a loss. Currency, backed by governmental fixed ratios, is relatively stable in value; commodities such as gems fluctuate.

This principle is confirmed by a cross-reference to *Mishna Bechoros*: *vechulan nifdin bechesef uveshaveh chesef, chutz mishkalim* — a range of obligations may be redeemed either with money or with money's equivalent in value, except for *shekalim*, which may only be redeemed with actual coins. The Yerushalmi cites Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak's explanation: *shema yazilu hakeilim venimtza hekdesh mafsid* — lest vessels depreciate and *hekdesh* lose out. The same concern about value instability that prohibits redeeming *shekalim* with vessels or gemstones explains why, when transporting collected funds, only the conversion to gold coins — with their governmentally fixed exchange ratio to silver — is permitted.

## Theft Liability

### Truma Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: בְּנֵי הָעִיר שֶׁשִּׁילְחוּ אֶת שִׁקְלֵיהֶן וְנִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ אִם נִתְרְמָה הַתְּרוּמָה נִשְׁבָּעִין לַגִּזְבָּרִים וְאִם לָאו נִשְׁבָּעִין לִבְנֵי הָעִיר וּבְנֵי הָעִיר שׁוֹקְלִין תַּחְתֵּיהֶן. נִמְצָאוּ אוֹ שֶׁהֶחֱזִירוּם הַגַּנָּבִים אֵילּוּ וָאֵילּוּ שְׁקָלִים וְאֵין עוֹלִין לָהֶן לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: If the people of a city sent their *sheqalim* and these were stolen or lost, if the sums were disbursed they will have to swear to the Temple custodians, otherwise they will have to swear to the city dwellers who have to replace them by new *sheqalim*. If they were found or the thieves returned them, both are *sheqalim* and cannot be credited for the coming year.

The *mishnah* then addresses the case of *bnei ha'ir sheshalchu et shikleihem* — a village that collected its *shekalim* and dispatched them to the *Beit HaMikdash* — and the funds were stolen or lost in transit. The question is: who bears the loss?

The answer turns on whether *nitrema hatruma* — whether the *trumah* has already been taken. Three times a year, the *Beit HaMikdash* would draw from the accumulated collected funds, removing the actual coins from the collection vessels and beginning to deploy them for the purchase of communal *korbanos*. This act of removal is called *terumah*. Crucially, when performing this *terumah*, the *Beit HaMikdash* drew not only on the funds physically present but on all funds yet to arrive — *min hagavuy umin ha'asid ligvos*, from what had been collected and from what would yet be collected. By drawing on anticipated funds, the *Beit HaMikdash* effectively treated the funds still in transit as already received. A village whose funds were on the road at the moment of *terumah* had, in a sense, already fulfilled its obligation, since the *Beit HaMikdash* had already counted on those funds.

### Before Truma

If the *terumah* has not yet been taken, the funds in transit remain the property of the village. They have not yet been received by the *Beit HaMikdash*, and the loss falls on the village. The carriers must therefore swear to the *bnei ha'ir* — *nishba'im livnei ha'ir* — that they were not negligent. And the *bnei ha'ir* themselves must contribute a second set of *shekalim* — *shoklim tachteichem* — to replace what was lost.

### After Truma

If the *terumah* has already been taken, the funds are considered constructively received by the *Beit HaMikdash*. The carriers are now effectively custodians of *hekdesh* property. They must swear to the *gizbarim*, the Temple treasurers — *nishba'im lagizbarim* — that they were not negligent, and the village is thereafter exempt from replacing the funds.

The *gemara* notes that this entire framework accords with the position that the *terumah* was indeed taken drawing on both collected and anticipated funds. According to an alternative view that the *terumah* was drawn only from funds physically present, even after the *terumah* act, funds still in transit would not yet be considered received, and the liability structure would differ accordingly.

### Old vs. New

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַתְנִיתָא בִתְקֵלִין חַדְּתִין. אֲבָל בִּתְקֵלִין עַתִּיקִין לָא בְדָא. וְתַנֵּי כֵן. עַתִּיקִין בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. אֵין עַתִּיקִין בַּמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Mishnah is about new *sheqalim*, but not about old *sheqalim*. And it was stated so: Old ones in the Temple, no old ones in the countryside.

A further point is established: *matnisa b'taklin chadashin, aval b'taklin atikin lo bada* — the *mishnah*'s collection system applies only to the current year's *shekalim*. Back-payments — funds owed from a prior year — could not be paid through the village collection and local *shofarot*. *Atikin bamikdash ve'ein atikin b'medinah*: old *shekalim* were accepted at the *Beit HaMikdash* directly but not through the local collection apparatus. The village infrastructure served only the current year's obligation.

## Oath Requirement

### Elazar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי הָעִיר שֶׁשִּׁילְחוּ אֶת שִׁקְלֵיהֶן. נִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ. אָמַר רִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא. דְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמַר. קֳדָשִׁים שֶׁהוּא חַיָיב בַּאֲחֵרִיּוּתָן כִּנְכָסָיו הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar said, this is Rebbi Simeon's, since Rebbi Simeon said, *sancta* for whose alienation he is responsible are like his own property.

Rabbi Elazar states that this entire mechanism — taking an oath when the village funds are stolen or lost — follows exclusively the position of Rabbi Shimon. The problem is that one does not ordinarily take oaths regarding *hekdesh* property. If the money is *hekdesh*, what basis is there for a *shevuah*? Rabbi Elazar resolves this by anchoring the ruling in Rabbi Shimon's principle: *kodshim she'hu chayav be'achrayutan ke'nechasav hen* — sacred objects for which a person bears financial liability are treated as though they were ordinary personal property. Since the village bears liability for the lost funds before the *terumah*, those funds are treated as ordinary village property from a monetary perspective, enabling a standard oath to be taken.

## Oath Requirement (II)

### Yochanan Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא. מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעַת תַּקָּנָה. עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן נִיחָא. נִשְׁבָּעִין לִבְנֵי הָעִיר וּבְנֵי הָעִיר שׁוֹקְלִין תַּחְתֵּיהֶן. הָדָא הִיא דְרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. מִשֵּׁם שְׁבוּעַת תַּקָּנָה. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ בְנֵי הָעִיר לְשַׁלֵּם אֵין הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ יוֹצֵא בְלֹא שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, it is everybody's opinion, because of an instituted oath. Rebbi Joḥanan's opinion is understandable; "they have to swear to the city dwellers who have to replace them by new *sheqalim.*" Still this is because of a regulation oath. Even though the city dwellers agreed to pay, Temple property cannot be released without an oath.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with Rabbi Elazar's limitation and holds that the oath applies universally — *divrei hakol hi* — not only according to Rabbi Shimon. The basis, however, is different: *mishum shevuat takana* — the oath is a Rabbinic enactment. Even though biblically there is no oath on *hekdesh*, the Rabbis instituted one for reasons of policy: allowing the custodians of communal *shekalim* to simply say *oops* without consequence would reflect poorly on *hekdesh*, and people would not be sufficiently careful. The *takana* thus serves both to ensure diligence and to maintain respect for the sanctity of *hekdesh* funds.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, the full structure of the *mishnah* is explained as *takana*: swearing to the *gizbarim* when funds are considered received, and swearing to the *bnei ha'ir* when they are not, are both Rabbinic enactments for these policy reasons.

### Possible Bavli Import

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִפְרִישׁ שִׁקְלוֹ וְאָבַד. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. חַיּיָּב בָּאַחֵרָיוּתוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּמְסוֹר לַגִּיזְבָּר. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. הֶקְדֵּשׁ בִּרְשׁוּת הַגָּבוֹהַּ בְּכָל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא. מַתְנִיתָא פְלִיגָא עַל רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. נִשְׁבָּעִין לִבְנֵי הָעִיר וּבְנֵי הָעִיר שׁוֹקְלִין תַּחְתֵּיהֶן. [עוֹד] הִיא מִשֵּׁם שְׁבוּעַת תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Somebody put aside his *sheqel* and it was lost. Rebbi Joḥanan said, he is responsible for its alienation until he delivers to the Temple trustee. Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish said, Temple property is Heaven's at any place where it may be. A Mishnah disagrees with Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish: "they have to swear to the city dwellers who have to replace them by new *sheqalim.*" Still this is because of a regulation oath.

This yields the *gemara*'s difficult question about Rabbi Elazar's position. In the scenario where the funds are considered already received — where *hekdesh* bears the loss and the carriers swear to the *gizbarim* — Rabbi Elazar faces a problem: the money is *hekdesh*, the owners are *hekdesh*, and there is no organic basis for a standard *shevuah*. Rabbi Elazar's framework, which relied on the villagers' liability to justify the oath, cannot easily account for this scenario.

The *gemara* offers an answer: *nishba'im livnei ha'ir bema'amad gizbarim* — they swear to the villagers in the presence of the *gizbarim*, not to the *gizbarim* themselves. The purpose is to prevent the carriers from being suspected of negligence or embezzlement by the villagers — not because the village has suffered a financial loss, but to protect the reputation of the carriers. The *gizbarim* are present but are not the recipients of the oath.

However, this answer is textually suspicious. The formulation *nishba'im livnei ha'ir bema'amad gizbarim* is a phrase that appears in the Bavli in a different discussion, addressing a different question. Several *mefarshim* point out that this line has been imported from the Bavli into this *sugya* of the Yerushalmi, where it does not naturally belong and answers a question the *sugya* is not actually asking. Tellingly, this line does not appear in the Ktav Yad Leiden. This is a concrete example of the Bavli layer that Sussman identified — a phrase grafted in from the Bavli that can be made to work, but only awkwardly, and which is absent from the manuscript tradition.

### Oath Binding

Even if the *bnei ha'ir* volunteer to cover the lost funds themselves — thereby relieving *hekdesh* of any financial loss — the oath requirement is not waived. *Ein hekdesh yotzei belo shevuah* — *hekdesh* does not release a custodian without an oath. The *takana* is categorical: regardless of reimbursement, the carriers must swear. The purpose of the oath is not solely to ensure financial recovery but to maintain accountability and respect for *hekdesh* funds. Voluntary restitution by the village does not substitute for that.

## Individual Shekel

### Delivery Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִפְרִישׁ שִׁקְלוֹ וְאָבַד. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. חַיּיָּב בָּאַחֵרָיוּתוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּמְסוֹר לַגִּיזְבָּר. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. הֶקְדֵּשׁ בִּרְשׁוּת הַגָּבוֹהַּ בְּכָל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Somebody put aside his *sheqel* and it was lost. Rebbi Joḥanan said, he is responsible for its alienation until he delivers to the Temple trustee. Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish said, Temple property is Heaven's at any place where it may be.

The *mishnah* moves to the case of an individual who has designated his *shekel* — *hifrish shkalo* — and then lost it. Rabbi Yochanan states that he is *chayav achrayuso*, he remains liable for the *shekel*, until *sheyimserena lagizbar* — until he actually hands it over to the *gizbar*. This is the intuitive position: personal liability attaches until physical delivery.

### Divine Ownership

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish offers a striking counterposition: *hekdesh birshut gavoha bechol makom shehi* — *hekdesh* is under the authority of the Most High wherever it may be. Once a person designates his *shekel* as *hekdesh*, the money belongs to God. God knows where it is. The bank of the *Beit HaMikdash* may not know its location, but the divine owner does. Accordingly, Reish Lakish holds that the individual is not liable once the *shekel* has been designated, since it is already, in an ultimate sense, in divine possession.

### No Replevin Needed

This position has significant implications for the broader *sugya*. If *hekdesh* belongs to God wherever it is, then the carriers who lost the village funds could theoretically argue: wherever it is, it belongs to *hekdesh*. Go retrieve it from the thieves. The *gemara* addresses this: *lo hi mishum takana* — the requirement for the village to pay a second time is itself a *takana*, not the strict letter of the law. Under Reish Lakish's view, *me'ikar hadin*, the *bnei ha'ir* would never owe a second payment. Once they designated the money as *hekdesh* — and once that money is wherever it is — *hekdesh*'s claim follows the money, and the village has fulfilled its obligation. The sole reason the village pays again, and the sole reason the oath is required, is the Rabbinic *takana*. It is a fascinating and somewhat striking conclusion: technically, no replevin would ever be required of the village at all.

## Fund Allocation

### Baraita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַנֵּי הָרִאשׁוֹנִים נוֹפְלִין לְתְקְלִין חַדְּתִין וְהַשְּׁנִיִים נוֹפְלִים לְתִקְלִין עֲתִיקִין. אֵילּוּ הֵן הָרִאשׁוֹנִים וְאֵילּוּ הֵן הַשְּׁנִיִים. רִבִּי פִּינְחָס בֵּירִבִּי חֲנַנְיָה וְרִבִּי אַבָּא מָרִי. חַד אָמַר. אֵילּוּ שֶׁשִּׁילְחוּ בְנֵי הָעִיר תְּחִילָּה. וְחוֹרָנָה אָמַר. אֵילּוּ שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לִידֵי גִיזְבָּרִין תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: The first ones fall to the new *sheqalim*, the second ones to the old *sheqalim*. Which ones are first and which ones are second? Rebbi Phineas ben Rebbi Ḥananiah and Rebbi Abba Mari. One said, those that the city dwellers sent first, but the other one said, those which came in to the hands of the Temple trustees first.

The *mishnah* addresses a practical scenario: the village paid its *shekalim*, the funds were stolen or lost, the village levied a second collection and paid again, and then the original funds were found or returned by the thieves. Now there are two sets of funds in hand. What becomes of them?

The *mishnah* rules: *elu va'elu shekalim ve'ein olin lahem leshana haba'ah* — both sets of funds are *shekalim* for the current year, and neither can be credited toward the following year's obligation. One might think that since the second set was given under duress — the village had no choice but to pay again — it could be applied to next year's account and the village would receive credit. The answer is no: both sets have been designated as *hekdesh* and both are consecrated for the current year's *korbanos*.

A *baraita* then provides further detail: *harishonim noflim letaklin chadashin, hashniyim noflim letaklin atikin* — the first set of funds goes toward the new *shekalim* account for the current year's communal offerings, while the second set goes toward *taklin atikin*, the account for old *shekalim*, covering prior obligations and related expenses.

### Pinchas & Abba

The natural question is: in a case where two sets of funds exist, which is "first" and which is "second"? Rabbi Pinchas and Rabbi Abba debate this. One holds that *elu sheshalchu bnei ha'ir techila* — those that the village originally sent are "first," and those that the village was compelled to pay a second time are "second." This is the straightforward chronological reading: what was sent first is first, what was sent second is second.

The other holds a more counterintuitive position: *elu shehigi'u liydei hagizbarim techila* — whichever set actually arrived in the hands of the *gizbarim* first is considered "first." Since the original funds were stolen or delayed, it is quite possible that the replacement funds arrived at the *Beit HaMikdash* before the originals were recovered and returned. On this view, receipt rather than dispatch determines priority. The funds that reach the *gizbarim* first go toward the new account; those that arrive later go toward the old account.
