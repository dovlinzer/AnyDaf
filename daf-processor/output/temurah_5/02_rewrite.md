# Temurah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye-Rava Debate Intro

### Fundamental Question

One of the most famous debates in all of Talmudic literature — known even to those who have never studied *Masechet Temurah* — opens at the bottom of daf 5b. It is the *machlokes* of Abaye and Rava concerning a deceptively simple question: if the Torah says "do not do" something, and a person goes ahead and does it anyway, does that act have a halachic effect?

It is important to clarify what kind of effect is at issue. No one disputes the physical, this-worldly consequences of a prohibited act — if a person steals, the money is in his pocket; if someone is murdered, the victim is dead. Nor is the question about punitive consequences, such as whether food cooked on Shabbos becomes forbidden in *hana'ah* (a position some *amoraim* hold but which we do not rule). The question concerns purely halachic or metaphysical change — a change in status — that results from an act the Torah has forbidden.

A useful illustration: a *kohen* is forbidden to marry a *gerushah*. If he does so, should the *kiddushin* take effect? On one hand, he violated the Torah's prohibition; on the other, a *lav* (negative prohibition) is less severe than an *issur kares* — everyone agrees that a marriage to a man's sister or to a married woman produces no *kiddushin*. The question is whether violating a *lav* is sufficient to prevent the halachic effect from taking hold.

### Abaye Position

Abaye holds: *im avid, mahani* — if you did it, it works. Even in violation, the act takes halachic effect. His proof is logical: *amai loki?* — why would the offender receive lashes? If a *kohen* tried to marry a *gerushah* but the *kiddushin* never took effect, then in what sense did he commit a transgression? You cannot receive lashes for a mere attempt. The fact that the Torah prescribes lashes implies that the prohibited act was realized — meaning it was effective.

### Rava Position

Rava holds: *im avid lo mahani* — if you did it, it does not take effect. His reasoning flows from an internal logic: if the entire act was in violation of the Torah's will, why should the Torah allow it to produce a halachic result? And as for lashes — *mishum de'avar ameimra deRachmana*, because he transgressed the word of God. According to Rava, lashes are imposed not because the act succeeded halachically, but simply because the person defied the Divine command. The transgression is the attempt itself, independent of whether the forbidden effect materialized.

With these two positions in place, the *Gemara* proceeds through a series of cases. In each, either Abaye or Rava will appear to be contradicted, and each will explain why the case before him is an exception to his general rule. One might wonder at the end whether the debate matters at all if every case can be explained away — and the *Gemara* will eventually identify one or two cases where the disagreement is genuinely operative. But this pattern, where a sweeping principled debate is followed by mutual concessions to the facts, is itself a classic Talmudic phenomenon.

## Rapist/Divorce Case

### Ones Shegeirash

The *Gemara* opens its first challenge with the case of *ones shegeirash* — a rapist who divorces the woman he was compelled to marry. The Torah requires that a man who rapes an unmarried woman marry her, and that once married, he may not divorce her (*lo yuchal leshalchah*). It must be acknowledged that these laws are deeply foreign to contemporary sensibilities; they can only be understood against the ancient social reality in which a woman who had been violated was frequently abandoned by her family and society, and the Torah sought at minimum to ensure she would have the security of marriage. The father or the woman may refuse, but assuming they do not, the marriage stands — and the man is thereafter forbidden to send her away.

If he nonetheless divorces her, the *braisa* states: *im Yisrael hu, machazir ve'eino lokeh* — if he is a non-*kohen*, he must take her back and does not receive lashes.

### First Version

The *Gemara*'s first framing of the challenge focuses on the lashes question. According to Rava — who says that transgressing a Divine command brings lashes even without the act being effective — why does this man not receive lashes? If we frame the matter as: he divorced her, and *kol yamav* teaches that the divorce cannot be allowed to stand permanently, then the reason he avoids lashes is that his divorce was not fully effective. But that reasoning works only for Abaye, who requires the act to be effective before lashes apply. According to Rava, the lack of a full effect should be irrelevant — he should still be lashed for transgressing.

### Rava's Mitzvah Answer

Rava responds: *shani hatam de'amar kra kol yamav* — there it is different, because the verse says *kol yamav*, "all his days." Tosafot explains that the correct framing is not about the incompleteness of the divorce but about a *mitzvas asei*: the Torah's statement of *kol yamav* creates an affirmative obligation to remarry, and that positive commandment counteracts the negative prohibition, thereby exempting him from lashes. This is the principle of *lav ha'nitak la'asei* — a negative prohibition that is linked to a corrective positive commandment does not result in lashes, because the positive act of rectification takes precedence.

The *Gemara*'s initial framing — which treated the avoidance of lashes as flowing from the divorce not being fully effective — was a somewhat confusing way to arrive at what is ultimately a straightforward answer: the positive *mitzvah* to remarry overrides the lashes. The *Gemara* does arrive there, but not before exploring the tension in how to read the interaction between the prohibition, the effect of the *gerushin*, and the obligation to restore the marriage.

### Lishna Achrina

A second version of the challenge (*lishna achrina*) reframes the issue. Here the *braisa* reads: *im Yisrael hu, machazir ve'eino lokeh; ve'im kohen, lokeh ve'eino machazir* — if a non-*kohen*, he remarries and is exempt from lashes; if a *kohen*, since he cannot remarry a *gerushah*, he receives lashes. This version focuses not on the lashes question but on the very fact that he remarries — which presupposes that the *gerushin* took effect. The Torah said do not divorce, he divorced, and it worked. This seems to support Abaye directly.

Yet the *Gemara* frames the challenge as *tiyuvta de'Abaye* — a difficulty for Abaye, not for Rava. Why? Tosafot addresses the apparent inversion: because the *Gemara* takes for granted that *gerushin* by its very nature takes effect — analogous to death, which severs the marriage without any halachic act of the wife. Since it is assumed from the outset that the divorce works (even according to Rava), the *Gemara* shifts focus to the *mitzvah* to reverse it. The obligation to remarry suggests the Torah refuses to allow the effect to be permanent — and this appears more aligned with Rava's view that violations are not meant to produce lasting halachic change. So the challenge to Abaye is: does not this case show that the Torah resists the permanence of a forbidden act's effect?

### Abaye on Kol Yamav

Abaye's response is: *shani hatam d'rachmana amar kol yamav* — there it is different, the Torah explicitly said *kol yamav*, and that creates an obligatory exception. As a general rule, Abaye maintains that forbidden acts do produce full and lasting effect. Here the Torah specifically intervenes to say otherwise. But that raises a question: if the Torah simply says *lo yuchal leshalchah*, that already means he may not divorce her. Why add *kol yamav*?

Abaye explains: *i lav de'amar Rachmana kol yamav, hava amina issura hu de'avid lei, i ba'ei lehader ve'i ba'ei lo lehader* — without the phrase *kol yamav*, one might have thought that reversing the divorce was his option, not his obligation. If he chose to remarry, his original act would lack a lasting effect and he would escape lashes; if he chose not to, he would be lashed. The Torah therefore said *kol yamav* to establish not an option but a binding obligation to remarry — regardless of his preference.

### Rava on Kol Yamav

Rava explains *kol yamav* differently. Without that phrase, he would have known from his own general principle that the divorce cannot be fully effective, and that the man would be obligated to reverse it. But he would also have concluded that lashes still apply, since the prohibition on divorce stands independently. The phrase *kol yamav* therefore comes to do more than merely obligate the remarriage — it transforms the case into a *lav she'nitak la'asei*, a negative prohibition attached to a positive corrective commandment: *l'shavyei l'ones lo ta'aseh she'nitak la'asei, d'ein lokin alav* — to establish that in the case of the rapist, the prohibition is of the type linked to a *mitzvas asei* and therefore carries no lashes.

## Terumah Bad-Good

### Terumah Mishna

The *Gemara* moves on to a cleaner set of examples drawn from the laws of *terumah* and *ma'aser*. The Torah states *mikol chelbo* — from all of its fat you shall take *terumah*, meaning from the best of the grain. The implication is that *terumah* must be taken from the superior portion — *chelbo in, geru'in lo* — not from inferior grain. It is worth noting that the violation here is not a classic *lav* in the form of an explicit negative prohibition; it is rather a departure from the prescribed manner of performing the *mitzvah*. Nonetheless, as Rashi and Tosafot note, Abaye and Rava debate not only explicit prohibitions but any act performed contrary to the Torah's direction — including *mitzvas asei* violations.

The *Mishnah* teaches: *ein tormin min hara al hayafa* — one may not take *terumah* from inferior grain upon superior grain, *ve'im taram, trumato trumah* — but if one did, it takes effect.

### Challenge to Rava

This is presented as *tiyuvta deRava*: the act was done contrary to the Torah's directive, yet it takes halachic effect. According to Rava's principle that violations do not produce halachic effects, this *terumah* should be null.

### Rava's Drasha

Rava responds: *shani hatam keRabbi Ila'i* — there it is different, based on a *drasha* of Rabbi Ila'i. The verse states, *velo tise'u alav chet beharimchem et chelbo mimenu* — "you shall not bear sin upon it when you take its fat from it." Rabbi Ila'i infers: if even when done improperly the *terumah* were not effective, why would there be a *chet*, a sin? If the act produced no halachic result, there would have been nothing to sin about. Therefore, *mikan* — from here we learn — that even when taken from the inferior upon the superior, it does take effect. That is why the Torah can say you will have sinned.

There is an important internal tension here, which Tosafot raises: Rabbi Ila'i's *drasha* appears to simply restate Abaye's logic — that an act only constitutes a *chet* if it was effective. How can Rava, who rejects that logic, rely on this *drasha*? Tosafot resolves this by reading the *drasha* differently: it is not the mere fact that the Torah calls it a *chet*, but the redundancy and emphasis of the verse that signals a special case. The Torah went out of its way to underscore that this particular act constitutes a sin precisely because — unlike the general rule — it does have an effect. The emphasis marks it as an exception, not as a universal principle.

### Abaye's Reading

According to Abaye, who already knows that violations produce effects, why does this verse need to say *velo tise'u alav chet*? *I lav de'amar Rachmana velo tise'u alav chet, hava amina avid mitzvah min hamuvchar* — without it, one might have thought that taking from the best on the best is simply the ideal (*l'chatchila*), but that taking from the inferior on the superior, while not ideal, does not rise to the level of *chet*. The verse therefore teaches us that it is not merely a failure to fulfill the *mitzvah* in the best manner — it is an actual sin.

## Species-to-Species Terum…

### Species Rule

The next case involves taking *terumah* from one species of produce on a different species. The Torah states: *kol cheilev yitzhar v'kol cheilev dagan v'tirosh reishitam* — all the fat of the oil, and all the fat of the grain and wine, their *reishit* you shall give. By separating the species into distinct phrases — oil on one side, grain and wine on the other — the Torah implies that each species must give *terumah* from within itself: grain on grain, wine on wine, oil on oil. The principle: *leitein cheilev lazeh v'cheilev lazeh* — the "fat" designation applies to each species separately.

The *Mishnah* teaches: *ein tormin mimin al she'eino mino* — one may not take *terumah* from one species upon a different species, *ve'im taram, ein tromaso trumah* — and if one did, it does not take effect. Here the violation renders the act null. This seems to validate Rava's position: *alma lo mahani*, and therefore *tiyuvta de'Abaye*.

### Challenge to Abaye

According to Abaye's principle that violations still produce halachic effects, this case is a direct counterexample — a case where acting improperly yields no *terumah* at all.

### Abaye's Reishitam

Abaye responds: *shani hatam, de'amar kra reishitam* — there it is different, because the verse says *reishitam*, "their first," using the possessive plural. This extra word teaches that the *reishit* must be appropriate to each species individually — *reishit lazeh v'reishit lazeh*. Each species must have its own distinct *terumah*, and if taken from the wrong species it is entirely invalid. This is an explicit Scriptural ruling that overrides his general principle, making this an exception.

Rabbi Ila'i is also cited here: *reishit o reishitam* — the word *reishitam* (rather than simply *reishit*) indicates that each species must have its own separate *terumah* and nothing else will suffice.

### Rava's Reading

Rava has a different account of why *reishitam* is necessary. According to Rava, the general principle already tells him that a violation does not produce effect — he did not need *reishitam* to know that cross-species *terumah* fails. So what does *reishitam* add?

The initial verses group *tirosh* (wine) and *dagan* (grain) under a single instance of the word *cheilev* — *v'kol cheilev dagan v'tirosh*. Without *reishitam*, one might have thought that since wine and grain share a single *cheilev* designation, one could take *terumah* from wine upon grain or vice versa. Similarly, within the category of grain — wheat and barley — since they are subsumed under one word, one might have thought you could take wheat *terumah* on barley. But the *halacha* is that only wheat on wheat and barley on barley is valid.

The word *reishitam* comes to clarify: even within the grouped phrase, every species retains a separate identity, and *terumah* may only be taken from a species upon itself. This is not because violations fail — that Rava already knew — but because the initial phrasing might have suggested these species were interchangeable for purposes of *terumah*, and *reishitam* corrects that impression.

A second formulation makes the same point more succinctly: *tirosh vedagan dechada chelev kesiv behu* — since wine and grain share one *cheilev*, one might have thought *sarim mehai ahai*, you could take from one to the other. *Kesav Rachmana reishitam* — the Torah therefore added *reishitam* to establish that each species is entirely separate.

## Consecrated Animals

### Charamim Case

The *Gemara* now turns to the laws of *charamim* and *bechor*, cases involving consecrated animals that cannot be redeemed. The Torah states regarding *cherem* property belonging to *kohanim*: *lo yimacher velo yiga'el* — it shall not be sold and shall not be redeemed. The item retains its *kedushah* until it is physically transferred to the *kohen*. The question is: if one attempts to redeem the *cherem* — transferring the *kedushah* onto money — does it take effect?

The *Mishnah* rules: *charmei kohanim ein lahem pidyon ella notnim l'kohein* — there is no redemption; it must be physically given to the *kohen*. Attempting to redeem it is ineffective: *alma lo mahani*, and therefore *tiyuvta de'Abaye*.

### Hu Emphasis

Abaye responds: *shani hatam de'amar Rachmana kodesh kodashim hi* — there it is different, because the verse says *kodesh kodashim* with the word *hu* (or *hi*) — "it is holy of holies." This word, *b'havayaso yehei*, teaches that the item must remain exactly as it is. Its status cannot be altered. The reason the redemption fails is not Rava's general principle that violations lack effect — it is the specific Scriptural word that anchors the *kedushah* in place. Therefore it is an exception.

### Rava on Hu

Since Rava already knows from his general principle that violations fail, he does not need *hu* for that purpose. What then does *hu* teach? *L'ma'utei bechor* — to exclude the case of a *bechor*.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraisa*: by *bechor* the Torah says *lo tifdeh* — it shall not be redeemed. The *bechor* has intrinsic sanctity by virtue of its being the firstborn, so the *kohen* cannot transfer that *kedushah* elsewhere. But unlike *cherem*, no verse forbids selling a *bechor*. The *halacha* is therefore that a *kohen* may sell his *bechor* if it has developed a *mum* (blemish). *Hu* thus draws the line: the prohibition on selling applies to *cherem* and *ma'aser*, but not to *bechor*. The word *hu* limits the scope of *lo yimacher velo yiga'el* — it cannot be extended to *bechor* by analogy.

Additionally, the *Gemara* digresses briefly to note how *ma'aser* relates to *bechor* on this point. The Torah says by *ma'aser*: *lo yiga'el*. Since the same phrase appears by *cherem*, and *ma'aser* and *bechor* are frequently linked, one might have concluded that just as *ma'aser* cannot be redeemed, it also cannot be sold — and the same should apply to *bechor*. The word *hu* forestalls this: *ma'aser* indeed cannot be sold (*lo chai velo shachut, lo tam velo ba'al mum*), but *bechor* retains the leniency of being sold when blemished.

### Bechor Case

The *Gemara* then challenges from *bechor* directly. The *Mishnah* states: *yesh lahem pidyon ultemuroteihem pidyon, chutz min habechor umin hama'aser* — all other sacrifices can be redeemed if blemished, as can their *temurot*, except for *bechor* and *ma'aser*, which cannot be redeemed. This again suggests that violation is ineffective — *alma lo mahani*, and *tiyuvta de'Abaye*.

### Abaye on Hem

Abaye responds: *shani hatam de'amar Kra hem, kodesh hem* — there it is different, because the verse says *hem*, "they are holy." *Be'havayatan yehu* — they retain their *kedushah* as is. As before, Abaye argues that this is not a general consequence of violation, but a specific Scriptural declaration. The word *hem* anchors their sacred status, making the failed redemption an exception rather than evidence for Rava's principle.

### Rava on Hem

Since Rava already knows redemption fails, he uses *hem* to derive a different *halacha*: *hem krevim ve'ein temuratam kreivah* — only the *bechor* and *ma'aser* themselves are brought as *korbanot*; their *temurot* are not brought, even though the *temura* of other sacrifices can be. The underlying reason is that the *kedushah* of *bechor* and *ma'aser* is inseparable from the physical identity of the animal — it was the firstborn, or it was the tenth. That reality cannot be relocated to a different animal. Even when the *kedushah* of *temura* extends to a second animal through the act of exchange, that second animal does not become a *bechor* or *ma'aser* in any meaningful sense, and therefore cannot be offered.

A question arises for Abaye: if he used *hem* to teach that the *kedushah* cannot be removed, where does he derive the rule that the *temura* of *bechor* and *ma'aser* cannot be brought? Abaye derives it from a different verse: *im shor im kesev la'Hashem hu* — whether an ox or sheep, it is designated to God. *La'Hashem kari* — only what was originally designated to God is brought, and the *temura*, not having been the original animal, is excluded.

Rava then derives from *hem* yet another *halacha*: *ain olim mevatlin zeh et zeh* — bloods of sacrifices that go on the *mizbe'ach* do not nullify one another. If the blood of a *bechor* or *ma'aser* became mixed with the blood of other sacrifices, the principle of *bittul* does not apply, and the blood is placed on the *mizbe'ach* regardless. *Hem* teaches that their identity persists even when commingled.

Abaye, who needed *hem* for a different purpose, derives *ein olim mevatlin* from the verse in Yom Kippur: *ulekach midam hapar umidam hasa'ir* — the *Kohen Gadol* takes from the blood of the ox and the blood of the goat. Since the blood of the ox greatly exceeds the blood of the goat, why is the goat's blood not nullified? *Mikan la'olim she'ein mevatlin zeh et zeh* — here we see that bloods designated for the *mizbe'ach* do not nullify one another, as per Rabbi Yoshi'a's reading that the two bloods are mixed together. Rava, however, does not read the verse this way: *hatam mizeh bifnei atzmo umizeh bifnei atzmo*, following Rabbi Yonatan's view that each blood is applied separately, so no mixing and no opportunity for *bittul* arises from that verse.

## Consecrated Animals (II)

### Maaser Case

The challenge from *ma'aser* follows naturally. The Torah states *lo yiga'el* — it shall not be redeemed. The same *Mishnah* that excludes *bechor* also excludes *ma'aser* from redemption, even with a blemish. Abaye's response: *de'alif evra evra mibechor* — the case of *ma'aser* is linked to *bechor* through a *gezerah shavah*. By *ma'aser* the Torah uses the root *ya'avor* (*kol asher ya'avor tachat hashavet*), and by *bechor* a parallel root of passage (*ha'avarta kol peter rechem*). This linguistic link draws the laws of *ma'aser* directly from *bechor*, and since we have already established that *bechor* is an exception — where Abaye acknowledged that a special verse (*hem*) prevents the redemption from taking effect — the same exception carries over to *ma'aser* by extension.

## Out-of-Order Terumah

### Order Violation

The *Gemara* turns now to a case that is in some ways the most conceptually direct of all the examples. The Torah states *mele'atcha vedim'acha lo te'acher* — you shall not delay your *mele'atcha* (read as *bikkurim*) and your *dim'acha* (read as *terumah*). This is interpreted to mean: do not take the later one before the earlier one — do not take *terumah* before *bikkurim*, and do not take *ma'aser* before *terumah*.

### Mishna Language

The *Mishnah* states: *hamakdim — af al pi shehu belo ta'aseh, ma she'asa asui* — if one went out of order, even though it constitutes a violation of a *lo ta'aseh*, what was done is done. What is striking about this case — and about the parallel formulation in the laws of *temura* — is that the conceptual tension is explicitly articulated in the language of the *Mishnah* itself: you are forbidden to do it, yet if you did, it takes effect. This is Abaye's principle stated in so many words.

### Challenge to Rava

The *Gemara* presents this as *tiyuvta deRava*: here the Mishnah explicitly says the act takes effect despite the violation. According to Rava's principle, it should not.

### Rava's Mikol

Rava responds: *shani hatam de'amar kra mikol matnoteichem tarimu* — there it is different, because the verse says "from all of your gifts you shall take (*tarimu*)." The word *mikol* — "from all" — teaches that even when taken out of order, the *terumah* is still effective. The Torah went out of its way to indicate that this act produces valid *terumah*, which means it is an exception to the general rule.

### Abaye on Obligation

According to Abaye, who already knows violations take effect, why is this verse necessary? The *Gemara* explains through an exchange that Rav Pappa had with Abaye directly.

### Rav Pappa

Rav Pappa raised a challenge to Abaye's teaching. Abaye had ruled that if a *Levi* comes to a farmer in the field before the grain has been threshed, piled, and processed into a *kri* (the requisite stage at which *terumot uma'asrot* obligations take hold), and asks for his *ma'aser* early, the farmer may give it and it takes effect — even though *terumah* has not yet been taken. This is a significant *chiddush*, as the *kohen* would receive a smaller share (since the *ma'aser* is taken off a larger pre-*terumah* base). Rav Pappa asked: if this works in the field before piling, should it not also work at the threshing floor once the grain has been piled — let the *Levi* also get his *ma'aser* there ahead of *terumah*?

### Obligation Timing

Abaye responded: *alecha amar kra mikol matnoteichem tarimu* — regarding this case the verse says "from all your gifts you shall take." This tells us that at the stage when the obligation of *terumot uma'asrot* has already kicked in — once the grain has been fully processed and piled, reaching the stage of *digun* — the *kohen*'s claim to *terumah* is active, and the *Levi* cannot preempt it. *Marbe ani et hakri* — I include the case of the piled grain as obligated; *umotzi ani et hashibolim* — and I exclude the case of the bundles in the field, *she'eino bechlal digun*, which has not yet reached the stage of obligation. The verse *mikol matnoteichem tarimu* thus serves Abaye not to confirm that violations take effect, but to define the boundary of when the *terumah* obligation is operative — and to establish that once that threshold is reached, it cannot be circumvented by the *Levi*'s early claim.

## Kohen Gadol-Widow

### Kiddushin Effect

The final case considered in this section concerns a *kohen gadol* who marries an *almanah* (widow). The Torah states: *almanah u'gerushah lo yikach* — a *kohen gadol* shall not take a widow or divorcee. Does *kiddushin* take effect if he does so?

The general rule, derived from the principle *kol makom she'yeish kiddushin v'yeish aveirah, havlad holeich achar hapagum*, is that when *kiddushin* is effective despite a prohibition, the child follows the stigmatized party — meaning the child is *pasul* (*chalal*) but not a *mamzer*. The severe *issurei arayos* — *kares*-level prohibitions such as incest or adultery — produce no *kiddushin* at all, and the child is a *mamzer*. But the case of a *kohen gadol* with an *almanah* or a regular *kohen* with a *gerushah* is at a lower level of severity, and *kiddushin* does take effect. The child becomes *pasul* for *kehunah*, but the marriage registers.

### Challenge to Rava

The *braisa* explicitly states *yeish kiddushin* — there is *kiddushin* in this case. This is a direct challenge to Rava: why does the forbidden act take effect?

### Rava's Zaroh

Rava responds: *shani hacha, de'amar kra lo yechalel zaro* — it is different here, because the verse says "he shall not profane his seed." Tosafot explains that the doubled language — *lo yechalel* rather than simply *lo yechel* — signals a special Scriptural emphasis that the act is effective. From this additional formulation, Rava learns that the *kiddushin* does register, making this a Scripturally mandated exception to his general principle.

### Abaye on Zaroh

According to Abaye, who already knows violations take effect and thus did not need the verse to establish validity, the doubled language *lo yechalel* serves a different purpose. *Echad lo v'echad lah* — it teaches that the forbidden union affects both the child and the woman. Not only does the child born of this union become a *chalal*, but the woman herself becomes a *chalalah*. The practical significance is considerable: an *almanah* is only forbidden to a *kohen gadol*, not to a regular *kohen*. But once she engages in a prohibited *issurei kehunah* union, the act of forbidden cohabitation transforms her into a *chalalah* — making her forbidden even to an ordinary *kohen*. The verse thus extends the consequence of the prohibition to both parties. Notably, the *kohen gadol* himself is not thereby rendered *pasul* in any new way. The *Gemara* will continue with additional proofs and counter-proofs in the next *amud*.
