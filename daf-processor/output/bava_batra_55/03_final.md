# Bava Batra 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel's Doctrine

### Money vs. Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נִכְסֵי גּוֹי הֲרֵי הֵן כְּמִדְבָּר, כׇּל הַמַּחְזִיק בָּהֶן זָכָה בָּהֶן. מַאי טַעְמָא? גּוֹי – מִכִּי מָטוּ זוּזֵי לִידֵיהּ אִסְתַּלַּק לֵיהּ, יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא קָנֵי עַד דְּמָטֵי שְׁטָרָא לִידֵיהּ. הִלְכָּךְ הֲרֵי הֵן כְּמִדְבָּר, וְכׇל הַמַּחְזִיק בָּהֶן זָכָה בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to the **property of a gentile** that was sold to a Jew for money, **it is** ownerless **like a desert** until the purchaser performs an act of acquisition; **anyone who takes possession of it** in the interim has **acquired it. What is the reason** for this? The **gentile relinquishes** ownership of it **from** the moment **when** the **money reaches his hand,** while the **Jew** who purchased it **does not acquire** it **until the deed reaches his hand. Therefore,** in the period of time between the giving of the money and the receiving of the deed, the property **is like a desert, and anyone who takes possession of it** has **acquired it.**

We begin a few lines into the daf. The discussion picks up from the startling position of Shmuel that when a non-Jew sells land to a Jew, once money changes hands the non-Jew is, from the perspective of non-Jewish law, out of the picture — yet from the perspective of Jewish law, the land has not yet been acquired until a *shtar* is exchanged. There is a whole discussion among the *Rishonim* about what to make of this, given that *karka* can also be acquired by money. The upshot, however, is that this gap creates a zone in which the land is *ke-midbar* — like the wilderness, ownerless — such that whoever seizes it first acquires it. The *Rishonim* debate at length whether such a third party would then be obligated to reimburse the person who had paid the money. The underlying strangeness of the situation is what happens when two legal systems intersect: any internally coherent legal system — whether Jewish or non-Jewish — would naturally hold that when one person's possession ends, the other's begins. It is the collision of two systems that creates this anomalous gap.

### Legal Systems Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי?! וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא; וּמַלְכָּא אָמַר: לָא לִיקְנֵי אַרְעָא אֶלָּא בְּאִיגַּרְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Did Shmuel** actually **say this? But doesn't Shmuel say** that **the law of the kingdom is the law,** i.e., the *halakha* obligates Jews to observe the laws of the locale in which they reside, **and the king said** that **land may not be acquired without a document?** Therefore, taking possession should not be effective for acquisition.

The *Gemara* raised a challenge to this ruling based on Shmuel's own principle of *dina de-malkhuta dina* — the law of the government is the law. That principle would seem to close the gap, since it would provide for the fact that such an ownerless interlude cannot arise. The *Gemara* never arrived at a fully satisfying resolution.

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לָא יָדַעְנָא; עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה בְּדוּרָא דְרָעֲוָתָא, בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל דִּזְבַן אַרְעָא מִגּוֹי, וַאֲתָא יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֲרִינָא רָפֵיק בָּהּ פּוּרְתָּא; אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, אוֹקְמַהּ בִּידָא דְּשֵׁנִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דּוּרָא דְרָעֲוָתָא קָאָמְרַתְּ?! הָתָם בָּאגֵי מִטַּמְּרִי הֲווֹ – דְּאִינְהוּ גּוּפַיְיהוּ לָא הֲווֹ יָהֲבִי טַסְקָא לְמַלְכָּא, וּמַלְכָּא אֲמַר: מַאן דְּיָהֵיב טַסְקָא, לֵיכוֹל אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: I do not know** how to reconcile this contradiction, but **there was an incident in** the village of **Dura that** was founded by **shepherds, where** there was **a Jew who purchased land from a gentile** by giving money, **and** in the interim **another Jew came** and **plowed it a bit.** The two Jews **came before Rav Yehuda** for a ruling, and he **established** the property **in the possession of the second** individual. This accords with the ruling of Shmuel that the property is ownerless until a Jew performs an act of acquisition. Abaye **said to him: Are you saying** that the incident occurred in **Dura that** was founded by **shepherds?** Proof cannot be brought from that case, as **there** the **fields were concealed, since** the owners of fields **would not pay the land tax [*taska*] to the king, and the king says** that **one who pays land tax** may **profit** from **the field.** Therefore, in that case, the gentile who sold the property did not actually own it, and consequently by the laws of the kingdom could not sell it. The one who took possession of the property acquired it in accordance with the law of the kingdom, as he committed to pay the land tax. Elsewhere, one would not acquire the field until he received a deed of sale from the gentile.

What the *Gemara* did note was that one of the cases which appeared to underlie Shmuel's ruling was actually consistent with *dina de-malkhuta dina*: it involved land on which taxes had not been paid, so that the government's rule — that whoever is willing to pay the outstanding tax acquires the land — was itself the operative principle. From this it seems that *dina de-malkhuta dina* would override the anomalous ruling Shmuel created. The precise parameters of *dina de-malkhuta dina* and what it does or does not override were explored at length in the previous session.

## Third-Party Seizure

### Rav Huna's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח רַב הוּנָא בַּר אָבִין: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלָּקַח שָׂדֶה מִגּוֹי, וּבָא יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֵר וְהֶחֱזִיק בָּהּ – אֵין מוֹצִיאִים אוֹתָהּ מִיָּדוֹ. וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אָבִין וְרַבִּי אִילְעָא וְכׇל רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שָׁוִין בַּדָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Huna bar Avin sent** a ruling: In the case of **a Jew who purchased a field from a gentile, and** then **another Jew came and took possession of it, it is not removed from** the **possession of** the second Jew. **And so too, Rabbi Avin, and Rabbi Ile'a, and all of our Rabbis agree with regard to** this **matter.**

With that background, the *Gemara* now transmits a message sent by Rav Huna bar Avin — apparently addressed either to the scholars of the yeshiva or to an entire community: *Yisrael she-lakakh sade me-oved kokhavim u-va Yisrael akher ve-hekhzik bah, ein motzi'in oto mi-yado* — a Jew who purchases a field from a non-Jew, and another Jew comes and takes possession of it, we do not remove it from the second Jew's hand. This reflects precisely the scenario underlying Shmuel's ruling: money has changed hands but the *shtar* has not, and a third party moves to seize the property and may legitimately do so. The question of whether the seizing party must reimburse the original purchaser remains, as noted, a matter of debate among the *Rishonim*.

### Rabbinic Agreement

The *Gemara* adds: *ve-khen haya Rav Avin ve-Rav Ilai ve-khol raboteinu shavin ba-davar* — Rav Avin, Rav Ilai, and all of our masters agreed on this ruling, which is essentially an endorsement of Shmuel's position.

## Three Teachings

### Ukba's Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי, אִישְׁתַּעִי לִי עוּקְבָן בַּר נְחֶמְיָה רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא, וַאֲרִיסוּתָא דְפָרְסָאֵי – עַד אַרְבְּעִין שְׁנִין, וְהָנֵי זַהֲרוּרֵי דְּזָבֵין אַרְעָא לְטַסְקָא – זְבִינַיְהוּ זְבִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba said: These three statements were told to me** by **Ukvan bar Neḥemya the Exilarch in the name of Shmuel: The law of the kingdom is the law; and** the term of **Persian sharecropping [*arisuta*]** is for **up to forty years,** since according to Persian laws the presumption of ownership is established after forty years of use; **and** in the case of **these tax officials [*zaharurei*] who sold land** in order **to** pay the **land tax, the sale is valid,** as the tax officials were justified in seizing it, and one may purchase the land from them.

Rava then introduces a set of teachings: *hani telat milei ishta'ei li Ukba bar Nehemya resh galuta mi-shmei de-Shmuel* — the following three matters were related to me by Ukba bar Nehemya, the *resh galuta* (the secular head of the Jewish community in exile), in the name of Shmuel.

### Dina De-Malkhuta

The first teaching is *dina de-malkhuta dina* itself — the law of the government is the law. How this squares with the previous ruling about *ke-midbar* is not made entirely clear. The *Gemara* is inclined to feel that *dina de-malkhuta dina* would override the anomalous gap, though other possibilities were discussed in the prior session: that *dina de-malkhuta dina* applies only to public law and not to private civil matters, among other limiting principles.

### 40-Year Possession

The second and third teachings are understood as concrete applications of *dina de-malkhuta dina* operative in Persian law. The second teaching is: *ve-arissuta de-Parsaei ad arba'im shanin* — the *arissuta* of the Persians, the *chazaka*, runs for forty years. The literal meaning of *arissuta* is sharecropping, but here it refers to the Persian rule of possession.

### Arissuta Debate

The *Rashbam* explains that this is part of the *dina de-malkhuta* of Persia, and offers two interpretations. The first is a form of squatters' rights — what some people mistakenly understand *chazaka* to be generally — whereby if a person occupies a property for forty years without challenge, even without claiming to have purchased it from the original owner, Persian law considers him the owner. If, operating under this rule, a Jew purchases land from such an occupant, he need not inquire into the basis of the occupant's title, since *dina de-malkhuta* has already established ownership. The second interpretation is that Persian law recognizes a *chazaka* backed by a *ta'ana* — a legal claim — only after forty years, not after three. Underpinning both readings is a claim about sustained, unchallenged possession as the basis of legal title.

### Override Chazaka

If the second interpretation is operative and is to be applied as *dina de-malkhuta dina*, it would mean that Persian law overrides the standard *halacha* of *chazakat gimmel shanim* — the three-year presumption of ownership. The *Rashbam*, for example, states explicitly: *af gav d'bishar makomos havei chazaka l'Yisrael b'gimmel shanim, b'eretz Parsa ein chazaka b'pochos mi-mem shanim d'dina de-malkhuta hu sham* — even though in other places a *chazaka* for a Jew is established in three years, in Persia there is no *chazaka* in less than forty years, because that is the *dina de-malkhuta* there.

This raises a pointed question: if a Jew were bringing another Jew to a *beis din* with a dispute over property, would you tell him that his three years of possession is insufficient because we happen to live in Persia? That does seem like a considerable incursion of secular law into the internal *halacha* governing disputes between Jews. This is precisely the issue the *poskim* grapple with when defining the scope of *dina de-malkhuta dina*. And yet one can understand the other side as well: if a person brings a dispute to a *din Torah* and the question is who owns the property, and one party clearly acquired it according to every operative rule of secular law and is the recognized legal owner, the other party cannot plausibly respond by invoking the rules of *karka* acquisition by *kesef* or *shtar* or *chazaka* as if the wider social context were irrelevant. One might not even need *dina de-malkhuta dina* for this — what the *poskim* call *situmta* and related concepts may already resolve it by treating the prevailing social convention as itself a recognized mode of acquisition. That said, the Persian forty-year rule is somewhat different in character, particularly if it means that in a dispute between two Jews, a three-year possession is treated as legally insufficient. One can argue that we live in a society in which people are expected to preserve their documents for forty years, and that the absence of a document therefore genuinely undermines a claim to the land — but the tension with standard *halacha* remains real.

### Tax Purchasers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לְטַסְקָא, אֲבָל לִכְרָגָא – לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? כְּרָגָא – אַקַּרְקַף דְּגַבְרֵי מַנַּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And this statement** applies to land seized **to** pay the **land tax, but not** to land seized **to** pay the **head tax. What is the reason** for this? The **head tax is placed on a man's head,** i.e., the obligation of this tax is on the individual and is unrelated to his property.

The third teaching: *ve-hanei zeharurei d'zavin ara l'tiska, zavinhu zvinah* — wealthy individuals who purchase land to pay the tax, their purchase is a valid purchase. This echoes the case mentioned earlier: if the king's rule is that land on which property taxes have not been paid will be awarded to whoever is willing to pay those taxes, and someone does so and takes possession, that person is considered the legal owner. Presumably this applies not only in transactions involving non-Jews but even when one Jew takes it from another, though here too the question of how broadly *dina de-malkhuta dina* extends to internal Jewish disputes is not fully resolved.

### Tax Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שְׂעָרֵי דְכַדָּא מִשְׁתַּעְבְּדִי לִכְרָגָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is therefore theft for the tax officials to sell land for this purpose. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** Everything, **even** the **barley in the pitcher, is mortgaged for** the payment of the **head tax.**

The *Gemara* then qualifies: *ve-hanei milei l'tiska* — this applies specifically to the regular land tax, perhaps specifically property tax. *Aval l'karga, lo* — but for *karga*, the poll tax, it would not be valid. Even if the government were to declare that whoever pays a person's outstanding poll tax may seize his land, that transfer would not be recognized. Why? *Karga akarkafta d'gavra manach* — the poll tax rests on the head of the person; it has nothing to do with any particular piece of land. There is therefore no logical nexus between the tax and the real estate.

This raises a striking point: does *dina de-malkhuta dina* require logical coherence in order to be binding? Can *Chazal* decline to recognize a government rule because it strikes them as lacking internal rationale — not merely as unjust, but as illogical? One possible resolution is that we are not speaking of a formal royal edict at all, but rather a practice of local tax collectors who exercised discretion to allow poll-tax payments to be satisfied by seizing a debtor's land. If so, the rejection is not that the rule is illogical but that it is not *dina de-malkhuta* in the first place — it is a local administrative practice rather than a royal decree. But even that framing leaves some ambiguity, since the *Gemara* phrases its rejection in terms of logical sense rather than formal authority. This is a somewhat unclear *Gemara* on this point.

## Poll Tax Lien

### Portable Items

Rav Papa the son of Rav Yehoshua pushes back on the conclusion about *karga*: *afilu sare de-kada mishtabdi le-karga* — even barley sitting in a pot is encumbered by a lien for the poll tax. His argument is that even if the poll tax is not logically tied to real estate specifically, it is a personal obligation of the debtor, and the government has a right to collect it from any of his property whatsoever — including his land. It therefore does make sense that the government would permit someone who pays the outstanding poll tax to seize land equal in value to that tax. The rule is thus coherent, and therefore should qualify as legitimate *dina de-malkhuta dina*.

### Reasonableness

This exchange implies that some *Rishonim* understand *dina de-malkhuta dina* to require not merely that a rule exists but that it be equitable — *dina de-malkhuta* and not *gazlanuta de-malkhuta*, the law of the government and not the brigandage of the government. But what is interesting is that the *Gemara* here seems to go further and require not just equity and fairness but actual rational coherence — a genuine logical connection between the rule and its subject matter. It is at least one reading of this *Gemara* that *dina de-malkhuta dina* demands internal logic, not only general reasonableness.

## Amemar's Challenge

### Lien Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲמַר לִי הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן, קָשֵׁי בַּהּ אַמֵּימָר: אִם כֵּן, בִּטַּלְתָּ יְרוּשַּׁת בְּנוֹ הַבְּכוֹר – דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ רָאוּי, וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כְּבַמּוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Huna bar Natan said to me** that **Ameimar raised a difficulty with regard to this** statement of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: **If so, you have abolished the inheritance of the firstborn son** of one who owes taxes to the kingdom. If everything can be seized by the tax collectors to pay the father's debt, any property that he left behind **is** only **a potential** inheritance, not actually property of the heirs, **and** the *halakha* is that **the firstborn does not take** a double share **in a potential** inheritance **as** he does **in** property that the deceased **possessed.**

Rav Ashi reports: Huna bar Natan told him that Amemar raised a difficulty with the ruling that the government has a lien on all of a person's property for an unpaid poll tax, such that even a third party who pays the tax may seize the person's land. *Im kein bitaleta yerushat beno bechor da-havei lei ra'uy* — if so, you have effectively undermined the *halacha* that a firstborn son receives a double portion. Why? Because the *halacha* is that a firstborn receives double only from property that is *muchzak* — actually under the possession of the father at the time of death. Property that is *ra'uy* — fit to come to the estate but not yet in the father's control — does not yield a double portion for the firstborn.

### Muchzak vs Ra'uy

To illustrate: if a father had outstanding debts owed to him, and after his death those debts were collected, the incoming funds are *ra'uy*, not *muchzak*, and the firstborn does not receive double from them. Now, Amemar's concern is this: if the government holds a lien over all of the father's property on account of an unpaid poll tax, then at the moment of the father's death, none of that property is truly and fully in his possession, and all of it would be *ra'uy* — effectively owed to the government — thereby eliminating the firstborn's double portion across the board.

One might object that this is not comparable to an outstanding debt owed to the father. Here the father does own the property; the government merely has a lien on it. After all, if a private individual held a lien on the father's property, we would not say the father is not *muchzak* — the firstborn would still receive double. So why should a government lien be different?

### Two Explanations

The *Rishonim* offer two explanations, both of which may be simultaneously valid. The first is that because it is the government — possessed of sovereign power — holding the lien, the government is in a qualitatively stronger position than a private creditor: it can exercise its right to seize the property at any moment, and therefore the property is not considered to be fully in the father's possession. This explanation is admittedly difficult — one would still need to first fail to pay the tax, and then have the government actually exercise the right of seizure, before the property changes hands. The second explanation, which would have been particularly intuitive to *Rishonim* living in feudal Europe, is the concept that all land ultimately belongs to the king. If sovereignty over the land is understood to reside in the government, then so long as you remain current in your obligations the land is in your possession, but if you fall behind, the land is in a meaningful sense still theirs — it has never fully left their conceptual ownership. It is on this basis that Amemar reasons: if the poll tax creates a government lien over all property, the firstborn is effectively deprived of his double portion.

One might ask whether this is really so troubling. The *halacha* of a firstborn's double portion has not been repealed — it is simply that the circumstances for its application do not exist in this particular social context. In a communist society where no one holds private property, there would similarly be no occasion for *yerushat beno bechor*, but that would not constitute a challenge to the *halacha* itself. Nevertheless, Amemar found the practical elimination of the firstborn's double portion deeply troubling — an outcome he was unwilling to accept.

## Gemara Response

### Property Tax Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ טַסְקָא נָמֵי! אֶלָּא מָה אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – דִּיהַיב טַסְקָא וּמִית; הָכָא נָמֵי – דִּיהַיב כְּרָגָא וּמִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the statement of Huna bar Natan: And **I said to** Ameimar: **If** it is **so** that this presents a difficulty, then **even** if the property can be seized as payment for the **land tax** this difficulty would present itself **as well,** as the sons would not inherit their father's estate in the event that it is seized to pay the land tax. **Rather, what have you to say** to deflect this question? That the firstborn son's right is not negated in a case **where** the father **gave** the **land tax and** then **died,** so that he is no longer indebted to the government and the field is completely his. **Here too,** the firstborn son maintains his rights in a case **where** the father **gave** the **head tax and** then **died.**

The *Gemara* responds to Amemar's challenge by turning it back on him: *i hachi afilu tiska nami* — if this is your concern, you should raise the very same objection with respect to the property tax. We already established that the government has rights to seize land for unpaid property taxes, and indeed the *Rashbam* indicates that this too creates a lien. Why doesn't that equally undermine *yerushat beno bechor*?

There is a difference, of course: the property tax lien attaches specifically to the real estate it is levied on, whereas the poll tax — according to Rav Papa — creates a lien on all property including chattels. If the poll-tax lien extends to all of a person's assets, then there is nothing in the estate that would be *muchzak*, and the firstborn's double portion would be completely eliminated. With the property tax lien, at least all the chattel is unencumbered, and the firstborn can still receive double from those assets.

### Tax Timing

But even for real estate under a property tax lien, the *Gemara* answers: *ela mai it lach lemeimar, de-chaiaf tiska u-mit* — the case where the father paid the property tax and then died before the next assessment came due. The language of the *Gemara* makes it sound as if he died immediately after payment, which would make it a very rare case. But Rabbeinu Gershom more sensibly explains: if the father paid at the beginning of the year and the new tax is not due until the following year, then for the remainder of the year his property is entirely unencumbered. He owns it free and clear. In that window, his property is *muchzak* in every sense, and the firstborn receives double. The same logic applies to the poll tax: *hacha nami de-yahiv karga u-mit* — here too, if he paid his poll tax and died before the next poll tax became due, the estate is fully *muchzak* and the firstborn takes double.

### Halacha Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר לִי הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן: שְׁאֵילְתִּינְהוּ לְסָפְרֵי דְרָבָא, וַאֲמַרוּ לִי: הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. וְלָא הִיא, הָתָם – לְאוֹקוֹמֵי מִילְּתֵיהּ הוּא דְּאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Huna bar Natan said to me: I asked** the **scribes** who wrote documents and recorded halakhic rulings in the court **of Rava, and they said to me** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** who states that one's possessions are all mortgaged for the payment of the head tax. The Gemara notes: **But** that **is not so,** as **there,** Huna bar Natan **said** that in order **to buttress his** previous **statement.**

Rav Ashi then reports that the same Huna bar Natan — who had conveyed Amemar's question — told him: *she'iltinhu le-sifre de-Rava, ve-amru li hilkheta ke-Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua* — I asked the scribes of Rava's yeshiva, and they told me that the ruling follows Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, namely that there is indeed a recognized lien on all property for the poll tax, and that whoever pays another's outstanding poll tax is considered the legitimate acquirer of that person's property, and that this is the accepted *halacha*.

### Gemara Skepticism

The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: *ve-lav hi* — that does not really prove anything. *Hasam le-ukmei milta hu de-amru* — they said this merely to sustain their practice, meaning these scribes write many documents — evidencing that someone paid a back poll tax and thereby acquired land, and then further documents for the subsequent sale of that land — and so they are motivated to hold that the underlying transaction is valid. Their testimony therefore does not establish the *halacha* independently.

This is a striking objection, and it raises obvious questions. These are *sofrim* connected to Rava's yeshiva; if their practice were illegitimate, presumably Rava would have corrected it. The most reasonable reading of the *Gemara*'s objection is not that these *sofrim* are dishonest, but that they are biased — predisposed to accept as *halacha* what is in fact only a practice — and that the voice of the *Gemara* itself is signaling that the *halacha* should not actually follow this position. The tension, however, remains.

## Pardachas Case

### Town vs Collector

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: פַּרְדָּכְתְּ – מְסַיַּיע מָתָא. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאַצֵּילְתֵּיהּ מָתָא, אֲבָל אַנְדִּיסְקֵי – סִיַּעְתָּא דִשְׁמַיָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ashi said: An idler [*pardakht*]** must **assist the town** by paying taxes even though he has no income in that town. **And this matter** applies in a case **where the town saved him** from his obligation by asking for a reduction on his behalf. **But** if the **tax collectors [*andisekei*]** do not seek to collect his debt this **is** regarded as **heavenly assistance,** and he is not obligated to volunteer to pay his share.

Rav Ashi adds a further teaching related to the topic of taxes: *pardachas mesayeia masa* — regarding a *pardachas*, understood by the *Rishonim* as a loafer or layabout who does no work and therefore has no means to pay his share of the communal tax levy. In such a case, if the townspeople persuaded the tax collector not to compel this person to pay and collectively covered his portion on his behalf, he is nevertheless obligated to ultimately contribute his share when he does come to have money. The tax was levied on the whole town, and if others absorbed his share, he owes them.

### Divine Help

However: *ve-hanei milei de-atzluse masa* — this is only where it was the *townspeople* who intervened and protected him, paying his share themselves. *Aval andiski* — but if it was the tax collector himself who, on his own initiative and at his own discretion, decided not to collect from this person and instead drew the full amount from everyone else, then *syata di-shmaya hi* — that is divine intercession, and this person is in fact exempt from paying his share back. The tax collector had the authority to make that determination, and when he chose to exercise it, the person's obligation was genuinely discharged. This connects to the *dina de-malkhuta dina* framework: when the government — through its authorized agent — makes a discretionary determination, that determination carries legal force.

## Boundary Markers

### Rav Assi Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמֶּצֶר וְהֶחָצָב מַפְסִיקִין בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן פֵּאָה וְטוּמְאָה – לָא. כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ לְפֵאָה וְטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The boundary** between fields **and the sea squill** that was planted to demarcate the border between fields **serve as a barrier** between fields **with regard to the property of a convert** who died without heirs, so that one who takes possession of the property acquires land only until the boundary or the sea squill, but not other land the convert had possessed beyond that point. **But** they do **not** serve as a barrier between fields **with regard to the matter of produce in the corner of the field, which is given to the poor [*pe'a*], and ritual impurity,** and even the area beyond it is considered to be part of the same field. **When Ravin came** to Babylonia from Eretz Yisrael, he **said** in the name of **Rabbi Yoḥanan:** They serve as a barrier between the fields **even with regard to** the *halakhot* of ***pe'a* and ritual impurity.**

Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, teaches: *ha-meitzar ve-ha-chatzav mafsikim be-nichsei ha-ger* — a boundary marker and *chatzav* (a particular plant used as a demarcation) serve to divide sections of a *ger*'s property. The *chatzav* is a plant that grows straight down with roots that go deep and do not spread laterally; Joshua reportedly used it to mark the boundaries of the tribes in *Eretz Yisrael*. The context here brings us back from the long digression about taxes and *dina de-malkhuta dina* to the earlier discussion: when a convert (*ger*) dies without heirs and his property becomes *hefker*, whoever establishes a *chazaka* acquires not the whole estate but only the specific plot he took possession of. The boundary markers divide the estate into separate parcels, so that a *chazaka* on one plot does not extend to an adjacent plot separated by a boundary or by *chatzav*.

*Aval le-inyan pe'ah ve-tumah lo* — but for purposes of *pe'ah* and *tumah*, this kind of simple demarcation does not suffice to divide fields. For those purposes, something more substantial is required.

### Mishna Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּאָה – מַאי הִיא? דִּתְנַן: וְאֵלּוּ מַפְסִיקִין לַפֵּאָה – הַנַּחַל, וְהַשְּׁלוּלִית, וְדֶרֶךְ הָרַבִּים, וְדֶרֶךְ הַיָּחִיד, וּשְׁבִיל הָרַבִּים, וּשְׁבִיל הַיָּחִיד הַקָּבוּעַ בֵּין בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וּבֵין בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is** the *halakha* of ***pe'a*** that is affected by determining whether it is one or two fields? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 2:1): **And these serve as a barrier for** the purpose of ***pe'a*,** i.e., the presence of any of these divides a field so that each section constitutes a distinct field from which *pe'a* must be given independently: **A stream** that passes through the field; **and a canal;** **and a public road** that is at least sixteen cubits wide; **and a private road** that is four cubits wide; **and a public trail; and a permanent private trail** that is used **whether in the summer or in the rainy season,** i.e., winter.

The *Gemara* asks what constitutes a sufficient physical break for *pe'ah*. The Mishna teaches: *ha-nachal, ve-ha-shulit, ve-derech ha-rabim, ve-derech ha-yachid* — a *nachal* (here not a river but a dry wadi, like the *nachal eitan* of the *egla arufa*), a *shulit* (a stream of running rainwater that constitutes a real geographical break), a public road, or a private road. The *Rashbam* explains that a road here means something used by vehicles — wagons — a real piece of infrastructure, not merely a line across a field. Any of these, running between two sections of a field owned by one person, requires *pe'ah* to be left separately in each section.

### Path Requirements

A *shvil* — a footpath, whether public or private, even one so narrow as to serve only a single pedestrian — can also divide fields, but with an additional requirement: *ha-kavu'a bein bi-yemot ha-chama bein bi-yemot ha-geshamim* — it must be a fixed path used in both the summer and the rainy season. In the rainy season, people avoid traversing fields because it is muddy and would damage the crops; so a path used even during the rainy season is genuinely embedded in the landscape. Without that criterion, a mere footpath would not constitute a meaningful division. A road, by contrast, does not require this additional criterion.

One might wonder why the Mishna bothers listing both the public road and the private road rather than simply stating the most minimal case of the private footpath. The *Yerushalmi* addresses this: when it comes to trees, even a public road does not divide sections of a field for *pe'ah* purposes. The Mishna therefore wants to establish both extremes: for non-tree fields, the rule applies across the full spectrum from public road down to private footpath (so long as it is used in both seasons); for trees, even the most substantial road is insufficient. The contrast requires both poles to be stated.

## Peah & Tumah

### Peah Example

As noted, Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan had said that for *pe'ah* and *tumah*, a simple boundary marker or *chatzav* plant does not divide the field, whereas for the *nichsei ha-ger* it does. The Mishna's list — *nachal*, *shulit*, public road, private road, *shvil* — represents the threshold required for *pe'ah*.

### Logic Difference

The underlying logic of this distinction is worth considering. When the question is one of ownership and acquisition — as in the *nichsei ha-ger* case — any marker that demarcates "I treat this as my property and I am taking possession of this plot as distinct from that one" is conceptually sufficient. Ownership is, to a significant degree, about intentional recognition: this is mine, that is a separate thing. But when the question is *pe'ah* — the obligation to leave a corner of the field from the actual grain growing there — the fact that the owner *considers* two sections of his field to be separate is beside the point. He owns both. The grain in both is his. The question is whether they are physically, geographically, truly distinct fields, and for that you need something real on the landscape. A line someone drew, or a plant someone placed, reflecting his private decision to regard them as separate — that carries no weight when the *halacha* looks at the physical field itself.

## Tumah Case

### Eliezer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָה – מַאי הִיא? דִּתְנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לְבִקְעָה בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְטוּמְאָה בְּשָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית, וְאָמַר: הָלַכְתִּי לַמָּקוֹם הַלָּז, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם נִכְנַסְתִּי לְאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם וְאִם לָאו – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִין. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: סְפֵק בִּיאָה – טָהוֹר, סְפֵק מַגַּע טוּמְאָה – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further clarifies: **What is** the *halakha* of **ritual impurity** that is affected by determining whether an area is one or two fields? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 6:5): With regard to **one who enters into a valley during the rainy season,** i.e., winter, when people generally do not enter this area, and therefore for the purpose of this *halakha* it is considered a private domain, and there is a principle that in a case of uncertainty concerning whether one contracted ritual impurity in a private domain he is ritually impure; **and** there was **ritual impurity in such and such a field, and** he **said:** I know **I walked to that place,** i.e., I walked in the valley, **but I do not know whether I entered that place** where the ritual impurity was **or whether I did not** enter, **Rabbi Eliezer deems him pure and the Rabbis deem him impure.** Rabbi Eliezer deems him pure, **as Rabbi Eliezer would say:** Concerning **uncertainty** with regard to **entry,** i.e., it is uncertain whether he entered the area where the ritual impurity is located, he is **ritually pure.** But if he certainly entered the area where the ritual impurity is located and the **uncertainty** is with regard to **contact** with **ritual impurity,** he is **ritually impure.** It is with regard to this *halakha* that Ravin said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan that a boundary or sea squill defines these fields as distinct areas.

The scenario for *tumah* is thus: a valley in the rainy season, multiple fields, a grave known to be in one of them, and a person who is uncertain whether he entered the specific field containing the grave. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages — whether uncertainty about *bi'ah* as opposed to uncertainty about *maga* changes the outcome — turns precisely on how we define and delimit the private domain in question, and what it means to have "entered" the relevant field when the fields may or may not be meaningfully divided.

The principle derived from *sotah* is that a *safek tumah* in a *reshut ha-yachid* renders one *tamei*, while a *safek tumah* in a *reshut ha-rabim* leaves one *tahor*. A valley in the rainy season is a *reshut ha-yachid* for *tumah* purposes — it is secluded, no one traverses it in that season, it is muddy, and entering would ruin the crops. If someone entered this valley and there is a grave known to be in one of the fields, and the person says: I went to the valley but I do not know whether I entered the field where the *tumah* is — *Rabbi Eliezer metaher ve-chakhamim metam'in*. Rabbi Eliezer holds the person is *tahor*; the Sages hold the person is *tamei*.

The reason: *shehaya Rabbi Eliezer omer, safek bi'ah tahor, safek maga tumah tamei* — Rabbi Eliezer distinguishes two categories of doubt. A *safek bi'ah* — a doubt as to whether one even entered the relevant space — renders one *tahor*, because the operative principle from *sotah* requires that the person was in the private space in the first place; if you do not know whether you were there, the default is *tahor*. A *safek maga* — a doubt as to whether one made contact with the source of *tumah* — renders one *tamei*, since this is a case where you were in the relevant enclosed space and the question is only whether you touched or walked over the grave. The Sages hold that in either case, since you are in a *reshut ha-yachid* for *tumah*, you are *tamei*.

### Rashbam & Tosfos

The *Rashbam* and *Tosfos* debate the underlying logic of Rabbi Eliezer's position. The *Rashbam* understands Rabbi Eliezer to be working with a principle of *sfeik sfeika* — a double doubt: I do not know if you entered the field, and even if you did, I do not know if you made contact with the *tumah*. Their debate then centers on whether *sfeik sfeika* renders one *tahor* or *tamei* in a *reshut ha-yachid*. *Tosfos* reads Rabbi Eliezer more simply, and closer to the plain language: the *sotah* principle applies specifically to a case of *safek maga* — where one was definitely in the private domain and the question is about contact. Where the doubt is about *bi'ah* itself — whether one ever entered the defined space — the mechanism of *safek tumah be-reshut ha-yachid tamei* simply does not engage, and the default is *tahor*. This reading of Rabbi Eliezer does not require invoking *sfeik sfeika* at all.

## Shabbat Melacha

### One Obliviousness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, דְּתַנְיָא: הוֹצִיא חֲצִי גְרוֹגֶרֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִנִּיחָה, וְחָזַר וְהוֹצִיא חֲצִי גְרוֹגֶרֶת אַחֶרֶת; בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד – חַיָּיב. בִּשְׁנֵי הֶעְלֵמוֹת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** They serve as a barrier between fields **even with regard to the matter of Shabbat, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who **carried out half** of **a dried fig** from a private domain **into the public domain and placed it** there, **and** then **returned and carried out another half** of **a dried fig,** if it was done **within one lapse of awareness,** i.e., he did not remember in the interim that this act is prohibited or that it was Shabbat, the two acts are considered as one, and since the two items together equal the size of a dried fig, he is **liable** to bring a sin-offering. But if it was done **within two lapses** of awareness, i.e., after he carried out the first half of a dried fig he remembered that this act is prohibited or that it was Shabbat, but subsequently forgot again and carried out the second half of a dried fig, he is **exempt.**

*Rava* extends the same underlying logic to *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: if someone carried a half-measure of a *grogeret* (dried fig) — that is, half the minimum volume required to be liable for carrying on *Shabbat*, since the *shiur* for most *melakhot* is the volume of a *grogeret* — into *reshut ha-rabim*, set it down, and then carried out another half-measure and set that down as well, do the two half-measures combine to constitute a single prohibited act? The answer is: yes, if they were performed *be-helem achas* — in a single state of obliviousness. The mechanism for combining two half-measures of a *melakha* on *Shabbat* is not time-based (as it is for eating and drinking on *Yom Kippur* or *Tisha B'Av*, where the standard is *kedei achilas pras* or *kedei shetiyas revi'is*), but rather continuity of mental state: as long as the person did not remember in the middle that it was *Shabbat* — and thus experience an intervening moment of awareness — both acts are considered part of one continuous prohibited act, and he is obligated to bring a *korban chatat*. *Bi-shnei helamos patur* — if there were two distinct states of forgetfulness, with an intervening awareness, the acts are separate and he is exempt.

### Same Domain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד; בִּרְשׁוּת אַחַת – חַיָּיב, בִּשְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. **Rabbi Yosei says:** If it was done **within one lapse of awareness,** and **in one domain,** i.e., he carried half the dried fig into the same public domain each time, he is **liable,** but if it was **in two domains,** i.e., he carried the item into two separate public domains, he is **exempt.**

Rabbi Yosei adds a further condition — at the top of 56a: *be-helem echad be-reshut achas, chayav; bi-shtei reshuyot, patur* — even within a single state of forgetfulness, the two half-measures only combine if they were deposited in the same *reshut ha-rabim*. If one half was placed in one public domain and the second half in a different public domain, they do not combine, and one is exempt. The act of carrying is conceptually complete only when something is brought *to a place*, and if I brought two half-measures to two separate places, I did not bring one thing to one place.

### Separating Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאָמַר רַבָּה: וְהוּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ חִיּוּב חַטָּאת בֵּינֵיהֶם, אֲבָל כַּרְמְלִית – לֹא. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ כַּרְמְלִית, אֲבָל פִּיסְלָא – לֹא. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ פִּיסְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says** in explanation of Rabbi Yosei's opinion: **And this** division of the public domain applies only **where there is** a property where one would incur **liability** to bring **a sin-offering** if one unintentionally carried out of it or into it, i.e., a private domain, **between** the two sections. **But** if there was only **a *karmelit*,** i.e., an area that is not defined as either a private domain or public domain and to and from which the prohibition against carrying is only of rabbinic origin, it does **not** divide the public domain. **Abaye says: Even a *karmelit*** divides the public domain into separate sections, **but a beam [*pisela*]** does **not. Rava says: Even a beam** divides the public domain, since it is no less than a boundary or sea squill, which do serve as a barrier between fields.

This raises the question of how to define one *reshut ha-rabim* versus two. If it is all one continuous road and you deposited one half higher up and one half further down, are they the same *reshut* or different? Rabbah states: *ve-hu she-yesh chiyuv chatat beineihem* — the two sections are considered separate *reshuyot* only if what lies between them would itself impose a *chiyuv chatat* — a Torah-level liability — if crossed, meaning the intervening space must constitute a *reshut ha-yachid*. If you have two stretches of *reshut ha-rabim* separated by a private building or a row of buildings — genuinely *reshut ha-yachid* — then they are two separate domains. *Aval Carmelis lo* — but if the intervening space is merely a *Carmelis* (an intermediate domain, neither fully public nor private), such as a wide expanse of grass or a park, that does not constitute a Torah-level domain separator and therefore does not divide the two stretches into separate *reshuyot* for this purpose.

Abaye disagrees: *afilu Carmelis* — even a *Carmelis* suffices to divide two stretches of *reshut ha-rabim* into separate domains for this *halacha*. However, Abaye draws his own line: *aval pislo* — a *pislo*, a small platform or minor constructed structure that does not rise to the level of a meaningful domain, is insufficient. Rava goes further still: *afilu pislo* — even a *pislo* divides the two stretches into separate *reshuyot*. This is closely analogous to the position Rava takes on the *meitzar* and *chatzav* for *Shabbat* purposes: just as Rava holds that even a minor constructed boundary can define separate domains in the property context, so too here, even a *pislo* suffices to define two separate *reshuyot ha-rabim* such that two half-measures placed on either side would not combine, and one would be exempt from a *chatat*.
