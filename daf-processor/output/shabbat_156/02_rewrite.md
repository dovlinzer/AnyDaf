# Shabbat 156 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Water & Bran

### Mishna Setup

The daf begins at the bottom of 155b, in the context of various rulings related to feeding farm animals. The particular concern that now arises touches on a potential *de'oraita* prohibition: when one adds water to bran, does that constitute *lisha* — the forbidden labor of kneading?

The *mishna* permits pouring water into bran but prohibits kneading it. Abaye reports that he posed the question before his master Rabba: *matnitin mani* — whose position does our *mishna* reflect? Rabba answered that it follows Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda.

### Lisha Debate

The underlying question is why adding water to bran should be permitted at all. When one pours water into flour, is that not itself a form of *lisha*? A *baraisa* frames the dispute explicitly: if one person places flour and another pours water into it, *Ha'acharon chayav* — the latter is liable, according to Rabbi. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: one is not liable until one actually kneads. Our *mishna*, which permits adding the water, therefore appears to reflect the position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda.

The *Gemara* pushes back: perhaps the permissive position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda applies only to flour, which is *bar gibul* — subject to kneading — but not necessarily to bran.

### Bar Gibbul

The *Gemara* introduces the concept of *bar gibul*: bran, unlike flour, does not readily dissolve when water is added. It does not quickly transform into a cohesive dough; one must work it considerably more, and even then it does not reach the consistency of classic dough.

Counterintuitively, the *Gemara* suggests that this very quality — being *lav bar gibul* — might make bran *more* subject to concern, not less. Since bran never achieves a full classical dough, even the earliest stage of mixing is considered significant, a kind of loose mixture being the most one can expect from it. As paradoxical as this sounds, the logic is that when the endpoint is already a degraded form of *lisha*, even the threshold stage is treated as meaningful.

The consequence of accepting this line of reasoning would be troubling: our *mishna* would have no *tanna* to follow. Even Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda would agree that adding water to bran is a problem.

### Baraita Rejects

The *Gemara* rejects this reading outright: *lo salka da'atach*. A *baraisa* states explicitly: *ein notnin mayim l'mursan dibrei Rabbi* — Rabbi holds one may not add water to bran at all. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda says one may. The *bar gibul* distinction does not alter the fundamental dispute. Our *mishna* does indeed follow Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that adding water alone does not constitute *lisha* — the prohibition applies only to actual kneading.

The broader question of how we rule remains complex. In general practice, one does not add water to substances that will dissolve and form a dough. Where leniency might apply, it is only with a *shinui* — a deviation from the normal manner of preparation.

### Kelli Kneading

The *Gemara* cites a *baraisa*: *ein govlin et hakelli* — one may not knead *kelli*, a type of wheat roasted in the oven that becomes sweet and is used to prepare a mixture called *shasisa* (combined with oil, vinegar, and salt). A dissenting view — *yeish omrim govlin* — permits it.

Rav Chisda identifies the lenient opinion as that of Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda. According to Rabbi, even adding the water creates a problem; according to Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda, since the addition of liquid is permitted, the concern focuses only on the kneading itself. Yet actual kneading is the classic act of *lisha* — how can it be permitted?

### Shinui: Bit by Bit

The *Gemara* immediately qualifies the lenient ruling: *vahani mili huda meshani* — it is only permitted if one does it with a *shinui*. This is a striking feature of certain *melakhot*: while most *de'oraita* prohibitions are guarded by layer upon layer of rabbinic safeguard, here we find a case where even an act that might constitute biblical *lisha* is permitted when performed with a *shinui*.

A parallel case is *tochin*: grinding spices may be permitted on *Shabbat* if both the grinding implement and the surface are each a *shinui* from their normal use — this is actually the ruling in *Shulchan Aruch*, even though *techina* is a *melakha de'oraita*. Bran kneading follows a similar pattern.

Rav Chisda specifies the *shinui*: *al yad al yad* — a little at a time. Even performing the same action in small increments constitutes a sufficient *shinui*, without doing anything qualitatively different. This is itself surprising — one is performing the same *melacha*, merely piecemeal.

The general *psak* as it emerges from the full *Gemara* incorporates multiple criteria together. First, one reverses the order of combining ingredients — if normally water is added to the solid, one adds the solid to the water first. Second, the mixture should ideally be *belila raka*, a loose mixture resembling batter rather than *belila ava*, which is thick dough. The distinction matters because *lisha de'oraita* arguably applies only to a thick dough-like consistency. Third, one employs a *shinui* in the manner of mixing as well, which the *Gemara* will develop further.

### Shatis Method

The *Gemara* notes that all opinions agree that stirring *shatis* — a honey-wheat mixture — on *Shabbat* is permitted. This seems to contradict what was just established about requiring a *shinui*. The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *lo kashya ha be'ava ha berakika* — the cases differ based on whether the mixture is thick or thin. A loose mixture is categorically different from a thick dough.

Yet even for the thin mixture, the *Gemara* immediately adds qualifications: *vahani mili huda meshani* — still only with a *shinui*. Rav Yosef specifies: the normal practice is to add the vinegar first, then the *shatis*; on *Shabbat*, one reverses the order — first the *shatis*, then the vinegar. This is the *shinui* of addition, which addresses how the components are combined.

The question remains whether reversing the order also resolves concerns about the act of stirring itself, or whether a separate *shinui* is needed for that step as well. Rashi's approach is that since the mixture is *belila raka*, it does not constitute *lisha* in the first place, which would explain why no additional *shinui* for stirring is required. Yet this reasoning is in some tension with the requirement to reverse the order of addition — if the loose mixture eliminates the *lisha* concern entirely, why is the order *shinui* needed at all?

The practical *halakha* incorporates all the leniencies together: a loose mixture (*belila raka*), reversal of the order of addition, and a *shinui* in the manner of mixing. The *Gemara* seems to suggest that while any one of these factors might be independently significant, one should seek to combine them all.

## Case: Animal Handlers

### Handlers Mixing

The *Gemara* now presents a concrete case. The son of Rav Huna bar Chiyya found the animal attendants of his father's household (*devei nasha* — a term that can refer to the women's household, or, as appears relevant here, to the household after the father's death) actually kneading bran with water and feeding it to the cattle. He kicked them — *batash beih* — insisting they stop, since this appeared to violate the *mishna*'s prohibition on kneading.

It is worth noting what the *mishna* says: *notnim mayim l'mursan aval lo govlin* — one may add water to bran but may not knead it. This attendant was performing actual *gibul*, which is precisely what the *mishna* forbids.

### Maternal Grandfather

Then his father arrived and found him. He said: *hachi amar avuha de'immach* — here is what your mother's father says — meaning the position of his maternal grandfather. He cited a ruling in the name of Rav, transmitted by Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba: *govlin velo masfin* — one may knead, but one may not hand-feed (*maspi*) the food directly into the animal's mouth. Furthermore, *dela lakit bilshoneih machalkitin leh* — if the calf is too young to lap it up with its own tongue, one may assist it in swallowing.

Rashi notes that the prohibition on *masfin* corresponds to the force-feeding issue discussed in an earlier *mishna*. The ruling of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba thus permits the kneading itself, contrary to the son's objection.

### Shinui Required

The *Gemara* immediately intervenes: *vahani mili dameshany* — even this leniency applies only if performed with a *shinui*. This editorial gloss consistently appears throughout the *sugya*, each time the *Gemara* states a permissive ruling, it adds the qualification that a *shinui* is required.

### Cross Stirring

Rav Yeimar bar Shmaya citing Abaye specifies the *shinui* for the kneading motion: *shesi ve'erev* — warp and woof. Rather than the normal elliptical stirring motion, one makes alternating strokes in perpendicular directions — effectively tracing a plus sign rather than circular motions. This is more laborious and less effective, since it does not reach all portions of the mixture evenly.

The *Gemara* itself notes the problem: *lo me'arev shapir* — it does not mix well.

### Vessel Method

This leads to a second alternative: Rav Yehuda rules that one may pour the mixture from one vessel to another — *mena'ar leh l'kli* — achieving mixing through the act of pouring between containers rather than through direct stirring. One may not perform the normal kneading motion, but this alternative method is permitted.

To summarize the full picture that has emerged: there is a debate about whether one may even add water to bran (*Rabbi* vs. *Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda*); where adding the water is permitted, reversing the order of addition constitutes a *shinui* for that step; and the actual mixing may be performed only with a *shinui* — either a little at a time, or with cross-directional strokes, or by pouring between vessels. All of this is ideally combined with the mixture being *belila raka*. What is extraordinary, and acknowledged as such by the *Gemara*, is that these *shinuyim* seem to permit even what might be a *melacha de'oraita*.

## Notebooks Section

### Zeiri Notebook

The *Gemara* introduces a new source: *kesav apinkasei di-Zeiri* — it was written in the notebook of Zeiri. Rashi explains *pinkas* as bound writing tablets of the kind used by merchants. The entry records that Zeiri said before his teacher Rabbi Chiya: *ma'u legabbei* — what is the ruling regarding kneading (*gibul*)? Rabbi Chiya responded: *assur* — it is forbidden. This aligns with the *mishna*'s prohibition and with the position of Rabbi.

Zeiri then asked: *ma'u lefarek* — what about *perika*, unloading? Rashi understands this as the transfer of fruit from one animal to another, discussed in an earlier *mishna*. Tosafot, however, interprets *perika* in this context as pouring from vessel to vessel — the same technique Rav Yehuda described as a *shinui* for mixing. On this, Rabbi Chiya ruled: *mutar* — permitted.

### Animal Feeding

Rav Menashya, whose ruling also appears in the context of the *pinkas*, addresses proportional feeding of animals: one may place one standard measure before one animal (*chad kammai chad*), and two before two (*trei kammai trei*). However, three measures before two animals is *assur* — excessive and involving unnecessary labor (*tircha*) on *Shabbat*.

Rav Yosef disagrees and sets no upper limit — *kav va'afilu kabayim* — even a *kav* or two *kabayim* is permitted. Ulla goes further still: *kur va'afilu kurayim* — even a *kur* or two *kurin*. A *kur* is a very large measure, making Ulla's ruling extremely permissive.

### Levi Notebook

The *Gemara* now cites *kesiv apinkasei deLevi* — what was written in the notebook of Levi. Levi recorded that he mentioned before his teacher Rabbi — the *Gemara* glosses: *Rabbeinu Hakadosh*, Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi — that in Bavel people were accustomed to actually knead *shatis* (the bran-and-honey mixture) on *Shabbat*. *Vetzavach Rabbi* — Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi would cry out in protest. *Ve'lais deshmia lei* — and no one would listen to him. *Ve'lais cheilei bidei lemisar* — and he did not have the ability to forbid it by authority, *mide'Yosei bar'Rabbi Yehuda* — because Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda's lenient position provided a basis for the established practice.

This is a notable example of the tension between *psak halacha* and entrenched communal practice. Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi accepted that *lisha* was prohibited and was disturbed by the practice in Bavel. Yet the people there relied on Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda's position, which, once it permitted adding the liquid, opened the door to permitting actual kneading with a *shinui*. The communal practice, backed by a legitimate rabbinic opinion, was beyond his power to overturn.

## Astrology Section

### Birth Days

From the notebook of Levi, the *Gemara* transitions — without any apparent thematic connection to the *Shabbat* laws just discussed — to the notebook of his son, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. What follows is a lengthy and remarkable passage on astrological influence.

The notebook records character traits associated with the day of the week on which one is born. Someone born on Sunday (*echad b'Shabbat*) will be a great person — *yehei gavar* — but without one element of the other direction: either entirely righteous or entirely wicked, not mixed. The *Gemara* illustrates with Rav Ashi, who says that he himself was born on Sunday, as was Dimi bar Kakuzta — and while Rav Ashi became the head of a *yeshiva*, the equivalent of a king, Dimi bar Kakuzta became the chief of bandits. The point is full greatness in one direction or the other. The rationale offered is that Sunday was the day on which both light and darkness were created, making it a day of complete extremes.

Someone born on Monday will be angry (*ragzan*), because on that day the waters were separated — a state of conflict and division. Someone born on Tuesday will be wealthy and promiscuous (*atir ve'zanai*), because the vegetation (*deshaim*) was created then — it grows quickly and spreads promiscuously everywhere, symbolizing both wealth and indulgence. Rashi notes that the word for promiscuous growth is used similarly about plants.

Someone born on Wednesday will be wise and luminous — or, according to some, will have a good memory — because the celestial luminaries (*me'orot*) were created on that day. Someone born on Thursday will be a doer of kindness (*gomel chasadim*), because the fish and birds were created then; Rashi explains that fish and birds have their food more readily available to them, symbolizing effortless divine provision, which mirrors the quality of *chesed*.

Someone born on Friday (*ma'alei Shabbata*) will be a *chazran* — one who is always running and pursuing things — just as everyone rushes on *Erev Shabbat*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds: *chazran b'mitzvot* — specifically one who runs after opportunities to perform *mitzvot*, since all the running on *Erev Shabbat* is in preparation for a sacred purpose.

Someone born on *Shabbat* will die on *Shabbat*, because the great day of *Shabbat* was profaned on his account at the moment of his birth. Yet this is immediately qualified: Rava bar Rav Shila adds that such a person will be called the great holy one, *kadisha raba*, by virtue of having been born on *Shabbat* itself.

### Mazal Debate

Rabbi Chanina, upon hearing all of this from the students who reported the notebook of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, responded: go and tell Bar Liva'i — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — that it is not *mazal yom* — the astrological significance of the day of birth — that determines a person's character. Rather, *mazal sha'ah gorem* — it is the star ruling at the hour of birth that matters.

The framework underlying this position is that the seven celestial bodies rotate through the hours of the day in a fixed sequence. Since neither a day nor a week is divisible evenly by seven, the celestial body presiding at any given hour differs based on the day of the week — a system charted by Steinsaltz in detail.

### Celestial Bodies

According to the *mazal sha'ah* system, someone born under the sun (*chama*) will be luminous and self-sufficient — eating and drinking from his own resources, just as the sun is self-sustaining and provides light to all. But his secrets will be exposed (*razohi galyan*), just as the sun illuminates everything and conceals nothing. If he is a thief, he will not succeed.

Someone born under Venus (*Noga*) will be wealthy and promiscuous (*atir ve'zanai*) — the same traits attributed to Tuesday births in the *mazal yom* system. The reason given is *mishum de'ityalid be'nura* — because a fire was kindled within him, Venus being associated with burning passion. Rashi specifies *yeter tashmish*.

Someone born under Mercury (*chochav*) will be luminous and wise, because Mercury is the *safra de'chama* — the scribe of the sun, always in close proximity to it, absorbing its light and wisdom. In Greek mythology Mercury is the divine messenger, and there may be an echo of that conception here.

Someone born under the moon (*levana*) will suffer much evil, building and destroying, destroying and building — reflecting the moon's constant waxing and waning. He eats and drinks what is not his own, since the moon's light is borrowed from the sun. His secrets will be hidden (*razohi kasyan*), and if he is a thief, he will succeed.

Someone born under Saturn (*Shabtai*) — whose name echoes *Shabbat* and the root of cessation — will have his plans frustrated (*machshavteih betalin*). An alternative view reverses this: whoever plots against him will have their plans frustrated.

Someone born under Jupiter (*Tzedek*) will be righteous (*tzadkan*). Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds: *tzadkan b'mitzvot*, and Rashi specifies here that *mitzvot* refers particularly to *tzedakah*.

### Mars & Blood

Someone born under Mars (*Ma'adim*) will be *ashir dami* — a shedder of blood. This is among the most cited lines in the passage. Rav Ashi immediately qualifies it: *ei umna ei ganva ei tavcha ei mahola* — he may be a bloodletter, a killer, a ritual slaughterer, or a *mohel*. In other words, the astrological inclination toward blood does not determine its expression — that is left to the individual's choices and circumstances.

This observation, if one brackets the astrological framework itself, reflects something recognizable: people are born with different temperaments and inclinations, whether through genetics, upbringing, or socioeconomic circumstance. Much is fixed; what is not fixed — what is always in one's own hands — is *tzadik o rasha*, the moral direction. The *Gemara* elsewhere states that before birth an angel decrees whether one will be rich or poor, strong or weak, but *not* whether one will be righteous or wicked, for that is given over entirely to the individual.

Rava himself states: *ana b'Ma'adim havai* — I was born under Mars, yet I am none of those. Abaye responds: *mar nami anish ve'katil* — but the master also punishes and causes death, in his capacity as a *dayan*. The redirected Mars energy manifests even in judicial authority. One participant noted that Rava once caused the death of Rav Zeira on *Purim*, an incident recorded in Tractate Megillah — another dark echo of *ashir dami* directed through unexpected channels.

### Mazal & Jews

The *Gemara* now addresses whether astrological influence applies to Jews at all. Rabbi Chanina states: *mazal machkim mazal ma'ashir* — the constellation makes one wise, makes one wealthy — *ve'yesh mazal l'Yisrael* — and it applies even to Jews. This is in tension with Rabbi Chanina's own famous dictum that *hakol bidei shamayim chutz me'yirat shamayim* — everything is in the hands of heaven except the fear of heaven. The *Gemara* understands him as saying that astrological influence is real and powerful, though not absolutely determinative.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *ein mazal l'Yisrael* — the constellations do not govern Jews. He derives this from the verse: *ko amar Hashem el derech hagoyim al tilmadu umi'otot hashamayim al techattu* — do not fear the signs of the heavens, for it is the nations who fear them, not Israel. Rav agrees: *ein mazal l'Yisrael*.

### Abraham Test

Rav Yehuda citing Rav — a passage famously quoted by Rashi in the *Chumash* — derives the principle from the episode of Avram. God took Avram outside (*vayotze oto hachutza*) and showed him the stars. Avram protested: *nistakalti be'itztagninut sheli* — I have looked in my astrology, *ve'eini ro'eh leholid ben* — and I see that I am not destined to have a son. God replied: *tze me'itztagninut shelcha* — step outside your astrology. *She'ein mazal l'Yisrael* — for there is no constellation for Israel.

The *Gemara* then asks: what was Avram's specific astrological concern? *Dekai tzedek be'ma'arava* — his sign, Tzedek (Jupiter), was in the west at the relevant time, which according to Rashi signifies a cooler, less generative position, since the sun rises in the east. God responds: *mahadrina u'mokeimna leh be'mizrach* — I will move the star to the east, to a more generative position.

This is connected to the verse *mi he'ir mi'mizrach tzedek yikra'ehu l'raglo* — who bestirred from the east, Tzedek called to his feet — a *pasuk* from the *haftarah* of the relevant *parasha*, which according to this reading refers not to Avraham himself coming from the east, but to God repositioning the constellation Tzedek in the east for Avraham's sake.

Rashi's comment in the *Chumash*, however, takes a different approach: *Avram eino molid aval Avraham molid* — Avram will not beget, but Avraham will. The change of name itself — the addition of the *hei* — altered the astrological reality. What this implies, importantly, is not that the astrology was simply wrong, but that God intervened to change it. The stars had genuine influence, but God overrode them.

### Shmuel Case

A story involving Shmuel illustrates how *ein mazal l'Yisrael* works in practice. Shmuel and the non-Jewish astrologer Avlat were sitting together, watching men walking toward a lake. Avlat declared that one of the men — identifiable by his astrological sign — would go and not return; a snake would bite him and he would die. Shmuel said: if he is a Jew, he will go and come back.

The man went, returned, and Avlat rose and examined his bundle, finding a snake that had been cut into two halves. He asked the man what he had done. The man explained: each day, the group would pool their bread for a communal meal. That day, one member had no bread to contribute and was visibly embarrassed. The man — so as not to embarrass him — went around collecting as if taking bread from everyone, but made it appear as though bread was also taken from the embarrassed man, and contributed his own bread in that person's stead, *ki heichi d'lo lichsif* — so that he would not be shamed.

Shmuel declared: *mitzvah avadat* — you performed a *mitzvah*. He then went out and expounded: *u'tzedakah tatzil mi'mavet* — *tzedakah* saves from death. And not merely from an unusual or painful death (*misah meshunah*), as another *Gemara* interprets the verse, but *ela mimisah atzmah* — from death itself, from the very death that had been decreed for the person.

This raises a question: if Shmuel believed that Jews are above astrological influence, how did he confidently predict the man would return? It seems he did not know that the man had already performed an act of *tzedakah*; he stated his prediction on general principle. But the story also suggests the mechanism: it was not that the constellation had no hold at all, but that the *tzedakah* had already neutralized it. How Shmuel knew with certainty the outcome is left unexplained.

### Akiva Daughter

Similarly, from Rabbi Akiva: *d'Rabbi Akiva havya lei barta* — Rabbi Akiva had a daughter. The *kalda'ei* — astrologers — told him: *hahu yoma d'aylah l'vei ganana* — on the very day she enters the bridal canopy, a snake will bite her and she will die. Rabbi Akiva's daughter was understandably consumed with anxiety over this prediction.

On her wedding night, she took her hair pin (*machvanta d'tzavta*) and inserted it into a hole in the wall. In the morning, when she withdrew it, she pulled out a snake impaled on the pin — it had been in the wall, waiting, and she had inadvertently driven the pin directly into its eye, killing it.

Her father asked: what did you do? She told him: a poor man had come to the door calling out for food, but everyone was preoccupied with the wedding meal and no one heard him. She rose, took the portion that had been given to her, and gave it to him. Rabbi Akiva declared: *mitzvah avadat* — you performed a *mitzvah* — and went out to expound: *u'tzedakah tatzil mi'mavet* — not from an unusual death, *ela mimisah atzmah* — but from the preordained death itself.

### Rav Nachman

A third case: the mother of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak was told by the *kalda'ei* that her son would be a thief. She therefore never allowed him to walk with his head uncovered. Her reasoning, which she would express to him: *kasei reisheich ki heichi d'teihavei lach eimata d'Shamaya* — cover your head so that the fear of heaven will be upon you. She also urged him always to pray for divine assistance in overcoming his inclinations, though she never told him what the *kalda'ei* had predicted.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak himself never knew why his mother insisted on this. One day he was sitting beneath a palm tree that was not his, studying, and his cloak slipped from his head. *Dali einei chaza l'dikla* — he lifted his eyes, saw the date palm, and his *yetzer hara* overwhelmed him: *salik paskei l'kibura b'shinei* — he climbed up and bit off a cluster of dates with his teeth, taking fruit that was not his own.

The story is instructive beyond its astrological dimension. The *Gemara* here illustrates not only the metaphysical mechanism of *tzedakah* and prayer overriding destiny, but also the practical power of education and habit in reshaping natural inclination — a theme with obvious resonance beyond any astrological framework.

### Rambam Letter

This *Gemara* is deeply striking: throughout the entire passage, not a single opinion unambiguously rejects astrology. Even those who hold *ein mazal l'Yisrael* do not deny the reality of astrological influence on non-Jews, and even for Jews they acknowledge that it has real pull that must be overcome through *mitzvot* or divine intervention. The *Gemara*'s assumption is consistently that the stars genuinely influence human destiny.

The Rambam's letter on astrology addresses this tension directly. He opens with a sweeping condemnation: anyone who examines the science will see that astrology is complete foolishness, refuted by reason through lucid proofs. From the perspective of tradition, he argues that if everything were truly determined by the stars, the entire system of *Torah*, commandments, and *Talmud* would be rendered meaningless.

Confronting the apparent contradiction posed by our *Gemara*, the Rambam does not simply ignore it. He writes: do not regard it as a difficulty that certain sages in the *Talmud* and *Midrashim* appear to maintain that the stars influence human fate. He then offers several possible explanations: perhaps only one or two sages held this view, not the consensus; perhaps they did not mean it literally and there is an underlying *allusion* in their words; or perhaps they addressed it as they did because that was the prevalent belief of their time and place, and they were speaking in those terms to reach their audience without necessarily affirming the underlying premise.

This approach is characteristic of the Rambam's broader method — seen also in his treatment of *Shmuel*'s dictum about messianic times, where he attributes to a single authority what is in fact a minority view and argues that the consensus lies elsewhere. The Rambam concludes that in matters where reason has already established clear proofs, it is not appropriate to abandon those conclusions based on individual statements of the sages — *she'ein raui la'adam la'azov divrei ha'sekhel*.

The question of whether the Rambam himself believed that God directs the world through stellar mechanisms — a more philosophical position distinct from popular astrology — is a more complex matter left unresolved here.

## Muktzah Debate

### Gourds Issue

The *Gemara* transitions to a new *mishna*: *mekatzin et hadla'at lifnei habehemah* — one may chop up a gourd in front of animals on *Shabbat*. The concern here shifts away from *lisha* and toward *muktzah*. A gourd, in the normal course of things, is set aside for human consumption — it would typically be cooked. The question is whether its designated status for human use renders it *muktzah* and forbidden from being used for animals.

### Muktzah Principle

The *mishna* addresses this by analogy: one may also chop up an animal carcass (*neveilah*) in front of dogs, even if the animal died on *Shabbat* — meaning that when *Shabbat* began, the carcass was not yet fit for dogs and was presumably intended for human consumption or in a status of set-aside.

The governing principle is: *muktzah le'adam lo havei muktzah livhemah* — something that is *muktzah* for a person is not thereby *muktzah* for animals. The *muktzah* status is person-relative; what is set aside from human use on *Shabbat* is not set aside from animal use.

### Defective Animals

Rabbi Yehuda disputes this: *im lo hayta neveilah me'erev Shabbat asura* — if the carcass was not already a *neveilah* (fit for dogs) from before *Shabbat*, it is forbidden on *Shabbat* — *lefi she'eino min hamuchan* — because it was not designated for this use when *Shabbat* began.

The *Gemara* frames this dispute as reflecting the broader debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda regarding *muktzah*. The *Tanna Kamma* of the *mishna* is understood as Rabbi Shimon, who does not hold of *muktzah*; Rabbi Yehuda, who does, disputes.

### Ulla vs. Shmuel

The *Gemara* records: *amar Ulla halacha ke'Rabbi Yehuda* — Ulla rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda. *Ve'Shmuel amar halacha ke'Rabbi Shimon* — Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Shimon. *Ve'af Rava savar lah ke'Rabbi Yehuda* — Rava also holds like Rabbi Yehuda, as evidenced by his ruling regarding covers (*tarps*) placed over boats: Rava ruled them forbidden, Shmuel permitted them, reflecting Rava's broader application of *muktzah* in line with Rabbi Yehuda.

### Levi's Stringency

The *Gemara* adds: *ve'af Levi savar lah ke'Rabbi Yehuda* — Levi also follows Rabbi Yehuda's position. This is demonstrated by the practice that when an animal was slaughtered on *Yom Tov* and brought before Levi for inspection, *lo havi chazei lah* — he would not examine it until he was seated by a dump (*akiklotei*). He would say: perhaps it will turn out to be non-kosher (*treifah*), and if so, *afilu leclavim lo chazi* — it would not even be permitted to feed it to dogs.

The reason is precisely the logic of the *mishna*: the animal was slaughtered on *Yom Tov* thinking it would be fit for human consumption. If it turns out to be *treif*, its only conceivable use is as food for dogs — yet according to Rabbi Yehuda, since it was not designated as dog food when *Yom Tov* began (*she'eino min hamuchan*), it is *muktzah* even for that purpose. It would have to be left at the dump.

Shmuel, by contrast, rules *halacha ke'Rabbi Shimon* — the animal may be fed to dogs regardless.
