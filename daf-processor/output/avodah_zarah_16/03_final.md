# Avodah Zarah 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Economic Reality

### Tosafot's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָזֵיל וּמְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ לְגוֹי! לְגוֹי קָא מְזַבְּנִין, לְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא קָא מְזַבְּנִין?
>
> **Translation:** **But he will go and sell it to a gentile.** Is it the only possibility that **he will sell to a gentile,** and **he will not sell it to a Jew?**

The opening of today's shiur revisits two critical issues that emerged from the previous daf's discussion of the prohibition on selling large animals — *behemah gassah* — to non-Jews. The primary concern underlying that prohibition is *Shabbat*: one must not place one's animal in a situation where it may perform *melacha* on *Shabbat*. Yet Tosafot confronts an uncomfortable reality: the practice of Jewish communities in his time did not conform to this prohibition. And unlike other areas where Tosafot resolves the discrepancy by arguing that medieval Christians are not to be classified as *ovdei Avodah Zarah*, that move is unavailable here. The prohibition on selling animals has nothing to do with *Avodah Zarah*; it concerns the *Shabbat* problem of one's animal performing labor. Tosafot must therefore find a different answer.

### Shabbat Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין מַעֲמִידִין בְּהֵמָה בְּפוּנְדְּקָאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, זְכָרִים אֵצֶל זְכָרִים, וּנְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר נְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל זְכָרִים וּזְכָרִים אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not keep an animal in the inns of gentiles. Male** animals may not be placed **with men,** as they are suspected of engaging in bestiality, **and female** animals may not be left **with women,** despite the fact that there is no concern that they may engage in bestiality. **And needless to say,** it is prohibited to leave **female** animals **with men, and male** animals **with women.**

The Gemara itself had noted that selling through a middleman is permitted, because the animal would no longer recognize its owner's voice and would therefore not come when called on *Shabbat* — thus eliminating the concern that the owner would inadvertently cause his animal to labor. Tosafot, on 15a, extends this reasoning: animals today no longer recognize their owners' voices at all — a kind of *yeridat hadorot* even in the animal kingdom — and therefore the triggering concern simply does not arise. Additionally, Tosafot notes, we do not commonly lend or rent out our animals, which addresses yet another avenue of potential violation.

But Tosafot then presses himself: what of animals that *are* regularly rented to non-Jews? For those, how can we permit even the initial sale, given that the sale may lead to ongoing rental arrangements in which the animal labors on *Shabbat*?

### Takkanah Limits

Here Tosafot offers a remarkable socioeconomic argument. The prohibition, he writes — *davka bi-ymeihem* — was specifically applicable in their time and context. In the era of the *Tannaim*, particularly in the Land of Israel, there were large, dense Jewish communities. If a person had an animal he no longer needed, *she-hayu harbeh Yehudim beyachad*, he could readily sell it to a fellow Jew. He would not suffer a loss, *velo haya mafsid*. But now, Tosafot writes, *mah ya'aseh she-lo yimtzah limkor yafsid* — what is a person to do if there are only a handful of Jews in his town and no Jewish buyer exists? The rabbis never intended their *takkanah* to force people to absorb a total financial loss.

This is a striking piece of Tosafot. He is not pretending that people have technically complied. He is openly acknowledging that the practice departed from the law, and he is retroactively explaining why the rabbis' *takkanah* was never meant to apply in circumstances of genuine economic hardship. He traces this leniency back to the *Geonim* — or at minimum to the earlier authorities — as an established practice, *hilkheta badavar*. One can also sense the unstated subtext: many of these prohibitions were rooted in the realities of the Land of Israel, where Jews were engaged in agriculture and commerce within a large community, and where strengthening Jewish economic presence was a live concern. That entire societal infrastructure was simply absent in the medieval Diaspora, where Jews were often legally barred from owning land and forced into far narrower economic roles.

### Business Restriction

Tosafot then records the position of Rabbeinu Baruch, who draws a critical distinction. Even granting the economic argument, the leniency applies only when a person already owns an animal and finds himself unable to sell it to a fellow Jew — *koneh sus le-tzorech atzmo ve-nimlach alav le-mochro*. In such a case, where he would otherwise absorb a loss, *im lo yimchrenu yafsid*, Rabbeinu Baruch acknowledges the necessity. But one who purchases animals specifically to resell them for profit — *lokeach susim le-mochram kedei le-harvi'ach* — that is forbidden. The prohibition was never meant to be relied upon as the foundation of a business model.

### Davar Ha'avad

This distinction mirrors a well-known principle in *halakha*: *davar ha'avad* — preventing an existing loss — often permits actions that would otherwise be prohibited, but it does not license entering into a new commercial enterprise that depends on the leniency. There is a significant difference between telling someone they need not destroy their own investment and telling someone they may build a business on the premise that the prohibition does not apply to them. As a practical matter, of course, Tosafot is aware that people were in fact conducting regular commerce in animals with non-Jews. But his *Tosafot* is powerful precisely because it honestly acknowledges the economic realities that drove these practices rather than constructing a purely formal legal justification.

## Lifnei Iver

### Lifnei De-Lifnei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ: מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִמְכּוֹר בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה לַכּוּתִים — מוֹכְרִין, שֶׁלֹּא לִמְכּוֹר — אֵין מוֹכְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rabba's opinion from a *baraita*: In **a place where** the people **were accustomed to sell small livestock to Samaritans, one may sell** the animals to them; in a place **where** the people were **not** accustomed **to sell** them **one may not sell** the animals to them.

There is a second issue from the previous daf worth examining. The Gemara had discussed whether one may sell an animal to a *Kuti* — a Samaritan — given that the *Kuti* will in turn sell it to a non-Jew. Tosafot raises a sharp question: we established earlier, on daf 14a, that *lifnei de-lifnei* — two degrees of removal from the transgression — is permitted. Here, even if the Jew sells to the *Kuti* and the *Kuti* then violates the rabbinic prohibition by selling to the non-Jew, the Jew is only one step removed from that rabbinic violation. Why should this be worse than the *lifnei de-lifnei* case discussed earlier? Tosafot formulates the question on the bottom of 15b: *u'mai shna mi-lifnei de-lifnei de-le'eil* — what distinguishes this from the earlier case where two degrees of removal was explicitly permitted?

### Non-Jews Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא לִמְכּוֹר — אֵין מוֹכְרִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲשִׁידִי אַרְבִיעָה, וּמִי חֲשִׁידִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין מַעֲמִידִין בְּהֵמָה בְּפוּנְדְּקָאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, זְכָרִים אֵצֶל זְכָרִים, וּנְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר נְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל זְכָרִים וּזְכָרִים אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** in a place **where** the people were **not** accustomed **to sell** them **one may not sell** the animals to them. **What is the reason** that the sale of small livestock to Samaritans is prohibited? **If we say** that it is **because** Samaritans **are suspected of** engaging in **bestiality, are they suspected** of this practice? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not keep an animal in the inns of gentiles. Male** animals may not be placed **with men,** as they are suspected of engaging in bestiality, **and female** animals may not be left **with women,** despite the fact that there is no concern that they may engage in bestiality. **And needless to say,** it is prohibited to leave **female** animals **with men, and male** animals **with women.**

Tosafot's answer turns on a fundamental asymmetry between the obligations of Jews and non-Jews. In the earlier case, the item — incense intended for use in *Avodah Zarah* — was sold first to a non-Jew. That non-Jew is not commanded in *lifnei iver*. Therefore, when non-Jew number one eventually passes the item to non-Jew number two, the first non-Jew has not violated any prohibition at all. And since my direct transaction was only with non-Jew number one — *she-eino muzhar al lifnei iver* — I have not caused anyone to sin. My connection to the ultimate transgression is simply too attenuated: the person in the middle committed no violation, and I therefore bear no responsibility for what comes next.

### Differential Duty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹסְרִין בְּהֵמָה לָרוֹעֶה שֶׁלָּהֶן, וּמְיַיחֲדִין עִמָּהֶם, וּמוֹסְרִין לָהֶם תִּינוֹק לְלַמְּדוֹ סֵפֶר וּלְלַמְּדוֹ אוּמָּנוּת; אַלְמָא לָא חֲשִׁידִי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **And one may entrust an animal to** a Samaritan **shepherd, and one may seclude oneself with** Samaritans, **and one may entrust a child to them to teach him** how to read **books and to teach him a craft.** The Gemara infers from the *baraita*: **Evidently,** Samaritans are **not suspected** of engaging in bestiality, yet livestock may not be sold to them, as they are suspected of selling it to gentiles.

But when I sell to a Jew who I know will then sell the animal to a non-Jew, a fundamentally different principle applies. Tosafot states it plainly: *anu metzuvim she-lo yavo shum Yisrael lidei takala al yadeinu* — we are obligated to ensure that no Jew comes to sin through our actions. Our responsibility toward a fellow Jew is not merely the negative duty of refraining from directly tripping him; it is an active concern for his *shmirat mitzvot*. This heightened responsibility operates independently of whether the technical definition of *lifnei iver* applies.

### Cheeseburger Case

The principle can be illustrated with a concrete case. The Gemara says that *lifnei iver* only applies in a situation of *trei avri de-nahara* — where the person cannot obtain the forbidden item without your help. If the cheeseburger is sitting right next to Michael and he could pick it up himself, there is no technical *lifnei iver* when you hand it to him. And yet the *Rishonim* are nearly unanimous that you may not do so. They frame the prohibition differently — *lifnei iver de-Rabbanan*, *machshil*, and other formulations — but the operative principle is clear: even absent the technical biblical prohibition, I may not facilitate a fellow Jew's *aveira*. Now consider the parallel case: Christopher asks you to hand him an *ever min ha-chai* that is right next to him. In that case, since it is not *trei avri de-nahara*, and since a non-Jew is not commanded in *lifnei iver*, it may actually be *muttar* to hand it to him.

### Responsibility Scope

The contrast is deliberate and philosophically significant. Toward non-Jews, our responsibility is limited to not actively enabling their transgression. As long as I am not the direct cause — as long as it is not *trei avri de-nahara* — I bear no obligation. Toward Jews, the scope of responsibility is broader: I am actively concerned with their *shmirat mitzvot*, not merely prohibited from directly tripping them. This is why *lifnei de-lifnei* works by non-Jews but not by Jews. By Jews, even if I am technically two degrees removed, the fact that a Jew in the middle will violate a rabbinic prohibition is something that concerns me directly — I am *machshil* that Jew in his transgression of the rabbinic prohibition, and my general responsibility to Jewish observance demands I avoid this. The formal answer Tosafot adds is that since I am selling directly to a Jew who will then violate a rabbinic prohibition, I am in effect *chad lifnei* — one degree of removal, not two — from that Jew's sin, since his transgression is the proximate consequence of my sale.

## Iron Ingots

### Ashisheiot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן עֲשָׁשִׁיּוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל, מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּחָלְשִׁי מִינַּיְיהוּ כְּלֵי זַיִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: One may not sell blocks [*ashashiot*] of iron to** gentiles. **What is the reason?** It is **because they forge weapons from them.**

Turning to daf 16 proper, the Gemara opens with a ruling of Rav Adda bar Ahava: *ein mochrin lahen ashisheiot shel barzel* — one may not sell iron ingots to non-Jews. The reason, *mishum de-chalshina minnaihu klei zayin*, is the concern that they will melt the iron down and forge it into weapons. Raw ingots of iron have no particular function other than being processed into something else, and the presumption is that weapons are the intended product.

### Spades Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ מָרֵי וַחֲצִינֵי נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** then **even hoes and axes** should not be sold to them, as they too can be used to forge weapons.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: if the concern is that they will smelt it into weapons, then surely one should also be prohibited from selling spades and pickaxes, which are also made of iron and could theoretically be melted down and reforged into weaponry. The implicit principle underlying the challenge is that we do not assume someone will destroy a finished, useful implement — a *kli* — simply in order to fashion a weapon. One does not ruin a perfectly good spade. Iron ingots, by contrast, are purpose-built raw material.

### Persian Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: בְּפַרְזְלָא הִינְדּוּאָה. וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּקָא מְזַבְּנִינַן, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לְפָרְסָאֵי דְּמַגְּנוּ עִילָּוַון.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said** in response: The ruling of Rav Adda bar Ahava was stated **with regard to Indian iron,** which is of a superior quality and used only for crafting weapons. The Gemara clarifies: **And** as for the fact **that nowadays we do sell** all weapons, **Rav Ashi said:** We sell the weapons **to the Persians, who protect us.**

The Gemara then introduces a further refinement. Rav Zvid clarifies that the prohibition applies specifically to Indian iron — *parzela Hindua* — which is of particularly high quality and especially well-suited for weapon-forging. And then Rav Ashi adds a crucial contextual observation: *le-Parsai de-magenu alan* — in the current situation, we sell this iron only to Persians, because the Persians protect us. The Babylonian Amoraim, operating under Persian rule rather than Roman, found themselves in a different geopolitical reality. The Persians were, by the standards of the time, a relatively welcoming host nation that took an active interest in the welfare of their Jewish population. If the Persians used this iron to forge weapons and fight wars, that was in a sense advantageous — they were fighting on the side that protected Jewish communities. The same material that in Roman hands would represent a threat here served a protective function.

## Young Animals

### Agnlim Vesichim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲגָלִים וּסְיָיחִים.
>
> **Translation:** One may not sell to gentiles **calves or foals.**

The Gemara now addresses a series of cases that extend the prohibition on selling animals to non-Jews. Young calves — *agalim* — and foals — *siachim* — may not be sold to non-Jews. The Mishnah states this categorically, regardless of whether the young animal is healthy or has a broken limb.

### Shvura

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר בִּשְׁבוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהִתְרַפְּאוֹת וְלִחְיוֹת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא מַרְבִּיעִין עָלֶיהָ וְיוֹלֶדֶת, וְכֵיוָן דְּמַרְבִּיעִין עָלֶיהָ וְיוֹלֶדֶת אָתוּ לְשַׁהוֹיַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda permits** the sale **of a damaged** animal **because it is incapable of being cured and living** normally. The Sages **said to him: But** if **one mates her, does she not bear offspring? And since one can mate her and she** will **bear offspring,** the gentile **will come to leave her** in his possession.

However, a *Braita* records that Rabbi Yehuda goes further and draws a distinction: he permits the sale of an animal with a broken limb — *shvura*. His reasoning is that an animal so injured cannot heal, *eina yechola le-hitrapos le-achiyos*, and will inevitably die. There is therefore no concern that it will be used as a beast of burden; the non-Jew will presumably slaughter it or it will perish. Since the entire prohibition rests on the concern that the animal will perform *melacha* on *Shabbat*, and that concern is eliminated when the animal will never be fit for work, Rabbi Yehuda permits the sale.

### Reproduction Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: לִכְשֶׁתֵּלֵד. אַלְמָא לָא (מְקַבֶּלֶת) [מְקַבְּלָא] זָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda **said to them** in response: **When she bears offspring,** I will agree to be concerned about such a possibility. The Gemara notes: **Apparently,** Rabbi Yehuda holds that a damaged animal **does not accept a male,** i.e., since its legs are broken, it cannot participate in intercourse.

The Rabbis counter with a different concern: *v'lo marbeena lah l'yoledess*? Even a female animal with a broken limb can still conceive and give birth. The non-Jew will not slaughter it immediately; he will keep it alive, mate it with a male, and thereby expand his livestock through her offspring. Rabbi Yehuda's response is *amar lahem lich'she-tailyad* — he will deal with that concern when and if she actually gives birth. His position, as the Gemara explains, is that an animal with a broken limb is in fact not receptive to mating — *lo mikabel ha-zachar* — and therefore the reproduction concern is not a live one in practice.

### Marit Ayin

Rashi frames the Rabbis' concern differently, invoking *marit ayin*: since the female animal will not be slaughtered immediately but will remain in the non-Jew's possession for some period while it might potentially become pregnant, observers will see the animal and assume that the Jew sold it without any broken limb, leading others to conclude that selling animals to non-Jews is generally permitted. This explanation is, on its face, somewhat far-fetched. The prohibition against selling large animals to non-Jews is itself only a rabbinic enactment, and it is itself a safeguard against other concerns — we do not typically worry about *marit ayin* to protect rabbinic safeguards at this degree of remove. The layering — a *marit ayin* concern to protect a rabbinic prohibition that itself exists to prevent another violation — seems extreme, and the Gemara does not appear to treat it as the primary explanation.

## Horses

### Ben Beteira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן בְּתִירָא מַתִּיר בַּסּוּס. תַּנְיָא: בֶּן בְּתִירָא מַתִּיר בַּסּוּס, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה בּוֹ מְלָאכָה שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also teaches that **ben Beteira permits** the sale **of a horse** to a gentile. The Gemara notes that **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Ben Beteira permits** the sale **of a horse because** the gentile **uses it for performing an act for which one is not liable** to bring **a sin-offering,** as riding a horse is not prohibited by Torah law.

The Mishnah records a special ruling of Ben Beteira: he permits the sale of a horse to a non-Jew. A *Braita* explains his reasoning explicitly — *mipnei she-hu oseh bo melacha she-ein chayavin aleha chatas*. A horse is used for riding. Even if the non-Jew rides the horse on *Shabbat*, this does not constitute a biblically prohibited act of *melacha* on the owner's part, because a horse carries its rider and the principle of *chai nosei et atzmo* — a living being supports its own weight — means that the animal is not technically carrying a load in the halakhically prohibited sense. Therefore a horse cannot cause its owner to violate a biblical prohibition on *Shabbat*, and Ben Beteira reasons that the rabbinic concern about large animals does not extend to this case. This *Braita* is notable because it makes the *Shabbat* rationale for the prohibition explicit in the name of Ben Beteira.

### Rabbi's Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אוֹסֵר מִפְּנֵי שְׁנֵי דְבָרִים: אֶחָד מִשּׁוּם תּוֹרַת כְּלֵי זַיִין, וְאֶחָד מִשּׁוּם תּוֹרַת בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **prohibits** its sale **due to two reasons: One** is **because** it has the **status of a weapon,** as horses are used in battle, **and** the other **one** is **because** it has the **status of large livestock.**

Rabbi, however, disagrees and prohibits the sale of a horse for two independent reasons. The first is *mishum toras klei zayin* — because horses are used in warfare and thus fall within the category of weapons. The second is *mishum toras behemah gassa* — because a horse is a large animal and the rabbis prohibited selling large animals to non-Jews as a categorical matter.

### Horse Weapon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא תּוֹרַת כְּלֵי זַיִין אִיכָּא, דְּקָטֵיל בְּסִחוּפֵיהּ, אֶלָּא תּוֹרַת בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה מַאי הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, there is** a reason to say that a horse has the **status of a weapon, as** a horse is taught to **kill by striking down** enemy troops. **But what is** the relevance of the observation that it has the **status of large livestock?**

The Gemara accepts the first reason without much difficulty: certain horses are trained to trample enemies in battle and thus can literally function as instruments of violence — *dekativi be-sichufeih*. What is worth noting here is that the concern seems to be limited to the item itself being a weapon, not merely to its use in a military context. A horse that itself tramples people is a weapon; something that merely supports the war effort more indirectly might not be included in the prohibition.

The second reason — *mishum toras behemah gassa* — is more difficult. If Ben Beteira is correct that horses present no *Shabbat* problem, what does it mean to say that a horse is prohibited because it is a large animal? The category of large animals was itself created because of *Shabbat* concerns.

### Millstone Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לִכְשֶׁיַּזְקִין מַטְחִינוֹ בְּרֵחַיִים בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When it becomes elderly** and is no longer suitable for use in battle, **one makes it grind with a millstone,** and therefore it will in fact be used to perform prohibited labor **on Shabbat.**

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the difficulty: *lich'she-yazkin matachino be-reichayim be-Shabbat* — when the horse grows old and is no longer suitable for riding, its owner will yoke it to turn a millstone, at which point it will be performing genuine *melacha* on *Shabbat*. So while the young horse presents no problem, an aged horse does, and therefore the category of horses is not entirely free of *Shabbat* concerns. One might object that at the time of sale the horse is young and fit for riding — the millstone scenario is a distant possibility. But the answer seems to be that we do not subdivide within a category based on the age or current condition of the animal.

### Lo Plug

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּבֶן בְּתִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Beteira,** and it is permitted to sell a horse to gentiles.

This points to the principle of *lo plug*: the rabbis, when they issue a categorical prohibition, do not typically invite analysis of each individual case within the category. The Mishnah itself reflects this approach — it prohibits large animals categorically, without asking whether this particular animal is old or young, whole or broken-limbed. *Lo plug* means that once a category is defined as prohibited, we do not make internal distinctions within it. The tension with Rabbi Yehuda's broken-limb exception and with Ben Beteira's horse ruling is precisely that both *Tannaim* are willing to make distinctions within the broader category — Ben Beteira because horses are fundamentally a different subcategory vis-à-vis *melacha*, and Rabbi Yehuda because a broken-limbed animal is, practically speaking, entirely outside the category of work-capable animals.

### Ruling

The Gemara ultimately rules: *halacha ke-Ven Beteira* — we follow Ben Beteira. This ruling is significant in its practical implications. Rather than taking a blanket *lo plug* approach that prohibits the entire category of large animals without qualification, the ruling of Ben Beteira directs us to actually examine the particular case and ask whether this specific animal presents a genuine concern. This affords considerably more latitude and is consistent with the earlier Gemara's permission to sell a cow when circumstances indicate it will be slaughtered.

## Shor Patam

### Patam Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שׁוֹר שֶׁל פַּטָּם מַהוּ? תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **an ox of a fattener,** which has been fattened for slaughter, **what is** the *halakha*? **Let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who permits the sale of a damaged animal, and **let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who dispute that ruling.

The Gemara now raises a question about a *shor shel patam* — a fattened ox. Such an animal is raised specifically for slaughter; its entire purpose is to produce meat. Given that the prohibition on selling large animals is rooted in the concern that they will be used for *melacha* on *Shabbat*, and a fattened ox seems categorically destined for the slaughterhouse rather than the field, one might think the prohibition does not apply. The Gemara poses the question as a double-sided inquiry: does Rabbi Yehuda's logic permit the sale of a fattened ox, or does he still prohibit it? And does the reasoning of the Rabbis who rejected Rabbi Yehuda's broken-limb allowance perhaps lead them to permit the fattened ox?

### Baraita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי הָיוּ מַקְרִיבִין שׁוֹר שֶׁל פַּטָּם בְּיוֹם אֵידָם, חָסֵר אַרְבַּע רִיבְבָן שֶׁאֵין מַקְרִיבִין אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם אֶלָּא לְמָחָר, חָסֵר אַרְבַּע רִיבְבָן שֶׁאֵין מַקְרִיבִין אוֹתוֹ חַי אֶלָּא שָׁחוּט, חָסֵר אַרְבַּע רִיבְבָן שֶׁאֵין מַקְרִיבִין אוֹתוֹ כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof: **Come** and **hear that** which **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The members **of** the **household of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **were** required to **bring** as a present for the authorities **an ox of a fattener on their festival day. They deprived** themselves of **forty-thousand** dinars, i.e., they paid this sum as a bribe, to ensure **that they** would **not** have to **bring it** on **the** actual **day** of their festival, but **rather on the next day. They deprived** themselves again, i.e., they paid a further bribe, of another **forty-thousand** dinars, to ensure **that they** would **not** have to **bring it alive** but **rather slaughtered. They deprived** themselves again and paid yet another bribe of **forty-thousand** dinars to ensure **that they** would **not** have to **bring it at all.**

The question might be framed as follows. Rabbi Yehuda permitted the sale of a broken-limbed animal on the grounds that it is permanently unfit for work — *delo chazya limlacha*. A fattened ox, one might argue, is different: leave it long enough, let it lose its excess weight, and it will eventually be fit for agricultural labor. Thus perhaps even Rabbi Yehuda would concede that a fattened ox remains within the prohibition. On the other hand, the Rabbis who rejected Rabbi Yehuda's broken-limb ruling did so on the grounds that the animal with a broken limb will be kept alive for reproduction — *stama lav lishchita kai*. A fattened ox, by contrast, is paradigmatically destined for slaughter — *stama lishchita kai* — and the Rabbis might therefore agree that it is permitted.

### Rabbi's Tribute

At this point the Gemara introduces a remarkable historical datum. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: the household of Rabbi — the *Nasi*, Rabban Yehuda Ha-Nasi — used to send a fattened calf to the Roman authorities on the occasion of their holiday. The Gemara uses the language *hayu makrivin*, which carries connotations of tribute and offering. Rashi is careful to interpret this as a political tribute — *mas ve-doron le-melech* — and explicitly resists the implication that the animal was used as a sacrifice; it would have been extraordinary and shocking if Rabbi had sent an animal that would be offered on the altar of a pagan deity, perhaps even in a temple dedicated to Zeus. The term *makrivin*, then, reflects the status of the act as a tribute from a subject people to their imperial overlords, framed in the language of offering, not literal sacrifice.

### Negotiation 1

What follows is equally significant. Rabbi spent forty thousand coins — an exaggerated figure conveying enormous expense — to arrange that the tribute should be delivered the day *after* the Roman holiday rather than on the holiday itself, so that he would not violate the rabbinic prohibition on conducting commerce with non-Jews on their festival day. The scale of the expenditure reflects both the seriousness with which Rabbi took the rabbinic prohibition and the degree of political pressure under which the Jewish leadership operated.

### Negotiation 2

A second payment of forty thousand coins secured an additional concession: that the animal could be delivered already slaughtered rather than alive. This addressed the concern about handing over a live large animal to non-Jews — whether framed as a prohibition on selling or giving such animals, and perhaps also with an eye toward ensuring the animal would not be used as a live sacrifice.

### Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְשַׁהוֹיֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that they paid a bribe to evade the responsibility of bringing a fattened ox to the authorities? **Is it not due to** the concern that **perhaps they will come to keep** the animal until it is fit for labor?

The Gemara now draws an inference from this narrative. If a *shor shel patam* were straightforwardly permitted — because it is presumptively destined for slaughter and thus presents no *Shabbat* concern — why would Rabbi go to such lengths and expense to maneuver around the prohibition? The fact that he paid so much to have the animal delivered slaughtered suggests that he regarded the live delivery of a fattened ox as problematic, which in turn implies that the prohibition applies to fattened oxen. This would mean the *shor shel patam* is indeed forbidden to give or sell to non-Jews.

### Elimination Goal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַבִּי מִיעְקָר מִילְּתָא בָּעֵי, וְסָבַר: יִעֲקַר וְאָתֵי פּוּרְתָּא פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be explained that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **wanted to abolish the matter** entirely, **and he reasoned:** It is best to **abolish** it **gradually, little** by **little,** and in this manner they ultimately had no obligation to bring the animal at all.

Rav Ashi resolves the difficulty with a different reading of Rabbi's conduct. Rabbi's goal, Rav Ashi explains, was ultimately to eliminate the tribute obligation altogether — *Rabbi me'akar milta ba'ei*. He pursued this in stages — *ye'akar ve-ati pursa pursa* — first getting the delivery moved off the holiday, then having it delivered slaughtered, and finally paying to be rid of the obligation entirely. The expenditure at each stage was not necessarily driven by a specific halakhic concern at that precise juncture; it was a long-term political negotiation aimed at complete extrication. Therefore one cannot draw a precise inference from any single step in that process about the technical status of the *shor shel patam*. The Gemara also notes in passing that this narrative implies gifts, not merely sales or rentals, may also be included within the prohibition — an important extension of the principle. Rav Ashi's further observation, vouched for by Zvida bar Tora — a man whose trade was in fattened animals — is that a *shor shel patam*, if left to lose its excess weight, actually ends up doing the work of two animals. This confirms that the concern about eventual *melacha* is not merely theoretical.

## Bears Lions

### Dangerous Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶם דּוּבִּין וַאֲרָיוֹת וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ נֶזֶק לָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not sell bears, or lions, or any item that can cause injury to the public, to** gentiles.

The Mishnah moves to a distinct category of prohibition: *ein mochrin lahem dubim va-arayot* — one may not sell to non-Jews bears, lions, or anything that poses a hazard to the public — *kol davar she-yesh bo nezek larabbim*. This category differs from the preceding discussion. The concern here is not *Shabbat* labor but public safety: these are dangerous creatures that, if not managed with appropriate care, will cause harm.

### Responsibility

The Gemara's underlying assumption is worth pausing over. The implication of the *Mishnah*'s formulation — *ta'ama de-ika nezek*, the reason is because of the hazard — is that the issue is specifically the non-Jew's handling of the animal. The assumption seems to be that Jews are more conscientious about preventing public harm, while non-Jews cannot be trusted to maintain adequate control over dangerous animals. Whether this reflects a general presumption about relative responsibility, or whether it simply reflects the specific context of the Roman arena — where lions and bears were routinely deployed in gladiatorial spectacles and therefore their acquisition by Roman parties was inherently dangerous — the practical result is the same. One might also note that the *Mishnah* in Bava Kamma treats lions and bears as fundamentally incompatible with private ownership; these are animals one is required to destroy, not keep. That background makes the question of who is selling them in the first place somewhat curious.

### Stadiums

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין בּוֹנִין עִמָּהֶם בָּסִילְקֵי, גַּרְדּוֹם, אִיצְטַדְיָיא, וּבִימָה, אֲבָל בּוֹנִין עִמָּהֶם בִּימוֹסְיָאוֹת וּבֵית מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not build with them a basilica [*basileki*], a tribunal [*gardom*], a stadium [*itztadeyya*], or a platform. But one may build with them small platforms [*bimmusiot*] and bathhouses.**

The Mishnah then lists another set of prohibitions concerning construction. One may not build with non-Jews a *basilikei*, a *gardom*, an *istadya*, or a *bima*. The stadium — *istadya* — is forbidden because it is the venue for gladiatorial combat, where captives, including Jewish captives, were forced to fight and die. Building such a structure makes one complicit in the bloodshed that occurs within it. The *gardom* and *bima* are associated with the Roman judicial and penal system. Rashi understands one as the platform or steps leading up to where the judges sit, and the other as the scaffold or structure used for public executions. The Gemara's explanation draws on the grim reality that Jewish communities frequently suffered at the hands of Roman courts and Roman executioners; any contribution to the infrastructure of Roman justice and Roman punishment is therefore prohibited.

As for the *basilikei* — the large basilica-style building — Rashi suggests this too functioned as a type of courthouse, which would bring it within the same rationale.

### Hatzalat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ לְכִיפָּה שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין בָּהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — אָסוּר לִבְנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Even in this case, once **he reaches the arched chamber** in the bath **where** the gentiles **put up** objects of **idol worship, it is prohibited to build** it.

Despite the prohibition on *building* a stadium, the *Braita* records that one is permitted to attend the stadium for the purpose of *hatzalat nefashos* — saving lives. The mechanism is well known: at a certain point in the gladiatorial contest, the crowd votes whether to spare the defeated combatant's life. A sufficient show of support for mercy could save him. Jewish spectators attending for this purpose — to add their voices to those calling for the combatant's life to be spared — are permitted to do so, notwithstanding all the other concerns that such attendance would ordinarily raise.

### Execution Structures

The *gardom* and *bima*, as noted, relate to Roman structures of capital justice. The lectured makes clear that the context is the Roman persecution of Jews: enough Jews were tried, sentenced, and executed under Roman authority that participation in constructing the spaces where those proceedings and executions took place was intolerable. Whether the specific structure is the judicial bench, the platform, or the scaffold, the prohibition covers the full apparatus of Roman judicial violence against Jewish communities.

### Bathhouses

However, the Mishnah draws a clear line: one *may* build with non-Jews a *bimosiot* and a *beit merchatzaot* — a bathhouse. A bathhouse is, fundamentally, a neutral structure. The difficulty arises at the point where one reaches the specific niche or alcove — *kipa* — in the wall where the idol is installed. From that point onward, construction is forbidden. Until that point, one is simply building a bathhouse.

### Tashmish

Rashi's understanding of *bimosiot* — which he takes to refer to a single-stone altar upon which sacrifices are offered — creates a serious difficulty. He argues that this is *tashmish de-Avodah Zarah* — an implement used in idolatrous service — and that since it is two degrees removed from the actual act of idol worship, it is permitted. Tosafot finds this position untenable. An altar upon which idolatrous sacrifices are actively offered is directly and immediately involved in *Avodah Zarah*; this is not a remote or attenuated connection by any reasonable measure. Even if one were to grant Rashi's technical framing of *tashmish*, the notion that a Jew should be permitted to build an altar for pagan sacrifice because of some formal degree-of-removal calculation is, as Tosafot puts it, simply unacceptable — we would not allow it on any technical ground. Tosafot therefore rejects Rashi's *girsa* and reads *dimosiot* instead of *bimosiot*, understanding it as a type of bathhouse or similar structure, bringing it comfortably within the permitted category of neutral construction.

## Chaya Gassa

### Pirchus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חָנִין בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: חַיָּה גַּסָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה לְפִירְכּוּס, אֲבָל לֹא לִמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥanin bar Rav Ḥisda says, and some say Rav Ḥanan bar Rava says** that **Rav says:** The status of **a large beast is like** that of **small livestock with regard to a spasm [*lefirkus*],** i.e., the symptoms of vitality required at the time of slaughtering. If an animal in danger of dying was slaughtered but did not display any spasmodic movement when it was slaughtered, it is not kosher. If it did spasm after being slaughtered, its meat is kosher **But** its status is **not** the same as that of small livestock **with regard to** its **sale.**

The Gemara now introduces a statement of Rav Chana bar Rav Chisda — and some attribute it to Rav Chanan bar Rava — in the name of Rav, concerning *chaya gassa*, large non-domesticated animals such as deer. The *halakha* of *pirchus* — the tremors required after *shechita* to confirm the animal had viable life-force at the time of slaughter — operates differently for large and small domesticated animals. For a large *behemah*, such as a cow, it is sufficient for the animal to extend a limb; for a small *behemah*, such as a sheep, the animal must extend and then retract the limb. The question is: into which category does a *chaya gassa* fall? Rav Chana bar Rav Chisda rules that a *chaya gassa* is like a small *behemah* with respect to *pirchus* — it must extend and retract, not merely extend.

### Not Forbidden

Having established that ruling regarding *pirchus*, Rav Chana bar Rav Chisda then makes a second, independent claim: when it comes to the laws of selling to non-Jews, a *chaya gassa* is in the category of a small animal — *behemah daka* — not a large one. This matters because the prohibition on selling to non-Jews applies categorically to large domesticated animals but varies by local custom for small ones. Rav Chana's reasoning is implicit but not difficult to supply: what *melacha* does one do with a deer or similar large wild animal? Unlike an ox or donkey, a *chaya gassa* is not yoked to a plow or a millstone. Without a realistic concern about *Shabbat* labor, the prohibition should not apply.

### Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה, כָּךְ אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן חַיָּה גַּסָּה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁמּוֹכְרִין לָהֶן בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה — חַיָּה גַּסָּה אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Just as one may not sell large livestock to** gentiles, **so too one may not sell large beasts to them. And even in a place where** the people were accustomed to **sell small livestock to** gentiles; nevertheless, **one may not sell large beasts to** them.

The Gemara challenges this immediately. A *Braita* — the Tosefta — states explicitly: *keshem she-ein mochrim behemah gassa kach ein mochrim chaya gassa* — just as one does not sell a large domesticated animal, so too one does not sell a large non-domesticated animal. And moreover: *afilu be-makom she-mochrim behemah daka, chaya gassa ein mochrim* — even in a locale where the custom permits selling small animals, a *chaya gassa* may not be sold. The *Braita* is unambiguous. This constitutes a *tiyuvta* — a decisive refutation — of Rav Chana bar Rav Chisda's position.

### Broken Leg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא רָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין אַבָּרַיְיתָא וּמְשַׁנֵּי, תְּנַן: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן דּוּבִּין וַאֲרָיוֹת וְלֹא כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ נֶזֶק לָרַבִּים, טַעְמָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ נֶזֶק, הָא לֵית בֵּיהּ נֶזֶק — מוֹכְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a different version of this discussion. **Ravina raises a contradiction between the mishna** here **and a *baraita* and resolves** the contradiction. **We learned** in the mishna: **One may not sell bears, or lions, or any item that can cause injury to the public, to** gentiles. Ravina analyzes the mishna: **The reason** a beast such as a lion cannot be sold to gentiles is **that it can cause injury to the public,** from which it may be inferred that with regard to another beast, which **does not cause injury to the public, one may sell** it to gentiles.

Ravina attempts to resolve the apparent contradiction between the *Mishnah*'s implication and the *Braita*. The *Mishnah*'s prohibition on selling bears and lions includes the qualifier that the prohibition applies because of *nezek larabbim* — public danger. The implication is that absent the public danger concern, a lion or bear could be sold, which suggests *chaya gassa* is not categorically prohibited. But the *Braita* says it is prohibited. Ravina resolves this: the *Mishnah*'s implication that a non-dangerous *chaya gassa* would be permitted refers specifically to a lion with a broken leg — *ari shavur* — following Rabbi Yehuda's logic that an animal permanently unfit for work is excluded from the prohibition. It is only that narrow case that the *Mishnah*'s language implies is permitted; a healthy *chaya gassa* remains forbidden according to the *Braita*.

### Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּי: בַּאֲרִי שָׁבוּר, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: סְתָם אֲרִי שָׁבוּר הוּא אֵצֶל מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Ravina **resolves** the contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita*: The ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to a damaged lion, in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda. Rav Ashi says** there is a different explanation: **An ordinary lion is** considered **damaged with regard to labor.**

Rav Ashi offers a different resolution with broader implications. A lion, he says, is *stam ari shavur hu etzel melacha* — as a matter of course, a lion is like an animal with a broken leg when it comes to work. Nobody uses a lion for agricultural labor. The reason the *Mishnah* implies that a non-dangerous lion or bear could be sold is not specifically because of a broken limb, but because lions are intrinsically unsuited for *melacha*. The implication for the broader category is significant: the prohibition on *chaya gassa* is not a blanket rule that excludes the entire category regardless of function. Rather, if a specific *chaya gassa* is genuinely incapable of being employed in *melacha*, it would fall outside the prohibition. But if a particular large wild animal *can* be used for work, it remains forbidden. The category is not uniformly excluded; it must be assessed functionally.

### Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: מַאן לֵימָא לַן דַּאֲרִי חַיָּה גַּסָּה הִיא? דִּלְמָא חַיָּה דַּקָּה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman objects to** the inference drawn from the mishna: **Who will tell us that a lion is** considered **a large beast? Perhaps it is** considered **a small beast,** in which case it cannot be inferred that the mishna permits the sale of large beasts.

Rav Nachman interjects with a different sort of challenge: *man de-amar lan de-ari chaya gassa hu* — who says a lion is even classified as a *chaya gassa*? Perhaps a lion is in fact a *chaya daka* — a small wild animal — in which case the entire preceding analysis has been applied to the wrong category. This question is cryptic, and the Gemara does not develop it extensively; it may refer to certain smaller members of the cat family that might be considered *ari*.

### What Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּה גַּסָּה מִיהַת מַאי מְלָאכָה עָבְדָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But in any event, what labor can a large beast perform?**

Having established through multiple analyses that the prohibition on *chaya gassa* remains in force wherever the animal *could* perform *melacha*, the Gemara finally asks the practical question that makes all of this concrete: *mai melacha avdi* — what kind of work would a large wild animal actually do? The answer is what gives the entire preceding discussion its practical bite. If no such animal is ever used for work, the entire functional analysis is academic.

## Arode

### Millstone Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲמַר לִי מָר יְהוּדָה, דְּבֵי מָר יוֹחָנִי טָחֲנִי רֵיחַיִם בַּעֲרוֹדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Mar Yehuda said to me** that **in the house of Mar Yoḥani, they grind the mill with wild asses,** which are considered large beasts.

Abaye provides the answer, citing a teaching he received from Mar Yehuda in the house of Mar Yochnai: *tochni rei-chaim be-arodei* — the *arode* is used to turn a millstone. The *arode* is a wild donkey — a large, powerful animal that, though non-domesticated, is captured and harnessed to grind grain. This proves the point: there exist *chayot gassot* that are in fact employed for *melacha*, and those animals would be prohibited from sale to non-Jews.

### Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב יְהוּדָה, אֲמַר לַן: גְּמִירוּ מִינַּאי הָא מִילְּתָא, דְּמִגַּבְרָא רַבָּה שְׁמִיעַ לִי, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא אִי מֵרַב אִי מִשְּׁמוּאֵל: חַיָּה גַּסָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה לְפִירְכּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: When we were in the** study **hall of Rav Yehuda, he said to us: Learn from me this matter, which I heard from a great man, but I do not know if** I heard it **from Rav or from Shmuel:** The status of **a large beast is like** that of **small livestock with regard to a spasm,** i.e., the symptoms of vitality required at the time of slaughtering.

With the substantive question resolved, the Gemara turns to an extended *sugya* tracing the transmission history of the *pirchus* ruling — the teaching that a *chaya gassa* requires the same post-*shechita* tremors as a small *behemah*. Rav Zeira narrates a chain of events. When he was studying in the academy of Rav Yehuda, Rav Yehuda stood before his students and addressed them with unusual emphasis: *smiru minai ha milta de-migavra raba shmia li* — commit this teaching to memory, for I heard it from a great authority. The teaching was *chaya gassa harei hi ke-behemah daka le-pirchus*. But Rav Yehuda could not recall whether he had received it from Rav or from Shmuel.

### Rav or Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתַאי לְקוּרְקוּנְיָא, אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַיָּה גַּסָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה לְפִירְכּוּס. אָמֵינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אִיתְּמַר. כִּי אֲתַאי לְסוּרָא, אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְרַבָּה בַּר יִרְמְיָה דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: חַיָּה גַּסָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה לְפִירְכּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira continued: **When I came to** the city of **Korkoneya, I found Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi sitting and saying in the name of Shmuel:** The status of **a large beast is like** that of **small livestock with regard to a spasm. I said** to myself: One can **conclude from** here that this **was stated in the name of Shmuel. When I came to Sura, I found Rabba bar Yirmeya sitting and saying in the name of Rav:** The status of **a large beast is like** that of **small livestock with regard to a spasm.**

The uncertainty itself was notable: Rav Yehuda was a direct disciple of both Rav and Shmuel, and the fact that he could not attribute the teaching to one or the other caused him enough discomfort that he flagged it explicitly while transmitting it to his own students. This kind of epistemic honesty — acknowledging uncertainty about the chain of tradition even while insisting on the importance of the ruling itself — is characteristic of the care taken in the transmission of oral Torah.

Rav Zeira then recounts his travels. When he arrived at a certain location — Kurkonya — he found Rav Chiya bar Ashi sitting and teaching the same ruling in the name of Shmuel. Rav Zeira concluded from this that the teaching originated with Shmuel, and he could now report back to Rav Yehuda's academy that the authority was Shmuel. But when Rav Zeira subsequently arrived in Sura, he found Rabba bar Yirmeya transmitting the same teaching in the name of Rav — not Shmuel.

### Both Authorities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיקִית לְהָתָם, אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְרַב אַסִּי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: חַיָּה גַּסָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה לְפִירְכּוּס. אֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: וְלָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר דְּמַאן מָרָא דִּשְׁמַעְתְּתָא רַבָּה בַּר יִרְמְיָה? אָמַר לִי: פַּתְיָא אוּכָּמָא, מִינַּאי וּמִינָּךְ תִּסְתַּיֵּים שְׁמַעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **When I ascended to there,** Eretz Yisrael, **I found Rav Asi sitting and saying** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya says in the name of Rav:** The status of **a large beast is like** that of **small livestock with regard to a spasm. I said to him: And doesn't the Master hold that the Master** who is responsible for dissemination **of** this ***halakha*** is **Rabba bar Yirmeya?** Why don't you attribute the statement to him? Rav Asi **said to me: Black pot [*patya*],** a term of endearment for a scholar who works hard studying Torah: **From me and from you** this ***halakha* may be concluded.** In other words, our two statements should be combined to form one accurate attribution of the *halakha*.

Now Rav Zeira was presented with what appeared to be a discrepancy: one authority traced the teaching to Shmuel, another traced it to Rav. When he reported this to Rav Assi, he asked: should not the proper attribution credit Rabba bar Yirmeya as the transmitter, since he had heard it from him in the name of Rav? Rav Assi responded with what became a well-known phrase: *minai u-minach tistayem shmata* — from me and from you together we will resolve this teaching. The resolution was that the teaching was transmitted through *both* channels: Rabba bar Yirmeya reported it in the name of Rav Chama bar Guria in the name of Rav, and this formed one chain. The other chain went through different transmitters to Shmuel. Both are valid attributions because the teaching genuinely came from both Rav and Shmuel.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי: אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, אָמַר רַב אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר יִרְמְיָה, אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא, אָמַר רַב: חַיָּה גַּסָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה לְפִירְכּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that in fact this ruling **was also stated: Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav Asi says** that **Rabba bar Yirmeya says** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya says** that **Rav says:** The status of **a large beast is like** that of **small livestock with regard to a spasm.**

The final formulation records the full chain of transmission: Rav Zeira, in the name of Rav Assi, in the name of Rabba bar Yirmeya, in the name of Rav Chama bar Guria, in the name of Rav — *chaya gassa harei hi ke-behemah daka le-pirchus*. A large wild animal requires the same post-*shechita* tremors as a small domesticated animal. This is the established ruling, and the careful reconstruction of its transmission history — tracing it through multiple independent chains back to Rav, and through a parallel chain to Shmuel — serves to confirm its authority and reliability. The practical upshot: if one slaughters a deer or similar *chaya gassa*, it must extend and retract its limb after *shechita* for the slaughter to be deemed *kosher*, just as a sheep must.
