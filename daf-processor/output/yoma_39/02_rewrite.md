# Yoma 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Context & Daily Order

### Daf 39 Setup

Daf 39 opens a new *perek*, and it is worth pausing to situate ourselves within the broader structure of the tractate. The *mishnayot* of Yoma have been doing a great deal of pulling in and out — offering framework about what happens during the rest of the year alongside the specific *avodot* of Yom Kippur. We discussed the lots drawn to distribute the various daily *avodot*, the *Kohen Gadol* going to the *mikvah*, the broader rule that anyone entering the *Beit HaMikdash* immersed, and then returned to the drawing of the lots and how they were specially fashioned. The *mishnah* states that the lots were originally made of *eshkora* wood, and then notes that *Ben Gamla asah shel zahav* — Ben Gamla had them made from gold — which led to a wider discussion about individuals who contributed special things to the *Beit HaMikdash* and those who withheld their talents from contributing in a more expansive way. That entire discussion was a digression. Now we are genuinely entering the stride of the *avodah* itself, and the pull-backs to the wider world of the year will diminish significantly as we move forward. The new *perek* opens with the words *tarof b'kalfi*.

### Tamid Service Review

Before arriving at the lot-drawing, it is helpful to recall the sequence of what has already taken place in the morning. The *Kohen Gadol* first performs *shechitah* on the *tamid* and catches its blood. He then enters and performs *zerikah* of the blood on the *mizbe'ach*. After that, he goes in to tend to the *menorah*, burn the *ketoret*, and complete the work of the *menorah*, and then, upon exiting, he places the *evarim* of the *tamid* on the *mizbe'ach*. This entire opening sequence — the *tamid*, the *ketoret*, the *menorah*, and the placing of the *evarim* on the *kevesh* — is performed while wearing the *bigdei zahav*. That constitutes the first stage of what will be five changes of clothing — three in *bigdei zahav* and two in *bigdei lavan*.

### Clothing Changes

Having completed stage one in *bigdei zahav*, the *Kohen Gadol* transitions into *bigdei lavan* for stage two. In this second stage, wearing the white garments, he stands before his *par* — whose head is oriented toward the west while it faces south — and performs the *viduy*. This *viduy* is among the first acts performed in *bigdei lavan*.

### Location Setup

Having completed the *viduy* over his *par*, the *Kohen Gadol* now moves to the location for the lot-drawing. The *mishnah* states: *ba lo l'mizrach azara l'tzfon ha'mizbe'ach* — he proceeds to the eastern section of the *azara*, to the north of the *mizbe'ach*. It is here, still in *bigdei lavan*, that he will draw lots over the two *se'irim* and proceed with the lottery.

## Lot Drawing Process

### Tarof B'Kalfi

The *perek* opens with the procedure itself. *Tarof b'kalfi* — he mixes around within the box that holds the lots. *V'he'ela shenei goralot* — he draws out two lots. One is inscribed *lashem*, for God, and one *la'azazel*, for Azazel. The act of mixing and drawing is not merely procedural — as we will see, the *Gemara* will probe why mixing is required at all and what the properties of the box must be.

### Sgan & Rosh Beit Av

Two figures stand alongside the *Kohen Gadol* during this ceremony: *hasgan mimino* — the *sgan*, the assistant *Kohen Gadol*, on his right — and *v'rosh beit av mismolo* — the head of the *beit av*, the priestly family whose day Yom Kippur falls on, on his left. The *sgan* functions essentially as the vice *Kohen Gadol*. His primary role is readiness — should the *Kohen Gadol* become disqualified, the *sgan* steps in to serve in his place. Beyond that, the *sgan*'s regular function carries little independent weight, which will become significant later. The *rosh beit av*, by contrast, has significance only on this one day, corresponding to his family's weekly *mishmar* divided by days.

### Hand Signaling

After the lots are drawn, the side on which the lot inscribed *lashem* appears determines the subsequent ceremony. *Im ala shem al biyamino* — if the lot bearing God's name came into the *Kohen Gadol*'s right hand, which was the auspicious *siman*, the *sgan* standing on his right would say: *ishi Kohen Gadol, hagbe yemincha* — Mr. *Kohen Gadol*, raise up your right hand. *V'im ala shem al bismolo* — if it came into his left hand, the *rosh beit av* on his left would say: *ishi Kohen Gadol, hagbe smolcha* — Mr. *Kohen Gadol*, raise up your left hand. In this way, the lot bearing God's name is elevated publicly, making clear which goat it designates.

### Placement on Goats

Following the signal, the *Kohen Gadol* places the lots onto the two goats. This is grounded in the *pasuk*: *v'natan al shenei hase'irim goralot* — literally placing the lots upon them. The goats are already positioned before him in alignment with the hands in which the lots rest, so that each lot naturally rests opposite the goat it designates.

### L'Hashem Chatat

After placing the lots, the *Kohen Gadol* pronounces: *lashem chatat* — for God, a sin-offering. He does not explicitly designate the other as *la'azazel*; that is inferred. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that he need not say *chatat* at all, but only *lashem*, because the language of the *pasuk* itself is *goral echad lashem v'goral echad la'azazel*, and further *asher ala alav hagoral lashem v'asahu chatat* — but the primary designation is *lashem*, which stands in contrast to *la'azazel*, indicating the direction of the offering rather than merely its category. Upon hearing God's explicit name, the people respond: *baruch shem kevod malchuto l'olam va'ed*.

## Theology of YK

### Two Goats Theme

It is worth pausing over the theological landscape that this ceremony inhabits, because it differs substantially from the way we tend to think about Yom Kippur today. If one reads the *pesukim* in the Torah carefully, the dominant theme surrounding the *Kohen Gadol*'s *par* and the *seir l'Hashem* is not personal atonement — it is the cleansing of the *mikdash* itself. The Torah articulates this directly: *v'chiper al hakodesh mitumot benei Yisrael umipisheihem l'chol chatatam* — he atones for the sanctum from the impurities of the Children of Israel and from their transgressions, all their sins. The concept of *chatat* in this context is better understood not as "sin-offering" in the narrow sense, but as a cleansing agent, much as the same term describes the offering brought by a *yoledet* or a *zav* at the end of their purification process.

The underlying metaphysical picture is this: sin, in the Torah's conception, does not merely injure the sinner. It dirties up the *mikdash* in a metaphysical sense — it creates, so to speak, stains upon the *mikdash*. Yom Kippur is therefore the once-a-year cleaning crew. The *Kohen Gadol* enters and removes all the accumulated metaphysical grime that has sullied the sacred space over the preceding year. The significance of this is profound: *v'shachanti betocham*, God dwells among the people, *hashochen itam betoch tumatam* — God is willing to tolerate the *tumah* to a degree and continue to dwell in our midst. But that *tumah* must be cleansed annually for God's presence to remain. The focus, then, is national — the continued indwelling of the divine presence within the nation — rather than the individual's personal atonement.

The *seir la'azazel*, by contrast, is the vehicle for addressing individual sin: *v'nasa alav et kol avonotam el eretz gezeira* — it bears upon itself all their iniquities to a desolate land. The personal dimension of Yom Kippur, the one most prominent in our religious consciousness, is concentrated in the scapegoat.

### Blood Detergent

One scholar's formulation captures this beautifully: the blood is the metaphysical agent, the detergent. The blood, as the seat of life, is the medium through which *tumah* is removed. This understanding reinforces the point that the sacrificial blood rites of the *Kohen Gadol* on Yom Kippur are fundamentally about cleansing the sacred space, not about individual absolution in the first instance.

### L'Hashem Focus

This is why the emphasis throughout the *mishnah* and the broader ritual falls so heavily on the *seir l'Hashem* — the one designated *lashem chatat*. The right hand is preferred for it, the announcement of *lashem chatat* belongs to it, the *la'azazel* goat is not even explicitly named in the proclamation. It may be honest to acknowledge, at least personally, that in popular consciousness the attention often gravitates toward the *seir la'azazel* — the dramatic image of the goat being sent off the cliff, the red string turning white as confirmation of atonement. But the Torah's own emphasis and the *mishnah*'s structure both place the weight on the *seir l'Hashem* and the *par hachatat*, the instruments of the *mikdash*'s purification.

### Lots Problem

This raises a genuinely puzzling philosophical question. If Yom Kippur is about owning responsibility, about not blaming forces beyond one's control, about the exercise of freedom and choice — what is the significance of drawing lots, an act that appears to introduce randomness into the most consequential ceremony of the year? This question connects to an earlier theme in the tractate: lots are used daily to distribute the *avodot* among the *kohanim*. The centrality of the *goral* in both contexts deserves reflection. One might argue, as has been suggested, that what appears to be chance is in fact *siyata dishmaya* — divine guidance — just as in the Book of Jonah the falling of the lot is understood not as arbitrary luck but as the revelation of divine providence. But exactly how that meaning translates into the theological substance of the Yom Kippur *avodah* remains an open question, one worth pursuing further.

## Lot Debate

### Why Shake Box

The *Gemara* asks: *lama itraf b'kalfi?* Why is it necessary to mix up the lots in the box? Why not simply reach in and draw one? The answer is: *ki heichi d'lo nechavein v'lishkol* — so that the *Kohen Gadol* should not intentionally try to draw the lot inscribed *lashem* into his right hand by feeling for it.

### Box Properties

*Amar Rava*: the *kalfi* was made of wood and was not sanctified — *shel etz hayeta v'shel chol hayeta*. Note the contrast: the lots themselves, as the earlier *mishnah* established, were made of gold by Ben Gamla. The box, however, was neither gold nor silver nor sanctified. Furthermore, *v'eina machazeket ela shtei yadayim* — it was only large enough to fit two hands. Ravina probes these properties. He understands the two-hand constraint: *ki heichi d'lo nechavein v'lishkol* — a tight space prevents the *Kohen Gadol* from maneuvering to feel the inscription on a lot before grasping it. (It also anticipates the other opinion, discussed below, in which both the *sgan* and the *Kohen Gadol* insert their right hands simultaneously — which would require room for exactly two hands.)

### Unsanctified Box

But why was the box not sanctified? *Nikadshe* — let it be sanctified! The *Gemara* answers: *im ken havei lei kli sharet* — if it were sanctified, it would become a *kli sharet*. And *kli sharet d'etz lo avidnan* — we do not make *klei sharet* from wood. Fine, then make it from gold or silver and sanctify it. The *Gemara* responds: *HaTorah chasa al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah has concern for the money of Israel; it does not wish us to expend unnecessary funds. This principle is derived from the case of the *metzora*: the Torah requires the *metzora* to clear out his house before the *kohen* inspects it for the plague, even though, were the vessels found impure, most could simply be taken to the *mikvah*. The rabbis explain that this is done to protect the *metzora*'s pottery vessels, which cannot be purified once declared *tamei*. The inference is that the Torah goes to extraordinary lengths to protect even small amounts of money, even for someone who may well have deserved his condition.

This answer is admittedly puzzling. The *Beit HaMikdash* was furnished throughout with gold and silver. The *Kohen Gadol*'s white garments alone cost twelve or eighteen *maneh*. To invoke financial concern as the reason for one small wooden box — when the lots themselves are gold — seems incongruous. One possible resolution, noted during the discussion, is the distinction between private donations and public funds: the lots of Ben Gamla were made from his personal wealth, whereas the box was maintained from the public treasury, and the Torah is particularly attentive to communal funds. Whether that distinction fully resolves the tension may be debated.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

The *Gemara* now introduces a *tannaitic* dispute. *Matnitin d'lo k'hai tanna* — our *mishnah* does not accord with the following *tanna*. In a *braisa*, *Rabbi Yehuda* says in the name of *Rabbi Eliezer*: *hasgan v'Kohen Gadol machnisin yadeihen b'kalfi* — both the *sgan* and the *Kohen Gadol* insert their hands into the box. According to this view, it is not the *Kohen Gadol* alone drawing one lot in his right and one in his left; rather, each man inserts his right hand and draws one lot apiece. The lot that emerges in the *Kohen Gadol*'s right hand is still the more significant, but the *sgan* actively participates in the drawing itself.

The procedure under this view unfolds as follows: if the lot inscribed *lashem* comes up in the right hand of the *Kohen Gadol*, the *sgan* says *ishi Kohen Gadol, hagbei yemincha* — raise your right hand. If, however, it comes up in the right hand of the *sgan*, then the *rosh beit av* speaks — not to the *sgan*, but to the *Kohen Gadol*: *dvar milcha* — say your words. The *Kohen Gadol* then pronounces *lashem chatat* over the animal positioned opposite the *sgan*'s hand, and he retains the role of designation even when the drawing itself was the *sgan*'s.

### Non-Avodah Role

This arrangement raises a significant question that requires attention before the *Gemara*'s own resolution. According to the first version in our *mishnah*, the *Kohen Gadol* holds one lot in his right and one in his left. But *avodah* performed with the left hand is *pasul*. Additionally, on Yom Kippur all *avodot* must be performed by the *Kohen Gadol* himself — yet Rabbi Eliezer's version has the *sgan* performing part of the act. How can either version work?

Rashi addresses this, noting in what may be a later gloss that the drawing of the lots must be understood as not constituting an *avodah* at all — it is what might be termed a *hechsher avodah*, a preparatory act that enables the *avodah* proper but does not itself carry the formal status of *avodah*. This explains why it can be performed with the *Kohen Gadol*'s left hand or with partial participation from the *sgan* without formal disqualification. Yet this framing creates its own conceptual tension: if the act lacks *avodah* status, can it still be *meakev* — can its omission invalidate the service? And conversely, does labeling something a *hechsher avodah* necessarily remove it from the category of the indispensable? The discussion of *shechitah*, for instance, which is *ksheirah b'zar* — valid even when performed by a non-*kohen* — raises the same question: is it an *avodah* or not? What we label an *avodah* is itself a genuine and unresolved question that threads through the subsequent *halakhic* analysis.

Similarly, the *kalfi* itself presents an interesting structural puzzle: it was used exclusively in the context of the *Beit HaMikdash*, yet because it was not formally sanctified it did not attain the status of *kli sharet*. Function and formal identity do not necessarily coincide.

### Hand Debate

The *Gemara* identifies the underlying point of contention between the *mishnah* and Rabbi Eliezer's view: *b'mai kamiphligi?* What is the disagreement about? *Mar savar yemina d'sgan adif mismala d'Kohen Gadol* — one position holds that the right hand of the *sgan* is preferable to the left hand of the *Kohen Gadol*, presumably from the perspective of how the act should be performed, even if it is not technically an *avodah*. *U'mar savar ki hadadi ninhu* — the other position holds that the two options are roughly equivalent, and once they are equivalent there is no reason to involve the *sgan* at all; the *Kohen Gadol* alone should perform the act.

### Rabbi Hanania

The *Gemara* then asks: *u'man hai tanna d'palig alei d'Rabbi Yehuda?* — who is the *tanna* behind our *mishnah*, the one who says the *Kohen Gadol* uses both hands? The answer is striking: *Rabbi Hanania Sgan HaKohanim hu* — it is Rabbi Hanania himself, who served as the *sgan*. And as the *braisa* records, Rabbi Hanania explains the *sgan*'s position on the right in purely functional terms: *she'im i'eir bo psul b'Kohen Gadol nichnas sgan u'meshammesh tachtav* — in case a disqualifying event befalls the *Kohen Gadol*, the *sgan* is ready to step in and serve in his place. The *Gemara* infers from this formulation that according to Rabbi Hanania, this readiness is the *sgan*'s sole function during the lot-drawing; had the *sgan* been actively drawing a lot, Rabbi Hanania would not have described his role exclusively in those terms. It is, as observed, quite remarkable that it is the *sgan* himself who most restricts the *sgan*'s role.

## Shimon HaTzaddik

### YK Signs

The *Gemara* now turns to a celebrated *aggadic* passage. *Tanu Rabbanan*: during the forty years that Shimon HaTzaddik served as *Kohen Gadol*, the lot inscribed *lashem* would always come up in his right hand — *arba'im shana she-shimmesh Shimon HaTzaddik haya goral oleh b'yamin*. The red string, *lashon shel zehorit*, would always turn white. The western light of the *menorah*, *ner ma'aravi*, remained perpetually burning. The fire on the *mizbe'ach* burned powerfully without constant feeding. And there was a *bracha* in the bread — the *omer*, the *shtei halechem*, and the *lechem hapanim* — such that a *kohen* who received even a *kezayit* would be fully satisfied, or would even have food to spare.

### Decline Begins

After the time of Shimon HaTzaddik, all of these signs began to waver. The lot was no longer always in the right hand — *p'amim oleh b'yamin p'amim oleh b'smol* — sometimes right, sometimes left. The red string no longer always turned white — *p'amim malbin p'amim eino malbin* — sometimes it would whiten and sometimes it would not.

### Further Signs

Simultaneously, the other signs of divine favor also became unreliable. The *ner ma'aravi* — *p'amim dolek p'amim kaveh* — sometimes continued burning beyond the other lights, sometimes was extinguished before them. The fire on the *mizbe'ach* — *p'amim mitgaber p'amim ein mitgaber* — sometimes burned powerfully on its own, sometimes the *kohanim* had to feed it continuously throughout the entire day. And instead of blessing in the bread, a curse was sent: *nishtalecha me'eira ba'omer uvishtei halechem uvlechem hapanim*. Each *kohen* now received not even a *kezayit* but the equivalent of a lentil — *kefol* — which the Maharsha notes is significant, because a *kezayit* is the minimum amount upon which *birkat hamazon* is recited and is the threshold for *sevi'ah*. Below that threshold, neither the capacity for satisfaction nor the fitness for *bracha* is present. This is not merely the withdrawal of miraculous blessing but the positive infusion of a curse — a qualitatively different and more alarming deterioration.

The reaction among the *kohanim* was telling: *hatznu'in moshchin et yedeihen* — the more modest ones withdrew their hands, unwilling to take even their portion, while *hagarganim notlin v'ochlin* — the voracious ones grabbed and ate. One individual became notorious for seizing not only his own portion but his colleague's as well, and was called *ben hamtzan* — son of the oppressor or thief.

A brief *Tosafot* on 39a addresses this: *Tosafot* notes that all the *garganim* were grabbing aggressively, so why was this particular individual singled out for the epithet? The answer: he did not merely grab from the communal distribution — he took directly off another person's plate. This crossed a qualitatively different line.

One further observation: one might think the most appropriate response would be simply to take one's allotted portion and eat it as a *mitzvah*, not to withdraw. While taking from the *lechem hapanim* may not constitute an individual *mitzvah* for a *kohen* in the precise sense, the *Gemara* seems to view the behavior of the modest ones as the admirable response to an impossible situation — though this is a nuanced point.

*Rabbah bar bar Shila* and *Rava* both offer *pesukim* grounding the negative sense of *chamtzan*: *Elokei faltzeiniy miyad rasha, mikaf me'avel v'chometz* — and *limdu heitev dirshu mishpat, ashru chamotz* — where *chamotz* clearly designates someone who has done violence or wrong to another.

### Shimon Death

The *Gemara* then records an *aggadic* narrative of striking power. *Tanu Rabbanan*: in the year that Shimon HaTzaddik died, he told those around him: *b'shana zo hu meit* — this year, he will die, referring to himself in the third person. When asked how he knew, he replied: *b'chol Yom HaKippurim haya mizdamen li zaken echad lavush levanim v'atuf levanim, nichnas imi v'yatza imi* — every Yom Kippur an elder figure would appear to him in the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, clothed in white and wrapped in white, entering with him and leaving with him. *V'hayom nizdamen li zaken echad lavush shechorim v'atuf shechorim, nichnas imi v'lo yatza imi* — but this time, the elder appeared clothed in black and wrapped in black, entered with him and did not leave with him. He understood this as an unmistakable sign of his impending death. After *Sukkot*, he fell ill for seven days and died. The *Yerushalmi* addresses the obvious difficulty — the Torah states *v'chol adam lo yihyeh b'ohel mo'ed b'vo'o el hakodesh* — and answers that this figure was not an *adam* but a *malach*.

### Divine Name

After Shimon HaTzaddik's death, *v'nimneu echav hakohanim levarech bashem* — his fellow *kohanim* refrained from blessing with the explicit divine name. Rashi explains that during the *Beit HaMikdash* era, blessings — including *birkat kohanim* — were ordinarily recited with the *shem hameforash*. After Shimon HaTzaddik, the *kohanim* considered themselves unworthy of this practice and discontinued it.

An important nuance emerges here: the subject is *echav hakohanim* — his fellow priests in the plural — not merely subsequent *Kohanim Gedolim*. This implies that his presence had elevated the entire generation to a level of worthiness, such that even ordinary *kohanim* were fit to pronounce the *shem hameforash* in blessing. With his death, that elevation was lost for all. *Tosafot* in *Sotah*, as cited, links this to the *Sifrei*'s principle that God's name may be proclaimed only where the divine presence is revealed — *bechol makom asher ani nigla sham tazkir shemi*. The *Ri* explains: the *kohanim* refrained *lefi she-lo zachu shuv l'giluy Shechina* — because they no longer merited the full revelation of the *Shechina* that the pronouncing of the divine name required. This reading harmonizes the cessation of the name with the broader pattern of the *Shechina*'s gradual withdrawal.

### Final Forty Years

A further and starker deterioration is recorded. *Tanu Rabbanan: arba'im shana kodem churban ha-Bayit* — for the forty years preceding the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, not a single positive sign remained. The *goral* no longer ever came up in the right hand; it was always the left. The red string never turned white. The *ner ma'aravi* never outlasted the other lights. And — a new and ominous sign — *v'hayu daltos Heichal niftachos me'aleihen* — the doors of the *Heichal* would open by themselves, as though welcoming the enemy in.

It was Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai who rebuked the doors directly: *Heichal Heichal, mipnei mah attah mave'is et atzmecha?* — why do you trouble yourself with these signals? *Yode'a ani b'cha she-sofcha atid l'ichorev* — I know you are destined to be destroyed. *U'chevar nitnaba alecha Zecharia ben Iddo* — and Zecharia ben Iddo has already prophesied this: *p'tach Levanon dlatecha v'tochal esh b'arozecha* — open your doors, O Lebanon, and let the fire consume your cedars.

### Lebanon Midrash

The *Gemara* appends a brief but beautiful *midrashic* coda. *Amar Rabbi Yitzchak ben Tablai*: why is the *Beit HaMikdash* called *Levanon*? *Shemalbin avonoteihem shel Yisrael* — because it whitens the sins of Israel, parallel to the red string that turned white. *Amar Rav Zutra bar Tuvia*: why is it called a forest — as in *Beit Yaar HaLevanon*? *Mah yaar melavlev, af Beit HaMikdash melavlev* — just as a forest blossoms, so too the *Beit HaMikdash* blossomed. For when Solomon built the *Beit HaMikdash*, he planted within it trees of gold of every kind, and they would produce fruit in their seasons. When the wind blew through them, the fruit would fall, providing sustenance for the *kohanim*. When the enemies finally entered the *Heichal*, the golden trees withered and dried — *u'ferach Levanon umilal* — the blossom of the Lebanon became bereaved. But the passage closes with consolation: *v'atid HaKadosh Baruch Hu l'hachzira lanu* — the Holy One will yet restore it to us, as the verse promises: *paroch tifrach v'tagel af gilat v'ranen kevod haLevanon nutan lah* — it shall blossom and rejoice, for the glory of the Lebanon shall be given to it.

## Sensory Reach

### Ten Utterances

Returning to the *halakhic* material, the *Gemara* records: *eser pe'amim mazkir Kohen Gadol et HaShem bo bayom* — ten times the *Kohen Gadol* pronounces God's explicit name on Yom Kippur. Three in the first *viduy* over his *par*: the *pasuk* language includes *Ana HaShem chatati*, *Ana HaShem kaper na*, and *lifnei HaShem titharu* — each containing the explicit name. Three again in the second *viduy* recited on behalf of the *kohanim*. Three in the *viduy* recited over the *seir hamishtalei'ach*, the goat sent off. And one at the lot-drawing, when he declares *lashem* or *lashem chatat*. That totals ten.

### Voice Range

When the *Kohen Gadol* pronounced the explicit divine name, *v'nishma kolo b'Yiricho* — his voice could be heard all the way in Jericho. *Amar Rabba bar bar Chana*: the distance from Jerusalem to Jericho is ten *parsaot* — a *parsa* being four *mil*, each *mil* eight thousand *amot*, making the distance roughly twenty miles.

### Ketoret Smell

The auditory marvels were matched by olfactory ones. *V'tzir daltos haHeichal nishma b'shmona techumei Shabbat* — the creaking of the hinges of the *Heichal* doors when opened could be heard within eight *techumei Shabbat*, that is, sixteen thousand *amot*. *Izim sheb'Yiricho hayu mitatshot mereiach haktoret* — the goats in Jericho would sneeze from the scent of the *ketoret* reaching all that distance. *Nashim sheb'Yiricho einan tzerichin l'hitbasem mereiach ketoret* — the women of Jericho had no need for perfume, as the fragrance of the *ketoret* was sufficient. As for the women of Jerusalem, only the *kalot* — the brides — needed additional perfume beyond what the *ketoret* provided, because a *kala* requires substantially more fragrance than an ordinary woman. This observation is attributed to the Maharsha as well. *Tosafot* raises the question of whether benefiting passively from the *ketoret*'s scent might be prohibited, and answers that the prohibition applies only to an active seeking-out of the smell — *shelo yikrevu atzmam lizeh kedei lehariach yoter* — not to passive benefit.

### Ketoret Stories

Two *aggadic* accounts round out this section. *Rabbi Yosi ben Dugla* reports that his father kept goats in the mountains of Michmar, and those goats would sneeze from the scent of the *ketoret*. *Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* said in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha*: an elder once told him that he had visited Shiloh — the site of the ancient *Mishkan* — and could still smell the *ketoret* emanating from between the cracks in the walls, a fragrance lingering long after the *Mishkan* had ceased to stand.

## Hagralah Meakev?

### Rabbi Yannai

The *Gemara* now turns to the central *halakhic* question of the lot-drawing's indispensability. *Amar Rabbi Yannai: aliyat goral mitoch kalfi meakevet* — drawing the lot from the box is *meakev*; if it is not performed, the subsequent designation of the animals as *lashem* and *la'azazel* is invalid and one cannot proceed. *Hanacha eina meakevet* — but the physical placement of the lots on top of the animals, which the *pasuk* prescribes, does not prevent *yetziat* the obligation if omitted.

### Rabbi Yochanan

*V'Rabbi Yochanan amar: af aliya eina meakevet* — Rabbi Yochanan goes further: even the drawing of the lot from the box is not *meakev*. If the *Kohen Gadol* simply assigned the roles arbitrarily — declaring this one *lashem* and that one *la'azazel* without performing the lot-drawing — the service would be valid *b'di'avad*.

### Order Debate

The *Gemara* situates this debate within the broader *tannaitic* dispute between *Rabbi Yehudah* and *Rabbi Nechemia* regarding the order of the Yom Kippur *avodot* performed outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim*. Note that in this context *bachutz* — "outside" — refers specifically to anything outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim* itself, encompassing the *Heichal* and the larger *azarah*. Rabbi Yehudah holds that if the prescribed order of acts performed in *bigdei lavan* outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim* is altered, *b'di'avad* the service remains valid. Rabbi Nechemia holds that even outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, order is *meakev* and a deviation invalidates. The *Gemara* assumes that this debate about the consequence of disordering acts may translate directly into whether performing the act at all is *meakev*.

### Hagralah Status

Two possible readings of this correlation emerge. The first reading: according to Rabbi Yehudah, who is more lenient about acts outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, everyone would agree that the *hagralah* is not *meakev* — it is performed in *bigdei lavan* outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, so Rabbi Yehudah would certainly not require it strictly. The debate between Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yochanan thus operates only within the framework of Rabbi Nechemia's stricter view. According to Rabbi Nechemia, the one who says the *hagralah* is *meakev* accords with his position, while the one who says it is not *meakev* even according to Rabbi Nechemia reasons that *hagralah lav avodah hi* — the lot-drawing is not a formal *avodah*; it is a *hechsher avodah*, and Rabbi Nechemia's strictures apply only to acts that constitute genuine *avodah*.

The second reading inverts the analysis: according to Rabbi Nechemia, who is strict even outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, everyone would agree that the *hagralah* is *meakev* — even this non-*avodah* act, when prescribed by the Torah, must be performed. The debate between Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yochanan then takes place within the framework of Rabbi Yehudah's more lenient view. According to Rabbi Yehudah, who normally gives latitude regarding acts outside the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, the one who says the *hagralah* is not *meakev* accords straightforwardly with his position. But the one who says it is *meakev* even according to Rabbi Yehudah argues: *shani hacha d'tana bei k'ra* — here it is different, because the Torah emphasizes this act repeatedly. The *pasuk* uses the phrase *asher ala alav hagoral* — and indeed repeats the language of *aliya* — implying that this act of the lot coming up must occur and cannot simply be bypassed.

The result is a genuinely unresolved tension: on one hand, the *hagralah* is not a formal *avodah* — it can be performed with the left hand, it can involve the *sgan*, suggesting a more lenient *halakhic* status. On the other hand, the Torah's explicit and repeated formulation points to the lot-drawing as something the Torah itself insists upon. Whether that tension resolves toward leniency or stringency depends on which *tanna*'s framework one adopts and which reading of their debate one follows.
