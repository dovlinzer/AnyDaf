# Gittin 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Value vs. Transfer

### Gold Plate Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: כָּתַב לָהּ גֵּט עַל טַס שֶׁל זָהָב, וְאָמַר לָהּ: "הִתְקַבְּלִי גִּיטִּיךְ וְהִתְקַבְּלִי כְּתוּבָּתִךְ", מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִתְקַבְּלָה גִּיטָּהּ וְנִתְקַבְּלָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman:** In the case of a husband who **wrote for her a bill of divorce on a plate [*tas*] of gold and said to her: Receive your bill of divorce and** also **receive** the payment of **your marriage contract** through this act, as the piece of gold is worth enough to pay for the value of her marriage contract, **what is** the *halakha*? Rav Naḥman **said to him: Her bill of divorce has been received and** the payment of **her marriage contract has been received.**

The daf opens with a question posed by Rava to Rav Nachman: what is the *halacha* in a case where a husband wrote a *get* on a gold plate — a *tass* of gold, some kind of gold foil — and then said to his wife, *hiskabli giteich v'hiskabli kesuvasech*, "receive your *get* and receive your *kesuva*"? The value of the gold on which the *get* is written is substantial, and the husband is proposing that this very value constitutes payment of her *kesuva*. What is the *halakha*? Is it a valid *get*, and does the value of the gold discharge the *kesuva* obligation?

The question turns on an issue already in play from the previous daf. The giving of a *get* requires a physical transfer of the object. But what exactly is the nature of that transfer — is it about the object's monetary value being conveyed, or is it simply the physical handing over of the thing? We established previously that even a leaf of an olive tree, worth less than a *perutah*, or even an item that is *issurei hana'ah* — something from which benefit is forbidden, which may not even be capable of being owned — nonetheless constitutes a valid *get* when physically handed over. That being so, if value is irrelevant to the act of giving a *get*, why can't the value of the gold *get* simultaneously serve as payment of the *kesuva*?

### Tosafot on Object

Tosafot addresses this directly. He acknowledges that we write a *get* on *issurei hana'ah* and it is valid — so value is clearly not the defining feature. Why, then, should attributing value to the *get* pose any problem? Tosafot answers: *hai mishum d'yehav la miah kol ha-get* — in the standard case, there is only one thing happening: the object is being given over entirely as a *get*. But here, *lo yehav la ela tofsa* — if you have a ten-thousand-dollar *get* and the focus is on the monetary value, the act of conveyance begins to look like the transfer of a valuable object rather than the giving of a *get*. The concern is that the nature of the act may be redefined by the monetary dimension. Tosafot ultimately concludes, however, that both things can be happening simultaneously — the physical giving constitutes the *get* and the taking of possession of the valuable object constitutes payment of the *kesuva*.

### Margins Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: "הִתְקַבְּלִי גִּיטִּיךְ, וְהַשְּׁאָר לִכְתוּבָּתִךָ" – נִתְקַבְּלָה גִּיטָּהּ, וְהַשְּׁאָר לִכְתוּבָּתָהּ. טַעְמָא דְּאִיכָּא שְׁאָר, הָא לֵיכָּא שְׁאָר – לֹא! הוּא הַדִּין אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁאָר; וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא שְׁאָר, אִי אָמַר לַהּ – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? אַוֵּירָא דִמְגִילְּתָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **raised an objection to his** statement from a *baraita*: If a husband gave his wife a bill of divorce on an oversized paper and said to her: **Receive your bill of divorce, and the remainder** of the paper, on which nothing is written, is designated **for** the payment of **your marriage contract,** then **her bill of divorce has been received and the remainder** should be **for** the payment of **her marriage contract.**
>
> The Gemara explains the objection: This indicates that **the reason** why the material upon which the bill of divorce is written may also serve as the payment for her marriage contract is **that there is** the **remainder** of the paper, on which the bill of divorce was not written. If **there is not** the **remainder** of the paper, then **no,** the bill of divorce may not serve as payment of the marriage contract. This is because the husband must give the bill of divorce to his wife for it to become hers. If so, even if the bill of divorce was written on a plate of gold, if there was no part of the plate of gold other than the space where the bill of divorce was written, it should not serve as payment of her marriage contract.
>
> Rav Naḥman responded: **The same is true although there is no remainder** of the paper, and he wrote the bill of divorce on the entire paper. **And** by stating the *halakha* in a case where there was an additional part of the paper, the *baraita* **teaches us this: Although there is** the **remainder** of the paper upon which nothing is written, **if he** explicitly **said to her** that it should be for the payment of her marriage contract, **yes,** it is considered to be a payment for her marriage contract. **If** he did **not** say this explicitly, then **no,** it is not for the payment of her marriage contract, and he must give her the value of her marriage contract in its entirety through a separate payment.
>
> The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? If he did not explicitly say that the remainder of the paper is designated toward the payment of her marriage contract, then the remainder is considered to be **the margin** around the border **of the scroll,** and all of the paper constitutes the bill of divorce. Therefore, it is necessary to inform her that the remainder of the paper is for the payment of her marriage contract.

The *Gemara* then introduces a *baraita* that raises a related question. Suppose the husband says to his wife: *hiskabli giteich v'hashar l'kesuvasech* — "receive your *get*, and the remainder goes toward your *kesuva*." That is, the *get* was written on a piece of gold, and the margins — the portions not covered by the writing — should serve as *kesuva* payment. This arrangement is ruled valid: it is a good *get* and the margins discharge the *kesuva*.

The *Gemara* infers from this that *taima d'ika shar* — the reason it works is precisely because there are margins. This implies that if the area of the *get* itself were to pay off the *kesuva*, that would not work — it would redefine the nature of the act. But the *Gemara* immediately rejects this inference: *hu hadin af al gav d'leka shar* — even with no margins at all, even if the entire *get* also serves as the *kesuva* payment, it is valid. The reason the *baraita* mentioned the case with margins is to teach something else entirely: that even when there are margins, the husband cannot unilaterally retain them. If he says nothing, *ada'ata d'megilta hu* — the margins are considered part of the scroll as a unified object, and she is entitled to the whole thing. Only if he explicitly specifies, before the giving, that the margins are to be credited toward the *kesuva*, does he retain that claim.

### Possession Principle

What emerges from these discussions is a consistent underlying principle: the giving of a *get* is not governed by the logic of *kinyan* — of acquiring monetary ownership. The *get* can be worth less than a *perutah*, can be worth nothing, and by some opinions, an item of *issurei hana'ah* may not even be capable of being owned at all. Nevertheless, the physical transfer of the *get* is valid. So complete is the separation between the act of giving the *get* and the logic of *dinei mamonot* that two entirely distinct acts can occur simultaneously: the physical giving of the *get* constitutes the divorce, while the taking of possession of the same object constitutes payment of the *kesuva*.

## Paper Ownership

### Husband Retains Paper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ וְהַנְּיָיר שֶׁלִּי" – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. "עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירִי לִי אֶת הַנְּיָיר" – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If the husband said to his wife: **Behold this is your bill of divorce,** but **the paper** on which it is written **is** still **mine,** then **she is not divorced,** as he must give her the actual bill of divorce in order for the divorce to take effect. Since the paper still belongs to him, it is as if he had given her only the writing. But if he said to her: Behold this is your bill of divorce **on the condition that you return the paper to me,** then **she is divorced.** The bill of divorce belongs entirely to her, and the returning of the paper is only a stipulation that must be fulfilled later.

The *Gemara* now turns to a *baraita* that directly tests the limits of this principle. If a husband says *harei ze giteich v'haniyar sheli* — "here is your *get*, but the paper is mine" — she is not divorced. This is qualitatively different from the case of *issurei hana'ah*. In the *issurei hana'ah* case, no one can own the paper, and yet it is fully in her possession; he has no claim to it. Here, by asserting ownership, the husband is in effect not fully giving the object over. The retention of ownership means the transfer is incomplete.

### Conditional Return

By contrast, if he says *al menat she-tachziri li es haniyar* — "on the condition that you return the paper to me" — she is divorced. The giving is complete. She takes full possession of the *get*; she simply has a future obligation to return the paper. This is structurally like a *matana al menat lehachzir* — a conditional gift — in which the act of giving is whole and unimpaired even though the object must eventually be returned. The *get* is fully under her control at the moment of giving, and she is therefore divorced.

### White Space Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: בֵּין שִׁיטָה לְשִׁיטָה וּבֵין תֵּיבָה לְתֵיבָה, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** If he said that the paper **between each line or between each word** in the bill of divorce will still belong to him but the paper where the words are written will be hers, **what** is the *halakha*? A solution is not found for this question, and the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Pappa then poses a more refined question: what if the husband says he is retaining not the paper on which the letters are written, but only the white space between the words and between the lines — *bein shita l'shita, bein teiva l'teiva*? Is she divorced?

On the one hand, perhaps what he is retaining is not really the *get* itself, since she receives full possession of everything the letters are written on. On the other hand, if one were to physically remove the paper he is claiming — all the white space surrounding the letters — what would remain would be tiny, disconnected scraps. Can that truly be called a *get*?

### Teiku on Letters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ "דְּסֵפֶר" אֶחָד אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה סְפָרִים! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּמְעוֹרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why isn't a solution offered for Rav Pappa's question? **Let him derive** an answer to this question from elsewhere, as **the Merciful One speaks** of **one scroll** in regard to the bill of divorce in the Torah: "And he writes her a scroll of severance" (Deuteronomy 24:1), **and not two or three scrolls.** If the woman receives only the paper on which the words are written but the rest of the paper belongs to the man, then this is not viewed as a single scroll. Rather, it is multiple scrolls, thereby invalidating it. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to discuss the question in a case where the letters are **intertwined,** meaning that they were written in such a manner that even if the paper between them was removed, the writing in the bill of divorce would remain attached as a single entity.

The *Gemara* leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved.

The *Gemara* considers whether this should be obvious from the rule that a *get* must be a single, unified document — *safra echad Rachmana, v'lo shnayim u'shlosha sfarim* — and not multiple pieces. But it answers: *lo tzricha dime'ura* — the case is one in which the letters are written in an interwoven fashion, such that removing the claimed white space would still leave the letters physically connected. For example, a *lamed* from one line might extend into the space below, connecting with the next line, so that even after excision of the "white space," the document would remain one physical unit.

The difficulty with this answer, however, is not resolved neatly. If the document is truly constructed so that it would remain whole even after the husband's space is removed, it is not clear why it should not simply be valid. And if it cannot remain whole, it is not clear why the *Gemara* even entertains the possibility of validity. This is, in truth, a puzzling *teiku*, and it is not entirely clear what the *Gemara* finds genuinely uncertain.

### Interwoven Letters

Rashi explains *dime'ura* as meaning that the letters were intentionally written long so as to reach from line to line at the head of each word — *otiyot arukot hamagi'ot mishita l'shita b'rosh kol teiva v'teiva* — thereby creating physical connection throughout the document. On Rashi's reading, the construction is comprehensive enough to ensure continuity.

The *Ribam* — Rabbeinu Yitzchak bar Mordechai — draws a different conclusion. He argues there is no proof here, because *dime'ura* refers only to cases where letters from one line intrude naturally into the space of the adjacent line — a *regel kaf* of an upper line into the space of the line below, or a *lamed* into a *heh* or *chet* above it — not to letters that are actually touching one another. Moreover, the *Ribam* argues, there is no proof that letters touching is required in a *get* at all; that requirement may be specific to a *Sefer Torah*.

Tosafot, meanwhile, derives a broader principle from this passage: since the *Gemara* discusses the case of *dime'ura* — interwoven letters — and treats it as the operative scenario where the question becomes meaningful, it follows that the letters must actually be touching for the concern to be meaningful. The *Ribam* disputes this, maintaining that touching letters are not required, and that the concern arises simply from the structural configuration described. The question of whether letters in a *get* must have white space around them, or whether touching letters invalidate it, is left as a live debate between these *rishonim*.

## Slave as Medium

### Slave Holding Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הָיוּ מוּחְזָקִים בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁהוּא שֶׁלּוֹ – וְגֵט כָּתוּב עַל יָדוֹ, וַהֲרֵי הוּא יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדָהּ, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן אַקְנוֹיֵי אַקְנִי לַהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא, הוּא מִנַּפְשֵׁיהּ עָיֵיל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** If the judges **had a presumption that a slave was his,** i.e., that it belonged to the husband; **and the bill of divorce** that this man gave his wife was **written on** that slave's **hand; and** now the slave **emerges from her possession,** i.e., he is with the woman, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to this bill of divorce?
>
> The Gemara explains the elements of the question: **Do we say that** the husband **transferred** the slave **to** his wife? Therefore, it is as though he gave her the bill of divorce in the proper manner, as is explained in the mishna, and she is divorced. **Or perhaps** we say that **he,** the slave, **entered** the possession of the woman **on his own,** because he prefers to be under her ownership rather than under her husband's ownership, in which case the husband did not give him to the woman at all, and she is not divorced, as the husband needs to give the bill of divorce to the woman?

Rami Bar Chama poses the following question: it was known that a certain slave belonged to the husband; a *get* was written on the slave's arm; and now the slave is found in the possession of the wife — *harei hu yotze mitachas yada*. What is the *halakha*? Is she divorced?

The question is whether we say *akneiye aknei lah* — that the husband formally transferred the slave to her — or *dilma hu minafshei azal* — perhaps the slave simply walked over of his own accord, since slaves are capable of independent movement, and her physical possession of the slave proves nothing about whether it was deliberately given over as a *get*. This seems, on its face, to be a general question of evidentiary standards in ownership, not a *get*-specific question.

### Tattooing Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דִּכְתַב שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהִזְדַּיֵּיף הוּא! וּלְרָבָא, קַשְׁיָא מַתְנִיתִין "עַל הַיָּד שֶׁל עֶבֶד"!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And let** Rami bar Ḥama **derive** an answer to his question from a different reason, **that** writing on a person's body **is writing that can be forged,** as it is easily erased and replaced with other writing, and a bill of divorce that is written in a manner susceptible to forgery is invalid. The Gemara clarifies: **But according to Rava,** who raised this challenge, **the mishna** is **difficult,** as it taught that a bill of divorce may be written **on a slave's hand.**

Rava interjects with a prior objection: before we even reach the question of whether the slave was transferred, the *get* written in ink on the slave's arm is susceptible to forgery — it could be erased and rewritten. That disqualifies it as a valid document, regardless of how it was transferred.

### Tattooed Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מַתְנִיתִין לְרָבָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, בְּעֵדֵי מְסִירָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא. אֶלָּא לְרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא קַשְׁיָא! לְרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא – בִּכְתוֹבֶת קַעֲקַע. הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, מַתְנִיתִין (לְרָבָא) נָמֵי לָא תִּיקְשֵׁי – בִּכְתוֹבֶת קַעֲקַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Granted, the mishna** is **not difficult for Rava,** as it is possible to say that the mishna is referring to a case where the husband gave her the slave **with witnesses** present **who observe the transmission of a legal document.** In other words, they were present when the slave, who has the legal document written on him, was given to the woman. **And it is** in accordance with **Rabbi Elazar,** who holds that the essential witnesses are those who witness the delivery, and since they witnessed the delivery, she is divorced. **However, for Rami bar Ḥama** it is **difficult.** In his case, there were no witnesses to the transfer of the slave, so the possibility that the writing was forged should invalidate it.
>
> The Gemara answers: **According to Rami bar Ḥama** it is **also not difficult,** as he did not ask his question in a case where the bill of divorce was written on the slave with ink but **with regard to** a case where it was **written as a tattoo,** so the writing certainly cannot be erased and forged. The Gemara notes: **Now that you have arrived at this, the mishna,** which deemed valid a bill of divorce that was written on the hand of the slave, **will not** be **difficult** for Rava **either,** as it is also stating the *halakha* **with regard to** a case where **it was written as a tattoo.**

The *Gemara* responds: this is *kashia matnitin* — how does Rava explain the *Mishna*, which explicitly permits writing a *get* on a slave's hand? Rava answers: the *Mishna* is referring to a case of *eidei mesira* — witnesses present at the moment of transfer, who can testify to what was written. This is consistent with Rabbi Elazar's position. In such a case, the document's susceptibility to forgery is irrelevant because the witnesses themselves attest to the content and the transfer.

Returning to Rami Bar Chama's question: since his case involves no witnesses to the transfer — the entire question is whether the slave's presence in her possession constitutes proof — the problem of forgery is real. The *Gemara* resolves this by stipulating that the case involves *biksivat ka'aka* — a tattoo. A tattooed *get* cannot be forged. The document is therefore valid and not subject to the forgery concern.

Tosafot notes that tattooing involves a potential *issur de'oraisa* only when performed with certain specific inks, as described in the third chapter of *Makkot*, and that even an *issur de'rabbanan* may attach in some configurations. However, Tosafot concludes that even if an *issur de'oraisa* were involved, it would not prevent the tattooed inscription from functioning as a valid *get*.

### Flocks Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַגּוֹדְרוֹת, אֵין לָהֶן חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, Rami bar Ḥama's question remains unresolved. If so, **what** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof based on **that** which **Reish Lakish says: With regard to livestock, there is no presumption** of ownership, since they wander from place to place. Therefore, a person cannot claim that his mere possession of livestock demonstrates ownership, because they may have wandered into his property on their own. The same *halakha* should apply to a slave. If there are no witnesses who saw the transfer to the woman, then her mere possession of the slave should not serve as proof that the slave, and by extension the bill of divorce, was given to her, and consequently the woman is not divorced.

The underlying question — whether the slave's presence in her possession is sufficient proof that it was transferred — is addressed by the principle stated by Resh Lakish: *ha-godot ein lahem chazakah* — flocks have no presumption of ownership based on possession alone. An animal that moves on its own can arrive in your yard without having been transferred. Accordingly, possession of a moving creature does not establish ownership the way possession of a static object does. For flocks — and similarly for slaves — one establishes ownership either through three years of uncontested possession or through a bill of sale.

### Slave Possession

Tosafot raises the question: if this rule is well established, what was the *safek* — the doubt — that prompted Rami Bar Chama's question? The answer Tosafot offers is that perhaps the combination of physical possession *and* the presence of a *get* written on the slave should together serve as proof that it was deliberately given over. But that argument ultimately fails, since the *halacha* of *ein lahem chazakah* is decisive.

There is, however, a more analytically interesting dimension to this question. We have established throughout this daf that the giving of a *get* does not require the kind of monetary transfer recognized by *dinei mamonot*. It can be given on *issurei hana'ah*, objects that may not be capable of conventional ownership. If that is so, perhaps the *standards of proof* for a *get* are also distinct from the standards of *dinei mamonot*. One might argue that even if physical possession of a slave does not prove ownership in *dinei mamonot*, it might nonetheless constitute sufficient evidence that she received the *get*, since for *get* purposes we are not operating within the framework of *Choshen Mishpat* but of *Even HaEzer*. The act of transfer might not need to mirror a monetary *kinyan*. 

Conversely, one could argue the reverse: that even if after three years she could establish ownership of the slave under *dinei mamonot*, that still would not retroactively prove that the *get* was given to her at the outset, since the evidence of *get* transfer must be apparent from the moment of giving — not established through a process that takes years to complete.

The *Gemara*'s resolution via *ein lahem chazakah* seems to cut across both frameworks, essentially holding that the slave was not even genuinely "in her possession" in any legally cognizable sense, and the question therefore fails on its own terms.

## Sefer Makneh

### Tablet Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הָיוּ מוּחְזָקִין בְּטַבְלָא שֶׁהִיא שֶׁלָּהּ – וְגֵט כָּתוּב עָלֶיהָ; וַהֲרֵי הִיא יוֹצְאָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן אַקְנוֹיֵי אַקְנִיתַהּ לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא (אִשָּׁה) לָא יָדְעָה לְאַקְנוֹיֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises** another **dilemma:** If the judges **had a presumption that a slate was hers, and a bill of divorce was written on it, and** now this slate **emerges from his possession,** and he wants to divorce his wife by giving her the slate, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say that she transferred the ownership** of the slate **to him,** and he may give it to her as a bill of divorce, and the divorce takes effect? **Or perhaps** a woman **does not understand** how **to transfer** an object that will be given back to her, and she believes it is a formality and not an actual legal transfer. If this were to be the case, they would not be divorced, as the slate did not in fact belong to the husband.

Rami Bar Chama poses a related question: it was known that a woman owned a particular *tavla* — a tablet — and a *get* was written on it. Now the husband is found holding it. What is the *halakha*? Can the *get* be valid?

The scenario is that she gave him the tablet specifically so he could write a *get* on it and give it back to her. But for a *get* to be valid, the husband must own the document — the principle of *sefer makneh* requires that the document be owned by the party transferring it. The question is whether she genuinely transferred ownership to him, or whether, since she expected to receive it back, her intention was never a full and complete transfer of ownership. This is analogous to the classic question of whether a bride can give her groom a family heirloom ring for him to give back to her under the *chuppa* — is the initial giving considered a real transfer if the return is anticipated from the outset?

### Woman's Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: אַף הוּא הֵעִיד עַל כְּפָר קָטָן שֶׁהָיָה בְּצַד יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, וְהָיָה בּוֹ זָקֵן אֶחָד, וְהָיָה מַלְוֶה לְכׇל בְּנֵי הַכְּפָר, וְכוֹתֵב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ וַאֲחֵרִים חוֹתְמִים, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְהִכְשִׁירוּהוּ. וְאַמַּאי? הָא בָּעֵינָא "סֵפֶר מִקְנָה", וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a proof based on what is stated in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 2:3): **Even he,** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, who was quoted earlier in this mishna, **testified about a small village that was adjacent to Jerusalem, and there was an old man there who would lend** money **to all the residents of the village. And he would write** the documents **in his handwriting and others** would **sign. And the incident came before the Sages and they deemed it valid. And why** did they deem it valid? **Isn't there a need** to fulfill this verse: "I took the **deed of purchase"** (Jeremiah 32:11), requiring that the document itself be transferred from the seller to the buyer, **and** this is **not** the case here, as the document attesting to the loan was in the possession of the creditor the entire time?

Abaye frames the uncertainty differently: *lo yad'ah la'akneye* — perhaps a person in this situation simply does not know that they are legally required to fully transfer ownership of the object even when expecting it back. The question is not only one of intent but of awareness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן אַקְנוֹיֵי מַקְנֶה לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not because we say** that he certainly **transferred** the ownership of the documents **to them** in a legally binding manner, although he knew that they would be returned to him immediately afterward. The same should apply in this case, and the assumption should be that the woman transferred ownership of the slate to the husband, and he then gave it back to her.

### Elder Lender Case

The *Gemara* then invokes a precedent: the testimony of Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava regarding a certain small village near Jerusalem, where there was one elder who would lend money to all the village residents. He would write the loan documents in his own handwriting — *bichtav yado* — and other people would sign as witnesses. This practice was brought before the *Chachamim* and validated.

The *Gemara* asks: how does this work? The concept of *sefer makneh* requires that the document be owned by the party from whom it passes. In a loan transaction, it is the borrower who must ultimately convey the document to the lender. But here, the lender was preparing all the documents himself. The answer implied by the *Gemara* is that the elder would prepare the document, hand it to the borrower, have witnesses sign it, and the borrower would then hand it back. Even though the borrower knew the document was coming back to the lender, the act of taking possession and returning it constitutes a valid transfer. We therefore conclude: *akneiye aknei lehu* — we presume that someone can and does fully transfer an object even knowing it will be returned to them.

### Tosafot Distinction

Tosafot questions whether *sefer makneh* truly applies to a loan document, as opposed to a document effecting a transfer of property. Tosafot's distinction is that the formal requirement of *sefer makneh* — which derives from a *pasuk* — applies to a *shtar* that itself operates as the instrument of transfer or change of status, such as a deed of sale or a *get*. A *shtar raya* — a document that merely serves as evidence that a transaction occurred, such as a simple loan record — may not carry the same formal requirement regarding which party must own the physical document, since it is the testimony of the witnesses, not the object itself, that does the legal work. Tosafot therefore suggests that the elder lender was also handling property transfer documents for his borrowers, not merely loan records, and those documents would indeed be subject to *sefer makneh*.

The plain reading of the *Gemara*, however, seems to apply *sefer makneh* across the board — whether the *shtar* effects a change or merely records evidence — requiring in all cases that the document be owned by the party conveying it.

### Co-signer Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא זָקֵן שָׁאנֵי, דְּיָדַע לְאַקְנוֹיֵי. אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מֵהָכָא – עָרֵב הַיּוֹצֵא לְאַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת, גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And what** is the **difficulty?** How can one prove anything from the mishna? **Perhaps** a knowledgeable **old man is different, as he understands** the need **to transfer** the documents, and this may not be true in the case of a woman and the bill of divorce.
>
> **Rather, Rava said:** A proof may be brought **from here:** If there was **a guarantor** whose commitment **emerged after the** promissory **note was signed,** then the creditor may **collect** only **from** the guarantor's **unsold property.** However, he does not have a lien on the guarantor's property with which he could collect from property sold after he signed on as a guarantor. Rava's proof is that it must be that ownership of the promissory note was transferred to the guarantor before he signed it, in order for his commitment to take effect. It may therefore be seen from this *baraita* that the participants understand the need to transfer ownership of the document.

Rava challenges whether the precedent of the elder lender proves anything about a woman's knowledge and intent. Perhaps the elder was different — *zaken shani de-yada l'akneye* — he was the professional, the literate man of business, the one who arranged all the transactions in the village. It is reasonable to assume he knew precisely what legal formalities were required. One cannot extrapolate from his conduct to a case involving a woman who may not be versed in these requirements.

Rav Ashi therefore seeks a better precedent: the case of an *arev ha-yotze l'achar chitum shtarot* — a guarantor who signs a document after it has already been sealed and handed over to the lender. If I borrowed money from someone, signed a document and gave it over, and then a guarantor subsequently comes and signs it while the document is already in the lender's possession, this is a valid *halacha* under which the lender may collect from the guarantor. But how does that work? The guarantor's signature can only create a *shtar* that functions against his property if the document was in his possession and conveyed to the lender — the logic of *sefer makneh* again. The only way this works is if the lender gave the document back to the guarantor so that the guarantor could sign it and formally re-convey it. And we trust that this is what happened, even without witnesses to the intermediate step of conveyance back and forth. The guarantor knew what he needed to do and did it.

### Woman Writes Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא גַּבְרָא שָׁאנֵי, דְּיָדַע לְאַקְנוֹיֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מֵהָכָא – אִשָּׁה כּוֹתֶבֶת אֶת גִּיטָּהּ, וְהָאִישׁ כּוֹתֵב אֶת שׁוֹבָרוֹ, שֶׁאֵין קִיּוּם הַגֵּט אֶלָּא בְּחוֹתְמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: What is the difficulty** raised by this *baraita*? **Perhaps a man is different,** in **that** he **understands** the need **to transfer** the document, and the question of the Gemara pertains to a woman, who may not be as well versed in the minutiae of monetary law. **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** There is a proof **from** what was taught **here** (22b): **A woman may write her bill of divorce** on her own or allow a scribe to write it on her behalf, and then give it to her husband, so that he will give it to her. Similarly, **a man may write his receipt** that he will receive from the woman after paying her marriage contract, **as the ratification of a bill of divorce is only through its signatories,** i.e., the witnesses who sign it, and the mere writing of the document or its receipt has no legal ramifications and may be done by anyone. In any case, it is clear from here that a woman understands the need to transfer the bill of divorce that will be given to her by her husband in the future.

Rava challenges this precedent as well: *gavra shani de-yada l'akneye* — a man, presumably familiar with business dealings, knows that he must formally transfer and reconvey the document. That tells us nothing about whether a woman understands the same requirement.

Rav Ashi then cites a final and decisive source: *isha kotevet et gittah* — a woman may write her own *get*. The *Mishna* teaches that a woman can be appointed as a *shaliach* to write the *get*, and *v'ish kotev et shovro* — and the husband can write his own receipt acknowledging payment of the *kesuva*. These are both valid. The critical point is that *she-ein kiyum ha-get ela b'chotmav* — the *get* only becomes effective once it is signed by witnesses.

Tosafot anticipates an objection: perhaps *isha kotevet et gittah* simply means she does the writing on whatever material is at hand, and proves nothing about whether she knows to formally transfer ownership of her own parchment. But the *Gemara*'s language is *stam* — unqualified — and therefore must be understood to include even a case where the parchment belongs to her. This means that even when the parchment is hers, we presume she knows to transfer it fully to her husband so that he can then give it back to her as a *get*. The *chiddush* is not merely that she can hold a quill, but that she is credited with knowing and executing the formal ownership transfer required by *sefer makneh*, even without anyone explicitly instructing her to do so.

All of this bears directly on the practical question of a ring given by a bride to a groom for use under the *chuppa*. The basic answer that emerges from the *Gemara* is that such a transfer is valid — the initial giving is recognized as a complete and legally cognizable act of transfer, even when the giver knows the object is coming back.

## Indirect Transfer

### Get+Slave+Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כָּתַב לָהּ גֵּט, וּנְתָנוֹ בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ, וְכָתַב לָהּ שְׁטַר מַתָּנָה עָלָיו – קְנָאַתְהוּ, וּמִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** If **he wrote her a bill of divorce and placed it in the hand of his slave, and he wrote her a deed of gift with regard to** the slave, then **she acquired** the slave due to the document, **and she is divorced** immediately **by the** bill of divorce that is in his hand. The slave is considered to be like her property, and it is as though the husband had placed the bill of divorce in her domain at the time that he transferred the slave to her, and she acquires the bill of divorce as though it were in her courtyard.

Rava now poses a striking case. A husband wrote a *get* and placed it in the hand of his slave, and then wrote a *shtar matana* — a deed of gift — giving the slave to his wife. She takes possession of the *shtar matana* on the slave, thereby acquiring the slave. Does this constitute a valid *get*?

This question probes the fundamental nature of *nesinat get* — the act of giving a *get*. Throughout this daf we have established that the giving does not require a *kinyan* in the *dinei mamonot* sense. But here the scenario is more radical: there was no direct act of giving the *get* to the wife at all. The *get* was placed in the hand of the slave; the slave was given to the wife; and she receives the *get* only indirectly — through taking possession of the slave who is holding it. One could argue that this is all one unified package — he is giving her the slave along with everything the slave holds, and the whole gift constitutes the act of *nesinat get*. Or one could argue that the act of giving the *get* and the act of taking possession are here so completely separated and indirect that it cannot be called a *nesinat get* at all.

### Moving Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? חָצֵר מְהַלֶּכֶת הִיא, וְחָצֵר מְהַלֶּכֶת לֹא קָנָה! וְכִי תֵּימָא בְּעוֹמֵד, וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל שֶׁאִילּוּ מְהַלֵּךְ לֹא קָנָה, עוֹמֵד וְיוֹשֵׁב לֹא קָנָה! וְהִלְכְתָא – בְּכָפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why** does she acquire the bill of divorce? The slave **is** considered **a mobile courtyard, and a mobile courtyard does not acquire** property. One's courtyard can acquire items for him only when the courtyard is fixed in its location. Since a slave is considered to be like land with regard to other areas of *halakha*, he should also be defined as a mobile courtyard, as he can move from place to place. **And if you would say** that Rava was referring **to a** slave who happens to be **standing,** who is not mobile, **but didn't Rava say: Anything that does not acquire when moving** also **does not acquire** when it is **standing or sitting.** The fact that the slave could move gives him the status of a moving courtyard, regardless if he is currently moving or not. The Gemara answers: **And the *halakha*** taught by Rava could apply **in** a case where the slave was **bound** and unable to move, as in that case he is not even able to move, and is not considered to be a mobile courtyard.

The *Gemara* accepts that this constitutes a valid *nesinat get* but raises a different concern: *ve-amai chatzer mehalechet hi* — is this not a *chatzer mehalechet* — a moving *chatzer*? The principle of *kinyan chatzer* holds that just as a woman's *chatzer* — her field or domain — can acquire a *get* on her behalf when it is placed therein, so too objects within her domain become hers. But a slave, because he moves of his own accord, is considered a *chatzer mehalechet* — a moving *chatzer* — and *chatzer mehalechet lo kana*, a moving *chatzer* does not effect acquisition. So even if we grant that the act of giving the slave with the *get* is a valid *nesinat get*, she may not actually acquire the piece of paper with the *get* written on it, because it is in the hand of a moving *chatzer*.

The *Gemara* considers the counterargument that the slave happened to be standing still at that moment — *bein omed*. Rava's rule, however, forecloses this: *kol she'ilu mahalech lo kana, omed ve'yoshev lo kana* — anything that would not effect a *kinyan* when moving does not effect a *kinyan* when standing still either. The category of *chatzer mehalechet* is definitional, not positional. The resolution is: *v'hu she-kafus* — the slave is physically bound, and a bound slave is in a different category.

### Get in Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: כָּתַב לָהּ גֵּט וּנְתָנוֹ בַּחֲצֵרוֹ, וְכָתַב לָהּ שְׁטַר מַתָּנָה עָלָיו – קְנָאַתְהוּ, וּמִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** also **says:** If **he wrote her a bill of divorce and placed it in his courtyard, and he wrote a deed of gift with regard to** the courtyard, then **she has acquired** the courtyard **and is divorced** immediately **by the** bill of divorce in the courtyard.

Rava then formulates the simpler version of the case: he wrote a *get*, placed it in his *chatzer* — his field — and then wrote a *shtar matana* giving the field to his wife. She takes possession of the field, and thereby acquires the *get* within it. This too, the *Gemara* confirms, is valid: *k'natahu u-mitgarshet bo*. The *chiddush* here is not about *chatzer mehalechet* at all — a field is entirely stable — but about something more fundamental: the act of giving the *get* is being achieved indirectly, through the giving of the field that contains it. This is one unified package that constitutes an act of *nesinat get*.

### Field vs Slave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עֶבֶד, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דַּוְקָא עֶבֶד, אֲבָל חָצֵר – לִיגְזַר, מִשּׁוּם חֲצֵרָהּ הַבָּאָה לְאַחַר מִכָּאן; וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן חָצֵר, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דַּוְקָא חָצֵר, אֲבָל עֶבֶד – לִיגְזַר כָּפוּת אַטּוּ שֶׁאֵינוֹ כָּפוּת; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** for Rava to teach his *halakha* both with regard to a slave and with regard to a courtyard. One could not have been learned from the other, **as had he taught us** only about **a slave, I would say** that the husband may transfer the bill of divorce to his wife **specifically** through **a slave, but** in the case of **a courtyard** the Sages may decide **to enact a decree** that this should not be a valid bill of divorce, **due to** the case of **her courtyard that comes afterward.** People may not differentiate between this case and a similar case, where the husband places the bill of divorce in the courtyard of a third party, and that courtyard is subsequently acquired by her. In that case, the couple is not divorced, as the bill of divorce was not given by the husband to the wife. Therefore, Rava has to teach that the Sages did not make this decree, and in this case, where the husband gave the courtyard together with the bill of divorce, it is valid.
>
> **And had** Rava **taught us** only about **a courtyard,** then **I would say** that a woman is divorced **specifically** if her husband placed the bill of divorce in **a courtyard, but** in the case of **a slave** the Sages may decide **to decree** with regard to **a bound** slave that the divorce will not take effect, **due to** the similarity to **an unbound** slave, where the divorce would not take effect, as explained above. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that it is a valid bill of divorce in both cases.

Tosafot raises a pointed question: if the field case works, why did Rava need to state the slave case at all? And conversely, if the slave case works, why state the field case? The *Gemara* answers that both cases are necessary: *utzricha*. Had we only learned from the slave case, we might have thought the reason it works is something specific to a slave, but a *chatzer* would be subject to the concern about a *chatzer haba'ah l'achar mikan* — a *chatzer* that comes into her possession only after the *get* was placed there, perhaps through a third-party intermediary — and we might not allow it. And had we only learned from the *chatzer* case, we might have thought the reason it works is the stability of a field, but we would not allow a slave even when bound, lest we come to allow an unbound slave. Each case therefore teaches something the other does not.

Rashi explains *chatzer haba'ah l'achar mikan* as referring to a scenario where the husband places the *get* in his yard, sells the yard to a third party, and the third party subsequently sells it to the wife. That obviously cannot constitute a *nesinat get*, since there is no act of giving from husband to wife. The concern is that if we allow the basic case, people will confuse it with this impermissible scenario.

### Active Participation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִכְּדֵי חָצֵר מֵהֵיכָא אִיתְרַבַּי? מִ"יָּדָהּ"; מַה יָדָהּ דְּאִיתַהּ בֵּין מִדַּעְתַּהּ וּבֵין בְּעַל כּוּרְחַהּ, אַף חֲצֵרָהּ דְּאִיתַהּ בֵּין מִדַּעְתַּהּ וּבֵין בְּעַל כּוּרְחַהּ; וְהָא מַתָּנָה – מִדַּעְתַּהּ אִיתַהּ, בְּעַל כּוּרְחַהּ לֵיתַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** as a challenge to Rava: **Now, from where was a courtyard included?** What is the source for the *halakha* that a man can divorce a woman by placing a bill of divorce in her courtyard? This can be derived **from** the phrase: "And gives it in **her hand"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), and the Sages derived that this is not limited to her actual hand but also includes anything that is an extension of her hand, such as her courtyard.
>
> Consequently, the following reasoning should apply: **Just as** with regard to **her hand, that it** acquires property for her **whether with her consent or against her will, so too,** with regard to **her courtyard,** it should be **that it** acquires property for her **whether with her consent or against her will. And** with regard to **a gift, it is** so that she acquires it **with her consent,** but **it is not** so that she acquires it **against her will.** Therefore, a courtyard that a husband transfers to his wife as a gift along with a bill of divorce is not the same as a bill of divorce that he gives into her hand. As a courtyard is different from her hand in this sense, it should not be able to be used as a means of transferring a bill of divorce.

The deeper *chiddush* of Rava's cases — both slave and field — is now brought into relief. The standard principle of *nesinat get* is that it is a unilateral act: the husband gives and it is effective regardless of whether the wife agrees, actively participates, or even knows. A *get* thrown into her *chatzer* divorces her without her consent. But in Rava's case, the wife must actively participate: she must agree to receive the gift of the field or the slave. Without her willing acceptance of the *matana*, she acquires nothing and there is no *get*. This is fundamentally different from the standard unilateral act of giving a *get*.

### Shlichut Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: וְהָא שְׁלִיחוּת לְקַבָּלָה, דְּמִדַּעְתַּהּ אִיתַהּ בְּעַל כּוּרְחַהּ לֵיתַהּ, וְקָא הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ לְִקַבָּלָה! וְאַבָּיֵי – אַטּוּ שְׁלִיחוּת מִ"יָּדָהּ" אִיתְרַבַּי?! מִ"וְּשִׁלַּח וְשִׁלְּחָהּ" אִיתְרַבַּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi objects to** the reasoning of Abaye: **But** what of **agency for receipt** of the bill of divorce, where the woman appoints an agent to receive a bill of divorce on her behalf, concerning **which** the agent can act **with her consent,** but **not against her will.** No one other than the wife can appoint an agent to receive the bill of divorce, and despite this **he is an agent for receipt.** Yet a woman is able to appoint an agent for receipt. Evidently, the comparison of other methods of acquisition to acquisition by placing the bill of divorce in her hand is not absolute.
>
> **And Abaye** would respond: **Is that to say** that the *halakha* of **agency is included from** the words **"her hand"?** It is not learned from there; rather, it **is included** based on the additional wording of the verse, as the verse does not state: **And he sent [*veshillaḥ*].** Rather, it states: **"And sends her out [*veshilleḥa*]"** (Deuteronomy 24:1). The expanded term teaches that a woman can also appoint an agent to receive a bill of divorce on her behalf. Since agency has a different source in the Torah, it is not impacted by the limitations of her hand. The *halakha* that a bill of divorce can be given to her by placing it in her courtyard is an extension of the *halakha* that it can be placed in her hand and does carry the limitations of her hand. Therefore, it must be able to work whether with her consent or against her will.

Abaye raises exactly this challenge: *chatzer meheicha itrabai* — from where do we derive that a *chatzer* works as a mechanism for *get*? It is derived from *yadah* — "her hand." Just as her hand receives the *get* regardless of her consent, so too her *chatzer* must receive it regardless of consent. *Mah yadah de'isa bein meda'atah bein be'al korchah, af chatzeirah de'isa bein meda'atah bein be'al korchah* — the *chatzer* analogy to the hand requires that it function even without her agreement. But in Rava's case of the gift, *meda'atah isa, be'al korchah leisa* — it only works with her consent, not without it. It is therefore of a fundamentally different character.

Rav Ashi responds with the case of *shlichut lekabbala* — a woman's agent appointed to receive her *get* on her behalf. This too requires her active participation: she must appoint the agent, and presumably if she revokes her consent during the process the *shlichut* lapses. Yet it is fully valid as an act of receiving a *get*. So active participation does not automatically preclude validity.

Abaye replies that *shlichut* is derived from a separate *parsha* — *ve-shalach ve-shilchah* — not from the analogy to *yad*. The laws of *shlichut* operate under their own derivation and framework. A field is not a *shaliach*; it is supposed to be an extension of her *yad*, and if it requires her active agreement to acquire the field, it cannot function as an extension of her hand receiving the *get*.

One can also conceptualize the distinction as follows: in *shlichut*, the woman sets up the arrangement in advance, and once done, the actual act of giving the *get* to the agent is still essentially unilateral. But in Rava's case, the act of giving cannot reach her at all without her active participation at the very moment of transfer. This is a categorically different kind of involvement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שְׁלִיחוּת לְקַבָּלָה נָמֵי אַשְׁכְּחַן בְּעַל כּוּרְחַהּ, שֶׁכֵּן אָב מְקַבֵּל גֵּט לְבִתּוֹ קְטַנָּה בְּעַל כּוֹרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** a different answer: **We also have found** that **agency for receipt** can be effective **against her will.** How so? **As** the *halakha* is that **a father can receive a bill of divorce for his minor daughter against her will.** The *halakhot* of agency are therefore consistent with the *halakhot* with regard to her hand, and Abaye's objection stands.

The *Gemara* then considers a further answer: *shlichut lekabbala nami ashkachan be'al korchah* — we find *shlichut lekabbala* that works even against the woman's will: a father may accept a *get* on behalf of his minor daughter even against her will — and Tosafot notes that it may even work against the father's own will. Since the institution of *shlichut lekabbala* functions in some cases entirely without consent, it operates validly even in the ordinary case where the woman does appoint her agent willingly.

Tosafot argues that this does not rehabilitate Rava's case, because the case of the *chatzer* is different: the woman can always, in theory, divest herself of her *chatzer*. There is always a theoretical opening through which she could undo the relationship. By contrast, the relationship of father to minor daughter is absolute and inescapable, which is why the institution of *shlichut* is understood to work even against all parties' will. That absolute form of the institution carries over to the voluntary case. But Rava's case involves an act where she must affirmatively participate at the moment of *netina*, and that remains a genuine *chiddush* for which the *Gemara* struggles to find a proper precedent or derivation.

Rava's *chiddush* ultimately stands — the *Gemara* does not formally reject it — and the standard ruling follows Rava. But the challenge of Abaye remains analytically significant: giving a *get* is normally a unilateral act, and any mechanism that requires the wife's active agreement at the moment of transfer is a *chiddush* of considerable magnitude.

### Horn of Cow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא יָד דְּעֶבֶד לָא אֶפְשָׁר לְמִקְצְיַיהּ, אֶלָּא קֶרֶן שֶׁל פָּרָה – לִיקְצְיַיהּ וְלִיתְּבַהּ לָהּ! אָמַר קְרָא: "וְכָתַב" – "וְנָתַן לַהּ", מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּסָּר אֶלָּא כְּתִיבָה וּנְתִינָה; יָצָא זֶה – שֶׁמְחוּסָּר כְּתִיבָה, קְצִיצָה וּנְתִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that a bill of divorce may be written **on an olive leaf,** on the horn of a cow, or on the hand of a slave, provided that the husband then gives her the slave or the cow. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** with regard to **the hand of a slave,** **it is not possible to cut it off,** as it is certainly prohibited to cut off the hand of a slave, and he therefore must give her the slave. **But** if he wrote the bill of divorce on **the horn of a cow, let him cut it off and give it to her.** Why does the mishna state that he must give her the cow?
>
> The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "And he writes** her a scroll of severance, **and gives it** in **her** hand" (Deuteronomy 24:1), meaning that **something** is valid as a bill of divorce **when it is lacking only writing and giving, excluding this,** a cow's horn, **which is lacking writing, cutting, and giving.** Since the additional step of cutting would be required in order for him to give her the horn alone, the horn would not be a valid bill of divorce, so he must give her the cow.

The *Gemara* turns the page and addresses a question that flows naturally from the laws about writing a *get* on different surfaces. The *Mishna* had discussed writing on a slave's hand and giving over the slave. The *Gemara* asks: granted that one cannot cut off the slave's hand — that is prohibited — but if one wrote a *get* on the horn of a cow, why not simply cut off the horn and hand it to the wife? *Ve-leisva lah* — just give it to her!

The answer is derived from the *pasuk*: *ve-kasav ve-nasan lah* — "and he shall write and give to her." The verse teaches: *mi she-eino mechusar ela kesiva ve-nesina* — only something that requires only writing and giving. *Yatza zeh she-mechusar kesiva ketzitza ve-nesina* — excluded is something that requires writing, then cutting, then giving. The requirement is that the transition from the writing stage to the giving stage be direct and unmediated by an act of *ketzitza* — cutting or detachment.

### Yose vs Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? דְּתַנְיָא: "סֵפֶר" – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא סֵפֶר, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְכָתַב לָהּ" – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "סֵפֶר"? מָה סֵפֶר – דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֶל; אַף כׇּל דָּבָר – שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֶל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says** that a bill of divorce may not be written on something living, nor on food. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's** opinion? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is said in the Torah with regard to a bill of divorce: "And he writes her **a scroll** of severance" (Deuteronomy 24:1). From the word "scroll," **I have** derived **only** that **a scroll** is valid; **from where** do I derive that it is correct **to include all objects** as valid materials upon which a bill of divorce may be written? **The verse states: "And he writes her," in any case,** i.e., a bill of divorce can be written on any type of surface. **If so, what is** the meaning when **the verse states "scroll"?** This teaches: **Just as a scroll is neither alive nor food, so too,** a bill of divorce may be written on **any object that is neither alive nor food.** This is why Rabbi Yosei HaGelili deems invalid a bill of divorce written on a living being.

Rabbi Yose HaGalili takes this further and rules that one may not write a *get* on a living creature at all — *baalei chayim* — not only that one may not cut from it. The *Gemara* proceeds to derive both his position and that of the *Rabbanan*.

### Sefer Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן – אִי כְּתִיב "בְּסֵפֶר", כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ; הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב "סֵפֶר" – לִסְפִירַת דְּבָרִים הוּא דַּאֲתָא. וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי "וְכָתַב" מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ "בִּכְתִיבָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, וְאֵינָהּ מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת בְּכֶסֶף" – סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, אַקֵּישׁ יְצִיאָה לַהֲוָיָיהּ: מָה הֲוָיָיה בְּכֶסֶף, אַף יְצִיאָה נָמֵי בְּכֶסֶף; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who disagree and say that a bill of divorce may be written even on a living creature or on food, interpret the verse? They contend: **If** the verse **were written:** And he shall write for her **in the scroll [*besefer*],** then it would be **as you said,** and it would indicate the type of surface on which the bill of divorce may be written. **Now that it is written: "Scroll [*sefer*]," it comes to** teach that a mere **account of the matters [*sefirat devarim*]** is required. In other words, *sefer* is referring not to the surface on which a bill of divorce must be written, but rather, to the essence of the bill of divorce. The verse teaches that the bill of divorce must contain particular content.
>
> The Gemara continues: **And the Rabbis, what do they do with this** phrase in the verse: **"And he writes** her"? The Gemara answers: **For them,** that phrase **is required** to teach the principle that a woman **is divorced** only **via writing,** i.e., a bill of divorce, **and she is not divorced via** giving **money.** It might **enter your mind to say: I should juxtapose leaving** marriage, i.e., divorce, **to becoming** married, i.e., betrothal, and I will say that **just as becoming** married **is** effected **with** giving **money, so too, leaving** marriage can **also** be effected **with** giving **money.** Therefore, the Torah **teaches us:** "And he writes her"; divorce can be effected only with a written bill of divorce.

The derivation for Rabbi Yose HaGalili runs as follows. The *pasuk* says *ve-kasav lah sefer kritut* — "and he shall write for her a *sefer* of severance." *Ein li ela sefer* — I would know only a scroll. *Minayin lerabbos kol davar* — how do I know to include any material at all? *Talmud lomar ve-kasav lah* — the word *ve-kasav*, "and he shall write," teaches that writing on any material is valid. *Im kein mah talmud lomar sefer* — if so, what does the word *sefer* come to add? It teaches: *mah sefer davar she-ein bo ruach chayim ve-eino ochel* — just as a scroll is something that is not a living creature and is not food, so too any material used for a *get* must be neither living nor food.

Tosafot adds a brief but important methodological observation: the selection of which characteristic of a *sefer* to use as the defining criterion — not that it must be rectangular, or must be made from vegetation, but specifically that it is not alive and not food — is determined by the sages according to what is contextually most relevant. The sages determine, in each instance, what constitutes the meaningful point of comparison. That is not a mechanical derivation but a rabbinic judgment about contextual relevance.

For the *Rabbanan*, who do not limit the material in the same way as Rabbi Yose HaGalili, the word *ve-kasav* serves a different purpose entirely. The *Gemara* asks: *mai avid leh* — what do the *Rabbanan* do with *ve-kasav*? They use it to teach that *bechtiva mitgarshet ve'eina mitgarshet bekesef* — she is divorced through writing, not through money. One might have thought — *salka da'atach amina* — that since marriage is effected through money, divorce could be effected through money as well, by analogy: *mah havaya bekesef, af yetzia nami bekesef*. The word *ve-kasav* therefore teaches that only a written document can effectuate a *get*, not a monetary conveyance.

### Krit/Kritut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ"סֵּפֶר כְּרִיתוּת" – סֵפֶר כּוֹרְתָהּ, וְאֵין דָּבָר אַחֵר כּוֹרְתָהּ. וְאִידַּךְ – מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ "דָּבָר הַכּוֹרֵת בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ", כִּדְתַנְיָא: "הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁתִּי יַיִן"; "עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא תֵּלְכִי לְבֵית אָבִיךְ"; "לְעוֹלָם" – אֵין זֶה כְּרִיתוּת. "עַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם" – הֲרֵי זֶה כְּרִיתוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, from where does he derive that a bill of divorce must contain particular content? **He derives it from** the phrase **"scroll of severance,"** which teaches that **a scroll,** i.e., a written document, **severs her** from her husband **and nothing else severs her** from him.
>
> The Gemara asks: **And** those who hold **the other** opinion, the Rabbis, how do they explain this phrase? **For them,** the phrase "scroll of severance" **is required** to teach that a bill of divorce must be **a matter that severs** all connection **between him and her, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a man says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce, on the condition that you will not** ever **drink wine,** or **on condition that you will never go to your father's house, that is not severance,** and the bill of divorce is not valid. If a bill of divorce imposes a condition upon the woman that permanently binds her to her husband, her relationship with her husband has not been completely severed, which is a prerequisite for divorce. If, however, he imposes a condition **until thirty days** have passed, or for any other limited period of time, **that is severance.** The bill of divorce is valid, as the relationship will be completely terminated at the end of the thirty-day period.

The dispute then extends to the derivation of *kritut* — complete severance. Rabbi Yehuda derives from the word *sefer* itself that the physical material must be limited, and derives the requirement of complete severance from the word *kritut* as opposed to *krit*. *Sefer karata ve'ein davar acher karata* — only a *sefer* creates the divorce, not another thing. The *Rabbanan* derive the material limitation differently, using *sefer* for one purpose and *ve-kasav* for another.

What both agree upon is that the word *kritut* — or the contrast between *krit* and *kritut* — teaches the requirement of *davar ha-koret beino leiveinah* — something that creates complete severance between the husband and wife. This is illustrated by the *baraita*: if a husband says "here is your *get* on condition that you never drink wine" or "on condition that you never visit your father's house," *ein ze kritut* — this is not a *get*, because the condition, being permanent, does not create a complete severance. But if the condition is for thirty days, *harei ze kritut* — it is valid, because the restriction is not permanent and the severance is therefore complete.

The question of what constitutes *mechusar ketzitza* — requiring an act of cutting that disqualifies the *get* — is left with some complexity. The *Gemara*'s language implies that even a voluntary, unnecessary act of cutting from a major object would disqualify the *get*, not only a case where cutting was required. A trivial trimming of edges presents no issue. The serious case is where the *get* is cut away from a large piece of material. Tosafot suggests that this concern may be limited to cases where one cuts from something alive and growing — like a leaf from a tree or a horn from a cow — because that constitutes a major transformation of the material, from something organic and living to something inert. Cutting paper from paper, even from a very large sheet, may not rise to the same level of concern. Tosafot does not resolve this definitively, however, and leaves it as an open question whether a *get* cut from a large inanimate sheet would be valid.
