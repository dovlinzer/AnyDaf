# Yevamot 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ten Categories

### Initial List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲשָׂרָה אֵין חוֹלְקִין לָהֶם תְּרוּמָה בְּבֵית הַגֳּרָנוֹת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה, וְקָטָן, טוּמְטוּם, וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס, וְהָעֶבֶד, וְהָאִשָּׁה, וְהֶעָרֵל, וְהַטָּמֵא, וְנוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ. וְכוּלָּן מְשַׁגְּרִין לָהֶם לְבָתֵּיהֶם, חוּץ מִטָּמֵא וְנוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There are **ten** types of priests **to whom one may not distribute *teruma* in the granary, and they are: A deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, a person whose sexual organs are concealed [*tumtum*], and a hermaphrodite, and a slave, and a woman, and an uncircumcised man, and a ritually impure man, and** one who **marries a woman** who is **unfit for him,** i.e., who is unfit to marry a priest. **And** with regard to **all of them, one may send** *teruma* **to them, to their homes, with the exception of a ritually impure man and** one who **marries a woman** who is **unfit for him.**

The *gemara* opens a *baraita* that emerges directly from the preceding discussion about the *kohen* and the slave of a *kohen* whose identities became confused — specifically, the question of whether one may distribute *terumah* to a slave in the absence of a *kohen* without creating the misleading impression that the slave is himself a *kohen*. With that context in mind, *tanu rabbanan*: there are ten categories of people who, although they possess a right to *terumah*, are not given their portion at the threshing floor. The *baraita* then proceeds to enumerate them: the *cheresh*, the *shoteh*, and the *katan*; the *tumtum* and the *androgynos*; the slave; the woman; the *arel* and the *tamei kohen*; and finally, a *kohen* who has married a woman he is forbidden to marry — for instance, a *gerushah*.

The *baraita* then adds that for all of these individuals, one does send the *terumah* to their homes, since they are in fact entitled to *terumah* and permitted to eat it. The exceptions are the *tamei* and the *kohen* married to an unsuitable woman, who are excluded even from home delivery. The reasons behind each category require the *gemara*'s elaboration, since it is not immediately obvious why people who are fully entitled to *terumah* should be denied it at the threshing floor.

### Cheresh, Shoteh, Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן — לָאו בְּנֵי דֵּיעָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** *teruma* may not be distributed to **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** as **they are not competent,** and it is unbecoming to give them *teruma* in public.

The *gemara* begins its analysis with the first group: *bishlama cheresh shoteh ve-katan* — understandable. *Lav benei dei'a inhu*, they are not people of mature intellect. But what exactly follows from that? Rashi explains that it is a matter of *ziluta de-terumah le-chalek lahem be-goren le-ein kol* — it is undignified for *terumah* to be distributed to them publicly at the threshing floor. The *goren* is a setting where all can observe who is receiving *terumah*, and handing it over to people whom society does not regard with full respect is seen as disrespectful to the sanctity of *terumah* itself. This is, of course, a striking basis for the ruling, since the concern is less about the legal standing of these individuals and more about how their receipt of *terumah* would be perceived by onlookers.

Tosafot raises a different concern. If the *cheresh* and *shoteh* lack mental competence, perhaps they will fail to guard the *terumah* in a state of *taharah* — which would seem to be the real problem. Yet the *baraita* itself establishes that you do send *terumah* to their homes, which seems to undercut that concern entirely. Tosafot resolves this by noting that presumably such individuals have an *apitropas* — a guardian — overseeing them at home. Even so, says Tosafot, you do not distribute to them at the *gronot* because there may be occasions when they arrive without their guardian. According to Tosafot, then, the *gronot* concern cannot primarily be about their inability to preserve the *terumah*'s *taharah*, since that concern applies at home as well. The reasoning that ultimately prevails in the *gronot* setting must be something else — and here Tosafot presumably would concur with Rashi's account of *ziluta*.

### Tumtum, Androgynous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס נָמֵי — בְּרִיָּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָּהּ נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is **also** inappropriate to distribute *teruma* to **a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite,** as **they are** each an unusual **creature of** their **own** kind.

Regarding the *tumtum* and the *androgynos*, the *gemara* explains: *biryah bifnei atzmo hinhu* — they are their own distinct category of creature, falling outside the standard gender binary. Rashi frames this again as *ziluta de-kodshim* — the same principle of unseemly public distribution. People perceive these individuals as unusual, and because they do not fit neatly into the recognized social categories, distributing *terumah* to them publicly at the *goren* is not regarded as befitting the sanctity of *terumah*.

This is a recurring theme in *hilchos kodshim* more broadly — the *kohanim* with physical blemishes are excluded from Temple service; everything associated with the *Beis Hamikdash* must conform to an aesthetic ideal of wholeness and dignity. There is a real tension here, since *kedushah* is presumably about internal quality, yet the *halacha* here accommodates prevailing social perceptions rather than correcting them. The *gemara* proceeds without pausing to resolve this tension; it simply legislates within the reality of how society actually functions.

### Slaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶבֶד נָמֵי — דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאַסּוֹקֵי מִתְּרוּמָה לְיוּחֲסִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a slave** it is **also** clear, since if he is given ***teruma*, perhaps** the court **will come to elevate** him **to** the presumptive status of priestly **lineage.**

The *eved* presents no difficulty: *dilma ati le-asuki mi-terumah le-yuchsin* — people will observe a slave receiving *terumah* at the threshing floor and conclude that he must be a free *kohen*, with the potential that they will then consider marrying him off as such. This is precisely the concern that gave rise to the *baraita* in the first place.

### Arel, Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָרֵל וְטָמֵא — מִשּׁוּם דִּמְאִיסִי. נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ — מִשּׁוּם קְנָסָא. אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא?
>
> **Translation:** *Teruma* may not be distributed to **an uncircumcised man and a ritually impure man, because** these situations **are repulsive** and it is unseemly to give them *teruma* in public. One may not distribute *teruma* to **one who marries a woman unfit for him, due to a penalty** that expropriates his priestly rights as long as he persists in his transgression. **But** for **what reason** is *teruma* **not** distributed to **a woman?**

Regarding the *arel* and the *tamei*, the *gemara* offers a different explanation: *mishum de-me'isi* — there is something abhorrent about it. These individuals are not in a condition that permits them to eat *terumah* at this moment, and handing *terumah* to someone who cannot eat it creates an inherently discordant situation. There is a meaningful distinction between this category and the earlier ones. *Ziluta* is a matter of social perception — how the act of distribution appears to onlookers. *Me'isi*, by contrast, may carry something more objective: it is the essential incompatibility of the transaction itself, giving *terumah* to someone for whom it is currently prohibited, regardless of whether anyone is watching. One might observe that *ziluta* concerns what people see, while *me'isi* concerns what the thing itself is — even if the status of the *arel* is known only to himself.

This distinction also illuminates why the *arel* receives his *terumah* at home even though the *tamei* does not. The *arel* is *anus* — his condition is entirely beyond his control; if his brothers died in *bris milah* he is a hemophiliac and bears no responsibility for his status. Why should he be penalized? You therefore send it to his home, where he can distribute it to his wife, children, and slaves who are permitted to eat it. The *tamei*, one might think, deserves the same treatment — he too may have become *tamei* in the course of fulfilling a *mitzvah*, such as burying a relative. Yet the *gemara* answers: *hai nafush unsei ve-hai lo nafush unsei* — the degree to which the *arel*'s condition is beyond his control is absolute and permanent, whereas the *tamei* can, in principle, immerse in a *mikvah*. As a general communal statement underscoring how important it is for *kohanim* to be zealous about maintaining *taharah*, there is value in not sending *terumah* to the *tamei* even at home — not necessarily as a condemnation of every individual case, but as a collective posture conveying that the community takes *tumah* seriously.

### Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. חַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם גְּרוּשָׁה. וְחַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם יִחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, disagree** on this issue. **One said** that it is **due to** the case of an Israelite woman who was married to a priest and got divorced, thereby losing her permission to partake of *teruma*. *Teruma* is not distributed to women in public at all, lest this **divorcée** continue to receive *teruma*. **And** the other **one said** that it is **due to** the concern that the owner and the woman might **be alone together** in the granary.

The remaining category is the woman, and the *gemara* notes that she does not fit any of the obvious explanations. She occupies a recognized place within the social order, she is fully *bat dei'a*, and she falls squarely within the standard gender binary. So *isha mai ta'ama lo*?

*Pligi bah Rav Pappa ve-Rav Huna breih de-Rav Yehoshua*. One says the concern is *mishum gerushah*: if you begin distributing *terumah* at the threshing floor to the wife or daughter of a *kohen*, people may continue to give it to her even after she has been divorced and lost her entitlement — either because they don't realize she has been divorced, or simply because the habit of giving has formed. The other says the concern is *mishum yichud*: a woman should not be going out to the threshing floor, an isolated location, in the manner that Boaz and Ruth illustrates — it is simply improper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ בֵּי דָרֵי דִּמְקָרַב לְמָתָא, וְלָא שְׁכִיחִי בְּהוּ אִינָשֵׁי. אִי נָמֵי: דִּמְרַחַק, וּשְׁכִיחִי בֵּהּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is in the case of **a granary that is close to town but is not frequented by people.** Because it is close to town, the owner of the granary would know if she was divorced. However, since there are not many people there, the concern about their being alone together remains. **Alternatively,** there is a practical difference in the case of a granary **that is distant but is frequented by people.** There, there is concern that the owner of the granary might not know if she was divorced, but the concern that they might be alone together does not exist.

The *gemara* identifies a practical difference between these two positions. Consider a threshing floor that is *de-makrav le-mata* — located close to the city — *ve-lo shchichi bah inshi* — but where there is not much foot traffic at the site itself. If the concern is *gerushah*, there is no problem: because the floor is close to the city, the woman is known there, people are aware of her marital status, and no one will mistakenly give her *terumah* after she is divorced. But because the site itself is quiet, there remains a *yichud* concern. Conversely, a floor that is distant from the city — so people do not know the woman's status — but has heavy traffic, presents no *yichud* concern while retaining a *gerushah* concern.

## Distribution Methods

### Tamei Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן מְשַׁגְּרִין לָהֶם לְבָתֵּיהֶן, חוּץ מִטָּמֵא וְנוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ. אֲבָל עָרֵל מְשַׁגְּרִינַן לֵיהּ, מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the *baraita* under discussion: **And** with regard to **all of them, one may send** *teruma* **to them, to their homes, with the exception of a ritually impure man and** one **who marries a woman unfit for him.** The Gemara infers: **However, to an uncircumcised man one may send** it. **What is the reason?** How does he differ from an impure man?

Returning to the rule that all ten categories receive their *terumah* at home — except the *tamei* and the *kohen* married to an unsuitable woman — the *gemara* presses on the *tamei*. The *kohen* married to a forbidden woman is comprehensible: withholding even home delivery functions as a communal sanction, signaling societal disapproval and applying pressure for him to address the situation. But the *tamei*? Unlike the case of *tumah* being a permanent or semi-permanent state, a *tamei kohen* may well be able to immerse today and become *tahor* by nightfall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲנִיס. טָמֵא נָמֵי, הָא אֲנִיס? הַאי נְפִישׁ אוּנְסֵיהּ, וְהַאי לָא נְפִישׁ אוּנְסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: One may send him *teruma*. It is **because of circumstances beyond his control,** i.e., the death of his brothers from their circumcision, that he was not circumcised. The Gemara asks: **Isn't an impure man also** in his state due to **circumstances beyond his control?** Why is *teruma* not sent to him? The Gemara answers: **This** man is uncircumcised because of **circumstances entirely beyond his control,** as circumcision is considered life-threatening for him, **whereas that** impure man **is not under circumstances entirely beyond his control,** as one can protect himself from ritual impurity.

The *gemara*'s answer — *hai nafush unsei ve-hai lo nafush unsei* — draws the line between a condition that is categorically beyond one's control (the *arel*) and one that, however understandable in any given instance, admits at least theoretically of remedy. The withholding of home delivery from the *tamei* is not so much a judgment of each individual case as it is a collective statement of communal seriousness about *tahara* among *kohanim*.

### General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָעֶבֶד וְהָאִשָּׁה, אֵין חוֹלְקִין לָהֶם תְּרוּמָה בְּבֵית הַגֳּרָנוֹת. וּבְמָקוֹם שֶׁחוֹלְקִין — נוֹתְנִין לָאִשָּׁה תְּחִלָּה וּפוֹטְרִין אוֹתָהּ מִיָּד. מַאי קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may not distribute *teruma* to a slave or a woman** if they are **in the granary. And in a place where** people do **distribute** it to them, **the woman is given first and released immediately.** The Gemara asks: **What is** this **saying?** If one may not distribute *teruma* to them, how can there be a place where it is distributed?

The *gemara* then introduces a second *baraita*: *ha-eved ve-ha-isha ein cholkin lahem terumah be-veit ha-granot* — the slave and the woman do not receive *terumah* at the threshing floor, for the reasons already discussed. This much is simply a restatement. What follows, however, is more complex.

### Women, Ma'aser Ani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁחוֹלְקִין מַעְשַׂר עָנִי — נוֹתְנִין לְאִשָּׁה תְּחִלָּה. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם זִילוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that this statement is not referring to *teruma*. **This is what it is saying: In a case where the poor man's tithe is distributed** to the poor from the owner's house, **the woman is given first. What is the reason?** She is given the tithe first **because** it is **demeaning** for a woman to have to wait in the company of men for a lengthy period of time.

The *baraita* continues: *u-ve-makom she-cholkin, notnim la-isha techila u-potrin otam mi-yad* — in a place where they do distribute, they give it to the woman first and send her off immediately. The *gemara* pauses: surely the *baraita* just said you don't give it to women at the threshing floor — so what does it mean to say "in a place where they do give"?

*Hachi kamar*: the reference is not to *terumah* at all. The *baraita* means: in a case where one distributes *ma'aser ani*, you give it to the woman first. *Ma'aser ani* — the poor tithe of the third and sixth years — is owed to impoverished women just as to men, and the concern about *gerushah* simply does not apply, since a woman's entitlement to *ma'aser ani* has nothing to do with her marital status. If the concern driving the *terumah* restriction was *gerushah*, that concern drops away entirely here.

What about *yichud*? Here too, *ma'aser ani* is different: Rashi explains that *ma'aser ani* is distributed not at the threshing floor but *be-toch ha-bayit ba-ir* — within the city — as derived from the verse *ve-achlu vish'arecha ve-save'u*, "they shall eat within your gates," meaning in the city rather than out in the fields. In the city, people are present, the setting is populated, and there is no *yichud* concern. Rashi adds a further reason: *mishum de-lo lichsefu aniyim* — so that the poor are not embarrassed. The precise sense of this last point is not entirely transparent; one might suggest that it means the poor should not have to journey out to the fields and thereby advertise their poverty to the broader public, though Rashi's formulation leaves room for further interpretation.

### Women Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מֵרֵישָׁא, כִּי הֲווֹ אָתוּ גַּבְרָא וְאִתְּתָא לְדִינָא קַמַּאי, הֲוָה שָׁרֵינָא תִּיגְרָא דְגַבְרָא בְּרֵישָׁא. אָמֵינָא: דְּמִיחַיַּיב בְּמִצְוֹת. כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהָא, שָׁרֵינָא תִּיגְרָא דְּאִתְּתָא בְּרֵישָׁא. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם זִילוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Initially, when a man and a woman would come for judgment before me,** each for a different case, **I would resolve the man's quarrel first. I would say that** since **he is obligated in** many positive **mitzvot** I should not waste his time by causing him to wait. However, **since I heard this** *baraita*, **I resolve the woman's quarrel first. What is the reason?** I resolve her quarrel first **because** it is **demeaning** for her to be waiting in the company of men.

Once it is established that women do receive *ma'aser ani* in the city, the *baraita* specifies that they receive it first. *Mah ta'ama mishum ziluta* — because it is unseemly for a woman to be made to stand and wait among a crowd of men. Having established that there is no *yichud* and no *gerushah* concern in this context, propriety requires that the distribution be handled quickly and the woman sent on her way.

Rava adds a striking personal observation. He says: *merish ki havu atu gavra ve-itata le-dina kamei, hava sharina tigra de-gavra be-reisha* — initially, when a man and a woman would come before him for a court case, he would attend to the man's case first, reasoning that the man's greater obligation in *mitzvot* confers a kind of priority status. *Keivan de-shamana lah le-ha* — once he heard this ruling about giving priority to women in the distribution of *ma'aser ani* — *sharina tigra de-itata be-reisha*, he changed his practice and began hearing the woman's case first. *Mah ta'ama mishum ziluta* — because making a woman stand and wait in such a public setting is itself a form of disrespect. Men are more habituated to the public sphere and to waiting in such contexts; women are not, and the norms of society make their prolonged presence in such settings uncomfortable for all. Rava draws a direct halachic inference from a procedural rule about agricultural distribution and applies it to judicial practice — a remarkable illustration of how sensitivity to *kavod ha-briot* can migrate across domains.

## Kohen-Slave Mix

### Forced Release

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגְדִּילוּ הַתַּעֲרוֹבוֹת וְכוּ׳. שִׁיחְרְרוּ, אִי בָּעֵי — אִין, אִי לָא בָּעֵי — לָא, וְאַמַּאי? לִישָּׂא שִׁפְחָה אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל, בַּת חוֹרִין אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל! אָמַר רָבָא, אֵימָא: כּוֹפִין אוֹתָן, וּמְשַׁחְרְרִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna: If **the mixed** sons **matured** and **freed** each other, they may marry women fit for the priesthood. The use of the past tense indicates that this *halakha* applies after the fact. **If one** of the sons **desires** to free the other, he may, but **if he does not desire** to do so, he is **not** obligated. **And why** not? **Neither** of them **can marry a maidservant** in case he is a priest, **nor can** either of them marry **a free woman,** as he might be a slave. They are therefore unable to fulfill the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply in their current state and should be obligated to free each other. **Rava said: Say** that the mishna means that **we coerce them and they free each other.**

The *gemara* now returns to the central case that has run through this section: two infants — one the son of a *kohen* and one the slave of that *kohen* — who were mixed up, so that neither can be identified with certainty. The *mishnah* had discussed the scenario in which these two men, now grown, might free one another. The *gemara* probes the language: the *mishnah* says *she-yesh la-hem*, which might suggest that freeing one another is optional. But *amar Rava*: *eima kofin otan u-mishachrin zeh es zeh* — read it as compulsory. The *mishnah* means they are compelled to free one another. The rationale is that neither one can currently marry: he cannot marry a slave woman because he might be a *kohen*, and he cannot marry a free woman because he might be a slave. Such an intolerable state of affairs compels the master to free the slave, as is the rule in analogous cases throughout the *gemara*. The mechanism is a *tenai* — whichever of the two is actually the slave is freed by the other.

### Mincha Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹתְנִין עֲלֵיהֶם חוּמְרֵי וְכוּ׳. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְמִנְחָתָם נִקְמֶצֶת כְּמִנְחַת יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת כְּמִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים. הָא כֵּיצַד? הַקּוֹמֶץ קָרֵב בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְהַשִּׁירַיִם קְרֵיבִין בְּעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna that **we place upon them** both **the stringencies of priests and the stringencies of Israelites.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this stated, beyond those cited specifically in the mishna? **Rav Pappa said:** It is stated with regard **to their meal-offering: The handful is taken from it like the meal-offering of an Israelite,** unlike that of a priest, which is burned in its entirety. **However,** the offering **does not get eaten, like the meal-offering of priests. How so?** How is the practice performed so that both stringencies are kept? **The handful is sacrificed** and burned **by itself, and the remainder** of the offering **is offered by itself.**

After the manumission, the *gemara* establishes that both men are treated with the stringencies of *kohanim* and the stringencies of *Yisrael*. The *gemara* asks: *le-mai hilchasa* — to what practical *halachah* does this double stringency apply, beyond what has already been spelled out?

*Amar Pappa*: *le-minchasam nikmetzes ke-minchas Yisrael, ve-einan ne'echeles ke-minchas kohanim* — regarding their *minchah* offering. When a *Yisrael* brings a *minchah*, the *kohen* takes the *kometz* — the handful — and burns it on the *mizbe'ach*, while the remainder (*shiyarayim*) is eaten by the *kohanim*. When a *kohen* brings a *minchah*, the entire offering is burned: *ve-chol minchas kohen kalil tihyeh, lo te'achel*. What, then, is done with the *minchah* of a person who is uncertain whether he is a *kohen* or a *Yisrael*?

If you perform the *kemitzah* and then eat the remainder, you may have treated as a *minchas Yisrael* something that is actually a *minchas kohen* and should have been fully burned. If you burn the entire thing, you may have burned the remainder of what is actually a *minchas Yisrael*, in which case the remainder ought to have been eaten by *kohanim* rather than burned. The *gemara* proposes: *ha keitzad* — how is it done? The *kometz* is burned first, by itself. The *shiyarayim* is then burned separately. But immediately the *gemara* raises an objection: *kol she-mimenu le-ishim harei hu be-val taktirun* — once any part of an offering has been placed on the altar fire, the remainder is prohibited from being burned on the altar. Burning the *shiyarayim* on the *mizbe'ach* would thus appear to violate this prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיקְּרִי כָּאן: כֹּל שֶׁמִּמֶּנּוּ לָאִישִּׁים, הֲרֵי הוּא בְּ״בַל תַּקְטִירוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can it be performed in this manner? There is a principle that should **apply here,** that **whatever is partly** burned **in** the **fire** on the altar **is subject to** the prohibition of **"you may not make…as an offering"** (Leviticus 2:11). This principle states that if part of an item, e.g., the blood of an animal offering or the handful of a meal-offering, is burned on the altar, then burning any of its other parts, which are not designated for burning, is prohibited. How, then, can the remainder of the meal-offering be sacrificed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: דְּמַסֵּיק לְהוּ לְשׁוּם עֵצִים, כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: ״לְרֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ״ אִי אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה לְשׁוּם עֵצִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, said that** the remainder **is brought up** to the altar only **for the purpose of wood,** i.e., as fuel for the altar, not as an offering. In this manner, it is permitted. This answer is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar said:** "But they shall not come up **for a sweet savor** on the altar" (Leviticus 2:12). This verse indicates that **you may not bring up** leaven and honey as a "sweet savor," i.e., an offering. **However, you may bring up** leaven, and honey, and other materials **for the purpose of wood.**

*Amar Rav Yehuda breih de-Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi*: the solution is *de-masik leihu le-shem eitzim* — one places the *shiyarayim* on the altar not as a sacrificial offering but as fuel. By stipulating that the act is one of fueling the altar fire rather than offering a *korban*, the *halakhic* character of the act changes, and the prohibition of *val taktirun* is not violated. This is, as noted, a powerful illustration of how *kavanah* — intention — defines the nature of an act on the *mizbe'ach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּעָבֵיד לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַקּוֹמֶץ קָרֵב לְעַצְמוֹ, וְהַשִּׁירַיִם מִתְפַּזְּרִין עַל בֵּית הַדֶּשֶׁן. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן לָא פְּלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֶלָּא בְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים, דְּבַת הַקְרָבָה הִיא, אֲבָל הָכָא — אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to Rabbi Elazar. However, according to the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Elazar and hold that it may not be burned for the purpose of fuel, **what can be said?** What is to be done with the remainder? The Gemara answers **that** the offering **is treated in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: The handful is sacrificed by itself, and the remainder is scattered over the place of the ashes. And even the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, only with regard to a sinner's meal-offering that belongs to priests, as it is fit to be sacrificed** in its entirety. **However, here,** in the case of an uncertain priest, **even the Rabbis agree** that the remainder is scattered over the ashes, as it cannot be offered in case he is a non-priest.

The *gemara* raises a further complication: this solution works according to Rabbi Elizer, who accepts the principle of designating remnants as fuel in cases of uncertainty. But what of the Rabbis, who reject that principle? *Avid lei ke-Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon* — the *gemara* invokes a related ruling by Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, which concerns the *minchas chotei* of a *kohen* — a *minchah* brought as a *chattas*. The question there is whether the entire offering must be burned, as with an ordinary *minchas kohen*, or whether a *kemitzah* is taken. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon holds that the *kemitzah* is taken but the *shiyarayim* are neither burned on the altar nor eaten; instead, they are scattered on the *beis ha-deshen* — the ash pile located beside the ramp of the altar — which is where disqualified *korbanos* are disposed of.

The *gemara* concludes that even the Rabbis would agree in our case of the *safek kohen safek Yisrael*. Their dispute with Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon concerns only the particulars of the *minchas chotei* of an actual *kohen* — where they hold that the *shiyarayim* should in fact be offered on the altar because it is a *bas hakrava*, a fully offered sacrifice. But in the present case, where one is genuinely uncertain and it is conceded that the *shiyarayim* cannot be eaten (lest the person be a *kohen*) and cannot be offered as a sacrificial remnant on the altar (due to *val taktirun*), even the Rabbis would concede that the *shiyarayim* are scattered on the *beis ha-deshen*, as is done with *pasul korbanos*.

## Paternity Unknown

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁלֹּא שָׁהֲתָה אַחַר בַּעֲלָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, וְנִשֵּׂאת, וְיָלְדָה, וְאֵין יָדוּעַ אִם בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה לָרִאשׁוֹן אִם בִּן שִׁבְעָה לָאַחֲרוֹן — הָיוּ לָהּ בָּנִים מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן וּבָנִים מִן הַשֵּׁנִי, חוֹלְצִין וְלֹא מְיַיבְּמִין. וְכֵן הוּא לָהֶם — חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a woman **who did not wait three months after** separating from **her husband, and remarried and gave birth** to a son, **and it is not known if he was born after nine** months of pregnancy **to the former** husband or **if he was born after seven** months **to the latter** husband, if **she had sons** of certain patrilineage **from the first** husband **and sons** of certain patrilineage **from the second** one, and the son of uncertain patrilineage married and died childless, then the brothers from both husbands **must perform *ḥalitza*** with his wife, as they might be his paternal brothers. **But they may not perform levirate marriage** with her, in case he is only their maternal half brother, and his wife is forbidden to them. **And similarly,** with regard to **him and their** wives, if one of them dies childless, **he performs *ḥalitza* and not levirate marriage.**

The *gemara* turns to a fresh category of cases: *mi she-lo shahasa achar ba'alah shloshah chodashim* — a woman who did not wait the required three months after separating from her first husband before remarrying. The concern is that she may have been pregnant when she remarried, so that a child born within an ambiguous window — potentially nine months from the first husband or seven months from the second — has an uncertain paternity. The *mishnah* stipulates that she *nisas ve-yalda* — she remarried and gave birth — and *ein yadu'a ben tesha la-rishon im ben sheva la-acharon* — it cannot be determined whether the child is a nine-month child from the first or a seven-month child from the second.

The *mishnah* further specifies that she also had unambiguous children from both husbands — establishing a clear sibling relationship between the child of uncertain paternity and children from each household, on the maternal side.

### Both Chalitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ דַּוְקָא מִיחְלָץ וַהֲדַר יַבּוֹמֵי, אֲבָל יַבּוֹמֵי בְּרֵישָׁא — לָא, דְּקָא פָגַע בִּיבָמָה לַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna stated that if the son has paternal half brothers from each of his two uncertain fathers and he dies childless, a half brother from one father performs *ḥalitza* and the other performs levirate marriage. The Gemara comments that ***ḥalitza* is specifically performed** first **and** only **afterward levirate marriage. However, levirate marriage is not** performed **first, as** that **would breach** the prohibition against **a *yevama*** engaging in intercourse **with** a member of **the public** in the event that she is not his *yevama* but rather the *yevama* of the other half brother.

When this son of uncertain paternity grows up, marries, and dies without children, the *mishnah* rules: *choltzin ve-lo meyabmin* — both sets of brothers must perform *chalitza*; none may perform *yibum*. This requires explanation. Why can one set not perform *chalitza* while the other performs *yibum*?

Consider the scenario: the brothers from husband one's household are definitely the maternal brothers of the deceased. The brothers from husband two's household are also definitely his maternal brothers. Neither set knows with certainty that they are also his paternal brothers. If one set were to perform *yibum* — even conditionally, reasoning that if they are not the paternal brother, the other set has already released the widow through *chalitza* — there is a fatal flaw: the one who performs *yibum* may be the maternal brother and not the paternal brother. Marrying one's maternal brother's wife falls under the category of *arayos* — incestuous unions. Since maternal brotherhood is certain and paternal brotherhood is uncertain, none of them may risk performing *yibum*. Both sets of brothers must therefore perform *chalitza*.

### Arayos Risk

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-chein hu lahem, choleitz ve-lo meyabem* — and symmetrically, if one of the brothers from either household dies, the son of uncertain paternity himself performs only *chalitza* and not *yibum* with the widow. The reason is the same: he is certainly the maternal brother of that deceased man, and since he cannot be certain he is also the paternal brother, *yibum* would expose him to the *issur arayos* of marrying his maternal brother's wife. Only certainty of paternal brotherhood, which would also establish the obligation of *yibum*, could override the prohibition, and that certainty is absent.

## Multiple Siblings

### Different Mothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ לוֹ אַחִים מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן וְאַחִים מִן הַשֵּׁנִי, שֶׁלֹּא מֵאוֹתָהּ הָאֵם — הוּא חוֹלֵץ וּמְיַיבֵּם, וְהֵם — אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ וְאֶחָד מְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** If **he had** half **brothers from the first** husband **and** half **brothers from the second, not from the same mother** but from the same father, **he performs *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage** with their widows. If he is indeed their paternal half brother, then the widows are his *yevamot*; if not, he may marry them like any other man. **And** similarly, with regard to **them** and his wife, **one** half brother from one father **performs *ḥalitza* and one** from the other father **performs levirate marriage.**

The *mishnah* now introduces a variation: *hayu lo achim min ha-rishon ve-achim min ha-sheini she-lo me'osah ha-eim* — the first and second husbands each had children from *different* mothers. These half-brothers of the child of uncertain paternity are thus not his maternal brothers at all — they share only a possible paternal connection with him.

This changes the analysis entirely. Take, for example, a son of husband one born from a different woman. His relation to the child of uncertain paternity is: possibly his paternal brother (if husband one was the father), but definitely not his maternal brother. If he is not the paternal brother, he is simply an unrelated man. There is therefore no *arayos* concern if he were to marry the widow: either he is the paternal brother and performs a proper *mitzvah* of *yibum*, or he is entirely unrelated to the deceased and the widow is simply a permitted woman to him.

### One Each Option

The *mishnah* rules accordingly: *hu choleitz u-meyabem* — the individual in question may perform either *chalitza* or *yibum*. And when it comes to the brothers collectively: *echad choleitz ve-echad meyabem* — one may perform *chalitza* and another may perform *yibum*. Crucially, the order matters: *chalitza* must come first, and only then may *yibum* follow. If the one who performs *chalitza* is in fact the true paternal brother, the widow is thereby released from the *yibum* obligation entirely and may remarry freely; the second man then simply marries her as any other woman. If the one who performs *chalitza* is not the paternal brother, his *chalitza* was meaningless, and the second man — who is the true paternal brother — performs *yibum* properly. Either way, the outcome is halachically sound.

## Kohen-Unsuitable

### Diet, Purity, Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה אֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶחָד כֹּהֵן — נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה רְאוּיָה לְכֹהֵן, וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים. וְאִם נִטְמָא — אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאִם אָכַל — אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ. וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק עַל הַגּוֹרֶן. וּמוֹכֵר הַתְּרוּמָה, וְהַדָּמִים שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** of his two uncertain fathers **was an Israelite and one** was **a priest, he may marry** only **a woman fit to** marry **a priest,** due to the possibility that he is a priest. **And he may not become ritually impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse** because he might be a priest. **But if he became impure, he does not receive the forty** lashes, as he might be a non-priest. Likewise, **he does not partake of *teruma*,** in case he is a non-priest. **However, if he ate** *teruma* **he does not pay** the **principal and the** additional **fifth,** as he might be a priest. **And he does not receive** *teruma* **at the granary. However, he may sell the *teruma*** of his own produce **and the money is his.** It cannot be taken away from him due to the uncertainty with regard to his status.

The *mishnah* turns to the child of uncertain paternity whose two possible fathers are, respectively, a *kohen* and a *Yisrael*. Since he might be a *kohen*, he is required to marry only a woman who is suitable for a *kohen* — *nosei ishah re'uyah le-kohen*. Similarly, he may not become *tamei le-meitim*, since he might be a *kohen* who is Biblically prohibited from corpse *tumah*. Yet if he does become *tamei*, he does not receive lashes — *eino sofeg et ha-arba'im* — because he might be a *Yisrael* to whom that prohibition does not apply, and in the absence of certainty there can be no punitive lashes.

He may not eat *terumah* — *eino ochel bi-trumah* — because he might be a *Yisrael*. Yet if he does eat it, he does not pay the *keren ve-chomesh* — the principal plus a fifth — that a non-*kohen* who eats *terumah* inadvertently is required to pay, because he might be a *kohen* who was entitled to eat it. He does not receive a portion at the threshing floor — *eino cholek al ha-goren* — for the reasons established earlier. However, *terumah* that he himself separates from his own produce belongs to him in the sense that one cannot prove he is not a *kohen*; since he may not eat it but owns it, he sells it and retains the proceeds — *ve-ha-damim shelo*. He does not receive a portion in *kodshei kodashim* — *eino cholek be-kodshei kodashim* — since he cannot prove entitlement, nor is he given priestly gifts.

### Bechor Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק בְּקׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים. וְאֵין נוֹתְנִים לוֹ אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים, וְאֵין מוֹצִיאִין אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ מִיָּדוֹ. וּפָטוּר מִן הַזְּרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּיבָה. וּבְכוֹרוֹ יְהֵא רוֹעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב. וְנוֹתְנִין עָלָיו חוּמְרֵי כֹּהֲנִים וְחוּמְרֵי יִשְׂרְאֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And he does not receive a share of the sacred of the consecrated** offerings, **and one may not give him the consecrated** offerings to sacrifice. **However,** the hides of **his own** offerings **may not be appropriated from his possession. And he is exempt from** giving a priest **the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw** of his non-consecrated animals. **And the firstling of his** animal **should graze until it becomes unfit** to be sacrificed because it gets a blemish. **And** in general, **we place upon him the stringencies of priests and the stringencies of Israelites.**

If someone gives him a *bechor* — a firstborn animal — it is not taken away from him, since he might be a *kohen* who is entitled to it. But since he might not be a *kohen*, he may not eat it while it is unblemished. The *mishnah* rules: *ye-hei ro'eh ad she-yista'ev* — it grazes until it develops a blemish, at which point a *kohen* of certain status may eat it. He is also exempt from the priestly gifts of *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* when he slaughters an animal — since he might be a *kohen* and *kohanim* do not give these gifts to themselves — and he may eat those portions himself, as they are not *kodshim*. The *mishnah* concludes, as with the earlier case: *ve-notnim lo chumrei kohanim ve-chumrei Yisra-elim*.

## Both Kohanim

### Onen, Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כֹּהֲנִים — הוּא אוֹנֵן עֲלֵיהֶם, וְהֵם אוֹנְנִים עָלָיו. הוּא אֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא לָהֶם, וְהֵם אֵינָן מְטַמְּאִים לוֹ. הוּא אֵינוֹ יוֹרֵשׁ אוֹתָן, אֲבָל הֵם יוֹרְשִׁין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **both** uncertain fathers **were priests,** then if they die **he is in** a state of **acute mourning over** each of **them,** in case the deceased is his father. **And** if he dies, **they are** both **in a** state of **acute mourning over him,** as one of them is his father. **He may not become ritually impure to** bury **them,** as each one may not be his relative, **and they may not become ritually impure to** bury **him** for the same reason. **He does not inherit** from **them,** as the heirs of both husbands can reject his claims. **However, they inherit** from **him** if he has no sons and split his inheritance equally.

If both possible fathers are *kohanim*, then the child is certainly a *kohen*, since the only circumstance in which a woman could legitimately have been married to a second *kohen* while the first was still alive would be that the first had divorced her — but if she is a *gerushah*, any child from the second *kohen* would be a *chalal*. For both possible sons to be valid *kohanim*, the first husband must have died and the woman remarried as a *widow*, not a *divorcée*. Certainty of *kohen* status is therefore established; what remains at issue is whose son he is.

When either of his two possible fathers dies, he is an *onen* — in the acute mourning status of the day of death — *mi-safek*, out of doubt, and therefore refrains from Temple service. Similarly, when he himself dies, both possible fathers must observe *onen* status, since for each of them he might be their son. Despite all this, he does not become *tamei* to either of them upon death — he might not be that man's son — and they do not become *tamei* to him, for the same reason.

Regarding inheritance: *hu eino yoresh otan* — he cannot inherit from either of them, because the heirs of each household tell him he cannot prove he is their father's son. *Ve-hem yorshim oto* — but if he dies without children, both potential fathers inherit from him, since one of them is certainly his father, and no third party has a superior claim. They divide the estate between them. If he strikes or curses either of his potential fathers, he is exempt from the Biblical prohibitions against striking or cursing one's father, since the paternity cannot be established.

### Temple Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפָטוּר עַל מַכָּתוֹ וְעַל קִלְלָתוֹ שֶׁל זֶה וְשֶׁל זֶה. עוֹלֶה בְּמִשְׁמָרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה וְשֶׁל זֶה, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק. אִם הָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם בְּמִשְׁמָר אֶחָד — נוֹטֵל חֵלֶק אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And he is exempt** from capital punishment **for striking and for cursing** both **this** father **and that one.** Although one who strikes or curses his father or mother is liable to receive the death penalty, he cannot be held liable, as it is unknown which of the men is his father. **He must ascend** to the Temple service **with the priestly watch of this** father **and of that one,** as he belongs to one of these watches and is obligated to serve with them. **However, he does not receive a share** of the portion of the offerings that gets eaten, as the members of each watch can claim that he is a member of the other watch. **If both** uncertain fathers **were in one priestly watch, he receives one share,** as he certainly belongs to that watch.

*Oleh be-mishmaro shel zeh ve-shel zeh* — he may perform Temple service during the *mishmar* week of either family, since one of them is certainly his, and no one can stop him from working. However, *ve-eino cholek* — he does not receive a portion of the *korbanos* distributed to that *mishmar*, since he cannot prove that this particular week is his. If, however, both potential fathers happened to be from the same *mishmar*, then he receives one portion — not two — since he has only one father, whoever that father is, and that father is certainly of this *mishmar*.

## Shtuki

### Shtuki Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עֲשָׂרָה כֹּהֲנִים עוֹמְדִים, וּפֵירַשׁ אֶחָד מֵהֶם, וּבָעַל — הַוָּלָד שְׁתוּקִי. מַאי ״שְׁתוּקִי״? אִילֵימָא שֶׁמְּשַׁתְּקִין אוֹתוֹ מִנִּכְסֵי אָבִיו — פְּשִׁיטָא! מִי יָדְעִינַן אֲבוּהּ מַנּוּ? אֶלָּא שֶׁמְּשַׁתְּקִין אוֹתוֹ מִדִּין כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said** that if **ten priests were standing** in one place, **and one of them,** who is unidentified, **left** the group **and engaged in intercourse** with a woman, and she gave birth, **the child is a silenced one,** i.e., a child whose father's identity is not known. The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the phrase **silenced one [*shetuki*]** in this regard? **If we say that he is silenced from** any claim to **his father's property,** this **is obvious; do we know who his father is? Rather,** it means **that he is silenced from the status of priesthood,** as well. Although his father certainly is a priest, he is not given this status.

The *gemara* now introduces the case of the *shtuki* in Shmuel's formulation. *Asarah kohanim omdim u-peirash echad meihen u-va'al* — ten *kohanim* were standing together, one of them went and had relations with a woman, and it is unknown which one. *Ve-ha-valad shtuki* — the child born of this encounter is a *shtuki*. The word itself carries the sense of being silenced or shut down: when the child asks "who is my father?" the answer is, in effect, "we don't know — be quiet."

The mere fact of uncertain paternity might seem to be a question only of inheritance or personal identification, not of fundamental status, since we know with certainty that the child is the son of some *kohen*. Why should the term *shtuki* carry the weight of a significant legal category?

### Genealogy Req.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיְתָה לּוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ אַחֲרָיו״, בָּעֵינַן זַרְעוֹ מְיוּחָס אַחֲרָיו, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers that the **verse states: "And it shall be to him and to his seed after him,** the covenant of an everlasting priesthood" (Numbers 25:13). It is derived from "and to his seed after him" that **we require** a priest's **descendants** to be **attributed to his lineage, and** here that is not the case, as **there is no** certain father.

The *gemara* clarifies: *ilima de-mashtakinan lei mi-nechasei de-aviv* — if it merely means he is deprived of his father's estate, that is obvious and needs no special ruling. The real *chiddush* of *shtuki* is *de-mashtakinan lei mi-dinei kehunah* — he is excluded from the privileges of *kehunah* proper, specifically from serving in the *Beis HaMikdash*, even though we know he is a *kohen* by blood.

The *gemara* derives this from the verse: *ve-hayesa lo u-le-zaro acharav bris kehunas olam* — "and it shall be to him and to his seed after him a covenant of eternal priesthood." *Ba'inan zaro meyuchas acharav* — we require that his lineage be traceable *after him*, meaning that a *kohen*'s descendant must be able to identify his father in order to serve. *Ve-leika* — and in the case of the *shtuki*, that identification is absent.

### Rishon Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה גַּבֵּי אַבְרָהָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״לִהְיוֹת לְךָ לֵאלֹהִים וּלְזַרְעֲךָ אַחֲרֶיךָ״, הָתָם מַאי קָא מַזְהַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא? הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא תִּנְסַב גּוֹיָה וְשִׁפְחָה, דְּלָא לֵיזִיל זַרְעָךְ בָּתְרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa strongly objects to this: However, if that is so, since** the same phrase **is written with regard to Abraham: "To be a God to you and to your seed after you"** (Genesis 17:7), **what is the Merciful One warning him there?** Can it possibly mean that one who cannot identify his parents is not obligated to serve God as a Jew? The Gemara answers that **this is what He said to him: You may not marry a gentile woman or a maidservant, so that** the status of **your offspring will not follow her** status, as the child of a Jewish man and a gentile woman or maidservant receives the status of his mother.

The *gemara* raises a challenge from an earlier *mishnah*. In the case discussed above — the child born during the ambiguous window whose mother married a second *kohen* after the first — the *mishnah* ruled that *rishon ra'ui lihayos Kohen Gadol* — even the first child, whose paternity between the two *kohanim* is uncertain, is considered fit to serve as *Kohen Gadol*. But if we require *zaro meyuchas acharav* for Temple service, how can someone who does not know which *kohen* was his father serve at all, let alone as *Kohen Gadol*? This appears to be a direct refutation of Shmuel's ruling.

### Znus Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַרְעוֹ מְיוּחָס אַחֲרָיו — דְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא. וְכִי גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן — בִּזְנוּת, בְּנִשּׂוּאִין לָא גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The requirement that **his descendants** be **attributed to his lineage is rabbinic** law, **and the verse is a mere support,** not the actual source. **And when the Sages decreed** that one whose father's identity is unknown is not a priest, they did so only **with regard to** a case of **licentious** intercourse. **With regard to** a case of **marriage,** as is the case in the *baraita*, **the Sages did not** apply the **decree.**

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *zera meyuchas acharav derabbanan u-kra asmachta be'alma* — the requirement of traceable genealogy for Temple service is rabbinic in origin, with the verse serving merely as an *asmachta*. The Rabbinic decree, however, was enacted only in cases involving *znus* — illicit sexual relations. *Ve-chi gazar rabbanan bi-znus?* — the question becomes: when the Rabbis enacted this disqualification, did they intend it to apply to cases of *znus* specifically, or more broadly?

The *gemara* asserts that the Rabbinic sanction applies specifically *bi-znus*: a child born of relations that were inherently illicit and outside the framework of marriage carries a more deeply compromised social and personal status, and it is there that the Rabbis imposed the disqualification from Temple service. *Be-nisu'in lo gazar rabbanan* — in cases of legitimate marriage, the Rabbis did not enact the disqualification. The child from the *mishnah*'s case was born to a woman who was legitimately married — first to husband one, then to husband two — without any *znus* involved. His uncertain paternity is a product of circumstances, not of illegitimacy. That child, therefore, can serve in the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Legit Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִזְנוּת מִי גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן? וְהָתְנַן: מִי שֶׁלֹּא שָׁהֲתָה אַחַר בַּעְלָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים וְנִשֵּׂאת וְיָלְדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And with regard to** a case of **licentious** intercourse, **did the Sages** in fact issue **a decree? Didn't we learn** in the mishna about a woman **who did not wait three months after** separating from **her husband and remarried and gave birth** to a son?

This resolution, however, creates a new problem. The *gemara* challenges: *u-vi-znus mi gazar rabbanan?* — is it actually true that the Rabbis imposed this disqualification in cases of *znus*? Consider our very *mishnah*: *mi she-lo shahasa achar ba'alah shloshah chodashim* — and the case is parsed carefully. The *mishnah* mentions *onen* status for both potential fathers when the child dies, implying that both are alive. If the first husband died, the wife is an *almana* and there is no *znus* at all. For both to be alive and for the child still to be a valid *kohen* on either side, the woman must not have been a *gerushah* — for if she were, the child from the second union would be a *chalal*. The only scenario in which both fathers are alive, the child is a *kohen* on either account, and she has not been divorced is if there was no prior marriage at all — meaning the first relationship was indeed *znus*.

Yet that *mishnah* rules that *oleh be-mishmaro shel zeh ve-shel zeh* — the child of uncertain paternity works in the Temple during the *mishmar* of either family. This appears to be a direct refutation: here is a child born of *znus* who is permitted to serve in the *Beis HaMikdash*, contradicting Shmuel's position.

### Kidushi Taus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אַחַר בַּעְלָהּ״? אִילֵימָא אַחַר מִיתַת בַּעְלָהּ, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הוּא אוֹנֵן עֲלֵיהֶם, וְהֵם אוֹנְנִים עָלָיו. בִּשְׁלָמָא הוּא אוֹנֵן עֲלֵיהֶם — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּנִשּׂוּאִין דְּשֵׁנִי וְלִיקּוּט עֲצָמוֹת דְּקַמָּא. אֶלָּא: הֵם אוֹנְנִים עָלָיו, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? קַמָּא הָא מִית לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **After** separating from **her husband? If we say** it means **after her husband's death, say the latter clause** of the mishna: If they die, **he is in a** state of **acute mourning over** each of **them, and** if he dies, **they are** both **in** a state of **acute mourning over him. Granted,** if they die, **he is in** a state of **acute mourning over them. You find** this case **with regard to** his uncertain father from his mother's **marriage to the second man.** If the second husband dies, the child must mourn for him, **and** he is also in a state of acute mourning following **the gathering of the bones of the first** husband, who died before he was born. When the bones of a person who was buried are dug up for proper burial in his ancestor's plot, his relatives mourn for him a second time. **But** with regard to the statement that if he dies **they are in** a state of **acute mourning for him, how can you find these** circumstances? **The first** husband **is** already **dead.**

The *gemara* offers one resolution: *amar Rav Shemaya be-mema'enes* — perhaps we are dealing with a case of *mi'un*, where the woman had been married to husband one as a minor, in a marriage contracted by her father or brothers, and she exercised her rabbinic right to repudiate it without a *get*. In that case, husband one is alive, she is not a *gerushah*, there was no *znus*, and yet both fathers could be alive. The difficulty with this answer is that a *mema'enes* — a minor girl young enough to exercise *mi'un* — is presumably too young to carry a pregnancy to term safely. As *Rav Bibi* taught before *Rav Nachman*: a *ketana* between the ages of eleven and twelve who becomes pregnant faces mortal danger, and younger than eleven she will not become pregnant at all. A child born of a marriage dissolved by *mi'un* would require the mother to have been a minor, making it nearly impossible for her to have also produced a viable child.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא בִּגְרוּשָׁה, וּמַאי ״אַחַר בַּעְלָהּ״ — אַחַר גֵּט בַּעְלָהּ. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הוּא אֵין מִטַּמֵּא לָהֶם וְהֵם אֵינָן מִטַּמְּאִין לוֹ. בִּשְׁלָמָא הֵן אֵין מִטַּמְּאִין לוֹ — לְחוּמְרָא, דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד דִּלְמָא לָאו בְּרֵיהּ הוּא. אֶלָּא הוּא אֵין מִטַּמֵּא לָהֶם, אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** **And** assume the mishna's statement is **rather with regard to a divorcée. And** accordingly **what is** the meaning of the phrase: **After** she received **a bill of divorce** from **her husband.** If so, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: **He may not become impure to** bury **them, and they may not become impure to** bury **him. Granted, they may not become impure to** bury **him, as** the ruling is **stringent, as** with regard to **each one** of them **perhaps he is not his son. However, why may he not become impure to** bury **them?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְשֵׁנִי לָא לִיטַּמֵּי לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא לְרִאשׁוֹן לִיטַּמֵּי לֵיהּ מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי בְּרֵיהּ הוּא — שַׁפִּיר קָא מִטַּמֵּא לֵיהּ, וְאִי בַּר בָּתְרָא הוּא — שַׁפִּיר קָא מִטַּמֵּא לֵיהּ, דְּחָלָל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, for the second one he should not become impure,** as he might not be his son. **However, for the first he should** be allowed to **become impure whichever way you** view it: **If he is his son, it is appropriate for him to become impure to** bury **him,** as even a priest must become ritually impure to bury his father. **And if he is the son of the latter one, it is appropriate for him to become impure to** bury **him, as he,** the son, **is a *ḥalal*.** If his mother is a divorcée, his father, the latter husband, is prohibited as a priest to marry her, and a child born from this relationship is a *ḥalal*, who is unfit for the priesthood. There would then be no prohibition against his becoming ritually impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, בִּזְנוּת. וּמַאי ״אַחַר בַּעְלָהּ״ — אַחַר בּוֹעֲלָהּ. וְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: עוֹלֶה בְּמִשְׁמָר שֶׁל זֶה וְשֶׁל זֶה. וּתְיוּבְתָּא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is** the mishna's statement **not with regard to** the **licentious** intercourse of an unmarried woman? **And what is** the meaning of the phrase: **After** she separated from **her husband [*ba'ala*]?** Is it not: **After** separating from **the man with whom she engaged in intercourse [*bo'ala*],** meaning that she engaged in intercourse with a man less than three months before marrying another man, and therefore she does not know the identity of the father? **And it is taught** in **the latter clause** of the mishna that the son **ascends** to the Temple service **with the priestly watch of this one and of that one,** which implies that he is considered a priest, although the identity of his father is uncertain due to the licentious intercourse of his mother. **And** this appears to be **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Shmuel.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמַעְיָא: בִּמְמָאֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shemaya said:** The mishna's statement **pertains to a girl who refused** her husband. A minor girl who was orphaned from her father may be married off by her brothers. However, she may subsequently refuse her husband before reaching majority. This nullifies the marriage entirely, so she is not considered a divorcée, for whom it is prohibited to marry a priest. In the case of the mishna, she did not wait three months after her refusal before marrying again, so she does not know who the father of her child is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְמָאֶנֶת מִי קָא יָלְדָה? וְהָתָנֵי רַב בִּיבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹת בְּמוֹךְ: קְטַנָּה, מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וּמְנִיקָה. קְטַנָּה — שֶׁמָּא תִּתְעַבֵּר וְתָמוּת, מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שֶׁמָּא תַּעֲשֶׂה עוּבָּרָהּ סַנְדָּל, מְנִיקָה — שֶׁמָּא תִּגְמוֹל אֶת בְּנָהּ וְיָמוּת. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא קְטַנָּה — מִבַּת אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד עַד בַּת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד. פָּחוֹת מִיכֵּן אוֹ יָתֵר עַל כֵּן — מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהוֹלֶכֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can a girl who refuses** her husband **give birth? Didn't Rav Beivai teach before Rav Naḥman** that **women** in **three** situations **may engage in intercourse with** a contraceptive **resorbent,** despite the fact that this practice is generally prohibited: **A minor girl, a pregnant woman, and a nursing woman? A minor girl** may do so **lest she become pregnant and die,** as the fetus might endanger her life. **A pregnant woman** may do so **lest** she get pregnant a second time and **her** previous **fetus becomes a sandal,** i.e., it is squashed by the pressure of the second fetus. **A nursing woman** may do so **lest she wean her child** prematurely, as pregnancy will cause her milk to dry up, **and he will die** of hunger. **And who is** considered **a minor girl** in this context? A girl **from** the **age** of **eleven years and one day until** the **age** of **twelve years and one day.** If she is **younger than this or older than this, she should go ahead and engage in intercourse in her usual manner.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהוֹלֶכֶת, וּמִן הַשָּׁמַיִם יְרַחֲמוּ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹמֵר פְּתָאִים ה׳״!
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** Both **this one and that one,** i.e., women in any cases, **should go ahead and engage in intercourse in their usual manner, and Heaven will have mercy** and prevent them from getting pregnant, **as it is stated: "The Lord preserves the simple"** (Psalms 116:6). Evidently, a girl who is a minor and therefore young enough to refuse her husband cannot become pregnant without endangering her health.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי טָעוּת, וְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה״ — אֲסוּרָה, הָא נִתְפָּשָׂה — מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You find** a solution **in** the case of **a mistaken betrothal.** For example, if the first husband betrothed her conditionally and the condition was unfulfilled, the marriage is nullified. This woman may marry a priest. If she did so within three months, the identity of her child's father is uncertain, which fits the statement in the mishna. **And** this is **in accordance with that** which **Rav Yehuda** said that **Shmuel said, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael:** The verse states with regard to a *sota*: **"Neither was she [*hi*] coerced in the act"** (Numbers 5:13). It may be inferred that she is **forbidden** to her husband only if she was not coerced by the adulterer; if **she was coerced she is permitted** to him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ לְךָ אַחֶרֶת, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְפָּשָׂה, מוּתֶּרֶת. וְאֵי זוֹ — זוֹ שֶׁקִּדּוּשֶׁיהָ קִידּוּשֵׁי טָעוּת, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בְּנָהּ מוּרְכָּב לָהּ עַל כְּתֵפָהּ — מְמָאֶנֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the superfluous word "she" indicates that **you have another woman who, although she** engaged in intercourse willingly and **was not coerced, is** nevertheless **permitted** to her husband, as the intercourse is not considered adultery. **And who is this? This** is referring to a woman **whose betrothal was a mistaken betrothal, as even if her child is riding on her shoulders, she may refuse** her husband **and go off** on **her** way. She is considered an unmarried woman, and she is therefore permitted to return to her husband, even if she engaged in intercourse with another man. The mishna may therefore be explained in a way that does not contradict Shmuel.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *mishtakcha lah be-kidushi taus* — the scenario involves a case of *kidushi taus*, a marriage contracted under false pretenses or based on an erroneous assumption, which is dissolved without a *get* when the error comes to light. Such a marriage is rabbinic in nature; its dissolution leaves the woman neither a *gerushah* nor an *almana*, and any child born within that union was not born of *znus* but of a legitimately constituted (if ultimately invalid) marriage. The *gemara* supports this with the ruling of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel*, citing *Rabbi Yishmael*: a woman who was not taken by force is forbidden to her husband if she committed adultery, but a woman who was raped is permitted. And there is yet another case — a woman who lay with a man even without compulsion and is nonetheless permitted: this is the case of *kidushi taus*, where the original marriage is voided retroactively, rendering her subsequent relations outside that marriage not adultery at all. *Sheafilu benah murkav al kesefah, mema'enes ve-holechet lah* — even if the child is already perched on her shoulder, she walks away from the marriage. The child of that union was born within the framework of a marriage, however flawed; there was no *znus*. And therefore Shmuel's disqualification — which applies only *bi-znus* — does not apply, and the child may serve in the *Beis HaMikdash*.
