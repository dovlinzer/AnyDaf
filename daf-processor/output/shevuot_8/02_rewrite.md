# Shevuot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumat Mikdash vs. Sin

### Intro: Tumat vs Sin

The daf opens by continuing a sustained inquiry into the precise function of the *korbanot* brought on *Yom Kippur*, and by extension on the *regalim* and *Rosh Chodesh*. The governing assumption, established in the preceding sugyot, is that all of these *korbanot* relate to *tumat mikdash* — the defilement of the sanctuary by someone who entered while ritually impure or who consumed *kodashim* in a state of impurity. The standard *korban* in the *Torah* for such a violation is the *oleh veyored*, the variable-cost offering whose price scales with the offender's means. That *korban* applies specifically when there was *yedi'ah bitchilah veyedi'ah besof* — awareness at the outset that one had become *tamei*, a period of forgetting, and renewed awareness at the end — with the transgression occurring during the interval of *helem*. The various *korbanot* associated with *Yom Kippur*, the *musaf* of *Yom Kippur*, and the *regalim* address distinct configurations of awareness, covering cases where one or both endpoints of knowledge are absent.

Before the *Gemara* can turn to those distinctions, however, it must first establish which category of *tumah* is even under discussion. The verse in *Vayikra* (16:16) reads: *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot benei Yisrael umipisheihem lechol chatotam* — "and he shall atone for the sanctuary from the impurities of the Children of Israel and from their transgressions, for all their sins." The plain sense of this verse, and one that many biblical commentators embrace as the *pshat*, is that *tumot* here does not refer to the technical ritual impurity of contact with corpses or bodily discharges, but rather to the metaphysical pollution generated by sin itself. According to this reading, grave transgressions — especially the three cardinal sins — contaminate the land and the *Mikdash*, driving God's presence inward. Once a year, the *Kohen Gadol* enters the innermost sanctum to cleanse what has accumulated and restore the conditions under which God can continue to dwell among Israel: *hashochen itam betoch tumotam*, "He who dwells among them amid their impurities." The impurity in that phrase does not refer to someone who touched a carcass; it refers to the reality that a sinful people nonetheless merits divine indwelling, enabled in part by this annual act of cosmic housekeeping.

The *Gemara* acknowledges this reading — at least as a *hava amina* — before ultimately setting it aside on technical grounds.

### Three Great Sins

The *Gemara* raises the possibility explicitly: *yachol al shalosh tumot halalu yehei zeh mechaper* — perhaps this *korban* atones for the three *tumot* associated with *avoda zara*, *gilui arayot*, and *shefichut damim*, the three cardinal sins for which the *Torah* itself uses the language of *tumah*. The rejection comes from the phrase *mitumot velo kol tumot* — "from the impurities," with the prefix implying a subset, not the totality of all *tumot*. The *Gemara* thus concedes that in principle some *tumot* could be intended, but not all of them; the question becomes which ones.

The logic of selection follows a principle of analogy: *mah matzinu shechilak hakatuv michlal kol tumot betumat mikdash vekodashav af kan betumat mikdash vekodashav* — just as we find that the *Torah* gave distinct, separate treatment to the *tumah* that involves the sanctuary and its sanctified objects (*tumat mikdash vekodashav*), so too here, it is that same category of *tumah* that is being addressed. This is the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Oleh Veyored

What does it mean that the *Torah* gave *tumat mikdash* "separate treatment" — *chilek hakatuv*? *Rabbi Yehuda* explains: *demayti oleh veyored* — it is the one offense for which the *Torah* prescribes a sliding-scale *korban*, an *oleh veyored*, making it easier for the sinner to obtain atonement by adjusting the cost to his means. This leniency signals that the *Torah* is actively facilitating atonement for this particular sin. By analogy, when *Yom Kippur* provides atonement for *tumat mikdash*, it is extending the same spirit of accessibility — addressing a sin for which the *Torah* has already demonstrated a desire to lower the barriers to *kapara*.

### Lenient vs Stringent

The question arises: why not say the *Torah* gave special treatment to *avoda zara* — after all, by *avoda zara* the *Torah* requires a female goat (*se'irah*) specifically, excluding the sheep that is normally available? *Rav Kahana* draws the crucial distinction: *anan chilek lehakel ka'amina vehai chilek lehachmir* — the principle we are applying is "separate treatment in a lenient direction." The *oleh veyored* is lenient because it makes atonement more accessible. The restriction to a goat by *avoda zara*, by contrast, is a *stringency* — it limits the offender's options. We are not looking for any instance of separate treatment; we are looking for separate treatment that eases the path to atonement. That criterion is met only by *tumat mikdash vekodashav* and the *oleh veyored*.

### Yoledet Case

The *Gemara* then considers the *yoledet* — the woman who gives birth and brings an *oleh veyored* as part of her post-partum purification. This offering is also variable in cost, just like the *korban* for *tumat mikdash*. Perhaps, the *Gemara* suggests, the *Yom Kippur* service atones for the *tumah* of the *yoledet*. *Rashi* understands the scenario as a woman who gave birth before *Yom Kippur* and had not yet brought her *korban*; perhaps *Yom Kippur* exempts her. *Tosafot*, noting a difficulty with this reading — since an established *korban* obligation is not ordinarily discharged by the *Yom Kippur* service — suggests instead a case of doubt, such as uncertainty about whether a miscarriage occurred before or after 40 days of gestation, where the obligation itself is ambiguous.

### Tumah and Sin

The response comes from *Rav Oshaya*: *lechol chatotam velo lechol tumotam* — "for all their sins," not "for all their impurities." The *Yom Kippur* service must address something that contains both a *tumah* dimension and a *sin* dimension. *Avoda zara*, *gilui arayot*, and *shefichut damim* have both. *Tumat mikdash* has both — the *tumah* of entering the *Mikdash* while impure, and the sin of having done so. But the *yoledet*'s offering involves no sin at all; she has done nothing wrong. Therefore, *lechol chatotam* excludes her case: the *Yom Kippur* *korban* cannot be addressing the *tumah* of the *yoledet*.

The *Gemara* then raises the view of *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*, who holds that the *yoledet* is, in fact, considered a sinner. His reasoning: during the agony of labor, a woman is presumed to take an oath that she will never again be intimate with her husband. The *korban* she brings is therefore a *chatat* in part to address this rash oath. According to *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*, the *yoledet* case does have a sin component — so why doesn't the *Yom Kippur* service apply to her? The answer is that *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* uses a different methodology altogether — he derives which *tumah* is at stake directly from context (*mimkomo hu muchra*), as will be explained shortly.

### Chatat Cleansing

This discussion opens a broader reflection on the nature of *chatat* itself. The word is commonly translated as "sin offering," and we naturally assume it is about personal guilt and divine forgiveness. But this framing may be post-*Mikdash* thinking. The verb *lechatei* can mean to cleanse or purify, not only to sin. A *chatat* can function as the culminating act of a process of purification — not as an acknowledgment of wrongdoing, but as the mechanism that restores a person to full ritual standing. The *metzora* provides the clearest example, as does the *yoledet*. Neither has sinned in the conventional sense, yet both bring a *chatat*. What the *chatat* does in those cases is address the *tumah* — to clean up, as it were, the disruption that occurred in the world, whether through birth or through affliction.

This suggests a fundamentally different way of understanding *korbanot* in general. Rather than centering on the individual's guilt, punishment, and need for forgiveness, the *korban* framework centers on rupture and repair — what was damaged in the world, the sanctuary, the relationship between *Bnei Yisrael* and *Hashem*, and how to restore it. The *chatat* is not primarily about me and my stain; it is about fixing what my action broke, or completing a process of transition from one status to another.

### Tinok Shenishba

A striking illustration of this principle is the case of the *tinok shenishba* — a child taken captive and raised among non-Jews, who grew up with no knowledge of his Jewish identity. If such a person spent decades performing *melacha* on *Shabbat* in complete and blameless ignorance, and then discovered his origins, is he required to bring a *korban*? The *Gemara* says yes — one *korban*, not one for every *Shabbat* or every *melacha*, but one nonetheless. This is remarkable: the person is entirely without fault. Yet the *korban* is still required. Why? Because the framework is not about guilt; it is about the objective fact that a disruption occurred in the world, and something is needed to address that, irrespective of personal culpability. You broke it, you fix it — even if it was not your fault. This case powerfully demonstrates that the *chatat* is not reducible to a mechanism of divine forgiveness for personal wrongdoing.

### Metzora Case

The *Gemara* next considers the *metzora* — the person afflicted with *tzaraat* — who also brings an *oleh veyored* as part of the purification process. The suggestion is that perhaps the *Yom Kippur* service addresses the *tumah* of the *metzora*. Again, *Rav Oshaya*'s principle applies: *lechol chatotam velo lechol tumotam* — there must be both *tumah* and sin. The *Gemara* acknowledges the tradition cited by *Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Yonatan* that *tzaraat* comes as punishment for seven categories of sin, including *lashon hara*. So there is indeed a sin component. Why, then, can't *Yom Kippur* serve the *metzora*?

The answer is subtle: *hasam negaei d'achpar leih* — there, the affliction of *tzaraat* itself served as the atonement for the sin. The suffering of *tzaraat* was his *kapara*. By the time he brings the *korban*, the sin has already been atoned for through the ordeal of the affliction itself. The *korban* at that point functions only to address the *tumah* and restore him to full participation — *l'ishtruye b'kodashim*, to permit him to eat *kodashim* again. With the sin already atoned for through suffering, the *korban* is a purely *tumah*-related act, and the *lechol chatotam* requirement remains unmet.

### Nazir Tamei

The *Gemara* then raises the case of the *nazir tamei* — the *nazir* who became impure and brings a *chatat* as part of his re-purification. This *chatat* is of birds rather than animals, making it an inexpensive and accessible offering — another instance of *chilek hakatuv* in a lenient direction. One might therefore suggest that this is the type of sin the *Yom Kippur* service addresses.

Once again, *Rav Oshaya*'s principle is invoked: the *chatat* of the *nazir tamei* relates only to *tumah*, not to sin. But here, too, the *Gemara* raises a counter-position: *Elazar Hakappar* teaches that a *nazir* is considered a sinner, because *shetziar atzmo min hayayin* — he denied himself the pleasure of wine. The verse says *asher chata al hanefesh*, "who sinned against the soul," referring to self-deprivation as a form of transgression. If the *nazir tamei*, by extending his *nezirut* beyond its justified purpose through the incident of impurity, compounds this sin — then there is both a *tumah* and a sin dimension. Why, then, couldn't the *Yom Kippur* service apply?

*Tosafot* addresses the difficulty by suggesting that an original *nezirut* of limited duration serves a legitimate purpose that counterbalances the self-denial; once the *nazir* becomes *tamei* and the *nezirut* is extended beyond its original scope and purpose, the sin element becomes more pronounced. Even so, the *Gemara* ultimately resolves the question by invoking *Rabbi Shimon*'s position: anyone who finds this line of reasoning unsatisfying can fall back on *Rabbi Shimon*'s methodology, which derives the answer directly from context.

### Rabbi Shimon Context

*Rabbi Shimon* does not need to work through the process of identifying *chilek hakatuv* and then filtering by the requirements of *tumah* plus sin. He reads the *pasuk* directly: *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot benei Yisrael* — it is a *tumah* that directly affects the *mikdash* itself. The phrase *al hakodesh* in conjunction with *mitumot* tells us immediately and contextually that we are dealing with the *tumah* of someone who entered the sanctuary in a state of impurity. No analogy to *oleh veyored* is required; no process of elimination is needed. The answer is embedded in the plain meaning of the verse, properly understood.

## R. Yehuda vs R. Shimon

### Yehuda: Act of Sprinkling

Having established the competing methodologies, the *Gemara* now examines why *Rabbi Yehuda* did not simply follow *Rabbi Shimon*'s contextual reading. If *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot benei Yisrael* so clearly refers to the *tumah* of the sanctuary, why does *Rabbi Yehuda* need the roundabout argument from *chilek hakatuv* and *oleh veyored*?

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s answer: the phrase *vechipper al hakodesh* does not tell you *what* is being atoned for. It tells you *where* the atonement is being performed. The *Kohen Gadol* enters the *Kodesh Kodashim* with the blood of the *par* and the *se'ir*, sprinkles it on and before the *kaporet* — the cover of the *Aron* — and then performs a parallel sprinkling outside, on the *parochet* of the *kodesh*. The phrase *vechipper al hakodesh* is describing the act of sprinkling upon the sanctuary structure itself, not identifying the nature of the sin being addressed. For all *Rabbi Yehuda* knows from that phrase alone, the *tumot* being atoned for could be those of *avoda zara* and *gilui arayot* — sins for which the *Torah* uses the language of *tumah*. The word *kodesh* in *vechipper al hakodesh* points to the location of the atonement rite, not to the category of defilement being cleansed.

### Shimon: Vekhen Ya'aseh

*Rabbi Shimon* responds by pointing to the continuation of the verse: *vechen ya'aseh le'ohel moed hashochen itam betoch tumotam* — "and thus shall he do for the Tent of Meeting that dwells among them amid their impurities." This phrase, *vechen ya'aseh*, tells us that a parallel sprinkling act is performed upon the *Ohel Moed* — meaning the instruction to repeat the sprinkling is already conveyed by this clause. Once *vechen ya'aseh* covers the procedural element — instructing the *Kohen Gadol* to perform the same act of blood sprinkling outside as inside — the earlier phrase *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot benei Yisrael* is freed from serving a procedural function and can instead convey substantive content about what is being atoned for. Since *al hakodesh* now functions to identify the sin — the *tumah* that directly impacted the *kodesh* — *Rabbi Shimon* reads it as referring to *tumat mikdash*: someone entered in a state of *tumah* and thereby defiled the sanctuary.

### Both Agree: Tumat Mikdash

*Rabbi Yehuda*, in turn, objects that *vechen ya'aseh* alone would have led to an erroneous conclusion — that the *Kohen Gadol* must slaughter an entirely new *par* and *se'ir* for the outer service, rather than using the same blood. It is specifically the combination of *vechipper al hakodesh* — connecting back to the inner service already described — together with *vechen ya'aseh*, that teaches us to use the same blood for the outer sprinkling. For *Rabbi Yehuda*, therefore, the entire phraseology of *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot benei Yisrael* is consumed by the procedural teaching, leaving no textual basis to derive the nature of the sin from context. That is why he requires the inferential route through *chilek hakatuv*.

Despite their methodological differences, both *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* converge on the same conclusion: the *Yom Kippur* service atones for *tumat mikdash vekodashav* — the sin of one who entered the sanctuary or consumed *kodashim* while in a state of ritual impurity.

## Which Awareness Scenario

### Four Knowledge Scenarios

With *tumat mikdash* established as the subject of atonement, the *Gemara* must now determine which *configuration* of awareness the *Yom Kippur* service addresses. There are four logical possibilities. First: *yesh yedia bitchilah veyesh yedia besof* — awareness both at the outset and at the end, with *helem* in between. Second: *yesh yedia bitchilah ve'ein yedia besof* — awareness at the beginning, but not at the end. Third: *ein yedia bitchilah veyesh yedia besof* — no awareness at the beginning, but awareness at the end. Fourth: no awareness at either point.

The first case — full *yedia* at both ends — is already addressed by the *oleh veyored*. The inner *korban* of *Yom Kippur* (the *pnimi*), the outer *korban* (*musaf*), the *korbanot* of the *regalim*, and *Rosh Chodesh* are each assigned to cover one of the remaining cases. The task now is to identify precisely which *korban* covers which scenario.

### Pisheihem Excludes

The verse's phrase *mipisheihem* — "from their transgressions" — provides the first limiting principle. *Pesha* is willful sin, for which no *korban* is brought. By analogy, the *Yom Kippur* service can only function for a case where no individual *korban* obligation exists — just as with *pesha*, where there is no *korban* at all. This immediately excludes the first scenario of full *yedia* at both ends, since that case carries an obligation in an *oleh veyored*. The individual is *bar korban* — obligated in his own *korban* — and the communal *Yom Kippur* service is not his remedy.

### Before Yom Kippur Case

One might object: the exclusion is obvious. If someone is obligated in a *korban*, of course the *Yom Kippur* service doesn't replace it. Why would we need *pisheihem* to teach something so self-evident? The *Gemara* answers: there is a non-trivial case where this is not obvious. Imagine that a person became aware of his sin — acquiring full *yedia* at both ends, thus incurring an obligation in an *oleh veyored* — just moments before sunset on *Erev Yom Kippur*. He had no time to bring his *korban* before *Yom Kippur* began. One might have thought that in this scenario, since he physically could not bring his *korban*, the *Yom Kippur* service would either atone for him entirely, or at minimum suspend his punishment until he could bring it after *Yom Kippur*. The word *pisheihem* comes to exclude this: even in that case, the *Yom Kippur* service provides no assistance. If the individual is obligated in his own *korban*, the communal service does not intervene.

### Pisheihem + Lechol

Having established via *pisheihem* that the *Yom Kippur* *korban* addresses only cases without a concurrent *korban* obligation — which includes the three remaining scenarios — the *Gemara* must now identify which of those three the *pnimi* (inner) *korban* covers. The question sharpens: why pick the case of *yesh yedia bitchilah ve'ein yedia besof* (awareness at the beginning, not at the end)?

One might argue that *pisheihem* demands something analogous to *pesha* in its totality: just as intentional sin *never* incurs a *korban*, perhaps the *Yom Kippur* service should address only a case where a *korban* is *permanently* unavailable — namely *ein yedia bitchilah veyesh yedia besof* (no awareness at the beginning, awareness at the end), since the absence of initial *yedia* means a *korban* obligation can never be triggered for that episode. The case of *yesh yedia bitchilah ve'ein yedia besof*, by contrast, may eventually generate a *korban* obligation if the person becomes aware at some future point.

Here the second phrase — *lechol chatotam*, "for all their sins" — enters the analysis. The word *chatat* refers to an unintentional sin offering. *Lechol chatotam* therefore signals that the *Yom Kippur* service applies to something that could *eventually* become a *chatat* — something in the nature of a *chatat*, even if the obligation has not yet been activated. This phrase excludes the scenario of *ein yedia bitchilah veyesh yedia besof* (no initial awareness, awareness at the end), because in that case the person *is already obligated* in a *korban* now, having gained final *yedia*. And it excludes the scenario of *ein yedia* at either end, because that case can *never* become a *chatat* — no *korban* obligation will ever arise, since *yedia* is permanently absent.

The only case that satisfies both conditions simultaneously — no *korban* obligation *now* (as required by *pisheihem*) but *eventual potential* for a *chatat* (as required by *lechol chatotam*) — is *yesh yedia bitchilah ve'ein yedia besof*: there was awareness at the beginning, but the person has not yet regained awareness at the end. Right now, no *korban* is owed. But when and if he becomes aware, a *chatat* obligation will crystallize. This is the scenario addressed by the inner *korban* of *Yom Kippur*.

### Pnimi: Toleh Not Mekapper

Having identified the scenario, the *Gemara* asks whether the inner *korban* provides complete atonement (*kapara gemura*) — meaning that even if the person later becomes aware of his sin, he would be exempt from bringing a *korban* — or whether it merely suspends punishment (*toleh*). The language of the verse itself argues for the latter: had the verse written *meichatotam* — "from their sins" — implying the sins are removed, one could conclude that the *pnimi* provides full atonement. But the verse writes *lechol chatotam* — "for all their *chatat*-type sins" — implying that the dimension of *chatat* remains intact, that an eventual *korban* obligation still lies ahead. The *pnimi* does not extinguish the sin; it manages it in the interim. If and when the person becomes aware, he must still bring his *chatat*.

### Rabbi Zeira: Protecting

This raises the obvious question: if the *pnimi korban* neither fully atones nor exempts the person from bringing his own *korban* — what exactly does it accomplish? *Rabbi Zeira* answers: *lomar she'im mes mes belo avon* — it ensures that if a person dies while still oblivious to his sin, he dies without iniquity. He is not held accountable for what he never knew he had done. The *korban* provides him cover, so to speak, against being judged for an unresolved sin at the time of death.

### Rava: Suffering Protecti…

*Rava* challenges *Rabbi Zeira*'s explanation: if the person dies before becoming aware, *misa memarekes* — death itself atones for sin. Divine death is inherently purifying; the person would be cleared in any event. So what unique contribution does the *pnimi korban* make? *Rava* answers: *lehagein alav min hayisurin* — it protects the person from *suffering* in this world. A person who sinned *bishogeg* — without full intentionality — and remains unaware of his transgression, may nonetheless be subject to divinely ordained suffering on account of that unresolved sin. The *pnimi korban* of *Yom Kippur* shields him from that suffering during the period of unawareness. This is a striking theological claim: one can suffer in life for a sin committed inadvertently and never consciously confronted, and the communal *Yom Kippur* service provides protection against precisely this.

### Viddui Hanistarot

This resonates directly with the *viddui* recited on *Yom Kippur*. We confess *al hoda v'lo hoda* — for what we know and for what we do not know. And at the conclusion of the *viddui* we quote the verse: *hanistarot l'Hashem Elokeinu veha-niglot lanu ulvaneinu* — "the hidden things belong to *Hashem* our God, but the revealed things are for us and our children." In its original context, this verse distinguishes communal accountability (only for publicly known sins) from individual accountability (which cannot extend to the hidden). But in the context of the *Yom Kippur viddui*, the citation carries a different weight: we are invoking it to say that we cannot be fully accountable for what is *hanistarot* — what is hidden even from ourselves. The *pnimi korban*, according to *Rava*, is the mechanism that stands behind this claim — protecting us from suffering for sins we genuinely did not know we committed.

## Musaf Korban Scenario

### Mishna Link: Musaf

With the inner *korban* assigned to the case of *yesh yedia bitchilah ve'ein yedia besof*, the *Gemara* turns to the *musaf korban* of *Yom Kippur* and determines which of the remaining awareness scenarios it covers. The *Mishna* established this through the formula *al ma shezeh mechaper al ma shezeh mechaper zeh mechaper* — "whatever the one atones for, the other atones for similarly." This linkage between the *pnimi* and *musaf* *korbanot* implies they function in parallel: both address *tumat mikdash*, both require some dimension of *yedia*, and both work in cases where no individual *korban* is currently obligatory. The difference is the configuration of *yedia*: the *musaf* addresses *ein yedia bitchilah veyesh yedia besof* — no awareness at the beginning, but awareness at the end.

### Linked Korbanos

The linkage established by the *Mishna* raises a further question: if the two *korbanot* are compared to one another as parallel instruments of atonement, perhaps one of them can cover both scenarios. Perhaps the *pnimi* — by virtue of its connection to the *musaf* — could atone for both the case of initial-awareness-without-final-awareness *and* the case of final-awareness-without-initial-awareness. If they are genuinely interlinked, why shouldn't a single *korban* serve a dual function?

### Achat: One Atonement

The verse provides the answer: *vechipper Aharon al karnotav achat bashanah* — "and *Aharon* shall atone for its horns, once in the year" (referring to the inner altar). The word *achat* — "one" — limits the inner *korban* to a single act of atonement. *Kapara achat mechaper ve'eino mechaper shtei kaparot* — it performs one atonement; it does not perform two. The linkage between the *pnimi* and the *musaf* establishes their similarity and shared domain (*tumat mikdash* with some *yedia*), but *achat* asserts that each is a distinct, single instrument of atonement. The inner *korban* does its one job; it does not absorb the role of the *musaf*.

### Between Inner & Musaf

The *Gemara* then asks the question in reverse: even if the *pnimi* is limited to one atonement by *achat*, why not say the *musaf* — for which no such limitation is explicitly stated — can cover *both* its own scenario and that of the *pnimi*? The practical relevance would be a case where someone became *tamei* in the interval *between* the completion of the inner service and the offering of the *musaf*. In such a case, the *musaf* might serve as a backup that covers both the standard *musaf* scenario and, for good measure, the *pnimi* scenario. Why not?

### Achat Bashanah Twice

The answer comes from a second occurrence of the same phrase. The verse continues: *midam chatat hakippurim achat bashanah yechaper* — "from the blood of the sin offering of atonements, once in the year, shall he atone." *Rashi* observes that *achat bashanah* appears twice in the immediate passage. The first *achat* limits the inner *korban* to one atonement. The second *achat bashanah* asserts that this particular atonement — the one addressed by the inner service — is performed only once per year, and by implication, is accounted for by only one act of atonement. It cannot be covered twice — neither by the *pnimi* and the *musaf* together, nor by the *musaf* alone serving as a double instrument.

The conclusion is that the two *korbanot* — the *pnimi* and the *musaf* of *Yom Kippur* — are genuinely linked: they share the same domain (*tumat mikdash*), the same general structure, and the same requirement of some *yedia*. But they remain irreducibly distinct. Each covers a single, specific awareness scenario. The *pnimi* covers *yesh yedia bitchilah ve'ein yedia besof*; the *musaf* covers *ein yedia bitchilah veyesh yedia besof*. And the two *achat*s together — one limiting each *korban* to its own atonement, the other ensuring that each atonement is performed and accounted for only once — prevent any blurring of their distinct roles.
