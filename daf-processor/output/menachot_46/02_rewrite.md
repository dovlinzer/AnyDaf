# Menachot 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtei Halechem Debate

### Kivsei Atzeret Intro

The daf begins at the bottom of 45b, with the statement *halechem ma'akevet hakevasim* — the bread prevents the lambs. This brings us into the *shtei halechem* discussion that closes the perek. While the broader theme of this section concerns which components of a *korban* are *not* mutually *me'akev*, the Gemara pauses on a notable exception: the relationship between the *shtei halechem* and the two lambs that accompany them on Shavuot. These lambs — the *kivsei atzeret* — are *shalmei tzibur*, communal peace offerings brought together with the bread. The Torah states that they are waved together with the bread and that they stand in a relationship of mutual dependency, but the precise direction of that dependency is the subject of a famous debate.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva holds that the bread is *me'akev* the lambs: without the bread, you do not bring the lambs. The lambs exist only for the sake of the bread. The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Akiva* — what is the reasoning behind this position?

The answer is that Rabbi Akiva derives it through a *gezeirah shavah* — *gamar "yihyu" mi-"tihiyena"*. In the passage in Emor, the Torah uses the word *yihyu* when speaking of both the bread and the two lambs together: *kodesh yihyu l'Hashem lakohen*. The underlying assumption is that this *yihyu* refers to only one of the two components, singling out whichever element is truly *me'akev*. Earlier in the same passage, when speaking of the bread alone, the Torah uses the word *tihiyena*: *solet tihiyena, chametz te'afena*. Rabbi Akiva therefore draws the connection: just as *tihiyena* there referred to bread, *yihyu* here refers to bread — *mah lahalan lechem, af kan lechem*. It is the bread that is the essential element.

### Ben Nannas Position

Ben Nannas reaches the opposite conclusion: the lambs are *me'akev*, not the bread. If you have the lambs alone, bring them; if you have the bread alone, do not bring it. His reasoning is also a *gezeirah shavah* — but he learns *yihyu mi-yihyu*. Earlier in the same *parsha*, the Torah uses *yihyu* in connection with the large group of animals that accompany the *shtei halechem*: *shivat kevasim temimim... par ben bakar... ve-eylim shnayim yihyu ola l'Hashem*. This *yihyu* refers to the animal component. Ben Nannas therefore reads the *yihyu* of *kodesh yihyu* as pointing back to the animal — specifically to the lambs — *mah lahalan kevasim, af kan kevasim*.

### Gezeirah Shavah Analysis

The Gemara immediately raises a question: why doesn't Ben Nannas simply learn from *tihiyena*, the same word used for the bread, as Rabbi Akiva does? Tosafot notes that while the words are not identical, the connection is direct — *tihiyena* appeared earlier in the very same passage describing the *shtei halechem*. Why not draw the obvious link?

The Gemara answers: *danin yihyu mi-yihyu, ve-ein danin yihyu mi-tihiyena*. We prefer a *gezeirah shavah* built from identical words. The principle invoked here — drawn from the *tanna de-vei Rabbi Yishmael* regarding *tzara'at*, where even words with merely similar meanings sufficed — is clarified: that leniency applies only when there is no identical word available. When an identical match exists, we must use it. Ben Nannas therefore correctly prefers the *yihyu-yihyu* connection over the *yihyu-tihiyena* one.

The Gemara then turns the question around on Rabbi Akiva: why does he not also use *yihyu mi-yihyu*, connecting to the earlier *yihyu* that referred to the animals? The answer is: *danin davar she-matanah la-kohen mi-davar she-matanah la-kohen*. The verse *kodesh yihyu l'Hashem lakohen* speaks of something that goes to the *kohen*. Both the bread and the lambs go to the *kohen* — the *kohen* eats the bread and eats the *shelamim*. But the other animals — the one bull, two rams, and seven lambs — are *olot*, which go entirely to God and not to the *kohen*. It is therefore more appropriate to link this *yihyu* to an earlier *yihyu* also associated with something given to the *kohen*, rather than to the *yihyu* of *olot* that go exclusively to God.

### Kodesh Yihyu Pasuk

The Gemara adds a second approach to the debate: *ve-iba'it eima bi-kra gufei kamifligi* — perhaps they are debating the *pasuk* itself. The verse *kodesh yihyu l'Hashem lakohen* is written at the conclusion of the section describing both the bread and the two lambs, and both Rabbi Akiva and Ben Nannas read the same word *yihyu* as pointing to only one of the two components.

Rabbi Akiva asks: *eizehu davar she-kulo la-kohen?* — which of these two components goes entirely to the *kohen*? It must be the bread. The lambs are *shelamim*, and the *eimurim* — the fats and certain innards — go to the altar. So the lambs do not go fully to the *kohen*. Therefore *yihyu l'Hashem lakohen* refers to the bread, which passes entirely to the *kohen*.

Ben Nannas counters: *mi ksiv kodesh yihyu la-kohen?* The verse does not say simply *la-kohen*. It says *l'Hashem lakohen* — to God, and to the *kohen*. *Eizehu davar she-miktzaso la-Shem u-miktzaso la-kohen?* Which component is partly to God and partly to the *kohen*? That is the lambs, which are *shelamim* — the *eimurim* go to God and the meat goes to the *kohen*.

Rabbi Akiva's rejoinder: *mi ksiv kodesh yihyu l'Hashem ve-la-kohen?* The conjunction *ve-* is not there. The verse says *l'Hashem lakohen*, not *l'Hashem ve-la-kohen*. The formulation follows the principle articulated by Rav Huna: *kana'ah Hashem u-netanah la-kohen* — God acquired it and gave it to the *kohen*. This is the general principle that *kohanim mishulchan gavoah zachu* — the *kohanim* eat from God's table, as it were. When a *korban* is consumed by the *kohanim*, it is not simply given to them; rather, it passes through God to them. The *kohen*'s eating is conceptually similar to the altar's consumption and is part of the *kapara* process. Accordingly, *l'Hashem lakohen* means it goes to God, and from God passes fully to the *kohen* — which describes the bread, not the lambs.

Whether the debate turns on the *gezeirah shavah* from *yihyu* and *tihiyena*, or on the internal reading of *kodesh yihyu l'Hashem lakohen*, the Gemara frames the dispute as a technical question about which component the *yihyu* is pointing to — not, as the *mishnah* might have suggested, a purely conceptual argument about which offering is the *ikar*.

## Zikah & One Unit

### Rabbi Yochanan Zikah

Rabbi Yochanan articulates a foundational principle: *hakol modim she-im huzkequ zeh la-zeh she-me'akvin zeh et zeh* — everyone agrees that once the bread and the lambs have become connected to one another, they are mutually *me'akev*. Even Rabbi Akiva, who holds that the bread can be brought without the lambs, concedes that when they come together and that connection is established, they function as a single unit. If any component is then missing, the whole is invalid.

The question is: what act creates this connection — this *zikah*? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *shechita*. When the lambs are slaughtered, that act of *shechita* is *mekadesh* the bread, conferring upon it *kedushat haguf*. Prior to *shechita*, the bread possesses only *kedushat damim* — it is owned by the *beis hamikdash* but not yet sanctified in body. Removing it from the *Mikdash* would not invalidate it, since *kedushat haguf* has not yet been conferred. Once the lambs are slaughtered, the bread acquires *kedushat haguf*, and at that moment the entire offering becomes one coherent unit.

This is conceptually parallel to what was said earlier regarding the *musaf* offerings: once you have the full set of components, that defines the whole as a package, and the loss of any one piece renders the whole invalid. Here too, once the *shechita* of the lambs establishes the *zikah*, the bread and lambs are that package.

It is worth noting the word *zikah* itself, familiar to anyone who has learned Yevamos. There, *zikah* describes the quasi-marital connection created between a surviving brother and his sister-in-law by virtue of the deceased brother — a connection that can manifest fully as *yibum* or be severed through *chalitza*. Here the same word appears in an entirely different context, describing the bond that links the bread and lambs into a single sacrificial unit. The word in both contexts captures the same essential concept: a legally meaningful linkage between two entities.

Regarding Rabbi Akiva's position within this framework: one might have thought that since Rabbi Akiva permits bringing the bread without the lambs, he would view the two components as always operating somewhat independently, even when brought together. The Gemara does not accept this. Even Rabbi Akiva agrees that the normative mode of bringing the bread is through the lambs — the *shechita* of the lambs and the sprinkling of their blood constitutes the act of *hakravah* that validates the offering. When they come together, they work as a unit through the lambs. Rabbi Akiva's leniency — allowing the bread to function independently — applies only in exceptional circumstances, not to the normal bringing of the offering.

### Tenufah vs Shechita

The Gemara cites a question that arose in Eretz Yisrael: *tnufa osa zikah o eina osa zikah?* Before the lambs are slaughtered, the *kohen* waves both the lambs and the bread together in the act of *tenufah*. Perhaps this waving — which is performed jointly with both components present — is itself sufficient to define them as a unit?

The Gemara initially proposes to resolve this from Rabbi Yochanan's statement: since Rabbi Yochanan says *shechita* creates *zikah*, one might infer that *tenufah*, which precedes *shechita*, does not. The Gemara rejects this inference, noting that Rabbi Yochanan's statement may itself be the subject of the question. Perhaps Rabbi Yochanan merely meant to establish with certainty that *shechita* creates *zikah*, without ruling out the possibility that *tenufah* creates it earlier.

### Shechita Makes Zikah

An attempt is made to resolve the question from a textual argument: the verse *kodesh yihyu l'Hashem lakohen* is written after the *tenufah* in the *parasha*, yet Rabbi Akiva and Ben Nannas still debate based on it — implying that even after the waving, only one component is *me'akev* the other, not both. If *tenufah* had created *zikah*, they would both be mutually *me'akev* and the debate would be moot. This would seem to prove that *tenufah* does not create *zikah*.

The Gemara deflects this: the verse appears at the end of the entire section, after *tenufah*, after *shechita*, and after the entire offering is brought. It cannot be read as speaking to any particular stage in the process. *Ela meiikara ka'ei* — the verse refers back to an earlier status, describing what the offering was at a prior point, before either waving or slaughtering. *Davar she-sofo la-kohen* — it describes something that would ultimately reach the *kohen*, not necessarily something already at that stage.

The Gemara then returns to the direct question of whether *shechita* truly creates *zikah*, and introduces a *baraita* about the *todah* offering as a challenge.

## Todah Case Study

### Bread Break Before

The *baraita* is elegantly structured, examining three types of invalidity that can befall the bread of a *todah* — a bread breaking, the bread going *yotzei* outside its proper boundary, and the bread becoming *tamei* — each considered at three points in time: before *shechita*, after *shechita*, and after *zerikah* (the sprinkling of the blood). The *todah* comes with forty loaves: ten of *chametz* and thirty of *matzah*, spread across four types, and the bread is eaten within Yerushalayim.

The underlying assumption is that if even one loaf is incomplete — a piece has broken off — the entire collection of bread is invalid. Rashi acknowledges uncertainty about the precise derivation of this rule, suggesting a possible analogy to the *lechem hapanim*.

If a piece of bread broke before *shechita*: there is no problem. Anything that occurs before *shechita* can be corrected. The broken loaf is replaced — *yavi lechem acher ve-shofre* — and the *korban* proceeds normally.

### Bread Break After

If the bread broke after *shechita*: the situation cannot be repaired. The *shechita* has already conferred *kedushat haguf* upon the bread and defined the offering as a unit; no substitution is possible at this stage. The *baraita* rules: *hadam yizarek ve-habasar ye'achel, ve-yidei nidro lo yatza* — sprinkle the blood and eat the meat, but the owner has not fulfilled his obligation of *todah*. Why? Because the offering was effectively brought without its bread. The entire collection of bread is invalid; the single broken piece invalidates all forty loaves, and since it cannot be replaced after *shechita*, the animal is brought as a *korban* without bread. A *todah* without bread is a *shelamim*. Rashi understands that one must have *kavanah* at the time of *zerikah* to bring it as *shelamim*; alternatively, it may revert automatically, analogous to the laws of *pesach* brought at other times of the year. Either way, the owner has brought a *shelamim*, not a *todah*, and his obligation remains unfulfilled. The bread, now invalid, is burned.

### Yotzei Before Shechita

If the bread went outside Yerushalayim before *shechita*: no problem. At this stage the bread has only *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*. Removing property of the *beis hamikdash* from Yerushalayim does not invalidate it, since the prohibition of *yotzei* that renders a *korban* invalid applies only once *kedushat haguf* is present. The solution is simply *machziro* — bring the bread back in — and proceed to *shechita*.

### Yotzei After Shechita

If the bread went outside Yerushalayim after *shechita*: now the bread has acquired *kedushat haguf* through the act of *shechita*, and removing it from Yerushalayim invalidates it. The blood has not yet been sprinkled. The *baraita* rules identically to the case of bread breaking after *shechita*: *hadam yizarek ve-habasar ye'achel ve-yidei nidro lo yatza* — proceed with the *korban*, but you have not fulfilled your obligation of *todah*. The animal reverts to *shelamim*. The bread that went outside is invalid, *ve-halechem pasul*.

If the blood was sprinkled before the bread went outside — *nizrak hadam* — then the *korban* was already valid at the moment of *zerikah*, and the owner has fulfilled his *todah* obligation. The loaves that subsequently went outside cannot be eaten, but the remaining bread inside Yerushalayim is valid. The *kohen* is given his portion — four whole loaves — from the bread that remained inside: *torem mimah shebifnim al shebichutz*.

### Tumah Before Shechita

If the bread became *tamei* before *shechita*: it can be corrected. Bring new bread and proceed to *shechita* — *meivi lechem acher ve-shochet*.

### Tumah After Shechita

If the bread became *tamei* after *shechita*: the situation is different from the other cases of post-*shechita* invalidity. Here the *baraita* rules: *hadam yizarek ve-habasar ye'achel, u-videi nidro yatza* — the owner *does* fulfill his obligation. Why? *She-ha-tzitz meratzeh al ha-tamei* — the *tzitz*, the golden forehead-plate of the High Priest, atones for and overrides problems of *tumah* in *korbanos*. This requires some analysis. It is not the *korban* itself that is *tamei* — the lambs are *tahor* — it is the bread. The *zerikah* therefore proceeds without any *tzitz* needed to permit it. The question is only whether a *todah* was validly brought, i.e., whether the *dam* was sprinkled in the context of a *todah* that had its bread present. The *tzitz* effectively renders the *tumah* of the bread transparent for purposes of the *korban*'s validity: it is treated as though the bread were present and valid, and the offering counts as a *todah*. The owner is therefore *yotzei*. The bread itself remains *pasul* — the *tzitz* does not permit eating *tamei* bread — but vis-à-vis the validity of the *korban*, it is counted as a *todah*.

After *zerikah* — if the blood was sprinkled while the bread was *tahor* and only then a loaf became *tamei*: the *korban* is fully valid, the obligation fully fulfilled. The *kohen* receives his four loaves from the *tahor* bread — *torem min ha-tahor al ha-tamei* — the *tamei* loaf is disposed of, and the owner eats the remaining *tahor* bread.

### Post-Shechita Irreversib…

The *baraita* establishes a clear structural principle: the moment of *shechita* is decisive. Everything that goes wrong before *shechita* can be remedied; everything that goes wrong after *shechita* — whether breaking, *yotzei*, or *tumah* — cannot be corrected by substitution, and the offering either reverts to *shelamim* (in the cases of breaking and *yotzei*) or is validated by the *tzitz* (in the case of *tumah*). The bread cannot be replaced once the *shechita* has occurred.

This now generates a sharp question. We have said that *shechita* creates *zikah* — it binds the bread and the animal into one unit. If that is so, then when a problem arises after *shechita* that invalidates the bread, the *zikah* should make the whole offering invalid. The animal cannot be brought as a *shelamim* if it has been fused into one unit with the bread. Why, then, is the animal salvageable?

## Todah vs Shtei Halechem

### Torah Names Todah

The Gemara answers: *shani toda de-Rachmana karyei shelamim* — the *todah* is different because the Torah itself calls it *shelamim*. The verse states *al zevach todat shelamav*, referring to the *todah* as a type of *shelamim*. From this the Gemara derives: *mah shelamim kereivim belo lechem af todah kereivah belo lechem* — just as *shelamim* is brought without bread, so too a *todah* can exist without its bread. The *todah* therefore always retains an independent *shelamim* identity to revert to, even after the *zikah* of *shechita* has been established. The bread can conceptually be separated off and the animal can continue to exist as a valid *korban*.

### Shtei Halechem Unique

The *shtei halechem* would seem to face the same problem. The lambs too are called *shelamim* — the verse refers to them as *shnei kevasim... lezeva'ch shelamim*. Why can they not similarly revert to an independent *shelamim* status when the bread is invalid after *shechita*?

Rashi and Tosafot note the distinction. The *todah* exists as a category of *korban* entirely independent of its bread. There is a concept of a *todah* animal; the bread is an additional component that elevates it to *todah* status, and without bread it is simply a *shelamim* of the ordinary kind. By contrast, there is no such thing as *shalmei tzibur* in the Torah apart from the *kivsei atzeret*. The entire category of *shalmei tzibur* is defined by and inseparable from the *shtei halechem*. There is no pre-existing *shelamim* identity for the lambs to revert to once they have been defined as part of the *shtei halechem* unit. Alternatively, one might frame it this way: the *todah* is fundamentally a *korban* to which bread is added as an accessory that confers *todah* status; the bread can be stripped away and the core *korban* remains. The *shtei halechem*, however, is fundamentally about the bread — the loaves are the *ikar*, and the lambs are what enable the bread to be brought. Once they form a unit, there is nothing independent for the lambs to revert to.

The discussion that follows elaborates on this distinction. If the answer to Abaye's later question about why only the lambs — and not the large *olah* complement — create *zikah* with the bread is to be "because only *shelamim* are linked to bread," then that principle must also explain why the *todah*'s link to its bread persists even after *shechita*. The *todah* is called *shelamim* by the Torah, and the association of bread with *shelamim* — not with *olah* — is the mechanism by which the link is created and sustained.

## Abaye's Question

### Why Only Lambs?

Abaye poses his question to Rava: *mai shna shnei kevasim d'makdeshei lechem u-me'akvi?* — what distinguishes the two lambs, such that their *shechita* sanctifies the bread and makes them mutually *me'akev*? And *mai shna shiva kevasim u-far ve-eilim* — what distinguishes the seven lambs, the bull, and the two rams (written in that order in the Torah) such that their *shechita* does *not* sanctify the bread and creates no *zikah* at all? All of these animals come with the *shtei halechem*. Why should the two lambs alone be linked to the bread?

### Rava Answer

### Tenufah Qualifier

Rava answers: *hov ve-huzkequ zeh la-zeh bi-tenufah* — the Torah specified that only the bread and the two lambs are waved together in *tenufah*. This act of joint waving defines them as a single unit and explains why the *shechita* of the lambs subsequently sanctifies the bread and why they are mutually *me'akev*. The large *olah* component — the bull, rams, and seven lambs — is never waved together with the bread and therefore stands in no such relationship to it.

### Todah Counterargument

The Gemara challenges this: *ve-harei todah de-lo huzkequ zeh la-zeh bi-tenufah* — the *todah* animal is never waved together with its bread, and yet the *shechita* of the *todah* sanctifies its bread and they are mutually *me'akev* in the sense that without the bread the offering does not count as a *todah*, and without a *todah* animal there is no reason to bring the bread. *Tenufah* cannot be the explanation for the link between the *todah* and its bread.

### Bread-Shelamim Link

The Gemara therefore reframes the answer: *ela ke-todah* — the principle is like the *todah*. *Mah toda shelamim, af hacha nami shelamim* — just as by the *todah* it is the *shelamim* component that is linked to the bread, so too here it is the *shelamim* component — the two lambs — that is linked to the bread, and not the *olah* component. This is not merely a formal rule but reflects a substantive principle: whenever the Torah links bread to a *korban*, that link is always to a *shelamim*, never to an *olah*. The association of bread with *shelamim* is built into the structure of *korbanos*.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: if that is so for the *todah*, why does the large *olah* component not also create a link? After all, there are multiple *zevachim* present, and perhaps they should all share equally in the connection to the bread. The Gemara answers: *hatam leika zevachim achrini bahadei* — by the *todah* there are no other *korbanos* present, so it is clear that the *shelamim* alone carries the link. Here, where multiple *korbanos* are present, one might have thought the link should be distributed among all of them. The Gemara will resolve this through the proof from the *nazir*.

### Nazir Ram Proof

The proof is brought from the ram of the *nazir*. When a *nazir* completes his term, he brings a set of *korbanos*: a lamb as an *olah*, a ewe-lamb as a *chatat*, and a ram as a *shelamim*, accompanied by a basket of *matzah* — *challot belulot bashamen* and *rekikei matzot meshuchim bashamen*. The Torah then says explicitly: *ve-et ha-ayil ya'aseh zevach shelamim l'Hashem al sal ha-matzot* — the ram shall be made a *shelamim* offering together with the basket of *matzah*. Even though an *olah* and a *chatat* are also being brought simultaneously, the Torah makes clear that the bread is associated exclusively with the *shelamim* ram, not with the other *korbanos*.

The *baraita* derives from this: *melamed she-ha-sal ba chavah la-ayil* — the basket comes as an obligation associated specifically with the ram. *Lefikach, shachto shelo lishmo, lo kidshu ha-lechem* — therefore, if the ram was slaughtered *shelo lishmo* (not for its designated purpose), the bread is not sanctified. The link between the bread and the *nazir*'s offering runs exclusively through the *shelamim* component, even in the presence of other *korbanos*.

This proves the principle definitively: bread is always associated with *shelamim*, not with *olah*. The large *olah* component of the *shtei halechem* offering — the bull, the two rams, the seven lambs — therefore creates no *zikah* with the bread. The *zikah* belongs solely to the two lambs that are *shalmei tzibur*. That is why their *shechita* sanctifies the bread, and that is why they alone are mutually *me'akev* with the bread once the *shechita* has occurred.
