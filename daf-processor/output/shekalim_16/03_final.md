# Shekalim 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shofars & Aron

### Intro & Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שׁוֹפָרוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שֻׁלְחָנוֹת שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָיוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְשֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים הָיוּ מִשְׁתַּחֲוִים בְּאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה. וְהֵיכָן הָיְתָה יְתֵרָה כְּנֶגֶד דִּיר הָעֵצִים שֶׁכֵּן מָסוֹרֶת בְּיָדָן מֵאֲבוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁשָּׁם הָאָרוֹן גָּנוּז: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּכֹהֵן אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מִתְעַסֵּק וְרָאָה הָרִיצְפָּה שֶׁהִיא מְשׁוּנָּה מֵחֲבֵרוֹתֶיהָ. בָּא וְאָמַר לַחֲבֵרוֹ. לֹא הִסְפִּיק לִגְמֹר אֶת הַדָּבָר עַד שֶׁיָּצְתָה נִשְׁמָתוֹ. וְיָדְעוּ בְיִיחוּד שֶׁשָּׁם הָאָרוֹן גָּנוּז:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: Thirteen horns, thirteen tables, and thirteen prostrations were in the Temple. The family of Rabban Gamliel and of Rebbi Ḥanania the Second in Command of the priests were prostrating themselves fourteen times. And where was the additional one? In front of the storage room of the wood since they had a tradition from their forefathers that there the Ark was hidden. It happened that a Cohen was occupied when he saw that a floor tile was different from the others. He went to tell it to a comrade but could not finish speaking before his soul left him; then they knew with certainty the the Ark was hidden there.

Today's *daf* is 96b. We pick up in the *Gemara* right after the *Mishna* on 96b. The *Mishna* mentions two sets of things that numbered thirteen: thirteen *shofrot*, which will be discussed in later *Mishnayot*, and a fascinating story connected to the thirteen bowings performed in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The house of Rabban Gamliel and the house of Rabbi Chananya Sgan HaKohanim had a tradition to add a fourteenth bowing opposite *Deir HaEtzim*, the room where the wood was stored, based on their tradition that the *Aron* was hidden beneath that very room.

### Shofar Design

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שׁוֹפָרוֹת כול׳. תַּנֵּי. הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת הַלָּלוּ עֲקוּמוֹת הָיוּ. צָרוֹת מִלְּמַעֲלָן וּרְחָבוֹת מִלְמַטָּן מִפְּנֵי הָרַמָּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: "Thirteen horns," etc. It was stated: these horns were curved; narrow on top and wide on the bottom because of the tricksters.

The *Gemara* opens with a *tani*: *hashofrot ha'elu akumot hayu, tzarot milmala v'rechavot milmata, mipnei haramaim* — these *shofrot* were bent, narrow at the top and wide at the bottom, as a safeguard against cheats. The pyramidal shape with its narrow slot at the top made it impossible to insert one's hand and remove the money, and the curved interior made it equally impossible to fish anything out with an implement.

### Fourteen Bowings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. הָאָרוֹן גָּלָה עִמָּהֶן לְבָבֶל. מָה טַעַם. לֹא יִוָותֵר דָּבָר אָמַר יְי. אֵין דָּבָר אֶלָּא שֶׁהַדִּיבְּרוֹת לְתוֹכוֹ. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר. וְלִתְשׁוּבַת הַשָּׁנָה שָׁלַח הַמֶּלֶךְ נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר וַיְבִיאֵהוּ בָבֶלָה עִם כְּלֵי חֶמְדַּת בֵּית יְי. אֵי זֶהוּ חֶמְדַּת בֵּית יְי. זֶה הָאָרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the name of Rebbi Eliezer: "The Ark was deported with them to Babylon. What is the reason? *There will not remain a word*. *Word* refers only to what contains the Words. And so it says, *at the turn of the year king Nebuchadnezzar sent and brought him to Babylon with the desirable vessels of the Eternal's House*. What are *the desirables of the Eternal's House*? This is the Ark.

The *Mishna*'s reference to the fourteenth bowing opposite *Deir HaEtzim* opens the gateway to the *Gemara*'s extended discussion of the *Aron*. The *Aron* was not present in the *Bayit Sheni* — and the question now to be addressed is: what became of it?

## Aron Location

### Eliezer: Exile

The *Gemara* presents a *tani beshem Rabbi Eliezer*: *ha'aron gala imahem l'Bavel* — the *Aron* was not hidden beneath the *Beit HaMikdash* at all; it was taken into exile in Bavel along with all the other vessels of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *Gemara* asks *mai taama* — what is the basis for this view? The proof-text is the verse *lo yivater davar amar Hashem* — nothing shall be left behind, said the Lord. The word *davar* is interpreted as *dibrot*, the *Aseret HaDibrot*, meaning that even the vessel containing the Ten Commandments would not be spared from exile. A supporting verse is then adduced: *bitshuvat hashana shelach hamelech Nevuchadnetzar v'yevi'ehu Bavel im klei chemdat Beit Hashem* — at the turn of the year, Nevuchadnetzar sent and brought the king of Israel to Bavel along with the most precious vessels of the House of God. The *Gemara* asks *eizu klei chemdat Beit Hashem* — which vessel is the most precious? *Zeh ha'aron* — it is the *Aron*. According to this view, then, the *Aron* was carried off to Bavel together with the rest of the Temple's treasures. This of course creates a *tiyuvta* against the *Mishna* — but it should be noted that the source for Rabbi Eliezer's position is a *Baraita*, not the *Mishna* itself.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. בִּמְקוֹמוֹ הָיָה הָאָרוֹן גָּנוּז. הָדָא הוּא דִכְתִיב וַיַּאֲרִיכוּ הַבַּדִּים וַיֵּרָאוּ רָאשֵׁי הַבַּדִּים אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ אֶל פְּנֵי הַדְּבִיר וְלֹא יֵרָאוּ הַחוּצָה. כְּתִיב וַיֵּרָאוּ וְאַתְּ אָמַר וְלֹא יֵרָאוּ. אֶלָּא נִרְאִין וְלֹא נִרְאִין. בּוֹלְטִין וְיוֹצְאִין כִּשְׁנֵי דַדֵּי הָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish said, at its place it was hidden. That is what is written, *the poles were long; the tips of the poles were seen in the Sanctuary, in front of the Hall, but were not seen outside,* It is written, *were seen*, and you are saying, *but were not seen*. They protrude and stick out like a woman's breasts.

Reish Lakish takes a different position: *bimkomo haya ha'aron nignaz* — the *Aron* was hidden in its own place, namely beneath the *Kodesh HaKodashim*. His proof-text is the verse *vayarichu habadim vayira'u rashei habadim min hakodesh al pnei hadevir v'lo yira'u hachutza* — the poles of the *Aron* extended so that their tips could be seen from the *Kodesh HaKodashim* toward the *devir*, yet they could not be seen outside. The *Korban HaEdah* notes that the end of this verse reads *vayiheyu sham ad hayom hazeh* — and they remained there until this very day. While the plain sense is that this phrase refers to the time of composition in *Melachim*, during the period of the *Bayit Rishon*, Reish Lakish reads *ad hayom hazeh* expansively: wherever you are reading this verse, that is where the *Aron* remains — hidden beneath the *Kodesh HaKodashim*.

Having cited this verse, the *Gemara* notes an internal tension: the verse begins *vayira'u* — they were seen — yet concludes *v'lo yira'u hachutza* — they were not seen outside. The resolution offered is *nirim v'lo nirim* — they were seen and yet not seen. The poles protruded into the *parochet*, the curtain, so that *boltim v'yotzim kishnei dadei ha'isha* — they pressed outward like a woman's breasts. One could perceive their form through the curtain, but the poles themselves remained invisible. This imagery belongs to a broader set of erotic or nurturing motifs associated with the *Aron* and the *keruvim*, which elsewhere are described as intertwined like lovers. The operative point, however, is the end of the verse — *ad hayom hazeh* — which establishes that the *Aron* remains in that place to this day.

### Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנִן אָמְרִין. בְּלִישְׁכַּת דִּיר הָעֵצִים הָיָה הָאָרוֹן גָּנוּז. מַעֲשֶׂה בְכֹהֵן אֶחָד בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁהָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַפְצִיעַ עֵצִים בְּלִישְׁכַּת דִּיר הָעֵצִים וְרָאָה אֶת הָרִצְפָּה שֶׁהִיא מְשׁוּנָּה מֵחֲבֵרוֹתֶיהָ. בָּא וְאָמַר לַחֲבֵירוֹ. בּוֹא וּרְאֵה אֶת הָרִצְפָּה הַזֹּאת שֶׁהִיא מְשׁוּנָּה מֵחֲבֵרוֹתֶיהָ. לֹא הִסְפִּיקוּ לִגְמוֹר אֶת הַדָּבָר עַד שֶׁיָּצְתָה נִשְׁמְתוֹ. וְיָדְעוּ בְיִיחוּד שֶׁשָּׁם הָאָרוֹן גָּנוּז.
>
> **Translation:** But the rabbis say, the Ark was hidden in the storage room of the wood. It happened that a blemished Cohen was splitting wood in the storage room of the wood and saw a floor plate different from the others. He came and said to a colleague, come and see this floor plate which is different from the others. They did not finish the matter before his soul left him; then they knew that there the Ark was hidden.

The *Mishna*'s own position is that of *Rabbanan*: *belishkat ha'etzim haya ha'Aron ganuz* — the *Aron* was hidden beneath the wood-storage room. No verse is adduced in support; it is presented simply as a received tradition.

### Kohen's Discovery

The *Gemara* then relates the *ma'aseh*: a *kohen* who had a physical *mum* and was therefore barred from performing the *avodah* was assigned to peel wood in *lishkat ha'etzim*. As he worked, *vera'ah et haritzpah shehi meshunah machavroteiha* — he noticed that one section of the floor looked different from the rest. He went to tell a colleague — *bo re'eh et haritzpah hazot shehi meshunah machavroteiha* — but *velo hispik ligmor et hadavar ad sheyatzata nishmato* — before he could finish speaking or show his colleague precisely where the unusual section lay, he died. From this they knew with certainty — *veyadu beyichud shesham Aron ganuz* — that he had discovered the hiding place of the *Aron*, and this is the very tradition referred to in the *Mishna*. The incident raises an obvious question: why could others not simply inspect the floor of *lishkat ha'etzim* themselves? Presumably the anomaly required extremely close attention to detect, and the knowledge died with the *kohen*.

### Digging & Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי רִבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָה. הִקִּישׁ עָלֶיהָ בְקוּרְנָס וְיָצָאת אֵשׁ וּשְׂרָפָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Hoshaia stated: He hit on it with a sledgehammer; fire erupted and burned him.

A *Baraita* of Rabbi Oshaya then describes what happened when someone tried to act on this knowledge: *hikishu aleiha bekurnas* — they struck the floor with a heavy sledgehammer-like implement, *veyatza esh vesarfato* — and fire emerged and burned him. It is possible, as one reading of the *Baraita* suggests, that this refers to the same *kohen* — that this is a parallel account of how he died. But the more natural reading is that others, having learned the approximate location, attempted to dig and were struck down by the fire. The episode is, as the lecturer notes, evocative of the deadly booby-traps of ancient lore: the hidden *Aron* is guarded by divine fire, and any attempt to unearth it is met with destruction.

## One or Two Arons

### Two Arons Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. רִבִּי יוּדָה בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. שְׁנֵי אֲרוֹנוֹת הָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר. אֶחָד שֶׁהָיְתָה הַתּוֹרָה נְתוּנָה בְתוֹכוֹ. וְאֶחָד שֶׁהָיוּ שִׁבְרֵי הַלּוּחוֹת נְתוּנִין בְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: "Rebbi Jehudah ben Laqish says, two arks were travelling with Israel in the desert; one in which the Torah was deposited and one in which the broken pieces of the tablets were deposited.

A *Baraita* in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish asserts: *shnei aronot hayu mehalchin im Yisrael bamidbar* — two *aronot* traveled with Israel through the wilderness. One contained the *Torah* — meaning the *luchot* — and one contained the *shivrei luchot*, the broken tablets. The proposal is motivated by tensions in the biblical text: if one follows the *pesukim* carefully, there is apparent confusion about whether the *Aron* traveled with the camp, whether it preceded them, and when exactly various events occurred. Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish resolves this by positing two distinct arks.

### Pessukim Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה שֶׁהָיְתָה הַתּוֹרָה נְתוּנָה בְתוֹכוֹ הָיָה מוּנַח בְּאֹהֵל מוֹעֵד. הָדָא הִיא דִכְתִיב וַאֲרוֹן בְּרִית יְי וּמֹשֶׁה לֹא מָשׁוּ מִקֶּרֶב הַמַּחֲנֶה. זֶה שֶׁהָיוּ שִׁבְרֵי הַלּוּחוֹת בְתוֹכוֹ הָיָה נִכְנַס וְיוֹצֵא עִמָּהֶן וּפְעָמִים הוּא מַתְרֶה עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The one in which the Torah was deposited was put into the Tent of Meeting; that is what is written: *Moses and the Ark of the Eternal's covenant did not move from the camp*. The one in which the broken pieces of the tablets were deposited was going out and coming in with them.

The first *Aron*, the one containing the *luchot*, *haya munach be'ohel mo'ed* — remained within the *Ohel Mo'ed*. This is derived from the verse *va'aron berit Hashem uMoshe lo mashu mi-kerev ha-machaneh* — the *Aron* of the covenant of God and Moshe did not move from the camp. The significance of this formulation becomes clear by contrast with the second *Aron*, the one containing the *shivrei luchot*: *haya nichnas v'yotzei imahem* — that one would go out with them when they traveled and when they went to war. The verse *va'aron berit Hashem nose'a lifneihem latur lahem menucha* — the *Aron* of the covenant of God traveled before them to seek out a resting place — refers to this second *Aron*. Similarly, the verse *kuma Hashem v'yafutzu* belongs to this second *Aron* that went out with them to battle. What initially appeared to be a contradiction — sometimes the *Aron* stays in the camp, sometimes it goes before them — is resolved by the existence of two separate arks.

### One Aron Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנִן אָמְרֵי. אָרוֹן אֶחָד הָיָה וּפַעַם אַחַת יָצָא בִימֵי עֵלִי וְנִשְׁבָּה. קִרְיָיה מְסַיֵיעַ לְהוֹן לְרַבָּנִן. אוֹי לָנוּ מִי יַצִּילֵנוּ מִיַּד הָאֱלֹהִים הָאַדִּירִים הָאֵלֶּה. מִילָּה דְלָא חֲמוּן מִן יוֹמֵיהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** But the Rabbis say, it was only one, and once it went out in the days of Eli and was taken prisoner. A verse supports the Rabbis: *Woe to us, who will save us from this mighty god?* A word which shows that they never had seen it before.

*Rabbanan*, however, maintain that there was only ever one *Aron*: *aron echad haya*. The single occasion on which it went out to war was at the time of Eli, when it was captured by the *Plishtim* — and that was an exceptional and unauthorized act, not standard practice. In support of the rabbinic view, the *Gemara* cites the verse *oy lanu mi yatzilenu miyad ha-elohim ha-adirim ha-eleh* — woe to us, who will save us from the hands of this great God? — which was the *Plishtim*'s cry upon seeing the *Aron* being brought out. The *Gemara* reads this as indicating that the *Plishtim* had never before witnessed such a thing in all their days — *mila d'lo chamun mi-yomeihon* — which implies that taking the *Aron* to battle was a genuine novelty, not a regular practice. This supports the rabbinic position that it went out only that once.

### More Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִרְיָיה מְסַיֵיעַ לְרִבִּי יוּדָה בֶּן לָקִישׁ. וַיֹּאמֶר שָׁאוּל לַאֲחִיָּה הַגִּישָׁה אֲרוֹן הָאֱלֹהִים. וַהֲלֹא אָרוֹן בְּקִרְיַת יְעָרִים הָיָה. מָה עָבְדוּן לֵיהּ רַבָּנִן. הַגִּישָׁה אֵלַי הַצִּיץ. קִרְיָיא מְסַיֵיעַ לְרִבִּי יוּדָה בֶּן לָקִישׁ. הָאָרוֹן וְיִשְׂרָאֵל וִיהוּדָה יֹשְׁבִים בַּסּוּכּוֹת. וַהֲלֹא אָרוֹן בְּצִיּוֹן הָיָה. מָה עָבְדוּן לֵיהּ רַבָּנִן. סְכָךְ שֶׁהוּא כְקִירוּי. שֶׁאַדַּיִין לֹא נִבְנֶה בֵית הַבְּחִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** A verse supports Rebbi Jehudah ben Laqish. *Saul said to Aḥiya: present God's Ark*. But was the Ark not at Qiryat Ye`arim? What do the rabbis do with it? 'Present to me the High Priest's diadem.' Another verse supports Rebbi Jehudah ben Laqish: *The Ark, Israel, and Jehudah, dwell in huts*. Was the Ark not in Zion? What do the rabbis with it? The straw roof cover that was in the walls, since the Temple was not yet built.

Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish, however, adduces counter-evidence from *Nach*. The first is the episode of Sha'ul and Achiya: *vayomer Sha'ul le-Achiya hagisha aron ha-elohim* — Sha'ul said to Achiya, bring forth the *Aron* of God. Yet, the *Gemara* notes, *v'halo aron beKiryat Ye'arim haya* — the *Aron* was in Kiryat Ye'arim at the time! If Sha'ul was calling for the *Aron*, there must have been a separate *Aron* that could be brought to the battlefield. The *Rabbanan* respond that what Sha'ul was calling for was not the *Aron* at all but rather the *tzitz*, which is referred to here as *aron ha-elohim* perhaps because it too carried divine communication in some sense. The *Gemara* acknowledges that this is a difficult reading.

The second proof-text adduced by Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish is from the story of Uriya and David: *ha'aron v'Yisrael v'Yehuda yoshvim basukkot* — the *Aron* and Israel and Judah are dwelling in booths, out on the battlefield. Yet *v'halo aron beTzion haya* — the *Aron* was in Tzion at the time. Must there not be a second *Aron* that had gone out with the troops? The *Rabbanan* explain that *sukkot* here does not mean literal field-tents; it refers to an impermanent dwelling, *she-adayin lo nivna beit ha-bechira* — because the permanent Temple had not yet been built. Uriya's point was that the *Aron* in Yerushalayim was still housed only in a temporary structure, not in a fully established home — which was reason enough not to enjoy his own home. The plain sense of the verse does support Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish's reading, but the *Rabbanan* find a way to read *basukkot* as a reference to impermanence rather than to a battlefield location.

## Hidden Items

### Manna & Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁנִּגְנַז הָאָרוֹן נִגְנַז עִמּוֹ צִנְצֶנֶּת הַמָּן וּצְלוֹחִית שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה וּמַקְלוֹ שֶׁלְאַהֲרוֹן וּפְרָחָיו וּשְׁקֵידָיו וְאַרְגָּז שֶׁהֵשִׁיבוּ פְלִשְׁתִּים אָשָׁם לֵאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** When the ark was hidden, there were hidden with it the flask of Manna, the bottle of anointing oil, Aaron's staff with its almonds and flowers, and the chest which the Philistines returned as a reparation sacrifice for Israel's God.

From the time the *Aron* was hidden — *mishenignaz ha'aron* — various other sacred objects were hidden alongside it. The first is the *tzintzenet ha-man*, the container of *manna*, about which the verse says *kach tzintzenet achat v'hanach otah lifnei ha-edut lemishmeret* — take a container and place it before the testimony as a safekeeping. Since it was placed *lifnei ha-edut*, alongside the *Aron*, the assumption is that whatever befell the *Aron* also befell the *manna*. The second is the *tzlochit shemen ha-mishchah*, the flask of *shemen hamishcha*, about which the verse says *shemen mishchat kodesh yihyeh zeh li ledoroteichem* — this shall be sacred anointing oil for Me throughout your generations. A *hekesh* is drawn between the two: just as the *tzintzenet ha-man* was *lemishmeret ledoroteichem*, so too the *shemen hamishcha* was preserved for all generations, and both were therefore hidden with the *Aron*.

### Aaron's Staff

Also hidden alongside the *Aron* was *maklo shel Aharon u-frachav u-shkedav* — Aaron's staff together with its blossoms and its almonds. Here too the verse instructs that the staff be placed *lifnei ha-edut lemishmeret le'ot livnei meri* — before the testimony as a safekeeping for a sign for the rebellious. The repeated formula of *lemishmeret* — preserved for all generations — establishes that the staff, like the other items, was to endure forever, and was accordingly hidden when the *Aron* was hidden.

### Philistine Gift

Finally, *v'argaz she-shigru Plishtim asham l'Elohei Yisrael* — the box of gifts that the *Plishtim* sent as a guilt-offering to the God of Israel when they returned the *Aron* — was also hidden with it. When the *Plishtim* returned the *Aron*, they sent a golden box alongside it, and the verse instructs *tasimu ba-argaz mitzido* — place it in the box next to the *Aron*. Since the verse places this gift-box *next to* the *Aron*, the tradition understands that it permanently remained beside it. As the *Gemara*'s *Kodesh HaKodashim* grew crowded with all these objects, whatever ultimately happened to the *Aron* happened to all of them together.

## Yoshiyahu Hides

### Yoshiyahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי גְנָזוֹ. יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ גְנָזוֹ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאָה שֶׁכָּתוּב יוֹלֵךְ יְי אוֹתְךָ וְאֶת מַלְכְּךָ אֲשֶׁר תָּקִים עָלֶיךָ אֶל גּוֹי אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָדַעְתָּ אַתָּה וַאֲבֹתֶיךָ. עָמַד וּגְנָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Who hid it? Josiah hid it. When he saw that it was written: *The Eternal will lead you and the king you set over yourself to a people whom* neither you nor your fathers had known, he started and hid it.

*Mi genazo* — who actually hid the *Aron*? *Yoshiyahu genazo* — it was Yoshiyahu. Not the last of the kings of Judah, but one who was already perceptive enough to see what was coming. *Keivan sher'ah shekatuv* — when he saw what was written in the verse *yolech Hashem otcha v'et malkecha asher takim alecha el goy asher lo yadata atah va'avotecha* — God will take you and the king you set over yourself to a nation that neither you nor your ancestors have known — he understood the exile was coming, and acted accordingly: *amad u-genazo* — he arose and hid it.

### Pessukim Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָדָא הוּא דִכְתִיב וַיֹּאמֶר לַלְוִיִּים הַמְּבִינִים לְכָל יִשְׂרָאֵל הַקְּדוֹשִׁים לַיי תְּנוּ אֶת אֲרוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ בַּבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה שְׁלֹמֹה בֶן דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין לָכֶם מַשָּׂא בַּכָּתֵף. אָמַר לָהֶן. אִם גּוֹלֶה הוּא עִמָּכֶם לְבָבֶל עוֹד אֵין אַתֶּם מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתוֹ לִמְקוֹמוֹ. אֶלָּא עַתָּה עִבְדוּ אֶת יְי אֱלֹהֵיכֶם וְאֵת עַמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** That is what is written: *He said to the Levites, who instruct all of Israel, the ones holy to the Eternal, put the Ark into the House that Salomon, son of David, King of Israel, built; you do not have to carry it on your shoulder."* He said, if it is exiled with you to Babylonia, you will never return it to its place. But *now, serve the Eternal, your God, and his people Israel.*

The proof-text for Yoshiyahu's action is the verse *vayomer laleviyim hamevinim lechol Yisrael hakedoshim l'Hashem tnu et aron hakodesh babayit asher banah Shlomo ben David melech Yisrael ein lachem masa bakatef* — he said to the Levites, place the sacred *Aron* in the house that Solomon son of David built; you shall no longer carry it on your shoulders. The straightforward reading of this verse is that it refers to the biblical era when the *Aron* was carried on the shoulders. But the *Gemara* reads it as Yoshiyahu's instructions to hide the *Aron* beneath the *Beit HaMikdash*. His reasoning: *im goleh hu imachem l'Vavel, ein atem machzirim oto od limkomo* — if it goes into exile with you to Bavel, you will never have the opportunity to bring it back. Therefore *ein lachem masa bakatef* — this object will no longer be transported at all; it must be secured before the exile comes. The verse concludes: *ela atah ivdu et Hashem Eloheichem ve'et amo Yisrael* — now serve God your God and His people Israel. This is read as a *midrashic* interpretation embedded in the *pesukim*, one that assumes the *Aron* was indeed hidden rather than exiled.

## Shemen HaMishcha

### Oil Recipe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּיטּוּם שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה וְאַתָּה קַח לְךָ בְּשָׂמִים רֹאשׁ וגו׳ וְקִדָּה חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת וגו׳. שֶׁהֵן אֶלֶף וַחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מָנִים. שֶׁמֶן זַיִת הִין. שֶׁהֵן שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר לוֹג שֶׁבּוֹ שׁוֹלְקִין אֶת הָעִיקָּרִין. דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי מֵאִיר. רִבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר. שׁוֹלְקָן הָיָה בַמַּיִם וְנוֹתֵן שֶׁמֶן עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן. מִשֶּׁהָיָה קוֹלֵט אֶת הָרֵיחַ הָיָה מַעֲבִירוֹ כַּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַפַּטָּמִין עושין.
>
> **Translation:** The preparation of the anointing oil. *Take for yourself select spices,* etc. *and cassia 500,* etc., altogether 1500 parts. *And olive oil one hin*, that is twelve *log*, in which the roots were cooked, the words of Rebbi Meνr. Rebbi Jehudah says, he cooked them in water, then poured the oil on them, and when it had absorbed the fragrance one removed it, just as the perfumers do.

Since the *shemen hamishcha* was mentioned among the items hidden with the *Aron*, the *Gemara* takes the opportunity for an extended digression on this remarkable substance. The *Torah* specifies its recipe: *v'atah kach lecha besamim rosh — mor dror chamesh me'ot, u-kinman besem machtzito chamishim u-matayim, u-kneh bosem chamishim u-matayim, u-kidah chamesh me'ot beshekel hakodesh, v'shemen zayit hin*. Adding up the aromatic components — *she-hen elef u-chamesh me'ot manin* — yields 1,500 *maneh* of fragrances to be combined with a single *hin*, twelve *log*, of olive oil. The question immediately arises: how does such a small quantity of oil absorb such a vast quantity of fragrance?

The answer is as follows. The oil served as the medium into which the fragrances were steeped. *She-hayu sholkim bo et ha-ikarim* — one would use the oil to boil the roots and herbs of the spices — according to Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda offers a different procedure: first *sholkan haya bemayim* — one would boil the fragrant materials in water, fully saturating them, so that they could absorb no more liquid. Then *venosein shemen al gabeihen* — one would place the oil on top of them. Since the materials were already fully saturated with water and could absorb no more, *mishaa shekalat et hare'ach, haya maaviro* — once the oil drew out the fragrance from the saturated herbs, it was removed, now bearing the full aromatic quality. This is the technique *kederech shehapitamim osim* — the way professional perfumers work.

### Meir vs Yehuda

The practical difference between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda lies in the sequence and medium. For Rabbi Meir, the oil itself is the cooking medium from the outset — the spices are boiled directly in the oil. For Rabbi Yehuda, the spices are first saturated with water so that the oil, when added, cannot be absorbed into the spices but instead draws only the fragrance out. Rabbi Yehuda's method, drawn from the practice of professional perfumers, ensures maximal transfer of fragrance into the oil with minimal absorption of the oil itself into the spice material.

### Oil Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי רִבִּי יוּדָה בֵּירִבִּי אִילְעַאי. שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה שֶׁעָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה בַמִּדְבָּר מַעֲשֵׂה נִיסִּים נַעֲשׂוּ בוֹ מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף. שֶׁמִּתְּחִילָּה לֹא הָיָה בוֹ אֶלָּא שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר לוֹג. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר שֶׁמֶן זַיִת הִין. אִם לָסוּךְ בּוֹ אֶת הָעִיקָּרִים לֹא הָיָה בוֹ סָפַק. עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה שֶׁהָאוֹר בּוֹלֵעַ וְהָעֵצִים בּוֹלְעִין וְהַיּוֹרָה בוֹלַעַת. וּמִמֶּנּוּ נִמשְׁחוּ הַמִּשְׁכָּן וְכָל כֵּלָיו. הַשֻּׁלְחָן וְכָל כֵּלָיו. הַמְּנוֹרָה וְכָל כֵּלֶיהָ. וּמִמֶּנּוּ נִמשְׁחוּ אַהֲרֹן כֹהֵן גָּדוֹל וּבָנָיו כָּל שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּילּוּאִים. מִמֶּנּוּ נִמְשְׁחוּ כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים וּמְלָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Jehudah bar Ilay stated: The anointing oil made by Moses in the desert was from the start to the end a work of wonders, since there were only twelve log to start with, as it was said, *and olive oil one hin*. It would not have been enough to rub the wooden planks with it; so much more since the fire swallows, wood absorbs, and the kettle absorbs! From it the Tabernacles and all its vessels were anointed, the table and its vessels, the candelabrum and all its vessels. From it Aaron and his sons were anointed all of the seven days of induction, from it all high priests and kings were anointed.

A *Baraita* in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Rabbi Ilai states: *shemen hamishcha she-asa Moshe bamidbar, ma'aseh nissim na'asu vo mitchila v'ad sof* — the *shemen hamishcha* that Moshe prepared in the wilderness was miraculous from beginning to end. It began as only twelve *log* — *shene'emar shemen zayit hin*, a *hin* being twelve *log*. Yet even the simple act of smearing the oil onto the fragrant materials to absorb their scent would, by ordinary reckoning, have used up all twelve *log* before any oil remained — *al achas kama v'chama* when one considers that fire evaporates oil, the wooden structure of the *mishkan* absorbs it, and the cooking vessel absorbs more. And yet from these twelve *log*, the following was accomplished: *umimeno nimshachu hamishkan v'chol kelav, hashulchan v'chol kelav, hamenorah v'chol keleiha* — the entire *Mishkan* and all its vessels were anointed; *umimeno nimshachu Aharon Kohen Gadol u-vanav kol shivat yemei hamilu'im* — Aaron and his sons were anointed every day for all seven days of the inauguration; and *umimeno nimshachu kohanim gedolim u-melachim* — from it, every subsequent *Kohen Gadol* and king was anointed.

The miracle is further illuminated by a *derasha* on the verse *shemen mishchat kodesh yihyeh zeh li ledoroteichem*. The word *zeh* in *gematria* equals twelve, and the *midrash* reads: the twelve *log* with which it began — *ledoroteichem* — that same amount was always there, generation after generation. Like the *nes Chanukah*, the oil was never diminished. One began with twelve *log*, performed all of this with it, and twelve *log* always remained.

## Uses of Oil

### King Anointing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֶלֶךְ בַּתְּחִילָּה טָעוּן מְשִׁיחָה. מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ אֵינוֹ טָעוּן מְשִׁיחָה. אֲבָל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בֶּן כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל אֲפִילוּ עַד עֲשָׂרָה דוֹרוֹת טְעוֹנִין מְשִׁיחָה. וְכולּוֹ קַיָים לְעָתִיד לָבוֹא.
>
> **Translation:** A king who is first needs anointing, a king who is a king's son does not need anointing. But a High Priest who is the son of a High Priest needs anointing, even for ten generations. Nevertheless, it is all there for the future.

The *Gemara* now turns to the uses of the *shemen hamishcha* in the investiture of kings and *kohanim gedolim*. The principle is: *melech betchila ta'un meshicha* — when someone becomes king for the first time, establishing a new royal line, anointing is required. This applies to the founding kings, such as Shaul and David. *Melech ben melech ein ta'un meshicha* — but a king who is the son of a king does not require anointing, since kingship is fundamentally hereditary. The *pasuk* cited is *kum mishcha'ehu ki zeh hu* — arise and anoint him, for this is the one. The word *zeh* implies that *this one* requires anointing, but not his son.

### Kohen Gadol

The rule is precisely the opposite for the *Kohen Gadol*: *aval Kohen Gadol ben Kohen Gadol, afilu ad asara doros, ta'un meshicha* — a *Kohen Gadol* who is the son of a *Kohen Gadol*, even if the position has passed through ten generations, still requires fresh anointing. The underlying logic is structural: kingship is intrinsically hereditary, so no new act of investiture is necessary — to perform one would actually undermine the sense of natural succession. The office of *Kohen Gadol*, by contrast, is not intrinsically hereditary. The *Gemara* in *Horayot* acknowledges that a son has some priority, all else being equal, but priority is not the same as automatic inheritance. Since the *Kohen Gadol*'s status is not self-transmitting, each holder of the office — including the son of a previous *Kohen Gadol* — requires a fresh act of anointing to be invested in that status.

The same principle governs the miraculous preservation of the oil: *v'chulo kayam la'atid lavo* — all of it will be intact into the future as well, as the verse *shemen mishchat kodesh yihyeh zeh li ledoroteichem* promises. The *derasha* on *zeh* — numerically twelve — reinforces that the original twelve *log* will always remain whole.

### Which Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹשְׁחִין מְלָכִים אֶלָּא עַל גַּבֵּי הַמַּעַיָין. הָדָא הוּא דִכְתִיב וְהִרְכַּבְתֶּם אֶת שְׁלֹמֹה בְנִי עַל הַפִּרְדָּה אֲשֶׁר לִי וְהוֹרַדְתֶּם אוֹתוֹ אֶל גִּחוֹן. וּמָשַׁח אֹתוֹ שָׁם צָדוֹק הַכֹּהֵן וְנָתָן הַנָּבִיא לְמֶלֶךְ עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֵין מוֹשְׁחִין מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי הַמַּחֲלוֹקֶת. מִפְּנֵי מַה נִמְשַׁח שְׁלֹמֹה. מִפְּנֵי מַחֲלוֹקָתוֹ שֶׁלָּאֲדוֹנִיָּהוּ. יוֹאָשׁ מִפְּנֵי עֲתַלְיָה. יֵהוּא מִפְּנֵי יוֹרָם. לֹא כֵן כָּתוּב קוּם מְשָׁחֵהוּ כִּי זֶה הוּא. זֶה טָעוּן מְשִׁיחָה וְאֵין מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל טְעוּנִים מְשִׁיחָה. אֶלָּא יְהוֹאָחָז מִפְּנֵי יְהוּיָקִים אָחִיו שֶׁהָיָה גָדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** One anoints kings only at a spring, as it was said: *Let Solomon, my son, ride on my mule and take him down to the Giḥon; there Ṣadoq the priest and* Nathan the prophet shall anoint him as king over Israel. One anoints kings sons of kings only because of disputes. Why was Solomon anointed? Because of the dispute of Adoniahu, Joash because of Athaliah, Jehu because of Joram. Is it not written: *do anoint him, for this one is it,* this one needs anointing, but the kings of Israel do not need anointing? But Joaḥaz because of his brother Joiakim who was two years his elder.

The *Gemara* now specifies when, exceptionally, a king who is the son of a king does require anointing: *ein moshchin melech ben melech ela mipnei hamachlokes* — only when there is a dispute over succession. Several historical examples are enumerated. Shlomo was anointed because of the competing claim of Adoniyahu. Yoash was anointed because of Atalyahu's usurpation. Yehoachaz was anointed because his brother Yehoyakim, who was two years older, had a competing claim. Yehu was anointed because of Yoram. The *Gemara* then raises a question against the inclusion of Yehu: a broader principle is now asserted that anointing with *shemen hamishcha* applies only to *malchei Beit David*, the kings of the Davidic dynasty, not to *Malchei Yisrael*, the kings of the Northern Kingdom. If so, the inclusion of Yehu and Yoram — kings of the North — in this list is problematic. The *Gemara* answers: trim those words from the *Baraita*. The list should end with Yehoachaz, and the mention of Yehu was not a reference to *shemen hamishcha* at all.

### Aromatic Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִאשִׁיָּהוּ גְּנָזוֹ. הָדָא אָמְרָה. בְּאַפֵּירְסָמוֹן מְשָׁחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** But did not Josiah hide it. Therefore none of Josia's sons could be anointed with the anointing oil. That means that they anointed with balsamum.

The *Gemara* then raises a further question: *v'halo Yoshiyahu genazo* — did Yoshiyahu not hide the *shemen hamishcha* together with the *Aron*? If so, how could Yehoyakim and Yehoachaz have been anointed with it? The answer is attributed to Ada: *bafarsimon nimshach* — they were anointed with plain aromatic oil, not with the true *shemen hamishcha*. The *shemen hamishcha* had been hidden by Yoshiyahu, and so the anointings of Yoshiyahu's sons were performed with an aromatic substitute.

Some of the *meforshim* draw from this an additional insight: since the anointing of Yehu too was presumably done without *shemen hamishcha* — as it occurred before Yoshiyahu but among *Malchei Yisrael* in any case — the reference to anointing with a substitute oil could extend to *Malchei Yisrael* as well under certain conditions. This would mean one need not strike the words about Yehu from the *Baraita* at all, since those anointings used only aromatic oil. And even with aromatic oil, the practice was reserved for situations of genuine uncertainty about succession — to perform it unnecessarily would subtly undermine the sense of hereditary legitimacy.

## Uses of Oil (II)

### Horn vs Pitcher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹשְׁחִין מְלָכִים אֶלָּא מִן הַקֶּרֶן. שָׁאוּל וְיֵהוּא נִמְשְׁחוּ מִן הַפָּךְ. שֶׁהָיְתָה מַלְכוּתָן מַלְכוּת עוֹבֶרֶת. דָּוִד וּשְׁלֹמֹה נִמְשְׁחוּ מִן הַקֶּרֶן. שֶׁהָיְתָה מַלְכוּתָן מַלְכוּת קַיֶימֶת.
>
> **Translation:** One anoints kings only from a horn. Saul and Jehu were anointed from a can because their kingdom was temporary; David and Solomon were anointed from a horn because their kingdom was permanent.

The *Gemara* adds a further distinction regarding the vessel from which kings are anointed: *ein moshchin hamelachim ela min hakeren* — kings are anointed from a horn, not from a simple container. The horn, like a cornucopia, evokes abundance, permanence, and the proclamation of a new reign. The proof comes from the contrast between Shaul and Yehu on one hand, and David and Shlomo on the other. *Shaul v'Yehu nimshakhu min hapakh* — Shaul and Yehu were anointed from a simple pitcher — *hayta malkhutam malkhut overet* — and their dynasties were temporary and did not endure. *David u-Shlomo nimshakhu min ha-keren* — David and Shlomo were anointed from a horn — *hayta malkhutam malkhut kayyemet* — and their dynasty was permanent. The verse *matzmiach keren yeshua* — the One who causes the horn of salvation to flourish — carries precisely this symbolism of royal permanence and saving power associated with the *keren*.

### No Kohen Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹשְׁחִין כֹּהֲנִים מְלָכִים. אָמַר רִבִּי יוּדָן עַנְתּוֹנְדְרַיָיא. עַל שֵׁם לֹא יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה. אָמַר רִבִּי חִיָיה בַּר אָדָא. לְמַעַן יַאֲרִיךְ יָמִים עַל מַמְלַכְתּוֹ הוּא וּבָנָיו בְּקֶרֶב יִשְׂרָאֵל. מַה כְתִיב בַּתְרֵיהּ. לֹא יִהְיֶה לַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם.
>
> **Translation:** One does not anoint priests as kings. Rebbi Jehudah Antordiya said, because *the scepter shall not be removed from Jehudah*. Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Ada said, because of *he shall have many days of his kingdom, he and his sons in the midst of Israel*. What is written after that? *The levitic Cohanim should not*.

*Ein moshchin kohanim melachim* — one does not anoint *kohanim* as kings. The *Gemara* here touches on the danger of combining religious and political authority in a single figure, a concern that resonates especially in light of the *Chashmonaim*. At one level the prohibition is about *meshicha* specifically — anointing for kingship is restricted to *malchut Beit David* — so the problem with anointing a *kohen* as king subsumes the general problem of anointing anyone outside the Davidic line. But there is also a distinct and pointed objection to *kohanim* taking kingship, rooted in the principle *lo yasur shevet miYehuda* — the scepter shall not depart from Judah. Rabbi Yehuda Antundaraya derives from this verse that kingship cannot belong to any other tribe.

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba adds a second verse: the Torah describes the king's obligation to write a *Sefer Torah* so that *lema'an ya'arich yamim al mamlachto hu u-vanav bekerev Yisrael* — his kingship should extend, he and his children. *Mah ketiv batreih* — what does the very next verse say? *Lo yihyeh lakohanim haleviyim* — juxtaposed immediately to the king and his children is the statement that this role does not belong to the *kohanim*. Reading the two verses as a single thought, kingship shall extend to his children — and this is not for the *kohanim*.

The *Ramban*, as is well known, comments in his *perush* on the verse *lo yasur shevet miYehuda* in connection with the *Chashmonaim*, noting that they transgressed by taking upon themselves the kingship. He also observes that *Chanukah* receives virtually no treatment in the *Mishna* — mentioned only once incidentally in *Bava Kamma* — and suggests that this reflects the *Tanna'im*'s disapproval of how the *Chashmonaim* claimed royal authority, a silence that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who understood himself as a descendant of David HaMelech, may have deliberately maintained.

## Historical Kings (II)

### Sons ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. הוּא יוֹחָנָן הוּא יְהוֹאָחָז. וְהָא כְתִיב הַבְּכוֹר יוֹחָנָן. בְּכוֹר לַמַּלְכוּת. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. הוּא שַׁלּוּם הוּא צִדְקִיָּהוּ. וְהָכְתִיב הַשְּׁלִישִׁי צִדְקִיָּהוּ וְהָרְבִיעִי שַׁלּוּם. שְׁלִישִׁי לְתוֹלְדוֹת רְבִיעִי לַמַּלְכוּת. צִדְקִיָּהוּ שֶׁצִּידֵּק עָלָיו מִידַּת הַדִּין. שַׁלּוּם שֶׁבְּיָמָיו שָׁלְמָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד. לָא שַׁלּוּם הֲוָה שְׁמֵיהּ וְלָא צִדְקִיָּהוּ הֲוָה שְׁמֵיהּ אֶלָּא מַתַּנְיָה. הָדָא הוּא דִכְתִיב וַיַּמְלֵךְ מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל אֶת מַתַּנְיָה דוֹדוֹ תַּחְתָּיו וַיַּסֵּב אֶת שְׁמוֹ צִדְקִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, Joḥanan is Joaḥaz. But is it not written: *The first born Joḥanan*, the first in kingdom. Rebbi Joḥanan said, Shallum is Sedekiah. But is it not written, *the third Sedekiah, the fourth Shallum*? *Sedekiah*, the third in birth, the fourth in kingdom. *Sedekiah*, because he accepted the judgment on himself, *Shallum*, because in his days the dynasty of David was completed. His name was neither Shallum nor Sedekiah but Mattaniah. That is what is written: *The king of Babylon made his uncle Mattaniah* king in his stead and changed his name into Sedekiah*.

The *Gemara* now works to identify the sons of Yoshiyahu mentioned across different biblical texts. Rabbi Yochanan says: *hu Yochanan hu Yehoachaz* — the same person is referred to in one text as Yochanan and in another as Yehoachaz. An apparent difficulty is raised: *v'ha ketiv habechor Yochanan* — in *Divrei Hayamim* the firstborn is listed as Yochanan — yet Yehoachaz was not the *bechor*. The resolution is *bechor lemalchuto* — he was first in *malchus*, meaning the first of Yoshiyahu's sons to reign. This is precisely the context we discussed earlier: Yehoachaz was anointed because Yehoyakim, who was two years older, had a competing claim. Yehoachaz/Yochanan received the kingship first despite not being the oldest.

Rabbi Yochanan further states: *hu Shalom hu Tzidkiyahu* — similarly, the same person appears in one text as Shalom and in another as Tzidkiyahu. But a textual problem immediately arises: *veha ketiv hashlishi Tzidkiyahu vehareviei Shalom* — one *pasuk* lists the third son as Tzidkiyahu and the fourth son as Shalom, making them appear to be two distinct individuals. The resolution: *gimel letoldot* — he was the third in birth order — *dalet lemalchus* — but the fourth of Yoshiyahu's sons to reign. Tzidkiyahu and Shalom were the same person, but two different aspects of his identity are being enumerated in different places.

The *Gemara* then addresses the significance of each name. The name *Tzidkiyahu* — *shetzidak alav midat hadin* — reflects the fact that he justified upon himself the divine judgment. The straightforward reading is that Tzidkiyahu accepted the justice of God's verdict when he was taken into exile by Nevuchadnetzar, acknowledging that divine justice had been done — a poignant acceptance given that this was the king whose eyes were put out. Some *meforshim* read the phrase differently, citing a passage in the *Bavli* suggesting that Nevuchadnetzar himself gave Tzidkiyahu this name as a kind of oath — *ka-yifra'acha* — that God would exact punishment from him if he rebelled, and of course he did rebel. This reading is forced, and the plain sense of the *Gemara* here is that Tzidkiyahu himself justified and accepted God's judgment.

As for the name *Shalom*, the *Gemara* reads it with dark irony: *shebeyamav shalma malchut beit David* — in his days the kingship of the house of David came to completion, to its end. *Shalom* as *shlemut* — finality, completion. Reish Lakish then adds a tradition on a different level: *lo Shalom hava shemo v'lo Tzidkiyahu hava shemo — ela Mattanya* — neither Shalom nor Tzidkiyahu was his original name. He was born Mattanya. This is not *midrash* but *peshat*, since the verse states explicitly: *vayimloch melech Bavel et Mattanya dodo tachtav vayasev et shemo Tzidkiyahu* — the king of Bavel placed Mattanya, his uncle, on the throne and changed his name to Tzidkiyahu. The text is clear: Nevuchadnetzar renamed him.

## Aron Contents

### Six-Tefach Amah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. בָּאַמָּה שֶׁל שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים הָיָה הָאָרוֹן עָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, the Ark was made with a cubit of six hand-breadths.

Rabbi Yochanan states: *be'ama shel shisha tefachim haya ha'aron asui* — the *Aron* was constructed using an *amah* of six *tefachim*. This is consistent with Rabbi Meir's position, recorded elsewhere in a *Mishna*: *Rabbi Meir omer kol ha'amot hayu beinoniyot* — Rabbi Meir holds that all the *amot* used in the *Beit HaMikdash* were standard six-*tefach* ones. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, distinguishes: *amot habinyan shisha* — structural *amot* were six *tefachim* — *v'shel kelim chamisha* — but the *amot* used for vessels were five *tefachim*. The *Aron*, being a *keli*, would according to Rabbi Yehuda have been built with a five-*tefach amah*.

Rabbi Yochanan's statement that the *Aron* used a six-*tefach amah* thus reflects Rabbi Meir's position.

### Length Calc

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי מֵאִיר דּוּ אָמַר. בָּאַמָּה שֶׁל שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים הָיָה הָאָרוֹן עָשׂוּי. אוֹרְכּוֹ שֶׁלְאָרוֹן חַמִּשָּׁה עָשָׂר טֶפַח. דִּכְתִיב אַמָּתַיִם וָחֵצִי אָרְכּוֹ. אַמְתָּא אֲשִׁיתָא וְאַמְתָּא אֲשִׁיתָא וּפַלְגּוּת אַמְתָּא תְּלָתָא. וְאַרְבָּעָה לוֹחוּת הָיוּ בּוֹ. שְׁנַיִם שְׁלֵימִים וּשְׁנַיִם שְׁבוּרִים. דִּכְתִיב אֲשֶׁר שִׁבַּרְתָּ וְשַׂמְתָּם בָּאָרוֹן. הַלּוּחוֹת הָיוּ כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד אָרְכּוֹ שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים וְרָחְבּוֹ שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rebbi Meνr who says that the Ark was made with a cubit of six hand-breadths, the length of the Ark was fifteen cubits, as it is written: *Its length two cubits and a half*. Each cubit was six and half a cubit three. Four tablets were in it, two whole ones and two broken ones, as it is written: *Which you broke and put into the Ark*. Each of the tablets was six hand-breadths in length and three in width.

According to Rabbi Meir's view — *al daiteh d'Rabbi Meir*, the six-*tefach amah* — the *Gemara* works out the dimensions in detail. The *Torah* specifies the *Aron*'s length as *amatayim vachetzi* — two and a half *amot*. At six *tefachim* per *amah*, this yields: one *amah* equals six, a second *amah* equals six, a half-*amah* equals three — a total of fifteen *tefachim* in length.

Within the *Aron* were four *luchot*: two whole and two broken. The verse *asher shibarta v'samtam ba'aron* — that which you broke, place them in the *Aron* — is read to indicate that both the intact *luchot* and the *shivrei luchot* were stored in the same *Aron*. This is consistent with the *Gemara*'s ruling elsewhere: *luchot v'shivrei luchot munachin ba'aron*. The *luchot* were square — six *tefachim* by six *tefachim* — as the *midrashic* tradition understands them, contrary to the familiar rectangular or arched popular image.

Placing the *luchot* in the length of the *Aron*: if the two intact *luchot* account for six *tefachim* each (twelve total), and the two broken ones are assumed to occupy the same space (stacked), then twelve *tefachim* of the fifteen-*tefach* length are accounted for. Three *tefachim* remain. Subtract half a *tefach* for each of the two walls — leaving two *tefachim* — and these two *tefachim* accommodate the *Sefer Torah*, which was six *tefachim* long but only two *tefachim* wide.

### Width Calc

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָחְבּוֹ שֶׁלְאָרוֹן תִשְׁעָה טְפָחִים. דִּכְתִיב אַמָּה וָחֵצִי רָחְבּוֹ. אַמְתָּא אֲשִׁיתָא וּפַלְגּוּת אַמְתָּא תְּלָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The width of the Ark was nine hand-breadths, as it is written: *A cubit and half a cubit*. A cubit six and half a cubit three.

The *Aron*'s width was *ama vachetzi rochbo* — one and a half *amot*. At six *tefachim* per *amah*, this yields: one *amah* equals six, half an *amah* equals three — nine *tefachim* total. The *luchot* being six *tefachim* wide, six *tefachim* of the nine are occupied. Three *tefachim* remain. Half a *tefach* for each wall accounts for one *tefach*, leaving two *tefachim*. These two *tefachim* provide *shirush makom* — breathing room, access space — alongside the *Sefer Torah*, allowing it to be removed when needed. Practically, this could mean one *tefach* of clearance on each side, providing access on both sides of the *Sefer Torah*.

Whether the four *luchot* were stacked two and two, or arranged side by side in a more rectangular configuration, depends on textual variants: one *girsa* reads that each *luach* was six *tefachim* by six *tefachim* (square), the other *girsa* reads three by six (rectangular). With the reading of three, the *luchot* would be arranged side by side in the width rather than stacked, which would yield a more intuitively familiar shape — though the *midrashic* tradition favors the square.

### Five-Tefach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. בָּאַמָּה שֶׁלְחֲמִשָּׁה טְפָחִים הָיָה הָאָרוֹן עָשׂוּי. וְהָהֵן אָרוֹן כֶּלִי הוּא. עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוּדָה דּוּ אָמַר. בָּאַמָּה שֶׁלְחֲמִשָּׁה טְפָחִים הָיָה אָרְכּוֹ שֶׁלְאָרוֹן עָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish said, the Ark was made using a cubit of five hand-breadths. And the Ark is a vessel. According to Rebbi Jehudah who says that the Ark was made with a cubit of five hand-breadths, the length of the Ark was twelve and one half cubits, as it is written: *Its length two cubits and a half*.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish holds that the *Aron* was constructed using a five-*tefach amah* — *be'ama bat chamisha tefachim haya ha'aron* — which corresponds to Rabbi Yehuda's view that vessels were measured by a five-*tefach amah*. *V'ha'aron keli hu* — the *Aron* is a vessel — and therefore falls under Rabbi Yehuda's rule.

At five *tefachim* per *amah*, two and a half *amot* yields: five plus five plus two and a half — twelve and a half *tefachim* in length. Six and six for the four *luchot* (twelve *tefachim*) leaves only half a *tefach*. With walls of one *etzba* (a quarter-*tefach*) on each side — accounting for the half *tefach* — there is no room whatsoever for the *Sefer Torah* inside the *Aron*. This is not an oversight; it is the basis for Rabbi Yehuda's position, derived from the verse *v'samtem ito mitzad aron berit Hashem* — place it beside the *Aron* — which he reads as *outside* the *Aron*.

In the width dimension — one and a half *amot* at five *tefachim* per *amah* — five plus two and a half yields seven and a half *tefachim*. Six *tefachim* for the *luchot*, leaving one and a half *tefachim*. After walls of one *etzba* each (a half-*tefach* total), one *tefach* remains. This is divided into half a *tefach* on each side — *chatzi tefach mikan v'chatzi tefach mikan lishilut* — a minimal clearance. Here there is no room for the *Sefer Torah* in the width either. According to this view, the *Sefer Torah* was stored externally in a special box attached to the side of the *Aron*.

## Aron Construction

### Three Boxes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצד עָשָׂה בְצַלְאֵל אֶת הָאָרוֹן. רִבִּי חֲנִינָה אָמַר. שָׁלֹשׁ תֵּיבוֹת עֲשָׂאוֹ שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁלְזָהָב וְאַחַת שֶׁלְעֵץ. נָתַן שֶׁלְעֵץ בְּשֶׁלְזָהָב וְשֶׁלְזָהָב בְּשֶׁלְעֵץ וְשֶׁל עֵץ בְּשֶׁלְזָהָב. וְצִיפָּהוּ. דִּכְתִיב וְצִפִּיתָ אוֹתוֹ זָהָב טָהוֹר מִבַּיִת וּמִחוּץ. מַה תַלְמוּד לוֹמַר תְּצַפֶּנּוּ. לְהָבִיא שְׂפָתוֹ הָעֶלְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** How did Beṣalel make the Ark? Rebbi Ḥanina said, he made three boxes, two of gold and one of wood. He put the one of wood inside one of gold and one of gold inside the one of wood, and the one of wood inside the other one of gold and covered it, as is written: *You shall cover it with pure gold inside and out.* Why does the verse say, *you shall cover it*? To include the upper rim.

The *Gemara* now addresses how Betzalel actually constructed the *Aron*. Rabbi Chananya holds: *shalosh teivot asao* — he made three separate boxes, *shtayim shel zahav v'achat shel etz* — two of gold and one of wood. These were made independently and then assembled: the wooden box was inserted into one golden box, and then the second golden box was placed inside the wooden one, so that the *Aron* had gold on the inside, wood in the middle, and gold on the outside. The verse *v'tzapita oto zahav tahor mi-bayit u-mi-chutz* — plate it with pure gold inside and outside — is fulfilled by the two golden boxes. The additional verbal form *v'tzipanu* — and he plated it — indicates the covering of the top edge where the boxes meet, so that the separate rims were joined and covered with gold plating.

### One Box

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. תֵּיבָה אַחַת עֲשָׂאוֹ וְצִיפָּהוּ. דִּכְתִיב וְצִפִּיתָ אוֹתוֹ זָהָב טָהוֹר מִבַּיִת וּמִחוּץ. מַה תַלְמוּד לוֹמַר תְּצַפֶּנּוּ. אָמַר רִבִּי פִינְחָס לְהָבִיא בֵין נֶסֶר לְנֶסֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish said, he made one box and gilded it, as is written: *You shall cover it with pure gold inside and out.* Why does the verse say, *you shall cover it*? Rebbi Phineas said, also between the planks.

Reish Lakish disagrees: *teivah achat asah* — he made one box. The procedure was simple: start with the wooden box, then plate it with gold. The verse *v'tzapita oto zahav tahor mi-bayit u-mi-chutz* describes this standard plating. The additional *v'tzipanu*, according to Rav Pinchas, indicates *le-havi bein neser le-neser* — that the gold plating was applied even between the individual planks of the wooden construction, covering every crevice so that no wood was left exposed anywhere. The *Aron* was thus entirely encased in gold, including the joints between its constituent wooden elements.

## Luchot Writing

### Five Each

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצד הָיוּ הַלּוּחוֹת כְּתוּבִים. רִבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר. חֲמִשָּׁה עַל לוּחַ זֶה וַחֲמִשָּׁה עַל לוּחַ זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** How were the tablets written? Rebbi Ḥanania ben Gamliel says, five on one tablet each.

The *Gemara* now turns to how the *luchot* were written. Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel holds: *chamishah al luach zeh v'chamishah al luach zeh* — five *dibrot* on one *luach* and five on the other. This is the familiar division, implying the first five — broadly characterized as *bein adam la-Makom* — on one tablet, and the second five — *bein adam la-chavero* — on the other. The *Torah* itself never specifies this arrangement explicitly, but it is the natural assumption given that there are ten and two tablets.

### Ten Each

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנִן אָמְרִין. עֲשָׂרָה עַל לוּחַ זֶה וַעֲשָׂרָה עַל לוּחַ זֶה. הָדָא הוּא דִּכְתִיב וַיַּגֵּד לָכֶם אֶת בְּרִיתוֹ אֲשֶׁר צִוָּה אֶתְכֶם לַעֲשׂוֹת עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדְּבָרִים. עֲשָׂרָה עַל לוּחַ זֶה וַעֲשָׂרָה עַל לוּחַ זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** But the rabbis say, ten on each tablet, as it is written: *He informed you of His covenant which He had commanded you to do, the ten words*, ten on each tablet.

The *Rabbanan* hold: *asarah al luach zeh v'asarah al luach zeh* — all ten on each tablet, so that each *luach* contained the full *Aseret HaDibrot*. The proof-text is *va-yaged lachem et brito asher tzivah etchem la-asot aseret ha-devarim va-yichtivem al shenei luchot avanim* — He told you His covenant, commanding you to observe the ten words, and He wrote them on two tablets of stone. The *Rabbanan* read *aseret ha-devarim va-yichtivem al shenei luchot* to mean that all ten were written on each of the two tablets.

### Front & Back

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אָמַר. עֶשְׂרִים עַל לוּחַ זֶה וְעֶשְׂרִים עַל לוּחַ זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simeon ben Yoḥai said, twenty on each tablet, as it is written: *He informed you of His covenant which He had commanded you to do, the ten words*, twenty on each tablet.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai goes further: *esrim al luach zeh v'esrim al luach zeh* — twenty on each tablet. His reasoning is that the same ten were written on each tablet both front and back, so that each tablet bore twenty inscriptions — ten on each face.

### All Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי סִימַאי אָמַר. אַרְבָּעִים עַל לוּחַ זֶה וְאַרְבָּעִים עַל לוּחַ זֶה. מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה הֵם כְּתוּבִים. טֶטְרַגוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simai says, forty on each tablet, as it is written, *on each side they are written*, a square.

Rabbi Simai extends the count further: *arbaim al luach zeh v'arbaim al luach zeh* — forty on each tablet, meaning all four sides of each *luach* were inscribed. The proof-text is *mi-zeh u-mi-zeh hem ketuvim* — from this side and from that side they are written — which Rabbi Simai understands as referring to all four faces of the tablet, like a *tetroga*, the Greek word for a four-sided figure — a cube or rectangular solid. One might expect the count to continue to eighty or more, but the *Gemara* does not pursue it further.

### Ocean Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן אֲחִי רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר. בֵּין כָל דִּיבּוּר וְדִיבּוּר דִּיקְדּוּקֶיהָ וְאוֹתוֹתֶיהָ שֶׁלְתּוֹרָה. מְמוּלָּאִים בַּתַּרְשִׁישׁ. כְּיַמָּא רַבָּא. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ כַּד הֲוָה מַטֵּי הָדֵין קִרְיָיא הֲוָה אֲמַר. יָפֶה לִימְּדֵנִי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן אֲחִי רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. מַה הַיָּם הַזֶּה בֵּין גַּל גָּדוֹל לְגַל גָּדוֹל גַּלִּים קְטַנִּים כָּךְ בֵּין כָּל דִּיבֵּר וְדִיבֵּר דִּיקְדּוּקֶיהָ וְאוֹתוֹתֶיהָ שֶׁלְתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥananiah, the son of Rebbi Joshua's brother, says: Between every two commandments, the details and the letters [of the Torah] were written, filled with tarsis, like the Great Sea. When Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish had occasion to discuss this verse, he said, Ḥananiah, the son of Rebbi Joshua's brother, did teach us correctly. Just as in the sea there are small waves between a large wave and the next, so between any two commandments there are the details and the letters of the Torah.

Chananya ben Achi Rabbi Yehoshua offers a different conception entirely. He does not count the *dibrot* and multiply them across surfaces. Instead, he says: *bein kol dibur v'dibur, dikdukeiha v'otioteiha shel Torah* — between each *dibur* and the next, all of the minute explications and letters of the *Torah* were written in the white space. The entire *Torah she-be'al peh*, in all its detail, was embedded in the space between the great *dibrot*.

His proof-text is a verse from *Shir HaShirim* describing something *memula'im ba-tarshish* — filled with *Tarshish*, which he understands as the name of the great sea, the ocean. Reish Lakish, whenever he would encounter this *pasuk*, would say: *yafe limadani Chananya ben achi Rabbi Yehoshua* — he taught me well. The metaphor is this: *mah ha-yam hazeh bein gal gadol le-gal gadol galim ktanim* — just as between every great wave in the ocean there are countless small ripples and eddies — *kach bein kol dibur v'dibur, dikdukeiha v'otioteiha shel Torah* — so between every great *dibur* and the next, there were all the fine details and letters of the *Torah*. The great *dibrot* are the towering waves, and between them, implicit and infinite, is the *Torah she-be'al peh* in all its detail. One might observe that this image anticipates the metaphor of the *yam shel Talmud*, though the explicit connection is not drawn.

### Sefer Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְּ מַה טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוּדָה. לָקוֹחַ אֵת סֵפֶר הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת וְשַׂמְתֶּם אוֹתוֹ מִצַּד אֲרוֹן בְּרִית יְי וגו׳. על דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוּדָה דוּ אָמַר. אֵיכָן הָיָה סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה נָתוּן. כְּמִין גְּלוֹסֳּקוֹס עָשָׂה לוֹ מִבַּחוּץ וְהָיָה סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה נָתוּן לְתוֹכוֹ. מַה טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי מֵאִיר. וְנָתַתָּה אֶת הַכַּפּוֹרֶת עַל הָאָרוֹן מִלְמָעְלָה וגו׳. עַל דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי מֵאִיר דוּ אָמַר. אֵין מוּקְדָּם וּמְאוּחַר בַּתּוֹרָה. אֶלָּא וְאֶל הָאָרוֹן תִּתֵּן אֵת הָעֵדוּת אֲשֶׁר אֶתֵּן אֵלֶיךָ. וְאַחַר כָּךְ וְנָתַתָּה אֶת הַכַּפֹּרֶת עַל הָאָרוֹן מִלְמָעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** What is the reason of Rebbi Jehudah? *To take this Torah scroll and put it next to the side of the Ark of the Eternal's covenant*, etc In the opinion of Rebbi Jehudah, who said, where the Torah scroll was put? They made for it a kind of case outside and the Torah scroll was put into it. What is the reason of Rebbi Meνr? *You should put the cover on top of the Ark*. In the opinion of Rebbi Meνr who says that there is no earlier and later in the Torah, *in the Ark you shall put the testimony that I shall give to you*, and after that, *you should put the cover on top of the Ark.*

Finally, the *Gemara* addresses the question of where the *Sefer Torah* was placed: inside the *Aron* or outside it, at its side.

According to Rabbi Yehuda, whose position flows from the five-*tefach amah* calculation that left no interior room, the *Sefer Torah* was placed *mitzad* — on the outside of the *Aron*, in a specially fashioned box, *k'min gluskamya asuyah lo mi-bachutz*, affixed to the exterior. The proof-text is *v'samtem ito mitzad aron berit Hashem* — place it beside the *Aron of the covenant*.

According to Rabbi Meir, whose six-*tefach amah* calculation does leave room inside, the *Sefer Torah* was placed inside. His proof-text is the verse *v'natata et ha-kaporet al ha-aron milma'ala… v'el ha-aron titein et ha-edut asher etein eilecha* — place the *kaporet* on the *Aron* from above, and into the *Aron* place the *edut* which I will give you. The *edut* here is understood to include the *Sefer Torah*, and the verse directs that it be placed inside the *Aron* before the *kaporet* is set in place. The apparent difficulty — that the *kaporet* seems to have already been placed before the instruction to insert the *edut* — is resolved by the principle *ein mukdam u-me'uchar ba-Torah*: the *Torah*'s narrative sequence does not always follow chronological order. The practical sequence was: place the *Sefer Torah* inside first, then cover the *Aron* with the *kaporet*.

## Divine Tablets

### Fire Writing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי פִינְחָס בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. הַתּוֹרָה שֶׁנָּתַן לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה נְתָנָהּ לוֹ אֵשׁ לְבָנָה חָרוּתָה בְאֵשׁ שְׁחוֹרָה. הִיא אֵשׁ מוּבְלֶלֶת בְאֵשׁ חֲצוּבָה מֵאֵשׁ וּנְתוּנָה מֵאֵשׁ. הָדָא הוּא דִכְתִיב מִימִינוֹ אֵשְׁ דָּת לָמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Phineas in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish: The Torah which the Holy One, Praise to Him, gave to Moses, was white fire engraved in black fire. It was fire mixed with fire; hewn from fire, given from fire: That is what is written: *From His right hand, the fiery law to them.*

The *Gemara* closes with a statement of Rabbi Pinchas in the name of Reish Lakish: *ha-Torah she-natan lo ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-Moshe, netanah lo esh levana charuta be-esh shechora* — the *Torah* that the Holy One gave to Moshe was given to him as white fire engraved upon black fire. Whether the letters were of white fire written on a black background, or black fire inscribed upon a white one — as black ink upon a white surface — the text describes fire interwoven with fire: *hi esh mevulla'a be-esh, esh chatzuvah me-esh, u-netunah me-esh*. Fire intertwined with fire, fire hewn from fire, given out of fire. The proof-text is *mi-ymino esh dat lamo* — from His right hand, a fiery law for them. All of the preceding discussion — the physical dimensions of the *Aron*, the geometry of the *luchot*, the layout of the *dibrot*, the techniques of the perfumers, the weight of the gold and the wood — ultimately gives way to this conclusion: whatever the *luchot* were physically, their essence was divine fire, irreducible to any material container.
