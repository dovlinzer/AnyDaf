# Makkot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemita Kesafim

### Shemita Tangent

The discussion of *shemita* in the preceding pages arose as a tangent from our *mishnah*, which contemplated the possibility of a loan extended for a ten-year term. The immediate difficulty was that *shemita* would inevitably intervene during such a period, forgiving the loan and apparently rendering the entire scenario impossible. That question prompted an extended treatment of how *shemitat kesafim* operates, anchored in a statement of *Amar Rav Yehuda Amar Shmuel*. We now turn to three further statements of Rav Yehuda — two quoting Rav and one returning to Rav — each of which introduces its own independent legal discussion.

### Hapote'ach Beit Hatzavar

The first statement: *Amar Rav Yehuda Amar Rav* — one who opens the neck of a new shirt on *Shabbat* is *chayav chatat*, liable for a biblical violation of *Shabbat*. The underlying principle is that this act constitutes a constructive form of tearing. In the manufacturing practice of the Talmudic period, the neck seam of a new garment would be basted shut, and the purchaser would rip it open when first wearing the garment. The tearing of those stitches is, on one level, destructive — it breaks the thread — but on another level it is constructive, because it creates a functional opening in the garment that allows it to be worn. This is why it cannot be dismissed under the general principle of *kol hamekalkelin peturin*, that destructive acts on *Shabbat* carry no liability. Here the destruction is in service of a constructive result, and so the act is classified as genuinely constructive.

### Lid of Barrel Challenge

*Matkif lah Rav Kahana* — Rav Kahana challenges Rav Yehuda's ruling: *vekhi mah bein zeh lemegufat havit?* What is the difference between opening the neck of a shirt and opening the lid of a barrel? The same logic would seem to apply — one is removing a covering to make the vessel usable. This question has evident practical resonance. The *poskim* have addressed at length whether opening a bottle cap on *Shabbat* falls into this category, a discussion that originated with the earlier and more pressing question of opening tin cans. The key variables are whether the cap constituted a functional *kli* in its own right before being opened, whether it was designed to be opened and reclosed, and whether opening it creates something genuinely new. Related questions arise with resealable bags and sealed boxes: if the item will be used across multiple occasions after opening, one has arguably fashioned a new functional vessel. The *Chazon Ish* and others devoted considerable attention to these distinctions.

### Chibur vs. Non-Chibur

Rav Yehuda's response to Rav Kahana: *zeh chibur vezeh eino chibur* — the neck of the shirt is actually sewn shut, making it a connected part of the garment itself, whereas the lid of the barrel is a separate piece and not genuinely attached. Since the shirt's neck constitutes a *chibur*, a joined unit with the garment, ripping it open is a meaningful constructive act upon the garment as a whole. The barrel lid, being a non-attached separate piece, involves no such consideration. Rav Kahana's challenge is thereby answered, and the ruling stands: opening the shirt neck on *Shabbat* does carry liability, while opening the barrel does not present the same problem.

### Three Logs & Wine

A second statement: *Ve-amar Rav Yehuda Amar Rav* — if one has three *log* of water into which a *kortov* of red wine has fallen, and the color of the water has been visibly affected such that *mareihen kemarei yayin*, they appear like wine, and then this liquid falls into a *mikveh*, *lo pesaluhu* — it does not disqualify the *mikveh*. The question is what happens when a small amount of wine colors a larger body of water and that colored water is then introduced into a *mikveh*.

### Dye Water Challenge

*Matkif lah Rav Kahana* once more — Rav Kahana presses again: *vekhi mah bein zeh lemei tzeva?* How is this different from dye water? The process of dyeing fabric involves dissolving pigment in water and immersing the material in the solution. The remaining dye water retains color. The *mishnah* in *mikvaot* records Rabbi Yossi's position that *mei tzeva poslin et hamikveh bishelosha lugin* — dye water can disqualify a *mikveh* in a quantity of three *log*. If colored water disqualifies the *mikveh*, why should wine-colored water be any different?

### Wine vs. Dye Water

*Amar lei Rava* — Rava intervenes to defend Rav Yehuda's ruling. *Hatam maya de-tziva mikru* — that liquid is called dye water. *Hacha chamra maziga mikrei* — but here, when a small amount of wine falls into water, the resulting liquid is not dye water but *chamra maziga*, diluted wine. In Talmudic practice, wine was routinely consumed heavily diluted — sometimes one part wine to three parts water, or even one part to five — yet it retained enough color to appear like wine and was still called wine. The character of the liquid has been transformed: it is no longer water with something foreign added, but rather a different kind of liquid altogether, namely *yayin mazug*. And since *yayin mazug* is simply wine, it is actually less problematic for the *mikveh* than colored water, because it is not water at all. This creates what is, at first glance, a counterintuitive result: a small amount of wine that colors the water and shifts its character into *yayin mazug* actually makes the liquid *less* stringent from the *mikveh*'s perspective. The same analysis applies to *mei peirot* such as fruit juice — these are categorized as a different liquid rather than water, and so the concern is of a different and lesser order.

### Appearance vs. Volume

A *braita* from *Tractate Mikvaot* is raised involving Rabbi Chiyya, which indicates that such a liquid *horeidu et hamikveh* — it diminishes the valid water count of the *mikveh*. *Amar Rava lo kashya ha Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri ha Rabbanan* — Rava resolves the apparent contradiction by identifying it as a *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the other *Rabbanan*. The relevant *mishnah* presents two parallel cases: three *log* of water, minus one *kortov*, into which a *kortov* of wine fell, such that the water appears like wine — it does not disqualify the *mikveh*; and three *log* of water, minus one *kortov*, into which a *kortov* of milk fell, such that the water appears like water — it similarly does not disqualify the *mikveh*. *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri omer hakol holekh achar hamarei* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that everything follows appearance, not volume. The question of whether there are a full three *log* of disqualifying liquid is determined entirely by how the mixture looks, not by measuring out the precise amounts of each component.

### Rav Pappa's Question

This leads to the question raised by Rav Pappa: *Rav tani chaser kartov be-reisha* — did Rav, when teaching this *mishnah*, include the qualification of *chaser kortov* in the first clause, the case involving wine, holding that the *tana kama* would consider a full three *log* of wine-colored water to be disqualifying, while Rav himself ruled like Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri? Or did Rav not read *chaser kortov* into the first clause at all — meaning his statement about the wine case applied to a full three *log* of water, and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's dispute was only with the second clause of the *mishnah*, the milk case, while Rav's ruling is in fact consistent with *divrei hakol*, with both the *tana kama* and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri? For Rav Pappa, this remained an unresolved question. For Rava, the answer was evident — Rav's position accords only with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, not with the *Rabbanan*, as Rava had already explained.

### Rav Yosef's Claim

*Amar Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef declares: *lo shemia li ha shmaita* — he had never heard this entire teaching, meaning he had no recollection of Rav's manner of reading the *mishnah* and the interpretive question that surrounded it.

### Abaye's Counter

*Amar lei Abaye* — Abaye responds to his own teacher: *at amarat lan nilan vehachi amarat lan nilan* — you yourself taught it to us, and this is precisely how you taught it. The discrepancy is striking. Whether Rav Yosef's amnesia reflects a gap in memory — he suffered a severe illness that affected his recollection — or whether Abaye simply retained what his teacher had once said with greater precision, the episode illustrates the fragility of oral transmission and the indispensability of diligent students. Abaye proceeds to reconstruct the teaching: Rav did not read *chaser kortov* into the first clause concerning wine; Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's disagreement is with the second clause concerning milk; and Rav's ruling accords with *divrei hakol*, both the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri.

## Barrel into Ocean

### Chavit into Yam

A fourth statement from Rav Yehuda quoting Rav: *chavit mele'a mayim she-nafla le-yam hagadol* — a barrel filled with water that fell into the ocean. *Ha-tovel sham lo alta lo tvila* — if a person immerses *there*, the *tevila* does not count. The word "there" is itself the subject of debate among the *Rishonim*: does it refer to the area of the ocean where the water from the barrel dispersed, or does it mean within the barrel itself if the water remained inside?

### Mayim She'uvim

The concern that drives this ruling is *chaishinan lishlosha lugin she-lo yiheyu bemakom echad* — we worry that within the forty *se'ah* zone one would draw around the *mikveh* area, there are three *log* of *mayim she'uvim*, drawn water, which would disqualify the *mikveh*. Normally one might think that a barrel of water poured into an entire ocean is entirely inconsequential — surely it is *batel*, nullified, in so vast a body. Yet the *halacha* treats the water as localized. One mentally circumscribes a forty-*se'ah* zone within the ocean, and within that zone the drawn water from the barrel may represent three *log* of *mayim she'uvim*, enough to disqualify the *mikveh*. This spatial subdivision of the ocean is itself a remarkable conceptual move.

### Density Differences

One of the more striking interpretations of this *sugya* is attributed to Rabbi Moshe HaDarshan, cited by the *Ra'avad* and quoted in the *Ritva*. Rabbi Moshe HaDarshan — better known from Rashi's citations of him in his *Torah* commentary, and understood more as a *darshan* than as a *Talmud* scholar — offers an explanation grounded in physical science. He invokes the difference in density between fresh water and salt water. Salt water is denser and therefore does not readily mix with fresh water poured into it. The barrel of fresh water that spills into the ocean may sit as a distinct, unmixed mass for some period before dispersing. This is why, in the ocean specifically, the concern for *mayim she'uvim* applies — the freshwater and saltwater do not instantly homogenize, creating a localized zone of drawn water.

### Ocean vs. River

*Ve-davka le-yam hagadol* — this concern is specific to the ocean, *d'ka'i ve-kaima*, because the water largely remains in place, even accounting for waves and currents. *Aval nahara be-alma* — in an ordinary river, the ruling does not apply. The current constantly moves the water downstream, so that if one immerses at the spot where the barrel fell in, the drawn water will no longer be present. This raises separately the question of whether a river may serve as a *mikveh* at all. If it is fed by a spring, it has the status of a *mayan* and immersion in flowing water is entirely valid. If it is composed of rainwater, it must be stationary to qualify as a *mikveh* — *be'ashboran* — which is derived from the *Sifra*. The extensive discussion of this issue in *Tosafot* and the *Ran* in *Nedarim* touches on *Masechet Niddah*, *Shabbat*, and *Bechorot*. For present purposes, the river is invoked simply to show that flowing water disperses the drawn water and so the concern does not arise there.

### Wine Barrel

A *braita* supports and extends the ruling: *chavit mele'a yayin she-nafal le-yam hagadol* — a barrel of wine that fell into the ocean. *Ha-tovel sham lo alta lo tvila* — immersion there does not count. The same *chaishinan* formula appears, though the concern here is not technically *mayim she'uvim* — wine is not water, so it is not disqualifying on those grounds — and the precise textual formulation of the *braita* is somewhat difficult in this respect.

### Terumah Impurity

The *braita* continues: *ve-khen kikar shel terumah she-nafela sham tamei* — if a loaf of *terumah* bread fell into that same area, it becomes *tamei*. One might have argued from a *status quo* reasoning that since the *terumah* was *tahor* to begin with, it should remain *tahor*. The *braita* uses the conjunction *ve-khen*, "and similarly," to signal that this follows as a further inference. *Ka mashma lan* — the teaching comes to tell us that not only does the *mikveh* lack the power to remove the *tumah* of one who immerses, but the *mashkin* present in that zone — the wine, the drawn water — render the *terumah* *tamei*. The logic does not permit us simply to leave the *terumah* in its prior status; rather, we actively assess it as impure.

## Eidim Zomemim

### Monetary Conspiracy

We return now to the *mishnah* and the topic of *eidim zomemim*, conspiring witnesses. *Me'idim anu be'ish ploni she-chayav le-chavero matayim zuz* — if witnesses testify that a certain individual owes his friend two hundred *zuz*, and they are subsequently shown to be *zomemim*, *lokin u-meshalmim* — they receive lashes and they pay. This ruling is contested between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim*, and it represents the first of several *machlokot* that run through this section of the *mishnah*.

### Rabbi Meir: Two Violatio…

The verse *ve-asitem lo ka-asher zamam la-asot le-achiv* mandates reciprocal punishment — they tried to impose a monetary loss, so they pay money. But they also violated the prohibition against false testimony. According to Rabbi Meir: *shelo hashem hamevi'an lidei makot mevi'an lidei tashlumin* — the legal category that renders them liable for lashes is not the same legal category that renders them liable for payment. Two distinct violations are at play, and therefore both punishments apply simultaneously.

### Sages: No Double

*Ve-chachamim omrim kol ha-meshalem eino lokeh* — the *Chachamim* disagree and establish a principle of mutually exclusive penalties: in any circumstance where a person pays money, he does not also receive lashes. The same wrongful act cannot generate two forms of corporal and financial punishment. The underlying verse *kedei rish'ato* — "according to his wickedness" — implies that one is held liable for a single wickedness at a time, not two simultaneously.

## Lashes Dispute

### Lashes Testimony

A second scenario: *me'idim anu be'ish ploni she-hu chayav malkot arba'im* — the witnesses testify that a certain individual has committed a transgression punishable by forty lashes, and they are shown to be *zomemim*.

### Rabbi Meir: Eighty

According to Rabbi Meir, *lokim shmonim* — they receive eighty lashes. *Mishum lo ta'aneh ve-rei'acha ed shaker* — because they violated the prohibition against false testimony, and *u-mishum ve-asitem lo ka-asher zamam* — because they violated the reciprocal punishment principle. Since these are two distinct prohibitions, they generate two distinct sets of lashes. The practical figure in either case, of course, is thirty-nine per set — yielding seventy-eight — since the Torah's prescription is understood as a maximum of thirty-nine for any single violation.

### Sages: Forty Only

*Ve-chachamim omrim ein lokin ela arba'im* — the *Chachamim* hold that only forty lashes are administered, one set for one violation. The *gemara* explains the *Chachamim*'s reasoning from the verse *kedei rish'ato*: *mishum rishah achat atah mechayvo ve-i atah mechayvo mishum shtei rishayot* — you hold him liable for one act of wickedness, not two. For being *eidim zomemim*, you receive one set of *makot*, not two.

### Motzi Shem Ra

*Amar Ulla* — Ulla identifies the source for Rabbi Meir's position: *gamar mi-motzi shem ra* — Rabbi Meir derives the principle by analogy to *motzi shem ra*, the case of a husband who falsely accuses his wife of infidelity during the period of *erusin*. In that case, the husband receives both *makot* and pays a *knas*. Rabbi Meir generalizes: *mah motzi shem ra lokeh u-meshalem af kol lokeh u-meshalem* — just as *motzi shem ra* involves both lashes and payment, so too in all analogous cases of *eidim zomemim* one both receives lashes and pays. The choice of *motzi shem ra* as the paradigmatic case follows the logic of *binyan av*: one finds a generative example and derives a general principle from it. Ulla is not citing Rabbi Meir explicitly on this point; he is reconstructing the reasoning on Rabbi Meir's behalf.

### Knas vs. Restitution

An objection is raised: *mah motzi shem ra she-ken knas* — the payment in *motzi shem ra* is a *knas*, a punitive fine, not ordinary monetary restitution. One cannot straightforwardly apply it to the case of *eidim zomemim*, where the witnesses were trying to impose a restitution-style payment, not a *knas*. If the source case involves a *knas* and the target case involves ordinary *mamon*, they may not be comparable.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

*Savar lah ke-Rabbi Akiva de-amar eidim zomemim knasa hu* — the defense of Rabbi Meir is that he holds like Rabbi Akiva's position, already seen in an earlier *daf*, that the entire institution of *eidim zomemim* and its attendant punishment is itself a *knas*. If the payment imposed on *zomemim* is itself a *knas* rather than simple restitution, then the analogy to *motzi shem ra* is entirely consistent: both cases are *knas*, and therefore the same principle applies — one both receives lashes and pays.

## Passive Violations

### Notar Principle

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* that uses the case of *notar* as its organizing example. *Notar* is the prohibition against leaving over sacrificial meat past the permitted time — *lo totiru mimenu ad boker*. Certain *korbanot*, notably the *korban chatat*, *korban pesach*, and others, must be consumed within a defined period; any meat remaining by morning must be burned: *ve-hanotar mimenu ad boker ba-esh tisrof*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Samach lei assei achar lo ta'aseh lomar she-ein lokin alav* — when the Torah places a positive commandment immediately following a prohibition, this signals that one does not receive lashes for violating that prohibition, since a corrective action remains available. This is the principle of *lav hanitak la-aseh*, a prohibition given over to a positive commandment as its remedy. This is the position of *divrei Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Lav She'ein Bo Ma'aseh

*Lo min hashem hazeh hu zeh* — Rabbi Akiva disagrees and says the reason is not *lav hanitak la-aseh* but rather *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh*: a prohibition that is violated without any active step. The prohibition of *notar* is violated passively — one simply fails to eat the meat, and by not eating it, the meat becomes *notar*. Since no physical action was performed, there is no punishment, for the principle is that a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* carries no *malkot*.

### Inference Question

From Rabbi Yehuda's formulation, one may infer — *michlal* — that Rabbi Yehuda holds the opposite position: that a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* does in fact carry *malkot*. If his reason for exempting *notar* from lashes is the *lav hanitak la-aseh* structure, then in the absence of that structure, a passive prohibition would still generate punishment. The question then becomes: from where does Rabbi Yehuda derive this position?

### Ulla Source

*Amar Ulla gamar mi-motzi shem ra* — Ulla says Rabbi Yehuda learns it from *motzi shem ra*. Just as *motzi shem ra* is a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — the husband simply makes an accusation, no physical action is performed — and yet he receives *makot*, so too every *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* carries lashes.

### Lo Tachmod

One of the more instructive discussions of what constitutes *ein bo ma'aseh* arises in connection with *lo tachmod*, the prohibition against coercing someone until they sell you something they did not wish to sell. The *Rambam* holds that there are no *makot* for *lo tachmod* because it is a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — the violation consists of sustained pressure, a speech act, not a physical deed. Yet in a preceding *halacha*, the *Rambam* states that the prohibition is triggered only when the coercion succeeds — when the other party finally agrees to sell. One might ask: is not the relentless verbal pressure itself an action? The *Ra'avad* disagrees with the *Rambam*'s categorization entirely, holding that the exemption from *makot* derives not from *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* but from *lav hanitan letashlumin* — it is a prohibition whose violation is remedied through payment, like *genevah* and *gezelah*, and therefore does not generate *malkot*. The contours of what counts as *ein bo ma'aseh* remain genuinely contested.

### Reish Lakish Source

*Ela amar Reish Lakish gamar me-eidim zomemim* — Reish Lakish proposes an alternative: Rabbi Yehuda derives his principle not from *motzi shem ra* but from *eidim zomemim*. Just as *eidim zomemim* constitutes a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — the witnesses appear in court and testify, performing no physical act against the defendant — and yet they receive *malkot*, so too every *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* generates punishment.

### No Warning Needed

An objection to using *eidim zomemim* as the paradigmatic case: *mah le-eidim zomemim she-ken ein tzrichin hatra'ah* — *eidim zomemim* is one of the few *aveirot* that does not require prior warning. We learned in *Masechet Sanhedrin* that certain categories of violations are exempt from the requirement of *hatra'ah*, and *eidim zomemim* is among them. The reason is partly logical impossibility: the proof of the conspiracy — *o imanu heyitem*, "were you not with us?" — only becomes relevant when the witnesses to the witnesses appear in court, potentially weeks after the original testimony. There would be no moment at which a warning could practically be administered. The *derisha ve-chakirah* process in court arguably functions as a quasi-warning, but the *Sages* nonetheless classified *eidim zomemim* as *ein tzrichin hatra'ah* because no formal warning in the classic sense is possible at the time of the violation.

### Common Denominator

Since we now have two potential sources — *motzi shem ra* and *eidim zomemim* — the argument proceeds through the standard Talmudic methodology of *tzad hashaveh*. *V-lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh ve-lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh* — each case has features that the other lacks, so neither alone can serve as a proof. *Hatzad hashaveh shebehen* — the common denominator between the two is *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh ve-lokin alav*: both are passive prohibitions and both carry *malkot*. *Af kol lav she-ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav* — from their shared characteristic we derive the general rule that every *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* carries *malkot*.

### Not Exceptional

One might press: *mah le-tzad hashaveh shebehen she-ken knas* — both cases have the characteristic of being a *knas*, and therefore they may be exceptional rather than generative. The response is that this is not a problem for Rabbi Yehuda, since *Rabbi Yehuda lo savar lah ke-Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Yehuda does not hold like Rabbi Akiva that *eidim zomemim* is a *knas*. The further challenge — *ela mah le-tzad hashaveh shebehen she-yesh lahen tzad chamur*, that both cases are unusually severe and therefore exceptional — does not trouble Rabbi Yehuda either. *Rabbi Yehuda tzad chamur lo parich*. Rashi explains: *ho'il ve-chumro shel zeh eino chumro shel zeh* — since the source of stringency in each case is different from the other, it cannot be identified as the defining characteristic that makes each case exceptional. The two cases are therefore not treated as rare exceptions but as generative examples from which a general principle is derived. Consequently, Rabbi Yehuda holds that *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* carries *malkot*, and Rabbi Meir's position — that *eidim zomemim* involves two violations — can be understood consistently within a framework where *lokeh u-meshalem* is possible.

## Sages' Interpretation

### Lo Ta'aneh Warning

The *gemara* now turns to how the *Rabbanan* interpret the verse *lo ta'aneh ve-rei'acha ed shaker*. In the *mishnah*, Rabbi Meir had cited this verse as the source for the second violation — false testimony — generating a separate set of lashes alongside the *ka-asher zamam* principle. But if the *Rabbanan* use only one of these as the basis for punishment, what do they do with the verse? *Hahu mibayelei le-azharah le-eidim zomemim* — they use it not as an independent prohibition generating lashes, but as the *azharah*, the formal warning, for the violation of *eidim zomemim* itself. The requirement of a separate *azharah* alongside the *onesh* typically applies in capital cases, and since *eidim zomemim* can in some scenarios carry a capital penalty, the separate mention of *lo ta'aneh* is needed to provide that formal warning. The *Rabbanan* thus understand *lo ta'aneh* and *ka-asher zamam* as two elements of a single legal structure rather than as two independent prohibitions.

### Hachrazah Source

*Amar Rabbi Yirmeyah* — Rabbi Yirmeyah explains how Rabbi Meir derives his *azharah* for *eidim zomemim* if he does not use *lo ta'aneh* for that purpose. *Nafka lei min vahanish'arim yishme'u ve-yira'u ve-lo yosifu od* — he derives the *azharah* from the verse describing the proclamation that follows punishment, the requirement that the broader public be informed of what was done to the conspiring witnesses. According to Rabbi Meir, the first part of that verse — the injunction that others should not continue such conduct — serves as the *azharah* against *eidim zomemim*, while the second part — *yishme'u ve-yira'u*, they shall hear and be afraid — provides the basis for the *hachrazah*, the formal public announcement. The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, use *vahanish'arim yishme'u ve-yira'u* exclusively for the *hachrazah* requirement. *Ve-Rabbi Meir hachrazah mi-yishme'u ve-yira'u nafka* — Rabbi Meir divides the verse, extracting both the *azharah* and the *hachrazah* from its two clauses, leaving him free to use *lo ta'aneh* as the source for an independent second violation.

## Division of Penalties

### Money Divided

The *mishnah* continues: *mishalshilin be-mamon ve-ein mishalshilin be-malkot*. When *eidim zomemim* are subject to a monetary penalty, the payment is divided among all the conspiring witnesses. If four witnesses conspired to impose a debt of a thousand *zuz*, and they are exposed as *zomemim*, they each pay two hundred and fifty — the total is divided equally among them.

### Lashes Not Divided

Physical punishment, however, is not divided. *Ve-ein mishalshilin be-malkot* — if the same four witnesses conspired to testify that someone had worn *shatnez* and is therefore liable for forty lashes, and they are exposed, each of the four witnesses receives the full thirty-nine lashes. There is no halving or quartering of the corporal punishment across the group.

### Half-Death Analogy

*Amar Abaye* — Abaye derives this rule through a textual parallel. The Torah uses the word *rasha* in the context of those liable for *malkot*, and it uses the same word *rasha* for those liable for capital punishment. *Mah lehalan ein mita lachatzi* — just as there is no such thing as a half-death in capital cases, *af kan ein malkot lachatzi* — there is likewise no such thing as half-*malkot*. The analogy is presented as a gezeirah shavah in structure: the shared term links the two domains and imports the indivisibility of capital punishment into the realm of *malkot*. One might ask whether the parallel is truly compelling — death is obviously impossible to divide, whereas lashes are physically divisible — and indeed the question is pressed in the *gemara*. The response involves understanding *malkot* as a subcategory, or near-equivalent, of the range of punishments that includes capital penalties. The tradition enumerates multiple modalities of punishment: *sekilah*, *srefah*, *hereg*, *chenek*, *kipah*, exclusion from *olam haba*, and *malkot* — and *malkot* is understood as occupying a position within that continuum, functioning as a punishment that carries the character of *kapparah* just as capital punishment does. The sense is that any punishment administered by *beit din* is intended as both deterrent and atonement, and *malkot* achieves this only when administered in its prescribed complete form.

### Ka'asher Zamam

*Rava amar* — Rava offers an independent derivation. *Ba'inan ka-asher zamam la-asot le-achiv ve-lo ika* — the principle of reciprocal punishment requires that what is done to the *zomemim* must correspond precisely to what they sought to do to their intended victim. If the victim would have received thirty-nine lashes, the witnesses must each receive thirty-nine lashes. Giving each witness only ten lashes — apportioning one-quarter of the punishment across four conspirators — does not satisfy the requirement of *ka-asher zamam*, because no individual witness was attempting to give the defendant one-quarter of a flogging.

### Money Joins, Pain Doesn't

One immediately asks: if Rava's logic is that the punishment must mirror exactly what was intended, why does money get divided? Should each witness not pay the full sum as well? *Mamon mitztaref* — monetary amounts join together. Two hundred and fifty plus two hundred and fifty plus two hundred and fifty plus two hundred and fifty equals a thousand, and that thousand matches the monetary harm the witnesses sought to impose. *Malkot lo mitztaref* — but physical suffering does not aggregate across persons. Ten lashes experienced by four individuals is qualitatively different from thirty-nine lashes experienced by one. The subjective human experience of corporal punishment is not additive across bodies; the pain of one person cannot be summed with the pain of another to yield an equivalent. And so while monetary penalties are divisible because the total amount can be reconstructed by summing the parts, *malkot* are not divisible because the individual experience of thirty-nine lashes is an irreducible unit. Each witness must bear the full weight of what they sought to impose — not a fraction of it.
