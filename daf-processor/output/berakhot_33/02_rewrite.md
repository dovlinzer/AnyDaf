# Berakhot 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Da'at & Intellect

### Gedolah De'ah

The sugya opens in the middle of 33a, roughly six lines before the lines widen, with a teaching of Rabbi Ami. *Amar Rabbi Ami: gedolah de'ah shenitnah bitchilat berakhah shel chol* — great is intellect, for it was placed as the very first of the weekday *berakhot*. The reasoning is that before one can engage meaningfully with *teshuvah*, *selichah*, and all the other petitions that follow, one first needs the capacity to assess and think about one's life. Intellect is therefore the most fundamental thing we ask of God, the prerequisite for everything else.

### De'ah Between Names

Rabbi Ami adds a second observation: *gedolah de'ah shenitnah bein shtei otiyot* — great is intellect because it appears between two of God's names. The verse supporting this is *ki El de'ot Hashem* (I Samuel 2:3): the word *de'ot* is bracketed by divine names, demonstrating how central knowledge is to the divine order.

The passage then makes a striking and seemingly harsh claim: *kol mi she'ein bo de'ah assur lerachem alav* — whoever lacks intellect, it is forbidden to have compassion for them. The proof-text is *ki lo am binot hu, al ken lo yerachamenu osehu* — they are not a people of understanding, therefore their Creator will not have compassion on them. The statement does invite concern about those who are born without certain intellectual capacities, but the context of the verse points in a different direction. The phrase *ki lo am binot hu* does not mean a people that has no intellect; it means a people that is not being thoughtful — that is not using its intellect responsibly. The statement, then, is less about withholding compassion from those unable to think and more about human responsibility: one who brings troubles upon oneself by acting irresponsibly and without reflection has, in a certain sense, received what their choices led to, in the way that a parent sometimes allows a child to learn from mistakes. This reading is reinforced by the parallel statement that follows: *kol adam sheyesh bo de'ah* — whoever has intellect — in which *yesh bo de'ah* must mean uses intellect in one's life, not merely possesses it innately. The *Maharsha*, it should be noted, has his own reading of this passage, understanding the value of compassion itself as rooted in the concept of *de'ah*.

### Da'ah as Beit HaMikdash

Rabbi Elazar then takes up the same structural point — a concept placed between two divine names — and applies it to the *Beit HaMikdash*: *gedolah mikdash shenitnah bein shtei otiyot*, derived from the verse *pa'alta Hashem mikdash Hashem* (Exodus 15:17), where "Sanctuary" appears between two instances of God's name.

Rabbi Elazar then connects the two themes: *kol adam sheyesh bo de'ah ke'ilu nivneh Beit HaMikdash b'yamav* — anyone who has (that is, uses) intellect, it is as if the *Beit HaMikdash* was built in his days. The reasoning follows directly from the structural parallel: just as *de'ah* is bracketed by two divine names and so is the *mikdash*, the person who lives a life of *de'ah* inhabits a kind of sanctified existence analogous to the presence of the Temple. And crucially, *yesh bo de'ah* here too should be read as one who actively employs intellect — not merely anyone who happens to be intelligent by birth.

This is worth pausing over, because it is the same Rabbi Elazar who earlier said that *tefillah* is greater than *korbanot*, and who also said that since the *churban habayit* it is as if the gates of prayer have been sealed. Taken together, these statements form a coherent picture of how one sustains religious meaning without the *Beit HaMikdash*: *tefillah* still matters, and even when the gates of prayer seem sealed, living a life of *de'ah* creates an intimate relationship with God — as though the *mikdash* itself had been rebuilt.

### Vengeance Challenge

Rav Achah Karchinah challenges the logical principle being applied: *ella me'atah gedolah nekamah shenitnah bein shtei otiyot* — if appearing between two divine names makes something praiseworthy, then vengeance too should be declared great, since the verse reads *El nekamot Hashem El nekamot hofia* (Psalms 94:1), with *nekamot* flanked on both sides by God's name.

The response is a direct affirmation: *in, lemilsayehi gedolah hi* — yes, when it is appropriate, that is indeed a great thing. The *Gemara* then cites Ulla's interpretation of why the word *nekamot* appears twice in that verse: one instance represents *nekamah* for good, the other for evil. The positive usage is derived from *hofia mehar Paran* — God's revelation from Mount Paran — which Rashi understands as a reference to the *midrash* whereby the nations' refusal of the Torah resulted in Israel's receiving it, making their loss Israel's benefit, a kind of redemptive *nekamah*.

This connects to a broader theological question: there is a *mitzvah* of *vehalachta bidrachav*, of emulating God's ways. The standard application, as *Chazal* formulate it, is *mah hu rachum af atah rachum, mah hu chanun af atah chanun* — as God is compassionate and gracious, so should you be. But one might ask: why limit the emulation to those particular attributes? The *Rambam* addresses this by arguing that the specific divine names in the verse — *rachum*, *chanun*, and so on — are those whose application has been authorized by the Torah as models for human conduct. The name *El nekamot* then raises a genuine question: is *nekamah* among the attributes we are meant to emulate? The *Gemara*'s answer is that in its proper context it is indeed valuable — but the very difficulty of defining that proper context, and of setting its limits, is what makes these questions about divine attributes and which ones are held up for emulation so consequential.

## Insertions Formula

The *Gemara* situates this discussion of *da'at* within the broader topic of where various insertions are made in the *Amidah*. *Mashiv haruach umorid hagashem* is said in the second *berakhah* of *gevurot*; *veten tal umatar livracha* is inserted into *birkat hashanim*; and *havdalah* is inserted into *chonen hada'at*, the first of the weekday petitions, because it marks the transition to the weekday cycle and connects naturally to the theme of *yeshuat da'at*. The *Gemara* treats *chonen hada'at* as the appropriate vessel for *havdalah* precisely because intellect is the gateway to purposeful weekday life.

## Chonen HaDa'at Status

### Rabbi Akiva Position

The *Gemara* turns, four lines into the wider section, to a dispute about the precise form of *chonen hada'at*. Rabbi Akiva holds that *havdalah* is recited as an independent, separate *berakhah* on *Motzaei Shabbat* — not inserted into an existing *berakhah* but standing on its own.

### AKG Enactment

Rav Shemen bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* established for Israel the *berakhot*, *tefillot*, *kedushos*, and *havdalot*. This is a general assumption of the *Gemara* — that the *tefillot* as a whole, including the *Shemoneh Esrei*, were composed during the period of the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*, which predates the *churban* and belongs to the early *Bayit Sheni* era. The question then follows: if it was all established then, why is there a debate about whether *havdalah* belongs inside *chonen hada'at* or stands as a separate *berakhah*? Should we not simply look back and determine what the original *takkanah* was?

*Tosafot* raises the obvious difficulty: we have been *davening* since that time — how can there be a debate about something practiced continuously? The most natural answer is that there was genuine variation in practice, and the debate is over which form is correct. This is not without parallel: the dispute between Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam about the order of the *parshiyot* in *tefillin* is a similar case, and the *Bar Kokhba* caves yielded *tefillin* with both orderings, confirming that divergent practices can and do coexist. Even when a basic form was established, the *Gemara* acknowledges — and the *Tosefta* bears out — that there was a period of latitude during which certain *berakhot* could be combined or arranged differently. The *Gemara* then explains concretely how the confusion arose, through the history of *havdalah*'s movement between the *Shemoneh Esrei* and recitation over a cup of wine.

## Havdalah History

### Havdalah Transitions

The *Gemara* explains the back-and-forth history of *havdalah*. Initially — *betichilah kvauha bitfilah* — it was fixed within the *Shemoneh Esrei*. Then, as the community became more prosperous — *he'ashiru* — they elevated *havdalah* into a more prominent ritual and established it over a cup of wine, presumably removing it from the *Shemoneh Esrei*. When poverty returned — *he'anu* — they reverted and placed it back in the *Shemoneh Esrei*. This movement back and forth is what caused confusion about the original form and accounts for the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the other *tanna'im* about its precise placement.

### Double Havdalah

Once *havdalah* was reinstated in the *Shemoneh Esrei*, the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* declared: *hamavdil bitfilah tzarich sheyavdil al hakos* — one who recites *havdalah* in the *tefillah* must also recite it over a cup of wine. The *Gemara* cites this in two parallel formulations — first attributed to Rabbi Yochanan via Rav Shemen bar Abba, then again via Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in a *baraita* — and then again as a ruling of Rava and Rav Yosef together.

One might ask: if the community had become poor and could not afford wine, how were they now being told to do both? A reasonable resolution is that the ruling means that even if one cannot afford wine, one fulfills the obligation through the *Shemoneh Esrei*, but once resources permit, one should also do *havdalah al hakos*. The *havdalah al hakos* is not merely a redundant ritual — it serves an additional function with its own logic. Just as *kiddush al hakos* frames *Shabbat* in the context of eating, *havdalah al hakos* frames the transition out of *Shabbat* and into weekday eating. This is why the *halakhah* is that even one who has recited *havdalah* in the *Shemoneh Esrei* may not eat on *Motzaei Shabbat* until reciting *havdalah al hakos* — the two performances serve distinct purposes, not redundant ones. Indeed, *havdalah al hakos* has an explicit counterpart in *kiddush al hakos*: just as *kiddush* is said both in the *Shemoneh Esrei* (in *mekadesh HaShabbat*) and over a cup of wine Friday night, so too *havdalah* belongs in both contexts.

### Sufficiency Debate

The *Gemara* then cites Rava's objection from a *baraita* about related cases. The *baraita* states: if one forgot to mention *gevurot geshamim* in *techiyat hameitim*, or forgot to ask for *veten tal umatar* in *birkat hashanim*, one goes back to the proper place. But if one forgot *havdalah* in *chonen hada'at*, one does not go back — *ein machzirin oto* — because one can recite it over a cup of wine. This raises a challenge: the language *mipnei sheyakhol leomra al hakos* — "because one can say it over the cup" — implies that reciting it over the cup is merely an option, available to compensate for missing it in the *Shemoneh Esrei*, not something required as a separate obligation every *Motzaei Shabbat*. The *Gemara* resolves this by rewriting the reading: *lo teima mipnei sheyakhol leomra al hakos, ela eima mipnei she'omra al hakos* — do not say "because one can recite it," but rather "because one will in any case recite it." One who missed *havdalah* in the *Shemoneh Esrei* need not repeat the *Shemoneh Esrei*, because the *havdalah al hakos* that will come later covers it, and both components will be present regardless.

This leads to a sharper question, introduced through a *baraita* taught by Rav Acha Rikha before Rav Chinana: *hamavdil bitfilah meshubach yoter mimi sheyavdil al hakos* — reciting *havdalah* in the *Shemoneh Esrei* is superior to reciting it over the cup of wine. The *baraita* continues: *ve'im hivdil b'zu u'v'zu yanuchu lo berakhot al rosho* — if one recited it in both, blessings should come upon his head. The *Gemara* immediately identifies a contradiction within the *baraita* itself — *hagufa kashya*: if reciting *havdalah* in the *Shemoneh Esrei* alone is superior and sufficient, then adding *havdalah al hakos* afterward would constitute a *berakhah she'einah tzrikhah*, a blessing that is not needed. And the *Gemara* cites Rav (or Reish Lakish, or Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish together) that one who recites an unnecessary *berakhah* transgresses *lo tissa* — taking God's name in vain. While it is generally assumed that this application of *lo tissa* is an *asmakhta* rather than a biblical prohibition, it is worth noting that many *Sephardic poskim* treat *berakhah levatalah* and *berakhah she'einah tzrikhah* with great stringency, holding it to be a genuine biblical violation.

The *Gemara* resolves the internal contradiction by rereading the *baraita*: it should be understood as saying that reciting *havdalah* in one context alone — whichever one — is praiseworthy, and if one managed to do both, it is also fine. This reading, however, effectively presents *havdalah al hakos* as optional when *havdalah* was said in the *Shemoneh Esrei*, which stands in tension with everything the *Gemara* said earlier. The *Gemara* thus reveals that there were in fact two positions: one that viewed the two forms of *havdalah* as presenting a choice (with *Shemoneh Esrei* being the better option), and one that insisted both were obligatory. Over time, the position requiring both prevailed — but the earlier tradition of optionality left traces in these *baraitot*.

### Forgot Both

Rav Chisda asked Rav Sheshet: *ta b'zu u'v'zu mahu* — what is the rule if one forgot *havdalah* in the *Shemoneh Esrei* and also missed it over a cup of wine? The precise scenario requires some thought — what exactly constitutes having "missed" it over the cup? Perhaps one already drank wine, or perhaps the night has passed. In any case, Rav Sheshet ruled: *ta b'zu u'v'zu chozer lerosh* — one must repeat the *Shemoneh Esrei* from the beginning. The *Gemara* does not return to explain the exact parameters of this scenario, but the principle stands.

### Havdalah Like Kiddush

Ravina then asked Rava: *hilkheta mai* — what is the practical ruling? Rava answered: *ki kiddush* — it is like *kiddush*. Just as one recites *mekadesh haShabbat* in the *Shemoneh Esrei* on Friday night and then recites *kiddush* over the cup, so too with *havdalah*: even though one said it in the *Shemoneh Esrei*, one recites it again *al hakos*. The parallel to *kiddush* is a strong one, and if anything strengthens the case for the mandatory double recitation, since *mekadesh HaShabbat* is an explicit, fully formed *berakhah* in its own right — and yet we still require *kiddush al hakos* afterward. How much more so *havdalah*, which in the opinion of the *rabanan* is merely an insertion within *chonen hada'at* and not a standalone *berakhah*, should require the additional ritual over the cup.

## Yom Tov After Shabbat

### Blessing Placement

The next section of the *Gemara* concerns *Yom Tov* that falls on *Motzaei Shabbat*, which creates a structural problem: on a regular weekday, *havdalah* is inserted into *chonen hada'at*, the first of the weekday *berakhot*. But on *Yom Tov*, the *Shemoneh Esrei* has only seven *berakhot* — the middle *berakhot* of the weekday are not recited. There is therefore no *chonen hada'at* into which *havdalah* can be inserted. The debate is then between Rabbi Akiva and the *rabanan* about what to do in this case.

The *Gemara* introduces this through a scene: Rav Zeira was riding on a donkey with Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin following behind him. Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin asked: is it true that you said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the *halakhah* follows Rabbi Eliezer when *Yom Tov* falls after *Shabbat* — that is, that *havdalah* is said in *modim*? Rav Zeira confirmed: *in*, yes.

The *Gemara* then asks: if it is stated as *halakhah*, that implies there is a genuine debate — but is there really one here? Perhaps all agree that when there is no *chonen hada'at* available, *havdalah* goes into *modim*? The *Gemara* answers that the *rabanan* do indeed dispute this in the ordinary case during the year, but as for *Yom Tov* after *Shabbat* specifically, perhaps all agree it goes into *modim*. The *Gemara* then raises the position of Rabbi Akiva, who would hold even in this case for a separate *berakhah*. But the response is decisive: the rest of the year we do not follow Rabbi Akiva's position of making *havdalah* a standalone *berakhah* — *de-tamnei sari tikkun taysha sari lo tikkun*, eighteen *berakhot* were enacted, not nineteen. By the same logic, on *Yom Tov* one cannot add an eighth *berakhah* — *sheva tikkun, tamnei lo tikkun*, seven were enacted, not eight. Rabbi Akiva's position is therefore rejected in both contexts.

### Modim Solution

The *Gemara* now seeks to determine the precise weight of the ruling for *Yom Tov* after *Shabbat*. Rav Zeira says to Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin: the formulation he heard was not *halakhah* but *matin* — we incline toward the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer, that *havdalah* is placed in *modim*. This is less than a definitive *pesak* but still an operative guidance.

The *Gemara* then records a range of positions: Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi said in the name of *Rabbeinu* — which Rashi identifies as Rav — *halakhah*, that we rule like Rabbi Eliezer. Others say *mattei*, we incline. Rabbi Yochanan said that *modim* is the placement and, going further, that there is no real dispute about it when *Yom Tov* falls after *Shabbat*, since there is simply no other place to put it. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba's formulation was the most cautious: *nirin* — it appears correct. Rav Zeira ultimately instructs: take Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba's formulation and rely on it — *nekot deRabbi Chiyya bar Abba beyadecha* — precisely because Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba was as careful with his *Rebbe*'s exact language as Rechava of Pumbedita. How careful was Rechava? He cited Rav Yehuda's teaching that the Temple Mount had a double *stoa* — a *stav* within a *stav* — using the term *stav* rather than the standard rabbinic term *istaba*, demonstrating that he preserved the precise word his *Rebbe* used. Here too, one can rely on Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba's *nirin* as an accurate transmission of what Rabbi Yochanan actually said.

The upshot is that the practical conclusion — confirmed by Rav Yosef's formulation of *vatodieinu* — is that when *Yom Tov* falls on *Motzaei Shabbat*, *havdalah* is incorporated into the *berakhah* of *mekadesh Yisrael vehazmanim*. The liturgy Rav and Shmuel composed for this purpose — *vatodieinu Hashem Elokeinu et mishpetei tzidkecha* — is described by Rav Yosef as *marganita beVavel*, a pearl established in Bavel: a beautiful piece of liturgy that threads together the distinction between the sanctity of *Shabbat* and the sanctity of *Yom Tov*, using the language *bein kedushat Shabbat likedushat Yom Tov hivdalta*, "You distinguished between the holiness of *Shabbat* and the holiness of *Yom Tov*." The separation is not between the sacred and the mundane, as in the ordinary *havdalah*, but between two degrees of sanctity — and this language captures that with elegance. This is the text we use today.

## Al Kan Tzipor

### Silencing Rules

The *Gemara* transitions to a *Mishnah* that lists expressions to be silenced during prayer. Two cases are specified: one who says *al kan tzipor yagi'u rakhamekha* — "Your compassion reaches to the bird's nest" — and one who says *modim modim*, repeating the word *modim* twice. In both cases, the ruling is *meshatikin oto* — the person is silenced. The context appears to be someone leading the prayer or perhaps interjecting during the *davening*, though the exact setting — whether in *Shemoneh Esrei*, in *tachanun*, or during *targum* (the Aramaic translation of the Torah reading, which the *Yerushalmi* names explicitly) — is not entirely clear.

The *Mishnah* also lists *ve'al tov yizakher shemekha* — "and for the good Your name shall be remembered" — as problematic, since it implies praising God only for good things, whereas the principle is that one must bless God for the bad as for the good, a topic the *Gemara* will return to in a later *Mishnah*.

The case of *modim modim* is understood readily: repeating the word creates the appearance of acknowledging two separate divine powers — *mishum de'mechzei ke'shtei reshuyot* — which in a Zoroastrian context was a live concern. Even if unintentional, such formulations cannot be permitted. The *Gemara* presents a case of Rabba waiting until a *shliach tzibbur* finished a passage in which he said *atah chasta al kan tzipor, atah chus verakhem aleinu* — You had compassion on the bird's nest, now have compassion on us. Rabba turned to Abaye and said, see how well this *talmid chakham* knows how to appease his Creator — a remark that Abaye recognized as sarcastic, since Rabba intended to test whether Abaye would catch what was wrong. Abaye responded by citing the *Mishnah*: we silence him.

### Two Concerns

The *Gemara* presents two opinions from *amora'im* in the West — Rabbi Yose bar Avin and Rabbi Yose bar Zvida — about what exactly is wrong with *al kan tzipor yagi'u rakhamekha*.

The first opinion is *mipnei shemattil kinah bema'aseh bereishit* — because it causes jealousy among God's creatures. The expression singles out the birds as recipients of divine compassion, implying that other creatures are not similarly cared for, which gives rise to a kind of hierarchical jealousy within creation. The *Yerushalmi*'s version of this concern frames it slightly differently: the one praying is essentially complaining to God — "You had compassion on the mother bird; why won't You have compassion on me?" — which is a kind of challenge or reproach, finding fault with divine conduct.

The second opinion is *mipnei she'oseh midosav shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu rakhamim ve'einam ela gezeirot* — because the person treats God's attributes as expressions of compassion when they are in fact no more than divine edicts. This is the more philosophically weighty position, and it directly implicates the question of *ta'amei hamitzvot*.

### Ta'amei HaMitzvot

The question of why *al kan tzipor* is problematic opens onto one of the central debates in Jewish thought: should the commandments be understood as expressions of underlying values — as *ta'amei hamitzvot* — or should they be treated as pure *gezeirot*, expressions of divine will whose value lies entirely in the act of obedience?

Rashi's reading of this *Gemara* (which appears similarly in Tractate *Megillah*) supports the latter view: the commandments, including *shiluach haken*, are to be approached purely as matters of obedience. To say that God's compassion reaches to the birds is to suggest that the *mitzvah* is motivated by God's care for animal suffering — and that, according to this position, is a misrepresentation. The commandment is a *gezeirah*, and its entire value is the obedience it demands.

Both the *Rambam* and *Ramban* resist this conclusion, though they approach it differently. The *Rambam* reinterprets *gezeirot* not as commandments without reason but as edicts issued precisely because God cares about His subjects — namely, us. God does not directly care about animal suffering; rather, the commandment is designed to cultivate within us the attribute of compassion, since how we treat animals shapes what kind of people we become. The problem with saying *al kan tzipor yagi'u rakhamekha* is therefore not that we sought a reason for the commandment, but that we found the wrong one — God's compassion is directed at us, not at the bird per se. The *Ramban* takes a different tack: he acknowledges that there is an opinion here which treats it as pure obedience, but he maintains that we do not *pasken* that way, and that the Torah intends its commandments as expressions of genuine values that should inform human life beyond the specific acts commanded.

The two opinions within the *Gemara* can themselves be read as taking positions on this debate from two different angles. The first opinion — that the prayer causes jealousy among God's creatures — may be understood as a critique not of *ta'amei hamitzvot* per se but of applying them too narrowly: by saying God is compassionate specifically toward birds, one has defined the value too restrictively, failing to see that the Torah's concern for compassion extends across all creation, not just one species. The second opinion — that it turns *midot* into expressions of *rakhamim* when they are really *gezeirot* — expresses the opposite concern: any engagement with the underlying value risks undermining the sense of obligation, because once one locates the *reason* for a commandment, one may feel entitled to substitute other means of achieving that value in place of the commandment itself. These two positions thus represent the two risks inherent in the project of *ta'amei hamitzvot*: going too far in defining the value, and going too far in allowing the value to displace the obligation.

## Excessive Praise

### Too Many Names

The *Gemara* introduces a case involving Rabbi Chanina. A certain person went down to lead the *Shemoneh Esrei* before Rabbi Chanina and composed an extended litany of divine attributes: *ha'El hagadol hagibor vehanora veha'adir veha'azuz veha'yirei veha'chazak veha'amitz vehavadai vehanichbad* — going on and on well beyond the standard three names. Rabbi Chanina waited until the *shliach tzibbur* had finished and then addressed him: *siyamtinhu lekulei shevakhei demarakh* — have you exhausted all the praises of your Master? *lama li kulei hai* — why all of this?

### Three Names Principle

Rabbi Chanina explains: the three names we use — *ha'El hagadol hagibor* — would themselves be impermissible were it not for the fact that *Moshe Rabbeinu* used them in the Torah (*Devarim* 10:17) and the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* subsequently established them in the liturgy. Once you begin praising God, there is in principle no place to stop — God is infinite, and any finite list of attributes is inadequate. The only license we have to praise God with any words at all is that these specific words were used in the Torah and fixed by the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*. To add further attributes is to step beyond that license without authorization.

### King Parable

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a *mashal*: a king of flesh and blood possessed millions of gold coins, and his subjects praised him for his wealth in silver. This is not a compliment — *vehalo genai hu lo* — it is unbecoming. To praise someone with lesser attributes than they actually possess, even if those attributes are genuinely impressive in ordinary terms, is a form of diminishment when applied to one whose actual stature renders those attributes trivial.

The broader principle is *lekha dumiyah tehillah* — to You, God, silence is praise. Recognizing that no human language can adequately describe the infinite, the most honest posture is restraint. This is consistent with the *Rambam*'s theology of negative theology: any positive attribute applied to God is, strictly speaking, a misrepresentation, because every human concept is finite and therefore categorically inadequate to describe God. In the specific context of prayer, the implication is clear: our license to praise God at all is dependent entirely on the formulations authorized by *Moshe* and fixed by the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*, and the appropriate response to the impulse to say more is to recognize that less — ultimately, silence — is truer.

## Free Will

### Heaven's Hands

Rabbi Chanina teaches: *hakol biydei shamayim chutz miyirat shamayim* — everything is in the hands of heaven except the fear of heaven. The verse he brings is Moshe's address in *Devarim* (10:12): *ve'atah Yisrael, mah Hashem Elohekha sho'el me'imakh ki im leyirah* — what does God ask of you but to fear Him? The asking presupposes that it is up to the person to give or withhold — God will not compel it. A person's genetic endowment, the circumstances into which they are born, the full range of natural and historical conditions — all of these may be determined from above. But whether one ultimately chooses to fear God and do what is right: that is the one domain God does not control, and therefore the one domain that is truly ours.

### Treasure Yirah

The *Gemara* then raises an apparent objection: if Moshe says *ki im leyirah*, as if fear of heaven were a modest request, is *yirat shamayim* really a small thing? Rabbi Chanina cites Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *ein lo leHakadosh Baruch Hu beveit genazav ela otzar shel yirat shamayim* — God has in His storehouse nothing but the treasury of fear of heaven. *Yirat shamayim* is the rarest and most precious thing there is — all the more so because it is the one thing God does not Himself supply. That it is left entirely to human choice makes it, when genuinely achieved, extraordinarily valuable.

### Moshe Perspective

The *Gemara* resolves the tension between Moshe's casual tone and the rarity of *yirat shamayim* through a *mashal* of Rabbi Chanina: if someone is asked for a large vessel and has one readily available, it feels like nothing to give. If someone is asked for a small vessel and lacks it entirely, even that small request feels enormous. The assessment of difficulty is always relative to the one being asked. For Moshe, who had attained *yirat shamayim* at the highest level, it appeared a simple thing — *in legabei Moshe milta zutraita hi*, for Moshe it was a small matter. But that does not mean it is small for us. The very fact that God treasures it so highly reflects how genuinely rare and difficult it is for human beings to achieve.

## Shema Repetition

### Word vs. Verse

The *Gemara* returns to the category of silencing. The *Mishnah* ruled that one who says *modim modim* is silenced. A *baraita* states: *ha-koreh et Shema vekhophlah, harei zeh meguneh* — one who reads *Shema* and doubles it is acting reprehensibly. The *Gemara* notes a distinction: the *Mishnah* calls for active silencing, whereas the *baraita* uses the word *meguneh*, which is less than silencing — the person's behavior is inappropriate, but one does not necessarily remove them or intervene in the same way.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension by distinguishing between two different kinds of repetition: one who repeats each word individually — *Shema Shema Yisrael Yisrael* — and one who repeats an entire *pasuk*. According to Rashi, repeating each individual word is the lesser offense — it is *meguneh* but not grounds for silencing, since it does not create the impression of acknowledging two divine powers; the person simply seems to be repeating themselves. Repeating an entire *pasuk*, however, gives the appearance of directing one *pasuk* to one divinity and the second *pasuk* to another — that is what rises to the level requiring silencing.

*Tosafot* takes the opposite position: it is repeating each individual word that is the more serious problem, while repeating the entire *pasuk* is the lesser issue. This is in some tension with Rashi, and notably has relevance to those who, out of anxiety about having had sufficient *kavanah*, repeat words or passages.

### Kavanah Later

Rav Papa asked Abaye: *ve-dilma me-ikara lo kivan date'yeh ve-hashta kivan date'yeh* — perhaps the reason someone repeats the *pasuk* is that he initially lacked sufficient *kavanah* and is now repeating it with proper intention? This is a recognizable concern: people do in practice repeat words or verses out of concern that they did not have adequate focus the first time.

### Severe Response

Abaye's response is sharp: *havruta klapei shamaya?* — are you pals with heaven? The implication is that claiming one lacked *kavanah* and therefore deserves a do-over is a kind of presumptuous familiarity — as if one can casually admit to God that one was inattentive and expect to simply be given another opportunity. The *Gemara* pushes the point further: *ei lo kivan date'yeh me-ikara, machinan leh bemarzafta de-nafcha ad de-makhvin date'yeh* — if one did not have *kavanah* the first time, we beat him with a blacksmith's hammer until he achieves *kavanah*. The violence of the image is clearly hyperbolic, but the point is serious: the correct response to insufficient *kavanah* is not to normalize the makeup, which in effect rewards the lack of attention and encourages it going forward. By refusing any remedy and insisting that the failure stands, one creates a stronger incentive to have *kavanah* in the first place. The *Gemara*'s approach here is not to deny that people struggle with *kavanah* — it is to say that offering a systematic makeup is precisely the wrong way to address the problem. The *sugya* breaks off at this point, to be continued.
