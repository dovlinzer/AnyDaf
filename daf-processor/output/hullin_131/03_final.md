# Hullin 131 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Chisda's Ruling

### Rav Chisda Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא דִּכְתִיב ״זֶה״, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מָמוֹן שֶׁאֵין לוֹ תּוֹבְעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say that** it is because **"this" is written** in the verse, indicating that only the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw themselves are given to the priests. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is **because it is money that has no claimants.**

The daf opens by returning to Rav Chisda's ruling that one who damages or destroys the priestly gifts — most prominently *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva*, but extending as well to classical gifts such as *terumah* and *ma'aser*, and by analogy to the gifts given to the poor such as *leket*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah* — is not obligated to pay in a court of law.

### Mamon She'ein Lo

The reason for this exemption is that these gifts belong to no single identifiable claimant. This is the principle of *mamon she'ein lo tov'in*: property without individual claimants. The gifts are owned by the collective — the collective of *kohanim* or the collective of the poor — and since no particular person was harmed in any provable or assignable sense, no individual can file suit. This is distinct from a class-action scenario in secular law, where many individuals are each separately injured and combine their claims. Here, no specific person was deprived of anything that was demonstrably theirs. One person among the group lost out, but it cannot be proven which one.

### Ownership vs Mitzvah

A more radical explanation was also considered, grounded in the verse of *vezeh* — that the *kohanim* and *levi'im* do not own these gifts at all until the moment of formal separation or bestowal, and that the giver carries only a *mitzvah* obligation rather than a genuine proprietary debt. However, because the *gemara* has broadened the discussion well beyond *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* to encompass *terumah*, *ma'aser*, and the gifts to the poor, the primary analytical weight falls on the *mamon she'ein lo tov'in* explanation: ownership resides in the collective, not in any individual who could enforce a claim.

### Midat Chasidut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִדַּת חֲסִידוּת שָׁנוּ כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The mishna **taught an attribute of piety here.**

That said, the *gemara* concludes that one is not entirely off the hook in a moral sense. Even though no court can compel payment, there exists an obligation — at minimum the dimension of *midat chasidut*, and perhaps stronger than that — to make good to the collective. If these were funds of the poor, one ought to give to the poor; if they were funds of the *kohanim*, one ought to give them something in compensation. The enforceability is absent; the moral weight is not.

## Tevel Eating Case

### Tevel Braitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁאָכַל פֵּירוֹתָיו טְבָלִין, וְכֵן לֵוִי שֶׁאָכַל מַעְשְׂרוֹתָיו טְבָלִים, מִנַּיִן שֶׁפָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת קׇדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יָרִימוּ״ – אֵין לְךָ בָּהֶן אֶלָּא מִשְּׁעַת הֲרָמָה וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived **with regard to a homeowner who consumed his produce** while it was **untithed, and similarly, a Levite who consumed his tithes** while they were **untithed, from where** is it derived **that** he is **exempt from payment?** The verse **states: "And they shall not profane the sacred things of the children of Israel, which they set apart** to the Lord" (Leviticus 22:15). This teaches that **you,** the priest, **have** rights **to them only from the time of separation onward.**

The *gemara* now brings a *ta shema* — a challenge to Rav Chisda's position from a *braita*. The *braita* asks: from where do we know that a homeowner who ate his produce while it was still *tevel* — that is, without having first separated *terumot uma'asrot* — and similarly a *levi* who ate his *ma'aser* without having separated *trumat ma'aser* for the *kohen*, is exempt from monetary payment to the *kohen* or *levi* who thereby lost out?

The answer provided is the verse: *velo yechalelu et kodshei benei Yisrael asher yarimu l'Hashem* — "they shall not desecrate the sanctified things of the children of Israel which they shall separate off to God." The *braita* derives: *ein lecha bahen ela mishe'at harama va'eilach* — you, the *kohen* or the *levi*, have no standing in them until the moment of separation. Their ownership does not begin until the gift is actually set apart.

### Ukimta Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ. אִי דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ, מַאי לְמֵימְרָה? דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ בְּטִבְלַיְיהוּ, וְקָסָבַר הַאי תַּנָּא: מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ – כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **Here, we are dealing with** a case **where** the gifts already **came into** the priest's **possession.** The *baraita* teaches that the priest may claim them in court if the owner stole them from him. The Gemara asks: **If** this is a case **where they came into his possession, what** is the purpose **of stating** that the priest may claim them in court? That is obvious. The Gemara responds: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where they came into his possession while they** were still **untithed, and this *tanna* holds** that **gifts that have not been separated are considered as though they have been separated.**

The *gemara* immediately draws an inference from this *braita*: the verse establishes that *before* separation there is no ownership and no obligation to pay — but the implication (*ha mishe'at harama va'eilach*) is that *after* separation, if you were to eat or destroy the *terumah* or *ma'aser*, you would be obligated to pay. This seems to be a strong challenge to Rav Chisda's blanket rule.

The *gemara* responds with a notably narrow *ukimta*. The contrast being drawn in the *braita* is not, as one might have assumed, between *tevel* in the owner's hands versus separated *terumah* in the owner's hands. Rather, the *braita* is contrasting *tevel* in the owner's hands with *tevel* that has already been physically transferred to the *kohen*. The *tanna* here holds the view of *matnot shelo hurmu kemi shehurmu damyan* — that even before formal separation, the gifts are treated as though they have already been designated. What therefore determines liability is not whether separation has occurred, but rather whose possession the produce is in. Once the *kohen* has received the grain — even if it still has the status of *tevel* — he fully owns the *terumah* embedded within it, and if you were then to damage it, you would owe him.

### Tosafot Challenge

This *ukimta* is striking, because it fundamentally inverts the plain reading of the *braita*. The entire thrust of the passage was to draw a legal distinction along the axis of separation: before separation, no ownership; after separation, full ownership. The *gemara* replaces that axis entirely with the axis of possession. Tosafot is deeply troubled by this move and attempts to explain the *gemara*'s direction. This is a recognizable Talmudic pattern: Rav Chisda has made a ruling, a *braita* seems to contradict it squarely, and the *gemara* constructs an unusual or narrow framing to avoid a direct conflict — even when that framing cuts against the *braita*'s own internal logic.

## Royal Seizure Case

### Seizure Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי שֶׁאָנְסוּ בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ גׇּרְנוֹ, אִם בְּחוֹבוֹ – חַיָּיב לְעַשֵּׂר, אִם בְּאַנְפָּרוּת – פָּטוּר מִלְּעַשֵּׂר.
>
> **Translation:** In a case **where the household of the king seized one's threshing floor** by force, **if** they took it **as** payment of **his debt** owed to the king, then **he is obligated to tithe** other grain. **If they took it without reason [*anparot*],** then **he is exempt from tithing.**

The *gemara* tries again with another *ta shema*. The case: the king seizes someone's entire granary — whether as payment for back taxes or through outright confiscation. Had that grain remained with the owner, he would have been obligated to separate *terumot uma'asrot* from it, including the *ma'aser* that would have gone to a *levi*. Does he still owe that *ma'aser* to the *levi*, even though the grain is gone?

### Debt vs Seizure

The *braita* draws a sharp distinction. If the seizure was *bechovo* — the owner genuinely owed those taxes, and the grain was taken as legitimate payment of a real debt — then he is *chayav le'aser*: he must give *ma'aser* from other grain to the *levi*. The *levi* was effectively cheated out of his portion because the owner used what would have been the *levi*'s *ma'aser* to satisfy his own debt. But if the seizure was *be'anparut* — an unjust, coercive taking, where the king simply grabbed the property without any real debt underlying it — then the owner is *patur*: he is exempt. Since he did not use the grain to discharge a legitimate obligation, and the grain was simply stolen from him, he is not required to compensate the *levi* from other resources.

This case seems to pose a powerful challenge to Rav Chisda, since it implies that there is a genuine monetary obligation to the *levi* that can be triggered even before any formal separation or transfer has taken place.

### Mishtarshe Leh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּקָא מִשְׁתָּרְשִׁי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It **is different there, since** the seizure **causes benefit for him.**

The *gemara* answers: *shani hatam dekamishtarshe leh* — that case is different, because there the benefit has taken root by him. This introduces a crucial halakhic concept. Even in the absence of a formal act of damage or theft or borrowing, there are situations where a person has concretely benefited from someone else's property, and that benefit is treated as though it has become embedded in his financial position. The *gemara* elsewhere offers analogous examples: if one's sheep falls from a roof and its fall is cushioned by another's grain, no formal damage has been committed — but the sheep owner derived a real benefit from the other's property, and that benefit is considered to have "taken root" in his ledger. Similarly, if a sheep ate grain in the public domain where no damage liability applies, the sheep owner nevertheless saved himself the cost of feeding the animal, and that saving constitutes a concrete benefit derived from the other's property.

In the royal seizure case, when the owner had a real debt and the king's seizure paid it off, the owner benefited by the amount of one box of *ma'aser*. He owed ten boxes but only had to use nine of his own; the tenth — which belonged conceptually to the collective of *levi'im* — covered his debt for him. That concrete financial benefit has "taken root" in his accounts. He cannot be taken to court by any particular *levi*, because no individual *levi* can prove it was his money — the principle of *mamon she'ein lo tov'in* still applies. But the abstract reality of having received a benefit from collective property generates an obligation to restore that benefit to the collective. This is the principle of *mishtarshe leh*, and the *gemara* emphasizes that it is a significant halakhic concept, whether its source is rabbinic or biblical.

### Tosafot Distinction

This answer, however, creates a problem that Tosafot raises directly. Recall that Rav Chisda's ruling extended not only to damaging the priestly gifts but also to eating them. If someone ate *matnot kehuna* before they were given, Rav Chisda says there is no obligation to pay. Yet eating also produces a benefit — one saved oneself a meal. Why does *mishtarshe leh* not apply to eating just as it applies to paying off a debt?

Tosafot's answer draws a meaningful distinction. When you eat something, the sense in which you have a concrete positive balance in your ledger is not straightforward. You might have gone without the meal; the benefit is diffuse and not precisely quantifiable. But when you pay off a real debt with property that wasn't entirely yours, the bottom line benefit is unmistakable and precisely calculable: you owed ten and only had to supply nine of your own. That elevated and concrete form of benefit — a genuine improvement to your financial position that can be measured — is what generates the *mishtarshe leh* obligation. Mere consumption, without that kind of clear positive ledger entry, does not rise to the same level. Hence Rav Chisda can maintain that eating does not obligate, while the debt-payment case does.

## Organ Purchase Case

### Organ Braitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מְכוֹר לִי בְּנֵי מֵעֶיהָ שֶׁל פָּרָה, וְהָיָה בָּהֶן מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה – נוֹתְנָן לַכֹּהֵן, וְאֵינוֹ מְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן הַדָּמִים. לָקַח הֵימֶנּוּ בְּמִשְׁקָל – נוֹתְנָן לַכֹּהֵן, וּמְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** If an Israelite **says** to a butcher: **Sell me the innards of** a particular **cow, and there were gifts of the priesthood** included **with it,** the purchaser **must give them to the priest, and** the butcher **may not deduct** the value of the gifts **from the money** that the purchaser pays **him.** If **he purchased** the innards **from** the butcher **by weight,** the purchaser **must give** the gifts **to the priest, and** the butcher **deducts** the value of the gifts **from the money** that the Israelite pays **him.**

Another *ta shema*. The *braita* describes a case where a person approaches a butcher and says: sell me the internal organs of this cow. The organs include the *keivah*, the stomach, which is one of the gifts owed to the *kohen*. The buyer paid a flat price for all the organs. The ruling: *noten l'kohen* — the buyer must give the *keivah* to the *kohen* — *v'eino menakeh lo min hadamim* — and he cannot claim a refund from the butcher. The rationale is straightforward: if you purchase "the organs" as a category, you are on notice as a buyer that the *keivah* is among them and that you will not be able to keep it. The price was set for all the organs including the one you must give away, and you cannot claim to have been misled.

### Price vs Weight

If, however, the buyer purchased by weight — he asked for a quantity of organ meat, it was weighed out, and the *keivah* happened to be among the pieces — the ruling differs. He still must give the *keivah* to the *kohen*, but here *umenakeh lo min hadamim*: he can seek a refund from the seller for the weight of the *keivah*, since he had no particular reason to expect it would be included and he was charged for weight he cannot keep.

### Transfer Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? לֶיהֱוֵי כְּמַזִּיק מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה אוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלָן! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִיתַנְהוּ בְּעֵינַיְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Why** must the buyer give the maw to the priest? **Let** the butcher's sale of the maw **be** considered **like** a case where one **causes damage to gifts of the priesthood or consumes them.** It **is different there, as** the gifts **are intact.**

The *gemara* then poses its challenge: once ownership has transferred through the sale, why must the buyer give the *keivah* to the *kohen* at all? Let this be like the case of *mazik matnot kehuna* or *ochlan* — let it be as though the gifts have been consumed or destroyed, and since no individual *kohen* can demand compensation in that scenario, why should the buyer be obligated to give anything?

### Gifts While Exist

The *gemara*'s answer here is relatively straightforward: *shani hatam d'isnehu b'eineihu* — the case is different there because the gifts still exist. The entire question of Rav Chisda's ruling concerned situations where the gifts had been damaged or destroyed and nothing remained. Wherever the gifts physically exist, they must be given — the *mitzvah* to give them and the collective ownership of them by the *kohanim* remain intact regardless of whose hands the gifts have passed through. The sale does not extinguish the obligation; it merely shifts whose hands the item is in. The *gemara* notes that one possible reason to have thought otherwise would be the position grounded in the *pasuk* of *vezeh* — that the obligation is purely situational and tied to the original owner's possession — but even on that view, the ruling is that wherever the gifts exist, they must be given.

## Nine Gifts List

### Gift Enumeration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּשְׁעָה נִכְסֵי כֹהֵן – תְּרוּמָה, וּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר, וְחַלָּה, רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז, וּמַתָּנוֹת, וְהַדְּמַאי, וְהַבִּכּוּרִים, וְהַקֶּרֶן, וְהַחוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Nine** items are the **property of a priest: *Teruma*, *teruma* of the tithe, *ḥalla*,** the portion of dough given to the priest, **the first sheared** wool, **gifts** of the priesthood, **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*], first fruits, the principal** value of the property of a convert, **and the** additional **one-fifth.**

The *gemara* now cites a *braita* listing nine gifts of the *kohen* — though the text is subject to variant readings: Rashi's version reads fifteen, Tosafot's reads seven, and the precise enumeration has exercised the commentators considerably. Setting aside the question of why some gifts are included and others omitted, the focus here is on the list's purpose and what it teaches.

The gifts enumerated include *terumah*; *terumat ma'aser*, the tenth-of-the-tenth that the *levi* gives to the *kohen* from the *ma'aser* he receives; *challah*, the dough gift; *reishis hagez*, the first shearings; *umatanos*, the gifts of *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* from the slaughter of an animal; *hademai*, which is a rabbinic category — when one buys grain from an *am ha'aretz* whose *terumot uma'asrot* observance is uncertain, one separates *terumat ma'aser* and gives it to a *kohen*, since *ma'aser* itself is assumed to have been taken but *terumat ma'aser* cannot be assumed; *habikkurim*, the first fruits; and *keren vachomesh* — the principal and fifth paid back in the case where someone stole from a convert, swore falsely in denial, and the convert subsequently died without heirs. The Torah directs that in such a case the repayment goes to the *kohanim*.

### Nichsei Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי, לָאו לְהוֹצִיאָן בְּדַיָּינִין? לָא, לִכְדִתְנַן: לָמָה אָמְרוּ נִכְסֵי כֹהֵן? שֶׁקּוֹנֶה בָּהֶן עֲבָדִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת וּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה, וּבַעַל חוּב נוֹטְלָן בְּחוֹבוֹ, וְאִשָּׁה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** matter are these items considered the property of a priest? **Is it not with regard to extracting them with judges?** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **with regard to that which we learned:** **To what** end did **they say** that these items are the **property of a priest?** It means **that** a priest may **purchase with them slaves and lands and a non-kosher animal; and a lender takes them** as payment **of his debt; and** if the **wife** of a priest is divorced, she takes them as payment **of her marriage contract; and** a priest may purchase **a Torah scroll** with them.

The question raised is: why does the *braita* call these gifts *nichsei kohen* — the property of the *kohen* — rather than simply calling them gifts to the *kohen*? The word *nichsei*, property, implies full ownership. Does this not suggest that the *kohen* owns them even before they have been physically given, such that if you damaged them you would have to pay him? Surely if calling them his "property" meant only that he owns them once he has received them, that would be patently obvious and not worth stating.

### Freedom of Use

The *gemara* answers: *lo* — that is not the point being made. The purpose of calling them *nichsei kohen* is explained by reference to a *mishna* in Bikkurim: *lama amru nichsei kohen* — why did they call them the property of the *kohen*? *Shekoneh bahen avadim vekarka'os uveheimah teme'ah* — to teach that the *kohen* has complete freedom in how he uses or spends the proceeds. Unlike certain sanctified things — such as the flesh of a *chatat* offering, which cannot be taken out of the *Beis HaMikdash* and cannot be exchanged for other goods — the gifts enumerated here, even those that carry *kedusha* such as *terumah*, may be freely used for any commercial purpose. The *kohen* can sell his *terumah* to other *kohanim*, use the proceeds to buy land, slaves, non-kosher animals, pay off debts, and a wife can collect her *kesuvah* from these goods. The emphasis of *nichsei kohen* is the *kohen*'s freedom of disposition, not a statement about pre-transfer liability. Rashi notes that the inclusion of a *sefer Torah* in the list is puzzling — if one may buy a pig with it, of course one may buy a *sefer Torah* — but regardless, the halakhic point stands: the phrase *nichsei kohen* does not constitute proof that one who damages these gifts before giving them is civilly liable.

## Levi Grabbing Case

### Levi Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא לֵיוָאָה דַּהֲוָה חָטֵף מַתְּנָתָא, אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַב, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא מִסָּתְיֵיהּ דְּלָא שָׁקְלִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, אֶלָּא מִיחְטָף נָמֵי חָטֵיף?!
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain Levite who would snatch gifts** of the priesthood from children who were delivering them. **They came** and **told Rav** about this Levite. Rav **said to them: Is it not enough that** when he slaughters his own animals **we do not take** the gifts **from him, but he also snatches** gifts that are being delivered to priests?

At this point the *gemara* shifts direction somewhat. There was a certain *levi* who would hover near the butcher's stall, and as soon as an animal was slaughtered he would rush in and grab the *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva*. The problem is obvious: the gifts are designated for a *kohen*, not a *levi*.

### Am Status Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, אִי אִיקְּרוֹ ״עַם״ – מִשְׁקָל נָמֵי לִשְׁקוֹל מִינַּיְיהוּ. אִי לָא אִיקְּרוֹ ״עַם״ – רַחֲמָנָא פַּטְרִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And** what does **Rav** maintain in this regard? **If** he maintains that Levites **are called** part of the **"people,"** then **let one take** the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw **from them as well.** **And if they are not called** part of the **people,** then **the Merciful One** has **exempted them.**

People came and reported this behavior to Rav. Rav responded: *lo mistaya delo shaklina minei* — is it not enough that we do not take his own *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* away from him when he slaughters his own animals? We refrain from forcing him to give it, and that already represents a significant concession. *Ella miktaf nami khatif* — and he also goes and grabs other people's? This expression of moral outrage reflects Rav's view that the *levi*'s behavior is at minimum improper, and that his being spared from giving his own gifts already represents unusual leniency.

The *gemara* now unpacks the logic of Rav's statement and finds a tension within it. The question is whether *levi'im* are included in the word *am* — the people — in the verse *me'et ha'am*, "from the people," which establishes the obligation to give *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva*. There are many contexts in which *levi'im* stand apart from the general Israelite community: they receive no tribal land inheritance and are not counted among the fighting force. So does *me'et ha'am* include *levi'im* or not?

If *levi'im* are included in *am* and are obligated to give *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva*, the *gemara* asks: *mishkal nami lishklo minaihu* — why didn't Rav compel the *levi* to give his own gifts? And if *levi'im* are not included in *am* and are genuinely exempt — *Rachmana patreinhu*, the Torah exempted them — then why did Rav speak in a tone implying that there was something questionable about the *levi* keeping his own? If he is exempt, he is simply exercising his exemption; why frame it as a concession?

### Rav's Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי אִיקְּרוֹ ״עַם״ אִי לָא אִיקְּרוֹ ״עַם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **is uncertain whether or not they are called** part of the **people.**

The *gemara* resolves this by saying: *mesafeka leh* — Rav was uncertain. He did not know whether *levi'im* are called *am* or not. Because of that doubt, Rav could not force the *levi* to give his own *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* — since in monetary matters the operative principle is *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah*: the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract property from another. Since Rav could not prove the obligation, he could not compel payment. But it was not enough for this *levi* to benefit from that doubt — he then proceeded to grab gifts belonging to others entirely.

This raises the broader question of why we apply the civil framework (*hamotzi mechaveiro*) rather than the religious framework of *safek de'oraita le'chumra*. The answer — as discussed in the *Birchat Shmuel* — is that when the fundamental question is civil in nature (does one person owe another money?), we adjudicate it as a civil matter, and the religious dimensions of the obligation are understood through that civil determination. The *kohen* or *levi* seeking the gift bears the burden of proving entitlement; if they cannot, the holder is not compelled.

## Kohen Gifts Rules

### Rav Idi Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב פָּפָּא וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין לְרַב פָּפָּא: אַרְבַּע מַתְּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים שֶׁבַּכֶּרֶם – הַפֶּרֶט, וְהָעוֹלֵלוֹת, וְהַשִּׁכְחָה, וְהַפֵּאָה; וְשָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁבַּתְּבוּאָה – הַלֶּקֶט, וְהַשִּׁכְחָה, וְהַפֵּאָה; שְׁנַיִם שֶׁבָּאִילָן – הַשִּׁכְחָה וְהַפֵּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Pappa was sitting and saying this *halakha***, in the name of Rav. **Rav Idi bar Avin raised an objection to Rav Pappa** from a *baraita*: **Four gifts** are left **to the poor** from the produce **of a vineyard: The individual fallen** grapes **[*peret*], and the incompletely formed clusters** of grapes **[*olelot*], and the forgotten** clusters, **and *pe'a*. And three** gifts are left to the poor **from grain: The gleanings,** i.e., sheaves that fell during the harvest, **and the forgotten** sheaves, **and the *pe'a*. Two** gifts are left to the poor from the fruit **of a tree: The forgotten** fruits **and the *pe'a*.**

Rav Pappa was sitting and presenting this entire discussion about Rav's doubt regarding the *levi*'s status. Rav Idi bar Avin approached Rav Pappa with an attempt to resolve the question by drawing on an analogy from the *braita* concerning gifts to the poor. The structure of the comparison — between how the gifts of the poor and the gifts of the *kohanim* are treated with respect to *tovat hana'ah* and compulsion — is meant to shed light on whether a *levi* would be obligated to give *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* to a *kohen*. This argument unfolds across the next section.

## Poor's Gifts

### Vineyard Gifts

The *braita* presents a systematic summary of the gifts given to the poor during the harvest season. In the vineyard there are four such gifts: *peret* and *olelot*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah*. *Peret* corresponds to *leket* in a grain field — the individual grapes that drop from the hand during harvesting. *Olelot* are underdeveloped clusters: a normal cluster has a shoulder (*katef*) and a lower dripping section (*nataf*); a cluster that has formed without one of these characteristic features is considered an *olelot* and must be left for the poor. *Shikcha* refers to clusters that were overlooked and left behind after the harvest — things "behind you" that you forgot. *Pe'ah* is the intentional leaving of a corner section of the vineyard for the poor.

### No Choice Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלָּן אֵין בָּהֶם טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה לַבְּעָלִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ עָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מוֹצִיאִין מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all of** these gifts, **the owner** of the produce **does not have the benefit of discretion.** **And even a poor person of Israel** who owns a vineyard, field, or tree must leave these gifts; and if he does not, **the court removes** them **from his possession.**

Of all these gifts, the *braita* rules: *ein bahen tovat hana'ah le-be'alim* — the owners have no right to designate which poor person receives them. The reason, as the *gemara* will derive, is that the verse uses the language of *azivah* — abandonment: *ta'azov otam*, you shall leave them. The owner steps back; the poor come and take. He does not give to specific recipients of his choosing; he abandons the produce and the poor gather it. The classic imagery is precisely of an owner who is absent while the poor collect freely.

### Poor Give Too

A significant ruling follows: *ve'afilu ani she'be'Yisrael motzi'in oto miyado* — even a poor person who happens to own a small piece of a vineyard or a field is compelled to leave these gifts for other poor. He cannot reason that since he himself is poor, he will simply retain everything for his own use. The *gemara* derives this from the verse *v'leket k'tzircha lo telaket*, reading it as addressed to the *ani*: even you, the *ani*, shall not gather your own *leket*. You must leave it for others.

### Maaser Ani House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעְשַׂר עָנִי הַמִּתְחַלֵּק בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – יֵשׁ בּוֹ טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה לַבְּעָלִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ עָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּדוֹ, וּשְׁאָר מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה, כְּגוֹן הַזְּרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּבָה – אֵין מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן מִיָּדוֹ, לֹא מִכֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן וְלֹא מִלֵּוִי לְלֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, with regard to the **poor man's tithe, which is distributed** from **within one's house,** the owner **has the benefit of discretion. And even** in the case of **a poor person in Israel,** if he fails to separate it, **the court removes it from his possession. And** with regard to **other gifts of the priesthood, such as the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw,** the court **does not remove them, neither from a priest** to give **to** another **priest, nor from a Levite** to give **to** another **Levite.**

The rules differ, however, for *ma'aser ani* — the tithe given to the poor in the third and sixth years of the Sabbatical cycle — when it is distributed from within the owner's house (*hamitchalek be-toch beito*). Here the *gemara* rules: *yesh bo tovat hana'ah le-be'alim* — the owner does have the right to decide which poor person receives it. The reason is that the verse uses the language of *netinah* — giving: *ki titein*, you shall give. When you give, you are an active agent; you direct the gift to a specific recipient of your choice. This is in contrast to the *azivah* model, where you relinquish control entirely.

Tosafot, drawing on the Yerushalmi, makes a further distinction: there may actually be two different statuses of *ma'aser ani* depending on where the separation occurs. If separated at the threshing floor, it has the status of the field gifts — the owner abandons it and the poor come to take. But if brought into the house and then separated, the owner's connection to it is stronger, and the *netinah* model applies — he gives it actively to a recipient of his choosing. Tosafot also notes that even where the owner has *tovat hana'ah* and may designate the recipient, if a poor person comes and forcibly takes possession, the owner cannot recover it; the choice has been subverted but the taking is valid.

## Kohen Gifts Rules

### Kohen No Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁאָר מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה כְּגוֹן זְרוֹעַ וְלִיחָיַיִם וְקֵבָה – אֵין מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן מִיָּדוֹ, לֹא מִכֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן וְלֹא מִלֵּוִי לְלֵוִי. הָא מִלֵּוִי לְכֹהֵן מוֹצִיאִין, אַלְמָא אִיקְּרוּ ״עַם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* taught: **And** with regard to **other gifts of the priesthood, such as the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw,** the court **does not remove them, neither from a priest** to give **to** another **priest nor from a Levite** to give **to** another **Levite.** It may be inferred that the court does **remove** the gifts **from a Levite** to give **to a priest. Evidently,** Levites **are called** part of the **"people."**

The *braita* continues: *ve'sha'ar matnot kehuna kegon zroa lechayayim vekeiva* — as for the other gifts of the *kehuna*, such as *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* — *ein motzi'in otan miyado*: you do not force a *kohen* to give them to another *kohen*. This stands in contrast to the poor, where we compel even a poor person to give to other poor. By the priestly gifts, a *kohen* who holds his own *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* is not forced to hand it over to another *kohen*.

### Levi to Kohen

The *braita* then adds — puzzlingly — *velo milevi l'levi*: nor do you compel a *levi* to give it to another *levi*. Since *levi'im* are not entitled to receive *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* in the first place, the statement seems odd on its face. But the *gemara* focuses on what this implies by contrast: if you don't force a *levi* to give it to another *levi*, the implication (*ha*) is that you would force a *levi* to give it to a *kohen*. This in turn would establish that *levi'im* are indeed obligated — they are included in *am* — and must give the gifts just as any *Yisrael* would. This constitutes a potential resolution of Rav's doubt. The *gemara* will now subject this inference to a series of interpretive challenges.

## Poor's Gifts…

### Field Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבַּע מַתְּנוֹת שֶׁבַּכֶּרֶם: הַפֶּרֶט, וְהָעוֹלֵלוֹת, וְהַשִּׁכְחָה, וְהַפֵּאָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכַרְמְךָ לֹא תְעוֹלֵל וּפֶרֶט כַּרְמְךָ לֹא תְלַקֵּט״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that **four gifts** are left to the poor from the produce **of a vineyard: The *peret*, and the *olelot*, and the forgotten** clusters, **and the *pe'a*, as it is written: "And you shall not glean [*te'olel*] your vineyard, neither shall you gather the fallen fruit [*peret*] of your vineyard;** you shall leave them for the poor and for the stranger" (Leviticus 19:10).

The *gemara* turns to enumerate the scriptural sources for each gift. For the three field gifts — *leket*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah* — the verse states: *uvkutzrechem et ketzir artzechem lo techaleh pe'at sadecha bekutzrecha* — do not finish the corner of your field in your harvesting (this is *pe'ah*); *v'leket k'tzircha lo telaket* — do not gather the droppings of your harvest (this is *leket*); and *ki tiktzor k'tzircha b'sadecha v'shachachta omer basadeh* — when you harvest and you forget a sheaf in the field (this is *shikcha*).

### Tree Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי תִבְצֹר כַּרְמְךָ לֹא תְעוֹלֵל אַחֲרֶיךָ״, אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: ״אַחֲרֶיךָ״ – זוֹ שִׁכְחָה. פֵּאָה – גָּמַר ״אַחֲרֶיךָ״ ״אַחֲרֶיךָ״ מִזַּיִת, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי תַחְבֹּט זֵיתְךָ לֹא תְפָאֵר אַחֲרֶיךָ״, וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: שֶׁלֹּא תִּטּוֹל תִּפְאַרְתּוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it **is** also **written: "When you gather the grapes of your vineyard, you shall not glean it [*te'olel*] after you;** it shall be for the stranger, for the fatherless, and for the widow" (Deuteronomy 24:21). **Rabbi Levi says** that **"after you," this** is a reference to **forgotten** clusters. The *halakha* that the mitzva of ***pe'a*** applies to one's vineyard is **derived** by a verbal analogy between **"after you"** in that verse and **"after you" from** another verse concerning **an olive** tree. **As it is written: "When you beat your olive tree, you shall not go over the boughs [*tefa'er*] after you;** it shall be for the stranger, for the fatherless, and for the widow" (Deuteronomy 24:20); **and the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that you should **not take** all of **its splendor [*tiferet*] from it.**

For trees, the *gemara* establishes only two gifts: *shikcha* and *pe'ah*; there is no *leket* equivalent for trees. The source is: *ki tachbot zeitecha lo tefa'er acharecha* — when you beat your olive tree, do not *tefa'er* it afterward. Rav Levi explains that the word *acharecha* — "after you" or "behind you" — refers to *shikcha*, things left behind that were forgotten. The school of Rabbi Yishmael interprets *lo tefa'er* as meaning "do not strip it of its glory" — that is, leave some behind — which is the law of *pe'ah*. The word *tefa'er* is understood as a verb meaning to remove *pe'ah*, parallel to the way certain Hebrew verbs denote the removal of something rather than its addition. Having established both *shikcha* and *pe'ah* for olive trees, the *gemara* applies a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *acharecha* to derive the same two gifts for vineyards.

### Poverty Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ עָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּדוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֶקֶט קְצִירְךָ לֹא תְלַקֵּט לֶעָנִי וְלַגֵּר תַּעֲזֹב אֹתָם״, לְהַזְהִיר עָנִי עַל שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also stated: **And even a poor person of Israel** who owns a vineyard, field, or tree must leave these gifts, and if he does not, **the court removes** them **from his possession.** This is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "Neither the gleaning of your harvest shall you gather; for the poor you shall leave them, and for the stranger"** (Leviticus 23:22). Since the verse juxtaposes the words "the poor" to the mitzva in the previous clause, this serves **to warn a poor person** that he must also separate these gifts from **his own** produce.

Returning to the ruling that even a poor person must give these gifts to other poor, the *gemara* notes the broader lesson embedded in this halakha. No one, however disadvantaged, is exempt from the obligations of giving. This reflects a profound principle: even those who are themselves recipients of communal charity remain full and equal members of a society structured around mutual obligation. The ability to give — even in small measure — carries its own dignity. It means something to be a contributor, not only a recipient. This is reflected as well in the concept of *maaser kesafim*: a person in genuine financial difficulty may direct much of his *maaser* toward his own needs, for *tzedaka* begins at home — but the framework of giving is never entirely suspended.

## Narrowing Attempts

### Ma'aser Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן הַזְּרוֹעַ וְלֹא זְרוֹעַ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ – מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* is not referring to the actual gifts of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw themselves. Rather, it is referring to a gift that is **like the foreleg,** the jaw, and the maw, **but not** the **foreleg,** the jaw, and the maw themselves. **And what is this?** It is the **first tithe.**

The *gemara* now attempts to narrow the *braita* that implied *levi'im* are obligated, seeking to read it as not referring to *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* at all. The first reinterpretation: when the *braita* says "something like the *zeroa*" but not the *zeroa* itself — *kegon hazroa velo zroa* — perhaps it refers to *ma'aser rishon*.

The logic runs as follows. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya holds that *ma'aser rishon* may be given to a *kohen*, since *kohanim* are themselves members of the tribe of Levi. Rabbi Akiva disagrees, holding that *terumah* goes to a *kohen* and *ma'aser rishon* goes to a *levi*. If we follow Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya post-Ezra's *takana*, the picture shifts: Ezra was angered that the *levi'im* did not make *aliyah* during the Second Temple period, and — though not explicitly stated in the biblical text — the *gemara* understands that he instituted a fine: henceforth, do not give *ma'aser rishon* to the *levi'im*; give it to the *kohanim* instead, since they too are Levites.

Under this reading, the *braita*'s statement that you do not force a *kohen* to give to another *kohen* means: a *kohen* who receives *ma'aser rishon* need not pass it to another *kohen*. You do not force a *levi* to give to another *levi* — meaning the *levi* may keep his own *ma'aser* without being forced to share with another *levi*. And the implied case where you would force the gift — from *levi* to *kohen* — refers to the post-Ezra situation where *ma'aser* should be directed to *kohanim*.

However, the *gemara* objects: even granting Ezra's fine, it could only mean that we stop giving *ma'aser* to *levi'im* going forward — *d'la yahavinan lehu*. It could never mean that we forcibly extract *ma'aser rishon* from a *levi*'s hands and transfer it to a *kohen*. So this reading collapses: we cannot use the post-Ezra framework to generate a case where a *levi* is compelled to give *ma'aser* to a *kohen*.

### Reishis HaGez

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּקַנְסִינְהוּ עֶזְרָא – דְּלָא יָהֲבִינַן לְהוּ מִשְׁקַל מִינַּיְיהוּ מִי אָמַר?! אֶלָּא, כְּגוֹן זְרוֹעַ וְלֹא זְרוֹעַ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ – רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז.
>
> **Translation:** **Say that Ezra penalized them** and decreed **that we should not give them** first tithe, but **did he say** that first tithe should even be **taken from them** and given to the priests? **Rather,** explain instead that the *baraita* is referring to a gift that is **like** the **foreleg,** the jaw, and the maw, **but** is **not** actually the **foreleg,** the jaw, or the maw. **And what is this?** It is **the first sheared** wool.

The *gemara* therefore tries a second reinterpretation: *umai nihu reshit hagez* — the gift referred to is *reishis hagez*, the first shearings. The verse mandating *reishis hagez* does not use the word *am*, so this is a gift that any Israelite — including a *levi* — must give to a *kohen*. Under this reading, the *braita* means: a *kohen* who shears his own sheep need not give *reishis hagez* to another *kohen*; a *levi* who shears his own sheep need not give to another *levi* (who is in any case not entitled to receive it); but a *levi* who refuses to give *reishis hagez* to a *kohen* would be compelled to do so. This reading at least avoids the problems of the *ma'aser rishon* interpretation. But it still does not resolve the question of whether *levi'im* are obligated specifically in *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva*.

### No Kedusha Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, כְּגוֹן תְּרוּמָה וּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר וְחַלָּה – מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן מִיָּדָם, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, כְּגוֹן הַזְּרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּבָה – אֵין מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof with regard to the uncertainty of Rav from a *baraita*. **This is the principle:** With regard to **any item that is of sanctity,** such as *teruma*, and *teruma* of the tithe, and *ḥalla*,** the court **removes it from the possession** of a Levite. **And** with regard to **any item that is not of sanctity, such as the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw,** the court **does not remove it from the possession** of the Levites.

A further *ta shema* is then brought, which states the rule in terms of *kedusha*: anything that has sanctity — *terumah*, *terumat ma'aser*, *challah* — you take out of a *levi*'s hands if he refuses to give it to a *kohen*, because it carries *kedusha* to which the *levi* is not entitled. Anything that does not have sanctity — such as *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* — you do not take out of the *levi*'s hands. At first glance, this seems to state directly that *levi'im* are exempt from *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva*. But the *gemara* applies the same move again: *kegon zroa velo zroa* — this is referring to something like the *zeroa* but not the *zeroa* itself, and *mai nihu* — what is it? *Ma'aser rishon* after Ezra's fine, which has no *kedusha*. Even post-Ezra, when *ma'aser rishon* is directed to *kohanim*, we would not forcibly extract it from a *levi*'s hands. The case of *zeroa lechayayim vekeiva* proper remains unresolved.

## Direct Proof

### Shechting Braitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְכֹהֵן וּלְגוֹי – פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, הָא לְלֵוִי וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל – חַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: An Israelite **who slaughters** an animal **for a priest or for a gentile** is **exempt from** giving **the gifts.** The Gemara infers: This indicates that if an Israelite slaughtered an animal **for a Levite or for an Israelite,** he is **obligated** to give the gifts.

The *gemara* now brings what appears to be a direct and explicit proof. A *braita* states: *hashocheit l'kohen u'l'oveid kochavim patur min hamatanos* — one who slaughters an animal on behalf of a *kohen* or on behalf of a non-Jew is exempt from giving the gifts. The reason in the case of a *kohen* is obvious: the *kohen* himself receives and keeps the gifts. In the case of a non-Jew, the non-Jew has no obligation and when the slaughter is performed for his benefit the gifts do not apply. The *braita* then implies: *ha l'levi u'l'yisrael chayav* — if you slaughter for a *levi* or for a *Yisrael*, you are obligated to give the gifts. This seems to prove that a *levi* is included in the obligation just as a *Yisrael* is.

### Corrective Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא תֵּימָא הָא לְלֵוִי וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל חַיָּיב, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הָא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Do not say** that one should infer that if an Israelite slaughtered an animal **for a Levite or for an Israelite** he is **obligated** to give the gifts. **Rather, say** merely that if an Israelite slaughtered an animal **for** another **Israelite,** he is **obligated** to give the gifts.

The *gemara* responds: *lo teima ha l'levi u'l'yisrael chayav* — do not read it as "for a *levi* or a *Yisrael* you are obligated." Instead, *ela eima ha Yisrael chayav* — read it as "for a *Yisrael* you are obligated." The *levi* case remains unstated and therefore unresolved.

### Counter Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְלֵוִי מַאי פָּטוּר? אִי הָכִי, לִיתְנֵי ״הַשּׁוֹחֵט לַלֵּוִי וְלַגּוֹי פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת״. וְעוֹד, הָא תַּנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט לַכֹּהֵן וְלַגּוֹי – פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, לַלֵּוִי וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל – חַיָּיב! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, when one slaughters an animal **for a Levite, what** is the *halakha*? Is one **exempt? If so, let** the *baraita* **teach: One who slaughters** an animal **for a Levite or for a gentile** is **exempt from** giving **the gifts,** and it would be obvious. **And furthermore, isn't it taught** explicitly in a *baraita* that **one who slaughters for a priest or for a gentile** is **exempt from** giving **the gifts,** whereas one who slaughters **for a Levite or for an Israelite** is **obligated** to give the gifts? This *baraita* apparently constitutes **a conclusive refutation** of the uncertainty **of Rav.**

This corrective reading is met with an immediate objection. If the *levi* were in fact exempt, the *braita* would have said so explicitly — it would have listed the *levi* alongside the *kohen* and the non-Jew as cases of exemption: *hashocheit l'kohen u'l'levi u'l'oveid kochavim patur*. The fact that the *braita* only mentions the *kohen* and the non-Jew as exempt, and then lists the *levi* alongside the *Yisrael* as obligated, is strong evidence that the *levi* is indeed obligated. Furthermore, a second *braita* states explicitly: *l'levi u'l'yisrael chayav* — for a *levi* or a *Yisrael*, you are obligated. This is presented as a *tiyuvta d'Rav* — a formal contradiction of Rav's position.

## Am Inclusion Dispute

### Yom Kippur Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְכִפֶּר אֶת מִקְדַּשׁ הַקֹּדֶשׁ״ – זֶה לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים. ״אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – זֶה הֵיכָל, ״מִזְבֵּחַ״ – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ, ״יְכַפֵּר״ – אֵלּוּ עֲזָרוֹת, ״כֹּהֲנִים״ – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ, ״עַם הַקָּהָל״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, ״יְכַפֵּר״ – אֵלּוּ הַלְוִיִּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rav** could **say to you** that the question of whether or not Levites are called part of the "people" **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And he shall make atonement for the most holy place, and he shall make atonement for the Tent of Meeting and for the altar; and he shall make atonement for the priests and for all the people of the assembly" (Leviticus 16:33). The *baraita* explains: **"And he shall make atonement for the most holy place"; this** is referring to the **innermost sanctum.** **"Tent of Meeting"; this** is referring to the **Sanctuary.** **"Altar";** this is understood **in accordance with its plain meaning.** **"He shall make atonement"; this** is referring to the Temple **courtyards.** **"For the priests";** this is understood **in accordance with its plain meaning.** **"And for all the people of the assembly"; these are the Israelites. "He shall make atonement"; this** is referring to **the Levites.**

Rav, however, does not concede. He responds: *tannai hi* — this is a dispute between *tanna'im*, and there is a *tanna* who supports his position. The *gemara* cites a *braita* on the verse concerning the Yom Kippur atonement: *v'al hakohanim v'al am hakahal yechapper* — "and for the *kohanim* and for the people of the congregation it will atone." The *braita* expounds each word: *kohanim kemashmao* — the *kohanim* as the word implies; *am hakahal eilu yisrael* — the people of the congregation refers to the Israelites; *yechapper eilu haleviyim* — "and it will atone" — this extra word atones for the *levi'im*. The significance: the *levi'im* needed their own separate word for inclusion, meaning they are not subsumed under the term *am*. Had they been included in *am*, the word *yechapper* would have been superfluous.

### Tana Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״יְכַפֵּר״ – אֵלּוּ עֲבָדִים. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר אִיקְּרוֹ ״עַם״, וּמָר סָבַר לָא אִיקְּרוֹ ״עַם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **"He shall make atonement"; this** is referring to Canaanite **slaves.** **What, is it not** that **they disagree about this, as** one **Sage** **holds** that Levites are **called** part of the **"people,"** and **one Sage** **holds** that **they are not called** part of the **"people."**

Another *braita* reads the same verse differently: *yechapper eilu avadim* — "and it will atone" refers to slaves. This *tanna* would say the *levi'im* are included in the word *am* and need no separate word; it is slaves who require the extra inclusion. The *gemara* concludes: *mah lav beha kamiflgi* — is this not precisely the dispute? One *tanna* holds that *levi'im* are called *am* and are included; another holds that they are not called *am* and require a separate word for inclusion. This is a genuine *tannaitic* disagreement.

### Rav's Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, אִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּהַאי תַּנָּא – לֵימָא, וְאִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּהַאי תַּנָּא – לֵימָא! מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי כְּהַאי תַּנָּא אִי כְּהַאי תַּנָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if this is a dispute between *tanna'im*, why is **Rav** uncertain with regard to the status of Levites? **If he holds in accordance with this *tanna*, let him say** that the *halakha* is in accordance with him, **and if he holds in accordance with that *tanna*, let him say** that the *halakha* is in accordance with him. The Gemara responds: Rav **is uncertain whether** the *halakha* is **in accordance with this *tanna* or in accordance with that *tanna*.**

The final line of the *gemara* addresses Rav directly: if Rav holds like the *tanna* who says *levi'im* are included in *am*, let him rule accordingly and compel the *levi* to give the gifts; if he holds like the *tanna* who says they are not included, let him rule accordingly and declare the *levi* exempt. The *gemara* answers: *mesafeka leh* — Rav genuinely does not know. He cannot determine which *tanna*'s view is correct, and he therefore cannot resolve the question with confidence.

The practical consequence follows directly from *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah*: the burden of proof falls on the one who seeks to extract a payment. Since the *kohen* cannot prove that the *levi* is obligated, the *levi* cannot be compelled to give. The evidence adduced in the *braita*ot strongly suggests that *levi'im* are indeed included in *am* and are obligated — but Rav maintains uncertainty, and that uncertainty is sufficient to insulate the *levi* from legal compulsion. Whether this uncertainty survives all the textual evidence brought against it is a question the *gemara* leaves open, to be taken up in the continuation.
