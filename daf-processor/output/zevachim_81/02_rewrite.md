# Zevachim 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye: Initial Blood Only

### Abaye's Limitation

The daf opens in the middle of 81a, at the word *bekosot*, where Abaye introduces an important qualification to the preceding debate. The extended discussion that preceded this point concerned what happens when the blood of a *chatas* — which must be applied in the upper half of the altar — becomes mixed with the blood of an *olah* — which must be applied in the lower half. Since applying the mixture anywhere will necessarily place the wrong blood in the wrong location for one of the two sacrifices, the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* was about how to proceed. Rabbi Eliezer held that one may designate the mismatched blood as water and proceed *lechatchila*, while the *chachamim* refused to sanction the wrong placement *lechatchila*, even though they agreed *bediavad* that it would be valid. Running through all of that debate was the implicit assumption of *yesh bila* — that blood disperses equally throughout a mixture — so that whichever portion of the mixture one applies can be presumed to contain both bloods in proportion. The *gemara* had tried, in light of a statement by Rabbi Eliezer regarding the water of a *chatas* of the *parah adumah*, to argue that Rabbi Eliezer might actually hold *ein bila* — that equal distribution cannot be assumed — and then worked strenuously to fit his positions accordingly. Ultimately the *gemara* concluded that even under *ein bila*, one could explain the cases not as the bloods mixing together but as the various collection bowls becoming confused, so that one pours from each bowl and demonstrably includes both bloods in both applications. With that settled, the *gemara* now turns to a related point that emerged implicitly during the debate but is significant in its own right.

Abaye states: *lo shanu ella techilat hachatas v'olah*. The debate over what to do with a mixture applies only to the initial blood of the *chatas* and the initial blood of the *olah*, where there is genuine tension between the required locations of the two sacrifices. However, *aval sof hachatas v'olah, divrei hakol makom olah makom shirayim* — if one has the remnant blood (*shirayim*) of a *chatas* mixed together with *olah* blood, everyone agrees *lechatchila* that this may be placed in the lower half of the altar, at the wall, as if doing the *olah* application.

### Initial vs. Final Blood

The reasoning behind this distinction requires some unpacking. When the *kohen* first applies the *chatas* blood at the upper portion of the altar, that fulfills the essential application for the *chatas*. What remains is the *shirayim* — the remnant blood — whose proper place is the base of the altar. Now, the base is in the lower portion of the altar, and the *olah* blood is also thrown in the lower portion. So when the *shirayim* of a *chatas* is mixed with *olah* blood, there is no fundamental tension: the lower portion is the correct location for both. This is in fact implicit in a *braita* already cited earlier, which stated that even the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Eliezer that one may *lechatchila* spill the remainder of the blood below — because the *chatas* blood is also properly placed below once the initial application at the top has already occurred.

The *chiddush* being made explicit here, however, is more subtle. When one performs the *olah* blood application, one throws the blood against the wall of the altar in the lower half — typically at a corner, striking two walls simultaneously — and from there the blood runs down toward the base. The *shirayim* of the *chatas*, on the other hand, is supposed to be spilled directly at the base, ideally onto a horizontal surface. Abaye's point is that throwing the mixture against the lower wall — as one would for the *olah* — also counts as an acceptable placement for the *chatas shirayim*, even though the blood reaches the base indirectly by running down rather than being poured directly onto it.

### Rav Yosef Disagrees

Rav Yosef, who was Abaye's teacher, challenges this. He cites a ruling in the name of Rav Yehuda: *shirayim tzrichim itztzaba* — the *shirayim* requires a horizontal platform. Pouring onto a vertical surface, even one from which the blood will naturally run down to the base, does not constitute a valid *shfichat shirayim*. The blood must be placed directly on the horizontal base, not on the wall. According to Rav Yosef, even if the blood runs to the base, the act of placing it on the wall is categorically insufficient.

### Rabbi Shimon Agrees

Rabbi Shimon is then cited as taking a position parallel to Abaye's: *lo shanu ella techilat hachatas v'olah* — the debate applies only to the initial blood. But when it comes to the *shirayim* of the *chatas* mixed with the *olah* blood, *divrei hakol makom olah makom shirayim* — all opinions agree that the wall in the lower portion of the altar is an acceptable place, and one may throw the mixture there *lechatchila*. This aligns with Abaye against Rav Yosef.

### Rabbi Yochanan Disputes

Rabbi Yochanan — with the alternative attribution of Rabbi Eliezer — disagrees, siding with Rav Yosef: *adayin hi machloket* — this question remains a matter of dispute even in the case of *shirayim*. The *chachamim* would still hold that this placement is insufficient, and it would be preferable to pour the remaining blood into the drain rather than treat the wall as an adequate location for the spilling of *shirayim*. So the debate between Abaye and Rabbi Yochanan turns on whether the lower wall of the altar constitutes an acceptable location for the discharge of *shirayim* — a question that will continue to generate analysis throughout the *sugya*.

## Ein Olin Mevatlin

### Kodesh Hem Proof

Rav Huna bar Yehuda now raises an objection to the preceding discussion — specifically to the assumption that this wall-versus-base question is the *chiddush* being taught by any relevant source. He cites the verse in Numbers 18:17, which discusses the *bechor*: *ach bechor shor o bechor kesev o bechor ez lo tifdeh kodesh hem, et damam tizrok al hamizbeach v'et chelvam taktira* — the firstborn of an ox, sheep, or goat may not be redeemed; they are holy. Throw their blood upon the altar and burn their fat. The word *kodesh hem* — "they are holy" — uses the plural, which is understood as carrying a *chiddush*: *she'im nitarav b'dam kodshim acheirim yikreivu* — even if the blood of a *bechor* becomes mixed with other sacrificial bloods, those bloods may still be brought on the altar. The plural *hem* signals that each blood retains its identity and does not disappear into the mixture.

Rav Huna bar Yehuda assumes that the specific *chiddush* of this verse is that the location where *olah* blood is applied — namely, the lower wall — counts as an acceptable place for the *shirayim* of the blood mixed with it. The case, as Rav Huna construes it, would be: you have already completed the primary *olah* application, and now you have the *shirayim* of the *olah* mixed with the blood of a *bechor*, and the verse tells you that you may throw this mixture against the wall as you would the *olah*. *U'shma mina makom olah makom shirayim* — one should learn from this verse that the location of the *olah* application counts as an acceptable place for the spilling of the *shirayim*.

### Source Debate

The *gemara* immediately deflects this inference. *Lo* — no. The case in the verse is not about *shirayim* but about the initial blood of the *olah* getting mixed with the initial blood of a *bechor*. Both involve initial applications, and neither has yet had any blood placed. But this raises the question: if it is the initial blood of an *olah* mixed with the initial blood of a *bechor*, what exactly is the *chiddush*? After all, *olah* blood requires four applications (two corners, each striking two walls, yielding four surfaces), while *bechor* blood requires only one application. When these two bloods become mixed together, one faces a dilemma between *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to the required service — and *bal tigra* — the prohibition against subtracting from it. If one does four applications to honor the *olah*, that is too many for the *bechor*; if one does only one application for the *bechor*, that is too few for the *olah*. This strikes Tosafot as the natural *chiddush* of the verse — the *pasuk* is telling you that you are still permitted to choose one or the other and proceed, rather than simply pouring the blood out.

The *gemara*, however, does not invoke this reasoning. Instead it says: *u'mai kamashma lan? De'ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh* — the *chiddush* is that things which are brought on the altar (*olin*) do not cause *bitul* for one another. This is the principle that when different sacrificial bloods become mixed, no blood is considered cancelled or nullified by the other, even if one blood vastly outnumbers the other. The minority blood does not disappear. In contrast to ordinary laws of *bitul* in *kashrut* — where Rabbi Yehuda holds that *min b'mino* (like substance with like substance) can never be *batel*, and the *chachamim* generally permit *bitul* in a majority — the *chachamim* agree that in the realm of sacrificial blood, *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh*: the blood of a *bechor* mixed with *olah* blood in any ratio is still considered present in its entirety.

This principle explains why all the preceding analysis about bloods getting mixed was never troubled by the question of whether one blood was simply too small to count: in the world of *kodshim*, there is no such thing. Now the *gemara* asks: if this is the *chiddush* of *kodesh hem*, why is there a need for an alternate derivation? The *gemara* answers: *tannai hi* — it is a dispute among *tannaim*. Some derive *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh* from *kodesh hem*, some from *u'lakach midam hapar u'midam hase'ir* — the verse about Yom Kippur — and some from the repetition of the word *dam dam* in the *parashah*. We will return shortly to analyze why each of these sources is preferred by different authorities and why no single verse commands universal agreement.

## Wall vs. Base Debate

### Tosafot's Questions

Before continuing the *gemara*'s own resolution of the source question, it is worth pausing on the serious questions raised by Tosafot regarding the premise of this whole discussion. Tosafot asks: what does it even mean to say that *makom olah* is *makom shirayim* — that the location of the *olah* application is an acceptable location for the spilling of *shirayim*? The question is not simply about the vertical-versus-horizontal problem that Rav Yosef raised. There is an additional geographic problem: the application of *olah* blood and the spilling of *shirayim* are not done at the same location on the altar. Visualizing the altar, with the ramp ascending on the southern side: when you descend the ramp, the designated place to spill the *shirayim* is at the southwestern corner of the base — the first base you encounter. But when you throw the *olah* blood, you throw it at the two corners — northeast and southwest, or whichever pair is designated. The corner where you throw the *olah* blood is not necessarily at the same location as the southwestern base where the *shirayim* would go. So even setting aside the wall-versus-base question, the two locations may not actually coincide.

Tosafot offers three responses. The first — that we may be discussing the second throwing of blood — is not the straightforward reading. The other two are more substantive. One is that perhaps since the *Torah* does not specify precisely which base is required, there may be some latitude: even if the *shirayim* is technically designated for one particular base, other bases might still be acceptable. The other answer turns on the direction the *kohen* faces when descending the ramp: the rule that one spills the *shirayim* at the southwest is based on the fact that if the *kohen* turns right upon descending, the southwest base is the first one he encounters. But if he turns left, the southeast is the first base he encounters — and that corner corresponds to the location of one of the *olah* blood-throwing positions. So Tosafot suggests that in this mixed-blood scenario, the *kohen* should simply turn left upon descending the ramp, and the location of the *olah* throwing would then coincide with the first base, making both applications valid at the same spot.

Tosafot raises the second question separately: if the *gemara* says the *chiddush* of *kodesh hem* is not about the wall counting for *shirayim* but rather about *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh*, is that truly a *chiddush* that requires a verse? Tosafot phrases this sharply: *teima tuva kamashma lan de'ikreivu, lemar kide'it lei matana shtayim shehen arba, u'lemar matana achat af al gav de'avar al bal tigra u'val tosif* — what a great *chiddush* the verse tells us, that when they are mixed you bring them — according to one view doing two applications that count as four, and according to another doing one application even though that involves *bal tigra* and *bal tosif*. This seems to be the genuinely counterintuitive ruling that a verse would be needed to convey: that you are permitted to proceed despite the *bal tosif/bal tigra* dilemma, rather than simply pouring the blood out. Tosafot answers that perhaps the logic of *bitul* — treating the extra blood as if it were water — would allow you to do the applications without technically violating *bal tosif* and *bal tigra*, or that the principle of *shev v'al ta'aseh* (passive inaction) neutralizes the concern. But Tosafot himself acknowledges that the question is stronger than the answer — if the natural concern when bloods are mixed is that one should pour them out rather than create a *bal tosif/bal tigra* situation, the verse that permits you to proceed is indeed a major *chiddush*, one the *gemara* seems to pass over without sufficient explanation.

### Baraita Proof

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* that appears to provide direct evidence that the location of the *olah* application counts as valid for *shirayim*. The *braita* states: *natan lema'ala v'lo nimlach* — the mixture of *chatas* and *olah* blood was applied above without seeking guidance — *elu v'eilu modim sheyachzor v'yitein lematah* — both Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* agree that one should go ahead and now apply it below. Moreover, *eilu v'eilu olin lo* — both applications count; the lower application is valid both for the *chatas* and for the *olah*.

At first glance, this appears to be the clearest possible proof. The case as read is: the *chatas* and *olah* blood were mixed. The *kohen* applied some of the mixture at the top, fulfilling the primary *chatas* application. Now what remains is the *shirayim* of the *chatas* mixed with the *olah* blood. The *braita* says: go ahead and apply it at the bottom — and this is precisely the *olah* application method of throwing it against the lower wall. The *chachamim* agree that this is done *lechatchila* in this situation. And the conclusion is stated explicitly: both count. This seems to be direct proof that *makom olah makom shirayim* — the wall location used for the *olah* is also counted as valid for the discharge of *shirayim*.

The *gemara* says: *u'shma mina makom olah makom shirayim* — is this not a proof? And indeed, so it appears. But as the *gemara* is wont to do even after presenting a compelling proof, it proceeds to examine whether the *braita* can be read differently.

## Ein Olin Mevatlin…

### Yom Kippur Distinction

Returning to the question of the sources for *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh*, the *gemara* now explains why certain *tannaim* do not learn the principle from the Yom Kippur blood. The verse states: *u'lakach midam hapar u'midam hase'ir* — one takes from the blood of the ox and from the blood of the goat. The apparent *chiddush* is that even though the blood of the ox vastly outnumbers the blood of the goat, both are considered present in the mixture when applied on the *mizbeach hazahav*. However, some *tannaim* hold *ein me'arvin lekaranos* — that the two bloods are not actually mixed together but are applied separately, first the blood of the ox and then the blood of the goat. If the bloods were never combined, there is no scenario of mixture to generate a *chiddush* about *bitul*, and thus no proof for *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh* can be drawn from this verse.

### Three Sources Analyzed

This brings us to a full accounting of why each of the three candidate sources was not universally accepted. The three possible sources are: (1) *u'lakach midam hapar u'midam hase'ir* from Yom Kippur, (2) *kodesh hem* from the verse about the *bechor*, and (3) the repetition of *dam dam* in the *parashah*.

For *dam dam*: *dam dam lo mashma lehu* — the mere repetition of the word *dam* does not carry sufficient textual weight to generate this *halacha*. The *Torah* says to throw the blood on the altar, and it is unremarkable that the word *dam* appears more than once; it is not obviously an extra word carrying independent meaning.

The Yom Kippur source, as just explained, is rejected by those who hold that the two bloods are applied separately and never actually mixed.

### Kodesh Hem Limited

As for *kodesh hem* — why do some *tannaim* not derive *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh* from this verse? The *gemara* answers: *kasavri kodesh hem lo yalpi* — because they derive a different *halacha* from *kodesh hem*. *Hem krevim v'ein tmuratam kreva* — the verse "they are holy" teaches specifically about the *bechor*: the *bechorim* themselves are brought as sacrifices, but their *tmurah* — the animal substituted for them — cannot be brought as a sacrifice. A *bechor* is not sanctified through an owner's verbal declaration; it is sacred by virtue of birth. Therefore, when someone attempts to make a *tmurah* for a *bechor*, unlike with other sanctified animals whose *tmurah* can be brought, the *tmurah* of a *bechor* is sacred in the sense that it cannot be redeemed, but it cannot actually be offered. This is the *halacha* taught by *kodesh hem*, according to those *tannaim* — it has nothing to do with the mixing of bloods.

Those who do derive *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh* from *kodesh hem* must obtain the *tmurah* ruling from elsewhere: *nafka lei mishor im seh lashem hu* — "whether it is an ox or a sheep, it is for God" — from this phrase one learns that the *bechor* itself is brought (*hu karev*), while its *tmurah* is not (*v'ein tmuratam kreva*). With that *halacha* derived from a separate source, *kodesh hem* is freed to teach the law of blood mixture and *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh*. The unresolved question of whether the lower wall counts as a valid location for *shirayim* remains open, as the *braita* proofs examined thus far have not yielded a definitive ruling.

## Wall vs. Base Debate…

### Two Chatasim Theory

The *gemara* now provides the promised reinterpretation of the *braita*. When Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef arrived from Eretz Yisrael — and recall that Rabbi Yochanan, who disputes whether the wall counts for *shirayim*, was from Eretz Yisrael — he transmitted the following: *hachi amri b'ma'arava, hacha b'mai askinan, kegon de'eirev chatas chitzona b'shirayim* — here is what they say in the west about what the *braita* is actually discussing. The *braita* is not dealing with a mixture of *chatas* and *olah* blood. Rather, it concerns a mixture of two types of *chatas* blood: the blood of an outer *chatas* (*chatas chitzona*) and the *shirayim* of an inner *chatas* (*chatas pnimit*).

The inner *chatas* — such as the ox of Yom Kippur or the *par he'elem davar shel tzibur* — has its primary applications performed inside on the golden incense altar. The *shirayim* of the inner *chatas* is to be spilled at the base of the outer altar. Now, if the *shirayim* of the inner *chatas* becomes mixed with the blood of an outer *chatas*, one cannot put it at the top, since the *shirayim* does not belong there. If the *kohen* nonetheless applied some of the mixture at the top, then what remains in the mixture is *shirayim* and *shirayim* — the remnant of the outer *chatas* (now that its primary application was done above) and the *shirayim* of the inner *chatas*. In that case, the *braita* says: go ahead and spill the remainder at the base below, and both count. This is not about throwing it on the wall as one does for an *olah*; it is about spilling it directly at the base, which is exactly where *shirayim* belongs. There is no proof here at all that the wall counts for *shirayim*.

Abaye then presses: *v'neima mar kegon de'eirev b'shirayim d'shirayim* — why must the reinterpretation involve *shirayim* of an inner *chatas* mixed with an outer *chatas* blood? Why not simply say it is the *shirayim* of an outer *chatas* mixed with the initial blood of another outer *chatas*?

The *gemara* responds: *dilma ha kamashma lan afilu man d'amar shirayim me'akvin, chasrei ein me'akvin* — perhaps the *braita* is specifically designed to teach a *chiddush* regarding inner *chatasim*. There is a position that the spilling of the *shirayim* is *me'akev* — an indispensable step — for an inner *chatas*. For ordinary outer sacrifices, the *shirayim* is never *me'akev*; the sacrifice is fully valid whether or not the *shirayim* is properly discharged. The one possible exception is the inner *chatas*, where some hold the *shirayim* is required for full validity. By presenting the scenario of inner *chatas shirayim* mixed with outer *chatas* blood, the *braita* teaches a double *chiddush*: first, that even when the *shirayim* is *me'akev* (as it may be for the inner *chatas*), the arrangement of spilling it at the base of the outer altar satisfies that requirement; and second, that even if some of the *shirayim* was already diverted — applied mistakenly at the top — whatever remains can still validate the obligation. If the *braita* had simply described outer *chatas shirayim* mixed with outer *chatas* initial blood, none of these *chiddushim* would be in play, since outer *shirayim* is never *me'akev* and the question of sufficiency would not arise.

### Unresolved Status

The result of this analysis is that the *gemara* has successfully shown that the *braita* need not be read as dealing with *chatas* and *olah* blood at all, and therefore need not be read as affirming that the wall is a valid location for *shirayim*. The *braita* can be entirely reinterpreted as involving two categories of *chatas* blood — inner and outer — in which case the conclusion "spill it at the base" is simply about spilling it at the base, with no wall application implied. The question of whether the lower wall of the altar counts as an acceptable location for the discharge of *shirayim* thus remains unresolved.

## Baraita Reconciliation

### Limited Validation

Rav Tosfaa addresses Ravina with a point connecting the *braita* discussion back to the analysis of the previous daf. He notes: the attempted proof from the *braita* — that if the *kohen* already applied the mixture above, both should go ahead and apply it below — was being offered as evidence that the lower wall counts for *shirayim*. But Rav Tosfaa recalls that in the earlier analysis, they had established something more limited: when the mixture of *chatas* and *olah* blood is applied below, only the *chatas* component is validated, not the *olah*. The *olah* is not rendered valid by this placement. So even on the straightforward reading of the *braita* — that it concerns *chatas* and *olah* blood — there would be no proof about *makom olah* being *makom shirayim*, because the *olah* was not being validated by the wall application in the first place.

### Ein Bila Context

Rav Tosfaa clarifies that this earlier limitation — that only the *chatas* was being validated, not the *olah* — was itself contingent on the position of *ein bila*. It was when one entertained the hypothesis that equal distribution of blood through a mixture cannot be assumed, requiring a very forced reading of the *braita* with specific quantities (six units of *chatas*, four of *olah*, and so forth), that the conclusion was: only the *chatas* is being made valid, not the *olah*.

### Final Resolution

Ultimately, the *gemara* did not retain the *ein bila* explanation. The final resolution was *maskana bekosot pligi* — in the end, the debate is about the cups, not about whether the bloods disperse within a single vessel. Even those who hold *ein bila* explain these cases by saying the collection bowls became confused, so that when blood is thrown, it is thrown from each bowl — guaranteeing that both bloods are applied in both locations. Once the *braita* is read on this basis, both the *chatas* and the *olah* are validated by the application below. And if that is the case — if the *chachamim* permit throwing the mixture against the lower wall and thereby validate both the *olah* and the *chatas shirayim* — then it would seem to follow as a proof that the wall indeed counts for *shirayim*. The *gemara* ultimately retreats from this by reinterpreting the *braita* as concerning two *chatasim* rather than *chatas* and *olah*, and the question of whether the wall counts remains *lo pesika* — not definitively resolved.

## Inner and Outer

### Spill When Mixed

The *gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*, which introduces a different kind of blood mixture: *hanitnin bifnim shenisarvu benitnin bachutz yishpechu la'amah* — blood designated for the inner altar (inside the *heichal*) that became mixed with blood designated for the outer altar should be poured out into the drain. The two types of blood are so fundamentally different in their required locations that no application combining them is sanctioned. The *gemara* will subsequently ask why Rabbi Eliezer — who throughout the prior *mishnayos* was prepared to treat the wrong blood as water and permit *lechatchila* application — does not similarly assert here that one may simply proceed, viewing the mismatched blood as water.

### Outer Then Inner

The *mishnah* addresses the case where a *kohen* did not seek guidance and proceeded anyway. *Natan bachutz v'chazar v'natan bifnim* — if one first applied the mixture to the outer altar and then brought it to apply on the inner altar, the ruling is *kasher bediavad*. The sacrifice is valid after the fact. The outer sacrifices were validated when the blood was thrown on the outer altar; the subsequent inner application, while it involved bringing outer blood into the *heichal*, did not retroactively invalidate what had already been rendered valid.

### Inner First Debate

The reverse case — *bifnim v'chazar v'natan bachutz*, bringing the blood first to the inner altar and then to the outer — generates a dispute. *Rabbi Akiva posel v'chachamim machshirim* — Rabbi Akiva rules it invalid while the *chachamim* rule it valid. The question turns on what it means when the blood of an outer sacrifice is brought inside.

### Rabbi Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva's position derives from the verse: *v'chol chatat asher yuva midama el ohel mo'ed l'chaper bakodesh lo te'achel ba'esh tisaref* — any sin offering whose blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting to make atonement in the holy place shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire. This verse is understood on multiple levels. In its most basic sense, it refers to the category of *chatasim pnimiyos* — inner sin offerings like those of Yom Kippur — whose blood is designed to be brought inside, and whose meat is therefore burned rather than eaten. But Rabbi Akiva reads the verse more expansively: any blood whatsoever that is brought inside the *heichal* for the purpose of atonement, even the blood of an outer sacrifice, invalidates that sacrifice if its status had not yet been finalized. The critical factor is timing: if the outer sacrifice's blood had already been validly applied to the outer altar — as in the case of outer-first-then-inner — then when the blood is subsequently brought inside, the sacrifice is already *kasher* and cannot be retroactively invalidated. But if the blood is brought inside *first*, before any valid application has occurred, then the sacrifice is invalidated by the inner entry.

This logic applies not only to *chatas* blood but, according to Rabbi Akiva, to any sacrificial blood — even *olah* and *shelamim*. The verse does not limit the invalidation to *chatas*; the category of blood that matters is whether it was brought inside while the sacrifice's validity remained pending.

### Chachamim Limit

The *chachamim* disagree. *V'chachamim omrim chatat bilvad* — only *chatas* blood, when brought inside while still pending, causes the sacrifice to become invalid. Other bloods — *olah*, *shelamim*, and the like — are not governed by this rule. The verse is read as specifically addressing the *chatas* and its inner counterpart, not as a general principle applicable to all sacrificial blood.

### Rabbi Eliezer Adds

Rabbi Eliezer occupies an intermediate position: *af asham* — the *asham* (guilt offering) is added to the *chatas* as a type of blood whose inner entry invalidates. He derives this from the principle *kechatas ke'asham* — as the *chatas* so the *asham* — a parallel that he had also invoked earlier in the tractate regarding the laws of *shelo lishmah*. Accordingly, if *asham* blood is brought inside while the sacrifice's status is still unresolved, the *asham* too is invalidated. The spectrum of opinion is thus: Rabbi Akiva — all bloods; *chachamim* — only *chatas*; Rabbi Eliezer — *chatas* and *asham*.

## Inner/Outer Sequence

### Inner Before Outer

The *gemara* now asks: given Rabbi Eliezer's general position that one may proceed with a mixed blood offering by treating the wrong component as water, why does he not apply that reasoning here? Why does he not say that *lechatchila* one should go ahead and apply the mixture in both locations, treating the mismatched blood as water in each case? The *gemara* notes a structural constraint: *keshem shemitzvah lehaktim elyonim letachtonim kach mitzvah lehaktim pnim lechutz* — just as it is a *mitzvah* to perform the upper application before the lower one (i.e., the *chatas* before the *olah*, since the *chatas* provides atonement that prepares the person for the subsequent *olah*), so too it is a *mitzvah* to perform the inner application before the outer one. Inner sacrifices take precedence over outer ones in the order of service. This means that if one has a mixture of inner and outer blood, one must begin inside — and only then proceed outside.

### Invalidation Problem

This creates the difficulty for Rabbi Eliezer. In theory, one might suggest: bring the mixture inside first to honor the inner sacrifice, then bring it outside. But Rabbi Eliezer holds that *chatas* and *asham* blood, when brought inside while the outer sacrifice remains unvalidated, invalidates that outer sacrifice. If the mixture contains *chatas* or *asham* blood, the act of entering the *heichal* with it first — as required by the principle of inner before outer — would necessarily invalidate the outer sacrifice before it has had a chance to be validated. One cannot escape this by going outside first, because that would violate the required ordering. So Rabbi Eliezer finds himself in an inescapable bind.

### Uncertainty Rule

Why cannot Rabbi Eliezer simply permit the sequence for the cases where it would work — namely, when the outer sacrifice mixed in is an *olah* or *shelamim* rather than a *chatas* or *asham*? After all, Rabbi Eliezer does not hold, unlike Rabbi Akiva, that *all* blood brought inside invalidates. If it is *olah* blood mixed with inner *chatas* blood, one could bring it inside first (validating the inner *chatas*), then outside (validating the *olah*), and the *olah* would not be invalidated by the inner entry since Rabbi Eliezer only invalidates *chatas* and *asham*, not *olah*. The *gemara* answers: *lo pesika lei* — it is not categorical for Rabbi Eliezer. Since there exist scenarios — namely, *chatas* and *asham* mixtures — where going inside first would definitely invalidate the outer sacrifice, Rabbi Eliezer cannot issue a blanket ruling permitting the inner-then-outer sequence. He cannot say as a general rule that one may proceed *lechatchila*, because the validity of that instruction depends on the precise composition of the mixture, which is unknown. This uncertainty prevents him from offering categorical permission, and therefore the default ruling — spill it out — stands even for Rabbi Eliezer. The *gemara* leaves the sugya here, to continue the following day with the further analysis of Rabbi Akiva's position and the debate between the *chachamim* about which blood triggers invalidation when brought into the *heichal*.
