# Pesachim 120 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Afikoman Concept

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַפֶּסַח אֲפִיקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One does not conclude after the Paschal lamb with an *afikoman*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי אֲפִיקוֹמָן? אָמַר רַב: שֶׁלֹּא יֵעָקְרוּ מֵחֲבוּרָה לַחֲבוּרָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: כְּגוֹן אוֹרְדִּילָאֵי לִי וְגוֹזָלַיָּיא לְאַבָּא. וְרַב חֲנִינָא בַּר שֵׁילָא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן (אָמַר) [אָמְרוּ]: כְּגוֹן תְּמָרִים קְלָיוֹת וֶאֱגוֹזִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***afikoman*? Rav said:** It means **that** a member of a group that ate the Paschal lamb together should **not leave** that **group to** join another **group.** **And Shmuel said:** It means that one may not eat dessert after the meal, **like mushrooms** [*urdila'ei*] **for me, and chicks for Abba,** Rav. It was customary for them to eat delicacies after the meal. **And Rav Ḥanina bar Sheila and Rabbi Yoḥanan** say: *Afikoman* refers to foods **such as dates, roasted grains, and nuts,** which are eaten during the meal.

The daf opens at the bottom of 119b, where the *mishnah* had stated: *ein maftirin achar hapesach afikoman* — one does not conclude after the *pesach* with an *afikoman*. It is striking that the final *mishnayot* of the tractate focus on the eating of the *korban pesach* rather than on *matzah* and *maror*, which go entirely unaddressed. This raises the broader question of how much this *perek* assumes *zman habayit* — the period when the Temple stood — versus *bizman hazeh*.

The term *afikoman* itself is a Greek word, and Rav's interpretation follows that etymology: the prohibition is against going to other *chavurot* — other groups' meals — in the manner of a Greco-Roman afterparty, with its carousing and inappropriate revelry. In the context of the *korban pesach*, the practical concern is that one might come to eat the *pesach* in two separate locations.

Shmuel's interpretation — the one with which we are all familiar — is that *afikoman* refers to eating *anything* after the *pesach*, on the presumption that doing so would displace the taste of the *korban pesach* from one's mouth. Notably, the *Gemara* has not yet stated this rationale explicitly; it is the operative concern behind Shmuel's position.

### Shmuel: No Food After Ma…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר מַצָּה אֲפִיקוֹמָן. תְּנַן: אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַפֶּסַח אֲפִיקוֹמָן. אַחַר הַפֶּסַח הוּא דְּלָא, אֲבָל לְאַחַר מַצָּה — מַפְטִירִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said** an additional halakha: Nowadays, when we have no Paschal lamb, **one does not conclude after *matza* with an *afikoman*.** The Gemara asks: **We learned** in the mishna that **one does not conclude after the Paschal lamb with an *afikoman*.** The Gemara infers from the mishna: It is **after the Paschal lamb that** one may **not** conclude with an *afikoman*; **however, after *matza* one** may **conclude** with an *afikoman*. This statement of the mishna apparently contradicts Shmuel's ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא אַחַר מַצָּה — דְּלָא נְפִישׁ טַעְמַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח, דִּנְפִישׁ טַעְמֵיהּ וְלָא מָצֵי עַבּוֹרֵיהּ — לֵית לַן בַּהּ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: That is an incorrect inference, as the mishna is stated in the style of: Needless to say. The mishna should be understood as follows: Needless to say that one may not conclude with an *afikoman* **after** eating ***matza*, as the taste** of *matza* **is slight.** If one eats anything else afterward, the taste of the *matza* will dissipate. **However, after the Paschal lamb, which has a strong taste that is not easily removed,** one might think that **we have no** problem **with it.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that it is prohibited to conclude with an *afikoman* after the Paschal lamb as well.

Following Shmuel's approach, the *Gemara* cites: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — not only after the *pesach* is there a prohibition, but even nowadays, after *matzah*, one may not eat anything. This ruling transforms the practical structure of the *Seder*: if nothing may follow the *matzah*, then the *matzah* must come at the very end of the meal. The familiar *afikoman* — the piece of *matzah* eaten at the conclusion of the *Seder* — exists precisely to ensure this: we eat it last so that nothing comes after it, thereby fulfilling the spirit of *ein maftirin achar hapesach afikoman* as applied to *matzah bizman hazeh*.

This is an important point: the eating of a final *kezayit* of *matzah* at the end of the meal is not a proactive institution celebrating some special importance of that last piece. It is, rather, the necessary consequence of the ruling that nothing may follow the *matzah*. Once you must have *matzah* last, the final piece becomes significant by default. The question of which *matzah* — first or last — bears the primary weight of the *mitzvah* will be addressed shortly.

### Matzah vs. Pesach Taste

The *Gemara* attempts to test Shmuel's position against the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* says *ein maftirin achar hapesach afikoman*, implying by its silence that after *matzah* there is no such problem. The *Gemara* deflects this with the principle of *lo mibaya ka'amar* — the *mishnah* is speaking in the form of "it goes without saying." The logic runs as follows: *lo mibaya achar matzah delo nafish tamayhu* — it goes without saying that after *matzah*, whose taste is weak and insubstantial, one should not eat anything afterwards; *aval achar pesach de-nafish ta'amei velo matzi avurei lei* — but after the *pesach*, which has a powerful taste that cannot easily be washed away, one might have thought there is no need to worry since the taste will linger on its own. The *mishnah* therefore teaches that even after the *pesach*, one may not eat.

This reading preserves Shmuel's position: the *mishnah* is not excluding *matzah* from the prohibition, but rather emphasizing that the prohibition extends even to the stronger-tasting *pesach*. The *mishnah* is thus operating in a *zman habayit* framework and innovating that even by *pesach* it is a concern — but of course the same concern applies *a fortiori* to *matzah bizman hazeh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הַסּוּפְגָּנִין וְהַדּוּבְשָׁנִין וְהָאִיסְקְרִיטִין, אָדָם מְמַלֵּא כְּרֵיסוֹ מֵהֶן, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת מַצָּה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה אִין, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection:** With regard to unleavened **sponge cakes, cakes fried in oil and honey, and honey cakes, a person** may **fill his stomach with them** on Passover night, **provided that he eats an olive-bulk** of ***matza* after** consuming them. The Gemara infers from here that if he eats *matza* **after** those cakes, **yes,** this is permitted; however, if one eats *matza* **before** these other foods, **no,** this is not an acceptable practice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה — דְּקָאָכֵיל לְתֵיאָבוֹן, אֲבָל בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְמֵיכַל אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה — אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: As explained above, the *Tosefta* is stated in the style of: Needless to say. Needless to say, one fulfills his obligation if he eats *matza* **before** other foods, **as he eats it with an appetite. However,** if he eats *matza* **after** eating other foods, **when he** might **come to eat it** in the manner of **an excessive eating, you** might **say** that one does **not** fulfill his obligation if he eats *matza* after all those other foods. Therefore, the *Tosefta* **teaches us** that one may eat *matza* even after consuming those foods.

Mar Zutra, however, reports a different version of the tradition — *Amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — in which Shmuel's ruling is precisely the opposite: *maftirin achar matzah afikoman*, meaning it is only a problem with the *korban pesach* and not with *matzah* nowadays. With this reversal, all the proofs and challenges trade places. The *mishnah* now seems to support this position — *achar hapesach hu de-lo*, after the *pesach* is the prohibition, *aval achar matzah maftirin* — and the *Gemara* counters again with *lo mibaya ka'amar*, though now in the reverse direction: it goes without saying you cannot eat after *matzah* since its taste is so weak, and the *mishnah* innovates regarding *pesach* that even its strong taste is a problem.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי: אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַמַּצָּה אֲפִיקוֹמָן. נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ, אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַפֶּסַח אֲפִיקוֹמָן: אַחַר הַפֶּסַח דְּלָא, אֲבָל אַחַר מַצָּה — מַפְטִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **This is** how **Mar Zutra taught** this discussion: **Rav Yosef said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: One** may **conclude after the *matza* with an *afikoman*.** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the mishna **supports his** opinion: **One does not conclude after the Paschal lamb with an *afikoman*.** The Gemara infers: It is **after the Paschal lamb that** one may **not** conclude with an *afikoman*; **however, after *matza* one** may **conclude** with an *afikoman*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא אַחַר מַצָּה — דְּלָא נְפִישׁ טַעְמֵיהּ, אֲבָל לְאַחַר פֶּסַח — אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: The mishna is stated in the style of: Needless to say. Needless to say, one may not conclude with an *afikoman* **after** eating ***matza*, as the taste** of *matza* **is slight; however, after the Paschal lamb,** one might **say** that this prohibition does **not** apply. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that it is prohibited to conclude with an *afikoman* after the Paschal lamb as well.

### Baraita: Matzah at End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַסּוּפְגָּנִין וְהַדּוּבְשָׁנִין וְהָאִיסְקְרִיטִין אָדָם מְמַלֵּא כְּרֵיסוֹ מֵהֶן וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל (אֲכִילַת) כְּזַיִת מַצָּה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה אִין, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection:** With regard to unleavened **sponge cakes, cakes fried in oil and honey, and honey cakes, a person** may **fill his stomach with them** on Passover night, **provided that he eats an olive-bulk** of ***matza* after** consuming them. The Gemara infers from here that if he eats *matza* **after** those cakes, **yes,** this is permitted; however, if one eats *matza* **before** these other foods, **no,** this is not an acceptable practice.

The *Gemara* adduces a *baraita* to press the question further. A *baraita* states that one may eat one's fill of *sofganin*, *duvshunin*, and *iskartin* — various types of *mezonos* — on the night of *Pesach*, *uvilvad she-yochal kezayit matzah ba-achrona* — provided that the *kezayit* of *matzah* is eaten last. The *baraita* further specifies: *ba-achrona in, barishona lo* — at the end, yes; at the beginning, no.

This appears to support Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel's position that *matzah* must come at the end. However, the *Gemara* again deploys *lo mibaya ka'amar*: naturally it is permitted to eat *matzah* at the beginning when one has an appetite — *leteiavon* — it is even preferable that way. The *baraita* teaches us only that eating it at the end is also acceptable, despite the concern that by that point one may be overly satiated, rendering the eating *achila gassa*. The *baraita*, on this reading, establishes that eating *matzah* at the end is *permitted*, not that it is *required*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה — דְּקָאָכֵיל לְתֵיאָבוֹן, אֲבָל בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה דְּאָתֵי לְמֵיכְלַהּ אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה — אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: As explained above, the *Tosefta* is stated in the style of: Needless to say. Needless to say, one fulfills his obligation if he eats *matza* **before** other foods, **as he eats it with an appetite. However,** if he eats *matza* **after** eating other foods, **when he** might **come to eat it** in the manner of **an excessive eating, you** might **say** that one does **not** fulfill his obligation if he eats *matza* after all those other foods. Therefore, the *Tosefta* **teaches us** that one may eat *matza* even after consuming those foods.

The result is that neither version of Shmuel's teaching can be decisively confirmed or refuted from these *mishnayot* and *braitot*.

There is a deeper question lurking here: why should the concern about preserving the taste in one's mouth apply to *matzah* at all? The *Gemara* elsewhere was only concerned with the taste of *maror*, not *matzah* — indeed, swallowing *matzah* whole satisfies the *mitzvah*. One might therefore argue that the obligation to end with *matzah* is uniquely a *pesach*-related concern, grounded in the profound symbolism of the *korban pesach* in a way that does not transfer to *matzah*.

*Tosafot* raises this very question — *ve-im tomar achar pesach mai ta'ama ein maftirin* — why would it be specifically a concern after *pesach*, given that its taste is *stronger* than *matzah's*? *Tosafot* then cites the *Yerushalmi*, which offers a different rationale altogether: the *pesach* is eaten *al hasova* — in a state of satiation — as a safeguard against the prohibition of *sheviras atzem*. If one is ravenous, one might chew the bones and extract the marrow, violating the prohibition of *ve-etzem lo tishberu bo*. Eating the *pesach* at the end, when satiated, guards against this. *Tosafot* further notes that by *minchat* there is a related idea of eating *al hasova*, derived not from the concern about bone-breaking but from the notion that one should not leave the table of one's Master still hungry. Applied to *pesach*, there is also the consideration that finishing the *korban* while still craving more food would be inappropriate.

This gives us multiple convergent reasons for eating the *pesach* at the end of the meal: not eating it when ravenous, so as not to break bones; not finishing and feeling unsatisfied; and maintaining the taste. It is precisely because these reasons are so specifically tied to the *korban pesach* that their extension to *matzah* is not self-evident — and that is what the *Gemara's* debate reflects.

## Two Matzah Roles

### Rashbam: Last Kezayit

The *Rashbam* takes a unified view: the final *kezayit* of *matzah* is the essential *matzah mitzvah*. Since this last piece is eaten at the end — mirroring the *korban pesach*, which was also eaten at the end — it stands as a *zecher* for the *matzah* eaten together with the *pesach*, *ne'echelet im ha-pesach*. On this view, the final *kezayit* performs double duty: it is both the commemoration of *matzah al ha-pesach* and the fulfillment of the independent *mitzvat achilat matzah*.

This raises an immediate question: if the *mitzvah* is fulfilled with the final piece, why do we recite *al achilat matzah* at the beginning of the meal? The *Rashbam* addresses this by analogy to a discussion earlier in the tractate about *maror*, where a *bracha* is recited in advance of the actual *mitzvah* act. He contends that since one begins eating *matzah* at the start of the meal, the *bracha* is recited then — but the essential fulfillment occurs with the closing *kezayit*.

### Tosafot: Divided Roles

*Tosafot* proposes a cleaner and more elegant structure by assigning distinct roles to each *kezayit*. The first *kezayit* of *matzah* fulfills the independent *mitzvah* of *achilat matzah* — the *de'oraita* obligation that applies *bizman hazeh*. The final *kezayit* — what we call the *afikoman* — represents the *matzah* eaten together with the *pesach*: a *zecher l'pesach*, consumed at the end of the meal just as the *korban* was eaten at the end, with its taste lingering in the mouth.

On this reading, each piece has its own identity. The *al achilat matzah* is recited at the beginning because the *mitzvah* of *achilat matzah* is being fulfilled there and then. The final piece is separately meaningful as a symbolic re-enactment of the Temple *seder*. One might ask: if the final *kezayit* is *zecher l'pesach*, should *korech* not be eaten at the end, since that too commemorates eating *matzah* with the *pesach*? The practical structure of the *Seder* as we observe it renders this question somewhat academic, but it is worth noting.

### Seder Matzah Structure

In practice, the *Seder* begins with three *matzot*. The reason for three is that since one will be broken — and we want *lechem mishneh* — we need two whole ones; the third provides the one that will be broken. The middle *matzah* is broken because it represents *lechem oni*, the bread of affliction, over which the *Haggadah* is recited, since the *midrash* connects *oni* to *oneh* — responding, answering — and one answers over a broken piece.

The half that is set aside — classically, the larger half — becomes the *afikoman*. The *Gemara* itself never specifies what to do with this piece; all of this detail is developed by the *Rishonim*. At the point of eating, the *bracha* of *Hamotzi lechem min ha-aretz* is recited over the whole *matzah*, and the *al achilat matzah* is recited over the broken piece. A *kezayit* is taken from each. This arrangement — assigning *Hamotzi* to the whole and *al achilat matzah* to the broken — may seem unusual, since *birkat hanehenin* is not normally parceled out between pieces, but it functions as a way of giving each element a distinct role.

*Korech* uses the third *matzah*, the remaining whole one, giving it a role as well. The set-aside half then fulfills the final *kezayit* — the *afikoman* — eaten after the meal. In this way every piece of *matzah* finds its place in the *Seder* structure.

Regarding the practice of eating two *kezayitim* for the *afikoman*: the common reason given in *Seder* guides is that since the *afikoman* may fulfill the *de'oraita* obligation, some hold one should double the measure to satisfy the *shiur* of the *Chazon Ish*. It does not appear to reflect an independent *din* of *shnei kezayitim*.

## Matzah Independence

### Rava: Matzah De'Oraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַצָּה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּמָרוֹר דְּרַבָּנַן. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מָרוֹר, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים״, בִּזְמַן דְּאִיכָּא פֶּסַח — יֵשׁ מָרוֹר, וּבִזְמַן דְּלֵיכָּא פֶּסַח — לֵיכָּא מָרוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The mitzva of ***matza* nowadays,** even after the destruction of the Temple, applies by **Torah** law; **but** the mitzva to eat **bitter herbs** applies **by rabbinic** law. The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** the mitzva of **bitter herbs different from** *matza*? **As it is written,** with regard to the Paschal lamb: "They shall eat it **with *matzot* and bitter herbs"** (Numbers 9:11), from which it is derived: **When there is** an obligation to eat **the Paschal lamb, there is** likewise a mitzva to eat **bitter herbs; and when there is no** obligation to eat **the Paschal lamb, there is** also **no** mitzva to eat **bitter herbs.**

*Rava* states plainly: *matzah bizman hazeh de'oraita* — the obligation to eat *matzah* on the night of *Pesach* is biblical, even in the absence of the *korban pesach*. It is not merely a rabbinic enactment; it is a full-fledged Torah obligation that stands independently.

### Maror Rabbinic Status

By contrast, *maror* is *derabbanan* in our time. The *Gemara* explains: *d'chtiv al matzot u-merorim yochluhu* — the Torah states that the *pesach* is eaten with *matzah* and *maror*. The *maror* is therefore an accompaniment to the *pesach*: *bizman d'ika pesach yesh maror, bizman d'leika pesach leika maror* — when there is a *pesach* there is *maror*, when there is no *pesach* there is no *maror*. Since we no longer bring the *korban pesach*, the obligation of *maror* reverts to rabbinic standing.

One might think the same logic applies to *matzah*: it too is mentioned in the verse as an accompaniment to the *pesach*. The *Gemara* answers that the Torah *returns* to *matzah* — *matzah mahadeir hadei bei kra* — and establishes an independent obligation.

### Torah Source for Matzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַצָּה נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב: ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים״! מַצָּה מִיהְדָּר הָדַר בֵּיהּ קְרָא: ״בָּעֶרֶב תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת״. וְרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if so, the same reasoning should apply to ***matza* as well,** as **it is written: "With *matzot* and bitter herbs."** The mitzva of *matza* should also depend on the obligation of the Paschal lamb. The Gemara rejects this contention: **The verse repeats** the obligation to eat ***matza*,** as it states: "In the first month, on the fourteenth day of the month **in the evening, you shall eat *matzot*"** (Exodus 12:18). This verse establishes a separate obligation to eat *matza*, unrelated to the Paschal lamb. **And Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** Nowadays, **both this,** the mitzva to eat *matza*, **and that,** the mitzva to eat bitter herbs, apply **by rabbinic** law, as the Torah obligation to eat these foods is in effect only when the Paschal lamb is sacrificed.

The *Gemara* points to the verse: *ba-erev tochlu matzot* — in the evening you shall eat *matzot*. If one asks any serious student of *Gemara* for the Torah source of the obligation to eat *matzah* on *Seder* night, this is the verse they will cite. The matter is, however, subtler than it first appears. The full verse reads: *barishon b'arba'ah asar yom la-chodesh ba-erev tochlu matzot ad yom ha-echad ve-esrim la-chodesh ba-erev* — from the fourteenth day of the first month in the evening you shall eat *matzot* until the twenty-first day of the month in the evening. This is a verse about all seven days.

What the *Gemara* effectively does is isolate the opening clause — *ba-erev tochlu matzot*, "in the evening you shall eat *matzot*" — treating the beginning of *Pesach* as establishing an independent, specific obligation for the first night, while severing it from the remainder of the verse. The Torah, in other words, was never legislating a distinct night-one obligation; it was describing the entire seven-day obligation. But the hermeneutical move of the *Gemara* reads into the opening words a *de'oraita* requirement unique to the first night, giving *matzah* its independent standing *bizman hazeh*.

### Rava bar Yitzchak: All R…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכְתִיב: ״בָּעֶרֶב תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת״! הָהִיא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְטָמֵא וְשֶׁהָיָה בְּדֶרֶךְ רְחוֹקָה, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא כֵּיוָן דְּפֶסַח לָא אָכְלִי — מַצָּה וּמָרוֹר נָמֵי לָא נֵיכוֹל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it written: "In the evening, you shall eat *matzot*"?** The Gemara answers: Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov **needs that** verse for the following *halakha*: When the Temple was standing, one who was **ritually impure or** one **who was on a distant road** was nonetheless obligated to eat *matza*. **As it could enter your mind to say** that **since** these two categories of people **do not eat** the Paschal lamb on **the** first *Pesaḥ*, **they also do not eat *matza* and bitter herbs.** According to Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov, this verse **teaches us** that even one who was ritually impure and one who was on a distant road are obligated to eat *matza* and bitter herbs, as these mitzvot do not depend on one's eligibility to sacrifice the Paschal lamb on the first *Pesaḥ*.

*Rava bar Yitzchak* takes a strikingly different position: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh derabbanan* — both *matzah* and *maror* are rabbinic in our time, and neither carries a biblical obligation. On this view, the verse *ba-erev tochlu matzot* is not establishing an independent obligation for *Seder* night at all. Rather, the *Gemara* responds to him, it speaks to the case of one who is *tamei* or on a distant road — *tamei ve-shehayah be-derech rechokah* — who is exempted from bringing the *korban pesach*. The verse teaches that even such a person must still eat *matzah* on that night, lest one think: since he is not eating the *pesach*, he need not eat *matzah* and *maror* either. The verse therefore addresses a *zman habayit* scenario and does not create an independent *bizman hazeh* obligation at all.

This is a striking position, because it implies that *matzah* exists as an obligation only when there is a *korban pesach* — yet independent of actually eating one. The person who cannot bring the *korban* because of *tumah* or distance is nonetheless obligated to eat *matzah* and *maror* at the time when the *korban* is brought. In our era, when there is no *korban* at all, neither *matzah* nor *maror* carries biblical force. According to *Rava bar Yitzchak*, there is no *de'oraita mitzvah* on *leil Seder*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ: טָמֵא וְשֶׁהָיָה בְּדֶרֶךְ רְחוֹקָה לָא צְרִיךְ קָרָא, דְּלָא גָּרְעִי מֵעָרֵל וּבֶן נֵכָר. דְּתַנְיָא: ״כׇּל עָרֵל לֹא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״ — ״בּוֹ״ אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל בְּמַצָּה וּמָרוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rava,** who maintains that it is a mitzva from the Torah to eat *matza* nowadays, how could he respond to that interpretation of the verse? Rava could have **said to you:** I do **not require** a special **verse** to teach that **a ritually impure person and a person who was on a distant road** are obligated to eat *matza*. These people are obligated because they **are no worse than an uncircumcised** man **or an alien,** i.e., one who does not observe the *mitzvot*, who are obligated to eat *matza* despite the fact that they do not sacrifice the Paschal lamb. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **"But no uncircumcised man shall eat from it"** (Exodus 12:48). **"From it"** indicates that he may **not eat from** the Paschal lamb; **however, he does eat *matza* and bitter herbs.** The same is true for anyone else who is prevented from eating the Paschal lamb.

*Rava*, by contrast, responds that no additional verse is needed to teach the case of *tamei ve-derech rechokah*: *d'lo gra'ei me-areil u-ven neichar* — they are no worse than an *areil* (an uncircumcised person) or a *ben neichar* (an apostate), who likewise cannot eat the *korban pesach* yet are still obligated in *matzah* and *maror*, as the *baraita* teaches: *bo eino ochel aval ochel be-matzah u-maror*. The verse therefore must be doing something else entirely — establishing a biblical obligation independent of the *pesach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? כְּתִיב בְּהַאי וּכְתִיב בְּהַאי, וּצְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other,** Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov, how does he respond to this argument? The Gemara answers: According to Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov, the *halakha* that one must eat *matza* and bitter herbs despite being unable to partake of the Paschal lamb **was written with regard to this** person, an uncircumcised man, **and it was written** also **with regard to that** one, a ritually impure person, **and** both verses **are necessary.** We cannot learn the *halakha* of a ritually impure person from that of an uncircumcised man, or vice versa, as is explained in several places.

The *Gemara* adds that the verse covering *tamei* and *derech rechokah* is still necessary even if one knows each case individually, because arguments might be constructed to distinguish one from the other, and the Torah therefore covers both explicitly.

### Six Days Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים תֹּאכַל מַצּוֹת וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי עֲצֶרֶת לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״. מָה שְׁבִיעִי רְשׁוּת, אַף שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava: "Six days you shall eat *matzot*, and on the seventh day shall be a solemn assembly to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 16:8). **Just as** eating *matza* on the **seventh** day is merely **optional,** i.e., there is no obligation to eat *matza* on the last day of Passover, but only to avoid eating leavened bread, as the verse states: "Six days you shall eat *matzot*," **so too,** eating *matza* during the first **six days** is **optional.**

The *Gemara* further develops the position of *Rava bar Yitzchak* by examining a second verse: *sheshes yamim tochal matzot u-va-yom ha-shevi'i atzeret la-Hashem Elokecha* — six days you shall eat *matzot* and on the seventh day is a solemn assembly. This verse mentions only six days of eating, not seven. The *Gemara* asks: *mah shevi'i reshut* — since the seventh day appears optional (it is a *Yom Tov* and eating *matzah* is clearly permitted but not commanded separately there), perhaps the entire six-day period is also optional? The hermeneutical principle is *davar she-hayah bi-khlal ve-yatza min ha-klal l'lamed* — something that was part of a general category and was singled out comes to teach about the entire category, not just itself. Since the verse singles out the seventh day and appears to render it *reshut*, the same status extends to all six days.

The logical conclusion is that throughout *Chol ha-Moed* and the final day, there is no obligation to eat *matzah*; if you choose to eat bread, eat *matzah*, but you are not compelled to. This would appear to extend to the first night as well — *yakhol af lailah ha-rishon reshut*? The *Gemara* answers: *talmud lomar al matzot u-merorim yochluhu* — the Torah verse about eating the *pesach* with *matzah* and *maror* establishes that on the first night there is an obligation. But this, as noted, is tied to the *korban pesach* in the time of the *Beit ha-Mikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? הָוֵי דָּבָר שֶׁהָיָה בַּכְּלָל, וְיָצָא מִן הַכְּלָל לְלַמֵּד, לֹא לְלַמֵּד עַל עַצְמוֹ יָצָא, אֶלָּא לְלַמֵּד עַל הַכְּלָל כּוּלּוֹ יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that it is optional to eat *matza* on the first six days of Passover as well as the seventh? The seventh day of Passover **is something that was included in a generalization but was explicitly singled out to teach.** According to the rules of exegesis, **it was intended to teach not just about itself but about the entire generalization.** In other words, the seventh day of Passover was initially included in the verse: "You shall eat *matzot* for seven days" (Exodus 12:15), but was excluded from this generalization by the verse: "Six days you shall eat *matzot*." In accordance with the above principle, the *halakha* of the seventh day applies to all the other days of Passover as well. That means there is no obligation to eat *matzah* for all seven days of the Festival, but only on the first day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן רְשׁוּת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים יֹאכְלוּהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: I **might** have thought that **even** the mitzva to eat *matza* on **the first night** of Passover is included by the above principle, and it too is merely **optional;** therefore, **the verse states: "They shall eat it with *matzot* and bitter herbs"** (Numbers 9:11).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בָּעֶרֶב תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת״, הַכָּתוּב קְבָעוֹ חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **nothing other** than that one is obligated to eat *matza* **when the Temple is standing. From where** is it derived that one is obligated to eat *matza* on the first night of Passover even **when the Temple is not standing? The verse states: "In the evening you shall eat *matzot*." The verse** here **establishes** the mitzva of *matza* as **obligatory,** in accordance with the opinion of Rava.

For *bizman hazeh*, the obligation on the first night is derived from *ba-erev tochlu matzot*, and *Rava* holds this as a fully independent *de'oraita*. Since *matzah* is established as a biblical obligation, *maggid* — the recitation of the *Haggadah* — is also biblical, because the verse *ba-avur zeh* is conditioned on the presence of *matzah* and *maror* before you: *ba-avur zeh lo amru ela be-sha'ah she-yesh matzah u-maror munachim le-fanecha*. Without a *de'oraita matzah*, there is no *de'oraita maggid*.

According to *Rava bar Yitzchak*, by contrast, virtually every *mitzvah* of *leil Seder* is rabbinic. The only *de'oraita* obligations that night might be *birkat ha-mazon* and possibly *kiddush* for *Yom Tov* — though even the latter is complicated by the question of whether *kiddush* for *Yom Tov* carries biblical force in the same way as *kiddush* for *Shabbat*. We rule in accordance with *Rava*, and *matzah bizman hazeh* is thus *de'oraita*, *maror* is *derabbanan*, and *maggid* is *de'oraita*.

## Sleeping Rule

### Mishna Sleep Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יָשְׁנוּ מִקְצָתָן — יֹאכֵלוּ. כּוּלָּן — לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If some of** the participants at the seder **fell asleep,** thereby interrupting their meal, **they** may **eat** from the Paschal lamb when they awake. If the **entire** company fell asleep, **they** may **not eat** any more. If they all fall asleep, this is considered a complete interruption, and if they were to resume their meal it would be akin to eating the offering in two different places.

The *mishnah* teaches: *yashnu miktzatan yochlu kulam, yashnu kulam lo yochlu* — if some members of the group fell asleep, the group may continue eating when they awaken; if all of them fell asleep, they may not continue. The context is the eating of the *korban pesach*: falling asleep creates an interruption analogous to departing from one *chavurah*, and resuming in such circumstances would be akin to eating the *pesach* in two separate settings.

One might also frame the concern as a variant of the *afikoman* prohibition: having fallen asleep and awoken, any further eating of *matzah* would constitute a new, post-meal eating — precisely the kind of *afikoman*-eating one is trying to avoid. The *Rashbam* frames the concern as resembling eating the *pesach* in two locations.

If some members remained awake, continuity is preserved and eating may resume. If all fell asleep, the communal meal has effectively ended, and resumption is forbidden.

### Rabbi Yose Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נִתְנַמְנְמוּ — יֹאכֵלוּ, נִרְדְּמוּ — לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ. הַפֶּסַח אַחַר חֲצוֹת מְטַמֵּא אֶת הַיָּדַיִם. הַפִּגּוּל וְהַנּוֹתָר מְטַמְּאִין אֶת הַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** If **they dozed they** may **eat** from the Paschal lamb when they awake, but if **they fell** fast **asleep they** may **not eat** from it. The Sages further said: **The Paschal lamb after midnight renders one's hands ritually impure,** as it becomes *notar*, an offering that remained after the time when they may be eaten has expired; and the Sages ruled that both ***piggul*,** offerings that were invalidated due to inappropriate intent while being sacrificed, **and *notar* render one's hands ritually impure.**

*Rabbi Yose* introduces a distinction between types of sleep: *nishnamnemu yochlu, nirdemu lo yochlu* — if they merely dozed, they may continue eating; if they fell into deep sleep, they may not. Whether *Rabbi Yose* is being *meikel* or *machmir* relative to the anonymous *mishnah* is ambiguous: he may be restricting even the case where only some fell asleep (requiring that the sleep be light), or he may be permitting even the case where all fell asleep as long as the sleep was only a doze.

## Chatzot Deadline…

### Pesach Nosar After Chatz…

The *mishnah* states: *ha-pesach achar chatzot metamei et ha-yadayim* — the *pesach* after midnight renders the hands *tamei*. This is because the *pesach* becomes *nosar* — leftover meat that may not be eaten — once *chatzot* passes, since according to this *mishnah* the entire night is not available for eating the *korban pesach*; only until midnight. *Nosar*, when touched, imparts *tumah* to the hands, as will be discussed below. The final line of the *mishnah* generalizes: *ha-pigul ve-ha-nosar metamim et ha-yadayim* — both *pigul* and *nosar* render the hands *tamei*.

## Sleeping Rule…

### Rav Ashi Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נִתְנַמְנְמוּ — יֹאכֵלוּ, נִרְדְּמוּ — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי נִתְנַמְנֵם? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נִים וְלָא נִים, תִּיר וְלָא תִּיר. כְּגוֹן דְּקָרֵי לֵיהּ וְעָנֵי, וְלָא יָדַע לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי סְבָרָא, וְכִי מַדְכְּרוּ לֵיהּ — מִדְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Yosei says:** If **they dozed they** may **eat** from the Paschal lamb, but if **they fell asleep they** may **not eat** from it. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **dozing? Rav Ashi said:** One is **asleep but not asleep, awake but not awake, when, if they call him, he** will **answer, but he is unable to provide a reasonable** answer. **And when they** later **inform him** of what happened, **he remembers** it.

The *Gemara* opens the discussion of *Rabbi Yose's* ruling by asking: *heichi dami nishnamnemu* — what exactly constitutes dozing? *Rav Ashi* defines it: *nim ve-lo nim, tir ve-lo tir* — sleeping but not quite sleeping, awake but not quite awake — the half-asleep state. The operational test: *dekari lei ve-ani* — you call the person and he responds, he reacts to his name; *ve-lo yada la-haduri sevara* — but if asked something that requires reasoning, he cannot generate a response on his own. However, *ve-chi midkeru lei midkar* — if you prompt him with the beginning of the thought, he can follow along and remember. He cannot produce independent reasoning, but he can track and respond to external prompts. This is a precise and recognizable phenomenological description of the hypnagogic state.

### Abaye-Rabbah Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה. חֲזָא דְּקָא נַמְנֵם, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵינָם קָא נָאֵים מָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵינוֹמֵי קָא (מְנַמְנֵם) [מְנַמְנַמְנָא], וּתְנַן: נִתְנַמְנְמוּ — יֹאכֵלוּ, נִרְדְּמוּ — לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related episode: **Abaye was sitting before Rabba,** and **he saw that** Rabba **was dozing** off after he had begun to eat the final obligatory piece of *matza*. **He said to him: Is the Master sleeping?** Rabba **said to him: I am dozing, and we learned** in the mishna: If **they dozed, they** may **eat** from the Paschal lamb, but if **they fell** fast **asleep they** may **not eat** from it.

*Abaye hava yasiv kamei de-Rabbah* — *Abaye* was sitting before *Rabbah* during a *shiur*. *Chazah de-ka minanem* — he observed that *Rabbah* was dozing off. *Amar lei: nim ka naim mar?* — he asked, "Master, were you dozing?" *Amar lei: nimumei ka menamnemna* — "I was dozing, yes." And then *Rabbah* cited the *mishnah* — *nishnamnemu yochlu, nirdemu lo yochlu* — to demonstrate that his dozing, being only *nishnamnemu* and not *nirdemu*, did not constitute the kind of sleep that would have legal consequence. The *Rashbam* explains that the applicable context was not the *Seder* but rather a *taanit*: the *Yerushalmi* teaches that falling asleep can constitute the beginning of a fast, since one who sleeps has effectively ended his day. *Rabbah* was therefore using *Rabbi Yose's* distinction to establish that his doze was too light to count as the sleep that initiates a *taanit*.

## Chatzot Deadline…

### Rabbi Elazar Chatzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפֶּסַח אַחַר חֲצוֹת מְטַמֵּא אֶת הַיָּדַיִם וְכוּ׳. אַלְמָא מֵחֲצוֹת הָוֵה לֵיהּ נוֹתָר. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֶת הַבָּשָׂר בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״וְעָבַרְתִּי בְאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **the Paschal lamb after midnight renders one's hands ritually impure.** The Gemara infers: **Apparently, from midnight** and onward the Paschal lamb **is** classified as ***notar*.** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the ***tanna*** who maintains this opinion? **Rav Yosef said: It is Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the verse **"And they shall eat of the meat on that night"** (Exodus 12:8), **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: Here it is stated "on that night,"** from which it cannot be determined when the night ends. **And below it is stated: "And I will pass through the land of Egypt on that night** and I will strike every firstborn in the land of Egypt" (Exodus 12:12). The Torah states with regard to the death of the firstborns: "Thus said the Lord: At about midnight, I will go out into the midst of Egypt and every firstborn in Egypt shall die" (Exodus 11:4–5).

*Rav Yosef* identifies the *tanna* behind the *mishnah's* ruling that the *pesach* becomes *nosar* at *chatzot*: it is *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah*. A *baraita* records his derivation: the Torah says *ve-achaltem et ha-basar ba-lailah ha-zeh* — you shall eat the meat this night. *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* draws a *gezerah shavah* from the phrase *ba-lailah ha-zeh* to the verse *ve-avarti be-eretz Mitzrayim ba-lailah ha-zeh* — I will pass through the land of Egypt this night. Since the plague occurred at *chatzot*, the word *ha-zeh* implies a limit until midnight. Therefore the eating of the *pesach* also extends only until *chatzot*, after which it becomes *nosar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה לְהַלָּן — עַד חֲצוֹת, אַף כָּאן — עַד חֲצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Just as** in the verse **below,** the striking of the firstborns took place **until midnight,** as stated explicitly in the verse, **so too,** in the verse **here,** the mitzva to eat the Paschal lamb continues **until midnight** but not beyond. Evidently, the Paschal lamb may not be eaten after midnight.

### Rabbi Akiva Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״חִפָּזוֹן״ — עַד שְׁעַת חִפָּזוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to** Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: **But wasn't it** already **stated:** "Thus you shall eat it, with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, your staffs in your hands, and you will eat it **in haste,** for it is the Paschal offering for the Lord" (Exodus 12:11)? This verse indicates that the Paschal lamb may be eaten **until the time of haste,** i.e., until dawn, as the Jewish people left Egypt the next day.

*Rabbi Akiva* challenges this derivation. The Torah also says *ve-achaltem oto be-chipazon* — you shall eat it in haste. *Chipazon* refers to the moment when the Egyptians urgently expelled the Israelites — which occurred in the morning, not at midnight. Therefore the eating extends until the morning.

### Ad Boker Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּלַּיְלָה״? יָכוֹל יְהֵא נֶאֱכָל כַּקֳּדָשִׁים בַּיּוֹם — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּלַּיְלָה״, בַּלַּיְלָה הוּא נֶאֱכָל, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל בְּיוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva continues: **If** that is **so, what is** the meaning when **the verse states: "On** that **night,"** with regard to eating the Paschal lamb? The Gemara explains that this phrase is necessary, as I **might** have thought that the Paschal lamb **is eaten during the day,** like all other **offerings,** which must be slaughtered and eaten during the day. Therefore, the **verse states: "On** that **night,"** to underscore that this particular offering **is eaten at night, and it is not eaten during the day.**

The *Gemara* records the extended exchange between the two positions. The word *balaila* — "at night" — might seem superfluous if *chipazon* already establishes the deadline as morning. *Rabbi Akiva* explains: *balaila* is needed to teach that eating begins at night rather than during the day on which the *korban* was offered — since all other *korbanos* may be eaten beginning the day of their offering, one might have thought the *pesach* similarly begins in the afternoon. *Balaila* teaches that it is eaten only at night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַאי ״הַזֶּה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי לַיְלָה אַחֵר הוּא דַּאֲתָא. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּפֶסַח קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וּשְׁלָמִים קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים — מָה שְׁלָמִים נֶאֱכָלִים לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד, אַף פֶּסַח;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Akiva, what does he do with** the word **"that"?** As he doesn't use it for a verbal analogy, what does Rabbi Akiva learn from this word? The Gemara answers: **He needs it to exclude another night. It could enter your mind to say that since the Paschal lamb** falls into the category of **offerings of lesser sanctity,** and **peace-offerings are** also **offerings of lesser sanctity, just as peace-offerings** may **be eaten for two days and one night,** i.e., the day they are sacrificed through the following day, as stated in the Torah, **so too,** the same *halakha* should apply to **the Paschal lamb.**

*Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* counters that the nighttime-eating limitation could have been derived from *ad boker* — "until morning" — rendering *ha-zeh* available to teach the *chatzot* limit. *Rabbi Akiva* responds that without *ha-zeh*, the word *boker* might have been read as referring to the morning of the second night, implying that eating is permitted until the following morning. *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* rejoins: wherever the Torah writes *boker*, it means the first morning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹקֵים לֵילוֹת בִּמְקוֹם יָמִים, וִיהֵא נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי לֵילוֹת וְיוֹם אֶחָד, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the previous statement. How could the Paschal lamb be eaten for two days and one night if one starts eating it at night? The Gemara explains: One may say: **I will substitute the nights** that the Paschal lamb may be eaten **instead of the days** that a peace-offering is eaten. **And** accordingly, the Paschal lamb may **be eaten for two nights and one day.** Therefore, **the Torah wrote** the word **"that,"** to teach that the Paschal lamb may be eaten only on that one night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אָמַר לָךְ: מִ״לֹּא תוֹתִירוּ עַד בֹּקֶר״ נָפְקָא הָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya,** from where might he derive the *halakha* that the Paschal lamb may not be eaten for two nights? Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya could have **said to you: This** *halakha* is **derived from** the verse: **"You shall let nothing** of it **remain until the morning;** and that of it which remains until the morning you shall burn with fire" (Exodus 12:10). If it is prohibited to leave any part of the Paschal lamb until the morning, it is certainly prohibited to leave any of it until the following night. Therefore, it is unnecessary to cite an additional source to teach that the Paschal lamb may be eaten only on the first night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אָמַר לָךְ: אִי לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״הַזֶּה״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי ״בֹּקֶר״ — בֹּקֶר שֵׁנִי. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר לָךְ: כׇּל הֵיכָא דִּכְתַב ״בֹּקֶר״ — בֹּקֶר רִאשׁוֹן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva** could have **said to you,** in response to this argument: **If the Torah hadn't written** "on **that** night," **I would have said: What is** indicated by the word **"morning"** in that verse? It means **the second morning** after the Festival, the day of the sixteenth of Nisan. Therefore, it was necessary for the Torah to write that one may eat the Paschal lamb only on that night and no other. **And Rabbi Elazar** could have **said to you** in response: **Anywhere that** the Torah **writes "morning,"** it is referring to **the first,** i.e., the next **morning.** If that were not the case, no biblical text could have any definitive meaning.

### Rava: Matzah Chatzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אָכַל מַצָּה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה אַחַר חֲצוֹת, לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא! דְּכֵיוָן דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְפֶסַח — כְּפֶסַח דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Nowadays,** if one **ate *matza* after midnight,** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, he has not fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this statement: It is **obvious** that this is the case, **for since** the verse juxtaposes *matza* to **the Paschal lamb,** it is **considered like the Paschal lamb,** and therefore *matza* may also be eaten only until midnight.

*Rava* applies this dispute to *matzah*: *achal matzah bizman hazeh achar chatzot, Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah lo yatza yedei chovato* — one who eats *matzah* in our time after *chatzot* has not fulfilled his obligation according to *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah*. The *Gemara* initially objects that this is obvious — *peshita* — since *matzah* is linked to the *pesach*. But *Rava's* point is not trivial: one might have thought that since the Torah explicitly extracted *matzah* from its linkage to the *pesach* and established it as an independent *mitzvah*, the time constraints of the *pesach* no longer apply. *Mah u-ditema, ha apkei kra me-heiksha* — one might argue the verse detached *matzah* from the *pesach*. *Rava* teaches: *de-chi ahadrei kra, le-milsa ka-maisa ahadrei* — when the Torah restored *matzah* as an independent obligation, it retained the essential linkage in terms of time. *Matzah* is independent as a *mitzvah*, but its temporal framework remains bound to that of the *korban pesach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָא אַפְּקֵיהּ קְרָא מֵהֶיקֵּישָׁא. קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּכִי אַהְדְּרֵיהּ קְרָא — לְמִילְּתָא קַמַּיְיתָא אַהְדְּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rava's statement is necessary, **lest you say** that **the verse has removed** the *halakha* of *matza* **from** this **juxtaposition,** as Rava maintains that eating *matza* is a distinct mitzva that applies even nowadays. One might therefore have thought that the *halakhot* of eating *matza* differ entirely from those of the Paschal lamb. Rava therefore **teaches us that when the verse repeats** the mitzva to eat *matza* on the first night, **it restores** this mitzva **to its original status,** which means that one may eat *matza* only at a time when he may also eat the Paschal lamb.

This is a significant *chiddush*: *matzah* is simultaneously a free-standing *de'oraita mitzvah* and temporally tethered to the *pesach*. One who eats *matzah* after *chatzot* has not fulfilled his obligation, according to *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* as applied by *Rava*. Consequently, *maggid* — which is linked to the *matzah* obligation — is also bounded by *chatzot*.

This is the basis for the widespread practice of trying to eat the *afikoman* before *chatzot*. It is worth noting the irony: *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* himself is one of the figures celebrated in the *Haggadah* for discussing the *Exodus* all night — *hayu mesaprim bi-ytziat Mitzrayim kol oto ha-lailah* — yet according to his own ruling, the *matzah* must be eaten by midnight. Some have noted that perhaps he believed elements of *maggid* not directly tied to the *matzah* could continue after *chatzot*; others suggest more creative solutions, such as the practice of one who ate an additional *kezayit* of *matzah* just before *chatzot* so that *mi-mah nafshach* he had fulfilled his obligation before the deadline, and could then continue the discussion through the night.

*Tosafot* acknowledges uncertainty about whether we rule like *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* or *Rabbi Akiva*, which is why not all authorities are equally stringent about the *chatzot* deadline.

A further question concerns the relationship between this *chatzot* and the *chatzot* mentioned in the first *mishnah* of *Berachot*, where *Rabbi Gamliel* implies that *keri'at shema* and certain *korbanos* extend until dawn (*amud ha-shachar*), while the *mishnah* sets *chatzot* as the deadline. *Tosafot* explains that in *Berachot*, the *chatzot* serves as a rabbinic fence — *be-di'avad*, if one missed it, one may still fulfill the obligation until dawn. But according to *Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah*, the *pesach's* limit at *chatzot* is grounded in the *gezerah shavah* to *ve-avarti be-eretz Mitzrayim ba-lailah ha-zeh*, making it a genuine biblical cutoff — *mamash* becoming *nosar* after midnight.

## Pigul & Nosar Tumah

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפִּיגּוּל וְהַנּוֹתָר מְטַמֵּא אֶת הַיָּדַיִם וְכוּ׳. רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא, חַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם חֲשֵׁדֵי כְהוּנָּה, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם עֲצֵלֵי כְהוּנָּה. חַד אָמַר: כְּזַיִת, וְחַד אָמַר: כְּבֵיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that ***piggul* and *notar* render one's hands ritually impure.** This issue is subject to a dispute between **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda. One** of them **said:** The reason for this enactment is **due to suspected priests,** i.e., priests who were suspected of invalidating offerings; **and** the other **one said** the reason is **due to lazy priests.** Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda also disagree about another matter: **One** of them **said** that the ritual impurity of *notar* and *piggul* applies even to **an olive-bulk** of the meat; **and one** of them **said** it applies only to **an egg-bulk.**

The *Gemara* now returns to explain why *pigul* and *nosar* render the hands *tamei*. *Rav Huna* and *Rav Chisda* each offer a reason, with one addressing *pigul* and the other *nosar*. *Chad amar mishum chashdei kehuna* — one says the concern is *kohanim* who are suspected of deliberately intending to invalidate *korbanos*: a *kohen* who plans to eat the offering outside its proper time or place renders it *pigul*, and the rabbinic imposition of *tumah* serves as a deterrent. The rationale is that *kohanim* have an almost visceral aversion to contracting *tumah* while serving in the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, and the threat of *tumah* may deter misconduct that the biblical prohibitions alone do not. *Ve-chad amar mishum atzlei kehuna* — the other says the concern is *kohanim* who are lazy and negligent, failing to ensure the meat is consumed within its time and thereby allowing it to become *nosar*. The *tumah* designation for *nosar* is a rabbinic safeguard against this carelessness.

### Kezayit vs. Kibeitzah

A further dispute concerns the measure of *tumah*: *chad amar kezayit ve-chad amar ke-beitzah* — one holds that a *kezayit* of *pigul* or *nosar* causes the hands to become *tamei*, while the other holds it requires a *ke-beitzah* (egg-size).

### Measure Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד תָּנֵי אַפִּיגּוּל, וְחַד תָּנֵי אַנּוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that there is no dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda concerning the reason for the prohibition. **One** of these two Sages **teaches** his explanation **with regard to** the case of ***piggul*,** mentioned in the mishna; **and** the other **one teaches** it **with regard to *notar*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּתָנֵי אַפִּיגּוּל — מִשּׁוּם חֲשֵׁדֵי כְהוּנָּה. מַאן דְּתָנֵי אַנּוֹתָר — מִשּׁוּם עֲצֵלֵי כְהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The one who teaches** it **with regard to *piggul*** maintains that the reason is **due to suspected priests.** As a result of enmity, a priest might cause the offerings to become *piggul*. To dissuade priests from doing so, the Sages instituted that one who touches *piggul* is rendered ritually impure, which ensures that the offending priest also suffers from his actions. **He who teaches** this explanation **with regard to *notar*** claims that the reason **is due to lazy priests,** to prevent sloth among the priests. The Sages decreed that *notar* causes ritual impurity, to ensure that the priests ate the sacrificial meat within the allotted time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד אָמַר כְּזַיִת וְחַד אָמַר כְּבֵיצָה. מַאן דְּאָמַר כְּזַיִת — כְּאִיסּוּרוֹ, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּבֵיצָה — כְּטוּמְאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated above that Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda disagree with regard to the size of the meat that confers ritual impurity. **One** of them **said** that **an olive-bulk** of meat contracts ritual impurity, and **one** of them **said** that only **an egg-bulk** contracts ritual impurity. The Gemara explains the reasoning behind this debate. **The one who said** that **an olive-bulk** contracts ritual impurity **maintains that** the ritual impurity of *notar* and *piggul* is **similar to its prohibition.** Since *notar* and *piggul* are prohibited when they are an olive-bulk, the same applies to their ritual impurity. **And the one who said** that sacrificial meat becomes ritually impure when it is **an egg-bulk** maintains that **it is similar to its ritual impurity.** In other words, just as the minimum size of ritual impurity for other types of meat is an egg-bulk, the same applies to *piggul* and *notar*.

The *Gemara* explains both positions. *Man d'amar kezayit — ke-issuro*: the one who says *kezayit* reasons that since the prohibited *act of eating* these substances is defined by the measure of a *kezayit* — that being the minimum for the prohibition of eating *pigul* or *nosar* — the *tumah* that mirrors this prohibition should likewise be triggered by a *kezayit*. *Ve-man d'amar ke-beitzah — ke-tumato*: the one who says *ke-beitzah* reasons from the standard framework of food *tumah*, in which the operative measure is an egg-sized quantity. Even though *pigul* and *nosar* are prohibited foods, they remain within the world of *tumah* and should conform to its standard measure of *ke-beitzah*.

## Blessing Exemption

### Mishna Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּירַךְ בִּרְכַּת הַפֶּסַח — פָּטַר אֶת שֶׁל זֶבַח. בֵּירַךְ אֶת שֶׁל זֶבַח — לֹא פָּטַר אֶת שֶׁל פֶּסַח, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא זוֹ פּוֹטֶרֶת זוֹ וְלֹא זוֹ פּוֹטֶרֶת זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one recited the blessing over the Paschal lamb,** which is: Who sanctified us with His mitzvot and commanded us to eat the Paschal lamb, **he has** also **exempted** himself from reciting a blessing over the Festival **offering.** The blessing for the Festival peace-offering of the fourteenth of Nisan is: Who sanctified us with His mitzvot and commanded us to eat the offering. However, if **he recited the blessing over the** Festival **offering, he has not exempted** himself from reciting a blessing over **the Paschal lamb.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: This** blessing **does not exempt** one from reciting a blessing over **this one, and that** blessing **does not exempt that one,** as there is a separate blessing for each offering.

The final *mishnah* of the tractate teaches: *birkat ha-pesach poteret et shel zevach* — the blessing recited over the *pesach* exempts the *chagigah* and other *korbanos* eaten alongside it from requiring their own separate blessing. The reverse, however, does not hold: *beirach et ha-zevach, lo poter shel pesach* — if one recited the blessing over the other *korban*, that does not exempt the *pesach*. This is the position of *Rabbi Yishmael*. *Rabbi Akiva* disagrees entirely: *lo zu poteret zu ve-lo zu poteret zu* — neither exempts the other.

This is a striking *mishnah* on several levels. The very notion that one blessing can exempt another *korban* is curious: if the blessing is *le-echol ha-pesach*, how does it extend to the *chagigah*? And the question of the order — one presumably eats the *chagigah* before the *pesach*, to come to the *pesach al ha-sova* — makes it unclear how the blessing on the *pesach* could have preceded the eating of the *chagigah*.

### Yishmael Overlap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כְּשֶׁתִּמְצָא לוֹמַר, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: זְרִיקָה בִּכְלַל שְׁפִיכָה. וְלֹא שְׁפִיכָה בִּכְלַל זְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains the opinions of the *tanna'im* in the mishna. When you analyze the matter you will find that **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, sprinkling** of the blood on the altar **is included** in the more general category of **pouring.** In other words, the blessing over the Paschal lamb, whose blood is poured, includes the Festival peace-offering, whose blood is sprinkled, as sprinkling is included within the general category of pouring. **But** conversely, **pouring is not included in sprinkling.** Consequently, when one recites the blessing over the Festival peace-offering, he has not exempted himself from reciting a blessing over the Paschal lamb.

The *Gemara* explains *Rabbi Yishmael's* position through the lens of the laws of blood service. For standard *korbanos*, the blood is offered through *zerikah* — throwing the blood against the corners of the *mizbeach* from a distance, so that it splatters against two walls. For the *korban pesach*, the blood is offered through *shefichah* — pouring it at the base of the *mizbeach*. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds: *zerikah bi-khlal shefichah* — *zerikah* is subsumed within *shefichah*. That is, a *korban* that requires *zerikah* could, under *Rabbi Yishmael's* view, also have its obligation satisfied through *shefichah*.

### Akiva Opposition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לֹא שְׁפִיכָה בִּכְלַל זְרִיקָה, וְלֹא זְרִיקָה בִּכְלַל שְׁפִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** these are two separate mitzvot: **Pouring is not included in sprinkling, and sprinkling is not included in pouring.** Therefore, Rabbi Akiva maintains that each offering requires its own blessing.

*Rabbi Akiva* holds the opposite: *lo shefichah bi-khlal zerikah ve-lo zerikah bi-khlal shefichah* — neither method is subsumed within the other. They are categorically distinct blood rites, and neither can serve as a substitute for the other.

### Gemara Explanation

The *Gemara* derives the ruling of the *mishnah* from this dispute: because according to *Rabbi Yishmael*, *zerikah bi-khlal shefichah* — what was done with the blood of the *pesach* (*shefichah*) could in theory have been done for the other *korban* as well — the blessing on the *pesach* extends to exempt the other *korban*. The blood rites overlap, creating a sufficient nexus for the blessing to carry over. But *lo shefichah bi-khlal zerikah* — the *shefichah* of the *pesach* could not have served as *zerikah* for a *korban* that requires *zerikah*; therefore the blessing on the other *korban* does not extend to the *pesach*. According to *Rabbi Akiva*, since neither overlaps with the other, no blessing exempts the other in any direction.

It must be noted that this explanation is difficult to follow. The *bracha* is recited over the *eating* of the *korban*, not over the *hakravat ha-korban* or the blood service. It is unclear how the theoretical interchangeability of blood-sprinkling methods — *zerikah* and *shefichah* — should bear on whether the blessing recited over the eating of one *korban* extends to the eating of another. The *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* explain the connection, but the full resolution of how the *Gemara* ties blood rites to blessings over eating remains a question with which we will open the *siyum* tomorrow.
