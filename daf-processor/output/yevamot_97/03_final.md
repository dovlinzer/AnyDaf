# Yevamot 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Underage Kiddushin

### Bi'ah of Minor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ וּמֵת — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וָמֵת — הֲרֵי זוֹ פְּטוּרָה. בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ, וּמִשֶּׁהִגְדִּיל נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת, וָמֵת, אִם לֹא יָדַע אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה מִשֶּׁהִגְדִּיל — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּיה אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a boy **aged nine years and one day had relations with his *yevama* and died,** that *yevama* **performs *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage. If the minor married a woman** in a regular manner **and died, she is exempt** from levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*, as by Torah law a minor cannot marry. If a boy **aged nine years and one day had relations with his *yevama*, and after he matured he married a different woman and** then **died** childless, **if he did not** carnally **know the first woman after he matured,** but only when he was a minor, **the first one performs *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** as she is in essence a *yevama* who had relations with a minor, **and the second woman either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage,** as she is his full-fledged wife.

The daf begins at the middle of 97b. The preceding *Mishnah* had established a case involving a young man who is nine years and a day old or more — past the threshold of *bi'ah* taking legal effect, yet still before *bar mitzvah* — whose act of intercourse with his sister-in-law functions as a kind of *ma'amar* but does not constitute *yibum* in the full halachic sense. The scenario that follows is this: if such an underage man performs *bi'ah* with his sister-in-law and subsequently dies, the next surviving brother faces what the *Mishnah* calls *zekat shnei yevamim* — an obligation to two sisters-in-law simultaneously. The first *mitzvah* of *yibum*, deriving from the original marriage that was never actualized through proper *yibum*, remains outstanding; and a second, *derabbanan* obligation to the deceased minor's quasi-wife also attaches. The result is that the surviving brother is not permitted to perform *yibum* at all, and instead *chalitzah* is required.

### No Co-Wife Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָה: הָא דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן זִיקַּת שְׁנֵי יְבָמִין, מִיחְלָץ חָלְצָה יַבּוֹמֵי לָא מִיַּבְּמָה, לָא תֵּימָא הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא צָרָה, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִגְזַר מִשּׁוּם צָרָה. דְּהָא הָכָא לֵיכָּא צָרָה, מִיחְלָץ חָלְצָה, יַבּוֹמֵי לָא מִיַּבְּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: If a brother performed levirate betrothal with a *yevama* and died, she has a levirate bond in relation to the remaining brothers from two deceased brothers. **Rava said:** With regard to **that which the Rabbis said,** that when **the bond of two *yevamin*** exists, **she performs *ḥalitza* and she does not enter into levirate marriage, you should not say that this applies only when there is a rival wife, as there is reason to decree due to a rival wife.** The proof that this is not the case is **that here, there is no rival wife,** as it is referring to one woman, which means that this *yevama* who had relations with the nine-year-old is tied by the bonds of both her first husband and the underage *yavam*, whose intercourse is like levirate betrothal, and even so **she performs *ḥalitza* but she does not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with Rabbah's clarification: the rabbinic rule that a woman bearing two *zekot* from two brothers-in-law requires *chalitzah* and not *yibum*. Rabbah stresses that one should not think this ruling is limited to cases where there is a co-wife. The prior discussions in an earlier *perek* had involved a co-wife scenario, and one might have supposed that the restriction was tied specifically to that concern. Rabbah therefore insists that the rule applies even when there is no co-wife.

To make the case concrete: using the paradigm of Er, Onan, and Shelah — if Onan, as an underage *kattan*, performed *bi'ah* with Tamar, this creates a *derabbanan* quasi-marriage but not a *de'oraita yibum*. When Onan then dies, Shelah's obligation to Tamar has two dimensions: a *de'oraita* obligation stemming from Er's genuine marriage to her, which was never resolved through *yibum* or *chalitzah*; and a *derabbanan* obligation stemming from Onan's quasi-marriage. Because Onan's *bi'ah* was not *de'oraita*, it did not displace Er from the picture. Tamar therefore stands under two *zekot*, and the *halacha* is *chalitzah*, not *yibum*. In this version of the case there is no co-wife at all — that is Rabbah's point.

### Appearance Concern

What then is the rationale for the restriction in the co-wife case that Rabbah is distinguishing from? In that scenario, where husband number two had done *ma'amar* and was also married to another woman, the concern is one of appearances. If the surviving brother performs *yibum* with the first woman — legitimately, on account of the *de'oraita* obligation from husband number one — it will appear as though he has performed *yibum* with two wives of the same deceased husband. People will not understand that one *yibum* is being done on account of Er and the other would have been on account of Onan. The obvious resolution of doing *yibum* with one and *chalitzah* with the other is itself rejected, because regardless of which direction one goes, the appearance of *yibum* with two women of the same deceased man will arise. The restriction on *yibum* in the co-wife case is therefore driven by this appearance concern. Rabbah's contribution is to establish that even in the absence of a co-wife — where that concern does not apply — the rule of *chalitzah* rather than *yibum* holds.

### Regular Marriage Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וּמֵת כּוּ׳. תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן שֶׁנָּשְׂאוּ וּמֵתוּ — נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּיבּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if a nine-year-old boy **married a woman and died,** she is exempt from levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*. The Gemara comments: **We** already **learned this, as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an imbecile and a minor who married women and died, their wives are exempt from *ḥalitza* and from levirate marriage,** as the marriage of a minor or an imbecile is of no account.

The *Gemara* then adds a brief but important note: if this *kattan* had simply contracted a normal marriage — *kiddushin* and *nisuin* — with a woman, and then died, that marriage carries no legal weight and the woman has no obligation of *chalitzah* or *yibum* with his brothers whatsoever.

## Capacity & Status

### Shotim Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּנֵי יוּדָן אַמְתָּא אִשְׁתַּחְרוּר, שְׁרָא לְהוּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב לְמִינְסַב נְשֵׁי דַּהֲדָדֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָסַר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוּא אָסַר וַאֲנָא שָׁרֵינָא. מִן הָאָב וְלֹא מִן הָאֵם — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי. מִן הָאֵם וְלֹא מִן הָאָב — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The sons of Yudan the maidservant were freed. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov allowed them to marry each other's wives** after divorce. **Rava said to him: Didn't Rav Sheshet prohibit** marriage in that case? Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov **said to him: He prohibited** it **and I permit** it. I disagree with his ruling. **If the two freed slaves or converts are half brothers from their father's side and not from their mother's side, everyone agrees that the marriage is permitted,** as even a gentile and certainly a convert are considered unrelated to their father's family. If they are half brothers **from their mother and not from their father, everyone agrees that it is prohibited.**

This point is underscored by a *tannaic* teaching: *shoteh* — a person of unsound mind — and *katan* — a minor — who contracted marriage and died: their wives are entirely exempt from both *chalitzah* and *yibum*. The marriage of a *shoteh* or *katan* carries no halachic recognition. The *Gemara* notes that the classic triad of those lacking legal capacity — *shoteh*, *katan*, and *cheresh* — is only partially represented here, since the Rabbis did institute *kiddushin* and *nisuin* for a *cheresh* at least on a rabbinic level, which is why the *cheresh* is treated differently and not mentioned in this context.

## Underage Kiddushin…

### Rav Ashi vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן תֵּשַׁע וְכוּ׳ מִשֶּׁהִגְדִּיל וְכוּ׳. וְיַעֲשׂוּ בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע כְּמַאֲמָר בַּגָּדוֹל, וְתִדָּחֶה צָרָה מִיִּבּוּם! אָמַר רַב: לֹא עָשׂוּ בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע כְּמַאֲמָר בַּגָּדוֹל. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עָשׂוּ וְעָשׂוּ. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָשׂוּ וְעָשׂוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches the case of **a nine-year-old** boy who had relations with his *yevama* and **after he matured** married another woman. The Gemara asks: **And let** the Sages at least **establish the sexual relations of a nine-year-old** to be **like the levirate betrothal of an adult, and** it would therefore **override** the requirement of the **rival wife to** enter into **levirate marriage,** in accordance with the *halakha* of the rival wife of a woman who has the bond of two *yevamin*. **Rav said: They did not establish the intercourse of a nine-year-old** to be **like the levirate betrothal of an adult** in all regards, **and Shmuel said: They certainly did. And similarly, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They certainly did.**

The *Gemara* now takes up a case where the underage Onan did *bi'ah* with Tamar but was also married to Devorah. If he subsequently comes of age and consummates his relationship with Tamar as an adult, she becomes a genuine wife alongside Devorah. But if he did not consummate the relationship with Tamar as an adult and then dies — Devorah is a real wife, and Shelah may perform *yibum* with her; Tamar, however, was only rabbinically acquired, and Shelah must do *chalitzah* with her. The reason Tamar's *chalitzah* cannot be replaced by *yibum* with Devorah alone — the normal resolution in a *zekat shnei yevamim* scenario — is that the *de'oraita* obligation stemming from Er has never been resolved.

This prompts the *Gemara* to ask from an earlier passage: if the *bi'ah* of a *kattan* functions like a *ma'amar begadol*, then by analogy to the *ma'amar* case, shouldn't the surviving brother be required to do *chalitzah* with both Tamar and Devorah, not *yibum* with Devorah and *chalitzah* with Tamar?

Rav Ashi says that the Sages did not equate the *bi'ah* of a nine-year-old fully with a *ma'amar* by an adult. It carries legal weight in various respects — enough to disqualify the brothers and produce other halachic consequences — but it is weaker than a full *ma'amar*, and therefore Devorah is not displaced from the picture. Shelah can still perform *yibum* with Devorah. Shmuel disagrees: the *bi'ah* of the *kattan* is fully equivalent to a *ma'amar begadol*. Rabbi Yochanan likewise agrees with Shmuel.

### Tannaitic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיַעֲשׂוּ? תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. הָךְ תַּנָּא דְּאַרְבָּעָה אַחִין גָּזַר מִשּׁוּם צָרָה. וְאַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּגָדוֹל, וְהוּא הַדִּין בְּקָטָן. וְהַאי דְּאָמַר גָּדוֹל — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְגָדוֹל קָאֵי. וְהַאי תַּנָּא דְּהָכָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ עָשׂוּ, וְלָא גָּזַר מִשּׁוּם צָרָה. וְאַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּקָטָן, וְהוּא הַדִּין בְּגָדוֹל. וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר בְּקָטָן — דִּבְקָטָן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** If so, the question remains: **And let them establish** the sexual relations of a nine-year-old to be considered like levirate betrothal. Why is he able to perform levirate marriage with her rival wife? The Gemara answers: This **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. This *tanna*** who discusses the case **of four brothers** [from the Perek Arba Achim], he maintains that the *yevama* and her rival wife may not perform levirate marriage with one of the surviving brothers. The reason is that **he** maintains that the Sages **decreed** that a woman who has the bond of two deceased brothers may not perform levirate marriage **due to** the case of **a rival wife. And** that *tanna* **taught us** this *halakha* **with regard to an adult** brother who performed levirate marriage, **and the same is true of a minor** who had relations with her. **And** the reason **that he stated** the case of **an adult** in particular is **because** he was **referring to an adult. And** conversely, **this *tanna*, of** the mishna **here, holds that they established** the sexual relations of a minor entirely like the levirate betrothal of an adult, **and he** maintains that the Sages **did not decree** that a woman who has the bond of two deceased brothers may not perform levirate marriage **due to** the case of **a rival wife. And he taught us** this *halakha* **with regard to a minor, and the same is true of an adult. And** the reason **that he stated** the case of **a minor** in particular is **because** he was **referring to a minor.**

The *Gemara* then reframes the apparent contradiction between two *mishnayot* — one requiring *chalitzah* with both women, the other permitting *yibum* with the co-wife — not as a question about the strength of the minor's *bi'ah* relative to an adult's *ma'amar*, but as a *machloket tanna'im*.

The *tanna* of the *Perek Arba Achim*, who rules that the co-wife also requires *chalitzah*, made a *gezeirah* on account of the co-wife scenario. He ruled that *yibum* is entirely prohibited in order to prevent the appearance of *yibum* being performed with two women simultaneously. This *tanna* would say the same *halacha* whether the deceased second husband had performed a *ma'amar* as an adult or was a *kattan* whose *bi'ah* is the equivalent — the *gezeirah* applies equally. The *tanna* of our *Mishnah*, by contrast, does not make this *gezeirah* at all. He permits *yibum* with the co-wife Devorah even while requiring *chalitzah* with Tamar. This *tanna* would similarly permit *yibum* with a co-wife even in the adult *ma'amar* case.

The upshot is striking: the real dispute between the two *mishnayot* is not whether *bi'ah* of a *kattan* equals *ma'amar begadol* — both *tanna'im* would treat them identically — but whether one makes a *gezeirah* to prevent *yibum* entirely when there is a co-wife. Each *tanna* applies his view equally to both *kattan* and *gadol* cases; the apparent difference in the *mishnayot* arises only because each *tanna* chose the example most directly relevant to his discussion.

## Attribution Dispute

### Teaching Without Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֲמַר לִשְׁמַעְתָּא בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, וְלָא אַמְרַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. שְׁמַע רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אִיקְּפַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar went** and **said this *halakha*** in **the study hall, but he did not state it in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Instead, he issued the *halakha* without attribution. **Rabbi Yoḥanan heard** that Rabbi Elazar omitted mention of his name **and became angry** with him.

Rabbi Elazar, the student of Rabbi Yochanan, went and presented this entire analysis — the framing of the contradiction as a *machloket tanna'im* — in the *beit midrash*. However, he did not attribute the teaching to Rabbi Yochanan. When Rabbi Yochanan learned of this, he was deeply offended that his *Torah* was being transmitted without his name.

### Tiberias Synagogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּל לְגַבֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה הַמַּעֲשֶׂה בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא בְּנֶגֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ גְּלוֹסְטְרָא, שֶׁנֶּחְלְקוּ בּוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, עַד שֶׁקָּרְעוּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בַּחֲמָתָן. קָרְעוּ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא שֶׁנִּקְרַע סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בַּחֲמָתָן. וְהָיָה שָׁם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן קִיסְמָא, אָמַר: תָּמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם לֹא יִהְיֶה בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת זוֹ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וְכֵן הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi visited** Rabbi Yoḥanan, to placate him so that he would not be annoyed with his beloved disciple. **They said to him: Wasn't there an incident in the synagogue of Tiberias involving a bolt** that secures a door in place and **that has** a thick **knob [*gelustera*] at its end?** And it was stated **that Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yosei argued over** this case **until** they became so upset with each other **that they tore a Torah scroll in their anger. Tore?** Can it **enter your mind** that such great Sages would intentionally tear a Torah scroll? **Rather,** you must **say that a Torah scroll was torn through their anger.** In the heat of their debate they pulled the scroll from one side to another until it tore. **And Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma,** who **was there** at the time, **said: I would be surprised if this synagogue does not become** a place of **idolatrous worship. And** indeed **this** eventually **occurred.**

Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi came to calm Rabbi Yochanan down. They invoked the famous episode of the synagogue in Tiberias. A dispute had arisen there between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yose concerning a bolt — a door-bolt that has a rounded bulb at its end, making it usable for grinding and thus more of a *kli*, which would reduce concerns of *binyan* and *muktzah*. The question was whether one may insert it to lock a door on *Shabbat*. The debate between the two Sages became so heated that, while it was not intentional, a *sefer Torah* was torn in the tumult. Rabbi Yose ben Kisma, who was present, declared: I would be utterly astonished if this synagogue does not ultimately become a house of idolatry. And so it came to pass.

The point was plain: it is not worth allowing a dispute to escalate to such destructive ends. But when Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi made this comparison, it only made matters worse.

### Yehoshua Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אִיקְּפַד טְפֵי, אֲמַר: חַבְרוּתָא נָמֵי?! עוּל לְגַבֵּיהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳ אֶת מֹשֶׁה עַבְדּוֹ כֵּן צִוָּה מֹשֶׁה אֶת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכֵן עָשָׂה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לֹא הֵסִיר דָּבָר מִכׇּל אֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳ אֶת מֹשֶׁה״, וְכִי עַל כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁאָמַר יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָיָה אוֹמֵר לָהֶם: כָּךְ אָמַר לִי מֹשֶׁה? אֶלָּא יְהוֹשֻׁעַ יוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹרֵשׁ סְתָם, וְהַכֹּל יוֹדְעִין שֶׁתּוֹרָתוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה הִיא. אַף רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר תַּלְמִידְךָ יוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹרֵשׁ סְתָם, וְהַכֹּל יוֹדְעִין כִּי שֶׁלְּךָ הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **grew even angrier, saying:** You are **even** making us **colleagues** now? Those two Sages were peers, whereas Rabbi Elazar is merely my student. **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi visited** Rabbi Yoḥanan and **said to him:** The verse states: **"As God commanded His servant Moses, so did Moses command Joshua, and so did Joshua, he left nothing undone of all that the Lord commanded Moses"** (Joshua 11:15). **Now did Joshua, with regard to every matter that he said, say to** the Jews: **Thus Moses said to me? Rather, Joshua** would **sit and teach** Torah **without attributing** his statements, **and everyone would know that it was** from **the Torah of Moses. So too, your disciple Rabbi Elazar sits and teaches without attribution, and everyone knows that** his teaching **is** from **your** instruction.

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi then came to try his hand at the matter. He cited the verse from Yehoshua: "As God commanded His servant Moses, so did Moses command Joshua, and so did Joshua, he left nothing undone of all that the Lord commanded Moses." The verse emphasizes how scrupulously Yehoshua carried out everything Moshe commanded. But, Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi asked: do you imagine that every time Yehoshua taught, he explicitly declared, "Here is what Moshe told me"? Rather, Yehoshua would simply teach *Torah* without constant attribution. And everyone understood that everything he taught came from Moshe. The verse's very emphasis on his fidelity to Moshe demonstrated precisely that he did not need to label each teaching explicitly, because his role as the faithful channel of Moshe's *Torah* was self-evident.

Similarly, your student Rabbi Elazar teaches without attribution, and everyone knows that his *Torah* is yours.

This argument succeeded where the earlier attempt had failed. Rabbi Yochanan said to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi: why did you not know how to appease me as Ben Idi does?

### Immortality via Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: מִפְּנֵי מָה אִי אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִין לְפַיֵּיס כְּבֶן אִידִי חֲבֵרֵינוּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַאי טַעְמָא קָפֵיד כּוּלֵּי הַאי? דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אָגוּרָה בְּאׇהׇלְךָ עוֹלָמִים״, וְכִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְאָדָם לָגוּר בִּשְׁנֵי עוֹלָמִים? אֶלָּא אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ דְּבַר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּי בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁאוֹמְרִים דְּבַר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּיו בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, שִׂפְתוֹתָיו דּוֹבְבוֹת בַּקֶּבֶר. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן זְעֵירָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא שִׁמְעוֹן נְזִירָא: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״וְחִכֵּךְ כְּיֵין הַטּוֹב הוֹלֵךְ לְדוֹדִי לְמֵישָׁרִים דּוֹבֵב שִׂפְתֵי יְשֵׁנִים״. כְּכוֹמֶר שֶׁל עֲנָבִים: מָה כּוֹמֶר שֶׁל עֲנָבִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ אָדָם אֶצְבָּעוֹ עָלָיו — מִיָּד דּוֹבֵב; אַף תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאוֹמְרִים דְּבַר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּיהֶם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה — שִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם דּוֹבְבוֹת בַּקֶּבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Later, after calming down, **he said to** Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi: **Why don't you know how to appease me like our colleague ben Idi?** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is the reason** that **he was so angry** about this matter? The Gemara answers that this is **as Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "I will dwell in Your tent in worlds"** (Psalms 61:5), literally, forever? **And is it possible for a person to live in two worlds** simultaneously? **Rather, David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He:** **that they will say a matter of *halakha* in my name in this world** when I have passed on to another world. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** With regard to **any Torah scholar in whose name a matter of *halakha* is said in this world, his lips mouth** the words **in the grave,** as though he is talking. **Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Ze'eira said, and some say** this was stated by **Shimon the Nazirite: What is the verse** from which it is derived? **"And the roof of your mouth is like the best wine that glides down smoothly for my beloved, moving gently the lips of those who are asleep"** (Song of Songs 7:10). He explains: This is **like a pile [*komer*] of grapes** left to warm before they are pressed: **Just as** in the case of **a pile of grapes, when a person places his finger on it, immediately it moves,** as the wine bursts through and the whole pile shakes, **so too** with **Torah scholars: When a teaching is said in their name in this world their lips mouth** the words **in the grave.**

The *Gemara* then explains what lay at the root of Rabbi Yochanan's concern — and it was not mere pride. The *Gemara* cites: what does David mean when he says "I will dwell in Your tent in two worlds"? Can a person actually dwell in two worlds? Rather, David was praying: that people in this world would transmit his teachings in his name.

The source for why this matters: Any *talmid chacham* whose *Torah* is transmitted in his name in this world, his lips continue to murmur in the grave. The continuity of attribution is not about honor; it is about a form of continued presence in this world, a way of remaining alive while dwelling in the world to come. Rabbi Yitzchak ben Zeira asks: what is the verse? And cites: "And your palate is like fine wine, going straight to my beloved, causing the lips of those who sleep to murmur." Like a press of grapes: the moment one lays a finger upon pressed grapes, they immediately flow. So too with the *Torah* of *talmidei chachamim* — the moment it is spoken in their name, their lips begin to murmur in the grave.

Rabbi Yochanan's concern was not vanity but this: the transmission of *Torah* in a teacher's name is the mechanism by which a *talmid chacham* remains present in this world even after death. When his student taught his insight without attribution, Rabbi Yochanan felt that something vital had been severed — not his reputation, but his continued living connection to this world. The *Gemara* notes that the *pasuk* in *Shir Hashirim* invoked here is also from Shlomo, making it in some sense an apt citation in this context.

## Capacity & Status…

### Saris Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יָבִיאוּ רְאָיָה שֶׁהוּא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, וְהוּא הַסָּרִיס — לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַיבֵּם. בַּת עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיאָה שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יָבִיאוּ רְאָיָה שֶׁהִיא בַּת עֶשְׂרִים, וְהִיא הָאַיְלוֹנִית — לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara addresses the statement of the mishna that this is the *halakha* **both** for a boy **who is nine years** and one day old as well as a twenty-year old who has not developed two pubic hairs. In both cases, their sexual relations are not considered proper intercourse with regard to levirate marriage. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** against this from the following source: With regard to a man **twenty years old who has not developed two** pubic **hairs, they must bring proof that he is twenty years old, and** he **is** established as **a eunuch,** who **may neither perform *ḥalitza* nor perform levirate marriage. Likewise, in the case of a woman who is twenty years old and has not developed two** pubic **hairs, they must bring proof that she is twenty years old, and** that **she is a sexually underdeveloped woman,** who **may neither perform *ḥalitza* nor enter into levirate marriage.** This shows that the status of a nine-year-old male and a twenty-year old man without pubic hairs are not the same, as the intercourse of a nine-year-old is considered of some significance, whereas that of a eunuch is entirely disregarded, as he may not even perform *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* returns to the question of the *kattan* who reaches the age of twenty without having developed pubic hair. The *Mishnah* treats such a person as still legally a *kattan*, still somewhat within the categories of *yibum* and *chalitzah*. But a *baraita* seems to say otherwise: if a twenty-year-old has not produced two pubic hairs, once it is established that he is twenty and lacks the hair, he is classified as the *saris* of whom the Rabbis spoke — exempt entirely from *chalitzah* and *yibum*. This would apparently undercut the *Mishnah*'s premise that a twenty-year-old without pubic hair still functions as a *kattan* in these matters.

The resolution:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב: וְהוּא שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ לוֹ סִימָנֵי סָרִיס. אָמַר רָבָא: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: וְהוּא הַסָּרִיס. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it stated with regard to this** *baraita* that **Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak said** that **Rav said: And this** *halakha* applies only **if he developed** other **signs of a eunuch** by the age of twenty. The mishna, by contrast, is referring to one who merely showed the signs of maturity at a late age. **Rava said:** The language of the *baraita* **is also precise, as it teaches: And he is the eunuch.** One can **learn from here** that this is referring to one who is definitely a eunuch.

It is not sufficient that the person be twenty and lack pubic hair; he must also manifest specific physiological signs of being a *saris*, which were discussed in earlier passages. Rava notes that the *baraita* itself says "and he is the *saris*," implying that additional identifying characteristics are required beyond age alone. The *Mishnah* therefore governs the case of a twenty-year-old who lacks pubic hair but does not display those other signs — such a person is still treated as a *kattan* who may yet reach maturity.

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי לֹא נוֹלְדוּ לוֹ סִימָנֵי סָרִיס, עַד כַּמָּה? תָּנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: עַד רוֹב שְׁנוֹתָיו. כִּי אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אִי כָּחוּשׁ אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ אַבְרְיוּהּ. וְאִי בָּרִיא אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ אַכְחֲשׁוּהּ. דְּהָנֵי סִימָנִין, זִמְנִין דְּנָתְרִי מֵחֲמַת כְּחִישׁוּתָא, וְזִמְנִין דְּנָתְרִי מֵחֲמַת בְּרִיּוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the *halakha* itself: **And** in a case **where he does not develop the signs of a eunuch, until what** age is he considered a minor? **The school of Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: Until most of his years** have passed, i.e., until he reaches the age of thirty-five, i.e., halfway to seventy, the standard length of a man's life. On the same issue, the Gemara relates: **When they would come before Rava** to inquire about someone who had reached the age of maturity but had not yet developed the physical signs, **if** the person in question was **thin, he would say to them: Go** and **fatten him** up before we decide on his status. **And if he** was **fat, he would say to them: Go** and **make him thin. As these signs,** the pubic hairs of maturity, **sometimes they fall off due to thinness and sometimes they fall off due to fatness.** It is therefore possible that after his bodily shape is adjusted he will develop the signs of maturity and will not have the status of a eunuch.

The *Gemara* then asks: if a person never develops those other signs of a *saris*, at what point must we conclude that he will never achieve biological maturity? Until the majority of his years — since seventy years is assumed to be the typical lifespan, once a person passes approximately thirty-six, he is presumed a *saris* even without the other specific indicators.

A practical dimension follows. When such cases were brought before Rava, he would not immediately declare the man a *saris*. If the person appeared lean and underfed, Rava would say: go and feed him properly first. Malnutrition can prevent the development of pubic hair just as it prevents menstruation in women who are severely undernourished. Conversely, if the man was overweight, Rava would prescribe an exercise regimen to reduce his weight. These signs sometimes fail to appear due to being underfed, and sometimes due to being overfed. Only after addressing those external factors could a proper determination be made.

### Ha-Isha Rabba End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הָאִשָּׁה רַבָּה
>
> **Translation:** 

And with that, the *perek* known as *Ha'isha Rabba* comes to its conclusion. It is called *Ha'isha Rabba* — the "great" *Ha'isha* — to distinguish it from a later chapter also beginning with *Ha'isha*, which is called *Ha'isha Shalom*. It was a rich and challenging *perek*, covering the case of the husband who returned from afar, then pivoting to the wife in the analogous situation, and ultimately returning to the core discussions of *yibum* centered on the underage young man whose *bi'ah* generates such complex legal consequences.

## Oneis & Mefutah

### Non-Marital Relations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹשְׂאִין עַל הָאֲנוּסָה וְעַל הַמְפוּתָּה, הָאוֹנֵס וְהַמְפַתֶּה עַל הַנְּשׂוּאָה — חַיָּיב. נוֹשֵׂא אָדָם אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו וּמְפוּתַּת אָבִיו, אֲנוּסַת בְּנוֹ וּמְפוּתַּת בְּנוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹסֵר בַּאֲנוּסַת אָבִיו וּמְפוּתַּת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may marry** a relative, e.g., the sister or the mother, **of the woman he raped and of the woman he seduced. However, one who rapes and one who seduces** a relative **of the woman who is married** to him **is liable** to receive capital punishment or *karet* for engaging in prohibited sexual intercourse, depending on the particular family relationship. **A man may marry a woman raped by his father, or a woman seduced by his father,** or **a woman raped by his son, or a woman seduced by his son. Rabbi Yehuda prohibits** marriage **in** the case of **a woman raped by his father or a woman seduced by his father.**

With the opening of the new *perek*, the discussion shifts from *yibum* to the subject of familial prohibitions arising from non-marital sexual relations. The *Mishnah* opens with a fundamental principle: one may marry a woman who is related to him only through an act of rape or seduction, meaning through a sexual relationship that was not a marriage.

The underlying principle is this: the prohibitions of wife's sister, wife's mother, and wife's daughter are predicated on the existence of a *wife* — on there having been a marriage. These prohibited relationships are defined by the man's marriage to the first woman. If no marriage took place, if the man merely had sexual relations with a woman outside of marriage — labeled by the *Mishnah* using the legal categories of *anusa* or *mefuta*, rape or seduction, since these are the two cases the Torah explicitly addresses involving non-marital intercourse — then the relational prohibitions that attach through wifehood do not apply. He may marry her sister, mother, or daughter. This is the rule of *nosein al ha-anusa*: when the first relationship was not a marriage, the subsequent relationship with the relative is permitted *de'oraita*.

### Father's Exception

The *Mishnah* then considers cases involving not the man's own wife but his father's or son's relationships. The *Tanna Kamma* holds: a man may marry a woman his father merely slept with, since she was never the father's wife. Similarly, he may marry a woman his son merely slept with, since she was never the son's wife. The prohibition of stepmother and daughter-in-law requires an actual marriage; without marriage, the relationship does not generate the prohibition.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees in one specific case: Rabbi Yehuda prohibits a man from marrying a woman his father had sexual relations with, even outside of marriage. This exception for the father's partner, while not shared by the *Tanna Kamma*, has some resonance with the prophetic condemnation in Amos, where father and son going to the same woman is condemned as a profound moral degradation — even if it does not rise to the level of a formal *issur* of *arayot*.

### Daughter Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָנַס אִשָּׁה — מוּתָּר לִישָּׂא בִּתָּהּ, נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה — אָסוּר לִישָּׂא בִּתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **We learned** in the mishna **that which the Sages teach** in a *baraita*: If a man **raped a woman, he is permitted to marry her daughter. If he married a woman, it is prohibited** for him **to marry her daughter.**

A *baraita* makes the principle more explicit: if a man had forced sexual relations with a woman, he is permitted to marry her daughter. The lack of marriage means that the woman-daughter chain of relationship never became a prohibited chain of *she'erah* — biological or marital kinship of the relevant type.

### Suspected Affair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַנִּטְעָן מִן הָאִשָּׁה — אָסוּר בְּאִמָּהּ וּבְבִתָּהּ וּבַאֲחוֹתָהּ! מִדְּרַבָּנַן. וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא אִיסּוּרָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן תָּנֵי נוֹשְׂאִין לְכַתְּחִלָּה?! כִּי תְּנַן מַתְנִיתִין — לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** However, the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: **One who is alleged** to have engaged in intercourse **with** a particular **woman is prohibited** from marriage **with her mother, and with her daughter, and with her sister.** Apparently, as a consequence of non-marital intercourse, there is a prohibition against the man marrying the woman's relatives. The Gemara answers: This is merely prohibited by **rabbinic** decree, lest the man continue to engage in intercourse with this particular woman after marrying one of her relatives, thereby transgressing a Torah prohibition. The Gemara asks: **And anywhere that there is a rabbinic prohibition,** does the mishna **teach** that **one may marry** the woman ***ab initio*?** Since the marriage is prohibited by rabbinic decree, the mishna should have taught that if he marries her, he is exempt from punishment. The Gemara answers: **When we learned** in **the mishna** that he may marry her *ab initio*, it was dealing with their marriage **after** the **death** of the woman that he raped or seduced. The rabbinic prohibition does not relate to this case, as concern with regard to forbidden relatives is not relevant there.

The *Gemara* then raises an apparent challenge from a different *baraita*: a man suspected of having an affair with a woman is prohibited from marrying her mother, daughter, or sister. This seems to contradict the principle just stated. If a man who actually slept with a woman may marry her relatives, why should a man *suspected* of an affair be *more* restricted?

The answer is that this prohibition is *mi-derabbanan* — rabbinic, not biblical. And the rationale is not difficult to see: if the suspicion has some basis, and the man proceeds to marry the woman's mother or daughter, it is likely that he will continue his relationship with the original woman after the marriage. At that point, he will be guilty of a biblical prohibition — sleeping with his wife's mother or daughter. The Rabbis therefore prohibited the marriage preemptively to prevent it from leading to a *de'oraita* violation.

But would the *Mishnah* then use the language of *nosein l'chatchilah* — permitted outright — in a case where there is at least a rabbinic concern? The *Gemara* resolves this: our *Mishnah* is speaking of a case where the first woman has already died. Once she is dead, the concern that the man will continue sleeping with her no longer applies, and the rabbinic prohibition accordingly falls away. He may freely marry her mother, daughter, or sister.

### Death Removes Ties

This leads to a clarification of the overall structure. The fundamental biblical scheme is: first there is a marriage to woman one, and then an act of sex with woman two — the wife's sister, mother, or daughter — is what is prohibited. The *Mishnah* establishes that the reverse — first sex with woman one, then marriage with woman two — generates no *de'oraita* prohibition, since the relational chain begins with sex rather than marriage. The *baraita* about suspected affairs then adds that even this permitted path is rabbinically restricted during the first woman's lifetime, lest it lead to the prohibited reverse scenario. Once the first woman dies, however, even the rabbinic concern dissolves entirely, and the marriage is fully permitted.

The *Mishnah* employs the legal terminology of *anusa* and *mefuta* because those are the two categories through which the Torah addresses non-marital intercourse. In practice, for the purposes of this *halacha*, the distinction between them is irrelevant — both simply denote sexual relations outside of marriage.

## Kichah vs. Shichvah

### Terminology Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּכוּלָּן נֶאֱמַר שְׁכִיבָה, וְכָאן נֶאֱמַר קִיחָה, לוֹמַר לְךָ: דֶּרֶךְ לִיקּוּחִין אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the mishna's ruling, **from where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught: With** regard to **all** other prohibited sexual relations, **lying is stated, whereas here,** with regard to a man's intercourse with his wife's relatives, **taking is stated.** This is **to tell you** that the **Torah prohibited** intercourse with these relatives only **through taking,** i.e., the man's acquisition of his wife through marriage. It did not prohibit intercourse with the relative of a woman with whom he engaged in nonmarital relations.

The *Gemara* now investigates the textual basis for the entire framework: why does the prohibition depend on whether the first relationship was a marriage? By all the *arayot*, the Torah uses the language of *shichivah* — lying with, i.e., sexual intercourse. But in the specific cases of wife, mother, and daughter-in-law (and sister), the Torah uses the language of *kichah* — taking, i.e., marriage. The verse in Leviticus reads: "A man who *takes* a woman and her mother"; and "His sister he shall not *take*." The Rabbis derive from this terminological distinction: the Torah's prohibition operates through the framework of marriage. Where the text says *kichah*, the operative relationship is a marital one; only once marriage is in place do the prohibited acts of *shichivah* with the relatives come into force.

### Rava Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, גַּבֵּי אֲחוֹתוֹ דִּכְתִיב: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִקַּח אֶת אֲחוֹתוֹ בַּת אָבִיו אוֹ בַת אִמּוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דֶּרֶךְ קִיחָה הוּא דַּאֲסִיר, דֶּרֶךְ שְׁכִיבָה שְׁרֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: However, if** that interpretation is **so,** then **with regard to** intercourse with **one's sister,** concerning whom **it is written: "And if a man takes his sister, his father's daughter, or his mother's daughter"** (Leviticus 20:17), you must **also** say that is it is only intercourse **through taking,** i.e., marriage, that is **prohibited. But intercourse through lying,** i.e., without marriage, is **permitted.** How can this be?

Rava then poses a sharp question to Abaye: if the use of *kichah* in these passages means the prohibition is triggered only through marriage, consider the prohibition of a man's sister, where the verse reads: "A man who *takes* his sister." Would one say, by the same logic, that it is only prohibited to *marry* one's sister, but sexual relations with her outside of marriage would be permitted? Clearly not. So how can *kichah* be the operative trigger?

### Context Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִיקּוּחִין כְּתִיבִי בַּתּוֹרָה סְתָם. הָרָאוּי לְקִיחָה — קִיחָה, הָרָאוּי לִשְׁכִיבָה — שְׁכִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: When the term **taking is stated in the Torah without specification,** it is interpreted in accordance with the context. With regard to the context of a relationship **that has potential for taking** through marriage, this verb is interpreted as **taking** through marriage. In the context of a relationship **that has potential** only **for lying,** as their marriage would be invalid, taking the woman is understood to mean **lying** with her.

The resolution: the word *kichah* in the Torah is generic; it can mean marriage or, depending on context, simply an act of sexual taking. Where the woman in question is one who could in principle be married, *kichah* means marriage in the full sense. This applies to the cases of wife, mother, and daughter-in-law, where a prior legitimate marriage is what establishes the relational framework. Where the woman is one who could never be married — as with one's sister, where marriage is simply impossible halachically — then *kichah* in that context means *shichivah*, sexual intercourse. The prohibition against one's sister attaches to any act of intercourse, not to a marital act that can never occur.

The approach is admittedly not perfectly clean — *kichah* does not always mean what it most naturally means — but the distinction is workable: in cases where marriage is a meaningful legal possibility, *kichah* means marriage; where it is not, *kichah* is read as referring to sexual relations.

## Granddaughter Rules

### Conflicting Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אָנַס אִשָּׁה — מוּתָּר לִישָּׂא בִּתָּהּ מֵהָכָא, כְּתִיב: ״עֶרְוַת בַּת בִּנְךָ אוֹ בַת בִּתְּךָ לֹא תְגַלֶּה״, הָא בַּת בְּנָהּ דִּידַהּ, וּבַת בִּתָּהּ דִּידַהּ — גַּלִּי, וּכְתִיב: ״עֶרְוַת אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ לֹא תְגַלֵּה אֶת בַּת בְּנָהּ וְאֶת בַּת בִּתָּהּ לֹא תִקַּח״, הָא כֵּיצַד? כָּאן בָּאוֹנָסִין, כָּאן בְּנִשּׂוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The *halakha* that if a man **raped a woman it is permitted** for him **to marry her daughter** is derived **from here. It is stated: "The nakedness of your son's daughter, or of your daughter's daughter, even their nakedness…you may not uncover"** (Leviticus 18:10). It may be inferred from here that **the daughter of** the woman's **own son** from a different relationship, **and the daughter of her own daughter,** may be **uncovered,** i.e., intercourse with them is not prohibited. **And it is** also **written: "You may not uncover the nakedness of a woman and her daughter; you may not take her son's daughter, or her daughter's daughter, to uncover her nakedness; they are near kinswomen, it is lewdness"** (Leviticus 18:17). **How so?** How can the two verses be reconciled? Evidently, the former verse is stated **with regard to rape.** A man may marry the daughter or granddaughter of the woman he raped, provided they are not his own offspring. The latter verse is stated **with regard to marriage.** Marrying the relatives of one's wife is prohibited.

The *Gemara* now examines the case of a granddaughter through two apparently contradictory verses. The verse in Vayikra reads: do not expose the nakedness of your son's daughter or your daughter's daughter. The plain sense seems to refer to one's own biological granddaughter. A separate verse reads: you may not expose the nakedness of a woman and her daughter, and you shall not take her son's daughter or her daughter's daughter. This second verse refers to *the wife's* granddaughter — a step-granddaughter who is not biologically the man's own.

How do we reconcile these? The first verse prohibits one's own biological granddaughter, and that prohibition exists regardless of marriage — it is a bond of *she'er*, of flesh and biological kinship. The second verse prohibits the *wife's* granddaughter — but only when there is a marriage to the grandmother. If the relationship with the grandmother was only sexual and not marital, the wife's granddaughter is not a step-granddaughter in any meaningful sense and is therefore permitted.

### Sheir Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא? עֲרָיוֹת ״שְׁאֵר״ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ. בְּנִשּׂוּאִין אִיכָּא שְׁאֵר, בָּאוֹנָסִין לֵיכָּא שְׁאֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Perhaps **I should reverse** the resolution of the contradiction between the verses and state that the daughter or granddaughter of his rape victim is forbidden, whereas the relatives of his wife are permitted. The Gemara answers: With** regard to **those with whom intercourse is forbidden,** the concept of **kinship is stated** (see Leviticus 18:13, 17). **In marriage there is kinship,** whereas **in** the case of **rape there is no kinship.** Therefore, the prohibition against intercourse with a woman and her daughter or granddaughter is clearly referring to the daughter and granddaughter of one's wife and not of the woman he raped.

One might ask whether to read the verses in reverse — prohibiting the wife's granddaughter in cases of non-marital relations and perhaps permitting the step-granddaughter in cases of marriage. But the *Gemara* rejects this: the prohibitions of *arayot* use the term *she'er* — *she'erah henah*, the flesh of one's flesh. In marriage, a step-relationship carries a degree of *she'er*, even if it is relational rather than biological. In a purely sexual relationship outside of marriage, the granddaughter of that woman is not one's biological granddaughter, is not one's step-granddaughter, and cannot be described as *she'er* in any meaningful sense.

The concept of *she'er* thus does the decisive work in this analysis. Biological kinship always carries *she'er*; marital kinship generates a kind of derivative *she'er* through the step-relationship; but a purely sexual relationship, absent marriage, generates no *she'er* at all. The entire framework of what is prohibited and what is permitted in cases of non-marital relations flows from this principle.

## Father & Son

### Shomeret Yavam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹסֵר בַּאֲנוּסַת אָבִיו וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יִקַּח אִישׁ אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו וְלֹא יְגַלֶּה כְּנַף אָבִיו״, כָּנָף שֶׁרָאָה אָבִיו לֹא יְגַלֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda prohibits** marrying **a woman raped by his father** or a woman seduced by his father. **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: What is the reasoning** for the ruling **of Rabbi Yehuda?** It is **as it is written: "A man may not take his father's wife, and may not uncover his father's skirt"** (Deuteronomy 23:1), meaning that **one may not uncover** the **skirt that his father has seen.**

The *Gemara* records that Rabbi Yose prohibits a man from marrying a woman his father had sexual relations with — a position aligned with Rabbi Yehuda's view in the *Mishnah*.

Rav Gidel cites Rav in explanation of Rabbi Yehuda's position: the verse in Devarim first prohibits marrying one's stepmother, and then adds the prohibition of *lo yigaleh knaf aviv* — do not uncover the hem of the father. The *knaf ha-av* — the father's hem — refers to the nakedness that the father himself saw: the woman with whom the father had sexual relations.

### Rabbanan Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּבַאֲנוּסָה כְּתִיב — דִּכְתִיב מֵעִילָּוֵיהּ דִּקְרָא: ״וְנָתַן הָאִישׁ הַשּׁוֹכֵב עִמָּהּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה חֲמִשִּׁים כָּסֶף״. וְרַבָּנַן: אִי הֲוָה סְמִיךְ לֵיהּ — כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ. הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא סְמִיךְ לֵיהּ, מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב עָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַב עָנָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁל אָבִיו הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. וּמַאי ״כְּנַף אָבִיו״ — כָּנָף הָרָאוּי לְאָבִיו לֹא יְגַלֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it apparent **that** this verse **is written with regard to a woman raped** by his father and not with regard to his father's wife? It is **as it is written** just **before that verse,** with regard to rape: **"And the man who lay with her shall give the young woman's father fifty shekels of silver"** (Deuteronomy 22:29). **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda, respond to this proof? **If** the prohibition stated **had been juxtaposed with** the verse dealing with rape, it would be **as you said.** However, **now that it is not juxtaposed with it,** as there is another prohibition in between, namely "a man may not take his father's wife" (Deuteronomy 23:1), the prohibition of "he may not uncover his father's skirt" **is necessary to** teach **that which Rav Anan** taught, **as Rav Anan said** that **Shmuel said: The verse is speaking of a widow waiting for her brother-in-law,** who is this man's **father,** to perform levirate marriage. **And what** is the meaning of the phrase **"his father's skirt"? The skirt that is potentially his father's, he may not uncover.**

The Rabbis, however, reject this reading. If the prohibition of *knaf aviv* were directly juxtaposed to the earlier passage dealing with rape and payment to the father, one could concede that the context establishes that *knaf aviv* refers to a woman the father merely had sexual relations with. But since those passages appear much earlier in the text and are not immediately adjacent, the juxtaposition argument fails. Instead, the Rabbis read the verse in accordance with *Rav Anan* in the name of Shmuel: the verse is speaking of a *shomeret yavam*, a woman who stands in a quasi-marital *zekah* relationship with the father, awaiting *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The prohibition of *lo yigaleh knaf aviv* refers to this woman — the father's *shomeret yavam* — not to a woman with whom the father merely had casual sexual relations. The *knaf* appropriate to the father refers to the woman who is fit to be, in some legal sense, the father's wife.

### Aunt Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דּוֹדָתוֹ! לַעֲבוֹר עָלֶיהָ בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם יְבָמָה לַשּׁוּק! לַעֲבוֹר עָלֶיהָ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה לָאוִין. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara immediately raises an objection: if the *shomeret yavam* of the father is the father's brother's wife, then she is already the man's *dodah* — his aunt — and as such she is already prohibited under the *de'oraita* prohibition of *arayot*, which carries *karet*. Why would one need an additional verse to establish a prohibition that is already in place? **To add a second *lav* to the transgression,** yielding two counts of prohibition. Furthermore, this woman is also forbidden as *yevama lashuk* — any woman who is a *shomeret yavam* is prohibited to other men until the *yibum* or *chalitzah* is completed. **That gives three *lavin* simultaneously. The Gemara offers a final possible resolution:** **Or one could say the verse is addressing the case after the father's death.** Once the father has died, she is no longer a *yevama lashuk* — if there was only one brother with the obligation and he has now died, the *zekah* lapses. She remains, however, a *dodah*, an aunt, and the *knaf aviv* prohibition adds a third *lav* even at that stage. **Three counts of prohibition are nonetheless established.**

## Relationship Riddles

### Stepdaughter Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַח מֵאַב וְלֹא מֵאֵם, וְהוּא בַּעֲלַהּ דְּאֵם, וַאֲנָא בְּרַתַּהּ דְּאִנְתְּתֵיהּ״. אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּמַתְנִיתִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The opinion that a man may marry a woman raped or seduced by his father can lead to the existence of an unusual family relationship. A woman says: I have a half **brother from my father and not from my mother, and** my half brother **is the husband of my mother, and I am the daughter of his wife. Rami bar Ḥama said:** This state of affairs is **not** legitimate **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda in the mishna,** who holds that a man may not marry a woman with whom his father engaged in intercourse, even if they were not married. However, according to the Rabbis, a woman whose father was not married to her mother can legitimately have a paternal half brother who is married to her mother.

Having concluded the legal analysis, the *Gemara* turns to a remarkable series of family-relationship riddles — puzzles about how a single person can bear multiple overlapping legal relationships to another. These were likely posed as intellectual exercises among *talmidei chachamim*, testing their ability to reconstruct unusual genealogical scenarios. As one observer once put it, this is what the Rabbis did before television.

The first riddle: he is my half-brother on my father's side but not on my mother's, he is the husband of my mother, and I am the daughter of his wife. Rashi explains: my father's son — my half-brother — married my mother. He is therefore my half-brother (through the father) and simultaneously my stepfather (through his marriage to my mother). I am his wife's daughter — his stepdaughter. And crucially, Rami bar Chama notes that this scenario is only possible if we do not follow Rabbi Yehuda's position in our *Mishnah*. Because the father must have had sexual relations with this man's mother without marrying her — otherwise the son could never have married his own stepmother. That makes her *anusat aviv*, and according to Rabbi Yehuda, this son would be prohibited from marrying her. Only the *Tanna Kamma*, who permits *anusat aviv*, allows the scenario to work.

### Son-Brother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָח הוּא, וּבְרִי הוּא, אֲחָתֵיהּ אֲנָא דְּהַאי דְּדָרֵינָא אַכַּתְפַּאי״ — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּגוֹי הַבָּא עַל בִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another riddle about a bizarre family relationship. A woman says: **He is my brother and he is my son; I am the sister of this one, whom I carry on my shoulders.** What is the solution? **You find it in** the case of **a gentile who engaged in intercourse with his daughter,** and she bore him a son, who is therefore both her brother and her son. The Gemara is referring to a gentile because it does not wish to entertain the idea that a Jew would act in such a manner.

The second riddle: he is my brother and my son; I am his sister; I am the sister of the one I am carrying on my shoulders. In other words: my son is my brother. How? The scenario involves a non-Jew who had sexual relations with his own daughter. Since the discussion of forbidden relations among non-Jews in this context does not carry the same formal prohibitions, the *Gemara* constructs these riddles using non-Jewish actors to avoid any implication that a Jew engaged in such conduct. The child born of this union is the mother's son and simultaneously her sibling, since both share the same father. She is thus the sister of the one she carries.

### Aunt Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁלָמָא לָךְ בְּרִי, בַּת אֲחָתִיךְ אֲנָא״ — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּגוֹי הַבָּא עַל בַּת בִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another riddle: **Peace upon you, my son; I am the daughter of your sister. You find** the solution **in** the case of **a gentile who engaged in intercourse with the daughter of his daughter,** who bore him a son. This son's mother is related to him from her mother's side as well, as she is his sister's daughter.

The third riddle: greetings, my friend; I am the daughter of your sister. Meaning, this person's daughter is his sister's daughter — his niece through his sister. A non-Jew who had sexual relations with his granddaughter. The child born of this union is simultaneously the grandfather's daughter — and therefore the father's sister — and the mother's daughter. The daughter of the mother is therefore the father's sister's daughter — his niece on his sister's side. The child's own child stands in the relationship of "daughter of your sister" to the grandfather-father.

### Multi-Relation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דַּלָּאֵי דְּדָלוּ דַּוְולָא, לִיפּוֹל בְּכוּ סְתַר פְּתַר: דְּהַאי דְּדָרֵינָא הוּא בַּר, וַאֲנָא בְּרַת אֲחוּהּ״ — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּגוֹי הַבָּא עַל בַּת בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another riddle: **Water drawers, who draw** water **in buckets** to irrigate fields, **let** this **cryptic** riddle **fall among you: This** boy **whom I carry is my son, and I am the daughter of his brother. You find** the solution **in** the case of **a gentile who engaged in intercourse with the daughter of his son,** as their son is also her uncle.

The fourth riddle is the most elaborate. A woman says: "Water, water! Woe, woe! He is a brother and he is a father and he is a husband and he is the son of a husband and he is a stepfather, and I am his wife's daughter, and yet he will not feed the orphaned brothers who are the sons of his daughter."

A non-Jew had sexual relations with his mother and produced a daughter. He then had sexual relations with that daughter and had children with her. From the perspective of the daughter: this man is her biological father and brother (since he was born of his mother, and she shares the same mother); he is her husband (since he had sexual relations with her); and since his father also had sexual relations with her, he is the son of her husband as well. He is thus simultaneously her brother, father, husband, and husband's son. And he withholds food from the orphaned sons — who are at once his children and the sons of his daughter.

### Cousins Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲנָא וְאַתְּ אַחֵי, אֲנָא וַאֲבוּךְ אַחֵי, אֲנָא וְאִמָּךְ אַחֵי״ — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּגוֹי הַבָּא עַל אִמּוֹ וְהוֹלִיד מִמֶּנָּה שְׁתֵּי בָנוֹת, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל אַחַת מֵהֶן, וְהוֹלִיד מִמֶּנָּה בֵּן, וְקָרְיָא לֵיהּ אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּאִימָּא וְקָאָמְרָה לֵיהּ הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another riddle: **You and I are siblings; your father and I are siblings; your mother and I are siblings. You find** the solution **in** the case of **a gentile who engaged in intercourse with his mother, and she bore him two daughters, and he then engaged in intercourse with one of them, and she bore him a son. And the sister of** the son's **mother calls him and says this** statement **to him,** as she is his sister from his father's side and his father's sister from their mother's side, and she is his mother's sister from both sides.

The fifth riddle: you and I are siblings; you and your father are siblings; you and your mother are siblings — meaning, I and your mother are siblings. A non-Jew had sexual relations with his mother and produced two daughters. He then had sexual relations with one of the daughters and had a son. The other daughter says to the son: you and I are siblings — we share the same father. I and your father are siblings — we also share the same father. I and your mother are siblings — your mother and I are sisters, born of the same mother.

### First Cousins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲנָא וְאַתְּ — בְּנֵי אַחֵי, אֲנָא וַאֲבוּךְ — בְּנֵי אַחֵי, אֲנָא וְאִמָּךְ — בְּנֵי אַחֵי״. הָא בְּהֶיתֵּירָא נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — כְּגוֹן רְאוּבֵן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי בָנוֹת, וַאֲתָא שִׁמְעוֹן וּנְסַב חֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ, וַאֲתָא בַּר לֵוִי וּנְסַב חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ בְּרֵיהּ דְּשִׁמְעוֹן לְבַר בְּרֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another riddle: **You and I are cousins; your father and I are cousins; your mother and I are cousins. You find** the solution to **this** riddle **in a permitted** manner **as well. For example, Reuven, who has two daughters, and** his brother **Shimon came and married one of them, and the son of Levi,** the third brother, **came and married one of them,** the other **one of them. And the son of Shimon says** this statement **to the grandson of Levi.** They are cousins from their mothers' sides, Shimon's son and Levi's son are cousins from their fathers' sides, and Shimon's son and the mother of Levi's grandson are cousins from their fathers' sides.

The sixth riddle is, by comparison, almost refreshingly simple: I and you are the children of siblings (i.e., first cousins); I and your father are first cousins; I and your mother are first cousins. The *Gemara* notes that this case does not require any incest scenario at all — you can construct it from entirely regular marriages. For instance, Reuven has two daughters. Shimon marries one of them. Levi's son marries the other. Then Shimon's son says to Levi's grandson: you and I are first cousins (our mothers are sisters); I and your father are first cousins (he is Levi's son and I am Shimon's son, whose mothers were sisters); and I and your mother are first cousins (your mother is Reuven's daughter, and so is my mother).

### I Am My Grandpa

The *Gemara*'s series of riddles calls to mind a famous American novelty song — *I Am My Own Grandpa* — which presents a superficially similar tangle of relationships arising from perfectly legal marriages:

> When I was 23, I was married to a widow who was pretty as can be. This widow had a grown-up daughter who had hair of red. My father fell in love with her and soon they, too, were wed. This made my dad my son-in-law and changed my very life. My daughter was my mother 'cause she was my father's wife. To complicate the matters, even though it brought me joy, I soon became the father of a bouncing baby boy. My little baby then became a brother-in-law to dad, and so became my uncle, though it made me very sad. For if he was my uncle, then that also made him brother of the widow's grown-up daughter who of course was my stepmother. Father's wife then had a son, kept him on the run, and he became my grandchild, for he was my daughter's son. My wife is now my mother's mother, and it makes me blue, because although she is my wife, she's my grandmother too. If my wife is my grandmother, then I'm her grandchild, and every time I think of it, it nearly drives me wild. For now I have become the strangest case you ever saw. As husband of my grandmother, I am my own grandpa!

The crucial distinction, of course, is that the *Gemara*'s riddles required incest — conducted, in its framing, by non-Jews — to generate the overlapping relationships, whereas the song achieves its tangle entirely through regular and entirely permitted marriages. All above board. Though, as is worth noting, in most jurisdictions at present, not all of the marriages involved would be legally permitted.
