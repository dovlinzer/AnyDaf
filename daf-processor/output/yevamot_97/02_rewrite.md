# Yevamot 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Underage Kiddushin

### Bi'ah of Minor

The daf begins at the middle of 97b. The preceding *Mishnah* had established a case involving a young man who is nine years and a day old or more — past the threshold of *bi'ah* taking legal effect, yet still before *bar mitzvah* — whose act of intercourse with his sister-in-law functions as a kind of *ma'amar* but does not constitute *yibum* in the full halachic sense. The scenario that follows is this: if such an underage man performs *bi'ah* with his sister-in-law and subsequently dies, the next surviving brother faces what the *Mishnah* calls *zekat shnei yevamim* — an obligation to two sisters-in-law simultaneously. The first *mitzvah* of *yibum*, deriving from the original marriage that was never actualized through proper *yibum*, remains outstanding; and a second, *derabbanan* obligation to the deceased minor's quasi-wife also attaches. The result is that the surviving brother is not permitted to perform *yibum* at all, and instead *chalitzah* is required.

### No Co-Wife Case

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with Rabbah's clarification: *Amar Rabbah: Hada amar rabbanan zekat shnei yevamim mechelatz chaletz yevamim lo meyabbem* — the rabbinic rule that a woman bearing two *zekot* from two brothers-in-law requires *chalitzah* and not *yibum*. Rabbah stresses that one should not think this ruling is limited to cases where there is a co-wife. The prior discussions in an earlier *perek* had involved a co-wife scenario, and one might have supposed that the restriction was tied specifically to that concern. Rabbah therefore insists: *lo teima heicha de'ika tzara*, do not say it applies only where there is a co-wife. In our case — *leka tzara*, there is no co-wife — and the rule of *chalitzah* rather than *yibum* still applies.

To make the case concrete: using the paradigm of Er, Onan, and Shelah — if Onan, as an underage *kattan*, performed *bi'ah* with Tamar, this creates a *derabbanan* quasi-marriage but not a *de'oraita yibum*. When Onan then dies, Shelah's obligation to Tamar has two dimensions: a *de'oraita* obligation stemming from Er's genuine marriage to her, which was never resolved through *yibum* or *chalitzah*; and a *derabbanan* obligation stemming from Onan's quasi-marriage. Because Onan's *bi'ah* was not *de'oraita*, it did not displace Er from the picture. Tamar therefore stands under two *zekot*, and the *halacha* is *chalitzah*, not *yibum*. In this version of the case there is no co-wife at all — that is Rabbah's point.

### Appearance Concern

What then is the rationale for the restriction in the co-wife case that Rabbah is distinguishing from? In that scenario, where husband number two had done *ma'amar* and was also married to another woman, the concern is one of appearances. If the surviving brother performs *yibum* with the first woman — legitimately, on account of the *de'oraita* obligation from husband number one — it will appear as though he has performed *yibum* with two wives of the same deceased husband. People will not understand that one *yibum* is being done on account of Er and the other would have been on account of Onan. The obvious resolution of doing *yibum* with one and *chalitzah* with the other is itself rejected, because regardless of which direction one goes, the appearance of *yibum* with two women of the same deceased man will arise. The restriction on *yibum* in the co-wife case is therefore driven by this appearance concern. Rabbah's contribution is to establish that even in the absence of a co-wife — where that concern does not apply — the rule of *chalitzah* rather than *yibum* holds.

### Regular Marriage Rules

The *Gemara* then adds a brief but important note: if this *kattan* had simply contracted a normal marriage — *kiddushin* and *nisuin* — with a woman, and then died, that marriage carries no legal weight and the woman has no obligation of *chalitzah* or *yibum* with his brothers whatsoever.

## Capacity & Status

### Shotim Exemption

This point is underscored by a *tannaic* teaching: *shoteh* — a person of unsound mind — and *katan* — a minor — who contracted marriage and died: *neshoteihem peturot min hachalitzah umin hayibum*, their wives are entirely exempt from both *chalitzah* and *yibum*. The marriage of a *shoteh* or *katan* carries no halachic recognition. The *Gemara* notes that the classic triad of those lacking legal capacity — *shoteh*, *katan*, and *cheresh* — is only partially represented here, since the Rabbis did institute *kiddushin* and *nisuin* for a *cheresh* at least on a rabbinic level, which is why the *cheresh* is treated differently and not mentioned in this context.

## Underage Kiddushin (II)

### Rav Ashi vs. Shmuel

The *Gemara* now takes up a case where the underage Onan did *bi'ah* with Tamar but was also married to Devorah. If he subsequently comes of age and consummates his relationship with Tamar as an adult, she becomes a genuine wife alongside Devorah. But if he did not consummate the relationship with Tamar as an adult and then dies — Devorah is a real wife, and Shelah may perform *yibum* with her; Tamar, however, was only rabbinically acquired, and Shelah must do *chalitzah* with her. The reason Tamar's *chalitzah* cannot be replaced by *yibum* with Devorah alone — the normal resolution in a *zekat shnei yevamim* scenario — is that the *de'oraita* obligation stemming from Er has never been resolved.

This prompts the *Gemara* to ask from an earlier passage: if the *bi'ah* of a *kattan* functions like a *ma'amar begadol*, then by analogy to the *ma'amar* case, shouldn't the surviving brother be required to do *chalitzah* with both Tamar and Devorah, not *yibum* with Devorah and *chalitzah* with Tamar?

*Amar Rav Ashi: Lo asu biat ben tesha kemamar begadol* — Rav Ashi says that the Sages did not equate the *bi'ah* of a nine-year-old fully with a *ma'amar* by an adult. It carries legal weight in various respects — enough to disqualify the brothers and produce other halachic consequences — but it is weaker than a full *ma'amar*, and therefore Devorah is not displaced from the picture. Shelah can still perform *yibum* with Devorah. Shmuel disagrees: *Ushemuel amar asu va'asu* — the *bi'ah* of the *kattan* is fully equivalent to a *ma'amar begadol*. Rabbi Yochanan likewise: *vechan amar Rabbi Yochanan asu va'asu*.

### Tannaitic Dispute

The *Gemara* then reframes the apparent contradiction between two *mishnayot* — one requiring *chalitzah* with both women, the other permitting *yibum* with the co-wife — not as a question about the strength of the minor's *bi'ah* relative to an adult's *ma'amar*, but as a *machloket tanna'im*.

The *tanna* of the *Perek Arba Achim*, who rules that the co-wife also requires *chalitzah*, made a *gezeirah* on account of the co-wife scenario: *gazar mishum tzara*. He ruled that *yibum* is entirely prohibited in order to prevent the appearance of *yibum* being performed with two women simultaneously. This *tanna* would say the same *halacha* whether the deceased second husband had performed a *ma'amar* as an adult or was a *kattan* whose *bi'ah* is the equivalent — the *gezeirah* applies equally. The *tanna* of our *Mishnah*, by contrast, does not make this *gezeirah* at all. He permits *yibum* with the co-wife Devorah even while requiring *chalitzah* with Tamar. This *tanna* would similarly permit *yibum* with a co-wife even in the adult *ma'amar* case.

The upshot is striking: the real dispute between the two *mishnayot* is not whether *bi'ah* of a *kattan* equals *ma'amar begadol* — both *tanna'im* would treat them identically — but whether one makes a *gezeirah* to prevent *yibum* entirely when there is a co-wife. Each *tanna* applies his view equally to both *kattan* and *gadol* cases; the apparent difference in the *mishnayot* arises only because each *tanna* chose the example most directly relevant to his discussion.

## Attribution Dispute

### Teaching Without Name

Rabbi Elazar, the student of Rabbi Yochanan, went and presented this entire analysis — the framing of the contradiction as a *machloket tanna'im* — in the *beit midrash*. However, *v'lo amrah mishmei d'Rabbi Yochanan* — he did not attribute the teaching to Rabbi Yochanan. When Rabbi Yochanan learned of this, *v'ikpad* — he was deeply offended that his *Torah* was being transmitted without his name.

### Tiberias Synagogue

Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi came to calm Rabbi Yochanan down. They invoked the famous episode of the synagogue in Tiberias. A dispute had arisen there between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yose concerning a bolt — *nagar sheyesh b'rosho glustra* — a door-bolt that has a rounded bulb at its end, making it usable for grinding and thus more of a *kli*, which would reduce concerns of *binyan* and *muktzah*. The question was whether one may insert it to lock a door on *Shabbat*. The debate between the two Sages became so heated that, while it was not intentional, a *sefer Torah* was torn in the tumult — *shenikra sefer Torah b'chamatan*. Rabbi Yose ben Kisma, who was present, declared: *temei'ani im lo yihyeh beit haknesset zu avodah zarah* — I would be utterly astonished if this synagogue does not ultimately become a house of idolatry. *V'chein havah* — and so it came to pass.

The point was plain: it is not worth allowing a dispute to escalate to such destructive ends. But when Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi made this comparison, it only made matters worse. *Hadar ikpeid zina achrina* — Rabbi Yochanan became still more angry, asking: *chavruta nami?* — are you comparing me to their situation, as if Rabbi Elazar is my colleague and peer? That dispute was between equals. Rabbi Elazar is my student. I have every right to be treated differently.

### Yehoshua Analogy

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi then came to try his hand at the matter. He cited the verse from Yehoshua: *Ka'asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe avdo kein tzivah Moshe et Yehoshua vecheyn asah Yehoshua, lo hesir davar mikol asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe*. The verse emphasizes how scrupulously Yehoshua carried out everything Moshe commanded. But, Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi asked: do you imagine that every time Yehoshua taught, he explicitly declared, "Here is what Moshe told me"? *Ela Yehoshua yoshev vedoresh stam* — Yehoshua would simply teach *Torah* without constant attribution. *Vehakol yod'im sheToraso shel Moshe hi* — everyone understood that everything he taught came from Moshe. The verse's very emphasis on his fidelity to Moshe demonstrated precisely that he did not need to label each teaching explicitly, because his role as the faithful channel of Moshe's *Torah* was self-evident.

*Af Rabbi Elazar talmidcha yoshev vedoresh stam* — similarly, your student Rabbi Elazar teaches without attribution, *vehakol yod'im sheshelcha hi* — and everyone knows that his *Torah* is yours.

This argument succeeded where the earlier attempt had failed. Rabbi Yochanan said to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi: *mipnei mah ein atem yod'in lepayeis keven Idi chaveireinu* — why did you not know how to appease me as Ben Idi does?

### Immortality via Torah

The *Gemara* then explains what lay at the root of Rabbi Yochanan's concern — and it was not mere pride. *De'amar Rabbi Yehuda amar Rav*: what does David mean when he says *agurah be'aholcha olamim* — I will dwell in Your tent in two worlds? Can a person actually dwell in two worlds? Rather, David was praying: *sheyomru davar shemuah mipi ba'olam hazeh* — that even after his death, people in this world would transmit his teachings in his name.

The source for why this matters: *De'amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: kol talmid chacham she'omrim davar shemuah mipi ba'olam hazeh siftosav dovvot bakever* — any *talmid chacham* whose *Torah* is transmitted in his name in this world, his lips continue to murmur in the grave. The continuity of attribution is not about honor; it is about a form of continued presence in this world, a way of remaining alive while dwelling in the world to come. *Amar Rabbi Yitzchak ben Zeira* — and what is the verse? *Vecheichech kayayin hatov holeich ledodi lemeisharim doveiv sifsei yeshenim* — and your palate is like fine wine, going straight to my beloved, causing the lips of those who sleep to murmur. *Kechomer shel anavim* — like a press of grapes: the moment one lays a finger upon pressed grapes, they immediately flow. So too with the *Torah* of *talmidei chachamim* — the moment it is spoken in their name, their lips begin to murmur in the grave.

Rabbi Yochanan's concern was not vanity but this: the transmission of *Torah* in a teacher's name is the mechanism by which a *talmid chacham* remains present in this world even after death. When his student taught his insight without attribution, Rabbi Yochanan felt that something vital had been severed — not his reputation, but his continued living connection to this world. The *Gemara* notes that the *pasuk* in *Shir Hashirim* invoked here is also from Shlomo, making it in some sense an apt citation in this context.

## Capacity & Status (II)

### Saris Definition

The *Gemara* returns to the question of the *kattan* who reaches the age of twenty without having developed pubic hair. The *Mishnah* treats such a person as still legally a *kattan*, still somewhat within the categories of *yibum* and *chalitzah*. But a *baraita* seems to say otherwise: *ben esrim shana shelo hevi shtei searot* — if a twenty-year-old has not produced two pubic hairs, *yaviu reaya shehu ben esrim vehu hasaris* — bring proof of his age, and once it is established that he is twenty and lacks the hair, he is classified as the *saris* of whom the Rabbis spoke — *lo choletz velo meyabem* — exempt entirely from *chalitzah* and *yibum*. This would apparently undercut the *Mishnah*'s premise that a twenty-year-old without pubic hair still functions as a *kattan* in these matters.

The resolution: *Amar Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak amar Rav: vehu shenoldu lo simanei saris* — it is not sufficient that the person be twenty and lack pubic hair; he must also manifest specific physiological signs of being a *saris*, which were discussed in earlier passages. *Amar Rava: deika nami d'katani vehu hasaris* — Rava notes that the *baraita* itself says "and he is the *saris*," implying that additional identifying characteristics are required beyond age alone. The *Mishnah* therefore governs the case of a twenty-year-old who lacks pubic hair but does not display those other signs — such a person is still treated as a *kattan* who may yet reach maturity.

### Rava's Ruling

The *Gemara* then asks: if a person never develops those other signs of a *saris*, at what point must we conclude that he will never achieve biological maturity? *Tani d'vei Rebbi Chiya: ad rov shnotav* — until the majority of his years. Since seventy years is assumed to be the typical lifespan, once a person passes approximately thirty-six, he is presumed a *saris* even without the other specific indicators.

A practical dimension follows. When such cases were brought before Rava, he would not immediately declare the man a *saris*. If the person appeared lean and underfed, *amar lehu zilu avriyuhu* — Rava would say: go and feed him properly first. Malnutrition can prevent the development of pubic hair just as it prevents menstruation in women who are severely undernourished. Conversely, if the man was overweight, *amar lehu zilu acchishuhu* — Rava would prescribe an exercise regimen to reduce his weight. *D'hanei simanei zimnin d'nafli machamat kachishuta vezimnin d'nafli machamat briyuta* — these signs sometimes fail to appear due to being underfed, and sometimes due to being overfed. Only after addressing those external factors could a proper determination be made.

### Ha-Isha Rabba End

*Hadran alach Ha'isha Rabba* — and with that, the *perek* known as *Ha'isha Rabba* comes to its conclusion. It is called *Ha'isha Rabba* — the "great" *Ha'isha* — to distinguish it from a later chapter also beginning with *Ha'isha*, which is called *Ha'isha Shalom*. It was a rich and challenging *perek*, covering the case of the husband who returned from afar, then pivoting to the wife in the analogous situation, and ultimately returning to the core discussions of *yibum* centered on the underage young man whose *bi'ah* generates such complex legal consequences.

## Oneis & Mefutah

### Non-Marital Relations

With the opening of the new *perek*, the discussion shifts from *yibum* to the subject of familial prohibitions arising from non-marital sexual relations. The *Mishnah* opens: *nosein al ha-anusa ve'al ha-mefuta* — one may marry a woman who is related to him only through an act of rape or seduction, meaning through a sexual relationship that was not a marriage.

The underlying principle is this: the prohibitions of wife's sister, wife's mother, and wife's daughter are predicated on the existence of a *wife* — on there having been a marriage. These prohibited relationships are defined by the man's marriage to the first woman. If no marriage took place, if the man merely had sexual relations with a woman outside of marriage — labeled by the *Mishnah* using the legal categories of *anusa* or *mefuta*, rape or seduction, since these are the two cases the Torah explicitly addresses involving non-marital intercourse — then the relational prohibitions that attach through wifehood do not apply. He may marry her sister, mother, or daughter. This is the rule of *nosein al ha-anusa*: when the first relationship was not a marriage, the subsequent relationship with the relative is permitted *de'oraita*.

### Father's Exception

The *Mishnah* then considers cases involving not the man's own wife but his father's or son's relationships. The *Tanna Kamma* holds: *nosei adam anusat aviv u-mefutat aviv* — a man may marry a woman his father merely slept with, since she was never the father's wife. Similarly, *anusat beno u-mefutat beno* — he may marry a woman his son merely slept with, since she was never the son's wife. The prohibition of stepmother and daughter-in-law requires an actual marriage; without marriage, the relationship does not generate the prohibition.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees in one specific case: *Rabbi Yehuda oser be-anusat aviv u-mefutat aviv* — Rabbi Yehuda prohibits a man from marrying a woman his father had sexual relations with, even outside of marriage. This exception for the father's partner, while not shared by the *Tanna Kamma*, has some resonance with the prophetic condemnation in Amos, where father and son going to the same woman is condemned as a profound moral degradation — even if it does not rise to the level of a formal *issur* of *arayot*.

### Daughter Prohibition

A *baraita* makes the principle more explicit: *oneis isha mutar liseis bita* — if a man had forced sexual relations with a woman, he is permitted to marry her daughter. The lack of marriage means that the woman-daughter chain of relationship never became a prohibited chain of *she'erah* — biological or marital kinship of the relevant type.

### Suspected Affair

The *Gemara* then raises an apparent challenge from a different *baraita*: *ha-nichshad min ha-isha assur be-imah u-ve-vitah ve-achotah* — a man suspected of having an affair with a woman is prohibited from marrying her mother, daughter, or sister. This seems to contradict the principle just stated. If a man who actually slept with a woman may marry her relatives, why should a man *suspected* of an affair be *more* restricted?

The answer is that this prohibition is *mi-derabbanan* — rabbinic, not biblical. And the rationale is not difficult to see: if the suspicion has some basis, and the man proceeds to marry the woman's mother or daughter, it is likely that he will continue his relationship with the original woman after the marriage. At that point, he will be guilty of a biblical prohibition — sleeping with his wife's mother or daughter. The Rabbis therefore prohibited the marriage preemptively to prevent it from leading to a *de'oraita* violation.

But would the *Mishnah* then use the language of *nosein l'chatchilah* — permitted outright — in a case where there is at least a rabbinic concern? The *Gemara* resolves this: *ki tnan matnisin le-achar misah* — our *Mishnah* is speaking of a case where the first woman has already died. Once she is dead, the concern that the man will continue sleeping with her no longer applies, and the rabbinic prohibition accordingly falls away. He may freely marry her mother, daughter, or sister.

### Death Removes Ties

This leads to a clarification of the overall structure. The fundamental biblical scheme is: first there is a marriage to woman one, and then an act of sex with woman two — the wife's sister, mother, or daughter — is what is prohibited. The *Mishnah* establishes that the reverse — first sex with woman one, then marriage with woman two — generates no *de'oraita* prohibition, since the relational chain begins with sex rather than marriage. The *baraita* about suspected affairs then adds that even this permitted path is rabbinically restricted during the first woman's lifetime, lest it lead to the prohibited reverse scenario. Once the first woman dies, however, even the rabbinic concern dissolves entirely, and the marriage is fully permitted.

The *Mishnah* employs the legal terminology of *anusa* and *mefuta* because those are the two categories through which the Torah addresses non-marital intercourse. In practice, for the purposes of this *halacha*, the distinction between them is irrelevant — both simply denote sexual relations outside of marriage.

## Kichah vs. Shichvah

### Terminology Pattern

The *Gemara* now investigates the textual basis for the entire framework: why does the prohibition depend on whether the first relationship was a marriage? *De-tanu rabbanan: be-chulhu ne'emar shichivah* — by all the *arayot*, the Torah uses the language of *shichivah* — lying with, i.e., sexual intercourse. *Ve-khan ne'emar kichah* — but in the specific cases of wife, mother, and daughter-in-law (and sister), the Torah uses the language of *kichah* — taking, i.e., marriage. Rashi points to the verse *ish ki yikach et ishah ve-et imah* — a man who *takes* a woman and her mother — and to *ve-achoto lo tikach* — his sister he shall not *take*. The Rabbis derive from this terminological distinction: *derech likuchin asra Torah* — the Torah's prohibition operates through the framework of marriage. Where the text says *kichah*, the operative relationship is a marital one; only once marriage is in place do the prohibited acts of *shichivah* with the relatives come into force.

### Rava Challenges

Rava then poses a sharp question to Abaye: if the use of *kichah* in these passages means the prohibition is triggered only through marriage, consider the prohibition of a man's sister, where the verse reads *ish asher yikach et achoto bat aviv o vat imo* — a man who *takes* his sister. Would one say, by the same logic, that it is only prohibited to *marry* one's sister, but sexual relations with her outside of marriage — *derech shichivah* — would be permitted? Clearly not. So how can *kichah* be the operative trigger?

### Context Determines

The resolution: *likuchin ketivah be-Torah stam* — the word *kichah* in the Torah is generic; it can mean marriage or, depending on context, simply an act of sexual taking. *Ra'uy likichah* — where the woman in question is one who could in principle be married, *kichah* means marriage in the full sense. This applies to the cases of wife, mother, and daughter-in-law, where a prior legitimate marriage is what establishes the relational framework. *Ra'uy lishchivah* — where the woman is one who could never be married — as with one's sister, where marriage is simply impossible halachically — then *kichah* in that context means *shichivah*, sexual intercourse. The prohibition against one's sister attaches to any act of intercourse, not to a marital act that can never occur.

The approach is admittedly not perfectly clean — *kichah* does not always mean what it most naturally means — but the distinction is workable: in cases where marriage is a meaningful legal possibility, *kichah* means marriage; where it is not, *kichah* is read as referring to sexual relations.

## Granddaughter Rules

### Conflicting Verses

The *Gemara* now examines the case of a granddaughter through two apparently contradictory verses. The verse in Vayikra reads: *ervat bat bincha o vat bitcha lo tegaleh* — do not expose the nakedness of your son's daughter or your daughter's daughter. The plain sense seems to refer to one's own biological granddaughter. A separate verse reads: *ervat ishah u-vitah lo tegaleh et bat benah ve-et bat bitah lo tikach* — you may not expose the nakedness of a woman and her daughter, and you shall not take her son's daughter or her daughter's daughter. This second verse refers to *the wife's* granddaughter — a step-granddaughter who is not biologically the man's own.

*Ha ketsad? Kan be-onsin kan be-nisuin* — how do we reconcile these? The first verse prohibits one's own biological granddaughter, and that prohibition exists regardless of marriage — it is a bond of *she'er*, of flesh and biological kinship. The second verse prohibits the *wife's* granddaughter — but only when there is a marriage to the grandmother. If the relationship with the grandmother was only sexual and not marital, the wife's granddaughter is not a step-granddaughter in any meaningful sense and is therefore permitted.

One might ask whether to read the verses in reverse — prohibiting the wife's granddaughter in cases of non-marital relations and perhaps permitting the step-granddaughter in cases of marriage. But the *Gemara* rejects this: the prohibitions of *arayot* use the term *she'er* — *she'erah henah*, the flesh of one's flesh. *Be-nisuin ika she'er* — in marriage, a step-relationship carries a degree of *she'er*, even if it is relational rather than biological. *U-ve-onsin leka she'er* — in a purely sexual relationship outside of marriage, the granddaughter of that woman is not one's biological granddaughter, is not one's step-granddaughter, and cannot be described as *she'er* in any meaningful sense.

### Sheir Concept

The concept of *she'er* thus does the decisive work in this analysis. Biological kinship always carries *she'er*; marital kinship generates a kind of derivative *she'er* through the step-relationship; but a purely sexual relationship, absent marriage, generates no *she'er* at all. The entire framework of what is prohibited and what is permitted in cases of non-marital relations flows from this principle.

## Father & Son

### Shomeret Yavam

The *Gemara* records that Rabbi Yose prohibits a man from marrying a woman his father had sexual relations with — *asar be-anusat aviv* — a position aligned with Rabbi Yehuda's view in the *Mishnah*.

### Knaf Aviv

Rav Gidel cites Rav in explanation of Rabbi Yehuda's position: *de-chtiv lo yikach ish et eshet aviv ve-lo yigaleh knaf aviv* — the verse in Devarim first prohibits marrying one's stepmother, and then adds the prohibition of *lo yigaleh knaf aviv* — do not uncover the hem of the father. The *knaf ha-av* — the father's hem — refers to the nakedness that the father himself saw: the woman with whom the father had sexual relations. *Lo yigaleh* — this man may not have sexual relations with that woman, even if she was never formally the father's wife. This is Rabbi Yehuda's source for his stricter position.

### Rabbanan Counter

The Rabbis, however, reject this reading. *Ei hava samich leh kedekamarat* — if the prohibition of *knaf aviv* were directly juxtaposed to the earlier passage dealing with rape and payment to the father — *venatan ha'ish hashochev imah la'avi ha-na'ara chamishim kasef* — one could concede that the context establishes that *knaf aviv* refers to a woman the father merely had sexual relations with. But *hashta d'lo samich leh* — since those passages appear much earlier in the text and are not immediately adjacent, the juxtaposition argument fails. Instead, the Rabbis read the verse in accordance with *Rav Anan* in the name of Shmuel: *beshomeret yavam shel aviv hakatuv medaber* — the verse is speaking of a *shomeret yavam*, a woman who stands in a quasi-marital *zekah* relationship with the father, awaiting *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The prohibition of *lo yigaleh knaf aviv* refers to this woman — the father's *shomeret yavam* — not to a woman with whom the father merely had casual sexual relations. *Knaf harauya l'aviv* — the hem appropriate to the father refers to the woman who is fit to be, in some legal sense, the father's wife.

### Aunt Status

The *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: if the *shomeret yavam* of the father is the father's brother's wife, then she is already the man's *dodah* — his aunt — and as such she is already prohibited under the *de'oraita* prohibition of *arayot*, which carries *karet*. Why would one need an additional verse to establish a prohibition that is already in place? *La'avor alav bishnei lavin* — to add a second *lav* to the transgression, yielding two counts of prohibition.

Furthermore, this woman is also forbidden as *yevama lashuk* — any woman who is a *shomeret yavam* is prohibited to other men until the *yibum* or *chalitzah* is completed. *La'avor alav bishloshav lavin* — that gives three *lavin* simultaneously.

### Post-Death

The *Gemara* offers a final possible resolution: *ve-iba'it eima le-achar mita* — or one could say the verse is addressing the case after the father's death. Once the father has died, she is no longer a *yevama lashuk* — if there was only one brother with the obligation and he has now died, the *zekah* lapses. She remains, however, a *dodah*, an aunt, and the *knaf aviv* prohibition adds a third *lav* even at that stage. *La'avor alav bishloshav lavin* — three counts of prohibition are nonetheless established.

## Relationship Riddles

### Stepdaughter Case

Having concluded the legal analysis, the *Gemara* turns to a remarkable series of family-relationship riddles — puzzles about how a single person can bear multiple overlapping legal relationships to another. These were likely posed as intellectual exercises among *talmidei chachamim*, testing their ability to reconstruct unusual genealogical scenarios. As one observer once put it, this is what the Rabbis did before television.

The first riddle: *achi me'av velo me'em, vehu ba'ala de'em, va'ana barta de'ittateih* — he is my half-brother on my father's side but not on my mother's, he is the husband of my mother, and I am the daughter of his wife. Rashi explains: my father's son — my half-brother — married my mother. He is therefore my half-brother (through the father) and simultaneously my stepfather (through his marriage to my mother). I am his wife's daughter — his stepdaughter. And crucially, Rami bar Chama notes that this scenario is only possible *d'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda d'matnitin* — if we do not follow Rabbi Yehuda's position in our *Mishnah*. Because the father must have had sexual relations with this man's mother without marrying her — otherwise the son could never have married his own stepmother. That makes her *anusat aviv*, and according to Rabbi Yehuda, this son would be prohibited from marrying her. Only the *Tanna Kamma*, who permits *anusat aviv*, allows the scenario to work.

### Son-Brother

The second riddle: *achi v'breih hu, achateih ana, d'hai d'darina aktephai* — he is my brother and my son; I am his sister; I am the sister of the one I am carrying on my shoulders. In other words: my son is my brother. How? *Mishkachat lah be-oved kochavim haba al bito* — the scenario involves a non-Jew who had sexual relations with his own daughter. Since the discussion of forbidden relations among non-Jews in this context does not carry the same formal prohibitions, the *Gemara* constructs these riddles using non-Jewish actors to avoid any implication that a Jew engaged in such conduct. The child born of this union is the mother's son and simultaneously her sibling, since both share the same father. She is thus the sister of the one she carries.

### Aunt Case

The third riddle: *shalom lach chavri, bat achatech ana* — greetings, my friend; I am the daughter of your sister. Meaning, this person's daughter is his sister's daughter — his niece through his sister. *Mishkachat lah be-oved kochavim haba al bat bito* — a non-Jew who had sexual relations with his granddaughter. The child born of this union is simultaneously the grandfather's daughter — and therefore the father's sister — and the mother's daughter. The daughter of the mother is therefore the father's sister's daughter — his niece on his sister's side. The child's own child stands in the relationship of "daughter of your sister" to the grandfather-father.

### Multi-Relation

The fourth riddle is the most elaborate. *Baia baia wo wo me'ach hu av v'hu ba'al v'hu bar ba'al v'hu ba'ala d'imei va'ana barta d'ittei v'lo yeihiv pita l'achoi yasmei b'nei bartei* — woe, woe, he is a brother and he is a father and he is a husband and he is the son of a husband and he is a stepfather, and I am his wife's daughter, and yet he will not feed the orphaned brothers who are the sons of his daughter.

*Mishkachat lah be-ovdei kochavim haba al imo, v'holid minah bat, v'chazar uva'al oto bat, v'chazar zakein uva'alah v'holid minah banim* — a non-Jew had sexual relations with his mother and produced a daughter. He then had sexual relations with that daughter and had children with her. From the perspective of the daughter: this man is her biological father and brother (since he was born of his mother, and she shares the same mother); he is her husband (since he had sexual relations with her); and since his father also had sexual relations with her, he is the son of her husband as well. He is thus simultaneously her brother, father, husband, and husband's son. And he withholds food from the orphaned sons — who are at once his children and the sons of his daughter.

### Cousins Case

The fifth riddle: *ana ve'at achin, ana ve'avuch achin, ana ve'imach achin* — you and I are siblings; you and your father are siblings; you and your mother are siblings — meaning, I and your mother are siblings. *Mishkachat lah be-ovdei kochavim haba al imo v'holid minah shetei banot, v'chazar uva'al achat meihen v'holid minah ben* — a non-Jew had sexual relations with his mother and produced two daughters. He then had sexual relations with one of the daughters and had a son. The other daughter says to the son: you and I are siblings — we share the same father. I and your father are siblings — we also share the same father. I and your mother are siblings — your mother and I are sisters, born of the same mother.

### First Cousins

The sixth riddle is, by comparison, almost refreshingly simple: *ana ve'at benei achi, ana ve'avuch benei achi, ana ve'imach benei achi* — you and I are the children of siblings (i.e., first cousins); I and your father are first cousins; I and your mother are first cousins. The *Gemara* notes that this case does not require any incest scenario at all — *habita nami mishkachat lah* — you can construct it from entirely regular marriages. For instance, *Reuven sheyesh lo shetei banot* — Reuven has two daughters. Shimon marries one of them. Levi's son marries the other. Then Shimon's son says to Levi's grandson: you and I are first cousins (our mothers are sisters); I and your father are first cousins (he is Levi's son and I am Shimon's son, whose mothers were sisters); and I and your mother are first cousins (your mother is Reuven's daughter, and so is my mother).

### I Am My Grandpa

The *Gemara*'s series of riddles calls to mind a famous American novelty song — *I Am My Own Grandpa* — which presents a superficially similar tangle of relationships arising from perfectly legal marriages:

> When I was 23, I was married to a widow who was pretty as can be. This widow had a grown-up daughter who had hair of red. My father fell in love with her and soon they, too, were wed. This made my dad my son-in-law and changed my very life. My daughter was my mother 'cause she was my father's wife. To complicate the matters, even though it brought me joy, I soon became the father of a bouncing baby boy. My little baby then became a brother-in-law to dad, and so became my uncle, though it made me very sad. For if he was my uncle, then that also made him brother of the widow's grown-up daughter who of course was my stepmother. Father's wife then had a son, kept him on the run, and he became my grandchild, for he was my daughter's son. My wife is now my mother's mother, and it makes me blue, because although she is my wife, she's my grandmother too. If my wife is my grandmother, then I'm her grandchild, and every time I think of it, it nearly drives me wild. For now I have become the strangest case you ever saw. As husband of my grandmother, I am my own grandpa!

The crucial distinction, of course, is that the *Gemara*'s riddles required incest — conducted, in its framing, by non-Jews — to generate the overlapping relationships, whereas the song achieves its tangle entirely through regular and entirely permitted marriages. *Beheter* — all above board. Though, as is worth noting, in most jurisdictions at present, not all of the marriages involved would be legally permitted.
