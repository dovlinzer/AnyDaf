# Berakhot 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Makeup Prayer Structure

### Missed Mincha Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: טָעָה וְלֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל מִנְחָה בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — מִתְפַּלֵּל בְּלֵיל שַׁבָּת שְׁתַּיִם. טָעָה וְלֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל מִנְחָה בְּשַׁבָּת — מִתְפַּלֵּל בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁל חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who erred and did not recite the afternoon prayer on the eve of Shabbat, prays in** the evening prayer **two** *Amida* prayers **on Shabbat evening. One who erred and did not recite the afternoon prayer on Shabbat, recites two weekday** *Amida* prayers in the evening prayer **at the conclusion of Shabbat.**

The discussion opens at the very top of 26b, building on the previous session's analysis of making up a missed *davening*. The *gemara* had established that makeup prayer is possible even when the original time has passed, grounded in the principle that *tefillot* are not only *keneged korbanot* — patterned after the sacrifices — but are also *rachamei*, a fundamental human outpouring of feeling and petition before God. This dual nature creates an ongoing tension: if prayer were purely *rachamei*, there would be no issue of missed times or fixed texts; one would simply pray whenever the spirit moved. Yet the very existence of fixed times and fixed text at the rabbinic level demonstrates that the *korban* model does impose real constraints, even while the *rachamei* dimension provides a certain latitude — including, notably, the possibility of making up a missed prayer.

The *gemara* now introduces a further complication. A *braita* teaches: if one forgot to pray *Mincha* on *Erev Shabbat*, one prays twice on Friday night — *ta'ah velo hitpalel Mincha b'Erev Shabbat, mitpalel b'leilei Shabbat shtayim*. Similarly, if one forgot *Mincha* on *Shabbat* itself, one prays twice on *Motzaei Shabbat* — and both of those prayers are weekday *davenings*, *shtayim shel chol*, even though the prayer one missed was a *Shabbat Mincha*. The principle, then, is that a missed *davening* is made up at the next available opportunity, even when the character of that opportunity differs from what was missed.

### Obligation Then Makeup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַבְדִּיל בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, וְאֵינוֹ מַבְדִּיל בִּשְׁנִיָּה. וְאִם הִבְדִּיל בַּשְּׁנִיָּה וְלֹא הִבְדִּיל בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה — שְׁנִיָּה עָלְתָה לוֹ, רִאשׁוֹנָה לֹא עָלְתָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** He recites *havdala* [the prayer of distinction] between the sanctity of Shabbat and the profanity of the week **in the first** prayer, as it is the actual evening prayer, **but he does not recite *havdala* in the second** prayer, which is in place of the afternoon prayer. Moreover, **if he recited *havdala* in the second** prayer **and did not recite *havdala* in the first, the second prayer fulfilled his** obligation, the **first one did not fulfill his** obligation.

This raises an immediate structural question: in what order does one perform these two prayers, and how does one designate which is the obligatory *davening* and which is the makeup? The *gemara* is explicit: *mavdil barishona v'eino mavdil bashniya* — one recites *havdalah* in the first prayer and not in the second. This formulation dispels any ambiguity left from the previous discussion. The second *davening* is not simply a continuation or an extension of the first time slot; it is a formally designated makeup. The *gemara* insists that the obligatory prayer must come first, and only afterwards the makeup.

The structural consequence is significant: if one recites *havdalah* in the second prayer but not the first — thereby implicitly designating the second as the obligatory *Ma'ariv* and the first as the makeup — the ruling is *shniya alta lo, rishona lo alta lo*. Only the second counts. The first, which was labeled by one's own actions as the makeup, does not fulfill its obligation, because the makeup is only valid when it follows the obligatory prayer.

### Formal vs. Experiential

One might pause here and ask: what is the experiential meaning of davening twice in immediate succession? At the level of inner religious experience, a second *Shmoneh Esrei* offered immediately after the first seems to fulfill very little. It is an honest question. The answer, upon reflection, is that the makeup prayer is not primarily about recreating a meaningful inner experience; it is about discharging an obligation. One owes a *davening*, one missed a *davening*, and one is checking it off the record. The halachic system is concerned with the *chiyuv* having been met.

This tension is precisely what is striking about the structure. The very permission to make up a missed prayer rests on the principle that prayer is *rachamei* — an inner human experience that transcends purely formal categories. Yet the actual mechanism by which one makes up that prayer is thoroughly formalistic: it must be done immediately after, it must be properly designated, and if the designation is incorrect, the makeup does not count at all. The *rachamei* rationale opens the door; the halachic structure governs everything that happens once inside. This interplay between the inner experiential justification and the formal external execution is one of the central dialectics of this entire *masechta*.

This formalistic dominance should not come as a surprise within a halachic system. The formal rules consistently tip the balance. Consider the well-known responsa of *poskim* on whether one achieves better *kavana* davening at home than in *shul*: overwhelmingly, the ruling is to daven in *shul*. The formal fulfillment — communal prayer, *minyan* — is favored over the subjective inner experience, even where the inner experience might, in a given case, be richer. Yet what makes this *masechta* unique is that the tension itself is made visible. Elsewhere, the question rarely surfaces. Here, the dialectic is openly examined.

### Havdalah Contradictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: טָעָה וְלֹא הִזְכִּיר גְּבוּרוֹת גְּשָׁמִים בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים וּשְׁאֵלָה בְּבִרְכַּת הַשָּׁנִים — מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתוֹ. הַבְדָּלָה בְּ״חוֹנֵן הַדַּעַת״ אֵין מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְאוֹמְרָהּ עַל הַכּוֹס! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to the above conclusion from the *Tosefta*: **One who erred and did not mention the might of the rains:** He makes the wind blow and rain fall **in** the second blessing of the *Amida*, the blessing on **the revival of the dead, and** one who erred and failed to recite **the request** for rain **in** the ninth blessing of the *Amida*, **the blessing of the years, we require him to return** to the beginning of the prayer and repeat it. However, one who erred and failed to recite ***havdala* in** the blessing: **Who graciously grants knowledge, we do not require him to return** to the beginning of the prayer and repeat it, **as he can recite** *havdala* **over the cup** of wine, independent of his prayer. This contradiction was not resolved and remains **difficult.**

The *gemara* then confronts a potential objection. One might think that the reason the first *davening* fails when *havdalah* is recited only in the second is because *Ma'ariv* on *Motzaei Shabbat* without *havdalah* is simply invalid — that a *Ma'ariv* missing *havdalah* does not count at all. The *gemara* challenges this assumption with a *braita*: if one forgot to mention *mashiv haruach umorid hagashem* in the second *bracha*, or *uten tal u'matar livracha* in the *bracha* of *birkat hashanim*, one must repeat the *Shmoneh Esrei* — *machzirin oto*. But if one forgot *havdalah* in *Honen Hada'at*, one does not repeat — *ein machzirin oto* — because *havdalah* can still be recited over a cup of wine later. This proves that *Ma'ariv* without *havdalah* in the *Shmoneh Esrei* is nonetheless valid. The *gemara* acknowledges the difficulty: *kashya*.

But as both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* explain, the difficulty is not so severe on reflection. Ordinarily, if one simply davened *Ma'ariv* and forgot *havdalah*, one is *yotzei* — the prayer counts. The issue here is different. Because we are constructing a formal makeup structure, the requirement is that the obligatory prayer be clearly designated as such, and the makeup designated as such. When one placed *havdalah* in the second rather than the first, one labeled them in the wrong order. The first was thereby marked as the makeup, and the makeup cannot precede the obligation. The invalidity of the first prayer does not arise from the absence of *havdalah* per se, but from the misidentification of the two prayers' roles.

This leads to a further observation. The permission to make up a missed prayer is rooted in the *rachamei* principle — in the non-formalistic, inner dimension of *tefillah*. Yet the practical execution is so highly structured that even two consecutive *Shmoneh Esreis*, both technically valid prayers, may fail to fulfill any obligation whatsoever if the formal designation is incorrect. The inner justification gives way entirely to external formal requirements at the moment of execution.

A question arises regarding whether this entire discussion assumes *Ma'ariv* is obligatory, a *chiyuv*. If *Ma'ariv* is a *reshut* — optional — the framework of makeup prayers would look quite different. *Tosafot* indeed notes this: discussions about making up *Ma'ariv* presuppose it is obligatory, a question that will be debated later in the *masechta*. For now, the *gemara* is operating within the framework that *Ma'ariv* is an obligation and must be made up.

### Omitted Insertions

*Tosafot* raises a closely related practical question: what if one did not entirely miss a *Shmoneh Esrei*, but recited it without a critical insertion — for example, on *Rosh Chodesh* without *Ya'aleh V'Yavo*? Must one do a makeup at *Ma'ariv*? *Tosafot* identifies two competing considerations. On one hand, if one was not *yotzei* the *Mincha*, a makeup is required. On the other hand, the makeup at *Ma'ariv* will itself not include *Ya'aleh V'Yavo*, since *Rosh Chodesh* will have ended — so what exactly is being made up?

The resolution turns on a conceptual distinction: is the failure to recite *Ya'aleh V'Yavo* a failure to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *Ya'aleh V'Yavo* within the *Shmoneh Esrei*, or does it constitute a failure to have fulfilled the *Shmoneh Esrei* itself? If the latter, then one simply was not *yotzei* the *Shmoneh Esrei*, and the situation is directly analogous to the *gemara*'s case of davening twice on *Motzaei Shabbat* for a missed *Shabbat Mincha* — the makeup at *Ma'ariv* does not recapitulate the missed content but fulfills the missed *Shmoneh Esrei* obligation. If the former, then repeating the *Shmoneh Esrei* at *Ma'ariv* accomplishes nothing with respect to the omitted *Ya'aleh V'Yavo*, since that cannot be fulfilled after *Rosh Chodesh*.

The ruling that follows — the accepted *pesak* — is that missing *Ya'aleh V'Yavo* in *Mincha* means one was not *yotzei* the *Shmoneh Esrei* itself, and one therefore davens a second *Shmoneh Esrei* at *Ma'ariv* even though it will not contain *Ya'aleh V'Yavo*. The focus is on the uncompleted *Shmoneh Esrei* obligation, not on the specific missing element.

## Avot vs. Korbanot (III)

### Source of Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: תְּפִלּוֹת אָבוֹת תִּקְּנוּם. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: תְּפִלּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד תְּמִידִין תִּקְּנוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda with regard to the times beyond which the different prayers may not be recited is rooted in a profound disagreement, also manifest in a later amoraic dispute. **It was stated: Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** The practice of praying three times daily is ancient, albeit not in its present form; **prayers were instituted by the Patriarchs.** However, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** that the **prayers were instituted based on the daily offerings** sacrificed in the Holy Temple, and the prayers parallel the offerings, in terms of both time and characteristics.

The *gemara* now presents a fundamental *machloket* among *Amoraim* regarding the very foundation of prayer. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* says: *tefillot Avot tiknum* — the prayers were established by the patriarchs. *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* says: *tefillot keneged temidim tiknum* — the prayers were established to correspond to the daily communal sacrifices. The *gemara* then notes that there is a *braita* that supports each position.

The question, read carefully, is not a historical one — not simply who established the prayers or when. As the *Yerushalmi* phrases it, the question is *keneged mi* — corresponding to what? In other words, what is the *model* of *tefillah*? The two answers do not address the same dimension. The first answer speaks to an inner human experience of prayer — what were the *Avot* actually doing? The second speaks to a fixed external structure — what do the prayers correspond to in terms of formal religious obligation? This is precisely the *korban* versus *rachamei* tension that has been running throughout the discussion.

### Avot Founders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַבְרָהָם תִּקֵּן תְּפִלַּת שַׁחֲרִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיַּשְׁכֵּם אַבְרָהָם בַּבֹּקֶר אֶל הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַד שָׁם", וְאֵין "עֲמִידָה" אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיַּעֲמֹד פִּינְחָס וַיְפַלֵּל".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina: Abraham instituted the morning prayer, as it is stated** when Abraham came to look out over Sodom the day after he had prayed on its behalf: **"And Abraham rose early in the morning to the place where he had stood** before the Lord" (Genesis 19:27), **and** from the context as well as the language utilized in the verse, the verb **standing** means **nothing other than prayer,** as this language is used to describe Pinehas' prayer after the plague, **as it is stated: "And Pinehas stood up and prayed** and the plague ended" (Psalms 106:30). Clearly, Abraham was accustomed to stand in prayer in the morning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִצְחָק תִּקֵּן תְּפִלַּת מִנְחָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "וַיֵּצֵא יִצְחָק לָשׂוּחַ בַּשָּׂדֶה לִפְנוֹת עָרֶב", וְאֵין "שִׂיחָה" אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "תְּפִלָּה לְעָנִי כִי יַעֲטֹף וְלִפְנֵי ה׳ יִשְׁפֹּךְ שִׂיחוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** **Isaac instituted the afternoon prayer, as it is stated: "And Isaac went out to converse [*lasuaḥ*] in the field toward evening"** (Genesis 24:63), **and conversation** means **nothing other than prayer, as it is stated: "A prayer of the afflicted when he is faint and pours out his complaint [*siḥo*] before the Lord"** (Psalms 102:1). Obviously, Isaac was the first to pray as evening approached, at the time of the afternoon prayer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַעֲקֹב תִּקֵּן תְּפִלַּת עַרְבִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּפְגַּע בַּמָּקוֹם וַיָּלֶן שָׁם", וְאֵין "פְּגִיעָה" אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי"
>
> **Translation:** **Jacob instituted the evening prayer, as it is stated: "And he encountered [*vayifga*] the place and he slept there** for the sun had set" (Genesis 28:11). The word **encounter** means **nothing other than prayer, as it is stated** when God spoke to Jeremiah: **"And you, do not pray on behalf of this nation and do not raise on their behalf song and prayer, and do not encounter [*tifga*] Me** for I do not hear you" (Jeremiah 7:16). Jacob prayed during the evening, after the sun had set.

The *braita* supporting *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* identifies each patriarch with one of the three daily prayers. *Avraham tikein tefilat Shacharit* — Abraham established the morning prayer, as it says *vayashkem Avraham baboker el hamakom asher amad sham* — Abraham rose early to the place where he had stood, preceding his intercession before God regarding Sodom. *Ve'ein amidah ela tefillah* — standing is interpreted as prayer, supported by *vaya'amod Pinchas vayefalel*.

*Yitzchak tikein tefilat Minchah* — Isaac established the afternoon prayer, as it says *vayetze Yitzchak lasuach basadeh lifnot arev* — Isaac went out to meditate in the field toward evening. *Ve'ein sichah ela tefillah* — speech or meditation is interpreted as prayer, supported by *tefillah le'ani ki ya'atof velifnei Hashem yishpoch sicho*.

*Yaakov tikein tefilat Arvit* — Jacob established the evening prayer, as it says *vayifga bamakom vayalen sham* — he encountered the place and slept there. *Ve'ein pegi'ah ela tefillah* — to encounter, in this context, means to importune or entreat, supported by the verse *ve'al tifga bi* — do not entreat me on their behalf.

## Shacharit Times (III)

### All Prayer Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ תְּפִלַּת הַשַּׁחַר עַד חֲצוֹת — שֶׁהֲרֵי תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר קָרֵב וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד חֲצוֹת. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד אַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת שֶׁהֲרֵי תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר קָרֵב וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד אַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And why did** the Rabbis **say** that **the morning prayer** may be recited **until noon? Because,** although the **daily morning offering** is typically brought early in the morning, it may be **sacrificed until noon. And Rabbi Yehuda says:** My opinion, that the morning prayer may be recited **until four hours** into the day, is **because the daily morning offering is sacrificed until four hours.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ תְּפִלַּת הַמִּנְחָה עַד הָעֶרֶב — שֶׁהֲרֵי, תָּמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם קָרֵב וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד הָעֶרֶב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד פְּלַג הַמִּנְחָה, שֶׁהֲרֵי תָּמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם קָרֵב וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד פְּלַג הַמִּנְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And why did** the Rabbis **say** that **the afternoon prayer** may be recited **until the evening? Because the daily afternoon offering is sacrificed until the evening. Rabbi Yehuda says** that **the afternoon prayer** may be recited only **until the midpoint of the afternoon because,** according to his opinion, **the daily afternoon offering is sacrificed until the midpoint of the afternoon.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ תְּפִלַּת הָעֶרֶב אֵין לָהּ קֶבַע — שֶׁהֲרֵי אֵבָרִים וּפְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַכְּלוּ מִבָּעֶרֶב, קְרֵבִים וְהוֹלְכִים כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And why did they say** that **the evening prayer is not fixed? Because** the burning of the **limbs and fats** of the offerings that were **not consumed** by the fire on the altar **until the evening.** They remained on the altar and were **offered continuously** throughout **the entire night.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ שֶׁל מוּסָפִין כׇּל הַיּוֹם — שֶׁהֲרֵי קׇרְבָּן שֶׁל מוּסָפִין קָרֵב כׇּל הַיּוֹם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁבַע שָׁעוֹת, שֶׁהֲרֵי קׇרְבַּן מוּסַף קָרֵב וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד שֶׁבַע שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And why did** the Rabbis **say** that **the additional prayer** may be recited **all day? Because the additional offering is brought** throughout **the entire day.** However, **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **the additional prayer** may be recited **until the seventh hour** of the day, **because the additional offering is sacrificed until the seventh hour.**

The *braita* supporting *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* takes an entirely different approach, grounding each prayer's time limits in the times of the corresponding sacrifices. *Mipnei mah amru tefilat hashachar ad chatzot* — why did the Sages say the morning prayer extends until midday? *Sheharei tamid shel shachar karev ve-holech ad chatzot* — because the morning *tamid* could be offered until midday. *Rabbi Yehudah* disagrees: it is only until four hours into the day — *ad arba sha'ot* — because the morning *tamid* was offered only until the fourth hour.

For *Mincha*: the afternoon prayer extends until nightfall because the afternoon *tamid* — *tamid shel bein ha-arbayim* — could be brought all the way until nightfall. *Rabbi Yehudah* again limits this, holding that the afternoon *tamid* was offered only until *pelag hamincha* — an hour and a quarter before sunset — and thus *Mincha* prayer may only be recited until that point.

For *Ma'ariv*: the evening prayer has no fixed time limit — *ein lah keva* — because the fats and limbs of the sacrifices that had not been consumed by nightfall could continue to be burned throughout the entire night.

For *Musaf*: one may recite *Musaf* all day — *kol hayom* — because the *korban Musaf* could be offered throughout the day. *Rabbi Yehudah* limits this to seven hours into the day — until 1:00 PM — because the *Musaf* sacrifice could not be brought past that point.

## Mincha Times

### Mincha Periods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִנְחָה גְדוֹלָה מִשֵּׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה וּלְמַעְלָה. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִנְחָה קְטַנָּה מִתֵּשַׁע שָׁעוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues and states that there are two times for the afternoon prayer. Greater, earlier *minḥa* [*minḥa gedola*] and lesser, later *minḥa* [*minḥa ketana*]. The Gemara clarifies the difference between them: **Which is *minḥa gedola*? From six-and-a-half hours** after sunrise **and on,** which is a half an hour after noon and on. It is the earliest time that the daily afternoon offering may be sacrificed, as in the case on the eve of Passover that occurs on Shabbat. **Which is *minḥa ketana*? From nine-and-a-half hours and on,** which is the standard time that the daily afternoon offering is sacrificed.

The *braita* continues by specifying the two windows of *Mincha* time. *Mincha gedolah* — the larger, earlier period — begins from six and a half hours into the day, 12:30 PM, and extends through the afternoon. This gives a window of five and a half hours during which *Mincha* may be recited. *Mincha ketanah* — the smaller, later period, considered the more ideal time because it more closely corresponds to when the afternoon *tamid* was actually offered — begins at nine and a half hours into the day, 3:30 PM, and extends until the end of the afternoon.

These two windows reflect the *korban* model directly. The *tamid shel bein ha-arbayim* defined when afternoon prayer was most properly situated, and the gradations of *Mincha gedolah* and *Mincha ketanah* reflect degrees of correspondence to that sacrificial time.

## Avot vs. Korbanot (III)

### Three Prayer Stages

Returning to the *Avot* model, attention is drawn to the three verbs the *gemara* uses to describe the prayer of each patriarch. *Avraham* stands — *amidah*. *Yitzchak* speaks — *sichah*. *Yaakov* entreats — *pegi'ah*. These three verbs correspond not merely to three different times of day but to three stages of the prayer experience itself.

The first stage — *amidah*, standing — precedes speech entirely. According to the *Rambam*, the foundational *kavana* required for prayer, even before one attends to the meaning of the words, is the awareness that one stands in the presence of God. This awareness alone — conscious presence before another — constitutes a profound form of connection, even without speech. The second stage — *sichah*, speech — represents the articulation of one's inner state: giving form, narrowing, and expressing what was first experienced as non-verbal presence. Only after that, in the third stage, comes *pegi'ah* — entreaty, *bakasha*, the explicit request. Prayer is often conceived of primarily as petition, but the deepest understanding of the prayer experience encompasses all three stages: non-verbal standing before God, verbal articulation of that connection, and finally the entreaty that emerges from it. This is the structure of *Avraham*, *Yitzchak*, and *Yaakov* as models of prayer.

### Prayer Types Framework

The second observation about the *Avot* model concerns what it means to say that *Avraham* established *Shacharit*, *Yitzchak* established *Mincha*, and *Yaakov* established *Maariv* — if the entire premise of the *Avot* model is that prayer is not about fixed times. The answer is that these are not times of prayer but *types* of prayer. The morning prayer, *Shacharit*, is not defined by the hour at which it occurs but by the quality of experience that morning evokes: the sense of possibility, of vision, of beginning, of going out to engage the world. *Avraham* is the archetype of this mode — *vayikra beshem Hashem*, he pioneered, he brought people to serve God, he fought wars, he was driven by dream and ambition and the desire to transform the world.

*Mincha*, the afternoon prayer, corresponds to the experience of midday and afternoon — not the freshness of morning dreams but the need to sustain, continue, maintain. *Yitzchak* is the model here: he did not pioneer new visions but secured and deepened what *Avraham* had established. He dug the same wells *Avraham* had dug, replicated the same experiences, ensured that *Avraham*'s achievements were not lost. Without *Yitzchak*, *Avraham*'s wells get stopped up. The *Mincha* prayer is the prayer of continuity and sustaining strength through the middle of the day and the middle of a life.

*Maariv*, the night prayer, corresponds to *Yaakov* — a man perpetually beset by danger and uncertainty, running from *Esav*, laboring under *Lavan*, mourning *Yosef*. The night prayer is the prayer of fear, anguish, and helplessness before an unknown future, the prayer of one who does not know what comes next and turns to God for salvation. These are not times but existential postures, and the *Avot* model names them as such.

### Communal vs. Personal

This brings the discussion to a question that might seem to favor the *Avot* model entirely: if prayer in its essence is this inner human experience of morning ambition, afternoon continuity, and nighttime anguish, why overlay it with the fixed external structure of the *korban* model at all? One answer is practical: not everyone is capable of generating that inner experience at will. Fixed times, fixed texts, and regular structure ensure that prayer happens even when the spirit does not spontaneously move. The structure creates the occasion; connection sometimes follows.

But there is a second, deeper answer. The *korban* to which prayer corresponds in the *gemara*'s formulation is the *tamid* — the communal sacrifice. What the formal structure of prayer achieves is not merely regularity but communality. When all Israel davens at the same time, with the same text, whether together in a *minyan* or separately in their homes, prayer ceases to be each person's private conversation with God and becomes a shared act — a community of prayer. The *tefillah* of the *tzibur* is something categorically different from the sum of individual prayers. This communal dimension is precisely what the *Avot* model, in its purely personal and spontaneous form, cannot by itself generate. The *korban* model — the *tamid*, offered on behalf of all Israel at fixed times — provides the structural analog for communal prayer.

The tension between personal inner experience and formal communal structure is not unique to *tefillah*; it is present in every human endeavor that carries the potential for meaning and inspiration. Whether in music, where the discipline of notation and technique opens the door to genuine expression, or in theater, or any other organized form of human creativity — the formal structure is not the enemy of inner experience but its necessary scaffold. One must first master the structure, and then one may forget it. In the domain of *halakha*, however, the formal rules are not ultimately to be forgotten but to be inhabited — which is why the *poskim* consistently favor davening in *shul* over davening at home for better *kavana*, and why the formalistic expression of the makeup prayer dominates even when its inner justification is the *rachamei* dimension of *tefillah*.

## Shacharit Times (III)

### Shacharit Cutoff

The *gemara* returns to the halachic debate regarding the time limit for *Shacharit*. *Rabbi Yehudah* holds that *Shacharit* may only be davened until four hours into the day — until 10:00 AM. The *Rabbanan* hold it may be davened until midday — *ad chatzot*. Both ground their positions in the *tamid shel shachar* and when that sacrifice could be offered, though as noted, the *Rabbanan*'s more expansive position also aligns naturally with the broader *Avot* model of prayer as a morning experience that encompasses the whole of the morning.

## Avot vs. Korbanot (III)…

### Model Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא? אָמַר לְךָ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ, תְּפִלּוֹת, אָבוֹת תִּקְּנוּם, וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקׇּרְבָּנוֹת. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, תְּפִלַּת מוּסָף לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מַאן תַּקְּנַהּ. אֶלָּא תְּפִלּוֹת אָבוֹת תִּקְּנוּם, וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקׇּרְבָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, it is clear that according to this *baraita* the *halakhot* of prayer are based on the Temple offerings. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that this is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** who held that the forefathers instituted the prayers. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** could have **said to you: Actually, I will say to you** that **the Patriarchs instituted the prayers and the Sages based** the times and characteristics of prayer **on the Temple offerings,** even though they do not stem from the same source. **As, if you do not say so,** that even Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, would agree that the laws of offerings and those of prayers are related, **then, according to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, who instituted the additional prayer?** It is not one of the prayers instituted by the forefathers. **Rather,** even according to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, **the prayers were instituted by the Patriarchs and the Sages based them** on the laws of the **offerings.**

Having presented the two *braitot* — one supporting *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* and one supporting *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — the *gemara* asks whether the *braita* supporting the *korban* model constitutes a refutation of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*'s position. *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* responds: *le'olam eima lach tefillot Avot tiknum* — the foundational truth remains that the prayers were established by the *Avot*, by the inner human experience of prayer. *Ve'asmechinenhu Rabbanan a'korbanot* — the Rabbis then *affixed* them, anchored them, to the *korbanot*. The *korban* model is a second layer, imposed by the Rabbis atop the foundational *Avot* model.

This synthesis is not merely a diplomatic resolution; it reflects a genuine structure. Prayer originates not from an external demand — here is a text, here is a time, say this — but from an inner human need, the kind of prayer that *Avraham*, *Yitzchak*, and *Yaakov* exemplified. The Rabbis then formalized this inner reality through the mold of the *korbanot*, giving it fixed times, fixed texts, and communal form. The *Avot* model is the foundation; the *korban* model is the architecture built upon it.

### Musaf Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, תְּפִלַּת מוּסָף לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מַאן תַּקְּנַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proves that the *korban* model exists as a genuine second layer — and is not merely rhetorical — is *Musaf*. *Tfilat Musaf leRabbi Yehoshua ben Levi man tiknah* — according to *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, who established *Musaf*?

The clearest evidence that the *korban* model exists as a genuine second layer — and is not merely rhetorical — is *Musaf*. *Tfilat Musaf leRabbi Yehoshua ben Levi man tiknah* — according to *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, who established *Musaf*? There is no *Musaf* among the prayers of *Avraham*, *Yitzchak*, and *Yaakov*. *Musaf* is purely a *korban* prayer, corresponding entirely to the additional sacrifice of *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. This proves that the *korban* element is real and not merely incidental. *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* therefore must concede that *Musaf* is anchored exclusively in the *korban* structure.

A significant halachic corollary follows: because *Musaf* is entirely a *korban* prayer with no *Avot* foundation, it cannot be made up if missed. The capacity to make up a missed *davening* — as discussed at the outset — rests on the *rachamei* dimension, on the inner human experience that transcends formal time constraints. *Musaf*, having no such dimension, is governed entirely by *avar yomo batel korbano* — once the day has passed, the sacrifice is nullified and there is nothing to make up. *Tosafot* and others draw this distinction explicitly.

The *Rambam*'s formulation of the biblical-rabbinic distinction in the laws of prayer expresses a similar double layer: a biblical obligation of prayer that has no fixed time, no fixed text — one says whatever one wishes — and a rabbinic overlay of fixed times, fixed texts, and all the formal structure. The relationship between *Avot* as foundation and *korbanot* as formal structure is the conceptual parallel.

## Shacharit Times (III)…

### Inclusive Cutoffs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר עַד אַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל, אוֹ דִילְמָא עַד וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל?! תָּא שְׁמַע רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד פְּלַג הַמִּנְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The morning prayer may be recited **until four hours** of the day. **A dilemma was raised before** the yeshiva students: When Rabbi Yehuda says **until,** does he mean **until and including** the fourth hour, **or, perhaps** when he says **"until"** he means **until and not including,** in which case one may not pray during the fourth hour? **Come and hear** a resolution to this dilemma based on the mishna. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The afternoon prayer may be recited only **until the midpoint of the afternoon.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא עַד וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל — הַיְינוּ דְּאִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְרַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַיְינוּ רַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** Now, **granted, if you say** that **until** means **until and not including, then there is** a difference **between** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and** the opinion of **the Rabbis. However, if you say** that **until** means **until and including,** then the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** is identical to the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as the end of the period that begins with the midpoint of the afternoon is sunset.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי, עַד וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְשֶׁל מוּסָפִין כׇּל הַיּוֹם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁבַע שָׁעוֹת. וְתַנְיָא: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁתֵּי תְּפִלּוֹת, אַחַת שֶׁל מוּסָף וְאַחַת שֶׁל מִנְחָה — מִתְפַּלֵּל שֶׁל מִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שֶׁל מוּסָף, שֶׁזּוֹ תְּדִירָה, וְזוֹ אֵינָהּ תְּדִירָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִתְפַּלֵּל שֶׁל מוּסָף וְאַחַר כָּךְ שֶׁל מִנְחָה, שֶׁזּוֹ עוֹבֶרֶת, וְזוֹ אֵינָהּ עוֹבֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara immediately rejects this proof: **Rather, what** is the alternative? That **until** means **until and not including?** It remains problematic. **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **The additional prayer** may be recited **all day. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It may be recited **until the seven hours. And it was taught** in a *baraita*: **If** the obligation to recite **two prayers was before him, one the additional prayer and one the afternoon prayer, he prays the afternoon prayer** first **and the additional prayer thereafter,** because **this,** the afternoon prayer, **is** recited on a **frequent** basis, **and that,** the additional prayer, **is** recited on a relatively **infrequent** basis as it is only recited on Shabbat, the New Moon, and Festivals. The principle states: When a frequent practice and an infrequent practice clash, the frequent practice takes precedence over the infrequent practice. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He recites the additional prayer** first **and the afternoon prayer thereafter,** because the time to recite **this, the additional prayer,** will soon **elapse, and this,** the time to recite **the afternoon prayer,** will **not** soon **elapse,** as one may recite it until the midpoint of the afternoon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל״, הַיְינוּ דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ שְׁתֵּי תְּפִלּוֹת בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״עַד וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל״, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ שְׁתֵּי תְּפִלּוֹת בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי? כֵּיוָן דְּאָתְיָא לַהּ שֶׁל מִנְחָה, אָזְלָא לַהּ שֶׁל מוּסָפִין!
>
> **Translation:** The relevant point is: **Granted, if you say** that **until** means **until and including, that is how you can find** a situation where the times to recite **two prayers,** the afternoon prayer and the additional prayer, **overlap. But if you say** that **until** means **until and not including,** and that until seven hours means until the beginning of the seventh hour, noon, then **how can you find** a situation where the times to recite **two prayers overlap? Once** the time to recite **the afternoon prayer,** a half hour past noon, **has arrived,** the time to recite **the additional prayer** is already **gone?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי, ״עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל״? קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא, מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְרַבָּנַן?! — מִי סָבְרַתְּ דְּהַאי ״פְּלַג מִנְחָה״ פְּלַג אַחֲרוֹנָה קָאָמַר? פְּלַג רִאשׁוֹנָה קָאָמַר, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵימַת נָפֵיק פְּלַג רִאשׁוֹנָה וְעָיֵיל פְּלַג אַחֲרוֹנָה — מִכִּי נָפְקִי אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁעוֹת חָסֵר רְבִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what** is the alternative? That **until** means **until and including?** Then **the first clause** of the mishna **is difficult,** as explained above with regard to the midpoint of the afternoon: **What is** the halakhic difference **between** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and** the opinion of **the Rabbis?** The Gemara answers: **Do you think that** when **this midpoint of the afternoon** was mentioned it was **speaking of** the period following **the midpoint, the last** part of the afternoon, from an hour-and-a-quarter before sunset until sunset? This was not the intention. Rather, it was **speaking of** the period prior to **the midpoint, the first** part of the afternoon, which, as explained above, is from nine-and-a-half hours after sunrise until an hour-and-a-quarter before sunset. Consequently, until the midpoint of the afternoon means until the end of the first half of that afternoon period. **And this is what he is saying: When does the first half leave and the second half enter? From when eleven hours minus a quarter have passed** since sunrise. Rabbi Yehuda's use of the term until always means until and including.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא הֵעִיד חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים: ... וְעַל תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר שֶׁקָּרֵב בְּאַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Practically speaking, this means that, according to Rabbi Yehuda, it is permissible to recite the morning prayer until the end of the fourth hour. In support of this **Rav Naḥman said: We, too, learned** this in a mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava testified** about **five matters of** *halakha*: ... **And** he testified **about** the **daily morning offering that was sacrificed at four hours** of the day.

The *gemara* now examines a textual ambiguity in *Rabbi Yehudah*'s formulation. When he says *ad arba sha'ot* — until four hours — does he mean *ad ve'ad bichlal* — up to and including the fourth hour, meaning through 10:00 AM — or *ad velo ad bichlal* — up to but not including the fourth hour, meaning only until 9:00 AM when the fourth hour begins?

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the parallel formulation regarding *pelag hamincha* for *Mincha*. If *ad* is inclusive, then *ad pelag hamincha* includes the entire *pelag hamincha* period, which would extend to nightfall — making *Rabbi Yehudah*'s position identical to the *Rabbanan* who permit *Mincha* until sunset. That cannot be right. So perhaps *ad* is non-inclusive? But if non-inclusive, then when *Rabbi Yehudah* permits *Musaf* until seven hours — *ad sheva sha'ot* — he would mean only until noon, and there would be no overlap between *Mincha* time (from 12:30 PM) and *Musaf* time. Yet the *braita* presents a case of *shtei tefillot* — two prayers that must be davened simultaneously, choosing between *Mincha* first or *Musaf* first. That case can only exist if there is an overlap. This proves *ad* is inclusive by *Rabbi Yehudah*: *Musaf* extends until 1:00 PM, creating an overlap with *Mincha gedolah* between 12:30 and 1:00.

But then how does one explain *pelag hamincha*? The *gemara* resolves this by reconsidering which *pelag* is meant. The *braita* had determined — based on an explicit ruling — that *Rabbi Yehudah* refers to *pelag Mincha achrona*, the second half of the *Mincha ketanah* period, from 3:30 to 6:00. If one takes that unit — 3:30 to 6:00 — and divides it into two halves, one has *plag rishona* (3:30 to 4:45) and *plag achrona* (4:45 to 6:00). When *Rabbi Yehudah* says *ad pelag hamincha* inclusively, he means through the first half, ending at the beginning of the second half, 4:45 PM — *michei nafik achas esrei sha'ot chasar revi'a* — when eleven hours minus a quarter have passed. This preserves a genuine difference between *Rabbi Yehudah* (until 4:45) and the *Rabbanan* (until sunset), while maintaining the principle that *ad* is inclusive throughout.

The parallel case regarding the order of *Mincha* and *Musaf* when both must be davened in the afternoon also emerges here. The general ruling is that *Mincha* precedes *Musaf* because *Mincha* is *tadir* — more frequent — and the principle is *tadir ve'eino tadir, tadir kodem*. *Rabbi Yehudah* rules the opposite: *Musaf* before *Mincha*, because *Musaf* has a passing time — *zo overet* — and once that window closes, *Musaf* cannot be made up. One should therefore discharge the time-sensitive obligation first. All times cited are in *sha'ot zmaniyot* — proportional hours calculated by dividing the daylight hours into twelve equal parts — and must be adjusted accordingly for any given day.

### Ben Bava Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ ״עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Learn from this** final testimony, which is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, that **until** means **until and including.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this.**

The *gemara* now anchors the ruling for *Shacharit* in a specific testimony. *Rav Nachman* notes that the *Mishnah* in *Eduyot* already supports this position. *Rabbi Yehudah ben Bava* testified five things — *chamisha devarim* — among them: *ve'al tamid shel shachar she-karav be-arba sha'ot* — that the morning *tamid* was offered in the fourth hour. The word is *be-arba sha'ot*, "in the fourth hour," not *ad arba sha'ot*, "until the fourth hour." This formulation confirms that the fourth hour was included, that it was offered during that hour — meaning the cutoff is inclusive of the fourth hour, through 10:00 AM.

*Eduyot* is referred to as *bechirata* — the select tractate — because it preserves particularly reliable testimony from early authorities, gathered largely at *Yavne* after the *churban*. The general halachic principle is that teachings preserved in *Eduyot* carry special weight and are generally followed.

### Rav Kahana Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן בִּבְחִירָתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Based on this mishna, **Rav Kahana said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda since we learned** in a mishna in the **preferred** tractate, *Eduyyot*, **in accordance with his** opinion. Since the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with all of the *mishnayot* in *Eduyyot*, the opinion of a *tanna* who rules in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda in that mishna means that the *halakha* is in accordance with that opinion.

*Rav Kahana* issues the ruling: *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehudah* for *Shacharit* — we rule like *Rabbi Yehudah* that the proper time of the morning prayer extends until four hours into the day, until 10:00 AM — *ho'il ve-tnan be-vchirata kevataih*, since we taught in *Eduyot* consistent with his position.

It is worth noting what this ruling actually demonstrates. The testimony in *Eduyot* concerns when the *korban tamid* was in practice offered in the morning. That a sacrifice was always brought before the fourth hour does not automatically prove that davening *Shacharit* after 10:00 AM is invalid — that conclusion follows only if one accepts that *tefillah* is substantively linked to the *korban* structure. The *gemara*'s readiness to derive the prayer time limit from the sacrifice time implicitly affirms the *korban* dimension. This is not in contradiction to the *Avot* model; it reflects the synthesis established above — the *Avot* experience is foundational, but the rabbis connected the formal expression of prayer to the *korbanot*, and it is that formal connection which governs the times.

The practical ruling that emerges: *zman Shacharit* — the proper time of the morning prayer — ends at four hours into the day, 10:00 AM on a day of equal daylight hours. If one misses this window, one may still daven *Shmoneh Esrei* until *chatzot* — midday — but it is considered *tefillah she-lo bi-zmanah*, prayer recited outside its proper time. After midday, the day's *Shacharit* obligation has passed entirely.

## Boker Definition

### Manna Melting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ דְּהַאי ״פְּלַג מִנְחָה״ פְּלַג אַחֲרוֹנָה קָאָמַר? פְּלַג רִאשׁוֹנָה קָאָמַר... דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״בִּטֵּל״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״טָעָה״... וְעַל תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר שֶׁקָּרֵב בְּאַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת, מַאי מַשְׁמַע?
>
> **Translation:** Having established the time limit for *Shacharit*, the *gemara* probes its meaning further through an unexpected source: the *manna* narrative.

A *Tosefta* or parallel source asks: when the verse says *va-cham ha-shemesh ve-namas* — the sun heated and the manna melted — at what hour did this occur? The answer given is the fourth hour, 10:00 AM. But the verse elsewhere says *ke-chom ha-yom* — like the heat of the day — referring to the hottest part of the day, which is noon. The resolution: *ke-chom ha-yom* refers to when the day is fully hot everywhere; *va-cham ha-shemesh ve-namas* refers to a condition where the sun is hot but the shaded areas are not yet hot. That is the fourth hour — when sun-exposed areas are already warm but the shade remains cool.

### Two Halves

The *gemara* then asks who holds this position — is it *Rabbi Yehudah* or the *Rabbanan*? The Torah commands that the manna be gathered *ba-voker* — in the morning. The manna melted at the beginning of the fourth hour, 9:00 AM. Yet according to *Rabbi Yehudah*, *voker* — morning — extends through the fourth hour, until 10:00 AM. If that is so, the manna would still be melting during *voker* time, which seems problematic — the people collected during *voker* and it melted during *voker* as well. According to the *Rabbanan*, *voker* extends until noon, so the manna melting at 9:00 AM is within *voker* as well.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty with the observation that the verse writes *ba-voker ba-voker* — "in the morning, in the morning" — doubling the word. This doubling indicates a division: *chalkehu li-shnei bekarim* — divide it into two mornings, a first half and a second half. According to the *Rabbanan*, whose *voker* runs until noon: the first half of *voker* ends at 9:00 AM and the second half runs from 9:00 AM to noon. The manna was gathered during the first half and melted at the transition into the second half — consistent with melting at the start of the fourth hour, 9:00 AM. According to *Rabbi Yehudah*, whose *voker* runs until 10:00 AM: the repetition of *ba-voker* does not split *voker* in two but indicates that the gathering must occur specifically in the earlier part — *le-hakdim lo sha'ah achat* — pressing the collection into the first part of *voker*, before 9:00 AM, after which the manna would melt even while *voker* technically continued.

### Shade/Sun Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְחַם הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְנָמָס״ — אֵיזוֹ הִיא שָׁעָה שֶׁהַשֶּׁמֶשׁ חַם וְהַצֵּל צוֹנֵן — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר בְּאַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: The verse states: "When the sun grew hot it melted." Which is the hour that the sun is hot but the shade** remains **cool,** before the heat of the day, when even the shade is hot? **You must say at four hours.**

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* offers the principle that identifies the fourth hour specifically as the moment of transition: *eizu hi sha'ah she-ha-shemesh cham ve-ha-tzel tsonen* — which is the hour when the sun is hot but the shade is still cool? That is the fourth hour. This contrasts with *ke-chom ha-yom*, when even the shaded areas are hot — a condition that arrives at noon. The manna, melting specifically at the sun-hot-shade-cool transition, places its melting precisely at 9:00 AM, the beginning of the fourth hour — and this is accepted by all positions as the correct reading of *va-cham ha-shemesh ve-namas*.

### Boker Layers

What is striking about this entire *sugya* is the direction of derivation. The debate between *Rabbi Yehudah* and the *Rabbanan* about how late *Shacharit* may be davened had been grounded in the *korban* model — when was the morning *tamid* offered. But the *gemara* here is probing the meaning of *voker*, and the *manna* narrative has nothing to do with sacrifices. The question of what constitutes *voker* — whether it runs until 10:00 AM or until noon — is a question about the human experience of morning itself, about what part of the day still carries the quality of *boker*, daybreak. The *manna* melting is a natural phenomenon marking the end of early morning; the cooling shade giving way to pervasive heat is the experiential signal that the morning has concluded. In this sense, the *gemara* is entertaining the possibility that the debate between *Rabbi Yehudah* and the *Rabbanan* is not ultimately about the *tamid* but about a more fundamental question: what is *tefillat ha-voker* — what is the morning prayer, and how long does morning last? The *korban* model and the *Avot* model converge on the same time limits through entirely different conceptual routes.

### Manna Prayer Link

One final observation about the *manna* parallel deserves mention. There is a way of reading prayer itself *keneged ha-man* — corresponding to the manna. The daily gathering of manna in the morning — the experience of going out each day to receive one's sustenance from God, never able to store it up in advance — is a paradigm for prayer as daily petition. Just as Israel prayed through the gathering of manna for their daily bread, each morning's *Shacharit* is a turning to God for the sustenance of the coming day. This layer of meaning sits beneath the formal structures of *korban* and *Avot* alike.

## Mincha Times (II)

### Mincha Cutoff Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּפִלַּת הַמִּנְחָה עַד הָעֶרֶב וְכוּ׳. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב יִצְחָק: הָתָם אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן בִּבְחִירָתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ, הָכָא מַאי? אִישְׁתִּיק וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: The Rabbis hold that **the afternoon prayer** may be recited **until the evening.** Rabbi Yehuda says: It may be recited only until the midpoint of the afternoon. **Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Yitzḥak: There,** with regard to the morning prayer, **Rav Kahana said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, since we learned** in a mishna in the **preferred** tractate, *Eduyyot*, **in accordance with his** opinion. **Here, what** is the ruling? **He was silent and said nothing to him,** as he was familiar with no established ruling in this matter.

*Rav Chisda* turns to *Rabbi Yitzchak* and raises the practical question directly. Having just ruled like *Rabbi Yehudah* for *Shacharit* — because of the testimony preserved in *Eduyot* — what is the ruling for *Mincha*? Does the time for *Mincha* end at *pelag hamincha* (4:45 PM), as *Rabbi Yehudah* holds, or does it extend until nightfall, as the *Rabbanan* hold? *Rabbi Yitzchak* has no answer — *ishtik velo amar lei midi* — he falls silent.

### Shabbat Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נֶחְזֵי אֲנַן, מִדְּרַב מְצַלֵּי שֶׁל שַׁבָּת בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אַדְּרַבָּה מִדְּרַב הוּנָא וְרַבָּנַן לָא הֲווֹ מְצַלּוּ עַד אוּרְתָּא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא אִתְּמַר הִלְכְתָא לָא כְּמָר וְלָא כְּמָר, דַּעֲבַד כְּמָר — עֲבַד, וְדַעֲבַד כְּמָר — עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Let us see** and try to resolve this ourselves **from** the fact **that Rav prayed** the **Shabbat** prayers on the eve of Shabbat **while it was still day. Learn from this** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and the time for the afternoon prayer ends at the midpoint of the afternoon, after which time one may recite the evening prayer. The Gemara immediately rejects the proof based on Rav's practice: **On the contrary, from** the fact **that Rav Huna and the Sages,** students of Rav, **would not pray until evening, learn from that** that **the *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara concludes: **Now that the *halakha* was stated neither in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage nor in accordance with** the opinion of that **Sage, one who acted in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage has acted** legitimately, **and one who acted in accordance with** the opinion of that **Sage has acted** legitimately, as this *halakha* is left to the decision of each individual.

*Rav Chisda* then attempts to resolve the question on his own from a practical precedent. *Mi-deRav matzli shel Shabbat b'erev Shabbat mi-be'od yom* — from the fact that *Rav* davened *Maariv* for *Shabbat* while it was still Friday, before sunset. If *Mincha* time ends at 4:45 PM (*pelag hamincha*), then *Maariv* time begins at 4:45 PM, and *Rav*'s early *Shabbat* davening would be consistent with ruling like *Rabbi Yehudah*. *Shema mina halakha ke-Rabbi Yehudah* — this suggests we rule like *Rabbi Yehudah*.

But the *gemara* counters: *mi-deRav Huna ve-Rabbanan lo havu matzli ad orta* — *Rav Huna* and the *Rabbanan* who were present did not daven an early *Shabbat*. From this one infers the opposite — *ein halakha ke-Rabbi Yehudah*.

### No Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא אִתְּמַר הִלְכְתָא לָא כְּמָר וְלָא כְּמָר, דַּעֲבַד כְּמָר — עֲבַד, וְדַעֲבַד כְּמָר — עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Now that the *halakha* was stated neither in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage nor in accordance with** the opinion of that **Sage, one who acted in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage has acted** legitimately, **and one who acted in accordance with** the opinion of that **Sage has acted** legitimately, as this *halakha* is left to the decision of each individual.

The *gemara* concludes with a formal declaration of inconclusive debate: *hashta de-lo itmar hilkhata la kemar vela kemar, de-avad kemar avad u-de-avad kemar avad* — since no clear ruling has been issued like either authority, one who acts like this one has acted properly and one who acts like that one has acted properly. This is an unusual outcome — a genuine *deaved kemar* situation without a definitive *pesak* for *Mincha* times, even while the *Shacharit* cutoff was definitively ruled.

### Mincha/Maariv Link

The *gemara*'s framing of the evidence — using *Rav*'s early *Shabbat* davening and *Rav Huna*'s refusal to do the same — reveals an implicit assumption: the time of *Mincha* and the time of *Maariv* are interdependent. If *Mincha* ends at *pelag hamincha*, *Maariv* may begin at *pelag hamincha*. If *Mincha* extends until sunset, *Maariv* cannot begin until after sunset. This linkage is important in practice, particularly for congregations that daven an early *Shabbat* and wish to daven both *Mincha* and *Maariv* before nightfall.

A real halachic problem arises when a congregation davens *Mincha* after *pelag hamincha* and then immediately davens *Maariv* as well — in the same pre-sunset window. This relies on *Rabbi Yehudah* for the permissibility of *Maariv* beginning at *pelag hamincha*, while relying on the *Rabbanan* for the continuation of *Mincha* beyond *pelag hamincha*. These two positions are not compatible with each other. Strictly speaking, one cannot use *Rabbi Yehudah* to begin *Maariv* while simultaneously using the *Rabbanan* to continue *Mincha*. The *deaved kemar* ruling means one may choose either position, but one may not blend them contradictorily in a single act of davening.

It should also be noted that nowhere does *Rabbi Yehudah* himself explicitly rule that *Maariv* begins at *pelag hamincha*. The connection is inferred by the *gemara*, and the *Rambam* does not directly derive this from the *pelag hamincha* debate but treats early *Maariv* on *Erev Shabbat* as a special case, since *Maariv* is *reshut* — not obligatory in the same sense. Nonetheless, the accepted practical understanding is that *pelag hamincha* functions as a liturgical boundary with implications for both the end of *Mincha* and the potential beginning of *Maariv*.

## Davening Etiquette

### Early Shabbat Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי גְּנִיבָא וְצַלִּי שֶׁל שַׁבָּת בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, וַהֲוָה מְצַלֵּי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא לַאֲחוֹרֵי דְרַב, וְסַיֵּים רַב וְלָא פַּסְקֵיהּ לִצְלוֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav happened by the house of** the Sage, **Geniva, and he prayed** the **Shabbat** prayer **on the eve of Shabbat** before nightfall. **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba was praying behind Rav, and Rav finished** his prayer **but did not** take three steps back and **interrupt the prayer** of **Rabbi Yirmeya. Derive from this** incident **three** *halakhot*:

The *gemara* concludes with a narrative involving *Rav*. *Rav ikla l'vei Gneiva* — *Rav* came to the house of *Gneiva*, and there he davened *Maariv* for *Shabbat* while it was still *Erev Shabbat*, before sunset. *Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba* was davening behind *Rav*. When *Rav* finished his *Shmoneh Esrei*, he did not interrupt *Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba*'s davening — he did not take his three steps back in a way that would intrude into *Rabbi Yirmiyah*'s space, nor did he walk in front of him to return to his seat.

### Three Lessons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם שֶׁל שַׁבָּת בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִתְפַּלֵּל תַּלְמִיד אֲחוֹרֵי רַבּוֹ. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אָסוּר לַעֲבוֹר כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּתְפַּלְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Derive from this** that **one may pray the Shabbat** prayer **on the eve of Shabbat** before nightfall. **And derive from this that a student** may **pray behind his rabbi. And derive from this** that it is **prohibited to pass before those who are praying.**

*Shema mina tlat* — three halachic conclusions are drawn from this incident. First: *mitpalel adam shel Shabbat b'erev Shabbat* — one may daven *Maariv* for *Shabbat* on Friday before sunset, confirming that early *Shabbat* is halachically permitted. Second: *mitpalel talmid achorei rabbo* — a student may daven directly behind his *rebbe*. This is not considered disrespectful; it does not carry the appearance of bowing to one's teacher, and it does not violate any expectation of deference — unlike davening immediately to the side of the teacher, which could raise concerns. Third: *assur la'avor keneged hamitpalelim* — it is prohibited to walk in front of people who are davening. This is derived from *Rav*'s refusal to step in front of *Rabbi Yirmiyah* once that *talmid* had begun his *Shmoneh Esrei*.

### Four Amos Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אָסוּר לַעֲבוֹר כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּתְפַּלְּלִין. אִינִי?! וְהָא רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי חָלְפִי. רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי — חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת הוּא דְּחָלְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi,** as **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: It is prohibited to pass before those who are praying.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi pass** before those who were praying? The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** were **beyond four cubits** from those who were praying **when they passed.**

The *gemara* explicitly endorses this prohibition by connecting it to an independent ruling of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: *assur la'avor keneged hamitpalelim* — one may not walk in front of those who are davening. The *gemara* then raises a challenge: did not *Rabbi Ammi* and *Rabbi Assi* walk in front of people who were davening? The resolution: *Rabbi Ammi* and *Rabbi Assi* walked *chutz le-arba amos* — outside the four-*amot* radius. The prohibition applies only within four *amot* of the person davening — one may not enter or cross that space while the person is in the middle of *Shmoneh Esrei*. Beyond four *amot*, passing in front of a davening individual is permitted.

This is a prohibition that is widely violated in practice. Beyond its halachic weight, it is also simply distracting to those in the midst of *davening*, and people ought to be *makpid* — careful — to avoid walking within four *amot* of anyone in the middle of *Shmoneh Esrei*.
