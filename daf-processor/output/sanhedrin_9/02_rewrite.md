# Sanhedrin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yerushalmi Framework

### Woman vs. Man

Today's daf is 9, though we began at the bottom of 8a, approximately five lines from the bottom, where the Gemara deals with *motzi shem ra*. We have just seen the Yerushalmi present two explanations of the underlying debate. The first frames it as a case against the woman, which begins as *dinei mamonot* and may draw close to *dinei nefashot*. The second frames it as a case against the man, where from the outset there are simultaneously *dinei nefashot* — against the witnesses — and *dinei mamonot* against the man himself. Those are two distinct ways the Yerushalmi frames the question, and it presents them in clear, well-ordered terms. Let us now see what the Bavli has to say.

### Yerushalmi's Debate

The Mishnah records: *v'chachamim omrim motzi shem ra b'esrim v'shlosha* — the Chachamim say the case of *motzi shem ra* requires twenty-three judges, while Rabbi Meir holds it requires only three. The Gemara on 8a, five lines from the bottom, immediately challenges this: *v'chi yesh bo dinei nefashot mai havei?* — so what if there is an element of *dinei nefashot* in it? We are dealing with the *dinei mamonot* aspect. Why should that aspect require twenty-three?

The Bavli's response to this question, it must be said, is considerably more cryptic than the Yerushalmi's. The Gemara records: *amar Ulla bechoshshim l'la'az miflagi* — the debate between Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim turns on the question of whether we are concerned about *la'az*, about rumor or ill repute. Rabbi Meir holds *ein choshshim l'la'az* — we are not concerned about rumor. The Chachamim hold *choshshim l'la'az* — we are indeed concerned. But what exactly does this mean?

## Three vs. Twenty-Three

### Basic Positions

Reading with Rashi, who is easier to follow, the case is against the woman. Rabbi Meir says you begin with a court of three, since the immediate matter is one of *dinei mamonot*. The Chachamim say you must begin with twenty-three.

### Ulla's Choshshim L'la'az

Ulla's explanation is that the concern is this: once people begin hearing that there is a case against this woman — that she was not found to be a virgin on her wedding night, or whatever the allegation — that very publicity might cause witnesses to come forward and actually testify that she committed adultery after her betrothal. The case could thus evolve from a *dinei mamonot* proceeding into a capital one requiring twenty-three. Rabbi Meir says we need not worry about that possibility; it is a *mamonot* case and three judges suffice. The Chachamim say: precisely because it could become *dinei nefashot*, we must begin with twenty-three.

### Rashi's Reading

This, then, is Rashi's understanding: the debate is about whether a *dinei mamonot* case against the woman might evolve into a *dinei nefashot* case, and the term *choshshim l'la'az* captures the concern that the rumor will spread and bring witnesses forward. It is an admittedly curious use of *la'az*, since the "rumor" in question is not quite a slander — it is more about the word getting out and drawing additional witnesses. Nevertheless, that is how Rashi reads it.

### Tosafot's Critique

Tosafot is deeply unsatisfied with this reading on multiple grounds. First, Tosafot argues that *la'az* does not carry this meaning. Second — and here we hear a direct echo of the Yerushalmi — Tosafot maintains that the phrase *motzi shem ra* always refers to the case against the man, not against the woman. This is precisely the objection raised in the Yerushalmi against its first opinion: what do you mean the debate is about the woman? *Motzi shem ra* means the case against the man.

Tosafot therefore insists that the entire discussion concerns the case against the man. But then what is the *choshshim l'la'az* type of concern? To arrive at an answer, Tosafot performs considerable interpretive acrobatics. He proposes that there can be a case against the man even without the witnesses becoming *chayav misa*. How? Suppose the husband brought witnesses who testified that his wife committed adultery, and then other witnesses came and contradicted them — not as *zomemim*, not by saying "you were elsewhere," but simply by testifying to the contrary, thereby neutralizing the first set. Tosafot makes the striking claim — which he himself acknowledges is a significant *chiddush* — that even in such a case, where the witnesses are merely neutralized rather than refuted through *zomemut*, the husband would still be liable to pay the fine and receive lashes. The case thus begins as *dinei mamonot* against the man — his witnesses were neutralized — but could potentially turn into *dinei nefashot* if those witnesses were subsequently proven to be *zomemim*.

### Two Meanings

This discussion surfaces two distinct meanings of *choshshim l'la'az*. The first, which can be attributed to Rashi's approach, is that the rumor will spread and the case will actually turn into a capital one. The second is more subtle: even granting that the case might evolve, we are concerned about what it would look like to add judges after the fact — *k'vod* for the original judges. If we started with three and then had to augment to twenty-three, people might say: something must have gone wrong with the original court. Rabbi Meir says *ein choshshim l'la'az* — if we have to add judges later, we add them and we do not worry about how it reflects on the original three. The Chachamim say *choshshim l'la'az* — to avoid that indignity to the first judges, we begin with twenty-three from the outset.

What both meanings share is the underlying concern that a *dinei mamonot* case might become a *dinei nefashot* case. The question that divides them is: does that possibility require us to start with twenty-three from the beginning, or can we deal with it if and when it arises?

An important observation emerges at the end of this Tosafot, on 8b. Tosafot writes: *u'vi'Yerushalmi matza Rashba* — that Rav Shimshon found in the Yerushalmi a debate among Amoraim. Some explain it in terms of the case against her — *k'id'mifaresh b'kuntres*, as Rashi explains — and some explain it in terms of the case against him — *k'id'mifaresh Rabbeinu Tam*. Tosafot is pointing out that the very debate between Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam over how to read the Bavli is itself the same debate recorded explicitly in the Yerushalmi. This is a pattern that recurs throughout the literature: disputes among the *Rishonim* about how to read the Bavli reflect ambiguities in the Bavli's text itself, ambiguities that may be spelled out explicitly in a parallel Yerushalmi passage. The Yerushalmi, then, actually provides a far clearer framing of the underlying question. In our Gemara it is much less transparent. But let us return to the Bavli.

## Rava's Framework

### Rava's Reversal

Four lines from the bottom of 8a, we pick up again: *amar Ulla b'chosheshin l'la'az kamipalgi* — and then the Gemara introduces Rava's alternative. *Rava amar kuli alma l'la'az lo chashinan* — everyone agrees we are not concerned about three becoming twenty-three. The debate, says Rava, is something altogether different.

### Honor Concern

*V'hacha b'chivodan shel rishonim kamipalgi* — the dispute is about concern for the honor of the first judges, but in a scenario that is the reverse of what Ulla described. *V'hacha b'mai askinan k'gon d'ichnisu bei esrin v'tlasa* — the case began with a court of twenty-three, assembled *l'meidan dinei nefashos*. Then *v'izdu* — the court dispersed, because it became apparent that the husband would not be able to produce proof that his wife committed adultery. At that point the husband says: fine, I cannot prove adultery, but at least adjudicate the monetary question and let me establish that she forfeits her *ketubah*. So the question becomes: can twenty of the judges go home, leaving three to handle the *dinei mamonot*? *Rabbi Meir says yes — let twenty go and three remain. The Chachamim say: since we began with twenty-three, we keep twenty-three. To dismiss twenty of them would be a slight to their dignity. That is the *la'az* here — not that people will gossip about the woman, but that it would be disrespectful to dismiss the majority of a court that was properly convened. This is a fascinating read, though perhaps less intuitive than Ulla's.

## Baraita Reconciliati (II)

### Apparent Contradiction

The Gemara then raises a challenge from a *braisa*: *v'chachamim omrim tavo'u mammon* — three; *tovu nefashos be-esrim ushlosha* — twenty-three. This sounds as though the Chachamim agree with Rabbi Meir: if the case involves only *dinei mamonot*, three suffice. But that seems to be exactly what Rabbi Meir said. In what sense, then, do the Chachamim disagree at all? This *braisa* appears to undermine Ulla's entire reading of the debate.

## Rava's Framework (II)

### Resolution

The Gemara notes: *bishlama le-Rava* — for Rava, this *braisa* works nicely. *Tovu mamon techila* — if the case began as a *dinei mamonot* matter from the start, everyone agrees: three. *Tovu nefashos techila* — if it began as a capital case but the witnesses did not materialize and you are left with a monetary question, then even for that remaining monetary matter you keep twenty-three, because it was initiated as a capital case. For Rava, this resolves cleanly: the Chachamim are saying precisely that — if it started as *mamonot*, we agree to three; if it started as *nefashot*, we keep twenty-three even if all that remains is a monetary issue. But *ella le-Ulla kashya* — for Ulla it is difficult. On Ulla's reading, the whole point is that the case starts as *mamonot* and might evolve into *nefashot*, and that is precisely what causes the Chachamim to require twenty-three. How then does this *braisa* show the Chachamim agreeing to three when it begins as *mamonot*?

## Baraita Reconciliati (II)

### Rava & Chiya Bar Abba

The Gemara records Rava's response: *ani ve-arya she-bachabura* — "I and the lion of our group have explained this *braisa* consistently with Ulla." Who is this lion? *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba*.

### Case Against Man

Here is the resolution: *hacha be-mai askinan she-heivi ha-ba'al eidim she-zinta ve-heivi ha-av eidim ve-hizimum* — the husband brought witnesses that she committed adultery, and the father brought witnesses who proved those witnesses were *zomemim*. We have now shifted from a case against the woman to a case against the man. His witnesses have been exposed as *zomemim*, and the question is how to adjudicate the monetary claim against him. *Ba likvos mamon mi-ba'al bishlosha* — when the court is convened to extract money from the husband, that requires three. The reason is that a case against the man himself can never escalate into *dinei nefashot*; it will always remain monetary. So the Chachamim readily agree: when it is the case against the man, three suffice.

### Case Against Woman

*U've-makom nefashos be-esrim ushlosha* — but when the case is against the woman, in a setting where *nefashot* are implicated, it requires twenty-three. Because the woman herself could potentially face a death penalty if the case evolves into one of capital adultery, we always demand twenty-three from the outset.

### Yerushalmi Mirroring

What the Bavli has now arrived at is precisely the same two-way framework the Yerushalmi laid out explicitly at the start. The Yerushalmi's first approach: it is a case against the woman, which begins as *mamonot* but may evolve toward *nefashot* — and therefore we debate whether to start with three or twenty-three. The Yerushalmi's second approach: it is a case against the man, which has *nefashot* implications for his witnesses but is always only *mamonot* for him — and therefore the debate is resolved differently depending on whom we are adjudicating against.

The Bavli arrives at this same bi-directional framework, but through a far more labyrinthine route. Once we have read the Yerushalmi, the Bavli's final resolution reads much more clearly. The case against the man is always three; the case against the woman, who might herself be condemned to death, is always twenty-three. Just as in the Yerushalmi, one could argue either direction — the woman's case more urgently demands twenty-three because she herself faces death; conversely, in the man's case someone is always going to be executed (his witnesses), suggesting that too should require twenty-three. The Bavli resolves it by distinguishing which party is actually being adjudicated.

A brief clarification is worth noting here: if she is found not to have been a virgin, that alone does not bring a death penalty. The death penalty applies only if she committed adultery after the betrothal — *kiddushin* — meaning during the year or so between betrothal and the actual marriage (*nisu'in*), when she was already legally bound to her husband. If the act occurred before the betrothal, it is only a question of *ketubah*. And of course the *sotah* procedure, triggered by a husband's jealousy, is an entirely separate framework — one that involves a ritual outcome rather than a judicial death sentence, though witnesses to actual adultery could always bring a capital case independently.

## Alternative Readings

### Abaye's Warning

At this point the Gemara could have stopped. The Yerushalmi stops here, having established the two primary frameworks for understanding the debate. But this is where the Bavli's distinctive character asserts itself. Because the underlying dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim is not fully transparent from the text of the Mishnah alone, the Bavli — particularly its later Amoraim — takes the opportunity to import other debates entirely and propose that our Mishnah's dispute might really be about any number of other legal questions. These readings are, frankly, imported from elsewhere and are largely absent from the Yerushalmi, but they allow the Gemara to expand the discussion in characteristic Babylonian fashion.

*Abaye amar dechulei alma chaishinan l'la'az* — Abaye says forget the question of whether we are concerned about *la'az*, and forget the question of the honor of the original judges. *Hacha b'mai askinan kegon d'atru bah stam* — the case is against her, as we have been assuming, but those who warned her gave the *hatra'ah* generically, without specifying which mode of death she would deserve if convicted. They simply said: if you commit adultery you are *chayav misa* — they did not specify which *misa*.

### Death Penalty Baraita

This distinction is grounded in the following *braisa*: the *Tosefta* discusses the *mesit u'madiach* — the one who seduces others to idolatry — where a uniquely concealed form of warning is used, almost entrapment-like. But regarding all other capital crimes in the Torah, *v'sha'ar kol chayavei mitot she-baTorah ein mamitim otam ela b'edah* — a full court of twenty-three is required — *v'edim* — witnesses who are present openly, not hidden — *v'hatra'ah* — and explicit warning — *v'ad sheyodi'uhu shehu chayav mitah b'veit din* — and the warning must convey not merely that the act is forbidden but that it carries a court-administered death penalty.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer ad sheyodi'uhu b'eizo mitah hu neherag* — Rabbi Yehuda goes further: it is not enough to say you will be *chayav misa*; the warning must specify which form of death. This is the crux of the debate Abaye is importing. According to Rabbi Yehuda, if the warning did not specify the mode of death, there can be no capital sentence. So on this reading, Rabbi Meir holds like Rabbi Yehuda: the witnesses warned her only that she would be *chayav misa* without specifying the method, and therefore she can never actually be executed — the case will only ever result in a monetary outcome, and three judges suffice. The Chachamim disagree: even a generic warning suffices for a capital sentence, and therefore the case could result in death, warranting twenty-three. This has nothing to do with the plain meaning of the Mishnah's dispute, but it is a coherent legal reading.

### Rav Pappa's Chaverah

*Rav Pappa amar hacha b'ishah chaverah askinan* — Rav Pappa says: the case involves a *chaverah*, a learned woman, and no warning at all was given to her. The debate then maps onto the dispute between Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbanan: *chaver ein tzarich hatra'ah* — a *talmid chacham* does not require warning, because the purpose of *hatra'ah* is to distinguish intentional from unintentional sin, and a knowledgeable person clearly understands the gravity of what they are doing. One does not need to warn a criminal lawyer that what they are doing is illegal. The Rabbanan disagree and require warning even for the learned, for any of several reasons: one might forget even what one knows in a moment of passion; or the purpose of *hatra'ah* is not merely to establish intent but to make the sin an act of defiance — *af al pi chen*, the person says, I proceed anyway; or, as the Gemara will develop later in Sanhedrin, *ad she'yatir atzmo le'otah mita* — the person must effectively grant the court license to take their life, which the explicit acceptance of the warning accomplishes.

On this reading: Rabbi Meir holds that a *chaverah* requires no warning, and therefore without *hatra'ah* she will never be found liable to death — three judges. The Chachamim require warning even for the learned, so she could in principle be executed — twenty-three.

### Rav Ashi's Lashes

*Rav Ashi amar kegon de'asru bah bemalkut velo asru bah bekatalah* — Rav Ashi proposes yet another scenario: the witnesses warned her that she would receive *malkut* — lashes — but did not warn her of the death penalty. Since one cannot be executed on the basis of a warning about a lesser punishment, she can never receive the death penalty. The question then becomes: does *malkut* require three judges or twenty-three? This maps onto the later Mishnah in our tractate: *malkut bishloshah mishum Rabbi Yishmael amru be'esrim ushloshah* — lashes normally require three, but Rabbi Yishmael says twenty-three. This is a very weak reading, it must be said, because the language of the Mishnah itself attributes the Chachamim's position to the presence of *dinei nefashot* — and on this read, there is no *nefashot* dimension at all. One might wonder why the Mishnah did not simply frame this as a debate about the case of the man, who also receives *malkut* and pays the hundred-shekel fine. But Rav Ashi is simply adding yet another possibility to the growing list.

### Ravina's Invalid Witness

*Ravina amar kegon shenimtza echad min ha'edim karov upasul* — Ravina proposes that the case involves three witnesses who testified that she committed adultery, and one of them turned out to be a relative and therefore disqualified. Is she then liable to the death penalty, or is the capital dimension eliminated entirely?

## Invalid Witnesses

### Rabbi Akiva Principle

This question leads into a substantial independent discussion. The background is the principle derived by Rabbi Akiva. The Torah says *al pi shenayim edim o sheloshah edim yumat hamet* — why mention three witnesses if two already suffice to impose a death sentence? Rabbi Akiva answers: it comes to be strict regarding the third witness. If Reuven, Shimon, and Levi all testified and were later proven to be *zomemim*, Levi might argue: even without my testimony, Reuven and Shimon had already made the defendant *chayav misa* — my addition changed nothing, so why should I be punished as a *zomem*? The Torah therefore links three to two to say: Levi receives the same punishment as the other two, even though his testimony was not strictly necessary. By the same logic, *nitpal le'ovrei averah ke'ovrei averah* — one who attaches himself peripherally to wrongdoers is treated as a principal wrongdoer. And by *kal v'chomer*, one who attaches himself peripherally to a group performing a *mitzvah* — say, joining twenty others on their way to do *bikur cholim* — receives the same reward as the principal actors.

Beyond the individual witness, this reasoning establishes that when witnesses are linked — two or three or even one hundred — they function as a single unit. And therefore: *u'mah shnayim nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul eidusam bateilah* — just as with two witnesses, if one is disqualified the entire testimony is void (since you are left with only one), so too with three — if one is disqualified, all are void. *V'minayin she-afilu meiah* — and how do we know this extends to a hundred? *Talmud lomar eidim* — "witnesses" — they are treated as one indivisible unit.

### Yosi vs. Akiva

But how far does this principle extend? Here the debate opens. *Amar Rabbi Yose: b'meh devarim amurim b'dinei nefashos* — Rabbi Yose limits the principle of *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* to capital cases. *Aval b'dinei mammonos tiska-yeim eidus b'sha'ar* — but in monetary cases, the valid witnesses among the group retain their validity even if one of them is disqualified. They are not treated as a single unit; the kosher ones stand on their own.

### Rabbi's Conditions

Rabbi (*Rebbi*) disagrees with the very distinction Rabbi Yose draws. *Echad dinei mammonos v'echad dinei nefashos* — even in monetary cases, if one witness is disqualified, all are invalidated. However, Rabbi introduces his own qualifier: *v'eimasai? Bizman she-hisru bahen* — this applies only when the witnesses actively participated by delivering the *hatra'ah*, the warning. This active participation identifies them as a unified witness group, and the disqualification of one then invalidates all. The corollary is that if they did not give the warning — if there was no act that singled them out as a collective — then the disqualification of one does not sweep away the rest, since they were never constituted as a unit in the first place. Rabbi's famous illustration: *mah ya'asu shnei achim b'soch echad she-harag es hanefesh* — what should two brothers do who happen to be walking with a friend when a murder is committed before their eyes? They did nothing to identify themselves as witnesses. Their mere passive presence should not disqualify the third witness by making all three a unit that collapses when the two brothers are disqualified as relatives.

### Tosafot's Questions

Tosafot picks up on this and raises a critical question. Tosafot notes that the mechanism of warning works to identify a group as a collective *eidut* — but warning is relevant only in capital cases. How does this operate in monetary cases or in matters like *gittin* and *kiddushin*? The Gemara in the first chapter of Makkot supplies a partial answer: the court would ask the witnesses, *l'as-hodei asisu o l'michzei asisu* — did you come to testify or did you come merely to observe? If they declared their intention to testify, then *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul eidusam bateilah* — the group is constituted as a unit and the disqualification of one invalidates all. If they said they came only to watch, then *mah ya'asu shnei achim* applies — they are not a collective.

But the Ri (*Rabbeinu Yitzchak*) goes further: even the declaration of intent to testify is not sufficient. *Nira l'Ri de-mishum she-nis-kavnu l'ha-id lo amrinan nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul eidusam bateilah* — merely intending to bear witness, even consciously, does not create the collective unit. What is required, says the Ri, is that they actually appear in *beit din* together and testify: *ela she-holchin u'me-idin kulam b'Beis Din* — it is their joint appearance in court that fuses them into a single *eidut*. For *re'iyah l'chudei* — mere seeing of the event, even with conscious intent to serve as a witness — does not suffice. The Ri's proof: *d'ei lo teima hachi kol gittin keshenosnim osam bifnei krovim o psulim yihyu psulim im nis-kavein ha-echad* — if intent alone were enough to form a collective, then any *get* given in the presence of relatives who happened to intend to observe would be invalidated whenever one of them is disqualified. That is clearly untenable.

This opens a rich question in the *Rishonim*: what exactly is the threshold that constitutes a person as a *witness* for purposes of *nimtza echad karov o pasul*? Is it passive presence? Conscious intent? Delivery of a warning? Appearance in *beit din*? These conditions operate differently across different legal contexts — capital cases, monetary cases, *kiddushin*, *gittin* — and no single answer covers them all.

### Applied to Mishnah

Returning to our Mishnah: on Ravina's reading, there were three witnesses. One was a relative and disqualified. Rabbi Meir holds like Rabbi Yose — the invalid witness eliminates the capital dimension but leaves the monetary case intact. The two remaining valid witnesses can still function as *eidim* for a *dinei mamonot* claim, so the case is adjudicated before three judges. The Chachamim hold like Rabbi (*Rebbi*): the invalid witness does not sweep away the remaining two for *dinei nefashot*, provided the invalid witness did not actively participate in the warning. Since the disqualified witness did not give *hatra'ah*, the other two are not constituted together with that witness as a unified group. They retain full validity for *dinei nefashot*, and therefore the case requires twenty-three.

## Further Readings

### Warning Source

*V'ee ba'is eima she'hisru ba'acherim v'lo hisru ba'edim* — yet another proposed reading: others gave the warning, but the witnesses themselves did not. The question here is whether the witnesses must deliver the *hatra'ah* personally. This debate maps onto Rabbi Yose, who derives from *al pi shenei edim* that the witnesses themselves must be the ones to warn: *l'olam eino neherag ad she'yiyu shenei edim masrin bo* — a person can never be executed unless two witnesses delivered the warning themselves. On this reading, Rabbi Meir holds like Rabbi Yose — the warning delivered by others is invalid and there can be no death sentence, so three suffice. The Chachamim disagree and hold that a warning from others is adequate, making a capital case possible and requiring twenty-three.

### Contradiction Types

*D'iskachashi b'vedikos v'lo iskachashi b'chakirosh* — a final proposed reading: the witnesses contradicted one another in the *badikos*, in the circumstantial details of what happened, but not in the *chakirot*, the essential interrogation about when and where. The distinction is significant. It is the *chakirot* — the time and place — that are fundamental to testimony, and it is contradiction there that renders witnesses invalid; moreover, it is consistency on time and place that opens the door to *zomemut*, since counter-witnesses can claim "you were elsewhere at that time." Contradiction in *badikos*, the fine details, is a different matter.

### Ben Zakkai Method

*Ma'aseh u'vadak Ben Zakkai b'uktzei te'einim* — the Gemara recalls that Ben Zakkai would probe witnesses with minute questions: if the alleged crime occurred under a fig tree, he would ask whether the stems of the figs pointed north or south, whether they were sharp or smooth. The point was to find any discrepancy that might unravel the testimony, since the Gemara is always cautious about *dinei nefashot*. The question is whether a contradiction in such picayune details — *badikos* rather than *chakirot* — is sufficient to disqualify witnesses entirely. Ben Zakkai says yes; the Chachamim say no. On this reading of our Mishnah, Rabbi Meir holds like Ben Zakkai: the contradiction in *badikos* disqualifies the witnesses for capital purposes, leaving only a monetary case — three. The Chachamim say a contradiction in *badikos* does not disqualify them, the testimony stands for capital purposes, and twenty-three are required.

In all, this gives us approximately ten different readings of the Mishnah's dispute, of which the vast majority are imported from entirely other legal debates. The genuine interpretive work was done with Ulla and Rava at the outset, paralleling what we saw in the Yerushalmi. Everything that follows is the Bavli's characteristic mode of using a textual ambiguity as a canvas on which to project and explore the full range of relevant legal debates — with no pretense, in many cases, that this is what the Mishnah was originally about.

## False Witnesses

### First Inversion

The Gemara concludes with a compact but striking discussion of the *zomemim* scenario at the heart of *motzi shem ra*. The husband brings witnesses testifying that his wife committed adultery. The father brings witnesses who prove that the husband's witnesses are *zomemim* — false witnesses. *Eidei haba'al neheragin* — the husband's witnesses are executed, since they tried to have her killed.

### First Consequence

*Ve'ein meshalmin mammon* — they do not also pay money. Even though their testimony, had it stood, would have also caused her to forfeit her *ketubah*, they are not liable for that monetary harm as well. Rashi explains this on the basis of *kim lei* — when one is liable to death, the monetary obligation is subsumed within the greater penalty.

### Second Inversion

*Chazar ve'hevi haba'al eidim ve'hizimum le'eidei ha'av* — the case then reverses again. The husband produces a new set of witnesses who prove that the father's witnesses — the very ones who had the first group condemned as *zomemim* — were themselves lying. The father's witnesses had falsely accused legitimate witnesses of being *zomemim* and had tried to have those witnesses killed.

### Second Consequence

*Eidei av neheragin* — the father's witnesses are now executed. *U'meshalmin mammon* — and they also pay money, namely one hundred shekels to the husband. Their false testimony, if accepted, would have exposed the husband's legitimate witnesses to death and forced the husband himself to pay the hundred-shekel fine of *motzi shem ra*. Now that they are proven to be *zomemim*, they pay that fine themselves — and are also executed.

### Two Crimes

Why do the father's witnesses pay money in addition to being executed, when the husband's witnesses in the first round did not? The answer is *mammon le'zeh u'nefashos le'zeh* — the monetary crime and the capital crime were committed against two different parties. The father's witnesses committed a capital offense against the husband's witnesses — they tried to have them executed. And they committed a monetary offense against the husband — they tried to impose a hundred-shekel fine upon him. These are two entirely separate wrongful acts, directed at two different victims, adjudicated in two separate proceedings. There is therefore no *kim lei*; the cases do not collapse into one another. Each must be answered for independently — and accordingly, the father's witnesses both lose their lives and, before that sentence is carried out, their estate pays the hundred shekels to the husband.
