# Sotah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Midwives & Batim

### Intro & Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי כִּי יָרְאוּ הַמְיַלְּדוֹת אֶת הָאֱלֹהִים וַיַּעַשׂ לָהֶם בָּתִּים״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: בָּתֵּי כְהוּנָּה וּלְוִיָּה, וְחַד אָמַר: בָּתֵּי מַלְכוּת. מַאן דְּאָמַר בָּתֵּי כְהוּנָּה וּלְוִיָּה — אַהֲרֹן וּמֹשֶׁה. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּתֵּי מַלְכוּת — דָּוִד נָמֵי מִמִּרְיָם קָאָתֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתָּמׇת עֲזוּבָה אֵשֶׁת כָּלֵב וַיִּקַּח לוֹ כָלֵב אֶת אֶפְרָת וַתֵּלֶד לוֹ אֶת חוּר״. וּכְתִיב: ״וְדָוִד בֶּן אִישׁ אֶפְרָתִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse relates the midwives' reward: **"And it came to pass, because the midwives feared God, that He made them houses"** (Exodus 1:21). **Rav and Shmuel** disagree as to the precise interpretation of these houses: **One says** that God made **the houses of** the **priesthood and** the **Levites** descend from the midwives, **and one says** that God made the **houses of royalty** descend from them. **The one who says** that it is referring to the **houses of** the **priesthood and** the **Levites** is referring to **Aaron and Moses,** who were sons of Jochebed. **And the one who says** that it is referring to **houses of royalty** is referring to **David,** who **also comes from Miriam, as it is written: "And Azubah," the wife of Caleb, "died, and Caleb took to him Ephrath, who bore him Hur"** (I Chronicles 2:19) and, as will be explained further, Ephrath is Miriam. **And it is written: "David was the son of that Ephrathite** of Bethlehem in Judah" (I Samuel 17:12). Therefore, he was a descendant of Miriam.

We pick up seven lines from the bottom of 11b, in the midst of the *aggadata* concerning the enslavement in Egypt — particularly fitting for *Chol HaMoed Pesach*. The broader context is the *mishnah*'s principle of *midah keneged midah*, measure for measure: just as God repays the *sotah* according to what she did, so too does the divine calculus operate throughout history.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The *pasuk* states: *vayehi ki yar'u hameyaldot et haElohim vaya'as lahem batim* — when the midwives, identified with Yocheved and Miriam, feared God, He made for them *batim*, houses. A dispute between Rav and Shmuel turns on the meaning of these *batim*. One opinion holds *batei kehunah veleviyah*: houses of priesthood and Levitical service, referring to Aharon and Moshe who descended from Yocheved. The other holds *batei malchut*: houses of kingship. The first interpretation is relatively straightforward — Aharon stands at the head of the *kehunah* and Moshe is the preeminent of the Levites. The second requires more unpacking.

### Calev & Miriam

How does kingship emerge from Yocheved and Miriam? The Gemara answers: *David nami miMiriam ka'ati* — David himself descends from Miriam. The relevant *pesukim* are in Divrei HaYamim, where Calev marries a woman named Azuvah, and then takes Efrat, who bears him Chur. Separately, David is called *ben ish Efrati*. The Gemara links these together: Calev ben Yefuneh married Miriam, who is identified with both Azuvah and Efrat. David descended from Calev and therefore, ultimately, from Miriam herself.

## Miriam's Genealogy

### Calev Marries Miriam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכָלֵב בֶּן חֶצְרוֹן הוֹלִיד אֶת עֲזוּבָה אִשָּׁה וְאֶת יְרִיעוֹת וְאֵלֶּה בָנֶיהָ יֵשֶׁר וְשׁוֹבָב וְאַרְדּוֹן״. בֶּן חֶצְרוֹן? בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה הוּא! בֵּן שֶׁפָּנָה מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the family of Caleb: In Chronicles it says: **"And Caleb, the son of Hezron, begot children of Azubah his wife, and of Jerioth, and these were her sons: Jesher, and Shobab, and Ardon"** (I Chronicles 2:18). The Gemara asks: Was Caleb actually the **son of Hezron? Wasn't he** the **son of Jephunneh,** as the verse states in Numbers 13:6? The Gemara answers: He was the son of Hezron, but he is called "son of Jephunneh" as an appellation indicating that he was **a son who turned away [*sheppana*] from the counsel of the spies.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דִּכְתִיב ״הַקְּנִזִּי״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The language of another verse **is also precise** based on this explanation, **as it is written:** "And Caleb the son of Jephunneh **the Kenizzite** said unto him" (Joshua 14:6). Although his father was Jephunneh, he is known as "the Kenizzite," although he was not actually a son of Kenaz. The Gemara accepts this proof and states: **Conclude from it** that Rava's explanation is correct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֲזוּבָה״ — זוֹ מִרְיָם, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ עֲזוּבָה — שֶׁהַכֹּל עֲזָבוּהָ מִתְּחִילָּתָהּ. ״הוֹלִיד״ — וַהֲלֹא מִינְסָב הֲוָה נָסֵיב לַהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנּוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם — מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ יְלָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "And Caleb, the son of Hezron, begot children of Azubah his wife, and of Jerioth, and these were her sons: Jesher, and Shobab, and Ardon" (I Chronicles 2:18). The Gemara analyzes the verse: The verse refers to the wife of Caleb by the name **Azubah.** The Sages teach that **this** is **Miriam. And why is she called Azubah? As everyone initially abandoned her [*azavuha*]** and did not want to marry her because she was sickly and unattractive. The verse additionally states: "And Caleb, the son of Hezron, **begot children [*holid*]** of Azubah his wife" (I Chronicles 2:18). The Gemara asks: Why use the term "*holid*," begot children? **But** doesn't this verse state that **he married her? Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This teaches us that with regard to **anyone who marries a woman for the sake of Heaven,** as he married her due to her righteousness without concern for her appearance, **the verse ascribes him** credit **as if he gave birth to her.**

Several difficulties immediately arise. First, the *pasuk* says *ben Chetzron*, but we know him as Calev *ben Yefuneh*. The Gemara resolves this: *ben Yefuneh* is a descriptive epithet — *she'pinah me'atzat hameraglim*, he turned himself away from the counsel of the spies — while Chetzron is his father's proper name.

A further complication: Calev is also called *haKenizi*, associated with Kenaz, since the *pasuk* in Shoftim describes Osniel ben Kenaz as *achi Calev*, the brother of Calev, implying they share a father. Rava resolves this: Calev was Kenaz's stepson, not his biological child. The designation *haKenizi* reflects that he was raised in Kenaz's household without being his natural son. *Shema mina* — this resolves all identifications, and the Calev in the *pasuk* is Calev ben Yefuneh.

Now, *zo Miriam* — Azuvah is Miriam. Why was she called Azuvah? *Shekol azvuha mitchilatah* — everyone had abandoned her, for she was sickly and no one wished to marry her. As for the word *holid* — "he begot" — the Gemara asks the obvious: surely he married her, he did not beget her? *Vehalo minsav hava nasiv lah* — of course he married her. This sets up the next teaching.

### Marriage L'Shem Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יְרִיעוֹת״ — שֶׁהָיוּ פָּנֶיהָ דּוֹמִין לִירִיעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The same verse refers to Miriam additionally as **Jerioth,** which the Gemara explains was appropriate, **for her face was like** extremely pallid **curtains [*yeriot*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֵלֶּה בָּנֶיהָ״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי בָּנֶיהָ אֶלָּא ״בּוֹנֶיהָ״, ״יֵשֶׁר״ — שֶׁיִּשֵּׁר אֶת עַצְמוֹ. ״שׁוֹבָב״ — שֶׁשִּׁיבֵּב אֶת יִצְרוֹ, ״וְאַרְדּוֹן״ — שֶׁרָדָה אֶת יִצְרוֹ, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: עַל שֶׁהָיוּ פָּנֶיהָ דּוֹמִין לְוֶרֶד.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And these were her sons [*vaneha*]."** The Gemara explains: **Do not read** it as ***vaneha*, her sons; rather,** read it as ***boneha*, her builders.** In other words, the rest of the names in the verse are not the names of her children, but rather appellations for her husband, whose marriage to her built her, as it were. The first appellation for Caleb, **"Jesher,"** is referring to his actions, **as he set himself straight [*yisher*]** and did not join in the counsel of the spies. The second appellation, **"Shobab,"** is referring to the fact **that he broke [*sibbev*] his** evil **inclination** by rebelling against the other spies. The third appellation, **"and Ardon [*veArdon*],"** is referring to the fact **that he ruled [*rada*]** over **his** evil **inclination. And some say: Because the face** of his wife Miriam became beautiful **like a rose [*vered*]** after they were married, she was also called Vardon, due to her rose-like complexion.

Rabbi Yochanan derives from here: *kol hanos'e isha l'shem shamayim ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu yeladah* — whoever marries a woman *l'shem shamayim*, for the sake of Heaven, Scripture accounts it as though he had fathered her. Here, Calev married Miriam not out of desire or attraction — she was sick and abandoned, with no one to care for her — but purely for her benefit and for the sake of Heaven. Therefore the Torah uses the language of *holid*, as if he had given birth to her. The image of a husband as father-figure is striking to modern sensibilities, but reflects the reality of a society where such a marriage was genuinely an act of protection and selfless provision.

The *pasuk* also mentions *Yeriyot* alongside Azuvah. Rather than reading this as a second wife, the Gemara interprets it as another name for Miriam: *she'hayu paneha domin liyeriyot* — her face resembled *yeriyot*, curtains, drawn and pale from illness. The following phrase, *ve'eileh baneha*, "these are her children," is read *al tikri baneha ella boneha* — not her children but that which built her, describing how Calev's marriage restored and strengthened her.

### Sons' Names Interpreted

The three sons listed — Yashar, Shovav, and Ardon — are interpreted not as literal children but as descriptions of Calev himself and what he accomplished. *Yashar*: *she'yishar et atzmo* — he made himself straight, whether by resisting the *meraglim* or by taking the upright path of marrying Miriam when others would not. *Shovav*: *she'shovev et yitzro* — he subjugated his *yetzer*, his inclination, again in resisting the *meraglim* or in choosing Miriam over someone more conventionally attractive. *Ardon*: *she'rada et yitzro* — he subdued his desire similarly. An alternative tradition reads *Ardon* as *she'panav domin levered* — his face was ruddy as a rose.

### Ashur & Tekoa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּלְאַשְׁחוּר אֲבִי תְקוֹעַ הָיוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים חֶלְאָה וְנַעֲרָה״. ״אַשְׁחוּר״ — זֶה כָּלֵב, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ ״אַשְׁחוּר״ — שֶׁהוּשְׁחֲרוּ פָּנָיו בְּתַעֲנִיּוֹת. ״אֲבִי״ — שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה לָהּ כְּאָב, ״תְּקוֹעַ״ — שֶׁתָּקַע אֶת לִבּוֹ לְאָבִיו שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets an additional verse as referring to Caleb. It is stated: **"And Ashhur the father of Tekoa had two wives, Helah and Naarah"** (I Chronicles 4:5). **Ashhur is Caleb. And why was he called Ashhur? Because his face became blackened [*husheḥaru*] from** the extensive **fasts** that he accepted upon himself so that he would not be entrapped by the counsel of the spies. **"The father of"** is also referring to Caleb, **as he became like a father to** his wife. The next word in the verse, **"Tekoa,"** is an additional reference to Caleb, **as he attached [*taka*] his heart to his Father in Heaven.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָיוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים״ — נַעֲשָׂה מִרְיָם כִּשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים. ״חֶלְאָה וְנַעֲרָה״ — לָא חֶלְאָה וְנַעֲרָה הֲוַאי, אֶלָּא בַּתְּחִילָּה חֶלְאָה, וּלְבַסּוֹף נַעֲרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase in the verse **"had two wives"** actually means it is as if **Miriam became like two wives,** because she changed over the course of time. And therefore the two names written in the verse: **"Helah and Naarah," were not** two separate women, **Helah and Naarah. Rather, initially** Miriam was **sickly [*ḥela*]** and forlorn, **and ultimately** she was healthy and beautiful like **a young woman [*na'ara*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבְנֵי חֶלְאָה צֶרֶת וְצֹהַר וְאֶתְנָן״. ״צֶרֶת״ — שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית צָרָה לְחַבְרוֹתֶיהָ. ״צֹהַר״ — שֶׁהָיוּ פָּנֶיהָ דּוֹמִין כַּצׇּהֳרַיִם, ״אֶתְנָן״ — שֶׁכׇּל הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתָהּ מוֹלִיךְ אֶתְנָן לְאִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expounds the following verse as referring to Miriam: **"And the children of Helah were Zereth [*Tzeret*] and Zohar and Ethnan"** (I Chronicles 4:7). She was now called **"*Tzeret*," for she became** so beautiful that she was like **a rival [*tzara*] to other** women, as they were jealous of her beauty. She is called **"Zohar," as her face** shined **like** the sun does at **noon [*tzohorayim*].** She is called **"Ethnan," as any** man **that saw her** would be aroused so much that he **would bring a gift [*etnan*] to his wife** to entice her.

The *pasuk* continues: *veAshur avi Tekoa hayu shtei nashim Hel'ah veNa'arah* — Ashur the father of Tekoa had two wives, Hel'ah and Na'arah. The Gemara identifies Ashur with Calev once more. Why is he called Ashur? *She'hush'cheru panav beta'aniyot* — his face grew dark from the fasts he undertook, praying for the strength to withstand the pressure of the *meraglim*. This is a remarkable detail: the same Calev who stood before all of Bnei Yisrael with seemingly unshakeable courage could not rely on his own fortitude alone, but had to pour out his heart to God through fasting and prayer.

*Avi* — father: *she'nasa lah ke'av*, he became like a father to Miriam. *Tekoa*: *she'taka libo le'aviv she'bashamayim* — he drove his heart like a stake into his Father in Heaven, whether in the context of the *meraglim* or in his decision to marry *l'shem shamayim*.

As for the "two wives," Hel'ah and Na'arah: *na'asit Miriam k'shtei nashim* — Miriam became like two wives. *Lo Hel'ah veNa'arah hava* — these are not two distinct women. Rather, originally she was *Hel'ah*, sickly, and ultimately she became *Na'arah*, as strong and healthy as a young woman.

The children listed as *bnei Hel'ah* — Tzereth, Tzohar, and Etnan — are likewise interpreted in terms of this transformation. *Tzereth*: *she'na'asit tzarah lechaveroteha* — she became a rival to all the women around her, so stunning had she become. *Tzohar*: *she'hayu paneha domim ketzohorayim* — her face shone like midday. *Etnan* — which ordinarily refers to a harlot's payment — is interpreted here as: *shekol haro'eh otah molich etnan le'ishto*, that any man who saw her was so stirred that he would go and seek intimacy with his own wife, even offering her something akin to payment. The image is jarring, but conveys the idea of Miriam's remarkable transformation.

### Path to David

With all this established, the *batei malchut* interpretation is complete. Miriam married Calev ben Yefuneh of the tribe of Yehudah, and David descended from that union. Thus the houses God made for the midwives include the house of kingship that ultimately produced David HaMelech.

## Pharaoh's Decrees

### Drowning Edict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְצַו פַּרְעֹה לְכׇל עַמּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַף עַל עַמּוֹ גָּזַר. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שָׁלֹשׁ גְּזֵירוֹת גָּזַר: בַּתְּחִילָּה ״אִם בֵּן הוּא וַהֲמִתֶּן אוֹתוֹ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף ״כׇּל הַבֵּן הַיִּלּוֹד הַיְאֹרָה תַּשְׁלִיכֻהוּ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף אַף עַל עַמּוֹ גָּזַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the discussion of the bondage in Egypt. **"And Pharaoh charged all his people,** saying: Every son that is born you shall cast into the river, and every daughter you shall save alive" (Exodus 1:22). **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** The use of the phrase "every son that is born" indicates that **he decreed even on his own nation** that all their male babies must be killed. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** further: **He decreed three decrees. Initially,** he commanded the midwives only with regard to Jewish infants: "You shall look upon the stones. **If it be a son, then you shall kill him;** but if it be a daughter, then she shall live" (Exodus 1:16). **And afterward,** he decreed with regard to the Jewish infants: **"Every son that is born you shall cast into the river"** (Exodus 1:22). **And ultimately, he decreed even on his own nation** that Egyptian infant boys should be cast into the river as well.

The Torah states: *vayetzav Paroh lechol amo* — Pharaoh commanded his entire nation that every son born should be thrown into the Nile. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina notes that the phrase *lechol amo* indicates that the edict extended even to Pharaoh's own people — Egyptian children as well. Rashi, drawing on the Midrash Tanchuma, explains why: Pharaoh's astrologers had seen that the savior of Israel would be born on that day, but could not determine whether he was Egyptian or Hebrew. Since the identity was ambiguous — and indeed Moshe grew up in Pharaoh's palace and was even called *ish Mitzri* by the daughters of Yisro — Pharaoh cast the net wide and commanded that all male children born that day be drowned.

### Three Decrees

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina also enumerates three distinct decrees that Pharaoh issued. First, the instruction to the midwives: if it is a boy, kill him. Second, the broader command to throw every Hebrew male newborn into the Nile. Third, the final sweeping edict to his own people to cast even their own sons born on that day into the river. The decrees thus escalated in scope and cruelty.

### Amram Divorces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּלֶךְ אִישׁ מִבֵּית לֵוִי״. לְהֵיכָן הָלַךְ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִינָא: שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעֲצַת בִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And there went a man of the house of Levi,** and took for a wife a daughter of Levi" (Exodus 2:1). The Gemara asks: **To where did he go? Rav Yehuda bar Zevina says: He went according to the advice of his daughter** Miriam, as the Gemara will proceed to explain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: עַמְרָם גְּדוֹל הַדּוֹר הָיָה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאָה שֶׁאָמַר פַּרְעֹה הָרָשָׁע ״כׇּל הַבֵּן הַיִּלּוֹד הַיְאֹרָה תַּשְׁלִיכֻהוּ״, אָמַר: לַשָּׁוְא אָנוּ עֲמֵלִין, עָמַד וְגֵירַשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. עָמְדוּ כּוּלָּן וְגֵירְשׁוּ אֶת נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **teaches: Amram,** the father of Moses, **was the great man of his generation. Once he saw that the wicked Pharaoh said: "Every son that is born you shall cast into the river,** and every daughter you shall save alive" (Exodus 1:22), **he said: We are laboring for nothing** by bringing children into the world to be killed. Therefore, **he arose and divorced his wife. All** others who saw this followed his example and **arose and divorced their wives.**

The Torah then says: *vayelech ish mibeit Levi* — a man went from the house of Levi. This is Amram taking Yocheved. But the word *vayelech*, "he went," requires explanation. Rav Yehuda bar Zvina says: *she'halach be'atzat bito* — he followed the advice of his daughter. This is the famous *midrash* connecting back to Miriam.

*Amram gadol hador hayah* — Amram was the preeminent figure of his generation. Once Pharaoh's edict to throw every newborn son into the Nile was issued, Amram reasoned: *l'shav anu ameilim* — it is for nothing that we labor, meaning that the effort of having children was futile if the boys would immediately be murdered. The word *ameilim* is slightly unusual here — it is the women who bear the burdens of pregnancy and birth — but the sense is clear. He then rose and divorced his wife. The purpose of the public divorce, rather than simply ceasing marital relations, seems to have been to make a statement that would prompt others to follow: *amdu kulam vegeirshu et neshoseihem* — all the men rose and divorced their wives.

### Miriam's Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ בִּתּוֹ: אַבָּא, קָשָׁה גְּזֵירָתְךָ יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁל פַּרְעֹה. שֶׁפַּרְעֹה לֹא גָּזַר אֶלָּא עַל הַזְּכָרִים, וְאַתָּה גָּזַרְתָּ עַל הַזְּכָרִים וְעַל הַנְּקֵיבוֹת. פַּרְעֹה לָא גָּזַר אֶלָּא בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וְאַתָּה בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וּלְעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **His daughter,** Miriam, **said to him: Father, your decree is more harsh** for the Jewish people **than that of Pharaoh, as Pharaoh decreed only with regard to the males, but you decreed** both **on the males and on the females.** And now no children will be born. Additionally, **Pharaoh decreed** to kill them **only in this world, but you** decreed **in this world and in the World-to-Come,** as those not born will not enter the World-to-Come.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּרְעֹה הָרָשָׁע — סָפֵק מִתְקַיֶּימֶת גְּזֵירָתוֹ, סָפֵק אֵינָהּ מִתְקַיֶּימֶת. אַתָּה צַדִּיק, בְּוַדַּאי שֶׁגְּזֵירָתְךָ מִתְקַיֶּימֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתִגְזַר אוֹמֶר וְיָקׇם לָךְ״. עָמַד וְהֶחְזִיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, עָמְדוּ כּוּלָּן וְהֶחְזִירוּ אֶת נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Miriam continued: Additionally, concerning **Pharaoh the wicked,** it is **uncertain** whether **his decree** will be **fulfilled,** and it is **uncertain** if his decree will **not** be **fulfilled. You are a righteous person,** and as such, **your decrees** will **certainly** be **fulfilled, as it is stated** with regard to the righteous: **"You shall also decree a thing, and it shall be established unto you"** (Job 22:28). Amram accepted his daughter's words and **arose and brought back,** i.e., remarried, **his wife,** and **all** others who saw this followed his example and **arose and brought back their wives.**

Miriam confronted her father: *abba, kasha gzeiratcha yoser mishel Paroh* — Father, your decree is harsher than Pharaoh's. The argument unfolds in three parts. First: Pharaoh decreed only against the males, but your act ensures that no female children will be born either. Second: Pharaoh decreed only for this world; by not bringing children into existence, you deny even those potential souls their share in *olam haba* — for even a child who is killed immediately has a soul that merits *olam haba*. Third — and this is the most striking element: *Paroh harasha, safek miskayemet gezeiraso safek einah miskayemet* — Pharaoh is a *rasha*, and it is uncertain whether his decrees will be fulfilled. But you, *tzaddik*, your decrees will certainly be fulfilled: *she'ne'emar vetigzar omer veyakom lach* — for it is said that when the righteous decree, it is established. Therefore, what you are doing is actually more harmful than what the *rasha* is doing.

### Remarriage Celebration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקַּח״. ״וַיַּחְזִיר״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִינָא: שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ מַעֲשֵׂה לִיקּוּחִין — הוֹשִׁיבָהּ בְּאַפִּרְיוֹן, וְאַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם מְרַקְּדִין לְפָנֶיהָ, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אָמְרוּ ״אֵם הַבָּנִים שְׂמֵחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If Amram remarried Jochebed, rather than say: **"And took** for a wife a daughter of Levi" (Exodus 2:1), **it should have** stated: **"And returned** for a wife the daughter of Levi." **Rav Yehuda bar Zevina says: He performed an act of marriage** just as one would do for a first marriage. **He sat her on a palanquin [*appiryon*], and Aaron and Miriam danced before her, and the ministering angels said: "A joyful mother of children"** (Psalms 113:9).

*Amad vehechezir et ishto* — Amram listened to Miriam and remarried Yocheved. *Amdu kulam vehecheziru et nesheihem* — and all the men followed suit. The *pasuk*'s language of *vayelech ish* and *vayikach et bat Levi* sounds as though this is a first marriage. This is precisely why the Gemara had to construct this backstory: Aharon and Miriam are clearly older than Moshe, as the *pasuk* later says *vatitatzev achito merachok* — his sister stood at a distance — so there must have been earlier children. The divorce and remarriage account explains the *pasuk*'s language while preserving the chronology.

Rabbi Yehuda bar Bizna adds: *she'asah lah ma'aseh likuchin* — he made of this remarriage a grand first-wedding celebration. *Hoshivah b'apiryon* — he seated her on a throne; *ve'Aharon veMiryam merakedin lefaneha* — Aharon and Miriam danced before her; *malachei hashareit amru* — the ministering angels proclaimed: *eim habanim semeichah* — the mother of children is rejoicing. Whether this refers to Yocheved's existing children or anticipates the birth of Moshe, the setting is one of great celebration and joy — which provides the backdrop for the conception and birth of Moshe.

## Yocheved's Birth

### Age Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶת בַּת לֵוִי״. אֶפְשָׁר בַּת מֵאָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים שָׁנָה הָוְיָא וְקָרֵי לַהּ ״בַּת״, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: זוֹ יוֹכֶבֶד, שֶׁהוֹרָתָהּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְלֵידָתָהּ בֵּין הַחוֹמוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה אוֹתָהּ לְלֵוִי בְּמִצְרָיִם״ —
>
> **Translation:** The verse is referring to Jochebed as **"a daughter of Levi"** (Exodus 2:1). The Gemara asks: Is it **possible** that this is Jochebed? Jochebed **was** then **130 years old and** the verse still **calls her a daughter?** Jochebed's age is established based on a tradition concerning the number of the descendants of Jacob who came to Egypt, as follows: While the verse states that Leah had thirty-three descendants (Genesis 46:15), only thirty-two were enumerated. This was explained **as Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** The "daughter of Levi" **is Jochebed, whose conception was on the road,** as the family of Jacob descended to Egypt, **and she was born between the walls,** i.e., in Egypt, **as it is stated:** "And the name of Amram's wife was Jochebed, the daughter of Levi, **who was born to Levi in Egypt"** (Numbers 26:59).

The *pasuk* calls Yocheved *bat Levi*, daughter of Levi, which implies youthfulness. The Gemara raises the difficulty: *efshar bat me'ah ushloshim shanah hava'i vekari lah bat?* — is it possible to call her a *bat*, a daughter, when she was 130 years old at the time of Moshe's birth? The tradition establishing her age is as follows: if she was born when Yaakov's family descended to Egypt and she was then 130 years old when Moshe was born, that aligns with Moshe being 80 years old at the time of the Exodus, having been born 130 years into the Egyptian sojourn.

### Yocheved Born in Egypt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵידָתָהּ בְּמִצְרַיִם, וְאֵין הוֹרָתָהּ בְּמִצְרַיִם. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בָּהּ סִימָנֵי נַעֲרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** This interpolation concerning her birth is interpreted: **Her birth** was **in Egypt, but her conception was not in Egypt.** Since the Jewish people were in Egypt for two hundred ten years and Moses was eighty years old at the time of the exodus, Jochebed was one hundred thirty years old when Moses was born. In light of this, the Gemara is asking how the verse can refer to her as a daughter. **Rabbi Yehuda says: The signs of a young woman were born in her** when her husband remarried her, and she became like a young girl again.

Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina addresses this through a textual reading: *zo Yocheved she'horatah baderech veleIdatah bein hachomot* — Yocheved's conception occurred while her mother was traveling down to Egypt, and she was born at the very moment of entry. The proof is the *pasuk*: *asher yaldah otah le-Levi beMitzrayim* — which is read as: her birth (*leidatah*) was in Egypt, but her conception (*horatah*) was not in Egypt. This could only be the case if she was born at the precise instant of entry into Egypt — between the walls, *bein hachomot*, at the city gates.

### 70 Souls Problem

This interpretation resolves a well-known difficulty in the *pesukim*. When the Torah enumerates the souls who descended to Egypt, the count comes to 69, yet the Torah states there were 70. The answer is Yocheved: she was only being gestated during the journey, so there were 69 persons traveling, but the moment they entered Egypt she was born, making 70. This is the interpretive motivation for Rabbi Chama's reading.

If Yocheved was born upon the entry to Egypt and the sojourn lasted 210 years, with Moshe being born 130 years in and living to 80 at the time of the Exodus, then Yocheved was indeed 130 years old at Moshe's birth. The question of how she can be called *bat* remains.

### Yocheved's Youth

Rabbi Yehuda answers: the signs of her youth returned to her, as they had to Sarah — she regained the physiological markers of youthfulness, including the ability to conceive. The *Ibn Ezra* raises a sharp objection: by Sarah, the Torah makes an enormous point of the miraculous nature of her conceiving at 90, yet here at 130 the Torah passes over it without comment. The *Ramban* responds at length, noting that there are many hidden miracles the Torah does not record explicitly. In any case, this is the Gemara's position.

### Three-Month Pregnancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּהַר הָאִשָּׁה וַתֵּלֶד בֵּן״. וְהָא הֲוָת מִיעַבְּרָא בֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא יַרְחֵי מֵעִיקָּרָא! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִינָא: מַקִּישׁ לֵידָתָהּ לְהוֹרָתָהּ: מָה הוֹרָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּצַעַר — אַף לֵידָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּצַעַר. מִכָּאן לְנָשִׁים צִדְקָנִיּוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ בְּפִיתְקָהּ שֶׁל חַוָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states concerning Moses: **"And the woman conceived, and bore a son;** and when she saw him that he was a goodly child, she hid him three months" (Exodus 2:2). The Gemara asks: **But** Jochebed **was pregnant with** Moses **for three months at the outset,** before Amram remarried her, as will be explained further. **Rav Yehuda bar Zevina said:** The intention of the verse is to **juxtapose her** giving **birth to her becoming pregnant. Just as her becoming pregnant was without pain, so too, her** giving **birth was without pain. From here** it is derived **concerning righteous women that they were not included in the verdict [*pitkah*] of Eve** that a woman will suffer pain during childbirth (see Genesis 3:16).

Rav Yehuda bar Zvina adds a further detail that will become important shortly: Yocheved was already three months pregnant at the time of Amram's remarriage. The great celebratory *vayikach* — all that fanfare of the wedding, the dancing, the angels singing — occurred when she had already been carrying Moshe for three months. We will see momentarily why the Gemara requires this detail.

## Moshe's Birth

### Painless Birth

The *pasuk* says *vatahar ha'isha vateled ben* — the woman became pregnant and gave birth to a son. If she was already pregnant at the time of the remarriage, why does the Torah speak of her pregnancy as beginning now? Rav Yehuda bar Zvina answers: *makish leidatah le-horatah* — the Torah juxtaposes her giving birth to her conceiving in order to teach something. *Mah horatah she'lo betza'ar af leidatah she'lo betza'ar* — just as her conception was without pain, so too her birth was without pain.

The connection between conception and birth here teaches the principle: *mikan l'nashim tzidkaniyot she'lo hayu befitko shel Chava* — righteous women are not subject to the curse of Chava, who was told she would give birth in pain. It is worth noting that when the epidural became available and voices in some religious communities raised objections on theological grounds — citing the curse of Chava — there was never any corresponding objection from the Jewish community. For us, a curse is not something to be preserved but to be escaped, and here the Gemara makes clear that righteous women stand outside it.

### House of Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּרֶא אוֹתוֹ כִּי טוֹב הוּא״. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: ״טוֹב״ שְׁמוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״טוֹבִיָּה״ שְׁמוֹ. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: הָגוּן לִנְבִיאוּת. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: נוֹלַד כְּשֶׁהוּא מָהוּל. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹלַד מֹשֶׁה, נִתְמַלֵּא הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ אוֹר. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַתֵּרֶא אוֹתוֹ כִּי טוֹב הוּא״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַיַּרְא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הָאוֹר כִּי טוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states with regard to the birth of Moses: "And the woman conceived, and bore a son; **and when she saw him that he was a goodly [*tov*] child,** she hid him three months" (Exodus 2:2). It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says: "Tov" is his,** Moses', real **name,** as it was given to him by his parents when he was born. **Rabbi Yehuda says: His name was Toviya. Rabbi Neḥemya says:** They said he was good because they saw that he was **fit for prophecy. Others say:** They said he was good because he was **born when he was** already **circumcised. And the Rabbis say: At the time when Moses was born, the entire house was filled with light,** as **it is written here: "And when she saw him that he was a goodly [*tov*] child," and it is written there: "And God saw the light, that it was good [*tov*]"** (Genesis 1:4).

The Torah says *vatere oso ki tov hu* — she saw that he was good. The question is what this means. Every mother finds her child beautiful; what does this observation tell us that is exceptional? The sages offer multiple answers.

The view of the Sages is that when Moshe was born, *nitmalei habayis kulo or* — the entire house filled with light. This is the image most are familiar with, as Rashi quotes it on the *pasuk* in the Torah. The connection to *ki tov* is drawn from a *gezerah shavah*: the same phrase appears at the creation of light — *vayar Elokim et ha'or ki tov* — and just as God saw the light and called it good, Yocheved saw Moshe and perceived the divine light radiating from him.

### Name Interpretations

Rabbi Meir says: *tov shemo* — *ki tov hu* simply means his name was Tov. This gives Moshe a Hebrew name bestowed by his mother, which is meaningful since *Moshe* is in fact an Egyptian name — present, for example, in *Rameses*, which means *Ra-Moshe*, born of Ra. *Ki min hamayim meshisihu* — "for I drew him from the water" — uses a Hebrew root that in context carries an Egyptian connotation of birth from water. Rabbi Meir's reading restores a Hebrew name to him.

Rabbi Yehuda says his name was Tuvia — reading *ki tov hu* as *tov* plus *Yah*, the divine name: God is good. Rabbi Nechemia says: *hogun lenevu'ah* — she perceived that he possessed the potential for prophecy, though the text does not indicate how she perceived this. *Acheirim* say he was *nolad keshe'hu mahul* — born already circumcised, without a foreskin, which was itself a visible sign of his singular nature.

### Three-Month Hiding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּצְפְּנֵהוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה יְרָחִים״ — דְּלָא מְנוֹ מִצְרִיִּם אֶלָּא מִשָּׁעָה דְּאַהְדְּרַהּ, וְהִיא הֲוָת מִיעַבְּרָא בֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא יַרְחֵי מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And she hid him three months"** (Exodus 2:2). The Gemara explains that she was able to hide him for three months **because the Egyptians counted** the nine months of her pregnancy **only from the time** her husband **took her back, but she was pregnant with** Moses **for three months from the outset** of her remarriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא יָכְלָה עוֹד הַצְּפִינוֹ״. אַמַּאי? תִּצְפְּנֵיהּ וְתֵיזִיל! אֶלָּא, כֹּל הֵיכָא דַּהֲווֹ שָׁמְעִי מִצְרָאֵי דְּמִתְיְלִיד יָנוֹקָא, מַמְטוּ יָנוֹקֵי הָתָם כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִישְׁמְעִינְהוּ וּמְעַוֵּי בַּהֲדַיְהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶחֱזוּ לָנוּ שׁוּעָלִים שׁוּעָלִים קְטַנִּים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And when she could no longer hide him"** (Exodus 2:3). The Gemara asks: **Why** couldn't she hide him any longer? **Let her continue to hide him. Rather, anywhere that the Egyptians heard that a baby was born** and they wanted to locate the baby, **they would bring** another **baby there in order that it could be heard** crying, **and** the two babies **would cry together, as it is written: "Take us the foxes, the little foxes,** that spoil the vineyards; for our vineyards are in blossom" (Song of Songs 2:15). The infants who were used to uncover the hidden babies are referred to as little foxes.

*Vatispenehu shlosha yerachim* — she hid him for three months. The Gemara asks: why only three months? Why could she not simply continue hiding him? The answer requires understanding how the Egyptians were tracking the situation. When the Egyptians saw that the Hebrew men had remarried their wives, they began counting nine months forward from that date, anticipating when births would occur. They then sent informants to locate hidden babies through a disturbing method: *kol heichah de'hava shamei Mitzrayim demisyalid yanuka, mamtu yanuka hatam* — wherever Egyptians heard that a baby was expected, they would bring an infant of their own, slap it to make it cry, and the hidden baby would cry in response, revealing its location. Based on a *pasuk* in Shir HaShirim — *achazo lanu shualim shualim ketanim* — the small foxes, little babies used to ferret out other babies.

Here the three-month detail becomes crucial. *Delo manu Mitzrayim ela mish'as hadra* — the Egyptians counted nine months from the date of the remarriage. But Yocheved was already three months pregnant at that point. So they expected birth nine months from the remarriage, while Moshe was actually born six months after the remarriage — three months earlier than the Egyptians anticipated. This bought Yocheved three months of undetected concealment. Once that window closed and the Egyptians began actively searching, she could no longer hide him.

## The Basket

### Wicker Material

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּקַּח לוֹ תֵּבַת גֹּמֶא״. מַאי שְׁנָא גּוֹמֶא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִיכָּן לְצַדִּיקִים שֶׁמָּמוֹנָם חָבִיב עֲלֵיהֶן יוֹתֵר מִגּוּפָן. וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין פּוֹשְׁטִין יְדֵיהֶן בְּגָזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "And when she could no longer hide him, **she took for him an ark of bulrushes,** and daubed it with bitumen and with pitch; and she put the child therein, and laid it in the willows by the river's bank" (Exodus 2:3). The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **bulrushes** that she decided to use them? **Rabbi Elazar says: From here** it is derived **concerning righteous people that their money is more precious to them than their bodies,** as she took an inexpensive material to build the ark. **And why** do they care **so much** about their money? **Because they do not stretch out their hands to** partake of **stolen property.** Therefore, their own property is very precious to them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: דָּבָר רַךְ, שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי דָּבָר רַךְ וּבִפְנֵי דָּבָר קָשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** an alternative reason for her taking bulrushes for the ark: She took **a soft material** like bulrush, **which is able to withstand** an impact both **before a soft item and before a hard item.** She feared that if she would have made the box from a hard material like wood, if it were to collide with a hard item in the water it might break.

*Vatikach lo teivat gome* — she took a basket of wicker reeds. Why specifically wicker? Rabbi Elazar says: *mikan l'tzadikim shemamounam chaviv aleihem yoter migufam* — from here we learn that the righteous value their money more than their bodies, choosing inexpensive materials. And why are they so careful with money? *Lefi she'ein poshtin yedeihem begazel* — because they scrupulously avoid theft, they are equally careful with what they spend. This is a striking interpretation, and the Gemara immediately senses the difficulty: being economical is one thing, but applying that principle to a basket meant to save your child's life seems misplaced. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani therefore offers a more functional explanation: wicker was chosen because it is flexible — *davar rach she'yachol la'amod bifnei davar rach uvifnei davar kasheh* — it can absorb impact whether it encounters something soft or something hard. A rigid material would risk puncturing and sinking; a flexible one could bend without breaking.

### Plastering Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּחְמְרָה בַחֵמָר וּבַזָּפֶת״, תָּנָא: חֵמָר מִבִּפְנִים וְזֶפֶת מִבַּחוּץ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָרִיחַ אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק רֵיחַ רַע.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And daubed it with bitumen and with pitch"** (Exodus 2:3). A Sage **teaches:** She daubed **bitumen on the interior and pitch on the exterior, so that righteous person,** i.e., Moses, **would not smell a foul odor,** such as that of pitch.

*Vatachmira vachamer uvazafet* — she plastered it with slime and pitch. A *tanna* teaches: *chamer mibifnim vezafet mibachutz* — the slime was on the inside and the pitch was on the outside. The reason: *kedei shelo yariach oso tzaddik rei'ach ra* — so that the *tzaddik*, the infant Moshe, should not have to smell the foul odor of the pitch. The plaster layer on the interior served to insulate him from the smell of the tar on the outside.

### Placement in Reeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתָּשֶׂם בָּהּ אֶת הַיֶּלֶד וַתָּשֶׂם בַּסּוּף״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יַם סוּף. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: אֲגַם, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״קָנֶה וָסוּף קָמֵלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And she put the child therein, and laid it in the willows [*bassuf*]"** (Exodus 2:3). **Rabbi Elazar says:** This means she placed him **in the Suf Sea,** i.e., the Red Sea. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** She placed him in **a marsh, as it is written: "The reeds and willows [*suf*] shall wither"** (Isaiah 19:6).

*Vatasem basuf* — she placed it among the *suf*. Rabbi Elazar says this refers to *Yam Suf*; Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says *suf* simply means a swamp or reed bed, as the *pasuk* elsewhere uses *kaneh vasuf* to refer to reeds, making *suf* a species of aquatic plant.

### Pharaoh's Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּרֶד בַּת פַּרְעֹה לִרְחוֹץ עַל הַיְאֹר״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁיָּרְדָה לִרְחוֹץ מִגִּלּוּלֵי [בֵּית] אָבִיהָ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִם רָחַץ ה׳ אֵת צוֹאַת בְּנוֹת צִיּוֹן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And the daughter of Pharaoh came down to bathe [*lirḥotz*] in the river"** (Exodus 2:5). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** This **teaches that she came down** to the river **to cleanse** herself **from** the impurity of **her father's idols,** as she was immersing herself as part of the conversion process. **And similarly it states: "When the Lord shall have washed [*raḥatz*] away the filth of the daughters of Zion,** and shall have purged the blood of Jerusalem from the midst thereof, by the spirit of judgment, and by the spirit of destruction" (Isaiah 4:4). This washing clearly refers to the purging of spiritual sins, rather than bathing for the sake of cleanliness.

*Vaterd bat Paroh lirchotz al haYe'or* — Pharaoh's daughter went down to bathe at the Nile. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai explains: *melamed sheyardah lirchotz migilulei aviha* — she went to cleanse herself from the excrement, meaning the idolatry of her father. The washing was a conversion ritual. The proof: the *pasuk* in Yeshayahu uses the image of washing to describe the removal of spiritual filth — *im rachatz Hashem et tzoa'at bnot Tzion* — establishing that washing can represent purification from sin rather than physical bathing.

### Maidservants Killed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנַעֲרֹתֶיהָ הוֹלְכוֹת וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הֲלִיכָה זוֹ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן מִיתָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי הוֹלֵךְ לָמוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And her maidens walked** along **[*holekhot*]** by the riverside" (Exodus 2:5). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This walking** is **nothing other** than the **terminology of** going toward **death, and similarly it states: "Behold, I am going [*holekh*] to die"** (Genesis 25:32).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּרֶא אֶת הַתֵּיבָה בְּתוֹךְ הַסּוּף״, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא בָעוּ לְאַצּוֹלֵי לְמֹשֶׁה, אָמְרוּ לָהּ: גְּבִירְתֵּנוּ, מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם מֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם גּוֹזֵר גְּזֵירָה, אִם כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ אֵין מְקַיְּימִין אוֹתָהּ — בָּנָיו וּבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ מְקַיְּימִין אוֹתָהּ, וְאַתְּ עוֹבֶרֶת עַל גְּזֵירַת אָבִיךְ? בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וַחֲבָטָן בַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And she saw the ark among the willows"** (Exodus 2:5). **Once** her maidens **saw that** the daughter of Pharaoh **was intending to save Moses, they said to her: Our mistress, the custom of the world** is that when **a king of flesh and blood decrees a decree,** even **if all the world does not fulfill it,** at least **his children and members of his household fulfill it, and** yet **you are violating the decree of your father.** After the maidens tried to convince her not to save Moses, the angel **Gabriel came and beat them to the ground** and they died.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר שִׁפְחָתָהּ, הָא אָמְרַתְּ בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וַחֲבָטָן בַּקַּרְקַע! דְּשַׁיַּיר לַהּ חֲדָא, דְּלָאו אוֹרְחַהּ דְּבַת מַלְכָּא לְמֵיקַם לְחוֹדַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that it means **her maidservant, didn't you say** earlier: **Gabriel came and beat them to the ground** and the maidservants died, so how could Pharaoh's daughter send her? The Gemara answers: It must be **that** Gabriel **left her one** maidservant, **as it is not proper that a princess should stand alone.**

*Vena'aroteha holchot* — her maidservants were walking alongside her. Rabbi Yochanan reads *holchot* here as a euphemism for death, in the same way the *pasuk* says *hineh anochi holech lamut* — I am going to die. When the maidservants saw that Pharaoh's daughter intended to rescue Moshe, they protested: even when the whole world does not obey a king's decree, his own household should. How could she violate her father's *gezeirah*? *Ba Gavriel vechavatam bakarka* — the angel Gavriel came and struck them into the ground, killing them.

The parallel to the earlier narrative is worth noting. Amram issued a *gezeirah* and his daughter Miriam rose to undermine it through persuasion. Here, Pharaoh issued a *gezeirah* and his daughter bat Paroh rose to violate it through action. A *tzaddik*'s decree is certain to stand; a *rasha*'s is not. The same principle echoes through both episodes.

One maidservant was left alive. *Delav orcha de-bas malka lemeikam lechudeih* — it would not be proper for the king's daughter to stand entirely alone without an attendant. Gavriel spared one for the sake of decorum.

## Discovery & Rescue

### Extended Arm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּשְׁלַח אֶת אֲמָתָהּ וַתִּקָּחֶהָ״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, חַד אָמַר: יָדָהּ, וְחַד אָמַר: שִׁפְחָתָהּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר יָדָהּ — דִּכְתִיב ״אַמָּתָהּ״. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר שִׁפְחָתָהּ — מִדְּלָא כְּתִיב ״יָדָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse concludes: **"And she sent *amatah* to take it"** (Exodus 2:5). **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya** disagree as to the definition of the word "*amatah*." **One says** that it means **her arm, and one says** that it means **her maidservant.** The Gemara explains: **The one who says** that it means **her arm** explained it in this manner, **as it is written "*amatah*,"** which denotes her forearm. **And the one who says** that it means **her maidservant** explained it in this manner **because it does not** explicitly **write** the more common term: **Her hand [*yadah*].** Therefore, he understands that this is the alternative term for a maidservant, *ama*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר שִׁפְחָתָהּ, הָא אָמְרַתְּ בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וַחֲבָטָן בַּקַּרְקַע! אָמְרוּ: דְּשַׁיַּיר לַהּ חֲדָא, דְּלָאו אוֹרְחַהּ דְּבַת מַלְכָּא לְמֵיקַם לְחוֹדַהּ. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר יָדָהּ, לִיכְתּוֹב ״יָדָהּ״! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאִישְׁתַּרְבַּב אִישְׁתַּרְבּוֹבֵי. דְּאָמַר מָר: וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בְּאַמָּתָהּ שֶׁל בַּת פַּרְעֹה, וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בְּשִׁינֵּי רְשָׁעִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׁנֵּי רְשָׁעִים שִׁבַּרְתָּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״שִׁבַּרְתָּ״, אֶלָּא ״שֶׁרִיבַּבְתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that it means **her hand, let** the Torah **write** explicitly: **Her hand [*yadah*].** Why use the more unusual term *amatah*? The Gemara answers: **This** verse **teaches us that her arm extended [*ishtarbav*]** many cubits. **As the Master said** in another context: **And similarly you find with regard to the hand of Pharaoh's daughter** that it extended, **and similarly you find with regard to the teeth of evildoers, as it is written: "You have broken [*shibbarta*] the teeth of the wicked"** (Psalms 3:8), **and Reish Lakish said: Do not read** the word as ***shibbarta*, rather** read it as ***sheribbavta*,** you have extended.

*Vatishlach et amatah vatikacheha* — she sent her *amah* and took the basket. A dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemia: one says *amah* means her hand — from *amah*, the cubit measure, referring to her arm — and the other says it means her maidservant, the standard meaning of *amah*. The one who reads *shifchah*, maidservant, asks: if you mean hand, why not use the clearer word *yad*? The one who reads *amah* as arm asks: if you mean maidservant, it should have said *shifchah*; the word *amah* is ambiguous.

If *amah* means her arm, then what does the text convey by using this unusual word? *De'ishtarbovei ishtarbuba* — her arm miraculously extended, stretching out an *amah* beyond its natural reach to grasp the basket. This is compared to Achashveirosh extending the scepter to Esther, and to the *pasuk*: *shinei resha'im shibarta* — Resh Lakish reads not *shibarta*, "you broke their teeth," but *sherivavta* — "you caused them to elongate," another miraculous extension in a different context.

### Seeing Divine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּפְתַּח וַתִּרְאֵהוּ אֶת הַיֶּלֶד״. ״וַתֵּרֶא״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שֶׁרָאֲתָה שְׁכִינָה עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And she opened it and saw it [*vatirehu*], even the child"** (Exodus 2:6). The Gemara comments: The verse states: "And she saw it"; **it should have** stated: **And she saw. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** In addition to Moses, **she saw the Divine Presence with him.** This is indicated by the usage of "saw it."

*Vatiftach vatireihu et hayeled* — she opened the basket and saw the child. The Hebrew uses *vatireihu* — she saw *him* — when we might have expected simply *vatire*, she saw the child. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina explains: *she'ra'atah Shechinah imo* — she saw the divine presence accompanying the child. *Vatireihu* — she saw God *et hayeled*, with the child. This connects directly to the earlier teaching that the house filled with light at Moshe's birth: here too, bat Paroh perceives something supernatural.

### Child Crying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִנֵּה נַעַר בֹּכֶה״. קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״יֶלֶד״, וְקָרֵי לֵיהּ ״נַעַר״. תָּנָא: הוּא יֶלֶד וְקוֹלוֹ כְּנַעַר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: אִם כֵּן, עֲשִׂיתוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ בַּעַל מוּם. אֶלָּא, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָשְׂתָה לוֹ אִמּוֹ חוּפַּת נְעוּרִים בַּתֵּיבָה, אָמְרָה: שֶׁמָּא לֹא אֶזְכֶּה לְחוּפָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "And saw it, even the child [*yeled*]; **and behold a lad [*na'ar*] that wept"** (Exodus 2:6). The verse **calls him "a child [*yeled*]," and** the same verse **calls him "a lad [*na'ar*]."** A Sage **teaches: He is** the age of **a child but his voice** is as loud and deep **as a lad;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Neḥemya said to him: If** that is **so, you made Moses our teacher blemished,** since his voice was unusually deep. **Rather,** this **teaches that his mother made a canopy of youth,** i.e., a small canopy, **for him in the ark,** as **she said: Perhaps I will not merit to** see **his** wedding **canopy.**

*Vehinei naar bocheh* — there was a child crying. The text uses two different words: *yeled*, an infant, and *naar*, a young man. A *tanna* offers: *hu yeled vekolo ke'naar* — he was an infant in body but his voice was that of a young man, a powerful, adult cry. This is the view of Rabbi Yehuda.

Rabbi Nechemia objects: *im kein asita lo l'Moshe Rabbeinu b'mum* — you have made Moshe Rabbeinu into a person with a defect. For an infant to have so deep and powerful a voice would itself be a physical anomaly, a blemish. Rashi adds an additional dimension: since the Levites served as singers in the *Beit HaMikdash*, such an aberrant voice in childhood would, somewhat anachronistically, invalidate someone from that service.

Rabbi Nechemia therefore offers an alternative reading: *melamed she'asta lo imo chuppat ne'urim* — his mother had made him a small *chuppah*, a wedding canopy, inside the basket. *Bateivah amrah: shema lo ezkeh l'chuppato* — in placing him in the basket she thought, perhaps I will never live to see his wedding, so let me make him one now. The word *naar* thus refers not to his voice but to this miniature *chuppah*, recalling the wedding celebration that had marked his parents' remarriage.

### Circumcision Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּחְמֹל עָלָיו וַתֹּאמֶר מִיַּלְדֵי הָעִבְרִים זֶה״. מְנָא יָדְעָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שֶׁרָאֲתָה אוֹתוֹ מָהוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The verse concludes: **"And she had compassion on him, and said: This [*zeh*] is one of the Hebrews' children"** (Exodus 2:6). The Gemara asks: **From where did she know** that he was a Hebrew child? **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: As she saw** that **he was circumcised.**

*Vatomer miyaldei ha'ivrim zeh* — she said, this is one of the Hebrew children. How did she know? Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina: *she'ra'atah oso mahul* — she saw that he was circumcised. This is presumably distinct from the earlier tradition that he was born without a foreskin — here the implication is that he had been circumcised in the ordinary manner.

### Unwitting Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֶה״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּתְנַבְּאָה שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ — זֶה נוֹפֵל, וְאֵין אַחֵר נוֹפֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The Pharaoh's daughter said: **"This [*zeh*]** is one of the Hebrews' children" (Exodus 2:6). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This **teaches that she prophesied unknowingly,** as the intention of the word "*zeh*" was: **This** one **falls,** i.e., is cast, into the water, **but no other** will **fall** by means of water, for on that day Pharaoh's decree was canceled.

The word *zeh* — "this one" — receives close attention. Rabbi Yochanan says: *melamed she'nisnaveit shelo mida'atah* — she prophesied without knowing what she was saying. *Zeh nofel, ve'ein acher nofel* — this one was cast into the Nile, and no other would be. The moment Moshe was placed in the Nile, the astrological sign that had prompted the edict was read as fulfilled, and the decree ceased. Bat Paroh's words were thus an unconscious prophecy: *this* child, and no other, was "the one cast into the Nile" in the sense the edict had in mind.

## Astrologers Confused

### Saw by Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִי יֹאמְרוּ אֲלֵיכֶם דִּרְשׁוּ אֶל הָאֹבוֹת וְאֶל הַיִּדְּעֹנִים הַמְצַפְצְפִים וְהַמַּהְגִּים״: צוֹפִין וְאֵינָם יוֹדְעִין מָה צוֹפִין, מְהַגִּים וְאֵינָן יוֹדְעִים מָה מְהַגִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And this is what Rabbi Elazar said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And when they shall say to you: Seek unto the necromancers and the diviners, that chirp [*metzaftzefim*] and that mutter [*mahggim*]"** (Isaiah 8:19)? The explanation of their chirping and muttering is: **They see [*tzofin*], but they do not know what they are seeing; they enunciate [*mahggim*], but they do not know what they are enunciating.** Although necromancers and diviners do have some insight into the future, they do not see clearly enough to understand what they are actually seeing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאוּ שֶׁמּוֹשִׁיעָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמַּיִם הוּא לוֹקֶה, עָמְדוּ וְגָזְרוּ: ״כׇּל הַבֵּן הַיִּלּוֹד הַיְאֹרָה תַּשְׁלִיכֻהוּ״. כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁדְיוּהּ לְמֹשֶׁה, אָמְרוּ: תּוּ לָא חָזֵינַן כִּי הָהוּא סִימָנָא, בַּטִּלוּ לִגְזֵירְתַּיְיהוּ. וְהֵם אֵינָן יוֹדְעִין שֶׁעַל מֵי מְרִיבָה הוּא לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara applies this to Pharaoh: Pharaoh's astrologers **saw that the savior of the Jewish people would be stricken by water.** Therefore, **they arose and decreed: "Every son that is born you shall cast into the river"** (Exodus 1:22); they thought that their vision indicated that Moses would be killed in the water. **Once** Jochebed **cast Moses** into the water, although he was protected in an ark, the astrologers **said: We no longer see** in the stars anything **like that sign** we saw as to the downfall of the leader of the Jews by water, and therefore at that moment **they canceled their decree. But they did not know that** what they saw foretold that Moses **would be stricken on account of the waters of Meribah.** They envisioned a downfall for Moses by water but didn't fully comprehend their vision.

Rabbi Elazar now brings a *pasuk* from Yeshayahu that mocks those who practice necromancy and divination: *hamitzpitzpim vehamehaggim*, those who chirp and mutter. The Gemara reads this as a description of astrologers generally: *tzofim* — they see something — *mitzpitzpim* — they read signs — *ve'einam yodim mah tzofim* — but they do not fully understand what they are seeing; *mahagim* — they speak — *ve'einam yodim mah mahagim* — but they do not fully understand what they are saying.

What did Pharaoh's astrologers see? *Ra'u shemoshi'an shel Yisrael bamayim hu lokeh* — they saw that the savior of Israel would be punished by water. On this basis they issued the decree to cast all male children into the Nile.

### Edict Ceased

*Keivan deshadyuhu le-Moshe* — once Moshe was thrown into the Nile, *amru: tu lo chazina ki hai simana* — the astrologers said, we no longer see that sign in the heavens. *Batlu legezerataihu* — and they annulled the decree. Their reasoning: the savior has been cast into the water, punished, and is presumably dead. There is no longer cause for alarm. *Vehem einam yodim* — but they did not understand.

### Mei Merivah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״הֵמָּה מֵי מְרִיבָה אֲשֶׁר רָבוּ״ — הֵמָּה שֶׁרָאוּ אִיצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה וְטָעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "These [*hemma*] are the waters of Meribah, where** the children of Israel **strove** with the Lord, and He was sanctified in them" (Numbers 20:13)? The verse indicates that **these are** the waters **that the astrologers of Pharaoh saw and** on account of which they **erred.**

*Ki al Mei Merivah hu lokeh* — the savior would not be punished by the Nile but by the waters of Merivah. The sign the astrologers saw was real; their interpretation was wrong. Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina derives this from the *pasuk*: *hemah Mei Merivah asher ravu* — these are the waters of Merivah, the very same waters that the astrologers of Pharaoh had seen *vetau*, and about which they were confused. They saw water, they thought the Nile; the true referent was Merivah, where Moshe would later strike the rock rather than speak to it, and for that he would be denied entry into the land.

### Moshe's Credit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר מֹשֶׁה: ״שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת אֶלֶף רַגְלִי וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: בִּשְׁבִילִי נִצַּלְתֶּם כּוּלְּכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what Moses said:** "The people, among whom I am, **are six hundred thousand men on foot [*ragli*];** and yet You have said: I will give them flesh, that they may eat a whole month" (Numbers 11:21). **Moses said to the Jewish people: On account of me,** which is an alternative meaning of the word ragli, **all of you were saved,** as the decree to throw all males into the river was canceled on my account.

Finally: *shesh me'ot elef ragli* — the word *ragli* is read by the Gemara not as "foot soldiers" but as *bishvili*, "because of me." Moshe says to Israel: *bishvili nitzaltem kulchem* — all 600,000 of you were saved because of me. When I was cast into the Nile, the astrological sign was read as fulfilled, the edict ceased, and you were spared.

Tosafot raises the obvious objection: the entire edict was made in the first place because of Moshe, since it was his birth that the astrologers feared. How can he take credit for ending a decree that he caused? Tosafot answers: once Moshe was cast into the Nile and the astrologers declared the sign fulfilled, not only did the decree targeting Moshe's generation end — even the older, pre-astrological impulse to persecute and destroy the Israelites, which had manifested even in the initial orders to the midwives, ceased entirely. In that broader sense, Moshe brought about the complete cessation of the murderous decrees, and it is for this that he takes credit.
