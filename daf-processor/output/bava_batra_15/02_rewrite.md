# Bava Batra 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Order of Nevi'im

### Opening & Context

The daf begins on 14b, transitioning from the physical dimensions and arrangement of the *Sefer Torah* — including its relationship to the *aron* and the *luchot*, and the contrast between the utter inaccessibility of the *luchot* (accessible only to the *Kohen Gadol* on Yom Kippur) and the full accessibility of the *Sefer Torah* — to the broader conceptual questions about the books of *Tanakh*: their canonical order, and their authorship. The *tana'im* address these questions in a *baraita* that opens with the words *Sidran shel nevi'im* — the order of the prophetic books.

### Hoshea's Priority

The *baraita* enumerates the *nevi'im* in the following order: Yehoshua, Shoftim, Shmuel, Melachim — so far, a straightforwardly chronological sequence — then, strikingly, Yirmiyahu, Yechezkel, Yeshayahu, and finally the *Shneim Asar*. The Gemara immediately notices that the sequence of the *nevi'im acharonim* (the Later Prophets) does not match their chronological order and seeks to unpack the underlying logic.

The Gemara's first observation concerns Hoshea. The verse states, *tekhilat dibber Hashem be-Hoshea* — "The beginning of God's speaking was with Hoshea" — which would seem to imply Hoshea was the very first prophet. But surely this cannot mean first among all prophets from Moshe onward, given how many prophets preceded him. Rabbi Yochanan explains: Hoshea is described as first among the four prophets who prophesied during the same historical period — Hoshea, Yeshayahu, Amos, and Micha — and he was chronologically the earliest of those four. So within the *nevi'im acharonim*, Hoshea rightly claims priority.

### Hoshea & Trei Asar

The Gemara then asks: if Hoshea is the earliest of the *nevi'im acharonim*, why not place him first among them as a standalone book? The answer is that his prophecies are written together with those of Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — who represent the very last of the prophets, at the opening of the *Bayit Sheni* period — and since Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi are at the end, Hoshea is grouped with them. This is because the *Shneim Asar* constitutes a single book; each individual prophetic work within it is too small to stand as a separate scroll without risking being lost. The Gemara reasons very practically here: a separate scroll of small dimensions — a physically tiny book — *mirkas*, gets lost. The concern is explicitly about physical scrolls: if you produce a tiny independent scroll, it will lose its identity and be misplaced. Therefore, Hoshea is embedded within the *Trei Asar*, which in turn is placed at the end because of its association with the final prophets.

### Thematic Ordering

With the *Trei Asar* settled at the end, the Gemara turns to the order among the remaining *nevi'im acharonim*. Chronologically, the sequence should be Yeshayahu, then Yirmiyahu, then Yechezkel — and indeed that is the order familiar from any standard *Tanakh*. So why does the *baraita* list them as Yirmiyahu, Yechezkel, Yeshayahu?

The Gemara's answer is thematic rather than chronological: the book of Melachim concludes with destruction — *Melachim sofei churbana*. Yirmiyahu is entirely about prophesying destruction — *Yirmiyahu kulei churbana*. Yechezkel opens with destruction and closes with consolation — *reisha churbana ve-seifei nechamta*. And Yeshayahu is overwhelmingly oriented toward consolation and the vision of *acharis hayamim* — *Yeshayahu kulei nechamta*. The organizing principle is therefore: *samchinan churbana le-churbana ve-nechamta le-nechamta* — we attach destruction to destruction and consolation to consolation. There is a beautiful narrative arc: Melachim flows into Yirmiyahu, Yirmiyahu into Yechezkel, and Yechezkel — transitioning from *churban* to redemptive vision — into Yeshayahu, who points toward the ultimate *geulah*.

This order, it should be noted, is not the one we follow today, which raises the natural question of why current printed *Tanakhim* depart from it. The most likely practical explanation for our current arrangement is that the adoption of standardized chapter divisions — originally a Christian innovation — came along with concordances that made precise cross-referencing possible, including in the context of disputations, and our order shifted accordingly. The Koren *Tanakh* famously attempted to restore a distinctively Jewish chapter division when it was first published, but the effort was not adopted.

## Order of Ketuvim

### Ketuvim Order List

The *baraita* continues with the order of the *Ketuvim*: Rus, Sefer Tehillim, Iyov, Mishlei, Koheles, Shir HaShirim, Kinos (i.e., Eicha), Daniel, Megillas Esther, Ezra, and Divrei HaYamim. Unlike our familiar arrangement of *Ketuvim*, which is loosely thematic, this order is largely chronological. Rus comes first — it is set in the period of the Shoftim and is the story of the great-grandmother of David. Tehillim is David's. Iyov's placement will require separate discussion, as will the internal order of Shlomo's books. The three books of Shlomo — Mishlei, Koheles, and Shir HaShirim — follow. Then comes Kinos, which belongs to the period of the *churban*. Daniel is set during the period of exile between the two *Batei Mikdash*. Megillas Esther belongs to the later period of *Galus Bavel*. Ezra is the return. Divrei HaYamim, though its narrative begins far back in history, was composed at the end of this entire period and is placed last accordingly.

### Why Not Begin Badly

The Gemara asks: if Iyov lived in the time of Moshe, why not place his book first — before even Rus — since it would be the earliest? The answer is: *atchulei be-puranusa lo matchilinan* — we do not begin with catastrophe. The book of Iyov is fundamentally a story of devastating suffering.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: does not Rus also begin with suffering — famine, departure from the land, and death? The response distinguishes the two cases: Rus is *puranusa de-is leih achris* — a suffering that has a good end. The arc of Rus is ultimately uplifting; it is the foundation story of David HaMelech. The basic story is not one of calamity. The book of Iyov, by contrast, is suffused with *puranusa* throughout — and even its ending, where God gives Iyov new children to replace those who died, leaves one profoundly disturbed. It does not carry the quality of *geulah* that Rus does. Rabbi Yochanan adds a further dimension: the very name *Rus*, he teaches, alludes to the fact that from her descended David, who *rivahu le-Hakadosh Baruch Hu be-shiros ve-tishbachos* — who satiated the Holy One, blessed be He, with songs and praises. This also explains the natural flow from Rus directly into Tehillim.

### Iyov's Placement

Tosafos raises a pointed question about Iyov's placement between Tehillim and Mishlei. If Iyov was early, one might understand placing him immediately after Rus. But that would interrupt the organic connection between Tehillim (David) and the books of Shlomo (Mishlei, Koheles, Shir HaShirim). Conversely, if Iyov was late — among those who returned in the *Bayit Sheni* period, as one opinion holds — why is he not placed at the end, together with Ezra?

Tosafos answers with a striking observation: Iyov, Tehillim, and Mishlei share a *niggun echad* — a single cantillation system. Their *trop* is not the standard *trop* of the rest of *Tanakh*. The familiar system of *mercha-tipcha-munach-esnachta* gives way to a distinctive system applied only to these three books, reflecting a common poetic genre of short, parallel half-verses (*mikraos ketzarim*) arranged in a particular meter. This is not merely a feature of the written text but was the established manner of public reading going back to antiquity, as the Gemara itself notes in connection with the phrase *meforash be-shum sechel* — *pesukei ta'amei*, the system of cantillation marks. These three books belong together not just generically as poetry but specifically as a single type of poetry with a single system of recitation.

This explains why Iyov must appear between Tehillim and Mishlei: it allows the three books sharing this special *trop* to be grouped together, while also keeping Mishlei, Koheles, and Shir HaShirim — the three books of Shlomo — consecutively together. Placing Iyov here is the only arrangement that achieves both goals simultaneously.

Tosafos adds a further refinement in the name of Rabbeinu Tam: the grouping with Tehillim is also supported by the Gemara's later teaching that David authored Tehillim *al yedei asarah zekenim* — through ten elders — which creates an additional associative link among these books. The result is a placement that is not chronological but is the product of multiple overlapping considerations: genre, cantillation system, compositional grouping, and authorial association.

## Authorship Discussion

### Moshe & Yehoshua

Having addressed the *seder* of the *nevi'im* and *Ketuvim*, the *baraita* turns to the question of *mi katvan* — who wrote them. It is important to note at the outset that "writing" here does not necessarily mean who physically took pen to paper; the concept encompasses authorship, compilation, editing, and the process of giving a prophetic composition its final canonical form.

The *baraita* states: *Moshe katav sifro u-farshat Bilam* — Moshe wrote his own book and the portion of Bilam. It is striking that the portion of Bilam is identified separately, distinct from the rest of the Torah. The reason is apparent: the *parshat Bilam* is narrated entirely from an external frame of reference. The omniscient narrator who views events from the perspective of *Bnei Yisrael* is absent; instead, the reader is positioned within the perspective of Bilam and the nations looking at Israel from the outside. This distinctive vantage point explains why *Chazal* felt it warranted separate mention, even though it is entirely part of *Torat Moshe*.

The *baraita* adds: *ve-Iyov* — Moshe also wrote Iyov, under the assumption that Iyov lived in his time. This association is deeply meaningful: Moshe is the *navi* who asks *hodeini na et derachecha* — according to *Chazal*'s understanding, a plea to comprehend why the righteous suffer and the wicked prosper, the same question that lies at the heart of Iyov.

*Yehoshua katav sifro u-shmonah pesukim she-ba-Torah* — Yehoshua wrote his own book and the last eight *pesukim* of the Torah, which describe Moshe's death. The question of those eight *pesukim* is taken up at length in a later section.

### Yirmiyahu's Works

*Yirmiyahu katav sifro ve-Sefer Melachim ve-Kinos* — Yirmiyahu wrote his own book, the book of Melachim, and Kinos (Eicha). The assignment is thematically coherent: Melachim concludes with the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*, Yirmiyahu's prophecy is the voice of that destruction from within, and Eicha is its lamentation. The three form a unified literary and theological response to the *churban*, and they are attributed to the same prophetic voice.

### Chizkiyahu's Role

*Chizkiyahu va-siato kasvu Yeshayahu, Mishlei, Shir HaShirim ve-Koheles* — Chizkiyahu and his group wrote Yeshayahu, Mishlei, Shir HaShirim, and Koheles. This attribution requires unpacking, and Rashi provides the key: Yeshayahu was killed by Menashe and therefore never had the opportunity to commit his prophecies to a final written book. As Rashi explains, prophets would typically compose their books only near the end of their lives, once their prophecies were complete; a violent death deprived Yeshayahu of that opportunity. Chizkiyahu's group therefore took on the task.

For the books of Shlomo, the *baraita* in Mishlei itself supplies the evidence: it states explicitly, *asher he'etiku anshei Chizkiyah* — "which the men of Chizkiyah copied out." Shlomo's wisdom was delivered over a lifetime in separate units — individual *meshalim*, individual poems, individual meditations — and Chizkiyahu's group organized, compiled, and gave these compositions their final form as books. Rashi further notes that Chizkiyahu himself planted the culture that made this possible: he famously drew his sword over the entrance to the *beis midrash* and decreed that anyone who did not study Torah would be penalized, generating a generation of scholars capable of undertaking precisely this kind of literary-religious enterprise.

This raises a profound question about the nature of authorship. *He'etiku* — "they copied out" — suggests something more than transcription. Shlomo did not dictate a book called Mishlei in sequential chapters. The *meshalim* existed as separate oral or written units; Chizkiyahu's people collected, arranged, and edited them into a unified composition. The same almost certainly applies to Koheles and, perhaps more surprisingly, to Shir HaShirim — though Shir HaShirim does appear to consist of discrete poems rather than a single continuous allegory. The attribution of all three to Chizkiyahu's group may reflect a logical extension from the explicit biblical testimony in Mishlei: since they are credited with organizing that book, they are credited with organizing Shlomo's other works as well.

### Great Assembly

*Anshei Knesset HaGedolah kasvu Yechezkel, Shneim Asar, Daniel, u-Megillas Esther* — the members of the Great Assembly wrote Yechezkel, the *Trei Asar*, Daniel, and Megillas Esther. The Great Assembly operated at the beginning of the *Bayit Sheni* period, with Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi among its leading figures.

Why did Yechezkel himself not write his own book? Rashi says plainly that he does not know for certain, but suggests the possibility that prophecies received *be-chutz la-aretz* — outside of *Eretz Yisrael* — could not be given their final canonical form outside the land; the process of a prophecy becoming a book of *Tanakh* in its definitive form was one that required the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael*. The *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*, upon their return, therefore undertook the task of bringing these works — Yechezkel, Daniel, Megillas Esther — into their final canonical form.

This raises a pointed question about Megillas Esther, whose events took place in *galus*. The answer lies in the Gemara in Maseches Megilla, which preserves a tradition that Esther herself sent a message to the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* saying *kitvuni le-dorot* — "write me down for all generations" — meaning: include me in the canon. The process reflected here involves at least three distinct stages: original authorship or prophetic utterance; the writing down and organization of the material; and the formal act of canonization — the decision that this text belongs to *sifrei Tanakh* and is *metamei es ha-yadayim*, the rabbinic marker of canonical status.

The question of canonization was not fully resolved even by the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*. Debates about whether specific books — Shir HaShirim, Yechezkel, and others — are *metamei es ha-yadayim* continued into the Tannaic period. The discussions recorded among the *Tannaim* about which books convey *tum'as yadayim* are effectively discussions about the boundaries of the canon, addressing books whose status may have remained contested even after the Great Assembly's work. By the time of the *Tannaim*, most of the canon was beyond question; only the peripheral cases remained for deliberation.

The *baraita* concludes: *Ezra katav sifro ve-ha-yachas shel Divrei HaYamim ad lo* — Ezra wrote his book and the genealogical record of Divrei HaYamim up to his own time. The Gemara cites Rav in support: Ezra did not ascend from Bavel until he had fully traced his own genealogy, which reflects the broader enterprise of reestablishing proper lineages for the priestly and levitical families upon the return from exile. The Gemara then asks who completed Sefer Ezra after Ezra himself, and answers: *Nechemiah ben Chakalyah askeih* — Nechemiah finished it.

## Authorship Discussion…

### Moshe's Death Passage

Having enumerated the authors of the biblical books, the Gemara now returns to unpack several of the more challenging attributions. It begins with the case of Yehoshua and the last eight *pesukim* of the Torah.

The *baraita* had stated that Yehoshua wrote the last eight *pesukim* of the Torah — the passage beginning *va-yamas sham Moshe eved Hashem*, "And Moshe died there, the servant of God." A *tanna* reasons: is it conceivable that Moshe himself wrote *va-yamas sham Moshe* — "Moshe died there"? *Efshar Moshe katav va-yamas sham Moshe*? Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda concludes, *ad kan katav Moshe, mi-kan ve-eilach katav Yehoshua* — up to this point Moshe wrote, from here onward Yehoshua wrote.

Rav Kahana objects in the name of Rabbi Shimon: is it possible that the *Sefer Torah* was missing even a single letter? The verse states *Lakoach es sefer ha-Torah ha-zeh* — Moshe took the Torah and gave it to the *Kohanim Leviyim*, which implies the book was complete and whole in Moshe's lifetime. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon maintains that Moshe himself wrote all eight *pesukim*, including the account of his own death.

How is this possible? The second *tanna* supplies the model: *Hakadosh Baruch Hu omer u-Moshe kosev be-dema* — God dictated and Moshe wrote while crying. The entire Torah, including the passage describing Moshe's death, was written by Moshe himself through direct divine dictation; God spoke each word and Moshe transcribed it, weeping as he wrote those final *pesukim*. The analogy cited is the verse describing Baruch ben Neriyah, Yirmiyahu's scribe: *mipiv yikra eilai es kol ha-devarim ha-eileh va-ani kosev al ha-sefer be-dyo* — "From his mouth he would dictate to me all these words and I would write them in the book with ink." This establishes the model of prophetic dictation, in which the prophet is the amanuensis of divine speech.

### Shmuel's Authorship

The Gemara addresses a parallel problem with Sefer Shmuel. If Shmuel authored his book, how could he have written the account of his own death, which appears already in the middle of the first book — well before the narrative ends? The answer is that *Gad ha-Chozeh ve-Natan ha-Navi askeih* — Gad the Seer and Natan the Prophet completed the book after Shmuel's death. Similarly with Sefer Yehoshua: the text records not only the death of Yehoshua but also the death of Elazar ben Aharon, who presumably wrote the earlier account. *De'askeih Elazar* — Elazar completed the account of Yehoshua's death; and then *de'askeih Pinchas* — Pinchas completed the account of Elazar's own death.

### David & Ten Elders

*David katav sefer Tehillim al yedei asarah zekenim* — David composed Sefer Tehillim through ten elders. These ten are: Adam HaRishon, Malki-Tzedek, Avraham, Moshe, Heiman, Yedutun, Asaf, and the three sons of Korach. The Gemara identifies these figures by connecting the headings of specific *pirkei Tehillim* with these names.

The attribution of *Eitan HaEzrachi* — the figure named in the heading of a *mizmor* — to Avraham is derived through a *gezeira shava*: the term *ezrachi* here is linked to the verse in Yeshayahu that asks *mi he'ir mi-mizrach* — "who aroused from the east" — referring, according to *Chazal*, to Avraham, the one who came from the east with righteousness at his feet. The *af al pi Eitanim* — even the mighty ones — refers to the *Avot*.

A more complex textual problem arises concerning Heiman. Rav had elsewhere said that Heiman in the verse *vayechkam mi-kol ha-adam me-Eitan ha-Ezrachi* — in Melachim — refers to Moshe, based on the parallel between *Heiman* and *ne'eman*, as in *be-chol beisi ne'eman hu* (God's description of Moshe as faithful in all His house). But here in Tehillim, Heiman appears as a separate author alongside Moshe. The Gemara resolves this by saying *trei Heiman havu* — there were two figures named Heiman: the Heiman of Melachim who is an epithet for Moshe, and the Heiman of Tehillim who is a distinct individual.

## Last 8 Pesukim

### Dictation Model

The Gemara now focuses at length on the question of the last eight *pesukim* of the Torah. As noted, Rabbi Yehuda holds that Moshe wrote everything *until* the account of his own death, and Yehoshua wrote the final eight *pesukim*. Rabbi Shimon disputes this on the grounds that the *Sefer Torah* could not have been incomplete in Moshe's time — the verse explicitly records Moshe handing over the complete *Sefer Torah* to the *Kohanim Leviyim*.

Rabbi Shimon's position requires an account of how Moshe could have written *va-yamas sham Moshe*. The Gemara's answer is the model of *be-dema* — dictation accompanied by tears. God continued to speak and Moshe continued to write, even as he wept, through those final eight *pesukim*. This is the model of pure, word-for-word dictation: *Hakadosh Baruch Hu omer u-Moshe omer ve-chosev* — for the main body of the Torah, God speaks and Moshe repeats and writes; *Hakadosh Baruch Hu omer u-Moshe kosev be-dema* — for the final eight *pesukim*, God speaks and Moshe writes with tears.

### Moshe's Tears

This model carries enormous theological weight. The second *tanna*'s formulation — that God dictated while Moshe wrote in tears — establishes not only who authored those final *pesukim* but also the precise nature of the relationship between divine speech and prophetic writing throughout the Torah. The analogy to Baruch writing down Yirmiyahu's words *be-dyo* — in ink — makes the point with precision: the *navi* or scribe is the recording instrument; the divine word is the source. Moshe's tears do not alter or mediate the content; they are the human response of a servant faithfully transcribing the words of his own death.

### Torah Authenticity

This passage is the primary locus in *Chazal* for the model of *Torah min ha-shamayim* as verbatim, word-for-word dictation. Other *gemoros* discuss the concept of *Torah mi-Sinai* and state emphatically that anyone who holds that even a single *pasuk* is *Moshe mi-pi atzmo* — from Moshe's own mind — has no share in the *olam ha-ba*. But there is a significant gap between "from Moshe's own mind" and "word-for-word dictation." Between those poles lies a wide range of models: prophecy, divine inspiration, *ruach ha-kodesh*, each of which could theoretically allow the Torah to be authentically divine without being a verbatim transcript. The model of direct dictation — God speaks, Moshe writes — is introduced specifically by the second *tanna* in this Gemara. It is noteworthy that even here, this model is not asserted by the first *tanna*; it is the second *tanna*'s response to the particular problem of the last eight *pesukim*. Nevertheless, this has become the standard model for understanding *Torah min ha-shamayim*, and it is from this Gemara that the model derives its authoritative grounding.

### Gra's Interpretation

The Vilna Gaon offers a striking mystical resolution that attempts to honor both positions simultaneously. The Gemara's formulation uses the word *be-dema* — in tears — while the parallel verse about Baruch uses *be-dyo* — in ink. The Gaon observes that *dema* can also carry the meaning of *dimua*, meaning something mixed or blurred. He suggests that Moshe wrote the letters of the final *pesukim* in a manner that was somehow not yet crystallized — perhaps invisible, perhaps undifferentiated — and that Yehoshua, after Moshe's death, then gave those letters their final, legible form. In this reading, Moshe is genuinely the author of all eight *pesukim* — the divine words passed through him — but the physical crystallization of those words into readable text was completed by Yehoshua. This synthesis allows Moshe to have truly written his own death while also allowing Yehoshua a role in finalizing those *pesukim*, without compromising the integrity of the *Sefer Torah* that Moshe delivered.

### Single Reader Rule

The Gemara cites a related ruling from Rav Gidel in the name of Rav: *shemonah pesukim she-ba-Torah yachid kore otan* — the last eight *pesukim* of the Torah are read by a single person. The Gemara asks whether this ruling reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda — who holds those *pesukim* have a different authorship — and therefore seems to conflict with Rabbi Shimon, who holds Moshe wrote everything. The Gemara responds: *afilu teima Rabbi Shimon* — even according to Rabbi Shimon, since those eight *pesukim* are distinctive in character — written with tears, describing a death not yet occurred — they retain a special status that warrants treating them as a discrete unit.

Rashi explains *yachid kore otan* as a prohibition against dividing that *aliyah*: the final eight *pesukim* must be read as a single complete unit by one person; they may not be split between multiple *aliyos*. Tosafos, however, raises a difficulty with an alternative interpretation — that *yachid* means the one receiving the *aliyah* reads without a *ba'al korei* — and rejects it on historical grounds: the practice of having a *ba'al korei* read while the *oleh* listens did not yet exist in the period of the Gemara. In the Talmudic period, the person who received the *aliyah* was himself the reader. Therefore *yachid kore otan* cannot be contrasting individual reading with the *ba'al korei* practice, and Rashi's explanation — that the *aliyah* may not be subdivided — remains the operative interpretation.

## Iyov Discussion

### Iyov in Moshe's Time

The Gemara now turns to a sustained discussion of the book of Iyov — its historical dating, its authorship, its status as historical narrative or *mashal*, and ultimately its place among the books of the Bible. The statement from the *mi katvan* section that Moshe wrote Iyov is now cited to support Rabbi Levi bar Lachma's view that Iyov lived in the time of Moshe.

The textual evidence for this dating is a *gezeira shava* from the word *eipo*. The verse in Iyov reads *mi yiten eipo ve-yichtevun milai* — "Would that my words were written down" — and concludes with the phrase *mi yiten va-sefer ve-yuchaku*, expressing a longing that his words be engraved. This connects to Moshe, who is called *mechokek* — the engraver, the lawgiver — as in the verse *ki sham chelkas mechokek safun*. The association of engraving with Moshe makes Moshe the natural candidate as the author who fulfilled Iyov's wish.

The Gemara immediately challenges this *gezeira shava*: the word *eipo* appears many times in *Tanakh*. It appears in the context of Yitzchak (*mi eipo hu ha-tzad tzayid*), Yaakov (*im kein eipo zot asu*), and Yosef (*eipo heim ro'im*). How can one particular occurrence of *eipo* be used to anchor a dating to Moshe's time? The Gemara answers: the key is not the word *eipo* alone but the additional phrase at the end of the verse — *mi yiten va-sefer ve-yuchaku* — which specifically invokes the idea of engraving, and it is Moshe who is uniquely designated *mechokek*. That combination clinches the identification.

### Iyov & Meraglim

Rava offers an alternative: *Iyov bi-mei meraglim hayah* — Iyov lived in the time of the spies. This is derived from a different connection: the verse in Iyov describing his homeland as *eretz Utz* is linked to the phrase of the *meraglim*, *ha-yesh bah eitz* — "is there a tree there?" The Gemara challenges this: *Utz* and *eitz* are not the same word. The Gemara responds that the connection is not merely orthographic but substantive: Moshe was saying to Bnei Yisrael, "Is there still alive that man whose years are long as a tree, and who protects his generation like a tree?" — that is, was Iyov still alive and possibly protecting the Canaanites, making the conquest more difficult?

Beyond the textual mechanics, there is a significant conceptual difference between these two datings. If Iyov was in Moshe's time and Moshe authored the book, the text functions as a *sefer chochmah* — a wisdom literature addressing the perennial question of *tzaddik ve-ra lo, rasha ve-tov lo* — through the pen of the greatest *navi*, the one who himself pleaded *hodeini na et derachecha*. If Iyov is associated with the *meraglim*, the historical frame shifts: Iyov becomes associated not with Moshe's authorship but with a particular moment of communal crisis and failure of faith, a moment when divine justice seemed profoundly unclear. The book is thus oriented differently in each case — less toward universal wisdom literature and more toward a specific historical experience of fractured *emunah*.

### Iyov as Mashal

A more radical view is raised anonymously in the Gemara: *Iyov lo hayah ve-lo nivra ela mashal hayah* — Iyov never existed and was never created; he was only a *mashal*. The purpose of the book, on this view, is to dramatize the questions of divine justice and suffering, but it makes no historical claim about an actual individual named Iyov.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani firmly rejects this view. He points to the opening verse: *ish hayah be-eretz Utz Iyov shemo* — "There was a man in the land of Utz, Iyov was his name." How can one claim he never existed when the text gives him a name and a geographic location? The anonymous opinion responds: consider the *mashal* of the poor man and his single lamb told to David by Natan the Prophet — it too employs concrete imagery, a name, and a setting, yet it is obviously a *mashal*. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani replies: that *mashal* does not name the individual or give the name of his city. Iyov is given *both* his personal name *and* the name of his land, which places him as a real historical figure. The *mashal* view is thus rejected by Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani, though it is worth noting that the view itself is presented anonymously — the Gemara records it without attributing it to any named *amora*.

### Iyov's Return

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Eliezer both hold that *Iyov me-olei golah hayah* — Iyov was among those who returned from Babylonian exile to rebuild the *Bayit Sheni*. After his return, his *beis midrash* was located in Tiberias. This dating situates the book of Iyov very differently from the earlier views: rather than a figure of the remote past, Iyov becomes a contemporary of the Second Temple period, someone who lived through the *churban ha-Bayit* and the *galus Bavel* and emerged on the other side. His questioning of divine justice takes on immediate historical resonance — just as Eicha is the voice of lamentation from within the destruction, Iyov could be read as the voice of an individual survivor grappling with the same questions of theodicy that the *churban* raised for the entire nation.

### 210 Years of Iyov

Rav Ashi raises a calculation: *yemei shnosaiv shel Iyov mi-sha'ah she-nichnesu Yisrael le-Mitzrayim ve-ad she-yatzu* — the years of Iyov's life spanned from when Yisrael entered Egypt until they departed. This yields 210 years — the traditional duration of *galus Mitzrayim* — a number that can be derived from the *pesukim* in Iyov itself. The text records that after Iyov's afflictions, God doubled what he had; if Iyov initially had 70 years, doubling produces 140 more, for a total of 210. This figure exactly matches the duration of the Egyptian exile according to *Chazal*.

The implication, taken at face value, is that Iyov actually lived *during* the period of *galus Mitzrayim*. The symbolic resonance is powerful: Iyov's life of suffering, which contains no visible redemption for 210 years, runs precisely parallel to Bnei Yisrael's slavery in Egypt. Both experiences raise with full force the question of where God is and what the logic of divine governance is. And in both cases — though in very different ways — the story ultimately ends with some form of divine response.

This dating connects interestingly with the *midrashic* tradition, not elaborated in this Gemara, that Iyov served as one of Pharaoh's advisors. If Iyov was a figure of the Egyptian period, he was not himself enslaved but was in proximity to the oppressors, which creates its own theological complexity.

### Duration vs. Period

The Gemara recognizes that Rav Ashi's calculation creates a tension with Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Eliezer's view that Iyov returned from *galus Bavel*. To resolve this, the Gemara adjusts the interpretation: *eima ki-mi-sha'ah she-nichnesu Yisrael le-Mitzrayim ve-ad she-yatzu* — perhaps the verse means only that the *duration* of Iyov's life was 210 years, matching the length of the Egyptian exile, without necessarily implying that he *lived during* that period. Iyov may have lived 210 years at some other historical moment; the number is what is significant, not the timing.

This resolution allows all views to coexist: Iyov lived 210 years. Those who place him in the time of Moshe, or the *meraglim*, or the *galus Bavel*, are offering different historical locations for those 210 years. Across all these datings, the various rabbinic opinions are unified in their perception that the book of Iyov is always associated with periods of profound national or individual crisis — times when God seems most hidden and the demands on *emunah* are most acute.

### Iyov & Nations

A *baraita* is then cited: *shiva nevi'im nisnab'u le-umos ha-olam* — seven prophets prophesied to the nations of the world: Bilam and his father, Iyov, Elifaz ha-Temani, Bildad ha-Shuchi, Tzofar ha-Na'amati, and Elihu ben Barachel ha-Buzi — that is, Iyov and his four friends from the book itself. This raises an immediate question: if Iyov is among the prophets to the nations, does that not imply he was non-Jewish? Yet the view of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Eliezer placed him among the Jewish returnees from Babylonian exile.

The Gemara challenges the premise: Elihu ben Barachel is identified in the *baraita* as a prophet to the nations, yet Elihu is clearly Jewish. The text itself says he was *mi-mishpachas Ram* — from the family of Ram — which *Chazal* understand as a reference to the lineage of Avraham. So being listed among the prophets to the nations does not in itself determine whether the prophet is Jewish or not; it indicates the direction of the prophecy, not the ethnic identity of the prophet.

### Universal Prophecy

The Gemara then asks a deeper question: all prophets of Israel presumably have relevance for the nations as well — why single out these figures specifically as prophets to the nations? The answer: *hasam ikar nevuasaihu le-Yisrael* — in the case of the prophets of Israel, their prophecy is primarily directed to Israel; the nations can derive insight from it as well, but that is secondary. *Hacha ikar nevuasaihu le-umos ha-olam* — in the case of Iyov and his companions, the primary address of their prophecy is the nations of the world.

This is a remarkable conclusion with which to close the discussion of Iyov. The book of Iyov is unique among the books of *Tanakh* not only in its themes — the suffering of the righteous, the opacity of divine justice, the testing of *emunah* — but in its fundamental orientation. Where the prophets of Israel speak primarily to Israel and only secondarily to humanity, the book of Iyov speaks primarily to all of humanity. It is, in the deepest sense, a universal book. The questions it raises — where is God in suffering? how does one maintain faith in the face of inexplicable evil? — are questions that belong to no single people. The Gemara's conclusion that *ikar nevuasaihu le-umos ha-olam* may thus be understood not as a relegation of Iyov to the periphery of *Tanakh* but as a recognition of its singular character: it is the book within *Tanakh* whose primary address is the human condition as such.
