# Yevamot 32b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achos Isha & Eshes Ach

### Two Prohibitions

The *gemara* on 32b picks up towards the bottom of the page, approximately six lines after the *gemara* begins, with *v'hashtah tanu rabbanan* — and now the Sages taught in a *baraita*. The case under discussion involves two brothers who have married two sisters, and the *gemara* has already established that even after one brother dies, his widow remains forbidden as *achos isha* — the surviving brother's wife's sister — to the surviving brother. Nothing here is dramatically new from what the *mishnah* has already introduced. But now the *gemara* presents a *baraita* that will open into a major conceptual discussion, taking us into very different territory.

The *baraita* describes a woman who is simultaneously forbidden in two distinct ways: she is both *eshes ach*, a brother's wife, and *achos isha*, a wife's sister. The scenario is not complicated to construct. One brother married a woman who happens to be the other brother's wife's sister. The brother dies — assume, for now, that there are children, so *yibbum* is not in play. This woman stands in two prohibited relationships to the surviving brother at once. If the surviving brother has relations with her, what is he liable for?

### Rabbi Yossi: Two

*Ba aleha chayav aleha mishum eshes ach umishum achos isha divrei Rabbi Yossi* — *Rabbi Yossi* says that he is liable under two headings: she is his brother's wife and she is his wife's sister. This seems entirely straightforward. She is in fact both, and the prohibition of each applies independently.

### Rabbi Shimon: One

*Rabbi Shimon omer eino chayav ela mishum eshes ach bilvad* — Rabbi Shimon says no: he is liable only for *eshes ach*, brother's wife, and not for *achos isha*, wife's sister. But this immediately generates a contradiction, because a second version of the *baraita* records Rabbi Shimon as saying precisely the reverse — *eino chayav ela mishum achos isha bilvad* — only wife's sister, not brother's wife. How can Rabbi Shimon say it is only one, and how can one version say it is *eshes ach* while the other says it is *achos isha*, if she is undeniably both?

### Lo Kashya Reconciliation

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction with *lo kashya*: there is no difficulty. The two versions of the *baraita* reflect two different factual sequences. *Kan shenasa chai v'achar kach nasa mes* — in one case, the surviving brother married first, and then the brother who would eventually die married her afterward. *Kan shenasa mes v'achar kach nasa chai* — in the other case, it was the reverse: the one who died married her first, and then the surviving brother married his own wife later.

The resolution flows from Rabbi Shimon's principle of *ein issur chal al issur* — one prohibited status does not take effect on top of a pre-existing one. The first prohibited relationship that was established is the operative one; the second cannot displace or supplement it.

### Marriage Sequence Example

To make this concrete: suppose Reuven is married to Rachel and Shimon is married to Leah, and Reuven and Shimon are brothers. Reuven dies. Rachel is now both *eshes ach* and *achos isha* to Shimon. Which status controls?

If Reuven married Rachel before Shimon married Leah — *nasa mes v'achar kach nasa chai* — then when Reuven married Rachel, she first became *eshes ach*, a brother's wife, to Shimon. Only later, when Shimon married Leah, did she become *achos isha*. Rabbi Shimon says the first status, *eshes ach*, is the operative one.

If the order was reversed — Shimon first married Leah, making Rachel his *achos isha*, and only afterward did Reuven marry Rachel — then when Reuven's marriage came about, she was already his *achos isha*, and the status of *eshes ach* was the second one to arrive. Rabbi Shimon again says go by the first status: she is *achos isha*. That explains why one version of the *baraita* records *eshes ach* as the operative prohibition and the other records *achos isha* — the difference is entirely a matter of which relationship was established first.

## Ein Issur Chal Issur

### Concept Introduction

This brings us to the central concept that will drive the *gemara* for the balance of today's material: *ein issur chal al issur*, one prohibited status does not take effect on top of another pre-existing one. It is the foundational principle behind Rabbi Shimon's ruling.

The obvious question is: what practical difference does it make? Both *eshes ach* and *achos isha* carry the penalty of *kares*. The person is going to the same fate regardless. But the difference is not trivial. If the act was performed *beshogeg* — without full awareness of the prohibition — the question of whether there is one prohibition or two determines whether one brings one *korban* or two. That is a concrete and significant *nafka minah*, as one of the students correctly observed, and it sets the table for why the disagreement matters at all.

The *gemara* also asks whether *ein issur chal al issur* operates in other familiar contexts — such as a person who eats *basar b'chalav* on Yom Kippur, stacking multiple prohibitions on a single act. The answer, as the *gemara* will clarify, depends on the precise nature of the second prohibition and its relationship to the first. But before reaching those distinctions, the *gemara* introduces another dimension to the problem.

### Rav Ashi: Hovering

*Amar Rav Ashi: issur achos isha miktala tali vekay.* Rav Ashi explains that the prohibition of *achos isha* does not simply disappear or become legally nonexistent merely because *eshes ach* was there first. Rather, it hovers. It is a real and applicable status — it simply cannot take effect because there is a pre-existing status occupying that space.

### Status Replacement Mechanism

The practical consequence of this hovering is striking. *Ee paka issur eshes ach* — if the prohibition of *eshes ach* were somehow removed, *asi issur achos isha vechayeil* — the hovering prohibition of *achos isha* would immediately descend and take effect. The moment the first status is vacated, the second rushes in.

### Yibbum Paradox

This creates a paradox for *yibbum*. Assume the order was: Reuven married Rachel first, making her *eshes ach* to Shimon; then Shimon married Leah, making Rachel *achos isha* as well, but that second status merely hovers. Now Reuven dies childless. Ordinarily, *eshes ach* would dissolve through the fulfillment of *yibbum*. But Rav Ashi explains: the very act of performing *yibbum* would vacate the *eshes ach* status, which would immediately invite the *achos isha* prohibition to land. Rachel would become Shimon's *achos isha* the moment *yibbum* is attempted.

*Vehilchach lo paka* — therefore the *eshes ach* status does not dissolve. Because *yibbum* would only trigger the hovering prohibition, it cannot be performed. Rachel remains forbidden under the operative prohibition of *eshes ach* even when Reuven dies childless, and the other wives in the *yibbum* structure are consequently treated as *tzaras erva* as well. The mechanism is self-sealing: *yibbum* is impossible precisely because it would activate the very *issur* it would otherwise seem to avoid.

## Rabbi Yossi Position

### Contradiction Question

The *gemara* now turns to a deep tension in Rabbi Yossi's position. Rabbi Yossi said in the *baraita* that the person who has relations with a woman who is both *eshes ach* and *achos isha* is liable for both. This implies that Rabbi Yossi holds *issur chal al issur* — that a second prohibition can indeed take effect on top of a first. But can that really be his view consistently?

### Mother-in-law Case

*Vehatanya* — and we have a *baraita* teaching that if someone committed a transgression that carries two different death penalties — *avar aveira sheyeish bah shtei misos* — the *halacha* is *nidon bachamura*, he is judged by the more severe punishment. A mother-in-law, *chamoso*, is punishable by *sreifa*, which is a weightier form of execution. A married woman, *eishet ish*, is punishable by *chenek*, which is considered the less severe form. Now consider a woman who is simultaneously a man's mother-in-law and a married woman — the most natural case being that his mother-in-law is married to his father-in-law.

The *tanna kamma* would say he is judged by *sreifa*, the heavier penalty, since that is the weightier of the two death sentences. But Rabbi Yossi says no: *nidon bezika harishona habaa alav* — he is judged according to the first relationship that defined their connection.

### First Relationship Rule

The *baraita* specifies how this plays out. If she was first his mother-in-law — she was widowed or divorced and then remarried — so that the *chamoso* status preceded the *eishet ish* status, then he is judged by *sreifa*. If, however, she was first a married woman to another man and then he married her daughter — so *eishet ish* came first and *chamoso* came later — then he is judged only by *chenek*, the penalty for *eishet ish*. The *chamoso* prohibition, though weightier, arrived second and does not displace the first.

The only coherent way to explain why Rabbi Yossi would ever impose the *lighter* penalty — *chenek* rather than *sreifa* — is to say that he holds the second prohibition simply does not attach when a first one is already in place. He is not punished by *sreifa* because, in his view, the *chamoso* status never legally took effect. That is precisely *ein issur chal al issur*.

### Sequence Determines Status

So we have a contradiction. In the case of *achos isha* and *eshes ach*, Rabbi Yossi seems to hold *issur chal al issur* — you can get both statuses. In the case of mother-in-law and married woman, he clearly holds *ein issur chal al issur* — the first status controls and the second does not take effect. Which is it?

### Rabbi Abbahu Resolution

*Amar Rabbi Abbahu: modeh Rabbi Yose be'issur mosif* — Rabbi Abbahu resolves the contradiction. Rabbi Yossi concedes, as a category, to *issur mosif*. This introduces the crucial distinction that will occupy the *gemara* from this point forward, and it opens the door to a broader taxonomy of how second prohibitions relate to first ones.

## Mosif vs. Kolel

### Issur Mosif Definition

There are, as the *gemara* now elaborates, multiple ways in which a second *issur* can relate to a first. The *gemara* identifies two principal categories: *issur mosif* and *issur kolel*. A third — *issur be'vas achas*, simultaneous prohibitions — will be addressed later.

*Issur mosif* means a prohibition that broadens the scope of *people* to whom the forbidden object or person is prohibited. The focus is on the *cheftza* — the object or person who is forbidden — and the question is whether that object or person has become forbidden to a wider circle of people under the second *issur* than it was under the first.

### Mosif Example

Return to the case of *achos isha* and *eshes ach*. Consider the sequence: Shimon first marries Leah, making Rachel forbidden to Shimon as *achos isha* — wife's sister. Subsequently, Reuven marries Rachel. At the moment Reuven marries Rachel, Rachel becomes *eshes ach* — brother's wife — not only to Shimon but to all of Reuven's brothers. Her status has expanded. Before Reuven's marriage, Rachel was forbidden to Shimon alone under the *achos isha* prohibition. After Reuven's marriage, she is forbidden to all of Reuven's brothers — a broader scope of people.

This is *issur mosif*: the second *issur*, *eshes ach*, is broader in scope than the first, *achos isha*, because it applies to a wider population. The logic is that since this new status, *eshes ach*, has genuine relevance to the other brothers — *migo d'itosaf issur legabei achim*, since an additional prohibition accrues with respect to the brothers — *itosaf issur legabei deidei* — it also takes effect with respect to Shimon, even though she was already forbidden to him under the first status. The new status has enough traction to be real because it matters to other people; and because it is real, it attaches even for Shimon.

This is why Rabbi Yossi can say, in one sequence, that the person is liable for both *eshes ach* and *achos isha*: when *achos isha* comes first and *eshes ach* comes second, the second *issur* is an *issur mosif* and can take effect despite the prior prohibition. Rabbi Yossi concedes to *issur mosif* — he agrees that a second *issur* of broader scope can impose itself on a first.

But a question immediately arises: what about the reverse sequence? If she first became *eshes ach* — Reuven married her first, making her the brother's wife, forbidden to all brothers — and then Shimon married Leah, now making Rachel his *achos isha* as well, why would Rabbi Yossi say both in that case? *Mai issur mosif ika?* — what broadening of scope is there here? She was already forbidden to all the brothers as *eshes ach*. *Achos isha* is actually a narrower *issur* — it applies only to Shimon, not to the other brothers. It adds no additional people. So there is no *issur mosif* in this sequence, and it seems Rabbi Yossi should agree that only the first status, *eshes ach*, applies.

Perhaps one might argue: *migu d'issar becholhu achvasa* — since when Shimon married Leah, all of her sisters became forbidden to Shimon, maybe Rachel now finds herself within a larger class of forbidden women, and that is a kind of broadening. But the *gemara* identifies this as something categorically different.

### Issur Kolel Definition

This brings us to *issur kolel*. While *issur mosif* focuses on the *cheftza* — the forbidden person's scope has broadened to include more people — *issur kolel* focuses on the *gavra*, the person who is prohibited. In *issur kolel*, the forbidden object has not become forbidden to more people. Rather, the person who is subject to the prohibition now finds himself prohibited in a broader range of activities or in a broader class of forbidden relationships.

The clearest way to distinguish them: in *issur mosif*, Rachel herself becomes forbidden to more people. In *issur kolel*, Shimon finds himself forbidden to more women — Rachel is now part of a larger category of women that Shimon cannot marry. She has not become prohibited to a wider circle; rather, she has been swept into a wider *klal* of things forbidden to him.

### Kolel Example

The example is exactly the scenario of the reverse sequence: Reuven married Rachel first, making her *eshes ach* to all the brothers. Then Shimon married Leah, making all of Leah's sisters — including Rachel — forbidden to Shimon as *achos isha*. Rachel's status vis-à-vis the brothers has not changed; she was forbidden to them all before and remains so. What has changed is that Shimon, by marrying Leah, now finds himself forbidden to a broader range of women: not only Rachel, but all of Leah's sisters. Rachel finds herself included in that broader class.

The question *issur kolel* asks is: since Rachel's sisters are now forbidden to Shimon, and Rachel is one of those sisters, does the fact that she has been drawn into this larger *klal* of forbidden women allow the *achos isha* prohibition to attach — even though she was already forbidden to Shimon under *eshes ach*? The argument would be: since the new *issur* covers a broader category of activities or relationships for the *gavra*, perhaps it can take hold even where a prior *issur* already exists, because it is operating at a different level.

The distinction between *mosif* and *kolel* can be summarized this way: *issur mosif* changes the status of the forbidden *cheftza* by expanding the scope of people to whom it is prohibited. *Issur kolel* changes the status of the *gavra* by expanding the range of things he is prohibited from. In the first case, the forbidden thing is now forbidden more broadly. In the second case, the forbidden person now has a broader set of prohibitions, of which this one is part.

### Kolel Application Question

The *gemara* then asks whether Rabbi Yossi, who concedes to *issur mosif*, would also hold of *issur kolel* — whether a second *issur* that broadens the *gavra*'s prohibited activities can also take effect on a pre-existing first *issur*. This question is what sets up Rava's intervention.

## Rava: As If Two

### Rava's Formulation

*Ella amar Rava* — Rava steps in with a formulation that is at once elegant and puzzling: *mah lani alav k'ilu asah shtayim v'eino chayav ela achas* — it is as if he transgressed two, but he is only liable for one.

Rava is acknowledging the force of the preceding analysis. Rabbi Yossi holds *issur mosif*, not *issur kolel*. In the sequence where *eshes ach* came first and *achos isha* came second, there is no *issur mosif* — the second *issur* does not broaden the scope of who is forbidden. And since Rabbi Yossi does not hold of *issur kolel* — that is clear from the mother-in-law case — technically, Rabbi Yossi in that reverse sequence ought to agree that only one prohibition applies. So why does he say you transgress both?

Rava's answer: in the reverse sequence, Rabbi Yossi does not mean that literally, technically, in terms of punishment and consequence, two prohibitions have taken effect. He means rather that *k'ilu* — it is *as if* — two prohibitions were transgressed. The same formulation is cited in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ki ata Ravin amar Reb Yochanan mah lani leh k'ilu asah shtayim v'eino chayav ela achas*.

### Mah Nafka Mina

The *gemara* immediately presses the obvious question: *mah nafka mina?* If he is only liable for one, what difference does the *k'ilu* make? What is the practical meaning of saying it is *as if* he transgressed two, if in terms of punishment and legal consequence there is only one? If we strip away punishment entirely — imagine the *Torah* had imposed no penalty — would we say this person violated one prohibition or two? *Ein issur chal al issur*, as Rabbi Shimon frames it, might not mean the second prohibition literally does not exist; it might mean only that its consequences are subsumed by the first. The act may still be a genuine transgression of both, even if the practical consequences are assessed through the first status alone.

### Burial Application

*Likavro bein reshaim gemurim* — the *nafka minah* is where the person is buried after *beis din* executes him. The Sanhedrin would bury those it executed in designated sections of the cemetery, calibrated to the severity of their crime. This determination is not about punishment per se — it is about personal status and identity, about what this person's actions say about *who he is*. If he is treated *b'gavra*, in terms of personal standing, as someone who transgressed two prohibitions, he is buried among the fully wicked. This is the tangible consequence of the *k'ilu* — not an additional penalty imposed on the act, but a statement about the person.

This is a strikingly thin *nafka minah*, and the precise nature of the dispute with Rabbi Shimon remains somewhat elusive. But what the *gemara* has now established is a full conceptual map: Rabbi Shimon holds categorically *ein issur chal al issur*; Rabbi Yossi concedes to *issur mosif* — when the second *issur* broadens the scope of people who are forbidden — but does not hold of *issur kolel*; and in cases that fall into neither category, Rava explains Rabbi Yossi's position as a *k'ilu* — a matter of identity and status, not of additional punishment.

## Three Parallel Cases

### Zar BeShabbat

The *gemara* now connects the principle of *issur chal al issur* to a famous set of three parallel debates, each involving a situation where two prohibitions seem to converge on a single act. The first case: *zar sheshimmesh b'shabbos* — a non-*kohen* who performs the *avodah* in the *Beis Hamikdash* on *Shabbos*. A non-*kohen* who performs the Temple service is liable with the death penalty. Someone who performs *melacha* on *Shabbos* is also liable. Here both seem to apply simultaneously. Is he liable twice?

### Rabbi Chiyya vs. Bar Kappara

*Rabbi Chiyya omer chayav shtayim* — Rabbi Chiyya says he is liable for two. *Bar Kappara omer eino chayav ela achas* — Bar Kappara says only one. Both then take dramatic oaths. *Kafatz Rabbi Chiyya v'nishba: ha'avodah, kach shamati meRebbi shtayim* — Rabbi Chiyya jumped up and swore, by the Temple service itself: this is what I heard from Rebbe, that he is liable twice. *Kafatz Bar Kappara v'nishba: ha'avodah, kach shamati meRebbi achas* — Bar Kappara jumped up and swore the same oath in the opposite direction: this is what I heard from Rebbe, only once. The oaths make it impossible to dismiss either report as simple error of transmission — both are insisting with the utmost solemnity on having heard from the same source.

### Chiyya's Logic

Rabbi Chiyya then offers his reasoning. *Shabbos l'chol nitra* — *Shabbos* was forbidden to everyone. *K'shehutra b'Mikdash* — when it was permitted in the *Beis Hamikdash*, *etzel kohanim hutra* — it was permitted specifically for *kohanim*. *L'kohanim hutra v'lo l'zarim* — the dispensation applies to *kohanim*, not to non-*kohanim*. Therefore this person, as a *zar*, never received any dispensation from the *Shabbos* prohibition. *Yesh kan mishum zarus v'yesh kan mishum Shabbos* — there is a violation of performing unauthorized *avodah* as a non-*kohen*, and there is also a violation of *Shabbos*. Both apply.

### Bar Kappara Logic

Bar Kappara's counter-argument is structurally similar but arrives at an opposite conclusion. *Shabbos l'chola nasra* — *Shabbos* was forbidden to all. *K'shehutra, b'Mikdash hutra* — when a dispensation was given, it was given for the *Mikdash* as such, not specifically for *kohanim*. The *Mikdash* context itself waives the *Shabbos* concern. Therefore, anyone performing any activity in the *Mikdash* on *Shabbos* — even a *zar* performing unauthorized *avodah* — is operating in a context where *Shabbos* has been waived. *Ein kan ela zarus* — the only violation is the prohibition of a non-*kohen* performing the *avodah*.

This position is, as one might note, genuinely surprising. It seems to imply that anyone who walks into the *Beis Hamikdash* on *Shabbos* and strikes a match incurs no *Shabbos* violation — which is bizarre. But Bar Kappara is apparently conceptualizing the *Mikdash* as a space where the *Shabbos* prohibition does not apply, full stop, rather than as a space where *kohanim* receive a specific dispensation to perform the *avodah*.

### Ba'al Mum BeTemeiut

The second parallel case: *ba'al mum sheshimmesh b'tuma* — a *kohen* with a physical blemish who performs the *avodah* while *tamei*. A *ba'al mum* is ordinarily forbidden to perform the *avodah*, though he may eat from the *korbanot*. A *tamei* person is also forbidden to perform the *avodah*. Here the relevant background is that *tumah* can be overridden for a *korban tzibbur* — a communal sacrifice. Were this a normal *kohen*, he would have a dispensation to perform the *avodah* despite being *tamei* when there is a *korban tzibbur*. But this *kohen* has a blemish, which bars him from performing the *avodah* regardless. When he nonetheless performs the *avodah* while *tamei* in the context of a *korban tzibbur*, is he liable for both prohibitions — *ba'al mum* and *tumah* — or only for *ba'al mum*?

Again Rabbi Chiyya says twice and Bar Kappara says once, and both take the same solemn oath attributing their position to Rebbe.

Rabbi Chiyya's reasoning: *tumah l'chola nasra* — *tumah* was forbidden to all. *K'shehutra b'Mikdash l'kohanim temimim hutra* — when a dispensation was given, it was given to unblemished *kohanim* who are actually eligible to perform the *avodah*. A *ba'al mum* is not among those for whom the dispensation was granted. Therefore the *tumah* prohibition still applies to him. *Yesh kan mishum ba'al mum v'yesh kan mishum tumah* — both prohibitions are in force.

Bar Kappara counters: *k'shehutra b'Mikdash, b'Mikdash hutra* — when the dispensation was given, it was given for anything performed in the *Mikdash* in the context of *korban tzibbur*. The waiver is contextual, not person-specific. In this context, *tumah* as such is not a violation. *Ein kan ela mishum ba'al mum* — the only operative prohibition is that of the blemished *kohen* performing the *avodah*. This argument, notably, carries more intuitive weight than the *Shabbos* case. The principle of *tumah hutra b'tzibbur* is itself a known categorical rule; one can plausibly argue that in the context of communal sacrifice, *tumah* concerns are simply set aside.

### Zar Melikah

The third case: *zar she'achal melikah* — a non-*kohen* who ate from a bird offering that was slaughtered by *melikah*. The *kohen* pierces the back of the bird's neck with his fingernail. This is the prescribed method for a bird *chatas*. If this were done outside the context of a *korban*, the bird would be a *neveilah* — an animal that did not die through a valid *shechitah*. In the context of the *korban*, it is kosher and may be eaten by *kohanim*. A non-*kohen* ate this bird. He has certainly violated the prohibition against a *zar* eating a *korban chatas*. Has he also violated the prohibition against eating *neveilah*?

Again Rabbi Chiyya says twice and Bar Kappara says once; again both swear by *ha'avodah* that Rebbe said their version.

Rabbi Chiyya's reasoning: *neveilah lechol ne'esra* — *neveilah* was forbidden to all. *K'shehutra b'Mikdash, etzel kohanim hutra* — when it was permitted in the *Mikdash*, by virtue of *melikah* making it a valid *korban*, that permission was granted specifically to *kohanim*. For a *zar*, it remains *neveilah*. *Yesh kan mishum zarus v'yesh kan mishum melikah* — two violations.

Bar Kappara responds: *k'shehutra b'Mikdash hutrah* — the Torah itself, in prescribing *melikah* as the method for bird sacrifices, effectively declared that a bird killed this way is not a *neveilah*. It is simply not in the category of *neveilah*. *Ein kan ela mishum zarus* — there is only the violation of a non-*kohen* eating from a *korban*. This argument, as noted, carries real force: one can reasonably read the Torah's prescription of *melikah* as a wholesale redefinition of that act, such that the *neveilah* category simply does not apply.

## Issur Chal Analysis

### Reframing Question

Having presented all three parallel debates, the *gemara* steps back and asks: *bemai kamifligi?* — what is the underlying disagreement? On the surface, each case looks like a local question about how to conceptualize a specific halakhic dispensation. But the *gemara* suggests that these three parallel disputes reflect a unified deeper question, and it proposes that the real debate is: *be'issur kolel ve'aliba d'Rabbi Yosei* — it concerns the principle of *issur kolel*, and specifically whether Rabbi Yossi would apply it.

We have already established that Rabbi Yossi holds *issur mosif* — he allows a second *issur* to take effect when it broadens the scope of people to whom the *cheftza* is forbidden. The question now is whether Rabbi Yossi would also hold *issur kolel* — whether a second *issur* that broadens the class of *activities* forbidden to the person can take effect on a pre-existing prohibition.

### Chiyya: Kolel Applies

*Rabbi Chiyya savar Rabbi Yosei be'issur kolel mechayev* — Rabbi Chiyya holds that Rabbi Yossi would say *issur kolel* does allow a second prohibition to take effect, generating liability twice. All three of the parallel cases are instances of *issur kolel*, and Rabbi Chiyya is arguing that Rabbi Yossi's logic extends to them.

### Bar Kappara: Kolel Doesn't Apply

*Bar Kappara savar lo mechayev ela chada* — Bar Kappara holds that even Rabbi Yossi would not apply *issur kolel* to create double liability. Only one prohibition counts.

### Zar BeShabbat Analysis

The *gemara* works through each case to demonstrate where *issur kolel* appears. For the *zar* who performs *avodah* on *Shabbos*: *zar me'ikara shari bemelacha v'assur ba'avodah* — initially, on any weekday, the *zar* is permitted to perform *melacha* but is forbidden to perform the *avodah* in the *Mikdash*. That is his one prohibition. Then *Shabbos* arrives. *Asya leShabbos: migu d'kamitra bemelacha, itsar nami ba'avodah* — since he is now prohibited from *melacha* in general, and the *avodah* falls within the category of *melacha*, the broader prohibition of *Shabbos melacha* now encompasses the *avodah* as well.

This is *issur kolel*: the *zar*'s prohibited activities have broadened. Before *Shabbos*, he could do most things but not the *avodah*. On *Shabbos*, he cannot do a wide class of activities — *melacha* generally — and the *avodah* is included within that broader class. The *avodah* has not become forbidden to more people (*mosif*); rather, the *zar* now has a broader set of prohibited activities (*kolel*), into which the *avodah* is swept.

### Ba'al Mum Analysis

In the case of the *ba'al mum*: initially, he could eat from *korbanot* but could not perform the *avodah*. Then he became *tamei*. *Migu d'itassar ba'achila, itassar nami ba'avodah* — since he is now prohibited from eating *korbanot* due to *tumah*, and his relationship to *korbanot* has broadly changed, that broader prohibition also extends to performing the *avodah*. The class of activities related to *korbanot* that are now forbidden to him has widened, and the *avodah* is within that class. Again, *issur kolel*: his prohibited activities have broadened.

---

## Issur Chal Analysis (II)

### Melikah Problem

But when the *gemara* tries to apply the same *issur kolel* framework to the third case — the *zar* who ate *melikah* — it runs into a problem. *Ela: melikah be'vas achas hi* — the *melikah* case is one where everything happens at once. The moment the *kohen* performs *melikah* on the bird, two things occur simultaneously: the bird becomes a valid *korban*, triggering the prohibition of a *zar* eating it; and it also has the status of *neveilah* for those not eligible to eat it. There is no sequence — no prior state in which one prohibition existed before the other. *Be'issur kolel lo mishkachas lah* — you simply cannot construct an *issur kolel* scenario in this case, because *issur kolel* requires a first *issur* already in place before a second, broader one arrives. Here both arrive at the same instant. The *issur kolel* analysis, therefore, cannot be what is at stake in the *melikah* case.

---

## Bat Achat Debate

### Simultaneous Prohibition

This leads the *gemara* to a fresh formulation. *Kamipalgi be'issur be'vas achas* — the real debate underlying all three cases is not *issur kolel* but rather *issur be'vas achas*: what happens when two prohibitions arise simultaneously, at the exact same moment? According to Rabbi Yossi, who is prepared under some conditions to hold that a second *issur* takes effect despite a first, would he say the same when the two *issurim* arrive together, with no sequencing at all? *Rabbi Chiyya savar Rabbi Yosei be'issur be'vas achat mechayev tarti* — Rabbi Chiyya holds that Rabbi Yossi would impose double liability even when both prohibitions arise at once. *Bar Kappara savar lo mechayev ela chada* — Bar Kappara holds that even Rabbi Yossi would impose only one.

### Scenarios Constructed

The *gemara* then addresses an obvious challenge: if this is about simultaneous prohibitions, where does the simultaneity appear in the *Shabbos* and *ba'al mum* cases? Those seem to involve a prior condition (*zar*; *ba'al mum*) already in place before *Shabbos* arrives or before *tumah* supervenes. The *gemara* constructs specific scenarios to generate genuine *be'vas achas* situations.

### Zar Bar Mitzvah

*Kegon d'aysi shtei sa'aros b'Shabbos* — imagine a young man who reaches adulthood — acquiring his two pubic hairs, the halachic sign of majority — at the very moment *Shabbos* begins. Before that moment, he was a minor and was not subject to these prohibitions in the same way. At the instant he becomes an adult, it is simultaneously *Shabbos*, and simultaneously he is a *zar*. Both the prohibition of a non-*kohen* performing the *avodah* and the prohibition of *Shabbos* *melacha* descend upon him at the exact same moment. *D'havya leh zarus v'shabbos bahadi hadadi* — *zarus* and *Shabbos* come to him together.

### Kohein Bar Mitzvah

For the *ba'al mum* case: *kegon d'afik shtei sa'aros b'mum* — imagine a young man who had a blemish and who was also *tamei*, and who acquired his two pubic hairs — reached adulthood — at this moment. Before majority, neither prohibition was operative on him in the full sense. At the moment of majority, both the *ba'al mum* prohibition and the *tumah* prohibition descended simultaneously. *D'havya leh ba'al mum v'tumah bahadi hadadi* — both came at the same time.

### Knife Injury Alternative

Alternatively — and this is presented as a somewhat more interesting construction — *shechatach etzba'o b'sakin tme'iah* — he cut his finger with a *tamei* knife. At the very moment he was cut, he simultaneously acquired a *mum* — the blemish disqualifying him from the *avodah* — and became *tamei* through contact with the *tamei* knife. Both statuses arrived at the same instant, without any possibility of sequencing one before the other.

## Disagreement Reconstruct…

### Both Heard Rebbe

With the theoretical framework now constructed, the *gemara* returns to the immediate puzzle with which it began: how can Rabbi Chiyya and Bar Kappara take opposite oaths about what Rebbe said? Rebbe said one thing. The *gemara* is unwilling to say Rebbe changed his mind, and it is unwilling to say either rabbi is lying — particularly given the oaths. So it seeks to reconstruct how both could have heard accurately and still arrived at opposite conclusions.

### Different Tannaim

*Bishlama l'Rabbi Chiyya* — it works easily according to Rabbi Chiyya's perspective. *Ki asnei l'didei aliba d'Rabbi Yosei* — when Rebbe taught Rabbi Chiyya, saying you get double liability, he was presenting the position according to Rabbi Yossi, who is the one prepared to hold *issur chal al issur* and who, per Rabbi Chiyya, extends it even to *be'vas achas* cases. *Ki asnei l'Bar Kappara aliba d'Rabbi Shimon* — and when Rebbe taught Bar Kappara, saying only once, he was presenting the view of Rabbi Shimon, who categorically holds *ein issur chal al issur*. From Rabbi Chiyya's vantage point, Bar Kappara's mistake is entirely understandable: he heard the teaching according to Rabbi Shimon and mistook it for Rebbe's definitive ruling.

But the symmetrical problem arises immediately. According to Bar Kappara's understanding — that even Rabbi Yossi would hold only once in a *be'vas achas* case — how can he explain what Rabbi Chiyya heard? *Ela l'Bar Kappara Rabbi Chiyya shikurei kameshaker?* — is Rabbi Chiyya simply lying? He swore. The *gemara* cannot accept that either sworn testimony is fabricated.

### Mutual Truthfulness

The *gemara* grapples with the fact that both rabbis are insisting with equal conviction on having heard from the same master. Any reconstruction must be capable of accounting for both directions — explaining how each could have heard accurately, and how each could charitably understand where the other went wrong. Simply asserting one framework or the other always leaves one of the two swearing truthfully and the other irreconcilably mistaken in a way that is hard to explain.

### Rabbi Shimon Focus

The *gemara* therefore shifts the frame of the entire debate. *Ela k'mipalgi b'issur bas achas aliba d'Rabbi Shimon* — rather than asking whether Rabbi Yossi would extend *issur* to *be'vas achas* cases, the real dispute may concern Rabbi Shimon's position on simultaneous prohibitions. Rabbi Shimon ordinarily and categorically holds *ein issur chal al issur*. The question is whether *be'vas achas* might be an exception even for Rabbi Shimon — since when both prohibitions arrive at the exact same instant, neither can be said to pre-exist the other. There is no first *issur* for the second to overlay. Perhaps even Rabbi Shimon would concede that both take effect simultaneously.

On this reading: Rabbi Chiyya's oath that Rebbe said twice reflects Rebbe teaching that even Rabbi Shimon holds double liability in a *be'vas achas* case. Bar Kappara's oath that Rebbe said once reflects Rebbe teaching that Rabbi Shimon holds his usual position — *ein issur chal al issur* — even when they arrive together. Both could have heard Rebbe accurately, each in a different teaching session or context, each presenting Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Chiyya is not saying Rabbi Yossi; both are debating what Rabbi Shimon holds about the *be'vas achas* scenario.

But the *gemara* then presses the same question in reverse: *bishlama l'Bar Kappara kamishhtava l'afuki l'Rabbi Shimon me'Chizkiya* — according to Bar Kappara, Rabbi Chiyya's oath makes sense as arguing that even Rabbi Shimon holds twice in a *be'vas achas* case, that being a non-trivial claim worth swearing about. *Ela l'Rabbi Chiyya lamma lei l'ishtabuei?* — but according to Rabbi Chiyya, why would Bar Kappara need to swear? Bar Kappara is simply saying Rabbi Shimon holds his normal position, one violation. That is not a controversial claim requiring an oath. *Kashya* — the *gemara* records this as a difficulty, unresolved at this point in the discussion. The full resolution of how both sworn testimonies can be vindicated simultaneously will be addressed as the *gemara* continues.
