# Bekhorot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sheep Redemption Value

### Rabbi Yehuda vs Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם לָא מַקֵּישׁ, אָמַר רָבָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְכׇל עֶרְכְּךָ יִהְיֶה בְּשֶׁקֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ״, כׇּל עֲרָכִין שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲרִיךְ לֹא יְהוּ פְּחוּתִין מִשֶּׁקֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Actually,** the verse **does not compare** them, and the reason a lamb worth a shekel is required is due to that which **Rava says: The verse states** with regard to redemption of consecrated property: **"And all your valuations shall be with the shekel of the Sanctuary"** (Leviticus 27:25), which indicates that **all valuations that you assess should not be less than** the value of **a shekel.**

The *daf* opens by picking up a thread introduced at the bottom of 10b. The *Gemara* had raised a question about the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon regarding whether the firstborn donkey (*peter chamor*) is forbidden in benefit (*asur b'hana'ah*) before redemption. Rabbi Shimon holds it is not forbidden; Rabbi Yehuda holds it is. A difficulty arose for Rabbi Yehuda's position: even if the donkey is technically forbidden in benefit, does it not have some monetary value, since the owner can redeem it with a sheep of lesser value and thereby profit the difference? If so, one could in theory use the donkey to betroth a woman — and there would always be some extractable value. The *Gemara* resolves this difficulty by noting that it reflects the position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, who requires that the redemption sheep be worth no less than a *shekel*. If the minimum is a *shekel*, then a donkey worth a *shekel* or less would yield no benefit from redemption, and the problem dissolves.

### Benefit Paradox

The resolution, then, hinges on whether a minimum value is truly required. This sets up the broader discussion of the section: what is the floor for the value of the sheep used to redeem a firstborn donkey?

### Minimum Shekel Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: הַהוּא בְּהֶשֵּׂג יָד כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** who disagree with him hold that **that** verse **is written with regard to affordability,** i.e., a case where one vows to give the valuation of an individual to the Temple but does not have sufficient funds to fulfill his vow. In such a case, he must pay at least a shekel. It does not apply to other matters.

The *Gemara* begins by quoting the teaching: *tifdeh tifdeh* — the word "you shall redeem" appears twice in two separate *pesukim*. One instance teaches that redemption may be performed immediately (*tifdeh miyad*); the second teaches that redemption may be done with something of virtually no value (*tifdeh kol shehu*). Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, however, demands that the redemption sheep be worth at least a *shekel*.

### Two Verses Teaching

The *Gemara* notes that the derivation of *tifdeh miyad* — that one need not wait before redeeming — is not as obvious as it seems. One might have thought: since the Torah juxtaposes the redemption of a human firstborn with the redemption of a firstborn donkey in the same *pasuk*, perhaps the laws of the donkey should parallel those of a human firstborn.

### Human Firstborn Analogy

The implication of that analogy would be significant: a human firstborn (*bechor adam*) is redeemed only after thirty days and only with five *shekalim*. One might therefore have thought that a firstborn donkey similarly requires waiting thirty days and a payment of five *shekalim* — whether in the form of money or a sheep worth that amount. The two *tifdeh* verses therefore come to teach: (1) you may redeem the donkey immediately, without waiting; and (2) the redemption is valid even with a sheep of minimal value (*kol shehu*). This second derivation stands in direct tension with Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Source Inquiry

The *Gemara* then turns to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of a minimum *shekel* and demands to know its source.

### Dilemma on Shekel

The *Gemara* poses a *mimah nafshach* — a dilemma from which there is no escape. If you compare the donkey's redemption to that of a human firstborn, the minimum should be five *shekalim*, not one. If you do not make that comparison, where do you derive the requirement of a *shekel* at all?

### Erech Verse Source

The *Gemara* responds: Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda never draws the comparison to human firstborns. Rather, *Rabba* cites a verse as his source: *v'chol erkecha yihyeh b'shekel hakodesh* — "all your valuations shall be in the shekel of the sanctuary" (end of *Sefer Vayikra*).

### Erech Principle

This verse appears in the context of *erchin* — fixed valuations of persons pledged to the *Beis Hamikdash*. The Torah provides a chart of fixed amounts based on gender and age, then concludes with this phrase. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda understands the verse to be establishing a general principle: *kol erchin she'ata maarich* — any time you perform an estimation of value for the purposes of a redemption-type ritual, *lo yifchatu mi'shekel*, it cannot be less than a *shekel*. The comparison is admittedly imperfect on two counts: in the *erech* context the money goes to the *Beis Hamikdash* and the object of valuation becomes sanctified, neither of which is true of the donkey redemption where the sheep goes to a *kohen* and carries no sanctity. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda sees a structural similarity — an act of valuation and ritual transfer — sufficient to import the *shekel* minimum.

### Rabbis' Counter

The Rabbis, however, reject this derivation. They read *v'chol erkecha yihyeh b'shekel hakodesh* as addressing a specific and narrow situation within the *parsha* of *erchin* itself: *l'hasig yad*, the poverty adjustment. The Torah states that if the person who pledged an *erech* cannot afford the fixed valuation, the amount is adjusted downward based on what he can afford (*al pi asher tasig yad hanoder*). The verse *v'chol erkecha* then sets a floor: even when reducing the amount for a poor person, the valuation cannot be set below a *shekel*. This *pasuk*, the Rabbis argue, is limited to that specific poverty-adjustment context and cannot be exported to other types of redemption.

### Halacha Kol Shehu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲפִילּוּ פַּטְרוֹזָא בַּר דַּנְקָא, אָמַר רָבָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא גָּדוֹל וְקָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis** that one may redeem a firstborn donkey with a lamb that is worth even less than a shekel. **And how much** must the lamb be worth? **Rav Yosef says: Even a lean lamb [*patruza*]** that is **worth** only **a *ma'a* [*danka*]** may be used. **Rava says: We learn** in the mishna (9a) **as well** that the owner may give either **a big or a small** lamb, which supports Rav Yosef's ruling.

The *Gemara* reports: *Rav Nachman* ruled that the *halacha* follows the *chachamim*, and there is no minimum value requirement. The sheep used for redemption may be worth even a *kol shehu* — virtually nothing.

### Practical Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא? אִי נָמֵי, פַּטְרוֹזָא לָא? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that even a lean lamb worth one *ma'a* may be used? The *baraita* states that the lamb may be worth any amount. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that if the lamb is of **such** little value one may **not** redeem with it, Rav Yosef states that one may redeem with it. **Alternatively,** it could be claimed that **a lean lamb** may **not** be used for redemption due to its physical state. Therefore, Rav Yosef **teaches us** that one may redeem a firstborn donkey even with such a lamb.

After establishing that no minimum applies, the *Gemara* characteristically asks: *v'chama* — so what, practically speaking, is the minimum? *Rav Yosef* answers: even a *patruza bar danka* — a scrawny, sickly sheep worth a *danka*, which is a sixth of a *zuz*. The clear implication is that even this is not a precise lower bound; the point is simply that virtually any sheep, regardless of condition or value, will suffice.

### Mishnah Supports

*Rava* then observes that this is already implicit in the *Mishnah* itself, which states *gadol v'katan* — the sheep may be large or small. The *Gemara* immediately asks: if that is obvious from the *Mishnah*, why did *Rav Yosef* need to say it? The *Gemara* answers that the *Mishnah*'s language alone would not have taught two things: first, that truly minimal value suffices, and second, that a *patruza* — a physically inferior, sickly sheep — is also acceptable. One might have supposed that even if value is irrelevant, there is some requirement of dignity or physical quality befitting the act of redemption. *Rav Yosef*'s ruling clarifies that neither quality nor value imposes any floor. The only thing that will not work, as we will see later in the *daf*, is a dead sheep.

## Practical Guidelines

### Rav Yehuda's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר, שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּמָּה בָּעֵינָא לְמִיתַּב לְכֹהֵן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda Nesia had a firstborn donkey. He sent it before Rabbi Tarfon,** and **said to him: How much do I need to give a priest** for its redemption, i.e., how much must the lamb used for its redemption be worth?

The *Gemara* now turns to practical *halacha* about how much one should actually give. *Rav Yehuda Nesia* — the grandson of *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* and thus already an *amora* — had a firstborn donkey. He sent it to *Rav Tarfon* with the question: *kama ba'ina l'meisav l'kohen* — how much do I need to give to the *kohen*? The question, given the context, concerns what value of sheep to use for the redemption. The fact that he apparently sent the actual donkey along with the query is intriguing. It likely reflects a concern that the answer might depend on the value of the donkey itself — perhaps one might think the sheep should bear some proportionality to the donkey being redeemed, or perhaps one should know the donkey's value to assess whether a very cheap sheep would be appropriate. It is also worth noting, as *Artscroll* points out, that *Rav Tarfon* was himself a *kohen* — which may explain why he gave a range rather than a single figure; he wished not to appear self-interested.

### Rav Tarfon's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: עַיִן יָפָה בְּסֶלַע, עַיִן רָעָה בְּשֶׁקֶל, בֵּינוֹנִית בְּרִגְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Tarfon **said to** him: **Didn't** the Sages **say:** One who has **a generous disposition** redeems his firstborn donkey **with** a lamb worth **a *sela*,** one who has **a miserly disposition** redeems his donkey **with** a lamb worth **a shekel,** and one who has **an intermediate** disposition redeems his donkey **with** a lamb worth **a *ragya*?**

*Rav Tarfon* responded: *harei amru* — they have said — *ayin yafa b'sela*, a generous person gives a *sela*; *ayin ra b'shekel*, a stingy person gives a *shekel*; *u'veinoni bargaya*, and an average person gives a *bargaya*. A *shekel* is half a *sela*, and a *bargaya* (vocalized by *Artscroll* as *birgiya*) is three-quarters of a *sela*, precisely midpoint between the *shekel* and the *sela*.

### Coin Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא בְּרִגְיָא. וְכַמָּה? תְּלָתָא זוּזֵי. רְגִיל הָכָא וְרָגִיל הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The *halakha*** is that one must redeem the firstborn donkey **with** a lamb that is worth at least **a *ragya*.** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is the value of a *ragya*? The Gemara answers: A *ragya* is worth **three dinars,** and is called a *ragya* because it is **close to [*ragil*] here and close to there,** i.e., its value is between that of a *sela*, which is worth fours dinars, and a shekel, which is worth two dinars.

To calibrate these in terms of *zuz*: a *shekel* is two *zuz*, a *sela* is four *zuz*, and a *bargaya* is three *zuz*. The *Gemara* appears to treat *bargaya* either as the actual name of a coin or as a descriptive phrase — *ragil hacha v'ragil hacha*, halfway here and halfway there.

### Rava's Ruling

*Rava* rules: *hilchasa birgiya* — the normative practice is to give three *zuz*, the middle value. This is the standard a person of average generosity should follow.

### Contradiction Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא! לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן — בְּבָא לִימָּלֵךְ, כָּאן — בְּעוֹשֶׂה מֵעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The contradiction between this ***halakha*,** that one must give a lamb worth three dinars, **and** the ***halakha*** stated by Rav Naḥman, that one may give a lamb of any monetary value, poses **a difficulty.** The Gemara responds: That is **not difficult,** as **here** the reference is **to one who comes to consult;** he is told to give a lamb worth three dinars. **There,** the reference is **to one who acts on his own,** i.e., one who redeems his donkey without consultation, in which case he has fulfilled his obligation even if the lamb is worth only a *ma'a*.

This immediately raises a question: we just ruled that *halacha* follows the *chachamim*, that even a *kol shehu* works. Now we are saying the standard is three *zuz*. These rulings appear contradictory.

The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. *Kan d'ba limalech, kan d'oseh me'atzmo* — one ruling addresses someone who comes to ask for guidance; the other addresses someone who acts on his own. If you already redeemed the donkey with a completely minimal sheep, it is perfectly valid — anything works *b'di'eved*. But if you are asking the *chachamim* for their advice, they will tell you what is appropriate: the range between a *shekel* and a *sela*, with three *zuz* as the recommended midpoint. This is directly parallel to the law of *terumah*: technically, a single grain of wheat can exempt an entire warehouse of produce, but if you ask the sages for guidance, they will tell you to give between a fortieth and a sixtieth, depending on your generosity. The same logic applies here.

## Value Redemption

### Value Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר וְאֵין לוֹ שֶׂה לִפְדּוֹתוֹ — פּוֹדֵהוּ בְּשׇׁוְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Reish Lakish says: One who has a firstborn donkey but does not have a lamb** with which **to redeem it may redeem** the donkey **with** a sum of money equal to the donkey's **value** and give it to a priest.

*Rav Yitzchak* cites *Reish Lakish*: if someone has a firstborn donkey and no sheep with which to redeem it, *podehu b'shavyo* — he redeems it with its monetary equivalent. This is a striking ruling, because the *Mishnayos* and *Beraisos* speak only of using a sheep. It is the *Amoraim* who articulate the principle that a sheep is in fact an option, designed to make redemption convenient — but one may always redeem using the donkey's actual value in any commodity. To be precise: the sheep may be of virtually any value regardless of the donkey's worth, but if one forgoes the sheep and redeems with another object, that object must equal the donkey's value.

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הָא אָמַר: הִקְפִּידָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה בְּשֶׂה! אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this statement? **If we say** that it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, doesn't he say** that **the Torah is particular** that the donkey be redeemed **with a lamb** (see 9a)? **Rather,** clearly it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *l'man* — according to whom is this ruling? If it is according to Rabbi Yehuda — *ha amar hikpida alav Torah b'seh* — did Rabbi Yehuda not insist that the Torah specifically required a sheep? That insistence was central to his argument that a formal ritual of redemption exists, which in turn is why the donkey is forbidden in benefit. If the Torah specifically required a sheep, how can we say that value without a sheep also works?

### Ruling Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא מַתְנִי הָכִי, רָבִינָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rav Aḥa teaches** it **in this way,** that the statement of Reish Lakish was made in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. **Ravina** found this **difficult,** as wherever there is a dispute between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

It therefore seemed that the ruling of *Reish Lakish* could only work according to Rabbi Shimon, who holds that the donkey is not forbidden in benefit in the first place, and therefore the entire framework is more flexible. *Rav Acha* taught the *sugya* accordingly. But *Ravina* raised an objection: if this ruling follows Rabbi Shimon, you are implicitly ruling like Rabbi Shimon. Yet we have two independent reasons to rule like Rabbi Yehuda over Rabbi Shimon. First, there is a general *klal*: when Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda. Second, our *Mishnah* itself is implicitly like Rabbi Yehuda: the *Mishnah* teaches that one redeems a doubtful firstborn donkey and keeps the sheep — an act that only makes sense if the donkey is forbidden in benefit and one wishes to remove that status. A *stam Mishnah* in Rabbi Yehuda's position is an additional strong argument for ruling like him. How, then, can *Reish Lakish* be issuing a ruling that only works according to Rabbi Shimon?

### Hekdesh Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לֹא יְהֵא חָמוּר מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְלֹא אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״בְּשֶׂה״ לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל עָלָיו. רַב נַחְמָן בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף פָּרֵיק לֵיהּ בְּשִׁילְקֵי בְּשׇׁוְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina concludes: **Rather,** the statement of Reish Lakish is correct **even** if **you say** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as** the *halakha* of a firstborn donkey **should not be more stringent than** that of **consecrated items.** One who redeems a consecrated animal from its consecrated status redeems it with any movable property that is worth its value. **And the Torah did not state** that one must redeem a firstborn donkey **with a lamb** in order **to be stringent with** the owner, **but** in order **to be lenient with** the owner such that he is not obligated to redeem it at its value, as a lamb is worth less than a donkey. The Gemara relates that **Rav Naḥman, son of Rav Yosef, redeemed** a firstborn donkey **with boiled vegetables** that were worth **its value.**

The *Gemara* resolves: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehuda* — the ruling of *Reish Lakish* works even according to Rabbi Yehuda. The reason: *lo yehei chamur min hahekdesh* — the donkey should not be treated more stringently than *hekdesh*, sanctified property.

### Hekdesh Comparison

When one sanctifies an object — say, one declares one's house or car as *hekdesh* — the object is forbidden in benefit and carries the full weight of *hekdesh* status, including the laws of *me'ilah*. Yet even *hekdesh* can be redeemed by paying its monetary value; the status transfers upon payment of equivalent worth. If the more stringent category of *hekdesh* can be redeemed by value, then certainly a firstborn donkey, whose status is less severe, should be redeemable by value as well.

### Torah Intent

Moreover, when the Torah specified a sheep, it was not being *l'hachmir* — it was not imposing a restrictive requirement. Rather, it was *l'hakel* — making things easier for the owner, by allowing him to redeem even with a cheap sheep rather than being forced to assess and pay the donkey's full value. The sheep is thus a lenient alternative, not an exclusive channel. Whether according to Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Shimon, both a sheep of minimal value and the donkey's equivalent value in any commodity are valid means of redemption.

One might ask why the *Gemara* was initially inclined to read *Reish Lakish*'s ruling as belonging to Rabbi Shimon rather than Rabbi Yehuda. In fact, the comparison to *hekdesh* might seem more natural under Rabbi Yehuda, since according to him the donkey genuinely has a status of being forbidden in benefit — making it more analogous to *hekdesh* — and therefore the idea of substituting value for value is conceptually coherent. Under Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, the donkey has no such status and the whole framework is merely a ritual. Yet the *Gemara*'s initial inclination arose from Rabbi Yehuda's language elsewhere, which seemed to demand specifically a sheep. The final resolution is that regardless of which view one holds, both agree: a sheep of virtually any value works, and equivalent monetary value also works in the absence of a sheep.

A student raises a valid question: when redeeming *hekdesh*, the owner must add a *chomesh* — a fifth above the object's value. Should the same apply to donkey redemption? The *Gemara* does not extend the comparison that far, and the implicit answer is that we limit the *hekdesh* analogy to those features actually derived from it — namely, that value suffices and that a third party may perform the redemption (as discussed next). The *chomesh* requirement is unique to *hekdesh* proper and applies only where the Torah explicitly mandates it.

## Ownership Rules

### Nachman's Case

The *Gemara* then introduces a conceptually bold question: *Rav Shizbi* in the name of *Rav Huna* rules that if someone redeems another person's firstborn donkey — without being asked, using his own sheep — the redemption is valid (*pidyono paduy*) and the sheep belongs to the *kohen*. This itself requires inference from the *hekdesh* analogy, since no *Mishnah* or *Beraita* explicitly contemplates third-party redemption of a donkey. Just as someone else can redeem my sanctified property by giving its value to the *Beis Hamikdash*, so too someone else can redeem my donkey by giving a sheep to the *kohen*.

### Third Party Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַפּוֹדֶה פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, פִּדְיוֹנוֹ פָּדוּי. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פִּדְיוֹנוֹ לַפּוֹדֶה, אוֹ דִלְמָא פִּדְיוֹנוֹ לַבְּעָלִים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi says** that **Rav Huna says:** In the case of **one who redeems the firstborn donkey of another, his** object of **redemption,** i.e., the firstborn donkey, **is redeemed,** despite the fact that the owner did not redeem it. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does **his** object of **redemption** belong **to the one who redeems** it, **or perhaps his** object of **redemption** belongs **to the owner?**

The *Gemara* confirms: if a third party redeems the donkey, the redemption stands and the sheep belongs to the *kohen*. But this immediately raises the next question.

### Donkey Ownership

*Bayu lei* — the question is raised: *pidyono la-podeh o pidyono la-be'alim* — does the redeemer get to keep the donkey, or does the original owner keep it? One can immediately see why the latter must be correct as a matter of social policy: if the redeemer could keep the donkey, anyone with a cheap sheep would be roaming the markets looking for valuable firstborn donkeys to redeem and claim. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* works through this carefully.

### Shimon Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה — מָמוֹנָא דִבְעָלִים הוּא, כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon you should not raise the dilemma, since he says** that prior to the redemption it is **permitted** for the owner **to** derive **benefit** from the firstborn donkey by using it for labor or renting it out. Therefore, it is **the property of the owner,** and giving a lamb to the priest does not change this status. **When you raise the dilemma,** it should be **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that it is **prohibited to** derive **benefit** from the firstborn donkey prior to its redemption. If so, it does not belong to the owner initially, and only redeeming the donkey with the lamb allows him to take possession of it.

According to Rabbi Shimon, there is no question: the donkey was never forbidden in benefit, so it was always fully in the owner's possession. The owner could always derive benefit from the donkey before redemption. The redeemer chose to perform the owner's *mitzvah* and spend his own money — that is admirable, but it grants him no title to the donkey. The donkey was the owner's from start to finish.

### Yehuda Problem

The question is sharper according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds the donkey was forbidden in benefit before redemption. In that case, the owner's benefit in the donkey was constrained, and one might argue the donkey resembles *hekdesh* — effectively out of the owner's hands. If it were truly analogous to *hekdesh*, then by the verse *v'natan ha-kesef v'kam lo* — whoever pays the money gets the object — the redeemer would take the donkey, just as a third-party redeemer of sanctified property walks away with the asset.

### Benefit Interest

Or, alternatively, perhaps even under Rabbi Yehuda the donkey is not fully analogous to *hekdesh*, because the owner retains a monetary interest even before redemption: the right to redeem with a cheaper sheep and profit the difference. That residual monetary stake, one might argue, keeps the donkey within the owner's legal orbit and distinguishes it from *hekdesh*.

### Theft Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, תָּא שְׁמַע: הַגּוֹנֵב פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ — מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל לַבְּעָלִים, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עַכְשָׁיו — יֵשׁ לוֹ לְאַחַר מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who steals the firstborn donkey of another** before it is redeemed and he is then caught, **he pays the payment of double** the principal **to the owner** (see Exodus 22:3). **And even though** the owner **does not have** rights to the donkey **now, he does have** rights to it **afterward,** i.e., after the redemption.

*Rav Nachman* resolves the question with a proof: *ta shema* — the law is that if someone steals another person's firstborn donkey, they pay the owner double (*tashlumei chefel*). This is the case even though the donkey is forbidden in benefit at the time of the theft. The reason: although the owner has no present benefit in the donkey, *yesh lo l'achar mikan* — he will have benefit once he redeems it — and therefore the donkey has monetary value to him now, and a thief who steals something of his value owes double.

### Scripture Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אַמַּאי אֵין לוֹ עַכְשָׁיו? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ, ״וְגֻנַּב מִבֵּית הָאִישׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא מִבֵּית הֶקְדֵּשׁ! וְתוּ לָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **Whose** opinion does this follow? **If we say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, why does** the owner **not have** rights to it **now?** Rabbi Shimon holds that it is permitted to derive benefit from a firstborn donkey even before its redemption. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who deems it prohibited to derive benefit from a firstborn donkey prior to its redemption. **And if it enters your mind** that **we compare it to consecrated** property, then why must the thief pay double the principal? Doesn't **the Merciful One state** with regard to the double payment of a thief: **"And it be stolen from the man's house"** (Exodus 22:6), indicating: **But not** when it is stolen **from the Temple treasury?** In such a case the thief does not pay double. Evidently, a firstborn donkey does not have the status of consecrated property, and if another person redeemed it, it still belongs to the owner. The Gemara concludes: **And nothing more** remains to be said about this issue, as this is a clear proof.

The proof works specifically under Rabbi Yehuda's position, since under Rabbi Shimon the owner plainly has present benefit and the double payment is unproblematic. Under Rabbi Yehuda, the double payment demonstrates that the donkey remains the owner's property. This is further confirmed by the verse underlying the double-payment law: *mibeito shel ish amar Rachmana v'lo mibeis hekdesh* — the Torah says double payment applies when stealing from *a man's house*, not from *hekdesh*. If the donkey were genuinely like *hekdesh*, the thief would not owe double. The very fact that double is owed proves that the donkey is not classified as *hekdesh*, and the owner's property interest is intact.

### Owner Benefits

The conclusion is clear: the donkey is not like *hekdesh* in the relevant sense. Even under Rabbi Yehuda, the owner retains full ownership and a real monetary stake in the donkey. A third party's redemption is valid, and the sheep goes to the *kohen* — but the donkey remains with its original owner. While the *hekdesh* analogy is productive in certain respects (allowing value-based redemption and third-party redemption), it does not go so far as to strip the owner of title. The *Gemara* is aware of the tension: the donkey is not *hekdesh* in the full sense — it does not become sanctified, the money given for its redemption carries no sanctity, and the owner retains ownership. Some comparisons are drawn to *hekdesh* for practical purposes, but the analogy has clear limits.

## Doubtful Donkey Case

### Maaser Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת בִּיכְּרָה וְאַחַת שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרָה [כּוּ׳]. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד אָמְרוּ נִכְנַס לְדִיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that if a person owned two donkeys, and **one had** previously **given birth and one had not** previously **given birth,** and they gave birth to a male and a female, and it is not clear which donkey gave birth to the male and which to the female, he designates one lamb as redemption due to the uncertainty, as perhaps the one that had not previously given birth was the one that gave birth to the male. The mishna also states that this lamb is tithed with the other non-sacred animals. With regard to this point, the Gemara states that **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **In what case did** the Sages **say** that this lamb **enters the pen in order to be tithed?**

The *Gemara* turns to an aspect of a *Mishnah* that mentioned taking *maaser* from the sheep used to redeem doubtful firstborn donkeys (*safek pitrei chamorim*). This requires explanation.

### Maaser Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר בְּבָא לְיַד כֹּהֵן, שֶׁהֲרֵי שָׁנִינוּ: הַלָּקוּחַ וְשֶׁנִּיתַּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה פָּטוּר מִמַּעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה, אֶלָּא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה סְפֵק פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁמַּפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן עֲשָׂרָה שֵׂיִין, וּמְעַשְּׂרָן, וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **You cannot say** that this is referring to a case **where** the lamb had already **entered the possession of a priest, as we learned** in a mishna (55b): An animal **that was purchased** by a person **or that was given to him as a present is exempt from animal tithe,** and this lamb was given as a present to the priest. **Rather,** it is referring **to an Israelite who has ten donkeys in his house** whose **firstborn** status is **uncertain, who designates ten lambs for them** in order to redeem them due to the uncertainty, **and he tithes** those lambs as well, **and they are his.**

The *Gemara* clarifies that the *Mishnah* cannot be referring to sheep that were actually given to a *kohen*, because a *kohen* who receives sheep as a gift or by purchase is exempt from *maaser behemah*. The law of *maaser behemah* — the animal tithe — applies only to animals born into one's own flock, not to animals received as gifts or purchases. The *kohen* therefore does not take *maaser behemah* on redemption sheep he receives.

### Ten Donkeys Case

The actual scenario is this: a Jew had ten doubtful firstborn donkeys in his house. He separates ten sheep — one for each donkey — to effect a conditional redemption: since he cannot prove any of the donkeys is firstborn, he performs the act of redemption so that if a given donkey is firstborn, it is no longer forbidden in benefit. Because the *kohen* cannot prove any entitlement, the owner keeps all ten sheep.

### Maaser Despite Keeping

The *Mishnah* teaches that even though the owner keeps the sheep, he still separates *maaser behemah* from them.

### Maaser Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנַלַן דְּסִימָנֵי טׇהֳרָה מִילְּתָא הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents support for this explanation: **And from where do we** derive **that indicators of a kosher** animal **are** considered a significant **matter** concerning the impurity of food?

Why? Because the owner keeps the sheep on the basis of monetary law (*al pi mamon*) — they are his, they were born into his flock that year. They are not firstborn animals; they are newly born animals of his, held by him. The fact that he used them in a conditional redemption ritual does not change his ownership. One might have thought that since the *kohen* might be entitled to them, they are somehow not fully "his" for purposes of *maaser behemah*. The *Gemara* rejects this: *al pi mamon* they belong to him entirely, they were born to him, and he must therefore separate *maaser behemah* from them.

## Inheritance Exemption

### Inheritance Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה סְפֵק פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים — מַפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן עֲשָׂרָה שֵׂיִין, וּמְעַשְּׂרָן, וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in addition, **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** In the case of **an Israelite who has** in his house **ten donkeys** whose **firstborn** status is **uncertain designates ten lambs** to redeem them, **and tithes** the lambs, separating one as a tithe, **and they** all **belong to him,** as a priest cannot prove that he is entitled to any of the ten.

*Rav Nachman* said in the name of *Rabbah bar Avuha*: a Jew had ten definite (*vadai*) firstborn donkeys in his possession, which had descended to him through his maternal grandfather who was a *kohen*. That grandfather had himself inherited them from his maternal grandfather, who was a *Yisrael* — and it was that great-great-grandfather who was originally obligated to redeem these donkeys and give a sheep to a *kohen*.

### Kohen Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים וַדָּאִין בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁנָּפְלוּ מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן, וְאוֹתוֹ אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן נָפְלוּ לוֹ מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise, **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** In the case of **an Israelite who has** in his house **ten donkeys** whose **firstborn** status is **uncertain designates ten lambs** to redeem them, **and tithes** the lambs, separating one as a tithe, **and they** all **belong to him,** as a priest cannot prove that he is entitled to any of the ten.

When these donkeys passed into the hands of the grandfather who was a *kohen*, the obligation to give to a *kohen* was suspended, because a *kohen* who owns firstborn donkeys is exempt — as established by the first *Mishnah* of the tractate. In the grandfather's hands, while the donkeys remain forbidden in benefit (requiring redemption), there is no obligation to give the redemption sheep to any other *kohen*.

### Status Change

When the grandfather died and these donkeys passed to his grandson, a *Yisrael*, the question arises: does the obligation to give the sheep to a *kohen* reemerge, or does the passage through the grandfather's exempt hands permanently waive that obligation? The ruling is that the obligation does not reemerge. Since the grandfather was exempt from giving, and the current owner receives his title through the grandfather, the exemption travels with the inheritance.

### Resolution

Practically: the Yisrael descendant must still redeem the donkeys — they remain forbidden in benefit and the act of redemption is required to lift that status. But having performed the redemption, he keeps the sheep himself. He is not obligated to give them to a *kohen*.

## Grain Parallel

### Grain Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ טְבָלִים מְמוֹרָחִין בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁנָּפְלוּ מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן, וְאוֹתוֹ אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן נָפְלוּ לוֹ מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל — מְעַשְּׂרָן וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise, **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** In the case of **an Israelite who has smoothed** piles of **untithed produce in his house,** from which one is obligated to separate *teruma* for the priests and tithes for the Levites, and it is produce **that was bequeathed** to him **from the household of his mother's father,** who was **a priest, and that mother's father** who was **a priest** previously had it **bequeathed to him from the household of his mother's father,** who was **an Israelite, he separates** *teruma* and **tithes** from the produce **and it is his,** just as if his grandfather who was a priest had tithed the produce, acquired the tithe, and bequeathed it to him as an inheritance.

*Rav Nachman* said in the name of *Rabbah bar Avuha* — the same authority and the same structural case, now applied to grain. A *Yisrael* had *tevel* (*memurachim* — grain whose heap has been smoothed and is therefore fully obligated in *terumos u'ma'asros*) in his house. This grain descended to him from his maternal grandfather, who was a *kohen*, who had in turn inherited it from his own maternal grandfather, who was a *Yisrael* — and in whose hands the full obligations to give *terumos u'ma'asros* to a *kohen* applied.

The structural parallel is close. When the grain was in the hands of the grandfather who was a *kohen*, he was still obligated to separate *terumos u'ma'asros* in order to remove the *tevel* status — but being a *kohen*, he was entitled to keep what he separated, including *maaser rishon* (which in the post-*Ezra* period may be given to a *kohen*). When the grain descended to the *Yisrael* grandson, the question is whether he must still give the *terumos u'ma'asros* to a *kohen* or whether the passage through the grandfather's hands exempts him from that giving.

### As-If Principle

The ruling is: *ma'asran v'hein shelo* — he separates the *terumos u'ma'asros* but keeps them himself. The reasoning, as explained, is that it is as if the grandfather had already performed the separation and was entitled to keep what he separated. Since the grandfather could have kept it and then bequeathed it, the descendant stands in the grandfather's position: obligated to perform the act of separation (because the *tevel* still has forbidden status), but entitled to retain the separated portions.

### Two Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן קַמַּיְיתָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַפְרַישׁ וְקָאֵי. אֲבָל הָכָא — מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ כְּמִי שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְועִינַן הָכָא — דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְעַשּׂוֹרֵי מִינֵּיהּ וּבֵיהּ, דְּהָא מַנַּח, אֲבָל הָתָם — כֵּיוָן דְּשֶׂה מֵעָלְמָא בָּעֵי אֵתוֹיֵי, וּמַפְרַישׁ וְקָאֵי, אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that both rulings of Rav Naḥman, i.e., the one concerning firstborn donkeys and the one concerning untithed produce, are **necessary. Because had he taught us** only **the first** case, i.e., that of the firstborn donkeys, one could claim that perhaps only in that case the Israelite grandson may keep the lambs; it is considered as if his grandfather who was a priest designated the lambs and acquired them **because** they **stand separated.** An act of separation would have been unnecessary, as the priest could have simply declared that his own lambs were serving to redeem the donkeys. **But here,** with regard to the untithed produce, one must separate the *teruma* and declare it as such in order for the priest to acquire it. Therefore, perhaps **gifts** to which members of the priesthood are entitled **that were not separated are** treated **as if they were not separated,** even if they were already in the possession of a priest. If so, **say** that it is **not** considered as if his grandfather who was a priest acquired the *teruma*. **And had** Rav Naḥman **taught us** only **here,** with regard to untithed produce, that it is treated as if his grandfather who was a priest separated the *teruma* and acquired it, one might claim that this is only **because it is possible to tithe** the produce **from itself, as** both the untithed produce and the *teruma* that will be separated from it **lie** together. **But there,** with regard to a firstborn donkey, **since one needs to bring a lamb from elsewhere** to redeem it, **say** that the principle that **the lamb stands separated** and does not require additional separation is **not** applied. Therefore, it is **necessary** for both cases to be stated.

The *Gemara* explains why both cases — the donkeys and the grain — are necessary: *u'tzricha*. Had only the donkey case been stated, one might have said: the reason the obligation to give is considered already fulfilled is that a donkey and a sheep are distinct, independent living animals — the sheep already exists as a separate entity, so it is easy to say the grandfather's exemption effectively discharged the obligation, as if the separation already happened. Perhaps with grain, where the *terumah* and *maaser* must be physically measured out and separated from the very same pile, I would not say that an unfulfilled separation (*matanah shelo hurma*) is treated as if already performed (*k'mi shehurmah dami*). Conversely, had only the grain case been stated, one might have said: grain is easy to regard as already separated because all the relevant material is present in the pile itself — the potential is entirely there. With the donkey, the redemption sheep must come from somewhere else entirely, unrelated to the donkey; perhaps there the exemption would not follow through the grandfather. Both cases together establish the full reach of the principle.

## Tevel Non-Jew

### Purchase from Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נָתָן אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַלּוֹקֵחַ טְבָלִים מְמוֹרָחִין מִן הַגּוֹי, מְעַשְּׂרָן וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a variation of the previous case. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Natan says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** With regard to **one who purchases untithed produce** that is gathered in **smoothed** piles, **from a gentile, he tithes** the piles **but they are his,** as he is not required to give the *teruma* to a priest or the tithes to a Levite.

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Natan* said in the name of *Rabbi Chanina*: if a person purchases *tevel memurachim* — grain already rendered obligatable by smoothing — from a non-Jew, he separates *terumos u'ma'asros* but keeps the separated portions himself.

### Keep Despite Maaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמָרְחִינְהוּ מַאן? אִילֵּימָא דְּמָרְחִינְהוּ גּוֹי — ״דְּגָנְךָ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא דְּגַן גּוֹי! אֶלָּא דְּמָרְחִינְהוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵרְשׁוּת גּוֹי. מְעַשְּׂרָן, דְּאֵין קִנְיָן לְגוֹי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַפְקִיעַ מִיַּד מַעֲשֵׂר, וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ — דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: קָאָתֵינָא מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא דְּלָא מָצֵית (אִישְׁתַּעֵית) [אִישְׁתַּעוֹיֵי] דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who smoothed** the piles? **If we say that a gentile smoothed them,** doesn't **the Merciful One state: "Your grain"** (Deuteronomy 12:17, 18:4), with regard to *teruma* and tithes, indicating that only grain whose processing is completed by a Jew is subject to the rules of *teruma* and tithes, **but not the grain of a gentile?** **Rather,** clearly **a Jew smoothed them** while they were **in the possession of the gentile** before purchasing them. Therefore, **he tithes them, as a gentile has no** capability of **acquisition** of land **in Eretz Yisrael that would cause the abrogation** of the sanctity of the land, thereby removing it **from** the obligation to **tithe** its produce. **But** the tithes **are** still the Jew's, **as** he **says to** the priest: **I have come** to own this produce **on the basis of** the rights of **a man with whom you cannot engage in litigation,** i.e., a gentile, from whom I bought the produce. Since he is not obligated to give *teruma* or tithes to the priest, I am not required to give them either.

The fact that he keeps the separated portions requires explanation. If he is obligated in *terumos u'ma'asros*, he should also be obligated to give them to a *kohen*. If he is exempt because it came through a non-Jew, he should not need to separate at all. Why the middle position — separate but keep?

### Non-Jew Ownership

The *Gemara* clarifies: *ein kinyan l'oved kochavim b'Eretz Yisrael l'hafki'ah miyad ma'aser* — non-Jewish ownership of land in *Eretz Yisrael* does not exempt its produce from *terumos u'ma'asros*. The grain remains obligatable even though a non-Jew owned the land. Furthermore, the obligation is activated only when a Jew performs the *meiruach* — the smoothing of the grain pile; if the non-Jew did the smoothing, the verse *degancha* ("your grain") would exempt it. Here we assume the Jew purchased it and then performed the smoothing, triggering the obligation. Why, then, does he not have to give to the *kohen*?

### Beis Din Rationale

The *Gemara*'s answer — at this stage — is: *ka'atina mi-koach gavra de-la mazit l'ishtauei dina bahadeih* — "I come through the power of a person you could never have brought to *beis din*." Because the non-Jew was never subject to the court's jurisdiction in these matters, the *kohen* never had a practically enforceable claim against the non-Jew. The person who purchased it from the non-Jew therefore stands, in some sense, beyond the reach of that claim. This is acknowledged as a conceptually unusual argument — it is not obvious that an unenforced obligation means the obligation is extinguished — but it is a principle that does appear elsewhere in the *Shas*, particularly in the context of fields that passed through non-Jewish hands.

## Swap Debate

### Grain Swap Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַמַּפְקִיד פֵּירוֹתָיו אֵצֶל הַכּוּתִי וְאֵצֶל עַם הָאָרֶץ, בְּחֶזְקָתָן לַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְלַשְּׁבִיעִית. אֵצֶל הַגּוֹי — כְּפֵירוֹתָיו, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional discussion involving this issue: **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Demai* 3:4) that with regard to **one who deposits his produce with a Samaritan or with one who is unreliable with regard to tithes [*am ha'aretz*],** when they return it to him, the produce retains **its presumptive status with regard to** the *halakhot* of **tithe and** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year, both of which must be removed from one's property. The Samaritan or *am ha'aretz* is not suspected of having exchanged the deposited produce with untithed produce or produce of the Sabbatical Year. But in the case of one who deposits his produce **with a gentile,** the produce returned to him is treated **as the produce of** the gentile, as he presumably exchanged it with his own. **Rabbi Shimon says:** It is treated as **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*],** as it is uncertain whether the gentile exchanged the produce or not.

The *Gemara* now cites a *Mishnah* from elsewhere: *ha-mafkid peiros etzel Kusi o etzel am ha-aretz* — if one deposits grain or produce with a *Kusi* (Samaritan) or an *am ha-aretz* (someone not careful in *terumos u'ma'asros*), we assume the grain is *b'chezkas* — in the same status — and we are not concerned that the keeper swapped it with their own grain. However, *aval etzel oved kochavim* — if deposited with a non-Jew — the returned grain is treated *k'peirosav*, like the non-Jew's own produce. We are concerned that he swapped the grain, and since the non-Jew's grain (which grew while he owned the land) is still obligatable in *terumos u'ma'asros* (given *ein kinyan*), the returned grain is treated accordingly.

### Separation vs Giving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְהַפְרִישׁ — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי — לִיתְּנָן לְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Elazar says: Everyone agrees** that one is obligated **to separate** *teruma* and tithes from the produce that the gentile returned to him. **When they disagree** it is **with regard to giving it to the priest** following the separation. **The first *tanna* holds** that the gentile **certainly exchanged** the produce, **and** therefore the owner **needs to give** the *teruma* **to the priest. And Rabbi Shimon holds** that the produce is treated as ***demai*,** so in order to receive *teruma*, the priest must bring proof that the produce requires tithing.

Rabbi Shimon says: the status is *demai* — there is doubt, not certainty, about whether swapping occurred. *Rabbi Elazar* clarifies the dispute: both opinions agree that you must separate *terumos u'ma'asros*, because the possibility of obligated grain in your hands is sufficient to require separation (given the severe prohibition of eating *tevel*). The dispute is about whether you must then *give* the separated portions to a *kohen*. The *tanna kamma* holds that we assume the swap was definite (*vadai chalufi*) and therefore you must give the *terumah* to a *kohen*. Rabbi Shimon holds it is only doubtful and therefore you separate but keep the portions (since the *kohen* cannot prove his entitlement against a *sfek*).

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב דִּימִי וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: טַעְמָא דִּמְסַפְּקָא לַן אִי חַלְּפִינְהוּ אִי לָא חַלְּפִינְהוּ, הָא וַדַּאי חַלְּפִינְהוּ — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְּבִינְהוּ לְכֹהֵן, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַלּוֹקֵחַ טְבָלִים מִן הַגּוֹי מְמוֹרָחִין — מְעַשְּׂרָן וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi sat and stated this *halakha*** of Rabbi Elazar. **Abaye said to him: The reason** that Rabbi Shimon disagrees is **that we are uncertain whether** the gentile **exchanged it** or **whether he did not exchange it. But** if **he definitely exchanged it, everyone** agrees **that** he is **required to give** the *teruma* and tithes **to a priest.** Why? **But doesn't Rabbi Shmuel say** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** In the case of **one who purchases untithed produce from a gentile** that is gathered in **smoothed** piles, i.e., it was completely processed, **he tithes** and separates *teruma* from the piles of produce **but they are his,** and he is not required to give the *teruma* to a priest?

This seems to directly contradict what was stated earlier: that if you purchase *tevel* from a non-Jew, you separate and keep — even definitively. Now the *tanna kamma* says that if the grain was definitely swapped by a non-Jew, you must give it to the *kohen*, even though it came through a non-Jew. The contradiction is apparent.

### Exception Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא כָּאן בִּתְרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, כָּאן בִּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi said to him: **Perhaps here,** in the mishna that indicates that he must give the *teruma* to a priest, it is referring **to *teruma gedola*,** i.e., the *teruma* that is separated from the produce before tithes, whereas **there,** Rabbi Ḥanina's statement that he separates tithes from them but they are his is referring **to** the ***teruma*** that is separated from **the tithe.**

The *Gemara* resolves: *dilma kan b'terumah gedolah, kan b'terumas ma'aser* — perhaps the two cases deal with different portions. The blanket rule that you "separate and keep" when grain comes through a non-Jew applies only to *terumas ma'aser* — the portion the *Levi'im* separate from their own *ma'aser*, which is a tenth of a tenth, totaling 1% of the grain. For everything else (*terumah gedolah* and *ma'aser*), if the grain was definitely swapped by the non-Jew, you must give it to the *kohen*.

### Verse Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַזְכַּרְתַּן מִילְּתָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מִנַּיִן לַלּוֹקֵחַ טְבָלִים מְמוֹרָחִין מִן הַגּוֹי שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר מִתְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶל הַלְוִיִּם תְּדַבֵּר וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵיהֶם כִּי תִקְחוּ מֵאֵת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, טְבָלִים שֶׁאַתָּה לוֹקֵחַ מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל — אַתָּה מַפְרִישׁ מֵהֶן תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר וְנוֹתְנָהּ לַכֹּהֵן, טְבָלִים שֶׁאַתָּה לוֹקֵחַ מִן הַגּוֹי — אִי אַתָּה מַפְרִישׁ מֵהֶן תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר וְנוֹתְנָהּ לַכֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to him: **You reminded me of a statement that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: From where** is it derived **that one who purchases from a gentile untithed produce** that is gathered in **smoothed** piles **is exempt from** separating the ***teruma* of the tithe?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And you shall speak to the Levites, and say to them: When you take** the tithe from **the children of Israel**…then you shall set apart of it a gift for the Lord, even a tithe of the tithe" (Numbers 18:26). It can be inferred from here that concerning **untithed produce that you purchase from the children of Israel, you separate *teruma* of the tithe from it and give** the *teruma* of the tithe **to a priest,** but concerning **untithed produce that you purchase from a gentile, you do not separate *teruma* of the tithe from it and give it to a priest.**

The source for the exemption by *terumas ma'aser* is derived from a verse: *v'el ha'Levi'im tedaber v'amarta aleihem ki tikchu mei'eis Bnei Yisrael* — "speak to the *Levi'im* and say to them: when you take from the children of Israel, you shall set aside a *ma'aser* from the *ma'aser*." The verse specifies *mei'eis Bnei Yisrael* — when you take from Israelites. If you take grain originating from a non-Jew, you separate *terumas ma'aser* but do not give it to the *kohen*. *Tosafos* adds nuance: the *pshat* of the verse addresses a scenario where a non-Jew voluntarily gave his *ma'aser* to a *Levi*, in which case the *Levi* does not need to separate *terumas ma'aser* — because the non-Jew's grain is intrinsically exempt from *terumas ma'aser*, and that exemption carries through to whoever receives it.

### Separation Still

Why does one still need to separate at all, even if exempt from giving? The answer reflects the broader principle: once you are in possession of obligated grain, the removal of the *tevel* status requires the physical act of separation. The question of *to whom* you give the separated portion is distinct from the question of whether you must separate. Because the grain was obligated (by virtue of *ein kinyan* and a Jew performing the *meiruach*), separation is required. Only the subsequent *giving* to a *kohen* is affected by the path of acquisition through a non-Jew.

The structural parallel to the earlier cases — donkeys passing through a *kohen*'s estate, grain passing through a *kohen*'s estate — is notable. In each case, the act of separation or redemption is still required to remove the relevant prohibited status, but the obligation to *give* is waived because it passed through hands that were exempt from giving. The principle is consistent across the *sugya*.

## Sheep Death

### Death Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם מֵת נֶהֱנִים בּוֹ. דְּמִית הֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּמִית בֵּי כֹהֵן, וְנֶהֱנֶה בּוֹ כֹּהֵן — פְּשִׁיטָא, מָמוֹנָא דִּידֵיהּ הוּא! אֶלָּא, דְּמִית בֵּי בְעָלִים וְנֶהֱנֶה בּוֹ כֹּהֵן — הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it dies, one may derive benefit from it.** The Gemara asks: **Where did it die? If we say that it died** in **the house of the priest, and** the mishna means that **the priest may derive benefit from it,** isn't it **obvious?** The lamb **is his property. Rather,** perhaps the mishna means **that it died** in **the house of the owner** before it was given to the priest, **and** teaches that **the priest may derive benefit from it.** Isn't **this also obvious?**

The *daf* concludes with the final *Mishnah* of the perek: a sheep used to redeem a firstborn donkey died in the owner's possession after the redemption was performed but before it was physically handed to the *kohen*.

### Kohen Benefits

The *Mishnah* rules: *nehenin bo kohen* — the *kohen* may derive benefit from the dead sheep. And, by implication, the owner's obligation of redemption is complete. He need not redeem the donkey again.

### Possession Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, כֹּל כַּמָּה דְּלָא מְטָא לִידֵיהּ לָא זְכָה בֵּיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמֵעִידָּנָא דְּאַפְרְשֵׁיהּ, בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דְּכֹהֵן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It might **enter your mind to say** that **as long as** the lamb **has not reached the possession of** the priest, the priest **has not acquired it.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us that from the time that** the Israelite **separated it, it stands in the possession of the priest.**

The *Gemara* asks: is this obvious? Once the sheep was handed to the *kohen*, of course he may use the carcass — it is his. The interesting case must be where it died before being handed over. The *Gemara* confirms this is indeed the *Mishnah*'s case. And it teaches: *be-idna de-afrashei, bi-reshusa de-kohen ka'i* — at the very moment the owner designated the sheep for the redemption, the *kohen* took possession of it in principle. The *kohen*'s right to the sheep vests at the moment of redemption, not at the moment of physical transfer. Therefore, if the sheep subsequently died in the owner's house, it died as the *kohen*'s sheep, and the owner's act of redemption is fully complete.

### Blessing Implication

*Tosafos* draws a practical consequence from this principle: the *berachah* (*bracha*) over the redemption of a firstborn donkey is recited at the moment of the redemption itself — when the owner designates the sheep — and not at the moment of handing it over to the *kohen*. This is because the *mitzvah* act is the designation and the transfer of status, not the physical delivery. Just as the *kohen*'s possession vests at that moment, so too the *mitzvah* is fulfilled at that moment.

*Tosafos* also notes that this parallels the principle established at the end of *Chullin* regarding *matanot kehunah*: the priestly gifts are considered to be owned by the *kehunah* collectively even before they are handed over to any specific *kohen*. Here too, the redemption sheep belongs to the *kehunah* from the moment of designation. The final act of physically delivering the sheep to a named *kohen* is the discharge of that delivery obligation — but the *mitzvah* of redemption, and with it the owner's liability, is already complete.
