# Eruvin 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Food Quantities

### Sheshisa/Kisoni

The daf begins at the bottom of 28b, dealing with various items and their use in an *eiruv techumin*. To recall the framework: while *eiruv chatzeiros* requires bread specifically, *eiruv techumin* may be established with any type of food sufficient to constitute two meals. The Gemara proceeds to address specific foods whose quantities require clarification.

*Sheshisa* is a preparation made from roasted grain combined with honey and other ingredients — a kind of sweet food. Since there is no question that it qualifies as human food, the only issue is the required amount. Rav Acha bar Pinchas rules: two scoops (*trei sargushi*), whatever that measure entails locally.

*Kisoni* is similar but simpler — roasted grain without the mixture. Abaye rules: two measures of Pumbedisa (*trei bunei de-Pumbedisa*), the local standard of that city. Abaye then adds a medical note in the name of the woman who raised him, whom he calls his *em* — his nursemaid, who served as his mother after his own mother died. She taught him that *kisoni*, roasted grain, is good for the heart (*ma'ali le-libba*) and dispels anxious thoughts (*mevatli machshavta*). Abaye further quotes her regarding a remedy for weakness of the heart: take meat from the right thigh of a ram, burn dried dung of grazing animals from the month of Nissan to make coals, roast the meat over them, eat it, and drink mixed wine afterward. Abaye frequently cited his nursemaid's guidance on such medical matters.

### Condiment vs. Main

Returning to the quantitative standards for *eiruv techumin*, Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel, five lines from the bottom of 28b: anything that functions as a condiment (*liftan*) requires the amount that one would eat *with* other food during two meals (*kedei le-echol bo*), while anything that is not a condiment requires the amount one would eat of it straight to constitute two meals (*kedei le-echol mimenu*).

There is a certain irony here: something less central to the meal — a condiment — requires a smaller quantity to count as two meals' worth for *eiruv techumin*, since one only uses a little of it per meal. As was discussed previously, even a small quantity of vinegar — the amount one would put on a salad — suffices, and according to one opinion it counts even if the salad was not the main dish.

### Raw/Roasted Meat

The Gemara turns to raw meat (*basar chai*). One might have thought raw meat could not be used for an *eiruv* at all — who eats raw meat? — yet the Gemara proceeds on the assumption that raw meat is edible and classifies it as *kedei le-echol mimenu*: one needs the amount one would eat of it straight, not as a condiment on bread. This is already quite surprising, since raw meat eaten as a stand-alone food is hardly a common practice.

*Basar tzelui*, roasted meat, generates a dispute. Rabba holds that roasted meat is a condiment (*kedei le-echol bo*) — you eat it together with bread, not straight. Rav Yosef disagrees and rules *kedei le-echol mimenu*, holding that roasted meat is eaten straight. Rav Yosef then asks: how do I know this? Because the Persians (*parsa'ei*) eat chunks of meat (*tavhikei*) without bread. Hence roasted meat is a stand-alone food, not merely a condiment.

### Persian Standard

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef: are Persians the majority of the world (*ruba de'alma*)? He demonstrates from a *baraita* that we do not allow a subgroup's practice to define the standard for everyone. The *baraita* teaches that the minimum size that constitutes a garment differs based on whether the owner is poor or wealthy. Clothing of the poor (*bigdei ani'im*) is classified as a garment at three finger-widths by three finger-widths, while clothing of the wealthy (*bigdei ashirim*) requires three *tefachim* by three *tefachim*. A piece of fabric that a poor person would use as a patch or small covering counts as a garment for him; the wealthy would discard anything of that size. These categories are therefore applied separately to each social group. One does not take the poor person's lower threshold and extend it to classify rags as garments for everyone; equally, one does not demand the wealthy person's standard of the poor.

The implication is clear: Persians' eating customs may define the *halacha* for Persians, but one cannot project that standard onto the rest of humanity. Each subgroup is assessed by the criteria relevant to it.

## Standards Debate

### Clothing Sizes

The discussion of Persian standards leads directly into a broader question about universal versus subjective or group-specific measures, and the Gemara introduces the clothing analogy as the governing precedent. As already noted, *bigdei ani'im la-ani'im* and *bigdei ashirim la-ashirim*: each group's garment-size threshold applies within that group only. The principle is that when different groups genuinely use objects differently, the relevant measure is determined by the practices of the group in question, not by some externally imposed uniform standard.

### Stringency/Leniency

The Gemara then raises a possible counter-principle: perhaps the criterion for applying a group-specific standard is whether it results in a *chumra* or a *kula*. In the clothing case, using the smaller standard for the poor person is a *chumra* applied to that group — a small scrap is enough to become *tamei*, which is the stringent result. So perhaps the rule is: we apply the subgroup's standard only when it produces a *stringency* for that subgroup.

By contrast, in the roasted meat case, invoking the Persian standard would produce a *chumra* for everyone — classifying roasted meat as a main dish requires a larger quantity for *eiruv*, making it harder to establish the *eiruv*. The Gemara seems to be suggesting that we could at least apply the Persian custom as a *chumra* for all. The way Rashi frames the issue is that perhaps the default assumption ought to be that a food is a main dish rather than a condiment, and that assumption should stand until there is evidence to the contrary that is relevant for the general population.

A *baraita* is then cited that complicates this *chumra*/*kula* framework considerably. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar rules: when establishing an *eiruv* for a sick person (*choleh*) or an elderly person (*zaken*), one uses the amount sufficient for *their* two meals — a subjective, reduced standard. But for someone who is ravenous (*ravtan*), one does not use his outsized meal as the measure; instead one applies the average standard (*seudah beinonis shel kol adam*).

This is striking. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar applies the subjective standard only in the lenient direction: when a person's particular circumstances call for a smaller quantity, we accommodate that. But when a person's exceptional appetite would call for a larger quantity, we revert to the universal average. The Gemara notes this is the exact opposite of what the *chumra*/*kula* framework might have predicted, and concludes: *kashya* — this is difficult. It is not straightforward to reconcile the various cases using a simple stringency-leniency principle.

It should be noted that the parallel is also imperfect on its own terms. In the case of the poor and the wealthy, each group's relevant standard is applied specifically to that group. The Gemara's attempt to use the Persian custom to establish a universal *chumra* does not fit that model cleanly: the issue there was not about applying Persians' own standard to Persians, but about extending it to everyone. And Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling is even more individualized — not a subgroup but a particular person — which is a greater *chiddush* than the *ani'im* and *ashirim* distinction.

### R. Shimon Standard

The Gemara questions whether Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is truly consistent in applying subjective standards. It asks: did Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar really say one can go leniently based on individual capacity?

A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar rules regarding Og Melech ha-Bashan — *pitcho k'milo'o*, his doorway must match his full size.

The context requires some background. There is a *halacha* known as *sof tumah latzes*: when a corpse lies in a house, the doorway through which the body will ultimately be removed is treated as if already opened, and *tumah* exits through it. If a specific exit is designated, only that exit transmits *tumah* to the outside; this spares the other exits. But a doorway can only serve as the designated exit if it is of minimal halachic size — at least four *tefachim* wide.

Now imagine the corpse of Og Melech ha-Bashan, the giant of Bashan, lies in a house. If one wishes to designate a specific exit so as to protect the other openings from *tumah*, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that the door must be large enough to actually extract the giant. A standard four-*tefachim* doorway would not suffice; one needs a doorway that matches the enormous body. This is subjective measurement in the stringent direction — applying a larger-than-normal standard because the particular object (in this case, the body) demands it.

This seems to contradict the leniency-only application of subjective standards that characterized Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's *eiruv* ruling. The Gemara asks: and did Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar really say this?

The Gemara cites Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: regarding Og Melech ha-Bashan's doorway, the standard four *tefachim* suffices (*pitcho b'arba*). This would mean we do not go by the giant's subjective size in the stringent direction after all — which would vindicate the claim that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar only applies subjective standards leniently.

The Gemara deflects this proof: perhaps Rabbi Yochanan's ruling applies only where there are *multiple* openings in the house, one of which is four *tefachim*, and one could designate that one as the exit since it is the most likely candidate for expansion — *de'ika chad de'hava arba'ah, de'vada'ei ki ka marvach behu ka marvach*. One expands the opening that is already at the minimal qualifying size. But if all openings are equally sub-standard for a body the size of Og's, none of them is a proper doorway for him at all, and then Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar might indeed hold that all are *tamei*.

The practical question of how Og came to be inside the house in the first place is left to the imagination.

Abaye then answers the challenge from Og's door more directly: *hasam heichi liyaved* — what do you want to do there, chop him up into pieces and carry him out? There is no alternative. With the ravenous person, by contrast, one could still manage with a standard two-meal quantity; it would merely be insufficient to fully sate him. The two cases are therefore not parallel. For Og's door, there is no way to call an undersized doorway his exit because physically he cannot fit through it. For the *ravtan*, the Gemara can still maintain the universal standard of two average meals.

### Og's Door

The Gemara then explores a further question: *ibaya lehu* — they raise the issue of whether the *rabbanan* dispute Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar or whether all agree with him. The Gemara brings a proof from Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan regarding Og's doorway — as just discussed, Rabbi Yochanan rules four *tefachim* suffices — and then deflects it on the grounds discussed above, that this could be a case where there is already one opening of four *tefachim* and one would expand that one.

The Gemara then returns to raw meat, having earlier assumed without discussion that it may be used for an *eiruv*. Rav Chiya bar Abba says in the name of Rav: one may indeed establish an *eiruv* with raw meat. Rav Shimi bar Chiya adds: one may establish an *eiruv* with raw eggs. How much raw egg constitutes two meals' worth? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — identified as *Sinai*, a title given to Rav Yosef for his encyclopedic mastery — said: two raw eggs, one per meal.

It is worth pausing on the strangeness of the underlying assumption. The Gemara takes it entirely for granted that raw meat is eaten straight, and that the only question about roasted meat is whether it is eaten straight or as a condiment. More people eat raw eggs than raw meat, to be sure. But the prior discussion's casual acceptance that raw meat is consumed *kedei le-echol mimenu* — as a straight food — remains puzzling.

The broader conceptual landscape emerging from this entire discussion is that there are at least three distinct axes along which standards may be set: (1) a single universal measure for all people; (2) a group-specific measure, such as for *ani'im* and *ashirim*, or for Persians; or (3) an individual-specific measure, as Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar applies for the *choleh* and *zaken*. And across these axes, there is a further distinction between what determines whether a food qualifies at all versus how much of it counts as two meals versus whether the food is a staple or a condiment. Different questions may call for different standards, and it is not always clear which axis governs which question.

## Neder/Shebuah

### Mazon/Zan

The Gemara shifts to a new topic introduced by the *Mishnah*: one who vows not to have *mazon* — *ha-noder min ha-mazon* — is nonetheless permitted water and salt. The implication is that water and salt are not called *mazon*, but everything else apparently is. The Gemara asks whether this poses a difficulty for Rav and Shmuel, who both hold that the blessing *borei minei mezonos* is recited only over the five grains (*chameshet ha-minim*) and not over other foods.

The question is pointed: if for *neder* purposes *mazon* encompasses virtually everything edible, how can Rav and Shmuel restrict *mezonos* to the five grains only? The Gemara notes that Rav and Shmuel have already been challenged on this point elsewhere in Berachos, and asks whether the present *Mishnah* constitutes yet another contradiction to their position.

Rav Huna resolves the difficulty by reframing the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* should be read not as *ha-noder min ha-mazon* — one who vows from *mazon* — but rather as *be-omer kol ha-zan alai*, one who declares forbidden anything that *zan* — that sustains — him. There is a conceptual distinction between *mazon*, which connotes that the item is a nutritive staple (its very identity is as a filler), and *zan*, which merely means that the item provides some degree of sustenance as one of its properties. Rav and Shmuel use *mazon* in the strict sense, limiting it to the five grains, which are the archetypal staple foods. The *Mishnah*, by contrast, uses the broader concept of *zan* — anything that fills to some degree — and therefore encompasses virtually all foods.

### Ginosar Fruits

The Gemara challenges even Rav Huna's reformulation. The claim is that water and salt don't *zan* — don't sustain — but everything else does. But does everything else truly provide satiation? The Gemara cites Rabbah bar bar Chana's account of following Rabbi Yochanan to eat the fruits of Ginosar in Eretz Yisrael — a passage that also appears in Berachos. The fruits of Ginosar were so extraordinarily delicious that quantities were gathered in enormous amounts: when a party of a hundred people went, each person gathered ten fruits; when a party of ten went, each gathered a hundred. Every hundred fruits would fill a basket holding three *se'ah*, an enormous volume. Yet after eating all of them, they would say — by way of a *shvua* — that they had not tasted enough to feel genuinely sated (*de-lo ta'amna zizuna*). The fruits were so pleasurable that the experience of fullness remained elusive. One is reminded of the popular observation about Chinese food.

The Gemara then clarifies the precise term used. The phrase is *zizuna* — a feeling of satiation — not *mazuna*, which would mean a staple food. This reinforces the distinction. The fruits of Ginosar do not fail to *zan* one; they merely fail to produce the feeling of being *mazuna*, of having consumed a staple. The distinction is between subjective satiation and the objective status of a food as a sustaining staple.

The Gemara concludes: according to Rav and Shmuel, *borei minei mezonos* is recited only over the five grains (or, in the view that extends the blessing, over rice as well). The *Mishnah* is broader because it uses *zan* rather than *mazon*. That resolves the apparent contradiction.

### Eat vs. Forbidden

Since the discussion has touched on *shvua* and *neder*, the Gemara proceeds to examine a related ruling. Rav Huna says in the name of Rav: if someone takes an oath that he will not eat a particular loaf of bread (*shvua she-lo ochel kikar zo*), one may nevertheless use that loaf for an *eiruv*. But if someone declares that this loaf is *forbidden to me* (*kikar zo alai*), one may not use it for an *eiruv*.

What distinguishes these two formulations? Rashi explains: the oath *she-lo ochel* — I will not eat — is personal and specific. The person has restricted his own act of eating, but the loaf itself retains its normal status; others may eat it freely. Since others can eat it, it qualifies as food, and one may use it for an *eiruv*. By contrast, *kikar zo alai* — this loaf is forbidden to me — is more generic and object-directed. The person has placed a broad prohibition on any benefit from this item, and using it for an *eiruv* constitutes a form of benefit that falls within that prohibition.

This raises a point flagged by Tosafos: according to a significant position in the Gemara, *eiruv techumin* may only be established as a *dvar mitzvah*. If so, does not the principle of *mitzvot lav le-hanot nitnu* — that commandments are not given for personal benefit — apply here, allowing one to use even a forbidden loaf? The standard application of *mitzvot lav le-hanot nitnu* is that the spiritual reward for fulfilling a commandment does not count as *hana'ah*. Here, however, one is gaining a concrete practical benefit — the ability to travel an additional distance — and so the principle may not apply straightforwardly. This tension is left open.

Tosafos makes an additional observation of significance for those who study Shvuos and Nedarim: there is a fundamental distinction between a *shvua* and a *neder* that underlies this entire discussion. A *shvua* is a first-person commitment — the person binds himself: *I* will not eat, *I* will not taste, *I* will not derive benefit. A *neder* operates on the object itself: *this item* is forbidden to me, as if the object has been rendered prohibited. This distinction appears almost explicitly in the final *baraita* under discussion: the emphasis on whether *I* am forbidden versus whether *the food* is forbidden captures the *shvua*/*neder* axis, not merely the difference between eating specifically and benefit generically.

### Baraita Disputes

The Gemara now examines a *baraita* that challenges Rav Huna's ruling. The *baraita* states: *ha-noder min ha-kikar, me'arvin lo bah* — if one vows not to eat from a loaf, one may use it for an *eiruv*. The Gemara initially reads this as the case where one has declared the loaf generically forbidden (*alai*), in which case Rav Huna's ruling would be contradicted. The Gemara deflects: no, the *baraita* means only the case where one said *I will not eat this* (*deamar zo*), which is consistent with Rav Huna.

The end of that *baraita* specifies: when is the loaf usable? When one said *shvua she-lo et'amenu* — an oath not to taste it. But if one said *alai* generically, what then? And the *baraita* concludes that if one declares *kikar zo hekdesh* — this loaf is consecrated — one may not use it for an *eiruv*, because *ein me'arvin be-hekdesh*. One cannot make an *eiruv* with consecrated property.

The problem is that the *baraita* seems to permit everything short of *hekdesh*, including the generic *alai* formulation. If *alai* were also forbidden, the *baraita* should have drawn that distinction rather than leaping to *hekdesh* as the only disqualifying case. The Gemara proposes reading the *baraita* as *chasurai mechasra* — incomplete as written — and reconstructing it to say: the *noder min ha-kikar* case is always permitted, even if one said *alai*; for *alai* is treated like *she-lo et'amenu* and is effective. Only *hekdesh* invalidates.

This reconstruction, however, creates difficulty for Rav Huna, whose ruling explicitly distinguishes *alai* from the oath not to eat. The Gemara concludes: that *baraita* follows Rabbi Eliezer (*d'amar ke-Rabbi Eliezer*), for we find in another *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer himself makes precisely Rav Huna's distinction: *shvua shelo ochel kikar zo, me'arvin lo bah; kikar zo alai, ein me'arvin lo bah*.

But then the Gemara questions whether Rabbi Eliezer was consistent, for yet another *baraita* in Rabbi Eliezer's name reads the opposite way — permitting everything except actual *hekdesh*. The resolution: *trei tanna'ei aliba de-Rabbi Eliezer* — there is a dispute among *tannaim* as to what Rabbi Eliezer's position was.

The net result is that there are two coherent positions: (1) the distinction lies between a personal oath not to eat and a generic object-directed prohibition, where the latter precludes using the loaf for an *eiruv* even though others may eat it; (2) everything is permitted until one actually sanctifies the loaf as *hekdesh*, which removes it from the category of permissible food for anyone, and only at that point is it disqualified.

## Who Cannot Eat

### Nazir/Wine Debate

The Gemara returns to the *Mishnah*'s ruling that an *eiruv* may be established with wine for a *nazir*. The assumption of the *tanna kamma* is that even if the person for whom the *eiruv* is placed cannot eat the food — since others can — it qualifies. A *nazir* is forbidden to drink wine, yet wine can be used for his *eiruv* because others can drink it. The *Mishnah*'s ruling is then identified as not following Beit Shammai.

### Yom Kippur Case

A *baraita* is cited: Beit Shammai say one may not make an *eiruv* for a *nazir* with wine nor for a *Yisrael* with *terumah*. Beit Hillel say one may do both. Our *Mishnah* follows Beit Hillel.

Beit Hillel press Beit Shammai with a challenge: do you not agree that one may establish an *eiruv* for an adult on Yom Kippur? On Yom Kippur no one may eat at all — the food is perfectly fit food, but the person for whom the *eiruv* is made cannot consume it on that day. Yet it is accepted that an *eiruv* may be placed. The food is fit to be eaten by Jews at other times, and it is food that was valid at the onset of Shabbos or Yom Kippur.

Beit Shammai respond: *aval* — they acknowledge the point — and Beit Hillel press the analogy: just as an *eiruv* may be made for an adult on Yom Kippur, so too for a *nazir* with wine, since others can drink it.

Beit Shammai might distinguish the cases: on Yom Kippur, the food was fit to be eaten by that very person a moment before Yom Kippur began — it was fit at the time of establishing the *eiruv*, when the boundary is set by being in the place as it becomes Yom Kippur. By contrast, wine for a *nazir* was never fit for him on the day the *eiruv* is placed. *Hacha leika seudah re'uyah mibod yom* — here there is no meal fit for him from the outset of that day.

The Gemara then asks: whose position is this debate following? *De-lo ke-Chananya*: not like Chananya. A *baraita* teaches that Chananya holds Beit Shammai categorically rejected the entire institution of *eiruv* as insufficient to establish the presence of a person in a location. For Beit Shammai, according to Chananya, a genuine establishment of one's location requires physically bringing all of one's possessions — one's bed, one's belongings — to that place. The debate about wine for a *nazir* presupposes that Beit Shammai accepts the general institution of *eiruv*; hence the dispute about wine for a *nazir* cannot be following Chananya's version of Beit Shammai.

The Gemara then asks who follows another *baraita* about one who made his *eiruv* wearing black clothes: if the next day he needs to go out wearing white clothes, he has not validly left (*lo yatza*). Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak identifies this as Chananya's formulation of Beit Shammai: since establishing one's presence requires bringing all one's possessions, needing different clothing the next day demonstrates that not everything was present at the time of the *eiruv*. The Gemara refines the ruling: the case is one who made the *eiruv* in white clothes and discovered he needs black — even the white clothes he had there are insufficient. One should appreciate that our normative practice follows Beit Hillel, and that considerably more stringent alternatives — including Chananya's Beit Shammai — were considered and rejected.

### Sumchus/Terumah

Sumchus dissents from the *tanna kamma*. He holds that a *Yisrael* cannot use *terumah* for an *eiruv*; the food must be fit for the person himself. The Gemara asks why Sumchus does not equally object to using wine for a *nazir* — there too the food is not fit for the person making the *eiruv*.

The answer: a *nazir* can in principle have his *nezirut* undone through *hatarat nedarim*. *Nezirut* is a verbal commitment, a species of *hekflaah*, and just as other verbal vows can be dissolved by a *chacham*, so can *nezirut*. Critically, *hatarat nedarim* is permitted even on Shabbos when done for the needs of Shabbos (*le-tzorech Shabbos*). Since he could — even during *bein ha-shmashos* — dissolve his *nezirut* in order to use the wine, the wine is theoretically fit for him. Hence Sumchus concedes that wine for a *nazir* qualifies.

But the same logic, argues the Gemara, would apply to *terumah*: the status of *terumah* is also produced by a verbal act of designation, itself a form of *haflaah*, and verbal designations can be undone. Why, then, can't a *Yisrael* theoretically undo the *terumah* status of the food during *bein ha-shmashos*, rendering it *chulin* and thus fit for him?

The Gemara proposes: undo it and re-designate *terumah* from other grain. But that grain is back at the house, not adjacent to the *eiruv* food — and *chaverim* do not separate *terumah* unless the grain is all together (*min ha-mukaf*). What about undoing it and re-designating from within the same portion? The problem is that the *eiruv* food contains exactly two meals' worth. If one were to peel off part of it to re-designate as *terumah*, one would be left with insufficient food for two meals.

The Gemara presses: perhaps that is only sometimes true; Sumchus must mean categorically that *terumah* never works. What is the principled distinction?

### Undoing Status

The Gemara provides the principled answer: Sumchus holds like the *rabbanan* against Rabbi Yehuda ha-Nasi. The debate concerns whether rabbinic prohibitions (*de-rabbanan*) are suspended during *bein ha-shmashos*. Rabbi Yehuda ha-Nasi holds that *bein ha-shmashos* is a period of doubt, and hence rabbinic prohibitions do not apply; it follows that rabbinic prohibitions are permitted *bein ha-shmashos* when the need is for Shabbos (*le-dvar mitzvah*). The *rabbanan* hold that even *bein ha-shmashos* is subject to rabbinic prohibitions.

Now, *hatarat nedarim* on Shabbos is itself a *de-rabbanan* prohibition — yet it is permitted *le-tzorech Shabbos* because everyone agrees to that dispensation. Separating *terumah* (*mefrish terumah*), however, is also a rabbinic prohibition during *bein ha-shmashos*, and there is no equivalent dispensation *le-tzorech Shabbos* for this act. Hence Sumchus, holding like the *rabbanan*, would not permit *mefrish terumah bein ha-shmashos*. One cannot say that in theory the *terumah* status could be undone, because the process of re-designating *terumah* is forbidden. Indeed, one of the things one recites as Shabbos approaches is *issartem eiravtem hadliku et ha-ner* — have you separated tithes, made your *eiruv*, lit the candles? Separating tithes must be done before *bein ha-shmashos*.

By contrast, *hatarat nedarim* is universally permitted *le-tzorech Shabbos* during *bein ha-shmashos*, even according to the *rabbanan*. Therefore, for a *nazir*, the theoretical path to making wine fit for him is open; for a *Yisrael* and *terumah*, it is not. That is the principled distinction that underlies Sumchus's position.

It is worth noting that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda ha-Nasi for a *dvar mitzvah*: a rabbinic prohibition during *bein ha-shmashos* on *erev Shabbos* or *motzai Shabbos* is permitted for the sake of a *mitzvah*. However, many *poskim* hold that this leniency is overridden once one has accepted Shabbos through *kabbalas Shabbos*, or before one has performed *havdalah*, since at that point the period is no longer merely objectively uncertain but has been subjectively designated as Shabbos or as weekday. The practical application therefore requires careful attention to circumstances.

## Personal Shiurim

### Individual Measures

The Gemara now links the subjective-standard discussion to a *Mishnah* elsewhere: *yesh she'amru ha-kol le-fi mah she-hu adam* — there are cases in which all the relevant measures follow the individual. Three such cases are cited. The *meloi kumtzo mincha*: when a *kohen* takes the *kometz* of the *mincha* offering, it is measured by his own handful, not a generic standard. The *meloi chofnav ketores*: when the *Kohen Gadol* takes two handfuls of incense on Yom Kippur, it is his own handfuls. And the quantity of liquid that constitutes a violation of the Yom Kippur fast is the amount that fills one's own cheek (*meloi lugmav*), not a universal measure. The *Mishnah* adds: and for *eiruv*, the measure of two meals follows how much the individual would eat.

### Subj. Agreement

Rav Zeira identifies this *Mishnah* as following Sumchus: *d'amar mai de-chazei lei bai'inan* — we require food that is fit for him, not food fit for others. By the same token, the amount is what he would eat, not what others would eat. The Gemara then raises the question whether this *Mishnah* is consistent with Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — and indeed it is, at least as regards the *choleh* and *zaken*: for them, the subjective, reduced amount suffices.

### Ravenous Limit

But the *Mishnah*'s statement that *ha-kol le-fi mah she-hu adam* seemingly applies to everyone — including someone who eats more than average. That would appear to contradict Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling that for a *ravtan*, one does not go beyond the *seudah beinonis* of the average person.

The Gemara resolves this: the *Mishnah*'s subjective standard applies to the *choleh* and *zaken*. The ravenous person is different — *batla da'ato eitzel kol adam*: his view is disregarded relative to most people. The *ravtan*'s appetite exceeds what any normal person in his situation would eat. By contrast, a sick person or elderly person genuinely eats less because of his condition, and that is a recognized category. The criterion is not simply the individual's personal preference but whether the individual belongs to a recognized category of people — *choleh*, *zaken* — for whom a different standard is genuinely appropriate. The *ravtan* does not constitute such a category; his ravenous appetite is not representative of any normal population of people.

The Gemara thus concludes: according to Sumchus and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, subjective standards govern both the type of food (it must be fit for the individual) and the quantity (it is measured by what the individual would eat, not the population average). The *rabbanan*, however, disagree at minimum on the type of food: it suffices that others can eat it. Whether the *rabbanan* also disagree on the question of quantity — reverting to a universal two-meal standard rather than the individual's own appetite — remains a point of genuine uncertainty from the discussion throughout this daf.
