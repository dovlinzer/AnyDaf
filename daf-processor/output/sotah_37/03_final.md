# Sotah 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Joseph's Restraint

### Joseph Intro

The daf opens against the backdrop of a broader discussion about the division of tribes at *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* and the figures of Yosef and Yehoshua. The Gemara had noted that two individuals are paradigmatic of *kiddush shem shamayim*: Yosef performed it *b'seter* — in secret — while Yehudah performed it *b'rabbim* — in public. The Gemara now turns to examine each case in depth, beginning with Yosef.

### Twelve Tribes from Joseph

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: הָיָה רָאוּי יוֹסֵף לָצֵאת מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁיָּצְאוּ מִיַּעֲקֹב אָבִיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵלֶּה תֹּלְדוֹת יַעֲקֹב יוֹסֵף״, אֶלָּא שֶׁיָּצָא שִׁכְבַת זַרְעוֹ מִבֵּין צִיפּוֹרְנֵי יָדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Joseph was deserving of having twelve tribes descend from him,** the same **as** twelve tribes **descended from his father Jacob, as it is stated: "These are the generations of Jacob, Joseph"** (Genesis 37:2). This implies that everything that happened to Jacob was destined to happen to Joseph. **However,** he did not merit this because **his semen was emitted from between his fingernails.**

A *tanna* teaches: *haya ra'ui Yosef latzeit mimenu yud-bet shvatim* — Yosef should rightfully have fathered all twelve tribes, just as Yaakov his father did. The textual basis is the juxtaposition in *Bereishit*: *Eleh toldot Yaakov Yosef* — "These are the generations of Yaakov: Yosef." The language suggests a direct parallel, as if Yosef were to be to his generation what Yaakov was to his. The *toldot* formula ordinarily introduces a genealogical listing, and the singular mention of Yosef implies he was meant to carry that generative role in full.

### Benjamin's Ten Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן, יָצְאוּ מִבִּנְיָמִין אָחִיו, וְכוּלָּן נִקְרְאוּ עַל שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְנֵי בִנְיָמִן בֶּלַע וָבֶכֶר וְאַשְׁבֵּל וְגוֹ׳״. ״בֶּלַע״ — שֶׁנִּבְלַע בֵּין הָאוּמּוֹת, ״וּבֶכֶר״ — בְּכוֹר לְאִמּוֹ הָיָה, ״וְאַשְׁבֵּל״ — שֶׁשְּׁבָאוֹ אֵל, ״גֵּרָא״ — שֶׁגָּר בְּאַכְסַנְיוֹת, ״וְנַעֲמָן״ — שֶׁנָּעִים בְּיוֹתֵר, ״אֵחִי וָרֹאשׁ״ — אָחִי הוּא וְרֹאשִׁי הוּא, ״מֻפִּים וְחֻפִּים״ — הוּא לֹא רָאָה בְּחוּפָּתִי וַאֲנִי לֹא רָאִיתִי בְּחוּפָּתוֹ, ״וָאָרְדְּ״ — שֶׁיָּרַד לְבֵין אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: ״וָאָרְדְּ״ — שֶׁפָּנָיו דּוֹמִין לְוֶורֶד.
>
> **Translation:** **And even so,** the offspring that were meant to descend from him **descended from his brother Benjamin,** who had ten sons. **And they were all named after** Joseph, **as it is stated: "And the sons of Benjamin: Bela, and Becher, and Ashbel,** Gera, and Naaman, Ehi, and Rosh, Muppim, and Huppim, and Ard" (Genesis 46:21). The Gemara explains how each name relates to Joseph: **Bela** was named after Joseph, **who was swallowed [*nivla*] among the nations. And Becher** received that name because Joseph **was the firstborn [*bekhor*] of his mother,** Rachel. **And Ashbel** received his name **because God sent** Joseph **into captivity [*shevao El*]** in Egypt. **Gera** was named after Joseph, **who dwelled [*gar*] in a foreign land [*akhsaneyut*]. And Naaman** was called that **because** Joseph **was extremely pleasing [*na'im*]. Ehi and Rosh** received these names, as Benjamin said: Joseph **is my brother [*aḥi*] and my leader [*roshi*].** Benjamin named his sons **Muppim and Huppim,** as Benjamin said: Joseph **did not see my wedding canopy [*ḥuppa*] and I did not see his wedding canopy. And Ard** was named after Joseph, **who descended [*yarad*] to** the lands of **the nations of the world. Some say** that the name **Ard** means **that** Joseph's **face was similar** in its beauty **to a rose [*vered*].**

Yet this potential was forfeited at the decisive moment of temptation with the wife of Potifar: *ela sheyatza shichvat zaro mibein tzipornei yadav* — his seed went forth from between his fingernails. The act of restraint was heroic, yet it entailed a real loss. The Gemara notes, somewhat ruefully, that even though the occasion was a *kiddush shem shamayim*, the circumstances that led to it could perhaps have been avoided altogether. As the old distinction goes: a wise person never enters the situation a clever person needs to escape.

Nevertheless, *ve'af al pi chen yatze'u miBinyamin achiv* — the ten tribes Yosef was meant to father emerged instead from his brother Binyamin. *Vekulan nikre'u al shemo* — all ten of Binyamin's sons were named in memory of Yosef and the separation between them. The names are parsed one by one: *Bela*, because Yosef was *nivla bein ha'umot* — swallowed among the nations; *Vacher*, because Yosef was at least the firstborn of their shared mother; *Ashbel*, because God had taken him captive (*shevao Kel*); *Gera*, because he dwelt in exile like a lodger (*ger be'achsanyot*); *Naaman*, because he was exceedingly pleasant; *Achi v'Rosh*, my brother who was also my head, my elder; *Mupim v'Chupim* — I did not see his wedding (*chupah*) and he did not see mine. Rashi adds that *Mupim* also alludes to *piv k'fi Yaakov Avinu*, the teaching he received from Shem and Ever; and *Ard* — who descended among the nations, or according to another opinion, whose face resembled a rose (*vered*). What is striking is the intimacy of the naming: Binyamin, who was perhaps ten years old when Yosef was sold, named every one of his children as an expression of that unrelenting grief and longing.

## Joseph in Egypt

### Pharaoh's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ פַּרְעֹה לְיוֹסֵף ״וּבִלְעָדֶיךָ לֹא יָרִים אִישׁ אֶת יָדוֹ וְגוֹ׳״, אָמְרוּ אִיצְטַגְנִינֵי פַרְעֹה: עֶבֶד שֶׁלְּקָחוֹ רַבּוֹ בְּעֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף תַּמְשִׁילֵהוּ עָלֵינוּ? אָמַר לָהֶן: גִּנּוּנֵי מַלְכוּת אֲנִי רוֹאֶה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When Pharaoh said to Joseph: "And without you no man shall lift up his hand** or his foot in all the land of Egypt" (Genesis 41:44), **Pharaoh's astrologers said: You will appoint a slave whose master bought him for twenty silver** coins **to rule over us? He said to them: I perceive royal characteristics [*ginnunei malkhut*] in him** and see that he was not initially a slave.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: when Pharaoh declared to Yosef, *uviladecha lo yarim ish et yado* — no man shall lift a hand without your authority — the astrologers of Pharaoh objected. *Eved shelekachato b'esrim kesef* — a slave purchased for twenty pieces of silver, you would set him over us? Pharaoh responded that he perceived in Yosef the *ginunei malchut* — the qualities of royalty — citing wisdom, strength, and beauty. The astrologers countered: if this is to be so, then let him demonstrate fitness for such office by knowing all seventy languages, as befits a man of truly royal upbringing.

### Gabriel & Languages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, יְהֵא יוֹדֵעַ בְּשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן! בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וְלִימְּדוֹ שִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן. לָא הֲוָה קָגָמַר. הוֹסִיף לוֹ אוֹת אַחַת מִשְּׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְלָמַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵדוּת בִּיהוֹסֵף שָׂמוֹ בְּצֵאתוֹ עַל אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם שְׂפַת לֹא יָדַעְתִּי אֶשְׁמָע״. וּלְמָחָר, כֹּל לִישָּׁנָא דְּאִישְׁתַּעִי פַּרְעֹה בַּהֲדֵיהּ, אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: If** that is **so** and he is a child of royalty, **he should know** the **seventy languages** that all kings' children learn. The angel **Gabriel** then **came and taught him** the **seventy languages,** but **he could not learn** all of them. Gabriel then **added one letter,** the letter *heh*, to Joseph's name **from the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He, and** then **he** was able to **learn** the languages, **as it is stated: "He appointed it in Joseph [*Yehosef*] for a testimony, when he went forth against the land of Egypt, the speech of one that I did not know I heard"** (Psalms 81:6). **And the next day,** when he appeared before Pharaoh, in **every language that Pharaoh spoke with him, he answered him.**

*V'ata Gavriel v'limdo shivim lashon* — the angel Gavriel came and taught Yosef the seventy languages. There is something deliberately inverted about this in relation to the story of Moshe, where Gavriel moved the infant's hand from gold to coals, resulting in Moshe's becoming *kvad lashon* — heavy of speech. Here the movement is in the opposite direction, opening Yosef's linguistic capacity rather than constraining it. Yet even with Gavriel's instruction, Yosef could not master the learning: *lo hava ka gamir*. At that point, *hosifu lo ot achat mishmo* — God added one letter of His own name to Yosef's name, transforming it into *Yehosef*, as the verse states: *eidut bi'Yehosef samo b'tzeito al eretz Mitzrayim*, with the completion of the verse *sfat lo yadati eshma* — a language I did not know I now hear. The letter unlocked what instruction alone could not.

This detail connects back to the theme of *kiddush shem shamayim b'seter*: the reward for Yosef's hidden sanctification of God's name is precisely this hidden gift — *ot achat mishmo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — one letter from God's own name. And it is worth noting the contrast this creates with Pharaoh, who presented himself to the world as a divine figure: the one who truly carries that *element* of divinity is Yosef, through the letter of God's name embedded in his own.

### Hebrew Language Secret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתַּעִי אִיהוּ בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ, לָא הֲוָה קָא יָדַע מַאי הֲוָה אָמַר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַגְמַרִי. אַגְמְרֵיהּ וְלָא גְּמַר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתְּבַע לִי דְּלָא מְגַלֵּית. אִישְׁתְּבַע לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Joseph then **spoke in the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, and Pharaoh **did not know what he was saying.** Pharaoh **said to him: Teach me** that language. **He taught him, but he** could **not learn** it. Pharaoh **said to him: Take an oath for my** benefit **that you will not reveal** that I do not know this language. **He took an oath for his** benefit.

The next day, whatever language Pharaoh tried, Yosef responded in kind — the test was passed. Then Yosef spoke to Pharaoh in *lashon hakodesh*, in Hebrew, and Pharaoh did not understand. Pharaoh asked Yosef to teach him this language. Yosef tried, but Pharaoh could not learn it — Gavriel was not there for him. Pharaoh then asked Yosef to swear that he would never reveal to anyone that there existed one language Pharaoh did not know, since a king who was worshipped as a god could not be known to lack mastery of any tongue. *V'ishtaba lei* — and Yosef swore.

### Oath Annulment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״אָבִי הִשְׁבִּיעַנִי לֵאמֹר״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אִיתְּשִׁיל אַשְּׁבוּעֲתָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאִיתְּשִׁיל נָמֵי אַדִּידָךְ. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עֲלֵה וּקְבֹר אֶת אָבִיךָ כַּאֲשֶׁר הִשְׁבִּיעֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Years later, **when** Joseph **said to** Pharaoh: **"My father made me swear, saying"** (Genesis 50:5) that I would bury him in Eretz Yisrael, Pharaoh **said to him: Go request** the dissolution **of your oath.** Joseph **said to him: And should I also request** dissolution **for** the oath that I took **for your** benefit? **And** consequently, **even though** Pharaoh **was not amenable** to letting Joseph go, he worried that Joseph would then request dissolution for the oath that he had taken for his benefit, and Pharaoh therefore **said to him: "Go up and bury your father according to what he made you swear"** (Genesis 50:6).

The relevance of this oath surfaces later, at the end of Yaakov's life, when Yosef tells Pharaoh: *avi hishpiani leimor* — my father made me swear to bury him in *Kena'an*. Pharaoh replied: *zil tishol ashvuatcha* — go and seek release from your oath. Yosef responded: *v'eishul nami ashvuatach* — if I seek release from my oath regarding my father, I will also seek release from the oath I made to you, the oath to keep your linguistic secret. And though Pharaoh was displeased, he had no choice: *alei ukvor et avicha ka'asher hishbi'echa* — go, bury your father as he made you swear. The Midrash reads *ka'asher hishbi'echa* as indicating that Pharaoh's acquiescence was entirely conditioned on the weight of the oath, and the passage subtly explains why Pharaoh honored it at all.

Tosafot raises a sharp halachic question on the phrase *v'eishul nami ashvuatach*. One who swears an oath to another person cannot seek *hatarat nedarim* — annulment of that vow — without that person's knowledge, or at least without some form of acquiescence, analogous to the episode of Tzidkiyahu and Nevuchadnetzar. Pharaoh never agreed to Yosef's annulling the oath made to him; he only agreed to release Yosef from his father's oath. So how could Yosef even threaten to do so?

Tosafot works through several possibilities before concluding: *veshema okminehu adigeizum* — perhaps Yosef was simply bluffing. He threatened something he in fact could not halachically have done, but Pharaoh did not know that. *Lo haya yachol lehishael alav velo nicha lei lePharaoh* — Yosef truly could not have obtained release from that oath, and Pharaoh was not happy about the situation, but Pharaoh did not know Yosef lacked the means to carry through. So Yosef's leverage was a bluff — and it worked.

## Judah's Leadership

### Benjamin Jumps First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּשֶׁעָמְדוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל הַיָּם הָיוּ שְׁבָטִים מְנַצְּחִים זֶה עִם זֶה, זֶה אוֹמֵר: אֲנִי יוֹרֵד תְּחִלָּה לַיָּם, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: אֲנִי יוֹרֵד תְּחִלָּה לַיָּם, קָפַץ שִׁבְטוֹ שֶׁל בִּנְיָמִין וְיָרַד לַיָּם תְּחִילָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שָׁם בִּנְיָמִין צָעִיר רֹדֵם״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״רֹדֵם״ אֶלָּא ״רָד יָם״. וְהָיוּ שָׂרֵי יְהוּדָה רוֹגְמִים אוֹתָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שָׂרֵי יְהוּדָה רִגְמָתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: When the Jewish people stood at the** Red **Sea, the tribes were arguing with one other. This one was saying: I am going into the sea first, and that one was saying: I am going into the sea first.** Then, in **jumped the tribe of Benjamin and descended into the sea first, as it is stated: "There is Benjamin, the youngest, ruling them [*rodem*]"** (Psalms 68:28). **Do not read** it as: "**Ruling them [*rodem*]"; rather,** read it as: **Descending [*red*]** into the **sea** **[*yam*]. And the princes of** the tribe of **Judah were stoning them [*rogmim otam*]** for plunging in first and not in the proper order, **as it is stated** in the continuation of the verse: **"The princes of Judah, their council [*rigmatam*]"** (Psalms 68:28).

The Gemara turns to the tribe of Yehudah and its *kiddush shem shamayim b'rabbim* at *Kriat Yam Suf*. A *tanna* teaches in Rabbi Meir's name: when the Jewish people stood at the sea, the tribes competed with one another — *zeh omer ani yored techilah layam, vezeh omer ani yored techilah layam* — each declaring that it would descend first. While they argued, *kafatz shivto shel Binyamin veyarad layam techilah* — the tribe of Binyamin leaped and went down into the sea first. The proof text is *sham Binyamin tzair verodem*, which is read: *al tikri verodem* — do not read "rules them" — *ela rad yam* — but "went down into the sea."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ זָכָה בִּנְיָמִין הַצַּדִּיק וְנַעֲשָׂה אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָן לַגְּבוּרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבֵין כְּתֵפָיו שָׁכֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore, Benjamin the righteous was privileged to serve as host to** the Divine Presence of **the Almighty,** as the Temple was built in the territory of Benjamin, **as it is stated** in Moses' blessing for the tribe of Benjamin: "The beloved of the Lord shall dwell in safety by Him; He covers him all the day, **and He rests between his shoulders"** (Deuteronomy 33:12).

The response of Yehudah was not gracious: *vehayu sarei Yehudah rogmem otam* — the princes of Yehudah were stoning them, as the verse states *sarei Yehudah rigmatam*. The implication, though not spelled out explicitly in the Gemara, is that Yehudah felt this was their prerogative, stolen from them by Binyamin's initiative. *Lefichach* — therefore — *zacha Binyamin hatzaddik venaaseh ushpizin lagvura* — Binyamin merited to become the host of the Divine Presence, as the verse promises: *uvein ktefav shachan* — between his shoulders it dwelt. This connects to the well-known geographical-theological point that the *Kodesh Kodashim* fell within Binyamin's *chelek*, even as the broader *Har Habayit* fell within Yehudah's — two tribes of *Bnei Rachel* and *Bnei Leah* standing right at the threshold of the Divine dwelling.

Tosafot cites a *mashal* from the *Tosefta* or *Mechilta* that frames this somewhat differently. A king had two sons; he told the younger to wake him at sunrise, and the older at the third hour. The younger came to wake the king at sunrise, but the older prevented him, insisting the time was wrong. While they argued, the king rose on his own and said: *mah banai l'kape'ach s'charchem* — I will not deprive you of your reward. Binyamin's reward for going first was *Shechinah b'chelko* — the Divine Presence resting in his portion. Yehudah's reward for its role — for the leadership it embodied even in the dispute — was *malchut*, as the verse implies: *ein rad ela malchut*. This version is notably more generous to Yehudah: both tribes were doing something they understood to be right, and each received a corresponding reward.

Yet the Gemara's version is harder on Yehudah. The stoning of Binyamin's tribe hardly sounds like an act of leadership. One response to this tension is that the *Tosefta* version reframes the dispute as a principled disagreement about *timing* — Yehudah believed the moment had not yet come — whereas the Gemara's account suggests a competition over privilege. The *Kotzker Rebbe* is said to have reconciled the two opinions: during the theoretical argument, everyone was talking; when the actual moment of action arrived, Binyamin moved — and *lo palgi ahadedi*, the two accounts do not truly contradict.

### Nachshon's Faith

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, אֶלָּא זֶה אוֹמֵר: אֵין אֲנִי יוֹרֵד תְּחִילָּה לַיָּם, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: אֵין אֲנִי יוֹרֵד תְּחִילָּה לַיָּם, קָפַץ נַחְשׁוֹן בֶּן עַמִּינָדָב וְיָרַד לַיָּם תְּחִילָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״סְבָבֻנִי בְכַחַשׁ אֶפְרַיִם וּבְמִרְמָה בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל וִיהוּדָה עֹד רָד עִם אֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said** to Rabbi Meir: **That is not how the incident** took place. **Rather, this tribe said: I am not going into the sea first, and that** tribe **said: I am not going into the sea first.** Then, in **jumped** the prince of Judah, **Nahshon ben Amminadab, and descended into the sea first,** accompanied by his entire tribe, **as it is stated: "Ephraim surrounds Me with lies and the house of Israel with deceit, and Judah is yet wayward toward God [*rad im El*]"** (Hosea 12:1), which is interpreted homiletically as: And Judah descended [*rad*] with God [*im El*].

Rabbi Yehudah offers a different version entirely: *lo kach hayah ma'aseh* — that is not how it happened. In Rabbi Yehudah's account, *kafatz Nachshon ben Aminadav veyarad layam techilah* — Nachshon ben Aminadav leaped first into the sea. The verse *v'Yehudah od rad im El* is read as "Yehudah went down with God," or on account of his faith in God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָלָיו מְפֹרָשׁ בַּקַּבָּלָה: ״הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי אֱלֹהִים כִּי בָאוּ מַיִם עַד נָפֶשׁ ... טָבַעְתִּי בִּיוֵן מְצוּלָה וְאֵין מׇעֳמָד וְגוֹ׳״, ״אַל תִּשְׁטְפֵנִי שִׁבֹּלֶת מַיִם וְאַל תִּבְלָעֵנִי מְצוּלָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And in this regard, the tradition,** i.e., the Writings, **explicates** Nahshon's prayer at that moment: **"Save me, God; for the waters are come in even unto the soul. I am sunk in deep mire, where there is no standing…let not the water flood overwhelm me, neither let the deep swallow me up"** (Psalms 69:2–3, 16).

And the verse cited immediately afterwards — *hoshieuni Elokim ki va'u mayim ad nafesh* — "save me, God, for the waters have reached my soul" — makes clear that the sea did not split immediately. Nachshon had gone in, and the waters were at his throat. *Tavati b'yevein metzulah v'ein ma'amad* — I have sunk into the deep mire and there is no footing. This is the Gemara's way of squaring the famous narrative question: who split the sea — Nachshon or Moshe? The answer given here is that both acts occurred, but sequentially, and with a frightening interval between them.

### Nachshon Drowning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה הָיָה מֹשֶׁה מַאֲרִיךְ בִּתְפִלָּה, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: יְדִידַיי טוֹבְעִים בַּיָּם וְאַתָּה מַאֲרִיךְ בִּתְפִלָּה לְפָנַי?! אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! וּמָה בְּיָדִי לַעֲשׂוֹת? אָמַר לוֹ: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִסָּעוּ. וְאַתָּה הָרֵם אֶת מַטְּךָ וּנְטֵה אֶת יָדְךָ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **At that time, Moses was prolonging** his **prayer. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: My beloved ones are drowning in the sea and you prolong your prayer to me?** Moses **said before Him: Master of the Universe, but what can I do?** God **said to him: "Speak to the children of Israel that they go forward. And you, lift up your rod and stretch out your hand"** (Exodus 14:15–16).

While Nachshon was drowning, *hayah Moshe maarich bitfilah* — Moshe was prolonging his prayer. God responded: *yedidai tov'im bayam* — My beloved ones are drowning in the sea — interestingly in the plural, perhaps echoing the famous Midrash that God's own handiwork was drowning: *ma'aseh yadai tov'im bayam v'atem omrim shirah*. God says to Moshe: *v'atah maarich bitfilah l'fanai?* — and you are prolonging your prayer before Me? Moshe replied: *Ribbono Shel Olam, u'mah b'yadi la'asot?* — Master of the Universe, what else should I do? And God's answer: *daber el Bnei Yisrael veyisau* — speak to the children of Israel and let them go forward — and then lift your staff.

This is an extraordinarily powerful moment that is not always appreciated in its full depth. Moshe was doing the entirely natural, even obligatory, thing: he was praying, awaiting divine instruction. And God essentially says: stop. There is a time for prayer, a time to wait for instruction — and a time to simply *know what you have to do and do it*. Nachshon embodied that third mode. He did not wait for the sea to split. He did not wait for instruction. He understood, at some deep level, that if this is the situation, this is what God wants — and he leaped.

This also illuminates the famous verse after the crossing: *vaya'aminu b'Hashem uvMoshe avdo* — and they believed in God and in Moshe His servant. If they had seen all the plagues and wonders, what does it mean that only now did they believe? The answer is that *emunah* here is not abstract theological belief, not assent to God's existence. It is live, active, embodied faith — the kind of faith that acts before it sees, that jumps into the water before the water parts. Nachshon demonstrated that faith before anyone else.

### Judah's Kingdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ, זָכָה יְהוּדָה לַעֲשׂוֹת מֶמְשָׁלָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָיְתָה יְהוּדָה לְקׇדְשׁוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל מַמְשְׁלוֹתָיו״, מָה טַעַם ״הָיְתָה יְהוּדָה לְקׇדְשׁוֹ וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מַמְשְׁלוֹתָיו״ — מִשּׁוּם דְּ״הַיָּם רָאָה וַיָּנֹס״.
>
> **Translation:** **For this reason,** because Nahshon and the tribe of Judah went into the sea first, the tribe of **Judah merited to govern Israel, as it is stated: "Judah became His sanctuary, Israel His dominion.** The sea saw it and fled" (Psalms 114:2–3). The *baraita* interprets the verses in this manner: **What is the reason** that **Judah became His sanctuary and Israel** came under **His dominion?** It is **because "the sea saw it and fled."**

*Vezachu Yehudah la'asot memshalah b'Yisrael* — Yehudah merited to exercise dominion in Israel. The verse: *haysah Yehudah l'kodsho, Yisrael mamshelosav* — Yehudah was for God's holy one, Israel for His dominion. And the reason: *mishum dehayam ra'ah vayanos* — because the sea saw and fled, because of the actions of Yehudah. It was the act of *Nachshon* — and the tribe that followed — which, together with Moshe's subsequent lifting of his staff, brought about the splitting of the sea.

It is worth noting the contrast between Yosef and Yehudah that runs beneath the surface here. Yosef operates throughout in a mode of *histapkut* — trusting that God is working through events, saying to his brothers *al te'atzvu*, do not be distressed, it is all God's doing. This is admirable from a theological standpoint, but it is not the posture of a leader. Yehudah, by contrast, says *anochi e'ervenu* — I take personal responsibility. The reward for Binyamin's leap was *Shechinah* — God's Presence. The reward for Yehudah's leadership was *memshalah* — dominion, kingship. Faith and leadership are not identical, and both have their distinct expressions and rewards.

## Gerizim & Eival

### Levi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר לֵוִי לְמַטָּה, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר לְמַעְלָה. וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר לְמַעְלָה, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discussing the blessing and curses. **It is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:9) that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: It is impossible to say** that the tribe of **Levi** stood **below,** between the two mountains, **as it is already stated** that they were **above,** in the verse: "These shall stand on Mount Gerizim to bless the people when you have passed over the Jordan: Simeon and Levi and Judah" (Deuteronomy 27:12). **And it is impossible to say** that they stood **above** on the mountain **because it is already stated:** "And all of Israel, and their elders and officers, and their judges, stood on this side of the Ark and on that side before the priests the Levites" (Joshua 8:33). This shows that the Levites stood **below,** between the mountains, with the Ark.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov opens a new problem: *iy efshar lomar Levi lemattah* — it is impossible to say that Levi was below, at the foot of the mountains, because the Torah's own division places Levi on *Har Gerizim*. But neither can one say they were above on the mountains, because the account in *Yehoshua* places the *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* in the center, with the *Aron*, below. How to reconcile?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? זִקְנֵי כְּהוּנָּה וּלְוִיָּה לְמַטָּה, וְהַשְּׁאָר לְמַעְלָה. רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְשָׁרֵת לְמַטָּה, וְהַשְּׁאָר לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How** is **this** possible? Only **the Elders of the priesthood and the Levites** stood **below, and the rest** of the Levites stood **above** on the mountain. **Rabbi Yoshiya says: Any** Levite **who was fit to serve** in the Temple stood **below,** between the mountains, **and the rest** of the tribe, who were too young or too old to serve in the Temple, stood **above** on the mountain.

The Gemara's answer: *ziknei kehunah v'leviya lemattah* — the elders of the *kohanim* and *Levi'im* were below with the *Aron*, while the remaining members of the tribe were above on the mountain, as the Torah prescribes.

Rabbi Yoshiya offers an alternative: everyone fit for *avodah* — those between the ages of 30 and 50, the active years of Levitical service — was below, while the rest were above.

### All Below Mountains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ לְמַטָּה הֵן עוֹמְדִים, הָפְכוּ פְּנֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי הַר גְּרִיזִים וּפָתְחוּ בִּבְרָכָה, כְּלַפֵּי הַר עֵיבָל וּפָתְחוּ בִּקְלָלָה. מַאי ״עַל״ — עַל בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Both** the Levites **and** the Israelites **were standing below. They turned to face Mount Gerizim and opened with a blessing,** and then they turned **toward Mount Ebal and opened with a curse.** Therefore, **what** is the meaning of the verse: "These shall stand **on [*al*]** Mount Gerizim to bless the people" (Deuteronomy 27:12)? **"*Al*"** means **adjacent to** the mountain but not actually on the mountain itself.

Rebbi disagrees with both: *elu v'elu lemattah* — both groups, all the tribes, were below. This resolves a different textual tension. The Torah says *eleh ya'amdu al Har Gerizim*, which sounds like they stood *on* the mountain. The account in *Yehoshua* says *mul Har Gerizim* — facing, or at the foot of, Har Gerizim. Rebbi reads *al* not as "upon" but as "near," *besamuch*, and marshals a parallel: the verse *venasata al hamareches levonah zakah* — you shall place the frankincense *on* the arrangement of the *lechem hapanim* — cannot mean literally atop the bread, as the incense sat in the space between the two stacks. So too *al* in this context means adjacent, not literally upon. Just as Rebbi in a separate *braita* rules *al besamuch* regarding the frankincense of the *lechem hapanim* — deriving this from the verse that the *parochet* shades *over* the *Aron* without literally resting on it — so too here: everyone stood *near* the mountains, not on them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַתָּ עַל הַמַּעֲרֶכֶת לְבוֹנָה זַכָּה״, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״עַל״ — בְּסָמוּךְ. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא עַל מַמָּשׁ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְסַכֹּתָ עַל הָאָרֹן״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that discusses the shewbread: **"And you shall put pure frankincense on [*al*] each row"** (Leviticus 24:7). **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: "*Al*"** in this instance means **adjacent to.** Do **you say** that **"*al*"** means **adjacent to, or** perhaps it carries **only** its **literal** meaning of **"on"? When it says** in the verse: **"And you shall screen the Ark [*al haAron*]** with the curtain" (Exodus 40:3), the word "*al*" cannot mean on, as the curtain that separated the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies was not placed on top of the Ark, but near it. Therefore, **you must say** that **"*al*"** means **adjacent to.**

The image that results is of the entire people gathered in the valley between the two mountains, all below, all present, all participants.

### Facing Mountains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָפְכוּ פְּנֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי הַר גְּרִיזִים וּפָתְחוּ בִּבְרָכָה, כְּלַפֵּי הַר עֵיבָל וּפָתְחוּ בִּקְלָלָה. מַאי ״עַל״ — עַל בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** They **turned to face Mount Gerizim and opened with a blessing,** and then they turned **toward Mount Ebal and opened with a curse.**

*Hafchu pneichem klapei Har Gerizim* — they all turned to face Har Gerizim — *upetchu b'bracha* — and they opened with the blessing. Then they turned toward *Har Eival* and opened with the *klala*. The choreography is total participation: no one stands apart while others recite; the entire people face the mountain of blessing and then face the mountain of curse. Every voice is included. The Torah's language confirms this: *v'anu kol ha-am v'amru amen* — all the people answered and said *amen*. One might feel that the original image — tribes positioned on visually distinct mountains, one lush and abundant, one barren — carries a certain dramatic power. But what this arrangement achieves is that every person owns every blessing and every curse, and no one is merely an observer.

## Covenant Framework

### Three Covenants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בָּרוּךְ״ בִּכְלָל, ״בָּרוּךְ״ בִּפְרָט. ״אָרוּר״ בִּכְלָל, ״אָרוּר״ בִּפְרָט, לִלְמוֹד וּלְלַמֵּד לִשְׁמוֹר וְלַעֲשׂוֹת, הֲרֵי אַרְבַּע, אַרְבַּע וְאַרְבַּע הֲרֵי שְׁמוֹנֶה, שְׁמוֹנֶה וּשְׁמוֹנֶה הֲרֵי שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה. וְכֵן בְּסִינַי, וְכֵן בְּעַרְבוֹת מוֹאָב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵלֶּה דִבְרֵי הַבְּרִית אֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳ אֶת מֹשֶׁה וְגוֹ׳, וּכְתִיב: ״וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת דִּבְרֵי הַבְּרִית הַזֹּאת וְגוֹ׳״. נִמְצָא אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה בְּרִיתוֹת עַל כׇּל מִצְוָה וּמִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 8:10): The blessings and curses include **a general blessing** for one who fulfills the entire Torah, and **a particular blessing** for each individual statement mentioned in the blessings and curses. Likewise, there is **a general curse** for one who does not fulfill the entire Torah and **a particular curse** for each individual statement. And for each of the blessings and curses there is a mitzva **to learn and to teach,** and **to keep and to perform. Consequently,** every mitzva contains **four** aspects. **Four** general aspects **and four** specific aspects add up to **eight. Eight** blessings **and eight** curses add up to **sixteen. And so too at** Mount **Sinai, and so too at the plains of Moab, as it is stated: "These are the words of the covenant that the Lord commanded Moses** to make with the children of Israel in the land of Moab, besides the covenant that He made with them in Horeb" (Deuteronomy 28:69). **And it is written: "Observe therefore the words of this covenant"** (Deuteronomy 29:8). **It follows** that between the three events where sixteen covenants were made, God established **forty-eight covenants for each and every mitzva.**

The Gemara now counts: *baruch bikhlal baruch biprat, arur bikhlal arur biprat* — there is a general blessing and a specific blessing, a general curse and a specific curse. Beyond that, each *mitzvah* has four dimensions: *lilmod u'lelamed lishmor v'la'asot* — to learn, to teach, to observe, and to do. For each of those four dimensions there is a corresponding *baruch*, and for each a corresponding *arur*. That is four plus four equals eight; eight blessings and eight curses equals sixteen dimensions per *mitzvah* — sixteen *britot*. And this was enacted three times: at *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*, at *Sinai*, and at *Arvot Moav*. Three times sixteen yields forty-eight *britot* — forty-eight covenantal dimensions — for every single *mitzvah* in the Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹצִיא הַר גְּרִיזִים וְהַר עֵיבָל, וּמַכְנִיס אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד שֶׁבַּמִּדְבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon excludes Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal** from this list because only some of the mitzvot were mentioned there, **and he includes** instead the covenant at **the Tent of Meeting in the desert.**

Rabbi Shimon, however, disputes whether *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* constituted one of the three *brit*-moments at all. His alternative three loci are *Har Sinai*, *Ohel Moed*, and *Arvot Moav*. His reasoning reflects a conceptual reframing: we have shifted from counting *brachot* and *klalot* to speaking of *brit* — covenant. And when the question is where the covenant was established, *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* are a ceremony of *baruch* and *arur*, not the *ikar brit*. The covenant proper is sealed at *Sinai*, elaborated at *Ohel Moed*, and renewed at *Arvot Moav*.

### Generalities & Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: כְּלָלוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּסִינַי וּפְרָטוֹת בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּלָלוֹת וּפְרָטוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּסִינַי, וְנִשְׁנוּ בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְנִשְׁתַּלְּשׁוּ בְּעַרְבוֹת מוֹאָב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is **in the dispute** between **these *tanna'im*** that they disagree, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:11): **Rabbi Yishmael says: General statements were said at Sinai,** i.e., Moses received general mitzvot at Sinai, including the Ten Commandments. **And** the **details** of the mitzvot were explained to Moses at a later time **in the Tent of Meeting. Rabbi Akiva says:** Both **general statements and** the **details** of mitzvot **were said at Sinai, and** later **repeated in the Tent of Meeting, and** reiterated **a third time** by Moses to the Jewish people **in the plains of Moab.** Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with his teacher, Rabbi Akiva, and counts Mount Sinai and the Tent of Meeting as two distinct places where all of the mitzvot were given.

This dispute maps onto a deeper *tannaitic* disagreement. Rabbi Yishmael holds: *klalot ne'emru b'Sinai, pratim b'Ohel Moed* — the general principles were stated at Sinai, the specific details at the Tent of Meeting. Rashi illustrates: the verse *vezavachta alav et olotecha ve'et shelamecha* — you shall offer your burnt offerings and peace offerings upon it — was stated in the context of Sinai, but it gives no detail about *matan damim*, the application of the blood, or *haktarat emurin*, or *hafshat v'nituach*. All of those details appear only in *Sefer Vayikra*, which is the Torah of *Ohel Moed*. On this reading, everything in the Torah from *Vayikra* onward is the *prat* of what was sketched at *Sinai*. This also implies a natural reading of what *Sinai* encompasses: it need not be limited to the *Aseret HaDibrot* alone, but may extend through *parshat Mishpatim*, where Moshe descended, read the Torah to the people, and sealed the *Sefer HaBrit* — the covenant scroll — at the end of *Mishpatim*. That is the natural terminus of the *Sinai* revelation as a legal-covenantal unit, and including *Mishpatim* makes it considerably more plausible to view it as the repository of *klalot* from which all subsequent detail is drawn.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *klalot u'pratot ne'emru b'Sinai* — both generalities and specifics were stated at Sinai. *V'nishnu b'Ohel Moed* — and they were repeated at the Tent of Meeting. *V'nishtalshu b'Arvot Moav* — and repeated a third time at Arvot Moav. On this view there are three fully parallel transmissions of the entire Torah, each in its own setting and context.

This is a dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva that extends well beyond the immediate question of covenant-locations. Rabbi Yishmael's famous principle is *dibra Torah kilshon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in human language, in accessible, contextually organic terms — and his approach to hermeneutics tends toward restraint, not pressing every detail to yield independent legal force. Rabbi Akiva, who *darshens* every letter, would naturally hold that even the details were already present at *Sinai* in their fullness — nothing was communicated in a lesser or more mediated mode. The question of how many times the Torah was transmitted, and whether details were already embedded in the original revelation or unfolded progressively, appears to track these broader commitments. This axis is, of course, a central concern of Heschel's *Torah Min Hashamayim*.

### 48 Covenants Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לָךְ כׇּל דְּבַר מִצְוָה וּמִצְוָה שֶׁכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא נִכְרְתוּ עָלֶיהָ אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה בְּרִיתוֹת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵין לְךָ מִצְוָה וּמִצְוָה שֶׁכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא נִכְרְתוּ עָלֶיהָ אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה בְּרִיתוֹת שֶׁל שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת אֶלֶף וּשְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים וַחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת וַחֲמִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And there is no mitzva written in the Torah for which forty-eight covenants were not established. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda Ish Kefar Akko said in the name of Rabbi Shimon: There is no mitzva written in the Torah for which forty-eight covenants were not established 603,550 times,** corresponding to the population of the Jewish people in the desert. This is because each member of the Jewish people received the covenant both for himself and as a guarantor for the rest of the Jewish people.

The mathematical upshot: once we have 48 *britot* per *mitzvah* (16 × 3), and this applies to every individual *mitzvah* in the Torah, we have 48 *britot* per *mitzvah* multiplied across 613 *mitzvot*. But Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda of Kfar Akko, citing Rabbi Shimon, goes further: *ein lecha mitzvah u'mitzvah she'ktuvah baTorah shelo nichratu aleha arbaim u'shmonah britot shel shesh me'ot elef u'shloshet alafim v'chamesh me'ot v'chamishim* — for every single *mitzvah*, the 48 *britot* are covenanted not just once but 603,550 times — the number of adult men counted in the census. For every *mitzvah*, each individual Israelite carries 48 covenantal obligations, and there were 603,550 individuals. The Gemara then notes: for every individual Israelite, the total number of covenantal obligations equals 603,550.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אֵין לְךָ כׇּל מִצְוָה וּמִצְוָה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא נִכְרְתוּ עָלֶיהָ אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה בְּרִיתוֹת שֶׁל שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת אֶלֶף וּשְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים וַחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת וַחֲמִשִּׁים. נִמְצָא לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת אֶלֶף וּשְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים וַחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת וַחֲמִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: According to the statement of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda Ish Kefar Akko, who spoke in the name of Rabbi Shimon, there is no mitzva in the Torah for which forty-eight covenants were not established 603,550 times; it follows** that **for every one of the Jewish people** there were **603,550** covenants.

### Mutual Guarantee

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: עָרְבָא וְעָרְבָא דְעָרְבָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the statements of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda Ish Kefar Akko and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi? What does the statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi add? **Rav Mesharshiyya said:** The matter of **a guarantor and a guarantor for a guarantor** is the difference **between them.** According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, every Jew is not only rendered a guarantor for every other Jew, but he is also rendered a guarantor for every other Jew's responsibility as a guarantor. Therefore, according to his calculation, the number of covenants is multiplied again by 603,550.

Rav Mesharshaya explains the distinction between the two positions: *arva d'arva ika beinaihu* — the second position adds the dimension of *arvut* upon *arvut* — guarantee upon guarantee. On the first reading, one might simply be multiplying the total *britot* across the total population, an arithmetic aggregation. But Rav Mesharshaya's point is different: each individual does not merely have 48 *britot* for each *mitzvah*. Each individual is also an *arev* — a guarantor — for every other individual's 48 *britot*. So for every *mitzvah*, each person carries 48 obligations on their own account, plus responsibility for 603,549 others, each carrying their own 48. The numbers become astronomical.

And one can extend the logic: I am not only responsible for your keeping your 48 *britot*, I am also responsible for your discharging your *arvut* obligations toward everyone else. That level of *arvut d'arvut* makes the total obligation truly exponential — and Rav Mesharshaya appears to intend exactly that, which is why he adds this observation rather than simply restating what came before. Tosafot works through some of the arithmetic, though by that point the numbers have grown well beyond the scope of a brief calculation.

## Curses & Adultery

### Adultery Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן נַחְמָנִי מְתוּרְגְּמָנֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ לֹא נֶאֶמְרָה אֶלָּא בְּנוֹאֵף וְנוֹאֶפֶת,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Naḥmani, the disseminator of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, taught: The entire passage** of the blessings and curses **is stated only** in reference **to an adulterer and adulteress.**

Rabbi Yehudah ben Nechemia, the *meturgamana* — the public announcer — of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, offered a striking interpretation: *kol haparsha kula lo ne'emra ela b'mna'ef u'mna'efet* — the entire section of *brachot* and *klalos* was stated only with reference to an adulterer and adulteress. This is a striking return to the *Sotah* theme, and it is notable that the formulation includes the male sinner, not only the woman. The *parasha* is not, on this reading, a general moral catalogue — it is a unified statement about the consequences of sexual betrayal within marriage.

### Curses Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָרוּר הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה פֶסֶל וּמַסֵּכָה וְגוֹ׳״, בְּ״אָרוּר״ סַגִּי לֵיהּ? אֶלָּא: זֶה הַבָּא עַל הָעֶרְוָה וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן, וְהָלַךְ לְבֵין אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם וְעָבַד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אֲרוּרִין אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ שֶׁל זֶה שֶׁכָּךְ גָּרְמוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This is proved from the verse: **"Cursed is the man who makes a graven or molten image"** (Deuteronomy 27:15). **Is a curse a sufficient** consequence **for** the actions of an idol worshipper? He has rebelled against the fundamental tenet of the Torah. **Rather, this** is referring to **one who engaged in sexual intercourse with a forbidden relative and bore** her a *mamzer* **son. And** the son, who is not allowed to marry a Jew of unflawed lineage, **went** to live **among the** other **nations of the world and engaged in idol worship. His father and mother are cursed for causing him** to worship idols. Likewise, the rest of the curses refer to sins that are the result of adultery.

The Gemara then proceeds to trace every *arur* back to the act of adultery, with varying degrees of directness. *Arur ha'ish asher ya'aseh pesel u'masecha* — the verse curses one who makes an idol. But, the Gemara explains, this means one who committed adultery, produced a *mamzer*, and that child — excluded from *Bnei Yisrael* — went among idol worshippers and worshipped *avoda zara*. The sin of adultery cascades into idolatry: *sheken garmu lo* — for they caused it.

Rashi extends the reading systematically: the adulterer fathers a *mamzer*; the child, shamed and excluded, turns to the nations and their worship. Similarly, *shochev im behemah* is read in the context of the *Sotah* ordeal: *eishes ish she'asa ma'aseh behemah she'mna'efet*. *Mikalel aviv v'imo* — one who curses his father and mother — is the child of an adulterous union, born into a household whose shame brought disgrace on the parents who raised such wrongdoing. *Mashgeh iver* — who misleads the blind — is the seducer of a married woman: *m'fateh eshet ish*, blinding her to the weight of the sin she is being drawn into, since *she'isuma badavar v'eino yodei'a onsho kamohu* — she is blinded in the matter and does not fully perceive the punishment as he does. *Masig gevul re'ehu* — who removes a neighbor's boundary — is one who takes another man's wife: *ba'al ishto, harei zeh hesig gevul*. *Makeh re'ehu baseter* — who strikes his neighbor in secret — is one who causes the bitter waters of the *Sotah* to work their effect through whispered manipulation. *Lokei'ach shochad l'hakot nefesh* — one who takes a bribe to cause death — is the one who accepts payment to aid the adulterer in seducing a married woman and thereby brings about her death through the *Sotah* ordeal.

The final verse — *mateh mishpat ger yatom* — the Gemara concedes: *eini yodei'a lidrosh* — I do not know how to interpret it in this framework. The intellectual honesty of that admission is, in its own way, as noteworthy as all the interpretations that preceded it.

## Blessings & Curses

### Alternating Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנָתַתָּ אֶת הַבְּרָכָה עַל הַר גְּרִזִים וְאֶת הַקְּלָלָה וְגוֹ׳״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אִם לְלַמֵּד שֶׁתְּהֵא בְּרָכָה עַל הַר גְּרִזִים וּקְלָלָה עַל הַר עֵיבָל, הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״אֵלֶּה יַעַמְדוּ לְבָרֵךְ אֶת הָעָם עַל הַר גְּרִזִים״, וּכְתִיב ״וְאֵלֶּה יַעַמְדוּ עַל הַקְּלָלָה בְּהַר עֵיבָל״! אֶלָּא לְהַקְדִּים בְּרָכָה לִקְלָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "And you shall give the blessing on Mount Gerizim and the curse** on Mount Ebal" (Deuteronomy 11:29). **Why** must **the verse state** this? **If** it is **to teach that the blessing must be** given **on Mount Gerizim and the curse on Mount Ebal, it is already stated: "These shall stand on Mount Gerizim to bless the people"** (Deuteronomy 27:12), **and it is written: "And these shall stand on Mount Ebal for the curse"** (Deuteronomy 27:13). **Rather,** the verse teaches that the proclamation of the **blessing must precede** the **curse.**

The Mishna states: *Tanu Rabanan*: the verse *venasata et habracha al Har Gerizim ve'et haklala al Har Eival* — why does the Torah need to state this, given that the allocation of tribes to each mountain has already been specified? The answer is: *ela l'hakdim bracha laklala* — to establish that the blessing is pronounced before the curse. And lest one think this means all the *brachot* are recited together before all the *klalos*, the singular language — *bracha v'klala* — indicates: one blessing, then one curse, then the next blessing, then the next curse. They alternate, one by one, individually.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִהְיוּ כׇּל הַבְּרָכוֹת קוֹדְמוֹת לַקְּלָלוֹת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּרָכָה״ וּ״קְלָלָה״ — בְּרָכָה אַחַת קוֹדֶמֶת לִקְלָלָה, וְאֵין כׇּל הַבְּרָכוֹת קוֹדְמוֹת לַקְּלָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **all of the blessings** should **precede the curses.** Therefore, **the verse states "blessing" and "curse"** in the singular, to teach that **one blessing precedes** each **curse, but all of the blessings do not precede the curses.** The blessings and curses were recited alternately, first one blessing and then one curse.

### Seven Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַקִּישׁ בְּרָכָה לִקְלָלָה, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה קְלָלָה בִּלְוִיִּם, אַף בְּרָכָה בִּלְוִיִּם. וּמָה קְלָלָה בְּקוֹל רָם, אַף בְּרָכָה בְּקוֹל רָם. וּמָה קְלָלָה בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, אַף בְּרָכָה בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ. וּמָה קְלָלָה בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט, אַף בְּרָכָה בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט. וּמָה קְלָלָה — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עוֹנִין וְאוֹמְרִים ״אָמֵן״, אַף בְּרָכָה — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עוֹנִין וְאוֹמְרִים ״אָמֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, the verse comes **to juxtapose** the **blessing with** the **curse, to say to you** that **just as** the **curse** is recited **by** the **Levites, so too,** the **blessing** is uttered **by** the **Levites; and just as** the **curse** is proclaimed **loudly, so too,** the **blessing** is proclaimed **loudly; and just as** the **curse** is proclaimed **in the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, **so too,** the **blessing** is proclaimed **in the sacred tongue; and just as** the **curse** is proclaimed both **in general and in detail, so too,** the **blessing is** proclaimed **in general and in detail. And just as** after the **curse** is uttered, **both** groups of people on each mountain **respond and say amen, so too,** after the **blessing** is uttered, **both** groups **respond and say amen.**

The Mishna then enumerates seven structural parallels between the *brachot* and the *klalos*. Each of the following features that characterizes the *klalot* applies equally to the *brachot*: they were pronounced by the *Levi'im* (*ma klalos b'Levi'im af brachot b'Levi'im*); they were said in a loud voice (*ma klala b'kol ram af bracha b'kol ram*), as the phrasing *v'anita v'amarta* implies; they were in *lashon hakodesh* (*ma klala b'lashon hakodesh af bracha b'lashon hakodesh*) — which connects this passage directly to the discussion that opened the tractate; they had both a general and specific form (*ma klala b'klal u'vfrat af bracha b'klal u'vfrat*); and finally, for both, *elu v'elu onim v'omrim amen* — both these and those, all the people together, answered and said *amen*. Not half the people for the blessings and the other half for the curses — everyone said *amen* to both. The structural symmetry is total: in form, in voice, in language, in scope, and in communal response, blessing and curse are mirror images of one another.
