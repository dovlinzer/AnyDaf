# Shabbat 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Avodah Zarah Tumah

### Sheretz vs Niddah

The daf opens at the very top of 83a with the words *v'Rabbi Akiva*, picking up a debate about the *tumah* status of *avodah zarah*. As will be established by the end of today's daf, the *tumah* of *avodah zarah* is entirely *derabbanan*. The *pesukim* invoked throughout this discussion are either drawn from Nach rather than the Torah, or derive from the phrase *shaketz teshaktzenu* — "you shall abominate it" — whose connection to *sheretz* is, at best, a considerable stretch. The rabbinic character of this *tumah* will prove significant for understanding the halachic conclusions reached later.

The central debate is between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Akiva. The *chachamim* hold that *avodah zarah* is comparable to a *sheretz*, while Rabbi Akiva holds it is comparable to a woman who is a *niddah*. These two comparisons determine the nature and scope of its *tumah*.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Masa

The most straightforward reading of this debate — the one advanced by Rabbi Eliezer — is that the dispute concerns *tumat masa*: whether the *tumah* of *avodah zarah* is transmitted through carrying as well as through direct contact. According to this reading, the *chachamim* hold that *avodah zarah* causes *tumah* only through touch, like a *sheretz*, while Rabbi Akiva holds that it causes *tumah* even through carrying, like a *niddah*.

### Rava's Even Mesama

Rava, however, reframes the dispute entirely. He maintains that all opinions agree *avodah zarah* is *metamei* through *masa* — carrying. The debate, in his view, concerns only whether it possesses the additional and highly specialized *tumah* known as *even mesama*. This refers to a scenario in which a boulder of such great weight is placed upon something that the *niddah*'s own weight cannot be felt through it; even so, the object beneath is rendered *tamei*. This is a *tumah* unique to the *niddah* category. According to Rava, the *chachamim* deny *avodah zarah* this extraordinary status, while Rabbi Akiva affirms it.

### Niddah vs Neveilah

The *gemara* now turns to work through the precise function of each scriptural comparison. According to the *chachamim*, the comparison to a *sheretz* teaches that there is no *tumat masa*, and the comparison to a *niddah* teaches that the *tumah* does not apply *l'evarim* — that is, only to an intact idol, not to one broken into component pieces, just as a *niddah*'s *tumah* attaches to her person and not to detached parts of her.

For Rabbi Akiva, the *gemara* asks: *ul'mai hilchata itkesh l'niddah* — why is *avodah zarah* compared specifically to a *niddah*? If the sole purpose is to establish *tumat masa*, one could just as well have compared it to a *neveilah*, since a *neveilah* also conveys *tumah* through carrying. The *niddah* is a far more specialized case. The *gemara* acknowledges this and concludes that the comparison to a *niddah* specifically — rather than to a *neveilah* — must be teaching something beyond *masa*. The inference is that just as a *niddah* is only *metamei* when whole and not when reduced to component parts, so too *avodah zarah* is only *metamei* when intact. This principle of *einah l'evarim* — that it does not apply in component parts — is thus agreed upon by all opinions: both Rabbi Akiva and the *rabbanan*.

### Rami Bar Chama

The *gemara* now preempts a question that will formally arise later in the daf. Rami bar Chama raises a question — to be discussed when we turn the page — about whether *avodah zarah* retains its *tumah* status when disassembled into its component pieces. The *gemara* immediately observes that this question appears to have already been resolved: since we have just established that *einah l'evarim* is agreed upon by both Rabbi Akiva and the *rabbanan*, how can there be any remaining doubt?

The *gemara* answers that Rami bar Chama poses his question according to the approach of Rabba — the opinion presented the previous day — not according to the approach of Rabbi Eliezer that we have been analyzing. The distinction is crucial. According to Rabbi Eliezer's framing, where everyone agrees that the maximum *tumah* of *avodah zarah* is *masa* — no more severe than a *neveilah* — both Rabbi Akiva and the *rabbanan* would have equal reason to draw the *niddah* comparison solely for the teaching of *einah l'evarim*. But according to Rabba, who holds that Rabbi Akiva goes so far as to assign *even masama* status to *avodah zarah*, Rabbi Akiva's rationale for invoking *niddah* is precisely to convey that unique and elevated *tumah*. Under Rabba's reading of Rabbi Akiva, the *niddah* comparison is fully occupied by *even masama*, leaving no room to derive *einah l'evarim* — and thus the question of component-part *tumah* remains genuinely open.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *Rami bar Chama bar Guria k'Rabba matni* — Rami bar Chama teaches according to the approach of Rabba, and poses his question specifically *aliba d'Rabbi Akiva*, according to Rabbi Akiva's position as Rabba reads it. One might add, as the lecturer observes, that while the conceptual structure here does not dramatically expand our understanding, it demonstrates the *gemara*'s careful effort to ensure that every *pasuk* and every comparison does precisely the work assigned to it — not more, not less. The accounting, as it were, must balance perfectly on every side.

## Rav Ashi's Reading

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

The *gemara* now raises a *baraita*: *avodah zarah* is like a *sheretz*, and its appurtenances — *meshamsheha* — are also like a *sheretz*. Rabbi Akiva says: *avodah zarah* is like a *niddah* and its appurtenances are like a *sheretz*. At first glance, the strict *sheretz* versus *niddah* categories appear to map directly onto the debate about *masa* — according to the first opinion, *tumah* only through touch, and according to Rabbi Akiva, at least the *avodah zarah* itself even through carrying. This fits squarely with Rabbi Eliezer's reading.

The *gemara* therefore notes: *l'bishlama d'Rabbi Eliezer nicha* — according to Rabbi Eliezer, this reads naturally. But *ella l'Rabba kashya* — according to Rava, it is difficult. Rava responds: this *baraita* is no stronger a contradiction than the *mishnah* already discussed at the outset of the *sugya*, which stated that wood and stones and dust are *metamei k'sheretz* — also apparently meaning only through touch, not carrying. Yet Rava resolved that *mishnah* by reading "*k'sheretz*" not as a literal equation with a rodent, but as meaning "not entirely like a *niddah*" — that is, lacking only the *tumah* of *even masama*. He applies the same resolution here: *hacha nami d'lo metamei b'even masama* — here too, the comparison to a *sheretz* teaches only the exclusion of *even masama*, not the exclusion of *masa* altogether.

### Masa vs Hesitan

The *gemara* then introduces a new *baraita* that proves to be a sharper challenge: *nochri v'nochrit, avodah zarah u'meshamesheha* — a non-Jewish man and woman, *avodah zarah*, and its appurtenances are *tamei*, but *hein v'lo hesitan* — they are *tamei* but not the moving of them. Rabbi Akiva says: *hein v'hesitan* — they and the moving of them are *tamei*.

The initial reading is that *hesitan* — moving — refers to the *tumah* of being carried, meaning the first opinion holds only touching causes *tumah*, not carrying. This seems to be a direct proof for Rabbi Eliezer against Rava.

Rava, however, argues that the text as read cannot be correct, because the *baraita* groups non-Jews together with *avodah zarah* under the heading "they but not their moving," yet we have a separate principle — derived from the verse *daber el bnei Yisrael*, which limits the *tumah* of *zav* and *zavah* to Jews — that the Rabbis decreed non-Jews should be treated as full *zavim l'chol divreihem*, as complete *zavim* in every respect. If so, it is impossible to say that non-Jews convey *tumah* only through touch and not through being moved, since a *zav* and *zavah* transmit *tumah* in all three modes: touch, being carried, and moving other things through *hesitan*. The text of the *baraita*, as initially read, is therefore untenable.

Since the text cannot be read literally, Rava rewrites the *baraita* according to his own position, and Rabbi Eliezer rewrites it according to his. This is a notable move: both Amoraim take an eraser, as it were, to the *baraita* and reconstruct its *girsa* to accord with their respective understandings.

Here it becomes essential to clarify the distinction between *masa* and *hesitan*, which Rav Ashi will shortly bring to the fore. *Masa* — carrying — is a *tumah* in which the *tamei* object is the subject and is being carried; the person doing the carrying becomes *tamei*. *Hesitan* — moving — describes the reverse: the *tamei* person is the subject who moves a *tahor* object, rendering it *tamei* without direct contact. *Masa* can apply to *tamei* objects — a *neveilah*, for example, conveys *tumat masa*. But *hesitan* is the distinctive *tumah* of those whose *tumah* derives from their personal status — *zav*, *zavah*, *niddah* — not from having contracted *tumah* from elsewhere. When such a person moves something without touching it, the object becomes *tamei*.

### Categories Chart

Rava's rewriting of the *baraita* proceeds as follows. Non-Jewish men and women are treated entirely like *zav* and *zavah*, so they transmit *tumah* in all three modes: *hein* — being carried, *hesitan* — moving something else, and even *even masama* — the boulder case. When it comes to *avodah zarah*, that is where the debate lies. According to the *rabbanan* under Rava's reading: the *avodah zarah* conveys *tumah* through *masa* (*hein*) and through being moved (*hesitan*, meaning here that others move it), but not through *even masama*. According to Rabbi Akiva, *avodah zarah* is like a full *niddah* in all respects, including *even masama*, and therefore *hein v'hesitan v'even masama shela* — all three modes.

Rabbi Eliezer similarly rewrites the *baraita*, but according to his understanding the debate is more fundamental. Non-Jews retain full *zav* status — all three categories. *Avodah zarah*, however, is debated: the first opinion holds only direct contact (*hi* — touching it), not even being carried; Rabbi Akiva holds it and being carried (*hi v'hesitan*), but neither opinion extends to *even masama*.

### Seesaw Example

Rav Ashi now challenges the entire preceding reading. He objects to the use of the word *hein* as seemingly redundant — if the debate is about *hesitan*, obviously touching is already included and need not be stated. More fundamentally, he observes that *hesitan* in standard halachic usage is not a term applied to an object being moved; it is a term applied to a *tamei person* moving something else. *Masa* is the *tumah* borne by the *tamei* object when carried by a person; *hesitan* is the *tumah* created by the *tamei* person acting as subject and displacing a *tahor* object.

Accordingly, Rav Ashi offers an entirely different reading of the *baraita*: *hein v'hesitan* does not mean "they" and "moving them" as two sides of the same coin, but rather — *bein hein she-hisitu es acherim bein acherim she-hisitu osan* — whether they (the non-Jews) moved other things, or whether others moved them. *Hein* refers to the case where they are the object being carried (*masa*), and *hesitan* refers to the case where they, as subjects, move something else. Both apply to non-Jews because they are fully like *zav* and *zavah*.

For *avodah zarah*, the *baraita* then reads: *she-hisita acherim tehorim* — if the *avodah zarah* moved other things, those others are *tahor*. Why? Because *avodah zarah* is an object, not a person. The *tumah* of *hesitan* — moving another object — belongs to the *tamei* person as subject. An *avodah zarah*, being a physical object rather than a human being, cannot function as the kind of subject that transmits *tumah* by displacement. But if *acherim she-hisitu osah* — if other people move it — they are *tamei*, because that is *masa*, and *masa* applies to *tamei* objects. As for its appurtenances (*meshamsheha*), neither mode — whether they moved something or were moved by others — causes *tumah*; they are *tahor* in either case.

One might ask: how could an *avodah zarah* ever move something else? The *gemara* supplies a concrete example through a case taught in the *mishnah*: *hazav b'chaf moznaim v'ochlin u'mashkin b'chaf sheniyah* — imagine a large balance scale with a *zav* sitting in one pan and food in the other. If the *zav* weighs down his side and thereby causes the other pan to rise and shift — *hechria hazav, teme'im* — the food is *tamei*, because the *zav*'s weight functioned as *hesitan*: he moved the food without touching it. The dynamic can be illustrated by a seesaw: the *zav* sits low, the food rises — that is *hesitan*, with the *tamei* person as subject. If the food is on the heavy side and the *zav* is lifted, that is not *hesitan*; that is no *tumah* at all, because there is no *tahor* person doing the carrying — only food. *Masa* requires a *tahor* person to serve as the carrying agent; without one, there is no *masa*.

Apply this to *avodah zarah*: if the *avodah zarah* is on the heavy side of the scale and displaces food upward — that could theoretically be *hesitan*. But since *hesitan* is a *tumah* of subjects, and *avodah zarah* is an object, the *rabbanan* would say this causes no *tumah*. However, for Rabba's reading of Rabbi Akiva — who holds that *avodah zarah* has the status of *even masama*, making it fully equivalent to a *niddah* — one might go so far as to say it acts as a subject, rendering the food *tamei* through displacement. This is, as the lecturer notes, a striking notion: it nearly anthropomorphizes the idol, treating it not merely as a *tamei* object but as a *tamei* agent capable of transmitting *tumah* through its own displacement of other things. There is something conceptually unsettling about that degree of equivalence — though the motivation behind Rabbi Akiva's position is, presumably, precisely to assign *avodah zarah* the most stringent possible *tumah* status.

## Zav Hesitan

### Zav Exception

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* with a sweeping rule: *kol hatum'ot hamesitot tehorin chutz mehesaito shel zav* — all *tamei* things that move other things, the things they move are *tahor*. The sole exception is the moving done by a *zav*, which is *tamei*, and for which *shelo matzinu lo chaver bechol haTorah kullah* — we have found no parallel in the entire Torah. The *tumah* created when a *tamei* subject displaces a *tahor* object is unique to the *zav* category — *zav*, *zavah*, and *niddah*.

### Attribution Question

The *gemara* immediately asks: *leima dela k'Rabbi Akiva* — does this *baraita* not follow Rabbi Akiva? For if it followed Rabba's reading of Rabbi Akiva, who assigns *avodah zarah* the full *tumah* of *niddah* including *even masama*, then *avodah zarah* would also convey *hesitan tumah*, and the *zav* would not be alone.

The *gemara* deflects: *afilu teima Rabbi Akiva, tanna zav v'chol dedami lei* — even if it is Rabbi Akiva's view, the *baraita* means *zav* and everything in his category. Just as *zavah* and *niddah* are subsumed in that category, according to Rabbi Akiva so too is *avodah zarah*. The *baraita*'s statement that *zav* "has no parallel" means no parallel outside this defined category — within the category, *avodah zarah*, under Rabbi Akiva's view, is included.

## Leivarim Question

### Disassembly Tumah

With all prior discussion resolved, the *gemara* arrives at the question that has been anticipated since early in the *sugya*. *Ba'ei Rav Chama bar Guria: avodah zarah yesh lah evarim o ein lah evarim?* — Does *avodah zarah* have *tumah* when it has been disassembled into its component parts? This question concerns the *tumah* status specifically, not the question of *issur hana'ah* — whether benefit from it remains forbidden — which is a separate discussion addressed in Masechet Avodah Zarah. Indeed, the *tumah* parameters and the *issur hana'ah* parameters need not be identical, and this question is addressed solely in the *tumah* domain.

### Layperson Test

The *gemara* begins to structure the question. *Heicha d'hedyot yachol lehachzira lo tiba'i lach* — where an ordinary person can easily reassemble the idol — slotting peg into slot, as it were — there is no question: *k'man de'mechabar dami*, it is considered as if already whole and retains its *tumah* status. The question arises specifically where a layperson cannot reassemble it and a craftsperson is required. *Mai?* — What is the ruling? Perhaps *k'man de'mitbar dami* — since an ordinary person cannot restore it, it is considered broken and loses its *tumah* status. Or perhaps — *hai lo mechasra* — since all the parts are present and nothing is missing, it retains its *tumah* even in disassembly.

### Wholeness Factor

Some authorities, however, frame the question from the opposite direction, employing a more lenient baseline. Where a layperson cannot reassemble the idol, there is no question: *k'man de'mitbar dami* — it is clearly considered broken, and there is no *tumah*. The question arises only where a layperson can easily reassemble it. *Mai?* — Perhaps *k'man de'mechabar dami* — since reassembly is simple, it is considered whole and *tamei*. Or perhaps *hashta meiha kashaifa v'sharya* — right now it is in fact disassembled, and for the moment it is permitted.

### Teiku Conclusion

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. The possibility that even an easily reassembled idol loses its *tumah* while disassembled is a striking leniency. As the lecturer observes, this can be understood in light of the fact that all of this is *derabbanan*. More conceptually, it reflects the distinction already drawn between *avodah zarah* as object and *avodah zarah* as subject. Other sources of *tumah* — a piece of a corpse, a fragment of a rodent — transmit *tumah* even in small pieces, provided they meet a minimum size threshold. The reason *niddah* is *einah l'evarim* — does not apply to her component parts — is that her *tumah* is a *tumah* of personhood, not of substance. A detached piece of skin is not her. To apply the same logic to *avodah zarah* is in a way to treat it as a subject, like a person, which makes the leniency conceptually curious even as it is practically welcome.

## Kezayit Minimum

### Yosef's Challenge

A further question is raised: *ba'ei rav achdaboi bar ami: avodah zarah pechuta mi'kezayit mahu* — what is the ruling concerning an *avodah zarah* that is smaller than a *kezayit*? Rav Yosef challenges the question: *l'mai* — for what *halacha* is this being asked? If the question concerns *issur hana'ah* — whether it remains forbidden in benefit — that is not in doubt.

### Baal Ekron Fly

Rav Yosef illustrates this with a *baraita*: *vayasimu ba'al berit l'elohim* — the verse in Shoftim says that the people made for themselves a covenant with their gods. The *baraita* identifies this as a reference to *zvuv ba'al ekron* — the fly-god of Baal Ekron, a tiny Phoenician idol. The *baraita* teaches that each person would make a miniature image of the deity they worshipped and keep it in their pocket. Whenever they thought of it while going about their day, they would take it out, embrace it, and kiss it — *mechabka umenashka* — maintaining constant devotion, as though bound by an eternal covenant. The lesson derived is that even something as tiny as this fly-idol is treated as a full *avodah zarah*, forbidden in benefit. So the question about less than a *kezayit* cannot be about *issur hana'ah*, since even the tiniest idol can be an object of worship and is fully forbidden. The question must therefore concern *tumah*.

### Kezayit Comparison

For *tumah*, two competing analogies present themselves. One could argue: *keivan de'itkesh lesheretz* — since *avodah zarah* is compared to a *sheretz*, and a *sheretz* conveys *tumah* even in a quantity as small as a lentil bean (*ke'adasha*), which is less than a *kezayit*, perhaps *avodah zarah* should likewise be *metamei* even below the *kezayit* threshold. Alternatively — *ha'itkesh lemet* — since *avodah zarah* is also compared to a corpse, and a corpse conveys *tumah* only from a *kezayit* and above, perhaps the same minimum applies to *avodah zarah*.

### No Tumah Below

The *gemara* resolves the question by citing a *baraita*: *avodah zarah pechuta mi'kezayit ein ba tumah kol ikkar* — an *avodah zarah* smaller than a *kezayit* has no *tumah* whatsoever. The source is the verse *vayashlech et afarah al kever bnei ha'am* — the dust of the idol was thrown onto the graves of the common people. The comparison of *avodah zarah* to a corpse is thus operative: *ma met bi'kezayit af avodah zarah bi'kezayit* — just as a corpse causes *tumah* only from a *kezayit*, so too *avodah zarah*.

The result is that both leniencies apply simultaneously. An *avodah zarah* must be intact — not disassembled — and must be at least the size of a *kezayit*. Only when both conditions are met does it convey *tumah*. To some degree this treats it more like an object; yet the *einah l'evarim* rule simultaneously treats it more like a subject. Both categories are invoked, but always in the direction of leniency.

## Leniency Logic

### Strict Alternative

The *gemara* now steps back and asks a broader question: why have we consistently derived leniencies from these comparisons? The *gemara* asks: *lemai hilchata itkesh lesheretz?* — Why is *avodah zarah* compared to a *sheretz*? According to Rabbi Eliezer's *rabbanan*, the answer is *delo metamei b'masa* — to exclude *tumat masa*. The comparison to *niddah* teaches *ein lah evarim* — it is not *metamei* in component parts. The comparison to a corpse teaches *delo metamei ke'adasha* — even if whole, it is not *metamei* below the size of a *kezayit*. Each comparison yielded a leniency.

But one might equally have used every one of these comparisons to derive a stringency. The comparison to *sheretz* could have taught that even a lentil-sized piece of *avodah zarah* causes *tumah* — a stringency relative to the *met* standard. The comparison to *niddah* could have taught *even masama* — the boulder *tumah* — a major stringency. The comparison to a corpse could have taught *tumat ohel* — that anything under the same roof as the *avodah zarah* becomes *tamei* — another significant stringency. Three comparisons, three possible stringencies, none of which were adopted.

### Derabanan Lenient

The *gemara* resolves this with the sweeping principle: *tumat avodah zarah derabbanan* — the *tumah* of *avodah zarah* is rabbinic in origin. *Hilchach lekula akshinan lechumra lo akshinan* — therefore, when we have a choice between a lenient derivation and a stringent one, we consistently choose the lenient path. This is the halachic posture appropriate for a rabbinic decree.

What is notable, as the lecturer observes, is that the *gemara* still frames this in the technical language of *hekesh* — analogical derivation from Scripture. It speaks as though genuine learning from *pesukim* is occurring, and then says that because the underlying law is rabbinic, we tilt the derivation toward leniency. This is more nuanced than simply saying the Rabbis invented these rules and then found *pesukim* as *asmachta*. The *pesukim* genuinely guide and constrain the analysis; the rabbinic character of the *tumah* determines which direction the guidance points.

## Boat Tumah

### Boat Purity

The daf now transitions to a new topic in the same pattern of *minayin* — *from where do we know* — inquiries that characterize this chapter. *Minayin lisvina shehi tehora?* — From where do we know that a boat is *tahora*? If a corpse is present on a large ocean vessel, the boat itself does not become *tamei*, and one who merely touches the hull without contact with the source of *tumah* remains *tahor*. Why should an obviously large wooden vessel not be treated as a *kli* that receives *tumah*?

### Two Explanations

One answer is drawn directly from a *pasuk*: *derech oniya b'lev yam* — "the way of a ship in the heart of the sea." The *gemara* initially asks — *peshita*, is it not obvious that a boat goes in the sea? What *halacha* could possibly emerge from this observation? The *gemara* answers: *keyam* — a boat is to be treated like the sea itself. *Ma yam tahor, af sfinah tehorah* — just as the sea is *tahor* and cannot receive *tumah*, so too a boat is *tahor* and cannot receive *tumah*. The boat's essential nature as a vessel of the sea exempts it from the laws governing *kelim*.

### Chananya Sak

Chananya offers a different explanation, deriving the boat's *tahara* from the *sak* category. The *Torah* teaches that wooden vessels share certain laws with *sak* — sackcloth. The principle derived from *sak* is *ma sak metaltel malei v'reikan* — just as a *sak* is carried both full and empty, so too all wood vessels in the same *parsha* must be portable whether filled or empty in order to be susceptible to *tumah*. *Leapukei sfina* — this excludes a boat, *d'ina mitaltelet malei v'reikan* — which cannot be carried when full and, as a practical matter, cannot really be carried even when empty. On Chananya's account, the boat's exemption from *tumah* has nothing to do with its being a nautical vessel; it follows from the general principle that only portable wooden vessels — those that can be lifted and transported regardless of their contents — are susceptible to *tumah* as *kelim*.

### Practical Cases

The two explanations — one focused on the category of *oniya*, boat, and one focused on portability — diverge in several practical cases. One is a pottery boat. For the opinion that derives the exemption from *derech oniya b'lev yam*, the category is simply "boat," and a pottery vessel that functions as a boat would likewise be *tahora*. For Chananya, however, the entire derivation operates through the *sak* analogy, which applies only to wood vessels enumerated alongside *sak* in the Torah. Pottery vessels operate under their own distinct laws in the Torah and are not part of that category. Therefore, even if a pottery vessel functions as a boat and cannot be carried when full, it would not be exempt on the basis of Chananya's reasoning, and its *tumah* status would be governed by the standard rules for pottery.

### Jordan Boat Tamei

A second practical difference involves *sefinat hayarden* — a small boat used to cross the Jordan River, essentially a raft or a canoe. For the opinion that derives the boat's exemption from the *pasuk* about the boat in the heart of the sea, the category is any vessel that functions as a boat, and a river canoe qualifies. *Ha nami oniya belev yam hi* — it too is a boat, and it is therefore *tahora*.

For Chananya, however, the question is portability. A canoe is light enough to carry, and in fact portage is precisely what travelers would do when overland travel required them to lift the canoe and carry it — with their gear loaded inside — from one body of water to another. *Ha nami mitaltelet malei v'reikan* — a canoe can indeed be carried both full and empty. On Chananya's reasoning, it therefore does not fall outside the *sak* category, and it is susceptible to *tumah* as a standard wooden vessel.

This ruling is stated explicitly: *mipnei mah amru sefinat hayarden tamei'ah?* — Why did the Sages rule that the Jordan River boat is *tamei*? *Mipnei she-te'unim otah ba-yabashah u-moridim otah la-mayim* — because people carry it on dry land and then bring it down into the water. The act of portage — carrying the canoe overland with its contents — establishes it as a vessel that is *metaltel malei v'reikan*, portable when full and when empty, and therefore subject to *tumah* like any portable wooden vessel. The discussion of boats will continue in the following daf.
