# Shevuot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumat Mikdash v'Niddah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבִין עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וּמְבִיאִין אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. אֲבָל חַיָּיבִין עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבְּנִדָּה, וּמְבִיאִין אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבְּנִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sanhedrin is not liable to bring a bull offering for an erroneous ruling with regard to a positive mitzva or prohibition concerning ritual impurity in the Temple, and one who is uncertain whether or not he unwittingly transgressed a prohibition that requires a sin-offering does not bring a provisional guilt-offering for a positive mitzva or prohibition concerning ritual impurity in the Temple. But the Sanhedrin is liable to bring a bull offering for an erroneous ruling with regard to a positive mitzva or prohibition concerning a menstruating woman. And one who is uncertain whether or not he unwittingly transgressed a prohibition that requires a sin-offering brings a provisional guilt-offering for a positive mitzva or prohibition concerning a menstruating woman.

### Asei v'Lo Ta'asei

We open on 18a continuing the discussion of the *mishnah*'s treatment of *tumat mikdash v'kodashov* — the laws governing one who enters the *Beis Hamikdash* in a state of *tumah*. The *mishnah* established that if one does so *b'shogeg*, one brings a sliding-scale *korban* known as an *oleh veyored*. The *mishnah* further stated that one is *chayav* for *asei v'lo ta'asei shebe-Mikdash* in one sense, and *chayav* for *asei v'lo ta'asei shebe-niddah* in another — a formulation that, as the *Gemara* recognizes, requires considerable unpacking.

### Horayos Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָאָמַר: זוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ; וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבְּנִדָּה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ? הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ עִם הַטְּהוֹרָה וְאָמְרָה לוֹ ״נִטְמֵאתִי״, וּפֵירַשׁ מִיָּד – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיְּצִיאָתוֹ הֲנָאָה לוֹ כְּבִיאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** And it is in reference to that mishna that the *tanna* in the mishna here says: This mitzva, that the ritually impure must be sent out of the Temple, is the positive mitzva concerning the Temple for which the Sanhedrin is not liable to bring a bull offering for an erroneous ruling. And which is the positive mitzva with regard to a menstruating woman for which the Sanhedrin is liable to bring a bull offering for an erroneous ruling? If a man was engaging in intercourse with a ritually pure woman, and during the course of their act of intercourse she experienced menstrual bleeding and said to him: I have become impure, and unwittingly he immediately withdrew from her and did not wait until his penis became flaccid, he is liable to bring a sin-offering for engaging in intercourse with a menstruating woman, because his withdrawal from her is as pleasant to him as his entry.

The *Gemara* connects this *mishnah* to a parallel discussion in Horayos, which addresses the *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur* — the special *korban* brought when the *Beis Din HaGadol* issues an erroneous ruling and the *tzibbur* acts upon it. That *korban* applies only where the underlying sin carries a fixed *chatas*, and therefore would not apply if the erroneous ruling concerned *tumat mikdash v'kodashov*, since that offense carries an *oleh veyored* rather than a fixed *chatas*. It would, however, apply if the ruling concerned *niddah*, since that offense does carry a fixed *chatas*. The *Gemara*'s choice to juxtapose these two areas of law is not arbitrary — both involve *tumah* and an obligation to separate or withdraw oneself, and that structural similarity seems to be the basis for the comparison.

### Rif's Approach

The peculiarity of finding an extended treatment of *niddah* law embedded in the tractate of Shevuot prompted the Rif to anchor his *hilchos niddah* here rather than in Taharos, on which he did not write. Since the *Gemara* on our *daf* develops the relevant obligations in considerable depth, the Rif uses this passage as the locus for his entire treatment of *hilchos niddah*, expanding outward from the discussion here.

## Chayav Shtayim

### Akhnisa v'Aprishah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. וְכֵן אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שֶׁבָּא, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, חֲדָא אַכְּנִיסָה וַחֲדָא אַפְּרִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated that Abaye says in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav: He is liable to bring two sin-offerings for this unwitting transgression. And so Rava says that Rav Shmuel bar Shaba says that Rav Huna says: He is liable to bring two sin-offerings, one for his initial entry and one for his immediate withdrawal.

The *mishnah* addresses a case in which a husband is engaged in intercourse with his wife and she informs him mid-act that she has become a *niddah*. If he separates immediately while still erect, he is *chayav* — because withdrawal while erect constitutes an act of intercourse in its own right, conferring upon him the same degree of pleasure as entry. The *Gemara* now undertakes to determine precisely how many *chataos* can result from this scenario.

The *Gemara* cites a tradition: *itmar Abaye amar mishmei deRabbi Chiya bar Rav chayav shtayim* — Abaye, citing Rabbi Chiya bar Rav, holds that the man is liable for two *chataos*. This is seconded by Rava citing Shmuel bar Shilas citing Rav Huna, who likewise holds *chayav shtayim*. The two obligations correspond to *akhnisa v'aprishah* — one for the initial act of intercourse and one for the withdrawal.

### Rava's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי בַּהּ רַבָּה: בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ – וּבְמַאן? אִילֵּימָא בְּתַלְמִיד חָכָם – בִּשְׁלָמָא אַכְּנִיסָה לִיחַיַּיב, קָסָבַר יָכוֹלְנִי לִבְעוֹל; אֶלָּא אַפְּרִישָׁה אַמַּאי לִיחַיַּיב? מֵזִיד הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabba discusses this matter, raising a question: About what case are we speaking? If we say that it was near her expected date of menstruation, when sexual intercourse is prohibited due to a concern that the woman might already be menstruating or that she might begin to menstruate during the act of intercourse, and nevertheless they engaged in intercourse, there is a difficulty: With whom are we dealing? If we say that we are dealing with a Torah scholar, granted that he will be liable to bring a sin-offering for his initial entry. That was an unwitting transgression, as he thought to himself: I can engage in intercourse with her before she begins to menstruate. But why will he be liable to bring a sin-offering for his immediate withdrawal? That transgression was intentional, since he is a Torah scholar and he knows that in such a case he must not withdraw immediately, and a sin-offering is not brought for an intentional transgression.

Rava then challenges the formulation: *bemai* — in what precise scenario do we achieve two distinct obligations? The obvious candidate is a case of *samuch l'vesta* — where they engaged in intercourse at a time when the woman was near her expected period — and where, as we will see, the obligation flows from the man's negligence. But Rava probes which type of man we are dealing with, since the characterization of his conduct as *shogeg*, *meizid*, or *oness* turns critically on his knowledge of *halacha*.

### Talmid Chacham

If the man is a *talmid chacham* who knows *halacha*, one can understand why he would be *chayav* for the initial act: *bishlama knisah lechayiv kasavar yachol lavo* — he thought he could manage to withdraw before she would become a *niddah*, and therefore he was a willful violator of the safeguard, but only negligent (*shogeg*) with respect to the actual prohibition of *niddah* itself. He knew he was transgressing the protective fence but believed the underlying prohibition would not be triggered. That negligence with respect to the actual prohibition of *niddah* makes him a *poshea*, and a *poshea* is *chayav* a *korban*.

But the problem arises with the *aprishah* — the withdrawal. If he is a *talmid chacham*, then at the moment she informs him she is a *niddah*, he knows that he must not separate immediately; he knows the *halacha* that requires him to wait. If he separates immediately anyway, that is *meizid* — a willful violation — and a *meizid* does not bring a *chatas*.

Turning to 18a: if instead the man is an *am ha'aretz* — someone ignorant of *halacha* — then both violations collapse into one. His initial entry when she was near her period and his subsequent withdrawal while erect are both *b'shogeg*, but they occurred within a single continuous state of unawareness, analogous to eating two *kezeitim* of forbidden fat in a single *he'elem* — one continuous state of ignorance — which yields only one *korban chatas*. Although one might object that when she informs him she is a *niddah*, he gains awareness mid-act and should therefore be obligated for a separate *chatas*, Rashi explains that if he is an *am ha'aretz*, he does not realize that his initial act of intercourse — engaging near the expected period — was itself wrongful. He does not understand that he was negligent before. So when she informs him, he has no sense that the first part of his conduct was culpable. He acquires awareness only later, and that awareness comes too late to separate the two acts into two distinct obligations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בְּעַם הָאָרֶץ – אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״אָכַל שְׁנֵי זֵיתֵי חֵלֶב בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד״ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** And if we say that we are dealing with an *am ha'aretz*, an ignoramus, who does not know that it is prohibited for him to engage in intercourse with a woman near the expected onset of her menstruation, and who also does not know that it is prohibited for him to immediately withdraw from her if she experiences menstrual bleeding during intercourse, then for both this and that, his entry and his withdrawal, he should be liable to bring only a single sin-offering, since he had no awareness of his transgression between his two actions. This is like one who ate two olive-bulks of forbidden fat in the course of one lapse of awareness and is therefore liable to bring only one sin-offering. Here too, although he performed two actions for which one could be liable to bring sin-offerings, entry and withdrawal, there was only one lapse of awareness.

So the *Gemara* concludes that a case of *chayav shtayim* cannot be constructed if we are either dealing with a *talmid chacham* in a *samuch l'vesta* scenario, or an *am ha'aretz* in either scenario.

### Samuch L'Vesta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם בְּסָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ, וּבְתַלְמִיד חָכָם, וְתַלְמִיד חָכָם לְזוֹ וְאֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם לָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava reconsidered and then said: Actually, it is a case where it was near the expected date of the woman's menstruation, and we are dealing with a Torah scholar. But he is a Torah scholar only with regard to this halakha, that it is prohibited to engage in intercourse with a woman near the expected onset of her menstruation, and he erred in thinking that he could complete the act of intercourse before she experienced menstrual bleeding. Therefore, once he learns that she has experienced menstrual bleeding, he is liable to bring a sin-offering for his unwitting transgression at the time of his entry. But he is not a Torah scholar with regard to that halakha, that it is prohibited for one to immediately withdraw from a woman if she experiences menstrual bleeding during intercourse. Since he had awareness of his transgression between his two actions, this is not an instance of one lapse of awareness, and therefore he is liable to bring a second sin-offering for his unwitting transgression at the time of his withdrawal.

The *Gemara* therefore proposes: *le'olam besamuch l'vesta uve'talmid chacham* — the case is indeed *samuch l'vesta* and the man is indeed a *talmid chacham* — but the *talmid chacham* distinction is applied unevenly. He is a *talmid chacham* with respect to the first *halacha* — knowing that one should not engage in intercourse when the woman is near her expected period — but not a *talmid chacham* with respect to the second *halacha* — he does not know that he must wait for his erection to subside before withdrawing. He is learned in one area and ignorant in another. That asymmetric knowledge is what generates two separate *chataos*: the first for the initial negligence (*shogeg* regarding the prohibition of *niddah* itself, even though *meizid* regarding the safeguard), and the second for the withdrawal while erect, on which he is *shogeg* because he does not know that *halacha* at all.

### Tanu Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ תְּנַנְהִי; כְּנִיסָה תְּנֵינָא, פְּרִישָׁה תְּנֵינָא. פְּרִישָׁה תְּנֵינָא – דְּקָתָנֵי: הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ עִם הַטְּהוֹרָה וְאָמְרָה לוֹ ״נִטְמֵאתִי״, וּפֵירַשׁ מִיָּד – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: And we learn both of these matters for which he is liable to bring a sin-offering; we learn about entry, and we learn about withdrawal. Rava now clarifies the matter: We learn about withdrawal, as the mishna teaches: If a man was engaging in intercourse with a ritually pure woman, and during the course of their act of intercourse she experienced menstrual bleeding and said to him: I have become impure, and unwittingly he immediately withdrew from her, he is liable to bring a sin-offering for engaging in intercourse with a menstruating woman.

Rava then notes that both obligations are in fact already attested in existing *mishnayos*: *vitravayhu taninhu* — we have taught both of them. The obligation of *aprishah* — that withdrawal while erect constitutes an act of intercourse — is explicit in our *mishnah*: a man was having intercourse with a woman *she'einah niddah*, she informed him *nitmeti*, he separated immediately — *chayav*. And the obligation that arises from the initial act of negligence — *knisah* — is attested in a *mishnah* in Niddah: *nimtze'u al shelo tme'im vechayavin bekorban* — if after separation the couple examines themselves and he finds blood on his cloth, they are *tamei* and *chayav* a *korban*. The blood on his cloth is proof she was a *niddah* during intercourse. The *Gemara* suggests that the only coherent explanation of that case is *besamuch l'vesta* — they were engaged near her expected period — which is why his negligence generates a *chiyuv*.

### Rav Acha's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּנִיסָה תְּנֵינָא: נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלּוֹ – טְמֵאִין וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן. מַאי, לָאו בְּסָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ – וְאַכְּנִיסָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא לְרָבָא: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ, בְּשֶׁלֹּא סָמוּךְ לְוִוסְתָּהּ – וְאַפְּרִישָׁה; וְכִי תֵּימָא: פְּרִישָׁה לְמָה לִי, הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ; הָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן: נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלָּהּ – טְמֵאִים בְּסָפֵק, וּפְטוּרִין מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן; וְאַיְּידֵי דְּבָעֵי מִיתְנֵא נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלָּהּ, תְּנָא נָמֵי נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: We learn about entry in a mishna (*Nidda* 14a): If a spot of blood is found on his rag, i.e., the rag that he uses to wipe his penis after intercourse, then it is clear that this blood came from the woman during their act of intercourse. Consequently, both the man and the woman are impure and are liable to bring a sin-offering for their unwitting transgression. Rava explains: What, is it not the case that the mishna speaks here of one who engaged in intercourse with a woman near the expected date of her menstruation, and it teaches that he is liable to bring a sin-offering for his initial entry at that time?
>
> Rav Adda bar Mattana said to Rava: Actually, I could say to you that the mishna speaks here of one who engaged in intercourse with a woman not near the expected date of her menstruation, and he is liable to bring a sin-offering for his immediate withdrawal upon learning that she had experienced menstrual bleeding. And if you would say: Why do I need to be taught once again about withdrawal? Didn't the *tanna* already teach this in the mishna here? One can respond: It was necessary to teach us this, i.e., the continuation of the mishna in tractate *Nidda*, which states: If a spot of blood is found on her rag, i.e., the rag that she uses to wipe herself after intercourse, they are impure only because of an uncertainty, as perhaps the bleeding commenced only after they completed their act of intercourse, and therefore they are exempt from bringing a sin-offering. And since the mishna had to teach the case where the blood was found on her rag, it also taught the case where it was found on his rag, even though there is no novel element in this ruling.

Rav Acha bar Matna objects to Rava's reading of the *mishnah* in Niddah. He argues: *le'olam eima lach beshelo samuch levistah vehafrishah* — perhaps the case in the Niddah *mishnah* is not *samuch l'vesta* at all, and the obligation does not arise from the initial act but from the act of *aprishah* — the separation. Rava's attempt to use that *mishnah* as proof of an obligation for initial negligence may be misplaced.

Rav Acha acknowledges, however, that this reading requires him to explain why that *mishnah* was needed at all if we already know the obligation of withdrawal from our *mishnah* here. His answer: the real *chiddush* of the Niddah *mishnah* is the other half of the case — *nimtze'u al shelah tme'im besafek ufturin min hakorban* — that if blood is found on her cloth after separation, they are only *tamei besafek* and exempt from a *korban*, because the bleeding may have begun after the separation. That is the true novelty, and the *mishnah* also mentions the case of blood on his cloth only in order to contrast it. The actual *chiyuv* in that first case, Rav Acha proposes, may be due to the act of withdrawal itself rather than initial negligence.

### Ravina's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אַדָּא: מִי מָצֵית לְאוֹקֹמַהּ לְהַהִיא בְּשֶׁלֹּא סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ, וְאַפְּרִישָׁה?! וְהָא ״נִמְצָא״ קָתָנֵי, וְ״נִמְצָא״ לְבָתַר הָכִי מַשְׁמַע; וְאִי אַפְּרִישָׁה, מֵעִיקָּרָא כִּי פָּרֵישׁ לֵיהּ – מֵעִיקָּרָא הָוְיָא לֵיהּ יְדִיעָה!
>
> **Translation:** Ravina said to Rav Adda: Can you really interpret that mishna as referring to a case where it was not near the expected date of the woman's menstruation, and that he is liable to bring a sin-offering for his withdrawal? But isn't it taught in that mishna: If blood is found on his rag? And these words indicate that the blood was found only afterward, after the man had already withdrawn from the woman. That is to say, only after he withdrew from the woman he learned that she had experienced menstrual bleeding. Ravina clarifies the difficulty: And if the mishna is referring to a case where he is liable for his withdrawal, at the outset, when he withdrew from the woman, it was from then that he had knowledge of her menstrual status, as he withdrew because she had informed him that she had experienced bleeding. Of what significance is his finding blood on his rag?

Ravina responds to Rav Acha's position sharply: *mi matzis le'ukmeh hachi beshelo samuch l'vista u'parsha* — how can you explain the case as one of *aprishah* when the *mishnah* says *nimtza* — it was discovered after the fact? The language *nimtza* implies that he only became aware after he had already separated. If there was no negligence at the outset — no *samuch l'vesta* — then there is no prior culpability. And if his awareness came only at the end, after separation was already complete, he cannot be retroactively obligated for the act of withdrawal, because that withdrawal was performed without any knowledge that she was a *niddah*. *Nimtza* means discovered afterwards — and that timing makes it impossible to ground the obligation in the withdrawal.

Furthermore: *ve-ha-prisha, me-ikara ki paresh lei me-ikara havei lei yedia* — if you insist the obligation arises from the withdrawal, then for him to know to withhold immediate separation, she would have had to inform him *during* intercourse. But if she informed him during intercourse, the *mishnah* would not say he *discovered* it afterward on his cloth. The two readings are mutually exclusive.

### Initial Negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: צָיֵית מַאי דְּקָאָמַר רַבָּךְ. הֵיכִי אֵצֵית? דְּתַנְיָא עֲלַהּ: זוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבְּנִדָּה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ, וְאִם אִיתָא – מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** Rava said to Rav Adda: Listen to what your teacher, Ravina, is saying, as he has explained the matter. Rav Adda said to Rava: How can I listen to his words and accept his explanation? As it is taught in a *baraita* with regard to this mishna concerning blood found on a rag: This is the positive mitzva with regard to a menstruating woman for which one is liable. Rav Adda explains: And if it is so that the mishna is referring to a case where the man is liable for his initial entry, this wording is difficult, as engaging in intercourse with a menstruating woman is not the violation of a positive mitzva, but a prohibition.

Rava closes the exchange: *tsayis mai de'ka'amar rabach* — Rav Adda, listen to your teacher. The attempt to ground the obligation in *aprishah* cannot be sustained. The *Gemara* then engages a *baraita* that states: *zu hi mitzvat asei she-be'niddah she-chayavin aleha* — this is the positive commandment regarding *niddah* for which one is *chayav*. Rava objects: if the obligation at issue is the initial negligence, then it should be described as a *lo ta'asei*, a negative prohibition — the prohibition against relations with a *niddah* — not a positive commandment. Rav Acha responds: if necessary, emend the *baraita* and read it as *mitzvat lo ta'asei she-be'niddah*. The *baraita* can then be read in two parts: the case in which a man engages in intercourse and she informs him she is a *niddah* and he separates immediately — that is grounded in the negative prohibition; and the case that emerges from the *mishnah* in Niddah, where the obligation is characterized as an *asei*, the positive commandment of *prisha* — separating in the proper manner when one learns one's wife is a *niddah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי תָּנֵיתָא – חַסַּר וּתְנִי הָכִי: זוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבְּנִדָּה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ. הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ עִם הַטְּהוֹרָה וְאָמְרָה לוֹ ״נִטְמֵאתִי״, וּפֵירַשׁ מִיָּד – חַיָּיב, זוֹ הִיא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבְּנִדָּה כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said to him: If you learned this *baraita* in this manner, its wording is imprecise, as it is missing words, and you should teach it like this: When blood is found on the man's rag, this is the prohibition with regard to a menstruating woman for which one is liable. And additionally, if a man was engaging in intercourse with a ritually pure woman, and during the course of their act of intercourse she experienced menstrual bleeding and said to him: I have become impure, and unwittingly he immediately withdrew from her, he is liable to bring a sin-offering for that act, and this is the positive mitzva for which one is liable with regard to a menstruating woman.

What is notable here is the structural parallel to the case of *tumat mikdash*: just as the *tamei* person in the *Beis Hamikdash* is obligated by a positive commandment to leave — and to leave in the most expeditious way — so too the husband is obligated by a positive commandment to separate, though paradoxically the correct mode of separation here requires *waiting* rather than haste.

## How to Separate

### Waiting for Detumescence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: פֵּירַשׁ מִיָּד – חַיָּיב. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: נוֹעֵץ עֶשֶׂר צִפׇּרְנָיו בַּקַּרְקַע עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת, וְטוּבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Master said above in the mishna: If he immediately withdrew from the woman after having been informed that she had experienced menstrual bleeding, he is liable to bring a sin-offering for engaging in intercourse with a menstruating woman. The Gemara asks: What should one do in such a situation? Rav Huna said in the name of Rava: He should press his ten fingernails into the ground, i.e., the bed, and restrain himself and do nothing until his penis becomes flaccid, and only then should he withdraw from her, and it is good for him to do so.

The *Gemara* now addresses the practical question: if immediate withdrawal is *chayav*, what *should* the man do? Rav Huna citing Rava answers: *no'etz eser tzipornav bakarka ad sheyamut* — he should dig his fingernails into the ground, remain perfectly still, and wait until the erection subsides before withdrawing. This is the proper method of *prisha*.

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מֵת בַּעֲרָיוֹת – פָּטוּר. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ חַיָּיב, הָכָא מַאי טַעְמָא פָּטוּר? מִשּׁוּם דְּאָנוּס הוּא. אִי אָנוּס הוּא, כִּי פֵּירַשׁ מִיָּד נָמֵי נִיפְּטַר – אָנוּס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: That is to say, one who engages in intercourse, with a flaccid penis, with those with whom relations are forbidden is exempt. As if it enters your mind to say that he is liable, here, in the mishna, what is the reason that he is exempt if he waits and withdraws only later, after he has lost his erection? You might say that it is because he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control in that the woman experienced menstrual bleeding while he was in the middle of the act of intercourse, and not because he withdrew with a flaccid penis, as one who engages in intercourse with a flaccid penis is liable. But if he is exempt from liability because he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control, then even if he withdraws immediately, before losing his erection, he should also be exempt, for the same reason, that he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control.

Rava then draws a further inference: *zos omeres hameshammesh meis ba'arayos patur* — this ruling implies that intercourse performed with a flaccid member does not constitute a prohibited act of intercourse that generates liability. For if it did, one would have to explain the exemption when the man withdraws after waiting as being due to *ones* — compulsion — rather than the absence of an act altogether. But as the *Gemara* demonstrates, that *ones* explanation creates its own problem.

### Minimizing Pleasure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ, הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מֵת בַּעֲרָיוֹת חַיָּיב; וְהָכָא מַאי טַעְמָא פָּטוּר – מִשּׁוּם דְּאָנוּס הוּא. וְהָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: כִּי פָרֵישׁ מִיָּד אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לִפְרוֹשׁ בַּהֲנָאָה מוּעֶטֶת, וּפֵירַשׁ בַּהֲנָאָה מְרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to Rava: Actually, I could say to you that one who engages in intercourse, with a flaccid penis, with those with whom relations are forbidden is liable. And here, what is the reason that one who waits and withdraws only later, after he has lost his erection, is exempt? It is because he is considered a victim of circumstances beyond his control. And concerning that which you said: Why, then, is he liable if he withdrew immediately, i.e., when you said: He should also be exempt, it is because he should have withdrawn with a flaccid penis and experienced little pleasure, but instead he withdrew with an erect organ and experienced great pleasure.

Abaye disputes this inference. In his view, intercourse with a flaccid member does constitute an act of intercourse. And here, when the man waits for detumescence and then withdraws, he is indeed technically performing an act, but he is exempt because he is an *oness* — he has no choice but to withdraw eventually. The reason he is *chayav* when he withdraws immediately while still erect is that *shehaya lo lifrosh behana'ah mu'etes upirash behana'ah merubah* — he could have performed the separation in a manner that minimized his pleasure, and instead he chose to do it in a manner that maximized it. The availability of a better option means he cannot invoke *ones*. When he waits, he has done everything possible to minimize the violation, and at that point he truly is an *oness* with no remaining choice.

### Aruka v'Ketzara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר חָנָן לְאַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן מָצִינוּ אֲרוּכָּה וּקְצָרָה בְּנִדָּה,
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Ḥanan said to Abaye: If so, according to your opinion, we find a difference in *halakha* between a long way and a short way with regard to a menstruating woman. If he withdrew the short way, i.e., without waiting, he has committed a transgression, and if he withdrew the long way, i.e., after waiting, he has not committed a transgression.

Rava bar Chanan raises an analogy: *im kein matzinu arucha u'ketzara b'niddah* — according to Abaye's position, we now find the same principle of "long path and short path" (*aruka v'ketzara*) applying to *niddah* that applies in the case of the *Beis Hamikdash*. A *tamei* person who must exit the *Beis Hamikdash* and takes the longer route rather than the shorter one is *chayav*; similarly, separating immediately (the "short path") is *chayav*, while waiting for detumescence (the "long path") is *patur*. But the *Gemara* had stated that the concept of *aruka v'ketzara* applies only in the context of the *Mikdash* — suggesting that in *niddah* the same principle does not operate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנַן בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ תְּנַן! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא דָּמֵי; אֲרוּכָּה דְּהָכָא – קְצָרָה דְּהָתָם, וַאֲרוּכָּה דְּהָתָם – קְצָרָה דְּהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** And we learned this difference in the mishna specifically with regard to ritual impurity in the Temple. If such a distinction were also in effect with regard to a menstruating woman, the mishna would mention it. Rather, one can explain: No parallel distinction is made, because the two cases are not similar in their details. The long way here, with regard to a menstruating woman, namely, that the man must wait, is like the shortest way there, with regard to impurity in the Temple, namely, that the impure person must leave the Temple by way of the most direct route. And the long way there, with regard to the Temple, is like the shortest way here, with regard to a menstruating woman.

The *Gemara* resolves this: there is no contradiction, only an inversion. In the *Mikdash*, the obligation is to leave as quickly as possible — so haste is *patur* and delay is *chayav*. In *niddah*, the obligation is to delay before separating — so waiting is *patur* and haste is *chayav*. The directions are reversed, but the underlying principle — that the manner of extrication determines liability — is perfectly analogous.

### Rav Huna Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן: מִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי אָנוּס הוּא – אַלְמָא בְּשֶׁלֹּא סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ קָאָמְרִינַן?! וְהָא אַבַּיֵּי דְּאָמַר חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם – אַלְמָא בְּסָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ עָסְקִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, objects to what Abaye said: Did Abaye really say with regard to the mishna that if the man withdraws with a flaccid penis he is exempt because he is considered a victim of circumstances beyond his control? Apparently, then, we are speaking of a man who engaged in intercourse with a woman not near the expected date of her menstruation, and therefore the situation is considered beyond his control. But is it not Abaye who says that he is liable to bring two sin-offerings for this transgression, one for his initial entry and one for his withdrawal? Apparently, we are dealing with a man who engaged in intercourse with a woman near the expected date of her menstruation, so he is considered an unwitting transgressor, who is liable to bring a sin-offering, and is not the victim of circumstances beyond his control. Consequently, Abaye's two statements contradict each other.

Rav Huna bar Rav Natan challenges Abaye's position directly. If the exemption for waiting until detumescence rests on *ones*, that claim of *ones* is unavailable in the standard case of the *mishnah*, where the act began *samuch l'vesta* — near the expected period — because the couple's negligence at the outset destroys any subsequent claim of compulsion. One cannot say *ones* when one created the dangerous situation through one's own *peshia*. Therefore, if Abaye maintains that waiting until detumescence still constitutes an act of intercourse, he cannot use *ones* to exempt it in the case where the whole encounter began negligently.

### Two Cases Distinguished

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אִיתְּמַר דְּאַבָּיֵי – בְּעָלְמָא אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** When this statement of Abaye was stated, that the man is liable to bring two sin-offerings, it was stated in general. It was not relating to the case in the mishna, but was an independent ruling concerning one who engages in intercourse with a woman near the expected date of her menstruation.

The *Gemara* resolves the tension by distinguishing the two cases. When Abaye said *chayav shtayim* — that the man is liable twice — he was speaking about a general scenario, not specifically about the *mishnah*'s case. The *mishnah*'s case, in which she informs him mid-act and he separates immediately, is one in which the encounter could have begun without any negligence — *beshelo samuch l'vesta* — and therefore the *ones* claim remains intact for the act of waiting and withdrawing. Abaye's ruling of *chayav shtayim*, by contrast, describes a case where the encounter *did* begin with negligence (*samuch l'vesta*), and the two obligations arise from the initial negligence and the improper withdrawal respectively. The two scenarios are analytically distinct, and Abaye's position is coherent in both.

## Sources & Warnings

### Azhara L'Boel Niddah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן יוֹסֵי בֶּן לָקוֹנְיָא מֵרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹסֵי בֶּן לָקוֹנְיָא: אַזְהָרָה לְבוֹעֵל נִדָּה, מִנַּיִן מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שְׁקַל קָלָא פְּתַק בֵּיהּ: אַזְהָרָה לְבוֹעֵל נִדָּה?! ״וְאֶל אִשָּׁה בְּנִדַּת טֻמְאָתָהּ לֹא תִקְרַב״!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosei ben Lakonya asked Rabbi Shimon ben Yosei ben Lakonya: From where in the Torah is the prohibition concerning one who engages in intercourse with a menstruating woman [*nidda*] derived? Rabbi Shimon ben Yosei ben Lakonya took a clod [*kala*] of earth and threw it at him in reproach and said to him: Is there a need to search the Torah for a derivation for the prohibition concerning one who engages in intercourse with a menstruating woman? The verse states: "And a woman who is impure by her uncleanness [*nidda*] you shall not approach, to uncover her nakedness" (Leviticus 18:19)?

The *Gemara* transitions to a discussion of the scriptural sources for the relevant prohibitions. Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosei ben Lakunya posed a question to Rabbi Shimon ben Yosei ben Lakunya — presumably his brother — asking: *azhara l'boel niddah minayin* — where in the Torah is the prohibition against intercourse with a *niddah*? Rabbi Shimon's reaction was to throw a clump of earth at him — the question was so obvious as to be almost insulting.

The answer, of course, is the explicit verse: *v'el isha b'niddat tumatah lo tikrav* — "And to a woman in her *niddah* impurity you shall not draw near." That is the *azhara* for the prohibition. But Rabbi Yonatan clarifies that his question was not about the basic prohibition — that much is elementary. What he was asking about was the source for the *halacha* we have been discussing all along: how do we know that a man who is engaged in intercourse and learns that his wife has become a *niddah* may not separate immediately?

### Lo Tikrav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה לִמְשַׁמֵּשׁ עִם הַטְּהוֹרָה וְאָמְרָה לוֹ ״נִטְמֵאתִי״, דְּלָא נִיפְרוֹשׁ מִיָּד – מְנָלַן? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּתְהִי נִדָּתָהּ עָלָיו״ – אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת נִדָּתָהּ תְּהֵא עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the intent of the question of Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosei ben Lakonya: Rather, from where do we derive the prohibition with regard to the case in the mishna concerning one who was engaging in intercourse with a ritually pure woman, and she experienced menstrual bleeding and said to him: I have become impure, that he must not withdraw immediately? In response to this question Ḥizkiyya said: The verse states: "And if any man lies with her, and her menstrual flow shall be upon him" (Leviticus 15:24), teaching that even at any time when she is menstruating, the prohibition shall be upon him; therefore, he must not withdraw from her immediately.

Hizkiyah answers from the verse *utehi niddatah alav* — "and her *niddah* state shall be upon him." While this verse primarily speaks to the transfer of *tumah*, the *Gemara* reads it as establishing that even at the moment she becomes a *niddah*, the responsibility (*alav*) is upon him — he must manage the separation responsibly. *Ashkachan asei* — this establishes the positive obligation. But whence the negative prohibition against immediate withdrawal?

### Rav Pappa's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן עֲשֵׂה, לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תִקְרַב״; ״לֹא תִקְרַב״ נָמֵי לָא תִּפְרוֹשׁ הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״הָאֹמְרִים קְרַב אֵלֶיךָ אַל תִּגַּשׁ בִּי כִּי קְדַשְׁתִּיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: We found a source for a positive mitzva with regard to the manner in which one must withdraw from a menstruating woman; from where do we derive that immediate withdrawal is also subject to a prohibition? Rav Pappa said: The verse states: "And a woman who is impure by her uncleanness you shall not approach, to uncover her nakedness" (Leviticus 18:19). The Gemara explains: "You shall not approach [*tikrav*]" means also the opposite: You shall not withdraw, as it is written: "Those who say: Withdraw [*kerav*] to yourself, come not near to me, for I am holier than you" (Isaiah 65:5), where "*kerav*" means remove or withdraw.

Rav Pappa derives the negative prohibition from the same verse used earlier: *lo tikrav* — "you shall not draw near." He reads *lo tikrav* as also meaning *lo tifrosh* — "you shall not withdraw" — in the sense of improper separation. His proof is from a different verse: *ha'omrim krav elecha al tigash bi ki kedashticha* — "those who say 'krav' to you, do not approach me, for I have made you holy" — where *krav* is used in the sense of withdrawing or standing apart from others. Rashi explains this as *amod b'atzmecha* — stand by yourself, away from others. The reading is admittedly not *peshat*, and the *Gemara* seems to require it precisely because the notion that the act of separation itself constitutes a forbidden act of intercourse is such a striking *chiddush* that a specific scriptural basis is warranted.

## Safeguard Period

### V'Hizartem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִזַּרְתֶּם אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִטֻּמְאָתָם״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: מִיכָּן אַזְהָרָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיִּפְרְשׁוּ מִנְּשׁוֹתֵיהֶן סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּן. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַבָּה: עוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned that it is prohibited for a man to engage in intercourse with a woman near the expected date of her menstruation, the Gemara cites a *baraita* in which the Sages taught: The verse with regard to a menstruating woman states: "And shall you separate the children of Israel from their uncleanness" (Leviticus 15:31), Rabbi Yoshiya says: From here we derive a prohibition to the children of Israel that they must separate from their wives near the expected date of their menstruation. And how long before must they separate? Rabba says: A set interval of time for the ritual impurity of a menstruating woman, which is half of a twenty-four hour day, either the daytime or the nighttime.

A *baraita* is cited: *tanu rabbanan — ve-hizartem et b'nei yisrael mi-tumatam*. Rabbi Yoshiyah derives from this verse — "you shall separate (*hizartem*) the children of Israel from their *tumah*" — an obligation upon men to separate from their wives near the time the wife's period is expected: *mikan azhara livnei yisrael sheyifreshu minshoteihem samuch l'vastan*. The word *ve-hizartem* connotes creating separation and building a fence, analogous to the term *nezorah achor* — to draw back. Rabbi Yishmael is cited alongside Rabbi Yoshiyah in this context as a parallel authority.

Rava defines the duration of this separation: one *onah* — a defined period of either daytime or nighttime. The *poskim* understand this to mean that if the woman is expected to begin her period during daytime hours, the couple refrains for the entire day-period, from approximately dawn to nightfall; if she is expected to begin at night, they refrain for the entire night-period. This formalism produces what might seem like an arbitrary result — the prohibited window could be as little as a few minutes or as long of the full twelve-hour block — but that is how *halacha* has understood the *tosefet onah* here.

### Severe Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ פּוֹרֵשׁ מֵאִשְׁתּוֹ סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ, אֲפִילּוּ הוֹיִין לוֹ בָּנִים כִּבְנֵי אַהֲרֹן – מֵתִים; דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִזַּרְתֶּם אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִטֻּמְאָתָם וְהַדָּוָה בְּנִדָּתָהּ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אַחֲרֵי מוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: With regard to anyone who does not separate from his wife near the expected date of her menstruation, even if he has sons who are fit to be great and holy like the sons of Aaron, these sons will die due to his sin, as it is written: "And shall you separate the children of Israel from their uncleanness…this is the law…of her that is sick with her menstrual flow" (Leviticus 15:31–33), and it is stated near it: "After the death of the two sons of Aaron" (Leviticus 16:1).

Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, issues a severe warning: *kol she'eino poresh me'ishto samuch l'visto afilu havyu lo banim kivnei Aharon meisim* — any man who does not observe this separation near his wife's expected period, even if his children were as great as the children of Aharon, they will die. The scriptural hook: *ve-hizartem et b'nei yisrael mi-tumatam* is immediately followed in the Torah by *v'lo yamutu b'tumtam b'tamma'am et mishkani asher b'tocham* — lest they die in their impurity by defiling My *mishkan* — and then the passage of *niddah* and *zav* concludes with *v'hadavah b'niddatah*. That juxtaposition of the warning to separate with the threat of death forms the basis of Rabbi Yochanan's statement. That this warning appears at the very close of the *parasha* of Metzora, with the very next verse opening Acharei Mot with the death of the sons of Aharon, reinforces the connection.

### Rewards for Compliance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מֵאִשְׁתּוֹ סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּהּ – הוֹיִין לוֹ בָּנִים זְכָרִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין הַטָּמֵא וּבֵין הַטָּהוֹר״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אִשָּׁה כִּי תַזְרִיעַ וְיָלְדָה זָכָר״. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: הוֹיִין לוֹ בָּנִים רְאוּיִין לְהוֹרָאָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְהַבְדִּיל... וּלְהוֹרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this matter, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Anyone who separates himself from his wife near the expected date of her menstruation will have male children, as it is written: "To distinguish between the impure and the pure" (Leviticus 11:47), and it is stated near it: "If a woman conceive and bear a male child" (Leviticus 12:2). Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: He will have sons who are worthy of teaching halakha, as it is written: "To distinguish…between the impure and the pure, and to teach the children of Israel all the statutes" (Leviticus 10:10–11).

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan offers the positive formulation: *kol haporesh me'ishto samuch l'visto havyu lo banim zachar* — one who does observe the separation near his wife's expected period will merit male children. The source: *l'havdil bein hatamei u'vein hatahor* — to distinguish between impure and pure — and the very next verse in Tazria-Metzora reads *ishah ki tazria v'yalda zachar* — a woman who conceives and gives birth to a male. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers an alternative formulation of the reward: *banim re'uyim l'hora'ah* — sons fit for *hora'ah*, for rendering halachic decisions — derived from *l'havdil u'lehorot*, to separate and to instruct.

### Havdalah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַמַּבְדִּיל עַל הַיַּיִן בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת – הוֹיִין לוֹ בָּנִים זְכָרִים; דִּכְתִיב: ״לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין הַחוֹל״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין הַטָּמֵא וּבֵין הַטָּהוֹר״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אִשָּׁה כִּי תַזְרִיעַ״. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: בָּנִים רְאוּיִן לְהוֹרָאָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְהַבְדִּיל... וּלְהוֹרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to expound these verses: Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Anyone who recites *havdala* over wine at the conclusion of *Shabbatot*, and not over some other beverage, will have male children, as it is written: "To distinguish between the holy and the unholy, and between the impure and the pure" (Leviticus 10:10), and it is written there once again: "To distinguish between the impure and the pure" (Leviticus 11:47), and it is stated near it: "If a woman conceive and bear a male child" (Leviticus 12:2). Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Anyone who recites *havdala* over wine at the conclusion of *Shabbatot* will have sons who are worthy of teaching halakha, as it is written: "To distinguish between the holy and the unholy…and to teach" (Leviticus 10:10–11).

The *Gemara* then applies the same textual derivation to a related context. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan states: *kol hamavdil al hayayin b'motza'ei Shabbatot havyu lo banim zachar* — anyone who recites *havdalah* over wine on *Motza'ei Shabbos* will merit male children. The connection: *l'havdil bein hakodesh u'vein hachol* — the language of *havdalah* between sacred and mundane — is linked via *gezeira shava* to *l'havdil bein hatamei u'vein hatahor*, which leads again to *ishah ki tazria v'yalda zachar*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi again adds: *banim re'uyim l'hora'ah*, derived from *l'havdil u'lehorot*.

### Mekadesh Atzmo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת עַצְמוֹ בִּשְׁעַת תַּשְׁמִישׁ – הוֹיִין לוֹ בָּנִים זְכָרִים; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִתְקַדִּשְׁתֶּם וִהְיִיתֶם קְדֹשִׁים״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אִשָּׁה כִּי תַזְרִיעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet says that Rabbi Elazar says: Anyone who sanctifies himself with modest conduct while engaging in sexual intercourse will have male children, as it is stated: "You shall sanctify yourselves, and you shall be holy" (Leviticus 11:44), and it is stated near it: "If a woman conceive and bear a male child" (Leviticus 12:2).

Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet citing Rabbi Elazar teaches: *kol hamekadesh et atzmo b'sha'at tashmish havyu lo banim zachar* — one who sanctifies himself at the time of intercourse will merit male children, as it says *ve-hitkadishtem viheyitem kedoshim*, and the adjacent verse is again *ishah ki tazria v'yalda zachar*. What exactly *mekadesh atzmo b'sha'at tashmish* means is a matter of significant dispute among the *poskim*. Some have read it as a call for asceticism during the marital act. Many others strongly disagree with that interpretation. A particularly compelling approach, grounded in the Gra, understands it to mean that one fully honors the *tzelem Elokim* of one's spouse — that one is not exclusively focused on one's own desires but genuinely attends to the other person's needs and personhood. The precise meaning of this *kedushah* in the context of *tashmish* remains contested.

### D'Oraita Status

Finally, the *Gemara*'s treatment of *samuch l'vesta* leaves open a fundamental question: is the obligation to separate near the expected period *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan*? A verse is cited — *ve-hizartem* — but the rhetorical register of the surrounding *aggadic* material — "if you do this you will have male children," "if you perform *havdalah* on wine you will have male children" — suggests something other than a hard normative requirement. It reads more like counsel and reward than categorical *halacha*. This question is indeed debated among the *Rishonim*. What is clear from the *Gemara*'s earlier analysis is that a couple who engages in intercourse *samuch l'vesta* and she then becomes a *niddah* is characterized as *poshea* — negligent — and they bring a *korban chatas*. That carries *d'Oraita* implications regardless of how one classifies the underlying obligation. Whether they have violated a formal *halacha* or have merely been reckless with a serious risk is the point the *poskim* continue to debate.
