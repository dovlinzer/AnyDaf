# Shevuot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumat Mikdash v'Niddah

### Asei v'Lo Ta'asei

We open on 18a continuing the discussion of the *mishnah*'s treatment of *tumat mikdash v'kodashov* — the laws governing one who enters the *Beis Hamikdash* in a state of *tumah*. The *mishnah* established that if one does so *b'shogeg*, one brings a sliding-scale *korban* known as an *oleh veyored*. The *mishnah* further stated that one is *chayav* for *asei v'lo ta'asei shebe-Mikdash* in one sense, and *chayav* for *asei v'lo ta'asei shebe-niddah* in another — a formulation that, as the *Gemara* recognizes, requires considerable unpacking.

### Horayos Parallel

The *Gemara* connects this *mishnah* to a parallel discussion in Horayos, which addresses the *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur* — the special *korban* brought when the *Beis Din HaGadol* issues an erroneous ruling and the *tzibbur* acts upon it. That *korban* applies only where the underlying sin carries a fixed *chatas*, and therefore would not apply if the erroneous ruling concerned *tumat mikdash v'kodashov*, since that offense carries an *oleh veyored* rather than a fixed *chatas*. It would, however, apply if the ruling concerned *niddah*, since that offense does carry a fixed *chatas*. The *Gemara*'s choice to juxtapose these two areas of law is not arbitrary — both involve *tumah* and an obligation to separate or withdraw oneself, and that structural similarity seems to be the basis for the comparison.

### Rif's Approach

The peculiarity of finding an extended treatment of *niddah* law embedded in the tractate of Shevuot prompted the Rif to anchor his *hilchos niddah* here rather than in Taharos, on which he did not write. Since the *Gemara* on our *daf* develops the relevant obligations in considerable depth, the Rif uses this passage as the locus for his entire treatment of *hilchos niddah*, expanding outward from the discussion here.

## Chayav Shtayim

### Akhnisa v'Aprishah

The *mishnah* addresses a case in which a husband is engaged in intercourse with his wife and she informs him mid-act that she has become a *niddah*. If he separates immediately while still erect, he is *chayav* — because withdrawal while erect constitutes an act of intercourse in its own right, conferring upon him the same degree of pleasure as entry. The *Gemara* now undertakes to determine precisely how many *chataos* can result from this scenario.

The *Gemara* cites a tradition: *itmar Abaye amar mishmei deRabbi Chiya bar Rav chayav shtayim* — Abaye, citing Rabbi Chiya bar Rav, holds that the man is liable for two *chataos*. This is seconded by Rava citing Shmuel bar Shilas citing Rav Huna, who likewise holds *chayav shtayim*. The two obligations correspond to *akhnisa v'aprishah* — one for the initial act of intercourse and one for the withdrawal.

### Rava's Query

Rava then challenges the formulation: *bemai* — in what precise scenario do we achieve two distinct obligations? The obvious candidate is a case of *samuch l'vesta* — where they engaged in intercourse at a time when the woman was near her expected period — and where, as we will see, the obligation flows from the man's negligence. But Rava probes which type of man we are dealing with, since the characterization of his conduct as *shogeg*, *meizid*, or *oness* turns critically on his knowledge of *halacha*.

### Talmid Chacham

If the man is a *talmid chacham* who knows *halacha*, one can understand why he would be *chayav* for the initial act: *bishlama knisah lechayiv kasavar yachol lavo* — he thought he could manage to withdraw before she would become a *niddah*, and therefore he was a willful violator of the safeguard, but only negligent (*shogeg*) with respect to the actual prohibition of *niddah* itself. He knew he was transgressing the protective fence but believed the underlying prohibition would not be triggered. That negligence with respect to the actual prohibition of *niddah* makes him a *poshea*, and a *poshea* is *chayav* a *korban*.

But the problem arises with the *aprishah* — the withdrawal. If he is a *talmid chacham*, then at the moment she informs him she is a *niddah*, he knows that he must not separate immediately; he knows the *halacha* that requires him to wait. If he separates immediately anyway, that is *meizid* — a willful violation — and a *meizid* does not bring a *chatas*.

Turning to 18a: if instead the man is an *am ha'aretz* — someone ignorant of *halacha* — then both violations collapse into one. His initial entry when she was near her period and his subsequent withdrawal while erect are both *b'shogeg*, but they occurred within a single continuous state of unawareness, analogous to eating two *kezeitim* of forbidden fat in a single *he'elem* — one continuous state of ignorance — which yields only one *korban chatas*. Although one might object that when she informs him she is a *niddah*, he gains awareness mid-act and should therefore be obligated for a separate *chatas*, Rashi explains that if he is an *am ha'aretz*, he does not realize that his initial act of intercourse — engaging near the expected period — was itself wrongful. He does not understand that he was negligent before. So when she informs him, he has no sense that the first part of his conduct was culpable. He acquires awareness only later, and that awareness comes too late to separate the two acts into two distinct obligations.

So the *Gemara* concludes that a case of *chayav shtayim* cannot be constructed if we are either dealing with a *talmid chacham* in a *samuch l'vesta* scenario, or an *am ha'aretz* in either scenario.

### Samuch L'Vesta

The *Gemara* therefore proposes: *le'olam besamuch l'vesta uve'talmid chacham* — the case is indeed *samuch l'vesta* and the man is indeed a *talmid chacham* — but the *talmid chacham* distinction is applied unevenly. He is a *talmid chacham* with respect to the first *halacha* — knowing that one should not engage in intercourse when the woman is near her expected period — but not a *talmid chacham* with respect to the second *halacha* — he does not know that he must wait for his erection to subside before withdrawing. He is learned in one area and ignorant in another. That asymmetric knowledge is what generates two separate *chataos*: the first for the initial negligence (*shogeg* regarding the prohibition of *niddah* itself, even though *meizid* regarding the safeguard), and the second for the withdrawal while erect, on which he is *shogeg* because he does not know that *halacha* at all.

### Tanu Rabbanan

Rava then notes that both obligations are in fact already attested in existing *mishnayos*: *vitravayhu taninhu* — we have taught both of them. The obligation of *aprishah* — that withdrawal while erect constitutes an act of intercourse — is explicit in our *mishnah*: a man was having intercourse with a woman *she'einah niddah*, she informed him *nitmeti*, he separated immediately — *chayav*. And the obligation that arises from the initial act of negligence — *knisah* — is attested in a *mishnah* in Niddah: *nimtze'u al shelo tme'im vechayavin bekorban* — if after separation the couple examines themselves and he finds blood on his cloth, they are *tamei* and *chayav* a *korban*. The blood on his cloth is proof she was a *niddah* during intercourse. The *Gemara* suggests that the only coherent explanation of that case is *besamuch l'vesta* — they were engaged near her expected period — which is why his negligence generates a *chiyuv*.

### Rav Acha's Objection

Rav Acha bar Matna objects to Rava's reading of the *mishnah* in Niddah. He argues: *le'olam eima lach beshelo samuch levistah vehafrishah* — perhaps the case in the Niddah *mishnah* is not *samuch l'vesta* at all, and the obligation does not arise from the initial act but from the act of *aprishah* — the separation. Rava's attempt to use that *mishnah* as proof of an obligation for initial negligence may be misplaced.

Rav Acha acknowledges, however, that this reading requires him to explain why that *mishnah* was needed at all if we already know the obligation of withdrawal from our *mishnah* here. His answer: the real *chiddush* of the Niddah *mishnah* is the other half of the case — *nimtze'u al shelah tme'im besafek ufturin min hakorban* — that if blood is found on her cloth after separation, they are only *tamei besafek* and exempt from a *korban*, because the bleeding may have begun after the separation. That is the true novelty, and the *mishnah* also mentions the case of blood on his cloth only in order to contrast it. The actual *chiyuv* in that first case, Rav Acha proposes, may be due to the act of withdrawal itself rather than initial negligence.

### Ravina's Logic

Ravina responds to Rav Acha's position sharply: *mi matzis le'ukmeh hachi beshelo samuch l'vista u'farsha* — how can you explain the case as one of *aprishah* when the *mishnah* says *nimtza* — it was discovered after the fact? The language *nimtza* implies that he only became aware after he had already separated. If there was no negligence at the outset — no *samuch l'vesta* — then there is no prior culpability. And if his awareness came only at the end, after separation was already complete, he cannot be retroactively obligated for the act of withdrawal, because that withdrawal was performed without any knowledge that she was a *niddah*. *Nimtza* means discovered afterwards — and that timing makes it impossible to ground the obligation in the withdrawal.

Furthermore: *ve-ha-prisha, me-ikara ki paresh lei me-ikara havei lei yedia* — if you insist the obligation arises from the withdrawal, then for him to know to withhold immediate separation, she would have had to inform him *during* intercourse. But if she informed him during intercourse, the *mishnah* would not say he *discovered* it afterward on his cloth. The two readings are mutually exclusive.

### Initial Negligence

Rava closes the exchange: *tsayis mai de'ka'amar rabach* — Rav Adda, listen to your teacher. The attempt to ground the obligation in *aprishah* cannot be sustained. The *Gemara* then engages a *baraita* that states: *zu hi mitzvat asei she-be'niddah she-chayavin aleha* — this is the positive commandment regarding *niddah* for which one is *chayav*. Rava objects: if the obligation at issue is the initial negligence, then it should be described as a *lo ta'asei*, a negative prohibition — the prohibition against relations with a *niddah* — not a positive commandment. Rav Acha responds: if necessary, emend the *baraita* and read it as *mitzvat lo ta'asei she-be'niddah*. The *baraita* can then be read in two parts: the case in which a man engages in intercourse and she informs him she is a *niddah* and he separates immediately — that is grounded in the negative prohibition; and the case that emerges from the *mishnah* in Niddah, where the obligation is characterized as an *asei*, the positive commandment of *prisha* — separating in the proper manner when one learns one's wife is a *niddah*.

What is notable here is the structural parallel to the case of *tumat mikdash*: just as the *tamei* person in the *Beis Hamikdash* is obligated by a positive commandment to leave — and to leave in the most expeditious way — so too the husband is obligated by a positive commandment to separate, though paradoxically the correct mode of separation here requires *waiting* rather than haste.

## How to Separate

### Waiting for Detumescence

The *Gemara* now addresses the practical question: if immediate withdrawal is *chayav*, what *should* the man do? Rav Huna citing Rava answers: *no'etz eser tzipornav bakarka ad sheyamut* — he should dig his fingernails into the ground, remain perfectly still, and wait until the erection subsides before withdrawing. This is the proper method of *prisha*.

### Abaye's Position

Rava then draws a further inference: *zos omeres hameshammesh meis ba'arayos patur* — this ruling implies that intercourse performed with a flaccid member does not constitute a prohibited act of intercourse that generates liability. For if it did, one would have to explain the exemption when the man withdraws after waiting as being due to *ones* — compulsion — rather than the absence of an act altogether. But as the *Gemara* demonstrates, that *ones* explanation creates its own problem.

### Minimizing Pleasure

Abaye disputes this inference. In his view, intercourse with a flaccid member does constitute an act of intercourse. And here, when the man waits for detumescence and then withdraws, he is indeed technically performing an act, but he is exempt because he is an *oness* — he has no choice but to withdraw eventually. The reason he is *chayav* when he withdraws immediately while still erect is that *shehaya lo lifrosh behana'ah mu'etes upirash behana'ah merubah* — he could have performed the separation in a manner that minimized his pleasure, and instead he chose to do it in a manner that maximized it. The availability of a better option means he cannot invoke *ones*. When he waits, he has done everything possible to minimize the violation, and at that point he truly is an *oness* with no remaining choice.

### Aruka v'Ketzara

Rava bar Chanan raises an analogy: *im kein matzinu arucha u'ketzara b'niddah* — according to Abaye's position, we now find the same principle of "long path and short path" (*aruka v'ketzara*) applying to *niddah* that applies in the case of the *Beis Hamikdash*. A *tamei* person who must exit the *Beis Hamikdash* and takes the longer route rather than the shorter one is *chayav*; similarly, separating immediately (the "short path") is *chayav*, while waiting for detumescence (the "long path") is *patur*. But the *Gemara* had stated that the concept of *aruka v'ketzara* applies only in the context of the *Mikdash* — suggesting that in *niddah* the same principle does not operate.

The *Gemara* resolves this: there is no contradiction, only an inversion. In the *Mikdash*, the obligation is to leave as quickly as possible — so haste is *patur* and delay is *chayav*. In *niddah*, the obligation is to delay before separating — so waiting is *patur* and haste is *chayav*. The directions are reversed, but the underlying principle — that the manner of extrication determines liability — is perfectly analogous.

### Rav Huna Challenges

Rav Huna bar Rav Natan challenges Abaye's position directly. If the exemption for waiting until detumescence rests on *ones*, that claim of *ones* is unavailable in the standard case of the *mishnah*, where the act began *samuch l'vesta* — near the expected period — because the couple's negligence at the outset destroys any subsequent claim of compulsion. One cannot say *ones* when one created the dangerous situation through one's own *peshia*. Therefore, if Abaye maintains that waiting until detumescence still constitutes an act of intercourse, he cannot use *ones* to exempt it in the case where the whole encounter began negligently.

### Two Cases Distinguished

The *Gemara* resolves the tension by distinguishing the two cases. When Abaye said *chayav shtayim* — that the man is liable twice — he was speaking about a general scenario, not specifically about the *mishnah*'s case. The *mishnah*'s case, in which she informs him mid-act and he separates immediately, is one in which the encounter could have begun without any negligence — *beshelo samuch l'vesta* — and therefore the *ones* claim remains intact for the act of waiting and withdrawing. Abaye's ruling of *chayav shtayim*, by contrast, describes a case where the encounter *did* begin with negligence (*samuch l'vesta*), and the two obligations arise from the initial negligence and the improper withdrawal respectively. The two scenarios are analytically distinct, and Abaye's position is coherent in both.

## Sources & Warnings

### Azhara L'Boel Niddah

The *Gemara* transitions to a discussion of the scriptural sources for the relevant prohibitions. Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosei ben Lakunya posed a question to Rabbi Shimon ben Yosei ben Lakunya — presumably his brother — asking: *azhara l'boel niddah minayin* — where in the Torah is the prohibition against intercourse with a *niddah*? Rabbi Shimon's reaction was to throw a clump of earth at him — the question was so obvious as to be almost insulting.

### Lo Tikrav

The answer, of course, is the explicit verse: *v'el isha b'niddat tumatah lo tikrav* — "And to a woman in her *niddah* impurity you shall not draw near." That is the *azhara* for the prohibition. But Rabbi Yonatan clarifies that his question was not about the basic prohibition — that much is elementary. What he was asking about was the source for the *halacha* we have been discussing all along: how do we know that a man who is engaged in intercourse and learns that his wife has become a *niddah* may not separate immediately?

### Utehi Niddatah

Hizkiyah answers from the verse *utehi niddatah alav* — "and her *niddah* state shall be upon him." While this verse primarily speaks to the transfer of *tumah*, the *Gemara* reads it as establishing that even at the moment she becomes a *niddah*, the responsibility (*alav*) is upon him — he must manage the separation responsibly. *Ashkachan asei* — this establishes the positive obligation. But whence the negative prohibition against immediate withdrawal?

### Rav Pappa's Reading

Rav Pappa derives the negative prohibition from the same verse used earlier: *lo tikrav* — "you shall not draw near." He reads *lo tikrav* as also meaning *lo tifrosh* — "you shall not withdraw" — in the sense of improper separation. His proof is from a different verse: *ha'omrim krav elecha al tigash bi ki kedashticha* — "those who say 'krav' to you, do not approach me, for I have made you holy" — where *krav* is used in the sense of withdrawing or standing apart from others. Rashi explains this as *amod b'atzmecha* — stand by yourself, away from others. The reading is admittedly not *peshat*, and the *Gemara* seems to require it precisely because the notion that the act of separation itself constitutes a forbidden act of intercourse is such a striking *chiddush* that a specific scriptural basis is warranted.

## Safeguard Period

### V'Hizartem

A *baraita* is cited: *tanu rabbanan — ve-hizartem et b'nei yisrael mi-tumatam*. Rabbi Yoshiyah derives from this verse — "you shall separate (*hizartem*) the children of Israel from their *tumah*" — an obligation upon men to separate from their wives near the time the wife's period is expected: *mikan azhara livnei yisrael sheyifreshu minshoteihem samuch l'vastan*. The word *ve-hizartem* connotes creating separation and building a fence, analogous to the term *nezorah achor* — to draw back. Rabbi Yishmael is cited alongside Rabbi Yoshiyah in this context as a parallel authority.

### One Onah

Rava defines the duration of this separation: one *onah* — a defined period of either daytime or nighttime. The *poskim* understand this to mean that if the woman is expected to begin her period during daytime hours, the couple refrains for the entire day-period, from approximately dawn to nightfall; if she is expected to begin at night, they refrain for the entire night-period. This formalism produces what might seem like an arbitrary result — the prohibited window could be as little as a few minutes or as long of the full twelve-hour block — but that is how *halacha* has understood the *tosefet onah* here.

### Severe Consequences

Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, issues a severe warning: *kol she'eino poresh me'ishto samuch l'visto afilu havyu lo banim kivnei Aharon meisim* — any man who does not observe this separation near his wife's expected period, even if his children were as great as the children of Aharon, they will die. The scriptural hook: *ve-hizartem et b'nei yisrael mi-tumatam* is immediately followed in the Torah by *v'lo yamutu b'tumtam b'tamma'am et mishkani asher b'tocham* — lest they die in their impurity by defiling My *mishkan* — and then the passage of *niddah* and *zav* concludes with *v'hadavah b'niddatah*. That juxtaposition of the warning to separate with the threat of death forms the basis of Rabbi Yochanan's statement. That this warning appears at the very close of the *parasha* of Metzora, with the very next verse opening Acharei Mot with the death of the sons of Aharon, reinforces the connection.

### Rewards for Compliance

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan offers the positive formulation: *kol haporesh me'ishto samuch l'visto havyu lo banim zachar* — one who does observe the separation near his wife's expected period will merit male children. The source: *l'havdil bein hatamei u'vein hatahor* — to distinguish between impure and pure — and the very next verse in Tazria-Metzora reads *ishah ki tazria v'yalda zachar* — a woman who conceives and gives birth to a male. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers an alternative formulation of the reward: *banim re'uyim l'hora'ah* — sons fit for *hora'ah*, for rendering halachic decisions — derived from *l'havdil u'lehorot*, to separate and to instruct.

### Havdalah Parallel

The *Gemara* then applies the same textual derivation to a related context. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan states: *kol hamavdil al hayayin b'motza'ei Shabbatot havyu lo banim zachar* — anyone who recites *havdalah* over wine on *Motza'ei Shabbos* will merit male children. The connection: *l'havdil bein hakodesh u'vein hachol* — the language of *havdalah* between sacred and mundane — is linked via *gezeira shava* to *l'havdil bein hatamei u'vein hatahor*, which leads again to *ishah ki tazria v'yalda zachar*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi again adds: *banim re'uyim l'hora'ah*, derived from *l'havdil u'lehorot*.

### Mekadesh Atzmo

Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet citing Rabbi Elazar teaches: *kol hamekadesh et atzmo b'sha'at tashmish havyu lo banim zachar* — one who sanctifies himself at the time of intercourse will merit male children, as it says *ve-hitkadishtem viheyitem kedoshim*, and the adjacent verse is again *ishah ki tazria v'yalda zachar*. What exactly *mekadesh atzmo b'sha'at tashmish* means is a matter of significant dispute among the *poskim*. Some have read it as a call for asceticism during the marital act. Many others strongly disagree with that interpretation. A particularly compelling approach, grounded in the Gra, understands it to mean that one fully honors the *tzelem Elokim* of one's spouse — that one is not exclusively focused on one's own desires but genuinely attends to the other person's needs and personhood. The precise meaning of this *kedushah* in the context of *tashmish* remains contested.

### D'Oraita Status

Finally, the *Gemara*'s treatment of *samuch l'vesta* leaves open a fundamental question: is the obligation to separate near the expected period *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan*? A verse is cited — *ve-hizartem* — but the rhetorical register of the surrounding *aggadic* material — "if you do this you will have male children," "if you perform *havdalah* on wine you will have male children" — suggests something other than a hard normative requirement. It reads more like counsel and reward than categorical *halacha*. This question is indeed debated among the *Rishonim*. What is clear from the *Gemara*'s earlier analysis is that a couple who engages in intercourse *samuch l'vesta* and she then becomes a *niddah* is characterized as *poshea* — negligent — and they bring a *korban chatas*. That carries *d'Oraita* implications regardless of how one classifies the underlying obligation. Whether they have violated a formal *halacha* or have merely been reckless with a serious risk is the point the *poskim* continue to debate.
