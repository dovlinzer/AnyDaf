# Yoma 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sick Person vs. Doctor

### Choleh vs. Rofeh

The daf opens with the *Gemara*'s discussion of the *Mishnah*'s statement that a sick person may be fed on Yom Kippur *al pi beki'in* — based on the ruling of experts. The *Mishnah* had already addressed the case of a pregnant woman seized by an irresistible craving, but it generalizes from there to the case of any sick person, specifically invoking the testimony of qualified physicians. Rabbi Yannai then introduces a striking ruling: if the *choleh* says he needs to eat and the doctor says he does not, *shomin lacholeh* — you listen to the sick person. Conversely, if the doctor says he needs to eat and the *choleh* says he does not, you listen to the doctor. In either case, the minority voice in favor of feeding prevails.

### Lev Yodea Marat

The reason Rabbi Yannai gives for following the *choleh* against the doctor is the verse *lev yodea marat nafsho* — "the heart knows the bitterness of its own soul." The patient has privileged access to his own subjective experience in a way no outside observer does. This is a remarkable *chidush*. One might have thought that knowing how one feels is distinct from knowing what one medically requires. A person may feel desperately hungry, or feel that he is starving, without that feeling accurately indicating that eating is in fact the correct medical intervention. The doctors, after all, may know that feeding him at this moment would actually worsen his condition. Yet Rabbi Yannai rules that the *choleh*'s self-assessment overrides expert medical opinion — and not merely as testimony to his subjective state, but as a determination of what is medically necessary for him.

### Rav Moshe Feinstein

This principle has been developed extensively by later *poskim*. Rav Moshe Feinstein, in a number of *teshuvot*, consistently weighs a person's psychological state of well-being as a central factor in *pikuach nefesh* determinations. Cases he addresses include whether a family member may violate *Shabbat* to accompany a patient in the hospital who feels he urgently needs that presence, or whether a feeding tube may be forced on a patient who is violently rejecting it. In many such cases, Rav Moshe's position is that the patient's own sense of his needs — including his psychological needs — is dispositive, and that psychological well-being is not peripheral but central to the *pikuach nefesh* calculation. These rulings stand in direct continuity with Rabbi Yannai's foundational principle.

### Assessment vs. Psychology

It is worth distinguishing two separate issues that converge here. The first is epistemic: who is in the best position to assess what the patient requires? Rabbi Yannai's *chidush* is that on this question, the *choleh* himself wins — *lev yodea marat nafsho*. The second issue is not about assessment but about psychological state itself: how does a patient's emotional condition factor into the definition of medical necessity? This second dimension is more prominent in the case of the pregnant woman from the earlier *Mishnah*, where there was no contradicting expert opinion and the driving concern may have been the woman's powerful psychological need. In the case of Rabbi Yannai, the focus is on the first issue — the *choleh*'s ability to assess his own condition — even when a doctor says otherwise. Both dimensions, taken together, articulate a powerful halachic principle: the patient's own voice carries enormous weight, even against the testimony of outside expertise.

### Pshita Response

The *Gemara* then says *pshita* — this seems obvious. The novelty of Rabbi Yannai's ruling, which had just occupied considerable analysis, is brushed aside with the comment: one might have thought *rofe kim lei tefai*, that the doctor simply knows better, so the *Gemara* must teach us otherwise. It is striking that the *Gemara* treats this as needing to be said explicitly, since in contexts where medical and scientific authority carry near-absolute weight, the intuition that the doctor knows better is quite natural. Nevertheless, that is precisely the *chidush*.

## Reconciling Sources

### Mishnah's Position

The *Gemara* then raises a contradiction from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* says *choleh ma'achilin oto al pi beki'in* — a sick person is fed based on what the experts say — which seems to imply two things that conflict with Rabbi Yannai. First, it is the doctors' opinion that matters, not the patient's own word — *al pi atzmo lo*. Second, the plural *beki'in* suggests that at least two experts are required — *al pi baki echad lo*.

### Chisurei Mechsera

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking *chisurei mechsera* — the *Mishnah* is missing words and must be read as containing an implicit clause. The *Mishnah*, properly understood, is not addressing the case where the *choleh* himself says he needs to eat. It is addressing the case where the *choleh* says he does not need to eat — and in that situation, the doctor's testimony is required to override him.

### Two Doctors Needed

But even granting that, why does the *Mishnah* require two doctors? If the *choleh* says he does not need to eat and one doctor says he does, why is one doctor insufficient? The *Gemara* explains that the *Mishnah* is addressing a case where not only the *choleh* but also one doctor says he does not need to be fed. In that configuration — the *choleh* plus one doctor against feeding — two doctors are needed on the other side to override them.

The *Gemara* initially asks why this is not obvious: surely *safeik nefashos l'hakel*, in any case of doubtful life-threat you are lenient? The *Gemara* answers that the novelty lies in a case where three say not to feed — two doctors and the *choleh* — and only two say to feed. One might have thought that because we are dealing with *umdena*, expert assessment rather than formal testimony, we follow majority opinion, as Rav Safra ruled in monetary contexts where you do follow the weight of numbers among assessors (*batar de'ot azlinan*). Perhaps even in a *pikuach nefesh* context the three-against-two majority would control. The *Gemara* rejects this: *hani mili le'inyan umdana demamona* — that principle applies to monetary assessments, but here it is *safeik nefashos*, and you are always lenient, feeding him based on the two doctors who say to do so.

This raises further questions that the *Gemara* does not fully resolve: what is the relevant threshold of expertise (*beki'in*)? Does the *choleh*'s own statement count as equal to a doctor's? What happens if one hundred doctors say not to feed and only two say to feed? The baseline answer that emerges is that you are always lenient — two opinions in favor of feeding outweigh any number against, once you have the minimum of corroboration. The one limitation that appears is the case of a single opinion in favor against multiple opposing opinions: there, it seems, you would not break Yom Kippur, because one uncorroborated opinion cannot carry the weight.

### Mar bar Rav Ashi

This limitation is itself contested. Mar bar Rav Ashi takes Rabbi Yannai's principle to its logical extreme: *kol heicha de'amar tzarich ani afilu ika me'ah de'amrei lo tzarich, bedidei shami'nan* — wherever the *choleh* himself says he needs to eat, even if a hundred people say he does not, you listen to him, as the verse says *lev yodea marat nafsho*. According to this view, no quantity of outside expert opinion can override the patient's own determination of his need.

### Reinterpreting Mishnah

This creates a difficulty with the *Mishnah*'s statement that when there are no experts (*ein sham beki'im*) you follow the *choleh* — implying that when experts are present, you would follow them against him. Mar bar Rav Ashi resolves this creatively: the *Mishnah*'s phrase *ein sham beki'im* does not mean there are no doctors in the room. It means that when the *choleh* says he needs to eat, expert testimony is as if it does not exist — *al pi atzmo ma'achilin oto*, you feed him based on his own word, and the experts are simply irrelevant to that determination. The only role for experts, on this reading, is when the *choleh* himself says he does not need to eat — then doctors can establish that he does.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is thus remarkably lenient: if the *choleh* says he needs to eat, you feed him regardless of how many doctors disagree; if the doctor says he needs to eat, you feed him regardless of what the *choleh* says. You are always lenient in the direction of *pikuach nefesh*. The sole possible constraint — one opinion against many, without corroboration — may be a limitation only according to the intermediate position, and is arguably dissolved entirely by Mar bar Rav Ashi.

## What Doctors Say

### Rashi's View

Having established the framework of who is listened to, the *Gemara* turns to a question that is equally important in practice: what exactly must the doctor say to trigger the permission to violate Yom Kippur? Rashi's apparent position is that the doctor must state that if the patient is not fed, he may die — a very high threshold.

### Resh's Correction

The *Rosh* sharply disagrees with this reading and quotes the *Tosafot* as well. He argues that requiring the doctor to predict death is an unreasonable stringency, since no doctor speaks that way. Doctors do not say: "If you don't feed him, he might die." Rather, as the *Rosh* puts it in his characteristic formulation, *rofe darko lomar* — the way doctors talk is to say: if you don't feed him, it is possible that his condition will become complicated and that will expose him to risk. The *Rosh* rules that this is sufficient. If the patient is already in a situation of *safeik pikuach nefesh*, then anything that could aggravate that situation and increase the exposure to danger is itself considered *pikuach nefesh* — you do not need the doctor to predict death. Complication and increased risk exposure suffice.

### Practical Implications

This distinction is of enormous practical importance. A doctor consulting on a Yom Kippur case will almost never say categorically that the patient will die if not fed. He will say the situation may worsen, or that fasting creates risk of complications. According to the *Rosh*, that is precisely enough. The threshold is not certain death but meaningful aggravation of a dangerous situation — and that standard, as the *Rosh* emphasizes, is what reflects how medical professionals actually communicate.

## Bulmus Case

### Bulmus Definition

The *Mishnah* introduces a new case: *mi she-achazo bulmus* — someone seized by *bulmus*. The term is related etymologically to *bulimia* and in its medical literature refers to a compulsive eating state, but in the *Mishnah* it appears to describe something different: a condition in which a person feels a powerful, urgent need to consume food and does not stabilize until eating to a certain point. The physical sign used by the *Gemara* to measure recovery is *ya'iru einav* — "his eyes light up." Apparently the person's vision becomes clouded or unfocused, and after eating sufficiently, his eyes clear. This has led some to suggest — consistent with the Gemara's own interest in sweetness as the remedy — that the condition reflects a drop in blood sugar, similar to the hypoglycemic episodes one can observe today where a glass of orange juice rapidly revives a person who has become disoriented and vacant.

### Non-Kosher Feeding

The *Mishnah* rules: *ma'achilin oto afilu devarim teme'im* — you feed someone seized by *bulmus* even non-kosher food, until *ya'iru einav*, until his eyes light up. This generalizes the *pikuach nefesh* principle beyond Yom Kippur itself.

### Dietary Law Violation

The *Mishnah* here is no longer exclusively about Yom Kippur. The principle that danger to life overrides prohibitions is extended to the violation of dietary laws in any context where someone's life is at risk. The case of *bulmus* thus serves as a transition: from Yom Kippur, to *treifus*, and eventually to *Shabbat* — each expanded in turn.

## Folk Remedies Debate

### Rabid Dog Case

The *Mishnah* then presents the case of *mi she-neshacho kelev shoteh* — someone bitten by a rabid dog. The classic folk remedy was to feed the patient a section of the dog's liver (*chatzer kaved shelo*). The first opinion in the *Mishnah* rules that you do not feed it to him, because it is non-kosher and it is not a medically legitimate treatment — it is merely a folk remedy. The implication is that the rabbis are empowered, and indeed obligated, to assess whether a proposed treatment constitutes real medicine or mere superstition. Only genuine medical necessity justifies violating a prohibition.

### Rabbi Authority

This raises a pointed question about the rabbi's role in *halachic* decision-making: is it the rabbi's place to rule that a given treatment is *narishkeit* and therefore cannot justify a prohibition? The tension is real. On the one hand, the *Gemara* itself contains numerous remedies that from a modern perspective would appear equally fanciful. On the other hand, someone has to make a determination, and if rabbis defer entirely to any claimed medical benefit, the protections of *issur* erode.

### Scorpion Remedy

This question has direct contemporary application. A student who suffered from Bell's palsy — partial facial paralysis — had tried conventional treatments without success and was advised by a holistic practitioner to brew a tea using desiccated scorpion: hot water run through a teabag containing the desiccated insect, producing what amounts to a *ta'am ke'ikar* of scorpion. He asked whether he could drink it. The question involved *treif* status, issues of *bitul*, *nosein ta'am lifgam*, and whether the extreme bitterness of the remedy (requiring large amounts of sugar and honey to make it palatable) affected the ruling. After consulting with colleagues, the conclusion was that the halachic obstacles were surmountable — but the broader question of whether the rabbi should have simply dismissed the remedy as *narishkeit* remained uncomfortable. When Rav Yehuda Herzl Henkin was consulted and asked what kind of remedy this was, the response was skeptical — but the challenge remained: who are we to adjudicate what counts as effective medicine, given the *Gemara*'s own pharmacopoeia?

### Avoiding Narishkeit

The underlying question is whether a rabbi should refuse to engage with a question on the grounds that the underlying premise — this treatment has medical validity — is implausible. The *Gemara* itself seems to treat this as a substantive question on which *tannaim* could disagree, with the first opinion in the *Mishnah* essentially ruling that the liver-of-the-dog remedy does not qualify as medicine and therefore cannot override the prohibition.

### Rabbi Mattia

Rabbi Mattia ben Charash permits feeding the dog's liver to someone who was bitten. His permissive position reflects a broader leniency: where there is any plausible medical rationale, the prohibition can be set aside for *pikuach nefesh*.

### Throat Concern

Rabbi Mattia ben Charash adds a further ruling: *hachoshesh bigrono matilin lo sam betoch piv beShabbat* — if someone has a throat ailment, you may administer medicine into his mouth on *Shabbat*, because *safeik nefashos* overrides *Shabbat*. He generalizes: any concern involving the throat is treated as a potential *pikuach nefesh*. What is notable about this ruling is its placement — it reads almost like a general introductory principle that should have opened the entire discussion, but appears here embedded within Rabbi Mattia's specific legal positions.

## Collapsed Building

### Triple Safeik

The *Mishnah* now introduces the well-known case of the collapsed building: *mi she-nafla alav mapoles* — someone upon whom a building has fallen. The *Mishnah* stacks three separate doubts: *safeik hu sham safeik eino sham* — perhaps the person was not even in the building when it collapsed; *safeik chai safeik meit* — perhaps he was there but has already died; *safeik Goy safeik Yisrael* — perhaps the person, even if alive, is not Jewish, which raises the separate question of whether *Shabbat* is overridden to save a non-Jew's life. Each doubt individually could argue against desecrating *Shabbat*; together, they form a *safeik safeik safeika*. Yet the *Mishnah* rules: *mefakchin alav es hagal* — you uncover the rubble.

### Safeik Safeik

The *chidush* is that even a compound series of doubts does not prevent rescue. The principle of *safeik nefashos l'hakel* — when there is any doubt about life, you are lenient — operates even through multiple layers of uncertainty. The *Mishnah* does not permit waiting to resolve each doubt in sequence.

### Alive vs. Dead

If when the rubble is cleared the person is found alive, you continue the work of rescue even though it means continued *Shabbat* violation. If he is found dead, you stop and leave the remaining rubble until after *Shabbat*. This is the straightforward application of the principle.

### Determining Death

The *chidush* that requires most attention is how death is determined. The *Gemara* makes clear that the standard for confirming death is the absence of breath and responsiveness — the cessation of vital function — and this determination must be made carefully before ceasing rescue efforts.

## Hierarchy of Violations

### Bulmus Feeding

The *Mishnah* adds that when someone is seized by *bulmus* and must be fed, one does not simply reach for whatever food is available. A new principle operates: *ma'achilim oto hakal hakal* — you feed him with the lesser prohibition first.

### Tevel vs. Nevelah

If the choice is between *tevel* — grain from which *terumot u'ma'asrot* have not been separated — and *nevelah* — an animal that was not slaughtered through proper *shechitah*, you give him the *nevelah*. Although both are forbidden, *nevelah* carries only a *lav*, a negative prohibition, while *tevel* carries the penalty of *chayav mitah bidei shamayim*, death at the hands of Heaven.

### Tevel vs. Shvi'it

If the choice is between *tevel* and *shvi'it* produce — sabbatical-year food that is past the point at which it should have been destroyed — you give him the *shvi'it* produce. The obligation to destroy *shvi'it* produce after its time is a *mitzvat aseh*, a positive commandment, which is less severe than the *lo ta'aseh* of consuming *tevel*.

### Tevel vs. Terumah Debate

The more complex case is *tevel* versus *terumah*. Both carry the penalty of *mitah bidei shamayim*, so neither is obviously lighter than the other. This is the subject of a *tannaitic* dispute. The first opinion says you feed him *tevel* rather than *terumah*. Ben Teima says the opposite — you feed him *terumah* rather than *tevel*.

### Rava's Distinction

Rava clarifies the terms of the debate. If it is possible to separate *terumah* from the grain and feed him the remaining *chulin*, everyone agrees you do that — you take the small portion as *terumah* and give him the bulk as fully permitted food. The dispute applies only where separation is insufficient: where the patient needs the full quantity including what would become *terumah* and *terumat ma'aser*, so that he must eat some portion that is categorically forbidden. In that case, one opinion holds *tevel chamur* — *tevel* is the more severe prohibition — so better to give him *terumah*, which at least is permitted to a *kohen* and thus not absolutely *treif* in all circumstances. The other opinion holds *terumah chamurah* — *terumah* is more severe — because *tevel* can theoretically be rectified: *tevel efshar letakunei*, the status of *tevel* is not permanent, it can be fixed by separation, and therefore it is not treated as fully realized *issur*. On this view, *tevel* is technically the lesser violation.

### Avoiding Slippage

The *Gemara* then raises a question about the case where separation is possible and the prohibition being avoided is only rabbinic. Specifically, what if the *tevel* grew in a sealed flower pot (*atzitz she'eino nakuv*) — disconnected from the ground — making the *tevel* status only rabbinic? Now you have a choice between two *derabanan* violations: feed him rabbinically forbidden *tevel*, or separate *terumah* on *Shabbat* (which is a rabbinic prohibition) and feed him fully permitted food. Which is preferable?

The answer is that it is better to violate the rabbinic *Shabbat* restriction by separating *terumah* than to feed him the rabbinically forbidden food. The reason, as the *Gemara* develops it elsewhere in a related discussion, is not merely about the visceral sense that ingesting forbidden food is particularly serious — though that intuition exists — but about slippage. If one permits eating rabbinic *tevel*, a person may come to permit eating biblical *tevel*; the two categories are easily confused. By contrast, performing the rabbinic act of separating *terumah* on *Shabbat* does not generate the same risk of slippage into violating a biblical *melachah*. The principle therefore is: prefer the *derabanan* violation that does not risk confusion with a *de'oraita*, over the *derabanan* that does.

## Snake Bite Remedies

### Multiple Violations

The *Gemara* introduces a *beraita* about someone who has been bitten by a snake: *korin lo rofe mimakom lemakom* — you summon a doctor even from a distant place, involving *Shabbat* travel; *umekarin lo et hatarnegolet* — you tear open a chicken and apply it to the wound, a traditional remedy for snake bites; *uvegozezin lo et hakreishin* — and you harvest cress, apparently another remedy used for snakebites, even though harvesting on *Shabbat* is a *melachah*. All of these are permitted *Shabbat* violations for *pikuach nefesh*.

### Terumot Dispute

The *beraita* then records a dispute about *terumot u'ma'asrot*. The view attributed to *Rabbi* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) is *ve'ein tzarikh le'aser* — you do not need to take *terumot u'ma'asrot* from the cress before feeding it to the patient. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon says *lo yokhal ad sheye'aser* — he may not eat it until *terumot u'ma'asrot* are separated. This maps onto the broader debate: is it better to feed the patient *tevel* (even rabbinic *tevel*) or to perform the rabbinic act of separation on *Shabbat* and feed him *chulin*?

### Vegetable vs. Grain

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent conflict between these positions and the analysis of Rava. *Rabbi*'s leniency about not separating applies specifically to vegetable cress, for which the obligation of *terumot u'ma'asrot* is only rabbinic (*ma'asar yerek derabanan*). In that case, feeding him without separation is permissible because eating rabbinic *tevel* of a vegetable will not lead anyone to confuse it with the biblical *tevel* of grain. The slippage risk is minimal. But when it comes to grain — even grain that grew in a sealed flower pot and is therefore only rabbinically *tevel* — the categories are more easily confused with biblical *tevel* of grain (*atzitz she'eino nakuv ati lemikhal be'atzitz shehu nakuv*), so in that case even *Rabbi* would agree that it is better to separate on *Shabbat* and avoid feeding the rabbinically prohibited grain altogether.

## Sweetness Remedies

### Honey Efficacy

The *Gemara* continues with the physiology of *bulmus* recovery. A *beraita* states: *ma'achilim oto dvash ve'chol minei metika* — you feed someone seized by *bulmus* honey and all sweet things, *she'hadvash ve'chol minei metika me'irin maor einav shel adam* — because honey and sweet things light up a person's eyes. This is strikingly consistent with the blood sugar hypothesis: what restores a hypoglycemic person is precisely a rapid infusion of sugar.

### Honey Timing

Abaye adds an important qualification: *lo shanu ela achar achila* — this is effective only after eating, meaning when there is already food in the stomach. *Aval kodem achila dvash al keiva reika migrar garir* — but honey on an empty stomach does not satisfy; it actually stimulates the appetite further and draws out the craving. This is medically interesting: rapid sugar on a completely empty stomach may cause a spike and subsequent crash, rather than stabilization.

### Egyptian Servant

The *Gemara* offers scriptural support from the story of the Egyptian servant found in the field (*Shmuel I* 30). He had not eaten or drunk for three days. David's men gave him bread and water first, and only afterward gave him *pelach devela* — a portion of pressed figs — and two raisins. The sweet foods came after the bread, demonstrating that sweets restore after food is already in the system, not before. The *Gemara* notes that *Yehonatan*'s famous episode with the honey (*Shmuel I* 14) does not constitute a proof for *bulmus* specifically, since Yehonatan was fasting, not in a state of *bulmus* — the honey revived him from the fast, but one cannot draw from that case to the distinct condition described in the *Mishnah*.

### Various Remedies

Rav Nachman citing Shmuel adds: *mi she-achazo bulmus ma'achilim oto alya bi'dvash* — fatty meat, specifically fat-tail, dipped in honey. Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua says that fine flour with honey is also effective — not only fat but carbohydrates combined with sweetness. Rav Papa extends it further: even barley flour with honey will do. The range of remedies emphasizes that what matters is the combination of caloric density and sweetness.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan recounts a personal experience: *pa'am achas achzani bulmus* — once I was seized by *bulmus*, and he ran to the east side of a fig tree and ate figs from that side. The reason, as elaborated by Rav Yosef, is that figs on the eastern side of the tree, exposed most directly to the sun, are sweetest — the sun draws out the sugars (*mimachar tvu'os shamesh*, "from the sweetness of the sun's yield"). Rabbi Yochanan adds that in doing this he fulfilled for himself the verse *ha'chochmah techayeh ba'aleha* — wisdom gives life to those who possess it — since his knowledge guided him in a moment of potential *pikuach nefesh*.

## Rabbinic Stories

### Shepherd Bread

The *Gemara* tells of Rav Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei traveling together. Rav Yehuda was seized by *bulmus* and compelled a shepherd they encountered, taking away all his bread in order to eat and recover. Rabbi Yosei rebuked him: *kapachita es ha'ro'eh* — you forcibly took the shepherd's food.

### Rabbi Yosei

Not long afterward, *ki matu le'masa* — when they arrived at a city — it was Rabbi Yosei who was seized by *bulmus*. The people of the city surrounded him with food and drink until he recovered, pressing all of it upon him. Rav Yehuda then said: *ani kapachti es ha'ro'eh echad* — I forced only one shepherd. *Ve'ata kapachta es ha'ir kulah* — you compelled the entire city.

### Three Tannaim

The *Gemara* then transitions to one of the *daf*'s most memorable stories. Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Yosei were traveling together. Rabbi Meir was in the practice of *diyuk bishma* — attending carefully to people's names as indicators of character — while Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei did not follow this practice.

### Names Character

When the three arrived at an inn and asked the innkeeper his name, he replied: *Kidor*. Rabbi Meir immediately concluded: *shma mina adam rasha hu* — this is obviously a wicked man — for the verse says *ki dor tahapuchot hema*, "they are a generation of perversity," and the name Kidor echoes *ki dor*. Acting on this suspicion, Rabbi Meir refused to leave his wallet with the innkeeper. Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei, dismissing this as overwrought, entrusted their wallets to him for *Shabbat*.

### Lentil Evidence

Rabbi Meir took his wallet and hid it at the grave of the innkeeper's father. That night, the innkeeper's father appeared to him in a dream telling him to retrieve the wallet resting on his head. The next morning the innkeeper reported the dream to the rabbis, but they told him *chelma d'vei shimshi leit behu mamasha* — dreams on *Shabbat* have no substance — and he was deterred. Rabbi Meir, knowing that the innkeeper now knew the wallet's location, went to the grave and stood watch over it the entire *Shabbat*. After *Shabbat* he retrieved it.

When the rabbis then asked the innkeeper to return their wallets, he claimed no knowledge of them — and they had no recourse. Rabbi Meir asked them: *amai lo dayekitu bishma* — why did you not pay attention to his name? They replied: why did you not warn us? He answered: *eimar d'amri ana chashasha, achzuki mi amina* — I expressed a concern, not a certainty; I could not say he was definitively wicked, only that suspicion was warranted.

### Moral Teaching

Determined to recover the wallets, the rabbis took the innkeeper into a shop and observed *tachlei asfamei* — lentils remaining in his mustache from that morning's meal. They went to his wife and said her husband had sent them to collect the wallets, offering as proof the detail that he had eaten lentils that morning. The wife, convinced by this knowledge that only her husband could have shared, returned the wallets. When the innkeeper came home and discovered what had happened, *katlei l'itetei* — he killed his wife. The *Gemara* links this to the broader teaching: *mayim rishonim he'echilu basar chazir* — inattention to washing *mayim rishonim* caused a man to be served *chazir*; *u'mayim achronim hargu et hanefesh* — and inattention to washing *mayim acharonim* after the meal caused a death, because had the innkeeper washed properly after eating, the lentils would not have been visible, the wife would not have been deceived, and she would not have been killed.

### Final Lesson

In the aftermath, Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei adopted Rabbi Meir's practice. When they subsequently came to a house whose owner's name was Bala, *lo ayilu lgabei* — they did not enter. A name evoking *v'amar l'Bala ni'ufim* — associated with promiscuity and wickedness — was sufficient warning. The lesson: pay attention to names; they can, in the right hands, reveal character — and inattention to detail, whether a name, a remnant of lentils, or the washing of one's hands, can have consequences far beyond what one imagines.
