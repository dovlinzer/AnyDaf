# Shabbat 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vessel Size Standards

### Pomegranate Standard

The daf opens in the midst of a sustained discussion about *keilim* — vessels — a recurring theme throughout this section of *masechet Shabbat*. To situate the broader context: the *mishnah* had been exploring the question of uprooting a plant from a flower pot with a hole in it, which is considered connected to the ground and thus constitutes a *melacha*, versus a pot without a hole, which lacks that status. Rabbi Shimon's view that a flower pot is never considered connected to the ground generated a *gemara* discussion about the varying sizes of holes that affect a vessel's legal status — whether for purposes of connection to the ground, or for *tumah* and *taharah*.

The final point from 95b provides the necessary context for what follows. Rav Asi states: *shamati, klei cheres shiuro kemotzi rimon* — I have heard that for a pottery vessel, the significant measure is the size of a pomegranate passing through. This is the largest size threshold mentioned in the discussion, and it is striking, since normally a hole the size of an olive suffices to strip an earthenware vessel of its vessel status. The only scenario where a pomegranate-sized hole would be required is if one had designated the vessel specifically to hold pomegranates — only then would a pomegranate-sized hole be the operative measure of loss of status.

Rava responds to Rav Asi: *shema lo shamata ela bemukaf tzamid patil* — perhaps what you heard applied specifically to a vessel surrounded by a sealed cover, a *tzamid patil*. The relevance of this context: the Torah specifies that a pottery vessel in a house containing a corpse becomes *tamei* through its interior space, not through its exterior wall. Accordingly, if the opening is sealed with a *tzamid patil*, the *tumah* cannot enter, and whatever food is inside the vessel remains *tahor*. But what if there is a hole in the side wall of the vessel even while the opening remains sealed? Can *tumah* enter through a crack in the side?

Rashi's position is that *tumah* can only enter through an official opening, not through an incidental crack or hole in the side wall. However, if the hole in the side is large enough that the vessel itself loses its status as a *kli*, then the special *halacha* of protection — whereby the vessel's sealed interior also shields the food within — no longer applies. At that point the food becomes *tamei*. The threshold at which a side-hole destroys the vessel's status under *tzamid patil* is *motzi rimon*, a pomegranate-sized hole.

### Pomegranate vs. Majority

This reading raises an immediate difficulty. The *gemara* notes: did not Rava himself rule *mukaf tzamid patil ad sheyipachet rubo* — that for a sealed vessel, it only loses the protection for its contents when the *majority* of the vessel is broken open? If so, is the operative measure a pomegranate, or a majority?

The *gemara* answers: *lo kashya — ha beravrevi ha bezutri* — there is no contradiction; it depends on whether the vessel is large or small. Rashi explains that for a large vessel, a pomegranate-sized hole may be insignificant relative to the vessel's overall size, so only a majority of the vessel's destruction would nullify it; for a small vessel, a pomegranate-sized hole is sufficient. Tosafot, however, challenges this: what are the precise thresholds of "large" and "small"? Rashi's explanation offers logical coherence but lacks a quantifiable standard that can actually be applied in practice.

Tosafot therefore reads the distinction in reverse. For a small vessel — one so small that a pomegranate-sized hole would exceed the majority — the threshold is simply the majority, since reaching majority-destruction is sufficient. For a large vessel, where the majority exceeds the pomegranate size, only the pomegranate measure is required. The underlying rule, as Tosafot frames it, is whichever is smaller: a majority of the vessel, or a pomegranate-sized hole. As soon as the smaller of those two thresholds is reached, the vessel loses its status of protecting the items inside.

### Olive-Sized Hole

Tosafot also raises a broader concern in the extended discussion *ul'inyan tzamid patil*: other *gemarot* appear to suggest that *tumah* can indeed penetrate through a side-hole even if that hole is not large enough to nullify the vessel's status entirely. Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing between a hole that was fashioned intentionally as part of the vessel's design — analogous to a spout — versus a hole that resulted from a break in the vessel's wall. Rashi's position applies to the latter: incidental damage on the side wall does not constitute an opening through which *tumah* can enter. But Tosafot acknowledges this is not the only possible reading, and the parameters of when *tumah* can or cannot pass through a side-hole remain a subject of deliberation.

### Pottery Shards

Returning to the main discussion, Rav Asi states further: *shonim, kli cheres shiuro b'koneis mashke* — we teach that the standard measure by which a pottery vessel loses its normal vessel status is a hole large enough for liquid to enter. At that threshold, for example, the vessel may no longer be used for *mei chatat*. The *gemara* distinguishes this from *motzi mashke* — a hole through which liquid exits. Due to the pressure of liquid inside a vessel, even a very small hole will allow liquid to seep out, whereas for liquid to enter from outside requires a somewhat larger opening. Accordingly, *motzi mashke* is a smaller threshold than *koneis mashke*.

The one context in which *motzi mashke* is halakhically significant is *l'inyan gistera bilvad* — regarding a pottery shard alone. If one takes a broken pottery fragment and designates it for continued use as a vessel, its status as a *kli* is already marginal. Consequently, even the smallest hole — at the level of *motzi mashke* — is sufficient to strip it of vessel status entirely.

Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman explains the reasoning: *lefi she'ein omrim havei gistera l'gistera* — one does not say "bring a patch for a patch." With an intact, serviceable vessel, one might plausibly repair even a notable hole, justifying a larger threshold before legal status changes. But with a shard, which was already barely a vessel, even the smallest breach is terminal — one simply discards it.

### Two Amoraim Debate

Ulla reports: *pligi bah trei amora'i b'ma'arava* — two *amoraim* in *Eretz Yisrael* debated this matter. This is a recognizable pattern in the Bavli, where pairs of Sages named Rabbi Yose appear together — Rabbi Yose bar Avin and Rabbi Yose bar Zavda — and the tradition preserves both positions without specifying which held which. One said the operative hole-size is *k'motzi rimon*, a pomegranate, the largest measure discussed; the other said *k'shoresh katan*, the size of a small root, which is considerably smaller than an olive. They took diametrically opposite positions.

A mnemonic for remembering that they took extreme rather than middle positions: *echad amar marbeh ve'echad amar memaet, uvilvad sheyachaven libo lashamayim* — one took the more expansive view and one the more restrictive, as long as one's intention is directed toward heaven — an allusion to the *gemara*'s familiar phrasing about prayer and sacrifice.

What exactly is the subject of their dispute? Rashi says they are debating *hechsher zera'im* — when the seeds or plant matter in the vessel are considered connected to the ground and thus cannot become *tamei*. Tosafot disagrees. Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Tam's observation that the measure of *shoresh katan* is already established in *masechet Uktzin* as the standard for *hechsher zera'im*, so there would be no debate there. Rabbeinu Tam therefore concludes that the dispute concerns *tzamid patil* — specifically, what size hole in the side wall of a sealed vessel is sufficient to terminate the vessel's status and expose its contents to *tumah*. Rashi's interpretation may have been prompted by the agricultural connotation of *shoresh katan*, which evokes either *zera'im* or the *melacha* of *tolesh* introduced in the *mishnah*; but Tosafot maintains the debate remains within the *tzamid patil* framework.

### Rabbi Chanina Ruling

Rabbi Chanina bar Kahana states in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *klei cheres shi'uro kemotzi zeitim* — for a pottery vessel, the operative measure of hole-size is that of an olive. Rashi understands this to refer to the basic *tumah* question: a hole the size of an olive is sufficient to strip the vessel of its status so that it can no longer become *tamei*. This restates what the *gemara* established the previous day.

Mar Kashisha son of Rava adds an additional ruling in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: once such a vessel has lost its status, *ve-harei hen ke-chlei glalim u-ke-chlei avanim u-ke-chlei adamah* — they are like vessels made of dried dung, stone, or earth. The significance of this comparison is explicit: such vessels *she'ein mekabelin tumah lo midivrei Torah velo midivrei sofrim* — they are entirely outside the category of *tumah*, neither biblically nor rabbinically. A pottery vessel with an olive-sized hole is therefore not merely diminished in status — it is completely removed from the framework of *tumah* and *taharah*, regardless of any subsequent designation.

Tosafot notes the pointed emphasis of this formulation. The *gemara* had previously said that if one has a pottery vessel with an olive-sized hole but then designates it for use with pomegranates, the vessel retains *tamei* status until a larger, pomegranate-sized hole is present. The statement of Rabbi Eliezer via Mar Kashisha comes to reject that: once the olive-sized hole is present, the designation is irrelevant — the vessel is fully outside the *tumah* framework and cannot become *tamei* under any circumstances.

As a concluding note on this section, *ul'inyan tzamid patil* — the protection of the vessel's sealed interior — reverts to the earlier discussion: that threshold is *ad sheyipachat rubo*, a majority of the vessel being opened, distinct from the *tumah*-status threshold of an olive-sized hole. The *gemara* thus tracks two separate standards running in parallel: one for the vessel's own susceptibility to *tumah*, and one for the vessel's capacity to protect its contents when sealed.

## Melachot Principles

### Recurring Themes

With the conclusion of this cluster of vessel-law discussions — signaled by *hadran alach Ha-Matzni'a* — the *gemara* transitions, though the transition itself is worth pausing over. As noted throughout, the recurring dominant theme of *masechet Shabbat* is *hotza'ah*, carrying. The *mishnayot* we have just concluded seemed to be moving the discussion toward broader principles and toward other *melachot*, yet the *gemara* now returns us to an entire *perek* devoted once again to carrying.

### Shinui & Shnayim

The preceding *mishnayot* surveyed core principles cutting across all *melachot*. The discussion of *ke-le'achar yad* — performing a *melacha* with a deviation, such as with the back of the hand or with the leg — established the principle of *shinui* as grounds for exemption. The *mishnah* then addressed *hotzio'u shnayim* — two people performing a *melacha* together — and the rules of *shnayim she'asu*: if each person individually could have performed the act, both are exempt; if both were needed, they are liable.

### Chai Nosei Principle

From *shnayim she'asu* the *gemara* moved to a case specific to *hotza'ah*: carrying out a living person, and the principle of *chai nosei et atzmo* — a living being carries its own weight. This principle is peculiar to *hotza'ah*. Tosafot raises the obvious question: if one carries out a 200-pound person, one is *patur*, yet if one carries out food the size of a pressed date, one is *chayav*. Clearly the exemption cannot rest on weight alone. Tosafot considers whether *chai nosei et atzmo* might be understood as a variant of *shnayim she'asu* — the carrier and the person being carried are both participating in the act. But Tosafot rejects this as well: the person being carried is only a *mesayei'a*, an assistant, and the rule is *mesayei'a ein bo mamash* — mere assistance carries no legal weight. Tosafot therefore concludes that the exemption is simply a consequence of the fact that in the *Mishkan*, the paradigm was carrying dead weight; a live person simply does not fit that paradigm. The practical ramifications — carrying a baby, whether the child can walk independently, and whether carrying in such cases is prohibited *de-rabbanan* — flow from this analysis.

### Fingernails & Body

The discussion also touched on cutting fingernails — originating as a question of *shinui*, since cutting with one's own fingernails rather than with an instrument may not constitute the normal manner of the act — and from there extended to broader questions about acts performed on the body: applying makeup, braiding hair, and similar personal grooming.

### Rabbi Eliezer Approach

Rabbi Eliezer emerged in that context as holding a broader definition of *melacha*. He considered braiding hair and applying cosmetics to be full-fledged *melachot*, whereas the *rabbanan* classified them as *de-rabbanan*. Rabbi Eliezer's wider conception of what constitutes a *melacha* on *Shabbat* is a theme that will recur and deepen as the *gemara* progresses.

### Milking & Cheese

From there the *gemara* moved to milking — is it a *melacha*, and if so, under what category? — and cheese-making, and the classification of these activities within the framework of *melachot*. Rabbi Eliezer ruled them *chayav*, and the *gemara* struggled to identify under which *melacha* heading each falls: *cholev mishum cholev, megaven mishum megaven*. The *gemara* could not easily fit these under standard *av* categories, which may itself reflect Rabbi Eliezer's different orientation toward the *melachot*, as will be articulated more fully below.

### Tolish Principle

Finally, the discussion returned to the *melacha* of *tolesh* — uprooting something from the ground — and specifically what constitutes "connection to the ground" when the plant is in a flower pot. This too is in some ways a question of *shinui* or of defining the precise parameters of a *melacha*. And with all of this as background, the *gemara* now opens yet another *perek* devoted to carrying.

## Carrying Domains

### Private-Public Rule

The new *perek* opens: *Ha-motzi me-reshut ha-yachid li-reshut ha-rabbim u-me-reshut ha-rabbim li-reshut ha-yachid* — one who carries out from a private domain to a public domain, or from a public domain to a private domain, is *chayav*. This applies not only to carrying but equally to throwing: if one throws an object from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-rabbim* or vice versa, one is equally liable. That is the first principle of the *mishnah*.

### Opposite Balconies

The *mishnah* then addresses a more complex case: *me-reshut ha-yachid l'reshut ha-yachid reshut ha-rabbim be-emtza* — one throws from one *reshut ha-yachid* to another, with a *reshut ha-rabbim* intervening. The paradigmatic case is throwing from a window on one side of the street through the air to a window on the opposite side, with the street — a *reshut ha-rabbim* — between them. Rabbi Akiva rules *chayav*; the *chachamim* rule *patur*.

The *mishnah* specifies the case: *shtei gezuztra'ot zo keneged zo be-reshut ha-rabbim* — two balconies facing one another on opposite sides of the street, with *reshut ha-rabbim* between them. Whether one hands an object across or throws it, one is *patur* — exempt. The rationale: going from one *reshut ha-yachid* to another *reshut ha-yachid* across *reshut ha-rabbim* does not constitute the classic forbidden act of carrying. The *gezuztra* is *reshut ha-yachid* because it is enclosed; the act traverses the *reshut ha-rabbim* without either origin or destination being *reshut ha-rabbim*.

Rabbi Akiva's position admits of two readings. One: the object passed through the air space of *reshut ha-rabbim* below ten *tefachim*, and by the principle of *keluta ke-man she-huncha dami* — absorption in the airspace is treated as if the object came to rest there — it is as though the object landed in *reshut ha-rabbim* and was picked up again. Under this reading, one might even be *chayav* twice: once for the passage from the balcony into the street, and once for the passage from the street into the receiving balcony. Alternatively, Rabbi Akiva may be taking a broader approach: from this case he derives a rule that any transfer from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-yachid* across *reshut ha-rabbim* is *chayav*, even where the object passed above ten *tefachim*. He is more willing to abstract.

### Same-Side Balconies

The *mishnah* then pivots to the case where there is a *chiyuv*: *hayu sheteihem be-gezuztra achat* — both balconies are on the same side of the street. Here the geometry is different: the two balconies overhang the street and project out over the *reshut ha-rabbim*, so that the transfer between adjacent balconies runs parallel to the length of the street rather than perpendicular across it. In this configuration: *ha-moshit chayav ve-ha-zorek patur* — one who hands an object from one balcony to the adjacent balcony is *chayav*, but one who throws is *patur*.

This result appears counterintuitive. We established at the outset that throwing is equivalent to carrying and equally *chayav*. Yet here, handing creates liability while throwing does not. The explanation is that this precise mode of transfer — handing, not throwing, from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-yachid* alongside the length of *reshut ha-rabbim* — is derived from a specific case in the *Mishkan*.

### Mishkan Wagon Case

The *mishnah* explains: *she-kach hayetah avodat ha-leviyim* — this was the mode of service of the Levites. When the time came to dismantle and transport the *Mishkan*, two wagons were arrayed side by side, followed by two more wagons behind them. Each wagon, with its walls, constituted a *reshut ha-yachid*. The *reshut ha-rabbim* — the open public thoroughfare of the encampment — ran between and around them.

The procedure was as follows: the components of the *Mishkan* were handed from the rear wagons to the forward wagons, passing them lengthwise alongside the *reshut ha-rabbim* beneath. The *mishnah* states: *shehayu zo achar zo birshut ha-rabbim moshitin ha-krashim mi-zo l'zo* — they would hand the beams from one wagon to the next, *aval lo zorkin* — but they never threw them. This is the paradigmatic act of *hotza'ah* in this configuration.

The conclusion is stark and illustrative: had they thrown instead of handed, there would have been no *melacha*. Had the wagons been on opposite sides of the *reshut ha-rabbim* rather than alongside it, there would have been no *melacha*. The liability is exquisitely narrow: *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-yachid*, alongside the length of *reshut ha-rabbim*, by handing. Any deviation exempts.

### Handing vs. Throwing

This brings into focus something the *gemara* keeps returning to: the fundamentally process-oriented nature of *hotza'ah*. Unlike most *melachot*, where one can reason from an *av* to its *toldot* and from the general principle to specific applications, *hotza'ah* is not a productive creative act, and therefore every aspect of the process must be derived independently. One cannot say: well, carrying involves movement of an object between domains — and then abstract freely. Each specific configuration must be either written, performed in the *Mishkan*, or derived by tradition.

This is why a slight deviation — throwing rather than handing, or crossing *reshut ha-rabbim* rather than moving alongside it — removes the *chiyuv* entirely. And as Tosafot observes, the implication is not that these other acts are somehow permissible, but that the *halacha* of *hotza'ah* is defined by the exact contours of the paradigm, and everything outside those contours must be derived on its own terms.

## Carrying Sources

### Throwing Tolada

The *gemara* opens the analysis with an acknowledgment: *zerika toldah dehotza'ah hi* — throwing is a *toldah*, a derivative, of *hotza'ah*. The classification is stated as given. But this immediately prompts the more foundational question.

### Moshe's Command

*Hotza'ah gufa heicha ktiva?* — Where is *hotza'ah* itself written in the Torah? How do we know that *hotza'ah* is an *av melacha*? Before the *gemara* answers, Tosafot notes a preliminary point: even though the activity of carrying was present in the *Mishkan* — the Levites carried the beams, as just described — one might have expected that, like other *melachot*, its status as a *melacha* could simply be derived from the fact that it was done in the *Mishkan* during the weekday construction. But Tosafot explains: *lefi shemelacha gerua hi* — carrying is a degraded *melacha*. It is not a productive creative act. Therefore, unlike weaving, cooking, building, or dyeing, one cannot simply infer that "they did this for the *Mishkan*, therefore it is a *melacha*." Every aspect of *hotza'ah* must be explicitly written or explicitly transmitted; nothing can be abstracted freely.

The *gemara* answers in the name of Rav Yochanan: the source is the *pasuk* — *vayetzav Moshe vayaviru kol bamachaneh* — Moshe commanded and a proclamation was made throughout the camp: *vayikalei ha-am me-havi* — and the people stopped bringing. *Ve-ha-melacha hayesah dayam* — what had been made was sufficient. The inference: Moshe was residing in *Machaneh Leviyah*, which is presumed to have been *reshut ha-rabbim* (Rashi explains that since Moshe was there, the entire people gathered there, constituting a public space). The people had been bringing contributions from their private homes to this public area. Moshe's proclamation — stop bringing — is read as a command: stop carrying from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-rabbim*.

### Gezerah Shava Link

The *gemara* then asks: who says this happened on *Shabbat*? Perhaps it was an ordinary weekday, and Moshe was simply saying they had enough material — not that he was prohibiting the act of *hotza'ah* specifically as a *Shabbat* prohibition. The *gemara* answers with a *gezerah shava*: the word *vayaviru* — they caused to pass — appears in this verse about Moshe's proclamation and also in the verse concerning *Yom Kippur* of *Yovel*: *ve-ha'avarta shofar teruah* — you shall cause the shofar blast to pass through all your dwelling places on the tenth day of the seventh month. Just as there the verse specifies a day forbidden in *melacha*, so too here, the act of causing the proclamation to pass corresponds to a day forbidden in *melacha* — i.e., *Shabbat*. Moshe's command to stop bringing was therefore a *Shabbat* prohibition against *hotzo'ah*.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot raises a significant question about this entire derivation. Why is it necessary to establish that Moshe's proclamation was made on *Shabbat*? The standard method for deriving a *melacha* is simply to show that the activity was done for the *Mishkan*; from that alone we conclude it is a *melacha* on *Shabbat*. The *pasuk* itself even calls it a *melacha*: *ve-ha-melacha hayesah dayam*. So why not simply say: carrying was done for the *Mishkan* and is called *melacha*, just as cooking and building were — and derive from that alone that it is forbidden on *Shabbat*?

In fact, Tosafot reports that there is a textual tradition — a *girsa* of the *gemara* — that omits the lengthy derivation through the *gezerah shava* entirely. Tosafot's reasoning: because *hotzo'ah* is a *melacha gerua*, a non-creative labor, the *gemara* may have been motivated to establish that the *pasuk* was specifically commanding them to stop on *Shabbat*, making the prohibition more explicit than inference alone would provide. But on another reading, there is no need for it, and the shorter *girsa* is coherent.

### Hachnasah Reverse

Having established *hotzo'ah* — carrying from private to public — the *gemara* asks: *hachnasah m'na lan?* — the reverse, bringing from *reshut ha-rabbim* to *reshut ha-yachid*, where is that derived? Here the *gemara* invokes *svara* — logical reasoning: *mah li apukei mah li iyulei* — what difference does it make whether one carries out or carries in? One domain is being traversed either way. Notably, in other areas of *hotza'ah* one cannot abstract so freely; yet here the *gemara* treats the reverse as self-evident. *Hotzo'ah* is nonetheless designated the *av*, since it is the one explicitly written; *hachnasah*, derived by logic, is the *toldah*. Both carry identical liability.

### Av Tolada Distinction

This raises the foundational question of the entire *av*-*toldah* framework: *amai kari l'hai av v'amai kari l'hai toldah* — why call one an *av* and the other a *toldah*, if the liability is equal in both cases? The *gemara* answers: the practical difference is in the domain of *korbanot*. If one performs two *avot* in a single state of forgetfulness — say, planting and cutting simultaneously — or two *toldot* of different *avot*, one brings two separate *chatat* offerings. But if one performs an *av* and its own *toldah* in a single state of forgetfulness, one brings only one *chatat*. The grouping of acts under an *av* with its *toldot* thus defines the unit of atonement: everything within one group requires only one offering, no matter how many acts within the group were performed in a single lapse of awareness.

## Rabbi Eliezer vs Categor…

### Two-Offering Rule

The logic of the *av*-*toldah* grouping, then, is fundamentally about *korbanot*: acts within one *av*-*toldah* cluster require one *chatat*; acts spanning two separate clusters require two *chatat* offerings. This is why the grouping matters in practice — it is not merely taxonomic.

### Rabbi Eliezer Multiple

But Rabbi Eliezer complicates this picture. *U'l'Rabbi Eliezer d'mechayev a'tolada b'makom av* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that even if one performs both an *av* and its own *toldah* in a single state of forgetfulness, one is *chayav* for each separately and must bring two *korbanot*. If that is true, what is the purpose of the *av*-*toldah* grouping for Rabbi Eliezer? Why call something an *av* and something else a *toldah* if the distinction carries no consequence in terms of liability or *korbanot*?

### No Categorization

The *gemara* answers: *hach d'hava b'Mishkan chashiva, kari lei av; hach d'la hava b'Mishkan chashiva, lo kari lei av* — those acts that were of significance in the *Mishkan* are labeled *av*; those that were not significant in the *Mishkan* are labeled *toldah*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the labels are purely descriptive: they identify where each act was derived from, but they carry no operative halakhic consequence. There is no functional grouping, no reduction in the number of *korbanot*; everything is liable on its own terms. The distinction between *av* and *toldah* is historical, not halakhic.

### Labels Historical

This conclusion illuminates Rabbi Eliezer's broader approach, which connects to what was observed earlier. Recall that the *gemara* was puzzled when it encountered Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that one is *chayav* for milking and for cheese-making: *cholev mishum mai mechayev mishum cholev, megaven mishum mai mechayev mishum megaven* — liable for milking because of milking, liable for cheese-making because of cheese-making. The *gemara* could not fit these acts neatly into standard *av* categories. That difficulty now makes sense: Rabbi Eliezer does not operate within a framework that requires every act to be subsumed under one of thirty-nine *avot*. He appears to have a broader, flatter conception of *melacha* on *Shabbat* — more acts may be independently *chayav* without needing to derive their status from a parent category.

Similarly, when Rabbi Eliezer called braiding hair and applying cosmetics a *melacha* while the *rabbanan* classified them as *de-rabbanan*, the difference was structural: the *rabbanan* ask, "Does this fit under one of the thirty-nine *avot*?" If not, it is at most *de-rabbanan*. Rabbi Eliezer, without that categorical constraint, can declare a wide range of acts independently *chayav*. The labels of *av* and *toldah* are, for him, simply a record of intellectual genealogy — which acts were prominent in the *Mishkan* and which were derived — not a mechanism for determining liability.

One might note, as the *gemara* discusses, that there is a view — associated with Rav Yosef — that not everyone even derived the thirty-nine *melachot* from the *Mishkan* in the same way. According to one approach, the Sages surveyed the categories of significant human activity and determined the *melachot* from there, without being strictly bound to the *Mishkan* template. Under such an approach, Rabbi Eliezer's methodology becomes all the more coherent: one need not derive each prohibited act from a specific paradigm in the *Mishkan* and then fit everything else as a derivative.

### Written Criteria

The *gemara* adds a further formulation of the *av*-*toldah* distinction: *i nami, hacha ketiva kari lei av; vehacha d'lo ketiva, kari lei toldah* — if an act is written in the Torah, it is an *av*; if it is not written, it is a *toldah*. Tosafot notes that this formulation is specifically needed for *hotza'ah*, which is the one *melacha* that required explicit scriptural derivation — because, as established, it is a *melacha gerua* and cannot be inferred from the *Mishkan* alone as other *melachot* can. For all other *melachot*, being "in the *Mishkan*" suffices for *av* status; for *hotzo'ah*, being "written" provides the basis. Thus, the criteria for *av* status are: either the act was in the *Mishkan* and thereby established, or — specifically for *hotza'ah* — it was explicitly written. Anything derived from these foundational sources, rather than being directly established in them, carries the status of *toldah*.

Applying this to the present case: carrying from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-rabbim*, which was read out of the *pasuk* about Moshe's command, is written — and therefore the *av*. Carrying in the reverse direction, *hachnasah*, which was derived by logical reasoning rather than from the text, is a *toldah*.

## Four Amot Tradition

### Four Amot Tradition

The *gemara* now turns to a category of carrying not yet accounted for: carrying or throwing four *amot* within *reshut ha-rabbim*. This is distinct from carrying between *reshut ha-yachid* and *reshut ha-rabbim*, and must be derived separately. As already noted, *hotza'ah* cannot be abstracted freely — every configuration requires its own source. The *gemara* therefore asks: *zorek arba amot b'reshut ha-rabbim, minalan demichayev?* — throwing four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabbim*, how do we know one is liable?

### Shuttle Example

Rav Yosef offers a source: *sheken orgei yeriyot zorkin machten ze la-ze* — for those who sewed the curtains of the *Mishkan* would throw their needles to one another. This would have involved throwing within *reshut ha-rabbim*, and would have covered a distance relevant to the four-*amot* standard. The *gemara* immediately objects: weavers (*orgin*) — what would they need needles for? Needles are instruments of sewing, not weaving. The *gemara* corrects: the reference is to the sewers (*tofrei yeriyot*), those who stitched the curtains together, who would throw their needles to one another.

### Shuttle Elimination

The *gemara* challenges: *dilma gabei hadadi havi yasvei* — perhaps they were sitting right next to one another, in which case no throwing was necessary and the distance would have been negligible. The *gemara* answers: *mathu hadadi be-machta* — you cannot sit directly adjacent when working with needles; you would poke one another. But the challenge continues: perhaps they sat within four *amot* of one another, which would still not establish the four-*amot* measure. The sewing-needle example cannot be relied upon to derive the four-*amot* standard, since the precise distance between the workers is unknown.

### Weavers' Shuttles

Rav Hisda then offers the weaving-shuttle case: *sheken orgei yeriyot zorkin bokhayer be-yeria* — those who wove the curtains would throw the shuttle through the fabric in the course of weaving. The shuttle is the instrument by which the horizontal thread is passed through the vertical warp threads — it is thrown from one side of the fabric to the other. For a large piece of fabric, this throw could span four *amot*.

The *gemara* objects: *ve-ha ihu ogdo be-yado* — the thread remains connected to the thrower's side. The shuttle is effectively still attached and has not been fully carried out beyond the thrower's possession. This parallels the case of a basket carried ninety percent out of a domain but still partially inside: the act is not complete until full separation. The *gemara* answers: *beniska batra* — the liability attaches with the very last thread. When the final thread leaves the thrower's hand on the return throw, the shuttle has been fully released and carried four *amot*.

A further challenge: *ve-ha be-makom patur ka-azla* — the area through which the shuttle travels, the interior of the curtain being woven, is a *makom patur* — an exempt space. It is not a *reshut ha-rabbim* proper, since it is not four *amot* by four *amot* in the transverse direction, and therefore does not establish the *chiyuv* for carrying in *reshut ha-rabbim*.

The *gemara* therefore shifts: *sheken orgei yeriyot zorkin bokhayer le-sha'aleiha* — the sewers would throw their shuttles to one another when borrowing was needed. Here the throw is from one person to another in open space, which would be *reshut ha-rabbim*. But the same objection recurs: *dilma gabei hadadi havi yasvei* — perhaps they were sitting next to one another. The *gemara* answers: *mathu hadadi be-khiba* — workers cannot be positioned directly adjacent when working along the edge of a garment, for they would interfere with one another.

### Self-Sufficiency

Yet a further challenge: *dilma she-khufei havi meshakhefi* — perhaps they were arranged in a staggered formation, which would keep them physically separated without requiring four *amot* of distance. And most forcefully: *mi shayli me-hadadi* — since when did workers in the *Mishkan* need to borrow from one another? A *baraisa* is cited: *ish ish mi-melakhto asher hem osim — mi-melakhto hu oseh ve-eino oseh mi-melakhat havero* — every person from their own work which they were doing; each person performed their own work and did not perform another's work. The *Mishkan*'s craftsmen were fully self-sufficient; no borrowing was necessary, and no one needed to throw materials to a colleague.

With the shuttle example thus eliminated, the *gemara* draws its conclusion: *ela, kol arba amot birshut ha-rabbim, gemara gemira lah* — as for carrying four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabbim*, we know it by tradition. It is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, not derivable from analogy or abstraction, but transmitted as a received legal norm. This again underscores the exceptional nature of *hotza'ah*: unlike all other *melachot*, where the general principle allows for derivation of related cases, *hotza'ah* requires independent sourcing for every sub-category — carrying out of a domain, carrying in, throwing across domains, and carrying within *reshut ha-rabbim* — each of which must come from explicit text, the *Mishkan* paradigm, logical inference, or direct tradition.

## Mekosshesh Case

### Mekosshesh Carrying

The *gemara* now introduces the famous case of the *mekosshesh* — the wood gatherer — who was executed for *chilul Shabbat* as recorded in *Sefer Bamidbar*. Given the ongoing effort to trace the sources of *hotza'ah* through the Torah's narrative, the *mekosshesh* case becomes directly relevant: what exactly was his transgression?

### Tolesh vs Meamer

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel says: *ma'avir arba amot birshut ha-rabbim havah* — he carried four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabbim*. This provides a biblical narrative instance of that very category whose derivation the *gemara* had just struggled to establish. A *baraisa* disagrees: *tolesh havah* — his transgression was uprooting, pulling wood from a tree that was still attached, which is the *melacha* of *tolesh*.

### Rav Achah Meamer

Rav Achah bar Yaakov offers a third position: *ma'amer havah* — his transgression was *me'amer*, gathering. He gathered wood that had already fallen from a tree and collected it into a bundle, which constitutes the *melacha* of *me'amer* — bundling or gathering. This reading seems intuitively natural: the image of a wood gatherer most readily suggests picking up scattered branches and gathering them together.

### Rav's Hidden Scroll

The *gemara* then asks: *l'mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does it make which *melacha* the *mekosshesh* violated? This is, after all, a historical question. The *gemara* answers: *l'chidarav* — the difference is relevant to what Rav taught. This is itself a striking formulation: the *gemara* has no interest in historical inquiry for its own sake, and the entire dispute is reframed as having practical *halachic* relevance.

Rav says: *matzasi megillat setarim bei Rav Chiyya* — I found a hidden scroll in the house of Rav Chiyya. Rashi explains that the designation "hidden" refers to the fact that *halachot* were generally not committed to writing, but this particular teaching was written down precisely because it could not be disseminated publicly — it was too sensitive. Written in that scroll was the following teaching from Isi ben Yehuda: *avot melachot arbaim chaser achas — ve-eino chayav ela achas* — there are thirty-nine *avot melachot*, and one is liable for only one of them. What does "only one" mean — that one is *chayav* on only one of the thirty-nine?

### Isi Exception

The *gemara* objects: we know from a *mishnah*, and from Rabbi Yochanan's explanation of it, that if one performs all thirty-nine *melachot* in a single lapse of awareness, one is *chayav* for each one separately and must bring up to thirty-nine *chatat* offerings. The number thirty-nine is stated explicitly in order to convey precisely that. How can Isi ben Yehuda say one is *chayav* on only one?

The *gemara* resolves: do not read it as "one is *chayav* only one [offering]." Read it as *eino chayav al achas mehen* — there is one among the thirty-nine for which one is not fully *chayav*. That is: thirty-eight of the thirty-nine *melachot* carry the full weight of *chiyuv misa* and *chiyuv chatat*, but one of them — unspecified — does not. Perhaps it is biblically forbidden, but it does not carry the death penalty and does not obligate a *chatat*. This is why the scroll was hidden: it is a dangerous idea. If one of the thirty-nine is not *chayav misa*, which one? What if the public concludes it is whichever *melacha* is most convenient?

### Which Melacha Exempt

The identification of *which* of the thirty-nine is the exceptional one is where the dispute about the *mekosshesh* becomes relevant. For Rav Yehuda, who holds that the *mekosshesh*'s transgression was carrying four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabbim*, it is immediately clear — *pshita lei* — that carrying is not the exceptional *melacha*: the *mekosshesh* was executed for it, proving beyond any doubt that carrying is fully *chayav misa*. For the *baraisa*, which holds the *mekosshesh* violated *tolesh*, it is clear that *tolesh* is not the exceptional *melacha*. For Rav Achah bar Yaakov, who holds it was *me'amer*, it is clear that *me'amer* is not the exceptional one. Each authority, based on his identification of the *mekosshesh*'s sin, thereby establishes with certainty which *melacha* cannot be the one Isi ben Yehuda exempts — because that *melacha* has been demonstrated to carry the death penalty.

There is something arresting in the *gemara*'s conclusion here. After the entire preceding discussion emphasizing how *hotza'ah* is uniquely different from all other *melachot* — not a creative act, requiring explicit derivation at every turn, resistant to abstraction — one might have naturally assumed that if any of the thirty-nine were to be the exceptional one exempt from *chiyuv misa*, it would be *hotza'ah*. The *gemara* explicitly notes this intuition, and then uses the *mekosshesh* narrative to rebut it, at least according to those who identify his transgression as carrying. According to that view, *hotzo'ah* is emphatically not the exceptional *melacha* — the *mekosshesh* died for it.

This is, of course, the ruling of Isi ben Yehuda alone, and the *halacha* does not follow this view. We rule that all thirty-nine *melachot* carry *chiyuv misa*. The *gemara* will continue tomorrow with the further treatment of the *mekosshesh* case.
