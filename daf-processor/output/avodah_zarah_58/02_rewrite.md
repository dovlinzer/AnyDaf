# Avodah Zarah 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Indirect + No Intent

### Overview of Touch Issues

Daf 58 continues several of the most practically significant discussions in *hilchos yayin nesech*. The preceding daf addressed the question of when grape juice becomes legally classified as wine — specifically, whether it acquires that status as it draws away from the grapes, and related questions about wine that puddles within the cask. Now the Gemara turns to what is arguably the more pressing and consequential issue for practical *halacha*: what type of touch makes wine forbidden, and to what degree is it forbidden? The relevant variables are whether the touch was direct or indirect, whether there was intent, and whether the resulting prohibition is one of drinking only (*assur beshtiya*) or extends to all benefit (*assur behana'ah*). These distinctions generate a matrix of cases that the Gemara works through methodically — and it is precisely because the *halachic* stakes are so high and the distinctions so nuanced that the *Tosafos* on these *dapim* are unusually long while the Gemara text itself is relatively brief.

### Palm Branch Story

The Gemara had introduced a story involving a non-Jew descending from a palm tree and unintentionally touching wine with the tip of the palm branch. This case combines two factors that each independently suggest leniency: the touch was indirect (mediated through the branch), and it was without intent. The question is what legal weight to assign to this combination.

### Tosafos Analysis

Tosafos, in his lengthy discussion on 57a, establishes a foundational principle: when both leniency factors are present simultaneously — indirect touch *and* no intent to touch — the wine is completely permissible, even for drinking. This is not merely a moderate leniency but a full dispensation. The principle is stated explicitly at the end of that *Tosafos*: *maga oved kochavim al yedei davar acher she-lo be-chavanat maga mutar bi-shtiya* — the touch of a non-Jew through an intermediary without intent to touch is permitted even for drinking. Rabbeinu Tam rules this way as a matter of practical *halacha*.

### Tosafos Text Reading

This conclusion forces Tosafos to reread the palm branch story itself. If the story genuinely described indirect touch combined with no intent, then the wine would be universally permissible and there would be nothing to debate. Tosafos therefore concludes that the story must have involved direct touch — the non-Jew actually touched the wine directly, not merely through the branch. The apparent combination of indirect and unintentional was thus not what the story was really about. The substantive legal discussion that followed in the Gemara must accordingly be situated in the context of direct touch without intent — which is a meaningfully different and more difficult case.

## Tinok & Christians

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The Gemara presents a dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning whether an infant (*tinok*) renders wine forbidden. The operative definition of "infant" here is not chronological but conceptual: a person who does not understand the nature of *avodah zarah* — *lo yadei be-tiv avodah zarah*. Rav holds that even such a touch renders the wine forbidden at least for drinking (*assur beshtiya*). The Gemara refines Rav's position: he does not say the wine becomes forbidden in benefit (*assur behana'ah*), only that it is forbidden to drink. Shmuel, by contrast, reads the relevant *braita* as indicating that the touch of a child produces no prohibition at all — not even for drinking.

Related to this dispute is Shmuel's independent ruling that a slave who converts still renders wine forbidden for a full year after conversion, so long as he continues to speak in terms associated with his former idolatrous practice. Tosafos addresses this practically in the context of medieval slave ownership, noting that it applies specifically to slaves — because even a technically voluntary conversion by a slave is not truly free, given the coercive power dynamics inherent in the master-slave relationship. A free convert, by contrast, is presumed to have genuinely abandoned the prior faith upon converting.

### Christians Like Infants

Tosafos then makes a striking move. Since the legal definition of "infant" is someone unfamiliar with the practice of using wine for libations, Tosafos argues that contemporary Christians fit this description. While he does not deny that Christianity constitutes *avodah zarah*, he observes that ordinary Christians — the laity, as distinguished from clergy performing the Eucharist — do not actually engage in the practice of wine libations. They are therefore functionally equivalent to the *tinok ben yomo*: they do not operate within the framework in which wine and *nisuch* are connected.

### Radical Conclusion

If one follows this reasoning to its logical end, a striking conclusion emerges. If contemporary Christians are like *tinokos* for purposes of wine law, and if we rule like Shmuel that an infant's touch does not even forbid the wine for drinking, then it would follow that we could drink wine touched by contemporary Christians entirely. This is precisely the argument that R. Yitzchak (the Ri) sent to Rabbeinu Tam: your grandfather Rashi ruled that contemporary non-Jews are like *tinokos*, and you yourself rule like Shmuel that a *tinok*'s touch does not forbid the wine even for drinking — therefore, one plus one, we should be able to drink the wine of non-Jews outright. Chaim Soloveitchik observed that had the *Rishonim* wished to pursue this argument, they could have effectively dismantled the entire body of law governing *yayin nesech* through a single concatenated ruling. But there was evidently too deep a communal and religious taboo around this conclusion for it to be seriously entertained.

### Actual Ruling

Rabbeinu Tam's response is pointed. He writes that whoever attributed to him the ruling permitting the wine of an infant even for drinking was overeager — *nivhal hayah lifsok*. God forbid, he says, that such a thought ever occurred to him. The actual ruling is that we follow Rav, not Shmuel: even an infant's touch renders wine forbidden for drinking. What the leniency of treating contemporary Christians as *tinokos* achieves is narrower: it removes the prohibition of *hana'ah*, allowing one to derive benefit from the wine and conduct commerce with it — for instance, seizing it in satisfaction of a debt — but it does not permit drinking.

### Logic of Ruling

Tosafos concludes with an elegant structural argument that explains why the ruling lands precisely where it does. There are two independent reasons why the Sages prohibited the wine of non-Jews: the concern of *avodah zarah* — that wine used in libations is categorically forbidden — and the concern of *kiruv*, socialization leading toward intermarriage. The great stringency of wine relative to other non-Jewish foods (whose bread and oil are forbidden only for eating, not for benefit) derives specifically from the *nisuch* dimension. If contemporary non-Jews genuinely do not use wine for libations, the *avodah zarah* component of the prohibition loses its force, and wine drops back into the category of ordinary non-Jewish foods — prohibited for eating due to the intermarriage concern, but not prohibited for benefit. As Tosafos puts it: *dayo lihyot ke-pittam ve-shamnam — o ke-bishulei ovdei kochavim le-esor bi-shtiya velo be-hana'ah* — it is sufficient to treat their wine like their bread and oil, forbidden for eating but not for benefit. And he adds: *ve-ha-machmir tavo alav beracha* — one who is stringent is praiseworthy.

The underlying logic is thus: the *gezeira* of *nisuch* was never extended to non-Jews who do not practice libations. Since contemporary Christians fall outside that category, no *issur hana'ah* applies. The *gezeira* of *stam yeinam* for purposes of preventing intermarriage does apply — and therefore one may not drink. The two concerns track two different prohibitions, and the contemporary situation removes one without removing the other.

## Angry Non-Jew Case

### Story Setup

The Gemara presents a case from *Mechuza*. A non-Jew entered a Jewish merchant's shop and asked whether there was wine for sale. The Jew said no. There was, in fact, wine sitting in a pail. The non-Jew, apparently angered, plunged his hand into the wine and moved it around — a gesture that says, in effect, "What is this, isn't this wine?" The Jew then grabbed the wine in anger and threw it into the main barrel. Now a larger problem has arisen: the non-Jew's touch made the wine in the pail forbidden, and that wine has now been mixed into the main barrel.

### Dispute over Wine

Rava ruled that the wine could be sold to non-Jews — implying that while it may be forbidden for Jews to drink, it is not forbidden in *hana'ah*, and selling it to non-Jews is permissible. Rav Huna bar Chinena and Rav Huna son of Rav Nachman disagreed and ruled it forbidden. Both sides issued formal announcements (*shipur*) — Rava's ruling came out permitting it, and their ruling came out forbidding it.

### Rava Questions Teacher

Subsequently, Rav Huna son of Rav Nachman came to *Mechuza*, which was Rava's city. Rava — who had studied under Rav Huna — went to speak with him privately. Tosafos reads the instruction to close the door differently from Rashi: rather than an attempt to keep Rav Huna out (which Tosafos finds both grammatically strained and uncharacteristic of Rava, who was known to concede when corrected), Tosafos reads it as Rava seeking private time with his teacher, away from disturbance. Rava then asked Rav Huna: given that the non-Jew acted in anger, with no intent toward *nisuch*, did you not teach me that such a case does not produce *yayin nesech*?

### Clarification: Subtracti…

Rav Huna clarified that Rava had misunderstood him. When Rav Huna had said the wine could be sold, he meant something precise: *eimar de'amri ana lebar midmei* — I meant that you subtract the value of the forbidden wine and sell the remainder. In the specific scenario where the non-Jew's touch affected a small quantity of wine that was then mixed into a larger barrel, the forbidden portion does not render the entire barrel forbidden in *hana'ah*. You may sell the full barrel but must not charge for the portion that was forbidden — that amount is deducted from the price. What Rav Huna never said was that the touched wine itself was permissible. It remains *assur behana'ah*; you simply are not required to discard the entire barrel on account of it.

### Abaye's Challenge

Rava then traveled to *Pumbedisa*, where Abaye challenged him with both an *Amoraic* ruling (*shmaita*) and a *braita* (*masneisa*) demonstrating that direct touch without *kavanat nisuch* still renders wine forbidden — even in benefit. The *Amoraic* evidence: a similar incident occurred in *Nehardea* and Shmuel ruled it forbidden; a similar incident occurred in *Tiberias* and Rabbi Yochanan ruled it forbidden. Rava attempted to deflect by arguing that those communities forbade it merely as a practical stringency because their populations were not sufficiently Torah-knowledgeable, not because it was technically required. Abaye dismissed this: are Tiberias and Nehardea less Torah-educated than *Mechuza*? These are all major urban centers. Even if Rava were technically correct, he should apply the same stringency in *Mechuza*.

### Inspector Story

The *braita* that Abaye adduced presents the case of the *argadamim* — a government inspector whose function was to verify that merchants were selling what they claimed to be selling, at the right quality. Rashi explains this is a Greek term for such an official. The *braita* describes a non-Jewish inspector who draws wine through a tube or tastes from a cup in order to verify its quality, then pours the remainder back into the barrel. The context is entirely commercial — his intent is regulatory inspection, clearly not *nisuch*. The *braita* rules this forbidden. Abaye argues that *forbidden* here must mean *assur behana'ah* — that is, the wine cannot even be sold — because if it were merely forbidden for drinking, the *braita* would have used the language of *yimacher* (it must be sold), as it does in the next case.

### Oil/Wine Confusion

The concluding case of the *braita* confirms this interpretive logic. A non-Jew — described as a *cherem*, apparently some impetuous figure — extends his hand into a barrel thinking it contains oil, only to discover it is wine. He did not intend to touch wine at all; he believed he was touching oil. This case is ruled *yimacher* — sell it. The wine is forbidden for drinking but permissible in benefit. The contrast between the two cases makes Abaye's point precisely: when the *braita* intends to permit *hana'ah*, it says *sell it*; when it intends to forbid *hana'ah*, it says *forbidden* without qualification. The inspector case says *forbidden*; the oil-confusion case says *sell it*. *Tiyuvta deRava tiyuvta* — this is a definitive refutation of Rava's position.

## Pouring & Matrix

### Pouring Incident

The Gemara introduces a further incident. Rabbi Yochanan bar Arza and Rabbi Yosi ben Nehorai were sitting and drinking wine, and asked a passerby to pour for them — presumably assuming, in context, that anyone nearby would be Jewish. After he poured wine into their cups, it emerged that he was a non-Jew. The question is what becomes of that wine.

### Two Opinions

Two opinions are recorded. One rules the wine forbidden even in *hana'ah*. The other rules it permissible even for drinking. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi validates both positions: *man de'assar shapir assar, u-man de-sharei shapir sharei* — each side has a sound argument.

### Forbidding Opinion

The opinion that forbids the wine reasons as follows: when the non-Jew was asked to pour, what must he have been thinking? He surely said to himself — these are distinguished Rabbis; important people drink wine, not beer. He therefore concluded that they were drinking wine. Knowing he was dealing with wine, his touch constitutes a standard case of *maga oved kochavim* — contact with awareness that the substance is wine — and therefore renders it forbidden.

### Permitting Opinion

The opinion that permits the wine argues the opposite. The non-Jew, far from concluding he was dealing with wine, must have reasoned as follows: everyone knows that Rabbis do not ask non-Jews to handle their wine. Therefore, if these Rabbis asked me to pour, it cannot be wine — it must be beer (*shichra*). He touched it without knowing it was wine. This reasoning aligns with the case of the man who plunged his hand into a barrel thinking it contained oil: direct touch without awareness of wine results in *assur beshtiya* at most. But then a further problem arises — even if he didn't know it was wine, he still directly touched it, which should at minimum forbid drinking.

### Resolution: Indirect

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: the non-Jew never actually touched the wine directly. He poured from a carafe or used a scoop (*natla*), and his hand never made contact with the wine itself. The touch was entirely indirect — *kocho shelo be-kavana*: force applied without intent to touch wine. The Gemara therefore classifies the case as indirect touch without intent to touch wine, which brings it under the principle Tosafos established at the outset.

### No Intent Principle

This resolution yields the governing principle: *kol de-lo bekavana lo gazru bei rabbanan* — wherever there is no intent to touch wine at all, the Rabbis did not include such contact within their decree. This is precisely why Tosafos reread the palm branch story: the conclusion of this Gemara makes clear that the legally operative category that produces full permission (*muttar* even for drinking) is indirect touch combined with no intent to touch wine. That combination was never subject to the rabbinic decree in the first place.

## Four-Category Matrix

### Direct + Intent

The foregoing discussion generates a coherent four-category matrix. The first category is direct touch with intent to touch wine — that is, the non-Jew knew he was dealing with wine and physically touched it. This is the standard case of *yayin nesech* and produces the most severe result: *assur behana'ah*. No benefit may be derived from the wine.

### Direct + No Intent

The second category is direct touch without intent to touch wine — the non-Jew made physical contact with the wine but did not know it was wine (believing it to be oil, as in the *braita*'s case). This produces a lesser prohibition: *assur beshtiya* — the wine is forbidden for drinking but permissible in benefit. It may be sold.

### Indirect + No Intent

The third category is indirect touch without intent to touch wine — the non-Jew's contact was mediated through an implement or pour, and he did not know the substance was wine. This is entirely permissible, even for drinking. This is the case resolved by the Gemara in the pouring incident and the theoretical case underlying the palm branch story as reread by Tosafos.

### Indirect + Intent

The fourth category — indirect touch with intent to touch wine (the non-Jew knows it is wine but touches only through an intermediary) — falls between the prior cases. Since the non-Jew knew it was wine, the situation is worse than the previous case; since the touch was indirect, it is better than the first two. This produces at minimum *assur beshtiya*, and the question of whether *hana'ah* is also forbidden remains somewhat open, though it appears to align with the *assur beshtiya* cases.

### Matrix Principle

The organizing principle of the matrix can be stated simply: two bad factors (direct touch and intent to touch wine) produce *assur behana'ah*; two good factors (indirect touch and no intent to touch wine) produce full permission; one bad and one good produce *assur beshtiya*. The logic is clean and maps neatly onto the cases the Gemara discusses. Practically, this means that if a non-Jew's incidental, indirect contact with wine occurs in a context where he has no awareness that the substance is wine, no prohibition attaches at all.

## Water & Safeguards

### Water Question

The Gemara pivots to a related question posed by Rav Assi to Rabbi Yochanan: what if a non-Jew mixes water into wine without touching the wine itself? He adds only water; his hand never comes into contact with the wine. Does the prohibition apply?

### Safeguard Ruling

Rabbi Yochanan — after briefly correcting Rav Assi's choice of language (Rav Assi used the Biblical *mascha* rather than the rabbinic *mazgo*, and Rabbi Yochanan notes that *lashon Torah le-atzmah, lashon chachamim le-atzmah* — the language of Scripture and the language of the Rabbis each follow their own conventions) — rules that such wine is forbidden. The rationale is not a strict legal prohibition but rather the principle of *lech lech amrinan nezira sechor sechor la-charmah lo tikrav* — we tell the *nazir*: go, go, keep away; do not even approach the vineyard. It is a protective fence, a *gezeira* erected not because the act itself technically violates the prohibition but because proximity to the situation risks violation.

### Safta Story

A practical incident illustrates the ruling. Rav Yirmeyah arrived in *Safta* and observed that non-Jews were mixing water into wine and Jews were drinking it without hesitation. He ruled the practice forbidden, invoking the same *lech lech* principle. The situation was not technically a case of direct touch, yet the rabbinic safeguard applies regardless.

### Mixing Principle

The principle is restated by Rav Yochanan (with a parallel attribution to Rav Assi citing Rav Yochanan): *yayin she-mazgo ovdei kochavim assur* — wine that non-Jews have mixed is forbidden, on the grounds of *lech lech amrinan nezira sechor sechor la-charmah lo tikrav*. Whether this forbidden status extends to *hana'ah* or only to drinking, Tosafos takes it to mean at minimum forbidden to drink.

## Botzrah Terumot

### Botzrah Story

The Gemara records that Resh Lakish traveled to *Botzrah* and found Jews eating untithed fruit. He ruled it forbidden as *tevel*. He also found water that non-Jews had bowed down to, and Jews were drinking it; he ruled that forbidden as well.

### Correction

Rav Yochanan told him — before he had even removed his coat — to turn around immediately and retract both rulings. His instruction to Resh Lakish: *betzer lav haynu Botzrah* — *Betzer* (a city within *Eretz Yisrael*) and *Botzrah* (a city outside *Eretz Yisrael*) are not the same place. Since *Botzrah* is outside *Eretz Yisrael*, the laws of *terumot u-ma'asrot* do not apply there.

### Terumot Geography

Tosafos addresses this at length in a *Tosafos* that extends across the next *amud*, engaging the question of whether *terumot u-ma'asrot* ever apply outside *Eretz Yisrael*. There are Talmudic passages suggesting they do apply in some contexts outside the land. Tosafos offers two answers. The first, historically specific answer is that in medieval feudal society the land legally belonged to the non-Jewish overlord, so Jews could not be said to own it in the way *terumot u-ma'asrot* presuppose. The second and more broadly accepted answer is that when the Gemara says *terumot u-ma'asrot* apply outside *Eretz Yisrael*, it refers only to the immediately adjoining territories — Syria, Transjordan, areas seen as an extension of the land — not to distant regions. The laws are fundamentally tied to *Eretz Yisrael* itself; any application beyond its borders is rabbinic and limited to areas treated as its functional extension.

### Community Water

As for the water that the non-Jews had worshipped, Rav Yochanan rules on separate grounds: *mayim shel rabbim ein ne'asarin* — water belonging to the community cannot be rendered forbidden through idolatrous worship, because *avodah zarah* can only prohibit objects that belong to someone. Community property has no individual owner capable of effecting such a prohibition. Rav Yochanan traces this ruling to Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak. The Gemara probes whether privately owned water drawn from a river and then worshipped could be forbidden, working through whether water that has been physically separated from its source (by a wave, for example) but never handled by a human is analogous to a rock that has rolled off a mountain — a case where earlier discussions left the *halacha* unresolved. The resolution is that the case of water becoming forbidden pertains specifically to water that a person physically drew out and then worshipped; naturally displaced water retains the status of the attached landscape and cannot be made forbidden.

## Or Zarua Ruling

### Or Zarua: Sellable

A practical ruling from the *Or Zarua* appears at the end of this *amud*, introduced on *Nun Chet amud beis* without being attached to a specific Gemara passage — it is presented as a direct *psak*. The Or Zarua writes: *bizman hazeh ovdei kochavim she-shichshach be-yeino shel Yisrael mutar lemochro le-ovdei kochavim* — in contemporary times, if a non-Jew moves his hand around in a Jew's wine, it is permissible to sell it to non-Jews.

### Reasoning

The reasoning is that contemporary non-Jews are not expert in the practice of *shichshuch* as it relates to *nisuch* — *de-lo baki be-shichshuch*. They are not operating within the framework in which moving wine in this way would constitute a meaningful act of *nisuch*. Therefore, even direct touch of this kind does not render the wine *assur behana'ah* in our times. Drinking, however, remains forbidden.

### Critical Caveat

The Or Zarua adds a critical qualification that inverts the expected analysis. The foregoing leniency — that contemporary non-Jews cannot render wine *assur behana'ah* — applies specifically when the non-Jew was not acting with the intent to make the wine forbidden in order to harm the Jew. This is *davka shelo niskaven liga kidei lehachis le-Yisrael ule-hafsido* — the leniency holds only when the non-Jew's touch was not a deliberate attempt to ruin the Jew by exploiting Jewish law.

### Intentional Harm Case

Counterintuitively, when a non-Jew touches wine specifically intending to make it forbidden — knowing Jewish law and deliberately weaponizing the *halacha* to harm the Jewish owner — the Or Zarua rules that the wine remains permissible even for drinking. *De-chi hai gavna lo gazru rabbanan* — the Rabbis never included such a case within their decree. The *gezeira* was enacted against the normal religious context of non-Jewish wine contact; it was never meant to give a hostile actor the power to destroy a Jew's property. A non-Jew acting with malicious intent to invoke Jewish law against a Jew falls entirely outside the decree.

### Pious Elder Story

The Or Zarua cites a responsum in which a powerful non-Jew (*anas*) came to a large vat of Jewish wine and thrust his hand into it, declaring in effect that he was rendering the wine forbidden. A pious elder present — a man who in the end gave his life *al kiddush ha-Shem* during the crusades, a detail the Or Zarua includes to establish his standing — immediately took a vessel, filled it from that very wine, and drank it in front of the non-Jew. His purpose was to demonstrate concretely that the non-Jew's act had not achieved what he intended: *kidei shelo yehei ragil la'asos ken le-Yisrael acher* — so that he would not make a habit of doing this to other Jews. Whether one reads this act primarily as a *halachic* demonstration or as a policy intervention, the Or Zarua treats it as reflective of the underlying ruling: that wine touched with the intent to harm is not included in the *gezeira* and remains permissible.

The overall conclusion of this *daf* can be summarized around the matrix. Two unfavorable factors — direct touch and awareness of touching wine — produce *assur behana'ah*. Two favorable factors — indirect touch and no awareness of touching wine — produce full *hetter*, even for drinking. One of each produces *assur beshtiya*. Once one applies the *Tosafos* framework to contemporary non-Jews — who do not function within the world of wine libations and thus fall into the *tinok* category for purposes of the *avodah zarah* concern — nothing they touch ever truly becomes *assur behana'ah*. The intermarriage concern persists, and therefore drinking remains forbidden, but commercial use of such wine is permissible.
