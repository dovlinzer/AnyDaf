# Megillah 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Overview

### Masechet Structure

Daf 30 of *Masechet Megillah* is the penultimate *daf* of the tractate. The *masechet* opened with the reading of the *Megillah*, then addressed other aspects of synagogue life — the reading of the *Torah*, *haftarah*, *Birchas Kohanim*, and the sanctity of the *Beit Knesset* itself. Now the Gemara returns to the specifics of which sections are read on particular occasions. The final *Mishnah* of the tractate deals with the various sections read on *Yomim Tovim*, though it mentions only the *Torah* readings, and even those only partially — it does not detail the *Torah* readings for *Pesach* specifically, and it omits *haftarah* readings altogether.

### Braita-Gemara Dialog

The *Gemara* opens with a *braita* and then continuously interrupts it with Aramaic interjections. One can identify when the *Gemara* is speaking rather than the *braita* not only from the shift in language but from the keyword *ve-ha'idna* — "nowadays" — signaling that contemporary practice diverges from the *braita*, often because we observe two days of *Yom Tov* in the *golah* rather than one. What is particularly striking is that the *Gemara* signals not only changes due to the one-day versus two-day *Yom Tov* distinction, but a broader fluidity in what was established as the *Torah* and *haftarah* reading for each occasion. As will become apparent — and as *Tosafot* explicitly flags — even what the *Gemara* prescribes is not always what we practice today. There is an ongoing, acknowledged evolution in these readings across generations.

## Pesach Readings

### Braita: Moadot

The *braita* states: *be-Pesach korei be-parashat moadot* — on *Pesach* you read from the *parsha* of *moadot*, which is found in *Emor* in *Vayikra*, beginning with *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled*. The opening section there concerns bringing an animal born to the *Beit HaMikdash*, and then the passage continues with *Daber el benei Yisrael ve-amarta aleihem mo'adei Hashem asher tikre'u otam mikra'ei kodesh* — *Shabbos* and then the listing of *Pesach* and the other *moadim*. As distinct from the listings in *Pinchas*, which focus narrowly on *korbanot*, the section in *Emor* has a broader dimension — it addresses the *chag* as celebrated beyond the *Beit HaMikdash*. For instance, by *Shavuot* the text includes *u-ve-kutzrechem et ketzir artzechem lo techaleh pe'at sadecha* — the obligation to leave portions of the harvest for the poor. By *Rosh Hashanah* the section begins with *innuy* before reaching the *korbanot*. This broader, more societal dimension explains why *Chazal* selected this section as the core *parsha* for reading on the *moadot*.

The *braita* further states that the *haftarah* for *Pesach* is the account of *Pesach be-Gilgal* — the *Pesach* observed when Bnei Yisrael first entered *Eretz Yisrael*, as recorded in *Yehoshua*.

### Two Days Practice

The *Gemara* interjects: *ve-ha'idna de-ika terei yomei* — nowadays that there are two days of *Yom Tov* in *Chutz La'aretz* — *yoma kama be-Pesach Gilgal*, the first day uses the *haftarah* of *Pesach be-Gilgal* from *Yehoshua*, and *u'lamachar be-Pesach Yoshiyahu* — the second day uses the *haftarah* from *Yoshiyahu*, who led a great national renewal and a celebrated observance of *Chag HaPesach*. According to this, the *Torah* reading of *shor chesev va-ez* would presumably remain for both first days, though as we will see, that is itself subject to modification.

### Chol HaMoed

The *braita* continues: *u-she'ar yemot ha-Pesach* — for the remaining days of *Pesach*, meaning *Chol HaMoed*, *melaket ve-korei me'inyano shel Pesach* — one collects and gathers various passages relating to *Pesach*, since the *Torah* contains numerous such passages. This instruction is too general to provide specifics, so the *Gemara* asks: *mai hi* — what exactly are the various readings for *Chol HaMoed Pesach*?

### Mnemonic System

*Amar Rav Pappa: Ma'apash siman* — Rav Pappa offers the mnemonic *Ma'apash* to recall the *Chol HaMoed* readings. According to *Rashi*, the letters stand for the following passages: *mem* — *Mashchu u-kechu lachem tzon lemishpachoteichem ve-shachatu ha-Pesach*, the command in Egypt to bring the *Korban Pesach*; *alef* — *Im kesef talveh et ami*, from the middle of *Mishpatim*, which briefly treats all three *Regalim* including *Pesach*; *peh* — *Psal lecha shnei luchot avanim ka-rishonim*, the second *luchot*, which similarly revisits the *shalosh regalim*; and *shin* — *Vayedaber*, from *Beha'alotcha* in *Bamidbar*, which is the passage of *Pesach Sheni*.

This mnemonic covers four days, which corresponds to the four days of *Chol HaMoed* in *Chutz La'aretz*. In *Eretz Yisrael*, where there are five days of *Chol HaMoed*, a fifth reading would need to be found.

The *braita* then continues: *Yom Tov acharon shel Pesach korin Vayehi Beshalach* — the last *Yom Tov* of *Pesach*, the seventh day, we read *Vayehi Beshalach* because *Kriat Yam Suf* occurred on the seventh day according to *Chazal*. The *haftarah* is *Vayedaber David*, the song of David, which parallels *Shirat HaYam*. As *Rashi* notes, there are elements of *Shirat David* that evoke the theme of *Yam Suf* — God unleashing His anger against His enemies. Then *u'lamachar — Kol Habechor*: the eighth day of *Pesach* (clearly addressing *Chutz La'aretz*, even though this is still in the Hebrew portion of the text) uses the reading of *Kol Habechor* from *Devarim*. The *haftarah* is *Od Hayom be-Nov la'amod*, a Messianic vision — appropriate for *Pesach* as a time of *geulah* that looks forward to future redemption.

It is worth pausing here on the structure underlying these selections. The *Torah* portions chosen for *Yom Tov* tend to fall into two groups: *shor o chesev o ez* from *Emor* and *Kol Habechor* (beginning with *aser te'aser*) from *Devarim*. The first type dominates the early days of *Yom Tov*; the second dominates the last days. Both share the common characteristic of addressing the *moadim* in a manner broader than the narrow *Mussaf korban* focus of *Pinchas*. The *aser te'aser* section in *Devarim*, for instance, encompasses a wide range of agricultural and social obligations — *ma'aser*, *shemita*, and responsibilities toward the poor — all deeply connected to the time of the harvest and the *chagim*.

### Abaye's Mnemonic

*Amar Abaye: Ha'idna nahag alma* — Abaye then offers a different and more comprehensive mnemonic for all eight days of *Pesach* in *Chutz La'aretz*: *Meshach Tora Kadesh Bakaspa Psal Bemadbara Shelach Buchra*. The mnemonic itself is in Aramaic — appropriately, since it is a memory device rather than a citation of biblical text, and the vernacular lends itself better to such a purpose.

The mapping is as follows. *Meshach* refers to *Mashchu u-kechu lachem tzon lemishpachoteichem ve-shachatu ha-Pesach* — the command in Egypt to bring the *Korban Pesach*, read on the **first day**. Note that this is a significant departure from the *braita*, which had the generic *shor o chesev o ez* on the first day. Abaye's approach begins with a passage directly and specifically about *Pesach*. *Tora* — *shor o chesev o ez*, the *shor* reference — is the **second day**. *Kadesh* refers to *Kadesh li kol bechor* from *Shemot*, which addresses the firstborn and the narrative of the Exodus, read on the **third day**. *Bakaspa* is *Im kesef talveh et ami* from *Mishpatim*, the third day's equivalent in Rav Pappa's system, now the **fourth day**. *Psal* is *Psal lecha shnei luchot avanim ka-rishonim* — the second *luchot*, **fifth day**. *Bemadbara* is from *Bemidbar Sinai* in *Beha'alotcha* — *Pesach Sheni*, **sixth day**. *Shelach* is *Vayehi Beshalach Paro et ha-am* — the seventh day and *Shirat HaYam*. *Buchra* is *Kol Habechor*, the eighth day.

The most significant shift from the *braita* is at the outset: rather than beginning with the generic *shor o chesev o ez*, the practice reflected in Abaye's mnemonic opens with the specific, narrative *Pesach* passage of *Mashchu u-kechu lachem tzon*. This reflects a broader tendency we will observe throughout: the *Gemara* and subsequent practice prefer to open *Yomim Tovim* with readings that speak directly and narratively to the character of the day, rather than with generic passages. Both *shor o chesev o ez* and *Kol Habechor* represent sections that treat all three *regalim* but without the narrow *Mikdash korban* focus; what distinguishes *Mashchu u-kechu* is that it opens the *Pesach* cycle with the very story of *Pesach* itself.

## Shavuot Readings

### Braita Dispute

The *braita* turns to *Shavuot*: *ba-Atzeret, Shiva Shavuot* — on *Shavuot* the reading is the passage about counting seven weeks and bringing the accompanying *korban*. The *haftarah* according to the first opinion is from *Chabakuk*, whose prophetic vision evokes the theophany at *Har Sinai*. *Acherim omrim* — others say: read *ba-chodesh ha-shlishi*, the narrative of *Matan Torah* at *Har Sinai* itself. And the *haftarah* in that view is from *Ma'aseh Merkavah* in *Yechezkel* — the heavens open and a divine vision unfolds, powerfully capturing the theophanic dimension of *Matan Torah*. It is worth noting that the significance of *Matan Torah* is not merely the transmission of law but the direct encounter with God — the theophany itself — and the *haftarah* of *Ma'aseh Merkavah* reflects precisely that.

### Two-Day Order

The *Gemara* interjects: *ve-haidna de-ika terei yomei, avdinan ke-tarveihu ve-ifcha* — nowadays that there are two days of *Shavuot*, we follow both opinions, but in reverse order. On the first day we read *ba-chodesh ha-shlishi* with the *haftarah* of *Ma'aseh Merkavah*, and on the second day we read the more generic *Shiva Shavuot*. This reversal follows the same pattern observed by *Pesach*: we prioritize the reading that most directly engages the essential character of the day, and so the narrative of *Matan Torah* takes precedence on the first day.

### Practical Question

This raises a fascinating practical question. A rabbi serving a small community where a *minyan* will only be present on the second day of *Shavuot* — which reading should he use? On one hand, the second day's prescribed reading is *Shiva Shavuot*. On the other hand, if there is only one opportunity to read from the *Torah* that *Shavuot*, one naturally wants it to be *ba-chodesh ha-shlishi* and *Matan Torah*. One might argue from the *Gemara*'s logic that since the *Gemara* goes out of its way to reverse the order and place *ba-chodesh ha-shlishi* first, this indicates it is the more weighty reading. On the other hand, since *Shiva Shavuot* was the first opinion in the *braita*, perhaps it holds primary standing. Interestingly, the *Rambam* seems to favor *Shiva Shavuot* as the more primary reading, though other sources do not point in that direction. Given the *Gemara*'s evident preference for the *ba-chodesh ha-shlishi* reading — signaled by placing it first — that would appear to be the stronger choice for a community with only one opportunity to read.

## Rosh Hashanah

### Braita & Opinions

For *Rosh Hashanah*, the *braita* prescribes *ba-chodesh ha-shevi'i* — the section in *Pinchas* dealing with the *korbanot* of the seventh month. This is specific to *Rosh Hashanah* by calendar placement, but does not really speak to the profound character of the day. The *haftarah* according to this view is *Ha-ben yakir li Ephraim, zachor ezkrenu od* — a passage about divine remembrance, appropriate for a day when God remembers us. *Ve-yesh omrim* — others say: read *ve-Hashem pakad et Sarah*, God's remembering of Sarah, which according to tradition occurred on *Rosh Hashanah*, and which is quintessentially about God remembering us for good. The *haftarah* in this view is *Chana*, whose story closely parallels that of Sarah.

### Two-Day Practice

*Ve-haidna de-ika terei yomei* — with two days of *Rosh Hashanah*, the *Gemara* prescribes: *yoma kama* — the first day — begins with the second opinion's reading: *ve-Hashem pakad et Sarah* with the *haftarah* of *Chana*. And for the second day — rather than reverting to the generic *ba-chodesh ha-shevi'i* — we read *ve-ha-Elokim nisa*, the *Akeidah*, with the *haftarah* of *Ha-ben yakir li Ephraim* — the merit of the *Akeidah* and its connection to the *shofar*. It is striking that even for the second day, the *Gemara* does not permit a generic or less evocative reading. Every day of *Rosh Hashanah* must be anchored in the essential themes of the day — divine remembrance, judgment, and the *zechus* of *Avraham*. One might attribute this to the gravity of *Yom HaDin*, which demands that the *kriyah* speak directly and urgently to the character of the day.

### Maftir Practice

One might note that the *Gemara* here does not separately identify a *maftir* reading from a different section. The implication is that there was no distinct *maftir* from a separate passage — a practice that differs from what we do today, where *ba-chodesh ha-shevi'i* from *Pinchas* serves as the *maftir*. As was correctly pointed out in discussion, we do read that passage on *Rosh Hashanah*, but as *maftir* rather than as the primary *kriyah*. The question of when exactly the association of the *Akeidah* with *Rosh Hashanah* entered into the *krias ha-Torah* is an open one; even if the broader theological and liturgical association is ancient, its formal expression in the *Torah* reading appears to be a later development.

## Yom Kippur

### Morning Service

*Beyom Hakippurim korin Acharei Mos* — on *Yom Kippur* the morning reading is *Acharei Mos*, which encompasses both the *avoda* of the *Kohen Gadol* and speaks directly to the nature of the day. The *haftarah* is *Ko amar Ram V'nisa* — "So says the One who is high and exalted" — a passage centrally concerned with *teshuva*.

### Afternoon Service

*U-ve-mincha korin ba-arayot* — in the *mincha* service, after the section of *avoda* in *Acharei Mos*, the reading continues with the *arayot*. The *haftarah* is *Yonah*, which is thematically connected to *teshuva* in the most direct way imaginable. As for why we read *arayot* in the afternoon, *Rashi* offers a specific rationale: transgressions in this domain are particularly prevalent, the *yetzer* is powerful regarding them, and therefore reading this section is deeply *me'inyano shel yom*. One might also observe that it functions as a continuation of the morning's reading. It was also wryly suggested that with so extensive a list of *arayot*, even the most troubled individual is likely to find at least some they have not transgressed — a source of comfort on a day of judgment.

### Aggadic Teaching

Because the *haftarah* of *Ram V'nisa* has been cited, the *Gemara* pauses for an *aggadic* teaching. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: kol makom she'ata motzei gevuraso shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu, ata motzei anvasnuso* — wherever you find God's power, you find His humility. And this teaching, the *Gemara* notes, is *davar zeh kasuv ba-Torah, ve-shanui ba-Nevi'im, u-meshulash ba-Kesuvim* — written in the *Torah*, repeated in the *Nevi'im*, and stated a third time in the *Kesuvim*.

In the *Torah*: *ki Hashem Elokeichem hu Elokei ha-elokim va-adonei ha-adonim ha-Eil ha-gadol ha-gibor ve-ha-nora asher lo yisa fanim ve-lo yikach shochad* — God is supreme, transcendent, above all. And immediately following: *oseh mishpat yasom ve-almana ve-oheiv geir lases lo lechem ve-simla* — yet this same transcendent God lowers Himself to care for the orphan, the widow, and the stranger.

In the *Nevi'im*: *Koh amar Ram V'nisa shochein ad ve-kadosh shemo marom ve-kadosh eshkon* — God dwells on high, holy and elevated. And immediately: *ve-es daka u-shfal ruach, le-hachayos ruach shfalim u-le-hachayos leiv nidka'im* — to give spirit to those of lowly bearing, to revive the broken-hearted.

In the *Kesuvim*: *solu la-rochev ba-aravot be-Yah shemo* — God is exalted in the heavens. And immediately: *avi yesomim ve-dayan almanot* — Father of orphans and judge of widows.

The *Gemara*'s point here seems to carry a message that extends beyond theology. The prophets — *Yeshayahu*, *Amos*, and others — were constantly rebuking the people for the disconnect between religious observance and basic ethical conduct: elaborate *korbanot* brought alongside the neglect of justice. The *pasuk* quoted here suggests that the truest expression of recognizing God's greatness is not separatism or exclusive ritual behavior, but rather translating that recognition into how one treats the most vulnerable members of society. Sanctity and care for the marginalized are not two separate things — they are, as this *pasuk* teaches, the same thing seen from two sides.

## Sukkot Readings

### First Days

*Yom tov ha-rishon shel chag korim be-parashat moadot she-be-Torat Kohanim* — the first day of *Sukkot* returns to the more generic reading of *shor o chesev o ez* from *Emor*. With *Sukkot*, there are simply fewer dedicated narrative passages available than for *Pesach*, so the generic *moadot* section is used more broadly. The *haftarah* is *Hineh yom ba la-Hashem* from *Zechariah*, which presents a Messianic vision in which all nations ultimately come to recognize God and observe *Chag HaSukkot*.

*Ve-idna* — the *Gemara* interjects — with two days of *Yom Tov*, the second day reads the same *Torah* portion as the first. This itself reflects how much less material is available for *Sukkot* compared to *Pesach*. The *haftarah* for the second day is *Vayikahalu el ha-Melech Shlomo ba-chag* — the dedication of the *Beit HaMikdash*, which took place on *Sukkot*.

### Intermediate Days

*Ve-she'ar kol yemei ha-chag korim be-korbanot ha-chag* — for the intermediate days of *Sukkot* one reads the *korbanot* of the *chag*. This works particularly well for *Sukkot* since the *korbanot* vary from day to day — the famous diminishing sequence of bulls — and thus each day's reading is naturally differentiated.

## Pesach Readings (II)

### Torah Selection

It is worth pausing to examine the structural logic underlying the *Torah* selections across the *Yomim Tovim*. The pattern that emerges is that *shor o chesev o ez* from *Emor* tends to be used for the early days of *Yom Tov*, while *Kol Habechor* — beginning with *aser te'aser* — from *Devarim* tends to serve the latter days. Both sections share a defining characteristic: although they address the *moadim* and their associated obligations, they do so in a manner considerably broader than the narrow *korban mussaf* focus of *Pinchas*. Both engage the *chag* as a societal and agricultural phenomenon, not merely a *Mikdash*-centered ritual one.

## Sukkot Readings (II)

### Last Days

*Yom tov ha-acharon* — the last day of *Sukkot* (and by extension *Shemini Atzeret*): *korim Kol Habechor*. The *Gemara* specifies: *mitzvot ve-chukim u-vechor* — one reads from the beginning of this larger section, starting with *aser te'aser*, and not from *Kol Habechor* alone. This means one begins with *aser te'aser* and reads through to *Kol Habechor*.

The *haftarah* is *Vayehi ke-chalot Shlomo* — a passage from toward the end of the *Beit HaMikdash* dedication narratives in *Melachim*, which mentions *u-va-yom ha-shemini shilach et ha-am* — on the eighth day he sent the people home. Whether that eighth day is *Shemini Atzeret* or the eighth day following *Sukkot* is a matter of interpretation, but the mention of the eighth day connects naturally to *Shemini Atzeret*.

### Aser Te'aser Theme

*Rashi* explains directly why the reading begins earlier, with *aser te'aser* rather than with *Kol Habechor* itself: *sheyesh be-osah parsha mitzvot ve-chukim harbe ha-nohagos ba-chag be-oso zman ki zman asif* — this section contains many *mitzvot* and laws that apply during the *chag*, since it is the time of the harvest gathering. The season of *Sukkot* is *zman asifenu* — the time of ingathering — and the *aser te'aser* section speaks directly to the obligations that arise at that time.

Indeed, one need only scan the content of *aser te'aser* to appreciate why it is so central. It opens with the laws of *ma'aser*, *shmita*, and agricultural tithes. It includes *ki yihyeh becha evyon me'achad acheicha be-achad she'arecha* — the obligation to lend to the poor. It includes the laws of the Hebrew slave and release. As *Rashi* notes: *ve-od yesh be-osah parsha ma'aser ani u-mitzvas maton, she-titen u-fasoach tiftach ve-ha'anek ta'anik le-eved Ivri ve-shiluach hofshi ve-ha-avtenu* — all of this sense of responsibility toward those who are less fortunate is a central theme that must be heard on the *chagim*, and in particular around *Sukkot*.

The *Torah* itself returns to this theme repeatedly when speaking of *Sukkot*: *ve-samachta ata u-veitecha ve-al ha-Levi asher bi-she'arecha lo sa'azvenu* — rejoice, you and your household — but do not abandon the *Levi*, who has no portion. The abundance of the harvest is precisely the moment to share with those who have nothing. This is why on *Shemini Atzeret* specifically — the culmination of the *Sukkot* period — the reading begins with *aser te'aser* and encompasses all of these obligations, even when on other *yomim tovim* one might begin directly with *Kol Habechor*.

In practice, consulting a standard *siddur* confirms a nuanced approach: if the last day of *Pesach* or the second day of *Shavuot* falls on *Shabbos*, one begins from *aser te'aser* — since the additional *aliyot* of *Shabbos* and the absence of time pressure allow for the fuller reading. On a weekday, one begins directly with *Kol Habechor*. On *Shemini Atzeret*, however, one always begins with *aser te'aser* regardless — because *Sukkot* is the very season to which these obligations most acutely apply, and the message of responsibility to the poor must be heard in full.

The *Gemara* then continues: *u-maftirin Vayehi ke-chalot Shlomo* — and indeed, these passages from the dedication of the *Beit HaMikdash* run through all of *Sukkot*, since the dedication itself took place on *Sukkot* and was completed with the sending home of the people on the eighth day.

*Le-machar korin Ve-zot ha-berachah* — the following day, *Shemini Atzeret* in *Chutz La'aretz* (i.e., *Simchat Torah*), one reads *Ve-zot ha-berachah*. The *haftarah* is *Vaya'amod Shlomo* — another passage from *Shlomo*'s prayer at the *Beit HaMikdash* dedication.

Here *Tosafot* raises an important point. He notes that some communities are accustomed to reading *Vayehi acharei mot Moshe* as the *haftarah* instead, and he calls this *shibush* — a corruption — since the *Shas* does not say so. He acknowledges that some attribute this change to *Rav Hai Gaon*, but adds: *aval einam yodim ha-svara mai shina seder ha-Shas ve-takana shel Rav Hai* — they do not know the reasoning for departing from the order of the *Gemara* and the enactment of Rav Hai.

This is a striking contrast with another *Tosafot* we will encounter shortly, which accepts changes from *Gemara* practice with equanimity when a literary source can be cited. What the *Gemara* itself makes clear through its repeated use of *ha'idna nahog* or *nahag alma* is that these practices carry an inherent fluidity. The *Gemara* is not recording a different *psak*; it is observing that practices have evolved — *nahag alma*, the world came to practice it this way — without any formal rabbinic enactment. The phrasing itself legitimizes organic change. In light of this, what is remarkable is not that practice has evolved, but that *Tosafot* in certain instances finds such evolution troubling. The answer appears to be that *Tosafot* is more comfortable when a change can be anchored in a literary source — *Masechet Sofrim*, the *Psikta*, or similar — rather than in undocumented popular evolution alone.

## Special Dates

### Chol HaMoed Shabbat

*Amar Rav Huna amar Rav: Shabbat she-chala lihyot be-cholo shel mo'ed, bein be-Pesach bein be-Sukkot* — for *Shabbat Chol HaMoed*, whether of *Pesach* or *Sukkot*, the reading is *Re'eh ata omer elai* — the passage about the second *luchot* and the *shalosh regalim*. The passage is chosen because its conclusion deals with the *regalim*, though we begin somewhat earlier to allow for more *aliyot*.

The *haftarah* for *Shabbat Chol HaMoed Pesach* is *ha-atzamot ha-yaveshot* — the vision of the dry bones from *Yechezkel*. According to *Chazal*, this vision refers to those who attempted to leave *Mitzrayim* prematurely. In the broader sense it speaks of revival, redemption, and the return of those who are literally and figuratively dead to life — deeply resonant with the *geulah* theme of *Pesach*.

For *Shabbat Chol HaMoed Sukkot*, the *haftarah* is *be-yom bo Gog* — the prophetic vision of *Gog u-Magog* from *Yechezkel*. Both *haftarot* carry a Messianic dimension: the *Pesach* reading focuses on redemption and revival, while the *Sukkot* reading centers on the eschatological battle that precedes the ultimate divine recognition by all nations — including the universal observance of *Chag HaSukkot* envisioned in *Zechariah*.

### Chanukah

*Be-Chanukah be-nesiyim* — on *Chanukah* we read the *nesiyim*, the dedication offerings of the tribal princes, from *Bamidbar*. This is appropriate because of *Chanukat ha-Bayit*, and conveniently there are enough readings across the eight days to cover the entirety of *Chanukah*. The *haftarah* is the vision of the *menorah* in *Zechariah*. If two *Shabbatot* fall within *Chanukah* — a rare occurrence — the first uses the *haftarah* of the *menorah* from *Zechariah*, and the second uses the vision of the *menorah* that *Shlomo* built, from *Melachim*.

### Purim & RH

*Be-Purim Vayavo Amalek* — on *Purim* the reading is *Vayavo Amalek*. From this point forward the *Gemara* ceases to mention *haftarot*, since the occasions that follow are neither *chagim* nor *Shabbat*, and therefore no *haftarah* is read.

For *Rosh Chodesh*, the reading is *Roshei Chodsheichem* — the section in *Pinchas* dealing with the *korbanot* of *Rosh Chodesh*. The *Gemara* moves directly to the *haftarah* without explicitly addressing when *Roshei Chodsheichem* is read (it serves as *maftir*). If *Rosh Chodesh* falls on *Shabbos*, the *haftarah* is *Ve-haya midei chodesh be-chodsho* from *Yeshayahu* — the passage that speaks of every *chodesh be-chodsho ve-Shabbos be-Shabbato*, people gathering to worship God. It is notable that in the time of the *Nevi'im*, *Rosh Chodesh* was a considerably more prominent occasion than we experience it today — *madua at holechet elav ha-yom lo chodesh ve-lo Shabbos* — and the pairing of *Chodesh* and *Shabbos* in that *haftarah* reflects that historical weight, even if our contemporary practice has diminished the standing of *Rosh Chodesh* considerably.

If *Rosh Chodesh* falls on a Sunday — *be-echad be-Shabbos* — the practice is to read the *haftarah* of *Vayomer lo Yehonatan: machar chodesh* — the passage of *Machar Chodesh* — on the *Shabbos* before. The *Gemara* does not specify what is done when *Rosh Chodesh* is two days, and in practice different communities handle this differently.

## Tisha B'Av Cycle

### RCA Av

*Amar Rav Huna: Rosh Chodesh Av she-chal lihyot be-Shabbos*, if *Rosh Chodesh Av* falls on *Shabbos* — *maftirin Chodsheichem u-Mo'adeichem sane'ah nafshi hayu alai le-torach*. This *haftarah* from *Yeshayahu* addresses *Rosh Chodesh* through *Chodsheichem*, and simultaneously carries the message of divine rebuke and impending catastrophe through *sane'ah nafshi* — fitting for the beginning of *Av*. This passage is part of the section known as *Chazon* — *Chazon Yeshayahu ben Amotz*. As we will see from *Tosafot*, this is not our practice.

The *Gemara* then pauses on the phrase *ve-hayu alai le-torach* — "they have been a burden to Me." What does this mean? *Amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu: lo dayan le-resha'im she-hem chotim le-fanai ela she-matrichim osi le-da'at eizeh gezeirah kasha avi aleihem* — it is not enough that they sin before Me; they then burden Me with having to determine what severe punishment to bring upon them.

### Tisha B'Av

*Be-Tisha b'Av gufay mai maftirin* — what is read on *Tisha b'Av* itself? The *Gemara* asks this without first addressing the *Shabbos* preceding *Tisha b'Av*, since that *Shabbos* has no special *kriyah* in the *Gemara*'s framework — only *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh* carries independent weight by virtue of being *Rosh Chodesh* itself.

*Amar Rav: Eicha hayesa le-zona* — the *haftarah* on *Tisha b'Av* is from *Chazon Yeshayahu* as well.

As for the *Torah* reading: *tanya im lo tishme'u li* — the *braita* prescribes the *brachot u-kelalot* in *Bechukotai*, consistent with the earlier principle that on *taaniyot* one reads *brachot u-kelalot* — a passage laying out the consequences of sin. *Ve-Noson bar Yosef omer* — another opinion holds: *ad ana yin'atzuni ha-am ha-zeh* — the passage about the *Meraglim*, since *Tisha b'Av* is the day associated with the sin of the *Meraglim*. *Ve-yesh omrim* — yet another opinion: *ad masai la-eidah ha-ra'ah ha-zot* — also from the *Meraglim* narrative. Both latter opinions reflect the drive, evident throughout this *Gemara*, to link the reading to a specific narrative rather than a generic passage.

*Amar Abaye: Ha'idna nahagu alma le-mikri ki tolid banim u-vnei vanim* — Abaye reports that the practice nowadays is to read *Ki tolid banim u-vnei vanim*, which addresses punishment for sin with particular focus on exile from the land — painful and appropriate for *Tisha b'Av*, but considerably less harsh than the *brachot u-kelalot* of *Bechukotai*. The *haftarah* in current practice is *Asof asifeim* — a bleak prophetic vision of the devastation of exile. This is our practice.

### Tosafot Defense

*Tosafot* acknowledges that our practice differs substantially from what the *Gemara* prescribes. *Ve-ein anu osin kein* — we do not follow the *Gemara*'s prescription for *Rosh Chodesh Av* falling on *Shabbos*. Instead, the *Shabbos* before *Tisha b'Av* uses *Chazon Yeshayahu*, and earlier *Shabbatot* in the period preceding use other *haftarot* from *Yirmiyahu*. *Ve-ha-ta'am: lefi she-anu nohagim al pi ha-Psikta* — the justification is that our practice follows the *Psikta*, a recognized literary source. *Tosafot* is at peace with this change because it is anchored in a literary authority. He contrasts this favorably with changes that lack such grounding.

Significantly, *Tosafot* here also acknowledges the change in *taanit* readings: we read *Vayeichal* on a *taanit*, while the *Mishnah* says *brachot u-kelalot*. He notes that this follows *Masechet Sofrim*. The pattern is consistent: *Tosafot* accepts organic evolution in practice when it can be attributed to a recognized literary source — *Psikta*, *Masechet Sofrim* — but is considerably more exercised when changes appear to lack such authority.

### Pur'anyot & Nechamah

The practice that *Tosafot* describes — which is entirely absent from the *Gemara* — is the well-known sequence of three *haftarot* of *pur'anyot* preceding *Tisha b'Av* and seven *haftarot* of *nechamah* following it. *Tosafot* outlines the system: the three of *pur'anyot* are *Divrei Yirmiyahu*, *Shim'u dvar Hashem*, and *Chazon Yeshayahu*. After *Tisha b'Av* come the seven of *nechamah* — *Nachamu nachamu*, *Vatomer Tzion*, *Aniyah so'arah*, *Anochi anochi*, *Rani akarah*, *Kumi ori ki va orech*, and *Sos asis* — followed by the two of *teshuvah*: *Dirshu Hashem be-himatzo* and *Shuva Yisrael*, the latter being *Shabbat Shuva*.

There is even a charming internal logic to the sequence of the seven *nechamah haftarot*, reading almost as a dialogue. God declares *Nachamu nachamu ami* — comfort, comfort My people — addressing the *nevi'im* as messengers. The *nevi'im*, in turn, relay this to *Knesset Yisrael*, who responds through *Aniyah so'arah lo nuchamah* — "the afflicted one has not been comforted" — because the people want to hear the comfort directly from God, not through intermediaries. God then responds directly: *Anochi anochi hu menachemchem* — I Myself will comfort you. At which point the rejoicing breaks out: *Rani akarah*, *Sos asis*, *Kumi ori ki va orech*. Whether or not one traces this precise narrative reading to the *Psikta* directly, it is the established *minhag* and *Tosafot* endorses it as inviolable: *ha-minhag lo yishtaneh le-olam al pi ha-Psikta*.

## Ma'amados

### Ma'amados Purpose

The *Gemara* now turns to *ma'amados*. The institution of *ma'amados* involves groups of representatives from cities throughout *Eretz Yisrael* who gather when the *kohanim* of their region are on rotation to serve in the *Beit HaMikdash*. These groups fast and read from *Ma'aseh Breishis* — the account of Creation — as a way of connecting the people to the *korbanot* being brought on their behalf. This occurs on a weekly rotating basis.

*Mina hanei milei* — from where is it derived that they read from *Ma'aseh Breishis*? *Amar Rav Ammi: ilmalei ma'amados lo niskaymu shamayim va'aretz* — were it not for the *ma'amados*, heaven and earth would not continue. *She-ne'emar: im lo brisi yomam va-layla, chukot shamayim va-aretz lo samti* — "were it not for My covenant day and night, the laws of heaven and earth I would not have set." Here *brisi* is understood to refer to the *korbanot*, not the *Torah*, since it is the people's connection to the *korbanot* — expressed through the *ma'amados* — that sustains the cosmic order. Reading *Ma'aseh Breishis* is therefore fitting because it is the very acts of creation that are being sustained through this connection.

### Abrahamic Covenant

*Ve-chtiv: vayomer Hashem Elokim bamei eda ki ireshena* — Avraham asks God: how will I know that I shall inherit the land? The *Gemara* reads this not as a challenge to God's truthfulness, but as an expression of concern about the people's ability to remain worthy of the divine promise. What if they sin? God responds: *kach li egla meshuleshet* — the *korbanot* will sustain them. Avraham then presses further: *Ribbono shel olam, teineh bi-she'at she-Beit HaMikdash kayam — bi-she'at she-ein Beit HaMikdash kayam mah te'ei aleihem* — what will be when there is no *Beit HaMikdash*?

God replies: *kvar tikanti lahem seder korbanot; kol zman she-korim bahem, ma'aleh ani aleihem ke'ilu makrivim le-fanai korban u-mochel ani al kol avonoseihem* — I have already given them the order of the *korbanot*; whenever they read these passages, I consider it as if they have brought a *korban* before Me, and I forgive all their sins.

The *Gemara* invokes this teaching to explain why the *ma'amados* read *Ma'aseh Breishis*: while the *Beit HaMikdash* stands, the *korbanot* themselves sustain creation. When it is absent, reading about the *korbanot* — or, as in the case of the *ma'amados*, reading about *Ma'aseh Breishis* which those *korbanot* sustain — serves in their stead. It also ties back elegantly to the festival readings discussed throughout this *daf*: when we read the passages of *korbanot* on the *moadim*, we are participating in this same substitution that God Himself established in covenant with Avraham. The *Gemara* universalizes the *pesuk*'s concern — it is not merely about inheriting *Eretz Yisrael*, but about what will keep the world itself in existence; and the answer is the study and reading of the *seder korbanot*.

## Taanis & Kelalot

### Taanis Rules

*Be-taaniyot: brachot u-kelalot, ein mafsikin ba-kelalot* — on public fasts, the *brachot* and *kelalot* are read, and one may not interrupt the *kelalot* in the middle by ending one *aliyah* and beginning another. *Minani milei* — from where is this derived? *Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda amar Rabbi Assi: de-amar kra — Musar Hashem beni al tima'as* — do not despise God's *musar*. To interrupt the *kelalot* mid-reading suggests a kind of rejection or distaste for the rebuke, as though one is eager to be rid of it.

*Reish Lakish amar* — alternatively: *lefi she-eino min ha-bracha al pur'aniyot* — one should not make a *bracha*, specifically *birchas ha-Torah*, directly over catastrophic content. It is not fitting to pronounce a blessing immediately juxtaposed with dire punishment. This reflects the practice where each *oleh* recited *brachot* before and after his portion.

*Ela heichi avid* — how then does one manage practically? A *tanna* taught: *ke-she-hu matchil, matchil be-pasuk she-lifneihem* — one begins a *pasuk* or several *pesukim* before the *kelalot* begin, so that the *bracha* is pronounced over a non-problematic passage. *U-che-she-hu mesayem, mesayem be-pasuk she-achareihem* — and when concluding, one continues one or several *pesukim* beyond the *kelalot*, so that the closing *bracha* is similarly not directly over the *kelalot* themselves. *Tosafot* notes that one begins a few *pesukim* before rather than just one, since one must not leave fewer than three *pesukim* at the end of a *parsha*.

### Klala Types

*Amar Abaye: lo shanu ela be-kelalot she-be-Torat Kohanim* — this prohibition against interrupting applies only to the *kelalot* in *Torat Kohanim*, i.e., the *kelalot* of *Bechukotai* in *Vayikra*. *Aval kelalot she-be-Mishneh Torah, posek* — for the *kelalot* in *Mishneh Torah*, in *Ki Tavo*, one may interrupt.

*Mai ta'ama* — why the distinction? *Halalu be-lashon rabbim amurot u-Moshe mi-pi ha-gevurah amran* — the *kelalot* of *Vayikra* are phrased in the plural: *im lo tishme'u li*, *ve-im telchu imi be-keri* — all the people are sinning — and they are spoken in God's own voice: *va-asafti le-yasra etchem sheva al chatoteichem*. God Himself declares what He will do. They are thus harsher in both content and speaker.

*Aval halalu be-lashon yachid amurot u-Moshe mi-pi atzmo amran* — the *kelalot* of *Ki Tavo* are in the singular: *ve-haya im lo tishmah*, *yakecha Hashem becha* — not *bachem* — and they are spoken in Moshe's voice rather than God's. *Tosafot*, noting the apparent theological difficulty, adds in just two words: *be-ruach ha-kodesh* — Moshe spoke *be-ruach ha-kodesh*, certainly not from his own invention. The point is rather that the mode of expression — God speaking in first person versus Moshe speaking in God's name — renders the *kelalot* of *Vayikra* qualitatively harsher. Because the *kelalot* of *Ki Tavo* are less severe, it is permissible to interrupt them by dividing the reading into separate *aliyot*.

A vivid illustration: *Levi bar Buti hava kari u-kemagumgam kamei de-Rav Huna* — Levi bar Buti was reading and was stumbling, reading haltingly and with visible discomfort through the *kelalot*. *Rashi* explains that the reader was reciting them rapidly but struggling to get the words out — overwhelmed by the weight of what he was reading. *Rav Huna* said to him: *achsof nafshecha* — do as you wish; if you need to make a break, interrupt it. *Lo shanu ela kelalot she-be-Torat Kohanim; aval she-be-Mishneh Torah — posek* — here in *Mishneh Torah*, you may indeed interrupt.

### Klala Timing

*Tanya: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer: Ezra tikan lahem le-Yisrael she-yihyu korin kelalot she-be-Torat Kohanim kodem Atzeret u-she-be-Mishneh Torah kodem Rosh Hashanah* — Ezra enacted that the *kelalot* of *Torat Kohanim* should be read before *Shavuot*, and the *kelalot* of *Mishneh Torah* before *Rosh Hashanah*. In the annual reading cycle, this roughly works out naturally: the end of *Vayikra* is reached around *Shavuot* time, and *Ki Tavo* is reached in the weeks before *Rosh Hashanah*.

*Mai ta'ama* — the reason? *Amar Abaye ve-itayma Reish Lakish: kedei she-tichleh shana ve-kileloteiha* — so that the year should end together with its curses. Do not carry the curses of the old year into the new one. Mention them at the year's close, be done with them, and enter the new year with a clean slate.

This works straightforwardly for *Mishneh Torah* before *Rosh Hashanah*, since *Rosh Hashanah* is the new year. But for *Torat Kohanim* before *Shavuot* — *atu Atzeret Rosh Hashanah hi*? Is *Shavuot* really a new year? *Atzeret nami Rosh Hashanah hi de-tnan: ba-Atzeret al peirot ha-ilan* — yes, *Shavuot* is also a *Rosh Hashanah* of sorts, as the *Mishnah* in *Rosh Hashanah* teaches: on *Shavuot* judgment is rendered on the fruit of the trees. Therefore the end of the previous year's fruit cycle is the appropriate time to conclude the *kelalot* of *Torat Kohanim*, so as to enter the new fruit year on a positive note. *Tosafot* further notes that in practice we ensure these *kelalot* are read at least one *Shabbos* before the *Shabbos* immediately preceding *Shavuot* or *Rosh Hashanah*, so that the *Shabbos* entering the new year is read in a positive spirit.

### Baal Koreh Training

A final teaching from *Tosafot* on this *daf* deserves mention — one that any *baal koreh* will find striking. *Tosafot* cites the *Yelamdenu*, a literary source quoted in the context of *Parshat Yitro*: *ein anu rashai likro ba-Torah ad she-yachzor ha-parsha gimel pe'amim* — one is not permitted to serve as *baal koreh* until one has reviewed the *parsha* three times. The source is the verse *az ra'ah va-yisperah, hechinah ve-gam chakarah* — "then He saw it and related it, prepared it and also examined it" — three verbs of preparation — *ve-acharei khen va-yomer le-adam*. Three preparatory reviews before one leads the community in *krias ha-Torah*.

*Tosafot* adds: *ve-assur lismoch al ha-bimah, de-zman netinasah me-umad* — and one must stand without leaning on the *bimah*, since the giving of the *Torah* was done standing. A fitting note on which to conclude a *daf* devoted in its entirety to the laws and customs of *krias ha-Torah*. *Masechet Megillah* reaches its conclusion tomorrow.
