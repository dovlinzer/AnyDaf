# Nedarim 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shabbat Lamplight

### Reading by Lamplight

There is a *halacha* found in the *gemara* in *Masechet Shabbat* that a person is not permitted to read by the light of an oil lamp on *Shabbat*. The reason the *chachamim* gave for this is a *gezeirah* of *shema yiteh* — lest a person forget that it is *Shabbat* and tilt the lamp in order to bring the oil closer to the wick, which would constitute a violation of the *melacha* of *mavir*.

### Electric Lights Today

The question naturally arises as to whether this *halacha* applies nowadays when we have electric lights. The majority of *poskim* rule that it does not apply, both because there is no analogous way to tilt an electric lamp to make the light stronger, and because we are so accustomed to electric lights that the original concern simply does not obtain. Nevertheless, some are *machmir* and maintain that one should still exercise caution.

### Muktzeh Candles

Another important concept in this area concerns the *muktzeh* status of candles. If a candle was lit during *bein hashmashos* — the twilight period between Friday and *Shabbat* — it becomes *muktzeh* for the entire day. This follows from the principle of *migo d'itaktzai l'bein hashmashos itaktzai l'kuly yoma*: since the object was set aside at the onset of the holy day, that status persists through the whole of *Shabbat*. Accordingly, even if the candle goes out in the middle of the night, one still may not move the candlestick, because it was a *basis l'davar assur* — a base for a forbidden object — at the moment *Shabbat* began.

## Kinyan: Gold & Silve (II)

### Gold Acquires Silver

At the bottom of the preceding page — 46b — the *mishnah* states: *haZahav koneh et haKesef vehaKesef eino koneh et haZahav*, gold acquires silver, but silver does not acquire gold. According to the *mishnah*, gold is considered *peira* — a commodity — while silver is considered *matbeia*, currency. The governing rule of *kinyanim* is that the commodity acquires the currency: if one performs *meshichah* on the commodity, the currency is acquired by the seller wherever it may be. But if one pulls the currency, the commodity is not acquired to the buyer until the buyer separately performs *meshichah* on the commodity itself.

## Kinyan: Gold & Silve (II)

### Rav vs. Levi Debate

The *gemara* on 47a asks why gold should be classified as the commodity. A *machloket* emerges between Rav and Levi on this point. One holds that gold is *matbeia* — currency — because it is more valuable, while the other holds that silver is *matbeia* because it is more common. The *halacha* follows the view that silver is the currency.

### Meshichah Takana

The *gemara* further establishes that *mi-de'Oraita* — on the level of biblical law — *maot konot*: money itself constitutes a valid *kinyan*. However, the *chachamim* enacted a *takana* requiring *meshichah* to complete the transaction.

### Buyer Protection

This *takana* was enacted to protect the buyer in the event of a fire — a principle expressed by the phrase *nisrefu peirotecha ba'aliyah*. So long as the seller retains responsibility for the goods, he will exert himself to save them. If, however, a party retracts after having given money, the *beit din* subjects him to the curse of *Mi ShePara*, declaring that the One who punished the generations of the flood and the dispersion will punish one who does not keep his word.

## Taanis Tzibur

### Public Fasts

The *mishnah* in *Masechet Ta'anit* addresses the institution of *taanis tzibur*. When a *tzarah* befalls *Eretz Yisrael* — such as a drought — the *beit din* would declare a series of public fasts.

### Hissorerus Address

During these periods of communal crisis, the people would gather in the *rechovah shel ir*, the city square, where a *zaken* would deliver words of *hissorerus* to inspire the community to engage in genuine *teshuva*.

### Aron & Ashes

As part of this assembly, the *aron* would be brought out and *eifer* placed upon it, expressing the community's distress and *hachna'ah* before Hashem. The *sak* — sackcloth — was also part of this public display of submission.

### Tikun Ma'asim

The *gemara* emphasizes, however, that it is not the *sak* or the *taanis* itself that brings forgiveness, but rather the *tikun hama'asim* — the genuine improvement of one's actions. This is the true goal of any *taanis*: to move a person toward authentic *chazara b'teshuva*.

### Nineveh Example

This principle is illustrated from the story of Nineveh. The *posuk* does not say that *Elokim* saw their fasting; rather, it says *vayar Elokim es ma'aseihem* — He saw their deeds. The emphasis on actions over mere fasting is explicit and instructive.

### Between Man & God

We must examine our behavior and ensure we are conducting ourselves appropriately in *bein adam l'chavero* and *bein adam l'makom*. Through this dual rectification, we merit the *rachamim* of the *Borei Olam* and, ultimately, the *geulah sheleimah* speedily in our days.

## Sukkah & Ohel

### Rava's Question

Rava raises a pointed question against Rav: if Rav holds that even a *sukkah* higher than twenty *amot* can be valid when its *defanos* reach all the way up to the *sechach*, why is it that in the case of a bed one requires *asara tefachim*? Even if the bed stood less than *asara tefachim* high, it should already be considered an *ohel* — for according to Rav, a space of *asara tefachim* is not a prerequisite for *ohel* status.

### Intent of Walls

The *gemara* answers on Rav's behalf: *hasam defanos le'ohel avidi* — there, in the case of the *sukkah*, the *defanos* are constructed for the sake of the *ohel*. But in the case of the bed: *hacha karayei hamita lav le'ohel avidi* — the legs of the bed are not made for the sake of forming an *ohel*. Since they were not constructed with *ohel* intent, they can only confer *ohel* status under specific conditions.

### Asara Tefachim

When the bed legs reach a height of *asara tefachim*, the space beneath them is considered significant enough to constitute an *ohel* regardless of the original purpose for which the legs were made. But if the height falls short of *asara tefachim* and the structure was not constructed for *ohel* purposes, it does not attain *ohel* status. Consequently, it would not create a *chatzitzah*, and one would fulfill the relevant obligation. This is how Rav would answer Rava's challenge from the later *mishnah*.

## Elul & Rosh Hashanah

### Birkat HaChodesh

There is a well-established custom to recite a special prayer on the *Shabbat* before the beginning of each new month, petitioning Hashem to bless the coming month with life, peace, health, and prosperity. This prayer is known as *Birkat HaChodesh*. Yet one month each year is conspicuously absent from this practice: the month of *Tishrei*. On the *Shabbat* before *Rosh Hashanah*, we do not recite *Birkat HaChodesh*. Since *Rosh Hashanah* is *Rosh Chodesh* — the first day of *Tishrei* — the question demands an explanation.

### Elul as Blessing

The Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel of Rymanov, explains that we do not bless the month of *Tishrei* because the entire preceding month — the month of *Elul* — is itself one long blessing for *Tishrei*. *Elul* functions as the blessing; the month of preparation is the very vehicle through which *Tishrei* receives its sanctity.

### Elul Preparation

The month of *Elul* is a month of preparation, of introspection, and of *teshuva*. Through the work we undertake during *Elul*, we provide the blessing for the coming year. These days are designed for reflection: we look back at the year that has passed, consider the choices we have made, the actions we have taken, and the words we have spoken, and we ask ourselves where we can improve and how we can grow.

### Personal Reflection

This is the essence of *teshuva* — often translated as repentance, but more precisely meaning return: a return to our true selves and a return to our relationship with the Almighty. We look at the areas where we have fallen short and ask honestly how we can become more aligned with who we truly wish to be.

### Rambam Teshuva

The Rambam teaches that *teshuva* involves several distinct steps: *viduy* — verbalizing one's mistakes; *charata* — feeling sincere regret; and *kabbalah al ha-atid* — making a firm commitment to do better in the future. Yet the goal is not merely to repair the past; the goal is to genuinely transform oneself for the future.

### Blessing Year Ahead

As we draw closer to *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur*, we should take full advantage of these final days of *Elul*. The coming year is blessed through our own efforts and growth. May we all be inscribed and sealed in the book of life for a good, healthy, and meaningful year — *ketiva v'chatima tova*.

## Neshama vs. Guf

### Neshama Tikkun

The *neshama* descends from a very elevated place specifically in order to perform its *tikkun*. We observe that different people are drawn to different *mitzvot* with greater intensity than others, and this is no accident — it reflects the particular *tikkun* that each *neshama* has come to accomplish. A person must recognize that the reason he is in this world is to fix something specific, and that this specificity is meaningful.

### Arizal Teaching

The Arizal HaKadosh teaches that certain *mitzvot* call to a person more powerfully than they do to others — not that they are easier, but that one finds oneself drawn to be especially careful and exacting in their performance. That particular *mitzvah* is very likely one's *tikkun*. And conversely, something that a person finds exceptionally difficult may also be part of what he must rectify — whether it manifests as a love for a *mitzvah* or as a struggle against a particular inclination.

## Neshama vs. Guf (II)

### Neshama as Royalty

The Chovos HaLevavos, in *Sha'ar HaBechina*, Rabbeinu Bachya describes the *neshama* as royalty — like a king or queen — that comes from a supremely elevated realm. When it is placed within the *guf*, it finds itself in a kind of prison: something lowly, physical, and coarse. The *guf* desires the physical and the mundane; the *neshama* yearns for the spiritual and the elevated.

### Soul-Body Battle

The result is a constant battle between *neshama* and *guf*. The *guf* pulls downward; the *neshama* reaches upward. The work of a person in this world is precisely to make the *guf* subservient to the *neshama* — to render the physical subordinate to the spiritual. This is the central task of human existence, and it is the reason the battle exists.

### Reward & Struggle

Nor should one be discouraged by the difficulty of this struggle, for the greater the struggle, the greater the reward: *lefum tza'ara agra* — according to the pain, so is the reward.

## Parshat Vayera

### Avraham's Guests

This week's *parsha* is *Parshat Vayera*. At its opening, the *posuk* states *Vayera Elav Hashem*. Rashi explains that it was the third day following the *brit milah* of Avraham Avinu, and despite the intense heat of the day — *k'chom hayom* — Avraham was actively searching for guests to welcome. When three *malachim* appeared in the guise of travelers, he ran to greet them. This exemplifies the *midah* of *chesed* and the practice of *hachnasat orchim* at its most exalted.

### Orchim Greatness

This is a striking *gemara*: the *gemara* in *Masechet Shabbat* states *gedolah hachnasat orchim mi'kabalat pnei hashechina* — welcoming guests is even greater than receiving the Divine Presence. Avraham's conduct in the opening of *Parshat Vayera* stands as the paradigmatic illustration of this teaching.

## Animals Shabbat

### Restraint Types

The fifth chapter of *Masechet Shabbat*, *Perek Bameh Behemah*, opens with the question: with what may an animal go out on *Shabbat*, and with what may it not go out? The *mishnah* enumerates: a camel may go out with a halter (*afsana*); a dromedary with a nose ring (*chotem*); a Libyan donkey with a bit (*farumbiya*); and a horse with a chain (*shir*).

### Chains Tumah

The *mishnah* further states: *ve-chol ba'alei ha-shir yotze'im be-shir ve-nimshachim be-shir* — all animals that have chains go out with a chain and are led by the chain. One may sprinkle on them or immerse them in their places (*u-mazin aleihen ve-tovlin bi-mekoman*) if they become *tamei*. This provision applies because the metal chains are susceptible to *tumah* by virtue of their status as a *kli* — a vessel — and yet the *mishnah* permits immersing them in a *mikvah* while they remain on the animal.

### Animal Rest

The underlying concern throughout is the prohibition against an animal carrying a burden on *Shabbat*. The *Torah* commands *lema'an yanuach shorecha va-chamorecha* — so that one's ox and donkey may rest — which means an animal may not perform *melacha* on *Shabbat*. Carrying a *masa*, a burden, in the *reshus ha-rabim* constitutes *melacha*.

### Burden Definition

However, if an animal is wearing something for its own protection or to prevent it from running away, that item is not a *masa* but a *tachshit* — an ornament, or necessary equipment. The distinction between a *masa* and a *tachshit* is critical: if more restraint is placed on an animal than it actually requires, the excess weight becomes a burden. A heavy halter placed on a small goat, for instance, is not necessary for restraint and therefore constitutes a prohibited *masa*; the same halter on a camel is indispensable and therefore permitted.

### Restraint Necessity

The *gemara* explains the specific restraints listed in the *mishnah* with reference to the nature of each animal. The *afsana* for a camel is a simple rope halter, sufficient for its control. A dromedary, however, is considerably more difficult to lead, and therefore requires the *chotem* — a ring passed through the nose — which provides the necessary degree of control. Each animal's permitted restraint is calibrated to what is genuinely required for its management.

## Rabbi Akiva

### Water & Stone

Rabbi Akiva and his wife Rachel chose to endure the most difficult conditions, understanding that they were building their foundation upon *Torah* and upon learning. Rabbi Akiva was not always a great scholar; for many years he was a simple shepherd who could not even read the *alef-bet*. One day, while tending his flock, he noticed a stone with a deep hole carved into it. Upon being told that the hole had been worn by water dripping on it day after day, Rabbi Akiva drew the lesson: if soft water can pierce hard stone through sheer persistence, then the words of *Torah* — which are like iron — can certainly penetrate his heart.

### Learning Begins

At the age of forty, Rabbi Akiva went to the schoolhouse with his young son and the two of them began to learn the *alef-bet* together, starting completely from the beginning.

### Study Success

He spent the next twenty-four years in intensive study until he became the leader of tens of thousands of *talmidim*. What began as an adult learning the *alef-bet* with his child culminated in one of the greatest rabbinic careers in Jewish history.

### Rachel's Role

When Rabbi Akiva finally returned home, he told his students that all of his *Torah* knowledge was only thanks to his wife Rachel. His words were direct and unambiguous: *sheli v'shelachem shela hu* — everything that I have and everything that you have belongs to her.

## Yosef Na'ar

### Yosef Grooming

The *posuk* describes Yosef as a *na'ar* — a young boy — in the phrase *v'hu na'ar et bnei Bilhah v'et bnei Zilpah*. Rashi explains that this refers to *na'arut*: Yosef would fix his eyes, tend to his hair, and conduct himself with the bearing of a young man conscious of his appearance. The question is striking — what was a *tzaddik* of Yosef's stature doing concerning himself with his grooming while enslaved in Egypt, while his father mourned him?

### Simcha Purpose

The answer is that Yosef understood, with great wisdom, that in order to survive his situation, he needed to maintain *simcha* and a sense of self-worth. Had he allowed himself to deteriorate, had he succumbed to depression and ceased to care about himself, he would never have been able to withstand the *nisayon* of *Eshet Potiphar*, and he would never have risen to become the ruler of *Mitzrayim*. He needed to keep himself together.

### Ben Melech

Yosef's self-presentation was not an expression of *ga'avah* — it was not vanity or self-absorption. It was a tool for survival. He reminded himself: I am a *ben melech*, a son of Yaakov Avinu; I am of worth; I matter. That sense of identity and dignity kept him going. This carries a vital lesson for us: when we find ourselves in difficult circumstances, in something of a *golus*, we must keep our heads high, remember that we are children of the King, and maintain that *simcha* — because *simcha* is the very means by which we overcome the challenges we face.

## Chanukah Identity

### Greek Threat

This theme connects directly to *Chanukah*. The Greeks sought to erase Jewish identity — to strip away our sense of self-worth and make us feel indistinct from everyone else. Their assault was not merely physical but existential: they wanted us to believe we were no different, no more special, no more connected to anything beyond the material world.

### Chashmonaim

The *Chashmonaim* refused. Their response was an unequivocal assertion: we are *kadosh*, we are different, and we have a connection to the *Ribbono Shel Olam* that cannot be severed. Their resistance was grounded not in politics but in identity.

### Chanukah Simcha

What we celebrate on *Chanukah* is precisely that sense of identity and that *simcha* — the joy of knowing who we are and refusing to relinquish it. Wishing everyone a *freilichen Chanukah* and a *gut Shabbos*.

## Aseret Yemei

### Judgment Sincerity

The *Mishnah Berurah* explains that during these days, when we stand before *HaKadosh Baruch Hu* in judgment, we wish to demonstrate that we are taking our *mitzvot* seriously — that our *teshuva* is genuine and not merely performative.

### Chumrot Days

One of the ways we express this sincerity is by adopting *chumrot* during the *Aseret Yemei Teshuva* — stringencies that go beyond our ordinary year-round practice.

### Pas Yisroel

A classic example is *pas Yisroel*. Many people rely all year on the more lenient position and eat *pas paltar* — bread baked by a non-Jewish baker. During the *Aseret Yemei Teshuva*, however, the practice is to be more careful to eat only *pas Yisroel*.

### Sincere Effort

This is a concrete way of showing Hashem that we are making an effort — that we wish to live more carefully and more holily. It is not about the bread itself; it is about the gesture of striving beyond the baseline requirement of *halacha*.

### Yesod Avodas

This is a fundamental *yesod* in the *avodas Hashem* of these days. We want to demonstrate that our *teshuva* is sincere and that we are willing to go beyond the minimal requirement in order to find greater *chen* in the eyes of the *Ribbono Shel Olam*.
