# Bekhorot 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'aser Behemah Overview

### Three Gornim

The daf begins at the very bottom of 57b and moves into the practical dimensions of *ma'aser behemah*. The *mishna* teaches that there are three *gornim* — threshing floors — at which the tithe of animals is taken. The term *goren*, threshing floor, is already telling: it signals that the institution of *ma'aser behemah* is being modeled on *terumot u'ma'asrot*, the separation of tithes from grain. Just as grain tithes are associated with the threshing floor and the harvest season, so too *ma'aser behemah* is structured around defined seasonal periods. This reflects a biological reality — unlike humans, most animals do not mate at all times of year but are only in heat during particular seasons, producing predictable birthing periods in the early spring, late spring, and early fall. The three *gornim* correspond to these three natural birthing windows.

### Two Primary Halachot

Two primary *halachot* govern this institution. The first concerns the status of an animal before *ma'aser behemah* has been taken. Unlike *ma'aser* of grain — where failing to separate *terumot u'ma'asrot* renders the produce *tevel*, a weighty biblical prohibition — *ma'aser behemah* does not render the flock *tevel* on a biblical level. In principle, one could slaughter and consume the entire flock without ever performing *ma'aser behemah* and would have done nothing more than fail to fulfill a *mitzvah*. Nevertheless, the Rabbis sought to create a structure parallel to grain tithes, likely as an incentive to ensure performance. They therefore declared that once the appointed period arrives, one is forbidden to eat from or *shecht* the flock until the *ma'aser* has been separated — a rabbinic form of *tevel*.

### Rosh Hashanah Divider

The second primary *halacha* concerns the *rosh hashanah* for *ma'aser behemah*, which functions as an absolute dividing line between years. Animals from one year cannot be grouped with animals from the following year for purposes of counting the tithe. This single hard boundary — as distinct from the three *gornim*, which are periodic counting occasions — is the point around which most of the *mishna*'s debate revolves: whether the *rosh hashanah le'ma'aser behemah* falls on *rosh chodesh Elul* or on *rosh chodesh Tishrei*.

---

## Three Birthing Seasons

### Birthing Periods

The *Gemara* opens with the question *mai shna tlat* — why specifically three times? Rava bar Shila answers: corresponding to *churfi*, the early births; *afli*, the late births; and *kayiti*, the summer births. These three natural birthing seasons among livestock give rise to three distinct cohorts of animals, each of which requires its own *ma'aser* count.

### Regalim Connection

The *Gemara* then asks a sharper question: *u'mai shna behani zimni* — why specifically these dates? What determined the precise calendar points that are now the subject of tannaitic debate? Rabbi Tanchum the son of Rabbi Chiya ish Kfar Akko explains: *kedei she'tehei behema metzuya le'olei regalim* — in order that animals be abundantly available when pilgrims ascend for the *regel*. The three *gornim* are timed to align with the three *regalim*.

---

## Religious Motivation

### Animals for Pilgrims

The connection to the *regalim* requires careful unpacking. One might initially assume that the reasoning is self-interested: since a person must ascend to Jerusalem for the *regel* anyway, the Rabbis conveniently timed *ma'aser behemah* to coincide so that he could bring his animals up in a single trip. But the *Gemara* does not say this. Instead, the reasoning runs in a more communal direction. Because one holds off from *shechting* or selling animals in order to perform *ma'aser behemah* properly, the full flock remains intact until the counting period arrives. At that point, two weeks before the *regel*, all those accumulated young calves and sheep — held back precisely because their owner was waiting to tithe — become available on the market. Pilgrims ascending to Jerusalem for the *regel* need animals for meat and for *korbanot*, and suddenly there is an abundant supply. The owner's religious discipline in deferring *shechita* creates a windfall of available animals for the community.

### Behavioral Assumptions

This explanation is, however, somewhat surprising. The *mishna* itself stated explicitly that before these periods arrive it is *mutar limkor ulishchot* — permitted to sell and slaughter. A person entirely focused on self-interest would therefore do the opposite: rush to sell or slaughter as much of the flock as possible before the *goren* period arrives, thereby reducing what he owes in *ma'aser behemah*. By the time the *regel* arrived, there would be very little left on the market. The *Gemara*'s explanation thus presupposes precisely the opposite behavior — that people do *not* act out of narrow self-interest when the period is approaching.

It is also worth noting that this explanation is primarily relevant to those already in Jerusalem or its immediate environs. The animals held over by farmers elsewhere in Israel — or in *chutz la'aretz* — are not obviously serving the pilgrims in any direct way. And from a market economics standpoint, flooding the market with supply right before a period of high demand benefits buyers but does not straightforwardly benefit sellers.

### Mitzvah Preference

The *Gemara* resolves this directly: *nichla lei le'inish nichla lei deleivid mitzvah bemamonei* — people *want* to perform a *mitzvah* with their own property. The *Gemara* assumes that when the *goren* period is two weeks away, a person's religious instinct will be to hold off, preserve the flock, and do the *mitzvah* fully. This is a striking assumption about human religious psychology, particularly given that financial self-interest points in the opposite direction. The *Gemara* here is asserting a model in which *nichla lei* — the innate desire to fulfill a *mitzvah* properly — overrides the incentive to minimize one's obligation. The implication is that most people, given the chance, would rather do more *mitzvah* than less, even at some financial cost. This is presented not as an ideal but as a working assumption about ordinary behavior.

The *Gemara* also explains the term *goren* for these periods: *d'tavlu k'goren* — because the Rabbis assimilated these periods to the threshing floor, in that one may not eat from the flock until *ma'aser behemah* is separated, just as one may not eat grain that remains *tevel*.

## Pras Duration

### Pras Definition

The *Gemara* then turns to the question *v'kamah Pras* — how long is a *pras*? The word *pras* literally means a half. Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Yehuda explains that a *pras* is no less than fifteen days. Rabbi Abbahu derives this: *pras* means *palga* — a half — but half of what?

### Learning Period

*Palga d'hilchot Pesach* — half of the thirty-day learning period that precedes the *regel*. A *baraita* teaches: *sho'alin v'dorshin b'hilchot ha'Pesach kodem ha'Pesach shloshim yom* — one asks and expounds the laws of Pesach for thirty days before the festival. Whether this thirty-day period relates primarily to the laws of the *chag* itself or to the *korbanot* of the *chag* is a separate question, but the operative point is that there exists a conceptual thirty-day window preceding the *regel*. A *pras* is half of that — fifteen days. This is why the phrases *pros ha'Pesach* and *pros Atzeret* serve as standard shorthand for "fifteen days before."

### Two Weeks Option

Within this broader discussion, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents and holds that one begins expounding the laws only two *shabbatot* — fourteen days — before the *regel*, not thirty. This alternative model of fourteen days rather than fifteen will resurface in the debate over the precise calendar dates.

---

## Date Opinions

### Ben Azzai Dates

Ben Azzai's position is: the 29th of Adar, the 1st of Sivan, and the 29th of Av. Some of these map closely onto the fifteen-days-before model; others require explanation.

### Rabbi Akiva Flexible

Rabbi Akiva does not fix a calendar date for the *goren* before Pesach. His reasoning: *Adar ha'samuch l'Nissan zimnin malei v'zimnin chaser* — the month of Adar immediately preceding Nissan may be either a full thirty-day month or a deficient twenty-nine-day month. Since Pesach falls on the 15th of Nissan, and fifteen days earlier may fall on either the 29th or 30th of Adar depending on the length of that Adar, Rabbi Akiva simply says "fifteen days before" without specifying a fixed date. Sometimes fifteen days before will be the 30th of Adar; sometimes the 29th.

### Adar Month Debate

Ben Azzai disagrees. He holds that *Adar ha'samuch l'Nissan l'olam chaser* — Adar is always a twenty-nine-day month — and therefore it is safe to specify the 29th of Adar as a fixed date. This is the extent of their practical disagreement on the pre-Pesach *goren*: Rabbi Akiva cannot specify a date because the month length varies; Ben Azzai can, because he holds the month is invariably deficient.

---

## 1st Sivan Reasoning

### Delay Rationale

The designation of the 1st of Sivan rather than fifteen days before Shavuot requires separate explanation. Shavuot falls on the 6th of Sivan, which means the 1st of Sivan is only five or six days beforehand — far short of the fifteen-day *pras*. The reason is that there are very few new births in the seven weeks between Pesach and Shavuot. The primary spring birthing season occurred around Nissan; whatever animals are born in the following seven weeks are, as it were, stragglers. The total number of eligible animals in this window is small to begin with.

Given this scarcity, the strategy shifts: rather than designating the *goren* fifteen days before and then allowing *shechita* and sale to deplete the limited supply, the goal is to postpone the *goren* as late as possible so that the maximum number of those few animals remains available when pilgrims arrive for Shavuot. If the *goren* were set fifteen days before — around the 20th of Iyar — owners would separate *ma'aser* and then begin selling and slaughtering their now-reduced flocks. By the time Shavuot arrived, there would be almost nothing left. By pushing the *goren* to the 1st of Sivan, practically everything is preserved until immediately before the *regel*.

---

## Elul Animals Dilemma

### Elul Separation

The 29th of Av requires the most involved explanation. Fifteen days before Sukkot — with Sukkot beginning on the 15th of Tishrei — would normally put the *goren* around *Rosh Hashanah* itself or near the end of Elul. But Ben Azzai's treatment of the pre-Sukkot *goren* is governed by his position on a more fundamental question: he is uncertain whether the *rosh hashanah le'ma'aser behemah* falls on *rosh chodesh Elul* or on *rosh chodesh Tishrei*. Since he cannot resolve this, animals born during Elul present an acute difficulty. They cannot be combined with animals from before *rosh chodesh Elul*, because if *rosh chodesh Elul* is the true *rosh hashanah*, they belong to a different year. They cannot be combined with animals born in Tishrei, because if *rosh chodesh Tishrei* is the true *rosh hashanah*, the Elul animals belong to the prior year. The only solution is to treat the Elul animals as a separate cohort — *ha'Elulin mitasrin bifnei atzman* — to be tithed on their own.

This being so, Ben Azzai cannot allow any of his three *gornim* to extend into Elul, because doing so would mix animals from before the possible dividing line with animals from after it. The pre-Sukkot *goren* must therefore be drawn before Elul begins — hence the 29th of Av.

### 29th Av Clarity

The *Gemara* asks: why the 29th of Av? Since Av typically has thirty days, the 30th of Av is the last day of the month, and logic might suggest that is the correct cutoff. The answer is that if we designate the 30th of Av as the *goren* date, then in years when Av happens to have only twenty-nine days, the day we designated as the 30th will actually be *rosh chodesh Elul*. Performing *ma'aser behemah* on *rosh chodesh Elul* would create the misleading impression that Av and Elul animals can be combined — the very confusion Ben Azzai is at pains to prevent. Moreover, even in a year when Av has thirty days, people who live at a distance from Jerusalem may not yet know when *rosh chodesh* was declared; they might perform the count on what turns out to be *rosh chodesh Elul* without realizing it.

Rashi adds that even on *rosh chodesh Elul* itself, there is no genuine risk of co-mingling in the technical sense, since any animal actually born that day would be less than eight days old and therefore ineligible for *ma'aser behemah*. But the concern is for signaling — *hekera bein chodesh ve'yashan* — one must maintain an unambiguous visual and practical distinction between years, and performing the count on *rosh chodesh Elul* compromises that clarity. The 29th of Av avoids all such confusion, since the 29th is always within Av regardless of how the month is reckoned.

## RE & RS Dates

### 1st Nissan

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon shift the pre-Pesach *goren* from the 29th of Adar to the 1st of Nissan. This is a difference of one day. They follow the model of Rabban Gamliel — *de'amar shtei shabbatot* — who holds that the period of expounding the laws of the festival before it arrives is two weeks, not thirty days. Accordingly, the relevant pre-festival window is fourteen days, not fifteen, and the *goren* falls fourteen days before Pesach. Since Pesach is the 15th of Nissan, fourteen days before is the 1st of Nissan. The pre-Shavuot *goren* remains the 1st of Sivan, as explained previously.

### 29th Elul Boundary

The major substantive divergence from Ben Azzai comes in the third *goren*. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon hold that *rosh hashanah le'ma'aser behemah* is the 1st of Tishrei. Because the dividing line between years falls there, the pre-Sukkot *goren* is drawn the day before that boundary — the 29th of Elul. There is no uncertainty here about Elul animals: on this view, everything born through the end of Elul belongs to the year that closes on *rosh chodesh Tishrei*, and it is all counted together in the 29th-of-Elul *goren*.

## Yom Tov Restriction

### Two Reasons

The *mishna* raises an obvious question: why not simply designate the 1st of Tishrei — *rosh hashanah* — as the *goren* date, rather than the 29th of Elul? The answer given is *mipnei shehu Yom Tov* — because it is Yom Tov and *ma'aser behemah* cannot be performed on Yom Tov.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: there is a more fundamental reason not to perform *ma'aser behemah* on the 1st of Tishrei, and it has nothing to do with Yom Tov. Even leaving aside the Yom Tov prohibition, performing the count *on* the very day that is *rosh hashanah* would mean conducting the pre-*rosh hashanah* count during the new year — blurring the boundary between the prior year's animals and the new year. Rashi notes that the mixing of actual animals is technically not a concern, since any animal born on *rosh hashanah* itself is less than eight days old and cannot be counted; but the optics are precisely the problem. Counting on *rosh hashanah* sends the signal that one may combine pre-*rosh hashanah* and post-*rosh hashanah* animals, which is the very distinction the *rosh hashanah* is meant to enforce.

The *Gemara* resolves the tension with *chada ve-od ka'amar* — the *mishna* is offering two reasons in sequence: the primary and more fundamental reason is the need to maintain an unambiguous distinction between years (*hekera bein chodesh ve'yashan*), and as an additional reason, *ve-od mipnei shehu Yom Tov* — moreover, one cannot perform *ma'aser behemah* on Yom Tov because of the *sikresta*, the red paint used to mark the tenth animal, which constitutes a form of writing or dyeing prohibited on Yom Tov.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot raises what appears to be an even more obvious objection: why emphasize the paint? There is a straightforward prohibition against performing acts of *hekdesh* — sanctification of property to the *Beit HaMikdash* — on Yom Tov, since such sanctification constitutes a form of transaction. Why does the *Gemara* invoke the relatively specific concern of *sikresta* when the more general prohibition of sanctifying on Yom Tov would suffice?

### Conceptual Questions

Tosafot's question opens into a deeper conceptual issue. With respect to *terumot u'ma'asrot*, the prohibition of performing them on Yom Tov rests on the principle that one thereby "fixes" the food — the act of separation renders the produce *muttar* to eat, which looks like a form of *tikkun*. But *ma'aser behemah* does not strictly work this way. On a biblical level, the flock is not *tevel* before separation; the rabbis merely prohibited eating from it until the *mitzvah* is performed. It may therefore be debatable whether the prohibition focuses on the *gavra* — the person who may not eat — rather than on the animal itself, in which case no *tikkun* of the flock occurs.

Tosafot's preferred answer is more structural: although *ma'aser behemah* results in the animal becoming *kadosh*, the mechanism is not an act of sanctification in the conventional sense. One does not declare "I hereby sanctify this animal as *ma'aser behemah*." Rather, one counts — one, two, three, ... ten — and the *kedusha* attaches automatically to whichever animal emerges as the tenth. It is an act of *designation* rather than an act of *hekdesh*, and it is that distinction that exempts it from the Yom Tov prohibition against sanctification. This is precisely why the *Gemara* must invoke the *sikresta* as the operative reason: absent that, there would be no Yom Tov violation in performing the count. This analysis raises important conceptual questions about *ma'aser behemah* — is the animal itself forbidden food before the count, or is the restriction a personal obligation on the owner? And is performing the count an act of sanctification or merely one of designation?

## Rabbi Meir Position

### Three RH Options

The *Gemara* now surfaces the explicit three-way debate about the *rosh hashanah le'ma'aser behemah*: Rabbi Meir holds it is the 1st of Elul; Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon hold it is the 1st of Tishrei; and implicit in the broader discussion is the position that the *rosh hashanah* falls at one of the three *gornim* dates themselves. It is against this background of unresolved disagreement that Ben Azzai's dilemma must be understood.

---

## Uncertainty Principle

### Ben Azzai Admits

A *baraita* records Ben Azzai's own acknowledgment of the impasse: *hallalu omrim kach ve-hallalu omrim kach* — these authorities say one thing and those say another. Faced with a genuine dispute over when the *rosh hashanah le'ma'aser behemah* falls, he cannot adjudicate between them.

### Elul Separation

His response — *Elulim mit'asrin le'atzman*, that Elul animals are tithed as their own cohort — is not simply an act of stringency. As was pointed out, it is simultaneously a significant leniency: by treating Elul animals as a separate group, one may well lack the minimum of ten animals in that cohort and thereby be entirely exempt from *ma'aser behemah* for that group. Moreover, the Elul separation may break apart cohorts that could otherwise have combined across the pre-Elul and post-Elul periods, again reducing the total obligation. The underlying principle — *ma'aser vadai amrachmana v'lo ma'aser safek*, the Torah requires tithe on that which is certainly obligated, not on that which is doubtful — means that once genuine uncertainty exists, the obligation dissolves.

### Make a Decision

The *Gemara* is not satisfied with Ben Azzai's non-decision. It presses: *velichzei ta'ama de-man mistaber* — let us examine whose reasoning is more persuasive and decide accordingly. This is a striking formulation. The *Gemara* is explicitly rejecting the model of responding to *machlokes* with blanket stringency, insisting instead that a *posek* has the obligation to analyze competing positions and reach a *psak*. The principle that one should be *machmir* in cases of doubt is not a substitute for intellectual adjudication — it is a fallback for when adjudication is genuinely impossible.

---

## Mesorah vs. Logic

### Self-Appraisal

If anyone could be expected to make a decision between competing positions, it would seem to be Ben Azzai, who famously declared *kol chachmei Yisrael domim elai ke-klipat ha-shum* — all the sages of Israel are to me like the peel of a garlic, like nothing — with one exception: *chutz min ha-kereach hazeh*, except for "this baldy." Rashi identifies the baldy as Rabbi Akiva, noting that Rabbi Akiva was bald and further connecting *kereach* to *korcha*, the patronymic of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, whom Rashi takes to be Rabbi Yehoshua the son of Rabbi Akiva. Tosafot rejects both points: chronologically Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha cannot have been Rabbi Akiva's son, and more fundamentally, why would Ben Azzai refer to Rabbi Akiva in so disparaging a manner? Tosafot suggests either that *kereach* was simply a name or that Ben Azzai was referring to some other bald individual — the text, remarkably, leaves the identity entirely unresolved. The broader point stands: Ben Azzai considered himself intellectually superior to virtually all his contemporaries, which makes his inability to decide between the positions all the more striking.

### Mesorah Answer

The *Gemara* resolves this through the voice of Yochanan: *mipi hashmua amruha, mipi Chagai Zecharia Malachi* — the ruling was transmitted orally, going back to the prophets Chagai, Zechariah, and Malachi. The dispute is not one of competing *svara* — of competing logical analyses where one can weigh the arguments and decide. It is a dispute about competing *mesorot*, competing traditions of transmission. One cannot adjudicate between traditions the way one adjudicates between arguments.

### Tosafot Clarify

Tosafot immediately clarifies Rashi's formulation. Rashi's language might suggest that the tradition was conveyed through *nevuah* — prophetic revelation — which would be problematic, since *halacha* is not determined by prophecy. Tosafot explains that the mention of Chagai, Zechariah, and Malachi does not invoke their role as *neviim* but rather their role as transmitters of the oral tradition. They appear in other contexts too as ancient links in the chain of transmission, signaling extreme antiquity — not prophetic authority. The point is that this is a very old *mesorah* whose content cannot be reconstructed through reasoning alone, not that it derives from *devar Hashem* in any direct prophetic sense.

### Rambam Issue

This raises a direct challenge to the Rambam's famous position. The Rambam argued, partly in response to Karaite attacks on the reliability of the oral tradition, that all recorded disputes in the *Gemara* arise from the use of human reasoning — of *drashos* and *sevarot* — to derive laws. Things that were received as direct *mesorah*, he maintained, were never subject to debate. The present *sugya* offers evidence against this. Here, the very source of the dispute is that there are competing *mesorot* — competing traditions about the *rosh hashanah le'ma'aser behemah* — and it is precisely the existence of that factual disagreement about the tradition that prevents adjudication. This is a case where a *mesorah* is debated, not derived, and that cuts against the Rambam's categorical claim. The *Gemara* here thus stands as significant counterevidence to that position.

## Elul Counting

### Three Groups

With Ben Azzai's framework established, the *Gemara* works through the practical consequences. Suppose one has five animals born in Av, five born in Elul, and five born in Tishrei. The rule that the Elul cohort cannot combine with either the pre-Elul or post-Elul cohort means these are three entirely separate groups, none of which reaches ten. Left to themselves, none of the three cohorts is subject to *ma'aser behemah*.

### Av-Tishrei Combine

However, the Av and Tishrei animals can combine with each other, even though they are separated by Elul, because they belong to the same halachic year — one simply goes eleven months from Tishrei back to Av without crossing the *rosh hashanah* boundary. The bridge of Elul is what cannot be traversed, but Av and Tishrei can meet without it.

### Rava Solution

Rava poses the following scenario: what if one has five animals from Av, five from Elul, and five from Tishrei, and wherever the *rosh hashanah* falls, one will end up with a group of ten that is certainly obligated? That is, if the *rosh hashanah* is *rosh chodesh Elul*, then Elul combines with Tishrei for ten; if it is *rosh chodesh Tishrei*, then Av combines with Elul for ten. In either case, some group of ten is definitely obligated in *ma'aser behemah* — one simply does not know which ten.

Rava's solution: *konsan l'dir l'atzer* — put all fifteen into the pen and ensure that at least one animal from the Elul group comes out as the tenth. As long as one animal from the Elul cohort is the designated *ma'aser*, the obligation is discharged regardless of how the underlying *rosh hashanah* question is resolved, since an Elul animal is part of the ten on either view. The remaining animals are then exempt in either scenario.

### Certainty Principle

The obvious objection to Rava's solution is that by combining all fifteen and ensuring one specific animal comes out tenth, one might be taking *ma'aser* from animals that are not properly in the same group — if the *rosh hashanah* is Tishrei, the Tishrei animals should not be in the count with Av and Elul, and those five Tishrei animals will need to combine with animals born later. Rava dismisses this: *asiri vadai amar Rachmana v'lo safek* — the Torah obligated only that which is certainly the tenth, not that which is doubtfully so. Once the Tishrei animals have been included in a count that resulted in a valid *ma'aser*, any residual uncertainty about whether those Tishrei animals were genuinely obligated dissolves in favor of exemption. The principle of *ma'aser vadai lo safek* operates symmetrically: it prevents imposing *ma'aser* where there is doubt, and it prevents re-imposing it on animals that have already passed through a valid *ma'aser* process.

This also responds directly to the broader question about Ben Azzai's *kula*. His ruling that Elul animals must be tithed separately is not merely stringency — the principle that *ma'aser* applies only to *vadai* cases means that the moment uncertainty is established, any obligation under that uncertainty dissolves. The farmer with few animals in each category may end up paying no *ma'aser behemah* at all, and that is the correct legal result, not a loophole.

## Ma'aser Method

### Standard Way

The *mishna* now turns from the question of *when* to take *ma'aser behemah* to the question of *how*. The procedure begins with *konsan l'dir ve'oseh petach katan* — gathering the animals into a pen and making a small opening, *kedei shelo shnayim yecholim latzet k'echad*, so that no two animals can exit simultaneously and must come out single file. One then counts aloud — *alef, bet, gimmel, dalet, hey, vav, zayin, chet, tet* — and the animal that emerges as the tenth is marked with red paint (*soker b'sikra*) and declared *ma'aser*: *harei zeh ma'aser*.

### Be'di'eved Option

The *mishna* then addresses cases of *b'di'eved*. If one did not paint the tenth animal, or did not count with a staff, or did not have the animals walk through the opening at all but simply counted them while they were standing or crouching, the *ma'aser* is still valid. The procedure of single-file exit, verbal counting, staff, and paint are *lechatchila* requirements; *b'di'eved*, even purely mental counting with the animals standing in place is sufficient.

### Tenth vs. Percent

What is *not* sufficient, even *b'di'eved*, is taking one out of every ten without sequential counting. If one had a hundred animals and divided them into groups of ten and removed one from each group — ten separate ones — without any sequential count, that does not constitute *ma'aser behemah*, even though the ratio is correct. Taking ten percent is not the same as designating the tenth.

### RY Disagrees

Rabbi Yosi the son of Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: he holds that taking one out of every ten does suffice, even without sequential counting.

## Jumping Back Issue

### Safek Created

If one of the already-counted animals jumps back into the pen — back among those not yet counted — a problem arises. That animal has already been exempt from the count by virtue of having been counted; it cannot be double-counted. But now it is indistinguishable from the uncounted animals. Any animal that subsequently exits may or may not be the already-counted one, creating *safek* — uncertainty — about the numbering of every subsequent animal.

Once this *safek* is introduced, the entire remaining group becomes exempt: *ma'aser vadai lo safek*. Since no animal in the remaining group can be identified with certainty as the tenth, the obligation of *ma'aser* cannot attach to any of them. Importantly, the animals already counted and exempt before the jump retain their status — they are not retroactively drawn back into obligation.

### Sacred Status

The difficulty is compounded if the *ma'aser* animal itself — the tenth — jumped back in and had not been painted. Now any one of the remaining animals might be the *ma'aser* animal, which carries the sanctity of *shelamim*-type *korban* and may not be eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The entire flock therefore carries *safek kedushat ma'aser*. The practical resolution is to let them graze until they develop a *mum* — a blemish — at which point they may be eaten by their owners as blemished *ma'aser*. This is precisely why the *mishna* prescribes the paint as standard procedure: a painted tenth is identifiable regardless of where it wanders, and the chaos of the jumping-back scenario is entirely avoided.

## Counting Sources

### Mother Incentive

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* that expands the counting procedure described in the *mishna*. The standard method places the mothers outside the pen and the young animals inside; the mothers call out (*vegayos*), and the young come out toward them, providing the incentive for the single-file exit. The *Gemara* asks why this elaborate system is necessary — why not simply remove the animals physically one by one? The answer is *yaavor* — the verse specifies that they must *pass under* the staff on their own; others may not carry them through. The *Gemara* also asks: why not simply throw food outside the pen and let the animals follow the food? Rav Huna answers: *gzeira mishum lakuach mishum yasom* — a rabbinic enactment on account of purchased animals and orphaned animals. Animals that were purchased by the owner, rather than born into his flock, are exempt from *ma'aser behemah*, as are animals whose mothers died at birth. By using the mothers as the draw, the procedure naturally excludes animals without mothers present — signaling to the owner that only animals born to his own flock are eligible, not purchased stock and not those whose mothers died at delivery.

### Treifa Exclusion

The *Gemara* then derives additional laws from the verse *kol asher yaavor tachat hashevet* — "all that passes under the staff." The phrase *kol asher yaavor* excludes the *treifa*: an animal that cannot walk on its own — for example, one missing a limb — does not "pass under" in the requisite sense and is therefore excluded from *ma'aser behemah*.

### Staff Mitzvah

From the phrase *tachat hashevet* — under the staff — the *Gemara* derives that it is a *mitzvah lechatchila* to count with a staff. If one did not use a staff, or counted the animals while they were standing or crouching rather than walking through, the *ma'aser* is still valid *b'di'eved*, as the *mishna* already stated. The verse *ha'asiri kodesh mikol makom* — "the tenth shall be holy in any case" — teaches that even when the procedural ideals were not followed, sanctity attaches to the tenth.

## Tenth Requirement

### Verbal vs. Mental

The *Gemara* further refines the *lechatchila* requirement. *V'ein li ela she-kara shemo asiri* — one might think the tenth is only valid if one verbally declares it "the tenth." What if one counted only mentally, without vocalizing? The verse *yiheyeh kodesh mikol makom* teaches that even purely mental counting suffices: the animal becomes *kadosh* as the tenth regardless. Note that the verse uses the word *asiri* — the tenth — not *ma'aser*, suggesting that the operative act is the designation of the animal as the tenth in a sequential count, not a formal declaration of sanctification.

### Reject Percent

The *Gemara* then addresses the position that one might suppose: perhaps it is enough to take any one animal out of every ten, without sequential counting, since the proportion is satisfied. *Talmud lomar asiri* — the verse insists on "the tenth," *v'ein zeh asiri* — and this is not "the tenth." One out of ten is a percentage; only a specific animal that emerged as the result of a sequential count reaching ten is "the tenth." Ten percent is not the tenth.

### Terumah Analogy

Rabbi Yosi the son of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that one out of ten does suffice, bases his position on an analogy cited by Abba Elazar ben Gomel (in some textual variants, ben Gamla or ben Gamla). Abba Elazar derives from the verse *v'nechshav lachem trumatchem* — "and your *terumah* shall be counted for you" — that the verse speaks of two types of *terumah* simultaneously: *terumah gedolah*, the standard priestly portion, and *trumat ma'aser*, the tenth that the Levites separate from their *ma'aser* and give to the *kohanim*. The verse establishes an analogy between them. Just as *terumah gedolah* may be taken *b'omed u'v'machshavah* — by estimate and by mental intention, without sequential counting — so too *trumat ma'aser* may be taken the same way. Rabbi Yosi then extends this analogy further: if *terumah* can be taken by estimate and mental intention without sequential counting, then *ma'aser* — whether of grain or of animals — can likewise be performed without making the animal specifically the tenth in a numbered sequence, provided the proportion is correct. This will be developed further in the continuation of the discussion.
