# Bava Batra 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Masechta Structure

### Daf 103 Opening

Today's daf is 103. We pick up immediately after the *mishnah* on 102b, beginning the seventh *perek* of the *masechta*.

### Perek Structure

To appreciate the position of *perek* seven within the larger structure of *Bava Batra*, it is worth stepping back for a bird's-eye view. The first two *perakim* form a natural unit centered on the laws of neighbors — building walls, questions of privacy, and activities in one's own domain that might encroach upon another's rights. The third *perek* addresses *chazaka*: the principle that three years of uncontested presence on land substantiates one's claim to it. *Perakim* four, five, and six then turn to matters of buying and selling — what is included in a sale of various objects, how acts of *kinyan* are performed, and questions of *mekach ta'us* when a seller delivers something other than what was promised. *Perek* seven, our present subject, is the concluding chapter of this group, the last of the *perakim* concerned with acts of commercial transaction.

### Future Sections

A meaningfully different section of the *masechta* begins with *perek* eight and runs through *perek* ten. Those chapters address death, inheritance, bequests, and the rabbinic forms of wills — material quite distinct in character from everything that precedes it. Within a few days, then, we will find ourselves in a significantly different part of *Bava Batra*.

## Beit Kor Dimensions

### Beit Kor Conversions

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 102b opens with the case of a *beit kor*, defined as thirty *se'ah*. To give a sense of scale: a single *beit se'ah* is approximately 5,000 square feet, or roughly 500-plus square yards. Thirty *beit se'ah* therefore amounts to approximately 18,000 square yards — somewhere in the vicinity of three and a half American football fields, or approximately four acres. That is the expanse of land under discussion.

### Se'ah & Mishkan

A helpful point of reference is the *chatzer* of the *mishkan*, which measured 50 by 100 *amot*, yielding 5,000 square *amot*. That corresponds to two *beit se'ah*, meaning a single *beit se'ah* is half the area of the *mishkan* courtyard. With that frame of reference in mind, the scale of a *beit kor* — thirty *beit se'ah* — becomes easier to appreciate.

## Rocks & Crevices

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: "בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ" – הָיוּ שָׁם נְקָעִים עֲמוּקִּים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אוֹ סְלָעִים גְּבוֹהִין עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים – אֵינָן נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: "כְּבֵית כּוֹר עָפָר" – אֲפִילּוּ הָיוּ שָׁם נְקָעִים עֲמוּקִּים יוֹתֵר מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אוֹ סְלָעִים גְּבוֹהִין יוֹתֵר מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one says to another: I am selling you** a plot of **earth** of the size **required for sowing one *kor* of seed [*beit kor*],** and **there** on that plot **there were crevices** [***neka'im***] **ten handbreadths deep or rocks ten handbreadths high, they are not measured** together **with** the rest of the field. Rather, the buyer must be provided with land measuring a *beit kor* exclusive of those crevices or rocks. If the crevices or rocks measured **less than** ten handbreadths, **they are measured** together **with** the rest of the field. **But if** the seller **said to** the buyer: I am selling you a plot of **earth** that is **about** the size of **a *beit kor*,** then **even if there** on that plot **there were crevices deeper than ten handbreadths or rocks higher than ten handbreadths, they are measured** together **with** the rest of the field.

The *mishnah* establishes that when land is sold, certain features within that land are not counted toward its measured area. Specifically, rocks that rise ten *tfachim* or higher, and crevices that descend ten *tfachim* or deeper, are excluded from the calculation of the field's area. Ten *tfachim* is approximately two and a half to three feet. If such features are present, the seller must compensate by providing additional tillable land to make up the difference.

### Rashbam 4x4

The *Rashbam* adds a further requirement not made explicit in the *mishnah* itself: beyond the height or depth of ten *tfachim*, the feature must also measure at least four *tfachim* by four *tfachim* in its horizontal dimensions. The *Rashbam* grounds this requirement in the laws of *reshut hayachid* for *Shabbat*. A private domain for purposes of *Shabbat* is defined as a space four *tfachim* by four *tfachim* in area and ten *tfachim* in height — approximately a foot by a foot by three feet. A rock meeting those dimensions would constitute its own *reshut hayachid* at its summit; a crevice of those dimensions would form a *reshut hayachid* at its base, naturally walled by its own sides.

### Domain Separation

The conceptual logic follows directly: if such a space is recognized by *halacha* as a separate domain for *Shabbat*, it is coherent to regard it as a separate unit within the field for purposes of a land sale as well. The field and the rock or crevice are, in the relevant sense, different places.

### Rock Ownership Q&A

This raises a question that the *mishnah* leaves unresolved: who owns the rock or the crevice? When we say it is not counted in the land area and the seller must make it up with other land, does that mean the rock was excluded from the sale altogether — in which case it remains the seller's property — or does the buyer receive the rock while simply being entitled to additional compensatory land? Logically, if the rock constitutes a separate unit and that is precisely why additional land must be provided, then the rock was never part of what was sold. It would remain the seller's. The practical stakes are not trivial: if oil were ever to be found beneath such a rock, the question of ownership would matter considerably.

## Hekdesh Redemption

### Tannaim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ שָׂדֵהוּ בִּשְׁעַת הַיּוֹבֵל, נוֹתֵן לוֹ לְבֵית זֶרַע חוֹמֶר שְׂעוֹרִים – חֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁקֶל כָּסֶף. הָיוּ שָׁם נְקָעִים עֲמוּקִּים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אוֹ סְלָעִים גְּבוֹהִין עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים – אֵין נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Arakhin* 25a): With regard to **one who consecrates his** ancestral **field during the time** that the *halakhot* **of the Jubilee** Year are practiced, if he wishes to redeem the field **he must give** the Temple treasurer **fifty silver shekels for** every **place that is fit for sowing a *ḥomer* of barley seed,** which is the biblical equivalent of a *kor*, for all the years of the Jubilee cycle, as this is the sum fixed by the Torah for this purpose (see Leviticus 27:16). But if **there were crevices ten handbreadths deep there,** in the field, **or rocks ten handbreadths high,** **they are not measured** together **with** the rest of the field. If the crevices or rocks were **less than** ten handbreadths, **they are measured** together **with** the rest of the field.

The *Gemara* now adduces a parallel case from an entirely different domain — the laws of *hekdesh* — and uses it to probe the logic of the *mishnah*. The *Gemara* opens with the phrase *tnan hatam*, "we have taught elsewhere," referring to the laws of one who sanctifies a field to the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *Torah* establishes that when a person sanctifies a *sadeh achuza* — a hereditary field of inheritance — and wishes to redeem it, the rate is fixed: fifty *shekel* of silver per *beit kor* of plantable land. This redemption rate is not market-dependent; it is a *Torah*-mandated fixed price, prorated based on how many years remain until the *yovel*.

### Rashbam Girsa

The *Gemara*'s source text reads *bishnat hayovel*, "in the year of the *yovel*." The *Rashbam*, however, emends the reading to *bishat hayovel*, "at the time of the *yovel*" — meaning during the historical period when *yovel* was operative, roughly the era of the First Temple and, debatably, part of the Second Temple period. His reason is straightforward: one cannot meaningfully sanctify a field during the actual *yovel* year itself, since the field reverts to its original owner at that moment and would not remain in the possession of the *hekdesh*. *Tosafot*, by contrast, defends the standard reading *bishnat hayovel* on the grounds that it can refer to the period immediately following the *yovel* year rather than within it.

### Logic of Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? לִיקְדְּשׁוּ בְּאַנְפֵּי נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ! וְכִי תֵּימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הָוֵי בֵּית כּוֹר, לָא קָדְשִׁי; וְהָא תַּנְיָא: "שָׂדֶה" – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "זֶרַע חֹמֶר שְׂעֹרִים בַּחֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁקֶל כָּסֶף"; אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ בָּעִנְיָן הַזֶּה, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת לֶתֶךְ וַחֲצִי לֶתֶךְ, סְאָה, תַּרְקַב וַחֲצִי תַּרְקַב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "שָׂדֶה", מִכׇּל מָקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** are the crevices that are deeper and the rocks that are higher than ten handbreadths not measured with the rest of the field? **Let them** at least **be consecrated separately** and require their own redemption. **And if you would say that since they are not** the size of **a *beit kor*, they cannot be consecrated,** there is a difficulty. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* concerning the verse: "And if he that sanctified the field will redeem it" (Leviticus 27:19): **Why** must **the verse state** the word **"field"?** Since the previous verses are all discussing a field, it would have sufficed for this verse to refer to the field with the pronoun: It. Rather, **as it is stated** in an earlier verse: "Then your valuation shall be according to the seed required for it; **a *ḥomer* of barley seed shall be valued at fifty shekels of silver"** (Leviticus 27:16), **I have** derived **only** the *halakha* in the case of one who **consecrated in this manner,** i.e., an area fit to sow a *ḥomer* of barley seed. The *baraita* continues: **From where** do I learn that this *halakha* is also meant **to include** the case of one who consecrated a smaller area, e.g., an area fit to sow **a *letekh*,** i.e., a half-*kor*, **a half-*letekh*, a *se'a*, a *tarkav*, and a half-*tarkav*?** From where is it derived that these smaller plots of land can also be consecrated and redeemed in accordance with the fixed values stated in the Torah? **The verse states: "A field,"** teaching that this *halakha* applies **in any case** of a field, even to smaller plots.

The *Gemara* now raises its challenge. The *mishnah* in the context of *hekdesh* states — precisely as our *mishnah* does about sales — that rocks ten *tfachim* high and crevices ten *tfachim* deep are not measured together with the field. The *Gemara* asks: *v'amai?* Why not? If one sanctifies one's entire field and it contains a rock or crevice that constitutes a distinct unit, let both be sanctified — the field at its rate and the rock at its own. The mere fact that they are separate units does not mean the rock escapes sanctity; it was included in the declaration of sanctification.

The *Gemara* then entertains the possibility that perhaps, since the rock does not itself constitute a *beit kor*, it does not fall under the *Torah*'s special redemption rate and must therefore go by market value. But the *Rashbam* is careful to clarify: the *Gemara* is not suggesting the rock would fail to become *hekdesh* at all. Rather, the point is that the fixed redemption rate of fifty *shekel* per *beit kor* applies specifically to a *sadeh achuza* — an actual cultivable field. Something that is not a field, such as a courtyard or a purchased field rather than an inherited one, is redeemed at market value. So the question is whether the rock, treated as a separate unit, would follow the *Torah*'s fixed rate or the market rate.

The *Gemara* then quotes a *baraita* that seems to resolve this: the *Torah* says "a field," and derives from the word *sadeh* that the fixed rate applies to fields of all sizes — not only those as large as a full *beit kor*, but even to a *lesech*, half a *lesech*, a *se'ah*, a *tarkav*, half a *tarkav*. Any proportional piece of a *sadeh achuza* follows the same rate. Thus even a small separate rock, if it constitutes a *sadeh achuza*, should follow the fixed rate. The question then reasserts itself: if both the main field and the rock are redeemed at the same rate, what difference does it make whether we treat them as one unit or two?

### Rav Ukva Untillable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: הָכָא בִּנְקָעִים מְלֵאִים מַיִם עָסְקִינַן, דְּלָאו בְּנֵי זְרִיעָה נִינְהוּ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי דּוּמְיָא דִסְלָעִים; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama said: Here,** in the mishna in *Arakhin*, **we are dealing with crevices filled with water.** Being **that they are not fit for sowing,** they cannot be consecrated, as the verse states: "Then your valuation shall be according to the seed required for it" (Leviticus 27:16). The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise** according to this interpretation, **as it teaches** the *halakha* about crevices that are **similar to rocks,** which are certainly not fit for sowing. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from this** that this explanation is correct.

Rav Ukva bar Chama resolves the difficulty: *hacha binkaim mele'im mayim askinan* — we are speaking here specifically of crevices filled with water, which are not tillable land. The *Torah*'s fixed redemption rate applies to a *sadeh achuza*, which means cultivable land. Non-tillable land — such as a rocky crevice filled with water — does not fall under that category and must be redeemed at market value instead.

The force of this answer is considerable. When a person sanctifies his field, the tillable portion — say, twenty-nine *se'ah* out of thirty — is redeemed at the rate of fifty *shekel* per *beit kor*, prorated accordingly. But the crevice, constituting a separate unit of non-tillable land, is redeemed at market rate. The *Gemara* adds that this reading is consistent with the *mishnah*'s language, since the case of rocks — which are paradigmatically non-tillable — is mentioned alongside that of crevices, supporting the interpretation that we are dealing with untillable spaces throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן נָמֵי! הָנְהוּ "נְגָאנֵי דְאַרְעָא" מִיקְּרוּ; "שִׁדְרֵי דְאַרְעָא" מִיקְּרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If** this is **so,** that the crevices and rocks of the mishna are excluded from being consecrated because they are unfit for sowing, then **even** if they **are less than** ten handbreadths in depth or height, they **also** should not be consecrated. The Gemara answers: If the crevices or rocks are less than ten handbreadths deep or high, **these** crevices **are called the cracks of the earth,** and these rocks **are called the spines [*shidrei*] of the earth.** In other words, since they are considered standard features of the field and do not constitute separate areas, they are consecrated together with the rest of the field.

The *Gemara* immediately follows with a question: if the reason crevices are excluded is that they are untillable, then even a small crevice — below the ten-*tfachim* threshold — should also be excluded, since it too is not tillable. The *Gemara* answers: *hani nigni d'ara mikru, shadrei d'ara mikru* — small bumps and minor dips are called simply "the unevenness of the land." They are absorbed into the overall character of the field and do not stand as independent units. A small imperfection is simply part of what it means to have a field. Only once a space is large enough and defined enough to constitute its own distinct unit does it cease to be *batel* to the larger field.

## Tillable vs. Untillable

### Bumps in Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מַאי? אֲמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מְלֵאִים מַיִם. מַאי טַעְמָא? אֵין אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּתֵּן מְעוֹתָיו בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד, וְיֵרָאוּ לוֹ כִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The *halakha* stated above was taught with regard to consecrated property. **Here,** in the case of a sale of land, with regard to which the mishna teaches that crevices ten handbreadths deep are not measured along with the land, **what** is the *halakha*? Does that *halakha* apply only when the crevices are filled with water? **Rav Pappa said: Even if they are not filled with water** they are not considered part of the field. **What is the reason** that in the case of a sale crevices are not considered part of the field even if they are not filled with water? Because **a person** buying a field **does not want to give his money for** the purchase of **one plot when it appears to him like two or three** different **plots** due to differences in the height of the terrain. Therefore, those crevices that are ten handbreadths deep are not measured along with the rest of the field, regardless of whether or not they are filled with water.

Having established the logic for *hekdesh*, the *Gemara* turns to apply the same framework to the case of an ordinary sale. The principle derived from the *hekdesh* discussion is that small bumps (*negligim*) and shallow dips (*shadrei d'area*) are simply part of the land and are subsumed within it. They do not stand as separate units and do not reduce the measured area of the field.

### Buyer Expectations

The question then becomes: in the context of a sale, does the same reasoning apply only when the rock or crevice is untillable, or does it apply even when it is tillable? In the *hekdesh* case, the distinction mattered because the redemption rate differed for untillable land. But when someone buys a field, the concern is different — the buyer wants flat, usable, contiguous land. Even a tillable mound may be objectionable if it disrupts the continuity of the field.

### Permafrost Discussion

This raises a broader question: what if a large portion of the land is not rocky or elevated, but simply of very poor quality — hard, untillable soil, like permafrost — across a wide expanse? Does the buyer have a grievance even if no individual feature reaches the ten-*tfachim* threshold? The answer, which the *Gemara* will address, is that once enough land is genuinely untillable and the area is large enough to stand on its own rather than being *batel* to the rest, the buyer does indeed have a claim. The critical distinction is not framed in terms of quality gradations but in terms of what can be called a *field*: a space defined as its own unit is not a field; a bump integrated into a larger field still is. As the *Rashbam* puts it, the seller's words were *beit kor afar* — a *kor* of tillable land — and if a substantial portion is not tillable, those words have not been fulfilled.

### Rav Papa Flat Land

Rav Papa articulates the principle for sales explicitly: *af al pi she'ein mele'im mayim* — even if the crevice is not filled with water, even if it is in principle tillable, it is still not included in the sold land. The reason he gives is: *einenu rotzeh sheyiten me'otav bemakom echad veyihyu lo kishnayim ulishlosha mekomot* — the buyer does not want to pay for one place and receive what amounts to two or three places. The buyer's expectation in purchasing a *beit kor* is a single, contiguous, flat parcel. A mound that must be climbed or a crevice that must be descended into violates that expectation, regardless of its agricultural usability. A feature defined enough by its height or depth to constitute its own space is therefore excluded from the sale and must be compensated for, whether or not one could theoretically plant there.

## Four Kav Rule

### Rav Yitzchak 4 Kav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: טְרָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ – בֵּית אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּין. אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: וְהוּא שֶׁמּוּבְלָעִין בַּחֲמֵשֶׁת קַבִּין. רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁמּוּבְלָעִין בְּרוּבָּהּ שֶׁל שָׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the crevices or rocks in the field measured **less than** ten handbreadths, **they are measured** together **with** the rest of the field. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The rocks** or crevices that measure less than ten handbreadths that the Sages **said** are measured together with the rest of the field must not measure more than **an area required for sowing four *kav*** of seed within an area required for sowing a *kor*. **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama says: This applies** specifically **where** the rocks **are scattered throughout an area required for sowing five *kav*. Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This applies** specifically **where** the rocks **are scattered throughout the greater part of the field.**

Rav Yitzchak now introduces a quantitative limit on the tolerance for untillable or irregular spaces within a field. The rule articulated earlier — that small bumps and dips are simply part of the land — applies only up to a point. Rav Yitzchak states: *terashim she'amru beit arba'at kabin* — the rocky or untillable areas tolerated without complaint must not exceed four *kav* for each *beit kor*.

### Math of 4 Kav

The mathematics are worth spelling out. There are six *kav* to a *se'ah* and thirty *se'ah* to a *beit kor*, yielding 180 *kav* per *beit kor*. Four *kav* out of 180 is approximately one forty-fifth of the total area, or just over two percent. Up to that threshold, minor untillable patches are considered absorbed into the larger field and are not grounds for complaint. Beyond it, the buyer has a legitimate grievance.

### Concentration Rule

Rav Ukva bar Chama refines this principle: the four *kav* of untillable area is only problematic — and only counts toward this threshold — if it is concentrated within a space of five *kav*. That is, if the untillable patches are scattered throughout the entire field, they may individually be negligible, each absorbed into its surroundings. But if they are all clustered together so that within a defined space of five *kav*, four *kav* of it is untillable, that constitutes a distinct, identifiable problem-area that the buyer cannot be expected to absorb.

### Majority Distribution

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan offers a different formulation: the four *kav* is tolerable if it is spread throughout the majority — more than sixty percent — of the field. The framing here is one of distribution relative to the field as a whole. If the minor imperfections are spread across the majority of the field's expanse, they are part of the texture of the land and the buyer must accept them. But if the concentration of untillable space exceeds four *kav*, or if that four *kav* falls in less than the majority of the field — meaning its density within the area it occupies is too great — then the buyer has a valid claim.

## Unresolved Questions

### Density Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: רוּבָּן בְּמִעוּטָהּ, וּמִעוּטָן בְּרוּבָּהּ, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba raises a dilemma** with regard to the *halakha* taught in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: If **the majority of** the rocks are scattered **throughout the smaller part** of the field, **and the minority of** the rocks are scattered **throughout the greater part** of the field, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved, as no answer was found.

Rabbi Chiya poses a case that the *Gemara* leaves unresolved. According to Rabbi Yochanan's principle, four *kav* distributed across the majority of the field is acceptable. But what if most of the untillable space — say, three *kav* — is compressed into a small portion of the field, while only a little remains scattered across the larger part? The majority of the problematic area is, paradoxically, in the minority of the field's space. Is that still acceptable because the overall quantity remains within the four-*kav* threshold, or is the high local density itself disqualifying? The *Gemara* answers: *teiku*.

### Shape Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּשִׁיר מַהוּ? כְּשׁוּרָה מַהוּ? אִיצְטְדִינִין מַהוּ? דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises** another **dilemma: If the rocks are scattered throughout the greater part of the field but are arranged like a ring, what is** the *halakha*? If the rocks stand **in a row, what is** the *halakha*? If the rocks form **an angle, what is** the *halakha*? If they are arranged in the shape of **a crooked path, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara states: All these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Yirmiah, characteristically drawn to edge cases that probe the conceptual boundaries of a principle, poses a series of further unresolved questions concerning the geometric configuration of untillable patches. If they are arranged in a circle (*ke-shir*), the enclosed form makes navigation around them especially difficult — is that configuration more objectionable even within the acceptable threshold? If arranged in a straight line (*ke-shura*), tilling becomes harder in one direction — the *Rashbam* notes that fields were plowed in both directions, making a linear obstacle disruptive in a way that scattered patches are not. If shaped like the horns of an ox (*itztadin*), curved and branching outward, or arranged in a zigzag (*derech akalton*), are any of these configurations inherently more problematic than an even distribution? On all of these questions, the *Gemara* answers *teiku*.

## Boundary Edge Cases

### Edge & Solo Rocks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אִם הָיָה סֶלַע יְחִידִי, אֲפִילּוּ כָּל שֶׁהוּא – אֵין נִמְדָּד עִמָּהּ. וְאִם הָיָה סָמוּךְ לַמֶּצֶר, אֲפִילּוּ כָּל שֶׁהוּא – אֵין נִמְדָּד עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: **If there is a solitary rock** on the outskirts of the field, **even** if it is of **a minimal** size, **it is not measured** together **with** the rest of the field. **And** further, **if** that rock **is adjacent to the** field's **border, even** if it is of **a minimal** size, **it is not measured** together **with** the rest of the field.

A *tanna* teaches: a *sela yechidi* — a solitary rock — is not measured with the field, even if it is quite small, *afilu kol shehu*. Similarly, a rock *samuch lemeitzar* — adjacent to the boundary of the field — is not measured with the field, again regardless of size.

### Rashbam Edge

The *Rashbam* explains: a rock at the edge of the field, even one too small to ordinarily constitute a separate unit, is not *batel* to the field as a whole. When a feature sits at the periphery, it does not become incorporated into the field in the way that a small bump in the interior would. It stands apart rather than being absorbed into the surrounding land. The *Rashbam* also addresses the case of the *sela yechidi* — a rock outside the formal boundary of the field but adjacent to it — as essentially the same principle: it is not organically part of the field and therefore not counted with it.

### Tosafot Named Rock

*Tosafot* reads the term *yechidi* differently. Rather than referring to physical isolation at the edge, *Tosafot* understands it to mean a rock with its own name — a recognized landmark in the community, known to everyone as a distinct place. Such a rock has a separate identity independent of the field it sits within, and that independent identity is what causes it to be excluded from the field's measurement. The two interpretations — edge-placement for the *Rashbam*, named identity for *Tosafot* — represent meaningfully different understandings of what constitutes separateness.

### Dirt Gap Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: מוּפְסָק עָפָר בֵּינְתַיִם, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: עָפָר מִלְּמַטָּה וְצוּנְמָא לְמַעְלָה; עָפָר מִלְּמַעְלָה וְצוּנְמָא מִלְּמַטָּה – מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** If **earth intervenes between** the rock and the border of the field, so that the rock does not actually touch the border, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara states: This dilemma as well **shall stand** unresolved. **Rav Ashi raises** another **dilemma:** If there was a layer of **earth below and rock above, or earth above and rock below, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara states: This dilemma as well **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Pappa poses the following question: *mushech afar beintayim mahu?* What if there is a small amount of dirt between the rock and the boundary line? The rock is not right up against the edge; there is a thin strip of earth separating it from the *meitzar*. Does that small gap suffice to integrate the rock into the field, so that it is treated like any interior feature subject to the ordinary thresholds? Or is its proximity to the boundary still sufficient to exclude it? The *Gemara* answers: *teiku*.

### Subterranean Shape

Rav Ashi poses a further question about cases where the rock's relationship to the boundary varies depending on depth. Suppose that at ground level the rock appears to sit exactly at the boundary — a case where we would ordinarily say it is not measured with the field — but underground the rock narrows away from the boundary, leaving dirt between the base of the rock and the border. Or conversely: at the surface there is a visible gap of dirt between the rock and the boundary, but below ground the rock widens until it is flush with or extends past the border. In which direction do we assess the rock's position? The *Gemara* entertains both scenarios — *afar milmata vetsunma milmala*, dirt below and rock above, and *afar milmala vetsunma milmata*, rock below and dirt above — and concludes on both: *teiku*.

## Measured Sale

### Precision Promise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ "בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ – מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל", פִּיחֵת כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – יְנַכֶּה, הוֹתִיר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – יַחֲזִיר. וְאִם אָמַר: "הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר", אֲפִילּוּ פִּיחֵת רוֹבַע לִסְאָה, אוֹ הוֹתִיר רוֹבַע לִסְאָה – הִגִּיעוֹ. יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן, יַעֲשֶׂה חֶשְׁבּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says to another: **I am selling you** a plot of **earth** the size of **a *beit kor*, measured** precisely **with a rope,** and **he gave** him even **the slightest amount less** than what was stipulated, the seller **must deduct** the difference from the purchase price of the field and return money to the buyer. If **he gave** him even **the slightest amount more** than what was stipulated, the buyer **must return** the difference to the seller. **And if** the seller **said** to the buyer that he is selling him a *beit kor* of land **more or less,** then **even if he gave** him **a quarter***-kav* **per *se'a* less** than what was stipulated, **or he gave him a quarter***-kav* **per *se'a* more** that what was stipulated, i.e., he gave him one twenty-fourth more or less than what was required, **it is his.** The sale is valid, since the seller told the buyer in advance that he was not committing himself to precise measurements. If the difference is **greater than that** amount, **he must make a calculation,** and the party that suffered a loss must be compensated.

The *mishnah* now introduces a distinct category of sale. Rather than a general transaction in which the seller describes the land approximately, the seller here uses the formula *beit kor ani mocher lecha mida bechevel* — "I am selling you a *beit kor*, measured by the rope." The phrase *mida bechevel* signals a commitment to precision: this is not an approximate description but a warranted measurement. The buyer is purchasing a specific, exactly-measured quantity of land.

### Deficit & Overage

The consequences of that precision commitment are rigorous. *Pichat kol shehu yinakeh* — if the actual measurement falls short by any amount whatsoever, even the smallest deficit, the seller must reduce the price proportionally. Similarly, *hotir kol shehu yachzir* — if it turns out the field is even slightly larger than stated, the buyer must return the value of the overage, either in money or, as we will see, in land.

### Approximate Sale

By contrast, when the seller uses the formula *hen chaser hen yeter* — "whether it is less or more" — he signals that the sale is approximate. He is explicitly acknowledging that the stated size is not guaranteed. In that case, the *mishnah* rules that discrepancies of up to a *rova* — one quarter of a *kav* — per *se'ah* are within the tolerable range. Neither party can make a claim against the other on account of such a variance.

### 7.5 Kav Tolerance

The arithmetic of this tolerance for a full *beit kor* is as follows. One quarter of a *kav* per *se'ah*, multiplied by thirty *se'ah* per *beit kor*, yields seven and a half *kav* of permissible variance for the entire parcel. Within that margin, the sale stands as stated. Beyond it, an adjustment must be made.

## Overpayment Remedy

### Return Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה הוּ מַחֲזִיר לוֹ? מָעוֹת. וְאִם רָצָה, מַחֲזִיר לוֹ קַרְקַע. וְלָמָה אָמְרוּ מַחֲזִיר לוֹ מָעוֹת? לְיַיפּוֹת כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל מוֹכֵר – שֶׁאִם שִׁיֵּיר בַּשָּׂדֶה בֵּית תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין, וּבַגִּינָּה בֵּית חֲצִי קַב; וּכְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – בֵּית רוֹבַע; מַחֲזִיר לוֹ אֶת הַקַּרְקַע. וְלֹא אֶת הָרוֹבַע בִּלְבַד הוּא מַחֲזִיר, אֶלָּא אֶת כָּל הַמּוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** If the buyer received too much land, so that he must now compensate the seller, **what does he return to him?** He returns **money,** i.e., he pays the seller for the surplus land. **And if** the seller so **wishes,** the buyer **returns** the surplus **land to him. Why** then **did** the Sages **say** that **he returns money to him?** They said this **in order to enhance the power of the seller,** and enable him to demand payment for the surplus land, rather than accept its return. **As, if the surplus in the field was an area required for sowing nine *kav*** of seed, **and in a garden an area required for sowing a half-*kav*** of seed, **or, according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva, an area required for sowing a quarter**-*kav* of seed (see 11a), the buyer must **return the land** itself **to** the seller, and the seller cannot demand payment in money. **And** if the surplus is greater than a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*, **it is not only the quarter**-*kav* **that he returns; rather,** he returns **all of the surplus.** Since he is already required to make a refund, the refund must be made in the precise amount.

The *mishnah* now asks: when the field proves to be larger than warranted and the buyer must return the overage, what form does that return take? Does the buyer return money (*ma'ot*) or land (*karka*)?

### Money Default

The *mishnah* answers: the default is that the buyer returns money. The *Rashbam* explains the rationale: if the overage is small, the seller cannot realistically make use of a tiny sliver of land returned to him. A seller whose field has been slightly over-measured would be left with an unusable strip of land if the buyer returned it physically. In order to protect the seller's practical interests — *liyafot kocho shel mocher* — the *halacha* defaults to monetary restitution.

### 9 Kav Threshold

However, if the seller *wishes* to reclaim the land rather than money, and the overage is large enough, the *mishnah* allows for that. The threshold is nine *kav*: if the overage amounts to *beit tishah kavin* — the area of nine *kav* — the seller can insist on receiving the land back rather than money, because nine *kav* constitutes a functionally usable plot of land.

### Usable Minimums

The *mishnah* sets out the minimum sizes that define usable land for these purposes. For a field (*sadeh*), the minimum is nine *kav*. For a vegetable garden (*gina*), it is half a *kav*. Rabbi Akiva holds that even a quarter of a *kav* suffices as a usable minimum. These thresholds determine at what point a returned parcel of land is considered a genuine, functional piece of property rather than a useless fragment.

### Total Return

A further principle concludes the *mishnah*: when the seller is entitled to a return because the overage exceeds the tolerable threshold, the buyer does not return only the portion above the threshold. He returns the entire overage. If the field was off by, say, two and a half percent when one and three-quarters percent would have been acceptable, the buyer does not return only the excess three-quarters of a percent — he returns the full two and a half percent.

To situate this within the *beit kor* framework: the tolerance in an approximate sale is seven and a half *kav*. If the overage is within that amount, no return is required. If it exceeds seven and a half *kav* but has not yet reached nine *kav*, the seller can demand money but cannot force the return of land. Once the overage reaches nine *kav*, the seller may demand the land itself — and when he does, he receives back not just the portion above seven and a half *kav* but the entire overage from the first *kav* onward.
