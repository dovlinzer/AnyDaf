# Yevamot 120 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Sisters-in-Law

### Mishna Review

The daf opens with a brief review of the mishna on 119b. The case involves two women each married to one of two brothers. Each woman comes and reports that her own husband has died, and each is believed — a woman's testimony about her husband's death is accepted in order to permit her to remarry. The complication, however, is that each woman also has a *yavam*: her deceased husband's brother. And the testimony of one co-wife about her sister-in-law's husband carries no legal weight for purposes of releasing the sister-in-law from the obligation of *yibum*.

To map out the case concretely: Reuven and Shimon are brothers. Rachel is married to Reuven and Sarah is married to Shimon. Rachel testifies that Reuven is dead — she is believed, and in principle could remarry, but she cannot, because Shimon is still alive and she requires either *yibum* or *chalitza* from him. Sarah testifies that Shimon is dead — again, believed for her own purposes, but she cannot act on it because Reuven may still be alive, and his status has only been established by Rachel's testimony, which does not function as admissible testimony vis-à-vis releasing Sarah from her *yibum* obligation. The mishna concludes that both women are stuck.

### Reb Eliezer Position

Rabbi Eliezer introduces a leniency. If these women went ahead and performed *yibum* with the surviving brothers — that is, if Rachel performed *yibum* with Levi (Reuven's brother) and Sarah performed *yibum* with Yehuda (Shimon's brother) — and those *yevamim* subsequently died, then Rabbi Eliezer holds that both women are free to remarry anyone they wish. They need not worry that one of the original husbands may still be alive.

### Acting on Testimony

The final line of the mishna articulates Rabbi Eliezer's rationale: *ho'il vehutru leyevamim hutru lechol adam* — since they have been permitted to the *yevamim*, they are permitted to all men. The key move is that by allowing Rachel to perform *yibum* with Levi, the legal system has not merely accepted Rachel's testimony in the abstract — it has acted upon it. The death of Reuven has been established as a legal fact. Correspondingly, Sarah's *yibum* with Yehuda establishes the death of Shimon as a legal fact. Once both deaths have been legally acted upon in this way, each woman can rely on the other's established fact, and both are free to remarry after the *yevamim* themselves die.

## Rava's Question

### Rava Inquiry

The Gemara opens with Rava's inquiry: *mai ta'ama deRabbi Eliezer?* — what is the underlying rationale of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling? Two possible explanations are offered. The first: *mishum dekasavar tzara me'ida lachavirtah* — perhaps Rabbi Eliezer holds that in general, a co-wife may testify on behalf of her competing co-wife. This would be a sweeping position, and both Rashi and Tosafot reject it as inconsistent with the language of the mishna. The mishna uses the formulation *hutru leyevamim*, not the language of testimony being accepted. Moreover, the entire prior discussion has established that co-wives, mothers-in-law, and daughters-in-law are precisely the category of women whose testimony is *not* accepted.

The more plausible explanation, suggested by Tosafot explicitly and Rashi implicitly, is that Rachel is fundamentally testifying about herself — about her own husband's death, for her own purposes of remarriage. The testimony is, at its core, testimony *for herself*. Rabbi Eliezer may be saying that once testimony is accepted as fundamentally self-directed, it carries weight even for the competing co-wife, even though that co-wife would normally fall outside the scope of acceptable testimony.

### Two Interpretations

This leads to a second layer of Rava's question. Even granting that Rabbi Eliezer extends the effect of self-directed testimony to the co-wife, *when* does that extension take effect? Two possibilities emerge: either the testimony alone suffices — the very act of testifying for oneself automatically confers permissibility on the co-wife (*tzara me'ida lachavirtah*) — or the extension only operates once the testifying woman has actually *acted* on her own testimony. Under the second reading, the operative factor is not the testimony per se but the willingness to personally stake one's own status on the truth of the claim: *mishum dehi lo mekalkela nafsha* — because she would not ruin herself.

### Co-Wife Case

The practical difference between these two interpretations is precisely the question of *le'insuvei letzara mikamei didah* — whether the co-wife may remarry before the testifying woman has herself acted. In a simpler case involving two co-wives whose joint husband went overseas: Sarah says the husband died. That testimony is accepted for Sarah's own purposes of remarriage. May Rachel immediately rely on that testimony to remarry as well, even before Sarah has herself taken any action?

If Rabbi Eliezer's principle is that self-directed testimony automatically extends to the co-wife, then Rachel can act immediately — *af agav dela insava mansavinan lah letzara*, even before Sarah herself has married, we permit the co-wife to marry. But if the operative principle requires action — that Sarah must demonstrate her own genuine conviction by actually remarrying — then *ei lo insava lo mansavinan lah*: until Sarah acts, Rachel cannot rely on it.

## Textual Analysis

### Language Analysis

The Gemara attempts to resolve Rava's question from the language of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in the mishna. The phrase is *ho'il vehutru leyevamim hutru lechol adam* — "since they have been *permitted* to the *yevamim*, they are permitted to all men." The operative word is *hutru*, "permitted," not "married." The mishna does not say "since they *married* the *yevamim*." This linguistic observation becomes the pivot of the Gemara's analysis.

### Automatic vs. Conditional

The Gemara argues: if Rabbi Eliezer's rationale requires action — that is, he only extends the testimony's effect once the woman has *married* her *yavam* — then the language *vehutru leyevamim* makes perfect sense, because *hainu dechi insav manzveina lah* — only because they married the *yevamim* does the permission flow to the co-wife. But if Rabbi Eliezer's principle is that the testimony alone automatically works for the co-wife even without any subsequent action, the mishna's wording becomes puzzling: it should have simply said *ho'il vehutru hutru*, without specifically tying it to the *yibum* having occurred. The fact that it anchors the permission in the *yibum* itself seems to indicate that action is required.

The Gemara, however, deflects this reading. *Rabbi Eliezer lidvreihem ka'amar* — Rabbi Eliezer is arguing within the framework of the Rabbis' position, not necessarily expressing his own full view. According to his own reasoning, Rabbi Eliezer may well hold that the co-wife is automatically permitted once testimony is given for oneself, even without any subsequent action. But addressing the Rabbis on their own terms, he argues: at least concede that once she has performed *yibum* and thereby staked her own status on the truth of her testimony — *lo mekalkela nafsha* — surely you must agree the co-wife should be permitted. To this the Rabbis respond with the principle of *tamus nafshi im pelishtim* — she is willing to bring the stadium down on her own head if she can take down her co-wife in the process.

### Baraita Case

A *baraita* is then introduced: a woman and her husband went overseas. She returned and declared that her husband had died. She may remarry and collect her *ketuva*. Her co-wife, however, is forbidden. This is the classic formulation — believed for herself, not for the co-wife. Rabbi Eliezer says: *ho'il vehutra hi hutra nami tzarata* — since she has been permitted, her co-wife is also permitted. Crucially, the baraita uses the language of *hutra* — "she has been permitted" — rather than saying she actually married. This seems to vindicate the reading that Rabbi Eliezer does not require prior action; the mere permissibility itself extends to the co-wife.

The Gemara attempts to read the baraita as implying *ho'il vehutra veniseit* — "permitted and then married" — so that action is still required. But the plain reading clearly supports the automatic interpretation.

## Hidden Divorce Problem

### Secret Get Concern

If Rabbi Eliezer's principle requires the woman to act on her own testimony before her co-wife can benefit, a serious objection arises: even when she acts, her action may not reliably demonstrate the truth of her testimony. The Gemara raises the concern of *veleichush shema begita asai* — what if she is coming with a *get*? That is, what if her husband had secretly sent her a *get* that no one else knows about? In that case, she is already divorced and not bound by her own claim that he died. She goes ahead and remarries, ostensibly "acting on" her testimony — but in reality she knows she is safe because of the hidden *get*. Her entire purpose is to ruin her co-wife by inducing the co-wife to remarry while the husband is, in fact, still alive. In this scenario, her act of remarriage proves nothing.

### Kohen Exception

The Gemara resolves this difficulty by limiting the applicability of Rabbi Eliezer's leniency to a specific circumstance: *de'insva l'Kohen* — she must have married a *kohen*. If there is a hidden *get*, then when the husband eventually returns alive, the truth will emerge, and at that point she will also be ruined — she will be divorced from the *kohen* because a *kohenet* who was previously divorced or who came through improper circumstances is *assura* to remain married to a *kohen*. Since she faces this additional exposure, she would not have concocted the *get* scheme in the first place. Marrying a ישראל, by contrast, does not eliminate the hidden *get* scenario: her risk there is limited, and the concern remains.

### Legal vs. Factual Truth

This exchange opens a broader question about the nature of halachic truth. When a *beit din* rules that a woman may remarry, is it establishing a legal fact or merely permitting action under uncertainty? One might think the ruling functions like a judicial determination of fact, binding on subsequent proceedings. Indeed, that might be Rabbi Eliezer's point — particularly under the reading that does not require prior action: once testimony is accepted for one party, it is accepted as established fact, and others may rely on it.

But the Gemara's willingness to entertain the hidden *get* scenario reveals that *beit din*'s acceptance of testimony does not constitute a universal determination of fact. It is a permission granted within a particular legal context, for particular parties, and it does not automatically extend as a factual determination to all related questions. This parallels the concept of *palginan dibura* found in tractate Sanhedrin — the same statement can be believed in one direction and disbelieved in another. Halacha regularly operates within a framework of legal truth that does not always correspond to objective, verifiable fact, in the same way that legal systems employ constructs like *rov* and *chazaka* not because they guarantee factual accuracy but because they provide workable presumptions for adjudication.

## Identifying the Dead

### Face with Nose

The daf now shifts to a new mishna addressing the *content* of valid testimony — specifically, how one establishes that the body one has seen is indeed the person in question. *Ein me'idin ela al partzuf panim im hachotem* — one may only testify based on having seen the face together with the nose. *Af al pi she'yesh simanim b'gufo uvkhelav* — even if there are identifying marks on the body or on the clothing, that is not sufficient. The standard requires direct facial identification.

### Three-Day Limit

An additional limitation: *ein me'idin ela ad gimmel yamim* — one may only testify within three days of the death. Beyond three days, the decomposition of the body alters the appearance to such a degree that reliable identification is no longer possible.

### Context-Dependent

Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava dissents from the three-day rule. He argues that no uniform rule can be applied: *lo kol ha'adam velo kol hamakom velo kol hasha'ot shavin* — not every person is the same, not every place is the same, and not every time is the same. Body decomposition varies by individual constitution, climate, and circumstance, and a rigid three-day cutoff fails to account for this variability.

## Facial Recognition

### Forehead & Nose

A *baraita* elaborates on the mishna's requirement: *padachat velo partzuf panim, partzuf panim velo padachat — ein me'idin* — neither a forehead alone without a face, nor a face without a forehead, is sufficient for testimony. One requires both together, along with the nose: *ad she'yihyu shneihem im hachotem*. A full, head-on view of the face is necessary.

### Scriptural Source

Abaye — and some attribute it to Rav Kahana — derives this from the verse *hakarat pneihem antah bam* — "the recognition of their face answered against them." Rashi reads the verse as establishing the face, rather than any other part of the body, as the locus of reliable identification. Tosafot notes that the word *hakarat* — "recognition" — implies not merely that a face was seen, but that a full identification of the face was achieved.

### Wax Disguise

The Gemara illustrates the sensitivity of facial recognition with a story about Abba bar Marta, also known as Abba bar Minyumi, who owed money to the house of the *Reish Galuta*. He took wax (*kira*), smeared it onto his forehead, and walked past their guards — *velo baskruha* — and they did not recognize him. The episode demonstrates that even minor alterations to the forehead and nose can defeat reliable identification, reinforcing why the requirement for a complete, unobstructed view of the face is necessary.

---

## Tosafot Reinterpretation

### Whole Body Recognition

Rabbeinu Tam, at the top of Tosafot on this daf, offers a significant reinterpretation of the mishna's requirements. He argues: *hainu davka keshein sham ela hapartzuf* — the requirement of face-with-nose applies only when all one has seen is the face. But *aval kol gufo shalem* — if the entire body is intact and has been seen — *afilu ein kan padachat vechotem nikar hu heitev al yedei simanei haguf* — even without the forehead and nose, the body is clearly identifiable through its physical characteristics.

### Recognition vs. Marks

Rabbeinu Tam draws a further distinction. He suggests that when the mishna says *simanim* on the body are insufficient, that ruling applies only to a case where the witness is relying on specific *marks* — pointing to particular features and saying, "I see a mole here, therefore this is him." That is fundamentally different from *tevi'as ayin* — genuine recognition, where the totality of what one sees produces confident identification. If the witness truly *recognizes* the person — not by pointing to individual marks but through the holistic experience of recognition — that may be perfectly valid even without the specific facial requirements.

### Extended Timeframes

Rabbeinu Tam extends this reasoning further: *va'afilu achar kama yamim* — even after many days, if the body is complete and the witness genuinely recognizes it, testimony may be valid. The three-day limit and the facial requirements, on this reading, apply specifically to the case of *simanim* — where one is relying on particular identifying marks rather than direct recognition. The creative thrust of this position is unmistakable: Rabbeinu Tam is deliberately reframing stringent Talmudic requirements as applying only to a narrower category of cases, opening the door to much more flexible standards of identification. Given the practical reality that these standards could leave many women unable to remarry, it is clear what pastoral and halakhic need is driving the reinterpretation.

## Marks: Biblical or R (II)

### Simanim Status

The Gemara returns to the mishna's statement that *simanim* on the body are insufficient for testimony. *Lemeimra desimanim lav de'oraita* — does this establish that identifying marks have no biblical validity? If so, that would be a significant and far-reaching ruling.

### Get Case

The Gemara challenges this inference from a ruling in *Gittin*. If a *get* was found tied to a pouch, wallet, or signet ring (*matza kashur bekis uve'arnaki uvetaba'as*), or found among the owner's possessions, it may be returned to its owner and used to divorce his wife — even after a considerable time has passed. The *get* is valid even for the weighty matter of *eishes ish*. The identification of the *get* as belonging to a particular person rests entirely on the *simanim* — the associated objects. This seems to demonstrate that *simanim* do have biblical validity, since the stakes here are clearly *de'oraita* in nature.

## Rava's Resolution

### Everyone Accepts Simanim

Rava proposes a resolution: *dechuli alma simanim d'oraita* — everyone agrees that identifying marks have biblical validity. The debate elsewhere — specifically the *baraita* involving testimony based on a *shuma* (mole) — is not about whether *simanim* work in principle, but about a more specific question.

## Marks: Biblical or R (II)

### Mole Case

The *baraita* records a debate: *ein me'idin al hashuma* — one may not testify to a man's death based on a mole. Rabbi Elazar ben Mahavai disagrees: *me'idin* — one may. Abaye initially suggests this debate reflects a disagreement about whether *simanim* are *de'oraita* or *derabbanan*. But Rava's resolution — that everyone agrees *simanim* are *de'oraita* — redirects the question: if so, what exactly are Rabbi Elazar ben Mahavai and the Rabbis debating?

---

## Rava's Resolution (II)

### Qualifying Marks

Rava explains that the debate about the mole concerns *shuma metzuya beven gilo* — whether a mole of that type is commonly found among people born under the same astrological influence (*ben gilo*), i.e., whether it constitutes a sufficiently distinctive identifying mark. One opinion holds that such moles are common enough to other individuals that they do not meet the threshold of a valid *siman*; the other holds they are uncommon enough to qualify. The underlying principle is that even if everyone agrees *simanim* are *de'oraita*, not every proposed *siman* actually meets the threshold. There is a spectrum: at the top is the *siman muvhak* — the distinctively unique mark, analogous to a fingerprint — about which there is no dispute. At the bottom is something so generic (tall, short) that no one calls it a *siman*. The debate about the mole lies somewhere in the middle.

### Change or Common

Tosafot introduces two additional considerations that bear on the validity of a *siman*: first, whether it may *change after death* — certain bodily marks shift in appearance following death, and if so, they cannot serve as reliable *simanim*; second, whether the mark is simply too common to be identifying. The Gemara presents a second version of this debate — *shuma ha'asuya lehishtanos le'achar misa* — where the dispute is specifically about whether moles of this kind change after death. One opinion holds they do; the other that they do not. This version does not require invoking the *ben gilo* framework at all.

---

## Generic vs. Specific

### Generic Characteristics

With the principle that *simanim de'oraita* established — at least according to one significant position — a new question arises: why does the mishna exclude *simanim* on the body from valid identification? The Gemara answers that the mishna's case is not one involving genuine *simanim* at all. *Gufo — d'arich vegutz*: the "identifying marks" referenced are nothing more than height — tall or short. Such characteristics are far too generic to count as a *siman*. They do not rise to the level of an identifying mark in any meaningful sense.

Regarding clothing — *keilav* — the Gemara raises the concern of *she'ila*: people lend their clothes to others, so clothing cannot serve as a reliable identifier. This concern, however, generates a counter-challenge: if we are worried about objects being lent, how do we return lost items (*hashavas aveida*) based on *simanim* such as the saddle (*uchaf*) of a donkey, as is done in tractate Bava Metzia? The Gemara replies: *lo sheili inshi uchfa demaska lei lechara* — people do not lend out saddles, because a saddle not fitted to a particular donkey will irritate its back. The same concern that produces the rule also supplies its exception.

### Ketubot Standard

A parallel challenge is raised regarding the *get* case: how can a *get* be returned based on its attachment to a pouch or signet ring if people lend such items? The Gemara answers: *taba'as chashinan leziyufa* — one does not lend out a signet ring for fear of forgery; *menachshi inshi velo moshli* — people treat their personal pouches and wallets as highly personal objects, associated with personal identity to such a degree that they will not lend them. The Gemara thus accounts for each category individually rather than resolving the lending concern universally.

An alternative resolution — *iba'is eima keilav d'chivri vesumki* — proposes that we are not concerned about lending of this kind in general, and that the mishna's exclusion of clothing applies only because the description was wholly generic: "he was wearing a white jacket" is too common to identify anyone.

### Final Ruling

The Gemara's conclusion, at least according to one strand of analysis, is that *simanim de'oraita*: identifying marks have biblical validity. The mishna's rejection of body characteristics is because those characteristics fell below the threshold of *simanim* entirely — they were generic descriptions, not identifying marks. A genuine *siman*, and certainly a *siman muvhak*, would in principle suffice even without seeing the face. This ruling substantially expands the halakhic basis for Rabbeinu Tam's more flexible approach, which now appears less as a creative departure and more as a logical extension of the Gemara's own framework.

## Wounded vs. Dead

### Wounds Alone

The mishna now addresses a second dimension of the testimony requirement: it is not enough to identify who the person was — one must also establish that he is actually dead. *Ein me'idin ela ad she'teitzei nafsho* — one may only testify once the soul has actually departed. *V'afilu ra'uhu meguyad* — even if witnesses saw him with severed arteries (*meguyad*, which Rashi explains as referring to severed *gidim*, and Jastrow renders as cut arteries, apparently a mode of execution in some places), *o tzaluv* — crucified, *o chayah ochelet bo* — or being consumed by a wild animal: none of these observations suffices. The man was still alive, however improbably he might have survived, and testimony of his death requires witnessing actual death.

### Severed Artery Debate

This ruling seems to imply that a *meguyad* — someone with severed arteries — can in fact survive. The Gemara challenges this from a mishna elsewhere stating that a person does not cause corpse-*tumah* until he has literally expired: *afilu meguyad ve'afilu goses*, even someone with cut arteries or someone in his final death throes. The implication, the Gemara observes, is that while such a person has not yet died, it is clear he will die — *hamachya lo chaya*. If so, how can our mishna say we cannot testify to his death?

Abaye resolves the contradiction: *ha Rabi Shimon ben Elazar veha rabbanan* — the two sources reflect a *tannaitic* dispute. A *baraita* records that one *may* testify about a *meguyad* but may *not* testify about someone crucified — but Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says *af al hameguyad ein me'idin*, even about a *meguyad* one may not testify, *mipnei sheyakhol lachvot velichyot* — because the wound might be stanched and the person might survive. Our mishna follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar; the *baraita*'s first position reflects the view of the Rabbis who hold that a *meguyad* will definitely die.

### Asia Case

The Gemara raises a further difficulty: if our mishna follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, it seems to contradict itself. The later portion of the mishna (to be discussed in full on the following daf) records a case from Asia in which a man was lowered into the sea and when the rope was pulled back up only his leg remained — *velo alta beyadam ela raglo*. The *chachamim* ruled: *min harkuva ulemala tinasei* — if the leg was severed from above the knee, his wife may remarry; *min harkuva ulemata lo tinasei* — below the knee, she may not. Severing a leg above the knee necessarily involves severing major arteries, making this essentially a case of *meguyad*. Yet the *chachamim* permit remarriage in that case. How can the mishna simultaneously follow Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and permit testimony in the Asia case?

## Fatal Wounds

### Water Irritation

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *shani mayim demirzaz maka* — water is different, because water irritates the wound. When a man is submerged in the ocean with a severed leg, the seawater irritates and aggravates the wound in a way that makes survival impossible. This is unlike a standard case of *meguyad* on dry land, where cauterization or stanching might allow survival. The Asia case is therefore a special category where death is certain even according to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.

### Camel Example

The Gemara then addresses a related challenge from a story told by Rabba bar bar Chana: *ledidi chazi li hahu taya* — "I myself saw a certain Arab nomad who took his sword and severed part of a camel, *velo afsiktiah line'urasei*" — and it did not cease its moaning." Rashi explains this as meaning the camel moaned continuously until it died. The story seems to prove that a *meguyad* dies — which supports the Rabbis against Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who says survival is possible. But of course the fact that a camel died in one instance does not establish that all *meguyad* cases are fatal; it merely shows death is possible, not inevitable.

The alternative and more workable reading of the story is that the camel survived despite the severing — the moaning continued because it was still alive — which would prove that a *meguyad* can indeed live, supporting Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's position. The Gemara then confronts the challenge: if the camel *lived*, how does this coexist with the Rabbis' view that a *meguyad* certainly dies?

### Special Circumstances

Two answers are offered. Abaye says: *haki kachisha havya* — it was a thin, sickly camel, and some particular feature of its weakened constitution explains the outcome. Rava offers a more satisfying resolution: *besakin meluvenet divrei hakol* — the sword was white-hot, and everyone would agree that in such a case the cauterizing effect of the heated blade would allow survival. The case of the Arab nomad was a special circumstance — a cauterizing wound — and proves nothing about ordinary *meguyad* cases. Both answers accept that the camel survived, and explain why the case is exceptional rather than representative.

## Gittin Get Case

### Two Simanim

Rav Yehuda cites Shmuel: *shachat bo shnayim o rov shnayim uvarach me'idin* — if the trachea and esophagus (the two *simanim* of *shechita*) or the majority of them were severed and the man fled, one may testify to his death. This ruling is consistent with the earlier discussion: just as an animal being consumed at the neck would permit testimony (since the vital site is directly involved), so too the severing of the two *simanim* at the throat establishes that death is certain.

Rav Yehuda further cites Shmuel in the *Gittin* context: *shachat bo shnayim o rov shnayim veramaz ve'amar kitvu get le'ishti harei elu yichtevu veyitnu* — if a man with his trachea and esophagus severed signals that a *get* should be written for his wife, it may be written and delivered. This is a striking ruling. Why would a man want his wife to receive a *get* at the moment of death? The answer — consistent with the *masechta* being studied — is to release her from *yibum*: she will receive the *get*, become a divorcée rather than a widow, and not require *yibum* from the surviving brothers.

### Alive Until Dead

The Gemara notes an apparent tension: if the man with severed *simanim* will certainly die, how can a *get* be valid? A *get* requires the husband to be alive at the time of delivery. The Gemara resolves it simply: *chai hu vesofo lamut* — he is alive right now; his death is coming, but he has not yet died. The *get* is valid because he is halachically alive at the moment of delivery. This point carries significant weight for broader halakhic discussions of the definition of death. Even a person whose trachea and esophagus are both severed is halachically alive — capable of giving a *get*, presumably capable of other halachic acts — until actual death supervenes. This understanding has direct implications for contemporary discussions of brain death, a topic the Gemara here deliberately sidesteps by focusing on the biological threshold as understood in its own framework.

### No Galut

The Gemara raises a difficulty: if severing the two *simanim* will certainly result in death, why does a *baraita* rule *harei ze eino goleh* — that someone who accidentally severs the trachea and esophagus of another person does not go into exile (*galut*)? Accidental killing normally requires exile. If death is certain from this wound, exile should follow.

## Multiple Causation

### Wind or Self-Action

Rav Hoshaya resolves the difficulty: *chayshinan shema haruach bilbelato* — we are concerned that perhaps the wind contributed to the person's death after the initial wounding. Or alternatively: *shema hu kirev mitato* — perhaps the victim himself hastened his own death, by convulsing or agitating the wound. The principle is one of multiple causation: when factors beyond the initial act may have contributed to death, the standard for *galut* is not met, because *galut* requires that the death be entirely and unambiguously attributable to the perpetrator's act.

### Marble vs. Outdoors

The Gemara distinguishes between the two possibilities raised by Rav Hoshaya. *Deshachte beveisa deshisha uperches* — if the severing occurred in a marble house (completely enclosed, no wind) but the victim convulsed (*parches*), wind is not a factor but self-action may be. *Deshachte bevara velo parches* — if it occurred outdoors (wind possible) but the victim did not convulse, then wind is a factor but self-action is not. The Gemara explores how these scenarios map onto the two distinct explanations offered by Rav Hoshaya.

### Intentional Acts Standard

Tosafot observes that the principle of multiple causation and the *galut* exemption are specific to the laws of *galut*, which have uniquely demanding standards for establishing causation. The analogy to the contemporary case of a person who was wounded decades ago and recently died from those wounds is instructive: in such a case, even if other factors over the intervening years contributed to the eventual death, the original wound remains the foundational cause. For *galut*, however, the halakha demands a much higher degree of exclusive causal attribution — if *any* other factor may have contributed, the exemption applies. If the severing had been intentional rather than accidental, there would be no question: the perpetrator would be liable, regardless of whether wind or other factors may have marginally accelerated death. The *galut* exemption is a function of the narrow and highly technical standards that govern that specific *din*, not a general principle about causation in homicide.
