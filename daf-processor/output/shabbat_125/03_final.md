# Shabbat 125 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Burning Vessels

### Three Baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַסִּיקִין בְּכֵלִים, וְאֵין מַסִּיקִין בְּשִׁבְרֵי כֵלִים. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמַּסִּיקִין בְּכֵלִים, כָּךְ מַסִּיקִין בְּשִׁבְרֵי כֵלִים. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין מַסִּיקִין לֹא בְּכֵלִים וְלָא בְּשִׁבְרֵי כֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in the *baraitot* regarding Festivals: **One may kindle a fire with whole vessels, but one may not kindle a fire with shards of vessels.** A second *baraita* **taught: Just as one may kindle a fire with vessels, so too one may kindle a fire with shards of vessels.** And a third *baraita* **taught: One may neither kindle a fire with vessels nor with shards of vessels.**

The daf opens in the midst of a sustained discussion of *muktzah* and *Yom Tov*, picking up from the previous amud (124b). The *mishnah* had addressed *kelim* that broke on *Shabbos*, and the *gemara* explored two ways of reading the case: on one view, a vessel that broke on *Shabbos* is advantaged because it began *Shabbos* in a state of *muchan*, ready for use and outside the *muktzah* category. On the other view, breaking on *Shabbos* itself creates a *nolad* problem, and it is better if the vessel broke *erev Shabbos*. With this backdrop in mind, the *gemara* introduces three conflicting *baraitot*.

### R. Yehuda's Nolad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. הָא — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. הָא — רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between the *baraitot*: **This baraita, which distinguishes between vessels and broken vessels, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that there is a prohibition of set-aside on a Festival. **That baraita, which permits kindling with both vessels and shards, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that there is no prohibition of set-aside on a Festival. **This third baraita, which prohibits kindling with both vessels and shards, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya,** who prohibits moving a vessel for any purpose other than its designated function.

The first *beraita* states: *masikin bekelim ve'ein masikin beshivrei kelim* — on *Yom Tov* one may burn vessels as fuel, but not broken vessels. The second *beraita* states the reverse: *kesheim shemasikin bekelim kach masikin beshivrei kelim* — just as one may burn whole vessels, so too one may burn broken vessels. A third *beraita* states: *ein masikin lo bekelim velo beshivrei kelim* — one may burn neither. (Presumably, the permitted fuel in all views remains ordinary wood.) The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by attributing each position to a different *tanna*. The first *beraita* reflects the view of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that breaking on *Yom Tov* creates a *nolad* problem. Therefore, whole *kelim* may be burned — they are not *muktzah* — but broken vessels, having newly acquired their broken status on *Yom Tov*, are *nolad* and may not be used.

### R. Shimon Lenient

The second *beraita*, which permits burning broken vessels as well, reflects the view of Rabbi Shimon, who does not accept the category of *nolad* at all. In his view, even a *kli* that broke on *Yom Tov* itself may be used.

### R. Nechemia Restrictive

The third *beraita*, which prohibits burning even whole *kelim*, reflects the well-known stringent position of Rabbi Nechemia, who holds that a *kli* may be used only for its intended purpose. Since a vessel's intended purpose is not to serve as firewood, burning it is impermissible. And a broken vessel is doubly precluded — it would both fail the intended-purpose test and, if it broke on *Yom Tov*, raise a *nolad* concern as well.

## Leftover Bricks

### Bricks Movable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָנֵי לִיבְנֵי דְּאִישְׁתַּיּוּר מִבִּנְיָינָא — שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלִינְהוּ, דַּחֲזוּ לְמִיזְגָּא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: With regard to these bricks that remained from the building after construction was completed, it is permitted to move them on Shabbat, because they are suited for one to sit on them.**

Rav Nachman rules: *hanei livnei de'ishtayer mibinyana* — bricks left over from a construction project are permitted to be moved (*shari letaltulinhu*). The reasoning is *dechazu lemismach aleihu* — they are fit to lean or sit upon. This is a striking ruling. One would naturally assume that leftover construction bricks are *muktzah*, having been set aside for building purposes. Yet Rav Nachman holds that precisely because the common context of a pile of leftover bricks is one where people would sit on them in passing, no formal act of designation is needed. This ruling anticipates a broader question that will occupy the *gemara* later in the daf: what exactly must be done to transform an object — whether a stone, a brick, or a piece of nature — into something considered *muchan* and not *muktzah*? Rav Nachman's ruling here suggests that context alone, the fact that it is the normal kind of thing people would use, suffices. It is worth noting that this is better than a random stone: a brick is a human artifact, fashioned to be used, even if not currently serving any particular function.

One might raise the question whether moving and arranging these bricks would constitute *boneh*. The *gemara* implicitly sets this aside: since the bricks do not interlock and no *ohel* is being formed, simple stacking would not constitute *boneh*.

### Stacked Becomes Muktzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁרְגִינְהוּ וַדַּאי אַקְצִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And if **one arranged them in a pile, he certainly thereby set them aside** from his consciousness, and it is prohibited to move them.

However, Rav Nachman qualifies this: *sharginhu vadai aktsinhu* — if one has already stacked the bricks, that act itself constitutes a clear expression of intent to reserve them for future construction. The deliberate stacking demonstrates that the owner has mentally set them aside (*hiktzah*), and they therefore become *muktzah*. The underlying principle is revealing: in the absence of any act of consecration for construction purposes, leftover bricks remain presumptively available and non-*muktzah*. But a positive act of setting-aside reverses that presumption.

## Pottery Shards Context

### Shards: Chatzer vs Karmelis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חֶרֶס קְטַנָּה — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ בֶּחָצֵר, אֲבָל בְּכַרְמְלִית — לֹא. וְרַב נַחְמָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּכַרְמְלִית, אֲבָל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — לֹא. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said: With regard to a small earthenware shard, it is permitted to move it on Shabbat in the courtyard, but in a *karmelit*, no,** one may not move it, because typically there are no vessels there and there would be no use for the shard. **And Rav Naḥman himself said: Even in a *karmelit* it is permitted, but in the public domain, no,** it is prohibited. **And Rava said: Even in the public domain,** it is permitted.

The *gemara* continues with closely related cases. Rav Nachman citing Shmuel rules: *cheres ketana* — a small shard of pottery — *mutar letaltela bechatzer*, may be moved in a *chatzer*. The discussion implicitly assumes the shard broke before *Shabbos* to set aside any *nolad* concern. In a courtyard, where people conduct domestic activity and commonly have pottery in use, it is normal to pick up and use a pottery shard — to cover a vessel, for instance — and so such a shard is considered *muchan* and not *muktzah*. However, *aval bekarmelis* — in a *karmelis*, a semi-public domain — it may not be moved, even within four *amos*, because this is framed as a *muktzah* question, not a carrying question. The *karmelis* does not provide the context of active domestic use that gives the shard its presumptive utility.

This leads to a broader question that runs through this entire section of the *gemara*: what makes a *shever kli* — or more broadly, any object of ambiguous status — considered *muchan*? The answer being developed here is context. A shard in a courtyard is the kind of thing one would pick up and use; in a *karmelis*, that is less self-evident.

### R. Nachman: All Contexts

Rav Nachman himself, going beyond his ruling in the name of Shmuel, states: *afilu bekarmelis* — even in a *karmelis*, the shard may be moved. The sole exception, in his own view, is a *reshus harabbim* — a true public thoroughfare — where pottery shards are simply not the sort of objects one normally encounters or uses.

### Rava: Common Use Standard

Rava goes further still: *afilu birshus harabbim* — even in a *reshus harabbim* the shard is permitted, provided one has a use for it at that moment. The *muktzah* question, in Rava's view, should not turn on the specific domain one is currently in. If the object is the kind of thing that is *ra'uy* — fit for use — in *some* common context, that suffices to render it non-*muktzah* universally. One need not demonstrate that it is typically used in the specific location where one encounters it.

### Rava's Shoe Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ. דְּרָבָא הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל בְּרִיתְקָא דְמָחוֹזָא, אִתַּוַּוס מְסָאנֵיהּ טִינָא. אֲתָא שַׁמָּעֵיהּ, שְׁקַל חַסְפָּא וְקָא מְכַפַּר לֵיהּ. רְמוֹ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן קָלָא. אָמַר: לָא מִיסָּתְיָיא דְּלָא גְּמִירִי, מִיגְמָר נָמֵי מַגְמְרִי?! אִילּוּ בְּחָצֵר הֲוַאי מִי לָא הֲוָה חַזְיָא לְכַסּוֹיֵי בַּיהּ מָנָא? הָכָא נָמֵי — חַזְיָא לְדִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava follows his regular line of reasoning, as Rava was walking in the street of Meḥoza** and **his shoes became dirty with clay. His servant came, took a shard of earthenware from the street, and wiped the clay off. The Sages raised their voice at him to reprimand him. Rava said: Is it not enough for them that they did not learn, but they are also teaching others? If the shard was in a courtyard, wouldn't it be suited to cover a vessel with it? Here too, the shard is also suited to me,** and moving it should not be prohibited.

The *gemara* illustrates Rava's position through a vivid anecdote. Rava was walking in the *Ritka* — which Rashi identifies as the name of the *reshus harabbim* of *Mechuza*, his city — when his shoes became muddied (*itvassa masaneh*). His attendant picked up a pottery shard from the road and began scraping the mud from Rava's shoe. The rabbinic students who were present began reproving the attendant: one cannot pick up a pottery shard on *Shabbos*! Rava intervened sharply: *lo mistayei dlo gamri* — it is not enough that you yourselves do not know the *halacha*, *ela migmar nami migmri* — you must also proclaim your ignorance to others?

Rava then articulated the principle: *ilu bachatzer havai, mi lo chazi lechasuyei bei mana* — if we were in a courtyard, everyone would agree the shard could be used to cover a vessel; that is effectively our *mishnah*. *Hacha nami chazi ledidi* — here too, in the *reshus harabbim*, I have a use for it. The operative rule Rava establishes is that if an object is *ra'uy* in some common context, it is non-*muktzah* in all contexts. What is required is not that the object be useful specifically where it is found, but that it belongs to the category of things that people actually use — that in at least some, perhaps typical, circumstances it would be considered a usable object rather than garbage.

## Discarding Vessels

### Barrel Cover Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְגוּפַת חָבִית שֶׁנִּכְתְּתָה — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּשַׁבָּת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מְגוּפָה שֶׁנִּכְתְּתָה — הִיא וּשְׁבָרֶיהָ מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: With regard to the clay seal of a jug that was broken, it is permitted to move it on Shabbat. That was also taught in a baraita: With regard to the clay seal that was broken, it is permitted to move it, and its shards are permitted to be carried on Shabbat.**

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *megufas chavis shenichtesa* — if a barrel has been shattered, *mutar letaltla b'shabbos* — one may move both the cover and the broken pieces of the barrel. A *beraita* supports this: *megufah shenichtesa, hi ushvareha, mutar letaltela b'shabbos* — the cover and the barrel's broken pieces may be moved. As a *shever kli* that retains some functional use, the pieces remain non-*muktzah*.

### Don't Modify Shards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִסְפּוֹת מִמֶּנָּה שֶׁבֶר לְכַסּוֹת בָּהּ אֶת הַכְּלִי וְלִסְמוֹךְ בָּהּ כַּרְעֵי הַמִּטָּה. וְאִם זְרָקָהּ בָּאַשְׁפָּה — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not break a shard from it to cover a vessel with it or to support the legs of a bed with it. And if one threw it into the garbage dump, it is prohibited** to move it because he set it aside from his consciousness.

However, the *beraita* immediately qualifies: *v'lo yispus mimena shever lechasuyei bo kli* — one may not break off an additional piece from the barrel in order to use it to cover a vessel. Doing so constitutes an act of fashioning a vessel on *Shabbos*. Rashi extends this further: one may not even smooth the edges of an existing shard, which would be an even graver form of fashioning. Similarly, one may not use a shard to prop up a bed leg (*velismoch bah karei hamita*).

### Pre-Shabbat Disposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם זְרָקָהּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם לְאַשְׁפָּה — אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If one threw the clay seal of a jug into the garbage dump while it is still day, before Shabbat, it is prohibited** to move it on Shabbat because he indicated that he set it aside from use.

A critical qualification follows: *ve'im zarka b'ashpa — asura* — if one threw the broken barrel into the garbage, it becomes *asur*. This is the key to the *gemara*'s conceptual framework for *shever kli*. A broken vessel, because it retains functional potential, is presumptively non-*muktzah*. But the owner can redefine it as garbage — and once defined as garbage, with no use, it becomes *muktzah*. Context can make something *muktzah* even if its physical properties would otherwise permit use.

### Rav Pappa's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, זָרֵיק לֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵיהּ הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאָסוּר?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa strongly objects to this: If that is so, if one throws his cloak into the garbage dump, would you also say that it is prohibited to move it because it is set-aside? Isn't the cloak still fit for use, and its status is not dependent on his intention? Rather, Rav Pappa said:**

Rav Pappa challenges this reasoning: if throwing something out makes it *muktzah*, then *zarkeih liglimeh* — if you threw out your coat, does that too become *muktzah*? Rav Pappa resolves this by clarifying the *gemara*'s intent: the operative moment is *mibod yom la'ashpah* — if you threw the item out before *Shabbos*, *erev Shabbos*, when *Shabbos* was set in with the object already defined as garbage, only then is it *asur*.

The resolution distinguishes between two types of objects. A true *kli*, like a garment, does not lose its *kli* status merely by being discarded; its fundamental identity as a functional vessel persists. But a *shever kli*, whose status was already ambiguous — it is functional but was never wholly a vessel in the usual sense — can be definitively classified as garbage through the act of throwing it out before *Shabbos*. Once *Shabbos* enters with the object having been designated as refuse, its status is fixed for the duration of *Shabbos*. Conversely, throwing it out on *Shabbos* itself would not retroactively change its status, since it entered *Shabbos* as a non-*muktzah* item. The broader principle being refined here is that *shever kli* is presumptively better than a random stone or stick because it started as a *kli* and retains that residual character — but that character can be affirmatively negated before *Shabbos* through a clear act of disposal.

## Mat Strands & Garment Scraps

### Mat Strands Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: קְרוּמִיּוֹת שֶׁל מַחְצֶלֶת — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָן בְּשַׁבָּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רָבָא, בַּר הַמְדּוּרֵי אַסְבְּרַהּ לִי: מַחְצֶלֶת גּוּפַהּ לְמַאי חַזְיָא — לְכַסּוֹיֵי בַּהּ עַפְרָא, הָנֵי נָמֵי חַזְיָין לְכַסּוֹיֵי בְּהוּ טִינּוּפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Hamduri said** that **Shmuel said: With regard to shreds of reeds that separated from a mat, it is permitted to move them on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for this? Rava said: Bar Hamduri explained it to me: The mat itself, for what use is it suited?** It is suited **to cover dirt with it. These shreds, too, are suited to cover filth with them.**

Mar bar Hamdura citing Shmuel rules: *kromei d'machatzeles mutar l'taltelan b'Shabbos* — strands that have come loose from a reed mat may be moved on *Shabbos*. The reasoning, which Bar Hamdura explains he received directly, runs as follows: *machatzeles gufah l'mai chazei* — what is a mat itself used for, if not sitting upon and covering things? *L'chasuyei bei afra* — to cover dirt, to prevent dust from rising. Therefore, *hani nami chazu l'chasuyei bei tinofes* — even a single loose strand can be used to cover some filth. This is noteworthy: the strand not only performs some function, it can arguably perform a function *me'ein melachtan* — similar to the original function of the mat itself. Under the more stringent reading of the *mishnah* (associated with Rabbi Yehuda), the standard is that a *shever kli* must be capable of performing something similar to its original function. Even by that standard, the mat strand qualifies.

### Scraps Too Small

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: שְׁיָרֵי פְּרוּזְמָיוֹת — אָסוּר לְטַלְטְלָן בְּשַׁבָּת. אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּמַטְלָנִיּוֹת שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ, דְּלָא חַזְיָין לָא לַעֲנִיִּים וְלָא לַעֲשִׁירִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said: With regard to the remains of cloaks, it is prohibited to move them on Shabbat. Abaye said: This is referring to small rags that do not have an area of three by three fingerbreadths, that are neither suited for use by the poor nor by the wealthy.**

Rabbi Zeira citing Rav rules: *shirei pruzmos assur l'taltelan b'Shabbos* — remnants of a *pruzma* (a garment) may not be moved on *Shabbos*. Abaye explains: *b'matlaniyos she'ein bah gimmel al gimmel* — we are discussing scraps of cloth that do not even measure three by three handbreadths. Such a piece, *d'lo chazi lo l'aniyim v'lo l'ashirim* — is not usable by either the poor or the wealthy; it is not large enough even to serve as a patch. When the fragment of a *shever kli* has reached a point where it is too small to perform any reasonable function whatsoever, it exits the category of the usable and becomes *muktzah*.

## Broken Ovens

### Oven Debate Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁבְרֵי תַּנּוּר יָשָׁן הֲרֵי הֵן כְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים הַנִּיטָּלִין בֶּחָצֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין נִיטָּלִין. הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב עַל שִׁבְרֵי תַּנּוּר יָשָׁן שֶׁנִּיטָּלִין בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְעַל כִּיסּוּיוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ בֵּית יָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught in the Tosefta: The shards of an old oven may be moved in a courtyard on Shabbat like all of the vessels that may be moved in a courtyard on Shabbat; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: They may not be moved. Rabbi Yosei testified in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov about shards of an old oven that may be moved on Shabbat, and about its cover, which does not require a handle.**

The *gemara* now cites a formal *baraita*: *shirei tanur yashan harei hen k'chol hakelim hani-talin b'chatzer* — the broken pieces of an old oven are like all *kelim* that may be moved in a courtyard. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *ein nitlin* — they may not be moved. Rabbi Yossi testified in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov in agreement with Rabbi Meir, permitting the broken pieces to be moved.

Before examining the debate, the *gemara* uses the *beraita* to establish the physical context. A *tanur* was a large pottery vessel set into the ground with plaster and used for baking: a fire was placed at the base and pots were set over the top. The *beraita* also mentions the cover of the oven — which is relevant because the cover lacked a handle — a point to be addressed in a later section.

### Abaye's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּעוֹשִׂין מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין מֵעֵין מְלַאכְתָּן קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree? Abaye said: It is with regard to shards when they serve some function but do not serve a function similar to their own original function that they disagree.**

Abaye explains the underlying debate: *b'osin me'ein melacha v'ein osin me'ein melachtan kamifligi* — they dispute the case of broken pieces that can perform *some* function but not their original function. A broken shard of oven pottery can perhaps be used to cover a vessel, but it cannot bake bread. Can it nevertheless do something? Of course. Is that enough? That is the debate. Abaye identifies this as the same argument between the *tanna kamma* (understood to be Rabbi Meir) and Rabbi Yehuda in the *mishnah*: Rabbi Meir holds that any function suffices; Rabbi Yehuda holds that the function must be similar to the original function (*me'ein melachtan*). Under Abaye's reading the dispute about the oven pieces is simply an application of the generic *mishnah* debate to a particular case, a very clean and straightforward explanation.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, אַדְּמִיפַּלְגִי בְּשִׁבְרֵי תַנּוּר, לִיפַּלְגוּ בְּשִׁבְרֵי כֵלִים בְּעָלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava strongly objects to this: If so, instead of disagreeing with regard to shards of an oven, let them disagree with regard to ordinary shards.** Why is the dispute specifically with regard to an oven?

Rava raises a pointed challenge to Abaye's account: *i hachi, de-miflegi be-shivei tannura, liflegu be-shivei kelim be-alma* — if the debate is simply about objects that can perform some function but not their original one, why argue specifically about broken oven pieces? Argue about broken vessels in general! This challenge has force precisely because there is another, independent dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda involving ovens. Rava reasons that the *tannaim*' choice to argue specifically about ovens must be intentional — it must relate to something particular about ovens, not merely to the generic question of *shever kli*.

## Oven in Well

### Oven Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בְּשִׁבְרֵי דְּהַאי תַּנּוּר קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דִּתְנַן: נְתָנוֹ עַל פִּי הַבּוֹר אוֹ עַל פִּי הַדּוּת וְנָתַן שָׁם אֶבֶן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם מַסִּיק מִלְּמַטָּה וְהוּא נִסּוֹק מִלְּמַעְלָה — טָמֵא, וְאִם לָאו — טָהוֹר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הוֹאִיל וְהוּסַּק מִכׇּל מָקוֹם — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: It is with regard to shards of this particular oven that they disagree, as we learned in a mishna: With regard to a clay oven that is not attached to the ground with mortar in the standard manner, but rather, one placed it over the mouth of a pit or over the mouth of a cistern, and he placed a stone there between the wall of the pit and the oven to secure the oven in place, Rabbi Yehuda says: If one heats the oven from beneath the oven, inside the pit, and the oven is thereby heated at the top, the oven serves its standard function; it is a full-fledged utensil and it can become ritually impure. And if it is not attached so tightly that it is heated at the top, it is ritually pure, because it is not a full-fledged vessel. And the Rabbis say: Since it can be heated in some manner, it can become ritually impure, because it serves its standard purpose.**

Rava therefore proposes that the broken oven pieces under debate are the shards of a very particular kind of oven. He cites a *mishnah* from tractate *Kelim*: *natno al pi ha-bor o al pi ha-dut* — if an oven was placed into a well or into a *dut* (a cistern with a surrounding structure), *ve-natan sham even* — and stones were inserted around it. Rashi explains the configuration: the walls of the well were close to the walls of the oven, and small stones were wedged into the gaps to create a tight fit, preventing heat from escaping around the sides. Since the heat was thus entirely contained within the oven just as it would be if the oven were plastered to the ground, this configuration is treated as equivalent to being plastered to the ground.

The relevance of "attached to the ground" requires brief explanation. Under the laws of *tumah ve-taharah*, objects generally become susceptible to *tumah* only when they are detached from the ground; items connected to the ground are considered part of the earth and are *tahor*. An oven is exceptional: it becomes susceptible to *tumah* specifically when it is attached to the ground. The Talmudic derivation comes from the verse: *tannur ve-khirayim yutatz temeim hem* — an oven and a *kirah* shall be demolished (*yutatz*), they are *tamei*. The word *yutatz* — to demolish, as opposed to *shevirah*, simply to break — implies connection to the ground, since one demolishes something connected to the earth. Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis dispute what this implies.

### R. Yehuda: Connection Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי — בְּהַאי קְרָא: ״תַּנּוּר וְכִירַיִם יוּתָּץ טְמֵאִים הֵם וּטְמֵאִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם״, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: מְחוּסָּר נְתִיצָה — טָמֵא, שֶׁאֵין מְחוּסָּר נְתִיצָה — טָהוֹר. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: ״טְמֵאִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם״ — מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to what do they disagree? It is with regard to this verse:** "And everything upon which any part of their carcass falls shall be impure; **whether oven, or stove, it shall be broken in pieces; they are impure, and they shall be impure to you" (**Leviticus 11:35). **Rabbi Yehuda holds:** An oven that **lacks smashing,** i.e., it is whole and can be broken, can become **impure.** One **that does not lack smashing,** but it is situated in a place where it is not completely effective, is considered broken and is **pure,** i.e., it cannot become ritually impure. **And the Rabbis hold** that the verse comes to add: **"They shall be impure to you," in any case,** under any circumstances.

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *mechusar netitza tammei* — only that which requires demolition, i.e., something connected to the ground, can become *tamei* as an oven. *She-ein mechusar netitza tahor* — an oven not connected to the ground when first fired is not a true oven and therefore is *tahor*. The *mishnah* in *Kelim* states Rabbi Yehuda's position as follows: *im masik milmata ve-hu nisok milmala* — if one fires from below and the oven heats fully at the top (meaning the heat is well contained by the tight stone-wedged fit), then *tamei*. *Ve-im lav* — if heat escapes around the sides, then *tahor*, because it is not sufficiently equivalent to being plastered to the ground.

### Rabbis: No Connection Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״יוּתָּץ״? הָהוּא לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דְּחַבְּרֵיהּ בְּאַרְעָא — כְּגוּפָא דְאַרְעָא דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the Rabbis, too, isn't it written:** "They **shall be broken into pieces,"** and why don't they interpret the verse in the same way that Rabbi Yehuda does? The Gemara explains: The Rabbis understand **this** verse **from another direction, as it could have entered your mind to say: Since he attached it to the ground,** its legal status is **like that of the ground itself,** and anything attached to the ground cannot become impure. Therefore, **it teaches us** that since it is possible to detach it from the ground, it is indeed impure.

The Rabbis hold: *temeim yihyu lachem mikol makom* — the verse's concluding phrase, "they shall be *tamei* to you," is inclusive and requires no condition of ground connection. Once the oven is fired, it becomes *tamei* regardless of whether it is connected to the ground. *Hu ve-hu sak mikol makom* — it makes no difference.

The Rabbis do not ignore the word *yutatz*, however. They interpret it differently: *de-salka da'atcha amina keivan de-chavrei be-ara, ke-gufa de-ara dami* — one might have thought that since the oven is connected to the ground, it is like the ground itself and cannot become *tamei*. The word *yutatz* comes to teach *ka mashma lan* that even a ground-connected oven becomes *tamei*. In other words, for the Rabbis *yutatz* does not impose a requirement of connection; it removes a potential exemption.

Conversely, Rabbi Yehuda, who requires connection, must explain the phrase *temeim yihyu lachem*. The answer — attributed to the tradition of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — leads to the next point.

### First vs Second Firing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״טְמֵאִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם״! הָהִיא, כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּהֶיסֵּק רִאשׁוֹן, אֲבָל בְּהֶיסֵּק שֵׁנִי, אֲפִילּוּ תָּלוּי בְּצַוַּאר גָּמָל. וּלָא הִיא, אֱלָא אָמַר עוּלָּא: וְהֶיסֵּק רִאשׁוֹן לְרַבָּנַן, אֲפִילּוּ תָּלוּי בְּצַוַּאר גָּמָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other tanna, Rabbi Yehuda, too, isn't it written: "They shall be impure to you"?** The Gemara answers: He understood **that** verse **in accordance with** that which **Rav Yehuda** said that **Shmuel said, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **is** specifically **with regard to the first lighting.** The first lighting transforms an earthenware oven that did not yet completely dry into a vessel. **However, with regard to the second lighting** they do not disagree, **even** if it was **hanging around a camel's neck;** since it had already been fired up once, it is impure. **And that is not so, rather Ulla said: And** with regard to **the first lighting** according **to the Rabbis, even** if it was **hanging around a camel's neck,** since it had already been fired up once it is a full-fledged oven and is impure.

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel clarifies: *machlokes be-heseik rishon* — this entire dispute applies only to the first firing of the oven, when the pottery walls are hardened and the oven acquires its essential identity. It is at that inaugural firing that Rabbi Yehuda requires the oven to be properly connected to the ground. *Aval heseik sheini* — but if the oven was subsequently detached and then refired, everyone agrees that ground connection is not required, *afilu talui be-tzavar gamal* — even if it were suspended from the neck of a camel. Ulla confirms this: even for the Rabbis' position, their ruling that no ground connection is required applies from the first firing; *afilu talui be-tzavar gamal* it would be *tamei*.

## Rava Resolves Oven Debate

### Rava's Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם הַתַּנּוּר לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הוּא שֶׁלֹּא חִזּוּק כִּדְקָמַן דִּתְנַן, הֲרֵי הוּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא הָוֵי מָנָא, וְשִׁבְרָיו לָא הָוֵי מְלִיטוּ דְּמָנָא, אֶלָּא שִׁבְרֵי תַּנּוּר יָשָׁן מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וּלְרַבָּנַן הַתַּנּוּר הוּא מָנָא וְשִׁבְרָיו מִלִּיטוּ דְּמָנָא וּמוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** (This segment is implicit in the text as Rava applies his logic to explain the positions.)

Rava applies this *tumah ve-taharah* dispute directly to the *Shabbos* question. If the oven was placed in the well without achieving sufficient ground connection as required by Rabbi Yehuda — that is, if heat escaped around the sides — then Rabbi Yehuda holds it was never a proper *kli* in the first place. Since it was not a true oven-*kli*, its broken pieces are certainly not pieces of a *kli*, and therefore they are *muktzah*. The Rabbis, who hold that even an unconnected oven is a valid *kli* susceptible to *tumah*, would naturally permit moving the broken pieces on *Shabbos*.

### Rav Ashi Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: אִי הָכִי, אַדְּמִיפַּלְגִי בְּשִׁבְרֵי תַנּוּר, לִיפַּלְגוּ בְּתַנּוּר גּוּפֵהּ. הַשְׁתָּה תַּנּוּר גּוּפֵהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא הָוֵי מָנָא, שְׁבָרָיו מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi strongly objects to this line of reasoning: If so, instead of disagreeing with regard to the shards of an oven, let them disagree with regard to the oven itself. Now the oven itself, according to Rabbi Yehuda, is not** considered **a vessel;** therefore, to say that **its broken pieces** are not vessels, **is it necessary?**

Rav Ashi objects sharply: *i hachi, ade-mifligi be-shivrei tanur, lifligi be-tanur gufa* — if that is what they are disputing, they should be arguing about the oven itself, not its broken pieces. If Rabbi Yehuda holds the oven was never a *kli*, that would be the more fundamental question; why frame the debate around the broken pieces? *Hashata tanur gufa le-rabbi Yehuda lo havei mana, ma shevarav mi-ba'ya* — if the oven itself is not a *kli* according to Rabbi Yehuda, there is obviously no question about its pieces. Rav Ashi therefore rejects Rava's explanation entirely.

## Tafka Usage Case

### Stovetop Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לְעוֹלָם כְּדַאֲמַרַן מֵעִיקָּרָא וּבְעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה טַפְקָא, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָאָמַר: לְדִידִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּעוֹשִׂין מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, אוֹדִי לִי מִיהָא דִּכְהַאי גַּוְונָא מְלַאכְתּוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: Actually, it is as we said initially,** that it is referring to shards of any oven, **and** with which **he crafts a ceramic board [*tapka*], and Rabbi Meir is speaking in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda:** According **to my** opinion, it is permitted to move **even** shards that **serve** any **purpose. However, according to your** opinion, **agree with me at least that in a case of this sort, it is a function** similar to their own original function. The shards can be used for baking.

Rav Ashi proposes that the debate is exactly what Abaye said it was originally — about broken pieces of a vessel that can perform some function but not their original function — and that there is nothing mysterious about the choice of an oven as the example. *U-ve-osa ma'aseh tafka* — the broken piece of an oven can be used as a *tafka*, which Rashi describes as functioning like a stovetop or grill. Instead of placing fire inside and containing heat within walls (as in an actual oven), one places a fire underneath the broken shard and the shard becomes hot, so that food can be placed on top of it. This is a useful function, but it is structurally different from baking in an oven.

### R. Meir's Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָאָמַר: לְדִידִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּעוֹשִׂין מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, אוֹדִי לִי מִיהָא דִּכְהַאי גַּוְונָא מְלַאכְתּוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir is speaking in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda:** According **to my** opinion, it is permitted to move **even** shards that **serve** any **purpose. However, according to your** opinion, **agree with me at least that in a case of this sort, it is a function** similar to their own original function. The shards can be used for baking.

Rabbi Meir addresses Rabbi Yehuda with an internal argument: *le-didi* — according to my own position, any function whatsoever suffices and there is no requirement of similarity to the original function. But *ella ledidach* — even granting your position, admit at least that this piece performs something close enough to its original function to satisfy your standard.

### R. Yehuda's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לָא דָּמֵי, הָתָם — הֶסֵּקוֹ מִבִּפְנִים, הָכָא — הֶסֵּקוֹ מִבַּחוּץ. הָתָם — מְעוּמָּד, הָכָא — לָאו מְעוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda** says: It is **not similar. There,** in the case of an intact oven, **its firing is from within. Here,** with regard to the shard, **its firing is from without. There,** in the case of an intact oven, it bakes **standing; here,** it does **not** bake **standing.** Therefore, its function is not similar to its original function.

Rabbi Yehuda replies: *lo dami* — it is not comparable. *Hasam haseka mibifnim, hacha haseka mibachutz* — in the oven the fire burns on the inside and the walls contain and radiate the heat; here the fire is on the outside. *Hasam be'umad* — in an oven the items being cooked stand upright within; *hacha lav be'umad* — here items lie flat on the surface. The two modes of cooking are fundamentally different and the broken shard's function does not qualify as *me'ein melachtan* — similar enough to its original function.

## Covers & Stoppers

### Oven Cover Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב עַל שִׁבְרֵי תַּנּוּר יָשָׁן שֶׁנִּיטָּלִין בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְעַל כִּיסּוּיוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ בֵּית יָד. אָמַר רָבִינָא: כְּמַאן מְטַלְטְלִינַן הָאִידָּנָא כִּיסּוּי דְּתַנּוּרֵי דְמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא דְּאֵין לָהֶם בֵּית אֲחִיזָה? כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** In that same *Tosefta* where **Rabbi Yosei testified in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov about shards of an old oven that may be moved on Shabbat, and about its cover, which does not require a handle,** and which is considered a vessel and may be moved as is, **Ravina says: In accordance with whose opinion do we now move the oven covers in the city of Meḥasya that do not have handles? In accordance with whose opinion is it? It is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.**

The *beraita* that introduced the oven debate also established that the cover of an oven (*kisuyo*) may be moved on *Shabbos she'eino tzarich beis yad* — even if it lacks a handle. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's testimony in the *beraita* covers both the broken oven pieces and the handle-less cover. This ruling is invoked practically: Avida asks, according to whom do we nowadays move the oven covers of *Matza Mechasya* that lack handles? *Ke-Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. The question of whether a handle-less cover raises *boneh* concerns is connected to the broader issue of whether an object that fits snugly into a structure begins to look less like a movable *kli* and more like a building component. A handle signals that removal is intended and expected; its absence raises that concern, which is part of why the ruling needed to be stated explicitly.

## Gourd & Stone

### Tight Fit Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאֶבֶן שֶׁבְּקֵירוּיָה, אִם מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ וְאֵינָהּ נוֹפֶלֶת — מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A stone that is in a gourd** used to draw water **[*kiruya*], if they fill it** with water **and** the stone **does not fall, one may fill with it** on Shabbat, **and if not,** and the stone does fall, **one may not fill with it.**

The *mishnah* introduces a new case: *ha'even shebakiruya* — the stone in the *kiruyah*, a type of hollowed-out gourd used for drawing water. A gourd floats and therefore cannot draw water without ballast. The common solution was to insert a stone to weigh it down. The *mishnah* rules: *im memalin bah ve'einah nofeles, memalin bah* — if when one draws water the stone does not fall out, the gourd may be used and the stone is considered part of the vessel. *Ve'im lav* — if the stone would float out, one may not use the gourd: the stone remains *muktzah*, the gourd may become a *basis* for a *muktzah* object, and the entire assembly is problematic.

This case raises in concrete form the question of what is needed to transform a stone — a piece of nature with no prior history as a *kli* — into a functional vessel component. The *mishnah* will be analyzed further in the *gemara* below.

## Window Stopper

### R. Eliezer: Tied Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּקַק הַחַלּוֹן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא קָשׁוּר וְתָלוּי — פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ — פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a window shutter, Rabbi Eliezer says: When it is tied and hanging from the window, i.e., it is not touching the ground, one may shutter the window with it,** because it is not considered building; **and if not,** i.e., it is touching the ground, **one may not shutter the window with it. And the Rabbis say: Both in this case and in that case one may shutter with it.**

The *mishnah* now turns to *pkak hachalon* — a stopper or plug for a window. In the absence of window shades, large pieces of wood or similar material were used to block window openings. The question is whether one may install such a stopper on *Shabbos*. Rabbi Eliezer rules: *bizman shekashur vetalui pokmin bo* — only when the stopper is both tied to the wall and suspended (i.e., not dragging on the ground) may one use it to stop the window. If it is not tied and not suspended, one may not — *ve'im lav ein pokmin bo*. The reasoning is that inserting an unattached, unsuspended piece into a wall opening looks like *boneh* — one is adding a new structural element to the building. But if the stopper is already tied to the wall and hangs suspended, it is recognizably part of the window structure, akin to a shutter, and closing the window with it is not an act of construction.

### Sages: Always Permitted

The Sages disagree: *bein kach uvein kach pokin bo* — regardless of whether the stopper is tied and suspended or not, one may use it. The Sages are less concerned that inserting a temporary stopper into a window constitutes *boneh*, and their position does not require the formalities Rabbi Eliezer demands.

## Forgotten Stone

### Forgotten Stone Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: אֶבֶן שֶׁעַל פִּי הֶחָבִית — מַטָּהּ עַל צִידָּהּ, וְהִיא נוֹפֶלֶת. אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשׁוֹכֵחַ, אֲבָל בְּמַנִּיחַ — נַעֲשָׂה בָּסִיס לְדָבָר הָאָסוּר. וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשׁוֹכֵחַ, אֲבָל בְּמַנִּיחַ — נַעֲשָׂה כִּיסּוּי לֶחָבִית.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **We learned** in a mishna **there:** In the case of **a stone that is atop a barrel** and one wants to open the barrel, **he tilts** the barrel **on its side and** the stone **falls. Rabba said** that **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They only taught** this in a case **where one forgets** the stone atop the barrel; **however,** in a case **where one places** the stone atop the barrel intentionally, the barrel **becomes a base for a prohibited object,** and it is therefore prohibited to move the barrel. **And Rav Yosef** said that **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They only taught** this in a case **where one forgets** it; **however,** in a case **where one places** it there, the stone **becomes a cover for the barrel** and it is permitted to use it like other barrel covers.

The *gemara* now cites a later *mishnah* in the tractate: *even sheal pi hachavit* — a stone placed on the mouth of a barrel as a stopper. The ruling is that one may tip the barrel so that the stone falls off (*mateh al tzidah vehi nofelet*), thereby accessing the barrel's contents. The stone is *muktzah* and the barrel itself is not a *basis*. Rabba citing Rav Ami citing Rabbi Yochanan qualifies: *lo shanu ela beshocheach* — this ruling applies only when the stone was left on the barrel through forgetfulness. *Aval bemaniach* — if the stone was intentionally left there as a stopper, *naaseh basis ledavar haasur* — the barrel becomes a *basis* for a *muktzah* object and the barrel itself becomes *muktzah*. Since the owner knowingly rested a *muktzah* stone on the barrel and left it there through *bein hashmashos*, the barrel is secondary to the stone and acquires *muktzah* status along with it.

### Intentional Cover

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשׁוֹכֵחַ, אֲבָל בְּמַנִּיחַ — נַעֲשָׂה כִּיסּוּי לֶחָבִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef** said that **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They only taught** this in a case **where one forgets** it; **however,** in a case **where one places** it there, the stone **becomes a cover for the barrel** and it is permitted to use it like other barrel covers.

Rav Yosef citing Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan presents the inverse ruling, stated in identical terms but with opposite import: *lo shanu ela beshocheach* — the *tuma* that the stone retains *muktzah* status applies only when it was left there by forgetfulness. *Aval bemaniach* — if one intentionally placed the stone there, *naaseh kisui lachavit* — the stone becomes a cover for the barrel, i.e., it becomes a *kli*. By deliberately using the stone in a functional role as a barrel cover, the owner has transformed it from a mere stone into a *kli*, and it is no longer *muktzah*.

This is the precise inverse of the *shever kli* discussion: there, an object began as a *kli* and broke, and we explored how much it retains its *kli* status. Here, an object begins as a piece of nature and we ask what is needed to elevate it into *kli* status. Rav Assi's position, as transmitted by Rav Yosef, is that intentional functional use alone suffices.

## Gourd & Stone Redux

### Action vs Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, מוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין: הָאֶבֶן שֶׁבְּקֵירוּיָה אִם מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ וְאֵינָהּ נוֹפֶלֶת — מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ! וְלָא הִיא. הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּהַדְּקַהּ — שַׁוְּיַהּ דּוֹפֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: We raise an objection to our *halakha*** from the mishna: With regard to **a stone that is in a gourd** used to draw water, **if they fill it** with water **and** the stone **does not fall, one may fill with it** on Shabbat. Apparently, if the stone is designated for a purpose, it is no longer set-aside. He rejects the proof: **And that is not so,** as these cases are not comparable. **There,** in the case of the stone in the gourd, **since one attached** it to the gourd, **he rendered** the stone **a wall** of the gourd and part of the vessel, unlike in the case of the stone atop the barrel.

The two positions regarding the barrel stone are in direct tension with the *mishnah*'s case of the stone in the gourd. Rabba raises the challenge against his own position (that the stone on the barrel remains *muktzah*): the *mishnah* in the *kiruyah* case says that if the stone does not fall out, one may use the gourd — implying that the tight-fitting stone is automatically rendered a *kli*, even without any deliberate act. Does that not prove that merely using an object functionally suffices to make it a *kli*?

### Rav Ami vs Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין: אִם לָאו — אֵין מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ! וְלָא הִיא. הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הַדְּקַהּ — בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלַהּ. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה, וּמָר סָבַר לָא בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: And we raise an objection to our *halakha*** from the mishna: **And if not,** and the stone does fall, **one may not fill with it.** A stone that is not attached is not considered to be part of the vessel and is therefore set-aside. He rejects the proof. **And that is not so,** as these cases are not comparable. **There, since he did not attach** the stone to the gourd, **he negates its** status as a part of the vessel and it remains set-aside. The Gemara explains: **With regard to what do they disagree? One Sage,** Rabba, **holds** that **we require an action** to change the status of a stone or another set-aside object into a vessel, **and one Sage,** Rav Yosef, **holds** that **we do not require an action.**

The *gemara* responds: *velo hi* — there is no contradiction. *Hasam keivan de-hatka, shavye dofen* — in the case of the gourd, one did something: the stone was fitted tightly, pressed in so it would not fall out, effectively making it a wall of the gourd. That physical integration constitutes an act. Rav Yosef, who holds the opposite position (that functionality alone suffices), is also challenged by the *kiruyah* case: if no action is needed, why does the stone become *muktzah* when it is loose and would float out? The answer for Rav Yosef's position is: *hasam keivan delo hatka, bei bitulei batlei* — there it is not that the absence of an act disqualifies the stone; rather, a stone that will float out is simply not functional, and without functionality there is no basis to treat it as a *kli*.

The *gemara* thus articulates the fundamental dispute: *mar savar ba'inan ma'aseh umar savar lo ba'inan ma'aseh* — one authority holds that physical action is required to transform a stone into a *kli*, while the other holds that demonstrated functionality alone suffices.

## Designation of Objects

### Rabbi's Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַךְ רַבִּי לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד וּמָצָא נִדְבָּךְ שֶׁל אֲבָנִים, וְאָמַר לְתַלְמִידָיו: צְאוּ וְחַשְּׁבוּ כְּדֵי שֶׁנֵּשֵׁב עֲלֵיהֶן לְמָחָר, וְלֹא הִצְרִיכָן רַבִּי לְמַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And they,** Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi, **follow their regular line of reasoning, as when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he said that **Rabbi Ḥanina said, and some say that** it was **Rabbi Zeira** who **said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Once Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **went to one place and found a course of building stones, and** he **said to his students: Go out and think** that you are designating these stones for Shabbat **so that we may sit on them tomorrow** on Shabbat, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **did not require them** to perform **an action** with those stones. Thought alone was sufficient.

The *gemara* now cites a tradition about Rabbi himself. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael he reported, in the name of Rabbi Chanina (with an alternative attribution to Rabbi Zeira citing Rabbi Chanina): *pa'am achat halach Rabbi l'makom echad* — on one occasion Rabbi traveled to a certain place and found *nidbach shel avanim* — a row of stones. It was *erev Shabbos*, and he said to his students: *tse'u v'chishvu k'dei she-neshev aleihem l'machar* — go and think about them, designate them mentally, so that we can use them tomorrow. The report states that Rabbi did not require any physical act (*velo hitchrichan Rabbi l'ma'aseh*) — mere mental designation was sufficient to render the stones non-*muktzah*. This appears to be direct evidence that action is not required.

### R. Yochanan Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הִצְרִיכָן רַבִּי לְמַעֲשֶׂה. מַאי אֲמַר לְהוּ? רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: ״צְאוּ וְלַמְּדוּם״ אֲמַר לְהוּ. רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: ״צְאוּ וְשַׁפְשְׁפוּם״ אֲמַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** That is not what happened. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **required them** to perform **an action** to designate the stones. The Gemara asks: **What** action **did he say to them** to perform? **Rabbi Ami said** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: Go out and arrange** the stones. **Rabbi Asi said** that **he said to them: Go out and rub** the mortar off of **them.** Rabbi Ami requires a more significant action to render the stones a vessel.

However, Rabbi Yochanan disputes this reading: *hitchrichan Rabbi l'ma'aseh* — Rabbi did in fact require that something be done to the stones. The question then becomes: what precisely did Rabbi demand?

### Rav Ami's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֵׂה טַפְקָא, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָאָמַר: לְדִידִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּעוֹשִׂין מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, אוֹדִי לִי מִיהָא דִּכְהַאי גַּוְונָא מְלַאכְתּוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** (This is Rav Ashi's explanation of the Meir-Yehuda dispute, applied analogously.)

Rav Ami — who holds that physical action is required — reports that Rabbi Yochanan said Rabbi instructed his students: *tse'u velamdu* — go and arrange them. Rashi clarifies the point: the instruction was not that arranging would suffice to make them non-*muktzah*, but rather the reverse. Because the stones are going to remain *muktzah* (you cannot change their status without real action, and there was no time to do so properly), Rabbi told the students: position them now while it is still permitted to move them, because once *Shabbos* enters you will not be able to. The act of arrangement was not a solution to the *muktzah* problem but an instruction to deal with the stones before *Shabbos*.

### Rav Assi's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: ״צְאוּ וְשַׁפְשְׁפוּם״ אֲמַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said** that **he said to them: Go out and rub** the mortar off of **them.**

Rav Assi reports a different version of what Rabbi said: *tse'u v'shifshifu* — go and dust them off, simply brush the dirt off them. This minimal gesture of attention, showing that one has taken an interest in the stones and intends to use them, would be sufficient designation to render them non-*muktzah*. Under this version, no substantial physical action is required — a trivial act of attention suffices.

### Alternative Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל אָמַר: סְוָאר שֶׁל קוֹרוֹת הֲוָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן שָׁאוּל אָמַר: גָּשׁוֹשׁ שֶׁל סְפִינָה הֲוָה. מַאן דַּאֲמַר גָּשׁוֹשׁ — כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן סְוָאר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר סְוָאר — אֲבָל גָּשׁוֹשׁ קָפֵיד עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there was a dispute with regard to this matter. **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul said: It was a new stack of beams,** not stones. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Shaul said: It was the sounding pole of a ship** used to determine the depth of the water. **The one who said** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi permitted sitting on a ship's **sounding pole, all the more so** he permitted doing so in the case of **beams. And** with regard to **the one who said** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi permitted sitting on a stack of **beams, but** in the case of the **sounding pole** he would prohibit doing so because it is set-aside due to monetary loss, as he is **particular about it** that it will not become warped and damaged.

The *gemara* also notes that the identity of the object in Rabbi's story is itself disputed. Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul says it was *soreh shel koros* — a row of beams, not stones. Rabbi Yochanan ben Shaul says it was *gishra she-safina* — a pole used to test water depth to prevent a boat from grounding. The identification matters: one who says it was the depth-testing pole would certainly agree that the same ruling applies *a fortiori* to a pile of beams; but one who says it was merely a pile of beams might hold differently for the valuable depth-testing pole, which the owner would not want anyone touching, making it *muktzah machamat chisron kis* — set aside due to financial concern. These two versions thus raise distinct concerns: the former is about how one transforms a piece of nature into a *kli*, while the latter is about whether an object's special value causes its owner to mentally restrict its use.

The underlying question of this entire section can be sharpened. The *shever kli* case and the stone-into-*kli* case are mirror images. A *shever kli* started as a *kli* and retains that residual character even in broken form — it is presumptively non-*muktzah* and requires a positive act (throwing it out) to become *muktzah*. An untouched piece of nature has no such presumption; it has never been a *kli* and must acquire that status. Whether acquisition requires physical action, demonstrated functionality, or mere mental designation is precisely what the various authorities dispute.

## Vine & Bucket

### Vine Tying Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְמוֹרָה שֶׁהִיא קְשׁוּרָה בַּטָּפִיחַ — מְמַלְּאִין בָּהּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a vine branch that is tied to a pitcher, one may fill water with it on Shabbat** because the branch became part of the vessel.

The *mishnah* rules: *zemora shekeshurah betapiach, memalin bah beShabbos* — a vine tied to a *tapiach* (some vessel used for drawing water) may be used on *Shabbos* to draw water. If it is not tied, it may not be used: it is merely a random vine and retains *muktzah* status. The tying — a deliberate act connecting the vine to the bucket — is what converts it from a piece of nature into a functional component of a water-drawing vessel.

## Palm Pieces as Seats

### Repurposing Palm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: חֲרִיּוֹת שֶׁל דֶּקֶל שֶׁגְּדָרָן לְעֵצִים וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן לִישִׁיבָה — צָרִיךְ לִקְשׁוֹר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין צָרִיךְ לִקְשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught in a baraita: With regard to hard branches of a palm tree that one cut for firewood or for construction, and** then **he reconsidered their designation and decided to use them for sitting, he must tie** the branches together on Shabbat eve so that they will not be set-aside. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He need not tie** them together, and nevertheless, it is permitted to move them. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, cut wood need not be specially prepared to be used on Shabbat.

The *gemara* introduces a closely related case: *chariyot shel dekel shegigeran le-eitzim* — pieces carved from a palm tree for use as firewood. *Ve-nimalach aleihen lishiva* — and one changes one's mind and decides to use them as seating instead. The situation is analogous to the vine and the stone: an object that had been designated for one use (firewood, a non-*kli* use) and now one wishes to repurpose it as a seat. What does one need to do?

### Designation Debate

The *beraita* states: *tzarich likshor* — one must tie the pieces together (to form a bench-like structure) in order to transform them into seating. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *ein tzarich likshor* — it is sufficient merely to designate them mentally; no physical act of tying is required. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel may be more lenient here because the palm pieces have already been cut and are thus already under human control, unlike a completely natural stone. Having already been fashioned into something (even if only firewood), perhaps mental designation alone suffices to redirect their use.

## Vine & Bucket Redux

### Attached vs Detached

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּמְחוּבֶּרֶת בְּאִבֶּיהָ. אִי הָכִי, קָא מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע! לְמַטָּה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בִּתְלוּשָׁה, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִקְטוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, with what are we dealing here? With** a case where the shoot is still **connected to its origin,** the vine. The Gemara asks: **If so, he is making use** of an item that **is attached to the ground,** and the Sages issued a decree prohibiting the use of any plant attached to the ground. The Gemara answers: This is referring to a branch attached to the vine **below three** handbreadths off the ground. A vine attached to the ground below three handbreadths off the ground was not prohibited in that decree, just as it is permitted to make use of tree roots adjacent to the ground. **Rav Ashi said: Even** if **you say** that it is referring **to a** branch that **is detached,** nevertheless, its use is prohibited due to the **decree lest one cut** and straighten the branch to prepare it for use with the bucket. Therefore, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel teaches that there is no need for concern.

Rav Sheshet resolves the apparent tension between the *mishnah*'s vine case and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's lenient position: even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would concede that the vine tied to the bucket requires actual tying rather than mere designation. *Hacha b'mai askinan — bemichuberet be-aviha* — the case of the vine involves a vine still attached to the ground (*mechuberet*). This is not merely a *muktzah* question; it involves the prohibition of using something attached to the ground (*mechubar lekarka*), out of concern one might come to detach it. A vine attached to the ground is an intrinsically harder case to permit, which is why tying to the bucket is required. One might ask: if the vine is attached to the ground, the concern should be the prohibition of using *mechubar*, not merely *muktzah* — even if it were tied to the bucket, should that not remain prohibited? The *gemara* addresses this: *lemata migimmel* — the vine is within three handbreadths of the ground. Objects attached to the ground but extending more than three handbreadths upward present a greater concern that one might forget they are attached; within three handbreadths, the attachment is obvious and the risk is reduced, making use permissible.

Rav Ashi adds: *afilu teima bitlusha* — even if the vine were detached, we might require actual tying before *Shabbos*. *Gezeira shema yikto* — there is a rabbinic concern that if one were permitted to use a detached vine merely by designating it, one might come to break off a vine from a living plant, which is biblically prohibited. Therefore even for a detached vine, the *gemara* demands the affirmative act of tying it to the bucket before *Shabbos*, rather than mere designation.

The underlying axis of the entire daf thus comes into relief. On one side stands the *shever kli*: it began as a *kli* and, even in fragments, retains more of its non-*muktzah* character, requiring only that one not actively discard it before *Shabbos*. On the other side stand pieces of nature — stones, vines, palm cuttings — which must be brought into the category of *kli* through some act or designation. Whether that act must be physical and substantial (*ma'aseh*), minimal (dusting off), or merely mental (thought alone) is the question around which this entire sugya revolves, with different authorities drawing the line at different points.
