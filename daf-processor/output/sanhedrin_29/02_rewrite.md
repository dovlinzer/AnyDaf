# Sanhedrin 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stepson & Brother-in-law

### Relatives Overview

The *daf* under discussion is 29. We begin with the *Bavli* and pick up from a broader discussion of *krovim* — relatives who are disqualified from serving as witnesses or judges. We have already established that the disqualification extends through uncles and first cousins, but not beyond: a *sheini b'shlishi* or *shlishi b'shlishi* relationship — second cousins once removed or second cousins — falls outside the scope of *pesul*. The disqualification extends even through marriage, and the *Gemara* had explored various approaches to that question, whether the chain begins with the mother-in-law or ends with the son-in-law. We also addressed the status of *mechutanim* and a betrothed wife.

We now pick up on the sixth wide line of 28b with the ruling *chorgo levado* — one's stepson alone is disqualified, but not his descendants. The stepson's son or son-in-law is not included; the *pesul* stops at the stepson himself.

### Yehuda vs. Yosi Debate

With that in hand, the *Gemara* presents a *braisa*: *tanu rabbanan* — *chorgo levado*, only the stepson himself is disqualified, without extension to later generations. *Rabbi Yosi omer giso* — Rabbi Yosi adds that the brother-in-law (*giso*) similarly does not pass down a generation. A second *braisa* then states *giso levado* — the brother-in-law himself is the limit of the disqualification — and Rabbi Yehuda counters with *chorgo*, pointing back to the stepson.

The immediate question is how these two positions relate to one another: are they complementary or mutually exclusive?

The *Gemara* entertains the first possibility: *ee'leima hachi ka'amar* — perhaps the first *tanna* means the stepson does not go down a generation, and the same is true of the brother-in-law, while Rabbi Yosi similarly means the brother-in-law does not go down, and the same is certainly true of the stepson. On this reading they are saying the same thing, merely from different starting points. But this interpretation immediately collides with our *mishnah*, which teaches *giso hu u'veno ve'chasno* — that a brother-in-law, together with his son and son-in-law (that is, the nephew through marriage), is included in the disqualification. If both *tannaim* agree that neither stepson nor brother-in-law extends to the next generation, who is the author of our *mishnah*, which holds that the brother-in-law *does* extend? It would fit neither Rabbi Yehuda nor Rabbi Yosi.

The *Gemara* therefore rereads the dispute as one of mutual exclusion. Rabbi Yehuda — who is the author of our *mishnah* — says only the stepson fails to pass down: *chorgo levado*, but *aval giso hu bino ve'chasno* — the brother-in-law does extend to his son and son-in-law, reaching the nephew through marriage. Rabbi Yosi counters: *giso levado* — it is the brother-in-law who does not extend, *aval chorgo hu bino ve'chasno* — but the stepson would pass down a generation. On this reading the two authorities are in complete disagreement, each identifying a different figure as the one who stops at himself.

### Chiyya's Eight & Twenty-…

Having established that the debate is one of mutual exclusion, the *Gemara* raises a further complication from Rabbi Chiyya's *braisa*: *shmonah avos shehen esrim ve'arba* — there are eight primary relatives, and when extended to their sons and stepsons, these become twenty-four. The *Gemara* asks: *keman* — who does this go like? It cannot fit Rabbi Yehuda, who removes only the stepson from the list of those who extend, and it cannot fit Rabbi Yosi either, who removes only the brother-in-law.

The resolution requires counting carefully. Our *mishnah*, even after removing the stepson, contains nine relatives for whom the disqualification extends generationally. Rabbi Chiyya's teaching of eight requires removing two figures from that list. The only two candidates are stepson and brother-in-law. There must therefore be a *tanna* who eliminates *both* from the set of those who extend down a generation.

The *Gemara* accordingly rereads the debate a final time. The author of our *mishnah* is Rabbi Yehuda, who removes only the stepson — *chorgo levado*, *aval giso hu bino ve'chasno*. Rabbi Yosi then comes and adds a further exclusion: *giso levado* — in addition to the stepson, the brother-in-law also does not extend — *ve'chol shekein chorgo*, and certainly the stepson does not, since the brother-in-law is if anything considered closer to the person than the stepson. Rabbi Yosi thus removes *both* from the extending list, leaving eight, which then become twenty-four through their children and stepsons. Our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda; the *braisa* of Rabbi Chiyya reflects the position of Rabbi Yosi. The question raised about whether our *mishnah* is framed within the broader structure of *mishnat Rabbi Akiva* is apt: the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi takes place within that framework, which has already dropped the earlier limitation of *rauy leyirsho* — that only those in the line of inheritance are disqualified.

### Psak: Follow Rabbi Yosi

The *Gemara* concludes this section: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel halacha keRabbi Yosi* — the ruling follows Rabbi Yosi. The practical consequence is significant: not only does the stepson fail to extend down a generation, but the brother-in-law as well. The nephew through marriage, which under Rabbi Yehuda's position would have been *pasul*, is now *kasher*.

## Document & Inheritance

### Two Brother-in-law Witne…

A case now arrives to ground this dispute in practice. A gift document — a *shtar* — was produced in which two brothers-in-law had signed as witnesses. The *kinyan* that effected the transfer of property was the *shtar* itself, so if the witnesses on the document were *pesulim*, the transfer never occurred. It was not merely a question of evidentiary proof; the validity of the *shtar* was the mechanism of acquisition itself.

### Shmuel's Ambiguity

This case immediately reopens the question of which Rabbi Yosi Shmuel intended when he ruled *halacha keRabbi Yosi*. The statement could refer to Rabbi Yosi of the *braisa* — the one who holds that brothers-in-law are *pasul* but their sons are not. Or it could refer to Rabbi Yosi of the *mishnah* itself, who there observes that the earlier teaching (*mishnah rishonah*) required *rauy leyirsho* — that the disqualified relative had to be in the line of inheritance. On that reading, Rabbi Yosi of the *mishnah* may actually be ruling in accordance with the pre-Rabbi Akiva tradition: only those who stand to inherit from one another are *pesulim*. Brothers-in-law cannot inherit from each other — the wife's property passes to the husband, not back through the wife's family — and they would therefore be entirely *kasher*.

This is the more radical reading: if Shmuel ruled like *that* Rabbi Yosi, then not only do we not extend the brother-in-law's disqualification to his sons, we do not disqualify brothers-in-law at all.

### Rav Yosef's Approach

Rav Yosef initially understood Shmuel's ruling in this more expansive sense — that brothers-in-law themselves are *kasher* — and on that basis *savar Rav Yosef le'achshura*, he inclined to validate the *shtar*. Abbaye challenged him: *mimai deRabbi Yossi dematnitin demachshar begisei* — on what basis do you assume the ruling follows Rabbi Yosi of the *mishnah* who permits brothers-in-law? Perhaps it follows Rabbi Yosi of the *braisa*, who holds that brothers-in-law are *pasul* and the *shtar* is therefore invalid.

To support the broader reading, Shmuel himself had given an illustration of *giso* — an example of who qualifies as a brother-in-law under the rule: *k'gon ana u-Pinchas d'havinan achi v'gisei* — like me and Pinchas, who are brothers and also brothers-in-law, having married two sisters. The *Gemara* considers what Shmuel meant. One reading: Shmuel is simply giving an example of brothers-in-law, bracketing the incidental fact that they are also brothers. Another reading: Shmuel is actually teaching that only brothers-in-law who are *also* brothers are *pasul* — *aval gise d'alma shappir dami* — ordinary brothers-in-law alone are perfectly *kasher*. That would place Shmuel squarely with Rabbi Yosi of the *mishnah*. But the *Gemara* then retreats: *ve-dilma*, perhaps Shmuel was merely illustrating what a brother-in-law relationship looks like without intending to exclude ordinary brothers-in-law from the *pesul*. The ambiguity remains unresolved.

Rav Yosef, accepting that the witnesses may indeed be *pasul*, sought an alternate route to validate the transfer: *zil kanyi be'eidei mesira* — go acquire the property through the witnesses who observed the delivery of the *shtar*. According to Rabbi Elazar's ruling that *eidei mesira karthi* — the witnesses who observe the handover of a document are the operative witnesses, not those signed within it — the delivery witnesses could in principle replace the invalid signatories.

### Abbaye's Challenge

Abbaye rejected this approach: *v'ha amar Rabbi Abba modeh Reb Elazar bi'mzuyaph mitocho she'hu pasul* — Rabbi Abba taught that even Rabbi Elazar concedes that if the *shtar* is *mezuyaph mitocho*, intrinsically defective from within, external witnesses cannot salvage it. The principle is broader than outright forgery: any internal invalidity — including *pasul* witnesses signed on the document — renders the *shtar* fundamentally compromised. Witnesses standing outside the document cannot erase the defect embedded within it.

### Final: Rabbi Akiva Model

With both avenues closed, Rav Yosef told the claimant plainly: *lo shavki li d'ezbin lach* — they will not let me award you the field. The case was lost.

The *Gemara* never fully resolves the ambiguity about which Rabbi Yosi Shmuel intended. What emerges as the operative *psak*, however, is that we follow Rabbi Akiva's *mishnah* in its broad scope — we do *not* rule like Rabbi Yosi of the *mishnah* in the limiting sense that would require *rauy leyirsho*. Disqualification is not confined to those in the line of inheritance. It extends through cousins, through maternal relatives, and through the wife's family — with the only refinements being those introduced by Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi in the *braisa*, concerning the stepson and the brother-in-law.

## Dead Wife Case

### Yehuda: Kids = Still Pas…

The *mishnah* now addresses a situation of *karov v'nitrachak* — someone who was once a relative and became distant. This arises typically through a marriage-based relationship that is severed by death or divorce. If a man's daughter was married to someone, that son-in-law is a *karov* through marriage; if the daughter dies, the connection dissolves and the ex-son-in-law is *kasher*.

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this: if there are surviving children, the relationship is not fully dissolved. The man is no longer simply an ex-son-in-law — he is the father of this man's grandchildren. That ongoing connection, according to Rabbi Yehuda, is sufficient to maintain the *pesul*. Without surviving children, the *pesul* lapses entirely.

The *Gemara* records a three-link tradition: *amar Rabbi Tanchum amar Rav Tavla amar Rav Bruna amar Rav — halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*. We rule like Rabbi Yehuda. But this is immediately contested: *Rava amar Rav Nachman — ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*, and *Rabba bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan — ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* as well. The weight of authority opposes Rabbi Yehuda.

Some transmit the teaching of Rabba bar bar Chana in connection with a related *derasha*. Rabbi Yose HaGlili expounded the verse *u'vas el ha'Kohanim ha'Levi'im ve'el ha'Shofet asher yi'hyeh b'yamim ha'hem* — you shall come to the judge who will be in those days — and asked: would it enter your mind that one goes to a judge who is *not* in those times? The point rather is: *zeh she'hayah karov v'nitrachak* — the verse addresses the one who was once a relative and became distant. *B'yamim ha'hem* — if at the time of judgment he is no longer a *karov*, he is eligible to judge, even though he was previously disqualified. On this formulation Rabba bar bar Chana reports: *amar Rabbi Yochanan halacha k'Rabbi Yose HaGlili* — we rule that *karov v'nitrachak* is *kasher*, without the qualification that Rabbi Yehuda introduces for surviving children.

### Mar Ukva's Case

A case is then brought involving Mar Ukva. *B'nei chamu'eh d'Mar Ukva* — the sons of Mar Ukva's mother-in-law, who are Mar Ukva's brothers-in-law, came before him for a *din*. These brothers-in-law had been *krovim* to him through his wife and had become *nitrachku* — distanced — when his wife passed away. The question was whether they remained *pesulim* to appear before him. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, if there are surviving children, the *pesul* persists — the brothers-in-law are now his children's uncles, even if they are no longer related to him directly. Under the majority position, the *pesul* has lapsed entirely.

*Atu l'kamei l'dina* — they came before him for judgment. Mar Ukva declared: *psilna l'chu l'dina* — I am *pasul* to judge your case.

### Real Reason: Troublemake…

The brothers-in-law protested: *mai daitach k'Rabbi Yehuda* — do you really rule like Rabbi Yehuda, finding yourself disqualified merely because you are still their children's uncle? *Anan maitina igarta mi'Maarava* — we will obtain a letter from the west, from Eretz Yisrael, attesting that *ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*, and you should be fully eligible to judge us.

Mar Ukva responded with force: *atu b'kafta d'dikula idbakna b'chu* — do you think I am bound to you like wax? The point was not kinship at all. *Lo kamina psilna l'chu l'dina — ela mishum d'la tzyyitu l'dina* — I declared myself *pasul* not because of any formal disqualification arising from relationship, but because you are troublemakers who refuse to accept the rulings of the court. He was, in effect, recusing himself — and the brothers-in-law's immediate maneuvering to counter-argue the *pesul* of kinship only confirmed his assessment. The episode concludes without a *psak* on the merits.

## Ohev & Sonei

### Ohev Narrowly Defined

Having completed the discussion of *krovim*, the *mishnah* addresses the categories of *ohev* — a close friend — and *sonei* — an enemy — as additional grounds for disqualification. The *mishnah* defines *ohev* with striking specificity: *echad ha'shoshvinim* — one of the groomsmen, a member of the wedding party. This already narrows the category considerably.

The *Gemara* further qualifies how long this status persists: *amar Rabbi Abba amar Rabbi Yirmiyah amar Rav — kol shivat yemei ha'mishteh* — for the full seven days of the wedding celebration. Beyond that week, the fact of having been a groomsman no longer creates a *pesul*. The *Rabanan*, citing Rava, go further: *afilu mi'yom rishon v'eilach* — even from the day after the wedding, the *ohev* disqualification no longer applies.

This extraordinary narrowness reflects a broader principle: *lo nechshadu Yisrael al kach* — we do not suspect Israel of falsifying testimony on account of friendship or animosity. The formal *pesul* of relatives is a structural rule, not a judgment about individual character. To then expand it to encompass friends and enemies would require much stronger grounds.

### Witnesses vs. Judges

This narrowing, however, applies specifically to witnesses. The *Gemara* develops a crucial distinction: the rules governing *ohev* and *sonei* operate differently when one is serving as a judge rather than a witness. The logic is instructive. A witness is called upon to report facts directly observed — what he saw, heard, or perceived. The concern that friendship or enmity might distort testimony, while real, is already filtered by the structural question of what happened. A judge, by contrast, must actively argue both sides of a case, probe the evidence, construct arguments on behalf of each party, and exercise independent evaluative judgment. In that role, even a subtle pull of loyalty or antipathy can fundamentally compromise the process — not merely at the level of individual bias in reporting, but at the level of the entire deliberative exercise. The requirement of impartiality is therefore correspondingly more demanding for judges.

It follows that *ohev* and *sonei* retain a broader disqualifying effect for *dayanim* than for *edim*. As a witness, the category of *ohev* is limited to the groomsman during the wedding week; as a judge, the standard of freedom from bias is more expansive.

### Sonei Definition

*Sonei* is defined more broadly than *ohev*. The *Gemara* states: *ha-soneh kol she-lo diber* — whoever has gone without speaking to the other party because of a quarrel is in the category of *sonei*. The minimum threshold is three days of non-communication due to enmity. As long as the parties remain estranged and unreconciled, the *pesul* of *sonei* applies.

It is notable that this category, grounded in ongoing conflict, can in practice encompass a far wider range of people than the narrowly defined *ohev*. Anger and enmity, as a matter of human experience, tend to be more durable motivators than affection — they drive people more forcefully and persistently.

### Ohev from Pasuk

The *Gemara* now seeks the biblical source for these disqualifications. The *pasuk* that emerges is from the laws of the *ir miklat*: *v'hu lo oyev lo v'lo mivakesh ra'ato* — and he was not his enemy and was not seeking his harm. The verse speaks of an unintentional killer who fled to a city of refuge. Rereading it through the lens of testimony and judgment, the *Gemara* derives: *lo oyev lo* — if he is not the enemy of the subject, *ye'idenu* — he may testify about him; *v'lo mivakesh ra'ato* — and if he does not seek the man's harm, *yadinenu* — he may judge him.

From *sonei* we thus have an explicit *pasuk*. But *ohev* is not found there. The *Gemara* suggests reading it into the gaps: *v'lo ohev... v'lo tovaso* — and not a lover, and not one seeking his benefit. But the *Gemara* immediately objects: *midi ohev ktiv*? Is the word *ohev* actually written there? It is not. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ella sevara hu* — it is a matter of *sevara*, logical reasoning. *Ohev mai ta'ama*? Why is *sonei pasul*? Because *de'marchaka da'ateih* — his thinking is distorted against the subject. *Ohev nami mikrava da'ateih* — and one who loves is equally distorted in the subject's favor. The disqualification of *ohev* flows from the same principle, derived by analogy.

### Judges Need Impartiality

With both categories established, the *Gemara* accounts for the position of the *Rabanan* who held that *ohev* and *sonei* do not disqualify witnesses at all — relying on the principle *lo nechshadu Yisrael al kach*. If witnesses are always *kasher* as *ohev* or *sonei*, what becomes of the *pasuk*? *Chad ledayan* — one application is to the judge. The *Rabanan* use the verse exclusively to disqualify *dayanim*, where the need for impartiality is more demanding, while leaving witnesses *kasher*. The companion phrase of the *pasuk* then covers *ohev* by the same logic applied to a judge.

*Amar Rabbi Yossi beRabbi Yehuda*: from the same verse — *v'hu lo oyev lo v'lo mivakesh ra'ato* — we derive *mikan lishnei talmidei chachamim she'son'im zeh et zeh she-ein yoshvin badin ke'echad* — two scholars who are enemies of one another may not sit together as a panel of judges. The prohibition is not only about judging someone one hates, but about sitting *alongside* someone one hates.

### Enemies Can't Sit Togeth…

This last point requires a distinction. Two judges who are close friends may serve together on a *beit din* — friendship does not impair the deliberative dynamic between them; they can disagree vigorously and reconcile warmly, as the model of *chavruta* study illustrates. Two judges who are enemies, however, cannot function together. Each will reflexively oppose the other's reasoning, resist listening, and subvert the open atmosphere the court requires. The *pesul* of enemies sitting together is therefore real and distinct from the general disqualification of *sonei* when judging the person one hates. Enemies cannot sit together as a panel. Regarding friends on a shared panel, no analogous concern applies.

## Witness Examination

### Frightening Witnesses

The *mishnah* turns to the procedure for examining witnesses in civil cases: *keitzad bodkin et ha-edim* — how does one examine the witnesses? The procedure begins with *hachnisan lacheder* — bringing the witnesses into a room — and *umaimin aleihem* — frightening them, impressing upon them the gravity of false testimony. By *dinei nefashot* we know the specific content of this admonition; the *mishnah* here does not specify it for civil cases, as we shall see shortly. The bystanders are then cleared from the chamber.

### Individual Examination

The *mishnah* records: *umshayirin et hagadol shebahen* — the senior witness is retained while the others are removed. He is questioned individually: *emor heich atah yodea shezeh chayav lazeh* — tell us how you know that this person owes something to the other. The witnesses are examined one by one, in isolation from one another.

### Hearsay Rejection

What types of answers will not suffice? If the witness says *hu amar li she-ani chayav lo* — the debtor himself told me in passing that he owed the money — *lo amar klum*, that amounts to nothing. Similarly, if he reports that a third party told him about the debt, that is pure hearsay and equally inadmissible. Even a first-person statement heard in casual conversation does not qualify, because such statements can be made without real seriousness — perhaps the speaker wished not to appear overly wealthy, or was simply making conversation.

### Admission Standard

The only admission that carries legal weight is: *befanenu hodeh lo she'hu chayav lo matayim zuz* — I was present when the debtor directly admitted to the claimant himself, in my presence, that he owed him the money. The admission must be made face-to-face between the parties, with the witness actually observing the moment of acknowledgment.

### Verdict Process

Once both witnesses have been examined separately, *ve'achar kach machnisin et hasheni u'vodkin oto* — the second witness is brought in and examined in the same fashion. *Im nimtzeu divreihem mechuvanim* — if their accounts align, *nosim ve'nosim ba'davar* — the judges deliberate and discuss the matter.

### Unanimity or Add Judges

The *mishnah* then addresses the voting: two saying *zakai* and one saying *chayav* yields *zakai*; two saying *chayav* and one saying *zakai* yields *chayav*. If two judges have reached a clear majority but the third says *eini yodea* — I do not know — that judge is effectively removed from the panel; one cannot maintain a functioning *beit din* of three with an abstaining member. *Yosifu ha'dayanim* — additional judges must be added, in pairs, until a proper majority is reached.

### Announcing Verdict

Once a conclusion is reached, the litigants are brought back in. The deliberations were conducted outside their presence. The most senior judge then speaks: *ish ploni atah zakai, ish ploni atah chayav* — so-and-so, you are exempt; so-and-so, you are liable. The court speaks in a single, unified voice through its most senior member.

### Secrecy of Votes

The *mishnah* then asks: *u'minayin she'kesheyeitzei lo yomar* — how do we know that after the case is concluded a judge may not approach the losing litigant and say privately: I voted in your favor, but my colleagues outvoted me, *aval mah eseh she'chaverai ravu alai* — what could I do, they were the majority? The source: *lo telech rachil beamecha* — do not go as a talebearer among your people — and the further verse: *holech rachil megaleh sod* — the talebearer reveals secrets.

The court must speak with a unified voice. There is a meaningful difference between preserving a *daat yachid* as an abstract legal position — which the tradition values, as minority opinions may later be relied upon — and revealing one's personal vote in a specific, concluded case. The latter undermines the authority of the court, invites partisanship, opens the door to after-the-fact pressure and influence, and compromises the integrity of the deliberative process. Just as parents speaking with one voice on a disciplinary matter protect the integrity of the household, so the court speaking with one voice protects the integrity of its judgment.

## Deterring False Testimony

### Clouds & Rain Warning

Having established that witnesses in civil cases are brought in and frightened, the *Gemara* asks: *heichi amrinan lehu* — what exactly do we say to them? This is a case of *dinei mamonot*, not *dinei nefashot*; you cannot invoke the weight of a human life. *Amar Rav Yehuda hachi amrinan lehu* — Rav Yehuda says we say to them the verse *nesi'im ve'ruach ve'geshem ayin, ish mithallel be'matat shaker* — clouds and wind and then no rain: a man who boasts of a false gift. False testimony, like clouds that promise rain and deliver none, will result in drought. The implication is that false testimony brings about a world in which there is no rain.

Rava objected: *yachil lemeimar* — the witness could simply say, *sheva shani havei kafna, de'bava umna lo chalif* — there can be a famine for seven years, but it never passes through the craftsman's door. If he is not a farmer, the threat of drought is no personal deterrent.

### Sword & Arrow Warning

*Ela amar Rava amrinan lehu* — Rava therefore proposes a different verse: *mafitz ve'cherev ve'chetz shanun ish onneh ve're'ehu ed shaker* — like a club, a sword, and a sharpened arrow is the man who bears false witness against his fellow. The consequence here is war and bloodshed. *Amar Rav Ashi* — but Rav Ashi noted the same objection applies: *yachil lemeimar sheva shani havei motna, ve'inash belo shnei lo shachiv* — there can be pestilence for seven years, but no one dies before his appointed time. The threat of death and war remains abstract.

### Despised Witnesses

*Ela amar Natan bar Mar Zutra amrinan lehu* — Natan bar Mar Zutra proposes the decisive warning: *sahadei shikrei ogreihu zilei* — false witnesses are despicable even to those who hire them. The verse cited is from the case of Navot's vineyard: the king arranged for *shnayim anashim bnei beliya'al* — two men of *beliya'al* — to testify falsely against Navot. Even Ahab, who commissioned the false testimony, regarded these witnesses as men of corrupt character. The warning is thus personal and immediate: whatever payment you receive, you will be viewed with contempt even by the one who paid you.

## Admissions & Retractions

### Direct Admission Required

The *mishnah* has established that the only valid admission is one in which the debtor acknowledged the debt *befanenu* — in the presence of the witnesses, directly to the claimant. Casual statements made in conversation, even to the claimant himself, do not rise to this standard, because they may not have been intended seriously.

### Yehuda's Strict Rule

Rav Yehuda takes this further, citing a ruling of Rav: *tzarich she'yomar atem edai* — it is not sufficient that witnesses happened to be present when the admission was made; the admitting party must explicitly designate them as his witnesses. Without that designation, the admission, even if made directly to the claimant before observers, does not create binding legal liability. The statement is supported by a parallel *braisa* cited immediately after: *mesaye'a lei le'Rav Yehuda*.

### Jest Retraction

The *Gemara* then introduces the claim of *meshateh ani bach* — I was only joking. *Amar Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*: if a person acknowledged a debt of one hundred *zuz* and the following day, when asked to pay, said *meshateh ani bach*, he is *patur* — exempt. The claim of having been insincere is believed, even in the presence of witnesses who heard the original admission.

A *braisa* confirms this, and goes further: even where the claimant had anticipated the retraction and hidden witnesses behind a wall to capture the admission — *hichmin lo edim achorei ha'geder* — and the debtor acknowledged the debt but then explicitly said he would not admit it formally because he feared being brought to court — even so, if the next day he claimed *meshateh ani bach*, he is *patur*. Remarkably, even the statement that he would not admit in front of witnesses because of the legal consequences does not strip him of the *meshateh* defense.

The *Gemara* adds a procedural point: *im lo ta'an ein to'anin lo* — if the defendant does not himself raise the claim of jest, the court does not raise it on his behalf. Where witnesses have attested to the admission, we treat it as genuine unless the defendant himself says otherwise. The court will not volunteer a defense the party has not made.

### Capital Cases Defense

In capital cases, however, the rule is reversed: *u've'dinei nefashot af al gav d'lo ta'an to'anin lo* — even if the defendant does not raise a potentially exculpatory claim, the court argues it on his behalf. Where a person's life is at stake, the court assumes an affirmative responsibility to explore every possible ground for acquittal.

### Mesit Exception

There is one exception to this rule of capital advocacy: *ve'ein to'anin le'masit* — the court does not argue on behalf of the *mesit*, the one who seduces another to idolatry. The *Gemara* questions: since when were we discussing a *mesit*? This leads to the observation that the text is missing an intermediate passage, *chassurei mechassra ve'hachi katni*, and the full rule is reconstructed: ordinarily, if a defendant does not raise a defense, the court does not raise it for him; in capital cases the court does raise it; but for the *mesit*, even in a capital case, the court does not raise defenses on his behalf.

The reason is explicit in the verse: *lo tachmol ve'lo techaseh alav* — have no compassion and do not cover for him. The *mesit* who seduces to *avodah zarah* presents so grave a threat to the community and to divine authority that the ordinary protections extended to a capital defendant are suspended. The tension with the general obligation to protect individual rights is intentional and acknowledged: the interest of the community in suppressing this particular threat outweighs the standard commitment to maximizing the individual's defenses.

### Mesit & Serpent

The *Gemara* derives this further from the case of the primordial serpent. *Amar Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: we learn that the court does not argue on behalf of the *mesit* from the serpent of Eden. *Amar Rabbi Simlai*: the serpent had many arguments it could have made in its own defense after God confronted it — *harbeh ta'anot hayah lo la'nachash lit'on* — and it did not make them. More pointedly, God Himself did not argue the serpent's case. Why? *Lefi she-lo ta'an hu lo ta'an lo* — because the serpent did not argue for itself, God did not argue on its behalf. The serpent was a *mesit*, seducing Adam and Chava to the primordial act of defying divine authority, and so the rule holds: *lo ta'aninan le'masit*.

What argument could the serpent have made? *Divrei ha'Rav ve'divrei ha'talmid, divrei mi shom'in* — if the master commands one thing and the student commands another, whose word does one obey? The master's, of course. The serpent could have argued: I told them to eat; God said not to eat; they should have listened to God, not to me. That is precisely the defense every *mesit* could offer, and it is why the *Gemara* notes that these arguments existed but were not accepted.

### Adding Takes Away

*Amar Chizkiyah*: from where do we know that *kol ha'mosif goreia* — whoever adds takes away? From the serpent's own case. The verse records Chava telling the serpent that God said not to eat *and not to touch* the fruit of the tree. God had in fact prohibited only eating; Chava added the prohibition of touching. The serpent thereupon pushed her against the tree, demonstrating that she could touch it without consequence — and used that as a basis to argue that eating would similarly be harmless. The addition to God's word became the instrument of the transgression.

The *Gemara* notes a remarkable tension here. The *Anshei Knesset Ha'Gedolah* teach in *Pirkei Avot* to *asu siyag la'Torah* — make a fence around the Torah. And *Avot D'Rabbi Natan* explicitly counts Adam's instruction to Chava not to touch as such a *siyag*. Yet here the same act is taken as the source for *kol ha'mosif goreia*. The tension is left unresolved — the two principles stand alongside one another, and the balance between making protective fences and the danger of adding to God's word is itself a subject for deeper reflection.

Additional derivations of the principle are cited: *Rav Mesharshiya* from the word *amatayim vachatzi* — two and a half cubits — arguing that the additional *alef* reduces the apparent measure; *Rav Ashi* from *ashtei esrei yeriot* — eleven curtains — arguing that the *ayin* reduces the count from twelve to eleven.

### Jest vs. Denial

*Amar Abaye lo shanu ela de'amar mishteh ani bach* — Abbaye clarifies the scope of the *meshateh* rule. The claim of jest is available only as long as the defendant affirmatively says *meshateh ani bach*. If instead the defendant says *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — it never happened — and witnesses then attest that it did happen, he is *huchzak kafran* — established as a proven liar. His credibility is destroyed; he can no longer make the *meshateh* claim having first denied the event entirely. According to Rashi, this *kafran* status would also preclude him from taking an oath.

### Papa: Jest & Forgetfulne…

Rav Papa, however, disagrees. His ruling is cited in the name of Rava: *kol midei de'chadi lo dachir inash* — things that are of no serious consequence people do not remember. The same lack of seriousness that underlies the claim of *meshateh* — the statement was made carelessly, without weight — also explains why the defendant might fail to remember having made it at all. His initial denial that the statement ever occurred is therefore not to be treated as a lie; it is equally consistent with having genuinely forgotten something that was said in passing without intent. Just as he may claim *meshateh*, so too may he claim *lo dachir* — I simply do not recall — and neither amounts to *huchzak kafran*.
