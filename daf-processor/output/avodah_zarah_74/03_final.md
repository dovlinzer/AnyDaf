# Avodah Zarah 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Assur B'Chol Shehu

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין, וְאוֹסְרִין בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּ: יֵין נֶסֶךְ, וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְעוֹרוֹת לְבוּבִין, וְשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, וְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, וְצִיפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע, וּשְׂעַר נָזִיר, וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר, וּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, וְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין וְאוֹסְרִין בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **These** following items are themselves **forbidden, and any amount** of them **renders** other items with which they become mixed **forbidden: Wine** used for **a libation; and** objects of **idol worship; and hides** with a tear opposite the **heart,** indicating the idolatrous practice of sacrificing hearts of live animals. **And** this *halakha* also applies to **an ox that** has been condemned to be **stoned** (see Exodus 21:28), from which it is prohibited to derive benefit even before its sentence is carried out; **and** it applies to **a heifer whose neck is broken** when a person is found killed in an area between two cities and the murderer is unknown (see Deuteronomy 21:1–9), which is likewise forbidden from the time it is taken down to the river to be killed. In these cases, if the animal becomes mixed in a herd of similar animals, all of the animals in the herd are forbidden. **And** this *halakha* also applies to **birds** designated for the purification **of a leper** (Leviticus 14:1–6), **and the** shorn **hair of a nazirite** (Numbers 6:18), **and a firstborn donkey** (Exodus 13:13), **and meat** that was cooked **in milk** (Exodus 23:19), **and the scapegoat** of Yom Kippur (Leviticus 16:7–10), **and** the meat of **a non-sacred** animal **that was slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard.** All of **these** are **forbidden** themselves, **and any amount** of them **renders** a mixture **forbidden.**

The mishna at the top of 74 continues the tractate's discussion of *bitul* and related concepts, presenting a list of things that are forbidden and that forbid even in the tiniest admixture: *eilu assurin ve'ossrin bechol shehu*. The items enumerated are *yayin nesech*, *avodat kochavim* (an idol — Rashi understands this as a worshipped image mixed up indistinguishably with other decorative images), *oros levuvim* (skins cut from the heart of an animal as an offering to *avoda zara*, a form of *tikroves avoda zara* recalling an earlier mishna), *shor haniskal* (an ox stoned for killing a person), *egla arufa* (the calf whose neck is broken in the unresolved murder procedure), *tzipporei metzora* (birds used in the purification of the *metzora*), *se'ar nazir* (the shaved hair of a *nazir* at the conclusion of his period), *peter chamor* (a firstborn donkey whose owner declined to redeem with a sheep and whose neck is therefore broken), *basar bechalav*, *se'ir hamishtaleach* (the scapegoat of Yom Kippur), and *chulin shenishchatu ba'azara* (non-sanctified animals slaughtered in the *azara*). All of these share the common denominator of being *issurei hana'ah* — forbidden in benefit.

The straightforward reading of the mishna would be that things forbidden in benefit carry the rule of *assur bechol shehu*, that even the smallest admixture is forbidden. One important clarification regarding *basar bechalav* is worth noting at the outset. The Gemara in Chullin is explicit that *basar bechalav* as a forbidden mixture only comes into being in the first place if there is enough of one ingredient to impart taste to the other. An infinitesimal drop of milk that cannot be tasted in the meat does not create *basar bechalav* at all. Indeed, the Gemara even entertains the idea that the principle of *ta'am ke'ikar* — that taste is halakhically equivalent to substance — may itself be derived from the requirement that a forbidden *basar bechalav* mixture must involve a tasteable quantity. The rule of *assur bechol shehu* therefore does not speak to what constitutes *basar bechalav*; it speaks rather to whether, once you have a piece of *basar bechalav*, it can become *batel* in a further mixture.

### Why Chametz Missing?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנָּא מַאי קָחָשֵׁיב? אִי דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִנְיָן קָחָשֵׁיב, לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי חֲתִיכוֹת נְבֵילָה! אִי אִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה קָא חָשֵׁיב, לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי חָמֵץ בְּפֶסַח!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: According to **what** criterion **does the *tanna*** who teaches this mishna **reckon** cases? **If he reckons** based on any **item that is counted,** i.e., any item that is significant enough to be considered individually, which therefore cannot be nullified in a mixture even in a very large majority of permitted items, **let him also teach** the case of significant **cuts of an** unslaughtered **animal carcass.** And **if he reckons** based on **items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited, let him also teach** the case of **leavened bread on Passover.**

The difficulty with the mishna's apparent logic is immediate. The Gemara asks: *tana mai kachashiv* — what principle organizes this list? If it is listing things that are *assur bechol shehu* because they are *issurei hana'ah*, then *chametz bePesach* should also appear — it is likewise *assur behana'ah*. And if the list is instead organized around the principle of *davar shebeminyan* — things sold individually rather than by weight or volume, such that each item retains its independent identity in a mixture rather than dissolving into a homogeneous mass — then why not list a *chatichas neveilah*, a presentable cut of non-kosher meat, which also retains its identity? That concept is the parallel principle of *chatichah harauyah lehitchabed*, a portion fit to serve to an honored guest, which confers the same kind of independent significance as *davar shebeminyan*. If the list is organized around *chatichah harauyah lehitchabed*, then a fine steak of *neveilah* should appear. Neither criterion alone explains the list as it stands.

### Two Criteria Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא: הַאי תַּנָּא תַּרְתֵּי אִית לֵיהּ — דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִנְיָן וְאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa** who said: **This *tanna* has two** criteria. He reckons based on any **item that is** both **counted and from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, and some say Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha, resolves the difficulty: *hai tana tartei it lei* — this tanna actually requires the intersection of both criteria simultaneously. Only things that are both *davar shebeminyan* and *issurei hana'ah* appear on the list. That explains why *neveilah* is absent — it may be a *davar shebeminyan* through the principle of *chatichah harauyah lehitchabed*, but it is not *assur behana'ah*. And it explains why *chametz bePesach* is absent — it is *assur behana'ah* but not inherently a *davar shebeminyan*.

### Orlah Already Taught

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי אֱגוֹזֵי פֶּרֶךְ וְרִימּוֹנֵי בָּדָן, דְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמִנְיָן וְאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה הוּא! הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ הָתָם: הָרָאוּי לְעׇרְלָה — עׇרְלָה, הָרָאוּי לְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם — כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let** the *tanna* **teach** the cases of ***perekh* nuts,** a type of nut that has a brittle shell, **and Badan pomegranates,** pomegranates from Badan; **as** these fruits are considered significant, and when they grow during the first three years after the tree was planted [*orla*], they belong to the category of **items that are counted and from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.** The Gemara responds: The Mishna **taught that** case **there,** in tractate *Orla* (3:7), where *perekh* nuts and Badan pomegranates are listed among the forbidden items that cannot be nullified in a mixture, and it is stated with regard to such items: **Those** items **to which** the prohibition of ***orla* applies** render the entire mixture forbidden by imparting to it the status of ***orla*,** while **those to which** the prohibition of **diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard applies** render the mixture forbidden by imparting to it the status of **diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard.** Therefore, it is unnecessary to mention those cases here.

The Gemara immediately presses further. Why not list the special nuts of the region of Perech and the pomegranates of Badan? Those items, familiar from the mishna in *Orlah*, are classic examples of *davar shebeminyan*, sold individually because of their significance. They are also *issurei hana'ah*, since both *orlah* and *kilayim* carry that prohibition. They would thus satisfy both criteria perfectly. The answer is that the mishna in *Orlah* already teaches them — *hatam tani lah*. The closing lines of that mishna state: *harauy le'orlah orlah, harauy lekil'ei hakerem kil'ei hakerem* — wherever the relevant fruits originate, they retain their status as *davar shebeminyan*, and they are also *issurei hana'ah*. Since they are already taught in their proper place, our mishna need not repeat them.

### Chametz B'Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לְעִנְיַן חָמֵץ בְּפֶסַח! מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ דַּאֲמַר לַהּ? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ הָתָם: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹסִיף אַף כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But let** the *tanna* **teach** the case of **loaves of a homeowner,** each of which is unique and significant, **with regard to** the prohibition against deriving benefit from **leavened bread on Passover.** The Gemara explains: **Whom did you hear who says that** such loaves are not nullified in a mixture? This is taught by **Rabbi Akiva,** and **he taught it there** in tractate *Orla* (3:7): **Rabbi Akiva adds** to the list **the loaves of a homeowner.**

The same logic applies to the case of home-baked loaves, *kikarot shel ba'al habayit*, which the same mishna in *Orlah* lists as a paradigmatic *davar shebeminyan*. In the context of *chametz bePesach*, those loaves are also *assur behana'ah* — and both criteria are thus met. The Gemara notes that the position that *chametz bePesach* is at all *assur behana'ah* is actually a matter of dispute: *man shamit d'amar leh Rabbi Akiva* — it is Rabbi Akiva who holds that view. We of course rule accordingly. And the Gemara answers that Rabbi Akiva himself adds the home-baked loaves to the list in the mishna in *Orlah* — *Rabbi Akiva mosif af kikarot shel ba'al habayit*. Since that case is already taught there, there is no need to repeat it in our mishna.

### What Gets Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִנְיָן וְלָאו אִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה, אִי נָמֵי לְמַעוֹטֵי אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה וְלֹא דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** At the end of the mishna here, the *tanna* reiterates the *halakha* stated at its beginning, saying: All of **these** are forbidden themselves, and any amount of them renders a mixture forbidden. The Gemara asks: The purpose of this reiteration is **to exclude what?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to exclude** any **item that is counted but from which** deriving **benefit is not prohibited, or to exclude items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited but** that **are not counted.**

The mishna's phrase *harei elu* — "these are the ones" — comes to exclude things that possess only one of the two criteria. *L'me'utei davar shebeminyan velo issurei hana'ah*, it excludes things that are *davar shebeminyan* but not *issurei hana'ah* — such as a presentable cut of *neveilah*. And *l'me'utei issurei hana'ah velo davar shebeminyan*, it excludes things that are *assur behana'ah* but are not *davar shebeminyan* — such as *chametz bePesach* that is not a fine home-baked loaf.

There is, however, a sharp difficulty here: those very examples the Gemara offers as excluded are in fact also not *batel* in practice. A fine *neveilah* steak is not *batel* because it is *chatichah harauyah lehitchabed*, functionally equivalent to *davar shebeminyan*. And *chametz bePesach* — even the smallest crumbs — is not *batel*, which is precisely why the stringencies of *bedikat chametz* extend to the most minute traces. Rashi appears to suggest, in a strikingly unusual comment, that *chametz bePesach* that is not a *davar shebeminyan* actually would be *batel* — a conclusion that is clearly not the operative *halacha*.

### Actual Rules Differ

Tosafot takes a different approach to resolve this tension, one that cuts sharply against the surface thrust of the Gemara. Tosafot argues that the Gemara here is not at all pronouncing what determines whether something is *batel* or not. Its sole concern is what determines which items are included in the mishna's list. The mishna chose to enumerate only things satisfying both criteria — *davar shebeminyan* and *issurei hana'ah* — but this tells us nothing definitive about the actual laws of *bitul*.

The actual rules are more complex and cannot be read off the mishna's structure. *Davar shebeminyan*, or its parallel *chatichah harauyah lehitchabed*, is always sufficient by itself to prevent *bitul* — regardless of whether the item is *assur behana'ah*. A *neveilah* steak and a *basar bechalav* steak are both not *batel*, the one despite not being *assur behana'ah*, the other because it is. That criterion stands alone and is always decisive.

*Issurei hana'ah*, on the other hand, is not a uniform category with respect to *bitul*. Some *issurei hana'ah* are *batel*, and some are not, and the distinction does not follow mechanically from the category. *Orlah* and *kilayim* — both *issurei hana'ah* — are *batel*. *Chametz bePesach* is *assur behana'ah* and yet is not *batel*. *Yayin nesech* and other *avoda zara*-related prohibitions are not *batel*, though that is partly because the gravity of *avoda zara* adds additional weight beyond the bare category of *issur hana'ah*. The mishna's two-criteria requirement thus reflects an editorial principle — what belongs on this list — not a halakhic formula for what is or is not *batel*. The actual determination of *bitul* for *issurei hana'ah* depends on separate, category-specific discussions and cannot be inferred from the mishna's structure alone.

## Yayin Nesech Falling

### Mishna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יֵין נֶסֶךְ שֶׁנָּפַל לַבּוֹר, כּוּלּוֹ אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: יִמָּכֵר כּוּלּוֹ לְגוֹי, חוּץ מִדְּמֵי יֵין נֶסֶךְ שֶׁבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **wine** used for **a libation that fell into** a wine **cistern,** it is **prohibited** to derive **benefit** from **all of** the wine in the cistern, even if the volume of the wine used for a libation was tiny in comparison to the volume of the wine in the cistern. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: All of the** wine in the cistern **may be sold to a gentile,** and the money paid for it is permitted **except for the value of the wine** used for **a libation** that is included **in it.**

The next mishna addresses the case of *yayin nesech she-nafal l'bor* — *yayin nesech* that falls into a vat of wine. Since *yayin nesech* is *assur bechol shehu*, even a drop falling into a full vat renders the entire contents forbidden in benefit.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel offers a practical remedy: sell the entire vat to a non-Jew, but charge only for the amount of kosher wine, not for the added *yayin nesech*. The elegance of this solution is that the seller receives payment only for the hundred gallons — or whatever the legitimate quantity is — and thereby derives no benefit from the forbidden drop. The *yayin nesech* has been absorbed into the transaction without the seller extracting its value.

### Rav's Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל חָבִית בְּחָבִיּוֹת, אֲבָל לֹא יַיִן בְּיַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** in a case where **a barrel** of wine used for a libation became intermingled **with barrels** of permitted wine, **but not** when the **wine** itself became mixed **with** permitted **wine.**

Rav rules that the *halacha* follows Rabban Gamliel, but with a restriction: *chavit bechaviyot* — this applies when it is a barrel among barrels, *aval lo yayin b'yayin* — but not when it is wine mixed into wine. The logic is clear. When a barrel of *yayin nesech* is lost among a thousand kosher barrels, the seller can plausibly say: I am selling you a thousand barrels; the extra one — whichever it is — I am simply not selling, I am merely handing it over. Each barrel retains its independent identity. But when the wine itself is mixed in a vat, there is no discrete unit that can be conceptually set aside. The forbidden wine has dispersed entirely and redefined the identity of the whole vat. It becomes impossible to say "I am selling only the kosher portion."

### Lenient Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן בְּיַיִן. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן בְּיַיִן. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן בְּיַיִן. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן בְּיַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: Even** when the **wine** itself became mixed **with** permitted **wine,** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. **And likewise, Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** when **wine** became mixed **with wine,** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. **And likewise, Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Even** when **wine** became mixed **with wine,** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. **And likewise, Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: Even** when **wine** became mixed **with wine,** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

Despite Rav's restriction, several Amoraim rule that even wine mixed into wine follows Rabban Gamliel's leniency. Shmuel states this explicitly, and his view is echoed by Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, Rabbi Shmuel bar Barana in the name of Rabbi Chanina, and Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha — all permitting wine in wine.

### Practical Halakha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה לְמַעֲשֶׂה, יֵין נֶסֶךְ יַיִן בְּיַיִן — אָסוּר, חָבִית בְּחָבִית — מוּתָּר, סְתָם יַיִן אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן בְּיַיִן — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says:** The **practical *halakha*** is that with regard to **wine** that was actually used for **a libation, wine** that became mixed **with wine** renders the entire mixture **forbidden,** but if **a barrel** became intermingled **with barrels** of permitted wine, it is **permitted** to sell the barrels in the manner described by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. With regard to **nondescript wine** of gentiles, which is forbidden due to the suspicion that it was used for a libation, **even** if **wine** became mixed **with wine,** it is **permitted** to sell the mixture in the manner described by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

Notwithstanding this array of lenient opinions, Rav Nachman draws the operative practical distinction: for actual *yayin nesech* — wine actively used for *avoda zara* — wine mixed into wine remains forbidden and Rabban Gamliel's ruling does not apply; only barrel among barrels is permitted. For *stam yeinam* — wine that a non-Jew has handled or had access to but that was not actively used for *avoda zara* — even wine mixed into wine is permitted under Rabban Gamliel's approach. In practical terms, the overwhelming majority of cases we encounter are *stam yeinam*, so the leniency is broadly applicable.

### Rashi's Method

When it comes to the barrel-in-barrels case, Rashi notes a technique that appears more prominently in discussions of *avoda zara* than elsewhere: one takes a random barrel and throws it away entirely. This allows one to say — at least constructively — that the disposed barrel was the forbidden one. This is not sufficient to permit drinking from the remaining barrels, but it does provide an additional basis for the sale — allowing the seller to charge only for the remaining barrels and to represent that the *yayin nesech* barrel was not included in the transaction.

## Koshering Wine Press

### Mishna Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גַּת שֶׁל אֶבֶן שֶׁזְּפָתָהּ גּוֹי, מְנַגְּבָהּ וְהִיא טְהוֹרָה. וְשֶׁל עֵץ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: יְנַגֵּב, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יִקְלוֹף אֶת הַזֶּפֶת. וְשֶׁל חֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁקָּלַף אֶת הַזֶּפֶת — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a stone winepress that a gentile lined with pitch** and then poured wine onto the pitch to neutralize its flavor, **one may cleanse it and it is pure,** i.e., wine pressed in it is permitted. **And** if the winepress is fashioned **of wood, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: One may cleanse it, but the Rabbis say: One must peel off the pitch** completely. **And** if the winepress is **of earthenware, even if one peeled off the pitch, this** press **is forbidden.**

The mishna now turns from *bitul* to *hachsharah* — the koshering of wine presses and related vessels. The context is vessels that non-Jews have used in the production of wine, raising concern that the wine has absorbed into the walls of the vessel or into the pitch lining it.

The background is relevant: pitch (*zifta*) was used to line wine presses and barrels, in part to prevent the wine from seeping into the walls of the vessel itself. To prevent the resulting wine from tasting of pitch, wine was often mixed into the pitch before application — a kind of seasoning. The result is that the pitch itself contains wine from a non-Jew, which may then leach back into the next batch of wine pressed there.

The mishna presents a graduated series of cases. A stone wine press that was pitched by a non-Jew: scour it and it is permitted. Wood: Rebbi says the same — scour it; the Chachamim say scouring is insufficient, and the pitch must be peeled away. Pottery: even peeling off the pitch does not help, because the wine penetrates through the pitch into the pottery walls themselves, where it cannot be removed. Whether this last ruling regarding pottery reflects the Chachamim alone or is also conceded by Rebbi is left open by the mishna and will require clarification in the Gemara.

### Rava's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: דַּוְקָא זְפָתָהּ, אֲבָל דָּרַךְ בָּהּ — לֹא. פְּשִׁיטָא, ״זְפָתָהּ״ תְּנַן! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוּא הַדִּין אֲפִילּוּ דָּרַךְ בָּהּ, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״זְפָתָהּ״ — אוֹרְחָא דְּמִלְּתָא קָתָנֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says:** The requirement to cleanse the winepress applies **specifically** if the gentile **lined it with pitch. But** if he only **trod** on his grapes **in it** without lining it with pitch, this is **not** required. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? We learned** in the mishna that cleansing the winepress is necessary if the gentile **lined it with pitch.** The Gemara responds: **Lest you say** that **the same is true** that it requires cleansing **even** if he **trod** on his grapes **in it, and** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** a case where he **lined it with pitch** is because the mishna **is teaching the manner** in **which the matter** typically occurs, therefore Rava **teaches us** that this is not the case.

Rava clarifies that the mishna's requirement of scouring applies *davka zifsa* — specifically where the press was lined with pitch. If a non-Jew merely stomped grapes directly on the stone, without pitch, the concern is only surface contamination and a rinse suffices. Stone does not absorb; whatever wine was there is on the surface.

The Gemara initially questions this: the mishna explicitly mentions pitch, so of course it is discussing a pitched press — Rava's clarification seems unnecessary. The Gemara responds: one might have thought the mishna mentions pitch only because that is the typical situation (*orcha demilta*), but the same rule would apply to an unpitched stone press in which grapes were stomped. Rava therefore informs us that the leniency of mere rinsing applies specifically to the unpitched case; the mishna's scouring requirement is genuinely restricted to pitched stone.

A second, more stringent version (*ika deamri*) of Rava's ruling addresses a three-way distinction. There are three scenarios: stomping on bare stone (rinse is sufficient — the stone does not absorb); pitch with no stomping (scour — the wine is in the pitch but can be removed by scouring); and pitch with stomping on top of it (scouring is not sufficient). In this third scenario, the stomping drives the wine deeply into the pitch and likely into hidden cracks beneath it, such that even Rebbi would agree that the pitch must be entirely removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: דַּוְקָא זְפָתָהּ, אֲבָל דָּרַךְ בָּהּ לָא סַגִּי לַהּ בְּנִיגּוּב. פְּשִׁיטָא, ״זְפָתָהּ״ תְּנַן! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ דָּרַךְ בָּהּ, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״זְפָתָהּ״ אוֹרְחָא דְמִלְּתָא קָתָנֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּוְקָא זְפָתָהּ, אֲבָל דָּרַךְ בָּהּ לָא סַגִּי לַהּ בְּנִיגּוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rava says:** Cleansing the winepress is effective **specifically** if the gentile **lined it with pitch. But** if he **trod** on his grapes **in it** as well, **cleansing** it **is not sufficient** to purify the winepress. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? We learned** in the mishna that cleansing the winepress is sufficient if the gentile **lined it with pitch.** The Gemara responds: **Lest you say** that **the same is true** and **that** cleansing is sufficient **even** if he **trod** on his grapes **in it, and** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** a case where he **lined it with pitch** is because the mishna **is teaching the manner** in **which the matter** typically occurs, therefore Rava **teaches us** that cleansing the winepress is sufficient **specifically** if **he lined it with pitch, but** if **he trod** on his grapes **in it, cleansing** it **is not sufficient.**

### Rav's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי גַּבְרָא דִּמְדַכֵּי לִי מַעְצַרְתַּאי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב: זִיל בַּהֲדֵיהּ וַחֲזִי דְּלָא מְצַוְּחַתְּ עֲלַי בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. אֲזַל חַזְיַיהּ דַּהֲוָה שִׁיעָא טְפֵי, אֲמַר: הָא וַדַּאי בְּנִיגּוּב סַגִּי לַהּ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא אָזֵיל וְאָתֵי, חֲזָא פִּילָא מִתּוּתֵיהּ וַחֲזָא דַּהֲוָה מְלֵא חַמְרָא, אֲמַר: הָא לָא סַגִּי לַהּ בְּנִיגּוּב אֶלָּא בְּקִילּוּף, וְהַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לִי חַבִּיבִי: חֲזִי דְּלָא מְצַוְּחַתְּ עֲלַי בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **similar to** an incident involving **a certain man who came before Rabbi Ḥiyya** and **said to him: Give me a man who will purify my winepress** that I purchased from a gentile. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to Rav: Go with him and see** to it **that you** conduct yourself in such a manner that will **not cause** people **to complain against me** in **the study hall.** Rav **went** with him and **saw that** the winepress **was very smooth** with pitch. Rav **said: Cleansing will certainly be sufficient for this,** because it does not absorb the wine. **While** Rav **was going and coming, he saw a crack underneath** his feet **and saw that it was full of wine.** He then **said: Cleansing is not sufficient for this; rather,** it requires **peeling. And this is what my uncle [*ḥavivi*]** meant when he **said to me: See** to it **that you do not cause** people **to complain against me** in **the study hall.**

This three-way distinction is dramatized by a narrative involving Rav. A man approached Rabbi Chiya requesting that a student be assigned to assist him in koshering his wine press. Rabbi Chiya dispatched Rav, his nephew, with the instruction: *chazi dela mitzavchat alai bevei midrasha* — be careful that this man does not later come to the *beis medrash* and complain that I caused his wine to be forbidden.

Rav arrived and inspected the wine press. He found that the pitch surface was extraordinarily smooth, and concluded that scouring would suffice — the smoothness of the pitch indicated that even if grapes had been stomped there, the wine had not penetrated into cracks, and the situation was equivalent to the middle scenario of pitch without deep absorption.

But as Rav walked back and forth examining the floor of the press, he noticed a crack concealed beneath the pitch surface, and within it, wine. This transformed the case: whatever the surface had looked like, cracks existed and wine had penetrated deeply. The situation was now unmistakably the third scenario — pitch plus stomping — and Rav ruled that the pitch had to be entirely peeled away. He reflected: *vahainu deamar lei chavivi chazi dela mitzavcha alai bevei midrasha* — this is exactly what my uncle had warned me about. The instruction was not to be cavalier, but to inspect carefully enough that the koshering would truly hold up to scrutiny. The concern was not to burden the man unnecessarily, but to ensure that what was declared kosher was indeed kosher.

### Baraita Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַגַּת וְהַמַּחַץ וְהַמַּשְׁפֵּךְ שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, רַבִּי מַתִּיר בְּנִיגּוּב, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי בְּקַנְקַנִּים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם שֶׁהֵן אֲסוּרִין. וּמָה הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? זֶה מַכְנִיסוֹ בְּקִיּוּם, וְזֶה אֵין מַכְנִיסוֹ בְּקִיּוּם. וְשֶׁל עֵץ וְשֶׁל אֶבֶן — יְנַגֵּב, וְאִם הָיוּ מְזוּפָּפִין — אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **the winepress and** its utensils, **the ladle and the funnel, which belong to gentiles, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **permits** them **by cleansing, and the Rabbis deem** them **forbidden. And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes with regard to** earthenware **jugs belonging to gentiles that they are forbidden. And what is the difference between this** case **and that** case? **This** jug **contains** the wine **for storage** purposes, **and that** case, involving a ladle and a funnel, involves utensils that **do not contain it for storage** purposes but only temporarily. **And** if the winepress or its utensils are fashioned **of wood or stone,** one **must cleanse** them, **but if they were lined with pitch, they are forbidden,** and cleansing is not sufficient to render them permitted.

A *baraita* adds detail regarding other wine-press equipment. The *gat* (wine press), the *machat* (according to Rashi, the bucket used to draw wine from the press), and the *mishpach* (funnel) — all made of pottery according to Rashi's reading — when belonging to non-Jews: Rebbi permits scouring, the Chachamim forbid it. Rebbi himself concedes, however, that pottery *kankanim* — actual storage jars — belonging to non-Jews are forbidden even with scouring.

### Storage vs. Temporary

What is the distinction? *Zeh machniso lekiyum vezeh ein machniso lekiyum* — one type is made for long-term storage, the other is not. Pottery storage jars are designed to hold wine for extended periods — sometimes years. Even though all of this involves cold liquid and cold absorption, the prolonged contact causes genuine absorption into the pottery walls, and simple scouring cannot draw it out. Wine-press equipment, by contrast, is used intensively only during the pressing season and sits empty for long stretches of time; the sustained contact that causes deep absorption does not occur in the same way. Therefore Rebbi holds that scouring is adequate for the press equipment. The Chachamim disagree and require more.

When long-term pottery storage is involved and scouring is inadequate, Rashi notes that a remedy discussed earlier in the tractate (on 33a) is available: filling the vessel with water and emptying it over three consecutive days, changing the water each time. That solution addresses even pottery in which wine has been absorbed over time. It is worth noting, as an aside, that this very technique underlies the practice — familiar from the laws of Pesach — of soaking glass vessels in water for three days.

### Reconciling Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: גַּת שֶׁל אֶבֶן שֶׁזְּפָתָהּ גּוֹי — מְנַגְּבָהּ וְהִיא טְהוֹרָה! מַתְנִיתִין דְּלֹא דָּרַךְ בָּהּ, בָּרַיְיתָא דְּדָרַךְ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to the last clause of the *baraita*: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that in the case of **a stone winepress that a gentile lined with pitch, one may cleanse it and it is pure?** The Gemara answers: **The mishna** is referring to a case **where** the gentile **did not tread** on his grapes **in it,** whereas **the *baraita*** is referring to a case **where he trod** on his grapes **in it.**

A difficulty emerges: the baraita says that Rebbi permits scouring for pottery wine-press equipment without pitch. But the mishna states that if the wine press is pottery (*shel cheres*), even peeling off the pitch does not help. If pottery is that problematic, how can Rebbi permit mere scouring when it is just pottery without pitch?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַגַּת וְהַמַּחַץ וְהַמַּשְׁפֵּךְ שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, רַבִּי מַתִּיר בְּנִיגּוּב, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: שֶׁל חֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁקָּלַף אֶת הַזֶּפֶת — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה! אָמַר רָבָא: סֵיפָא דְּמַתְנִיתִין אֲתָאן לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above: With regard to **the winepress and the ladle and the funnel that belong to gentiles, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **permits** them **by cleansing, and the Rabbis deem** them **forbidden.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that if the winepress is **of earthenware, even if one peeled off the pitch this** press **is forbidden? Rava said:** In **the last clause of the mishna we arrive at** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Rava resolves this: *seifa dematnitin atya keRabbanan* — the closing clause of the mishna, which rules so stringently about pottery, follows the Chachamim, not Rebbi. According to Rebbi, pottery wine-press equipment without pitch can indeed be koshered by scouring, since it is not made for long-term storage. The mishna's stringent pottery ruling reflects only the Chachamim's position.

### Boiling Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: נַעֲוָה אַרְתַּחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught:** If one has **a tank** of wine used by gentiles, he must **scald it** in order to render it permitted for use.

Rava issues a further practical ruling: the wine press (*na'aveh*, the Aramaic term) can be koshered by *artach* — scalding with boiling hot water. This introduces an additional method alongside the progression of solutions already surveyed: surface rinsing (for unpitched stone), scouring with water and ashes (for pitched stone), peeling off the pitch (where scouring cannot reach), and the three-day water soaking (for long-term pottery absorption). Boiling water, Rava indicates, provides a further effective method — and given that cold absorption is what is at issue throughout, pouring boiling hot water offers a natural counterpoint.

### Tosafot Discussion

Tosafot raises a classic question occasioned by Rava's ruling. When Rava says to scald the wine press with boiling hot water, there is no practical way to build a fire beneath the press itself. The only feasible method is to heat water separately in a vessel over a fire and then pour it into the press. That is, by definition, *irui kli rishon* — pouring from a vessel that was itself on the fire. The question Tosafot then pursues is whether *irui kli rishon* is halakhically equivalent to a *kli rishon* (a vessel still on the fire) for purposes of koshering.

Tosafot cautions against deriving a broad ruling from Rava's case. The absorption that occurred in the wine press was cold absorption — wine sitting in pitch over time, not hot absorption from direct cooking. Because the original absorption was cold, pouring boiling hot water (*irui kli rishon*) is sufficient to draw it out. But this tells us nothing about whether *irui kli rishon* would suffice to kasher a vessel in which something was absorbed through direct heat on a fire. That question remains separate. What Rava's ruling does inform is cases of indirect absorption — as, for example, a countertop onto which hot food has spilled from a pot. Since such absorption is itself indirect and not from direct fire contact, pouring boiling hot water (*irui kli rishon*) onto it would similarly suffice. A sink would be another practical example of this category.

### Rava Shipping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא כִּי הֲוָה מְשַׁדַּר גּוּלְפֵי לְהַרְפַּנְיָא, סָחֵיף לְהוּ אַפּוּמַּיְיהוּ וְחָתֵים לְהוּ אַבִּירְצַיְיהוּ. קָסָבַר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁמַּכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם, אֲפִילּוּ לְפִי שָׁעָה — גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rava would dispatch** empty **kegs to Harpanya, he would turn them over,** placing them in their sacks **on their openings, and seal** the sacks **on their brims,** so that the gentile carriers would not be able to use them for wine. **He maintained** that with regard to **anything that** is used to **contain** wine **for storage, even** if the wine may be stored in it only **temporarily, the Sages decreed** that **it** is forbidden for use as though it had contained wine.

A further incident involving Rava adds an important nuance. When Rava would send empty pottery vessels to the city of Harpania, he would turn them upside down in the sack — mouths facing the bottom — and seal the opening of the sack on their bases, creating a *chotam betoch chotam*, a double seal. He was not sending wine, only the vessels themselves. Why the elaborate precaution?

*Kasavar kol davar shemachniso lekiyum afilu lefi sha'ah gazru beh rabanan* — even though the *machniso lekiyum* principle had previously been used as a basis for leniency (since wine-press equipment not made for long-term storage is easier to kasher), the same principle operates in a stringent direction as well. Vessels that are *designed* for long-term storage — even if used only momentarily — must be treated as if wine had been stored in them long-term. Rava therefore did not want the non-Jew transporting the vessels to help himself to one along the way, pour wine into it briefly, rinse it, and replace it. Such brief use of a long-term storage vessel would require full koshering. The *chotam betoch chotam* ensured the vessels arrived unopened.

This distinguishes the two sides of the *machniso lekiyum* analysis clearly: a vessel designed for temporary use that happened to store wine for a long time is treated leniently; a vessel designed for long-term storage that is used briefly for wine must be treated stringently — as though the long-term storage had occurred.

### Scouring Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה מְנַגְּבָן? רַב אָמַר: בְּמַיִם, רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר: בְּאֵפֶר. רַב אָמַר בְּמַיִם — בְּמַיִם וְלֹא בְּאֵפֶר? רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר בְּאֵפֶר — בְּאֵפֶר וְלֹא בְּמַיִם? אֶלָּא
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks with regard to the cleansing mentioned in the mishna: **How does one cleanse** a winepress, or utensils used by a gentile for wine? **Rav says:** One cleans it **with water. Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** One cleans it **with ashes.** The Gemara asks: When **Rav says:** One cleans it **with water,** does he mean only **with water and not with ashes** as well? Furthermore, when **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** One cleans it **with ashes,** does he mean only **with ashes, and not with water** as well? **Rather,** their statements must be understood as follows:

Finally, the Gemara addresses the practical question of how scouring is performed. Rav says with water; Rabbah bar bar Chana says with ashes. Neither can mean water alone (that is merely rinsing) or ashes alone (ashes without water cannot clean anything). The Gemara therefore concludes that both authorities agree on water combined with ashes — ashes function as a kind of soap — and the only question between them is which ingredient to emphasize. Rashi explains that the order of application depends on the current condition of the press: if the wine press is still moist with wine, begin with ashes, which absorb the surface moisture; then add water. If the press is dry, begin with water, which gives the ashes a medium to dissolve into and work with. Each Amora mentioned the element that begins the process in the more common scenario relevant to his teaching, but both require both water and ashes, and neither disagrees with the other on the substance.
