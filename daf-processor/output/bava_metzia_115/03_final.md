# Bava Metzia 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Entering Home, Guarantor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא תָבֹא אֶל בֵּיתוֹ לַעֲבֹט עֲבֹטוֹ״. לְבֵיתוֹ אִי אַתָּה נִכְנָס, אֲבָל אַתָּה נִכְנָס לְבֵיתוֹ שֶׁל עָרֵב. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לְקַח בִּגְדוֹ כִּי עָרַב זָר וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states: "When you lend your neighbor any manner of loan, **you shall not go into his house to take his collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:10). This verse indicates that **you may not enter his house, but you** may **enter the house of a guarantor** to take collateral from him. **And similarly it states: "Take his garment that is a surety for a stranger"** (Proverbs 20:16).

The daf opens by recalling the discussion from the previous day. The *Gemara* concluded with the important point about why a creditor bothers taking collateral from a poor person at all, given that certain items — clothing and things that directly serve the borrower's bodily needs — must be returned on a daily basis. *Tosafos* explained that the creditor benefits in two ways: the initial taking of collateral prevents *shmita* from canceling the debt and preserves the right to collect even if the borrower dies, while the ongoing back-and-forth creates a constant prod for the borrower to pay up and makes it difficult for him to deny the debt.

We now pick up on 115a with the *baraisa*: *lo tavo el beito la'avot avoto* — do not enter into the borrower's home to take collateral. This prohibition applies to any borrower, not only a poor one. The underlying question here is how broadly to read this *halacha*. Is the prohibition against entry into the borrower's home a specific *din* rooted in the power dynamics of the creditor-borrower relationship, or does it reflect a broader principle — something like a general right to privacy, a protection against unlawful entry? This question is directly relevant to the cases that follow.

### Guarantor's Home

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אַתָּה נִכְנָס לְבֵיתוֹ שֶׁל עָרֵב
>
> **Translation:** but you may enter the house of a guarantor

The *baraisa* continues: *aval atta nichnas levaito shel areiv* — but you may enter into the home of a guarantor. This is a genuine *chiddush*. The *areiv*, the co-signer, stands in a different position than the borrower. The creditor does not lord it over the guarantor in the same way; there is no direct relationship of indebtedness between them in the ordinary sense. And yet the *halacha* is that if the borrower has not paid up and the creditor now has a rightful claim against the guarantor, he is permitted to enter the guarantor's home to collect collateral or payment.

### Proverbs Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לְקַח בִּגְדוֹ כִּי עָרַב זָר״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״בְּנִי אִם עָרַבְתָּ לְרֵעֶךָ תָּקַעְתָּ לַזָּר כַּפֶּיךָ. נוֹקַשְׁתָּ בְאִמְרֵי פִיךָ נִלְכַּדְתָּ בְּאִמְרֵי פִיךָ. עֲשֵׂה זֹאת אֵפוֹא בְּנִי וְהִנָּצֵל כִּי בָאתָ בְכַף רֵעֶךָ לֵךְ הִתְרַפֵּס וּרְהַב רֵעֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly it states: "Take his garment that is a surety for a stranger."** And it further **states** with regard to the same issue: **"My son, if you are a guarantor for your neighbor, if you have struck your hands for a stranger, you are ensnared by the words of your mouth; you are caught by the words of your mouth. Do this, now, my son, and deliver yourself, when you have come into the hand of your friend; go humble yourself and strengthen your friend"** (Proverbs 6:1–3).

The *baraisa* cites two verses from *Mishlei* in support. The first is: *lakach bigdo ki arav zar* — he took his cloak because he became a guarantor for a stranger. The *mussar* dimension of the verse is clear — do not foolishly stand surety for someone you do not know — but simultaneously the verse reflects a legal reality in which the creditor entered and took the *areiv*'s cloak, which the *Gemara* takes to include entry into the home.

The second verse: *im aravta l'rei'acha takata l'zar kapecha... asei zot eifo bni v'hinatzel* — if you became a guarantor for your friend, if you shook hands with a stranger, you have been trapped by the words of your mouth — do this now, my son, and save yourself. *Rashi* reads the verse as addressing two distinct situations. *Ki vata b'chaf rei'acha* — when you have come into the hand of your neighbor — refers to the case where the creditor now has a monetary claim against you as guarantor. *Leich hitrappeis v'rahav rei'acha* — *Rashi* reads *hitrappeis* as *hateir poseis yad*, open your hand to him; that is, pay him off. Alternatively, in a different reading, *harbei alav rei'im* — bring many friends to intercede on your behalf.

### Wages & Fees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְצַד שֵׁנִי – לְבֵיתוֹ אִי אַתָּה נִכְנָס, אֲבָל אַתָּה נִכְנָס לִשְׂכַר כַּתָּף, לִשְׂכַר חַמָּר, לִשְׂכַר פּוּנְדָּק, לִשְׂכַר דְּיוֹקְנָאוֹת. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ זְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מַשַּׁאת מְאוּמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse "When you lend your neighbor any manner of loan, you shall not go into his house to take his collateral" can be interpreted **in a different direction,** i.e., in another manner: **You may not enter his house** to take collateral for the loan, **but** if he owes wages, **you may enter** and take collateral **for a porter's wages, for a donkey driver's wages, for an innkeeper's payment,** or **for the wages for** one who made **drawings [*diyokanaot*]** for him. One **might** have thought that this applies **even** if the one owed the money **establishes it as a loan for** the one who owes the money after he was already liable for such payment. Therefore, **the verse states: "Any manner** of loan" (Deuteronomy 24:10), which demonstrates that once the debt has been converted into a loan, it is like any other loan, and therefore one may not take collateral against the debtor's will.

The *baraisa* presents a second exception: *aval atta nichnas lisechar kateif* — you may enter the home of someone who owes you portage fees, or more broadly, anyone who owes you money because you performed a service for them. The examples cited are striking in their specificity: *lisechar chamor*, for unpaid fees from donkey driving; *lisechar pundak*, for an unpaid hotel bill; *lisechar diyukna'os*, for artwork or portraiture you created for someone. The power dynamic here is essentially the reverse of the standard creditor-borrower relationship. In the typical *malveh-loveh* scenario, the creditor holds power and the borrower is in a disadvantaged position. But when it comes to unpaid wages or service fees, it is usually the worker who is disadvantaged and the homeowner who is withholding payment. In such a case, the worker may enter the debtor's home to collect.

### Wages to Loan

The *baraisa* then raises a complication: *yachol afilu zakfan alav bemilveh* — what if the unpaid wages were converted into a loan? That is, the debtor says to the worker: "I know I owe you the money — let's treat it as a loan." Nothing formally changes in terms of the underlying obligation, but now the debt is reframed. The question of motivation is worth noting: the worker might agree in order to obtain documentation through witnesses; the debtor might gain additional time to pay. But what are the legal consequences? The *baraisa* says: *talmud lomar massa u-ma* — any kind of loan, even one that originated as unpaid wages, is subject to the standard *din malveh*. If it has been converted into a loan, the protections of the borrower-creditor relationship kick in, including the prohibition against entering the home. This is directly analogous to the question in *shmita*: whether unpaid store credit is subject to *shemitat kesafim* depends on whether it has been converted into a formal loan obligation.

### Rambam Force Entry

The *Rambam*'s formulation of these laws is striking. Regarding the guarantor, he writes: *ha-areiv mutar lemashkon bizroa u-lehikanes levaito u-litol ha-mashkon* — it is permitted to seize collateral from the guarantor by force and to enter his home and take the collateral. Similarly, one who is owed wages for labor, animals, utensils, or the use of a home: *harei zeh mutar lemashkeno shelo al pi beit din, ve-nichnas levaito ve-notel ha-mashkon b'sacharo* — he may take collateral without court authorization, enter the home, and remove the collateral as payment. The language of *bizroa* — by force — is notable.

### Power Dynamics

This analysis confirms the point made earlier. The prohibition of *lo tavo el beito la'avot avoto* is not a broad protection of personal privacy or an across-the-board right against entry. It is specifically rooted in the dynamics of the *malveh-loveh* relationship. Where that relationship does not apply — where the debt arises from a guaranty, from unpaid wages, or from a service fee — the protection does not follow. The law is not about protecting me from all unwanted entry into my home; it is about the particular power relationship between a creditor and a borrower, and that is where the Torah's protection is directed.

## Widow Collateral

### No Widow Collateral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַלְמָנָה, בֵּין שֶׁהִיא עֲנִיָּיה בֵּין שֶׁהִיא עֲשִׁירָה – אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא תַחֲבֹל בֶּגֶד אַלְמָנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a widow, whether she is poor** or **whether she is wealthy,** one **may not take collateral from her, as it is stated: "And you may not take the garment of a widow as collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:17).

The *Mishna* now addresses two further prohibitions: taking collateral from a widow, and taking *rechayim va-rachev* — items used for producing food. Neither of these cases is limited to a poor person. The *Mishna* states: *almana — bein shehi aniya bein shehi ashira — ein memashkenin otah* — a widow, whether poor or whether wealthy, may not be subjected to collateral seizure. The language is categorical and absolute. It does not matter whether she is poor or rich; it does not matter whether the collateral is taken by the creditor himself or through a *beit din*; it does not matter whether it happens inside or outside her home. The *Mishna* cites the verse: *ve-lo tachvol beged almana* — do not take collateral from a widow. Notably, the verse specifies only *beged*, a garment, but *Chazal* understand this to extend to any collateral at all — the basis for that expansion would be found in the relevant *midreshei halacha*, though that discussion does not appear in this *Gemara*. The one clear exception, which the *Mishna* acknowledges implicitly, is that if the widow herself willingly offers collateral *bish'at halva'ah* — at the time the loan is made — that is a different matter entirely.

### Rich vs Poor Widow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַלְמָנָה, בֵּין שֶׁהִיא עֲנִיָּיה בֵּין שֶׁהִיא עֲשִׁירָה – אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עֲשִׁירָה – מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ, עֲנִיָּיה – אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ. שֶׁאַתָּה חַיָּיב לְהַחֲזִיר לַהּ, וְאַתָּה מַשִּׂיאָהּ שֵׁם רַע בִּשְׁכֵנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** With regard to **a widow, whether she is poor** or **whether she is wealthy,** one **may not take collateral from her.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** **Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **a wealthy** widow, one may **take collateral from her.** But with regard to **a poor** widow, one may **not take collateral from her,** because **you** are **obligated to return** it **to her,** in accordance with the *halakha* that the collateral of a poor person must be returned to him whenever he needs it. **And** since you will be entering every day to return the collateral to her, **you** will thereby **give her a bad name among her neighbors,** as they will suspect her of developing an inappropriate relationship with you. By contrast, in the case of a wealthy widow, since there is no obligation to return her collateral, it is permitted to take collateral from her.

The *baraisa* records a dispute. *Divrei Rabbi Yehuda*: a widow, whether poor or wealthy, may not have collateral taken from her — consistent with the *Mishna*'s categorical reading. *Rabbi Shimon* disagrees: *ha-ashira memashkenin otah, ha-aniya ein memashkenin otah* — a wealthy widow may have collateral taken, a poor one may not.

One might assume that Rabbi Shimon's reasoning follows the obvious logic: a wealthy widow is not in a disadvantaged position, she does not need the Torah's protection, and therefore the prohibition does not apply to her. But the *Gemara* reveals that Rabbi Shimon's actual reasoning is entirely different, and far more surprising. The reason one may not take collateral from a poor widow is that when you take collateral from a poor person, you must return it every day — the garment by day, the pillow by night. And if the widow is poor and the creditor must therefore come to her home each day to return the collateral, *v'ata masi shem ra bish'chenoteha* — he brings a bad reputation upon her among her neighbors. A single woman, living alone, with a man coming and going to her home daily — people begin to gossip. According to Rabbi Shimon, this is the underlying reason for the prohibition of *chovel beged almana*. If she is wealthy, there is no need to return the collateral daily, there are no repeated visits, and therefore there is no concern. If she is poor, the visits are unavoidable and the prohibition applies.

This is a remarkable reading. It locates the reason for the prohibition not in the widow's disadvantaged social status — the classic vulnerability of *yatom v'almana* — but in a concern about reputational harm caused by the mechanics of daily collateral return.

### Derishing Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא דָּרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִקְרָא, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דָּרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִקְרָא? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that Rabbi Yehuda does not interpret the rationale** behind the mitzva **in the verse** and draw halakhic conclusions based on that interpretation, and **Rabbi Shimon does interpret the rationale** behind the mitzva **in the verse? But** haven't we **heard them** holding **the reverse** opinions elsewhere?

The *Gemara* now draws the obvious conclusion: it appears that Rabbi Yehuda does not engage in *darshinan ta'ama dikra* — he does not expound on or use the underlying reason of the verse to qualify the law's scope — while Rabbi Shimon does. Rabbi Yehuda takes the verse *lo tachvol beged almana* at face value and applies it categorically. Rabbi Shimon identifies what he takes to be the reason behind the prohibition and uses that reason to introduce a distinction the text itself does not contain.

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing to be precise about what *darshinan ta'ama dikra* does and does not mean. When we say Rabbi Yehuda *lo darash ta'ama dikra*, this does not mean he is unwilling to engage theologically or intellectually with the purposes of the *mitzvot*. Ask Rabbi Yehuda what he thinks the Torah's reason is for *shalei'ach tishalach et ha-em*, and he will surely answer. The question is not whether one may reflect on the reasons of the *mitzvot*, but whether one may use those assumed reasons to modify the legal scope of a *mitzvah halachically* — particularly where the text itself draws no distinctions.

There are in fact two distinct challenges to doing so. The first: how do you know you have identified the correct reason? Rabbi Shimon's reading is instructive here. He identified a reason for the prohibition of *chovel beged almana* that would never have occurred to most of us — not the widow's social vulnerability, but the concern about daily visits and reputational harm. If that is the reason, a distinction between rich and poor widows follows naturally. But how certain can we be that this, and not something else entirely, is the Torah's actual reason? The second challenge: even if you are confident in the reason, the text of the law as formulated draws no distinctions. *Lo tachvol beged almana* is categorical. By what authority do you override the plain formulation of the law based on your inference about its underlying purpose?

### Expand vs Narrow

One might ask whether the same logic applies symmetrically: if *darshinan ta'ama dikra* can narrow the scope of a prohibition — ruling that the law does not apply where the reason is absent — can it also expand the scope? As one student raised, if the concern is reputational harm to a single woman living alone, should the same prohibition apply to a single woman who is not a widow? This is precisely the danger of the enterprise. Once you are in the business of deriving scope from reason rather than from text, the law stops looking like *halacha* as we know it in the *Gemara*. Typically, the formulation of the law takes priority. Deriving legal distinctions from assumed reasons, without any textual hook, risks substituting one's own judgment about purpose for the carefully worded rules the Torah provides. The strength of *lo darshinan ta'ama dikra* lies precisely here.

### Yehuda: Reason Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְלֹא יַרְבֶּה לּוֹ נָשִׁים״, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מַרְבֶּה הוּא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מְסִירוֹת אֶת לִבּוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת וְהִיא מְסִירָה אֶת לִבּוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִשָּׂאֶנָּה. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְלֹא יַרְבֶּה לּוֹ נָשִׁים״ – אֲפִילּוּ כַּאֲבִיגַיִל!
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 21a) concerning the mitzvot of a king: **"And he should not multiply wives for himself,** that his heart not turn away" (Deuteronomy 17:17). **Rabbi Yehuda says: He** may **accumulate many** wives **for himself, provided that they are not** like those who **turn his heart** from reverence for God. **Rabbi Shimon says: Even one who turns his heart away, he should not marry her. If so, why is it stated: "He shall not multiply wives for himself"?** This teaches that **even** with regard to wives **like Abigail,** who was righteous and prevented David from sin (see I Samuel chapter 25), he is forbidden to have many. In this case, Rabbi Shimon does not interpret the rationale of the verse, while Rabbi Yehuda does interpret its rationale.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge: we find a *baraisa* in which their positions appear to be reversed. The verse about the king states: *lo yarbeh lo nashim v'lo yasur levavo* — he shall not multiply wives for himself lest his heart turn away. Here the Torah itself states the reason — *v'lo yasur levavo*. What do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon say?

*Rabbi Yehuda omer*: the king may in fact have many wives, *u'vilvad shelo yihyu mesirot et libo* — as long as they are righteous women who will not lead his heart astray. Rabbi Yehuda is willing to limit the scope of the prohibition based on the stated reason — which sounds like *darshinan ta'ama dikra*. Rabbi Shimon says: *afilu achat v'hi mesirah et libo harei zeh lo yis'ena* — even one wife, if she will lead his heart astray, he transgresses. He then adds: *im ken mah talmud lomar lo yarbeh lo nashim?* — if so, why does the Torah say "he shall not multiply"? *Afilu k'Gaya* — even if they are the most righteous women imaginable, he still may not multiply wives. Rabbi Shimon thus refuses to limit the scope of *lo yarbeh* based on the reason. The positions appear reversed from the *almana* case.

### Yehuda: Reason Explicit (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא דָּרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִקְרָא, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דִּמְפָרֵשׁ קְרָא ״וְלֹא יַרְבֶּה לּוֹ נָשִׁים וְלֹא יָסוּר״. מַאי טַעְמָא ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּ״לֹא יָסוּר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, Rabbi Yehuda does not** generally **interpret the rationale of the verse, and** it **is different here,** with regard to a king, **as the verse** itself **specifies** the reason: **"And he should not multiply wives for himself, that** his heart **not turn away." What is the reason** that **he may not multiply wives for himself?** It is **because** he must ensure **that** his heart **will not turn away.**

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction. *Le'olam Rabbi Yehuda lo darash ta'ama dikra* — Rabbi Yehuda fundamentally does not use the reason of the verse to modify the law's scope. *Ve-shani hacha de-mifaresh kra* — here it is different because the verse itself makes the reason explicit: *v'lo yarbe lo nashim... v'lo yasur levavo* — the reason is embedded in the formulation of the law itself. *Ma ta'am lo yarbe lo nashim? Mishum d'lo yasur* — the Torah tells us openly that the purpose of *lo yarbeh* is *lo yasur levavo*. When the reason is explicit in the *pasuk* itself, Rabbi Yehuda feels he has the authority to use it — not because he is generally willing to speculate about reasons and derive distinctions from them, but because here the Torah itself has provided both the law and its rationale in a single formulation, and the reason is therefore not guesswork. Moreover, when the reason is embedded in the text, it arguably qualifies the formulation itself, making the law inherently less categorical than it would appear in isolation.

### Shimon: Derives Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מִכְּדֵי בְּעָלְמָא דָּרְשִׁינַן טַעְמָא דִקְרָא, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה״, וְלָא בָּעֵינַן ״לֹא יָסוּר״! וַאֲנָא יָדַעְנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּ״לֹא יָסוּר״. ״לֹא יָסוּר״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת וּמְסִירָה אֶת לִבּוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִשָּׂאֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon** maintains: **Since we generally interpret the rationale in the verse,** there is no need for the verse itself to supply the rationale for the prohibition. **Let the Merciful One state: "He may not multiply," and we do not need** the Torah to add "that his heart **not turn away," and I** would already **know** the answer to the question: **What is the reason** that **he may not accumulate** many wives? It is **because** of the concern that his heart **not turn away.** If so, **why do I** need the phrase "His heart **not turn away" that the Merciful One writes?** It must certainly be necessary in order to increase the scope of the prohibition: **Even** if there is **one** woman who **turns his heart away, he may not marry her.** Therefore, the verse includes two *halakhot*: A general stricture against a king's marrying too many women, and a further *halakha* that a king may not marry even one woman who will lead him astray.

How then does Rabbi Shimon explain his position in the king's wives case? The *Gemara* states: *be'alma darshinan ta'ama dikra* — normally Rabbi Shimon is happy to derive the reason and limit the scope accordingly, even without the Torah spelling it out. He does this in the *almana* case, where the Torah gives no reason at all. In the king's wives case, he would have done the same: he would have derived that the concern is *lo yasur levavo*, and limited the prohibition accordingly.

*Lichtov rachmana lo yarbeh v'lo ba'inan lo yasur* — had the Torah only written *lo yarbeh* and not stated the reason, Rabbi Shimon would have figured out the reason himself and applied it, just as he did by the widow. *V'lo yasur d'chtav rachmana lama li?* — so why does the Torah need to state *lo yasur* explicitly? Since he would have derived it on his own, the explicit statement must be coming to add something. And what does it add? *Afilu achat u'mesira libo harei zu lo yis'ena* — that even one wife who leads his heart astray constitutes a separate prohibition: *lo yasur levavo* operates as an independent *issur* beyond *lo yarbeh*. The explicit statement is not confirming the reason but generating an additional law — that even a single problematic wife transgresses a prohibition. This explains why Rabbi Shimon does not draw the obvious conclusion that many righteous wives would be permitted: the *lo yarbeh* prohibition stands independently, and *lo yasur* adds a further prohibition rather than qualifying the first one.

### Asymmetry

The *Gemara*'s resolution reveals an important asymmetry. Using *darshinan ta'ama dikra* to be more stringent — expanding a prohibition beyond what the text says — requires less justification than using it to be more lenient. *Darshinan ta'ama dikra l'chumra* — if you want to extend a prohibition based on the reason, the threshold is lower. But to use the reason to *limit* the scope of an explicit, categorically formulated prohibition requires significantly more authority. One must not lose sight of the broader implications: if one can simply determine a reason for a *mitzvah* and use that determination to restrict its application, the door is opened to re-reading the *halacha* wholesale based on one's assumptions about purpose. The strength of Rabbi Yehuda's position is precisely that he demands a textual anchor — specifically, an explicit statement of the reason embedded in the formulation of the law itself — before allowing the reason to do legal work.

## Millstone Prohibition

### Nefesh Hu Chovel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחוֹבֵל אֶת הָרֵיחַיִם – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְחַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם שְׁנֵי כֵּלִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יַחֲבֹל רֵיחַיִם וָרָכֶב״. וְלֹא רֵיחַיִם וָרֶכֶב בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁעוֹשִׂין בּוֹ אוֹכֶל נֶפֶשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי נֶפֶשׁ הוּא חֹבֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One **who takes a millstone as collateral violates a prohibition, and** he is **liable for** taking **two vessels,** i.e., both millstones in the pair, **as it is stated: "He shall not take the lower or upper millstone as collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:6). The *tanna* adds: **Not only** did the Sages **say** that it is prohibited to take **the lower or upper millstone** as collateral, **but** they also said that one may not take **anything that** people **use in the preparation of food [*okhel nefesh*], as it is stated: "For he takes a man's life [*nefesh*] as collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:6).

The next *Mishna* addresses: *ha-chovel et ha-rechayim* — one who takes a millstone as collateral. The relevant verse is: *lo yachavol rechayim va-rachev ki nefesh hu chovel* — do not take the millstone and the upper grinding stone as collateral, because you are taking a livelihood. The *rechayim* and *rachev* refer to the two parts of the grinding apparatus: a lower stationary base stone and an upper stone that rotates — the latter apparently connected to an ox that walks around it. The *Gemara* later clarifies which is which, with the *rachev* being the upper, moving stone, perhaps named because it is "ridden" or moved by the animal.

The *Mishna* states: *ha-chovel et ha-rechayim — over mishum lo ta'aseh* — one who takes the millstone transgresses a negative prohibition. *Ve-chayav mishum shenei kelim* — if one takes both the *rechayim* and the *rachev*, he transgresses two prohibitions, one for each. The *Mishna* then states a general principle: *ve-lo rechayim va-rachev bilvad amru — ela kol davar she-osim bo ochel nefesh* — not only these two specific items, but anything used for food production. This derives from *ki nefesh hu chovel* — because you are depriving him of his livelihood. The scope of *ochel nefesh*, however, is interpreted to refer specifically to things directly engaged in food preparation, not to all tools used in a livelihood more broadly. A plow, for example, grows food ultimately, but does not directly work with the food itself — and the question of whether it is included is one the *Gemara* will address.

### Lashes Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָבַל רֵיחַיִם – לוֹקֶה שְׁתַּיִם: מִשּׁוּם ״רֵיחַיִם״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״כִּי נֶפֶשׁ הוּא חֹבֵל״. רֵיחַיִם וָרֶכֶב – לוֹקֶה שָׁלֹשׁ: מִשּׁוּם ״רֵיחַיִם״, ״וָרֶכֶב״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״כִּי נֶפֶשׁ הוּא חֹבֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna says:** One who **took a lower millstone as collateral is flogged** with **two** sets of lashes: One set is **due to** violating the prohibition of taking the **lower millstone** as collateral, **and** the second is **due to: "For he takes a man's life as collateral,"** since he took an item used in the preparation of food. If he took **the lower and upper millstone,** he **is flogged** with **three** sets of lashes: Two sets are **due to** violating the prohibitions of taking **a lower millstone and an upper millstone** as collateral, **and** the third is **due to** violating the prohibition: **"For he takes a man's life as collateral."**

*Amar Rav Huna*: if one takes the *rechayim* alone, he receives two sets of lashes — one for *rechayim* and one for *ki nefesh hu chovel*. If he takes both the *rechayim* and the *rachev*, he receives three sets of lashes — one for *rechayim*, one for *rachev*, and one for *ki nefesh hu chovel*. The general clause *ki nefesh hu chovel* applies not only to other items of *ochel nefesh* but also doubles up against the specifically mentioned *rechayim va-rachev*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: חָבַל רֵיחַיִם – לוֹקֶה אַחַת, וְרֶכֶב – לוֹקֶה אַחַת, רֵיחַיִם וָרֶכֶב – לוֹקֶה שְׁתַּיִם. ״כִּי נֶפֶשׁ הוּא חֹבֵל״ – לִשְׁאָר דְּבָרִים הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says:** If he **took a lower millstone as collateral he is flogged** with **one** set of lashes, if he took **an upper millstone he is flogged** with **one** set of lashes, and if he took both **the lower and upper millstones** together as collateral **he is flogged** with **two** sets of lashes. With regard to the verse: **"For he takes a man's life as collateral,"** **it comes to** teach **about** the prohibition **against** taking as collateral **other items** used in the preparation of food.

*Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees: taking the *rechayim* alone results in one set of lashes; taking the *rachev* alone results in one set of lashes; taking both results in two — but no more. *Ki nefesh hu chovel* is there to include other items of *ochel nefesh*, not to double up against what has already been explicitly mentioned: *ki nefesh hu chovel lish'ar devarim hu de'asa* — it comes for other things, not to create an additional *lav* for the *rechayim* and *rachev* themselves.

### Pesach Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא, בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי? דְּאָמַר רָבָא: אָכַל נָא – לוֹקֶה שְׁתַּיִם: מִשּׁוּם ״נָא״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״כִּי אִם צְלִי אֵשׁ״. מְבוּשָּׁל – לוֹקֶה שְׁתַּיִם: מִשּׁוּם ״מְבוּשָּׁל״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״כִּי אִם צְלִי אֵשׁ״. נָא וּמְבוּשָּׁל – לוֹקֶה שָׁלֹשׁ: מִשּׁוּם ״נָא״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״מְבוּשָּׁל״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ כִּי אִם צְלִי אֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that **Abaye and Rava,** in another dispute, **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of this **dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda** concerning the collateral? The Torah commands with regard to the preparation of the Paschal offering: "Do not eat of it raw, nor cooked in water, but roasted with fire, its head with its legs and with the innards thereof" (Exodus 12:9). Abaye and Rava engage in a dispute concerning the case of one who ate the meat when it was not properly roasted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֵין לוֹקִין עַל לָאו שֶׁבִּכְלָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Abaye says:** The prohibition "Do not eat of it…but roasted with fire" is not referring exclusively to this issue, but includes many cases, and one **is not flogged for** violating **a general prohibition.**

The *Gemara* proposes that this dispute between Rav Huna and Rabbi Yehuda is parallel to a debate between Abaye and Rava regarding the *korban pesach*. The relevant verse states: *al tochlu mimenu na u-vashel mevushal bamayim ki im tzeli esh* — do not eat it raw, and do not eat it cooked in water, but rather roasted over fire. There are thus two explicit prohibitions — eating it raw (*na*) and eating it cooked (*mevushal*) — and a general formulation — *ki im tzeli esh*, anything other than roasted. The question is the same: if one eats it raw, does he transgress twice — once for *na* and once for *ki im tzeli esh* — or only once for *na*, since *ki im tzeli esh* is there to include other cases and does not double up against the explicitly named ones?

*Rava* says: eating it raw results in two sets of lashes — once for *na* and once for *ki im tzeli esh*. Eating it cooked results in two sets of lashes — once for *mevushal* and once for *ki im tzeli esh*. Eating part raw and part cooked results in three sets of lashes. The general clause *ki im tzeli esh* overlaps with and doubles against the specific cases.

### Generic vs Specific

The *Gemara* asks whether the debate between Abaye and Rava is simply a restatement of the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rav Huna. *Leima Abaye d'amar k'Rabbi Yehuda* — Abaye holds like Rabbi Yehuda that the generic does not double up against the specific; *ve-Rava d'amar k'Rav Huna* — and Rava holds like Rav Huna that it does.

### Rava: Doubles Up

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ כְּרַב יְהוּדָה, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּ״כִי נֶפֶשׁ הוּא חֹבֵל״ לָא מַשְׁמַע רֵיחַיִם וָרֶכֶב, הִלְכָּךְ לִשְׁאָר דְּבָרִים הוּא דַּאֲתָא. אֲבָל הָכָא ״כִּי אִם צְלִי אֵשׁ״ לְמַאי אֲתָא? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְלָאו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rava** could have **said to you: I state** my opinion **even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** as there is a difference between the two cases: **Rav Yehuda states** his opinion **only there,** with regard to collateral, **because** the phrase: **"For he takes a man's life as collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:6), **does not** itself **indicate** that the verse is referring to **the lower and upper millstones.** Rather, it is a general statement, and **therefore it comes to** teach **about** the prohibition **against** taking as collateral **other items** used in the preparation of food, not to add a prohibition to take as collateral the lower and upper millstones. **But here,** the phrase **"but roasted with fire" comes for what** purpose? It excludes only raw or cooked meat, which were already mentioned. Therefore, **learn from** it that it comes **to** include another **prohibition** in addition to those specific directives referring to raw and cooked meat.

*Rava* rejects this. He says: *ana d'amri afilu k'Rabbi Yehuda* — I can actually hold like Rabbi Yehuda in the case of *rechayim va-rachev* and still say what I say about the *korban pesach*. The cases are distinguishable. In the case of *rechayim va-rachev*, *ki nefesh hu chovel* is needed to include a large universe of other items — all the other tools of *ochel nefesh* that are not *rechayim* and *rachev*. Since the clause is needed for those other things, its purpose is not to double up against *rechayim va-rachev* themselves. *Hilkach lish'ar devarim hu de'asa* — therefore it comes only for the other things. But by the *korban pesach*, *ki im tzeli esh* — for what other purpose does this clause exist? There is nothing other than *na* and *mevushal*. Since there is nothing else for *ki im tzeli esh* to include, it must be coming precisely to double up against the explicitly mentioned prohibitions. When a clause has no other purpose, its function is to create an additional *lav* for the concrete cases.

### Abaye: Other Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי אָמַר לָךְ: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַב הוּנָא: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּ״כִי נֶפֶשׁ הוּא חֹבֵל״ יַתִּירָא הוּא, כֵּיוָן דְּיַתִּירָא הוּא – שִׁדְיֵיהּ אַרֵיחַיִם וָרָכֶב. אֲבָל הָכָא, ״כִּי אִם צְלִי אֵשׁ״ לָאו יַתִּירָא הוּא, דְּמִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּקוּם אֱכוֹל צָלִי, יֶשְׁנוֹ – בְּבַל תֹּאכַל נָא. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּקוּם אֱכוֹל צָלִי – אֵינוֹ בְּבַל תֹּאכַל נָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** could have **said to you: I state** my opinion **even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna.** This is because **Rav Huna states** his opinion **only there,** with regard to collateral, **because** the prohibition **"For he takes a man's life as collateral"** **is superfluous** and therefore includes other utensils as well. **Since it is superfluous, apply it** as another prohibition **upon the lower and upper millstones.** **But here,** with regard to the Paschal offering, the phrase **"but roasted with fire" is not superfluous, as he requires it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **At the time when one is included in** the mitzva **to arise** and **eat** the **roasted** Paschal offering, **he is** also **included in** the prohibition **not to eat** of it **raw,** but **at a time when one is not included in** the mitzva **to arise** and **eat the roasted** Paschal offering, **he is not** included **in** the prohibition **not to eat** of it **raw** either. Consequently, one who eats the Paschal offering at that time is not liable to receive lashes.

*Abaye* likewise rejects the proposed identification. He says: *ana d'amri afilu l'Rav Huna* — I can hold like Rav Huna in the case of *rechayim va-rachev* and still say what I say about the *korban pesach*. In the case of *rechayim va-rachev*, Rav Huna holds that *ki nefesh hu chovel* is *yeseira* — extra, superfluous in a certain sense — because the entire rest of the universe of *ochel nefesh* items is what it comes to address. And since it is functioning as a prohibition for those other things, it is understood to be a genuine *lav* — and as a genuine *lav*, it also applies to the concrete case of *rechayim va-rachev* themselves. *Keivan deyeseira hu, shadyei rechayim va-rachev* — since it is already established as a prohibition, it encompasses *rechayim va-rachev* as well.

By contrast, in the case of the *korban pesach*, *ki im tzeli esh* is not needed for any additional prohibition beyond *na* and *mevushal*. But rather than saying it doubles up, *Abaye* explains: *de-mibei lei le-che-detanya* — it is there to teach something other than a prohibition. The clause connects the prohibition against eating raw to the *mitzvah* to eat the *korban pesach* roasted. *Be-sha'ah she-yeshno be-kumei achol tzeli* — at the time when you are obligated to eat it roasted, you transgress for eating it raw; *be-sha'ah she-eino be-kumei achol tzeli* — when you are not yet obligated to eat it roasted (before nightfall), you do not transgress for eating it raw. The clause is therefore not creating an additional prohibition at all; it is defining the time-frame of the prohibition of *na*. Since *ki im tzeli esh* serves a non-prohibitory purpose, it cannot be used to generate lashes against the specific cases of *na* and *mevushal*.

### Scissors Oxen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: חָבַל זוּג שֶׁל סַפָּרִים וְצֶמֶד שֶׁל פָּרוֹת – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. זֶה בְּעַצְמוֹ וְזֶה בְּעַצְמוֹ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: חָבַל זוּג שֶׁל סַפָּרִים וְצֶמֶד שֶׁל פָּרוֹת, יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יַחֲבֹל רֵיחַיִם וָרָכֶב״. מָה רֵיחַיִם וָרֶכֶב שֶׁהֵן מְיוּחָדִין שְׁנֵי כֵּלִים וְעוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה אַחַת, וְחַיָּיב עַל זֶה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וְעַל זֶה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – אַף כׇּל דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵן שְׁנֵי כֵּלִים מְיוּחָדִים וְעוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה אַחַת – חַיָּיב עַל זֶה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וְעַל זֶה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the dispute concerning the lower and upper millstones. It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yehuda:** If one **took as collateral barbers' scissors [*zog*] or a pair of cows,** he is **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes. If he took **this** one blade of the pair of scissors **by itself or that** one cow **by itself,** he is **liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. According to this *tanna*, he is not liable for the general prohibition. **And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If one **took as collateral barbers' scissors or a pair of cows,** one **might** have thought that he is **liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. Therefore, **the verse states: "He may not take as collateral the lower or upper millstone,"** which indicates that **just as the lower and upper millstones are unique** in that they are **two** distinct **vessels and** they **perform one task** together, and nevertheless one is **liable separately for this and separately for that, so too,** with regard to **all items that are** composed of **two individual vessels,** such as barbers' scissors or a pair of cows, **and** they **perform one task,** he is **liable separately for this and separately for that.**

The *Gemara* states: *tanya kevatei de-Rabbi Yehuda* — a *baraisa* is taught in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda. The *baraisa* discusses two cases: *chaval zug shel saparim* — taking a pair of scissors; *be-tzemed shel parot* — a team of oxen. For each pair taken together, one receives two sets of lashes — *zeh be-atzmo ve-zeh be-atzmo* — one for each item treated individually. If one took only one blade of the scissors or only one ox, one receives only one set of lashes.

*Rashi* notes that the first *baraisa* about the scissors and the team of oxen does not directly prove the point about whether *ki nefesh hu chovel* doubles up against *rechayim va-rachev*, since the scissors and the team of oxen are not themselves *rechayim va-rachev*. The real proof comes from the second *baraisa*: *chaval zug shel saparim ve-tzemed shel parot — yachol lo yehei chayav ella achat* — one might have thought the pair counts as only one unit. *Talmud lomar lo yachvol rechayim va-rachev* — the model of *rechayim va-rachev* teaches us that even when two items work together as a pair to accomplish a single task, you receive lashes for each one separately: *mah rechayim va-rachev she-meyuchadim shenei keilim ve-osim melacha achat ve-chayav al ze bifnei atzmo v'al ze bifnei atzmo* — just as the *rechayim* and *rachev* are two distinct *keilim* that work together and yet each generates a separate *lav*, so too any two *keilim* used in tandem each generate a separate prohibition. The implication of the *baraisa* is that taking *rechayim va-rachev* together results in two sets of lashes — not three — which aligns with Rabbi Yehuda's position against Rav Huna's three.

### Ochel Nefesh Scope

A crucial question concerns the precise scope of *ochel nefesh*. The *Mishna* states that the prohibition extends to *kol davar she-osim bo ochel nefesh* — anything used for food. But how broadly is this read?

*Tosafos* limits the scope significantly: *ochel nefesh* means something directly engaged with food itself. On this basis, *Tosafos* explains that the scissors (*zug shel saparim*) mentioned in the *baraisa* are not barber's scissors but kitchen scissors used for trimming food. Similarly, regarding the team of oxen (*tzemed shel parot*): *Tosafos* argues that oxen engaged in plowing the land are not included, since plowing is only indirectly related to food production. The *tzemed* in the *baraisa* would refer to oxen used in threshing grain — directly working with the food — rather than plowing. Additionally, *Tosafos* raises the view, reflecting a debate between the *Rambam* and the *Ra'avad*, that the prohibition cannot apply to oxen as animals since they are not *keilim*; consequently the *tzemed* should be understood as referring to the yoke rather than the animals themselves.

The *Rambam*'s position on the scope of *ochel nefesh* represents a broader understanding of the prohibition, and this debate about whether items must directly touch or process food — as opposed to merely being used in conjunction with food production — remains a live question in the *halacha*. Even something like a can opener poses an interesting edge case: it is used with food but does not itself process the food in any transformative sense.
