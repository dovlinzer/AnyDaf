# Kiddushin 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Issur Chal al Issur

### Chalal/Zonah/Almanah

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion concerning the statuses of *chalalah*, *zonah*, and *almanah* as they pertain to a *kohen*. The preceding sugya had explored the possibility that a single woman might simultaneously bear all of these designations — *chalalah*, *zonah*, *almanah*, and *grushah* — and whether each status could take effect sequentially even under the principle of *ein issur chal al issur*, the rule that once a person or object is already forbidden, a new and distinct prohibition cannot take effect upon it. The answer, as we had seen, was that this could be possible when each successive prohibition expands the scope of the original one.

### Braita: Kohen Gadol

A *tana* taught the following before Rav Sheshet: *kol shehu b'yikach harei hu b'lo yikach* — any woman who could theoretically have been eligible for a *kohen gadol* to marry had she been a *betulah* is now subject to the prohibitions of *lo yikach* enumerated in the verse: *almanah*, *grushah*, *chalalah*, and *zonah*. Conversely, *kol she'eino b'yikach eino b'lo yikach* — a woman who could not have been married by a *kohen gadol* even had she been a *betulah* and free of any prior marital status, is not further forbidden by virtue of being an *almanah* or *grushah*. The operative exclusion the *braita* has in mind is: *prat l'Kohen Gadol sheba al achoto almanah* — this would exclude the case of his sister who happens to be a widow. Since he could not marry her even if she were a virgin, by virtue of the *issur* of *achot*, the subsequent status of *almanah* or *grushah* cannot take effect upon her from his perspective — which is essentially the application of *ein issur chal al issur*.

### Rav Sheshet/Rabbi Shimon

Rav Sheshet responded to the *tana* and asked: whose teaching is this? It must be that of Rabbi Shimon, *d'amar ein issur chal al issur* — who holds that a prohibition cannot take effect upon an already-forbidden object or person. Since she is already forbidden to him as his sister, she cannot become additionally forbidden as an *almanah* or *grushah*.

### Neveilah/Yom Kippur

The Gemara cites the classical *braita* in support of this attribution: *Rabbi Shimon omer ha'ochel neveilah b'Yom HaKippurim patur* — one who eats *neveilah* on Yom Kippur is exempt, because *ein issur chal al issur*. It is worth pausing here, because this example is actually quite striking. The standard cases of *issur chal al issur* involve multiple statuses applied to the same object — the food itself is forbidden. But on Yom Kippur, the prohibition of fasting applies to the *person*, not to the food. The *neveilah* is a forbidden object; Yom Kippur is an obligation that rests on the individual. It is therefore fascinating that Rabbi Shimon applies *ein issur chal al issur* even across these two distinct planes.

### Issur Chamur/Kal

The Gemara then considers: perhaps the *braita* actually reflects the position of the *Rabbanan* rather than Rabbi Shimon? After all, the *Rabbanan* generally say *issur chal al issur* — that a prohibition does take effect upon a pre-existing prohibition. Under that view, the sister of a *kohen* who is also an *almanah* would be forbidden to him under both designations. Yet the Gemara rejects this: *afilu teima Rabbanan* — even according to the *Rabbanan*, *ki amri Rabbanan issur chal al issur hanei mili issur chamur al issur kal* — when the *Rabbanan* say one *issur* takes effect on top of another, that applies when the second prohibition is weightier or at least equal in severity. What they would not say is that a lighter *issur* (*issur kal*) takes effect upon a heavier one (*issur chamur*). The *issur* of *almanah* is a simple *lav*, whereas the *issur* of a sister carries the weight of *kares*. Accordingly, even the *Rabbanan* would concede that the *almanah* status cannot take effect upon what is already a *kares*-level prohibition.

### Sister Cannot Add Status

A second version (*ika d'amri*) holds that Rav Sheshet's response was precisely this point — that the *braita* reflects the view of the *Rabbanan*, who generally permit *issur chal al issur*, but who nonetheless would agree that a lighter prohibition cannot take effect upon a heavier one. The *braita* thus comes to spell out the limiting principle: the *Rabbanan*'s rule applies only when the subsequent prohibition is of equal or greater severity than the first.

### Kehunah Shani

One might have thought, however, that *issur kehunah* operates differently — that since *kohanim* carry a uniquely elevated set of marital prohibitions, the Torah may have intended to extend the reach of those prohibitions even beyond the normal parameters of *issur chal al issur*. The Gemara dismisses this as well: *ka mashma lan* — the *braita* teaches us that this is not the case. Even a *kohen*'s special status does not endow the *issur kehunah* with the capacity to take effect on top of an existing *issur kares*. The *issur kehunah* is always a *lav*, and a *lav* cannot override a pre-existing *kares*-level prohibition simply by virtue of the *kohen*'s heightened obligations.

## Zonah vs. Chalalah

### Rav Pappa Query

The Gemara now turns to a fundamental question posed by Rav Pappa to Abaye: *Yisrael ha-ba al achoto* — if a non-*kohen* has sexual relations with his sister, the act is certainly *assur*, and such a woman becomes a *zonah* through an act of forbidden sex not connected to *kehunah*. The question is: *chalalah mashvi lah o lo mashvi lah?* — does this act also confer upon her the status of *chalalah*?

To situate the question properly: a *zonah* is a woman who becomes forbidden through an act of illicit sexual relations, a category that applies even outside the world of *kehunah*. A *chalalah*, by contrast, would seem to be a status created either by birth — the daughter of a *chalal* — or through an act of sexual relations that is specifically forbidden to a *kohen*. For example, a *grushah* who has relations with a *kohen* becomes a *chalalah*, having already been forbidden to him; or an *almanah* who has relations with a *kohen gadol* becomes a *chalalah* as well, now forbidden to all *kohanim*. So the question becomes: is the category of *chalalah* confined to *issurei kehunah* — prohibitions specific to *kohanim* — or might it extend to any severe act of forbidden relations, such as the *issur kares* of a sister?

### Kal Vechomer

The Gemara considers whether one might construct a *kal vachomer*: *mi amrinan kal v'chomer me'chayavei lavin* — since a woman can become a *chalalah* from relations that are prohibited by a simple *lav*, such as a *kohen gadol* with an *almanah*, certainly relations prohibited by *kares*, such as a sister, should create *chalalah* status as well. The *kal vachomer* has a superficial appeal, yet it is also structurally awkward, since it conflates two qualitatively different categories — relations that are forbidden specifically to *kohanim* versus relations that are universally forbidden.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye responds definitively: *ein chalalah ela mi'issur kehunah bilvad* — the status of *chalalah* applies exclusively to prohibitions that are specific to *kohanim*. The concept of *chalalah* is rooted in the desecration of *kehunah* — *yichalel* — and therefore it is bounded by the world of *issurei kehunah*. Acts that are forbidden generally, even if weightier, belong to the category of *zonah*, not *chalalah*. Greater severity does not translate into *chalalah* status; the determinative factor is the specific relationship to *kehunah*.

### Rava's Source

Rava then asks: what is the textual source for this principle? He brings a *braita*: the *braita* asks why the Torah needs to write *grushah* in the passage concerning the *kohen gadol* — *lo yomar grushah b'kohen gadol v'tasi b'kal vachomer mi'kohen hedyot*. Since a normal *kohen* is already forbidden to marry a *grushah*, one could have derived through a *kal vachomer* that a *kohen gadol*, who has additional marital restrictions, is certainly forbidden. Why, then, does the verse enumerate *grushah* explicitly in the *kohen gadol*'s passage?

The verse concerning the *kohen gadol* lists: *almanah, grushah, v'chalalah, zonah es eileh lo yikach*. Three of these — *grushah*, *chalalah*, *zonah* — are already forbidden to a regular *kohen*. The only genuinely new addition is *almanah*. So why repeat the others?

### Grushah Enumeration

By the regular *kohen*, the Torah separates *grushah* syntactically from *chalalah* and *zonah*: it says *ishah chalalah v'zonah lo yikachu* and then, separately, *v'ishah grushah me'ishah lo yikachu*. This separation teaches that each designation — *grushah*, *chalalah*, *zonah* — constitutes its own independent prohibition, for which separate *malkus* may be administered. The same principle — that each *shem* stands independently — applies equally to the *kohen gadol*.

By the *kohen gadol*, then, the repetition of *grushah* serves not merely to reiterate a known prohibition, but to signal that just as *grushah* is separated from *chalalah* and *zonah* by the regular *kohen* to indicate independent prohibitions, so too each status in the *kohen gadol*'s verse is an independent *lav*, including the newly added *almanah*: *kach almanah chaluka mi'grushah v'chalalah v'zonah b'Kohen Gadol* — the *almanah* is to be treated as a distinct *lav* in its own right, distinct from the other enumerated statuses.

### Chalalah Context

The *braita* then asks: why does the verse by the *kohen gadol* repeat *chalalah*? The answer: *ein chalalah ela mi'issur kehunah* — the repetition of *chalalah* in the context of *almanah* and *grushah*, which are both specifically *issurei kehunah*, places *chalalah* squarely within that same domain. As Rashi explains, the proximity of *chalalah* to *almanah* and *grushah* in the verse is contextually determinative: *chalalah* belongs to the same category as those prohibitions, i.e., prohibitions that pertain specifically to *kohanim*. The *zonah* status, by contrast, can arise from relations that are universally forbidden and that apply to non-*kohanim* as well.

### Zonah Linkage

The *braita* continues: *zona lama ne'emra* — why does the verse by the *kohen gadol* mention *zonah* at all? The answer: *ne'emar kan zona v'ne'emar lehalan zona* — because the word *zona* appears in both passages, a textual linkage (*gezeirah shavah* or analogy) is created. By the *kohen gadol*, the verse states *v'lo yechalel zaro* — he shall not desecrate his offspring. This concept of *yechalel zaro*, that the child born of a forbidden union is a *chalal*, had not been stated explicitly by the regular *kohen*. The repetition of *zona* in both passages links them and teaches that the concept of *lo yechalel zaro* applies to a regular *kohen* as well.

### Rav Ashi Conclusion

Rav Ashi draws the practical conclusion from all of the foregoing: *Kohen haba al achoso, zona mashvi lah, chalalah lo mashvi lah* — a *kohen* who has relations with his sister renders her a *zonah* (since the act is an illicit union) but does not render her a *chalalah* (since it is not an *issur kehunah*). However, *chozar u-va aleha* — if subsequently another *kohen* has relations with her now that she bears the status of *zonah*, that second act does constitute a forbidden union specific to *kehunah*, since *zonah* is among the women a *kohen* is prohibited from. That second act would therefore render her a *chalalah*.

## Lo Yikach/Lo Yechallel

### Rav Yehudah Two Lashes

The Gemara now introduces a major principle through the statement of Rav Yehudah: *Kohen Gadol b'almanah lokeh shtayim* — a *kohen gadol* who has relations with an *almanah* receives two sets of lashes: *achas mishum lo yikach ve'achas mishum lo yechalel* — one for violating the prohibition of *lo yikach* (the prohibition against taking a forbidden woman in marriage) and one for violating *lo yechalel* (the prohibition of desecrating, understood here as rendering the woman a *chalalah*). Until now, the discussion had focused on *lo yikach* and the role of the act of relations in fulfilling or transgressing the marital prohibition. Rav Yehudah introduces the idea that *lo yechalel zaro* — normally read as forbidding the desecration of one's offspring — also constitutes a standalone prohibition on the *act of sex* itself, distinct from and in addition to the prohibition of *lo yikach*.

One might note that this potentially yields not two but three prohibitions: *lo yikach*, *lo yechalel* for rendering her a *chalalah*, and *lo yechalel zaro* for rendering the offspring a *chalal*. The Gemara addresses this: the statement of Rav Yehudah involves a case of *shelo gamar bi'aso* — where there was no completion of the sexual act in the full halakhic sense. Without *gmar bi'ah* — full completion — there is no concern that a child would be conceived, and therefore the third prohibition of *lo yechalel zaro* in the sense of the offspring does not apply. This is a remarkable halakhic move: rather than reading *lo yechalel zaro* as a prohibition triggered only upon the birth (or even conception) of an actual *chalal* child, the Gemara reads it as a prohibition structured around the act of relations itself — specifically, an act that has the character of one that could lead to conception.

### Lo Yechallel Zaro

With *gmar bi'ah*, then, all three prohibitions would potentially apply. Without it, only two.

### Gamur Biah

As noted, the operative concept of *gmar bi'ah* is determinative in distinguishing between a case where *lo yechalel zaro* is engaged and one where it is not. The Gemara's assumption here is that *lo yechalel zaro*, as a prohibition, attaches to the act of relations understood as reproductive — i.e., complete intercourse — rather than to the actual birth of a *chalal* child.

### Almanah/Grushah

Rava then raises a challenge: the *braita* teaches that if a *kohen gadol* has relations with a woman who is simultaneously an *almanah* and a *grushah*, he receives lashes for *shnei shemos* — two distinct designations. If we take this at face value, it sounds like he receives exactly two sets of lashes. But according to Rav Yehudah's principle, each act that renders her a *chalalah* should generate an additional set of lashes for *lo yechalel*. So there ought to be more than two.

### Two Shemot

The Gemara resolves this: *lo, shnei shemos al zeh u'shnei shemos al zeh* — no, the *braita* means two *shemot* for each of the two categories. For the *almanah* designation, he incurs lashes for *almanah* and for *lo yechalel*; for the *grushah* designation, he incurs lashes for *grushah* and for *lo yechalel*. Even though the woman is rendered a *chalalah* only once by the single act, since each prohibition — *almanah* and *grushah* — independently carries the potential to render her a *chalalah*, each one independently triggers the *lo yechalel* prohibition as well. The result is that for each *shem*, there are always two sets of lashes.

### Grushah/Chalutzah

The Gemara then challenges this reading from the *seifa* of the same *braita*: *grushah va'chalutzah eino chayav ela achas* — if the woman is a *grushah* and a *chalutzah* rather than an *almanah* and a *grushah*, he is liable for only *achat* — one. This sounds like a single set of lashes, which would contradict the structure of two-per-*shem* that was just established. The Gemara resolves: *hachi ka'amar, eino chayav ela al achas* — the *braita* means he is liable only for one *category* (since *chalutzah* is *derabanan*), but within that one category the lashes are still two sets. The *braita* says *achat* in contrast to the two full categories of the *almanah*/*grushah* case, not in contrast to the doubled lashes within a single category.

Notably, this leads the Gemara to affirm that *chalutzah* is *derabanan*. A *braita* does derive *chalutzah* from the verse *v'ishah grushah me'ishah* via the extra *vav*, but the Gemara dismisses this as a mere *asmachta* — a textual anchor for a rabbinic ruling rather than a genuine biblical derivation.

### Each Shemot

To summarize the structure: each *shem* — each independent prohibited designation — carries with it two sets of lashes: one for the *shem* itself and one for the *lo yechalel* that attaches to any act by a *kohen* (or specifically a *kohen gadol*, as we will see) that renders a woman a *chalalah*.

### Rashi/Rambam

Rashi reads this entire *sugya* as applying to all *kohanim*, not only to the *kohen gadol*. Whenever a *kohen* has forbidden relations with a woman, and that act renders her a *chalalah*, the *kohen* incurs an additional set of lashes for *lo yechalel*, since each act of forbidden relations by a *kohen* with an otherwise-eligible woman creates *chalalah* status as a byproduct of the violation. On Rashi's reading, this is a general feature of the *issur kehunah* that applies regardless of whether the *kohen* is a *kohen gadol* or a *kohen hedyot*.

Rambam reads it otherwise. The *psukim* of *lo yechalel* are stated specifically in the context of the *kohen gadol*, and there are compelling reasons to limit the rule to that case. First, the textual locus of *lo yechalel* is the *kohen gadol* passage. Second, and perhaps more consequentially: by any prohibition applicable to a regular *kohen* — *grushah*, *zonah*, etc. — the woman was already forbidden before the act; rendering her additionally a *chalalah* adds a new status but does not materially expand her forbidden territory. By contrast, a *kohen gadol* who has relations with an *almanah* transforms a woman who was previously permissible to all *kohanim* into one who is now forbidden to all *kohanim*. The act of desecration has concrete and expansive effect specifically in that case. For the Rambam, therefore, the prohibition of *lo yechalel* as an independent *lav* triggered by sex alone applies only to a *kohen gadol*, not to a *kohen hedyot*.

## Marriage vs. Sex

### Abaye Position

The Gemara now turns to what is perhaps the most consequential structural question of the *sugya*: where precisely does the prohibition of the *kohen* reside — in the act of marriage, in the act of relations, or in both? Abaye states: *kidesh lokeh, ba'al lokeh* — when he betroths (performs *kiddushin*), he receives lashes; when he has relations, he receives lashes. *Kidesh lokeh mishum lo yikach, ba'al lokeh mishum lo yechalel* — the *kiddushin* itself violates *lo yikach*, and the subsequent sexual relations violate *lo yechalel*.

### Rashi Interpretation

Rashi qualifies this significantly. He does not read Abaye as saying that the act of *kiddushin* itself, as a formal legal transaction, triggers *malkus*. Throughout the *sugya*, the focus has always been on the act of relations as the site of transgression — indeed, the Gemara elsewhere established that a *kohen* who repeats the act with the same forbidden woman receives lashes each time, demonstrating that it is the sex and not the betrothal that is the operative violation. What Abaye means, on Rashi's reading, is conceptual: when *kiddushin* is followed by sex, one set of lashes is attributed conceptually to the *lo yikach* of the *kiddushin*, and a second to the *lo yechalel* of the relations.

### Rava Position

Rava disagrees: *ba'al lokeh, lo ba'al eino lokeh* — he receives lashes only when he actually has relations; without relations, no lashes are incurred for *kiddushin* alone. The reason: *mishum d'ksiv lo yikach v'lo yechalel, mah ta'am lo yikach? mishum lo yechalel* — the verse writes *lo yikach* and *lo yechalel* in juxtaposition, and the implication is that the reason for *lo yikach* is precisely because it leads to *lo yechalel*. The prohibition of marriage, therefore, is operative only when it is in fact followed by sexual relations. *Lo yikach* that does not lead to *lo yechalel* — i.e., *kiddushin* without subsequent sex — does not trigger lashes.

### Machzir Gerushah

A further refinement: Abaye concedes that in the case of *machzir gerushato* — a man who remarries his divorced wife after she has been married to another man — even he agrees that *kiddushin* alone does not trigger lashes. The reason is that the verse in that case says *lakachtah lihyos lo l'ishah* — the Torah frames the prohibition around the act of taking her to be a wife in the full relational sense, i.e., including the sexual consummation. Without the sex, the prohibition is not complete. Abaye's position that *kiddushin* by itself is a violation applies to the standard *issurei kehunah*, not to *machzir gerushato*.

### Sex Alone

Rava, however, concedes in the opposite direction for the case of a *kohen gadol* with an *almanah*: *u'modeh Rava b'Kohen Gadol b'almanah she'im ba'al v'lo kidesh she'loke* — even Rava agrees that if the *kohen gadol* had relations without any prior *kiddushin*, he still receives lashes, because the verse says *v'lo yechalel zaro b'amav* — and he did in fact desecrate, having rendered her a *chalalah* through the act itself.

Both Abaye and Rava agree, however, that by *machzir gerushato*, if he had relations without *kiddushin*, no lashes apply — *derech likuchim asra Torah* — the Torah's prohibition in that case is framed specifically around the act of taking (i.e., marriage), not the act of relations standing alone.

### Abaye/Rava Summary

To restate the positions clearly: for *machzir gerushato*, both Abaye and Rava agree that marriage *and* sex are both required — neither alone suffices. For the *kohen gadol* with an *almanah*, both agree that sex alone transgresses *lo yechalel*. The debate between Abaye and Rava concerns *kiddushin* without sex in the standard *issurei kehunah* context: Abaye says this independently violates *lo yikach*; Rava says it does not, since *lo yikach* is only operative when it leads to *lo yechalel*.

### Rambam Limitation

For the Rambam, as noted above, this entire framework — in which sex alone, without *kiddushin*, constitutes a violation through *lo yechalel* — is limited to a *kohen gadol*. A *kohen hedyot* always requires the context of *lo yikach*; the prohibition is grounded in the act of marriage, and sex divorced from the marital framework, while generating *chalalah* status and being otherwise *assur*, does not constitute a separate biblical transgression of *lo yechalel* by a *kohen hedyot*. This distinction is practically significant in cases where *poskim* grapple with a *kohen* who has had relations with a *grushah* without formal *kiddushin*: according to the Rambam, there is no biblical transgression in that scenario, even as the children would be *challalim* and she would become a *chalalah*.

## Ger's Daughter

### Rabbi Yehudah Position

The *sugya* now transitions to a *braita* concerning the status of a *ger*'s daughter. Rabbi Yehudah holds: *bas ger zachar kebas chalal* — the daughter of a male convert is like the daughter of a male *chalal* and is therefore a *chalalah*, disqualified for *kohanim*. This is a far-reaching position, and it must be noted at the outset that we do not rule according to Rabbi Yehudah here.

### Logic Argument

Rabbi Yehudah supports this with what appears to be a logical derivation: *mah challal sheba mitipa kesheira bito pesulah* — a *chalal*, who was born from a technically valid ("kosher") drop of seed yet is disqualified because of the forbidden nature of his parents' union, has a daughter who is *pesulah*. *Ger sheba mitipa pesulah* — a *ger*, who was born from seed that is "invalid" in the sense that it preceded his joining the Jewish people, *eino din shebito pesulah* — should certainly have a *pesulah* daughter. The argument seems to be that if the *chalal*'s flawed status (arising from a sin) makes his daughter invalid, then the *ger*'s even more fundamental prior status of non-Jewishness should equally or more strongly disqualify his daughter.

### Gemarah Challenge

The Gemara challenges this immediately: *mah lechallal shekein yetziraso ba'aveira* — what makes a *chalal* different is precisely that his very creation (*yetzira*) came through sin — through a forbidden act of relations between a *kohen* and a woman forbidden to him. Can we derive anything from this about a *ger*, who was not "created in sin" in any such sense?

The Gemara then invokes a counterexample: *kohen gadol be'almanah yochiach* — look at the case of the *chalalah* who comes from a *kohen gadol*'s union with an *almanah*. There, *ein yetzirasa ba'aveira* — her becoming a *chalalah* does not involve her being created through sin in the sense of birth; she herself is made a *chalalah* through an act of forbidden sex. So the category of *chalalah* is not limited to cases of creation through sin.

### Two Ways Chalalah

This establishes that a woman becomes a *chalalah* in one of two ways: either by being born from a *chalal* (and thus "created" in proximity to sin), or by herself participating in an act of forbidden relations specific to *kehunah* (and thus through an act of sin). In neither of these two cases is the *ger* implicated in any obvious way.

### Common Denominator

The Gemara attempts to construct a common denominator (*tzad hashaveh*) by combining both ways of becoming *chalalah*: the *chalal* (whose daughter becomes a *chalalah*) and the *almanah* who had relations with a *kohen gadol* (who herself becomes a *chalalah*) — what do they share? *She'einan b'rov hakahal* — neither is representative of the majority of the population. They occupy some marginal or exceptional status. And therefore, one might argue, a *ger* — who is also not part of the normative majority — should similarly disqualify his daughter.

### Too Broad

The Gemara is dissatisfied with this: the commonality is far too broad and generic to be halakhically meaningful. Almost any exceptional population could be swept in on such a standard.

### Aveira Element

A more refined common denominator is then proposed: what the *chalal* and the *almanah*-made-*chalalah* actually share is the presence of *aveira* — transgression — in some form. The *chalal* owes his status to the sin of his parents' union; the woman owes her *chalalah* status to the forbidden act she participated in. There is, in each case, an element of sin underlying the disqualification.

### Mitzri Rishon

But this too has a flaw. The Gemara counters with the case of *Mitzri rishon yochiach* — a first-generation Egyptian can serve as a counterexample. His son (a second-generation Egyptian) is forbidden (to the community, not only to *kohanim*), yet there was no act of sin involved in the child's birth. The Gemara goes through the familiar dialectic of *chalal yochiach* and *chozar hadin* — each proposed counterexample is met with a counter-counterexample, and the logical circle continues without yielding a clean principle. The attempt to establish *ger* as part of the category through a *tzad hashaveh* ultimately fails to produce a coherent, satisfying derivation.

### Final Application

Despite the failure to produce a logically compelling argument, the Gemara notes: *l'Rabbi Yehudah ger nami posel b'vi'ato* — Rabbi Yehudah extends his position even to hold that the act of a *ger* having relations with a woman would render her a *chalalah*, just as he holds that the *ger*'s daughter is a *chalalah*. His position is internally consistent: once one accepts that *ger* status is categorically disqualifying for *kehunah* purposes, all of its implications follow, including that a *ger* cannot make a woman suitable for *kehunah* through his relations with her. Yet the difficulty of the logic is, as noted, something that the Gemara itself presses without resolution. We do not rule like Rabbi Yehudah.

## Alternate Positions

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov offers a more circumscribed position: only the offspring of a *ger* and a *giyoret* together are disqualified from *kehunah*. If only one parent was a convert and the other was a natural-born Jew, the daughter is permitted to a *kohen*. The reasoning flows naturally: a *giyoret* herself is disqualified from *kehunah*; a daughter born of both a *ger* and a *giyoret* is similarly disqualified. But if one parent provides naturally-born Jewish status, that parent's lineage is sufficient.

### Rabbi Shimon Age

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai takes a different approach entirely, focusing on age of conversion rather than parentage: *giyoret p'chutah mibat shalosh shanim v'yom echad kesherah l'kehunah* — a female convert who converted before the age of three years and one day is *kesherah* for *kehunah*. His concern, as will become clear, is not with lineage per se but with the presumption that she may have had sexual relations while not Jewish — a presumption that does not apply to a child converted under the age of three.

### Midianite Source

The textual basis invoked for Rabbi Shimon's position is the verse regarding the women of Midian: *v'chol hataf banashim hachayu lachem* — and all the young girls, keep alive for yourselves. This implies that the surviving girls were kept for the purpose of marriage, including marriage to Pinchas, who was a *kohen*. Since Pinchas is explicitly included among those who could take them (*b'lo Pinchas hayu imahem*), young girls from among non-Jewish nations, when they are below the age of three, are deemed suitable for *kehunah*.

### Rabbanan Alternative

The *Rabbanan* reject this reading of the verse: *v'rabbanan, hachayu lachem l'avadim ul'shfachot* — they say the verse means the girls were kept as servants and maidservants, not as potential wives. The verse therefore cannot serve as a source for permitting young *giyorot* to *kohanim*.

### Yechezkel Pasuk

All of these positions — Rabbi Yehudah, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, and the *Rabbanan* — are derived from a single verse in Yechezkel: *almanah u'grushah lo yikchu lahem l'nashim ki im betulot mizera beit Yisrael* — widows and divorcées they shall not take as wives, but only virgins from the seed of the house of Israel. This verse, notably, reads like a description of the *kohen gadol*'s restrictions (since the regular *kohen* may marry an *almanah*), yet it is read here as the textual anchor for the broader question of who qualifies as *beit Yisrael* for purposes of *kehunah*.

### Zera Beit Yisrael

Rabbi Yehudah reads *mizera beit Yisrael* with the emphasis on *zera* — seed — in a patrilineal sense: *ad d'ika kol zera miYisrael* — as long as there is fully Jewish male seed, meaning the father must be a natural-born Jew. A *ger*, regardless of the mother's status, lacks this *zera Yisrael* in the paternal line, and therefore his daughter is disqualified. Conversely, a Jewish father and a *giyoret* mother would produce a *kesherah* daughter, since the seed (father's side) is fully Jewish. This is a far more coherent derivation than the attempted *tzad hashaveh* above, and it grounds the disqualification not in any problematization of the *ger* himself but in a criterion of patrilineal Jewish origin.

### Miktzat Zera

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov reads *mizera* more leniently: *va'afilu miktzat zera* — even part of the seed being from *beit Yisrael* suffices. As long as one parent — mother or father — is a natural-born Jew, the requirement is met. Only when both parents are converts does the daughter fail to qualify.

### Rabbi Yosei

Rabbi Yosei understands *mizera beit Yisrael* temporally: *mishenizre'u beYisrael* — as long as she was conceived among Jews, i.e., while her parents were already Jewish. On this reading, even the daughter of two converts is permitted, since both parents were Jewish at the time of her conception. The only woman truly disqualified is one who was herself born or conceived before her parents converted — i.e., the *giyoret* herself.

### Core Issue

This divergence of opinion reveals the fundamental halakhic question underlying the entire *sugya*: is the disqualification of a *giyoret* from *kehunah* rooted in a concern about *zenut* — the presumption that she may have had forbidden sexual relations while not Jewish, the position associated with Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai? Or is it rooted in a separate criterion of *mizera beit Yisrael* — Jewish lineage or Jewish-conceived origin — which has nothing to do with sexual history and might well be *derabanan* since the source is a verse in Yechezkel rather than the Torah itself? This is a question of considerable practical importance, since the two approaches would generate different distinctions: the *zenut*-based approach lends itself to an age-of-conversion analysis (since a child under three cannot have had relations), while the *zera Yisrael* approach generates lineage-based distinctions regardless of age.

### Betuloteha

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai advances his position further: *mishenizre'u betuloha beYisrael* — the disqualifying concern is that she lost her *betulot* — her virginity — while not Jewish. The age of three is the operative threshold because it is understood that the hymen is regenerated up to that age; a girl converted before age three, even if something had occurred, would not have lost her virginity in a halakhically meaningful sense. Accordingly, only a *giyoret* who converted at age three or older might be presumed to have had relations as a non-Jew and is therefore disqualified. One who converted younger than three is *kesherah* for *kehunah*.

The *sugya* thus closes with a rich set of positions on the question of who qualifies as *beit Yisrael* for purposes of *kehunah*: whether the criterion is entirely patrilineal natural-born Jewish seed (Rabbi Yehudah), partially Jewish seed from either parent (Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov), Jewish conception regardless of convert status (Rabbi Yosei), or rather a concern with sexual history resolved by an age threshold (Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai). The normative ruling follows none of the most restrictive positions: a *giyoret* conceived after her parents' conversion is *kesherah*, in accordance with Rabbi Yosei's principle that *mishenizre'u beYisrael* is the operative standard.
