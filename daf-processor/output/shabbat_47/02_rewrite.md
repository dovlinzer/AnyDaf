# Shabbat 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Shimon & Muktza

### Lamp & Unintended Acts

The sugya on 47a picks up from the bottom of 46b, where the discussion has centered on the position of Rabbi Shimon and the narrow domains of *muktza* that he accepts. The specific question at hand is this: Rabbi Shimon clearly prohibits moving a burning lamp — that much seems agreed upon — but why? If one moves the lamp and it happens to go out, that extinguishing is unintended. It is a *davar she'eino mitkaven*, and according to Rabbi Shimon, unintentional acts are permitted. So why should moving the burning lamp be problematic?

The Gemara's first proposed answer is that Rabbi Shimon's leniency regarding *eino mitkaven* applies only when the act, were it done intentionally, would constitute a *d'rabbanan* violation. The classic example is dragging a bench across the ground. Even if one intentionally dragged a bench in order to form a furrow, that would only be a rabbinic prohibition, since digging in such a manner is a *shinui* — an unusual mode of performing the prohibited act. By contrast, extinguishing a flame intentionally would in certain circumstances constitute a *d'oraisa* violation of *mechabeh* — for instance, if one's purpose were to produce charcoal. Therefore, the Gemara tentatively suggests, in cases where the intentional act would be *d'oraisa*, Rabbi Shimon holds that even *eino mitkaven* is forbidden rabbinically.

### Rejecting First Answer

Rava challenges this proposed distinction with a pointed *meitivi*. The Mishnah records that clothing merchants may wear the garments they sell in their ordinary manner of transport — even if those garments contain *shatnez* — provided they do not intend to wear them for protection from sun or rain. This ruling is attributed to Rabbi Shimon, as the permissibility turns entirely on the absence of intent. And yet, if one were to intentionally wear *shatnez* for protection, that would plainly be a *d'oraisa* transgression. It follows that Rabbi Shimon permits *eino mitkaven* even in the face of a biblical prohibition. The proposed distinction collapses.

This point has significant practical consequence. A central pillar of Shabbat observance is the principle that an unintended act which will not inevitably occur is permitted — *eino mitkaven* — and this applies even when the potential violation would be *d'oraisa*. The Gemara's challenge from the *shatnez* case confirms this across domains, not merely on Shabbat. One might observe, however, that the *shatnez* example is not as generalizable as it first appears. Rashi explains there that the very definition of "wearing" requires donning a garment with the intent for it to serve as clothing. When the merchants wear the garments to carry them, they are by definition not "wearing" them in the halachically operative sense — they are transporting. If that is correct, then the *shatnez* case is not really an instance of *eino mitkaven* overriding a *d'oraisa* in the usual sense; it is rather a dispute about the definitional scope of the prohibited act. In that reading, the case may not illuminate the broader question of *kavana* and intent across other prohibitions as straightforwardly as the Gemara implies.

In any case, the Gemara now must abandon its first answer entirely. Rabbi Shimon allows *eino mitkaven* even by *d'oraisa* violations, so the concern about extinguishing cannot explain why the burning lamp is off-limits. A new explanation is needed.

It is worth pausing here to note the parallel issue of *huktza l'mitzvato* — the principle that an object designated for a *mitzvah* cannot be diverted from that function. Some had suggested that the burning lamp falls into this category, analogous to the *s'chach* of a *sukkah* which may not be removed. But as Tosafot points out, moving the lamp around the house to illuminate wherever one needs it does not diminish its *mitzvah* function at all — the lamp continues to serve. The only application of *huktza l'mitzvato* relevant to the lamp is benefiting from the oil that has dripped away, which is precisely what the Mishnah prohibits under the formulation *ein nehenin mimenu*. Moving the lamp as such is not the concern of *huktza l'mitzvato*.

### Flame as Muktza

The Gemara now offers Rava's solution: *hanach l'ner shemen u'ftila* — leave aside the lamp, the oil, and the wick. These three components are *muktza* in the eyes of Rabbi Shimon by virtue of a different category altogether: *basis l'davar ha'assur*, a support for a forbidden object. The forbidden object in question is the flame itself. Rashi makes this explicit: *l'davar ha'assur u'leshalheves* — the forbidden thing is the flame, and the vessel is secondary to it while the flame burns within it. It is the flame that is *muktza*, and everything that supports it — the wick, the oil, and the lamp — becomes *muktza* as a consequence of being a *basis* for that flame.

This is a striking category. The flame itself is what is *muktza* in the primary sense, yet the flame cannot be physically moved in isolation. The concept here is that the actively burning flame represents something utterly prohibited on Shabbat, something that Rabbi Shimon acknowledges as *muktza* in a way that transcends the ordinary mechanics of his usually narrow *muktza* position. Everything else functions as a *basis l'davar ha'assur*. This explains why even Rabbi Shimon, who otherwise rejects most forms of *muktza*, concedes that the burning lamp may not be moved.

### Three Components

Tosafot raises a precise analytical question about this ruling. Why must all three elements — the lamp, the oil, and the wick — be considered a *basis*? Could one not say that only the wick, being the most proximate support for the flame, is the *basis*, while the oil and the vessel are more remote?

The answer Tosafot provides is critical. If only the wick were considered a *basis l'davar ha'assur*, then the lamp itself would be a *basis* to two things simultaneously: the wick, which is prohibited, and the oil, which is permitted. And a *basis* that supports both a permitted and a prohibited object is not treated as a *basis l'davar ha'assur* and may be moved. Therefore, the oil must itself be considered a *basis* as well — for if the oil were deemed permitted, it would neutralize the lamp's status as a *basis* to something forbidden. Only by establishing that all three components are in the category of *basis* can we explain why the lamp as a whole may not be moved.

This generates an important and open question in the laws of *basis*: does a *basis* to a *basis* become a *basis* in its own right? The lamp, oil, and wick are all considered as a single unit serving the flame, so perhaps the rule applies here because they function organically as one integrated system. Whether in a more ordinary case — say, a lamp sitting on a tray sitting on a table — each layer of support would likewise inherit *basis* status is a question that this passage raises but does not fully resolve.

## Ash Shovel Case

### Romanus Ruling

The Gemara transitions, with characteristic elegance and without an explicit header, from the discussion of *basis* to a case involving an ash shovel. Rabbi Chanina quotes Rabbi Romanos: Rebbe permitted him to carry a *machta* — an ash shovel or incense burner — together with its ashes. This immediately invites a challenge from Rabbi Zeira, who poses the question to Rav Assi: could Rabbi Yochanan really have endorsed this? For we have a Mishnah which states that a person may lift a child even if the child is holding a stone, or a basket even if it contains a stone — both instances of moving a permitted object together with a *muktza* item. And yet Rabbi Yochanan himself explained in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chana that the basket case only works if the basket is *filled with fruit* — so that the basket is a *basis* to both a permitted thing and a prohibited thing simultaneously.

### Basis Status

This sets up the central principle at stake. When a *basis* serves both a permitted and a prohibited object, it does not take on the identity of a *basis l'davar ha'assur* and may be moved. This is why the fruit-filled basket with a stone can be carried — the basket is supporting the fruit just as much as the stone, and therefore its character is not defined exclusively by the *muktza* content. But, the Gemara continues, if there is no fruit — if the basket is *only* carrying the stone — then it cannot be moved. So why can the ash shovel be moved when it contains ashes, which are genuine *muktza* with no permitted use?

Before proceeding, an important distinction must be kept in mind. *Kli she-melachto l'issur* — a vessel whose primary use is for a prohibited purpose, such as a hammer — is not the same category as true *muktza*. A hammer retains some hypothetical permitted use on Shabbat (cracking nuts, for instance), and therefore does not become a *basis* in the strong sense. The *basis* principle applies only to genuine *muktza* objects, things that are entirely off-limits with no possible permitted use. The ashes in this case are true *muktza* — they have no use — and so the question of *basis* arises acutely.

### Incense Explanation

The Gemara records that upon being confronted with this challenge, the sage was dumbfounded for a moment, quoting the expression from Daniel: he was astonished for a time. Then he offered an answer: *hacha nami de'is bei kartin* — perhaps in this incense burner there were a few small unburned granules of incense mixed in with the ashes. Those granules are not *muktza* — one can smell them directly even on Shabbat, as Rashi explains — and therefore the incense burner is a *basis* to both a permitted object (the granules) and a prohibited object (the ashes), which would allow it to be moved.

Abaye objects: *kartin bei rabbi mi chashivi?* — are tiny granules of incense considered significant in the household of Rebbe? Such items would be entirely beneath notice in so distinguished a setting. The Gemara raises the possibility that even if they are insignificant to Rebbe, they might matter to poorer people, since *vechi teima chazu l'aniyim* — perhaps they are fit for the poor. The Gemara then invokes a *braita* which states that garments of the poor are judged by the standards of the poor, and garments of the rich by the standards of the rich. A small, coarse scrap of fabric is significant to a poor person and may therefore contract *tumah*, while a wealthy person would discard it and it would not. So too, what is trivial in Rebbe's home might be significant elsewhere. But the Gemara rejects this: *li hitye rabbi* — Rebbe permitted this for himself, in his own home. What matters to a poor person is irrelevant to what is significant in Rebbe's household. If the granules are trivial for Rebbe, they cannot transform the incense burner into a *basis l'davar ha'assur v'l'davar hamuttar*.

### Copper Vessel

Rava now intervenes with a counter-proposal. He reports: when I was in the house of Rav Nachman, *havina metalpelinan kanuna agav kitma* — we would move a copper vessel used to hold burning coals for warmth, together with its ashes. Before accepting this at face value, however, Rava first dismisses Abaye's explanation entirely. Two objections: first, excrement is genuinely disgusting, which is what allows the special *agaraf shel rei* exception; ashes are not disgusting and so the analogy fails. Second, the feces in a chamber pot are exposed, whereas the ashes here are enclosed within the covered vessel — and an enclosed vessel loses the urgency that drives the *agaraf shel rei* leniency. Both conditions are absent, so that category cannot save the ruling.

### Ash's Usefulness

The solution Rava offers, as Rashi clarifies, turns on the word *agav* — "together with." The key insight is that, contrary to appearances, it was not the ashes that posed the *muktza* problem. The ashes themselves, Rashi explains, had a legitimate use: *shehayu tzerichim l'afrah lechasos rok o tzoah* — they would use the ashes to cover spittle or excrement on the floor. Ashes spread over something unpleasant served a hygienic function. If a person customarily saved ashes for this purpose, the ashes were not *muktza* at all. In that case, both the vessel and the ashes are permitted. What then is the *chiddush* of the ruling?

### Wood Chips Resolution

The answer, which Rashi makes explicit, is that the vessel also contained unburned wood chips, and those wood chips are true *muktza* — they have no permitted use on Shabbat. The Gemara confirms this explicitly: *v'af al gav d'ika lashivay etzim* — even though there were wood chips in it. So the vessel is permitted, the ashes are permitted, and only the wood chips are *muktza*. The vessel is therefore a *basis* simultaneously to a permitted object (the ashes) and a prohibited object (the wood chips), and since it supports both, it may be moved. The entire discussion has been resolved by recognizing that the problematic element was not what it initially appeared to be.

## Wick Problem

### Oil & Wick Issue

The Gemara now raises a new objection from a *braita*: if a lamp contains a remnant of a burned-out wick, it may not be moved — *she'assur l'taltel*. This is a ruling that both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon agree on, according to Rashi. The difficulty is apparent. If the lamp contains both oil and a wick, the oil is a permitted object, and the wick is a *muktza* object. By the principle just established — that a *basis* to both a permitted and a prohibited thing may be moved — the lamp should be permitted. Why can it not be carried?

### Galilee Context

Abaye's answer is striking: *b'galila shanu* — this ruling was taught with respect to the Galilee. Rashi explains that in the Galilee, linen fabric was scarce and therefore even the remnant of a wick was considered a significant and valuable item: *she'ein bgadim shel pishtan metzuyim lahem*. Because of this, the wick remnant was far from negligible — it was a significant object in its own right. But this raises a puzzling reversal: the more significant the wick, one would expect the stronger the case that it constitutes genuine *muktza*, and hence the clearer the problem. Why does the significance of the wick increase the prohibition? If anything, a significant wick should simply make the question sharper.

### Tosafot's Logic

Tosafot offers a far superior explanation. The question of the Gemara, properly understood, is not why the wick is *muktza* — it plainly is — but why the oil does not neutralize the lamp's *basis* status. The lamp contains oil and a wick. The oil is permitted on Shabbat — one can use oil for many purposes. The wick is *muktza*. So the lamp should be a *basis* to a permitted object and a prohibited object, and should therefore be movable. Why not?

Tosafot's answer: *shehaya lahem rov shemen* — in the Galilee, oil was extremely abundant, so plentiful that it was considered cheap and insignificant. *Ul'chach lo batli shivei ptila agav shemen* — because the oil was so plentiful as to be nearly worthless, the remnant of the wick was actually the more significant object in the lamp. The lamp was therefore considered *primarily* a *basis* to the wick, not to the oil. And when the primary content of a *basis* is the *muktza* item, the *basis* is prohibited.

This reading aligns the case perfectly with the earlier ash-and-wood-chips discussion and makes the logic consistent throughout. The principle that emerges is that when determining whether a *basis* is being used for a permitted or prohibited purpose, the relative weight of those objects matters. If the *muktza* item is by far the more significant, the presence of a trivial permitted item does not transform the vessel into a *basis* to both and thereby render it movable.

### Gaon's Comment

Rabbeinu Nissim Gaon, whose commentary appears alongside this passage, provides a poignant historical footnote. He writes: *netarchu b'zeh hashinuy sheshina Abaye harbe min hachachamim v'lo shamati mehem klum v'lo matzati vo l'echad min hageonim perush* — many scholars labored over Abaye's peculiar answer of "in the Galilee," and he had not heard a satisfying explanation from any of them, nor found one in any of the Geonic literature. He eventually discovered an answer through his own diligent searching in Massekhet Nazir — the same answer that Tosafot would later arrive at, concerning the abundance of oil in the Galilee. The observation is a reminder of how hard-won these explanations were, and how much ground scholars had to cover across *Shas* to find the relevant material. Rabbeinu Nissim Gaon predates Tosafot, and whether Tosafot had access to his commentary is not established.

## Assembly & Construction

### Self-Assembly Bed

The Gemara shifts to a new topic, though one that echoes an earlier discussion of the *menorah shel chulyot*. Levi bar Shmuel found Rabbi Abba and Rav Huna bar Chiya standing at the entrance to Rav Huna's house, and asked them: *mahu lehachazir mittah shel tarsin be-Shabbat?* May one reassemble on Shabbat a bed of the sort used by traveling metalworkers — a collapsible, self-assembly bed whose pieces fit together in slots or holes? He was told it was permitted.

### Rav vs Shmuel

When they subsequently brought the question before Rav Yehuda, he was alarmed: both Rav and Shmuel had ruled that *hamachzir mittah shel tarsin be-Shabbat chayav chatat* — one who reassembles such a bed on Shabbat has committed a biblical transgression. The gap between the two positions could not be wider: one authority ruled it entirely permitted, while Rav and Shmuel declared it a violation carrying liability for a *chatat* offering.

### Candelabra Cases

The Gemara then brings what appears to be corroborating evidence for the strict view. A *braita* rules that one who reassembles the arms of a *menorah* on Shabbat is *chayav chatat*. What *melacha* is involved? Rashi notes that the principle of *ein binyan b'keilim* — construction does not apply to vessels — means this is not technically *boneh*. Rather, it falls under *makeh b'patish*, the act of completing a vessel or giving it its final usable form.

However, the *braita* then introduces a distinction: if the candelabra has extendable arms — sections that can be inserted to lengthen the reach, like the segments of a paint roller — then one should not reassemble them, but if one does so, one is exempt. And Rabbi Shimon adds a further refinement: a *keren agulah*, a circular type of horn or musical instrument, makes one *chayav*, while a *keren peshutah*, a straight type, leaves one merely exempt.

### Melacha Criteria

Rashi articulates the principles that distinguish these cases with precision. The circular instrument involves *maaseh uman* — it requires skilled craftsmanship — and must be inserted *b'chozek*, with force, in a tight fit. These two qualities — expert assembly and tight fitting — combine to produce a genuine *melacha*. The straight instrument, by contrast, is inserted loosely and is *darkan le-farkam tamid* — characteristically taken apart and reassembled on a regular basis, such that it has no single "finished" state. Because it is constantly adjusted and never permanently assembled, doing so carries no *chiyuv*.

### Loose Fitting Ruling

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel goes further: *im hayah rafeh, muttar* — if the fitting is loose, it is not only that one is exempt after the fact, but it is permitted *ab initio*. The Gemara concludes that Rav and Shmuel, who declared the bed case a *chiyuv chatat*, were speaking of a tightly assembled bed. The opinion that ruled it permitted must have been following Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view: the bed in question was a loose-fitting assembly.

Tosafot adds that it would be unusual for one position to declare something entirely permitted while another pronounces it a *d'oraisa* violation — they must have been addressing different factual situations. The tight case generates *chiyuv chatat*; the loose case is either *patur aval assur* according to the middle view, or fully permitted according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Practical Distinctions

These principles carry significant practical relevance. Several factors emerge as defining the boundary of the *melacha*: whether the assembly requires forcing or banging pieces together (*taka*); whether it involves a screw mechanism that tightens into a permanent fit; whether the item is assembled into a single finished state or is routinely adjusted in and out. The more clearly a construction involves tight, expert, permanent assembly, the more serious the concern. At the other end of the spectrum, things that are loosely fitted and habitually adjusted — such as a table leaf, which slides in and out easily and is not designed to remain permanently in one position — present little difficulty.

### Modern Examples

One might ask about common contemporary objects. Regarding toys such as Lego, the concern depends on how they are actually used. Standard Lego pieces that are regularly assembled and disassembled and not left in a fixed configuration present a weaker case; specialized model kits that are tightly fitted and left permanently assembled are more problematic. Mr. Potato Head, which involves loose, easily removable pieces that are constantly rearranged, presents no serious issue.

A clarinet, which comes in several distinct pieces that must be joined together before use, raises a more interesting question. Unlike a table leaf which occupies different states as a matter of course, the clarinet has a clear unassembled and assembled state — like the bed. However, the pieces are not forced together or screwed tightly; they are joined with a cork fitting, greased for smooth insertion, and the fit remains loose enough to adjust. Whether this brings it closer to the loose-fitting case that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits, or whether the functional finality of assembly militates toward at least *patur aval assur*, is a question the sugya raises without fully resolving for every scenario.

### Rava's Position

The Gemara closes this section with a practical application. At Rav Chama's house, there was a *mita galnisa* — a rolling or collapsible assembly bed — which Rava would reassemble on Yom Tov. One of the rabbis challenged him: was he perhaps relying on the principle of *binyan min ha-tzad* — constructing something in a sideways, indirect manner, which would constitute a *shinui* — or at least ensure that only a rabbinic and not a biblical prohibition was involved? But even rabbinic prohibition is still forbidden, the questioner noted.

Rava replied: *ana ke-Rabban Gamliel svira li de-amar im haya rafuy muttar* — I hold like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who ruled that if the fitting is loose it is entirely permitted. Rashi explains that Rava assembled the bed in a deliberately loose fashion. The *chiddush* of this ruling, as noted, is that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's leniency applies even to objects that have a clear finished/unfinished binary state — the key variable is the looseness of fit, not the nature of the assembly process per se.

## Spark Catching

### Vessel for Sparks

The final Mishnah of the chapter rules: *notnin kli tachat ha-ner le-kabbel nitzotzot* — one may place a vessel beneath a lamp to catch sparks, thereby preventing them from igniting the floor or furniture. However, *ve-lo yitten le-tocho mayim mipnei she-hu mechabbeh* — one may not put water in the vessel, because that causes extinguishing.

### Why Permitted

The first question the Gemara raises is how placing the vessel under the lamp is permitted at all. Does not the vessel then become a *basis* to the sparks that land in it — and would that not render the vessel prohibited, as it has now become a support for *muktza* matter?

### Sparks Negligible

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua answers: *nitzotzot ein bahem mamash* — sparks, once they die out, leave essentially nothing of substance. They produce a negligible residue that has no halachic weight and does not constitute the kind of *muktza* matter that would render the vessel a prohibited *basis*. The vessel may therefore be placed there and removed freely.

### Water Prohibition

The prohibition on water in the vessel is more complex. At first glance, the concern might appear to align with the position of Rabbi Yossi, who holds that *gram kibbuy assur* — even indirectly causing extinguishing is forbidden — and who does not permit even placing water-filled vessels around a spreading fire on Shabbat to let the fire extinguish itself. But the Gemara objects: Rabbi Yossi's ruling concerns actions taken *on* Shabbat. If the vessel of water were placed before Shabbat, Rabbi Yossi presumably would have no objection. Yet the *braita* states explicitly: *notnin kli tachat ha-ner le-kabbel nitzotzot be-Shabbat* — one may place the vessel on Shabbat itself, *ve-ein tzarich lomar me-erev Shabbat* — and certainly from before Shabbat. But the water is forbidden even *me-erev Shabbat*, certainly and *a fortiori* on Shabbat itself. This prohibition cannot therefore be derived from Rabbi Yossi's position regarding Shabbat actions. The *seder* of the *braita* implies the water is forbidden even before Shabbat begins.

### Proximity Issue

Rav Ashi provides the resolution: *shani hacha mipnei she-mekarev kibuya* — here is different, because placing the water brings the extinguishing close. This is not simply a case of *gram kibbuy* in Rabbi Yossi's sense; even the Rabbis, who generally permit indirect causation of extinguishing in cases of need, would agree this is problematic. According to Rashi, the very act of setting the water in place constitutes a form of *kibbuy*, since the extinguishing is directly enabled at the moment of placement.

### Floating Wicks

Tosafot takes issue with Rashi's formulation and offers its own reading. Tosafot understands the concern to be more specific: the fear is not that the water passively awaits a falling spark, but that the person might actively lift the vessel toward a falling spark, thereby participating directly in the extinguishing at the moment it occurs. It is this possibility of active involvement that renders placing water there forbidden even before Shabbat — lest one come to do it on Shabbat.

A practical question follows naturally from Tosafot's reasoning: what about the common practice of floating wicks in oil that rests atop water? When the oil is consumed, the wick reaches the water and the flame is extinguished. Is that not the same as placing water to cause extinguishing? Tosafot responds: *ain zeh kari kibuya b'yadav keivan she'eino mitkaven lekibbuy klal ella lehagbiya et ha-shemen* — this is not called extinguishing by one's hand, since the person has no intent toward extinguishing at all. The water is placed there to elevate and support the oil, not to extinguish the flame. The extinguishing, when it eventually occurs, is incidental to the entire arrangement. Because the intent and the design of the setup are directed toward keeping the flame burning — by keeping the oil at the right level — and not toward causing it to go out, there is no concern of *kibbuy*. Tosafot therefore concludes that this practice is permitted: *lo shayach lemigezar midi shema yaaseh be-Shabbat u-shari*.

## Insulation Intro

### Insulation Materials

The chapter concludes and the next *perek* opens with a new Mishnah: *bameh tommnin u-vameh ein tommnin* — in what materials may one insulate food before Shabbat to retain its heat, and in what may one not? The Mishnah enumerates a series of materials in which insulation is forbidden: *gefet* (grape pressings), *zevel* (dung), *melach* (salt), *sid* (plaster), *chol* (sand) — whether wet or dry — as well as straw, grape skins (*zogin*), cloth padding (*muchin*), and grasses when moist. Dry grasses, however, are permitted for insulation, since they do not generate the same degree of heat.

### Heat Generation Principle

The underlying principle is that the Rabbis prohibited insulating food before Shabbat in any material that actively generates heat — not merely retains heat, but adds to it. This stands in contrast to the previous chapter's topic, which concerned placing food on a flame before Shabbat. Both involve the concern that the food may be cooked or warmed inappropriately on Shabbat, but the *perek* of *hatmana* — insulation — addresses the distinct technique of wrapping food in insulating material.

### Ash Stirring Concern

The reason behind this prohibition is practical and well-grounded in rabbinic reasoning. The most effective heat-adding insulation material, especially in an era of wood-burning stoves and hearths, would be hot ashes. If one insulated one's food pot within hot ashes, the ashes would continue to radiate heat and potentially cook or raise the temperature of the food on Shabbat. The danger is that a person, noticing the temperature dropping, might be tempted to stir the ashes — *chatiya* — in order to increase the heat, which would constitute a prohibited act of kindling on Shabbat. To preempt this, the Rabbis enacted a broad prohibition against insulating in any heat-generating material before Shabbat. This is the theme to which the new *perek* is devoted, and it will be developed further in the coming discussions.
