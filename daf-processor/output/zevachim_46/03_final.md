# Zevachim 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul Overview

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִים עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם נוֹתָר וּמִשּׁוּם טָמֵא; חוּץ מִן הַדָּם. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּיב בְּדָבָר שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לֶאֱכוֹל, אֲבָל הָעֵצִים וְהַלְּבוֹנָה וְהַקְּטוֹרֶת אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Even with regard to those **items** enumerated in the previous mishna (42b) for **which one is not liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** e.g., the handful, the frankincense, and the incense, **one is,** nevertheless, **liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***notar*, and due to** violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated food while **ritually impure, except for the blood. Rabbi Shimon deems one liable for an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **eats** it. **But** with regard to **the wood, the frankincense, and the incense, one is not liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition against eating a consecrated item while **ritually impure.**

Today's daf begins two lines from the top, continuing the discussion of the *Mishnah*'s treatment of categories that invalidate a *korban*. The organizing theme is *piggul* — the disqualification of a sacrifice through improper intent regarding the time of consumption or burning — alongside two related categories: *notar* (sacrificial meat left past its permitted time, the consumption of which carries *kares*) and *tumah* (the prohibition, likewise carrying *kares*, of eating *kodashim* while ritually impure). All three categories share the common feature that violating them subjects one to *kares*, the divine punishment of excision.

### Piggul vs Notar/Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ חַיָּיבִים מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין – בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ? וְדִין הוּא; וּמָה פִּיגּוּל, שֶׁהוּא בִּקְבִיעָה וּבִידִיעָה אַחַת וְלֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ; טוּמְאָה, שֶׁהִיא בְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד וּבִשְׁתֵּי יְדִיעוֹת וְהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** It **might** have been thought that **one should be liable due to** violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated food while in a state of **ritual impurity only for an item that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar.** The *baraita* explains: **And** this may be derived via **a logical derivation: Just as** with regard to ***piggul*, which** renders one who unwittingly eats it liable to bring **a fixed** sin offering, **and** this liability is incurred **with one** change in his **awareness,** i.e., it suffices for the sinner to become aware after the fact that he had sinned unwittingly, **and** it has **no permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition, as there are no circumstances in which one is permitted to eat *piggul*, and yet **one is liable due to** violation of the prohibition against eating *piggul* **only for an item that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar,** so too, the same should certainly apply to the more lenient case of ritual impurity. **Impurity** is more lenient than *piggul*, **as** it renders the unwitting sinner liable only to bring **a sliding-scale offering,** which varies according to his financial circumstances: A poor person brings a bird offering or even a meal offering (see Leviticus 5:6–13). **And** liability is incurred only **with two** changes in his **awareness,** i.e., when the sinner was aware of his impurity beforehand, then forgot about it at the time of his sin, and then once again become aware of his impurity. **And** it has **permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition with regard to the community, as it is permitted to sacrifice communal offerings in the Temple in a state of impurity. With these leniencies in mind, **is it not right that one should be liable** due to violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated food while ritually impure **only for an item that has permitting factors, either for a person or for the altar.**

The critical distinction introduced here is one of scope. *Piggul* applies only to the body of the sacrifice — to what is eaten or burned — but not to the *matir*, the enabling agent that renders the sacrifice valid. The *dam* (blood), the *kometz* (the handful of flour), the *levonah* (frankincense), and similar items are *matirim*: they are what "permit" the remainder of the offering, whether for human consumption or for the altar. *Piggul* cannot apply to the *matir* itself. By contrast, *notar* and *tumah* have a broader application. They do apply to the *matir*, as we learned from yesterday's discussion, derived from the inclusive language of *asher yakdishu b'nei Yisrael* and *asher heim makdishim li*, which is read expansively to encompass these enabling elements.

### Blood as Matir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִן הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **except for the blood.**

The blood, however, stands in its own category. Even though *dam* is itself the *matir* — the very thing upon which the entire *korban* rests — it is not included in the extended application of *notar* and *tumah*. If blood left over were eaten, there is certainly a *kares* for eating blood, but there is no additional *kares* for *notar* on account of the blood, and no additional *kares* for *tumah*. The practical *nafka mina* (legal difference) would manifest, for example, in whether one must bring multiple *korbanot chatat* for inadvertent violations, or incur multiple sets of lashes if warned. The question the *Gemara* now takes up is: what is the source for this exclusion?

## Three Verses on Dam

### Ulla's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר עוּלָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וַאֲנִי נְתַתִּיו לָכֶם״ – שֶׁלָּכֶם יְהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? Before answering this question, the Gemara cites another discussion as to why the *halakha* of misuse of consecrated property does not apply to blood. **Ulla says: The verse states** with regard to blood: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, **and I have given it to you** upon the altar to make atonement for your souls" (Leviticus 17:11). "To you" indicates that **it shall be yours;** it is not the property of the Temple, so it is not subject to the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property.

*Amar Ulla amar kra*: the verse states, *v'ani nasati lachem al hamizbeach l'chaper* — "and I have given it to you upon the altar to atone" (Vayikra 17:11). The *drasha* reads *lachem* — "to you" — as implying *shelachem yehei*, it is yours. Since God declares the blood to be given over as it were to you, it does not fully belong to the sacred domain in the sense that would generate *me'ilah*. As *Rashi* notes, this *drasha* was originally articulated in a different context, to establish that there is no *me'ilah* by blood. The *Gemara* will show that we have three separate verses that exclude the blood from *me'ilah*, and from there the *Gemara* derives that all three exclusions are being put to work: one for *me'ilah*, one for *notar*, and one for *tumah*.

It is worth pausing to appreciate the striking theological irony here. The blood is not merely an element of the *korban* — it is, in the *Gemara*'s understanding, the sole agent of atonement. Rabbinic tradition insists, against what one might read from the narrative passages of the *Torah* with their emphasis on the *reiach nichoach* and the smoke rising heavenward, that the *korban* is fundamentally about the blood. The verse *v'ani nasati lachem al hamizbeach l'chaper* is itself the basis for that insistence. And yet it is precisely this verse that serves to remove the blood from the domain of *me'ilah* — to say that it is "yours," not fully consecrated in the technical sense. The one element without which the *korban* has no validity is the one element to which the most severe categories of sacred-property law do not adhere. Perhaps, as one might suggest, this connects to the idea of *ki hadam hu hanefesh*: if blood represents the life-force itself, it partakes of a different order than the merely physical components of the sacrifice, and therefore the technical statuses that adhere to physical sacred matter do not apply to it.

### Beis Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְכַפֵּר״ – לְכַפָּרָה נְתַתִּיו, וְלֹא לִמְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael** similarly **taught: The verse states: "To make atonement,"** teaching that God is saying: **I gave it for atonement, and not for** the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property.

*Dvei Rebbi Yishmael tana amar kra*: a separate *drasha* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael is brought on the word *l'chaper*. The verse gives the blood *l'chaparah*, for atonement, but not for *me'ilah* — *l'chaparah nasativ v'lo l'me'ilah*. It is designated to achieve atonement; transgressing its sanctity through benefit (*me'ilah*) is simply not within its scope.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא״ – הוּא לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה כִּלְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה; מָה אַחַר כַּפָּרָה אֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה, אַף לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה אֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that this *halakha* is derived from the latter part of **the verse,** which **states:** "For **it is** the blood that makes atonement for the soul" (Leviticus 17:11). The term "it is" teaches that the status of the blood remains as it is, i.e., **it is before atonement as** it is **after atonement.** As the Gemara will state, there is a principle that once the mitzva involving a consecrated item has been performed, the item is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property. Accordingly, the term "it is" teaches that **just as after atonement,** i.e., after the blood has been presented on the altar, it is **not subject to** the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property, as the mitzva has already been performed, **so too, before atonement,** i.e., before the blood has been presented on the altar, **it is not subject to** the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a third approach, based on the verse *ki hadam hu banefesh yechaper* (Vayikra 17:11): *hu lifnei chaparah ke'achar chaparah* — the blood has the same legal status before it achieves atonement (i.e., before it is placed on the altar) as after it achieves atonement. Since after the blood is placed on the altar there is no *me'ilah*, the same is true before it is placed on the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה כְּלִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה – מָה לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה יֵשׁ בּוֹ מְעִילָה, אַף לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה יֵשׁ בּוֹ מְעִילָה! אֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית מִצְוָתוֹ וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if the term "it is" teaches that the status of the blood remains the same before and after atonement, one can **say** just the opposite: It is **after atonement as** it is **before atonement. Just as before atonement** the blood **is subject to** the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property, **so too after atonement it is subject to** the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property. The Gemara rejects this: This cannot be the case, as there is a principle: **There is no item whose mitzva has been performed that is** still **subject to** the prohibition of **misusing** consecrated property.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning: why does it follow that after the blood is on the altar there is no *me'ilah*? Why not read the equation in reverse — since normally items designated for a *korban* carry *me'ilah* before their function is performed, perhaps the verse is equating the two and saying that even after the blood is placed on the altar, *me'ilah* still applies? *Eima le'achar chaparah kelifnei chaparah: ma lifnei chaparah yesh bo me'ilah af le'achar chaparah yesh bo me'ilah.*

The *Gemara* answers with a general principle: *ein lecha davar shena'aseit mitzvato u'mo'alin bo* — there is nothing, once its *mitzvah* is completed (*ne'aseit mitzvato*), regarding which *me'ilah* still applies. This principle has a clear logic. *Me'ilah* by items designated for the altar — *kodshei mizbeach* — is not simply a matter of appropriating Temple property (which is the character of *me'ilah* by *bedek habayit*). It is rather a matter of diverting a sacred object from its designated purpose. Once that purpose is fulfilled, the object's sanctified designation is, as it were, exhausted, and the prohibition of *me'ilah* no longer applies. An analogous principle operates with respect to *chametz* that one has fulfilled the *mitzvah* of burning: once burned, it is no longer *asur behana'ah*, because *ne'aseit mitzvato* — its designated fate has been accomplished.

## Ne'asita Mitzvato

### Principle Explained

The general principle that once a *mitzvah* is complete *me'ilah* no longer applies is established here as the background rule. With respect to blood, once it is placed on the altar — *ne'aseit mitzvato* — there is certainly no *me'ilah*, and Rabbi Yochanan's verse establishes that the same is therefore true even before it reaches the altar.

### Three Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וַהֲרֵי תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן וּבִגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is there **no** such case? **But there is** the mitzva of **the removal of the ashes** of offerings burned on the altar. Any benefit derived from them between their removal and their required burial constitutes misuse of consecrated property, despite the fact that their mitzva has already been performed. The Gemara answers: The principle does not apply in that case, **because** the matter of **the removal of the ashes and** the matter of the **priestly vestments,** the four white garments worn by the High Priest on Yom Kippur, are both subject to the *halakha* that misuse of consecrated property applies to them even after their mitzva has been performed. Consequently, they **are two verses that come as one,** i.e., they share a unique *halakha* not found elsewhere. **And** there is a principle: **Any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common element to apply to other cases. Instead, they are considered exceptional instances that cannot serve as models for other cases.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges the universality of the *ne'aseit mitzvato* principle: *ve-harei trumat hadeshen* — what about the removal of the ashes? The *Torah* prescribes that the *kohen* remove a portion of the ashes from the outer altar and place them beside the eastern side of the ramp (*vesamo etzel hamizbeach*). Once this act is performed, the *mitzvah* is complete. Yet the *drasha* derived from the word *vesamo* — "and he shall place them there" — establishes that they must remain there and may not be put to any other use. Here is a case of *ne'aseit mitzvato* where the prohibition endures afterward.

The *Gemara* answers: *mishum dehavah trumat hadeshen u'bigdei kehunah shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad* — because we have two exceptional cases: *trumat hadeshen* and the priestly garments worn by the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. Regarding the latter, the verse says *vehinicham sham* — "and he shall leave them there" — indicating that these garments may not be reused. The rule of *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* applies: when the *Torah* needs to spell out a rule in two separate places, it signals that those are exceptions, not illustrations of a general principle. Thus the *ne'aseit mitzvato* rule stands for everything else, including blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי ״וְהִנִּיחָם שָׁם״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁטְּעוּנִין גְּנִיזָה. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא, דְּאָמַר: מוּתָּרוֹת הֵן לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אַחֵר – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן וְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִין הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that the verse: "And he shall take off the linen garments, which he wore when he went into the Sanctuary, **and shall leave them there"** (Leviticus 16:23), **teaches that** the four white garments worn by the High Priest on Yom Kippur are not fit for further use, and **they require interment. But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa, who says** that these priestly vestments **are fit for an ordinary priest** and do not require interment, **provided that** a High Priest **does not use them on Yom Kippur** in **a different** year, one does not misuse consecrated property by using them after their mitzva has been performed, and therefore **what is there to say?** In his opinion, the *halakha* of misuse of consecrated property after the performance of a mitzva applies only to the removal of ashes from the altar, not to the priestly vestments, which means it is stated in only a single case. Why, then, does this case not serve as a paradigm for other instances in the Torah? It is **because** the cases of **the removal of the ashes and** the *halakha* of **the heifer whose neck is broken** are **two verses that come as one,** as it is prohibited to derive benefit from either of them even after their mitzva is completed, **and any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common element to apply to other cases.

The *Gemara* notes the substance of the *bigdei kehunah* case further: the standard rabbinic view is that the *kohen gadol*'s *Yom Kippur* garments require *genizah* (burial), which implies that *me'ilah* continues to apply to them even after the service. The dissenting view of Rabbi Dosa holds that they may actually be used by an ordinary *kohen*, provided they are not worn on another *Yom Kippur* — the restriction being that each *Yom Kippur* must be served with its own unique set of garments, a principle in its own right.

### Debate on Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְלַמְּדִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? תְּרֵי מִיעוּטֵי כְּתִיבִי; הָכָא כְּתִיב: ״הָעֲרוּפָה״, וְהָתָם כְּתִיב: ״וְשָׂמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this from a different angle: **This works out well according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one **do not teach** their common element to apply to other cases, **but according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one do **teach** their common element to apply to other cases, **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: **Two exclusions are written** in these two cases, which indicate that this *halakha* applies to them alone. **Here,** with regard to the heifer whose neck is broken, **it is written: "Whose neck was broken"** (Deuteronomy 21:6), and this superfluous description teaches that this *halakha*, that the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property is in effect even after the performance of a mitzva, applies solely to this case and should not be extended to others. **And there,** with regard to the removal of ashes, **it is written: "And he shall put it"** (Leviticus 6:3), indicating that this *halakha* applies to "it," and nothing else.

The *Gemara* then considers: if one follows Rabbi Dosa and does not accept *bigdei kehunah* as an independent case of *ne'aseit mitzvato* where the prohibition endures, then we have only one exception (*trumat hadeshen*), and perhaps the principle of *ne'aseit mitzvato* is not well established enough to serve as the background rule from which we derive the status of blood. The *Gemara* responds that even according to Rabbi Dosa, there is a second exception: *eglah arufah*. By the calf whose neck is broken in the valley (*ve'arfu sham et ha'eglah banachal*), the *Torah* uses the same word *sham* to indicate that it must be left there and may not be used for any benefit. Though *eglah arufah* is not technically a case of *me'ilah* — it is not a *korban* — it is a case of a sacred act (*mitzvah*) whose completion does not dissolve the object's prohibited status. The *Gemara* broadens the principle accordingly: there are things where the *ne'aseit mitzvato* rule does not dissolve their prohibited status, but these are clearly marked as exceptional. Therefore, blood follows the general rule.

### Resolution via Extra Letters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְלָתָא קְרָאֵי בְּדָם לְמָה לִי? לְמַעוֹטֵי מִמְּעִילָה, מִנּוֹתָר וּמִטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **these three verses** stated **with regard to blood,** from which it is derived that the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property does not apply to blood? The reference is to the three terms specified earlier, in the expositions of Ulla, the school of Rabbi Yishmael, and Rabbi Yoḥanan, from the verse: "I have given it to you upon the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement for the soul" (Leviticus 17:11). The Gemara answers: One of those terms serves **to exclude** the blood **from** the prohibition of misuse of consecrated property. Another phrase serves to exclude blood **from** the prohibition of ***notar*.** If one consumed leftover blood, he is not liable for consuming *notar*. Rather, he is liable for violating only the prohibition against consuming blood. **And** the last phrase serves to exclude it **from** the prohibition of **ritual impurity.** If one consumed this blood in a state of ritual impurity, he is liable only for consuming blood, but not on account of consuming consecrated food while ritually impure.

The *Gemara* then addresses a further refinement. The principle of *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — that two isolated verses are not applied broadly — is itself debated. According to the view that *ein melamdin* requires two verses, the two we have (*trumat hadeshen* and *eglah arufah* under Rabbi Dosa) suffice to prevent generalization, and blood is confirmed as following the rule. But according to the view that three verses are needed before one stops generalizing (*melammadin* — two verses can still establish a rule), we need all three cases — *trumat hadeshen*, *bigdei kehunah*, and *eglah arufah* — to prevent the principle from being applied to blood.

The *Gemara* resolves this through *trei mi'utei ketivi* — there are two extra letters in the relevant verses that mark these cases as uniquely exceptional. By *eglah arufah* the verse writes *ha'arufah* (with the definite article *heh*), marking that broken-necked calf specifically. By *trumat hadeshen* the verse writes *vesamo* (with the full form *vav-shin-mem-vav*), not merely *vesham* — the fuller form signals exclusivity. These textual markers establish that those cases, and only those cases, retain their prohibited status after *ne'aseit mitzvato*. Blood therefore follows the general rule without exception.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל פִּיגּוּל לָא צְרִיךְ קְרָא, דִּתְנַן: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, בֵּין לָאָדָם בֵּין לַמִּזְבֵּחַ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל. וְדָם גּוּפֵיהּ מַתִּיר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But no verse is required** to exclude this blood from the *halakha* of ***piggul*,** because this is already derived from another source, **as we learned** in the mishna (43a): With regard to **any** item **that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar, one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.** The permitting factor itself is not subject to *piggul*. **And** consequently, *piggul* does not apply to **blood itself,** as **it** renders the offering **permitted** for human consumption or for the altar.

We thus have three biblical sources establishing that there is no *me'ilah* by blood: (1) *v'ani nasati lachem* — it is given to you, implying *shelachem yehei*; (2) *l'chaparah nasativ v'lo l'me'ilah* — it is given for atonement, not for *me'ilah*; and (3) *hu lifnei chaparah ke'achar chaparah* — the equation of before and after the altar, combined with the *ne'aseit mitzvato* principle. These three verses in turn serve the additional purpose of excluding blood from *notar* and from *tumah*: one verse for each category.

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* here raises a genuine difficulty. There is a *Gemara* elsewhere that discusses the blood of sacrifices being channeled through a conduit out to *Nachal Kidron*, where it was sold as fertilizer, and that passage implies that *me'ilah* did apply to that blood before it was formally redeemed. *Tosafot* offers several answers — among them the possibility that that *me'ilah* was only *derabbanan* — but the tension is worth noting and the matter is not entirely resolved.

## Three Verses on Tumah

### Three Verses Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה כָּרֵיתוֹת בִּשְׁלָמִים לָמָּה?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Why do I** need **three** mentions of ***karet* with regard to** one who eats **peace offerings** in a state of ritual impurity? The three verses are: "But the soul that eats of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings, that pertain to the Lord, having his impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off [*venikhreta*] from his people" (Leviticus 7:20); "And when anyone shall touch any impure item, whether it is the impurity of man, or an impure animal, or any unclean detestable thing, and eat of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings, that pertain to the Lord, that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:21); and: "Whoever he is of all your seed among your generations that approaches the sacred items, which the children of Israel consecrate to the Lord, having his impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off from before Me; I am the Lord" (Leviticus 22:3).

Having established the three exclusions for blood, the *Gemara* now turns to a related set of three verses, cited by Rabbi Yochanan, dealing with *tumah* and *kares*. Why are three separate verses in the *Torah* needed to establish that one incurs *kares* for eating *kodashim* while *tamei*?

The relevant passages are: twice in Vayikra 7 (verses 20 and 21), and once in Vayikra 22 (verse 3), all relating to the *shelamim* and to *kodashim* more broadly. The verse at 7:20 — *v'hanefesh asher tochal basar mizevach hashlamim asher l'Hashem v'tumaso alav v'nichresa hanefesh hahi mei'ameha* — deals with one who is *tamei* eating from the *shelamim*. The adjacent verse at 7:21 — *v'nefesh ki tiga b'chol tamei... v'achal mizevach hashlamim asher l'Hashem v'nichresa* — similarly refers to a person who has come into contact with various sources of *tumah*. And the verse at Vayikra 22:3 — *kol ish asher yikrav mikol zar'achem el hakodashim asher yakdishu v'tumaso alav v'nichresa* — uses more general language about *kodashim*.

The answer given is: one verse establishes the principle for *shelamim*; one repeats the point by *shelamim* in a way that — following the rule of *davar shehayah bikhlal veyatza min haklal lilamed* — signals that the *halacha* applies to all sacrifices, not only *shelamim*; and a third verse establishes that the prohibition applies even to things that are *einam ne'echalim*, inedible items offered on the altar — the *ketores*, the *kometz*, and similar items. This last extension is a considerable *chiddush*: one would incur *kares* for eating in a state of *tumah* something that is itself inherently inedible.

### Rabbi Shimon Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת לִכְלָל, וְאַחַת לִפְרָט, וְאַחַת לִדְבָרִים שֶׁאֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין. וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָלִין – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי חַטָּאוֹת הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **One** mention of *karet* in the verse: "That approaches the sacred items," is **for a generalization, and one** mention in the verse: "Of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings," is **for a detail.** This detail serves to include all items consecrated to the altar, in accordance with the hermeneutical principle that any matter that was included in a generalization but was explicitly specified to teach a certain *halakha* was intended to teach not just about itself but about the entire generalization. **And one** mention of *karet* in the verse: "And when anyone shall touch any impure item," serves **to** include **items whose** typical manner is such that they are **not to be eaten,** e.g., wood and frankincense, and teaches that one who eats even these items is liable to receive *karet*. The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** in the mishna with regard to **items whose** typical manner is such that they are **not to be eaten,** that one **is not liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated items in a state of **ritual impurity, what** does this last mention of *karet* serve **to include?** The Gemara answers that it serves **to include the sin offerings** whose blood is presented **inside** the Sanctuary.

But the *Mishnah* we encountered yesterday included Rabbi Shimon's dissenting position: *mechayyev b'davar shedarko le'echol* — Rabbi Shimon holds that *kares* for eating *kodashim* while *tamei* applies only to things normally eaten, not to inedible items. So for Rabbi Shimon, the third verse cannot be serving to include inedible items. What then is the third verse teaching? The *Gemara* answers: *l'asuyei chatas hapnimiyot* — to include the inner *chatat*, whose blood is applied to the inner altar rather than the outer one. One might have thought that since Rabbi Shimon excludes the inner *chatat* from *piggul* (because its blood does not go on the outer *mizbeach* as the *shelamim*'s does), the same exemption would extend to *tumah*. The third verse therefore comes to establish that even according to Rabbi Shimon, one incurs *kares* for eating the inner *chatat* while *tamei*.

### Amoraim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן כִּשְׁלָמִים – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה נָמֵי לָא לִיחַיַּיב; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why this inclusion is necessary. It might **enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Shimon says** that concerning **any** offering **whose** blood **is not** presented **on the external altar like** that of **a peace offering, one is not liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** one should likewise **not be liable** for eating it **due to** violation of the prohibition of **ritual impurity.** Therefore, the additional mention of *karet* **teaches us** that one who is impure is in fact liable for eating these sin offerings brought inside the Sanctuary.

The *Gemara* now presents two versions of an *Amora'ic* debate — reported in one version by Rav Tavyumi and in another by Rav Kahana — concerning the exact scope of the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Chachamim*.

The debate turns on two distinct situations: *tumat haguf* (the person himself is *tamei*) and *tumat basar* (the sacrificial meat itself is *tamei*). For the latter case there is a separate verse — *v'habasar asher yiga b'chol tamei lo ye'achel* (Vayikra 7:19) — and the *drasha* reads the *vav* of *v'habasar* as inclusive, expanding the prohibition to cover even inedible items (*eitzim u'levonah*). There is therefore more textual basis to argue that the prohibition when the *basar* is *tamei* applies even to inedible things.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – חַד מֵהַאי זוּזָא וְחַד מֵהַאי זוּזָא; חַד אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. וְחַד אָמַר: כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that two pairs of *amora'im* disagreed with regard to this issue; one pair is **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish,** and the other pair is **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina.** The two different opinions that follow were respectively stated by **one of this pair and one of that pair.** **One says:** The **dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon is **with regard to the impurity of meat,** i.e., the item that is eaten is impure. The Rabbis maintain that one who eats impure wood or frankincense is flogged, as derived from the two mentions of flesh in the verse: "And the meat that touches any impure item shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire. And as for the meat, everyone who is pure may eat of it" (Leviticus 7:19). **But** if one ate wood or frankincense **in** a state of **impurity of the body, everyone agrees** that **he is not flogged.** **And one says: As** there is **a dispute with regard to this** case, when the item eaten is impure, **so** is there **a dispute with regard to that** case, when the one eating the wood or frankincense is impure.

One position in the *Amora'ic* debate holds: *machloket b'tumat basar* — the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Chachamim* is specifically about *tumat basar*, where the *drasha* might extend the prohibition to inedible items. But *b'tumat haguf divrei hakol ein loke* — when it is the person who is *tamei*, there is no textual basis to extend the prohibition to inedible items, and even the *Chachamim* would agree with Rabbi Shimon that one is exempt.

The other position holds: *k'machloket b'zo kach machloket b'zo* — the same debate applies to both scenarios. The reasoning is that the two *pesukim* are juxtaposed and interconnected: *keivan d'karinan bei v'habasar asher yiga b'chol tamei* to include inedible items, *karinan bei v'tumaso alav* — the adjacent verse about personal *tumah* is thereby linked, and the same extension follows in both directions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי מַתְנֵי הָכִי; רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי: חַד מֵהַאי זוּזָא וְחַד מֵהַאי זוּזָא אַסֵּיפָא – חַד אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לוֹקֶה; וְחַד אָמַר: כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Rav Tavyumei teaches** the discussion in **this** preceding manner, whereas **Rav Kahana teaches** it in **this** alternative manner: **One of this pair** of *amora'im* **and one of that pair** disagreed **with regard to the latter clause** in the mishna, i.e., the meaning of Rabbi Shimon's statement. **One says** that Rabbi Shimon's **dispute** with the Rabbis is **with regard to the impurity of the body,** as he maintains that an impure individual who eats wood or frankincense is exempt. **But with regard to the impurity of flesh,** i.e., these items themselves, **everyone agrees** that one is **flogged. And one says: As** there is **a dispute with regard to this** case, **so** is there **a dispute with regard to that** case, i.e., Rabbi Shimon holds that one is exempt in both cases.

The same structure appears in reverse in Rav Kahana's version, where the question is which case Rabbi Shimon — the one exempting — is speaking about. One version says Rabbi Shimon exempts only in the case of *tumat haguf*, while even he would agree that when the *basar* is *tamei* one incurs *malkot* because of the explicit *drasha* on that verse. The other version again holds *k'machloket b'zo kach machloket b'zo*.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּלָא קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ ״וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו וְנִכְרְתָה״, לָא קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It **stands to reason** that the correct interpretation is **like the one who says: As** there is **a dispute with regard to this** case, **so** is there **a dispute with regard to that** case. **What is the reason? Since one does not read with regard to** these inedible items: **"Having his impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off,"** as according to both alternative explanations Rabbi Shimon deems exempt one who eats these items when he is in a state of impurity, similarly **one does not read with regard to** them the previous verse: **"And the flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten."**

Rava rules: *mistabra k'man d'amar k'machloket b'zo kach machloket b'zo* — it stands to reason that the two cases rise and fall together. His reasoning is the mirror image of the argument above: *keivan d'lo karinan bei v'tumaso alav v'nichresa, lo karinan bei v'habasar asher yiga b'chol tamei lo ye'achel* — since Rabbi Shimon reads the verse about personal *tumah* as not extending to inedible things, he similarly reads the verse about the *basar*'s *tumah* as not extending there. The two are textually linked, and Rabbi Shimon's exemption encompasses both.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר״ – לְרַבּוֹת עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה! (לְפוֹסְלָהּ) [לִפְסוּלָא] בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But doesn't the Master say** that the term: **"And as for the flesh,"** in the same verse, serves **to include wood and frankincense?** This indicates that even these items can become ritually impure, and the prohibition against eating consecrated items that are impure applies to them. The Gemara answers: This is referring **merely to disqualification,** i.e., this inclusion applies by rabbinic law, and the verse is cited in mere support.

The *Gemara* then raises a final difficulty: how can Rabbi Shimon exempt in the case of *tumat basar* given the classic *drasha* that *v'habasar* includes *eitzim u'levonah*? The answer: *psula b'alma* — that inclusion yields only a rabbinic-level prohibition, not a biblical one. Intrinsically, the idea of transgressing through eating something inedible is conceptually strained. One might suggest in passing that the framework borrowed from elsewhere in the *Gemara* — comparing *achilat mizbeach* with *achilat adam* — could be relevant: what the altar "consumes" might in some sense be considered "eaten" even if a human cannot literally eat it. But as the *Gemara* stands, Rabbi Shimon does not recognize a biblical-level prohibition in such a case.

## Six Intentions

### Six Intentions Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לְשֵׁם שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים הַזֶּבַח נִזְבָּח: לְשֵׁם זֶבַח, לְשֵׁם זוֹבֵחַ, לְשֵׁם הַשֵּׁם, לְשֵׁם אִשִּׁים, לְשֵׁם רֵיחַ, לְשֵׁם נִיחוֹחַ. וְהַחַטָּאת וְהָאָשָׁם – לְשֵׁם חֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The slaughtered offering is slaughtered for the sake of six matters,** and one must have all of these matters in mind: **For the sake of** the particular type of **offering** being sacrificed; **for the sake of the one who sacrifices** the offering; **for the sake of God; for the sake of** consumption by the **fires** of the altar; **for the sake of** the **aroma; for the sake of** the **pleasing** of God, i.e., in fulfillment of God's will; **and,** in the cases of **a sin offering and a guilt offering, for the sake of** atonement for the **sin.**

We now arrive at the *Mishnah* that opens a new section of the perek. One might argue that this *Mishnah* — or the entire following chapter of *Eizehu Mekoman* — ought to have come first in the tractate, as it lays out the basic affirmative framework for how a *korban* is properly offered. The placement of this foundational material after all the discussions of invalidity reflects the *Gemara*'s orientation toward the rabbinic class rather than the uninitiated: the interesting cases of disqualification are treated first, and the basic rules follow.

*Leshem shisha devarim hazevach nizbach* — the sacrifice is offered with six things in mind. The *Mishnah* lists: *leshem zevach* — that it should be sacrificed as the right type of *korban* (an *olah* should not be intended as a *shelamim*, which connects to the *Mishnah*'s opening discussion of *shinui shemo*); *leshem zoveach* — for the sake of the owner (*shinui be'alim*); *leshem Hashem* — with awareness that it is being offered to God; *leshem ishim* — with awareness that part of it will be offered on the fire of the altar; *leshem reiach* — with the intent that smoke will rise; and *leshem nichoach* — that it should be a pleasing offering.

The final two items on this list mark a notable shift in the texture of the discussion. The *Mishnah* here is operating in a different register than the technical-halachic discussions of blood, *piggul*, and *me'ilah*. These intentions speak to the relational dimension of offering a sacrifice: the consciousness of the offerer, the direction toward God, the desire that the offering ascend and please. The stories of sacrifice throughout the *Torah* — *vayarach Hashem et reiach hanichoach* — live here. The *Chachamim* acknowledge this dimension, even as they situate it as secondary to the strictly operative elements of *lishma* and *leshem be'alim*. The tractate has, in effect, kept the relational framing in reserve, and now presents it — but only briefly, before returning to the technical.

*Vechattat ve'asham leshem cheit* — and the *chattat* and *asham* must be brought with conscious intent toward the specific sin for which they atone.

### Rabbi Yose's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַף מִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ לְשֵׁם אֶחָד מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ – כָּשֵׁר; שֶׁהוּא תְּנַאי בֵּית דִּין, שֶׁאֵין הַמַּחְשָׁבָה הוֹלֶכֶת אֶלָּא אַחַר הָעוֹבֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even** in the case of **one who did not have in mind** to slaughter the offering **for the sake of any one of these,** the offering is **valid, as it is a stipulation of the court** that he should not state any intent *ab initio*. This is necessary **because the intent follows only the one performing the** sacrificial **rite,** and therefore if the one who slaughters the animal is not careful and states the wrong intent, the offering would be disqualified through his improper intent.

Rabbi Yose offers a broad qualification: *af mi shelo hayah belibo leshem echad mikol elu kasher* — even one who had none of these six things consciously in mind, the sacrifice is nonetheless *kasher*. The basis: *shehu tenai beit din* — it is a standing stipulation of the court (a *tenai beit din*) that this is so. And further: *she'ein hamachshavah holeches ela achar ha'oved* — the *machshavah* follows only the one performing the service.

The relationship between these two clauses is not immediately obvious, and *Rashi* reads the second clause as a somewhat independent addendum: not only is *stam lishmah* acceptable, but even if the owner had a wrong intent, that does not invalidate, because the operative *machshavah* is that of the *kohen*, not the owner.

### Alternative Readings

Two alternative readings are worth examining closely. The *Rambam* in his *Peirush HaMishnayot* suggests that the *Tanna Kamma* may have implicitly assumed that it is the owner's intent that governs — and that Rabbi Yose is therefore correcting this: even if the owner had none of these thoughts, the sacrifice is *kasher*, because the *machshavah* does not follow the owner but rather the one performing the *avodah*. On this reading, the *tenai beit din* is not simply about *stam lishmah*, but is making the more fundamental point that the owner's intent is legally inoperative.

A third reading — which may find support in the *Tosefta* — turns on the word *nizbach* in the opening phrase *leshem shisha devarim hazevach nizbach*. *Lizboach* is the act of *shechitah*, and *shechitah* does not require a *kohen* — anyone may perform it. The term *oved* in the final clause, by contrast, refers to the one performing the *avodah*, which is specifically priestly service (the *kabbalah*, *holachah*, and *zerikah* of the blood). On this reading, the *Tanna Kamma* may have held that the operative *machshavah* is that of the *shochet*, while Rabbi Yose holds it is only the *kohen* performing the blood service whose intent governs. This connects to the *Gemara*'s opening *sugyot* in the tractate, where we derived that the rules of *shelo lishmo* apply to each stage of the *avodah* in turn — which, on this reading, may have been resolving an actual historical debate rather than merely extending an agreed principle.

There is something additionally interesting about the *shochet* reading. In practice, the *shochet* at the Temple was often the owner himself, since *shechitah* did not require priestly status. Thus holding that the operative *machshavah* is that of the *shochet* is not entirely different from the position that the owner's intent governs — it situates the *machshavah* in the same person, but specifically at the moment of that person's act within the ritual framework. Rabbi Yose then removes that power from the *shochet-owner* entirely and locates it solely in the *kohen* performing the subsequent blood service.

In the end the accepted position is that *machshavah* governs at all stages — *shechitah* and the subsequent *avodot* — but this excludes the owner when not performing an *avodah*.

## Intent in Detail

### Chattat Intent Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: ״עוֹלָה״ – לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים דְּלָא. ״אִשִּׁים״ – לְשֵׁם אִשִּׁים, לְאַפּוֹקֵי כְּבָבָא דְּלָא. ״רֵיחַ״ – לְשֵׁם רֵיחַ, לְאַפּוֹקֵי אֵבָרִים שֶׁצְּלָאָן וְהֶעֱלָן – דְּלָא. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֵבָרִים שֶׁצְּלָאָן וְהֶעֱלָן – אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם רֵיחַ. ״נִיחוֹחַ״ – לְשֵׁם הֲנָחַת רוּחַ. ״לַה׳״ – לְשֵׁם מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says** with regard to the verse: "A burnt offering, an offering made by fire, of a pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Leviticus 1:9), the term **"a burnt offering"** means that one must intend that it be **for the sake of a burnt offering, to the exclusion of** one who sacrifices a burnt offering **for the sake of a peace offering, which** one may **not** do. The mention of **fires,** i.e., the term "an offering made by fire," teaches that the offering must be sacrificed **for the sake of** complete consumption by the **fires** of the altar, **to the exclusion of** one who intends that its portions should only be **roasted** on the fire, **as** one may **not** do so. Rav continues to expound the verse. The term **"aroma"** indicates that the offering must be sacrificed **for the sake of** the **aroma, to the exclusion of limbs that one** first **roasted and** afterward **took them up** to the altar, **which** he may **not** do. **As Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **limbs that one** first **roasted and** afterward **took them up** to the altar, **they do not constitute** fulfillment of the requirement that the offering must be sacrificed for the sake of the **aroma.** The term **"pleasing"** means **for the sake of pleasing** God's **spirit,** i.e., that the offering should be accepted by God. Finally, the expression **"to the Lord"** indicates that it should be **for the sake of the One Who spoke and the world came into being.**

It is worth noting that the *Gemara* treats these elements — the relational, direction-toward-God aspects of the offering — efficiently and then moves immediately to the more technically operative material. The entire dimension of *reiach nichoach* as a framework for understanding what a *korban* is receives, in effect, a brief acknowledgment and is then set aside.

Now the *Gemara* turns to the more technically operative question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה – פְּסוּלָה. לְשֵׁם חוּלִּין – כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? ״וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת קׇדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – קֳדָשִׁים מְחַלְּלִין קֳדָשִׁים, וְאֵין חוּלִּין מְחַלְּלִין קֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **a sin offering that one slaughtered** with intent that the slaughter be **for the sake of a burnt offering,** it is **disqualified.** As explained (2a), a sin offering that was slaughtered for the sake of another offering is entirely disqualified. But if he intended the slaughter to be **for the sake of a non-sacred** animal, it is **valid. Rabbi Elazar says: What is the reason of Rav?** The verse states: **"And they shall not profane the sacred items of the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 22:15). This teaches that **consecrated items,** i.e., the intent to slaughter an offering for the sake of another type of offering, **profane consecrated items, but** the intent to slaughter an offering for the sake of **a non-sacred** animal **does not profane consecrated items.**

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: a *chattat* that was slaughtered with the intent that it be *leshem olah* — for the sake of an *olah* — is *psulah*. A *chattat* that was slaughtered with the intent that it be *leshem chulin* — for the sake of ordinary, non-sacred meat — is *kesheirah*.

### Why Intent Matters

Rav Elazar explains the reasoning: *velo yechalelu et kodshei b'nei Yisrael* — one category of *kodashim* desecrates another category of *kodashim*, while ordinary *chulin* cannot desecrate *kodashim*. The intent to make a *chattat* into a different *korban* is a relevant religious act of misdirection; the intent to make it into *chulin* is so categorically outside the domain of the sacred that it has no operative force. The thought simply has no traction.

### Rabba's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבָּה, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַף מִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ לְשֵׁם אֶחָד מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁהוּא תְּנַאי בֵּית דִּין. טַעְמָא שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ כְּלָל, הָא הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ לְשֵׁם חוּלִּין – פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba raises an objection** from the mishna: **Rabbi Yosei says: Even** in the case of **one who did not have in mind** to slaughter the offering **for the sake of any one of these,** the offering is **fit, as it is a stipulation of the court** that one not state any intent *ab initio*. Rabba infers from this that **the reason** the offering is valid and it effects acceptance is that **he did not have in mind** any intent **at all. But** if he **had in mind** to slaughter the offering **for the sake of a non-sacred** animal, it is **disqualified.**

Rabba challenges from the *Mishnah*. Rabbi Yose ruled: *af mi shelo hayah belibo leshem echad mikol elu kasher* — even with no intent at all, the sacrifice is *kasher*. This is the principle of *stam lishmah*. Reading the implication: *ta'ama shelo hayah belibo klal* — the reason it is *kasher* is that there was no active thought at all. *Ha hayah belibo leshem chulin pasul* — this implies that if one did actively think *leshem chulin*, it would be *pasul*. So Rabba challenges Rav: Rabbi Yose seems to indicate that *leshem chulin* intent does invalidate.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּלְמָא לֹא הָיָה כְּלָל – כָּשֵׁר וּמְרַצֶּה, הָא הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ לְשֵׁם חוּלִּין – כָּשֵׁר וְאֵינוֹ מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: **Perhaps** Rabbi Yosei means the following: If he **did not have** in mind any intent **at all,** the offering is **valid and** it **effects acceptance, but** if he **had in mind** to slaughter the offering **for the sake of a non-sacred** animal, the offering is **valid, but it does not effect acceptance,** as it is a flawed offering. Rav's statement can be understood in a similar fashion.

Abaye responds: *dilma lo hayah klal kasher u'meratzeh* — perhaps the reading is that with no intent at all, the sacrifice is both *kasher* and *meratzeh* (accepted on behalf of the owner, fulfilling the owner's obligation). *Ha hayah belibo leshem chulin kasher ve'eino meratzeh* — whereas if intent was *leshem chulin*, the sacrifice is still *kasher* (the meat remains permitted, no technical disqualification applies), but it is not *meratzeh*, not *olah l'be'alim leshem chovah*.

This establishes an interesting intermediate category specifically for *chattat*. Normally a *chattat shelo lishmo* is fully *pasul* — the meat cannot be eaten. But a *chattat leshem chulin* occupies a middle space: technically valid enough that the meat remains permitted, yet not fulfilling the owner's obligation. This distinction flows from Rav's original ruling and is now resolved by Abaye to be consistent with Rabbi Yose's *Mishnah*.

## Intent Distinctions

### Intentional vs Mistaken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חוּלִּין – כְּשֵׁרָה. מִשּׁוּם חוּלִּין – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** Indeed, **a sin offering that one** intentionally **slaughtered for the sake of a non-sacred** animal is **valid,** as stated by Rav. But if he slaughtered it **as a non-sacred** animal, actually thinking that it was non-sacred, it is **disqualified,** as when he slaughtered it, he did not intend to perform a sacrificial rite.

Rav Elazar introduces a further qualification. A *chattat* shechted with conscious intent that it be *leshem chulin* — where the *shochet* knows it is a *chattat* but deliberately redirects his intent toward *chulin* — is *kesheirah* in the sense described above (not *meratzeh* but not fully *pasul*). However, if the *shochet* actually believed the animal to be *chulin* — if he labored under a mistaken assumption about what he was shechting — then it is *psulah*, entirely disqualified.

### Misyasek Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל מֵרַב הוּנָא: מִנַּיִן לַמִּתְעַסֵּק בְּקָדָשִׁים שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת בֶּן הַבָּקָר לִפְנֵי ה׳״ – עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא שְׁחִיטָה לְשֵׁם בֶּן בָּקָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוֹ בְּיָדֵינוּ הִיא; לְעַכֵּב מִנַּיִן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לִרְצֹנְכֶם תִּזְבָּחוּהוּ״ – לְדַעְתְּכֶם זְבֻיחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like the dilemma that Shmuel raised before Rav Huna:** **From where** is it derived with regard **to one who acts unawares** in the case **of consecrated** items, i.e., if one slaughtered an offering without intending to perform the act of slaughter at all, but rather like one occupied with other matters, **that** the offering **is disqualified?** Rav Huna said to Shmuel: It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And he shall slaughter the young bull before the Lord"** (Leviticus 1:5), teaching that the mitzva is not performed properly **unless the slaughter is for the sake of a young bull,** i.e., knowing that he is performing an act of slaughter. Shmuel **said to** Rav Huna: **We have this** as an established *halakha* already, that it is a mitzva to slaughter the offering for the sake of a bull, but **from where** is it derived that this requirement is **indispensable?** Rav Huna **said to him** that the verse states: **"With your will you shall slaughter it"** (Leviticus 19:5), i.e., **with your** full **awareness you shall slaughter** it, in the form of a purposeful action.

This distinction raises the question of *misyasek*: what is the status of one who is entirely unaware of what he is doing? The question posed — *minayin l'misyasek b'kodashim she'hu pasul* — asks for the source that one who performs the *shechitah* of a *korban* while not consciously aware that he is doing so has performed an invalid act.

The application here is instructive. If the *shochet* is in the precincts of the *Beis Hamikdash* and assumes that the animal before him is ordinary *chulin* — perhaps believing that someone brought him an ordinary cow — and shechts it without any awareness that it is a *korban*, that is *misyasek*. The act is not an act of *shechitat korbanot* in any meaningful sense. The one who intentionally directs his intent toward *chulin* but knows he is dealing with a *korban* is in a different position: his act is at least consciously directed, even if misdirected. The one who has no awareness at all that a *korban* is involved is performing an act that is halachically inert as a *shechitat korban*.

### Misyasek Source

The source is derived from the verse *v'shachat et ben habakar lifnei Hashem* (Vayikra 1:5). Shmuel asked Rav Huna for the source; Rav Huna said the verse implies *ad she'tehei shechitah l'shem ben habakar* — the *shechitah* must be conscious that it is being performed on a bovine, that one is at least aware of the nature of the act. Shmuel replied: that we already know; the question is the source that *be'diavad* it is invalid. Rav Huna responded with *lirtzonchem tizbachu*.

### Conscious Intent Required

The verse *lirtzonchem tizbachu* establishes that the act of *zevichah* must be performed *l'da'atchem* — with conscious awareness, with one's own will. The *ratzton* must be present: not just that God should receive the offering willingly, but that the one performing the act should be doing so with full, willing, conscious intent. An act performed in a state of unawareness — *misyasek* — lacks this quality entirely and is therefore invalid from the outset. This applies not just to the specific case of *chattat*, but to any *shechitat korbanot* performed without conscious awareness that one is engaged in that act.

To summarize the full spectrum of distinctions arrived at: a *chattat* slaughtered with intent *leshem olah* is *psulah* (*chattat shelo lishmo*); a *chattat* slaughtered knowingly *leshem chulin* is *kasher* but not *meratzeh*; a *chattat* slaughtered under the mistaken belief that it is *chulin* is fully *psulah* as *misyasek*; and a *chattat* slaughtered with no active intent at all (*stam*) is both *kasher* and *meratzeh*, by the principle of *stam lishmah* and the *tenai beit din* of Rabbi Yose.
