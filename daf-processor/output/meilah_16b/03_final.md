# Meilah 16b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'ilah Foundations

### Kodshim Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַנְּבֵילוֹת – מִצְטָרְפִין זוֹ עִם זוֹ. וְכׇל הַשְּׁקָצִים – מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All animal carcasses,** whose consumption is prohibited and which transmit impurity through contact with them and through carrying, **join together with one another** to constitute the requisite olive-bulk measure. **And all repugnant creatures join together with one another** to constitute the requisite olive-bulk measure to render one who consumes it liable to receive lashes. The eight creeping animals enumerated in the Torah join together to constitute the measure of a lentil-bulk, which transmits impurity through contact, and to render one who consumes it liable to receive lashes.

The present daf continues the perek of *kodshei mizbe'ach*, and before proceeding into its primary discussions — which concern not *me'ilah* strictly speaking, but the broader principles of *tziruf*, the combining of prohibited substances — it is worth recalling the foundational *me'ilah* rule established at the opening of this perek. The mishna there taught that *kodshei mizbe'ach* and *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* combine for *me'ilah*: even half a *perutah*'s worth of benefit derived from a *korban* and half a *perutah*'s worth from Temple treasury property join together to constitute actionable *me'ilah*.

### Me'ilah Concept

This rule carries significant conceptual weight. The very fact that the mishna measures *me'ilah* in units of a *perutah* — a monetary unit — already steers us away from thinking of *me'ilah* primarily in terms of *achila*, eating. The prohibition could reflect either *hana'ah*, the derived benefit that is inappropriate for a *korban*, or *gezeila*, the theft of consecrated property, which would be the more natural framework for *bedek ha-bayit*. One might have thought these two categories of *kedusha* represent fundamentally different types of *me'ilah* — *hana'ah* for *korbanot*, *gezeila* for Temple property — and therefore cannot be combined. The mishna's ruling that they do combine establishes that *me'ilah* is a single unified concept that transcends the particular form of *kedusha* involved. It does not resolve, however, what the primary concept actually is. The position attributed to Rav Yannai, that the paradigmatic *me'ilah* is from *bedek ha-bayit*, would certainly tilt the analysis toward *gezeila* as the organizing principle — but the underlying tension remains open.

### Nevelos & Sheratzim

With that principle established, the perek proceeds to examine related questions of *tziruf* in other domains. The mishna taught that all *nevelos* combine and all *sheratzim* combine. As was discussed, the combining of *nevelos* admits of two possible readings: at minimum it means they combine for *tumah* — half a *kezayis* from one *neveilah* and half a *kezayis* from another join together to convey *tumah* — but possibly it extends to *issur achila* as well, raising the question of whether a non-kosher animal that has died also carries the status of *neveilah*, and whether *issur chal al issur* would allow half a *kezayis* of pig and half a *kezayis* of a dead cow to combine for the *issur* of *neveilah*. Whether the mishna intends this broader reading was left unresolved; the minimum reading is for *tumah*. The mishna then moves to *sheratzim*: a half-*shiur* from one type of *sheretz* and a half-*shiur* from another combine. The question now is identical in form — combine for *achila*, for *tumah*, or both?

## Sheratzim Shiurim

### Rav: Kezayis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: אֲכִילַת שְׁרָצִים – לוֹקֶה עָלָיו בִּכְזַיִת. מַאי טַעְמָא? ׳אֲכִילָה׳ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **eating creeping animals,** one **is flogged for** eating **an olive-bulk** of them. **What is the reason?** It is because the term **"eating" is written** in the Torah **with regard to them.** The verse states: "And every creeping thing that swarms upon the earth is a detestable thing; it shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 11:41). The term "eating" is invariably referring to consuming an olive-bulk.

At the top of 16b, Rav Yehuda cites Rav: *achilat sheratzim lokeh alav be-kezayis* — one who eats *sheratzim* receives lashes upon consuming a *kezayis*. At first glance this seems unremarkable; the standard *shiur* for prohibited foods is a *kezayis*. One might raise the question of a *beriya*, a whole creature — there is a position that eating a whole creature incurs *malkos* even if it is less than a *kezayis* — but setting that aside, for partial quantities the *shiur* is a *kezayis*.

A terminological observation is worth noting: the mishna uses the word *shkatzim*, while Rav's statement uses *sheratzim*. The Torah itself, in the passage listing the creatures that convey *tumah*, uses *sheretz* — *ve-zeh lachem ha-tamei ba-sheretz ha-shoreitz al ha-aretz*, followed by the enumeration of the eight creatures: *ha-choled, ha-achbar, ha-tzav*, and the rest — concluding *eileh ha-tmei'im lachem be-chol ha-sheretz*. Yet even there the Torah ends *sheketz yiheyu lachem*. The Talmudic literature appears to treat the terms interchangeably in this context, and for our purposes that is the working assumption.

### Parshas Shemini Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״וְהִבְדַּלְתֶּם בֵּין הַבְּהֵמָה הַטְּהֹרָה לַטְּמֵאָה וּבֵין הָעוֹף הַטָּמֵא לַטָּהוֹר וְלֹא תְשַׁקְּצוּ אֶת נַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם בַּבְּהֵמָה וּבָעוֹף וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּרְמֹשׂ הָאֲדָמָה אֲשֶׁר הִבְדַּלְתִּי לָכֶם לְטַמֵּא״ – פָּתַח הַכָּתוּב בַּאֲכִילָה, וְסִיֵּים בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. **But didn't Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Ḥanina teach** the following *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The verse states: **"You shall separate between the kosher animal and the non-kosher, and between the non-kosher bird and the kosher; and you shall not make your souls detestable by animal, or by bird, or by anything that swarms on the ground, which I have set apart for you as impure"** (Leviticus 20:25). **The verse opens with eating** creeping animals, in the phrase "You shall not make your souls detestable," **and** it **ends with** the **ritual impurity** of creeping animals: "Which I have set apart for you as impure."

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reasoning for this *shiur*? The answer seems immediate: *achila ksiv*, the Torah writes a prohibition of eating, and the standard *shiur* for eating violations is a *kezayis*. But then the *gemara* raises a *braisa*: Rav Yosef b'Rebbi Chanina taught the following before Rabbi Yochanan. The *braisa* cites the passage from the end of *Parshas Shemini*: *ve-hivdaltem bein ha-behemah ha-tehorah la-teme'ah, uvein ha-of ha-tamei la-tahor* — you shall distinguish between the *tahor* and *tamei* animal, and between the *tamei* and *tahor* bird. Importantly, the *tumah* and *tahara* of animals and birds in this passage means *kashrut* — kosher versus non-kosher — not *tumah* in the metaphysical ritual sense, since birds, for instance, are never described by the Torah as sources of *tumah* in the ritual sense. The verse then continues: *velo tishaktzu es nafshoseichem ba-behemah uva'of, uvechol asher tirmos ha'adamah, asher hivdalti lachem letamei* — do not make your souls abominable through the animals, the birds, and all that creeps on the ground, which I have separated out for you to be *tamei*.

The *braisa* identifies a structural feature of this passage: *pasach ha-kasuv be-achila* — the verse opens with the dimension of *tumah* that means forbidden eating (the *kashrut* distinctions involving animals and birds), *v'siyem be-tumah* — and it closes with *tumah* in the literal ritual sense, referring to those creatures whose carcasses convey *tumah*. Rashi and Tosafot note that the phrase *velo tishaktzu es nafshoseichem* has an intrinsically eating-related connotation — *nafshoseichem* speaks to what one ingests, what one allows into oneself — whereas *asher hivdalti lachem letamei* refers to the transmission of ritual *tumah*.

### Tumah = Achila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה טוּמְאָה בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, אַף אֲכִילָה בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה. וְקַלְּסֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְקַשְׁיָא לִדְרַב!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: This teaches that **just as** the carcass of a creeping animal imparts **ritual impurity** through contact **when** it is the volume of **a lentil-bulk, so too,** one is liable for the prohibition of **eating** a creeping animal **when** it is the volume of **a lentil-bulk. And Rabbi Yoḥanan praised [*vekilseih*]** Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Ḥanina for citing this *baraita*. **And** this poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rav,** who maintains that one is flogged only if he eats an olive-bulk of creeping animals.

The *braisa* draws the following conclusion from this structural parallel: *ma tumah ke'adasha af achila ke'adasha* — just as the *shiur* for *tumah* is a *ke'adasha*, so too the *shiur* for *achila* is a *ke'adasha*. The logic is that the Torah, by linking the eating prohibition and the *tumah* transmission within a single passage, signals that the operative quantity for one equals the operative quantity for the other.

By *behemot* and birds, this equation is uninteresting because both *achila* and *tumah* carry the same *shiur* of a *kezayis* — the equation is trivially satisfied. But for *sheratzim*, the *shiur* for *tumah* is a *ke'adasha* — the size of a lentil bean, considerably smaller than a *kezayis*. The *braisa* posits a *drasha* explaining why the *tumah* of *sheratzim* is a *ke'adasha*: certain of these creatures begin their existence at the size of a lentil bean, and the word *bahem* — "in their entirety" — establishes the smallest complete form of such a creature as the paradigmatic *shiur*. There is also a general principle that *shiurim* are *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, though if that were the only basis, the equation between *tumah* and *achila* would be unnecessary.

The *braisa*'s ruling is that since the *tumah* *shiur* for *sheratzim* is a *ke'adasha*, the *achila* *shiur* is likewise a *ke'adasha* — an exception to the normal rule that eating prohibitions require a *kezayis*. Rabbi Yochanan praised this *braisa* enthusiastically. This creates an apparent contradiction with Rav's ruling that eating *sheratzim* requires a *kezayis*.

### Alive vs. Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – בְּמִיתָתָן, כָּאן – בְּחַיֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* is referring to eating creeping animals **when they are dead,** at which stage they impart ritual impurity and one is liable for eating a lentil-bulk. By contrast, **there,** Rav is speaking about eating creeping animals **when they are alive,** which do not yet impart ritual impurity. For this reason one is flogged only if he eats an olive-bulk.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — kan be-mitatan, kan be-chayeihen* — there is no difficulty, because the two statements refer to different cases. When the *sheratzim* are dead, they convey *tumah* of a *ke'adasha*, and accordingly the *achila* *shiur* is also a *ke'adasha*. When they are alive, they convey no *tumah* at all, and therefore the equation does not apply — the *achila* *shiur* reverts to the standard *kezayis*.

This resolution invites a clarifying question: is there any concept of *ever min ha-chai* or *basar min ha-chai* with respect to *sheratzim*? The answer is no. The concepts of *ever min ha-chai*, *basar min ha-chai*, and *neveilah* apply only to animals in whose case *shechita* is halachically relevant — mammals and birds. *Sheratzim* have no *shechita*, no *neveilah*, and therefore no *ever min ha-chai*. This is demonstrated by the very structure of the Torah's laws: of all the various rodents and creeping things, only the eight enumerated *sheratzim* convey *tumah* when dead. Other similar creatures that die convey no *tumah* whatsoever — a clear indication that the Torah has not categorized their death through the conceptual framework of *neveilah*. Similarly, just as fish have no *shechita* and therefore no *ever min ha-chai*, so too *sheratzim*. The *issur* when *sheratzim* are alive is simply the *issur* of *sheratzim* — not *ever min ha-chai*. And since there is no *tumah* in the case of a living *sheretz*, the *achila* *shiur* remains a *kezayis*, consistent with Rav's statement.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא רַב אַמַּתְנִיתִין קָאֵי, וּמַתְנִיתִין ״כׇּל הַשְּׁרָצִים״ קָתָנֵי, אֲפִילּוּ בְּמִיתָתָן. לָאו דְּאִיכָּא פּוּרְתָּא מֵהַאי וּפוּרְתָּא מֵהַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **But** in his statement **Rav** was **referring to the mishna, and the mishna teaches: And all the creeping animals** join together to constitute the requisite olive-bulk measure to render one who consumes it liable to receive lashes. This indicates that this *halakha* applies **even when they are dead.** Is the mishna **not** referring to a case **where there is a bit of this** live creeping animal **and a bit of that** carcass of a creeping animal, which together combine to amount to an olive-bulk?

Abaye raises an objection: Rav's statement was made in the context of our mishna — *Rav amtnitin kai* — and our mishna says *all sheratzim* combine, even when they are dead. Abaye's reasoning is supported by context: Rav also commented on the first half of the mishna regarding *nevelos*, distinguishing there between combining for *tumah* and combining for *achilat tahorim*. This shows Rav was engaged with our mishna throughout. If so, when Rav says that eating *sheratzim* requires a *kezayis*, he is explaining the mishna's combining rule — and the mishna speaks categorically, without distinguishing alive from dead. The resolution that Rav was only speaking of living *sheratzim* would then be unavailable.

### Rav Yosef: Independent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: הָהִיא דִּיּוּקָא דִּילָךְ הוּא. רַב – שְׁמַעְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to** Abaye: **This** deductive **inference,** that Rav is referring to the mishna, **is yours.** But in fact **Rav** was **merely saying a *halakha*** unconnected to the mishna. Therefore, there is no proof that Rav was speaking about the carcasses of creeping animals.

Rav Yosef responds: *hahi diyuka dilchu* — that reading of Rav into the mishna is your own inference, not established fact. Rav's statement was *shme'ata b'alma* — an independent ruling, not a commentary on the mishna. Some texts have a more colorful version of this response: *hahi dufka didach* — that is your way of pressing things together, gluing them into a forced connection. Once Rav's statement is understood as independent of the mishna, there is no compulsion to read it as encompassing dead *sheratzim*, and the resolution stands: Rav spoke of living *sheratzim* (where the *shiur* is a *kezayis*), while Rabbi Yochanan's *braisa* spoke of dead ones (where the *shiur* is a *ke'adasha*).

The *gemara*'s conclusion, then, is that when eating the eight *sheratzim* that convey *tumah* in their dead state, the *shiur* for *achila* is a *ke'adasha* — the *tumah* *shiur* — because the Torah explicitly links the two in the passage at the end of *Parshas Shemini*. For living *sheratzim*, which convey no *tumah*, the standard *kezayis* applies.

## Rabbi Yochanan Rule

### Limbs & Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָאֵיבָרִין – אֵין לָהֶן שִׁיעוּר, אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת נְבֵלָה וּפָחוֹת מִכַּעֲדָשָׁה מִן הַשֶּׁרֶץ – מְטַמְּאִין. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין לוֹקִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶלָּא בִּכְזַיִת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Oholot* 1:7): **The** whole **limbs** of impure bodies **have no** minimum **measure** with regard to imparting ritual impurity. **Even** if the limbs were **less than an olive-bulk of a carcass or less than a lentil-bulk of a creeping animal,** they **impart** ritual **impurity. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This mishna is referring to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity; but with regard to the minimum measure which renders one liable for consumption, **one is flogged for** eating **them only** if they amount to **an olive-bulk.**

Having established that for the eight dead *sheratzim*, *tumah* and *achila* are linked at a *ke'adasha*, the *gemara* raises a further challenge from a *braisa*: *evarim ein lahem shiur* — limbs have no minimum *shiur*. Even less than a *kezayis* of *neveilah*, or less than a *ke'adasha* of a *sheretz*, conveys *tumah* if it constitutes a whole limb — *afilu pachot mi-kezayis neveilah, pachot mi-ke'adasha min ha-sheretz metamei*. This holds even for *sheratzim*: a whole limb, even smaller than a *ke'adasha*, conveys *tumah*. The *braisa* then cites Rabbi Yochanan: *ein lokin aleihem ela be-kezayis* — even so, one does not receive *malkos* for eating such a limb unless the quantity amounts to a *kezayis*. But Rabbi Yochanan himself had just praised the *braisa* establishing that for dead *sheratzim* the *achila* *shiur* is a *ke'adasha*! If *tumah* and *achila* are equated, why does the *tumah* of a sub-*ke'adasha* limb not trigger *malkos* upon eating?

### Muvdalim Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּמוּבְדָּלִין דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** in resolution of the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yoḥanan's statements: When Rabbi Yoḥanan said that one is flogged for eating even a lentil-bulk of a creeping animal, he was referring only to those eight creeping animals of which **the verse speaks, which are separated** from all other creeping animals. The Torah (Leviticus 11:29–32) lists eight types of creeping animals, and Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring specifically to those eight. He maintains that one is flogged for eating a lentil-bulk of such creatures, whereas in the case of other creeping animals one is flogged only for eating an olive-bulk of them.

Rava resolves the contradiction: *be-muvdalim diber ha-kasuv* — the Torah spoke specifically about the *muvdalim*, the "distinguished" ones. The end of the verse reads *asher hivdalti lachem letamei* — "which I have separated out for you to be *tamei*." Within the category of *sheratzim*, some cause *tumah* and some do not — some are among the eight enumerated dead *sheratzim*, others are not. It is precisely this internal distinction, this *havdalah* within *sheratzim* themselves, that the phrase *asher hivdalti lachem letamei* points to. Because such a distinction exists within *sheratzim*, the Torah's equation of *tumah* and *achila* applies to them — and only to them.

According to Tosafot's reading, when Rabbi Yochanan says *ein lokin ela be-kezayis*, he is not speaking about *sheratzim* at all. For *sheratzim* that cause *tumah*, Rabbi Yochanan maintains his earlier ruling: the *achila* *shiur* follows the *tumah* *shiur* and is therefore a *ke'adasha*. Rather, Rabbi Yochanan's statement *ein lokin ela be-kezayis* refers to *behemot*. A whole limb of a *behemah* conveys *tumah* even when smaller than a *kezayis* — yet one might suppose that the *tumah*-*achila* equation should apply here too, meaning one would receive *malkos* for eating a sub-*kezayis* limb of a *behemah*. To preclude this, Rabbi Yochanan rules that *malkos* for eating *behemah* requires a *kezayis* regardless. The *tumah* of a limb does not drag down the *achila* *shiur* for *behemot* because *behemot* are not subject to the *tumah*-*achila* equation in the first place — that equation is the exclusive domain of *sheratzim*, the *muvdalim*.

### Adda's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְרָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בְּהֵמָה נָמֵי, לִיפְלְגִי בֵּין מוּבְדֶּלֶת לְשֶׁאֵינָהּ מוּבְדֶּלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to Rava: If that is so,** that there is a difference in the measure of liability for consumption between various types of creeping animals, the same should **also** apply with regard to **an animal.** Since the Torah in Leviticus 20:25 juxtaposes both kosher animals and non-kosher animals to creeping animals, one can say that an analogous difference should apply here as well: **Let** the *halakha* **distinguish between** flesh from the carcass of kosher animals, which is **separated** from other types in that it is permitted in consumption by the Torah, and flesh from the carcass of a non-kosher animal, **which is not separated,** i.e., which is not permitted by the Torah. Consequently, if the carcass of a kosher animal imparts ritual impurity by the amount of an olive-bulk of flesh, the measure of flesh from the carcass of a non-kosher animal that imparts ritual impurity should be larger, i.e., an egg-bulk.

Adda bar Ahava presses Rava: if that is so, why not apply the same *muvdalim* reasoning to *behemot* as well? The verse — *ve-hivdaltem bein ha-behemah ha-tehorah la-teme'ah, uvein ha-of ha-tamei la-tahor... asher hivdalti lachem letamei* — encompasses *behemot* and birds within the same passage as *sheratzim*. If the *tumah*-*achila* equation was derived from this passage's linkage, the linkage encompasses *behemot* too. Tosafot explains the force of this objection: even if the terminal phrase *asher hivdalti lachem letamei* specifically flags *sheratzim* (among whom there is an internal distinction between those that cause *tumah* and those that do not), the entire verse addresses *behemot* and birds together with *sheratzim*. One might therefore extend the *sheratzim* principle — that *tumah* *shiur* governs *achila* *shiur* — to *behemot* as well, at least in the case where *tumah* occurs at a quantity smaller than a *kezayis*, namely the case of a whole limb.

### Final Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי קָא מַקֵּישׁ רַחֲמָנָא – לְבַל תְּשַׁקְּצוּ, אֲבָל לְשִׁיעוּרִין – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Adda bar Ahava: **When the Merciful One juxtaposes** kosher and non-kosher animals to creeping animals, this is referring **to** the prohibition of: "You shall **not make** your souls **detestable"** (Leviticus 20:25), teaching that they are all included in that prohibition. **But with regard to measures** the Torah does **not** juxtapose those animals to creeping animals. For this reason there is no difference between the measure of impurity of carcasses of kosher animals and non-kosher animals.

Rava answers: *ki ka-makish Rachmana le-val teshaktzu — aval le-shiurim lo* — the Torah's linkage through the verse applies to establish the *lav* of *bal teshaktzu*, not to equate the *shiurim* across categories. The verse *velo tishaktzu es nafshoseichem ba-behemah uva'of uvechol asher tirmos ha'adamah asher hivdalti lachem letamei* does indeed combine all these categories — *behemot*, birds, and *sheratzim* — but its purpose is to establish that eating from any of them, in addition to all the other *lavin* that apply, also violates the *lav* of *bal teshaktzu*. The equation of *tumah* *shiur* to *achila* *shiur* is a distinct *halacha* that applies only to *sheratzim* — those creatures among which the Torah itself makes a *havdalah*, distinguishing which cause *tumah* and which do not. For *behemot*, no such internal *havdalah* exists in this regard: all dead *behemot* convey *tumah* uniformly. The *havdalah* language of *asher hivdalti lachem letamei* points specifically to *sheratzim*, and only *sheratzim* are subject to the equation.

The *gemara*'s conclusion is therefore as follows. Among the eight *sheratzim* that convey *tumah* when dead: eating them dead triggers *malkos* at the *ke'adasha* *shiur*, because the Torah links *tumah* and *achila* for the *muvdalim*. For other *sheratzim* — those that are forbidden to eat but do not cause *tumah* — or for *behemot*, the *tumah*-*achila* equation does not apply, and the *achila* *shiur* remains a standard *kezayis* even where *tumah* is conveyed at a lesser quantity, as in the case of a whole limb. The phrase *asher hivdalti lachem letamei* is the key: it is precisely the internal distinction within *sheratzim* — some cause *tumah*, some do not — that renders them, and them alone, subject to the equation. The combining discussed in the mishna accordingly operates within this framework: *sheratzim* combine both for *tumah* and for *achila*, but the applicable *shiur* depends on whether they are alive or dead, and the *muvdalim* principle governs which category of creature falls under the *tumah*-*achila* equation at all.
