# Bava Metzia 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Captive Property Debate

### Fruit & Captive Parallel

The Gemara on 38b opens in the shadow of the Mishnah's central concern: when fruit entrusted to a bailee begins to rot, may — or must — the bailee sell it to protect the owner's investment, even though no such instruction was given? The Mishnah debated at what stage of deterioration such a sale becomes appropriate. The Gemara now draws a parallel case that is structurally similar but legally distinct: a person is taken captive — lost to war, whereabouts unknown — and his fields lie fallow. May a relative, who stands to inherit and therefore has a stake in the property's preservation, be sent down to work the land? This is the question of *moridin karov l'nichsei shavuy* — whether we deploy a relative to tend the captive's property. The two cases share the same underlying tension: at what point does the imperative to protect someone's investment override the presumption that one should not handle another person's property without explicit authorization?

### Rabbi Eliezer Drasha

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *baraita* citing Rabbi Eliezer, who derives a *drasha* from the verse in the context of divine punishment: *v'charah api v'haragti eschem becharev* — "My anger will be kindled and I will smite you with the sword." The verse then adds that your wives will become *almonos* and your children orphans. Rabbi Eliezer asks: if the husbands have been killed by the sword, it is obvious that their wives will be widows and their sons orphans — why does the verse need to state this explicitly? The answer, he says, is to convey the worst possible situation: *shenisheichem mevakshos lihnasei v'ein manichin osan* — the wives will seek to remarry but *beis din* will not permit them, because the husbands went off to war and their deaths are unconfirmed. And similarly: *beneichem rotzin lereid l'nichsei avihem v'ein manichin osan* — the children wish to take possession of their father's property, believing him to have been killed, but *beis din* does not allow them.

The Gemara reads this *baraita* as an apparent challenge to the position of Shmuel, who holds *moridin* — that we do deploy a relative to work the captive's property. If children cannot take possession of the father's property when his death is unconfirmed, that seems to support the view of Rav, who holds *ein moridin*.

### Possession vs. Working

Rava resolves this difficulty with a careful distinction: *lereid v'limkor tnan* — what the *baraita* prohibits is full possession and the power to sell. The *drasha* on the verse teaches only that the children may not treat their father as definitively dead, seize the property, and sell it off as their own. It says nothing about the more limited act of working the fields — tilling, planting, tending — in a way that preserves the investment without asserting ownership. This reading defuses the challenge to Shmuel entirely.

### Rav Sheshet Ruling

A practical case then arose in Nahardea: a person had been lost to war or captivity, and the question was whether his children could begin working the land. Rav Sheshet applied the same *baraita* from *Avot d'Rabbi Natan* and ruled absolutely: *ein moridin karov l'nichsei shavuy* — a relative may not go down to work the property of someone whose fate is unknown. His ruling was categorical; he read the *baraita* as prohibiting even the limited act of tending the land.

### Rav Amram Challenge

Rav Amram challenged Rav Sheshet: perhaps, as Rava had suggested, the *baraita* means only *lereid v'limkor* — that only full possession and sale are forbidden, but working the land is permitted? Rav Sheshet's response was sharp: *dilma mipumbedisa at? d'mayil pila be-kufa d'machta* — "Perhaps you are from Pumbedisa, who try to squeeze an elephant through the eye of a needle?" Such hairsplitting interpretations, he implied, are not to be entertained. His argument then turned substantive: the *baraita* draws a parallel between the wives' situation and the children's. Just as the wives can do nothing — they cannot remarry, they have no recourse — *klalo* — so too the children can do nothing with the property at all. One might respond that remarriage is more like selling the estate outright — the fullest assumption of the husband's death — whereas working the land falls short of that. But Rav Sheshet rejects this as a strained reading: the parallel is absolute, and so is the prohibition.

## Three Categories

### Captive Braita

The Gemara now establishes that the debate over *nichsei shavuy* is in fact a dispute among *Tannaim*, as a *baraita* lays out three distinct categories of abandoned or uncertain property.

The first category is *nichsei shevuyin* — the property of captives. *Hayored l'nichsei shavuy ein motzi'in oso mi-yado* — one who goes down to work the fields of a captive is not removed from possession. More strikingly, *afilu shama she-memashmeshin ba'in* — even if he hears that the original owner is on his way back, if he moves quickly and harvests the produce, *harei zeh zariz v'niskar* — he is praised for his alacrity and keeps what he harvested. The Gemara is evidently amused by this: we are ostensibly working this field to benefit the owner, yet the one who harvests it all before the owner returns is called praiseworthy?

The category of *shevuyin* is further defined: it includes cases where a father, brother, or potential heir went overseas and reports came that he died — *shamu bahen she-meis*. This is crucial to understanding why the worker keeps all the produce. If the worker has reliable reports that the owner is dead, he goes down to the property treating himself as the practical owner. Given that assumption, he invests in the property as an owner would, and therefore he keeps all the profit. If it turns out the owner is alive, the land is returned and the produce is retained — a genuine win-win. The owner's property was maintained, and the worker is compensated for his investment and effort.

### Netushin Retushin

The second category is *netushin* — abandoned property. Here a father, brother, or potential heir has gone overseas but *lo shamu lahem she-meitu* — there are no reports of death. He simply has not returned. In this case, *motzin otam miyado* — what was worked is taken back. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* dissents, saying: *shamati she-netushin kishvuyin* — I have a tradition that the *netushin* case is treated like the *shevuyin* case. Someone who has been overseas with no word for a long time may well be dead; we treat his property accordingly.

The third category is *retushin* — a term that the Gemara carefully distinguishes from *netushin*. Both words translate roughly as "abandoned," and Jastrow renders them similarly, but the Gemara establishes a semantic and legal difference. *Netushin*, as derived from the verse describing *shemita* — *tishmetenna u-netashtah* — carries the connotation of abandonment imposed by external force, an *afkasta de-malka*, an act of God. Going overseas with no return is like that: something may have happened to him beyond his control, his ship may have sunk. *Retushin*, by contrast, is derived from a verse meaning willful abandonment — *eim al banim ruttasha* — as Rashi explains. In the case of *retushin*, the owner is locally present, his whereabouts are simply unknown to others — he told no one where he went. We assume he is a responsible adult who simply failed to arrange for his property, and therefore no one has any license to work it. *Motzin otam miyado* — the worker is removed entirely, and no debate applies.

## Aris Sharecropping

### All as Aris

Having established these three categories, the Gemara cites a further teaching: *v'kulan shamnin k'aris* — all of them are treated like an *aris*, a sharecropper, meaning the one who works the property takes a portion of the produce rather than keeping it all. But which of the three categories does this apply to? If it applies to the *shevuyin* category — where the owner is believed dead — this cannot be right, since the *baraita* already says *zariz v'niskar*, the worker keeps everything. If it applies to the *retushin* category — where the owner seems simply irresponsible — the *baraita* already says the worker is removed entirely. It must therefore apply to the middle case, the *netushin* category, where the owner's fate is genuinely uncertain.

But according to whom? If it reflects the opinion of the *Rabbanan*, they hold that even in the *netushin* case the worker has no right and must return everything — so there is no middle category for them at all. If it reflects the opinion of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, he treats the *netushin* case like *shevuyin*, where the worker keeps everything — leaving again no middle ground. The Gemara resolves this: for *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, *netushin* is *k'shvuyin v'lo shvuyin* — like the *shevuyin* category in that you are not removed from the field, but not identical to it. In the *shevuyin* case you keep all the produce; in the *netushin* case, because you went down knowing the owner's fate was genuinely uncertain, you receive only a portion — the share of an *aris*.

---

## Three Categories…

### Captive vs. Overseas

A question arises as to why these two cases are treated differently. Rashi's explanation is precise: in the *shevuyin* case — where there were reports of death — *keivan she-shamu bo she-met, ki nachis ada'ata d'cula peira nachis* — since it was reported that the owner died, the worker went down with the full expectation of owning everything. He treated himself as the actual owner, invested accordingly, and is entitled to the full reward even if the report turned out to be false. But in the *netushin* case — *d'lo shamu she-met* — there was never a report of death. The worker knew from the outset that the owner might still be alive. He therefore went down in a state of uncertainty, and his entitlement reflects that: he gets something for his efforts, but not everything.

## Aris Sharecropping…

### Psychology of Aris

This raises a further question. The Gemara asks how the *aris* model for the *netushin* case differs from the husband-wife model where a husband works his wife's property — *nichsei milog* — and the rule is *mah she-hotzi hotzi u-mah she-achal achal*: whatever he spent he spent, and whatever he gained he gained. The husband bears all expenses and keeps all profit, with no accounting to the wife. Why is the *netushin* case not structured the same way?

The Gemara clarifies that the *aris* model in the *netushin* case is actually not parallel to the standard husband-wife case at all. Rather, it is parallel to a different case cited in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav Chisda: *hamotzi ho-tza'ot al nichsei ishto ketana* — a husband who works the property of his minor wife, a wife married only through the mother and brothers after the father's death, a rabbinic marriage that the minor wife may dissolve at will through *kiddushei mi'un*. In that marriage, the husband does not know whether the property will remain his, since at any moment his wife may walk away. This is precisely the situation of the worker in the *netushin* case: he is working the field knowing the owner may return.

### Minor Wife Case

The Gemara draws out the logic in full: *keivan de-lo samcha date'ih* — since the husband in the minor-wife case is not confident the property will remain his, *takinu lei Rabbanan* — the rabbis established that he receives a portion, like an *aris*, so that he will not simply strip mine the property. The concern, as the Gemara frames it, is *ki heichi de-lo le-fasdinhu* — lest he destroy or exhaust the property. If he knew he would receive everything, he might rush to extract maximum short-term profit — harvest it all as fast as possible before she walks away, before the owner returns. By giving him only a portion, the rabbis created an incentive structure in which he is invested in the property's long-term health. The parallel to the *netushin* case is then explicit: *hacha nami, takinu Rabbanan ki heichi de-lo le-fasdinhu* — here too the rabbis structured the arrangement as an *aris* arrangement precisely to encourage responsible stewardship.

One might wonder: if the worker gets nothing, he simply will not work the field, so there is no destruction — what exactly are we contrasting the *aris* portion to? The answer the Gemara seems to suggest is that the danger is not between getting nothing and getting a portion, but between getting everything and getting a portion. If you think you might get everything, you have incentive to extract the maximum quickly, before your claim evaporates. If you know you get only a portion regardless, you are less tempted to strip mine and more inclined to pace yourself and preserve the property — since a long-term well-maintained property yields more over time even at a third of the produce than a destroyed one would.

The connection back to the Mishnah's core question is now visible. The debate over whether to touch someone else's property when its value is at risk runs through all of these cases. The *Rabbanan* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* in the *netushin* context parallel the tannaitic disagreement in the Mishnah about rotting fruit: when does the imperative to protect an owner's investment override the rule that one should not handle property without authorization? *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* is consistently more willing to say that protecting the investment matters enough to act, while the *Rabbanan* insist on a firmer evidentiary basis before touching what is not yours.

## Boreach Runaway

### Murder Suspect Case

The Gemara cites Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *shavuy she-nishba moridin karov l'nichsav, yatza lada'at ein moridin karov l'nichsav* — if a person was taken captive against his will, we deploy a relative to work his property; if he simply left on his own accord and his property has lain fallow, we do not. Rav Nachman then adds in his own name: *boreach harei hu ke-shavuy* — a fugitive is treated like a captive. The reasoning is significant: ordinarily, if a person is absent and has not arranged for his property, we say that a responsible adult should have left instructions, and his failure to do so is his own problem. But a *boreach* — someone fleeing for his life — could not leave instructions. His silence is not irresponsibility; it is necessity. Therefore, despite the fact that he is not literally seized and taken captive, his situation is functionally equivalent, and we tend his property accordingly.

## Harvest vs. Tilling

### Ready Harvest

This is qualified by a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. If a *shavuy* left behind grain already ripe for harvest, grapes ready to be cut, dates ready to be picked, or olives ready to be pressed — *beis din yordin l'nichsav u-ma'amidin apotropus* — the court appoints an *apotropus*, an overseer, and simply arranges for the harvest. *V'achar kach moridin karov l'nichsav* — only after that, when ongoing effort and investment are required to maintain the land, do we bring in the relative and give him his portion. Immediate harvest from already-ripened produce requires minimal effort and yields full reward; that is not the kind of arrangement where an heir-relative should profit. But ongoing stewardship of the land — plowing, planting, tending through seasons — that requires genuine investment and justifies the *aris* arrangement.

---

## Boreach Runaway…

### Tax vs. Murder

The case of the *boreach* must be specified further. If someone is running from the government because of unpaid taxes — *machmat karga* — that is not a situation that justifies the same treatment as a *shavuy*. A tax debtor may be in trouble, but he is not in hiding for his life; he has had the opportunity to arrange his affairs and give instructions. His failure to do so falls on him. The case that Rav Nachman means is *boreach machmat mardin* — fleeing on account of murder, the same term that appears in Bava Kama. Whether he committed the act or is merely suspected of it, he is being hunted, he is silent for survival, and therefore *rachmanus* applies: we treat his property as we would a captive's.

---

## Harvest vs. Tilling…

### Apotropus Limits

One might ask: why not simply appoint an *apotropus* for the long term and avoid the entire complicated arrangement with relatives and *aris* portions? The Gemara's answer is terse but telling: *apotropa le-diknani lo mukminan* — we do not appoint an *apotropus* for men with beards, meaning for adults. The institution of the court-appointed *apotropus* exists for orphans and minors who cannot manage their own affairs. As Rashi explains, an *apotropus* does his work without compensation, motivated by the honor of a court appointment — it is, in a word, thankless service. That kind of volunteer stewardship works when it is for helpless children whose plight moves people to act without reward. It does not work as a long-term arrangement for an adult male who happened to be taken captive. No one will volunteer to do it indefinitely without compensation. The solution, therefore, is the relative-as-*aris* model: he is invested, he profits from his portion, and he has every reason to maintain the property well.

## Minor Workers Banned

### Rav Huna Ban

Rav Huna now introduces a set of restrictions on who may work property in these circumstances. Three prohibitions: *ein moridin katan l'nichsei shavuy* — you do not send a minor to work the property of a captive; *v'lo karov l'nichsei katan* — you do not send a relative to work the property of a minor; and *v'lo karov machmat karov l'nichsei katan* — you do not send a one-degree-removed relative either.

### Brother Exploit Risk

The first rule is straightforward: a minor cannot be trusted to manage property responsibly — *dilma mafsid lehu* — he may simply ruin it.

The more striking rule is the second: why may a relative not work the property of a minor? The case is this: *Reuven* is a minor, and his brother *Shimon* is an adult. *Shimon* begins working *Reuven*'s property — perhaps legitimately, to preserve it until *Reuven* comes of age. Years pass. *Reuven* becomes an adult and demands his property back. *Shimon* says: what do you mean your property? This was always mine, part of what I inherited from our father *Yaakov*. And this claim is dangerous, because *keivan d'lo machi ati l'achzukei beih* — since *Reuven* was a minor and could not effectively protest, *Shimon*'s prolonged possession of the field becomes the basis for a claim of *chazaka*. In Bava Batra, three years of unchallenged possession can establish ownership. A minor's failure to protest is precisely the kind of silence that creates this vulnerability.

The problem, critically, is not that *Shimon* is inherently dishonest. It is that the arrangement creates too much structural opportunity for exploitation — an older brother working a younger brother's property, with the younger brother unable to legally protest, in a context where all parties know the properties derived from the same father. The claim will be believable, and *beis din* cannot easily refute it.

### Chazaka Fails

Rava derives from Rav Huna's ruling a broader principle: *ein machzikin b'nichsei katan* — *chazaka* does not operate against a minor's property. If a total stranger works a minor's field for three years, that stranger cannot later claim that his prolonged possession proves ownership, because the minor's silence is explained by his incapacity — he did not know his father's business dealings and could not protest. And *va'afilu higdil* — even after the minor grew up, if the stranger continued working the field, the initial period of silent possession is tainted by the minority, and no *chazaka* is established. This is why a stranger can theoretically be appointed to work a minor's field without the same fear: the stranger's possession cannot generate a credible ownership claim, because *chazaka* simply does not apply.

### Mother-Side Exception

This explains why the prohibition extends even to the third case — *karov machmat karov*, a one-degree-removed relative. Take *Shimon*'s half-brother on his mother's side, someone with no line of inheritance from *Yaakov*. Could he not work *Reuven*'s property without the same danger? The Gemara answers that even this creates risk. If that half-brother works the field, he can later say: this field was not *Yaakov*'s to begin with — it was always my mother's property, which *Yaakov* had been working as *nichsei milog*, returning to her at his death and thus never passing to *Reuven*. This is a false claim, but it is a claim that the scheme enables. The half-brother and *Shimon* may have some arrangement, and the half-brother effectively launders *Shimon*'s potential claim.

However, *lo amran ela b'achi d'abba, aval b'achi d'ima leit lan bah* — this concern applies only to a brother on the father's side, not to a half-brother on the mother's side alone. A half-brother who shares only a mother with *Shimon* is never in the line of inheritance from *Yaakov* at all, and therefore he cannot himself generate a believable claim, nor launder one for *Shimon*, since *Shimon* himself would not exist in this scenario. If there is no father-side brother whose claim is being enabled, the one-degree-removed relative creates no danger.

### Houses & Documents

Further distinctions are raised: perhaps the concern about brothers working a minor's field applies only to land, not to houses, since houses have neighbors who would know and could challenge any false ownership claim. And perhaps even for land the concern evaporates if there is an *ityada* — a formal document laying out how the estate was divided — since in that case the division is publicly known and the brother cannot credibly claim the land was his.

### Restrictions Absolute

The Gemara rejects all of these distinctions: *lo hi*. *Lo shena achi d'abba v'lo shena achi d'ima, lo shena ara'a v'lo shena b'vatei, lo shana avid ityada v'lo shana d'lo avid ityada* — it makes no difference whether it is a father-side or mother-side brother, land or houses, with or without a document. *Lo machtinan* — we do not allow it under any circumstances. The structural opportunity for exploitation is too great, and the Gemara, as Rashi explains, ultimately envisions even more creative false claims — such as asserting that the property was always the mother's *nichsei milog* and therefore never passed to the minor as an heir of the father at all.

## Grandmother Case

### Case Setup

The Gemara presents a complex practical case to illustrate these principles in combination. An old woman — a grandmother — had three daughters. She and one of her daughters were taken captive — *ishtavai hi v'chada barta*. A second daughter died and left behind a minor child — *shchiv chada minaihu v'shavka yinuka*. The third daughter, *Leah*, is the only adult present and accounted for.

We will call the three daughters *Sarah* (captive), *Rivka* (captive alongside the grandmother), and *Rachel* (dead, leaving baby *Binyamin*). *Leah* is the adult sister who remains. The question is: what arrangement do we make for the grandmother's property?

### Abaye First

Abaye frames the dilemma. You cannot simply give *Leah* control over all the property: *dilma shchiv safta* — perhaps the grandmother (*Sarah*, or whichever captive is meant) is dead, in which case part of the property belongs to *Binyamin*, and you cannot have a relative work the property of a minor. You cannot give *Binyamin* the portion that would be his if the grandmother is dead: *dilma lo shchiv safta* — perhaps the grandmother is alive, in which case a minor would be working the property of a *shevuya*, which is also forbidden.

Abaye's solution: *palga yahavinan lah l'achata* — give *Leah* half the property to work. If the grandmother is alive and both captives survive, *Leah* is entitled to work the entire property on their behalf. If both captives are dead, *Leah* is entitled to half — her own portion — while *Binyamin* receives the other half. In either scenario, *Leah* can legitimately work half. *V'idach palga mokminan lah apotropa l'yanuka* — for the other half, appoint an *apotropus* on behalf of *Binyamin*, which is proper regardless of whether the grandmother is alive or dead, since if she is alive it is the captive's property being managed by a court overseer, and if she is dead it is *Binyamin*'s property being managed for him by an *apotropus*.

### Rava Counter

Rava disagrees with giving *Leah* direct control over her half: *migu de-mokminan apotropus le-palga, mokminan leh apotropus le-idach palga* — since we are already appointing an *apotropus* for one portion, let the same *apotropus* manage the entire property, including *Leah*'s half. Once a court-appointed overseer is in place and managing part of the estate, administrative efficiency and propriety dictate that he manage the whole.

### After Death News

Subsequently, news arrives: *lsof shamu de-shachiva savta* — they learned that the grandmother had in fact died. Now the picture clarifies somewhat: *Leah* is alive, *Binyamin* is a minor heir, and *Rivka*'s status remains unknown — she was taken captive and her fate is uncertain.

### Final Thirds

Abaye now recalculates. The property divides into thirds: *Leah* gets a third, *Binyamin* gets a third, and *Rivka* would get a third if alive. What is certain: *Leah* gets at minimum a third — *mima nafshach*, regardless of what happens with *Rivka*. *Binyamin* gets at minimum a third — again, regardless of *Rivka*'s fate. *Tilsa yahavinan le-Leia achasa, v'tilsa yahavinan le-yanuka*.

The uncertain third — *Rivka*'s portion — is divided into two sixths, a *danka* each. If *Rivka* is dead, *Leah* is entitled to this sixth as her full half. So *Leah* gets to work that sixth: *v'idach tilsa yahavinan danka le-achasa*. For the remaining sixth, if *Rivka* is dead, *Binyamin* inherits it; but if *Rivka* is alive, *Binyamin* cannot work it since a minor cannot work the property of a captive adult. So that sixth goes to an *apotropus*: *v'idach danka mokminan apotropus le-yanuka*.

Rava again applies his principle: *migu de-mokmi apotropus le-danka, mokminan apotropus le-idach danka* — since we are appointing an *apotropus* for one sixth, appoint the same *apotropus* for the other doubtful sixth. Unlike his earlier expansion where he extended the *apotropus* to all property including *Leah*'s certain portion, here Rava limits the expansion: the *apotropus* covers the two uncertain sixths, not the portions that are definitively allocated. The certain thirds are left as allocated; only the doubtful portions are consolidated under court oversight.
