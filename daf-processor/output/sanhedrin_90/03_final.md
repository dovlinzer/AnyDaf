# Sanhedrin 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Navi Shehidiach

### Intro: Navi & Avodah Zar…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָבִיא שֶׁהִדִּיחַ – בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּחֶנֶק. מַדִּיחֵי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת – בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A prophet who incites** others to worship idols is executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says:** He is executed **by strangulation.** Those **who incite** residents of a city to worship idols, leading the city to be declared **an idolatrous city,** are executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says:** They are executed **by strangulation.**

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about a *navi* who prophesies something he did not actually receive, or who prophesies in the name of another god — both of which carry the penalty of *chenek*. There is also the related case of one who fails to heed the words of a prophet, which carries *misa b'yedei shamayim*. These categories prompt the broader question of how one identifies a true prophet and why one is obligated to obey him — a question that ultimately opens into the *agadata* of *akeidas Yitzchak*, exploring why Yitzchak listened to Avraham.

The present discussion, however, turns to a distinct case that has appeared earlier in the sugya: not a *navi* who prophesies in the name of *avoda zara*, but one who claims to have received a prophecy and uses it to incite others to worship *avoda zara*. This case appears in the Torah directly before the *parsha* of the *mases* — the individual who privately entices others to idolatry, whose punishment is *skila*. The question is: what is the punishment for a *navi* who does the same thing publicly?

### Tanu Rabbanan Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָבִיא שֶׁהִדִּיחַ – בִּסְקִילָה. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? אָתְיָא "הַדָּחָה" "הַדָּחָה" מִמֵּסִית: מָה לְהַלָּן בִּסְקִילָה, אַף כָּאן בִּסְקִילָה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מִיתָה כְּתִיבָא בֵּיהּ, וְכׇל מִיתָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּתוֹרָה סְתָם – אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא חֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **A prophet who incites** others to worship idols is executed **by stoning. What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis?** They **derive** a verbal analogy to clarify the meaning of the word **incitement** written with regard to a prophet who incites others to worship idols **from** the word **incitement** written with regard to a layman who **instigates** others to worship idols. **Just as there,** the layman who incites others to worship idols is executed **by stoning, so too here,** the prophet who incites others to worship idols is executed **by stoning.**
>
> **And Rabbi Shimon** says: With regard to a prophet, the term **death is written concerning him. And every death stated in the Torah without specification** is referring to **nothing other than strangulation.**

A *tanu rabbanan* addresses this directly. The *navi shehidiach* — the prophet who incites to *avoda zara* — receives *skila*, just like the individual *mases*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds that such a *navi* receives *chenek*. The same dispute applies to the *midichei ir nidachas*, those who cause an entire city to worship *avoda zara*: the Rabbis say *skila*, Rabbi Shimon says *chenek*.

This is striking. An individual *mases* receives *skila*, the most severe of the four capital punishments. One would expect that a *navi* who incites masses to *avoda zara* — surely a graver offense — would carry at least the same penalty. Yet Rabbi Shimon rules that both the *navi* and the *midichei ir nidachas* receive the lesser punishment of *chenek*.

### Gezeira Shava

The Gemara explains the Rabbinic position first. The verse in the *navi shehidiach* passage uses the language *ki vikaish lehadichacha*, and the very next section on the individual *mases* uses the identical phrase *ki vikaish lehadichacha*. This *gezeira shava* — a verbal analogy between the two passages — establishes that just as the *mases* receives *skila*, so too does the *navi shehidiach*: *mah le'halan biskila af kan biskila*.

Rabbi Shimon, however, rejects this *gezeira shava*. The verse in the *navi shehidiach* passage simply says *u'mas* — an unqualified death. The rule is that any *misa* stated in the Torah without specification is *chenek*. Rabbi Shimon therefore holds that the *navi shehidiach* receives *chenek*, rendering the ironic result that a *navi* inciting to *avoda zara* receives a lesser punishment than an ordinary individual doing the same.

### Midichei Ir Nidachas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַדִּיחֵי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת בִּסְקִילָה, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? גְּמִירִי "הַדָּחָה" "הַדָּחָה", אוֹ מִמֵּסִית, אוֹ מִנָּבִיא שֶׁהִדִּיחַ. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן גָּמַר "הַדָּחָה" "הַדָּחָה" מִנָּבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Those **who incite** residents of a city to worship idols, leading the city to be declared **an idolatrous city,** are executed **by stoning. What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis? They derive** a verbal analogy to clarify the meaning of the word **incitement** written with regard to those who incite residents of a city to worship idols, leading the city to be declared an idolatrous city, **from** the word **incitement** written **either** with regard to a layman who **instigates** others to worship idols **or from** the word incitement written with regard to **a prophet who incites** others to worship idols. Just as there, the layman who incites others to worship idols is executed by stoning, so too here, the prophet who incites others to worship idols is executed by stoning.
>
> **And Rabbi Shimon derives** a verbal analogy to clarify the meaning of the word **incitement** written with regard to those who incite residents of a city to worship idols, leading the city to be declared an idolatrous city, **from** the word **incitement** written with regard to **a prophet** who incites others to worship idols, who, in his opinion, is executed by strangulation.

The same debate plays out regarding the *midichei ir nidachas*. The Rabbis are consistent: since the *mases* gets *skila* and since the same *gezeira shava* of *hadacha* language applies here as well — either drawn from the *mases* directly, or from the *navi shehidiach* who already received *skila* — the *midichei ir nidachas* also receive *skila*.

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, draws the *gezeira shava* of *hadacha hadacha* from the *navi* rather than from the *mases*. Having established that the *navi shehidiach* receives *chenek*, Rabbi Shimon applies the same ruling to the *midichei ir nidachas*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְמַר מִמֵּסִית! דָּנִין מֵסִית רַבִּים מִמֵּסִית רַבִּים, וְאֵין דָּנִין מֵסִית רַבִּים מִמֵּסִית יָחִיד. אַדְּרַבָּה, דָּנִין הֶדְיוֹט מֵהֶדְיוֹט, וְאֵין דָּנִין הֶדְיוֹט מִנָּבִיא! וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִדִּיחַ – אֵין לְךָ הֶדְיוֹט גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Let him derive** the punishment **from** the punishment of one who is not a prophet who **instigates** others to worship idols, as those cases are similar. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon **derives** the punishment for one who **instigates the multitudes from** the punishment of one who **instigates the multitudes, and does not derive** the punishment for one who **instigates the multitudes from** the punishment of one who **instigates an individual.** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary, one derives** the punishment for **an ordinary** person, i.e., one who is not a prophet, who instigates others **from** the punishment of **an ordinary** person who incites an idolatrous city, **and one does not derive** the punishment for **an ordinary** person who instigates others **from** the punishment of **a prophet** who instigates others.
>
> **And Rabbi Shimon** holds that in this case there is no distinction between prophet and layman; **once** the prophet has **incited** others to idol worship, **you have no greater** example of **an ordinary** person **than that.**

The Gemara presses Rabbi Shimon: why not learn the *midichei ir nidachas* from the *mases* instead? The answer is *danin mases rabbim mimases rabbim*: the *midichei ir nidachas* are a case involving the masses, and the *navi shehidiach* similarly involves the masses. It is therefore more appropriate to draw the analogy from that case rather than from the individual *mases*, which involves a *yachid*. The Gemara then suggests the opposite — perhaps the ordinary people who are *midichei ir nidachas* should be compared to the ordinary *mases*, not to the *navi*. To this, Rabbi Shimon replies: *keivan shehidiach ein lecha hediyot gadol mizeh* — a *navi* who incites to *avoda zara* has forfeited his prophetic status and is no more than a common person. Once the *navi* is placed in the *chenek* category, the *midichei ir nidachas* follow.

### Rav Chisda Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּעוֹקֵר הַגּוּף דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְקִיּוּם מִקְצָת וּבִיטּוּל מִקְצָת דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: "מִן הַדֶּרֶךְ" – אֲפִילּוּ מִקְצָת הַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:**
> **The dispute is with regard to** a prophet who prophesies to **abolish the essence of** the prohibition of **idol worship, or** a prophet who espouses **fulfillment** of **part of** the prohibition **and nullification** of **part of** the prohibition **of idol worship,** asserting that idol worship is permitted in certain circumstances, **as the Merciful One says:** "And that prophet…shall be put to death; because he has spoken perversion against the Lord…to divert you **from the way** on which the Lord your God commanded you to walk" (Deuteronomy 13:6). It may be inferred from this verse: If he diverts you **even** from **part of the way.** In these cases, Rabbi Shimon holds that he is executed by strangulation, and the Rabbis hold that he is executed by stoning.

Rav Chisda steps back to clarify what kind of case the *navi shehidiach* involves. He explains that the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon concerns a *navi* who is either *oker haguf de'avoda kochavim* — completely uprooting the prohibition against *avoda zara*, effectively saying all idolatry is now permitted — or who is engaged in *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas de'avoda kochavim*, partially permitting some forms of idolatry while prohibiting others. Either of these falls within the scope of Devarim 13 and is the subject of the dispute.

## Oker Haguf vs. Miktzas

### Two Types False Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל עוֹקֵר הַגּוּף דִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּחֶנֶק. וְקִיּוּם מִקְצָת וּבִיטּוּל מִקְצָת דְּבִשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if a prophet **abolishes the essence of** the **rest of** the **mitzvot, everyone agrees** that he is executed **by strangulation. And** if the prophet espouses **fulfillment of part and nullification of part of other mitzvot, everyone agrees** that he is **exempt** from punishment. If a true prophet espouses temporary nullification of a specific mitzva, one must heed his words.

To understand Rav Chisda's framework fully, it is necessary to distinguish between two categories of false prophecy. The first is the *navi shehidiach*, the prophet who incites to *avoda zara*, governed by Devarim 13. The second is the *navi sheker* more broadly — a prophet who testifies to a prophecy he did not receive — which is governed by Devarim 18 and is the subject of the *mishna* we encountered earlier. The punishment for the *navi sheker* is *chenek*, and that is undisputed.

The question Rav Chisda is addressing is how to situate various types of prophetic transgression within one or the other of these two categories.

### Devarim 13 vs. 18

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: "לָלֶכֶת" – זוֹ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה, "בָּהּ" – זוֹ מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, עֲשֵׂה בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: "וְנִתַּצְתֶּם".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** from a *baraita* that interprets the phrase in the verse "to divert you from the way on which the Lord your God commanded you to walk." **"To walk"; this** is a reference to **a positive mitzva. "On which"; this** is a reference to **a prohibition. And if it enters your mind** that the verse is stated **with regard to idol worship, how can you find a positive mitzva** relating to **idol worship?** The Gemara answers: **Rav Ḥisda interpreted** that the positive mitzva referred to in the verse is: **"And you shall smash** their altars, and break their pillars, and burn their *asherim* in fire; and you shall hew down the graven images of their gods; and you shall destroy their name from that place" (Deuteronomy 12:3). A prophet is liable to receive the death penalty if he abolishes this mitzva.

The relevant verse in Devarim 13 reads: *ki yakum bekirbecha navi o cholem chalom* — if a prophet or dreamer arises and gives you a sign or wonder, and says *nelcha acharei elohim acherim*, let us follow other gods and worship them. The *navi* is inducing *avoda zara*. The verse then mandates his execution: *umes hanavi hahu*, and it concludes with *lehadi-chacha min haderech asher tzivecha Hashem elokecha laleches bah* — to lead you astray from the path that God commanded you to walk.

The relevant verse in Devarim 18 is different in character. It reads: *ach hanavi asher yazid ledaber davar bishmi es asher lo tzivisiv ledaber* — the prophet who presumptuously speaks in My name what I did not command him — *va'asher yedaber beshem elohim acherim umes hanavi hahu*. That *umes* is unqualified, yielding *chenek*. This is the generic *navi sheker* — not someone inciting to *avoda zara*, but someone falsely claiming prophetic authority.

One marker that distinguishes the *navi sheker* from the *navi shehidiach* is *oker kol haguf*: a *navi* who claims there is no longer a *mitzvah* to perform some commandment — say, that *tefillin* are no longer required — is *oker kol haguf* with respect to that *mitzvah*, which identifies him as a *navi sheker* and places him squarely in the Devarim 18 category, carrying *chenek* by consensus. By contrast, a *navi* who temporarily overrides a *mitzvah* in a specific circumstance — as Eliyahu did on Har HaCarmel — is not a *navi sheker* at all. That kind of partial, time-bound override is permitted and carries no liability.

### Rav Chisda: Laleches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמִּתְנַבֵּא לַעֲקוֹר דָּבָר מִן הַתּוֹרָה – חַיָּיב. לְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת וּלְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר. וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אֲפִילּוּ אוֹמֵר: "הַיּוֹם עִיבְדוּהָ וּלְמָחָר בַּטְּלוּהָ" – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** In the case of **one who prophesies to abolish a matter from the Torah,** he is deemed a false prophet and is **liable** to be executed by strangulation. If he prophesies **to fulfill part or to nullify part** of a matter in the Torah, **Rabbi Shimon deems** him **exempt** from execution. **And with regard to idol worship, even** if he **says: Worship it today and tomorrow nullify it, everyone agrees** that he is **liable.**

Rav Chisda's framework is challenged by a *braisa* that expounds on the word *laleches* in the verse from Devarim 13. The *braisa* derives: *laleches* — this refers to a *mitzvas asei*; *bah* — this refers to a *mitzvas lo sa'asei*. The implication is that the case of the *navi shehidiach* in Devarim 13 can encompass even a prophet who leads people astray from a positive commandment, not only one who incites to outright *avoda zara*.

This creates a difficulty for Rav Chisda, who limits Devarim 13 to cases involving *avoda zara*. If the passage is solely about *avoda zara*, what would a *mitzvas asei* in that context even look like, since all the prohibitions of *avoda zara* are negative commandments? Rav Chisda resolves this by interpreting *ve'nitz'atzem* — the positive commandment to destroy places of *avoda zara* worship. A *navi* who tells people not to fulfill this positive obligation of demolishing idolatrous shrines would be included in the *navi shehidiach* category under Devarim 13. This preserves Rav Chisda's position that Devarim 13 remains entirely within the domain of *avoda zara*.

### Rav Hamnuna View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּעוֹקֵר הַגּוּף, בֵּין בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בֵּין בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת, וְקִיּוּם מִקְצָת וּבִיטּוּל מִקְצָת דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: "מִן הַדֶּרֶךְ", אֲפִילּוּ מִקְצָת הַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Although his objection was resolved, **Rav Hamnuna** suggests an alternative explanation of the dispute and **says: The dispute is with regard to** a prophet who prophesies to **abolish the essence** of a mitzva, **whether** it is **with regard to idol worship or with regard to** the **rest of** the **mitzvot; or** a prophet who espouses **fulfillment** of **part of** the prohibition **and nullification** of **part of** the prohibition **of idol worship.** This is **as the Merciful One says: "From the way,"** from which it may be inferred: If he diverts you from **even part of the way.** In these cases, Rabbi Shimon holds that he is executed by strangulation, and the Rabbis hold that he is executed by stoning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קִיּוּם מִקְצָת וּבִיטּוּל מִקְצָת, דְּבִשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the prophet espouses **fulfillment of part and nullification of part of other mitzvot, everyone agrees** that he is **exempt** from punishment, as that is within the prophet's mandate.

Rav Hamnuna challenges Rav Chisda's framework directly. In his view, the category of *oker haguf* — completely uprooting a principle — is itself what defines the *navi shehidiach* classification, regardless of whether the subject matter is *avoda zara* or other *mitzvos*. A *navi* who claims there is no longer any obligation to observe *tzitzis*, for example, is not simply a *navi sheker* in the narrow Devarim 18 sense; he is fundamentally challenging the authority of the Torah, which is conceptually equivalent to inciting *avoda zara*. Both represent a root-level attack on the foundations of faith, not merely a false or unauthorized prophecy.

Under Rav Hamnuna's view, then, the line between Devarim 13 and Devarim 18 is drawn differently. Devarim 13 applies to any case of *oker haguf* — whether the *navi* is uprooting *avoda zara* restrictions or any other *mitzvah*. The *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas* of *avoda zara* also falls under Devarim 13 since the Torah explicitly says *min haderech afilu miktzas haderech* — even a partial deviation regarding *avoda zara* places one in the graver category. The word *laleches* accordingly refers to *oker kol haguf* by any *mitzvas asei*, placing that squarely within the *navi shehidiach* category. And *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas* by *shear mitzvos* — other commandments — remains exempt entirely, as that is the domain of legitimate prophetic override.

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וּמְתָרֵץ לַהּ כְּרַב חִסְדָּא: הַמִּתְנַבֵּא לַעֲקוֹר דָּבָר מִן הַתּוֹרָה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּחֶנֶק. לְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת וּלְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְרַבָּנַן. וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר "הַיּוֹם עִיבְדוּהָ וּלְמָחָר בַּטְּלוּהָ" – חַיָּיב. מָר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ, וּמַר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Abaye holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda and explains** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda.** In the case of **one who prophesies to abolish a matter from the Torah, everyone agrees** that he is executed **by strangulation.** If he prophesies **to fulfill part or to nullify part** of a Torah law, **Rabbi Shimon deems** him **exempt** from execution, **and the same is true of the Rabbis,** who also hold that he is exempt. **And with regard to idol worship, even if he says: Worship it today and nullify it tomorrow,** everyone agrees that he is **liable** to be executed. What form of death penalty is imposed? One **Sage,** the Rabbis, rules **in accordance with** what **he holds,** i.e., stoning, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, rules **in accordance with** what **he holds,** i.e., strangulation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא, וּמְתָרֵץ לַהּ כְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא: הַמִּתְנַבֵּא לַעֲקוֹר דָּבָר מִן הַתּוֹרָה, בֵּין בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בֵּין בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת – חַיָּיב, מָר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ וּמַר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ. לְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת וּלְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְרַבָּנַן. וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אֲפִילּוּ אוֹמֵר "הַיּוֹם עִיבְדוּהָ וּלְמָחָר בַּטְּלוּהָ" – חַיָּיב, מָר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ וּמַר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna and explains** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna.** In the case of **one who prophesies to abolish a matter from the Torah, whether** it is **with regard to idol worship or with regard to** the **rest of** the **mitzvot,** he is **liable** to be executed. What form of death penalty is imposed? One **Sage,** the Rabbis, rules **in accordance with** what **he holds,** i.e., stoning, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, rules **in accordance with** what **he holds,** i.e., strangulation. If he prophesies **to fulfill part or to nullify part** of a Torah law other than idol worship, **Rabbi Shimon deems** him **exempt** from execution, **and the same is true of the Rabbis,** who also hold that he is exempt. **And with regard to idol worship, even** if **he says: Worship it today and nullify it tomorrow,** everyone agrees that he is **liable** to be executed. What form of death penalty is imposed? One **Sage,** the Rabbis, rules **in accordance with** what **he holds,** i.e., stoning, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, rules **in accordance with** what **he holds,** i.e., strangulation.

The dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Hamnuna generates a corresponding dispute between Abaye and Rava in their reading of the *braisa*. The *braisa* states: one who prophesies to uproot a principle of the Torah is *chayav*; one who prophesies to establish some and cancel some — Rabbi Shimon exempts; and regarding *avoda zara*, even if he says worship it today and abolish it tomorrow, all agree he is *chayav*.

Abaye follows Rav Chisda. On his reading, the first clause — *hamisnabe l'akor davar min hatorah chayav* — refers to *shear mitzvos* that are completely uprooted. This places the *navi* in the *navi sheker* category of Devarim 18, and the liability is *chenek*, with no dispute. The second clause — *l'kayem miktzas u'levatel miktzas* — addresses *shear mitzvos* with partial override; here Rabbi Shimon (and in truth all opinions) concur that there is no liability, since such override is the legitimate domain of prophetic authority. The final clause about *avoda zara* governs the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon, with the Rabbis imposing *skila* and Rabbi Shimon *chenek* — *mar ki isa u'mar ki isa*.

Rava follows Rav Hamnuna. On his reading, the opening clause — *hamisnabe l'akor davar min hatorah* — is not limited to *shear mitzvos* but applies broadly: whether the *navi* is uprooting *avoda zara* restrictions or any other *mitzvah*, if he is *oker kol haguf*, he falls into the *navi shehidiach* category of Devarim 13. The clause *l'kayem miktzas u'levatel miktzas* then addresses *shear mitzvos* with partial override, and all agree there is no liability. The final clause about *avoda zara* — even temporary worship — again belongs to the *navi shehidiach* category, with the liability following each authority's position: *mar ki isa u'mar ki isa*.

## True Prophet Override

### Prophet Override Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּכֹּל, אִם יֹאמַר לְךָ נָבִיא "עֲבוֹר עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה" – שְׁמַע לוֹ, חוּץ מֵעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ מַעֲמִיד לְךָ חַמָּה בְּאֶמְצַע הָרָקִיעַ – אַל תִּשְׁמַע לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says,** in summary: **With regard to all** mitzvot, **if a prophet will say to you: Violate matters of Torah** on a provisional basis, **heed him, except for idol worship, as even** if he establishes that he is a bona fide prophet and **stops the sun for you in the middle of the sky, do not heed him.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav Yochanan states a general principle: in all cases, if a *navi* instructs you to transgress a Torah commandment, you must listen to him — except when it comes to *avoda zara*. This is consistent with the framework developed above. A legitimate *navi* exercising *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas* over *shear mitzvos* is operating within the proper scope of prophetic authority, and one is obligated to comply. The sole exception is *avoda zara*, which is always in the *navi shehidiach* category — never a case of legitimate override. This is true no matter how compelling the sign he produces: even if he were to halt the sun in the middle of the sky, one must not heed him.

### God's Test Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: הִגִּיעָה תּוֹרָה לְסוֹף דַּעְתָּהּ שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, לְפִיכָךְ נָתְנָה תּוֹרָה מֶמְשָׁלָה בָּהּ, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ מַעֲמִיד לְךָ חַמָּה בְּאֶמְצַע הָרָקִיעַ – אַל תִּשְׁמַע לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: The Torah ascertained the depth of the mentality of idol worship,** the danger that it presents, and the lure of its ideology. **Therefore, the Torah ascribed** the false prophet with **dominion in its** regard, recognizing that a false prophet could perform wonders on the basis of idol worship. Therefore, **even** if the false prophet **stops the sun for you in the middle of the sky, do not heed him.**

Rabbi Yossi HaGlili offers an explanation for why the Torah permits such extreme temptation. The Torah assessed the full danger and power of *avoda zara* and therefore granted a kind of authority even to those prophesying in its name. The intention was to test the people: could they withstand even the most dramatic signs and wonders produced in favor of idolatry? The instruction not to heed such a prophet — no matter how spectacular his demonstration — is precisely because the Torah knows the temptation is real and formidable. The precise formulation of this idea is admittedly difficult to parse, but the thrust is that the Torah deliberately allows for the possibility of extreme temptation in this domain, in order to train Israel to resist it absolutely.

### Rabbi Akiva Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַעֲמִיד חַמָּה לְעוֹבְרֵי רְצוֹנוֹ! אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עַזּוּר, שֶׁמִּתְּחִלָּתוֹ נְבִיא אֱמֶת, וּלְבַסּוֹף נְבִיא שֶׁקֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: Heaven forfend that the Holy One, Blessed be He,** would **stop the sun for** those who **violate His will.** A false prophet could never perform an actual miracle. **Rather,** this warning is relevant only in the case of a prophet, **for example, Hananiah, son of Azzur, whose origin** was as **a true prophet,** at which point he could perform miracles; **and ultimately,** he was **a false prophet.** Therefore, although he had already been established as a true prophet, once he espouses idol worship, it is clear that he is a false prophet.

Rabbi Akiva objects. It is, he says, inconceivable — *chas v'shalom* — that God would empower a violator of His will to produce miraculous signs in the service of *avoda zara*. The more plausible explanation for how such a person could display apparent wonders is the case of someone like Chananya ben Azur: a person who was initially a true prophet and received genuine prophetic capacity from God, but who subsequently became a *rasha*. The signs he is able to produce are residual from his prior authentic prophetic status. That is how a person can seem to perform wonders while inciting to *avoda zara* — not because God grants such power to transgressors, but because the power was granted earlier, when he was still a true prophet.

## Chenek: Edim & Others

### Edim Zomemin & Bat Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹמְמֵי בַּת כֹּהֵן – מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו"? לְפִי שֶׁכׇּל הַמְזוּמָּמִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, זוֹמְמֵיהֶן וּבוֹעֲלֵיהֶן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And conspiring** witnesses who testified that **the daughter of a priest** and her paramour committed adultery are executed by strangulation. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, says:** It is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: What** is the meaning when **the verse states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: **"And you shall do to him as he conspired to do to his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:19)? The verse is formulated in this manner **due to** the fact **that** in **all** cases involving those **rendered conspiring** witnesses **who are** mentioned **in the Torah,** e.g., forbidden relatives, the sentences of **their conspiring** witnesses **and their paramours are similar** to their own sentences, and the witnesses are executed by strangulation.

The *mishna* concludes its enumeration of those who receive *chenek* by addressing *edim zomemim* — perjured witnesses — in the case of a *bas Kohen* who commits adultery while married, and the men involved in that adultery. The distinctive feature of this case is that the *bas Kohen* herself receives *sreifa* — burning — while the adulterer receives *chenek*, the standard punishment for adultery by an ordinary married woman. The *edim zomemim* who conspired to have her executed also receive *chenek*.

### Rabbi Yossi Gezeira

The Gemara asks for the source of these rulings. Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika cites a *braisa* in which Rabbi Yossi expounds the verse *v'asisem lo ka'asher zamam la'asos l'achiv* — you shall do to him as he plotted to do to his brother. This establishes a general principle: wherever *edim zomemim* appear in the Torah, they receive the same punishment that would have been inflicted on the accused, and similarly, those who commit *arayos* with a woman receive the punishment applicable to her relationship. The difficulty arises when, as in the case of *bas Kohen*, the man and the woman receive different punishments.

### Zomemin Follow Man

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת כֹּהֵן הִיא בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, וְאֵין בּוֹעֲלָהּ בִּשְׂרֵיפָה. זוֹמְמִין – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם לוֹ הוּקְשׁוּ, אִם לָהּ הוּקְשׁוּ. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר "לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו" – לְאָחִיו וְלֹא לַאֲחוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **The daughter of a priest** is an exception, as **she** is executed **by burning** if she commits adultery, **and her paramour is not** executed **by burning.** With regard to **conspiring** witnesses who testified that they committed adultery, **I do not know if the** witnesses **are likened to** the paramour or **if** they **are likened to** the daughter of the priest, in terms of punishment. **When** the verse **states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: "As he conspired **to do to his brother,"** one infers: As he conspired to do **to his brother, but not** as he conspired to do **to his sister.** The execution of the conspiring witnesses is likened to the execution of the paramour, who is executed by strangulation, and is not likened to the execution of the daughter of the priest, who is executed by burning.

In the case of the *bas Kohen*, the Torah specifies *hi ba'esh tisaref* — she shall be burned — but the men involved are not subject to *sreifa*. The question then is: when the *edim zomemim* are refuted, do they follow the punishment designated for her or for him? The Gemara resolves this from the verse *la'asos leachiv* — what he plotted to do to his brother, not to his sister. In a case where the man's and woman's judgments diverge, the *edim zomemim* follow the man's ruling, and therefore receive *chenek* rather than *sreifa*.

With that, the *perek* of *eilu hein hanichnakin* is complete, and the masechta turns to its final chapter.

## Olam HaBa Introduction

### Kol Yisrael Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל יֵשׁ לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְעַמֵּךְ כֻּלָּם צַדִּיקִים לְעוֹלָם יִירְשׁוּ אָרֶץ נֵצֶר מַטָּעַי מַעֲשֵׂה יָדַי לְהִתְפָּאֵר".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All of the Jewish people,** even sinners and those who are liable to be executed with a court-imposed death penalty, **have a share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And your people also shall be all righteous, they shall inherit the land forever; the branch of My planting, the work of My hands, for My name to be glorified"** (Isaiah 60:21).

The last *perek* of Sanhedrin opens with the famous declaration: *kol Yisrael yeish lahem cheilek l'Olam HaBa* — all Israel has a portion in the world to come — deriving this from the verse *ve'ameich kulam tzadikim le'olam yirshu aretz netzer matai ma'aseh yadai lehispa'er*: your people are all righteous; they shall inherit the land forever, the branch of My planting, the work of My hands, that I may be glorified.

It is worth noting that this opening declaration was not originally part of the *mishna* itself. Rashi remarks on this — and though his comment appears to have been excised, the Maharsha preserves it. The *mishna* actually began with *eilu she'ein lahem cheilek l'Olam HaBa*, a list of exclusions. The affirmative declaration *kol Yisrael yeish lahem cheilek l'Olam HaBa* was prepended precisely because it would be jarring to begin with the exclusions without first establishing that the default is inclusion. The opening therefore functions as a frame: everyone has a portion — *except* the following.

This framing, however, sits in tension with other teachings in *Shas* that suggest only *tzadikim* have a portion in *Olam HaBa*. On Rosh Hashanah, for instance, *tzadikim* are immediately granted *Olam HaBa*, *reshaim* face *Gehennom*, and *beinonim* are suspended in judgment. Similarly, when one converts, one is told not to assume that conversion automatically confers *Olam HaBa* — *Olam HaBa eino ela letzadikim*. The *mishna* here bridges this tension through the verse *ve'ameich kulam tzadikim*: by default, all Jews are classified as *tzadikim*. There is a genuine unresolved strand of tradition, however, as to whether *Olam HaBa* is a default that can only be forfeited, or an achievement that must be earned.

### Excluded Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא: הָאוֹמֵר אֵין תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וְאֵין תּוֹרָה מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, וְאֶפִּיקוֹרוֹס. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אַף הַקּוֹרֵא בַּסְּפָרִים הַחִיצוֹנִים, וְהַלּוֹחֵשׁ עַל הַמַּכָּה וְאוֹמֵר "כׇּל הַמַּחֲלָה אֲשֶׁר שַׂמְתִּי בְמִצְרַיִם לֹא אָשִׂים עָלֶיךָ כִּי אֲנִי ה׳ רֹפְאֶךָ". אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַהוֹגֶה אֶת הַשֵּׁם בְּאוֹתִיּוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** are the exceptions, the people **who have no share in the World-to-Come,** even when they fulfilled many mitzvot: **One who says: There is no resurrection of the dead** derived **from the Torah, and** one who says: The **Torah** did **not** originate **from Heaven, and an *epikoros*,** who treats Torah scholars and the Torah that they teach with contempt.
>
> **Rabbi Akiva says: Also** included in the exceptions are **one who reads external literature, and one who whispers** invocations **over a wound and says** as an invocation for healing: **"Every illness that I placed upon Egypt I will not place upon you, for I am the Lord, your Healer"** (Exodus 15:26). By doing so, he shows contempt for the sanctity of the name of God and therefore has no share in the World-to-Come. **Abba Shaul says: Also** included in the exceptions is **one who pronounces the** ineffable **name** of God as it is written, **with its letters.**

The *mishna* then enumerates those who forfeit their portion. The first category is one who says *ein techiyas hameisim min haTorah* — there is no resurrection of the dead rooted in the Torah. Note that the disqualification is not merely denying resurrection as such; one must specifically deny that it is derived from the Torah. Second is one who denies *Torah min hashamayim*. Third is the *apikorus*, whose precise meaning the Gemara will elaborate upon.

Rabbi Akiva adds two further categories: one who reads in *seforim chitzoniyim* — external books excluded from the canon — and one who whispers an incantation over a wound while reciting the verse *kol hamachalah asher samti bemitzrayim lo asim alecha ki ani Hashem rofecha*. Even citing a *pasuk* as part of a healing incantation places one in this category.

At first glance, these final two exclusions seem categorically different from denying *techiyas hameisim* or *Torah min hashamayim*. Reading sectarian literature or whispering a verse over a wound appears far less severe than denying foundational doctrines of faith. One way to understand the grouping is that *Chazal* are drawing lines of communal identity and sectarianism: there were sects in their time — likely including certain strands of Jewish Christianity — that denied *techiyas hameisim* and *Torah min hashamayim*, and some of these practices were associated with those movements. The exclusions may therefore share a common thread: each marks one as aligned with a sectarian movement that has severed its connection with mainstream Israel, whether through doctrinal denial or through characteristic sectarian behavior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים וְאַרְבָּעָה הֶדְיוֹטוֹת אֵין לָהֶן חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. שְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים: יָרׇבְעָם, אַחְאָב, וּמְנַשֶּׁה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְנַשֶּׁה יֵשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "וַיִּתְפַּלֵּל אֵלָיו וַיִּשְׁמַע תְּחִנָּתוֹ וַיְשִׁיבֵהוּ יְרוּשָׁלִַם לְמַלְכוּתוֹ". אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לְמַלְכוּתוֹ הֱשִׁיבוֹ וְלֹא לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא הֱשִׁיבוֹ. אַרְבָּעָה הֶדְיוֹטוֹת: בִּלְעָם, וְדוֹאֵג, וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, וְגֵחֲזִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Three** prominent **kings** mentioned in the Bible **and four** prominent **commoners** who are described in the Bible as men of great wisdom **have no share in the World-to-Come.** The **three kings** are: **Jeroboam,** son of Nebat, and **Ahab,** both of whom were kings of Israel, **and Manasseh,** king of Judea. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Manasseh has a share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated** concerning Manasseh: **"And he prayed to Him,** and He received his entreaty, **and heard his supplication and brought him back to Jerusalem unto his kingdom"** (II Chronicles 33:13), indicating that he repented wholeheartedly and effectively. The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yehuda: He regretted his actions, and his repentance was effective to the extent that God **restored him to his kingdom, but** God **did not restore him to his** share in **life in the World-to-Come.** The **four commoners** are: **Balaam,** son of Beor; **Doeg** the Edomite; **Ahithophel; and Gehazi.**

Abba Shaul adds a final exclusion: *hahogeh es Hashem be'osyosov* — one who articulates the divine name as it is spelled, letter by letter.

The *mishna* then lists specific historical figures who, despite being Israelites, do not receive a portion in *Olam HaBa*. Three kings are named: Yerovam, Achav, and Menashe. Rabbi Yehuda holds that Menashe does have a portion, citing the verse *vayitpalel eilav* — Menashe prayed to God — and God heard his plea and restored him to Jerusalem and his kingship. The Sages respond: God restored him to his *malchus*, his earthly kingship, but not to *chayei Olam HaBa*.

Four commoners complete the list: Bilam, Doeg, Achitofel, and Geichazi. The Gemara will develop each of these cases in turn.

## Resurrection Proofs

### Denial Consequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? תָּנָא: הוּא כָּפַר בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, לְפִיכָךְ לֹא יִהְיֶה לוֹ חֵלֶק בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, שֶׁכׇּל מִדּוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדָּה כְּנֶגֶד מִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And why** is one punished **to that extent** for saying that there is no resurrection of the dead derived from the Torah? The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **He denied the resurrection of the dead; therefore he will not have a share in the resurrection of the dead, as all measures** dispensed **by the Holy One, Blessed be He,** to His people are dispensed **measure for measure,** i.e., the response is commensurate with the action.

The Gemara asks: why does one who denies *techiyas hameisim min haTorah* forfeit a portion in *Olam HaBa*? The answer is measure for measure: *hu kafar b'techiyat hameisim l'fichach lo yihiyeh lo chelek b'techiyat hameisim*. By denying resurrection, one disqualifies oneself from experiencing it. In identifying the consequence this way, the Gemara implicitly equates *Olam HaBa* in this context with *techiyas hameisim* — not the individual soul's existence after death, but the future resurrection of the body in historical time. This is worth pausing over. The Rambam's conception of *Olam HaBa* centers on the immortal soul's existence in a purely spiritual realm, a world that runs parallel to ours and receives souls after death. The Gemara's *pshat* here is quite different: *Olam HaBa* is the world that will arrive in the future, the era of resurrection, the culmination of history. The one who denies it will not share in it.

### Mida K'Neged Mida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁכׇּל מִדּוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדָּה כְּנֶגֶד מִדָּה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלִישָׁע שִׁמְעוּ דְּבַר ה׳ [וְגוֹ׳] כָּעֵת מָחָר סְאָה סֹלֶת בְּשֶׁקֶל וְסָאתַיִם שְׂעֹרִים בְּשֶׁקֶל בְּשַׁעַר שֹׁמְרוֹן". וּכְתִיב: "וַיַּעַן הַשָּׁלִישׁ אֲשֶׁר הַמֶּלֶךְ נִשְׁעָן עַל יָדוֹ אֶת אִישׁ הָאֱלֹהִים וַיֹּאמַר הִנֵּה ה׳ עוֹשֶׂה אֲרֻבּוֹת בַּשָּׁמַיִם הֲיִהְיֶה הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה וַיֹּאמֶר הִנְּךָ רֹאֶה בְּעֵינֶיךָ וּמִשָּׁם לֹא תֹאכֵל".
>
> **Translation:** This is based on that **which Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: From where** is it derived **that all measures** dispensed **by the Holy One, Blessed be He,** are dispensed **measure for measure?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** concerning the siege of Jerusalem: **"And Elisha said: Hear the word of the Lord;** so said the Lord: **Tomorrow at this time one *se'a* of fine flour will be sold for one shekel and two *se'a* of barley for one shekel in the gate of Samaria"** (II Kings 7:1). **And it is written: "And the officer on whose hand the king leaned answered the man of God and said: Will the Lord make windows in heaven? Might this thing be? And he said: You shall see it with your eyes, but you shall not eat from there"** (II Kings 7:2).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: "וַיְהִי לוֹ כֵּן וַיִּרְמְסוּ אֹתוֹ הָעָם בַּשַּׁעַר וַיָּמֹת". וְדִילְמָא קִלְלַת אֱלִישָׁע גְּרַמָה לֵיהּ? דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: קִלְלַת חָכָם, אֲפִילּוּ עַל חִנָּם הִיא בָּאָה. אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: "וַיִּרְמְסוּהוּ וַיָּמֹת". מַאי "בַּשַּׁעַר"? עַל עִסְקֵי שַׁעַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written: "And it was for him so, and the people trampled him in the gate, and he died"** (II Kings 7:20). The Gemara challenges: **Perhaps** it was **the curse of Elisha** that **caused** the officer to die in that manner, not the principle of punishment measure for measure for his lack of belief, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: The curse of a Sage, even** if **baseless, comes** to be fulfilled? This is all the more so true concerning the curse of Elisha, which was warranted. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse write: And they trampled him and he died. What** does the term **"in the gate"** serve to teach? It teaches that he died **over matters** relating **to the gate [*sha'ar*].** It was for the cynical dismissal of the prophecy of Elisha that the officer voiced at the city gate that he was punished measure for measure and was trampled at the city gate.

The Gemara then pauses to establish the principle of *mida k'neged mida* as a divine operating principle. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan derives it from the episode of Elisha and the officer. Elisha had prophesied during a famine that by the next day grain would be available at remarkably cheap prices at the gates of Shomron. The officer upon whom the king leaned scoffed: could God make windows in the heavens? Elisha responded: *hincha ro'eh b'einecha u'misham lo tochal* — you will see it with your own eyes, but you will not eat from it.

Indeed, as the verse records, the Aramean army panicked and fled, leaving abundant provisions, and the people rushed out through the gate — trampling the officer there, exactly as foretold.

The Gemara challenges: perhaps the officer's fate was simply due to Elisha's personal curse, since the curse of a sage takes effect even when unwarranted (*kilelat chacham afilu al chinam hi ba'ah*). If so, this episode merely demonstrates the power of a sage's curse, not a general divine principle of *mida k'neged mida*. The Gemara responds: if that were the entire point, the verse need only have said that they trampled him and he died. Why specify *b'sha'ar* — at the gate? The word *sha'ar* echoes the context of the grain prices at the gate — *b'sha'ar Shomron* — and thus the specific detail signals that the punishment was calibrated to the sin: he mocked the prophecy about the *sha'ar*, and he was destroyed at the *sha'ar*. This demonstrates that an overarching principle of *mida k'neged mida* is at work.

### Terumah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וּנְתַתֶּם מִמֶּנּוּ [אֶת] תְּרוּמַת ה׳ לְאַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן". וְכִי אַהֲרֹן לְעוֹלָם קַיָּים? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נִכְנַס לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין לוֹ תְּרוּמָה! אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָתִיד לִחְיוֹת, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹתְנִין לוֹ תְּרוּמָה. מִכָּאן לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From where** is the **resurrection of the dead** derived **from the Torah?** It is derived from this verse, **as it is stated** with regard to *teruma* of the tithe: **"And you shall give the *teruma* of the Lord to Aaron the priest"** (Numbers 18:28). **And does Aaron exist forever** so that one can fulfill the mitzva by giving him the *teruma* of the tithe? **But is it not** so that Aaron **did not enter Eretz Yisrael,** the only place **where** the people would **give him *teruma*? Rather,** the verse **teaches that** Aaron is destined **to live in the future and the Jewish people** will **give him *teruma*. From here** it is derived that **the resurrection of the dead is from the Torah.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנוּ: "לְאַהֲרֹן" – כְּאַהֲרֹן, מָה אַהֲרֹן חָבֵר – אַף בָּנָיו חֲבֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a different derivation from this verse. From the term **"to Aaron"** one derives that *teruma* must be given to a priest **like Aaron; just as Aaron is one devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot, particularly those relating to ritual purity, *teruma*, and tithes [*ḥaver*], so too** one gives *teruma* to **his descendants** who are ***ḥaverim*.**

The Gemara proceeds to demonstrate *techiyas hameisim* from the Torah itself. The verse states: *venatatem mimenu et terumat Hashem l'Aharon HaKohen* — you shall give from it the *terumah* of God to Aharon the *Kohen*. The Gemara notes: would Aharon actually be alive to receive *terumah* when the Israelites enter the land? He did not enter *Eretz Yisrael* at all. The verse must therefore be teaching that Aharon is destined to be resurrected, and Israel will give him *terumah*. This is the derivation of *techiyas hameisim min haTorah*.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael, however, reads *l'Aharon* not as a literal reference to Aharon himself but as meaning "like Aharon" — a *Kohen* of Aharon's character. The requirement to give *terumah* applies specifically to a *Kohen* who, like Aharon, is a *chaver* — one who can be trusted regarding the laws of *tumah* and *taharah*.

### Terumah Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁאֵין נוֹתְנִין תְּרוּמָה לְכֹהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֹּאמֶר לָעָם לְיוֹשְׁבֵי יְרוּשָׁלִַם לָתֵת מְנָת הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם לְמַעַן יֶחֶזְקוּ בְּתוֹרַת ה׳". כׇּל הַמַּחְזִיק בְּתוֹרַת ה׳ – יֵשׁ לוֹ מְנָת, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַחְזִיק בְּתוֹרַת ה׳ – אֵין לוֹ מְנָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: From where** is it derived **that one does not give *teruma* to a priest** who is **an *am ha'aretz*?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And he commanded the people who dwelled in Jerusalem to give the portion of the priests and of the Levites, so that they may firmly adhere to the Torah of the Lord"** (II Chronicles 31:4). **Everyone who firmly adheres to the Torah of the Lord has a portion, and anyone who does not firmly adhere to the Torah of the Lord does not have a portion.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר אַדָּא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כׇּל הַנּוֹתֵן תְּרוּמָה לְכֹהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ, כְּאִילּוּ נוֹתְנָהּ לִפְנֵי אֲרִי. מָה אֲרִי - סָפֵק דּוֹרֵס וְאוֹכֵל, סָפֵק אֵינוֹ דּוֹרֵס וְאוֹכֵל, אַף כֹּהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ - סָפֵק אוֹכְלָהּ בְּטׇהֳרָה, סָפֵק אוֹכְלָהּ בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Adda says** that **Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **anyone who gives *teruma* to a priest** who is **an *am ha'aretz*, it is as though he placed** the *teruma* **before a lion. Just as** with regard to **a lion,** there is **uncertainty** whether it will **maul** its prey **and eat** it, and **uncertainty** whether it will **not maul** its prey **and** instead **eat** it alive, **so too,** with regard to **a priest** who is **an *am ha'aretz*** to whom one gives *teruma*, there is **uncertainty** whether he will **eat it in purity,** and there is **uncertainty** whether he will **eat it in impurity,** thereby violating a prohibition by Torah law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף גּוֹרֵם לוֹ מִיתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּמֵתוּ בוֹ כִּי יְחַלְּלֻהוּ״. דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב תָּנָא: אַף מַשִּׂיאוֹ עֲוֹן אַשְׁמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהִשִּׂיאוּ אוֹתָם עֲוֹן אַשְׁמָה בְּאׇכְלָם אֶת קׇדְשֵׁיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** One who gives *teruma* to a priest who is an *am ha'aretz* **even causes** the priest's **death, as it is stated** with regard to *teruma*: **"And die therefore if they profane it"** (Leviticus 22:9). Priests who partake of *teruma* in a state of ritual impurity profane it and are liable to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven. **The school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov taught:** By giving *teruma* to a priest who is an *am ha'aretz*, **one also brings upon him a sin of guilt,** i.e., a sin that will lead to additional sins, **as it is stated: "And so bring upon them a sin of guilt when they eat their sacred items"** (Leviticus 22:16).

This leads into a brief but significant digression on the laws governing to whom *terumah* may be given. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yochanan asks: from where do we know that one may not give *terumah* to a *Kohen am ha'aretz* — an unlearned *Kohen* who cannot be relied upon to maintain the proper standards of purity? The source is a verse in Chronicles: *vayomer la'am yoshvei Yerushalayim laten menat haKohanim v'haLevi'im lema'an yechezku b'Torat Hashem* — he said to the people of Jerusalem to give the portions to the *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* so that they may be strengthened in God's Torah. The implication is that only one who strengthens himself in God's Torah has a claim to a *manat* — a portion. One who does not strengthen himself in God's Torah has no such claim.

Rav Acha bar Adda in the name of Rav Yehuda adds that giving *terumah* to a *Kohen am ha'aretz* is like placing it before a lion: just as with a lion one cannot know whether he will claw into his prey and devour it or not, so too one cannot know whether this *Kohen* will eat the *terumah* in a state of *taharah* or *tumah*. Rabbi Yochanan adds that one who gives *terumah* to a *Kohen am ha'aretz* may bring about his death, as the verse states *umetu vo ki yechalelu'u* — they shall die in it if they desecrate it by eating it in *tumah*. The school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov further teaches that one thereby causes the *Kohen* to incur *avon ashmah* — the iniquity of guilt — as the verse states *v'hisi'u otam avon ashmah b'ochlam et kodsheihem*.

### Simai's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי סִימַאי אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְגַם הֲקִמֹתִי אֶת בְּרִיתִי אִתָּם לָתֵת לָהֶם אֶת אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן". "לָכֶם" לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא "לָהֶם". מִכָּאן לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Simai says: From where** is **resurrection of the dead** derived **from the Torah?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** with regard to the Patriarchs: **"I have also established My covenant with them to give to them the land of Canaan"** (Exodus 6:4). The phrase: To give **to you** the land of Canaan, **is not stated,** as the meaning of the verse is not that God fulfilled the covenant with the Patriarchs when he gave the land of Canaan to the children of Israel; **rather,** it is stated: "To give **to them** the land of Canaan," meaning to the Patriarchs themselves. **From here** is it derived that **the resurrection of the dead is from the Torah,** as in the future the Patriarchs will come to life and inherit the land.

After the digression, the Gemara returns to the proofs for *techiyas hameisim*. A *braisa* records Rabbi Simai as asking: from where do we derive *techiyas hameisim min haTorah*? From the verse: *v'gam hakimoti et briti itam latet lahem et Eretz Kena'an* — I established My covenant with them to give *them* the land of Kena'an. The verse does not say *lachem*, to you — the descendants — but *lahem*, to them — the *avot* themselves. Since the *avot* did not receive the land in their lifetimes, the verse implies that they will receive it at the time of the resurrection. This, Rabbi Simai concludes, is a proof for *techiyas hameisim* from the Torah.

### Minim Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (צֶדֶ״ק גַּ״ם גֶּשֶׁ״ם קָ״ם סִימָן) שָׁאֲלוּ צַדּוּקִים אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מִנַּיִין שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְחַיֶּיה מֵתִים? אָמַר לָהֶם: מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וּמִן הַנְּבִיאִים, וּמִן הַכְּתוּבִים. וְלֹא קִיבְּלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a mnemonic for those cited in the upcoming discussion: ***Tzadi*, *dalet*, *kuf*; *gimmel*, *mem*; *gimmel*, *shin*, *mem*; *kuf*, *mem*. Heretics asked Rabban Gamliel: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, revives the dead?** Rabban Gamliel **said to them** that this matter can be proven **from the Torah, from the Prophets, and from Writings, but they did not accept** the proofs **from him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַתּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה הִנְּךָ שֹׁכֵב עִם אֲבֹתֶיךָ וְקָם". אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וְדִילְמָא "וְקָם הָעָם הַזֶּה וְזָנָה"?
>
> **Translation:** The proof **from the Torah** is **as it is written: "And the Lord said to Moses, behold, you shall lie with your fathers and arise"** (Deuteronomy 31:16). The heretics **said to him: But perhaps** the verse should be divided in a different manner, and it should be read: "Behold, you shall lie with your fathers, **and this people will arise and stray** after the foreign gods of the land."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַנְּבִיאִים, דִּכְתִיב: "יִחְיוּ מֵתֶיךָ נְבֵלָתִי יְקוּמוּן הָקִיצוּ וְרַנְּנוּ שֹׁכְנֵי עָפָר כִּי טַל אוֹרֹת טַלֶּךָ וָאָרֶץ רְפָאִים תַּפִּיל". וְדִילְמָ "מֵתִים שֶׁהֶחְיָה יְחֶזְקֵאל?
>
> **Translation:** The proof **from the Prophets** is **as it is written: "Your dead shall live, my corpse shall arise. Awake and sing, you that dwell in the dust, for your dew is as the dew of vegetation, and the land shall cast out the dead"** (Isaiah 26:19). The heretics said to him: **But perhaps** the prophecy was fulfilled with **the dead that Ezekiel revived.** No proof may be cited from that verse with regard to any future resurrection.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַכְּתוּבִים, דִּכְתִיב: "וְחִכֵּךְ כְּיֵין הַטּוֹב הוֹלֵךְ לְדוֹדִי לְמֵישָׁרִים דּוֹבֵב שִׂפְתֵי יְשֵׁנִים". וְדִילְמָ "רַחוֹשֵׁי מְרַחֲשָׁן שִׂפְוָותֵיהּ בְּעָלְמָא"? כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: כׇּל מִי שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרָה הֲלָכָה בִּשְׁמוֹ בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, שִׂפְתוֹתָיו דּוֹבְבוֹת בַּקֶּבֶר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "דּוֹבֵב שִׂפְתֵי יְשֵׁנִים".
>
> **Translation:** The proof **from Writings** is **as it is written: "And your palate is like the best wine that glides down smoothly for my beloved, moving gently the lips of those that sleep"** (Song of Songs 7:10), indicating that the dead will ultimately rise and speak. The heretics said to him: **But perhaps merely their lips will move, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: Anyone in whose name a *halakha* is stated in this world, his lips move in the grave** as if repeating the statement cited in his name, **as it is stated: "Moving gently the lips of those that sleep."** No proof may be cited from that verse, as it is unrelated to resurrection.

The Gemara then relates that *minim* — heretics, often understood as referring to Jewish Christians — challenged Rabban Gamliel: from where do you derive that God resurrects the dead? Rabban Gamliel replied that he could demonstrate it from the Torah, from the *Nevi'im*, and from the *Ketuvim*. He proceeded to offer proofs from each, but the *minim* rejected each one.

From the Torah, he cited the verse *hinecha shochev im avotecha v'kam* — behold, you will lie down with your fathers, and arise. The *minim* responded that the continuation of the verse — *v'kam ha'am hazeh v'zanah acharei elohei nechar ha'aretz* — shows that it is the nation that rises and strays, not that the dead individual will literally be resurrected.

From the *Nevi'im*, he cited *yichyu meisecha neveilasi yakumu* — your dead shall live, my corpses shall arise — and *ki tal orot talecha ve'aretz refaim tapil* — for the dew of light is Your dew. The *minim* responded that perhaps this refers only to the particular resurrection of dry bones performed by Yechezkel, not a general doctrine of universal resurrection.

From the *Ketuvim*, he cited *vechichecha keyayin hatov...dovev siftei yeshenim* — your palate is like good wine...causing the lips of the sleeping to murmur. The *minim* pushed back, suggesting that *dovev siftei yeshenim* does not imply physical resurrection but merely that the lips of the dead murmur — a reference to the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: anyone who cites a *halacha* in the name of a deceased sage causes his lips to murmur in the grave, as it is said *dovev siftei yeshenim*. In other words, the *minim* deflected each proof by offering an alternative reading.

### Final Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁאָמַר לָהֶם מִקְרָא זֶה: "אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּע ה׳ לַאֲבֹתֵיכֶם לָתֵת לָהֶם". "לָכֶם" לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא "לָהֶם". מִיכָּן לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** This exchange continued **until** Rabban Gamliel **stated to them this verse:** "That your days may be multiplied, and the days of your children, upon the land **that the Lord took an oath to your forefathers to give them"** (Deuteronomy 11:21). The phrase: **To** give **you, is not stated; rather,** it is stated: **"To** give **them,"** to the Patriarchs themselves, as in the future the Patriarchs will come to life and inherit the land. **From here resurrection of the dead** is derived **from the Torah.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה אָמַר לָהֶם, "וְאַתֶּם הַדְּבֵקִים בַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם חַיִּים כֻּלְּכֶם הַיּוֹם". פְּשִׁיטָא דְּחַיִּים כֻּלְּכֶם הַיּוֹם! אֶלָּא, אֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹם שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלָּם מֵתִים – אַתֶּם חַיִּים. מָה הַיּוֹם כּוּלְּכֶם קַיָּימִין, אַף לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא כּוּלְּכֶם קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are those who say** that it is **from this** following **verse** that **he said to them** his ultimate proof: **"But you who cleave to the Lord your God every one of you is alive this day"** (Deuteronomy 4:4). Wasn't it **obvious** with regard to the children of Israel whom God was addressing, that **"every one of you is alive this day"? Rather,** the meaning of the verse is: **Even on the day when everyone is dead you will live; just as today every one of you is alive, so too, in the World-to-Come every one of you** will be **alive.**

After each attempted proof was met with a rebuttal, Rabban Gamliel produced a verse they could not contest: *asher nishba Hashem la'avoteichem latet lahem* — which God swore to your forefathers to give *them*. The verse does not say *lachem*, to you, but *lahem*, to them — the *avot* themselves. Since the forefathers never received the land in their lifetimes, the oath implies a future fulfillment through resurrection. This is the same proof Rabbi Simai had offered, and it was decisive. *Mikan l'techiyas hameisim min haTorah.*

### Alternative Proof

The Gemara records an alternative tradition, according to which Rabban Gamliel's clinching proof was a different verse: *ve'atem hadeveikim baHashem elokeichem chayim kulchem hayom* — and you who cleave to God are all alive today. The inference is: just as you are alive today, so too will you remain alive in *Olam HaBa*. *Mah hayom kulchem chayim af l'Olam HaBa kulchem chayim.* The verse thus links continued life to cleaving to God, implying that this bond endures even beyond death — and that those who do cleave will be alive in the world to come as well.
