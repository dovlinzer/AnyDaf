# Sanhedrin 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Navi Shehidiach

### Intro: Navi & Avodah Zar…

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about a *navi* who prophesies something he did not actually receive, or who prophesies in the name of another god — both of which carry the penalty of *chenek*. There is also the related case of one who fails to heed the words of a prophet, which carries *misa b'yedei shamayim*. These categories prompt the broader question of how one identifies a true prophet and why one is obligated to obey him — a question that ultimately opens into the *agadata* of *akeidas Yitzchak*, exploring why Yitzchak listened to Avraham.

The present discussion, however, turns to a distinct case that has appeared earlier in the sugya: not a *navi* who prophesies in the name of *avoda zara*, but one who claims to have received a prophecy and uses it to incite others to worship *avoda zara*. This case appears in the Torah directly before the *parsha* of the *mases* — the individual who privately entices others to idolatry, whose punishment is *skila*. The question is: what is the punishment for a *navi* who does the same thing publicly?

### Tanu Rabbanan Debate

A *tanu rabbanan* addresses this directly. The *navi shehidiach* — the prophet who incites to *avoda zara* — receives *skila*, just like the individual *mases*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds that such a *navi* receives *chenek*. The same dispute applies to the *midichei ir nidachas*, those who cause an entire city to worship *avoda zara*: the Rabbis say *skila*, Rabbi Shimon says *chenek*.

This is striking. An individual *mases* receives *skila*, the most severe of the four capital punishments. One would expect that a *navi* who incites masses to *avoda zara* — surely a graver offense — would carry at least the same penalty. Yet Rabbi Shimon rules that both the *navi* and the *midichei ir nidachas* receive the lesser punishment of *chenek*.

### Gezeira Shava

The Gemara explains the Rabbinic position first. The verse in the *navi shehidiach* passage uses the language *ki vikaish lehadichacha*, and the very next section on the individual *mases* uses the identical phrase *ki vikaish lehadichacha*. This *gezeira shava* — a verbal analogy between the two passages — establishes that just as the *mases* receives *skila*, so too does the *navi shehidiach*: *mah le'halan biskila af kan biskila*.

Rabbi Shimon, however, rejects this *gezeira shava*. The verse in the *navi shehidiach* passage simply says *u'mas* — an unqualified death. The rule is that any *misa* stated in the Torah without specification is *chenek*. Rabbi Shimon therefore holds that the *navi shehidiach* receives *chenek*, rendering the ironic result that a *navi* inciting to *avoda zara* receives a lesser punishment than an ordinary individual doing the same.

### Midichei Ir Nidachas

The same debate plays out regarding the *midichei ir nidachas*. The Rabbis are consistent: since the *mases* gets *skila* and since the same *gezeira shava* of *hadacha* language applies here as well — either drawn from the *mases* directly, or from the *navi shehidiach* who already received *skila* — the *midichei ir nidachas* also receive *skila*.

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, draws the *gezeira shava* of *hadacha hadacha* from the *navi* rather than from the *mases*. Having established that the *navi shehidiach* receives *chenek*, Rabbi Shimon applies the same ruling to the *midichei ir nidachas*.

The Gemara presses Rabbi Shimon: why not learn the *midichei ir nidachas* from the *mases* instead? The answer is *danin mases rabbim mimases rabbim*: the *midichei ir nidachas* are a case involving the masses, and the *navi shehidiach* similarly involves the masses. It is therefore more appropriate to draw the analogy from that case rather than from the individual *mases*, which involves a *yachid*. The Gemara then suggests the opposite — perhaps the ordinary people who are *midichei ir nidachas* should be compared to the ordinary *mases*, not to the *navi*. To this, Rabbi Shimon replies: *keivan shehidiach ein lecha hediyot gadol mizeh* — a *navi* who incites to *avoda zara* has forfeited his prophetic status and is no more than a common person. Once the *navi* is placed in the *chenek* category, the *midichei ir nidachas* follow.

### Rav Chisda Framework

Rav Chisda steps back to clarify what kind of case the *navi shehidiach* involves. He explains that the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon concerns a *navi* who is either *oker haguf de'avoda kochavim* — completely uprooting the prohibition against *avoda zara*, effectively saying all idolatry is now permitted — or who is engaged in *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas de'avoda kochavim*, partially permitting some forms of idolatry while prohibiting others. Either of these falls within the scope of Devarim 13 and is the subject of the dispute.

## Oker Haguf vs. Miktzas

### Two Types False Prophecy

To understand Rav Chisda's framework fully, it is necessary to distinguish between two categories of false prophecy. The first is the *navi shehidiach*, the prophet who incites to *avoda zara*, governed by Devarim 13. The second is the *navi sheker* more broadly — a prophet who testifies to a prophecy he did not receive — which is governed by Devarim 18 and is the subject of the *mishna* we encountered earlier. The punishment for the *navi sheker* is *chenek*, and that is undisputed.

The question Rav Chisda is addressing is how to situate various types of prophetic transgression within one or the other of these two categories.

### Devarim 13 vs. 18

The relevant verse in Devarim 13 reads: *ki yakum bekirbecha navi o cholem chalom* — if a prophet or dreamer arises and gives you a sign or wonder, and says *nelcha acharei elohim acherim*, let us follow other gods and worship them. The *navi* is inducing *avoda zara*. The verse then mandates his execution: *umes hanavi hahu*, and it concludes with *lehadi-chacha min haderech asher tzivecha Hashem elokecha laleches bah* — to lead you astray from the path that God commanded you to walk.

The relevant verse in Devarim 18 is different in character. It reads: *ach hanavi asher yazid ledaber davar bishmi es asher lo tzivisiv ledaber* — the prophet who presumptuously speaks in My name what I did not command him — *va'asher yedaber beshem elohim acherim umes hanavi hahu*. That *umes* is unqualified, yielding *chenek*. This is the generic *navi sheker* — not someone inciting to *avoda zara*, but someone falsely claiming prophetic authority.

One marker that distinguishes the *navi sheker* from the *navi shehidiach* is *oker kol haguf*: a *navi* who claims there is no longer a *mitzvah* to perform some commandment — say, that *tefillin* are no longer required — is *oker kol haguf* with respect to that *mitzvah*, which identifies him as a *navi sheker* and places him squarely in the Devarim 18 category, carrying *chenek* by consensus. By contrast, a *navi* who temporarily overrides a *mitzvah* in a specific circumstance — as Eliyahu did on Har HaCarmel — is not a *navi sheker* at all. That kind of partial, time-bound override is permitted and carries no liability.

### Rav Chisda: Laleches

Rav Chisda's framework is challenged by a *braisa* that expounds on the word *laleches* in the verse from Devarim 13. The *braisa* derives: *laleches* — this refers to a *mitzvas asei*; *bah* — this refers to a *mitzvas lo sa'asei*. The implication is that the case of the *navi shehidiach* in Devarim 13 can encompass even a prophet who leads people astray from a positive commandment, not only one who incites to outright *avoda zara*.

This creates a difficulty for Rav Chisda, who limits Devarim 13 to cases involving *avoda zara*. If the passage is solely about *avoda zara*, what would a *mitzvas asei* in that context even look like, since all the prohibitions of *avoda zara* are negative commandments? Rav Chisda resolves this by interpreting *ve'nitz'atzem* — the positive commandment to destroy places of *avoda zara* worship. A *navi* who tells people not to fulfill this positive obligation of demolishing idolatrous shrines would be included in the *navi shehidiach* category under Devarim 13. This preserves Rav Chisda's position that Devarim 13 remains entirely within the domain of *avoda zara*.

### Rav Hamnuna View

Rav Hamnuna challenges Rav Chisda's framework directly. In his view, the category of *oker haguf* — completely uprooting a principle — is itself what defines the *navi shehidiach* classification, regardless of whether the subject matter is *avoda zara* or other *mitzvos*. A *navi* who claims there is no longer any obligation to observe *tzitzis*, for example, is not simply a *navi sheker* in the narrow Devarim 18 sense; he is fundamentally challenging the authority of the Torah, which is conceptually equivalent to inciting *avoda zara*. Both represent a root-level attack on the foundations of faith, not merely a false or unauthorized prophecy.

Under Rav Hamnuna's view, then, the line between Devarim 13 and Devarim 18 is drawn differently. Devarim 13 applies to any case of *oker haguf* — whether the *navi* is uprooting *avoda zara* restrictions or any other *mitzvah*. The *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas* of *avoda zara* also falls under Devarim 13 since the Torah explicitly says *min haderech afilu miktzas haderech* — even a partial deviation regarding *avoda zara* places one in the graver category. The word *laleches* accordingly refers to *oker kol haguf* by any *mitzvas asei*, placing that squarely within the *navi shehidiach* category. And *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas* by *shear mitzvos* — other commandments — remains exempt entirely, as that is the domain of legitimate prophetic override.

### Abaye vs. Rava

The dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Hamnuna generates a corresponding dispute between Abaye and Rava in their reading of a subsequent *braisa*. The *braisa* states: one who prophesies to uproot a principle of the Torah is *chayav*; one who prophesies to establish some and cancel some — Rabbi Shimon exempts; and regarding *avoda zara*, even if he says worship it today and abolish it tomorrow, all agree he is *chayav*.

Abaye follows Rav Chisda. On his reading, the first clause — *hamisnabe l'akor davar min hatorah chayav* — refers to *shear mitzvos* that are completely uprooted. This places the *navi* in the *navi sheker* category of Devarim 18, and the liability is *chenek*, with no dispute. The second clause — *l'kayem miktzas u'levatel miktzas* — addresses *shear mitzvos* with partial override; here Rabbi Shimon (and in truth all opinions) concur that there is no liability, since such override is the legitimate domain of prophetic authority. The final clause about *avoda zara* governs the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon, with the Rabbis imposing *skila* and Rabbi Shimon *chenek* — *mar ki isa u'mar ki isa*.

Rava follows Rav Hamnuna. On his reading, the opening clause — *hamisnabe l'akor davar min hatorah* — is not limited to *shear mitzvos* but applies broadly: whether the *navi* is uprooting *avoda zara* restrictions or any other *mitzvah*, if he is *oker kol haguf*, he falls into the *navi shehidiach* category of Devarim 13. The clause *l'kayem miktzas u'levatel miktzas* then addresses *shear mitzvos* with partial override, and all agree there is no liability. The final clause about *avoda zara* — even temporary worship — again belongs to the *navi shehidiach* category, with the liability following each authority's position: *mar ki isa u'mar ki isa*.

## True Prophet Override

### Prophet Override Rule

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav Yochanan states a general principle: in all cases, if a *navi* instructs you to transgress a Torah commandment, you must listen to him — except when it comes to *avoda zara*. This is consistent with the framework developed above. A legitimate *navi* exercising *kiyum miktzas u'bitul miktzas* over *shear mitzvos* is operating within the proper scope of prophetic authority, and one is obligated to comply. The sole exception is *avoda zara*, which is always in the *navi shehidiach* category — never a case of legitimate override. This is true no matter how compelling the sign he produces: even if he were to halt the sun in the middle of the sky, one must not heed him.

### God's Test Rationale

Rabbi Yossi HaGlili offers an explanation for why the Torah permits such extreme temptation. The Torah assessed the full danger and power of *avoda zara* and therefore granted a kind of authority even to those prophesying in its name. The intention was to test the people: could they withstand even the most dramatic signs and wonders produced in favor of idolatry? The instruction not to heed such a prophet — no matter how spectacular his demonstration — is precisely because the Torah knows the temptation is real and formidable. The precise formulation of this idea is admittedly difficult to parse, but the thrust is that the Torah deliberately allows for the possibility of extreme temptation in this domain, in order to train Israel to resist it absolutely.

### Rabbi Akiva Response

Rabbi Akiva objects. It is, he says, inconceivable — *chas v'shalom* — that God would empower a violator of His will to produce miraculous signs in the service of *avoda zara*. The more plausible explanation for how such a person could display apparent wonders is the case of someone like Chananya ben Azur: a person who was initially a true prophet and received genuine prophetic capacity from God, but who subsequently became a *rasha*. The signs he is able to produce are residual from his prior authentic prophetic status. That is how a person can seem to perform wonders while inciting to *avoda zara* — not because God grants such power to transgressors, but because the power was granted earlier, when he was still a true prophet.

## Chenek: Edim & Others

### Edim Zomemin & Bat Kohen

The *mishna* concludes its enumeration of those who receive *chenek* by addressing *edim zomemim* — perjured witnesses — in the case of a *bas Kohen* who commits adultery while married, and the men involved in that adultery. The distinctive feature of this case is that the *bas Kohen* herself receives *sreifa* — burning — while the adulterer receives *chenek*, the standard punishment for adultery by an ordinary married woman. The *edim zomemim* who conspired to have her executed also receive *chenek*.

### Rabbi Yossi Gezeira

The Gemara asks for the source of these rulings. Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika cites a *braisa* in which Rabbi Yossi expounds the verse *v'asisem lo ka'asher zamam la'asos l'achiv* — you shall do to him as he plotted to do to his brother. This establishes a general principle: wherever *edim zomemim* appear in the Torah, they receive the same punishment that would have been inflicted on the accused, and similarly, those who commit *arayos* with a woman receive the punishment applicable to her relationship. The difficulty arises when, as in the case of *bas Kohen*, the man and the woman receive different punishments.

### Zomemin Follow Man

In the case of the *bas Kohen*, the Torah specifies *hi ba'esh tisaref* — she shall be burned — but the men involved are not subject to *sreifa*. The question then is: when the *edim zomemim* are refuted, do they follow the punishment designated for her or for him? The Gemara resolves this from the verse *la'asos leachiv* — what he plotted to do to his brother, not to his sister. In a case where the man's and woman's judgments diverge, the *edim zomemim* follow the man's ruling, and therefore receive *chenek* rather than *sreifa*.

With that, the *perek* of *eilu hein hanichnakin* is complete, and the masechta turns to its final chapter.

## Olam HaBa Introduction

### Kol Yisrael Opening

The last *perek* of Sanhedrin opens with the famous declaration: *kol Yisrael yeish lahem cheilek l'Olam HaBa* — all Israel has a portion in the world to come — deriving this from the verse *ve'ameich kulam tzadikim le'olam yirshu aretz netzer matai ma'aseh yadai lehispa'er*: your people are all righteous; they shall inherit the land forever, the branch of My planting, the work of My hands, that I may be glorified.

It is worth noting that this opening declaration was not originally part of the *mishna* itself. Rashi remarks on this — and though his comment appears to have been excised, the Maharsha preserves it. The *mishna* actually began with *eilu she'ein lahem cheilek l'Olam HaBa*, a list of exclusions. The affirmative declaration *kol Yisrael yeish lahem cheilek l'Olam HaBa* was prepended precisely because it would be jarring to begin with the exclusions without first establishing that the default is inclusion. The opening therefore functions as a frame: everyone has a portion — *except* the following.

This framing, however, sits in tension with other teachings in *Shas* that suggest only *tzadikim* have a portion in *Olam HaBa*. On Rosh Hashanah, for instance, *tzadikim* are immediately granted *Olam HaBa*, *reshaim* face *Gehennom*, and *beinonim* are suspended in judgment. Similarly, when one converts, one is told not to assume that conversion automatically confers *Olam HaBa* — *Olam HaBa eino ela letzadikim*. The *mishna* here bridges this tension through the verse *ve'ameich kulam tzadikim*: by default, all Jews are classified as *tzadikim*. There is a genuine unresolved strand of tradition, however, as to whether *Olam HaBa* is a default that can only be forfeited, or an achievement that must be earned.

### Excluded Categories

The *mishna* then enumerates those who forfeit their portion. The first category is one who says *ein techiyas hameisim min haTorah* — there is no resurrection of the dead rooted in the Torah. Note that the disqualification is not merely denying resurrection as such; one must specifically deny that it is derived from the Torah. Second is one who denies *Torah min hashamayim*. Third is the *apikorus*, whose precise meaning the Gemara will elaborate upon.

Rabbi Akiva adds two further categories: one who reads in *seforim chitzoniyim* — external books excluded from the canon — and one who whispers an incantation over a wound while reciting the verse *kol hamachalah asher samti bemitzrayim lo asim alecha ki ani Hashem rofecha*. Even citing a *pasuk* as part of a healing incantation places one in this category.

At first glance, these final two exclusions seem categorically different from denying *techiyas hameisim* or *Torah min hashamayim*. Reading sectarian literature or whispering a verse over a wound appears far less severe than denying foundational doctrines of faith. One way to understand the grouping is that *Chazal* are drawing lines of communal identity and sectarianism: there were sects in their time — likely including certain strands of Jewish Christianity — that denied *techiyas hameisim* and *Torah min hashamayim*, and some of these practices were associated with those movements. The exclusions may therefore share a common thread: each marks one as aligned with a sectarian movement that has severed its connection with mainstream Israel, whether through doctrinal denial or through characteristic sectarian behavior.

Abba Shaul adds a final exclusion: *hahogeh es Hashem be'osyosov* — one who articulates the divine name as it is spelled, letter by letter.

### Kings & Commoners

The *mishna* then lists specific historical figures who, despite being Israelites, do not receive a portion in *Olam HaBa*. Three kings are named: Yerovam, Achav, and Menashe. Rabbi Yehuda holds that Menashe does have a portion, citing the verse *vayitpalel eilav* — Menashe prayed to God — and God heard his plea and restored him to Jerusalem and his kingship. The Sages respond: God restored him to his *malchus*, his earthly kingship, but not to *chayei Olam HaBa*.

Four commoners complete the list: Bilam, Doeg, Achitofel, and Geichazi. The Gemara will develop each of these cases in turn.

## Resurrection Proofs

### Denial Consequence

The Gemara asks: why does one who denies *techiyas hameisim min haTorah* forfeit a portion in *Olam HaBa*? The answer is measure for measure: *hu kafar b'techiyat hameisim l'fichach lo yihiyeh lo chelek b'techiyat hameisim*. By denying resurrection, one disqualifies oneself from experiencing it. In identifying the consequence this way, the Gemara implicitly equates *Olam HaBa* in this context with *techiyas hameisim* — not the individual soul's existence after death, but the future resurrection of the body in historical time. This is worth pausing over. The Rambam's conception of *Olam HaBa* centers on the immortal soul's existence in a purely spiritual realm, a world that runs parallel to ours and receives souls after death. The Gemara's *pshat* here is quite different: *Olam HaBa* is the world that will arrive in the future, the era of resurrection, the culmination of history. The one who denies it will not share in it.

### Mida K'Neged Mida

The Gemara then pauses to establish the principle of *mida k'neged mida* as a divine operating principle. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan derives it from the episode of Elisha and the officer. Elisha had prophesied during a famine that by the next day grain would be available at remarkably cheap prices at the gates of Shomron. The officer upon whom the king leaned scoffed: could God make windows in the heavens? Elisha responded: *hincha ro'eh b'einecha u'misham lo tochal* — you will see it with your own eyes, but you will not eat from it.

Indeed, as the verse records, the Aramean army panicked and fled, leaving abundant provisions, and the people rushed out through the gate — trampling the officer there, exactly as foretold.

The Gemara challenges: perhaps the officer's fate was simply due to Elisha's personal curse, since the curse of a sage takes effect even when unwarranted (*kilelat chacham afilu al chinam hi ba'ah*). If so, this episode merely demonstrates the power of a sage's curse, not a general divine principle of *mida k'neged mida*. The Gemara responds: if that were the entire point, the verse need only have said that they trampled him and he died. Why specify *b'sha'ar* — at the gate? The word *sha'ar* echoes the context of the grain prices at the gate — *b'sha'ar Shomron* — and thus the specific detail signals that the punishment was calibrated to the sin: he mocked the prophecy about the *sha'ar*, and he was destroyed at the *sha'ar*. This demonstrates that an overarching principle of *mida k'neged mida* is at work.

### Terumah Proof

The Gemara proceeds to demonstrate *techiyas hameisim* from the Torah itself. The verse states: *venatatem mimenu et terumat Hashem l'Aharon HaKohen* — you shall give from it the *terumah* of God to Aharon the *Kohen*. The Gemara notes: would Aharon actually be alive to receive *terumah* when the Israelites enter the land? He did not enter *Eretz Yisrael* at all. The verse must therefore be teaching that Aharon is destined to be resurrected, and Israel will give him *terumah*. This is the derivation of *techiyas hameisim min haTorah*.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael, however, reads *l'Aharon* not as a literal reference to Aharon himself but as meaning "like Aharon" — a *Kohen* of Aharon's character. The requirement to give *terumah* applies specifically to a *Kohen* who, like Aharon, is a *chaver* — one who can be trusted regarding the laws of *tumah* and *taharah*.

### Terumah Conditions

This leads into a brief but significant digression on the laws governing to whom *terumah* may be given. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yochanan asks: from where do we know that one may not give *terumah* to a *Kohen am ha'aretz* — an unlearned *Kohen* who cannot be relied upon to maintain the proper standards of purity? The source is a verse in Chronicles: *vayomer la'am yoshvei Yerushalayim latet menat haKohanim v'haLevi'im lema'an yechezku b'Torat Hashem* — he said to the people of Jerusalem to give the portions to the *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* so that they may be strengthened in God's Torah. The implication is that only one who strengthens himself in God's Torah has a claim to a *manat* — a portion. One who does not strengthen himself in God's Torah has no such claim.

Rav Acha bar Adda in the name of Rav Yehuda adds that giving *terumah* to a *Kohen am ha'aretz* is like placing it before a lion: just as with a lion one cannot know whether he will claw into his prey and devour it or not, so too one cannot know whether this *Kohen* will eat the *terumah* in a state of *taharah* or *tumah*. Rabbi Yochanan adds that one who gives *terumah* to a *Kohen am ha'aretz* may bring about his death, as the verse states *umetu vo ki yechalelu'u* — they shall die in it if they desecrate it by eating it in *tumah*. The school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov further teaches that one thereby causes the *Kohen* to incur *avon ashmah* — the iniquity of guilt — as the verse states *v'hisi'u otam avon ashmah b'ochlam et kodsheihem*.

### Simai's Proof

After the digression, the Gemara returns to the proofs for *techiyas hameisim*. A *braisa* records Rabbi Simai as asking: from where do we derive *techiyas hameisim min haTorah*? From the verse: *v'gam hakimoti et briti itam latet lahem et Eretz Kena'an* — I established My covenant with them to give *them* the land of Kena'an. The verse does not say *lachem*, to you — the descendants — but *lahem*, to them — the *avot* themselves. Since the *avot* did not receive the land in their lifetimes, the verse implies that they will receive it at the time of the resurrection. This, Rabbi Simai concludes, is a proof for *techiyas hameisim* from the Torah.

### Minim Challenges

The Gemara then relates that *minim* — heretics, often understood as referring to Jewish Christians — challenged Rabban Gamliel: from where do you derive that God resurrects the dead? Rabban Gamliel replied that he could demonstrate it from the Torah, from the *Nevi'im*, and from the *Ketuvim*. He proceeded to offer proofs from each, but the *minim* rejected each one.

From the Torah, he cited the verse *hinecha shochev im avotecha v'kam* — behold, you will lie down with your fathers, and arise. The *minim* responded that the continuation of the verse — *v'kam ha'am hazeh v'zanah acharei elohei nechar ha'aretz* — shows that it is the nation that rises and strays, not that the dead individual will literally be resurrected.

From the *Nevi'im*, he cited *yichyu meisecha neveilasi yakumu* — your dead shall live, my corpses shall arise — and *ki tal orot talecha ve'aretz refaim tapil* — for the dew of light is Your dew. The *minim* responded that perhaps this refers only to the particular resurrection of dry bones performed by Yechezkel, not a general doctrine of universal resurrection.

From the *Ketuvim*, he cited *vechichecha keyayin hatov...dovev siftei yeshenim* — your palate is like good wine...causing the lips of the sleeping to murmur. The *minim* pushed back, suggesting that *dovev siftei yeshenim* does not imply physical resurrection but merely that the lips of the dead murmur — a reference to the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: anyone who cites a *halacha* in the name of a deceased sage causes his lips to murmur in the grave, as it is said *dovev siftei yeshenim*. In other words, the *minim* deflected each proof by offering an alternative reading.

### Final Proof

After each attempted proof was met with a rebuttal, Rabban Gamliel produced a verse they could not contest: *asher nishba Hashem la'avoteichem latet lahem* — which God swore to your forefathers to give *them*. The verse does not say *lachem*, to you, but *lahem*, to them — the *avot* themselves. Since the forefathers never received the land in their lifetimes, the oath implies a future fulfillment through resurrection. This is the same proof Rabbi Simai had offered, and it was decisive. *Mikan l'techiyas hameisim min haTorah.*

### Alternative Proof

The Gemara records an alternative tradition, according to which Rabban Gamliel's clinching proof was a different verse: *ve'atem hadeveikim baHashem elokeichem chayim kulchem hayom* — and you who cleave to God are all alive today. The inference is: just as you are alive today, so too will you remain alive in *Olam HaBa*. *Mah hayom kulchem chayim af l'Olam HaBa kulchem chayim.* The verse thus links continued life to cleaving to God, implying that this bond endures even beyond death — and that those who do cleave will be alive in the world to come as well.
