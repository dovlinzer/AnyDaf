# Sanhedrin 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ben Noach Execution Rules

### Source Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכַּח רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַחָא דַּהֲוָה כְּתִיב בְּסֵפֶר אַגַּדְתָּא דְּבֵי רַב: בֶּן נֹחַ נֶהֱרָג בְּדַיָּין אֶחָד, וּבְעֵד אֶחָד, שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַתְרָאָה, מִפִּי אִישׁ וְלֹא מִפִּי אִשָּׁה, וַאֲפִילּוּ קָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Aḥa found that it was written in a book of *Aggadot* in the study hall of Rav:** Contrary to the *halakha* with regard to a Jew, **a descendant of Noah is executed on the basis of** the verdict of even **one judge, and by** the testimony of even **one witness,** and **without** being given **forewarning** before committing the transgression. He can be judged or testified against only **by the mouth of a man and not by the mouth of a woman; but even a relative** may judge his case or testify against him.

The sugya opens with a passage discovered by Rav Yaakov bar Acha, who found it written in the *Sefer Aggadata d'bei Rav* — a collection that, despite its name suggesting aggadic content, appears to contain halakhic material rather than narrative legend. The citation appears at the top of 57b, the third word in from the top: *ashkah Rav Yaakov bar Acha de-iktav b'Sefer Aggadata d'bei Rav* — Rav Yaakov bar Acha found written in the *Sefer Aggadata d'bei Rav*.

### One Judge, One Witness

The text that Rav Yaakov bar Acha found reads: *ben Noach neherag b'dayan echad u-v'ed echad she-lo b'haztara mipi ish v'lo mipi ishah v'afilu karov* — a *ben Noach* can be executed by one judge, with one witness, and without a prior warning, provided the testimony comes from a man and not a woman, and — strikingly — even from a relative.

### Jewish vs. Non-Jew Rules

This stands in sharp contrast to the halakha as it applies to Jews, which requires at minimum twenty-three *dayanim* for capital cases, two *edim*, and a formal *haztara*. The requirement of *ish v'lo ishah* — that testimony come from a man and not from a woman — applies in both systems alike, making it one of the few points of symmetry between them.

### ish velo isha

The limitation of *ish v'lo mipi ishah* means that neither the judge nor the witness in such a proceeding may be a woman. This rule applies both in the Jewish system and in the Noachide system, even as the other procedural requirements diverge dramatically.

### Relatives Permitted

What is perhaps most striking is the phrase *v'afilu karov* — even a relative may testify. In a Jewish court, a relative serving as either judge or witness disqualifies the proceeding entirely. In Noachide law, however, this restriction does not apply, and the testimony of a kinsman is admissible.

## Feticide Liability

### Fetus Killing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמְרוּ: אַף עַל הָעוּבָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael** that a descendant of Noah is executed **even for** killing **fetuses.**

Also recorded in the *Sefer Aggadata d'bei Rav* in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: *af al ha-ubarim* — that even one who kills a fetus is subject to capital liability under Noachide law. This is a significant extension of the prohibition against murder, applying it to the taking of fetal life.

### Textual Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? דִּכְתִיב: ״שֹׁפֵךְ דַּם הָאָדָם בָּאָדָם דָּמוֹ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״. אֵיזֶהוּ אָדָם שֶׁהוּא בָּאָדָם? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה עוּבָּר שֶׁבִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael?** The Gemara answers: It is derived from that **which is written: "One who sheds the blood of a person, by a person [*ba'adam*] his blood shall be shed"** (Genesis 9:6). The word *ba'adam* literally means: In a person, and is interpreted homiletically: **What is a person that is in a person? You must say: This is a fetus that is in its mother's womb.** Accordingly, a descendant of Noah is liable for killing a fetus.

The Gemara asks *minayan* — from where do we derive this? The answer is *amar kra: ach et dimchem l'nafshoteichem edrosh* — "however your blood which belongs to your souls, I will demand" (Bereishit 9:5). This pasuk and the verses that follow serve as the textual anchor for the entire Noachide judicial system developed in this sugya, though one might fairly observe that the derivations are not always the most straightforward in their exegetical logic. Nonetheless, each rule is grounded in a specific word or phrase from these pesukim.

### Single Judge Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: דְּאָמַר קְרָא, ״אַךְ אֶת דִּמְכֶם לְנַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם אֶדְרֹשׁ״, אֲפִילּוּ בְּדַיָּין אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** They are derived from that **which the verse states:** "And **your blood of your lives I will require;** at the hand of every animal I will require it; and at the hand of man, even at the hand of every man's brother, I will require the life of man" (Genesis 9:5). It is derived from the term "I will require," which is stated in the singular, that a descendant of Noah is executed **on the basis** of the verdict of **even one judge.**

The Gemara picks up on the singular form *edrosh* — "I will demand" — and derives from it that even one judge suffices to adjudicate a capital case involving a *ben Noach*: *afilu b'dayan echad*.

### Beast Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִיָּד כׇּל חַיָּה״, אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַתְרָאָה. ״אֶדְרְשֶׁנּוּ וּמִיַּד הָאָדָם״, אֲפִילּוּ בְּעֵד אֶחָד. ״מִיַּד אִישׁ״, וְלֹא מִיַּד אִשָּׁה. ״אָחִיו״, אֲפִילּוּ קָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from the phrase **"at the hand of every animal"** that one is executed **even without forewarning,** as an animal certainly cannot forewarn someone. It is derived from the phrase **"I will require it; and at the hand of man,"** with "I" stated in the singular, that the sentence is issued **on the basis** of the testimony of **even one witness.** It is derived from the phrase **"at the hand of every man,"** that the judgment and testimony must be at the hand of a man, **but not at the hand of a woman.** It is derived from the term **"his brother"** that the testimony of the witness is accepted **even** if he is **a relative** of the defendant.

The continuation of the pasuk reads *mi-yad kol chaya edroshenu* — "and of every beast I will demand it." Since a beast cannot receive a formal warning, having no capacity for consciousness or understanding, and yet the Torah mandates that the beast be put to death for killing a human being, this becomes the precedent establishing that a *ben Noach* can likewise be executed without a prior *haztara*.

### Single Witness

The phrase *mi-yad ha-adam edroshenu* — "from the hand of man I will demand it" — is read as establishing that a single human witness suffices: *afilu b'ed echad*.

### Male Witness Only

The further phrase *mi-yad ish* — specifically "from the hand of a man" — teaches *ish v'lo miyad ishah*: a man and not a woman. This applies equally to the *dayan* and the *ed* who function in such a proceeding.

### Relative Testimony

Finally, the pasuk adds *achiv* — "his brother." From the word "brother" the Gemara derives that even a relative may testify. In ordinary Jewish law this would be categorically disqualifying; here it explicitly broadens the category of valid witnesses to include kinsmen.

The Gemara then turns to the reason underlying Rabbi Yishmael's ruling that feticide is a capital offense for a *ben Noach*. The textual basis is: *shofech dam ha-adam ba-adam damo yishapech* — "one who sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed." Rabbi Yishmael reads *shofech dam ha-adam ba-adam* and asks: *eizehu adam she-betoch adam* — when is there a human being inside another human being? The answer: this describes a fetus inside its mother's womb. And therefore, one who kills a fetus — even under the limited procedural requirements of Noachide law, with one witness and one judge — is liable for murder.

## All Deaths via Chenek

### Chenek Only Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא תַּנָּא דְבֵי מְנַשֶּׁה הוּא, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל מִיתָה הָאֲמוּרָה לִבְנֵי נֹחַ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חֶנֶק, וּשְׁדִי לֵיהּ הַאי ״בָּאָדָם״ אַסֵּיפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא, וּדְרוֹשׁ בֵּיהּ הָכִי: ״בָּאָדָם דָּמוֹ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״ – אֵיזֶהוּ שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים שֶׁל אָדָם שֶׁהוּא בְּגוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה חֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And the first *tanna*,** who does not derive the *halakha* concerning fetuses, **is the *tanna* of the school of Menashe, who says** that **all death** penalties **stated with regard to the descendants of Noah are** referring to **nothing other than strangulation. And** he interprets this verse as follows: **Cast,** i.e., redirect, **this** term: **"In a person,"** and explain it **with regard to the latter** part **of the verse, and interpret it homiletically like this: "In a person, his blood shall be shed."** In **what** manner **is a person's blood shed while it is in** the **person's body,** without external bleeding? **You must say** that **this is strangulation.** It is therefore derived that the execution of a descendant of Noah is by strangulation.

The *Tanna Kamma* who does not include feticide in the Noachide capital code raises the question of why not. The answer comes from a different school: *Tanna d'bei Menashe*, who maintains that *kol mitah ha-amurah l'vnei Noach einah ela chenek* — any death prescribed for a *ben Noach* is executed exclusively by strangulation, *chenek*.

### Ba-Adam Reread

Rather than reading *ba-adam* as "inside a person" — the reading that supports Rabbi Yishmael's derivation of feticide — *Tanna d'bei Menashe* attaches *ba-adam* to the *seifa* of the pasuk, *damo yishapech*, and reads it as specifying the *method* of execution rather than the *subject* being killed.

### Blood Remains Inside

Having redeployed *ba-adam* as a description of method, *Tanna d'bei Menashe* asks: *eizehu shefichut damim shel adam she-hu b'gufo shel adam?* — what form of bloodshed involves the blood remaining within the body of the person? The answer is strangulation, *chenek*. Unlike stoning, beheading with a sword, or other forms of execution in which blood is visibly expelled from the body, strangulation kills while keeping the blood interior. This therefore becomes the exclusive mode of capital punishment for Noachide law.

## Women's Status

### Women Included

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: וְאִשָּׁה לָא מִפַּקְדָה? וְהָכְתִיב: ״כִּי יְדַעְתִּיו לְמַעַן אֲשֶׁר יְצַוֶּה וְגוֹ׳״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** to the statement in the book of *Aggadot* that a descendant of Noah can be judged or testified against only by a man and not by a woman: **And is a woman** who is a descendant of Noah **not commanded** to establish courts of judgment? **But isn't it written** with regard to Abraham, who at that point had the status of a descendant of Noah: **"For I have known him, to the end that he may command** his sons and his household after him, that they may keep the way of the Lord, to do righteousness and justice" (Genesis 18:19). The word "household" is referring to the women, indicating that they are also commanded to execute justice.

Rav Hamnuna challenges the exclusion of women from Noachide judicial proceedings. He cites the famous pasuk in which Hashem describes His relationship with Avraham: *ki yedativ l'maan asher yetzaveh et banav v'et beito acharav v'shamru derech Hashem la-asot tzedakah u-mishpat* — "for I have known him, so that he will command his sons and his household after him, and they will guard the way of Hashem to do charity and justice." Since Avraham is himself a *ben Noach*, and the pasuk explicitly includes both *banav* and *beito* in the obligation of *tzedakah u-mishpat*, it would seem to follow that women are included in *mishpat* — the judicial dimension of Noachide law.

### Avraham's Mission

Rav Hamnuna reads the phrase *banav v'et beito* as encompassing both male descendants and the women of the household. Both are commanded to maintain the ways of Hashem and to practice *tzedakah u-mishpat*. From this, one might conclude that women are not merely subject to Noachide law as defendants but are in some sense participants in its application.

### Sons vs. Household

The key interpretive question is what *banav* refers to and what *beito* refers to. Rav Hamnuna understands *beito* — the household — as including women, and therefore argues they should be included in *mishpat*, i.e., in the judicial process. The question of whether this means they are subject to the laws as defendants, or whether they can serve as judges and witnesses, is the crux of the inquiry.

### Nifkada Definition

The relevant term is *nifkada* — "appointed" or "designated." The phrase *v'ishah lo nifkada l'dinin* means she is not appointed to serve in the judicial capacity. The debate is thus whether the pasuk in question is speaking about women's subjection to the law as governed parties, which everyone appears to accept, or about their role within the apparatus of adjudication itself.

## Sons/Charity Split

### Din vs. Tzedakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ: ״בָּנָיו״ – לְדִין, ״בֵּיתוֹ״ – לִצְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He raises the objection and he resolves it:** Abraham commanded **his sons to** carry out **justice,** whereas **his household,** the women in his family, he commanded **to** give **charity;** the Hebrew word for righteousness [*tzedek*] can also mean charity [*tzedaka*].

The Gemara adds that *bar Bereida* not only pressed the question but supplied the answer: *banav l'dinin, beito l'tzedakah* — the sons are commanded with respect to *mishpat*, judicial law, while the household — i.e., the women — are entrusted with *tzedakah*, charitable giving. The two applications of *tzedakah u-mishpat* in the pasuk are thus divided along these lines, resolving Rav Hamnuna's challenge by assigning women to one dimension of the verse and men to another.

### Woman as Victim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַוְיָא סָבָא לְרַב פָּפָּא: אֵימָא בַּת נֹחַ שֶׁהָרְגָה לֹא תֵּיהָרֵג, ״מִיַּד אִישׁ״, וְלֹא ״מִיַּד אִשָּׁה״ כְּתִיב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״שֹׁפֵךְ דַּם הָאָדָם״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avya the Elder said to Rav Pappa:** Why not **say** that **a female descendant of Noah who killed** someone **should not be executed;** as **it is written: "At the hand of every man," and not "at the hand of every woman"?** Rav Pappa **said to him: This** is what **Rav Yehuda says:** It is derived from the phrase **"one who sheds the blood of a person"** that one who murders is liable to be executed **in any case,** whether that person is male or female.

Rav Avia Sava raises a further question to Rav Pappa: perhaps a *bat Noach* — a non-Jewish woman — should not be killed at all, not because of her exclusion from the judicial role but because of the pasuk's language regarding victims. Since the pasuk concerning Noach uses *mi-yad ish* — "from the hand of a man" — one might read this as excluding women both from giving testimony and from being victims of the homicide prohibition.

### Anyone Kills Anyone

Rav Yehuda responds by citing the broader pasuk: *shofech dam ha-adam ba-adam damo yishapech*. The phrase *shofech dam ha-adam* encompasses any killer — male or female — and the victim *ha-adam* similarly encompasses any human being. The identity of the killer or the victim is not restricted by gender.

### Adultery Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: בַּת נֹחַ שֶׁזִּינְּתָה לֹא תֵּיהָרֵג, דִּכְתִיב ״עַל כֵּן יַעֲזׇב אִישׁ״ וְלֹא אִשָּׁה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״וְהָיוּ לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד״ – הֲדַר עָרְבִינְהוּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Avya asked further: Why not **say** that **a female descendant of Noah who committed adultery should not be executed, as it is written: "Therefore a man shall leave** his father and his mother, and shall cleave to his wife, and they shall be one flesh" (Genesis 2:24); a man, **but not a woman?** Rav Pappa **said to him: This** is what **Rav Yehuda says:** At the end of the verse it states: **"And they shall be one flesh." The verse then combines** men and women, indicating that the same *halakha* applies to both.

The Gemara then raises the question of whether a *bat Noach* who commits adultery might be exempt from liability. The basis for the question is the pasuk *al ken ya'azov ish* — "therefore a man shall leave" — which speaks specifically in terms of the male. Since the commandment to establish a marital bond is framed in masculine terms, one might argue that women are not bound by it in the same way and therefore cannot be liable for adultery.

## One Flesh Principle

### Ve-Hayu Principle

Rav Yehuda answers this by pointing to the continuation of the same pasuk: *v'hayu l'vasar echad* — "and they shall become one flesh." This phrase explicitly encompasses both the man and the woman, as it takes both to constitute "one flesh."

### Reading Back

The Gemara describes this as *hadar arebinhu kra* — the verse loops back and re-includes them together. Once the pasuk concludes with a phrase that applies to both genders, we read that inclusion retroactively back into the earlier words of the verse, even though those words used only the masculine form *ish*. Both men and women are therefore bound by the Noachide prohibition against adultery.

## Kutim & Arayot

### Ish Ish Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״? לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגּוֹיִים, שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין עַל הָעֲרָיוֹת כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "No one [*ish ish*] shall approach any that is kin to him, to uncover their nakedness" (Leviticus 18:6): The verse could have stated: **One [*ish*]** shall not approach. **Why** must **the verse state "no one"?** It is **to include the gentiles, who are prohibited from** engaging in **forbidden sexual relations, as Jews** are.

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan, ish ish* — what does the doubling of the word *ish* in Vayikra 18 teach? The extra word comes to include the *Kutim* — the Samaritans — establishing that they are warned against *arayot* just as Yisrael is: *she-muzharin al ha-arayot k'Yisrael*.

### Separate Systems

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא! ״לֵאמֹר״ – זֶה גִּילּוּי עֲרָיוֹת. הָתָם בַּעֲרָיוֹת דִּידְהוּ, וְהָכָא בַּעֲרָיוֹת דִּידַן, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: בָּא עַל עֲרָיוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל – נִידּוֹן בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is it derived from here? It is derived from there,** from the verse that was already interpreted as teaching this *halakha*: "And the Lord God commanded the man, **saying"** (Genesis 2:16), **this** alludes to **forbidden sexual relations** (see 56b). The Gemara answers: **There,** the verse is referring **to their** women, gentiles, **with whom relations are forbidden. And here** it is referring **to our** women, Jews, **with whom relations are forbidden.** In other words, a gentile who engages in intercourse with a married Jewish woman is liable. **As it is taught in the latter clause** of the *baraita*: If a gentile **engages in intercourse with those Jewish** women **with whom relations are forbidden,** i.e., a married Jewish woman, **he is judged according to the *halakhot* of the Jews.**

The Gemara immediately questions this derivation. Was this not already established from a different source — from the word *leimor* in the *arayot* section, used a daf or two earlier to derive that non-Jews are commanded regarding *arayot*? The Gemara resolves the apparent redundancy: the two derivations govern two distinct systems. The *leimor* derivation establishes that non-Jews are commanded regarding their *own* system of *arayot* — *arayot dideyhu*. The doubling of *ish ish* establishes that they are also bound when interacting with the Jewish system — *arayot didan*. These are two separate bodies of law, each with its own source.

### Nafka Mina

The practical consequence of distinguishing these two systems is spelled out from the *seifa* of the earlier passage: if a non-Jew commits *arayot* with a Jew, he is adjudicated under Jewish law — *nidon b'dinei Yisrael*. If he commits *arayot* within his own system, he is judged by Noachide law. The two sets of rules carry different procedural standards and different penalties, making the distinction between the systems consequential.

## Exemption Debate

### One-One Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לָעֵדָה, וְעֵדִים, וְהַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this gentile judged according to the *halakhot* of the Jews? **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The statement of the *baraita* **is necessary only** to teach these *halakhot*: That he must be judged **by a Sanhedrin, and** that he is punished only if two **witnesses** testify concerning him, **and** only if he was issued **a forewarning** before his transgression.

The Gemara asks: *l'mai hilchata* — what is the practical difference? Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, answers: the distinction matters for the evidentiary and procedural requirements. When a non-Jew is judged under his own system — Noachide law — one witness and one judge suffice, with no *haztara* required. When judged under Jewish law, the full apparatus of two witnesses, twenty-three judges, and a formal warning applies.

### Lower Standard Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִגְרָע גָּרַע?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Should the *halakha* of a gentile who engaged in intercourse with a forbidden Jewish woman be **less** stringent than that of a gentile who engaged in intercourse with a forbidden gentile woman, in which case these conditions do not apply?

The Gemara raises a sharp objection: *atu migra gara?* — is this not a perverse result? If a non-Jew commits adultery with a non-Jew, the lower Noachide standard applies and he can be executed more easily. But if he commits the same act with a Jew, the Jewish standard kicks in, requiring far more stringent procedural conditions — making it *harder* to execute him. This seems to invert the logic: one would expect greater protection for Jewish victims, not a higher evidentiary bar that makes punishment more difficult.

### Betrothed Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹחָנָן: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְנַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה, דִּלְדִידְהוּ לֵית לְהוּ, דְּדָיְינִינַן לְהוּ בְּדִינָא דִּידַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The statement of the *baraita* **is necessary only to** teach the *halakha* in the case of a gentile who engages in intercourse with **a betrothed young** Jewish **woman, which** does **not** apply **to** gentiles. By *halakha*, only marriage applies to gentiles, not betrothal. Therefore, **we judge them according to our *halakha*** in that case.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the difficulty by reframing the question entirely. The point of distinction between the two systems is not about the general evidentiary standard but about one specific case: *na'arah me-orasah* — a betrothed young woman. The institution of *eirusin*, formal betrothal, exists only in Jewish law. Non-Jews do not have a status of *eirusin* separable from full marriage. Therefore, if a non-Jew commits adultery with a *na'arah me-orasah*, Noachide law has no category for it at all — for them, she is simply unmarried. In such a case, we apply Jewish law, which does recognize her betrothed status and imposes the appropriate penalty.

## Na'arah vs. Eshes Ish

### Eirsusin Unique

The institution of *eirusin* — the formal betrothal that creates a binding marital status even before *nissu'in* — is unique to Jewish law. Non-Jews do not divide marriage into these two stages. As a result, the category of *na'arah me-orasah*, a woman who is legally a wife in the most serious sense yet has not yet entered the marital home, simply does not exist in the Noachide framework.

### Jewish Law Applies

When a non-Jew commits *arayot* with a *na'arah me-orasah*, since Noachide law has no corresponding category, Jewish law must govern the case. This is the single narrow exception that Rabbi Yochanan identifies as requiring the application of Jewish rather than Noachide law.

### Adultery Standard

By contrast, the category of *eshes ish* — a fully married woman — does exist in Noachide law. Non-Jews recognize marriage and prohibit adultery. Therefore, a non-Jew who commits adultery with a married Jewish woman can be adjudicated under Noachide law, with its single judge, single witness, and no *haztara* requirement.

## Death Penalty Mismatch

### Jewish Penalties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, בְּדִינָא דִּידְהוּ דָּיְינִינַן לְהוּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: בָּא עַל נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה – נִידּוֹן בִּסְקִילָה, עַל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ – נִידּוֹן בְּחֶנֶק. וְאִי בְּדִינָא דִּידְהוּ, סַיִיף הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to gentiles who engage in intercourse with **a married** Jewish **woman, do we judge them according to their *halakha*? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If a gentile **engages in intercourse with a betrothed young** Jewish **woman, he is punished by stoning;** if he engages in intercourse **with a married** Jewish **woman he is punished by strangulation?** The Gemara explains its question: **And if** they are judged **according to their *halakha*,** he would **be** executed by the **sword.**

A *baraita* is cited that creates a new difficulty. It states: one who has relations with a *na'arah me-orasah* is punished by *sekila* — stoning; one who has relations with an *eshes ish* is punished by *chenek* — strangulation. This language describes the Jewish system of penalties, which is appropriate for the *na'arah me-orasah* case since, as established, that case is adjudicated under Jewish law. But the *eshes ish* case presents a problem.

### Non-Jewish Penalty

If the *eshes ish* case is governed by Noachide law — as Rabbi Yochanan maintains — the penalty should be *sayif*, beheading. *Tanna d'bei Menashe* holds that all Noachide executions are performed by *chenek*, but setting that aside, the language of the *baraita* describes *chenek* specifically in the context that sounds like Jewish law adjudication. If the *eshes ish* case is truly being handled under Noachide law, why does the *baraita* use the language of the Jewish penalty system?

### Eshes Ish Redefined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ דְּקָתָנֵי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לְחוּפָּה וְלֹא נִבְעֲלָה, דִּלְדִידְהוּ לֵית לְהוּ. דָּיְינִינַן לְהוּ בְּדִינָא דִּידַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of the phrase: **A married woman, which** the *tanna* **teaches?** It is referring to a case **where** the woman **had entered the wedding canopy but had not** yet **engaged in intercourse** with her husband, in which case she is considered married according to the *halakhot* that apply to Jews but not according to the *halakhot* that apply to gentiles. **Since with regard to** gentiles, marriage has **not** yet taken effect, **we judge them according to our *halakhot*.** Therefore, a gentile who engages in intercourse with such a Jewish woman is executed by strangulation.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this by asking a fundamental question: *mai eshes ish?* — what exactly does "married woman" mean in this context? He proposes a refined definition: the *baraita* is speaking of a woman who has entered the *chuppah* but has not yet had marital relations — *she-nichnesah la-chuppah v'lo niv'alah*. For Jews, the *chuppah* itself completes the *nissu'in* and creates the full status of *eshes ish*. But for non-Jews, who have no *chuppah* ceremony in the Jewish sense, the finalization of marriage requires the act of consummation — *bi'ah*. Therefore, a woman in the post-*chuppah*, pre-*bi'ah* state occupies a zone that Jewish law recognizes as full marriage but Noachide law does not. In this narrow category, Jewish law must be applied — hence *chenek*, the Jewish penalty, rather than *sayif*.

## Post-Chuppah Status

### Chuppah Without Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בְּעוּלַת בַּעַל – יֵשׁ לָהֶן, נִכְנְסָה לְחוּפָּה וְלֹא נִבְעֲלָה – אֵין לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Ḥanina teaches:** Gentiles can **have** the status of a married woman **who has engaged in intercourse with her husband,** i.e., such a woman is considered married according to their laws, but **they cannot have** the status of a married woman **who has entered the wedding canopy but has not engaged in intercourse** with her husband.

The case of *nichnesah la-chuppah v'lo niv'alah* — she entered the *chuppah* but was not yet intimate — thus becomes the second exception, alongside *na'arah me-orasah*, in which Jewish law is applied to a non-Jewish defendant. For non-Jews, the marriage is not finalized until consummation; without *bi'ah*, she does not yet have the status of a full *eshes ish* in their system.

### Sex Finalizes

The underlying principle is that in Noachide marriage law, the sexual act is what finalizes the union. Without it, even a woman who has gone through whatever ceremony exists in the non-Jewish community is not yet considered a married woman for the purposes of Noachide *arayot* law.

## Post-Chuppah Status (II)

### Beulas Baal Only

A *baraita* confirms this position: non-Jews recognize *beulas baal* — a woman who has had marital relations with her husband — but they do not recognize the category of *nichnesah la-chuppah v'lo niv'alah*. The post-ceremony, pre-consummation status simply does not exist in Noachide law.

## Arayot Obligation

### Meir's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל עֶרְוָה שֶׁבֵּית דִּין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל מְמִיתִין עָלֶיהָ – בֶּן נֹחַ מוּזְהָר עָלֶיהָ. אֵין בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל מְמִיתִין עָלֶיהָ – אֵין בֶּן נֹחַ מוּזְהָר עָלֶיהָ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַרְבֵּה עֲרָיוֹת יֵשׁ שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל מְמִיתִין עֲלֵיהֶן, וּבֶן נֹחַ מוּזְהָר עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Any forbidden sexual relations for which a Jewish court administers capital punishment are prohibited to a descendant of Noah,** and any forbidden sexual relations **for which a Jewish court does not administer capital punishment are not prohibited to a descendant of Noah;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: There are many** types of **forbidden sexual relations for which a Jewish court does not administer capital punishment and are** nevertheless **prohibited to a descendant of Noah.**

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* that records a dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* concerning which *arayot* bind non-Jews. Rabbi Meir holds: any *ervah* for which the Jewish *beit din* imposes the death penalty — a *ben Noach* is warned against that *ervah* as well. Conversely, if the Jewish *beit din* does not impose death for a given *ervah*, the *ben Noach* is not warned against it.

### Broader Arayot

The *Chachamim* dispute this sharply: *harbeh arayot yesh she-ein beit din shel Yisrael memitin aleihen u-ven Noach muzhar aleihen* — there are many *arayot* for which Jewish law does not prescribe death, and yet a *ben Noach* is still obligated and warned against them. The Noachide system of *arayot* is not merely a subset of the Jewish system; it has its own scope and may in some cases be broader.

### System Selection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא עַל עֲרָיוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל – נִידּוֹן בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. בָּא עַל עֲרָיוֹת בֶּן נֹחַ – נִידּוֹן בְּדִינֵי בֶּן נֹחַ. וְאָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** If a gentile **engages in intercourse with those Jews with whom relations are forbidden, he is judged according to the *halakhot* of the Jews.** If he **engages in intercourse with those descendants of Noah with whom relations are forbidden, he is judged according to the *halakhot* of the descendants of Noah. And we have only** the case of **a betrothed young woman** as a case where a gentile is judged according to the *halakhot* of the Jews, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *baraita* further articulates the principle of system selection: if a non-Jew commits *arayot* with a Jewish woman, he is judged by Jewish law — *nidon b'dinei Yisrael*. If he commits *arayot* with a non-Jewish woman, he is judged by Noachide law — *nidon b'dinei ben Noach*.

### Exceptions Listed

The *baraita* identifies the exceptions to this framework — cases where a non-Jewish defendant is pulled into the Jewish system even when the interaction is with a Jewish woman within categories Noachide law would not normally recognize: specifically *na'arah me-orasah*, and additionally the case of *nichnesah la-chuppah v'lo niv'alah*.

## Ger Lineage

### Conception/Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיְתָה הוֹרָתוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, וְלֵידָתוֹ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה – יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁאֵר הָאֵם, וְאֵין לוֹ שְׁאֵר הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** **who was not conceived in sanctity,** as his parents were still gentiles, **but his birth was in sanctity,** as his mother converted before his birth, **he has maternal kinship,** i.e., his relationship to his mother's relatives is recognized, **but he does not have paternal kinship.**

The Gemara now raises a challenge to Rabbi Meir's formulation from a *baraita* concerning the *ger* — a convert — whose conception (*horato*) occurred before his mother's conversion but whose birth (*lidato*) occurred after she converted: *ger she-hayetah horato shelo bi-kedushah v'lidato bi-kedushah*. This individual presents a unique status: he was conceived as a non-Jew but born as a Jew.

### Maternal Kin Bar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָא כֵּיצַד? נָשָׂא אֲחוֹתוֹ מִן הָאֵם – יוֹצִיא, מִן הָאָב – יְקַיֵּים. אֲחוֹת הָאָב מִן הָאֵם – יוֹצִיא, מִן הָאָב – יְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If **he married his maternal half sister,** who was born before him and converted, **he must divorce** her. Although by Torah law they are considered unrelated, as a convert is considered to be reborn and all his previous family relationships are disregarded according to *halakha*, the Sages prohibited their marriage, lest he think that as a Jew it is permitted for him to engage in behaviors that were previously forbidden to him. If she is his **paternal** half sister, **he may maintain** her as his wife, as it is permitted for a gentile to marry his paternal half sister. If he married his **father's maternal half sister, he must divorce her.** If he married his father's **paternal** half sister, **he may maintain** her as his wife.

The *baraita* rules: *yesh lo she'er ha-em v'ein lo she'er ha-av* — he retains relatives through his mother but not through his father. Since his mother converted while carrying him, he is born as a Jew with a full Jewish mother, and the maternal kinship bonds carry over. He therefore cannot marry anyone to whom he is related through his mother.

### Paternal Kin OK

By contrast, his connection to his father's line existed entirely in the pre-Jewish period. Since the entire paternal relationship belongs to his non-Jewish past, the kinship bonds through his father do not carry over. He may, in principle, marry someone related to him through his father.

### Line Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחוֹת הָאֵם מִן הָאֵם – יוֹצִיא. אֲחוֹת הָאֵם מִן הָאָב, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יוֹצִיא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יְקַיֵּים. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל עֶרְוָה שֶׁהִיא מִשּׁוּם שְׁאֵר אֵם – יוֹצִיא, מִשּׁוּם שְׁאֵר הָאָב – יְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** If she is his **mother's maternal half sister, he must divorce** her. If she is his **mother's paternal half sister, Rabbi Meir says he must divorce** her, **and the Rabbis say he may maintain** her as his wife. **As Rabbi Meir would say:** With regard to **any forbidden relative who is** forbidden **due to maternal kinship,** whether the woman is his paternal relative, e.g., his father's maternal half sister, or his maternal relative, **he must divorce** her; whereas if she is forbidden **due to paternal kinship, he may maintain** her as his wife.

The practical implications of this distinction are spelled out in detail. If he marries his half-sister through his mother — a daughter she had previously — the marriage must be dissolved: *yotzi*. But if he marries a half-sister through his father, the marriage stands: *yekayem*. The same logic applies to his father's sisters. If his father's sister is a maternal half-sister of his father — sharing a grandmother — then *yotzi*. But if she shares only a grandfather with his father, then *yekayem*, the marriage is sustained. The principle emerging is that the maternal connection is determinative: kinship through women creates binding *arayot* prohibitions, while purely paternal kinship, rooted in the pre-conversion non-Jewish world, does not.

## Father/Mother Sisters

### Maternal Sister

Turning to the *ger*'s maternal aunts: if he seeks to marry his *achot ha-em min ha-em* — his mother's sister who shares the same mother — everyone agrees this is prohibited and the marriage must be dissolved.

### Half-Sister Debate

The dispute arises regarding *achot ha-em min ha-av* — his mother's half-sister who shares only a father with his mother. Rabbi Meir says *yotzi* — this too must be dissolved. The *Chachamim* say *yekayem* — this marriage may stand.

### Meir's Logic

Rabbi Meir's underlying principle is: *kol ervah she-hi mishum she'er em yotzi, mishum she'er av yekayem* — any *ervah* arising from a maternal kinship connection requires dissolution; any arising only from a paternal kinship connection is sustained. The maternal line is consistently the more restrictive one, generating prohibitions that the paternal line alone does not produce.

## Permitted Marriages

### Brother's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוּתָּר בְּאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו, וּבְאֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אָבִיו, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת לוֹ. לְאֵתוֹיֵי אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to all opinions, he is **permitted to** marry **his brother's wife and his father's brother's wife, and all other** relatives **with whom relations are forbidden** in the case of born Jew **are** also **permitted to him.** The expression: And all other relatives with whom relations are forbidden, is added **to include his father's wife,** who is permitted to him if she was widowed or divorced from his father.

The *baraita* continues: the *ger* is permitted to marry his brother's wife — *mutar b'eshet achiv*. The brother relationship, rooted in the pre-conversion non-Jewish world, does not carry over into the *ger*'s new Jewish status, and therefore the prohibition of *yibum*-related *arayot* does not apply.

### Uncle's Wife

Similarly, the *ger* is permitted to marry *eshet achi aviv* — the wife of his uncle, his father's brother. Again, the paternal-line kinship does not generate *arayot* prohibitions for the *ger*.

### Father's Wife

The *baraita* adds — and this is significant — that the *ger* is also permitted to marry his father's wife: *l'atuyei eshet aviv*. When the *baraita* says *she'er kol ha-arayot mutarot lo* — and all other *arayot* are permitted — this includes specifically his father's wife.

### Woman/Daughter Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, כּוֹנֵס אַחַת וּמוֹצִיא אַחַת. וּלְכַתְּחִילָּה לֹא יִכְנוֹס. מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ, מוּתָּר בַּחֲמוֹתוֹ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָסוּר בַּחֲמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a gentile who **married a woman and her daughter** and they all converted, **he may marry one but must divorce** the other **one; and he should not marry** the second of them ***ab initio*.** If **his wife,** the daughter, **died,** he is **permitted to** maintain **his mother-in-law** as his wife. **And some say** that he is **prohibited from** maintaining **his mother-in-law.** In any event, Rabbi Meir clearly holds that several forbidden relationships for which a Jew is not liable to receive capital punishment, but only *karet*, are forbidden to descendants of Noah as well, namely, intercourse with one's sister or one's parent's sister.

If the *ger* married a woman and her daughter simultaneously — *nasa isha u-vitah* — he must maintain relations with one and dissolve the other: *kones achat u-motzi achat*. Ideally, *l'chatchila*, he should never have entered such an arrangement. This reflects that even for the *ger*, the prohibition of a woman and her daughter applies in some form.

### Mother-in-Law

If his wife dies, he is permitted to his mother-in-law — *mutar b'chamato*. However, there are those who say he remains *asur* to his mother-in-law even after his wife's death: *yesh omrim asur b'chamato*.

## Rashi's Clarification

### Father's Wife

Rashi points out something that requires attention: the *ger* is permitted to marry his father's wife even though Jewish law imposes the death penalty for that relationship. This seems to directly contradict Rabbi Meir's principle that wherever Jewish *beit din* executes, the *ben Noach* — and by extension the *ger* in this pre-conversion state — is warned.

### Mother-in-Law

Similarly, the *ger* is permitted to his mother-in-law — again, a relationship for which Jewish law imposes capital punishment. Yet the *baraita* permits it for the *ger*.

### Sisters Bar

Conversely, the *ger* is prohibited from marrying his sister and certain aunts even though Jewish law does not impose the death penalty for those relationships. Yet Rabbi Meir apparently holds that wherever Jewish law does not kill, the *ben Noach* is not warned.

### Rashi Problem

The apparent contradiction is direct: the *baraita* about the *ger* seems to falsify Rabbi Meir's stated principle in both directions — permitting what Rabbi Meir says should be forbidden, and forbidding what Rabbi Meir says should be permitted. The Gemara addresses this by noting that there are in fact two different versions of Rabbi Meir's position, aligned with two different *Tannaim*.

## Meir Variations

### Al Kein Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״עַל כֵּן יַעֲזׇב אִישׁ אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ״. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״אָבִיו״ – אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, ״אִמּוֹ״ – אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אָבִיו״ – אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו, ״אִמּוֹ״ – אִמּוֹ מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother,** and shall cleave to his wife, and they shall be one flesh" (Genesis 2:24), that **Rabbi Eliezer says: "His father"** is referring to **his father's sister,** i.e., one must abandon the possibility of marrying his father's sister and marry someone else. **"His mother"** is referring to **his mother's sister. Rabbi Akiva says: "His father"** is referring to **his father's wife; "his mother"** is referring to **his mother, literally.**

The resolution begins with the pasuk *al kein ya'azov ish et aviv v'et imo* — "therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother." This verse, which appears in the context of marriage and is famously invoked at weddings, carries significant halakhic content in the Noachide context. The words *aviv* and *imo* — his father and his mother — are interpreted by Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Akiva in quite different ways.

### Eleazar View

Rabbi Elazar reads *aviv* as referring to the paternal aunt — *achot aviv* — and *imo* as referring to the maternal aunt — *achot imo*. The prohibition for a non-Jew against marrying his aunt is derived from these words. A non-Jewish man "leaves" not his literal parents but the family relationships embodied by his parents' sisters.

### Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva disagrees entirely. He reads *aviv* as *eshet aviv* — his father's wife — and *imo* as his literal mother, *imo mamash*. On this reading, the pasuk is teaching that a man must leave behind — i.e., not have relations with — his father's wife and his own mother. The aunts are simply not in view here.

### Bestiality Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְדָבַק״ – וְלֹא בְּזָכָר. ״בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בְּאֵשֶׁת חֲבֵירוֹ. ״וְהָיוּ לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד״ – מִי שֶׁנַּעֲשִׂים בָּשָׂר אֶחָד, יָצְאוּ בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה שֶׁאֵין נַעֲשִׂין בָּשָׂר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **"And shall cleave** to his wife," **but not to a male;** such a relationship is not defined as cleaving. **"To his wife," but not to the wife of another** man. **"And they shall be one flesh"** indicates that he should marry one of **those** with **whom** he can **become one flesh,** i.e., they can bear children together. This **excludes domesticated and undomesticated animals,** with which one is prohibited from engaging in bestiality, **as they do not become one flesh.** All these are forbidden to the descendants of Noah.

The pasuk continues: *v'davak b'ishto* — "and he shall cleave to his wife." From *v'davak*, the Gemara derives *v'lo b'zachar* — not to another male — establishing the Noachide prohibition against male homosexual relations. Further, from *b'ishto* — his wife — and not *eishes chaveiro* — his fellow's wife — the prohibition against adultery is established. And from *v'hayu l'vasar echad* — "and they shall become one flesh" — since only humans can become one flesh in the sense of producing children, this verse implicitly excludes bestiality, which cannot produce offspring: *yatzu behemah v'chayah she-ein na-asim basar echad*.

## Eleazar Challenge

### Father Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר ״אָבִיו״ – אֲחוֹת אָבִיו. אֵימָא: אָבִיו מַמָּשׁ? הַיְינוּ ״וְדָבַק״, וְלֹא בְּזָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in this *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: "His father"** is referring to **his father's sister.** The Gemara asks: Why not **say** that it is referring to **his father, literally,** prohibiting homosexual intercourse with one's father? The Gemara answers: **This** prohibition **is** derived from another phrase in the verse: **"And shall cleave** to his wife," **but not to a male;** this includes his father.

Having stated Rabbi Elazar's position that *aviv* refers to the paternal aunt, the Gemara challenges: why do we need a special derivation from *aviv* to prohibit the paternal aunt? Why not simply say that the paternal aunt is prohibited because of the prohibition against the father himself?

### Father Covered

The answer: the father is already excluded by the later verse, *v'lo b'zachar* — not with a male. Since a man cannot have relations with any man, that obviously encompasses his own father. Therefore, *aviv* cannot be referring to the father himself — he is already covered. The word *aviv* must therefore be pointing to a different relationship, and Rabbi Elazar identifies it as the paternal aunt.

## Akiva Challenge

### Mother Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אָבִיו״ – אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו. וְאֵימָא: אָבִיו מַמָּשׁ? הַיְינוּ ״וְדָבַק״ – וְלֹא בְּזָכָר. אִי הָכִי, אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו נָמֵי הַיְינוּ ״בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ״ וְלֹא בְּאֵשֶׁת חֲבֵרוֹ! לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: "His father"** is referring to **his father's wife.** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** it is referring to **his father, literally?** The Gemara answers: **That** prohibition **is** derived from the term **"and shall cleave** to his wife," **but not to a male.** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** the prohibition of **his father's wife is also** derived from another term: **"To his wife," but not to the wife of another** man. The Gemara explains: The term "to his wife" is referring to his father's wife **after** his father's **death,** when she is no longer married.

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Akiva's reading that *imo* means the mother literally. If *aviv* means *eshet aviv* — the father's wife — what is the basis for treating *imo* differently and reading it literally as the mother? Rabbi Akiva answers: the mother is analogous to the father's wife — *damya l'imo*. Just as the father's wife is prohibited not because of a marriage-based category (*eshes ish*) but because of her intimate connection to the father, so too the mother is prohibited through that same family-based logic rather than as an *eshes ish*. In other words, the prohibition of the mother is a *she'er*-based prohibition, not an *ervah*-based one.

## Eleazar Challenge (II)

### Mother Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִמּוֹ״ – אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ. וְאֵימָא אִמּוֹ מַמָּשׁ? הַיְינוּ בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ וְלֹא בְּאֵשֶׁת חֲבֵירוֹ. וְאֵימָא לְאַחַר מִיתָה? דּוּמְיָא דְּאָבִיו: מָה אָבִיו דְּלָאו מַמָּשׁ, אַף אִמּוֹ דְּלָאו מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer says that **"his mother"** is referring to **his mother's sister.** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that it is referring to **his mother, literally?** The Gemara answers: **This** prohibition **is** derived from the term **"to his wife," but not to the wife of another** man; and his mother is his father's wife. The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that it is referring to his mother **after** his father's **death,** when she is no longer married? The Gemara answers: The term "his mother" is interpreted in a way that is **similar to** the interpretation of the term **"his father." Just as** the term **"his father" is not** interpreted **literally, so too,** the term **"his mother" is not** interpreted **literally.**

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Elazar from the other direction. If *imo* refers to the maternal aunt, why do we need a specific derivation? Why not simply derive the prohibition from the prohibition of the mother herself?

## Akiva Challenge (II)

### Father Objection 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִמּוֹ״ – אִמּוֹ מַמָּשׁ. הַיְינוּ ״בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ״ וְלֹא בְּאֵשֶׁת חֲבֵרוֹ! אִמּוֹ מֵאֲנוּסָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva says: **"His mother"** is referring to **his mother, literally.** The Gemara challenges: **That** prohibition **is** derived from the term **"to his wife," but not to the wife of another** man. The Gemara explains: According to Rabbi Akiva, the term "his mother" is referring to **his mother whom** his father **raped,** i.e., she was never his father's wife.

Returning to Rabbi Akiva, the Gemara asks: if *aviv* means *eshet aviv* — the father's wife — why do we need to derive it from *aviv*? Should it not already follow from the prohibition of *eshet chaveiro* — another man's wife — which applies to all married women?

### Post-Death Question

The answer is that the prohibition of *eshet aviv* extends even after the father's death. An ordinary *eshes ish* prohibition dissolves when the husband dies — a widow is free to remarry. But the *eshet aviv* relationship carries a different quality: she remains prohibited to the son not merely as someone's wife, but because of her connection to the father, analogous to the way a mother remains prohibited regardless of the father's status.

### Household Rule

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Akiva's reading of *imo* as the literal mother: this should already be prohibited by the category of *eshet chaveiro*, since she was his father's wife and thus falls under the general prohibition against another man's wife. The answer, as worked out above, is that the mother may not have been his father's wife at all — she may have been a woman his father violated, *imo me-anusato*, never having been formally married. In that case, she would not be covered by *eshet chaveiro*, and a separate derivation from *imo mamash* is required.

## Meir Dispute Core

### Eleazar Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבִיו דּוּמְיָא דְּאִמּוֹ, וְאִמּוֹ דּוּמְיָא דְּאָבִיו. לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַחְוָוה.
>
> **Translation:** that the term **"his father"** should be interpreted in a way that is **similar to** the term **"his mother," and "his mother"** should be interpreted in a way that is **similar to "his father." You find** such an interpretation **only with regard to sisterhood,** i.e., "his father" is referring to his father's sister, and "his mother" is referring to his mother's sister.

The core dispute between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Akiva is now laid bare. Rabbi Elazar holds that *aviv* and *imo* in the pasuk are constructed as parallel, equivalent categories. If *aviv* refers to a female relative connected through the father's line, then *imo* by parallel must refer to a female relative connected through the mother's line. The logical outcome is that both words refer to the same category — sisters, specifically aunts — distinguished only by which parental line they come through. Rabbi Elazar's reading thus creates a symmetrical structure: *aviv* = paternal aunt, *imo* = maternal aunt.

### Akiva Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מוּטָב לְאוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּאֵשֶׁת אָבִיו, דְּאִיקְּרַי ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיו״, לְאַפּוֹקֵי אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, דִּ״שְׁאֵר אָבִיו״ אִיקְּרַי, ״עֶרְוַת אָבִיו״ לָא אִיקְּרַי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva** holds that **it is preferable to interpret** the term "his father" as referring **to his father's wife, who is referred to as his father's nakedness** in the verse: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of your father's wife; it is your father's nakedness" (Leviticus 18:8), **to the exclusion of his father's sister, who is referred to as his father's kin** in the verse: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of your father's sister; she is your father's kin" (Leviticus 18:12), and who **is not referred to as his father's nakedness.**

Rabbi Akiva rejects this categorical equivalence. He does not require *aviv* and *imo* to be parallel. Instead, he reads each independently: *aviv* refers to *ervat aviv* — his father's wife, who is the paradigmatic *ervah* connected to the father — while *imo* refers to his literal mother, *imo mamash*. Crucially, on Rabbi Akiva's reading, *achot aviv* — the paternal aunt — is not *ervat aviv* but *she'er aviv*, a kinship-based prohibition rather than a marital one.

### Ervah vs. She'er

The distinction between *ervah* and *she'er* is fundamental to understanding this dispute. *Ervah* refers to a prohibited relationship defined by its connection to a specific sexual or marital bond — a wife, and by extension the intimacy associated with that bond. *She'er* refers to a prohibited relationship defined by blood kinship alone, without the marital component. Rabbi Akiva argues that the paternal aunt is *she'er aviv*, not *ervat aviv*, and therefore is not captured by the word *aviv* in the pasuk.

## Aunt Marriage Case

### Amram/Yocheved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיִּקַּח עַמְרָם אֶת יוֹכֶבֶד דֹּדָתוֹ״. מַאי לָאו, דּוֹדָתוֹ מִן הָאֵם?
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof for the opinion of Rabbi Akiva from the verse: **"And Amram took Jochebed his aunt** as a wife" (Exodus 6:20). **What,** was she **not his maternal aunt?** Presumably, Jochebed was the sister of Kohath, Amram's father, from both of Kohath's parents, and not from his father alone. Evidently, a descendant of Noah may marry his father's sister.

The Gemara brings a textual proof: *vayikach Amram et Yocheved dodato* — "and Amram took Yocheved his aunt" (Shemot 6:20). This apparently demonstrates that marrying an aunt was practiced before the giving of the Torah.

### Which Side?

The question is immediately raised: which kind of aunt was Yocheved? Was she Amram's *dodat min ha-em* — his aunt through his mother — or his *dodat min ha-av* — his aunt through his father?

### Genealogy

The genealogy makes clear that Yocheved is Levi's daughter, and Amram is Levi's grandson through Kehat — Levi's son. Yocheved is therefore Kehat's sister and Amram's father's sister: she is his *dodat min ha-av*, his paternal aunt. She is not his aunt through his mother.

### Permission Inferred

From the Amram-Yocheved marriage, the Gemara infers that marrying one's father's sister — the paternal aunt — was permitted in the pre-Sinaitic period, which is precisely the period that governs Noachide law.

## Abraham's Sister

### Abraham's Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְגַם אׇמְנָה אֲחֹתִי בַת אָבִי הִיא אַךְ לֹא בַת אִמִּי״ – מִכְּלָל דְּבַת הָאֵם אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof for the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer from what Abraham said to Abimelech with regard to Sarah: **"And moreover, she is my sister, the daughter of my father, but not the daughter of my mother;** and so she became my wife" (Genesis 20:12). **By inference, the daughter of the mother** of a descendant of Noah **is forbidden** to him.

A second proof is brought from Avraham's own words regarding Sarah: *v'gam omnah achoti bat avi hi ach lo bat imi* — "and indeed she is my sister, the daughter of my father, but not the daughter of my mother."

### Maternal Bar

The implication of Avraham's formulation is significant: had Sarah been the daughter of his mother — a maternal half-sister — she would have been prohibited to him. By specifying that she is *bat avi* and not *bat imi*, Avraham signals that the paternal connection is the permissive factor and the maternal connection would have been the prohibiting one.

## Niece Interpretation

### Niece Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא אֲחוֹתוֹ הֲוַאי? בַּת אָחִיו הֲוַאי! וְכֵיוָן דְּהָכִי הוּא, לָא שְׁנָא מִן הָאָב וְלָא שְׁנָא מִן הָאֵם – שַׁרְיָא. אֶלָּא הָתָם הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: קוּרְבָּא דְּאָחוֹת אִית לִי בַּהֲדַהּ, מֵאַבָּא וְלָא מֵאִמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **But** how can **you understand** that Sarah **was** Abraham's **sister? She was his brother's daughter.** By tradition, it is known that Sarah was Haran's daughter Iscah. **And since that was so,** there **is no difference** whether they were **paternal** relatives, **and** there **is no difference** whether they were **maternal** relatives; in any event she was **permitted** to him, even according to the *halakha* of Jews. **Rather, this** is what Abraham **was saying to** Abimelech **there: She is related to me like a sister,** as the daughter of my brother is like a sister, and our relationship is **from** the side of my **father but not from** the side of my **mother.**

The Gemara offers an alternative interpretation: perhaps *achoto* in the relevant context means not "sister" but "niece" — *bat achiv*, the daughter of his brother.

### No Side Difference

If the word refers to a niece rather than a sister, then the distinction between paternal and maternal sides becomes irrelevant for this context, since the prohibition or permission of niece-marriage does not depend on which side the relationship runs through.

### Kinship Relation

Another possibility is raised: *kurba d'achot* — a relationship of "sister-type kinship" without being a direct sibling. This refers to a close collateral relative on the father's side — perhaps a paternal first cousin — where the connection is through the father's line without any direct maternal descent being involved.

## Adam & Daughter

### Adam's Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נָשָׂא אָדָם אֶת בִּתּוֹ? כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּשָּׂא קַיִן אֶת אֲחוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי אָמַרְתִּי עוֹלָם חֶסֶד יִבָּנֶה״. הָא לָאו הָכִי – אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **For what** reason **did Adam not marry his daughter? So that Cain would marry his sister** and they would procreate immediately, **as it is stated: "For I have said: The world shall be built on kindness [*ḥesed*]"** (Psalms 89:3). This verse alludes to the fact that at the beginning of the world's existence it was permitted for men to marry their sisters, which was later forbidden in the verse: "And if a man shall take his sister…it is a shameful thing [*ḥesed*]" (Leviticus 20:17). The Gemara infers: If it had **not** been **so,** if God had not specially permitted Cain to marry his sister, she would have been **forbidden** to him. This is difficult according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who deems it permitted for a gentile to marry his sister.

The Gemara raises a striking question: why did Adam not marry his own daughter? Adam and Chava had daughters — why was marriage with them not the arrangement that was made?

### Kayin Precedent

The answer given is: in order that Kayin could marry his sister. Had Adam married his daughter, that arrangement would have precluded Kayin from marrying the sister he ultimately married — presumably because of the precedent it would set or the structural arrangement of the family unit.

### Divine Justification

The basis for permitting Kayin to marry his sister is the verse: *ki amarti olam chesed yibaneh* — "for I said: the world shall be built with kindness" (Tehillim 89:3). The propagation of humanity required these early marriages, and the verse provides a divine sanction for what would otherwise appear to be a prohibited arrangement.

### Absence Implies Ban

The implication is notable: had this verse not existed, one would have concluded that marrying a sister is *asur* — prohibited. The very need to derive a permission from *olam chesed yibaneh* presupposes that without it, such a marriage would fall under the general Noachide prohibition.

### Permanence Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִשְׁתְּרַי, אִשְׁתְּרַי.
>
> **Translation:** **Once it was permitted** for Cain to marry his sister, **it was permitted** for all descendants of Noah to do so, and it was forbidden only to Jews.

Once the permission was granted, however, it remains permanently in effect: *d'keivan d'ishtarei ishtarei* — once it was permitted, it stays permitted. The Gemara does not treat this as a temporary dispensation for the generation of Kayin but as an established permission that cannot be withdrawn once granted.
