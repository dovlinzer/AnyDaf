# Menachot 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sweet Wine Validity

### Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין לֹא מָתוֹק וְלֹא מְבוּשָּׁל וְלֹא מְעוּשָּׁן, וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not bring** libations from **sweet** wine, **nor** from wine produced from **smoked** grapes, **nor** libations **from boiled** wine, **and if one did bring** a libation from such wine, it is **not valid.**

The *gemara* opens by citing the *mishnah*'s ruling: *ein mevi'in lo matok velo mevushal velo me'ushan* — one may not bring wine that is sweet, cooked, or smoky for the *nesachim*, and if one does bring such wine, it is invalid. This ruling is worth noting for a practical aside: there are those who hold that *yayin mevushal* may not be used for *kiddush* on the grounds that it is not *ra'uy agabei mizbe'ach* — not fit for use on the *mizbe'ach*. That position is not the widespread practice, but it reflects the real halachic weight of these Temple-based categories.

### Heliston Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין אֶת הֶלִיסְטְיוֹן, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not bring** libations from **sweet wine made from sun-dried grapes [*hilyasteyon*], but if one did bring** a libation from such wine, it is **valid.**

A tension immediately presents itself. The earlier clause of the *mishnah* stated: *ein mevi'in et ha-Heliston* — one does not bring *heliston*, which is understood to be a particularly sweet type of wine — *ve'im hevi kasher*, yet if one did bring it, it is valid *b'di'avad*. How does this square with the subsequent ruling that sweet wine is invalid even *b'di'avad*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: כְּרוֹךְ וְתָנֵי. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: חוּלְיָא דְּשִׁימְשָׁא – לָא מְאִיס, חוּלְיָא דְּפֵירָא – מְאִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The text of the mishna is corrupt. To correct it, **combine** the two clauses into one **and teach** with regard to all the wines mentioned that they are unfit to be used for libations. **Rav Ashi said:** The text of the mishna is correct. The reason for the difference between the two wines is that **the sweetness** of grapes sweetened **by the sun is not objectionable,** so libations of wine made from such grapes are valid, while **sweetness** that results from the sugars **of the fruit** itself **is objectionable,** so libations of wine made from such grapes are not valid.

Ravina resolves the contradiction with the principle of *karch u-teni* — read the two clauses together, weaving them into a unified ruling. *rashi* understands this to mean that the word *kasher* is effectively removed from the earlier clause, and sweet wine is invalid in all cases. *tosafot*, however, reads it in the opposite direction: the ruling on sweet wine is reinterpreted so that *b'di'avad* it is in fact valid. Either way, the contradiction is resolved by harmonizing the text — the direction of the harmonization is what remains debated.

### Rav Ashi Analysis


Rav Ashi offers an independent resolution that turns not on textual harmonization but on a substantive distinction in the nature of the sweetness itself. *chuya deshimshe lo ma'is* — sweetness that results from overexposure to the sun is not considered disgusting or wholly inappropriate. *chuya de-pere ma'is* — sweetness that is intrinsic to the grape variety itself is regarded as an inappropriate quality in sacrificial wine. The underlying principle, though no explicit *pesukim* are cited here, appears to be the broader concept of *hakrivehu na le-pechatecha* — would you offer this to a person of high standing? Wine whose character is fundamentally unsuited to refined consumption falls outside the acceptable range for the *mizbe'ach*, regardless of how it got that way. If, however, the sweetness is merely an artifact of sun conditions and not native to the grape, the wine does not cross that threshold.

## Old Wine Debate

### Rabbi vs. Rabbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין יָשָׁן – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי, וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not bring** wine **aged** for one year; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **but the Rabbis deem it valid.**

The *mishnah* rules: *ein mevi'in yashan divrei Rebbi* — one does not bring old wine; this is the position of *rebbi*. The *chakhamim*, by contrast, are *makhshirin* — they permit it outright. The instinct here might be to wonder why older wine would be problematic at all, given that wine often improves with age. The question is not one of quality in the conventional sense, but whether there is a specific requirement that the wine be of the current year.

### Lamb-Wine Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? אָמַר קְרָא ״לַכֶּבֶשׂ יָיִן״, מָה כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ, אַף יַיִן בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥizkiyya said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **The verse states** with regard to the libations that accompany the New Moon offering: "And their libations: Half a *hin* for a bull, a third of a *hin* for a ram, and a quarter of a *hin* **for a lamb, of wine"** (Numbers 28:14). The juxtaposition of the terms lamb and wine teaches that **just as a lamb** is fit to be used as an offering only if brought **in its** first **year, so too wine** is fit to be used as a libation only if it is **in its** first **year.**

*Chizkiya* offers the rationale behind *rebbi*'s position. The *pasuk* juxtaposes the wine offering with the lamb — *lakeves yayin* — and since a *keves* must be *ben shnato*, one year old, for the same linguistic juxtaposition teaches that the wine too must be *ben shnato*, of that year. The *gemara* immediately presses this analogy: just as a lamb that is two years old is invalid as a *keves* (having become an *ayil*), should we not say that wine more than one year old is also invalid *b'di'avad*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים – פָּסוּל, אַף יַיִן בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים – פָּסוּל! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא: יַיִן בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר! מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר ״לֹא יָבִיא״? רַבִּי, וְקָאָמַר: אִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** so, take the analogy further and conclude that **just as** if one offers **a lamb in** its **second year,** it is **not valid, so too** a libation of **wine in** its **second year** is **not valid. And if you would say** that this is **indeed** the *halakha*, that is difficult: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **wine in** its **second year may not be brought** *ab initio*, but **if one did bring it** as a libation, it is **valid?** That *baraita* certainly expresses the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, as **whom did you hear who said** that aged wine **may not be brought?** Only **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who explicitly states this opinion in the mishna. **And yet he says** in the *baraita*: **If one did bring** a libation of aged wine, it is **valid.** According to Rabbi Ḥizkiyya's explanation of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion, such an opinion is illogical.

*Rebbi* never went that far. Moreover, a *braita* states explicitly: *yayin ben shtei shanim lo yavi* — one should not bring two-year-old wine, *v'im heivi kasher* — but if one did, it is valid. Since *rebbi* is the only authority who holds *l'chat'chila* that old wine is problematic, this *kasher b'di'avad* must reflect even his own position. The lamb-wine analogy therefore cannot be the real basis for *rebbi*'s view, since that analogy, taken seriously, would generate a *b'di'avad* concern that the sources plainly reject.

### Rava's Redness Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי, דִּכְתִיב ״אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן כִּי יִתְאַדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: This is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **As it is written** in the verse exhorting a person not to be enticed by fine wines: **"Look not upon the wine when it is red"** (Proverbs 23:31). Evidently, the redness of wine is indicative of its quality. After a year, wine begins to lose its redness and so it should not be used, *ab initio*. Nevertheless, it is still of a sufficient quality to be acceptable, after the fact.

*Rava* therefore offers an alternative foundation for *rebbi*'s position. The *pasuk* states: *al tere yayin ki yitadam* — do not be drawn to wine because it is red — which implies that redness is what makes wine most attractive and desirable. The preference for red wine is well established, and finds expression in the practice of *leil ha-seder* as well. Apparently, wine in its first year retains its redness most vividly, and *rebbi* therefore holds that *l'chat'chila* one should bring wine of the current year. This raises a genuinely interesting conceptual question: when presenting something on the *mizbe'ach*, is the relevant standard one of taste or of appearance? The *torah* describes the *mizbe'ach* as consuming *lechem isheh* — food of fire — which might suggest taste is paramount. Yet since we certainly do not imagine that God consumes the offering in any literal sense, perhaps the symbolic and visual dimension is what matters most, and the standard would then be one of appearance. *rava*'s derivation seems to favor that latter position — *rebbi* grounds his *l'chat'chila* preference in the visual quality of wine's redness, not in its taste.

## Wine Source & Cultivation

### Trellis vs. Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְבִיאִין מִן הַדָּלִיּוֹת, אֶלָּא מִן הָרַגְלִיּוֹת, וּמִן הַכְּרָמִים הָעֲבוּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not bring** wine produced from **grapes suspended** on stakes or trees; **rather,** one brings it **from grapes at foot** height, i.e., that rest on the ground, which are superior-quality grapes, **and from vineyards that are cultivated,** i.e., where one hoes beneath the vines twice a year.

The *mishnah* further specifies that one does not bring wine from *daliyot* — grapevines grown on trellises — but rather from *ragliyot*, vines that grow along the ground. Beyond that, the wine should come specifically from *kramim ha-avudim* — worked, cultivated vineyards, not wild or neglected ones, where the ground has been dug and tended.

### Rav Yosef Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: כְּרָמִים הָעֲבוּדִים פַּעֲמַיִם בַּשָּׁנָה. רַב יוֹסֵף הֲוָה לֵיהּ קַרְנָא דְּפַרְדֵּיסָא, דְּרָפֵיק בֵּיהּ טְפֵי רִיפְקָא, וְעָבֵד חַמְרָא דְּדָרֵי מַיָּא עַל חַד תְּרֵין.
>
> **Translation:** The definition of vineyards that are cultivated is clarified in a *baraita* that **taught: Vineyards that are cultivated twice a year.** This is done by hoeing the earth underneath the vines. The Gemara relates the efficacy of cultivating the land twice a year: **Rav Yosef had a tract** of land **that was** used as **an orchard [*depardeisa*]** to **which he** used to **give an extra hoeing, and** consequently **it produced wine** of such superior quality that when preparing the wine for drinking it required **a dilution using twice** the amount **of water** than that which is usually used to dilute wine.

A *baraita* teaches that *kramim avudim* are those that are worked twice a year. The *gemara* illustrates this with a striking practical example: Rav Yosef had a corner of a vineyard, *karna d'pardisa*, in which he performed an extra digging — more than what was standard. The result was a wine, *chamra d'dari maya chad b'trei*, so potent that it could be diluted at twice the normal ratio. Typically wine was mixed with water at a three-to-one ratio; this wine was strong enough to be diluted six-to-one. The story concretely demonstrates how extra cultivation of the soil produces grapes of superior quality and concentration.

### Wine Storage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הָיוּ כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתָן בַּחֲצָבִין גְּדוֹלִים, תָּנָא: חָבִיּוֹת כַּדִּיּוֹת לוּדִיּוֹת וּבֵינוֹנִיּוֹת. אֵין מַנִּיחִין אוֹתָן שְׁתַּיִם שְׁתַּיִם, אֶלָּא אַחַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** When people produced wine for libations **they would not collect** the wine **into large barrels,** as it causes the wine to spoil; rather, it would be placed in small casks. The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: The **casks** referred to by the mishna are **flasks** that are made in **Lod and** that **are medium-sized.** The Gemara adds another *halakha*: When storing casks containing wine for libations, **they should not be placed in twos,** i.e., one atop the other, but **rather singly,** i.e., each one should be placed separately.

The *mishnah* further rules that the wine would not be stored in large barrels but in medium-sized jars — *chaviyot*, *kadiyot*, *ludiyot* — with the *baraita* specifying jars of the *lod* variety. Additionally, *ein manichin oto shnayim shnayim* — the jars would not be stored two together in the same place. *ela echad echad* — each jar was kept separately, so that if one were to go bad it would not contaminate its neighbor and compromise the quality of the wine.

## Wine Quality Control

### Gizbar Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד בּוֹדֵק? הַגִּזְבָּר יוֹשֵׁב, וְהַקָּנֶה בְּיָדוֹ, זָרַק הַגִּיר, הִקִּישׁ בַּקָּנֶה. תָּנָא: זָרַק הַגִּיר שֶׁל שְׁמָרִים – גִּזְבָּר הִקִּישׁ בַּקָּנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **How does** the Temple treasurer **inspect** wine to determine that it is from the middle of the cask? The **treasurer sits** alongside the cask **and** has **the** measuring **reed in his hand.** The spigot is opened and the wine begins to flow. If he sees that the wine emerging **draws** with it **chalk**-like scum, **he** immediately **knocks with the reed** to indicate that the spigot should be closed. The precise point at which he knocks is clarified in a *baraita* that **taught:** If the wine **draws** with it **chalk**-like scum, which comes **from the sediment, the treasurer knocks with the reed.**

Returning to the *mishnah*: *keitzad bodek ha-gizbar* — how does the *gizbar* inspect the wine to ensure its quality? He would sit holding a testing rod — *yoshev v'kaneh b'yado*. According to *rashi*'s reading, when the spigot was opened and white dross emerged near the top, the *gizbar* would bang the rod to signal that the spigot should be closed. A *tana* elaborates: *zarak ha-gir shel shemarim* — if the lees and white dross came spurting out, the *gizbar* would bang his rod to signal rejection.

### Silent Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ מֵימָר? מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַדִּיבּוּר יָפֶה לַבְּשָׂמִים, כָּךְ דִּיבּוּר רַע לַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: Why does the treasurer knock with the reed; **let him** simply **speak.** The Gemara explains: This **supports** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Just as speech is beneficial to the** incense **spices, so is speech detrimental to wine,** and so the treasurer avoids speaking.

One might ask: why does the *gizbar* not simply say aloud that the wine is unacceptable? Why does he reject it wordlessly? The *gemara* answers that this practice supports the teaching of *rabbi yochanan*.

### Speech Effect


*Rabbi Yochanan* taught: *keshem shehadibur yafeh labsamin* — just as speech is beneficial when working with spices (in the preparation of the *ketoret*, speaking certain words enhances the quality of the beating and blending), *kach dibur ra l'yayin* — so too speech is harmful to wine. When wine is in a sensitive state, speaking near it negatively affects its quality. The *gizbar* therefore communicates by gesture rather than voice, so as not to damage the very wine he is inspecting.

## Flour Impurities

### Impurity Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ קְמָחִין – פָּסוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּמִימִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם״, ״וּמִנְחָתָם״, ״תְּמִימִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** Wine **in which there is flour-**like white scum is **unfit** for libations, **as it is stated** with regard to animal offerings: **"Unblemished they shall be for you…and their meal offering** shall be fine flour mixed with **oil…unblemished they shall be for you, and their libations"** (Numbers 28:19–20, 31). This indicates that animal offerings, meal offerings, and libations must all be brought from flawless products. Therefore, the presence of flour-like white scum in wine renders it unfit.

The *mishnah* concludes with the ruling of *rabbi yossi bar rabbi yehuda*: if the wine contains *kemachim* — flour-like impurities and similar adulterants — it is invalid. His source is the *pasuk*: *temimim yihyu lachem uminchatam veniskeihem kamishpat* — the sacrifices must be *temimim*, unblemished, and the *mincha* and the *nesachim* are included in that requirement of wholeness.

### Mum Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הִקְדִּישׁוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּלְקֶה עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם? כֵּיוָן דְּפָסוּל – כְּבַעַל מוּם דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין בַּעַל מוּם אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a dilemma** concerning such wine: If **one consecrated it** to be used as a libation, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he should be flogged for** consecrating **it due to** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item as an offering? Does one say that **since it** is **unfit, it is comparable to a blemished** animal? **Or perhaps,** the prohibition to consecrate **a flawed** item **applies only to an animal.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Rabbi Yochanan* raises a question that he poses in several analogous contexts throughout the *gemara*: does the concept of *mum* — blemish — extend beyond animals to non-animal offerings? The question is specifically: if one sanctifies this wine — wine containing these flour-like impurities — does one incur lashes for having sanctified a *ba'al mum*? The prohibition of sanctifying a blemished animal carries *malkot*; does that *lav* extend to non-animal sacrificial items?

The argument for extending the prohibition is straightforward: *keivan d'pasul, ba'al mum dami* — since it is invalid, it is treated like a *ba'al mum*. And in this specific case there is particular force to the argument, since the *pasuk* of *temimim* is explicitly applied. The counterargument is: *ein ba'al mum ela b'beheimah* — the concept of *mum* and the accompanying *lav* apply only to animals. The *gemara* here, as in the parallel discussions, concludes with *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

## Animal Quality

### Geographic Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵילִים מִמּוֹאָב, כְּבָשִׂים מֵחֶבְרוֹן, עֲגָלִים מִשָּׁרוֹן, גּוֹזָלוֹת מֵהַר הַמֶּלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The choicest **rams** are those **from Moab;** the choicest **lambs** are those **from Hebron;** the choicest **calves** are those **from Sharon;** and the choicest **fledglings,** i.e., doves and pigeons, are those **from the King's Mountain.**

Having completed the *mishnah* and its *gemara*, a *baraita* now rounds out the picture of where the best sacrificial materials were sourced. The Sages taught: *eilim miMoav* — the finest rams came from Moav; *kevasim miHevron* — the best lambs from Chevron; *agalim miSharon* — the finest calves from the Sharon; *gozalot meHar hamelech* — the best birds from Har Hamelech.

### Wide-Backed Lambs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְבִיאִין כְּבָשִׂים שֶׁגׇּבְהָן כְּרׇחְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: One should bring lambs whose height is like their width,** i.e., they are so robust that they are as wide as they are tall.

*Rabbi Yehuda* adds a qualitative specification: *mevi'im kevasim shegavham kerachbam* — one should bring lambs whose backs are as wide as they are high, or in the variant reading, *shegavan rechovim*, lambs with notably wide backs — large, broad-backed animals.

### Scripture Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דִּכְתִיב: ״יִרְעֶה מִקְנְךָ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא כַּר נִרְחָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Sheila said: What is the reason of Rabbi Yehuda? As it is written:** "And He will give the rain for your seed, with which you sow the ground, and bread of the produce of the ground, and it shall be fat and bountiful; **your cattle shall graze in wide pastures [*kar nirḥav*] on that day"** (Isaiah 30:23). The word "*kar*" can also mean a lamb, and "*nirḥav*" means wide. Accordingly, Rabbi Yehuda interprets this verse, on a homiletical level, to be alluding to robust sheep.

*Rava bar Rav Shila* identifies the scriptural source for *rabbi yehuda*'s preference. The *pasuk* states: *yireh miknecha bayom hahu kar nirchav* — your cattle shall graze on that day in a wide pasture. The word *kar* can denote both a lamb and something wide and expansive — *kar nirchav* can also mean a broad cushion or wide support. *rabbi yehuda* thus reads into the term *kar* a preference for lambs that are themselves *nirchav*, wide and broad.

This discussion reinforces the earlier question about whether sacrificial standards are governed by taste or by appearance. The emphasis here is clearly on appearance — on the visual impressiveness and aesthetic appeal of the animal — rather than on any quality that would bear on taste. The focus on appearances in this context seems to be the animating principle across these rulings.

## Watchers Coda

### Watchers' Plea

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״עַל חוֹמֹתַיִךְ יְרוּשָׁלִַם הִפְקַדְתִּי שֹׁמְרִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם וְכׇל הַלַּיְלָה תָּמִיד לֹא יֶחֱשׁוּ הַמַּזְכִּירִים אֶת ה׳ אַל דֳּמִי לָכֶם״. מַאי אָמְרִי? הָכִי אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: ״אַתָּה תָקוּם תְּרַחֵם צִיּוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The chapter concludes by quoting an additional prophecy of Isaiah concerning the rebuilding of Eretz Yisrael: It **is written: "I have set watchmen upon your walls, Jerusalem; they shall never be silent day nor night; those who remind the Lord, take no rest"** (Isaiah 62:6). This is referring to the angels appointed by God to bring the redemption. The Gemara asks: **What** do these watchmen **say** to remind the Lord? **This** is what **Rava bar Rav Sheila said:** They recite the verse: **"You will arise and have compassion upon Zion;** for it is time to be gracious to her, for the appointed time has come" (Psalms 102:14).

The *perek* of *kol korbanot tzibur* closes not with a strictly halachic passage but with a moving *aggadeta*, introduced by a quotation from *rava bar rav shila* and naturally arising from the broader lens the *gemara* has adopted — looking not just at individual *korbanot* in their technical details but at the entire living reality of the *beit haMikdash*: where the wine came from, where the flour came from, where the oil came from, how these materials were cultivated and prepared. From that broader perspective the *gemara* brings a verse from *yeshayahu*: *al chomosayich Yerushalayim hifkadeti shomrim kol hayom v'chol halayla tamid lo yecheshu hamazkirim et Hashem al dami lachem* — on your walls, O Jerusalem, I have appointed watchers; all day and all night they shall never be silent; those who call upon God, do not be silent. These are the watchmen stationed around the ruins of Jerusalem after the *churban*, constantly speaking, evoking the memory of the *mikdash*. What would these watchers say? *rava bar rav shila* teaches: *ata takum terachem tziyon* — may You, God, arise and have compassion upon Zion, rebuild the *beit haMikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: ״בּוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם ה׳״. וּמֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי הֲווֹ אָמְרִי? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: ״כִּי בָחַר ה׳ בְּצִיּוֹן אִוָּהּ לְמוֹשָׁב לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** They recite the verse: **"The Lord builds up Jerusalem,** He gathers together the dispersed of Israel" (Psalms 147:2). The Gemara asks: **And initially,** when the Temple still stood and the Jewish people were gathered together in Eretz Yisrael, **what would** the watchmen **say? Rava bar Rav Sheila says:** They would say: **"For the Lord has chosen Zion; He has desired it for His habitation.** This is My resting place forever; here will I dwell for I have desired it" (Psalms 132:13–14).

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* adds: *bonei yerushalayim hashem* — God will rebuild Jerusalem.

With that, the *gemara* declares *hadran alach kol korbanot tzibur* — the conclusion of the *perek*.

### Original Statement


And what did these same watchers say originally, when the *beit haMikdash* still stood? *rava bar rav shila* teaches again: *ki bachar hashem b'tziyon ivah l'moshav lo* — for God has chosen Zion; He desired it to be His dwelling place.

With that, the *gemara* declares *hadran alach kol korbanot tzibur* — the conclusion of the *perek*.

## Dry Measures

### Two Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי מִדּוֹת שֶׁל יָבֵשׁ הָיוּ בְּמִקְדָּשׁ, עִשָּׂרוֹן וַחֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: עִשָּׂרוֹן, עִשָּׂרוֹן, וַחֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Two** sizes of **measuring vessels for dry** substances **were** used **in the Temple** for measuring flour for the meal offerings. One held **a tenth** of an ephah **and** the other held **one-half of a tenth** of an ephah. **Rabbi Meir says:** There were three measuring vessels; one that held **a tenth** of an ephah, another one that also held **a tenth** of an ephah, **and** a third one that held **one**-**half of a tenth** of an ephah.

The new *perek* opens with the *mishnah*: *shtei midot shel yavesh hayu b'mikdash* — there were two dry measures in the *beit haMikdash*. These were used for measuring flour and are thus directly relevant to *menachot*: an *isaron* and a *chatzi isaron* — a tenth-measure and a half-tenth-measure. The *isaron* is the standard unit for a *mincha*. Larger *menachot* exist — the *minchat nesachim* for a lamb requires one *isaron*, for a ram two, for an ox (*par*) three — but all measurement was done in individual *isaron* increments. Crucially, the Temple did not maintain a two-*isaron* or three-*isaron* vessel; one would simply use the single *isaron* measuring cup multiple times. The *chatzi isaron*, as the *gemara* will explain, was used specifically for the *chavitay Kohen Gadol* — the daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*, which consisted of one *isaron* divided equally between morning and afternoon.

### Meir vs. Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עִשָּׂרוֹן, מֶה הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ? שֶׁבּוֹ הָיָה מוֹדֵד לְכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת, לֹא הָיָה מוֹדֵד לֹא בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה לַפָּר, וְלֹא בְּשֶׁל שְׁנַיִם לָאַיִל, אֶלָּא מוֹדְדָן עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **What** purpose **did** the **tenth** of an ephah measuring vessel **serve?** It was the vessel **with which one would measure** flour **for all the meal offerings. One would not measure** the flour by using a measuring vessel of a size that held the entire volume of flour required at once, i.e., **neither with** a vessel of **three-**tenths of an ephah **for** the meal offering accompanying the sacrifice of **a bull, nor with** a vessel of **two**-tenths of an ephah **for** the meal offering accompanying the sacrifice of **a ram. Rather, one measures** the flour for **them** by repeatedly using the tenth of an ephah measuring vessel to measure the required number of **tenths.**

*Rabbi Meir* holds: *isaron isaron v'chatzi isaron* — there were in fact two types of *isaron* vessels plus the *chatzi isaron*, making three measuring vessels in total. Since the two *isaron* vessels must have served distinct purposes, they cannot have been identical in size; they were presumably of slightly different measurement. *the sages* maintain that there was only one type of *isaron* vessel, plus the *chatzi isaron*, making two in total.

## Heaped vs. Leveled

### Double Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עִשָּׂרוֹן עִשָּׂרוֹן לַכֶּבֶשׂ הָאֶחָד״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשְׁתֵּי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, אֶחָד גָּדוּשׁ וְאֶחָד מָחוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a *baraita* that clarifies Rabbi Meir's opinion. It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "A tenth, a tenth, for every lamb"** (Numbers 28:29)? The fact the word "tenth" appears twice **teaches that there were two** measuring vessels that each held **a tenth** of an ephah **in the Temple. One** of them held that volume when it was **heaped, and** the other **one** was slightly larger and held that same volume when the flour was **leveled** with the rim.

A *baraita* cites *rabbi meir*'s derivation. The *torah* in *parshat pinchas*, listing the festival *korbanot*, uses the doubled formulation *isaron isaron lakeves ha'echad* — one *isaron*, one *isaron* — for each of the seven lambs. Why the repetition? *melamed sheshtei isronot hayu bamikdash* — it teaches that there were two *isaron* vessels in the *beit haMikdash*: *echad gadush ve'echad machuk* — one was heaped (*gadush*) and one was leveled (*machuk*). The distinction is familiar from everyday usage: a heaping measure versus a level measure. The *gadush* vessel was slightly smaller in physical capacity, such that when flour was allowed to mound above the rim, the total quantity equaled one *isaron*. The *machuk* vessel was precisely one *isaron* when leveled flat to the rim — and therefore physically larger as a container.

### Usage by Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּדוּשׁ שֶׁבּוֹ הָיָה מוֹדֵד לְכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת, מָחוּק שֶׁבּוֹ הָיָה מוֹדֵד לַחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The one that held a tenth of an ephah when **heaped** was the vessel **with which one would measure** the flour **for all the meal offerings.** The one that held a tenth of an ephah when **leveled** was the vessel **with which one would measure** the flour **for** the **High Priest's griddle-cake** offering, and then that flour would be divided into two equal parts.

*Rabbi Meir* teaches that the *gadush* vessel — the heaping measure — was used *shebo hayu modedin lechol haminchos*, for measuring all the standard *menachot*. The implication may be that for those offerings a small degree of imprecision was tolerable; the mound atop the rim could conceivably add a slight extra amount, but that was acceptable. The *machuk* vessel — the precisely leveled measure — *shebo hayu modedin lechavitei Kohen Gadol*, was used specifically for the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha*. *rashi* explains the reasoning: when you level off the top of the vessel, you can pour and divide the contents into two equal halves without any spillage from a mound. Precision in division demands a level starting point. One might add that the very fact of dividing into exact halves calls for a more exacting standard of measurement throughout.

## Sages' Position

### Single Type Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא הָיָה שָׁם אֶלָּא עִשָּׂרוֹן אֶחָד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעִשָּׂרוֹן אֶחָד לַכֶּבֶשׂ הָאֶחָד״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עִשָּׂרוֹן עִשָּׂרוֹן״? לְרַבּוֹת חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** disagree with Rabbi Meir and **say: There was only one** measuring vessel that held **a tenth** of an ephah **there** in the Temple, **as it is stated: "And one tenth-part for every lamb"** (Numbers 29:4). But **if so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "A tenth, a tenth,** for every lamb" (Numbers 28:29)? Rabbi Meir derived from the repetition of "a tenth" that there were two measuring vessels that held a tenth of an ephah. How do the Rabbis expound that? They hold it serves **to include** another measuring vessel for dry substances, one that holds **one-half of a tenth** of an ephah.

The Sages counter *rabbi meir*'s position by citing a different verse: *ve'isaron echad lakeves ha'echad* — one *isaron* for one lamb. The phrase *isaron echad*, "one *isaron*," teaches that there was only one type of *isaron* vessel in the *beit haMikdash*, not two.

### Half-Isaron Derivation


If so, what does the Sages' position do with the doubled language of *isaron isaron* from which *rabbi meir* derived two types of *isaron*? *lirbot chatzi isaron* — the repetition comes to include the *chatzi isaron*, teaching that there is a variant on the *isaron* in the form of the half-measure, but not that there are two distinct full-*isaron* vessels. And *rabbi meir* — where does he derive the *chatzi isaron*? From the *vav* of *ve'isaron echad lakeves ha'echad* — the conjunctive *vav* indicates something additional to the standard *isaron*, which he reads as the *chatzi isaron*. The Sages, however, do not derive anything from that *vav*, and so they take the *chatzi isaron* from the doubled *isaron isaron* instead.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וְּעִשָּׂרוֹן״. וְרַבָּנַן, וָי״ו לָא דָּרְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** Having cited the *baraita*, the Gemara discusses each opinion: **And** as for **Rabbi Meir, from where** does **he** derive that there was a measuring vessel that held **one-half of a tenth** of an ephah? **He derives it from** the superfluous "and" in the phrase **"and one tenth-part** for every lamb." The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** why don't they expound this from the term "and"? **They do not derive** anything from **"and."** They hold that the addition of the word is not significant enough to be expounded.

A *baraita* cited by *rabbi yossi* further illuminates this: the *torah* places a scribal dot — a *nekuda* — over the *vav* of the first *isaron* in the *succot* readings, on the first day of *yom tov*. The purpose is to teach *she'lo yimdod lo beshel gimel lapar v'lo beshel shnayim la'ayil* — that even for an ox, which requires three *isronot*, and for a ram, which requires two, one must never use a larger vessel but must use only the single *isaron* measure repeatedly. The dotted *vav*, in effect, emphasizes: only the *isaron*, singular.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַאי ״וְעִשָּׂרוֹן אֶחָד לַכֶּבֶשׂ הָאֶחָד״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? הַהוּא שֶׁלֹּא יִמְדּוֹד לֹא בְּשֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה לַפָּר, וְלֹא בְּשֶׁל שְׁנַיִם לָאַיִל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Meir, this** verse: **"And one tenth-part for every lamb,"** from which the Rabbis derive that there was only one measuring vessel of a tenth of an ephah, **what does he do with it?** The Gemara answers: **That** verse teaches that **one should not measure** the flour by using a measuring vessel of a size that holds the entire volume of flour required, i.e., **neither with** a vessel that holds **three-**tenths of an ephah **for** the meal offering accompanying the sacrifice of **a bull, nor with** a vessel that holds **two**-tenths of an ephah **for** the meal offering accompanying the sacrifice of **a ram.** Rather, one measures the flour for them by using the tenth-ephah measuring vessel multiple times.

For *rabbi meir*, the *nekuda* teaches nothing — he does not derive *halacha* from scribal dots — and he learns the prohibition against multi-*isaron* vessels from the verse *isaron echad lakeves ha'echad*, which the Sages instead read as teaching a single type of *isaron*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, נָפְקָא לְהוּ מִנְּקוּדֵּי, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לָמָּה נָקוּד וָי״ו שֶׁבְּאֶמְצַע ״עִשָּׂרוֹן״ שֶׁל ״עִשָּׂרוֹן״ רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג? שֶׁלֹּא יִמְדּוֹד לֹא בְּשֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה לְפַר וְלֹא בְּשֶׁל שְׁנַיִם לָאַיִל. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר נְקוּדֵּי לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** from where do they derive that *halakha*? The Gemara explains: **They derive** it **from its dot.** In the Torah text, a dot appears above the term "a tenth." This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says: Why is** the letter ***vav* in the middle of** the term **"a tenth [*issaron*]" dotted** the **first** time that the term **"a tenth"** appears in the verse **concerning** the **first festival day of the Festival** of *Sukkot*? The verse there states: "And a tenth, a tenth, for every lamb of the fourteen lambs" (Numbers 29:15). This serves to teach **that one should not measure** flour using a vessel of a size that holds the entire volume required, i.e., **neither with** a vessel that holds **three-**tenths of an ephah **for** the meal offering of **a bull, nor with** a vessel that holds two-tenths of an ephah **for** the meal offering of **a ram. And** as for **Rabbi Meir,** what does he derive from the dot? **He does not derive** anything from **its dot.** He holds that the dot is not significant enough to be expounded.

## HP Mincha Division

### Measure Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן, מָה הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ? שֶׁבּוֹ הָיָה מוֹדֵד לַחֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, מֶחֱצָה בַּבֹּקֶר וּמֶחֱצָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **What** purpose **did** the **one**-**half of a tenth** of an ephah measuring vessel **serve?** It was the vessel **with which one would measure** the flour **for the High Priest's griddle-cake** offering. A tenth of an ephah was required each day; he sacrificed **half** of it **in the morning and** the other **half** of it **in the afternoon.**

An apparent tension arises from the *mishnah*'s statement that one would *moded* — measure — with the *chatzi isaron* for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*: half in the morning, half in the afternoon. Yet a separate *braita* rules explicitly: *chavitei Kohen Gadol lo hayu ba'im chatzaim* — one does not bring the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha* in separate halves. Rather, *ela meivi isaron shalem v'chotzeihu* — one brings a full *isaron* and then divides it in two. This means the primary measurement was done with a full *isaron*, not a *chatzi isaron*. In what sense, then, was the *chatzi isaron* used for *measuring*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹדֵד? וּרְמִינְהִי: חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לֹא הָיוּ בָּאִין חֲצָאִין, אֶלָּא מֵבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם וְחוֹצֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna indicates that the half-tenth vessel was used for **measuring. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this from a mishna (50b): The twelve loaves of *matza*, baked from a tenth of an ephah of flour, of the **griddle-cake** offering **of the High Priest did not come** from the house of the High Priest **in halves. Rather,** the High Priest **brings** from his house **a complete tenth** of an ephah of flour (see Leviticus 6:13) **and divides it** in half, and he sacrifices half in the morning and half in the afternoon. It is apparent from this mishna that the tenth of an ephah is first measured in its entirety and only then divided.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַאי ״מוֹדֵד״ נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי? מְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** To resolve the contradiction **Rav Sheshet said: What is** the meaning of: Used for **measuring, that** the mishna here **teaches?** It means only that the High Priest would **divide** the tenth of an ephah into two equal portions using the half-tenth measure, but the quantity would initially be measured in its entirety, as the mishna on 50b states.

*Rav Sheshet* resolves this: *mai moded nami de'katani mechaleik* — when the *mishnah* says the *chatzi isaron* was used for *moding*, it means it was used for *dividing* — specifically, for dividing the already-measured full *isaron* into its two equal halves. The *chatzi isaron* was not the primary measuring instrument but the tool by which the full *isaron* was apportioned correctly.

## Heaping Logic

### Rami's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן, לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – גָּדוּשׁ הָיָה אוֹ מָחוּק הָיָה?
>
> **Translation:** § In light of Rabbi Meir's opinion that there were two vessels for measuring a tenth of an ephah, one that held its measure when leveled and one when it was heaped, **Rami bar Ḥama raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** With regard to the **one-half of a tenth** of an ephah measuring vessel, **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, was** it one that held its measure when **heaped or was** it one that held its measure when **leveled?**

*Rami bar Chama* posed a question to *rav chisda*: for *rabbi meir*, who holds that there were two types of *isaron* vessels — one *gadush* and one *machuk* — what was the *chatzi isaron*? Was it a heaped measure or a leveled measure?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיבְּעֵי לָךְ לְרַבָּנַן? לְרַבָּנַן, עִשָּׂרוֹן גּוּפֵיהּ קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: גָּדוּשׁ הָיָה אוֹ מָחוּק הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said to Rami bar Ḥama: **But** why do you ask specifically with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Meir? **The dilemma can be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** Rami bar Ḥama answered: **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** it would be with regard to the measuring vessel of **a tenth** of an ephah **itself** that **one raises the dilemma,** asking whether it **was** one that held its measure when **heaped or was** one that held its measure when **leveled.** The dilemma I raised concerned the half-tenth vessel, which is pertinent specifically according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, as he holds that there were both heaped and leveled measuring vessels of a tenth of an ephah.

The *gemara* immediately broadens the question: and why ask only about *rabbi meir*? For the *rabbanan*, too, we want to know what their single *isaron* vessel was — heaped or leveled?

### All Measures Leveled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר נִשְׁמַע לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וּמִדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר נִשְׁמַע לְרַבָּנַן. מִדְּאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: עִשָּׂרוֹן מָחוּק, חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן נָמֵי מָחוּק; מִדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר מָחוּק, לְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי מָחוּק.
>
> **Translation:** In answer to Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma, Rav Ḥisda **said to him: From** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir** concerning the vessel of a tenth of an ephah **we can extrapolate** what **Rabbi Meir** holds concerning the vessel of one-half of a tenth of an ephah; **and from** that opinion **of Rabbi Meir we can extrapolate** what **the Rabbis** hold concerning both vessels. Rav Ḥisda elaborates: **From** the fact **that Rabbi Meir said** that the vessel of **a tenth** of an ephah used for the High Priest's griddle-cake offering held its measure when **leveled,** we can extrapolate that the vessel of **one-half of a tenth, as well,** was such that it held its measure when **leveled.** It is reasonable that since both vessels were used for the same offering, they should be of the same type. And **from** the fact **that Rabbi Meir** holds that vessels used for the High Priest's griddle-cake offering held their measures when **leveled,** we can extrapolate that **also according to the Rabbis,** who hold that both vessels were used for the High Priest's griddle-cake offering, both of them held their measures when **leveled.**

*Rav Chisda* responds that from *rabbi meir*'s position one can derive the answer for *rabbi meir* regarding his *chatzi isaron*, and from *rabbi meir* one can also derive the answer for the *rabbanan*. Here is the logical chain: since *rabbi meir* explicitly states that the *machuk* — leveled — *isaron* was used for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, and since the *chatzi isaron* was also used for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, it follows that the *chatzi isaron* for *rabbi meir* was likewise *machuk*. The logic is reinforced by the practical consideration: if you need to divide an *isaron* into two precise halves, working with a leveled measurement affords far greater precision than working with a mound.

From this one can derive the *rabbanan*'s position as well. The *rabbanan* never disagreed with *rabbi meir* about the *chatzi isaron* — their dispute was only about whether there were two types of full *isaron* vessels. Since both agree the *chatzi isaron* was *machuk*, and since the *rabbanan* use their single *isaron* for everything including the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, and since that *isaron* must accommodate precise halving just as the *chatzi isaron* must, one can conclude that the *rabbanan*'s single *isaron* was also *machuk*. The result is that across all opinions, all Temple dry measures were leveled — a conclusion that makes sense on its own terms, since greater precision is served by a level measure, and the requirement of exact halving makes imprecision particularly problematic.

## Dough Division

### By Hand Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בַּמֶּה מְחַלְּקָן לְחַלּוֹת, בַּיָּד אוֹ בִּכְלִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised** another **dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** With regard to the dough used for the **High Priest's griddle-cake** offering, **with what** would **one divide it into** the **loaves** of the offering, six in the morning and six in the afternoon? Was the division done **with** one's **hand or with** a measuring **vessel?**

*Rami bar Chama* asked *rav chisda* a further question: when dividing the dough for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol* — taking each half-*isaron* of flour and forming six individual *challot* from it — *bameh mechalkan lachallot*? How does one divide the dough into sixths? *beyad*, by hand and approximation? Or *bichli*, using some kind of precise measuring vessel? *rashi* understands the question as arising after the flour has been mixed and one is working with actual dough; *tosafot* understands it as a question about dividing the dry flour into sixths before mixing.

### Against Scales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא דִּבְיַד, דְּאִי בִּכְלִי – טוּרְטָנֵי יַכְנִיס? וְיַכְנִיס! כֵּיוָן דְּבִקְלָלָה כְּתִיב, לָאו אוּרַח אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said to him: It is **obvious that** it was divided **with** one's **hand, as if** one suggests that it was done **with** a measuring **vessel, would one bring scales [*turtanei*]** into the Temple courtyard for this purpose? Rami bar Ḥama responds: **And let him bring** scales into the courtyard. Rav Ḥisda explains: **Since in** God's admonition of the Jewish people the act of weighing bread is **written** as part of **a curse:** "When I break your staff of bread, ten women shall bake your bread in one oven, and they shall deliver your bread again by weight; and you shall eat and not be satisfied" (Leviticus 26:26), as weighing bread is generally performed only at a time of famine, therefore it is **not proper conduct** to weigh the dough in the Temple.

*Rav Chisda* responds: it is obvious that the division is done *beyad*, by hand. The reason is captured in the phrase *torat aniyah tachnis* — do you want to bring in the *torah* of poverty, the symbolism of scarcity? To measure out flour or dough in such small increments as a twelfth of an *isaron*, one would need a precision scale. That kind of weighing is associated with *dochak* — deprivation and shortage. The *pasuk* in the curses of *vayikra* warns: *veheishivu lachmechem bemishkal* — they will weigh out your bread — meaning bread rationed by weight in times of famine. To measure dough on a scale is to evoke that curse. *rashi* applies this directly to the dough context. *tosafot* extends it even to the flour: using a precision scale in the *mikdash* to measure tiny amounts is itself inappropriate, since the *mikdash* should project abundance rather than scarcity.

## Shulchan Sanctity

### Sanctification Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: שֻׁלְחָן מַהוּ שֶׁיְּקַדֵּשׁ קְמָצִים בְּגוֹדֶשׁ שֶׁלּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised** another **dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** With regard to the shewbread **Table, what is** the *halakha* **as** to whether **it consecrates handfuls** removed from meal offerings or from frankincense that were not consecrated by being placed in a service vessel, as they should have been, but were instead placed **on the upper part of** the Table, between the rows of shewbread?

*Rami bar Chama* posed yet another question to *rav chisda*: *shulchan mahu sheyekadesh kematzim begodesh shelo* — can the *shulchan* in the *beit haMikdash* sanctify things placed upon it other than the *lechem hapanim*, specifically in the mound (*godesh*) above its surface? *rashi* understands *kematzim* as referring to the *levonah* — the frankincense offered as part of the *lechem hapanim* service — which, if placed directly on the *shulchan* surface rather than in the *bezychin* (the small spoons designated for it), would raise the question of whether the *shulchan* sanctifies it. *tosafot* understands the question as referring to a *kometz* of a *mincha*: ordinarily one places a *kometz* in a *kli sharet* to sanctify it before bringing it to the *mizbe'ach*; what if instead it were placed on the *shulchan*?

Either way, the question has two dimensions. First, does the *shulchan* function as a *kli sharet* capable of sanctifying things that are not the *lechem hapanim* it was designed for? Second — and this is where the *godesh* language becomes critical — even if the *shulchan* sanctifies what rests on its surface, does that sanctity extend to a mound of material rising above the surface, which has no enclosing walls around it? The conceptual parallel to the *gadush/machuk* debate about dry measures is precise: just as one asked whether the measuring vessel sanctifies the mounded flour that rises above its rim, so too one asks whether the *shulchan* sanctifies whatever rises above its flat surface.

### Mound Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדִּמְקַדֵּשׁ לֶחֶם, קְמָצִים נָמֵי מְקַדֵּשׁ, אוֹ דִלְמָא דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ מְקַדֵּשׁ, דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ לָא מְקַדֵּשׁ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara frames the alternatives: **From** the fact that the Table **consecrates** the **loaves** of shewbread placed upon it, it follows that it **also consecrates handfuls** placed upon it? **Or perhaps** the Table **consecrates** only that **which is fit for it,** i.e., the loaves, but it **does not consecrate** that **which is not fit for it,** such as the handfuls? Rav Ḥisda **said to him:** The Table **does not consecrate** these handfuls.

The *gemara* frames the alternatives: *midei demakadesh lechem kematzim nami makadesh* — since the *shulchan* sanctifies the *lechem hapanim*, perhaps it sanctifies these other items as well, since the bread itself sat directly on the flat surface with no enclosing walls around it. Or: *dechazi lei makadesh dela chazi lei lo makadesh* — perhaps the *shulchan* sanctifies only what it was designated for; frankincense placed directly on the surface, or a *kometz* placed there, is simply not what the *shulchan* was made to sanctify, and therefore no sanctification occurs.

*Rav Chisda* answers: *eino makadesh* — it does not sanctify them, presumably because the *shulchan*'s sanctifying power is limited to what it was designated for.

### Airspace Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר טִפְחַיִים וּמֶחֱצָה קוֹפֵל, נִמְצָא שֻׁלְחָן מְקַדֵּשׁ חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר טֶפַח לְמַעְלָה. לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר טִפְחַיִים קוֹפֵל, נִמְצָא שֻׁלְחָן מְקַדֵּשׁ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר טֶפַח לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama retorted: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** The shewbread loaves were longer than the length of the Table. Accordingly, it was necessary to fold the edges of the loaves in order that they would rest entirely upon the Table itself and not protrude past its edges. How much of each side needed to be folded up is subject to a tannaitic dispute concerning the length of the Table (see 96a). **According to the statement of the one who says** that **one folds two and a half handbreadths** from each side of the loaves, **it emerges** that the **Table consecrates fifteen handbreadths above it,** as there were six loaves on each side of the Table, which were each two and a half handbreadths high. And **according to the statement of the one who says** that **one folds two handbreadths** from each side of the loaves, **it emerges** that the **Table consecrates twelve handbreadths above it.** Rabbi Yoḥanan stated that the Table consecrates that which is placed above it, regardless of whether it is fit for the Table or not.

The *gemara* challenges this from a teaching of *rabbi yochanan*. *rabbi yochanan* said — and this connects to a later discussion about the dimensions of the *lechem hapanim* — that the *shulchan* sanctifies a vertical airspace above its surface. If the walls of each loaf of *lechem hapanim* were folded up two and a half *tefachim*, then with six loaves stacked in each column, the *shulchan* sanctifies up to fifteen *tefachim* above its surface. If the walls were two *tefachim*, it sanctifies up to twelve *tefachim* above its surface. The point is that *rabbi yochanan* is teaching something beyond the obvious — we all know the *shulchan* sanctifies the *lechem hapanim* itself. The teaching about height must be coming to tell us that something placed within that airspace — something other than the *lechem hapanim* — becomes sanctified. This seems to directly contradict *rav chisda*'s ruling that the *shulchan* does not sanctify items other than those it was designated for.

### Partial Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ לִיקְרַב, אֲבָל מְקַדֵּשׁ לִיפָּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him:** When I said that the Table does not consecrate the handfuls, I meant that it **does not consecrate** them **to** the extent that they can **be sacrificed** on the altar. **But** it certainly **consecrates** them **to** the extent that they will **be disqualified** by the same disqualifications that apply to **kodashim**, e.g., by being left overnight or by coming in contact with a ritually impure person who had immersed that day.

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *eino makadesh lehikarev aval makadesh lehipasel*. The *shulchan* does not sanctify such items with the full sanctity that would make them fit to be offered on the *mizbe'ach*. It does not transform the *levonah* into something ready for the altar, nor does it complete the sanctification of a *kometz* for burning. However, it does impart a partial sanctity — enough that the item is now susceptible to the categories of *pesul* that apply to *kodashim*: it can become invalid through *tumah*, or through being taken outside its proper domain. This is the concept of *makadesh lehipasel* — sanctified to the extent that it can become disqualified — without being *makadesh lehikarev* — fully sanctified to the point of fitness for the *mizbe'ach*. This nuanced category of partial sanctification, in which an object acquires enough *kedusha* to be vulnerable to invalidation but not enough to be offered, is a concept the *gemara* has visited before and will return to again.
