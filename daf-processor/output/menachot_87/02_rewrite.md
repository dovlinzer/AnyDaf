# Menachot 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sweet Wine Validity

### Prohibitions

The *gemara* opens by citing the *mishnah*'s ruling: *ein mevi'in lo matok velo mevushal velo me'ushan* — one may not bring wine that is sweet, cooked, or smoky for the *nesachim*, and if one does bring such wine, it is invalid. This ruling is worth noting for a practical aside: there are those who hold that *yayin mevushal* may not be used for *kiddush* on the grounds that it is not *ra'uy agabei mizbe'ach* — not fit for use on the *mizbe'ach*. That position is not the widespread practice, but it reflects the real halachic weight of these Temple-based categories.

### Heliston Contradiction

A tension immediately presents itself. The earlier clause of the *mishnah* stated: *ein mevi'in et ha-Heliston* — one does not bring *heliston*, which is understood to be a particularly sweet type of wine — *ve'im hevi kasher*, yet if one did bring it, it is valid *b'di'avad*. How does this square with the subsequent ruling that sweet wine is invalid even *b'di'avad*?

Ravina resolves the contradiction with the principle of *karch u-teni* — read the two clauses together, weaving them into a unified ruling. *rashi* understands this to mean that the word *kasher* is effectively removed from the earlier clause, and sweet wine is invalid in all cases. *tosafot*, however, reads it in the opposite direction: the ruling on sweet wine is reinterpreted so that *b'di'avad* it is in fact valid. Either way, the contradiction is resolved by harmonizing the text — the direction of the harmonization is what remains debated.

### Rav Ashi Analysis

Rav Ashi offers an independent resolution that turns not on textual harmonization but on a substantive distinction in the nature of the sweetness itself. *chuya deshimshe lo ma'is* — sweetness that results from overexposure to the sun is not considered disgusting or wholly inappropriate. *chuya de-pere ma'is* — sweetness that is intrinsic to the grape variety itself is regarded as an inappropriate quality in sacrificial wine. The underlying principle, though no explicit *pesukim* are cited here, appears to be the broader concept of *hakrivehu na le-pechatecha* — would you offer this to a person of high standing? Wine whose character is fundamentally unsuited to refined consumption falls outside the acceptable range for the *mizbe'ach*, regardless of how it got that way. If, however, the sweetness is merely an artifact of sun conditions and not native to the grape, the wine does not cross that threshold.

## Old Wine Debate

### Rabbi vs. Rabbis

The *mishnah* rules: *ein mevi'in yashan divrei Rebbi* — one does not bring old wine; this is the position of *rebbi*. The *chakhamim*, by contrast, are *makhshirin* — they permit it outright. The instinct here might be to wonder why older wine would be problematic at all, given that wine often improves with age. The question is not one of quality in the conventional sense, but whether there is a specific requirement that the wine be of the current year.

### Lamb-Wine Analogy

*Chizkiya* offers the rationale behind *rebbi*'s position. The *pasuk* juxtaposes the wine offering with the lamb — *lakeves yayin* — and since a *keves* must be *ben shnato*, one year old, for the same linguistic juxtaposition teaches that the wine too must be *ben shnato*, of that year. The *gemara* immediately presses this analogy: just as a lamb that is two years old is invalid as a *keves* (having become an *ayil*), should we not say that wine more than one year old is also invalid *b'di'avad*? *rebbi* never went that far. Moreover, a *braita* states explicitly: *yayin ben shtei shanim lo yavi* — one should not bring two-year-old wine, *v'im heivi kasher* — but if one did, it is valid. Since *rebbi* is the only authority who holds *l'chat'chila* that old wine is problematic, this *kasher b'di'avad* must reflect even his own position. The lamb-wine analogy therefore cannot be the real basis for *rebbi*'s view, since that analogy, taken seriously, would generate a *b'di'avad* concern that the sources plainly reject.

### Rava's Redness Basis

*Rava* therefore offers an alternative foundation for *rebbi*'s position. The *pasuk* states: *al tere yayin ki yitadam* — do not be drawn to wine because it is red — which implies that redness is what makes wine most attractive and desirable. The preference for red wine is well established, and finds expression in the practice of *leil ha-seder* as well. Apparently, wine in its first year retains its redness most vividly, and *rebbi* therefore holds that *l'chat'chila* one should bring wine of the current year. This raises a genuinely interesting conceptual question: when presenting something on the *mizbe'ach*, is the relevant standard one of taste or of appearance? The *torah* describes the *mizbe'ach* as consuming *lechem isheh* — food of fire — which might suggest taste is paramount. Yet since we certainly do not imagine that God consumes the offering in any literal sense, perhaps the symbolic and visual dimension is what matters most, and the standard would then be one of appearance. *rava*'s derivation seems to favor that latter position — *rebbi* grounds his *l'chat'chila* preference in the visual quality of wine's redness, not in its taste.

## Wine Source & Cultivation

### Trellis vs. Ground

The *mishnah* further specifies that one does not bring wine from *daliyot* — grapevines grown on trellises — but rather from *ragliyot*, vines that grow along the ground. Beyond that, the wine should come specifically from *kramim ha-avudim* — worked, cultivated vineyards, not wild or neglected ones, where the ground has been dug and tended.

### Rav Yosef Example

A *baraita* teaches that *kramim avudim* are those that are worked twice a year. The *gemara* illustrates this with a striking practical example: Rav Yosef had a corner of a vineyard, *karna d'pardisa*, in which he performed an extra digging — more than what was standard. The result was a wine, *chamra d'dari maya chad b'trei*, so potent that it could be diluted at twice the normal ratio. Typically wine was mixed with water at a three-to-one ratio; this wine was strong enough to be diluted six-to-one. The story concretely demonstrates how extra cultivation of the soil produces grapes of superior quality and concentration.

### Wine Storage

The *mishnah* further rules that the wine would not be stored in large barrels but in medium-sized jars — *chaviyot*, *kadiyot*, *ludiyot* — with the *baraita* specifying jars of the *lod* variety. Additionally, *ein manichin oto shnayim shnayim* — the jars would not be stored two together in the same place. *ela echad echad* — each jar was kept separately, so that if one were to go bad it would not contaminate its neighbor and compromise the quality of the wine.

## Wine Quality Control

### Gizbar Inspection

Returning to the *mishnah*: *keitzad bodek ha-gizbar* — how does the *gizbar* inspect the wine to ensure its quality? He would sit holding a testing rod — *yoshev v'kaneh b'yado*. According to *rashi*'s reading, when the spigot was opened and white dross emerged near the top, the *gizbar* would bang the rod to signal that the spigot should be closed. A *tana* elaborates: *zarak ha-gir shel shemarim* — if the lees and white dross came spurting out, the *gizbar* would bang his rod to signal rejection.

### Silent Rejection

One might ask: why does the *gizbar* not simply say aloud that the wine is unacceptable? Why does he reject it wordlessly? The *gemara* answers that this practice supports the teaching of *rabbi yochanan*.

### Speech Effect

*Rabbi Yochanan* taught: *keshem shehadibur yafeh labsamin* — just as speech is beneficial when working with spices (in the preparation of the *ketoret*, speaking certain words enhances the quality of the beating and blending), *kach dibur ra l'yayin* — so too speech is harmful to wine. When wine is in a sensitive state, speaking near it negatively affects its quality. The *gizbar* therefore communicates by gesture rather than voice, so as not to damage the very wine he is inspecting.

## Flour Impurities

### Impurity Ruling

The *mishnah* concludes with the ruling of *rabbi yossi bar rabbi yehuda*: if the wine contains *kemachim* — flour-like impurities and similar adulterants — it is invalid. His source is the *pasuk*: *temimim yihyu lachem uminchatam veniskeihem kamishpat* — the sacrifices must be *temimim*, unblemished, and the *mincha* and the *nesachim* are included in that requirement of wholeness.

### Mum Query

*Rabbi Yochanan* raises a question that he poses in several analogous contexts throughout the *gemara*: does the concept of *mum* — blemish — extend beyond animals to non-animal offerings? The question is specifically: if one sanctifies this wine — wine containing these flour-like impurities — does one incur lashes for having sanctified a *ba'al mum*? The prohibition of sanctifying a blemished animal carries *malkot*; does that *lav* extend to non-animal sacrificial items?

The argument for extending the prohibition is straightforward: *keivan d'pasul, ba'al mum dami* — since it is invalid, it is treated like a *ba'al mum*. And in this specific case there is particular force to the argument, since the *pasuk* of *temimim* is explicitly applied. The counterargument is: *ein ba'al mum ela b'beheimah* — the concept of *mum* and the accompanying *lav* apply only to animals. The *gemara* here, as in the parallel discussions, concludes with *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

## Animal Quality

### Geographic Sources

Having completed the *mishnah* and its *gemara*, a *baraita* now rounds out the picture of where the best sacrificial materials were sourced. The Sages taught: *eilim miMoav* — the finest rams came from Moav; *kevasim miHevron* — the best lambs from Chevron; *agalim miSharon* — the finest calves from the Sharon; *gozalot meHar hamelech* — the best birds from Har Hamelech.

### Wide-Backed Lambs

*Rabbi Yehuda* adds a qualitative specification: *mevi'im kevasim shegavham kerachbam* — one should bring lambs whose backs are as wide as they are high, or in the variant reading, *shegavan rechovim*, lambs with notably wide backs — large, broad-backed animals.

### Scripture Basis

*Rava bar Rav Shila* identifies the scriptural source for *rabbi yehuda*'s preference. The *pasuk* states: *yireh miknecha bayom hahu kar nirchav* — your cattle shall graze on that day in a wide pasture. The word *kar* can denote both a lamb and something wide and expansive — *kar nirchav* can also mean a broad cushion or wide support. *rabbi yehuda* thus reads into the term *kar* a preference for lambs that are themselves *nirchav*, wide and broad.

This discussion reinforces the earlier question about whether sacrificial standards are governed by taste or by appearance. The emphasis here is clearly on appearance — on the visual impressiveness and aesthetic appeal of the animal — rather than on any quality that would bear on taste. The focus on appearances in this context seems to be the animating principle across these rulings.

## Watchers Coda

### Watchers' Plea

The *perek* of *kol korbanot tzibur* closes not with a strictly halachic passage but with a moving *aggadeta*, introduced by a quotation from *rava bar rav shila* and naturally arising from the broader lens the *gemara* has adopted — looking not just at individual *korbanot* in their technical details but at the entire living reality of the *beit haMikdash*: where the wine came from, where the flour came from, where the oil came from, how these materials were cultivated and prepared. From that broader perspective the *gemara* brings a verse from *yeshayahu*: *al chomosayich Yerushalayim hifkadeti shomrim kol hayom v'chol halayla tamid lo yecheshu hamazkirim et Hashem al dami lachem* — on your walls, O Jerusalem, I have appointed watchers; all day and all night they shall never be silent; those who call upon God, do not be silent. These are the watchmen stationed around the ruins of Jerusalem after the *churban*, constantly speaking, evoking the memory of the *mikdash*. What would these watchers say? *rava bar rav shila* teaches: *ata takum terachem tziyon* — may You, God, arise and have compassion upon Zion, rebuild the *beit haMikdash*. *rav nachman bar yitzchak* adds: *bonei yerushalayim hashem* — God will rebuild Jerusalem.

### Original Statement

And what did these same watchers say originally, when the *beit haMikdash* still stood? *rava bar rav shila* teaches again: *ki bachar hashem b'tziyon ivah l'moshav lo* — for God has chosen Zion; He desired it to be His dwelling place.

With that, the *gemara* declares *hadran alach kol korbanot tzibur* — the conclusion of the *perek*.

## Dry Measures

### Two Measures

The new *perek* opens with the *mishnah*: *shtei midot shel yavesh hayu b'mikdash* — there were two dry measures in the *beit haMikdash*. These were used for measuring flour and are thus directly relevant to *menachot*: an *isaron* and a *chatzi isaron* — a tenth-measure and a half-tenth-measure. The *isaron* is the standard unit for a *mincha*. Larger *menachot* exist — the *minchat nesachim* for a lamb requires one *isaron*, for a ram two, for an ox (*par*) three — but all measurement was done in individual *isaron* increments. Crucially, the Temple did not maintain a two-*isaron* or three-*isaron* vessel; one would simply use the single *isaron* measuring cup multiple times. The *chatzi isaron*, as the *gemara* will explain, was used specifically for the *chavitay Kohen Gadol* — the daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*, which consisted of one *isaron* divided equally between morning and afternoon.

### Meir vs. Sages

*Rabbi Meir* holds: *isaron isaron v'chatzi isaron* — there were in fact two types of *isaron* vessels plus the *chatzi isaron*, making three measuring vessels in total. Since the two *isaron* vessels must have served distinct purposes, they cannot have been identical in size; they were presumably of slightly different measurement. *the sages* maintain that there was only one type of *isaron* vessel, plus the *chatzi isaron*, making two in total.

---

## Heaped vs. Leveled

### Double Language

A *baraita* cites *rabbi meir*'s derivation. The *torah* in *parshat pinchas*, listing the festival *korbanot*, uses the doubled formulation *isaron isaron lakeves ha'echad* — one *isaron*, one *isaron* — for each of the seven lambs. Why the repetition? *melamed sheshtei isronot hayu bamikdash* — it teaches that there were two *isaron* vessels in the *beit haMikdash*: *echad gadush ve'echad machuk* — one was heaped (*gadush*) and one was leveled (*machuk*). The distinction is familiar from everyday usage: a heaping measure versus a level measure. The *gadush* vessel was slightly smaller in physical capacity, such that when flour was allowed to mound above the rim, the total quantity equaled one *isaron*. The *machuk* vessel was precisely one *isaron* when leveled flat to the rim — and therefore physically larger as a container.

### Usage by Type

*Rabbi Meir* teaches that the *gadush* vessel — the heaping measure — was used *shebo hayu modedin lechol haminchos*, for measuring all the standard *menachot*. The implication may be that for those offerings a small degree of imprecision was tolerable; the mound atop the rim could conceivably add a slight extra amount, but that was acceptable. The *machuk* vessel — the precisely leveled measure — *shebo hayu modedin lechavitei Kohen Gadol*, was used specifically for the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha*. *rashi* explains the reasoning: when you level off the top of the vessel, you can pour and divide the contents into two equal halves without any spillage from a mound. Precision in division demands a level starting point. One might add that the very fact of dividing into exact halves calls for a more exacting standard of measurement throughout.

---

## Sages' Position

### Single Type Argument

The Sages counter *rabbi meir*'s position by citing a different verse: *ve'isaron echad lakeves ha'echad* — one *isaron* for one lamb. The phrase *isaron echad*, "one *isaron*," teaches that there was only one type of *isaron* vessel in the *beit haMikdash*, not two.

### Half-Isaron Derivation

If so, what does the Sages' position do with the doubled language of *isaron isaron* from which *rabbi meir* derived two types of *isaron*? *lirbot chatzi isaron* — the repetition comes to include the *chatzi isaron*, teaching that there is a variant on the *isaron* in the form of the half-measure, but not that there are two distinct full-*isaron* vessels. And *rabbi meir* — where does he derive the *chatzi isaron*? From the *vav* of *ve'isaron echad lakeves ha'echad* — the conjunctive *vav* indicates something additional to the standard *isaron*, which he reads as the *chatzi isaron*. The Sages, however, do not derive anything from that *vav*, and so they take the *chatzi isaron* from the doubled *isaron isaron* instead.

A *baraita* cited by *rabbi yossi* further illuminates this: the *torah* places a scribal dot — a *nekuda* — over the *vav* of the first *isaron* in the *succot* readings, on the first day of *yom tov*. The purpose is to teach *she'lo yimdod lo beshel gimel lapar v'lo beshel shnayim la'ayil* — that even for an ox, which requires three *isronot*, and for a ram, which requires two, one must never use a larger vessel but must use only the single *isaron* measure repeatedly. The dotted *vav*, in effect, emphasizes: only the *isaron*, singular.

### No Large Measures

For *rabbi meir*, the *nekuda* teaches nothing — he does not derive *halacha* from scribal dots — and he learns the prohibition against multi-*isaron* vessels from the verse *isaron echad lakeves ha'echad*, which the Sages instead read as teaching a single type of *isaron*.

## HP Mincha Division

### Measure Clarified

An apparent tension arises from the *mishnah*'s statement that one would *moded* — measure — with the *chatzi isaron* for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*: half in the morning, half in the afternoon. Yet a separate *braita* rules explicitly: *chavitei Kohen Gadol lo hayu ba'im chatzaim* — one does not bring the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha* in separate halves. Rather, *ela meivi isaron shalem v'chotzeihu* — one brings a full *isaron* and then divides it in two. This means the primary measurement was done with a full *isaron*, not a *chatzi isaron*. In what sense, then, was the *chatzi isaron* used for *measuring*?

*Rav Sheshet* resolves this: *mai moded nami de'katani mechaleik* — when the *mishnah* says the *chatzi isaron* was used for *moding*, it means it was used for *dividing* — specifically, for dividing the already-measured full *isaron* into its two equal halves. The *chatzi isaron* was not the primary measuring instrument but the tool by which the full *isaron* was apportioned correctly.

## Heaping Logic

### Rami's Question

*Rami bar Chama* posed a question to *rav chisda*: for *rabbi meir*, who holds that there were two types of *isaron* vessels — one *gadush* and one *machuk* — what was the *chatzi isaron*? Was it a heaped measure or a leveled measure?

The *gemara* immediately broadens the question: and why ask only about *rabbi meir*? For the *rabbanan*, too, we want to know what their single *isaron* vessel was — heaped or leveled?

### All Measures Leveled

*Rav Chisda* responds that from *rabbi meir*'s position one can derive the answer for *rabbi meir* regarding his *chatzi isaron*, and from *rabbi meir* one can also derive the answer for the *rabbanan*. Here is the logical chain: since *rabbi meir* explicitly states that the *machuk* — leveled — *isaron* was used for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, and since the *chatzi isaron* was also used for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, it follows that the *chatzi isaron* for *rabbi meir* was likewise *machuk*. The logic is reinforced by the practical consideration: if you need to divide an *isaron* into two precise halves, working with a leveled measurement affords far greater precision than working with a mound.

From this one can derive the *rabbanan*'s position as well. The *rabbanan* never disagreed with *rabbi meir* about the *chatzi isaron* — their dispute was only about whether there were two types of full *isaron* vessels. Since both agree the *chatzi isaron* was *machuk*, and since the *rabbanan* use their single *isaron* for everything including the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, and since that *isaron* must accommodate precise halving just as the *chatzi isaron* must, one can conclude that the *rabbanan*'s single *isaron* was also *machuk*. The result is that across all opinions, all Temple dry measures were leveled — a conclusion that makes sense on its own terms, since greater precision is served by a level measure, and the requirement of exact halving makes imprecision particularly problematic.

## Dough Division

### By Hand Method

*Rami bar Chama* asked *rav chisda* a further question: when dividing the dough for the *chavitei Kohen Gadol* — taking each half-*isaron* of flour and forming six individual *challot* from it — *bameh mechalkan lachallot*? How does one divide the dough into sixths? *beyad*, by hand and approximation? Or *bichli*, using some kind of precise measuring vessel? *rashi* understands the question as arising after the flour has been mixed and one is working with actual dough; *tosafot* understands it as a question about dividing the dry flour into sixths before mixing.

### Against Scales

*Rav Chisda* responds: it is obvious that the division is done *beyad*, by hand. The reason is captured in the phrase *torat aniyah tachnis* — do you want to bring in the *torah* of poverty, the symbolism of scarcity? To measure out flour or dough in such small increments as a twelfth of an *isaron*, one would need a precision scale. That kind of weighing is associated with *dochak* — deprivation and shortage. The *pasuk* in the curses of *vayikra* warns: *veheishivu lachmechem bemishkal* — they will weigh out your bread — meaning bread rationed by weight in times of famine. To measure dough on a scale is to evoke that curse. *rashi* applies this directly to the dough context. *tosafot* extends it even to the flour: using a precision scale in the *mikdash* to measure tiny amounts is itself inappropriate, since the *mikdash* should project abundance rather than scarcity.

## Shulchan Sanctity

### Sanctification Q

*Rami bar Chama* posed yet another question to *rav chisda*: *shulchan mahu sheyekadesh kematzim begodesh shelo* — can the *shulchan* in the *beit haMikdash* sanctify things placed upon it other than the *lechem hapanim*, specifically in the mound (*godesh*) above its surface? *rashi* understands *kematzim* as referring to the *levonah* — the frankincense offered as part of the *lechem hapanim* service — which, if placed directly on the *shulchan* surface rather than in the *bezychin* (the small spoons designated for it), would raise the question of whether the *shulchan* sanctifies it. *tosafot* understands the question as referring to a *kometz* of a *mincha*: ordinarily one places a *kometz* in a *kli sharet* to sanctify it before bringing it to the *mizbe'ach*; what if instead it were placed on the *shulchan*?

Either way, the question has two dimensions. First, does the *shulchan* function as a *kli sharet* capable of sanctifying things that are not the *lechem hapanim* it was designed for? Second — and this is where the *godesh* language becomes critical — even if the *shulchan* sanctifies what rests on its surface, does that sanctity extend to a mound of material rising above the surface, which has no enclosing walls around it? The conceptual parallel to the *gadush/machuk* debate about dry measures is precise: just as one asked whether the measuring vessel sanctifies the mounded flour that rises above its rim, so too one asks whether the *shulchan* sanctifies whatever rises above its flat surface.

### Mound Sanctification

The *gemara* frames the alternatives: *midei demakadesh lechem kematzim nami makadesh* — since the *shulchan* sanctifies the *lechem hapanim*, perhaps it sanctifies these other items as well, since the bread itself sat directly on the flat surface with no enclosing walls around it. Or: *dechazi lei makadesh dela chazi lei lo makadesh* — perhaps the *shulchan* sanctifies only what it was designated for; frankincense placed directly on the surface, or a *kometz* placed there, is simply not what the *shulchan* was made to sanctify, and therefore no sanctification occurs.

*Rav Chisda* answers: *eino makadesh* — it does not sanctify them, presumably because the *shulchan*'s sanctifying power is limited to what it was designated for.

### Airspace Height

The *gemara* challenges this from a teaching of *rabbi yochanan*. *rabbi yochanan* said — and this connects to a later discussion about the dimensions of the *lechem hapanim* — that the *shulchan* sanctifies a vertical airspace above its surface. If the walls of each loaf of *lechem hapanim* were folded up two and a half *tefachim*, then with six loaves stacked in each column, the *shulchan* sanctifies up to fifteen *tefachim* above its surface. If the walls were two *tefachim*, it sanctifies up to twelve *tefachim* above its surface. The point is that *rabbi yochanan* is teaching something beyond the obvious — we all know the *shulchan* sanctifies the *lechem hapanim* itself. The teaching about height must be coming to tell us that something placed within that airspace — something other than the *lechem hapanim* — becomes sanctified. This seems to directly contradict *rav chisda*'s ruling that the *shulchan* does not sanctify items other than those it was designated for.

### Partial Sanctity

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *eino makadesh lehikarev aval makadesh lehipasel*. The *shulchan* does not sanctify such items with the full sanctity that would make them fit to be offered on the *mizbe'ach*. It does not transform the *levonah* into something ready for the altar, nor does it complete the sanctification of a *kometz* for burning. However, it does impart a partial sanctity — enough that the item is now susceptible to the categories of *pesul* that apply to *kodashim*: it can become invalid through *tumah*, or through being taken outside its proper domain. This is the concept of *makadesh lehipasel* — sanctified to the extent that it can become disqualified — without being *makadesh lehikarev* — fully sanctified to the point of fitness for the *mizbe'ach*. This nuanced category of partial sanctification, in which an object acquires enough *kedusha* to be vulnerable to invalidation but not enough to be offered, is a concept the *gemara* has visited before and will return to again.
