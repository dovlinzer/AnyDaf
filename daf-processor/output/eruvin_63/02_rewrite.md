# Eruvin 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ruling b'Makom Rav

### Non-Jewish House Debate

The daf begins against the backdrop of a dispute in the *Mishnah* concerning whether a non-Jewish house situated in a *chatzer* creates a halachic problem for *eruvin*. According to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, such a house is only problematic when there are two Jews in the *chatzer*; a single Jew presents no difficulty. The *Gemara* rules in accordance with his position, and this ruling immediately raises a sensitive question: may a *talmid* issue this ruling *b'makom rav* — in the presence of, or within the jurisdiction of, his teacher? The *Gemara*'s instinct is that even an apparently obvious *psak* creates a problem. Even something as clear-cut as an egg cooked in milk, or a ruling recorded in *Megillat Taanit*, cannot be decided by a student in his teacher's presence. The prohibition, it seems, is categorical.

### Tosafot's Qualifications

Tosafot, however, introduces three significant qualifications that soften this categorical picture. The first is the case of *afrushe mei'issura*: if someone is about to commit a prohibition and is asking whether something is *assur*, the student must answer. Tosafot understands that even framed as a *shaila*, if the answer is *assur*, responding falls within the obligation to prevent another from sinning — a category the *Gemara* will address explicitly in a later passage. The second qualification is that purely theoretical Torah discussion is permitted; only issuing a practical ruling to a practical question is restricted. The third, and perhaps most consequential, qualification concerns cases where the ruling genuinely requires no judgment whatsoever — where the answer is truly self-evident and involves no real weighing of factors. Tosafot suggests that in such cases there is no prohibition, though this sits in some tension with the *Gemara*'s apparent absolutism.

### Perception & Kavod

What, then, is the underlying concern? Tosafot makes clear that the prohibition is fundamentally about *kavod ha-rav* — respect for one's teacher — and that perception plays a central role. Reading from the relevant passage in Tosafot on 62b, four lines from the bottom of the long *tosafot* beginning *v'amar Rav Chisda*: *v'amar Ri d'davka b'davar shenirei chiddush l'shoel* — the Ri explains that the issue is specifically when the ruling appears to the questioner as a *chiddush*, as something novel and authoritative. Even *Megillat Taanit*, though written down, is not known to everyone, and someone who does not know the *Shulchan Aruch* may experience even a well-established ruling as a *chiddush* when a student pronounces it in the teacher's presence.

The key line follows: *v'lo naktinu ela l'ashmi'inan d'afillu b'hanei shehatalmid be'atzmo yodea she'ino toeh* — even in a case where the student himself knows with complete certainty that he is not making an error, it is still *assur l'horos* if the questioner perceives it as a *chiddush*. The prohibition, in other words, is not merely about the epistemological status of the ruling — it is about whether the act of ruling carries the appearance of a student assuming authority in his teacher's presence. That perception itself constitutes a breach of *kavod ha-rav*.

### Tosafot Examples

Tosafot continues: *aval davar shehashoel yodea sheyadu'a minhago shel davar* — but something that even the questioner himself essentially already knows, such as *nosein ta'am lifgam*, is different. When a person asks about a pot that hasn't been used for days and is really just seeking reassurance rather than a genuine ruling, and the answer is not experienced as a *chiddush* when pronounced — *k'shomoreh lo heter ein zeh karui davar chiddush* — that is permitted. Tosafot thus draws the line not around the difficulty of the question per se, but around whether the act of answering will be experienced as an exercise of independent rabbinic authority. The examples of *megillat taanit* and *beita b'chutcha* are understood not as cases of genuinely trivial rulings but as cases where, for at least some questioners, the ruling carries the weight of a novel determination.

## Historical Cases

### Rav Chisda & Hamnuna

The *Gemara* at the bottom of 62b and the top of 63a records a series of historical cases that illuminate the practical scope of this prohibition. Rav Chisda issued rulings in Kafri during the lifetime of Rav Huna, his teacher — but Kafri was a different city. Then Rav Hamnuna issued rulings in Charsa de'Arga during the lifetime of Rav Chisda. The symmetry is pointed: what Rav Chisda did to his teacher, Rav Hamnuna did to him. This raises the question of what geographic scope the prohibition covers. Is it limited to the same room, the same city, or does it extend across an entire country?

### Ravina's Knife

The question becomes sharper with Ravina. The *Gemara* reports that *Ravina sar skina be'Bavel* — Ravina inspected knives in Bavel — during the lifetime of Rav Ashi, who was also in Bavel, though not in the same city. Rav Ashi challenged him: *mai ta'ama avid mar hachi* — why are you doing this? I am still alive.

It is important to understand what knife inspection (*sar skina*) represented. When a new rabbinic figure arrived in a city, the *shochtim* would present their knives for inspection. This was not primarily about the technical halachic question of whether the blade was acceptable; it was a symbolic gesture that declared who held rabbinic authority over the community's *kashrut*. The *Gemara* later makes this explicit: *lo amru le'harot sakin le'chacham ela mipnei kvodo shel chacham* — the reason for showing a knife to a sage is precisely on account of the sage's honor, as a demonstration of authority. This is why the act raised the issue of *kavod ha-rav* even more starkly than a routine ruling might: it was a public claim to institutional authority, not merely an answer to a *shaila*.

### Rav-Talmid Chaver

Ravina defended himself by citing the case of Rav Hamnuna ruling in a different city during Rav Chisda's lifetime. Rav Ashi corrected him: two versions of that story circulate — one in which Rav Hamnuna ruled and one in which he did not. In fact, both versions are accurate and refer to different periods. When Rav Huna was alive, Rav Hamnuna did not rule even in another city, because Rav Huna was his full *rav*. After Rav Huna died, Rav Hamnuna did rule in Rav Chisda's lifetime — *de'talmid chaver dilei hava* — because by that point his relationship with Rav Chisda was that of a *talmid chaver*, a student-colleague, not a full subordinate student. As a *talmid chaver*, he had greater latitude, at least when operating in a different city.

Ravina then applied this logic to himself: *ve'ana nami talmid chaver de'mar ana* — I too am your student-colleague. Since their relationship had matured beyond the strict *rav-talmid* dynamic, the latitude afforded a *talmid chaver* should apply to him as well, permitting the knife inspection at least when conducted in another city.

### Authority Gesture

The *Gemara* then confirms what was implicit throughout: the act of showing a knife to a sage was never about a *psak shaila* in any technical sense — it was entirely about the gesture of rabbinic authority. This is why Ravina's act raised the problem in the first place and why the discussion turns on the nature of the relationship between the parties rather than on any halachic complexity of the knife itself.

## Modern Framework

### Shulchan Aruch

The *Gemara*'s treatment of this topic finds its halachic codification in the *Shulchan Aruch* under *Hilchot Kavod Rav* in *Yoreh De'ah*. The *Shulchan Aruch* states plainly: *ve'assur le'adam le'horot lifnei rabbo* — it is forbidden to issue rulings in one's teacher's presence. The prohibition extends beyond the teacher's literal physical proximity: *aval likvo'a atzmo le'hora'ah lashevet le'horot afilu besof ha'olam assur le'horot ad sheyamut rabbo o ad sheyiten lo reshut* — even to establish oneself as a decisor anywhere in the world is forbidden until the teacher dies or explicitly grants permission. That permission — *reshut* — is a substantive halachic concept, not merely a courtesy.

### Semicha

This brings into focus the halachic function of *semicha* in the modern period. Contemporary *semicha* serves two distinct roles. The first is sociological: it functions as a form of licensing, informing the community that this individual has demonstrated the knowledge and competence to render halachic decisions and can be relied upon. The second, and halachically operative, role is that it constitutes a *reshut hora'ah b'makom rabbo* — the teacher's explicit authorization for the student to rule even in his presence. The *Shulchan Aruch*'s formulation *o ad sheyiten lo reshut* encompasses precisely this: the *yoreh yoreh* that appears on a *semicha* certificate is not merely an honorific but a formal grant of halachic permission to rule even where the teacher is present. Without such permission, absent the teacher's death, issuing rulings would constitute a violation of this prohibition.

### Disagreement

One might ask how this framework coexists with the give-and-take of *Torah she-be'al peh*, in which students and later authorities regularly dispute and disagree with their predecessors and teachers. The *Rema* addresses this directly, noting that *mutar lachlok alav be'eizeh psak o hora'ah im yesh lo re'ayot ve'hochachot le'devarav she'hadin imo* — one may dispute even a teacher's ruling if one can bring evidence and proof that the law is otherwise.

The *Pitchei Teshuva* on *siman* 242 takes up a related concern. He cites the *Teshuvat Chut HaShani*, in which a complaint was lodged against an authority for disagreeing with earlier *poskim* — and worse, for suggesting that those *poskim* had overlooked a *Gemara*. The *Chut HaShani* responds that there is nothing objectionable about this: *she-kol dor va-dor lo nimnu le'hasig al gedolim she-lifneihem* — every generation has always argued with and critiqued the authorities that preceded it, and this is the very nature of *Torah she-be'al peh*. Indeed, framing a disagreement as "perhaps they did not have access to this *Gemara*" is itself a form of respect — it attributes the divergence to circumstance rather than error. The distinction the *Pitchei Teshuva* preserves is between the free discourse of *Torah she-be'al peh*, which is unrestricted, and the specific act of assuming practical decisory authority in one's teacher's presence, which the prohibition addresses.

## Ravina & Innkeeper

### Ravina Declines

The *Gemara* presents a further case involving knife inspection. Rava had taught: *tzurba me'rabanan chazi l'nafshei* — a *talmid chacham*, even a student, may inspect his own knife before slaughtering his own meat. This is categorically different from the symbolic act of inspecting a *shochet*'s knife as an assertion of communal authority; inspecting one's own knife for one's own purposes is simply managing one's own *halachic* life and carries none of the authority-laden implications of the formal inspection ritual.

Ravina came to Mechuza — Rava's city, where Rava was the recognized rabbinic authority. His host, an innkeeper, brought him a knife before slaughtering an animal, showing it to him for inspection. Ravina declined: *zil amteih l'Rava* — go bring it to Rava. This is Rava's city and Rava is the authority here.

### Pre-Purchase Issue

The innkeeper pressed back, citing Rava's own ruling that a *talmid chacham* may inspect for himself. Ravina's response was precise: *ana mizban zavina* — I am going to be purchasing this meat from you. At the moment of inspection, the animal has not yet been slaughtered and the meat is not yet his. Because he would only acquire it after the fact, inspecting the knife now is not inspecting for himself but for someone else — it is an exercise of authority over the *shechita* of meat that is not yet his. The permission to inspect for oneself does not extend to this case.

## Two Visiting Rabbis

### Respect for Elder

The *Gemara* presents another scenario involving knife inspection that turns on a more subtle point. Rabbi Eliezer mi-Gronya and Rav Abba bar Tachlifa came as guests to the home of Rav Acha brei d'Rav Ika, in the locale where Rav Acha bar Yaakov — a different figure — was the established halachic authority. The host wished to prepare a fine calf (*igla tilsa*) for his guests and brought out a knife, showing it to the two visiting rabbis for inspection. Rav Abba bar Tachlifa raised an objection: *lo lachush lei l'saba* — should one not show concern for the elder, meaning Rav Acha bar Yaakov, the local authority? Ought the knife not be brought to him, as the one whose rabbinic jurisdiction covers this locale?

### Eliezer's Citation

Rabbi Eliezer mi-Gronya dismissed the concern. He cited Rava's ruling — *tzurba me'rabanan chazi l'nafshei* — and reasoned that since the two visiting rabbis were going to eat this meat, they were effectively inspecting for themselves as guests who would be consuming it.

### Punishment & Resolution

The *Gemara* then reports: *chazi v'ianash* — they inspected the knife, and Rabbi Eliezer mi-Gronya was punished. Something befell him, understood as divine retribution for proceeding with the inspection. The *Gemara* challenges this outcome: had not Rava himself taught that a *talmid chacham* may inspect for himself? The resolution is pointed: *shani hasam d'atchilu bichvoda* — the case is different here because they had already introduced the question of showing it to the local authority. Once Rav Abba bar Tachlifa raised the idea of deferring to Rav Acha bar Yaakov, the matter of his honor was on the table. To then dismiss that consideration — to say "never mind, we'll go ahead anyway" — transformed the act from a neutral inspection into an affirmative rejection of the elder's honor. Had the question never been raised, proceeding would have been unproblematic. But once it was raised and then set aside, the inspection became an act of disrespect rather than a permissible exercise of self-interest.

### Rav Acha Muflag

An alternative explanation is offered: *shani Rav Acha bar Yaakov d'muflyag* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov was exceptional. Rashi explains *muflyag* as meaning he was *harbeh me'od zaken ve'chacham* — exceptionally old and wise, a *gadol* of singular stature. When the authority in question is of such exceptional distinction, the ordinary permission for a *talmid chacham* to inspect for himself may not apply; the standards of deference are correspondingly elevated.

## Afrushe Mei'Issura

### Rava's Teaching

The *Gemara* now records Rava's explicit ruling on the exception carved out for preventing sin: *l'afroshe mei'issura afilu bifnei rabbo shafir dami* — to stop someone from committing a prohibition, even in one's teacher's presence, is entirely permissible. This was already anticipated by Tosafot's first qualification. What the *Gemara* now adds is a formal, named principle: *afrushe mei'issura* is not merely a practical concession but a recognized category that overrides the ordinary restrictions of *b'makom rav*.

### Ravina's Niddui

The *Gemara* presents a dramatic case. Ravina was sitting before Rav Ashi — in his immediate presence — when he observed a man tying his donkey to a palm tree on *Shabbat*. Using a tree that is attached to the ground (*mechubar*) on *Shabbat* is prohibited. Ravina called out to the man to stop; the man ignored him. Ravina then declared: *lehavyei hai gavra b'shamsa* — this man should be placed in *niddui*. Having done so, Ravina turned to Rav Ashi and asked: *ki hai gavna mi chazai k'apikorsus* — does this seem like *apikorsus*? The term *apikorus*, as the *Gemara* in Sanhedrin explains in the famous *Mishnah* of those who have no share in the *olam ha-ba*, refers to one who is *m'vazeh talmid chacham* — who flagrantly demeans Torah authority. Ravina was asking whether his own act of imposing *niddui* in Rav Ashi's presence might itself constitute that kind of affront — an act of rabbinic authority exercised in the presence of the greater authority, and thus a form of disrespect.

### God's Honor Override

Rav Ashi's response draws on a principle from elsewhere in the *Gemara*: *ein chochma v'ein tevuna v'ein eitzah l'neged Hashem* — there is no wisdom, understanding, or counsel in the face of God. *Kol makom she-yeish bo chillul Hashem ein cholkin kavod l'rav* — wherever there is a desecration of God's name, one does not defer to the honor of the teacher. This ruling goes further than simple *afrushe mei'issura*. In this case, the man had already ignored Ravina's verbal intervention and was not being stopped. Imposing *niddui* was not about preventing the act — the act was proceeding — but about publicly defending the honor of God and of the Torah. To allow that act to pass without consequence, in that society, would have been understood as a failure to stand up for divine authority. In such a case, the teacher's honor is entirely displaced by the imperative to defend God's honor, and the student is not only permitted but obligated to act.

## Consequences

### Rava's Statement

Having established the nature and exceptions of the prohibition, the *Gemara* now turns to its consequences. Rava formulates the graded framework: one who rules *halacha* literally in the teacher's physical presence — *befanav* — deserves heavenly death. One who does so outside the teacher's physical presence — *shelo befanav*, meaning perhaps the same city or same country, depending on the analyses above — commits a prohibition, but is not deserving of death.

### Bnei Aharon

The *Gemara* immediately challenges the second part of this statement. The *Tanna* Rabbi Eliezer teaches: *lo mesu Bnei Aharon ad she-horu halacha bifnei Moshe Rabbeinu* — the sons of Aharon died specifically because they issued a ruling in the presence of Moshe, not merely because of the *eish zarah* they brought. The *drasha* is from the opening of *Tzav*, where the *pasuk* states that the sons of Aharon should place fire on the altar. They derived from this that despite the heavenly fire that descended when the *Mishkan* was inaugurated, it is still a *mitzva* to bring fire from an ordinary source. That act of independent legal derivation — *darash* — in Moshe's presence was the sin. The problem was not the fire itself but the assumption of decisory authority in the presence of the supreme authority. Tosafot notes a minor difficulty — those *pesukim* refer to the outer altar, while Nadav and Avihu brought the *ketoret* on the inner altar — but the *Gemara*'s point stands that the death was a consequence of ruling *befanav*.

### Eliezer's Student

A second proof follows. Rabbi Eliezer had a student who issued a halachic ruling in his presence. Rabbi Eliezer said to his wife Ima Shalom: *tamiah ani im yotzi zeh shenaso* — I would be astonished if this person lived out the year. And indeed he did not. When Ima Shalom marveled at what seemed like prophecy, Rabbi Eliezer replied: *lo navi anochi velo ben navi anochi* — I am no prophet. *Ela kach mekubalni* — I have received this as a tradition: *kol hamoreh halacha bifnei rabbo chayav mita* — whoever rules halacha in the presence of his teacher deserves death.

### Three Parsaos

The *Gemara* then cites Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: that student was Yehuda ben Guria, and he lived three *parsaot* from Rabbi Eliezer — twelve *mil* away. This seems to prove that even *shelo bifanav*, even at a meaningful distance, the punishment of death applies, which would undermine Rava's distinction.

The *Gemara* responds: *bifanav havay* — it was actually considered "in his presence." The three *parsaot* referred to where Yehuda ben Guria lived, not where he issued the ruling relative to Rabbi Eliezer. But why mention where he lived at all? *Shelo tomar mashal haya* — so that one does not dismiss this as a *mashal*, a parable rather than a real event. The biographical detail — his name, his father's name, his place of residence — establishes the historicity of the account. The *Gemara* therefore resolves the challenge: Rava's distinction between *befanav* and *shelo befanav* stands, and the consequence of heavenly death is limited to one who rules literally in the teacher's presence.

## Snake Bite & Sinner

### Snake Bite

Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan offers a parallel formulation: *kol hamoreh halacha lifnei rabbo ra'uy lehakhisho nachash* — whoever rules halacha before his teacher is deserving to be bitten by a snake. This echoes the gravity of *chayav mita* but expresses it through a somewhat different image.

### Biblical Verses

The *Gemara* derives this from *Iyov*, where Elihu ben Barkhel ha-Buzi says: *tza'ir ani l'yamim ve'atem yeshishim al ken zachalti* — I am young in years and you are elders, therefore I *zachalti* — held back. The word *zachalti* evokes *zochel*, to slither, as in a snake; and this is linked to the *pasuk* *im chamat zochelei afar* — with the venom of those who slither in the dust. One who does not restrain himself in the presence of his elders deserves precisely what he evoked by failing to *zachal*.

A further formulation, cited by Ze'iri in the name of Rabbi Chanina, tones the consequence down further: such a person is simply called a *chotei* — a sinner. The *pasuk* is from *Tehillim*: *belibi tzafanti imratecha l'ma'an lo echta lach* — I have stored your words in my heart so that I not sin against You. Holding back one's words in the presence of a greater authority is itself the fulfillment of this verse; speaking when one should remain silent is the sin.

### David & Ira

Rav Hamnuna observes a contradiction between two verses attributed to David. One verse says *belibi tzafanti imratecha* — I have held your words in my heart — suggesting David kept Torah wisdom to himself. Another says *bisarti tzedek b'kahal rav* — I have proclaimed your righteousness to a great congregation — suggesting David taught publicly and freely. The resolution: *lo kashya*. The first verse describes the period when Ira ha-Yairi was alive — Ira ha-Yairi, in the rabbinic reading, served as David's teacher — and during that time David refrained from teaching in his presence. The second verse describes the period after Ira ha-Yairi was no longer living, when David was free to teach. The case of David and Ira ha-Yairi thus provides a biblical paradigm for the entire discussion: the restriction applies only during the teacher's lifetime, and with the teacher's passing, the student's full authority is unlocked.

## Yehoshua

### Responding Lose Children

Rabbi Levi introduces a further and sharper consequence: *kol ha-mosif mila lifnei rabbo* — whoever adds a word in the presence of his teacher — meaning whoever responds to a question asked of the teacher, interjecting before the teacher has an opportunity to answer — *ozil li-she'ol be-lo banim* — goes to his grave without children. This is a severe consequence, more visceral in its nature than death by divine decree, and it signals that the problem of responding to another's question in the teacher's presence carries distinct weight.

### Numbers Verse

The *Gemara* derives this from the episode in *Bamidbar* where Yehoshua bin Nun responds to the report that Eldad and Medad are prophesying within the camp: *va-ya'an Yehoshua bin Nun mesharet Moshe mi-bechurav va-yomar adoni Moshe kela'em* — Yehoshua rose up and said, my master Moshe, restrain them. The irony is profound: Yehoshua was acting out of zeal for Moshe's honor, seeking to protect his teacher's unique prophetic authority. Yet the very act of interjecting his own response — of answering a situation that was presented to Moshe — was itself a breach of the deference he owed. The subsequent genealogical record lists: Nun, his son Yehoshua — and stops. Nowhere in *Tanach* do we find that Yehoshua had children.

### Jericho Siege

The *Gemara* notes that this conflicts with the view of Rabbi Abba bar Pappa, who provides an entirely different explanation for Yehoshua's childlessness: *lo ne'enash Yehoshua ela bishvil she-bitel et Yisrael laila achat mi-pirya ve-rivya* — Yehoshua was punished for having prevented the Jewish people from engaging in procreation for one night. This explanation is grounded in the episode at Jericho. The *pasuk* states: *va-yhi bi-hyot Yehoshua bi-Yericho* — when Yehoshua was at Jericho — and describes an encounter with an angelic figure standing with a drawn sword. Yehoshua asked: *ha-lanu ata im le-tzareinu* — are you with us or against us? The angel replied: *ani sar tzva Hashem ata bati* — I am the commander of God's army; I have come now.

The *Gemara* fills in the dialogue. The angel charged Yehoshua on two counts: the afternoon *tamid* sacrifice had not been brought the previous evening, and now, during the siege of Jericho, the people were neglecting *Talmud Torah*. Yehoshua asked which failure was more serious; the angel answered that it was the neglect of *Talmud Torah*. Tosafot notes the difficulty of knowing how the *Gemara* derives these details, and offers an interpretation based on the language of Yehoshua's original question — *ha-lanu ata im le-tzareinu* — reading *ha-lanu* as an allusion to *Torah tziva lanu Moshe* and *le-tzareinu* as an allusion to the sacrifices that protect from *tzarot*. The angel's answer, *ata bati* — I have come now — signals that the current, pressing failure is *Talmud Torah*, not the sacrifice.

The *Gemara* then adds: *mi-yad va-yalen Yehoshua ba-laila ha-hu be-toch ha-emek* — and Yehoshua immediately went and spent that night in the depths of *halacha*, learning Torah, correcting the failure. Rabbi Yochanan comments: *melamed she-halach be-omka shel halacha* — this teaches that he immersed himself in the depths of *halacha*.

### Wrong Ark

Having presented the account of Yehoshua's neglect of *Talmud Torah* — which in its original form concludes there — the *Gemara* now grafts onto it the explanation for Yehoshua's childlessness invoked by Rabbi Abba bar Pappa. A tradition is cited: *de-gmiri de-chol zman she-aron u-shchina shruyim shelo bi-mkomam asurim be-tashmish ha-mita* — whenever the Ark and the divine presence were not in their proper place, marital relations were prohibited. Since Yehoshua brought the Ark to the Jericho campaign, the people were forbidden to engage in *tashmish ha-mita* during that period.

Tosafot addresses the difficulty: the Ark was regularly brought to war — we know this from David's era and the case of Uriah — and yet there is no general prohibition on marital relations during wartime campaigns. Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing between two Arks. After Moshe descended from Sinai and broke the *luchot*, he fashioned a wooden Ark to house the fragments; a second, gold-inlaid Ark was constructed for the *Mishkan* to hold the whole *luchot*. Normally the wooden Ark with the broken *luchot* would be brought to war, and in that case marital relations remained permitted. On a few occasions — including the episode when the Ark was captured by the *Plishtim* — the gold Ark was taken instead, and that is when the prohibition applied. Tosafot suggests that Yehoshua's error was precisely this: he took the wrong Ark to war, thereby imposing the prohibition on the entire camp. The childlessness then follows as *midah keneged midah*: he prevented an entire night of legitimate marital intimacy, and so he himself was left without children.

As Tosafot notes, these two explanations — responding to a question in Moshe's presence, and taking the wrong Ark — are two independent *midrashim* that the *Gemara* has joined together. The primary *drasha* of the Jericho passage, as it circulates elsewhere, is about *Talmud Torah*, and the material about *tashmish ha-mita* is appended to it here to serve the explanation of Yehoshua's childlessness.

### Talmud Torah Priority

With this episode as its springboard, the *Gemara* arrives at a sweeping general principle, cited by Shmuel bar Ina in the name of Rav: *gadol Talmud Torah yoter me'hakravat temidin* — *Talmud Torah* is greater than offering the daily sacrifices. The proof is the angel's own declaration: *ata bati* — I have come now, on account of this — referring to the neglect of *Talmud Torah*, not of the *korbanot*. This principle deserves to be appreciated in its full cultural weight: the *Gemara* is addressing an audience that understood the *Beit ha-Mikdash* and its sacrificial service as the apex of religious life. To assert that *Talmud Torah* supersedes even the *tamid* offering is a bold statement of rabbinic values, one that helped define the character of Jewish religious experience in the absence of the Temple.

## Marital Intimacy

### Rav Huna

The discussion of *tashmish ha-mita* leads naturally to a cluster of teachings about the sanctity of marital intimacy and the obligations that flow from it. Rav Huna states in the name of Rav: *kol ha-yashan be-kila she-ish ve-ishto shruyin bah* — whoever sleeps in a tent, meaning a room, in which a husband and wife are dwelling — *alav ha-katuv omer n'shei ami te-gareshun mi-beit ta'anugeha* — of him the *pasuk* says: the women of my people you have driven from the house of their pleasure. By inserting himself into the couple's private space, a person effectively drives husband and wife apart, preventing the intimacy they are entitled to. The *pasuk* applies to such a person as though he had forcibly separated them.

### Rav Yosef

Rav Yosef extends this further: *va-afilu be-ishah niddah* — even if the wife is a *niddah*, and physical relations are in any case prohibited, the presence of a third person in the room still violates the couple's intimacy. This is a striking ruling. Even when there is no possibility of sexual relations, the couple is entitled to emotional closeness and private space, and a person who intrudes on that space is held accountable by the same *pasuk*.

### Rava Disagrees

Rava takes a different view: *im ishah niddah hi, tavo alav berachah* — if she is a *niddah*, one should actually sleep in that room, as a kind of guardian to prevent any potential transgression. Rava then immediately deflates this suggestion: *v'lav hi, de-ato idi man natra* — but actually, this does not hold. Who keeps them apart on all the other days of the year? They keep themselves apart. The *halacha* of *niddah* is observed by the couple themselves, without the need for external supervision. To post oneself in their room as a chaperone is to show distrust where trust is owed, and the ruling stands with Rav Yosef: one does not enter the couple's space, even when she is a *niddah*.

## Modern Framework…

### Written Rulings

The *Shulchan Aruch* preserves a notable position on how the prohibition of *hora'ah b'makom rav* adapts to a world in which Torah law is comprehensively codified in written texts. The *Shulchan Aruch* records: *yeish mi she-kasav she-kol ha-kasuv be-sfarim mi-piskei ha-geonim yachol le-horot bi-mei rabbo* — there is an opinion that anything written in the books of the *Geonim* may be ruled upon even during one's teacher's lifetime. The limitation is equally clear: *rak lo yoreh davar mi-libo ve-lo yismach al ha-ketav li-lmos milsa le-milsa me-atzmo* — but one may not rule from one's own judgment, and may not rely on written texts to reason independently from case to case on one's own. The permission to convey what is already written does not extend to the exercise of independent judgment even with written texts as one's basis.

### Consulting Books

The *Shevut Yaakov* draws out the full implications of this shift. Since we live in a world where Torah law is written down, and since the written books are the repositories of transmitted wisdom in a way that previously only living teachers were, the *Shevut Yaakov* teaches: *oy le-chol mi she-higia le-hora'ah she-lo yoreh shum hora'ah bilti iyun techilah ba-sefer* — woe to anyone who has reached the status of a decisor and issues any ruling without first consulting the books. The reason: *ki raboseinu hein hein ha-sfarim asher nispashtu be-kerev Yisrael* — our teachers are the books that have spread among the Jewish people. The living teacher has, in a meaningful sense, been succeeded by the codified tradition. The obligation of *b'makom rav* — not to rule in the teacher's presence — now primarily expresses itself as an obligation not to rule without consulting the *sfarim* that embody the accumulated authority of all previous generations.
