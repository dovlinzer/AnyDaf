# Gittin 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Half-Slave Monetary Stat…

### Half-Slave Intro

Today's *daf* is 43. We pick up in the middle of the bottom of 42b, dealing with the fascinating phenomenon of an *eved* who occupies a middle status: half-free and half-owned by his master. The practical arrangement was that he works for his master half the time and for himself the other half. Yet his personal status creates intractable problems — he cannot marry a free woman nor a slave woman. Beyond the realm of *issur*, monetary questions arise as well: what happens if he is gored?

### Permanent vs. Temporary

The *Gemara* on the previous *daf* established a distinction based on the nature of the damage. If the injury is permanent — his arm is severed, or he is killed — he is treated as being owned by both parties, and the award is split between himself (or his heirs, a point we will return to) and his master. If the damage is temporary — his arm swells and he cannot work for a period — and if that temporary incapacitation is classified as a form of injury rather than merely lost labor, then since the ox that caused it is not liable for lost labor, the payment goes to whichever party "owns" him on the day the goring occurred.

### Me'ukav Get Shichrur

A second scenario concerns a slave awaiting a writ of freedom — a *me'ukav get shichrur*. Two sub-cases arise. First, the slave who is half-owned: following the ruling of *Beis Shammai*, the master is compelled to free him. Second, and perhaps the cleaner case for isolating the conceptual issue: the master renounced ownership — *mafkir* him — or sold him to *Chutz La'aretz*, thereby incurring an obligation to free him. In these situations, monetary ownership has already dissolved; only the personal status of *eved* persists.

The *Gemara* raises whether even in this state the master can still collect *knas* — the fixed penalty paid when a slave is gored and killed. The *knas* of thirty *shekalim* need not be understood as compensation for the loss of property; it is a fixed punishment imposed on the negligent ox-owner, and conceivably it could still flow to the former master even absent ongoing property ownership. What is striking is that the *Gemara* entertains the further possibility that even personal-injury payments might still reach the master, despite the fact that he no longer owns the slave as property. That is genuinely difficult to understand, since any such payment would ordinarily be grounded in a proprietary relationship that has already lapsed.

## Terumah Eligibility

### Can He Eat Terumah?

The discussion now shifts from monetary matters to questions of status, beginning approximately twenty lines from the bottom of the *amud*, at the line opening with the words *u-midrash Chachamim hu*. The *Gemara* poses the question: a *me'ukav get shichrur* — a slave who no longer has any monetary ownership over him but is still awaiting his writ of freedom — may he eat *terumah* if his master is a *kohen*?

The *Gemara* frames the tension around the verse. The *pasuk* identifies the basis for a slave eating *terumah* as *kinyan kaspo* — he is the monetary acquisition of his master. Yet this slave is *lav kinyan kaspo* — he is no longer property in any meaningful sense. On the other hand, perhaps *terumah* is fundamentally about belonging to the household, a relational rather than strictly proprietary status, and perhaps that relationship persists. Yet the *Gemara* does not frame the question in those terms. The emphasis falls squarely on the *pasuk's* insistence on *kinyan kaspo*, and the question is whether that language can still apply to someone who is no longer owned.

### Mixed Babies Case

The *Gemara* offers a *ta shema* from a ruling of Rav Mesharshiya: a *kohenes* whose infant became mixed up with the infant of her *shifcha* — one of the babies is the master's heir and one is the slave, but no one knows which is which. The ruling is that *harei elu ochlin b'terumah* — both may eat *terumah* — because *terumah* may be eaten either as a *kohen* or as the slave of a *kohen*, and each child qualifies under one of those categories, even if the identities are uncertain. Furthermore, when they go to collect *terumah* at the threshing floor, they receive only one portion together — and the reason is that a slave may not collect *terumah* alone at the threshing floor, lest people mistake him for a *kohen*. He must therefore go together with his master. In this confused situation the two children must appear together.

### Babies Grow & Free

When the mixture grows up — *higdilu hata'arovet* — the father has died and one of them now owns the estate, which means one of them owns the other, though no one knows which is which. So they free one another: *meshachrerin zeh et zeh*. Each writes the other a *shtar shichrur* — if you are the slave, I am freeing you; if I am the slave, you are freeing me. The *Gemara* identifies this as a case of *me'ukav get shichrur* that eats *terumah*.

### Status Comparison

The *Gemara* immediately rejects the comparison, however: *hachi ata?* — is that really a parallel? *Hatam*, there, if *Eliyahu* would come and identify which child is the slave, we would say *kinyan kaspo karinan bei* — we still call him property in the full sense, because in reality one of them fully is. There is simply an unresolved *safek*. The reality beneath the uncertainty is intact. *Hacha*, here, by contrast, the *me'ukav get shichrur* is *lav kinyan kaspo hu kelal* — not owned as property at all. There is no underlying reality of ownership; only the procedural barrier of the missing *shtar shichrur* remains. The phrase *lav kinyan kaspo hu kelal* suggests the logic would point toward this person *not* eating *terumah* if the *pasuk* requires genuine property status. Yet the *Gemara* leaves the question unresolved.

## Selling for Knas

### Can Sell for Knas?

The *Gemara* now poses a fresh *iba'ya lehu*: can a master sell his slave specifically for the purpose of the buyer collecting future *knas*? Normally, when a slave is gored and killed, the owner collects the *knas*. The question is whether the owner can in effect sell off that future entitlement — you pay me something now, and if he is ever gored, you collect the *knas*, not me. This is analogous to a case in *Bava Metzia* regarding an object that is burgled: can the owner sell to a third party the right to collect *kefel*, the double payment, if the object is stolen? Or must the collector of *kefel* actually be the owner? Here the question is whether one can carve off this particular financial right and transfer it separately.

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *Gemara* frames the question as one that may be asked both according to Rabbi Meir and according to the *Rabbanan*, with different force in each direction. Even for Rabbi Meir, who holds that a person may sell *davar shelo ba le'olam* — something that does not yet exist in the world — one might argue that the *knas* cannot be sold. When Rabbi Meir permits selling future items, the paradigm case is buying the future dates of a palm tree: the fruit will naturally come into being, it is a predictable natural outcome. *Aval hacha mi yemar demingach* — but here, who says the slave will even be gored? It is not a natural eventuality arising from the slave himself. And further: *mi yemar demishalem* — even if the slave is gored, who says the tortfeasor will actually pay? If the person who was negligent admits to it, he is exempt from *knas* — *modeh umiptar*. The potential liability is therefore doubly speculative: the goring may never happen, and even if it does, admission forecloses payment.

### Rabbanan Position

Conversely, even for the *Rabbanan*, who generally hold that one cannot sell *davar shelo ba le'olam*, there may be grounds to permit this transaction. *Ad kan lo ka'amri Rabbanan hatam ein adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam* — when did they say you cannot sell something that does not yet exist? In a case like *peires dekla* — selling the fruit of the palm tree — the fruit does not yet physically exist and one is attempting to transfer the fruit itself. *Aval hacha, ha kai shor ha kai eved* — here, however, the slave is present and the ox is present. As *Tosafot* explains, this is structurally different: everyone agrees that I can sell you ownership in the tree for the purpose of its fruit. I am giving you something concrete now — a present ownership — even though the fruit has not yet grown. So too here: perhaps I am not selling you a future right; I am selling you the slave himself for this purpose, and that constitutes a present transaction in a concrete object. Perhaps that works even for the *Rabbanan*. On the other hand, *knas* for goring is not a natural outgrowth of owning a slave the way fruit is a natural outgrowth of owning a tree. The *knas* arises from a tortious event inflicted by an outside party; it is a peculiar and unnatural basis for an ownership transfer. The question remains unresolved on both sides.

### Zero Value Evidence

Rabbi Abba then introduces a *ta shema* from an exegetical discussion of the *pasuk* about *terumah*, citing the phrase *v'yelid beiso hem yochlu belachmo*. The *Gemara* asks: why does the *pasuk* need to mention both *kinyan kesef* and *yelid bayis* — a slave purchased with money and one born into the household? If one who is purchased may eat *terumah*, surely one born into the house may eat all the more so. The answer is that if we had only the phrase *kinyan kesef*, we might have inferred: only a slave who currently has monetary value — *shaveh kesef* — may eat *terumah*, since the *pasuk* emphasizes the monetary dimension of the acquisition. The *yelid bayis* verse teaches that even a slave born into the household may eat regardless of whether he has present monetary value. But then one might argue the reverse: a *yelid bayis* eats *terumah* whether or not he has value, but a *kinyan kesef* — a purchased slave — perhaps requires present monetary value, and if after purchase he becomes worthless due to illness or disability, he may no longer eat *terumah*. Therefore the *pasuk* places *kinyan kaspo* and *yelid beiso* side by side to teach: just as one born into the house eats *terumah* even with no monetary value, so too one purchased eats *terumah* even with no monetary value — *af al pi she'eino shaveh klum ochel*.

The point for our purposes is that there exists a slave with zero monetary value who nonetheless eats *terumah*.

### Zero Value Objection

Now the *Gemara* asks: *v'i salka daiteich eved she'machro rabbo l'knas machur* — if one could sell a slave solely for the purpose of *knas*, then how could any slave ever have zero monetary value? A slave with no value — one whose upkeep exceeds his productivity, or one whom no one wishes to take into their household — could always be sold for at least a *perutah*, because someone would be willing to pay a small amount on the chance of eventually collecting thirty *shekalim* in *knas*. The logic resembles a lottery: the purchaser takes possession not in the ordinary sense but only for the *knas*, and pays a *perutah* for the possibility of collecting a substantial sum. Since someone would always take that wager, no slave could ever be worth less than a *perutah*, and the *Gemara's* premise of a slave with zero monetary value would be impossible.

This generates a productive discussion. One might ask: why would the owner sell for a *perutah* if the potential upside is entirely his? The answer is that the owner, doing the expected-value calculation, may conclude the risk is too small to bother holding — while a poorer party may rationally pay a *perutah* for the lottery-like chance at thirty *shekalim*. Conversely, if the arrangement allows the buyer to take the *knas* risk without actually taking the slave into his household — leaving all the upkeep obligations with the original owner — then the buyer has essentially pure upside, and the owner might prefer that arrangement. The *Gemara's* logic therefore stands: if such a sale is valid, the slave would always have at least minimal market value, and the case of *eino shaveh klum* would be impossible.

### Unfit Slave Exception

The *Gemara* responds: *in, ika avda treifa* — yes, there is a slave who is a *treifa*, one who has received an internal fatal injury. A *treifa* is halachically not considered fully *chai*, and accordingly, the *knas* for killing a *treifa* slave would not apply. Such a slave could not be sold for even a *perutah* on the basis of *knas*, since no *knas* would ever be owed. But the *Gemara* presses further: even a *treifa* slave can stand at your side and serve you — *chazi lemeikom kamei* — so how can any slave have zero value? The *Gemara* concludes with the limiting case: *bimnuval umukeh shechin* — a slave who is both a *treifa* and physically repulsive, afflicted with a skin disease so severe that no one wishes to be in his presence. In such a case he truly has zero value, no *knas* can ever be collected for him, and the *Gemara's* premise holds.

## Kiddushin Half-Status

### Half-Slave Man Marriage

The *Gemara* returns to the half-slave, half-free status — *mi shechetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin* — presenting what is perhaps the most fascinating cluster of questions. Can such a man validly perform *kiddushin* with a free woman? The *eved* half of him is legally incapable of marrying a free woman, but is the marriage binding to the extent of his free half?

### Half Woman Marriage

Before resolving the question about the half-slave man, the *Gemara* introduces two analogous cases involving free persons. First: *ben Yisrael she'amar l'vas chorin hitkadshi bechetzi* — a fully free man who says to a free woman, "be married to half of me." The *Gemara* indicates this is *mekudeshes* — it works. The reason is that *kiddushin* restricts the woman, not the man. In a halachic framework that permits polygamy, marriage creates exclusivity for the woman but not for the man, and so the man being "half married" is coherent: his other half could theoretically be married to another woman. The woman is fully restricted by the relationship; the man speaking in halves is merely describing a social reality, not performing a defective act.

By contrast, if a man says to a free woman "half of you is married to me," that does not work — *einah mekudeshes* — because for the woman, marriage is fully exclusive. The half of her that is not married to this man could not be married to another man; therefore the category of "half a woman married" is incoherent.

Now the question is pressed in the more complex case. *Im timtzei lomar* — if the rule is that a free man marrying half a woman does not work because *shiyyer b'kinyano* — he held back, he performed only a half-act — perhaps the half-slave man who says to a free woman "I marry you" presents a different case. He did not hold anything back — *lo shiyyer b'kinyano*. He performed a full act; it simply could only take half-effect, because half of him is a slave. Does that make a difference?

On the other hand, one might argue: *dechazi lekulei* — in the case of the free man saying "half of me," the woman is in fact fully married to him, since his whole status is that of a free person who could potentially marry her entirely. The "half" is a manner of speaking. *Ha lo chazi lekulei* — here, by contrast, half of this man is fundamentally incapable of being married to her. Perhaps one cannot conceive of her being married to the free portion of him, *shebo*, and therefore the *kiddushin* is not binding.

### Full vs. Half Act

The distinction the *Gemara* is working toward is between two types of partial *kiddushin*. In the case where a man says "half of you is married to me," the act itself is defective — he performed a half-act, he held back. In the case of the half-slave man marrying a free woman, the act is complete and unqualified; the limitation is not in the act but in his status. Similarly, when a man says "half of me is married to you," he may be performing a full act that takes full effect, with the "half" being descriptive rather than constitutive of the act. These structural differences motivate the subsequent rulings regarding the half-slave woman.

### Half-Woman Evidence

The discussion of the half-slave woman proceeds as follows. רבא *Rava* states: *kesheim she-hamkadesh chatzi isha einah mekudeshes — kach chatzi shifcha v'chatzi bas chorin she-nitkadesha ein kiddusheha kiddushin*. Just as marrying half a woman produces no valid *kiddushin*, so too the *kiddushin* of a half-slave woman is not binding. Rabba bar Rav Huna subsequently delivers the same ruling publicly. Rav Chisda challenges him: *mi dami?* How can you compare? *Hasam shiyyer b'kinyano* — there, by marrying half a woman, the man held back and did not do a full act. *Hacha lo shiyyer b'kinyano* — here, in marrying the half-slave woman, the man held nothing back; he married her fully, as fully as he was able to. Rav Chisda implies this distinction should lead to the *kiddushin* being valid.

Rabba bar Rav Huna thereupon reversed his position entirely. He convened a public lecture and acknowledged the correction, prefacing his revised ruling with the famous dictum: *ein adam omed al divrei Torah ela im kein nichshal bahen* — a person does not truly come to stand on his Torah unless he has first stumbled in it. Having publicly erred and retracted, the lesson is genuinely internalized.

He then ruled: even though a free man marrying half a woman produces no *kiddushin*, by a half-slave half-free woman the *kiddushin* does take effect. *Hasam shiyyer b'kinyano, hacha lo shiyyer b'kinyano* — there he held back and performed a half-act; here he performed a full act that took half-effect. Since the act was complete and unqualified, it takes effect to whatever degree it can, and the half of her that is free is *mekudeshes*.

Rav Sheshet, however, did not accept this revision and maintained his original position: just as marrying half a woman produces no *kiddushin*, so too the half-slave half-free woman's *kiddushin* is not binding.

### Half-Woman Evidence

In response to Rav Sheshet's position, an interlocutor raises the case of *shifcha charufa* from the *Torah*: the passage describes a woman who is half-slave, half-free, designated to an *eved Ivri*, and refers to her as *me'ureset l'eved Ivri* — betrothed to a Hebrew slave. Does this not demonstrate that *kiddushin* is binding with a half-slave woman?

### Rabbi Yishmael View

Rav Sheshet replies: go and see what Rabbi Yishmael says. Rabbi Yishmael holds that the *Torah's* case of *shifcha charufa* is not about a half-slave woman at all — it is about a fully enslaved *shifcha Kena'anit*, one hundred percent a slave. And yet the *Torah* uses the term *me'ureset l'eved Ivri*. Now, a fully enslaved Canaanite woman is *not bat ishut* — she is legally incapable of *kiddushin* — so what does *me'ureset* mean in that context? *Meyushedet* — she has been designated to him, she is living with him in a recognized domestic arrangement, but there is no legally binding *kiddushin*. The master has assigned her to the *eved Ivri* to cohabit and produce slave-children; the word *me'ureset* denotes designation and cohabitation, not formal marriage.

Therefore, Rav Sheshet argues, the *Torah's* use of *me'ureset* proves nothing about whether *kiddushin* is valid for a half-slave woman. Even if the *Torah's* case involves a half-slave woman, the term means only *meyushedet* — designated — not *mekudeshes* in the legally binding sense.

### Heirs Contradiction

The *Gemara* had earlier cited a ruling about an ox that kills a *mi shechetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin*: the payment is split — half *knas* to the master and half *kofer* to *yorshav*, his heirs. The difficulty is immediately apparent: *v'i amart kiddushav lav kiddushin — yorshin mena lei?* If the *kiddushin* of a half-slave is invalid, how does he have heirs?

Rav Adda bar Ahava proposes: *keshe'asa'o treifah* — the ox did not actually kill him; it merely rendered him a *treifah*, delivered a fatal internal injury but did not cause immediate death. Since he is still alive — Monty Python's "I'm not dead yet" — he collects himself; *nafshei*, he is his own heir. *Rava* objects with two responses: first, the text says *yorshav* — his heirs — not him; second, *kofer* by definition is only paid after death — *kofer ein mishtalem ela le'acher mitah*, as Reish Lakish rules — it is an atonement payment for a death, not for a wound, even a fatal one.

*Rava* therefore offers an alternative: *ra'uy liten v'ein lo* — those heirs should receive the *kofer* payment, but since in fact there are no heirs, that half of the *kofer* goes uncollected. No one receives it.

*Tosafot* notes a subtle point: why does the *Gemara* frame the question of heirs as dependent on *kiddushin* at all? Even illegitimate children — even a *mamzer* — are their father's heirs. One does not need *kiddushin* to have heirs. *Tosafot* explains that the issue is not legitimacy but the recognition of family structure. The principle *eved ein lo chiyus* — a slave has no legally recognized family — means that a slave's biological children are not halachically recognized as his children. It is analogous to the secular legal concept of a bastard child: biology alone was insufficient to establish legal parenthood; formal recognition was required. In *halacha*, a child born out of wedlock is legally recognized, but a slave's children are not. This applies even if the slave has children with a free woman — the *halacha* refuses to recognize the family structure of a slave, and the children are legally his master's slaves, not his heirs.

*Tosafot* therefore argues: the relevance of *kiddushin* here is that valid *kiddushin* is an affirmation that family structure is recognized for this person. If we say that the half-slave man is capable of *kiddushin*, we are simultaneously affirming that his family structure is legally cognizable — and therefore that children resulting from such a union would be legally recognized as his heirs. The question of heirs is thus not a question of legitimacy but of status-recognition.

## Kiddushin After Freedom

### Scenario Setup

Rav Chisda now introduces the further scenario: *chatziya shifcha v'chatziya bas chorin she-nitkadsha l'Reuven* — a half-slave half-free woman who was married to Reuven, and then *v'nishtachrerah* — the remaining half of her was freed — and *v'chazra v'nitkadsha l'Shimon* — she then went ahead and performed *kiddushin* with Shimon. She is now somehow half-married to Reuven and half-married to Shimon. Neither man could fully consummate the marriage while the other remained. Then both Reuven and Shimon died, and they happened to be brothers. Now Levi, their brother, has an obligation of *yibum*. And Rav Chisda says: *v'eini korei bah eishes shnei meisim* — I do not read about her as "the wife of two dead men," which would have been an obstacle to *yibum*. The *Gemara* in *Yevamos* establishes that a widow who comes to a surviving brother from two dead brothers — because one did *maamar* but not full *yibum*, leaving his connection to her overlapping with the first brother's — cannot perform *yibum* with the third brother. Yet here, even though she was seemingly half-married to two men, Levi can do *yibum*.

### Nullify or Complete?

The reason is that this situation cannot be characterized as her being half-married to both. When she was freed, one of two things necessarily happened to the original *kiddushin* with Reuven: either it expanded and completed, making her fully Reuven's wife, in which case the subsequent *kiddushin* with Shimon was entirely ineffective — or it dissolved entirely, making her fully available for Shimon's *kiddushin*, in which case the connection to Reuven is wholly gone. She is therefore either entirely Reuven's wife or entirely Shimon's wife. *Iy kiddushei d'Reuven kiddushei — kiddushei d'Shimon lav kiddushei; v'kiddushei d'Shimon kiddushei — kiddushei d'Reuven lav kiddushei.* She cannot be simultaneously half-married to each. And therefore she does not come to Levi as *eishes shnei meisim*. The question of which branch obtained remains unresolved — but either way, *yibum* with Levi is permissible.

### Kiddushin Bursts

The debate is now stated explicitly: *chatzi shifcha v'chatzi bas chorin sheniskadsha l'Reuven v'nishtachrerah v'chazra v'nitkadsha l'Shimon* — what is the law? Rav Yosef bar Chama in the name of Rav Nachman: *paku kiddushei rishon* — the original *kiddushin* burst. When she was freed, her legal identity changed so fundamentally that the half-*kiddushin* she had with Reuven dissolved. She became a new legal person, and Reuven's *kiddushin* evaporated. She is therefore fully available for Shimon's *kiddushin*.

### Kiddushin Completes

Rav Zeira in the name of Rav Nachman: *gamru kiddushei rishon* — the original *kiddushin* was completed. Once the impediment of her partial slavery was removed, there was nothing holding back the *kiddushin* from taking full effect. The half-*kiddushin* expanded to cover her entirely, and she is fully Reuven's wife. Shimon's subsequent *kiddushin* was therefore meaningless.

### Shifcha Charufa Text

Rav Zeira adds: *kavasi didi mistabra* — it makes sense like my position. The proof is from the *pasuk* of *shifcha charufa*: *lo yumsu ki lo chufasha* — they will not be put to death because she has not yet been freed. The *Gemara* reads this as a case of a half-slave half-free woman who is half-married to a man; another man sleeps with her and the *Torah* says it is not a capital crime because it is not fully adultery — she is only half-married, because she has not yet been freed. *Ha chufasha — yumsu*: had she been freed, they would be put to death, meaning had that other half of her been freed, the original half-*kiddushin* would have become full *kiddushin*, and adultery with her would have been a capital crime. This supports the view of *gamru kiddushei rishon*: freedom causes the original *kiddushin* to complete itself.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye challenges Rav Zeira: *u'letana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael d'amar b'shifcha Kena'anit hame'ureset l'eved Ivri* — what does this *pasuk* say according to Rabbi Yishmael? Rabbi Yishmael holds that the woman in the *pasuk* is a fully enslaved *shifcha Kena'anit* with no valid *kiddushin* at all, only *eirusin* in the sense of designation and cohabitation. According to Rabbi Yishmael, then, how does the *pasuk* support *gamru kiddushei rishon*? There was no *kiddushin* to complete! Rav Zeira's reading of the *pasuk* collapses.

Abaye explains how Rabbi Yishmael reads the *pasuk*: *she'chufasha v'chazra v'nitkadsha* — had she been freed and then performed a new *kiddushin*, and then someone committed adultery with her, only then would it be a capital crime. The *pasuk* does not say that freedom automatically completes an existing *kiddushin*; it says that freedom followed by a new *kiddushin* would create a fully binding marriage. Therefore the *pasuk* cannot serve as evidence for *gamru kiddushei rishon* — it is equally compatible with *paku kiddushei rishon*, where freedom dissolves the original *kiddushin* and necessitates a fresh one. The question is left unresolved.

## Historical Precedent

### Forced Freedom

Rav Huna bar Chinena in the name of Rav Yitzchak reports: *maaseh b'shifcha she'chetziyah shifcha v'chetziyah bas chorin* — there was an actual case of a half-slave half-free woman, and *v'chafu es rabbo v'asah bas chorin* — the rabbinic authorities compelled her master to free her entirely.

### Childbearing Motive

This raises a question. Throughout our *sugya*, the case compelling the freeing of a half-slave *man* was justified by his inability to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *p'ru u'rvu* while trapped in this status. But does that reasoning apply to a woman? The *Gemara* asks: is this ruling in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, *d'amar al shneichem hu omer v'yevarech osam Elokim vayomer pru u'rvu* — who derives from the verse that the obligation of *p'ru u'rvu* applies to women as well? Perhaps the reason the rabbis forced the master to free this woman was specifically to allow her to fulfill that obligation by marrying and bearing children.

### Protection Motive

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, however, offers an entirely different explanation: *lo, minhag hefker nahagu bah* — that was not the reason at all. The actual motivation was that people were taking advantage of her. Because she was neither fully a slave nor fully free, her status was anomalous in both directions: she may not have been fully designated to a slave as a *shifcha* might be, and she could not be fully protected through marriage as a free woman could be. She had no man — whether master or husband — to protect her, and she was therefore being exploited. The rabbis freed her not to enable *p'ru u'rvu* but to protect her from that exploitation. The compelled manumission had nothing to do with the positive obligation of bearing children.
