# Sanhedrin 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shlomo & Divine Voice

### Bat Kol & Shlomo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְּשׁוּ עוֹד לִמְנוֹת אֶחָד. בָּאָה דְּמוּת דְּיוֹקְנוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיו וְנִשְׁתַּטְּחָה לִפְנֵיהֶם, וְלֹא הִשְׁגִּיחוּ עָלֶיהָ. בָּאָה אֵשׁ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם וְלִחֲכָה אֵשׁ בְּסַפְסְלֵיהֶם, וְלֹא הִשְׁגִּיחוּ עָלֶיהָ. יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: "חָזִיתָ אִישׁ מָהִיר בִּמְלַאכְתּוֹ לִפְנֵי מְלָכִים יִתְיַצָּב בַּל יִתְיַצֵּב לִפְנֵי חֲשֻׁכִּים״. מִי שֶׁהִקְדִּים בֵּיתִי לְבֵיתוֹ, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁבֵּיתִי בָּנָה בְּשֶׁבַע שָׁנִים וּבֵיתוֹ בָּנָה בִּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה. וְלֹא הִשְׁגִּיחַ עָלֶיהָ. יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: "הַמֵעִמְּךָ יְשַׁלְמֶנָּה כִּי מָאַסְתָּ כִּי אַתָּה תִבְחַר וְלֹא אֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **They sought to enumerate one more,** King Solomon, in the list of kings with no share in the World-to-Come. **The image of the face of his father,** King David, **came and prostrated itself before them,** pleading to omit Solomon from the list, **and they paid it no heed. A fire came from Heaven and the fire scorched their benches [*safseleihem*], and they paid it no heed. A Divine Voice emerged and said to** the members of the Great Assembly: **"Do you see a man diligent in his business? He shall stand before kings; he shall stand not before obscure men"** (Proverbs 22:29). See the greatness of Solomon, **who preceded** construction of **My House,** the Temple, **to** the construction of **his house. Moreover, My House he built** quickly, **in** just **seven years, and his house he constructed in thirteen years.** **And they paid it no heed. A Divine Voice emerged and said: "Shall His recompense be as you will it? For you loathe it, so that you must choose, and not I?** Therefore, speak what you know" (Job 34:33). Only God, and not the people, determines who has a share in the World-to-Come.

Today's *daf* is 105, picking up at the bottom of 104b. The discussion has centered on the list of kings who lack a portion in the *olam haba*, with the previous *daf* concluding on a remarkable note: the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* sought to include Shlomo among those kings, and God responded by sending fire from heaven accompanied by a *bat kol*. The dynamic here invites comparison with the famous story of the *tanur shel Achnai*, but with a crucial difference. That episode concluded with *lo bashamayim hi* — the authority to determine *halacha* rests with the rabbis, not with heavenly voices. Here, however, the resolution runs in the opposite direction.

### God Decides Olam HaBa

The *bat kol* declares: *hame'imcha yishalmena* — will compensation come from you? *Ki ma'asta* — you have chosen who to despise. *Ki ata tivchar, v'lo ani* — do you think you get to choose, and not I? The message is unmistakable: while the *rabbanim* derive their authority over *halacha* from *psukim* and *derasha*, the question of who enters *olam haba* is ultimately God's domain. The list the Sages compile is, in a meaningful sense, beside the point — God reserves that judgment for Himself. This answers a question that naturally arises: who authorized the *rabbanim* to determine who has a portion in *olam haba* or not?

### Tashlumin Interpretation

Rashi explains that the key word in the *bat kol* is *yishalmena*, which he connects not to *shalom* but to *tashlumin* — compensation. The question God poses is whether the rabbis are the ones who will compensate, that is, make good on these judgments. The answer is no. That authority belongs to God alone.

## Dorshei Reshumot

### Reject Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוֹרְשֵׁי רְשׁוּמוֹת הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: כּוּלָּן בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לִי גִלְעָד וְלִי מְנַשֶּׁה וְאֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי. מוֹאָב סִיר רַחְצִי עַל אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי עָלַי פְּלֶשֶׁת הִתְרוֹעָעִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Those who interpret the Torah metaphorically would say: All of** those enumerated who have no share ultimately **enter the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Gilead is Mine, and Manasseh is Mine, and Ephraim also is the strength of My head; Judah is My scepter; Moab is My washing pot; over Edom I will cast My shoe; Philistia, cry aloud because of Me"** (Psalms 60:9–10, and see 108:9–10).

The *Dorshei Reshumot* — those who interpret *psukim* in the manner of *aggadata* — go even further than God's protest over Shlomo. Rather than merely objecting to a particular inclusion on the list, they reject the entire framework of exclusion. *Kulam ba'im l'olam haba* — everyone, even those wicked kings, receives a portion in *olam haba*. Their proof derives from a creative and remarkably ingenious *derasha* on a *pasuk* in Psalms.

### Psalm Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "לִי גִלְעָד (וְלִי מְנַשֶּׁה)״ – זֶה אַחְאָב שֶׁנָּפַל בְּרָמוֹת גִּלְעָד. ״מְנַשֶּׁה״ – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ. ״אֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי״ – זֶה יָרׇבְעָם דְּקָאָתֵי מֵאֶפְרַיִם. ״יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי״ – זֶה אֲחִיתוֹפֶל דְּקָאָתֵי מִיהוּדָה. ״מוֹאָב סִיר רַחְצִי״ – זֶה גֵּחֲזִי, שֶׁלָּקָה עַל עִסְקֵי רְחִיצָה. ״עַל אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי״ – זֶה דּוֹאֵג הָאֲדוֹמִי. ״עָלַי פְּלֶשֶׁת הִתְרוֹעָעִי״ – אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אִם יָבֹא דָּוִד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַפְּלִשְׁתִּי וְהוֹרִישׁ אֶת בָּנֶיךָ גַּת, מָה אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ? אָמַר לָהֶן: עָלַי לַעֲשׂוֹתָן רֵיעִים זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Gilead is Mine and Manasseh is Mine"; this** is referring to **Ahab, who fell in Ramoth Gilead. "Manasseh", in its plain meaning,** is referring to King Manasseh. **"Ephraim also is the strength of My head"; this** is referring to **Jeroboam, who comes** from the tribe of **Ephraim. "Judah is My scepter"; this** is referring to **Ahithophel,** who comes from the tribe of **Judah. "Moab is My washing pot"; this** is referring to **Gehazi, who was afflicted** with leprosy **over matters of washing,** as he took money from Naaman, whom he instructed to immerse in the Jordan River. **"Over Edom I will cast My shoe"; this** is referring to **Doeg the Edomite. "Philistia, cry aloud [*hitroa'i*] because of Me";** this is referring to the fact that **the ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, if David, who killed the Philistine and bequeathed** the city of **Gath to your sons, will come** and complain that You gave a share in the World-to-Come to his enemies Doeg and Ahithophel, **what will You do concerning him?** Will you accept his complaint? God **said to** the ministering angels: **It is upon me to render** David and his enemies **friends [*re'im*] with each other,** and even David will agree.

The *pasuk* reads: *li Gilead v'li Menashe, v'Ephraim ma'oz roshi, Yehuda mechokki, Moav sir rachtzi, al Edom ashlich na'ali, alai Pleshet hitro'ai* — to me is Gilead and to me is Menashe; Ephraim is the strength of my head; Yehuda is my scepter; Moav is the pot in which I will bathe; on Edom I will cast my shoes; upon the Philistines I will shout in triumph. Read simply, the *pasuk* contrasts God's chosen tribes of Israel with nations like Edom and Moav that are treated contemptuously. The *Dorshei Reshumot*, however, read it as a divine declaration of ownership and inclusion for a roster of sinners.

### Figure Identifications

The *derasha* proceeds as follows: *li Gilead* — to me is Gilead: *zeh Achav*, that is Achav, *she'nafal b'Ramot Gilead*, who fell at Ramot Gilead. Rashi explains that God is saying, in effect, I will carry his sin — he too will receive *olam haba*. *V'li Menashe k'mashma'o* — and to me is Menashe, taken literally as King Menashe rather than the tribe, meaning God has him covered as well. *Ephraim ma'oz roshi zeh Yerovam* — Yerovam, who comes from the tribe of Ephraim, is also included. *Yehuda mechokki zeh Achitofel* — Yehuda is my scepter, referring to Achitofel, who comes from Yehuda. Achitofel is one of the four commoners (*hedyotot*) who will be introduced shortly; this *derasha* already incorporates him.

### Commoners Included

*Moav sir rachtzi zeh Geichazi* — Moav is the pot in which I bathe, referring to Geichazi, *she'laka iskei rechitza*, whose entire episode with Na'aman centered on bathing. *Al Edom ashlich na'ali zeh Doeg HaEdomi* — on Edom I cast my shoes: the connection between *Edom* and *Edomi* is direct.

### Making Friends

The *pasuk* concludes: *alai Pleshet hitro'ai* — upon the Philistines I will triumph. What does this refer to? The angels before God ask: if David, who slew Goliath the Philistine and bequeathed the city of Gat to Israel's children, comes before You, what will You do for him, given that You are protecting Doeg — who informed against David to Shaul and brought about such tragedy? God replies: *alai la'asotan rei'im zeh la-zeh* — it is upon Me to make them friends with one another. Similarly, Achitofel and Doeg were enemies of David; God assures the angels He will reconcile them all. This is a beautiful and inventive *derasha*, managing to include every figure on the list of sinners within a single *pasuk* while resolving the apparent injustice to David at the same time.

## People & Prophets

### Teshuvah Nitzachat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "מַדּוּעַ שׁוֹבְבָה הָעָם הַזֶּה יְרוּשָׁלִַים מְשֻׁבָה נִצַּחַת״. אָמַר רַב: תְּשׁוּבָה נִצַּחַַת הֵשִׁיבָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לַנָּבִיא. אָמַר לָהֶן נָבִיא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: חִזְרוּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה! אֲבוֹתֵיכֶם שֶׁחָטְאוּ הֵיכָן הֵם? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וּנְבִיאֵיכֶם שֶׁלֹּא חָטְאוּ הֵיכָן הֵם? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֲבוֹתֵיכֶם אַיֵּה הֵם וְהַנְּבִאִים הַלְעוֹלָם יִחְיוּ״. אָמַר לָהֶן: אֲבוֹתֵיכֶם חָזְרוּ וְהוֹדוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אַךְ דְּבָרַי וְחֻקַּי אֲשֶׁר צִוִּיתִי אֶת עֲבָדַי הַנְּבִיאִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to the verse: "Why is this people of Jerusalem slid back in perpetual backsliding?"** (Jeremiah 8:5), **Rav says: The congregation of Israel answered** with **a convincing response to the prophet. The prophet said to the Jewish people: Repent,** as **your ancestors sinned,** and **where are they? They said** to the prophets: **And your prophets who did not sin, where are they?** They too died, **as it is stated: "Your fathers, where are they, and the prophets; do they live forever?"** (Zechariah 1:5). The prophet **said to** the Jewish people: **Your ancestors reconsidered and conceded** that the admonitions of the prophets were fulfilled, **as it is stated: "By my words and My statutes, which I commanded My servants the prophets,** did they not overtake your fathers? And they repented and said: As the Lord of hosts intended to do to us, according to our ways, and according to our doings, so has He dealt with us" (Zechariah 1:6).

The *Gemara* now shifts from the kings to the people themselves, introducing a verse: *Madua shovevah ha'am hazeh Yerushalayim meshuvah nitzachat* — why does this people persistently rebel, a rebellion that is *nitzachat*, enduring. Rav reads *shovevah* as referring to a *teshuvah* — not repentance, but a response — and *nitzachat* as meaning devastating, unanswerable. His claim is that the people of Israel responded to the prophet with a *teshuvah nitzachat*, a reply so powerful that it could not be answered.

### Fathers & Prophets

What was that response? Rav explains: the prophet said to Israel, *chizru b'teshuvah* — repent. He then argued: *avoseichem shechatu heichan heim* — your fathers who sinned, where are they now? They are dead. You don't want to share their fate, so repent. The people replied: *u'nevi'eichem shelo chatu heichan heim* — and your friends the prophets who did not sin, where are they? Also dead. If everyone dies regardless, that argument gives us no particular reason to repent.

The *Gemara* cites the verse: *avoseichem ayeih heim vehanevi'im haleolam yichyu* — your fathers, where are they? And the prophets, do they live forever? The prophet's rejoinder is either that even the prophets are mortal, yet God's word endures and the people should acknowledge that — or, alternatively, that at least your forefathers, when rebuked, admitted their wrongdoing and returned. As it says: *ach devarai vechukai asher tzivisi es avadai hanevi'im* — even My words which I commanded through My servants the prophets were eventually heeded, the people admitted their sin. One reading of this ends with Israel doing *teshuvah* — which, of course, would make it a less than fully devastating response. The tension in these readings is left unresolved.

### Slave Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: "בָּאוּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם וְיָשְׁבוּ לְפָנָיו. אָמַר לָהֶן: חִזְרוּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: עֶבֶד שֶׁמְּכָרוֹ רַבּוֹ, וְאִשָּׁה שֶׁגֵּרְשָׁהּ בַּעְלָהּ, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ לָזֶה עַל זֶה כְּלוּם? אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַנָּבִיא: לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶן: "אֵי זֶה סֵפֶר כְּרִיתוּת אִמְּכֶם אֲשֶׁר שִׁלַּחְתִּיהָ אוֹ מִי מִנּוֹשַׁי אֲשֶׁר מָכַרְתִּי אֶתְכֶם לוֹ הֵן בַּעֲוֹנוֹתֵיכֶם נִמְכַּרְתֶּם וּבְפִשְׁעֵיכֶם שֻׁלְּחָה אִמְּכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says** that this was the convincing answer: **Ten people came and sat before** the prophet Ezekiel. **He said to them: Repent. They said to** Ezekiel: In the case of **a slave sold by his owner** to another master, **or a woman divorced by her husband, does this** person **have any** claim **upon that** person? Since God gave the Jewish people to other masters, the ties that existed between Him and us were severed. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the prophet: Go say to them: "Where is your mother's scroll of severance, with which I sent her away? Or to which of My creditors have I sold you? For your iniquities you sold yourselves and for your transgressions was your mother sent away"** (Isaiah 50:1). Learn from this that God did not sever His ties to the Jewish people.

Shmuel offers a different version of the *teshuvah nitzachat*. Ten people came and sat before the prophet. He told them to repent. They replied: *eved shemachro rabo v'ishah shegerashah ba'alah, klum yesh lazeh al zeh klum* — if a master sells his slave, or a husband divorces his wife, they have nothing further to do with one another. God has sent us into exile; God has, in effect, abandoned us. Do not tell us to repent — it is over. This indeed sounds like an unanswerable response.

### No Divorce

Yet the *Gemara* shows that it was not unanswerable — because God responded through the prophet Yeshayahu: *eizeh sefer kerisus imchem asher shillachtiyah* — show me the bill of divorce I sent your mother. *Umi minoshai asher macharti eschem lo* — who is the creditor to whom I sold you off? The answer: *hein ba'avonoseichem nimkartem uvifsha'eichem shullechah imchem* — it was through your own sins that you sold yourselves; it was through your own transgressions that your mother was sent away. God did not divorce Israel. Israel brought the exile upon itself — and therefore Israel, not God, holds the power to end it. The slave argument is thus refuted: you were never sold to anyone but your own sin; you can return.

## Nebuchadnezzar Drasha

### David & Nebuchadnezzar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "דָּוִד עַבְדִּי״, "נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר עַבְדִּי״? גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם שֶׁעֲתִידִין יִשְׂרָאֵל לוֹמַר כָּךְ, לְפִיכָךְ הִקְדִּים הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וּקְרָאוֹ עַבְדּוֹ. עֶבֶד שֶׁקָּנָה נְכָסִים – עֶבֶד לְמִי? נְכָסִים לְמִי?
>
> **Translation:** **And that is what Reish Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "David, My slave"** (II Samuel 3:18), and: **"Nebuchadnezzar, my slave"** (Jeremiah 43:10)? How can the wicked Nebuchadnezzar be depicted as a slave of God in the same manner that David was depicted? Rather, **it is revealed and known before the One Who spoke and the world came into being, that the Jewish people are destined to say that** God sold them to the nations and they no longer have ties to Him. **Therefore, the Holy One, Blessed be He, preemptively called** Nebuchadnezzar **His slave.** With regard to the *halakha* concerning **a slave who acquires property, the slave** belongs **to whom** and **the property** belongs **to whom?** They both belong to the master, in this case, the Holy One, Blessed be He.

Rav, citing Reish Lakish, raises a striking observation: the verse refers to both David and Nebuchadnezzar as God's *avdi* — My servant. What possible comparison is there between God's faithful servant David and the destroyer Nebuchadnezzar? The explanation is as follows: God foreknew that Israel would make the very argument about the slave being sold — that once God sent them into exile via Nebuchadnezzar, they would claim to be cut off from any further relationship with God. To preempt this, God designated Nebuchadnezzar as His own *eved*, His own slave.

### Acquired Property

The operative legal principle is: *eved shekanah nechasim, eved l'mi, nechasim l'mi* — if a slave acquires property, who owns the slave and who owns the property? The master owns both. By establishing Nebuchadnezzar as His slave, God ensured that whatever Nebuchadnezzar acquired — including the Israelites he took into captivity — belongs to God.

### Never Left Servitude

The implication is profound: you never left God's servitude. Even in Babylonian captivity, you remained God's people, because Nebuchadnezzar himself was God's slave and his possessions were God's possessions. The claim that exile constitutes a severance of the divine relationship is therefore untenable.

## Redemption & Kingship

### God's Anger Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וְהָעֹלָה עַל רוּחֲכֶם הָיוֹ לֹא תִהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם אֹמְרִים נִהְיֶה כַגּוֹיִם כְּמִשְׁפְּחוֹת הָאֲרָצוֹת לְשָׁרֵת עֵץ וָאָבֶן. חַי אָנִי נְאֻם ה׳ אֱלֹהִים אִם לֹא בְּיָד חֲזָקָה וּבִזְרוֹעַ נְטוּיָה וּבְחֵמָה שְׁפוּכָה אֶמְלוֹךְ עֲלֵיכֶם״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כֹּל כִּי הַאי רִיתְחָא לִירְתַּח רַחְמָנָא עֲלַן וְלִפְרוֹקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And what comes into your mind shall never come to be, that you say: We will be like the nations, like the families of the countries, to serve wood and stone. As I live, says the Lord God, surely with a mighty hand, and an outstretched arm, and with wrath poured out, will I rule over you"** (Ezekiel 20:32–33), **Rav Naḥman says: Let the Merciful One become wrathful at us** with **all that wrath, and redeem us.**

Another verse addresses the people's resistance directly. They say: *nihyeh kagoyim kemishpachot ha'aratzot lisharet etz va'even* — let us be like the nations, like the families of the lands, worshipping wood and stone. God responds through Yechezkel: *chai ani neum Hashem Elokim, im lo b'yad chazakah uvizro'a netuyah uvicheimah shefuchah emloch aleichem* — as I live, says the Lord God, even if it requires a powerful hand, an outstretched arm, and outpoured fiery wrath, I will reign over you. There is no escaping the divine relationship, no matter how much Israel resists.

### Accept Anger

Rav Nachman responds to this verse with a striking declaration: *ko ki hai ritcha lirtach Rachmana alan, dilfarkinan, velimloch aleichan* — let God pour out all that anger upon us, let us endure all of it, as long as in the end He redeems us and reigns over us. This is a remarkable contrast to the view mentioned earlier in the *masechta* — that some *rabbanim* preferred not to live in the days of *yemot haMashiach* because they feared the suffering of *chevlei Mashiach*. Rav Nachman takes the opposite position: whatever divine anger may come, redemption makes it worthwhile.

## Yetzer Hara Claim

### Evil Inclination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וְיִסְּרוֹ לַמִּשְׁפָּט אֱלֹהָיו יוֹרֶנּוּ״ – אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: אָמַר לָהֶן נָבִיא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: חִזְרוּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין אָנוּ יְכוֹלִין, יֵצֶר הָרָע שׁוֹלֵט בָּנוּ. אָמַר לָהֶם: יַסְּרוּ יִצְרֵיכֶם! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: "אֱלֹהָיו יוֹרֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And chastise in judgment; his God will instruct him"** (Isaiah 28:26), **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **the prophet said to the Jewish people: Repent. They said to him: We cannot,** since **the evil inclination dominates us. He said to them: Chastise your inclinations. They said to him: "His God will instruct him,"** i.e., God should instruct the evil inclination to allow us to overcome him, as we are incapable of doing so on our own.

The *Gemara* presents yet another version of the people's response to prophetic rebuke. Rabbah bar bar Chana teaches: the prophet said to Israel, *chizru b'teshuvah*. They replied: *ein anu yocholim, yetzer hara sholet banu* — we cannot; the *yetzer hara* rules over us. We are simply unable to resist it.

### Deflect to God

The prophet responded: *yisru yitzrechem* — subjugate your evil inclination, bring it under control. The people replied: *elohav yorenu* — let the God who created the *yetzer hara* teach us how to control it, and then we will do so. This final deflection — placing the responsibility for overcoming the *yetzer hara* back on God — serves as another instance of the people's resistance to *teshuvah*.

The discussion of the people's sinfulness here connects, thematically, to the broader discussion of the kings: the people, in a sense, get the leaders they deserve, and vice versa. The question also arises whether the *dorshei reshumot*'s inclusive approach — covering the kings and the *hedyotot* alike — reflects the general principle of *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba*. That line, it should be noted, was a later addition to the *Mishnah*, not original to it, and its normative status is itself debated. Urbach, among others, has argued about whether this principle represents the standard theology of *Chazal*. The standard view seems to be that *tzaddikim* receive *olam haba*, *resha'im* go to *Gehinnom*, and *beinonim* fall somewhere in between. Yet the very fact that the *Mishnah* lists only a small number of individuals who are excluded implies that the default assumption is inclusion. The *dorshei reshumot* push that inclusivity to its furthest limit — even the most wicked among Israel retain their portion.

## Four Commoners

### List of Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבָּעָה הֶדְיוֹטוֹת: בִּלְעָם, וְדוֹאֵג, וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, וְגֵחֲזִי. בִּלְעָם – בְּלֹא עָם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: בִּלְעָם – שֶׁבִּלָּה עָם. בֶּן בְּעוֹר – שֶׁבָּא עַל בְּעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **four** prominent **commoners, Balaam, Doeg, Ahithophel, and Gehazi,** have no share in the World-to-Come. The Gemara elaborates: The name **Balaam** is interpreted as a contraction of: **Without a nation [*belo am*],** or one who has no share in the World-to-Come with the Jewish nation. **Alternatively,** the name **Balaam** is interpreted as one **who wore down the** Jewish **people [*bila am*].** He is the **son of Beor,** one **who engaged in bestiality [*be'ir*].**

The *Mishnah* now turns to the four *hedyotot* — commoners — who have no portion in *olam haba*: Bilam, Doeg, Achitofel, and Geichazi. Note that the *derasha* of the *dorshei reshumot*, which we just encountered, included Doeg, Achitofel, and Geichazi but conspicuously omitted Bilam. This is deliberate: the principle of *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba* applies to Jews. For non-Jews, the standard is different — one must be among the *chasidei umot ha'olam* to merit *olam haba*, and that is precisely the discussion the *Gemara* is about to enter.

### Bilam Etymology

The *Gemara* now derives multiple meanings from the name *Bilam*. First: *b'lo am* — without a nation. Second, *davar acher*: *she'bala am* — who swallowed, wiped out, a nation. Third, he is identified as *Ben Be'or*, and the *Gemara* derives from *be'or* — *she'ba al be'ir* — that he, or his father, had intercourse with an animal. The *Gemara* will develop this point more explicitly later in connection with Bilam and his donkey.

### Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הוּא בְּעוֹר, הוּא כּוּשַׁן רִשְׁעָתַיִם, הוּא לָבָן הָאֲרַמִּי. בְּעוֹר – שֶׁבָּא עַל בְּעִיר. כּוּשַׁן רִשְׁעָתַיִם – דַּעֲבַד שְׁתֵּי רִשְׁעָיוֹת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל: אַחַת בִּימֵי יַעֲקֹב, וְאַחַת בִּימֵי שְׁפוֹט הַשּׁוֹפְטִים. וּמָה שְׁמוֹ? לָבָן הָאֲרַמִּי שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught** in a *baraita*: **He is Beor,** father of Balaam, **he is Cushan-Rishathaim, he is Laban the Aramean.** He was called **Beor because he engaged in bestiality.** He was called **Cushan-Rishathaim because he performed two evil deeds [*rishiyyot*] to the Jewish people, one during the time of Jacob,** when he pursued him intending to kill him, **and one during the time when the judges judged. And what was his** actual **name? His name was Laban the Aramean.**

The *Gemara* then presents the tradition that Be'or, Bilam's father, is the same person as Cushan-Rishathaim, who is in turn the same person as Lavan Ha-Arami. One individual with multiple names, living across an extraordinarily long span of history — from the era of Yaakov through the period of the *Shofetim*. Why the multiple names? *She'ba al be'ir* — the name Be'or reflects his act with an animal. *Cushan-Rishathaim d'avad shtei risha'os b'Yisrael* — Cushan-Rishathaim, whose name encodes "two evils against Israel": one in the days of Yaakov, when as Lavan he sought to destroy Yaakov and thereby wipe out the Jewish people in its infancy, and once in the period of the *Shofetim*, when he oppressed Israel. Bilam himself would constitute a third attempt. *U'ma shemo? Lavan Ha-Arami shemo* — his true name is Lavan Ha-Arami. The *Gemara* seems to be connecting Bilam's lineage to a pattern of implacable enmity toward the Jewish people across generations.

### Father & Son Dynamics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: "בֶּן בְּעוֹר״, וּכְתִיב: "בְּנוֹ בְעֹר״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָבִיו בְּנוֹ הוּא לוֹ בִּנְבִיאוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is written: "Son of Beor"** (Numbers 22:5), **and it is written** elsewhere: **"His son Beor"** (Numbers 24:3). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in resolving the apparent contradiction: Balaam's **father was his son in** terms of **prophecy,** as Balaam was a much greater prophet.

The *pasuk* itself uses both *Ben Be'or* and *b'no Be'or* — one reading meaning "son of Be'or," the other potentially meaning "Be'or is his son." Rabbi Yochanan explains: when it says *b'no*, it means that in the realm of prophecy, Be'or was *Bilam's* subordinate — Bilam's junior. His father was, as it were, a lesser prophet than he was.

## Gentiles & Olam HaBa

### Bilam Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּלְעָם הוּא דְּלָא אָתֵי לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי, הָא אַחֲרִינֵי אָתוּ. מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from the mishna: **Balaam is** the **one who does not come into the World-to-Come; but other** gentiles **come** into the World-to-Come. **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?**

The *Gemara* makes an observation from the *Mishnah*'s singling out of Bilam: *Bilam hu d'lo asi l'alma d'asi* — it is Bilam specifically who does not come to *olam haba*. The implication is that *other* non-Jews do have a portion in *olam haba*. Which non-Jews? The question prompts a discussion of the *masnisin*: whose view does our *Mishnah* represent?

### Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, "יָשׁוּבוּ רְשָׁעִים לִשְׁאוֹלָה כׇּל גּוֹיִם שְׁכֵחֵי אֱלֹהִים״. "יָשׁוּבוּ רְשָׁעִים לִשְׁאוֹלָה״ – אֵלּוּ פּוֹשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, "כׇּל גּוֹיִם שְׁכֵחֵי אֱלֹהִים״ – אֵלּוּ פּוֹשְׁעֵי גּוֹיִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר "בְּכׇל גּוֹיִם״? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא "כׇּל גּוֹיִם שְׁכֵחֵי אֱלֹהִים״! אֶלָּא "יָשׁוּבוּ רְשָׁעִים לִשְׁאוֹלָה״ – מַאן נִינְהוּ? "כׇּל גּוֹיִם שְׁכֵחֵי אֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** It is written: **"The wicked shall be turned back to the netherworld, all the nations that forget God"** (Psalms 9:18). **"The wicked shall be turned back to the netherworld"; these are the sinners of the Jewish people,** as only the sinners are sentenced to the netherworld. **"All the gentiles that forget God"; these are the sinners of the gentiles.** From the fact that it is written: "All the gentiles," it is apparent that none of the gentiles have a share in the World-to-Come. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: But is it stated** in the verse that the sinners of the Jewish people will be **like all of the gentiles? It is stated only: "All the gentiles that forget God." Rather, the wicked shall be turned back to the netherworld,** and **who are they?** They are **all the gentiles that forget God.** Gentiles who fear God do have a share in the World-to-Come.

The *Gemara* answers: it reflects the position of Rabbi Yehoshua, as opposed to Rabbi Eliezer. As the *braita* teaches: Rabbi Eliezer interprets the verse *yashuvu resha'im lish'olah kol goyim shochechei Elohim* — the wicked shall return to the underworld, all nations who forget God. *Yashuvu resha'im lish'olah* — these are the sinners of Israel. *Kol goyim shochechei Elohim* — these are the sinners among the nations. But his deeper *derasha* reads the verse as establishing an identity rather than a subset: by definition, *all* the nations are categorized as those who forget God. All gentiles, on this reading, are treated as *resha'im* — not merely the wicked among them, but the entire category.

### Yehoshua Position

Rabbi Yehoshua disputes this. His position is that *resha'im* refers only to the wicked — among both Israel and the nations — and that the qualifier *shochechei Elohim* narrows the verse to those specifically who forget God, not to all gentiles universally. From this, Rabbi Yehoshua concludes: *ha yesh tzaddikim b'ovdei kochavim sheyesh lahem chelek l'olam haba* — there are righteous people among the nations who do have a portion in *olam haba*. The debate, then, is whether *kol goyim shochechei Elohim* is read as an identity (*all* nations *are* forgetters of God) or as a restrictive clause (those *among* the nations who forget God are the ones excluded).

### Tosefta Clarity

The debate is considerably clearer in the *Tosefta* (*Perek* 13), and it is worth presenting that version. The *Tosefta* opens with a related but distinct question: *bnei rish'ei Yisrael ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the children of the wicked of Israel have no portion in *olam haba* — based on the verse *hinei yom ba bo'er katanur*, in the words of Rabban Gamliel. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees and says they do come to *olam haba*. His reasoning: first, *shomer peta'im Hashem* — God protects the simple, and these children were raised in wickedness through no fault of their own, a case akin to *tinok shenishba*. Second, he cites the verse from Daniel about cutting down the tree while leaving the roots — punish the parent, but allow the possibility of redemption to remain for the children.

Turning to the main debate: the *Tosefta* has Rabbi Eliezer saying: *kol rish'ei ovdei kochavim ein lahem chelek l'olam haba, shene'emar: yashuvu resha'im lish'olah — elu rish'ei Yisrael; kol goyim shochechei Elohim — elu rish'ei ovdei kochavim*. Rabbi Yehoshua responds with precision: had the verse said *yashuvu resha'im lish'olah, kol goyim* and then stopped, you would be right — all the nations are treated as wicked. But since the verse adds *shochechei Elohim* — those who forget God — it demonstrates that there are those among the nations who are not forgetters, and therefore: *ha yesh tzaddikim b'ovdei kochavim sheyesh lahem chelek l'olam haba*. The *Tosefta* thus lays out the structure of the argument with considerably more clarity than the *Gemara*'s version.

### Rambam Ruling

One might ask whether one can even *pasken* in matters of theology. As we saw, the *bat kol* itself declared that God decides who enters *olam haba*. Nevertheless, the Rambam rules that *chasidei umot ha'olam yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the righteous among the nations have a portion in *olam haba* — aligning with Rabbi Yehoshua's position. This ruling carries profound theological significance. It is a remarkable feature of Jewish thought that one need not be Jewish to merit *olam haba*. As far as one can tell, Judaism is the only major religion that explicitly provides a path to salvation — or its equivalent — for those outside the faith. Other traditions may be more inclusive in certain respects, but the notion of a divinely mandated code for non-Jews (*sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach*) that carries with it genuine eschatological standing is, to the best of our knowledge, unique.

### Sheva Mitzvot

This leads directly to the question of the *sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach*. If God gave the nations a binding set of laws, and if the fundamental system of divine reward and punishment is ultimately rooted in *olam haba*, then it would be strange indeed to say those laws carry no ultimate reward. The linkage between the *sheva mitzvos* and the *chasidei umot ha'olam*'s portion in *olam haba* would seem to follow naturally. How Rabbi Eliezer's position coheres with the existence of the *sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach* is genuinely difficult to understand.

The Rambam adds an important qualification: a non-Jew merits the status of *chasidei umot ha'olam* only if he observes the *sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach* because he believes God commanded them — specifically, because God commanded Moshe at Sinai that the earlier Noahide covenant remained binding. If he observes them purely out of his own rational judgment, without grounding them in divine command, the Rambam says *eino m'chasidei umot ha'olam*. Here, a famous textual dispute arises among the *acharonim*. Some versions of the Rambam read *v'lo m'chachameihem* — he is neither among the pious of the nations nor among their wise men. Other versions read *ela m'chachameihem* — he is not among the pious, but he is among their wise men. The difference is significant: the first version implies he has no standing at all; the second grants him the status of *chacham* even without the theological grounding. The Rambam does not explicitly state in either version that such a person lacks *olam haba*; that conclusion is implied but not stated directly.

### Radbaz View

Some *nosei keilim* of the Rambam were uncomfortable with the implication that non-Jews who observe only seven *mitzvos* might receive the same portion in *olam haba* as Jews who observe the full *taryag mitzvos* — a disparity in obligation with an apparently equal reward. The Radbaz addresses this directly, arguing that while the *chasidei umot ha'olam* do have a portion in *olam haba*, that portion is not equivalent in quality to the portion of Israel. It is a portion, but a lesser one. This formulation allows for both the inclusion of righteous gentiles and the preservation of a meaningful distinction corresponding to the difference in covenantal obligation.

## Bilam's Sign

### Righteous Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע נָתַן סִימָן בְּעַצְמוֹ. אָמַר: "תָּמֹת נַפְשִׁי מוֹת יְשָׁרִים״. אִם תָּמוֹת נַפְשִׁי מוֹת יְשָׁרִים – "תְּהֵא אַחֲרִיתִי כָּמוֹהוּ״, וְאִם לָאו – "הִנְנִי הוֹלֵךְ לְעַמִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And that wicked person,** Balaam, **also provided a sign with regard to himself. He said: "Let me die the death of the righteous,** and let my end be like his" (Numbers 23:10). **If I die the death of the righteous,** by natural causes, **my end will be like his,** i.e., I will receive a share in the World-to-Come like the Jewish people. **And if** I do **not** die by natural causes: **"I will go to my people"** (Numbers 24:14), i.e., my fate will be that of the rest of the wicked people in my generation, who have no share in the World-to-Come.

Returning to Bilam: *v'af oso rasha nasan siman b'atzmo* — even that *rasha* gave a sign by which to know whether he would receive *olam haba*. Bilam himself declared: *tamus nafshi mos yesharim vetehi achrisi kamohu* — let my soul die the death of the righteous, and let my end be like theirs. This statement contains his own criterion: if he were to die like the righteous, he would share their ultimate destiny and receive *olam haba*.

### Death in Battle

But Bilam did not die like the righteous. He died in battle, killed when Israel smote the Midianites. That violent death is itself the sign that he does not receive *olam haba*. His own words serve as the standard against which his fate is measured — and he falls short of it.

## Moav & Midyan

### Common Enemy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיֵּלְכוּ זִקְנֵי מוֹאָב וְזִקְנֵי מִדְיָן״. תָּנָא: מִדְיָן וּמוֹאָב לֹא הָיָה לָהֶם שָׁלוֹם מֵעוֹלָם. מָשָׁל לִשְׁנֵי כְּלָבִים שֶׁהָיוּ בָּעֵדֶר, וְהָיוּ צְהוּבִּין זֶה לָזֶה. בָּא זְאֵב עַל הָאֶחָד. אָמַר הָאֶחָד: אִם אֵינִי עוֹזְרוֹ, הַיּוֹם הוֹרֵג אוֹתוֹ, וּלְמָחָר בָּא עָלַי. הָלְכוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם וְהָרְגוּ הַזְּאֵב. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: כַּרְכּוּשְׁתָּא וְשׁוּנָּרָא עֲבַדוּ הִלּוּלָא מִתַּרְבָּא דְּבִישׁ גַּדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And the elders of Moab and the elders of Midian set out** with their divinations in their hands, and they came to Balaam" (Numbers 22:7), it was **taught** in a *baraita*: **Midian and Moab** had previously **never had peace** between them, and they were always at war with each other. What led them to make peace at that time? There is **a parable of two dogs that were with the flock, and they were hostile to one another. A wolf came** and attacked **one. The** other **one said: If I do not help him, today he kills him and tomorrow he comes to** attack **me. They both went and killed the wolf.** Moab and Midian joined together to face the potential common threat, the Jewish people. **Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: A weasel [*karkushta*] and a cat made a wedding from the fat of the luckless.** Despite their hatred of one another, they join together for their mutual benefit at the expense of a third party.

The *Gemara* now examines the background of the encounter between Balak and Bilam. The verse records: *vayelchu ziknei Moav v'ziknei Midyan* — the elders of Moav and the elders of Midyan went together. This is historically striking: *Midyan uMoav lo hayah lahem shalom me'olam* — Midyan and Moav had never had peaceful relations with one another. They were perpetual enemies. Yet here they cooperated. The reason, of course, is that they faced a common threat in Israel.

### Dog Parable

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a *mashal*: there were two dogs in a flock who were enraged at one another, *tzehuvim zeh lazeh* — their hackles raised against each other. A wolf came and attacked one of them. The first dog reasoned: if I do not help my fellow dog, the wolf will kill him today and come for me tomorrow. So they united and killed the wolf together. Here too, Balak was protecting his people of Moav, and they joined with the leaders of Midyan to address what they perceived as the threat of Israel. Rav Pappa adds the folk saying: *karkushta v'shunra* — the weasel and the cat, sworn enemies, *avdu hilula mitarva d'vish gada* — made a feast together from the fat of one who suffered misfortune. Common interest bridges even the deepest enmity.

### Midianite Officers Depart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אוֹתוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי בִּלְעָם וְזִקְנֵי מוֹאָב וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת הַקּוֹל שֶׁל וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ וְנִשְׁמְרוּ כִּי אָמַר כִּי הָאֱלֹהִים אִם אֵינִי עוֹשֶׂה לָהֶם צַדִּיק לָא עַם שִׁקְּר וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אוֹתוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי בִּלְעָם וְזִקְנֵי מוֹאָב וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת הַקּוֹל שֶׁל וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ וְנִשְׁמְרוּ כִּי אָמַר כִּי הָאֱלֹהִים אִם אֵינִי עוֹשֶׂה לָהֶם צַדִּיק לָא עַם שִׁקְּר כִּי הָעַם הַזֶּה הִיא עַם בִּרְכָתִי״. אֶלָּא אָמַר לָהֶם בִּלְעָם: ״לִינוּ פֹה הַלַּיְלָה וַהֲשִׁיבֹתִי אֶתְכֶם דָּבָר״, אָמְרוּ: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ אָב שֶׁשּׂוֹנֵא אֶת בְּנוֹ?

They recognized that the God of Israel is Israel's father, and concluded that God would never agree to curse His own people. Seeing that any appeal to Bilam was therefore pointless, they left.

### Chutzpah and Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: חוּצְפָּא אֲפִילּוּ כְּלַפֵּי שְׁמַיָּא מַהֲנֵי. מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב: "לֹא תֵלֵךְ עִמָּהֶם״, וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּתִיב: "קוּם לֵךְ אִתָּם״. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: חוּצְפָּא מַלְכוּתָא בְּלָא תָּאגָא הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: "וְאָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם רַךְ וּמָשׁוּחַ מֶלֶךְ וְהָאֲנָשִׁים הָאֵלֶּה בְּנֵי צְרוּיָה קָשִׁים מִמֶּנִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** This prompts Rav Nachman to observe: *chutzpah afilu klapei shamaya mahani* — brazenness works even toward Heaven. For at first, God told Bilam *lo teleich imahem* — do not go with them. Yet Bilam kept pressing, and ultimately God said *kum leich itam* — arise and go with them. The *chutzpah* of his persistence, whether directed at the officers of Moav by asking them to remain another night in hopes of a different divine response, or directed at God Himself by daring to ask again after being refused, produced results. Rashi connects the *chutzpah* to Bilam's invitation to the officers to stay, suggesting it was bold of him to imply he might obtain a different answer; on a straightforward reading, the *chutzpah* was simply in approaching God again after a clear refusal.

### Rav Sheshet and Kingship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: חוּצְפָּא מַלְכוּתָא בְּלָא תָּאגָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: Impudence is monarchy without a crown,** as it is an assertion of leadership and lacks only the official coronation as king.

Rav Sheshet adds a related observation: *chutzpah malchusa bela taga* — *chutzpah* is like kingship without a crown. Boldness confers informal power exceeding one's formal standing. David himself acknowledged this: *va'anochi hayom rach umishuach melech v'ha'anashim ha'eileh bnei Tzeruyah kashim mimeni* — I am today a young anointed king, and the sons of Tzeruyah are more powerful than I. Even David, the anointed king, found himself constrained by their audacity.

## Bilam's Flaws

### Lameness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּלְעָם חִיגֵּר בְּרַגְלוֹ אַחַת הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֵּלֶךְ שֶׁפִי״. שִׁמְשׁוֹן בִּשְׁתֵּי רַגְלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "שְׁפִיפֹן עֲלֵי אֹרַח הַנּוֹשֵׁךְ עִקְּבֵי סוּס״. בִּלְעָם סוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "שְׁתֻם הָעָיִן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Balaam was disabled in one of his legs, as it is stated** concerning him: **"And he went limping [*shefi*]"** (Numbers 23:3). **Samson** was disabled **in both his legs, as it is stated** with regard to Samson, who was from the tribe of Dan, in the prophetic blessing of Jacob: "Dan shall be a serpent by the way, **an adder [*shefifon*] in the path that bites the horse's heels"** (Genesis 49:17). Rabbi Yoḥanan interprets *shefifon* as the plural of *shefi*, indicating disability in both legs. **Balaam was blind in one of his eyes, as it is stated: "Whose eye is open"** (Numbers 24:3), indicating that one eye was open and the other was blind.

Rabbi Yochanan enumerates several physical and moral deficiencies of Bilam. He was lame in one leg — *chiga b'raglo achas haya* — as derived from *vayelech shefi*, where Rashi reads *shefi* as indicating a displaced, dislocated limb. By contrast, Shimshon was lame in both legs, derived from the double language of *shefifon alei orach*, referring to a descendant of Dan who bit horses' heels: the doubled root indicates lameness in both legs.

### Blindness

Bilam was also blind in one eye — *suma b'achas me'einav haya* — as derived from his self-description *sh'sum ha'ayin*. Rashi reads the word *sh'sum* as meaning "open" in one eye, which by implication means the other is closed. The derivation is unusual, but the conclusion is that Bilam's vision was impaired in one eye.

### Necromancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹסֵם בְּאַמָּתוֹ הָיָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: "נֹפֵל וּגְלוּי עֵינָיִם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: "וְהָמָן נֹפֵל עַל הַמִּטָּה וְגוֹ׳״. אִיתְּמַר, מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: קוֹסֵם בְּאַמָּתוֹ הָיָה. מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא אָמַר: שֶׁבָּא עַל אֲתוֹנוֹ. מַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹסֵם בְּאַמָּתוֹ הָיָה – כְּדַאֲמַרַן, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּא עַל אֲתוֹנוֹ הָיָה – כְּתִיב הָכָא: "כָּרַע שָׁכַב״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: "בֵּין רַגְלֶיהָ כָּרַע נָפַל שָׁכָב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: Balaam **was a diviner by** using **his penis. It is written here: "Fallen, yet with opened eyes"** (Numbers 24:4), **and it is written there: "And Haman was fallen upon the divan** whereupon Esther was" (Esther 7:8), indicating that the verb fallen has sexual connotations. **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to this matter. **Mar Zutra says:** Balaam **was a diviner by** using **his penis. Mar, son of Ravina, says: He engaged in bestiality with his donkey. The one who says** that he **was a diviner by** using **his penis** derives it **as we stated. And the one who says** that **he engaged in bestiality with his donkey** derives it as follows: **It is written here: "He crouched, he lay down"** (Numbers 24:9), **and it is written there: "Between her legs he sunk, he fell, he lay"** (Judges 5:27), which is interpreted as a reference to sexual intercourse between Sisera and Jael.

He also engaged in a form of *kosem b'amato* — necromancy involving his genitalia — derived from the phrase *nofel v'galui einayim*, which is compared to the verse about Haman's fall: *hineh Haman nofel al hamita asher Ester aleha*, where falling clearly carries sexual connotations. There is a dispute between Mar Zutra and Mar bar Ravina: Mar Zutra says the first interpretation stands — Bilam practiced necromancy in a sexually deviant manner. Mar bar Ravina says: rather, *sheba al atono* — he actually had intercourse with his donkey. Each interpretation uses a different comparative verse. Those who hold *ba al atono* derive it from the language of *kara shachav k'ari* in Bilam's prophecy, compared to *bein ragleha kara nafal shachav* in the episode of Yael and Sisera, where the language is clearly sexually charged.

## Donkey Argument

### God Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וְיֹדֵעַ דַּעַת עֶלְיוֹן״. הַשְׁתָּא דַּעַת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ לָא הֲוָה יָדַע, דַּעַת עֶלְיוֹן הֲוָה יָדַע? מַאי דַּעַת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ? דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא רָכְבַתְּ סוּסְיָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׁדַאי לֵהּוֹ בִּרְטִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § Balaam describes himself: **"And he knows the knowledge of the Most High"** (Numbers 24:16), and the Gemara asks: **Now,** if **the knowledge of his animal he did not know,** is it possible **that the knowledge of the Most High he knew?** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of **the knowledge of his animal?** The princes accompanying him **said to him: What is the reason that you did not ride horses?** Balaam **said to them: I brought** the horses to graze **in the pasture** and rest there.

This leads into one of the more striking passages of the *daf*. Bilam describes himself as *v'yodea da'as elyon* — one who knows the thoughts of the Most High. The *Gemara* responds with biting irony: *hashta da'as behemto lo hava yada, da'as elyon hava yada?* — he did not even know the mind of his own animal, yet he claims to know the mind of God? Bilam could not see the angel standing before him, which his donkey could clearly perceive. But the *Gemara* takes this a step further: Bilam was not merely outclassed perceptually. He was actually bested in argumentation by his donkey.

### Constant Companion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: "הֲלֹא אָנֹכִי אֲתֹנְךָ״! לִטְעִינָא בְּעָלְמָא. "אֲשֶׁר רָכַבְתָּ עָלַי״! אַקְרַאי בְּעָלְמָא. "מֵעוֹדְךָ עַד הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״!
>
> **Translation:** The donkey **said to him: "Am I not your donkey"** (Numbers 22:30)? Balaam said to the donkey: **Merely for burdens,** not for riding. The donkey said to him: **"Upon which you have ridden"** (Numbers 22:30). Balaam said to the donkey: That **was mere happenstance** and is not a regular occurrence. The donkey said to him: **"Your whole life until this day"** (Numbers 22:30).

The occasion for the argument was this: the officers of Moav asked Bilam why he was riding a donkey rather than a horse. Riding a horse would have been more dignified, more fitting for a person of his stature. Bilam replied that the donkey was practical — *d'shadei lehu birtiva*, it grazes on the wet grass of the swamp, so the cost of maintaining it is low. The donkey then spoke: *halo anochi asonecha* — am I not your donkey? You are using me today as if this is an ad hoc arrangement, but I am your regular mount. Bilam countered: I use you only for carrying burdens, not for riding. The donkey replied: *asher rachavta alai* — no, I am the one you always ride. Bilam dismissed this: *akrai b'alma* — occasionally, not regularly. The donkey rejected this too: *me'odecha ad hayom hazeh* — from your very beginning until today, I have always been your mount.

### Spousal Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֲנִי עוֹשָׂה [לְךָ] מַעֲשֵׂה אִישׁוּת בַּלַּיְלָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: "הַהַסְכֵּן הִסְכַּנְתִּי״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: "וַתְּהִי לוֹ סֹכֶנֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** The donkey continued: **Moreover, I perform a conjugal act for you** and we engage in bestiality **at night.** From where is this derived? **It is written here: "Was I ever wont [*hahasken hiskanti*]** to do so to you" (Numbers 22:30)? **And it is written there** with regard to Abishag the Shunammite: **"And let her be a companion [*sokhenet*] to him"** (I Kings 1:2). Just as the root *samekh*, *kaf*, *nun* indicates sexual relations in the case of Abishag, the same is true with regard to the donkey. Balaam was unable to best his donkey in a debate.

The donkey then raises the stakes entirely: *v'lo od ella she'ani osa lach ma'aseh ishus balailah* — not only that, but I perform spousal acts for you at night. The word *hasken haskanti* — have I ever treated you this way before — is compared to *v'tehi lo sochenes*, the description of Avishag who kept David warm at night, reading *sochenot* as denoting intimate service.

### Outsmarts

The point of the entire exchange is pointed: if Bilam cannot hold his own in an argument with his donkey — a donkey that knows his history, his habits, and his most intimate arrangements better than he does — how plausible is his claim to know the thoughts of the Most High?

## Cursing Moment

### When Angry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי "וְיֹדֵעַ דַּעַת עֶלְיוֹן״? שֶׁהָיָה יוֹדֵעַ לְכַוֵּן אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כּוֹעֵס בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what,** then, is the meaning of the phrase **"And he knows knowledge of the Most High"?** Is it possible that he knew the knowledge of the Most High? Rather, **he would know to determine the moment during which the Holy One, Blessed be He, is angry.** He would curse at that moment and the curse was effective.

What, then, was the genuine power Bilam possessed? The *Gemara* explains: *shehaya yodea l'chaven osa sha'a she'haKadosh Baruch Hu ko'es bah* — he knew how to time his action precisely to the moment in each day when God becomes angry. Had he managed to deliver his curse at that precise moment, it would have been effective.

### Shittim-Gilgal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ נָבִיא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: "עַמִּי זְכָר נָא מַה יָּעַץ בָּלָק מֶלֶךְ מוֹאָב וּמֶה עָנָה אֹתוֹ בִּלְעָם בֶּן בְּעוֹר מִן הַשִּׁטִּים עַד הַגִּלְגָּל לְמַעַן דַּעַת צִדְקוֹת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is what the prophet said to Israel: "My nation, remember what Balak, king of Moab, advised, and how Bilaam, son of Beor, responded; from Shittim to Gilgal, so that you may know the righteous acts of the Lord"** (Micah 6:5).

This is the background for the prophet's reminder to Israel: *ami zachor na mah ya'atz Balak melech Moav, umah ana oso Bilam ben Be'or min haShittim ad haGilgal* — My people, remember what Balak king of Moav schemed, and what Bilam son of Be'or answered him, from Shittim to Gilgal, *l'maan da'as tzidkos Hashem* — so that you may know the righteous acts of God.

### Avoided Anger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "לְמַעַן דַּעַת צִדְקוֹת ה׳״? אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: דְּעוּ נָא כַּמָּה צְדָקוֹת עָשִׂיתִי עִמָּכֶם, שֶׁלֹּא כָּעַסְתִּי כׇּל אוֹתָן הַיָּמִים בִּימֵי בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵא כָּעַסְתִּי כׇּל אוֹתָן הַיָּמִים לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מִשּׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שָׂרִיד וּפָלִיט. הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ בִּלְעָם לְבָלָק: "מָה אֶקֹּב לֹא קַבֹּה אֵל וְגוֹ׳״ (אוֹתָן הַיּוֹם לֹא זָעַם ה׳).
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"So that you may know the righteous acts of the Lord"? The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Israel: Know how many acts of kindness I performed on your behalf, that I did not become angry** during **all the days of Bilaam the wicked, as had I become angry** during **all those days, no remnant or refugee would have remained among the enemies of Israel,** a euphemism for Israel itself. Instead, God restrained His anger and Bilaam's curse went unfulfilled. **That is what Balaam said to Balak: "How can I curse, whom God has not cursed?** And how can I condemn, whom God has not condemned?" (Numbers 23:8). Since God did not become angry, Balaam was unable to curse the Jewish people.

God says to Israel: know how great was My kindness to you — *shelo ka'asti kol otan hayamim* — throughout all those days, from Shittim to Gilgal, I deliberately refrained from becoming angry, so that Bilam should have no opening through which to curse you. This is implied by Bilam's own words to Balak: *mah ekov lo kaboh E-l, umah za'am lo za'am Hashem* — how can I curse whom God has not cursed, and how can I be wrathful when God has not been wrathful? This is read as testimony that throughout that entire period, *lo za'am Hashem* — God did not become angry — removing any possibility for Bilam's curse to take effect. Bilam knew the mechanism, but God chose not to activate it.

### Three Hours

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "אֵל זֹעֵם בְּכׇל יוֹם״, וְכַמָּה זַעְמוֹ? רֶגַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי רֶגַע בְּאַפּוֹ חַיִּים בִּרְצוֹנוֹ״. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: "לֵךְ עַמִּי בֹּא בַחֲדָרֶיךָ וּסְגֹר דְּלָתְךָ בַּעֲדֶךָ חֲבִי כִמְעַט רֶגַע עַד יַעֲבׇר זָעַם״. אֵימַת רָתַח? בִּתְלָת שָׁעֵי קַמָּיָיתָא, כִּי חָוְורָא כַּרְבָּלְתָּא דְּתַרְנְגוֹלָא. כֹּל שַׁעְתָּא וְשַׁעְתָּא נָמֵי חָוְורָא! כֹּל שַׁעְתָּא וְשַׁעְתָּא אִית בֵּיהּ סוּרְיָקֵי סוּמָּקֵי, הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא לֵית בֵּיהּ סוּרְיָקֵי סוּמָּקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: "And **God is angry every day"** (Psalms 7:12). **And how long is** the duration of **His wrath?** It is **one moment, as it is stated: "For His anger endures but a moment; His favor is for a lifetime"** (Psalms 30:6). **If you wish, say** instead this proof from another source, as it is stated: **"Come, my people, enter you into your chambers, and shut your doors about you; hide yourself for a brief moment, until the anger passes"** (Isaiah 26:20), meaning that God's anger passes in a mere moment. And **when is** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **angry?** It is **during the first three hours** of the day, **when the crest of the rooster is white** from the sun. The Gemara challenges: **Each and every hour** of the day the rooster's crest **is also white,** as it does not always remain red. The Gemara explains: The difference is that during **each and every** other **hour** when the rooster stands that way, **there are red streaks in** his crest. But **at that moment** when God is angry, **there are no red streaks in** the rooster's crest.

The general principle is that God does experience *za'am* — wrath — each day, but only for a moment: *ki rega b'apo chayim birtzono*. Bilam's unique skill was knowing precisely which moment that was. The verse *lech ami bo b'chadrecha, sgor d'latecha ba'adecha, chavi k'mat rega, ad ya'avor za'am* — go, My people, enter your chambers, close your doors, hide for a brief moment, until the wrath passes — also alludes to this fleeting moment of divine anger.

When exactly does God become angry? The *Gemara* specifies: during the first three hours of the day — from dawn until approximately mid-morning — when *k'chivra d'charbalta d'tarnagolta*, the comb of the rooster turns white. The *Gemara* clarifies that the rooster's comb is ordinarily white but has red streaks throughout most of the day. During that brief window, *leit bei sarki sumki* — the red streaks disappear entirely, and the comb is pure white. That is the moment.

### Rooster Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִינָא דַּהֲוָה בְּשִׁיבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דַּהֲוָה קָא מְצַעַר לֵיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד נְקַט תַּרְנוּגְלָא, וַאֲסַר לֵיהּ בְּכַרְעֵיהּ, וְאוֹתֵיב. אָמַר: כִּי מְטָא הָהוּא שַׁעְתָּא – אֶילְטְיֵיהּ. כִּי מְטָא הָהוּא שַׁעְתָּא, נַמְנֵם. אֲמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא, דִּכְתִיב "גַּם עֲנוֹשׁ לַצַּדִּיק לֹא טוֹב״. אֲפִילּוּ בְּמִינֵי לָא אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain heretic who was in Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's neighborhood who would upset him** by incessantly challenging the legitimacy of biblical verses. **One day,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **took a rooster and tied it by its legs and sat** and waited. **He said: When that moment** of God's wrath **arrives, I will curse him. When that moment** of God's wrath **arrived,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **dozed off.** When he awakened, **he said: Conclude from** the fact that I dozed off that it is **not proper conduct** to curse even the wicked, **as it is written: "Punishment, even for the righteous, is not good"** (Proverbs 17:26). **Even with regard to heretics,** a righteous person **should not state** a curse in order to punish them.

This leads to a story about a *mina* — a heretic or sectarian — who lived in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and caused him great anguish. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi decided he would use that propitious moment to curse the man. He took a rooster, tied it to his bedpost by its legs, and sat watching for that precise moment of pure white. The *Gemara* notes, with something close to rueful humor: a watched rooster, it seems, never turns white. When that moment arrived, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi had dozed off. When he awoke, he concluded: *shma mina lav orach ara* — it is clearly not appropriate behavior. *Ktiv gam anosh l'tzaddik lo tov* — punishing another, even a *min*, is not fitting for a righteous person. *Afilu b'minei lo iba'i leh l'meimar hachi* — even against heretics one should not resort to cursing. The entire exercise led him to a moral conclusion rather than a curse.

There is a broader question lurking here: is the whole framework — that God becomes angry at a specific moment, and that Bilam knew how to exploit it — mechanistic? Does the one who knows the moment effectively compel a divine response? The answer, as the *Gemara* makes clear, is no. God chose not to become angry precisely because He foresaw the danger. *Olam keminhago noheg* — the world operates according to its nature — but God retains the capacity to override that nature. Bilam knew the mechanism; God simply chose not to engage it.

## Love & Hatred

### Love Breaks Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: אַהֲבָה מְבַטֶּלֶת שׁוּרָה שֶׁל גְּדוּלָּה מֵאַבְרָהָם, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיַּשְׁכֵּם אַבְרָהָם בַּבֹּקֶר״. שִׂנְאָה מְבַטֶּלֶת שׁוּרָה שֶׁל גְּדוּלָּה מִבִּלְעָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיָּקׇם בִּלְעָם בַּבֹּקֶר וַיַּחֲבֹשׁ אֶת אֲתֹנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated: **"And Balaam rose early in the morning and saddled his donkey"** (Numbers 22:21). **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: Love negates the standard** conduct **of** those of **prominence.** This is derived **from Abraham, as it is written: "And Abraham rose early in the morning** and saddled his donkey" (Genesis 22:3). Atypically, he saddled the donkey himself and he did not wait for his servants. Likewise, **hatred negates the standard** conduct **of** those of **prominence.** This is derived **from Balaam, as it is stated: "And Balaam rose early in the morning and saddled his donkey"** (Numbers 22:21).

The *Gemara* teaches in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *ahavah mevateles shurah shel gedolah* — exceeding love destroys the normal boundaries of conduct expected of people of stature. A great person will normally delegate tasks beneath his dignity to servants or attendants. But when love overrides judgment, those boundaries dissolve. The proof is from Avraham: *vayashkem Avraham baboker* — Avraham rose early in the morning himself, and personally saddled his donkey for the *Akeidah*, an act he would normally have delegated. Whether motivated by love of God or by love of Yitzchak, the force of the emotion caused him to bypass the normal conventions of his station.

### Hatred Breaks Bounds

Symmetrically: *sin'ah mevateles shurah shel gedolah* — hatred equally causes people of stature to cross normal behavioral boundaries. The proof is from Bilam: *vayakam Bilam baboker vayachavosh es atono* — Bilam rose in the morning and saddled his own donkey. Bilam would not ordinarily saddle his own animal; his hatred of Israel was so consuming that he eagerly did so himself, without waiting for his servants.

## Balak's Merit

### Wrong Motivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יַעֲסוֹק אָדָם בְּתוֹרָה וּבְמִצְוָה, אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ בָּא לִשְׁמָהּ. שֶׁבִּשְׂכַר אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁתַּיִם קׇרְבָּנוֹת שֶׁהִקְרִיב בָּלָק, זָכָה וְיָצְאָה מִמֶּנּוּ רוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person should always engage in Torah** study **and** performance of **a mitzva even** if he does **not** do so **for their own sake, as through** engaging in them **not for their** own **sake,** he will ultimately **come** to engage in them **for their** own **sake.** Proof for this can be cited from the example of Balak, **as in reward for the forty-two offerings that Balak sacrificed,** even though he sacrificed them to facilitate the destruction of the Jewish people, **he was privileged and Ruth descended from him.**

The *Gemara* now introduces the principle of *shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — doing something for the wrong reason can ultimately lead to doing it for the right reason, or at least to producing good. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav states: a person should always engage in Torah and *mitzvos*, even *shelo lishmah*, even for impure or extrinsic motives, *shemitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — because from doing it for the wrong reason, he will ultimately come to do it for the right reason.

### Forty-Two Sacrifices

The proof is Balak. Balak brought forty-two sacrifices — seven bulls and seven rams per occasion, across three occasions, totaling forty-two — for the explicit purpose of enabling Bilam to curse Israel. This is almost the inverse of *shelo lishmah*: not merely a less-than-ideal motive, but an actively malevolent one. Yet he was offering these sacrifices to God, and there is an assumption that even the most corrupt motivation behind a genuine religious act leaves some residue of merit. The question of what exactly made these sacrifices a *mitzvah* — given that they were offered outside the *Mishkan*, constituting *chutz* — is complicated, but the *Gemara* assumes some form of divine service was involved.

### Ruth's Origin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר הוּנָא: רוּת בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל עֶגְלוֹן, בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל בָּלָק מֶלֶךְ מוֹאָב הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Huna says: Ruth was the daughter of Eglon, son of the son of Balak, king of Moab.**

By the merit of those forty-two sacrifices, *vayatsa mimenu Rut* — Ruth emerged from Balak's lineage. Rabbi Yosi bar Chunya specifies the genealogy: Ruth was the daughter of Eglon, who was the grandson of Balak, king of Moav. The *ba lishmah* here operates in an unusual register: ordinarily the principle means that impure motivations, if channeled into genuine action, will eventually reform themselves into pure ones in the actor. Here, Balak himself never improved — but the merit he accumulated through the act generated a reward that materialized generations later in the form of Ruth, ancestress of David.

## King's Blessing

### Throne Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: כְּתִיב "יֵיטֵב אֱלֹהִים אֶת שֵׁם שְׁלֹמֹה מִשְּׁמֶךָ וִיגַדֵּל [אֶת] כִּסְאוֹ מִכִּסְאֶךָ״. אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמֵימְרָא לֵיהּ לְמַלְכָּא הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵעֵין קָאָמְרָה לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Ruth, matriarch of the Davidic line, the Gemara cites that which **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: It is written** that the servants of David said to David: **"May God make the name of Solomon better than your name and make his throne greater than your throne [*mikisekha*]"** (I Kings 1:47). Rava asks: **Is it proper conduct to speak to a king in that manner** and say that his son will be greater than he is? Rabba bar Mari **said to** Rava: **She said to him:** May the throne of Solomon be **of the nature** of your throne.

Rava poses a question to Rabba bar Mari: the verse says *yeteiv Elokim es shem Shlomo mishemcha, viyagdel kiso mikisecha* — may God make the name of Shlomo good from your name, and make his throne greater than yours. This was said to David when he designated Shlomo as his successor. But is it proper etiquette to say to a king that his son's throne should exceed his own? That would seem to be a slight — implying that the father's kingship was somehow deficient.

### Derive From

The answer: *mishimcha* does not mean "greater than your name." It means *me'ein kamre leh* — deriving from your name, taking something of the greatness of your name as its source. May Shlomo's greatness be an extension of David's, not a surpassing of it.

### Yael Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, "תְּבֹרַךְ מִנָּשִׁים יָעֵל אֵשֶׁת חֶבֶר הַקֵּינִי מִנָּשִׁים בָּאֹהֶל תְּבֹרָךְ״. נָשִׁים בָּאֹהֶל מַאן נִינְהוּ? שָׂרָה, רִבְקָה, רָחֵל, וְלֵאָה. אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמֵימַר הָכִי? אֶלָּא מֵעֵין קָאָמַר. הָכִי נָמֵי, מֵעֵין קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: **As if you do not say so** and do not explain it in this manner, then the verse: **"Blessed above women Jael, wife of Heber the Kenite; blessed is she more than the women [*minashim*] in the tent"** (Judges 5:24), is difficult. **"The women in the tent," who are they?** They are **Sarah, Rebecca, Rachel, and Leah. Is it proper conduct to speak in that manner** about the Matriarchs? **Rather, of the nature** of the Matriarchs is what the verse **says,** not greater. **So too** in the case of David's servants: **Of the nature** of his throne, is what the verse **says.**

The *Gemara* proves this reading from a parallel case: *tivorach minashim Yael* — Yael should be blessed among women, *eshet Chever HaKeini, minashim ba'ohel tivorach* — from among the women of the tent she should be blessed. The *nashim ba'ohel* — the women of the tent — are identified as Sarah, Rivka, Rachel, and Leah, the matriarchs. Could one possibly mean that Yael is greater than the matriarchs? Clearly not. *Ela me'ein kamre* — the blessing means that her greatness derives in some way from theirs, that she is blessed in the manner of those great women. *Hacha nami me'ein kamre* — so too by Shlomo, the blessing means deriving from David's greatness, not exceeding it.

### No Jealousy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵי בַּר חוֹנִי, דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵי בַּר חוֹנִי: בַּכֹּל אָדָם מִתְקַנֵּא, חוּץ מִבְּנוֹ וְתַלְמִידוֹ. בְּנוֹ – מִשְּׁלֹמֹה, וְתַלְמִידוֹ – אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: "וִיהִי נָא פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּרוּחֲךָ אֵלָי״, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: "וַיִּסְמֹךְ אֶת יָדָיו עָלָיו וַיְצַוֵּהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This explanation **disagrees** with the opinion **of Rav Yosei bar Ḥoni, as Rav Yosei bar Ḥoni says: A person is jealous of everyone except for his son and his student.** Since their success reflects well upon him, he celebrates their success. The fact that one is not jealous of **his son** is derived **from** King David's servants telling him: May **Solomon** be greater than he is. **And** the fact that one is not jealous of **his student; if you wish, say** that it is derived from that which Elisha said to Elijah: **"I pray you that a double portion of your spirit be upon me"** (II Kings 2:9). He asked of his mentor to be greater than him, indicating that it is not an objectionable matter. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is derived from Moses: **"And he laid his hands upon him and commanded him"** (Numbers 27:23). God commanded Moses to lay only one hand upon Joshua, and instead he laid both hands upon him with all his strength.

The *Gemara* adds an observation from Rabbi Yose bar Chanina: *bechol adam mitkanei chutz mibno v'talmido* — a person is jealous of everyone except his son and his student. The absence of jealousy toward one's son is illustrated precisely from this episode: David's court could say "let Shlomo's throne be greater" without causing David offense, because a father naturally wants his son to surpass him. The same applies to a student: one genuinely wishes one's student to excel and surpass even oneself — as illustrated by Elisha asking for *pi shnayim b'ruchacha*, a double portion of Eliyahu's spirit, and by Moshe placing both hands on Yehoshua when God had only specified one — an act born of wholehearted generosity rather than reluctant compliance.

## Mouth Control

### Three Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיָּשֶׂם דָּבָר בְּפִי בִּלְעָם״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר – מַלְאָךְ, רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אָמַר – חַכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the verse: **"And the Lord placed a matter in Balaam's mouth"** (Numbers 23:5), **Rabbi Elazar says:** It was **an angel** that spoke from his mouth. **Rabbi Yonatan says:** It was **a hook** placed in his mouth to prevent him from saying anything else.

The *Gemara* returns to Bilam. *Vayasem davar b'fi Bilam* — and He placed a word in the mouth of Bilam. Three interpretations are offered. Rabbi Elazar says: *malach* — God sent an angel who accompanied Bilam to ensure that the words coming from his mouth were blessings rather than curses. Rabbi Yochanan, or Rabbi Yonatan, says: *chitah* — God placed a hook or bit in Bilam's mouth, physically dragging and controlling his speech the way a rider controls a horse with a bit.

### Prevent Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִבִּרְכָתוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע אַתָּה לָמֵד מָה הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ. בִּיקֵּשׁ לוֹמַר שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ לָהֶם בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת – ״מַה טֹּבוּ אֹהָלֶיךָ יַעֲקֹב״. לֹא תִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה עֲלֵיהֶם – ״וּמִשְׁכְּנֹתֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״. לֹא תְּהֵא מַלְכוּתָן נִמְשֶׁכֶת – ״כִּנְחָלִים נִטָּיוּ״. לֹא יְהֵא לָהֶם זֵיתִים וּכְרָמִים – ״כְּגַנֹּת עֲלֵי נָהָר״. לֹא יְהֵא רֵיחָן נוֹדֵף – ״כַּאֲהָלִים נָטַע ה׳״. לֹא יִהְיוּ לָהֶם מְלָכִים בַּעֲלֵי קוֹמָה – ״כַּאֲרָזִים עֲלֵי מַיִם״. לֹא יִהְיֶה לָהֶם מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ – ״יִזַּל מַיִם מִדָּלְיָו״. לֹא תְּהֵא מַלְכוּתָן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּאוּמּוֹת – ״וְזַרְעוֹ בְּמַיִם רַבִּים״. לֹא תְּהֵא עַזָּה מַלְכוּתָן – ״וְיָרֹם מֵאֲגַג מַלְכּוֹ״. לֹא תְּהֵא אֵימַת מַלְכוּתָן – ״וְתִנַּשֵּׂא מַלְכֻתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From the blessing of that wicked person,** Balaam, **you** can **ascertain what was in his heart.** God transformed the curses that he planned into blessings. **He sought to say that they should not have synagogues and study halls,** and he said instead: **"How goodly are your tents, Jacob"** (Numbers 24:5), a blessing on their synagogues. He sought to say **that the Divine Presence [*shekhina*] will not rest upon them,** and he said instead: **"And your dwellings [*mishkenot*] Israel."** He sought to say that **the kingdom of Israel would not continue,** and he said instead that it would continue: **"Like the winding brooks"** (Numbers 24:6), which flow continuously. He sought to say that **they would have no olive trees and vineyards,** and he said instead: **"Like gardens by the river's side"** (Numbers 24:6). He sought to say that **their fragrance would not diffuse** from their fulfillment of mitzvot, and he said instead: **"Like aloes that the Lord has planted"** (Numbers 24:6). He sought to say that **they would not have kings of stature,** and he said instead: **"Like cedar trees beside the waters"** (Numbers 24:6). He sought to say that **they will not have a king the son of a king,** and he said instead: **"Water shall flow from his branches"** (Numbers 24:7). He sought to say that **their kingdom would not rule over the nations,** and he said instead: **"And his seed shall be in many waters"** (Numbers 24:7). He sought to say that **their kingdom would not be fierce,** and he said instead: **"And his king shall be higher than Agag."** He sought to say **that there will be no fear of their kingdom,** and he said instead: **"And his kingdom shall be exalted"** (Numbers 24:7).

The purpose in either interpretation is the same: ensuring that Bilam could not curse Israel despite every intention to do so. Tomorrow's *daf* will develop this theme further, examining each of Bilam's blessings and showing how the very structure of the language reveals that Bilam was reaching for curses — and how God's control of his mouth transformed each intended curse into its opposite. This is, after all, what the *psukim* themselves say: *vayahafoch Hashem Elokecha lcha es hakelalah libracha* — God transformed the curse into a blessing, precisely because God loved Israel.
