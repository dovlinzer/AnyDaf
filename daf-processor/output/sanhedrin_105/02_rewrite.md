# Sanhedrin 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shlomo & Divine Voice

### Bat Kol & Shlomo

Today's *daf* is 105, picking up at the bottom of 104b. The discussion has centered on the list of kings who lack a portion in the *olam haba*, with the previous *daf* concluding on a remarkable note: the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* sought to include Shlomo among those kings, and God responded by sending fire from heaven accompanied by a *bat kol*. The dynamic here invites comparison with the famous story of the *tanur shel Achnai*, but with a crucial difference. That episode concluded with *lo bashamayim hi* — the authority to determine *halacha* rests with the rabbis, not with heavenly voices. Here, however, the resolution runs in the opposite direction.

### God Decides Olam HaBa

The *bat kol* declares: *hame'imcha yishalmena* — will compensation come from you? *Ki ma'asta* — you have chosen who to despise. *Ki ata tivchar, v'lo ani* — do you think you get to choose, and not I? The message is unmistakable: while the *rabbanim* derive their authority over *halacha* from *psukim* and *derasha*, the question of who enters *olam haba* is ultimately God's domain. The list the Sages compile is, in a meaningful sense, beside the point — God reserves that judgment for Himself. This answers a question that naturally arises: who authorized the *rabbanim* to determine who has a portion in *olam haba* or not?

### Tashlumin Interpretation

Rashi explains that the key word in the *bat kol* is *yishalmena*, which he connects not to *shalom* but to *tashlumin* — compensation. The question God poses is whether the rabbis are the ones who will compensate, that is, make good on these judgments. The answer is no. That authority belongs to God alone.

## Dorshei Reshumot

### Reject Exclusions

The *Dorshei Reshumot* — those who interpret *psukim* in the manner of *aggadata* — go even further than God's protest over Shlomo. Rather than merely objecting to a particular inclusion on the list, they reject the entire framework of exclusion. *Kulam ba'im l'olam haba* — everyone, even those wicked kings, receives a portion in *olam haba*. Their proof derives from a creative and remarkably ingenious *derasha* on a *pasuk* in Psalms.

### Psalm Interpretation

The *pasuk* reads: *li Gilead v'li Menashe, v'Ephraim ma'oz roshi, Yehuda mechokki, Moav sir rachtzi, al Edom ashlich na'ali, alai Pleshet hitro'ai* — to me is Gilead and to me is Menashe; Ephraim is the strength of my head; Yehuda is my scepter; Moav is the pot in which I will bathe; on Edom I will cast my shoes; upon the Philistines I will shout in triumph. Read simply, the *pasuk* contrasts God's chosen tribes of Israel with nations like Edom and Moav that are treated contemptuously. The *Dorshei Reshumot*, however, read it as a divine declaration of ownership and inclusion for a roster of sinners.

### Figure Identifications

The *derasha* proceeds as follows: *li Gilead* — to me is Gilead: *zeh Achav*, that is Achav, *she'nafal b'Ramot Gilead*, who fell at Ramot Gilead. Rashi explains that God is saying, in effect, I will carry his sin — he too will receive *olam haba*. *V'li Menashe k'mashma'o* — and to me is Menashe, taken literally as King Menashe rather than the tribe, meaning God has him covered as well. *Ephraim ma'oz roshi zeh Yerovam* — Yerovam, who comes from the tribe of Ephraim, is also included. *Yehuda mechokki zeh Achitofel* — Yehuda is my scepter, referring to Achitofel, who comes from Yehuda. Achitofel is one of the four commoners (*hedyotot*) who will be introduced shortly; this *derasha* already incorporates him.

### Commoners Included

*Moav sir rachtzi zeh Geichazi* — Moav is the pot in which I bathe, referring to Geichazi, *she'laka iskei rechitza*, whose entire episode with Na'aman centered on bathing. *Al Edom ashlich na'ali zeh Doeg HaEdomi* — on Edom I cast my shoes: the connection between *Edom* and *Edomi* is direct.

### Making Friends

The *pasuk* concludes: *alai Pleshet hitro'ai* — upon the Philistines I will triumph. What does this refer to? The angels before God ask: if David, who slew Goliath the Philistine and bequeathed the city of Gat to Israel's children, comes before You, what will You do for him, given that You are protecting Doeg — who informed against David to Shaul and brought about such tragedy? God replies: *alai la'asotan rei'im zeh la-zeh* — it is upon Me to make them friends with one another. Similarly, Achitofel and Doeg were enemies of David; God assures the angels He will reconcile them all. This is a beautiful and inventive *derasha*, managing to include every figure on the list of sinners within a single *pasuk* while resolving the apparent injustice to David at the same time.

## People & Prophets

### Teshuvah Nitzachat

The *Gemara* now shifts from the kings to the people themselves, introducing a verse: *Madua shovevah ha'am hazeh Yerushalayim meshuvah nitzachat* — why does this people persistently rebel, a rebellion that is *nitzachat*, enduring. Rav reads *shovevah* as referring to a *teshuvah* — not repentance, but a response — and *nitzachat* as meaning devastating, unanswerable. His claim is that the people of Israel responded to the prophet with a *teshuvah nitzachat*, a reply so powerful that it could not be answered.

### Fathers & Prophets

What was that response? Rav explains: the prophet said to Israel, *chizru b'teshuvah* — repent. He then argued: *avoseichem shechatu heichan heim* — your fathers who sinned, where are they now? They are dead. You don't want to share their fate, so repent. The people replied: *u'nevi'eichem shelo chatu heichan heim* — and your friends the prophets who did not sin, where are they? Also dead. If everyone dies regardless, that argument gives us no particular reason to repent.

The *Gemara* cites the verse: *avoseichem ayeih heim vehanevi'im haleolam yichyu* — your fathers, where are they? And the prophets, do they live forever? The prophet's rejoinder is either that even the prophets are mortal, yet God's word endures and the people should acknowledge that — or, alternatively, that at least your forefathers, when rebuked, admitted their wrongdoing and returned. As it says: *ach devarai vechukai asher tzivisi es avadai hanevi'im* — even My words which I commanded through My servants the prophets were eventually heeded, the people admitted their sin. One reading of this ends with Israel doing *teshuvah* — which, of course, would make it a less than fully devastating response. The tension in these readings is left unresolved.

### Slave Analogy

Shmuel offers a different version of the *teshuvah nitzachat*. Ten people came and sat before the prophet. He told them to repent. They replied: *eved shemachro rabo v'ishah shegerashah ba'alah, klum yesh lazeh al zeh klum* — if a master sells his slave, or a husband divorces his wife, they have nothing further to do with one another. God has sent us into exile; God has, in effect, abandoned us. Do not tell us to repent — it is over. This indeed sounds like an unanswerable response.

### No Divorce

Yet the *Gemara* shows that it was not unanswerable — because God responded through the prophet Yeshayahu: *eizeh sefer kerisus imchem asher shillachtiyah* — show me the bill of divorce I sent your mother. *Umi minoshai asher macharti eschem lo* — who is the creditor to whom I sold you off? The answer: *hein ba'avonoseichem nimkartem uvifsha'eichem shullechah imchem* — it was through your own sins that you sold yourselves; it was through your own transgressions that your mother was sent away. God did not divorce Israel. Israel brought the exile upon itself — and therefore Israel, not God, holds the power to end it. The slave argument is thus refuted: you were never sold to anyone but your own sin; you can return.

## Nebuchadnezzar Drasha

### David & Nebuchadnezzar

Rav, citing Reish Lakish, raises a striking observation: the verse refers to both David and Nebuchadnezzar as God's *avdi* — My servant. What possible comparison is there between God's faithful servant David and the destroyer Nebuchadnezzar? The explanation is as follows: God foreknew that Israel would make the very argument about the slave being sold — that once God sent them into exile via Nebuchadnezzar, they would claim to be cut off from any further relationship with God. To preempt this, God designated Nebuchadnezzar as His own *eved*, His own slave.

### Acquired Property

The operative legal principle is: *eved shekanah nechasim, eved l'mi, nechasim l'mi* — if a slave acquires property, who owns the slave and who owns the property? The master owns both. By establishing Nebuchadnezzar as His slave, God ensured that whatever Nebuchadnezzar acquired — including the Israelites he took into captivity — belongs to God.

### Never Left Servitude

The implication is profound: you never left God's servitude. Even in Babylonian captivity, you remained God's people, because Nebuchadnezzar himself was God's slave and his possessions were God's possessions. The claim that exile constitutes a severance of the divine relationship is therefore untenable.

## Redemption & Kingship

### God's Anger Verse

Another verse addresses the people's resistance directly. They say: *nihyeh kagoyim kemishpachot ha'aratzot lisharet etz va'even* — let us be like the nations, like the families of the lands, worshipping wood and stone. God responds through Yechezkel: *chai ani neum Hashem Elokim, im lo b'yad chazakah uvizro'a netuyah uvicheimah shefuchah emloch aleichem* — as I live, says the Lord God, even if it requires a powerful hand, an outstretched arm, and outpoured fiery wrath, I will reign over you. There is no escaping the divine relationship, no matter how much Israel resists.

### Accept Anger

Rav Nachman responds to this verse with a striking declaration: *ko ki hai ritcha lirtach Rachmana alan, dilfarkinan, velimloch aleichan* — let God pour out all that anger upon us, let us endure all of it, as long as in the end He redeems us and reigns over us. This is a remarkable contrast to the view mentioned earlier in the *masechta* — that some *rabbanim* preferred not to live in the days of *yemot haMashiach* because they feared the suffering of *chevlei Mashiach*. Rav Nachman takes the opposite position: whatever divine anger may come, redemption makes it worthwhile.

## Yetzer Hara Claim

### Evil Inclination

The *Gemara* presents yet another version of the people's response to prophetic rebuke. Rabbah bar bar Chana teaches: the prophet said to Israel, *chizru b'teshuvah*. They replied: *ein anu yocholim, yetzer hara sholet banu* — we cannot; the *yetzer hara* rules over us. We are simply unable to resist it.

### Deflect to God

The prophet responded: *yisru yitzrechem* — subjugate your evil inclination, bring it under control. The people replied: *elohav yorenu* — let the God who created the *yetzer hara* teach us how to control it, and then we will do so. This final deflection — placing the responsibility for overcoming the *yetzer hara* back on God — serves as another instance of the people's resistance to *teshuvah*.

The discussion of the people's sinfulness here connects, thematically, to the broader discussion of the kings: the people, in a sense, get the leaders they deserve, and vice versa. The question also arises whether the *dorshei reshumot*'s inclusive approach — covering the kings and the *hedyotot* alike — reflects the general principle of *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba*. That line, it should be noted, was a later addition to the *Mishnah*, not original to it, and its normative status is itself debated. Urbach, among others, has argued about whether this principle represents the standard theology of *Chazal*. The standard view seems to be that *tzaddikim* receive *olam haba*, *resha'im* go to *Gehinnom*, and *beinonim* fall somewhere in between. Yet the very fact that the *Mishnah* lists only a small number of individuals who are excluded implies that the default assumption is inclusion. The *dorshei reshumot* push that inclusivity to its furthest limit — even the most wicked among Israel retain their portion.

## Four Commoners

### List of Four

The *Mishnah* now turns to the four *hedyotot* — commoners — who have no portion in *olam haba*: Bilam, Doeg, Achitofel, and Geichazi. Note that the *derasha* of the *dorshei reshumot*, which we just encountered, included Doeg, Achitofel, and Geichazi but conspicuously omitted Bilam. This is deliberate: the principle of *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba* applies to Jews. For non-Jews, the standard is different — one must be among the *chasidei umot ha'olam* to merit *olam haba*, and that is precisely the discussion the *Gemara* is about to enter.

### Bilam Etymology

The *Gemara* now derives multiple meanings from the name *Bilam*. First: *b'lo am* — without a nation. Second, *davar acher*: *she'bala am* — who swallowed, wiped out, a nation. Third, he is identified as *Ben Be'or*, and the *Gemara* derives from *be'or* — *she'ba al be'ir* — that he, or his father, had intercourse with an animal. The *Gemara* will develop this point more explicitly later in connection with Bilam and his donkey.

### Genealogy

The *Gemara* then presents the tradition that Be'or, Bilam's father, is the same person as Cushan-Rishathaim, who is in turn the same person as Lavan Ha-Arami. One individual with multiple names, living across an extraordinarily long span of history — from the era of Yaakov through the period of the *Shofetim*. Why the multiple names? *She'ba al be'ir* — the name Be'or reflects his act with an animal. *Cushan-Rishathaim d'avad shtei risha'os b'Yisrael* — Cushan-Rishathaim, whose name encodes "two evils against Israel": one in the days of Yaakov, when as Lavan he sought to destroy Yaakov and thereby wipe out the Jewish people in its infancy, and once in the period of the *Shofetim*, when he oppressed Israel. Bilam himself would constitute a third attempt. *U'ma shemo? Lavan Ha-Arami shemo* — his true name is Lavan Ha-Arami. The *Gemara* seems to be connecting Bilam's lineage to a pattern of implacable enmity toward the Jewish people across generations.

The *pasuk* itself uses both *Ben Be'or* and *b'no Be'or* — one reading meaning "son of Be'or," the other potentially meaning "Be'or is his son." Rabbi Yochanan explains: when it says *b'no*, it means that in the realm of prophecy, Be'or was *Bilam's* subordinate — Bilam's junior. His father was, as it were, a lesser prophet than he was.

## Gentiles & Olam HaBa

### Bilam Excluded

The *Gemara* makes an observation from the *Mishnah*'s singling out of Bilam: *Bilam hu d'lo asi l'alma d'asi* — it is Bilam specifically who does not come to *olam haba*. The implication is that *other* non-Jews do have a portion in *olam haba*. Which non-Jews? The question prompts a discussion of the *masnisin*: whose view does our *Mishnah* represent?

### Eliezer Position

The *Gemara* answers: it reflects the position of Rabbi Yehoshua, as opposed to Rabbi Eliezer. As the *braita* teaches: Rabbi Eliezer interprets the verse *yashuvu resha'im lish'olah kol goyim shochechei Elohim* — the wicked shall return to the underworld, all nations who forget God. *Yashuvu resha'im lish'olah* — these are the sinners of Israel. *Kol goyim shochechei Elohim* — these are the sinners among the nations. But his deeper *derasha* reads the verse as establishing an identity rather than a subset: by definition, *all* the nations are categorized as those who forget God. All gentiles, on this reading, are treated as *resha'im* — not merely the wicked among them, but the entire category.

### Yehoshua Position

Rabbi Yehoshua disputes this. His position is that *resha'im* refers only to the wicked — among both Israel and the nations — and that the qualifier *shochechei Elohim* narrows the verse to those specifically who forget God, not to all gentiles universally. From this, Rabbi Yehoshua concludes: *ha yesh tzaddikim b'ovdei kochavim sheyesh lahem chelek l'olam haba* — there are righteous people among the nations who do have a portion in *olam haba*. The debate, then, is whether *kol goyim shochechei Elohim* is read as an identity (*all* nations *are* forgetters of God) or as a restrictive clause (those *among* the nations who forget God are the ones excluded).

### Tosefta Clarity

The debate is considerably clearer in the *Tosefta* (*Perek* 13), and it is worth presenting that version. The *Tosefta* opens with a related but distinct question: *bnei rish'ei Yisrael ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the children of the wicked of Israel have no portion in *olam haba* — based on the verse *hinei yom ba bo'er katanur*, in the words of Rabban Gamliel. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees and says they do come to *olam haba*. His reasoning: first, *shomer peta'im Hashem* — God protects the simple, and these children were raised in wickedness through no fault of their own, a case akin to *tinok shenishba*. Second, he cites the verse from Daniel about cutting down the tree while leaving the roots — punish the parent, but allow the possibility of redemption to remain for the children.

Turning to the main debate: the *Tosefta* has Rabbi Eliezer saying: *kol rish'ei ovdei kochavim ein lahem chelek l'olam haba, shene'emar: yashuvu resha'im lish'olah — elu rish'ei Yisrael; kol goyim shochechei Elohim — elu rish'ei ovdei kochavim*. Rabbi Yehoshua responds with precision: had the verse said *yashuvu resha'im lish'olah, kol goyim* and then stopped, you would be right — all the nations are treated as wicked. But since the verse adds *shochechei Elohim* — those who forget God — it demonstrates that there are those among the nations who are not forgetters, and therefore: *ha yesh tzaddikim b'ovdei kochavim sheyesh lahem chelek l'olam haba*. The *Tosefta* thus lays out the structure of the argument with considerably more clarity than the *Gemara*'s version.

### Rambam Ruling

One might ask whether one can even *pasken* in matters of theology. As we saw, the *bat kol* itself declared that God decides who enters *olam haba*. Nevertheless, the Rambam rules that *chasidei umot ha'olam yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the righteous among the nations have a portion in *olam haba* — aligning with Rabbi Yehoshua's position. This ruling carries profound theological significance. It is a remarkable feature of Jewish thought that one need not be Jewish to merit *olam haba*. As far as one can tell, Judaism is the only major religion that explicitly provides a path to salvation — or its equivalent — for those outside the faith. Other traditions may be more inclusive in certain respects, but the notion of a divinely mandated code for non-Jews (*sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach*) that carries with it genuine eschatological standing is, to the best of our knowledge, unique.

### Sheva Mitzvot

This leads directly to the question of the *sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach*. If God gave the nations a binding set of laws, and if the fundamental system of divine reward and punishment is ultimately rooted in *olam haba*, then it would be strange indeed to say those laws carry no ultimate reward. The linkage between the *sheva mitzvos* and the *chasidei umot ha'olam*'s portion in *olam haba* would seem to follow naturally. How Rabbi Eliezer's position coheres with the existence of the *sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach* is genuinely difficult to understand.

The Rambam adds an important qualification: a non-Jew merits the status of *chasidei umot ha'olam* only if he observes the *sheva mitzvos b'nei Noach* because he believes God commanded them — specifically, because God commanded Moshe at Sinai that the earlier Noahide covenant remained binding. If he observes them purely out of his own rational judgment, without grounding them in divine command, the Rambam says *eino m'chasidei umot ha'olam*. Here, a famous textual dispute arises among the *acharonim*. Some versions of the Rambam read *v'lo m'chachameihem* — he is neither among the pious of the nations nor among their wise men. Other versions read *ela m'chachameihem* — he is not among the pious, but he is among their wise men. The difference is significant: the first version implies he has no standing at all; the second grants him the status of *chacham* even without the theological grounding. The Rambam does not explicitly state in either version that such a person lacks *olam haba*; that conclusion is implied but not stated directly.

### Radbaz View

Some *nosei keilim* of the Rambam were uncomfortable with the implication that non-Jews who observe only seven *mitzvos* might receive the same portion in *olam haba* as Jews who observe the full *taryag mitzvos* — a disparity in obligation with an apparently equal reward. The Radbaz addresses this directly, arguing that while the *chasidei umot ha'olam* do have a portion in *olam haba*, that portion is not equivalent in quality to the portion of Israel. It is a portion, but a lesser one. This formulation allows for both the inclusion of righteous gentiles and the preservation of a meaningful distinction corresponding to the difference in covenantal obligation.

## Bilam's Sign

### Righteous Death

Returning to Bilam: *v'af oso rasha nasan siman b'atzmo* — even that *rasha* gave a sign by which to know whether he would receive *olam haba*. Bilam himself declared: *tamus nafshi mos yesharim vetehi achrisi kamohu* — let my soul die the death of the righteous, and let my end be like theirs. This statement contains his own criterion: if he were to die like the righteous, he would share their ultimate destiny and receive *olam haba*.

### Death in Battle

But Bilam did not die like the righteous. He died in battle, killed when Israel smote the Midianites. That violent death is itself the sign that he does not receive *olam haba*. His own words serve as the standard against which his fate is measured — and he falls short of it.

## Moav & Midyan

### Common Enemy

The *Gemara* now examines the background of the encounter between Balak and Bilam. The verse records: *vayelchu ziknei Moav v'ziknei Midyan* — the elders of Moav and the elders of Midyan went together. This is historically striking: *Midyan uMoav lo hayah lahem shalom me'olam* — Midyan and Moav had never had peaceful relations with one another. They were perpetual enemies. Yet here they cooperated. The reason, of course, is that they faced a common threat in Israel.

### Dog Parable

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a *mashal*: there were two dogs in a flock who were enraged at one another, *tzehuvim zeh lazeh* — their hackles raised against each other. A wolf came and attacked one of them. The first dog reasoned: if I do not help my fellow dog, the wolf will kill him today and come for me tomorrow. So they united and killed the wolf together. Here too, Balak was protecting his people of Moav, and they joined with the leaders of Midyan to address what they perceived as the threat of Israel. Rav Pappa adds the folk saying: *karkushta v'shunra* — the weasel and the cat, sworn enemies, *avdu hilula mitarva d'vish gada* — made a feast together from the fat of one who suffered misfortune. Common interest bridges even the deepest enmity.

The *Gemara* then explains why the officers of Midyan departed after Bilam asked them to stay the night. Bilam said: *linu poh halailah vahashivosi eschem davar* — stay here tonight, and I will bring you God's word. The Midianite officers replied: *klum yesh av shesonei es bno* — is there a father who hates his own son? They recognized that the God of Israel is Israel's father, and concluded that God would never agree to curse His own people. Seeing that any appeal to Bilam was therefore pointless, they left.

This prompts Rav Nachman to observe: *chutzpah afilu klapei shamaya mahani* — brazenness works even toward Heaven. For at first, God told Bilam *lo teleich imahem* — do not go with them. Yet Bilam kept pressing, and ultimately God said *kum leich itam* — arise and go with them. The *chutzpah* of his persistence, whether directed at the officers of Moav by asking them to remain another night in hopes of a different divine response, or directed at God Himself by daring to ask again after being refused, produced results. Rashi connects the *chutzpah* to Bilam's invitation to the officers to stay, suggesting it was bold of him to imply he might obtain a different answer; on a straightforward reading, the *chutzpah* was simply in approaching God again after a clear refusal.

Rav Sheshes adds a related observation: *chutzpah malchusa bela taga* — *chutzpah* is like kingship without a crown. Boldness confers informal power exceeding one's formal standing. David himself acknowledged this: *va'anochi hayom rach umishuach melech v'ha'anashim ha'eileh bnei Tzeruyah kashim mimeni* — I am today a young anointed king, and the sons of Tzeruyah are more powerful than I. Even David, the anointed king, found himself constrained by their audacity.

## Bilam's Flaws

### Lameness

Rabbi Yochanan enumerates several physical and moral deficiencies of Bilam. He was lame in one leg — *chiga b'raglo achas haya* — as derived from *vayelech shefi*, where Rashi reads *shefi* as indicating a displaced, dislocated limb. By contrast, Shimshon was lame in both legs, derived from the double language of *shefifon alei orach*, referring to a descendant of Dan who bit horses' heels: the doubled root indicates lameness in both legs.

### Blindness

Bilam was also blind in one eye — *suma b'achas me'einav haya* — as derived from his self-description *sh'sum ha'ayin*. Rashi reads the word *sh'sum* as meaning "open" in one eye, which by implication means the other is closed. The derivation is unusual, but the conclusion is that Bilam's vision was impaired in one eye.

### Necromancy

He also engaged in a form of *kosem b'amato* — necromancy involving his genitalia — derived from the phrase *nofel v'galui einayim*, which is compared to the verse about Haman's fall: *hineh Haman nofel al hamita asher Ester aleha*, where falling clearly carries sexual connotations. There is a dispute between Mar Zutra and Mar bar Ravina: Mar Zutra says the first interpretation stands — Bilam practiced necromancy in a sexually deviant manner. Mar bar Ravina says: rather, *sheba al atono* — he actually had intercourse with his donkey. Each interpretation uses a different comparative verse. Those who hold *ba al atono* derive it from the language of *kara shachav k'ari* in Bilam's prophecy, compared to *bein ragleha kara nafal shachav* in the episode of Yael and Sisera, where the language is clearly sexually charged.

## Donkey Argument

### God Knowledge

This leads into one of the more striking passages of the *daf*. Bilam describes himself as *v'yodea da'as elyon* — one who knows the thoughts of the Most High. The *Gemara* responds with biting irony: *hashta da'as behemto lo hava yada, da'as elyon hava yada?* — he did not even know the mind of his own animal, yet he claims to know the mind of God? Bilam could not see the angel standing before him, which his donkey could clearly perceive. But the *Gemara* takes this a step further: Bilam was not merely outclassed perceptually. He was actually bested in argumentation by his donkey.

### Constant Companion

The occasion for the argument was this: the officers of Moav asked Bilam why he was riding a donkey rather than a horse. Riding a horse would have been more dignified, more fitting for a person of his stature. Bilam replied that the donkey was practical — *d'shadei lehu birtiva*, it grazes on the wet grass of the swamp, so the cost of maintaining it is low. The donkey then spoke: *halo anochi asonecha* — am I not your donkey? You are using me today as if this is an ad hoc arrangement, but I am your regular mount. Bilam countered: I use you only for carrying burdens, not for riding. The donkey replied: *asher rachavta alai* — no, I am the one you always ride. Bilam dismissed this: *akrai b'alma* — occasionally, not regularly. The donkey rejected this too: *me'odecha ad hayom hazeh* — from your very beginning until today, I have always been your mount.

### Spousal Acts

The donkey then raises the stakes entirely: *v'lo od ela she'ani osa lach ma'aseh ishus balailah* — not only that, but I perform spousal acts for you at night. The word *hasken haskanti* — have I ever treated you this way before — is compared to *v'tehi lo sochenes*, the description of Avishag who kept David warm at night, reading *sochenot* as denoting intimate service.

### Outsmarts

The point of the entire exchange is pointed: if Bilam cannot hold his own in an argument with his donkey — a donkey that knows his history, his habits, and his most intimate arrangements better than he does — how plausible is his claim to know the thoughts of the Most High?

## Cursing Moment

### When Angry

What, then, was the genuine power Bilam possessed? The *Gemara* explains: *shehaya yodea l'chaven osa sha'a she'haKadosh Baruch Hu ko'es bah* — he knew how to time his action precisely to the moment in each day when God becomes angry. Had he managed to deliver his curse at that precise moment, it would have been effective.

### Shittim-Gilgal

This is the background for the prophet's reminder to Israel: *ami zachor na mah ya'atz Balak melech Moav, umah ana oso Bilam ben Be'or min haShittim ad haGilgal* — My people, remember what Balak king of Moav schemed, and what Bilam son of Be'or answered him, from Shittim to Gilgal, *l'maan da'as tzidkos Hashem* — so that you may know the righteous acts of God. What righteous acts? The *Gemara* explains:

### Avoided Anger

God says to Israel: know how great was My kindness to you — *shelo ka'asti kol otan hayamim* — throughout all those days, from Shittim to Gilgal, I deliberately refrained from becoming angry, so that Bilam should have no opening through which to curse you. This is implied by Bilam's own words to Balak: *mah ekov lo kaboh E-l, umah za'am lo za'am Hashem* — how can I curse whom God has not cursed, and how can I be wrathful when God has not been wrathful? This is read as testimony that throughout that entire period, *lo za'am Hashem* — God did not become angry — removing any possibility for Bilam's curse to take effect. Bilam knew the mechanism, but God chose not to activate it.

The general principle is that God does experience *za'am* — wrath — each day, but only for a moment: *ki rega b'apo chayim birtzono*. Bilam's unique skill was knowing precisely which moment that was. The verse *lech ami bo b'chadrecha, sgor d'latecha ba'adecha, chavi k'mat rega, ad ya'avor za'am* — go, My people, enter your chambers, close your doors, hide for a brief moment until the wrath passes — also alludes to this fleeting moment of divine anger.

### Three Hours

When exactly does God become angry? The *Gemara* specifies: during the first three hours of the day — from dawn until approximately mid-morning — when *k'chivra d'charbalta d'tarnagolta*, the comb of the rooster turns white. The *Gemara* clarifies that the rooster's comb is ordinarily white but has red streaks throughout most of the day. During that brief window, *leit bei sarki sumki* — the red streaks disappear entirely, and the comb is pure white. That is the moment.

### Rooster Story

This leads to a story about a *mina* — a heretic or sectarian — who lived in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and caused him great anguish. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi decided he would use that propitious moment to curse the man. He took a rooster, tied it to his bedpost by its legs, and sat watching for that precise moment of pure white. The *Gemara* notes, with something close to rueful humor: a watched rooster, it seems, never turns white. When that moment arrived, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi had dozed off. When he awoke, he concluded: *shma mina lav orach ara* — it is clearly not appropriate behavior. *Ktiv gam anosh l'tzaddik lo tov* — punishing another, even a *min*, is not fitting for a righteous person. *Afilu b'minei lo iba'i leh l'meimar hachi* — even against heretics one should not resort to cursing. The entire exercise led him to a moral conclusion rather than a curse.

There is a broader question lurking here: is the whole framework — that God becomes angry at a specific moment, and that Bilam knew how to exploit it — mechanistic? Does the one who knows the moment effectively compel a divine response? The answer, as the *Gemara* makes clear, is no. God chose not to become angry precisely because He foresaw the danger. *Olam keminhago noheg* — the world operates according to its nature — but God retains the capacity to override that nature. Bilam knew the mechanism; God simply chose not to engage it.

## Love & Hatred

### Love Breaks Hierarchy

The *Gemara* teaches in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *ahavah mevateles shurah shel gedolah* — exceeding love destroys the normal boundaries of conduct expected of people of stature. A great person will normally delegate tasks beneath his dignity to servants or attendants. But when love overrides judgment, those boundaries dissolve. The proof is from Avraham: *vayashkem Avraham baboker* — Avraham rose early in the morning himself, and personally saddled his donkey for the *Akeidah*, an act he would normally have delegated. Whether motivated by love of God or by love of Yitzchak, the force of the emotion caused him to bypass the normal conventions of his station.

### Hatred Breaks Bounds

Symmetrically: *sin'ah mevateles shurah shel gedolah* — hatred equally causes people of stature to cross normal behavioral boundaries. The proof is from Bilam: *vayakam Bilam baboker vayachavosh es atono* — Bilam rose in the morning and saddled his own donkey. Bilam would not ordinarily saddle his own animal; his hatred of Israel was so consuming that he eagerly did so himself, without waiting for his servants.

## Balak's Merit

### Wrong Motivation

The *Gemara* now introduces the principle of *shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — doing something for the wrong reason can ultimately lead to doing it for the right reason, or at least to producing good. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav states: a person should always engage in Torah and *mitzvos*, even *shelo lishmah*, even for impure or extrinsic motives, *shemitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — because from doing it for the wrong reason, he will ultimately come to do it for the right reason.

### Forty-Two Sacrifices

The proof is Balak. Balak brought forty-two sacrifices — seven bulls and seven rams per occasion, across three occasions, totaling forty-two — for the explicit purpose of enabling Bilam to curse Israel. This is almost the inverse of *shelo lishmah*: not merely a less-than-ideal motive, but an actively malevolent one. Yet he was offering these sacrifices to God, and there is an assumption that even the most corrupt motivation behind a genuine religious act leaves some residue of merit. The question of what exactly made these sacrifices a *mitzvah* — given that they were offered outside the *Mishkan*, constituting *chutz* — is complicated, but the *Gemara* assumes some form of divine service was involved.

### Ruth's Origin

By the merit of those forty-two sacrifices, *vayatsa mimenu Rut* — Ruth emerged from Balak's lineage. Rabbi Yosi bar Chunya specifies the genealogy: Ruth was the daughter of Eglon, who was the grandson of Balak, king of Moav. The *ba lishmah* here operates in an unusual register: ordinarily the principle means that impure motivations, if channeled into genuine action, will eventually reform themselves into pure ones in the actor. Here, Balak himself never improved — but the merit he accumulated through the act generated a reward that materialized generations later in the form of Ruth, ancestress of David.

## King's Blessing

### Throne Question

Rava poses a question to Rabba bar Mari: the verse says *yeteiv Elokim es shem Shlomo mishemcha, viyagdel kiso mikisecha* — may God make the name of Shlomo good from your name, and make his throne greater than yours. This was said to David when he designated Shlomo as his successor. But is it proper etiquette to say to a king that his son's throne should exceed his own? That would seem to be a slight — implying that the father's kingship was somehow deficient.

### Derive From

The answer: *mishimcha* does not mean "greater than your name." It means *me'ein kamre leh* — deriving from your name, taking something of the greatness of your name as its source. May Shlomo's greatness be an extension of David's, not a surpassing of it.

### Yael Analogy

The *Gemara* proves this reading from a parallel case: *tivorach minashim Yael* — Yael should be blessed among women, *eshet Chever HaKeini, minashim ba'ohel tivorach* — from among the women of the tent she should be blessed. The *nashim ba'ohel* — the women of the tent — are identified as Sarah, Rivka, Rachel, and Leah, the matriarchs. Could one possibly mean that Yael is greater than the matriarchs? Clearly not. *Ela me'ein kamre* — the blessing means that her greatness derives in some way from theirs, that she is blessed in the manner of those great women. *Hacha nami me'ein kamre* — so too by Shlomo, the blessing means deriving from David's greatness, not exceeding it.

### No Jealousy

The *Gemara* adds an observation from Rabbi Yose bar Chanina: *bechol adam mitkanei chutz mibno v'talmido* — a person is jealous of everyone except his son and his student. The absence of jealousy toward one's son is illustrated precisely from this episode: David's court could say "let Shlomo's throne be greater" without causing David offense, because a father naturally wants his son to surpass him. The same applies to a student: one genuinely wishes one's student to excel and surpass even oneself — as illustrated by Elisha asking for *pi shnayim b'ruchacha*, a double portion of Eliyahu's spirit, and by Moshe placing both hands on Yehoshua when God had only specified one — an act born of wholehearted generosity rather than reluctant compliance.

## Mouth Control

### Three Views

The *Gemara* returns to Bilam. *Vayasem davar b'fi Bilam* — and He placed a word in the mouth of Bilam. Three interpretations are offered. Rabbi Elazar says: *malach* — God sent an angel who accompanied Bilam to ensure that the words coming from his mouth were blessings rather than curses. Rabbi Yochanan, or Rabbi Yonatan, says: *chitah* — God placed a hook or bit in Bilam's mouth, physically dragging and controlling his speech the way a rider controls a horse with a bit.

### Prevent Curse

The purpose in either interpretation is the same: ensuring that Bilam could not curse Israel despite every intention to do so. Tomorrow's *daf* will develop this theme further, examining each of Bilam's blessings and showing how the very structure of the language reveals that Bilam was reaching for curses — and how God's control of his mouth transformed each intended curse into its opposite. This is, after all, what the *psukim* themselves say: *vayahafoch Hashem Elokecha lecha es hakelalah libracha* — God transformed the curse into a blessing, precisely because God loved Israel.
