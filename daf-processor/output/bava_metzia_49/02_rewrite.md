# Bava Metzia 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish

### Meshicha vs. Kesef

We pick up from the bottom of 48b, resuming the major debate that has occupied the preceding pages. The question is one that runs to the heart of commercial *kinyan* on *metaltelin*: what is the operative act of acquisition for chattel? As a practical matter, all agree that *meshicha* — taking physical possession — is what effects the transfer. The debate concerns the conceptual and legal basis for this rule. Reish Lakish holds that *meshicha* is the biblically defined mode of acquisition; there is nothing more to say. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, maintains that biblically the operative act is *kesef* — the payment of money. The rabbis, however, instituted *meshicha* in place of the biblical *kesef* because they wanted the owner of the object to retain responsibility for it until it is physically taken. If money alone completed the sale, a buyer could lose an object still sitting in the seller's domain through no fault of the seller, and that seemed wrong. So the rabbis overrode the biblical mode by requiring physical possession. There is a longstanding question among the *rishonim* as to how we rule between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — the *Tosafot* discuss this at length without reaching a clean resolution.

### Conceptual Framework

Within Rabbi Yochanan's position, however, there is a further and deeper question about how to conceptualize what the rabbinic institution actually did. Two possibilities emerge. The first: the rabbis completely nullified the effect of *kesef*, so that money payment does nothing at all legally, and only *meshicha* effects the transfer. The second, more nuanced reading: the biblical *kesef* still works — money payment creates a binding sale — but the rabbis gave both parties a right to retract until *meshicha* is performed. On this reading, the sale remains valid and operative unless and until one side exercises the right of retraction. These two readings are not merely theoretical; they have real *nafka mina* implications. Rashi, for instance, raises the case of a man who pays money for an apple but never performs *meshicha* on it, and then uses that apple to effect *kiddushin*. According to the second reading of Rabbi Yochanan — that money creates a binding sale, subject to retraction — the *kiddushin* would take effect, since the man's ownership of the apple is real as long as it has not been reversed.

### Rabbi Shimon's Evidence

This conceptual question is also illuminated by the position of Rabbi Shimon in the *mishnah*, who rules that only the party holding the money — the seller — has the right to retract; the buyer who has not yet taken possession cannot retract. This asymmetry implies that the payment of money does create something binding: the sale is valid, but there is a right on one side to negate it. If the money had done nothing at all, it would be hard to explain why one side's right of retraction should be privileged over the other's. The *mi shepara* — the rabbinic declaration that God will exact payment from one who retracts after money has changed hands — likewise becomes more coherent on this reading. Either the money payment represents a serious and concrete commitment whose reversal is morally weighty, or, on the stronger reading of Rabbi Yochanan, it was actually a binding sale that is now being undone. In either case, the *mi shepara* attaches once money has changed hands, and does not attach to a purely verbal agreement where no money moved at all.

## Koneh K'neged Kullo

### Karka Analogy

The conceptual question about the force of *kesef* carries directly into the discussion of partial payment. If I agree to buy a thousand acres of land for a million dollars but pay only a hundred dollars, have I purchased the entire parcel or only a hundred dollars' worth? For *karka* — real property — which is acquired by *kesef* as a matter of biblical law, the *gemara* indicates that you have purchased the whole. The logic is that we treat the initial payment as though the entire price has been paid, with the remainder outstanding as a debt. It is, in effect, a mortgage: the bank pays the seller in full, and the buyer owes the bank. Here, it is as though the buyer paid in full and now owes the balance — but the acquisition of the whole parcel is complete.

### Metaltelin Debate

The more pressing question is what happens with *metaltelin* when only partial payment is made. The *gemara* frames this as a debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan, expressed in the language *koneh k'negdo* versus *koneh k'neged kulo*. The language itself is telling: even in the context of *metaltelin*, where *meshicha* is the operative rabbinic act, the *gemara* speaks as though the money is effecting a *kinyan* — fitting naturally with Rabbi Yochanan's position that the biblical *kesef* still operates, subject to retraction.

### Mi Shepara Question

The practical question this debate generates is the *mi shepara*. Suppose I agree to sell a hundred bushels of apples at a dollar a bushel, and the buyer pays me five dollars. Clearly, if the buyer retracts and demands back his five dollars without taking any apples, he gets a *mi shepara* — he paid money and is backing out of that portion. But what if he says: fine, I'll take the five bushels I paid for, but I do not want the other ninety-five. Does he get a *mi shepara* on the remaining ninety-five? That depends entirely on whether his five-dollar payment is understood as having purchased only five bushels (*k'negdo*) or the entire hundred (*k'neged kulo*). Rav says *k'negdo*; Rabbi Yochanan says *k'neged kulo*. One might ask about an indivisible unit — a single cow, for instance — and there it seems even stronger reason to say *k'neged kulo*, since the object cannot be partially transferred. But for divisible quantities, the question is genuinely open between the two positions.

## Mashkon & Shemittah

### Mashkon Shemittah Case

The *gemara* asks whether the debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan on *k'negdo* versus *k'neged kulo* is actually a *tannaitic* dispute — *leima k'tannaei*. The case introduced is: one lends money to a friend and takes a *mashkon* — collateral — and then the *shemittah* year arrives. The rule is that *shemittah* annuls outstanding loans, derived from the verse *lo yigos et re'eihu ve'et achiv* — you shall not press your fellow or your brother — because if you hold the collateral, you are not "pressing" anyone; you already have something in hand. The specific scenario: the loan is for a thousand dollars, but the *mashkon* is worth only two hundred. What does *shemittah* annul?

Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel says: even though the *mashkon* is worth only half (or less) of the loan, *shemittah* does not annul the loan. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — referred to simply as "Rabbi" — says: *shemittah* does not annul only if the *mashkon* is equivalent in value to the entire loan; if not, *shemittah* does annul.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

The *gemara* initially reads this dispute as a direct parallel to *k'negdo* versus *k'neged kulo*. On that reading, when Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel says *shemittah* does not annul, he means it does not annul *any* of the loan — because holding the *mashkon*, even one worth only two hundred dollars, constitutes possession of the entire loan. This is *koneh k'neged kulo*: partial possession represents full possession.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, on this initial reading, holds *k'negdo*: you are in possession only to the extent of what you actually hold. The *mashkon* is worth two hundred, so *shemittah* does not annul two hundred dollars of the loan, but it does annul the remaining eight hundred. Each side holds according to what they actually possess.

### Gemara Resolution

The *gemara* pushes back on this alignment. If that is the debate — whether the two hundred dollars' worth of *mashkon* counts as possession of the full thousand — then according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, even the two hundred dollars would be annulled by *shemittah*. But that is impossible: everyone agrees that if you literally hold something worth two hundred dollars, *shemittah* cannot annul that two hundred. How could it annul what is already in your hand?

The *gemara* therefore retreats to a different resolution: *l'zikron devarim b'alma* — the *mashkon*, when it is worth less than the loan, functions merely as a reminder. Holding an object worth less than the full loan is not really "possessing" the loan in any meaningful sense. It is there so that the lender does not forget, perhaps as a bit of leverage to prompt repayment, but it does not constitute genuine possession of any portion of the loan's value. On this reading, the debate between Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is about whether holding such a symbolic *mashkon* constitutes possession of the loan at all — not a direct parallel to the question of partial payment in a sale. The conceptual analogy is instructive, but the cases are not identical: in the sale context, the question is whether partial money creates a *kinyan* on the whole object; in the *mashkon* context, the question is whether a symbolic token of possession counts as genuine possession of the debt's value. The *rishonim* do not, for the most part, press hard to equate the two, and one can see why: the mechanisms are different even if the underlying logic echoes.

A practical implication is raised: according to Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel on the stronger reading — that holding any *mashkon* prevents *shemittah* from operating on the entire loan — one could solve the problem of *shemittah* annulling loans simply by having every borrower hand over a token *mashkon*, making the institution of *pruzbul* seemingly unnecessary. The *gemara* notes this difficulty; the resolution offered by the reading of *l'zikron devarim* closes the loophole, since on that reading, a sub-value *mashkon* prevents *shemittah* only on the amount one genuinely possesses, which may be nothing at all.

## Rav Kahana Flax

### Case Setup

The *gemara* now narrates a concrete case involving Rav Kahana, who had received money for flax. The price of flax subsequently rose, and Rav Kahana wished to back out of the deal — at least partially. The buyers had not paid the full price; they had given only a down payment. Rav Kahana had no interest in retracting on the amount already paid — he understood that doing so would subject him to a *mi shepara* — but he wanted to retract on the remainder, the portion for which no money had yet changed hands.

### Rav's Position

He brought the question to Rav — and notably, he knew whose door to knock on, since Rav is the one who says *k'negdo*. Rav ruled: *b'mai d'nakitat zuzei* — for the amount of money you received, give them the corresponding flax. *Ve-idach devarim ninhu* — the remainder is only a verbal commitment. *U-devarim ein bahem mishum mechusrei amanah* — and a verbal commitment does not implicate the concern of violation of trust.

This is Rav's double position: first, the money paid purchased only that corresponding portion (*k'negdo*, not *k'neged kulo*); second, the unpaid remainder is a purely verbal undertaking, and backing out of a verbal undertaking is not morally wrong. This second point is striking. Legally, there is no question: one cannot be sued for failing to follow through on a verbal agreement. There is no *mi shepara* either, because no money changed hands on that portion. But Rav goes further and says there is not even a moral failing — *ein bahem mishum mechusrei amanah*. It is simply business: prices change, commitments not backed by money are understood by all parties to be subject to revision.

One might object that this renders a down payment essentially meaningless — if the seller can retract on everything beyond what was paid, and doing so carries no moral stigma, then the partial payment accomplishes nothing beyond purchasing that precise portion. Rav apparently accepts this consequence. As long as everyone understands the rules, the buyer who wants protection must pay in full or secure some other form of commitment.

## Devarim & Trust

### Rav vs. Yochanan

The *gemara* now formally records the *itmar* — the tradition of this debate as a named dispute. Rav says: *devarim ein bahem mishum mechusrei amanah* — verbal commitments do not implicate violation of trust. Rabbi Yochanan says: *devarim yesh bahem mishum mechusrei amanah* — verbal commitments do implicate violation of trust, and backing out of them is morally wrong. The parallelism between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan here is notable: Rav, who says *k'negdo* and thereby gives the seller more latitude, also says that the verbal portion carries no moral obligation. Rabbi Yochanan, who says *k'neged kulo* and is more protective of the buyer, also maintains that verbal commitments bind morally.

### Hin Tzedek Teaching

The *gemara* challenges Rav's position from a *baraita* citing Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda, who derives from the verse *hin tzedek yihiyeh lach* — "you shall have a just measure" — a principle about verbal integrity. The *hin* is a smaller measure than the *eifah*, and since the verse already obligates a just *eifah*, by *kal v'chomer* the *hin* is obviously included. Why then does the Torah mention the *hin* separately? To teach: *she-hen shelcha tzedek v'lav shelcha tzedek* — your "yes" should be a yes and your "no" should be a no. This is a powerful formulation: when you commit to something, you are bound by that commitment. Some *rishonim* understand Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda as stating a *d'oraita* obligation — not merely an *asmakhta* — to stand behind one's word.

### Abaye's Caveat

Abaye responds on behalf of Rav: the verse is teaching something different. *Shelo yedaber echad ba-peh v'echad ba-lev* — one should not say one thing with the mouth while intending another in the heart. The prohibition is against *duplicity* at the moment of the commitment — making a promise while already planning not to fulfill it. But if one is sincere at the moment of commitment and later changes one's mind, that is a different matter entirely; people change their minds, and that is understood. Abaye's caveat is philosophically important: it distinguishes between bad faith at the moment of promising and a genuine change of mind thereafter. Only the former is prohibited; the latter, according to Rav, carries no moral stain. This is admittedly a troubling conclusion — that there is no moral obligation whatsoever to follow through on a sincere verbal commitment — and the *gemara* notes the discomfort implicitly.

## Yochanan ben Matzya

### Worker Hiring Case

The *gemara* attempts to bring a proof against Rav from a *mishnah* involving Rabbi Yochanan ben Matzya (a *tanna*, not to be confused with Rabbi Yochanan the *amora*). Rabbi Yochanan ben Matzya told his son: go hire workers for us today. The son went, hired them, and agreed to pay them a wage plus meals. When the son returned to the father, the father said: my son, even if you made for them a feast like that of Solomon in his glory, you would not have satisfied your obligation to them — for they are descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and their dignity is extraordinary. Therefore, before they begin work, go and tell them: when I said "meals," I meant only bread and legumes — nothing more.

The significance for our sugya is that the father instructed the son to go back and revise the terms *before the workers began their labor*. This implies that revising a verbal commitment is permissible — which seems to support Rav. But doesn't it also imply, against Rav, that once they had begun work it would *not* have been permissible? The *gemara* presses this question.

### Reliance Analysis

The *gemara* deflects: *shani hatam de-poalim gufaihu lo samcha da'atayu* — this case is different because the workers themselves did not fully rely on the commitment. Why? Because the son was clearly acting on behalf of the father, and the workers understood that the arrangement was still pending the father's final approval. They knew the son was not the principal; the father was. So even if they had started working, the workers appreciated that the father's word had not yet been given. This is why the father could still revise the terms even after the son's commitment.

But the *gemara* then asks: if that is the case — that the workers understood the commitment was pending — why does it matter whether they had begun work or not? If the arrangement was contingent throughout, the father could always revise it. The *gemara* answers: *hitchilu b'melacha vadai samcha da'atayu* — once they begin the work, we presume they have relied fully. The reasoning is that by starting work, they are signaling that they assume the father has been informed and has approved. *Meimar amar kamei d'avua v'nicha lei* — they assume the son must have communicated the terms to the father and the father is on board.

### Reliance Principle

The crucial principle that emerges is this: *mechusrei amanah* — violation of trust — is not a categorical rule that applies whenever any verbal commitment is made. It is a function of whether the other side has genuinely relied upon the commitment. This limits Rabbi Yochanan's position significantly: even he acknowledges that not every verbal commitment creates a moral obligation to perform; it depends on the degree and nature of the other party's reliance. What makes Rav's position even more radical in light of this is that Rav apparently holds there is no moral violation *even when the other side is genuinely relying on the commitment*. In cases of real reliance and real opportunity loss, Rav still says: *devarim ein bahem mishum mechusrei amanah*.

## Gift Case

### Gift Retraction

The *gemara* questions whether Rabbi Yochanan was entirely consistent. Rabbah bar bar Chana reported in Rabbi Yochanan's name: *ha-omer l'chavero matanah ani noten lach* — one who tells his friend "I am giving you a gift" — *mutar lachzor bo* — is permitted to retract. If Rabbi Yochanan holds that verbal commitments implicate violation of trust, how can he permit retracting a promise to give a gift?

### Reliance Qualification

Rav Papa resolves this: Rabbi Yochanan agrees that when it is a *matanah mu'etet* — a small or modest gift — one may retract, because in that case *samcha da'atayu* — the recipient truly relies on the promise and genuinely trusts that it will happen. Wait — the formulation seems backward. Rav Papa's point is that Rabbi Yochanan's general ruling permitting retraction of a gift promise applies specifically to cases where the other side's reliance is not significant. When it is a small gift promised in passing, the recipient may hope for it but does not deeply rely on it in a way that imposes a moral obligation. But when the promise is one on which the other side truly and deeply relies — *samcha da'atayu* — Rabbi Yochanan agrees there is *mechusrei amanah* even if there is no concrete opportunity loss. The distinction is therefore not only about forgoing other opportunities; it is about the quality of trust and reliance the other party places in the commitment. Even without tangible economic harm from the retraction, the violation of that trust is itself the moral wrong.

## Levi Maaser

### Maaser Setup

The *gemara* brings a teaching of Rav Avin in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that further illustrates the principle. A *Yisrael* tells a *ben Levi*: I have a *kor* (a large measure) of *maaser* in my threshing floor that I am going to give you. On the basis of this commitment, the *ben Levi* goes home and designates that promised *maaser* — still sitting in the *Yisrael*'s threshing floor, not yet in the *Levi*'s possession — as *trumat maaser* to exempt other *maaser* he already has. This is a remarkable act: he is using produce he does not yet own to perform *hafrashat trumat maaser* on produce he does own.

### Moral Bindingness

The *rishonim* do not extensively discuss the ownership problem here; one *acharon* notes that since the *Yisrael* has promised to give it and we operate on the assumption — per Rabbi Yochanan — that he will keep his promise, the *Levi* can treat the promised *maaser* as effectively his for purposes of designation. It is as though the *Yisrael* has given the *Levi* the right to use his produce in this way. This works only because the *Levi* can be genuinely confident that the *Yisrael* will deliver.

### Tevel Risk

The *gemara* makes the argument explicit: *ei amrat bishlama lo matzu mehadar bei* — if you say the *Yisrael* cannot retract, then it is clear why the *Levi* may perform this *hafrashah* in confidence. But *ela ei amrat matzu mehadar bei* — if you say the *Yisrael* may retract — *amai rashai?* why would the *Levi* be permitted to do it? If the *Yisrael* retracts and gives the *maaser* to a different *Levi*, the first *Levi*'s *hafrashah* was invalid, and *ishtakach d'kachil tavlim* — it will turn out that he has been eating *tevel* — untithed produce whose terumah was never properly separated. The gravity of eating *tevel* makes this an acute concern. The only way to permit the *Levi* to perform this *hafrashah* is if we can say the *Yisrael* is morally bound not to retract — precisely Rabbi Yochanan's position.

The *gemara* then asks how Rav would explain this teaching, since Rav holds that verbal commitments carry no moral obligation. The answer: *k'gon she-natlo heimenu v'chazar v'hipkido etzlo* — the case must be that the *Levi* already physically received the *maaser* from the *Yisrael* and then re-deposited it with him. In that case, the *Yisrael* is merely a *shomer* for the *Levi*, and the produce already belongs to the *Levi* — no promise needs to bind the *Yisrael* at all.

But the *gemara* challenges this from the *seifa*: if the *Yisrael* violated his commitment and gave the *maaser* to a *different* Levi — *ein lo alav ela taromet* — the first *Levi* has only a complaint, no legal claim. If the produce had already been physically transferred to the first *Levi* and re-deposited, it would belong to him, and giving it to someone else would be outright theft. The fact that there is only a *taromet* — a grievance, not a legal claim — shows that the produce was never in the first *Levi*'s physical possession. This dismantles Rav's interpretation and, as the *gemara* notes, *shema mina*: the teaching supports Rabbi Yochanan. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the moral commitment to see the arrangement through is real and the *Levi* can rely on it — but if the *Yisrael* violates it and gives to another *Levi*, the second *Levi*'s acquisition is legally valid, and the first *Levi* is left with only a moral grievance.

## Sesame Seeds

### Case Narrative

The *gemara* narrates a new story. A certain person gave money for sesame seeds. Subsequently the price rose, and the sellers wanted to back out. They said to the buyer: we have no sesame seeds for you — take your money back. The buyer refused to take his money back; he insisted on the sesame seeds. The sellers held the money and attempted to return it; he would not accept it. Then the money was stolen from the sellers' possession.

### Shomer Status

The case came before Rava. Rava ruled: since the sellers said to the buyer "take your money" and he refused — *lo miba'ei shomer sachar delo havei* — it goes without saying they are not a *shomer sachar* on the money, since no one is paying them to guard it. *Ela afilu shomer chinam nami lo havei* — they are not even a *shomer chinam*. The analogy is vivid: if a guest at dinner forgets his coat and the host calls after him and says take your coat and the guest refuses, the host does not become a *shomer* on the coat simply because the guest left it there. The host did not consent to be a *shomer*; the object was left against his will. Similarly, the sellers did not consent to hold the buyer's money — they tried to return it and he refused. They bear no *shomer* liability.

### Mi Shepara Acceptance

The Rabbis challenged Rava: *ha ba'ei lekabulei alei mi she-para* — wait, the sellers would have to formally accept a *mi shepara* before the retraction is complete! This introduces a significant new idea: the retraction of a sale after money has changed hands is not simply effected by one side declaring "I am backing out." The reversal requires the retracting party to accept a *mi shepara* upon themselves. Until that acceptance occurs, the sale remains in force. And if the sale remains in force, the sellers hold the buyer's money not as *shomrim* but as parties to a still-valid sale — they are, effectively, *ba'alim* of the money and fully liable for it regardless of what happens.

The concept of "accepting a *mi shepara*" raises interesting questions about mechanism. Does the retracting party stand up and formally declare acceptance of the curse? Does the other party demand it? The *gemara* does not fully spell out the procedure, though the general sense is that the reversal is not complete until the consequences are acknowledged. There is also the question of retroactivity: if acceptance occurs in court after the money was lost, does that mean the sale was retroactively reversed from the moment of declaration, or only from the moment of acceptance? The *gemara* leaves this genuinely unclear.

Rava, upon hearing the Rabbis' challenge, said *hachi nami* — indeed, he agrees they must accept a *mi shepara*. Whether he means they already had accepted it before the theft, or that their accepting it now retroactively resolves the case, is itself uncertain.

### Rav Tuvyumi Account

The *gemara* then records a second version of the story, as told by Rav Papa in the name of Ravina, who heard it from a certain rabbi named Rav Tuvyumi (or, according to another version, Rav Shmuel bar Zutra). This rabbi testified: if someone had offered me all the wealth in the world, I would never have reneged on a commitment I had made — *lo hava kemishani be-diburei*. He was identifying himself as the principal in the story, and he wanted to clarify that it was misreported.

His version: it was a Friday afternoon, close to *shabbat*. A man knocked on his door asking if he had sesame seeds to sell. He said no. The man then said: I have all this money and nowhere safe to put it before *shabbat* — can you keep it for me? The rabbi gestured to his house and said, in effect, my house is here. He did not formally agree to be a *shomer*. The man placed the money in the house, and it was subsequently stolen. When the case came before Rava, Rava ruled: when you say to someone "my house is here" in response to a request to hold money — *lo miba'ei shomer sachar delo havei*, needless to say you are not a *shomer sachar*, *ela afilu shomer chinam nami lo havei* — you are not even a *shomer chinam*. Pointing to your house is not acceptance of *shmirah*.

When the Rabbis' objection about the *mi shepara* was raised — *iba'ei lei lekabulei alei mi she-para* — Rav Tuvyumi said: *lo haya devarim me-olam* — that objection was not applicable. The story the Rabbis were referencing, with the *mi shepara* issue, was a different story entirely — it involved a *pikadon*, a deposit, not a commercial sale retraction. The two stories were confused and conflated; the *mi shepara* element belonged to the other account, not to his.

## Money Payment Liability

### Ownership vs. Custody

This brings the *gemara* to a broader question about the legal status of money paid for a sale before *meshicha* is performed. Once money changes hands but before the buyer takes physical possession of the object, what is the relationship of the seller to that money? There is a significant debate among the *rishonim*: is the seller a *shomer* on the money, obligated with whatever level of care a *shomer* carries? Or, alternatively, does the money effectively belong to the seller by virtue of the sale — which, as we have seen, is binding at some level according to Rabbi Yochanan — making the seller not a *shomer* at all but rather a *ba'al*, an owner, who now has a corresponding obligation to either deliver the object or return the money?

### Prevailing View

The prevailing view among the *rishonim* is the latter: money payment creates a binding, if reversible, sale. The seller is not a *shomer* on the money; he is, in effect, an owner of it, and that ownership carries with it the full obligation either to deliver the goods or to return the money — even in a case of *ones*, an unavoidable accident. If the object is lost through *ones* after money was paid but before *meshicha*, the seller still owes the buyer either the object or its value, because he is not merely a custodian but a party to a binding transaction. This accords with the conceptual framework of Rabbi Yochanan: the payment of money creates a real and binding *kinyan*, which can be reversed, but as long as it has not been reversed, the parties are in a full sale relationship with the attendant obligations.

## Rabbi Shimon Rule

### Money Holder Rule

The *mishnah* concludes with Rabbi Shimon's rule: *kol she-hakessef be-yado yado al ha-elyona* — whoever holds the money has the upper hand, meaning the right to retract. Since we do not rule like Rabbi Shimon — we hold that both sides may retract before *meshicha*, not only the seller — Rabbi Shimon's rule applies only to himself. But his formulation is instructive.

### Seller Cannot Retract

A *tanna* clarifies: when does Rabbi Shimon's rule apply? When the seller has both the money and the fruit — the money received and the object still in his possession. But if the money is with the seller and the fruit is with the buyer — meaning the buyer already has the object — then the seller cannot retract, because the buyer has already received the value for his money.

### Possession Analysis

The *gemara* probes the language: *be-yado* — "in his hands" — sounds like the buyer is physically holding the object, in which case the statement is obvious. The *gemara* clarifies: the language does not mean the object is literally in the buyer's hands; rather, it means the buyer has not yet performed *meshicha*. So why can the seller not retract in the case described? Rava explains: *de-havsa aliyah shel lokei'ach muskeret be-yad mokher* — the case is where the seller is renting the *aliyah*, the upper story, from the buyer. The fruit is sitting in the buyer's building, but formally rented to the seller, so the seller has legal possession of the space. Yet despite not having performed *meshicha* on the fruit, the buyer cannot be exposed to the risk the rabbis were worried about — that the seller might negligently allow the object to be destroyed — because *bershuta d'lokei'ach ninhu*, the objects are in the buyer's domain. *Ihu tarach u-matzil* — if a fire breaks out, the buyer is right there and will protect his own investment. The rabbis' reason for requiring *meshicha* — to ensure the buyer does not suffer a loss through the seller's negligence — does not apply here, and therefore the sale is treated as more final even without *meshicha*.

## Wine Seizure

### Seizure Scenario

The final case of the *daf*: a certain person gave money for wine. He then heard that the government forces of Parzik Rufila — some kind of official or military authority — were about to seize the wine, confiscating it regardless of whose possession it was in. The buyer wished to retract before taking possession, saying: give me back my money, I do not want the wine.

### Rav Chisda Ruling

The case came before Rav Chisda. He ruled: *k'derech she-taknu meshicha be-mokhrin kach taknu meshicha be-lokachot* — just as the rabbis enacted that *meshicha* is required for sellers before the sale is final, so too they enacted *meshicha* for buyers. In other words, contrary to the view of Rabbi Shimon — who held only the seller could retract — the *halakha* is that both sides may retract before *meshicha* is completed. So this buyer may retract and get his money back. But several *rishonim* note that normally, retracting after paying money would subject one to a *mi shepara*. Here, they hold, it does not — because this is not an arbitrary change of mind or ordinary market fluctuation, but a retraction in the face of serious and imminent catastrophic loss.

### Mi Shepara Principle

This generates an important principle about the *mi shepara*. There is a spectrum. At one end: arbitrary retraction in normal market conditions, where one simply changed one's mind — this incurs a *mi shepara*. At the other end: retraction to avoid a catastrophic and extraordinary loss, such as government seizure — this does not incur a *mi shepara*. In between: normal price fluctuation. Whether backing out due to routine price changes triggers a *mi shepara* is itself a matter of debate.

Paralleling this is the question of verbal commitments. At one end: Rabbi Yochanan holds that backing out of a verbal commitment when the other side genuinely relies on it is *mechusrei amanah*, a violation of trust — even without opportunity loss, if the other side truly trusted in the promise. At the other end: both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan agree that when circumstances have changed dramatically, or when the other side never truly relied on the commitment, backing out carries no moral stigma. Rav's position is the more radical one, holding that even genuine reliance does not create a moral obligation to perform on a purely verbal commitment. Rabbi Yochanan draws the line differently, insisting that trust is itself a value that, once it has genuinely been extended and relied upon, creates a moral bond — even without legal enforceability and even without a *mi shepara*.
