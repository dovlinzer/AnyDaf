# Bava Kamma 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Liens on Slaves

### Gobvin min ha-avadim

We pick up at the very top line of Bava Kamma 12a, in the midst of a series of statements transmitted by Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar. The most recent of these is the ruling of *gobvin min ha-avadim* — that debts may be collected from slaves. The claim is that just as there exists a lien on real estate, there is similarly a lien on slaves: if a debtor sells his slave to a third party, or dies and his heirs inherit, the creditor may nonetheless collect from that slave. This stands in contrast to ordinary chattel, from which no such collection may be made.

### Rav Nachman's objection

This position was challenged by Rav Nachman, who rejected the idea that any general lien could attach to a slave. Under the pressure of that challenge, Ulla appeared to retreat, saying that he was speaking only of a case involving an *apotiki* — an explicit lien formally placed on the slave through a document. Without such an explicit designation, he seemed to concede, the general rule is that liens apply only to land, not to slaves.

### Ulla's real position

However, when we pick up at the top of 12a, we discover that this was not Ulla's genuine position. *Le-basar de-nafak* — after Rav Nachman had left — Ulla said to those present: know that this is truly what Rabbi Elazar taught, namely that one may collect *afilu mi-yasmei*, even from the heirs, because there is a real lien on slaves just as there is a lien on real estate. Rav Nachman, hearing of this, remarked that Ulla had been *ishtamititen* — evading him, declining to state his true position directly rather than confront Rav Nachman's strong opposition. Ulla's authentic tradition from Rabbi Elazar maintains that liens on slaves are real and operative.

### Neharde'a and Pumbedita…

The weight of practice appears to support Ulla's view. There was a case in Neharde'a — the seat of Shmuel's authority and the city where Rav Nachman himself held sway — in which the judges of Neharde'a were prepared to collect a debt from slaves, treating a loan as conferring a lien not only on land but also on slaves. They followed the ruling of Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar. Similarly, *hava uvda be-Pumbedita* — there was a case in Pumbedita in which Rav Chana bar Bizna permitted a creditor to collect from slaves that had either passed to heirs or been sold to a third party. The principle was being applied consistently: slaves are fully analogous to landed property for the purpose of liens.

### Rav Nachman's threat

Despite this, Rav Nachman remained entirely unconvinced. He instructed the judges — *zilu ahadureh* — to go and reverse their rulings, returning the slaves to the purchasers. There is no lien on slaves, he insisted. And if the judges would not comply, he declared, *magbinan lechu apandaichu* — he would seize their very castles from them, treating their erroneous rulings as actionable judicial misconduct. His opposition was fierce and unambiguous.

### Rava's challenge

Rava then pushed back directly. He enumerated the authorities: Ulla holds there are liens on slaves; Rabbi Elazar holds it; the judges of Neharde'a ruled on it; Rav Chana bar Bizna in Pumbedita ruled on it. *Mar keman svara lei* — like whom exactly does Rav Nachman hold, that he dismisses all of these positions?

### Rav Nachman's defense

Rav Nachman's reply is disarming in its simplicity: *ana matnita yedana* — I know only the *braita*. It is not that he is unaware of the distinguished chain of authorities; it is that his anchor is a specific *braita* taught by Avimi, and that *braita* is the basis upon which he stands.

## Pruzbul and liens

### Pruzbul on land/slaves

The *braita* to which Rav Nachman refers is the teaching of Avimi: *pruzbul chal al ha-karka ve-eino chal al ha-avadim* — a *pruzbul* takes effect when the debtor possesses land, but not when the debtor possesses only slaves. The *pruzbul*, it will be recalled, is the rabbinic mechanism enacted by Hillel permitting the collection of debts even after the *shemita* year, which would otherwise annul outstanding loans.

### Pruzbul principle

The logic underlying this rule is as follows. Ordinarily, *shemita* annuls debts because the creditor cannot compel collection. But when the creditor holds a lien on the debtor's land, it is as though the debt is already effectively secured — the creditor can seize that land at any moment. The land is stable, fixed, and accessible, and the lien upon it is therefore meaningful. On that basis, the *pruzbul* can be written when the debtor owns land: the lien is real and the debt is effectively under the creditor's control. But if the debtor has only a slave, the *pruzbul* does not work — apparently because, on Rav Nachman's reading, there is no lien on slaves to begin with.

### Kinyan Agav principle

The same *braita* continues with a second principle: *metaltelin niknim im ha-karka ve-einam niknim im ha-avadim* — chattel may be acquired together with land, but not together with slaves. This refers to the principle of *kinyan agav* established in tractate Kiddushin: when one takes formal possession of land, the seller may simultaneously transfer ownership of movable property located anywhere — even at a great distance — by stipulating that the chattel is acquired along with the land. This mechanism works when the anchor is *karka*, but the *braita* states it does not work when the anchor is a slave.

### Slaves third category

In both of these rules, slaves diverge from land: there is no *pruzbul* on a slave as there is on land, and *kinyan agav* does not operate with a slave as the vehicle. Yet the second clause of the *braita* also distinguishes slaves from ordinary chattel — because one does not say that taking possession of slaves automatically carries along with it possession of *metaltelin*. Slaves appear, in both dimensions, to stand in a category of their own.

### Tosafot's distinction

Tosafot notes that throughout *Shas*, the standard assumption is that *avda ke-karka'ei* — a slave is like land. Slaves and land are grouped together in numerous exemptions: from the laws of theft and robbery (*gneiva ve-gezeila*), from the obligation against overcharging, from the requirement of an oath, and so forth. So what is the controversy here?

Tosafot answers that the question is specifically whether we extend the equation of slaves with land to *rabbinic* law. Both *pruzbul* and *kinyan agav* are rabbinic enactments. The Torah-level equation of slaves with land is established and uncontested; the question is whether we carry that equation into the rabbinic sphere, where the rationale might differ.

One way to understand why the equation might not hold for these particular *halachot* is by attending to their underlying logic. Liens attach to land rather than chattel because land is fixed, identifiable, and its ownership can be precisely recorded — factors that make it fair to third-party purchasers who can research the record. A slave, despite being legally analogous to land in many ways, does not share those characteristics in the same fashion. Similarly, the concept of *kinyan agav* — using land as a vehicle to simultaneously transfer chattel — may depend on land functioning as a stable repository for property, a stable location to which things are conceptually anchored. That rationale does not translate straightforwardly to a human being who moves. These considerations help explain why, within the domain of liens and *kinyanim*, the question of whether slaves are treated like land or like chattel becomes live, even though in other domains the equation is settled.

## Two conflicting braitot

### First braita details

With this backdrop established, the *Gemara* proposes that the dispute between Ulla and Rav Nachman may correspond to a *tannaic* debate — *leima k'tana'ei*. It turns to a *braita* that works through several combinations of property types. If someone sold slaves and land to another person: if the buyer took possession of the slave, he did not thereby acquire the land; and if he took possession of the land, he did not thereby acquire the slave. Each stands alone.

### Kinyan Agav explained

The *braita* then clarifies the rule for land and chattel: if one takes possession of land (*karka*), one simultaneously acquires *metaltelin* — that is *kinyan agav*. But the reverse does not hold: taking possession of chattel does not confer possession of land.

### Slaves and chattel

When it comes to slaves and *metaltelin*, the *braita* states that taking possession of the slave does not confer possession of the chattel, and taking possession of the chattel does not confer possession of the slave. Slaves do not function as a vehicle for *kinyan agav* in either direction with respect to chattel.

### Second braita intro

However, a second *braita* is then cited that teaches the opposite regarding this last case: *hechezik ba-avadim kanah metaltelin* — if one takes possession of slaves, one does acquire the chattel along with them.

### Slaves like land or chat…

The *Gemara* proposes that this is the crux of the *tannaic* dispute. The first *braita* holds that slaves are like *metaltelin* — chattel — and therefore taking possession of a slave no more carries along other chattel than taking possession of one piece of chattel carries along another. The second *braita* holds that slaves are like *karka* — land — and therefore taking possession of a slave can serve as the vehicle for *kinyan agav*, conferring possession of chattel just as taking possession of land does. This appears to map directly onto the debate between Ulla (slaves are like land, liens apply) and Rav Nachman (slaves are like chattel, no liens).

---

## Rav Ika's first view

### Everyone agrees like land

Rav Ika son of Rav Ami intervenes to argue that the two *braitot* need not reflect a dispute about whether slaves are fundamentally like land or like chattel. On his first explanation, *de-chulei alma avadim ke-mika-rke-dami* — everyone agrees that slaves are like land. The *braita* that says *kinyan agav* works with slaves follows naturally from that premise. But the *braita* that says it does not work — that also can be reconciled without disagreeing about the underlying status of slaves.

### Biblical basis Kinyan Ag…

The *Gemara* has already established the scriptural basis for *kinyan agav*. The *mishna* in Kiddushin states that property without liens (i.e., chattel) may be acquired along with property that has liens (i.e., land), through the same modes by which land is acquired — money, deed, or *chazaka*. The verse underlying this, as taught by Chizkiyah, is from Chronicles: *va-yiten lahem avihem matnot rabot le-kesef u-le-zahav u-le-migdanot im arei mitzurot bi-Yehuda* — their father gave them many gifts of silver, gold, and precious things together with the walled cities of Judah. The act of taking possession of the walled cities carried along the transfer of all the movable gifts simultaneously.

### Stability requirement

From this verse, Rav Ika derives a qualification: *kinyan agav* requires *karka dumya de-arei mitzurot bi-Yehuda* — land that resembles the walled cities of Judah. The essential characteristic of those cities is stability: they do not move. The *kinyan agav* mechanism is predicated on something fixed and immovable serving as the vehicle.

### Slaves move, don't work

A slave is a human being who walks about. Even if we agree that a slave is juridically equivalent to land in the standard sense, a slave is not the kind of stable, unmoving property that the verse envisions as the vehicle for *kinyan agav*. Therefore, even on the view that slaves are like land, using a slave as the vehicle for acquiring chattel through *agav* does not work. The *braita* that denies it is not disagreeing about the fundamental status of slaves — it is simply applying the more specific requirement that *kinyan agav* demands actual, rooted property that does not move. Ulla's position that there are liens on slaves, and the broader equation of slaves with land, can therefore remain intact; the failure of *kinyan agav* is a separate and narrower rule.

---

## Rav Ika's second view

### Slaves like chattel

The *Gemara* then presents an alternative version of Rav Ika's reasoning — attributed there to Rav Ika son of Rav Idi — proceeding from the opposite premise: *de-chulei alma avdi ke-metaltelin dami* — everyone agrees that slaves are like chattel. On this reading, the *braita* that denies *kinyan agav* for slaves follows straightforwardly. But the *braita* that seems to permit it requires an alternative explanation.

### Kinyan chatzer concept

The *braita* that says taking possession of slaves confers possession of chattel is, on this reading, not describing *kinyan agav* at all. Rather, it describes a case *be-oden alav* — where the chattel is physically resting on the slave. When you take possession of my slave, and my watch happens to be on that slave's person, you acquire the watch — not through *agav*, but through *kinyan chatzer*: just as taking possession of one's courtyard can effect acquisition of objects placed within it, so too, when the slave enters your possession, anything resting on the slave comes along with it.

### Chatzer mehalechet

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *chatzer mehalechet hi* — a moving yard. The whole premise of *kinyan chatzer* is that the courtyard is a stable enclosure under the owner's control. A slave walks around freely; how can a slave function as a *chatzer* for purposes of acquisition?

### Conditions for chatzer

Rava addresses this: *kol she-illu mehalekh lo kana omed ve-yoshev lo kana* — any entity that, were it walking, would not serve as a *chatzer* for acquisition, does not serve as one even when standing or sitting. A slave who has the capacity to walk is, by that very fact, not analogous to a stationary courtyard, even when momentarily still. The only way this can work, therefore, is if the slave is bound — physically restrained so that he lacks the ability to walk. Tosafot adds that the slave may also need to be asleep, so that the object is kept in place solely by the owner's domain and not by the slave's own volition.

### Limited explanation

This is a deeply constrained explanation. It applies only in a narrow circumstance — the slave is physically bound, the chattel is resting upon him — and it operates through *kinyan chatzer* rather than *kinyan agav*. On this entire approach, slaves are categorically like chattel, there are no liens on them, and the appearance of *agav* in the second *braita* is an illusion to be explained away.

## Slaves with land

### Land acquires slaves

The *Gemara* now turns to a further *braita*: *hechzik be-karka kanah avadim* — if one takes possession of land, one acquires slaves along with it. This seems to stand in tension with the first *braita* cited earlier, which stated that taking possession of land does not yield possession of slaves. The *Gemara* resolves the apparent conflict: the *braita* that says it works is referring to a case *be-omdim be-tokhah* — where the slaves are physically standing within the land being acquired. The *braita* that says it does not work refers to slaves who are not standing on the land at the moment of acquisition.

### Distinction if chattel

The *Gemara* then explores whether this distinction holds together on either view of slaves' status. If slaves are like chattel (*ke-metaltelin*), one might understand why their physical presence in the land matters: it would be a case of either *kinyan agav* (chattel being acquired together with the land) or *kinyan chatzer* (the land functioning as a courtyard that draws in what is physically present within it). On this view, the presence of the slave within the land makes the acquisition operative, and the absence of the slave from the land leaves it inoperative.

### Kinyan Agav problem

But the *Gemara* immediately presses: even if slaves are like chattel, the requirement of physical presence should not matter for *kinyan agav*. The established principle is *de-lo ba'inan tziburin* — we do not require the chattel to be physically piled up in the land being acquired. *Kinyan agav* works even if the chattel is entirely elsewhere. So why should it matter whether the slave is standing in the land or not?

### Moving vs. non-moving

The *Gemara* answers: *shani metaltelin de-naydi mi-metaltelin de-lo naydi* — there is a distinction between property that moves and property that does not. Tosafot clarifies that "moves" here means moves like a human being, not simply like a cow. The mechanism of *kinyan agav* — by which the seller mentally packages all of his property together and the buyer's acquisition of the land carries the rest — works when the ancillary property is inanimate or even an animal without its own will. In those cases, the seller's intent is sufficient to unite them conceptually. But a slave has intellect and will of his own; he is not conceptually fused with the land unless he is physically present on it.

### Moving land concept

If, alternatively, slaves are treated as land (*ke-karka*), the same structural problem arises in a different form. Shmuel teaches that if someone sells ten fields in ten different countries, the buyer's act of taking possession of any one of them acquires all of them simultaneously — one act of *chazaka* on one piece of land works for every other piece of land the seller intends to transfer. If slaves are like land, why should it matter whether the slave is physically standing on the particular parcel being acquired? One piece of land should suffice for all others. The *Gemara* answers: *shani mi-karka'ei de-naydi mi-mi-karka'ei de-lo naydi* — even on the view that a slave is like land, there is a difference between land that does not move and land that does. A slave is, on this view, *mi-karka'ei de-naydi hu* — moving land. Being *koneh* a stationary field in one location does not extend to acquiring a slave, even if that slave is conceptually in the category of land, because the slave is a fundamentally detached and independent entity.

### Land as one unit

The reason Shmuel's principle works for fields in distant countries is captured by the phrase *sadna de-ara chad hu* — the crust of the earth is one unit. All land, however distant, is somehow connected as part of the same continuous ground; taking possession of any piece of it is, at some level, taking possession of the same fabric of earth. A slave — even if categorized as a form of land — shares no such connection. He is ambulatory and independent, not part of the unbroken crust of the earth. And therefore, taking possession of a parcel of land in one place does not reach out to acquire a slave who happens to be somewhere else.

## Summary of discussion

### Liens as central

Stepping back, the central question animating the entire discussion is whether liens apply to slaves as they do to land. Ulla, transmitting from Rabbi Elazar, and supported by the practice of the judges of Neharde'a and Rav Chana bar Bizna of Pumbedita, holds that they do. Rav Nachman, anchored in the *braita* of Avimi concerning *pruzbul*, holds that they do not.

### Exceptions for kinyanim

When it comes to *kinyanim*, the picture is more nuanced. Even if one accepts that slaves are fundamentally like land — as is the standard position throughout *Shas* for Torah-level rules — there may be exceptions in the domain of *kinyanim*. A slave may not serve as the vehicle for *kinyan agav* because the relevant verse requires stable, non-moving property. And even when one begins with a piece of land, that act of *chazaka* may not extend to a slave, because the slave is moving land — detached from the earth's continuous fabric. These are specific features of *kinyan* law that may operate differently even when the slave's general legal status is settled.

## New topic: me'ila

### Me'ila principle

We now move to the next section of the *Mishnah*, which states a general principle: one is liable for damages done to property for which there is no *me'ila* obligation — *nechasim she'ein bahem me'ila*. The point is that the exemption from damage liability applies to *hekdesh* — consecrated property — and specifically to property that carries the *me'ila* obligation. But there exists a category of sanctified property for which there is no *me'ila*, and for such property one remains liable for damages as one would be for ordinary property.

### What is me'ila?

*Me'ila* is the offense of misappropriating consecrated property — making improper use of something dedicated to *hekdesh* — which triggers both a financial penalty and the obligation to bring a special *korban asham*. The *Gemara* asks: what is the category of property that is sanctified and yet carries no *me'ila* obligation?

### Kodshim Kalim example

The paradigmatic answer is *kodshim kalim* — sacrifices of lesser sanctity, such as a *shelamim*. A *shelamim* is brought by an individual, the blood is sprinkled on the *mizbeach*, but the meat is eaten by the owner and his household in Jerusalem, and the *kohanim* receive the breast and thigh. There is no *me'ila* on the *shelamim*'s meat; *me'ila* applies only to the *eimurim* — the fats and innards burned entirely on the *mizbeach*, which belong wholly to God. The rest, being eaten by humans, carries no *me'ila* obligation. The implication of the *Mishnah* is therefore: if one's ox gores another person's *shelamim*, one is obligated to pay, since the victim's *shelamim* is in a category of sanctified property that nonetheless triggers damage liability.

---

## Rabbi Yosi's view

### Attribution

Rabbi Yochanan identifies the *tanna* whose position underlies this *Mishnah*: it follows Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, and it applies specifically to *kodshim kalim*.

### Mammon ba'alim

The cornerstone of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position is the principle that *kodshim kalim* is *mammon ba'alim* — it remains the owner's property. Even though the animal is sanctified and will be brought as a *korban*, its status is akin to that of a person who owns a *sefer torah* or *tefillin*: the object carries sanctity, but ownership has not been transferred away from the individual. Unlike *kodshei kodashim* — the most sacred offerings such as a *chatas* or *olah*, which belong entirely to *hekdesh* — *kodshim kalim* remains in the owner's domain.

### Damage liability

The practical consequence follows directly: if your ox gores my *shelamim*, you owe me damages, just as if it had gored an ordinary cow. The fact that the animal is sanctified does not shield the tortfeasor from liability, because the property is still mine — *mammon ba'alim*.

### Oath braita

The *Gemara* brings a *braita* in support of this from the law of false oaths. The Torah describes a case in which a person denies under oath having received a deposit from his fellow and thereby commits a *me'ila* against God — *u-ma'ala ma'al b'Hashem*. The *braita* derives from this that *l'Hashem l'rabot kodshim kalim she-hein mamono* — the phrase "against God" comes to include *kodshim kalim*, which is the owner's property. Even though the object has sanctity, the denial of it to one's fellow constitutes a wrong against that fellow — *v'kichash b'amito* — and the laws of denial and false oath apply. This is the express teaching of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili.

### Me'ila extension

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili thus extends the full range of consequences that flow from *mammon ba'alim* to *kodshim kalim*: the owner's property rights are real, the standard laws of torts apply, and the laws of oaths and denial apply as well.

## Kiddushin contradiction

### Priest kiddushin case

The *Gemara* now raises an apparent contradiction from a *Mishnah* in Kiddushin. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-mekadesh b'chelko* — if a *kohen* attempts to betroth a woman using his portion of a *korban*, whether from *kodshei kodashim* or from *kodshim kalim* (specifically the *chazeh v'shok* — the breast and thigh that the *kohen* receives), she is *eina mekudeshet* — the betrothal does not take effect.

### Not his property

The reason the *kiddushin* fails is that the *kohen*'s portion is not considered his property in the relevant sense. *Kiddushin* requires that the groom give the woman something of value that belongs to him. If the item does not truly belong to him, the *kiddushin* is invalid.

### Mishulchan gavoa

The phrase the *Gemara* invokes to capture this is *mishulchan gavoa k'zachu* — he has acquired this food by virtue of eating from God's table. The *kohen* is invited to partake; he does not own what he is partaking of. He eats the breast and thigh not as an owner consuming his own property, but as a guest at God's table. Therefore, it cannot serve as the instrument of *kiddushin*.

### Contradiction emerges

But this seems to contradict Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position. The *kohen*'s portion of a *shelamim* is *kodshim kalim*. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili says *kodshim kalim* is *mammon ba'alim*. If so, why can't the *kohen* use it for *kiddushin*? The *Gemara* asks: *leima d'lo Rabi Yose HaGlili* — is this *Mishnah* in Kiddushin simply not following Rabbi Yosi HaGlili?

---

## Rabbi Yosi limited

### Living vs. slaughtered

The *Gemara* answers that the apparent contradiction can be resolved even within the framework of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili. *Ki amar Rabi Yose HaGlili michayim* — when Rabbi Yosi HaGlili says that *kodshim kalim* is *mammon ba'alim*, he is speaking specifically about the living animal. While the animal is alive, it is still the owner's *korban*; the owner is bringing it, the owner will eat it, it is his in every meaningful sense. At that stage, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili says it remains *mammon ba'alim*.

### After slaughter change

But once the animal has been slaughtered and offered as a *korban*, and the portions are being distributed and eaten, the character of the property changes. At that point, *aval le'achar shechita afilu Rabi Yose HaGlili modeh d'ki k'zachu mishulchan gavoa k'zachu* — even Rabbi Yosi HaGlili agrees that when one is partaking of the meat after *shechita*, one is eating from God's table. The *kohen* who receives the breast and thigh after the animal has been brought as a *korban* receives it as a *matnas kehuna* — a divinely ordained gift dispensed from God's bounty — not as his own property. The *kiddushin* therefore fails, and this is entirely consistent with Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position, which applies only to the living animal.

### Rashi-Tosafot debate

Rashi and Tosafot disagree about the scope of this ruling. Rashi holds that the principle of *mishulchan gavoa k'zachu* applies even to the owners when they eat their portion of the *shelamim* after *shechita*. On Rashi's reading, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's *mammon ba'alim* is operative only while the animal is alive; once slaughtered, everyone — owners and *kohanim* alike — is eating from God's table, and the practical implications of *mammon ba'alim* are extinguished. Tosafot draws a sharper distinction: the owner's portion is different from the *kohen*'s portion. The *shelamim* began as the owner's animal; the owner raised it, consecrated it, and is now eating his own animal — just in a sanctified context, in Jerusalem. According to Tosafot, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili would say that the owner is eating *his own* portion of the *shelamim* even after *shechita*, and if the owner were to use that portion for *kiddushin*, it would be considered *mammon hedyot* and the *kiddushin* would work. The *kohen*, by contrast, never owned the animal; the *kohen*'s portion was carved out of someone else's offering and given to the *kohen* as God's dispensation. The *kohen* is always, on Tosafot's reading, eating *mishulchan gavoa*, even according to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili.

## Bechor case test

### Bechor sales

The *Gemara* now tests Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position against a *braita* concerning a *bechor* — a firstborn animal, which the Torah requires be given to a *kohen* and brought as a *korban*. If the animal has a *mum* — a blemish — it cannot be offered on the *mizbeach* and the *kohen* may simply slaughter and eat it outside the Temple precincts. The *braita* teaches: *mochrin oso tam chai* — if the *bechor* is unblemished and must be brought as a *korban*, nevertheless the *kohen* may sell it to another *kohen* who will bring it. *U-va'al mum chai v'shachut* — if it has a blemish, it may be sold whether alive or after slaughter, since it is no longer destined to be brought as an offering. *U-m'kadshin bo es ha-isha* — and one may use it to betroth a woman, demonstrating that it is considered the *kohen*'s actual property.

This *braita* appears to confirm that even an unblemished *bechor* — a *kodshim kalim* animal alive and theoretically destined for the *mizbeach* — is treated as *mammon ba'alim* by the *kohen*. This would vindicate Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's principle in its fullest form: the living *korban* is the owner's property.

### Rav Nachman limit

However, Rav Nachman qualifies this sharply. *Lo shanu ela b'bechor b'zman hazeh* — the *braita*'s permission to sell and use the *bechor* for *kiddushin* applies only nowadays, when there is no *Beis HaMikdash*. Without a Temple, the *bechor* cannot in fact be brought as a *korban*. It may be unblemished in principle, but since it will never be sacrificed, the *kohen* is simply waiting for a blemish to appear so he can eat it. In that circumstance, it is on the path toward being ordinary meat, and it is therefore considered *mammon ba'alim* in an unqualified sense.

### With Beis HaMikdash

When the *Beis HaMikdash* exists and the *bechor* could genuinely be brought as a *korban*, the ruling is reversed: *aval bi-zman she-Beis HaMikdash kayam d'chazi l'hakrava, lo* — when it is destined for the *mizbeach*, it is not considered the *kohen*'s property in that sense, and neither the sale nor the *kiddushin* would be valid. The *bechor*'s status as *mammon ba'alim* is contingent on it not being genuinely on the path toward *hakrava*.

### Rava's challenge

Rava challenges this construction. According to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, *kodshim kalim* is *mammon ba'alim* while alive — why should that not apply to a living *bechor* even when the *Beis HaMikdash* stands? The *bechor* is alive, it is *kodshim kalim*, and Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's principle should make it *mammon ba'alim* straightforwardly, regardless of whether a Temple exists. Why does Rav Nachman insist on the special circumstance of no *Beis HaMikdash*, or on the *bechor* being outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, rather than simply applying Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's rule?

The *Gemara* here introduces a further wrinkle. Ravina proposes that the *braita* about the *bechor* should be read as referring specifically to a *bechor* in *chutz la'aretz* — outside of *Eretz Yisrael* — and following the position of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds that *im ba'u temimim yikrevu* — if unblemished *bechorot* from outside *Eretz Yisrael* are brought to the Temple, they are sacrificed, but *lechatchila lo* — there is no obligation to bring them. Outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, the *bechor* is not truly on a path toward *hakrava*, and it is therefore effectively equivalent to the case of no *Beis HaMikdash*. On this reading, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's general principle that a living *kodshim kalim* is *mammon ba'alim* would not, after all, cover a standard *bechor* in *Eretz Yisrael* when the Temple stands.

---

## Matnas kehuna resolution

### Matnas kehuna

The *Gemara* now provides the definitive resolution. The challenge to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position — why does the *bechor* not function as *mammon ba'alim* even while alive, given his principle — is answered with a fundamental distinction: *shani matnas kehuna d'ki ka zachu meshulchan gavoah ka zachu* — *matnas kehuna* is categorically different, because when the *kohen* acquires it, he acquires it from God's table.

### Owner's portion only

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's principle of *mammon ba'alim* applies specifically to the owner's *korban*. The paradigm is a *shelamim*: I own a cow, I sanctify it as a *shelamim*, I bring it as my offering, and I eat the meat. Throughout this process, it started as mine; it remains, in the sense Rabbi Yosi HaGlili means, mine. The laws of torts apply; the laws of oaths apply; it is *mammon ba'alim*. This is the case Rabbi Yosi HaGlili is addressing.

### Bechor as matnas

A *bechor* given to a *kohen* is categorically different. It was never the *kohen*'s own animal. The animal was born, became *sacred* automatically by virtue of being a firstborn, and was handed over to the *kohen* as a *matnas kehuna* — a divinely prescribed gift. The *kohen* did not start with this as his sheep. God gave the *bechor* to the *kohen* as part of the priestly entitlement; the *kohen* receives it from God's table. Just as the *kohen*'s portion of the breast and thigh of a *shelamim* — though *kodshim kalim* — is *mishulchan gavoa*, because it flows to the *kohen* from God's dispensation rather than from the *kohen*'s own prior ownership, so too the *bechor* in its entirety is *mishulchan gavoa k'zachu*: the *kohen* acquired it from God's table.

### Not usable kiddushin

The conclusion follows: the *bechor* cannot be used for *kiddushin* because it is not the *kohen*'s actual property. It is a *matnas kehuna*, and as such it always has the character of something received from God rather than something owned outright. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's *mammon ba'alim* principle does not extend to it — not because the animal is slaughtered or because there is no *Beis HaMikdash*, but because the *kohen* never possessed this animal as his own from the outset. The *matnas kehuna* distinction cuts across the entire analysis and leaves the *bechor* outside the scope of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's ruling, regardless of whether the animal is alive or dead, in the Temple era or outside it.
