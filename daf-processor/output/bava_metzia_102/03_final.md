# Bava Metzia 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mezuzah in Rentals

### Baraita: Mezuzah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ – עַל הַשּׂוֹכֵר לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ מְזוּזָה, וּכְשֶׁהוּא יוֹצֵא – לֹא יִטְּלֶנָּה בְּיָדוֹ וְיֵצֵא. וּמִנׇּכְרִי, נוֹטְלָהּ בְּיָדוֹ וְיוֹצֵא. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁנְּטָלָהּ בְּיָדוֹ וְיָצָא, וְקָבַר אִשְׁתּוֹ וּשְׁנֵי בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one rents out a house to another,** the responsibility **to prepare a *mezuza* for it** and affix it is **upon the renter. And when he leaves, he may not take it in his hand and leave** with it; rather, he must leave it there. **But** if he rented a house **from a gentile, he may take it in his hand and leave** with it. **And** there was **an incident in** which a renter **took** his *mezuza* **in his hand and left** with it, **and** as a punishment **he** eventually **buried his wife and two sons.**

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *hamaskir bayit le-chavero* — one who rents a house to another. The *baraita* establishes that it is the tenant's (*socher's*) responsibility to affix the *mezuzah*. It should be noted that *mezuzah* here refers not to the decorative case, which people often confuse for the mitzvah object itself, but to the scroll. The *baraita* continues: *u-cheshe-hu yotzei lo yittalena be-yado ve-yotze* — when the tenant moves out, he may not take the *mezuzah* with him. This is a point of considerable practical importance for rental properties. However, *u-mi-nochri* — if the next occupant will be a non-Jewish tenant, then *notla be-yado ve-yotzei* — he may take the *mezuzah* when he leaves.

### Tragic Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר?! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Was the **incident** cited **to contradict** the ruling immediately preceding it, which permits one to take the *mezuza*? **Rav Sheshet said:** The incident relates **to the first clause.**

The *baraita* then records a sobering episode: *u-ma'aseh be-echad she-natla be-yado ve-yatza* — there was a case of someone who improperly took the *mezuzah* with him upon departure, and subsequently *ve-kavar ishto u-shenei vanav* — his wife and two children predeceased him. A question immediately arises: *ma'aseh li-settor* — is this story cited to contradict the ruling? Rav Sheshet clarifies: *arisha* — no, the story comes to confirm the *reisha*, the opening clause. It reinforces the obligation to leave the *mezuzot* behind when the next tenant is Jewish; it does not come to revise the rule about a non-Jewish tenant.

### Eretz Yisrael vs. Diaspora

One complicating dimension of *hilkhot mezuzah* is the distinction between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Chutz La'aretz*, which affects practical details — in particular, the grace period one has after moving in before being obligated to affix *mezuzot*. As for the rule about leaving the *mezuzah* upon departure, the practical *halakhic* guidance is: when one knows for certain that a Jewish tenant will be moving in after, the obligation is to leave the *mezuzah* itself in place. A common recommendation is to remove the decorative case — which can be costly — and leave the scroll, then acquire new *mezuzot* for the next residence. A quality *mezuzah* scroll can run between twenty and fifty dollars, which is a non-trivial consideration.

### Reasons for Mezuzah

Two explanations are offered by the *rishonim* for why a departing tenant should not remove the *mezuzah*. *Tosafot* grounds the concern in the danger of *mazikin* — harmful spiritual forces — that enter a house bereft of *mezuzot*. The *Ritva* frames the matter more positively: the *mezuzah* on the doorpost brings God's *shekhinah* to dwell within and protect the home, and removing it withdraws that divine presence. The animating value, then, is to increase rather than diminish the places where God's presence resides.

### Modern Rental Uncertainty

A practical question arises directly from the *baraita*: in contemporary living situations, one often does not know whether the next occupant will be Jewish or not. In such cases of genuine uncertainty, the general sense is that a tenant should take the *mezuzot* down upon moving out — not only because of the ambiguity, but because of a further concern: that a *mezuzah* left behind might be discarded or treated with disrespect. Unless one knows with certainty that the incoming tenant is Jewish, the precautionary approach would be to remove the *mezuzah* scroll.

### Nochri Terminology

The term *nochri* appears in this passage uncensored — that is, without being replaced by the censored circumlocution *oved kokhavim u-mazalot* (or its abbreviation *akkum*) that is found in many other places in the *gemara*. The reason is straightforward: there is nothing derogatory about the use of *nochri* in this context. The statement is simply that the outgoing Jewish tenant is permitted to take the *mezuzah* because a non-Jewish occupant has no obligation in it. A non-Jewish tenant moving in would have no cause for offense at such a practice. Many other places in the *gemara* where non-Jews are mentioned in more charged legal contexts had those terms altered by censors, but this instance presented no such sensitivity.

### Multi-Unit Buildings

A further question concerns multi-unit buildings: when a *maskir* owns many apartments within the same structure, who bears the responsibility for *mezuzot* in each unit? Even when individual apartments are occupied by separate tenants, the question is whether the overarching obligation falls on the owner of the building as a whole. This follows from the larger question of why it is the *socher* who bears the responsibility in the first place rather than the *maskir*.

### Rashi: Doorway Analysis

The underlying rationale for placing the obligation on the tenant is explained by *Rashi* — not in this *gemara* directly, but in the *gemara* in *Pesachim*, 4a. *Rashi*'s analysis there holds that the obligation follows from who passes through the doorway. It is the person living in the dwelling — the one who regularly enters and exits — who is the primary subject of the *mezuzah* obligation, and therefore the *socher* rather than the *maskir* bears the responsibility.

## Manure & Ash Rights

### Mishna: Manure/Ash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַזֶּבֶל שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְאֵין לַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶלָּא הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַתַּנּוּר וּמִן הַכִּירַיִם בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The manure** found in the courtyard of a rented house is the property **of the landlord, and the renter has** rights **only** to the ashes **that come out of the oven and the stove,** which can also be used as a fertilizer.

The *mishnah* proceeds to address rights over byproducts produced during a tenancy: *ha-zevel shel ba'al ha-bayit* — the manure belongs to the property owner. However, *ve-ein la-socher ella ha-yotzei min ha-tannur u-min ha-kiirayim bilvad* — all the *socher* receives are the ashes produced from his use of the oven (*tannur*) and the stove (*kiirayim*) that are in the *chatzer*. The underlying scenario is one where the *socher* rented the house but not the *chatzer* itself. While the tenant has license to use the *chatzer* — to keep animals there, to use the oven — he has no rental claim on the *chatzer* as such. Manure deposited in the *chatzer* therefore belongs to the owner of the *chatzer*, while ashes from wood the tenant himself burned belong to the tenant.

### Foreign Animals Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּחָצֵר דַּאֲגִירָ[א] לֵיהּ לְשׂוֹכֵר וְתוֹרֵי דְּשׂוֹכֵר – אַמַּאי שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת? אֶלָּא בְּחָצֵר דְּלָא אֲגִירָא לְשׂוֹכֵר וְתוֹרֵי דְּמַשְׂכִּיר, פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With what are we dealing? If we say** that the mishna is referring **to a courtyard that is rented out to the renter, or** where the manure was produced by the **oxen of the renter,** then **why** should it be the property **of the landlord?** It is clearly the property of the renter. **Rather,** the mishna must be **referring to a courtyard that is not rented out to the renter, and** the manure was produced by the **oxen of the landlord.** The Gemara asks: But if so, the ruling is **obvious** and need not have been taught.

The *gemara* probes the precise case the *mishnah* is addressing. *Bemai askinan* — what are the circumstances? If the *chatzer* was rented to the *socher* and the animals in it belong to the *socher*, why should the manure belong to the *ba'al ha-bayit*? And if the *chatzer* was not rented to the *socher* and the animals also belong to the *maskir*, the answer is equally obvious — of course the manure belongs to the property owner. *Lo tzericha* — the *mishnah* is needed for a third case: *be-chatzer de-maskir ve-torei de-atu me-alma* — the *chatzer* remains under the owner's control, but the animals that deposited the manure wandered in from elsewhere, belonging to third parties. In such a case, the *hava amina* — the initial assumption one might have — is that the *socher*, who has some use of the *chatzer* and could have scooped up the manure before the owner was aware, might be entitled to it, since the manure was *hefker*, ownerless. Animals do not retain legal ownership of their excrement. Yet the *mishnah* teaches otherwise: the *chatzer* of the *ba'al ha-bayit* performs a *kinyan chatzer* — an acquisition on behalf of the owner — and that acquisition overrides any claim the *socher* might have made.

### R. Yosi b. Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא: בְּחָצֵר דְּמַשְׂכִּיר, וְתוֹרֵי דְּאָתוּ מֵעָלְמָא קָמוּ בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the ruling is **necessary** in a case **where** the manure is **in a courtyard of the landlord, and** the source of the manure was **oxen that came from the world** at large and **stood in** the courtyard and produced the manure. The mishna rules that in such a case, the manure belongs to the landlord.

This leads the *gemara* to invoke a well-known principle: *mesaye lei le-Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina* — the *mishnah* supports the ruling of Rabbi Yosi the son of Rabbi Chanina. The principle is stated explicitly: *de-amar Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina chatzero shel adam koneh lo she-lo mi-da'ato* — a person's *chatzer* can acquire ownerless property on his behalf even without his knowledge. When the animal deposits manure in the owner's *chatzer*, the *chatzer* effects a *kinyan* automatically, preempting any attempt by the *socher* to claim it first.

### Lost Objects Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: חֲצֵרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנָה לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: That ruling in the mishna that any manure deposited in the landlord's courtyard belongs to him **supports** the ruling **of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: A person's courtyard effects acquisition for him** of an item placed in it, even **without his knowledge.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this principle from a *baraita*: *im amar kol metziot she-yavo le-tocho ha-yom tikni li chatzeri* — if someone declares that any lost objects entering his *chatzer* today should be acquired for him by his *chatzer*, *lo amar klum* — his declaration is legally meaningless. But this seems to contradict Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina: if the *chatzer* can acquire on his behalf without his knowledge, surely it should do so all the more when he has explicitly declared his intent? The apparent contradiction is pointed.

### Guarded Courtyard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אִם אָמַר ״כׇּל מְצִיאוֹת שֶׁיָּבֹאוּ לְתוֹכוֹ הַיּוֹם תִּקְנֶה לִי חֲצֵרִי״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. וְאִם אִיתָא לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא חֲצֵרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנָה לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ, אַמַּאי לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this ruling from a *baraita*: **If** one **says: Any lost items that come into** my courtyard **today, my courtyard should effect acquisition** of them **for me, he has not said anything** of legal significance, and does not acquire those items. The Gemara explains the objection: **And if it is so, that this** ruling **that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** is correct, i.e., that **a person's courtyard effects acquisition for him** of an item placed in it, even **without his knowledge,** then **why has he not said anything** of legal significance?

The *gemara* resolves the challenge: the *baraita*'s case involves a *chatzer she-einah mishtameret* — a *chatzer* that is not properly enclosed or guarded. The mechanism of *kinyan chatzer* is not a mystical power by which property automatically draws objects into legal ownership. Rather, the principle rests on the reality of control: if something ownerless lands in a well-enclosed space, no one else will come to retrieve it before the owner. Ownership requires control, and *kinyan chatzer* works only for a *chatzer ha-mishtameret* — one that is fenced and guarded such that the owner can be presumed to retain dominion over whatever enters it. In an unenclosed space, there is no such presumption, and therefore no *kinyan* is effected — whether or not the owner made a prior declaration.

### Psychological Fence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּחָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the difficulty: **With what are we dealing here** in the *baraita*? We are dealing **with a courtyard that is not secured,** as the *halakha* is that such a courtyard does not effect acquisition of items for its owner.

The *gemara* then reads the continuation of that same *baraita*: *yatzah lo shem metziah ba'ir, devarim kayamin* — if word has spread through the town that a lost object has landed in this *chatzer*, then his declaration is legally effective. How can this be if the *chatzer* is unenclosed? The *gemara* explains: *keivan de-yatzah lo shem metziah ba'ir, mibdal bdili inshi minei* — once people in town are talking about the lost object having entered this particular field, they will distance themselves from it and not attempt to retrieve it. *Ve-havaia lah ke-chatzer ha-mishtameret* — and thus it functions as a *chatzer ha-mishtameret*, not because of a physical fence but because of a psychological one. Public knowledge of the owner's claim effectively deters interference, replicating the practical effect of enclosure.

### Manure from Air

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי אֵימָא סֵיפָא: יָצָא לוֹ שֵׁם מְצִיאָה בָּעִיר – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין. וְאִי בְּחָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת, כִּי יָצָא לוֹ שֵׁם מְצִיאָה בָּעִיר, מַאי הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this resolution: **If so, say** and try to explain accordingly **the latter clause** of the *baraita* that states: If **knowledge of** the existence of that **lost item spread through the town, his statement stands** and his courtyard acquires it. The Gemara explains the difficulty: **And if** the *baraita* is referring **to a courtyard that is not secured,** even **where knowledge of** the existence of that **lost item spread through the town, what of it?** Such a courtyard is unable to effect acquisition for its owner of items placed in it.

A third attempt to disprove Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina's principle comes from another *baraita*: *zevel ha-yotzei min ha-tannur u-min ha-kira ve-ha-koleit min ha-avir, harei hu shelo* — whatever comes from the oven and the stove, and anything snatched from the air, belongs to the one who grabs it. *Ve-she-ba-refet ve-she-ba-chatzer shel ba'al ha-bayit* — but things remaining in the stable or in the *chatzer* of the owner belong to the *ba'al ha-bayit*. The challenge to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina: if the *chatzer* acquires on behalf of the owner even without his knowledge, why should something grabbed from the air belong to the one who grabbed it? *Avir chatzero hu* — the airspace of the *chatzer* is legally equivalent to the *chatzer* itself, so the owner's *chatzer* should have already acquired the manure while it was in the air, before anyone could snatch it.

### Abaye: Container

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּיָצָא לוֹ שֵׁם מְצִיאָה בָּעִיר – מִיבְדָּל בְּדִילִי אִינָשֵׁי מִינַּהּ, וְהָוְיָא לַהּ כְּחָצֵר הַמִּשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Once knowledge of** the existence of that **lost item spreads though the town, people withdraw themselves from it,** as they assume that the owner of the courtyard will take it. Therefore, no one will even try to take it **and** the courtyard **will be like a secured courtyard** which can effect acquisition of items for its owner.

Abaye resolves the challenge factually rather than conceptually: *be-madbik kli be-shulei para* — the *baraita* is describing a case where someone has attached a container directly beneath the animal to catch the manure as it falls. In such a case, the manure passes directly from the animal into the container, never entering the airspace of the *chatzer* at all. There is therefore no moment during which the owner's *chatzer* could effect a *kinyan* on it.

### Rava: Midair Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: זֶבֶל הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַתַּנּוּר וּמִן הַכִּירַיִם וְהַקּוֹלֵט מִן הָאֲוִיר – הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁלּוֹ, וְשֶׁבָּרֶפֶת וְשֶׁבֶּחָצֵר – שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. וְאִם אִיתָא לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר חֲצֵרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנָה לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ, קוֹלֵט מֵאֲוִיר אַמַּאי הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁלּוֹ? אֲוִיר חֲצֵרוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rabbi Yosei's ruling from a *baraita*: **Refuse of the oven and of the stove,** i.e., ashes, **and that which is collected** in the renter's vessel **from** the **airspace** of the courtyard, **is** the renter's property. **And** refuse **that is in the cowshed and in the courtyard,** i.e., the manure, is the property **of the landlord.** The Gemara explains the question: **And if it is so, that this** ruling **that Rabbi Yossi, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** is correct, i.e., that **a person's courtyard effects acquisition for him** of an item placed in it even **without his knowledge,** then with regard to refuse **collected from the airspace** of the courtyard, **why is it** the renter's property? It was first in the **airspace of** the landlord's **courtyard,** and should consequently be acquired by him immediately.

Rava disagrees and offers a more conceptual resolution. *Avir she-ein sofo lanuach lav ke-munach dami* — airspace in which an object is not ultimately going to come to rest is not legally equivalent to the ground of the *chatzer*. In the case of the *baraita*, someone intercepted the manure while it was airborne, which itself demonstrates that it was not destined to land in the *chatzer*. Since it was not going to rest there, the airspace cannot be treated as part of the *chatzer*, and therefore no *kinyan chatzer* was effected. The key principle at stake is *klutah ke-mi she-hunchah damya* — the question of whether an object in motion through the air is treated as if already resting on the ground beneath it, a debate that runs through *Hilkhot Shabbat* and *Bava Kamma*.

### Airspace Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּמַדְבִּיק כְּלִי בְּשׁוּלֵי פָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the renter **attached** his **vessel to a cow's rear.** Any manure produced by the cow would immediately enter the renter's vessel, without first entering the airspace of the courtyard, and the renter would consequently acquire it.

The *rishonim* debate how exactly to understand *klutah ke-mi she-hunchah damya*: does one imagine the ground rising up to meet the object, or the object projected downward onto the surface? But for Rava, the conceptual point is clear: the fact that someone was able to interrupt the object's trajectory mid-flight breaks the presumption that the object was ever going to land in the *chatzer*. If it was not going to land, the airspace cannot function as the *chatzer*'s surrogate for purposes of *kinyan*.

### Wallet Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲוִיר שֶׁאֵין סוֹפוֹ לָנוּחַ – לָאו כְּמוּנָּח דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** An item in the **airspace** of a courtyard **that will not eventually come to rest** in the courtyard itself **is not** regarded **as though it had come to rest.** Accordingly, even if the refuse traveled through the airspace of the courtyard, since it was always on course to enter the renter's vessel, it is not acquired by means of the landlord's courtyard.

This same conceptual issue underlies a question Rava himself raises: *zarak arnaki be-fetach zeh ve-yatza be-fetach zeh, mahu* — someone threw a wallet in through one door of a house and it exited through the other door. Did the owner of the house acquire the wallet while it was passing through? The question is whether *avir she-ein sofo lanuach ke-munach dami o lav ke-munach dami* — whether airspace through which an object passes without ultimately resting there is treated as equivalent to the ground. The fact that Rava poses this as an open question (*teku*) illuminates his position: he is consistent in holding that when an object is not destined to come to rest in a space, that space's airspace does not function as *chatzer* for purposes of acquisition. The distinction between Abaye and Rava thus turns on whether the issue is one of *metziut* — practical fact (was there a container?) — or legal concept (does unresolved airspace confer *kinyan*?).

### Stable & Courtyard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמִי פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא? וְהָא מִיבְעֵי בָּעֵי לַהּ, דְּבָעֵי רָבָא: זָרַק אַרְנָקִי בְּפֶתַח זֶה וְיָצָאתָה בְּפֶתַח זֶה, מַהוּ? אֲוִיר שֶׁאֵין סוֹפוֹ לָנוּחַ כְּמוּנָּח דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָאו כְּמוּנָּח דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** this principle really so **obvious to Rava? But didn't he raise it as a dilemma? As Rava raised a dilemma:** If one **cast a purse through this doorway** of a house and it went through the house **and exited through that doorway, what is** the *halakha*? Is an item in the **airspace** of a courtyard **that will not eventually come to rest** in the courtyard itself regarded **as though it had come to rest, or** is it **not** regarded **as though it had come to rest?**

The *baraita* also rules: *ve-she-ba-refet ve-she-ba-chatzer harei eilu shel ba'al ha-bayit* — things remaining in the stable or in the *chatzer* belong to the *ba'al ha-bayit*. Abaye clarifies the reading: it should be understood as *she-ba-refet she-ba-chatzer* — items in the stable that is located within the *chatzer*, not two separate locations. Whatever is already resting within the owner's enclosed domain belongs to him.

### Rav Ashi: Stable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁבָּרֶפֶת וְשֶׁבֶּחָצֵר, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. תַּרְתֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the latter clause of the above cited *baraita*: **And** refuse **that is in the cowshed and in the courtyard** is the property **of the landlord.** The Gemara asks: Can these **two** statements coincide? By stating that the refuse in the cowshed belongs to the landlord, it indicates that the refuse in the courtyard belongs to the renter. How, then, can the *baraita* continue to rule that even the refuse in the courtyard belongs to the landlord?

From this reading, Rav Ashi draws a further inference: *zot omeret ha-maskir chatzero stam lo hiskir refet she-bah* — one who rents out his *chatzer* without specification has not included the *refet* (stable) in the rental, since the *refet* is treated as a distinct space separate from the *chatzer* proper.

### Dovecote Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְשֶׁבָּרֶפֶת שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: הַמַּשְׂכִּיר חֲצֵירוֹ סְתָם – לֹא הִשְׂכִּיר רֶפֶת שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: And** refuse **that is in the cowshed that is** located **in the courtyard** rented out to the renter is the property **of the landlord.** Extrapolating from Abaye's statement, **Rav Ashi said: That is to say** that **one who rents out his courtyard without specification** of what is included in the rental agreement **has not rented out a cowshed that is** located **in it.**

A third challenge to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina's principle of *kinyan chatzer she-lo mi-da'ato* is mounted from a *baraita* in *Chullin*: *yonei shovach ve-yonei aliyah chayavot be-shiluach ve-asurot be-gezel mipnei darchei shalom* — doves of a dovecote and doves nesting in an attic are subject to *shiluach ha-ken* (the obligation to send away the mother bird from her eggs before taking them), and taking them is prohibited on account of *gezel*, though only rabbinically, *mipnei darchei shalom* — for the sake of communal peace and social order. The legal status of dovecote doves is inherently liminal: the owner constructs the dovecote, but the doves fly freely between locations and ownership is only loosely maintained, recognized by the rabbis on a *derabban* level for reasons of *darchei shalom*. The challenge to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina: if a person's *chatzer* acquires ownerless objects on his behalf without his knowledge, then as soon as a dove alights in his dovecote and lays eggs, both the dove and its eggs should be fully acquired by *kinyan chatzer* — making them owned property and exempting the owner from *shiluach ha-ken* entirely, since the obligation of *shiluach* does not apply to one's own birds (*perat le-mizuman*).

### Rava: Egg Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: יוֹנֵי שׁוֹבָךְ וְיוֹנֵי עֲלִיָּיה חַיָּיבוֹת בְּשִׁילּוּחַ, וַאֲסוּרוֹת בְּגָזֵל מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rabbi Yosei's ruling from another *baraita*: There is a mitzva to dispatch a mother bird if one wishes to take eggs from her nest (Deuteronomy 22:6–7). The mitzva applies only if the bird and eggs are ownerless. **Doves of a dovecote and doves of an attic are subject to the obligation of dispatching** the mother bird, as they are ownerless. Nevertheless, **they are subject to the prohibition of robbery, due to** a rabbinic ordinance to maintain the **ways of peace.**

Rava resolves this with a careful analysis of timing: *beitza be-tziat rubba hi de-itch-ayva lah be-shiluach u-kanyah lo kanyah ad de-nafla le-chatzero* — the obligation of *shiluach* attaches at the moment the egg is majority-emerged from the mother bird, but *kinyan chatzer* cannot take effect until the egg has actually landed in the nest. There is therefore a brief but legally decisive window — the moment from majority-emergence until landing — during which the *mitzvah* of *shiluach ha-ken* is in force yet the egg has not been acquired by the *chatzer*. The egg remains ownerless in that instant; the *mitzvah* obligation is therefore genuine, and the *baraita* cannot be used to disprove Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina's principle, since the two legal facts (obligation of *shiluach* and *kinyan chatzer*) operate at different moments.

### Rav Yehuda Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: חֲצֵרוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנָה לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ. קְרִי כָּאן ״כִּי יִקָּרֵא״ – פְּרָט לִמְזוּמָּן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the question: **And if it is so, that this** ruling **that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** is correct, i.e., that **a person's courtyard effects acquisition for him** of an item placed in it even **without his knowledge,** then a dovecote or attic will effect acquisition for its owner of any eggs inside them. Accordingly, one should **apply here** the principle that the mitzva to dispatch the mother bird from upon her nest applies only: **"If it happened** before you" (Deuteronomy 22:6) which **excludes** a bird or egg that is **readily accessible,** such as one that one owns, from the mitzva to dispatch the mother. Yet the *baraita* rules that the mitzva does apply in this case.

A further dimension is introduced from the teaching of Rav Yehuda quoting Rav: *assur li-zekhot be-betzim kol zman she-ha-eim rovetzet aleihem* — it is prohibited to acquire the eggs so long as the mother bird is still sitting on them, as the verse states *shale'ach te-shale'ach et ha-eim* — you must first send away the mother — *ve-hadar et ha-banim tikach lach* — and only thereafter take the eggs. This prohibition is operative: even if the eggs have landed in the *chatzer*, the owner cannot legally acquire them through *kinyan chatzer* while the mother remains on the nest, because the *kinyan* is blocked by the prohibition against taking the eggs before *shiluach*.

### Courtyard as Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בֵּיצָה בִּיצִיאַת רוּבָּהּ הוּא דְּאִיחַיַּיבָה לַהּ בְּשִׁילּוּחַ, וּמִיקְנָא לָא קַנְיָא עַד דְּנָפְלָה לַחֲצֵרוֹ, וְכִי קָתָנֵי ״חַיָּיבוֹת בְּשִׁילּוּחַ״ – מִקַּמֵּי דְּתִיפּוֹל לַחֲצֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **from the** time of the **emergence of the majority of an egg** from a mother bird's body **that** one **becomes subject to the obligation of dispatching** her from upon her eggs. **And** the owner of a courtyard **does not acquire** the egg **until it** fully emerges and **falls into his courtyard. And** therefore, **when** the *baraita* **teaches** that in the case of doves of a dovecote and of an attic, one is **subject to the obligation of dispatching** the mother bird, it is referring to a time **before** the egg **falls into his courtyard.**

This leads to a general structural principle: *kol heicha de-ihu matzi zakhei lei, zakhei lei chatzeiro; ve-chol heicha de-ihu lo matzi zakhei lei, chatzeiro nami lo zakhei lei* — wherever the owner himself could effect an acquisition, his *chatzer* can do so on his behalf; wherever he himself could not acquire — because doing so is prohibited — his *chatzer* likewise cannot acquire for him. This parallels the principle governing *shlichut* (agency): an agent can only perform acts that the principal himself could perform. The *chatzer* functions here as a kind of agent, and its power to acquire is correspondingly bounded by the owner's own legal capacity.

### Darchei Shalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לִזְכּוֹת בַּבֵּיצִים כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהָאֵם רוֹבֶצֶת עֲלֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שַׁלֵּחַ תְּשַׁלַּח אֶת הָאֵם״ וַהֲדַר ״אֶת הַבָּנִים תִּקַּח לָךְ״. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דִּנְפַלָה לַהּ לַחֲצֵרוֹ, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיהוּ מָצֵי זָכֵי לֵיהּ – חֲצֵרוֹ זָכְיָא לֵיהּ, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיהוּ לָא מָצֵי זָכֵי לֵיהּ – חֲצֵרוֹ נָמֵי לָא זָכְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And now that Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: It is prohibited to acquire the eggs as long as the mother bird is upon them, as it is** first **stated: "Send away the mother" and** only **then:** "And **take the young for yourself"** (Deuteronomy 22:7), **even** if **you say that** the eggs fully emerged and **fell into his courtyard,** he will not acquire them, because in **any case** in which a courtyard owner **is able to acquire** an item by himself, **his courtyard can effect acquisition** of it **for him, but** in **any case** in which **he is unable to acquire** an item by himself, **his courtyard cannot effect acquisition** of it **for him either.**

This raises an apparent circularity: if the eggs can only be acquired after *shiluach*, and *shiluach* must be performed before taking the eggs, when exactly does the owner acquire them? And if a person who takes the eggs without performing *shiluach* is guilty of actual *gezel*, why does the *baraita* say the prohibition is only *mipnei darchei shalom*? The *gemara* resolves this by specifying that the case concerns a *katan* — a minor — who is not personally obligated in the *mitzvah* of *shiluach ha-ken* but can have financial ownership. The follow-up question — *katan bar darchei shalom hu*, is a minor really subject to the *takkanah* of *darchei shalom*? — is answered: *aviv shel katan chayav lehachazir lo mipnei darchei shalom* — the father of the minor is obligated to return on his child's behalf what was taken in violation of *darchei shalom*. The prohibition, it emerges, is only *mi-derabban* — rabbinic — by virtue of *darchei shalom*, not a true *gezel* from the Torah. This is consistent with the overall picture of dovecote doves: legal ownership is maintained only at the *derabban* level, grounded in considerations of communal peace rather than strict property law.

## Leap Year Rent

### Mishna: Leap Month

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ לְשָׁנָה, נִתְעַבְּרָה הַשָּׁנָה – נִתְעַבְּרָה לַשּׂוֹכֵר. הִשְׂכִּיר לוֹ לָחֳדָשִׁים, נִתְעַבְּרָה הַשָּׁנָה – נִתְעַבְּרָה לַמַּשְׂכִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who rents out a house to another for a year** and then **the year was intercalated,** adding an additional month to that year, the fact that **it was intercalated** is **to** the benefit of **the renter.** Since the rental was defined in terms of a year, the additional month is automatically included, and the renter need not pay additional rent for it. If a landlord **rented out** a house **to** another for a year, with the price set as a certain sum **for** each of the **months,** and then **the year was intercalated,** the fact that **it was intercalated** is **to** the benefit of **the landlord.**

The *mishnah* presents the next case: *ha-maskir bayit le-chavero li-shana* — one who rents a house to another for one year. In mishnaic times, every year had the potential to be a leap year, with the *beit din* determining at the end of *Adar* whether a second *Adar* would be added. The question posed is: when a thirteenth month is intercalated into the year, who bears the rental obligation for that extra month? The *mishnah* rules: *nit'abra le-socher* — the leap month is absorbed by the *socher*. Since the agreement was for one year, and this is still one year — albeit a year with thirteen months — the *socher* must pay for it. However, *hiskir lo le-chodashim* — if the agreement was structured month by month, then *nit'abra la-maskir* — the added month benefits the *maskir*, who is entitled to an additional month's rent beyond the twelve already paid.

### Tzippori Bathhouse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּצִיפּוֹרִי בְּאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׂכַר מֶרְחָץ מֵחֲבֵירוֹ בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר זָהָב לְשָׁנָה, מִדִּינַר זָהָב לְחֹדֶשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** **An incident** occurred **in Tzippori involving one who rented a bathhouse from another** where they stated that the rent would be: **Twelve gold** dinars **per year, a gold dinar per month,** and then the year was intercalated.

A case from *Tzippori* is then reported: someone rented a bathhouse from his friend *bi-shneim asar zahav li-shana, mi-dinar zahav la-chodesh* — for twelve gold coins for the year, at one gold dinar per month. The agreement is redundant but ambiguous — it states both a yearly total and a monthly rate, and it is not clear whether the rental should be governed as a yearly or monthly arrangement.

### Split Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְלִפְנֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְאָמְרוּ: יַחְלוֹקוּ אֶת חֹדֶשׁ הָעִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this **incident came** to court **before Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and before Rabbi Yosei, and they said:** The two expressions have contradictory implications, and it is uncertain which expression should be followed. Therefore, the landlord and the renter **should divide the intercalary month** between them, i.e., the renter should pay half a gold dinar for it.

The case came before Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yosi, who ruled *yachloku et chodesh ha-ibur* — the rental cost of the thirteenth month should be split between the parties. Given the genuine ambiguity in the agreement, neither party can establish a clear exclusive claim.

### Chasorei Mechasra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר?! חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר זְהוּבִים לְשָׁנָה מִדִּינַר זָהָב לְחֹדֶשׁ – יַחְלוֹקוּ. וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּצִיפּוֹרִי בְּאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׂכַר מֶרְחָץ מֵחֲבֵירוֹ בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר זְהוּבִים לְשָׁנָה מִדִּינַר זָהָב לְחֹדֶשׁ, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְלִפְנֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְאָמְרוּ: יַחְלוֹקוּ אֶת חֹדֶשׁ הָעִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: Was **an incident** cited **to contradict** the mishna's initial ruling? The Gemara explains: The latter clause **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching: But if** the landlord **said to** the renter that the rent would be: **Twelve gold** dinars **per year, a gold dinar per month,** and then the year was intercalated, the intercalary month **should be divided** between them. **And in addition, an incident** like this occurred **in Tzippori involving one who rented a bathhouse from another,** and they stated that the rent would be: **Twelve gold** dinars **per year, a gold dinar per month,** and then the year was intercalated. **And** this **incident came** to court **before Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and before Rabbi Yosei, and they said:** The landlord and the renter **should divide the intercalary month** between them.

The *gemara* raises a difficulty: this case from *Tzippori* appears to contradict the *mishnah*'s straightforward rule, since by the normal interpretive principle one would resolve ambiguous language in favor of one party rather than splitting. The *gemara* therefore applies the formula *chasorei mechasra ve-hachi katani* — the *mishnah* is to be read as though it included additional material. Specifically, the *mishnah* should first state the rule: when the agreement explicitly contains both a yearly and monthly formulation (*bi-shneim asar zehuvim li-shana mi-dinar zahav la-chodesh*), the parties split the *ibur* month. Then the *mishnah* should record the *ma'aseh* from *Tzippori* as a case in which exactly this formulation arose — so the case confirms rather than contradicts the rule. The question of what *chasorei mechasra* actually means — whether it represents a genuine emendation of the *mishnah*'s text or simply a clarification of its legal intent — is itself a substantial debate. The more traditionalist reading holds that the *amora'im* are not revising the text but specifying how it should be understood; a more revisionist reading would understand the *mishnah*'s text as actually incomplete and the *gemara* as filling it in.

### Rav: All to Landlord

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אִי הֲוַאי הָתָם, הֲוָה יָהֵיבְנָא לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ לְמַשְׂכִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: If I had been there,** as a judge, **I would have given the entire** month **to the landlord** and ruled that the renter must pay for it. Rav understood that the statement defining the rent should be understood based on the final expression used, i.e., a gold dinar per month.

Rav states: *ei havei hatam hava yahavina lei kulei le-mashkir* — had this case come before me, I would have awarded the entire thirteenth month's rent to the *maskir*. The reasoning rests on the principle that the *maskir* holds a *chazakah* on the property itself. Where two competing presumptions clash — the *socher*'s *chezkat mammon* (possession of his own money) and the *maskir*'s property *chazakah* — Rav holds that the property *chazakah* is the stronger claim, entitling the *maskir* to the full month.

### Tfus Lashon Acharon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? ״תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן אַחֲרוֹן״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav **teaching us?** Could he be teaching that when a statement consists of two expressions with contradictory implications one should **attend** only to **the last statement?**

The *gemara* asks what new principle Rav's ruling is intended to convey — *mai ka-mashma lan*. The answer: *tefus lashon acharon* — seize, i.e., follow, the last clause of an ambiguous agreement. In this case, the last clause stated the monthly rate (*mi-dinar zahav la-chodesh*), and it is by that clause that the arrangement is governed, giving the *maskir* the superior claim. A parallel is cited from the school of Rav: an *asteira* is worth one hundred *ma'ot*, stated in both directions (*asteira ma'ah, ma'ah asteira*) to indicate a genuine equivalence between two coin denominations rather than a one-directional definition. The point of stating both formulations is to make clear that either can govern — and so too in the rental case, the direction in which the clauses are stated matters for *tefus lashon acharon*.

### Shmuel: Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּבָא בְּאֶמְצַע חֹדֶשׁ עָסְקִינַן. אֲבָל בָּא בִּתְחִלַּת חֹדֶשׁ – כּוּלֵּיהּ לְמַשְׂכִּיר, בָּא בְּסוֹף חֹדֶשׁ – כּוּלֵּיהּ לְשׂוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said** in explanation of the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yosei: In the mishna, **we are dealing with** a case **where** the landlord **came** before the court **in the middle of the month.** Only in that case is the disputed month divided between them. **But** if **he came at the beginning of the month, the entire** month's rent would be awarded **to the landlord,** and if **he came at the end of the month, the entire** month's rent would be awarded **to the renter.** The reason is that it is uncertain whether or not one always acts in accordance with the final expression, and, consequently, whether the renter must pay for the extra month. With regard to upcoming days of rental, since the landlord has possession of the property, he can demand rent. And with regard to days that have already passed, where the question is whether the renter must pay for them, since he is in possession of his money, he cannot be made to pay. Shmuel does not hold that only the second statement is followed.

Shmuel offers a distinct reading of the *Tzippori* case. The reason Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yosi ruled to split the month is not because of the ambiguity of the language per se, but because the *maskir* arrived to collect rent *be-emtza chodesh* — in the middle of the thirteenth month. Shmuel thus introduces a temporal dimension that is absent from Rav's analysis: the *maskir*'s ability to claim the month depends on when he shows up to assert that claim.

### Shmuel: Three Cases

Shmuel's position produces three distinct outcomes. If the *maskir* arrives at the beginning of the thirteenth month to collect, *kulah la-maskir* — the entire month belongs to the *maskir*, because he has promptly asserted his claim. If he arrives in the middle of the month, *yachloku* — they split it, because the *socher* occupied the property for part of the month without challenge. If the *maskir* appears only at the end of the month, *kulah la-socher* — the entire month belongs to the *socher*, because the *maskir* effectively forfeited his claim by his delay. Shmuel's position thus represents a middle path: like Rav, he recognizes that the *maskir* has the stronger underlying claim, but unlike Rav he holds that the *maskir* can only collect from the moment he actually appears to assert that claim. In monetary law, the presumption generally is that *halakha* follows Shmuel, *be-dinei*.

### Rav Nachman: Chazaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קַרְקַע בְּחֶזְקַת בְּעָלֶיהָ קַיֶּימֶת. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – ״תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן אַחֲרוֹן״, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב? אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָפֵיךְ מֵיפָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman said:** I would have ruled that since the *halakha* is that **land is** always **in its owner's possession,** even when rented out, the landlord is entitled to the entire month's rent. The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav Naḥman **teaching us?** Could his ruling be based on saying that one should **attend** only to **the last statement?** But then **this is** identical to the ruling **of Rav.** The Gemara clarifies: Rav Naḥman's ruling is unlike Rav's, as Rav Naḥman would award the extra month to the landlord **even where** the order of the two expressions **is reversed.** Rather, as he explained, his ruling is based on the fact the landlord has possession of the property.

Rav Nachman offers a third interpretation. His ruling is: *karka be-chezkat be'aleha kayemet* — land remains in the presumptive possession of its owner. The *maskir* always holds the superior claim by virtue of property *chazakah*, irrespective of timing or the language of the agreement. Some *rishonim* suggest that Rav Nachman is not disagreeing with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling directly, but rather holding that the *mishnah* reflects the position of Sumchus — *mammon ha-mutal be-safek yachloku* — that doubtful monetary claims are split. Since the *halakha* follows *rabanan* against Sumchus, Rav Nachman rules that the property *chazakah* of the *maskir* is dispositive, and the *maskir* is entitled to the full month.

### Tfus Lashon Reconciled

The *gemara* then reconciles Rav's principle of *tefus lashon acharon* with Rav Nachman's *chazakah*-based approach. The key observation is: *af al gav de-afich* — even if the clauses of the agreement were reversed (i.e., the monthly formulation stated first and the yearly formulation stated second), the analysis would shift accordingly. If *tefus lashon acharon* is the operative principle, then whichever clause comes last governs, and reversing the clauses would reverse the result — giving the *socher* the advantage if the yearly formulation came last. But if the underlying basis is *chazakah* (as per Rav Nachman), the result would remain constant regardless of the order of clauses, since the *maskir*'s property *chazakah* is not dependent on which clause was stated last.

## Proof in Disputes

### Payment Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי יַנַּאי: שׂוֹכֵר אָמַר נָתַתִּי, וּמַשְׂכִּיר אָמַר לֹא נָטַלְתִּי – עַל מִי לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yannai:** If **a renter says** to his landlord: **I** already **gave** the rental fee to you, **and** the **landlord says: I did not take** any payment from you, **upon whom is the burden of proof?**

A question is posed to Rabbi Yannai: where the *socher* claims *natati* — I have already paid the rent — and the *maskir* counters *lo natalti* — I did not receive it — who bears the burden of proof?

### Before/After Deadline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִי בְּתוֹךְ זְמַנּוֹ – תְּנֵינָא, אִי לְאַחַר זְמַנּוֹ – תְּנֵינָא. דִּתְנַן: מֵת הָאָב בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדָּה עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁנִּפְדָּה. לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁנִּפְדָּה, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dilemma: **When** did the renter make his claim? **If** it was **during his** rental **period, we** already **learned** the *halakha* in this case and the Sages would not have asked about it. Similarly, **if** it was **after his** rental **period, we** already **learned** the *halakha* in that case as well. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 49a): A father is obligated to redeem his firstborn son after the son is thirty days old by paying five shekels to a priest. If **the father died within thirty days** of the birth, the son **has the presumptive status of being unredeemed, until he brings a proof that he was redeemed.** If he died **after thirty days,** the son **has the presumptive status of having been redeemed, until** people **tell him that he was not redeemed.** It is apparent from this mishna that a person is presumed not to pay money before he must, and he is presumed to have paid money once he is required to do so. This logic can be applied to the paying of a rental fee.

The *gemara* notes that the answer depends on timing: *i be-toch zemano* — if the deadline for payment has not yet arrived, the *mishnah*'s ruling already covers this case; *i le-achar zemano* — if the deadline has passed, there is likewise a *mishnah* that addresses it.

### Bechorot Parallel

The parallel is drawn from the *mishnah* in *Bechorot*: *met ha-av be-toch sheloshim yom, be-chezkat she-lo nifdeh* — if a father dies within thirty days of his son's birth, before the time for *pidyon ha-ben* has arrived, the presumption is that the child has not yet been redeemed, and proof is required to establish otherwise. Conversely, *le-achar sheloshim yom, be-chezkat she-nifdeh* — if the father dies after thirty days, the presumption is that *pidyon ha-ben* was performed, and it falls on the *kohanim* to prove that it was not. The burden of proof follows the deadline.

### Debtor Advantage

The underlying monetary principle is this: a debtor has no obligation to pay before the deadline arrives, and therefore no assumption of early payment can be made. People naturally hold onto their money until the last moment. Consequently, if a claim is made before the due date, the *socher* who claims early payment bears the burden of proof — it is unusual to pay early, and that unusual claim requires evidence.

### Hamotzi Burden

The general rule of *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah* — the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract money from another — applies here with a significant exception. Before the payment deadline, the *maskir* is in the posture of the claimant and must prove non-payment, but the *socher* who claims he paid early must also prove it, since early payment is presumptively improbable. After the deadline has passed, however, the *chazakah* shifts: it is now assumed that the debt was paid, and the *maskir* who claims non-payment bears the burden.

### Payment Day Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא: בְּיוֹמָא דְּמִשְׁלַם זִמְנֵיהּ. מִי עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּפָרַע בְּיוֹמָא דְּמִשְׁלַם זִמְנֵיהּ, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to raise the dilemma in a case where the renter makes his claim **on the day on which his** rental **period is completed.** The dilemma is **whether a person is apt to pay** his debts **on the** very **day on which** the **period** in which to pay them **is completed, or** he is **not.**

A subtler question arises: *mi avidi inishei de-parai be-yomei de-mashlim zimnei o lo* — are people accustomed to paying precisely on the day the debt falls due, or does the *chazakah* shift only the following day? The question is whether the deadline day itself is presumptively the payment day, such that the assumption of payment attaches as of that day, or whether common practice is to pay somewhat after the due date.

### Sachir Bizmano

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, תְּנֵיתוּהָ:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to them:** Also this, **you have** already **learned** in a mishna (111a):

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this by reference to an established rule: *sachir bi-zmano nishba ve-notel* — a hired worker who has not received his wages when they fall due is entitled to take an oath and collect. The reason, as the *gemara* explains, is *mishum de-ba'al ha-bayit tarud* — because the employer is presumed to be preoccupied with his affairs and may have forgotten to pay. The fact that the worker swears and collects *bi-zmano* — precisely on the day the wages are due — indicates that the deadline day itself is the operative moment, and the *chazakah* attaches as of that day rather than the day after.
