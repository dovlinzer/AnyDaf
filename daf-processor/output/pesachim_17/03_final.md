# Pesachim 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Haggai's Questions

### Rav's Position Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב: אִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ כָּהֲנֵי. מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא אֶלָּא לְרַב? רַב ״מַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא״ תָּנֵי, אֲבָל מַשְׁקֵי בֵּי מַדְבְּחַיָּא — מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav said: The priests erred** in this regard, as those substances are actually impure. This source indicates that along with the meat, the stew, wine, and oil, which are liquids, also become ritually impure. The Gemara rejects this proof. **This** difficulty **is reasonable only** according **to Rav,** and **Rav teaches** that the testimony of Yosei ben Yo'ezer was that **the liquids of the slaughterhouse,** blood and water associated with the slaughter of offerings, do not become ritually impure. **However,** Rav agrees that **the liquids of the chamber of the altar,** wine and oil that accompany the offering on the altar, can **become impure.** The rabbinic decree that liquids can become impure is not in effect with regard to the liquids of the Temple slaughterhouse, but it is in effect with regard to the liquids offered on the altar. Therefore, the liquids listed by Haggai can become impure and transmit impurity by rabbinic law.

The sugya opens at the close of the discussion of Rav's interpretation of Yosi ben Yoezer. Rav's position was that Yosi ben Yoezer's famous testimony established that liquids are exempt from *tumah* as a matter of biblical law — *tumat mashkin* does not apply *mi-d'oraita*. Before the Gemara brings its final proof on this question, it pauses to examine the passage in Haggai, which the Gemara quotes as a *tashma* — a potential challenge or support to the discussion at hand.

### Haggai Pasuk Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״הֵן יִשָּׂא אִישׁ בְּשַׂר קֹדֶשׁ בִּכְנַף בִּגְדוֹ וְנָגַע בִּכְנָפוֹ אֶל הַלֶּחֶם וְאֶל הַנָּזִיד וְאֶל הַיַּיִן וְאֶל שֶׁמֶן וְאֶל כׇּל מַאֲכָל הֲיִקְדָּשׁ וַיַּעֲנוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים וַיֹּאמְרוּ לֹא (יִקְדָּשׁ)״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional proof. **Come** and **hear** that which was said to the prophet Haggai: "Thus said the Lord of Hosts: Ask now the priests with regard to the Torah, saying: **If a person bears hallowed flesh in the corner of his garment, and with his garment he touches bread, or stew, or wine, or oil, or any food, shall it be sacred? And the priests answered and said: No"** (Haggai 2:11–12). This question is asked with regard to the flesh of a creeping animal and whether or not the substances that come into contact with it become impure.

The prophet Haggai addresses the *kohanim* in the post-exilic period, posing what appears to be a parable framed as a series of legal questions about *tumah* and *taharah*. The relevant verses read:

> *Ko amar Hashem Tzeva'os: sha'al na es ha-kohanim Torah leimor: hein yisa ish b'sar kodesh bichnaf bigdo, v'naga bichnafo el ha-lechem v'el ha-nazid v'el ha-yayin v'el ha-shemen v'el kol ma'achal, ha-yitkadash? Va-ya'anu ha-kohanim va-yomru lo.*

The literal translation: if a person carries holy meat in the corner of his garment, and that corner touches bread, stew, wine, oil, or any food — does it become holy? The *kohanim* answered: no. Haggai then asked a second question:

> *Im yiga t'mei nefesh b'chol eileh, ha-yitma? Va-ya'anu ha-kohanim va-yomru yitma.*

If someone who is *tamei* by contact with a corpse touches any of these things, do they become *tamei*? The *kohanim* answered: yes. To this Haggai responded:

> *Ken ha-am ha-zeh v'chen ha-goy ha-zeh lefanai ne'um Hashem, v'chen kol ma'aseh yedeihem, v'asher yakrivu sham tamei hu.*

Haggai's apparent aim was to draw an analogy: he elicited the answer *tamei* so that he could apply it metaphorically to the nation and everything they bring upon the *mizbeach*.

### Sheretz Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: אִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ כָּהֲנֵי — רְבִיעִי בַּקֹּדֶשׁ בְּעָא מִינַּיְיהוּ, וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Rav said** that **the priests erred,** as Haggai **raised the dilemma before them** whether or not **consecrated** items become impure with **fourth-**degree ritual impurity. The question in the verse pertains to the following case: One is carrying a dead creeping animal in the corner of his garment and bread comes into contact with it, conferring upon the bread first-degree ritual impurity status; and stew comes in contact with the bread, conferring upon the stew second-degree ritual impurity status; and wine comes in contact with the stew, conferring upon the wine third-degree ritual impurity status; and oil comes into contact with the wine. The question is: In that case, does the wine confer upon the oil fourth-degree ritual impurity? **And** when **the priests said to him** that it is **pure,** they erred. In fact, the oil is disqualified with fourth-degree ritual impurity, because it is a consecrated item.

The Gemara, however, interprets the opening verses quite differently from their plain sense. Following Rashi's reading — which is really the only way to make sense of the Gemara's analysis — the passage is reread as describing a chain of *tumah* transmission. A *sheretz* (a dead creeping thing) is present in the corner of the man's garment. The *sheretz* in the corner touches the bread; the bread touches the stew; the stew touches the wine; the wine touches the oil. Each successive item contracts a progressively more distant level of *tumah* from the original source.

It should be noted that the verse also mentions *v'el kol ma'achal* — "and any food" — as a fifth item in the sequence, yet the Gemara entirely ignores this fifth element in its chain analysis, which is puzzling. Likewise, the plain sense of the verse has the *garment* touching the food items, not the *sheretz* inside it, but the Gemara reinterprets the possessive *bichnafo* to refer to the *sheretz* lodged in the corner. These interpretive moves are admittedly difficult, but the Gemara's purpose is not to read the verse peshat — it is to extract a halakhic question about the chain of *tumah*.

On this reading, the *kohanim* answered that the sequence posed no problem — and Rav says: *ishtabesh kahanei* — the *kohanim* made a mistake. This is initially surprising. Rav holds that *tumat mashkin* does not exist *mi-d'oraita*. How can Rav say the *kohanim* were wrong to declare these liquids *tahor*?

The Gemara resolves this directly: there is no contradiction for Rav, because Rav's exemption applied only to *mashkeh beit mitbachaya* — the liquids of the slaughterhouse, i.e., blood and water. Here, Haggai is asking about wine and oil — *mashkeh beit midbachaya*, the liquids of the *mizbeach* (the *dalet* and *zayin* are interchangeable in Aramaic, yielding *midbachaya* from *mizbe'ach*). Wine accompanies most *korbanot* as a *nesach*, and oil is mixed into the *minchah* offering. These are categorically different from slaughterhouse liquids, and Rav holds they are fully subject to *tumah*. Hence the *kohanim* erred when they declared the chain *tahor*.

### Revii vs. Chamishi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ושְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לָא אִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ כָּהֲנֵי — חֲמִישִׁי בַּקֹּדֶשׁ בְּעָא מִינַּיְיהוּ, וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: The priests did not err,** as Haggai **raised the dilemma before them** whether or not **consecrated** items become impure with **fifth-**degree ritual impurity. According to Shmuel's explanation, the case is as follows: The corner of the garment comes into contact with a dead creeping animal, conferring upon the garment first-degree ritual impurity status; bread comes into contact with the garment, conferring upon the bread second-degree ritual impurity status; stew comes in contact with the bread, conferring upon the stew third-degree ritual impurity status; and wine comes in contact with the stew, conferring upon the wine fourth-degree ritual impurity status; and oil comes into contact with the wine. In that case, does the wine confer upon the oil fifth-degree ritual impurity? **And** the priests correctly **said to him** that the oil is **pure.**

The Gemara now develops the argument in a formal dispute between Rav and Shmuel:

*Rav amar ishtabesh kahanei* — Rav says the *kohanim* were wrong. Why? *Revii bakodesh ba'u mineihu va-amru lei tahor* — Haggai was asking about a *revii*, a fourth-level *tumah*, and they wrongly declared it *tahor*. Working through the chain: the *sheretz* is the source (*av ha-tumah*); the bread it touches becomes a *rishon*; the stew becomes a *sheni*; the wine becomes a *shelishi*; and the oil is a *revii*. In the domain of *kodshim*, a *revii letumah* is still *tamei* — the *kohanim* should have said so, but they said *tahor*. That was their error.

*U-Shmuel amar lo ishtabesh kahanei* — Shmuel says the *kohanim* were not wrong. According to Shmuel, Haggai was asking about a *chamishi*, a fifth-level *tumah*. Since there is no *chamishi letumah* even in *kodshim*, the *kohanim* were correct to say *tahor*.

But where does Shmuel find the fifth level? The Gemara notes that the verse counts only four food items after the *sheretz*: bread, stew, wine, oil — which fits Rav's four-level chain perfectly. *Bishlama le-Rav, hainu deksiv arba'ah* — for Rav, four items, four levels, all works out. *Ella le-Shmuel, chamisha menaslan?* — from where does Shmuel derive the fifth level?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב אַרְבְּעָה: לֶחֶם וְנָזִיד וְיַיִן וָשֶׁמֶן. אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, חַמְשָׁה מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this dispute: **Granted,** according **to** the opinion of **Rav, that is** the reason that it is **four** items **that are written** in the dilemma raised in the verse: **Bread, stew, wine, and oil,** as the dilemma pertains to fourth-degree ritual impurity. **However,** according **to Shmuel, from where does he** learn that the dilemma involves **five** items?

The Gemara's answer turns on the word *bichnafo* — "in his corner." Shmuel reads this to mean that it was not the *sheretz* itself that directly touched the bread, but rather something that had itself touched the *sheretz* — an unmentioned intermediary, which is implied by the prefix *be-* (indicating contact *through* the corner rather than *by* the corner itself). This introduces an additional step at the beginning of the chain, yielding five levels total: *sheretz* → garment-corner → bread (*rishon*) → stew (*sheni*) → wine (*shelishi*) → oil (*revii*) — wait, even with the intermediary, the chain as presented in the verse still terminates at a *revii* for oil. The Gemara's point is that Haggai's question, by Shmuel's reckoning, was really aimed at a *chamishi* — whether anything touched after all these intermediaries would be *tamei* — and the *kohanim* correctly said no, there is no *chamishi*. This reading of *bichnafo* is conceded to be a stretch, but it is the only way to make Shmuel's position cohere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי כְּתִיב ״וְנָגַע כְּנָפוֹ״? ״וְנָגַע בִּכְנָפוֹ״ כְּתִיב — בְּמַה שֶּׁנָּגַע בִּכְנָפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Shmuel's opinion. **Is it written** in the verse: **And its corner touched** the bread, indicating that the primary source of impurity that was in the corner of the garment touched the bread? **It is** actually **written** in the verse: **"And it touched the corner of his garment,"** meaning the bread came into contact **with that** which **touched** the primary source of ritual impurity that was in **the corner of his garment.** The bread came into contact with the corner of the garment, not with the primary source of impurity itself. Accordingly, the garment assumes first-degree ritual impurity status, which confers upon the bread second-degree ritual impurity status, which confers upon the stew third-degree ritual impurity status, which confers upon the wine fourth-degree ritual impurity status. Since the wine cannot confer fifth-degree ritual impurity status upon the oil, the oil remains pure.

### Why Kohanim Erred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיֹּאמֶר חַגַּי אִם יִגַּע טְמֵא נֶפֶשׁ בְּכׇל אֵלֶּה הֲיִטְמָא וַיַּעֲנוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים וַיֹּאמְרוּ יִטְמָא״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, מִדְּהָכָא לָא אִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ — הָתָם נָמֵי לָא אִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ. אֶלָּא לְרַב, מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּאִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּלָא אִישְׁתַּבֻּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof from the subsequent verse. **Come** and **hear: "And Haggai said: If one who is impure with** impurity imparted by **a corpse touches any of these, shall he be impure? And the priests answered and said: He shall be impure"** (Haggai 2:13). **Granted,** according **to Shmuel, from** the fact that **here,** with regard to a dead creeping animal, the priests **did not err,** as Shmuel maintains that the dilemma was with regard to fifth-degree ritual impurity, **there too they did not err. However,** according **to Rav, what is different here,** concerning the impurity of a creeping animal, such **that** the priests **erred, and what is different there,** with regard to impurity imparted by a corpse, such **that they did not err?**

The Gemara then raises a difficulty for Rav. In the second part of Haggai's exchange, where a *tamei nefesh* (one who has contacted a corpse) touches the same sequence of foods, the *kohanim* answered correctly — *yitma*. But according to Rav, the *kohanim* were wrong in the first case. If they are ignorant enough to err once, why didn't they err in the second case as well?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: בְּקִיאִין הֵן בְּטוּמְאַת מֵת, וְאֵין בְּקִיאִין הֵן בְּטוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** The priests of Haggai's era **were experts with regard to** the severe **ritual impurity** imparted by **a corpse,** as they knew that the impurity of a dead body confers upon a consecrated item fourth-degree ritual impurity status. However, **they were not experts with regard to the** lesser **impurity of a creeping animal.**

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, answers: the *kohanim* were expert in the laws of *tum'at met* (corpse-impurity) but not in the laws of *tum'at sheretz* (impurity from creeping things). Hence they got the *tamei nefesh* question right and the *sheretz* question wrong.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: הָתָם רְבִיעִי, הָכָא שְׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that the distinction between the cases is different. **There,** the first dilemma addressed **fourth-**degree ritual impurity, whereas **here,** the dilemma addressed **third-**degree ritual impurity. Haggai's second dilemma does not begin with contact with an item that came into contact with a corpse; rather, it begins with contact with the corpse itself. Since a corpse is the ultimate primary source of impurity, the fourth item is impure with third-degree ritual impurity. The priests knew that *halakha*.

Ravina offers a different resolution: the two questions are not actually parallel cases of the same level of *tumah*. In the second question, when the *tamei nefesh* (an *av ha-tumah*) directly touches the bread, the chain runs: *tamei nefesh* → bread (*rishon*) → stew (*sheni*) → wine (*shelishi*) → oil (*revii*). The *kohanim* knew the rule of *revii letumah* and answered correctly. In the first case, according to Rav, there was also a *revii* — but the *kohanim* had never properly internalized that a *sheretz* chain can produce a *revii letumah*. So the asymmetry is between their mastery of *revii* arising from *tum'at met* versus *revii* arising from *tum'at sheretz*, not between a *revii* and a *chamishi*.

### People as Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיַּעַן חַגַּי וַיֹּאמֶר כֵּן הָעָם הַזֶּה וְכֵן הַגּוֹי הַזֶּה לְפָנַי נְאֻם ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״טָמֵא״. אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, אַמַּאי טָמֵא?
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this matter from the next verse: **"And Haggai answered and said: So is this people, and so is this nation before me, said God;** and so is all the work of their hands; and that which they offer there is impure" (Haggai 2:14). **Granted,** according **to** the opinion of **Rav** that the priests erred, **that is** the reason **that it is written:** "That which they offer there is **impure,"** as the priests' lack of familiarity with the *halakhot* of impurity increase the likelihood that all of their Temple service is ritually impure. **However,** according **to Shmuel, why** does the verse say that their offerings are **ritually impure?**

Turning to Haggai's conclusion — *ken ha-am ha-zeh... tamei hu* — the Gemara considers how each position makes sense of this statement. For Rav, it is straightforward: Haggai was asking a case where the correct answer was *tamei*, the *kohanim* said *tahor*, and then Haggai declares the people are like this — *tamei*. The flow of the passage coheres naturally.

For Shmuel, however, the first question yielded a *tahor* answer — and the *kohanim* were correct. So when Haggai says "so too the nation is *tamei*," what does that mean? The Gemara suggests that Haggai is posing a rhetorical question — *atmuhei ka matma* — he is asking in astonishment: are the people really *tamei*? The implication would be a compliment: the people are as pure as the *tahor* answer you just gave. But the Gemara immediately rejects this, noting that the *nevi'im acharonim* are not in the business of flattering the people, and the verse continues: *v'chen kol ma'aseh yedeihem* — "so too all that they do" — clearly implying censure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיתְמוֹהֵי קָא מַתְמַהּ. וְהָא ״וְכֵן כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה יְדֵיהֶם״ כְּתִיב! אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אָשֵׁי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁקִּלְקְלוּ אֶת מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם — מַעֲלֶה עֲלֵיהֶם הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיבוּ בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: According to Shmuel, this is not a statement. Rather, the verse **is** a rhetorical question expressing **bewilderment:** Is all the Temple service of the priests really impure? Apparently, they are familiar with the *halakhot* of impurity. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it written** in that verse: **"And so is all the work of their hands,"** which is a statement, not a question? **Mar Zutra, and some say** it was **Rav Ashi, said: Since they corrupted their deeds** by sinning in general, **the verse ascribes to them** wrongdoing **as if they sacrificed offerings in** a state of **impurity.**

The Gemara therefore concludes, in the name of Zutra (and some say Rav Ashi): *mitoch she-kilkelu et ma'aseihem, ma'aleh aleihem ha-katuv k'ilu hikrivu b'tumah* — because the people's deeds were corrupt, Scripture reckons it as though they had sacrificed in a state of *tumah*. In other words, Haggai is not drawing a strict halakhic equivalence but rather a moral-metaphorical one: the people are behaviorally so defiled that it is *as if* they are *tamei*. Rashi adds a striking nuance: *l'achar zman she-kilkelu ma'aseihem b'Bayit Sheni b'sha'ar aveirot* — Rashi places this rebuke prophetically in the future, referring to future corruptions in the era of the Second Temple. Just as Moshe rebuked the people for what he knew they would do after his death, Haggai's words carry a forward-looking dimension — a remarkable interpretive move.

## Mitbachaya vs. Midbachaya

### Rav v. Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. רַב תָּנֵי ״מַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא״, וְלֵוִי תָּנָא ״מַשְׁקֵי בֵּי מַדְבְּחַיָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the two versions of Yosei ben Yo'ezer's testimony that the liquids in the Temple are ritually pure, the Gemara addresses **the** matter **itself. Rav teaches** that Yosei ben Yo'ezer spoke of **the liquids of the slaughterhouse,** blood and water, **and Levi taught** that this *halakha* applies to **the liquids of the altar,** which include the wine of libations and oil of meal-offerings in addition to blood and water.

The Gemara now turns *gufa* — to the substance of the dispute itself — regarding which liquids Yosi ben Yoezer's ruling exempts from *tumah*. *Rav tanei mashkei beit mitbachaya* — Rav's version of the text has Yosi ben Yoezer ruling about *beit mitbachaya*, the slaughterhouse. *V'Levi tanei mashkei be-mitbachaya* — Levi's version reads *be-midbachaya*, the *mizbeach*, which encompasses not only blood and water but also wine and oil.

### Levi's Braisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלֵוִי, הָנִיחָא אִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: ״דְּכַן״ מִלְּטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֲחֵרִים אֲבָל טוּמְאַת עַצְמָן יֵשׁ לָהֶן — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דְּנָגְעִי כּוּלְּהוּ בְּרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **According to** the opinion of **Levi, this** works out **well if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, who said** that Yosei ben Yo'ezer testified that the liquids are **ritually pure** in the sense that they do not **transmit impurity to other** items **but they themselves** can **become impure.** In that case, **you** can **find** a scenario for Haggai's second question; **all** the substances mentioned **made contact** with an object with first-degree ritual impurity status. Levi can explain that the prophet's dilemma is not in a case where the different items came into contact with each other, as he is of the opinion that liquids do not transmit impurity. Rather, the stew, wine, and oil each came in contact with an object with first-degree impurity, in accordance with Shmuel's opinion that consecrated liquids themselves can become impure. Accordingly, the priests answered that these objects are impure.

The Gemara examines Levi's position. If Levi reads Yosi ben Yoezer as exempting *mizbeach* liquids broadly — including wine and oil — this works out well if Levi agrees with Shmuel's interpretation of Yosi ben Yoezer, namely that the liquids themselves can become *tamei* but cannot be *metamei* other things (*dachun mi-letamei tumas acheirim, aval tumas atzmam yesh lahem*). Under that reading, all four *mizbeach* liquids — blood, wine, oil, and water — could have contracted *tumah* when they touched a *rishon letumah*, even if they cannot then pass *tumah* further.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי: הַדָּם וְהַיַּיִן וְהַשֶּׁמֶן וְהַמַּיִם, מַשְׁקֵי בֵּי מַדְבְּחַיָּא שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ בִּפְנִים וְהוֹצִיאָן לַחוּץ — טְהוֹרִין. נִטְמְאוּ בַּחוּץ וְהִכְנִיסָן בִּפְנִים — טְמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Levi:** With regard to **the blood, the wine, the oil, and the water,** in the case of all **liquids of the altar that became ritually impure inside** the Temple **and** one **took them outside, they are pure** in the sense that they do not transmit impurity to other objects. This is because when they were within the Temple confines that more stringent form of impurity did not take effect. However, **if they became impure outside** the Temple **and** one **took them inside** the Temple, **they are impure** even in terms of transmitting impurity to other items, as they too retain their prior level of impurity.

A *braisa* is cited in support of Levi, enumerating these four liquids — *ha-dam, ha-yayin, ha-shemen, ve-ha-mayim* — as *mashkei beit midbachaya*. The *braisa* states: if these liquids became *tamei* inside the *mikdash* and were then taken outside, they are *tehorin* — meaning, as Rashi clarifies, *tehorin mi-letamei acheirim*: they themselves remain *tamei*, but they cannot render other things *tamei*. However, if they became *tamei* outside the *mikdash* and were then brought inside, they are *tmei'im* — they do have the power to transmit *tumah*.

### Rav's Braisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: הַדָּם וְהַמַּיִם מַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ בֵּין בְּכֵלִים בֵּין בְּקַרְקַע — טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** And it **was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav: The blood and the water, the liquids of the slaughterhouse, that became ritually impure, whether** they were **in vessels or on the ground, are pure.**

A corresponding *braisa* supports Rav's narrower reading. It specifies only *ha-dam ve-ha-mayim* — blood and water — as *mashkei beit mitbachaya* (slaughterhouse liquids). These, whether they became *tamei* in vessels or while still in the ground, are *tehorim* — they cannot be *metamei* other things. Rabbi Shimon dissents within this *braisa*, ruling that if these liquids are in vessels (*keilim*), they are *tamei*, but if they are still in the ground, they are *tahor*. The *braisa*'s identification of only two liquids — blood and water — rather than four clearly supports Rav's reading of Yosi ben Yoezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (רַבָּן) שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּכֵלִים טְמֵאִין, בְּקַרְקַע טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon says:** If the liquids were **in vessels,** they are **ritually impure;** however, if they were **in the ground,** they are **ritually pure,** as explained below.

The Gemara also notes a tension: if Levi holds like Rav (rather than Shmuel) — that is, if even *mizbeach* liquids are exempt from *tumah* entirely — then the Haggai passage involving wine and oil creates no problem of a *revii* or *chamishi* at all, and Levi would be forced to say the *kohanim* were wrong for different reasons. Working through the permutations, the Gemara concludes that *al karchach ke-Shmuel sevira lei* — Levi must hold like Shmuel, that *mizbeach* liquids can themselves become *tamei* but cannot be *metamei* other things. And by parallel logic, Shmuel's view of the *kohanim*'s error must align with Rav's version of the text — *mashkei beit mitbachaya* — since if Shmuel accepted Levi's version (wine and oil are categorically exempt even from contracting *tumah*), there would be no need to invoke the *revii/chamishi* analysis at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּרַב, דְּאָמַר: ״דְּכַן״ מַמָּשׁ — הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לָהּ? עַל כׇּרְחָךְ כִּשְׁמוּאֵל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if Levi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav** that the liquids are **actually ritually pure, under what circumstances can** a scenario in which wine and oil, liquids offered on the altar, can become ritually impure **be found?** Rather, **perforce** you must say that **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel** with regard to the meaning of the term pure in this context.

### Halacha L'Moshe MiSinai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — מַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Even according to the one who says** that in general **the ritual impurity of liquids** is **by Torah law,** the purity of **the liquids of the** Temple **slaughterhouse is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, which the Sages **learned through tradition.**

Rav Pappa introduces a new framework for understanding the exemption of slaughterhouse liquids. Even if one holds, as a general proposition, that *tumat mashkin* applies *mi-d'oraita*, Rav Pappa says: *mashkei beit mitbachaya hilchata gemiri la* — the exemption of slaughterhouse liquids from *tumah* is a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, a transmitted tradition from Sinai, not a deduction from logic or an extension of general principle. The exemption thus floats free of the broader debate about whether *tumat mashkin* is biblical or rabbinic.

### Challenge to Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אֵין טוּמְאָה לְמַשְׁקִין כׇּל עִיקָּר, תֵּדַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי הֵעִיד (יוֹסֵף) בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה עַל מַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא דְּכַן. וְאִי הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ — מִי גָּמְרִינַן מִינַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa: **But** with regard to **that which Rabbi Eliezer said: There is no impurity for liquids at all** by Torah law, **know** that this is so, **as Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida testified about liquids** in **the slaughterhouse** in the Temple that they were **ritually pure.** This statement indicates that liquids become impure only by rabbinic decree, a decree that is not in effect in the Temple. Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, continued: **And if they learned** the ritual purity of liquids of the slaughterhouse as **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **through tradition, do we derive** other *halakhot* **from it?** There is a principle that one cannot derive halakhic principles from *halakhot* transmitted to Moses from Sinai. How, then, could Rabbi Eliezer cite this *halakha* as a proof for his opinion?

Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan immediately challenges this. If the exemption of blood and water is only a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* applying specifically to slaughterhouse liquids, how could Rabbi Elazar in our Mishnah derive from it the sweeping rule *ein tumah le-mashkin kol ikkar* — that no liquid whatsoever can contract *tumah*? Rabbi Elazar's proof was: look, Yosi ben Yoezer testified that slaughterhouse liquids are *tahor*, which shows that liquids in general have no *tumah*. But if the slaughterhouse exemption is merely a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, it is self-contained and cannot be extrapolated to other liquids. *Halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* applies only to its specific domain — one cannot reason *kal va-chomer* or *binyan av* from it to generate new rulings. This objection is substantial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: לְכֵלִים טְהוֹרִין, לָאוֹכָלִין טְמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi,** also in rejection of Rav Pappa's statement: **Isn't it Rabbi Shimon, who said** that in general **the ritual impurity of liquids** is **by Torah law? As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** With regard **to vessels** that came into contact with impure liquid, the vessels **are pure,** as by Torah law liquids do not transmit impurity to vessels. However, with regard **to foods** that came into contact with impure liquid, the foods are **impure,** as by Torah law liquids transmit impurity to foods.

Ravina raises a second challenge from the *braisa* cited above, in which Rabbi Shimon distinguishes between liquids in vessels (*tamei*) and liquids in the ground (*tahor*). If the exemption were a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* with no qualifications, why would there be any difference between the two? A *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* either exempts or it does not — the vessel-versus-ground distinction suggests we are not dealing with a blanket traditional exemption but rather with some principled analysis that can accommodate internal distinctions. The Gemara concludes simply: *kashya* — this stands as an unresolved difficulty against Rav Pappa.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא קָאָמַר (רַבָּן) שִׁמְעוֹן: בְּכֵלִים טְמֵאִין, בְּקַרְקַע טְהוֹרִין. וְאִי הִלְכְתָא הִיא, מָה לִי בְּכֵלִים מָה לִי בְּקַרְקַע? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina continues: **And** yet **here,** with regard to the liquids in the Temple, **Rabban Shimon said:** Liquids **in vessels are ritually impure,** and liquids **in the ground are pure. And if** you say that the purity of the liquids of the Temple slaughterhouse **is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **what** difference **is** there **to me** if the liquids are **in vessels** and **what** difference **is** there **to me** if the liquids are **in the ground?** If there is an accepted halakhic tradition that ritual impurity does not apply to these liquids, there should be no difference whether the liquid is in vessels or in the ground. The Gemara comments: Indeed, it is **difficult** according to Rav Pappa's opinion.

### Reviyit Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ בְּקַרְקַע טְהוֹרִין — לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מַיִם, אֲבָל דָּם — לֹא. וּמַיִם נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּהָוֵי רְבִיעִית, דְּחָזֵי לְהַטְבִּיל בֵּיהּ מְחָטִין וְצִינּוֹרוֹת. אֲבָל לָא הָוֵי רְבִיעִית — טְמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: That** which **you said,** that the liquids in the Temple **are ritually pure** when **in the ground,** the Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **water, but** with regard to **blood, no,** it does not apply. According to Rabbi Shimon, blood can become impure even in the ground. **And even** with regard to **water, we said** that it is ritually pure **only when it is a quarter-***log*, **which is a suitable** measure **in which to immerse needles and hooks.** By Torah law, a quarter-*log* of water collected in one place can serve as a ritual bath in which one can immerse objects that can be completely immersed in that amount of water. Because it has the status of a ritual bath, it too does not become ritually impure. Although the Sages decreed that one should not immerse vessels in a quarter-log of water, the water is pure by Torah law. Therefore, the Sages did not extend their decree to this measure of water inside the Temple. **However,** if the water **is less than a quarter-***log*, it is **ritually impure** even in the ground, as that water cannot be used as a ritual bath.

Rav Pappa continues with a clarification of the *braisa*'s ruling about water in the ground. When the *braisa* says that water in the ground is *tahor*, this is limited to a specific case. First, it applies only to water, not blood — blood originates in the animal, not the ground, so the "in the ground" consideration is irrelevant to blood. Second, even for water, the ground-based *taharah* applies only when there is at least a *reviyit* of water collected there. The reason: a *reviyit* of water is sufficient — apparently as a matter of law — to immerse needles and small metal implements (*tzinorot*) and render them *tahor*. Water of that volume in the ground has the character of a valid *mikveh*-like body and is therefore not susceptible to *tumah*. But if the collected water is less than a *reviyit*, it loses that status and is *tamei*. The Gemara stops here without resolving all the questions raised against Rav Pappa — the matrix of positions is laid out, but a definitive ruling is not issued.

## Rabbi Yehudah's Liquids

### Cushion Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לַכֹּל טָמֵא. לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת כֵּלִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא?
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* cited above, with regard to uncertainty about contact with impure liquids, that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** They are **impure in all** cases. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that Rabbi Yehuda maintains** that **the impurity** of **liquids** in terms of their capability **to transmit impurity to vessels** is **by Torah law?**

Returning now to the larger *braisa* with which the Gemara had been occupied — the four-way dispute about *safek mashkeh tamei* — the Gemara focuses on Rabbi Yehudah's position. Rabbi Yehudah had said *lakol tamei*: in all cases of doubt about whether a *tamei* liquid touched something, we rule *tamei*. This seems to indicate that Rabbi Yehudah holds *tumat mashkin le-tamei keilim de'oraita* — that *tamei* liquids rendering vessels *tamei* is a biblical prohibition. That would explain why a *safek* is treated strictly, since the rule for a *safek d'oraita* is to be stringent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: כׇּל הַכֵּלִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן אֲחוֹרַיִם וָתוֹךְ, כְּגוֹן הַכָּרִים וְהַכְּסָתוֹת וְהַשַּׂקִּין וְהַמַּרְצוּפִין, נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ — נִטְמָא גַּבּוֹ, נִטְמָא גַּבּוֹ — לֹא נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to **all vessels that have an exterior** that can be used **and an interior** that serves as a receptacle, **such as cushions, blankets, sacks, and leather** grain **sacks,** if the **interior of** one of these vessels **became ritually impure, its exterior is impure** as well. However, if **its exterior became impure, its interior is not impure,** because the primary use of these vessels is as a receptacle.

The Gemara challenges this from a Mishnah. The Mishnah rules: a vessel that has an inside and an outside — cushions (*karim*), pillows (*kisasot*), sacks (*sakin*), leather bags (*martzufin*) — follows this rule: if the inside contracted *tumah*, the outside also becomes *tamei*; but if only the outside contracted *tumah*, the inside remains *tahor*. Rabbi Yehudah qualifies: *bameh devarim amurim she-nitme'u machmas mashkin* — this asymmetric rule (outside *tamei* does not render inside *tamei*) applies only when the *tumah* arrived through liquids. *Aval nitme'u machmas sheretz* — but if the cushion came into direct contact with a *sheretz*, then regardless of which side was touched, the entire object — inside and outside alike — becomes *tamei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ מֵחֲמַת מַשְׁקִין, אֲבָל נִטְמְאוּ מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ, נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ — נִטְמָא גַּבּוֹ, נִטְמָא גַּבּוֹ — נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is in a case **where** the vessels **became impure due to** contact with impure **liquids. However,** if **they became impure due to** contact with **a creeping animal,** then if **the interior became impure the exterior is impure,** and likewise, if **the exterior became impure the interior is** also **impure.**

### Logical Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךְ טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין לְטַמֵּא כֵּלִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מָה לִי נִטְמָא מֵחֲמַת מַשְׁקִין, מָה לִי נִטְמָא מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty posed by Rabbi Yehuda's statement in this mishna: **And if it enters your mind** that **the ritual impurity of liquids** with regard to their capacity **to transmit impurity to vessels** is **by Torah law, what** difference **is** there **to me** if the vessel **is rendered impure due to liquids,** and **what** difference **is** there **to me** if the vessel **is rendered impure due to a creeping animal?** Rather, Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the impurity of liquids is by rabbinic law, and the Sages distinguished between the impurity of the exterior and interior of a vessel to distinguish between impurity by Torah law and impurity by rabbinic law and prevent the burning of *teruma* that is impure with impurity by rabbinic law.

This distinction creates an internal tension in Rabbi Yehudah's position. If *tumat mashkin* is *de'oraita* — as his ruling of *lakol tamei* seems to imply — then why should it matter whether the *tumah* reached the vessel through a liquid or directly through a *sheretz*? If anything, liquid *tumah* should generate the same or stricter results, not more lenient ones. The leniency Rabbi Yehudah grants to liquid-transmitted *tumah* of the vessel (outside does not affect inside) appears to contradict a *de'oraita* premise.

### Yehudah Changed Mind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָזַר בּוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: The mishna is referring to blood that is impure **by rabbinic law, and** here too, it **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida,** who says that consecrated blood does not become impure at all.

Rav Yehudah, citing Shmuel, resolves this by asserting: *chazar bo Rabbi Yehudah* — Rabbi Yehudah retracted his position. The statement that *lakol tamei* — that *safek mashkeh tamei* renders everything *tamei* — was an earlier view. After encountering the difficulty posed by the cushion cases, Rabbi Yehudah reconsidered and backed away from the claim that *tumat mashkin* of vessels is *de'oraita*. Under his revised position, the strict ruling of *lakol tamei* no longer stands as stated.

### Hands vs. Sheretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא הָדַר, הָא — בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת יָדַיִם. הָא — בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Actually,** it is possible that Rabbi Yehuda **did not retract** his previous statement, as **this** case, where he distinguishes between impurity on the interior and the exterior of vessels, was stated with regard **to liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with impure **hands** that did not undergo ritual washing. Unwashed hands are impure by rabbinic decree, and therefore the impurity of such liquid is likewise rabbinic. Conversely, **that** case, where the impurity of liquids is by Torah law, was stated with regard **to liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a creeping animal.** As that impurity is by Torah law, no distinction is made between the interior and exterior of vessels.

Ravina disagrees. He says: *le'olam lo hadar* — Rabbi Yehudah never retracted at all. There is no contradiction in his two rulings because they address categorically different types of liquid *tumah*. *Ha b'mashkin ha-ba'im machmas yadayim, ha b'mashkin ha-ba'im machmas sheretz* — one case involves liquids that became *tamei* by virtue of contact with *yadayim* (hands), while the other involves liquids contaminated by direct contact with a *sheretz*.

The status of ordinary hands is a *rabbinic* presumption of *tumah* — unless one has been careful about them, hands are assumed *tamei*. Liquid rendered *tamei* through such hands is thus *tamei mi-d'rabbanan*. Rabbi Yehudah's leniency for liquid-transmitted *tumah* of the cushion applies in this case: the *tumah* being rabbinic, the lenient asymmetric rule (outside does not render inside *tamei*) is appropriate.

When the liquid, however, contracted *tumah* by direct contact with a *sheretz*, that *tumah* is *de'oraita*. In such a case, Rabbi Yehudah would agree that the resulting *tumah* of the vessel is full and bilateral — touching any part of the cushion renders the whole thing *tamei* — matching the *sheretz* rule entirely. On Ravina's reading, Rabbi Yehudah's two statements are not contradictions but applications to different categories, requiring no retraction.

### Mishnah's Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי ״בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ מֵחֲמַת מַשְׁקִין״, לִיפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת יָדַיִם. אֲבָל בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ, נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ — נִטְמָא גַּבּוֹ, נִטְמָא גַּבּוֹ — נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא: חָזַר בּוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, instead of teaching: In what** case **is this statement said?** In a case where the vessels **were rendered impure due to** contact with **liquids,** as opposed to the other case in which the vessels became impure due to contact with creeping animals, **let him distinguish and teach** the distinction **within** the case **itself: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is in a case where these vessels **became impure by** contact with **liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with impure **hands. However,** in a case where these vessels **became impure by** contact with **liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a creeping animal,** if **the interior became impure, the exterior is impure,** and if **the exterior became impure, the interior is** likewise **impure. Rather,** the Gemara rejects Ravina's explanation and states that **it is clear as we initially answered,** that **Rabbi Yehuda retracted** his previous ruling.

The Gemara responds to Ravina by pointing to the language of the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah draws its distinction between two cases: *tumah* arriving through liquids versus *tumah* arriving through a *sheretz*. If Ravina is correct that the real operative distinction is between liquids contaminated by hands (rabbinic) and liquids contaminated by a *sheretz* (biblical), the Mishnah should have stated *that* distinction explicitly. It should have said: when does the asymmetric leniency apply? When the *tumah* came through liquids that were contaminated by hands. But when the *tumah* came through liquids contaminated by a *sheretz* — then the object is *tamei* in full. The fact that the Mishnah did not frame the distinction this way, instead presenting it simply as liquid versus *sheretz*, indicates that no such sub-category distinction was intended. The implication is that Rabbi Yehudah's original position was stated without qualification — and therefore the Gemara concludes that Rav Yehudah's report in the name of Shmuel is correct: *chazar bo Rabbi Yehudah* — Rabbi Yehudah did indeed retract.
