# Avodah Zarah 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Forbidden Foods Intro

### Section Overview

With the *gemara* on 29b, we enter a new and substantially different section of *Maseches Avodah Zarah*. Up to this point, the tractate has focused primarily on interactions with non-Jews — selling goods, engaging in commerce, attending their markets — where the concern was largely about facilitating *avodah zarah*. The new section shifts that orientation decisively. Here the concern is with the object itself, and the object-focus is essentially one of *kashrus*. Whatever the background rationale, the question is whether this particular item, in this particular state, is permitted.

### Benefit Restrictions

*Kashrus* concerns come in two varieties. Some foods are forbidden to eat but remain *mutar b'hana'ah* — permitted for use or financial benefit — which signals a pure dietary prohibition. The *mishna* that is coming up deals primarily with that category. The present *mishna*, however, deals with items that are forbidden even for *hana'ah*, and that near-total prohibition is itself a signal: when something is forbidden even for benefit, the concern almost certainly traces back to *avodah zarah*. The very severity of the prohibition marks its origin.

### Wine Introduction

The first and most significant item in this *mishna* is wine. Wine is a natural transition case: it is forbidden even for *hana'ah*, linking it to *avodah zarah* concerns, yet the discussion will quickly open into a broader world of *kashrus* — fermentation, cooking, mixtures, grape juice, and ultimately the hazard of exposure to snakes. It is worth noting that the cheese prohibition, which also appears in this context, is connected to wine: the Rabbis forbade non-Jewish cheese precisely because of its association with wine and the socializing that follows from it.

## Wine Prohibition Sources

### Yayin Nisech Source

The *gemara* opens with the question: *yayin mi-nalan* — from where do we derive that the wine of non-Jews is forbidden? Rabba bar Avuha cites the verse (Devarim 32:38): *asher chelev zivcheimo yocheilu yishtu yein nesicham* — "who ate the fat of their sacrifices and drank the wine of their libations." The *gemara* draws an analogy: just as the *zevach*, the idolatrous sacrifice, is *assur b'hana'ah*, so too is the wine used in libations, *yayin nesech*, forbidden for benefit.

The *gemara* then asks: how do we know that the sacrifice to an idol is *assur b'hana'ah* in the first place? The verse states (Tehillim 106:28): *vayitzmadu l'Baal Peor vayochlu zivchei meisim* — they ate the sacrifices of the dead. The halachic inference is that just as a *meis*, a corpse, is *assur b'hana'ah*, so too is the idolatrous sacrifice. One might pause here and note that the prohibition on deriving benefit from a corpse underlies the entire chain — which raises the interesting question of why autopsies and the use of bodies for medical education are permitted. One possible answer is that *hana'ah* is defined narrowly as bodily or financial benefit, and the intellectual gain derived from medical study may fall outside that definition.

Continuing the chain: the *gemara* asks from where we know that a corpse is *assur b'hana'ah*. The answer comes through a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy — linking the word *sham* ("there") in the verse about Miriam's death to the word *sham* in the passage about the *eglah arufah*, the calf whose neck is broken (Devarim 21:4). Just as the *eglah arufah* is *assur b'hana'ah*, so too is a corpse. And the *eglah arufah* itself is assimilated to sacrifices (*kappara* is written regarding it, as regarding *kodashim*), completing the chain. The derivation thus runs: *kodashim* → *eglah arufah* → corpse → idolatrous sacrifice → *yayin nesech*.

### Tosafot's Question

This derivation, however, raises a sharp question that Tosafot addresses. The *mishna* here is discussing *stam yeinam* — ordinary wine of non-Jews, wine that was not set aside for any idolatrous purpose and that we have no particular reason to treat as *yayin nesech*. So why does the *gemara* cite a verse about *yayin nesicham*, wine of libations, as the source for the prohibition? The verse speaks of wine actually used in idolatrous ritual, whereas the *mishna* is about everyday wine. *Teima*, Tosafot asks, *mai ka ba'i minalan* — what exactly is the *gemara* looking for?

### Tosafot Resolution

Tosafot answers that the primary *gezeira* prohibiting *stam yeinam* is indeed not about *yayin nesech* at all. The original rabbinic enactment was *mishum bnoseihem* — out of concern for intermarriage and excessive socializing. Wine is a social lubricant; Jews and non-Jews drinking together leads to familiarity, which leads to intermarriage. That is the root of the prohibition. However, a prohibition grounded solely in social concerns would naturally be limited: one should not drink their wine, just as one does not eat their bread or use their oil — items that are forbidden but permitted for *hana'ah*. That alone would not generate a prohibition on benefit.

The Rabbis, therefore, added a second layer. Since *stam yeinam* resembles *yayin nesech* — it is their wine, and their wine is associated with libations — the Rabbis treated it with the full severity of actual *yayin nesech*, rendering it *assur b'hana'ah*. The verse from Devarim is not the original source of the prohibition; rather, it is the basis for the *hana'ah* component specifically. And it is to teach us that additional stringency — that their wine is forbidden even for benefit — that the *gemara* cites the verse.

### Two Categories Matter

One might ask: once the Rabbis have declared *stam yeinam* forbidden even for *hana'ah*, what practical difference does it make that the prohibition also rests on the *bnoseihem* rationale? If it is already *assur b'hana'ah*, the strictest category, why does the intermarriage concern add anything?

The answer is that the two rationales operate independently, and there are scenarios where only one of them applies. Consider a *ger toshav* — a non-Jew who has formally accepted the Noahide laws and is, by definition, not an *oved avodah zarah*. His wine presents no concern of idolatrous libation whatsoever. On the purely *avodah zarah* analysis, there is no reason to forbid benefit from his wine. Yet we would still prohibit drinking it, because the *bnoseihem* concern — the concern about social intimacy and its consequences — remains fully in force.

The Rambam applies this logic to Muslim wine. Since Islam is unambiguously not *avodah zarah*, one may engage in commerce with Muslim wine — buying and selling it — because the *hana'ah* prohibition is limited to the *avodah zarah* category. However, drinking it remains forbidden, because the *bnoseihem* concern applies regardless. This has further implications for the longstanding debate among the *rishonim* about Christian wine: given that some *rishonim* question whether contemporary Christianity constitutes *avodah zarah* in the technical halachic sense, there may be room, in certain circumstances, to permit benefit from Christian wine even while maintaining the drinking prohibition. Both categories thus remain halachically operative, and identifying which one is triggered in any given case determines the practical ruling.

## Wine Vinegar Rule

### Vinegar Persistence

The *gemara* next addresses wine vinegar: *chometz shel ovdei kochavim she-hayah mitchilato yayin* — vinegar of non-Jews that originally began as wine. Is this also forbidden? The *gemara* says *peshita* — obviously. One might think that once wine becomes vinegar, the prohibition lapses, since the substance has been transformed. But the answer is that object-status, once acquired, does not dissolve through transformation. If the wine was already forbidden when the non-Jew had it, the fact that it has since turned to vinegar does not remove that status. This is analogous to *chazir*: cooking a piece of pig does not render it kosher. So long as the substance remains recognizable and usable, the prohibition persists.

The implicit distinction is that if a non-Jew received the vinegar from Jews *after* it had already become vinegar — meaning they never possessed it as wine — it presents no problem. It is only when they originally held it as wine that the prohibition attached to the object.

### Single Seal Rule

Rav Ashi clarifies the teaching's real *chiddush*: *chometz shelanu b'yad ovdei kochavim ein tzarich chotam betoch chotam* — if we give our vinegar to a non-Jew to hold or sell on our behalf, a single seal is sufficient; we need not require the double seal.

The reasoning involves two distinct concerns. First, *mishum nisuchi* — perhaps they will use our vinegar as a libation for their idolatry. The *gemara* answers: *lo menaschi* — they do not pour vinegar as a libation. Non-Jews would not use wine vinegar in their idolatrous rituals. This is actually the *gemara*'s substantive point: since there is no concern about libations, a single seal suffices to address the remaining worry. Second, *mishum ichlufi* — perhaps they will swap our high-quality vinegar for inferior vinegar of their own, which might be halachically problematic. Here, the rule is that *keivan de'ika chotam lo tarach umezayef* — once there is even a single seal, the effort required to break, forge, and replace it is sufficient deterrent for a purely financial motivation. A double seal is only required when the motivation to tamper is high — as it might be if a non-Jew were strongly motivated to use something for *avodah zarah*. For an ordinary concern of financial substitution, one seal is enough.

## Cooked Wine Rules

### Cooked Wine Forbidden

Rav Ila teaches: *yayin mevushal shel ovdei kochavim she-hayah mitchilato yayin assur* — cooked wine of non-Jews is forbidden if they had possession of it when it was still wine. The *gemara* again asks *peshita* — isn't this obvious? And again the answer invokes the same principle.

### Status Immutability

The *gemara*'s response makes the governing rule explicit: *mishum d'itvashal pakla issura* — does cooking remove the prohibition? No. Once an object has acquired forbidden status, subsequent transformation — whether becoming vinegar, being cooked, or being mixed with other substances — does not dissolve that status. This principle is structural to the entire discussion and recurs throughout.

### Cooked from Start

Rav Ashi then teaches the obverse case: *yayin mevushal shelanu b'yad ovdei kochavim* — if the wine was cooked first, in our possession, and then given to non-Jews to handle or sell, it does not become forbidden. This is the basis for the contemporary practice of *yayin mevushal*: once wine has been cooked, a non-Jew's handling of it does not render it prohibited. The reason is that cooked wine is not suitable for libations. Non-Jews use wine in their rituals in its natural state; wine that has been cooked is not wine they would employ for *nisuch*.

### Cooked Wine Sealing

Here too, *ein tzarich chotam betoch chotam* — a single seal suffices for cooked wine in non-Jewish hands. As with vinegar, the two concerns are the same: *mishum nisuchi* — no, they do not use cooked wine for libations; *mishum chilufei* — a single seal adequately deters a purely financial motivation to substitute. The *chiddush* of the single-seal rule is essentially that neither concern reaches the level that would demand a double seal. The more important practical point is simply that cooked wine, once it has been cooked in Jewish possession, may be handled by non-Jews without halachic consequence — a ruling of enormous contemporary relevance.

This is also where the distinction between the two categories of prohibition becomes structurally important once more. If the sole rationale for prohibiting non-Jewish wine were *bnoseihem* — social concerns and intermarriage — one might still argue that cooked wine should be forbidden for drinking, since people do socialize over cooked wine. The reason *yayin mevushal* escapes the prohibition entirely, and not merely the *hana'ah* component, is because it falls entirely outside the *yayin nesech* category: it is not wine that would be used in libations. Once removed from the *avodah zarah* framework, the *bnoseihem* concern does not independently attach to it.

### Wine Mixtures

The *Tanna'im* also address *alontit* — a medicinal mixture added to wine. The rule is parallel: *yayin mevushal ve'alontit shel ovdei kochavim assurim* — cooked wine and *alontit* prepared by non-Jews are forbidden. The prohibition applies when the non-Jews had possession of it while it was still uncooked wine. But *alontit kevriyata muteret* — the original *alontit*, before the wine was added, is permitted. Two interpretations are offered by *rishonim*: Rashi reads *kevriyata* as meaning the non-Jews received it after the wine had already been incorporated and cooked; Tosafot reads it as meaning the dry additives alone, before the wine component was mixed in. Either way, the principle is the same: what the non-Jew receives in an already-altered state cannot be subsequently forbidden by their handling, whereas what they received as wine and then mixed or cooked remains forbidden despite the transformation.

### Alontit Definition

The *gemara* identifies *alontit* through a *baraita* concerning Shabbat. On Shabbat, one may prepare *anumalin* but not *alontit*. The distinction clarifies the nature of each. *Anumalin* — wine, honey, and pepper — is a spiced wine prepared for drinking, and mixing liquids for drinking on Shabbat involves no prohibition. *Alontit*, by contrast — old wine, clear water, and *aparsimon* (likely balsam) — is used for medicinal purposes, specifically in the context of bathing and recuperation. Preparing medicine on Shabbat is prohibited. The practical point for our discussion is that *alontit*, being a medicinal substance rather than a drinking wine, occupies a status comparable to *yayin mevushal*: once mixed, it is no longer the kind of wine that would be used for libations, and so non-Jewish handling of the finished mixture does not render it forbidden.

## Exposure Concerns

### Giluy Introduction

The *gemara* now shifts to an entirely different concern that occupies the remainder of the *daf*: *giluy*, the hazard posed by wine, water, and certain foods that have been left exposed and unattended. The rabbinic concern is that a snake may have drunk from the liquid or bitten into the food and left behind poison. Whether this reflects a literal understanding of snake behavior or is better understood as a broader concern about contamination — bacteria or other invisible hazards — the category became fully formalized in halachic terms, with its own rules, categories, and disputes.

It is worth noting upfront that Tosafot essentially rules that *giluy* concerns do not apply in our times. Since the prohibition is ultimately grounded in a health-based concern — the presence of venomous snakes — and that hazard no longer exists in the same form in most Jewish communities, Tosafot held that the *gezeira* has lapsed. Nevertheless, the *gemara*'s detailed treatment of the subject generates real halachic categories.

### Mixed & Cooked

Rava and Rav Yosef both state two parallel rules. First: *yayin mazug ein bo mishum giluy* — mixed wine, diluted with water, is not subject to the *giluy* concern, because snakes prefer undiluted wine and will not drink the watered-down variety. Second: *yayin mevushal ein bo mishum nisuch* — cooked wine is not subject to the *yayin nesech* prohibition, confirming what has already been established. The *gemara* then asks about the cross-categories: is *yayin mevushal* a problem for *giluy*? Is *yayin mazug* a problem for *yayin nesech*?

### Cooked No Giluy

Rav Yaakov bar Idi states directly: *yayin mevushal she-ein bo mishum giluy* — cooked wine is not a problem for exposure. Just as it is not subject to *yayin nesech*, it is also not subject to the snake-based concern. Snakes, it seems, are no more interested in cooked wine than idolaters are.

### Yannai Illness Story

A narrative illustrates the practical stakes of this question. Rav Yannai bar Yishmael fell ill, and Rav Yishmael ben Zirud and other rabbis came to visit. The question of whether *yayin mevushal* has a *giluy* problem arose. Rabbi Yishmael ben Zirud reported: *hachi amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish mishum gavra raba, u-manu Rabbi Chiya* — Resh Lakish said in the name of a great authority, namely Rabbi Chiya, that *yayin mevushal ein bo mishum giluy*. When asked whether this ruling could be relied upon, Rav Yannai bar Yishmael — himself lying on his sickbed — gestured, *alai ve-al tzavari*, placing the responsibility on his own shoulders. The juxtaposition is striking: a man who is ill and for whom health concerns are immediate is the one affirming that cooked wine poses no danger from exposure.

### Shmuel & Avlat

A story confirms the *yayin nesech* ruling. Shmuel was sitting with Avlat, a non-Jewish scholar who was well-versed in Jewish law and scrupulous about not causing Shmuel halachic problems. When *yayin mevushal* was brought before them, Avlat withdrew his hand, not wanting to touch it and render it forbidden. Shmuel corrected him: *harei amru yayin mevushal ein bo mishum yayin nesech* — cooked wine is not subject to the *yayin nesech* prohibition; you may touch it freely.

### Chiya Maidservant

Rabbi Chiya's maidservant had been watching a quantity of *yayin mevushal* that became exposed. She came to Rabbi Chiya with her concern. He told her: *harei amru yayin mevushal ein bo mishum giluy* — the ruling is that cooked wine does not have a *giluy* problem. This story is notable for two reasons. First, it provides direct testimony of Rabbi Chiya's ruling, confirming what Resh Lakish had cited in his name. Second, Rabbi Chiya himself says *harei amru* — "they have said" — attributing the ruling to an earlier tradition, suggesting the principle predates even Rabbi Chiya.

## Mixed Wine & Snakes

### Well-Mixed Requirement

The servant of Rav Ada bar Ahava had *yayin mazug* — mixed wine — that became exposed. Rav Ada bar Ahava cited the principle that mixed wine is not a *giluy* problem. Rav Papa, however, qualifies this: *lo amran ela de-mazig tuva* — we only said there is no concern when the wine is very thoroughly mixed, with a substantial proportion of water. *Aval mazig ve-lo mazig* — if it is only slightly diluted, *shatei* — snakes will still drink from it.

### Snake in Boat Story

The *gemara* challenges this qualification. If even slightly diluted wine is a problem, that seems to contradict a story about Rabba bar Rav Huna, who was traveling by boat with a cask of wine. He saw a snake cutting through the water toward the boat — apparently intent on drinking the wine. He told his attendant to frustrate the snake's approach. The attendant took a small amount of water and added it to the wine. The snake perceived the dilution and turned away.

The implication of the story would seem to be that even a small addition of water is sufficient to deter snakes, which would contradict Rav Papa's ruling. 

### Snake Risk Assessment

The *gemara* resolves the tension by distinguishing between levels of snake motivation. For undiluted wine, a snake will *masar nafsheih* — risk its life — swimming through water, boarding a vessel, doing whatever is necessary to reach it. For wine that is mixed — even slightly mixed — the snake will not take such risks. It will still drink from exposed, slightly diluted wine if it is readily accessible, but it will not exert itself to get there. So the story about the boat is about motivation, not about whether snakes find diluted wine palatable at all.

### Snake Dilution Story

A second story reinforces that snakes do care about mixed wine even when they need to work for it. Either Rav Yannai or Bar Haddaya was in a place, and those present were drinking *yayin mazug*. Some wine remained in a cask, which was covered with cloth. They then observed a snake taking water and pouring it into the cask — apparently working to raise the wine level until it seeped above the cloth, and drinking from it. The implication seems to be that even mixed wine is worth effort for a snake under the right conditions.

### Rava's Ruling

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by distinguishing between wine the snake itself dilutes versus wine someone else has already diluted. But Rav Ashi — or Rav Mesharshya — dismisses this kind of reasoning outright: *peruka l'sakanta* — you cannot offer a forced *teretz* for a matter of physical danger. When the issue is health and safety, not a theoretical halachic category, a hair-splitting distinction that might not actually hold is not an acceptable answer.

Accordingly, Rava rules definitively: *hilcheta yayin mazug yesh bo mishum giluy ve-yesh bo mishum yayin nesech* — mixed wine is a problem both for *giluy* and for *yayin nesech*. On the *yayin nesech* point: a small amount of dilution does not take wine out of the category of wine for the purposes of idolatrous libation. On the *giluy* point: the evidence from the stories shows that snakes will indeed drink from mixed wine. Conversely, *yayin mevushal ein bo mishum giluy ve-ein bo mishum yayin nesech* — cooked wine is a problem for neither.

## Water & Food Exposure

### Sleeping Protection

The attendant of Rav Chelkiya bar Tuvi had an uncovered flask of water while he dozed nearby. The question was whether his physical proximity — even while asleep — was sufficient to keep snakes away and thus spare him the *giluy* concern. Rav Chelkiya bar Tuvi cited a principle: sleeping nearby does deter snakes during the day, because the snake's fear of a person persists even when he is unconscious. However, this only applies in the daytime; at night, *lo* — sleeping does not offer the same protection. The *gemara* then rejects this distinction entirely: *lo shna b'yemama ve-lo shna b'leilya* — there is no difference between day and night. Sleeping does not protect at all, regardless of the time. To prevent *giluy*, one must be awake.

### Rav's Stringency

Rav would not drink water from the house of a non-Jew. His stated reason: *lo zeiri b'giluy* — they are not careful about keeping their water covered. Since they are not vigilant about exposure, drinking from their household water carries a genuine health risk. This is a practical *chumra* rooted entirely in the *sakana* concern, not in any *yayin nesech* category.

### Widow Exception

Rav, however, would drink from the house of a widow. His reasoning: *shibshta d'gavra nakita* — she still maintains the habits of her husband. While her husband was alive, the household kept the water properly covered; after his death, she continues those practices. There is an implicit assumption here that men are the ones knowledgeable about these laws and who maintained the household discipline, while a woman on her own might be less vigilant. This is, of course, rather obviously at odds with the observed reality that women are typically the primary guardians of *kashrus* in Jewish homes, but that is the *gemara*'s stated reasoning.

### Shmuel's Stringency

Shmuel takes a more stringent position. He would not drink water from the house of a widow, because *leika eimta d'gavra* — there is no longer the fear of the husband to motivate her vigilance. Without his presence, she will not be as careful. *Ve-lo machsya maya* — she will not keep the water covered.

### Version Variants

There is an alternate version of the tradition. In this second formulation: Rav would not drink from the house of a non-Jew but would drink from the house of a widow — as stated above. Shmuel, however, would drink from neither: not from a non-Jew's house for the *giluy* reason, and not from a widow's house either. In Shmuel's view, the concern about the widow's lack of vigilance is real and overrides any presumption of her maintaining her late husband's habits. As for why Rav would drink from a non-Jew's house in this version: *nehi d'giluya lo kapidi, a-mankruta maya kapidi* — even if they are not careful about the *giluy* hazard per se, non-Jews are careful about keeping their water clean and uncontaminated for their own practical purposes, and so they will keep it covered as a matter of general hygiene. Jewish widows, apparently, were not presumed to share the same motivation.

## Non-Giluy Wines

### Three Wine Types

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states a general rule: there are three categories of wine that have no *giluy* concern — not because they have been diluted or cooked, but because of their intrinsic character, which snakes find unappealing. These are: *chad* — acrid or extremely sharp wine; *mar* — bitter wine; and *matok* — sweet wine.

### Wine Definitions

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi identifies each type. Sharp wine (*chad*) is *tila charifa* — an extremely pungent wine that is so acrid it causes flasks to burst. Bitter wine (*mar*) is *yarketa* — from unripe grapes. Sweet wine (*matok*) is *chulya* — Rashi explains as grapes that have been exposed excessively to the sun, producing an overly sweet wine. Rav Chama characterizes these same three categories in terms of actual well-known preparations: sharp wine is wine with pepper; bitter wine is *afasinti* — wormwood wine, a preparation still known today as absinthe; and sweet wine is *mei barg*, perhaps a sparkling or particularly high-quality sweet wine. All three repel snakes, and so exposed wine of these types presents no *giluy* concern — though it would presumably still be subject to *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam* concerns in the ordinary course.

### Sweet Wine Asia

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish adds: *karina ein bo mishum giluy* — a wine known as *karina* is not a *giluy* problem. Rabbi Abahu identifies *karina* as *chamra chalya d'ati me'asya* — sweet wine that comes from Asia (or Asia Minor).

### Local Wine Problem

Rava qualifies this: *u-vimkomo yesh bo mishum giluy* — in the region where this wine is actually produced and the local snakes know it, there *is* a *giluy* problem. *Chamar medina hu* — it is the local wine of that place, and the snakes there are accustomed to it. The wine's sweetness deters snakes elsewhere, but at home, where the snakes know it well, they will drink from it regardless.

### Sour Wine Timeline

Rava further rules on wine that has begun to turn sour — wine that has started fermenting into vinegar but has not yet fully turned. *Ad tlasa yomei yesh bo mishum giluy u-mishum yayin nesech* — for the first three days after it begins to sour, it is still close enough to wine that both concerns apply: snakes will drink from it, and it is still considered wine for purposes of the non-Jewish wine prohibition. *Mi-kan va'eilach ein bo mishum giluy ve-ein bo mishum yayin nesech* — after three days, it has sufficiently crossed into vinegar territory; it is no longer a *giluy* concern and is also no longer treated as wine for *yayin nesech* purposes. This, of course, assumes that the non-Jew did not possess it from the outset.

### Nehardean Dissent

The Nehardeans disagree with Rava's ruling on the *giluy* side of this rule. Even after three days, they hold that there remains a *giluy* problem. Their reasoning: *zimnin dimikrei u-shati* — there are occasions when snakes do in fact drink from sour wine, and when it comes to matters of physical danger, we are stringent even in borderline cases. The *yayin nesech* ruling of Rava may stand — after three days it is no longer considered wine — but the health-based *giluy* concern persists, because safety demands extra caution.

## Unfermented Wine

### Toses Status

Unfermented wine — *yayin toses*, grape juice in the process of initial fermentation — has no *giluy* problem. Snakes, it seems, do not care for it. However, it remains subject to *yayin nesech* concerns: it is still wine, still used in libations, and still subject to the full prohibition if handled by non-Jews.

There is an important distinction to draw here between *yayin toses* and commercially pasteurized grape juice. *Yayin toses* is unfermented wine that *can* ferment — it is wine in its natural early state. Pasteurized grape juice, by contrast, cannot ferment at all, and it is therefore a genuine question whether *kiddush* and *havdalah* can be recited over it, given that it may not halachically qualify as wine. That said, contemporary practice treats pasteurized grape juice as equivalent for purposes of *kiddush* and *havdalah*. For *yayin nesech* purposes, the determining factor is the state of the wine at the time of non-Jewish contact: if the non-Jew had it before pasteurization, it was still wine and the prohibition attaches; if they touch it after pasteurization, it is already *mevushal*.

### Three-Day Window

*Ve-chama tsisato* — how long is wine considered *yayin toses* for purposes of the *giluy* exemption? Three days. Within three days of pressing, it remains in the unfermented state and is not a *giluy* concern. After three days, it has begun to ferment sufficiently to attract snakes, and the ordinary rules apply.

### Cress & Vinegar

*Ha-shachalayim* — cress — has no *giluy* concern intrinsically; snakes do not find it appealing. Nevertheless, *u-venei golah nahagu ba-hen issur* — those in exile treated it as forbidden. This stringency applies *when there is no vinegar added* to the cress. If vinegar is present, *magrei behu* — it repels the snakes — and the concern is moot. The stricter practice in exile therefore refers to cress without vinegar.

### Babylonian Dip

*Kutach ha-bavli* — Babylonian dip, a mixture of bread crumbs, whey, and other ingredients — similarly has no intrinsic *giluy* concern. Yet *u-venei golah nahagu ba-hen issur* — those in exile treated it as forbidden as well, maintaining a general posture of caution about exposed foods.

### Puncture Mark Test

Rav Menashya adds a practical observation: if there are *nikkurei* — puncture marks or bite marks — on an exposed food, *chashinan* — we must be concerned that a snake has actually eaten from it. The physical evidence of a snake's presence creates a concrete, no-longer-theoretical concern.

## Exposure Deterrents

### Dripping Sound

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Shmuel teaches: *mei tip tip ein bo mishum giluy* — water or wine into which there is a constant dripping sound — *tip, tip, tip* — is not a *giluy* concern. The persistent sound deters snakes. Rav Ashi qualifies: *ve-hu de-avid tip lahadei tip tip* — the dripping must be genuinely constant, a steady and uninterrupted sound. An occasional or intermittent drip does not suffice.

### Fig Rule

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi also teaches in the name of Shmuel: *pi te'einim ein ba-hem mishum giluy* — figs from which the stem has been removed do not present a *giluy* concern, even where the skin has been breached. The *gemara* identifies this ruling as consistent with the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who states that a person may eat grapes and figs at night without concern, on the basis of the verse: *shomer peta'im Hashem* — God watches over the simple. The connection to our ruling is that since grapes and figs are generally safe from *giluy*, the specific case of figs with removed stems falls within that general exemption.

## Snake Poison Types

### Poison Stratification

Rav Safra in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua of the South — and some say in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — teaches: there are three types of snake venom, and they behave differently in a liquid or food into which they have been deposited. The poison of a young snake — *shel bachur* — is *shokei'a*, dense and heavy; it sinks to the bottom. The poison of a middle-aged snake — *shel beinoni* — is *mefa'pei'a*; it disperses throughout the middle of the liquid, neither rising nor sinking fully. The poison of an elderly snake — *shel zaken* — is *tzaf*; it is weak and light, and it floats at the top.

### Age Paradox

This generates an apparent tension. The ruling implies that as snakes age, their venom weakens — the poison of an old snake is the least potent, floating rather than sinking. But a *baraita* states that three creatures grow stronger with age: fish, snakes, and wild boars. Snakes grow physically larger and more powerful over time; the old snakes at the zoo are enormous. How can we say their venom weakens?

The *gemara* resolves this: *koach ossufei hu de-ka-mossif* — yes, the animal itself grows stronger, larger, more physically formidable. But *erseih kalish* — its poison grows weaker. The venom that a young snake produces is actually its most potent; the snake's capacity to generate powerful venom diminishes with age, even as the animal itself becomes more imposing.

### Nine & Tenth Story

The practical relevance of knowing that young snake poison sinks is illustrated by a *baraita*: if a barrel of wine has been exposed and nine people have drunk from it without ill effect, one might think this proves no snake touched it. Do not drink as the tenth person. The reason: if a young snake drank from the barrel, its poison sank entirely to the bottom. The first nine people all drank from above the settled poison and were unharmed. The tenth person, drinking from the bottom, or drinking the final portion where the poison has concentrated, could be killed. A real incident is cited: nine people drank and survived; the tenth drank and died. Rabbi Yirmiya identifies the cause: *zehu shokei'a* — this was sinking poison from a young snake.

### Melon Case

The same principle applies to food. A melon that has been exposed: even if nine people ate from it without harm, a tenth person should not eat it, for the same reason — the poison may be concentrated in a lower or inner portion untouched by the earlier consumers. Again, a historical incident is cited: nine ate and survived; the tenth ate and died.

### Poison Distribution

The reason all three types of venom — sinking, middle-dispersed, and floating — must be specified is to eliminate any inference of safety from the behavior of prior consumers. One might reason: "Three people drank from this and survived, so if the poison is neither at the very bottom nor at the very top, I am safe drinking from the middle." This reasoning is invalid, because there is a type of poison that concentrates precisely in the middle. No number of safe prior consumers can establish that the remaining liquid or food is free of danger, because the poison may simply not have been reached yet.

## Handling Restrictions

### Water Disposal

If water has been exposed and may contain snake venom, the *baraita* establishes a series of restrictions on how it may be disposed of or used. One may not spill it into the public domain — *lo yishpechem be-reshut ha-rabim* — because someone walking barefoot through the water could absorb the poison through the skin. One may not use it to settle dust in the house — *velo yarbitz ba-hem et ha-bayit* — for the same reason. One may not mix it into plaster — *velo yagbel ba-hem et ha-tit* — because the poison could seep out over time, analogous to the concern about toxic materials absorbed into building materials.

### Personal Use Ban

One may not give exposed water to animals to drink — *velo yashkem lo li-vahemto velo li-vahemat chavero* — neither to one's own animal nor to a friend's. One may not wash one's face, hands, or feet with it — *velo yirchatz ba-hem panav yadav ve-raglav*.

### Crevice Debate

*Acherim omrim* — others say the restriction on washing is not absolute but depends on whether the body part has crevices. *Makom she-yesh sirta assur* — a place on the body where there are crevices or folds is forbidden, because the poison can enter through those openings. *Ein sirta* — a smooth area — *mutar*, it is permitted, since the Rabbis did not fear absorption through unbroken skin. The *gemara* observes that this is not necessarily a dispute with the first *tanna*: the first *tanna*'s prohibition on face, hands, and feet may itself be because those are areas with crevices. The practical difference between the two positions concerns the back of the hand, the back of the foot, and the smooth part of the cheek — areas without crevices. According to the first *tanna*, these are included in the prohibition; according to *Acherim*, they would be permitted.

### Animal Drinking

The prohibition on giving exposed water to animals — one's own or a friend's — is challenged by a *baraita* that seems to permit giving it to one's own animal. The *gemara* resolves this: the *baraita* that permits it is referring specifically to a cat. Cats, since they are known to eat snakes, are presumed not to be harmed by snake venom. Therefore, the concern about killing the animal does not apply to a cat.

### Cat Exception

But if one's own cat can drink exposed water, why not a friend's cat? The *gemara* answers: *de-chavrei kachish* — a friend's cat may not die from the poison, but it will grow weaker. This is not an acceptable harm to cause to another person's animal. But *didei nami kachish* — so too will your own cat weaken? Yes, but *hadar bari* — it will recover. And *de-chavrei nami hadar bari* — a friend's cat will also recover, so why can't you give it to his cat as well? The final answer: *zimnin de-ba'ei lizvonei u-maphsed lei minei* — sometimes the friend intends to sell his cat, and if the cat is weakened — even temporarily — it will fetch a lower price, and you will have caused him financial loss. Your own cat you may allow to get sick and recover; your friend's cat is not yours to subject to that process.
