# Shabbat 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kavod & Oneg Shabbat

### Previous Kavod Shabbat

We pick up on 119b, six lines down in the wide lines. The previous daf opened a rich discussion centered on *kavod Shabbat* and *oneg Shabbat* — the twin obligations of honoring and enjoying the Shabbat. A central theme was that it is not enough merely to have preparations completed; the *Rishonim* and *Amoraim* emphasize the importance of personal involvement in the act of preparation itself, as one would invest in receiving an important guest. There was also a nuanced discussion about dress during the preparation process — whether one should don fine clothing befitting the day or more workmanlike attire suited to the physical labor of preparing. Even the question of appropriate dress for one's *kavod Shabbat* preparations is treated as a substantive issue rather than a mere aside. This is the broader context within which the following discussion unfolds: the laws of eating and honoring Shabbat.

### Yosef Mokir Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹסֵף מוֹקַר שַׁבֵּי, הֲוָה הָהוּא גּוֹי בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה נְפִישִׁי נִכְסֵיהּ טוּבָא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ כַּלְדָּאֵי: כּוּלְּהוּ נִכְסֵי — יוֹסֵף מוֹקַר שַׁבֵּי אָכֵיל לְהוּ. אֲזַל זַבְּנִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ נִיכְסֵי, זְבַן בְּהוּ מַרְגָּנִיתָא, אוֹתְבַהּ בִּסְיָינֵיהּ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא עָבַר מַבָּרָא — אַפְרְחֵיהּ זִיקָא, שַׁדְיֵיהּ בְּמַיָּא, בַּלְעֵיהּ כַּוְורָא. אַסְּקוּהּ, אַיְיתוּהּ אַפַּנְיָא דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא. אָמְרִי: מַאן זָבֵין כִּי הַשְׁתָּא? אָמְרִי לְהוּ: זִילוּ אַמְטְיוּהּ לְגַבֵּי יוֹסֵף מוֹקַר שַׁבֵּי דִּרְגִיל דְּזָבֵין. אַמְטְיוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, זַבְנֵיהּ, קַרְעֵיהּ אַשְׁכַּח בֵּיהּ מַרְגָּנִיתָא. זַבְּנַיהּ בִּתְלֵיסַר עִילִּיָּתָא דְּדִינָרֵי דְּדַהֲבָא. פְּגַע בֵּיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא אֲמַר: מַאן דְּיָזֵיף שַׁבְּתָא — פַּרְעֵיהּ שַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The famous *aggadata* of **Yosef who honors Shabbat: There was a gentile in his neighborhood whose property was extremely plentiful. The astrologers said to** the gentile with regard to **all his property: Yosef who honors Shabbat will consume it.** The gentile **went and sold all of his property,** and with the money he received **he bought a pearl,** and he **placed it in his hat. When he was crossing a river** in **a ferry, the wind blew** his hat **and cast it into the water,** and **a fish swallowed it.** The fish **was** caught and **removed** from the water and **it was brought** to shore adjacent to **nightfall on Shabbat eve.** The fishermen **said: Who buys** fish at a time **like this?** The townspeople **said to** the fishermen: **Go bring it to Yosef who honors Shabbat, as he regularly purchases** delicacies in deference to Shabbat. **They brought it to him** and **he purchased it. He ripped** the fish open and **found a pearl inside it. He sold it for thirteen vessels filled with golden dinars.** An elderly man **encountered him and said: One who lends** to **Shabbat, Shabbat repays him.**

The story illustrates not only the reward for honoring Shabbat but the spiritual logic behind personal investment in its observance: when a person throws himself into the preparations with genuine enthusiasm, it reflects an internalized appreciation of Shabbat's sanctity that no mere delegation of tasks can replicate.

## Fire & Shabbat Desecration

### Fire & Chillul Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אֵין הַדְּלֵיקָה מְצוּיָה אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ חִילּוּל שַׁבָּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם לֹא תִשְׁמְעוּ אֵלַי לְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת וּלְבִלְתִּי שְׂאֵת מַשָּׂא וְגוֹ׳ וְהִצַּתִּי אֵשׁ בִּשְׁעָרֶיהָ וְאָכְלָה אַרְמְנוֹת יְרוּשָׁלִַם וְלֹא תִכְבֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Rav Yehuda berei de-Rav Shmuel mishmei de-Rav*: **Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel said in the name of Rav: Fire is only prevalent in a place where there is desecration of Shabbat, as it is stated: "And if you do not heed Me to sanctify the day of Shabbat, and to refrain from carrying burdens** and come to the gates of Jerusalem on the day of Shabbat, **and I will light a fire in its gates and it will consume the palaces of Jerusalem and it will not be extinguished"** (Jeremiah 17:27).

This statement ties directly into the broader discussion of fire breaking out on Shabbat, which is the formal topic of the *sugya* — namely, what food may be saved when a fire breaks out. Having established great praise for those who honor Shabbat, and having noted that if Israel were to keep two Shabbatot in full observance redemption would come immediately, the *Gemara* now turns to the rhetorical counterpart: the negative consequences of *chillul Shabbat*. The pivot is natural, as the very scenario of a fire on Shabbat invites the question of causation.

### Jeremiah Verse

The source for Rav Yehuda's statement is the verse in Yirmiyahu: *ve-im lo tishme'u elai lekadesh es yom ha-Shabbat ve-lavitasei masa* — if you do not listen to Me to sanctify the Shabbat and to refrain from carrying burdens — *ve-hitzati esh be-sha'areha ve-achla armonot Yerushalayim velo tichbe* — then I will kindle a fire in its gates, and it will consume the towers of Jerusalem, and it will not be extinguished.

### Nachman: Fire Not Put Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְלֹא תִכְבֶּה״? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵין בְּנֵי אָדָם מְצוּיִין לְכַבּוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *Gemara* asks: **What is the meaning of the phrase "it will not be extinguished"?** *Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak*: **It will occur at a time when people are not around to put it out, so that it continues to burn.**

Presumably that time is Shabbat itself, when people are in *shul* and not positioned to fight a fire.

### Midda Kneged Midda

This constitutes a measure of *midda keneged midda* — measure for measure — for not keeping Shabbat. One might ask whether statements of this kind are meant literally as divine retribution, or metaphorically as rhetorical exhortation to greater observance. They are clearly punitive in nature, not descriptive. It is important to note the danger in applying this kind of rhetoric retroactively as a form of *onaat devarim* — causing pain through words — by blaming individuals or communities for tragedies that befall them. The *Gemara*'s intent is plainly to encourage observance, not to provide a framework for finger-pointing at those who suffer. The arrogance of claiming to know precisely how divine providence operates, combined with the cruelty of compounding someone's tragedy with blame, makes such after-the-fact attribution deeply problematic — even if the *Gemara* itself speaks in these terms as a hortatory device.

## Jerusalem's Destruction

### Abbaye: Shabbat Violation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁחִלְּלוּ בָּהּ אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִשַׁבְּתוֹתַי הֶעְלִימוּ עֵינֵיהֶם וָאֵחַל בְּתוֹכָם״.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Abbaye*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because Shabbat was desecrated within it. The verse cited is: "And from My Shabbatot they averted their eyes, and I was profaned among them"** (Ezekiel 22:26).

The language of *ve-achel* here connects to the root of *le-hatchil*, beginning, the commencement of devastation.

### Rabbi Abbahu: Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁבִּיטְּלוּ קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי מַשְׁכִּימֵי בַבֹּקֶר שֵׁכָר יִרְדֹּפוּ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה כִנּוֹר וָנֶבֶל תּוֹף וְחָלִיל וָיַיִן מִשְׁתֵּיהֶם וְאֵת פּוֹעַל ה׳ לֹא יַבִּיטוּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״לָכֵן גָּלָה עַמִּי מִבְּלִי דָעַת״.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Rabbi Abbahu*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because they ceased to recite the *shema* in the morning and in the evenings. The verse cited is: "Woe to those who rise early in the morning to pursue intoxicating drink.** And there was harp, lute, drum, flute, and wine at their feasts, and they do not look at the work of God. And then: "Therefore My people was exiled for lack of knowledge"** (Isaiah 5:11–13).

Rashi explains this last phrase with reference to the declaration of God's unity — *meyached shemo*, the proclamation of *Hashem Echad*. The broader reading, however, encompasses something more: a failure to perceive God's presence in the world, which is precisely what the *berakhot* surrounding *shema* in *shacharit* are about — *yotzer ha-me'orot*, the creation of light, *ma'aseh bereishit*, the ongoing creation. Instead of cultivating this awareness, the people pursued pleasure.

What is striking about this shift in the *Gemara* is the move to what might seem like a relatively minor infraction — neglecting *keriat shema* — in the context of explaining a national catastrophe. The point may be precisely that before one focuses on gross violations, it is worth identifying the smaller failures that are foundational to the entire religious orientation. A lack of that bedrock awareness of God in the world — the very thing *shema* is designed to cultivate morning and evening — is what ultimately opens the door to the larger spiritual and moral collapse that leads to destruction.

### Rav Hamnuna: Education

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁבִּיטְּלוּ בָּהּ תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁפוֹךְ עַל עוֹלָל בַּחוּץ וְגוֹ׳״. מַה טַּעַם שְׁפוֹךְ? — מִשּׁוּם דְּ״עוֹלָל בַּחוּץ״.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Rav Hamnuna*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because the schoolchildren who attended religious instruction were prevented from learning. The verse is: "Pour out wrath upon the suckling in the street.** The *Gemara* interprets: "Why is wrath poured out? Because the children are outside rather than in school where they should be."** (Jeremiah 6:11).

This too fits the pattern of the preceding statements: perhaps a community is diligent in observance, perhaps it respects authority, but if it does not invest sufficiently in the Jewish education of the next generation, it reveals a fundamental indifference to continuity and to the weight of Torah as the animating force of communal life. That indifference ultimately erodes everything else. It is worth noting that this teaching was eventually translated into practical law: the *Rambam* records in *Hilkhot Talmud Torah* that school children may not be dismissed from learning even for the purpose of building the *Beit HaMikdash* — a legal ruling that reflects how seriously the *Gemara* takes this principle.

### Ulla: Shame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לָהֶם בּוֹשֶׁת פָּנִים זֶה מִזֶּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹבִישׁוּ כִּי תוֹעֵבָה עָשׂוּ גַּם בּוֹשׁ לֹא יֵבוֹשׁוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Ulla*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because they had no shame before one another for acting improperly. The verse is: "They should have been ashamed, for they committed an abomination, yet they are not ashamed"** (Jeremiah 6:15).

When a basic sense of shame for misconduct disappears from a society, the practical restraint that shame provides disappears with it. Here again the common denominator with the preceding statements becomes clear: these are not descriptions of some spectacular transgression, but of characterological and attitudinal failures — a way of being in the world that removes the internal and social brakes on sin.

### Rav Yitzchak: Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁהוּשְׁווּ קָטָן וְגָדוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה כָעָם כַּכֹּהֵן״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״הִבּוֹק תִּבּוֹק הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Rav Yitzchak*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because they equated the small with the great, failing to recognize and appropriately honor differences in stature and merit. The verse cited is: "The people were like the *kohen*, indicating a total flattening of distinction between laity and religious leadership. The continuation — "The earth shall be utterly desolate,"** signals the inevitable emptying out that follows.

### Rabbi Chanina: Tochacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא הוֹכִיחוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָיוּ שָׂרֶיהָ כְּאַיָּלִים לֹא מָצְאוּ מִרְעֶה״. מָה אַיִל זֶה, רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד זְנָבוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, אַף יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר כָּבְשׁוּ פְּנֵיהֶם בַּקַּרְקַע וְלֹא הוֹכִיחוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Rav Amram brei de-Rabbi Shimon bar Abba, amar Rabbi Shimon bar Abba, amar Rabbi Chanina*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because people did not give *tochacha* — remonstrance — to one another. The verse cited is: "Its officers were like harts that found no pasture.** Just as harts walk in a line with each head at the tail of the one before it, so too in that generation, everyone buried their faces in the ground and did not look one another in the eye, and thus did not rebuke one another.**

This failure of moral courage and mutual accountability then led to compounding sin.

### Rav Yehuda: Respect Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁבִּיזּוּ בָּהּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּהְיוּ מַלְעִיבִים בְּמַלְאֲכֵי הָאֱלֹהִים וּבוֹזִים דְּבָרָיו וּמִתַּעְתְּעִים בִּנְבִיאָיו עַד עֲלוֹת חֲמַת ה׳ בְּעַמּוֹ עַד [לְ]אֵין מַרְפֵּא״. מַאי ״עַד לְאֵין מַרְפֵּא״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמְבַזֶּה תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים אֵין לוֹ רְפוּאָה לְמַכָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Rav Yehuda*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because they showed contempt for Torah scholars within it. The verse is: "And they mocked God's emissaries, disdained His word, and scorned His prophets, until the anger of God rose against His people, until there was no remedy"** (II Chronicles 36:16). *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: **Whoever denigrates a *talmid chacham*, the wound he incurs will have no healing** — a direct reading of the phrase *ad le-ein marpe*.

The *Gemara* then draws on an additional verse:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אַל תִּגְּעוּ בִּמְשִׁיחָי וּבִנְבִיאַי אַל תָּרֵעוּ״, ״אַל תִּגְּעוּ בִּמְשִׁיחָי״ — אֵלּוּ תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן, ״וּבִנְבִיאַי אַל תָּרֵעוּ״ — אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** *Al tigu bi-meshichai u-vi-nvi'ai al tera'u* — **do not touch My anointed ones, and do not harm My prophets.** *Alu tinokot shel beit rabban* — **"My anointed ones" refers to the schoolchildren, who were anointed with oil as part of their initiation into learning.** *Ve-elu talmidei chachamim* — **"My prophets" refers to the Torah sages.**

Both sets of statements converge: whether one speaks of the youngest children in *cheder* or the most accomplished scholars, the disregard for Torah and its bearers at every level is the thread connecting all of these explanations for Jerusalem's destruction.

## School Children & Torah

### Reish Lakish: Children's Breath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: אֵין הָעוֹלָם מִתְקַיֵּים אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל הֶבֶל תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Reish Lakish mishum Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah*: **The world only continues to exist by virtue of the breath of schoolchildren, the pure, innocent words of Torah emerging from their mouths.**

### Papa & Abaye Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: דִּידִי וְדִידָךְ מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה הֶבֶל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ חֵטְא לְהֶבֶל שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ חֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar lei Rav Papa le-Abaye*: **"What about our Torah learning, does it not similarly sustain the world? The response: Breath that is accompanied by sin is not comparable to breath that is without sin.**

There is a quality of innocence and purity in the learning of children who have not yet sinned that gives their Torah a distinctive power — not merely intellectual achievement, but something about moral innocence that sustains the world and gives it hope for the future.

### School Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: אֵין מְבַטְּלִין תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן אֲפִילּוּ לְבִנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** *Ve-amar Reish Lakish mishum Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah*: **One does not dismiss schoolchildren from their learning even for the sake of building the *Beit HaMikdash*.**

While much of this discussion has the character of *derasha* and *aggadata*, the *Rambam* codifies this principle as practical *halakha* in *Hilkhot Talmud Torah* — that children remain in school even if the Temple is being built. So seriously does the *Gemara* take the continuity of Torah education.

### City Without School

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: כָּךְ מְקּוּבְּלַנִי מֵאֲבוֹתַי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מֵאֲבוֹתֶיךָ: כׇּל עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן מַחֲרִיבִין אוֹתָהּ, רָבִינָא אָמַר: מַחְרִימִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** *Ve-amar Reish Lakish mishum Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah*: **Here is the tradition I received from my forefathers: Any city that does not have schoolchildren — that has not established a *cheder*, a Jewish school — will be destroyed.** *Ravina* adds that **the word is not merely *machrivin* — destruction — but *machrimin otah*, invoking *cherem*, a total and more severe form of destruction.**

## Trustworthiness & Ethics

### Rava: Trustworthy People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: לֹא חָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁפָּסְקוּ מִמֶּנָּה אַנְשֵׁי אֲמָנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹטְטוּ בְּחוּצוֹת יְרוּשָׁלִַים וּרְאוּ נָא [וּדְעוּ וּבַקְשׁוּ בִרְחוֹבוֹתֶיהָ אִם תִּמְצְאוּ אִישׁ] (אִם יֵשׁ אִישׁ) עוֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט מְבַקֵּשׁ אֱמוּנָה וְאֶסְלַח לָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** *Ve-amar Rava*: **Jerusalem was destroyed because trustworthy people, people of integrity, ceased to dwell within it. The verse cited is from Yirmiyahu: "Roam through the streets of Jerusalem and look, seek in its public squares — can you find even one person who acts with justice, who seeks faithfulness? If so, I will forgive it."** (Jeremiah 5:1).

This shifts the discussion from the realm of Torah and its study to a more general statement about basic moral character and faithfulness. The implication is that no such person could be found, and therefore it was destroyed. This connects back thematically to earlier material in the *sugya* about *shomer emunim* — keeping faith — and the importance of trustworthiness as a foundational civic and spiritual virtue.

### Ketina: Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת כִּשְׁלוֹנָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלָיִם לֹא פָּסְקוּ מִמֶּנָּה אַנְשֵׁי אֲמָנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִתְפֹּשׂ אִישׁ בְּאָחִיו בֵּית אָבִיו לֵאמֹר שִׂמְלָה לְכָה קָצִין תִּהְיֶה לָּנוּ״, דְּבָרִים שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם מִתְכַּסִּין בָּהֶן כְּשִׂמְלָה יֶשְׁנָן בְּיָדֶיךָ. ״וְהַמַּכְשֵׁלָה הַזֹּאת תַּחַת יָדֶךָ״,
>
> **Translation:** The *Gemara* challenges: *eini?* — is that really true? *Ve-amar Rav Ketina*: **Even at the time of Jerusalem's stumbling and decline, trustworthy people did not altogether cease. The verse cited is: "A man will grab his kinsman from his father's house and say: You have a cloak, be our ruler.** The *Gemara* explains the verse's imagery: the "cloak" (*simlah*) refers to **matters that people conceal within themselves as a garment conceals the body** — meaning, you possess Torah knowledge that you keep hidden. *Yeshnan be-yadecha* — you have this. You should therefore be our leader.**

### Rejected Leader Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בְּנֵי אָדָם עוֹמְדִין עֲלֵיהֶם אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נִכְשָׁלִים בָּהֶן יֶשְׁנָן תַּחַת יָדֶיךָ, ״קָצִין תִּהְיֶה לָנוּ״. ״יִשָּׂא בַיּוֹם הַהוּא לֵאמֹר לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ וְגוֹ׳״ — אֵין יִשָּׂא אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן שְׁבוּעָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת שֵׁם ה׳״. ״לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ״ — לֹא אֶהְיֶה מֵחוֹבְשֵׁי עַצְמָן בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. ״וּבְבֵיתִי אֵין לֶחֶם וְאֵין שִׂמְלָה״ — שֶׁאֵין בְּיָדִי לֹא מִקְרָא וְלֹא מִשְׁנָה וְלֹא גְּמָרָא. וּמִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִי אֲמַר לְהוּ גְּמִירְנָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לַן. הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר גְּמַר וּשְׁכַח, מַאי ״לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ״? — כְּלָל! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, כָּאן בְּמַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Matters so difficult that people cannot truly grasp them without first making mistakes and learning from those errors are in your hands; therefore, be our leader. To this the man responds with an oath: "He will take an oath on that day, saying: I will not be bound."** *Ein yisa ela leshon shevuah* — **Oath language is used here. And he explains: I will not be one of those who bind themselves to their seat in the *beit midrash*.** *U-ve-veiti ein lechem ve-ein simlah* — **In my house there is no bread and no cloak** — **I have neither Scripture nor Mishnah nor Gemara. But perhaps he could have simply claimed he forgot? Rather, he openly declares his ignorance. This demonstrates that even in the period of Jerusalem's decline, there existed people of basic honesty.**

### Torah vs. Business

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִי אֲמַר לְהוּ גְּמִירְנָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לַן. הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר גְּמַר וּשְׁכַח, מַאי ״לֹא אֶהְיֶה חוֹבֵשׁ״? — כְּלָל! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, כָּאן בְּמַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The *Gemara* probes: **But how does this prove trustworthiness? Perhaps he is not refusing to lie out of genuine honesty, but rather out of self-interest: if he claims to be a *talmid chacham*, they will immediately demand that he deliver a *Gemara shiur*, and he cannot. The *Gemara* responds: If his concern were self-image rather than truth, he could have simply said: "You're right, I was once learned, but I have forgotten."** That would have gotten him out of the difficulty without full disclosure. The fact that he admits to never having possessed Torah knowledge at all demonstrates genuine honesty. The *Gemara* then resolves the apparent contradiction between Rava's statement — that Jerusalem was destroyed because trustworthy people ceased — and the evidence that even in its decline, at least some honest people could be found: **There is no difficulty. In matters of Torah, perhaps people retained enough residual respect for Torah that they would not lie about their learning. In business dealings, where the *yetzer ha-ra* is stronger and the temptations greater, they ceased to be trustworthy** — and that is what ultimately brought about the destruction.

This is a striking resolution. The *sugya* begins with the centrality of Shabbat observance and the respect for Torah, and ends with the observation that even when other forms of honesty remained, the destruction came from a basic failure of commercial integrity — the loss of faithfulness in everyday economic life. While the earlier causes focused on observance and Torah, the final cause points to fundamental morality and *emunah* in human dealings as the cornerstone without which a community cannot endure.

## Saving Food Rules

### Basket of Bread

The *Gemara* now returns to the practical *sugya* of saving food during a fire on Shabbat, moving from the quantity limitation of three meals to more specific rules. The *Mishnah* states: *matzilim sal malei kikarot af al pi she-yesh bah meah seudot* — one may save a basket full of loaves of bread even if it contains a hundred portions. The basket, as a single object, may be taken out in its entirety — one is not required to remove bread from it in order to limit the quantity. The key is that this is one act of saving a single vessel.

### Single Objects

Similarly: *ve-igul shel dveilah* — a round pressed mass of figs — and *ve-chavis shel yayin* — a barrel of wine — may each be taken out in their entirety as single objects, even if their contents exceed three meals' worth. The principle in each case is that it is a *kli echad*, a single object, which may be carried out as one act regardless of how much food it contains.

### Save for Yourselves

In addition to the single-object exception, the *Mishnah* offers another method for saving more than three meals' worth: *ve-omer la-acherim: bo'u ve-hatzilu lachem* — one may say to others: come and save for yourselves. By relinquishing his ownership and inviting others to take the food, each person who comes may save three meals for himself, and the total amount preserved may far exceed what the homeowner could save on his own.

The *Gemara* follows: *im hayu pikchin, osin imahem cheshbon achar Shabbat* — if they were clever, they would settle accounts with the homeowner after Shabbat. The *Gemara* asks: *cheshbon mai avidtei?* — why is a settlement necessary at all? Once the homeowner declared the food ownerless (*hefker*), ownership passed to those who took it. *Amar Rav Chisda*: this is a matter of *midat chasidut* — a measure of piety. Strictly speaking, those who took the food may keep it; but as an act of righteousness, they would return it.

*Amar Rava*: but how can someone be called a *chasid* here if they intend to be compensated? A true *chasid* would return the food without expectation of payment. The *Gemara* responds: *hacha bi-yerei shamayim askinan* — here we are dealing not with a *chasid* but with a *yerei shamayim*, a God-fearing person with a strict moral code. Such a person operates in a middle register: *ve-lo nicha lei de-lihani me-acherim* — he does not want to profit from another's misfortune, but *u-ve-chinam nami lo nicha lei de-litrach* — he also does not want to expend effort without recompense. He neither appropriates the food as a windfall nor donates his effort for nothing. Therefore, *ve-hachi ka-amar* — this is what the *Mishnah* means: if they were *pikchin*, meaning, if they understood the arrangement clearly enough to know that settling accounts afterward does not constitute *sechar Shabbat* — payment for labor on Shabbat — because technically they were taking for themselves and the food was truly theirs, then they could make such an arrangement after Shabbat without violating any prohibition.

### Where to Save

The *Mishnah* is more stringent about food than it was about sacred books. For *sifrei kodesh*, the *Gemara* earlier allowed saving even to a courtyard with some architectural deficiency. For food, the rule is stricter: *le-chatzer me'urevet* — to a courtyard that is both architecturally complete and has an *eruv*. No allowances are made, because the concern is that permitting too much latitude will lead a person to extinguish the fire actively.

### Ben Beteira Lenient

*Ben Beteira omer*: *af le-she-eina me'urevet* — even to a courtyard without an *eruv*, as long as it is architecturally correct, saving food there is permitted. The tension here is instructive: for *sifrei kodesh*, leniency was extended on account of the sanctity of the texts; for food, the *Tanna Kamma* is stricter than even the standard rule of carrying would require, precisely because of the fear that one will come to extinguish the fire. Ben Beteira disagrees and applies a more lenient standard.

## Saving Clothes & Objects

### Vessels for Shabbat

The *Mishnah* continues: *u-le-sham motzi klei tashmisho* — there one may also bring out vessels and utensils needed for Shabbat use. Rashi understands this to mean objects one will actually use on Shabbat — furniture, household items, and the like — though the precise limits of what qualifies as needed for Shabbat remain subject to discussion.

### Wearing Clothes

By clothing, the *Mishnah* takes a different approach than it does with food. Rather than specifying a fixed quantity, it states: *u-lovesh kol mah she-yuchal lilbosh* — one may wear and take out whatever one is able to wear. *Ve-otef kol mah she-yuchal la-atof* — and one may wrap whatever one is able to wrap. Anything that can be carried out on one's body — piled on, layered, draped — may be taken out, even if far more than one would normally wear.

### Back & Forth

The *Tanna Kamma* permits going back and forth repeatedly: *ve-chozer ve-lovesh u-motzi* — returning, dressing again in additional garments, and going out again. This parallels the loophole by food — where others could be invited to take for themselves — except that here the food equivalent permitted others to act, while for clothing the homeowner himself may make multiple trips, each time loading on as many garments as he can wear.

### Rabbi Yose: 18 Garments

*Rabbi Yose omer shmonah asar keilim* — Rabbi Yose limits this to eighteen standard garments. The *Gemara* enumerates them: a *miktron* (an outer cloak), an *onkli* (a cape), a *punda* (a type of belt), a *kalvuvus shel pishtan* (a linen outer garment), a *chaluk* (a shirt), an *apilyon* (a cape or covering), a *ma'aforet* (a head scarf), two *safarkim* (straps, perhaps for holding up clothing), two *minalim* (shoes), two *anpalyot* (socks), *veis pargad* (boots), a *chagor she-bemotnav* (a belt at the waist), a *kova she-berosho* (a hat), and a *sudar she-be-tzavaro* (a kerchief around the neck) — in sum, eighteen items corresponding to the standard garments typically worn in that era.

### Save with Me vs. For Yourself

The *Gemara* notes an apparent asymmetry between the two loophole formulations in the *Mishnah*: by food, it says *bo'u ve-hatzilu lachem* — come and save for yourselves — while by clothing it says *bo'u ve-hatzilu imi* — come and save with me. The explanation is that by food, the homeowner is limited to three meals for himself, so in order for more to be saved, the food must actually become the property of others — hence *lachem*, for yourselves. By clothing, however, no such per-person limit applies to the homeowner: he may take out as many garments as he can carry on his body. Therefore, the clothing remains his, and he simply recruits others to assist him — *imi*, together with me.

## Fire Protection Methods

### Fireproof Leather Covering

The *Mishnah* now shifts from what may be removed to what may be done in place to prevent further burning. *Rabbi Shimon ben Nanas omer*: *porsin or shel gedi al gabei shidah, teivah u-migdal* — one may spread a goat-leather hide over furniture — a chest, a box, a cabinet — *she-achaz bah ha-or* — when fire has already caught one part of it — *mipnei she-hu mechareich* — because the leather will singe but not burn through. The leather functions as a fireproof cover, protecting the remaining portion of the furniture from the spreading flame. This is an active protective measure, and Rabbi Shimon ben Nanas permits it.

### Vessel Barriers

*Ve-osin mechitza be-chol ha-keilim, bein melai'in bein reikanim, bishvil she-lo ta'avor ha-dleikah* — one may also create a barrier using any kind of vessels, whether filled or empty, to prevent the fire from spreading. The idea is that surrounding the burning item with vessels — even empty ones — can contain or redirect the fire.

### Rabbi Yosi: Water Vessels

*Rabbi Yosi oser bi-khlei cheres chadashim melei'im mayim* — Rabbi Yosi prohibits using new pottery vessels filled with water. His reasoning: *lefi she-einan yekholim lekabel et ha-or ve-hem mipakin u-makhabin et ha-dleikah* — new pottery vessels cannot withstand the heat of the fire; they will crack open, and the water inside will spill out and extinguish the fire. From the *Tanna Kamma*'s silence on this point, the *Gemara* infers that the first opinion permits even this — the indirect extinguishment that results when the vessel bursts and the water douses the flames.

### Water Saturation Method

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: *tallit she-achazah bah ha-or mitzad echad, notnin aleha mayim mitzad acher* — if a garment has caught fire on one side, one may pour water on the other side of the garment. *Im kavta kavta* — if it goes out, it goes out. The mechanism is that the water saturates the unburned portion, and the fire extinguishes itself before reaching the saturated area. This is *geram kibbuy* — indirect extinguishment — rather than active dousing of the fire, and the *Tanna Kamma* of the *Mishnah* permits it.

*Tosafot* raises the question of whether saturating the garment with water involves a concern about *libun* — the prohibited melakhah of bleaching — but that analysis belongs elsewhere.

A *baraita* presents a parallel ruling: *tallit she-achazah bah ha-or mitzad echad — poshtah u-mitkasseh bah, ve-im kavtah kavtah* — if a garment has caught fire on one side, one may remove it, put it on, and if the act of wearing it causes the fire to go out, that is permissible. Similarly: *vekhein Sefer Torah she-achazah bo ha-or — poshto ve-korei bo, ve-im kavtah kavtah* — if a *Sefer Torah* has caught fire, one may open it and read from it, and if the fire goes out as a result, that is permissible.

Both of these cases raise a genuine conceptual difficulty. In what sense are these acts truly *eino mitkaven* — without intention? If one spreads the garment or opens the *Sefer Torah* specifically hoping the fire will be extinguished, this looks less like an unintended consequence and more like a desired outcome achieved through an ostensibly different act. Perhaps the best reading of these cases is that the person genuinely is not intending the extinguishment — he is simply wearing his garment or reading the *Sefer Torah* — and if the fire goes out, it goes out. That reading at least preserves the formal structure of *eino mitkaven*, and the later *Gemara* analysis of the door-and-lamp case suggests that this is indeed how the *Gemara* reads these scenarios.

### Natural Extinguish

The *Gemara* asks why the *baraita* does not simply permit the active use of water, as Rabbi Shimon ben Nanos does. The answer: *keman de-amar ke-Rabbi Shimon ben Nanos* — the *baraita* presumably follows Rabbi Shimon ben Nanos's position. But then the *Gemara* objects: *aima de-amar Rabbi Shimon ben Nanos mishum de-mechareich* — Rabbi Shimon ben Nanos permits only the leather cover because it singes rather than extinguishes. *Geram kibbuy mi amar?* — who ever said Rabbi Shimon ben Nanos permits indirect extinguishment? The *Gemara* answers: yes, he does permit it — as is proven from the fact that the *Tanna Kamma* of the *Mishnah*, by contrast with Rabbi Yosi, permits placing vessels of water that will burst and douse the fire, which is itself a form of *geram kibbuy*. This demonstrates that the first opinion endorses *geram kibbuy* in principle.

## Grama & Fire Out

### Grama Permitted?

The *Gemara* now addresses the question of *grama* — indirect causation — more broadly, and whether the *Tanna Kamma* truly permits indirect extinguishment through water. The inference from Rabbi Yosi's prohibition: *mikhlal de-Tanna Kamma sharei* — since Rabbi Yosi explicitly forbids the water-filled pottery vessels that will burst and put out the fire, the implication is that the *Tanna Kamma* permits this form of indirect extinguishment. This is a significant ruling, and the *Gemara* proceeds to test its limits through a series of related cases.

It must be emphasized that this is a very important *Gemara* for practical *halakha*. Much of the modern discussion around Shabbat-mode appliances, the devices developed by *Tzomet*, and other indirect methods of activating or deactivating electrical systems draws on the principles being developed here around *grama* and its permissibility.

### Shaking Off Lamp

*Ner she-al gabei tavla* — if there is a lamp on top of a table, one may shake the table and allow the lamp to fall off, *ve-im kavtah kavtah* — and if it goes out, it goes out. This is permitted because the primary act is shaking the table — perhaps for some other purpose, such as clearing or cleaning it — and the extinguishment, if it occurs, is a secondary consequence.

The school of Rabbi Yannai qualifies this: *lo shanu ela be-sho'eiach* — this ruling applies only if the lamp was left on the table by accident, i.e., forgotten there. *Aval be-mani'ach* — but if the lamp was deliberately placed on the table, *na'aseh basis le-davar asur* — the table becomes a *basis* — a supporting base — for a prohibited object, and as such it takes on the status of *muktzeh* itself, making it impermissible to move.

### Door & Lamp

*Ner she-achorei ha-delet* — if there is a lamp burning behind a door, *pote'ach ve-no'el ke-darko, ve-im kavtah kavtah* — one may open and close the door in the normal way, and if the lamp goes out as a result, that is permitted. This case is more straightforwardly *eino mitkaven*: the person genuinely wants to open his door, not to extinguish the lamp. The question is only whether the opening of the door will foreseeably extinguish the flame.

### Rav's Curse

*Lait alei Rav* — Rav cursed at this ruling or at one who would act upon it. The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama lait lei Rav?* — why did Rav curse? One might think it is because Rav holds like Rabbi Yehuda that *eino mitkaven* is prohibited, and the *Tanna* who taught the leniency holds like Rabbi Shimon who permits *eino mitkaven*. But surely Rav would not curse everyone who rules like Rabbi Shimon in this domain.

The *Gemara* rejects this explanation: *be-ha afilu Rabbi Shimon modeh* — in this case, even Rabbi Shimon would agree that there is a problem. The reason is the principle of *psik reisha*: *modeh Rabbi Shimon bi-psik reisha ve-lo yamut* — Rabbi Shimon agrees that if an act inevitably produces a prohibited consequence, it is forbidden even if unintended. If the lamp is situated directly behind the door such that opening the door will certainly extinguish it, that is *psik reisha*, and Rabbi Shimon would prohibit it.

### Psik Reisha

The question then becomes one of certainty versus probability. *Psik reisha* requires that the prohibited consequence be definite — if one cuts off a head, it will certainly die. But here, it may not be certain that opening the door will extinguish the lamp; it depends on conditions such as the proximity of the lamp to the door and the force of any draft. This leads some *Acharonim* to develop the category of *karov le-psik reisha* — something approaching but not quite reaching the certainty of *psik reisha*, yet still highly probable. Whether such a near-certainty is treated as *psik reisha* or retains the more lenient status of *eino mitkaven* is itself a significant debate among later authorities.

At minimum, the *Gemara*'s framing here clarifies what was difficult about the earlier cases: the permissibility of wearing the burning garment or opening the *Sefer Torah* rests on the assumption that the extinguishment is genuinely not the person's aim and is not an inevitable consequence of the act. If extinguishment were certain, it would be *psik reisha* and prohibited even for Rabbi Shimon.

### Wind Principle

*Amar Rav Yehuda*: *pote'ach adam delet keneged medura be-Shabbat* — one may open a door opposite a bonfire on Shabbat. The assumption is that a full bonfire will not be extinguished by the draft from an opening door.

*Abaye* questions this: *be-mai askinan?* — what conditions are we discussing? *Eileima be-ruach metzuya* — if we are speaking of ordinary, calm wind, *mai ta'ama de-man de-assar?* — who would ever prohibit opening the door? A normal draft will not affect a bonfire. *Ei be-ruach she-eina metzuya* — if we are speaking of a strong, unusual wind, *mai ta'ama de-man de-sharei?* — why should anyone permit it, if the wind is strong enough that extinguishment becomes likely?

The *Gemara* resolves: *le-olam be-ruach metzuya* — the case is indeed ordinary wind. *Mar savar gazrinan* — one authority holds that we make a *gezeirah*: we prohibit opening the door even in ordinary wind, lest a person grow accustomed to doing so and then fail to be careful when the wind is strong. *U-mar savar lo gazrinan* — and the other authority holds that no such *gezeirah* is necessary; when the wind is strong, a person will naturally be alert to the danger and will refrain.

The broader principle emerging from this discussion is significant: it raises the question of whether *psik reisha* reasoning applies only to cases of absolute certainty, or whether cases of high probability — *karov le-psik reisha* — similarly trigger a prohibition, whether as a matter of substantive law or as a *gezeirah*. The *Acharonim* develop this point extensively from the present *Gemara*. The discussion of *garam kibbuy* — indirect extinguishment — continues in the following daf.
