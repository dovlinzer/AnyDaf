# Bava Batra 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chazakah & Boundaries

### Field Demarcation Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, דְּתַנְיָא: הוֹצִיא חֲצִי גְרוֹגֶרֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִנִּיחָה, וְחָזַר וְהוֹצִיא חֲצִי גְרוֹגֶרֶת אַחֶרֶת; בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד – חַיָּיב. בִּשְׁנֵי הֶעְלֵמוֹת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** They serve as a barrier between fields **even with regard to the matter of Shabbat, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who **carried out half** of **a dried fig** from a private domain **into the public domain and placed it** there, **and** then **returned and carried out another half** of **a dried fig,** if it was done **within one lapse of awareness,** i.e., he did not remember in the interim that this act is prohibited or that it was Shabbat, the two acts are considered as one, and since the two items together equal the size of a dried fig, he is **liable** to bring a sin-offering. But if it was done **within two lapses** of awareness, i.e., after he carried out the first half of a dried fig he remembered that this act is prohibited or that it was Shabbat, but subsequently forgot again and carried out the second half of a dried fig, he is **exempt.**

The daf opens by returning to a question that had occupied the previous session: what type of boundary marker suffices to demarcate two separate fields for the purposes of *chazakah*? The practical context is *nichsei ha-ger* — property left ownerless when a convert dies without heirs. When someone performs an act of *chazakah* on such a field, the question is how far that acquisition extends. If the field were being sold or transferred, the transaction itself would define the scope of the *chazakah*, even across physically separate parcels. But when the property is *hefker*, the physical reality must do all the definitional work. Accordingly, if there is a boundary line, a mound, trees, or a strip of demarcating grass between two plots, an act of *chazakah* on one does not reach the other.

### Pe'ah & Tum'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲבָל לְשַׁבָּת לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers, though, that even Ravin holds that a boundary or sea squill serves as a barrier only with regard to *pe'a* and ritual impurity, **but with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat,** they do **not** serve as a barrier between fields.

The previous day's discussion had examined this question through two other areas of *halacha*. The first was *pe'ah*: does one leave a single corner of the field for the poor, or two separate corners — one from each plot? The second was *tum'ah*: when there is doubt whether a person entered a field containing a source of ritual impurity, Rabbi Eliezer's rule that doubt in a private domain resolves to *tamei* applies only if the person definitely entered that field. If the very entry is in doubt, the person is *tahor*. The question then becomes whether a boundary marker causes two plots to be treated as distinct fields for this purpose, or as one. The discussion there reached the conclusion that while such a marker might not create two separate domains for most purposes, Rava held that it would for *Shabbat*.

### Shabbat Domains

The Gemara's last lines on 55b introduce the relevance of Rava's position to *Shabbat*. The general view is that a boundary marker within a continuous field would not create two separate *Shabbat* domains — *aval l'Shabbos lo*. Rava, however, maintains — *afilu l'inyan Shabbos* — that even for *Shabbat* purposes, such a demarcation creates two. The Gemara now proceeds to develop the *Shabbat* question fully.

## Reshut Separation

### Half Fig Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד; בִּרְשׁוּת אַחַת – חַיָּיב, בִּשְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. **Rabbi Yosei says:** If it was done **within one lapse of awareness,** and **in one domain,** i.e., he carried half the dried fig into the same public domain each time, he is **liable,** but if it was **in two domains,** i.e., he carried the item into two separate public domains, he is **exempt.**

The *beraita* cited to frame the *Shabbat* discussion involves a case of carrying half a *grogeres* — half a pressed fig, which is the minimum volume of food whose transfer from a private to a public domain constitutes a biblical violation of *Shabbat*. The case is: one carried half the required measure from a private domain into a public domain, set it down, and then carried another half measure. Do these two acts combine to constitute a single violation?

The operative principle is *he'elam echad* — one uninterrupted state of forgetting. If the person never remembered between the two acts that it was *Shabbat*, those acts collapse into a single continuous episode of inadvertent violation, obligating a *korban chattas*. If the person remembered in between — breaking the continuity of the lapse — then there are two separate acts and no combined liability, even if each act involved only half the minimum measure. The passage of time as such is not what breaks the *he'elam*; one could perform the second act three hours after the first and still be in one uninterrupted state of forgetting. What ruptures the *he'elam* is the moment of awareness in between.

### R. Yosi Requirement

Rabbi Yosi introduces an additional criterion beyond *he'elam echad*. Even granting a single continuous lapse, the two acts combine to form one violation only if both took place *bi-reshut achas* — within one domain. If the person brought half a measure into one public domain and then half a measure into a different public domain, Rabbi Yosi exempts. Tosafot notes that another Gemara implies an even stricter standard — not merely the same domain but the same specific location within that domain — though the *beraita* here is content to speak of the same domain generally.

The natural question that flows from Rabbi Yosi's ruling is: what is the minimum threshold for considering two public domains as distinct, such that carrying into each would yield the exemption?

### Rava Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: וְהוּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ חִיּוּב חַטָּאת בֵּינֵיהֶם, אֲבָל כַּרְמְלִית – לֹא. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ כַּרְמְלִית, אֲבָל פִּיסְלָא – לֹא. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ פִּיסְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says** in explanation of Rabbi Yosei's opinion: **And this** division of the public domain applies only **where there is** a property where one would incur **liability** to bring **a sin-offering** if one unintentionally carried out of it or into it, i.e., a private domain, **between** the two sections. **But** if there was only **a *karmelit*,** i.e., an area that is not defined as either a private domain or public domain and to and from which the prohibition against carrying is only of rabbinic origin, it does **not** divide the public domain. **Abaye says: Even a *karmelit*** divides the public domain into separate sections, **but a beam [*pisela*]** does **not. Rava says: Even a beam** divides the public domain, since it is no less than a boundary or sea squill, which do serve as a barrier between fields.

Rava defines the boundary: for two public domains to be considered separate under Rabbi Yosi's rule, there must be a *reshut ha-yachid* between them — an enclosed private domain. The logic is rooted in biblical categories. For *Shabbat* purposes, the Torah recognizes only two domains that carry biblical force: the enclosed *reshut ha-yachid* and the open *reshut ha-rabim*. Spaces that fall into neither category — neither fully enclosed nor a major thoroughfare — are a *karmelit*, a rabbinic domain. If two public domains are separated only by a *karmelit*, they are not genuinely distinct for these purposes; the carrying of half a measure into each would still combine to create one violation.

### Abaye Karmelit

Abaye disputes this threshold. In his view, even a *karmelit* suffices to separate two public domains under Rabbi Yosi's rule. As long as one public domain ends and something else begins before the next public domain opens, that intermediate zone — even if not a biblical *reshut ha-yachid* — constitutes a genuine separation.

### Rava Pisla

Rava extends his position in the other direction: even a *pisla* — a raised platform set within what is otherwise one continuous public thoroughfare — is sufficient to separate the domains on either side of it. The *pisla* does not transform part of the street into a different *reshut*; it is entirely embedded within the *reshut ha-rabim*. Yet Rava holds that its mere presence as a physical demarcation creates two separate domains for the purposes of Rabbi Yosi's rule.

### Shabbat-Gittin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: רְשׁוּת שַׁבָּת כִּרְשׁוּת גִּיטִּין דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rava follows his** own line of **reasoning, as Rava says:** The definition of **a domain** for the purpose of **Shabbat is like** the definition of **a domain** for the purpose of **bills of divorce:** Just as a beam is defined as a distinct domain for the purpose of bills of divorce, so too it is considered a distinct domain for the purpose of Shabbat.

The Gemara concludes this unit with Rava's programmatic statement: *reshut Shabbat ke-reshut Gittin dami* — the concept of domain for *Shabbat* purposes operates analogously to the concept of domain for *Gittin* purposes. The Rashbam explains the *Gittin* parallel: if a wife is standing in her husband's courtyard and he throws the *get* toward her, but the *get* lands on a small raised platform rather than on the ground beside her, the *get* does not take effect. The husband is presumed to have transferred ownership of one space to her, not two. Even a small platform, though located within the same courtyard, is treated as a distinct space — and it is precisely because of that distinction that the *get* falls outside the space she was *koneh*. Rabbeinu Gershom cites a related *halacha* that the wife and the *get* must occupy the same zone for the ground to function as a *yad*, an extension of the person receiving the *get*; a *get* falling on a platform while she stands on the ground fails this requirement. In either reading, Rava's point is that the same conception of spatial distinctness — in which even minimal physical differentiation creates a separate domain — governs both *Shabbat* and *Gittin*.

## Chatzav Plant

### No Marker Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שָׁם לֹא מֶצֶר וְלֹא חָצָב, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss the acquisition of a field that belonged to a convert who died without heirs. The Gemara asks: If **there was no boundary and** there was **no sea squill, what** are the limits to the acquisition?

The Gemara now returns to the original question of field boundaries for *nichsei ha-ger* and asks: *ein sham lo meitzar velo chatzav mai* — what is the law where there is no boundary marker and no *chatzav* plant? If there is no physical demarcation, how does one define the scope of the field within which an act of *chazakah* acquires?

### Community Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּירֵשׁ רַבִּי מָרִינוּס מִשְּׁמוֹ: כׇּל שֶׁנִּקְרֵאת עַל שְׁמוֹ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״בֵּי גַרְגּוּתָא דִּפְלָנְיָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Marinus explains in the name** of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Any** area **that is called by his name.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where it is called by his name? **Rav Pappa said: Where it is called: The place** that is irrigated by **so-and-so's well.** The entire area referred to as such would be considered one section with regard to acquisition.

The Rashbam understands the question to be asking how much land is acquired when a person performs a single act of *chazakah* on an ownerless estate with no visible boundary. One might have thought that without a marker, everything the deceased owned — however extensive — is acquired in one act; or alternatively, that only the precise area physically worked is acquired. The Gemara resolves this by citing the explanation given in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol shenikreit al shemah* — the defining criterion is community recognition. Whatever extent of land is commonly called by the name of the original owner — "the field of so-and-so," or as Rav Pappa specifies, "the irrigation area of so-and-so" — that constitutes the recognized unit. An act of *chazakah* on any part of it acquires the whole.

### Tosafot Tum'ah

Tosafot reads the question differently. Citing the *Tosefta* in *Taharos*, Tosafot argues that the question of *ein sham lo meitzar velo chatzav* is not a return to the *nichsei ha-ger* problem at all, but a continuation of the *tum'ah* question discussed earlier. When someone enters a valley during the rainy season and there is a source of *tum'ah* in a particular field, the halachic outcome depends on whether the person definitively entered that field. What counts as "that field" when there are no markers? The answer — *kol shenikreit al shemah*, recognized by the community as a distinct unit — provides the definition for *tum'ah* purposes as well.

### Sea Squill ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב אַחָא בַּר עַוְיָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי בַּר חֲנִינָא: חֲצוּבָא מַפְסֵיק בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר. מַאי חֲצוּבָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁבּוֹ תִּיחֵם יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Avya sat before Rabbi Asi, and he sat and was saying** the following **in the name of Rabbi Asi bar Ḥanina:** A row of **sea squill serves as a barrier with regard to the property of a convert** who died without heirs, so that each section is considered a distinct field. The Gemara asks: **What** is **sea squill? Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is the growth **by which Joshua established the boundaries of Eretz** Yisrael **for the Jews.**

The *chatzav* plant, which the Gemara lists alongside the *meitzar* as a recognized field boundary marker, is identified as sea squill. The Rashbam explains the name: *chotzev ba'adamah* — it cuts straight down into the earth. Its roots descend vertically without spreading laterally, and the plant itself grows straight up, making it an ideal boundary marker that does not encroach on either neighboring plot. Sea squill has a large bulb that is typically planted deep in the ground, making it extremely difficult to remove entirely — a property of obvious practical value for a permanent land marker. It produces green leaves in winter, dries out completely in summer, and at the end of the summer grows a tall white-flowered inflorescence. Because of its straight vertical form and annual regrowth, sea squill was used to demarcate field boundaries in ancient times and reportedly continues to serve this function in some Arab villages today. The fact that it is poisonous also discourages people from tampering with it.

### Joshua Boundary

Rav Yehuda cites this identification in the name of Rav: the *chatzuva* is the plant that Joshua used to demarcate the tribal and family land divisions in the land of Israel — *shebo ticham Yehoshua l'Yisrael et ha'aretz*. The choice of plant was deliberate: its straight-down, straight-up growth ensured it would not siphon nutrients from either adjacent plot and would remain precisely where placed, making it a reliable and self-maintaining marker of the divisions between tribe and tribe and between one family's portion and another's.

## Eretz Yisrael

### Border Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא מָנָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶלָּא עֲיָירוֹת הָעוֹמְדוֹת עַל הַגְּבוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara teaches a related statement. **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** In his book, **Joshua enumerated only the towns that stand upon the borders,** but not the towns that were within the portions of each tribe.

The mention of Joshua's method of demarcation prompts the Gemara to cite a related teaching in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav: *lo manah Yehoshua ela ayaros ha'omdos al ha-gvulin* — in Sefer Yehoshua, only the cities situated on the borders are enumerated. The reason is that those cities are the relevant ones for defining the territorial extent of the land. Tosafot, however, objects that this cannot mean the external borders of the land of Israel, since many cities listed in Yehoshua are located in the interior. Tosafot therefore reads the statement as referring to the inter-tribal borders — the cities mentioned are those that served as boundary markers between one tribe's territory and another's, a use of the same principle of marking divisions that motivated Joshua's use of the *chatzav* plant.

### Terumot Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל שֶׁהֶרְאָהוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה – חַיָּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** On the subject of the boundaries of Eretz Yisrael, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Any** area **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, showed to Moses** before his death, as it is written: "And the Lord showed him all the land, Gilead…as far as Zoar" (Deuteronomy 34:1–3), is within the boundaries of Eretz Yisrael, and therefore produce that grows there is **obligated in tithe.**

Immediately following is a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — a separate tradition, though reported by the same Rav Yehuda: *kol she'her'ahu Ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-Moshe chayav be-ma'aser* — everything that God showed Moses in his final vision from the heights of Moab is obligated in *ma'aser*. The Torah describes that vision in Devarim: God showed Moses all the land, from Gilead to Dan, all the land of Naftali, the land of Efrayim and Menasheh, and so on. That sweep of land defines what is subject to *terumot u-ma'aser*.

### Extra Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מַאי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי קֵינִי, קְנִיזִּי וְקַדְמוֹנִי. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נַפְתּוֹחָא, עַרְבָאָה וְשַׁלְמָאָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַר שֵׂעִיר, עַמּוֹן וּמוֹאָב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַרְדִּיסָקִיס, אַסְיָא וְאַסְפַּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To exclude what** area? The Gemara answers: **To exclude** the lands of the **Kenite, Kenizzite, and Kadmonite,** as God had promised to Abraham at the Covenant between the Pieces: "To your offspring have I given this land…to…the Kenite, and the Kenizzite, and the Kadmonite" (Genesis 15:18–19). These areas are not obligated in tithe. What are these three areas? It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says:** They are **Naftuḥa, Arva'a, and Shalma'a. Rabbi Yehuda says:** They are **Mount Seir, Ammon, and Moab. Rabbi Shimon says:** They are **Ardisekis, Asya, and Aspamya.**

The statement's exclusionary force — *le-appukei mai*, what does it come to exclude? — is directed at the lands of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni*. When God makes the covenant with Abraham in the *Brit Bein Ha-Betarim*, He promises ten nations' lands, not the seven Canaanite nations enumerated elsewhere in the Torah. Three nations — *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni* — appear in the covenant with Abraham but are conspicuously absent from every subsequent enumeration of the Canaanite nations. The Rabbis understood this discrepancy to mean that these three lands occupy a different status: they were promised to Abraham, but they are not *Eretz Yisrael* proper and their conquest would not subject them to the obligations of *terumot u-ma'aser*.

Tosafot raises a preliminary methodological objection: if the Gemara wants to demarcate *Eretz Yisrael* obligated in *ma'aser*, it would have been more natural to cite the explicit border description in Bamidbar, which specifically draws the boundaries of the land. Tosafot notes this without resolution, simply observing that the two passages are presumably consistent.

### Rashbam Distance

The Rashbam's reading of what it means that *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni* are excluded is striking. He maintains that even if Israel were to conquer those lands — even through a proper *kibbush rabbim*, a conquest authorized by the Sanhedrin after the conquest of *Eretz Yisrael* proper — those lands would still never become incorporated into *Eretz Yisrael* and never be obligated in *terumot u-ma'aser*.

This position creates an apparent difficulty from the Gemara's discussion of *Surya* — identified with a Transjordanian region conquered by David — where the question of whether it carries the obligations of *Eretz Yisrael* turns entirely on whether it was *kibbush yachid* or *kibbush rabbim*. That Gemara assumes that had the conquest been a proper *kibbush rabbim*, *Surya* would indeed be obligated in *terumot u-ma'aser*. If so, why would the lands of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni* be treated differently?

The Rashbam's answer appears to be one of geographic distance. *Surya* is immediately contiguous with *Eretz Yisrael* and is the type of land that can be absorbed into it under the right conditions. The lands of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni*, by contrast, are so distant that they can never become *Eretz Yisrael* proper regardless of the manner of their conquest. The Rashbam reads the Gemara in *Bereishit Rabbah* in support: even though the Torah promises these lands to Abraham, and even though we believe we will eventually receive them, they remain permanently outside the framework of *Eretz Yisrael* for purposes of *ma'aser*. The promise of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni* is of a different order — a future, perhaps messianic, addition — whereas the seven Canaanite nations represent the true promised land confirmed by oath.

### Tosafot Conquest

Tosafot substantially moderates the Rashbam's position. In Tosafot's reading, all the Gemara is saying is that the *kedusha rishona* — the sanctification of *Eretz Yisrael* that determines its obligations in *terumot u-ma'aser* — is defined by what was actually conquered in the time of Joshua, not by what was divinely promised to Abraham. The exclusion of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni* is simply the exclusion of what Joshua did not conquer. This is not a surprising statement: those lands were not part of Joshua's conquest, and therefore they are not included in the *kedusha rishona* regardless of the covenant with Abraham.

On this reading, Tosafot is making a conceptual point about the relationship between divine promise and halachic reality: *Eretz Yisrael* for the purpose of agricultural obligations is not determined by the full scope of what God promised Abraham. It is determined by the reality of what was conquered and settled. The boundaries described in Bamidbar function not as a definition of what is *kadosh* but as a directive — this is what you are expected to conquer — and the halachic status follows the actual history of conquest.

The question of *Surya* and David's conquests does not necessarily contradict this, since that discussion may operate under the framework of *kedusha shniya*, the sanctification established in the time of Ezra, which itself runs through the period of the *Chashmonaim* and potentially beyond. According to Tosafot, the conquest of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni* in some future scenario might indeed confer *kedusha* — Tosafot is not closing that door — but the *kedusha rishona* achieved through Joshua's conquest does not automatically extend to those lands.

### Opinions Lands

The Gemara presents three Tannaitic opinions on the geographic identity of *Keini, Kenizi, ve-Kadmoni*. Rabbi Meir identifies them as *Navtiya*, *Arviya*, and *Shalmiya* — nations situated, according to the commentators, in the southern reaches, from the Dead Sea extending toward Eilat and into northern Arabia, encompassing the area of the ancient Nabatean kingdoms. Rabbi Yehuda identifies them as *Har Seir*, *Amon*, and *Moav* — the territories of Esau and Lot, the lands bordering *Eretz Yisrael* immediately to the south and east, which the Torah explicitly instructs Israel not to conquer, for they have been given as a heritage to Esau and to the descendants of Lot. Rabbi Shimon identifies them as *Ardiscus*, *Asya*, and *Aspamya* — lands in Asia Minor and northern Syria.

Rabbi Yehuda's identification is particularly striking. The lands that God promised Abraham beyond the seven Canaanite nations — the lands that went to Abraham's family but not to the Jewish part of that family — turn out to be the territories of Esau and Lot. Abraham was promised ten nations; his descendants received seven. The other three went to his other descendants. It is a remarkable reading of the scriptural record: the covenant with Abraham was fulfilled in full, just not for *Bnei Yisrael* alone.

## False Witnesses

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלָהּ שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִים – מְשַׁלְּמִין לוֹ אֶת הַכֹּל. שְׁנַיִם בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, שְׁנַיִם בַּשְּׁנִיָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית – מְשַׁלְּשִׁין בֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **there were two** witnesses **testifying on his behalf that** he, the possessor of the land, worked and **profited** from a field for **three years,** and therefore has presumptive ownership, **and they were found** to be **conspiring** witnesses, as it was proven that they were not present to witness the matter about which they had testified, they must **pay** the true owner of the field **the full** value of the field that they attempted, through their testimony, to remove from his possession, as it is written in the Torah: "Then shall you do to him, as he had planned to do to his brother" (Deuteronomy 19:19). If **two** witnesses testify that he worked and profited from the field **during the first** year, another **two** testify that he worked and profited from it **during the second** year, **and** another **two** testify that he worked and profited from it **during the third,** and all were found to be conspiring witnesses, payment of the value of the field to the owner is **divided among them.**

The Gemara now turns to the *Mishnah* at the opening of a new chapter, which deals with *edim zomemim* — witnesses who are exposed as having given false testimony through the *hazamah* process, in which a second set of witnesses testifies that the first set was elsewhere at the time they claimed to have observed the events in question. In the context of *chazakah*, the situation is as follows: a claimant brings witnesses who testify that he occupied and worked a disputed field for three consecutive years, thereby establishing a *chazakah*. If those witnesses are subsequently exposed as *zomemim*, they must compensate the true owner for the value of the field they attempted to steal through testimony. *Meshalmim lo et ha-kol* — they pay him everything.

The Mishnah then complicates the scenario. If the three-year *chazakah* was established not by one pair of witnesses who testified to all three years, but by three separate pairs — the first pair testifying to year one, the second to year two, the third to year three — and all three pairs are subsequently exposed as *zomemim*, then *meshaleshin beineihem*: the obligation is divided equally among the three pairs, each bearing one-third of the liability. They acted together to achieve a single result and share the consequences.

### Brothers Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִים, וְאֶחָד מִצְטָרֵף עִמָּהֶם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שָׁלֹשׁ עֵדֻיוֹת, וְהֵן עֵדוּת אַחַת לַהֲזָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the testimony was given by **three brothers,** each of whom testify about one year, **and another** unrelated individual **joined with** each of the brothers as the second witness, **these are three** distinct **testimonies** and they are accepted by the court. If they were to be considered one testimony, it would not be accepted, as brothers may not testify together. **But they are one testimony for** the purpose of rendering them as **conspiring** witnesses, and the payment is divided among them.

The Mishnah then presents a further case: suppose the three pairs of witnesses were composed of three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — each brother pairing with an unrelated fourth witness, Mordechai, who happened to observe all three years. Group one consists of Reuven and Mordechai (year one); group two of Shimon and Mordechai (year two); group three of Levi and Mordechai (year three). The Mishnah rules that these constitute three valid and distinct testimonies — *harei elu shalosh eduyot*. Since each brother testified only to a single year, and each year is a distinct factual matter, there is no problem of two brothers testifying to the same event.

However, for purposes of *hazamah*, the Mishnah rules that it is not one unified testimony — *ve-ein edut achat le-hazamah*. The three testimonies are seen as a combined enterprise, all working toward a single result: awarding the *chazakah* to David. The *hazamah* therefore falls on them collectively, and none of the pairs is penalized as *zomemim* until all three are exposed simultaneously. A related observation is that if the claimant brought all three groups together, they are clearly viewed as one coordinated effort. Tosafot notes the question of why the first two pairs of witnesses cannot claim they were merely testifying that the claimant unlawfully ate the field's produce in those years — which, if true, would mean they were testifying on the original owner's behalf, not against him. Tosafot resolves this by noting that when the claimant himself produces the witnesses and introduces them together, their combined purpose is transparently directed at establishing the *chazakah*, and they are treated as a unified set.

## Years Combine

### Davar Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כְּשֶׁהָלַךְ אַבָּא חֲלַפְתָּא אֵצֶל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אֵצֶל אַבָּא חֲלַפְתָּא לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁאֲכָלָהּ שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם, שְׁנִיָּה בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם, שְׁלִישִׁית בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם – מַהוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, 2:10) that **Rabbi Yosei said: When Abba Ḥalafta,** Rabbi Yosei's father, **went to Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri to study Torah, and some say:** When **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri** went **to Abba Ḥalafta to study Torah, he said to him: What is** the *halakha* if **there is** one **who** worked and **profited** from a field **in the presence of two** witnesses during the **first year,** then **in the presence of two** other witnesses during the **second** year, and finally **in the presence of two** other witnesses during the **third** year? He **said to him: This is** sufficient for establishing the **presumption** of ownership.

The Gemara now flags that the *Mishnah* — which assumes that three separate pairs of witnesses, one per year, can collectively establish a *chazakah* — does not follow the view of Rabbi Akiva. A *beraita* records that Abba Chalafta went to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri (or, in the alternate version, Rabbi Yochanan went to Abba Chalafta) to study Torah, and the question was posed: if three different pairs of witnesses testified, respectively, to years one, two, and three of an occupation, do they combine to establish a *chazakah*? The answer offered was: *harei zo chazakah* — yes, they combine. Rabbi Yosi ben Chalafta reports that both parties agreed on this, but notes that Rabbi Akiva dissents.

### Akiva Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אַף אֲנִי אוֹמֵר כֵּן, אֶלָּא שֶׁרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא חוֹלֵק בְּדָבָר זֶה; שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״דָּבָר״ – וְלֹא חֲצִי דָבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The latter **said to him: I say this as well, but Rabbi Akiva disagrees with regard to this matter, as Rabbi Akiva would say** that since the verse states: "At the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15), one can derive that testimony is accepted only with regard to a complete **matter, and not** with regard to **half** of **a matter.** In this mishna, although presumptive ownership requires testimony that the property had been worked and profited from for three years, testimony is accepted from each pair of witnesses with regard to one year. Consequently, the ruling of the mishna does not accord with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva.

Rabbi Akiva's position is grounded in the scriptural requirement of testimony: *al pi shenayim edim yakum davar* — a matter is established on the testimony of two witnesses. Rabbi Akiva infers: *davar*, a full matter — *ve-lo chatzi davar*, not a half-matter. Since any single year of occupation does not by itself constitute a *chazakah* — indeed, one year of another person's presence on one's land proves the opposite of ownership, establishing that the occupier owes a year's produce to the true owner — each pair of witnesses is testifying to only a partial matter. A *chazakah* by definition requires three years; testimony to one year is not testimony to a *chazakah*, it is testimony to *chatzi davar* or less. Rabbi Akiva therefore demands that each pair of witnesses testify to the full three-year *chazakah* — a standard that is practically very difficult to meet.

### Half Matter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן – הַאי ״דָּבָר״ וְלֹא חֲצִי דָבָר, מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי אֶחָד אוֹמֵר אַחַת בְּגַבָּהּ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר אַחַת בִּכְרֵיסָהּ; הַאי חֲצִי דָבָר וַחֲצִי עֵדוּת הִיא! אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים אַחַת בְּגַבָּהּ, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים אַחַת בִּכְרֵסָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the Rabbis,** who accept the testimony of each of the three pairs of witnesses, **what do they do with this** derivation of: A complete **matter, and not half** of **a matter,** i.e., what type of testimony is disqualified based on this derivation? **If we say** that it serves **to exclude** a case where two witnesses testify that a young woman has two pubic hairs and has therefore reached maturity, where **one says** she has **one** hair **on her back and one says** she has **one** hair **on her** lower **abdomen,** i.e., they are testifying to two different pubic hairs, and in this case the Rabbis say this testimony is not accepted, since they each testify with regard to only half of the matter, that is difficult. But **this is** both **half** of **a matter and half** of **a testimony,** as there is only one witness with regard to each pubic hair. This testimony would not be valid even without the derivation. **Rather,** in the opinion of the Rabbis the derivation serves **to exclude** a case where **two** witnesses **say** she has **one** hair **on her back and two** witnesses **say** she has **one** hair **on her** lower **abdomen.** In this case, each group of witnesses gives full testimony with regard to half of a matter, i.e., one pubic hair, as both hairs must be present concurrently in order for her to assume the status of an adult. By contrast, in the case of the mishna, the years are by definition not concurrent. Therefore, the Rabbis rule that testimony with regard to one year is accepted.

The Gemara asks: according to the Rabbis, who hold that these three yearly testimonies do combine, what then does the verse *ve-lo chatzi davar* come to exclude? They must agree that partial testimonies cannot combine in some cases; otherwise the verse teaches nothing. The Gemara proposes: perhaps the verse excludes a case where one witness testifies to seeing a pubic hair from the front (*begava*) and a second witness testifies to seeing a pubic hair from behind (*be-kreisa*), in the context of establishing that a girl has reached halachic adulthood. Could those combine?

The Gemara rejects this formulation. If there is one witness to each half observation, the problem is not merely that we have *chatzi davar* — it is that we have *chatzi davar ve-chatzi edut*: not only is each witness's testimony incomplete in subject matter, but each testimony also lacks the required two witnesses for anything at all. That deficiency is so fundamental it needs no special verse. The verse must be teaching something more nuanced.

The correct exclusion, the Gemara concludes, is where two witnesses testify to one aspect and a different pair of two witnesses testifies to the other aspect — and those two full pairs cannot combine. That is *chatzi davar*: two complete sets of witnesses, each testifying to a genuine partial observation.

### Davar Meaning

The Rashbam and Tosafot offer two different accounts of why year-by-year *chazakah* testimony is not *chatzi davar* while the split pubic-hair testimony is. The Rashbam grounds the distinction in the completeness of what was available to be witnessed. When a witness testifies to year one, he has seen everything that was available to see during year one; there was nothing more to observe. He could not have seen year two at the time he was present. His testimony is therefore a complete account of a complete unit of time, even if that unit is one component of a larger structure. The pubic-hair witnesses, by contrast, were present and could have observed the full picture; they simply reported on one aspect of it. Their testimony is incomplete because the complete information was available and they testified to only part of it — that is *chatzi davar*.

Tosafot disagrees with this criterion. What matters, Tosafot argues, is not what could have been seen, but whether the testimony has independent halachic significance. A witness who testifies that David worked a field for one year provides testimony that can stand on its own in a different context: it establishes that David owes the true owner one year's rent or produce. That testimony is independently meaningful and therefore constitutes a full *davar* that can combine with similarly meaningful testimonies on subsequent years. A witness who testifies to one pubic hair contributes testimony that is halachically meaningless by itself; it serves no independent purpose in *halacha*, and without independent meaning it cannot stand as a *davar* at all.

## Wheat-Barley

### Contradiction Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר אֲכָלָהּ חִטִּים, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר אֲכָלָהּ שְׂעוֹרִים – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: אֲכָלָהּ רִאשׁוֹנָה שְׁלִישִׁית וַחֲמִישִׁית, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: אֲכָלָהּ שְׁנִיָּה רְבִיעִית וְשִׁשִּׁית, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּהָוְיָא חֲזָקָה?!
>
> **Translation:** § In a related matter, **Rav Yehuda says:** If two witnesses testify that one had worked and profited from a field for three years, where **one** witness **says he consumed wheat** from the field, **and one says he consumed barley** from **it, this is** sufficient for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. **Rav Naḥman objects to this** ruling: **If that is so,** then if **one** witness **says** he worked and **profited** from the field during the **first, third, and fifth** years; **and one** witness **says he** worked and **profited** from **it** during the **second, fourth, and sixth** years, would you **also** say **that** this **is** sufficient for establishing the **presumption** of ownership? What is the difference between testifying about different crops and testifying about different years?

Rav Yehuda raises the following case: one witness testifies that the claimant worked the field for three years growing wheat, and another witness testifies that the claimant worked that same field for three years growing barley. Is this a valid *chazakah*?

One way to understand why it might be valid is by way of abstraction. In *dinei mamonot* there is a view that if two witnesses each testify to the same monetary obligation — even if they describe completely different transactions — their testimonies can combine, since at the level of abstraction both are saying the same thing: that this person owes this amount of money. So here, both witnesses are saying, at the level of abstraction, that the claimant has a three-year *chazakah*. The Rashbam suggests this is the operative principle: one says *chazakah* through wheat, the other says *chazakah* through barley, and we abstract to say there is testimony of *chazakah*.

### Gaps Challenge

Rav Nachman challenges this by pushing the logic to what he regards as an untenable conclusion. If we can combine contradictory testimonies by abstracting to their common claim, then consider: one witness says the claimant worked years one, three, and five; another witness says the claimant worked years two, four, and six. By the same logic, should those not also combine? Together they cover six years of occupation. Yet intuitively this seems problematic — each witness is testifying to a pattern of alternate-year use that implicitly denies the other's years.

Tosafot notes that alternate-year occupation can itself be a valid *chazakah* in some circumstances, specifically where the custom is to leave a field fallow in alternate years. So the case Rav Nachman is positing is not simply incoherent; it is a genuine challenge to the principle of abstraction.

### Minor Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, בְּשַׁתָּא דְּקָא מַסְהֵיד מָר – לָא קָא מַסְהֵיד מָר; הָכָא, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בַּחֲדָא שַׁתָּא קָא מַסְהֲדִי. מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר – בֵּין חִיטֵּי לִשְׂעָרֵי? לָאו אַדַּעְתַּיְיהוּ דְּאִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said to him: How can** these cases **be compared? There,** i.e., in your example, **with regard to the year** about which one **Master,** i.e., witness, **is testifying,** the other **Master is not testifying** about it, while **here, both are testifying with regard to one year. What is there to say,** that there is a contradiction in their testimonies **between wheat and barley? It does not enter people's minds** to note this distinction. Two witnesses did, however, testify that he worked and profited from the field for three years.

Rav Yehuda responds: the two cases are not analogous. In the years-one-three-five versus years-two-four-six scenario, the witnesses are speaking about entirely different time periods — *beshata de-ka-mashid mar, lo ka-mashid mar* — when one was testifying, the other was not. They are not saying the same thing at all, even at an abstract level. In the wheat-barley case, by contrast, both witnesses are testifying about the same three years — *tarvaihu be-chad shata ka-mashdi*. They claim to have witnessed the same events and differ only in a detail. And as to that detail, the Gemara concludes: *bein chiti le-se'ari lav adatei de-inshei* — people simply do not pay close attention to whether it was wheat or barley. Witnesses are not being contradictory; they are being imprecise about a distinction that ordinary observers routinely miss. That level of imprecision does not constitute a halachic contradiction, and the testimonies validly combine.

### Unresolved Gap

What the Gemara leaves unresolved is a related scenario: suppose one pair of witnesses testifies to years one through three and another pair testifies to years four through six — each pair offering a complete consecutive three-year *chazakah* for a distinct period, with no overlap and no contradiction. Can those two testimonies be combined? This question is analogous to the case of two witnesses who each testify that a defendant owes a hundred dollars, but for entirely different and unrelated reasons. There is genuine debate in the Gemara about whether such abstracted combination is valid. The question is left open: whether one may take two independent claims of *chazakah* covering non-overlapping years and combine them into a single *chazakah* claim is not definitively answered.

### Crop Rotation

The Rashbam reads the entire wheat-barley discussion quite differently from the straightforward reading. In his interpretation, the case involves one witness saying the claimant grew wheat in years one, three, and five, and a second saying the claimant grew barley in years two, four, and six — explicitly covering six years in a staggered pattern. The question is whether these together constitute a *chazakah*. The Rashbam then reports that he went and investigated the matter empirically: *sha'alti le-ovdei ha-adama* — he actually asked farmers how they work their land. They told him — *le-olam kach hu ha-minhag* — that it is always the standard practice to rotate crops, one year wheat and one year barley, and that when one does so one need not leave the field fallow at all. On this basis the Rashbam concludes that the two witnesses are not contradicting one another in the slightest. They are each describing a different set of years of an entirely standard six-year cultivation cycle. Their testimonies are perfectly consistent and combine without difficulty to establish a *chazakah*.

Whatever the correct reading, the Gemara's conclusion is clear: testimonies that appear to differ on minor details that witnesses routinely miss — like the specific crop grown — are not treated as contradictions and do combine. Whether testimonies can be combined at a higher level of abstraction, when they describe genuinely distinct acts that merely point to the same legal conclusion, remains a question for further analysis.
