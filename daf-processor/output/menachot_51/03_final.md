# Menachot 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Death Between Portions

### Chavitai Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא מִינּוּ כֹּהֵן אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, מִשֶּׁל מִי הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁל יוֹרְשִׁין. וּשְׁלֵימָה הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **they did not appoint another** High **Priest in his stead, from whose** property **was** the griddle-cake offering brought and **sacrificed? Rabbi Shimon says:** It is brought and sacrificed **from** the property **of the community. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is brought and sacrificed **from** the property **of the heirs** of the High Priest. **And** for the duration of the period until a new High Priest was appointed, the griddle-cake offering **was sacrificed** as **a complete** tenth of an ephah of fine flour.

The daf begins with a *mishna* concerning the *chavitai kohen gadol* — the *mincha* offering that the *kohen gadol* brings each day, half an *issaron* in the morning and half in the afternoon. The preceding *mishna* had addressed the case where the *kohen gadol* dies between the two portions and a new *kohen gadol* is immediately appointed in his stead; it detailed how that successor brings his half. The present *mishna* takes up the same basic scenario but with a different conclusion.

### No Replacement

Here, the *kohen gadol* dies between the morning and afternoon portions, but no new *kohen gadol* is appointed right away — it simply takes time. The *mishna* therefore asks: *mishel mi haysah kreiva?* — from whose funds does the offering continue to be brought, whether for that very afternoon or during the interim period more generally? The assumption of the *mishna* is that it does continue to be brought; the question is one of funding and not of obligation.

### Shimon vs. Yehuda

On this question, *Rebbi Shimon* and *Rebbi Yehuda* disagree. *Rebbi Shimon omer mishel tzibur* — it is brought from communal funds. His underlying logic seems to be that the *chavitai* is fundamentally a daily obligation assigned to the office of *kohen gadol*, and when there is no *kohen gadol*, the *tzibur* assumes responsibility for it. *Rebbi Yehuda omer mishel yorshim* — it is brought from the heirs of the deceased *kohen gadol*. According to this view, the *chavitai* is more properly understood as the *kohen gadol*'s own *korban*, funded from his personal wealth; since it ordinarily comes from his money, it remains, even in his death, the obligation of his estate rather than a communal sacrifice.

### Full Portion Agreement

Despite their disagreement on funding, both *Rebbi Shimon* and *Rebbi Yehuda* agree on one critical point: when the offering is being brought in the absence of a living *kohen gadol* — whether by the *tzibur* or by the heirs — it is not split into two half-portions but is brought as a full *issaron*. The *mishna* states explicitly: *ushleimah haysah kreiva*, it was brought whole.

## Rabbi Yehuda's Source

### General Continuation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁמֵּת וְלֹא מִינּוּ כֹּהֵן אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, מִנַּיִן שֶׁתְּהֵא מִנְחָתוֹ קְרֵיבָה מִשֶּׁל יוֹרְשִׁין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ תַּחְתָּיו מִבָּנָיו יַעֲשֶׂה אֹתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where the **High Priest died and they did not appoint another** High **Priest in his stead, from where** is it derived **that his** griddle-cake **meal offering should be sacrificed from** the property **of the heirs** of the High Priest? **The verse states** in reference to the griddle-cake offering: **"And the anointed priest that shall be in his stead from among his sons shall offer it"** (Leviticus 6:15).

The *Gemara* opens with a *Tannu Rabbanan* that frames the question in broader terms. The *kohen gadol* died and no replacement was immediately appointed: *minayin shetehai minchato kreiva?* — how do we know the offering continues to be brought at all? One might have assumed that since the *chavitai* belongs specifically to the *kohen gadol*, with no *kohen gadol* there is simply no obligation. The *baraita* insists otherwise, and proceeds to derive — consistent with *Rebbi Yehuda*'s position — both the fact of continuation and the identity of the payer.

### Mibnav Ya'aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יַקְרִיבֶנָּה חֲצָאִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֹתָהּ״ – כּוּלָּהּ וְלֹא חֶצְיָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that the heirs should **sacrifice it in halves** as the High Priest does. Therefore **the verse states "it,"** teaching that they should sacrifice **all of** the tenth of an ephah **and not half of it;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The source for *Rebbi Yehuda* is a creative reading of the *pasuk*: *v'ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav mi-banav ya'aseh otah* — the *kohen* who is anointed in his stead, of his sons, shall make it. At the plain level, *mi-banav* indicates that one looks first to the sons of the deceased *kohen gadol* when selecting a successor. The *baraita*, however, reads *mi-banav ya'aseh otah* as a legislative statement: when the deceased *kohen gadol* is no longer present to bring the offering himself, it is *mi-banav* — from his sons, his heirs — who are responsible for funding it.

### Otah = Complete

The same *pasuk* also supplies *Rebbi Yehuda*'s source for the *mishna*'s second ruling, that the offering is brought whole. *Yachol yakrivennah chatzyim* — one might have supposed that when brought by heirs, it is still divided into two half-portions, morning and afternoon. The *pasuk* says *ya'aseh otah*: he shall make *it* — *otah*, *kullah v'lo chetzyah*, the entire offering and not half of it. These are the words of *Rebbi Yehuda*: from *mi-banav* he derives that heirs are the payers, and from *otah* he derives that they bring a complete unit.

## Rabbi Shimon's Chok

### Chok Olam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״חׇק עוֹלָם״ – מִשֶּׁל עוֹלָם, ״כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא כּוּלָּהּ בְּהַקְטָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** The continuation of the verse: **"It is a statute forever [*olam*]** to the Lord," teaches that in this case of a High Priest who has died and has not yet been replaced, the offering is brought **from** the property **of the world [*olam*],** i.e., the community. The end of the verse: **"It shall be wholly made to smoke** to the Lord," teaches **that** although it is brought by the community and not by a priest, **the entire** tenth of an ephah **should be sacrificed** and not eaten.

*Rebbi Shimon* derives his position from a different part of the same *pasuk*. The conclusion of the verse reads *chok olam l'Hashem kalil toktar* — an eternal edict for Hashem, it shall be burnt completely. *Rebbi Shimon* reads *chok olam* as *mishel olam*: the offering belongs to the world at large, to the community. This phrase simultaneously teaches that the obligation is perpetual — even without a living *kohen gadol* — and that it falls upon communal funds.

### Kalil Toktar

*Rebbi Shimon* derives from *kalil toktar* — it shall be burnt completely — that when the *tzibur* brings the offering, it must be brought as a single complete unit. *Shetehai kullah behaktarah*: all of it goes up in fire. The *tzibur* does not divide it into two halves of an *issaron*, morning and afternoon, but presents it as one whole. In this way *Rebbi Shimon* extracts from the concluding phrase of the *pasuk* both the identity of the funder (*chok olam* — the community) and the requirement of wholeness (*kalil toktar* — brought complete).

## Hedyot vs. Gadol

### Challenge to Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did that** verse: **"And the anointed priest** that shall be in his stead from among his sons shall offer it," **come to** teach **this** *halakha* that Rabbi Yehuda derived from it?

The *Gemara* now presses *Rebbi Yehuda*: how can he use *mi-banav ya'aseh otah* to derive that the heirs are responsible for funding the *korban*? We need that *pasuk* for something else entirely — namely, for what is derived in a *baraita*. The *pasuk* reads: *zeh korban Aharon u-vanav asher yakrivu l'Hashem b'yom himashach oto asirit ha-eifah solet minchah tamid machatzitah ba-boker u-machatzitah ba-erev* — this is the offering of Aharon and his sons that they shall bring before Hashem on the day he is anointed, a tenth of an *eifah* of fine flour as a perpetual *mincha*, half in the morning and half in the afternoon. The verse then continues: *v'ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav mi-banav ya'aseh otah, chok olam l'Hashem kalil toktar, v'chol minchat kohen kalil tihyeh lo teiachel* — the *kohen* anointed in his stead, of his sons, shall make it; an eternal edict for Hashem, it shall be burnt completely; and every *mincha* of a *kohen* shall be complete, it shall not be eaten.

The question is whether *Aharon u-vanav* in the opening section refers to *Aharon* and all future *kohanim* generally, or more narrowly to *Aharon* and his successors as *kohen gadol*. One might reasonably read the opening and closing sections as a single unit all describing the *kohen gadol*'s offering, since the end of the *pasuk* speaks of the anointed successor.

### Section Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״זֶה קׇרְבַּן אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ בְּיוֹם הִמָּשַׁח אֹתוֹ״, יָכוֹל יְהוּ אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו מַקְרִיבִין קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳״, אַהֲרֹן בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וּבָנָיו בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן. ״בָּנָיו״ – אֵלּוּ כֹּהֲנִים הֶדְיוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **That** verse **is needed for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **"This is the offering of Aaron and of his sons, which they shall offer to the Lord in the day when he is anointed"** (Leviticus 6:13). One **might** have thought that since the verse speaks of the offering in the singular, it means that **Aaron and his sons should sacrifice one offering.** Therefore **the verse states: "Which they shall offer to the Lord,"** in plural, teaching that **Aaron** sacrifices an offering **by himself** as the High Priest, **and his sons** sacrifice offerings **by themselves** as ordinary priests. When the verse refers to **"his sons," these are the ordinary priests.** Each priest must bring a griddle-cake offering as an offering of initiation when he begins his service.

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between two sections of the *parsha*. The phrase *Aharon u-vanav* in the opening section is meant to teach something distinct from the phrase *mi-banav* in the closing section. Perhaps they are bringing the same *korban*, one could say — *yachlu yihiyu Aharon u-vanav makrivim korban echad*, maybe they together bring a single unified *korban*. No: *asher yakrivu l'Hashem* reads them in the plural, as distinct categories — *Aharon bifnei atzmo u-vanav bifnei atzmam*, Aharon in his own right and his sons in their own right, teaching that they represent different halachic categories.

### Hedyot's First Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֹּהֲנִים הֶדְיוֹטוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְהַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ תַּחְתָּיו מִבָּנָיו״ – הֲרֵי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״בָּנָיו״? אֵלּוּ כֹּהֲנִים הֶדְיוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Do you say** that this is referring to the offering of initiation of **ordinary priests, or is it** referring **only** to the griddle-cake offering of the **High Priests? When** the verse **states** in the continuation of that passage: **"And the anointed priest that shall be in his stead from among his sons** shall offer it; it is a statute forever to the Lord; it shall be wholly made to smoke to the Lord" (Leviticus 6:15), the griddle-cake offering of the **High Priest is thereby mentioned. How do I realize** the meaning of the term **"his sons"** in Leviticus 6:13? **These are the ordinary priests,** and the verse is referring to their offering of initiation. Consequently, verse 15 is referring to the basic obligation of the High Priest to bring the griddle-cake offering, rather than referring to a case of a High Priest who died.

The *Gemara* then raises the question: *banav eilu kohanim hedyotot* — does *banav* in the opening refer to ordinary *kohanim*, or perhaps *kohanim gedolim*? After all, the closing section explicitly describes the *kohen gadol* who succeeds Aharon; perhaps the *banav* at the beginning of the section refers only to those sons who themselves become *kohen gadol* after him. To this the *Gemara* answers: *keshehu omer v'ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav mi-banav* — since the closing section already explicitly speaks of the successor *kohen gadol*, *ha-mah ani mekayem banav eilu kohanim hedyotot* — the *banav* in the opening section must be referring to ordinary *kohanim*. The two sections are thus parsed as addressing two separate categories: the opening section (*zeh korban Aharon u-vanav*) covers a *kohen hedyot* bringing a *mincha* at the outset of his *avodah*; the closing section (*v'ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav mi-banav ya'aseh otah*) addresses the *kohen gadol*'s perpetual daily offering.

Once this division is established, the *halacha* of the *kohen hedyot* comes into focus. Before a *kohen hedyot* begins his Temple service for the first time, he brings a *mincha* — a full *issaron* of *solet* — as his inaugural offering. This is the meaning of *zeh korban Aharon u-vanav asher yakrivu l'Hashem b'yom himashach oto*: on the day they are initiated into their *avodah*, each *kohen* brings this offering. Note that even the *kohen gadol* himself, when he first assumes his role, brings such a *minchat solet*. The transition to *machatzitah ba-boker u-machatzitah ba-erev* — half in the morning and half in the afternoon — then shifts attention to the *kohen gadol*'s ongoing daily obligation, the *chavitai* that continues every day, split between morning and afternoon.

### Mibnav Reconciled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ תַּחְתָּיו בָּנָיו יַעֲשֶׂה״, מַאי ״מִבָּנָיו״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse teaches both the basic obligation of the High Priest to bring the griddle-cake offering daily and the fact that when he dies his heirs must bring the offering until a new High Priest is appointed. **If** it were **so** that the verse is teaching only that the heirs of a High Priest who died must bring the griddle-cake offering until a new High Priest is appointed, **let the verse** merely **write: The anointed priest that shall be in his stead, his sons shall offer. What** is the need to say: **"From among** his sons"? **Learn from** the fact that verse uses this term that **two** *halakhot* are derived from the verse.

This analysis, however, creates a problem for *Rebbi Yehuda*: if *mi-banav ya'aseh otah* is already needed to divide the opening and closing sections of the *parsha* — establishing that the opening *banav* speaks of *kohanim hedyotim* while the closing refers to the successor *kohen gadol* — how can *Rebbi Yehuda* simultaneously derive from *mi-banav ya'aseh otah* that the heirs fund the offering when the *kohen gadol* is deceased?

The *Gemara* resolves this: *im ken likhtov kra ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav mi-banav ya'aseh* — if the *pasuk* were only coming to tell us that the heirs bring the offering, it would have sufficed to write *v'ha-kohen ha-mashiach tachtav banav ya'aseh otah*, with *banav* alone. Why does it add the prefix *mem* and say *mi-banav*? The *mem* signals the *peshat* reading: the *kohen* who takes his place *from among* his sons — referring to the properly anointed successor. *Shma mina tarti* — we learn two things simultaneously. At the *peshat* level, *mi-banav* identifies the anointed heir who assumes the *kehunah gedolah* and thereby preserves the section division. At the *drash* level, *banav ya'aseh otah* teaches that when the *kohen gadol* is deceased, it is his sons — his heirs — who fund the bringing of the offering.

## Shimon's Otah

### Succession Otah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הַאי ״אֹתָהּ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁמֵּת וּמִינּוּ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ וְלֹא חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Shimon,** who derives the *halakha* that it should be entirely sacrificed from the phrase: "It shall be wholly made to smoke," **do** with **that** word **"it,"** from which Rabbi Yehuda derives this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **He requires** it **to** teach that in the case of **a High Priest who died** after bringing the first half of his griddle-cake offering, **and** then **they appointed another** High Priest **in his stead,** the replacement High Priest **should neither bring half of a tenth** of an ephah of flour **from his house nor** sacrifice the remaining **half of the tenth of an ephah of the first** High Priest, i.e., his predecessor.

The *Gemara* now turns to the other side and asks: *Rabbi Shimon, hai otah mai avid lei?* What does *Rebbi Shimon* do with the word *otah*? For *Rebbi Yehuda*, *otah* teaches that when the heirs bring the offering, they bring it as a complete unit rather than divided into halves. But *Rebbi Shimon* does not read *mi-banav ya'aseh otah* as referring to the heirs at all — he reads it as the standard succession scenario. So what does *otah* teach *Rebbi Shimon*?

### New Kohen Full Unit

*Rebbi Shimon* applies *ya'aseh otah* to the very case the *pasuk* describes at face value: the new *kohen gadol* who is appointed after his predecessor dies. The teaching is *she-lo yavi chetzi issaron mi-beito v'lo chetzi issaron shel rishon* — the incoming *kohen gadol* should not bring merely half an *issaron* from his own home, nor should he use the remaining half-portion of the *issaron* that belonged to the first *kohen gadol*. Rather, *ya'aseh otah* — he must begin with a full *issaron*, bring it whole, and divide it himself. This is precisely what was taught the previous day: even if he is appointed only in the afternoon, the new *kohen gadol* must start with a complete *issaron* and offer his half from that whole. He cannot pick up where his predecessor left off.

### No Vav Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִן ״וּמַחֲצִיתָהּ״? וָי״ו לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let him derive** this *halakha* **from** the expression: **"And half of it"** (Leviticus 6:13), as discussed on 50b; the word "and," which is added by the letter *vav* at the beginning of the word, is expounded to mean that the replacement High Priest must bring a complete tenth of an ephah of fine flour. The Gemara answers: He did not derive the *halakha* from there because **he does not expound** the extra letter ***vav*** in that word, as he holds that its addition is not significant.

One might ask: why does *Rebbi Shimon* need *otah* to teach this ruling? Yesterday's discussion derived the same point from the extra *vav* in *u-machatzitah* — the *vav* adding emphasis that even for a successor, the afternoon half must come from a whole. The *Gemara* answers simply: *vav lo darish* — *Rebbi Shimon* does not make *drashot* from an extra *vav*. Since he does not employ that device, he needs *otah* explicitly to convey the requirement that the new *kohen gadol* bring a full unit.

## Yehuda's Chok Olam

### Chok = Perpetual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הַאי ״חׇק עוֹלָם״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? חוּקָּה לְעוֹלָם תְּהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that if the High Priest dies and a new one has not yet been appointed the griddle-cake offering is brought by the previous High Priest's heirs, **do** with **that** phrase: **"It is a statute forever** to the Lord," from which Rabbi Shimon derives that it is brought from communal resources? The Gemara answers: It teaches that the **statute** requiring the High Priest to sacrifice the griddle-cake offering **is to** apply **forever.**

The *Gemara* now asks the parallel question about *Rebbi Yehuda*: *hai chok olam mai avid lei?* What does *Rebbi Yehuda* do with *chok olam*? He does not need it to derive communal funding — he derives that from *mi-banav* — so what purpose does *chok olam* serve for him? The answer is *chukah l'olam tehei*: at its most straightforward level, *chok olam* teaches that this offering is always in force, not merely a one-time inauguration for the generation of Aharon. This is simply *peshat*: the *chavitai* is a permanent, perpetual daily obligation.

### Kalil Gzerah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עֶלְיוֹנָה (מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל) בְּ״כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר״, וְתַחְתּוֹנָה (מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט) בְּ״לֹא תֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to Rabbi Yehuda, **why do I** need the phrase: **"It shall be wholly made to smoke"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **I have** derived **only** that the griddle-cake **meal offering of the High Priest** mentioned **above is** included **in** the mandate: **"It shall be wholly made to smoke"** (Leviticus 6:15), **and** that the voluntary **meal offering** of the **ordinary priest** mentioned **below is** included **in** the prohibition: **"It shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 6:16).

That still leaves *kalil toktar* — it shall be burnt completely. For *Rebbi Shimon*, this teaches that when the *tzibur* brings the offering, it must be presented as a whole unit. But *Rebbi Yehuda* already derives the requirement of wholeness from *otah*. What then does he do with *kalil toktar*? The *Gemara* answers: *mi-baei lei l'chid'tanya* — he needs it for what is taught in a *baraita*. The *baraita* states: *ein li ella elyonah minchat kohen gadol b'kalil toktar v'tachtonah minchat kohen hedyot b'lo teiachel* — I know only that the upper offering, the *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*, must be burnt completely (*kalil toktar*), and the lower offering, the *mincha* of the *kohen hedyot*, must not be eaten (*lo teiachel*). But how do we know that the *kohen hedyot*'s *mincha* must also be fully burnt? And how do we know that there is a *lav* against eating the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha*?

### Eating Issurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לִיתֵּן אֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה וְאֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כָּלִיל״ ״כָּלִיל״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״כָּלִיל״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״כָּלִיל״, מָה כָּאן בְּ״כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר״, אַף לְהַלָּן בְּ״כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר״. וּמָה לְהַלָּן לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ, אַף כָּאן לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that one is mandated **to apply what is said about that** verse **to this** one, **and** what is said **about this** verse **to that** one? **The verse states** with regard to the griddle-cake offering: **"Wholly,"** and the verse uses the word **"wholly"** with regard to the voluntary meal offering of a priest, in order **to** teach **a verbal analogy: It is stated here,** with regard to the griddle-cake meal offering of the High Priest: **"Wholly"** (Leviticus 6:15), **and it is stated there,** with regard to the voluntary meal offering of the ordinary priest: **"Wholly"** (Leviticus 6:16). **Just as here,** with regard to the griddle-cake meal offering, it is included **in** the mandate: **"It shall be wholly made to smoke," so too there,** the voluntary meal offering of the ordinary priest is included **in** the mandate: **It shall be wholly made to smoke. And just as there,** with regard to the voluntary meal offering of the ordinary priest, the verse comes **to place a prohibition on its consumption, so too here,** with regard to the griddle-cake meal offering of the High Priest, the verse comes **to place a prohibition on its consumption.**

The answer is a *gzerah shavah*: *kalil kalil ligzeirah shavah* — the word *kalil* appears in the verse concerning the *kohen gadol* and also in the verse concerning the *kohen hedyot*. *Ne'emar kan kalil v'ne'emar lehallan kalil*: just as *kalil toktar* by the *kohen gadol* mandates complete burning, so too *kalil tihyeh* by the *kohen hedyot* mandates complete burning. And just as *lo teiachel* by the *kohen hedyot* creates a *lav* against eating his *mincha*, so too a *lav* applies to eating the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha* — *af kan litein lo ta'aseh al achilato*. One might wonder why *kalil* cannot simply be taken at face value to mandate burning, without requiring a *gzerah shavah*. The answer is that since the surrounding verses already state *machatzitah ba-boker u-machatzitah ba-erev*, it is already evident that the entire offering goes onto the *mizbeach*; once that is clear, the additional *kalil toktar* must be carrying out another function — and that function is to establish the *gzerah shavah* that binds the two categories of *minchat kohen* together.

## Seven Takkanot

### Funding Source Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? וְהָתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים הִתְקִינוּ בֵּית דִּין, וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן. גּוֹי שֶׁשָּׁלַח עוֹלָתוֹ מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, שָׁלַח עִמָּהּ נְסָכִים – (קְרֵיבָה) [קְרֵיבִין] מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – קְרֵיבִין מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Shimon hold** that in a case where the High Priest died and a new one has not been appointed, the requirement that the griddle offering be brought **from** the property **of the community is by Torah law,** as indicated by the fact that he derives this *halakha* from a verse? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 7:6) that **Rabbi Shimon said: The court instituted seven ordinances** with regard to the financial aspects of offerings and consecrations; **and this** ordinance, namely, that the cost of the libations accompanying the sacrifice of a found animal is borne by the public, **is one of them.** These are the other ordinances: In the case of **a gentile who sent his burnt offering from a country overseas,** and **he sent with it** money for the purchase of the **libations** that must accompany it, the libations **are sacrificed at his** expense. **And if** the gentile did **not** cover the cost of the libations, it is a condition of the court that the libations are **sacrificed at the public's** expense, with funds taken from the Temple treasury.

Having established how each *tanna* derives his position, the *Gemara* raises a striking challenge against *Rebbi Shimon*. His position is that the *chavitai* during the interim period is brought *mishel tzibur* from communal funds. But is this really a *d'oraita* ruling? The *Gemara* quotes *Rebbi Shimon* himself in another *mishna*: *amar Rebbi Shimon shiv'ah devarim hitkinu beit din v'zeh echad mehem* — there were seven things that *beit din* enacted, and the communal funding of the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha* is one of them. If it is a rabbinic *takkanah*, how can the *Gemara* have been treating it as Torah-derived?

### Takkanot List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן גֵּר שֶׁמֵּת, וְהִנִּיחַ זְבָחִים – יֵשׁ לוֹ נְסָכִים קְרֵיבִין מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – קְרֵיבִין מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר. וּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא, כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁמֵּת וְלֹא מִינּוּ כֹּהֵן אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, שֶׁתְּהֵא מִנְחָתוֹ קְרֵיבָה מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** in the case of **a convert who died** without heirs **and left** animals that he had designated as **offerings, if he has the libations,** i.e., if he also had set aside libations or money for that purpose, the libations **are sacrificed from his** estate. **And if** he did **not** do so, the libations **are sacrificed from public** funds. **And** another ordinance: **It is a stipulation of the court** with regard to **a High Priest who died, and they did not** yet **appoint another** High **Priest in his stead, that his** griddle-cake **meal offering** would be **sacrificed from public** funds.

*Rebbi Shimon* presents a cluster of *takkanot* of a similar genre, all involving the use of communal funds to cover the *minchat nesachim* or the *mincha* itself in cases where the individual responsible cannot or does not provide them. These are not arbitrary extensions of communal obligation but responses to specific practical gaps that arose in the operation of Temple service.

### Found Animals

The first case mentioned just before our passage involves animals found near Yerushalayim within certain designated areas. Such animals are presumptively *shlamim*, and whoever discovers them is obligated to bring them to the *Beis HaMikdash* along with funding for the *minchat nesachim* that accompanies the offering. When people began declining to bother with this obligation — simply not returning the animals — the *takkanah* was instituted that the animal should be brought and the communal funds would cover the *nesachim*, removing the burden from the finder.

### Non-Jew Olah

A second case: a non-Jew who sends an *olah* offering from overseas. Non-Jews may send *korbanot* to be brought in the *Beis HaMikdash*. If he sent along sufficient funds for the *minchat nesachim* that accompanies the offering — *shalach imah nesachim* — those funds are used. *V'im lav kreivin mishel tzibur*: if he did not, the community provides. It is not feasible to send word back overseas demanding the additional funds; communal resources fill the gap.

### Ger's Sacrifices

A third case: a *ger* who dies leaving behind *korbanot* but no heirs. This can happen when a convert has no blood relatives remaining after conversion. If he left funds designated for *nesachim*, those are used; if not — *v'im lav kreivin mishel tzibur*. The *Gemara* adds: *v'tena'i beis din hu* — this is a condition of the court. When communal funds are collected annually, the court has standing discretion over their application, and covering *nesachim* in such cases is one of the purposes to which those funds may legitimately be directed.

### Kohen Gadol Death

The fourth case is our own: *Kohen Gadol shemeis v'lo minu Kohen acher tachtav* — the *kohen gadol* dies and no replacement is immediately appointed. *Shtei minchosav kreivos mishel tzibur* — both of his *minchos* are brought from communal funds. This parallels the previous cases structurally: a *mincha* must be brought, the individual responsible is absent, and communal funds fill the obligation. The language of *takkanah* here seems to imply a rabbinic enactment, which sits in tension with the *Gemara*'s earlier analysis treating communal funding as Torah-derived.

## Two-Stage Theory

### De'oraita Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: (שְׁנֵי) [שְׁתֵּי] תַּקָּנוֹת הֲווֹ. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מִדְּצִיבּוּר, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא מִידַּחְקָא לִישְׁכָּה – תַּקִּינוּ דְּלִגְבֵּי מִיּוֹרְשִׁים, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא פָשְׁעִי בַּהּ – אוֹקְמוּהָ אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said** in response: In fact, Rabbi Shimon holds that this *halakha* is by Torah law. But in fact, there **were two ordinances** that were enacted concerning this matter. Initially, they acted in accordance with that which is prescribed **by Torah law,** and if a High Priest died and a new High Priest had not yet been appointed in his stead, his griddle-cake meal offering would be sacrificed **from public** funds. **Once they saw that the** funds in the **chamber** of the Temple treasury were being **depleted,** the Sages **instituted** an ordinance **that** the payment for the offering **should be collected from the** previous High Priest's **heirs. Once they saw that** the heirs **were negligent in** the matter and did not bring the offering, **they** revoked the previous ordinance and **established it in accordance with** the *halakha* as it is **by Torah law,** that it is brought from public funds.

*Rabbi Abbahu* resolves the tension: *shtei takkanos havo* — there were not one but two stages to this arrangement. The starting point is *de'oraisa mishel tzibur*: the Torah itself establishes that in the absence of a *kohen gadol*, the *chavitai* is brought from communal funds. This is what the *Gemara* derived earlier from *chok olam* and the surrounding *psukim*. The rabbinic *takkanah* comes later, responding to a practical problem.

### Burden Relief

The difficulty was that during the period of the second *Beis HaMikdash*, *kohanim gedolim* were being replaced with troubling frequency — sometimes annually — which meant that extended periods without an appointed *kohen gadol* were recurrent. The communal fund was being drained: *keivan d'chazu d'ka midchaka lishka*, they saw that the *lishka*, the communal treasury, was being pressed hard.

### Heirs Pay

In response, the first *takkanah* was enacted: *tiknu d'ligavei miyorshin* — they established that the heirs of the deceased *kohen gadol* would bear the cost. The heirs would transfer funds to the *tzibur*, but the financial burden shifted away from communal resources onto the estate of the deceased. This is the ruling of *Rebbi Yehuda*: the heirs pay.

### Heirs Default

The arrangement did not hold. *Keivan d'chazu d'ka pash'ei bah* — they saw that the heirs were being negligent in fulfilling this obligation. *Ukma ade'oraisa* — they restored the original Torah rule. What *Rebbi Shimon* calls a *takkanah*, then, is in fact a *takkanah* to revert to the *de'oraita*: the second rabbinic enactment undid the first and reinstated communal funding as the Torah had originally mandated. The word *takkanah* in *Rebbi Shimon*'s list thus refers to this second-stage restoration, not a novel rabbinic creation.

## Red Heifer Parallel

### Ashes No Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל פָּרָה שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מוֹעֲלִין בְּאֶפְרָהּ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא! דְּתַנְיָא: ״חַטָּאת הִיא״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמּוֹעֲלִין בַּהּ – ״הִיא״, בָּהּ מוֹעֲלִין, בְּאֶפְרָהּ אֵין מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites the continuation of the mishna in *Shekalim* (7:7): **And** the court enacted an ordinance **with regard to the** red **heifer that one is not** liable to bring an offering **for misusing** consecrated property if he derives benefit **from its ashes.** The Gemara asks: Why does the *baraita* state that this is an ordinance of the court, when in fact **it is by Torah law? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a red heifer: **"It is a sin offering"** (Numbers 19:9), which **teaches** that a red heifer is treated like a sin offering in that **one** is liable for **misusing it.** The fact that it states: **"It** is a sin offering" indicates that if one derives benefit from **it,** the animal itself, he is liable for **misusing** consecrated property, but if one derives benefit **from its ashes, one is not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property. It is clear from the *baraita* that by Torah law one is not liable for misuse of consecrated property if he derives benefit from the ashes of a red heifer.

The *mishna* of *Rebbi Shimon*'s seven *takkanot* includes another item of this same structure: *v'al parah shelo yehei mo'alin be'afrah* — the ashes of the *parah adumah* are not subject to *me'ilah*. If someone uses those ashes for personal benefit, there is no transgression of *me'ilah*. This, says the *mishna*, is also among the seven things enacted by *beit din*.

### Chatas Derivation

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this framing: *de'oraisa hi* — this is a Torah law, not a rabbinic enactment. The proof is from a *baraita*: *chatas hi* — the *parah adumah* is called a *chatas*, and this teaches *shemo'alin bah*, that there is *me'ilah* while the *parah* is still intact as an offering. But the verse specifies: *bah mo'alin, be'afrah ein mo'alin* — the *me'ilah* applies to *it*, the *parah* itself; it does not apply to *its ashes*. The exemption of the ashes from *me'ilah* is thus a Torah ruling. How then does *Rebbi Shimon* describe it as a *takkanah*?

### Ashes Misused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁתֵּי תַּקָּנוֹת הֲוַאי, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – בָּהּ מוֹעֲלִין, בְּאֶפְרָהּ אֵין מוֹעֲלִין. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא מְזַלְזְלִי בַּהּ, וְקָא עָבְדִי מִינֵּיהּ לְמַכָּתָן – גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** in response: In fact, this *halakha* is by Torah law, but **there were two ordinances** that were enacted concerning this matter. **By Torah law,** if one derives benefit from **it,** the animal itself, he is liable for **misusing** consecrated property, but if he derives benefit **from its ashes** he is not liable for **misusing** consecrated property. **Once** the Sages **saw that** people **were treating** the ashes of the heifer **disrespectfully, and making** salves **for their wounds from it, they decreed** that it is subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse** of consecrated property and one may not derive benefit from it.

*Rav Ashi* applies the same two-stage analysis: *shtei takkanos havo* — there were two stages here as well. The Torah baseline is clear: *de'oraisa bah mo'alin be'afrah ein mo'alin* — by Torah law, one is liable for *me'ilah* on the *parah* itself but not on its ashes. The problem arose when *keivan d'chazu d'ka mezalzeli bah* — they saw that people were treating the ashes disrespectfully. Specifically, *vaka'avdi minah lemachtan* — they were using the ashes to apply to their wounds, as ashes were considered medicinally beneficial. This is a striking detail: the ashes of the *parah adumah* were extraordinarily precious and presumably guarded carefully, yet somehow the ruling that ashes carry no *me'ilah* led to their being treated as ordinary material.

### Rabbinic Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא פָּרְשִׁי מִסְּפֵק הַזָּאוֹת, אוֹקְמוּהָ אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Once they saw that** as a result of this decree people **were refraining from** sprinkling it in cases where there was **uncertainty** as to whether or not an individual was impure and required **sprinkling, they** revoked the decree and **established it in accordance with** the *halakha* as it is **by Torah law,** that one is not liable for misusing the ashes of a red heifer.

In response, *gazru bah me'ilah* — the Sages decreed rabbinic *me'ilah* on the ashes. Using them for personal benefit would now constitute a rabbinic transgression, even though Torah law exempts the ashes. This was the first *takkanah*.

### Purification Reluctance

The decree, however, created an unintended consequence. *Keivan d'chazu d'ka parshi misafek haza'os* — they saw that people were avoiding the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes even when it was genuinely needed for purification. The reasoning was: if I am uncertain whether I incurred *tumas meis* — corpse impurity — and I am not entirely sure I need the purification, then being sprinkled would mean benefiting from the ashes under doubtful circumstances, risking a *me'ilah* violation. This fear caused people to forgo the *taharah* process when they should have undergone it.

### Back to Torah

The second *takkanah* undid the first, restoring the original *de'oraita* position. As with the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha*, the *takkanah* listed by *Rebbi Shimon* refers to this second-stage restoration. Both cases follow an identical three-part pattern: the Torah establishes the base rule; a rabbinic *takkanah* modifies it in response to misuse; a second *takkanah* reverts to the Torah rule when the modification produces worse results than the original problem. The *mishna*'s designation of both as *takkanot* refers to the final enactment — the restoration — rather than an original rabbinic innovation.
