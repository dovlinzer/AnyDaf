# Shabbat 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatas vs. Asham

### Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אָשָׁם וַדַּאי, לָא בָּעֵי יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִילָּה,
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: According to the one who said** that in order to designate **a definite guilt-offering,** a sacrifice brought by one who committed a robbery, misused sacred objects, or had relations with a designated maidservant, one **does not require prior knowledge** that he definitely sinned,

Daf 72 opens by completing the discussion of how many *chataos* one must bring under various circumstances — a topic that occupied much of the preceding folio — before transitioning, on the second *amud*, to the far more practically resonant question of what constitutes an intentional versus an unintentional act on *Shabbos*. The two discussions are connected by the underlying question of *kavannah* and awareness, but they occupy very different conceptual territory, and the *sugya* marks the transition clearly.

### Three Asham Cases

To understand where the *Gemara* is heading, it is necessary to first survey the landscape of the *asham*. Unlike the *chatas*, which is a female sheep or goat brought for unintentional violations of a negative prohibition, the *asham* is a male ram and follows a distinct set of rules. The primary cases in which a *asham* is brought are three: *me'ilah* — misappropriating consecrated property; stealing and swearing falsely about it; and the case of *shifcha charufa* — a man who has relations with a woman who is half-slave and half-freed. This third case is notable for a striking feature: the *asham* is brought even if the act was committed *be-meizid*, intentionally. That anomaly will become central to the *Gemara*'s analysis.

These categories appear explicitly in the fifth chapter of *Zevachim*, which is recited as part of the *korbanot* section of the morning liturgy: *asham gezeilos* for theft and false oath, *asham me'ilos* for misappropriation of *hekdesh*, *asham shifcha charufa*, *asham nazir*, *asham metzora*, and *asham talui*. The first five are *asham vadai* — definite guilt offerings — while the *asham talui* stands in its own category.

### Asham Talui & Safek

The *asham talui* is brought when a person suspects he may have sinned but is not certain. The classic case is where two pieces of food were before him — one of forbidden fat (*chelev*) and one of permissible fat (*shuman*) — he ate one but does not know which. Because the forbidden object was concretely present and the doubt is genuine, there is a sufficient basis to bring the *asham talui*. This applies to any case of doubtful violation for which, had the sin been certain, a *chatas* would have been required. The technical term for this is *asham safek* — a guilt offering predicated on an uncertain sin.

### Akiva vs. Tarfon

The *Gemara* in *Kerisos* records a debate about *asham* in the context of *me'ilah*. If a person is uncertain whether he committed *me'ilah*, does he bring an *asham safek* even though the definite violation calls for an *asham vadai* rather than a *chatas*? Rabbi Akiva maintains that one does — the logic of *asham talui* extends to any case of genuine doubt, regardless of what offering the definite sin would have required.

Rabbi Tarfon, however, offers a more economical approach. Since this person, if he eventually discovers he did sin, will in any case be obligated in an *asham vadai*, why should he bring two separate offerings — both of them rams — the one on suspicion and the other on certainty? Instead, Rabbi Tarfon advises: bring one *asham* now, with the stipulation that if it turns out the sin was definite, this will serve as the *asham vadai*, and if the sin never becomes certain, it will serve as the *asham safek*.

### Asham without Knowledge

The *Gemara* draws a striking inference from Rabbi Tarfon's position. For this stipulation to be coherent — for an offering brought before certainty to retroactively qualify as an *asham vadai* — it must be that an *asham vadai* does not require prior *yedia*, prior awareness of the sin, as a precondition. By contrast, the *chatas* is inextricably bound to the moment of awareness: it is precisely the moment of becoming aware (*yedia*) that creates the obligation and defines the act as subject to a sin offering. But by the *asham*, at least according to one view, this is not the case. The sin itself — independent of whether the sinner has yet come to know that he sinned — generates the obligation, and one may bring the offering even before that awareness crystallizes into certainty.

This is the full weight of the phrase with which *Ulla* opens the discussion: *le-man de-amar asham vadai lo ba'i yedia be-techilah* — according to the view that a definite *asham* does not require prior knowledge, we must rethink what role awareness plays in dividing multiple acts of the same sin.

## Shifcha Charufa Acts

### Five Acts Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעַל חָמֵשׁ בְּעִילוֹת בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** one who **had relations with a designated maidservant five times is only liable to** bring **one** guilt-offering, even if he became aware of his transgression between each instance of relations with her. Because awareness is insignificant with regard to this sacrifice, it does not demarcate in terms of the number of guilt-offerings that he is liable to bring.

Having established that *yedia* may not be a constitutive requirement for the *asham*, *Ulla* turns to the case of *shifcha charufa* to draw out the implications. If a man committed five separate acts of intercourse with this half-freed slave woman, and became aware of his sin in between each act, does he bring five separate *ashamim* or only one?

The intuition borrowed from the laws of *chatas* would suggest that awareness divides. By *chatas*, the moment of *yedia* is what creates the obligation, and therefore *yedia* occurring between two acts generates two separate obligations. But if *yedia* is not the constitutive element of an *asham vadai*, then it lacks the power to divide.

### Ulah's Position

*Ulla*'s position is precisely this: since *yedia* is not required for an *asham vadai* — since the sin itself is what obligates, independent of awareness — then awareness occurring between acts has no dividing force. Even across five separate acts with intervening moments of *yedia*, only one *asham* is owed.

### Hamnuna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה בָּעַל וְחָזַר וּבָעַל וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן, וְאָמַר: הַמְתִּינוּ לִי עַד שֶׁאֶבְעוֹל, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַעֲשֶׂה דִּלְאַחַר הַפְרָשָׁה קָאָמְרַתְּ?! מַעֲשֶׂה דִּלְאַחַר הַפְרָשָׁה לָא קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna strongly objects to** this *halakha*: **But if** what you say is **so, one who had relations** with a designated maidservant, **and again had relations, and designated** an animal for the **offering, and said: Wait for me** before sacrificing the offering **until I have relations** again, so that the guilt-offering will atone for this transgression as well, would you say that the **same** applies in that case **too,** that **he is only liable to** bring **one** guilt-offering? Ulla **said to him: You referred** to **an action** performed **after designation** of an animal for the guilt-offering. **I did not say** that *halakha* with regard to **an action** that was performed **after designation.**

Rav Hamnuna challenges this reasoning. Consider the case where a man had relations with a *shifcha charufa*, then designated an animal as his *asham* — *hifrish korban* — and then explicitly said, "Hold off, don't bring it yet; I want to have relations again, and then it will count for both." Surely, Rav Hamnuna argues, the act performed after the designation of the *korban* would generate an independent obligation. If so, doesn't that demonstrate that awareness between acts is equally sufficient to divide them?

### Korban Designation

The response is sharp and clarifying. The two cases are not analogous. When a *korban* has been formally designated — set aside and sanctified — the next act is by definition performed after a concrete, legally significant event has intervened. That creates a genuine division. But mere awareness — *yedia* without *hafrasha* — does not constitute such an event. The principle of *hafrasha* divides; *yedia* alone does not. This is the *chiddush*: even in a case where one is acting intentionally, where one would have expected separate acts to generate separate obligations, the *asham* of *shifcha charufa* resists division unless an actual designation of a *korban* has taken place between acts.

The *Gemara* acknowledges that there is something structurally interesting here. When acts are performed *be-meizid* — willfully — there is already stronger reason to see each act as independent. And yet the *Torah*'s treatment of *shifcha charufa* is a *chiddush* that even multiple intentional acts generate only one *asham*. The division comes only from *hafrasha*, not from awareness.

## Rav Dimi Position

### Dimi's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אָשָׁם וַדַּאי בָּעֵי יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִלָּה, בָּעַל חָמֵשׁ בְּעִילוֹת בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** According **to the one who said** that in order to designate **a definite guilt-offering one requires prior knowledge** that he definitely sinned, one who **had relations five times with a designated maidservant is liable to** bring a guilt-offering **for each and every one.**

When Rav Dimi arrived from *Eretz Yisrael*, he presented the flip side of *Ulla*'s analysis. *Rashi* notes that they are not in direct dispute; rather, each articulates what follows from a different premise. *Ulla* explored the implications of the view that *asham vadai* does not require *yedia be-techilah*. Rav Dimi articulates the view that it does.

According to the position that *yedia* is indeed a prerequisite for bringing an *asham vadai* — making it structurally analogous to the *chatas* in this respect — then awareness between acts functions precisely as it does by *chatas*: it divides. Therefore, if a man had relations with a *shifcha charufa* five times with *yedia* intervening between each act, he would be obligated in five separate *ashamim*.

### Abaye Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי חַטָּאת, דְּבָעֵינַן יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִלָּה, וּפְלִיגִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Dimi: That is not so, as with regard to a **sin-offering for which we require prior knowledge, Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish disagree** whether or not awareness following an unwitting sin demarcates one transgression from another.

At this point Abaye enters with a comparison that is admittedly not exact but is analytically productive. He notes: by *chatas*, everyone agrees that *yedia* is essential. Yet even there, Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish dispute a related case. The scenario is this: a person ate two *kezaytim* of *chelev* in one state of forgetfulness — one act on Sunday, another on Sunday — and then became aware of the first on Monday and the second on Tuesday. Rabbi Yochanan holds that he brings two *chataos*. Reish Lakish holds he brings one.

What is Reish Lakish's logic? If *yedia* is the dividing element by *chatas*, how can separate acts of *yedia* fail to divide the obligations? This is the crux of Abaye's comparison. He is mapping the case of *yedia* occurring *between* the acts — as in the *shifcha charufa* scenario — onto the case of separate *yedia* occurring *after* the acts — as in the Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish dispute. The analogy is inexact, but *Rashi* and *Tosafot* both try to articulate why it is nonetheless instructive.

The key observation is that *shifcha charufa* is already a *chiddush* in that even multiple intentional acts yield one *asham*. Since the *Torah* has indicated that dividing these acts is harder than usual, if *yedia* between acts is sufficient to divide them here, that should certainly mean that separate *yedia* after acts is sufficient to divide by *chatas*. Put differently: awareness that can break even the harder-to-divide *shifcha charufa* scenario should a fortiori be able to break the more readily divided *chatas* scenario.

### Awareness Comparison

To make the analogy fully visible, it helps to lay out the cases spatially. By *chatas*: if you commit two violations in one state of forgetfulness and then become aware of them in one moment, it is one *korban*. If you commit one, become aware, and then commit another, it is two *korbanot* — that is undisputed. The dispute of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerns a middle case: two violations in one state of forgetfulness, followed by two separate moments of awareness — one on Monday, one on Tuesday. Rabbi Yochanan says two *korbanot*; Reish Lakish says one.

By *asham*: one act followed by *yedia* followed by another act — that is the *shifcha charufa* scenario under discussion. The *Gemara* treats this as analogous to the Rabbi Yochanan–Reish Lakish case, because if *yedia* between acts divides by *asham*, it should correspond to separate moments of *yedia* after the acts dividing by *chatas*.

### Reish Lakish View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא בְּמַעֲשֶׂה דִּלְאַחַר הַפְרָשָׁה קָאָמְרַתְּ, וְכִדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** In an attempt to resolve the problem, Abaye **said to him: Perhaps you said** your statement with regard to **an act** that the sinner seeks to commit **after designating** an animal as a guilt-offering but wants to attain atonement for by means of that same designated sacrifice. That statement is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rav Hamnuna.** Rav Dimi remembered and **said to** Abaye: **Yes,** that was what was originally stated.

After Abaye raises this analogy, *ishtik* — silence. The question lands without an immediate answer. Then the *Gemara* reconstructs what Reish Lakish's position must actually be. If Reish Lakish says one *korban* in the case of two separate post-act moments of *yedia* by *chatas*, then by the same logic, *yedia* between acts by *asham* would also not divide. For Reish Lakish, *yedia* is not in itself a dividing force — even when it is a constitutive requirement for the obligation. The only thing that divides, even according to Reish Lakish, is *hafrasha* — formal designation of a *korban*. This is consistent with what Rav Hamnuna demonstrated, and the *Gemara* invokes him explicitly: *u-che-Rav Hamnuna* — the division by *hafrasha* is the point of agreement; the division by *yedia* is the point of dispute.

## Ravin Summary

### All Agree Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה, וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה. הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה דְּאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת — כִּדְעוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he cited several disputes concerning the *halakhot* of guilt-offerings and **said: Everyone agrees with regard to a designated maidservant, and everyone agrees with regard to a designated maidservant, and there is a dispute with regard to a designated maidservant.** The Gemara elaborates: **Everyone agrees with regard to a designated maidservant; one is liable to** bring **only one** guilt-offering, even for many acts of cohabitation, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Ulla.**

When Ravin arrived from *Eretz Yisrael*, he offered a crisp, three-part summary of where the consensus lies and where the dispute falls. *Hakol modim* — both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree — that in the *shifcha charufa* case, if *asham vadai* does not require *yedia be-techilah*, then awareness between acts does not divide, and only one *asham* is owed. This follows *Ulla*'s position: if *yedia* is not constitutive of the obligation, it lacks the power to divide. Even Rabbi Yochanan, who says two *korbanot* by *chatas*, would concede that by *asham vadai* — if *yedia* is not required — a single *asham* suffices.

### Hafrasha Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה דְּחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת — כְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And everyone agrees with regard to a designated maidservant; one is liable for each and every** act of cohabitation, if it occurred after designation of an animal for a guilt-offering for the previous transgression, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna.**

*Hakol modim* as well that when *hafrasha* — formal designation of the *korban* — occurs between acts, a separate *asham* is required for the subsequent act. This is the case demonstrated by Rav Hamnuna: even Reish Lakish, who does not treat *yedia* as a dividing force, agrees that *hafrasha* divides.

### Knowledge Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה — לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אָשָׁם וַדַּאי בָּעֵי יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִלָּה, מַחֲלוֹקֶת דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And there is a dispute with regard to a designated maidservant;** this refers **to the one who said** that, in order to designate **a definite guilt-offering, one requires prior knowledge** that he definitely sinned. In that case, the legal status of the guilt-offering is equal to that of the sin-offering and is the subject of **a dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** who disagree whether awareness between two unwitting transgressions demarcates and requires two offerings.

The *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish plays out specifically in the case where *asham vadai* does require *yedia be-techilah* — according to the view that treats it as structurally parallel to *chatas*. There, the question is whether *yedia* occurring between acts divides them or not. Rabbi Yochanan holds that it does; Reish Lakish holds that it does not. This maps onto their dispute about separate *yedia* after acts in the *chatas* context — and with that summary, the entire discussion of multiple *chataos* and multiple *ashamim* reaches its conclusion.

## Intentionality Debate

### Mitasek Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַגְבִּיהַּ אֶת הַתָּלוּשׁ, וְחָתַךְ אֶת הַמְחוּבָּר — פָּטוּר. לַחְתּוֹךְ אֶת הַתָּלוּשׁ, וְחָתַךְ אֶת הַמְחוּבָּר, רָבָא אָמַר: פָּטוּר. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **One who intended to lift** a plant **detached** from the ground on Shabbat **and** mistakenly **severed** a plant still **attached** to the ground, which under other circumstances constitutes performance of the prohibited labor of reaping, **is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering for his mistaken act, since he did not intend to perform an act of cutting. One who performs an action unawares [*mitasek*], i.e., he had no intention to perform the act at all, incurs no liability whatsoever. One who intended **to cut** a **detached** plant **and** unwittingly **severed** a plant still **attached** to the ground, **Rava said:** He too **is exempt. Abaye said: He is liable.**

The *Gemara* now turns to what will prove to be among the most practically significant distinctions in the laws of *Shabbos*: the line between *shogeg* — unintentional transgression, which obligates a *korban* — and *mit'asek* — a completely incidental act that does not. The word *mit'asek* derives from the root *esek*, involvement or occupation; in its reflexive form, it connotes a person who was occupied with his own affair and the prohibited act simply happened as a byproduct, wholly unintended. The contrast with *shogeg* is precise: a *shogeg* knows what he is doing but is unaware that it is forbidden; a *mit'asek* did not even intend the action that constituted the prohibition.

### Lift vs. Cut

The first case illustrates *mit'asek* clearly: *nitkavein le-hagbi'a et ha-tallush ve-chatach et ha-mechubar* — he intended to lift an object that was detached from the ground (*tallush*), but as he did so, he inadvertently severed something that was still attached (*mechubar*). According to *Tosafot*'s reading of this case, he thought the object was already detached from the tree, reached to pick it up, and in doing so tore it from a delicate twig connecting it to the ground — an act of *kotzer*. Everyone agrees he is exempt. The act he performed — cutting something attached — was not his act at all. He was lifting; the cutting was entirely incidental.

### Cut Mistake

The second case is where the debate ignites. *Le-chtoch et ha-tallush ve-chatach et ha-mechubar* — he intended to cut something detached, took out his knife and cut, but the object he cut turned out to be attached to the ground. He performed exactly the category of act he intended — cutting — but he was mistaken about the status of the object he cut. This is the *machloket* of Rava and Abaye.

### Rava Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר פָּטוּר — דְּהָא לָא מִיכַּוֵּין לַחֲתִיכָה דְאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **he is exempt because** he **did not intend** to perform an act of **prohibited severing.** He intended to perform an action completely permitted on Shabbat. He had no misconception with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat. It was merely a mistaken act.

Rava says: *patur* — exempt. His reasoning is *deha lo nitkavein la-chatichah de-issura* — he was not intending to perform a prohibited cut. Now this cannot mean he was unaware of the prohibition, since that would be ordinary *shogeg*. What Rava means is more fundamental: for an act to be "his," it must be the act he envisioned in its essential character. A normal *shogeg* is someone who knows he is performing *kotzer* — severing something from the earth — but believes it is permitted, either because he thinks it is not *Shabbos* or because he thinks this particular act is allowed. That person owns the act. But here, the man did not conceive of himself as cutting something attached to the ground. The act he performed — *kotzer* — was not the act he intended in any meaningful sense, even though physically his knife moved and something was cut. Rava holds that a mistake about the facts of the situation is sufficient to render the act not truly his, placing it in the category of *mit'asek*, and exempting him entirely.

### Abaye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אֲמַר חַיָּיב — דְּהָא קָמִיכַּוֵּין לַחֲתִיכָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said** that **he is liable because he intended** to perform a **standard** act **of cutting.** Since he intended to perform that act, and he carried out his intent, the Torah characterizes it as unwitting and not as unawares.

Abaye disagrees: *chayav* — obligated. His reasoning is *deha ka michavein la-chatichah be-alma* — he was intending to cut, and he cut. He cannot disclaim the action. The mistake he made was not about what type of act he was performing — he knew he was cutting — but about the factual circumstances of what he was cutting. For Abaye, once a person intentionally performs the physical act that constitutes the *melacha*, a factual mistake about the specific object does not dissolve his ownership of the act. It remains his act; he is a *shogeg* and obligated in a *korban*.

## Chomer Shabbat

### Multiple Melachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: חוֹמֶר שַׁבָּת מִשְּׁאָר מִצְוֹת, וְחוֹמֶר שְׁאָר מִצְוֹת מִשַּׁבָּת. חוֹמֶר שַׁבָּת מִשְּׁאָר מִצְוֹת, שֶׁהַשַּׁבָּת עָשָׂה שְׁתַּיִם בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I** derive to **say this** opinion? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: There is **a stricture** with regard to the prohibitions of **Shabbat** that is greater **than** the stricture with regard to **other mitzvot, and a stricture** with regard to **other mitzvot** that is greater **than** the stricture with regard to **Shabbat.** The Gemara elaborates: **A stricture** with regard to **Shabbat** that is greater **than** the stricture with regard to **other mitzvot** is **that,** with regard to **Shabbat, one who performed two** transgressions on Shabbat, even if he did so **in** the course of **one lapse of awareness, is liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each and every one, which is not** the case **in other mitzvot.**

Rava proceeds to ground his position in a *braita* that draws a systematic comparison between *Shabbos* and other *mitzvot*. The *braita* states: *chomer Shabbat mish'ar mitzvot ve-chomer sh'ar mitzvot mi-Shabbat* — *Shabbos* is more stringent than other *mitzvot* in certain respects, and other *mitzvot* are more stringent than *Shabbos* in others.

The stringency of *Shabbos*: if a person violated two *melachot* in one state of forgetfulness, he brings two separate *korbanot*, one for each. This is the principle of *chiluk melachot* — the categories of prohibited labor on *Shabbos* are conceptually distinct, and each generates an independent obligation. By other *mitzvot*, violating two categories in one state of forgetfulness yields only one *korban*.

### Categories vs. Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דַּעֲבַד קְצִירָה וּטְחִינָה, דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי שְׁאָר מִצְוֹת אֲכַל חֵלֶב וְדָם. הָכָא תַּרְתֵּי מִיחַיַּיב, וְהָכָא תַּרְתֵּי מִיחַיַּיב! אֶלָּא שְׁאָר מִצְוֹת דְּלָא מִיחַיַּיב אֶלָּא חֲדָא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי — דַּאֲכַל חֵלֶב וְחֵלֶב. דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי שַׁבָּת — דַּעֲבַד קְצִירָה וּקְצִירָה, הָכָא חֲדָא מִיחַיַּיב, וְהָכָא חֲדָא מִיחַיַּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If you say that he** unwittingly **performed** acts of **reaping and grinding** on Shabbat, **the corresponding** situation with regard to **other mitzvot** is a case where **he ate forbidden fat and blood.** If so, there is no difference between Shabbat and other mitzvot. **Here** one **is liable to** bring **two** sin-offerings **and here** one **is liable to** bring **two** sin-offerings. **Rather, what are the circumstances** in **other mitzvot where he is liable to** bring **only one** sin-offering? It is in a case **where** he **ate forbidden fat and** again ate **forbidden fat** within one lapse of awareness. **The corresponding** situation with regard to **Shabbat** is a case where **one performed** an act of **reaping and** performed another act of **reaping** within one lapse of awareness. However, in that case too there is no difference between Shabbat and other mitzvot. **Here** one **is liable to** bring **one** sin-offering, **and here** one **is liable to** bring **one** sin-offering.

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is being compared? If the point is two different categories — say, *kotzer* and *tochen* on *Shabbos* — versus eating *chelev* and *dam* by other prohibitions, then both cases yield two offerings, and there is no contrast. And if the point is two acts of the same category — *kotzer* twice on *Shabbos* — that is also one *korban*, the same as eating *chelev* twice. The *Gemara* therefore clarifies that the comparison is between two distinct *melachot* on *Shabbos* — which yield two *korbanot* — and the analogous case among other *mitzvot*, where even multiple distinct forms of violation in one forgetfulness yield only one. The clearest example of the latter is *avodah zarah*.

### Avodah Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דַּעֲבַד קְצִירָה וּטְחִינָה, וּמַאי ״מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת״ — אַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְכִדְרַבִּי אַמֵּי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: זִיבַּח וְקִיטֵּר וְנִיסֵּךְ בְּהַעֲלָמָה אַחַת — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Actually,** it is referring to a case **where** one **performed** acts of **reaping and grinding. And what** is the meaning of the phrase: **Which is not** the case **with** regard to **other mitzvot?** It is not referring to all mitzvot in general. It is referring to the prohibition of **idolatry,** which is comprised of separate component prohibitions, each of which incurs independent liability. This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami, as Rabbi Ami said: One who sacrificed** to idolatry, **and burned** incense before it, **and poured** wine as a libation before it in the course of **one lapse of awareness is liable to** bring **only one** sin-offering.

The principle is cited in the name of Rav Ami: *zivach ve-kitar ve-nisach* — if a person slaughtered a sacrifice to an idol, burned incense to it, and poured a libation to it, all in one state of forgetfulness, he brings only one *korban*. These are three distinct forms of *avodah zarah*, yet they are treated as one violation. The contrast with *Shabbos* is striking. By *Shabbos*, the *Torah* has differentiated the categories of *melacha* to such a degree that each stands independently; by *avodah zarah*, notwithstanding the conceptual distinctions between different modes of worship, a single *korban* covers all.

## Other Mitzvot Stringent

### Fat Mistake Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוֹמֶר שְׁאָר מִצְוֹת מִשַּׁבָּת — שֶׁבִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת שָׁגַג בְּלֹא מִתְכַּוֵּין חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And a stricture** with regard to **other mitzvot** that is greater **than** the stricture with regard to Shabbat is **that,** with regard to **other mitzvot,** one who performs an act **unwittingly without intent is liable, which is not** the case **with** regard to **Shabbat.**

The *braita* continues with the flip side: *chomer she'ar mitzvot mi-Shabbat* — the respects in which other *mitzvot* are more stringent than *Shabbos*. The *braita* states: *be-sh'ar mitzvot shogeg be-lo mitkaven chayav* — in other *mitzvot*, a person who transgressed without full intention — *shogeg be-lo mitkaven* — is obligated in a sin offering. This is not the case by *Shabbos*, where such a person would be exempt.

The *Gemara* presses immediately: what is this case of *shogeg be-lo mitkaven* by other *mitzvot*? The most natural reading is: *de-savar de-shuman hu ve-achalo* — he thought the fat in front of him was permissible fat (*shuman*) and ate it, but it turned out to be forbidden fat (*chelev*). He was doing the same act he intended — eating — but was mistaken about the factual nature of what he was eating. By other *mitzvot*, he brings a *korban*. By *Shabbos*, the analogous case — intending to cut *tallush* and cutting *mechubar* — yields no obligation. And this, Rava argues, is the proof for his position.

### Avodah Zarah Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ — בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** case **did you establish** the *baraita*, **in** the case of **idolatry?**

The *Gemara* pauses to ask: what is the *shogeg be-lo mitkaven* case in the specific context of *avodah zarah*? The question is whether *avodah zarah* has its own internal version of this phenomenon.

### Synagogue Bow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא כְּסָבוּר בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת הוּא וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ — הֲרֵי לִבּוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **If you say** that it is referring to a case **where he thought** that **it was a synagogue and bowed to it,** and it turned out that he bowed to idolatry, he committed no transgression. Since **his heart was** directed **toward Heaven,** it is not even an unwitting transgression.

One candidate: *ke-savar beis ha-keneset hu ve-yishtachaveh lo* — he thought he was in a synagogue and bowed toward it, but he was actually in a temple of idol worship and bowed in front of an idol. This would seem to be a case of unintentional worship. But the *Gemara* rejects it as an *asham* case: *harei libo la-shamayim* — his heart was directed toward Heaven. This is not a case of mistaken *avodah zarah* at all; it is an act of prayer directed at God, which happened to take place in front of an idol.

### Statue Bow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא דְּחָזֵי אִנְדְּרָטָא וְסָגֵיד לֵהּ, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּקַבְּלֵהּ עֲלֵיהּ בֶּאֱלוֹהַּ — מֵזִיד הוּא. וְאִי דְּלָא קַבְּלֵהּ עֲלֵיהּ בֶּאֱלוֹהַּ — לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case **where he saw a statue [*andarta*]** in the image of the king **and bowed to it. What are the circumstances? If** the *baraita* is referring to a case **in which** he bowed because **he accepted** that image **upon himself as a god, he** worshipped idolatry **intentionally** and is not liable to bring a sin-offering. **And if he did not accept** that image **upon himself as a god** and bowed merely in deference to the monarch, **it is meaningless** and is not an act of idolatry.

A second candidate: *de-chaza andarta ve-sagid lah* — he saw a statue and bowed down before it. But again, the *Gemara* analyzes the act carefully. If he accepted the statue as a god — *kibblah alav ke-eloha* — then his act was *meizid*, fully intentional idolatry. If he did not accept it as a god — *lo kibblah alav ke-eloha* — then bowing before a statue as a sign of respect, without any acceptance of its divinity, is *lav klum* — it is nothing, an act of reverence rather than worship. Neither case is the relevant *shogeg be-lo mitkaven*.

### Ahava Yirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵאַהֲבָה וּמִיִּרְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case where one bowed **due to love** of someone who requested that he bow before the statue **and due to fear** of someone coercing him to do so.

The *Gemara* therefore identifies the case as: *ella be-ahavah u-ve-yirah* — he worshipped *avodah zarah* out of *ahavah* and *yirah*. According to *Rashi*'s standard explanation, this means he did so for material gain — *ahavas mamon* — or out of fear of harm, but without any genuine belief in the divinity of the idol. He fully intends the outward act of worship; he is not bowing accidentally or directing his heart elsewhere. Yet deep down he does not really believe in the god he is propitiating. He is performing the act knowingly but without genuine religious conviction. This is *shogeg be-lo mitkaven* as it applies to *avodah zarah*.

### Omer Mutar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּאוֹמֵר ״מוּתָּר״. מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּשַׁבָּת דְּפָטוּר — לִגְמָרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case **where one says** to himself that this **is permitted.** He is under the impression that idolatry is permitted, and his unwitting act was the result of ignorance, not forgetfulness. The statement in the *baraita*: **Which is not** the case **with** regard to **Shabbat,** is referring to one who was under the impression that performing labors on Shabbat is permitted. One **who** performs prohibited labors under those circumstances **is completely exempt.**

Rava, however, reads the case differently. He maintains that the operative case is simply *be-omer mutar* — the standard case of *shogeg*, where the person thought the act was permissible. He did not realize that worshipping this deity was forbidden. In Rava's reading, the *braita*'s *shogeg be-lo mitkaven* for other *mitzvot* refers to the plain, classic case of thinking the act was permitted — *omer mutar* — which is the most common form of *shogeg*.

### Analogy Shabbat

The comparison to *Shabbos* then comes into focus. By *avodah zarah*: you knew you were performing an act of worship but thought it was permissible — that is a *korban*. By *Shabbos*: you intended to cut something *tallush* and cut something *mechubar* — according to Rava, that is *patur*. The cases are analogous: both involve knowing the type of act one is performing, while being mistaken about the specific circumstances. Rava argues that the *braita* confirms his position by demonstrating that this class of factual-mistake case is treated differently by *Shabbos* than by other *mitzvot*.

## Abaye Counter

### Spit vs. Fat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, רֵישָׁא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְסֵיפָא בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת. וְשָׁגַג בְּלֹא מִתְכַּוֵּין בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? — דְּסָבוּר דְּשׁוּמָּן הוּא, וַאֲכָלוֹ. מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּשַׁבָּת דְּפָטוּר, דְּנִתְכַּוֵּון לַחְתּוֹךְ אֶת הַתָּלוּשׁ וְחָתַךְ אֶת הַמְחוּבָּר פָּטוּר. וְאַבָּיֵי שָׁגַג בְּלֹא מִתְכַּוֵּין הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? — דְּסָבוּר רוֹק הוּא, וּבְלָעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** is it **not** that the **first** clause of the *baraita* is dealing **with** the contrast between Shabbat and **idolatry, and the latter** clause of the *baraita* is dealing **with** contrasting Shabbat and **other mitzvot? And what are the circumstances** of: **Unwitting without intent, with** regard to **other mitzvot?** It is in a case **where** one **thought** that it **was** permitted **fat, and ate it,** and later discovered that it was forbidden fat. This is one example of other mitzvot where one is liable. **That is not** the case **with** regard to **Shabbat, where** he **is exempt, as** one who **intended to cut** a **detached** plant **and** unwittingly **severed** a plant still **attached** to the ground is **exempt. And** according to **Abaye**, who holds that he is liable in that case, **what are the circumstances** of: **Unwitting without intent,** with regard to other mitzvot? It is in a case **where** one had something in his mouth and **he thought it was spittle and swallowed it** with no intention to eat it, and it turned out to be forbidden fat that he swallowed.

Abaye responds by denying the analogy that grounds Rava's proof. He reidentifies the *shogeg be-lo mitkaven* case for *she'ar mitzvot* not as thinking one was cutting *tallush* when it was really *mechubar*, but rather as: *de-savar roku u-vala'o* — he thought he was swallowing his own saliva and swallowed it, but it turned out there was forbidden fat in his mouth. He swallowed it unknowingly.

In Abaye's reading, this is the case that is truly analogous to the first, uncontested case of *mit'asek* by *Shabbos* — intending to lift *tallush* and ending up cutting *mechubar*. In both cases, the person intended a qualitatively different act from the one that constituted the sin: swallowing spit versus eating forbidden fat; lifting versus cutting. Abaye concedes this case is exempt by *Shabbos* — and it is precisely what the *braita* identifies as the *chomer she'ar mitzvot* case for other *mitzvot*.

### Benefit Principle

The *Gemara* explains why swallowing forbidden fat unknowingly creates a liability even in *she'ar mitzvot*: because of the principle: *sheken neheneh* — because he derived benefit. By *chalavin va-arayot* — forbidden fats and forbidden sexual relations — the experience of benefit creates an inescapable bond between the person and the act. You cannot say "that was not my act" when the substance entered your body and you derived benefit from it. The physical reality of benefit-taking constitutes ownership of the act in a way that cannot be disclaimed.

### No Mitasek

This is why *mit'asek* does not apply to *chalavin va-arayot*. In the domain of *Shabbos*, a person can sometimes say: the act that occurred was not the act I intended, and therefore I do not own it. But where one has eaten or engaged in a forbidden sexual relationship, the reality of benefit-taking forecloses that defense. The act is yours, and at minimum you are a *shogeg* obligated in a *korban*.

### Rava Abaye

To summarize the positions: Rava holds that a mistake about the facts of the object — thinking one is cutting *tallush* when cutting *mechubar* — is equivalent to *mit'asek* by *Shabbos* and exempts entirely. By *chalavin va-arayot* this defense is unavailable due to the principle of benefit-derived ownership, but everywhere else it applies. Abaye holds that once a person intends the category of act — cutting, in this case — and performs it, a factual mistake about the specific object does not dissolve ownership of the act; that remains *shogeg*, obligating a *korban*. The *mit'asek* exemption is reserved for cases where the person intended a qualitatively different act altogether — as in lifting versus cutting — or, where benefit is derived, it does not apply at all.

## Martyrdom

### Technical Issue

The final passage of the *shiur* addresses a pointed question that arises from the *Gemara*'s treatment of *ahavah va-yirah* in *avodah zarah* — specifically from Rava's position. If a person who worships an idol out of love of gain or fear, without genuine belief in its divinity, is exempt from the technical *korban* obligation — and by extension, is not truly performing an act of *avodah zarah* — then what is the basis for the principle of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor*? If someone holds a weapon to a person's head and demands that he bow to an idol, and if such a coerced act motivated by fear (*yirah*) is technically not *avodah zarah*, why should one be obligated to give up one's life rather than comply?

### Tosafot Limit

*Tosafot* addresses this question directly. One approach offered by *Tosafot* is to limit Rava's ruling to cases where it is patently obvious to all observers that the worship is not sincere — where the absence of genuine religious conviction is transparent. This would preserve the technical exemption in clear-cut cases while not undermining the principle of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* in ambiguous ones.

### Love of God

A more fundamental resolution, and arguably more satisfying, is that the obligation of martyrdom is not derived from the technical prohibition of *avodah zarah* at all. The source of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* is the positive commandment of *ve-ahavta et Hashem Elokecha be-chol levavcha u-ve-chol nafshecha u-ve-chol me'odecha* — to love God with all one's soul, even at the cost of life. The imperative to die rather than transgress is grounded in the *mitzvah* of love of God and total devotion to Him, not in the technical definition of what constitutes an actionable violation of *avodah zarah*.

The practical implication of this distinction is significant. Even if the act being demanded is not technically *avodah zarah* in the strict halachic sense — for example, being compelled to convert to a monotheistic religion that does not constitute *avodah zarah* — the obligation of martyrdom may still apply. What is at stake is not a precise halachic category of prohibited worship but rather the question of whether performing the demanded act constitutes a repudiation of one's fidelity to God. Where it does, the principle of self-sacrifice derives from love of God and is not contingent on the technical classification of the act as *avodah zarah*.
