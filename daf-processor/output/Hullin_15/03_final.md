# Chullin 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechita Shabbat, Eating…

### Review: Lighting & Blood

The shiur on Daf 15 begins by picking up from the bottom of 14b. After the Mishnah had covered who may perform shechita, the Gemara turned to the question of *when* shechita may be performed. Two cases were addressed: nighttime — where shechita is valid only b'di'eved if lighting is insufficient — and Shabbat, where a violation of Shabbat does not invalidate the shechita.

Tosafot drew two striking observations from this ruling. First, the slaughterer is not classified as a *mumar l'chalal Shabbos* — a habitual Shabbat violator — whose shechita would be invalid, because that status requires habitual, not one-time, Shabbat desecration. Second, Tosafot raised the question: granted one might not cook the meat, but what of the blood absorbed within it? Tosafot's answer is that blood which remains absorbed within the meat is not yet legally considered "blood." One may therefore eat raw meat without first removing the blood through salting. The reason we salt meat is specifically because cooking causes the blood to be released and reabsorbed; for raw consumption or broiling, the salting requirement does not apply. These are two important principles derived from the Mishnah, though the Gemara's own focus was on Rav's statement — attributed to the opinion of Rebbi Yehuda — that one may not eat this meat on Shabbat itself.

### Muktzeh Machmas Mius

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּנֵרוֹת הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: מְטַלְטְלִין נֵר חָדָשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא יָשָׁן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav Sheshet, son of Rav Idi, said: When Rav said that the *halakha* that it is prohibited to consume the animal that day is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, the reference **is** to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to lamps, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may move,** for purposes other than lighting it, **a new** earthenware **lamp** that was never used. **But** one may **not** move **an old** lamp covered with residue of oil and soot, because a person sets it aside from use due to repugnance. Since it was set aside at the beginning of Shabbat, it is set aside for the entire Shabbat and it may not be moved even if a need to move it arises; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** The same *halakha* applies with regard to an animal slaughtered on Shabbat: Since it was prohibited when Shabbat began as the limb of a living being, it remains prohibited for the entire Shabbat.

The bulk of the previous daf was devoted to identifying which ruling of Rebbi Yehuda underlies Rav's statement. The Gemara now continues that investigation. Rav Sheshes, the son of Rav Idi, proposes that the relevant position is Rebbi Yehuda's ruling about lamps — *Rebbi Yehuda d'neros*. A Baraita teaches: *metaltelin ner chadash aval lo yashan divrei Rebbi Yehuda* — one may move a brand-new lamp on Shabbat but not an old one. The old lamp, blackened and oil-soaked after repeated use, is considered *ma'us* — disgusting — and falls into the category of *muktzeh machmas mius*, muktzeh on account of disgust. The proposal is that Rebbi Yehuda's sensitivity to this category would similarly apply to the animal: since it could not be used when Shabbat began, it would be muktzeh.

### Muktzeh Machmas Issur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמוּקְצֶה מֵחֲמַת מִיאוּס, מוּקְצֶה מֵחֲמַת אִיסּוּר מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? אִין, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that analogy. **Say that you heard Rabbi Yehuda** rule that it is prohibited for the entire Shabbat **in** a case where it is **set aside due to repugnance,** like the old lamp. **Did you hear** that **he** said that it is prohibited for the entire Shabbat in a case where it is **set aside due to a prohibition,** like the animal? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as we learned** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:**

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *Eima deshamat leih l'Rebbi Yehuda b'muktzeh machmas mius — muktzeh machmas issur mi shamat leih?* When did we ever hear that Rebbi Yehuda extends his stringency to *muktzeh machmas issur*? These are distinct categories. The Gemara then answers its own challenge by citing a Mishnah at the top of 15a: Rebbi Yehuda rules that all metal lamps may be moved on Shabbat. This implies that the restriction on certain lamps is not merely about disgust but about the lamp's status as something engaged in a prohibited act — *muktzeh machmas issur* — when Shabbat began.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל נֵרוֹת שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת מִטַּלְטְלִין, חוּץ מִן הַנֵּר שֶׁהִדְלִיקוּ בּוֹ בְּאוֹתָהּ שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **One may move all metal lamps** on Shabbat, even old ones, because they do not become repugnant like earthenware lamps, **except for** a metal **lamp that one kindled on that same Shabbat** and that was burning when Shabbat began, which it is prohibited to move for the entire Shabbat due to the prohibition against extinguishing.

The nature of this category, however, requires some unpacking. A cow on Shabbat is *muktzeh machmas issur* in a clear sense: there is no permitted use for it on Shabbat — one cannot slaughter it, one cannot plow with it. But a candle presents a different conceptual picture. A candle is not inherently prohibited; one may light a room with it. What makes it *muktzeh machmas issur* is either that it was actively performing a *melacha* — burning — when Shabbat began, or that the very act of moving it raises concerns: moving a burning candle risks extinguishing it or altering the rate of combustion, either of which is prohibited. These distinctions have practical relevance today for questions such as whether one may reposition a halogen lamp that is on during Shabbat.

The Gemara then adds a further complication. The proposal had been that the cow falls into this category of *muktzeh machmas issur*. The Gemara now pushes back: *v'dilma shani hasam d'hu dachai lei b'yadayim* — perhaps the lamp case is different because when one lights a candle before Shabbat, one actively pushes it away, making a conscious act of setting it beyond reach for Shabbat. A cow that was never actively designated as off-limits — it simply happens to be prohibited by virtue of Shabbat — may not carry the same status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּהוּא דָּחֵי לֵיהּ בְּיָדַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that analogy. **And perhaps** it **is different there,** in the case of the burning lamp, **as he set it aside by** direct **action** when he kindled the lamp. By contrast, in the case of an animal, he did not set it aside, and therefore, perhaps once it is slaughtered it is permitted.

Tosafot, however, notes that this is a *dehuya b'alma* — a forced deflection. If Rebbi Yehuda has a problem with *muktzeh machmas mius*, he most certainly has a problem with *muktzeh machmas issur*, and of course a cow is muktzeh on Shabbat. The Gemara's resistance to confirming this does not reflect genuine doubt about whether a cow is muktzeh, but rather a technical failure to prove it from the specific sources cited in connection with Rebbi Yehuda. The upshot is that the Gemara brackets the muktzeh explanation and turns to an entirely different basis for why the meat cannot be eaten on Shabbat itself.

### Candle vs. Cow Logic

The Gemara's resistance to the muktzeh argument — specifically the claim that the lamp case involves actively pushing the object away before Shabbat — leads to a reframing of the question. The discussion so far had operated on the assumption that the problem with eating the shechted meat on Shabbat is a muktzeh problem. The Gemara has now established that this cannot be proven decisively from Rebbi Yehuda's statements about lamps, and therefore a different explanation must be found. It is worth noting, as Tosafot makes clear, that this does not mean there is no muktzeh problem — there almost certainly is — but the Gemara is not satisfied to ground Rav's ruling there.

## Ma'aseh Shabbat Principle

### Rav Ashi: Cooking Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּמְבַשֵּׁל הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַמְבַשֵּׁל בְּשַׁבָּת, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav Ashi said: When Rav said that the *halakha* that consumption of the animal is prohibited for that day is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, the reference **is** to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to one who cooks, as we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who cooks on Shabbat,** if he did so **unwittingly, he may eat** what he cooked. If he acted **intentionally, he may not eat** what he cooked. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

Rav Ashi proposes an entirely different approach: *Rebbi Yehuda d'mevashel hi* — the relevant opinion of Rebbi Yehuda is not about muktzeh at all but about the category of *ma'aseh Shabbat*. The principle here is distinct from muktzeh: if a person performs a *melacha* on Shabbat and thereby changes the status of an object — rendering it fit for use or consumption — one may not derive benefit from that act of violation on Shabbat itself. According to most Tannaim this prohibition is rabbinic in origin, grounded in the concern that one not benefit from a Shabbat violation lest it encourage future violations. According to Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar, as we will see, it rises to a biblical level. The relevance to our case is direct: if one slaughtered an animal on Shabbat, the shechita — though technically valid — constitutes a Shabbat violation, and to eat the meat on that same Shabbat would be to derive benefit from that violation. Waiting until after Shabbat eliminates the benefit, since one could simply have slaughtered the animal after Shabbat in any case.

### Rebbi Yehuda: Cooking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל עוֹלָמִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he cooked the food **unwittingly, he may eat** it **at the conclusion of Shabbat,** as the Sages penalized even one who sinned unwittingly by prohibiting him from deriving immediate benefit from the dish that he cooked. If he cooked it **intentionally, he may never eat** from it.

The Gemara cites the relevant Mishnah: *Hamevashel b'Shabbos b'shogeg yochal b'meizid *lo yochal* divrei Rebbi Meir* — if one cooks on Shabbat unintentionally, one may eat it; if intentionally, one may not — this is Rebbi Meir's position. Rashi explains that Rebbi Meir is the most lenient: an unintentional violation carries no consequence whatsoever, even for the perpetrator on Shabbat itself. A willful violation means that nobody may eat from it — though only until after Shabbat.

Rebbi Yehuda then states: *b'shogeg yochal l'motzei Shabbos* — if unintentional, it may be eaten, but not on Shabbat itself; only after Shabbat. *B'meizid *lo yochal* olamis* — if willful, it may never be eaten; that is a permanent prohibition in the nature of a *knas*, a fine imposed on the perpetrator. This is precisely the position relevant to Rav's ruling: even an unintentional violation prohibits eating on Shabbat itself.

### Rebbi Yochanan Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת לַאֲחֵרִים וְלֹא לוֹ, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל עוֹלָמִית, לֹא לוֹ וְלֹא לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan HaSandlar says:** If he acted **unwittingly,** the food **may be eaten at the conclusion of Shabbat by others, but not by him.** If he cooked the food **intentionally, it may never be eaten, neither by him nor by others.** According to Rav, the mishna is referring to a case where one slaughtered the animal unwittingly. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the slaughter is valid but it is prohibited to eat the animal on Shabbat.

Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar goes further still. He rules: *b'shogeg yochal l'motzei Shabbos l'acherim v'lo lo* — if unintentional, others may eat after Shabbat, but the perpetrator himself may never eat it. *B'meizid *lo yochal* olamis lo lo v'lo l'acherim* — if willful, it may never be eaten — not by the perpetrator and not by anyone else. The stringency here is derived from the verse *kodesh hi lachem* — Shabbat is holy to you — and Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar understands this to mean that the products of a willful Shabbat violation take on a kind of prohibited sanctity, off-limits to all. A willful sin creates an object that is permanently forbidden to the entire world, not merely to the sinner.

One critical element is absent from the statements of both Rebbi Meir and Rebbi Yehuda: the distinction between the perpetrator and others. Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar explicitly introduces this distinction, but the earlier Tannaim do not state it directly. This omission generates a significant dispute among the Rishonim as to how to read their positions on this point.

Regarding the broader question of what constitutes "deriving benefit" in Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar's framework — the answer is that any personal gain from the violation is prohibited. Giving the food to a non-Jew with no expectation of reciprocity might be permitted, but selling it would not be.

### Tosafot Mnemonic

Tosafot offers a useful mnemonic for keeping these positions straight: each authority is precisely one step stricter than the one before. Rebbi Meir's case of *meizid* — willful violation, where no one may eat on Shabbat itself — is identical in its consequence to Rebbi Yehuda's case of *shogeg* — unintentional violation. And Rebbi Yehuda's case of *meizid* — where the perpetrator may never eat — is identical in its consequence to Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar's case of *shogeg* — unintentional violation, where the perpetrator himself may never eat. The entire structure shifts one category at a time, with each Tanna treating the more lenient case of the previous authority as the basis for his more stringent ruling.

### Mishnah via Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי דֻּמְיָא דְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – מָה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לָא שְׁנָא בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּמֵזִיד – לָא אָכֵיל, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּמֵזִיד – לָא אָכֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: That possibility should **not enter your mind, as** the case of slaughter on Shabbat is juxtaposed to and **taught** in a manner **similar to** the case of slaughter on **Yom Kippur. Just as** with regard to slaughter on **Yom Kippur,** it **is no different** whether one slaughtered it **unwittingly and** it **is no different** whether he slaughtered it **intentionally, he may not eat** it that day due to the fast, **so too here,** with regard to slaughter on Shabbat, it **is no different** whether he slaughtered it **unwittingly and** it **is no different** whether he slaughtered it **intentionally, he may not eat** it that day. Rabbi Meir, though, deems it permitted for one who cooked unwittingly to eat the cooked food on Shabbat.

The Gemara now explains what motivated Rav to say that the meat cannot be eaten on Shabbat itself. The Mishnah mentions both Shabbat and Yom HaKippurim together: *dumya d'Yom HaKippurim* — the case of Shabbat is analogous to Yom Kippur. On Yom Kippur, it does not matter whether the shechita was performed *b'shogeg* or *b'meizid* — one cannot eat the meat regardless, because eating itself is forbidden. By analogy, on Shabbat too, one cannot eat the meat on that day regardless of whether the violation was intentional or unintentional.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? וְהָא ״אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמִּתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ״ קָתָנֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי דִּבְמֵזִיד מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ הוּא, הָכָא דִּבְשׁוֹגֵג – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And can you interpret** the mishna as referring **to** a case of **unwitting** slaughter **and** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? But isn't it taught** in the mishna: **Although he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty? One is liable to be executed only if he intentionally performs labor on Shabbat. The Gemara answers that **this** is what the mishna **is saying: Although** if he slaughtered it **intentionally he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty, **here,** in a case **where** he slaughtered the animal **unwittingly, his slaughter is valid.**

Now, who holds that even *b'shogeg* one cannot eat the product of a Shabbat violation on Shabbat itself? That is Rebbi Yehuda. And who holds that *b'shogeg* is eventually permitted to everyone, consistent with the Mishnah's language that *shechitaso k'sheira* — the shechita is valid and the meat is ultimately fit for consumption? Also Rebbi Yehuda. According to Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar, the perpetrator could never eat from it even after Shabbat, which would conflict with the implication that *shechitaso k'sheira* means the shechita produces meat that is ultimately available to all. The Gemara is therefore satisfied: the Mishnah reflects the position of Rebbi Yehuda.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹקְמַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר, דְּאָמַר: לָא שְׁנָא בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּמֵזִיד – לָא אָכֵיל! רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר קָמְפַלֵּיג בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת, לַאֲחֵרִים וְלֹא לוֹ, תַּנָּא דִּידַן ״שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה״ קָתָנֵי, לָא שְׁנָא לוֹ וְלָא שְׁנָא לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let us interpret** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan HaSandlar, who says:** It **is no different** whether he cooked **unwittingly and** it **is no different** whether he cooked **intentionally; he may not eat it** on Shabbat. The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yoḥanan HaSandlar draws a distinction with regard to the conclusion of Shabbat,** in that he permits eating food cooked on Shabbat **for others and not for him,** while **the *tanna* of our** mishna **teaches: His slaughter is valid,** without qualification, indicating that with regard to his ruling it **is no different for him and** it **is no different for others.**

A further question arises: why not attribute the Mishnah to Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar, who also prohibits eating on Shabbat itself regardless of *shogeg* or *meizid*? The Gemara answers that Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar differs in his treatment of *motzei Shabbat*: according to him, the perpetrator who acted *b'shogeg* can never eat from the meat. But our Mishnah says *shechitaso k'sheira*, implying that the shechita is ultimately valid for everyone — including the one who performed it. That fits only Rebbi Yehuda, not Rebbi Yochanan HaSandlar.

## Rav's Authority Choice

### Shogeg Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: הַמְבַשֵּׁל בְּשַׁבָּת, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל, וּמְשַׁתֵּיק לֵיהּ רַב.
>
> **Translation:** § **The *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav: One who cooks on Shabbat unwittingly may eat** the food that he cooked; if he did so **intentionally, he may not eat** the food that he cooked, **and Rav silenced him.**

The Gemara reports: *tani Tanna kamei d'Rav* — a Tanna recited in front of Rav the following formulation: *hamevashel b'Shabbos b'shogeg yochal b'meizid lo yochal* — if one cooks on Shabbat unintentionally it may be eaten; if willfully it may not. This is Rebbi Meir's lenient position. Yet *u'mashtikei lei Rav* — Rav silenced him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא מְשַׁתֵּיק לֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְתַנָּא תָּנֵי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מִשּׁוּם דִּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מַאן דְּתָנֵי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר מְשַׁתֵּיק לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that **Rav silenced him? If we say** it is **because** Rav **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and the *tanna* taught** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** can it be that merely **because he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda he silences one who teaches** a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir?**

The Gemara considers an initial explanation: perhaps Rav silenced him because Rav holds like Rebbi Yehuda and did not want Rebbi Meir's more lenient ruling publicized. But the Gemara immediately rejects this: *man d'svira lei k'Rebbi Meir mashtikei lei?* — Just because you personally rule like Rebbi Yehuda, do you silence someone presenting Rebbi Meir's view? Moreover, the Gemara challenges the premise itself: do we even know that Rav rules like Rebbi Yehuda? In fact, from what Rav Chanan bar Ami reports, Rav rules like Rebbi Meir.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, מִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? וְהָאָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר אַמֵּי: כִּי מוֹרִי לְהוּ רַב לְתַלְמִידֵיהּ – מוֹרֵי לְהוּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְכִי דָּרֵישׁ בְּפִירְקָא – דָּרֵישׁ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִשּׁוּם עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, does** Rav **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? But doesn't Rav Ḥanan bar Ami say: When Rav issues a ruling to his students, he issues a ruling in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and when he teaches in** his **public lecture** delivered on the Festival, **he teaches in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, due to** his concern that **the ignoramuses** would treat the prohibition of labor on Shabbat with disdain?

### Rav Silences Tanna

The Gemara introduces a fascinating distinction: *ki morei lehu Rav l'talmidei morei lehu k'Rebbi Meir* — when Rav would instruct his private students, he would rule leniently like Rebbi Meir. *V'chi darash b'pirka darash k'Rebbi Yehuda* — but when he would deliver a public lecture, he would expound Rebbi Yehuda's stricter position. The reasoning was that his students were sufficiently scrupulous that they could be trusted with the leniency without sliding into carelessness; the general public could not be given the same ruling in a public forum without risk of misapplication.

This phenomenon has a very recognizable contemporary parallel. The popular halakhic handbooks in English typically present the strictest positions, with leniencies occasionally buried in Hebrew footnotes — on the apparent assumption that those who can read the Hebrew and navigate the scholarly apparatus are the ones who can be trusted with the nuance. There is something both familiar and uncomfortable about this structure. The *b'di'eved* that a posek communicates privately to one individual has a way, once publicized, of becoming a *l'chatchila* in practice. The concern is real. And yet the alternative — controlling the information entirely — raises its own questions.

### Rav's Public/Private

This phenomenon of differential rulings — one position for students, a stricter one for the public — raises genuine tension. On one hand, the Gemara itself invokes the language of *mishum amei ha'aretz* — on account of the unlearned — as the rationale for the stricter public ruling. On the other hand, whether this constitutes responsible leadership or a kind of paternalistic arrogance depends entirely on whether the assessment is accurate.

Those who find it troubling would argue that treating the public as incapable of handling nuanced rulings ultimately infantilizes them and may drive some people away from observance altogether when they encounter standards they cannot sustain. Those who defend the practice would point to the observable dynamic in which leniencies, when publicized, tend to expand far beyond the circumstances that justified them — a *b'di'eved* heard publicly is experienced as a *l'chatchila*. Rabbis regularly encounter this reality when a carefully qualified private ruling becomes their public reputation.

There is also a distinction within the Gemara's own narrative worth noting. The Gemara itself has no difficulty presenting Rebbi Meir's lenient position as legitimate data — as a Tannaitic opinion — even as Rav apparently resisted its public dissemination in the context of practical ruling. The category of *halacha v'ein morin kein* — a ruling that is technically correct but not to be announced publicly — is a distinct phenomenon, and operates even with respect to students in some cases. Here, the issue is different: it concerns what gets announced in the context of a public *drasha* versus what gets taught in the beit midrash.

The word *darash* itself is worth noting. A *drasha* in the amoraic period was not simply a halakhic ruling delivered from the pulpit. Based on early works like the *Sheiltot*, it appears that the standard format involved beginning with a halakhic or aggadic topic, weaving in a *drasha* on the weekly *parasha*, and then circling back to the practical ruling. The information emerging from such a public setting had a different character and reach than private instruction.

### Control of Info Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: תַּנָּא שׁוֹחֵט תְּנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּשַׁבָּת, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? עַד כָּאן לָא קָשָׁרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא בִּמְבַשֵּׁל, דְּרָאוּי לָכוֹס, אֲבָל שׁוֹחֵט דְּאֵין רָאוּי לָכוֹס – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: The *tanna* taught** the *halakha* of one who **slaughters before Rav: One who slaughters** an animal **on Shabbat unwittingly may eat** from the slaughtered animal; if he slaughtered it **intentionally, he may not eat** from the slaughtered animal. Rav **said to** the *tanna*: **What do you think,** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir? Rabbi Meir deems** eating **permitted only in** the case of **one who cooks** unwittingly on Shabbat, **as** even before he cooks the food **it is fit to be chewed [*lakhos*],** i.e., to be eaten uncooked, in a permitted manner, and therefore it was not set aside from use when Shabbat began. **But** in the case of one who **slaughters** an animal, **where** the meat was **not fit to chew,** Rabbi Meir does **not** permit eating it on Shabbat, because it was set aside from use on Shabbat.

The Gemara ultimately resolves why Rav silenced the Tanna. It was not because the Tanna was presenting Rebbi Meir's position — that would have been no reason at all. Rather, *tana shochet kamei* — the Tanna had changed the category: instead of teaching *hamevashel b'Shabbos*, he was teaching *hashochet b'Shabbos*. He had applied Rebbi Meir's ruling about cooking to a case of slaughter. Rav objected: that is incorrect. Rebbi Meir's leniency does not extend from *bishul* to *shechita*.

### Cooking vs. Slaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּשׁוֹחֵט הוּא, וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: דָּרֵשׁ חִיָּיא בַּר רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה לְיוֹמָא, וְנָסְבִין חַבְרַיָּא לְמֵימֵר: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר שָׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't the mishna** addressing the case **of one who slaughters** an animal, **and Rav Huna says** that **Ḥiyya bar Rav taught in the name of Rav: Consumption** of the animal is **prohibited for** that **day, and** the members of **the company** of Sages, i.e., those in the academy, **tended to say** that this *halakha* **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** from which it may be inferred: **But Rabbi Meir permits** consumption of the slaughtered animal even on Shabbat, and he is not concerned that the animal was set aside from use when Shabbat began?

The Gemara explains the distinction: *ad kan lo kashari Rebbi Meir ela b'mevashel d'ra'ui l'kos* — Rebbi Meir permits benefiting from the unintentional cooking precisely because the food being cooked was *ra'ui l'kos* — fit to be eaten raw, even if one would not normally do so. Vegetables or meat are at least theoretically chewable in their raw state. Therefore they are not *muktzeh* — they were fit for use when Shabbat began — and the only issue is *ma'aseh Shabbat*. Since the violation was *b'shogeg*, Rebbi Meir permits it entirely. But *aval shochet d'ein ra'ui l'kos* — in the case of slaughter, the living animal cannot be eaten; it is not fit for consumption in any form when Shabbat begins. Therefore, in addition to any question of *ma'aseh Shabbat*, there is an independent *muktzeh* problem. And that *muktzeh* problem — about which the Gemara has now come full circle — applies even where *ma'aseh Shabbat* does not.

## Sick Person Cases

### Meat Not Edible Raw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי שָׁרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ חוֹלֶה מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When Rabbi Meir permits** consumption of the slaughtered animal even on Shabbat, it is in a case **where one had** a critically **ill** person in his household **while it was still day,** before Shabbat, as it is permitted to slaughter the animal for such a person even on Shabbat. Therefore, the unslaughtered animal was not set aside from use.

The Gemara now asks: given that the Mishnah attributes a ruling to Rebbi Meir — specifically the implication that according to Rebbi Meir, one may eat the meat even on Shabbat itself — how is that possible if there is a muktzeh problem? The Gemara answers: *k'gon shehaya lo choleh mi'be'od yom* — for example, a case where someone in the household was critically ill when Shabbat began. Because there was a seriously ill person present from before Shabbat, the owner recognized that this animal might need to be slaughtered on Shabbat to provide fresh meat for the patient. The animal was therefore mentally designated for possible slaughter on Shabbat, and that designation removes the *muktzeh* status. Under those circumstances, the *muktzeh* problem is eliminated, and the only remaining question is *ma'aseh Shabbat* — deriving benefit from a Shabbat violation. According to Rebbi Meir, if the violation was *b'shogeg*, there is no problem even on Shabbat itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָסַר? כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ חוֹלֶה וְהִבְרִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, what is the reason that Rabbi Yehuda prohibited** consumption of the meat on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: He issued this ruling in a case **where one had** a critically **ill** person in his household before Shabbat **and** that person **recovered.** In that case, although the unslaughtered animal was not set aside from use when Shabbat began, it is prohibited to slaughter it on Shabbat. According to Rabbi Yehuda, if he slaughtered it unwittingly, its consumption is prohibited on Shabbat.

But if the animal was not muktzeh only because there was a *choleh* — a sick person — from before Shabbat, how does a prohibition of *ma'aseh Shabbat* then enter the picture? After all, slaughtering for a *choleh* whose life is at risk is permitted on Shabbat. The Gemara introduces the case of *shehaya lo choleh v'hipri* — the person was ill when Shabbat began, so the animal was not muktzeh, but then the person recovered before the slaughter was performed. With no longer any justification to slaughter on Shabbat, the person went ahead and did it anyway. In this case there is no muktzeh problem — that was set aside when Shabbat began — but there is a Shabbat violation, and the question of *ma'aseh Shabbat* is fully operative. It is precisely here that Rebbi Yehuda's rule becomes relevant: even if the violation was *b'shogeg*, one must wait until after Shabbat.

### Rebbi Meir: Sick Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר אַדָּא אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַדָּא אָמַר רַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט לַחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת – אָסוּר לַבָּרִיא, הַמְבַשֵּׁל לַחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת – מוּתָּר לַבָּרִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** that which Rav silenced the *tanna* for not stating that an unslaughtered animal is set aside from use, even when the prohibited labor of slaughter was performed unwittingly, is **in accordance with that which Rav Aḥa bar Adda says** that **Rav says, and some say** it is that which **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Adda says** that **Rav says:** In the case of **one who slaughters** an animal **to** feed **an ill** person **on Shabbat,** it is **prohibited for a healthy** person to partake of the slaughtered animal on Shabbat. In a case of **one who cooks** food **to** feed **an ill** person **on Shabbat,** it is **permitted for a healthy** person to partake of that food.

Rav Achah bar Ada, and some say Rebbi Yitzchak bar Ada, rule in the name of Rav: *hashochet l'choleh b'Shabbos assur l'bari* — if one slaughters for a sick person on Shabbat, a healthy person may not eat from the meat. The reason is muktzeh: since the sick person's condition arose during Shabbat — the animal was not designated for this purpose before Shabbat began — it remains muktzeh for anyone other than those for whom the shechita was permitted.

By contrast, *hamevashel l'choleh b'Shabbos mutar l'bari* — if one cooks for a sick person on Shabbat, a healthy person may eat from it. The distinction turns on *ra'ui l'kos*: food that is being cooked was at least theoretically edible in its raw state when Shabbat began and is therefore not muktzeh. The living animal, however, was not fit for any form of consumption when Shabbat began, and so it is muktzeh to all but those for whose benefit the shechita was halachically permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי רָאוּי לָכוֹס, וְהַאי אֵינוֹ רָאוּי לָכוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this distinction? **This** food **was fit to be chewed** before it was cooked, and therefore it was not set aside from use when Shabbat began, **and** the meat of **that** animal **was not fit to be chewed** before the animal was slaughtered, and therefore it was set aside from use when Shabbat began.

This distinction has a vivid practical application: if one is spending Shabbat with a patient in a hospital and the patient is brought hot food but is too ill to eat it, may the visitor eat from it? There is no concern of deriving benefit from a Shabbat violation, since the cooking was done for the patient's sake. The question turns entirely on whether the food was of a type that was edible before Shabbat began — if so, there is no muktzeh concern and the visitor may eat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַשּׁוֹחֵט מוּתָּר, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ חוֹלֶה מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם; מְבַשֵּׁל אָסוּר, כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּצַץ לוֹ דַּלַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** There are **times when one slaughters** for an ill person on Shabbat and it is **permitted** for a healthy person to eat the meat on Shabbat, such as **where he had** a critically **ill** person in his household before Shabbat and the animal was designated for slaughter **while it was still day,** before Shabbat; in that case, it was not set aside from use. And there are times when one **cooks** on Shabbat for an ill person and it is **prohibited** for a healthy person to eat the food on Shabbat, such as **where one cut a gourd** that was attached to the ground **for** the ill person on Shabbat. Because it is prohibited to detach the gourd on Shabbat, it is set aside from use and forbidden.

Rav Pappa adds a further observation: there are circumstances in which even a case of *shechita* permits a healthy person to eat, and circumstances in which even *bishul* forbids it. Shechita is permitted to a healthy person when the animal had been designated from before Shabbat for a person who was ill — as discussed above. Cooking is forbidden to a healthy person when, for example, a *delaat* was cut from the ground during Shabbat: either it was muktzeh for an independent reason, or — as Tosafot suggests — it was a variety so hard as to be entirely inedible without cooking, and therefore had the status of *eino ra'ui l'kos* when Shabbat began.

### Sick & Healthy Eat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא, הִלְכְתָּא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט לַחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת – מוּתָּר לַבָּרִיא בְּאוּמְצָא, מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִכְזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּלֹא שְׁחִיטָה, כִּי קָא שָׁחֵיט – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּחוֹלֶה קָא שָׁחֵיט. הַמְבַשֵּׁל לַחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת – אָסוּר לַבָּרִיא, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַרְבֶּה בִּשְׁבִילוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi of Neharde'a says** that **the *halakha*** is: In the case of **one who slaughters for an ill** person **on Shabbat,** it is **permitted for a healthy** person **to** eat the **raw meat [*be'umtza*]. What is the reason** that it is permitted? **Since it is impossible for an olive-bulk of meat** to be permitted **without slaughter** of the entire animal, **when he slaughters** the animal, **he slaughters** it **with the ill** person **in mind.** Since slaughter of the animal was permitted, all its meat is permitted even for a healthy person. In the case of **one who cooks for an ill** person **on Shabbat,** it is **prohibited for a healthy** person to eat the food on Shabbat. What is the reason that it is prohibited? It is due to a rabbinic **decree lest he increase** the amount of food that he is cooking **on behalf of** the healthy person.

Rav Dimi of Nehardea states the practical halacha: *hashochet l'choleh b'Shabbos mutar l'bari b'umtsa* — if one slaughters for a sick person on Shabbat, a healthy person may eat from it raw, provided that the sick person was ill when Shabbat began so that the animal was not muktzeh. One may not cook it, but one may eat it raw. The reason is formulated as follows: *keivan d'efshar l'kezayis basar belo shechita — ki shachit adaata d'cholah kashachit* — since it is impossible to obtain even an olive's worth of meat without slaughtering an entire animal, any additional meat that results from the shechita was incidentally produced in the course of acting for the patient's benefit. The healthy person consumes what was in effect an unavoidable byproduct; no additional *melacha* was performed on his behalf.

### Halacha Summary

The ruling on the cooking side is the inverse. If one cooks for a *choleh* on Shabbat, *assur l'bari* — a healthy person may not eat from it — even though there is no muktzeh problem and no personal violation of Shabbat. Why? *Gezeirah shema yarbe bishvilo* — a rabbinic safeguard against the risk that the cook, knowing someone else will benefit, will cook more than is strictly required for the patient. Perhaps consciously, perhaps unconsciously, the knowledge that the healthy person will eat from the food will lead to additional cooking. To eliminate that risk entirely, the Sages categorically forbade the healthy person from eating from what was cooked for the *choleh*. The ruling is structural, not personal: the cook did nothing wrong, and the food is not prohibited as a product of sin — but the potential for abuse is real enough to warrant the rabbinic fence.

The halacha as ruled by the **Shulchan Aruch** follows Rebbi Yehuda: even a *b'shogeg* Shabbat violation prohibits the violator and others from eating on Shabbat itself, and the food must wait until after Shabbat. The **Mishnah Berurah** notes, however, that *b'sha'at had'chak* — in a pressing situation — one may rely on the position of Rebbi Meir, since a significant group of Rishonim, including Tosafot, rule leniently. In practice, this becomes relevant in common situations such as when a guest in someone's home has a Shabbat violation committed on one's behalf, or when refusing to eat would cause offense or result in having no food for the Shabbat meal. The separate question of how to define *shogeg* — and how to treat someone who knows the orthodox prohibition but personally rejects its authority — remains a matter requiring independent analysis.

## Shechita Instruments

### Gezeirah Shema Yarbe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּמַגַּל יָד, בְּצוֹר, וּבְקָנֶה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters** an animal **with** the smooth side of **a hand sickle,** which has both a smooth and a serrated side, or **with** a sharpened **flint, or with a reed** that was cut lengthwise and sharpened, **his slaughter is valid.**

Having dealt with *who* may perform shechita and *when* it may be performed, the Mishnah turns to the instrument. *Hashochet b'magal yad b'tzur u've'kaneh — shechitaso kesheira* — one who slaughters with a hand sickle, a sharpened rock, or a sharpened reed produces a valid shechita. The latter two cases establish that shechita does not require a formal *kli* — a designated utensil — so long as the implement is sufficiently smooth and sharp. The hand sickle is more complicated: it has serrated edges on the interior. The ruling is that since the inner edge is serrated but the outer edge is smooth, and one uses only the smooth outer edge, no *gezeirah* is enacted and the shechita is valid.

### Acceptable Tools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, וּלְעוֹלָם שׁוֹחֲטִין, וּבַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, חוּץ מִמַּגַּל קָצִיר, וְהַמְּגֵירָה, וְהַשִּׁינַּיִם, וְהַצִּיפּוֹרֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵם חוֹנְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **All slaughter [*hakkol shoḥatin*], and one may always slaughter, and one may slaughter with any** item that cuts, **except for** the serrated side of **the harvest sickle, a saw, the teeth** of an animal when attached to its jawbone, **and a fingernail, because** they are serrated and **they** consequently **strangle** the animal and do not cut its windpipe and gullet as required.

The Mishnah then introduces a summary formulation: *HaKol shochtin, *u'le'olam shochtin*, u've'chol shochtin* — everyone may slaughter, one may always slaughter, and one may slaughter with anything. These three phrases encapsulate the entire discussion to this point: the first addresses who may perform shechita, the second addresses when, and the third addresses what instrument may be used. It is possible that these three words represent an early, compressed teaching that predates the elaborations recorded in the surrounding Mishnayot — a terse original formulation that the Mishnah then proceeded to unpack in detail.

The summary Mishnah immediately introduces exceptions. *Chutz mi'magal katzir ve'ha'magera ve'ha'shinayim ve'ha'tzipporen* — one may not use a harvest sickle, a saw, animal teeth removed from the animal, or a fingernail. Most of these share a common defect: they are serrated. The Mishnah provides the unifying explanation: *mipnei she'hen chonkin* — because they strangle. A serrated or nicked instrument does not cut cleanly; it tears. The entire requirement of a smooth, nick-free blade is grounded in this principle: shechita must be accomplished by the sharpness of the knife, not by tearing or pressure. Any implement that tears rather than cuts causes *chanikah* — strangulation — which invalidates the shechita. The fingernail differs slightly in the nature of its disqualification, but the result is the same.

One might ask: does a kohen not use his fingernail for *melikah* of a bird? Yes, but *melikah* is performed from the back of the neck, not through the throat, and it would constitute a *neveilah* in any other context — it is permitted only as an exception specific to *korbanot*.

### Three Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט – דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא. בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּמַגַּל יָד – דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְמֶעְבַּד בְּאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא, אֶלָּא צוֹר וְקָנֶה לְכִתְחִלָּה לָא? וּרְמִינְהִי: בַּכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, בֵּין בְּצוֹר, בֵּין בִּזְכוּכִית, בֵּין בִּקְרוּמִית שֶׁל קָנֶה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes that the language of the mishna, which states: **One who slaughters** an animal with a hand sickle, with a flint, or with a reed, rather than: One may slaughter, indicates that **after the fact, yes,** the slaughter is valid, but one may **not** slaughter with those blades ***ab initio*.** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** one may not slaughter it **with a hand sickle, lest he come to perform** the slaughter **with the other,** serrated, **side; but** as **a flint and a reed** have no serrated side, is it so that one may **not** slaughter with those ***ab initio*? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **One may slaughter with any** item that cuts, **whether with a flint, or with glass** shards, **or with the stalk of a reed.**

The Gemara notes that the Mishnah's language — *hashochet* — suggests these instruments are valid only *b'di'eved*, after the fact, but not *l'chatchila*, at the outset. The Gemara is puzzled by the hand sickle: *bishlama b'magal yad* — that one is understandable as a *b'di'eved* case, since we are concerned that using the smooth outer edge might lead one to mistakenly use the serrated inner side. But why would a rock or reed be valid only *b'di'eved*? There is no such slippery-slope concern.

The Gemara then raises a counter-source: a Tosefta states *b'chol shochtin* — one may slaughter with anything — and proceeds to enumerate rocks, glass, and the hard outer shell of a reed, all described as permissible even *l'chatchila*. If so, why does the Mishnah imply a *b'di'eved* limitation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּתָלוּשׁ, כָּאן בִּמְחוּבָּר. דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע – רַבִּי פּוֹסֵל וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא מַכְשִׁיר; עַד כָּאן לָא קָא מַכְשִׁיר רַבִּי חִיָּיא אֶלָּא בְּדִיעֲבַד, אֲבָל לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. There,** where the *baraita* permits slaughter *ab initio*, it is referring to slaughter with a flint and a reed **when** they are **detached. Here,** where the mishna says that the slaughter is valid only after the fact, it is referring to slaughter with a flint and a reed **when** they are **attached** to the ground, **as Rav Kahana says:** In the case of **one who slaughters with** a blade that is **attached to the ground, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** the slaughter **not valid and Rabbi Ḥiyya deems** it **valid.** The Gemara infers: Even **Rabbi Ḥiyya deems** the slaughter **valid only after the fact; but** one may **not** do so ***ab initio*.**

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *kan b'talush kan b'mechubar* — the distinction is whether the object is detached from the ground (*talush*) or still attached to it (*mechubar*). If the rock or reed is detached and held in the hand, it is valid even *l'chatchila*. If it is still attached — a boulder with a sharpened edge, or a reed growing from the ground — the status changes. The Mishnah's language of *b'di'eved* refers to the case of something still attached, where one must bring the animal's neck to the object rather than the object to the animal. The Tosefta's permissive formulation refers to detached objects.

### Forbidden Tools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ כְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא וְדִיעֲבַד? אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: בַּכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, בֵּין בְּתָלוּשׁ בֵּין בִּמְחוּבָּר, בֵּין שֶׁהַסַּכִּין לְמַעְלָה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַטָּה, בֵּין שֶׁהַסַּכִּין לְמַטָּה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְלָה – מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי וְלָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא! אִי רַבִּי חִיָּיא – דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכִתְחִלָּה לָא; אִי רַבִּי – דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **In accordance with which** opinion **did you interpret** the mishna? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya, and** the slaughter is valid **after the fact? But** if so, with regard to **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may slaughter with any** item that cuts, **whether with** a blade that is **detached** from the ground **or with** a blade that is **attached** to the ground, **whether the knife is above and the neck of the animal is below or the knife is below and the neck of the animal is above;** in accordance with **whose** opinion is it? It is in accordance **neither** with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya. If** one would claim that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya,** he says: **After the fact, yes,** the slaughter is valid, but it is **not** permitted to slaughter in this manner ***ab initio*. If** one would claim that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, he says: **Even after the fact,** the slaughter is **not** valid.

Rav Kahana reports a dispute: *hashochet b'mechubar l'karka — Rebbi possel, v'Rebbi Chiya machshir* — if one slaughters with something still attached to the ground, Rebbi invalidates the shechita even after the fact, while Rebbi Chiya validates it *b'di'eved*. Rav Kahana adds that Rebbi Chiya's validation is only *b'di'eved* — not *l'chatchila*.

A Baraita, however, teaches that one may slaughter with anything, *bein b'talush bein b'mechubar* — whether detached or attached — and it uses permissive language that appears to extend even to *l'chatchila*. The Baraita further specifies that shechita is valid whether the knife is above and the animal below (i.e., the animal is on the ground), or the knife is below and the animal is above (i.e., the animal stands and the knife is applied from beneath). The concern in the latter case — that the weight of the animal's neck pressing down on the knife might constitute *derasah* rather than clean cutting — is dismissed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי חִיָּיא, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, וְהַאי דְּקָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּדִיעֲבַד – לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya, and** he permits slaughter with these blades **even *ab initio*. And** the fact **that** the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Ḥiyya were formulated such that they **disagree** concerning the *halakha* **after the fact** is **to convey to you the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi that the slaughter is not valid even after the fact.

The Gemara is now troubled: this Baraita, which validates even attached objects *l'chatchila*, seems to match neither Rebbi nor Rebbi Chiya. Rebbi invalidates *mechubar* even *b'di'eved*; Rebbi Chiya validates it only *b'di'eved*, not *l'chatchila*. If neither authority accounts for the Baraita's leniency, *manni* — whose opinion is it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי ״הַשּׁוֹחֵט״, דִּיעֲבַד – אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה – לָא, מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי וְלָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא, אִי רַבִּי חִיָּיא – אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, אִי רַבִּי – דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But rather, the mishna** here, **which teaches:** With regard to **one who slaughters, after the fact, yes,** it is valid, but it is **not *ab initio*,** in accordance with **whose** opinion is it? It is in accordance **neither** with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya. If** one would claim that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya,** he says: The slaughter is permitted **even *ab initio*. If** one would claim that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, he says: **Even after the fact,** the slaughter is **not** valid.

The Gemara resolves this as follows: the Baraita does in fact follow Rebbi Chiya. *V'afillu l'chatchila* — Rebbi Chiya actually holds that even an attached object is valid *l'chatchila*. The reason Rav Kahana described Rebbi Chiya's position as only *b'di'eved* was to show how far Rebbi would go in the opposite direction — he invalidates even *b'di'eved*. But Rebbi Chiya's own position is permissive even at the outset.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי חִיָּיא, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, וּמַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי ״הַשּׁוֹחֵט״ – רַבִּי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, Rabbi Ḥiyya** holds that it is permitted to slaughter with these blades, **and even *ab initio*; and the mishna** here, **which teaches: One who slaughters, is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

This still leaves the Mishnah unexplained, since the Mishnah's *b'di'eved* language fits neither Rebbi (who invalidates even *b'di'eved*) nor Rebbi Chiya (who permits even *l'chatchila*). The Gemara therefore returns to the same answer: *l'olam Rebbi Chiya* — it is indeed Rebbi Chiya — *v'matnisin d'ktani hashochet Rebbi* — and the Mishnah that implies *b'di'eved* reflects Rebbi's view. But this creates an internal contradiction in Rebbi's position: does Rebbi invalidate *mechubar* even *b'di'eved*, or does he validate it *b'di'eved*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי אַדְּרַבִּי? לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּמְחוּבָּר מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, כָּאן בְּתָלוּשׁ וּלְבַסּוֹף חִיבְּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction **between** one statement of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and** another statement **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, as in his dispute with Rabbi Ḥiyya he holds that the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara answers: This contradiction is **not difficult. There,** in his dispute with Rabbi Ḥiyya, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that slaughter is not valid in a case **where** the blade was **attached from the outset; here,** in the mishna, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi deems the slaughter valid after the fact in a case **where** the blade was **detached and ultimately he reattached it.**

*No kashya* — it is not difficult. The resolution turns on a distinction of great practical significance in many areas of halacha: *kan b'mechubar meikara — kan b'talush u'lisof chibro* — there are two distinct categories of "attached." Something that was always attached to the ground from the beginning — a natural rock, a growing plant, a tree — has the full status of *mechubar*. Something that began as a detached object and was later attached — a knife inserted into a wall and plastered over, a beam built into a structure — retains a different status despite being physically connected. When Rebbi invalidates the shechita even *b'di'eved*, he refers to an object that was always attached from the outset, *mechubar meikara*. When Rebbi permits *b'di'eved*, he refers to something that was once detached and only subsequently attached, *talush u'lisof chibro*. Rebbi Chiya, by contrast, permits even *mechubar meikara* at least *b'di'eved*, and according to the Gemara's final reading, even *l'chatchila*. This three-way structure — Rebbi's complete invalidation of original attachment, Rebbi's post-facto validation of secondary attachment, and Rebbi Chiya's across-the-board permission — will be developed further in the discussion that continues on the following daf.
