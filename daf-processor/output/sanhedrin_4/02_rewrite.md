# Sanhedrin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi vs. Rabbis

### Rabbi's Five-Judge View

The *gemara* opens with a *beraisa* — apparently a *tosefta* — that states explicitly what the *Yerushalmi* had implied: *dinei mamonot bishlosha*, monetary cases require three judges, while *Rebbi omer bachamisha*, Rabbi maintains they require five. Rabbi's reasoning is that five judges are needed *kdei sheyigmor din bishlosha* — so that the final judgment (*gmar din*) is rendered by three.

One might object immediately: if you already have a *beis din* of three, surely the *gmar din* is rendered by all three — or at worst by two of them. Why would you need five for that purpose? The *gemara* clarifies: Rabbi's point is not that a *beis din* of three is incapable of reaching a verdict, but that the *pasuk* itself demands that three judges stand on the side finding for guilt or innocence. The *pesukím* referencing *Elohim* — the judges — require that the *gmar din* rest with a full three, not merely two out of three.

Rabbi's derivation proceeds as follows. The *pasuk* reads *asher yarshiun Elohim* — "whom the judges shall condemn" — where *yarshiun* is in the plural, indicating two judges find guilty. Separately, the *pasuk* opens with *ad ha'Elohim yavo devar shneihem* — "the matter of both of them shall come before the judges" — using *Elohim* for the *beis din* they come to. Since *yarshiun* implies two judges on the condemning side, Rabbi reads the *Elohim* above — the body they appear before — as also comprising two. But *ein beis din shakul*: a court cannot be evenly divided, so a court of two becomes a court of three. Now, since the lower *Elohim* (those finding guilt) corresponds to two, and the upper *Elohim* (the whole *beis din*) also corresponds to two, with one added for *ein beis din shakul*, the total is five: *mah lemata shnayim, af lemala shnayim*, two below and two above, and one added — *harei kan chamisha*.

### Rabbis' Three-Judge View

The Rabbis respond: *yarshi'an kesiv* — the word *yarshi'un* is in fact written without the *vav*. Because it is written in the defective spelling, you cannot derive from it that two judges are finding for guilt; you can only read it as referring to a single judge. With only one judge on the condemning side, the structure is one plus one plus one — three judges total, not five. The Rabbis thus hold the standard position: *dinei mamonot bishlosha*, three.

### Rabbi Abba's Challenge

Rabbi Abba — the same Rabbi Abba who raises this challenge in the *Yerushalmi* as well — objects forcefully to Rabbi's entire logic. If the principle is that the deciding side must comprise the full constitutionally required number, the consequences are absurd: the *sanhedrin gedola* of seventy-one would need a hundred and forty-one — enough that seventy-one could constitute one deciding side; the *sanhedrin ketana* of twenty-three would need forty-five — so that twenty-three could form the majority. No one accepts these conclusions.

### Resolved via Fixed Numbe…

The resolution is that these fixed numbers refer to the composition of the *beis din* as a whole, not to the numerical size of the deciding majority. The *Rachmana* said *esfi li shivim ish* — gather me seventy men — and the convocation is seventy, full stop. *V'shaftu ha'eida v'hitzilu ha'eida*: when the Torah speaks of "the congregation" judging or saving, it means the entire body — the ten and the ten and the twenty-three — not the winning side. Similarly, *v'nikrav ba'al habayit el ha'Elohim* — the litigant comes before the judges — means the body they come to is a body of three, *mishas kriva shlosha*. The full number describes the institution, not the voting bloc.

With this established, Rabbi's actual derivation becomes clearer. It is not the naive claim that three must sit on the deciding side. Rather, *asher yarshiun Elohim* uses *yarshiun* in the plural, indicating two. *Nemar Elohim lemata v'nemar Elohim lemala* — since *Elohim* appears both above and below, and the lower reference implies two, the upper reference — the body they come before — must also be two. But two is an even court, which is forbidden, so it becomes three. That gives us two (below) plus two (above, now raised to three), plus one for *ein beis din shakul*, yielding five. The Rabbis reply that since *yarshi'an* is written defectively, without the *vav*, the plural cannot be derived, and the whole structure collapses to three.

## Yesh Eim Lamikra

### All Agree: Reading Prior…

Having resolved the debate between Rabbi and the Rabbis, the *gemara* pivots entirely — occupying the remainder of the *daf* — to a question that the foregoing dispute has placed front and center: does one derive *halachic* conclusions from the way a *pasuk* is read aloud, or from the way it is written in the Torah?

The *gemara* opens with a sweeping statement attributed to Rabbi Yochanan: *Rebbi ve-Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz, u-veit Shammai ve-Rabbi Shimon ve-Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi, Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz, Beit Shammai, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Akiva — *kulu sevira lehu yesh eim lamikra*. All of these *Tannaim* hold that hermeneutic interpretation follows the reading (*mikra*), not the scribal tradition of spelling (*mesorah* / *mesoret*).

A brief terminological note: the word *mikra* means "reading," referring to our tradition of how the text is vocalized and pronounced. *Mesoret* means the scribal tradition of how the text is spelled — and counterintuitively, it is the *mesoret*, the "tradition," that refers to the written form, while *mikra*, "reading," refers to the oral pronunciation. This inversion of expectations is worth keeping in mind throughout.

### Yehuda ben Roeitz Story

The *beraisa* about Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz is a remarkably rich episode. His students posed to him what seemed like a genuine textual question: when the Torah says *ve'im nekeiva teled vetame'a shevu'ayim kenidasah* — that a woman who gives birth to a girl is *teme'ah* for *shevu'ayim* — the word is written without a *vav*, making it technically legible as *shivim*, seventy. Could it be that the mother of a girl is *teme'ah* for seventy days?

Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz answered them with an elegant *pshat* argument. The Torah establishes an exact parallel structure: by a male child there is a period of *tumah* (seven days) followed by a period of *dam tahor* (thirty-three days); by a female child those periods are precisely doubled — fourteen days of *tumah* and sixty-six days of *dam tahor*. The parallelism is unambiguous: *tima vetiher bezachar vetima vetiher banekeivah — mah shetihar bezachar banekeivah kiflayim*, just as the period of purity is doubled for a girl, so too the period of *tumah* is doubled. Two weeks, not seventy days.

The students left satisfied. But Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz ran after them: *iy atem zekukim lekach* — you don't even need that proof. The reason is simply *shevuayim karinan ve-yesh eim lemikra*: we read the word *shevuayim*, two weeks, and the principle of *yesh eim lamikra* determines the *halacha*. It does not matter that the defective spelling could theoretically support an alternative reading. The tradition of pronunciation takes precedence.

### The Principle Explained

The pedagogical structure of this episode is itself instructive. Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz taught the logical proof first and only then articulated the formal principle. This sequence carries a more powerful message than the reverse would have. Had he led with *yesh eim lamikra* and then offered the textual proof as corroboration, the listener might conclude that the principle of *yesh eim lamikra* requires independent textual backup. By demonstrating the elegant proof and then setting it aside, he conveys the opposite: even where I *can* prove it to you from the internal structure of the *pesukim*, that is ultimately beside the point. The tradition of reading is the anchor, and no alternative derivation is needed — or should be sought.

This connects to a foundational principle of *Torah she-baal peh*. The Talmudic enterprise of interpreting *Torah she-biktav* does not begin from the bare written letters in isolation, as if the reader had no prior knowledge of how those letters are meant to be read. The starting point is always the received tradition of vocalization and reading. Only from there does interpretation proceed.

## Beit Shammai vs. Hil (II)

### Shammai's Blood Analysis

The *mishnah* in question teaches: *kol hanitnin al hamizbe'ach hachitzon shenatan matanah achat kipper* — all blood applications that belong on the outer altar, if performed with only a single application, have achieved atonement *bedi'eved*. This applies to the *olah*, *shlamim*, *asham*, and the like. For the *chatat*, whose blood is applied to the *karnot hamizbeach* — the four corners (horns) of the altar — Beit Shammai rules that two applications suffice *bedi'eved*, while Beit Hillel rules that even a single application achieves atonement.

Rav Huna explains Beit Shammai's derivation. In the *parshiyot* toward the end of *Parshat Vayikra*, dealing with various *chataot*, the word *karnot* (corners) appears three times. The word *karnot* is plural, indicating a minimum of two each time. Three times two is six. But there are only four corners. Therefore: four is the prescribed *mitzvah*, and two is the minimum required (*me'akev*). Beit Shammai's derivation follows the reading (*yesh eim lamikra*): the word is read *karnot* in the plural, and from that plural they derive the number two.

### Hillel's Written Form

Beit Hillel, by contrast, notes that two of the three occurrences of *karnot* are written without the *vav* — *karnat* in defective spelling. Going by the written form (*yesh eim lamesoret*), two of the three instances mean only one, and one instance means two: one plus one plus two equals four. Of those four, three are the prescribed *mitzvah* and one is the minimum *me'akev*. Thus for Beit Hillel, only one application is minimally required *bedi'eved*.

The *gemara* raises an objection: if all four are the *mitzvah* component and the *me'akev* is zero, one might say no application is required *bedi'eved*. But *kapara belo netinah lo ashkechan* — we have no precedent for atonement being achieved without any blood application at all. Hence at least one is taken as *me'akev*, and three are the *mitzvah* portion.

## Sukkah Walls Debate

### Standard Ruling

The standard ruling, as taught in the *beraisa*, is *shtayim kehilchatan ve-shlishit afilu tefach*: a *sukkah* requires two full walls and a third wall of even a minimal *tefach*. This is the position of the *chachamim*.

### Rabbi Shimon's Position

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *shalosh kehilchatan ve-revi'it afilu tefach* — three full walls are required, with the fourth wall needing only a *tefach*. Elsewhere in the *gemara* his position is explained by the view that a *sukkah* must be a *dirat keva*, a permanent-style dwelling, requiring more complete walls. Here, however, the *gemara* frames the dispute as one of hermeneutics.

---

## Sukkah Walls Debate (II)

### Gemara's Analysis

The *gemara* explains the dispute as follows. The word *sukkot* appears three times in the relevant *pesukim*. According to *yesh eim lamesoret*, two of those appearances are written defectively — *basukat, basukat* — as singular, and one is written in the full plural form — *basukkot*. That yields: one plus one plus two equals four walls. One *pasuk* is needed to establish the basic topic of *sukkah* (the principle of *ein dorshin techilot* — you do not derive quantities from the opening word that introduces the subject). That leaves three, and a *halacha lemoshe misinai* reduces the third wall to even a *tefach*, yielding two full walls and a *tefach*. This is the position of the *Rabbanan*, who hold *yesh eim lamesoret*.

Rabbi Shimon holds *yesh eim lamikra*. All three appearances are read in the full plural, *basukkot*, yielding two each time — six total. Subtract one for *ein dorshin techilot*, leaving five. But five is two-and-a-half *sukkot*, which is meaningless as a count of walls; rather, the doubling reflects four walls from two *sukkot*. Subtract one more for *ein dorshin techilot* — now we have four. The *halacha lemoshe misinai* then reduces the fourth wall to a *tefach*, yielding three full walls and a *tefach*. The *gemara* notes that the identification of this *halacheta* as *halacha lemoshe misinai* is explicit in *Masechet Sukkah*.

## Beit Shammai vs. Hil (II)

### Reading vs. Writing Deba…

Having worked through the *sukkah* walls dispute, the *gemara* now revisits the *Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel* debate about blood application, clarifying that it too maps onto the *yesh eim lamikra* versus *yesh eim lamesoret* divide: Beit Shammai reads *karnot* by the oral tradition of the plural and derives six; Beit Hillel reads it by the defective written form and derives four. The *sukkah* analysis and the blood-application analysis thus operate by identical hermeneutic logic, with each *tannaitic* position consistently applying one principle across different domains.

## Blood from Corpses

### Rabbi Akiva's Reading

The *gemara* now presents Rabbi Akiva's position. The *beraisa* asks: *minayin lerevi'is dam sheba'ah mishnei meisim shemetame'ah b'ohel* — how do we know that a *revi'is* of blood that comes from two different corpses causes *tumah* under a tent (*tumah b'ohel*)?

The *halacha* is that a *revi'is* of blood — approximately three and a half ounces, the volume of blood needed to sustain a newborn — represents the person and therefore causes *tumah b'ohel*, like a *kezayit* of flesh or a certain number of bones. The question is whether this applies when the *revi'is* is composed of blood from two separate corpses, rather than a single one. If the rationale is that the blood represents an individual body, perhaps the blood must all come from a single *meis*.

Rabbi Akiva derives the answer from the *pasuk*: *al kol nafshot meit lo yavo* — do not come under the same roof as a dead soul. Since the word *nafshot* is read in the plural — two souls, two corpses — and the word *meit* is in the singular — one measure — Rabbi Akiva reads this as: the minimum measure from two different corpses is what the *pasuk* addresses. Blood representing two *nefashot* but comprising only one *shiur* still causes *tumah b'ohel*. His derivation follows *yesh eim lamikra*, going by the plural as read.

### Rabbis' Response

The Rabbis respond: *nafshat ketiv* — the word is written without the *vav*, in the defective spelling, making it singular. You cannot derive a plural meaning from a defective spelling. Going by the written form (*yesh eim lamesoret*), the *pasuk* refers to one dead body, and the blood must therefore come from a single *meis*.

## Challenge to Consensus

### Rav Acha's Objection

The *gemara* now challenges the sweeping claim that all of the listed *Tannaim* hold *yesh eim lamikra*. Rav Acha bar Yaakov objects: if everyone holds *yesh eim lamikra*, who would ever hold *yesh eim lamesoret*? Surely no *tanna* could reject the reading tradition altogether. A *beraisa* makes this vivid: the prohibition of *basar b'chalav* is derived from *lo tevashel gedi b'chalav imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. The word written is *chelev* (fat) and the word read is *chalav* (milk). The *beraisa* explicitly invokes *yesh eim lamikra* to establish that the prohibition is milk-based: because we have a tradition of how the word is read, we know that *chalav* means milk. Everyone accepts that *basar b'chalav* involves milk, not fat. Evidently, no *tanna* would dispute the primacy of the reading in such a case. So what does it mean to say some hold *yesh eim lamesoret*?

### Consensus on Reading

The *gemara* initially accepts the objection and states: *de-chulei alma yesh eim lamikra* — in truth, everyone agrees that reading takes primacy. The earlier debates among the *Tannaim* must therefore be based on something other than a fundamental disagreement about *yesh eim lamikra* versus *yesh eim lamesoret*.

The *gemara* then revisits each dispute in turn. The debate between Rabbi and the Rabbis about a *beis din* of five versus three is not about the general principle of reading versus writing; everyone reads *yarshi'un* in the plural. The debate is whether *yarshi'un Elohim* refers to a different *Elohim* than the one at the beginning of the *pasuk* (Rabbi's position), or to the same one (the Rabbis' position). Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz faced no actual halakhic dispute at all — he simply articulated *yesh eim lamikra* as the answer, and no one contested it. The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel about blood application, the *gemara* now suggests, is actually rooted in a separate *drasha* — the series of *ve-chiper, ve-chiper, ve-chiper* in *Parshat Vayikra*. Each *ve-chiper* reduces the requirement by one, and the debate is about the logic of that reduction, not about *yesh eim lamikra*. Similarly, the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan* about *sukkah* walls may reflect a deeper disagreement about whether a *sukkah* is a *dirat keva* or *dirat arai*, not a hermeneutic dispute about reading versus writing. And in the case of Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis regarding blood from two corpses, the debate is over whether *nefashot* in the plural refers to two specific corpses (*Rabbi Akiva*) or simply uses a generic collective (*Rabbanan*) — not over the principle of reading.

But then the *gemara* pushes back in the opposite direction. *Ve-ha tanya letotafet letotafet letotafet harei kan arba* — the *beraisa* about *tefillin* derives four compartments for the *shel rosh* from the word *totafot*: twice written defectively (without the *vav*), yielding one each, and once in the full form, yielding two — total four. That is *yesh eim lamesoret*, explicitly. Rabbi Yishmael makes this *drasha*. Rabbi Akiva says it is unnecessary since *tat* in one language means two and *pat* in another means two — arriving at four by a different route — but he does not dispute the *drasha* in principle. So apparently there is a real dispute between *Tannaim* about *yesh eim lamikra* versus *yesh eim lamesoret* after all. The *gemara* withdraws the claim that everyone agrees and returns to the position that there is indeed a fundamental disagreement.

## When Debate Arises

### Divergence Required

With the dispute reinstated, the *gemara* must now address the *beraisa* about *basar b'chalav*, which seemed to demonstrate universal agreement. The resolution is the crucial conceptual distinction of this entire sugya: *ve-hani mili ki paligi heicha de-shani kra mi-mesoret* — the debate between *yesh eim lamikra* and *yesh eim lamesoret* only arises when the written spelling diverges from the oral reading. That is the precise condition under which a choice must be made.

Consider the examples: *yarshi'an* is written without the *vav* but read *yarshi'un*; *karnot* is written defectively in two of its appearances but read as the full plural; *totafot* is written without the *vav* but read *totafot* in the full form; *basukat* is written as singular but read *basukkot*. In each of these cases, the Torah could have spelled the word to match its pronunciation but chose not to. That divergence creates two legitimate data points — the reading and the spelling — and the question becomes which one to privilege for hermeneutic purposes. That is where the camps divide.

But when the written spelling and the oral reading correspond — when there was simply no other way to write the word — there is no divergence to adjudicate, and everyone agrees that the reading determines the meaning. The word *chalav* (milk) and the word *chelev* (fat) are spelled identically, using the same three letters. The Torah had no alternative spelling available. There is therefore no scribal "choice" to read into the defective or non-defective form, and everyone must defer to the tradition of how the word is read — which is *chalav*, milk.

### Yira'eh Example

The *gemara* raises a striking challenge to this resolution. The word *yira'eh* — appearing in the context of the commandment to appear before God in the *Beit HaMikdash* — is in the same position as *chalav*: it can be vocalized as *yera'eh* (shall appear, in the *nif'al*) or as *yir'eh* (shall see, in the *kal*), and both vocalizations use the exact same written letters. There is no alternative spelling that could have disambiguated them. Yet there is a *tannaitic* debate that exploits precisely this ambiguity.

The *beraisa* teaches: *ha-suma be-achat me-einav patur min ha-reiyah* — one who is blind in one eye is exempt from the obligation to appear in the *Beit HaMikdash* during the *regel*. The derivation by Yochanan ben Dahavai in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima is: *ki-derech she-ba lir'ot kach ba le-hera'ot* — just as one comes to see, so one comes to be seen. The word is read as the obligation to appear (*le-hera'ot*), but it is equally writable as the obligation to see (*lir'ot*). Since the requirement of sight — *bishtei einav*, with both eyes — is built into the word's alternate reading, one who cannot see with both eyes is also exempt from appearing. The Rabbis, however, do not accept this *drasha*; they read the word only as *yera'eh* (shall appear) and do not give weight to the alternate reading.

This is a case where the spelling did not diverge from the reading — yet there is a debate. Some *Tannaim* are willing to derive meaning from an alternate reading of the same letters even when that alternate is not the traditional pronunciation.

### Chalav Resolved

The *gemara* therefore revises its conclusion. It cannot be that the debate only arises from a spelling divergence. Perhaps those who hold *yesh eim lamesoret* always look for what the written letters *could* mean, even when there is no defective spelling.

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika resolves the case of *basar b'chalav* differently. The *pasuk* says *lo tevashel gedi* — do not cook a kid. *Derech bishul asrah Torah* — the Torah prohibits the normal manner of cooking. Cooking is done with liquids. Therefore the word must mean *chalav*, a liquid, and cannot mean *chelev*, a fat — at least not in its solid form. Even those who normally give weight to alternate readings cannot extract the meaning of *chelev* here, because the context of *bishul*, cooking, establishes that a liquid is intended. The restriction to milk is thus secured not by *yesh eim lamikra* alone but by the contextual logic of the cooking process itself.

With this, the sugya reaches its conclusion. The fundamental distinction is this: when there is a genuine spelling divergence between the written and the read form, there is a real dispute among *Tannaim* as to which to privilege for *drasha*. When there is no spelling divergence, the debate can still arise if the letters support an alternate vocalization — but in a case like *basar b'chalav*, the context of the *pasuk* independently resolves the ambiguity. Tomorrow the *gemara* returns to the substantive issues of *Sanhedrin*.
