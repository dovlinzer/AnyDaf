# Sanhedrin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi vs. Rabbis

### Rabbi's Five-Judge View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בַּחֲמִשָּׁה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּגָּמֵר הַדִּין בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** Cases concerning **monetary law** are adjudicated **by three** judges. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** They are adjudicated **by five** judges, **so that a verdict can be issued with three** judges.

The *gemara* opens with a *beraisa* — apparently a *tosefta* — that states explicitly what the *Yerushalmi* had implied: *dinei mamonot bishlosha*, monetary cases require three judges, while *Rebbi omer bachamisha*, Rabbi maintains they require five. Rabbi's reasoning is that five judges are needed *kdei sheyigmor din bishlosha* — so that the final judgment (*gmar din*) is rendered by three.

One might object immediately: if you already have a *beis din* of three, surely the *gmar din* is rendered by all three — or at worst by two of them. Why would you need five for that purpose? The *gemara* clarifies: Rabbi's point is not that a *beis din* of three is incapable of reaching a verdict, but that the *pasuk* itself demands that three judges stand on the side finding for guilt or innocence. The *pesukím* referencing *Elohim* — the judges — require that the *gmar din* rest with a full three, not merely two out of three.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי: ״אֲשֶׁר יַרְשִׁיעֻן אֱלֹהִים״ – תְּרֵי. נֶאֱמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ לְמַטָּה וְנֶאֱמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ לְמַעְלָה. מָה לְמַטָּה שְׁנַיִם, אַף לְמַעְלָה שְׁנַיִם. וְאֵין בֵּית דִּין שָׁקוּל – מוֹסִיפִין עֲלֵיהֶם עוֹד אֶחָד. הֲרֵי כָּאן חֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, the previous explanation must be rejected. **Rather, this is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: It is stated in the next verse: **"He whom the court shall condemn"** (Exodus 22:8) in the plural, which means there must be **two** judges. Consequently, **it is stated: "The court,"** in the verse **below and it is stated: "The court,"** in the verse **above. Just as below** it is referring to **two** judges, as the word: "Shall condemn," is written in the plural, **so too above** it is referring to **two** judges, making a total of four. **And** since **a court may not** be composed of **an even** number of judges, **they add another one to them, so there are five** judges **here.**

Rabbi's derivation proceeds as follows. The *pasuk* reads *asher yarshiun Elohim* — "whom the judges shall condemn" — where *yarshiun* is in the plural, indicating two judges find guilty. Separately, the *pasuk* opens with *ad ha'Elohim yavo devar shneihem* — "the matter of both of them shall come before the judges" — using *Elohim* for the *beis din* they come to. Since *yarshiun* implies two judges on the condemning side, Rabbi reads the *Elohim* above — the body they appear before — as also comprising two. But *ein beis din shakul*: a court cannot be evenly divided, so a court of two becomes a court of three. Now, since the lower *Elohim* (those finding guilt) corresponds to two, and the upper *Elohim* (the whole *beis din*) also corresponds to two, with one added for *ein beis din shakul*, the total is five: *mah lemata shnayim, af lemala shnayim*, two below and two above, and one added — *harei kan chamisha*.

### Rabbis' Three-Judge View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: ״יַרְשִׁיעֻן״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how would **the Rabbis** respond to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's proof? They would say that although the term: **"Shall condemn"** is pronounced as a plural verb, it is **written** in a way that could also be read in the singular. Consequently, one cannot derive a requirement for more than one judge from there.

The Rabbis respond: *yarshi'an kesiv* — the word *yarshi'un* is in fact written without the *vav*. Because it is written in the defective spelling, you cannot derive from it that two judges are finding for guilt; you can only read it as referring to a single judge. With only one judge on the condemning side, the structure is one plus one plus one — three judges total, not five. The Rabbis thus hold the standard position: *dinei mamonot bishlosha*, three.

### Rabbi Abba's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְגַדֵּף בַּהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִי גְּדוֹלָה צְרִיכָה מֵאָה וְאַרְבָּעִים וְאֶחָד, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּגָּמֵר הַדִּין בְּשִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד? וּתְהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִי קְטַנָּה צְרִיכָה אַרְבָּעִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּגָּמֵר הַדִּין בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה וְעֶשְׂרִים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu** would **ridicule [*megaddef* ] this** suggestion. **If that is so,** then **the Great Sanhedrin would need** to consist of **141** judges, **so that the verdict can be issued by seventy-one, and a lesser Sanhedrin would need** to consist of **forty-five** judges, **so that the verdict can be issued by twenty-three.** But these assertions contradict the *halakha* as derived from the verses.

Rabbi Abba — the same Rabbi Abba who raises this challenge in the *Yerushalmi* as well — objects forcefully to Rabbi's entire logic. If the principle is that the deciding side must comprise the full constitutively required number, the consequences are absurd: the *sanhedrin gedola* of seventy-one would need a hundred and forty-one — enough that seventy-one could constitute one deciding side; the *sanhedrin ketana* of twenty-three would need forty-five — so that twenty-three could form the majority. No one accepts these conclusions.

### Resolved via Fixed Number

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״אֶסְפָה לִּי שִׁבְעִים אִישׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מִשְּׁעַת אֲסִיפָה שִׁבְעִים. ״וְשָׁפְטוּ הָעֵדָה וְהִצִּילוּ הָעֵדָה״ נָמֵי, מִשְּׁעַת שְׁפִיטַת הָעֵדָה. הָכִי נָמֵי: ״וְנִקְרַב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים״, מִשְּׁעַת קְרִיבָה שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the Merciful One states: "Gather Me seventy men** of the Elders of Israel" (Numbers 11:16), and this means that **from the time of gathering,** there must be **seventy.** Likewise, the verse states: **"And the congregation shall judge…and the congregation shall save"** (Numbers 35:24–25), from which it is derived that cases of capital law are adjudicated by twenty-three judges, and this **also** means that **from the time that the congregation judges** there must be twenty-three. **Similarly,** with regard to a case of monetary law, the verse states: **"The owner of the house shall come near to the court"** (Exodus 22:7), and this means **from the time of coming near** to judge the cases there must be **three** judges. Consequently, there is no requirement that the verdict be decided by a majority who could themselves form a court.

The resolution is that these fixed numbers refer to the composition of the *beis din* as a whole, not to the numerical size of the deciding majority. The *Rachmana* said *esfi li shivim ish* — gather me seventy men — and the convocation is seventy, full stop. *V'shaftu ha'eida v'hitzilu ha'eida*: when the Torah speaks of "the congregation" judging or saving, it means the entire body — the ten and the ten and the twenty-three — not the winning side. Similarly, *v'nikrav ba'al habayit el ha'Elohim* — the litigant comes before the judges — means the body they come to is a body of three, *mishas kriva shlosha*. The full number describes the institution, not the voting bloc.

With this established, Rabbi's actual derivation becomes clearer. It is not the naive claim that three must sit on the deciding side. Rather, *asher yarshiun Elohim* uses *yarshiun* in the plural, indicating two. *Nemar Elohim lemata v'nemar Elohim lemala* — since *Elohim* appears both above and below, and the lower reference implies two, the upper reference — the body they come before — must also be two. But two is an even court, which is forbidden, so it becomes three. That gives us two (below) plus two (above, now raised to three), plus one for *ein beis din shakul*, yielding five. The Rabbis reply that since *yarshi'an* is written defectively, without the *vav*, the plural cannot be derived, and the whole structure collapses to three.

## Yesh Eim Lamikra

### All Agree: Reading Prior…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן רוֹעֵץ, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Sages is explained as an example of the more general question of whether the written consonantal text or the vocalization of the Torah is authoritative. As **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosei says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **Rabbi Yehuda ben Roetz, Beit Shammai, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Akiva, they all hold** that the **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative,** and that the *halakha* is therefore decided based on the meaning of the word as pronounced, and not on possible alternative readings of the written text.

Having resolved the debate between Rabbi and the Rabbis, the *gemara* pivots entirely — occupying the remainder of the *daf* — to a question that the foregoing dispute has placed front and center: does one derive *halachic* conclusions from the way a *pasuk* is read aloud, or from the way it is written in the Torah?

The *gemara* opens with a sweeping statement attributed to Rabbi Yochanan: *Rebbi ve-Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz, u-veit Shammai ve-Rabbi Shimon ve-Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi, Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz, Beit Shammai, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Akiva — *kulu sevira lehu yesh eim lamikra*. All of these *Tannaim* hold that hermeneutic interpretation follows the reading (*mikra*), not the scribal tradition of spelling (*mesorah* / *mesoret*).

A brief terminological note: the word *mikra* means "reading," referring to our tradition of how the text is vocalized and pronounced. *Mesoret* means the scribal tradition of how the text is spelled — and counterintuitively, it is the *mesoret*, the "tradition," that refers to the written form, while *mikra*, "reading," refers to the oral pronunciation. This inversion of expectations is worth keeping in mind throughout.

### Yehuda ben Roeitz Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן רוֹעֵץ, דְּתַנְיָא: שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידִים אֶת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן רוֹעֵץ: אֶקְרָא אֲנִי ״שִׁבְעִים״, יָכוֹל תְּהֵא יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה טְמֵאָה שִׁבְעִים?
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **Rabbi Yehuda ben Roetz,** it is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: His **students asked Rabbi Yehuda ben Roetz:** The verse states: "But if she gives birth to a female, then she shall be unclean for two weeks, as in her menstrual impurity; and she shall then continue in the blood of purification for sixty-six days" (Leviticus 12:5). Based on the written consonantal text, **I can read** the amount of time she is impure as: **"Seventy [*shivim*]** days," and not as: "Two weeks [*shevuayim*]." One **might** have thought, therefore, that a woman who **gives birth to a female should be impure** for **seventy** days.

The *beraisa* about Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz is a remarkably rich episode. His students posed to him what seemed like a genuine textual question: when the Torah says *ve'im nekeiva teled vetame'a shevu'ayim kenidasah* — that a woman who gives birth to a girl is *teme'ah* for *shevu'ayim* — the word is written without a *vav*, making it technically legible as *shivim*, seventy. Could it be that the mother of a girl is *teme'ah* for seventy days?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: טִימֵּא וְטִיהֵר בַּזָּכָר, וְטִימֵּא וְטִיהֵר בַּנְּקֵבָה. מָה כְּשֶׁטִּיהֵר בַּזָּכָר – בַּנְּקֵבָה כִּפְלַיִם, אַף כְּשֶׁטִּימֵּא בַּזָּכָר – בַּנְּקֵבָה כִּפְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda ben Roetz **said to them:** It can be proven that this is not the *halakha*, as the Torah **deemed** her **impure and deemed** her **pure** when she gave birth **to a male, and deemed** her **impure and deemed** her **pure** when she gave birth **to a female. Just as when it deemed** her **pure** for thirty-three days after the initial period of impurity when she gave birth **to a male,** when she gives birth **to a female** she is pure for sixty-six days, which is **double** the amount of time; **so too, when it deemed** her **impure** for seven days when she gave birth **to a male,** when she gives birth **to a female** she is also impure for **double** the amount of time. Consequently, the verse must be read according to the vocalized reading: "Two weeks," and not according to the consonantal text, which could be read: "Seventy."

Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz answered them with an elegant *pshat* argument. The Torah establishes an exact parallel structure: by a male child there is a period of *tumah* (seven days) followed by a period of *dam tahor* (thirty-three days); by a female child those periods are precisely doubled — fourteen days of *tumah* and sixty-six days of *dam tahor*. The parallelism is unambiguous: *tima vetiher bezachar vetima vetiher banekeivah — mah shetihar bezachar banekeivah kiflayim*, just as the period of purity is doubled for a girl, so too the period of *tumah* is doubled. Two weeks, not seventy days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּצְאוּ, יָצָא וּמַחֲזִיר אַחֲרֵיהֶם. אָמַר לָהֶן: אִי אַתֶּם זְקוּקִים לְכָךְ. ״שְׁבוּעַיִים״ קָרֵינַן, וְיֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **After they left,** Rabbi Yehuda ben Roetz **went out and followed them.** He then **said to them: You do not need this** proof that I gave based on the comparison of the periods of impurity with the periods of purity. Rather, **we read** the verse as: **"Two weeks," and** the **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative.**

The students left satisfied. But Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz ran after them: *iy atem zekukim lekach* — you don't even need that proof. The reason is simply *shevuayim karinan ve-yesh eim lemikra*: we read the word *shevuayim*, two weeks, and the principle of *yesh eim lamikra* determines the *halacha*. It does not matter that the defective spelling could theoretically support an alternative reading. The tradition of pronunciation takes precedence.

### The Principle Explained

The pedagogical structure of this episode is itself instructive. Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz taught the logical proof first and only then articulated the formal principle. This sequence carries a more powerful message than the reverse would have. Had he led with *yesh eim lamikra* and then offered the textual proof as corroboration, the listener might conclude that the principle of *yesh eim lamikra* requires independent textual backup. By demonstrating the elegant proof and then setting it aside, he conveys the opposite: even where I *can* prove it to you from the internal structure of the *pesukim*, that is ultimately beside the point. The tradition of reading is the anchor, and no alternative derivation is needed — or should be sought.

This connects to a foundational principle of *Torah she-baal peh*. The Talmudic enterprise of interpreting *Torah she-biktav* does not begin from the bare written letters in isolation, as if the reader had no prior knowledge of how those letters are meant to be read. The starting point is always the received tradition of vocalization and reading. Only from there does interpretation proceed.

## Beit Shammai vs. Hil (II)

### Shammai's Blood Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, דִּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים, כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״. וּבְחַטָּאת – שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף בְּחַטָּאת שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Beit Shammai,** the proof that they also hold that the vocalization of the Torah is authoritative is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 36b): **Beit Shammai say:** With regard to **all** offerings **whose** blood must be **presented on the external altar,** once the blood has been **presented with one presentation** the offering **has effected atonement,** even if more presentations are ideally required, **as it is stated: "And the blood of your offerings shall be poured out** against the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:27). This verse teaches that even with regard to a burnt-offering, which requires multiple presentations of the blood, a single presentation is sufficient to render the offering valid after the fact. **But with regard to a sin-offering,** it is valid only if there were at least **two presentations. And Beit Hillel say: Even with regard to a sin-offering that one presented with one presentation, it has effected atonement** after the fact.

The *mishnah* in question teaches: *kol hanitnin al hamizbe'ach hachitzon shenatan matanah achat kipper* — all blood applications that belong on the outer altar, if performed with only a single application, have achieved atonement *bedi'eved*. This applies to the *olah*, *shlamim*, *asham*, and the like. For the *chatat*, whose blood is applied to the *karnot hamizbeach* — the four corners (horns) of the altar — Beit Shammai rules that two applications suffice *bedi'eved*, while Beit Hillel rules that even a single application achieves atonement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? ״קַרְנֹת״, ״קַרְנֹת״, ״קַרְנֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ: אַרְבַּע לְמִצְוָה, וּשְׁתַּיִם לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Huna says: What is the reasoning** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai?** The verse states: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin-offering with his finger, and put it on the **horns** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:25); and "The priest shall take of the blood thereof with his finger, and put it on the **horns** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:30); and again: "The priest shall take of the blood of the sin-offering with his finger, and put it on the **horns** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:34). As the minimum amount justifying the use of plural, i.e., "horns," is two, one may conclude that **there are six** references to the horns of the altar **here. Four** of them are mentioned **for the mitzva,** meaning that he should present the blood on all four horns of the altar *ab initio*, **and** the other **two** were mentioned **to invalidate** the offering if he did not present it on at least two horns.

Rav Huna explains Beit Shammai's derivation. In the *parshiyot* toward the end of *Parshat Vayikra*, dealing with various *chataot*, the word *karnot* (corners) appears three times. The word *karnot* is plural, indicating a minimum of two each time. Three times two is six. But there are only four corners. Therefore: four is the prescribed *mitzvah*, and two is the minimum required (*me'akev*). Beit Shammai's derivation follows the reading (*yesh eim lamikra*): the word is read *karnot* in the plural, and from that plural they derive the number two.

### Hillel's Written Form

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: קַרְנוֹת, קַרְנַת, קַרְנַת – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע. שָׁלֹשׁ לְמִצְוָה, וְאַחַת לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say:** The matter should be understood according to the written consonantal text. The word **"horns"** is written once plene, with a *vav*, which means that it must be read in the plural; and the other two times the words **"horns"** and **"horns"** are written deficient, without a *vav*, in a way that can be vocalized in the singular. Therefore, **there are four** references to horns **here. Three** of these presentations are written **to** indicate that they are performed only as **a mitzva,** i.e., they are performed *ab initio*, but the offering is valid even absent their presentation. **And** the remaining **one,** i.e., the fourth presentation, is written **to** indicate that its absence **invalidates** the offering, i.e., the offering is not valid if the blood was not presented against at least one horn of the altar. Evidently, Beit Shammai hold the vocalization is authoritative, whereas Beit Hillel hold the consonantal text is authoritative.

Beit Hillel, by contrast, notes that two of the three occurrences of *karnot* are written without the *vav* — *karnat* in defective spelling. Going by the written form (*yesh eim lamesoret*), two of the three instances mean only one, and one instance means two: one plus one plus two equals four. Of those four, three are the prescribed *mitzvah* and one is the minimum *me'akev*. Thus for Beit Hillel, only one application is minimally required *bedi'eved*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִפֶּר״, ״וְכִפֶּר״, ״וְכִפֶּר״ – מִפְּנֵי הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states with regard to the sin-offering brought by a ruler: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:26), and with regard to a sin-offering of a goat brought by an ordinary person: "And the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:31), and with regard to a sin-offering of a lamb brought by an ordinary person: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:35), repeating this term three times. This repetition is **due to the** following **logical inference:**

The *gemara* raises an objection: if all four are the *mitzvah* component and the *me'akev* is zero, one might say no application is required *bedi'eved*. But *kapara belo netinah lo ashkechan* — we have no precedent for atonement being achieved without any blood application at all. Hence at least one is taken as *me'akev*, and three are the *mitzvah* portion.

## Sukkah Walls Debate (II)

### Standard Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת. ״בַּסֻּכָּת״, ״בַּסֻּכָּת״, ״בַּסֻּכּוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע. דַּל חַד קְרָא לְגוּפֵיהּ, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תְּלָת. אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא, גְרַעְתָּא לִשְׁלִישִׁית, וְאוֹקֵימְתָּא אַטֶּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The Rabbis hold that** the **tradition** of the manner in which the verses are written **is authoritative.** The Torah states: "You shall reside **in *sukkot*"** (Leviticus 23:42) spelled deficient, without the letter *vav*, in a way that can be read in the singular. And later in that verse it also states: "All that are homeborn in Israel shall reside **in *sukkot*"** spelled the same way. And in the next verse it states: "So that your future generations will know that I caused the children of Israel to reside **in *sukkot*,"** this time spelled plene, with a *vav*, which means it must be plural. Therefore, **there are four** walls **here. Remove one verse,** which is needed **to** teach the mitzva to sit in a *sukka* **itself,** and **three** walls **remain. The *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, which says that one of the walls may be incomplete, **comes** and **reduces the third and establishes it as** a minimum of one **handbreadth.**

The standard ruling, as taught in the *beraisa*, is *shtayim kehilchatan ve-shlishit afilu tefach*: a *sukkah* requires two full walls and a third wall of even a minimal *tefach*. This is the position of the *chachamim*.

### Rabbi Shimon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: ״בַּסֻּכֹּת״, ״בַּסֻּכֹּת״, ״בַּסֻּכּוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ. דַּל חַד קְרָא לְגוּפֵיהּ, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ אַרְבַּע. אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא גְרַעְתָּא לִרְבִיעִית, וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ אַטֶּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon holds** that the vocalization is authoritative. Since the verses state: "You shall reside **in *sukkot*,"** and: "All that are homeborn in Israel shall reside **in *sukkot*,"** and: "So that your future generations will know that I caused the children of Israel to reside **in *sukkot*,"** all of which are pronounced as plural nouns, **there are six** walls **here. Remove one verse to** teach the mitzva of *sukka* **itself,** and **four** walls **remain. The *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai then **comes** and **reduces the fourth** wall, **and establishes it at** a minimum of one **handbreadth.** Consequently, it is clear that Rabbi Shimon also holds that the vocalization is authoritative.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *shalosh kehilchatan ve-revi'it afilu tefach* — three full walls are required, with the fourth wall needing only a *tefach*. Elsewhere in the *gemara* his position is explained by the view that a *sukkah* must be a *dirat keva*, a permanent-style dwelling, requiring more complete walls. Here, however, the *gemara* frames the dispute as one of hermeneutics.

## Sukkah Walls Debate (II)

### Gemara's Analysis

The *gemara* explains the dispute as follows. The word *sukkot* appears three times in the relevant *pesukim*. According to *yesh eim lamesoret*, two of those appearances are written defectively — *basukat, basukat* — as singular, and one is written in the full plural form — *basukkot*. That yields: one plus one plus two equals four walls. One *pasuk* is needed to establish the basic topic of *sukkah* (the principle of *ein dorshin techilot* — you do not derive quantities from the opening word that introduces the subject). That leaves three, and a *halacha lemoshe misinai* reduces the third wall to even a *tefach*, yielding two full walls and a *tefach*. This is the position of the *Rabbanan*, who hold *yesh eim lamesoret*.

Rabbi Shimon holds *yesh eim lamikra*. All three appearances are read in the full plural, *basukkot*, yielding two each time — six total. Subtract one for *ein dorshin techilot*, leaving five. But five is two-and-a-half *sukkot*, which is meaningless as a count of walls; rather, the doubling reflects four walls from two *sukkot*. Subtract one more for *ein dorshin techilot* — now we have four. The *halacha lemoshe misinai* then reduces the fourth wall to a *tefach*, yielding three full walls and a *tefach*. The *gemara* notes that the identification of this *halacheta* as *halacha lemoshe misinai* is explicit in *Masechet Sukkah*.

## Beit Shammai vs. Hil (II)

### Reading vs. Writing Debate

Having worked through the *sukkah* walls dispute, the *gemara* now revisits the *Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel* debate about blood application, clarifying that it too maps onto the *yesh eim lamikra* versus *yesh eim lamesoret* divide: Beit Shammai reads *karnot* by the oral tradition of the plural and derives six; Beit Hillel reads it by the defective written form and derives four. The *sukkah* analysis and the blood-application analysis thus operate by identical hermeneutic logic, with each *tannaitic* position consistently applying one principle across different domains.

## Blood from Corpses

### Rabbi Akiva's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר, מִנַּיִין לִרְבִיעִית דָּם שֶׁיָּצְאָה מִשְּׁנֵי מֵתִים שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כׇּל נַפְשֹׁת מֵת לֹא יָבֹא״ – שְׁתֵּי נְפָשׁוֹת וְשִׁיעוּר אֶחָד. וְרַבָּנַן: ״נפשת״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Rabbi Akiva,** it is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is known that a quarter*-log* of blood that came from a corpse imparts ritual impurity in a tent. And **Rabbi Akiva says: From where** is it derived **that a quarter***-log* **of blood that came out of two** separate **corpses** also **imparts ritual impurity in a tent? As it is stated** with regard to a priest: **"He shall not come upon any dead bodies"** (Leviticus 21:11). The word "bodies" is pronounced as a plural word, and since the Torah teaches elsewhere that: "The blood is the life" (Deuteronomy 12:23), this indicates that impurity can be imparted by **one measure** of blood that came from **two bodies. And the Rabbis** say: The word **"bodies" is written** deficient, without a *vav*, so that it can also be read as a singular word, indicating that a quarter*-log* of blood imparts impurity in a tent only if it came from a single corpse.

The *gemara* now presents Rabbi Akiva's position. The *beraisa* asks: *minayin lerevi'is dam sheba'ah mishnei meisim shemetame'ah b'ohel* — how do we know that a *revi'is* of blood that comes from two different corpses causes *tumah* under a tent (*tumah b'ohel*)?

The *halacha* is that a *revi'is* of blood — approximately three and a half ounces, the volume of blood needed to sustain a newborn — represents the person and therefore causes *tumah b'ohel*, like a *kezayit* of flesh or a certain number of bones. The question is whether this applies when the *revi'is* is composed of blood from two separate corpses, rather than a single one. If the rationale is that the blood represents an individual body, perhaps the blood must all come from a single *meis*.

Rabbi Akiva derives the answer from the *pasuk*: *al kol nafshot meit lo yavo* — do not come under the same roof as a dead soul. Since the word *nafshot* is read in the plural — two souls, two corpses — and the word *meit* is in the singular — one measure — Rabbi Akiva reads this as: the minimum measure from two different corpses is what the *pasuk* addresses. Blood representing two *nefashot* but comprising only one *shiur* still causes *tumah b'ohel*. His derivation follows *yesh eim lamikra*, going by the plural as read.

### Rabbis' Response

The Rabbis respond: *nafshat ketiv* — the word is written without the *vav*, in the defective spelling, making it singular. You cannot derive a plural meaning from a defective spelling. Going by the written form (*yesh eim lamesoret*), the *pasuk* refers to one dead body, and the blood must therefore come from a single *meis*.

## Challenge to Consensus

### Rav Acha's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: מִי אִיכָּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ ״יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא״? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – יָכוֹל ״בְּחֵלֶב״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov objects to** Rabbi Yoḥanan's assertion that all of the above disputes are based on the question of whether the traditional consonantal text or the vocalized text of the Torah is authoritative: **Is there** a Sage **who does not accept** the principle that the **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "You shall not cook a young goat **in its mother's milk [*baḥalev*]"** (Exodus 23:19). One **might** have thought the verse should be read as prohibiting the cooking of the young goat **in the fat [*beḥelev*]** of the mother, and there is no prohibition against cooking the meat with milk.

The *gemara* now challenges the sweeping claim that all of the listed *Tannaim* hold *yesh eim lamikra*. Rav Acha bar Yaakov objects: if everyone holds *yesh eim lamikra*, who would ever hold *yesh eim lamesoret*? Surely no *tanna* could reject the reading tradition altogether. A *beraisa* makes this vivid: the prohibition of *basar b'chalav* is derived from *lo tevashel gedi b'chalav imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. The word written is *chelev* (fat) and the word read is *chalav* (milk). The *beraisa* explicitly invokes *yesh eim lamikra* to establish that the prohibition is milk-based: because we have a tradition of how the word is read, we know that *chalav* means milk. Everyone accepts that *basar b'chalav* involves milk, not fat. Evidently, no *tanna* would dispute the primacy of the reading in such a case. So what does it mean to say some hold *yesh eim lamesoret*?

### Consensus on Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **You** therefore **say** in response: The **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative,** and the verse prohibits cooking the young goat in its mother's milk.

The *gemara* initially accepts the objection and states: *de-chulei alma yesh eim lamikra* — in truth, everyone agrees that reading takes primacy. The earlier debates among the *Tannaim* must therefore be based on something other than a fundamental disagreement about *yesh eim lamikra* versus *yesh eim lamesoret*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא, וְרַבִּי וְרַבָּנַן בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: רַבִּי סָבַר ״יַרְשִׁיעֻן אֱלֹהִים״ – אַחֲרִינֵי, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי ״יַרְשִׁיעֻן״ – דְּהַאיְךְ וְהַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, everyone** agrees that the **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative. But** in actuality, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and the Rabbis disagree with regard to this: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that the phrase **"the court shall condemn"** is referring to **other** judges, in addition to the three that were derived from the earlier verse, leading to a total of five, whereas **the Rabbis hold** that the term **"shall condemn"** means **these** judges, i.e., **those** who have already been mentioned, and therefore there are only three.

The *gemara* then revisits each dispute in turn. The debate between Rabbi and the Rabbis about a *beis din* of five versus three is not about the general principle of reading versus writing; everyone reads *yarshi'un* in the plural. The debate is whether *yarshi'un Elohim* refers to a different *Elohim* than the one at the beginning of the *pasuk* (Rabbi's position), or to the same one (the Rabbis' position). Rabbi Yehuda ben Roeitz faced no actual halakhic dispute at all — he simply articulated *yesh eim lamikra* as the answer, and no one contested it. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הִלֵּל, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְכִפֶּר״, ״וְכִפֶּר״, ״וְכִפֶּר״ – מִפְּנֵי הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Beit Hillel,** who hold that a single presentation of blood is sufficient to atone even when bringing a sin-offering, this is not because they hold the traditional consonantal text is authoritative. Rather, their opinion is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the sin-offering brought by a ruler: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:26), and with regard to a sin-offering of a goat brought by an ordinary person: "And the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:31), and with regard to a sin-offering of a lamb brought by an ordinary person: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:35), repeating this term three times. This repetition is **due to the** following **logical inference:**

The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel about blood application, the *gemara* now suggests, is actually rooted in a separate *drasha* — the series of *ve-chiper, ve-chiper, ve-chiper* in *Parshat Vayikra*. Each *ve-chiper* reduces the requirement by one, and the debate is about the logic of that reduction, not about *yesh eim lamikra*. Similarly, the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan* about *sukkah* walls may reflect a deeper disagreement about whether a *sukkah* is a *dirat keva* or *dirat arai*, not a hermeneutic dispute about reading versus writing. And in the case of Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis regarding blood from two corpses, the debate is over whether *nefashot* in the plural refers to two specific corpses (*Rabbi Akiva*) or simply uses a generic collective (*Rabbanan*) — not over the principle of reading.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״לְטוֹטֶפֶת״, ״לְטֹטֶפֶת״, ״לְטוֹטָפוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does everyone** actually hold that the **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the number of compartments in the phylacteries of the head, the verse states: "It shall be for a sign upon your hand, and **for *totafot*** between your eyes" (Exodus 13:16), with the word *totafot* spelled deficient, without a second *vav*, in a way that can be read as singular; and again: "They shall be **for *totafot*** between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8), spelled as a singular word; and again: "They shall be **for *totafot*** between your eyes" (11:18), this time spelled plene, with a second *vav*, in a manner that must be plural? **There are four** mentions of *totafot* **here,** as the third one is written in the plural and therefore counts as two. Consequently, it is derived that the phylacteries of the head must have four compartments. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

But then the *gemara* pushes back in the opposite direction. *Ve-ha tanya letotafet letotafet letotafet harei kan arba* — the *beraisa* about *tefillin* derives four compartments for the *shel rosh* from the word *totafot*: twice written defectively (without the *vav*), yielding one each, and once in the full form, yielding two — total four. That is *yesh eim lamesoret*, explicitly. Rabbi Yishmael makes this *drasha*. Rabbi Akiva says it is unnecessary since *tat* in one language means two and *pat* in another means two — arriving at four by a different route — but he does not dispute the *drasha* in principle. So apparently there is a real dispute between *Tannaim* about *yesh eim lamikra* versus *yesh eim lamesoret* after all. The *gemara* withdraws the claim that everyone agrees and returns to the position that there is indeed a fundamental disagreement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, טַט בְּכַתְפִּי שְׁתַּיִם, פַּת בְּאַפְרִיקִי שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: There is no need** for this proof, as the requirement of four compartments can be derived from the word *totafot* itself: The word ***tat* in** the language of the ***Katfei*** means **two,** and the word ***pat* in** the language of ***Afriki*** also means **two,** and therefore *totafot* can be understood as a compound word meaning: Four. The *baraita* therefore indicates that Rabbi Yishmael holds that not the vocalization but rather the tradition of the manner in which the verses in the Torah are written is authoritative.

## When Debate Arises

### Divergence Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם פְּלִיגִי. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי כִּי פְּלִיגִי – הֵיכָא דְּשָׁנֵי קְרָא מִמָּסוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the explanation that everyone holds that the vocalization of the Torah is authoritative must be rejected, and it must be explained that the Sages **actually** do **disagree** whether it is the vocalization of the Torah or the tradition of the manner in which the verses in the Torah are written that is authoritative. **And** in order to explain the unresolved problem with regard to the *baraita* about the prohibition of cooking a young goat in its mother's milk, the explanation is that **this statement, that they disagree** as to whether the vocalization or the tradition is authoritative, applies **where the vocalization** of the word **differs from the tradition** of the manner in which the word is written.

With the dispute reinstated, the *gemara* must now address the *beraisa* about *basar b'chalav*, which seemed to demonstrate universal agreement. The resolution is the crucial conceptual distinction of this entire sugya: *ve-hani mili ki paligi heicha de-shani kra mi-mesoret* — the debate between *yesh eim lamikra* and *yesh eim lamesoret* only arises when the written spelling diverges from the oral reading. That is the precise condition under which a choice must be made.

Consider the examples: *yarshi'an* is written without the *vav* but read *yarshi'un*; *karnot* is written defectively in two of its appearances but read as the full plural; *totafot* is written without the *vav* but read *totafot* in the full form; *basukat* is written as singular but read *basukkot*. In each of these cases, the Torah could have spelled the word to match its pronunciation but chose not to. That divergence creates two legitimate data points — the reading and the spelling — and the question becomes which one to privilege for hermeneutic purposes. That is where the camps divide.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַאי ״חֵלֶב״ וַ״חֲלֵב״ דְּכִי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ, יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** in **this** case the words **milk [*ḥalev*] and fat [*ḥelev*] are** written in **an identical** manner, as there is no difference in the writing at all, only in the way they are vocalized. Therefore, all agree that the **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative.**

But when the written spelling and the oral reading correspond — when there was simply no other way to write the word — there is no divergence to adjudicate, and everyone agrees that the reading determines the meaning. The word *chalav* (milk) and the word *chelev* (fat) are spelled identically, using the same three letters. The Torah had no alternative spelling available. There is therefore no scribal "choice" to read into the defective or non-defective form, and everyone must defer to the tradition of how the word is read — which is *chalav*, milk.

### Yira'eh Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי ״יִרְאֶה״ ״יֵרָאֶה״ דְּכִי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ, וּפְּלִיגִי? דְּתַנְיָא: יוֹחָנָן בֶּן דַּהֲבַאי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא: הַסּוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו – פָּטוּר מִן הָרְאִיָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״יִרְאֶה״ ״יֵרָאֶה״ – כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁבָּא לִרְאוֹת כָּךְ בָּא לֵירָאוֹת. מָה לִרְאוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו, אַף לֵירָאוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** the words: **"Shall see"** and **"shall appear" are** written in **an identical** matter, **and** nevertheless the Sages **disagree** about which reading is authoritative. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Yoḥanan ben Dahavai says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima: One who is blind in one of his eyes is exempt from the** mitzva of **appearance,** i.e., the obligation to appear in the Temple and to sacrifice an offering on the three pilgrimage Festivals, **as it is stated:** "Three times in the year all your males shall appear [*yera'eh*] before the Lord God" (Exodus 23:17). According to the way in which the verse is written, without vocalization, it can be read as ***yireh*,** meaning: **Shall see,** instead of ***yera'eh*,** meaning: **Shall appear.** This teaches that **in the** same **manner that one comes to see, so he comes to appear,** i.e., to be seen: **Just as** the usual way **to see** is **with both of one's eyes, so too,** the obligation **to appear** applies only to one who comes **with** the sight of **both his eyes.**

The *gemara* therefore revises its conclusion. It cannot be that the debate only arises from a spelling divergence. Perhaps those who hold *yesh eim lamesoret* always look for what the written letters *could* mean, even when that alternate is not the traditional pronunciation.

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika resolves the case of *basar b'chalav* differently. The *pasuk* says *lo tevashel gedi* — do not cook a kid. *Derech bishul asrah Torah* — the Torah prohibits the normal manner of cooking. Cooking is done with liquids. Therefore the word must mean *chalav*, a liquid, and cannot mean *chelev*, a fat — at least not in its solid form. Even those who normally give weight to alternate readings cannot extract the meaning of *chelev* here, because the context of *bishul*, cooking, establishes that a liquid is intended. The restriction to milk is thus secured not by *yesh eim lamikra* alone but by the contextual logic of the cooking process itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי״, דֶּרֶךְ בִּישּׁוּל אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is therefore apparent that even when there is no difference in the way the words are written, some say that the tradition, not the vocalization, is authoritative. **Rather, Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** The reason all agree that there is a prohibition to cook a young goat in its mother's milk and not in its mother's fat is that **the verse states: "You shall not cook a young goat"** (Exodus 23:19). The verse teaches that the action **the Torah prohibited** is in **the manner of cooking,** which is generally done in a liquid such as milk and not in a solid substance such as fat.

With this, the sugya reaches its conclusion. The fundamental distinction is this: when there is a genuine spelling divergence between the written and the read form, there is a real dispute among *Tannaim* as to which to privilege for *drasha*. When there is no spelling divergence, the debate can still arise if the letters support an alternate vocalization — but in a case like *basar b'chalav*, the context of the *pasuk* independently resolves the ambiguity.
