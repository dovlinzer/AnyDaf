# Bava Metzia 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Worker Eating Rights

### Torah Sources

The topic of today's daf is the right of a worker to eat from the produce he is harvesting as he performs his work. The basic principle is grounded in two *pesukim* in the Torah. The first reads: *ki tavo bekamat re'echa vekatafta melilot beyadecha vechermesh lo tanif al kamat re'echa* — when you enter your friend's standing grain, you may take some kernels by hand, but you may not use a scythe. The second, addressing a vineyard, states that you may eat grapes until you are satisfied but may not place them into your vessel. Although the *pesukim* do not explicitly identify the subject as a hired worker, *Chazal* understood both verses to be speaking about a *po'el*, and in the previous daf the relevant *drashot* were set out in detail.

### All Produce Types

Those *drashot* established that the worker's right to eat is not limited to grain or grapes but extends to all types of growing produce. The word *chermesh* — scythe — implies anything that can be cut by a scythe, and *kama* — standing grain — implies anything growing and upright. Together they expand the right beyond the two examples the Torah provides.

### Growing vs Harvested

The right applies at the moment of *gmar melacha* — here meaning the point at which produce is being cut from the ground, i.e., the harvesting itself. This is a distinct usage of *gmar melacha* from the one that will be introduced shortly in the context of *teruma* and *ma'aser*. In addition to produce that is still attached to the ground (*mechubar*), there is a separate discussion about produce that has already been detached (*talush*), which will be addressed later.

## Vineyard Derasha

### Two Verses

The Gemara, at the bottom of 87b, asks: now that we have derived from the words *chermesh* and *kama* that the worker's right applies to all forms of growing produce, why does the Torah also need to state the verse about the vineyard — *ki tavo becharem re'echa*? The verse about grain alone should suffice, and the rest could be derived from it. The Gemara answers, in the name of Rava: *lehilchotav* — the vineyard verse is needed to teach specific parameters of this *halacha*, as the *braita* proceeds to demonstrate through a careful *derasha* of each phrase in that verse.

### Ki Tavo

The *braita* begins with the phrase *ki tavo* — when you come. A *gezeira shava* is drawn from the same root in *lo tavo alav hashemesh*, the verse prohibiting an employer from delaying a worker's wages past sunset. Just as the subject there is a *po'el*, a hired laborer, so too here: the verse is speaking of a *po'el*, not merely someone passing through a vineyard.

### Jewish vs Non-Jewish

The phrase *becherem re'echa* — your friend's vineyard — is interpreted to mean *velo becherem goy*: the rules of the verse do not apply when working in a non-Jew's vineyard. This is a permissive reading: when the worker is employed by a non-Jew, the restrictions that accompany the right to eat do not apply — he is not prohibited, for example, from storing some for later or eating outside the moment of *gmar melacha*.

### Gezel Goy

This raises an immediate difficulty. The Gemara notes: *ha nicha leman d'amar gezel goy asur* — this formulation makes sense if one holds that stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden. In that case, the Torah's specification that the rules apply only in a Jew's vineyard provides a necessary dispensation for the *po'el* — telling him that taking produce in the course of his work is not theft. But *ela leman d'amar gezel goy mutar* — if one holds that stealing from a non-Jew is permitted, why would any special dispensation be required? The worker could simply take whatever he wished anyway.

This is a striking passage. The Gemara treats the view that *gezel goy* is *mutar* as a live option — not to endorse it, but as a methodological counterpoint. Tosafot addresses this directly. Citing *Rashi*'s reference to the *sugya* in Bava Kamma (*Hagozel Umachil*), Tosafot notes that even the lenient opinion in that *sugya* does not permit outright theft; at most it permits *afka'at halva'ato* — evading repayment of a loan — which is itself problematic, but is not straightforward theft. The Gemara there effectively rejects the view that *gezel mamash* from a non-Jew is *mutar*: *aval gezeila mamash modeh kuli alma de'asur*.

Tosafot then adds, in the name of the *Ri*, that there is a voice later in the tractate that does permit outright theft from a non-Jew — so the position is not entirely without a basis in the *sugya*. Nevertheless, Tosafot raises a further difficulty: there is a *pasuk* — *vehishav im koneihu* — from which the Gemara in a later context derives that one must deal honestly and not defraud a non-Jew, which seems to be an explicit prohibition. Tosafot suggests that even according to the view that *gezel goy* is technically *mutar*, that verse may prohibit it on grounds of *chillul Hashem*: the non-Jew trusted him and was conducting business with him, and to take advantage would constitute a desecration of God's name. The practical and dominant position, as reflected in the main *sugya* of Bava Kamma, is that *gezel goy* is *asur*.

The Gemara also derives from the phrase *becherem re'echa* — your friend's — that the allowance does not apply when working for *hekdesh*. Here the direction is reversed: with a Jewish employer the worker has the allowance; with *hekdesh* he does not. One might have thought that just as the Torah permits taking the owner's produce without it constituting theft, it might similarly waive the concern of *me'ila* for produce belonging to *hekdesh*. Tosafot entertains this *hava amina* and notes that it makes more sense if *me'ila* is understood as a species of *gezeila* — taking from *hekdesh* what belongs to it. If *me'ila* instead concerns impinging on the *kedusha* of consecrated property, it is harder to see why the Torah would have waived it. One possible way to construct the *hava amina* is a scenario where something is owned by *hekdesh* but does not possess *kedusha* — for instance, an object that fell into *hekdesh*'s field and was acquired through *kinyan chatzer* without explicit verbal consecration, as the *Ramban* discusses in some contexts.

### Eating Restrictions

Returning to the *derasha* of the vineyard verse: *ve'achalta* — and you shall eat — is interpreted to mean *ve'lo motzetz*: you may eat the grapes but you may not suck out their juice, because that would constitute *shtiya* (drinking) rather than *achila* (eating). The line between the two may seem fine, but the principle appears to be that the worker's right is to eat incidentally as he works — once he begins processing the grape into juice, he has crossed from eating into something different. Similarly, *anavim* — grapes — *ve'lo anavim ve'davar acher*: you may not eat grapes together with something else. *Rashi* explains that this does not mean taking a different fruit from a nearby tree, but rather that you may not add salt or some other enhancer to the grapes to make it easier to consume larger quantities. The right to eat is not a right to strategize one's consumption. *Savecha* — to your fill — excludes *achila gassa*, forced overeating beyond satiety.

## Worker vs Owner

### Like Owner

The phrase *kenafshecha* — like yourself — is interpreted in the *braita* as: *k'nefesh shel ba'al habayit kach nafsho shel po'el* — just as the owner is, so is the worker. And the application: *mah nafsho ochel u'patur af nafsho shel po'el ochel u'patur* — just as the owner may eat from his own grapes and is exempt from *teruma u'ma'aser*, so too the worker eats and is exempt.

### Before Gmar Melacha

The reason the owner is exempt is that the produce has not yet reached the stage of *gmar melacha* that creates an obligation in *teruma u'ma'aser* — a different use of the term *gmar melacha* than the one applying to the moment of harvesting. For *terumot u'ma'asrot*, *gmar melacha* refers to the completion of the standard processing: for vegetables, trimming off the leaves and stems; for grain, threshing, winnowing, and heaping into a smoothed mound; for grapes or olives intended for wine or oil, pressing and conversion. Once processing is complete and the produce is ready for market, it becomes obligated. But something additional is also required — a concept called *kovia*, a "fixing" of the produce's status that triggers the obligation. The primary *kovia min haTorah* is *rei'yat pnei habayit* — the produce being brought before the face of the house, i.e., into the home or, as we will see, into the *chatzer*.

### Purchaser Obligation

The *chidush* in the *braita* is that while the owner and the worker are exempt before these conditions are met, a purchaser who acquires the produce out in the field may not eat it without first separating *teruma u'ma'aser*. The purchase itself, it will emerge, functions as a *kovia* — a trigger that creates the obligation. But before working out why, the Gemara must first establish what the nature of that obligation is and on what level it applies.

## Vineyard Derasha (II)

### Until Full

The *braita* interprets *savecha* — your fill — to permit eating until genuinely satisfied, while at the same time excluding *achila gassa*, eating beyond one's fill in an artificially forced manner.

### No Own Vessel

*Ve'el kelyecha lo titen* — do not place into your own vessel — is read to delineate the temporal scope of the right: *b'sha'a she'ata noten l'kelyo shel ba'al habayit ata ochel* — at the time when you are placing into the owner's vessel, you may eat; when you are not placing into the owner's vessel, you may not eat. The right attaches specifically while the worker is actively engaged in harvesting, placing produce into the owner's containers. This confirms that the right is tied to the moment of active labor during *gmar melacha* — the harvesting — and not to periods outside that work.

## Gmar Melacha & Kovia

### Rei'yat Pnei Bayit

The Gemara cites Rabbi Yannai: *ein hatevel mitchayev b'ma'aser ad sheyeira'eh pnei habayit* — untithed produce does not become obligated in *ma'aser* until it is brought before the face of the house. The source is the verse *bi'arti hakodesh min habayit* — I have removed the holy from the house — which implies that the obligation of *teruma u'ma'aser* pertains to produce once it is situated in the house.

To understand what is actually at stake, it helps to trace the full structure of the obligation. Two conditions must be satisfied before produce becomes obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*: first, *gmar melacha* — the completion of processing — and second, *kovia* — something that "fixes" the produce's status and renders it fully obligated. The primary *kovia* according to Rabbi Yannai is *rei'yat pnei habayit*. In addition to being brought into the house, other things function as *kovia*: cooking the produce, the onset of *Shabbat*, and — crucially for our *sugya* — *mecher*, a sale. These latter categories are mostly *d'rabbanan*.

### Chatzer Kovia

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and holds that even bringing the produce into the *chatzer* — the enclosed yard adjacent to the house — constitutes *kovia* at the Torah level. His source is the verse *ve'achlu bish'arecha v'save'u* — they shall eat within your gates and be satisfied — referring to the *ma'aser* given to the Levite, the stranger, the orphan, and the widow. Since *sha'arecha* refers to the city gates, and the fields lie outside the city, produce brought within the gates is treated as having been "brought in" and thus becomes obligated.

Rabbi Yannai, for his part, does not dispute that the word *bish'arecha* appears; he reads it differently. For him, it teaches that the produce must be brought in *derech sha'ar* — through the front gate — as opposed to *derech gagot v'karfifot*, i.e., through the roof or through a back enclosure. Only entry through the proper front entrance renders the produce obligated; bringing it in through a back route does not.

### Chatzer Defined

Rabbi Yochanan's position that the *chatzer* is *kovia* comes with an important qualification. His proof-text and his reasoning establish what kind of *chatzer* qualifies: *mah bayit mishtamer, af chatzer mishtamer* — just as a house is protected and enclosed, so too the *chatzer* must be protected, i.e., fenced or walled. An open, unguarded yard to which anyone has access does not have the status of a *chatzer* for these purposes. The *chatzer* must resemble a house in its degree of enclosure.

## Field Purchaser

### Field Obligation

Rabbi Chanina raises a challenge from the *braita* itself. The *braita* states *kenafshecha* — just as you are, so is the worker — deriving that just as the owner eats from his grapes and is exempt from *teruma u'ma'aser*, so too the worker eats and is exempt. The Gemara infers from this contrast: *ha-loke'ach chayav* — the implication is that a purchaser out in the field *would* be obligated. But how can a purchaser be obligated for *terumot u'ma'asrot* out in the field, before the produce has been brought to the house or even to the *chatzer*? There is no *rei'yat pnei habayit* and no *rei'yat pnei hachatzer*. This seems to show that *rei'yat pnei habayit* is not an absolute requirement.

### Fig Tree Case

Rav Pappa resolves the difficulty with a specific scenario: *hacha bite'enah ha-omedet begina venofah noteh lechatzer askinan* — the case involves a fig tree standing in the garden (*gina*), whose foliage extends over into the *chatzer*. Crucially, the *gina* is not the *chatzer*: it is a garden plot, analogous to a field, where the produce grows. The *chatzer* is the enclosed yard. For the opinion that the house is the operative location, the scenario is that the foliage extends into the house itself. Either way, the fruit that hangs within the *chatzer* or the house does satisfy the condition of *rei'yat pnei habayit* or *rei'yat pnei hachatzer*.

But then a new question arises: *ba'al habayit nami nichayiv* — if the produce is in the *chatzer*, why isn't the owner also obligated? Why is the *ba'al habayit* still exempt?

### Tree vs Fruit

The Gemara answers: *ba'al habayit einav bite'inato veloke'ach einav benofcho* — the owner's eyes are on the tree, while the purchaser's eyes are on the fruit. *Rashi* explains as follows: the owner's job — and by extension the worker's job — is to harvest the entire tree. Even if some of the fruit physically hangs in the *chatzer*, the owner and worker are conceptually located wherever the tree is, i.e., in the garden, in the field. If asked where the harvesting is taking place, they would answer: out there, where the tree stands. Their relationship is to the tree, not to this particular piece of fruit. Therefore, even though the fruit is physically within the *chatzer*, there is no *rei'yat pnei habayit* for them — conceptually, the harvest is still occurring in the field.

This reading is deeply congruent with a classic type of *Gemara* analysis: when a tree's trunk is in one legal domain and its foliage extends into another — say, the trunk is in *Eretz Yisrael* and the fruit hangs over the border into *Chutz La'aretz* — the question of where the fruit is "located" for legal purposes turns on exactly this kind of conceptual analysis. Here too: the owner says the fruit belongs to the tree and the tree is in the garden.

The purchaser, by contrast, has no interest in the tree. He bought a single apple — or a specific quantity of fruit. He is standing in the *chatzer*, he has his fruit, and he is done. Conceptually, the purchaser is in the *chatzer* with his fruit, and that satisfies *rei'yat pnei habayit*.

### Job Completion

Tosafot offers a different explanation that does not require the tree-in-the-garden setup at all, and suggests that the Gemara's scenario need not be taken literally. According to Tosafot, the real distinction is not about where the tree is located but about whether *gmar melacha* has been completed. *Gmar melacha*, says Tosafot, is not measured fruit by fruit — it is measured by the completion of the job as a whole. Even if the owner or the worker brings one apple into the house, if thousands more remain to be picked, the job is not done. There is no *gmar melacha* yet, and therefore no obligation in *terumot u'ma'asrot* regardless of where the produce physically is. The purchaser who bought only that one apple, however, is done — that is his entire purchase, his *gmar melacha* is complete — and he therefore cannot eat it without separating *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

Tosafot's reading explains why the Gemara constructs a scenario with the tree in the garden: it would seem strange for a fig tree to grow in a front yard, so the Gemara describes it as growing in the garden with the foliage reaching over — but the substantive distinction, says Tosafot, is simply job completion versus job completion.

*Rashi*'s reading is certainly the more natural *pshat* of the Gemara. But in either case, the practical bottom line — as the Gemara will note — is that on a *d'rabbanan* level, purchase itself functions as a *kovia*, substituting for *rei'yat pnei habayit*. The Gemara's elaborate constructions here are attempting to work out the distinction on a *d'oraita* level.

## Beis Hino Case

### Torah Level

The Gemara now asks a fundamental question: if we are working on a *d'oraita* level, how can a purchaser ever be obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot* from the Torah at all? A *braita* is cited: *mipnei mah charvu chanuyot shel beit hino* — why were the shops of Beit Hino destroyed three years before Jerusalem? Because they relied exclusively on the letter of Torah law and disregarded rabbinic enactment. Their practice was grounded in two *derashot*: *aser ta'aser* — you shall tithe — is followed by *ve'achalta*, you shall eat — *velo mocher*, but not one who sells. If you are selling your produce rather than eating it yourself, you have no Torah obligation of *ma'aser*. The other *derasha*: *tvuat zar'echa* — the produce of your planting — *velo loke'ach*, not one who purchases it. A buyer who did not grow the produce has no Torah obligation of *teruma u'ma'aser*.

The Gemara does not contest these derivations on a Torah level. The problem with Beit Hino was that they were ignoring the *m'derabbanan* obligation, not that their Torah-level analysis was wrong.

### Not Your Seed

The upshot is stark: *min haTorah*, only a *ba'al habayit* — the original grower — is obligated in *teruma u'ma'aser*. A seller who intends his produce for the market, not for personal consumption, is exempt, and a purchaser is exempt because the produce is not *tvuat zar'echo* — not the product of his own planting. Taken to its logical conclusion, this means that a chain of commercial transactions could, on a Torah level, result in no one ever separating *teruma u'ma'aser*. The Gemara does not contest this conclusion. It only applies to the Torah level, and the rabbis addressed it through the *d'rabbanan* obligation of purchasers and sellers alike.

### Sale Obligation

This also resolves the conceptual difficulty raised earlier: the contrast in the *braita* between the exempt worker and the potentially obligated purchaser cannot be operating at the *d'oraita* level, since a purchaser has no *d'oraita* obligation at all. The Gemara concludes that the entire contrast is *m'derabbanan* — the verse is a mere *asmachta*, a textual support for a rabbinic enactment. On the *d'rabbanan* level, *mecher* — purchase — functions as a *kovia*, and a purchaser becomes obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot* at the moment of acquisition. That is what distinguishes the purchaser from the worker in practice.

Tosafot, troubled by other *sugyot* that seem to assume purchasers are always obligated, introduces a distinction between purchasing before and after *gmar melacha*. One opinion holds that if the buyer acquires the produce before *gmar melacha* and then completes the processing himself, the produce is considered *tvuat zar'echo* — he was the owner at the critical moment — and he is obligated. But if he buys after *gmar melacha*, he is exempt. A second and ultimately more compelling opinion holds the opposite: once the original owner completes *gmar melacha*, an obligation of *terumot u'ma'asrot* inheres in the produce itself as a fixed status. Whoever subsequently holds it remains obligated. The exemption for a purchaser applies only when he acquired the produce before *gmar melacha* was reached — that is, before the critical moment when the obligation was created — and thus the obligation never attached to him as a consequence of someone else's ownership at that moment. This reading is preferable both practically — because it means that almost all commercially sold grain remains obligated, consistent with normative practice — and conceptually, because it parallels the rule that a non-Jew's ownership exempts produce only if he owns it at the moment of *gmar melacha*. The obligation is understood as a status that crystallizes at a specific moment and then inheres in the produce.

## Post-Harvest Rights

### Two Domains

Returning to the workers: the Gemara notes that, from the *pesukim* alone, we have established two domains separately — *adam b'mechubar*, a person's right to eat when produce is still attached and being harvested, and *shor b'talush*, an ox's right to eat (or more precisely, the prohibition on muzzling the ox) when produce has already been detached and is being processed in threshing. The question is how to extend each domain to the other case: how do we know a person may eat *b'talush*, and how do we know an ox may eat *b'mechubar*?

### Kal Va-Chomer

The Gemara proposes a *kal va-chomer* for the first extension: *umah shor she'ein ochel b'mechubar ochel b'talush* — an ox, which has no right to eat when produce is *mechubar*, nonetheless may eat when it is *talush*. *Adam she'ochel b'mechubar* — a person who does have the right to eat when produce is *mechubar* — should certainly have the right to eat when it is *talush* as well.

### Muzzling Only Ox

The *kal va-chomer* is rejected. *Mah leshor sheken atah metzuveh chasimato* — an ox is categorically more protected than a worker in one respect: if you muzzle an ox while it is threshing, you transgress a biblical prohibition. If you prevent a worker from eating by muzzling him — or through some equivalent stipulation — you may be violating his monetary rights, but you do not transgress the *lav* of *lo tachsom shor bedisho*. Since the ox's right to eat *b'talush* is bound up with a stricter prohibition, perhaps the ox's right is limited to the ox, and the *kal va-chomer* fails.

The Gemara then turns the logic around: *veyehei adam metzuveh chasimato mikal va-chomer mishor* — perhaps we should derive from this same *kal va-chomer* that one *does* transgress a prohibition when muzzling a worker. After all, *umah shor she'ein atah metzuveh le'hachayoto* — an ox, which you are not commanded to keep alive (you may slaughter it for food) — *atah metzuveh chasimato* — you are nonetheless prohibited from muzzling it. *Adam she'atah metzuveh le'hachayoso* — a human being, whom you are commanded to keep alive — surely you should be prohibited from muzzling him and should transgress the *lav*. The Torah deflects this with the word *kenafshecha*: just as you yourself, if you choose not to eat, do not transgress the prohibition — the prohibition applies only to an ox, not to a person.

### Kama Kama

With the *kal va-chomer* thus exhausted in both directions, the Gemara turns to a textual doubling. The word *kama* — standing grain — appears twice in the *pasuk*: once in *ki tavo bekamat re'echa* and once in *vechermesh lo tanif al kamat re'echa*. Since one occurrence suffices to tell us that a person may eat *b'mechubar*, the redundant second occurrence is redirected: *tineihu inyan le'adam b'talush* — apply it to teach that a person also has the right to eat when the produce is *talush*.

### Ki Tavo Includes

The Gemara adds an alternative source. One need not rely on the doubling at all, because *ki tavo becharme re'echa* — when you come into your friend's vineyard — does not specify what kind of work the worker is hired to do. Perhaps he was hired to carry (*likatef*) the already-harvested grapes rather than to pick them. Since the Torah grants the right to eat in the vineyard without specifying the nature of the work, it encompasses both the picker and the carrier — and the carrier is working with *talush*. This source may be more straightforward and closer to *pshat*.

## Ox Rights B'Mechubar

### Ox Attached Rights

Having established that a person has the right to eat *b'talush*, the Gemara now turns to the corresponding question: how do we know an ox has the right to eat *b'mechubar*? The *pasuk* of *lo tachsom* speaks only of threshing, which is a *talush* context.

### Keep Alive Only Humans

A *kal va-chomer* is proposed: *mah adam she'eino ochel b'talush* — a person who does not (explicitly from the *pasuk*) have the right to eat *b'talush* — *ochel b'mechubar* — does have the right to eat *b'mechubar*. *Shor she'ochel b'talush* — an ox that does eat *b'talush* — *eino din she'yochal b'mechubar*? — should certainly be permitted to eat *b'mechubar* as well. This *kal va-chomer* rests on the premise that the person does not have the *talush* right from the *pesukim*, even though we have just established that he does — so the Gemara is working here from the surface reading of the *pesukim* alone, which do not explicitly grant it.

The *kal va-chomer* is challenged: *mah adam sheken atah metzuveh le'hachayoto* — perhaps the person has the right to eat *b'mechubar* specifically because you are commanded to keep him alive. An ox, which you are not commanded to keep alive, might not have that right.

### Reverse Kal Va-Chomer

The Gemara then inverts the *kal va-chomer*: perhaps you *are* obligated to keep an ox alive, derived as follows — *umah adam she'ee atah metzuveh al chasimato* — a human being, whom you are not prohibited from muzzling — *atah metzuveh le'hachayoso* — you are nonetheless commanded to keep alive. *Shor she'atah metzuveh al chasimato* — an ox, which you are prohibited from muzzling — *eino din she'atah metzuveh le'hachayoso*? — should you not certainly be obligated to keep it alive? This would imply a prohibition on slaughtering oxen altogether — a conclusion that would have surprised the many *pesukim* permitting the consumption of meat.

### Fellow Human Only

The Torah deflects this with the verse *v'chei achicha imach* — and your fellow shall live with you: *achicha v'lo shor* — your fellow human being, not an ox. The obligation to sustain life applies only to human beings.

### Re'cha Re'cha

With the *kal va-chomer* neutralized, the Gemara turns again to textual doubling. The word *re'echa* — your friend — appears twice in the *pasuk* about harvesting standing grain: *ki tavo bekamat re'echa* and *vechermesh lo tanif al kamat re'echa*. One occurrence of *re'echa* tells us that the *adam*, the worker, may eat *b'mechubar* — that is explicit. The other occurrence of *re'echa* is likewise not needed to teach the worker's right *b'talush*, which we have already derived. So: *im eino inyan le'adam b'mechubar, teneihu inyan le'shor b'mechubar* — since it is not needed for the person, apply it to teach that an ox too may eat *b'mechubar* when it is engaged in harvesting attached produce.
