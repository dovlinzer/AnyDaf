# Sanhedrin 19b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Melech Lo Dan

### King's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֶלֶךְ לֹא דָּן וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא מֵעִיד וְלֹא מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A king does not judge and is not judged; he does not testify and is not testified against.**

We have been discussing the laws of royalty as presented in the *mishnah* and comparing them to the laws of the *kohen gadol*. The king occupies a higher status, and this affects numerous areas — including testimony and adjudication. One of the central rules is *melech lo dan v'lo danin oto*: the king neither judges nor is judged. In contemporary terms, one might compare this to diplomatic immunity on one side, and exemption from jury duty on the other.

This is, at first glance, somewhat surprising, given that *shefatenu malkenu* — judging the nation — is itself listed among the prerogatives of kingship in *mishpat hamelucha*. The resolution appears to be that the king does adjudicate in various senses, but he does not participate in the formal legal process of the *sanhedrin*. He may govern by royal decree, or he may legislate broad state policy, but he does not sit and adjudicate cases through the procedural framework of *din Torah* in the traditional manner. There may also be a distinction between *din* — the process of adjudicating a specific case under existing law — and *lishpot* in the broader sense of formulating national policy, though this remains somewhat speculative.

### Israel vs. David

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל מַלְכֵי בֵּית דָּוִד דָּן וְדָנִין אוֹתָן, דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּית דָּוִד כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ דִּינוּ לַבֹּקֶר מִשְׁפָּט״. וְאִי לָא דָּיְינִינַן לֵיהּ, אִינְהוּ הֵיכִי דָּיְינִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״הִתְקוֹשְׁשׁוּ וָקוֹשּׁוּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: קְשֹׁט עַצְמְךָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ קְשֹׁט אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **the kings of Israel,** who were violent and disobedient of Torah laws, **but** with regard to **the kings of the house of David,** the king **judges and is judged, as it is written: "O house of David, so says the Lord: Execute justice in the morning"** (Jeremiah 21:12). **If they do not judge him, how can he judge? But isn't it written: "Gather yourselves together, yea, gather together [*hitkosheshu vakoshu*]"** (Zephaniah 2:1), **and Reish Lakish says:** This verse teaches a moral principle: **Adorn [*kashet*] yourself** first, **and then adorn others,** i.e., one who is not subject to judgment may not judge others. Since it is understood from the verse in Jeremiah that kings from the Davidic dynasty can judge others, it is implicit that they can also be judged.

*Amar Rav Yosef: lo shanu ela Malkhei Yisrael, aval Malkhei Beit David dan v'danin oto.* Rav Yosef teaches that this exemption applies only to the kings of Israel — that is, the northern kingdom — but not to the kings of the Davidic dynasty. This is counterintuitive. One would have expected the more traditionally legitimate dynasty, the line of David, to enjoy greater immunity, while the kings of Israel, who split off from that line, would face stricter standards. Yet the rule runs in precisely the opposite direction.

How do we know that kings of the House of David may judge and be judged? *De-ketiv: Beit David ko amar Hashem dinu la-boker mishpat* — "House of David, thus says God: judge righteous judgment in the morning." God explicitly addresses the Davidic kings with a command to adjudicate *mishpat*, which demonstrates that they are, in principle, participants in the legal process.

From this same *pasuk* one might ask: we can derive that the Davidic king judges — but how do we learn that he is also subject to being judged? The answer follows the same logic employed earlier in the *gemara*: *ve-ketiv hitchosheshu va-khoshu* — "Gather yourselves together, and gather." If one must impose discipline on others, one must first submit to it oneself. *Amar Reish Lakish: kshot atzmekha ve-ahar kshot acherim* — prepare yourself first, and only thereafter prepare others. The same standard applies reciprocally.

### Yannai Slave Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה, דְּעַבְדֵּיהּ דְּיַנַּאי מַלְכָּא קְטַל נַפְשָׁא. אֲמַר לְהוּ שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח לַחֲכָמִים: תְּנוּ עֵינֵיכֶם בּוֹ וּנְדוּנֶנּוּ. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: עַבְדָּךְ קְטַל נַפְשָׁא. שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְהוּ. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: תָּא אַנְתְּ נָמֵי לְהָכָא, ״וְהוּעַד בִּבְעָלָיו״ אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה – יָבֹא בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר וְיַעֲמוֹד עַל שׁוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But what is the reason** that others **do not** judge **the kings of Israel?** It is **because of an incident that happened, as the slave of Yannai the king killed a person. Shimon ben Shataḥ said to the Sages: Put your eyes on him and let us judge him. They sent** word **to** Yannai: **Your slave killed a person.** Yannai **sent** the slave **to them. They sent** word **to** Yannai: **You also come here,** as the verse states with regard to an ox that gored a person to death: **"He should be testified against with his owner"** (Exodus 21:29). **The Torah stated: The owner of the ox should come and stand over his ox.**

This brings us to the *ma'aseh* that established the rule for the kings of Israel. The *gemara* explains: *mai ta'ama lo mishum ma'aseh shehayah* — the reason this exemption applies to *Malkhei Yisrael* is because of a specific incident. The incident involved the slave of King Yannai — *Yannai Malka*, who appears frequently throughout *Shas*, often alongside Shimon ben Shatach, who was, according to tradition, his brother-in-law — whose slave killed a person. Shimon ben Shatach addressed the *chachamim*: *tnu eineichem bo venidunenu* — "Turn your eyes to him and let us judge him."

They sent word to King Yannai: *avdach katal nafsha* — "Your servant has killed a person." They instructed him to send the slave to be judged. Then they went further and summoned the king himself: *ta ant nami lehacha* — "You must come here as well." The legal basis for this is the principle of *ve-hu'ad biv'alav*: *amrah Torah yavo ba'al ha-shor ve-ya'amod al shoro* — the Torah requires that the owner of an ox be present at the trial of a *shor haniskal*. The comparison of the slave to the ox is, admittedly, a discomfiting one, but the legal principle being invoked is that one whose property causes harm bears a legal stake in the proceedings and must be present. This concept has obvious parallels in the legal doctrine of *respondeat superior*, where an employer bears liability for harm caused by an employee acting in the course of his duties — though the precise contours of that parallel are a separate discussion.

### Shatach & Yannai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא וִיתֵיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: יַנַּאי הַמֶּלֶךְ, עֲמוֹד עַל רַגְלֶיךָ וְיָעִידוּ בָּךְ. וְלֹא לְפָנֵינוּ אַתָּה עוֹמֵד, אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם אַתָּה עוֹמֵד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָמְדוּ שְׁנֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר לָהֶם הָרִיב וְגוֹ״. אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא כְּשֶׁתֹּאמַר אַתָּה, אֶלָּא כְּמָה שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ חֲבֵרֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to narrate the incident: Yannai **came and sat down. Shimon ben Shataḥ said to him: Yannai the king, stand on your feet and** witnesses **will testify against you. And** it is **not before us** that **you are standing,** to give us honor, **but** it is **before the One Who spoke and the world came into being** that **you are standing, as it is stated: "Then both the people, between whom the controversy is, shall stand** before the Lord, before the priests and the judges that shall be in those days" (Deuteronomy 19:17). Yannai the king **said to him:** I will **not** stand **when you** alone **say** this to me, **but according to what your colleagues say,** and if the whole court tells me, I will stand.

King Yannai came and took his seat. Shimon ben Shatach then addressed him directly: *Yannai HaMelech, amod al raglecha ve-ya'idu bach* — "King Yannai, stand on your feet, and let them testify against you." The legal basis for this demand is the *pasuk*: *ve-amdu shnei ha-anashim asher lahem ha-riv lifnei Hashem* — the litigants must stand before God. Shimon ben Shatach was careful to add: *ve-lo lifneinu atah omed ela lifnei mi she-amar vehayah ha-olam atah omed* — "It is not before us that you stand, but before the One who spoke and the world came into being." He is deflecting any suggestion of personal presumption: the king is not being required to stand out of deference to the judges themselves, but because God is the ultimate backdrop of all judicial proceedings.

King Yannai replied: *lo keshetomar atah ela kemah sheyomru chaveirecha* — "I will not act on your word alone; I want to hear what your colleagues say."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִפְנָה לִימִינוֹ – כָּבְשׁוּ פְּנֵיהֶם בַּקַּרְקַע. נִפְנָה לִשְׂמֹאלוֹ – וְכָבְשׁוּ פְּנֵיהֶם בַּקַּרְקַע. אָמַר לָהֶן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: בַּעֲלֵי מַחְשָׁבוֹת אַתֶּם, יָבֹא בַּעַל מַחְשָׁבוֹת וְיִפָּרַע מִכֶּם! מִיָּד בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וַחֲבָטָן בַּקַּרְקַע, וּמֵתוּ. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: מֶלֶךְ לֹא דָּן וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא מֵעִיד וְלֹא מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Shimon ben Shataḥ **turned to his right.** The judges **forced their faces to the ground** out of fear and said nothing. **He turned to his left, and they forced their faces to the ground** and said nothing. **Shimon ben Shataḥ said to them: You are masters of thoughts,** enjoying your private thoughts, and not speaking. **May the Master of thoughts,** God, **come and punish you. Immediately,** the angel **Gabriel came and struck** those judges **to the ground, and they died. At that moment,** when they saw that the Sanhedrin does not have power to force the king to heed its instructions, the Sages **said: A king does not judge** others **and** others **do not judge him,** and **he does not testify and** others **do not testify concerning him,** due to the danger of the matter.

Shimon ben Shatach turned to the right. The judges averted their eyes downward — *ve-chavshu pneihem bakarka* — burying their faces toward the ground. He turned to the left; the same silence. Shimon ben Shatach said to them: *ba'alei machshavot atem* — you are full of thoughts, but you do nothing. *Yavo Ba'al Hamachshavot*, the ultimate Reader of thoughts, which is God, *ve-yifra mikem* — and He will exact justice from you. And indeed, *miyad ba Gavriel ve-chavatan bakarka va-metu* — the angel Gabriel immediately came and struck them to the ground, and they died.

*Ve-otah sha'ah amru: melech lo dan ve-lo danin oto, lo meyid ve-lo meyidin oto.* It was at that hour that the Sages declared: a king neither judges nor is judged, neither testifies nor is testified against.

The reasoning is striking. One might have expected this rule to have been generated by something directly involving the king's own dignity or conduct. Instead, it arose from a case involving a slave — the king's property. And yet that makes the point even more powerfully: even an indirect connection to the king, even a matter involving his property, was sufficient to paralyze the court. When the king's presence intimidates the judges to the point where justice cannot be administered, the process itself becomes meaningless. Hence the sweeping ruling.

It is worth noting that even if one takes this *ma'aseh* as the origin of the rule, it applies only to *Malkhei Yisrael*, since that is the context in which it occurred. The *malkhei Beit David* remain bound by the *pasuk* requiring them to judge and be judged, and a *ma'aseh shehayah* is generally interpreted narrowly — it governs only the circumstances that produced it, especially where there is countervailing scriptural evidence.

## King vs. Scholar

### Judging Authority

Turning to the *Tosafot* on 19a, the third *Tosafot* raises a difficulty: if *Malkhei Beit David dan v'danin oto* — they can judge and be judged — how can this be, given that we established earlier that one does not seat a king in the *sanhedrin*? The basis for that prohibition is the *pasuk*: *lo ta'aneh al riv* — interpreted to mean that one does not contradict the *mufla shebebeit din*, the most senior authority on the court. If the king is present, the other judges may be reluctant to disagree with him, which undermines the integrity of the process.

The *Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between types of cases: *hacha mairi davka ledun dinei mamonot* — this refers specifically to monetary cases. In *dinei mamonot*, the stakes are lower, and judges feel less constrained about contradicting even a king. By contrast, in *dinei nefashot* — capital cases — the matter is different, and even standard procedure requires that deliberation begin with the most junior judge rather than the most senior, precisely to avoid undue influence. So the king's participation is permitted, but only in *dinei mamonot*.

### Standing Honor

The adjacent *Tosafot* takes up a conceptual comparison between the *talmid chacham* and the *melech* with respect to the obligation to stand. The premise of the question is: a *talmid chacham* is not required to stand before others in certain contexts; should it not follow, *kal vachomer*, that the king, who is even more exalted, is equally exempt? The *Tosafot* rejects this framing. The answer lies in the *pasuk* cited earlier: *som tasim alecha melech she'tehei eimato alecha* — you must appoint a king over you, and his *eimah* — his aura of authority — must rest upon you at all times. This obligation is constant and cannot be waived.

### Torah Acquisition

Why, then, can the *talmid chacham* be *mochel* on his *kavod* — waive the honor due him — while the king cannot?

The *Tosafot* offers a penetrating distinction: *ki kevodo ba lo mishum de'mitvat hamakom kach hu* — the king's honor is extrinsic. It does not originate in the king himself; it is conferred upon him by divine command. One inherits kingship; it descends through lineage, not through personal achievement. Since the *kavod* comes from God, the king cannot unilaterally *hafkiah mitvat hamakom* — annul a Divine obligation. It is not his to waive.

The *talmid chacham*, by contrast, possesses *torato dilei* — his Torah is his own. He has struggled, invested, and acquired it through personal effort. As the *pasuk* puts it: *ki im betorat Hashem cheftzo* — initially, Torah is described as God's Torah; *uvetorato yehgeh yomam valaylah* — but once one has truly acquired it, it is called his Torah. *Bitechilah nikret al shemo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu, ulevasof nikret al shemo* — first it is called on God's name, and ultimately it is called on the name of the one who learned it. Since the *talmid chacham* earned this status through his own toil, he has standing to relinquish it as he sees fit — *shelo hi*, it is genuinely his. The king's honor, by contrast, belongs to God. This is, one might note, a striking conceptual asymmetry: earned accomplishment carries with it the prerogative of renunciation, while inherited or externally imposed status does not.

## Mitzvot Exemptions

### Chalitzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא חוֹלְצִין וְכוּ׳. אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר נָשִׂיא שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, מֶלֶךְ שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – אֵין כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׂוֹם תָּשִׂים עָלֶיךָ מֶלֶךְ״, שֶׁתְּהֵא אֵימָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ! מִצְוָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the king **does not perform *ḥalitza*** with his brother's widow **and** his brother **does not perform *ḥalitza*** with his wife, and Rabbi Yehuda says that he may do so if he wishes. The Gemara challenges Rabbi Yehuda's opinion: **Is that so? But doesn't Rav Ashi say: Even according to the one who says** that with regard to **a *Nasi* who relinquished** the **honor** due **him, his honor is relinquished,** nevertheless, with regard to **a king who relinquished** the **honor** due **him, his honor is not relinquished, as it is stated: "You shall set a king over you"** (Deuteronomy 17:15), meaning **that his fear should be upon you.** The preservation of a king's honor is mandated by the Torah. How could Rabbi Yehuda allow him to waive it? The Gemara answers: **A mitzva is different;** a king is not disgraced if he relinquishes his honor to perform a mitzva.

The *mishnah* continues: *lo chalitzah ve-lo choltzin* — a king neither performs *chalitzah* nor does anyone perform *chalitzah* with him. *Chalitzah*, as is well known, involves a degree of public humiliation — the widow removes the *yavam*'s sandal and spits, if not directly at him then in his direction and before the court. This does not comport with the *kavod* of kingship.

A *beraita* is cited: *amar Rav Ashi, afilu man d'amar nasi shemachal al kevodo kevodo machul, melech shemachal al kevodo ein kevodo machul* — even according to the opinion that a *nasi* who waives his honor, his honor is considered waived, a king who waives his honor — his honor is not waived. The source is again *som tasim alecha melech she'eimato alecha*: the *pasuk* demands that the king's *eimah* rest upon the people at all times, which means his *kavod* must remain intact regardless of his own wishes.

One might object: even if this applies in ordinary situations, surely a *mitzvah* should override the concern for royal dignity — *mitzvah shani*. This argument does appear as a position in the *mishnah*. *Rabbi Yehudah omer: im ratzah lachlots o le'yabbem, l'zachur latov* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that if the king wishes to perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*, he is to be remembered favorably for doing so. The *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda thus disagree on whether the concern for royal dignity overrides even a *mitzvah* obligation.

### King's Widow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נוֹשְׂאִין כּוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נָשִׁים הָרְאוּיוֹת לוֹ מִבֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ. וּמַאי נִינְהוּ? מֵירַב וּמִיכַל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And no one may marry** the king's widow, and Rabbi Yehuda says that a king may marry another king's widow, as proven by King David, who was promised with regard to King Saul after his death: "And I have given you the house of your master and the wives of your master" (II Samuel 12:8). **It is taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **said to Rabbi Yehuda:** The meaning of the verse is not that David married Saul's widows, but that he married **women appropriate for him from the house of the king. And who are they? Merab and Michal,** the daughters of Saul.

Similarly, *ve-ein nos'im almenato* — one does not marry the widow of a king. This, too, is connected to the inviolability of royal *kavod*. In the ancient world, taking the wives of a predecessor was a well-understood symbol of usurpation. When Avshalom rebelled against David, his attempt to take David's wives was a declaration of sovereignty — those were fighting words, a coup in symbolic form.

*Rabbi Yehudah omer: nosei melech almenato shel melech* — Rabbi Yehuda permits one king to marry the widow of another king, not a commoner. His proof text: *ve-etnah lecha et beit adonecha ve-et neshei adonecha b'cheikecha* — God said to David, "I will give you the house of your master and the wives of your master into your lap." The word *neshei adonecha*, on a straightforward reading, refers to wives — *eshet cheiko*, the language of marital intimacy.

The response to Rabbi Yehuda is: *nashim eten lecha mibeit hamelech* — the word *nashim* here means women, not wives specifically. *U'mani hu? Merav u'Michal* — and who are these women? Merav and Michal, the daughters of Shaul. So the *pasuk* does not authorize marrying a king's widows at all; it refers to David being given Shaul's daughters in marriage. This reading, admittedly, is counterintuitive at first glance — *beit adonecha* and *neshei adonecha b'cheikecha* would naturally evoke a different meaning — but that is the *gemara*'s resolution. And once this *pasuk* is repurposed to refer to Merav and Michal, it opens the door to a broader discussion of David's marriages to Shaul's daughters.

## David & Saul's Daughters

### Merav & Michal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: הֵיאַךְ נָשָׂא דָּוִד שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת בְּחַיֵּיהֶן? אָמַר לָהֶן: מִיכַל אַחַר מִיתַת מֵירַב נְשָׂאָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: קִידּוּשֵׁי טָעוּת הָיוּ לוֹ בְּמֵירַב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּנָה אֶת אִשְׁתִּי אֶת מִיכַל אֲשֶׁר אֵרַסְתִּי לִי בְּמֵאָה עׇרְלוֹת פְּלִשְׁתִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a discussion about David's marriage to Merab and Michal from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Sota* 11:9): **Rabbi Yosei's students asked him: How did David marry two sisters while they were** both **alive?** Rabbi Yosei **said to them: He married Michal** only **after the death of Merab,** which is permitted. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says** a different explanation: **His betrothal to Merab was in error** and therefore void from the outset, and so Michal was permitted to him. This is **as it is stated** in the words of King David to Saul's son Ish-bosheth: **"Deliver me my wife Michal, whom I betrothed to me for one hundred foreskins of the Philistines"** (II Samuel 3:14).

Shaul had two daughters: Merav, the elder, and Michal, the younger. The *gemara* now proceeds to untangle the complex and somewhat tortured history of David's relationship to both of them.

The immediate question is: if *nashim* in the *pasuk* refers to Merav and Michal, and David married both of them, how is that possible? They were sisters. The Torah prohibits marrying two sisters while both are alive — *shnei achayot b'chayeihen*.

Two answers are offered. *Amar lei: Michal achar mot Merav nasa* — the first answer is that David married Michal only after Merav had died, so the two marriages did not overlap. The second answer, offered by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, is that one of the betrothals was a *kiddushei ta'ut* — a mistaken or flawed betrothal — and therefore David was never fully married to one of them.

### Sisters Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: ״מִיכַל אִשְׁתִּי״, וְלֹא מֵירַב אִשְׁתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is **the** biblical **derivation** here? How does Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa learn from this verse that King David's betrothal to Merab was in error? **Rav Pappa says:** In the verse, David indicates: **Michal** is **my wife but Merab** is **not my wife.**

The *gemara* pursues the question of which betrothal was the problematic one and what exactly rendered it defective. The proof that there was something wrong with the *kiddushin* to Merav is derived from a *pasuk*: *shene'emar tena et ishti et Michal asher erasti li beme'ah orlot Plishtim* — "Give me my wife Michal, whom I betrothed with a hundred foreskins of the Philistines." David says *ishti et Michal* — my wife, Michal. *Amar Pappa: Michal ishti, lo Merav ishti* — he is specifying Michal as his wife, thereby implying that Merav is not his wife. This supports the position that the betrothal to Merav was invalid.

### Goliath Prize

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קִידּוּשֵׁי טָעוּת? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יַכֶּנּוּ יַעְשְׁרֶנּוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ עֹשֶׁר גָּדוֹל וְגוֹ׳״. אֲזַל קַטְלֵיהּ. אָמַר לוֹ: מִלְוָה אִית לָךְ גַּבַּאי, וְהַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוֶה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** caused the betrothal between David and Merab to be **a mistaken betrothal?** The Gemara responds: **As it is written** about Israel's war against the Philistines and Goliath: **"And it shall be that the man who kills him, the king will enrich him with great riches** and will give him his daughter, and make his father's house free in Israel" (I Samuel 17:25). David **went** and **killed** Goliath. King Saul **said to him: You have a loan** in **my** possession, as I owe you the great wealth that I promised, though David had not given him an actual monetary loan. **And** the *halakha* is that with regard to **one who betroths** a woman **by** forgiving **a loan, she is not betrothed,** and therefore David's betrothal of Merab did not take effect.

What was the background of the betrothal to Merav? The *pasuk* records: *vehayah ha'ish asher yakenu ya'asrenu hamelech osher gadol* — whoever defeated Goliath would be greatly enriched by the king, and the king would give his daughter to that man as a wife. David killed Goliath. So far so good — the betrothal should have been valid.

The problem is the mechanics of the *kiddushin*. *Kiddushin* requires *kesef, shtar*, or *biah* — money, a document, or cohabitation. In this case, David proposed to use the *osher gadol* — the prize money that Shaul owed him — as the *kesef kiddushin*. But he never actually received the money. The money remained a debt owed to him. *Amar lo: milve yesh li gavach, hamekadesh b'milve eina mekudeshet* — one who betroths a woman with a loan owed to him has not effected a valid *kiddushin*. Since the money never passed through David's hands, it could not serve as the consideration for the betrothal.

Shaul, in the meantime, gave Merav to someone else entirely. *Dichtiv: vayehi be'et tet et Merav bat Shaul leDavid vehia nitna l'Adriel haMechollati l'isha* — Merav was given to Adriel the Mecholathite as a wife.

### Foreskins Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל יַהֲבַהּ לְעַדְרִיאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בְּעֵת תֵּת אֶת מֵירַב בַּת שָׁאוּל לְדָוִד וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי בָּעֵית דְּאֶתֵּן לָךְ מִיכַל, זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי מֵאָה עׇרְלוֹת פְּלִשְׁתִּים. אֲזַל אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה אִית לָךְ גַּבַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** Saul **went** and **gave** Merab **to Adriel, as it is written: "But it came to pass at the time when Merab, Saul's daughter, should have been given to David,** that she was given to Adriel the Meholathite as a wife" (I Samuel 18:19). Saul **said to** David: **If you want me to give you Michal, go bring me one hundred foreskins of the Philistines** (see I Samuel 18:25–27). David **went** and **brought** Saul two hundred foreskins. Saul **said to him:** Even though you brought the foreskins, the betrothal is not valid, as **you,** David, **have a loan and** one ***peruta*** in **my** possession, i.e., the wealth Saul owed him for slaying Goliath as well as the item of lesser monetary value, the foreskins of the Philistines.

The story then continues. Shaul made a second offer: if you want Michal, go and bring me a hundred foreskins of the Philistines — *zil aiti li me'ah orlot Plishtim*. David went and brought them. Now, David had two things to offer for the *kiddushin*: the *milveh* — the outstanding debt from the Goliath affair — and the *pruta*, meaning the foreskins themselves.

*Amar lei: milve upruta it lach gavai* — "You have a loan and a *pruta* owed by me." The question becomes: what was David's *da'at* — his intent — when he offered these two items? *Shavyah milve upruta da'ateih amilve* — if he had in mind primarily the *milveh*, then the *kiddushin* still fails, since the *milveh* cannot serve as *kesef kiddushin*. *Milve upruta da'ateih apruta* — but if his intent was focused on the *pruta*, i.e., the foreskins themselves, then the *kiddushin* succeeds, provided the foreskins have the minimum value of a *pruta*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאוּל סְבַר: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה – דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַמִּלְוָה, וְדָוִד סְבַר: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה – דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַפְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** Saul and David had a halakhic dispute on this point: **Saul reasoned** that in the case of one who betroths a woman **by** forgiving **a loan and** giving her one ***peruta*, his mind** is focused **on the loan** and not on the additional *peruta*, and therefore the betrothal is not valid. **And David reasoned** that in the case of one who betroths a woman **by** forgiving **a loan and** giving her one ***peruta*, his mind** is focused **on the *peruta*** and therefore the betrothal is valid.

And here is where Shaul and David disagreed. Shaul held *lo chazi l'midei* — the foreskins are worthless, not even worth a *pruta*. What use does anyone have for two hundred foreskins? David held *chazu lei, chavi l'shunrei* — they are worth something; you can feed them to household animals, which saves you the cost of their feed. Meat is meat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה – דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַפְּרוּטָה. שָׁאוּל סְבַר: לָא חֲזוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי, וְדָוִד סְבַר: חֲזוֹ לְכַלְבֵי וְשׁוּנָּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Everyone** reasons that in the case of one who betroths a woman **by** forgiving **a loan and** giving her one ***peruta*, his mind** is focused **on the *peruta*. Saul reasoned** that foreskins of Philistines **are not fit for any** purpose and as such are worth not even one *peruta*, and that consequently the betrothal did not take effect. **And David reasoned** that **they are fit for dogs and cats** as food and as such are worth at least one *peruta*, and therefore the betrothal takes effect.

This is a relevant conceptual point for *kiddushin* more broadly. The reason the practice under the *chuppah* is to use a plain gold ring rather than an elaborate gemstone is related to this concern. If a bride believes the ring is worth ten thousand dollars and it is actually a cheap imitation worth far less, questions arise about whether her consent was given on accurate information, which could affect the validity of the *kiddushin*. By using a simple ring of known minimal value and having witnesses confirm it is worth at least a *pruta*, these concerns are preempted from the outset.

### Merav Bore

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הַאי ״תְּנָה אֶת אִשְׁתִּי אֶת מִיכַל״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ מִקְרָאוֹת מְעוֹרָבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Yosei,** who explains that David married Michal after the death of Merab, with regard to **this** verse: **"Deliver me my wife Michal"** (II Samuel 3:14), **what** does he **derive from it?** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yosei** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Sota* 11:8): **Rabbi Yosei would derive** meaning from **mixed verses** that seem contradictory.

We now return to Rabbi Yose, who holds the first position: that David actually married both Merav and Michal sequentially — Merav first, and after her death, Michal. According to this view, there is no *kiddushei ta'ut*; the betrothal to Merav was valid. But then what does David mean when he says *et ishti et Michal* — singling out Michal as his wife? Rabbi Yose would say he is simply being specific, as Yaakov said *Rachel bitcha haktana* — specifying which daughter he wanted.

*Rabbi Yose l'ta'amei: d'tanya, Rabbi Yose haya doresh mikra'ot m'rubavin* — Rabbi Yose was known for interpreting verses that appear mixed or interchanged, reading them as internally rearranged in order to yield a coherent meaning.

### Proof Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶת שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי רִצְפָּה בַת אַיָּה אֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה לְשָׁאוּל אֶת אַרְמֹנִי וְאֶת מְפִיבֹשֶׁת וְאֶת חֲמֵשֶׁת בְּנֵי מִיכַל אֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה לְעַדְרִיאֵל הַמְּחֹלָתִי וְגוֹ׳״. וְכִי לְעַדְרִיאֵל נִתְּנָה? וַהֲלֹא לְפַלְטִי בֶּן לַיִשׁ נִתְּנָה! דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשָׁאוּל נָתַן אֶת מִיכַל בִּתּוֹ אֵשֶׁת דָּוִד לְפַלְטִי בֶן לַיִשׁ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* continues. **It is written: "But the king took the two sons of Rizpah, daughter of Aiah, whom she bore unto Saul, Armoni and Mephibosheth, and the five sons of Michal,** daughter of Saul, **whom she bore to Adriel,** son of Barzillai **the Meholathite"** (II Samuel 21:8). **But did** Saul **give** Michal **to Adriel? But didn't he give her to Palti, son of Laish, as it is written: "Now Saul had given Michal his daughter, David's wife, to Palti, son of Laish"** (I Samuel 25:44)?

The *gemara* uses this transposition as a *hekesh* — a textual analogy: *makish kiddushei Merav l'Adriel l'kiddushei Michal l'Palti* — just as Michal's marriage to Palti was *ba'aveirah*, a transgression (since she was really David's wife), so too Merav's marriage to Adriel was *ba'aveirah* (since she too was really David's wife). The point of the comparison is not to excuse either marriage, but to demonstrate through the juxtaposition that David's betrothal to both women was valid — which in turn establishes that he was married to both, and consequently that one must have died before he married the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַקִּישׁ קִידּוּשֵׁי מֵירַב לְעַדְרִיאֵל לְקִידּוּשֵׁי מִיכַל לְפַלְטִי: מָה קִידּוּשֵׁי מִיכַל לְפַלְטִי – בַּעֲבֵירָה, אַף קִידּוּשֵׁי מֵירַב לְעַדְרִיאֵל – בַּעֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* continues: The first verse does not mean, then, that Michal married Adriel. **Rather,** the verse **compares Merab's betrothal to Adriel to Michal's betrothal to Palti: Just as Michal's betrothal to Palti** was effected **in transgression,** according to all opinions, since she was already married to David, **so, too, Merab's betrothal to Adriel** was effected **in transgression,** since according to *halakha* she was betrothed to David.

### Korcha's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״אֶת חֲמֵשֶׁת בְּנֵי מִיכַל בַּת שָׁאוּל״? אָמַר לְךָ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְכִי מִיכַל יָלְדָה? וַהֲלֹא מֵירַב יָלְדָה! מֵירַב יָלְדָה וּמִיכַל גִּידְּלָה, לְפִיכָךְ נִקְרְאוּ עַל שְׁמָהּ. לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁכׇּל הַמְגַדֵּל יָתוֹם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ יְלָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa as well, isn't it written: "And the five sons of Michal, daughter of Saul,** whom she bore to Adriel" (II Samuel 21:8). **Rabbi Yehoshua** ben Korḥa could have **said to you** to understand it this way: **And did Michal give birth** to these children? **But didn't Merab give birth** to them for Adriel? Rather, **Merab gave birth** to them and died, **and Michal raised** them in her house. **Therefore,** the children **were called by her name, to teach you that** with regard to **anyone who raises an orphan in his house, the verse ascribes him** credit **as if he gave birth to him.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha asks his own question on this *pasuk*: *ve-chi Michal yalda? Halo Merav yalda* — did Michal give birth to these children? Surely it was Merav who bore them, not Michal! Michal, as the text elsewhere records, had no children. The resolution is: *Merav yaldah, Michal giddelah, ve-kach nikreu al shemah* — Merav bore these children, and Michal raised them as an orphaned aunt after Merav died, and therefore they are called after Michal's name.

This brings the *gemara* to a broader principle.

## Raising Orphans

### Raising Teaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלַמֶּדְךָ: שֶׁכׇּל הַמְגַדֵּל יָתוֹם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ יְלָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This teaches us that **anyone who raises an orphan in his home, the Torah accounts it to him as if he had given birth to that child.**

*Lelamedcha: shekol hamigadel yatom betoch beito, ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu yelado* — this teaches us that whoever raises an orphan in his home, the Torah accounts it to him as if he had given birth to that child. Michal's raising of Merav's children is the proof text for this principle, and the *gemara* proceeds to elaborate upon it through a series of expansions.

### Ruth Naomi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אוֹמֵר, מֵהָכָא: ״וַתִּקְרֶאנָה לוֹ הַשְּׁכֵנוֹת שֵׁם לֵאמֹר יוּלַּד בֵּן לְנׇעֳמִי״. וְכִי נָעֳמִי יָלְדָה? וַהֲלֹא רוּת יָלְדָה! אֶלָּא רוּת יָלְדָה וְנׇעֳמִי גִּידְּלָה, לְפִיכָךְ נִקְרָא עַל שְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** Proof for the aforementioned statement can be derived **from here: "And the neighbors gave him a name, saying: There is a son born to Naomi"** (Ruth 4:17). **And did Naomi give birth** to the son? **But didn't Ruth give birth** to him? **Rather, Ruth gave birth and Naomi raised** him. **Therefore, he was called by her name:** "A son born to Naomi."

Rabbi Chanina derives the same principle from the Book of Ruth. *Vatikerena lo hashenot shem lemor: yulad ben le-Naomi* — the neighbors declared, "A son has been born to Naomi." But the son was born to Ruth, not to Naomi. So why is he called Naomi's son? *Ruth yalda, Naomi gidla, u'lefikach nikra al shemah* — Ruth bore him, Naomi raised him, and therefore he was called after Naomi's name.

One might wonder what exactly Ruth's role was in all this, since she was very much alive. Perhaps there is something to the suggestion that Ruth's status as a *Moaviyah* made the community reluctant to credit her publicly, and so the child was attributed to Naomi, the Israelite grandmother. But in any case, the principle holds: the one who raises a child receives the credit, even when the birth mother is still living.

### Bitya Moses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְאִשְׁתּוֹ הַיְהֻדִיָּה יָלְדָה אֶת יֶרֶד אֲבִי גְדוֹר וְגוֹ׳ וְאֵלֶּה בְּנֵי בִּתְיָה בַת פַּרְעֹה אֲשֶׁר לָקַח (לוֹ) מָרֶד״. מָרֶד זֶה כָּלֵב, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מֶרֶד? שֶׁמָּרַד בַּעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים. וְכִי בִּתְיָה יָלְדָה? וַהֲלֹא יוֹכֶבֶד יָלְדָה! אֶלָּא יוֹכֶבֶד יָלְדָה וּבִתְיָה גִּידְּלָה, לְפִיכָךְ נִקְרָא עַל שְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Proof for the aforementioned statement can be derived **from here: "And his wife Hajehudijah gave birth to Jered the father of Gedor,** and Heber the father of Soco, and Jekuthiel the father of Zanoah, **and these are the sons of Bithiah, daughter of Pharaoh, whom Mered took"** (I Chronicles 4:18). **Mered is Caleb, and why was his name called Mered? Because he rebelled [*marad*] against the counsel of the spies.** And according to the midrash, Jered, Heber, and Jekuthiel all refer to Moses our teacher. **And did Bithiah give birth** to Moses? **But didn't Jochebed give birth** to him? **Rather, Jochebed gave birth** to him **and Bithiah raised** him. **Therefore, he was called by her name** as though she had given birth to him.

Rabbi Yochanan draws the same principle from a verse in Chronicles. *Ve-ishto ha'Yehudiyah yalda et Yered avi Gedor* — his Jewish wife bore Yered, and the text continues listing names that the *gemara* identifies as epithets for Moshe. The *Yehudiyah* is identified as Bitya bat Paroh.

The chronology here is dizzying. Calev ben Yefuneh, who married Bitya bat Paroh, was forty years old among the *meraglim* and was sustained for another forty years in the wilderness — as the *pasuk* records, *kechochi az kachochi ata*, his strength was undiminished. Moshe was eighty years old when he stood before Paroh — *Moshe ben shemonim shanah ve-Aharon ben shalosh u'shemonim shanah b'daberam el Paroh*. So Moshe was twice Calev's age, and yet Bitya bat Paroh, who had drawn Moshe from the Nile when he was an infant, was, on this reading, eventually married to someone far younger than Moshe himself. The timelines do not resolve easily.

Regardless: *Yocheved yalda, Bitya gidla, u'lefikach nikra al shemah* — Yocheved bore Moshe, Bitya raised him, and therefore Moshe is reckoned as her child. This is the principle at work. Note that in this case, Moshe was not an orphan — his birth mother was alive and was even, unknowingly, hired as his nursemaid. The principle of raising conferring parenthood applies even when the birth parent is living.

### Support & Teach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״גָּאַלְתָּ בִּזְרוֹעַ עַמֶּךָ בְּנֵי יַעֲקֹב וְיוֹסֵף סֶלָה״. וְכִי יוֹסֵף יָלַד? וַהֲלֹא יַעֲקֹב יָלַד! אֶלָּא, יַעֲקֹב יָלַד וְיוֹסֵף כִּילְכֵּל, לְפִיכָךְ נִקְרְאוּ עַל שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** Proof for the aforementioned statement can be derived **from here: "You have with Your arm redeemed your people, the children of Jacob and Joseph, Selah"** (Psalms 77:16). **And did Joseph sire** all of the children of Israel? **But didn't Jacob sire them? Rather, Jacob sired** them **and Joseph sustained** them financially. **Therefore, they were called by his name;** all of Israel were called the children of Joseph.

The principle now expands beyond literal orphan-raising. Rabbi Elazar cites the verse: *ga'alta bizroa amecha b'nei Yaakov v'Yosef sela* — "You redeemed with Your arm Your people, the children of Yaakov and Yosef." Why does Yosef appear alongside Yaakov? *V'chi Yosef yalad? Ela Yaakov yalad v'Yosef kilkel* — Yosef did not father these people; Yaakov did. But Yosef *kilkel* — he sustained and supported the entire family when they descended to Egypt. *Lefikach nikru al shmo* — therefore they are called after his name as well.

The principle has now extended beyond raising to merely providing for someone's sustenance. Supporting a person financially — *kilkul* — is sufficient to render one, in a sense, their parent.

### Torah Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד בֶּן חֲבֵירוֹ תּוֹרָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ יְלָדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵלֶּה תּוֹלְדֹת אַהֲרֹן וּמֹשֶׁה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאֵלֶּה שְׁמוֹת בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״. לוֹמַר לָךְ: אַהֲרֹן יָלַד וּמֹשֶׁה לִימֵּד, לְפִיכָךְ נִקְרְאוּ עַל שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Anyone who teaches another** person's **son Torah, the verse ascribes him** credit **as if he sired him, as it is stated: "Now these are the generations of Aaron and Moses"** (Numbers 3:1), **and it is written** immediately afterward: **"And these are the names of the sons of Aaron:** Nadav the firstborn and Avihu, Eleazar, and Ithamar" (Numbers 3:2), but it does not mention the names of Moses' children. This serves **to say to you** that **Aaron sired** his children, **but Moses taught** them Torah. **Therefore,** the children **were** also **called by his name.**

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol hamelamed ben chavero Torah, ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu yelado* — whoever teaches his fellow's son Torah, the verse accounts it to him as if he gave birth to him. The source: *ve-eleh toldot Aharon u'Moshe* — "These are the generations of Aharon and Moshe." But then the text continues only with the names of Aharon's sons, making no mention of Moshe's children. The answer: *Aharon yalad u'Moshe limed, lefikach nikru al shmo* — Aharon fathered them biologically, but Moshe taught them Torah, and therefore they are also called Moshe's generations.

### Joseph & Jacob

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָכֵן כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ אֶל בֵּית יַעֲקֹב אֲשֶׁר פָּדָה אֶת אַבְרָהָם״, וְכִי הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ בְּיַעֲקֹב שֶׁפְּדָאוֹ לְאַבְרָהָם? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שֶׁפְּדָאוֹ מִצַּעַר גִּידּוּל בָּנִים. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא עַתָּה יֵבוֹשׁ יַעֲקֹב וְגוֹ׳״, ״לֹא עַתָּה יֵבוֹשׁ יַעֲקֹב״ – מֵאָבִיו, ״וְלֹא עַתָּה פָּנָיו יֶחֱוָרוּ״ – מֵאֲבִי אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another derivation connected to child-rearing: **"Therefore, so says the Lord to the house of Jacob, who redeemed Abraham;** Jacob shall not now be ashamed, neither shall his face now wax pale" (Isaiah 29:22). **But where have we found** any indication **about Jacob that he redeemed Abraham? Rav Yehuda says:** It means **that he redeemed him from the suffering of raising children,** in that Abraham did not have twelve tribes and the resultant hardships involved in raising them, as Jacob did, as Jacob assumed the burden of raising the tribes of Israel. **And this is as it is written: "Jacob shall not now be ashamed,** neither shall his face now wax pale," meaning: **"Jacob shall not now be ashamed" before his father,** and **"neither shall his face now wax pale" before his father's father,** since he took upon himself the role that they bore as well.

The expansion continues with one further example. *Ve-chen hu omer: asher padah Avraham* — "who redeemed Avraham." The verse associates Yaakov with having redeemed Avraham. But in what sense did Yaakov redeem Avraham? *Amar Rav Yehuda: she'pado m'tza'ar gidul banim* — Yaakov freed Avraham from the anguish of childbearing. God had promised Avraham that he would father multitudes. From Sarah, he had only one child. Yaakov, by producing the twelve *shevatim*, fulfilled that promise in Avraham's stead — he bore the generational burden that Avraham could not complete on his own. In this sense, Yaakov redeemed Avraham from the unmet obligation. Therefore, *lo ata yeivosh Yaakov me'aviv, ve-lo ata panav yeichavaru me'avi aviv* — Yaakov will not be ashamed before his father Yitzchak, nor before his grandfather Avraham — because he fulfilled what they could not.

## Palti Restraint

### Name Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״פַּלְטִי״ וּכְתִיב ״פַּלְטִיאֵל״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פַּלְטִי שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ פַּלְטִיאֵל? שֶׁפִּלְּטוֹ אֵל מִן הָעֲבֵירָה. מָה עָשָׂה? נָעַץ חֶרֶב בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ. אָמַר: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בְּדָבָר זֶה יִדָּקֵר בֶּחָרֶב (זֶה).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a tradition with regard to Palti, son of Laish: **It is written** in one place **"Palti"** (I Samuel 25:44), **and it is written** in another place **"Paltiel"** (II Samuel 3:15). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Palti** was **his** real **name, and why was his name called Paltiel?** To teach **that God [*El* ] saved [*pelato*] him from the sin,** by giving him the insight that he may not touch Michal, understanding that she was still David's wife and therefore forbidden to him. **What did he do? He embedded a sword** in the bed **between him and her,** and **said: Anyone who engages in this matter,** i.e., sexual intercourse, **should be stabbed by this sword.**

The *gemara* now returns to Palti ben Laish, the man to whom Shaul gave Michal after taking her from David. *Kesiv Palti u'chesiv Paltiel* — the text alternates between the names *Palti* and *Paltiel*. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: lamah nikra shemo Paltiel? She'pelato E-l* — God saved him, *palat*, from an *aveirah*. The *aveirah* in question was the prohibition against sleeping with a married woman — and according to the view that David's *kiddushin* to Michal was valid, she remained his wife even while living in Palti's house.

### Sword Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ אִתָּהּ אִישָׁהּ״? שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה לָהּ כְּאִישָׁהּ. וְהָכְתִיב: ״הָלוֹךְ וּבָכֹה״? עַל מִצְוָה דַּאֲזַיל מִינֵּיהּ. ״עַד בַּחֻרִים״, שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּבַחוּרִים, שֶׁלֹּא טָעֲמוּ טַעַם בִּיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **But isn't it written: "And her husband went with her,** weeping as he went, and followed her to Bahurim" (II Samuel 3:16), referring to Palti as Michal's husband? The Gemara responds: This means **that he became like a husband for her** through his affection and concern for her. The Gemara counters: **But isn't it written** in that very verse: **"weeping as he went"?** If from the outset he thought that she was David's wife, why was he crying? The Gemara responds: He was weeping **about the mitzva that left him,** as from now on, he would receive no reward for restraining his desire. The end of the verse says that they went **"to Bahurim,"** meaning **that they both became like young men [*baḥurim*]** in **that they did not taste sexual intercourse** at all.

How was Palti saved? *Mah asah? Na'atz cherev beino l'veinah* — he took a sword and drove it into the ground between himself and Michal. *Amar: kol ha'osek b'davar zeh yidaker b'cherev zeh* — he declared: whoever reaches toward the other for the purpose of relations will be pierced by this sword. Through this means, they coexisted in the same household without transgressing.

The verse then records: *vayeilech itah ishah bacho* — her husband walked after her weeping. One might ask: if they never had relations, in what sense was he her *ish*, her husband? The answer is *ishta d'lah hi* — he regarded her as his wife in his heart. And why was he weeping? *Al mitzvah d'azla mineih* — he was crying over the *mitzvah* that was departing from him. He had sustained himself through an extraordinary test of restraint, and as she was taken from him, he mourned not the loss of a woman, but the loss of the ongoing opportunity to perform this *mitzvah* of self-discipline. *Vayeilech achareiah ad Bachurim* — he followed her until *Bachurim*, and the *gemara* notes: *Bachurim* can be read as suggesting *bachurim* — young people — *she'na'asu sheneihem k'vachurim shelo ta'amu ta'am bi'ah* — for both of them had remained like youths who had never experienced relations with one another.

### Trial Compare

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תּוֹקְפּוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף – עִנְוְותָנוּתוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹעַז, תּוֹקְפּוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹעַז – עִנְוְותָנוּתוֹ שֶׁל פַּלְטִי בֶּן לַיִשׁ. תּוֹקְפּוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף – עִנְוְותָנוּתוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹעַז, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בַּחֲצִי הַלַּיְלָה וַיֶּחֱרַד הָאִישׁ וַיִּלָּפֵת״. מַאי ״וַיִּלָּפֵת״? אָמַר רַב: שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה בְּשָׂרוֹ כְּרָאשֵׁי לְפָתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Joseph's power** is the **humility of Boaz,** as Joseph is praised for showing strength with regard to an accomplishment that was insignificant for Boaz (see Genesis, chapter 39). Likewise, **Boaz's power** is the **humility of Palti, son of Laish,** as Palti's capacity for restraint was greater still. **Joseph's power** is the **humility of Boaz, as it is written** about Boaz: **"And it came to pass at midnight that the man was startled and turned himself,** and behold, a woman lay at his feet" (Ruth 3:8). **What** is the meaning of **"and turned himself [*vayyilafet*]"? Rav says:** The meaning is **that his flesh became like the heads of turnips [*lefatot*],** his sexual organ hardening out of arousal, but even though Ruth was not married he refrained from engaging in intercourse with her; while Joseph had to exert more effort, despite the fact that Potiphar's wife was married.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan: tokpo shel Yosef anvetanuto shel Boaz, v'tokpo shel Boaz anvetanuto shel Palti ben Laish.* The comparison is striking. One might take it to mean: Yosef's *tokef*, his power, comes out looking like the *anavah* (humility) of Boaz when set against it. And Boaz's power looks like Palti's humility by comparison.

How so? Yosef resisted the advances of his master's wife — a married woman, a single incident, a moment of acute temptation. Boaz faced a different trial. The verse in Ruth records: *vayehi bachatzot halaylah vayecherad ha'ish vayilafes* — in the middle of the night, Boaz trembled and recoiled. *Mai vayilafes? Amar Rava: she'na'asa b'saro k'roshei lefasos* — his flesh reacted involuntarily. But Boaz's trial involved a single woman — Ruth — and a single night — and she was not married. That was Boaz's trial.

Palti ben Laish, by contrast, lived with Michal night after night, year after year. She was, on this account, a married woman — another man's wife. He overcame this trial not once, but continuously, over an extended period of time. That, the *gemara* tells us, was a greater *nisayon* than anything Yosef or Boaz faced.
