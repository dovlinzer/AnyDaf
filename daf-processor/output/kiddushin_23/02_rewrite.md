# Kiddushin 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eved Knaani: Kesef, Shta…

### Eved Knaani Status

The *daf* opens by situating the *eved Knaani* within a broader taxonomy of *kinyanim*. The *mishnah* has just concluded its treatment of *kiddushei isha*, *yibum*, and *eved Ivri* — forms of acquisition or obligation that pertain to persons with varying degrees of legal personhood. The *eved Knaani* represents an altogether different category. On one hand, he is treated as fully fungible property: if an object is placed upon an *eved Knaani*, it is as though it were placed on the master's chattel, and the master acquires it through *kinyan chatzer*. On the other hand, the *Torah* insists on a revolutionary principle — this slave remains a human being. He goes free if the master destroys his eye or tooth; in certain circumstances the master may even be *chayav misa*. This dual status, simultaneously property and person, is the defining tension of the entire *sugya*.

### Three Kinyanim

The *mishnah* states: *eved Knaani nikneh be-kesef u-ve-shtar u-ve-chazakah* — a *Canaanite slave* is acquired through money, a legal document, and *chazakah*, an act of taking possession. The *mishnah* further establishes the mechanisms by which the slave may acquire his own freedom. *Ve-koneh et atzmo be-kesef al yedei acheirim* — he may be redeemed through money provided by others, provided the master agrees to accept it. The dynamic here is analogous to *kesef kiddushin*: the amount is entirely at the master's discretion, but once the agreed-upon sum changes hands, the slave goes free.

Regarding the *shtar*: *u-ve-shtar al yedei atzmo, divrei Rabbi Meir* — a writ of manumission (*shtar shichrur*), in which the master writes *harei ata ben chorin*, is delivered directly to the slave. Whether this precludes delivery to others is a question to be resolved in the *gemara*.

*Ve-chachamim omrim: be-kesef al yedei atzmo* — the Sages hold that the slave may redeem himself even with his own money, *u-ve-shtar al yedei acheirim* — and the writ may be delivered through others. The *mishnah* adds: *u-vilvad she-yihyeh ha-kesef mi-shel acheirim* — even when the slave is himself handing money to the master, that money must have originated with others. This is not self-contradictory: *al yedei atzmo* means he himself is the one delivering the payment, but since any money he earns automatically belongs to his master, the only money he can legitimately call his own is money given to him by outsiders — for example, as a gift explicitly designated for him rather than for his master.

### Comparison to Karka

The *gemara* asks *minalan* — what is the biblical source for acquiring an *eved Knaani* through *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*? The derivation comes from the verse *ve-hisnachaltem otam li-vneichem achareichem la-reshet achuzah* — you shall bequeath them to your children after you as hereditary property (*Vayikra* 25:46). The *katuv* equates the slave to an *sdeh achuzah*, an ancestral field: just as land is acquired through money, document, and *chazakah*, so too is an *eved Knaani*. The comparison to *karka* is deliberate and carries broad halakhic consequences, as throughout *Shas* there is a recurring discussion of whether an *eved Knaani* shares the legal status of *karka* in various contexts.

One might raise the objection: if the *eved Knaani* is like a field, perhaps he should also return to his original owner in the *Yovel*, just as an *sdeh achuzah* does. The *gemara* responds with the verse *le-olam bahem ta'avodu* — you shall work them forever. Unlike *eved Ivri*, for whom the word *le-olam* implied release at *Yovel* given an additional *pasuk*, here there is no such indication, and so the slave remains a permanent acquisition. Crucially, however, he is acquired in the same manner as land — by *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*. It is also worth noting that *chazakah* did not appear among the methods of acquiring an *eved Ivri*: that *chazakah* — the most demonstrative act of asserting property-like control — is specific to *eved Knaani* underscores just how fully he is treated as property.

### Chalipin Method

A *baraita* adds: *af be-chalipin* — an *eved Knaani* may also be acquired through *chalipin*, whether through an actual exchange of objects or through the symbolic handkerchief transaction in which the acquiring party takes the handkerchief and thereby effects the transfer of ownership. Why did the *tanna* of the *mishnah* omit this? *Milta de-leita be-metaltelin katani, milta de-ita be-metaltelin lo katani* — the *mishnah* wished to list only those modes of acquisition that are distinctive to *karka* and not to *metaltelin* (movable goods). *Chalipin* works for both *karka* and *metaltelin*, and even works for *karka* — though notably not for *ishah* — so it does not belong to the set of *karka*-specific *kinyanim* that the *mishnah* seeks to highlight. The outer *tanna* of the *baraita*, who does include *chalipin*, was willing to broaden the list to anything that works for *karka*, even if it also works for *metaltelin*.

## Meshicha & Chazakah

### Shmuel on Meshicha

*Amar Shmuel: eved Knaani nikneh bi-mshichah* — Shmuel holds that an *eved Knaani* may be acquired by *meshicha*, that is, by physically dragging him toward the buyer. This is unmistakably a *kinyan* of *metaltelin*, not of *karka*. The *gemara* defines how it works: *takfo u-va etzlo kna-o* — if you grabbed hold of him and pulled him, you acquire him; *kara-o u-va atzlo lo kna-o* — if you merely called him and he came on his own, you did not acquire him. This distinction will require explanation shortly.

### Why No Meshicha?

The *gemara* observes that Shmuel's ruling coheres with the *mishnah*'s silence on *meshicha*: since *meshicha* is a *kinyan* of *metaltelin*, the *mishnah* — which limited itself to *kinyanim* distinctive to *karka* — naturally omitted it. By the same logic, the outer *tanna* of the *baraita*, who included *chalipin*, still restricted his list to methods that operate for *karka* as well; *meshicha* does not apply to *karka*, so even that *tanna* would not have included it. The omission is thus explained across all opinions. Everyone concedes, it seems, that *meshicha* works — but the *mishnah* and *baraita* were organized around a different taxonomic concern.

### Dehumanization Debate

This is, however, a striking *chiddush* of Shmuel's, and it is worth pausing over its conceptual significance. The *mishnah* grounds the acquisition of an *eved Knaani* in the *pasuk* comparing him to *karka*. Why is *karka* the operative comparison? One might suggest that *karka* was chosen precisely because of the theological resonance of land-ownership: *la-Hashem ha-aretz*, the earth belongs to God; no one truly owns land absolutely, it reverts in *Yovel*. There remains an irreducible gap between the landowner and his land. Movable property is different — once it is in your pocket, in your house, under your full control, the ownership is total and the gap disappears. To treat an *eved Knaani* as *karka* would thus mean that he is never fully objectified; the master's ownership, like ownership of land, retains an element of incompleteness. By contrast, *kinyanim* of *metaltelin* — *meshicha*, *hagbahah* — assert a kind of absolute, intimate dominion that more completely reduces the slave to an object. Shmuel's move to permit *meshicha* therefore deepens the dehumanizing logic rather than ameliorating it, even if Shmuel's intent was purely technical.

### Animals vs. Slaves

This conceptual tension is sharpened by a *baraita* that the *gemara* cites in the context of *meshicha* by animals. How does *meshicha* work for an animal? Grabbing it by the hoof, the hair, the saddle, the saddlebag, the bit, or the bell around its neck constitutes *mesira*. As for *meshicha* itself: calling the animal and having it come, or striking it with a stick so that it runs — once it lifts a leg and takes a step, the acquisition is complete. According to some *tannaim*, it must travel a distance equal to its full height. The critical point is that for animals, calling and having the animal come *does* work as *meshicha*. Why does the same not apply to a slave?

### Will & Personhood

*Behema da'atah de-marah azlah* — an animal has no independent will; when it comes in response to a call, it is simply responding instinctively to the controller's directive, and so the controller's *da'at* is driving the movement. *Eved da'atah de-nafsheih azlah* — the slave, however, decides to listen. Even in a condition of servitude, even where the choice is severely constrained, the slave exhibits something that looks like will, like personhood. Calling him and having him come does not demonstrate that you have taken him as an object under your control, because he is still the one choosing to move toward you. To objectify him — to make him, for the purposes of *kinyan*, like a piece of movable goods — you must physically seize him and drag him. And yet that very act of seizing and dragging is, as observed above, far more dehumanizing. The rule that *meshicha* requires physical dragging thus rests on an acknowledgment of the slave's subjectivity, even as the *kinyan* itself treats him as an object.

### Minor Slave Exception

*Amar Rav Ashi: eved katan ke-vehema dami* — if the slave is a minor, we do not assume he is exercising genuine will; he is more like an animal in this regard. Accordingly, if you call a minor slave and he comes, that would constitute a valid *meshicha* for purposes of acquisition.

## Chazakah Examples

### Service Acts

*Tanu rabbanan: keitzad be-chazakah* — the Rabbis teach how *chazakah* functions for an *eved Knaani*. For *karka*, *chazakah* is performed by improving or working the land, such as plowing or repairing a fence. For a slave, the demonstrative act goes in one direction only: the slave performs an act of personal service for the master. The *baraita* enumerates examples: removing the master's shoe (*hin'ilo*), carrying the master's bathing implements to the bathhouse (*holich keilav acharav le-veit ha-merchatz*), undressing the master (*hifshito*), bathing him (*hirchitzo*), anointing him with oil (*sacho*), scraping him (*gerado*), dressing him again (*hilbisho*), and putting his shoe back on. The preponderance of bathhouse scenarios likely reflects the deeply personal, servile nature of the service required — these are acts that only one who stands in a relationship of submission would perform for another.

Whether this list is exhaustive or merely illustrative — whether, for instance, having the slave mow a lawn would also suffice — is not explicitly resolved, though the pattern suggests that any act of personal service would qualify.

### Higbiho & Gevira

The *baraita* continues: *higbiho* — if he lifted him up — *kna-o*, he acquires him. The *gemara* initially finds this ambiguous. *Rabbi Shimon* objects: *lo tehei chazakah gedolah me-hagbahah* — *chazakah* should not be superior to *hagbahah*, which after all effects acquisition in all contexts (*hagbahah konah be-chol makom*). *Rav Ashi* clarifies: the *higbiho* in the list means *higbiho hu le-rabbo kna-o* — the slave lifted the master, which is consistent with the pattern of the slave performing a service. But if the master lifted the slave, *lo kna-o* — that would not constitute *chazakah*. Rabbi Shimon's objection, then, is precisely that if the master lifts the slave, that should work as *hagbahah* — the same *kinyan* that operates for all movable goods. This is, in miniature, the same debate as Shmuel's broader claim: *Rabbi Shimon* among the *tannaim* was already arguing for the applicability of *kinyanei metaltelin* to an *eved Knaani*.

### Bia & Chazakah

Having established that the slave lifting the master constitutes a valid *chazakah*, the *gemara* raises a provocative *hava amina*: *ela me'atah shifchah Knaanit tikneh be-viah* — might sexual intercourse with a female slave constitute a *chazakah* for her acquisition? The reasoning, formally at least, follows from the prior case: in the act, the slave's body supports and effectively elevates the master's body, which could be characterized as a form of *hagbahah*. Moreover, she is performing a kind of service.

### Rejection of Bia

The *gemara* rejects this on the basis that the standard logic of *chazakah* through lifting requires that the act be one-sided: *zeh neheneh ve-zeh mitzta'er* — one party benefits while the other is burdened. Ordinary *hagbahah* is uncomfortable for the one being made to lift; it demonstrates the master's power by imposing effort on the slave. In the case of *bi'ah*, the *gemara* says *zeh neheneh ve-zeh neheneh* — both parties experience pleasure, which disqualifies it from the structure of *chazakah*. The *gemara* then considers whether *shelo ke-darka* (anal intercourse) might change the analysis, since the *gemara* assumes the woman does not derive pleasure from that act. *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba de-min Archei* challenges even this assumption, asking how we know that both parties do not in fact experience pleasure in that act. The *gemara* responds: since the verse uses the formulation *mishkevei ishah*, which is the source for equating *shelo ke-darka* with *ke-darka* across *kol ha-Torah kulloh*, if *ke-darka* does not work for this purpose, *shelo ke-darka* does not either. The entire *hava amina* is, as one must acknowledge, deeply disturbing — not least because it proceeds without directly confronting the reality of rape — but the *gemara*'s technical rejection is firm.

## Hindua Case

### Ger Death Scenario

The *gemara* presents a famous case study. *Rav Yehuda Hindua* — *Rashi* explains that *Hindua* renders *Kush*, translated variously as Ethiopia or India, meaning he was a convert from a distant land — was a *ger* who had no heirs, having converted without subsequently having children. He became gravely ill and was clearly near death. *Mar Zutra* came to pay a *bikkur cholim* visit and observed that the man was *takif leih alma* — the world had seized him, meaning he was at the very threshold of death.

The halakhic significance of the moment is immediately apparent. When a *ger* dies without heirs, his property becomes ownerless — *hefker*. His slaves, who had been owned by him, are at that instant no longer owned by anyone, and can therefore acquire themselves as *benei chorin*. To prevent this, a potential acquirer must already be in the middle of a valid *chazakah* at the precise moment of death, so that there is no intervening moment in which the slave becomes free.

*Mar Zutra* therefore said to the slave of *Rav Yehuda Hindua*: take off my shoe (*shalof li masanai*) and bring it to my house (*ve-amtinhu le-veitei*). The journey to the house would take some twenty minutes. If the *ger* died while the slave was in the middle of this act of service, the slave would have had no moment of freedom — he passes directly from performing *chazakah* for *Mar Zutra* without ever becoming ownerless.

### Adult vs. Minor

*Ika d'amri*: some say the slave was an adult (*gadol*). In that reading, *Rashi* explains, the moment the master dies, the adult slave transitions immediately to the control of the living — he is transferred over. *Ve-ika d'amri*: others say the slave was a minor (*katan*). The *Rashi* text reads *u-de-lo ke-Abba Shaul* — not in accordance with *Abba Shaul* — though most other *rishonim* have the *girsa ke-Abba Shaul*.

The *baraita* is cited: if a *ger* died and Jews subsequently seized his property, and among that property were slaves — whether adult or minor — they acquire themselves as *benei chorin*, because there was a gap in time between the death and the seizure. Even a minor slave can acquire himself in that gap. This is the position of the *tanna kamma*. *Abba Shaul* dissents: only adult (*gedolim*) slaves acquire themselves, because an adult has a *yad* — a capacity for legal acquisition of himself. Minors lack that *yad*, and so if there is a gap, their status remains unresolved and anyone who takes possession of them at that point acquires them.

### Tosafot Logic

*Tosafot* and many other *rishonim* read the *girsa* as *ke-Abba Shaul*, and offer a different explanation for why *Mar Zutra* had to be in mid-*chazakah* at the moment of death. Even according to *Abba Shaul*, where a minor cannot acquire himself in the gap, *Mar Zutra* still needed to be in possession at that precise instant — not to prevent the slave from acquiring himself, but to prevent another person from beating him to the acquisition. The story thus reflects a race against other potential claimants, not only against the slave's own potential freedom.

### Gadol vs. Katan

*Tosafot* goes further, however, and argues that the case *must* have involved a *katan*, not a *gadol* — and that this is precisely the point of the reading *ke-Abba Shaul*. Even if *Mar Zutra* had been performing *chazakah* at the exact moment of death, a *gadol* would nonetheless go free. At a deep halakhic and conceptual level, an adult's ownership of himself takes precedence over any *chazakah* being performed upon him at that very moment. The slave's fundamental self-possession reasserts itself the instant the master dies, overriding even a concurrent act of external acquisition. Only in the case of a *katan*, who according to *Abba Shaul* cannot acquire himself, does the *chazakah* of another party prevail. This reading gives the case sharp halakhic logic: the story works only if the slave is a minor, and only according to *Abba Shaul* who holds that minors cannot acquire themselves.

## Rabbi Meir Debate

### Rabbi Meir Rules

The *mishnah* now turns to the mechanisms of self-redemption, and here *Rabbi Meir* and the *Chachamim* fundamentally disagree. According to *Rabbi Meir*: *be-kesef al yedei acheirim* — redemption by money requires that the money come from others, not the slave himself; *u-ve-shtar al yedei atzmo* — and the writ of manumission must be delivered directly to the slave. The *gemara* must establish what case is contemplated here and why these particular rules follow.

### No Slave Money

The *gemara* works through *Rabbi Meir*'s position. Is the case one where the third party pays the money without the slave's knowledge or consent (*shelo mi-da'ato*)? A difficulty arises immediately: *Rabbi Meir* holds *chov hu le-eved she-yetzei mi-yad rabo le-cheirus* — that liberation may actually be a *chov*, a detriment, from the slave's perspective. A freed slave takes on additional obligations, loses certain privileges, and may be worse off in various ways; therefore one cannot say *zachin* (operate on someone's behalf without their consent) when it is not clear that freedom is a benefit. A third party cannot receive a *shtar shichrur* on behalf of the slave unless the slave has agreed. So the redemption through third-party money must in fact be *mi-da'ato* — with the slave's knowledge and consent.

What, then, is the *chiddush* of the *mishnah*? *Al yedei acheirim in, adidech atzmo lo* — the news is that only others' money can redeem him; his own money cannot. *Alma ein kinyan le-eved belo rabbo* — according to *Rabbi Meir*, a slave has no proprietary capacity independent of his master. Any money he earns belongs to the master automatically; he cannot possess money in his own right.

### Gito ve-Yado

This raises a sharp question. If the slave cannot own anything — since his hand is, as it were, like his master's hand — how can the master give him a *shtar shichrur* directly? Anything placed in the slave's hand is as though it were placed in the master's own hand. The answer is *gito ve-yado ba'im ke'echad*: in the very moment that the *shtar shichrur* reaches the slave's hand, he simultaneously acquires it and acquires himself. The manumission and the acquisition happen as a single instantaneous event, so there is no logical problem of the slave's hand being an extension of the master's at the moment of receipt. This is the *chiddush* of the *mishnah*'s ruling that *shtar* is *al yedei atzmo* — even though he cannot normally own anything, he can receive his own *shtar shichrur* by virtue of this principle.

But the *mishnah* then seems to be read as excluding third-party delivery of the *shtar*: *al yedei atzmo* but not *al yedei acheirim*. That is puzzling: since the slave has agreed to be freed (*mi-da'ato*), why can a third party not receive the *shtar* on his behalf? The *gemara* brings a *baraita*: *be-shtar al yedei atzmo ve-lo al yedei acheirim, divrei Rabbi Meir* — only *al yedei atzmo*, explicitly excluding others. The reason emerges from the subsequent analysis.

### Abbaye: Kesef Shelo M'Da…

*Amar Abbaye le-olam shelo mi-da'ato* — Abbaye proposes a fundamental reorientation: in fact, the third-party money works even *without* the slave's consent. *Ho'il ve-kana-o be'al korcho, makni-o be'al korcho* — since the slave was originally sold against his will, he can be freed against his will. The act of purchasing a slave is done without requiring the slave's agreement, and therefore the parallel act of purchasing his freedom is likewise valid without his agreement.

### Sale-Freedom Parallel

The conceptual architecture of Abbaye's argument is that the *kesef* that acquires and the *kesef* that frees are the same kind of instrument doing the same kind of work; the slave's will is simply not a variable in money transactions involving him. It is a striking and somewhat counterintuitive move — freedom is normally something one would want — but Abbaye's point is structural: the *kinyan* logic of *kesef* operates identically in both directions.

The *gemara* challenges: if so, why not free him with a *shtar* delivered by a third party *shelo mi-da'ato* as well, since he was acquired by a *shtar* against his will? *Ha-i shtar la-chud ve-ha-i shtar la-chud* — no, a *shtar* of sale is fundamentally different in character from a *shtar* of manumission. They cannot be equated across the two contexts. By contrast, money is money: *tiv'a mi-chad hu* — it is all the same coin. So the symmetry argument works for *kesef* but not for *shtar*. The conclusion under Abbaye: the *mishnah* is describing a case *shelo mi-da'ato*; third-party *kesef* works even without the slave's consent; third-party *shtar* does not.

### Rava: Master Accepts

*Rava* offers an alternative explanation for why third-party *kesef* works without requiring *zachin*. *Kesef kabbalat rabbo garma lo* — in a *kesef* redemption, what actually effects the liberation is the master's acceptance of the money. The third party who provides the funds is not, in the halakhic sense, *representing the slave* — he is simply providing the instrument. The master's act of taking the money is the operative moment of freedom. Therefore the principle of *zachin* — which governs representation of a person's interests — is never implicated. No one needs to act as the slave's agent, because the slave is not the actor in this transaction.

*Shtar kabbalat acheirim garma lo* — a *shtar*, however, effects liberation through the act of receipt. The natural recipient is the slave himself; if a third party receives it instead, that third party is necessarily acting as the slave's representative, which requires *zachin* and therefore the slave's prior consent. Under Rava's formulation, then, *shtar al yedei acheirim* would work *mi-da'ato* — if the slave agrees, a third party can certainly receive the *shtar* on his behalf. But it will not work *shelo mi-da'ato*, because representation requires consent.

## Sages Position

### Slave's Own Money

*Ve-chachamim omrim: be-kesef al yedei atzmo* — the Sages hold that a slave may redeem himself with his own money. This immediately implies a foundational disagreement with *Rabbi Meir*: the *Chachamim* maintain that a slave can indeed own money, at least in certain circumstances, independently of his master.

### Kesef Both Ways

The *gemara* asks whether *al yedei atzmo* is exclusive — only his own money, not others'. This seems unlikely: the *Chachamim* hold that freedom is a *zachut* and that *zachin le-adam shelo be-fanav* — one may act on another's behalf when it is clearly to his benefit, even without his knowledge. Surely others could also provide the money. The *gemara* resolves: *mai al yedei atzmo, af al yedei atzmo* — the *mishnah*'s formulation means "even through his own money," not "exclusively through his own money." The *chiddush* of the *Chachamim* is the ruling that *yesh kinyan le-eved be-lo rabbo* — a slave can own property in his own right, making self-redemption through his own funds possible. Third-party money certainly also works under the *Chachamim*'s view.

### Shtar by Others

For *shtar*, the *Chachamim* say *al yedei acheirim* — the writ is delivered to others. The *gemara* interprets this as meaning others can receive it on his behalf — which is straightforwardly explicable by *zachin*: since freedom is a *zachut*, third parties can receive the *shtar* for the slave's benefit without his even being present.

### Gito ve-Yado Q

But this raises a sharp question. The *Chachamim* hold that a slave *can* own money. If so, they presumably also accept *gito ve-yado ba'im ke'echad*, since that principle is needed to explain how the slave can receive his own *shtar*. Why then does the *mishnah* under the *Chachamim*'s position say *al yedei acheirim* rather than allow both? If *al yedei acheirim* means "even *al yedei acheirim*" — i.e., additionally to *al yedei atzmo* — then the *mishnah* should have formulated it to cover both together. But if *al yedei acheirim* means "only *al yidei acheirim*," then why can the slave not receive the *shtar* himself, given that the *Chachamim* recognize both his capacity for ownership and the principle of *gito ve-yado*?

### Rabbi Shimon

The *gemara* answers: *ve-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar hi* — this reflects the position of *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, as taught in a *baraita*: *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer: af be-shtar al yedei acheirim ve-lo al yedei atzmo* — the *shtar* may only be delivered to others, not directly to the slave. This is a distinct position that requires its own explanation, which *Rava* will provide.

### Three Core Issues

*Ve-shalosh machlokos ba-davar* — there are in fact three independent debates embedded in this *mishnah*, though the *rishonim* differ on how exactly to align them. The first is the question of *zachin* versus *chov*: are the *Chachamim* correct that freedom is a *zachut* (permitting third-party action without consent), or does *Rabbi Meir* have the better of the argument that it is a *chov* (requiring the slave's consent)? The main practical consequence of this debate is whether *shtar al yidei acheirim* can work without the slave's consent. The second debate concerns whether a slave can own money at all: *Rabbi Meir* says *ein kinyan le-eved be-lo rabbo*, the *Chachamim* say *yesh kinyan*. This determines whether *kesef al yidei atzmo* is possible. The third debate is whether *gito ve-yado ba'im ke'echad* applies — whether the slave can receive a *shtar* directly and thereby acquire himself simultaneously. This governs *shtar al yidei atzmo*. *Rashi* reads the *Chachamim* as actually endorsing both *al yidei atzmo* and *al yidei acheirim* for *shtar*, with the *mishnah*'s phrasing *al yidei acheirim* merely alluding to the minority position of *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* rather than endorsing it.

### Get Analogy

*Amar Rava: mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar?* — why does *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* hold that you cannot give the *shtar* directly to the slave? *Gamar lah la-mah me-ishah* — he derives it by analogy to the *get* given to a woman. *Mai ishah — ad she-yotzi get li-reshut she-eino shelo* — when a husband gives a *get*, he gives it to a domain that is not his own. The woman is a separate legal entity, outside the husband's *reshut*. *Af eved nami — ad she-yotzi get li-reshut she-eino shelo* — so too the *shtar shichrur* must be given to a domain outside the master's own. The slave himself, being entirely within the master's legal domain, cannot be that recipient; the *shtar* must be given to a third party who genuinely stands outside the master's ownership. Although there is the principle of *gito ve-yado ba'im ke'echad*, *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — and by extension *Rava*'s explanation — holds that the slave's hand does not attain true independence of domain in the relevant sense: the woman in the *get* is not the property of the husband to the same degree that the slave is the property of the master, and this distinction makes the analogy work in a specific direction. This point is noted as requiring further development.
