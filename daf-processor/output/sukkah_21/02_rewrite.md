# Sukkah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bed in Sukkah

### Intro: Second Perek

Today's daf is 21, and with it we begin the second perek. It is admittedly difficult to identify precisely what distinguishes this perek from the first. While the new perek appears to open on a fresh topic — not the structure of the *sukkah* per se, but the ways in which one may sit within a valid *sukkah* yet still fail to fulfill the *mitzvah* — it quickly returns to questions of structure. The bulk of the second perek will continue to address issues of the *sukkah*'s construction, with the question of the *mitzvah* of sitting in the *sukkah* introduced only toward the end.

### Mishna: Bed Negates

The *mishna* appears at the bottom third of 20b. The opening line reads: *hayashen tachat hamita basukkah* — one who sleeps underneath a bed in the *sukkah*. It is worth pausing on the choice of activity. When the *mishnayos* discuss whether one is *yotzei*, the paradigmatic activity they address is sleeping. Eating, drinking, and sleeping are all primary activities — as Rashi makes explicit: *ikar yeshivat hasukkah achilah shtiyah v'shinah* — but sleeping is treated as the primary one, since even a brief nap must be performed in the *sukkah*, whereas a casual, non-meal snack may be eaten outside. The *mishna* thus uses sleeping to test the question of whether a person fulfills the obligation even within a technically valid *sukkah*.

The ruling is *lo yatza yedei chovato* — one who sleeps under a bed in the *sukkah* has not fulfilled the obligation. Rashi explains: *d'ohel mafsik beino l'sukkah* — the top of the bed functions as a separate roofing, a distinct *ohel*, and it interposes between the person and the *schach* of the *sukkah*. The person is effectively beneath the bed's roof rather than the *sukkah*'s roof.

### R. Yehuda: Elders Silent

Rabbi Yehuda dissents. He invokes the same form of evidence we saw earlier in the first perek — the argument from silence. *Nohagin hayinu shehayinu yeshenim tachat hamita bifnei hazekenim v'lo amru lanu davar*: it was our practice to sleep under beds in the *sukkah* in the presence of the elders, and they never said a word of objection. This is reminiscent of how, earlier in the tractate, Rabbi Yehuda cited the story of Helni Hamalka and the tall *sukkah* to demonstrate that the *zekenim* tacitly approved of a position that his colleagues disputed. Here he employs the same rhetorical strategy — the silence of the sages constitutes implicit halachic endorsement.

### R. Shimon: Tavi Story

Rabbi Shimon responds with a counter-narrative. *Maaseh b'Tavi avdo shel Rabban Gamliel* — there was an incident involving Tavi, the slave of Rabban Gamliel, who was sleeping underneath a bed in the *sukkah*. Rabban Gamliel turned to those same *zekenim* — the very elders whose silence Rabbi Yehuda wished to cite as a proof — and said: *re'item Tavi avdi shehu talmid chacham v'yodeia she'avadim pturim min hasukkah* — have you seen Tavi my servant? He is such a Torah scholar that he knows slaves are exempt from the *sukkah* — *l'fichach yashen hu tachat hamita* — and that is precisely why he sleeps under a bed.

Rabbi Shimon's point is incisive: far from the elders' silence indicating approval of sleeping under a bed as a valid fulfillment of the *mitzvah*, the episode with Rabban Gamliel clarifies that sleeping under a bed is something only an exempt individual would do. The *mishna* concludes — *shayashen tachat hamita lo yatza yedei chovato* — that sleeping under a bed does not constitute fulfillment of the obligation.

### Tosafot: Tavi Analysis

Tosafot raises a pointed question regarding Tavi. Tavi, the *eved kasher* of Rabban Gamliel, is described in the *Yerushalmi* in the second perek of Berachos as one who would even don *tefillin*, and the sages did not object. Yet here, in the context of the *sukkah*, Rabban Gamliel seems to ensure Tavi sleeps under the bed — which Tosafot understands as the sages effectively keeping him from sitting in the *sukkah* as a fulfillment of the *mitzvah*. How can the same authorities who permitted Tavi to wear *tefillin* simultaneously prevent him from performing the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*?

Tosafot resolves this: Tavi was not forced under the bed out of a principled objection to his participation. Rather, the *sukkah* was simply too crowded — *shelo lidchok es hachachamim*, so as not to squeeze out the sages who were obligated. There was not enough room for all those who were *chayav* in the *sukkah*, so Tavi voluntarily took the space under the bed. A further question presses: if that was the reason, why did he not simply go outside the *sukkah* entirely? The answer is that he wished to remain close in order to hear the Torah discussion — *lirtzos hayah lishmoa divrei chachamim*. He was not merely an *eved kasher* in the sense of knowing his exemption; he was an *eved kasher* who preferred eavesdropping on the sages from beneath the bed to removing himself from their presence entirely. It should also be noted that Tavi was an *eved Kenaani*, not an *eved Ivri* — which is precisely why he was *patur min hasukkah*. An *eved Ivri* would have been *chayav*. And based on the *Gemara* in Bava Kamma and the *Gemara* in Berachos, which discusses the question of whether one accepts condolences for the death of slaves, it appears that Tavi died still in the status of an *eved*, without having been freed.

## Ten Tefachim & Bed

### Gemara: 10 Tefachim

The *Gemara* raises an immediate challenge to the *mishna*'s ruling: *veha lika asarah* — why should sleeping under a bed in a *sukkah* even be a problem? Presumably a bed does not have ten *tefachim* of height from the floor to its underside, and one would think that ten *tefachim* of height are required for something to be considered a separate *ohel*. If the bed lacks that height, why would it interpose at all?

### Shmuel: Consistency

The *Gemara* resolves this: *targemah Shmuel bemitah asarah* — Shmuel situates the *mishna* as dealing with a bed that is in fact ten *tefachim* high. The case under discussion is specifically one where the bed reaches that height.

Tosafot notes that this is not merely an ad hoc solution. In the very first Tosafot of this perek, it is observed that Shmuel is being consistent with his own position. At the end of the first perek, Shmuel ruled that in the case of *sukkah tachas hasukkah* — a *sukkah* beneath another *sukkah* — the upper *sukkah* is only considered a distinct entity from the lower one if it has ten *tefachim* of height. Shmuel thus maintains, across both cases, that less than ten *tefachim* does not constitute a meaningful, separate roof. One might argue that there is an asymmetry: in the case of *sukkah tachas hasukkah*, both layers of *schach* are valid, and ten *tefachim* is needed to define the upper one as a distinct *sukkah*; in the case of a bed, which is invalid *schach*, perhaps even a lesser height — a *tefach* or four *tefachim* — suffices to create a separate covering and thereby compromise one's fulfillment. Yet Shmuel, at minimum, is internally consistent: in both cases he does not consider anything less than ten *tefachim* to constitute a problematic separate roof. Tosafot in several places examines how the various positions — ten *tefachim*, four *tefachim*, one *tefach* — play out across the different questions about a separate tent or roof.

## Oholos & Ohel

### Oholos: Intro

The *Gemara* now introduces a discussion from *Masechet Oholos* in order to illuminate Rabbi Yehuda's position. This is a striking feature of our *masechta*: two areas of *taharos* intersect throughout its sugyas. On one side, the laws of *schach* require sustained attention to the laws of *mekabel tumah*, since *schach* must not be made from anything susceptible to impurity. On the other, the laws of *schach* as a form of roofing bring us into the world of *Masechet Oholos*, which governs what kind of roof or covering transmits or blocks the *tumah* of a *meis*. To understand why Rabbi Yehuda permits sleeping under a bed, we turn to Rabbi Yehuda's own framework for what constitutes an *ohel* in the context of corpse impurity.

The *Gemara* cites a *mishna* from Oholos: *echad chor shecharu mayim* — a tunnel or hollow formed by water erosion; *o sheratzim* — by rodents; *o she'achalato melach* — by minerals dissolving the ground. In all these cases the cavity was not formed by human hands at all. Similarly, *vechen madbach avanim* — a pile of stones — and *vechen soar shel korot* — a pile of beams — where the overhang is formed by human hands but not with the intent of creating a roof over the space below. Imagine stacking beams and inadvertently leaving a gap through which a piece of a corpse passes: the pile was made by human agency but was never intended to serve as a covering for the space beneath it. These two categories — entirely natural formations and intentional constructions whose roofing quality is purely incidental — are the cases that Rabbi Yehuda addresses.

### R. Yehuda: Intent

*Kol ohel she'eino asui bidei adam eino ohel* — Rabbi Yehuda rules: any *ohel* not made by human agency and intent is not an *ohel* for purposes of *tumah*. The application to the *sukkah* case is becoming clear. When one places a bed in a *sukkah*, one does not intend the bed to serve as a roof over the space below. Like stacking beams, a bed placed in a room is not being deployed as a roofing structure for the space beneath it — and therefore, in Rabbi Yehuda's view, it does not constitute an *ohel* capable of interposing between the person and the *schach*.

### R. Yehuda: Mishkan

What is the textual basis for Rabbi Yehuda's position? *Yalif ohel ohel mimishkan* — he derives it by means of a *gezeirah shavah* from the *Mishkan*. The *pasuk* in the laws of *tumah* reads: *zot hatorah adam ki yamut be'ohel* — this is the law when a man dies in a tent. The word *ohel* there is the governing concept for corpse-impurity transmission. And in the *parsha* describing the *Mishkan*, the *pasuk* reads: *vayifros et ha'ohel al haMishkan* — he spread the tent over the *Mishkan*. Just as the *ohel* of the *Mishkan* was made intentionally by human hands to serve as a covering — *ma lehalan bidei adam, af kan bidei adam* — so too, any *ohel* relevant to the laws of *tumah* must have been made intentionally by human agency to serve that roofing function.

### Rabbis: Ribui

The Rabbis disagree. *Ohel ohel leribuyah* — the very *parsha* of *tum'at meis* in Parashat Chukat (*adam ki yamut be'ohel*) repeats the word *ohel* extensively: *kol asher be'ohel*, *kol be'ohel*, *ohel*, *ohel* — the multiplicity of usages is not incidental but expansive, coming to include all types of tents and coverings, even those formed naturally and without human intent. Each repetition broadens the category rather than restricting it. Indeed, as was noted, Rabbi Yosef identified something like eighteen instances of *ohel* in that section — which the Rabbis would say broadens the category maximally.

## Parah Adumah Setup

### Courtyard on Rock

The *Gemara* now poses a challenge to Rabbi Yehuda's position from a *mishna* in Parah. But before turning to that challenge, it is worth understanding the background of those *mishnayos*, because the elaborate preparations they describe require close attention.

*Chatzerot hayu venuyot biYerushalayim al gabei hasela* — courtyards were built in Jerusalem on outcroppings of rock, *vetachteihem challal* — with a hollow space beneath the rock. The concern motivating this entire enterprise was *tum'at hatehom* — the impurity of the depths: the anxiety that somewhere beneath any given patch of ground there may be a buried corpse or grave, unknown and undetectable. Anyone walking over such a buried *tumah* could become *tamei meis* without realizing it. The principle is *tumah retzutza bokaat veola* — compressed *tumah* pushes straight upward. If there is no gap, no *ohel* creating an interruption, the *tumah* travels directly up and strikes whoever is above it. The only protection is a gap of at least a *tefach*, which constitutes an *ohel*, causing the *tumah* to halt at the underside of the overhang rather than continuing upward.

Building on an outcropping of rock addresses this: since nothing is buried within solid rock, and there is a gap between the rock and whatever lies beneath it, any *tumah* buried beneath cannot reach those living on top of the rock. The Rabbis went to extraordinary lengths in preparing for the *parah adumah* — lengths that were in part compensatory. They intentionally introduced a certain leniency by having the *kohen* who burned the *parah* be a *tevul yom* — one who had immersed in the *mikvah* that day but had not yet waited until nightfall — specifically to undermine the position of the *Tzedukim*, who held that only a fully *tahor* kohen could perform the burning. Having adopted that intentional leniency, the Rabbis overcompensated in every other direction with remarkable stringencies to ensure that no one involved in the process of the *parah adumah* could become *tamei meis*.

### Children Born There

*Umevi'im nashim ubarot veyoldot sham* — they would bring pregnant women and have them give birth on this rock courtyard, *umegadlim beneihem sham laparah* — and raise their children there for the sake of the *parah*. Children born and raised on this rock outcropping would never have had the opportunity to walk on regular ground, never exposed to *tum'at hatehom*. They would draw the water for the *parah adumah* — *mayim chayim el kli* — without ever having risked contact with an unknown buried grave.

Tosafot raises the obvious question: the children, when born, were themselves *tamei*, since their mothers were *teme'iot*, in a state of *tum'at leidah*. The answer is that the specific concern was *tumah temes met* — corpse impurity — and, in a related vein, *tumah yotzei migufo*, impurity arising from within one's own body, primarily *ba'al keri*. For the purpose of the *parah adumah*, they wanted children young enough never to have experienced a seminal emission and who had never been exposed to *tumah meis*. The *tumah* of birth could be purified; what they sought to ensure was that the children had never contracted the most severe categories of *tumah* in the first place.

### Oxen & Doors

But even these children, raised on the rock, eventually had to leave it in order to draw the water from the *Shiloach*. Once they stepped off the rock, any buried *tumah* beneath the surrounding ground could rise and strike them. The solution: *umaviim shevarim veal gabeihem dlatot* — they would bring oxen and place large doors on their backs. A child sitting atop the door, with the door resting on the ox's back, was now sitting on top of an *ohel*: the space between the underside of the door and the ground constituted a gap, and the door served as a roof. Any *tumah* from below would travel upward, strike the underside of the door, and be blocked. The child could ride through the streets of Jerusalem to the *Shiloach* without risk of *tumah meis*.

The vessels they carried were *kosos shel even* — stone cups, because stone cannot become *mekabel tumah*. When they arrived at the *Shiloach*, *yardu letoch hamayim umilaum* — they descended from the oxen, filled the stone vessels with water, and returned to their positions atop the doors.

The question of whether an airplane flying over a cemetery functions analogously to these doors is, in fact, a live halachic issue involving *kohanim*. Two problems arise. First, the airplane is constructed of metal, which is *mekabel tumah*, and *kol davar shemekabel tumah eino chotzeitz bifnei hatumah* — anything susceptible to *tumah* cannot itself serve as a barrier against *tumah*. Second, Tosafot raises the question of *ohel zaruk* — a moving *ohel*, one that is in motion. Whether an *ohel zaruk shemo ohel* — whether a moving covering retains the legal status of an *ohel* and can block *tumah* — is debated. Our *mishna* in Parah clearly assumes that even the moving ox-and-door combination functions as a valid *ohel*, but there are opinions that a moving *ohel* is not a proper barrier.

### R. Yosef: Bucket

Rabbi Yosei adds an additional stringency: *mimkomo haya meshalshel umemale mipnei kever hatehom* — even the brief moment of descending from the ox to draw the water was deemed too risky. Even that inch of ground might conceal a buried *tumah*. Therefore, the child would lower the bucket from the ox itself without dismounting, and draw the water without ever touching the ground. This is the extreme extension of the principle: no moment of contact with the ground could be permitted throughout the entire process.

## Tavi & Ohalim

### Beraita: No Doors

The *Gemara* now introduces a *beraita* that bears directly on Rabbi Yehuda's position: *Rabbi Yehuda omer lo hayu mevi'im daltos ela shevarim* — Rabbi Yehuda says they did not need to bring doors at all; the oxen themselves were sufficient. The space beneath the ox's body constituted a valid *ohel* that would block *tumah meis*.

### Oxen: Natural Ohel

This is immediately striking. The *Gemara* observes: *vehashevarim d'ohel she'eino asui bidei adam hu* — the oxen represent an *ohel* that is not made by human agency. Yet Rabbi Yehuda, who has just told us that *kol ohel she'eino asui bidei adam eino ohel*, is apparently relying on them as a valid protective covering. The *beraita* thus appears to contradict Rabbi Yehuda's own principle. This tension sets up the discussion that follows about when and under what conditions Rabbi Yehuda will concede that naturally formed structures do function as *ohalim*.

## Large = Ohel

### Size of Head

*Ki ata Rav Dimi amar Rabi Elazar*: when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a teaching in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *modeh Rabi Yehuda kemalei egrof* — Rabbi Yehuda concedes when the structure is the size of a fist. An *egrof*, a fist, is approximately the size of a human head — roughly three *tefachim*. The principle Rabbi Yehuda is advancing is a subtle and conceptually rich one. When a structure is so small that its roofing quality requires the imposition of legal definition — when it takes only a *tefach* off the ground and does not naturally or intuitively read as a roof — then the intent with which it was made and the purposefulness of its construction are what give it its identity as an *ohel*. Without that intent, a *tefach* of height is simply a gap, not a roof. But when a structure is large enough that it *naturally* functions as a covering — a cave, a walkable tunnel of stacked beams — it does not require intentional construction to take on the identity of an *ohel*. At sufficient size, the thing announces itself as a roof regardless of how it came to be. Rabbi Yehuda would therefore concede that a cave or a large natural overhang is an *ohel* even without human intent.

Rashi initially understands *malei egrof* to refer to the area of the roof's surface, though he goes on to address the height of the hollow beneath it as well. The two dimensions — the expanse of the covering and the clearance below — together constitute what Rabbi Yehuda would regard as sufficient for a naturally formed structure to be recognized as an *ohel* on its own terms.

The *Gemara* cites a supporting *beraita*: *u'modeh Rabi Yehuda b'shakifin u'binikikei hasela'im* — Rabbi Yehuda concedes in the case of rock overhangs and fissures in cliffs, meaning naturally formed caves of significant size. He disputes the case of a mole tunnel — a tiny, rodent-dug passage — but would agree that a large, naturally formed rock cavity constitutes an *ohel*. Tosafot notes that this supporting source is actually a *mishna* rather than a *beraita*.

### Testing Consistency

This concession, however, creates a fresh difficulty. *Vaharei delet deyash bah kama egrofin* — the door placed on the ox's back clearly has many *egrofin* of span, whether measured by the surface area of the door or the clearance between the door's underside and the ground. It is also deliberately placed on the ox precisely to serve as an *ohel*. It has every quality Rabbi Yehuda could want. Why, then, does Rabbi Yehuda in the *beraita* rule that they did not need to bring doors — implying something was wrong with them?

### Abaye: No Need

Abaye resolves this simply: *lo hutrechu l'havi dlatot* — there was no need to bring the doors. Rabbi Yehuda's statement is not a rejection of the door as an invalid *ohel*; it is a practical ruling that the ox alone was sufficient. The door would have worked, but it was unnecessary given that the ox's own anatomy provided the protection. This is a clean resolution that requires no tension within Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Rava: Safety Risk

Rava offers a different explanation — and it is one that goes beyond Rabbi Yehuda's theoretical framework. *Lo hayu mevi'im dlatot kol ikar mipnei sheda'ato shel tinok gasa aleha* — they specifically did not bring the doors precisely because of the danger that a child riding on a broad, flat door would feel *gasa aleha* — overly at ease, too much in charge, insufficiently cautious. A child seated on a wide, spacious door might lean over the edge, stretch out an arm or head beyond the protected zone, *shemma yotzi rosho oh echad me'evarav ve-yitamei bekever hatehom* — and become *tamei* from a grave hidden in the depths. Riding on the narrow, uneven back of an ox naturally produces a sense of precariousness that keeps the child alert and careful. The door was therefore deliberately excluded not because it is halachically invalid but because it is psychologically dangerous.

*Tani kevasei de-Rava* — a *beraita* confirms Rava's explanation word for word, stating explicitly that Rabbi Yehuda's reason for excluding the doors was the concern that the child's sense of confidence on a broad platform would lead to extending a limb outside the protected area and contracting *tumah meis* from the depths.

## Ox Anatomy

### Above vs. Below

With this context established, the *Gemara* raises a structural objection to the ox itself as an *ohel*. *Shevarim nami legava asuyan* — oxen, too, are made to serve the space above. The entire cultural function of an ox is to carry loads on its back, to serve what is above it. By Rabbi Yehuda's logic — that a bed does not count as an *ohel* because it is made to serve the space above rather than protect the space below — should not the same critique apply to oxen?

### Rib Cage Protects

*Ela amar Rava shani shevarim ho'il ve'asuyin lehagein al bnei mei'eihem* — Rava answers: oxen are different, because the relevant structure is not the back of the ox or its exterior, but the rib cage. The rib cage of the ox does not serve the space above — it serves the space *below*: it exists to protect the inner organs. The ox's anatomy is, in this sense, a naturally formed *ohel* directed inward and downward, shielding the organs housed within it. The *pasuk* confirms this reading: *or uvasar talbisheni uv'atzamot vegiddin teshochecheini* — skin and flesh You clothe me with, and with bones and sinews You *sukkah* over me — the word *teshochecheini* shares the root of *schach*, of covering. The rib cage, as the *pasuk* describes it, is a covering whose function is directed toward protecting what lies within and below it.

### Bed vs. Ox

The contrast is now fully articulated. A bed is made to serve the space above it — the person sleeping on top. Its entire purpose is oriented upward. An ox's rib cage is structurally oriented downward, protecting the inner organs that lie beneath it. This is why Rabbi Yehuda accepts the ox as a valid *ohel* even without human intent, while rejecting the bed: the ox's anatomy genuinely functions as a shelter for the space below, while the bed, regardless of its size, fundamentally serves the space above.

The asymmetry the *Gemara* exploits is anatomical as much as halachic: an animal that walks on four legs has its rib cage positioned horizontally, oriented as a canopy over its organs. Were the animal to stand upright as a human does, the rib cage would protect in the horizontal plane rather than the vertical, and the argument would not apply in the same way.

## Sukkah Debate

### Permanent or Temp?

Having resolved the tension in Rabbi Yehuda's position through the framework of *oholos*, the *Gemara* now offers an alternative explanation — *ibba'it eima* — one that sets aside the entire comparison to *Masechet Oholos* and instead roots the dispute in a different conceptual category entirely: the question of *dirat keva* versus *dirat arai*, permanent dwelling versus temporary dwelling.

### R. Yehuda: Temp Void

*Rabi Yehuda l'ta'ameih* — Rabbi Yehuda follows his own broader reasoning about the nature of the *sukkah*. Rabbi Yehuda, recall, held at the very opening of the tractate that a *sukkah* may be built higher than twenty *amot* — a position that Abaye demonstrated corresponds to a view that a *sukkah* is to be treated as a *dirat keva*, a permanent-style dwelling. Given that framework, Rabbi Yehuda holds: *lo ati ohel arai u'mevatel ohel keva* — a temporary *ohel* cannot negate a permanent one. The bed is a transient structure; the *sukkah*'s *schach* represents a halachically permanent roofing. When asked under what roof you are sitting, the answer is the *sukkah* — the temporary bed simply does not carry the weight to redefine the person's position as being under a separate, interposing roof.

### Both: Dirat Keva

Crucially, Rabbi Shimon also holds that a *sukkah* requires *dirat keva* — this was evidenced by his position that a *sukkah* requires three full walls, a more demanding structural standard than his colleagues. So both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon agree on the basic premise: a *sukkah* is halachically permanent. The dispute is not about the nature of the *sukkah* itself but about what follows from that premise when a temporary structure is present within it.

### R. Shimon: Temp Matters

*Mar savar ati ohel arai u'mevatlo keva* — Rabbi Shimon holds that even a temporary *ohel* can negate a permanent one. The person beneath the bed is beneath the bed, and the fact that the *sukkah*'s *schach* is halachically the more significant structure does not override the physical reality that the most proximate roofing over the person is the bed. *U'mar savar lo ati ohel arai u'mevatlo keva* — Rabbi Yehuda holds the opposite: relative to the permanent roofing of the *sukkah*, the temporary bed is legally invisible, and one is still considered to be sitting under the *schach*.

Both authorities thus agree that the *sukkah* is *dirat keva*; they disagree on whether a *dirat arai* within it can displace the *dirat keva* for purposes of determining under which roof the occupant is situated.

One might ask: if the *sukkah* is meant to be a substitute for one's home, and what distinguishes it from one's home is precisely its impermanence, how can we simultaneously call it *dirat keva* and require that its *schach* remain impermanent? The answer is that even those who classify the *sukkah* as *keva* do not take that characterization to an extreme. The *schach* must still allow rain through — there must remain an element of impermanence that distinguishes it from a permanent roof. The designation of *keva* is a halachic construct that captures the dwelling's significance and legal weight, not a claim that it is structurally identical to a house. Both dimensions coexist: halachically it is treated as permanent; physically it must retain markers of impermanence. The *sukkah* walks that tightrope throughout.

## Schach Support

### Mishna: Bed Legs

A new *mishna* now takes up a related but distinct question. *Hasomech sukato be'karei hamitah keshaira* — if one supports one's *sukkah* with the legs of a bed, it is *keshaira*. This is the ruling of the *Chachamim*. The scenario envisions a bed in the *sukkah* whose legs serve as supports for the *schach* above — either as a four-poster frame over which *schach* is laid, or some similar arrangement by which the structure of the bed props up the *sukkah*'s covering. In any case, the bed, which is *mekabel tumah*, is functioning as a structural support for the *schach*.

### R. Yehuda: Stand Alone

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. *Im einah yacholah la'amod bifnei atzmah pesulah* — if the *sukkah* cannot stand on its own were the bed removed, it is invalid. If the *schach* would collapse without the bed's support, Rabbi Yehuda rules the *sukkah* invalid. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, see no problem with a *sukkah* so supported.

### Two Reasons

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ameh de'Rabbi Yehuda*? What is Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning? *Peligi bah Rabbi Abba bar Mamal* — two *Amoraim* debate this point. One says: *chad amar mipnei she'ein lah keva* — the problem is impermanence. A *sukkah* that would collapse if the bed were removed is not a stable, fixed structure; it is held together only by a temporary object that was never intended to be a permanent part of the *sukkah*. Remove the bed and there is no *sukkah*. That radical dependence on a transient object makes the entire *sukkah* too impermanent in Rabbi Yehuda's view.

The other opinion says: *ve'chad amar mipnei she'ma'amidah bedavar hamekabel tumah* — the problem is that the *schach* is being supported by something that is *mekabel tumah*. Just as the *schach* itself cannot be made of something susceptible to *tumah*, so too the direct support of the *schach* cannot be such an object. The walls of the *sukkah* may be made of anything, but what physically holds up the *schach* must not be *mekabel tumah*.

## Direct vs. Indirect Support

### On Top vs. By

*Amar Abaye: lo shanu ela samach aval sichach al gabei mitah keshaira* — the dispute applies only when the *schach* is directly *supported by* the bed. But if the *schach* is laid *on top of* the bed — in a configuration where the bed is present beneath the *schach* but is not the structural support for it — the *sukkah* is valid. If the *schach* would remain standing even without the bed, the bed's presence is irrelevant, and neither concern applies.

### Which Reason?

*Mai ta'ama* — why does this distinction resolve both concerns? *Le'man de'amar mipnei she'ein lah keva harei yesh lah keva* — if the concern is impermanence, the *sukkah* in Abaye's case is permanent; remove the bed and the *schach* remains. *U'le'man de'amar mipnei she'ma'amidah bedavar hamekabel tumah harei ein ma'amid bedavar hamekabel tumah* — and if the concern is *ma'amid bedavar hamekabel tumah*, here there is no such *ma'amid*; the bed is present but it is not holding up the *schach*. The practical halachic question that remains is: do we rule like Rabbi Yehuda or the *Chachamim*? If like the *Chachamim*, the entire discussion is moot. If like Rabbi Yehuda, we must then determine which of his two possible reasons governs, since the practical implications differ considerably.

### Indirect Support

The case that distinguishes the two reasons is explicit in the *Gemara*: *kegon she'ni'etz shipudin shel barzel vesichach aleihem* — iron spikes are driven into the ground and the *schach* is laid across them. *Le'man de'amar mipnei she'ein lah keva* — if the concern is impermanence, this *sukkah* is entirely permanent and has no problem; the iron frame is as fixed as one could wish. *U'le'man de'amar mipnei she'ma'amidah bedavar hamekabel tumah* — but if the concern is that the *schach* is directly supported by something *mekabel tumah*, here that is precisely what is happening: the iron spikes are *mekabel tumah* and they are directly holding up the *schach*.

This is precisely the real-world question of whether one may use a metal-frame *sukkah*. The further question is that of *ma'amid de'ma'amid* — indirect support. If the rule against *ma'amid bedavar hamekabel tumah* extends beyond the direct support to whatever indirectly holds the support in place, then even a wooden frame secured with metal nails could be problematic: the nails hold the frame together, and the frame supports the *schach*, making the nails indirect supporters of the *schach* and themselves *mekabel tumah*. This is the stringent position of the Chazon Ish, who ruled that one may not even use nails in the construction of the *sukkah*'s supporting frame. The result is a *sukkah* assembled with wooden pegs — famously compared by some to the construction of a coffin — representing the most extreme application of the principle of *ma'amid bedavar mekabel tumah*.
