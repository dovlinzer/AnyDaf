# Bava Batra 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hezek Re'iyah

### Two Mishnah Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּרָצוּ, הָא לֹא רָצוּ – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ; אַלְמָא הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק. וְאֵימָא ״מְחִיצָה״ – פְּלוּגְתָּא, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וַתְּהִי מֶחֱצַת הָעֵדָה״; וְכֵיוָן דְּרָצוּ – בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ, אַלְמָא הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק!
>
> **Translation:** According to the understanding that the term *meḥitza* means a partition, one can infer: **The reason** that they build a wall **is that they** both **wished** to make a partition in their jointly owned courtyard. **But if they did not** both **wish** to do so, the court **does not obligate** the reluctant partner to build such a wall, although his neighbor objects to the fact that the partner can see what he is doing in his courtyard. **Apparently,** it may be concluded that **damage** caused by **sight is not called damage.** The Gemara objects to this conclusion: **But say** that the term ***meḥitza*** used in the mishna means **a division, as it is written: "And the division of [*meḥetzat*] the congregation was"** (Numbers 31:43), referring to the half of the spoil that belonged to the entire congregation. According to this interpretation the mishna means: **Since they wished** to divide the jointly owned courtyard, **they build** a proper **wall** in the center even **against the will** of one of the partners. **Apparently,** it may be concluded that **damage** caused by **sight is called damage.**

The daf opens in the middle of a central discussion that has occupied the Gemara on and off: whether *hezek re'iyah* — looking into somebody else's private affairs as they conduct them in their home or yard — constitutes a legally cognizable form of damage. The stakes of this question are significant, because if visual invasion is classified as *hezek*, it would presumably obligate one to prevent it from occurring, including through the construction of a wall.

This question is bound up with a textual dispute about how to read the opening of the Mishnah. The more straightforward reading is that once two partners in a shared courtyard agree to build a wall, they must build one — but there is no independent obligation to build without such agreement. A competing reading holds that the very agreement to divide the courtyard triggers an obligation to build a wall, suggesting that *hezek re'iyah* is itself the operative legal basis. The plain reading favors the former, yet a later Mishnah seems to require wall construction even in the case of a garden (*ginah*), which creates difficulties for either reading.

### Tosafot & Hezek

Tosafot anchors the entire discussion in the framework of *hezek re'iyah*. In explaining a later Mishnah — which states that if a shared wall falls down, neither party can force the other to rebuild it — Rashi suggests a contractual theory: there was an implicit prior agreement to maintain the wall, and the obligation is merely its continuation. Tosafot, however, grounds this ruling explicitly in *hezek re'iyah*: once the parties had grown accustomed to conducting private activities in their yard under the protection of the wall, its collapse now constitutes an invasion of that established privacy. The *hezek* arises precisely because a pre-existing norm of private use has been disrupted.

### Gatehouse Tashma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹת בֵּית שַׁעַר וְדֶלֶת לֶחָצֵר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק! הַזִּיקָא דְרַבִּים שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an additional proof that damage caused by sight is called damage, from what is taught in a mishna (7b): The residents of a courtyard **can compel** each inhabitant of that courtyard **to** financially participate in the **building of a gatehouse and a door to the** jointly owned **courtyard,** so that the courtyard not be open to the eyes of those standing in the public domain. **Learn from it** that **damage** caused by **sight is called damage.** The Gemara answers: This is not a proof to the *halakha* in the case of two neighbors, as the **damage** of being exposed to the gaze **of** the general **public,** which has unimpeded sight of what is happening in the courtyard, **is different** and certainly called damage.

The Gemara now brings a *tashma* — an attempted proof from a different Mishnah. The case involves a shared courtyard accessed by multiple homes: *kofinoso livnot beit sha'ar vedalet lachatzer* — one may compel one's fellow courtyard members to contribute to the construction of a gatehouse and door for the courtyard. The gatehouse serves to prevent passersby in the public domain (*reshut harabim*) from seeing into the courtyard. Notably, the compulsion here is not directed at the passersby who are causing the visual harm — they cannot practically be forced to do anything — but at the fellow members of the courtyard, who are compelled to collectively protect themselves from this intrusion. The Gemara infers: *shema mina hezek re'iyah shemei hezek* — since the members can be forced to act, visual invasion must be a recognized legal harm.

This is precisely where the framing as *hezek* rather than as a "right to privacy" carries legal weight. One might ask: why does calling it a *right* not achieve the same result? If we speak in the language of rights, it is unclear why the right of one party to privacy entitles him to coerce another party — who is not himself invading that privacy — to fund a gatehouse. But if we speak in the language of damage, the logic follows naturally: the courtyard as a whole is being harmed, and just as members can force one another to build a protective wall against physical intruders, they can equally compel one another to guard against this form of harm.

The Gemara pushes back: *hezeka derabbim shani* — damage caused by the many, by every passerby who glances in, is qualitatively different from the damage caused by a single neighbor's ability to look in. The Gemara is not disputing the principle; it is disputing the scope. The Mishnah may prove only that *hezek re'iyah* applies when the source of the intrusion is the general public, not necessarily when it is a single individual.

### Hezek vs Rights

This distinction matters precisely because the category being invoked is *hezek* — damage — rather than a right. Within the *hezek* framework, the Gemara can ask whether different magnitudes or types of visual intrusion cross the threshold of legal harm. A single neighbor looking in may simply not generate the same kind of damage as the collective gaze of the public street. The framing as *hezek* enables the Gemara to calibrate the scope of the obligation in a way that purely rights-based language would not naturally accommodate.

### Public vs Private Hezek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְיָחִיד לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין חוֹלְקִין אֶת הֶחָצֵר, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה. הָא יֵשׁ בָּהּ כְּדֵי לָזֶה וּכְדֵי לָזֶה – חוֹלְקִין; מַאי, לָאו בְּכוֹתֶל? לָא, בִּמְסִיפָס בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** exposure to the sight of **an individual not** considered damage? **Come** and **hear** a proof that it is called damage from what is taught in a mishna (11a): **One divides a courtyard** at the request of one of the co-owners **only** if its area is sufficient so **that there will be in it four** by four **cubits for this one and four** by four **cubits for that one,** that is, the same minimal dimensions for each of the co-owners. This means that **if there is enough for this** one **and enough for that** one, **they do divide** the courtyard at the request of one of the owners. **What, is it not** so that the courtyard must be divided **with a wall** that will prevent one neighbor from seeing the other? The Gemara answers: **No,** perhaps it is divided **with a mere partition of pegs** through which one can still see.

The Gemara continues to narrow the question. After conceding that *hezek re'iyah* applies when the public can look in (*hezeka derabbim shani*), it asks whether the same applies in the case of a single individual. Another Mishnah is cited: one may not divide a courtyard unless each party receives at least four *amot* — *ein cholkin et hachatzer ad sheyehei bo arba amot lezeh ve'arba amot lazeh*. If there is sufficient space, division can be forced; if not, it cannot. The Gemara asks whether this implies that a forced division would include a forced wall — *mai lav bekosel* — but the answer is no: the Mishnah speaks only of establishing a boundary (*bemistama*), not of compelling wall construction. This again leaves the core question open.

### Windows & Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַחַלּוֹנוֹת, בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה בֵּין מִלְּמַטָּה וּבֵין מִכְּנֶגְדָּן – אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: מִלְּמַעְלָן – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָצִיץ וְיִרְאֶה; מִלְּמַטָּן – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲמוֹד וְיִרְאֶה; מִכְּנֶגְדָּן – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַאֲפִיל! הֶזֵּיקָא דְבַיִת שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof that damage caused by sight is called damage from what is taught in a mishna (22a): One who desires to build a wall opposite **the windows** of a neighbor's house must distance the wall **four cubits** from the windows, **whether above, below, or opposite. And** a *baraita* **is taught with regard to** that mishna: Concerning the requirement of a distance **above,** the wall must be high **enough so that one cannot peer** into the window **and see** into the window; concerning the requirement of a distance **below,** the wall must be low **so that he will not** be able to **stand** on top of it **and see** into the window; **and** concerning the requirement of a distance **opposite,** one must distance the wall from the windows **so that it will not darken** his neighbor's house by blocking the light that enters the house through the window. This indicates that there is a concern about the damage caused by exposure to the gaze of others. The Gemara rejects this argument: The **damage** of being exposed to the sight of others while **in** one's own **house is different,** as people engage in activities in their homes that they do not want others to see. By contrast, a courtyard is out in the open and it is possible that the residents are indifferent to being observed.

A further *tashma* addresses windows. If a neighbor has windows in his home and one wishes to build a wall or structure on one's own side of the property line, strict limitations apply. The wall must be either more than four *amot* higher than the top window, or more than four *amot* lower than the bottom window, and must be four *amot* removed horizontally — *bein mimalah bein mimatah bein miknegdan arba amot*. The rationale: *kedei shelo yatzitz veyireh* — so that one cannot stand atop one's own wall and look into the neighbor's windows. Even the potential for visual access is regulated; one cannot construct something on one's own property that creates a reasonable opportunity to look in.

This would seem to establish *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek* quite decisively. The Gemara responds: *hezeka dbeita shani* — the privacy of the home is categorically different. One's home is a domain where the expectation of privacy is at its highest, and someone's ability to see inside is recognized as a genuine *hezek*, sufficient to require preventive construction. But this concession still does not resolve whether the same applies to a yard.

### Rooftop vs Yard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גַּג הַסָּמוּךְ לַחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ מַעֲקֶה גָּבוֹהַּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ בַּעַל הֶחָצֵר לְבַעַל הַגָּג: לְדִידִי קְבִיעָה לִי תַּשְׁמִישִׁי, לְדִידָךְ לָא קְבִיעָה לָךְ תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּךְ; וְלָא יָדַעְנָא בְּהֵי עִידָּנָא סְלִיקָא וְאָתֵית – דְּאִיצְטַנַּע מִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this distinction: **Come** and **hear** a proof, **as Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** If one's **roof is adjacent to another's courtyard, he must make a parapet** around the roof **four cubits high** so that he will not be able to see into his neighbor's courtyard. This indicates that the damage of being exposed to the eyes of others even in a courtyard is called damage. The Gemara refutes this proof: The situation **is different there, as the owner of the courtyard can say to the owner of the roof: I** make **use** of **my** courtyard **on a regular** basis. **You,** by contrast, **do not** make **use** of **your** roof **on a regular** basis, but only infrequently. Consequently, **I do not know when you will go up** to the roof, **so that I can hide from you** at that time and avoid coming under your gaze.

A final *tashma* introduces the case of a rooftop adjacent to a neighbor's courtyard, as stated by Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *gag hasamuch lachatzer chaveiro oseh lo maakeh gavoah arba amot* — a rooftop adjacent to the neighbor's yard requires a parapet four *amot* high, well beyond what is required to prevent falls, specifically to prevent one from looking straight down into the yard below.

This appears to be a case of *hezek re'iyah* applied to a yard — precisely what was in question. The Gemara again distinguishes: *shani hasam deamrei lei bachatzer livaal gag* — there the owner of the yard can say to the rooftop owner: *lididi kevia li tashmishta lididach lo kevia lach tashmishta* — I use my yard regularly and continuously, while you go up to your rooftop only occasionally. And since I do not know when you will choose to ascend, *deitzatna minach* — I cannot take precautions in advance, I cannot know when to adjust my behavior to account for your presence.

Rashi captures this as *divrei hatznei'ah*: in the yard, private activity is a constant and regular occurrence. In the rooftop case, there is an established pattern of private use that is intermittently and unpredictably invaded. The *hezek* lies in the invasion of an existing, established norm. The question that remains unresolved is whether the initial situation — where no wall exists between two yards and people refrain from private activities from the outset — also constitutes a *hezek*. In that scenario, no established private activity is being disrupted; rather, the freedom to engage in such activity is never actualized in the first place. Is the suppression of that freedom itself a *hezek*? The Gemara leaves this open. The weight of the evidence is accumulating strongly in favor of *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek*, yet the plain reading of the Mishnah continues to resist it.

## Mishnah Reading

### Lishna Achrina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ: סַבְרוּהָ, מַאי ״מְחִיצָה״ – פְּלוּגְתָּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתְּהִי מֶחֱצַת הָעֵדָה״; וְכֵיוָן דְּרָצוּ – בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל בְּעַל כּוֹרְחָן, אַלְמָא הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara has so far presented one version of the discussion of the mishna. **A different version relates** the discussion as follows: The Sages initially **assumed: What** is the meaning of the term ***meḥitza*** mentioned in the mishna? **A division,** not a partition, **as it is written: "And the division of [*meḥetzat*] the congregation was"** (Numbers 31:43). According to this interpretation, the mishna means to say: **Since they wished** to divide the jointly owned courtyard, **they build** a proper **wall** in the center even **against the will** of one of the partners. **Apparently,** it may be concluded that **damage** caused by **sight is called damage.**

The Gemara now presents *lishna achrina* — an alternative formulation of the preceding argument that begins from the opposite direction. In this version, the initial assumption is that the Mishnah should be read as obligating a wall once the parties agree to *divide* the courtyard, not merely when they agree to *build* a wall. This reading, if correct, would make *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek* the operative premise — since the obligation to build follows automatically from the act of division.

### Mechitza Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: מַאי ״מְחִיצָה״ – גּוּדָּא; דִּתְנַן: מְחִיצַת הַכֶּרֶם שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״גְּדוֹר״. נִפְרְצָה – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״גְּדוֹר״. נִתְיָאֵשׁ הֵימֶנָּה וְלֹא גְּדָרָהּ – הֲרֵי זֶה קִידֵּשׁ, וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָהּ. וְטַעְמָא דְּרָצוּ, הָא לֹא רָצוּ – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ, אַלְמָא הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to this conclusion: **But** why not **say: What** is the meaning of the term ***meḥitza*** mentioned in the mishna? It means **a partition.** This usage would be **as we learned** in a *baraita*: Consider the case where **a partition of [*meḥitzat*] a vineyard** which separates the vineyard from a field of grain **was breached,** resulting, if the situation is not rectified, in the grain and grapes becoming items from which deriving benefit is prohibited. The owner of the field of grain may **say to** the owner of the vineyard: **Build a partition** between the vineyard and the field of grain. If the owner of the vineyard did so, and the partition **was breached again,** the owner of the field of grain may **say to him** again: **Build a partition.** If the owner of the vineyard **neglected** to make the necessary repairs **and did not** properly **build a partition** between the fields, the grain and grapes are **rendered forbidden** due to the prohibition of diverse kinds planted in a vineyard, **and he is liable for the** monetary **loss.** According to the understanding that the term *meḥitza* means a partition, one can infer: **And** according to the understanding that the term *meḥitza* means a partition, one can infer: **The reason** that they build a wall is **that they** both **wished** to make a partition in their jointly owned courtyard. **But if they did not** both **wish** to do so, the court **does not obligate** the reluctant partner to build such a wall, although his neighbor objects to the fact that the partner can see what he is doing in his courtyard. **Apparently,** it may be concluded that **damage** caused by **sight is not called damage.**

The *lishna achrina* turns on the word *mechitza*. Those who read the Mishnah as beginning with division understand *mechitza* to mean *plugta* — division or partition — citing the verse *vatehi mechetza ha'edah* as evidence. On this reading, once the parties agree to divide, the wall follows necessarily. The Gemara then proposes that *mechitza* could just as well mean *guda* — a wall — citing the case of *mechitzat hakerem* from the laws of *kilayim*, where the term clearly refers to a physical wall. If *mechitza* means wall, then the Mishnah requires only that the parties have agreed to build a wall — without such agreement, there is no obligation, which would imply *hezek re'iyah lav shemei hezek*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל״?! ״בּוֹנִין אוֹתוֹ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא מַאי – פְּלוּגְתָּא? אִי הָכִי, ״שֶׁרָצוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְחִיצָה״?! ״שֶׁרָצוּ לַחֲצוֹת״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: תָּא נַעֲבֵיד פְּלוּגְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **If so,** the words: **They build the wall,** are imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have said: They build it,** since the wall and the partition are one and the same. The Gemara retorts: **Rather, what** is the meaning of the term *meḥitza*? **A division. If** it is **so** that the term *meḥitza* means a division, the words: **Who wished to make a division,** are imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have said: Who wished to divide.** The Gemara answers: The phrasing of the mishna is **as people** commonly **say: Come, let us make a division.** Consequently, the mishna can also be understood as referring to two people who wished to divide a jointly owned area.

The Gemara raises a linguistic objection: if the Mishnah had meant to say that they agreed to build a wall (*mechitza* as wall), it should have said simply *bonin oto* — they build it — rather than *bonin et hakotel*, which introduces the word for wall separately, as if *mechitza* referred to something other than the wall itself. The Gemara then turns this around: if *mechitza* means division, the Mishnah should have used the verb *lachatzot* — to divide — rather than the noun form. The response: *tana avid plugta* — this is simply how people speak, using a nominal form rather than a verbal one.

### Kinyan Devarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק – מַאי אִירְיָא רָצוּ? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא רָצוּ נָמֵי! אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ – כְּשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ, וְהוּא דְּרָצוּ. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּכִי לֵית בֵּיהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ, כִּי רָצוּ – פְּלִיגִי?! תְּנֵינָא: אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין שְׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים, אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים – אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן חוֹלְקִין! אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – בִּמְסִיפָס בְּעָלְמָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הָכָא, כּוֹתֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks, according to the understanding that *meḥitza* means division: **But if damage** caused by **sight is called damage, why** does the *tanna* **specifically** teach that **if they wish,** they build a wall? **Even if they did not** both **wish** to do so, it should **also** be possible to compel the reluctant party to build a wall between them. **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Our mishna** is referring to a courtyard **that is not subject to the *halakha* of division.** Joint owners of a courtyard cannot be compelled to divide the courtyard unless each party will receive at least four square cubits of the courtyard. **And** therefore, **this** ruling of the mishna applies only in the case **where they** both **wished** to divide the courtyard. The Gemara asks: According to this understanding, **what is** the *tanna* **teaching us?** Is he teaching us **that when** a courtyard **is not subject to the *halakha* of division, if they** nevertheless **wished** to do so, **they divide** it? But **we** already **learned** this in the latter clause of a different mishna (11a): **When** do they not divide the courtyard because it is not large enough to compel division? **When** the joint owners **do not both wish** to divide it. **But when both of them wish** to divide it, **they divide** it **even** if it is **smaller than this,** i.e., smaller than four square cubits for each party. The Gemara answers: **If** we had learned this *halakha* only **from there, I would say** that they divide the courtyard **even** if it is **smaller than this by** constructing **a mere partition of pegs,** which does not prevent invasion of privacy. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us here** in this mishna that if they wish to divide the courtyard they can be compelled to build a proper **wall.**

Both readings of the Mishnah raise an acute legal difficulty. If one reading holds that the obligation to build the wall flows from the agreement to divide — and if *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek* — then the Gemara asks: *mai irya sheratzu* — why must they have agreed? If visual invasion is a recognized form of damage, why can't one partner simply compel the other to build a wall regardless of any agreement?

The answer, developed by Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is that the Mishnah is speaking of a case where the yard is not large enough to compel division (*keshein bah din chalukah*) — each party would not receive the minimum four *amot* required for a forced split. In such a case, one cannot impose division unilaterally. But if the parties agree to divide even a small yard, the division can take effect — and once it takes effect, the wall follows.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל״?! ״בּוֹנִין אוֹתוֹ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא מַאי – פְּלוּגְתָּא? אִי הָכִי, ״שֶׁרָצוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְחִיצָה״?! ״שֶׁרָצוּ לַחֲצוֹת״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: תָּא נַעֲבֵיד פְּלוּגְתָּא. (לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: וְכִי רָצוּ מַאי הָוֵי? לִיהְדַּר בֵּיהּ! אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ כּוּ׳. וְכִי קָנוּ מִיָּדָן מַאי הָוֵי? קִנְיַן דְּבָרִים בְּעָלְמָא הוּא! בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדָן בְּרוּחוֹת. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָלַךְ זֶה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהֶחֱזִיק, וְזֶה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהֶחֱזִיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **If so,** the words: **They build the wall,** are imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have said: They build it,** since the wall and the partition are one and the same. The Gemara retorts: **Rather, what** is the meaning of the term *meḥitza*? **A division. If** it is **so** that the term *meḥitza* means a division, the words: **Who wished to make a division,** are imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have said: Who wished to divide.** The Gemara answers: The phrasing of the mishna is **as people** commonly **say: Come, let us make a division.** Consequently, the mishna can also be understood as referring to two people who wished to divide a jointly owned area. The Gemara brings **a different version** of the previous discussion: **And if they wished** to divide the courtyard, **what of it?** What forces them to build the wall? If one of the parties does not wish to build a wall, **let him retract. Rav Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the mishna is not discussing a case where they merely reached a verbal agreement to divide the courtyard, but rather with a case where each party **performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other, confirming their respective commitments. Therefore, neither side can retract. The Gemara asks: **But** even **if** each party **performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other, **what of it? It is merely a verbal acquisition,** meaning there was no actual transfer of property, but only a verbal agreement to act in a certain manner in the future and not a true act of acquisition. The Gemara answers: **They performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other **with regard to directions,** i.e., not only did they verbally agree to divide the courtyard, they also determined which of them would get which part of the courtyard. Consequently, the acquisition related to actual property, a particular plot of land. **Rav Ashi said: For example, this one walked through his** designated portion and **performed an act demonstrating ownership** there, **and that one walked through his** designated portion **and performed an act demonstrating ownership** there.

This raises an immediate objection: *nahadru behu* — if the yard is too small to compel division, what binds the parties to their agreement? Can they not simply retract? The Gemara notes that even performing a formal *kinyan sudar* — the handkerchief act of acquisition — would not seem to help. *Kinyan devarim be'alma hu* — it is merely a *kinyan* to do what was promised, which the halakha does not recognize as binding.

This is the crucial principle of *kinyan devarim*. The category is not named because the mechanism of *kinyan* was weak — one could use a handkerchief, sign a contract, or make any formal act — but because the underlying commitment is a promise to perform a future action, and the halakha does not treat such a promise as legally binding in the way it treats a transfer of property, the creation of a debt, or the assumption of liability.

### Rashi & Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי?! וְהָא קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין שְׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים, אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים – יַחְלוֹקוּ. מַאי, לָאו אַכּוֹתֶל? לָא, אַמְּסִיפָס בְּעָלְמָא. לִיתְנֵי הַאי וְלָא לִיתְנֵי הַאי! סֵיפָא אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ – וּבְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים – לֹא יַחְלוֹקוּ. בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לְמַתְנִיתִין – בְּשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ; אִי בְּשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ, כִּי רָצוּ מַאי הָוֵי? נֶהְדְּרוּ בְּהוּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But how can you say this? Doesn't the latter clause** of the mishna (11a) **teach: When** do they not divide the courtyard because it is not large enough to compel division? **When the** joint owners **do not both wish** to divide it. **But when both of them wish** to divide it, **they divide** it even if it is smaller than this. **What,** is this clause of the mishna **not** referring **to** the fact that either one can force the other to build a proper **wall?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring to **a mere partition of pegs** and not to an actual wall. The Gemara asks: If so, **let** the *tanna* **teach this** mishna **and not teach that** other mishna, as this mishna teaches more details than the later one. The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach the other mishna for **the last clause** of that mishna, which states: **And** jointly owned **sacred writings** that are contained in a single scroll **should not be divided even if both** owners **wish** to do so. The Gemara continues its analysis of the mishna: **To what** case **did you interpret the mishna** to be referring? To a case **where** the courtyard **is not subject to the *halakha* of division.** But **if there is no *halakha* of division,** then **if they wished** to divide the courtyard, **what of it;** how can either one force the other to build a wall? If the parties no longer want to build a wall, **let them retract. Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is referring to a case where each party **performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other, confirming their respective commitments. Therefore, neither party can retract.

Rashi explains the principle directly: *ve'ein chalipin konin ella davar ha-nikneh* — the act of *kinyan* only takes legal effect on something that can be acquired: a sale, a gift, a lien on land (*shibud karkaot*), or a transfer of chattels. Abstract promises to perform an action in the future do not fall within these categories.

Tosafot raises a pointed question: earlier in the sugya, where the Mishnah discussed the obligation to build a wall when parties had agreed to do so, no one asked about *kinyan devarim*. Why not? Tosafot answers that agreeing to contribute to the cost of building a wall is fundamentally different from agreeing to divide a yard. When one agrees to share in the expense of wall construction, one is creating a financial obligation — effectively a monetary liability — and financial obligations can be assumed through *kinyan*. One can create a debt; one can obligate oneself to pay. But agreeing to divide a yard in the future does not create a financial reality, does not transfer property, and does not assume liability. It is simply a promise to do something, and that promise — however formalized — cannot be made legally binding. The contrast Tosafot draws is precise: liability and financial obligation can be undertaken through *kinyan*; performance of a future act cannot.

### Kinyan in Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לְמַתְנִיתִין – בְּשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ; אִי בְּשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ, כִּי רָצוּ מַאי הָוֵי? נֶהְדְּרוּ בְּהוּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדָן. וְכִי קָנוּ מִיָּדָן מַאי הָוֵי? קִנְיַן דְּבָרִים בְּעָלְמָא הוּא! בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדָן בְּרוּחוֹת. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָלַךְ זֶה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהֶחֱזִיק, וְזֶה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהֶחֱזִיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the mishna: **To what** case **did you interpret the mishna** to be referring? To a case **where** the courtyard **is not subject to the *halakha* of division.** But **if there is no *halakha* of division,** then **if they wished** to divide the courtyard, **what of it;** how can either one force the other to build a wall? If the parties no longer want to build a wall, **let them retract. Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is referring to a case where each party **performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other, confirming their respective commitments. Therefore, neither party can retract. The Gemara asks: **But** even **if** each party **performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other, **what of it? It is merely a verbal acquisition,** meaning there was no actual transfer of property, but only a verbal agreement to act in a certain manner in the future and not a true act of acquisition. The Gemara answers: **They performed** an act of **acquisition with** the other **with regard to directions,** i.e., not only did they verbally agree to divide the courtyard, they also determined which of them would get which part of the courtyard. Consequently, the acquisition related to actual property, a particular plot of land. **Rav Ashi said: For example, this one walked through his** designated portion and **performed an act demonstrating ownership** there, **and that one walked through his** designated portion **and performed an act demonstrating ownership** there.

The resolution, stated by Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is *shekanu miyadam beruchot* — they performed a *kinyan* on the directions, meaning on the actual division itself. The act of *kinyan* does not here commit them to divide in the future; it effects the division in the present. As Rashi explains, this may work through *kinyan chazakah* — each party goes to his newly allocated portion and performs some act of physical possession, thereby acquiring actual, present ownership of his half.

The principle, then, is sharp: one cannot commit in a binding way to divide a property in the future, but one *can* divide it now. The *kinyan* operates to create a present legal reality — the transfer of ownership — not to secure a promise of future performance. Once the division has been effected through a proper *kinyan*, the party who now has his own separate yard and is subject to visual invasion by the neighbor can be compelled to build a wall, because *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek*. The obligation emerges after the fact from the legal reality that has been created, not from any prior contractual promise.

## Building Materials

### Material Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִבְנוֹת כּוּ׳. גְּוִיל – אַבְנֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁפְּיָא. גָּזִית – אַבְנֵי דִּמְשַׁפְּיָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כָּל אֵלֶּה אֲבָנִים יְקָרוֹת כְּמִדּוֹת גָּזִית (וְגוֹ׳)״ כְּפִיסִין – אַרְחֵי. לְבֵינִין – לִיבְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In **a place where it is customary to build** such a wall with non-chiseled stone [*gevil*], or chiseled stone [*gazit*], or small bricks [*kefisin*], or large bricks [*leveinim*], they must build the wall with that material. The Gemara identifies the various building materials: ***Gevil*** refers to **stones that are not planed. *Gazit*** means **stones that are planed, as it is written: "All these were of costly stones, according to the measures of chiseled stones [*gazit*],** sawed with saws, within and without" (I Kings 7:9). This teaches that chiseled stones are those that have been planed and smoothened. ***Kefisin*** refers to **small bricks. *Leveinim*** means **large bricks.**

The Gemara now turns from the conceptual architecture of *hezek re'iyah* and *kinyan devarim* to the more concrete halakhic discussion of building materials specified in the Mishnah. The Mishnah lists four types: *gevil*, *gazit*, *kfisin*, and *levanim*. The Gemara explains them as follows. *Gevil* are *avnei dela mashpaya* — unsmoothed, rough stones. *Gazit* are *avnei demashpaya* — hewn or smoothed stones, as the verse states, *kol eileh avanim yekarot kemidot gazit*. *Kfisin* are *arichei* — half-bricks. *Levanim* are *livnei* — full bricks.

### Smooth Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִמַּאי דִּגְוִיל אַבְנֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁפְּיָא נִינְהוּ, וְהַאי טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – לְמוּרְשָׁא דְקַרְנָתָא? דִּילְמָא פַּלְגָא דְגָזִית הוּא, וְהַאי טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – לְבֵינֵי אוּרְבֵי הוּא; כִּדְקָאָמְרִינַן: כְּפִיסִין – אַרְחֵי, לְבֵינִין – לִיבְנֵי, וְהַאי טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – לְבֵינֵי אוּרְבֵי הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba, the son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: From where** do you know **that *gevil*** refers to **stones that are not planed, and this extra handbreadth** that a wall of *gevil* has compared to what a wall of *gazit* has is **for the protruding edges?** That is, a wall of *gevil* is six handbreadths thick because the stones have not been planed and smoothened, and therefore protrude somewhat outward. **Perhaps** *gevil* refers to planed stones that **are half** the thickness **of *gazit*,** namely, just two and a half handbreadths, as compared to *gazit*, which is five handbreadths thick; **and this extra handbreadth** in a wall of *gevil* **is** for the space **between the** two **rows [*urbei*].** That is, a wall of *gevil* is actually two walls of planed stones that are each two and a half handbreadths thick; and the two walls are separated by one handbreadth, which is later filled in with mortar for added strength. A proof for this explanation can be brought from **what we say,** i.e., that ***kefisin*** are **small bricks,** whereas ***leveinim*** are **large bricks,** twice the thickness of small bricks. **And this extra handbreadth** of thickness that a wall of *kefisin* has compared to what a wall of *levinim* has is for the space **between the** two **rows** of small bricks.

Rav Acha the son of Rava posed a challenge to Rav Ashi: how do we know that *gevil* means unsmoothed stones? The reasoning behind the question is structural. The Mishnah specifies that a *gevil* wall is one *tefach* wider than a *gazit* wall — six *tefachim* as opposed to five. If *gevil* means unsmoothed stones, this extra *tefach* is explained by the protruding, irregular edges (*morsha dekarnata*) that rough stone requires in the overall measurement. But perhaps *gevil* means smooth stones laid in two halves with plaster (*tina*) between them, and the extra *tefach* is accounted for by that mortar layer — just as *kfisin* (half-bricks kept together with cement) are wider than full bricks because of the binding material.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, כְּפִיסִין – אַרְחֵי, מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, גְּוִיל נָמֵי – אַבְנֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁפְּיָא, גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: And according to your reasoning, from where do we** derive that ***kefisin*** are **small bricks? Rather,** the Sages **learned this as a tradition.** And **so too, they learned as a tradition** that ***gevil* refers to non-planed stones.**

The Gemara's response: *ela gemara gemiri lah* — we have a received tradition that *gevil* means unsmoothed stones, and that is simply what the term signifies. The same applies to *kfisin*: we know from tradition that they are half-bricks joined by cement, not unsmoothed bricks whose extra width is due to rough edges. These are building terms with fixed meanings transmitted through tradition, not derivable from structural inference alone.

### Half-Bricks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִמַּאי דְּהַאי כְּפִיסִין – אַרְחֵי נִינְהוּ, וְהַאי טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – לְבֵינֵי אוּרְבֵי? דִּילְמָא מַאי כְּפִיסִין – אַבְנֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁפְּיָין, וְהַאי טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – לְמוּרְשָׁא דְקַרְנָתָא; כִּדְקָאָמְרִינַן: גְּוִיל – אַבְנֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁפְּיָין, גָּזִית – אַבְנֵי דִּמְשַׁפְּיָין, וְהַאי טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – לְמוּרְשָׁא דְקַרְנָתָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, גְּוִיל – אַבְנֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁפְּיָין, מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי – גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a different version of the discussion. **There are** those **who say** that **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Avya, said to Rav Ashi: From where** do you know **that *kefisin* are small bricks,** one-half the width of large bricks, **and this extra handbreadth** of thickness that a wall of *kefisin* has compared to what a wall of *leveinim* has covers the space **between the** two **rows** of *kefisin*? **Perhaps** you should say **what** are ***kefisin*? Stones that** are **not planed, and this extra handbreadth** of thickness that a wall of *kefisin* has in comparison to what a wall of *leveinim* has **is for the protruding edges.** And proof for this explanation can be brought from **what we say,** i.e., that ***gevil*** refers to **stones that** are **not planed,** whereas ***gazit*** means **planed stones, and this extra handbreadth** of thickness that a wall of *gevil* has compared to what a wall of *gazit* has **is for the protruding edges.** Rav Ashi **said to him: And according to your reasoning, from where do we** derive that ***gevil* are stones that** are **not planed? Rather,** the Sages **learned** this **as a tradition. Here too, they learned as a tradition** that *kefisin* are small bricks.

A parallel version of the same exchange is then cited. Some say Rav Acha the son of Rav Avya posed the same question in reverse to Rav Ashi: how do we know *kfisin* are half-bricks whose extra width comes from the cement between them? Perhaps *kfisin* are simply unsmoothed bricks, and their extra *tefach* is due to protruding edges — just as *gevil* are unsmoothed stones with protruding edges. Again the answer is the same: *gemara gemiri lah* — tradition establishes the meaning of each term. The materials mean what they mean; the tradition is the authority.

### Width & Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כָּל בֵּינֵי אוּרְבֵי – טֶפַח; הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּטִינָא, אֲבָל בְּרִיכְסָא בָּעֵי טְפֵי. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּרִיכְסָא, אֲבָל בְּטִינָא לָא בָּעֵי כּוּלֵּי הַאי. לְמֵימְרָא דִּבְגָזִית; דְּכֹל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת גּוֹבַהּ, אִי הָוֵי פּוּתְיָא חַמְשָׁא – קָאֵי, אִי לָא – לָא קָאֵי?! וְהָא אַמָּה טְרַקְסִין; דַּהֲוַאי גָּבוֹהַּ תְּלָתִין אַמְּהָתָא, וְלָא הֲוָה פּוּתְיָא אֶלָּא שֵׁית פּוּשְׁכֵי; וְקָם! כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא טֶפַח יַתִּירָא – קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Learn from it** that the space left **between the** two **rows** of a wall **is always a handbreadth.** The Gemara comments: **This matter** applies only when the two rows of the wall are filled in **with mortar. But** when they are filled in **with gravel [*berikhsa*], more** space **is required. And there are** those **who say** that **this matter** applies only when the two rows of the wall are filled in **with gravel. But when mortar** is used to fill in the space, **not as much** space **is required,** and less than a handbreadth suffices. § The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that** in the case of a wall of **chiseled stone, if for every four cubits of height there are five** handbreadths **of thickness** the wall **will stand,** and **if not it will not stand,** as this is the required ratio between a wall's height and its thickness? **But wasn't there** the **one-cubit-thick wall separating the Holy of Holies from the Sanctuary of the Temple [*amah teraksin*]** separating the Holy of Holies from the Sanctuary, **which was thirty cubits high and its thickness was only six handbreadths and** nevertheless **stood?** The Gemara answers: **Since there was an extra handbreadth** of thickness, it was able to **stand** even to such a great height.

From this discussion Abaye infers a practical halakhic norm: *kol beinei urbai tefach* — the standard thickness of the cement used to bind two half-bricks is a full *tefach*. As Rashi explains, this has practical consequences: if one contracts to build a wall for another and uses less than a full *tefach* of cement between the half-bricks, the owner can claim the wall has been built below the implied standard, since the Mishnah demonstrates that the accepted measure is a full *tefach* of binding material.

The Gemara then raises a question about the relationship between wall width and height. The Mishnah specifies widths for each building material, and one might ask why width matters if the concern animating the Mishnah is *hezek re'iyah*, which is fundamentally about height. The answer is that structural stability requires a certain width to support a given height: for a wall of *gazit* (smoothed stones) that is meant to reach the standard four *amot* height, a width of five *tefachim* is sufficient to keep it standing — no more, no less.

The Gemara then raises a remarkable challenge from the *Beit HaMikdash*: the partition wall that separated the *kodesh* from the *kodesh kodashim* in the Temple of Shlomo was thirty *amot* high, yet only one *amah* — six *tefachim* — wide. If a width of five *tefachim* can support only four *amot* of height, how could a single extra *tefach* make it possible to support thirty *amot*? The Gemara offers an initial response: *keyvan de-ika tefach yeseira kai* — one extra *tefach* does make a substantial difference. But a more satisfying answer is forthcoming.

## Beit HaMikdash Wall

### First Temple Wall

The wall of partition in the first *Beit HaMikdash* — *ammat traxin* — was thirty *amot* high and one *amah* wide. The wall marked the boundary between the *kodesh* and the *kodesh kodashim*, and its dimensions were fixed accordingly. The structural puzzle, as noted, is how such a narrow base could support such extreme height.

### Extra Tefach

The Gemara's initial response is that one extra *tefach* of width does wonders — moving from five to six *tefachim* dramatically changes what height can be sustained. However, this answer is provisional, and the Gemara will replace it with a structurally more compelling explanation.

### Second Temple Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי מַאי טַעְמָא לָא עֲבוּד אַמָּה טְרַקְסִין? כִּי קָאֵי – בִּתְלָתִין קָאֵי, טְפֵי לָא קָאֵי. וּמְנָלַן דַּהֲוָה גָּבוֹהַּ טְפֵי? דִּכְתִיב: ״גָּדוֹל יִהְיֶה כְּבוֹד הַבַּיִת הַזֶּה הָאַחֲרוֹן מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן״ – רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל; וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; חַד אָמַר: בְּבִנְיָן, וְחַד אָמַר: בְּשָׁנִים. וְאִיתָא לְהָא וְאִיתָא לְהָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **in the Second Temple they did not fashion an *amah teraksin*** to separate between the Holy of Holies and the Sanctuary, as they had done in the First Temple? The Gemara answers: **When** a partition **stands** even though it is only six handbreadths thick, **it is** able to remain standing up to **thirty** cubits in height. But **it will not** be able to **stand** if it is **more** than that height. The Second Temple was taller than the First Temple, and therefore the partition separating the Holy of Holies from the Sanctuary also had to be higher. The Gemara comments: **And from where do we** derive **that** the Second Temple **was taller** than the First Temple? **As it is written: "The glory of this latter house shall be greater than that of the former"** (Haggai 2:9). **Rav and Shmuel** disagree about the meaning of this verse, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** who disagreed as to its meaning. **One** of them **said** that it means that the Second Temple will be greater **in** the size of its **structure,** i.e., taller. **And one** of them **said** that it will be greater **in years,** meaning that the Second Temple will stand for a longer period of time than the First Temple. **And** the Gemara comments that **this is** true **and that is** true, meaning that the Second Temple was taller than the First Temple and also stood for a longer period of time.

The Gemara asks: in the second *Beit HaMikdash*, where the building was taller, why was a similar partition wall not constructed? The Mishnah Gemara cites the verse *gadol yiheyeh kevod habayit ha'acharon min harishon* — the glory of the latter Temple shall be greater than the former. A dispute is recorded between Rav and Shmuel, and similarly between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar: one holds that *gadol* refers to the building itself — the structure of the second Temple was greater, as it indeed was in the Herodian period — while the other holds that it refers to the years of its duration. The Gemara concludes *ve'isa lehai ve'isa lehai* — both are true.

### Ceiling Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֶיעְבְּדוּ תְּלָתִין אַמִּין בְּבִנְיָן, וְאִידַּךְ נֶיעְבֵּיד פָּרוֹכֶת! כִּי קָאֵי תְּלָתִין אַמְּהָתָא נָמֵי – אַגַּב תִּקְרָה וּמַעֲזִיבָה הֲוָה קָאֵי, בְּלֹא תִּקְרָה וּמַעֲזִיבָה – לָא הֲוָה קָאֵי. וְנֶיעְבְּדוּ מָה דְאֶפְשָׁר בְּבִנְיָן, וְלֶיעְבֵּיד אִידַּךְ פָּרוֹכֶת! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, גְּמִירִי: אִי כּוּלְּהוּ בְּבִנְיָן, אִי כּוּלְּהוּ בְּפָרוֹכֶת. אִי כּוּלְּהוּ בְּבִנְיָן – מִמִּקְדָּשׁ, אִי כּוּלְּהוּ בְּפָרוֹכֶת – מִמִּשְׁכָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **But they should have made a wall** as high **as** can **possibly** stand by itself, **and** then should have **made a curtain** for **the rest** of the height. **Abaye said:** The Sages **learned** as a tradition that the partition separating the Holy of Holies from the Sanctuary should be built **either entirely as a wall or entirely as a curtain.** It should be built **either entirely as a wall,** as is learned **from** the First **Temple, or** it should be built **entirely as a curtain,** as is learned **from** the **Tabernacle.** At no time, however, was there a partition that combined a wall and a curtain. [As to why, because] **even when a thirty-cubit** wall that is six handbreadths thick **stands, it is due to the ceiling and plaster** which attaches it to the ceiling that **it stands.** But **without a ceiling and plaster** holding it in place, **it does not stand.**

The structurally superior answer now emerges. The Gemara explains that the first Temple's partition wall, while only one *amah* wide, could stand at thirty *amot* because of the support provided by the ceiling: *agav tikrah maziva havei kai* — by virtue of the ceiling holding it at the top, the wall could achieve that height. A free-standing wall of one *amah* width cannot sustain thirty *amot* on its own; it is the ceiling that stabilizes it. Without ceiling support, *lo havei kai* — it simply could not stand.

This reframes the entire structural discussion. The relevant variable is not merely the ratio of width to height in a free-standing wall, but whether the wall is sealed by a ceiling above. A wall that reaches the ceiling and is held by it can achieve heights that a free-standing wall of the same width cannot.

### Curtains vs Wall

One might suggest a compromise: build a wall up to thirty *amot* — the maximum height the wall could achieve with ceiling support — and then use curtains (*parochet*) for whatever additional height was needed in the second Temple. The Gemara rejects this on the basis of a received tradition: *ai kula bevinyan ai kula beparoches* — the separation must be entirely of one type or the other. Either the whole partition is a wall, as in the first *Beit HaMikdash*, or the whole partition is a curtain, as in the *mishkan*. A hybrid of the two is not permitted. Since the second Temple was too tall for the wall to reach the ceiling and be supported by it — *tfei lo kai* — the only option was a curtain for the entire partition, and thus curtains were used throughout.

## Building Materials (II)

### Plaster Edges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵן וְסִידָן, אוֹ דִילְמָא הֵן בְּלֹא סִידָן? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, מִסְתַּבְּרָא: הֵן וְסִידָן; דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הֵן בְּלֹא סִידָן, לִיתְנְיֵיהּ לְשִׁיעוּרֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֵן וְסִידָן? לָא; לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: הֵן בְּלֹא סִידָן; וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא הָוֵי טֶפַח, לָא תָּנֵי. וְהָא קָתָנֵי: בִּלְבֵינִין – זֶה נוֹתֵן טֶפַח וּמֶחֱצָה וְזֶה נוֹתֵן טֶפַח וּמֶחֱצָה! הָתָם חֲזִי לְאִיצְטְרוֹפֵי. תָּא שְׁמַע: הַקּוֹרָה שֶׁאָמְרוּ – רְחָבָה כְּדֵי לְקַבֵּל אָרִיחַ, וְהָאָרִיחַ – חֲצִי לְבֵינָה שֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים! הָתָם בְּרַבְרְבָתָא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״שֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים״, מִכְלָל דְּאִיכָּא זוּטְרָא; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Do the measurements given in the mishna apply to **them,** the thickness of the materials themselves, **and the plaster** with which the materials were coated, **or perhaps** just to **them without their plaster? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: It is reasonable** to say the measurements refer to **them and their plaster, as, if** it should **enter your mind** to say they refer to **them without their plaster,** then the *tanna* **should have taught the measurements** of the plaster as well. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from here** that the measurements refer to **them and their plaster?** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **No, actually I** could **say to you** that they apply to **them without their plaster, and since** the plaster **does not have** the thickness of **one handbreadth** the *tanna* **did not teach** such a small measurement. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the *tanna* **teach with regard to bricks** that **this** one **provides one and a half handbreadths, and that** one **provides one and a half handbreadths?** Evidently, the *tanna* lists even an amount less than one handbreadth. The Gemara answers: **There** mention is made of half-handbreadths because **they are fit to be combined** into a full handbreadth. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a solution to the question, from a mishna (*Eiruvin* 13b) in which it is taught: **The** cross **beam, which** the Sages **stated** may be used to render an alleyway fit for one to carry within it on Shabbat, must be **wide enough to receive** and hold **a small brick. And** this **small brick** is **half a large brick,** the width **of** which is **three handbreadths.** That mishna is referring to a brick without the plaster. The Gemara answers: **There,** the mishna in *Eiruvin* is referring to **large bricks** that measure three full handbreadths, whereas here the mishna is referring to bricks that measure slightly less than three handbreadths, and the measurement of three handbreadths includes the plaster with which they are coated. The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna there **is also precise, as it teaches** about a brick **of three handbreadths,** from which one can conclude **by inference that there exists** also **a smaller-**sized brick. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from here** that the mishna there is referring to large bricks.

The Gemara now returns to the Mishnah's wall-width measurements and raises a practical question: when the Mishnah specifies widths — three *tefachim* for full bricks, or six for *gevil* stones — does this figure include the plaster or stucco (*sid*) applied to the outer surface of the wall, or does it refer only to the width of the building material itself?

*Hen vesidan o dilma hen belo sidan.* The Gemara reasons initially that the measurement must include the plaster: if the Mishnah meant to specify the width of the materials alone, it should have also told us separately how much plaster is to be applied. Since it does not, the total figure presumably encompasses both material and finish. *Shema mina hen vesidan.*

The Gemara then pushes back. Perhaps the width refers to the building material alone, and the reason the Mishnah does not specify the plaster thickness separately is that the amount of plaster does not reach even a full *tefach* — *vekivan delo havei tefach lo tanei* — and measurements below a *tefach* are simply not cited.

The Gemara raises a counter-example: when the Mishnah discusses full bricks (*levanim*), it speaks of each party contributing one and a half *tefachim* of yard width — a non-whole-*tefach* figure that is nonetheless mentioned explicitly. This would suggest that fractional measurements are cited when they aggregate to a meaningful total. The Gemara distinguishes: *hasam chazei leitztarovei* — in that case, the two halves of one and a half *tefachim* each combine to make a total of three *tefachim*, which is a full unit. Here, the thin layer of plaster on each side would bring the total to, say, three and a fraction *tefachim* — not a round figure — and that is why it goes unmentioned.

A further *tashma* is introduced from the laws of *eruvin*: the beam (*korah*) placed across an alleyway entrance must be wide enough to support a half-brick (*ariach*). Since this tells us the width of a half-brick is one and a half *tefachim*, and a full brick (*laveina*) is three *tefachim*, this figure is cited without any mention of plaster — suggesting that the three-*tefach* measurement refers to the brick alone, without the stucco finish. The Gemara responds: *hasam beravrevata* — that case concerns large bricks, which are indeed three *tefachim* of solid material without plaster. The bricks in our Mishnah may be slightly smaller, with the three-*tefach* figure including the plaster.

The Gemara infers from the phrasing *shel shlosha tefachim* — a brick *of* three *tefachim* — that there are bricks that are not three *tefachim* wide, confirming the existence of smaller bricks. This supports the possibility that the standard in our Mishnah is a smaller brick for which the three-*tefach* total includes the plaster. The question — whether the Mishnah's wall-width measurements include or exclude the outer plastering — is left without a definitive resolution.

## Synagogue Demolition

### Build Before Destroy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא לִיסְתּוֹר אִינִישׁ בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא, עַד דְּבָנֵי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: מִשּׁוּם פְּשִׁיעוּתָא, וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: מִשּׁוּם צַלּוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says: A person may not demolish a synagogue until he** first **builds another synagogue** to take its place. **There are** those **who say** that the reason for this *halakha* is **due to** potential **negligence,** lest he fail to build a new structure after the old one has been razed. **And there are** those **who say** that the reason for this *halakha* is **due to** the disruption of **prayer,** for in the meantime there will be nowhere to pray.

The Gemara now turns to a halakhic principle stated by Rav Chisda: *lo listor inish bei knishta ad d'bani bei knishta achrita* — one may not demolish a synagogue until another synagogue has already been built in its place. This ruling applies even when the demolition is for the purpose of reconstruction and improvement; the replacement must be standing before the original is torn down.

### Negligence vs Prayer

Two rationales are offered for this requirement. The first is *mishum pshiyuta* — the concern for negligence: once the original synagogue has been torn down, people will simply fail to get around to rebuilding it in a timely fashion. The second is *mishum tzloyi* — the concern for having a place to pray: even if one assumes the rebuilding will eventually happen, in the interim the community lacks a place for prayer.

The Gemara asks what practical difference there is between these two approaches. The difference, it explains, lies in the case where an alternative place of prayer (*duchta l'tzloyi*) exists elsewhere. If the only concern is having a place to pray, and such a place exists, the demolition should be permissible. If the concern is negligence, the existence of an alternative venue does not guarantee that the new synagogue will actually get built — and so even with another place available, demolition should be prohibited.

### Seasonal Synagogues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאִיכָּא בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי. מָרִימָר וּמַר זוּטְרָא סָתְרִי וּבָנוּ בֵּי קַיְיטָא בְּסִיתְווֹא, וּבָנוּ בֵּי סִיתְווֹא בְּקַיְיטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** these two explanations? The Gemara answers that **there is** a difference **between them** in a situation **where there is another synagogue.** Even though the community has an alternative place to pray there is still a concern that the new synagogue will never get built. It is related that **Mareimar and Mar Zutra demolished and built a summer synagogue in the winter, and,** in like manner, **they built a winter synagogue in the summer,** so that the community would never be left without a synagogue.

The Gemara records the practice of Mareimar and Mar Zutra: *satri u-vani b'kayita u-v'sitva* — they tore down the winter synagogue in the summer and rebuilt it then, and tore down the summer synagogue in the winter and rebuilt it then. Rashi explains that the winter synagogue was constructed with low ceilings, thick walls, and few windows to retain heat, while the summer synagogue had high ceilings and ample ventilation for cooler conditions. Since the community was using one synagogue while rebuilding the other, there was always a place to pray — which suggests this practice accords with the view that the primary concern is having a place to pray. Tosafot, however, attempts to explain how this can work even within the negligence-based approach, though on its face the practice maps most naturally onto the prayer-venue rationale.

### Funds & Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: גְּבוּ זוּזֵי וּמַחֲתִי, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּילְמָא מִיתְרְמֵי לְהוּ פִּדְיוֹן שְׁבוּיִים, וְיָהֲבִי לְהוּ. שְׁרִיגִי לִיבְנֵי, וְהָדְרִי הוּדְרֵי, וּמַחֲתִי כְּשׁוּרֵי, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִמְנִין דְּמִתְרְמֵי לְהוּ פִּדְיוֹן שְׁבוּיִים, מְזַבְּנִי וְיָהֲבִי לְהוּ. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ בָּנוּ נָמֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּירְתֵיהּ דְּאִינָשֵׁי לָא מְזַבְּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: What** is the *halakha* if **money** for the construction of a new synagogue **has** already **been collected and it rests** before us for that purpose? Is it then permitted to demolish the old synagogue before building the new one? Rav Ashi **said to him:** Even if the money has been collected there is still concern that **perhaps** an opportunity for **redeeming captives will present itself, and they will hand over** the money for that urgent requirement, and the community will be left without a synagogue. Ravina continues: **What** is the *halakha* if **the bricks** to be used for the construction of the new synagogue **are piled up, the boards are prepared, and the beams are ready?** Is it permitted to demolish the old synagogue before building the new one? Rav Ashi **said to him:** Even so, **sometimes** an opportunity for **redeeming captives will present itself, and they will sell** the building materials **and hand over** the proceeds for this purpose. Ravina raises an objection: **If so,** that is, if you are concerned that they will sell the materials to redeem captives, then **even** in a case where **they** already **built** the synagogue there should be a concern that they might come to sell the structure for that purpose, and therefore one should never be permitted to destroy an old synagogue. Rav Ashi **said to him: People do not sell their residences,** and certainly not their synagogues.

The Gemara poses a series of escalating questions to Rav Ashi, asked by Ravina. First: what if the community has already collected sufficient funds (*gavu zuzei u-machatsify*) — can they then tear down the old synagogue, since there is no longer a concern about negligence? The answer is no: *dilma mitrami lehu pidyon shevuyim veyahavi lehu* — it is possible that at the last moment an opportunity to redeem captives will arise, and the community will redirect those funds to *pidyon shevuyim*. Collected money alone is not sufficient assurance.

Next: what if all the building materials have already been delivered — the bricks stacked, the beams ready, the roof tiles assembled (*shavizi livni vehadrei vehadrei umachasei keshura*)? Again the answer is no: *zimnin demitrami lehu pidyon shevuyim mizabni veyahavi lehu* — sometimes a *pidyon shevuyim* emergency arises and the community sells off the materials to fund it. Even a full stockpile of building materials does not eliminate the concern.

The Gemara asks whether even building the new synagogue is sufficient: could one argue that the old synagogue may not be demolished until people have actually begun using the new one? The response: *dirastya de-inshei lo mizabni* — once the new structure is occupied and in use, people will not sell it off. The building itself, once in use, constitutes a sufficiently firm guarantee.

### Decay Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא חָזֵי בָּהּ תִּיוְהָא, אֲבָל חָזֵי בָּהּ תִּיוְהָא – סָתְרִי וּבָנֵי. כִּי הָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי חֲזָא בָּהּ תִּיוְהָא בִּכְנִישְׁתָּא דְּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא, סַתְרֵיהּ וְעַיֵּיל לְפוּרְיֵיהּ לְהָתָם, וְלָא אַפְּקֵיהּ עַד דִּמְתַקֵּין לֵיהּ שְׁפִיכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And we said** that an old synagogue must not be razed before its replacement is built **only** in a case **where cracks are not seen** in the old synagogue. **But if cracks are seen they may** first **demolish** the old synagogue **and** then **build** the new one. **This is like** the incident involving **Rav Ashi,** who **saw cracks in the synagogue** in his town **of Mata Meḥasya** and immediately **demolished it. He** then **brought his bed in there,** to the building site, so that there should be no delays in the construction, as he himself required shelter from the rain, **and he did not remove** his bed from there **until they** finished building the synagogue and even **affixed drainpipes** to the structure.

The Gemara then carves out an exception: *velo amran ela delo chazi bei tyuva* — all of the above applies only when the existing synagogue shows no signs of serious deterioration. If, however, the building is visibly rotting or cracking (*chazi bei tyuva*), the urgency of the situation overrides the usual requirement: *satrei u-vanai* — one may tear it down and rebuild immediately, without waiting for the replacement to be complete. When the structure is genuinely unsafe, the concern about negligence yields to the more pressing concern about the building's condition.

### Rav Ashi Story

The Gemara closes with an instructive story about Rav Ashi himself. He observed that the synagogue of Mata Machsaya was visibly deteriorating — Rashi reads this as cracking — and that the community was doing nothing about it. Rav Ashi tore it down and then, remarkably, moved his own cot onto the grounds and camped there. He refused to remove it until the community rebuilt the synagogue properly, including the drainage infrastructure (*shefichi*).

The story carries a double message. On one hand, the existence of genuine decay and disrepair is sufficient to permit immediate demolition, even before a replacement is built. On the other hand, Rav Ashi's action makes clear that the concern about *peshiya* does not simply disappear in an urgent case. He recognized that without additional pressure, the community might well address the demolition but then drag its feet on reconstruction. By installing himself on the premises, he created the urgency needed to move the project forward. The *peshiya* concern, in other words, is not merely a theoretical worry that the law addresses through procedural requirements; it is a real behavioral tendency that sometimes requires active countermeasures, even after the halakhic threshold for demolition has been crossed.

Tosafot raises a separate question in this context: whether one is even permitted to sleep in a synagogue — and, further, whether the *kedushah* of the synagogue persists even after its physical structure has been torn down, which would raise additional concerns. These questions are left for further discussion.
