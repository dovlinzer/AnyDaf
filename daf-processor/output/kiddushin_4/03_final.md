# Kiddushin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father Sells Daughter

### Intro: Kesef & Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם אֵין כָּסֶף״ – אֵין כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן זֶה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן אַחֵר. וּמַאן נִיהוּ – אָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** The reason is **that the verse states** with regard to a Hebrew maidservant acquiring freedom from her master: **"Then shall she go out for nothing, without money"** (Exodus 21:11). The extraneous phrase: Without money, indicates that **there is no money for this master,** i.e., in this case the master she leaves loses the money he paid for her, **but there is money for a different master,** i.e., another master receives money for her when she leaves his authority. **And who is** the other master who can transfer her to someone else and receives money for her? This is her **father.**

The *daf* opens in the middle of an ongoing discussion about how we derive the rules of *kiddushei kesef* — betrothal effected through money. Although the *stam* of the *gemara* earlier introduced this topic through the famous *gezerah shavah* of *kicha kicha* from the field of Ephron, the *gemara* here focuses primarily on the *pasuk* dealing with a father selling his daughter into slavery. The thematic proximity of that sale to a father marrying off his daughter makes it a natural anchor for the derivation.

The *gemara* picks up approximately in the middle of 3b. The *gemara* establishes that from the *pasuk* in the context of *motzi shem ra* — *es biti natati la'ish hazeh*, "I gave my daughter to this man" — we see that the father has the authority to marry off his daughter. And from the *pasuk* of *ein kesef* in the context of the sale of a daughter as a slave, we learn that *kiddushin* is effected through money. These two sources together establish: *kiddushei kesef* exists, the father performs it, and the father keeps the proceeds.

### Ktana: Father Controls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי קְטַנָּה, דְּלֵית לַהּ יָד לְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשִׁין, אֲבָל נַעֲרָה, דְּאִית לַהּ יָד לְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשִׁין – תְּקַדֵּישׁ אִיהִי נַפְשָׁהּ וְתִשְׁקוֹל כַּסְפָּא! אָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּנְעֻרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״ – כׇּל שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that **this** *halakha* **applies** only when she is **a minor girl, as she does not have the power to receive** her **betrothal.** Since she lacks the intellectual capacity, she likewise does not have the legal right to conduct this transaction. **But** with regard to **a young woman, who does have the power to receive** her **betrothal,** as a female older than twelve years is considered an adult, **let her betroth herself and take the money.** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "Being in her youth, in her father's house"** (Numbers 30:17), which teaches: **Any profit** she gains in her **youth** belongs **to her father.**

But this raises an immediate question: from *es biti natati la'ish hazeh*, all we can demonstrate is the father's authority when she is a *ktana* — a minor. The *mishna* in *Ketubot* teaches that these rights apply even when she is a *na'arah*, a girl between twelve and twelve and a half years old, who already has some legal standing. How do we extend the father's rights to that later stage?

The *gemara* quotes the text: *v'eima hani mili ktana d'leit lah yad* — perhaps these rules apply only to a minor who has no independent legal hand. According to our *girsa*, the phrase *likabel kiddushin* is included: she has no hand to receive her own *kiddushin*. Rashi, however, appears not to have had that word, and understood the question more broadly — a *ktana* has no legal standing whatsoever.

### Na'arah Question

The *gemara* then presses the question: *aval na'arah d'it lah yad likabel kiddushin* — a *na'arah* who does have the capacity to receive her own *kiddushin*, perhaps *eima takdish ihi nafshah v'tishkol kaspah* — she should marry herself off and pocket the money herself? The scope of the question is somewhat ambiguous: is the *gemara* asking whether the father loses his right entirely when she is a *na'arah*, or merely whether she can act alongside him? In any case, the question is sharp: what is the source for the father's authority and his right to the money at this stage?

### Slave Sale Source

The *gemara* answers from the *pasuk*: *b'neureiha beit aviha* — "in her youth, in her father's house." This phrase appears in the context of the father's power to annul her vows. From it the *gemara* derives a broader principle.

### Neurim to Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂה הַבַּת לָאָב? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִי יִמְכֹּר אִישׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ לְאָמָה״, מָה אָמָה מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְרַבָּהּ – אַף בַּת נָמֵי מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But** with regard to **that which Rav Huna says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived **that the earnings of a daughter,** i.e., the profit from her labor, belongs **to** her **father? As it is stated: "And if a man sells his daughter as a maidservant"** (Exodus 21:7). This comparison between a daughter and a Hebrew maidservant teaches the following: **Just as** with regard to a Hebrew **maidservant, her earnings** belong **to her master, so too,** with regard to **a daughter, her earnings** belong **to her father.**

*Kol shevach neurim l'aviha* — all the benefit of her youth goes to her father. Therefore, any money she would receive through an act of *kiddushin* that she herself performs would flow to him. The *gemara* then cites a related teaching: Rav Huna said in the name of Rav — how do we know that a daughter's earnings go to her father? From the *pasuk* *v'chi yimkor ish et bito l'amah*: just as a female slave's labor belongs to her master, so a daughter's labor belongs to her father — *mah amah ma'aseh yadeiha l'rabbah af bat nami ma'aseh yadeiha l'aviha*.

As Rashi notes, when she is a *ktana*, one doesn't even need a *pasuk* for this — it follows by *kal va'chomer*: if he can sell her labor to someone else as a slave, obviously he himself is entitled to it. The novelty of the *pasuk* is that it extends this right even to the stage of *na'arah*, when he can no longer sell her as a slave.

## Three Categories

### Chart Three Rights

At this point it becomes useful to lay out the full picture. The father has three distinct categories of rights over his daughter. First, as a *ktana*, he can sell her as a slave — *v'chi yimkor ish et bito l'amah* — and this obviously entails his right to her *ma'aseh yadeiha*. Second, he can perform *kiddushin* on her behalf, derived from *es biti natati la'ish hazeh*. Third, he has the power of *hifrat nedarim*, annulment of her vows, from *b'neureiha beit aviha*.

Now the *gemara* asks: for the stage of *na'arah*, we know *hifrat nedarim* applies because the *pasuk* itself says *b'neureiha*. We know *ma'aseh yadeiha* applies from the *drasha* on *v'chi yimkor*. But we still need to establish that the father keeps the *kiddushei kesef* even when she marries herself as a *na'arah*. The *gemara* attempts several routes and rejects each.

### Why Not Nedarim?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִקְּנָסָא – מָמוֹנָא מִקְּנָסָא לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Let us derive** this *halakha* of the betrothal of a young woman **from** the *halakha* of **fines,** as it is stated explicitly in the Torah that a man who rapes a young woman must pay a fine to her father, there is another principle that is applicable here: **We do not derive monetary matters from fines,** as the imposition of a fine is considered a unique case from which ordinary monetary rights cannot be derived.

Perhaps we could learn *kiddushei kesef* from the principle of *hifrat nedarim* — if he has all the rights of her youth generally, surely he gets her money. The *gemara* rejects this: *mamoni me'issuri lo yalfinan* — monetary law cannot be derived from laws of *issur v'heter*. The right to annul vows is a religious prerogative that tells us nothing about financial entitlements.

### Knas Distinction

What about the fact that in cases of *ones u'mifateh* — rape and seduction — and *motzi shem ra*, the Torah explicitly gives the *knas* to the father, *v'natnu l'avi ha-na'arah*? Surely that establishes that money flows to the father? The *gemara* again rejects this: *mamona miknasa lo yalfinan* — one cannot derive ordinary monetary law from a *knas*. A *knas* is a penal fine rooted in punishment, not compensation, and its rules carry no implications for a straightforward monetary transaction like *kiddushei kesef*.

### Boshet & Pegam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִבּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם – שָׁאנֵי בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם דַּאֲבוּהּ שָׁיֵךְ בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Let us derive** this *halakha* **from** the reimbursements for the **humiliation and degradation** of a young woman who was raped, which are also given to the father, one can answer that the following distinction applies: Reimbursement for **humiliation and degradation** are **different, as her father has an interest in them.** The father has the ability to derive benefit from her humiliation and degradation in other ways, e.g., by marrying her to a man afflicted with boils, which would humiliate her and cause a reduction in her value. Therefore, one cannot derive from the fact that the father receives the reimbursements for the humiliation and degradation of a young woman who was raped that he receives other monies due to her.

One might then suggest: what about *boshet* and *pegam* — the genuine compensation payments in a case of rape? These are not punitive fines but actual damages for the shame and physical injury suffered by the victim. If these real compensation payments go to the father, that should prove that *kiddushei kesef* goes to him as well.

## Three Categories…

### Father's Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּכִי קָא מְמַעֵט –
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* that a young woman's betrothal money belongs to her father is derived from the verse: "Then shall she go out for nothing, without money" (Exodus 21:11), as previously stated. As for the question why the money does not belong to her, the Gemara answers that **it is reasonable that when** the verse **excludes** another situation and indicates that there is no money for this master but there is money for a different master,

The *gemara* responds: *d'avua shaich b'gufa* — the father has a stake in her body, meaning in his rights over her, that justifies his receiving *boshet* and *pegam*. But does this help us prove *kiddushei kesef*?

## Boshet/Pegam Logic

### Rashi: Lost Profit

Rashi explains the underlying logic in stark terms. The father has a direct financial interest in her body in the sense that he could have arranged her marriage and received money for doing so. By being raped or harmed, she is now worth less on the marriage market. The *boshet* and *pegam* compensate for that lost opportunity. This is a disturbing framing, but it is the *gemara*'s own logic as Rashi describes it. Notably, if the father's right to *boshet* and *pegam* is rooted in his ability to marry her off and profit from doing so, this very logic would seem to imply that *kiddushei kesef* should go to him as well.

### Why Not Boshet?

Yet the *gemara* struggles with this very point. If *boshet* and *pegam* go to the father because of his authority to marry her off, why can't we use that to prove he gets *kiddushei kesef*? The argument becomes murky here, and the *gemara* has difficulty cleanly distinguishing the cases.

### Tosafot Resolves

Tosafot resolves this by reframing the scenario. The argument is specifically about the case where she marries herself as a *na'arah*. Even in that case, the father retains the ability to marry her off himself — he has not lost that right. That is why he is entitled to *boshet* and *pegam*. But one might still argue: in the specific sub-case where she herself performs the act of *kiddushin*, perhaps in that moment she should get the money — not because he lacks rights generally, but because in this particular transaction she is the active party.

### Misavra: Ein Kesef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְצִיאָה דִּכְווֹתַהּ קָא מְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **it excludes a departure** that is **like it,** i.e., a departure wherein the two masters are the potential recipients. Just as when no money is paid, the individual who might have received the payment is her master, so too, when money is paid, in a different set of circumstances, the money goes to the one who has authority over her, i.e., her father.

The *gemara* ultimately resolves the question not through a logical proof but through *misavra* — the weight of contextual reasoning. The *pasuk* of *ein kesef* establishes that when a slave goes free from her master, the master receives no money. The *gemara* notes that the implicit parallel is: when she leaves the domain of another authority figure, money does change hands. And when the *gemara* seeks who that authority figure is whose analogue receives the money, it is not her but the father. Whether he arranged the *kiddushin* or she did, the recipient on the father's side of the transaction is the father himself.

## Departure Parallel

### Still in Father House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא דָּמְיָא הַאי יְצִיאָה לְהַאי יְצִיאָה, הָתָם נָפְקָא לַהּ מֵרְשׁוּת אָדוֹן לִגְמָרֵי, וְהָכָא אַכַּתִּי מִיחַסְּרָא מְסִירָה לְחוּפָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But this departure is not similar to that departure,** as **there, she leaves the authority of the master entirely** upon being freed and she no longer retains any connection to him, **and here, she still lacks** the act of **passing** her over **to the** wedding **canopy.** Until she actually enters the wedding canopy, she has not left her father's authority completely.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this parallel. The model of *yetzi'ah* — departure from the master — does not translate cleanly to *kiddushin*, because after *kiddushin* a *na'arah* does not actually leave her father's house. *Hasha'm nafkah me'reshus adon legamrei* — in the case of the slave, she departs fully from her master's domain. *V'hacha achat michsra mesirah lechupah* — in the case of *kiddushin*, until she actually enters the husband's home at *nisuin*, she remains entirely under her father's control and within his household.

### Shared Vow Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהֲפָרַת נְדָרִים מִיהָא נָפְקָא לַהּ מֵרְשׁוּתֵיהּ. דִּתְנַן: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה אָבִיהָ וּבַעְלָהּ מְפִירִין נְדָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, there is a similarity between her departures in both cases, as **she leaves** her father's **authority at least as** far as **the nullification of vows** is concerned. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 66b): With regard to **a betrothed young woman, her father and her husband** together **nullify her vows,** and her father cannot nullify them alone.

The *gemara* responds: *leafroshai nedarim mi'ha nafkah me'reshusei* — there is at least a partial departure in the domain of vow annulment. The *mishna* teaches that once a *na'arah* is betrothed, *aviha u'va'alah meferin nedareha* — both her father and her husband must jointly annul her vows. His authority in this area is no longer exclusive.

### Limited Status Change

This is, admittedly, a limited change of status. He still retains vow annulment power; he simply now shares it. It is therefore somewhat puzzling that the *gemara* treats this partial shift as sufficient to sustain the comparison to a slave's departure from her master. The point seems to be not a genuine parallel in terms of lived experience, but a formal change of status that the *halacha* recognizes. And once we see the *gemara* is working with a formal model rather than a realistic one, the question can be turned on its head: if she doesn't truly leave his domain, that is all the more reason why — once we accept *kiddushei kesef* in principle — the money should go to him rather than to her.

### Transactional Model

What emerges from this entire discussion is that the dominant model in the *gemara*'s analysis of *kiddushei kesef* is the sale of a daughter as a slave. The *gemara* repeatedly returns to that framework. The father has control over her, she is sold or transferred to another domain, and money changes hands. The model is transactional in a deep structural sense. One cannot read through this *sugya* — even if some of the arguments are difficult to follow — without absorbing the message that *kiddushei kesef* is being derived from and explained through the template of the father selling his daughter into slavery.

## Economics & Symbolism

### 50 Coins & Burden

It is worth pausing to acknowledge the economic reality underlying all of this. In the world of the Torah, the payment involved was fifty silver *shekalim* — equivalent to two hundred *zuz* — a substantial sum. In that context, one might understand the financial logic that daughters, who in the ancient world generally did not go out and earn wages for the household, were perceived as financial burdens, and the father's primary obligation was to marry them off to someone who would provide for them.

### Dowry Shift

The deep irony is that once *kiddushei kesef* became purely symbolic — reduced to a ring worth a *perutah* — the financial burden shifted entirely to the father. It is now the father who must provide a substantial dowry if he wishes to arrange a favorable match. The one who once received money now pays.

### Ketubah Balance

The *ketubah* reflects this more complex economic reality. On one side, the groom undertakes financial obligations payable in the event of divorce. On the other, the bride's family brings property into the marriage. The system is not a simple sale; it involves financial flows in both directions, and the *ketubah* functions as a kind of balance between these competing obligations.

## Ein Kesef Derasha

### Extra Yud Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הָא מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם״ – אֵלּוּ יְמֵי בַּגְרוּת, ״אֵין כָּסֶף״ – אֵלּוּ יְמֵי נַעֲרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** does **this** verse: **"Then shall she go out for nothing"** (Exodus 21:11), **come for that** purpose, to teach that there is no money for this master, but there is money for a different master? **But it is required for that which is taught** in the following *baraita*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse "Then shall she go out for nothing, without money" should be understood as follows: **"Then shall she go out for nothing"; these** words **are** referring to **the days of adulthood,** i.e., a Hebrew maidservant leaves her owner's authority once she becomes an adult. **"Without money [*ein kasef*]"; these** words **are** referring to **the days of** her **youth,** i.e., when she becomes a young woman she leaves his authority.

The *gemara* now turns to a textual puzzle. The *pasuk* regarding the release of a Hebrew slave-girl says both *yatz'ah chinam* — she goes free without payment — and *ein kesef* — there is no money. If *chinam* already tells me she goes free at no cost, what does *ein kesef* add? The *braita* teaches: *yatz'ah chinam elu yemei bagrut, ein kesef elu yemei na'arut* — the first phrase refers to her going free as a *bogeret*, the second to her going free already as a *na'arah*. But if that is what *ein kesef* teaches, how can the *gemara* also use *ein kesef* to derive *kiddushei kesef*?

### Rabina's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״אֵן כָּסֶף״. מַאי ״אֵין כָּסֶף״ – אֵין כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן זֶה אֲבָל יֵשׁ כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן אַחֵר, וּמַאן נִיהוּ – אָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: If so,** that the verse is to be used only for this derivation, **let the verse say: *En kasef*,** without the letter *yod*. **What** is indicated by the full spelling with a *yod*: ***Ein kasef*?** This serves to teach the *halakha* stated above: **There is no money for this master, but there is money for a different master. And who is he?** He is her **father,** who has a right to receive the money when his daughter leaves his authority upon her betrothal.

Ravina answers: had the Torah meant only to convey the law of *na'arut*, it could have written *an kesef* — without a *yud*. The fact that it writes *ein* with a *yud* allows us to derive two teachings simultaneously. The phrase itself — *ein kesef* — teaches that she goes free as a *na'arah*, and the extra *yud* teaches *ein kesef la'adon zeh v'yesh kesef la'adon acher u'manihu av* — there is no money to this master, but there is money to another master, namely the father. Thus from a single letter, two separate *halachot* are derived.

### Zera Ein Lah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמִמַּאי דְּדָרְשִׁינַן הָכִי – דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְזֶרַע אֵין לָהּ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זַרְעָהּ. זֶרַע זַרְעָהּ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זֶרַע אֵין לָהּ״ – עַיֵּין לָהּ. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא זֶרַע כָּשֵׁר. זֶרַע פָּסוּל מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זֶרַע אֵין לָהּ״ – עַיֵּין לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And from where** is it derived that **one interprets** the verse **homiletically** in **this** manner? How is it known that the full form of the word *ein* teaches a *halakha*? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the daughter of a priest who married a non-priest and was subsequently widowed or divorced: **"And she has no child,** and is returned to her father's house, as in her youth, she may eat of her father's bread" (Leviticus 22:13). This verse indicates that if she has no children from her non-priest husband she may once again partake of *teruma*. **I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to **her** actual **children. From where** do I derive that **her children's children** are equivalent to children with regard to this *halakha*? **The verse states: "She has no [*ein lah*] child,"** where *ein* is spelled with a *yod* inserted in the middle. This additional letter serves to enable an alternative articulation of the term, specifically, **one examines her [*ayyein lah*]** to see if she has any descendants. **And I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to **children** of **unflawed** lineage, i.e., her legitimate offspring. **From where** do I derive that **children** of **flawed** lineage, e.g., *mamzerim*, are also considered her children for the purposes of this *halakha*? **The verse states: "She has no child,"** which indicates that **one examines her,** as explained above.

The *gemara* validates this method of interpretation by analogy to the *pasuk* of *v'zera ein lah* — the *bat kohen* who married a *yisrael* and is now widowed without children returns to her father's house and may eat *terumah* again. The *braita* asks: I know only that her own children constitute *zera*; how do I know that grandchildren — *zera zarah* — would also bar her return? *Talmud lomar v'zera ein lah* — the *gemara* reads *ein* as *ayin lah*, "look into her" — investigate all connections. Furthermore, one might think only *zera kasher* — legitimate offspring — counts; how do I know that a *mamzer* would also constitute *zera*? Again from *zera ein lah*, *ayin lah*. The *gemara* then asks: how can you use *ein lah* for both grandchildren and *zera pasul*? For grandchildren you did not even need the *pasuk* — *benei vanim harei hem kevanim*, grandchildren are like children by a general principle. Therefore the *pasuk* is needed specifically for the case of *zera pasul*, and that is what the *ayin lah* comes to teach. The methodology of deriving multiple teachings from an extra *yud* is thus established.

### Both Verses Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִךְ לְמִכְתַּב קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ לְאָבִיהָ, וְאִיצְטְרִךְ לְמִכְתַּב מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְאָבִיהָ. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ לְאָבִיהָ, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא טָרְחָא בְּהוּ, אֲבָל מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ דְּקָא טָרְחָא בְּהוּ אֵימָא דִּידַהּ הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it was necessary to write** a verse that teaches that **her betrothal,** i.e., the money or document of betrothal, belongs **to her father, and it was necessary to write** another verse that teaches that **her earnings** belong **to her father,** as one could not derive one *halakha* from the other. **As, if the Merciful One had written** only that **her betrothal** money belongs **to her father, I would say** that this is **because she did not toil for it** and therefore is not entitled to this sum. **But** with regard to **her earnings, for which she toiled, say** that **they are hers.** Therefore, it is necessary to state that her earnings also belong to her father.

The *gemara* confirms that both *pesukim* are in fact necessary — both the *ein kesef* teaching (that the father receives money) and the teaching that she goes free as a *na'arah*. Neither can be dispensed with.

## Why Both Verses

### If Only Kesef...

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, דְּקָא מִתַּזְנָא מִינֵּיהּ, אֲבָל קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ דְּמֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי לַהּ אֵימָא דִּידַהּ הָווּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** the verse **had taught us** only the *halakha* of **her earnings,** one would have said **that** they belong to her father because his daughter **is sustained by him** through his property. **But** with regard to **her betrothal,** i.e., the money or document of betrothal, **which comes to her from an external** source, I would **say** that **it is hers.** Therefore, it is **necessary** for the verse to teach both *halakhot*.

If the Torah had only written *kiddusheiha l'aviha* — that the *kiddushei kesef* goes to her father — one might have thought: this is because she didn't exert any effort to earn it, like *kesef metziah*, found money. Therefore it goes to him rather than to her. But from this I would not know that her ongoing earnings also go to him.

Conversely, if the Torah had only taught *ma'aseh yadeiha l'aviha* — her earnings go to her father — the logic is clear: she lives in his house, he feeds her, and in exchange she works and he takes her earnings. This is an implicit reciprocal arrangement, the very model that *Chazal* later built into the marital relationship between husband and wife. But by that logic, *kiddushei kesef* would not obviously belong to him — it is not something she earns through ongoing labor. It falls, as the *gemara* puts it, *me'alma ka'ati*, out of the sky, unexpectedly.

### Reciprocity Model

The two *pesukim* together thus paint a complete picture: the father supports her, she provides her labor, and the reciprocal arrangement extends even to *kiddushei kesef*. Each *pasuk* provides what the other cannot.

### Unexpected Kesef

Precisely because *kiddushei kesef* is unexpected and not part of the regular rhythm of her labor and his support, it cannot be derived by inference alone — it requires explicit scriptural teaching. This is why both verses are indispensable.

## Na'arah Status

### Father Can Override

The *gemara* has been clear that the father can marry off his *na'arah* even against her will. His authority is not conditioned on her consent.

### Self-Marriage Debate

A closer question is whether a *na'arah* can marry herself off. The *gemara* appears to suggest that she can, given the entire discussion about what happens when she performs the act of *kiddushin* herself. But this requires examination in light of the *rishonim*.

### Rambam: Prior OK

The Rambam rules *lehalacha* that a *na'arah* cannot independently perform her own *kiddushin*. If she does, the validity is entirely contingent on her father. And crucially, the Rambam holds that even the father's subsequent approval — after the fact — does not validate the act. The only way her participation in *kiddushin* works is if the father gave his approval beforehand. In that framing, she is essentially acting as his *shaliach*; he grants her permission in advance, and she executes his decision.

### Retroactive Approval

Others disagree, holding that if she marries herself and the father subsequently ratifies the act, it may be valid. On this view, his approval after the fact is sufficient to confirm what she initiated.

## Double Language

### Redundancy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא נַעֲרוּת, וְלָא בָּעֵי בַּגְרוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let the Merciful One write** that she leaves her master when she reaches her **youth, and it would not be necessary** to state that she leaves upon reaching **adulthood.** If she has already left her master when she becomes a young woman, it is not necessary to state that she leaves him upon reaching adulthood.

The *gemara* now addresses a related textual puzzle. The *pasuk* about the release of the Hebrew slave-girl mentions both *na'arut* and *bagrut* — the stages of *na'arah* and *bogeret*. Since a girl can only be sold as a slave when she is a *ktana*, she will necessarily go free either as a *na'arah* or as a *bogeret*, never later. So why does the verse need to mention *bagrut* at all? If we are told she goes free as a *na'arah*, she will obviously be free before reaching *bagrut*.

### Rabba: Both Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בָּא זֶה וְלִמֵּד עַל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says: This** phrase **comes and teaches about that** phrase. In other words, since it is not explicitly stated that this particular verse is referring to her departure when she becomes a young woman, if there was only one superfluous phrase one would conclude that it is referring to adulthood, as the *halakha* that she leaves the master when she becomes an adult is a lesser novelty. Therefore, two extraneous verses are required.

Rabba answers: *bazzeh u'limad al zeh* — one phrase teaches through the other. Without the double language, a reader would naturally apply the single phrase to the later, more extreme stage — *bagrut*. The presence of the second phrase is what allows us to extend the rule to the earlier stage of *na'arut* as well. Had there been only one phrase, we would not have derived *na'arut* independently.

### Bazzeh U'Limad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַתּוֹשָׁב וְשָׂכִיר. דְּתַנְיָא: ״תּוֹשָׁב״ – זֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם, ״שָׂכִיר״ – זֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba cites an analogous case. This is **just as it is** with regard to **a tenant and a hired worker. As it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning *teruma*: The verse states: "A tenant of a priest or a hired worker shall not eat of the consecrated" (Leviticus 22:10). **"A tenant"; this** is referring to a Hebrew slave **who has been acquired as a permanent acquisition,** i.e., one who said he wishes to stay with his master. This Hebrew slave has his ear pierced and he remains with his master until the Jubilee Year. **"A hired worker"; this** is referring to a Hebrew slave who has been **acquired for an acquisition of** six **years,** the standard period of servitude for a Hebrew slave.

The *gemara* illustrates this principle through the case of *toshav v'sachir* in the laws of *teruma*. The Torah states that a *kohen*'s *toshav* and *sachir* may not eat his *teruma*. The *braita* establishes: *toshav* refers to an *eved ivri* acquired permanently — the one who had his ear pierced and serves until *yovel*; *sachir* refers to the *eved ivri* who serves for six years. One might ask: just say *toshav* — the more permanent one — and I would derive by *kal va'chomer* that the *sachir* certainly doesn't eat *teruma*. But had the Torah written only one term, one would have applied it to the more limited case — the *sachir* — and I would have concluded that the permanent *eved ivri*, the *toshav*, actually does eat *teruma*. The second term is necessary precisely to cover the case that would otherwise seem like the more extreme and more doubtful instance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵאָמֵר ״תּוֹשָׁב״ וְלֹא יֵאָמֵר ״שָׂכִיר״, וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: קָנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **Let** the verse **say "tenant" and let it not say "hired worker," and I** would **say:** If one who is acquired **as a permanent acquisition does not partake** of his owner's, i.e., the priest's, *teruma*, as despite his status as a Hebrew slave he is not considered his owner's property, is it **not all the more so** that **one who is acquired for an acquisition of** six **years** should not be permitted to partake of *teruma*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילּוּ כֵּן, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: ״תּוֹשָׁב״ – זֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים. אֲבָל קְנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם – אוֹכֵל. בָּא ״שָׂכִיר״ וְלִימֵּד עַל ״תּוֹשָׁב״, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁקָּנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם – אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers: **If so,** that the verse were stated in this manner, **I would say: "A tenant"; this is one who was acquired for an acquisition of** six **years,** as the term itself is ambiguous. **But one who was acquired as a permanent acquisition may partake** of *teruma*. Therefore, the term **"hired worker,"** which is certainly referring to one who is less permanent than a tenant, **comes and teaches about** the meaning of the term **"tenant," that even if** a Hebrew slave **was acquired as a permanent acquisition he may not partake** of *teruma*. A similar line of reasoning applies in the above case of a young woman and an adult.

Abbaye, however, challenges this analogy. In the *toshav v'sachir* case, we are dealing with two different people — a *toshav* and a *sachir* are distinct individuals. Even if one were obvious, the Torah can reiterate it because the two statements are independent. But in the case of the slave-girl, it is one person. She cannot go free as both a *na'arah* and a *bogeret* — if she goes free at *na'arut*, she never reaches *bagrut* as a slave. So the two phrases cannot both refer to the same girl going free at two different stages. There must be a different explanation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם תְּרֵי גוּפֵי נִינְהוּ, דְּכִי נָמֵי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״תּוֹשָׁב נִרְצָע לֹא יֹאכַל״ וַהֲדַר כְּתַב אִידַּךְ, הֲוָה שָׂכִיר מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר טָרַח וְכָתַב לַהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: **Are** these cases really **comparable? There,** the tenant and the hired worker **are two bodies.** This is significant, **as even if the Merciful One had written** explicitly that **a pierced tenant may not partake** of *teruma*, from which the *halakha* of a Hebrew slave for six years could have been inferred, **and then** the Merciful One **wrote the other** case of a Hebrew slave acquired temporarily, this would not present a serious difficulty. The reason is that, although the *halakha* of **a hired worker is a matter** that could **be derived by** means of **an *a fortiori*** inference, and therefore it does not have to be stated explicitly, there is a principle: Sometimes with regard to **a matter that can be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, the **verse** nevertheless **takes the trouble and writes it** explicitly.

## Aylonit Case

### Aylonit Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְבֶגֶר דְּאַיְלוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye** rejects the previous suggestion that one verse teaches about the other, and **said:** The claim that the verse "Then shall she go out for nothing" is referring to adulthood **is necessary only for the adulthood of a sexually underdeveloped woman** who is incapable of bearing children **[*ailonit*].** An *ailonit* will never develop the physical signs of maturity, i.e., two pubic hairs. Consequently, she does not go through the halakhic stage of a young woman. Instead, she remains a minor until the age of twenty, at which point she immediately becomes an adult.

Abbaye therefore proposes a new resolution: *ela amar Abbaye lo nitzrecha ela l'vagar de'aylonit* — the verse is needed for the case of the *aylonit*, a woman who never develops the normal signs of puberty and therefore never passes through the halachic stage of *na'arut*. The *aylonit* transitions from *ketana* directly to *bogeret* at age twenty, when she is legally recognized as an adult by default, having failed to develop the physical markers that would have defined her as a *na'arah*.

### Skips Freedom?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: בְּנַעֲרוּת תִּיפּוֹק, בְּבַגְרוּת לָא תִּיפּוֹק, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, if the Torah did not teach that a Hebrew maidservant leaves her master upon becoming an adult, it might **enter your mind to say: She leaves** only **when** entering her **youth,** but **when** entering **adulthood** directly **she does not leave.** Since an *ailonit* is never classified as a young woman, she would never leave servitude. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that a Hebrew maidservant leaves her master even if she reaches adulthood directly.

The concern is straightforward: since the *aylonit* skips *na'arut* entirely, one might have thought she also skips the liberation that occurs at *na'arut*. Perhaps she remains enslaved indefinitely, since the trigger for her freedom — reaching *na'arut* — never arrives.

The verse therefore teaches *ka mashma lan*: she does go free when she reaches *bagrut*, even though she never passed through *na'arut*. The stage of *bagrut* — whenever it arrives — is sufficient to effect her release.

### Kal Va'Chomer?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: וְלָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? וּמָה סִימָנִין שֶׁאֵין מוֹצִיאִין מֵרְשׁוּת אָב – מוֹצִיאִין מֵרְשׁוּת אָדוֹן, בַּגְרוּת שֶׁמּוֹצִיאָה מֵרְשׁוּת אָב – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמּוֹצִיאָה מֵרְשׁוּת אָדוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi objects to this: But is it not an *a fortiori*** inference? **And if the signs** indicating that a young woman has entered **puberty do not** fully **release** a young woman **from her father's authority,** as he can still betroth her, nevertheless, **they do release** her **from the master's authority; is it not logical that adulthood, which** completely **releases** her **from her father's authority, should release** her **from** the **master's authority?** If so, one can derive by this reasoning that an *ailonit* leaves her master in adulthood, which means the verse is unnecessary.

Mar bar Rav Ashi challenges this: *v'lav kal va'chomer hu*? Is this not simply a *kal va'chomer*? When a girl develops the signs of puberty — *simanim* — this does not free her from her father's domain; she remains a *na'arah* under his authority. Nevertheless, those same signs do free her from her master. Now, *bagrut* — which does free her from her father's domain entirely — surely frees her from her master as well. This seems so obvious that no *pasuk* should be needed to teach it.

### Chidush: Sell Her

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְעִיקַּר זְבִינָא דְאַיְלוֹנִית, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: דְּאַתְיָא סִימָנֵי נַעֲרוּת – הָוֵי זְבִינָא, דְּלָא אַתְיָא סִימָנֵי נַעֲרוּת – לָא הָוֵי זְבִינַהּ זְבִינָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Mar bar Rav Ashi says:** This verse **is necessary only with regard to the basic** *halakha* of the **sale of an *ailonit*** as a Hebrew maidservant. As it might **enter your mind to say that** if a female **will show** the **signs of a young woman,** i.e., puberty, her sale **is** a valid **sale,** whereas in a case **where** she **will not show** the **signs of a young woman, her sale is not** a valid **sale.** The reason one might think this is the case is that if the maidservant turns out to be an *ailonit*, one cannot fulfill the requirements of the verse, as she will never become a young woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם כּוּ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore the superfluous phrase: **"Then shall she go out for nothing,** without money" (Exodus 21:11), **teaches us** that even an *ailonit* can be sold as a Hebrew maidservant and is released upon reaching adulthood.

Mar bar Rav Ashi himself resolves the difficulty: *lo nitzrecha ela l'ikar zevina de'aylonit* — the novel teaching of the *pasuk* is not about her liberation but about the permissibility of selling her in the first place. The *hava amina* would have been: only a girl who will become a *na'arah* can be sold; a girl who will never become a *na'arah* — like this *aylonit* — perhaps cannot be sold at all. *Ka mashma lan* that even an *aylonit* may be sold as a Hebrew slave. This is the genuine *chidush*, and since we have no need for the *pasuk* to tell us she goes free at *bagrut* — that follows from a *kal va'chomer* — we apply the *pasuk* to this more surprising teaching.

Tosafot raises the question of how we could know at the time of sale — before age twelve — that this girl is an *aylonit*. Tosafot notes that there is a discussion about whether *simanei aylonit* can be detected even at a young age. The *gemara* appears to assume that she would remain enslaved until age twenty. It is also worth noting the striking halachic point embedded here: this woman is nineteen years old, fully an adult by any intuitive measure, yet is legally classified as a *ketana* — because the *halacha* defines majority through specific physical markers rather than age alone. A twelve-year-old with minimal signs of puberty is a legal adult; a nineteen-year-old without them is not.

It is also worth stepping back to appreciate the broader significance of this discussion. The repeated emphasis on the father's sale of his daughter as a slave throughout this *sugya* is somewhat mitigated when one reflects on the actual purpose of that sale in the Torah's framework. The *gemara* itself implies that the *amah ivriyah* arrangement is ultimately oriented toward marriage — the master or his son is expected to marry her, and if neither does, she goes free. The labor of a young *ketana* is minimal; the real point of the transaction is the eventual marriage decision. Understood this way, the model is less about slavery and more about a structured arrangement for securing her marital future.

## Why Ein Kesef?

### Braita: Ki Yikach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ אִשָּׁה וּבְעָלָהּ וְהָיָה אִם לֹא תִמְצָא חֵן בְּעֵינָיו כִּי מָצָא בָהּ וְגוֹ׳״ – אֵין קִיחָה אֶלָּא בְּכֶסֶף. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״נָתַתִּי כֶּסֶף הַשָּׂדֶה קַח מִמֶּנִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara notes: **And a *tanna* cites** the *halakha* that a woman can be betrothed with money **from here,** a different source. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that when the verse states: **"When a man takes a woman and engages in sexual intercourse with her, and it comes to pass, if she finds no favor in his eyes, because he has found** some unseemly matter **in her,** and he writes her a scroll of severance" (Deuteronomy 24:1), in this verse, the term **taking is only with money. And so it says: "I will give money for the field; take it from me"** (Genesis 23:13).

The *gemara* now transitions to the *braita* that introduces the derivation of *kiddushei kesef* from the *pasuk* of *ki yikach ish isha u'va'alah* — "when a man takes a woman and has relations with her." The *braita* states: *ein kicha ela b'kesef* — the word *kicha* (taking) refers to acquisition through money, as evidenced by *natati kesef hasadeh kach mimeni* — Ephron's statement to Abraham that he should take the money for the field.

### Slave to Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה – נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף, זוֹ, שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּקָּנֶה בְּכֶסֶף?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** is there any need for this derivation? Could this **not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference,** as follows: **And if** a Hebrew **maidservant, who cannot be acquired** as a maidservant **through intercourse,** nevertheless **can be acquired through money, is it not logical that this** woman, **who can be acquired through intercourse** for the purpose of betrothal, **can** likewise **be acquired through money?**

But the *braita* immediately asks: why do I need a *pasuk* at all? Is this not derivable by *kal va'chomer*? *U'mah amah ivriyah she'eina niknes b'biah niknes b'kesef* — a Hebrew slave-girl, who is not acquired through an act of *biah*, is nevertheless acquired through money. *Zu she'niknes b'biah* — this woman, who is acquired through *biah* (as the continuation of the *pasuk* states, *u'va'alah*), *eino din she'tikne b'kesef*? Should it not follow all the more so that she can be acquired through money?

### Yevamah Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְבָמָה תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה וְאֵינָהּ נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף! מָה לִיבָמָה, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין נִקְנֵית בִּשְׁטָר, תֹּאמַר בְּזוֹ, שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בִּשְׁטָר!
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, this *a fortiori* inference can be refuted. **A *yevama* proves** otherwise, **as she is acquired** by the *yavam* **through intercourse, and** yet **she cannot be acquired through money.** The Gemara rejects this refutation: **What** is unique about **a *yevama*** is **that** she **cannot be acquired through a document. Will you say** that the same applies **to this** woman, **who can be acquired through a document** for the purpose of betrothal, and consequently the acquisition of money applies to her as well?

The *gemara* immediately raises a counterexample: *yevamah tochiah* — the *yevamah* disproves this. A *yevamah* is acquired through *biah* — the act of *yibum* — yet she is not acquired through money. So the *kal va'chomer* breaks down: acquiring through *biah* does not guarantee acquiring through *kesef*.

The *braita* responds by distinguishing the *yevamah*: *mah l'yevamah she'ken eina niknes b'shtar* — the *yevamah* is a unique case in which only *biah* works, not even a *shtar*. The methods of *kiddushin* for a *yevamah* are radically limited. In contrast, marriage (*kiddushin*) works through both *biah* and *shtar* — as we are about to derive. If the methods of acquisition are broader for ordinary *kiddushin*, then money should certainly also be available as an avenue, and we can restore the *kal va'chomer* from *amah ivriyah*.

### Derive from Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ״. הָא לְמָה לִי קְרָא, הָא אָתְיָא לַהּ! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּדִינָא פִּירְכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reiterates its question. **The verse states: "When a man takes** a woman." **Why do I** need **a verse** for **this** purpose? **It was** already **derived** through this *a fortiori* inference, as the proposed refutation of this inference was rejected. **Rav Ashi said:** It is needed **because one can say that** the **refutation of the** *a fortiori* **inference** is present **from the outset,** i.e., there is a difficulty with the comparison between a woman and a Hebrew maidservant.

Since *shtar* works for *kiddushin* but not for *yibum*, the *yevamah* is not a valid parallel. And since the *kal va'chomer* from *amah ivriyah* can be restored, the *pasuk* of *ki yikach* — together with the *kicha-kesef* analogy from Ephron — serves to anchor *kiddushei kesef* and *kiddushei shtar* firmly in the realm of ordinary marriage, distinct from the narrow rules of *yibum*.

## Both Verses Needed

### Woman Keeps?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם״, וְאִיצְטְרִךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ״. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כִּי יִקַּח״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קִידּוּשִׁין דִּיהַב לַהּ בַּעַל – דִּידַהּ הָווּ, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** After mentioning an additional proof for the option of betrothal with money, the Gemara comments: **And it was necessary** for the Torah **to write,** with regard to a Hebrew maidservant: **"Then shall she go out for nothing," and it was** also **necessary to write: "When a man takes** a woman." **As, if the Merciful One had written** only: **"When he takes," I would say:** The **betrothal money that the husband gives her is** invariably **hers.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "Then shall she go out for nothing,"** to teach that when she is not an adult her father has the right to her betrothal money.

Having established that *ki yikach* is needed, the *gemara* asks why we need both *ki yikach* and *yatz'ah chinam ein kesef*. If the Torah had written only *ki yikach* — derived through the *kicha-kesef* analogy from Ephron — the *hava amina* would have been: *kiddushin d'yahiv ba'al dedidah havu* — the money of *kiddushin* belongs to her. This follows naturally from the *ki yikach* context, where there are only two parties and the woman herself appears to be the relevant figure in the transaction.

The Ephron-Abraham model is significant here. In that narrative, there are only two parties: Abraham and Ephron. There is no third-party authority selling the woman as a father sells a daughter. The story therefore naturally suggests a model in which the woman herself is the central figure — a two-party transaction rather than a three-party arrangement with a father as intermediary. If *kiddushei kesef* were derived from this model alone, one might have assumed that the money goes to her.

### Ein Kesef Ambiguous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הֵיכָא דְּיָהֲבָה לֵיהּ אִיהִי לְדִידֵיהּ וְקִידְּשַׁתּוּ – הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כִּי יִקַּח״ – וְלָא ״כִּי תִּקַּח״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **had the Merciful One written** only: **"Then shall she go out for nothing," I would say** that in a case **where she gave him** money **and betrothed him, it is** a valid **betrothal,** as this verse does not specify who gives the money to whom. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "When he takes," and not: When she takes,** to teach that only a man may betroth a woman, not vice versa.

Had the Torah written only *ein kesef* — without *ki yikach* — the ambiguity would run in a different direction. *Ein kesef* tells us that money changes hands, but it does not specify who gives and who receives. The *pasuk* merely implies that in the analogous case of *kiddushin*, money is involved. But in which direction does it flow?

Rashi identifies the *hava amina* explicitly: *heicha d'yahava ihi ledidei kesef v'kidashto d'amra lei hiskadesh li* — perhaps she gives the money to him and says "marry me." The *pasuk* of *ein kesef* alone would not rule this out; it simply asserts that money is part of the equation without specifying the direction.

*Ki yikach* resolves this: *ki yikach* — he takes, he is the active party. The grammatical and legal subject of the act is the man. *Ki tikach* — she takes — is not what the Torah wrote. The husband must be the one who effects the acquisition, not the woman. Thus the two *pesukim* are mutually necessary: *ein kesef* teaches that the father receives the money even when she is a *na'arah*, and *ki yikach* teaches that he — the man — is the active party who gives the money.

## Status Asymmetry

### She Becomes M'k

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבְעָלָהּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה. וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה יְבָמָה שֶׁאֵין נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף – נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה, זוֹ, שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues to interpret the verse. The phrase: **"And engages in sexual intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), **teaches that** a woman **can be acquired through intercourse.** Why is it necessary for the verse to state this explicitly? Could this **not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference: And if a *yevama*, who cannot be acquired through money, can be acquired through intercourse, is it not logical that this** woman, **who can be acquired through money,** as derived above, **can** also **be acquired through intercourse?**

Tosafot raises a pointed observation about Rashi's *hava amina*. Rashi suggested that without *ki yikach*, one might have thought she gives the money and says *harei ata mekudash li* — "you are hereby betrothed to me." Tosafot challenges this: the language of *kiddushin* does not grammatically apply to the man, because *kiddushin* does not change his status. He is not rendered prohibited to other women by virtue of being married — polygamy was technically permitted. The status of *mekudeshet* — betrothed — is a status that applies to her, not to him. When he marries her, her relationship to all other men is transformed. His relationship to other women is not equivalently transformed. Therefore, saying *harei ata mekudash li* is not a coherent formulation.

Tosafot therefore reformulates: perhaps the *hava amina* was that she says *harei ani mekudeshet lach* — "I am hereby betrothed to you." She is still the speaker who articulates her own status change, even if the language is now correctly formulated.

Tosafot takes this one step further. Tosafot argues that *d'yahava ihi ledidei* — "she gives the money to him" — was never meant literally. We have just established at length that the father receives the *kiddushei kesef*. How could the *hava amina* be that she gives money to the groom? Rather, Tosafot proposes, the *hava amina* was *natan hu v'amrah hi* — he gives the money and she speaks the declaration. The question was about who recites the formula of *kiddushin*, not about who physically transfers the money.

### She Declares

This is not, however, the plain reading of the *gemara*, as Tosafot acknowledges. Rashi's reading — that the *hava amina* was literally that she hands over the money — is the straightforward *pshat* of the text. Tosafot is reframing the entire discussion to make it consistent with the conclusion that the father receives the money. In doing so, Tosafot surfaces a deeper tension: the *gemara* has two very different models in play simultaneously — the father-centered model derived from *ein kesef* and the *amah ivriyah*, and the two-party transactional model derived from *ki yikach* and Abraham's purchase of the field. At the very last moment, the *gemara* suggests that even the *ki yikach* model, taken alone, could have generated an unexpected conclusion — not that she receives the money, but that she gives it. These two contrasting models, and the tension between them, run through the entire *sugya* and animate the *gemara*'s sustained need to derive *kiddushei kesef* from two independent *pesukim* rather than one.
