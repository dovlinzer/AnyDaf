# Zevachim 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Machshava L'Haniach

### Daf 36 Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁחָטוֹ עַל מְנָת לְהַנִּיחַ אֶת דָּמוֹ אוֹ אֵימוּרָיו לְמָחָר, אוֹ לְהוֹצִיאָן לַחוּץ – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one slaughters** the animal **in order to leave its blood or its sacrificial portions for the next day, or to remove them outside** the designated area, but not in order to sacrifice them the next day, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the offering **unfit, and the Rabbis deem** it **fit.**

Daf 36 brings the third *perek* to its close, a chapter that has been devoted throughout to the laws of *pigul*. The *mishna* at the bottom of 35b launches the discussion that will occupy much of the daf, and the *gemara* that follows takes us deep into the logic of *Rabbi Yehuda*, the scope of invalidating *machshava*, and a series of important positions by the *amoraim*.

### Planning to Leave Over

The *mishna* opens with a case that sits at the edge of the classic *pigul* framework. The standard case of *pigul* or *chutz l'makomo* involves a *kohen* who, during one of the four *avodos*, intends to eat the meat, throw the blood, or offer the *emurim* at the wrong time or in the wrong place. But what happens when the *kohen* does not intend to perform any of those acts in the wrong time or place — he merely plans to leave the blood or the *emurim* over until the next day, or to bring them outside the *beis hamikdash*? The *mishna* states: *shachat al menat l'haniach et damo o emurav l'machar o l'hotzi l'chutz* — he slaughtered with the intent to leave the blood or the *emurim* until tomorrow, or to take them outside.

### Intent vs. Action

This is a fundamentally different category from standard *pigul*. In the classic case, the invalidating thought follows the structure of *he'achol ye'achel*, the eating-language of the *Torah*'s *pigul* verse — whether it is *achilas adam*, human consumption, or *achilas mizbeach*, offering on the altar. Here, there is no thought about eating or about *zerika* in a disqualified time or place. The *kohen* is simply planning on leaving the blood and *emurim* in place — committing the sin of *nosar* — or planning to bring them outside the precincts without any improper *zerika*. This is what the *mishna* calls *machshava l'haniach*.

### Rabbi Yehuda vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת אֶלָּא בְּחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וְהַפֶּסַח וְהַחַטָּאת שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The reason is **that intent does not render** the offering **unfit except in** cases of intent to eat or to burn the offering **beyond its** designated **time and outside its** designated **area, and** in addition, **the Paschal offering and the sin offering** are disqualified by intent to sacrifice them **not for their sake.**

*Rabbi Yehuda posel v'chachamim machshirim*. *Rabbi Yehuda* rules the *korban* is *pasul*, while the *chachamim* rule it is *kasher*. Importantly, neither side says this is *pigul* — since it does not meet the *he'achol ye'achel* criterion, it cannot carry the full *pigul* status. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that it occupies a middle ground: not *pigul*, but nevertheless *pasul*. The *chachamim* disagree entirely. In their view, if the *machshava* does not fall into the category of *chutz l'zmano* or *chutz l'makomo* as defined by the *Torah* — meaning a thought about an act of eating or offering at the wrong time or wrong place — then it has no invalidating power whatsoever, and the *korban* remains *kasher*. There is no intermediate state of *pasul* for a *machshava l'haniach* according to the *chachamim*.

### Non-Category Invalidations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּפְנִים – בַּחוּץ; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין בַּחוּץ – בִּפְנִים; שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּהוּ טְמֵאִים; שֶׁיַּקְרִיבוּהוּ טְמֵאִים; שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּהוּ עֲרֵלִים; וְשֶׁיַּקְרִיבוּהוּ עֲרֵלִים; לְשַׁבֵּר עַצְמוֹת הַפֶּסַח; לֶאֱכוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ נָא; וּלְעָרֵב דָּמוֹ בְּדַם הַפְּסוּלִים – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **inside** the Sanctuary **outside** the Sanctuary, **or** the blood **that is** to be **placed outside** the Sanctuary **inside** the Sanctuary; and likewise, if he slaughtered the animal with the intent **that ritually impure** people **will partake of it,** or **that ritually impure** people **will sacrifice it,** or **that uncircumcised** people **will partake of it, or that uncircumcised** people **will sacrifice it;** and likewise, with regard to the Paschal offering, if he had intent during the slaughter **to break the bones of the Paschal offering,** or **to eat from** the meat of the Paschal offering **partially roasted, or to mix the blood of** an offering **with the blood of unfit** offerings, in all these cases, although he intended to perform one of these prohibited acts, some of which would render the offering unfit, the offering is **fit.**

The *mishna* continues by listing a series of improper intentions that everyone — including *Rabbi Yehuda* — agrees do not invalidate the *korban*. These include: *al menat l'nitnam al agav hakevesh shelo kineged hayesod*, intending to place the blood on the ramp or at the south-east corner that lacks a *yesod*; intending to place blood that belongs above the *chut hasikra* line below it, or blood that belongs below above it; and intending to place blood meant for the outer *mizbeach* on the inner *mizbeach*, or vice versa. These are all cases of *zerika* in the wrong place within the *beis hamikdash*, but they do not constitute *chutz l'makomo* in the technical *pigul* sense of outside the precincts.

The *mishna* further lists: intending that *tmei'im* eat the meat or offer the *emurim*; intending that *arelim* eat or offer; intending to break bones of the *korban Pesach*, which violates *etzem lo tishbru bo*; intending to eat the meat raw, violating *lo tochlu mimenu na*; and intending to mix the blood with invalid blood, which would render it unusable. All of these are intentions to commit an *aveira* with the *korban*, some of which could cause problems with the *korban*, yet none of them constitutes *chutz l'zmano* or *chutz l'makomo* in the relevant halachic sense.

### What Invalidates

The *mishna* concludes by articulating the principle of the *chachamim*: *she'ein machshava poselet ela b'chutz l'zmano u'b'chutz l'makomo* — the only *machshava* that can invalidate is one of *chutz l'zmano*, which produces *pigul*, or one of *chutz l'makomo*. Anything outside those categories is *kasher* and beyond the scope of the discussion. The *mishna* also recalls the opening case of the first *perek* — a *Pesach* or *chatas* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* — as another type of invalidating *machshava*, but again, only those specific categories carry weight. If the thought is of *chutz l'zmano* or *chutz l'makomo* but in the *l'haniach* form — planning to leave the material rather than to eat or offer it — that is the precise point of dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *chachamim*.

## Rabbi Elazar's Source

### Nosar Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי בְּנוֹתָר; כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: "לֹא תוֹתִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר", וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: "לֹא יַנִּיחַ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר"; אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְהִנִּיחַ, תְּנֵהוּ לְעִנְיַן מַחְשֶׁבֶת הִינּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda,** who deems the offering unfit if there was intent to leave it over until the next day or to take it out of its designated area? **Rabbi Elazar said:** There are **two verses** that **are written with regard to *notar*. One verse states: "You shall let nothing of it remain until the morning,** and that which remains of it until the morning you shall burn with fire" (Exodus 12:10), **and one verse states: "He shall not leave any of it until the morning"** (Leviticus 7:15). **If** the additional verse **is not** necessary for **the matter of** the prohibition **against leaving** it overnight, which is already mentioned by the first verse, **apply it to the matter of intent of leaving** it overnight, which would therefore be prohibited as well.

The *gemara* opens by asking: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda?* What is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s source for this middle category of *pasul* that is neither *pigul* nor fully *kasher*? The question is pointed: since the invalidating *machshava* does not fit the *he'achol ye'achel* framework, what entitles *Rabbi Yehuda* to invent a new intermediate category? *Rabbi Elazar* offers an answer. He says there are two *pesukim* regarding *nosar*, and the doubling is the key.

### L'Haniach Language

One *pasuk* says *lo sosiru mimenu ad boker* — do not leave it over until morning — using the standard verb of *nosar*, meaning leftovers. A second *pasuk* says *lo yaniach mimenu ad boker* — do not leave it until morning — but uses the different root *l'haniach*, meaning simply "to leave," not "to leave over as a remainder." *Tosafot* notes that these two *pesukim* address different *korbanot*, so in principle they might not be directly redundant. Nevertheless, the *gemara* works with the assumption that the *Torah* could have expressed both laws with a single formulation. Why then the change of language in the second *pasuk*? *Im eino inyan l'hinuach, tnahu inyan l'machsheves hinuach* — if the alternate language is not needed simply to state the *issur* of actually leaving it over, since we already learned that from the first *pasuk*, then the shift in language must be teaching a new category: even the *thought* of leaving it over.

### Pasuk Needed Elsewhere

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הַאי קְרָא לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: "וּבְשַׂר זֶבַח תּוֹדַת שְׁלָמָיו" – לָמַדְנוּ לְתוֹדָה שֶׁנֶּאֱכֶלֶת לְיוֹם וָלַיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, does this verse come to** teach **this** idea? **This** verse is **necessary for him to** derive **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanksgiving shall be eaten on the day of his offering; he shall not leave any of it until the morning" (Leviticus 7:15). From the words: **"And the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanksgiving," we learned with regard to a thanks offering that it is eaten for a day and a night.**

The *gemara* challenges *Rabbi Elazar*'s derivation immediately. *Hai mibaei lei l'chid'sanya* — *Rabbi Yehuda* needs that *pasuk* for something else entirely, as demonstrated by a *baraita*. The *baraita* uses the *pasuk* beginning *u'vesar zevach todat shelamav* — the verse about the *todah* — which includes the phrase *lo yaniach mimenu ad boker*, to derive a vast range of halachos about how long various *korbanot* may be eaten. If that *pasuk* is consumed entirely by this *baraita*, *Rabbi Elazar* cannot simultaneously use it to derive the law of *machsheves hinuach*.

### Leviticus 7:15 Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלִיפִין, וְולָדוֹת, תְּמוּרוֹת – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וּבְשַׂר״. חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "זֶבַח". וּמִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר וְשַׁלְמֵי פֶסַח? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "שְׁלָמָיו". לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, וְחַלּוֹת וּרְקִיקִים שֶׁבְּנָזִיר – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "קׇרְבָּנוֹ" – כּוּלָּן קוֹרֵא אֲנִי בָּהֶן "לֹא יַנִּיחַ".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to animals **exchanged** for thanks offerings, **and** the **offspring** of thanks offerings, and the **substitutes** of thanks offerings, **from where** is it derived that they too are eaten for only a day and a night? **The verse states: "And the flesh [*uvesar*],"** and the additional "and" includes them. With regard to **a sin offering and a guilt offering, from where** is it derived that they are eaten for only a day and a night? **The verse states: "Sacrifice,"** to include other types of offerings. The *baraita* continues: **And from where** is it derived **to include the peace offering of a nazirite,** i.e., the ram that a nazirite brings at the close of his term of naziriteship, **and the peace offering of Passover? The verse** therefore **states: "His peace offerings,"** in the plural. With regard to **the loaves of** the **thanks offering, and the loaves and wafers that** are part **of** the offering brought by **a nazirite, from where** is it derived that they are eaten for only a day and a night? **The verse states: "His offering." With regard to all of them I** will **read** the phrase **"He shall not leave** any of it until the morning" as being applicable. Evidently, this verse is necessary to teach numerous *halakhot* concerning the allotted time to partake of offerings.

The *baraita* is built around Vayikra 7:15, *u'vesar zevach todat shelamav b'yom korbano ye'achel* — the meat of a *todah shlamim* shall be eaten on the day it is brought. The *baraita* proceeds to derive, from each word in the verse, additional categories that share the *todah*'s one-day eating limit. The word *basar* — "meat" — includes derivatives of the *todah* itself: a *chalifin* (a substitute animal designated when the original was lost, distinct from *tmurah*), offspring born from the *todah* animal, and *tmurah* proper (where *kedushah* is attempted to be transferred, and by *Torah* law both animals become *kadosh*). How do we know these derivative animals also have only one day? From the word *basar*, "flesh," which is more expansive than *zevach*, teaching that even things that derive from the *todah*'s flesh-category are treated like the *todah* itself.

### Extension to All Types

The word *zevach* — "sacrifice" — extends the one-day limit to *korbanot* that are neither *shlamim* nor *todah*, including *chatas* and *asham*. This is a striking observation, since the *Torah* nowhere states explicitly how long *kohanim* have to eat the meat of a *chatas* or *asham*. It is entirely derived from this *pasuk*. The word *shelamav* — "his *shlamim*" — extends the rule to special types of *shlamim* that are not the generic two-day *shlamim*: *shlomei nazir*, the ram a *nazir* brings upon completing his *nezirut*, and *shlomei pesach*, which *Tosafot* understands as a *Pesach* that became *shlamim* because it was not offered on the 14th of Nissan (as distinct from *Rashi*'s interpretation that it refers to the *chagigah* of the 14th). The word *korbano* — broader still than *basar* or *zevach*, extending beyond slaughtered offerings — includes the bread loaves that accompany the *todah* and the *chalot* and *rekikin* that the *nazir* brings with his *shlamim*. *Kulan korei bihen lo yaniach mimenu ad boker* — all of them may not be left until morning. So the *pasuk* is indispensable for this entire *baraita*; it cannot simultaneously serve as the source for *machsheves hinuach*.

### L'Haniach Teaches Machshava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: "לֹא תוֹתִירוּ", מַאי "לֹא יַנִּיחַ"? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְהִינּוּחַ, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הִינּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse state: And you shall let nothing** of it **remain** until the morning, as stated in the other verse. **What** is indicated by the phrase **"He shall not leave** any of it"? **If** it **is not** necessary for the **matter of** the prohibition **against leaving** it overnight, **apply it** to the **matter of intent** to **leave** it overnight.

The *gemara* resolves this by noting that while the *pasuk* is indeed needed for the *baraita*, the specific word *lo yaniach* — rather than *lo sotiru* — remains available to teach *machsheves hinuach*. Had the *Torah* simply wished to state the general prohibition and extend it to all *korbanot*, it could have used the standard language of *lo sotiru*. The deliberate shift to *yaniach* signals that beyond the act itself, there is a further teaching about *machshava*: *im eino inyan l'hinuach, tnahu inyan l'machsheves hinuach*. The content of the *baraita* — the extension to all *korbanot* — can be derived from the *pasuk* generally, while the choice of language specifically teaches about *machsheves hinuach*. The two teachings are complementary, not competing.

## Rabbi Yehuda's Logic

### Consequence to Thought

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבָרָא הוּא! דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִי אַתֶּם מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם הִנִּיחוֹ לְמָחָר – שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל? אַף חִישֵּׁב לְהַנִּיחוֹ לְמָחָר – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** the statement of Rabbi Elazar can be questioned, as **the rationale of Rabbi Yehuda is** based on **logical reasoning** and is not derived from a verse. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said to** the Rabbis: **Do you not concede that if he left it over until the next day that it is disqualified? So too,** if **he intended to leave it over until the next day** it is **disqualified.**

The *gemara* now presses further. Even granting *Rabbi Elazar*'s derivation for the *l'haniach* case — planning to leave the blood over — this only explains *chutz l'zmano* in its *l'haniach* form. What is the source for the parallel case of *chutz l'makomo* — planning to bring the blood or *emurim* outside the *beis hamikdash*? There is no comparable *pasuk* to derive it from. Moreover, the *gemara* notes, *taameh d'Rabbi Yehuda sevara hu* — *Rabbi Yehuda* is not actually basing himself on a *pasuk* at all; his reasoning is pure logic. This is evident from a *baraita*. In the *baraita*, *Rabbi Yehuda* addresses the *chachamim* directly: *ee atem modim she'im hinicho l'machar shehu pasul* — do you not agree that if you actually left the blood over until the next day, the *korban* is *pasul*? Once it becomes night, the blood can no longer be thrown, so the *korban* is invalidated. *Af chishev lehaniach l'machar pasul* — likewise, if you planned to leave it over, the *korban* is *pasul*. The same logic extends to *chutz l'makomo*: if you bring the blood outside the *beis hamikdash*, it becomes *pasul b'yotzei*, unfit for use, and so too planning to do so invalidates.

### Act vs. Thought Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבָרָא הוּא?! וְנִיפְלוֹג נָמֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּכוּלְּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Clearly, Rabbi Yehuda bases his opinion on logical reasoning, as he equates intent with action. The Gemara asks: **Rather, is the rationale of Rabbi Yehuda** based on **logical reasoning** and not derived from the verse? **But** if so, **Rabbi Yehuda should also disagree with regard to all of** the other cases in the mishna due to the same reasoning.

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s underlying principle is now exposed: any act that, if performed, would invalidate the *korban*, invalidates equally when it is merely the subject of a *machshava* during the *avodah*. This is a broad abstraction — it is not limited to the specific *Torah* categories of *chutz l'zmano* or *chutz l'makomo*, but applies generically to anything that would disqualify the *korban* if done. But if this is the logic, says the *gemara*, *veniflog nami Rabbi Yehuda bechulhu* — *Rabbi Yehuda* should argue on every case listed in the *mishna*, since most of those cases involve acts that, if performed, would have some effect on the *korban* or its associated objects.

### Other Mishna Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵי נִיפְלוֹג? בְּשׁוֹבֵר עַצְמוֹת הַפֶּסַח וְלֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ נָא – זִיבְחָא גּוּפֵיהּ מִי מִיפְּסִיל?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **With regard to which** case **should** Rabbi Yehuda **disagree?** Should he disagree **with regard to one who** slaughters the offering with intent to **break the bones of the Paschal offering, or** with intent **to partake of** the Paschal offering while it is **partially roasted?** Even had he actually realized such intent, **would the offering itself** thereby **be disqualified?** It presumably would not, as the presenting of the blood effects acceptance.

The *gemara* works through the *mishna*'s cases methodically, asking for each one why *Rabbi Yehuda* does not dispute its *kasher* status. This analysis is what forces the *gemara* to clarify the precise contours of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s principle.

### Pesach Bones Case

The easiest cases to dispose of are breaking the bones of the *Pesach* and eating from it raw. The *gemara* asks: *zivcha gufeh mi mipsal?* Does the *korban* itself become *pasul* if you break a bone or eat from it raw? These are *aveiros*, but they do not invalidate the *korban*. Since performing the act would not invalidate the *korban*, a *machshava* to do so certainly would not.

### Tamei/Arel Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּהוּ טְמֵאִים וְשֶׁיַּקְרִיבוּהוּ טְמֵאִים – זִיבְחָא גּוּפֵיהּ מִי מִיפְּסִיל?! שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּהוּ עֲרֵלִים וְשֶׁיַּקְרִיבוּהוּ עֲרֵלִים – זִיבְחָא גּוּפֵיהּ מִי מִיפְּסִיל?! לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, if a rite was performed **on the condition that ritually impure** individuals **will partake of it, or that ritually impure** individuals **will sacrifice it,** i.e., burn the sacrificial portions on the altar, **would the offering itself be disqualified** if such actions occurred? Likewise, if it was done with the intent **that uncircumcised** individuals **will partake of it, or that uncircumcised** individuals **will sacrifice it, would the offering itself be disqualified** if this occurred? The Gemara presents **another version** of this question: **Is it in his** power to execute these actions that are contingent upon the will of others?

The cases of *tmei'im* or *arelim* eating or offering require more analysis. At first glance, one might think that if a *tamei* or *arel* person performs the *zerika*, the *korban* becomes *pasul* — and if the act invalidates, should not the *machshava* also invalidate according to *Rabbi Yehuda*? *Rashi* explains that *yakrivuhu* in the *mishna* refers specifically to the offering of the *emurim* on the *mizbeach*, not to the *zerika* of the blood. Neither a *tamei* nor an *arel* person offering the *emurim*, nor a *tamei* or *arel* person eating the meat, invalidates the *korban* itself. Only the *zerika* can invalidate, and these cases do not involve *zerika*. This reading is somewhat strained, as *Rabbi Yehuda*'s own case in the *mishna* involves a *machshava* about leaving the *emurim* over, and the *gemara* acknowledges the tension. An alternative explanation is offered — *ko k'minei?* — meaning that the *machshava* must be something within your control. Thinking that other people, *tmei'im* or *arelim*, will eat or offer is not under your control and therefore does not constitute a meaningful *machshava* capable of invalidating the *korban*.

### Mixing Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעָרֵב דָּמָן בְּדַם הַפְּסוּלִין – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אֵין דָּם מְבַטֵּל דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where one had intent **to mix their blood with the blood of unfit** offerings, where **Rabbi Yehuda** also does not disagree with the ruling of the mishna that the offering is valid, he conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as he says: Blood does not nullify blood.**

The case of planning to mix the blood with invalid blood is resolved by noting that *Rabbi Yehuda l'taimei* — *Rabbi Yehuda* follows his own reasoning that blood of *korbanot* is not *batel* when mixed with other blood. *Min b'mino* is not *batel* according to *Rabbi Yehuda* for various reasons, and therefore mixing the blood would not actually invalidate it. Since performing the act would not invalidate the *korban* according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, neither does the *machshava*.

### Makom Meshulasch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיתֵּן אֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה, לְמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: שֶׁלֹּא לִמְקוֹמוֹ נָמֵי מְקוֹמוֹ קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, in cases where one had intent **to place** the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **below** the red line, or those that are to be placed **below** the red line **above** the red line, **Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as he says:** Blood **that** was **not** placed **in its** proper **place** on the altar is **also considered** to be placed in **its** proper **place,** and it effects acceptance of the offering.

The cases of intending to place blood that belongs above the *chut hasikra* below it, or vice versa, are resolved by the principle of *shelo l'mkomo k'bmkomo* — according to at least one approach in the earlier *gemara*, placing blood in the wrong location on the *mizbeach* still counts, so it does not invalidate. Since the act itself would not invalidate according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, the *machshava* is similarly harmless.

More complex is the case of intending to bring blood that belongs on the outer *mizbeach* inside to the inner *mizbeach*, or vice versa. This does invalidate. So why does *Rabbi Yehuda* not say the *machshava* of doing so also invalidates? The *gemara* answers that *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *beinan makom shemeshulash bedam bebasar ba'imurim* — the relevant category of *makom* for purposes of *machshava* requires a place that is triply defined by blood, meat, and *emurim*. The inner *mizbeach*, the *heichal*, does not satisfy this criterion: the *emurim* are not offered there, and *basar* is not consumed there under normal circumstances. Therefore, a *machshava* involving the inner *heichal* falls outside the operative category of *makom* for these purposes, and does not invalidate. This is admittedly a surprising restriction on an otherwise broad principle, but it is what the *gemara* holds.

### Kol Davar Ra Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַאי סְבָרָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״דָּבָר רָע״ – רִיבָּה כָּאן חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ בַּדָּרוֹם וְחַטָּאת שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמָהּ לִפְנִים, פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yehuda accept this reasoning** that it must be a place of threefold functionality? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the verse states: "You shall not sacrifice to the Lord your God an ox, or a sheep, that has a blemish, even any **evil thing"** (Deuteronomy 17:1). The verse **included here a sin offering that one slaughtered in the south** and not on the northern side of the Temple courtyard as required, **and** likewise **a sin offering whose blood entered inside,** i.e., it was slaughtered with the intent that its blood would be brought inside the Sanctuary, that they are **disqualified.** This indicates that an offering slaughtered with improper intent with regard to a place that lacks threefold functionality nevertheless renders the offering not valid.

A *baraita* now creates a sharp contradiction. *Rabbi Yehuda* himself is cited as deriving from the *pasuk* *lo tizbach l'Hashem Elokecha... kol davar ra* (Devarim 17:1) that a *chatas* slaughtered in the south of the *azarah* rather than the north is *pasul*, and that this falls under the *lav* of the *pasuk*, implying one would receive *malkus*. Furthermore, the *baraita* implies that thinking about bringing the blood of an outer *chatas* into the *heichal* during the *shechita* would also invalidate the *korban* — *Rashi* explains this because the *pasuk* speaks at the moment of *lo tizbach*, which is at *shechita*, and *Tosafot* adds that *davar* may also suggest thought or speech rather than a physical act.

If *Rabbi Yehuda* truly says that a *machshava* about bringing the blood inside the *heichal* invalidates, then his position would be entirely consistent with the broad *sevara* — thought about an invalidating act is itself invalidating. But the *gemara* then immediately pulls in the other direction: *Rabbi Yehuda* elsewhere states *hechnis bishogeg kasher*, *ha b'meizid pasul* — bringing the blood inside by mistake leaves the *chatas* valid; only doing so intentionally invalidates. And *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the invalidation occurs specifically at the moment of *zerika* inside the *heichal*, not at the moment of bringing it in. If even the act of bringing the blood in does not invalidate unless one performs *zerika* inside, then certainly a mere *machshava* to bring it in would not invalidate. A *kal vachomer*: *ilu ayeilei i kiper, in i lo kiper lo* — when you actually bring it in, the *korban* is only invalidated if you perform *zerika* there; *hacha dechashiv chishuvey lo kol shekein* — here, where you merely thought about bringing it in, certainly it would not invalidate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵית לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שְׁלִישִׁי?! וְהָתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הִכְנִיס בְּשׁוֹגֵג – כָּשֵׁר. הָא בְּמֵזִיד – פָּסוּל; וְקַיְימָא לַן בְּשֶׁכִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And** does **Rabbi Yehuda not accept** the requirement of a place of **three** functions? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (82a) that **Rabbi Yehuda said:** If **he brought** the blood into the Sanctuary **unwittingly,** the blood remains **fit** for presenting, from which it may be inferred: **But** if he brought it in **intentionally,** it is **unfit; and we maintain** that Rabbi Yehuda deems it unfit only **where** one actually **effected atonement,** i.e., he presented the blood on the inner altar.

*Tosafot* notes, incidentally, that this *kal vachomer* is strange on its face, since it would seem to undermine the entire institution of *pigul* — after all, eating the meat tomorrow also does not currently invalidate anything, yet a *machshava* to do so produces *pigul*. *Tosafot* attempts an answer, though it is not fully satisfying, and the *gemara* presses on regardless.

### Two Tannai'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה הָתָם (עָיְלִי עָיְלָא) [דְּעַיּוֹלֵי עַיְּילֵיהּ], אִי כִּיפֵּר אִין אִי לֹא כִּיפֵּר לָא; הָכָא דְּחַשֵּׁב חַשּׁוֹבֵי, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Now,** it stands to reason that **if there,** where one actually **brought** the blood inside, only **if he effected atonement, yes,** it is unfit, but **if he did not effect atonement** it is **not** unfit, then **here, where** he merely **thought** to bring it inside, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that it should be valid? The Gemara answers: There are **two *tanna'im*, and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** concerning a place with threefold functionality.

The *gemara* now resolves the contradictions by invoking the standard mechanism: *trei tannai v'aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda* — two *tanna'im* transmit *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position differently. One *tanna*, the author of the *kol davar ra baraita*, holds that *Rabbi Yehuda* says a *machshava* about bringing the blood of an outer *chatas* into the *heichal* does invalidate, consistent with the broad principle that a *machshava* about any invalidating act is itself invalidating. A second *tanna*, the author of the *mishna* who does not record *Rabbi Yehuda* as arguing about this case, holds that *Rabbi Yehuda* exempts thoughts related to the *heichal* due to the requirement of *makom meshulash*, and the *kal vachomer* from actual bringing-in-without-*zerika* further supports this exemption. Similarly, a separate contradiction exists in the *baraita* about whether *Rabbi Yehuda* includes a *chatas* slaughtered in the south under the *lav* of *kol davar ra* — one *baraita* includes it, another explicitly excludes it by limiting *kol davar ra* to an intrinsic defect in the animal itself, like a *mum*, rather than a procedural error in the *avodah*. Here too the *gemara* concludes *trei tannai aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda*.

## Rabbi Abba's Claim

### Piggul Still Applies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, שֶׁחוֹזֵר וְקוֹבְעוֹ לְפִיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Abba says:** Although Rabbi Yehuda says that an offering is rendered not valid if it was slaughtered with the intent that its blood would be left over until the next day, **Rabbi Yehuda concedes that** if the priest subsequently collected, conveyed, or presented the blood with the intent to sacrifice or partake of the offering beyond its designated time, **he then establishes** the offering **as *piggul*.** The intent to leave it overnight does not prevent it from being rendered *piggul*, in contrast to intent to sacrifice or partake of it outside its designated area, or performing a sacrificial rite of the Paschal offering or a sin offering not for its own sake, which do prevent the offering from subsequently being rendered *piggul* (see 29b).

*Rabbi Abba* now advances a novel claim: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda she-chozer v'kovah l'pigul* — *Rabbi Yehuda* agrees that even where the *machshava l'haniach* has rendered the *korban pasul*, one can still subsequently create *pigul*. In other words, once a *machshava l'haniach* has invalidated the *korban*, a subsequent *machshava* of *chutz l'zmano* during the *avodah* would transform it into *pigul*. This is a striking claim, because the standard rule is that a *korban* already invalidated for another reason cannot then become *pigul*. *Rabbi Abba* argues that the generic *machshava l'haniach* of *Rabbi Yehuda* — which is not a formal *Torah*-category disqualification like *chutz l'makomo* — lacks the power to permanently remove the *korban* from the *pigul* framework.

### Piggul Before Zerika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תִּדַּע – דְּפִיגּוּל לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה לָא כְּלוּם הוּא, וְאָתְיָא זְרִיקָה וְקָבְעָה לַהּ בְּפִיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Know** that this is so, **as** intent to sprinkle the blood the next day, which renders an offering ***piggul*** and which inherently includes the intent to leave the blood over until the next day so that he can then sprinkle it, **is nothing before** the **sprinkling** of the blood. And his intent during the slaughter to sprinkle the blood the next day does not render the offering *piggul* until the blood is sprinkled, **and** then the **sprinkling** of the blood **comes and establishes** the offering **as *piggul*.** Clearly, the intent to leave the blood over until the next day does not prevent the offering from subsequently being rendered *piggul*.

*Rava* attempts to support *Rabbi Abba*'s position with an analogy. *D'basar lifnei zerika lav klum hi* — the meat before the *zerika* is not yet fit to be eaten. Nevertheless, a *machshava* of *chutz l'zmano* during the *zerika* produces *pigul* for that meat. *V'asya zerika v'kavah lah b'pigul* — the *zerika* comes and establishes *pigul* status, even though the meat was unfit beforehand. So too, *Rava* argues, even after a *machshava l'haniach* has made the *korban pasul*, a subsequent *machshava* of *chutz l'zmano* should be able to establish *pigul*.

### Two Machshavos Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; הָתָם הוּא חֲדָא מַחְשָׁבָה הִיא, הָכָא תְּרֵי מַחְשָׁבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rava's proof: **But it is not so** that this is a valid proof, as **there,** with regard to standard *piggul* intent, **it is one intent** that is established with the sprinkling of the blood. By contrast, **here,** where he initially had intent to leave the blood until the next day and subsequently had intent to sprinkle it beyond its designated time, there are **two** separate **intents.** Therefore, since an improper intent already exists, the offering cannot thereafter be rendered *piggul*.

The *gemara* rejects the analogy immediately: *v'lo hi*. The cases are not comparable. *Hasam hu chad machshava hi* — in the normal *pigul* case, there is only one *machshava*, the *machshava* of *chutz l'zmano* during the *zerika*. The meat was not yet fit to be eaten, but this was not because of any independent *machshava* that had actively invalidated it; it was simply not yet ready, waiting for the *zerika* to permit it. *Hacha trei machshavos* — here there are two distinct *machshavos*: one that actively invalidated the *korban*, and a subsequent one attempting to impose *pigul*. An active invalidating *machshava* removes the *korban* from the *pigul* category entirely; a subsequent *pigul* thought cannot restore it.

### Blood Tomorrow Invalidates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַבִּי אַבָּא: לִיתֵּן אֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה, לְמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה; לְאַלְתַּר – כָּשֵׁר. חָזַר וְחִישֵּׁב חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. לְמָחָר – פָּסוּל. חָזַר וְחִישֵּׁב בֵּין חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ בֵּין חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna raised an objection to Rabbi Abba** from a *baraita*: If one slaughtered an offering with the intent **to place** the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **below** the red line, or to place the blood that is to be placed **below** the red line **above** the red line, and he had intent to do so **immediately,** i.e., on the same day, then it is **valid,** as such intent does not disqualify the offering. If **he then had intent** to sacrifice the offering **outside its** designated **area,** it is **disqualified, but there is no** liability to receive ***karet* for it.** If he had intent to sacrifice it **beyond its** designated **time,** then it is rendered ***piggul*, and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for it.** The *baraita* continues: If one had intent to place the blood that is to be placed above the red line below the red line or vice versa **the next day,** then it is **disqualified,** due to his intent to leave it overnight, in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. In this case, if **he then had intent** to sacrifice the same offering **either beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area,** it is **disqualified, and there is no** liability to receive ***karet* for it.** Evidently, intent to leave it overnight prevents the offering from subsequently being rendered *piggul*. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Abba** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

*Rav Huna* challenges *Rabbi Abba* directly from a *baraita*. The *baraita* addresses a sequence of thoughts during the *avodah*. If you intended to place the blood above when it should go below, or below when it should go above — intending it *la'altar*, immediately — then according to the *chachamim*, since it is neither *chutz l'zmano* nor *chutz l'makomo*, the *korban* is *kasher*. If you then had a thought of *chutz l'makomo* — intending a genuine wrong-place *zerika* — the *korban* is *pasul v'ein bo kares*, the standard *chutz l'makomo* result. And if you had a *chutz l'zmano* thought, the *korban* is *pigul v'chayavin alav kares*. So far, nothing surprising: the first thought was inert, and the subsequent *chutz l'zmano* thought created *pigul*.

Now the *baraita* introduces the *l'machar* variant. If your intention to place the blood in the wrong position was framed as doing so *tomorrow* — that is, you plan to place it in the wrong place not now but the next day — this is functionally equivalent to *machshava l'haniach*, planning to leave the blood over. And the *baraita* rules: *pasul* — this is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position.

### Cannot Become Piggul After

The *baraita* then states its critical conclusion: *chozar v'chishev bein chutz l'zmano bein chutz l'makomo pasul v'ein bo kares* — if after this *machshava l'haniach* you then had a thought of *chutz l'zmano* or *chutz l'makomo*, the *korban* is *pasul* but carries no *kares*. It does not become *pigul*. *Tyuvta d'Rabbi Abba tyuvta* — this is a complete refutation of *Rabbi Abba*'s position. Once the *machshava l'haniach* has rendered the *korban pasul*, even *Rabbi Yehuda* agrees that no subsequent *machshava* of *chutz l'zmano* can elevate it to *pigul* status.

## Tamei Eating as Piggul

### Tamei Eating Tomorrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא בַּר סֵילָא: חִישֵּׁב שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּהוּ טְמֵאִים לְמָחָר – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Ravina bar Sila says:** If one slaughtered an offering with **intent that impure** individuals **would eat** the meat of the offering **the next day,** one is **liable** to receive *karet* due the prohibition of *piggul*. Although impure individuals are not fit to partake of the meat, this is nevertheless considered intent to partake of it beyond its designated time.

*Rav Chisda* cites a ruling in the name of *Ravina bar Shila*: *chishev sheyachalu tmei'im l'machar chayav* — if during the *avodah* one had a *machshava* that *tmei'im* would eat the meat tomorrow, one is *chayav*, meaning this constitutes *pigul*. This is surprising on two counts. First, we discussed earlier that thoughts involving other people — *tmei'im* eating — are not under one's control and therefore do not invalidate. But here, the thought is structured as a *machsheva l'achila*, a thought of eating, directed toward the next day, which is the classic *pigul* framework. It is not a *machshava* about an external invalidating act; it is a *machsheva* of consumption framed in *chutz l'zmano* terms. The fact that the eaters are *tmei'im* might seem to take it out of the category of valid *achilas adam*, but *Rav Chisda* holds that since it remains a *machsheva* about eating, it is sufficient to produce *pigul*.

### Meat Before Zerika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תִּדַּע, דְּבָשָׂר לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה לָא חֲזֵי, וְכִי מְחַשֵּׁב בֵּיהּ – מִיפְּסִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Know** that this is so, **as meat prior to** the **sprinkling** of the blood is **not fit** to be eaten, **and when** one **has** improper **intent with regard to it,** it is **disqualified.** Clearly, intent to partake of a forbidden item beyond its designated time renders the offering *piggul*.

*Rava* once again supports the ruling with an analogy. The meat before *zerika* is unfit to be eaten — *d'basar lifnei zerika lo chazi*. Nevertheless, a *machshava* during the *zerika* about eating it *chutz l'zmano* constitutes *pigul*. So too, a *machshava* of eating by *tmei'im* — even though the act of eating by *tmei'im* makes the meat unfit — should still qualify as a *machsheva l'achila* sufficient for *pigul*.

### Unfit vs. Unready

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; הָתָם זָרֵיק וּמִיחֲזֵי, הָכָא לָא מִיחְזֵי כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rava's proof: **But it is not so,** as **there,** with regard to standard *piggul* intent, **he sprinkles** the blood, **and** the meat **is rendered fit** to be eaten the next day. By contrast, **here,** it is **not rendered fit** to be eaten by impure individuals **at all.** Therefore, such intent is not considered significant intent to consume the meat after its designated time.

The *gemara* again rejects *Rava*'s analogy: *v'lo hi*. *Hasam zerika zorek u'michzi* — in the normal *pigul* case, the *zerika* is being performed, and through the *zerika* the meat will become fit to be eaten. The meat is not yet fit, but it is on its way to becoming fit; there is nothing about it that makes it inherently or permanently unfit. *Hacha lo michzi klal* — here, with a plan to have *tmei'im* eat it, the meat is not fit at all, and the very mode of eating one is contemplating makes the meat unfit rather than being a form of valid consumption. The *gemara* thus distinguishes between meat that is merely not yet ready and meat that is rendered unfit by the very contemplated act. *Tosafot* notes that this reasoning requires distinguishing the *tmei'im* case from the earlier discussions involving dogs or fire eating the meat, where *machsheva l'achila* was still recognized as potentially operative.

### Pesach/Toda Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, מַרְגְּלָא בְּפוּמֵּיהּ דְּרַב דִּימִי בַּר חִינָּנָא: בְּשַׂר פֶּסַח שֶׁלֹּא הוּצְלָה, וְלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ – חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Dimi bar Ḥinnana was wont to say** the following *halakha*: With regard to **the meat of a Paschal offering that was not roasted, and the loaves of a thanks offering** from **which** the priests' portion **was not separated,** although it is prohibited for them to be eaten, nevertheless **one is liable** to receive *karet* **due to** violating the prohibition against eating **them** in a state of **impurity.**

*Rav Chanina bar Ravina* — cited by *Rav Chisda* — raises two additional cases. *Basar Pesach shelo hutzla* — the meat of a *Pesach* that has not been roasted. After the blood has been thrown, the meat is permitted from a *kodashim* perspective, but the *Pesach* specifically must be eaten roasted, and eating it raw violates *lo tochlu mimenu na*. If a *tamei* person were to eat this raw *Pesach* meat, would they be *chayav kares* for eating *kodashim b'tuma*? Similarly, *lachmei todah shelo hurmu* — the bread loaves accompanying a *todah*, before one out of every ten has been separated and given to the *kohen*, which functions like *terumah* and prohibits eating the rest. After the blood of the *todah* has been thrown, the bread is permitted from a *kodashim* standpoint, but prohibited from the *terumah* standpoint. If a *tamei* person ate this bread, would they be *chayav kares*? The ruling, cited by *Rav Chisda*, is *chayavin aleihem mishum tumah* — yes, they are *chayav*, since the *korban* dimension of the *kodashim* has been completed and the material is fit from that perspective, even though there is an additional prohibition making it currently forbidden.

### Emurim After Zerika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תִּדַּע, דְּתַנְיָא: ״אֲשֶׁר לַה׳״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים לְטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Know** that this is so, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "But the soul that eats of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings that pertain to the Lord, having his impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:20). The added words **"that pertain to the Lord"** serve **to include the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity with regard to impurity,** teaching that one who partakes of them while impure is liable to receive *karet*.

*Rava* attempts a third analogy in support of *Rav Chisda*'s ruling about *tmei'im*. The *baraita* cited by the *gemara* derives from the *pasuk* *asher la-Hashem* (Vayikra 7:21) that eating the *emurim* — the portions of the *korban* designated for the *mizbeach* — while *tamei* makes one *chayav kares*. This is remarkable because *emurim* are not food for human beings and are indeed prohibited from human consumption; they must be placed on the *mizbeach*. Yet once the blood has been thrown, eating them in *tumah* carries *kares*. So too, *Rava* argues, a *machsheva* about having *tmei'im* eat the meat should qualify, since we see that even non-food items can generate *chiyuv* in the *tumah* framework.

### Emurim Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; הָתָם – אֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים חֲזוּ לְגָבוֹהַּ; לְאַפּוֹקֵי בְּשַׂר פֶּסַח שֶׁלֹּא הוּצְלָה וְלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ – דְּלָא חֲזוּ לָא לְגָבוֹהַּ וְלָא לְהֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rava's proof: **But it is not so,** as **there, the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity** that are burned on the altar are at least **fit for** the **Most High,** i.e., for the consumption of the altar, and therefore one is liable to receive *karet* for eating them while ritually impure. This is **to the exclusion of the meat of a Paschal offering that was not roasted and** the **loaves of a thanks offering** from **which** the priests' portion **was not separated, which are not fit** at all, **not for** the **Most High, and not for an ordinary** person.

The *gemara* once again rejects this: *v'lo hi*. *Hasam imurei kodshi kalim chazu legavoah* — there, once the blood is thrown, the *emurim* have fully reached their intended purpose. They are completely fit for what they were designated to do — to be placed on the *mizbeach*. Even though they are not fit for human consumption, they have achieved their final halachic state. *L'afukei basar pesach shelo nitzla v'lachmei todah shelo hurmu* — as opposed to the roasted meat of *Pesach* and the *todah* breads, which in their current state are fit neither for *gavoah* nor for *hediot*: the *Pesach* meat is not yet roasted, and the breads have not yet been separated. These items have not fully completed their process, and therefore one could argue they remain outside the full *chiyuv kares* for *tumah*. The distinction is between something that has fully arrived at its purpose and something still in an intermediate state.

## Blood Placement Debate

### Minimal Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, שֶׁנָּתַן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר. וּבְחַטָּאת – שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף חַטָּאת שֶׁנְּתָנָהּ מַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say:** With regard to **all** the offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed on the external altar,** even those that require that the blood be sprinkled on two opposite corners of the altar so that it will run down each of its four sides, in a case **where** the priest **placed** the blood on the altar **with** only **one placement, he facilitated atonement. And in** the case of **a sin offering,** which requires four placements, one on each of the four corners of the altar, at least **two placements** are necessary to facilitate atonement. **And Beit Hillel say: Even** with regard to **a sin offering,** in a case **where** the priest **placed** the blood with **one placement, he facilitated atonement** after the fact.

With the conclusion of *Perek Kol HaPsulim*, the *gemara* transitions into the opening *mishna* of the next *perek*. The subject shifts to a debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* about how many *matanos* — applications of blood — are minimally required for the *korban* to be valid *bedieved*. The *mishna* enters this topic not for its own sake but because of its implications for *pigul*, as the word *l'fichach* ("therefore") indicates. *Beis Shammai* holds that for any *korban* whose blood is applied to the outer *mizbeach* — where the standard procedure involves two diagonal corner applications, effectively hitting all four walls — a single application suffices *bedieved*. The *pasuk* *kiper* implies that even one placement achieves atonement. For a *chatas*, where four finger applications at four corners are required, *Beis Shammai* requires two applications *bedieved*. *Beis Hillel* is more lenient: even a *chatas* is valid *bedieved* with a single application.

### First Valid, Second Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אִם נָתַן אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ – כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** since the priest facilitates atonement with one placement in all cases other than a sin offering according to Beit Shammai, and even in the case of a sin offering according to Beit Hillel, **if he placed the first** placement **in its proper manner, and the second** with the intent to eat the offering **beyond its** designated **time, he facilitated atonement.** Since the second placement is not indispensable with regard to achieving atonement, improper intent while performing that rite does not invalidate the offering.

The *mishna* draws out the *pigul* implications of this dispute. Suppose the first placement is done properly, *ketiknah*, and the second is done with a *chutz l'zmanah* thought. According to *Beis Hillel* — and for any non-*chatas* according to *Beis Shammai* — the first application alone was sufficient to establish the *korban*'s validity. The *korban* is therefore already valid at that point, and the subsequent *pigul* thought during the second application cannot retroactively invalidate it. *Im nasan et harishonah ketiknah vehashniyah chutz l'zmanah, kiper* — the *korban* is valid, and the additional applications, though a *mitzvah* to perform, are no longer definitionally constitutive of the *korban*. Once the minimum has been achieved, the *korban* is sealed.

### Both Invalid Remains Piggul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נָתַן אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה חוּץ לִמְקוֹמָהּ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** based on the same reasoning, **if he placed the first** placement with the intent to eat the offering **beyond its** designated **time and** he placed **the second** placement with the intent to eat the offering **outside its** designated **area,** the second of which does not render an offering *piggul*, the offering is ***piggul*,** an offering disqualified by improper intention, **and one is liable** to receive **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*] for its** consumption. This is because the intent that accompanied the second placement does not supersede the *piggul* status of the offering.

Conversely, if the first application was done with a *chutz l'zmanah* thought — making the *korban pigul* — and the second application was done with a *chutz l'makomo* thought, one might expect the second *machshava* to remove the *pigul* status, since ordinarily a subsequent invalidating *machshava* of *chutz l'makomo* takes the *korban* out of *pigul* and reduces it to the lesser status of *pasul* without *kares*. But here this does not apply, because after the first *matanah* the *korban* is already complete — whatever its status. The *pigul* is established by the first application, and the subsequent applications, being beyond what is needed, cannot alter the *korban*'s status in either direction. *V'im nasan et harishonah chutz l'zmanah vehashniyah chutz l'makomo, pigul v'chayavin alav kares* — it remains *pigul* with *kares*.

### Inner Mizbei'ach Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי – שֶׁאִם חִיסֵּר אַחַת מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, כְּאִילּוּ לֹא כִּיפֵּר. לְפִיכָךְ, נָתַן כּוּלָּן כְּתִיקְנָן וְאַחַת שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – פְּסוּלָה, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** All this applies to those offerings whose blood is to be placed on the external altar. But with regard to **all** the offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed on the inner altar, if** the priest **omitted** even **one of the placements, it is as though he did not facilitate atonement. Therefore,** if **he placed all** the placements **in their proper manner, and one** placement **in an improper manner,** i.e., with the intent to eat the offering beyond its designated time, the offering is **disqualified,** as it is lacking one placement; **but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** one who partakes of the offering, as the improper intention related to only part of the blood that renders the offering permitted for consumption, and an offering becomes *piggul* only when the improper intention relates to the entire portion of the offering that renders it permitted for consumption.

For *korbanot* whose blood is applied to the inner *mizbeach* — the *mizbeach hazahav* inside the *heichal*, used for *chatas hapenimit* — all four corner applications are essential. Unlike the outer *mizbeach*, where a single application suffices *bedieved*, the inner *mizbeach* requires all four, and *she'im chisar achas min hamatanos k'ilu lo kiper* — missing even one is as if no atonement was achieved. The *mishna* therefore rules: *nasan kullan ketiknah v'achas shelo ketiknah psulah v'ein bo kares* — if three applications were done correctly and one was done with a thought of *chutz l'zmano* or *chutz l'makomo*, the *korban* is *pasul* but carries no *kares*.

### Partial Invalid Not Piggul

This is the crux: why does even a *chutz l'zmano* thought during one of the four inner applications not produce *pigul*? The *mishna*'s *tanna* holds that since all four applications together constitute a single act of *kippur*, a *machshava* of *pigul* during only one of the four — only one quarter of the *matir* — is not sufficient to create *pigul*. This parallels the earlier *gemara* discussion of *mifaglin b'chatzi matir*: a *pigul* thought during only part of the *matir* does not create *pigul*. Here, since each of the four applications is an indispensable component of the single *matir* act, a *pigul* thought on one component alone is *machshava* on a fraction of the *matir*, insufficient to establish *pigul* and its *kares*. The *korban* is *pasul* — invalidated by the wrong thought — but *ein bo kares*. The discussion of the next *perek* will continue from here.
