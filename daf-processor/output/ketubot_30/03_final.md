# Ketubot 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Knas & Prohibited Status

### Knas Prohibited Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבָא עַל הַנִּדָּה שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Everyone agrees with regard to one who engaged in** forced **intercourse with a menstruating woman that he pays** the **fine.**

The sugya opens with a statement of Rav Chisda, found approximately seven or eight lines into the daf. The background issue concerns the *knas* — the statutory fine imposed upon a man who rapes or seduces a young, unmarried girl — specifically in cases where the woman is someone he is prohibited from marrying, whether due to an *issur kares* or an *issur lav*. The *mishnah* has already stated that even in such cases a *knas* is owed. Yet the upcoming *mishnah* will rule that when the act entails a *chiyuv misah* — when the woman is forbidden to him under penalty of court execution — no *knas* is paid. The reasoning behind that latter ruling will become clearer as the discussion unfolds.

### Niddah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ בָּהּ הֲוָיָה — הָא נָמֵי יֵשׁ בָּהּ הֲוָיָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רְאוּיָה לְקַיְּימָהּ — הָא נָמֵי רְאוּיָה לְקַיְּימָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to the one who says** that the criterion is whether **there is betrothal,** for **this** woman **too there is betrothal. According to the one who says** that the criterion is whether the woman is **suitable for** him to **sustain, this** woman **is suitable for** him to **sustain.**

Before engaging the broader debate, Rav Chisda introduces a point of consensus: *hakol modim bevo'al haniddah meshalem knas* — everyone agrees that a man who has relations with a *niddah* does pay the fine, even though the act constitutes an *issur kares*. The question is why this case is uncontroversial.

### Niddah Status

The *Gemara* addresses this from two angles, corresponding to the two opinions about what qualifies a woman for *knas*. According to the view that the woman must be someone to whom *kiddushin* is binding (*yesh bah havayah*), this too is unproblematic — *kiddushin* is perfectly valid when performed with a *niddah*. There is a separate and interesting question regarding *nisu'in*: can *chuppah* take place when the woman is a *niddah*? Ashkenazim permit it; Sephardim as a matter of principle do not, since *nisu'in* is bound up with the initiation of shared marital life, which presupposes the possibility of conjugal relations. But *kiddushin* unambiguously takes effect even when the woman is a *niddah*. According to the other view — that the question is whether the man is *permitted* to remain married to this woman (*re'uyah lekayyema*) — the answer is equally clear: a man is permitted to be married to a *niddah*, even though he may not currently have relations with her. Her status as a *niddah* is a transitory condition, not a permanent disqualification from marriage.

This analysis reveals a crucial conceptual distinction that runs throughout the entire discussion. Until now, the debate about *knas* when there is a prohibition has focused on the *woman's personal status* — whether she is someone to whom marriage can bind, or whom the man may lawfully keep as a wife. The *niddah* case raises something different: there is nothing wrong with her personal status, yet the *act itself* — the act of intercourse — was an *issur kares*. The same applies to any of the cases in our *mishnah* where the man engages in an act that is prohibited under penalty of *kares*, independent of any issue with her personal status. The question then becomes: should that *issur kares* of the act itself — not of the person — override the *knas* through the principle of *kim lei bidrabba minei*?

This is precisely the tension the *mishnah* seems to navigate. When there is a *chiyuv misah*, the next *mishnah* exempts from payment — and that is readily understood as an application of *kim lei*: the court cannot simultaneously prosecute for a capital crime and impose a monetary fine. But our *mishnah* assumes that an *issur kares* does *not* trigger *kim lei*. It is against this assumption that the *Gemara* now introduces a dissenting opinion.

## R. Nechunya Kim Lei

### R. Nechunya Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **And** the mishna's ruling that one who has relations with his sister is liable to pay the fine comes **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana,**

The *Gemara* introduces the phrase *ul'afokei mid'Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah* — the ruling that *knas* is paid even in the case of an *issur kares* comes to exclude, to reject, the position of Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah. For Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah holds that even in a case of *kares* we apply *kim lei bidrabba minei*, and the weightier prohibition overrides the monetary liability.

### Haystack Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה אֶת יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים כַּשַּׁבָּת לְתַשְׁלוּמִין. מָה שַׁבָּת — מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ וּפָטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין, אַף יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ וּפָטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana would render Yom Kippur like Shabbat with regard to payment** for damages. **Just as** one who intentionally desecrates **Shabbat is liable to** receive the **death** penalty **and** is therefore **exempt from** the obligation of **payment** for damages caused while desecrating Shabbat, **so too,** one who intentionally desecrates **Yom Kippur is liable to** receive the **death** penalty **and** is therefore **exempt from** the obligation of **payment** for damages caused while desecrating Yom Kippur.

This position is expressed in a *baraita*: *Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah hayah oseh Yom HaKippurim ke-Shabbos le-tashlumin* — Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah treated Yom Kippur like Shabbat with respect to payment obligations. The reference point is the classic case of burning down someone's haystack on Shabbat: the perpetrator is exempt from damages, because the very act that caused the loss — the burning — was simultaneously an act of *chilul Shabbos*, which carries capital liability. This is the principle of *kim lei*: the court must choose how to prosecute the matter, and the capital crime overshadows the civil one. Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah extends this: if one burned down a friend's haystack on Yom Kippur, even though that transgression is punishable by *kares* rather than court execution, it would equally exempt from payment. *Mah Shabbos mitchayev benafsho patur min hatashlumin, af Yom HaKippurim mitchayev benafsho patur min hatashlumin* — just as one who becomes liable with his very life on Shabbat is exempt from monetary payment, so too one who becomes liable "with his life" on Yom Kippur — understood as the excision of the soul — is likewise exempt.

This position is, as the *Gemara* signals, a significant *chiddush* on multiple levels. First, *kares* is objectively a less severe penalty than court-imposed execution. Second — and more fundamentally — *kim lei* is typically understood as a principle operating within the framework of the *beit din*. The court cannot prosecute a person simultaneously for a capital crime and for civil damages; it must, as it were, choose its docket. But *kares* is entirely a matter between the person and God. It is not a court sanction at all. Why should the *beit din*, adjudicating a civil claim for property damage, be deflected by a divine punishment that is not within the court's purview? One can understand why an act deserving of execution — even if technically unprosecutable due to lack of witnesses, lack of *hasra'ah*, or because the act was done *beshogeg* — still exempts from payment, since the *nature* of the act was a capital offense. But to say the same about something that is categorically and exclusively a *chiyuv* toward Heaven, never an issue for the court — that is the considerable *chiddush* of Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah.

## Ason Tragedy Basis

### Abaye Ason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נֶאֱמַר "אָסוֹן״ בִּידֵי אָדָם. וְנֶאֱמַר ״אָסוֹן״ בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. מָה ״אָסוֹן״ הָאָמוּר בִּידֵי אָדָם — פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין, אַף ״אָסוֹן״ הָאָמוּר בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם — פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana? Abaye said: It states** the word **harm at the hands of man,** in the verse "But if any harm follow, then you shall give a soul for a soul" (Exodus 21:23) and **it states** the word **harm at the hand of Heaven,** in the verse in which Jacob states: "My son shall not descend with you…and harm befalls him on the way" (Genesis 42:38). **Just as** with regard to **harm that is stated at the hands of man,** e.g., one who kills and is liable to be executed, one is **exempt from the** associated **payment, so too,** with regard to **harm that is stated at the hand of Heaven,** one is **exempt from the** associated **payment.**

The *Gemara* now asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah?* — what is the underlying source for this position? Abaye derives it from the concept of *ason* in Scripture. The relevant passage describes two men fighting, and one of them strikes a pregnant woman standing nearby, causing a miscarriage. The Torah states: *im ason yihyeh venasata nefesh tachas nafesh* — if there is a tragedy, you give a life for a life — and by implication, *lo yihyeh ason*, if there is no tragedy, you pay the monetary fine for the miscarriage. The rabbinic reading is that *ason* refers to the death of an already-born person in the incident. If such a death occurs, the perpetrator faces the capital charge of murder and does not pay the fine for the miscarriage. This is the scriptural basis for *kim lei* itself: when a death arises from the same act, monetary liability is displaced.

The significance for Abaye's derivation lies in the additional usage of the word *ason* — this time in the story of Yaakov and Binyamin, where Yaakov fears that if he sends Binyamin down to Egypt, *ason* will befall him on the way. This *ason* at the hands of Yaakov is understood as a divinely-ordained misfortune — *ason bid'ei shamayim*. The *ason* in the earlier passage, by contrast, is one caused through human agency — *ason bid'ei adam*. Abaye draws the *gezerah shavah*: just as the human-caused *ason* exempts from monetary payment, so too a divinely-ordained *ason* — death or excision brought about by God — similarly exempts. And *kares*, the divinely imposed cutting-off of the soul, falls within the category of *ason bid'ei shamayim*.

Admittedly, the analogy is not seamless. In the original case of *im ason yihyeh*, the tragedy strikes a *victim* — the perpetrator's act caused someone else's death, and *that* death displaces the fine. Here, the *kares* falls upon the *perpetrator* himself. The two situations are structurally distinct. Nevertheless, this is Abaye's derivation.

### Challenge Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מִמַּאי דְּכִי קָא מַזְהַר לְהוּ יַעֲקֹב לִבְנֵיהּ, עַל צִינִּים וּפַחִים דְּבִידֵי שָׁמַיִם נִינְהוּ? דִּלְמָא עַל אַרְיָא וְגַנָּבֵי דְּבִידֵי אָדָם נִינְהוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava** strongly **objects to this: From where** is it derived **that when Jacob is warning his sons** he is warning them **about cold and heat [*tzinim paḥim*], which are at the hand of Heaven? Perhaps** he was warning them **about a lion and thieves, which are** harm **at the hands of man,** meaning that unlike heat and cold, these dangers are not calibrated by God.

Rav Abba bar Abba challenges the derivation. The question is: how do we know that the *ason* Yaakov feared for Binyamin refers specifically to a divine punishment — the kind of providential misfortune (*tzinin u'pachim*, cold and heat, understood as God's direct intervention) — as opposed to something caused through human agency, such as lions and thieves (*arya u'ganavei d'viyedei adam ninhu*)? The *Gemara* is operating here with a specific understanding of *bid'ei shamayim*: it does not simply mean "natural causes" (i.e., anything not done by a person), but specifically means *divinely directed* punishment — something God actively brings about as a consequence of sin. By contrast, *bid'ei adam* encompasses not only human acts but random or negligent occurrences that God did not specifically ordain. On this understanding, lions and thieves might not qualify as *bid'ei shamayim* in the relevant sense, since their actions are not necessarily a divine decree. The challenge, then, is that Yaakov's *ason* might refer to non-divinely-directed tragedies — and if so, it cannot serve as the source for *kares* being an exempting *ason bid'ei shamayim*.

### All Tragedies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ יַעֲקֹב אַהָא אַזְהַר, אַהָא לָא אַזְהַר? יַעֲקֹב עַל כֹּל מִילֵּי אַזְהַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this: **Is that to say** that **Jacob warned** them **about this** harm at the hand of man, but **about that** harm at the hand of Heaven **he did not warn** them? **Jacob warned** them **about all** potentially harmful **matters** that might befall Benjamin, not merely one particular form of catastrophe.

The *Gemara* dismisses the challenge directly: *Yaakov ha mi-kuleihu azhir* — Yaakov was warning about all types of tragedies. When Yaakov says "lest an *ason* occur," he encompasses the entire spectrum of possible misfortune, whether divinely directed, humanly caused, or naturally random. Since his usage of *ason* includes divinely ordained punishment, it suffices to establish the principle — even if it also includes other categories.

### Heat Cold Divine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצִינִּים פַּחִים בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם נִינְהוּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַכֹּל בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם חוּץ מִצִּינִּים פַּחִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צִינִּים פַּחִים בְּדֶרֶךְ עִיקֵּשׁ שׁוֹמֵר נַפְשׁוֹ יִרְחַק מֵהֶם״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are cold** and **heat at the hand of Heaven? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **All** matters are **at the hand of Heaven except for cold** and **heat, as it is stated: "Cold** and **heat are on the path of the crooked, he who guards his soul shall keep far from them"** (Proverbs 22:5)? This indicates that cold and heat are forms of harm caused by man, from which one can protect himself.

The *Gemara* then indulges a delightful digression prompted by the mention of *tzinin u'pachim* (cold and heat). A *baraita* states: *hakol bid'ei shamayim chutz mi-tzinin u'pachim* — everything is in divine hands except for cold and heat. The verse cited is *tzinim pachim b'derech itkesh; shomer nafsho yirchak me-hem* — cold and traps are in the path of the crooked; one who guards his life keeps away from them. Rashi and Tosafot read this as referring to hot and cold weather. The implication is striking: of all the misfortunes that might befall a person, extreme weather is the one that lies within human control — bundle up, seek shade, stay hydrated — and therefore God does not deploy it as an instrument of directed punishment.

But this creates an apparent contradiction. If cold and heat are *bid'ei adam* — within one's own hands to prevent — and lions and thieves are *bid'ei shamayim* — divinely directed — then the earlier challenge would be fully resolved. Yet wait: are lions and thieves really divinely directed? The *Gemara* then cites Rav Yosef, and so too the school of Rabbi Chiyya taught it, that since the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash* and the cessation of the *Sanhedrin*, the four modes of execution were not abolished — *arba misos lo batlu* — meaning that divine providence ensures analogous fates befall those who deserve them. Someone liable to *sekilah* (stoning) will either fall from a roof or be trampled by an animal. Someone liable to *sereifah* (burning) will either perish in a fire or be bitten by a venomous serpent. Someone liable to *harigah* (the sword) will be handed over to the Roman authorities or attacked by bandits. Someone liable to *chenek* (strangulation) will drown or die of a respiratory ailment (*srunki*). The *Gemara* thus confirms that lions and thieves are indeed instruments of divine punishment — *bid'ei shamayim* in the fullest sense — while cold and heat are categorically in one's own hands.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ אַרְיָא וְגַנָּבֵי בִּידֵי אָדָם נִינְהוּ? וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבָּטְלוּ סַנְהֶדְרִין, אַרְבַּע מִיתוֹת לֹא בָּטְלוּ. לֹא בָּטְלוּ?! הָא בָּטְלוּ לְהוּ! אֶלָּא: דִּין אַרְבַּע מִיתוֹת לֹא בָּטְלוּ. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב סְקִילָה — אוֹ נוֹפֵל מִן הַגָּג, אוֹ חַיָּה דּוֹרַסְתּוֹ. וּמִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׂרֵיפָה — אוֹ נוֹפֵל בִּדְלֵיקָה, אוֹ נָחָשׁ מַכִּישׁוֹ. וּמִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב הֲרִיגָה — אוֹ נִמְסָר לַמַּלְכוּת, אוֹ לִיסְטִים בָּאִין עָלָיו. וּמִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב חֶנֶק — אוֹ טוֹבֵעַ בַּנָּהָר, אוֹ מֵת בִּסְרוֹנְכֵי?! אֶלָּא, אֵיפוֹךְ: אַרְיָא וְגַנָּבֵי בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם, צִינִּים וּפַחִים בִּידֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, are a lion and thieves** forms of harm **at the hands of man? But didn't Rav Yosef say, and similarly,** didn't **Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** a *baraita*: **From the day that the Temple was destroyed, although the Sanhedrin was abolished** the **four death** penalties **were not abolished?** The Gemara asks: Were they **not abolished?** It is clear **that they were abolished,** as today there is neither Sanhedrin nor capital punishment. **Rather,** it means that although there are no court-imposed executions, the **punishment** of the **four death** penalties **was not abolished.** How so? **One who was liable** to be executed by **stoning either falls from the roof or a beast tramples him.** That is similar to stoning, which involves being pushed off an elevated place and then stoned. **And one who was liable** to be executed by **burning either falls into a conflagration or a snake bites him,** which creates a burning sensation. **And one who was liable** to be executed by **decapitation** is **either handed over to the** ruling **monarchy** for execution by sword, **or bandits attack** and kill **him. And one who was liable** to be executed by **strangulation either drowns in a river, or dies of diphtheria [*serunki*]. Rather, reverse** the order of the previous statement: **A lion and thieves** are cases of harm **at the hand of Heaven,** while **cold and heat** are cases of harm **at the hands of man.**

Tosafot, noting this, explains that while in principle any misfortune can serve as a vehicle for divinely ordained punishment, cold and heat are the one category God specifically does not use, because the relevant verse teaches that a person can always take preventive measures against them, and God does not override those measures. Everything else — including bandits and wild animals — can be the vehicle through which God exacts what is deserved.

The *Gemara* therefore reverses the terms of the *baraita*: *chaya v'ganavei bid'ei shamayim; tzinin u'pachim bid'ei adam* — lions and thieves are divinely directed; cold and heat are in human hands. And either way, the point stands: when Yaakov says *v'karah ason*, he is speaking about the full range of misfortune, including those that are divinely ordained. It follows that divinely imposed *ason bid'ei shamayim* — and by extension *kares* — belongs in the same category as the court-imposed death that exempts from monetary payment.

Tosafot raises at this juncture what is, frankly, an ancient theological question: if divine punishment invariably catches up with sinners, why do we observe so many sinners dying peacefully in their beds? The question receives a characteristically concise answer — the possibility of *teshuvah* entirely removes the punishment, or a meritorious deed is being repaid in this world, or the reckoning is deferred to the next. The brevity of the treatment is itself telling. As a legal tradition, the *Gemara* allots this question of theodicy two lines in Tosafot and moves on.

### Four Executions

The passage about divine execution also touches on the principle of collective responsibility implicit in the phrase *ba'ish ha'hu u'v'mishpachto* — "that man and his family." Rashi explains that the family is included because no sinner operates without some degree of enablement from those around him. Onkelos, by contrast, translates *mishpachto* as *u'v'sa'adohi* — his supporters — avoiding the presumption that biological family members are necessarily complicit, and instead understanding the term figuratively as referring to those who actively enable him.

## Rava Kim Lei

### Korban Molech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה מֵהָכָא: ״וְאִם הַעְלֵם יַעְלִימוּ עַם הָאָרֶץ אֶת עֵינֵיהֶם מִן הָאִישׁ הַהוּא בְּתִתּוֹ מִזַּרְעוֹ לַמּוֹלֶךְ. וְשַׂמְתִּי אֲנִי אֶת פָּנַי בָּאִישׁ הַהוּא וּבְמִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ וְהִכְרַתִּי אוֹתוֹ״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: כָּרֵת שֶׁלִּי כְּמִיתָה שֶׁלָּכֶם, מָה מִיתָה שֶׁלָּכֶם — פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין. אַף כָּרֵת שֶׁלִּי — פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** an additional explanation: **The rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana is from here.** It is written that one who gives his children to Molech is liable to be executed by stoning: **"And if the people of the land do at all hide their eyes from that man, when he gives of his seed to Molech,** and do not put him to death; **then I will set My face against that man and against his family, and will cut him off [*vehikhrati*]"** (Leviticus 20:4–5). Through the juxtaposition in this verse **the Torah said: My *karet*** is **like your death** penalty; **just as** one who is liable to receive **your death** penalty **is exempt from the** associated **payments, so too,** one who is liable to receive **My *karet* is exempt from the** associated **payments.**

Rava offers an alternative derivation for Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah's position. The proof text he adduces is from the passage concerning one who gives his child to *Molech*: if the people of the land avert their eyes and fail to execute him, God declares *v'samti ani et panai ba'ish ha'hu u'v'mishpachto, v'hichratti oto mikerev ammo* — I will direct My attention toward that man and his family and will cut him off from his people. This is a far more direct source: the Torah explicitly equates God's *kares* with the court's execution. Where the court fails to execute, God steps in with *kares* as the substitute. This establishes the equivalence of the two punishments as a general principle.

### Rava vs Abaye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ זָר שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה — לְאַבָּיֵי פָּטוּר, וּלְרָבָא חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** practical difference **is there between** the opinions of **Rava and Abaye** with regard to the rationale for the opinion of Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to **a non-priest who** intentionally **ate *teruma*.** According **to Abaye** he is **exempt** from paying the priest the value of the *teruma*, as a non-priest who ate *teruma* is liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven. Abaye maintains that the legal status of all forms of death at the hand of Heaven is equivalent to that of death at the hands of man, and therefore, one is exempt from payment. **And** according **to Rava,** who derives the rationale from the juxtaposition between *karet* and death at the hands of man, since a non-priest who ate *teruma* is not liable to receive *karet*, he is **liable** to pay the priest for the *teruma* that he ate.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ikka bein Rava l'Abaye* — what practical difference exists between deriving the principle from Abaye's *ason* source versus Rava's *Molech* source? The answer is the case of *zar she'achal terumah* — a non-*Kohen* who eats *terumah* belonging to a *Kohen*. In doing so, he simultaneously steals (a civil wrong entailing payment) and commits a transgression that is *chayav misah bid'ei shamayim*. The question is whether *kim lei* applies here to exempt him from payment.

It is worth pausing to note that *misah bid'ei shamayim*, despite containing the word *misah*, is understood by the *Rishonim* as actually less severe than *kares*. Rashi holds that *kares* additionally entails the premature death of one's children, while Tosafot disagrees about the precise details; but either way, the halakhic consensus treats *misah bid'ei shamayim* as a lesser category than *kares*.

According to Abaye — who derives *kim lei* from *ason*, encompassing all forms of divinely ordained punishment regardless of degree — even *misah bid'ei shamayim* would suffice to trigger the exemption. *Abaye patur*: the non-*Kohen* who eats the *terumah* is exempt from payment. According to Rava — who derives it from the *Molech* passage specifically equating *kares* with court execution — only *kares*, as the direct divine analogue of *mitat beit din*, qualifies. Anything less severe, such as *misah bid'ei shamayim*, does not. *Rava chayav*: the non-*Kohen* is liable to pay.

## Kares vs Mita

### Terumah Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאַבָּיֵי פָּטוּר?! וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה בְּגוֹנֵב חֶלְבּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ וַאֲכָלוֹ, שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב — שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְחַיֵּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה קוֹדֶם שֶׁבָּא לִידֵי אִיסּוּר חֵלֶב. אַלְמָא דְּמֵעִידָּנָא דְּאַגְבְּהֵיהּ קַנְיֵיהּ, מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ לָא הֲוָה עַד דְּאָכֵיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Abaye,** is a non-priest actually **exempt** from payment for the *teruma*? **But didn't Rav Ḥisda say** that **Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana concedes with regard to** one who **steals another's** forbidden **fat and eats it that he is obligated** to pay for the fat, even though he is liable to receive *karet*, **as he was already liable for theft before he came** to violate **the prohibition** against eating forbidden **fat? Apparently, from the moment he lifts** the fat to steal **it he acquired it,** and he bears responsibility to repay it, but **he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty **only when he eats it.**

With the dispute between Abaye and Rava established, the *Gemara* turns back to scrutinize Abaye's position more carefully. *Vha'amar Rav Chisda: modeh Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah begonev chelvo shel chaveiro v'achalo shehu chayav* — Rav Chisda stated that even Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah concedes that one who steals another's *chelev* (forbidden fat) and then eats it is liable to pay. The reason: *shekevar nitchayev bigniva kodem sheba lidei issur chelev* — the obligation to pay arose at the moment of theft, *before* the transgression of eating the forbidden fat occurred. The moment he lifted the *chelev*, he acquired it through the act of theft and became liable for payment. The *chiyuv nefesh* — the liability that might trigger *kim lei* — only arose later, when he ate it. Since the two liabilities arose at separate points in time, one cannot overshadow the other.

## One Act Temporal

### Theft Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, בְּעִידָּנָא דְּאַגְבְּהֵיהּ — קַנְיֵיהּ, מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ לָא הָוֵי עַד דְּאָכֵיל לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Here, too,** with regard to a non-priest who ate *teruma*, **at the moment he lifts** the *teruma* **he acquired it** and is responsible to repay it, and **he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty **only when he eats it.**

This principle — that temporal separation between the civil and the religious liability prevents *kim lei* from applying — now becomes the central analytical focus. All the cases considered until now involved the civil and religious dimensions of the *same act*: the burning of the haystack was simultaneously the *chilul Shabbos* and the property damage; the sexual act was simultaneously the *kares* violation and the basis for the *knas*. But if the theft and the eating are separated even by a split second, they are two distinct events and each is prosecuted independently.

### Forced Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁתָּחַב לוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו. סוֹף סוֹף כֵּיוָן דְּלַעֲסֵיהּ קַנְיֵיהּ, מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ לָא הָוֵי עַד דְּבַלְעַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where another inserted** the *teruma* **into his mouth.** In that case, acquisition and liability to receive the death penalty are simultaneous. The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, once he chewed** the *teruma* **he acquired it** and is liable to pay, and **he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty **only when he swallows it.**

The *Gemara* therefore constructs increasingly precise hypotheticals to create scenarios in which the two liabilities genuinely coincide. *Hacha bemai askinan? Kegon she'dachvo chaveiro letoch piv* — what is the case we are discussing? It is where one's friend forcibly thrust the *terumah* into his mouth. If that happened, the person never lifted the food himself and therefore never committed the act of *gneivah* through lifting. The act of eating might then simultaneously be the act of acquiring (and thus the theft) and the act of consuming *terumah* (the religious violation). But the *Gemara* objects: *sof sof, keivan dile'asei kanyei* — in the end, when he chews it, he acquires it. And *nitchayev benafsho lo havai ad de'balyei* — the religious prohibition of eating *terumah* is only transgressed at swallowing, not chewing. So even here, the civil liability (at chewing) and the religious liability (at swallowing) are still temporally distinct.

### Spitting Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן שֶׁתָּחַב לוֹ לְתוֹךְ בֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּמָצֵי לְאַהְדּוֹרַהּ — נֶיהְדַּר. אִי לָא מָצֵי לְאַהְדּוֹרַהּ — אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּמָצֵי לְאַהְדּוֹרַהּ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is a case **where** another **inserted it into the pharynx,** so the liability for payment and liability for the death penalty were both achieved through swallowing. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** it is **possible to retrieve** the *teruma* by removing it without ruining it, **let him retrieve** it. If one does so, he would not be liable to pay. If he fails to do so, liability or payment precedes liability for the death penalty. **If** it is **not possible to retrieve** the *teruma*, **why** is he **liable?** He did nothing; another person inserted the food in his throat. The Gemara answers: It is **necessary only** in a situation **where** it is **possible to retrieve** the *teruma* **under duress,** with great effort.

The *Gemara* refines the scenario further: *kegon she'dachvo lo letoch beit habli'ah* — the friend thrust it directly into the throat. Now, swallowing is the act in question, and swallowing would simultaneously be the act of eating *terumah* and the act of civil liability. But the *Gemara* challenges: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? *Ei dematzi le'ahadura, nahadar* — if he can spit it back out, let him do so. *Ei lo matzi le'ahadura, amai chayav* — if he cannot, why is he liable at all, since everything was compelled?

Rashi's resolution is illuminating: if he *can* spit it out but chooses not to, it is that very choice — the decision not to return it — that constitutes the act of *gneivah*. And the subsequent swallowing, which is the act that triggers the religious prohibition, comes *after* the theft is already complete. So they are still not simultaneous. The *Gemara* therefore specifies: *lo tzricha dematzi le'ahadura adei d'chak* — the case is where he can spit it out, but only with great difficulty. Rashi explains that by this point the food has become revolting, such that spitting it out would return nothing of value — it is already effectively destroyed. Consequently, the friend who initially thrust it in is the one who caused the *nezek*, and our person cannot be said to have stolen anything by not spitting it out (since the object is already ruined). Yet when he swallows, he derives benefit from something that belonged to another.

### Stomach Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁתָּחַב לוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ מַשְׁקִין שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה לְתוֹךְ פִּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** It is referring to a case **where another inserted liquids of *teruma* into his mouth.** As soon as the liquid enters his mouth, it is ruined. Therefore, the acquisition and his enjoyment are simultaneous.

This brings in the concept of *hana'as meiav* — the benefit of one's stomach. Even when no act of *mazik* or *gneivah* can be clearly attributed to a person, if he derived tangible benefit from another's property, he owes restitution for the value of that benefit. The benefit is concretized precisely at the moment of swallowing, when the food enters and nourishes the body. And it is at that same moment that the religious violation of eating *terumah* is completed. The two liabilities thus converge at a single point, and the question of *kim lei* becomes applicable.

### Liquids Terumah

Rav Pappa applies the same framework to a parallel case: *kegon shetachvo lo chaveiro mashkin shel terumah letoch piv* — where one's friend thrust liquids of *terumah* into his mouth. Rashi explains that liquids become immediately revolting when placed in someone else's mouth, so the same analysis applies: the friend has already caused the initial damage, the person faces only a liability of *hana'as meiav* upon swallowing, and this coincides with the consumption of *terumah* — such that the two arise simultaneously.

### Simultaneous Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּזָר שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְקָרַע שִׁירָאִין שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** It is referring **to a non-priest who partook of his own *teruma*,** e.g., if the non-priest inherited *teruma* from a priest, or acquired ownership from a priest. In that case, he did not steal the *teruma* and there is no payment for it, but he is liable to receive the death penalty for eating *teruma*, **and** at the same time **he tore another's silk [*shira'in*].** The question is whether the liability to receive the death penalty exempts him from the liability for payment incurred at precisely the same moment.

Rav Ashi takes a completely different approach: *bezar she'achal terumah mishelo v'kara shirayin shel chaveiro* — a non-*Kohen* who eats his own *terumah* (legitimately owned *terumah*, say given to him) and at the same time tears his friend's fine silk garment. Here, there is no connection whatsoever between the two acts — one is the eating of *terumah* and the other is a separate act of *nezek* to an entirely different object. Yet they happen to occur at the exact same moment. Rav Ashi holds that temporal simultaneity is itself sufficient to trigger *kim lei*, regardless of whether the two acts are part of the same process or even related to one another.

This is a striking and counterintuitive position. If anything, one might expect the principle to apply more naturally in cases where the two acts are part of a single integrated process — like chewing and swallowing — even if separated in time. Rav Ashi seems to hold the opposite: only the fact of occurring at the same moment matters, not the relationship between the acts. On Rav Ashi's view, if a person happens to be eating *terumah* with one hand and tearing a garment with the other, the *kim lei* of the eating exempts him from payment for the garment. The conceptual difficulty is plain: the logic of *kim lei* has generally been understood as the court being unable to simultaneously prosecute the same act under two different legal categories. Applying it to two entirely unrelated acts that happen to coincide in time is a very different — and considerably more expansive — claim.

### Timeline Separate

Rav Chisda's foundational principle, then, is that liability attaches at the distinct moment of each act. When theft and eating are separated on the timeline, even if one leads naturally to the other, they generate independent obligations that do not cancel each other out.

## Arrow Shabbat

### Arrow Four Amos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא פְּלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אָבִין. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: הַזּוֹרֵק חֵץ מִתְּחִילַּת אַרְבַּע לְסוֹף אַרְבַּע, וְקָרַע שִׁירָאִין בַּהֲלִיכָתוֹ — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself. Rav Ḥisda said: Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana concedes in** the case of one who **steals another's** forbidden **fat and eats it that he is obligated** to pay for the fat, **as he is already liable for theft before he comes to** violate **the prohibition** against eating forbidden **fat.** The Gemara comments: **Let us say** that Rav Ḥisda **disagrees** with **Rabbi Avin, as Rabbi Avin said: One who shoots an arrow from the beginning of four** cubits **to the end of four** cubits in the public domain on Shabbat, thereby performing a prohibited labor for which he is liable to receive a court-imposed death penalty, **and** the arrow **ripped silk as it proceeds, is exempt** from the obligation to pay for the silk because he is liable for the more severe punishment for desecrating Shabbat. Although the silk was ripped prior to completion of the prohibited labor, as the arrow had not yet come to rest, he is nevertheless exempt, **as lifting is a prerequisite for placement.** The prohibited labor of carrying on Shabbat is comprised of lifting of the object and placement. Once he shot the arrow, its movement through the air is a continuation of his act of Shabbat desecration, for which he is liable to be executed.

The *Gemara* now asks whether this ruling of Rav Chisda conflicts with a position of Rabbi Avin. Rabbi Avin states: *hazorek chetz mitchilat arba l'sof arba* — one who shoots an arrow from the beginning of four *amos* to the end of four *amos* in *reshut harabim* on Shabbat, *v'kara shirayin bahalichata* — and in the course of its flight the arrow tears through someone's fine garment — the ruling is *patur*: *kim lei* applies and the *chiyuv* of *chilul Shabbos* overrides the civil liability. The *chiyuv* for *chilul Shabbos*, strictly speaking, is only complete when the arrow lands — yet the tearing of the garment occurred mid-flight, *before* the *chiyuv Shabbos* was finalized. How, then, can one say *kim lei*?

### Akira Hanachah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁעֲקִירָה צוֹרֶךְ הַנָּחָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **as lifting is a prerequisite for placement.** The prohibited labor of carrying on Shabbat is comprised of lifting of the object and placement. Once he shot the arrow, its movement through the air is a continuation of his act of Shabbat desecration, for which he is liable to be executed.

The answer lies in the principle *she'akira tzorech hanacha hi* — the picking up (*akira*) is a necessary part of the putting down (*hanacha*). In the laws of *Shabbos*, the prohibited act of carrying (*hotza'ah*) requires three components: *akira* (lifting the object), traversal of four *amos* in *reshut harabim*, and *hanacha* (setting it down). The entire sequence constitutes a single *ma'aseh aveirah*. Since the *akira* is legally part of the *aveirah*, the whole process — from the moment the arrow was launched to the moment it landed — is treated as one continuous act. The garment was torn in the middle of this legal act, and therefore it is within the compass of the *aveirah*.

### Eating No Pickup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי: הַגְבָּהָה צוֹרֶךְ אֲכִילָה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Here, too,** say that **lifting** the fat **is a prerequisite for eating,** and therefore he should be exempt from payment.

The *Gemara* distinguishes this from the case of theft and eating. One might think: just as the picking up of the arrow was part of the process that led to its landing, so too picking up the food was part of the process that led to eating it — and therefore we should say *hagbahah tzorech achilah hi* and treat them as one process. The *Gemara* rejects this: *hasam ee efshar l'hanacha b'lo akira* — there, it is physically impossible to put the arrow down without first having picked it up. You cannot complete the act of carrying without the *akira*. *Hacha efshar l'achilah b'lo hagbahah* — here, you can eat without picking up, since *de-ba'ei gachin v'achil* — one can crouch down and eat directly off the ground.

### Interrupt Criterion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְהַנָּחָה בְּלֹא עֲקִירָה. הָכָא, אֶפְשָׁר לַאֲכִילָה בְּלֹא הַגְבָּהָה, דְּאִי בָּעֵי, גָּחֵין וְאָכֵיל. אִי נָמֵי: הָתָם אִי בָּעֵי לְאַהְדּוֹרַהּ — לָא מָצֵי מַהְדַּר לַהּ. הָכָא — מָצֵי מַהְדַּר לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this argument: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the arrow, **placement is impossible without lifting,** as placement without lifting is not a labor prohibited on Shabbat. Therefore, lifting and placement are a single unit. In contrast, **here, eating is possible without lifting as, if one wishes,** he could **bend down and eat** without lifting the food to his mouth. **Alternatively,** there is another difference between the cases: **There,** in the case of the arrow, even **if he seeks to take back** the arrow after shooting it, **he cannot take it back;** therefore, lifting and placement constitute one action. **Here, he could replace** the fat after lifting it.

The second, and likely stronger, answer: *hasam ee ba'ei l'ahadorei lo matzi mehadar lei* — there, if one wanted to interrupt the process and retrieve the arrow, one could not — once shot, the arrow cannot be recalled mid-flight. *Hacha matzi mehadar lei* — here, after picking up the food, one can always stop and put it back. The interruptibility of the process is decisive. When a process cannot be interrupted once begun, it is treated as a single continuous act and *kim lei* may apply to everything occurring within it. When a process *can* be interrupted — when at any moment the actor could choose to stop — then distinct stages on the timeline remain legally distinct, and each generates its own independent liability.

The *Gemara* here is notably bracketing the strictly legal definition of the *aveirah* and focusing on a physical criterion of continuity: either the practical necessity of the earlier stage for the later stage, or the impossibility of interruption once begun. One might argue — and there is something to this — that the principle of *akira tzorech hanacha* is properly a legal point: it is because the *akira* is legally constitutive of the *ma'aseh aveirah* of *hotza'ah* that the entire process is treated as one act. On this reading, anything that is part of the *halachically* defined act would be encompassed by *kim lei*, and the physical criteria the *Gemara* invokes are approximations of that legal definition rather than independent factors.

### Knife Reshus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין הַאי לִישָּׁנָא לְהַאי לִישָּׁנָא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ הַמַּעֲבִיר סַכִּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְקָרַע שִׁירָאִין בַּהֲלִיכָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** practical difference **is there between this formulation,** where the criterion is whether the second stage could be performed independent of the first stage, **and that formulation,** where the criterion is whether the second stage is inevitable after performing the first stage? The Gemara responds: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **one who carries a knife in the public domain and tears silk as he proceeds.**

The *Gemara* notes the practical difference between the two formulations by means of a test case: *maavir sakin b'reshut harabim v'kara shirayin b'halichato* — one who carries a knife through *reshut harabim* on Shabbat and in the course of walking it tears someone's garment. According to the first formulation — that the criterion is whether the beginning of the process is physically necessary for the end — here too *ee efshar l'hanacha b'lo akira*: you cannot transport the knife four *amos* without first having picked it up. The *kim lei* of *chilul Shabbos* would apply and exempt from payment. According to the second formulation — that the criterion is whether the process can be interrupted — here *matzi mehadar lei*: one could stop walking at any moment, set the knife down, and reverse the act. The process is interruptible, and therefore the garment-tearing and the *chilul Shabbos* are not sufficiently fused to trigger *kim lei*; the civil liability stands independently.
