# Eruvin 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Korah Dimensions

### Korah & Brick Basics

The *mishnah* that opens our *daf* — found on the bottom of 13b — shifts from the question of what a *lechi* and *korah* are required for, to the question of what they must actually look like. The *mishnah* states: *ha-korah she-amru, rechava kidei lekabel ariach* — the horizontal beam about which the Rabbis spoke must be wide enough to receive a brick. An *ariach* is half of a *leveinah*, a tile that measured three by three *tefachim*; accordingly, a brick is three *tefachim* long by one and a half *tefachim* wide. The *mishnah* then clarifies: *dai lakorah she-tehe rechava tefach kidei lekabel ariach leracho* — it is sufficient that the *korah* be one *tefach* wide, enough to receive the brick placed widthwise.

The geometry here deserves attention. If the brick is one and a half *tefachim* wide and the *korah* is only one *tefach* wide, there is a quarter-*tefach* overhang on each side. The *gemara* will explain that this is acceptable because one could theoretically secure the overhanging portions with plaster. The underlying conceptual point of the *mishnah*, then, is that the *korah* functions as an element of a doorframe, and the measurement of one *tefach* is derived from the practical image of a brick resting atop it.

Beyond width, the *mishnah* adds a second criterion: *ubri'ah kidei lekabel ariach* — the *korah* must also be strong enough to support a brick placed upon it. Notably, this does not require the strength to bear an entire row of bricks; a single brick is the standard.

### Chachamim vs. R. Yehuda

*Rabbi Yehuda omer: rechava af al pi she-eina bri'ah.* Rabbi Yehuda holds that width alone is sufficient; the *korah* need not actually be strong enough to bear the brick. This position is striking, because our general working assumption regarding the halakhic constructs of *eruvin* has been that they are largely formal or symbolic — and here we find the *chachamim* imposing a genuinely functional standard that Rabbi Yehuda explicitly rejects. In other words, it is Rabbi Yehuda who is the formalist, and the *chachamim* who demand practical functionality.

### R. Yehuda's Fictions

The *mishnah* elaborates Rabbi Yehuda's position through a series of imaginative constructs. First: *hayeta shel kash veshel kanim* — if the *korah* was made of straw or reeds, *ro'in otah ke'ilu hayeta shel matechet* — we view it as though it were made of metal. The material is irrelevant to Rabbi Yehuda; what matters is the formal dimension, not the actual capacity to bear weight.

Second: *akumah* — if the *korah* was bent, *ro'in otah ke'ilu peshutah* — we view it as though it were straight. The *gemara* will later explore what "bent" means in context and where this principle has limits.

Third: *agulah* — if the *korah* is circular, like a pole rather than a beam, *ro'in otah ke'ilu hi meruba'at* — we view it as though it were squared off. Since a cylindrical pole cannot practically support a brick, this is another instance of Rabbi Yehuda's willingness to operate in the realm of formal imagination. For him, as long as the *korah* is one *tefach* wide at any point — meaning that if you took a cross-section of the cylinder the diameter would be one *tefach* — it qualifies.

The *mishnah* then states the underlying geometric principle: *kol she-yesh beheikefo sheloshah tefachim yesh bo rochav tefach* — anything with a circumference of three *tefachim* has a diameter of one *tefach*. This is, of course, the approximation of *pi* as three, which we will return to at length later in the *sugya*. For now it is worth noting that this approximation is actually a *kula* in certain cases: since the true value of *pi* is approximately 3.14159, a circumference of exactly three *tefachim* corresponds to a diameter slightly less than one *tefach*. Rabbi Yehuda would nonetheless validate the *korah*.

It is also important to distinguish this application from other contexts where a circular object must contain a square of one *tefach* by one *tefach* — as in certain discussions of *ohel*. Here, because the width requirement is one-dimensional, Rabbi Yehuda is satisfied with the diameter alone; there is no demand to inscribe a full *tefach*-by-*tefach* square within the circle.

One question arises naturally: how long must the *korah* extend? The answer is that it must span the full width of the *mavoi*, from one wall to the other. The one-*tefach* width requirement applies at every point along that length, but there is no requirement for any particular thickness.

As for whether these width and strength standards apply equally to the top of a *tzurat ha-petach*: the answer is no. The *chachamim*, according to many authorities — and it is their view we rule by — require the *korah* to be strong enough to support a brick, but they do not make this demand for a *tzurat ha-petach*. More to the point, the horizontal element of a *tzurat ha-petach* does not even need to be one *tefach* wide; a string suffices. The present discussion pertains specifically to a *korah* standing on its own.

## Korah Width Standards

### Plaster Support

The *gemara* at the top of 14a raises an immediate question on the *mishnah*. If a brick is one and a half *tefachim* wide, the *korah* should need to be one and a half *tefachim* wide in order to receive it. Why does the *mishnah* require only one *tefach*?

The *gemara* answers: *keivan d'rachav v'kabel tefach, idach chatzi tefach, mashvei lei betina mashehu mehai gisa u'mashehu mehai gisa, v'kayma* — since the *korah* is one *tefach* wide and the brick rests upon it with a quarter-*tefach* overhang on each side, one can plaster that small extension on both sides, and the brick will stay in place. Thus, a *korah* that is one *tefach* wide can in practice support a one-and-a-half-*tefach* brick once the edges are secured with plaster.

### Beam vs. Support

*Amar Rabba bar Rav Huna*: the *korah* must be strong enough to support a brick — that is the standard of the *chachamim* — but the supports of the *korah* itself need not be strong enough to bear the combined weight of the *korah* and the brick. If the *korah* rests on pegs at the end of the *mavoi*, and those pegs would collapse under the combined weight of beam and brick, that is not a disqualifying concern. One is not actually placing a brick on the *korah*; the standard is that the *korah* itself be capable of bearing the brick, not that the entire structural system could withstand that load.

*Rav Chisda* disagrees. He holds that both the *korah* and its supports must be strong enough — *echad zeh v'echad zeh tzarichim sheyehu briim kedei lekabel kora v'ariach* — each must be capable of sustaining the beam with the brick upon it. Even so, both opinions agree that only a single brick is the benchmark; there is no requirement to support an entire row.

### Covered Korah Issue

*Rav Sheshet* presents a striking case. Suppose one places a *korah* across the *mavoi* and then, in an excess of stringency, spreads a mat over it so that the entryway looks more like a genuine door. If the bottom of the hanging mat is more than three *tefachim* above the ground, the result is a halakhic disaster: *korah ein kan, mechitzah ein kan* — neither a valid *korah* nor a valid *mechitzah* exists.

Why is there no *korah*? Because the *korah* is covered — *deha michasya* — and the function of a *korah* is *mishum heker*, to serve as a distinctive marker signaling the boundary of the *mavoi*. Once it is hidden beneath the mat, it no longer functions as a *heker*.

Why is the mat not a *mechitzah*? Because it does not reach the ground — *dehavei lei mechitzah shehagedayim boke'in bah* — it is a partition through which goats could run underneath. The *gemara* frames this in functional, not merely formal, terms: it is not simply that the mat fails to make contact with the ground, but that it demonstrably fails to block passage. This is a classic case of *kol hamosif goreia* — one who adds ends up subtracting.

Two considerations explain why the *gemara* needed to articulate the goats-running-underneath point rather than simply noting that the mat does not touch the ground. First, since *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds that *lavud* extends up to four *tefachim*, one might have thought that a gap of less than four *tefachim* would be treated as if connected — the *gemara* therefore clarifies that even according to him, practical passage through the gap disqualifies it as a *mechitzah*. Second, in various other contexts the principle exists that a *mechitzah* can be deemed to extend downward through halakhic imagination — but once we acknowledge that in practice goats traverse the opening, it becomes very difficult to invoke that imaginative extension. The practical contradiction overrides the theoretical construction.

## Lavud & Gaps

### Single Beam Gap

The *gemara* presents a *baraita* addressing cases where a *korah* does not fully bridge the *mavoi*. If a beam extends from one wall but does not reach the opposite wall, and similarly if two beams extend from opposite walls but fail to meet in the middle — what is the rule?

*Pachot mishlosha ein tzarich lehavi korah acheret* — if the gap is less than three *tefachim*, no additional beam is needed. The principle of *lavud* applies: a gap of less than three *tefachim* is treated as though closed. *Shlosha tzarich lehavi korah acheret* — if the gap is three *tefachim* or more, another beam must be supplied.

### RSBG Four Tefach

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer*: the threshold is four *tefachim*, not three. *Pachot me'arba'ah ein tzarich lehavi korah acheret* — less than four *tefachim* requires no additional beam. *Arba'ah tzarich lehavi korah acheret* — four *tefachim* requires one. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the operative definition of *lavud* extends to four *tefachim*. The halakhic ruling follows the *chachamim*, who set the threshold at three.

### Parallel Beams

The *baraita* continues with a distinct scenario: *shtei korot ha-mas'imot* — two beams running parallel to one another across the *mavoi*, each too narrow to independently support a brick. Can *lavud* combine them into a single valid *korah*?

Here the analysis diverges from the simple gap case. The standard of the *chachamim* — that the *korah* must in practice be able to support a brick — governs the answer. If the two parallel beams together could support the brick widthwise (*im m'kablos ariach l'rachbo tefach*), no additional beam is needed. If not, one is required. Crucially, the gap-closing logic of *lavud* does not apply here in the ordinary way: even a gap less than three *tefachim* between the two beams does not suffice unless the result is that the brick can actually be supported.

The operative criterion seems to be that the gap between the two beams must be less than one *tefach*, so that the brick — one and a half *tefachim* wide — has at least some meaningful purchase on each beam and will not simply fall through. The plaster solution discussed earlier can help stabilize the overhang, but if the gap is too wide, the support is insufficiently stable. Thus, while we might have expected a gap of one and a half *tefachim* to be the threshold (matching the brick's width), the *gemara* seems to require the gap to be less than one *tefach* so that the brick is seated adequately on both beams.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* is more lenient in this case as well, though by a different measure: he permits the brick to be oriented lengthwise rather than widthwise. *Im m'kabales ariach l'archo shlosha* — if the two beams together can support a brick along its three-*tefach* length, no additional beam is needed. This means that even beams as much as three *tefachim* apart could suffice, since the brick laid lengthwise spans the gap. This further suggests that both opinions are measuring the outer edges of the combined *korah* rather than the gap between them, since Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's leniency is calibrated to the full three-*tefach* length of the brick.

One naturally wonders what Rabbi Yehuda would say in the parallel-beam scenario, given that he does not require the *korah* to practically support a brick at all. The *baraita* does not address this, but it follows from his position that he would likely be more permissive.

## Different Heights & Bends

### Vertical Adjustment

Having discussed a beam that does not span the full width and two parallel beams that are each too narrow, the *gemara* now turns to two beams at different heights. Each beam extends halfway across the *mavoi* from its respective wall, but they are positioned at different elevations. Even if the beams are within three *tefachim* of one another horizontally, there is no continuous horizontal surface; the vertical offset means no brick could bridge them.

*Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda omer: ro'in es ha'elyona k'ilu hi lemata, vehatachtona k'ilu hi lemala* — we view the upper beam as though it were lower, or the lower beam as though it were higher. By imagining them on the same plane, and if they would then connect, the combined structure is valid. The condition is that each beam individually must be in a legitimate position: the upper beam must not be above twenty *amot*, and the lower must not be less than ten *tefachim* from the ground.

This is a notably generous application of halakhic imagination. The gap between the two beams in the vertical plane could theoretically be quite large — as long as each beam is independently valid, the intervening distance can be traversed through the fiction of vertical adjustment. This connects to the broader principle, familiar from the laws of *sukkah*, that walls can be extended upward or downward through halakhic imagination.

### Abaye on R. Yose

*Amar Abaye: Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda savar lah ke'avuh bechada ufalig aleh bechada* — Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda agrees with his father Rabbi Yehuda in one matter but disagrees in another.

The agreement: Rabbi Yehuda employs the concept of *ro'in* — we imagine as if. Now, this is a more nuanced position than it might first appear. When Rabbi Yehuda says the *korah* need not actually be strong enough to support a brick, there are two possible ways to understand him. One is that he simply rejects the criterion entirely: the strength requirement is irrelevant. The other is that he accepts the criterion in principle but applies imaginative reasoning to satisfy it — straw is viewed as if it were metal, so the notional strength requirement is fulfilled. Rabbi Yehuda takes the second approach. This is why it matters: if you accept the underlying criterion but operate through imagination, you can extend that same imaginative principle to the vertical-plane scenario. Since the *korah* does not practically need to support a brick — or rather, we imagine it as if it does — we can equally imagine two beams at different heights as if they are level. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda inherits this logic from his father.

The disagreement: Rabbi Yehuda in the first *mishnah* (of tractate *Eruvin*) holds that a *korah* may be placed higher than twenty *amot*. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda disagrees — *betoch esrim in, lemala m'esrim lo* — the beam must be within twenty *amot*. This is the one stringency that Rabbi Yose adds relative to his father.

This analysis also clarifies why the *chachamim* would presumably not permit vertical adjustment at all: since they require the *korah* to practically support a brick, a *korah* at a different elevation cannot do so regardless of any imaginative construction. The imaginative move is available only to those who operate within Rabbi Yehuda's framework of *ro'in*.

The *gemara* then revisits the *mishnah*'s statement that Rabbi Yehuda says *rechava af pi she-eina bri'ah*. *Rav Yehudah* — the Amora, not the Tanna Rabbi Yehuda — taught this *mishnah* to *Chiya bar Rav* in the presence of *Rav*, who corrected him: the text should read *rechava u-vri'ah* — wide and strong. At first glance this seems puzzling, since "wide and strong" is the position of the *chachamim*, not Rabbi Yehuda. How does Rav understand the dispute?

*Tosafot* offers an ingenious resolution. Rav was not changing Rabbi Yehuda's statement; he was correcting the *chachamim*'s statement. In the original text of the *mishnah*, the *chachamim* had been recorded as saying *rechava af al pi she-eina bri'ah* — width without strength is sufficient. Rav corrected this: the *chachamim* actually say *rechava u-vri'ah*, both width and strength. It is Rabbi Yehuda who holds that width alone suffices. Our current text of the *mishnah*, according to *Tosafot*, reflects Rav's emendation.

This reading is confirmed by a subsequent statement: *Rebbi Ilai* cites Rav as ruling that a *korah* four *tefachim* wide is valid even if it is not strong enough. The *gemara* reconciles this with Rav's emendation: a *korah* four *tefachim* wide is a qualitatively different case — it is so prominently visible that the *heker* function is independently satisfied, and the strength requirement does not apply to it. For a *korah* of only one *tefach*, however, Rav rules in accordance with the *chachamim* that both width and strength are required.

Returning to the *mishnah*'s elaborations on Rabbi Yehuda's position: the *gemara* asks why it was necessary to state the straw-viewed-as-metal principle, given that we already know Rabbi Yehuda does not require the *korah* to be strong. The *gemara* answers: one might have thought that imaginative reasoning applies only within the same category of material — a weak wood imagined as strong wood — but that leaping from straw to metal is too remote. The *mishnah* teaches that even this is permitted.

For the *akumah* — the bent *korah* — the *gemara* first says this is obvious, then notes that the novel teaching concerns the case where the bend extends outside the *mavoi* itself (*hi be-tokh ha-mavoi ve-akmumitah chutz la-mavoi*). One might have thought that even if the visible part of the beam is within the *mavoi*, the outward bow would lead people to carry beyond the *mavoi* boundary, thinking they are still within a permissible zone (*lichosh dilma ati le'amshuchi batrah*). The *mishnah* teaches that we are not concerned with this.

### Bent Beams

The *gemara* then addresses the case where the bend pushes into prohibited territory — either outside the *mavoi* horizontally, above twenty *amot*, or below ten *tefachim* from the ground — and introduces Rabbi Zeira's formulation. *Ro'in*, we imagine: *kol she'ilu yinnatel akmumitah, ve'ein bein zo lazo sheloshah, ein tzarich lehavi korah acheret* — if you were to mentally remove the unusable bent section, and the remaining straight portions are within three *tefachim* of one another, no additional beam is required. If the remaining gap is three *tefachim* or more, one is needed.

This is not exactly the plain reading of the *mishnah*'s *ro'in otah ke'ilu hi peshutah*. The *mishnah* says we imagine the bent beam as straight; Rabbi Zeira's more precise formulation is that we excise the unusable section and apply *lavud* to what remains. The *gemara* confirms that the novel teaching in the *mishnah* cannot simply be the gap-closure principle — that much was already established — but rather the case where the bend goes outside the *mavoi* entirely, raising the concern of leading people to carry in a forbidden area. The *mishnah* teaches that this concern does not invalidate the *korah*.

One might wonder whether the *chachamim* would accept even this. The *chachamim* require the *korah* to support a brick; a severely bent beam cannot. But as one participant noted, perhaps in the *mishnah*'s original scenario the beam is bent in a way that prevents brick placement while remaining within legitimate boundaries — and the *chachamim*'s objection would be on purely practical grounds. In the scenario Rabbi Zeira addresses — where the bend exits into forbidden territory but the remaining straight portions are within three *tefachim* — even the *chachamim* might agree that the visible, functional portions of the beam constitute a valid *korah*.

## Pi & Shlomo's Yam

### Circle to Square

The *mishnah* concluded with the rule *agulah ro'in otah ke'ilu hi meruba'at* — a circular *korah* is viewed as though it were squared off. The *gemara* asks: this is self-evident from Rabbi Yehuda's position; why state it separately?

The *gemara* answers: *seifa itztericha leh* — the clause is needed for what follows: *kol she-yesh behikefo sheloshah tefachim yesh bo rochav tefach* — anything with a circumference of three *tefachim* has a diameter of one *tefach*. The *mishnah* wishes to articulate this geometric principle, which is the operative assumption underlying the circular-*korah* rule.

### 3:1 Pi Ratio

*Mina hani milei* — where does this principle come from? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan amar kra* — Rabbi Yochanan derives it from a scriptural source.

### Shlomo's Yam Text

The verse (I Kings 7:23) describes the great basin — *ha-yam* — that King Shlomo cast for the *Beit Hamikdash*: *vaya'as et ha-yam mutzak, eser ba'amah misfato el sfato, agol saviv, vechamesh ba'amah komato, vekov shloshim ba'amah yasov oto saviv* — he made the molten sea, ten *amot* from one edge to the other, circular all around, five *amot* high, and a line of thirty *amot* would encompass it around. Thus Scripture records a diameter of ten and a circumference of thirty — a ratio of 3:1.

The *gemara* immediately raises a complication: the thirty-*amah* circumference presumably refers to the inner measurement, while the ten-*amah* diameter refers to the outer edge. If so, the wall thickness skews the proportion. *Rav Pappa* resolves this: *sfato kisfat kos perach shoshan ktiv bei* — the verse describes the lip of the basin as being like the petal of a rose blossom, extraordinarily thin. Furthermore, the verse explicitly states that the wall was one *tefach* thick (*ve'avyo tefach*), and the lip was fashioned like a goblet's rim — *kema'aseh kos perach shoshan* — tapering to a negligible edge. The *gemara* notes that even so there remains some minimal thickness (*mashhu*), and concludes: *ki kachashiv miguai kachashiv* — we measure both the diameter and the circumference along the interior, eliminating any discrepancy from wall thickness.

### Rambam Rounding

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question. The *gemara*'s anxiety about even the smallest deviation — the *mashhu* of the rim — implies that the 3:1 ratio is being taken as a precise mathematical truth. But as *Tosafot* observes: *vekasheh de'ein ha-cheshbon medukdak lefi chachmei ha-middot* — this is problematic, because those versed in geometry know that this calculation is not accurate. *Pi* is not three; it is approximately 3.14159.

The *Rambam*, in his *Peirush ha-Mishnah*, addresses this directly. He acknowledges that the true value of *pi* cannot be stated as a simple ratio, and explains that for halakhic purposes we are authorized to round *pi* to three. This is a formal ruling: when determining whether a circular object meets a dimensional threshold in *halakhah*, one uses the ratio of 3:1 as a sanctioned approximation. This can yield a *kula* — a circular beam with a circumference of exactly three *tefachim* has a true diameter slightly less than one *tefach*, yet it is treated as valid.

*Tosafot*'s difficulty with this approach is that if the ratio is merely a halakhic rounding, the *gemara* should not be troubled by the *mashhu* of the lip — why would a negligible additional thickness matter if the whole ratio is only approximate anyway? *Tosafot* leaves this as a question.

### Vilna Gaon Gematria

The *Vilna Gaon* offers a remarkable resolution to the underlying mathematical tension. The word in the *Tanakh* for the measuring line — *kav* — is written in one spelling (*ktiv*) but read in another (*kri*). The *kri* is *kav* (קו), with a *gematria* of 106. The *ktiv* is *kavah* (קוה), with a *gematria* of 111. The *Vilna Gaon* suggests: take 111 divided by 106, and multiply the result by three. The computation yields 111 ÷ 106 × 3 = 1.04717 × 3 ≈ 3.14151 — which is *pi* accurate to four significant digits.

This is a genuinely stunning piece of *gematria*. The *Vilna Gaon*, who was deeply versed in mathematics and considered secular knowledge essential for Torah scholarship — having had Euclid's *Elements* translated into Hebrew — was evidently aware of the precise value of *pi*. The implication of his observation may be precisely that the Talmudic tradition understood *pi* accurately, but formally employed a rounded value of three for halakhic purposes. What remains unresolved, as *Tosafot* asks, is why the *gemara* is troubled by the minute discrepancy of the *mashhu* if the ratio is understood as a rounded approximation. For practical *bein adam la-chaveiro* matters — say, calculating the area of a circular field for commercial purposes — one would presumably use a more precise value of *pi*.

## Mikveh Volume Math

### Cubic Conversion

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita*: *Rabbi Chiya* taught that the *yam she'asa Shlomo* — the sea that Shlomo built — could contain 150 *mikva'ot* of purification water. The *gemara* works through this carefully.

A *mikveh* requires forty *se'ah* of water, as derived from the verse *verachatz et besaro bamayim* — "and he shall immerse all his flesh in water" — which is understood to mean water in which one's entire body can be submerged. The dimensions of such a volume are one *amah* by one *amah* by three *amot* high. Three cubic *amot* equal forty *se'ah*; this is the standard *mikveh*.

The *gemara* begins the calculation by temporarily treating the *yam* as though it were square rather than circular, to simplify the arithmetic. A square basin ten *amot* by ten *amot* by five *amot* high would contain 500 cubic *amot*. Since each *mikveh* requires three cubic *amot*, 300 cubic *amot* yield 100 *mikva'ot*, and the next 150 cubic *amot* yield another 50 — for a total of 150 *mikva'ot* from just 450 cubic *amot*.

But then why is the answer 150 *mikva'ot* and not more, since we started with 500 cubic *amot*? The answer is that the *yam* was not square — it was circular. And a circle is smaller in area than the square that contains it. The ratio, using the approximation of *pi* as three, is three-quarters: the area of a circle is three-quarters the area of the circumscribed square. So 500 cubic *amot* for a square basin becomes 375 cubic *amot* for a circular basin of the same diameter and height.

But 375 cubic *amot* at three cubic *amot* per *mikveh* gives only 125 *mikva'ot*, not 150. The *baraita*'s figure of 150 remains unaccounted for.

### Square-Circle Mix

The *gemara* resolves this by citing *Rami bar Yechezkel*: *Yam she'asa Shlomo shlosha amos tachtonos merubaos v'shtayim elyonos agulos* — the lower three *amot* of the *yam* were square, and the upper two *amot* were circular.

With this reconstruction the calculation works precisely. The lower square section: 10 × 10 × 3 = 300 cubic *amot*, yielding 100 *mikva'ot*. The upper circular section: had it been square it would be 10 × 10 × 2 = 200 cubic *amot*; reducing by one-quarter for the circular shape gives 150 cubic *amot*, yielding 50 *mikva'ot*. Total: 150 *mikva'ot*.

The *gemara* confirms that the upper portion could not have been square, since Scripture describes the lip as circular — *sfato k'kos perach shoshan*. Nor is it arbitrary which three *amot* are square and which two are circular: the math only works out to 150 *mikva'ot* with exactly this configuration. A different distribution — say, four *amot* square and one *amah* circular — yields a different total, and the *gemara* rules out that possibility.

### Bat Measure

The *gemara* now cross-checks the result against the *pasuk*'s explicit statement of volume: *alpayim bat yachil* — it shall contain 2,000 *bat*. What is a *bat*? From the verse *ma'aser ha-bat min ha-kor*, a *bat* is one-tenth of a *kor*. A *kor* is thirty *se'ah*, so a *bat* is three *se'ah*. Therefore 2,000 *bat* equals 6,000 *se'ah*. A *mikveh* is forty *se'ah*. Dividing: 6,000 ÷ 40 = 150 *mikva'ot*. The number 150 is thus independently confirmed by Scripture's stated capacity of 2,000 *bat*, which in turn anchors the geometric reconstruction.

### Dry Measure Hump

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty: a second verse (II Chronicles 4:5) states that the *yam* held 3,000 *bat*, not 2,000. The resolution: *hahu legudsha* — that verse refers to dry measure, which accounts for the heaped or mounded surplus above the rim. When a container this large and wide is filled with a dry commodity like wheat, the grain mounds significantly above the lip. That additional mounded volume is the *gudsha*, the hump.

*Abaye* draws a practical conclusion: *hai gudsha tlata havei* — from the difference between 2,000 and 3,000, the hump of a dry measure for a vessel of these proportions equals one-third of the liquid volume. The dry measure is thus fifty percent more than the liquid measure (from 2,000 to 3,000 is an increase of one-half, though the *gemara* frames the incremental portion as one-third of the whole).

*Rashi* notes that this ratio — dry measure exceeding liquid measure by a third — applies specifically to a vessel that is much wider than it is tall, such as the *yam*, which was ten *amot* wide but only five *amot* high. Such a shape allows for an exceptionally large dome of mounded grain. This principle has practical halakhic implications as well: a *baraita* cited by the *gemara* states that a *teivah* (large box), a tower, a straw hive, a reed hive, or an Alexandrian ship's water container — all exceedingly large vessels — are not susceptible to *tumah*. The criterion is that they hold forty *se'ah* in liquid measure, equivalent to sixty *se'ah* in dry measure (two *kor*). Here the ratio of liquid to dry is 1 to 1.5, and the *gemara* cites this as a general principle — the dry measure is fifty percent more than the liquid.

*Tosafot* notes that this could have commercial halakhic significance: if someone sells a container of unmounded grain — literally, a heaped measure — the Talmudic tradition provides a framework for calculating what that mound must look like, based on these ratios. As *Rashi* observed, a vessel much wider than it is tall will produce a proportionally larger mound than a narrower, taller vessel. More on related topics is reserved for further discussion.
