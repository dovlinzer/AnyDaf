# Moed Katan 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Grooming & Categories

### Mishna Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר בְּתִסְפּוֹרֶת. מִדְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לִבְנֵי אַהֲרֹן: ״רָאשֵׁיכֶם אַל תִּפְרָעוּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The source for the *halakha* that **it is prohibited for a mourner to** receive **a haircut is** derived **from** the fact that **the Merciful One states to the sons of Aaron: "Let not the hair of your heads go loose"** (Leviticus 10:6). It was prohibited for them to let their hair grow long during their period of mourning over the death of their brothers, Nadav and Avihu. **By inference,** it is teaching **that for everyone** else, i.e., non-priests, **it is prohibited** to cut their hair during the period of mourning.

We begin at the bottom of 14b, where the *gemara* introduces *Amar Abaye ta shma*. The discussion at hand concerns the *mishna*, which introduced the prohibitions against getting a haircut and doing laundry on *Chol HaMoed*, along with a series of exceptions for individuals who had no opportunity to attend to these matters beforehand: one freed from prison, one who returned from an overseas journey, a *nazir* or *metzora* who was becoming *tahor*, and a person who had been in *niddui*—a mild form of ostracism—whose status was then released, thereby permitting him to get a haircut and launder his clothes on *Chol HaMoed*.

### Melacha & Tircha

This *perek* serves not only as a bridge into *Hilchos Aveilus* but as an occasion to place several distinct categories alongside one another and think seriously about them. One of the notable features of this discussion is that the *gemara* felt compelled to find a special rationale for why haircuts and laundry were prohibited on *Chol HaMoed*—namely, to ensure that people come into *Yom Tov* already having attended to these matters—rather than simply grounding the prohibition in the concept of *melacha*. This raises the question of what exactly constitutes *melacha* on *Chol HaMoed*. There is very little evidence in the *gemara* that it has anything to do with the thirty-nine *melachos*, although Tosafos repeatedly attempts to bring that framework in. The term the *gemara* more consistently uses is *tircha*—effort—though not all the prohibited activities on *Chol HaMoed* fall neatly into that category either. It may be more accurate to describe the concern as something amorphous, a notion of weekday activity, which is why things like working the fields and engaging in commerce were particularly highlighted. It remains an open practical question even today: if someone cuts their hair or does laundry on *Chol HaMoed* without one of the recognized allowances, have they violated a *melacha*, or have they merely transgressed the rabbinic ruling that one must attend to these things in advance?

### Three Categories

The other striking feature of this *perek* is that these very same practices—not cutting hair, not doing laundry—are identical to the practices we encounter in the context of mourning. An *avel* does not do these things. Neither does someone in *niddui*, and neither does a *metzora*. The *metzora* is not in mourning in any conventional sense, but his condition represents a kind of being cast out from society, an experience entirely at odds with *Simchas HaRegel*. The *gemara* therefore sets these two realms in direct opposition.

### Regel vs. Aveilus

The *gemara* had already established that *aveilus* is not observed during the *regel*—indeed, if someone is in *aveilus* when the *regel* arrives, the *regel* terminates that period of mourning. The question now before us is whether the *regel* similarly overrides the practices of someone in *niddui* or the practices of a *metzora*.

## Menudeh & Regel

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: וּמְנוּדֶּה שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** another support from a *baraita*: **And** it is the case with regard to **one who is ostracized, that the Sages permitted him** to get a haircut during the intermediate days of the Festival. This indicates that one who is ostracized need not observe the practices of ostracism during the Festival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִי קָתָנֵי שֶׁהִתִּירוּהוּ חֲכָמִים? שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים קָתָנֵי, דַּאֲזַל וּפַיְּיסֵיהּ לְבַעַל דִּינֵיהּ, וַאֲתָא קַמֵּי דְרַבָּנַן וּשְׁרוֹ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is it taught** in the *baraita*: **That they permitted it,** which would indicate that **the Sages** permitted all those who were ostracized to cut their hair on the intermediate days of the Festival? Rather, **it is taught: They permitted him.** This indicates that it is referring to an individual case, **where one went and appeased his** opposing **litigant and the Sages came and released him** from his decree of ostracism.

*Abaye* argues from our *mishna* itself: *ta shma*, the *mishna* lists a *menudeh* whose status was released as someone permitted to get a haircut and launder clothes on *Chol HaMoed*. *Abaye* reads this as referring to an automatic release—the arrival of the *regel* itself dissolved the *niddui*—thereby proving that the *regel* does override *niddui*. *Rava* disagrees with this reading. The *mishna* does not say *sheheetiruhoo chachamim*—that the *chachamim* permitted such a person in a general way on the *regel*—but rather *sheheetiru lo chachamim*, with the *lo* indicating that the *chachamim* specifically released this individual. The plain meaning is that he went and appeased the person he had been in conflict with, and then came before the rabbis, who removed his state of *niddui*. Were it not for that particular resolution, he would still have been observing his *niddui* practices through the *regel*. The *gemara* does not reach a definitive conclusion, but the simple evidence according to *Rava* is that *niddui* does apply during the *regel*.

### Tosafos Question

Tosafos raises the question: why does *niddui* apply during the *regel* if *aveilus* does not? After all, both are experienced by an individual. Tosafos suggests that although *niddui* is observed by a *yachid*, it may be considered a concern of the *rabbim*—because if this is how the rabbis maintain social order, then the practice of *niddui* has a communal dimension, and therefore it continues even during the *regel*, which overrides only *aveilus de'yachid* in the face of *simcha de'rabbim*.

### Ostracism Framework

An alternative approach presents itself, one that will become clearer as we work through the list of practices that distinguish these three categories from one another. The *metzora* and the *menudeh* are not in mourning. So what are their practices expressing? The answer is that their practices are much more about being cast outside of and ostracized from society. The *metzora* is *michutz lamachaneh*—the Torah makes this vivid: *bigdav yihyu frumim v'rosho yihyeh parua, tamei tamei yikra*. The image projected is: stay away. The garments are rent, the hair is wild—this is a person who is not fit for normal social participation. Similarly, *niddui* places a person in a castigated, quasi-ostracized role until his conduct comes back into alignment with communal expectations and rabbinic standards.

*Aveilus*, too, has an element of withdrawal from society—the *avel* sits *shiva*, removed from the normal flow and rhythms of the world, focused on grief and *nechama*. But *aveilus* has something beyond that: it is an expression of profound sadness and mourning, something that does not apply to *metzora* or *menudeh* at all.

With this in mind, the reason the *regel* overrides *aveilus* but not these other two categories becomes clear. The *regel* and *aveilus* are direct antitheses: the *regel* is *simcha*, and *aveilus* is sadness. There is a head-on collision. But the practices of *metzora* and *menudeh*, while they may make it harder for a person to participate fully in *Simchas HaRegel*, are not expressions of sadness. They are expressions of ostracism. The *regel*'s mandate of *simcha* operates on a different plane from a declaration of social ostracization. Therefore, the *regel*'s power to override is diminished in their case—it addresses the emotional state directly, and these practices are not about an emotional state.

## Metzora & Regel

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצוֹרָע, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּנְהִיג צָרַעְתּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks a similar question: With regard to **a leper, what is** the *halakha*? Must he **observe the practices of his leper status,** or are they overridden by the mitzva to rejoice **during the Festival?**

Having addressed the *menudeh*, the *gemara* turns to the *metzora*. The question posed is: *metzora mahu sheyanhig tzoraso baregel*—does a *metzora* observe his *tzaraas* practices during the *regel*? This is a striking *hava amina*. One would think it explicit in the *pesukim* that this is what a *metzora* does, and to imagine that the *regel* might suspend these obligations is genuinely startling.

### Abaye's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: וְהַנָּזִיר וְהַמְּצוֹרָע מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ לְטׇהֳרָתוֹ. הָא בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתוֹ — נָהֵיג.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **And** both **the nazirite and the leper** who transfers **from his** state of **ritual impurity to his** new state of **purity** may shave on the intermediate days of the Festival. **This** implies that **during the days of his impurity** he must **observe** all of the ordinary practices, even during the Festival.

*Abaye* offers a proof: *ta shma*—who is permitted to get a haircut and launder clothes on *Chol HaMoed*? A *nazir* and a *metzora* who are *mitumasan letaharasan*, becoming *tahor*. From the fact that the permission is granted specifically at the point of becoming *tahor*, one can infer—*ha b'imei tumaso lo*—that during his days of *tumah*, a *metzora* may not do these things, even during the *regel*.

### Postponement Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתוֹ דְּלָא נָהֵיג, אֲבָל לְטׇהֳרָתוֹ נִיגְזוֹר שֶׁמָּא יְשַׁהֶה קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: The *baraita* **is speaking** employing the didactic style of: **Needless to say. Needless to say, during the days of his impurity** on the Festival, **he does not observe** the practices of ostracism. **However,** with regard to one who regains **his** state of **purity** during the intermediate days of the Festival, one might think that **we should issue a decree** that he not be permitted to shave, **lest he delay** sacrificing **his offerings** until the last day of the Festival, when it is prohibited to sacrifice offerings of an individual. Perhaps prohibiting him from shaving will prevent this possibility. Therefore, because one might have thought that shaving should be prohibited, the *baraita* **teaches us** that it is, in fact, permitted for him to shave during the intermediate days of the Festival.

The *gemara* deflects this proof. One might say *lo tzricha*: it goes without saying that during his full *tumah* he would not do these things on the *regel*, for the *regel* overrides. The *mishna*'s novelty lies in the case where he is in the process of becoming *tahor*. There, you might think: all the more so, he should be permitted—he is becoming *tahor* and it is the *regel*. But the *gemara* raises a concern: *nigzar shema yishahei korbanosav*—perhaps, paradoxically, if we allow a *metzora* undergoing *tahara* to perform his *tahara* practices during the *regel*, we create an incentive for him to delay. A person who becomes *tahor* a week or a month before *Succos* might decide not to trouble himself making the pilgrimage to *Yerushalayim* in advance, but to simply wait until *Succos* and complete the *tahara* process then. To prevent this temptation, one might have thought to forbid the *metzora* from performing his *tahara* practices during the *regel*—thereby forcing him to complete the process before the *regel* arrives. The *mishna* teaches us *ka mashma lan* that we do not in fact impose this restriction, and he is permitted to complete his *tahara* on the *regel*.

### Rava's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְהַצָּרוּעַ״ — לְרַבּוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל. וְהָא כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, דְּכׇל הַשָּׁנָה כְּרֶגֶל לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא דָּמֵי, דִּתְנַן: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ נוֹהֵג צָרַעְתּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** another source. The verse states: **"And the leper** in whom the plague is, his clothes shall be rent" (Leviticus 13:45). The extra emphasis of the phrase: "And the leper" comes **to include the High Priest,** teaching that all the *halakhot* of the leper apply to him. **And** the status of **the High Priest** during **the entire year is like** that of **everyone on a Festival, as we learned** in a mishna (*Horayot* 12b): The **High Priest sacrifices** animals when he has the status of **an acute mourner,** i.e., before the burial of a close relative who died, **but he may not eat** of the offering. **Learn from it** that a leper **practices the customs of his leprosy during the Festival.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is so.

*Rava* offers a different proof. The Torah writes *ha-tzarua asher bo ha-naga*—the word *ha-tzarua* is seemingly superfluous, as *asher bo ha-naga* would have sufficed. The extra word serves *lerabot Kohen Gadol*—to teach that even a *Kohen Gadol* must observe the practices of a *metzora*. And the *gemara* makes a remarkable observation: *de-Kohen Gadol de-khol ha-shanah ke-regel le-khol alma dami*—a *Kohen Gadol*, on any day of the year, is like a normal person on the *regel*. This is derived from the *mishna* that states *Kohen Gadol makriv onen ve-eino okhel*—a *Kohen Gadol* may bring *korbanos* even while he is an *onen*, though he may not eat them. The only logic for this is that his status as *Kohen Gadol* overrides his status as *onen*. And where else do we find that something overrides *onen* or *aveilus* status for a normal person? Only on the *regel*. Therefore, a *Kohen Gadol* is perpetually in a *regel*-like state. And yet, even he observes the practices of a *metzora*. *Shma mina*—this is good evidence that a *metzora* continues his practices even during the *regel*.

This principle—that the *Kohen Gadol* is always in a kind of *regel* state—reflects a broader idea: the *regel* is fundamentally about going up to the *Beis HaMikdash*, about being in the presence of God. The *simcha* of the *regel* is rooted in this connection, as the *gemara* elsewhere notes that *ein simcha ela be-vasar shelamim*—the joy of the *regel* is bound up with the *korbanos* and the sense of nearness to the Divine. The *Kohen Gadol*, conceptually, never leaves the *Mikdash*—*u-min ha-Mikdash lo yeitzei*. That perpetual presence constitutes his *regel*-state. And nevertheless, he observes the practices of a *metzora*.

The conclusion, then, is that the plain meaning of the *mishna* is confirmed: both *metzora* and *menudeh* observe their practices during the *regel*, while an *avel* does not. The reason, as argued above, is that the *regel* directly counters *aveilus* as an antithetical expression of sadness—whereas the practices of *metzora* and *menudeh*, though they impede *simcha*, are not themselves expressions of grief. They express ostracism, and the *regel*'s *simcha* mandate does not directly dissolve that.

## Haircut & Wrap

### Avel: Hair

The *gemara* now proceeds to enumerate the practices common to all three categories, identifying where they converge and where they diverge. The first is the haircut prohibition. An *avel* is forbidden in *tisporet*—he may not cut his hair. The source is derived from God's words to the sons of Aaron following the deaths of Nadav and Avihu: *rosheikhem al tifrau*—do not let your hair grow wild. The *gemara* understands this as God granting them an exception from what would normally be required: *michlal de-chulei alma asur*—from this we infer that for everyone else, the normal practice of *aveilus* involves letting the hair grow, that is, not cutting it.

### Menudeh & Metzora: Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדִּין וּמְצוֹרָעִין מָה הֵן בְּתִסְפּוֹרֶת? תָּא שְׁמַע: מְנוּדִּין וּמְצוֹרָעִין אֲסוּרִין לְסַפֵּר וּלְכַבֵּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* governing **those who are ostracized and lepers,** with regard to **a haircut?** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear that which** was taught in a *baraita*: **Those who are ostracized and lepers are prohibited from cutting** their hair **and laundering** their clothes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה שֶׁמֵּת, בֵּית דִּין סוֹקְלִין אֶת אֲרוֹנוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא שֶׁיַּעֲמִידוּ עָלָיו גַּל אֲבָנִים כְּגַלּוֹ שֶׁל עָכָן, אֶלָּא בֵּית דִּין שׁוֹלְחִין וּמַנִּיחִין אֶבֶן גְּדוֹלָה עַל אֲרוֹנוֹ, לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁכׇּל הַמִּתְנַדֶּה וּמֵת בְּנִידּוּיוֹ — בֵּית דִּין סוֹקְלִין אֶת אֲרוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was further taught there: If **one who is ostracized dies, the court** places **stones on his coffin. Rabbi Yehuda says:** This does **not** mean **that they pile up a heap of stones over him like the heap** of stones that was placed over the grave **of Achan** (see Joshua 7:26). **Rather, the court sends** its agents, **who place a large stone on his coffin** as a symbolic gesture. This comes **to teach you** that **anyone who is ostracized and dies while in his** period of **ostracism,** without seeking to be released from it, **the court** places **stones on his coffin.**

The *gemara* then asks: *menudeh u-metzora mah hen be-tisporet*—what is the law for a *menudeh* and a *metzora* with respect to haircuts? *Ta shma*: we have a *beraisa* that states explicitly—*menudeh u-metzora asurin le-saper u-lekhabbes*—a *menudeh* and a *metzora* may not get a haircut and may not do laundry. This is already implicit in our *mishna*, though the *beraisa* does not provide a biblical source for it.

The *beraisa* continues: *menudeh shemeis*—if a *menudeh* dies while still in a state of *niddui*, *beis din soklin es arono*—the *beis din* stones his coffin. *Rabbi Yehuda* says this does not mean piling stones upon it as was done in the case of *Achan* in the story of *Yehoshua*, but rather that the *beis din* sends and places a single large stone upon the coffin—a statement that this person died still in a state of ostracization. The language of *soklin* is harsh, evoking execution figuratively, and it is difficult to fully account for its severity. The *beraisa* concludes by restating the principle: whoever dies in *niddui*, *beis din soklin es arono*.

In terms of sourcing, by the *metzora* the source is comparatively clear: *rosho yihyeh parua* states explicitly that the hair must remain wild. And although the Torah says the garments are rent—*bgadav yihyu frumim*—it does not explicitly say he may not launder them; yet the sense of dishevelment and social alienation expressed through the garments clearly encompasses the prohibition on laundering.

### Avel: Head Wrap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל חַיָּיב בַּעֲטִיפַת הָרֹאשׁ. מִדְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: ״וְלֹא תַעְטֶה עַל שָׂפָם״, מִכְּלָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיחַיְּיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues: **A mourner is obligated to wrap** his **head** as a sign of mourning, covering his head and face. This is derived from the fact **that the Merciful One says to Ezekiel,** while he is in mourning: **"And cover not your upper lip"** (Ezekiel 24:17). God commands Ezekiel not to display outward signs of mourning, which proves **by inference that everyone** else **is obligated** to wrap their heads in this manner.

The *gemara* now addresses *atifat harosh*—the wrapping of the head. An *avel* is obligated to enveil his face—in a society in which people wore turbans and face-coverings, this meant pulling a veil across one's face. The source is from God's words to *Yechezkel*: *v'lo ta'ateh al safah*—do not wrap a garment around your lips. Since *Yechezkel* was told not to do this as part of his symbolic *aveilus*, the *gemara* derives *michlal de-chulei alma michayvei*—by inference, ordinary mourners are required to do so.

What makes the *Yechezkel* passages interesting is that they cut in both directions. In some practices, *Yechezkel* was told to do what a normal *avel* does; in others, he was told to do the opposite. Through careful interpretation—guided by an existing sense of what *aveilus* entails—the *gemara* determines in each case which applies. Here, since *Yechezkel* was commanded not to wrap his face, we infer that ordinary *aveilim* are obligated to do so.

### Menudeh & Metzora: Head Wrap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בַּעֲטִיפַת הָרֹאשׁ? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: וְהֵן מִתְעַטְּפִין וְיוֹשְׁבִין כִּמְנוּדִּין וְכַאֲבֵלִים עַד שֶׁיְּרַחֲמוּ עֲלֵיהֶם מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּלְמָא מְנוּדֶּה לַשָּׁמַיִם שָׁאנֵי, דַּחֲמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* governing **a person who is ostracized,** with regard to **wrapping of the head? Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** that which was taught in a *baraita* about those who were fasting for rain and whose prayers were not answered: **And they wrap themselves and sit as those who are ostracized and as mourners, until they are shown mercy from Heaven.** This implies that those who are ostracized must wrap their heads like mourners. **Abaye said to him: Perhaps one who is ostracized by Heaven is different, as that is more serious** than being ostracized by an earthly court.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בַּעֲטִיפַת הָרֹאשׁ? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְעַל שָׂפָם יַעְטֶה״, מִכְּלָל שֶׁחַיָּיב בַּעֲטִיפַת הָרֹאשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **What is** the *halakha* in the case of **a leper** with regard to **wrapping of the head? Come** and **hear** a proof based upon the verse: **"And he shall cover his upper lip"** (Leviticus 13:45), from which it may be learned **by inference that he is obligated to wrap** his **head.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from here** that this is the case.

The *gemara* asks: *menudeh mahu b'atifat harosh*—must a *menudeh* wrap his head? *Rav Yosef* cites a proof: the *mishna* in *Maseches Taanis* describes how, after a series of unanswered fasts, the community wraps themselves and sits *ke-menudin u'cha'avelim*—like those who are ostracized and in mourning—until God has compassion on them. This suggests that wrapping the face is indeed a practice of a *menudeh*. *Abaye* objects: *dilma menudeh l'shamayim shani d'chamir*—perhaps this refers to a *menudeh l'shamayim*, one ostracized by Heaven, which may be a more severe category. One cannot necessarily derive from there that an ordinary *menudeh* is subject to the same practice. So the proof remains inconclusive.

Regarding the *metzora*: *ta shma*—*v'al safam ya'ateh*, which applies to the *metzora*, is nearly word-for-word what was said about *Yechezkel*. Here the *metzora* is told explicitly to cover the mouth—*michlal shechayav b'atifat harosh*, he is obligated to wrap his head. *Shema mina*, this is a good proof.

The plain sense of the *mishna* supports the same conclusion regarding the *menudeh*. And this makes conceptual sense: covering one's face means not engaging directly with other people. One is not making direct face-to-face contact, not participating in ordinary interpersonal interaction. One is sealing oneself off from society. This is precisely the element that *menudeh* and *metzora* share with *aveilus*—a cutting off from the social world—even if their underlying reasons differ from those of the *avel*.

## Tefillin Distinction

### Avel: No Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, מִדְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: ״פְּאֵרְךָ חֲבוֹשׁ עָלֶיךָ״, מִכְּלָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara moves on to another *halakha*: **A mourner is prohibited from donning phylacteries.** This is derived from the fact **that the Merciful One says to Ezekiel,** while he is in mourning: **"Bind your headwear [*pe'er*] upon yourself"** (Ezekiel 24:17). The word *pe'er* alludes to phylacteries. Ezekiel was unique in that he was commanded to put on phylacteries while in mourning, which proves **by inference that everyone** else **is prohibited** from doing so.

The third category is *tefillin*. An *avel* is forbidden from putting on *tefillin*—this applies to the first day specifically, though the *gemara* here does not always distinguish between the first day, subsequent days, *aninut*, and *aveilus*. The source, again from *Yechezkel*, is *pe'ercha chavosh alecha*—your glory, put it upon yourself. *Pe'er*—glory—is understood as referring to *tefillin*. Since *Yechezkel* was told to don his glory, the *gemara* infers *michlal de-chulei alma asur*—ordinary mourners are forbidden, because wearing *tefillin* is an expression of glory, and glory is not the appropriate state for an *avel*.

### M&M: Tefillin Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בִּתְפִילִּין? תֵּיקוּ. מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בִּתְפִילִּין? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְהַצָּרוּעַ״ — לְרַבּוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל. ״בְּגָדָיו יִהְיוּ פְרוּמִים״ — שֶׁיְּהוּ מְקוֹרָעִים, ״וְרֹאשׁוֹ יִהְיֶה פָרוּעַ״ — אֵין פְּרִיעָה אֶלָּא גִּידּוּל שֵׂעָר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* governing **one who is ostracized,** with regard to phylacteries? The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved. The Gemara proceeds to its next question: **What is** the *halakha* in the case of **a leper** with regard to phylacteries? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the following *baraita*: The verse states: **"And the leper"** (Leviticus 13:45); as explained earlier, this comes **to include the High Priest** in all the *halakhot* of the leper. The verse states there: **"His garments shall be *perumim*,"** meaning **that they shall be rent.** The verse continues: **"And his head shall be *parua*,"** and this ***peria*** means **only growing the hair long;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: נֶאֶמְרָה הֲוָיָיה בָּרֹאשׁ, וְנֶאֶמְרָה הֲוָיָיה בַּבֶּגֶד. מָה הֲוָיָיה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּבֶּגֶד — דָּבָר שֶׁחוּץ מִגּוּפוֹ. אַף הֲוָיָיה בָּרֹאשׁ דָּבָר שֶׁחוּץ מִגּוּפוֹ. מַאי לָאו, אַתְּפִילִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** The meaning of these terms is derived through a verbal analogy. The verse **states** a form of the verb **being, with regard to** one's **head** in the verse: "His head shall be," teaching that a leper must perform a certain action with his head. **And the verse states** a form of the verb **being with regard to** a leper's **garment,** in the verse: "His garment shall be." **Just as** the term **being stated with regard to** his **garment** is referring to **an object external to his body, so too,** the term **being** used **with regard to** his **head** is referring to **an object external to his body.** The Gemara derives from this: **What, is it not** referring to **phylacteries,** and the verse teaches that it is prohibited for a leper to wear phylacteries?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא, אַכּוּמְתָא וְסוּדָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: No,** there is no proof from here, as the verse may be referring **to a cap [*kumta*] or a scarf [*sudara*],** which people wear on their heads. It is possible to explain that according to Rabbi Akiva, it is prohibited for a leper to wear such headwear because it is excessive adornment.

The *gemara* then asks: *menudeh mai b'tefillin*—what is the law for a *menudeh* regarding *tefillin*? *Teiku*—no evidence is available in either direction.

The question is then posed regarding a *metzora*. *Ta shma*: *v'ha-tzarua*—the extra word *ha-tzarua* in the verse—serves *lerabot Kohen Gadol*, even a *Kohen Gadol* must observe the *metzora*'s practices. These are enumerated: *bgadav yihyu frumim, sheyihyu k'ru'im*—the clothes are rent; *v'rosho yihyeh parua, ein pri'ah ela gidul se'ar*—the hair must grow wild, meaning no haircut. This is the view of *Rabbi Eliezer*. *Rabbi Akiva* argues from a *gezeirah shavah*: just as *havayah* said of the garment (*bgadav yihyu frumim*) refers to something external to the body—the manner of wearing the garment—so too *havayah* said of the head (*rosho yihyeh parua*) refers to something external to the body, something placed upon the head. One might then think *Rabbi Akiva* means the head is uncovered because the *metzora* is not wearing *tefillin*—*mai lav tefillin*? *Rav Papa* rejects this: *akumsa ve-sudra*—he is referring to a hat and a head-covering, a turban. The point is that the hair is considered wild when it is uncovered, not necessarily uncut. Tosafos clarifies that *Rabbi Akiva* does not disagree with the prohibition on cutting hair—he is simply adding that in addition, the *metzora* may not cover his head, an element structurally parallel to the rending of the garment.

In any case, we do not find evidence that a *menudeh* or a *metzora* is forbidden in *tefillin*, and the logical conclusion is that they are *muttar b'tefillin*.

### Why Tefillin Allowed

The reason *tefillin* does not apply to *menudeh* and *metzora* is that it belongs to a different category of practice. *Tefillin* is about a sense of pride and glory—it is an expression of one's emotional and spiritual standing. For an *avel*, the *gemara* cites a second *pasuk*: *acharisa k'yom mar*—the bitterness of the first day of mourning is such that the sense of glory expressed by wearing *tefillin* with God's name is simply incompatible with one's inner state. This is why the prohibition is concentrated on the first day. It is a practice rooted in an emotional condition, not in societal ostracism.

Accordingly, *menudeh* and *metzora*, whose practices are about being cast outside of society rather than about expressing an inner state of grief, have no reason to be forbidden in *tefillin*. One might argue that someone ostracized from society perhaps should not feel the full sense of *kavod* that *tefillin* represents—but the prohibition on *tefillin* is not framed as a response to ostracism, and there is no evidence that it was ever extended to these categories. Without evidence and without conceptual grounding in the ostracism framework, they remain *muttar*.

## She'elat Shalom

### Avel: Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם. דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: ״הֵאָנֵק דּוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues: **A mourner is prohibited from greeting** others or being greeted. This is derived from the fact **that the Merciful One says to Ezekiel: "Sigh in silence"** (Ezekiel 24:17), implying that aside from what was absolutely essential, he was prohibited from speaking.

The *gemara* proceeds to *she'elat shalom*—inquiring after one another's welfare. An *avel* may neither ask after others' welfare nor receive such inquiries. This is not merely about saying hello; in the *gemara*'s context it means substantive personal engagement: how are you, how is your family, how is everything going. This form of social contact is obviously inconsistent with the state of someone who is not in *shalom*—and is removed from ordinary social flow. The source is from God's command to *Yechezkel*: *he'anek dom*—be silent—which is interpreted in this context as encompassing the prohibition on *she'elat shalom*.

### Menudeh: No Greetings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: וּבִשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם שֶׁבֵּין אָדָם לַחֲבֵרוֹ כִּבְנֵי אָדָם הַנְּזוּפִין לַמָּקוֹם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּלְמָא מְנוּדֶּה לַשָּׁמַיִם שָׁאנֵי, דַּחֲמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* governing **one who is ostracized** with regard to **greeting** others? **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* dealing with those who were fasting for rain and whose prayers were not answered. These people were prohibited **from greeting one another, as people who are reprimanded by the Omnipresent.** This indicates that those who are ostracized must not greet each other, just as mourners may not greet each other. **Abaye said to him: Perhaps one who is ostracized by Heaven is different, as that is more serious** than being ostracized by an earthly court.

The *gemara* asks: *menudeh mahu b'she'elat shalom*? Based on the framework of ostracism, a *menudeh* logically should be forbidden—this prohibition is precisely about social interaction. *Rav Yosef* offers a proof from *Maseches Taanis*: after the community has not been answered in prayer following a series of fasts, they observe *she'elat shalom she'bein adam l'chavero*—they refrain from inquiring after one another's welfare—*kivnei adam ha-nizufim l'makom*, like people rejected by God. *Nizufim* is understood as equivalent to *menudin*—those who have been ostracized. This is strong evidence that a *menudeh* is forbidden in *she'elat shalom*. *Abaye* again objects: *dilma menudeh l'shamayim shani d'chamir*—perhaps this case, involving divine rejection, is more severe and therefore cannot serve as proof for an ordinary *menudeh*.

### Metzora: No Greetings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְעַל שָׂפָם יַעְטֶה״ — שֶׁיְּהוּ שִׂפְתוֹתָיו מְדוּבָּקוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ, שֶׁיְּהֵא כִּמְנוּדֶּה וּכְאָבֵל, וְאָסוּר בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **a leper** with regard to **greeting** others? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the following *baraita*: The verse states: **"And he shall cover his upper lip"** (Leviticus 13:45). This means that **his lips should be stuck together, that he should be like one who is ostracized and like a mourner, and he is prohibited from greeting** others or being greeted. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that this is so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיפְשׁוֹט מִינַּהּ לִמְנוּדֶּה? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: מִי קָתָנֵי שֶׁאָסוּר? שֶׁיְּהֵא כִּמְנוּדֶּה וּכְאָבֵל [קָתָנֵי] בְּמִילֵּי אַחְרָנְיָיתָא, וְאָסוּר נָמֵי בִּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If this is the case, then **we should** also **resolve** the question asked above **with regard to one who is ostracized** and say that such a person is prohibited from offering greetings. **Rav Aḥa bar Pineḥas said in the name of Rav Yosef: Does it** explicitly **teach** that a leper **is prohibited** from greeting others, like one who is ostracized? **It teaches** only **that he should be** treated **like one who is ostracized and like a mourner,** i.e., he should be like them **with regard to other things, and** that **he is also prohibited from greeting** others. This being the case, no proof can be derived from here with regard to whether or not one who is ostracized may offer greetings.

The *gemara* then asks: *metzora mahu b'she'elat shalom*? *Ta shma*: *v'al safah ya'ateh*—by the *metzora*, just as we said previously about covering the face. The *gemara* elaborates: *she'yehu sfosov dvukos zu b'zu*—his lips should be as though stuck together, one to the next. *She'yehei k'menudeh u'k'avel v'assur b'she'elat shalom*—he shall be like a *menudeh* and like an *avel*, and is forbidden in *she'elat shalom*. *Shema mina*—this is an explicit proof.

The *gemara* then asks: since that same *beraisa* mentions *menudeh*, why not resolve the question about *menudeh* from this passage as well? The *gemara* responds in the name of *Rav Yosef*: *mi katani she'assur*—the *beraisa* does not actually say he is forbidden in *she'elat shalom* in the same way as a *menudeh*. Rather, *she'yehei k'menudeh u'k'avel* refers to other practices—the practices of the *menudeh* and *avel* that were enumerated earlier, such as not laundering or cutting hair—*b'millei achranyasa*. And *v'assur nami b'she'elat shalom* is a separate point about the *metzora* himself. The *gemara* acknowledges this is a difficult reading of the text: the phrase as written seems to be saying one continuous thing—his lips are sealed, he is like a *menudeh* and an *avel*, and he is forbidden in *she'elat shalom*—not three separate observations. Nevertheless, the *gemara* does not fully resolve this.

### Common Societal Practice

The plain sense of everything we have seen is that a *metzora* is clearly forbidden in *she'elat shalom*, and that the *pshot* of the relevant passages supports the same for a *menudeh*. This stands to reason given the framework: *she'elat shalom* is quintessentially about social engagement—it is the fabric of ordinary human interaction—and it is therefore squarely within the domain of practices that express and enforce the social separation of both *menudeh* and *metzora*. What cannot be done on *Chol HaMoed* under normal circumstances—*she'elat shalom* being conspicuously irrelevant there since it is of course practiced during *Chol HaMoed*—is nonetheless prohibited in these states. The practice is entirely consistent with the ostracism framework.

## Torah Study

### Avel: No Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, מִדְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: ״דֹּם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara moves on to a new topic: **A mourner is prohibited** from studying **words of Torah.** This prohibition is derived **from** the fact **that the Merciful One says to Ezekiel:** "Sigh in **silence"** (Ezekiel 24:17). Ezekiel was commanded to be silent and not discuss even Torah matters.

The *gemara* now addresses *divrei Torah*. An *avel* is forbidden from learning *Torah*. The source, once again, is from *Yechezkel*: *he'anek dom*—be silent—now read as encompassing the prohibition on *Torah* study. Additionally, the *gemara* invokes a passage from *Sefer Devarim*: *v'hodatam l'vanecha v'livnei vanecha yom asher amadta lifnei Hashem Elokecha b'Chorev*—you shall tell your children the day you stood before God at Sinai. *Mah lehalan b'eima u'v'yira u'v'reses u'v'zeia*—just as the original experience at *Chorev* was accompanied by fear, awe, trembling, and sweat, so too *Torah* study must be approached in a corresponding state of mind. *Mikan amru*—from here the *gemara* learns that *Torah* study requires a certain inner orientation, which conflicts with the state of an *avel*.

### Menudeh: Torah Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: מְנוּדֶּה שׁוֹנֶה וְשׁוֹנִין לוֹ, נִשְׂכָּר וְנִשְׂכָּרִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **one who is ostracized** with regard to speaking **words of Torah? Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear that which** was taught in the following *baraita*: **One who is ostracized may teach** Torah to others, **and** others **may teach him** Torah. Similarly, **he may be hired** for work by others, **and** others **may be hired by him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּחְרָם — לֹא שׁוֹנֶה וְלֹא שׁוֹנִין לוֹ, לֹא נִשְׂכָּר וְלֹא נִשְׂכָּרִין לוֹ. אֲבָל שׁוֹנֶה הוּא לְעַצְמוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יַפְסִיק אֶת לִמּוּדוֹ, וְעוֹשֶׂה לוֹ חֲנוּת קְטַנָּה בִּשְׁבִיל פַּרְנָסָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **One who has been excommunicated,** which is a more severe form of ostracism, **may not teach** Torah to others **and** others **may not teach him. He may not be hired** by others, **and** others **may not be hired by him. However, he may study by himself,** so **that he will not interrupt his study** entirely and forget everything he knows. **And he may build a small store for his livelihood,** so that he can earn enough money to cover his most basic needs, but not more.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב — זַבּוֹנֵי מַיָּא בְּפַקְתָּא דַעֲרָבוֹת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav said:** What is the small store referred to here? This is referring to **selling water in the valley of Aravot,** where no water was to be found. One selling water there could earn a small income. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** *baraita* that one who is ostracized is permitted to study Torah.

What would one expect, given the framework established so far? Learning *Torah* has little to do with societal interaction. It is about affirming meaning, about the presence of God's word in one's life. If the prohibition on *Torah* for an *avel* is rooted in the emotional state of grief and the unsuitability of the joy and glory of *Torah* study for someone shattered by loss—similar to why *aninut* involves a *petur* from *mitzvos* entirely—then a *menudeh* and a *metzora* have no such reason to be forbidden.

*Rav Yosef* cites a *beraisa*: a *menudeh shoneh v'shonin lo*—he may give a *shiur* and others may learn from him, *niskar v'niscarin lo*—he may hire himself out and hire others. He is not completely cut off. By contrast, a *muchram*—one who is fully excommunicated by *cherem*—*lo shoneh v'lo shonin lo*—may neither teach nor be taught. But even a *muchram, shoneh hu l'atzmo*—may learn *Torah* on his own, *shelo yifosek limudo*—so that his learning not be interrupted entirely. And even a *muchram v'oseh lo chanut ktana bishvil parnasato*—may maintain a small shop to earn his livelihood. *Rav Zevid* adds in the name of *Rav*: even selling water at *piksa d'Arvos*—a place out in the middle of nowhere, as *Rabbeinu Chananel* describes it—is permitted for a *muchram*. *Shema mina*—a *menudeh* may certainly learn *Torah*.

### Metzora: Torah Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ וְלִבְנֵי בָנֶיךָ. יוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַדְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בְּחוֹרֵב״ — מָה לְהַלָּן בְּאֵימָה וּבְיִרְאָה וּבִרְתֵת וּבְזִיעַ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a leper** speaking **words of Torah?** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** that which is taught in a *baraita*: It is written: **"And you shall impart them to your children and your children's children; the day that you stood before the Lord your God in Horeb"** (Deuteronomy 4:9–10). **Just as there,** the revelation at Sinai was **in reverence, fear, and trembling,** so too, here, in every generation, Torah must be studied in a similar fashion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַזָּבִין וְהַמְצוֹרָעִין וּבוֹעֲלֵי נִדּוֹת — מוּתָּרִין לִקְרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה וּבַנְּבִיאִים וּבַכְּתוּבִים, וְלִשְׁנוֹת בַּמִּדְרָשׁ וּבַתַּלְמוּד, בַּהֲלָכוֹת וּבָאַגָּדוֹת. וּבַעֲלֵי קְרָיִין אֲסוּרִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **From here** the Sages **stated: *Zavim*, lepers, and those who had intercourse with menstruating women** despite their severe ritual impurity **are permitted to read the Torah, Prophets, and Writings, and to study midrash, Talmud, *halakhot*, and *aggada*. But those who experienced a seminal emission are prohibited** from doing so. The reason for this distinction is that the cases of severe impurity are caused by ailment or other circumstances beyond one's control; as a result, they do not necessarily preclude a sense of reverence and awe as one studies Torah. However, a seminal emission usually occurs due to frivolity and a lack of reverence and awe, and it is therefore inappropriate for one who experiences a seminal emission to engage in matters of Torah. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that a leper is permitted to study words of Torah.

The *gemara* asks: *metzora mahu b'divrei Torah*? May a *metzora* learn? *Ta shma*: *ha-zavim ve-ha-metzoraim ve-ha-baim al ha-niddot mutarin likrot ba-Torah bi-Nevi'im u-vi-Khtuvim*—those who are *tamei* due to various bodily conditions, including a *zav*, a *metzora*, and men who have been intimate with a *niddah*, may read from *Torah*, *Nevi'im*, and *Ketuvim*, and learn *Midrash*, *Shas*, *Halachos*, and *Aggados*. Their *tumah* is irrelevant—*tumah* is a *halachic* status, not an improper state of mind. *U-ba'al keryan asurin*—but a *ba'al keri*, one who has had a seminal emission, is in a state reflecting a certain mode of physical engagement with the world and is therefore restricted until he has gone to the *mikvah*. This is part of a broader discussion in *Maseches Berachos* that was largely overridden in later practice, but that is a separate matter.

The point is clear: a *metzora* is permitted to learn *Torah*. *Shema mina*.

### Torah & Emotion

The pattern is now firm: what a *menudeh* and a *metzora* may do that an *avel* cannot are learn *Torah* and wear *tefillin*. Both of these are practices tied to the emotional and spiritual inner state. *Aveilus* directly disrupts that state—the *avel*'s grief and bitterness render incompatible the affirmation of divine meaning expressed through *Torah* and the sense of glory expressed through *tefillin*. The practices of *menudeh* and *metzora*, by contrast, are about expressions of social ostracism. Learning *Torah* privately, wearing *tefillin*—these are not expressions of being part of or outside of society. They belong to the *avel*'s distinctive category because *aveilus* reaches into one's inner world in a way that *niddui* and *tzaraas* do not.

## Laundry Prohibition

### Avel: Laundry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר בְּתִכְבּוֹסֶת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח יוֹאָב תְּקוֹעָה וַיִּקַּח מִשָּׁם אִשָּׁה חֲכָמָה וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלֶיהָ הִתְאַבְּלִי נָא וְלִבְשִׁי נָא בִגְדֵי אֵבֶל וְאַל תָּסוּכִי שֶׁמֶן וְהָיִית כְּאִשָּׁה זֶה יָמִים רַבִּים מִתְאַבֶּלֶת עַל מֵת״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss another issue: **A mourner is prohibited from laundering his clothes, as it is written: "And Joab sent to Tekoa, and fetched from there a wise woman, and said to her, I pray you, feign yourself to be a mourner, and put on now mourning apparel, and do not anoint yourself with oil, but be as a woman that had a long time mourned for the dead"** (II Samuel 14:2).

An *avel* is forbidden in *tichboset*—laundry. The source is from *Sefer Shmuel*, in the story of *Yoav*'s scheme with the woman of *Tekoa*: *va'yomer eleiha hit'abli na u-livshi na bigdei eivel ve-al tasuchi shemen*—he told her to act as though she were mourning, to wear *bigdei eivel*, and not to anoint herself with oil. The instruction continues: *ve-hayit ke-isha zeh yamim rabbim mit'abelet al meit*—and you shall be like a woman who has been mourning over the dead for many days. Rather than understanding *bigdei eivel* as black clothing, the *gemara* understands it as unlaundered garments. This is the source connecting *aveilus* with the prohibition on laundry.

### M&M: Laundry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדִּין וּמְצוֹרָעִין מָה הֵן בְּתִכְבּוֹסֶת? תָּא שְׁמַע: מְנוּדִּין וּמְצוֹרָעִין אֲסוּרִין לְסַפֵּר וּלְכַבֵּס. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* concerning **laundering** with regard to **those who are ostracized or lepers? Come** and **hear** that which is taught in a *baraita*: **Those who are ostracized or lepers are prohibited from cutting** their hair **or laundering** their clothes. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from** here that they are prohibited from laundering their clothes.

The *gemara* asks: *menudin u-metzorain ma hen be-tichboset*—what is the law for *menudin* and *metzorain* regarding laundry? We already know the answer from our *mishna*: they are forbidden, since the *mishna* permits them to do laundry upon the *regel* specifically because they could not do so beforehand. The *gemara* confirms this with the *beraisa* cited earlier: *menudin u-metzorain assur le-saper u-lekhabes*—forbidden to get a haircut and to do laundry. *Shma mina*.

No independent biblical source is provided for laundry by the *menudeh* specifically. By the *metzora*, the Torah states *bgadav yihyu frumim*—the garments are rent—which conveys a sensibility about the garments expressing a state of being. Just as there are *bigdei aveilus*, there are garments that express the state of *menudeh* and *metzora*, and these are understood to be unlaundered garments. This too falls squarely within the societal dimension—the visible, external appearance that marks a person as separated from ordinary social life.

## Kri'ah: Rent Garments

### Avel: Must Rend

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל חַיָּיב בִּקְרִיעָה, דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לִבְנֵי אַהֲרֹן: ״לֹא תִפְרוֹמוּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיחַיְּיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **A mourner is obligated to rend** his clothes. This is derived from the fact **that the Merciful One says to the sons of Aaron: "Neither rend** your clothes" (Leviticus 10:6), which proves **by inference that everyone** else, all other mourners, **are obligated** to rend their clothes.

The *gemara* now addresses *kriyah*—the rending of garments. An *avel* is obligated to rend his garment. The source is God's command to the sons of Aaron: *lo tifromu*—do not rend your garments—and from this exception we derive *de-chulei alma mechayev*, that everyone else is obligated.

### M&M: No Rending

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בִּקְרִיעָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* of **rending** clothes with regard to **one who is ostracized**? No answer is found, and the question **shall stand** unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בִּקְרִיעָה? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בְּגָדָיו יִהְיוּ פְרוּמִים״ — שֶׁיְּהוּ מְקוֹרָעִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **a leper** with regard to **rending? Come** and **hear that which** is taught in the following *baraita*: The verse states: **"His garments shall be rent"** (Leviticus 13:45), meaning **that they shall be torn.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from here** that this is the case.

The *gemara* asks about *menudeh*: *teiku*—no evidence in either direction. About *metzora*: *ta shma*—*bgadav yihyu frumim, sheyihyu mikura'im*—the clothes shall be rent, meaning torn garments. *Shma minah*.

Here a conceptual distinction is worth drawing, even though the *gemara* does not state it explicitly. While the *metzora* is required to wear rent garments, this is different from what the *avel* is obligated to do. For the *avel*, the obligation encompasses not only the wearing of rent garments but the act of *kriyah* itself—the act of rending, which is an expression of being shattered by loss. Could a *metzora* have someone else rent his garments for him? Presumably yes—it is about the wearing of rent garments, not the performative act of tearing. The act of *kriyah* as a distinct expression of grief belongs uniquely to the *avel*.

### Kfiyat HaMittah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל חַיָּיב בִּכְפִיַּית הַמִּטָּה, דְּתָנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא:
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara moves to a different issue: **A mourner is obligated to overturn** his **bed,** so that he sleeps on the underside of it, **as bar Kappara taught** a *baraita* that states:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמוּת דְּיוֹקְנִי נָתַתִּי בָּהֶן, וּבַעֲוֹנוֹתֵיהֶם הֲפַכְתִּיהָ. (כָּפוּ) [יֵהָפְכוּ] מִטּוֹתֵיהֶן עָלֶיהָ. מְנוּדֶּה וּמְצוֹרָע מָה הֵן בִּכְפִיַּית הַמִּטָּה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** God stated: **I have placed the likeness of My image [*deyokan*] within** humans, as they were created in My image, **and owing to their sins I have overturned it,** as when this person died the Divine image in him was removed. Therefore, you must also **overturn your beds on account of this.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* governing **one who was ostracized or a leper,** with regard to **overturning the bed?** The Gemara has no answer, and the question **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* next addresses *kefiyas ha-mittah*—the practice of turning beds upside down. An *avel* is obligated to do this. The source comes from *Bar Kappara*: *demut diyukni natati bahen*—I have given my image, the *tzelem Elokim*, to human beings. *U-be'avonoteihem hafachtiha*—and through their sins I have been forced to turn it upside down. The idea is that death represents a loss of the divine image, a turning upside down of something fundamental, and the overturning of the beds reflects this. The practice of covering mirrors in contemporary mourning practice derives from a related sensibility—something about the human face and image in the context of loss.

The *gemara* asks: *menudeh u-metzora mahen bi-kefiyas ha-mittah*? *Teiku*. But as has been noted throughout, when there is no evidence, the presumption is that the practice does not apply. And *kefiyas ha-mattah*, as we see from the *beraisa* of *Bar Kappara*, is distinctively rooted in the theology of death and loss—it is not a practice of societal ostracism. It belongs to the inner world of *aveilus* alone.

## Melacha & Work

### Avel: No Melacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָפַכְתִּי חַגֵּיכֶם לְאֵבֶל״. מָה חַג אָסוּר בִּמְלָאכָה — אַף אָבֵל אָסוּר בִּמְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to the next topic: **A mourner is prohibited from performing work, as it is written: "And I will turn your Festivals into mourning"** (Amos 8:10). The Gemara infers: **Just as a Festival** is a time when it is **prohibited to work, so too, a mourner is prohibited from performing work.**

An *avel* is forbidden in *asiyas melacha*—engaging in labor and professional activity. The source is the verse *ve-hafachti chageichem le-avel*—I will turn your holidays into mourning. *Mah chag assur ba-asiyas melacha af avel assur ba-asiyas melacha*—just as one does not do *melacha* on a *chag*, so too an *avel* does not do *melacha*.

### Menudeh: Work Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: כְּשֶׁאָמְרוּ אָסוּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה, לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, אֲבָל בַּלַּיְלָה מוּתָּר. וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בִּמְנוּדֶּה וּבְאָבֵל. מַאי לָאו, אַכּוּלְּהוּ? לָא, אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **one who was ostracized** with regard to **the performance of work? Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** that which is taught in the following *baraita*: **When** the Sages **said that the performance of work is prohibited** on a communal fast due to lack of rain, **they meant only** that work is prohibited **during the day** of the fast, **but during the night** of the fast **it is permitted. And you find a similar** *halakha* **with regard to a person** who was **ostracized and** with regard to **a mourner. What, is it not with regard to all** of the prohibitions stated that the *baraita* says that they apply to one who was ostracized as well, including the prohibition to engage in work? The Gemara rejects this argument: **No,** this **is referring to the rest** of the prohibitions, but not to work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מְנוּדֶּה שׁוֹנֶה וְשׁוֹנִין לוֹ, נִשְׂכָּר וְנִשְׂכָּרִין לוֹ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a different proof: **Come** and **hear that which** was taught in the following *baraita*: **One who is ostracized may teach Torah** to others, **and** others **may teach him** Torah. Similarly, **he may be hired** for work by others, **and** others **may be hired by him.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from here** that it is permitted for one who is ostracized to engage in work. The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **a leper,** with regard to **the performance of work?** An answer is not found, and the question **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* asks: *menudeh mahu ba-asiyas melacha*? *Rav Yosef* cites a proof from *Maseches Taanis*: when the community was forbidden in *asiyas melacha* during severe *taaniyos*, the prohibition applied only *ba-yom*—during the day—while at night it was *muttar*. And the same, the source says, *ve-chein attah motzei bi-menudeh u-va-avel*—you find by *menudeh* and *avel*—seemingly indicating they are both forbidden in *melacha*. The *gemara* asks: *mai lav a-kuleihu*—does this not mean all three are being compared, that a *menudeh* and an *avel* cannot do *melacha*? The *gemara* deflects: *lo a-she'ara*—no, the comparison is to the other practices of *menudeh* and *avel*, not to the specific practice of *melacha* under discussion.

The *gemara* then brings the explicit *beraisa*: *menudeh shoneh v'shoneh lo, niskar v'niscarin lo*—a *menudeh* may teach and be taught, and may hire himself out and hire others. This is a direct proof that a *menudeh* is *muttar ba-asiyas melacha*. *Shema mina*.

### Metzora: Melacha Status

The *gemara* asks: *metzora mahu ba-asiyas melacha*? *Teiku*. No evidence either way. Here too, the lack of evidence is presumed to mean he is *muttar*.

### Melacha Analysis

This is one of the more interesting cases, because *asiyas melacha* might seem to belong to the societal dimension—going out to work means engaging with society and the normal economic rhythms of life. One might have expected it to be prohibited for *menudeh* and *metzora* as an expression of their separation. But as we saw from the *beraisa*, a *menudeh* is not a *muchram*—he is not completely severed from all social interaction. He can go to a *shiur*, give a *shiur*, hire workers, and be hired. The *niddui* imposes certain visible practices that set him apart, but it does not cut off all intercourse with society. Accordingly, *asiyas melacha* is permitted.

The reason the *avel* is forbidden in *melacha* is itself telling. The normal explanation given is *masiach daas min ha-aveilus*—labor distracts a person from the *aveilus*. The prohibition is designed to keep the *avel* present in his grief. This is rooted entirely in the emotional state. It has nothing to do with societal ostracization, and that is precisely why it does not extend to *menudeh* and *metzora*.

Regarding what *melacha* means in this context: it is clearly not the technical category of the thirty-nine *melachos*. Even on *Chol HaMoed*—where *melacha* is already a difficult concept to define, as we discussed at the outset—that technical framework is difficult to apply, as Tosafos acknowledged. Here the reference is to normal professional and commercial activity, the engagement in the daily economic life of society.

## Bathing Status

### Avel: No Bathing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אָסוּר בִּרְחִיצָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַל תָּסוּכִי שֶׁמֶן״, וּרְחִיצָה בִּכְלַל סִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses a different prohibition: **A mourner is prohibited from bathing, as it is written: "And do not anoint yourself with oil,** but be as a woman that had for a long time mourned for the dead" (II Samuel 14:2). **And bathing is included in** the category of **anointing,** as both activities have a similar goal, i.e., cleanliness.

The *gemara* addresses the final category: *rechitza*—bathing. An *avel* is forbidden from bathing. The source is the verse from the story of *Yoav*: *ve-al tasuchi shemen*—do not anoint yourself with oil. *Ve-rechitza bi-khlal sikah*—bathing is included within the same category as anointing, both being ways of attending to one's physical comfort and bodily care.

### Menudeh: Bathing Unclear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה מַהוּ בִּרְחִיצָה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: כְּשֶׁאָמְרוּ אָסוּר בִּרְחִיצָה לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ, אֲבָל פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו — מוּתָּר, וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בִּמְנוּדֶּה וּבְאָבֵל. מַאי לָאו אַכּוּלְּהוּ? לָא, אַשְּׁאָרָא. מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ בִּרְחִיצָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **one who is ostracized** with regard to **bathing? Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear that which** was taught in the following *baraita*: **When** the Sages **said that bathing is prohibited** on a communal fast, **they meant only** that a person may not wash **his entire body, but** washing **his face, his hands, and his feet is permitted. And you find a similar** *halakha* **with regard to a person who was ostracized and** with regard to **a mourner. What, is it not with regard to all** the prohibitions stated in the *baraita*, including the prohibition against bathing, that they apply also to one who is ostracized? The Gemara rejects this argument: **No,** this **is referring to the rest** of the prohibitions, but not to bathing. The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* concerning **a leper** with regard to **bathing?** An answer is not found, and the question **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* asks: *menudeh mahu bi-rechitza*? *Rav Yosef* cites the *Taanis* source: *k'she-omru assur bi-rechitza lo omru ela kol gufo*—when they said one may not bathe, this applied only to bathing the full body; *aval panav yadav ve-raglav muttar*—the face, hands, and feet remain permitted. *Ve-chein attah motzei bi-menudeh u-va-avel*—and similarly you find by *menudeh* and *avel*, apparently meaning that they too are forbidden in bathing with these distinctions. The *gemara* again deflects: *lo a-she'ara*—perhaps the comparison is to other practices of *menudeh* and *avel*, not specifically to bathing. The evidence is thus ambiguous.

### Bathing Framework

This is a case where the conceptual framework suggests a possible answer—if someone does not bathe they will be somewhat more on the outskirts of society, physically set apart in a visible way—but bathing is less clearly about societal ostracism than haircuts or head-covering. If the lack of bathing has not yet reached the point of being visibly offensive, then it may be less about societal signaling and more about simply attending to one's physical needs. Alternatively, the prohibition on bathing may be more about *tzaar*—physical discomfort as an expression of mourning—which would place it squarely in the *aveilus* category and out of the domain of the *menudeh* and *metzora*.

### Metzora: Bathing Unclear

The *gemara* asks: *metzora mahu bi-rechitza*? *Teiku*. No evidence in either direction. The *gemara* thus ends without a clear resolution regarding bathing. The evidence by *menudeh* points toward a prohibition but is not explicit; by *metzora* there is simply no evidence. This is accordingly a case where the *gemara* leaves matters genuinely unresolved, and it is noted as such.
