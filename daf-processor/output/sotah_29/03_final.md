# Sotah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sotah & Safek Tumah

### Intro: Sotah to Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמָּקוֹם שֶׁבָּאתָ: מָה סוֹטָה רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — אַף שֶׁרֶץ רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד. וּמָה סוֹטָה דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל — אַף שֶׁרֶץ דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל. וּמִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל, בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — סְפֵיקוֹ טָמֵא, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — סְפֵיקוֹ טָהוֹר. וְשֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל, בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — סְפֵיקוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** since the case of *sota* is the source for the *halakha* with regard to uncertain ritual impurity, the details of the *halakha* are also derived from the case of *sota*. Therefore, **from the place that you came** from, i.e., from the source, it is derived that **just as** the prohibition with regard to **a *sota*** applies only when the uncertainty arises in **the private domain,** i.e., when she has secluded herself with the alleged paramour, **so too,** uncertain contact with the carcass of **a creeping animal** renders an item impure only if the contact was in **the private domain.** **And** furthermore, **just as a *sota* is an entity that has awareness** in order for her **to be asked** whether she actually committed adultery, **so too,** contact with **a creeping animal** renders an item impure only if it is **an entity that has awareness** in order for it **to be asked,** i.e., a person, or an item that may have contracted impurity in a place where a person was present and could have known. The *baraita* concludes: **And from here** the Sages **stated** that if **an entity that has awareness** in order for it **to be asked** may have contracted impurity **in the private domain, its uncertain** impurity renders it **impure;** but if it may have contracted impurity **in the public domain, its uncertain** impurity leaves it **pure. And** with regard to an entity **that lacks awareness** in order for it **to be asked, whether** the uncertainty arose **in the private domain or in the public domain, its uncertain** impurity is deemed **pure,** as it is not similar to a *sota*.

Today's daf is 29, and we pick up at the bottom of 28b. The sugya has now transitioned from *sotah* to *terumah* — which, somewhat surprisingly, will be the focus of this entire daf. The immediate reason for the shift is not primarily the connection between *sotah* and *terumah* as such, but rather that our *mishnah* of this *perek* recorded a series of *drashos* under the heading *bo bayom darash Rabbi Akiva*, several of which concern *tumah*, and it is those *drashos* that will occupy us.

The original connection was that from the case of *sotah* — a wife who secluded herself (*yichud*) with another man after her husband's formal warning — we derived the broader principle governing *safek tumah bereshut hayachid*. The *sotah's* status demonstrates that once the relevant circumstances are established, the presumption shifts: she is treated as definitively forbidden rather than merely doubtfully so.

### Sotah Status & Tosafos

The *halacha* is that a *sotah* is *vadai assur* — definitively forbidden — on the basis of the *safek*. This status holds until it is clarified through the formal procedure of drinking the bitter water. *Tosafos* draws out an important implication: even if it later becomes clear that she had not in fact committed adultery, or even if she herself knows she has not, the violation of her current status — such as sleeping with her husband before the matter is resolved — constitutes a genuine transgression. Her situation is one of definitive prohibition, not merely doubtful prohibition.

### Safek Tumah Principle

From this, the Gemara derives the general principle of *safek tumah bereshut hayachid*. The language of *tumah* in the *sotah* context carries its own specific meaning, but the transfer to the general laws of *tumah* throughout the Torah is significant. The conditions are formal and precise: the doubt must arise in a *reshut hayachid*, a private domain, and there must be *yesh bo da'at lisha'el* — someone present who is capable of being asked about the circumstances.

One way of understanding why the *safek* generates *vadai tumah* is that treating the matter as definitively impure, pending clarification, creates incentive for resolution: witnesses may come forward, participants may remember. This social function, however, only makes sense when there is actually someone present who might know something — hence the requirement of *yesh bo da'at lisha'el*. If no one is present who could be questioned, *ein bo da'at lisha'el*, then even in a private domain the doubt is resolved leniently as *tahor*.

### Kinuy & Stira Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא תְּרוּמָה, וּמַהְדַּר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ כְּהוּנָּה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara begins its discussion of the *baraita* by inquiring about the exchange between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva: **And** what was the intention of **Rabbi Yishmael?** Apparently, he commented on a statement of **Rabbi Akiva,** who **said** that it is prohibited for a *sota* to partake of ***teruma*, and he answered him** concerning the matter of the woman's being prohibited to marry into the **priesthood,** which was not mentioned by Rabbi Akiva at all.

*Tosafos* raises a pointed question about the analogy between *sotah* and *safek tumah*. In the case of ordinary *tumah*, the doubt is a straightforward fifty-fifty uncertainty. By contrast, the *sotah* case seems to involve *raglayim ladavar* — circumstantial indicators suggesting guilt — and not a mere equipoise. Where, then, is the fifty-fifty doubt?

*Tosafos* introduces a crucial conceptual framework in response. The *kinuy* (the husband's formal warning) and *stira* (the subsequent seclusion) together produce a fifty-fifty doubt. Without them, the situation would not even qualify as a genuine *safek* in the first place. The *kinuy* and *stira* are thus not evidence of guilt; they are the precondition that elevates the situation to the status of a doubt worth taking seriously. This is an important lens through which to understand the *sotah* institution, and one that had not been fully foregrounded in earlier discussion.

It should also be noted that *reshut hayachid* in this context does not carry its Shabbos meaning. Here it refers more to a private location, and the determination is functional rather than spatial.

### Three Restrictions

The Gemara recaps the three restrictions the *sotah* incurs from the word *venitma'ah*: she is forbidden to her *ba'al* (her husband), forbidden to the *bo'el* (the suspected adulterer), and forbidden to eat *terumah*. The last applies whether she is the wife of a *kohen* — in which case her marital status no longer entitles her to *terumah* — or a *bat kohen* herself, in which case this act is understood to invalidate her personal entitlement as well. It is worth noting, as was observed in discussion, that while her husband's financial obligations toward her presumably continue during the pendency of the process, the prohibition from *terumah* takes immediate effect.

## R. Akiva vs. R. Yishmael

### R. Yishmael's Kal VaChom…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ: לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, כְּהוּנָּה מְנָא לֵיהּ? וְכִי תֵּימָא כְּהוּנָּה לָא צְרִיכָא קְרָא, שֶׁהֲרֵי עָשָׂה בָּהּ סְפֵק זוֹנָה כְּזוֹנָה. תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי לָא תִּיבְּעֵי קְרָא, שֶׁהֲרֵי עָשָׂה בָּהּ סְפֵק זוֹנָה כְּזוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, from where does Rabbi Akiva derive** that it is prohibited for a *sota* to marry into the **priesthood? And if you would say** that with regard to her prohibition against marrying into the **priesthood a verse is not necessary, as** the Torah has already **rendered an uncertain** case of **a woman who has engaged in sexual intercourse with a man forbidden to her by the Torah [*zona*] as though** she is certainly **a *zona*,** since a *sota* is forbidden to her husband after seclusion even though there are no witnesses that she committed adultery, and therefore it should be prohibited for her to marry a priest just like any *zona*, then with regard to her prohibition against partaking of ***teruma* a verse should also not be necessary, as** it is prohibited for a *zona* to partake of *teruma*. Therefore, the Torah **renders an uncertain *zona* like** a certain ***zona*.

Rabbi Yishmael, responding to Rabbi Akiva's derivation of *terumah* from *venitma'ah*, proposes a *kal vachomer*: if there exist women who are forbidden to marry a *kohen* yet are still permitted to eat *terumah* — such as a *gerushah* who is a *bat kohen* — then a woman who cannot eat *terumah* at all should certainly be forbidden from marrying a *kohen*. The scenario being contemplated is where this woman's husband subsequently dies; her status as a presumed adulteress or as a *sotah* should, by this logic, render her permanently forbidden to a *kohen* just as a *zonah* is.

Rabbi Akiva's position, as introduced in the *mishnah*, had been that she is forbidden to the *ba'al*, forbidden to the *bo'el*, and forbidden to eat *terumah* — three distinct restrictions, each derived from a separate word or letter. The Gemara now examines whether Rabbi Yishmael's *kal vachomer* was meant as an addition to Rabbi Akiva's teaching or as a disagreement with it.

### Gemara's Challenge

The Gemara raises a sharp challenge: according to Rabbi Akiva, where does the prohibition of *kehunah* come from? And if Rabbi Akiva would say he simply does not need a *pasuk* for *kehunah* — because a woman who commits adultery acquires the status of a *zonah*, and since we treat *safek* like *vadai* we would say *safek zonah ke-zonah*, rendering her forbidden to a *kohen* automatically — then the same argument should equally spare him the need for a *pasuk* regarding *terumah*. The prohibition of a *zonah* from *terumah* is already established by the verse *u'vat kohen ki tihyeh le-ish zar hi betrumas hakodashim lo tochel*, which Rabbi Akiva interprets to cover a woman who has relations with a man forbidden to her. So why would he need a separate *pasuk* to tell him she is forbidden to *terumah*?

This is a tight and well-constructed challenge: if the logic of *zonah* status can do the work for *kehunah*, it should equally do the work for *terumah*, leaving Rabbi Akiva's three *venitma'ah* derivations without adequate content.

### Akiva's Four Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אַרְבְּעָה קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: חַד לְבַעַל, וְחַד לְבוֹעֵל, וְחַד לִכְהוּנָּה, וְחַד לִתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be explained that **according to Rabbi Akiva,** the equivalent of **four verses** worthy of exposition **are written** with regard to the defilement of a *sota*, as he maintains that an additional *halakha* should be derived from the superfluous prefix *vav* in the verse: "And is defiled [*venitma'a*]" (Numbers 5:29). Therefore, **one** verse is written to forbid her **to** her **husband, and one** is to forbid her **to** her **paramour, and one** is to forbid her **to** marry into the **priesthood, and one** is to forbid her **to** partake of ***teruma*.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty by noting that according to Rabbi Akiva there are in fact four textual indicators, not three. The word *venitma'ah* appears three times in the *sotah* section, but one of the instances is preceded by a *vav* that Rabbi Akiva treats as generating an additional derivation. So we have three *venitma'ahs* plus one *vav* — four sources in total. One teaches that she is forbidden to her *ba'al*, one that she is forbidden to the *bo'el*, one that she is forbidden to *terumah*, and one — derived from the extra *vav* — that she is forbidden in *kehunah*.

The need for the *kehunah* derivation from the *vav* is that we cannot simply assume her status as a *zonah* provides it. Evidently Rabbi Akiva holds that a woman in the uncertain status of *sotah* does not automatically become a *zonah* in a way that would independently establish all the attendant restrictions. Hence the *pasuk* must supply the *kehunah* prohibition explicitly.

### Yishmael's Three + Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: חַד לְבַעַל, וְחַד לְבוֹעֵל, וְחַד לִתְרוּמָה, וּכְהוּנָּה אָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael** disagrees with Rabbi Akiva, as he does not expound on the superfluous *vav*, and therefore maintains that only **three verses are written: One** is to forbid her **to** her **husband, and one** is to forbid her **to** her **paramour, and one** is to forbid her **to** partake of ***teruma*. And** her being prohibited to marry into the **priesthood is derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, as described in the *baraita*.

Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, does not derive from the extra *vav*. He therefore has only three *venitma'ahs*: one for *ba'al*, one for *bo'el*, and one for *terumah*. From *terumah* he then constructs a *kal vachomer* to *kehunah*: since she is forbidden from eating *terumah*, she should certainly be forbidden from marrying a *kohen*.

### Relevance Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מִמַּאי דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לִתְרוּמָה, וּכְהוּנָּה אָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר? דִּילְמָא כִּי אִצְטְרִיךְ לִכְהוּנָּה, וּתְרוּמָה שַׁרְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yishmael** derive **that the verse was necessary** to teach the prohibition of a *sota* **to** partake of ***teruma*, and** her prohibition against marrying into the **priesthood is derived through an *a fortiori*** inference? **Perhaps** the verse **was necessary** in order to teach that it is prohibited for the woman to marry **into** the **priesthood, but *teruma* is permitted** for her?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: מִסְתַּבְּרָא דּוּמְיָא דְּבַעַל וּבוֹעֵל. מָה בַּעַל וּבוֹעֵל מֵחַיִּים — אַף תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי מֵחַיִּים, לְאַפּוֹקֵי כְּהוּנָּה דִּלְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael could have **said to you: It is reasonable** to derive from this verse a prohibition that is **similar to** the prohibitions derived from the other verses, i.e., that she is forbidden **to** her **husband and** her **paramour. Just as** she is forbidden to her **husband and** her **paramour** even **during** the **lifetime** of her husband, **so too,** the prohibition against partaking of ***teruma*** applies **also during** the **lifetime** of her husband, **to the exclusion of** her prohibition against marrying into the **priesthood, which** is relevant only **after** the **death** of her husband. The reason is that if her husband would divorce her it would be prohibited for her to marry a priest anyway.

The Gemara presses: how does Rabbi Yishmael know that the third *venitma'ah* teaches *terumah* rather than *kehunah*? One could argue the reverse — that the third *venitma'ah* establishes the prohibition of *kehunah*, and that *terumah* would then be permitted, since we know there are women forbidden to marry a *kohen* who nevertheless retain the right to eat *terumah* (a *gerushah bat kohen* being the paradigm case).

Rabbi Yishmael's response is elegant: the third *venitma'ah* should be read in parallel with the first two. What do the first two establish? Prohibitions that take effect *m'chayim* — while everyone is still alive: she is forbidden to her husband and to the suspected adulterer here and now. A prohibition of *terumah* similarly applies immediately: she is a *bat kohen*, say, and she loses her right to eat *terumah* at this moment. A prohibition of *kehunah*, by contrast, only becomes relevant if her husband dies — only then would the question of whether she may marry another *kohen* arise. Rabbi Yishmael therefore concludes that the third *venitma'ah* must be targeting the immediately applicable prohibition, which is *terumah*, and *kehunah* is then derived by *kal vachomer*.

### Akiva Rejects Dumiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דּוּמְיָא דְּבַעַל וּבוֹעֵל לֵית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how would **Rabbi Akiva** respond? The Gemara answers: **He is not** of the opinion that it is more reasonable to derive a prohibition from the phrase "and is defiled" that is **similar to** the prohibitions involving her **husband and** her **paramour,** and therefore he requires two separate derivations; one for *teruma* and one for the priesthood.

Rabbi Akiva does not accept this framework of *dumya de-ba'al u-vo'el* — the comparison that presumes the third *venitma'ah* must, like the first two, address something immediately applicable. In his view, the third *venitma'ah* might well teach the lesser *chiddush*, namely the prohibition of *kehunah*, which applies only post-mortem, while *terumah* would remain unknown without an additional source. That is why he requires the extra *vav* — to capture *kehunah* — while dedicating the third *venitma'ah* to *terumah*.

### Redundancy Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי נָמֵי אִית לֵיהּ, מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וְחוֹמֶר טָרַח וְכָתֵב לַהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Or alternatively,** perhaps **he accepts** the opinion that the *halakha* derived from "and is defiled" should be similar to the prohibitions involving the husband and paramour, but nevertheless, in certain instances with regard to **a matter that can be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, the **verse** nevertheless **takes the trouble and writes** explicitly. Therefore, although unnecessary, two verses are stated, one for *teruma* and one for the priesthood.

Alternatively, the Gemara suggests, Rabbi Akiva might agree that in principle a *kal vachomer* could yield the *kehunah* prohibition, but he nonetheless uses the *vav* to state it explicitly. The principle invoked is *milsa d'asi b'kal vachomer tarach v'kasav lah kra*: something that could have been derived logically, the Torah nevertheless troubled itself to write explicitly.

This principle stands in interesting tension with the Gemara's occasional declaration elsewhere of *lama li kra, sevara hi* — why do I need a *pasuk*, I could figure it out myself? That formulation reflects the remarkable weight accorded to human reason and *sevara* in the tradition of *Torah she-be'al peh*, to the point that a verse can be set aside or interpreted non-literally when it conflicts with what logic demands. The flip side, captured by *milsa d'asi b'kal vachomer tarach v'kasav lah kra*, is that this same rational force does not make a scriptural reaffirmation unwelcome or superfluous. It is not a problem for the Torah to state explicitly what one could have derived — the two sources are not in conflict, they simply converge on the same conclusion from different directions.

## Safek Tumah from Korban

### Safek Tumah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל וְאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל מֵהַאי קְרָא נָפְקָא: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל״; וַדַּאי טָמֵא הוּא דְּלֹא יֵאָכֵל, הָא סָפֵק טָמֵא וְסָפֵק טָהוֹר — יֵאָכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues discussing the *baraita*. **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** The *halakha* with regard to **an entity that has awareness** in order for it **to be asked and** an entity that **lacks awareness** in order for it **to be asked** in cases of uncertain ritual impurity **is derived from this verse: "And the flesh that touches any impure thing shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 7:19). This would seem to indicate that specifically food that is **impure** for **certain is** that **which shall not be eaten, but** food for which it is **uncertain** whether it is **impure and uncertain** whether it is **pure can be eaten.**

Rav Gidal said in the name of Rav: the rule that *davar she-yesh bo da'at lisha'el* in a *reshut hayachid* yields *safek tamei*, while *ein bo da'at lisha'el* yields *safek tahor* regardless of domain — this rule is not derived primarily from *sotah*. It is derived from a different *pasuk* entirely.

### Korban Meat Source

The source is the verse *ve-habasar asher yiga b'chol tamei lo ye'achel* — meat of a *korban* that touches anything *tamei* may not be eaten. The Gemara reads this carefully: *vadai tamei hu de-lo ye'achel* — it is only if it is definitively known to be *tamei* that it may not be eaten; if there is doubt, *ha-safek tamei safek tahor*, it may be eaten.

### Safek Tahor Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר כׇּל טָהוֹר יֹאכַל בָּשָׂר״; וַדַּאי טָהוֹר הוּא דְּיֹאכַל בָּשָׂר, הָא סָפֵק טָמֵא וְסָפֵק טָהוֹר — לֹא יֹאכַל.
>
> **Translation:** However, **say the latter clause** of the verse: **"And as for the flesh, every one that is pure may eat the flesh"** (Leviticus 7:19), which would seem to indicate that specifically one who is **pure** for **certain is** one **who shall eat meat, but** one for whom it is **uncertain** whether he is **impure and uncertain** whether he is **pure shall not eat.** The two clauses in the verse seem to contradict one another with regard to the status of uncertain purity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כָּאן שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל, כָּאן שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that the Torah differentiates between two different types of uncertainty? **Here,** the latter clause, which discusses "every one that is pure," is referring to an entity **that has awareness** in order for it **to be asked,** e.g., a person, who is considered impure if he is uncertain whether he contracted ritual impurity. **There,** the former clause which discusses impure meat presents the principle of an entity **that lacks awareness** in order for it **to be asked,** where an uncertain case of ritual impurity is deemed pure.

This reading, however, creates an apparent contradiction. The end of the verse states *kol tahor yochal basar* — whoever is *tahor* may eat the meat — from which one might derive that it is only when one is definitively *tahor* that eating is permitted, and that *safek tamei* is therefore forbidden. The beginning of the verse seems to teach *safek tahor* while the end teaches *safek tamei*. The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing between contexts: one clause addresses the case where no person is on the scene — only the object, *ein bo da'at lisha'el* — and the doubt about the meat itself is resolved leniently; the other clause addresses the case where a person is present, *yesh bo da'at lisha'el*, and in that case doubt is resolved strictly.

One might have proposed a different distinction — that the beginning of the verse speaks about doubt concerning the meat and the end about doubt concerning the person — but the Gemara does not adopt this reading. The determining factor is not which party is in doubt but whether anyone capable of being consulted is present in the situation at all.

### Domain Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ דְּרַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיגְמַר מִסּוֹטָה. דְּאִי מִדְּרַב, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא — בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּבֵין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיגְמַר מִסּוֹטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the need for two separate derivations concerning uncertain contractions of ritual impurity: **And** the derivation **that Rav Giddel** said that **Rav said was necessary, and** it **was** also **necessary to derive** the principle of uncertain ritual impurity **from *sota*; since if** it were derived only **through** the derivation **of Rav, I would say** that it makes no difference **whether** the uncertain contraction of impurity occurred **in the private domain or whether** it occurred **in the public domain.** Therefore, it **was necessary to derive from *sota*** that uncertain impurity is considered impure only in the private domain.

If one had only this *pasuk* from *korban* meat, one might have concluded that the *yesh bo da'at* / *ein bo da'at* distinction applies equally regardless of whether the incident occurred in a public or private domain. It is the *sotah* case that teaches the additional variable of *reshut hayachid* versus *reshut harabbim*.

### Two Sources Combined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִסּוֹטָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא — עַד דְּאִיכָּא דַּעַת נוֹגֵעַ וּמַגִּיעַ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** it is derived only **from *sota*, I would say** that similar to *sota*, where both the woman and the paramour possess awareness in order to be asked if they committed the act, so too, items with uncertain impurity should not be deemed impure **unless there is awareness** on the part of both **the one who touches** the impure item **and the one who causes him to touch,** i.e., the agent of impurity and the recipient of impurity are both competent people. Therefore, the derivation of Rav was **necessary,** as it teaches that in an uncertain case of impurity in the private domain, one is deemed impure even if only the contractor of impurity possesses the awareness to be asked.

Conversely, if one had only the *sotah* model, one might have concluded that *safek tumah* in a *reshut hayachid* requires *da'at* on the part of both parties — the one who touches and the one who is touched — since in *sotah* both the man and the woman are present and aware of the circumstances. By *sotah* alone, one might limit the strict ruling to cases where both the *tamei* and *tahor* parties have *da'at*, making the rule inapplicable when, for instance, a rodent touches an object with no human witness. It is the *korban* meat *pasuk* that teaches the rule requires only that some person be on the scene who is capable of being asked — not that both parties to the contact must have *da'at*.

Both sources are therefore necessary: *sotah* supplies the *reshut hayachid* dimension, and the *korban* meat verse supplies the *da'at* principle in its correct formulation.

## Shlishi LeTerumah

### R. Yehoshua's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְכׇל כְּלִי חֶרֶשׂ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna: **On that same day Rabbi Akiva interpreted** the phrase "shall be impure" in the verse: **"And every earthen vessel** into which any of them falls, whatever is in it shall be impure [*yitma*], and you shall break it" (Leviticus 11:33), as indicating that a loaf that has second-degree ritual impurity can render other food with which it comes into contact impure with third-degree impurity. Rabbi Yehoshua related that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai had predicted that a future generation would purify a loaf that contracted third-degree impurity, as there is no explicit verse in the Torah stating that this degree of impurity exists.

The Gemara returns to the *mishnah*, where Rabbi Akiva's *bo bayom* teachings include a derivation from the *pasuk* of *kli cheres*: *kol asher betocho yitma* — anything inside an earthenware vessel contaminated by a *sheretz* becomes *tamei*. Rabbi Akiva's *drasha* of *yitma* — reading it as *yitamei*, meaning it has the capacity to further transmit *tumah* — establishes that food can become a *shlishi*, a third-degree derivative of *tumah*, with respect to *terumah*.

Rabbi Yehoshua's famous reaction was: *mi gala afar me'einecha, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai?* — "Who removed the dust from your eyes, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai?" The comment is pointed: Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai had taught that the concept of *shlishi biterumah* rests only on a *kal vachomer*, not on an explicit *pasuk*. Rabbi Akiva's derivation demonstrated that it has a scriptural basis after all, sparing it from challenge. The remark comes in appreciation — the earlier master had said we must acknowledge the weakness of the derivation, and the later master has now shored it up.

### Pre-Akiva Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵאַחַר דְּאֵין לוֹ, לָמָּה טָמֵא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But since** the loaf **does not have** an explicit verse stating that it is impure, **why** did Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai himself maintain that it is **impure?**

This leads the Gemara to ask: if before Rabbi Akiva there was no *pasuk*, why did everyone nonetheless assume that food could become a *shlishi* with respect to *terumah*? Why was it treated as *tamei* without scriptural warrant?

### Rav Yehuda's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מִן הַתּוֹרָה אֵין לוֹ, מִדִּין קַל וָחוֹמֶר יֵשׁ לוֹ. וּמָה טְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁמּוּתָּר בְּחוּלִּין — פּוֹסֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, כִּכָּר שֵׁנִי שֶׁפָּסוּל בְּחוּלִּין — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בִּתְרוּמָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai maintained that although **it has no** explicit basis **from** a verse in **the Torah, it has** proof for its impurity **through an *a fortiori* inference: If** even **one who immersed** in a ritual bath **that day** and will become completely purified after nightfall, **who is** therefore **permitted to** touch **non-sacred** articles, i.e., he does not transmit impurity to them, **disqualifies *teruma*** that he touches, then with regard to **a loaf** that has **second-**degree impurity as result of contact with an impure item of the first degree, **which is disqualified,** i.e., it is rendered impure, even if it is **non-sacred, isn't it logical that it should impart third-**degree impurity **upon *teruma*?**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: although there was no explicit *pasuk*, there is nevertheless a *din kal vachomer* — the concept of *shlishi* for *terumah* can be derived logically. The *halacha* was assumed based on this *kal vachomer* even before Rabbi Akiva supplied the *pasuk*.

### Tevul Yom Kal VaChomer

The *kal vachomer* proceeds as follows: a *tevul yom* — a person who has immersed in a *mikvah* but for whom sunset has not yet arrived — is *mutar b'chullin*, permitted with respect to non-sanctified food, meaning he is not considered *tamei* in a way that would compromise ordinary *chullin*. Yet *poseil bitrumah* — he invalidates *terumah* that he touches. Now, a *kikar sheini* — a loaf of bread that is a second-degree derivative of *tumah* — is itself *pasul b'chullin*, meaning it already creates a problem even at the level of non-sanctified food. Surely, then — *eino din sheyaaseh shlishi biterumah* — this loaf, which is already *tamei* with respect to *chullin*, should certainly be able to render *terumah* it touches *pasul* as a *shlishi*.

The structural logic of the *kal vachomer* calls for something that is *tahor* with respect to *chullin* yet invalidates *terumah*, from which we argue *a fortiori* that something *tamei* with respect to *chullin* should certainly invalidate *terumah*. The *tevul yom* approximates the first role — he does not disqualify *chullin* yet he cannot eat *terumah*. There is, however, an acknowledged imprecision in comparing a person to a loaf of bread, and one might equally observe that a *tevul yom* is not formally classified as a *sheini betu'mah*; his status is its own category of residual *tumah*. But that is the argument as it stands, and we proceed to examine where it can be challenged.

## Tzad HaShaVeh Analysis

### Avi HaTumah Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לִטְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁכֵּן אַב הַטּוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: This *a fortiori* inference **can be refuted. What** is unique **about one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **is that** prior to his immersion, he was **a primary source of impurity.** He therefore retains his stringent status even after immersion with regard to his capability of disqualifying *teruma*. The loaf, by contrast, has second-degree impurity from the outset, and should therefore be treated more lightly.

The *kal vachomer* is immediately open to challenge: *mah letevul yom she-kein avi hatumah*. The *tevul yom*'s residual *tumah* may seem weak, but it originated from something weightier. The person in question began as an *av hatumah* or at least a *rishon* — perhaps he was a *baal keri* who himself had the status of an *av hatumah*, or at minimum he had directly touched a *sheretz* and began as a *rishon*. The residual *tumah* of a person who started at a higher level may be more potent than the initial *tumah* of a *kikar sheini*, which never started above *sheini*. One cannot therefore conclude from the *tevul yom*'s power over *terumah* that a *sheini* of food equally has that power.

### Gemara's Middle Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תֵּיתֵי מִטְּבוּל יוֹם דְּשֶׁרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Derive** this *halakha* **from one who immersed that day** who was never a primary source of impurity, as he was rendered impure only **through** contact with **a creeping animal,** and nevertheless he disqualifies *teruma* upon contact.

The Gemara attempts to circumvent this objection by substituting a different *tevul yom*: *tevul yom de-sheretz*, a person who touched a *sheretz* and then immersed. Such a person was only a *rishon*, not an *av hatumah*, and his residual *tumah* after immersion started from a lower point. If even the residual *tumah* of someone who started only as a *rishon* is powerful enough to disqualify *terumah*, then the argument toward *sheini* of food should be stronger.

### Persons vs. Foods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִטְבוּל יוֹם דְּשֶׁרֶץ שֶׁכֵּן בְּמִינוֹ אַב הַטּוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to challenge the inference: **What** is unique **about** the impurity of **one who immersed that day** who was rendered impure **through** contact with **a creeping animal is that** this impurity can apply to either a person or a utensil, as people and utensils are capable of achieving purity through immersion, and **within its type** there can be **a primary source of impurity.** A loaf, however, is food, which can never be a primary source of impurity. It can only be rendered impure as a secondary source of impurity.

But this middle ground still does not close the gap. *Mah letevul yom de-sheretz she-kein bemino avi hatumah* — even in the case of *tevul yom de-sheretz*, the *tumah* of persons belongs to a category that is intrinsically more potent, because a person is capable, at least in principle, of becoming an *av hatumah*. Food, by contrast, can never become an *av hatumah* no matter what it touches — even if food were to come into contact with a *meit*, it becomes a *rishon* at most, never an *av*. The *tumah* of persons may therefore be categorically weightier than the *tumah* of foods, and one cannot use residual personal *tumah* as a lever to prove anything about the initial *tumah* of a *sheini* in food. This distinction — that the category of personal *tumah* carries an intrinsic weight that food *tumah* does not, even before considering the actual degree — is striking and conceptually important.

### Combined Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלִי חֶרֶשׂ יוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakhot* of **an earthenware vessel can prove** that the fact that there are primary sources of impurity within its type is not a relevant factor. An earthenware vessel can never become a primary source of impurity, and nevertheless, if it is impure it disqualifies *teruma* upon contact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ — שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא מֵאֲוִירוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **What** is unique **about an earthenware vessel is that** unlike a loaf, it **can render** items **impure** or it can itself become impure **from its airspace.** An earthenware vessel is the only vessel that does not require direct contact with another item in order to contract or transfer impurity, but can effect or contract impurity through its airspace.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְבוּל יוֹם יוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **One who immersed that day can prove** that being able to render items impure through airspace is not a relevant factor. Such an item disqualifies *teruma*, but imparts impurity only through direct contact and not through its airspace.

Since the *kal vachomer* from *tevul yom* cannot stand alone, the Gemara introduces *kli cheres* as a second source and proposes learning from a *tzad hashaveh* — a common denominator between the two. The argument runs as follows: a *tevul yom* (a person with residual *tumah*) can invalidate *terumah*; a *kli cheres* that is a *rishon* (and which, like food, can never become an *av hatumah*) can invalidate *terumah*. Since we have two instances — one a person, one a vessel — we can extract what is common to both and argue that anything sharing that common feature should similarly invalidate *terumah*.

The *kli cheres* case is useful because it removes the objection about personal *tumah* being categorically weightier: *kli cheres* is not a person, it cannot become an *av hatumah*, and yet it invalidates *terumah*. Against this, one might note that *kli cheres* is a *rishon*, not a *sheini*, and its case involves *tumah me'aviro* — it transmits *tumah* even without direct contact through the airspace of the vessel — which is a distinctive stringency not found in the *tevul yom*. Hence neither source is free of distinctive characteristics.

### Weakness Eliminated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוֹזֵר הַדִּין. לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁמּוּתָּרִין בְּחוּלִּין, וּפוֹסְלִין בִּתְרוּמָה: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן כִּכָּר שֵׁנִי שֶׁפּוֹסֵל בְּחוּלִּין — דְּפוֹסֵל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the derivation has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case, as each case has its own unique stringencies. However, **their common denominator is that non-sacred** food they come into contact with **is permitted,** i.e., is not rendered impure, **but they disqualify *teruma*.** Therefore, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai infers *a fortiori* that **all the more so, a loaf** that contracted **second-**degree impurity, **which is disqualified,** i.e., rendered impure, even if it is **non-sacred,** should also **disqualify *teruma*** with which it comes into contact. This was Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai's logical *a fortiori* inference that led him to rule that a loaf of second-degree impurity status disqualifies *teruma*.

This is precisely the *chozar ha-din* dynamic: each of the two sources has characteristics that might explain its power over *terumah* that the other does not share, and neither characteristic is present in the *kikar sheini*. The argument oscillates between the two — one deflects the objection raised against the other — until we are left not with a true *kal vachomer* but with a *tzad hashaveh*.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדוֹר אַחֵר פָּרֵיךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן — שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן צַד חָמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And another generation,** i.e., the later generation that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai predicted would deem *teruma* that came into contact with second-degree impurity pure, would **refute** this inference as follows: **What** is unique **about their common denominator is that** both cases **have a stringent aspect** that does not exist in other impure items.

The *tzad hashaveh*, as formulated by Rashi (who emends the text from *she-mutarin b'chullin* to *she-metammim*, since a *kli cheres* that is a *rishon* does in fact render *chullin* a *sheini*), is that both a *tevul yom* and a *kli cheres* are called *tamei* and both invalidate *terumah*. Anything that is called *tamei*, including a *kikar sheini*, should therefore invalidate *terumah*. This is the *tzad hashaveh*, and it is, as the Gemara notes, a weaker form of argument than the original *kal vachomer* — it is more analogical than deductive, resting on the extraction of a common property rather than on a strict hierarchy of stringency.

## Tzad Chamur Challenge

### Tzad Chamur Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, צַד חָמוּר לָא פָּרֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai** maintained that *teruma* that came into contact with second-degree impurity is pure, as he **would not refute** an inference that is based on two sources due to the fact that both sources have **a stringent aspect,** since each source's stringency is not shared by the other.

The vulnerability of any *tzad hashaveh* is well known: it is always possible to object that the distinctive characteristics of the two source cases — rather than their common denominator — are what explain the *halacha* in each instance. If that is so, then the common denominator cannot carry the *halacha* to the new case. This is the *tzad chamur* challenge. In our case, the distinctive characteristics are: the *kli cheres* transmits *tumah* through airspace (*me'aviro*), which is a unique stringency; and the *tevul yom* either originated from a higher level of *tumah* or belongs to the categorically weightier domain of personal *tumah*. A *kikar sheini* has neither of these features, and one cannot therefore conclude from the *tzad hashaveh* that it invalidates *terumah*.

### Generational Challenge

This *tzad chamur* objection is what later generations raised against the derivation. The Gemara notes that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was apparently not troubled by this challenge — perhaps because raising a *tzad chamur* objection against every *tzad hashaveh* would effectively eliminate all such arguments from the tradition, which he was unwilling to do. But the later generation was not prepared to rest easy with a *tzad hashaveh* that could be challenged, and it was Rabbi Akiva's contribution that saved the *halacha* from ongoing uncertainty.

### Akiva's Pesach Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלִישִׁי לַקּוֹדֶשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה מְנָלַן — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל״. מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דִּנְגַע בְּשֵׁנִי — וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where in the Torah do we** derive that third-degree impurity applies **to sacrificial** food? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And the flesh that touches any impure thing shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 7:19). **Are we not dealing** in the verse with meat **that touches** even an item of **second-**degree impurity? **And the Merciful One states** that it **"shall not be eaten,"** indicating that it assumes third-degree impurity.

Rabbi Akiva's derivation from the *kli cheres pasuk* gave *shlishi biterumah* an explicit scriptural foundation. Once there is a *pasuk*, the *tzad hashaveh* and its attendant vulnerability to *tzad chamur* objection become moot. The *halacha* rests on the *derasha*, not on the logical argument, and the challenge of the later generation is answered. This is a concrete illustration of the principle noted earlier: a *pasuk* is more secure than a *sevara*, because *sevara* can always be countered by a counter-*sevara*, while an explicit scriptural derivation — or one firmly grounded in *drasha* — cannot be similarly dislodged.

## Revi'i BeKodesh

### R. Yosei's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִנַּיִן לִרְבִיעִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ שֶׁפָּסוּל?
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ḥagiga* 3:18) that **Rabbi Yosei said: From where** is it derived with regard **to sacrificial** food with **fourth-**degree ritual impurity **that it is disqualified** although it is not capable of imparting impurity to other items?

The Gemara continues: given that we now know *shlishi biterumah*, how do we derive the parallel rule for *kodesh* — namely, *revi'i bekodesh*, that a fourth-degree derivative of *tumah* invalidates sanctified food? A *braita* records Rabbi Yosei's question: *minayin lerevi'i bekodesh she-pasul?* The answer, he says, is a *kal vachomer*.

### Mechusar Kipurim Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: מָה מְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים, שֶׁמּוּתָּר בַּתְּרוּמָה — פָּסוּל בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ. שְׁלִישִׁי, שֶׁפָּסוּל בַּתְּרוּמָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין הוּא שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה רְבִיעִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **It is** derived through **logical** inference: **Just as one who is lacking atonement,** e.g., a *zav* or leper who immersed at the conclusion of his period of impurity but has not yet brought an offering for his atonement, **who is permitted** to partake **of *teruma*,** nevertheless **disqualifies sacrificial** food if he comes into contact with it, so too, with regard to an item of **third-**degree impurity status, **which disqualifies *teruma*** and is therefore more severe than one who is lacking atonement, **is it not logical that it should render sacrificial** food with which it comes into contact as having **fourth-**degree impurity?

The *kal vachomer* parallels the one used to derive *shlishi biterumah* from *tevul yom*, but operates one level higher. *Mechusar kipurim* refers to a person who has completed the immersion and waited for sunset but whose *tahara* process is not yet complete because he still must bring a *korban* — such as a *zav*, *zavah*, or *yoledet*. After sunset, such a person may eat *terumah*, but remains forbidden from eating *kodashim* until the *korban* is brought the next morning. This is an even more residual *tumah* than that of a *tevul yom*: by *mechusar kipurim* it is barely a *tumah* at all, yet it is enough to bar the person from *kodashim*.

The *kal vachomer* runs: if a *mechusar kipurim*, who is *mutar biterumah* — permitted to eat *terumah* — is nonetheless *pasul bekodesh* — barred from *kodashim* — then *shlishi she-pasul biterumah* — something that is a *shlishi* and has thereby become invalid for *terumah* — surely should be *pasul bekodesh* as well. The parallel to the original *tevul yom* argument is tight: just as the residual *tumah* of *tevul yom* proves that *sheini* of food creates a problem for *terumah*, so the residual *tumah* of *mechusar kipurim* proves that *shlishi* of food creates a problem for *kodesh*, yielding the status of *revi'i bekodesh*.

### Shlishi LKodesh Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָמַדְנוּ שְׁלִישִׁי לַקּוֹדֶשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וּרְבִיעִי מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **And we have** therefore **derived** that **third-**degree impurity applies **to sacrificial** food **from** an explicit verse in **the Torah, and** we have derived that **fourth-**degree impurity applies to sacrificial food **by** means of the above ***a fortiori*** inference.

Before arriving at *revi'i*, however, we must first establish *shlishi lekodesh* — that food which is a *shlishi* is invalid for *kodashim*. This is derived from the verse *ve-habasar asher yiga b'chol tamei lo ye'achel*, which concerns meat of a *korban*. The Torah called food that fell into a *kli cheres* contaminated by a *sheretz* — a *sheini* — by the term *tamei* (as established earlier in the *pasuk* of *kol asher betocho yitma*). Thus when this verse says that meat of a *korban* that touches anything *tamei* — including a *sheini* — may not be eaten, it establishes that if *korban* meat becomes a *shlishi* by touching a *sheini*, it is forbidden. *Shlishi lekodesh* is therefore scripturally grounded.

### Revi'i Derivation

From *shlishi lekodesh* being established by the *pasuk*, we then derive *revi'i* by the *kal vachomer* from *mechusar kipurim* as described: if someone whose *tumah* is so residual that he may eat *terumah* yet cannot eat *kodashim*, then *shlishi* food — which genuinely cannot be eaten as *terumah* — should certainly be able to invalidate *kodashim* it touches, producing a *revi'i bekodesh*.

### R. Yochanan's Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: טַעַם בְּרִיבִּי אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מָה הוּא, שֶׁהֲרֵי תְּשׁוּבָתוֹ בְּצִדּוֹ: אוֹכֶל הַבָּא מֵחֲמַת טְבוּל יוֹם יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁפָּסוּל בִּתְרוּמָה וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה רְבִיעִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **the reasoning** behind **the Distinguished** Rabbi Yosei's *a fortiori* inference, **I do not know what it is, as the response to his** inference **is** right **by its side: Food whose** impurity **came from** contact with **one who immersed that day can prove** that a degree of impurity that disqualifies *teruma* does not necessarily impart fourth-degree impurity to sacrificial food, **as** this food **disqualifies *teruma*** upon contact, **but it does not impart fourth-**degree impurity **upon sacrificial** food.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, objects sharply: *ta'am de-Rabbi Yosei ani yode'a, mahu? She-harei teshuvato betzido* — I understand what Rabbi Yosei is doing, but the refutation is built right into the argument. *Ochel ha-ba machmas tevul yom yochiach* — consider food that has been rendered *pasul* by contact with a *tevul yom*. Such food is *pasul biterumah* — it invalidates *terumah* — yet *eino oseh revi'i bekodesh* — it does not create a *revi'i* for *kodesh*.

This is a striking counter-example. The entire *kal vachomer* rested on analogizing the *shlishi* in food to the *mechusar kipurim* — if a person with only residual *tumah* can bar *kodashim*, then food at the *shlishi* level should certainly do so. But the case of food touched by a *tevul yom* reveals that the analogy between persons and foods in this chain does not hold cleanly: food touched by a *tevul yom* is *pasul biterumah* and yet does not produce a *revi'i bekodesh*. This breaks the very logical bridge on which the *kal vachomer* depends, and the full resolution of this challenge must await further analysis.
