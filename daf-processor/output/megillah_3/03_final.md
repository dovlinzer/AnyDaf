# Megillah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Masechet Overview

### Rabbinic Authority

Before entering the body of the *sugya*, it is worth pausing to orient ourselves to the central concerns of Masechet Megillah as a whole. Later in the *masechet* we will encounter material on *beit knesset*, *tefilla*, and *kriat hatorah* that ranges well beyond the subject of *Megillah* itself. But in terms of the *Megillah* issues proper, two foundational questions animate much of what follows.

The first is the authority to institute a rabbinic holiday. Where does the power to create a new entry in the liturgical calendar come from? This question, though it surfaces most explicitly in connection with Purim, bears equally on Chanukah — a holiday that, notably, has no *masechet* devoted to it. The second question is the inclusion of *Sefer Esther* in *Tanakh*. Esther is a book that does not even mention God's name, and depending on when one dates its composition — whether before or after the *binyan habayit hasheni* began — it may be among the very last *sfarim* to have been written. *Chaggai*, *Zecharya*, and *Malachi* were active at the very beginning of the Second Temple period, so Esther is at the very least among the last books of *Tanakh*. And yet it enters the canon. The *midrash* captures this tension in the image of Esther herself approaching the *chachamim* and saying *kitvuni l'dorot* — write me for generations. The *Gemara* has a formal discussion about whether Esther was *nitna lehikatev*, whether it was given to be written, which may mean whether it was accepted into the *sifrei Tanakh*.

These two questions converge in a striking way already in the first *amud*. The *Gemara* states: *kulu Anshei Knesset HaGedola tikninuhu* — all of these arrangements were established by the *Anshei Knesset HaGedola*. The *Gemara* then shows where each element was hinted at in the *pesukim*. But this raises the deeper question: do we keep Purim because of the authority of the *Megillah* and its *psukim*, or do we keep it because the *Anshei Knesset HaGedola* — the rabbis — looked at those *pesukim* and ultimately determined the practice? The *pesukim* provide hints, but it is rabbinic authority that establishes why we observe the *Megillah* and why Esther is a *sefer Tanakh*. This tension — between scriptural warrant and rabbinic determination — is a theme that will run throughout today's learning and for much of the *masechet*.

### Multiple Purim Days

There is a related phenomenon in this *sugya* that deserves attention in its own right, namely the extraordinary fact that the *mitzvat Megillah* can be fulfilled on multiple different days depending on where one lives. The *Gemara* introduces readings on the 11th, 12th, 13th, 14th, and 15th. Before even getting to the question of why there are so many days, we should note how strange it already is that there are two distinct primary days at all — the 14th and the 15th — such that some Jews celebrate Purim on one day and others on another. This is unlike the rabbinic institution of *yomtov sheni*, where the second day is a doubling of the same day. Here, the 14th and the 15th are fundamentally distinct days for distinct populations, and this has no parallel in the entire liturgical calendar. The whole concept of a central calendar is precisely that it unifies *klal Yisrael* in the simultaneous observance of holidays. That shared time creates religious community. The fact that Purim structurally defies this, with different cities observing on different days, is truly unique. Once that foundational difference has been established, the further expansion to accommodate even more days — *zmanim harbei tiknu lahem chachamim* — is perhaps a bit excessive, but at least one can understand the logic that follows from the already-unusual baseline.

## Walled vs. Unwalled

### Prazim/Kerach Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַכִּים הַמּוּקָּפִים חוֹמָה מִימוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן קוֹרִין בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר וּכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רָבָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״עַל כֵּן הַיְּהוּדִים הַפְּרָזִים הַיּוֹשְׁבִים בְּעָרֵי הַפְּרָזוֹת וְגוֹ׳״. מִדִּפְרָזִים בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר — מוּקָּפִין בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Cities that have been surrounded by a wall since the days of Joshua, son of Nun, read** the Megilla **on the fifteenth** of Adar. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, as they are not stated explicitly in the Megilla? **Rava said:** It is **as the verse states: "Therefore the Jews of the villages, who dwell in the unwalled towns,** make the fourteenth day of the month of Adar a day of gladness and feasting" (Esther 9:19). **From** the fact that **the unwalled towns** celebrate Purim **on the fourteenth,** it may be derived that **the walled cities** celebrate Purim **on the fifteenth.**

The *Gemara* grounds the distinction between different categories of cities in the *pesukim*. The term *prazi* — appearing in *Bamidbar* and *Devarim* in the phrase *levad me'arei haprazi harbei me'od* — designates unwalled settlements, in contrast to *krakhim*, which are walled cities. On a straightforward reading of the *pasuk* in the *Megillah* itself — *al ken haYehudim haprazim hayoshvim be'arei haprazot* — one might read *prazi* simply as "not Shushan," meaning anyone outside of *Shushan haBirah*, the capital city. On that reading, the distinction would merely be between Shushan, which observes the 15th, and everywhere else, which observes the 14th. The *Gemara*, however, reads the distinction as walled versus unwalled, a significantly more developed legal category.

A preliminary difficulty is raised regarding Shushan itself. According to the view that the relevant walled-city status is determined from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun*, Shushan would presumably not be considered *mukaf chomah miyemos Yehoshua bin Nun*, and should therefore observe the 14th like any other unwalled settlement. Yet Shushan observes the 15th. *Rava* — and some attribute this to *kadi*, perhaps an anonymous source or a sage by that name — resolves the difficulty: *shani Shushan ho'il vena'aseh bah nes*. Shushan is exceptional precisely because the miracle of Purim occurred there. It is the paradigm case from which the entire framework derives, and so it retains its 15th observance as a matter of special status, even though by the general rule it would not qualify.

### Yehoshua vs. Achashverosh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה לָא אָמַר כְּתַנָּא דִּידַן — דְּלֵית לֵיהּ ״פְּרָזִי״ ״פְּרָזִי״, אֶלָּא תַּנָּא דִּידַן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה? מַאי טַעְמָא?! דְּהָא אִית לֵיהּ ״פְּרָזִי״ ״פְּרָזִי״! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶלָּא שׁוּשָׁן, דְּעָבְדָא כְּמַאן? לָא כִּפְרָזִים וְלָא כְּמוּקָּפִין! אָמַר רָבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ כְּדִי: שָׁאנֵי שׁוּשָׁן — הוֹאִיל וְנַעֲשָׂה בָּהּ נֵס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Granted** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa did not state** his explanation **in accordance with** the opinion of **the *tanna* of our** mishna **because he did not hold** that a verbal analogy can be established between one verse that employs the word **unwalled** and the other verse that employs the word **unwalled. However, what is the reason** that **the *tanna*** of **our** mishna **did not state** his explanation **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa?** The Gemara expresses astonishment: **What is the reason? Isn't it** because **he holds** that it is derived from the verbal analogy between one usage of the word **unwalled** and the other usage of the word **unwalled?** The Gemara explains: **This is what he said,** i.e., this was the question: According to the *tanna* of our mishna, **in accordance with whom does Shushan observe** Purim? Shushan is **not like the unwalled towns and not like the walled cities,** as residents of Shushan celebrate Purim on the fifteenth, but the city was not surrounded by a wall since the days of Joshua. **Rava said, and some say it unattributed** to any particular Sage: **Shushan is different since the miracle occurred in it** on the fifteenth of Adar, and therefore Purim is celebrated on that day. However, other cities are only considered walled cities and read the Megilla on the fifteenth of Adar if they were walled since the days of Joshua.

The *Gemara* now addresses the internal logic of each position. According to our *Mishnah* — the view that walled-city status is determined from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun* — the phrase *medinah u'medinah* in the *Megillah* must do two pieces of work. First, it distinguishes between cities walled from the time of *Yehoshua bin Nun* (which observe the 15th) and cities walled only from the time of *Achashverosh* (which observe the 14th). These are both walled cities — *medinah u'medinah* — but of different vintages. Second, the phrase *ir v'ir* distinguishes among unwalled settlements, because even among those, Shushan stands apart as an exception, observing the 15th despite not being *mukaf* from the time of *Yehoshua*.

According to the view of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha — who holds that walled-city status is reckoned from the time of *Achashverosh* — the phrase *medinah u'medinah* establishes the basic rule of walled cities from the time of *Achashverosh*, and Shushan's exceptional status explains the distinction within *ir v'ir*. But Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha then presses back: according to our *Mishnah*, does its derivation really work cleanly? Once the *Mishnah* already derives the rule from a *gezeirah shavah* based on the word *prazi prazi* — connecting the *Megillah* to *Devarim* to establish that walled-city status goes back to *yemos Yehoshua bin Nun* — why is *medinah u'medinah* needed at all? You already know the rule. The answer the *Gemara* gives is: *ella drasha hu d'ata* — that phrase is not there to teach the legal rule, but rather to support a *derasha*, as in the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

### Medinah/Ir Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְתַנָּא דִּידַן, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״מְדִינָה וּמְדִינָה וְעִיר וָעִיר״. ״מְדִינָה וּמְדִינָה״ — לְחַלֵּק בֵּין מוּקָּפִין חוֹמָה מִימוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן לְמוּקֶּפֶת חוֹמָה מִימוֹת אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ. ״עִיר וְעִיר״ נָמֵי, לְחַלֵּק בֵּין שׁוּשָׁן לִשְׁאָר עֲיָירוֹת. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, בִּשְׁלָמָא ״מְדִינָה וּמְדִינָה״ — לְחַלֵּק בֵּין שׁוּשָׁן לִשְׁאָר עֲיָירוֹת, אֶלָּא ״עִיר וְעִיר״ לְמַאי אֲתָא? אָמַר לְךָ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: וּלְתַנָּא דִּידַן מִי נִיחָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לֵיהּ ״פְּרָזִי״ ״פְּרָזִי״ — ״מְדִינָה וּמְדִינָה״ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא קְרָא לִדְרָשָׁה הוּא דַּאֲתָא, וְכִדְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כְּרַךְ, וְכׇל הַסָּמוּךְ לוֹ, וְכׇל הַנִּרְאֶה עִמּוֹ — נִידּוֹן כִּכְרַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according** to **the *tanna* of our** mishna, **this is** the meaning of **what is written:** "And these days should be remembered and observed throughout every generation, every family, **every province, and every city"** (Esther 9:28). The phrase **"every province [*medina*]"** is expressed in the verse using repetition, so that it reads literally: Every province and province, and therefore contains a superfluous usage of the word province, is meant **to distinguish between** cities **that were surrounded by a wall since the days of Joshua, son of Nun,** where the Megilla is read on the fifteenth, **and** a city that **was surrounded by a wall since the days of Ahasuerus,** where the Megilla is read on the fourteenth. The phrase **"every city,"** which is similarly expressed through repetition and contains a superfluous usage of the word city, **also** serves **to distinguish between Shushan and other cities,** as Purim is celebrated in Shushan on the fifteenth despite the fact that it was not walled since the time of Joshua. **However, according to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa, granted** that the phrase **"every province"** comes **to distinguish between Shushan and other cities** that were not walled since the days of Ahasuerus; **but what does** the phrase **"every city" come** to teach? **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** could have **said to you: According to the *tanna* of our** mishna, **does it work out well? Since he holds** that it is derived from the verbal analogy between one verse that employs the word **unwalled** and the other verse that employs the word **unwalled, why do I** need the phrase **"every province"? Rather, the verse comes for** a midrashic **exposition, and it comes** to indicate that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the ruling issued by **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. As Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: A** walled **city, and all** settlements **adjacent to it, and all** settlements that can be **seen with it,** i.e., that can be seen from the walled city, are **considered like the** walled **city,** and the Megilla is read there on the fifteenth.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derives from *medinah u'medinah* a different lesson entirely: *krach v'chol hasamuch lo* — a walled city and everything near it, and *v'chol hanireh imo* — and everything that is visible from it. Any settlement that is either physically close to a *krach* or visible from it is treated as part of the *krach* and observes the 15th. This principle stands independently of the legal-historical question of the vintage of the wall.

It is worth pausing to appreciate what this means halachically. The phrase *medinah u'medinah*, which one might read as a simple rhetorical flourish, is made to carry halachic weight in two entirely distinct directions — both establishing a legal distinction between categories of walled cities and establishing the geographic extension of a *krach* to surrounding areas. This is the *Gemara* treating the verses of *Megillah* exactly as it would treat a *pasuk* from the *Torah*.

### City Extensions/Mil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: כְּמֵחַמָּתָן לִטְבֶרְיָא — מִיל. וְלֵימָא מִיל! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְמִיל כַּמָּה הָוֵי — כְּמֵחַמָּתָן לִטְבֶרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Up to what** distance is considered adjacent? **Rabbi Yirmeya said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** who said: The limit is **like** the distance **from** the town of **Ḥamtan to Tiberias,** a ***mil*.** The Gemara asks: **Let him say** simply that the limit is a ***mil*;** why did he have to mention these places? The Gemara answers that the formulation of the answer **teaches us this: How much** distance comprises **the measure** of a ***mil*?** It is **like** the distance **from Ḥamtan to Tiberias.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah — and some attribute this to Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba — provides a concrete measure for the *samuch* extension: *k'meichamtan l'Tveryah mil*. The distance from *Chamtan* to *Tveryah* is a *mil*. A *mil* is approximately one kilometer. Any settlement within a *mil* of a *krach* is treated as part of it and observes the 15th. The *Gemara* asks: why not simply state that the standard is a *mil*? The answer is that the *amora* wants to communicate not only the abstract measure but its concrete embodiment — the distance between these two specific places — so that the standard is vivid and tangible.

In addition to the *samuch* criterion, the *nireh* criterion stands independently. A settlement that is visible from the *krach*, even if it is more than a *mil* away, is also treated as part of the *krach*. This raises practical questions that remain highly relevant. Today, the question of whether outlying neighborhoods of *Yerushalayim* are to be treated as extensions of the original walled city — for the purpose of reading the *Megillah* on the 15th — is actively discussed. In some areas of *Yerushalayim* one cannot see the old city and the distance is greater than a *mil*, yet the construction is continuous. These criteria of *samuch* and *nireh* become the operative framework for working through such questions.

## Darshaning Megillah

### Megillah as Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מַנְצְפַךְ — צוֹפִים אֲמָרוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § Having cited a statement of Rabbi Yirmeya, which some attribute to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, the Gemara cites other statements attributed to these Sages. **Rabbi Yirmeya said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** who said: **The Seers,** i.e., the prophets, were the ones who **said** that the letters ***mem*, *nun*, *tzadi*, *peh*,** and ***kaf*** [***mantzepakh***], have a different form when they appear at the end of a word.

There is something worth pausing on in what the *Gemara* has been doing throughout this section that deserves explicit attention. The *Gemara* has been treating the verses of the *Megillah* with precisely the same hermeneutical approach it would apply to a *pasuk* from the *Torah*. Every word is assumed to be necessary; if a word's apparent legal function is already supplied by another source, it must be teaching something else. A *gezeirah shavah* is drawn between the *Megillah* and *Devarim*. The phrase *medinah u'medinah v'ir v'ir* is parsed syllable by syllable to yield distinct *halachot*.

This is striking. The *gezeirah shavah* as a hermeneutical device traditionally must trace back to *Har Sinai*. How can a *gezeirah shavah* be drawn from a text composed more than a millennium after *Matan Torah*? And more broadly, the *Gemara* will not ordinarily learn *halacha* from *Nakh* — from the prophetic books or the writings. Even if a *pasuk* in *Nakh* illuminates a question of *halacha*, there is always the objection that *halacha* cannot be derived from *Tanakh*. Yet here, everything is derived through these *drashot*, and the derivations proceed with the full confidence and naturalness of Talmudic interpretation of biblical law.

Part of what is happening is that the *Gemara* is not deriving *mitzvot* of the *Torah* from these verses. It is deriving the *mitzvot* of the *Megillah* from the *Megillah* itself. The *mitzvot* in question are *mitzvot d'rabbanan*, and their contours are being worked out through the internal verses of the text that establishes them. Nevertheless, the mode of interpretation is identical to the mode used for biblical law, and *gezeirot shavot* are made between the *Megillah* and *pesukim* in *Devarim* as if the two texts stand on the same plane.

The deeper point, again, connects to the larger theme of the *masechet*. By subjecting the *Megillah* to the full apparatus of Talmudic *drasha* — by treating its words with the same interpretive seriousness as the words of the *Torah* — the *Gemara* is implicitly incorporating the *Megillah* into the canon of authoritative texts. It is doing so not through an explicit declaration but through practice, through the way it handles the text. One might also note the strange circularity: the authority of the *Megillah* as a *sefer Tanakh* is part of what is being established, yet the *Gemara* reasons from its verses as if that authority is already settled. This is not a flaw in the *Gemara*'s reasoning but rather, perhaps, a window into how canonical authority is constructed — not declared once but enacted continuously through the practice of interpretation.

## Innovation Question

### Sofiyot Letters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״אֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת״, שֶׁאֵין נָבִיא רַשַּׁאי לְחַדֵּשׁ דָּבָר מֵעַתָּה? וְעוֹד, הָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מֵם וְסָמֶךְ שֶׁבַּלּוּחוֹת בְּנֵס הָיוּ עוֹמְדִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how can **you understand** it that way? **Isn't it written: "These are the commandments** that the Lord commanded Moses for the children of Israel in Mount Sinai" (Leviticus 27:34), which indicates **that a prophet is not permitted to initiate** or change **any matter** of *halakha* **from now on?** Consequently, how could the prophets establish new forms for the letters? **And furthermore, didn't Rav Ḥisda say:** The letters ***mem* and *samekh* in the tablets** of the covenant given at Sinai **stood by way of a miracle?**

The *Gemara* now introduces another statement attributed to Rabbi Yirmiyah — and some say to Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba. The claim is: *mantzpach tzofim amrum* — the *sofiyot* letters, the five letters *mem*, *nun*, *tzadi*, *pei*, and *kaf*, which take a different written form at the end of a word than in the middle, were established by the *nevi'im*. The word *tzofim* is embedded within the acronym *mantzpach* itself, and the term means "the seers," i.e., the prophets. The original statement thus asserts that this scribal distinction — between the open and closed forms of these five letters — is a prophetic innovation.

The *Gemara* immediately pushes back. The verse states *eleh hamitzvot* — these are the commandments that God gave at Sinai — from which the *Gemara* derives: *she'ein navi rashai l'chadesh davar mei'atah*. A prophet has no authority to introduce a new practice after the *Torah* has been given. How then could the prophets have established a new way of writing the letters?

A secondary proof follows from Rav Chisda's statement: *mem v'samach she-baluchot b'nes hayu omdim*. The *mem sofit* and *samach* in the *luchot* stood by a miracle — if the stone tablets were carved all the way through, the middle section of a closed *mem* or *samach* has nothing holding it in place. This proves that the closed form of these letters already existed at the time the *luchot* were given, well before any prophets came on the scene. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* note that perhaps what the prophets introduced was the open form used in the middle of a word, while the closed form always existed. But however one works out the details, the larger point is clear: the closed letter forms already existed at Sinai.

### No Prophetic Innovation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, מִהְוָה הֲווֹ, וְלָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי הֵי בְּאֶמְצַע תֵּיבָה וְהֵי בְּסוֹף תֵּיבָה, וַאֲתוֹ צוֹפִים וְתַקִּינוּ פְּתוּחִין בְּאֶמְצַע תֵּיבָה וּסְתוּמִין בְּסוֹף תֵּיבָה. סוֹף סוֹף ״אֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת״, שֶׁאֵין נָבִיא עָתִיד לְחַדֵּשׁ דָּבָר מֵעַתָּה! אֶלָּא שְׁכָחוּם וְחָזְרוּ וְיִסְּדוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** two forms of these letters **did exist** at that time, **but** the people **did not know which** one of them was to be used **in the middle of the word and which at the end of the word, and the Seers came and established** that **the open** forms are to used be **in the middle of the word and the closed** forms **at the end of the word.** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately,** however, doesn't the phrase **"these are the commandments"** (Leviticus 27:34) indicate **that a prophet is not permitted to initiate any matter** of *halakha* **from now on? Rather,** it may be suggested that the final letters already existed at the time of the giving of the Torah, but over the course of time the people **forgot them,** and the prophets **then** came and **reestablished them.**

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction as follows: *in hachi hava* — granted, the distinction between the two forms existed from the beginning. But perhaps the precise rule of which form appears where — the open form in the middle of a word, the closed form at the end — was not consistently maintained. *V'lo hava yada'ei hai b'emtza teiva v'hai b'sof teiva* — they did not know, or perhaps were not precise about, which form belonged in the middle of a word and which at the end. The prophets came along and *tiknu* — they established a systematic rule: *psuchin b'emtza teiva v'stumin b'sof teiva*, the open forms in the middle, the closed forms at the end.

But this resolution is itself then rejected. Even merely establishing the systematic application of a rule that already existed is, apparently, a form of *chiddush* that a prophet is not permitted to introduce. The *Gemara* insists: *eleh hamitzvot she'ein navi rashai l'chadesh davar me'atah* — this prohibition is absolute, not graduated. The final resolution is therefore: *ella sheshachachum v'chazru v'yasdum*. The rule was always there, was forgotten, and the prophets simply restored it. They did not innovate; they recovered.

It is historically worth noting — and this point was raised in the course of discussion — that ancient Semitic inscriptions do show imprecision in the use of final letter forms. There are instances where what we would call a *mem sofit* appears in the middle of a word, and the *pasuk* in *Yeshaya* with *l'marbe hamisrah* — which contains a *mem sofit* in a medial position — is the most famous biblical example of this phenomenon. So the *Gemara*'s account of a period of imprecision that was later regularized is not without historical plausibility.

The *Gemara*'s position is that even this modest step of systematization cannot be credited as a prophetic innovation. The *Yerushalmi*, by contrast, has no difficulty with such claims. In fact, the *Yerushalmi* does not even share the concern about the closed *mem* and *samach* in the *luchot*, because it identifies the *ayin* as the letter that stood by a miracle — since in the ancient Hebrew script (*Ashurit* was adopted later), the *ayin* was written as a circle, making it the letter with a fully enclosed interior. The *Yerushalmi* has no problem whatsoever with the assertion that the *Ashurit* script itself was adopted at some historical point rather than always having been in use.

Now, the contrast with everything that preceded this discussion is sharp and worth dwelling on. The entire framework of Purim and *Megillah* — the establishment of a new holiday, the inclusion of a new *sefer* in *Tanakh*, the derivation of *halachot* from its verses — is itself a development that occurs in the later period of the *nevi'im* or even afterward. These are, by any reckoning, far more significant innovations than the regularization of which form of a letter appears at the end of a word. Yet the *stam* of the *Gemara* refuses to call even the scribal systematization an innovation, while apparently accepting, at least implicitly, the weight and validity of the entire enterprise of *Megillah*.

The key observation is that the statement of Rabbi Yirmiyah — that the *sofiyot* forms were established by the prophets — is an *amora'ic* *memra*. It is the *stam* of the *Gemara*, the anonymous voice of the *Gemara*, that refuses to accept that the prophets could have been innovating, and insists on the formula of forgetting and restoration. The same *stam* voice is the one that, throughout the preceding analysis, has been treating *Megillah* as authoritative text and deriving *halachot* from its verses with full confidence. These are not competing voices from different sources stitched together; they come from the same editorial layer. The *stam* is simultaneously asserting that nothing can be innovated and demonstrating that everything it is doing with *Megillah* is therefore not innovation either. The extreme insistence that even something as minor as letter placement cannot be a *chiddush* functions rhetorically to reinforce the claim that the entire edifice of *Megillah* — its *halachot*, its canonical status, its *mitzvot* — is equally not a *chiddush*. It is all, in the *stam*'s presentation, a recovery and transmission of what always was.

### Targumim Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: תַּרְגּוּם שֶׁל תּוֹרָה — אוּנְקְלוֹס הַגֵּר אֲמָרוֹ מִפִּי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. תַּרְגּוּם שֶׁל נְבִיאִים — יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל אֲמָרוֹ מִפִּי חַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי, וְנִזְדַּעְזְעָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה עַל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: מִי הוּא זֶה שֶׁגִּילָּה סְתָרַיי לִבְנֵי אָדָם?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another ruling of Rabbi Yirmeya or Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba. **Rabbi Yirmeya said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** who said: **The** Aramaic **translation of the Torah** used in the synagogues **was composed by Onkelos the convert based on** the teachings of **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. The** Aramaic **translation of the Prophets was composed by Yonatan ben Uzziel based on** a tradition going back to the last prophets, **Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi.** The Gemara relates that when Yonatan ben Uzziel wrote his translation, **Eretz Yisrael quaked** over an area of **four hundred parasangs [*parsa*] by four hundred parasangs,** and **a Divine Voice emerged and said: Who is this who has revealed My secrets to mankind?**

Continuing in the same vein — and from the same *marei d'amra*, Rabbi Yirmiyah, and some say Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba — the *Gemara* now states: *Targum shel Torah Onkelos HaGer amaro mipi Rabbi Eliezer v'Rabbi Yehoshua*. The Aramaic translation of the *Torah* was composed by Onkelos the proselyte, and it was transmitted from the teachings of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, who are also the primary teachers of Rabbi Akiva. The *Targum* is not a personal creation of Onkelos; it is the Aramaic rendering of the *Torah* as seen through the lens of the rabbinic tradition and the rulings of these great *tanna'im*.

Then: *Targum shel Nevi'im Yonatan ben Uziel amaro mipi Chaggai Zecharya u'Malachi*. The *Targum* on the prophetic books was composed by Yonatan ben Uziel, who was the greatest of Hillel's students — the *Gemara* in Sukkah describes how when he would study *Torah*, birds passing overhead would be consumed by fire because of the heavenly angels that surrounded him. His *Targum* he received from Chaggai, Zecharya, and Malachi themselves.

The contrast between these two attributions is significant. The interpretation of *Torah* flows through the rabbinic chain — Onkelos receives it from Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. The interpretation of *Nevi'im* flows through the prophetic chain — Yonatan receives it from the last of the prophets. This maps onto the distinction the *Gemara* has been developing: *Torah* and its binding legal meaning come through the rabbinic tradition; the meaning of *Nakh*, which is not the domain of *halacha* in the same way, can be transmitted through the prophetic tradition. In both cases, crucially, nothing is being innovated. The interpretation is being transmitted, received, passed down.

A textual note is in order. What appears in printed editions of *Mikraot Gedolot* under the label *Targum Yonatan ben Uziel* on the *Torah* is not, in the judgment of scholars, what the *Gemara* here is referring to. The *Gemara* appears to indicate that Yonatan only composed a *Targum* on *Nevi'im*, not on *Torah*. What we call *Targum Yonatan* on the *Torah* is more accurately called *Targum Yerushalmi*; it derives from *Eretz Yisrael* and is written primarily in Palestinian Aramaic, though with some Babylonian Aramaic strands, and is generally dated to around the eighth century. One theory for the confusion is that the text was abbreviated *tav yud*, and later readers, knowing the *Gemara* refers to *Targum Yonatan*, assumed this was it. Another indication of its late dating: the *Targum Yerushalmi* identifies one of *Yishmael*'s wives by a name that corresponds to one of Muhammad's wives — an anachronism that places it well after the rise of Islam. What the *Gemara* here describes as *Targum Yonatan ben Uziel* is thus not a text we currently possess.

## Interpretation Authority

### Yonatan Revelation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: אֲנִי הוּא שֶׁגִּלִּיתִי סְתָרֶיךָ לִבְנֵי אָדָם, גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ שֶׁלֹּא לִכְבוֹדִי עָשִׂיתִי, וְלֹא לִכְבוֹד בֵּית אַבָּא, אֶלָּא לִכְבוֹדְךָ עָשִׂיתִי, שֶׁלֹּא יִרְבּוּ מַחֲלוֹקֹת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. וְעוֹד בִּיקֵּשׁ לְגַלּוֹת תַּרְגּוּם שֶׁל כְּתוּבִים, יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: דַּיֶּיךָּ! מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִית בֵּיהּ קֵץ מָשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Yonatan ben Uzziel stood up on his feet and said: I am the one who has revealed Your secrets to mankind** through my translation. However, **it is revealed and known to You that I did this not for my** own **honor, and not for the honor of the house of** my **father, but rather** it was **for Your honor** that **I did this, so that discord not increase among the Jewish people.** In the absence of an accepted translation, people will disagree about the meaning of obscure verses, but with a translation, the meaning will be clear. **And** Yonatan ben Uzziel **also sought to reveal a translation of the Writings,** but **a Divine Voice emerged and said to him: It is enough for you** that you translated the Prophets. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that he was denied permission to translate the Writings? **Because it has in it** a revelation of **the end,** when the **Messiah** will arrive. The end is foretold in a cryptic manner in the book of Daniel, and were the book of Daniel translated, the end would become manifestly revealed to all.

When Yonatan ben Uziel gave over his *Targum* on *Nevi'im*, the land of Israel shook — *arba me'os parsa al arba me'os parsa*, four hundred *parsa* by four hundred *parsa*, which presumably corresponds to the dimensions of the land. A *bat kol* — a heavenly voice — emerged and declared: *mi hu ze she'gilah sitrai livnei adam* — who is this who has revealed My secrets to human beings? You have told them the true meaning of these prophecies.

Yonatan ben Uziel stood and replied: *ani hu she'gilisi sitrecha livnei adam*. I am the one who revealed Your hidden secrets to human beings. *Galui ve'yadua lefanecha shelo lichvodi asiti v'lo lichvod beit abba ela lichvodecha asiti shelo yirbu machloket be-Yisrael*. It is known before You that I did not do this for my honor or for the honor of my father's house, but for Your sake, so that disagreement should not multiply in Israel.

The framing here is important. The act of revelation — making the meaning of cryptic prophecies accessible — is presented not as intellectual achievement but as a religious obligation performed for the sake of preventing *machloket*. The notion of *riboy machloket* as something to be avoided is deeply embedded in the rabbinic conception of *Torah she-be-al peh*: once there were too many students who had not fully served their teachers, *nitrabta machloket be-Yisrael*. Here, Yonatan applies the same concern to the prophetic tradition. Cryptic, unexplained prophecy generates competing interpretations; authoritative *Targum* forecloses some of those competing possibilities. At the same time, one can observe the tension: authoritative interpretation limits some forms of *machloket* but by establishing a canonical reading, it also opens the door to debate about whether that interpretation is correct. Transmission resolves *machloket* in a way that interpretation alone cannot.

Yonatan then sought to reveal the *Targum* on *Ketuvim* as well. A *bat kol* emerged again and told him: *dayyekha* — enough. What is the reason the line is drawn at *Ketuvim*? *Mishum de-it beih ketz Mashiach* — because *Ketuvim* contains hints about the end of days, particularly *Sefer Daniel*, whose eschatological visions and cryptic verses about the ultimate timeline have drawn intense interest across the generations. Those secrets cannot be publicly decoded. There must be a limit.

### Targum/Peshat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְגּוּם שֶׁל תּוֹרָה, אוּנְקְלוֹס הַגֵּר אֲמָרוֹ? וְהָא אָמַר רַב אִיקָא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרְאוּ בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת הָאֱלֹהִים מְפוֹרָשׁ וְשׂוֹם שֶׂכֶל וַיָּבִינוּ בַּמִּקְרָא״. ״וַיִּקְרְאוּ בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת הָאֱלֹהִים״ — זֶה מִקְרָא; ״מְפוֹרָשׁ״ — זֶה תַּרְגּוּם; ״וְשׂוֹם שֶׂכֶל״ — אֵלּוּ הַפְּסוּקִין; ״וַיָּבִינוּ בַּמִּקְרָא״ — אֵלּוּ פִּיסְקֵי טְעָמִים, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ — אֵלּוּ הַמָּסוֹרֹת! — שְׁכָחוּם וְחָזְרוּ וְיִסְּדוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Was the translation of the Torah** really **composed by Onkelos the convert? Didn't Rav Ika bar Avin say** that **Rav Ḥananel said** that **Rav said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written** with respect to the days of Ezra: **"And they read in the book, the Torah of God, distinctly; and they gave the sense, and they caused them to understand the reading"** (Nehemiah 8:8)? The verse should be understood as follows: **"And they read in the book, the Torah of God," this is the** scriptural **text; "distinctly," this is the translation,** indicating that they immediately translated the text into Aramaic, as was customary during public Torah readings. **"And they gave the sense," these are** the divisions of the text into separate **verses. "And they caused them to understand the reading," these are the cantillation notes,** through which the meaning of the text is further clarified. **And some say** that **these are the** Masoretic **traditions** with regard to the manner in which each word is to be written. This indicates that the Aramaic translation already existed at the beginning of the Second Temple period, well before the time of Onkelos. The Gemara answers: **The** ancient Aramaic translation **was forgotten and then** Onkelos came and **reestablished it.**

The *Gemara* then presses the question: the *Targum* of the *Torah* was also attributed to Yonatan ben Uziel — or rather to Onkelos — and was received from great *tanna'im*. How can that be, given that the *Gemara* elsewhere attributes the *Targum* tradition back much further? The verse in *Nechemia* — which *Rashi* quotes as from *Sefer Ezra* (since *Ezra* and *Nechemia* were originally a single book, later divided by the Christian tradition) — states: *Vayikre'u be-sefer Torat ha-Elokim meforash v'som sekhel va-yavinu ba-mikra*. The *Gemara* reads this verse as a four-part description of what was done at the great public reading in the time of Ezra. *Vayikre'u be-sefer Torat ha-Elokim* — they read the verses; *meforash* — this is the *Targum*, which explicates and translates; *v'som sekhel* — these are the divisions between *pesukim*, knowing where one verse ends and the next begins; *va-yavinu ba-mikra* — this refers to the *piskei te'amim*, the cantillation notes that further structure the meaning within each verse. Some say *va-yavinu ba-mikra* refers instead to the *Mesora*, the precise scribal tradition of *malei* and *chaser* spellings and related matters.

The implication is that the *Targum* tradition goes back to Ezra's time, long before Onkelos. The resolution is once again: *shachechum v'chazru v'yasdum* — it was forgotten and then re-established. Onkelos did not create; he recovered. This is the same resolution that was applied to the *sofiyot* letters, and it reflects a consistent principle: *kol mah she'atid talmid vatik lehitchadesh kvar ne'emar leMoshe miSinai*. Whatever a sharp student will eventually articulate was already said at *Har Sinai*. The authority of the rabbinic interpretation of *Torah* — whether in the form of *Targum* or in the form of *halacha* — is not that it innovates, but that it faithfully recovers and transmits what was always the meaning.

When the *Gemara* points out that the *Targum* of the *Torah* did not cause the world to tremble, while the *Targum* of *Nevi'im* did, it explains: *d'Oraita mefarshem milsa* — the *Torah*'s verses are more explicit; the translation is largely transparent and does not reveal something that was sealed beforehand. The prophetic books, by contrast, contain passages that are both clear and opaque — *ika mili d'mifarshei v'ika mili d'mistammei* — and the *Targum* on *Nevi'im* genuinely unlocks things that were previously inaccessible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא דְּלָא אִזְדַּעְזְעָה וְאַדִּנְבִיאֵי אִזְדַּעְזְעָה? דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — מִיפָּרְשָׁא מִלְּתָא, דִּנְבִיאֵי — אִיכָּא מִילֵּי דְּמִיפָּרְשָׁן וְאִיכָּא מִילֵּי דִּמְסַתְּמָן, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִגְדַּל הַמִּסְפֵּד בִּירוּשָׁלִַם כְּמִסְפַּד הֲדַדְרִימּוֹן בְּבִקְעַת מְגִידּוֹן״, וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אִלְמָלֵא תַּרְגּוּמָא דְּהַאי קְרָא — לָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי קָאָמַר: בְּיוֹמָא הַהוּא יִסְגֵּי מִסְפְּדָא בִּירוּשְׁלֶים כְּמִסְפְּדָא דְּאַחְאָב בַּר עָמְרִי דִּקְטַל יָתֵיהּ הֲדַדְרִימּוֹן בֶּן טַבְרִימּוֹן בְּרָמוֹת גִּלְעָד וּכְמִסְפְּדָא דְּיֹאשִׁיָּה בַּר אָמוֹן דִּקְטַל יָתֵיהּ פַּרְעֹה חֲגִירָא בְּבִקְעַת מְגִידּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about the translation of Prophets? Why is it that when Onkelos revealed the translation **of the Torah,** Eretz Yisrael **did not quake, and** when he revealed the translation **of the Prophets, it quaked?** The Gemara explains: **The** meaning of **matters** discussed **in the Torah is clear,** and therefore its Aramaic translation did not reveal the meaning of passages that had not been understood previously. Conversely, in **the Prophets, there are matters that are clear and there are matters that are obscure,** and the Aramaic translation revealed the meaning of obscure passages. The Gemara cites an example of an obscure verse that is clarified by the Aramaic translation: **As it is written: "On that day shall there be a great mourning in Jerusalem, like the mourning of Hadadrimmon in the valley of Megiddon"** (Zechariah 12:11). **And** with regard to that verse, **Rav Yosef said: Were it not for the** Aramaic **translation of this verse, we would not have known what it is saying,** as the Bible does not mention any incident involving Hadadrimmon in the valley of Megiddon. The Aramaic translation reads as follows: **On that day, the mourning in Jerusalem will be as great as the mourning for Ahab, son of Omri, who was slain by Hadadrimmon, son of Tavrimon, in Ramoth-Gilead, and like the mourning for Josiah, son of Amon, who was slain by Pharaoh the lame in the valley of Megiddon.** The translation clarifies that the verse is referring to two separate incidents of mourning, and thereby clarifies the meaning of this verse.

The example the *Gemara* gives is the verse from *Zecharya*: *bayom hahu yigdal hamisped biYerushalayim k'misped Hadadrimmon bevikat Megiddo*. The difficulty is that there is no known eulogy for Hadadrimmon in the valley of Megiddo. Rav Yosef says: *ilmalei Targuma d'hai kra lo yadina mai ka'amar* — were it not for the *Targum* of this verse, I would not know what it means. The *Targum* clarifies: on that day the wailing will increase in Jerusalem, like the mourning for Achav bar Omri who was killed by Hadadrimmon ben Tabrimmon at Ramot Gilead, and like the mourning for Yoshiyahu bar Amon who was killed by Par'oh chagira in the valley of Megiddo. The verse is not describing a single event but conflating two eulogies — the wailing over Achav's death at Ramot Gilead, and the wailing over Yoshiyahu's death at Megiddo — and the *Targum* is what makes this intelligible.

The *Gemara*'s choice of this as its illustration is interesting. This is not an exceptionally obscure prophecy; one can imagine working it out independently. One possible reason the *Gemara* uses this example: by showing that even a relatively decipherable verse required the *Targum* for its clarification, the *Gemara* underscores how much more transparent the *Targum* of the *Torah* must be by contrast. If this counts as a "revelation of secrets" that caused the earth to tremble, then the *Targum* of *Torah* — which is *peshat* and which explicates what the verse itself says plainly — is even less of a *chiddush*. The rabbinic interpretation of *Torah* is, in this framing, simply a making-explicit of what is already present in the text. Which, once again, reinforces the message: nothing is being innovated. Everything the rabbis do with *Torah* — the *drashot*, the *targum*, the *halachot* derived from *Megillah* — is all continuous with, and a recovery of, what was always there.

### Daniel/Nevi'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְרָאִיתִי אֲנִי דָנִיֵּאל לְבַדִּי אֶת הַמַּרְאָה וְהָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר הָיוּ עִמִּי לֹא רָאוּ אֶת הַמַּרְאָה אֲבָל חֲרָדָה גְדוֹלָה נָפְלָה עֲלֵיהֶם וַיִּבְרְחוּ בְּהֵחָבֵא״. מַאן נִינְהוּ אֲנָשִׁים? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: זֶה חַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי. אִינְהוּ עֲדִיפִי מִינֵּיהּ, וְאִיהוּ עֲדִיף מִינַּיְיהוּ. אִינְהוּ עֲדִיפִי מִינֵּיהּ — דְּאִינְהוּ נְבִיאֵי וְאִיהוּ לָאו נָבִיא. אִיהוּ עֲדִיף מִינַּיְיהוּ — דְּאִיהוּ חֲזָא וְאִינְהוּ לָא חֲזוֹ. וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּלָא חֲזוֹ, מַאי טַעְמָא אִיבְּעִיתוּ? אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִינְהוּ לָא חֲזוֹ, מַזָּלַיְיהוּ חֲזוֹ. אָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַאי מַאן דְּמִיבְּעִית, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיהוּ לָא חָזֵי, מַזָּלֵיהּ חָזֵי. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לִיקְרֵי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. וְאִי קָאֵים בִּמְקוֹם הַטִּנּוֹפֶת — לִינְשׁוֹף מִדּוּכְתֵּיהּ אַרְבַּע גַּרְמִידֵי. וְאִי לָא — לֵימָא הָכִי: ״עִיזָּא דְּבֵי טַבָּחֵי שַׁמִּינָא מִינַּאי״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara introduces another statement from the same line of tradition. The verse states: **"And I, Daniel, alone saw the vision, for the men who were with me did not see the vision; but a great trembling fell upon them, so that they fled to hide themselves"** (Daniel 10:7). **Who were these men?** The term "men" in the Bible indicates important people; who were they? **Rabbi Yirmeya said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** who said: **These are** the prophets **Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi.** In certain ways **they,** the prophets, **were greater than him,** Daniel, and in certain ways **he,** Daniel, **was greater than them. They were greater than him, as they were prophets and he was not a prophet.** Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi were sent to convey the word of God to the Jewish people, while Daniel was not sent to reveal his visions to others. In another way, however, **he was greater than them, as he saw** this vision, **and they did not see** this vision, indicating that his ability to perceive obscure and cryptic visions was greater than theirs. The Gemara asks: **Since they did not see** the vision, **what is the reason that they were frightened?** The Gemara answers: **Even though they did not see** the vision, **their guardian angels saw** it, and therefore they sensed that there was something fearful there and they fled. **Ravina said: Learn from** this incident that with regard to **one who is frightened** for no apparent reason, **although he does not see** anything menacing, **his guardian angel sees** it, and therefore he should take steps in order to escape the danger. The Gemara asks: **What is his remedy? He should recite *Shema*,** which will afford him protection. **And if he is standing in a place of filth,** where it is prohibited to recite verses from the Torah, **he should distance himself four cubits from his** current **location** in order to escape the danger. **And if** he is **not** able to do so, **let him say the following** incantation: **The goat of the slaughterhouse is fatter than I am,** and if a calamity must fall upon something, it should fall upon it.

A further statement attributed to Rabbi Yirmiyah — and some say to Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba — addresses a verse in *Daniel*: *ve-ra'iti ani Daniyel levaddi et ha-mareh, ve-ha-anashim asher hayu immi lo ra'u et ha-mareh, aval haradah gedolah naflah aleihem va-yivrechu be-hahbe* — I, Daniel, alone saw the vision; the people who were with me did not see it, but a great trembling fell upon them, and they fled into hiding. The question is: who were these men who were with Daniel and did not see the vision?

The answer is: *zeh Chaggai Zecharya u'Malachi* — these were Chaggai, Zecharya, and Malachi, great *nevi'im* of that era. The *Gemara* then notes the reciprocal relationship: *inhu adifei minei de-inhu nevi'ei ve-ihu lo navi* — they were greater than Daniel in one dimension, because they were *nevi'im* and he was not a *navi*. But *ihu adif minhu de-ihu haza ve-inhu lo hazu* — he was greater than them in another, because he saw visions that they could not see.

The first part of this is remarkable and requires clarification. Daniel has entire books of visions — how can he not be called a *navi*? *Rashi* explains: *de-inhu nevi'ei shenitnab'u le-Yisrael bi-shlihuto shel Makom ve-hu lo nishtalah le-Yisrael be-shum nevu'ah* — they prophesied to Israel as messengers of God, while he was never sent to Israel with any prophetic message. This reflects a fundamental principle in the rabbinic understanding of prophecy. A *navi* is not simply someone who has divine visions. A *navi* is someone commissioned to deliver God's message to the people — *Aharon ahikha yihyeh nevi'ekha*, as the *pasuk* says. *Navi* is related to *niv sefatayim*, speech of the lips. Daniel had visions, but those visions were not messages sent to *Am Yisrael* through him as a divine messenger. He was a visionary, not a prophet in the technical sense.

This explains the strange scene: the greatest prophets standing with Daniel could not see what he saw, yet they — not he — were the *nevi'im*. They felt the terror nonetheless: *mazleyhu hazu* — their *mazel* perceived the vision even if they themselves did not. From this *Ravina* derives a practical application: if a person is suddenly overcome by fear for no apparent reason — *hai man de-miv'it*, someone who becomes frightened — *mazleh hazi*, their spiritual intuition is perceiving something even if they cannot see it directly. Some spiritual force may be present. The remedy: recite *Kri'at Shema*. If one is in an unclean location where *Kri'at Shema* cannot be said, jump four *amot* from one's place, to exit whatever zone the unseen presence inhabits. If that is not possible, say the phrase: *iza d'bei tapachei shmeina minai* — the goats of the slaughterhouse are fatter than me, suggesting to any harmful force that there are more suitable targets elsewhere.

## Megillah Mitzvah Weight

### Suspension Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ ״מְדִינָה וּמְדִינָה וְעִיר וָעִיר״ — לִדְרָשָׁה, ״מִשְׁפָּחָה וּמִשְׁפָּחָה״ לְמַאי אֲתָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: לְהָבִיא מִשְׁפְּחוֹת כְּהוּנָּה וּלְוִיָּה, שֶׁמְּבַטְּלִין עֲבוֹדָתָן וּבָאִים לִשְׁמוֹעַ מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כֹּהֲנִים בַּעֲבוֹדָתָן וּלְוִיִּם בְּדוּכָנָן וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַעֲמָדָן — כּוּלָּן מְבַטְּלִין עֲבוֹדָתָן וּבָאִים לִשְׁמוֹעַ מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כֹּהֲנִים בַּעֲבוֹדָתָן, וּלְוִיִּם בְּדוּכָנָן, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַעֲמָדָן — כּוּלָּן מְבַטְּלִין עֲבוֹדָתָן וּבָאִים לִשְׁמוֹעַ מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § After this digression, the Gemara returns to the exposition of a verse cited above. **Now** that **you have said** that the phrases **"every province" and "every city"** appear **for** the purposes of midrashic **exposition, for what** exposition do the words **"every family"** appear in that same verse (Esther 9:28)? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** These words come **to include the priestly and Levitical families,** and indicate **that they cancel their service** in the Temple **and come to hear the reading of the Megilla.** **As Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The priests at their** Temple **service, the Levites on their platform** in the Temple, where they sung the daily psalm, **and the Israelites at their watches,** i.e., the group of Israelites, corresponding to the priestly watches, who would come to Jerusalem and gather in other locations as representatives of the entire nation to observe or pray for the success of the Temple service, **all cancel their service and come to hear the reading of the Megilla.** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **The priests at their service, the Levites on the platform, and the Israelites at their watches, all cancel their service and come to hear the reading of the Megilla.**

The *Gemara* returns to the topic of the *Megillah*'s legal weight and what obligations must yield to it. The phrase *mishpachah u'mishpachah* in the *Megillah* is taken by Rabbi Yose bar Chanina to extend the obligation across all families — including, crucially, the priestly and Levitical families. *L'havi mishpachot kehunah u'leviyah she'mvatlin avodatan u'va'im lishmo'a mikra megillah* — the families of *kohanim* and *levi'im* must leave their service in the *Beit HaMikdash* and come to hear the *Megillah*.

This is then amplified by a *baraita* and a statement of Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav: *Kohanim be'avodatam Levi'im b'duchanam Yisrael b'ma'amadam kulam mevatlin avodatan u'va'im lishmo'a mikra megillah* — those doing the *avodah*, those on the *duchan*, and those standing at the *ma'amad*, all of them abandon their posts and come to hear the *Megillah*.

*Tosafot* raises the obvious question: why would there ever need to be a *bitul avodah*? Could the *kohanim* not simply bring the *tamid*, complete the *avodah*, and then hear the *Megillah* privately? *Tosafot* answers: *detov l'ikre'o im hatzibbur mishum d'havi tfei pirsumei nisa* — it is better to read it together with the community because *pirsumei nisa*, the publicizing of the miracle, is greater when done collectively. Since the preferred time to read is *shacharis*, and the *tamid* was brought in the morning, there is a practical conflict; to catch the morning communal reading, the *tamid* must be delayed. The point of the *Gemara*, however, is not primarily logistical — it is establishing the enormous weight given to *mikra megillah*, treating it as an obligation weighty enough to displace *avodah*.

One should also note the striking historical dimension of this *halacha*. During the period of the Second Temple, Purim might have seemed like a *galut* holiday, a commemoration of troubles that had passed and were no longer directly relevant. The fact that *avodah* — the very center of Temple-era religious life — yields to *Megillah* reading is thus a pointed statement about the enduring weight of the *mitzvah* regardless of historical context.

### Talmud Torah Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן סָמְכוּ שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי שֶׁמְּבַטְּלִין תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה וּבָאִים לִשְׁמוֹעַ מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵעֲבוֹדָה: וּמָה עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁהִיא חֲמוּרָה — מְבַטְּלִינַן, תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. וַעֲבוֹדָה חֲמוּרָה מִתַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בִּהְיוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּירִיחוֹ וַיִּשָּׂא עֵינָיו וַיַּרְא וְהִנֵּה אִישׁ עוֹמֵד לְנֶגְדּוֹ [וְגוֹ׳]״. וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיִּתֵּן שָׁלוֹם לַחֲבֵירוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה, חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא שֵׁד הוּא! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״כִּי אֲנִי שַׂר צְבָא ה׳״. וְדִלְמָא מְשַׁקְּרִי? גְּמִירִי דְּלָא מַפְּקִי שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם לְבַטָּלָה. אָמַר לוֹ: אֶמֶשׁ בִּטַּלְתֶּם תָּמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, וְעַכְשָׁיו בִּטַּלְתֶּם תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה. אָמַר לוֹ: עַל אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן בָּאתָ? אָמַר לוֹ: ״עַתָּה בָאתִי״, מִיָּד: ״וַיָּלֶן יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּלַּיְלָה הַהוּא בְּתוֹךְ הָעֵמֶק״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלָּן בְּעוּמְקָהּ שֶׁל הֲלָכָה. וְאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אוּנְיָא: גָּדוֹל תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה יוֹתֵר מֵהַקְרָבַת תְּמִידִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַתָּה בָאתִי״!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of **the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **relied** upon the *halakha* stated **here** and determined **that one cancels his Torah study and comes to hear the reading of the Megilla.** They derived this principle by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from the** Temple **service: Just as** one who is engaged in performing **service** in the Temple, **which is** very **important, cancels** his service in order to hear the Megilla, is it **not all the more so** obvious that one who is engaged in **Torah study** cancels his study to hear the Megilla? The Gemara asks: **Is the** Temple **service more important than Torah study? Isn't it written: "And it came to pass when Joshua was by Jericho that he lifted up his eyes and looked, and behold, a man stood over against him** with his sword drawn in his hand. And Joshua went over to him and said to him: Are you for us, or for our adversaries? And he said, No, but I am captain of the host of the Lord, I have come now. And Joshua fell on his face to the earth, **and bowed down"** (Joshua 5:13–14). The Gemara first seeks to clarify the incident described in the verse. **How did** Joshua **do this,** i.e., how could he bow to a figure he did not recognize? **Didn't Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say: It is prohibited for a person to greet his fellow at night** if he does not recognize him, as **we are concerned that perhaps it is a demon?** How did Joshua know that it was not a demon? The Gemara answers: **There it was different, as** the visitor **said to him: But I am captain of the host of the Lord.** The Gemara asks: **Perhaps** this was a demon **and he lied?** The Gemara answers: It **is learned** as a tradition that demons **do not utter the name of Heaven for naught,** and therefore since the visitor had mentioned the name of God, Joshua was certain that this was indeed an angel. As for the angel's mission, the Gemara explains that the angel **said to** Joshua: **Yesterday,** i.e., during the afternoon, **you neglected the afternoon daily offering** due to the impending battle, **and now,** at night, **you have neglected Torah study,** and I have come to rebuke you. Joshua **said to him: For which of these** sins **have you come? He said to him: I have come now,** indicating that neglecting Torah study is more severe than neglecting to sacrifice the daily offering. Joshua **immediately** determined to rectify the matter, as the verses states: **"And Joshua lodged that night"** (Joshua 8:9) **"in the midst of the valley** [***ha'emek***]" (Joshua 8:13), and **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This **teaches that he spent the night in the depths** [***be'umeka***] **of *halakha*,** i.e., that he spent the night studying Torah with the Jewish people. **And Rav Shmuel bar Unya said: Torah study is greater than sacrificing the daily offerings, as it is stated: "I have come now"** (Joshua 5:14), indicating that the angel came to rebuke Joshua for neglecting Torah study and not for neglecting the daily offering. Consequently, how did the Sages of the house of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi determine that the Temple service is more important than Torah study?

The *Gemara* then uses the principle established above as the foundation for a further *kal v'chomer*: *mikan samchos shel beit rebbi she'mevatlin talmud Torah u'va'im lishmo'a mikra megillah* — from here, the household of *Rebbi* derived support for the practice of interrupting *Torah* study to come hear the *Megillah*. In his era, there was no longer a *Beit HaMikdash*; the parallel to *avodah* was *talmud Torah*. The *kal v'chomer*: if *avodah* — which is weighty — yields to *Megillah*, how much more so should *talmud Torah* yield?

The *Gemara* refuses to accept this straightforwardly, because it implies that *talmud Torah* is less weighty than *avodah*. The *Gemara* brings the episode from *Sefer Yehoshua* to demonstrate that *talmud Torah* is in fact weightier than *korbanot*. When the angel appeared to Yehoshua before the battle, he rebuked him on two counts: the afternoon *tamid* had not been brought the previous evening, and *talmud Torah* was being neglected. Yehoshua asked: which one brought you here? *Ata basi* — it was the *bitul talmud Torah* that was the more serious failure. Immediately afterward, the verse says *vayalen Yehoshua balaylah hahu besoch ha-emek* — Yehoshua lay that night in the depths of the valley — which Rabbi Yochanan reads as *she-lan b'omka shel halacha*, he immersed himself in the depths of *halacha*. Yehoshua received the message and acted on it. From this Rav Shmuel bar Unya derives: *gadol talmud Torah yoseir me-hakravat t'midin* — *talmud Torah* is weightier than the daily sacrifices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּרַבִּים, וְהָא דְּיָחִיד. וּדְיָחִיד קַל? וְהָתְנַן: נָשִׁים בַּמּוֹעֵד מְעַנּוֹת אֲבָל לֹא מְטַפְּחוֹת, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיוּ סְמוּכוֹת לַמִּטָּה — מְטַפְּחוֹת. בְּרָאשֵׁי חֳדָשִׁים בַּחֲנוּכָּה וּבְפוּרִים, מְעַנּוֹת וּמְטַפְּחוֹת בָּזֶה וּבָזֶה, אֲבָל לֹא מְקוֹנְנוֹת. וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר הוּנָא: אֵין מוֹעֵד בִּפְנֵי תַּלְמִיד חָכָם — כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן חֲנוּכָּה וּפוּרִים. כְּבוֹד תּוֹרָה קָאָמְרַתְּ. כְּבוֹד תּוֹרָה דְּיָחִיד — חָמוּר, תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה דְּיָחִיד — קַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that it is **not difficult. This** statement, with regard to the story of Joshua, is referring to Torah study **by the masses,** which is greater than the Temple service. **That** statement of the Sages of the house of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is referring to Torah study **by an individual,** which is less significant than the Temple service. The Gemara asks: Is the Torah study **of an individual a light** matter? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: **On** the intermediate days of **a Festival, women may lament** the demise of the deceased in unison, **but they may not clap** their hands in mourning? **Rabbi Yishmael says: Those that are close to the bier may clap. On the New Moon, on Hanukkah, and on Purim,** which are not mandated by Torah law, **they may** both **lament and clap** their hands in mourning. However, **on both** groups of days, **they may not wail** responsively, a form of wailing where one woman wails and the others repeat after her. **And Rabba bar Huna said:** All these regulations were said with regard to an ordinary person, but **there are no** restrictions on expressions of mourning on the intermediate days of **a Festival in the presence of a** deceased **Torah scholar.** If a Torah scholar dies on the intermediate days of a Festival, the women may lament, clap, and wail responsively as on any other day, **and all the more so on Hanukkah and Purim.** This indicates that even the Torah study of an individual is of great importance. The Gemara rejects this argument: **You speak of the honor** that must be shown **to the Torah,** and indeed, **the honor** that must be shown to **the Torah** in the case of **an individual** Torah scholar **is important;** but **the Torah study of an individual** in itself **is light** and is less significant than the Temple service.

*Tosafot* at the bottom of 3a notes a textual difficulty: the verse *vayalen Yehoshua balaylah hahu besoch ha-emek* is not from the passage describing the siege of *Yericho* but from the account of the siege of *Ai*. The verse at *Yericho* reads *besoch ha-am* rather than *besoch ha-emek*, while *vayelech* rather than *vayalen* is used at *Ai*. *Tosafot* explains: *v'derech ha-Shas l'katzeir pesukim u'l'arvam yachad* — the *Gemara* sometimes combines and splices verses rather than quoting any single verse exactly. This is a known *Shas* practice and not an error.

The resolution the *Gemara* offers to the apparent contradiction — *talmud Torah* is weightier than *avodah*, yet *rebbi* makes a *kal v'chomer* from *avodah* to *talmud Torah* — is: *lo kashya ha d'rabim ha d'yachid*. There are two categories of *talmud Torah*: *talmud Torah d'rabim*, communal Torah study, which is indeed weightier than *korbanot*; and *talmud Torah d'yachid*, individual Torah study, which is less so. *Rebbi*'s *kal v'chomer* applies only to *talmud Torah d'yachid*, which yields to *Megillah* just as *avodah* yields to *Megillah*.

The *Gemara* then presses the question: is *talmud Torah d'yachid* really the lesser obligation? It brings a *mishnah* about mourning practices on *chol ha-moed*: women may be *meanos* — leading communal wailing — but not *metapchos* — beating on the breast. On *Rosh Chodesh*, *Chanukah*, and Purim, they may do both, but not *m'konenot* — formal dirges. Rava bar Huna rules that by the death of a *talmid chacham*, even *chol ha-moed* does not restrict the full mourning rites; *kal v'chomer Chanukah u'Purim*. This suggests that even one individual *talmid chacham* — whose death occasions *kvod Torah* — outweighs the concerns of *Chanukah* and Purim.

The *Gemara*'s response is careful: *kvod Torah chamur* — honoring the Torah is weighty, and this is what overrides *Chanukah* and Purim in that case. *Kvod Torah d'yachid chamur* — showing honor to the Torah, even in the case of a single individual, outweighs those concerns. But the act of learning Torah oneself — *talmud Torah d'yachid* — is of lesser weight, and does yield to *mikra megillah*.

The framework that has thus emerged is a hierarchy: *talmud Torah d'rabim* is at the top, weightier even than *avodah*; *avodah* yields to *Megillah*; *talmud Torah d'yachid* similarly yields to *Megillah*; but *kvod Torah* — honoring a *talmid chacham*, showing deference to the Torah itself — stands in a category of its own that overrides even *Megillah*. The unresolved question left for the next *sugya* is whether *talmud Torah d'rabim* itself yields to *mikra megillah*, or whether it retains precedence.
