# Avodah Zarah 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Melach Salkondarit

### Definition & Etymology

The daf opens on 39b, three lines before the wider lines of the *mishna*, and takes up the case of *melach salkondarit* — salt that is included among items forbidden for consumption yet not forbidden in *hana'ah*. The Gemara begins with the basic question: *mai melach salkondarit*? What exactly is this salt?

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel explains that it is the salt eaten by all the *salkondari* of Rome, which Rashi interprets as the bakers of Rome. The Koren Gemara offers additional etymological context, suggesting the term may derive from the Greek *hals kondaris*, denoting a coarse, large-granular salt, or alternatively from the Latin *sal conditum*, a salt seasoned with various herbs. Rashi's rendering of *salkondari* as bakers may alternatively be read as referring to people who are gathered — suggesting a salt associated with important government officials.

### Color Disputes

A *baraita* records a three-way dispute over which variety of this salt is forbidden. Rabbi Meir holds that the black variety is forbidden and the white is permitted. Rabbi Yehuda takes the opposite position: the white is forbidden and the black is permitted. Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamaliel in the name of Rabbi Chananya ben Gamaliel rules that both are forbidden.

### Fish Preparation

Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan explains the reasoning behind each position. According to the view that white is forbidden, the preparation method involves mixing in the innards of white non-kosher fish. According to the view that black is forbidden, the innards of black non-kosher fish are mixed in. And according to the view that both are forbidden, both types of fish innards are blended into the salt — presumably to congeal it into large blocks, though the precise purpose is not entirely clear.

### Pig Fat Anecdote

Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Chananya ben Gamaliel adds a further detail: there was an elder in his neighborhood who would smear the surface of this salt with the fat of a pig. This anecdote provides yet another possible ground for the prohibition recorded in the *mishna*.

### Prohibition & Benefit

The *mishna*'s closing phrase — *harei elu asurin aval ein asurin behana'ah*, these are forbidden but not forbidden in *hana'ah* — prompts the Gemara to ask what is being excluded. According to Chizkiyah, the exclusion targets cases where one definitively knows that wine was added to a pickled food; in such a case, the item would be forbidden even in *hana'ah*, unlike the ambiguous cases of *melach salkondarit* and its like. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who disagrees with Chizkiyah's framework, the exclusion refers back to *muris* and the cheeses mentioned in the previous *mishna*, which carry a wine-related or idolatry-related concern and are therefore forbidden even in *hana'ah* — consistent with the view of Rabbi Meir.

## Permitted Foods

### Milk Supervision

The *mishna* now turns to things that are permitted for consumption, opening with *chalav shechalvo akum veYisrael ro'ehu* — milk milked by a non-Jew while a Jew is observing, which is permissible.

### Honey & Grapes

*Devash* — honey — is permitted, as is *dvadvaniyot* — grape clusters. The *mishna* notes that even if the grape clusters are dripping, the dripping liquid does not render them susceptible to *tumah* via *hechsher mashkeh*, the mechanism by which liquid desired by a person renders produce susceptible. Since the person wants the grapes themselves and not the juice, the dripping is not *leratsono* — not something the person desires — and therefore does not activate the susceptibility. The precise connection to the broader theme of what is permitted in the context of foods from non-Jews is not entirely transparent from the *mishna* itself.

### Pickled Foods

Similarly permitted are *kevashim she'ein darkan liten letochan yayin vechometz* — pickled foods into which wine or vinegar would not normally be added. The logic is that since the preparation of these foods does not standardly involve wine, there is no concern.

### Tarith Fish

*Tarith she'einah terufah* — the *tarith* fish when it is not chopped up — is permitted, because when the fish remains intact enough to be identified, one need not fear that non-kosher fish have been mixed in. The chopped-up form is problematic precisely because the identity can no longer be verified.

### Tzir & Kilbis

*Tzir sheyesh bo dag* — fish brine that contains *kilbis* fish swimming in it — is also permitted. Rashi explains that the presence of these small swimming fish, perhaps akin to a type of small sardine, attests that the brine derives from kosher fish. The converse, according to Rashi, is that if the brine were from non-kosher fish, these *kilbis* would not be swimming in it — a claim whose precise empirical basis is difficult to verify, but which the tradition accepts.

### Chiltis & Olives

*Aleh chiltit* — the leaf of the *chiltit* plant — is permitted. The *chiltit* itself, a sharp spice, can absorb flavors and would raise concerns if used with non-kosher knives, but the leaf is innocuous. Finally, *zeitei gluskaot hamugagalin* — olive cakes that have been pressed together into tight rounds and have softened from sitting in their own juices — are also permitted.

### Shalchin & Locust

Rabbi Yossi introduces two exceptions. He holds that *shlachim* olives — extremely soft olives, as will be explained in the Gemara — are forbidden. He further rules that *chagavim habaim min hasalula* — locusts coming from the merchant's basket — are forbidden, because when displayed for sale, wine would be sprinkled over them to enhance their smell or taste. However, *min haheftek mutarim* — those coming directly from the storeroom are permitted, since the wine was applied only at the point of public display. The *mishna* closes by noting that a parallel distinction applies regarding *teruma*, as the Gemara will explain.

## Milk Supervision Debate

### Sitting vs. Standing

The Gemara notes that the *mishna*'s ruling permitting milk observed by a Jew is consistent with a *baraita*, which states that a Jew may sit beside the non-Jew's entire flock while the non-Jew milks, and may even receive the milk without concern about non-kosher milk being added.

The Gemara then probes the specific case. If there are no non-kosher animals present in the flock, the leniency is self-evident — *pshita* — and no novel ruling is needed. But if there are non-kosher animals present, why should the Jew's merely sitting nearby suffice, given that while seated he cannot necessarily see the milking at every moment?

The Gemara resolves this: indeed, the case involves non-kosher animals in the vicinity. When the Jew stands, he can see the non-Jew; when seated, he cannot. The *chidush* of the *baraita* is that sitting is sufficient — not because the Jew sees every moment, but because *mirsas*, the non-Jew is afraid. Since the Jew could stand at any moment and observe, the non-Jew will not risk mixing in non-kosher milk. Continuous active observation is thus not required.

### Ashkenazi-Sephardi

This analysis opens onto a major contemporary debate regarding *chalav Yisrael*. The Gemara's formulation that if there are no non-kosher animals the case is *pshita* — self-evident, requiring no Jew at all — might suggest that the *gezeira* requiring Jewish supervision simply does not apply where there is no physical possibility of adulteration. This is essentially the Sephardic position: where no non-kosher animal milk is available, the requirement of *chalav Yisrael* does not apply.

The Ashkenazic counter-position reads the Gemara differently: the *pshita* means only that if there are no non-kosher animals, a Jew sitting nearby is obviously sufficient — but a Jew's presence is still formally required. The absence of non-kosher animals does not in itself dispense with the need for supervision.

### Rav Moshe Solution

This leads to the famous *chidush* of Rav Moshe Feinstein. Even according to the Ashkenazic position — which requires the formal presence of Jewish oversight and does not rely solely on the absence of non-kosher animals — Rav Moshe argued that under modern regulatory conditions, the situation is qualitatively different. Government oversight through agencies such as the USDA creates real legal and financial consequences for adulteration. There is no financial incentive, only a substantial financial disincentive, to mix in other milks. Under these conditions, the reality of oversight is so patent that it constitutes *anan sahdei* — we are all effectively witnesses — making it equivalent to *Yisrael ro'ehu*, a Jew observing. Rav Moshe's position thus holds that even for Ashkenazim who generally require formal Jewish supervision, modern commercial milk production meets that standard.

## Honey Analysis

### Honey Concerns

The Gemara examines why honey from non-Jews should be permitted. What concerns might apply, and why do none of them hold?

Three possible grounds for prohibition are considered and dismissed. First, concern for adulteration with wine: *sarvei misrei* — it would spoil the honey, so a non-Jew would never do it. Second, concern for *bishul akum* — that the honey was cooked by a non-Jew, which would normally prohibit it — is dismissed because honey *ne'echal kemo shehu chai*, it can be consumed raw, and the prohibition of *bishul akum* does not apply to foods that can be eaten without cooking. Third, concern for *gi'ulei akum* — that the honey was cooked in non-kosher pots, imparting a forbidden taste — is dismissed because even if such pots were used, *noten ta'am lifgam hu umutar*: whatever flavor was absorbed would only impart a bad taste to the honey, and *noten ta'am lifgam* is permitted.

### Non-Kosher Pots

This Gemara, along with other passages encountered in this tractate, assumes that cooking in non-kosher pots imparts a bad taste. This assumption is never stated explicitly in the Gemara itself, but is articulated by Tosafot here and by the *rishonim* generally in the form of the rule: *stam keilim shel akum einam bnei yoman* — we presume that the vessels of non-Jews are not *bnei yoman*, that is, they have not been used for non-kosher food within the past twenty-four hours, and therefore any absorbed taste they impart is considered *noten ta'am lifgam* and does not prohibit *bedi'avad*.

Tosafot goes further, explaining this assumption as a *sfek sfeika*: perhaps the pot was used today, perhaps yesterday; and even if today, perhaps it was used with something that gives a bad taste. This double doubt is what underlies the general assumption that non-Jewish pots are not a problem *bedi'avad*, though they may not be used *lechatchila*.

## Grape Clusters

### Shammai vs. Hillel

The *mishna*'s ruling that grape clusters are permitted even if dripping prompts a challenge from a well-known dispute. The *mishna* in *Tahorot* discusses *habotzer legat* — one who harvests grapes intending to press them for wine — where Shammai holds that the grapes become susceptible to *tumah* via *hechsher mashkeh* once liquid drips out, since the person desires that liquid. Hillel initially disagrees but ultimately concedes to Shammai. If so, dripping grape juice creates susceptibility — and that would raise a concern in our *mishna* as well. Why then are the grape clusters permitted?

### Intention Principle

The Gemara's answer is direct. *Hatam ka ba'ei lei lemashkeh* — in the case of the one harvesting to press wine, he desires the liquid; *hacha lo ka ba'ei lei lemashkeh* — here, the person wants to eat the grapes, not collect the juice. Since the dripping is not *leratsono*, not something the person wants, it does not activate susceptibility to *tumah*. The intention of the person handling the produce is determinative.

## Tarith Fish ID

### Definition

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *eizehu tarith she'einah terufah?* — what constitutes a *tarith* that is "not chopped up" and therefore identifiable? *Kol she'rosh veshidra nikar* — anything whose head and spine can be identified. Rashi explains that this means you must be able to see the head and spine of each and every fish; if only some of them are visible, one would still have to be concerned about those whose identity cannot be verified.

### Huna vs. Nachman

A dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Nachman follows. Rav Huna holds that identification requires both the head and the spine to be recognizable. Rav Nachman holds that either one is sufficient — if the fish can be identified by the head alone or the spine alone, that is enough.

The practical analogy is instructive: may one purchase, say, a salmon fillet without fins and scales visible, relying instead on the color or texture of the flesh and the profile of the flesh itself? This is precisely the question — whether one identifying feature alone can vouch for the kosher status of the fish.

### Ukva Challenge

Rav Ukva bar Chama challenges both positions from the rules in Tractate Chullin: the identifying mark for fish is *snapir vekaskeset*, fins and scales. The *simanim* for fish are established by the Torah itself, and no mention is made of head or spine as alternative identifiers. This seems to undermine any permission based on those features.

### Abaye Resolution

Abaye resolves the difficulty. The ruling in Chullin that only fins and scales serve as *simanim* applies specifically to fish of the type *ara ufumda* — certain fish whose heads look similar to those of non-kosher fish and therefore cannot be relied upon for identification. For those fish, only fins and scales will do. But for fish whose heads and spines are distinctive enough to identify them, such features can indeed serve as valid indicators. The question in Chullin is therefore not addressing this situation.

### Body vs. Brine

Rav Yehuda in the name of Ulla introduces a further distinction. The debate between Rav Huna and Rav Nachman — whether one needs both head and spine or just one — pertains specifically to dipping in the *tzir*, the fish brine or oil that comes off the fish. When it comes to eating the body of the fish itself, however, everyone agrees that one must be able to see both the head and the spine; one alone does not suffice.

Tosafot raises the obvious question: why should there be any leniency for the *tzir*? Even the liquid that drains from non-kosher fish is itself non-kosher — *ta'am ke'ikar de'oraisa*. Tosafot answers that the leniency concerns a case where the *tzir* has been mixed with water, in which case the forbidden substance would be *batel berov* on a Torah level, even though rabbinic law would generally not permit that *bittul*. That explanation has an element of an *okimta* — it is somewhat forced — but Tosafot offers it as the basis for the distinction.

### Zeira Stringency

Rabbi Zeira reports his own evolution on this question. He had originally assumed that everyone agreed that for the *tzir*, even a single identifying mark was sufficient, and he would accordingly permit dipping in the *tzir* when only one of the two features was visible. Upon hearing the teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Ulla that the debate itself is about the *tzir*, and that eating the body is unanimously more stringent, Rabbi Zeira became *machmir* and resolved that he would not permit even dipping in the *tzir* unless both head and spine are visible.

### Rav Papa Ruling

Rav Papa rules definitively: *hilcheta ad sheyehei rosh veshidra nikar* — the *halacha* requires both head and spine to be recognizable, even when the question is merely about the *tzir*. This is the most stringent ruling and represents the final, authoritative position on the matter.

## Fish Pieces

### Baraita Teaching

The Gemara now addresses a challenge to Rav Papa's ruling that every individual fish must have its identifying marks visible. A *baraita* teaches that when pieces of fish are present — some bearing identifying marks, some not — whether the identifying mark is present on all of them, on some of them, or even on just one of them, they are all permitted. This is a striking ruling: a single piece with visible fins and scales, out of a hundred unidentifiable fillets, renders the entire batch permissible. The logic is that presumably the seller cut up a single fish and sold the pieces together, but it is nonetheless a significant leniency.

### Gamliel Story

The *baraita* recounts a supporting incident: a non-Jew brought a container of fish pieces, and an identifying mark was found on one of them. Rabban Gamliel permitted the entire container on that basis. This illustrates the principle in action at the highest level of *halachic* authority.

### Equal Pieces

Rav Papa resolves the apparent contradiction between this teaching and his own ruling requiring every piece to be identifiable: *keshechatichot shaveiot* — this applies only when the pieces are equal. Rashi explains that equal here means the pieces can be physically fitted together, demonstrating that they derive from the same fish — like a puzzle. They are not merely similar in appearance; one can verify their common origin by assembling them. The Gemara then asks: if so, is this not obvious? Why does the *baraita* need to state it?

The answer is that one might have thought to be concerned: even if the pieces seem to fit together, perhaps one of them is from a different fish and merely happens to fit. The *baraita* teaches that we do not entertain that suspicion.

### Huna Position

A separate incident: a boat carrying salted fish arrived at the city of Sichra. Rav Huna bar Chinana went out, saw scales mixed in among the fish, and permitted the entire load. This again suggests that a visible identifying mark found among a batch — here, loose scales — can render the whole permissible.

### Rava Objection

Rava objected: is there anyone who would permit on this basis in a place where scales are common? In a location where many fish with scales are processed and traded, loose scales in a boat could easily have arrived from a previous cargo, from different fish, or from other sources entirely. Their presence cannot reliably attest that these particular fish are kosher. Accordingly, Rava's announcement went out forbidding the fish. Rav Huna bar Chinana was undeterred and issued his own announcement permitting them.

### Ashi Report

Rav Yirmiyah miDiphti reported in the name of Rav Pappi that when Rav Huna bar Chinana permitted, he did so only for the *tzir*, not for the fish bodies themselves — consistent with the greater leniency already noted for *tzir* by Tosafot. Rav Ashi, however, reports a different version: Rav Pappi told him that Rav Huna bar Chinana actually permitted even the fish bodies. Rav Ashi adds that he himself would neither forbid — as Rava had announced — nor permit, because while Rav Pappi's report supports leniency, the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Ulla establishes that for the fish body itself, everyone agrees one needs the head and spine visible for each individual fish. That ruling stands as a strong barrier against permitting the entire load on the basis of loose scales, and Rav Ashi therefore declined to rule definitively in either direction.

## Fish Brine Barrels

### Four Barrels

The Gemara proceeds to a new case. Rav Chinana bar Idi, seated before Rav Adda bar Ahava, posed the following scenario: a non-Jew brings a boat full of barrels, and *kilbis* fish are found in one of them. Can one extrapolate from that one barrel to permit the others?

### Open vs. Closed

The answer depends on whether the barrels are open or closed. If the barrels are open, all of them are permitted. Rashi's explanation here is that the presence of *kilbis* in one open barrel provides a basis for extrapolation: if the brine were from non-kosher fish, the *kilbis* would not be found there. And for the other open barrels, the absence of *kilbis* does not prove they are non-kosher — the *kilbis* may simply have swum out. One therefore extrapolates from the positive evidence in one barrel to permit the rest. If the barrels are sealed, by contrast, only the one containing *kilbis* is permitted, while the others are forbidden. Since they are closed, the *kilbis* could not have left, and their absence in the other barrels is genuine negative evidence that cannot be explained away.

### Three Authorities

When asked the source for this ruling, Rav Chinana bar Idi replied that he had heard it from three great authorities — Rav, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan — and therefore relied on it with confidence.

## Fish Roe & Innards

### Expert Requirement

Rav Bruna in the name of Rav rules that *kirvei dagim v'ubaran* — the innards of fish and their *ubar*, meaning their roe or eggs — may only be purchased from an expert, a *mumche*. Since there are no fins and scales to observe, there are no standard external *simanim* to rely upon, and one must therefore trust someone with the knowledge and integrity to certify that these derive from a kosher fish.

### Ulla Challenge

Ulla challenged Rabbi Dostai with a contradiction. The requirement to buy roe only from an expert implies that non-kosher fish can also produce roe, which is therefore a concern. Yet a teaching in Tractate Bechorot states that *dag tamei mashritz* — non-kosher fish reproduce viviparously, gestating their offspring within their bodies — while *dag tahor matil beitzim* — kosher fish lay their eggs outside their bodies. If only kosher fish lay eggs, then all roe by definition comes from kosher fish, and no expert would be needed.

The Gemara's initial response is *sami mikan v'ubaran* — remove the word *ubaran* from Rav's ruling. The concern about experts applies only to innards, not to roe; roe need not be bought from an expert since it is always from kosher fish.

### Fish Reproduction

The resolution through deletion of *ubaran* is based on the premise that only kosher fish produce eggs. It should be noted that this is not biologically accurate — certain non-kosher aquatic animals, such as some species of shark, do lay eggs outside the body. The Talmudic sources work with the understanding available to them, and the ruling flows from that framework.

### Zeira Correction

Rabbi Zeira objects to the textual deletion: *lo tismi* — do not erase it. *Traivaihu matilei beitzim ninhu* — both kosher and non-kosher fish produce roe. The distinction of the *baraita* in Bechorot is not about the presence or absence of eggs, but about where the reproductive process takes place: *zeh mashritz mibachutz v'zeh mashritz mibifnim* — one deposits eggs outside its body, the other inside. Both have eggs, but in kosher fish the eggs develop and are deposited externally, while in non-kosher fish they remain internal. When one opens either a kosher or a non-kosher fish, one may find a cluster of eggs inside. The word *ubaran* therefore belongs in Rav's ruling, and roe must indeed be purchased from an expert.

Here too, the biological claim is not fully accurate by modern standards — certain non-kosher species do lay eggs externally. But the underlying *halachic* framework is clear: roe requires an expert because it can be found in both kosher and non-kosher fish.

### Roe Characteristics

The Gemara then asks: why rely on an expert at all? Why not simply examine the roe itself for identifying marks? A *baraita* teaches *k'simanei beitzim kach simanei dagim* — just as there are identifying characteristics for eggs from birds, there are similarly identifying characteristics for fish. If bird eggs have *simanim*, then presumably fish products do as well.

The Gemara initially takes *simanei dagim* to refer to fish themselves, and objects: of course fish can be identified — the Torah explicitly requires fins and scales, and no novel *baraita* is needed to tell us that. The *baraita* must therefore be read as: just as there are identifying characteristics for eggs from kosher versus non-kosher birds, similarly there are identifying characteristics for *uvrei dagim* — the roe of fish.

The *baraita* then details the *simanim* for kosher bird eggs: an egg that is rounded at one end and tapered at the other, with one wide end and one narrow end, is *tehorah* — kosher. An egg with both ends pointed, or both ends wide, is *temeiah* — not kosher. If the yolk is on the outside and the white on the inside, it is not kosher; if the white is on the outside and the yolk inside, it is kosher. If the yolk and white are mixed together with no distinction, that is the egg of a *sheretz* and is not kosher.

The question that follows is what the corresponding *simanim* for fish roe look like. Does the *baraita* mean the same *simanim* apply — one end wide, one end narrow — or something different? The Gemara understands the parallel to be exact: just as the kosher bird egg is wide on one end and narrow on the other, so too are the identifying marks for kosher fish roe based on shape, specifically the presence of one broader and one narrower pole.

### Expert Still Needed

If, then, one can identify kosher fish roe by its shape, why is a *mumche* still required? The answer is given by Rava at the top of 41b: *keshenimochu* — when the eggs have melted and congealed together into an undifferentiated mass, the identifying marks are no longer visible, and there is no way to inspect them. Under those conditions, which are standard for processed roe, one cannot rely on *simanim* and must therefore depend on an expert's testimony.

As for the position of Rabbi Dimi from Biri, who had argued for deleting *ubaran* from Rav's ruling on the grounds that all roe is kosher, the Gemara now addresses his view: once the *baraita* is properly edited to read *k'simanei beitzim kach simanei uvrei dagim* — just as there are identifying marks for eggs, so too for fish roe — this itself implies that roe can be non-kosher and therefore requires examination. Rabbi Dimi's counter-reading is that the *baraita* was never about roe at all, but about fish innards (*kirvei dagim*), and that the comparison is: just as eggs have identifying marks, so too do fish innards, specifically the *shalpucha*, the swim bladder of the fish, which can also present with one side wider and one side narrower. According to this reading, the *baraita* supports the need for examination of innards, not of roe — though we do not rule in accordance with Rabbi Dimi's position that roe requires no expert.

### Processor Claim

The Gemara now turns to what can be done when no *mumche* is available. Rav Yehuda rules: *keivan d'amar ani melachtim mutarin* — once the seller states "I salted these myself," they are permitted. The reasoning is that a person will stand behind his own work; if he claims personal responsibility for the processing, that testimony is credible even from someone who has not been previously established as a reliable expert. A middleman, by contrast, may not know the origins of what he is selling, but a processor who handled the fish himself can be trusted to know what they are.

### Show Fish

Rav Nachman is more stringent: *ad sheyomar elu dagim v'elu kirvehen* — it is not enough for the seller to claim he salted them. He must be able to show you the fish and their innards or roe together. Once you can see the gutted fish alongside the extracted material, you can verify for yourself that these are the innards or roe of the kosher fish before you, even if you cannot see them being extracted in real time. Rav Yehuda communicated his more lenient ruling to Ada, his attendant, instructing him that a seller's claim of personal salting is sufficient without requiring him to produce the fish alongside.

## Leaf & Cakes

### Chiltis Leaf

The Gemara now addresses the *aleh chiltit* — the leaf of the *chiltit* plant. The *chiltit* itself is an extremely sharp spice that can absorb flavor from non-kosher knives and render them *l'shevach*, imparting a beneficial absorbed taste. But the leaf is innocuous, and its permissibility seems self-evident — *pshita*.

The Gemara clarifies that the ruling is needed for a specific situation: *l'kortim shebah* — for the small pieces of *chiltit* that happen to be found attached to the leaf. One might have thought to be concerned that the non-Jew deliberately mixed cut pieces of *chiltit* into the leaves, which would be a problem since those pieces could carry absorbed flavors. The ruling teaches otherwise: *ishtaruki hu d'ishtaruq v'ati bahadah* — it is natural for small fragments of *chiltit* to find their way onto the leaves during harvesting and handling. No deliberate mixing need be suspected, and no motivation to do so exists.

### Olive Cakes

The Gemara turns to the case of *zeitei gluskaot hamigulgalim* — olive cakes that have been pressed together and are noticeably soft. This too seems self-evident: what concern should there be? The Gemara explains that the ruling addresses a case where the olives are *rafi tuva* — exceptionally soft. One might have thought that their softness indicates that wine was added to them. The Gemara rules otherwise: *hanei mechamat mishcha hu d'rafi* — they are soft because they are sitting in their own oil, not because of any added wine.

## Locusts & Produce

### Marketplace Wine

The Gemara discusses the case of locusts found in the marketplace. When the merchant displays his locusts for sale, he sprinkles wine over them to improve their smell or taste. This is the reason locusts in the basket at the front of the store — *habaim min hasalula* — are forbidden.

### Produce Rules

A *baraita* elaborates the general rule governing these concerns. Locusts, caper buds, and leeks — *hachagavim vehakafrisin vehakaplutot* — are all permissible if they come from the storehouse, the storeroom, or the boat: any stage before they are put out for retail sale. Wine would not be applied during storage or transport, only when the goods are displayed in front of the merchant for sale. Anything sold in the marketplace and placed before a storekeeper is forbidden on account of the wine he sprinkles over it.

A separate clause of the same *baraita* addresses apple cider made by non-Jews. Apple cider coming from the storehouse, storeroom, or even the basket is permitted. Unlike the other goods, where wine was applied specifically at the point of display, here even in the basket it is not yet a concern. However, once it is sold in the open marketplace — *hanimkar bekatloza* — it is forbidden because at that stage wine is mixed into it. The precise reason why wine is added to the apple cider only in the marketplace and not in the basket is not entirely clear from the *baraita*.

## Apple Cider Story

### Rabbi Illness

The *baraita* continues with a narrative: once, Rabbi suffered from a stomach ailment. He asked whether anyone knew if the apple cider of non-Jews — *yayin tapuchim shel ovdei kochavim* — was forbidden or permitted, implying that he thought it might help him.

### Yishmael Account

Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yossi responded: once my father, Rabbi Yossi, suffered from a stomach ailment, and they brought him apple cider of non-Jews that had fermented for seventy years — *yayin tapuchim shel ayin shana* — and it healed him.

### Cider Search

Rabbi thereupon exclaimed: if you had this knowledge, why did you allow me to suffer in pain? A search was conducted and a non-Jew was found who possessed three hundred flasks of apple cider that was seventy years old. Rabbi drank it and was healed.

### Praise

Upon his recovery, Rabbi declared: *baruch hamakom shemasar olamo leshomrim* — praised be God who has handed over His world to those who watch over it. The comment is striking in context: after an entire tractate focused on the dangers and prohibitions associated with the non-Jewish world, here Rabbi acknowledges that non-Jews can be instruments of healing and benefit, and expresses genuine gratitude. The entirety of this Gemara's concerns with the non-Jewish world does not preclude recognizing that there is much to gain from what that world has produced.

## Teruma Rules

### Suspect Kohen

The *mishna*'s closing phrase — *vechen litruma* — is now explained. Rav Sheshet interprets it as referring to a *kohen* who is suspected of selling his *teruma* while claiming it is *chullin*. *Teruma* is undesirable to many buyers: only *kohanim* may eat it, and if it becomes *tamei* it cannot be eaten at all. A suspect *kohen* might attempt to offload his *teruma* by misrepresenting it as ordinary produce. Items sold directly by such a *kohen* in his store or in front of him are forbidden, because there is genuine concern about this substitution.

### Storage Leniency

However, goods coming from his storehouse, storeroom, or basket — further removed from the immediate point of sale — are permitted. The reasoning is *irtzuyei mirtzei*: he is afraid. If he were to have his entire stock of *teruma* discovered and condemned, the Rabbis would cause him to lose everything. Selling a handful of *teruma* directly in the store to an unsuspecting customer may not expose him to much risk. But if the goods are coming from the storehouse or from a larger quantity in transit, the exposure is far greater, and he would not risk his entire supply. Therefore, one need not be concerned about *teruma* mixed into such goods.

This concludes the second *perek* of Avodah Zarah, which has addressed the full range of concerns governing interactions with the non-Jewish world: food, commerce, social proximity, and the dangers of idolatry by association. The third *perek* now begins, turning from those broader interactions to the laws of idols directly.

## Idols Introduction

### Rabbi Meir

*Kol hatzlamim* — all images, meaning statues or figures — are forbidden, according to Rabbi Meir, *mipnei shenevadin pa'am achas b'shana* — because they are worshipped at least once a year. Rabbi Meir acknowledges that such statues, including those of Roman emperors, are primarily decorative or civic in character, but holds that because they are worshipped at least annually — perhaps on the emperor's birthday or some other occasion within the Roman imperial cult — they are treated as objects of *avodah zarah* across the board.

### Rabbis

The Rabbis disagree and hold that not all statues are presumptively forbidden. Only a statue that holds a staff, a bird, or a globe is forbidden. These attributes indicate that the statue is regarded as divine — a staff may represent authority over the world, a bird some symbolic power, and a sphere the globe, representing universal dominion. An ordinary statue lacking these attributes does not warrant the presumption of worship.

### Rabban Shimon

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel goes further still: *af kol sheyeish beyado kol davar* — any statue holding any object in its hand is treated as the representation of a deity. Any object held by a figure suffices to identify it as an idol.

## Idols Debate

### Meir Minority

The Gemara now examines how Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis actually relate to each other. If the Rabbis concede that some statues are worshipped once a year, how can they permit them at all? Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: in the locale of Rabbi Meir, statues were in fact worshipped once a year. The debate is therefore not about *metzius* — about whether such worship occurs — but about legal policy: does a minority practice in some places generate a universal prohibition?

Rabbi Meir, who is *chaish l'miuta* — who is concerned even with minority cases — extends the prohibition from places where such worship occurs to all other places as well. Since some locales worship these statues annually, he forbids all statues everywhere by extension.

### Rabbis Indifference

The Rabbis, who are *lo chaishi l'miuta* — not concerned with minority cases — do not apply this extension. A practice that exists in some locales does not generate a prohibition for all other locales where the practice does not occur. Accordingly, they require an affirmative indicator — a staff, bird, or globe — to presume that a given statue is an object of worship. Both positions agree on the underlying facts; their disagreement is entirely a matter of legal methodology in the face of geographic variation.
