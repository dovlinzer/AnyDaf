# Zevachim 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Avodot & Lishmah

### Mishna: Pesach & Chatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפֶּסַח וְהַחַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, קִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן; אוֹ לִשְׁמָן וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, אוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן וְלִשְׁמָן – פְּסוּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the Paschal offering and the sin offering, where one slaughtered them not for their sake,** or where he **collected** their blood in a vessel, **conveyed** this blood to the altar, **or sprinkled** this blood on the altar **not for their sake, or** where he performed one of these sacrificial rites **for their sake and not for their sake, or not for their sake and for their sake,** in all these cases the offerings **are unfit.**

The thirteenth daf of Masechet Zevachim opens with a *mishna* that reads almost like a poem in its elegant comprehensiveness. The *mishna* states that a *pesach* offering and a *chatat* that were slaughtered *shelo lishmah* — not for their own sake — and whose blood was received, carried, and sprinkled either entirely *shelo lishmah*, or partly *lishmah* and partly *shelo lishmah*, or partly *shelo lishmah* and partly *lishmah*, are *pesulim*, disqualified. The underlying principle is striking in its strictness: it does not matter in what sequence the proper and improper intentions appear. Whether the *avodah* begins properly and deteriorates, or begins improperly and is later corrected, the result is the same — the offering is invalid. Proper intent must characterize each and every one of the four *avodot* throughout.

### Four Avodot Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד לִשְׁמָן וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן? לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּלְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים. שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן וְלִשְׁמָן? לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים וּלְשֵׁם פֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **How** are these rites performed **for their sake and not for their sake?** In a case where one slaughtered the Paschal offering **for the sake of a Paschal offering and for the sake of a peace offering.** How are these rites performed **not for their sake and for their sake?** In a case where one slaughtered the Paschal offering **for the sake of a peace offering and for the sake of a Paschal offering.**

The four *avodot* under discussion are *shechitah* (slaughter), *kabbalah* (receiving the blood), *holachah* (carrying the blood to the altar), and *zrikah* (sprinkling the blood). These four acts together constitute the complete sacrificial process, and the *mishna* teaches that the disqualifying intent — *machshavah shelo lishmah* — can take effect in any one of them.

The *mishna* then illustrates what it means to have mixed intent: *lishmah* for the sake of a *pesach* and *shelo lishmah* for the sake of a *shelamim*, or the reverse — starting with *shelamim* intent and redirecting toward *pesach*. Once the intent has been misdirected, there is no rectification. The sacrifice has been disqualified.

### R. Shimon on Holachah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהַזֶּבַח נִפְסָל בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים: בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וּבְקִבּוּל, וּבְהִילּוּךְ, וּבִזְרִיקָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר בְּהִילּוּךְ, שֶׁהָיָה אוֹמֵר: אִי אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּקַבָּלָה וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּזְרִיקָה, אֲבָל אֶפְשָׁר בְּלֹא הִילּוּךְ – שׁוֹחֵט בְּצַד הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְזוֹרֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The offering is unfit **because a slaughtered offering is disqualified** due to prohibited intent **in four matters: In** the performance of the sacrificial rites of **slaughtering** the animal, **in collecting** the blood, **in conveying** the blood, **and in sprinkling** the blood. **Rabbi Shimon deems** the offering **fit** if the prohibited intent was **during** the rite of **conveying** the blood, **as he would say: It is impossible** to sacrifice an offering **without slaughtering** the animal, **or without collection** of the blood, **or without sprinkling** the blood, **but it is possible** to sacrifice an offering **without conveying** the blood to the altar. How so? If one **slaughters** the animal **alongside the altar and sprinkles** the blood, the blood does not need to be conveyed. Therefore, the rite of conveying the blood is not significant enough to cause the offering to be disqualified due to prohibited intent while performing it.

Rabbi Shimon dissents on one point: he rules that the *holachah* does not require *lishmah* intent in the same way the other three *avodot* do. His reasoning is rooted in a distinction between the inevitable and the contingent. *Shechitah*, *kabbalah*, and *zrikah* are inherently unavoidable — there can be no sacrifice without each of them. The *holachah*, by contrast, is avoidable. If the *kohen* slaughters the animal directly adjacent to the altar, no carrying is required at all, and the blood can be sprinkled immediately. Since it is possible to perform the sacrifice without a *holachah* at all, Rabbi Shimon reasons that an improper thought during this dispensable act should not invalidate the offering.

### R. Eliezer's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְיעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הַמְהַלֵּךְ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהוּא צָרִיךְ לְהַלֵּךְ – מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת; בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַלֵּךְ – אֵין מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to **one who conveys** the blood **in a situation where he is required to convey** it, prohibited **intent** while conveying it **disqualifies** the offering. If he conveys the blood **in a situation where he is not required to convey** it, prohibited **intent** while conveying it **does not disqualify** the offering.

Rabbi Eliezer introduces a further nuance. In his view, a *machshavah* is *poselet* — disqualifying — only when it occurs during a *holachah* performed in the place where walking is required. If, however, the *kohen* happens to be walking in an area where no *holachah* is obligatory — perhaps traversing a route that is not part of the required path — then a problematic thought arising in that context does not disqualify the offering. Improper intent is only operative when it attaches itself to a required act; when the act itself is unnecessary in the given circumstances, the errant thought has no surface on which to take effect.

At this point, the *Gemara* signals a transition that will define much of the daf. There are, in fact, two distinct categories of problematic intent in the realm of *korbanot*. The first, *machshavah shelo lishmah*, which the *mishna* has been discussing, concerns intent directed at a different offering than the one being brought. The second, *machshevat pigul*, concerns improper intent related to time — specifically, intending to eat or otherwise complete the offering outside its designated time. These two categories are not interchangeable, and much of the *Gemara*'s analysis will depend on keeping them carefully distinguished.

## Tarfon vs. Akiva

### Kabbalah Disqualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְקַבָּלָה מִי פָּסְלָה?!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a slaughtered offering is disqualified due to prohibited intent while performing four rites, including collection of the blood. The Gemara asks: **And does collection** of the blood with prohibited intent **disqualify** the offering?

The *Gemara* now raises a pointed question: does the *kabbalah* — receiving the blood — actually have the capacity to disqualify an offering through improper intent? The question arises from a *beraita* that appears to complicate matters, and the *Gemara* begins its inquiry by examining the biblical source for the four *avodot* themselves.

### Deriving from Vayikra 7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְהִקְרִיבוּ״ – זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר קַבָּלַת הַדָּם, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא זְרִיקָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְזָרְקוּ״ – הֲרֵי זְרִיקָה אָמוּר; הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְהִקְרִיבוּ״? זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם. ״בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֲנִים״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא בְּכֹהֵן כָּשֵׁר וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And** the sons of Aaron, the priests, **shall present** the blood" (Leviticus 1:5); **this** is referring to **collection of the blood. Do you say** it is referring to **collection of the blood, or is it** referring to **only sprinkling** the blood? **When it states** immediately afterward: **"And sprinkle** the blood roundabout against the altar," **sprinkling** the blood **is thereby stated.** If so, **how do I realize** the meaning of: **"And** the sons of Aaron, the priests, **shall present"? This** is referring to **collection of the blood.** And the phrase **"the sons of Aaron, the priests"** teaches **that** collection of the blood **must be** performed **by a fit priest,** i.e., one who has no blemish and who was not disqualified due to flawed lineage, **and** that he must do so while he is wearing priestly **service vestments.**

The *Gemara* identifies the middle verb, *ve'hikrivu*, as corresponding to *kabalat ha'dam* — receiving the blood. The question immediately follows: how do we know that *ve'hikrivu* refers to *kabbalah* and not to *zrikah*? The *Gemara*'s answer is straightforward: since *zrikah* is explicitly mentioned later in the same verse with the word *ve'zarku*, the term *ve'hikrivu* must refer to something else — and that something else is *kabbalah*.

The *Gemara* continues, noting that the verse specifies *bnei Aharon ha'kohanim* — that the act must be performed by a fit *kohen*, one who is not a *chalal* and not a *ba'al mum*, and moreover that it must be performed with *kli sharet*, meaning the *kohen* must be wearing his priestly vestments.

Rabbi Akiva then asks: what is the source that *kabbalah* requires a fit *kohen* and priestly vestments? He draws a comparison to a verse in Bamidbar at the opening of that book, which mentions *bnei Aharon ha'kohanim ha'meshuchim asher milei yadam l'khahen* — Aaron's anointed sons who were consecrated to serve. Since that passage refers to fit *kohanim* serving in their vestments, the same requirement extends to the *kabbalah* here.

### Rashi: Kabalah & Holachah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִנַּיִן לְקַבָּלָה שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּכֹהֵן כָּשֵׁר וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״אֵלֶּה שְׁמוֹת בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֲנִים הַמְּשֻׁחִים״; מָה לְהַלָּן – בְּכֹהֵן כָּשֵׁר וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת, אַף כָּאן – בְּכֹהֵן כָּשֵׁר וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Akiva said: From where** is it derived **that collection** of the blood **may be** performed **only by a fit priest and** while he is wearing **service vestments?** The expression **"the sons of Aaron" is stated here** in the verse in Leviticus, **and it is stated there: "These are the names of the sons of Aaron, the priests that were anointed,** whom he consecrated to minister in the priest's office" (Numbers 3:3). **Just as there,** in the verse in Numbers, it mentions the word "priests" **with regard to fit priests,** i.e., Aaron's sons, **and with regard to** priests who were consecrated to serve by being dressed with **service vestments** (see Exodus 29:30), **so too here,** the reference is **to a fit priest and with regard to** one who is wearing **service vestments.**

Rashi's comment on the identification of *ve'hikrivu* as *kabbalah* is particularly illuminating. One might ask: *ve'hikrivu* could refer equally to *holachah*, the act of bringing the blood near by carrying it to the altar — after all, *holachah* also involves coming close. How do we know it refers to *kabbalah* and not *holachah*?

Rashi explains that both *kabbalah* and *holachah* share the linguistic sense of drawing near. The root of *ve'hikrivu* — *karov*, nearness — captures both acts. *Kabbalah* involves receiving the blood as it pours from the slaughtered animal, a kind of passive encounter with what comes toward you. *Holachah* is active, a deliberate movement toward the altar. Rashi notes that the *svarah* underlying the *Gemara* is that *mah she'noheg b'kabbalah noheg b'holachah* — the same principles that govern *kabbalah* govern *holachah*, because they are intrinsically linked as two expressions of the same fundamental movement of drawing near.

There is something quietly profound in this linguistic observation. Both *kabbalah* and *halachah* — the reception of tradition and the path of practice — share this quality of approaching the Divine. One is receiving; the other is moving. Rashi sees them as of a piece.

The verse's phrase *bnei Aharon ha'kohanim* applies equally to the *kabbalah*, and Rashi further notes that the requirement of a *kli sharet* can be derived from the fact that Moshe himself received blood in a vessel at Sinai, establishing the vessel as an integral part of the act.

### R. Tarfon Affirms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: אֲקַפֵּחַ אֶת בָּנַיי, אִם לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי לְהַבְחִין הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין קַבָּלָה לִזְרִיקָה. וְאֵין לִי לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said: I will bury my sons if I did not hear** a *halakha* from my teachers **discerning a distinction between collection** of **and sprinkling** the blood, **but I cannot explicate** the distinction, as I do not remember what it was.

What follows is one of the most emotionally charged exchanges in the *Gemara*. Rabbi Tarfon, the elder, cries out: *akapeach et banai im lo shamati l'havchin hefreish bein kabbalah l'zerikah ve'ein li l'fareish* — "By my sons' lives, if I have not heard a distinction between *kabbalah* and *zrikah*, yet I cannot now articulate it." He remembers that there is a difference, but the memory has escaped him. Rabbi Akiva, the younger, responds with perhaps a touch of audacity: *mah Rabbi Akiva, ani afareish* — "What is this? I, Rabbi Akiva, will explain it."

### R. Akiva's Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲנִי אֲפָרֵשׁ: קַבָּלָה – לֹא עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַחְשָׁבָה כְּמַעֲשֶׂה, זְרִיקָה – עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַחְשָׁבָה כְּמַעֲשֶׂה. קִבְּלוֹ בַּחוּץ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב כָּרֵת, זְרָקוֹ בַּחוּץ – עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת. קִבְּלוּהוּ פְּסוּלִין – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו, זְרָקוּהוּ פְּסוּלִין – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Akiva said: I will explicate:** With regard to **collection,** the Torah **does not render intent like action.** Collecting the blood with prohibited intent does not disqualify the offering. But with regard to **sprinkling,** the Torah **does render intent like action.** Furthermore, if one **collected** the blood **outside** the Temple, **he is not liable** to be punished with ***karet*.** But if one **sprinkled** the blood **outside** the Temple, **he is punished** with ***karet*.** Moreover, if those who are **unfit** for the Temple service **collected** the blood, **they are not liable for** that action. But if those who are **unfit** for the Temple service **sprinkled** the blood, **they are liable** to receive the penalty of death at the hand of Heaven **for that** action.

Rabbi Akiva proceeds to enumerate three distinctions between *kabbalah* and *zrikah*.

The first: *kabbalah lo asah bah machshavah kema'aseh* — in the context of *kabbalah*, a wrong thought does not have the same legal force as a wrong action. *Zrikah*, by contrast, operates under the principle that *machshavah kema'aseh* — intent is treated as equivalent to action.

The second: if the *kabbalah* is performed outside the Temple precincts, there is no liability for *karet*. If the *zrikah* is performed outside, the person is liable for *karet*.

The third: if *pesulim* — disqualified persons — performed the *kabbalah*, there is no capital liability. If *pesulim* performed the *zrikah*, there is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: הָעֲבוֹדָה אִם הִטֵּיתָ יָמִין וּשְׂמֹאל! אֲנִי שָׁמַעְתִּי וְאֵין לִי לְפָרֵשׁ, אַתָּה דּוֹרֵשׁ וּמַסְכִּים לַשְּׁמוּעָה! בַּלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה אָמַר לוֹ: עֲקִיבָא, כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמְּךָ כְּפוֹרֵשׁ מֵחַיָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said to him** excitedly: I take an oath by **the** Temple **service** that **you have** not **deviated left or right** from what I heard from my teachers. **I** myself **heard** them **and I cannot explicate** what they taught, whereas **you teach** this *halakha* on your own **in concurrence with the *halakha*** I heard. **He said to** Rabbi Akiva, **using this language: Akiva, anyone who leaves you is like one who leaves his life.** In any event, Rabbi Akiva's statement in the *baraita* apparently contradicts the mishna with regard to the matter of whether the collection of the blood with prohibited intent disqualifies the offering.

Rabbi Tarfon's response is extraordinary: *ha'avodah, at hitit yamin u'smol, ani shamati ve'ein li l'fareish ve'atah doreish u'mas'kim la'shmu'ah* — "By the Temple service, you have veered neither right nor left! I heard it and cannot explain it; you derive it and arrive at the same teaching." And then, in an act of profound tribute, he says: *kol ha'poreish mimcha ke'poreish mechayav* — anyone who separates from you is like one who separates from life itself.

The linguistic resonance is striking. The root *p-r-sh* appears throughout the entire passage: Rabbi Tarfon says he cannot *l'fareish*; Rabbi Akiva says *ani afareish*; and Rabbi Tarfon concludes that anyone who *poreish* from Rabbi Akiva is *poreish* from life. The word that signifies "to explain," "to articulate," "to separate" binds the entire drama together.

Yet this celebrated exchange creates a legal problem. The *mishna* stated that all four *avodot* must be performed *lishmah*, implying that a wrong thought in *kabbalah* is disqualifying. But Rabbi Akiva has just told us that *lo asah bah machshavah kema'aseh* — an improper thought in *kabbalah* is not as consequential as a wrong action. How do we reconcile the two?

## Pigul vs. Shelo Lish (II)

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בְּמַחְשֶׁבֶת פִּיגּוּל, כָּאן בְּמַחְשֶׁבֶת שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **not difficult.** The *baraita* **here** is referring **to intent of *piggul*,** i.e., intent to consume or burn the offering after its appointed time. The mishna **there** is referring **to intent not for the sake of** the offering.

Rava resolves the contradiction with characteristic economy: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty at all. The two sources are simply discussing different categories of improper intent. *Kan be'machshevet pigul, kan be'machshavah shelo lishmah* — the *mishna* concerns *machshavah shelo lishmah* (intent directed at a different type of offering), while the *beraita* of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon concerns *machshevet pigul* (intent involving time-related impropriety). These are two distinct legal categories, and each has its own set of rules.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״שֶׁהַזֶּבַח נִפְסָל״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״מִתְפַּגֵּל״; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches that a slaughtered offering is disqualified** in this manner, **and it does not teach** that an offering **is rendered *piggul*** in this manner. Apparently, the mishna is referring to intent that merely disqualifies the offering without rendering it *piggul*, one who partakes of which is liable to receive *karet*. The Gemara concludes: **Conclude from** the mishna's language that this interpretation is correct.

The *Gemara* further notes that the *mishna*'s own language supports this reading: it describes the offering becoming *pasul*, disqualified, and does not use the language of *mitpagel*, becoming *pigul* — a precise linguistic signal that the *mishna* is not discussing *pigul* at all.

### Pigul Definition

*Pigul* is defined by its relationship to time and consumption. When a *kohen* performs one of the *avodot* with the intention of eating the offering, or completing a required act pertaining to the offering, after its designated time has elapsed, this intent renders the offering *pigul* — a category of disqualification that carries with it the penalty of *karet* for one who eats of the offering. *Machshavah shelo lishmah*, by contrast, means intending to bring the offering for the sake of a different sacrifice entirely — redirecting its identity rather than its timing. These two categories operate on distinct tracks, and Rabbi Akiva's teaching applies only to the domain of *pigul*, not to *shelo lishmah*.

## Pigul Source & Rules (II)

### Vayikra 7:18 & Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַחְשֶׁבֶת פִּיגּוּל לָא פָּסְלָה בְּקַבָּלָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does intent of *piggul* during** the **collection** of the blood **not disqualify** the offering?

The *Gemara* now introduces the central scriptural source for *pigul*, a verse in Vayikra 7:18: *ve'im he'achol ye'achel mibesar zevach shelamav ba'yom ha'shelishi lo yeiratzeh ha'makriv oto lo yechashev lo pigul yiheyeh ve'ha'nefesh ha'ochelet mimenu avonah tisa* — "And if the flesh of his *shelamim* sacrifice shall indeed be eaten on the third day, the one who offered it shall not be accepted; it shall not be counted for him — it is *pigul*, and the person who eats of it shall bear his iniquity."

Several features of this verse arrest the rabbinic attention. The verb for eating appears twice — *he'achol ye'achel* — a grammatical doubling that the *Gemara* will exploit. The offering in view is the *shelamim*. The operative concept is *lo yeiratzeh* — it will not be accepted, which the *Gemara* identifies with the *ritzui*, the acceptance effected through *zrikah*. And the verse establishes *pigul* as the consequence of wrong intent regarding time of consumption.

The *Gemara* understands from this verse that *pigul* applies to everything connected with *devarim ha'mevi'im lidei achilah* — acts that bring the sacrifice toward consumption. The question becomes: which acts are included, and which are excluded?

## Pigul Source & Rules (II)

### Which Avodot = Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא מַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת אֶלָּא בִּזְרִיקָה בִּלְבָד; מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת שְׁחִיטָה וְקַבָּלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו (פִּיגּוּל הוּא) בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי לֹא יֵרָצֶה״ – בִּדְבָרִים הַמְּבִיאִין לִידֵי אֲכִילָה הַכָּתוּק מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **intent** to consume an offering after its designated time **is effective** to render the offering *piggul* **only** when one has that intent **during sprinkling. From where** is it derived **to include slaughter and collection** of the blood in this *halakha*? **The verse states: "And if any of the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings is at all eaten on the third day, it shall not be accepted,** neither shall it be imputed to the one who offers it; it shall be an abhorred thing [*piggul* ]" (Leviticus 7:18). As derived on 29a, the verse is referring to performance of the sacrificial rites with intent to consume the offering after its designated time. The phrase "is at all eaten" teaches that **the verse speaks of matters that lead to consumption,** i.e., those sacrificial rites that enable consumption of the offering, including slaughter of the animal and collecting the blood.

The *beraita* begins from a position of maximal inclusion and then narrows. One might think that only *zrikah* can generate *pigul*, since it is the *zrikah* that constitutes the *ritzui*, the act of acceptance. But the *beraita* asks: from where do we include *shechitah* and *kabbalah*? The answer derives from the verse's language. Since the verse specifies that *lo yeiratzeh* — it will not be accepted — and since *ritzui* is the *zrikah*, the *zrikah* is taken as the paradigm. Every *avodah* that is *me'akevet kaparah* — that is indispensable for atonement, without which the atonement does not take effect — is included in the *pigul* framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אַף שְׁפִיכַת שִׁירַיִים וְהַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִין?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought **that I** should **include even** the **pouring of** the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar, after its sprinkling, **and** the **burning of** the **sacrificial portions** on the altar.

The verse then serves to exclude two further acts: *shefichat shirayim* (pouring the remainder of the blood on the base of the altar) and *haktarat imurim* (burning the sacrificial portions on the altar). These are not *me'akevet kaparah*; the atonement is complete without them. Therefore, an improper time-related thought during these acts does not render the offering *pigul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי לֹא יֵרָצֶה, הַמַּקְרִיב אוֹתוֹ לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב״ – זְרִיקָה בַּכְּלָל הָיְתָה, וְלָמָּה יָצְתָה? לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ. לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה זְרִיקָה מְיוּחֶדֶת – שֶׁהִיא עֲבוֹדָה (וּמְכַפֶּרֶת), וּמְעַכֶּבֶת כַּפָּרָה; אַף כׇּל – עֲבוֹדָה, וּמְעַכֶּבֶת כַּפָּרָה. יָצְאוּ שְׁפִיכַת שִׁירַיִים וְהַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִין – שֶׁאֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַכַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "On the third day, it shall not be accepted, neither shall it be imputed to the one who offers it"** (Leviticus 7:18). The phrase "the one who offers it" indicates the sprinkling of the blood. **Sprinkling was included** in the general category of rites that enable consumption. **And for what** purpose **was it singled out? To compare** other *halakhot* **to it,** i.e., **to say to you: Just as sprinkling is unique, as it is a rite and** if not performed **it prevents atonement, so too, any rite** that **prevents atonement** if not performed is included in the *halakha* of *piggul*. **Pouring** the **remainder** of the blood **and burning** the **sacrificial portions are** therefore **excluded, as they do not prevent atonement** if not performed. Evidently, intent of *piggul* applies to the collection of the blood, contrary to Rava's resolution of the contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita*.

The *ritzui* — acceptance — is thus identified specifically with *zrikah*, the sprinkling that finalizes the offering and creates the connection between the blood and the altar. Without *zrikah*, the *korban* does not atone. All four of the primary *avodot* — *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, and *zrikah* — share this quality of being indispensable to the completion of the sacrifice, and therefore all four are subject to *machshevet pigul*.

### Pigul Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דְּאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט עַל מְנָת לְקַבֵּל דָּמָהּ לְמָחָר״, הָא דְּאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי מְקַבֵּל דָּמָהּ עַל מְנָת לִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** it is **not difficult. This** statement of Rava, that intent of *piggul* does not apply to collection, is referring to a case **where** the one slaughtering the offering **says: I am hereby slaughtering** this offering **in order to collect its blood tomorrow.** Because the collection of the blood is not considered consumption, such intent does not render the offering *piggul*. **That** statement of the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the one collecting the blood **says: I am hereby collecting its blood in order to pour** the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar **tomorrow.** Since pouring the remains is considered consumption of the blood on the altar, such intent renders the offering *piggul*.

The *Gemara* then works through specific cases to illustrate the boundaries. One sage poses a question to Rava: does a wrong thought regarding *shefichat shirayim* and *haktarat imurim* not disqualify the offering at all? A *beraita* had seemed to suggest that even these acts are covered by *pigul* — so how can we say they are excluded?

Rava resolves this by distinguishing between two different scenarios. In the first case: *hareini shochet al menat lekabel damah machar* — "I am slaughtering this animal with the intention of receiving its blood tomorrow." This is a wrong thought during *shechitah* about an act (*kabbalah*) that will happen the next day. This does not constitute *machshevet pigul*, since the *kabbalah* itself is not an act of consumption. In the second case: *hareini mekabel damah al menat lishpoch shirayim lemachar* — "I am receiving the blood with the intention of pouring the remainder on the altar tomorrow." Here, the intent during *kabbalah* concerns *shefichat shirayim*, and since *shefichat shirayim* is considered a form of *achilat mizbe'ach* — the altar's consumption — this intent does render the offering *pigul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: וּשְׁפִיכַת שִׁירַיִם וְהַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִין – לָא פָּסְלָה בְּהוּ מַחְשָׁבָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **One of the Sages said to Rava: And does** prohibited **intent with regard to** the **pouring of** the **remainder** of the blood **and** the **burning of** the **sacrificial portions not disqualify** the offering, as this explanation of the *baraita* seems to indicate?

The resolution of the further difficulty — where the *Gemara* cites a *mishna* suggesting that even thoughts about *shefichat shirayim* and *haktarat imurim* are covered — proceeds similarly. The verb *achol ye'achel* appears twice in the verse, corresponding to two types of consumption: *achilat adam* (human eating of the flesh) and *achilat mizbeach* (the altar's consumption of the blood and portions). A wrong thought during *zrikah* intending to pour the remainders the next day renders the offering *pigul*, because the *zrikah* itself is *me'akevet kaparah*. But a wrong thought during the *shefichat shirayim* itself, intending to burn the *imurim* the next day, does not render it *pigul*, since *shefichat shirayim* is not *me'akevet kaparah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא מַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילַת בָּשָׂר, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת שְׁפִיכַת שִׁירַיִים וְהַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר: אַחַת אֲכִילַת אָדָם, וְאַחַת אֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **only** prohibited **intent with regard to consumption of the meat is effective** to render the offering *piggul*. **From where** is it derived **to include** the **pouring of** the **remainder** of the blood **and** the **burning of** the **sacrificial portions? The verse states: "And if** any of the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings **is at all eaten [*he'akhol ye'akhel* ]** on the third day, it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 7:18). It is derived from the repeated verb that **the verse speaks of two** types of **consumption: One is consumption** of the meat **by man,** i.e., by the priests or the owner of the offering, **and** the other **one is consumption** of the blood and the sacrificial portions **on the altar.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דְּאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי זוֹרֵק עַל מְנָת לִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר״, הָא דְּאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹפֵךְ שִׁירַיִים עַל מְנָת לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרִין לְמָחָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. This** case, where intent renders the offering *piggul*, is **where** the one sprinkling the blood **says: I am hereby sprinkling** the blood **in order to pour** the **remainder tomorrow.** Since sprinkling the blood is necessary for atonement, if it is performed with the intent that consumption occur beyond its designated time, the offering is rendered *piggul*. **That** case, where intent does not render the offering *piggul*, is **where he says: I am hereby pouring** the **remainder in order to burn** the **sacrificial portions tomorrow.** Although burning the portions is considered consumption, pouring the remainder of the blood is not indispensable for atonement, and therefore the offering is not rendered *piggul*.

In summary: two conditions must be met for *pigul* to apply. First, the improper intent must occur during one of the *avodot* that is *me'akevet kaparah* — indispensable for atonement. Second, the intent must concern the performance of an act of consumption — whether human eating of the meat or the altar's consumption of blood or portions — after the allotted time. If either condition is absent, the offering does not acquire *pigul* status.

---

## Pigul vs. Shelo Lish (II)

### Pigul & Eating Acts

The *beraita* thus establishes a coherent principle: *pigul* is generated only by thoughts that concern acts directly related to consumption — *devarim ha'mevi'im lidei achilah* — and only when those acts are themselves *me'akevet kaparah*. The *shefichat shirayim* and *haktarat imurim*, while they are acts of the altar's consumption in some sense, are not indispensable to the atonement and therefore fall outside the *pigul* framework when they are the object of an improper intent during a non-critical act. The verse's doubling of the verb of eating is the peg on which the *Gemara* hangs this entire structure.

## Inner Chatat Pigul

### R. Yehuda's Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁטְּבִילַת אֶצְבַּע מְפַגֶּלֶת בְּחַטָּאת פְּנִימִית.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says: I heard that with regard to an inner sin offering,** whose blood is sprinkled on the curtain separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, **dipping** one's **finger** in the blood in order to sprinkle it, if he does so with the intent of burning the offering on the altar the following day, **renders** the offering ***piggul*.**

The discussion now shifts to a particularly intricate case: the *chatat pnimit*, the inner sin offering whose blood is sprinkled on the curtain separating the Sanctuary from the Holy of Holies. Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Chiyya reports: *shamati she'tevilat etzba mefagelet be'chatat pnimit* — I have heard that dipping one's finger in the blood of the inner *chatat* renders it *pigul* if done with improper time-related intent. The act in question is *tevilat etzba* — dipping the finger in the blood before sprinkling it on the curtain — which is the characteristic procedure of the inner *chatat*.

Ilfa heard this teaching and reported it before Rav Padda. Rav Padda raises an immediate objection.

### Learning Pigul from Shel…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְעַהּ אִילְפָא, אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פְּדָא. אֲמַר: כְּלוּם לָמַדְנוּ פִּיגּוּל – אֶלָּא מִשְּׁלָמִים; מָה שְּׁלָמִים – אֵין טְבִילַת אֶצְבַּע מְפַגֶּלֶת בָּהֶן, אַף חַטָּאת – אֵין טְבִילַת אֶצְבַּע מְפַגֶּלֶת (בהן) [בָּהּ].
>
> **Translation:** **Ilfa heard** this statement and **said it before Rav Padda.** Rav Padda **said** in response: **Did we derive** the *halakha* of ***piggul*** with regard to all offerings **from anything but a peace offering?** The source for the *halakha* of *piggul* is the verse stated with regard to peace offerings (Leviticus 7:18). Therefore, **just as** with regard to **a peace offering,** prohibited intent while **dipping** one's **finger** in the blood to sprinkle it on the altar **does not render it *piggul*,** as there is no requirement to sprinkle the blood of a peace offering with one's finger rather than the containing vessel, **so too,** with regard to **a sin offering,** prohibited intent while **dipping** one's **finger** in the blood **does not render it *piggul*.**

Rav Padda's objection is pointed: *kelum lamadnu pigul ela mishlamim?* — Have we not derived all the laws of *pigul* from the *shelamim*, from the verse in Vayikra 7:18? And if all *pigul* law flows from that verse, then any application of *pigul* to other offerings must be derived by analogy to *shelamim*. But this creates a serious problem: in *shelamim*, there is no act of *tevilat etzba*, no dipping of the finger in the blood. The *shelamim*'s blood is collected in a vessel and sprinkled; the finger-dipping procedure simply has no parallel there. How, then, can we derive that *tevilat etzba* generates *pigul* in the inner *chatat* when there is nothing in *shelamim* with which to compare it?

This is reinforced by a further argument. If we truly learn everything from *shelamim*, then we should also learn: just as *shelamim* sacrificed *shelo lishmah* does not preclude it from becoming *pigul* — for an offering can be *shelo lishmah* and yet still be rendered *pigul* — so too a *chatat shelo lishmah* should not preclude it from becoming *pigul*. But this contradicts established *halachah*: a *chatat* sacrificed *shelo lishmah* is disqualified entirely and cannot become *pigul*. If we cannot fully learn the *chatat* from *shelamim* in this respect, how can we selectively apply the comparison only where it suits us?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הַכֹּל מִשְּׁלָמִים לָמְדוּ?! אִי, מָה שְׁלָמִים – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן אֵין מוֹצִיא מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, אַף חַטָּאת – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ אֵין מוֹצִיא מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But are all** of the *halakhot* of *piggul* **derived from** the *halakha* of **a peace offering? If** that is so, then **just as** sacrifice of **a peace offering not for its sake does not preclude** its being rendered ***piggul*,** as it is still fit, **so too,** the *halakha* should be that sacrifice of **a sin offering not for its sake does not preclude** its being rendered ***piggul*.** In fact, the *halakha* is that a sin offering sacrificed not for its sake cannot become *piggul*.

The *Gemara* raises this question and notes that it requires derivation from *ribbui de'kra'ei* — an amplification drawn from multiple verses — rather than simple analogy.

The exchanges between Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who reports having heard in a particular upper chamber that *tevilat etzba* does indeed render the inner *chatat pigul*, and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, who challenges this on precisely the grounds Rav Padda articulates, rehearse the same fundamental tension.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בַּעֲלִיָּה זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי, שֶׁטְּבִילַת אֶצְבַּע מְפַגֶּלֶת. תָּהֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כְּלוּם לָמְדוּ פִּיגּוּל – אֶלָּא מִשְּׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים – אֵין טְבִילַת אֶצְבַּע מְפַגֶּלֶת בָּהֶן, אַף חַטָּאת – אֵין טְבִילַת אֶצְבַּע מְפַגֶּלֶת (בהן) [בָּהּ]!
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: I heard in this upper story that** prohibited intent while **dipping** one's **finger** in the blood of an inner sin offering **renders** it ***piggul*. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish wondered [*tahei*] at this** *halakha*: **Did** the Sages **derive** the *halakha* of ***piggul* from anything but a peace offering? Just as** with regard to **a peace offering,** prohibited intent while **dipping** one's **finger** in the blood **does not render it *piggul*, so too,** with regard to **a sin offering,** the *halakha* should be that prohibited intent while **dipping** one's **finger** in the blood **does not render it *piggul*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הַכֹּל מִשְּׁלָמִים לָמְדוּ?! אִי, מָה שְׁלָמִים – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן אֵין מוֹצִיא מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, אַף חַטָּאת – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ אֵין מוֹצִיא מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But are all** of the *halakhot* of *piggul* **derived from** those of **a peace offering? If** so, **just as** sacrifice of **a peace offering not for its sake does not preclude** its being rendered ***piggul*, so too,** the *halakha* should be that sacrifice of **a sin offering not for its sake does not preclude** its being rendered ***piggul*.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish insists: *kelum lamdu pigul ela mishlamim?* — everything we know about *pigul* comes from *shelamim*, and since *shelamim* has no *tevilat etzba*, there is no basis for saying that *tevilat etzba* creates *pigul* in the *chatat*.

### R. Yosi's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אִין, הַכֹּל מִשְּׁלָמִים לָמְדוּ. הוֹאִיל וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ פּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלָמִים, וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ פּוֹסֵל בְּחַטָּאת; מָה חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ הַפּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלָמִים – מוֹצִיא מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, אַף שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ הַפּוֹסֵל בְּחַטָּאת – מוֹצִיאָהּ מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: Indeed, all** of the *halakhot* of *piggul*, even this one, **are derived from a peace offering. Since in** the case of **a peace offering,** intent to consume it **outside its** designated **area disqualifies** it, **and in** the case of **a sin offering,** sacrifice **not for its sake disqualifies** it, one can infer that **just as** intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area, which disqualifies a peace offering, precludes** its being rendered ***piggul*, so too,** sacrifice **not for its sake, which disqualifies a sin offering, precludes its** being rendered ***piggul*.** By contrast, dipping one's finger in the blood of an inner sin offering with intent to consume it after its designated time does not render it *piggul*, as there is no parallel *halakha* with regard to a peace offering.

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina proposes a resolution. He agrees that we learn everything from *shelamim*, but the comparison must be conducted through the lens of shared disqualifying factors. He states: *hoil ve'chutz limkomo posel be'shelamim ve'shelo lishmo posel be'chatat* — since the disqualification of *chutz limkomo* (consuming the offering outside the designated area) applies to *shelamim*, and the disqualification of *shelo lishmo* (offering not for its sake) applies to *chatat*, we can learn from one to the other through this parallel structure of disqualification.

The argument unfolds as follows: just as *chutz limkomo ha'posel be'shelamim motzi midei pigul* — the disqualification of *chutz limkomo* in *shelamim* precludes the offering from becoming *pigul* — so too *shelo lishmo ha'posel be'chatat motziah midei pigul* — the disqualification of *shelo lishmah* in *chatat* precludes the *chatat* from becoming *pigul*. The comparison works by identifying the structural parallel: each offering has a factor that disqualifies it and that, precisely because it disqualifies, removes the offering from the domain of *pigul*.

## Posel vs. Me'akev

### R. Yirmiyah's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִצִּדַּהּ תְּבָרַאּ! מָה לְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ הַפּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁלָמִים – שֶׁכֵּן נוֹהֵג בְּכׇל הַזְּבָחִים; תֹּאמַר בְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ – שֶׁאֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בְּפֶסַח וְחַטָּאת בִּלְבַד?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** This inference is **inherently disjointed: What** is notable **about** intent to consume the offering **outside its** designated **area, which disqualifies a peace offering?** It is notable **in that** its disqualification **applies to all slaughtered offerings.** This is why it precludes the offering from becoming *piggul*. Need **you say** the same **with regard to** disqualification by sacrifice **not for its sake, which applies only to a Paschal offering and a sin offering?**

Rabbi Yirmiyah, however, challenges Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina's approach from within. He argues that the comparison is structurally flawed: *mah le'chutz limkomo ha'posel be'shelamim, she'ken noheg be'chol ha'zevachim* — what is distinctive about *chutz limkomo* as a disqualification in *shelamim* is that it applies universally, to all slaughtered offerings. Every *korban* is subject to the disqualification of *chutz limkomo*. By contrast, *shelo lishmo* does not apply universally; it applies specifically to *pesach* and *chatat* alone — as we saw at the very opening of the *mishna* with which this daf began. Because the two disqualifying factors do not share the same scope of application, the analogy is imprecise, and the derivation does not hold cleanly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – דָּבָר הַפּוֹסֵל בָּהֶן מוֹצִיאָם מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, דָּבָר הַמְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן מְבִיאָן לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what have you to say** to explain this derivation? One must say that it is not based on a case-to-case comparison, but on a principle that is derived from a peace offering, namely: Any **matter that disqualifies** a peace offering, e.g., intent to consume it outside its designated area, **precludes its** being rendered ***piggul*,** and any **matter that prevents** atonement if not performed **renders** the offering ***piggul*** if performed with intent to consume it beyond its designated time.

Rabbi Yirmiyah then proposes his own framework, one that proves to be among the most conceptually important principles of the daf: *davar ha'posel bahen motzi'am midei pigul; davar ha'me'akev bahen mevi'am lidei pigul* — something that disqualifies the offering prevents it from being rendered *pigul*; something that is indispensable to the offering's completion — that impedes the *kaparah* if absent — brings the offering within the domain of *pigul*.

The logic is as follows. A *davar ha'posel* is an element whose presence or improper performance renders the offering invalid from the outset. Because the offering is already disqualified, there is no valid offering remaining upon which *pigul* can operate. *Pigul* requires a valid offering as its substrate. But a *davar ha'me'akev* — an act that is essential to the completion of the atonement, without which the *kaparah* is not effected — is precisely the kind of act during which improper intent regarding time can create *pigul*. It is indispensable, which means the offering depends on it, which means an improper thought during it can determine the offering's ultimate status.

### Inner Chatat Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי – דָּבָר הַפּוֹסֵל בָּהּ מוֹצִיא מִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, דָּבָר הַמְעַכֵּב בָּהּ מְבִיאָה לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** One infers therefore that **here too,** with regard to an inner sin offering, any **matter that disqualifies it,** e.g., sacrifice not for its sake, **precludes** its being rendered ***piggul*,** and any **matter that prevents** atonement if not performed **renders it *piggul*** if it is performed with intent to consume it beyond its designated time. Accordingly, dipping one's finger in the blood with intent to consume the offering beyond its designated time renders it *piggul*, as this rite is indispensable.

Applying this framework to the inner *chatat*: *tevilat etzba* — dipping the finger in the blood — is it a *davar ha'posel* or a *davar ha'me'akev*? Rabbi Yirmiyah's framework would generate *pigul* from it only if it is a *davar ha'me'akev*. This becomes the question driving the remainder of the analysis.

## Meal Offering Avodot

### Rav Mari's Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא – זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַקּוֹמֵץ, וְנוֹתֵן בִּכְלִי, וְהַמּוֹלִיךְ, וְהַמַּקְטִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari says: We learn** in a mishna **as well** that dipping one's finger in the blood of an inner sin offering with intent to consume it beyond its designated time renders the offering *piggul* (*Menaḥot* 12a): **This is the principle:** With regard to **anyone who removes a handful** from a meal offering, **or** who **places** the handful **in a vessel, or who conveys** the handful to the altar, **or who burns** it on the altar, if he does so with intent that the offering will be consumed after its designated time the offering is *piggul*.

Rav Mari brings a *mishna* from Menachot to bear on the question, drawing an analogy between the four *avodot* of the *minchah* (meal offering) and the four *avodot* of the animal sacrifice. The *mishna* in Menachot enumerates: *kol ha'kometz ve'noten bikli ve'hamolich ve'hamaktir* — all who take the handful, place it in a vessel, carry it, and burn it. These four acts of the *minchah* parallel the four acts of the blood offering.

The parallels are largely transparent: *kometz* corresponds to *shechitah* (both prepare the essential sacrificial substance); *molich* corresponds to *holachah* (both involve carrying toward the altar); *maktir* corresponds to *zrikah* (both are the finalizing act of the offering upon the altar).

### Noten Bikli Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא קוֹמֵץ – הַיְינוּ שׁוֹחֵט; מוֹלִיךְ – הַיְינוּ מוֹלִיךְ; מַקְטִיר נָמֵי – הַיְינוּ זוֹרֵק; אֶלָּא נוֹתֵן בִּכְלִי מַאי נִיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Since the *halakhot* of *piggul* are derived from those of a peace offering, one can ask: **Granted,** the one who **removes** a handful of a meal offering can render the offering *piggul*, as he **is** considered equivalent to the one who **slaughters** a peace offering. Just as the blood is the essential part of an animal offering, and it is removed by slaughter, so too, the handful of a meal offering is its essential part. Likewise, the one who **conveys** the handful **is** equivalent to the one who **conveys** the blood of a peace offering, and the one who **burns** the handful **is** equivalent to the one who **sprinkles** the blood **too. But** to **what is** the one who **places** the handful **in a vessel** equivalent? From where is it derived that he, too, can render the offering *piggul*?

The problematic parallel is *noten bikli* — placing the handful in a vessel. Rav Mari suggests this corresponds to *kabbalah*. But the *Gemara* challenges this: the cases are not fully comparable. In *kabbalah*, the blood flows into the vessel *memeilah* — on its own, spontaneously, once the animal is slaughtered. The *kohen* need not actively place anything; the blood comes to the vessel. In *noten bikli*, the *kohen* actively takes the *kometz* and places it into the vessel — it does not happen on its own.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא דְּדָמֵי לְקַבָּלָה – מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם מִמֵּילָא, הָכָא אִיהוּ קָא שָׁקֵיל וְרָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say that** placing the handful in a vessel is **comparable to collection** of the blood, as both rites involve placing the essential part of the offering in a vessel, one may respond: **Are** these rites actually **comparable? There,** in the case of a peace offering, the collection of the blood is performed passively. The blood spills into the vessel **by itself. Here,** in the case of a meal offering, the priest **takes** the handful himself **and places** it in a vessel. Clearly, placing the handful in a vessel is not equivalent to any specific rite in the sacrifice of a peace offering.

The *Gemara* responds that this distinction is ultimately inconsequential:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּלָא סַגִּי דְּלָא יָהֵיב – עַל כָּרְחֵיהּ עֲבוֹדָה חֲשׁוּבָה הִיא; הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא סַגִּי דְּלָא עָבֵיד לַהּ – עַל כָּרְחֵיהּ הַיְינוּ הוֹלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, since it is not sufficient** for the handful to be sacrificed **without** first **placing** it in a service vessel, **it is, perforce, an important rite.** One who performs it can therefore render the offering *piggul* like those who perform other essential rites. **Here too,** with regard to dipping one's finger in the blood of an inner sin offering, although it has no equivalent in the sacrifice of a peace offering, **since it is not sufficient** if the priest **does not perform it, it is perforce** considered part of the **conveying** of the blood, and the priest can render the offering *piggul* while performing it.

*Keivan de'idi ve'idi matan kli hu* — since in both cases the defining feature is the placement into a vessel, whether it arrives there spontaneously or is actively placed makes no difference. *Mah li memeilah, mah li ka shakil ve'yahiv* — it is the same to me whether the blood flows in by itself or whether the *kohen* takes and places it. The vessel is the common denominator.

### No Pigul for Finger Dipp…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּדָמֵי לְקַבָּלָה; וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: הָכָא מִמֵּילָא, הָתָם אִיהוּ קָא שָׁקֵיל וְיָהֵיב – כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי מַתַּן כְּלִי הוּא, מָה לִי מִמֵּילָא מָה לִי קָא שָׁקֵיל וְיָהֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No, actually,** placing the handful in a vessel is **comparable to** the **collection** of the blood. **And** despite the distinction **that you say,** namely, that **here,** in the case of a peace offering, the blood spills **by itself,** while **there,** in the case of a meal offering, the priest **takes** the handful himself **and places** it in the vessel, nevertheless, **since** both **this** rite **and that** rite involve **placement** of the essential part of the offering in **a vessel,** they are equivalent. **What** difference is it **to me** if the blood spills into the vessel **by itself,** and **what** difference is it **to me** if the priest **takes** the handful himself **and places** it in the vessel? By contrast, dipping one's finger in the blood of an inner sin offering has no equivalent in a peace offering. Therefore, *piggul* does not apply to it.

But this resolution, while it establishes the parallel between *noten bikli* and *kabbalah*, actually sharpens the problem regarding *tevilat etzba* in the inner *chatat*. The *noten bikli* of the *minchah* and the *kabbalah* of the blood offering can be compared because both involve the vessel as the defining element. *Tevilat etzba*, however — the dipping of the finger in the blood of the inner *chatat* — has no counterpart whatsoever in the *shelamim* procedure. There is no vessel, no spontaneous collection, no analogous act in the offering from which we derive all *pigul* law.

The conclusion therefore follows: since *tevilat etzba* in the inner *chatat* has no equivalent act in *shelamim*, and since all *pigul* law is derived from the *shelamim* verse, there is no basis for applying *pigul* to *tevilat etzba*. The dipping of the finger in the blood of the inner *chatat* does not render the offering *pigul*, because the act has no comparable *avodah* against which to measure it and from which the *pigul* status could be derived.
