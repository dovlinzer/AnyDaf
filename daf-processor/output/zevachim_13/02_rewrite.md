# Zevachim 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Avodot & Lishmah

### Mishna: Pesach & Chatat

The thirteenth daf of Masechet Zevachim opens with a *mishna* that reads almost like a poem in its elegant comprehensiveness. The *mishna* states that a *pesach* offering and a *chatat* that were slaughtered *shelo lishmah* — not for their own sake — and whose blood was received, carried, and sprinkled either entirely *shelo lishmah*, or partly *lishmah* and partly *shelo lishmah*, or partly *shelo lishmah* and partly *lishmah*, are *pesulim*, disqualified. The underlying principle is striking in its strictness: it does not matter in what sequence the proper and improper intentions appear. Whether the *avodah* begins properly and deteriorates, or begins improperly and is later corrected, the result is the same — the offering is invalid. Proper intent must characterize each and every one of the four *avodot* throughout.

### Four Avodot Explained

The four *avodot* under discussion are *shechitah* (slaughter), *kabbalah* (receiving the blood), *holachah* (carrying the blood to the altar), and *zrikah* (sprinkling the blood). These four acts together constitute the complete sacrificial process, and the *mishna* teaches that the disqualifying intent — *machshavah shelo lishmah* — can take effect in any one of them.

The *mishna* then illustrates what it means to have mixed intent: *lishmah* for the sake of a *pesach* and *shelo lishmah* for the sake of a *shelamim*, or the reverse — starting with *shelamim* intent and redirecting toward *pesach*. Once the intent has been misdirected, there is no rectification. The sacrifice has been disqualified.

### R. Shimon on Holachah

Rabbi Shimon dissents on one point: he rules that the *holachah* does not require *lishmah* intent in the same way the other three *avodot* do. His reasoning is rooted in a distinction between the inevitable and the contingent. *Shechitah*, *kabbalah*, and *zrikah* are inherently unavoidable — there can be no sacrifice without each of them. The *holachah*, by contrast, is avoidable. If the *kohen* slaughters the animal directly adjacent to the altar, no carrying is required at all, and the blood can be sprinkled immediately. Since it is possible to perform the sacrifice without a *holachah* at all, Rabbi Shimon reasons that an improper thought during this dispensable act should not invalidate the offering.

### R. Eliezer's Distinction

Rabbi Eliezer introduces a further nuance. In his view, a *machshavah* is *poselet* — disqualifying — only when it occurs during a *holachah* performed in the place where walking is required. If, however, the *kohen* happens to be walking in an area where no *holachah* is obligatory — perhaps traversing a route that is not part of the required path — then a problematic thought arising in that context does not disqualify the offering. Improper intent is only operative when it attaches itself to a required act; when the act itself is unnecessary in the given circumstances, the errant thought has no surface on which to take effect.

At this point, the *Gemara* signals a transition that will define much of the daf. There are, in fact, two distinct categories of problematic intent in the realm of *korbanot*. The first, *machshavah shelo lishmah*, which the *mishna* has been discussing, concerns intent directed at a different offering than the one being brought. The second, *machshevat pigul*, concerns improper intent related to time — specifically, intending to eat or otherwise complete the offering outside its designated time. These two categories are not interchangeable, and much of the *Gemara*'s analysis will depend on keeping them carefully distinguished.

## Tarfon vs. Akiva

### Kabbalah Disqualification

The *Gemara* now raises a pointed question: does the *kabbalah* — receiving the blood — actually have the capacity to disqualify an offering through improper intent? The question arises from a *beraita* that appears to complicate matters, and the *Gemara* begins its inquiry by examining the biblical source for the four *avodot* themselves.

### Deriving from Vayikra 7

The *beraita* cites a verse from Vayikra (chapter 7) that serves as the scriptural basis for the four sacrificial acts. The verse reads: *ve'shachat et ben ha'bakar lifnei Hashem ve'hikrivu bnei Aharon ha'kohanim et ha'dam ve'zarku et ha'dam al ha'mizbeach saviv asher petach Ohel Moed* — "And he shall slaughter the young bull before God, and Aaron's sons the priests shall bring the blood near and sprinkle the blood on the altar all around, at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting." Three verbs emerge from this verse: *shachat* (slaughter), *ve'hikrivu* (and they shall bring near), and *ve'zarku* (and they shall sprinkle). Two of these verbs correspond directly to two of the four *avodot*: *shechitah* and *zrikah*. But what of the other two — *kabbalah* and *holachah*?

### Hikrivu = Kabalah

The *Gemara* identifies the middle verb, *ve'hikrivu*, as corresponding to *kabalat ha'dam* — receiving the blood. The question immediately follows: how do we know that *ve'hikrivu* refers to *kabbalah* and not to *zrikah*? The *Gemara*'s answer is straightforward: since *zrikah* is explicitly mentioned later in the same verse with the word *ve'zarku*, the term *ve'hikrivu* must refer to something else — and that something else is *kabbalah*.

The *Gemara* continues, noting that the verse specifies *bnei Aharon ha'kohanim* — that the act must be performed by a fit *kohen*, one who is not a *chalal* and not a *ba'al mum*, and moreover that it must be performed with *kli sharet*, meaning the *kohen* must be wearing his priestly vestments.

Rabbi Akiva then asks: what is the source that *kabbalah* requires a fit *kohen* and priestly vestments? He draws a comparison to a verse in Bamidbar at the opening of that book, which mentions *bnei Aharon ha'kohanim ha'meshuchim asher milei yadam l'khahen* — Aaron's anointed sons who were consecrated to serve. Since that passage refers to fit *kohanim* serving in their vestments, the same requirement extends to the *kabbalah* here.

### Rashi: Kabalah & Holachah

Rashi's comment on the identification of *ve'hikrivu* as *kabbalah* is particularly illuminating. One might ask: *ve'hikrivu* could refer equally to *holachah*, the act of bringing the blood near by carrying it to the altar — after all, *holachah* also involves coming close. How do we know it refers to *kabbalah* and not *holachah*?

Rashi explains that both *kabbalah* and *holachah* share the linguistic sense of drawing near. The root of *ve'hikrivu* — *karov*, nearness — captures both acts. *Kabbalah* involves receiving the blood as it pours from the slaughtered animal, a kind of passive encounter with what comes toward you. *Holachah* is active, a deliberate movement toward the altar. Rashi notes that the *svarah* underlying the *Gemara* is that *mah she'noheg b'kabbalah noheg b'holachah* — the same principles that govern *kabbalah* govern *holachah*, because they are intrinsically linked as two expressions of the same fundamental movement of drawing near.

There is something quietly profound in this linguistic observation. Both *kabbalah* and *halachah* — the reception of tradition and the path of practice — share this quality of approaching the Divine. One is receiving; the other is moving. Rashi sees them as of a piece.

The verse's phrase *bnei Aharon ha'kohanim* applies equally to the *kabbalah*, and Rashi further notes that the requirement of a *kli sharet* can be derived from the fact that Moshe himself received blood in a vessel at Sinai, establishing the vessel as an integral part of the act.

### R. Tarfon Affirms

What follows is one of the most emotionally charged exchanges in the *Gemara*. Rabbi Tarfon, the elder, cries out: *akapeach et banai im lo shamati l'havchin hefreish bein kabbalah l'zerikah ve'ein li l'fareish* — "By my sons' lives, if I have not heard a distinction between *kabbalah* and *zrikah*, yet I cannot now articulate it." He remembers that there is a difference, but the memory has escaped him. Rabbi Akiva, the younger, responds with perhaps a touch of audacity: *mah Rabbi Akiva, ani afareish* — "What is this? I, Rabbi Akiva, will explain it."

### R. Akiva's Distinctions

Rabbi Akiva proceeds to enumerate three distinctions between *kabbalah* and *zrikah*.

The first: *kabbalah lo asah bah machshavah kema'aseh* — in the context of *kabbalah*, a wrong thought does not have the same legal force as a wrong action. *Zrikah*, by contrast, operates under the principle that *machshavah kema'aseh* — intent is treated as equivalent to action.

The second: if the *kabbalah* is performed outside the Temple precincts, there is no liability for *karet*. If the *zrikah* is performed outside, the person is liable for *karet*.

The third: if *pesulim* — disqualified persons — performed the *kabbalah*, there is no capital liability. If *pesulim* performed the *zrikah*, there is.

Rabbi Tarfon's response is extraordinary: *ha'avodah, at hitit yamin u'smol, ani shamati ve'ein li l'fareish ve'atah doreish u'mas'kim la'shmu'ah* — "By the Temple service, you have veered neither right nor left! I heard it and cannot explain it; you derive it and arrive at the same teaching." And then, in an act of profound tribute, he says: *kol ha'poreish mimcha ke'poreish mechayav* — anyone who separates from you is like one who separates from life itself.

The linguistic resonance is striking. The root *p-r-sh* appears throughout the entire passage: Rabbi Tarfon says he cannot *l'fareish*; Rabbi Akiva says *ani afareish*; and Rabbi Tarfon concludes that anyone who *poreish* from Rabbi Akiva is *poreish* from life. The word that signifies "to explain," "to articulate," "to separate" binds the entire drama together.

Yet this celebrated exchange creates a legal problem. The *mishna* stated that all four *avodot* must be performed *lishmah*, implying that a wrong thought in *kabbalah* is disqualifying. But Rabbi Akiva has just told us that *lo asah bah machshavah kema'aseh* — an improper thought in *kabbalah* is not as consequential as a wrong action. How do we reconcile the two?

## Pigul vs. Shelo Lish (II)

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves the contradiction with characteristic economy: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty at all. The two sources are simply discussing different categories of improper intent. *Kan be'machshevet pigul, kan be'machshavah shelo lishmah* — the *mishna* concerns *machshavah shelo lishmah* (intent directed at a different type of offering), while the *beraita* of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon concerns *machshevet pigul* (intent involving time-related impropriety). These are two distinct legal categories, and each has its own set of rules. The *Gemara* further notes that the *mishna*'s own language supports this reading: it describes the offering becoming *pasul*, disqualified, and does not use the language of *mitpagel*, becoming *pigul* — a precise linguistic signal that the *mishna* is not discussing *pigul* at all.

### Pigul Definition

*Pigul* is defined by its relationship to time and consumption. When a *kohen* performs one of the *avodot* with the intention of eating the offering, or completing a required act pertaining to the offering, after its designated time has elapsed, this intent renders the offering *pigul* — a category of disqualification that carries with it the penalty of *karet* for one who eats of the offering. *Machshavah shelo lishmah*, by contrast, means intending to bring the offering for the sake of a different sacrifice entirely — redirecting its identity rather than its timing. These two categories operate on distinct tracks, and Rabbi Akiva's teaching applies only to the domain of *pigul*, not to *shelo lishmah*.

---

## Pigul Source & Rules

### Vayikra 7:18 & Pigul

The *Gemara* now introduces the central scriptural source for *pigul*, a verse in Vayikra 7:18: *ve'im he'achol ye'achel mibesar zevach shelamav ba'yom ha'shelishi lo yeiratzeh ha'makriv oto lo yechashev lo pigul yiheyeh ve'ha'nefesh ha'ochelet mimenu avonah tisa* — "And if the flesh of his *shelamim* sacrifice shall indeed be eaten on the third day, the one who offered it shall not be accepted; it shall not be counted for him — it is *pigul*, and the person who eats of it shall bear his iniquity."

Several features of this verse arrest the rabbinic attention. The verb for eating appears twice — *he'achol ye'achel* — a grammatical doubling that the *Gemara* will exploit. The offering in view is the *shelamim*. The operative concept is *lo yeiratzeh* — it will not be accepted, which the *Gemara* identifies with the *ritzui*, the acceptance effected through *zrikah*. And the verse establishes *pigul* as the consequence of wrong intent regarding time of consumption.

The *Gemara* understands from this verse that *pigul* applies to everything connected with *devarim ha'mevi'im lidei achilah* — acts that bring the sacrifice toward consumption. The question becomes: which acts are included, and which are excluded?

---

## Pigul Source & Rules (II)

### Which Avodot = Pigul

The *beraita* begins from a position of maximal inclusion and then narrows. One might think that only *zrikah* can generate *pigul*, since it is the *zrikah* that constitutes the *ritzui*, the act of acceptance. But the *beraita* asks: from where do we include *shechitah* and *kabbalah*? The answer derives from the verse's language. Since the verse specifies that *lo yeiratzeh* — it will not be accepted — and since *ritzui* is the *zrikah*, the *zrikah* is taken as the paradigm. Every *avodah* that is *me'akevet kaparah* — that is indispensable for atonement, without which the atonement does not take effect — is included in the *pigul* framework.

The verse then serves to exclude two further acts: *shefichat shirayim* (pouring the remainder of the blood on the base of the altar) and *haktarat imurim* (burning the sacrificial portions on the altar). These are not *me'akevet kaparah*; the atonement is complete without them. Therefore, an improper time-related thought during these acts does not render the offering *pigul*.

The *ritzui* — acceptance — is thus identified specifically with *zrikah*, the sprinkling that finalizes the offering and creates the connection between the blood and the altar. Without *zrikah*, the *korban* does not atone. All four of the primary *avodot* — *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, and *zrikah* — share this quality of being indispensable to the completion of the sacrifice, and therefore all four are subject to *machshevet pigul*.

### Pigul Examples

The *Gemara* then works through specific cases to illustrate the boundaries. One sage poses a question to Rava: does a wrong thought regarding *shefichat shirayim* and *haktarat imurim* not disqualify the offering at all? A *beraita* had seemed to suggest that even these acts are covered by *pigul* — so how can we say they are excluded?

Rava resolves this by distinguishing between two different scenarios. In the first case: *hareini shochet al menat lekabel damah machar* — "I am slaughtering this animal with the intention of receiving its blood tomorrow." This is a wrong thought during *shechitah* about an act (*kabbalah*) that will happen the next day. This does not constitute *machshevet pigul*, since the *kabbalah* itself is not an act of consumption. In the second case: *hareini mekabel damah al menat lishpoch shirayim lemachar* — "I am receiving the blood with the intention of pouring the remainder on the altar tomorrow." Here, the intent during *kabbalah* concerns *shefichat shirayim*, and since *shefichat shirayim* is considered a form of *achilat mizbe'ach* — the altar's consumption — this intent does render the offering *pigul*.

The resolution of the further difficulty — where the *Gemara* cites a *mishna* suggesting that even thoughts about *shefichat shirayim* and *haktarat imurim* are covered — proceeds similarly. The verb *achol ye'achel* appears twice in the verse, corresponding to two types of consumption: *achilat adam* (human eating of the flesh) and *achilat mizbeach* (the altar's consumption of the blood and portions). A wrong thought during *zrikah* intending to pour the remainders the next day renders the offering *pigul*, because the *zrikah* itself is *me'akevet kaparah*. But a wrong thought during the *shefichat shirayim* itself, intending to burn the *imurim* the next day, does not render it *pigul*, since *shefichat shirayim* is not *me'akevet kaparah*.

In summary: two conditions must be met for *pigul* to apply. First, the improper intent must occur during one of the *avodot* that is *me'akevet kaparah* — indispensable for atonement. Second, the intent must concern the performance of an act of consumption — whether human eating of the meat or the altar's consumption of blood or portions — after the allotted time. If either condition is absent, the offering does not acquire *pigul* status.

---

## Pigul vs. Shelo Lish (II)

### Pigul & Eating Acts

The *beraita* thus establishes a coherent principle: *pigul* is generated only by thoughts that concern acts directly related to consumption — *devarim ha'mevi'im lidei achilah* — and only when those acts are themselves *me'akevet kaparah*. The *shefichat shirayim* and *haktarat imurim*, while they are acts of the altar's consumption in some sense, are not indispensable to the atonement and therefore fall outside the *pigul* framework when they are the object of an improper intent during a non-critical act. The verse's doubling of the verb of eating is the peg on which the *Gemara* hangs this entire structure.

## Inner Chatat Pigul

### R. Yehuda's Testimony

The discussion now shifts to a particularly intricate case: the *chatat pnimit*, the inner sin offering whose blood is sprinkled on the curtain separating the Sanctuary from the Holy of Holies. Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Chiyya reports: *shamati she'tevilat etzba mefagelet be'chatat pnimit* — I have heard that dipping one's finger in the blood of the inner *chatat* renders it *pigul* if done with improper time-related intent. The act in question is *tevilat etzba* — dipping the finger in the blood before sprinkling it on the curtain — which is the characteristic procedure of the inner *chatat*.

Ilfa heard this teaching and reported it before Rav Padda. Rav Padda raises an immediate objection.

### Learning Pigul from Shel…

Rav Padda's objection is pointed: *kelum lamadnu pigul ela mishlamim?* — Have we not derived all the laws of *pigul* from the *shelamim*, from the verse in Vayikra 7:18? And if all *pigul* law flows from that verse, then any application of *pigul* to other offerings must be derived by analogy to *shelamim*. But this creates a serious problem: in *shelamim*, there is no act of *tevilat etzba*, no dipping of the finger in the blood. The *shelamim*'s blood is collected in a vessel and sprinkled; the finger-dipping procedure simply has no parallel there. How, then, can we derive that *tevilat etzba* generates *pigul* in the inner *chatat* when there is nothing in *shelamim* with which to compare it?

This is reinforced by a further argument. If we truly learn everything from *shelamim*, then we should also learn: just as *shelamim* sacrificed *shelo lishmah* does not preclude it from becoming *pigul* — for an offering can be *shelo lishmah* and yet still be rendered *pigul* — so too a *chatat shelo lishmah* should not preclude it from becoming *pigul*. But this contradicts established *halachah*: a *chatat* sacrificed *shelo lishmah* is disqualified entirely and cannot become *pigul*. If we cannot fully learn the *chatat* from *shelamim* in this respect, how can we selectively apply the comparison only where it suits us?

The *Gemara* raises this question and notes that it requires derivation from *ribbui de'kra'ei* — an amplification drawn from multiple verses — rather than simple analogy.

The exchanges between Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who reports having heard in a particular upper chamber that *tevilat etzba* does indeed render the inner *chatat pigul*, and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, who challenges this on precisely the grounds Rav Padda articulates, rehearse the same fundamental tension. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish insists: *kelum lamdu pigul ela mishlamim?* — everything we know about *pigul* comes from *shelamim*, and since *shelamim* has no *tevilat etzba*, there is no basis for saying that *tevilat etzba* creates *pigul* in the *chatat*.

### R. Yosi's Approach

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina proposes a resolution. He agrees that we learn everything from *shelamim*, but the comparison must be conducted through the lens of shared disqualifying factors. He states: *hoil ve'chutz limkomo posel be'shelamim ve'shelo lishmo posel be'chatat* — since the disqualification of *chutz limkomo* (consuming the offering outside the designated area) applies to *shelamim*, and the disqualification of *shelo lishmo* (offering not for its sake) applies to *chatat*, we can learn from one to the other through this parallel structure of disqualification.

The argument unfolds as follows: just as *chutz limkomo ha'posel be'shelamim motzi midei pigul* — the disqualification of *chutz limkomo* in *shelamim* precludes the offering from becoming *pigul* — so too *shelo lishmo ha'posel be'chatat motziah midei pigul* — the disqualification of *shelo lishmah* in *chatat* precludes the *chatat* from becoming *pigul*. The comparison works by identifying the structural parallel: each offering has a factor that disqualifies it and that, precisely because it disqualifies, removes the offering from the domain of *pigul*.

## Posel vs. Me'akev

### R. Yirmiyah's Framework

Rabbi Yirmiyah, however, challenges Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina's approach from within. He argues that the comparison is structurally flawed: *mah le'chutz limkomo ha'posel be'shelamim, she'ken noheg be'chol ha'zevachim* — what is distinctive about *chutz limkomo* as a disqualification in *shelamim* is that it applies universally, to all slaughtered offerings. Every *korban* is subject to the disqualification of *chutz limkomo*. By contrast, *shelo lishmo* does not apply universally; it applies specifically to *pesach* and *chatat* alone — as we saw at the very opening of the *mishna* with which this daf began. Because the two disqualifying factors do not share the same scope of application, the analogy is imprecise, and the derivation does not hold cleanly.

Rabbi Yirmiyah then proposes his own framework, one that proves to be among the most conceptually important principles of the daf: *davar ha'posel bahen motzi'am midei pigul; davar ha'me'akev bahen mevi'am lidei pigul* — something that disqualifies the offering prevents it from being rendered *pigul*; something that is indispensable to the offering's completion — that impedes the *kaparah* if absent — brings the offering within the domain of *pigul*.

The logic is as follows. A *davar ha'posel* is an element whose presence or improper performance renders the offering invalid from the outset. Because the offering is already disqualified, there is no valid offering remaining upon which *pigul* can operate. *Pigul* requires a valid offering as its substrate. But a *davar ha'me'akev* — an act that is essential to the completion of the atonement, without which the *kaparah* is not effected — is precisely the kind of act during which improper intent regarding time can create *pigul*. It is indispensable, which means the offering depends on it, which means an improper thought during it can determine the offering's ultimate status.

### Inner Chatat Ruling

Applying this framework to the inner *chatat*: *tevilat etzba* — dipping the finger in the blood — is it a *davar ha'posel* or a *davar ha'me'akev*? Rabbi Yirmiyah's framework would generate *pigul* from it only if it is a *davar ha'me'akev*. This becomes the question driving the remainder of the analysis.

## Meal Offering Avodot

### Rav Mari's Analogy

Rav Mari brings a *mishna* from Menachot to bear on the question, drawing an analogy between the four *avodot* of the *minchah* (meal offering) and the four *avodot* of the animal sacrifice. The *mishna* in Menachot enumerates: *kol ha'kometz ve'noten bikli ve'hamolich ve'hamaktir* — all who take the handful, place it in a vessel, carry it, and burn it. These four acts of the *minchah* parallel the four acts of the blood offering.

The parallels are largely transparent: *kometz* corresponds to *shechitah* (both prepare the essential sacrificial substance); *molich* corresponds to *holachah* (both involve carrying toward the altar); *maktir* corresponds to *zrikah* (both are the finalizing act of the offering upon the altar).

### Noten Bikli Parallel

The problematic parallel is *noten bikli* — placing the handful in a vessel. Rav Mari suggests this corresponds to *kabbalah*. But the *Gemara* challenges this: the cases are not fully comparable. In *kabbalah*, the blood flows into the vessel *memeilah* — on its own, spontaneously, once the animal is slaughtered. The *kohen* need not actively place anything; the blood comes to the vessel. In *noten bikli*, the *kohen* actively takes the *kometz* and places it into the vessel — it does not happen on its own.

The *Gemara* responds that this distinction is ultimately inconsequential: *keivan de'idi ve'idi matan kli hu* — since in both cases the defining feature is the placement into a vessel, whether it arrives there spontaneously or is actively placed makes no difference. *Mah li memeilah, mah li ka shakil ve'yahiv* — it is the same to me whether the blood flows in by itself or whether the *kohen* takes and places it. The vessel is the common denominator.

### No Pigul for Finger Dipp…

But this resolution, while it establishes the parallel between *noten bikli* and *kabbalah*, actually sharpens the problem regarding *tevilat etzba* in the inner *chatat*. The *noten bikli* of the *minchah* and the *kabbalah* of the blood offering can be compared because both involve the vessel as the defining element. *Tevilat etzba*, however — the dipping of the finger in the blood of the inner *chatat* — has no counterpart whatsoever in the *shelamim* procedure. There is no vessel, no spontaneous collection, no analogous act in the offering from which we derive all *pigul* law.

The conclusion therefore follows: since *tevilat etzba* in the inner *chatat* has no equivalent act in *shelamim*, and since all *pigul* law is derived from the *shelamim* verse, there is no basis for applying *pigul* to *tevilat etzba*. The dipping of the finger in the blood of the inner *chatat* does not render the offering *pigul*, because the act has no comparable *avodah* against which to measure it and from which the *pigul* status could be derived.
