# Zevachim 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatas Me'akev Overview

### Perek Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מַתַּן שֶׁבַע שֶׁמְּעַכְּבוֹת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. הֵיכָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּפָרָה וּבִנְגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita* in detail. **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **I have** a derivation **only** with regard to the **seven placements** on the Curtain separating between the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, that they are indispensable, **as** these seven **are indispensable in all cases.** The Gemara asks: **Where** are the seven indispensable? **Rav Pappa says: In** the case of the red **heifer** (see Numbers 19:2–4), **and in** the purification process of one afflicted with leprous **marks** (see Leviticus 14:16).

We open on daf 40a with the words *amar mar*, picking up in the middle of unpacking a *braita*. The *perek* has been engaged throughout with the question of which applications of the *chatas* are *me'akev* — that is, which are so essential that if omitted the atonement is invalid. The governing framework is well-established: when it comes to the outer *chatas*, *Beis Hillel* holds that even a single application of blood to one corner suffices *bedi'avad*, while *Beis Shammai* requires two. But for the inner *chatas*, all authorities agree that all four corners are indispensable.

### Four Corners Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתַּן אַרְבַּע מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה״. מַאי שְׁנָא מַתַּן שֶׁבַע – דִּכְתִיבָן וּכְפִילָן, מַתַּן אַרְבַּע נָמֵי – כְּתִיבָן וּכְפִילָן!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that the same applies to the **four placements** on the inner altar? **The verse states: "So shall he do"** (Leviticus 4:20). The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **seven placements,** that they should be indispensable? If you say that the reason is **that** the seven placements **are written and repeated,** by means of the terms "And he shall do…as he did," which teaches that they are indispensable, I can say that the **four placements as well are written and repeated.** Why then should their *halakha* be any different?

The point of consensus regarding the inner *chatas* — that all four *matanot* are *me'akev* — raises an immediate exegetical question: where in the text is this derived? The Torah, when describing the *matanot* for the inner *chata'ot*, does not explicitly say "all four corners are required." It simply says to put blood on the *karnot hamizbe'ach*. The word *karnot* by itself does not necessarily imply all four.

### Scriptural Source

The *Gemara* identifies the key *pasuk* as the one governing the *Par He'elam Davar Shel Tzibbur*, where the procedure is described and then restated. The *pasuk* reads: *v'taval hakohen etzba'o min hadam v'hizah sheva pe'amim lifnei Hashem et p'nei haparoches* — seven applications toward the *paroches* — followed by the instruction to place blood *al karnot hamizbe'ach asher lifnei Hashem asher b'ohel mo'ed*, with the remaining blood poured at the base of the *mizbe'ach ha'olah*. Then comes the pivotal phrase: *v'asah lapar ka'asher asah lapar hachatas ken ya'aseh lo*, "and he shall do for this bull as he did for the sin-offering bull — thus shall he do for it."

### Par He'elam Davar

The *pshat* of this *pasuk* would seem to be a cross-reference: do for this bull what you did for that bull. But the *Gemara* reads it self-referentially — not "as you did for some other offering" but "do exactly as I have just told you to do here." The repetition (*shina hakatuv*) signals that the entire procedure must be performed precisely as stated, rendering all four *matanot* — and indeed all seven applications on the *paroches* — *me'akev*. The phrase *v'chiper aleihem hakohen v'nisla'ach lahem*, by contrast, signals that *bedi'avad* atonement is achieved even without *semicha* or the pouring of the *shirayim*. So the structure of the *pasuk* parcels out what is and is not *me'akev*: the blood applications are *me'akev*; *semicha* and *shfichat shirayim* are not.

## Seven vs. Four

### Seven Me'akev

The *braita* continues and states: *ein li ela matnot sheva she'me'akev bechol makom* — "I know only that the seven applications are *me'akev* everywhere." This implies that the seven applications of blood toward the *paroches* have a more self-evident *me'akev* status than the four applications on the *karnot*. The *braita*'s formulation, then, treats the seven as the primary category and requires a separate derivation to extend *me'akev* status to the four.

### Why Seven Privileged

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this framing: *heicha?* — on what basis does the seven have a more privileged *me'akev* status than the four? Why should the *hava amina* be that *me'akev* applies to the seven but not the four? What elevates the seven?

### Rav Pappa Answer

*Rav Pappa* answers: *b'par u'b'nega'im* — in the context of *Parah Adumah* and in the *taharah* of the *metzora*, we already know from other sources that seven sprinklings are *me'akev*. Rashi notes that by the *Parah Adumah* the *pasuk* says *v'hizah nochach p'nei ohel mo'ed* seven times, and the word *chukah* appears (*zos chukas hatorah*), indicating that it is *me'akev*. Similarly, for the *metzora*, the seven applications of oil — *v'hizah min hashemen b'etzba'o* — are accompanied by the phrase *zos tihyeh torat hametzora*, establishing their *me'akev* character. Since we know from these independent contexts that seven applications are *me'akev*, the *hava amina* naturally extends that privileged status to the seven here, before extending it to the four.

### Ken Yaase Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתַּן אַרְבַּע מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* proceeds to ask: **From where** is it derived that the **four applications** are equally *me'akev*? **The verse states: "So shall he do."**

Having established why the seven are a more natural candidate for *me'akev* status, the *braita* proceeds to ask: *matan arba minayin?* — how do I know that the four applications are equally *me'akev*? The answer is *talmud lomar ken ya'aseh* — the *pasuk*'s concluding phrase "thus shall he do." The *Gemara* reads the two phrases in the *pasuk* as carrying distinct weight: *v'asah ka'asher asah* ("he shall do as he did") teaches the *me'akev* status of the seven, while *ken ya'aseh* ("thus shall he do") is an additional phrase that teaches the *me'akev* status of the four. The phrase *v'chiper aleihem* then teaches that *bedi'avad* — even without *semicha* or the pouring of the *shirayim* — atonement is achieved.

### Both Repeated

The *Gemara* then pushes back and seems to revisit what it just answered. If the earlier body of the *pasuk* already mentions both the seven and the four applications, and then the *pasuk* concludes by saying "do as I have just told you," why single out the seven as the more natural candidate? Both are written above, and both are repeated by the concluding phrase. Why shouldn't the repetition be understood as encompassing both equally — making a separate derivation for the four unnecessary? The *Gemara* articulates this precisely: *matan arba nami ktivan v'chefilan* — the four are also "written and doubled," just as the seven are. This appears to undercut the original answer.

## Four Corners Exegesis

### R. Shimon Two Plus Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא, לְמַעְלָה אוֹמֵר: ״קֶרֶן״–״קַרְנוֹת״ – שְׁתַּיִם; לְמַטָּה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״קֶרֶן״–״קַרְנוֹת״ – אַרְבַּע. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** This **is necessary only according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that only two placements are written in this chapter, while the other two are derived through a juxtaposition. Consequently, a specific derivation is required for these. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the placements on the inner altar: **Above,** in the case of the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest, the verse **states corner** in the plural form of **corners** (see Leviticus 4:7), i.e., it says "corners [*karnot*]," in the plural, where it could have written corner, in the singular. These are **two** corners. And **below,** with regard to the bull for an unwitting communal sin, **it** again **states corner** in the plural form of **corners** (see Leviticus 4:18). Together these amount to **four** corners. This is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon.**

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty by invoking *Rabbi Yirmiyah*, who explains that the extra derivation for the four is specifically necessary according to *Rabbi Shimon*. The underlying issue is that the *Torah* never explicitly says "all four corners." It simply says *karnot hamizbe'ach*. So before we can even speak about extending the *me'akev* status to the four, we need to establish that the four in question means all four corners.

*Rabbi Shimon* addresses this through a method of textual combination. The *Gemara* cites a *braita*: *l'ma'ala hu omer keren karnot shtayim, l'mata hu omer keren karnot arba* — in the upper passage the *pasuk* uses the word *karnot* (implying a minimum of two), while in the lower passage, concerning the *Par He'elam Davar*, it again uses *karnot*. Two plus two equals four. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, the phrase *v'kein ya'aseh lo* instructs us to go back and combine the two usages of *karnot* — yielding all four corners. This is not the *pshat* of the *pasuk*, but it is how *Rabbi Shimon* arrives at the requirement for all four corners, and it is why *ken ya'aseh* is needed.

### R. Yehuda Ohel Moed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ; הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – עַל כׇּל הָאָמוּר בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, ״כֵּן יֵעָשֶׂה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This derivation **is not necessary, as it states** in these same verses: "And he shall put of the blood upon the corners of the altar which is before the Lord, which is **in the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 4:18). The superfluous expression "in the Tent of Meeting" teaches that the blood must be placed **on all** the corners of the altar **that are stated with regard to the Tent of Meeting,** i.e., on all four corners. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yehuda, what does he do with** the phrase **"so shall he do,"** which Rabbi Shimon interprets as referring to the four placements?

*Rabbi Yehuda* has no need for this two-plus-two method because he derives the requirement for all four corners through a different route. The *pasuk* says the blood is placed *al karnot hamizbe'ach asher b'ohel mo'ed* — on the corners of the altar that is in the *Ohel Mo'ed*. *Rabbi Yehuda* reads the phrase *b'ohel mo'ed* as meaning: do everything that pertains to the altar in the *Ohel Mo'ed*, which means apply blood to all of the *karnot* that are present there — all four of them. The phrase *asher b'ohel mo'ed* does the exegetical work of requiring all four corners without the need to combine two textual instances.

One might ask: what does *Rabbi Yehuda* do with the distinctive spelling of *karnot* — whether *malei* or *chaser* — that *Rabbi Shimon* uses? The *Gemara* notes that one instance is spelled with a *vav* and one without, and that this orthographic variation figures in the *drasha*. But *Rabbi Yehuda* does not rely on that method, as he derives all four from the phrase *asher b'ohel mo'ed* itself.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא לָמַדְנוּ לְפַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לִסְמִיכָה. וּשְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yehuda **requires** this verse **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **As we did not learn** with regard **to the bull of Yom Kippur that placing hands** is required, i.e., that the High Priest must place his hands on this animal before it is slaughtered, **and** likewise it is not stated that the **remainder of** its **blood** must be poured on the base of the altar. **From where** is it derived that these actions must be performed? **The verse states: "So shall he do."**

The *Gemara* now poses a challenge to *Rabbi Yehuda*: since *Rabbi Yehuda* already derives all four corners from the phrase *b'ohel mo'ed* — independently of *ken ya'aseh* — what does *ken ya'aseh* teach him? For *Rabbi Shimon*, *ken ya'aseh* was needed to produce the two-plus-two derivation. But for *Rabbi Yehuda*, that problem does not exist. The *Gemara* answers: *mibaei lei l'chid'tanya* — he needs it for what is taught in a *braita* that addresses the *Par Yom HaKippurim*. The phrase *ken ya'aseh* is not doing exegetical work on the four corners for *Rabbi Yehuda*; rather, it is the hook for a separate teaching about which elements of the procedure apply to the *par* brought on Yom Kippur — specifically, *semicha* and *shiyarei hadam*.

## Yom Kippur Par

### Yom Kippur Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְפַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לֹא לָמַדְנוּ? הָא אָמַרְתָּ: ״לַפָּר״ – זֶה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did we not learn** with regard **to the bull of Yom Kippur** that these requirements apply? **But you said** earlier in the *baraita*: **"With the bull"** (Leviticus 4:20); **this** alludes to the bull of **Yom Kippur,** which indicates that all the rites performed in connection with the bull for an unwitting communal sin apply also to the bull of Yom Kippur.

The *braita* under discussion refers explicitly to the *Par Yom HaKippurim*, and the *Gemara* turns to examine what procedure is described there and how it compares to the *Par He'elam Davar*. The text in *Acharei Mos* describes the Yom Kippur blood service in considerable detail: *v'lakach midam hapar v'hizah b'etzba'o al p'nei hakaporet keidmah v'lifnei hakaporet yazeh sheva pe'amim min hadam b'etzba'o* — first the *achas achas v'achas* sprinkling inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* on the *kaporet*, a feature absent from the *Par He'elam Davar*. Then, after the *se'ir* is slaughtered and its blood brought, it says *v'asah ka'asher asah* — he shall do as he did — linking the goat's procedure to the bull's.

The *pasuk* continues with reference to the *paroches* and the *mizbe'ach haktoret*: *v'kein ya'aseh l'ohel mo'ed hashochein itam b'soch tum'asam*, and then, after exiting, *v'yatza el hamizbe'ach asher lifnei Hashem* — which many *mefarshim* read *pshat* as the *mizbe'ach ha'olah*, though *Chazal* interpret it as the *mizbe'ach haktoret*. There, blood from both the *par* and the *se'ir* is placed on the *karnot*, and seven additional applications are made. This represents an expanded procedure relative to the *Par He'elam Davar*: more applications, a different inner architecture, and the *kaporet* inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

### Missing Elements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי – עֲבוֹדָה דִּמְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל עֲבוֹדָה דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה – אֵימָא לָא; קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The derivation from the phrase "so shall he do" **was necessary,** as it could **enter your mind to say** that **this matter,** the comparison between the bull for an unwitting communal sin and the bull of Yom Kippur, applies only to **a service that is indispensable for atonement,** e.g., the sprinkling of the blood. **But** with regard to **a service that is not indispensable for atonement,** such as placing hands on the head of the animal or pouring out the remainder of the blood, one might **say** that these actions need **not** be performed. Therefore, the verse **teaches us:** "So shall he do," i.e., these services, too, must be performed with the bull of Yom Kippur.

Against this backdrop of detailed procedure, the *Gemara* notes what is conspicuously absent from the Yom Kippur *par*'s description. Two elements mentioned in connection with other *korbanot* are never stated explicitly here: *semicha* (the laying of hands on the animal) and *shfichat shirayim* (the pouring of the remaining blood at the base of the outer *mizbe'ach*). The *pasuk* in *Acharei Mos* does say *v'hikriv Aharon es par hachatas asher lo v'chiper ba'ado uva'ad beiso*, which *Chazal* understand to include the *viduy*, but the act of *semicha* as a physical laying-on of hands is not stated. Similarly, no pouring of residual blood at the *yesod* of the outer *mizbe'ach* is mentioned. Notably, neither of these elements is *me'akev*, but they are nonetheless required as part of the procedure.

### Ken Yaase Teaching

It is for these missing elements — *semicha* and *shfichat shirayim* — that the phrase *ken ya'aseh* does its teaching work according to *Rabbi Yehuda*. The *pasuk* in the context of the *Par He'elam Davar* says: do for this *par* as was done for that *par*, do thus for it (*ken ya'aseh lo*). The plain sense (*pshat*) of that phrase is a cross-reference to another *par*. And indeed, *Rabbi Yehuda* takes it at face value as a cross-reference — but to the *Par Yom HaKippurim*, telling us: bring to this *par* everything that is said in that context. The phrase *v'kein ya'aseh lo* becomes a mechanism to import into the *Par Yom HaKippurim*'s description the elements it lacks: namely, *semicha* and *shfichat shirayim*.

The *Gemara* asks: but the *braita* used *ken ya'aseh* to teach the *me'akev* status of the four applications. How can *Rabbi Yehuda* now use the same phrase to teach about *semicha* and *shfichat shirayim*? The *Gemara* clarifies that for *Rabbi Yehuda*, the focus of this phrase is not to establish *me'akev* status for the blood applications — that he derives from *b'ohel mo'ed*. Rather, *ken ya'aseh* tells us something qualitatively different: that extra procedures not enumerated in the Yom Kippur passage are nonetheless obligatory. The *Gemara* also asks: could one have thought that *semicha* and *shfichat shirayim* simply do not apply to the *Par Yom HaKippurim* at all? Yes — the *Torah* never mentions them there. So *ken ya'aseh* teaches that they are required, even if not *me'akev*.

## Seven vs. Four…

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַסְּמִיכָה וּשְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם – דְּאַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיבָן וּכְפִילָן לָא מְעַכְּבָא, מַתַּן אַרְבַּע נָמֵי לָא תִּתְעַכַּב; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda as well,** it **was necessary** to learn from the phrase "so shall he do" that the four placements are indispensable. As it might **enter your mind to say** that **just as it is with regard to placing hands and** the **remainder of the blood, that even though they are written and repeated they are not indispensable,** so **too,** the **four placements** of blood **should not be indispensable.** Therefore, the phrase "so shall he do" **teaches us** that this is not the case, and the four sprinklings are indeed indispensable.

Having established the multiple exegetical uses of *ken ya'aseh*, the *Gemara* returns to the original question that prompted this entire inquiry: why does the *braita* treat the seven applications as a distinct category from the four, requiring a separate phrase to establish the four's *me'akev* status? The earlier back-and-forth seemed to leave this unresolved — both the seven and the four are "written and doubled" in the text, so why not simply say the concluding phrase covers both?

*Abaye* resolves this cleanly. *Salka da'atcha amina midi d'hava a'smicha v'shirei hadam d'af al gav d'chsivan v'chefilan lo m'akva* — you might have thought: since *semicha* and *shfichat shirayim* are also described above and are also "repeated" by the phrase *v'asah ka'asher asah*, yet we ultimately rule they are *not me'akev* (as the concluding *v'chiper* teaches), we cannot simply say "the repetition covers everything." We know from *v'chiper* that not everything is *me'akev*. Therefore, the repetition cannot automatically mean everything is *me'akev*. The seven, having an independent *me'akev* pedigree from *Parah Adumah* and *metzora*, are the natural first candidate. The extra phrase *ken ya'aseh* is then required to extend *me'akev* status to the four. The structure of the *braita* is thus fully justified: one phrase for the seven, one phrase for the four, and *v'chiper* for what is not *me'akev*. Once we understand that *v'chiper* is necessary to carve out exceptions, we need two phrases to establish the two categories of blood applications — and the *braita*'s formulation is exactly right.

## Yom Kippur Par…

### Gemara Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לְעַכֵּב – פְּשִׁיטָא, ״חֻקָּה״ כְּתִיבָה בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **If** it serves **to** teach that all matters stated with regard to the bull of Yom Kippur are **indispensable,** this is **obvious,** since the word **"statute" is written concerning it:** "And this shall be an everlasting statute to you" (Leviticus 16:29), and there is a principle that *halakhot* described as statutes are indispensable.

Returning to the word *lapar* in the *braita* — which teaches that the listed procedures apply to the *Par Yom HaKippurim* — the *Gemara* raises a sharp question: *ai l'akev pshita, chuka ksiva bei*. If *lapar* is coming to teach that the blood applications for Yom Kippur are *me'akev*, this is obvious — the *Torah* uses the word *chukah* in the context of Yom Kippur, and from *chukah* we derive that the procedure must be performed precisely as prescribed, rendering every element *me'akev*. Why does *lapar* need to add this?

### Rav Nachman Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: כִּי כְּתִיבָה ״חֻקָּה״ – אַדְּבָרִים הַנַּעֲשִׂים בְּבִגְדֵי לָבָן בִּפְנִים, שֶׁאִם הִקְדִּים מַעֲשֶׂה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This statement **is necessary only according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: When** the term **"statute" is written** concerning the Yom Kippur service, indicating that no details may be altered, it is written only **with regard to actions performed in white garments inside** the Holy of Holies, e.g., burning the incense and sprinkling the blood, which are the essential services of the day, and it teaches **that if** the High Priest **performed** one of the **actions before another,** i.e., not in the proper order, **he has done nothing.**

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* answers that *lapar* is necessary specifically for the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*, whose reading of *chukah* is more limited than one might assume. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that when the *Torah* writes *chukah* in the context of Yom Kippur, this applies only to *devarim hana'asim b'vigdei lavan bifnim* — the service performed inside in white garments — and specifically, *bifnim* means inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, if a kohen performed the inner service in the wrong order, *lo asa v'lo klum* — he accomplished nothing. But this stringent *me'akev* requirement, derived from *chukah*, applies only to the *Kodesh Kodashim* service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל דְּבָרִים הַנַּעֲשִׂים בְּבִגְדֵי לָבָן בַּחוּץ, הִקְדִּים מַעֲשֶׂה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי; אֵימָא מִדִּכְסִידְרָן לָא מְעַכְּבִי, הַזָּאוֹת נָמֵי לָא מְעַכְּבִי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **those actions performed in white garments outside,** in the Sanctuary, if he **performed** one **action before another, what he did is done** and he is not required to repeat the rite. Consequently, one might **say** that **from** the fact **that their order is not indispensable,** it may be derived that the **sprinklings,** i.e., placements, **themselves are also not indispensable.** Therefore, the term "with the bull" **teaches us** that the placements are indeed indispensable.

For the blood applications performed in the *Heichal* — on the *paroches* and on the *mizbe'ach haktoret* — the order is not held to the same standard by *Rabbi Yehuda*: *hikdim ma'aseh lachaveiro mah she'asa asuy*, if he performed the steps out of order in the *Heichal*, what he did is valid. Once the exact ordering is not *me'akev* outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, one might then ask: are even the individual blood applications themselves *me'akev* for *Rabbi Yehuda* in the *Heichal*? The *chukah* word does not speak to that directly. So one might say: *aima mideksidra lo me'akvi, haza'os nami lo me'akvi* — since exact order is not *me'akev* outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, perhaps even the individual applications of blood are not *me'akev* either.

To counter this, the *Torah* uses the word *lapar*, teaching that all the blood work is indeed *me'akev* even outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, at least for the *Par Yom HaKippurim*. This is needed specifically because *Rabbi Yehuda* cannot derive that from *chukah* alone.

### Rav Pappa Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נֹאמַר: אִם כִּילָּה כִּיפֵּר, וְאִם לֹא כִּילָּה לֹא כִּיפֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: For what** reason **do we not say: If he finished** the entire service, which includes sprinkling the blood in the Sanctuary and pouring the remainder of the blood onto the base of the altar, **he has facilitated atonement; and if he did not finish, he has not facilitated atonement?** This indicates that Rabbi Yehuda derives from this verse the *halakha* that the sprinklings in the Sanctuary are indispensable.

At this point, *Rav Pappa* objects: even *Rabbi Yehuda* has a source internal to the Yom Kippur passage itself. The *pasuk* says *v'chila mikaper es hakodesh* — and a *braita* quotes *Rabbi Yehuda* as saying: *im lo kila lo kiper* — only when you have completed all the *kapara* work have you achieved atonement. This teaches that all the blood applications are indispensable. *Rav Pappa* is more precise still: another formulation of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is *im kila kiper v'im lo kila lo kiper* — only when he is completely done does he achieve atonement — which could even imply that *shfichat shirayim* is *me'akev*. Either way, the blood work as a whole is *me'akev* internally from the Yom Kippur *parashah*. Why then does *lapar* need to teach this?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְאֶת (דם) [בְּדָם] וּבִטְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** This reference to the bull of Yom Kippur in the term "with the bull," **is necessary only to** apply to the bull of Yom Kippur three *halakhot* that are derived from that which is stated with regard to the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest: "And the priest shall immerse his finger [*et etzba'o*] in the blood" (Leviticus 4:6). These *halakhot* can be summarized in the shortened form: ***Et*, blood, and with immersion.**

*Rav Pappa* therefore concludes: *lo nitzrecha ela l'es b'dam uv'tvilah* — the word *lapar* is not needed to establish that the blood applications are *me'akev*; it is needed for something far more technical. Specifically, it links the *Par Yom HaKippurim* to the *par nasi*'s passage, where three distinctive words appear — *es*, *ba-dam*, and *v'taval* — each of which carries a distinct *halacha* about how the dipping of the finger in the blood is to be performed. It is these precise technical details that *lapar* imports into the Yom Kippur service.

## Technical Details

### Es = Wart Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶת״ – אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: לֹא נִצְרְכָא לְהַכְשִׁיר אַמִּין שֶׁבָּאֶצְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the word ***et*, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says:** This word **is necessary only to render fit** service performed by a priest who has **a wart** or blister **on** his **finger.** These are not considered an interposition between his finger and the blood.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* expounds the word *es* (in *es etzba'o*). As a general rule, *es* in a *pasuk* signals inclusion of something additional. Here, *es etzba'o* — literally "his finger" but with the inclusive *es* — teaches *l'hachshir amin she-ba-etzba*: even a finger covered with a wart or blister may be used for the dipping. One might have thought that only the bare skin of the finger counts as "his finger" for this purpose; the *es* teaches that even if a blister mediates between finger and blood, the dipping is valid.

### Ba'dam = Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּדָּם״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא בַּדָּם שִׁיעוּר טְבִילָה מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the term **"in the blood"** (Leviticus 4:6), this teaches **that the blood** in the service vessel **must be of a** sufficient **measure for immersion from the outset.**

The word *ba-dam* — "in the blood" — teaches *she-yehei ba-dam shiur tvila me-ikara*: the requisite volume of blood must be present from the outset, at the time of the initial collection. A kohen cannot collect a small amount of blood in one vessel and a separate small amount in another and then combine them, reaching the necessary *shiur* only after the fact. The blood must already contain the sufficient volume when it is first gathered.

### V'taval = Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְטָבַל״ – וְלֹא מְסַפֵּג.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, the term **"and** the priest **shall immerse** his finger in the blood" indicates that there must be enough blood in the vessel such that the priest can immerse his finger in it **and not** have to **wipe** the sides of the utensil to collect blood for sprinkling.

The word *v'taval* — "and he shall dip" — teaches *v'lo mesafeg*: genuine immersion is required. It is insufficient to merely mop up blood from the inner edges or lip of the vessel. The finger must be dipped into the body of the liquid.

### Both Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״בַּדָּם״, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְטָבַל״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא שִׁיעוּר טְבִילָה מֵעִיקָּרָא; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And** it **was necessary** for the verse to state both these last two terms. It had **to write "in the blood," as had the Merciful One written** only **"and** the priest **shall immerse," I would say** that the sprinklings are valid **even if there was not** a sufficient **measure** of blood **for immersion from the outset,** but only enough for a single sprinkling, provided that the priest then added more blood to the vessel for each sprinkling. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "in the blood"** to teach that from the outset there must be enough blood in the vessel for all the sprinklings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּדָּם״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ מְסַפֵּג; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְטָבַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **had the Merciful One written** only **"in the blood," I would say** that **even** if there was enough blood in the vessel at the outset it is not necessary that there be enough blood for immersion for the last sprinklings, as the priest can **wipe** the sides of the utensil to collect blood for sprinkling. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "and** the priest **shall immerse."**

The *Gemara* notes that both *ba-dam* and *v'taval* are necessary, as neither alone would suffice to convey both rulings. Had the *Torah* written only *v'taval*, one might have thought that as long as the finger is immersed at the moment of dipping, the blood need not have been collected at the proper volume from the outset — perhaps later mixing is acceptable. The word *ba-dam* closes that possibility. Conversely, had the *Torah* written only *ba-dam* — ensuring the right amount of blood — one might have thought that mopping up at the vessel's edge suffices as long as the blood was collected properly. The word *v'taval* closes that possibility. Both are required, and it is through *lapar* that these three words (*es*, *ba-dam*, *v'taval*) — which appear explicitly only in the context of the *par* described earlier — are applied to the *Par Yom HaKippurim*.

## Incense Altar

### Incense Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִזְבַּח קְטֹרֶת סַמִּים״ לְמָה לִי? שֶׁאִם לֹא נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים – לֹא הָיָה מַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara addresses another apparently superfluous phrase in the same chapter: "And the priest shall put some of the blood upon the corners of **the altar of sweet incense** before the Lord" (Leviticus 4:7). **Why do I** need the verse to mention the sweet incense? It would have been enough to identify the altar as being "before the Lord" and one would have understood that the reference is to the inner altar. Rather, this serves to teach **that if the altar had not been inaugurated with sweet incense,** the priest **would not sprinkle** blood on it.

Having explored what *lapar* teaches, the *Gemara* pauses to address a separate question about the *pasuk* describing where blood is applied on the inner *mizbe'ach*. The verse says *v'natan hakohen min hadam al karnot mizbe'ach ketoret hasamim lifnei Hashem asher b'ohel mo'ed* — it specifies not merely "the altar that is in the *Ohel Mo'ed*" but "the altar of *ketoret hasamim*." The phrase *asher b'ohel mo'ed* was already used to derive the requirement for all four *karnot* (according to *Rabbi Yehuda*) and the need for the roof to remain intact (according to *Rabbi Shimon*). Why the additional specification of *ketoret hasamim*?

The *Gemara* answers: *she'im lo nitchanech hamizbe'ach biketoret hasamim lo haya mazeh* — if the *mizbe'ach haktoret* has not yet been inaugurated through the offering of incense upon it, blood may not be applied to it in this ceremony. The altar must have been inducted into service specifically through *ketores*, and a brand-new altar that has never had incense burned upon it cannot serve as the recipient of this blood application.

## R. Yishmael Kal Vachomer

### No Word Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: קַל וָחוֹמֶר! וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא הוּשְׁוָה קׇרְבָּן לְקׇרְבָּן – הִשְׁוָה מַעֲשִׂים לְמַעֲשִׂים; מָקוֹם שֶׁהִשְׁוָה קׇרְבָּן לְקׇרְבָּן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיַּשְׁוֶה מַעֲשֶׂה לְמַעֲשֶׂה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael said:** This inclusion is unnecessary, as these *halakhot* can be derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as in a case in which** one **offering is not equated with** another **offering** of a different type of animal, the Torah **equated** the **actions** of sprinkling the blood in one offering **with** the **actions** of the blood in the other offering, as will be explained, in **a case in which** the Torah **equated** one **offering with** another **offering,** i.e., the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest and the bull of Yom Kippur, **isn't it logical that** the Torah **should equate** the **actions** of sprinkling the blood in the one offering **with** the **actions** of the blood in the other offering? Therefore, the derivation by way of a special inclusion is not necessary.

*Rabbi Yishmael* disputes the need for the word *lapar* to teach that the Yom Kippur *par*'s procedures parallel those of the *Par He'elam Davar*. In his view, a *kal vachomer* accomplishes this without requiring any additional word. The structure of the *kal vachomer*: *u'mah bimkom shelo hushvu korban l'korban hushvu ma'asim l'ma'asim, makom shehushva korban l'korban eino din sheyishvu ma'asim l'ma'asim?* — If in a situation where the animals are different, the procedures are nonetheless the same, then in a situation where the animals are the same (*par* and *par*), certainly the procedures should be the same.

If this *kal vachomer* suffices, then *lapar* does not need to teach the application to Yom Kippur. What, then, does *lapar* teach? *Rabbi Yishmael* reads the word *lapar* as linking the *Par He'elam Davar* to the *Par Kohen Mashiach* (not, it should be noted, the *par nasi* — the *nasi* brings a *se'ir*, not a *par*). The word *lapar* (and the parallel *velapar*) is doing something to combine or link these two types of *par* — a point to be fully elaborated.

### Kal Vachomer Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא פַּר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים וּשְׂעִיר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן נִכְנָס דָּמָם לִפְנַיי וְלִפְנִים!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that Rabbi Yishmael is referring to **the bull of Yom Kippur and the goat of Yom Kippur,** and from them he derives by way of an *a fortiori* inference that the actions concerning the bull of Yom Kippur are the same as those concerning the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest, which are the same animal, this **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is notable **about these** offerings, the bull and the goat of Yom Kippur? They are notable **in that their blood enters the innermost sanctum,** the Holy of Holies. This is not so of the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest, the blood of which is sprinkled only in the outer area of the Sanctuary.

The *Gemara* unpacks the premise of *Rabbi Yishmael*'s *kal vachomer*: which case demonstrates that when animals differ the procedures are the same? One might suggest: the *par* and the *se'ir* of Yom Kippur itself — animals are different, procedures are stated to be the same. But the *Gemara* rejects this as a basis for the *kal vachomer*: *mah lehanach sheken mechapperim al averat mitzvat yedu'ah* — these offerings share the characteristic of atoning for a known sin (*cheit shehu yedu'ah*), whereas the Yom Kippur *par* addresses inadvertent sin tied to concealed *tumah* as described in *Shevuot*. The similarity in what they atone for could explain the procedural overlap, making this a weaker proof.

### Overlapping Procedures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פַּר הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר שֶׁל צִבּוּר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא הוּשְׁווּ קׇרְבָּן לְקׇרְבָּן – דְּהַאי פַּר וְהַאי שָׂעִיר, הוּשְׁווּ מַעֲשִׂים לְמַעֲשִׂים – לְמַאי דִּכְתַב בְּהוּ; מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּשְׁוָה קׇרְבָּן לְקׇרְבָּן – דְּהַאי פַּר וְהַאי פַּר, אֵינוֹ דִּין
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reference here is to **the bull for an unwitting communal sin and the goat of Yom Kippur, and this** is what Rabbi Yishmael **is saying: Just as in a case in which** one **offering is not equated with** another **offering, as this** is **a bull and that** is **a goat,** nevertheless the **actions** of sprinkling the blood in one offering **are equated with** the **actions** of the blood in the other offering **with regard to that which is written concerning it,** in **a case in which** one **offering is equated with** another **offering,** i.e., the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest and the bull of Yom Kippur, **as this** is **a bull and that** is **a bull, isn't it logical**

The *Gemara* therefore identifies the correct pair for the *kal vachomer*'s premise: the *Par He'elam Davar Shel Tzibbur* and the *Se'ir Yom HaKippurim*. These are different animals — one a bull, one a goat — yet the procedures show substantial overlap. The *Gemara* clarifies with *vehachi ka'amar*: the argument is not that the procedures are entirely identical, since the Yom Kippur *par* involves applications inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* and the *kaporet* that are absent from the *Par He'elam Davar*, while the *Se'ir Yom HaKippurim* similarly has additional elements. Rather, in the areas where their procedures *do* overlap — the seven applications and the four — they are the same, even though the animals are different. As Rashi notes, the Yom Kippur procedures constitute a superset; what is present in the *Par He'elam Davar* is also present there.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲתָא לֵיהּ פַּר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים (נָמֵי) לְאֶת בְּדָם וּטְבִילָה מִפַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ; וְאָתֵי לֵיהּ שְׂעִיר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מִשְּׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** by means of this *a fortiori* inference the case of **the bull of Yom Kippur is derived from** the case of **the bull of** an unwitting sin of the **anointed priest, with regard to** the *halakhot* summarized by the words ***et*, in the blood, and immersion,** as they are both bulls. **And** likewise, the case of **the goat of Yom Kippur is derived from** the case of **the goats for** an unwitting communal sin of **idol worship,** with regard to the *halakhot* of *et*, in the blood, and immersion, **via** this ***a fortiori* inference,** as they are both goats.

From this foundation, *Rabbi Yishmael* builds the *kal vachomer*: since even where the *korbanot* differ (*par* and *se'ir*), the overlapping procedures are identical, certainly where the *korbanot* are the same (two *parim*), the procedures should be fully equivalent. This then allows one to apply to the *Par Yom HaKippurim* the technical *halachot* of *es*, *ba-dam*, and *v'taval* that were explicit in the passage of the *par* under discussion — without needing the word *lapar* to do that work. The *Gemara* summarizes: *v'asi lei par Yom HaKippurim l'es dam tvila mi'par Kohen Mashiach* — the *Par Yom HaKippurim* receives the *es*, *dam*, and *tvila* rules from the *Par Kohen Mashiach* via this *kal vachomer*.

### Second Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר?! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: קָסָבַר תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can the *halakhot* of the goat of Yom Kippur be derived from the goats for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship? **But does a matter derived by juxtaposition,** i.e., a *halakha* that is not written explicitly in the Torah but that is learned by means of a comparison, **again teach by *a fortiori*** inference? There is a principle that a *halakha* derived by juxtaposition with regard to consecrated matters cannot subsequently teach another *halakha* via an *a fortiori* inference. The *halakhot* alluded to by the words *et*, in the blood, and immersion, are not explicitly stated with regard to the goats of an unwitting communal sin of idol worship. Rather, they are derived from the comparison found in the verse: "And he shall do…as he did" (Leviticus 4:20), which alludes to the goats for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship and the bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest. **Rav Pappa said: The school of Rabbi Yishmael maintains** that **a matter derived by juxtaposition does again teach by *a fortiori*** inference, even with regard to consecrated matters.

This accounts for the *par*, but the *Se'ir Yom HaKippurim* remains. One might expect the *kal vachomer* to cover the goat as well, since the goat was itself part of the premise — it was the *Se'ir Yom HaKippurim* that demonstrated that differing animals can share procedures with the *Par He'elam Davar*. Yet the *Gemara* does not apply the extra *halachot* to the goat through this *kal vachomer*. Instead, it invokes a separate route: *v'asi lei se'ir Yom HaKippurim mi'se'irei avodat kochavim mi'kal vachomer* — the *Se'ir Yom HaKippurim* receives these *halachot* from the *se'irei avodat kochavim* (the inner *chatas* goats brought for communal idolatrous sin) by way of another *kal vachomer*. The logic runs: goat and goat — when the animals are the same, certainly the procedures are the same. The details of how *es*, *ba-dam*, and *v'taval* are established by the *se'irei avodat kochavim* remain to be worked out; this is what the *Gemara* will address on the following *daf*.

### Hekesh Kal Vachomer

The *Gemara* raises a formal objection to this chain of reasoning. The technical *halachot* of *es*, *ba-dam*, and *v'taval* for the *se'irei avodat kochavim* are themselves derived through a *hekesh* — a textual linkage to another source. The question is then: *v'chi davar halamad b'hekesh chozer u'melamed b'kal vachomer?* — Can something learned through a *hekesh* turn around and serve as the basis for teaching elsewhere through a *kal vachomer*? The standard principle is that it cannot; a *hekesh* derivation has limited transferability.

*Rav Pappa* resolves this by stating that *tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* — the school of *Rabbi Yishmael* — holds the position that *davar ha'lamed b'hekesh chozer u'melamed b'kal vachomer*: yes, something derived through a *hekesh* can subsequently teach elsewhere through a *kal vachomer*. This is a disputed methodological principle, and *Rabbi Yishmael*'s school takes the permissive position.

The *Gemara* thus ends on this note. Two threads remain open for the following *daf*: first, how exactly are the *halachot* of *es*, *ba-dam*, and *v'taval* established for the *se'irei avodat kochavim*; and second, what precisely does *lapar* accomplish according to *Rabbi Yishmael* — if not applying procedures to the *Par Yom HaKippurim*, then what is the linkage between the *Par He'elam Davar* and the *Par Kohen Mashiach* that the word is establishing?
