# Avodah Zarah 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jewish Doctor & Life…

### Medical Risk & Pikuach N…

The daf is 28, and we pick up at the very bottom of 27b. The preceding discussion had established the *gemara*'s grave concern about seeking medical care from a non-Jewish physician. The *gemara* operated on the assumption — whether reflecting actual reality or the perception of reality — that the danger was real enough to warrant the following rule: unless one is in a situation in which one believes one will certainly die without medical intervention, it is preferable not to seek treatment from a non-Jewish doctor rather than risk the danger of doing so. The *gemara* thus regarded this as a matter of the gravest concern. There had also been a fascinating discussion about heresy, touching on whether even the fear of death would not override the prohibition given that danger, and a critical discussion about *chayei sha'ah* — the question of whether a terminally ill person may take certain risks when balancing the protection of the short remaining time of life against undergoing a treatment that might extend it. This is obviously of enormous importance for contemporary *halachic* questions in medical *halacha*.

### Wounds & Shabbat Violati…

With that context in mind, the *gemara* picks up two lines from the bottom of the previous amud. *Amar Rava bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: kol maka she-mechalelin aleha es ha-Shabbos, ein misrapin bahen.* Rava bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: any wound for which one may violate *Shabbos* — that is, any wound that represents a danger to life, as measured by the standard of *chillul Shabbos* — one may not seek treatment for that wound from a non-Jewish doctor. The concern, presumably, is not merely that the doctor will exacerbate the condition but perhaps that he will fail to provide sufficient care. The prohibition applies specifically to life-threatening conditions.

The last line of 27b then offers an alternative formulation: *Ve-ika d'amri amar Rava bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: kol maka shel challal, ein misrapin mehen.* According to this version, the rule is framed differently — it is not wounds for which one may violate *Shabbos*, but rather internal wounds (*maka shel challal*) for which one may not seek treatment from a non-Jewish physician. The *gemara* immediately asks *mai beinaihu* — what practical difference exists between these two formulations? Since in general any internal wound is one that represents a life-threatening danger, the two versions seem to say the same thing. They are, effectively, two ways of expressing the same prohibition.

### Internal vs. External Wo…

The *gemara* answers: *ika beinaihu gav yadei ve-gav raglei* — the difference lies in a wound on the back of the hand or the back of the leg. Such a wound is not technically an internal wound, and yet it is the sort of condition for which one may violate *Shabbos*. This raises an obvious question: if it is the kind of wound that may require *chillul Shabbos* because it represents a possible risk to life, why would one be permitted to go to a non-Jewish doctor for it, given the concern that the doctor may exacerbate it or fail to treat it adequately?

Rashi explains that because it is an external wound, one can observe it visually. If it begins to worsen, one can still seek alternative treatment or apply a salve in time. Since it is external, even though it represents a potentially serious danger, one need not fear dying in the doctor's office; one retains the ability to monitor the situation and respond. The concern is not that something irreversible will happen without one's knowledge, as it might with an internal wound.

It is worth flagging a tension that this passage creates with the *gemara* on the preceding daf. Yesterday's *gemara* included a statement from Rav Yehuda to the effect that he would not go to a non-Jewish doctor even for a minor ache from a bloodletting wound — and the *gemara* appeared to use that as evidence that the prohibition applies even where there is no life-threatening concern. The present *gemara* seems to operate on an entirely different premise: that it is only where there is a life-threatening danger that the prohibition obtains, and for minor conditions, going to a non-Jewish doctor would be permissible. *Tosafos* attempts to reconcile the two *gemaros*, but the simpler reading is that they reflect different approaches: one position holds that one may never go to a non-Jewish doctor for any ailment, while the other restricts the prohibition to life-threatening conditions alone.

### Assessment as Life Danger

The *gemara* continues: *Amar Rav Zutra bar Tuvia amar Rav: kol maka she-tzricha omed, mechallalin aleha es ha-Shabbos.* Rav Zutra bar Tuvia said in the name of Rav: any wound that requires assessment — that is, any situation in which one must evaluate whether it might be life-threatening — one violates *Shabbos* for it. The principle being articulated is that *safek pikuach nefesh mechallalin es ha-Shabbos*: even uncertainty as to whether a condition falls into the category of life-threatening is itself sufficient grounds to violate *Shabbos*. The very need to assess the situation — to ask, is this dangerous or is it not? — is already enough. The requirement of assessment is itself the indicator.

### High Fever & Pikuach Nef…

Rav Zutra's principle was not an abstract one, as the *gemara* immediately provides a concrete illustration: *Ve-hai eshta tzmireta, ke-maka shel challal dami, mechallalin aleha es ha-Shabbos* — this very high fever is like an internal injury, and one violates *Shabbos* for it, as Rav Shemen bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. The example of the high fever was not chosen arbitrarily; it is precisely the kind of condition that requires assessment. A fever of 104 or 105 degrees causes great alarm — sometimes a physician will determine that it is not dangerous, while other times the patient must go to an emergency room immediately. Since such a fever falls into the category of something requiring evaluation, it is treated like an internal injury, and *Shabbos* may be violated.

This raises a fascinating question that *poskim* have grappled with: what about conditions that are listed in the *gemara* as grounds for *chillul Shabbos* but that we today do not regard as life-threatening? Conversely, what about conditions we know today to be life-threatening but that the *gemara* does not mention? Some *poskim* hold that if the *gemara* explicitly names something, one may violate *Shabbos* for it. Others are more hesitant. In any event, high fevers can certainly be very dangerous, and the principle stated here is clear.

## Teeth & Gums Classificat…

### Internal Wound Definition

Having established the general framework for what constitutes a *maka shel challal*, the *gemara* now asks: *Me-heichan maka shel challal?* — from where does an internal wound begin? The answer is given by Rabbi Ammi: *min ha-safah u-lefanim* — from the lips inward. Everything inside the mouth is classified as internal.

### Teeth & Gums Debate

The *gemara* immediately raises a more specific question: *Ba'ei Rabbi Elazar: kachi ve-shinei mai?* — what is the law with respect to the teeth and the gums? *Shinei* refers to the teeth. *Kachi*, according to Rashi, refers to the gums or the interior portion of the jaw in which the teeth are embedded. Tosafos, by contrast, understands *kachi* as referring to a different type of tooth — perhaps the molars — such that the question involves two categories of teeth. So according to Rashi the question is about the gums and the teeth, and according to Tosafos it concerns two types of teeth.

The substance of the question is as follows: *Keivan de-akushye ninhu, ke-maka de-barai damu* — since the teeth and gums are hard, perhaps they are to be treated as external, since they are solid structures and infection there is unlikely to spread to the internal organs. *O dilma keivan de-gevai kaimei, ke-maka shel challal damu* — or perhaps, since they are located inside the body, they should be classified as internal, since an infection might spread inward and affect the internal organs.

### Toothache & Vinegar

Abaye proposes a resolution: *Ta shma. Ha-choshesh be-shinav, lo yigma bahen es ha-chometz* — come and hear. If one has a toothache, one may not swish or swallow vinegar to dull the pain. The significance of this ruling lies in what it tells us about the category of toothache. The *gemara* has three functional tiers of illness: first, *safek pikuach nefesh* or outright *pikuach nefesh*, which overrides even biblical prohibitions on *Shabbos*; second, *choleh kol ha-guf*, a systemic illness that, while not life-threatening, is severe enough that the sufferer would want to lie down — for which *derabanan* prohibitions and even *amira l'goy* may be permitted, though biblical violations may not be; and third, *meichush b'alma*, a minor ache or discomfort, such as an ordinary headache or toothache, for which even otherwise permissible treatments are rabbinically forbidden on *Shabbos*. The remarkable feature of this third category is that the prohibition runs in the opposite direction from what one might expect: rather than permitting things that would normally be forbidden, it prohibits things that would normally be permitted.

The reason behind the prohibition in the case of *meichush b'alma* is the rabbinic concern about *shechikas samamanin* — the grinding of medicinal substances. In the ancient world, one did not simply open a medicine cabinet; one had to grind raw materials to produce remedies. The Rabbis therefore prohibited medical treatment for minor ailments on *Shabbos* to forestall this activity. The unfortunate cost is that the sufferer must endure discomfort. Many contemporary *poskim*, recognizing that this reason is no longer operative since medicines come pre-prepared in bottles, seek to narrow the category of *meichush b'alma* as much as possible, arguing that almost no one today falls squarely within it.

Returning to the *gemara*'s proof attempt: the ruling that one may not swish vinegar for a toothache places an ordinary toothache (*choshesh*) in the *meichush b'alma* category, where medical treatment is forbidden. The *gemara* then notes: *Ha ke'ev lei tuva, shapir dami* — if, however, it is seriously painful, one may swallow the vinegar. But this only demonstrates that a serious toothache falls into the intermediate *choleh kol ha-guf* category — where permissible treatments are allowed — not that it constitutes *pikuach nefesh*. The question of whether a dental condition rises to the level of *pikuach nefesh* remains unresolved.

The *gemara* itself also pushes back on Abaye's proposed proof for a different reason: *dilma tanna heicha de-ke'ev lei tuva, choshesh nami kari lei* — perhaps the *tanna*'s use of the word "toothache" (*choshesh*) already encompasses serious dental pain, and the prohibition applies even then. One cannot determine from the *tanna*'s language where exactly the line falls between the ordinary case and the severe case. The attempted proof thus fails on its own terms as well.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Cure

The *gemara* next brings a narrative proof: *Rabbi Yochanan chash b'tzaftina* — Rabbi Yochanan suffered from *tzaftina*, a particular dental or gum disease. *Azal le-gabbei d'hahu matrunisa* — he went to a non-Jewish noblewoman (*matrunisa*). She treated him on Thursday and Friday — a course of treatment over two days. He said to her: *Le-machar mai* — I cannot come to you tomorrow, which is *Shabbos*. What should I do? She replied: *Lo tzrichat* — you will not need it; two days of treatment is sufficient. He pressed her: *Ei tzrichna, mai* — and if I do find I do need it? She responded: *Ishtava li de-lo maglina lah* — swear to me that you will not reveal this secret remedy, for it is her livelihood. He swore: *Le-Elohei Yisrael lo maglina* — to the God of Israel, I will not reveal it. She then disclosed the remedy to him.

### Oath & Revelation

The very next day, which was *Shabbos*, Rabbi Yochanan went before the entire community — *nafik darsha b'pirka* — and publicly announced the remedy, evidently understanding part of a rabbi's responsibility to extend not only to Torah teaching but to the health of the community.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *Ve-ha ishtava lah* — but he had sworn to her! How could he do this? The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Yochanan had a technical interpretation in mind: *Le-Elohei Yisrael lo maglina, aval le-ammei Yisrael maglina* — I swore not to reveal it to the God of Israel, but to the nation of Israel I will reveal it. This is a transparently strained reading of his oath, akin to the famously clever response of *Anochi Esav bechorecha*.

The *gemara* then raises a further objection: *Ve-ha ika chillul Hashem* — even if he was technically not violating the oath, is this not a desecration of God's name? Would she not think that a Jew had taken an oath in God's name and broken it? The *gemara* responds: *De-galya leh mei'ikara* — he had disclosed the clever interpretation of his oath to her beforehand, before revealing the remedy, so that she understood he was not actually violating it. Once she understood that his oath was technically fulfilled, there was no *chillul Hashem*.

One finds this resolution somewhat difficult to accept at face value. The fact that a Jew employed deceptive language that sounded like a firm promise — in order to extract a secret and then publicize it — would seem itself to constitute a *chillul Hashem*, quite apart from whether she thought the oath was technically broken. The *gemara*'s position seems to be that once she understands no oath was violated, the concern disappears entirely. That is genuinely difficult to explain. The notes to the text record two traditions about the woman's ultimate response: some say she converted to Judaism, recognizing that these Jews had a kind of ingenuity and that Rabbi Yochanan had publicized the remedy freely when he could have charged for it; others say she took her own life upon losing control of her livelihood.

The *gemara* now notes the halakhic implication of the narrative: *alma ke-maka shel challal damya* — evidently, *tzaftina* is classified as an internal wound (*maka shel challal*). The reason is that Rabbi Yochanan was asking what he could do on *Shabbos*, meaning he was prepared to perform the treatment on *Shabbos*, which implies it was in the category of *pikuach nefesh*. This is a striking moment in the *gemara*: it appears to skip entirely over the intermediate category of *choleh kol ha-guf*, for which only *derabanan* violations — not biblical ones — might be permitted. The *gemara* seems to infer directly from the fact that something may be done on *Shabbos* that it is *pikuach nefesh*, collapsing the intermediate tier. This is a consistent feature of the present *gemara*'s framework and deserves attention.

### Tzaftina & Internal Dang…

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak immediately qualifies the inference: *Shani tzaftina* — *tzaftina* is different from other dental conditions, and one cannot derive from it that all problems with teeth and gums are classified as *pikuach nefesh*. Why is *tzaftina* different? *Ho'il u-matchil be-feh ve-gomer bi-vnei mei'ayim* — because it begins in the mouth and ends in the internal organs; it can spread from the gums deep into the body. This specific disease is therefore genuinely internal in character, even though it presents in the mouth. A general inflammation of the gums or an ordinary toothache, however serious, does not necessarily carry this risk of systemic spread.

### Tzaftina Symptoms

*Mai simanei* — what are the identifying signs of *tzaftina*? When one places something between the teeth or gums, blood emerges from between the rows of teeth — bleeding gums are the hallmark symptom. As for its causes, the *gemara* offers several: eating very cold wheat (*karirei karirei de-chitei*), very hot barley (*chamimei chamimei de-sari*), or consuming the leftovers and dregs of a fish hash (*shi-yurei kasa de-harsana*). A contemporary note observes that a diet comprised exclusively of grain-based foods and fish would be severely deficient in vitamin C, producing symptoms consistent with scurvy. This is a suggestive parallel: scurvy causes bleeding gums, is systemic in its effects, and is caused by nutritional deficiency — all of which aligns with the *gemara*'s description of *tzaftina* as a disease that begins in the mouth and spreads internally, and that is caused by limited dietary variety.

### Tzaftina Treatments

*Mai avta lei* — and what was the treatment she had given him? Now that Rabbi Yochanan had made it public, the *gemara* records the remedy. Rav Acha the son of Rava says: *mei se'ora u-shemen zayis u-melach* — water from sourdough, olive oil, and salt, rubbed on the affected area. Mar bar Rav Ashi says: *mishcha de-avza be-gadfa de-avza* — rendered goose fat, applied using a goose feather wing. Abaye then says: *ana avdi kulhu ve-lo itzsai* — I tried all of those remedies and was not cured, *ad de-amar li hahu tayya* — until a certain nomad told him: take an olive pit that is not yet a third ripe, grill it on a new shovel, and press it against the affected area. *Avdi hachi ve-itzsai* — I did this and was cured.

## Expert Doctor Exception

### Standard Prohibition

Now the *gemara* circles back to ask the more fundamental question: *Heichi avid hachi?* — how was Rabbi Yochanan able to go to a non-Jewish doctor in the first place? We have already established, in the name of Rabba bar bar Chana quoting Rabbi Yochanan himself, that for any condition that would permit *chillul Shabbos*, one may not seek treatment from a non-Jewish physician. And *tzaftina* has just been established as such a condition. So how did Rabbi Yochanan go to this non-Jewish woman?

### Important Persons Except…

The first answer offered is: *Adam chashuv shani* — an important person is different. Just as we had seen on 25b regarding an *isha chashuva*, where an important woman need not fear being killed, so too an important person known to the secular government will be protected; those treating him will be careful with him. His prominence acts as a safeguard.

### Rabbi Abbahu & Heretic

The *gemara* immediately challenges this answer with a counterexample: *Ve-ha Rabbi Abbahu de-adam chashuv hu* — Rabbi Abbahu was himself a man of great prominence and closely connected to the Roman government. Yet *ve-ramya lei Yaakov Mina* — this Yaakov the heretic, a disciple associated with *Yeshu ha-Notzri*, placed a harmful salve (*sama*) on his calf. *Ve-ilu Rabbi Ami ve-Rabbi Assi de-lo chachuhu le-shakeh* — had Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi not removed the salve by licking it off his leg, *paskei le-shakeh* — his leg would have been destroyed. (Whether this harmed those who removed it is not discussed.) The point is clear: even an *adam chashuv* was nearly killed by a hostile practitioner. The *adam chashuv* answer is therefore insufficient.

A note on historical chronology: the *gemara* treats this Yaakov as the same heretic mentioned elsewhere, but Tosafos points out that the Yaakov associated with Rabbi Ishmael preceded Rabbi Abbahu by several generations — this is a different Yaakov entirely.

### Expert Doctor's Reputati…

The *gemara* abandons the *adam chashuv* answer and offers a different explanation: *Rabbi Yochanan rofe mumche havah* — Rabbi Yochanan's case was different because the woman was an expert doctor (*rofe mumche*). An expert doctor, whose professional reputation depends entirely on results, would not risk that reputation by harming a patient. The concern that motivates the general prohibition — that the physician might intentionally or negligently cause harm — does not apply to a recognized expert who has too much to lose. This is the real answer to how Rabbi Yochanan was permitted to go to her.

### Revenge Over Reputation

But this raises the obvious question about Rabbi Abbahu's case: *Rabbi Abbahu nami rofe mumche havah* — was Rabbi Abbahu's attacker not also an expert? The *gemara* responds: *Shani Rabbi Abbahu de-mukmi binei binashayu* — Rabbi Abbahu was different because he was perpetually in public disputes with the heretics. Yaakov the heretic therefore had a powerful personal motivation to harm him that overrode every professional consideration. He was prepared, in the language of Shimshon's declaration — *tamut nafshi im pelishtim*, let me die with the Philistines — to sacrifice his own reputation for the sake of revenge against Rabbi Abbahu. This is not a general case; it is a singular instance in which personal enmity overcame the professional deterrent that normally protects patients from expert physicians.

Tosafos raises a further question: in the earlier discussion, going to a heretic was prohibited because it might draw one toward heresy. Why is that not the relevant prohibition here? Tosafos answers that the concern about religious influence applies only when the healer employs *avodah zarah* or heretical practices as part of the actual treatment. When the practitioner happens to be a heretic or idolater but performs treatment through entirely ordinary means, that concern does not apply. The prohibition in the earlier discussion was specifically about treatment intertwined with idolatrous practice, not about the identity of the practitioner alone.

## Metal Wounds & Remedies

### Padasa Classification

The *gemara* now transitions from the legal framework to the practical catalogue of dangerous conditions and their treatments. *Amar Shmuel: hai padasa, sakanta hi* — Shmuel declares that *padasa*, a wound caused by metal — something like what we might identify with tetanus — is dangerous. *U-mechallalin aleha es ha-Shabbos* — one therefore violates *Shabbos* for it.

### Metal Wound Treatment

*Mai asuta* — what is the treatment? To stop the bleeding: *le-mifsak dama, tichlei be-chala* — take cress in vinegar and either eat it or apply it to the wound. To promote healing and close the skin: *garda divla ve-girda de-asna* — scraping from a type of grass and scraping from a thorn bush, respectively. Or alternatively, *nikra mi-kilkulta* — a worm taken from the garbage dump. Since this is a case of *pikuach nefesh*, all such means are permitted even if they would otherwise be restricted.

## Boils & Dark Omens

### Enbasa Death-Herald

*Amar Rav Safra: hai invasa parvanka de-malcha de-mota hi* — Rav Safra declares that this particular type of large, grape-like boil (*invasa*) is a harbinger of the angel of death; it is a forerunner that announces what is coming, as a courier precedes an executioner.

### Enbasa Therapy

*Ma asuta* — what is the treatment? Take some type of herb (*tigna*) — Rashi says some type of grass — in honey. Alternatively, *krafsa bi-tlaya* — wild celery in strong wine (or, according to Tosafos, in weak wine). While one is gathering these materials in the interim, one should also take a grape (*invata*) of roughly the same size as the boil and rub it on. The symmetry is deliberate: *chivarta le-chivarta* — if the boil is white, use a white grape; *uchmata le-uchmata* — if it is dark, use a dark grape. This is a form of sympathetic treatment, corresponding like to like.

### Simta Fever-Sign

*Amar Rava: hai simta, parvanka de-eshta hi* — Rava declares that *simta*, which Rashi explains refers to a type of blister or abscess (the word ordinarily means a narrow alleyway), is a harbinger of fever. *Ma asuta* — what should be done? *Le-mimchei shitin atkutai* — snap at it sixty times (Rashi understands this as flicking against the blister repeatedly to gather the contents). Then *le-mikre sheti ve-erev* — cut it open in a crosshatch pattern to release the pus. *Ve-hani mili de-lo chivar reisheih* — this procedure is indicated only if the head of the blister has not yet turned white. *Aval chivar reisheih, leit lan bah* — if it has turned white, there is no danger and no intervention is required.

## Pika Conditions & Cures

### Aloe & Myrrh Wrapping

*Rav Yaakov chash be-pika* — Rav Yaakov suffered from *pika*, an affliction in the anal region (what the texts describe in terms consistent with hemorrhoids or a related condition). Rabbi Ami — and some say Rabbi Assi — instructed him as follows. *Layti sheva bnei ahala* — procure seven seeds of *ahala*, which is red aloe — the plant that appears in the classic pairing of *mor ve-ahalot*, myrrh and aloe. Take them, *tulna* — Rashi says red, *ve-tzur leih be-chalala de-veit tzavarei* — and wrap them in a piece of cloth cut from the neck of a garment, *ve-lichroch yadeih nira barka* — and wrap it around with a string or hair from a certain animal, *ve-tamish leih be-nafta chivara* — and submerge it in white naphtha, *ve-liklayeih* — and burn it, *u-vadrei leih ilaveih* — and then sprinkle the residue on the affected area.

### Alternative Pika Cures

*Ve-adehachi ve-hachi* — while waiting for those materials to be assembled, one may in the interim: *layti kshisha de-asna* — take a seed of a thorn bush, and apply the cracked part of the seed against the cracked skin. *Ve-hani mili pika ila ila* — this is for a condition that is external, on the outside. *Pika tata* — if the condition is internal, one proceeds differently: *layti tarba de-tzipirta de-lo iptach* — take the rendered fat of a female goat that has not yet given birth, and insert it internally. *Ve-i lo* — if that is unavailable or ineffective, *leisi tlata tarpa kara de-myavshei be-tulla* — take three leaves of a gourd that have been dried in the shade, *ve-likli ve-livdar lavyayhu* — burn them and sprinkle the ash on it. *Ve-i lo* — if that too is unavailable, *leisi mishkaday de-chalzone* — take the shells of the *chalzone* (a snail or similar shellfish) and use them accordingly.

### Oil & Linen Seasons

*Ve-i lo* — if none of the preceding remedies are available or effective, *maysei mishcha ve-kira* — take oil and wax mixed together, as Rashi explains, *ve-linkot be-schakay de-chisna* — and apply it wrapped in worn linen garments. The *gemara* then adds a general principle of seasonal self-care for this condition: *be-kayta* — in summer, the patient should wear linen garments to remain cool; *ve-de-amar gufna, be-sitva* — and cotton garments in the winter, to retain warmth. The *gemara* in fact operates with two seasons — a spring-summer period and a fall-winter period — reflecting the climate of Babylonia, where autumn transitions directly into the rainy, cold season without the intermediate season familiar to those in other latitudes.

## Ear Diseases & Heat

### Kidney Water Cure

*Rabbi Abbahu chash be-udnay* — Rabbi Abbahu suffered from an ear ailment. He was instructed either by Rabbi Yochanan directly or through a teaching in the *beis midrash*, the substance of which is what Abaye records: *Amra li aim* — my mother said to me, *lo ivray kulyasa ela le-udna* — the kidneys were created for no other purpose than to heal the ears. Rava adds that Minyumi the physician told him: *kulhu shakyani kashu le-udna le-var mi-maya de-kulyasa* — all liquids applied to the ear are harmful to it, except for the fluid extracted from kidneys.

### Kidney Heat Method

The procedure is as follows: *layti kulyasa de-barcha karcha* — procure the kidneys of a hairless goat, *ve-likro shesi ve-erev* — cut them in a crosshatch pattern, *ve-linach a-malala de-nura* — place them on a burning coal, *ve-hanu maya de-nafki minei* — and collect the liquid that flows out as they heat, *lishtinhu be-udnay* — and introduce that liquid into the ear. *Lo kreyru ve-lo chamimu ela pshurey* — it should be neither cold nor hot, but lukewarm.

### Beetle & Oil Wicks

*Ve-i lo* — if that is unavailable or ineffective, *laysi tarba de-chipushta gamlinisa* — take the rendered fat of a large beetle or scarab, *ve-lifsha ve-lishday be-udnay* — render it and place it in the ear. *Ve-i lo* — if that too is unavailable, *le-malaye le-udnay mishcha* — fill the ear with oil, *ve-la-avid sheva ptilta de-aspasta* — and fashion seven wicks from green wheat stalks.

### Wool & Reed Methods

One then takes *shupta de-suma* — the papery covering of a garlic head, *ve-lisar be-arka be-chada reisha* — and ties the wicks with animal hair at one end, *ve-likli behu nura* — and lights one end with fire, *ve-chad reisha be-udnei* — placing the unlit end in the ear where it has been filled with oil, creating a heat-transfer mechanism. *Ve-nishkof chada ve-ninach chada* — as one wick is consumed, remove it and replace it with another. *Ve-izdaher mi-zika* — and be careful of the wind.

An alternative version (*lishna achrina*) describes a similar procedure using seven wicks of animal hair rubbed with balsam oil, one end lit and the other placed in the ear, with the same sequential replacement as each wick burns down. Again: *ve-izdaher mi-zika* — take care of the wind.

*Ve-i lo* — if those methods are unavailable, *leisi udra de-nada de-lo mashkif* — take uncombed violet wool that has not been processed, *ve-ninach bei ve-nislye le-udnei lahadi nura* — place it in or on the ear and suspend it close to a fire, *ve-izdaher mi-zika* — and again guard against the wind. *Ve-i lo* — as a final option, *leisi gufsa de-kanya atika bar mea shanin* — take a tube fashioned from a reed that is approximately a hundred years old, *ve-limlei milcha galanisa* — fill it with rock salt, *ve-liklei ve-lidbak* — burn it and press the result against the ear.

### Wet/Dry Balance

A general diagnostic and therapeutic principle is then given: *rativa le-yaveshta* — liquid treatments (such as the oil and kidney fluid methods) are for a dry ear, one that is clogged or desiccated; *ve-yaveshta le-rativa* — and dry treatments (such as the reed and salt method) are for a wet ear, one that is weeping with pus or discharge. Applying the wrong category of treatment to the wrong condition would be counterproductive or harmful.

### Shabbat Ear Lifting

*Amar Rabba bar Zuti amar Rav Chanina: ma'alin oznayim be-Shabbos* — one may perform a manual lifting or manipulation of the ears on *Shabbos*. Rashi explains this may also refer to some treatment of the musculature around the cheeks or jaw. *Tani Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda: be-yad, aval lo be-sam* — this is done by hand, but not with a salve. The reasoning is not that a salve would violate *Shabbos* per se, but that Rashi considers if it can be done equally effectively without a salve, one should avoid the salve. However, *ika de-amri: be-sam, aval lo be-yad* — an alternative tradition holds the reverse: one uses a salve but not the hand. *Mai ta'ama: mi-zrav zarif* — because using one's hand may tear the tissue or cause bleeding, whereas the salve is gentler. Rashi notes that the salve would in any case involve only a *derabanan* violation, while the hand could cause a biblical violation through wounding.

## Eye Inflammation & Shabb…

### Inflamed Eye Ruling

*Amar Rav Zutra bar Tuvia amar Rav: ayin she-marada, mutar le-kochla be-Shabbos* — if one has a rebellious or inflamed eye (*ayin shemarda*), one is permitted to apply the standard eye ointment (*kechala*) on *Shabbos*. This ruling transitions the discussion from the specific remedies catalogued above to a broader and extremely important *halachic* discussion about *pikuach nefesh* and the eye.

### Pre-ground vs. Grinding

One of the rabbis hearing this ruling — identified as Rav Yaakov — assumed it was limited in scope: *hanei mili de-shachik samani me-etmol* — that it applies only when the ointment has been ground the day before *Shabbos*, so that no biblical act of grinding (*shechikah*) occurs on *Shabbos* itself. *Aval mishchak be-Shabbos ve-atuyei derech reshus ha-rabim lo* — but to grind the ointment on *Shabbos* itself, or to carry it through a public domain, he assumed, would not be permitted. Treating an eye as equivalent to an internal life-threatening injury seemed a step too far.

### Shmuel's Eye Crisis

Rav Yaakov reports a tradition he received directly from Rav Yehuda: *afilu mishchak be-Shabbos ve-atuyei derech reshus ha-rabim mutar* — even grinding the ointment on *Shabbos* and carrying it through a public domain are permitted. This is a full *pikuach nefesh* ruling. *Rav Yehuda shara le-michal aina be-Shabbos* — Rav Yehuda himself permitted the application of this ointment to an eye on *Shabbos*.

Upon hearing this, Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda (who may or may not have been his son) objected sharply: *man tzayis le-Yehuda, mechal shabei* — whoever listens to Yehuda will be desecrating *Shabbos*. He refused to accept the ruling. Then, in a turn of events the *gemara* presents as morally instructive, *u-lesof chash be-eineih* — Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda himself developed an eye inflammation on *Shabbos*. *Shalach lei* — he sent a query to Rav Yehuda: am I permitted or forbidden to apply the salve? Rav Yehuda sent back: *le-kulei alma shari, le-didach assur* — for everyone else it is permitted; for you it is forbidden. One understands this as a pointed lesson rather than a sincere ruling: it is easy to dismiss a leniency from the comfort of theoretical discussion; experiencing the fear and pain of an actual eye inflammation on *Shabbos* produces an entirely different perspective. *Ve-chi mi-didi hu?* Rav Yehuda then adds — do you think I invented this? *De-mar Shmuel hi* — this ruling I received as a tradition from Shmuel himself.

The *gemara* then narrates the incident that gave rise to Shmuel's original ruling. A maidservant in the household of Mar Shmuel developed an eye inflammation on *Shabbos*. *Tzevacha ve-leika de-ashgach bah* — she cried out in pain, and no one paid attention to her. *Paka'ah einah* — her eye was lost. The next day, Shmuel publicly taught: *ayin she-marada, mutar le-kochla be-Shabbos*. It is a striking and sobering story. The original ruling arose from a tragedy — from the failure to respond to someone in distress — and yet the lesson, once formulated, was itself resisted by a later scholar until he personally experienced the condition. The cost of learning, in this case, was a maidservant's eye.

### Eye-Heart Connection

*Mai ta'ama* — what is the rationale for treating an inflamed eye as *pikuach nefesh*? *De-shuryana de-eina be-uvnata de-liba talu* — the tendons or sinews of the eye are connected to the chambers of the heart. The anatomical claim may not map precisely onto modern physiology, but its halakhic force is clear: an eye inflammation is not merely a threat to the limb; it is a potential threat to life itself. This framing raises a critical question in *halacha* that the *gemara* engages with implicitly: is the rationale for violating *Shabbos* for an eye condition that it involves an intrinsic risk of a limb severe enough to override *Shabbos* in its own right, or is it only because the connection to the heart makes it a risk to life? These are two very different principles.

The *Meiri* actually brackets these final words and argues that *chillul Shabbos* is warranted for risk of a limb even without establishing a connection to life-threatening danger. In practice, however, the accepted rule is that risk of a limb alone does not technically permit *chillul Shabbos* — it must be framed as *pikuach nefesh*. Yet in practice the operative assumption is that virtually any significant loss of a limb carries potential complications — infection, systemic consequences, and so on — such that risk of limb is treated as tantamount to risk of life, and *Shabbos* is violated accordingly.

### Serious Eye Types

*Ke-gon mai* — what specific eye conditions fall within this category and would warrant *chillul Shabbos*? Rav Yehuda enumerates them: *rira* — some form of discharge or pus from the eye; *daytza* — a stabbing pain in the eye; *dama* — bleeding from the eye; *dimata* — uncontrollable tearing; and *kidcha* — inflammation. These five conditions constitute the category of *ayin she-marada*. Importantly, *ve-tchilas uchla* — this applies only at the beginning of the inflammatory process. *Le-apukei sof uchla* — but if the condition is clearly in the healing stage, even if the eye remains red and inflamed in appearance, one would not violate *Shabbos* for it, since the danger is past. Furthermore, *u-ptzuchei aina* — if one merely wishes to apply the ointment to improve vision or as a cosmetic enhancement, with no actual inflammatory condition present, that is not permitted on *Shabbos*.

### Hot/Cold Opposition

The *gemara* now turns to a different but related set of medical guidelines — the principle that many conditions require treatment that is opposite in quality to the condition itself, and that reversing this polarity is dangerous. For the conditions discussed — a wasp sting (*zibura*), a puncture from a nail or thorn (*sila*), a blister (*simta*), a problematic eye, or a fever — *kulhu bei banai sakanta* — entering a bathhouse is dangerous; the heat and steam will aggravate all of these conditions.

As for the treatments: Rashi's reading suggests that *chamei* in this context refers to a radish (reading it with an *alef*) rather than hot water — eat a radish for inflammation or fever. *Ve-silka* — a beet for something involving cold or chilling; and *ve-chilufa sakanta* — reversing these, that is, applying the wrong remedy, constitutes a danger.

### Sting & Wound Specifics

The *gemara* then provides specific pairs: *chamimi le-akraba* — hot water for a scorpion sting; *kariri le-ziburta* — cold compress for a wasp sting; *ve-chilufa sakanta* — and the reverse of each is dangerous. Similarly: *chamimi le-sila* — hot water for a thorn puncture; *kariri le-chaspunisa* — and a cold compress for a crack or fissure on the face; *ve-chilufa sakanta* — and the reverse is a danger. These hot-and-cold pairings reflect a systematic therapeutic logic in which each condition has a specific and inviolable correct treatment, and misapplication is not merely ineffective but actively harmful. With this, the *gemara* leaves off, and the discussion of these medical treatments will continue on the following daf.
