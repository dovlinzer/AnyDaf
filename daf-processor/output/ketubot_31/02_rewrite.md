# Ketubot 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiyma Lei Intro

### Opening

We pick up in the middle of 31a, approximately ten lines before the lines widen, beginning with the words *lo hachi matnina hadola*. The word *gufa* signals that the Gemara is now returning to analyze a previously cited source in full.

### Kiyma Lei Concept

The broader context has already introduced the principle that if someone performs an act that renders them liable to execution by *beis din* — or, according to Rabbi Nechunia ben HaKanah, even liable to *kares* — that capital liability overrides any concurrent monetary payment or fine. The question driving the discussion is: what precisely constitutes the relevant "overlap"? In what sense must the capital and monetary obligations coincide in order for the capital one to override?

### Single Act Scenarios

The earlier discussion began with cases in which a single act simultaneously generated both liabilities — for instance, burning a haystack on *Shabbos* or *Yom Kippur*. That is the clearest instance: one action, two consequences. But the Gemara has since moved to more complex territory, examining situations involving two distinct acts that either occur sequentially or appear to occur at the same time. An extreme formulation held that timing alone is dispositive: if someone is simultaneously eating *chelev* and, with his feet, destroying property, the two obligations coincide in time and the monetary one is overridden.

### Multiple Acts Timing

We now return to a different framing: rather than focusing purely on the clock, the Gemara considers whether a series of actions can be understood as constituting a single unified *process*. If the act that makes a person deserving of the death penalty is defined as a process — with a beginning, a middle, and an end — then any monetary damage or fine incurred at any point within that process would be overridden. This is the conceptual lens through which the Gemara proceeds.

## Arrow Case

### Arrow Setup

The Gemara opens with a teaching of Rabbi Avin: *hazorek chetz mit-techilas arba l'sof arba* — one who shoots an arrow from the beginning of four *amos* in *reshus harabim* to the end, and in its flight it tears fine silk garments, is *patur* — exempt from paying for the damage. The technical violation of *Shabbos* is not completed until the arrow lands — that is, until the end of its trajectory — yet the shooter is exempt from the monetary damages incurred along the way.

### Carrying Process

The reason given is: *she-akira tzarach hanacha hi* — the *akira*, the picking up, is necessary for the *hanacha*, the putting down. One might understand this physically: once the arrow is in flight, the archer cannot retrieve it, so the entire arc of motion is one indivisible event. But the deeper and more compelling reading is a halachic one.

### Carrying Definition

The act of carrying is not defined merely by the moment of *hanacha*. One does not carry by depositing an object in *reshus harabim*; one carries by picking it up, traversing the domain, and setting it down. The entire sequence — from the initial *akira* through the traversal to the final *hanacha* — constitutes a single, unified act of carrying. Since liability is incurred at the end of this process, but the process itself began at the moment of pickup, any damage occurring anywhere within that process is subsumed under the same act. The exemption from monetary liability follows, not from some physical impossibility of interruption, but from the halachic conception of carrying as an integral whole.

One might ask: is the *patur* here referring to exemption from the monetary damages for the silk? It is. The archer remains fully *chayav* for *chillul Shabbos*. The exemption is solely monetary, not religious.

## Theft & Shabbat

### Wallet Theft

The Gemara now introduces a challenge from Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye. The *braisa* teaches: *hagonev kis b'Shabbos chayav* — one who steals a wallet on *Shabbos* is still liable to pay, because the obligation of *geneiva* was incurred before the *issur* of *sekila* (execution) came into play. The act of theft is completed the moment the thief picks up the wallet — that is the act of *kinyan* by *hagba'ah* — while the violation of *Shabbos* occurs only later, when the object is carried four *amos* or transferred from the owner's *reshus hayachid* to a *reshus harabim*. The two obligations arise from distinct acts at distinct moments, so the later capital liability does not retroactively override the earlier monetary one.

### Dragging Property

The *braisa* continues: *ha im megorer v'yotzei, megorer v'yotzei, patur* — but if the thief was dragging it and going, dragging it and going, he is *patur*, because the *issur Shabbos* and the act of *geneiva* arrive simultaneously. To understand this, consider the following. If the thief drags the wallet along the ground rather than lifting it, he has not yet performed *hagba'ah*, and thus has not yet acquired it through a recognized act of *kinyan*. He drags it through the owner's domain — still not stolen — and then crosses the threshold into some other domain. That crossing is where both the *issur Shabbos* and the act of *geneiva* are completed at the same moment, and therefore they coincide.

### Domains Explained

This raises an immediate structural question: what counts as an act of *geneiva* in the first place? The general assumption is that theft is consummated through the same type of act by which property is legitimately transferred — that is, through an act of *kinyan*. Picking the object up (*hagba'ah*) in the owner's domain would accomplish this. But if one does not lift it, then *geneiva* can only be accomplished by moving it into a domain where one has some degree of control — one's own property, or at minimum some semi-private space in which one can be considered to have possession. That space is not *reshus harabim*. Meanwhile, *chillul Shabbos* requires precisely the transfer from *reshus hayachid* to *reshus harabim*. These two requirements seem at first to be mutually exclusive: the act that triggers *chillul Shabbos* is not the act that triggers *geneiva*, and vice versa. Much of the remainder of the Gemara's discussion will resolve this tension.

## Why Dragging?

### Comparison

The Gemara now asks a pointed question. The arrow case established the principle that the entire process of carrying — from the moment of *akira* — counts as a single act. If so, why does the *braisa* require the unusual case of dragging at all? In the normal case of picking up a wallet and walking it out of the house, the theft by *hagba'ah* occurs first, and then the *Shabbos* violation follows when the carrier crosses into *reshus harabim*. The arrow principle would seem to apply here as well: the *akira* — the picking up that constituted the theft — is the beginning of the process of carrying, and therefore the two offenses should be considered part of the same act, rendering the thief *patur*. Why then does the *braisa* not simply state: if you picked it up and walked it out, you are *patur*?

### Process Challenge

The Gemara answers: *kegon shehigbiha al menas lehatznio* — the case in the *braisa* is one where the thief picked up the wallet intending not to carry it out to *reshus harabim*, but to hide it somewhere else within the house. He then changed his mind and walked it out. In such a case, the theft by *hagba'ah* and the beginning of the carrying process are not the same act, because the act of *akira* that commences carrying must include the intent to carry it out. If he picked it up with intent to rehide it indoors, that picking up was an act of *geneiva* but not the beginning of the carrying process. When he subsequently decided to walk it out, that is when the carrying process began — but he did not pick it up again, so there is no *akira* at that moment.

Yet this immediately generates a further difficulty: if he is now walking toward the door with no fresh act of picking up, how can there be a valid *akira* for *Shabbos* purposes at all? The Gemara invokes the ruling of Rav Simon in the name of Rabbi Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: one who moves objects from corner to corner within his house without intending to take them outside, and then changes his mind and carries them out, is *patur* — *she-lo hayetah akirah mi-sha'ah rishonah le-kach* — because the original picking up was not directed toward that purpose, and there was no subsequent valid *akira*.

### Rest vs Adjust

The Gemara then discards the "intent to hide" scenario and reformulates: *ela eima al menat le-hotzi* — say instead that he picked it up intending to carry it out. Still, the *braisa* describes him as pausing. The Gemara asks: *amad le-mai?* For what purpose did he stop? If he stopped *le-chateif* — to shift the object from one shoulder to the other, or to adjust his grip — *orcheih hu*, that is simply the normal manner of carrying, and such an adjustment does not constitute an interruption. It does not end one process and begin another. But if he stopped *be-omed la-fush* — to take a genuine rest — that is a meaningful pause. In that case, the object has "settled" in its current location, and when he resumes walking, that resumption functions as a new *akira*. Thus the theft, which occurred at the original *hagba'ah*, is temporally separated from the beginning of the carrying process, and the two offenses are distinct. He would then be liable for the monetary penalty.

The insight here is precise: *akiras gufo ke-akiras chafetz* — when a person who has been stationary begins to move, that is treated as the object being lifted from its resting place. If he truly paused for rest, the object effectively settled; his subsequent movement constitutes a new *akira*. If he only adjusted without pausing, no such resetting occurred.

### Ben Azzai

Having established that one who picks up the wallet, pauses, and then carries it out would be *chayav* — because the theft and the carrying process are separated — the Gemara asks again why the *braisa* chose the unusual case of dragging rather than formulating a simpler distinction within the normal pickup case. Specifically, *niflog venisni bedidei* — let the *braisa* draw the distinction internally: one who picks it up and carries it straight out without stopping is *patur* (subsumed in one process), but one who pauses for rest is *chayav* (two distinct acts). Why introduce the strange case of dragging?

The Gemara answers: *ben Azzai hi* — this *braisa* follows the view of Ben Azzai, who holds *mehalach keomed dami* — that walking is like standing. According to Ben Azzai, every step a person takes constitutes a momentary pause. Each footfall is a brief settling, so continuous walking is a series of micro-stops rather than one unbroken motion. This means that the only act truly qualifying as *akira* from *reshus hayachid* to *reshus harabim* is the single step crossing the threshold — the step just before the line is the *akira*, and the step over the line is the *hanacha*. There is no extended unified process that begins at the original pickup; each step resets. Consequently, picking up the wallet inside the house and walking it out is never a case where the theft and the *Shabbos* violation form part of the same continuous process, because every step effectively interrupts the process.

The natural question follows: if walking is like standing under Ben Azzai's principle, how does one ever become liable for carrying *arba amos* in *reshus harabim*, since every step would reset the process? The *Yerushalmi* addresses this, suggesting that according to Ben Azzai, liability for the *arba amos* carrying would require actually jumping the four *amos* in one motion. Alternatively, for the *reshus hayachid*-to-*reshus harabim* transfer, only that single crossing step is the act — and it is enough.

Under Ben Azzai's framework, then, in the normal case of picking up a wallet inside the house and walking it out, the theft (the *hagba'ah*) and the *Shabbos* violation (the final crossing step) are never part of the same unified act. They are invariably separate events, and the thief is always *chayav* for the monetary penalty. The *braisa* needed a case where they genuinely coincide — and that case is dragging, or alternatively, throwing it out. Yet the *braisa* chose dragging rather than throwing, and the Gemara explains why.

The Gemara briefly raises the possibility of formulating the novel case as throwing rather than dragging — *aval zorek, patur* — since throwing, like the arrow, constitutes a unified process from the moment of release. But the Gemara deflects: *megarer deika itztaricha lei* — the case of dragging was specifically needed as a teaching in its own right. One might have thought that dragging an object along the ground is a *shinui*, an unusual mode of carrying, and that one would therefore be exempt from *Shabbos* liability. The *braisa* comes to teach that depending on the size of the object, dragging can be a perfectly normal mode of transport. If the object is large, dragging is obviously normal; if tiny, it is obviously abnormal. The *braisa* addresses the intermediate case — *b'mitziei* — where the object is medium-sized and dragging is just barely within the range of normal handling, sufficient to constitute *hotza'ah* for *Shabbos* purposes.

## Dragging Details

### Destinations

Having established why dragging is the case of choice, the Gemara now asks the central structural question: *u'd'afkeih l'heicha?* — where exactly did the thief drag the wallet? This is not a casual question. As established above, *chillul Shabbos* requires transfer from *reshus hayachid* to *reshus harabim*, while *geneiva* — without *hagba'ah* — requires bringing the object into a space where the thief exercises some measure of control. These requirements seem to pull in opposite directions.

If the thief dragged it straight into the major public thoroughfare: *issur Shabbos ika, issur geneiva leika* — there is a *Shabbos* violation, but no *geneiva*, because the public thoroughfare is not a space over which the thief has any personal control. If he dragged it into his own *reshus hayachid*: *issur geneiva ika, issur Shabbos leika* — there is *geneiva*, but no *Shabbos* violation. And if he dragged it from one *reshus hayachid* into another adjacent *reshus hayachid*, there is neither.

### Thoroughfare Sides

The Gemara then proposes: *d'afkeih l'tzidei reshus harabim* — he dragged it onto the sides of the public thoroughfare, the edges or shoulders flanking the main road. But this immediately raises the question of which authority governs. *K'man?* Like whom?

According to Rabbi Eliezer, the sides of *reshus harabim* are legally equivalent to *reshus harabim* itself. Under that view, *issur Shabbos ika* — there is a *Shabbos* violation — but *issur geneiva leika* — still no *geneiva*, since it remains *reshus harabim*. According to the Rabbis, the sides are not equivalent to *reshus harabim*, in which case *issur geneiva ika* — one can acquire there — but *issur Shabbos leika* — no *Shabbos* violation. So the problem appears equally intractable under either authority.

The Gemara resolves this elegantly: *l'olam k'Rabbi Eliezer* — the *braisa* follows Rabbi Eliezer. But Rabbi Eliezer's equation of the sides with *reshus harabim* applies only for purposes of *chillul Shabbos* — *hani mili l'chiyuva d'Shabbos* — because *d'zimnin d'dachki rabim v'asu l'hatam*, sometimes the press of the crowd overflows from the thoroughfare onto the shoulders, and the public uses them. This makes the sides sufficiently connected to, and occasionally part of, the main public artery that they count as *reshus harabim* for *Shabbos* purposes. However, *aval l'inyan mikna* — for purposes of acquisition — *d'la shechichi rabim* — since most of the time the public is not crowding those edges, the space is quiet enough that a person standing there has a measure of personal control over his immediate surroundings. He can therefore effect a *kinyan* there.

This creates the solution: a space that simultaneously satisfies the *Shabbos* requirement (it is treated as *reshus harabim* for *chillul Shabbos*) and the *geneiva* requirement (it affords enough private control to effect acquisition). The thief drags the wallet from the owner's house across the threshold into this border space — the *simta*, in Rashi's terminology — and at that single moment both the *chillul Shabbos* and the *geneiva* are complete.

### Hand Acquisition

Rav Ashi offers an alternative resolution: *k'gon shetziref yado l'mata mishlosha v'kiblo* — the case is one where the thief, as the wallet crosses into *reshus harabim*, reaches down with his hand and catches it. The legal principle underlying this is *kinyan yad* — if an object is on one's person, in one's hand, it is as though it is in one's own domain. Just as I can acquire an object by bringing it into my yard, I can acquire an object that comes to rest upon my person.

The key specification is that the hand must be kept below three *tefachim* from the ground. If the thief were to raise the wallet higher than three *tefachim* in order to catch it in his hand, that lifting would itself constitute *hagba'ah*, and the *geneiva* would be complete before the crossing into *reshus harabim* — which is the *Shabbos* violation — meaning the two offenses would once again be sequential rather than simultaneous. By keeping the hand below three *tefachim*, no *hagba'ah* occurs, and the moment the object lands in the thief's hand in *reshus harabim* is the moment both offenses are consummated together.

### Rava's Hand Rule

This solution draws on a principle of Rava: *yado shel adam chashuva lo k'arba al arba* — a person's hand is considered equivalent to a four-by-four *tefachim* space for *Shabbos* purposes. An object resting in one's hand is legally treated as though it has been set down on the ground, satisfying the *hanacha* requirement. Simultaneously, by virtue of *kinyan yad*, the object is acquired. So catching the wallet in one's hand in *reshus harabim* accomplishes both *geneiva* and *chillul Shabbos* at once.

### Ravina Position

Ravina takes a more radical position. None of these special mechanisms — *tzidei reshus harabim* or catching in the hand — are necessary. *L'olam d'afkeih l'reshus harabim* — the simple case is that the thief dragged it straight into *reshus harabim*. And *u'v'reshus harabim nami kana* — one can actually effect acquisition even in *reshus harabim*. This challenges everything assumed up to this point.

## Geneiva vs Kinyan

### Animal Mishna

Ravina and Rav Acha debate how to read this position, and both ground their dispute in a *mishna*. The *mishna* discusses a case of someone stealing an animal: *haya moshcho ve'yotze*, he was dragging it out of the owner's property. The animal then dies. If it died *b'reshus ba'alin* — while still in the owner's domain — the thief is *patur*, exempt. If he already performed *hagba'ah*, lifting it, or *she'hotzi'o* — took it out — and it then died, he is *chayav*.

The question driving the debate between Ravina and Rav Acha is: what does it mean to have "taken it out" so as to become *chayav*? Where must the animal end up?

### Ravina Read

*Ravina deyiek me'reisha* — Ravina derives his interpretation from the opening clause of the *mishna*. The *mishna* says: if it died *b'reshus ba'alin*, in the owner's domain, *patur*. The implication of this phrasing, as Ravina reads it, is: *ta'ama d'meis b'reshus ba'alin* — the only reason for exemption is that it died while still in the owner's domain. *Ha hotzio me'reshus ba'alin, u'meis chayav* — as soon as it is removed from the owner's domain, even if it enters *reshus harabim*, the thief becomes *chayav*. The emphasis falls entirely on removal from the victim's domain. Where the object goes next is irrelevant.

### Rav Acha Read

*Rav Acha deyiek me'seifa* — Rav Acha derives from the closing clause. The *mishna* states: *higbi'o o shehotzio* — he lifted it or he took it out. Rav Acha reads this as *hotza'ah dumiya d'hagba'ah* — the act of "taking out" must be analogous to *hagba'ah*. Just as *hagba'ah* means bringing the object fully into one's own control, so too *hotza'ah* — removal — must result in the object coming into the thief's control. It is insufficient merely to remove the object from the owner's sphere; the thief must bring it into his own sphere. Therefore, dragging it into *reshus harabim* alone does not constitute *geneiva*, because *reshus harabim* is nobody's controlled domain.

### Debate Resolution

This debate is subtle and important. It would be a mistake to read this as a disagreement about *kinyan* in the ordinary sense. The assumption throughout *Shas* is that one cannot acquire an object in *reshus harabim* without *hagba'ah* or *yad*. Neither Ravina nor Rav Acha is challenging that general principle of property law. The dispute is specifically about the *definition of geneiva*. Does committing *geneiva* require an act structurally equivalent to a *kinyan* — taking the object out of the victim's control and into the thief's control? Or is *geneiva* defined solely from the victim's perspective: once the object is removed from the victim's domain, the theft is complete, regardless of where it goes?

Ravina holds the latter: the act of *geneiva* is the deprivation of the owner's possession. The moment the object exits the owner's *reshus*, the theft is complete. This is a perfectly coherent reading — from the victim's standpoint, what matters is that they no longer have control, not that the thief has assumed it. Under this view, dragging the wallet straight into *reshus harabim* causes *geneiva* and *chillul Shabbos* to coincide immediately.

Rav Acha holds the former: *geneiva* must mirror *kinyan*, requiring that the thief bring the object into his own control. Taking it out and leaving it in *reshus harabim* — where no one controls it — is not yet a completed act of theft.

The Gemara then resolves how each Amora handles the textual difficulty posed by the other's preferred clause. The beginning of the *mishna* is not difficult for Rav Acha: he reads "owner's domain" expansively — *kama d'lo asyei l'reshusei, reshus ba'alin karinan beih* — as long as the object has not entered the thief's domain, it is still considered as though it remains in the owner's. And the end of the *mishna* is not difficult for Ravina: *hotza'ah dumiya d'hagba'ah lo aminan* — he simply does not accept the analogy between *hotza'ah* and *hagba'ah*. *Hagba'ah* is one method of theft effected within the owner's domain; *hotza'ah* is another method — removal from the owner's domain — and the two need not be structurally identical.

It is worth pausing to note that this entire extended *sugya* — the arrow case, the wallet dragging, the analysis of *tzidei reshus harabim*, the debate between Rav Acha and Ravina — is a construction of the *stam*, the anonymous editorial voice of the Gemara. There is almost no case where an Amora steps forward to make a direct statement in his own right. The entire analysis is the *stam* weaving together statements made by various authorities in other contexts, forming a unified *sugya* around the concept of *kiymah lei* and the question of what it means for two prohibitions to arise "at the same time" or as part of "the same process." Notably, the phrase *kiymah lei* itself never actually appears in this entire discussion — the concept has been explored throughout without the term ever being invoked.

## Incest & Lashes

### Incest Acts

The Gemara now transitions to a new application of the same underlying principle. The case is one who engages in sexual relations with his sister or his father's sister — an *issur kares*. Despite the severity of the prohibition, the Gemara records that such a person nonetheless pays the *knas*, the statutory fine associated with forbidden relations. The reason is that *kares* is a heavenly punishment; it does not on its own preclude a court-imposed monetary penalty. This is consistent with the framework established earlier in the *sugya*.

### Ain Lokeh

But the Gemara now raises a different and sharper challenge. A *braisa* enumerates a series of forbidden sexual relationships whose violation is punishable by *malkus* — lashes administered by the court. These include relations with one's sister, father's sister, mother's sister, wife's sister, brother's wife, uncle's wife, and a *niddah*. And the Gemara states the established principle: *de'eino lokeh umishalem* — one who receives lashes does not also pay. This is *ain lokeh u'meshalem*.

### Challenge

The challenge is immediate. If a man who has relations with his sister is subject to *malkus* — a real, court-administered punishment — then by the principle of *ain lokeh u'meshalem*, he should not also pay the monetary fine. Yet the *mishna* appears to say he does pay. This is a genuine tension.

It is important to note a distinction that clarifies the structure of the question. The *kiymah lei* principle discussed throughout this *sugya* — the capital crime overriding monetary liability — operates even when the court never actually administers the punishment. The act is of sufficient gravity that one is considered "already situated in" the greater punishment, and the monetary obligation falls away. The principle of *ain lokeh u'meshalem*, by contrast, seems to be triggered specifically by actual administration of *malkus*: if the court actually gives you lashes, it will not also make you pay. But even that distinction may be too simple, and the Gemara is pressing the question.

### Ulla Answer

Ulla begins to provide an answer. *Amar Ulla lo kashya* — Ulla says there is no difficulty. *Kan be'achoto na—* The Gemara cuts off at that point. Ulla appears to be drawing a distinction between different sub-cases within the list — perhaps between his sister as a *na'arah* (a minor girl subject to the fine) and other configurations. The full development of Ulla's answer will be taken up in the next session.
