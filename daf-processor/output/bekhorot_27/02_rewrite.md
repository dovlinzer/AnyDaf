# Bekhorot 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbinic Discretion

### Tanya Nami Hachi

Although our discussion properly begins on 27a, the Gemara picks up from a passage on 26b, approximately fifteen lines in, beginning with the words *tanya nami hachi* — "it has likewise been taught." By way of background: the Mishnah had established that before transferring a *bechor* to a *kohen*, whether or not the animal bears a *mum*, the Israelite owner must raise it for thirty days if it is a small grazing animal, or fifty days if it is a large animal such as a cow. An attempt had been made to derive this from a verse, but the conclusion was that the verse itself is not explicit on the precise time periods; rather, the rabbis possessed the latitude to interpret the exact parameters.

The *baraita* now cited reinforces this. The verse states: *bechor banecha titein li* — "give me the firstborn of your children" — and then, *kein ta'aseh l'tzonecha* — "so shall you do for your sheep." Just as *pidyon haben* is performed after thirty days, so too the sheep is transferred to the *kohen* after thirty days. One might then ask: perhaps the ox is also given after thirty days? The verse therefore adds: *kein ta'aseh l'shorcha ul'tzonecha* — teaching that an additional effort, an extra *asiyah*, is required for the ox. The implication is that the ox must be raised to a more developed stage, which requires more time. But precisely why that amounts to fifty days rather than some other figure rests on *shemasru hakatuv l'Chachamim* — the biblical text entrusted the determination of those parameters to the sages.

### Shemasru HaKatuv

This concept deserves emphasis, because the phrase *shemasru hakatuv l'Chachamim* represents a mode of legal reasoning that operates differently from the standard hermeneutic process. Normally, we understand the parameters of biblical law through established principles of derivation, and there is a fairly direct correlation between the language of the verse and the conclusion reached. Every so often, however, the Gemara acknowledges that a verse is deliberately open-ended, and that what exactly its boundaries are is left to rabbinic discretion — not to create rabbinic law, but to interpret the exact contours of the biblical obligation itself.

### Broader Examples

Two important examples where this principle is genuinely debated are *chol hamo'ed* and the additional acts of affliction on *Yom Kippur* beyond eating and drinking. Because the Gemara is ambiguous about whether these are biblical or rabbinic in character, some hold they are rabbinic, and the existence of exceptions — where *melacha* is permitted in cases of financial loss, or where a bride or king may wash their face on *Yom Kippur* — lends support to that view. Yet certain *rishonim* maintain that these obligations are in fact *d'Oraisa*, and that the exceptions are accounted for precisely through the principle of *shemasru hakatuv l'Chachamim*. Under this reading, it is not merely that the sages determine the original parameters at the outset; they retain ongoing discretion, including the authority to incorporate exceptions — even within the domain of biblical law. That is a considerably more expansive application of the principle.

Perhaps the most striking invocation of this idea is a position attributed, if memory serves, to the *Yere'im*, holding that the *lamed tes melachos* of *Shabbos* were not derived directly from the categories of labor performed in the *mishkan*, but were instead entrusted to the sages' discretion to define what constitutes paradigmatic productive labor. Examining the list, one can see that the thirty-nine categories describe classic forms of human industry — the stages of producing bread, parchment, and clothing — more than they appear to be a precise technical derivation from the *mishkan*. Ordinarily we would not say this, because framing the *melachos* as resting on rabbinic discretion rather than direct derivation can make people feel less confident in their biblical status. Nevertheless, the principle stands: there are instances in which the parameters of biblical law are genuinely left to rabbinic determination.

A student raised the following observation: if the entire waiting period before giving the *bechor* to the *kohen* is left to rabbinic discretion, the rabbis could theoretically have eliminated the delay altogether — which would have preempted the concern, raised earlier in the sugya, about *bizuye* (degradation of the animal). The response is that the concern about degradation applies not only to the delay in transferring the animal but also to any premature *shechita*. The reason for the waiting period is that no one would realistically want to *shecht* the animal too early — the point is to allow it to develop to a stage where it is genuinely worth *shechting*, where there is meaningful meat on its bones. The waiting period serves that constructive purpose, and the earlier concern about degradation presumably reflects a separate dynamic.

## Rabbi Yossi Dispute

### Thirty/Fifty Days

The Gemara now continues: *mikan amru* — from here the sages derived: *bevehemah dakah sheloshim yom* — a small grazing animal must be raised for thirty days; *bevehemah gasah chamishim yom* — a large animal, such as a cow, for fifty days.

### Rabbi Yossi's Position

Rabbi Yossi disagrees with respect to small animals: *bevehemah dakah sheloshah chodashim* — small grazing animals require three full months. He does not appear to dispute the fifty-day rule for large animals. His reasoning: *mipnei shetipulam merubeh* — because the effort involved in raising them during that early period is considerable.

### Reasoning: Small Teeth

A *baraita* explains the basis for Rabbi Yossi's position: *mipnei sheshineihen dakot* — because in the early weeks of its life, the grazing animal's teeth have not yet fully grown in. Since it is a grazing animal that depends on its teeth for feeding, the owner must nurse and tend to it intensively until it is capable of grazing independently. That early period of intensive care — analogous, one might say, to the most labor-intensive phase of raising any young creature — is precisely what the Torah assigns to the Israelite owner rather than to the *kohen*.

## Early Transfer Prohibiti…

### Mishnah's Rule

The Gemara now addresses a specific rule of the Mishnah: *im amar lo haKohen betoch hazman hazeh tenahu li harei zeh lo yitnu lo* — if the *kohen* says, within the waiting period, "give it to me, I'll take it off your hands," one should not give it to him. This is qualified: if the *kohen* can use it immediately — because it bears a *mum*, or, when the *Beit HaMikdash* stands, because it can be offered immediately as a *korban* — then the *kohen* may take it early. The question, then, is why the prohibition applies when the *kohen* cannot use it immediately.

### Kohen HaMaseya

Rav Sheshet provides the explanation: *mipnei shenireh keKohen hamaseya b'veit hageranot* — it resembles the case of a *kohen* who helps at the threshing floor. What does this mean? Imagine a *kohen* who comes to an Israelite during the threshing of grain and offers to help with the work, with the understanding that in return he will receive all of that Israelite's *terumah* or *ma'aser*. This appears to be a perfectly practical arrangement — it benefits both parties. The problem is that it fundamentally subverts the institution of *terumah*, which is supposed to be a free gift to the *kohen*, not a payment for services rendered. Such an explicit exchange is therefore prohibited.

### Payment Logic

Some cases are not an explicit arrangement but are sufficiently close in appearance to be treated similarly. Consider our case: the *kohen* says he will take the *bechor* early. No explicit deal was articulated. But what is effectively happening? The *kohen* knows he is receiving the animal as a *bechor*, and in exchange he is raising it for the remaining days — say, twenty days — that the Israelite would otherwise have been obligated to raise it. If the *kohen* could use it immediately — bring it as a *korban* or *shecht* it right away — then while he may be relieving the Israelite of some effort, there is no real exchange of services. But if he cannot use it and will instead be raising the animal for those remaining thirty days, it is effectively as though he is providing a service (raising the animal) and receiving the *bechor* as payment. That is far too close to a *kohen mesayeya b'veit hageranot* — not technically identical, since no explicit deal was struck — but close enough in appearance that we prohibit it.

A distinction was raised: if the *kohen* in question is a *makiri kehuna* — someone to whom this Israelite has a longstanding, generations-old practice of giving the animal — and that *kohen* says "don't trouble yourself, I'll take it now," the element of exchange is absent. He is simply saying, save yourself the effort; the gift was already going to him regardless. But where the Israelite is actively choosing to give the animal to this *kohen*, and the *kohen* is in turn raising it during the remaining period, the exchange structure is present, and that is the problem.

## Terumah Ma'aser Rules

### Baraita Statement

The Gemara now brings a *baraita* that addresses the broader phenomenon of *kohen mesayeya b'veit hageranot*. The *baraita* states: *hakohanim vehaleviyim veha'aniyim hamesa'yim b'veit haro'im uveit hagramos uveit hamitbachayim* — *kohanim* who help with the shepherds (to receive the *bechor* or *zero'a lechayayim vekeiva*), *leviyim* who help at the threshing floor (to receive *ma'aser*), and poor people who help there (to receive *ma'aser ani* in the third and sixth years) — *ein nosnin lahem terumah uma'aser biskharan* — one may not give them *terumot* and *ma'asrot* as their wages. This applies even where no explicit deal was made; if they helped out and the owner gives them these gifts afterward, that too is prohibited.

### Institution Desecration

*Ve'im osin kein chillilu* — if one does so, one has desecrated. The desecration here is not of the object in the sense of *me'ilah* — it does not cause the *terumah* to lose its sanctity — but of the institution as a whole. The *terumot* and *ma'asrot* are meant to be freely given gifts; using them as payment corrupts that foundational character.

One might ask: why prevent poor people from working in exchange for *ma'aser ani*? After all, giving someone a job rather than a handout maintains their dignity and fosters self-sufficiency — values that Torah sources clearly affirm. The response requires a distinction. In general, yes, it is better to find someone employment than to give them an outright gift. But we are speaking here not about general charitable practice but about the specific institution of *terumot* and *ma'asrot*, which are constitutively defined as freely given gifts. The question of whether employment is preferable to charity is a separate discussion about the hierarchy of *tzedakah* and does not override the inherent character of this institution.

Moreover, with respect to *kohanim* and *leviyim* specifically, the issue is even sharper. The verse itself says *chelef avodatam asher hem ovdim* — the *ma'aser* is given in exchange for the service that the *leviyim* render, whether in the *Beit HaMikdash* or as teachers of Torah. When one gives *ma'aser* to a *levi*, the act acknowledges his role as a religious functionary and reflects recognition of his sacred service. When one gives it to him because he worked in the threshing house, one is paying him as a farmhand. The *terumot* and *ma'asrot* no longer carry their proper meaning; the covenant relationship they express has been degraded.

### Real vs. Apparent

The *baraita* goes on: *vekhi teima misa lo?* — one might think, it is a bad thing but not so bad as to deserve death. The verse therefore states: *es kodshei bnei Yisrael lo sechalelu velo samusu* — do not desecrate the sanctified things of Israel, lest you die. *Tosafot* notes that this is not meant literally — this is not among the transgressions for which one incurs divinely imposed death — but is stated in the spirit of: it is as if. The rabbis also considered fining violators by requiring them to give *terumah* a second time, so that the first giving would now represent a genuine fulfillment. But that penalty was not imposed, for fear that a person might think the second taking was a new biblical obligation and erroneously use it to exempt other grain that was still *biblically* obligated — the concern of *shema yafrish min hapatur al hachiyuv*.

The *baraita* then introduces an important qualification: *uvechulan yesh bahem tovas hana'ah labaalim* — in all of these cases, the owner retains the *tovas hana'ah*, the benefit of discretion over to whom the gift is directed, and that discretion can itself be monetized under certain conditions.

### Preventing Appearances

The underlying principle at work throughout this passage is the need to prevent not only actual exchanges of *terumot* and *ma'asrot* for services but even arrangements that closely resemble such exchanges. The case of the *kohen* who volunteers to help and receives the gifts afterward may in principle reflect entirely sincere motives on both sides. But because it is indistinguishable in practice from an explicit arrangement — and because allowing the one will inevitably open the door to the other — the rabbis prohibited even the appearance of the exchange.

## Torah Values Discussion

### Work-for-Welfare Question

A student raised a broader question: why does the Torah seem to disapprove of a work-for-welfare arrangement, when such an arrangement maintains the dignity and self-sufficiency of the recipient — values that the Torah elsewhere affirms? Is this not contrary to what we might consider the Torah's own hierarchy of *tzedakah*?

### Institution vs. Employme…

The response requires distinguishing between two different frameworks. The general principle that employment is preferable to outright charity — because it maintains dignity, fosters self-sufficiency, and acknowledges the recipient as a productive member of the community — is a real Torah value. But it operates at the level of general *tzedakah* practice. What we are discussing here is an entirely different matter: the specific institution of *terumot* and *ma'asrot*, which the Torah defines as freely given gifts. One may certainly pay a poor person out of pocket for work performed; what one may not do is discharge the obligation of *ma'aser ani* through that transaction. The *ma'aser ani* is supposed to be given freely, not earned. Confusing the two does not elevate the institution of charity — it degrades the institution of *ma'aser*.

### Religious Functionaries

This distinction is perhaps clearest when applied to *kohanim* and *leviyim*. When a person gives *terumah* or *ma'aser*, the act is not merely a transfer of food or money — it is a recognition of the *kohen* or *levi* as a religious functionary, one who serves God and the community in a capacity that the ordinary Israelite does not. Giving *terumah* to a *kohen* who helped you in the threshing house communicates something entirely different: you are paying a farmhand. In that moment, the *kohen*'s sacred role — the very meaning that *terumah* is meant to express — is replaced by an economic transaction. The institution is not merely bent; it is inverted.

## Money for Discretion

### Permitted Payment

The Gemara now turns to 27a and takes up the rule of *tovas hana'ah* — the owner's right to direct the *terumah* to a *kohen* of his choosing, and the permitted monetization of that right. The case presented: a Yisrael has separated *terumah* from his grain and must find a *kohen* to receive it. Another Israelite approaches him and says: *heilach sela zeh* — here, take this *sela* — and give it to my grandson, who is a *kohen* (the grandson of a Yisrael through a daughter who married a *kohen*). This, the Gemara rules, is *mutar* — permissible. The Israelite owner is receiving money in exchange for directing his discretion, not in exchange for the *terumah* itself. He was going to give it to some *kohen* regardless; this person is simply paying for the privilege of influencing that decision. That is not an exchange of *terumah* for services.

### Prohibited Payment

However, the next case is prohibited: *im hayah Kohen leKohen asur* — if a *kohen* offers the money to direct the *terumah* to another *kohen*, that is forbidden. Note the precision: even if the *kohen* paying is not the *kohen* who will receive the *terumah*, it is still prohibited. Why? Because when a *kohen* is involved in the transaction, it no longer looks merely like payment for influencing someone's discretion. It looks like a purchase of the *terumah* itself. If a Yisrael gives twenty dollars and the *terumah* — worth, say, a hundred dollars — goes to his grandson, the disproportion and the nature of the relationship make it clear that the twenty dollars was not a purchase price. But if a *kohen* pays twenty dollars and receives a hundred dollars worth of *terumah*, it looks very much as though he bought it.

### Discretion vs. Object

The critical line, then, is between paying for the right to influence a decision and paying for the object itself. The former — when done by a Yisrael, directing the *terumah* to a *kohen* of his choice — is permitted. The latter — any arrangement that appears to be the purchase of the *terumah* — is forbidden. The institution demands that *terumah* be received as a gift, not acquired as a commodity.

## Kedushah Rationale

### Why Only Terumah

The Gemara raises a pointed question: the *baraita* had discussed not only *terumah* but also *bechor*, *zero'a lechayayim vekeiva*, and *ma'aser ani*. Why, when it arrives at the *tovas hana'ah* loophole — the permission to accept money for directing one's discretion — does it only provide the example of *terumah*? What about the other priestly gifts?

### Kedushat HaGuf

The answer: *terumah de'kedushat haguf hi* — *terumah* possesses *kedushat haguf*, intrinsic sanctity. Because of this intrinsic sanctity, even after money changes hands, *the kedushah of the terumah is not transferred*. No one will confuse the twenty dollars with a purchase of the grain itself — the sacred character of the *terumah* remains self-evident and distinguishable from any monetary transaction. The sanctity serves, in effect, as a kind of internal marker that prevents confusion.

### Gifts Without Kedushah

The other priestly gifts operate differently. The *bechor* — and more strikingly, *zero'a lechayayim vekeiva* — fall under a different analysis. *Zero'a lechayayim vekeiva*, as those familiar with the end of *Chullin* will recall, are gifts to the *kohen* from specific portions of an animal, but they carry no technical *kedushah* whatsoever. The Gemara characterizes the *bechor* (particularly when bearing a *mum*) and these other gifts as having at most *kedushat damim* — a sanctity that attaches to their monetary value rather than to their physical substance. *Zero'a lechayayim vekeiva* has not even that; its status rests entirely on its character as a designated gift that must be given and received in a manner that acknowledges the *kohen*'s standing. As the Gemara in *Chullin* discussed, there is a proper mode of presenting and consuming these gifts — one that expresses recognition of the *kohen*'s dignity — but this is a matter of conduct rather than *kedushah* in the technical sense.

Because these gifts lack *kedushat haguf*, the concern arises that if money were to change hands in connection with them, observers might conclude that the money represents the purchase price — that *kedushasan mitchallah*, their sanctity has transferred to the money, and the physical gifts are now essentially *chullin*. If one were to reach that conclusion, the implication would be that there is no longer any obligation to present or receive them in the respectful manner that their status demands. The institution surrounding these gifts would be quietly dismantled.

### Conclusion: Status Conce…

The rule is therefore calibrated to protect the status of these lesser-sanctified gifts precisely because of their vulnerability. With *terumah*, the *kedushat haguf* provides sufficient protection against confusion; one may therefore permit the *tovas hana'ah* transaction. With *zero'a lechayayim vekeiva*, *bechor* bearing a *mum*, and similar gifts, any exchange involving money risks appearing to transform the gift into a purchase, thereby stripping away the special character through which the institution conveys its meaning. We therefore do not permit such transactions for those gifts at all.

A related question was raised: *ma'aser ani* has no physical sanctity either. Could one therefore give money to influence who receives one's *ma'aser ani*? That seems plausible — the concern about appearing to purchase the *terumah* would not apply to something that has no special sacred status in the first place. The Gemara's discussion here does not resolve this question definitively, and the point was acknowledged as genuinely open.

## Terumah Chutz La'Aretz

### Rava's Ruling

Rava rules: *trumat chutz la'aretz ein bah mishum koneh mibeis hageranot* — *terumah* from outside *Eretz Yisrael* is only of rabbinic force, and therefore the prohibition on using it as payment for services does not apply. Because the entire framework of service-exchange restrictions was instituted to protect the integrity of a Biblically mandated institution, it does not extend to obligations that are merely rabbinic in character.

### Practical Examples

This ruling had immediate practical application among the Babylonian *Amora'im*. Rav Chama gave his *terumah* (or possibly his *zero'a lechayayim vekeiva*, though the context suggests *terumah*) to his servant as payment for services. The very sages who transmitted these laws lived outside *Eretz Yisrael* and conducted themselves in accordance with Rava's principle.

### Betela BeRov

Shmuel adds a further leniency: *trumat chutz la'aretz betela berov* — *terumah* from outside *Eretz Yisrael* is nullified by a simple majority. In *Eretz Yisrael*, *terumah* requires sixty parts (or by some opinions one hundred) of *chullin* to be *batel*; here, a bare majority suffices. This opens the door to the next set of leniencies we are about to encounter.

---

## Additional Leniencies

### Rabba's Practice

Not only is *terumah* outside *Eretz Yisrael* nullifiable in a majority — one may proactively create such a majority. Rabba, who was a *kohen* — a descendant, as both Rashi and Tosafot note, of the house of Eli, the *kohen* in the time of the prophet Shmuel — would *mevatel b'rov* his *terumah chutz la'aretz* in a majority and then eat it during periods of *tum'ah*. Normally the rule of *ein mevatlin issur l'chatchila* — one may not proactively engineer *bittul* — would prohibit this. But Tosafot clarifies that this leniency applies specifically to allow a *kohen* who is *tamei* to eat *terumah chutz la'aretz*, not to permit a Yisrael to simply eat the *terumah* as though it were *chullin*.

### Two-to-One Method

Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua, also a *kohen*, developed a somewhat more exacting application of the same principle. When he had wine that included *terumah*, he would mix two units of *chullin* wine with one unit of *terumah* wine, then remove one unit. This maintained a two-to-one ratio — always a majority of *chullin* — and allowed him to assume that what he withdrew was the *chullin* portion, with the *terumah* remaining in the barrel. After removing that first unit, he would replenish with another unit of *chullin*, and then remove one again — always maintaining the same ratio, always leaving at least twice as much *chullin* as *terumah* in the vessel. This was a somewhat more fastidious approach to the same *bittul* procedure, but it was not substantially more demanding, and it enabled him to drink even while *tamei*.

### Ochel VeHoleich

Shmuel adds a third and particularly striking leniency: *trumat chutz la'aretz ochel v'holeich v'achar kach mafrish* — one may eat through an entire pile of grain outside *Eretz Yisrael* and separate *terumah* only from whatever remains at the end. Normally one must separate *terumah* at the outset, before consuming any of the grain; the grain is *tevel* and may not be eaten until *terumah* has been taken. Here, the separation at the very end suffices. The underlying implication is that the grain does not truly have the status of *tevel* outside *Eretz Yisrael* in the full biblical sense — the prohibition is sufficiently attenuated that the timing of separation carries no practical weight.

## Tumah Restrictions

### Tumah Yotzei Migufo

Shmuel states a further principle: *ein trumat chutz la'aretz asurah ella b'mi shetum'ah yotzei alav migufo* — *terumah* outside *Eretz Yisrael* is forbidden only to one whose *tum'ah* issues from within his own body. Normally, any form of *tum'ah* disqualifies a *kohen* from eating *terumah*. Here, the restriction is narrowed dramatically: only *tum'ah* that is defined by and expressive of one's own personal physical state creates a prohibition.

### Definitions

For men, this means a *zav* — one who has an abnormal penile discharge — or a *ba'al keri* — one who has had a seminal emission. For women, it means a *niddah* or a *zavah* — conditions arising from uterine blood flows, whether during the menstrual period or outside of it. These are *tumah yotzei migufo* because the *tum'ah* is constitutive of the person's physical state. Other forms of *tum'ah*, including the most severe — *tum'at meit* — do not prohibit eating *terumah chutz la'aretz* at all.

---

## Balancing Kedushah

### Rationale for Institution

One might reasonably ask: if *terumah chutz la'aretz* is this attenuated, why maintain the institution at all? Two reasons suggest themselves. The first is practical: *kohanim* and *leviyim* living in communities near *Eretz Yisrael* but outside its borders still require support, and the *terumah* framework provides a mechanism for that. The second is conceptual: just as one might wish to extend the institution of *terumah* beyond the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* as a way of reinforcing and protecting the *d'Oraisa* obligation that applies within the land, the rabbis had reason to want its reach to extend outward.

### Tightrope Balance

But the very leniencies we have been cataloguing reveal the tension at the heart of this extension. If *terumah chutz la'aretz* were given the same weight as *terumah* in *Eretz Yisrael*, the differences between them would effectively be erased — and that erasure would undermine the significance of *Eretz Yisrael* itself. The institution of *terumah* draws much of its meaning from the holiness of the land; to apply it with equal force everywhere would dilute that meaning. The rabbis therefore walk a tightrope: they maintain the institution outside the land but make it sufficiently lenient that it never competes with — or diminishes the status of — the original institution in *Eretz Yisrael*.

---

## Tumah Restrictions…

### Eating vs. Touching

Shmuel adds one further specification: *v'hani milei b'achila, aval b'negi'ah leis lan bah* — the restriction on *tamei* consumption of *terumah chutz la'aretz* applies only to eating, not to touching. Even a person whose *tum'ah* is of the *yotzei migufo* variety may handle the *terumah* — kneading it, separating it, preparing it — as long as they do not eat it.

## Challah Example

### Niddah Challah Case

Avina states: *hilekhach niddah kotza challah* — a woman who is *niddah* and is making *challah* (which has the status of *terumah* outside *Eretz Yisrael*) may touch it, since the restriction is limited to eating. She cannot eat the *challah* herself even though she is married to a *kohen*, because she has *tumah yotzei migufo*. But she may handle it and give it to her minor son, who is a *kohen*. A young son who has not yet reached the age where seminal emission is a concern does not have *tumah yotzei migufo*, and may therefore eat it.

### Tosafot's Question

Tosafot raises a question about the specificity of this ruling: why is a minor son mentioned in particular? If the only issue is *tumah yotzei migufo*, any adult *kohen* who is currently in a *tahor* state — a man who has been to the *mikveh* after a *keri*, for instance — could eat it just as well. Tosafot suggests that perhaps the reason a minor is specified is connected to the quantity involved: *challah chutz la'aretz* is only a small amount, and perhaps the ruling was framed with that in mind, or perhaps there is a view that one should ideally give it to someone who has never had the relevant *tum'ah* at all. However, that reading is a stretch, and it is generally assumed that a minor is mentioned only as an example rather than as a *davka* requirement.

### Burn Alternative

If no minor is available — and more broadly, if there is no one available to eat the *challah* given the *tum'ah* situation — *shakla l'reish masa v'shadi b'tanura*: take it on a skewer and throw it into the oven to burn. This is the familiar practice of burning *challah chutz la'aretz* when it cannot be eaten: the piece separated as *challah* is placed in the oven and burned.

### Two Challot

But burning the *challah* is not the end of the process: *mefarsha challah achrisi, k'heichi d'lo tishtakkach toras challah v'ochla kohen gadol* — one separates a second piece of *challah* so that the institution of *challah* is not forgotten, and this second piece is eaten by an adult *kohen* who is *tahor*. The point is to ensure that the practice of *challah* being given to and eaten by a *kohen* — which is its essential meaning — remains visible and operative, not reduced entirely to the burning of an uneatable piece. Tosafot engages at length with the broader topic of *shtei challahs* — the two *challot* separated in areas of different halachic status relative to *Eretz Yisrael* — a topic that would require considerable time to develop fully.

## Woman on Boat

### Tamei Meis Question

The Gemara now presents a narrative. Rav Nachman, Rav Amram, and Rami bar Chama were traveling together on a boat. Rav Amram stepped away momentarily. A woman approached the two remaining rabbis and asked: *tamei meit mahu sheyitbol v'yochal trumas chutza'aretz* — if someone is *tamei* from contact with the dead, may they immerse in a *mikveh* and eat *terumah chutz la'aretz*? The question carries a subtle difficulty: ordinarily, *tum'at meit* cannot be removed by *mikveh* alone — the full purification process requires the sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes. So what good does immersion do here?

### Rav Nachman Response

Rav Nachman turned to Rami bar Chama and said: *v'chi haza'ah yesh lanu* — do we have the sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes? The implication: since we do not have the ashes and cannot complete the purification of *tum'at meit* in any case, there is no reason to require waiting for nightfall after immersion either. In fact, the logic extends further: if we already permit *tamei* people to eat *terumah chutz la'aretz* (since only *tumah yotzei migufo* is restricted), and this person's *tum'ah* is *tum'at meit* rather than *yotzei migufo*, they may eat the *terumah* regardless.

### Rav Amram Ruling

Rami bar Chama counseled waiting for Rav Amram's return before issuing a ruling. When Rav Amram returned, he said in the name of Rav: *tamei meit tovel v'ochel b'trumat chutza'aretz* — one who is *tamei* from the dead immerses and may eat *terumah chutz la'aretz* without waiting for nightfall. The Gemara immediately adds: *v'leis hilchasa k'vasei* — and the halacha does not follow this ruling. A similar statement appears regarding *tamei sheretz*: one who became *tamei* through contact with a crawling creature immerses and may eat *terumah chutz la'aretz*, and again *v'leis hilchasa k'vasei*.

### Tosafot Interpretation

The question is: how does the Gemara mean that the halacha does not follow these rulings? One reading would be that the halacha requires waiting until nightfall after immersion. But Tosafot reads it very differently, in a manner consistent with everything we have established: *v'leis hilchasa k'vasei b'ha d'matriach tvilah — ella afilu tvilah lo ba'ei* — the halacha does not follow these rulings in the specific sense that they require immersion at all. One does not even need to go to the *mikveh*. If a person's *tum'ah* is not *tumah yotzei migufo* — and *tum'at meit* and *tum'at sheretz* are not — they may eat *terumah chutz la'aretz* without any intervention whatsoever. Rashi's reading, that one must wait for nightfall, is noted but rejected by Tosafot.

### Inherent Tumah Logic

The underlying logic of this position is captured in a remark made during the discussion. The land of *chutz la'aretz* is itself inherently tamei — *tum'at eretz ha'amim*, the *tum'ah* of the lands of the nations, is a well-known rabbinic concept. The practical upshot: requiring someone living outside *Eretz Yisrael* to purify themselves from *tum'at meit* before eating *terumah* would be entirely futile, since the moment they stepped back outside they would be *tamei* again. Tosafot reads Rav Nachman's rhetorical question — "do we have *haza'ah*?" — not as referring to the absence of *parah adumah* ashes in our time, but as referring to the absurdity of requiring someone in *chutz la'aretz* to travel to *Eretz Yisrael*, obtain sprinkling, and then return — only to immediately incur *tum'ah* again upon leaving. Restricting *terumah chutz la'aretz* to those with *tumah yotzei migufo* is the only logically coherent restriction given this reality.

## Year of Bechor

### Shana Beshana

The Gemara now transitions to a new Mishnah: *bechor ne'echal shana beshana* — the *bechor* must be eaten within a year, whether brought as a *korban* or eaten in the context of a *bamah*. A clarification is in order: the Torah elsewhere specifies that certain *korbanot* must come from animals less than a year old, and an animal that exceeds that age is rendered *pasul* as a *korban* because one of its criteria — its age — has not been met. The *bechor* is different. The Torah does not specify a maximum age for a *bechor* as a *korban*; what it specifies is the owner's personal obligation to bring or eat it within the year. Failure to do so does not *pasul* the animal — it simply means the owner was derelict in his obligation. He may still bring it afterward.

### Animal's Own Year

The Mishnah speaks of *blemish arising "within its year"* — using a possessive construction that implies the animal's own year, from birthday to birthday, rather than a calendrical year. Rav Yehuda citing Rav derives this from the verse: *lifnei Hashem Elokecha tochelenu shana beshana* — eat it before God year by year. Reading *shana beshana* as one year entering into another: *eizu shana shenichneset bachaverta* — which year goes into another year? It must be the animal's birthday year, because a birthday year, by definition, spans from one calendar year into the next. A calendar year does not go "into" another calendar year — when one ends, the other begins. A birthday year, however, intersects two calendar years. The possessive *shnato* — "its year" — thus refers to the twelve months from the animal's birth.

### Verse Derivation

The derivation is elegant: the doubled language *shana beshana*, if read straightforwardly as two separate calendar years, would imply only "every year." But read as one year entering into another, it points toward a year that bridges two calendrical periods — the animal's biographical year. That is how we know the obligation is measured from birthday to birthday rather than from the new year.

## School of Rav

### Two Calendar Years

The school of Rav (*debei Rav*) offers a different *derasha* from the same phrase *shana beshana*. Rather than reading it as pointing toward the animal's biographical year, they read it as teaching a new halacha about the span over which the *bechor* may be eaten as a *korban*: *yom echad beshana zu v'yom echad beshana zo* — one day in one calendar year and one day in another calendar year. That is, the *bechor* may be eaten across two different calendar years. This would be the case if it is brought on *erev Rosh Hashanah* — one day's eating in the outgoing year, another day's eating in the incoming year.

### Two Days One Night

This *derasha* teaches a new halacha: *limed al habechor shene'echal lishnei yamim velaylah echad* — the *bechor* is eaten over two days and the intervening night. Until now, it was not clear how long the *bechor* may be eaten. Some *korbanot* are eaten for only one day; others, like *shelamim*, for two days. The *derasha* of *shana beshana* — that it may span two calendar years — tells us it has the eating-period of a two-day *korban*. If it may be brought on *erev Rosh Hashanah* and eaten into *Rosh Hashanah*, it has an eating span that crosses two days.

### Linked to Shelamim

But if *debei Rav* uses *shana beshana* to establish the two-day eating period, where does Rav himself (who used that verse to establish the birthday-year measurement) derive the two-day eating period? The answer is from a different verse. When the Torah speaks about the *bechor* given to the *kohanim*, it says: *u'vesaram yihyeh lach, ke-chazeh hatenufah u'khe-shok hayamin* — the flesh will be yours, like the breast and the right thigh of the *shelamim*. The Torah explicitly equates the *bechor* with the breast and thigh of the *shelamim*. Since *shelamim* is the paradigmatic two-day *korban*, the equation teaches that the *bechor* is also eaten for two days and the intervening night: *hikisho hakatuv le-chazeh v'shok shel shelamim — mah halan lishnei yamim velaylah echad, af kan lishnei yamim velaylah echad*.

### Todah Objection

An objection is raised: the *shelamim* is not a monolithic category. The *todah* — the thanksgiving offering — is a type of *shelamim*, yet it is eaten for only one day and night. Perhaps the Torah's equation of the *bechor* with the breast and thigh of the *shelamim* should be understood more narrowly, and we should link the *bechor* to the *todah* rather than to the standard *shelamim*. If so, the eating period would be only one day.

### Lach Yihyeh Response

The response: *amar kra lach yihyeh* — the verse adds the phrase "it will be for you," which *hosif lach hakatuv havayah acheret* — the Torah grants to the recipient of the *bechor* an additional measure of ownership. This extra ownership indicates that the *bechor* is not like the *todah*, which is more restricted; it carries the more generous scope of the standard *shelamim*, and therefore it is eaten for two days and the intervening night.
