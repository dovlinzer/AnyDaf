# Shevuot 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Chazaka

### Overview

Today's *daf* is 45. We are in the penultimate *perek*, *Kol HaNishba'in*, which deals with the Rabbinic *shvuot*. By way of orientation: under Torah law, one takes an oath and does not pay; under the Rabbinic framework, one takes an oath and is able to collect. We have already worked through half of the *mishna*, whose entire text spans the first *daf* and a half.

### Substantiating Evidence

The first set of cases enumerated in the *mishna* involves situations where there is some substantiating evidence — some reason to believe that the person making the claim is actually telling the truth. These include a case of someone coming to collect wages, someone who was broken into and claims the intruder stole from him, and someone who was beaten after leaving a particular person's company. In each such case, the claimant takes an oath and collects. Rabbi Yehuda, however, maintains that the case must begin with a Torah-level oath of *modeh bemiktzat* — that is, the defendant must admit to part of the claim, after which we reverse the usual model and allow the claimant to take the oath and collect.

### Chashud al HaShe'ua

We now continue with the remaining entries on this list. The very last line of 44b reads: *shekinegdo chashud al hashvua* — if the opposing party is suspect with respect to oaths, he is obligated to take an oath but cannot be trusted to do so. Therefore we allow the claimant to take the oath and collect instead.

### Examples of Chashud

What constitutes being *chashud*? The *mishna* gives examples: *shvuat ha'edut* and *shvuat hapikadon* — if someone has sworn falsely in the past, whether in the context of testimony or regarding a deposit entrusted to their care. These instances render one a *chashud al hashvua*.

### Shevuat Shav

The *mishna* adds: *va'afilu shvuat shav* — even if the person did not swear falsely in the strict sense but took a *shvuat shav*, such as swearing to an obvious or trivially true proposition. One might have thought that invoking God's name on a meaningless statement is categorically different from lying under oath, but apparently the *mishna* treats them identically. Apparently, it is the failure to treat invocation of God's name with the appropriate weight that matters — however that weight is defined.

### Chidush of Mixing Oaths

This is the *chidush* of the *mishna*: once a person is established as having transgressed in any of these categories of oath, they are *chashud* across the board. The equation of these disparate categories is itself the novel teaching.

### Teshuva & Chashud

A natural question arises: can one ever do *teshuva* and recover from this status? How would one even detect that sufficient *teshuva* had been done? This question is explicitly addressed by the *Gemara* elsewhere in *Shas* regarding one who is *chashud* on monetary matters — for instance, a butcher who sold *treif* meat for profit. There, the way one detects genuine *teshuva* is by observing the person's conduct in situations where they believe no one is watching, verifying that they have become scrupulously honest. How one would detect analogous *teshuva* with respect to oaths is a good question that remains to be explored. What is clear from the *mishna* is that the initial status, once established, results in being blacklisted: once you are known to have sworn falsely, that status applies.

## Chashud al HaMemona

### Dice Players & Usurers

The *mishna* continues: *haya echad meihem mesachek bekubia umalveh beribit* — if one of the parties was a dice player or lent money with *ribit*, or *umafrichei yonim* — pigeon racers — or *vesochrei shvi'is* — traders in *shvi'is* produce. All of these individuals are *pasul le'edut*, disqualified from giving testimony, out of concern that they will compromise themselves for monetary gain. That is the standard explanation for their disqualification.

### Memona vs She'ua

The question that the *Gemara* raises throughout *Shas* is: *chashud al mamona chashud al shvuato*? The straightforward reading of this *mishna* is that these individuals, being *chashud al mamona* — willing to transgress for financial gain — are therefore suspected of taking a false oath as well. There is, however, an opinion in the *Gemara* that disputes this logic: one might steal, but stealing does not carry the same weight to a person as invoking God's name. Under that view, *chashud al mamona* does not automatically render one *chashud al shvuato*.

### Unproductive Activity

The *Gemara* describes these individuals in terms of their lifestyle: they are not *oskin b'yishuvo shel olam* — not engaged in productive activity — and as a result do not necessarily live up to the standards expected of members of civil society.

### Professional vs Occasion…

Even focusing only on monetary violations, there is a critical distinction: these are people for whom violation is their *profession*. It is the dice player's business, the *shvi'is* trader's business, to transgress prohibitions in the service of profit, regularly and systematically. That is quite different from a case where someone perhaps stole once or is merely suspected of having done so. The occasional sinner and the professional violator occupy distinct categories.

### Both Suspect (Chasudin)

The *mishna* now presents the scenario where both parties are suspect: *hayu shneihem chashudim*. In that case, I was supposed to take the oath and collect, but now I am also *chashud*. Rabbi Yose says *hazra hashvua limkomah* — the oath returns to its place. The straightforward reading is that it reverts to the defendant, who cannot take it and therefore must pay. The *Gemara* will discuss the possible readings of this.

### Meir vs Yose Split

Rabbi Meir says *yachloku* — neither party can take the oath, and therefore the defendant pays half the disputed amount. The underlying logic: you claim he owes you a hundred dollars and he admits fifty — a case of *modeh bemiktzat* — meaning a *shvuat deoraita* is triggered, which would ordinarily be taken by the defendant. Since neither party can take it, Rabbi Meir resolves the impasse by splitting the claim, and the defendant pays twenty-five. The principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* might seem to argue against any payment, but since the defendant is obligated to take a *shevua* he cannot take, the consequence is payment.

## Storekeeper & Modeh

### Ledger Insufficient

The *mishna* turns to a new case: *vehachvani al pinkaso keitzad* — what about the storekeeper with his ledger? The *mishna* clarifies that the storekeeper does not establish a claim simply by pointing to his ledger and saying *katuv al pinkasi sheata chayav li masayim zuz* — "I have written in my ledger that you owe me two hundred *zuz*." A storekeeper can write whatever he likes in his notebook, and there is no inherent trustworthiness to his records alone. That is not sufficient substantiating evidence to allow him to take an oath and collect.

### Third Party Claims

The case where something *is* substantiated involves a different arrangement. I approach Michael the storekeeper and say: give my son two hundred measures of wheat when he comes by, or give my worker Charlie a *sela*'s worth of goods in lieu of the wages I owe him, and I will pay you back. Michael records all of this in his *pinkas*. That background is agreed upon by all parties. The dispute arises when Michael says *natati* — "I gave exactly what was arranged, now pay me" — and my son and my workers respond *lo natalnu* — "we never went to the store, we never collected."

### Double Collection

Now there are two competing claims against me. My worker Charlie says he never received his wages; Michael says he paid Charlie on my behalf. The *mishna* rules: *hu nishba venotel vehu nishba venotel* — Michael takes an oath and collects from me, and my workers take an oath and collect from me, and I am out double.

The logic is as follows. With respect to my workers: we know I owed them wages, and there is no definitive proof they were paid — only Michael's claim that he paid them on my behalf. I cannot counter their claim with certainty; I can only point to Michael's assertion. As for Michael: we know the arrangement was made, but whether he actually fulfilled his end of it is unverified. So by both my workers and Michael, there exists a prior obligation on my part, and the circumstances give enough weight to both claims that the *mishnah* allows each to take an oath.

### Ben Nanos Objection

Ben Nanos objects: *keitzad elu va'elu ba'im lidei shvuat shav* — how can both sides swear when one of them is necessarily swearing falsely? It is worth noting he uses the term *shvuat shav* where one might have expected *shvuat sheker* — perhaps indicating that demanding a sworn claim when a false oath is structurally inevitable is itself a kind of vain invocation of God's name. Ben Nanos therefore says: *ela hu notel shelo bishvua vehen notlim shelo bishvua* — rather, they all collect without taking an oath at all. That is a sweeping ruling: I am deprived even of an oath to satisfy my position.

### Ran's Version

The *Ran*'s *girsa* actually makes a bit more sense here: *hu notel bishvua vehen notlim shelo bishvua* — Michael takes an oath and collects, but my workers collect without an oath. The reasoning: with respect to my workers, we know I owed them and there is no evidence of payment, so they collect without an oath. With respect to Michael, we know the arrangement was made but not that he fulfilled it, so he still needs to swear. Critically, under the *Ran*'s version, only one party is taking an oath, which means we cannot say there is a definitive *shvuat sheker* built into the system. This is how the *Chachamim* would implement the rule without structurally guaranteeing a false oath.

## Nishba VeNiftar Cases

### Fruit Payment Case

A somewhat different case follows. *Amar lachanvani ten li bedinar perot* — I go to Michael the storekeeper and say, give me twenty dollars worth of oranges. We all agree that Michael gave me two crates of oranges. Now Michael says *ten li oto dinar* — where is my twenty dollars? And I say *natati lach* — I already paid you, you put it in your cash register. Here, in contrast to the earlier cases, Michael is making a claim and I am denying it: I am *kofer hakol*. Normally one who denies everything takes no oath. The *chidush* is that because of the surrounding circumstances — we all agree the transaction took place, the only dispute is whether it was satisfied — there is enough of a question that I cannot simply walk away. I must take an oath. But this is *nishba veniftar*, not *nishba venotel*: Michael does not get to swear and collect; rather, I take the oath and am exempted from paying.

### Shevuat Hesset

Rashi describes this as a *shvuat hesset*, but Tosafot points out — in multiple places — that this is imprecise. *Shvuat hesset* was specifically instituted by Rav Nachman, and Tosafot explicitly states that Rav Nachman was not claiming that *shvuat hesset* had always existed. Rav Nachman introduced the concept of *shvuat hesset* — a standard oath imposed even when there is insufficient substantiating evidence, simply to prevent a claimant from walking away based on a bare denial.

What distinguishes the present case is that there *are* substantiating circumstances: all parties concede the transaction, and the only question is whether it was concluded. This is not the classic *shvuat hesset* scenario, in which I accost someone on the street and say you owe me a hundred dollars and he says he has no idea what I am talking about. Rather, it is a rabbinic oath of the *nishba veniftar* variety — not *nishba venotel* — grounded in genuine circumstantial weight. The principle remains *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*, the one seeking to extract payment must bring proof, so Michael cannot collect. But there is sufficient basis to require me, the defender, to take an oath before being exempt.

### Possession Presumption

Rabbi Yehuda says: *kol shehaparot beyado yado al ha'elyona* — whoever has the fruit has the upper hand. Returning to the first version of this case — where Michael gave me the crates of oranges and I have taken them home, and he now says I never paid him — the *Chachamim* hold that I must take an oath that I paid the twenty dollars before I am exempt. Rabbi Yehuda says I do not even have to take an oath, because it is highly unusual that I would be walking away with the oranges in my possession without having already paid for them. The normal course of a transaction is that payment precedes taking possession — just as at a supermarket, one does not wheel the cart out before handing over the money.

Rashi notes explicitly that this reasoning applies to a case where the merchant does not extend credit. If credit is extended, the presumption would be different. But in a standard retail transaction where goods are only released upon payment, the very fact that I have the oranges at home constitutes a strong *chazaka* that I paid, and Rabbi Yehuda holds I need not swear at all.

The second configuration: I gave Michael the twenty dollars and now demand the oranges, and Michael says *natati lecha veholachtam letoch beitecha* — I already gave you the crates and you took them home. Here again it is a *kofer hakol* situation, but the circumstances are sufficient to require Michael to take an oath rather than simply deny and walk away. *Yishava chanvani* — the storekeeper must swear. The rule across both configurations is consistent with *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: the party seeking to extract payment or goods must bring proof, but the defending party, while ultimately exempt, cannot walk away without an oath.

### Money Changer

An analogous set of cases involves a *shulchani* — a money changer. *Amar lashulchani ten li bedinar maot* — give me change for this *dinar*. He gives me the small coins. Now he says *ten li et hadinar* — give me the *dinar*. I say *natati lecha* — I already gave it to you, you put it in your register. He is demanding, I am denying, I am *kofer hakol*, and yet I must take an oath. *Yishava baal habayis*.

In the reverse: I give the *dinar* to Michael and ask for my change. He says *natati lecha vehishlachtam letoch kisecha* — I gave you the change and you put it in your pocket. I am demanding the change, he is denying having withheld it, and he should simply walk away — but because of the circumstances, he must first swear. *Yishava shulchani*.

Rabbi Yehuda says again, applying the same logic as with the oranges: *ein derech hashulchani sheyiten issar ad sheyitol dinar* — the money changer does not give out singles until he has received the bill. If I have already received all my change, the presumption is that I first gave the *dinar*. The *Chachamim* say I must still swear; Rabbi Yehuda says no oath is necessary.

### Real-World Examples

Reading this *mishna*, one cannot help thinking about situations that arise at the cash register — handing over a twenty-dollar bill and then being told one only gave a ten. These situations arise even in perfect good faith, which is why some people are very *makpid* to place the bill on top of the counter rather than into the drawer before the change is counted out. One sometimes even announces aloud, "I'm giving you a twenty-dollar bill." Understanding how these disputes arise even among honest people illuminates the entire framework of substantiating circumstances that underpins these Rabbinic oaths.

## Keshem SheAmru

### Keshem Formula

We now turn to yet another type of Rabbinic oath. The structure of the upcoming *mishna* is somewhat unusual, because it uses the formula *keshem she'amru* — "just as they said" — referring to cases that have not yet been presented in the text before us. The *mishna* will enumerate a whole series of cases where the Rabbis instituted a *shvua* — the cases that function as the precedents for the new cases being introduced — even though the reader has not yet encountered them. We proceed through the list.

### Ketuba Collection

*Hapogemet ktuvatah lo tipara ela bishvua* — a woman who concedes that part of her *ktuva* has been paid may not collect the remainder without taking an oath. The scenario: the husband owes her two hundred *zuz* plus whatever she brought into the marriage. He says he has paid it all in full; she says he paid a hundred but not the rest. Her partial admission — that he did pay some — raises the question of whether he may have paid it all. After all, she is still holding the *shtar*, the *ktuva* document, despite having acknowledged partial payment. Therefore she must take an oath that the outstanding balance remains unpaid.

This is a case of *nishba venotel*, but it differs from the earlier examples in that the money's being owed is not really in question — she holds the *shtar* — it is the question of remaining balance that creates the need for a *shvua*.

One might ask whether this is a case of *modeh bemiktzat*. It is not, and the distinction is important. In *modeh bemiktzat*, the defendant — the one from whom money is being demanded — concedes to part of the claim. Here, it is the claimant herself who is conceding that part of the outstanding debt has been settled. If I say you owe me a hundred dollars and you say I only owe fifty, that is *modeh bemiktzat*. But if I say you owe me a hundred and you say you have paid it all off, you are *kofer hakol* — and if I say no, you paid ten, the partial concession is mine, not yours. This case is structurally similar to *modeh bemiktzat* but is not identical to it.

### Structure Analysis

The *mishna* then adds: *ve'ed echad meidah shehi pruah* — if a single witness testifies that the *ktuva* has been paid. Normally an *ed echad* creates an obligation to swear for the one against whom the testimony is directed, and that *shvua* in the Torah framework is taken by the defendant. Here the *ed echad* testifies that the *ktuva* has been satisfied, and although the *shtar* ordinarily outweighs a single witness, the woman must nevertheless swear against the *ed echad* in order to collect. Both of these cases are structurally analogous to Torah-level oath situations — the first to *modeh bemiktzat*, the second to *ed echad* — but applied in a different configuration.

### Liens & Orphans

*Minichasim meshubadim uminichasei yetomim lo tipara ela bishvua* — if one comes to collect from property encumbered by liens, or from the property of orphans, a *shvua* is required even when a *shtar* is produced. In the case of *nichasim meshubadim*: I have a debt owed to me by Charlie, but Gloria holds liens on Charlie's property and stands to lose if I collect it. Gloria cannot speak for herself in the proceedings, but she is an interested party. To protect her interests, I must take an oath before extracting the liened property. Similarly, when collecting from heirs whose father passed away and left an estate — the heirs may not know enough to defend themselves — I must swear that the debt is still outstanding.

### Collection Absence

*Vehanipra shelo befanav* — if I am collecting from Charlie's property while Charlie himself is absent, perhaps unreachable, *beit din* may attempt to defend his interests but also requires me to take an oath. This is analogous to the case of orphans: as the father is no longer present to defend himself, so too the absent debtor's interests require the additional protection of a *shvua*.

## Orphans Collecting

### Orphans Chidush

Now comes the *chidush* of our *mishna*: *vekhen hayetomim lo yifre'u ela bishvua*. This is the novel teaching — not orphans defending against a claim, but orphans coming to *collect* on their father's behalf. They present a *shtar* found among the father's papers showing that someone owes the estate money. And yet, even though they hold a *shtar*, they must take an oath before collecting. Why should heirs bearing a valid *shtar* need to swear at all?

### Orphans Oath Content

The content of the oath makes the reasoning clear: *shvua shelo pkadanu aba velo amar lanu aba veshelo matzanu bein shtarosav shel aba sheshtar zeh parua* — they swear that their father never told them this debt was paid, never instructed them that the *shtar* was no longer valid, and that among his papers they found no document indicating the debt had been settled.

The concern is twofold. First, a father on his deathbed will not go through every outstanding receivable and confirm it is still owed. But he also might have specifically said: "Reuven still owes me a thousand dollars — make sure you collect." If he never said the former but might have said the latter, the orphans swear to the negative. Second, perhaps clipped to Reuven's *shtar* was a note saying the debt had been paid — the father simply had not gotten around to returning the document. The orphans therefore swear that among the father's papers they found nothing that would indicate the *shtar* had already been satisfied.

### Posthumous Sons

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka adds: *afilu nolad haben le'achar misat ha'av harei zeh nishba venotel* — even if a son was born after the father's death, he still takes the oath and collects. One might have thought that since such a son could never have received instructions from his father, the oath about what the father said is obviously unnecessary. But the oath also covers the possibility of discovering relevant paperwork — and a posthumous son has just as much access to the father's files as any other heir. He must still swear.

### Deathbed Exception

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *im yesh eidim she'amar ha'av bishas misaso shtar zeh eino parua* — if witnesses testify that the father on his deathbed declared that Reuven still owes the money, that the debt has never been paid, then *hu notel shelo bishvua* — the heirs collect without any oath. When we have clear evidence that the father himself confirmed the debt's validity up to the moment of his death, no further *shvua* is needed.

## Oaths Without Claim

### Suspicion Oaths

The *mishna* continues: *ve'elu nishba'in shelo beta'ana* — these are individuals who take oaths even when no specific claim has been made against them. The *Gemara* clarifies: this does not mean there is literally no claim, but rather that there is no *bari* claim — no definite assertion. The demand for an oath is based on *shema*, on non-specific suspicion.

### Partners & Sharecroppers

*Hashutfin veha'arisin* — partners and sharecroppers. If I am in a business partnership with Michael and I suspect — without knowing specifically — that he has been cooking the books or skimming off the top, I can demand that he take an oath. The same applies to a sharecropper: if I suspect my sharecropper has not been honest about the yield, I can demand an oath even without a concrete allegation.

### Agents & Women

*Veha'apitropin* — not here an *apitropus* for orphans, but an agent who effectively has power of attorney or manages investments. *Veha'isha hanoseit vehanotenet betoch habayit* — or a woman who manages the household finances or runs the family store with her husband's money, and he suspects she has been skimming. She too can be required to swear on the basis of non-specific suspicion.

### Ben HaBayis

*Uven habayit* — similarly, when one of several brothers manages the estate they have all inherited, the other brothers can demand an oath that the books are accurate and that he has been entirely honest in managing the shared assets.

### Non-Specific Suspicion

One might ask: what if the brother says back, "What exactly are you accusing me of? Show me specifically what I did wrong." Even if the demanding party cannot point to anything concrete and says only *retzoni shetishva li* — "I just want you to swear to me" — the respondent is still *chayav*. Non-specific suspicion, with no particularized allegation at all, is sufficient grounds in these relationships.

### Partnership Dissolution

*Chelku hashutfin veha'arisin, ein yachol lehashvi'o* — once partners have dissolved their arrangement or the sharecropper's year has ended, one can no longer come after the fact and demand an oath based on that prior relationship. Once the *shutfut* is dissolved, the grounds for demanding an oath on that basis are gone.

### Gilgul She'ua

There is, however, one avenue that remains: *nitgalgel lo shvua mimakom acher* — if I can establish that Michael must take an oath about some other matter, then *migalgalin alav et hakol* — we can extend that oath to encompass everything, including matters from the prior partnership. But absent such a hook, once the partnership has ended, one cannot generate a new oath obligation on that basis alone.

---

## Shmitta Annuls Oath

### Shmitta Release

A final, entirely separate point: *hashvi'is meshmetes et hashvua*. If Michael owed me a *shvua* — for instance, there was a dispute about fifty dollars, he admitted to fifty and was obligated to swear regarding the other fifty, but then *shmitta* intervened — the *shmitta* year exempts him from that *shvua* as well. Just as the sabbatical year releases monetary debts, it releases the obligation of a *shvua* that stands in the place of such a debt. This is a discrete ruling, not connected to the preceding discussion of partnerships.

## Gemara Sources

### Torah Oaths

The *Gemara* now turns to examining the underlying sources and reasoning. *Kol hanishba'in shebatorah nishba'in velo meshalmin* — the basic principle in Torah law is that you take an oath and you do not pay. The *Gemara* asks *menalan* — where do we derive this?

### Scriptural Source

Rather than simply observing the pattern across the Torah's cases, the *Gemara* cites a verse: *de'amar kra ulakach be'alav velo yeshalem* — the *pasuk* in the section dealing with a *shomer* states that *shvuat Hashem tihye bein shneihem*, an oath of God shall stand between them, and then describes the owner "taking" while the other party does not pay. The "taking" here is conceptually the *shvua* itself — not merely a corroboration of a claim, but something more like a form of settlement. The oath is what the party owes; it is, so to speak, the payment. This framing resonates with the closing *mishna* about *shmitta*: if the *shvua* is itself the debt, then *shmitta*, which annuls debts, annuls the *shvua* as well. *Mi she'alav leshalem lo shvua* — the one who would otherwise pay instead takes the oath and does not pay.

### Hired Hand Oath

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai shena sachir dekav'u lei rabanan* — why did the Rabbis establish that a hired worker takes an oath and collects? *Demishbata veshakil* — that he swears and then collects his wages, overturning the standard Torah model.

### Rabbis' Reason

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *halachos gedolos shanu kan* — great laws were taught here. The *Gemara* then analyzes each word: *halachos*? This term typically denotes *halacha le'Moshe miSinai*, but these are Rabbinic institutions. The *Gemara* emends: *ella takanos gedolos shanu kan* — great *takanos* were established here. And the word *gedolos* prompts the *Gemara* to ask: does the use of "great" imply there are also lesser ones? This leads to Rav Nachman's formulation in the name of Shmuel: *takanos kvu'os shanu kan* — established, fixed *takanos*. The essential point is clear: what was done here is remarkable. The entire principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* would seem to preclude the worker from swearing and collecting, yet the Rabbis established exactly that. This is a big *takana*.

## Takana for Workers

### Big Edicts

The *Gemara* elaborates: *akru rabanan lishvua miba'al habayis* — the Rabbis took the *shvua* away from the employer. Tosafot notes that the employer in this scenario is actually *kofer hakol* — he says he paid — so ordinarily he would not even be obligated to swear; he would simply walk away. But even in contexts where there is some circumstantial basis for a rabbinic *shvua*, the Torah model would have been for him to take the oath and be exempt. The radical innovation is *uremuha asachir* — the Rabbis placed the oath on the worker, who then takes it and collects. The Rabbis introduced an entirely new model.

### Economic Argument

Why did the Rabbis do this? *Mishum kedei chayav* — because poor Michael needs to make a living and cannot afford to have employers cheat him out of his wages.

### Oath Transfer

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *mishum kedei chayav dechas sachir kansinan lei leba'al habayis*? So what? It is all very sympathetic, but why is it fair to the employer? Perhaps he genuinely paid his workers and now they will swear falsely and collect a second time. The *Gemara* responds: *ba'al habayis gufei nicha lei demishtaba sachir veshakil* — the employer actually prefers this arrangement.

### Owner's Benefit

Why would the employer prefer it? *Ki heichi detagru lei poalim* — so that he can attract workers. If the employer were the one taking the oath whenever disputes arose, word would spread that he does not pay his workers, that he swears falsely, and no one would want to work for him. Under the Rabbinic system, however, every worker knows that if there is a dispute, he can take an oath and collect. That assurance makes workers willing to be hired, which is to the employer's benefit.

### Worker Counterargument

The *Gemara* immediately turns this argument around: *aderaba, sachir nicha lei demishtaba ba'al habayis venapik* — on the contrary, the worker would prefer that the employer take the oath and be exempt from paying. Why? *Ki heichi deligruhu ba'alei batim* — so that other employers would be willing to hire him. Word gets around about a worker who is always in dispute with employers, always swearing and collecting. Such a worker develops a reputation that makes prospective employers wary. This is not so different from the fear of speaking out in certain workplace situations — the concern that asserting one's rights, even legitimately, leads to being seen as difficult and reduces future employment opportunities.

### Competition Rebuttal

But any economic argument that can be made for one side can be made for the other. The *Gemara* therefore dismisses the entire line of reasoning. *Ba'al habayis al korcho agar* — the employer hires workers because he has no choice; he needs them. *Sachir nami al korcho mitagor* — workers seek employment because they have no choice either. Neither side has sufficient economic leverage to make the argument cut decisively one way or the other.

## Owner's Distraction

### Owner Distracted

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ella ba'al habayis tarud bpoalav* — the real reason is evidentiary. It is more likely that the worker is telling the truth, because the employer has many workers and many concerns and may simply not remember whether he paid a particular worker. The worker, focused entirely on his own wages, has no such confusion. *Tarud bpoalav* — he is distracted by his workers.

### Tosafot Objection

Tosafot raises a pointed question: if it is so much more likely that the employer has made an honest mistake, why not simply have him pay without requiring an oath at all? The *Gemara*'s framing seems to suggest that when the employer says "I definitely paid Michael," what we are really hearing is someone who *thinks* he paid but may not have. If the employer's certainty is effectively to be treated as a *shema* — a "perhaps" — then why is an oath even necessary? Just pay the worker.

Tosafot frames this precisely: even if I only hired one worker — not a large-scale employer with many employees — the *Yerushalmi* says it does not matter. The employer has a thousand other things on his mind: the children want to go somewhere, there are groceries to buy, there are other obligations. The worker has exactly one thing on his mind after finishing the job, which is getting paid. So even with a single-worker scenario, it is more likely that the employer forgot he paid than that the worker forgot he collected.

### Placate Owner

The *Gemara* answers Tosafot's objection with a surprising formulation: *kedei lehafis da'ato shel ba'al habayis* — to placate the employer. The answer is not framed in terms of residual uncertainty about whether the worker is also potentially lying. Rather, the oath is presented as a concession to the employer's feelings: we do believe Michael is owed the money and that is why he collects, but the employer is insisting and upset — "I paid him!" — so to placate him, we require the worker to take an oath. It is a striking answer: the oath serves a psychological function rather than a strictly epistemic one.

### Alternatives

The *Gemara* considers whether there are simpler solutions. *V'leisiv leh be'edim* — why not simply require witnesses when paying workers? The *Gemara* responds: *tricha lei milta* — that system is too burdensome and impractical. *V'leisiv leh meikara* — why not pay up front before the work begins, when the employer is not yet distracted? The *Gemara* answers: *shneihem rotzin b'hakafa* — both parties actually prefer deferred payment.

### Delayed Payment Preferen…

The employer prefers to pay later because he does not always have cash on hand, and also because advance payment reduces the worker's incentive to perform diligently. The worker prefers to be paid later because he does not want to worry about carrying cash during the workday — he may fear losing it or having it taken from him — and would rather receive it when he is ready to go home and use it. Both sides choose the arrangement that creates the very conditions for these disputes.

## Wage Disputes & Timing

### Wage Amount Dispute

The *Gemara* now uses a series of questions to introduce additional *halachot* within this framework. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: if there is a dispute not about whether wages were paid but about what the wages were — *katzatz*, the fixing of the wages — so that the worker says you agreed to pay me twenty dollars and the employer says I agreed only to ten — in that case the worker does not take an oath and collect.

### Different Rule Question

The *Gemara* asks: *afilu katzatz nami*? Why is this any different from the payment dispute? If the employer is distracted and likely to make honest mistakes, why does that not apply here as well?

### Amount Remembered

The *Gemara* answers: *ktzitza vadai midkar dachir leh* — people remember clearly what wage was agreed upon. The amount set at the outset of a contract is a defined, specific number that both parties fix at the start of their arrangement. It is not subject to the same distraction-induced confusion that affects whether a particular payment was actually made during a busy workday.

### Time Period Passed

Another *halacha* emerges: if the time for payment has passed — *avar zemano* — the worker may not take an oath and collect. The normal rule is that a day worker must be paid by nightfall: *lo talin pe'ulat sachir* — do not let the daily wages of the hired hand sleep overnight. If Michael worked during the day and it is now the following morning, and he is only now claiming he has not been paid, the rule shifts: he may no longer take an oath and collect.

### Bal Talin Presumption

The reasoning: *chazaka ein ba'al habayis over bebal talin* — there is a presumption that the employer would not transgress the prohibition of *bal talin*. We presume the employer would be careful about this, and therefore once the night has passed without a claim being made, the employer's denial is believed.

---

## Nightfall Focus

### Owner Focuses at Night

But this raises a *Gemara* question: did we not just say that the reason the worker is believed during the day is that the employer is distracted? Surely the employer's distraction would also apply at nightfall — he will have thought he paid during the day and will not bother paying again at night. The *Gemara* answers: *hanei mili mikamei delimtei zman chiyuveh* — the distraction applies only before the time of payment obligation has arrived. *Ki mati zman chiyuveh rami anafshei umidkar* — once nightfall comes and the obligation to pay kicks in, the employer stops and focuses: he forces himself to remember whether he has paid each worker. It is during the bustle of the day, before the moment of obligation arrives, that his distraction operates.

### Day vs Night Question

This generates a further question: if at nightfall the employer will stop and consciously review whom he has paid, why does that not resolve the problem even when a claim is made during the day? The suggestion is: if Michael claims unpaid wages at 5:00 PM, rather than having him take an oath immediately, why not simply wait until nightfall, at which point the employer will focus and, if he truly forgot to pay, will go find Michael and pay him?

### Defensive Psychology

Tosafot provides a penetrating psychological answer. Once the employer has already told Michael at 5:00 PM "I paid you," he will be defensive. He has staked a position. When nightfall comes and he reviews his payments, he will not redo that analysis with fresh eyes — he has already resolved the question in his own mind by denying it earlier. His prior denial will foreclose genuine reconsideration.

### No Reconsideration

By contrast, if Michael never made a claim during the day, then at nightfall the employer approaches the question without a prior defensive position. He genuinely asks himself: did I pay Michael? And if he genuinely did not, he will realize it and go pay him. The difference is not about the time of day per se; it is about whether a prior assertion has already calcified the employer's position. Once denied during the day, the nightfall review is no longer genuinely open.

### Double Chazaka

After nightfall has passed without payment, and the worker only makes his claim the following morning, a double *chazaka* operates against him.

### Two Presumptions

The two presumptions are: first, *ein ba'al habayis over bebal talin* — the employer would not transgress letting wages sleep over; and second, *ein sachir masheh secharo* — the worker would not have waited this long to demand his wages. A hungry worker wants his pay. The fact that he is only now coming the next morning and was not pressing the claim before nightfall is itself evidence that he was already paid. Between these two *chazakot*, the employer is believed and the worker does not take the oath.

## Witnesses for Hiring

### Rav Nachman's Rule

Rav Nachman said in the name of Shmuel: *lo shanu ela secharo be'edim* — this entire framework — the worker taking an oath and collecting — applies only when the worker was hired with witnesses, meaning there is proof of the employment relationship.

### Migo Argument

If Michael has no witnesses that he was even hired to mow the lawn — no one saw him hired, no one saw him working — then *mitoch sheyachol lomar lo lo secharticha me'olam* — the employer could simply say "I never hired you, you are making this up entirely." Since he could have made that broader denial, he is believed when he says the narrower claim: "I hired you and I paid you." *Yachol lomar lo secharticha venatati lecha secharecha* — he may say I hired you and I paid you, and this *migo* exempts him from the worker's claim.

Tosafot raises a difficulty with this reasoning: the entire basis for the Rabbinic *takana* was that the employer is making an honest mistake, not that he is consciously lying. A *migo* argument — "I could have said X, so I am believed when I say Y" — is a tool that operates in the realm of conscious assertion, not honest confusion. If we believe the employer is genuinely confused rather than deliberately dishonest, it is not clear how the *migo* helps. Tosafot addresses this tension, though the full resolution belongs to a broader discussion.

### Yesher Koach

Rav Yitzchak responded to this analysis: since the employer admitted that he did hire Michael — he said "I hired you and I paid you" — even though he could have denied the hiring entirely, the *migo* operates in his favor with respect to the payment claim. By voluntarily admitting the hiring when he could have denied it, he gains credibility on the payment. *Amar lei Rav Yitzchak yasher* — well said, well done.

### Rabbi Yochanan Agrees

*V'chein amar Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan independently stated the same rule: the worker's ability to take an oath and collect applies only where the hiring was done with witnesses.

### Reish Lakish Silent

The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan's view is cited without reference to Reish Lakish, implying they might have differed. Did Reish Lakish disagree? Two versions are given. One: *ika de'amri mishta hava shati lei veshatik* — Reish Lakish was drinking something and could not speak up at that moment. Another: *ika de'amri mishha hava shahi lei veshatik* — Reish Lakish was waiting to defer to Rabbi Yochanan as his *rebbi* and therefore held back from speaking. But in the interim, as the *Gemara* notes from Rashi — *uvtoch kach yatza Rav Yitzchak mibeis hamidrash* — Rav Yitzchak had already left the *beit hamidrash*, and by the time Reish Lakish was ready to speak the moment had passed.

### Rav Menashia Repeats

The *Gemara* adds: *itmar nami amar Rav Menashia bar Zavid amar Rav* — it was likewise stated that Rav Menashia bar Zavid said in the name of Rav: *lo shanu ela secharo be'edim* — only if he was hired with witnesses. *Aval secharo shelo be'edim, mitoch sheyachol lomar lo lo secharticha me'olam, yachol lomar lo secharticha venatati lecha secharecha* — but if without witnesses, since the employer could have denied the hiring entirely, he is believed to say he hired and paid. This is the same rule as above, independently transmitted through Rav Menashia bar Zavid, confirming the position.
