# Bava Batra 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boundaries & Sixth

### Boundaries vs. Estimate

The 106th daf opens a new *mishnah* dealing with a familiar theme: the sale of land described as a *beis kor* — but now with an important variation. Where the earlier *mishnayos* discussed a sale framed primarily in terms of acreage ("a *beis kor* more or less," "a *beis kor* exactly"), this *mishnah* introduces the case of *besimanav uvemetzarav* — a sale defined by explicit boundary markers. The paradigm here is the purchase of *sadeh Ephron* by Avraham, where the Torah enumerates the field's borders, its cave, and every tree within its boundaries with meticulous precision. When a seller specifies the northern, southern, eastern, and western borders with that same exactitude, the description of acreage becomes a secondary element: the true object of the sale is the bounded plot itself, whatever it contains.

This shift in emphasis has a direct *halachic* consequence. Because the boundaries are so clearly defined, the parties are presumed to understand precisely what is being conveyed, and therefore a greater tolerance for measurement error is permitted before any adjustment is required. Unlike the case of *beis kor* sold by estimate — where the tolerable margin is only one twenty-fourth — here the *mishnah* permits a discrepancy of up to one sixth. As the Rashbam notes, that is exactly *kiflei kiflayim* — fourfold more tolerant — than in the estimate case. The logic is straightforward: the claim of acreage is less weighty than the claim of explicit boundaries, because anyone can go measure the plot themselves.

A further point concerns the mechanism of adjustment. When the sale was purely by acreage estimate and the discrepancy exceeded the tolerable threshold, the Gemara previously considered the possibility that the seller could offer to return money in lieu of land. Here, by contrast, where the boundaries were spelled out with precision, that option seems unavailable: the parties clearly contracted for what lies within those borders. Adjustment would take the form of a price correction, not a return of land — absent bilateral agreement.

### Mishnah Ambiguity

The *mishnah*'s precise language, however, generates a significant interpretive difficulty. It states: *pochos mishtus higio* — less than a sixth, the recipient must accept; and *ad shtus yinake* — "up to a sixth, he must deduct." The first clause is clear enough. But the second clause is puzzling. What does "up to a sixth" mean? If the rule is that amounts exceeding one sixth must be corrected, the *mishnah* should have said "more than a sixth, deduct." Instead it says "up to a sixth, deduct" — an odd formulation that can be read in more than one way.

One reading is that when you are off by more than a tolerable amount, you must make corrections until you are down to only being a sixth off — meaning that at exactly a sixth, no correction is needed. Another reading is that "up to and including a sixth" triggers the obligation to deduct. The ambiguity in the word *ad* — whether it is inclusive (*ad v'ad bichlal*) or exclusive (*ad v'lo ad bichlal*) — is at the heart of the dispute that follows. It is worth noting that this ambiguity produces not a sweeping theoretical disagreement but a precise boundary question: on which side of the one-sixth line does the law land?

### Huna vs. Yehuda

The Gemara records: *itmar* — Rav Huna says *shtus kepochos mishtus*: a discrepancy of exactly one sixth is treated like less than a sixth and is therefore tolerable, requiring no adjustment. Rav Yehuda disagrees: *shtus keyoser mishtus* — a full sixth is like more than a sixth, and the seller or buyer must correct for it.

Both are interpreting the same ambiguous *mishnah*. Rav Huna reads it as: *pochos mishtus v'shtus bichlal hevia* — "less than a sixth, including a sixth itself, must be absorbed." He then reads *ad shtus yinake* as referring to amounts greater than a sixth — using *ad* in the exclusive sense, meaning the correction kicks in only when you exceed a sixth. The Rashbam notes that Rav Huna actually prefers to read the first clause not as *pachos* (less than) but as *pichet* (reduced by), shifting the meaning subtly to "reduced by up to a sixth." That reading, while strained by the *mishnah*'s text, is needed to make his position internally coherent.

Rav Yehuda's position is straightforwardly the better reading of the *mishnah* as written: *pachos mishtus hevia* — less than a sixth, absorb it; *ad shtus v'shtus bichlal yenakei* — more than a sixth and up to and including a sixth, must be deducted. Here *ad* is inclusive. This reading is clearly more natural.

### Ad Inclusive/Exclusive

To illustrate the stakes of this dispute, consider a simple schematic. Take a base amount, say one *kor*. One sixth of one *kor* represents the boundary of tolerance. If the sale came in at one and one-sixth *kor*, the parties are at exactly that line. If it came in higher still — say one and a half *kor* — they are comfortably over it.

When the *mishnah* says *ad shtus yinake*, the question is: does the obligation to deduct extend "up to and including a sixth," or only "up to but not including a sixth"? If you are off by any amount greater than one sixth all the way down to — but not reaching — a full sixth, must you correct? And if you land precisely at a sixth, is that the point where you are still safe, or the point where you have crossed into the zone requiring correction?

Rav Huna says: exactly at a sixth, you are still safe. The *mishnah*'s *ad* is exclusive. Rav Yehuda says: exactly at a sixth, you must correct. The *mishnah*'s *ad* is inclusive. Both positions are linguistically available from the *mishnah*'s text, and the dispute is whether the word *ad* in this context carries the one or the other meaning.

## Judges' Assessment

### Braita Contradiction

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* that seems to complicate things. *Besimana u'metzra picheis shtusa o hosif shtusa* — when selling land by explicit boundaries, if the seller was off by a sixth less or a sixth more — *harei hu keshum hadayanim* — it is like the assessment of judges, and *higiyu* — the party must accept it.

The connection to *shum hadayanim* requires explanation. The standard case of a judges' assessment (*shuma*) arises when a creditor comes to collect a debt and the debtor has no cash. The court assesses the value of a piece of the debtor's land and the creditor takes it at that assessed value. Now suppose the judges' assessment turns out to have been mistaken — either they overvalued or undervalued the property. How much error is tolerable before the assessment is invalidated and must be redone? The principle is that up to one sixth, the assessment stands as binding. A discrepancy of one sixth or more voids the assessment entirely.

This is precisely the problem the *braita* creates. It says: when you are off by a sixth in a land sale by boundaries, it is *like the assessment of judges* — and then says *higiyu*, you must accept it. But we know that the assessment of judges is *voided* at a sixth! So this comparison seems to contradict itself: if being off by a sixth is "like the assessment of judges," the consequence should be that the deal is void — yet the *braita* says the contrary, that you accept it.

One might ask how this contradiction is resolved, or whose position this *braita* supports. That is precisely what the Gemara addresses next.

### Partial Comparison

Rav Huna responds: *u'l'ta'ameich higia katani* — if you want to use this *braita* as proof that a sixth voids the deal by analogy to the judges, you must contend with the fact that the same *braita* concludes *higiyu* — you must accept it. So the *braita* cannot be saying that a sixth voids the deal here.

Rather, says Rav Huna: *ella keshum hadayanim velo keshum hadayanim* — the *braita* is comparing this case to the judges' assessment only partially. *Keshum hadayanim leshtusa*: it is "like the assessment of judges" only in the sense that a sixth is the *relevant threshold* — the comparison is invoked to signal that we mean a full sixth, not less. But *lo k'shuma d'dayani* — the legal consequence is the opposite. *D'ilu hasam bitul mekach v'ilu hacha higiyu*: there, the deal is voided; here, the party must absorb it.

This is a striking reading of the *braita*. It deploys the analogy to *shum hadayanim* not to import its ruling, but merely to borrow its numerical threshold. The *braita* is essentially saying: the amount we are talking about is the same amount that matters in the context of judges' assessments — namely one sixth — but the *halacha* runs in the opposite direction. The Rashbam notes that this could also be read to support the position that a sixth is indeed tolerable, and that the word *higiyu* is the operative ruling of the *braita*, with the comparison to judges serving only as a reference point for the magnitude involved.

## Rav Papa Case

### Facts & Claim

The Gemara now records a case involving Rav Papa himself. *Rav Papa zavin ara me'hahu gavra* — Rav Papa bought land from a certain person. The seller told him the field was twenty *girive* in size. Rav Papa then measured it and found it was only fifteen. Since fifteen is three-quarters of twenty, this is a discrepancy of one quarter — far beyond the one-sixth tolerance established by the *mishnah*. Rav Papa accordingly came before Abaye seeking a price adjustment.

### Savar v'Kabil

Abaye refused the adjustment. *Amar leh savarta v'kabilta* — you assessed it and accepted it. Abaye's ruling invokes the principle that when a buyer knows the true measurement of the land at the time of purchase, he cannot later claim adjustment based on the seller's inflated representation.

The Gemara anticipates the obvious objection: but the *mishnah* says that even in the case of explicit boundaries, one sixth is the threshold, and here the discrepancy is far greater — surely an adjustment is warranted? *Hani mili heicha d'lo kam leh b'gavah* — that rule applies only when the buyer did not know the true measurement and was relying on the seller's claim. *Aval heicha d'kam leh b'gavah* — but when the buyer knew the true acreage, *savar v'kabil* — he assessed the situation and accepted it.

### Rashbam Explanation

How is it possible that Rav Papa knew the field was fifteen *girive* yet the seller was claiming twenty, and Rav Papa still agreed to the price? The Rashbam explains the case as follows: Rav Papa knew the true acreage was fifteen. The seller, however, claimed it was twenty and set a price accordingly. Rav Papa, knowing the seller was wrong but choosing not to correct him, reasoned thus: if the seller is claiming twenty and the actual measurement is fifteen — a discrepancy well beyond one sixth — then under the normal rules of the *mishnah*, I would be entitled to a price adjustment after the fact, because the seller implicitly warranted that the acreage would come in within the tolerable threshold. So Rav Papa stayed quiet, let the deal go through at the twenty-*girive* price, and then came to court expecting an adjustment.

Abaye rejected this reasoning. Since Rav Papa knew all along what the true acreage was, the *mishnah*'s framework — which is designed to protect a buyer who was genuinely in error — does not apply to him. His acceptance of the price, given his actual knowledge, constitutes a binding *savar v'kabil*: he assessed the reality, he accepted the terms, and he cannot manufacture a claim for adjustment by strategically keeping silent.

This is an interesting principle with broader implications in *halacha*. The *savar v'kabil* doctrine applies similarly in cases of *mum* — a defect in a purchased item. If the buyer was aware of the defect at the time of purchase, even if the defect later proves worse than expected, he generally cannot claim *bitul mekach*, since he knowingly accepted the conditions. The concept of *kim leh b'gavah* — being familiar with, or standing in knowledge of, the reality one is confronting — is the operative standard.

One difficulty, acknowledged by the discussion, is the evidentiary question: how does Abaye know that Rav Papa knew? Presumably the specific circumstances of the case — perhaps testimony or Rav Papa's own concession — made this clear. The principle itself, however, is well established: innocent error permits adjustment; informed acceptance does not. What remains unresolved is the hypothetical edge case in which the seller genuinely believed the acreage was twenty, the buyer knew it was fifteen, and there is no way to reinterpret the seller's language as meaning "worth twenty." In that scenario, the Gemara does not provide a clear answer.

## Lot Division

### One vs. All Draw

The Gemara pivots to a related topic introduced through the theme of *shum hadayanim* and other mechanisms of assessment. The *taniya* introduces a ruling of Rabbi Yose: *ha'achim shehalku* — when brothers divide an inherited estate by drawing lots. The typical scenario is that a father has left multiple plots of land and several sons must determine who receives which plot. Rather than negotiating directly, they employ a *goral* — a lottery. The lots are cast and whoever draws which number receives the corresponding field.

The question is this: must all the brothers draw their lots before the division takes effect, or does the act of one brother drawing his lot suffice to finalize the entire division? This matters because one can imagine the following scenario: Reuven draws his lot and receives field three. Shimon and Levi have not yet drawn. Does Reuven's draw already lock in the entire division, or can the remaining brothers still undo it?

### Mishnah Rule

The *mishnah* rules: *keivan she'ala goral l'echad mehem kanu kulam* — once one brother has drawn his lot, all of the brothers have acquired their respective portions. Reuven's drawing of lot three not only gives Reuven his field; it simultaneously vests Shimon and Levi in whatever fields the remaining lots correspond to, even though they have not yet individually drawn.

The practical consequence the Rashbam draws from this is instructive. Once Reuven draws, Shimon and Levi jointly own the remaining two fields. They may continue the lottery to determine who gets which of those two, or they may reach their own agreement on division. They are not compelled to continue the lot process, because their joint ownership of the remaining two fields is already established and they may divide it however they see fit through other *halachically* valid means.

### Eretz Yisrael Model

But what is the *ta'ama* — the underlying reason — that drawing lots operates to effect an acquisition at all? Ordinarily, acquiring land requires a formal *ma'aseh kinyan*, specifically *chazakah* — taking physical possession through some demonstrative act. Drawing a slip of paper from a box seems to have none of the hallmarks of a standard *kinyan*.

Rabbi Elazar explains: *k'chalukat Eretz Yisrael* — the lot division is modeled on the division of the land of Israel under Yehoshua. Just as the tribes received their portions through the casting of lots, so too in the case of brothers dividing an estate, the lot mechanism works as a valid mode of division. *Ma techila b'goral af kan b'goral* — just as it was by lot then, so too here it is by lot.

This immediately raises a further question: *ee ma lehalan b'kalpi v'urim v'tumim* — there, the division was accompanied not merely by a box with slips but by the *Urim v'Tumim*, the oracular breastplate of the *Kohen Gadol*, which provided divine confirmation of the allocation. Do we require the same apparatus here? Clearly not — but the question presses us to articulate what it is about the lot-drawing that actually functions as the operative act.

### Gmiras Da'at

Rav Ashi addresses this directly. *B'hahu hana'ah d'ka tzayti l'hadadi gamri u'makni l'hadadi* — it is by virtue of the mutual benefit and shared agreement of all parties that each one has fully committed himself to the division and granted his portion to whoever receives it by lot. This language invokes the concept of *gmiras da'at* — the full and final resolution of intent, the wholehearted willingness to make the transaction binding.

This is an important principle in the laws of *kinyan*. Normally, *kinyan* requires two elements: a formal *ma'aseh kinyan* — a recognized act of acquisition — and *da'at*, the intent of both parties. In some contexts, where *gmiras da'at* is especially strong and unambiguous, a less formal act can suffice. The classic example is *chalipin* — the exchange of a kerchief or similar article — which does not look like a transfer of ownership but rather like a handshake that signifies finality of commitment. Because everyone understands and agrees that this act seals the deal, *gmiras da'at* supplies what the act itself might seem to lack.

What Rav Ashi is saying is that in the brothers' lot division, the shared agreement to proceed by lottery, the mutual readiness of all parties to be bound by the outcome, substitutes for the *Urim v'Tumim*. The brothers' *gmiras da'at* functions as the binding element that makes the lot mechanism work — even without the divine apparatus that accompanied the division of Eretz Yisrael.

### Rashbam on Rav Ashi

The Rashbam makes a critical textual observation. Rav Ashi's statement is introduced with the words *amar Rav Ashi*, not *ella amar Rav Ashi* — "rather, said Rav Ashi." In the Gemara's technical vocabulary, when an answer is introduced with *ella* ("rather"), it replaces the previous answer entirely. When it is introduced without *ella*, it modifies or supplements the previous answer. The absence of *ella* here therefore means that Rav Ashi is not proposing an independent basis for the lot's efficacy; he is explaining *why* the model of Eretz Yisrael can operate without the *Urim v'Tumim* in later generations. The two answers work together.

The Rashbam articulates this clearly: *kach zakuk ani lefareish* — I am compelled to explain it this way, because *ein kasuv basfarim ella amar Rav Ashi* — the text does not say "rather said Rav Ashi." *Hilchach lo ba listor ta'am mori Rabbi Elazar ella letartzo* — Rav Ashi comes not to displace Rabbi Elazar's reason but to resolve the difficulty it raises.

The practical implication is significant. If Rav Ashi's answer merely supplements Rabbi Elazar's and does not stand on its own, then the lot mechanism's validity is still grounded in the model of *chalukas Eretz Yisrael*. This means that other modes of division — a coin flip, an agreement to take north and south halves, or any other creative arrangement — would not work through this mechanism alone. They would require a proper *chazakah* or *kinyan sudar* to finalize. Only the *goral*, anchored in the Eretz Yisrael paradigm and animated by the brothers' *gmiras da'at*, constitutes a complete act of division.

## Third Brother

### Case Setup

The Gemara now presents a scenario that tests the finality of the lot division. Two brothers divided an estate between themselves by drawing lots. Then a third brother emerges — he was overseas and unknown to the others — and produces proof of his kinship. He now claims his share of the inheritance. The two brothers have already completed a full division and each has received his portion. What happens now?

### Rava: Void All

Rava rules: *batla machaloket* — the entire division is voided. The estate must be redistribted from the start among all three brothers. In effect, the lot division between the two is treated as having been made in error — the parties assumed a two-way split when the situation called for a three-way split — and therefore it cannot stand.

### Shmuel: Carve Out

Shmuel takes the opposite position: *mekamtzim mekamtzim* — you carve out. The term *mekamtzim* evokes *kemitza*, the act of gathering or pinching — as in the *kohen*'s pinching of the flour offering. What Shmuel means is that the existing division is kept as fixed as possible. Each of the two brothers contributes proportionally from his portion to constitute a third share for the newly discovered brother, while the structure of the original division otherwise remains intact.

### Fairness Concerns

This position immediately raises concerns about fairness, which the Rashbam addresses and Tosafot presses more forcefully. Consider the practical mechanics. Suppose Reuven received field A and Shimon received field B. The arriving third brother Levi is entitled to one third of the estate. Under Shmuel's approach, Reuven gives up part of field A and Shimon gives up part of field B, and from those contributed portions Levi receives his share. But what does Levi get? He may end up with two fragmented pieces of land — one slice from each brother's plot — which may or may not be of equal or comparable quality. He had no voice in the lottery and no opportunity to receive a coherent, unified parcel through a fair draw. This seems deeply inequitable.

The Rashbam's position, as best reconstructed, is that Shmuel's *mekamtzim* mechanism stands, and that a *beis din* would need to be involved to ensure that what Levi receives is assessed as genuinely fair — but the original division itself is not undone.

Tosafot is not satisfied. The possibility that Reuven and Shimon each carve out a piece — and that Levi is left with two disjointed fragments, potentially the worst land from each plot — strikes Tosafot as fundamentally unjust. Tosafot therefore proposes that what *mekamtzim* really means in practice is that a new lottery is held. The three brothers draw lots again, but the new lottery is structured to preserve as much of the original division as possible. For example, if the new draw assigns Levi the same general territory as field B, then Shimon is shifted to a reduced portion of that field, and Reuven's entire original portion is left undisturbed. Alternatively, if the new draw assigns Levi a portion spanning both original fields, each brother contributes equally. In this way, Levi gets a fair chance at an equitable allocation, and the disruption to the original division is minimized rather than eliminated. Tosafot acknowledges that this reading is not *pshat* in the Gemara, but is compelled by the fairness concerns that Shmuel's simple *mekamtzim* seems to leave unresolved.

## Kam Dina

### Two Divide

The Gemara now introduces a parallel case that tests the limits of these principles. There are three partners — not brothers inheriting, but partners who jointly own property. Two of the three go ahead and divide the jointly held estate without the knowledge or consent of the third. The third partner then shows up. Is the division made by the two valid, or must it be entirely redone?

This case seems troubling at first glance. How could two of three partners divide property that also belongs to the third? The Rashbam inserts at this point that a *beis din* was involved in overseeing the division — the court assessed the portions and confirmed their fairness — which is what gives the two-person division any validity at all. Even so, the third partner was absent and had no say. The Gemara nonetheless appears to take for granted that such a division can be legitimate in certain circumstances.

### Error vs. Knowledge

The Gemara draws the crucial distinction: *hasam neichisa adata d'vei tlata* — there, in the partners' case, they proceeded with full awareness that there were three partners. The division was structured with three portions, and each was assessed as a fair one-third share. There was no mistake about the number of parties or the intended allocation. The third partner's absence was a practical matter, not a conceptual error.

*Hacha lo neichisa adata d'vei tlata mei'ikara* — here, in the brothers' case, the two who divided did not know there were three brothers at all. Their division was premised on the assumption that there were only two heirs. That premise was false. The entire division was therefore infected by a fundamental error from the outset, and Rava holds that it cannot stand.

This distinction between mistake and knowledge resolves the apparent tension. When the division was made with full awareness of all relevant facts — even if one party was absent — the framework of the division was sound, and the result is valid. When the division was made under a false assumption about how many heirs existed, the framework itself was flawed, and the entire division must be voided.

### Kam Dina Challenge

Rav Pappa challenges Abaye from a different angle. He invokes Shmuel's position of *mekamtzim* — carving out — as an application of the broader principle of *kam dina*, the idea that a valid legal act or judgment, once done, should not be fully reversed. *L'meimra d'kam dina* — Shmuel's approach implies that what was done remains in force to the maximum extent possible, with adjustments made only as necessary.

Rav Pappa then points to another ruling, attributed jointly to Rav and Shmuel, that seems to invoke the same logic: if one says *kora b'shloshim ani mocher lach* — I am selling you this *kor* for thirty — and the buyer takes possession of twenty-nine *se'ah* before backing out, *rishon rishon kanah* — each unit as it was acquired is valid. The buyer successfully took possession of twenty-nine units even though the deal was never completed. One might extend this logic and say: the buyer should be able to hold those twenty-nine even if he did not want to complete the sale, because *kam dina* — what was already done stands.

### Partial Acquisition

The parallel Rav Pappa draws is this: just as Shmuel in the brothers' case tries to preserve the original division to the extent possible rather than void it entirely, so too in the case of the *kor* sale, one might preserve each unit's acquisition as valid even if the whole transaction fell apart. The principle in both cases would be: do not undo what was already completed; adjust only at the margins.

This argument has a certain intuitive appeal. If twenty-nine of thirty units were validly acquired, why should the failure to complete the thirtieth unit retroactively unravel what was already done? And by the same token, if two brothers validly divided an estate through a proper lottery, why should the appearance of a third brother unravel the entire division rather than just requiring a marginal adjustment?

### Intent Requirement

The Gemara's resolution distinguishes the cases on the basis of the parties' intent. *Hasam avad rabanan milta d'nicha lei l'mocher v'nicha lei l'lokei'ach* — in the case of the *kor* sale, the Sages established the rule that *either* party may retract until the final unit is acquired, because *that is what both the seller and the buyer want*. When you frame a transaction as selling a *kor* for thirty, both parties understand that the deal is meant to be completed as a unit. Until the last *se'ah* is transferred, neither side is fully bound — and both sides benefit from preserving that right to retract. This is a necessary feature of commercial life: parties need to know they can negotiate and adjust until a deal is truly final.

The brothers' lot division operates differently. There, the whole point of the lottery is to achieve a division that is *pleasing to both parties* — each accepted the lot's outcome as binding. The division was made with a specific framework of agreement. When that framework was built on a false premise — the false belief that there were only two heirs — the consent underlying the division was not truly free and knowing. It is not merely a matter of adjusting numbers at the margin; the *gmiras da'at* that gave the division its validity was formed under a mistake, and therefore the division itself cannot be maintained.

The broader question — the principle of *kam dina* and how far it extends in cases of innocent error — remains open at the end of the daf, carrying forward into the continuing discussion.
