# Yevamot 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get from Overseas

### Get Overseas Intro

The daf opens on 25a with a *mishna* that, at first glance, seems more at home in *Masechet Gittin* than in *Yevamos*. *Ha-mevi get mi-medinat ha-yam* — the one who brings a *get* from overseas. As is familiar from the opening *mishna* of *Gittin*, a messenger who delivers a *get* from *chutz la'aretz* must declare *bifnei nechtav bifnei nechtam* — that the document was written and signed in his presence, in order to validate its authenticity. The *mishna* here rules that such a messenger, *lo yisa ishto* — may not subsequently marry the newly divorced woman. The reason is evident: his interest in marrying her would cast retroactive suspicion on the validity of his testimony.

### Murder Confession

The *mishna* extends this principle in a striking direction. If the witness declares *haragti* — I killed the husband — or *haragnu* — we killed the husband — he likewise cannot marry the wife. What is remarkable is that the *mishna* makes no move to prosecute him for murder; the entire concern is the integrity of the testimony that permits the woman to remarry. *Rabbi Yehuda* draws a distinction: if he says *haragti*, I killed him, she may not remarry from his testimony; but if he says *haragnu*, we killed him, she may. The precise basis of this distinction will emerge in the *gemara*.

### Reason for Restriction

The *gemara* articulates the logic underlying the *mishna*'s ruling about the overseas *get*: *ta'ama de-medinat ha-yam* — the reason he cannot remarry applies specifically when he brings the *get* from overseas, *de-alei kasamchina*, because we are relying on his testimony. But *aval me-eretz yisrael*, if he delivers the *get* within *Eretz Yisrael*, where no such declaration is required, *de-lav alei kasamchina* — since we are not relying on his testimony — *yisa ishto*, he may marry the woman.

### Husband Death Testimony

The *gemara* then raises a challenge: in the case where the witness testifies that the husband has died, *de-lav alei kasamchina* — we do not ultimately rely on him in the same direct sense, because the principle established elsewhere is *isha dayka u-minsava* — the woman herself is trusted to investigate. The *gemara* elsewhere grapples with the conceptual difficulty of permitting remarriage on the basis of a single witness; the resolution it offers is that since the woman is fully aware of the severe consequences should the testimony prove false — should her husband turn out to be alive — we rely entirely on her own careful investigation following the testimony. On this reading, the operative reliability is hers, not the witness's.

If so, one might ask, why can he not marry her? If we are essentially relying on the woman rather than on him, why should his interest in the outcome disqualify him? The *gemara*'s answer brings us to the next distinction.

### Death vs Get

*Hatam leka ksava hacha ikka ksava* — there is still a critical difference between the case of testifying to the husband's death and the case of delivering a *get* within *Eretz Yisrael*. Even when the testimony about death is ultimately bolstered or even superseded by the woman's investigation, the witness is still testifying to facts; there is still some testimonial act that requires integrity. By contrast, when delivering a *get* within *Eretz Yisrael* — where no declaration is required — the document effectively validates itself. *Kidtnan, mah bein get l'misa — shehaksav mochiach* — what is the difference between divorce and death? The written document proves its own legitimacy.

The underlying principle is the well-known rule that *eidim shehachatumim al hashtar k'mi shenechkra edusan b'veis din* — witnesses whose signatures appear on a document are treated as if their testimony has already been examined before a court. Within *Eretz Yisrael*, the signatures are presumed reliable without external validation; the need for *bifnei nechtav uvifnei nechtam* arises only when the document originates from *chutz la'aretz*, where the reliability of signatures is less certain.

## Splitting Testimony

### Rape Witness Case

*Hu nihu d'lo yisav iteseih, ha l'acher tinasei* — he himself cannot marry her, but she may marry another man. With this the *gemara* transitions to a celebrated discussion, introduced by Rav Yosef. If a man testifies *ploni revani le'onsi* — a certain man had intercourse with me against my will — then *hu ve'acher mitztarfim le'hargo*: he may join with another witness to have that man executed by the court. Since he himself was a victim, he bears no culpability and retains his standing as a witness.

But if he says *birtzoni* — it was done willingly, I was a consenting participant — then *rasha hu*. He has admitted to a transgression. The Torah states *al tashet yadcha im rasha lihyot ed chamas* — do not place your hand with a wicked person to be a corrupt witness — which excludes a *rasha* from giving testimony. By his own admission he has rendered himself invalid, and his testimony implicating the other party cannot stand.

This introduces the concept of *palginan dibura* — the question of whether we may split a witness's statement, accepting the portion that relates to another person while discarding the self-incriminating portion. Here, Rav Yosef rules that we cannot: the moment he admits to willing participation, he has disqualified himself entirely, and we cannot bracket that admission and salvage the remainder of his testimony.

The parallel to the *mishna*'s case of murder is apparent. There, too, the witness is confessing to a serious crime. If we cannot split the testimony in the case of the rape witness, we might expect to be equally unable to split it in the murder case — and thus unable to use the testimony to permit the woman to remarry at all.

One might suggest a distinction: in the testimony about the murdered husband, we are operating in the domain of *edus isha*, about which the Rabbis were especially lenient. Perhaps in that domain, a more flexible standard applies, including the possibility of *palginan dibura*.

### Rav Yosef vs Menashe

The *gemara* now presents the positions of Rav Yosef and Rav Menashe in structured opposition, and this is a striking *gemara* to follow carefully.

Rav Menashe states: *gazlan de-divreihem kasher le-edus isha* — a person who is rabbinically invalid as a witness, such as a gambler, is nonetheless valid for testimony concerning a woman's ability to remarry. But *gazlan de-divrei Torah pasul le-edus isha* — one who is biblically invalid is excluded even in the more lenient domain of *edus isha*.

It is worth noting that *Tosafos* raises the question of whether the invalidity of a witness due to monetary corruption — where the logic is that such a person will shade testimony for financial gain — necessarily applies to one invalidated merely as a habitual sinner who poses no such temptation to lie about the death of a husband. The *gemara* does not distinguish along these lines.

The *gemara* then asks: should we say that Rav Menashe holds like *Rabbi Yehuda* in the *mishna* — who refuses to accept the testimony of one who says he is the murderer — while the *Tanna Kamma* permits it? The *gemara* rejects this forced identification. *Amar lach Rav Menashe* — Rav Menashe would say: *ve-ein adam mesim atzmo rasha*. A person cannot make himself into a *rasha* by his own testimony against himself. This is the principle of *palginan dibura* applied in the affirmative: because one cannot legally self-incriminate in matters of *misa* or *malkos* — confession of a criminal offense has no halachic standing — the self-incriminating portion of the statement is simply discarded. We retain only the relevant claim that the husband is dead.

But if Rav Menashe holds that we can do *palginan dibura* — split the testimony — why does he still rule that a biblically invalid witness is excluded from *edus isha*? And conversely, if Rav Yosef holds that *palginan dibura* is rejected, how can the *mishna's Tanna Kamma* accept the murderer's testimony at all?

The *gemara* resolves this by assigning each position a different hinge. Rav Menashe, who does *palginan dibura*, would therefore disagree with Rav Yosef in the rape case — he would split the testimony there as well and not disqualify the witness for the self-incriminating part. Conversely, Rav Yosef, who refuses *palginan dibura* in the rape case, can still accept the testimony in the *edus isha* context by holding that even a biblically invalid witness is acceptable there — and therefore the self-incriminating admission becomes irrelevant in any event.

The *gemara* also notes a possible synthesis: one might hold that neither *palginan dibura* alone nor the leniency of *edus isha* alone would suffice, but that the combination of both considerations together permits the testimony. Thus the *Tanna Kamma*, who accepts the murderer's testimony, could be understood as relying on the convergence of the two grounds — the unique latitude of *edus isha* and the doctrine of *palginan dibura* — each of which independently might not cross the threshold.

### Bandit Confession

A *baraisa* is then introduced in which the Rabbis challenge the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*. A bandit, *listis*, was being led out to execution in a place called Kaputkia. Before his death he instructed those present to go and tell the wife of Shimon ben Kohen: *ani haragti et ba'alah* — I murdered her husband. The story records that the woman's husband was subsequently declared dead and she was permitted to remarry. Here, then, is a clear case of accepting the testimony of someone who confesses to murder — precisely what *Rabbi Yehuda* would reject.

### Partial Responsibility

*Rabbi Yehuda* deflects the challenge: *be'omer ani hayiti im horgav* — I was with those who killed him. The statement attributed to the bandit, he maintains, did not mean that he personally committed the murder but rather that he was present, part of the group, a participant of some degree — but not the one who physically carried it out. Thus *Rabbi Yehuda* does not necessarily hold that one who directly admits to the killing can be used as a witness even for *edus isha*.

The *gemara* objects: but the *baraisa* explicitly identifies the man as a *listis*, a bandit, and says he was going out to execution — surely that implies he was convicted of murder? The *gemara* answers: *beit din shel ovdei kochavim delo dayki vekatli* — the non-Jewish courts would execute on the basis of suspicion without the rigorous evidentiary standards of a Jewish court. His being led to execution by a non-Jewish court does not halachically establish him as a murderer.

The upshot is that both the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabbi Yehuda* agree that if a witness is clearly established as a murderer by proper *beit din* standards, the testimony cannot be relied upon — at least according to Rav Menashe's position. The disagreement between them is narrower: does the witness's own self-incriminating statement suffice to disqualify him? The *Tanna Kamma* says no, because we invoke *palginan dibura*, or because we are lenient in *edus isha*, or both. *Rabbi Yehuda* says yes — a self-incriminating admission of personal murder does disqualify, though not an admission of mere presence.

## Vow Annulment Case

### Vow Prohibition

The *mishna* turns to yet another scenario in the running theme of persons who are prohibited from marrying a woman they have in some way facilitated into being divorced. *He'chacham she'asar et ha'ishah b'neder al ba'alah* — a *chacham* who was asked to annul a vow and was unable to do so, with the result that the vow rendered the woman forbidden to her husband. This is actually a more extreme scenario than it might initially appear: it is not merely that the husband threatened divorce over the vow, but that the vow itself made cohabitation impossible. Such a *chacham* may not subsequently marry the woman.

### Beit Din Exception

However, the *mishna* adds important qualifications. If the *chacham* was involved in a *mi'un* proceeding — a minor girl who was married through the consent of her brothers or mother exercising her right of verbal refusal — or supervised a *chalitzah* ceremony, and he did so as part of a *beit din*, then *yisa'enah*, he may marry the woman afterward. The reason is *mipnei shehu beit din* — because he acted as part of a court. When he acts as one member of a three-person panel, the decision cannot be attributed to him personally, and there is far less reason to suspect that his judgment was distorted by personal interest.

### Expert Authority

The *gemara* now interrogates the precise configuration assumed by the *mishna*. If the *chacham* annulled the vow as a single authority, the *gemara* asks: *chad mi matzi matir* — can a single person annul a vow? After all, Rav, citing Rabbi Chiya or Rabbi Chiya bar Avin, taught: *tana hataras nedarim b'shlosha* — the release of vows requires three. Alternatively, if it was done by three, why is any one of them suspect at all?

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a ruling of Rav Chisda in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *b'yachid mumcheh* — even where three laypeople are normally required, a single recognized expert, a *mumcheh*, may annul vows. The *mishna* is therefore discussing the case of such an individual expert. And the concluding clause — that if he acted as part of a *beit din*, he may marry the woman — is distinguished precisely because in the *beit din* context he was not acting unilaterally.

### Document Witnesses

The *gemara* extends the analysis. *Ta'ama d'beit din, ha bitrei lo* — if the exemption from suspicion applies specifically when there is a full *beit din*, then perhaps two witnesses acting jointly should still fall under suspicion? A *baraita* is cited: witnesses who signed a field-sale document or a woman's *get* — *lo chashu chachamim l'davar zeh* — the Sages were not concerned that a witness might have forged the document in order to profit from the transaction or marry the woman. Any time one is not acting entirely alone, the concern dissipates.

The *gemara* concludes that the *mishna*'s use of *beit din* language is incidentally conveying an additional ruling: do not think that *mi'un* may be performed before two — *mi'un b'gimmel*, it requires three. But for the purpose of permitting remarriage, even two witnesses acting together suffice.

## Marriage Occurred

### Divorce Required

*Ibaya lehu: knas mahu sheyotzi?* — the question is finally posed that has been lurking beneath all the preceding cases: if one of these men actually went ahead and married the woman despite the prohibition, must he now divorce her? This is the *knas* question — whether the Rabbis imposed a punitive requirement of divorce in cases where the initial marriage should not have taken place. Notably, *Rashi* understands this question as applying both to the case of the *chacham* and the *neder* and to the case of the one who brought the *get*. The *Rashba*, however, holds that the question applies only to the case of the *chacham* in the current *mishna*.

### Kahana vs Ashi

*Rav Kahana amar kanas motzi* — Rav Kahana rules that he must divorce. *Rav Ashi amar kanas eino motzi* — Rav Ashi rules that he need not. A tradition was taught in the school of Rav Papa supporting the position of Rav Ashi. When the Rabbis pressed Rav Ashi — is this a received tradition or a logical inference? — he replied: *matnitin hi* — I derive it from the *mishna* itself.

### Rumor Doctrine

The *mishna* cited as support is the ruling concerning one suspected of an affair who subsequently converted or whose circumstances changed: *harei zeh lo yichnos v'im kanas ein motzi* — he should not have married, but if he did, he need not divorce. From there the *gemara* draws the inference: *b'rinana lo mafkinan* — based on rumor and communal suspicion alone, we do not compel divorce.

*Hacha nami b'rinana lo mafkinan* — here too, the concern that the community might draw adverse conclusions does not rise to the level of requiring a divorce after the fact.

It is worth pausing on the disanalogy. In the case of the suspected affair, even if the rumor were true, the woman is still halachically permitted — the act was wrong but the marriage can stand. In the cases under discussion here, however, if the underlying concern is correct — if the *get* was forged or the husband is actually alive — the woman may be an *eishet ish*. The *Rashba*'s position that the question applies only to the *neder* case and not to the *get*-delivery case becomes understandable: in the *neder* case, even if the *chacham* manipulated matters, the husband still gave the woman a *get* of his own accord, and her divorce is fully valid. But in the *get*-delivery case, doubts about the legitimacy of the document or the testimony about death carry potentially severe consequences, making the *b'rinana* framework less clearly applicable.

## Remarriage Conditions

### Already Married

*V'kulam shehayu lahem nashim umetu mutarot l'hinasay lahem* — all these individuals, if they were already married at the time they delivered the *get* or testified to the husband's death, and their own wives subsequently died, the woman is permitted to marry them.

This is a remarkable *mishna*. Although *halacha* technically permits polygamy, the *mishna* operates on the sociological assumption that a man who already has a wife will not, as a practical matter, seek to take a second wife. This assumption is stated casually and taken for granted, which is itself striking — *halacha* permits something but the *mishna* treats it as effectively non-occurring for most men. Therefore, if a man was already married when he facilitated the divorce or testified about the husband's death, we do not suspect that he was manipulating events in order to free this woman for himself. He had a wife; he was not in the market for another.

One might counter: even if we assume he was not planning to take a second wife, he might have been planning to divorce his first wife unilaterally, which he is halachically entitled to do. This precise objection is taken up by the *gemara*.

### Divorce Distinction

*Metu in, nitgarshu lo* — the *gemara* infers from the *mishna* that if his wife died, the woman may marry him; but if he divorced his wife, she may not. A *baraita*, however, states *afilu nitgarshu* — even if they divorced their wives, the woman may marry them. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha d'havi ktata, ha d'la havi ktata* — there is no difficulty; the cases differ depending on whether there was preexisting marital strife.

### Pre-conflict Logic

*Rashi* explains the rule in a direction that may at first seem counterintuitive. Where there was preexisting strife — *d'havi ktata* — and he subsequently divorced his wife, the woman may marry him. Where there was no preexisting strife, she may not. The reasoning, as *Rashi* explains, is *d'achti lo natan da'ato*: if the couple was already in conflict before this woman came onto the scene, the decision to divorce was not generated by interest in the new woman. He would have divorced his wife regardless. The delivery of the *get* or the testimony about the husband's death was therefore not motivated by desire for this woman.

If there was no prior conflict, however, the divorce appears to have originated precisely when this woman became available — which creates the inference that the entire chain of events was engineered.

### Who Initiated

A second resolution is offered: *ha deargil hu, ha deargila hi* — in the case where he was the one pushing for divorce, she may not marry him; in the case where she was the one pushing for divorce, she may. If the wife initiated the desire to separate, then the husband was evidently committed to the marriage and his role in facilitating the other woman's divorce was not self-interested. If he was the one pressing for divorce, the suspicion is stronger.

The *gemara* elsewhere acknowledges that one can subtly maneuver a spouse into seeking divorce — but that level of psychological complexity is not developed further here.

### Intervening Marriage

*V'chulan shenisu l'acherim v'nitgarshu o shenit'almenu mutarot l'hinnase lahem* — if all these women went on to marry another man, and that second man then died or divorced them, the original messenger or witness may now marry the woman. Since she married an entirely different person in the interim, it is inconceivable that the witness's original act was designed to enable this eventual marriage to himself. No one fabricates an elaborate scheme to free a woman for another man's marriage, and then waits to see if that marriage will end, hoping eventually to marry her himself.

The *gemara* notes a further detail: the pairing of outcomes — husband testified dead, second husband divorces; or first husband gave *get*, second husband dies — should not necessarily be read as parallel. The *mishna* may intend a cross-matched reading, which also avoids any entanglement with the ruling of *Rabbi* that two deceased husbands may establish a *chazaka* of fatal ill fortune — a discussion reserved for later in *Yevamos*.

### Relatives Marriage

*V'chulan mutarot livneihem o l'acheihem* — all these women are permitted to marry the brother or the son of the person who brought the *get* or testified to the husband's death. The witness is not assumed to be lying and falsifying testimony so that a relative of his can benefit. The concern is confined to the witness's own potential interest; we do not project the suspicion outward to his family.

## Relative Analogy

### Affair Suspicion

The *gemara* challenges this ruling by comparing it to the law of one who is suspected of an illicit affair. The *gemara* cites: *ha'nitan min ha'isha asur be'ima uvevita* — one who is suspected of an affair with a woman is forbidden to marry her mother or her daughter — *uv'achotah* — and her sister. Once suspicion attaches, it is extended to close relatives. Why, then, are we not similarly concerned that the witness in our *mishna* might have delivered a false *get* or false testimony precisely in order that a brother or son could marry this woman, with the witness himself intending to continue an affair with her through access provided by the relative's household?

It is important to understand what the *issur* in the affair case actually is. *Rashi* clarifies: the Torah's prohibitions of a woman and her daughter, or of a wife's sister, are triggered only by marriage. One who had illicit relations with a woman before marrying her daughter has not technically violated *arayot* — that violation requires a marriage to be in place first. The concern in the affair case is therefore prospective: *shema le'achar sheyissa et imma o et bitta tizneh zo immo* — perhaps after marrying the mother or daughter, he will continue the affair with the original woman, and now it will constitute a full biblical violation of *issur arayot*. The family context creates opportunity and proximity.

So the question is: does the same logic apply here? If the witness's brother marries the woman, will the witness have opportunity — and motivation — to pursue an illicit relationship with her?

### Visit Frequency

The *gemara* offers a first answer: *nashi legabi nashi shechichan de'azlan, gavrei legabi gavrei lo shechichan* — women visit one another's homes frequently; mothers, sisters, and daughters spend time together and create natural opportunity for continued encounters. Men, however, do not visit their brothers' homes with comparable frequency. The access that the family-marriage dynamic creates is substantially less in the male-relative scenario.

### Wife Concern

*Ee nami* — alternatively, the *gemara* proposes: *nashi delo asran shechivotan ahadadei lo kapdi ahadadei; gavrei de'asran shechivotan ahadadei kapdi ahadadei* — women are not rendered halachically forbidden to one another by what one does with another woman's relative; therefore a wife is not especially vigilant about her husband's access to her own relatives. But a man whose brother's wife becomes sexually involved with his brother is in a serious situation: *she* becomes forbidden to *his brother*, potentially causing a forced divorce. Therefore brothers are acutely vigilant about one another's wives, which itself curtails opportunity.

This is a striking argument. The *gemara* is suggesting that the protective jealousy of the brother-in-law himself serves as a deterrent, in a way that does not operate in the case of a woman's female relatives.

### Father Exception

*Ee hachi, aviv nami* — if these are the operative distinctions, why does the *mishna* specify brothers and sons but not fathers? Should a father similarly be permitted to marry this woman? The *gemara* answers: *lo mibaya ka'amar, lo mibaya aviv* — of course the father may marry her, and the *mishna* need not even state it, because *de'bayit bereih mineih* — a son is embarrassed before his father and would not dare pursue an affair with his father's wife. *Aval beno delo bayit aviv mineih* — but a son's wife, regarding whom a father might feel he can take liberties with his own child — *eima lo*, one might have thought not to permit; *ka mashma lan* — the *mishna* teaches us that even so, the father is permitted to marry this woman.

The organizing concern throughout this entire section is thus not that someone would sin purely for the benefit of another person. We assume people sin for themselves. The question is whether marrying a relative would create opportunity and occasion for the witness to pursue an ongoing relationship with this woman. Given the distinctions in proximity, access, and marital stakes, the *gemara* finds sufficient grounds to confine the suspicion to the witness himself and not extend it to his male relatives.

## Achot Zekuka

### Setup Sisters

The daf concludes with a return to the *mishna* that has, in various forms, punctuated the entire discussion of *Yevamos*. *Arba'ah achim, shenayim mehem nesu'im shtei achayot* — four brothers, two of whom, Reuven and Shimon, are married to two sisters, Rachel and Leah. Reuven and Shimon both die.

### Yibum Restriction

Levi and Yehuda now face the *yibum* obligation. The *mishna* rules: *harei elu cholsot velo mityabmot* — these women must perform *chalitzah* and may not be taken in *yibum*. The reason is the configuration of *achot zekukato* — a *zika*, a quasi-marital bond, attaches each surviving brother to each of the widows. Levi cannot perform *yibum* with Rachel, because Rachel is connected through *zika* to Yehuda as well, making her *achot zekukato*. The same applies symmetrically to Yehuda with Leah. Even if Levi were to perform *chalitzah* with Rachel, he could not then proceed to *yibum* with Leah, because having performed *chalitzah* with Rachel, Leah would become *achot chalutzato* — the sister of his *chalutza* — which is itself rabbinically forbidden. The only resolution is *chalitzah* by both brothers with both women.

### Shammai Hillel

If one of them performed *yibum* in violation of this ruling, the question of whether the marriage stands becomes a matter of dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. *Beit Shammai* permits the marriage to remain, holding that the prohibition may have been only rabbinic and therefore does not require dissolution *bedi'avad*. *Beit Hillel* rules *yotsiu* — they must divorce. And indeed, the severity of performing *yibum* improperly here may rise to the level of a *d'oraita* violation — the *zika* may constitute a quasi-marriage, making the completed *yibum* an act involving a woman who remains in some sense *eshet ach* to the other brother.

### One Ervah

*Hayeta achat mehen asura alav al ha'echad issur ervah* — if Rachel is actually Levi's daughter, Rachel is categorically an *ervah* to him, not merely a *zekukah*. In that case, the *ervah* removes the *zika*: Levi has no *yibum* bond to Rachel at all, and Rachel therefore does not function as *achot zekukato* in relation to Leah. Levi may perform *yibum* with Leah directly. Yehuda, however, still has *zika* to both and cannot do *yibum* with either.

### Both Eruvin

*Hayeta achat mehen asura al zeh issur ervah vehashniya asura al zeh issur ervah* — if Rachel is Levi's daughter and Leah is Yehuda's daughter, each woman is a full *ervah* to one brother, eliminating his *zika* to her. Rachel is out of the picture for Levi; he may do *yibum* with Leah. Leah is out of the picture for Yehuda; he may do *yibum* with Rachel. *Ha'asura lazeh muteret lazeh veha'asura lazeh muteret lazeh* — the one forbidden to this one is permitted to the other, and vice versa. This is the case the *mishna* describes with the phrase *achotah keshe'hi yevamtah* — her sister when she is also her sister-in-law: where one is an *ervah*, the other may do *chalitzah* or *yibum* freely.

### Zika Question

*Shma mina yesh zika* — the *gemara* infers from all of this that *zika* must be a real halachic bond, because the entire structure of the *mishna* depends upon it. If there were no *zika*, *hanei mitrei batei ka'atu* — these women come from two separate households, there is no *tzarah* relationship between them. *Hai liyabbem chad, vehai liyabbem chad* — Levi could simply take Rachel and Yehuda could simply take Leah, with no conflict at all. The fact that the *mishna* prohibits this entire arrangement must be because *zika* creates a cross-linkage that generates the problem of *achot zekukato*.

### Death Prevention

The *gemara* anticipates a counter-position: *le'olam eima lach ein zika* — perhaps there is in fact no *zika*, and yet *yibum* is still forbidden here. The reason would be *mishum dekasavar asur levatel mitzvat yevamin* — because performing *yibum* with one woman might lead to the *mitzva* of *yibum* for the other being lost entirely. *De'ad meyabbem chad meis idach* — while Levi is in the process of completing *yibum* with Rachel, Yehuda might die, and then Leah would have no one to perform either *yibum* or *chalitzah* for her.

By contrast, if both brothers perform *chalitzah* with both women, that possibility is foreclosed: once *chalitzah* has been performed with Rachel, even if Yehuda dies, *chalitzah* can still be completed with Leah. The prohibition on *yibum* is thus not rooted in *zika* per se, but in the protection of the *mitzva* from being rendered impossible through circumstance. The *gemara* notes that this is an exceedingly far-fetched explanation, and the discussion will continue on the following daf.
