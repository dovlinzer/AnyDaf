# Gittin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava vs. Abaye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶבֶד שֶׁהֵבִיא גִּיטּוֹ, וְכָתוּב בּוֹ: ״עַצְמְךָ וּנְכָסַיי קְנוּיִין לָךְ״, עַצְמוֹ – קָנָה, נְכָסִים – לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **a slave who brought his bill** of manumission to a court, **and it is written in it: You and my property are transferred to you, he acquires himself** via this document, and he is emancipated. However, **he does not acquire** the **property** unless the document is confirmed in court through its witnesses, like other documents.

### Property & Freedom

The sugya opens with a scenario involving a single document — a *get* — that simultaneously transfers property to a slave and frees him. The document contains one line stating that all of the master's property is given to the slave, and the question concerns whether the *kiyum* performed through *befanai nichtav uvefanai nechtam* — the declaration required and permitted specifically for a *shtar shichrur* — also validates the property transfer embedded in the same statement.

### Rava: Split Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״כֹּל נְכָסַיי קְנוּיִין לָךְ״, מַהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁקָּנָה עַצְמוֹ, קָנָה נְכָסִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא עַצְמוֹ לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַגֵּט אִשָּׁה, אֶלָּא נְכָסִים לָא לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקִּיּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא!
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the bill of manumission stated: **All of my property is transferred to you, what is** the *halakha*? **Abaye said: Since he acquired himself** as a freeman, as he is included in the property mentioned in the document, **he acquires** the rest of the **property** as well. **Rava said to** Abaye: **Granted, he should acquire himself, just as it is in** the case of **a bill of divorce** of **a woman,** who is divorced when she brings the document herself. **However, he should not acquire** the **property, just as it is in** the case of the **ratification of typical** legal **documents.** If someone brings a typical document that deals with monetary matters that has not been ratified, the court will not rely on that document. So too here, as the bill of manumission, which includes a transfer of property, has not been ratified, he should not acquire the property.

Rava holds that it does not need to do both or neither. The effect of the *get* can be split: the aspect of the statement that applies to the slave's *shichrur* is validated by the *kiyum*, while the aspect that would transfer property to him is not. A single sentence can thus be partially effective.

### Abaye: All or Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא קָנָה נְכָסִים – לֹא קָנָה עַצְמוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא נְכָסִים לָא לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקִּיּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא, אֶלָּא עַצְמוֹ לִיקְנֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַגֵּט אִשָּׁה!
>
> **Translation:** After hearing Rava's objection, **Abaye then said** the opposite: **Since he did not acquire the property, he does not acquire himself** either. **Rava said to him: Granted, he does not acquire the property, just as it is in** the case of the **ratification of typical** legal **documents; however, he should acquire himself, just as it is in** the case of **a bill of divorce** of **a woman,** who can bring her own bill of divorce and testify about it.

Abaye takes the opposing position: it is all or nothing. One cannot apply a *kiyum* selectively to one legal effect of a statement while leaving the other unaffected. Though notably, Abaye's position will shift over the course of the discussion.

## Palginan Dibura

### Rav Adda's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה, עַצְמוֹ – קָנָה, נְכָסִים – לֹא קָנָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה לְרָבָא: כְּמַאן – כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר פָּלְגִינַן דִּיבּוּרָא;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says:** With regard to **both this and that,** both in the case when the bill of manumission states: You and my property, and when it says: All of my property, **he acquires himself** but **he does not acquire** the **property. Rav Adda bar Mattana said to Rava: In accordance with whose** opinion do you say this? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that **we divide the statement.** In other words, even if there is only one document or a single testimony, containing one general statement, it can be divided so that the court accepts it in part and rejects the rest.

Rav Adda bar Matna challenges Rava directly: whose opinion are you following? Is this not the position of Rabbi Shimon, who maintains *palginan diburei* — that we split the words, or rather the legal effects of words?

### Mishnah: Reservation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַכּוֹתֵב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לְעַבְדּוֹ – יָצָא בֶּן חוֹרִין. שִׁיֵּיר קַרְקַע כָּל שֶׁהוּ, לֹא יָצָא בֶּן חוֹרִין. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הוּא בֶּן חוֹרִין, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״כֹּל נְכָסַיי נְתוּנִין לִפְלוֹנִי עַבְדִּי חוּץ מֵאֶחָד מֵרִיבּוֹא שֶׁבָּהֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:8): **One who writes,** i.e., gives via a document, **all of his property to his slave,** the slave **has been emancipated,** but if he **reserved** for himself even **any amount of land,** then he **has not been emancipated,** as perhaps he reserved the slave for himself as well. **Rabbi Shimon says: He always** becomes **a freeman** regardless of the wording of the document, even if the owner reserved land for himself, **unless it says** in the document: **All of my property is given to so-and-so my slave, except for one ten-thousandth of it,** as in that case it is possible that the master meant to include the slave in the portion that he is not giving. Consequently, the slave is not emancipated. In any case, according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, when the document states: All my property, a distinction is drawn between the emancipated slave and the property, as claimed by Rava.

The source for this principle is a *mishnah*: if one writes all his property to his slave, the slave goes free — because the slave's own person is included among the property, so the master is effectively giving the slave to himself, rendering the document both a gift and a writ of manumission. However, if the master reserves even a minimal amount of land — *kol she-hu* — the slave does not go free, because the reservation implies that the transfer is incomplete, that something is being held back, and there is a sense that the slave himself may not be fully released.

### R. Shimon's Exception

Rabbi Shimon qualifies this ruling. He says the slave is *always* freed — *le-olam hu ben chorin* — with only one exception: if the master says "all my property is given to my slave except for one part in ten thousand of it." The difference between this case and a reservation of a specific piece of land is significant. When an abstract, fractional percentage is withheld, we do not know what is being held back. It could be that the thing being withheld is the slave himself. In that case, there is genuine ambiguity as to whether the slave was ever given his own person. But when something concrete like a house is excluded, and there is nothing left to transfer other than the slave himself, the master is effectively just freeing the slave — and Rabbi Shimon holds that this works.

### Tosafot's Explanation

Rashi offers his own reading of how Rabbi Shimon's position relates to *palginan diburei*, but the discussion here follows Tosafot's explanation, which is more directly illuminating for the issue at hand.

Tosafot explains the connection as follows. Suppose the master owns a house and a slave, and he declares: "I am giving you all my property except for my house." According to Rabbi Shimon, the slave goes free. But why should this work? The statement, taken as a whole, is an attempted transfer of property. If we say that excluding the house effectively means there is nothing left to give other than the slave himself, then this statement is really only operative with respect to the slave's *shichrur* — it cannot function as a gift of property because no property, apart from the slave's person, is actually being conveyed. The *gemara* therefore identifies this as a form of *palginan diburei*: you have one statement, "all my property," but it is only going to take legal effect with respect to the slave's freedom, not with respect to any transfer of assets. That, says Tosafot, is what the *gemara* recognizes as analogous to Rava's position: one statement, *kol nechasai konuyim lach*, validated by *kiyum* only as a *shichrur*, not as a gift. The parallel is precise.

## Ruling: R. Meir

### Halacha: R. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁקִּילֵּס רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁנֶּאְמְרוּ דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, קָרָא עָלָיו הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה: ״שְׂפָתַיִם יִשָּׁק מֵשִׁיב דְּבָרִים נְכוֹחִים״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Yosef bar Minyumi say** that **Rav Naḥman said: Even though Rabbi Yosei praised** the ruling of **Rabbi Shimon** with regard to this issue, **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** the first *tanna* in that mishna. The Gemara elaborates how Rabbi Yosei bestowed praise. **As it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Pe'a* 1:13): **When** these **matters were said** by the Sages **before Rabbi Yosei, he recited this verse about him: "He kisses the lips that give the right answer"** (Proverbs 24:26). Despite this praise, the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

At this point the *gemara* raises a challenge from a ruling attributed to Rav Nachman, as cited by Rav Yosef bar Minyumi: even though Rabbi Yoshiyah praised Rabbi Shimon, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir — which here means following the *Tanna Kamma* who holds that if anything is held back, if the transfer is in any way incomplete, the slave does not go free. This would appear to reject *palginan diburei* entirely, and by extension, to undermine Rava's position.

### R. Yosei's Praise

A *baraita* records that when the words of Rabbi Shimon were brought before Rabbi Yosei, he applied to them the verse: *sefatayim yishak meshiv devarim nechochim* — "lips will kiss the one who returns proper words." The verse is a mark of high praise. Tosafot offers a striking reading of what *sefatayim yishak* means in this context: when someone says something truly correct and compelling, those around him press their lips together — that is, they fall silent, because they have nothing to say in response. The brilliance of the answer leaves no room for rebuttal. It is not a warm kiss, but the silence of intellectual defeat.

### Despite Praise

Yet despite this extraordinary praise, the *gemara* affirms: we still rule like Rabbi Meir. We do not apply *palginan diburei*, and if anything at all is held back within the text, the *get* or *shichrur* does not take effect. This appears to be a decisive ruling against Rava.

## Shachiv Mera

### Dying Donor Recovers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁכָּתַב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לְעַבְדּוֹ, וְעָמַד – חוֹזֵר בַּנְּכָסִים, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר בָּעֶבֶד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Rav Adda bar Mattana's proof, the Gemara asks: **And did Rav Naḥman** actually **say this,** that we do not divide the statement? **But didn't Rav Yosef bar Minyumi say** that **Rav Naḥman said:** With regard to a person **on his deathbed who wrote all** of **his property to his slave,** and afterward he recuperated **and arose** from his illness, **he can retract** his transfer of property **with regard to** the gift of **the property** to the slave, **but he cannot retract** his transfer **with regard to** the emancipation of **the slave.**

The *gemara* immediately presses back. Did Rav Nachman actually rule categorically against *palginan diburei*? There is another ruling attributed to him, also via Rav Yosef bar Minyumi: in the case of a *shachiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — who writes all his property to his slave, and then recovers, what is the outcome?

### Split Effect Outcome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹזֵר בַּנְּכָסִים – מַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע הוּא, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר בָּעֶבֶד – שֶׁהֲרֵי יָצָא עָלָיו שֵׁם בֶּן חוֹרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **He can retract** the transfer of **the property,** as **it is the gift** of a person **on his deathbed.** By rabbinic decree, no formal act of acquisition is required for a gift of this kind, as it was given based on the assumption that the owner is about to die. If he does survive, the gift is canceled. **But he cannot retract** the transfer **with regard to** the emancipation of **the slave, as it has been publicized about** the slave that he has the **status** of **a freeman.** This shows that Rav Naḥman accepts the principle that we divide the statement, as one portion of this document is accepted while the other part is rejected.

The answer is striking: *chozer b'nechasim v'eino chozer b'eved* — he retracts the property but cannot retract the freeing of the slave. The property reverts because a deathbed gift is given on the assumption that the donor will die from his current illness; if he recovers, the gift returns to him. But the manumission does not revert, because *yatza lo shem ben chorin* — the slave has already been publicly proclaimed free, and once that status has been announced, it cannot be undone.

One might ask: if technically speaking the *shichrur* was part of a deathbed gift that reverts upon recovery, why should the slave's status be any different? The *gemara*'s invocation of *yatza lo shem* — a kind of proclaimed status — is admittedly unusual as a basis for *halacha*. The parallel offered in the shiur to a mistakenly announced Miss Universe winner captures the intuition: once the declaration has been made publicly, withdrawing it creates impossible social confusion. But how does that translate into hard *halachic* consequence?

### Tosafot on Institution

Tosafot addresses this directly. The entire institution of *matnas shachiv mera* is a rabbinic creation, and it operates under the parameters that the rabbis set for it. One of those parameters is that if you free a slave on your deathbed, even if you recover, the *shichrur* stands. That is simply how the rabbis structured the institution.

The key point for the present discussion, however, is what this ruling reveals about *palginan diburei*. The gift to the slave was made in a single statement — *kol nechasai knuyim lach*, all my property is given to you. And yet the outcome is that the slave receives his freedom but not the property. This is a classic application of *palginan diburei*: one sentence, two possible legal effects, and only one of them takes hold. It therefore appears that Rav Nachman himself — the very authority cited to rule against *palginan diburei* — applied it in the case of *shachiv mera*. This contradiction is what forces Abaye to clarify.

## Lav Krisus

### When It Applies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָאו כְּרוּת גִּיטָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: There,** where Rav Naḥman ruled in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, he did not do so because he holds that we do not divide the statement, as this is not the issue in dispute. Instead, **this is the reason: Because it is not a document that** fully **severs** the ownership of the slave. A bill of manumission must fully sever the bond between slave and master. Since the master left over some property, which may include the slave, for himself, the bill of manumission does not fully sever their relationship. However, with regard to the basic issue of whether or not we divide a single statement, Rav Naḥman agrees with Rava that we do divide a statement.

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction. He concedes that Rava is correct: we do say *palginan diburei*. The *kiyum* of a *shtar shichrur* can free the slave even if it does not simultaneously effect a property transfer. Similarly, in the *shachiv mera* case, the *shichrur* holds even though the property reverts. These are genuine applications of *palginan diburei*, and they are valid.

### Qualified vs. Unqualified

The question then is: why, according to Rabbi Meir, does the slave *not* go free when the master says "all my property except for this plot of land"? If we apply *palginan diburei*, we should be able to let the *shichrur* take effect even if we disregard the property transfer — just as we do in the *shachiv mera* case.

Abaye draws a critical distinction. In the *shachiv mera* case, the text of the document is unqualified. The statement *kol nechasai knuyim lach* contains no internal reservation — the split in effect occurs externally, through the operation of the *shachiv mera* institution, not through anything embedded in the language of the document itself. Similarly, when Rava speaks of a *kiyum* that validates the *shichrur* but not the property transfer, the document's text is clean; it is the *kiyum* mechanism that selectively applies. *Palginan diburei* means that the effects of an otherwise unqualified statement are split — one effect is operative, another is not.

### Complete Severance

But when the text itself contains a qualification — "all my property *except for* this house" — the problem is not a failure of *palginan diburei*. The problem is *lav krisus*: it is not a complete severance. The concept of *krisus* — severance — which the Torah requires of a *sefer krisus*, applies equally to a *get isha* and to a *get shel eved*. The parallel between divorce and manumission is striking precisely here: just as a *get isha* must effect a total and unconditional severance of the marital relationship, so too a *shtar shichrur* must effect a complete and unconditional release from servitude.

When the document itself contains a reservation — when within the language of the *get* something is being held back — that is not a case of splitting effects externally. That is a document that, on its face, is not a full *krisus*. It is an incomplete severance, and no amount of *palginan diburei* can remedy that defect. The text itself is defective. This is the reason Rabbi Meir holds that the slave does not go free when a specific property is excepted: not because we reject *palginan diburei* in principle, but because the document's language is internally qualified, and an internally qualified document is *lav krisus* — it is not a *get* at all.

## Irur & Validation

### Mishnah: Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ עָלָיו עוֹרְרִין, יִתְקַיֵּים בְּחוֹתְמָיו: עַרְעָר כַּמָּה? אִילֵימָא עַרְעָר חַד, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין עַרְעָר פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **if there are those who contest** a bill of divorce that was brought within Eretz Yisrael, where it is not necessary to state the declaration: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, **it should be ratified through its signatories.** The Gemara asks: **How many** people raise this **contestation? If we say** that this **contestation** is by **one** person, who claims that the bill of divorce is a forgery, **but didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Everyone agrees** that **a contestation** to a document may be brought by **no fewer than two** people?

The *mishnah* addresses a different scenario: a *get* given in *Eretz Yisrael*, where the declaration of *befanai nichtav uvefanai nechtam* is not required. If such a *get* is subsequently challenged — *yatza alav irur* — its signatures must then be validated.

### Number of Challengers

The *gemara* asks: how many challengers are needed to trigger this requirement? If only one person challenges the *get*, Rabbi Yochanan rules that universally, a single challenger does not constitute a valid *irur* — challenges require at least two people, and so a lone voice of protest should not require any validation. That seems to leave two challengers as the operative case. But if two people challenge the *get*, the *gemara* notes that we are now facing two witnesses against two witnesses — *trei u'trei ninhu* — and validation by an additional two witnesses would merely create an equal and opposite set of testimonies. *Mai chazit d'samchat ahani smoch ahani* — why should we favor one pair over the other? In that case, even successful validation would not seem sufficient.

### Husband's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא עַרְעָר תְּרֵי? תְּרֵי וּתְרֵי נִינְהוּ, מַאי חָזֵית דְּסָמְכַתְּ אַהָנֵי, סְמוֹךְ אַהָנֵי! אֶלָּא עַרְעָר דְּבַעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **But** rather, you will say that the **contestation** involves **two** people claiming that the bill of divorce is a forgery, who are subsequently countered by two others who ratify it. If so, **they are two and two. What did you see that you relied on these** witnesses? **Rely** instead **on these.** Why does the court accept the testimony of the witnesses who ratify the bill of divorce rather than those who contest its validity? The Gemara therefore concludes that this is referring to a case in which one person contests, **however,** the **contestation** is raised **by** the **husband** himself, who claims that the bill of divorce is a forgery.

The *gemara* resolves both difficulties by identifying the operative case: the challenger is the husband himself. The husband who issued the *get* now claims it was a forgery — that he never wrote it, that the signatures are not authentic. His challenge is weighty enough to require a response, and so we demand that the signatures be validated. But once validation is obtained through proper witnesses, those witnesses overcome even the husband's own objection. The husband's challenge demands validation; validation satisfies it.

## Get from Overseas

### Unable to Say

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לוֹמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״, אִם יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – יִתְקַיֵּים בְּחוֹתְמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who brings a bill of divorce from a country overseas and is unable to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, if** the bill of divorce **has witnesses** signed **on it, it should be ratified through its signatories.** The witnesses themselves or someone who recognizes their signatures should ratify it, in the manner of typical documents.

The *mishnah* states: one who brings a *get* from *medinat hayam* and is unable (*eino yachol*) to say *befanai nichtav uvefanai nechtam* — if there are witnesses whose signatures appear on the document, it may be validated through those signatures. This provides an alternative route: though the agent cannot offer the standard oral declaration, the *get* may still be delivered once the signatures are confirmed through independent testimony.

The *gemara* takes note of the language *eino yachol* — literally "he is unable" — and presses on its meaning. If the word is meant literally, suggesting a deaf-mute who is physically incapable of speech, then a prior question arises: can a deaf-mute (*cheresh*) serve as a *mevi get* at all? The *mishnah* in a different context explicitly excludes a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* from delivering a *get*, since they lack the full intellectual standing — *bar deiah* — required for this role.

## Gittin & Manumission

### Agunah & Iguan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְאֶחָד שִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים, שָׁווּ לַמּוֹלִיךְ וְלַמֵּבִיא, וְזוֹ אַחַת מִן הַדְּרָכִים שֶׁשָּׁווּ גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים לְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Both bills of divorce and** bills **of manumission are the same with regard to** the *halakhot* of **delivering** the document from Eretz Yisrael to a country overseas **and with regard to bringing** it from a country overseas to Eretz Yisrael, i.e., the agents for both types of documents must declare that it was written and signed in their presence, and their statement is accepted. **And this is one of the ways in which** the *halakhot* of **bills of divorce are equal to** the *halakhot* of bills **of manumission.**

The *mishnah* states that in this regard — the requirement of *befanai nechtam* when bringing a *get* into or out of *Eretz Yisrael* — a *get* given to a woman and a *shtar shichrur* given to a slave are treated identically. This is introduced as one of the ways in which *gittei nashim* and *shichrurei avadim* are compared.

Tosafot raises an important question here. We understand why the rabbis instituted *befanai nechtam* for a woman: without it, there is a serious risk of *agunah* — a woman unable to remarry because her *get* cannot be properly authenticated. The concern for the *agunah* is a powerful and well-recognized institutional driver. But what parallel concern applies to the slave? What is the slave's *igun*?

One might argue the concern is marriage-related as well: if the slave has received a *shtar shichrur* of uncertain validity, he is caught in between — he cannot marry a free woman because he may still be a slave, and he cannot properly marry a slave woman because he may already be free. But Tosafot rejects this as a parallel to *agunah*, because the solution seems obvious: simply do not hand the *shtar shichrur* to the slave until the signatures have been validated. In the interim, the slave remains a slave and may continue living with a slave woman. There is no abandonment, no limbo — just a delay in delivery. The woman's situation is different: she is living apart from her husband without access to the *get*, with no recourse. That is *agunah*. The slave does not face the same predicament.

Tosafot answers that *igun* for the slave is not primarily about marriage at all. Simply withholding his freedom is itself an *igun*. The inability to live a fully free life — to enter into a normal marriage in the social framework of free people, and to take on the full complement of *mitzvot* — constitutes a deprivation that the rabbis wanted to address. When a master is in the process of freeing his slave, we want to facilitate that *shichrur* as smoothly as possible, in the same way that we work to prevent a woman from becoming an *agunah*. The slave's interest in freedom is weighty enough to justify parallel institutional protections.

## Get from Overseas…

### Deafness/Muteness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לוֹמַר״? אִילֵימָא חֵרֵשׁ, חֵרֵשׁ בַּר אֵתוֹיֵי גִּיטָּא הוּא?! וְהָתְנַן: הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לְהָבִיא אֶת הַגֵּט, חוּץ מֵחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the statement: **He is unable to say? If we say** that this is referring to **a deaf-mute, is a deaf-mute fit to bring a bill of divorce? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (23a): **Anyone is fit** to serve as an agent **to bring a bill of divorce** to a woman **except for a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** all of whom may not be appointed as agents at all, as they are not intellectually competent according to *halakha*?

Returning to the question raised by the *gemara* regarding *eino yachol lomar*: the straightforward reading might be that the agent is mute, physically unable to speak. But as noted, the language of the *mishnah* specifically raises the scenario in the context of an agent who is otherwise qualified to bring the *get*, while the *mishnah* elsewhere explicitly excludes a *cheresh* — a deaf-mute — from serving in this role.

### Given While Able

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לָהּ כְּשֶׁהוּא פִּיקֵּחַ, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לוֹמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״ עַד שֶׁנִּתְחָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where** the agent **gave** the bill of divorce **to her when he was** halakhically **competent, but he did not manage to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, before he became a deaf-mute.** In other words, although at the time he was appointed he was fit to be appointed as an agent, he is currently unable to say anything.

Rav Yosef resolves this by clarifying the case: we are dealing with an agent who received the *get* and was fully competent — *pikeach* — at the time he took it. He was present, witnessed the writing and signing, and set out to deliver it. But before he could pronounce the declaration *befanai nichtav uvefanai nechtam*, he became a *cheresh*. He is therefore *eino yachol lomar* in the most literal sense: he was capable, he witnessed everything, but he is now physically unable to give voice to what he knows. In such a case, the *mishnah* permits the alternative route of signature validation. This is, admittedly, a peculiar case — the far simpler reading of *eino yachol* would be that the agent simply was not present at the writing. But the *gemara*'s insistence on the precise phrasing *eino yachol* pushes toward an interpretation of genuine physical incapacity.

## Gittin & Manumission…

### Befanai Nechtam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּרָכִים שָׁווּ גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים לְשִׁיחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים: שָׁווּ לַמּוֹלִיךְ וְלַמֵּבִיא; וְכׇל גֵּט שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵד כּוּתִי – פָּסוּל, חוּץ מִגִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים; וְכׇל הַשְּׁטָרוֹת הָעוֹלִים בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחוֹתְמֵיהֶן גּוֹיִם – כְּשֵׁירִין, חוּץ מִגִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages taught: In three ways the *halakhot* of **bills of divorce are equal to** the *halakhot* of bills **of manumission: They are equal with regard to one who delivers and one who brings,** i.e., if one takes a bill of divorce or a bill of manumission to a country overseas from Eretz Yisrael, or if he brings it from there, he is required to testify that it was written and signed in his presence. **And any document that has a Samaritan witness** signed **on it is invalid, except for bills of divorce and** bills **of manumission,** as Samaritan witnesses are permitted to serve as witnesses for these documents. **And** with regard to **all documents that are produced in gentile courts [*arkaot*], even though their signatories** are **gentiles,** they are **valid, except for bills of divorce and** bills **of manumission.** These documents are not valid when prepared by gentiles.

Returning now to the formal comparison between *gittei nashim* and *shichrurei avadim*, a *baraita* enumerates three ways in which they are alike. The first is the requirement of *befanai nechtam*: both a *get* delivered to a woman and a *shtar shichrur* delivered to a slave require the declaration when the document is being brought into or out of *Eretz Yisrael*. This was treated at length in the earlier part of the *masechet* and is now cited as the first formal point of parallel.

### Samaritan Witnesses

The second similarity — and a surprising one — is the rule regarding Samaritan witnesses. The *baraita* states: any document bearing a Samaritan (*Kuti*) witness is invalid — *except* for a *get isha* and a *shtar shichrur*. This is striking because these are precisely the documents one would expect to require the most rigorous standards, given that they effect fundamental changes in personal status. They are not merely evidentiary — they are the operative instruments of the status change itself.

Yet it is exactly this heightened significance that generates the *kula*. Because *gittei nashim* and *shichrurei avadim* are written with such extraordinary care and oversight, under so many more conditions and criteria than an ordinary commercial document, the mere fact that a Samaritan appears as a witness can itself be presumed to reflect carefulness. The assumption is that people involved in such a weighty process knew what they were doing — that this Samaritan witness was indeed a *kasher* witness, someone whose testimony can be relied upon. We will return to the specifics of this analysis, but the structural point is clear: the *chumra* of how *gittin* are written generates a *kula* in the assumptions we make about who signed them.

### Non-Jewish Courts

The third similarity is a *chumra*, and one that is entirely expected. As a general rule, documents produced by secular non-Jewish courts — *arkaot shel ovdei kochavim* — are valid, even when the signatories are non-Jews. For ordinary property documents, such as a bill of sale or a deed, we accept secular legal instruments as binding. However, *gittei nashim* and *shichrurei avadim* are explicitly excepted. A *get* or *shtar shichrur* issued by a non-Jewish court, signed by non-Jewish witnesses, is entirely invalid. These documents must conform to the requirements of *halacha*, and they cannot simply be processed through the general secular system as though they were property transactions.

### Dina D'Malchuta

Rashi's explanation of why secular property documents are valid is worth examining closely, because he identifies the conceptual foundation in an illuminating way. Rashi states simply: *dina d'malchuta dina* — the law of the land is law. In matters of property and commerce, the Jewish legal system defers to the governing civil authority, and therefore a secular document recording a property transaction between two Jews is fully binding under *halacha*. There is no requirement to conduct the transaction through Jewish legal mechanisms such as a formal *kinyan* under the rules of *Choshen Mishpat*; the secular system is sufficient.

But Rashi adds a further dimension. He notes: *aval hadinim nitztavu b'nei Noach* — matters of law and adjudication are among the *sheva mitzvot b'nei Noach*, the seven Noahide laws binding on all humanity. Among those seven is the commandment to establish courts and a functioning legal order. Rashi appears to be saying that the *Torah* itself validates the non-Jewish legal system in matters of property and civil law, precisely because the obligation to maintain such a system is itself divinely mandated for all nations. It is this divine mandate — the *sheva mitzvot b'nei Noach* — that provides the conceptual basis for *dina d'malchuta dina*: the secular legal order is not merely tolerated, it is endorsed by the *Torah* as a legitimate system for adjudicating property matters.

This, however, has its limits. Non-Jewish legal authority does not extend to matters of *halacha* and personal status. *Gittin* and *shichrurei avadim* are outside the *parsha* of non-Jewish law entirely — *lav bnei kritut ninhu*, non-Jews are not within the framework of *gittin* and *kidushin*, and their documents and signatures cannot effect the changes that only *halacha* can effect.

## Retraction Rights

### Both Retractable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר – בְּאַרְבָּעָה: הָאוֹמֵר ״תְּנוּ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי, וּשְׁטַר שִׁחְרוּר זֶה לְעַבְדִּי״, רָצָה לַחֲזוֹר בִּשְׁנֵיהֶם – יַחְזוֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to the statement of Rabbi Meir,** bills of divorce and manumission are equal **in four** ways, the three aforementioned *halakhot* and also with regard to a man **who says: Give this bill of divorce to my wife, or:** Give **this bill of manumission to my slave.** They are equal in that if he **desires to retract** his instruction **with regard to both of these** documents, before they have reached the woman or slave, **he can retract.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

Rabbi Meir identifies a fourth area of comparison between a *get* and a *shtar shichrur* — though on this point, Rabbi Meir and the *chakhamim* disagree. Rabbi Meir holds that if a man says "give this *get* to my wife" or "give this *shtar shichrur* to my slave," and then before delivery changes his mind, he may retract in both cases. Both a *get* and a *shtar shichrur* can be recalled before they are actually delivered.

### Sages' View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבָּנַן, מִנְיָנָא לְמַעוֹטֵי הָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מִנְיָנָא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** they state **the number** three **to exclude this** opinion **of Rabbi Meir,** by emphasizing that there are only three ways, not four. **However, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, what** does **the number** four serve **to exclude?** Wouldn't it have been enough to say that Rabbi Meir adds another case?

The *chakhamim* disagree on the second half. They agree that a *get* can be retracted before delivery — the act of divorce is not complete until the *get* reaches the woman's hand or domain, and the husband retains the ability to reconsider until that moment. But a *shtar shichrur* cannot be retracted once the agent has received it. Why? Because *zakhin l'adam shelo befanav* — one may represent a person's interests, and act on his behalf, even without being formally appointed and without the person's presence, when the act is unambiguously to that person's benefit.

### Zakhin Rationale

The logic is as follows. When the master hands the *shtar shichrur* to an agent and says "give this to my slave," there is no question whatsoever that the slave desires his freedom. Freedom is an obvious and unqualified benefit. Therefore, the moment the agent receives the *shtar*, he automatically becomes the slave's representative — not by the slave's appointment, not in the slave's presence, but by operation of the *zakhin* principle: since it is unambiguously in the slave's interest, the agent acquires it on his behalf. At the instant the agent takes the document, the slave's interests are already represented, and the *shichrur* effectively takes hold. The master can no longer retract.

### R. Meir Dissents

Rabbi Meir challenges this reasoning. He argues that one cannot simply assume it is in every slave's interest to be free. The *zakhin* principle applies when the benefit is certain and uncontested. But freedom carries obligations — a freed slave must now observe all *mitzvot*, enters a different social and legal world, and may in fact be leaving a situation of relative security and stability. Rabbi Meir's position is that we cannot automatically presume every slave wishes to be free, and therefore we cannot apply *zakhin* to represent the slave's presumed desire. Without *zakhin*, the agent is no more than the master's messenger, and the master may recall his messenger — and his *shtar* — until the moment of actual delivery.

It is worth noting the historical resonance of Rabbi Meir's position. The logic that a slave's interests are served by remaining enslaved was precisely the argument deployed by slaveholders in another era to justify the institution. That Rabbi Meir invokes a version of this reasoning as a matter of *halachic* analysis — questioning whether freedom is automatically and universally beneficial — is itself a striking feature of this passage, one that the *gemara* leaves for us to sit with.
