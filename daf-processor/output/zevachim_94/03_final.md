# Zevachim 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Leather & Flaying

### Opening: Blood & Launder…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִיתַּז עַל הָעוֹר עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּפְשַׁט – אֵינוֹ טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס, מִשֶּׁהוּפְשַׁט – טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Apropos laundering the blood of a sin offering from garments onto which it sprayed, the mishna discusses what is considered a garment. If the blood of a sin offering **sprayed onto the hide** of an animal **before it was flayed** from the animal, the hide **does not require laundering,** because its status is not that of a garment, which is susceptible to ritual impurity. If the blood sprayed onto the hide **after it was flayed, it requires laundering;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *daf* begins at the bottom of 93b, continuing the *halacha* concerning blood of a *chatat* that splatters on a garment and must be laundered in the *azarah*. The *mishna* now turns from the question of what type of blood triggers this obligation to the question of what type of material the blood must land on.

### Material Types

The *mishna* introduces the case of *nitas al ha-or ad shelo hupshat* — blood that splatters onto the skin of an animal that has not yet been flayed. In the *Beis HaMikdash*, this is a readily imaginable scenario: an animal has been slaughtered, the blood has been caught in a *kli shareis*, and as one rushes toward the *mizbeach*, some of it splashes onto the hide of the animal still lying there. The implicit assumption is that once the skin has been flayed it is sufficiently analogous to a garment — a *beged* — even though it has not been woven or fashioned into clothing. The question is at what stage, prior to flaying, the skin remains raw material rather than a textile equivalent.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

On this question, the *mishna* records that *eino taun kibus* — before the hide is flayed, there is no obligation to launder. *Mishehufshat* — once it has been flayed — *taun kibus*, laundering is required. This is *divrei Rabbi Yehuda*. This ruling carries an additional dimension beyond merely classifying leather as a *beged*: the very concept of *kibus*, laundering, is normally associated with cloth. The *pasuk* in the *Torah* uses the term *v'yichabes*, and one might have supposed this term simply does not apply to leather. The *mishna*'s ruling establishes that it does.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף מִשֶּׁהוּפְשַׁט; אֵינוֹ טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס אֶלָּא מְקוֹם הַדָּם, וְדָבָר שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה, וְרָאוּי לְכִיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Even** if the blood sprayed onto the hide **after it was flayed, it does not require laundering** until it is crafted into a vessel or garment that is actually susceptible to ritual impurity. This is the principle with regard to laundering: A garment must be laundered **only** in the **place** where **the blood** was sprayed, **and** only if it is **an item that is fit to become ritually impure, and** only if it is an item **fit for laundering.**

*Rabbi Eliezer omer af mishehufshat eino taun kibus* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that even after the hide has been flayed, there is still no obligation to launder. For Rabbi Eliezer, a further stage of processing is apparently required before the leather qualifies. One might add that at the point of flaying, it remains entirely uncertain what use the leather will be put to — it could be tanned, shaped into a vessel, cut into strips, or fashioned into a scroll. That ambiguity, as the *Gemara* will develop, is precisely the basis of the debate.

### Laundering Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הַבֶּגֶד וְאֶחָד הַשַּׂק וְאֶחָד הָעוֹר – טְעוּנִין כִּיבּוּס; וְהַכִּיבּוּס בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, וּשְׁבִירַת כְּלִי חֶרֶס בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, וּמְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה בִּכְלִי נְחוֹשֶׁת בִּמְקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ. זֶה חוֹמֶר בְּחַטָּאת מִקׇּדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the garment** mentioned explicitly in the Torah, **and the sackcloth, and the hide, all** of these **require laundering. And the laundering** must be performed **in a sacred place,** the Temple courtyard, **and the breaking of an earthenware vessel** in which a sin offering was cooked must be performed **in a sacred place, and scouring and rinsing of a copper vessel** in which a sin offering was cooked must be performed **in a sacred place.** With regard to **this** matter, **a stringency** applies **to a sin offering** more **than** it applies to **offerings of the most sacred order.**

The *stam mishna* then continues with three criteria that govern this *halacha* in general. First, *eino taun kibus ela makom hadam* — only the precise spot where the blood landed requires laundering; one need not launder the entire garment. Second, *v'davar she-hu ra'uy lekabel tumah* — the object must be fit to receive *tumah*. A scrap of cloth so small as to be unusable, for example, would not qualify. Third, *v'ra'uy lekibus* — the object must be suitable for laundering. The *mishna* then lists the qualifying materials: *echad habeged v'echad hasak v'echad ha-or* — whether a cloth garment, sackcloth made of goat hair, or leather, *teunin kibus*, all require laundering. *V'hakibus b'makom kadosh* — the laundering itself must take place within the *azarah*.

The *mishna* also anticipates the subsequent topics of the *perek*: *v'shviras kli cheres b'makom kadosh*, that earthenware vessels in which a *chatat* was cooked must be broken — also within the *azarah*, since the absorbed residue of the *chatat* cannot be removed and the vessel cannot leave the *Beis HaMikdash* — and *v'mrika v'shtifa b'kli nechoshes b'makom kadosh*, that copper vessels used to cook a *chatat* must be scoured and rinsed within the holy precincts. *Zeh chomer b'chatas mikodshei kodashim* — and this *halacha* of the blood is more stringent for a *chatat* than for other *korbanot*, since the remaining *halachos* of cooking and vessels apply more broadly, while the blood obligation applies to *chatat* alone.

## Gezeira Shava

### Baraita Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בֶּגֶד״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בֶּגֶד, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת עוֹר מִשֶּׁהוּפְשַׁט? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יִזֶּה עָלֶיהָ תְּכַבֵּס״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to blood sprayed on a flayed hide, **from where are these matters,** i.e., the divergent opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar, derived? The Gemara explains: They are derived **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated with regard to laundering: "And when any of its blood shall be sprinkled on **a garment"** (Leviticus 6:20). **I have** derived **only a garment; from where** do I **include** an animal's **hide after it was flayed? The** same **verse states: "You shall launder that on which it shall be sprinkled,"** to include any item on which the blood sprayed.

*Mana hanei milei* — what is the Talmudic source for this debate about leather? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan*, the Rabbis taught: *beged* — a garment. *Ein li ela beged* — I have only a garment; *minayin l'rabot or mishe'hufshat*, how do I know to extend the *halacha* to leather after it has been flayed? *Talmud lomar asher yizeh aleha* — the verse adds the phrase "upon which it splatters," expanding beyond the single word *beged*. But then one might have extended it further: *yachol sh'ani m'rabeh b'or ad shelo hufshat*, perhaps even to leather that has not yet been flayed? *Talmud lomar beged* — the word *beged* reins in that expansion. The *Gemara* thus reads the verse as bracketed between *beged* and the broader phrase, a balance of *mi'ut v'ribui*.

### Raui l'Kabbel Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה עוֹר עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּפְשַׁט? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בֶּגֶד״ – מָה בֶּגֶד הָרָאוּי לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה, אַף כֹּל הָרָאוּי לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought **that I** would **include a hide** even **before it was flayed.** To counter this, **the verse states: "Garment."** Consequently, **just as** any manner of **garment** is an item **fit to become ritually impure** if one intends to use it, e.g., making it a patch for his clothing, **so too** the requirement of laundering applies to **any** item **that** becomes **fit to become ritually impure** when one intends to use it as is. A hide is fit to become ritually impure after it has been flayed, when one intends to use it for a rug or the like; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

Rabbi Yehuda's reading: *ma beged davar ha-raui l'kabbel tumah, af kol ha-raui l'kabbel tumah* — just as a garment is something *fit* to receive *tumah*, so too anything fit to receive *tumah* is included. The operative term is *raui l'kabbel tumah* — fit for, not yet necessarily in a state of actually receiving *tumah*. This expansion is striking: it moves beyond the category of garment entirely. One might ask whether blood splattering on a table would then trigger an obligation — after all, a table is *mekabbel tumah*. The answer, as the *Gemara* will clarify, lies in the third criterion: the object must also be suitable for *kibus*, laundering, not merely scraping. A table is cleaned by scraping, not washing, so it is excluded on those grounds regardless.

### Mekabbel Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: ״בֶּגֶד״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בֶּגֶד, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת שַׂק
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar** holds that, even after it is flayed, the hide does not require laundering. In interpreting the verse, he **says:** The verse states: **"Garment,"** and from this **I have** derived **only a garment; from where** do I **include sackcloth**

Rabbi Eliezer's reading parallels the structure of Rabbi Yehuda's but employs a subtly different phrase: *ma beged davar m'kabbel tumah, af kol davar m'kabbel tumah* — just as a garment actually receives *tumah*, so too the object must actually be in a state of receiving *tumah*. The term *m'kabbel tumah* — actively receiving — stands in contrast to *raui l'kabbel tumah*, which speaks of fitness or potentiality.

### Raui vs. Mekabbel

This single word difference — *raui* versus *m'kabbel* — is the hinge of the entire debate. Rashi explains: leather after flaying is physically capable of becoming a vessel subject to *tumah*, but it requires one further element to actually be *m'kabbel tumah*, namely the owner's intention — *machshava* — to use it in its current state. If one intends to continue processing it, tanning it, shaping it, or designating it for some other purpose, it has not yet been assigned a use that would confer *tumah*-susceptibility. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *raui* — the physical fitness — suffices, even without the *machshava* being declared. Rabbi Eliezer requires that the *machshava* be present, making the leather an actual *m'kabbel tumah*, not merely a potential one.

A broader question lurks here — Tosafot engages it — about whether raw leather, even after flaying, is genuinely in a state to receive *tumah* before it has undergone any tanning or preparation. The *Gemara*'s framing, as Rashi presents it, assumes that the only thing standing between this leather and *tumah*-susceptibility is the owner's intent: physically, it is already in the relevant zone.

## Machshava Examples

### Cloth Patch Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַטְלֵית פְּחוּתָה מִשָּׁלֹשׁ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ; מַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי – הָא נָמֵי רָאוּי, דְּאִי בָּעֵי חַשֵּׁיב עֲלֵיהּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר הַמְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה – הָא מִיהָא לָאו בַּת קַבּוֹלֵי טוּמְאָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the practical difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar? Is there an item that is fit to become ritually impure, but is not actually susceptible to impurity? **Abaye said: A patch** of cloth **less than three** by three fingerbreadths presents a practical difference **between** the two opinions. According to **the one who says** that any garment **fit** to become ritually impure must be laundered, **this** patch of cloth **is also fit** to become ritually impure, **as if** the owner **wants, he can intend it** for a specific use, as in patching his garment. According to **the one who says** that only **an object** already **susceptible to impurity** must be laundered, **this** patch, **in any event, is not** yet **susceptible to impurity**, so it does not require laundering.

*Mai beinaihu* — the *Gemara* asks for concrete examples that distinguish the two positions. Abaye offers: *matlit p'chuta mishalosh ika beinaihu* — a small cloth patch, less than three finger-widths by three finger-widths. Normally an object this small cannot serve any purpose and is therefore not *mekabbel tumah*. However, if one intends to use it as a patch to cover a tear in a garment, that *machshava* renders it functionally significant. According to Rabbi Yehuda — *man d'amar raui* — this patch is *raui*, physically fit to be elevated to *tumah* status if one chooses: *d'i ibaye chashiv aleih*, if one wished, one could designate it. Even without having done so, it belongs in the *raui* category. According to Rabbi Eliezer — *man d'amar davar ha-mekabbel tumah* — since the intent has not yet been expressed, *lav bar kibulei tumah hi*, it is not currently *mekabbel tumah* at all.

### Embroidery Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בֶּגֶד שֶׁחִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ לְצוּרָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ; מַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי – הָא נָמֵי רָאוּי, דְּאִי בָּעֵי מְבַטֵּיל לֵיהּ לְמַחְשַׁבְתֵּיהּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר הַמְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה – הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָאו בַּת קַבּוֹלֵי טוּמְאָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: A garment upon which** an individual initially **intended to** place **an image** constitutes a practical difference **between** the two opinions. Since the garment was initially intended to have an adornment, the garment is considered incomplete and not yet susceptible to impurity until the image is added. According to **the one who says** that any garment **fit** to become ritually impure requires laundering, **this** garment **is also fit** to become ritually impure, **as if** the owner **wants** to, **he can void his intention** to add the image, and the garment will be automatically susceptible to impurity. According to **the one who says** that only **an item** already **susceptible to impurity** requires laundering, **now, at least,** this garment **is not susceptible to impurity** and does not require laundering.

Rava adds a further example: *beged shechishav alav litzura ika beinaihu* — a completed garment that the owner has held back from normal use because he intends to have it embroidered. Under ordinary circumstances, a finished garment is unambiguously *mekabbel tumah*. But here the owner's *machshava* is working in the opposite direction — not elevating a marginal object into *tumah*-susceptibility, but withholding a finished object from it. According to Rabbi Yehuda — *raui*, it is physically fit — *d'i ibaye mevatel lei lemachshavtei*, the owner could at any moment abandon the plan to embroider and immediately the garment would be *mekabbel tumah*. That latent fitness is sufficient. According to Rabbi Eliezer, since currently *hashta miha lav bat kibulei tumah*, it is not presently in that state.

### Floor Mat Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: עוּצְבָּא דְּחַשֵּׁיב עֲלַהּ לְקַצְּעָהּ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ; מַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי – הָא נָמֵי רְאוּיָה; מַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר הַמְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה – הָא לָאו מְקַבְּלָה טוּמְאָה עַד דִּמְקַצַּע לַהּ. וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: עוּצְבָּא שֶׁחִשְׁבָה עָלֶיהָ לְקַצְּעָהּ – טְהוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּקַצְּעֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** According to a different version, **Rava said: An unfinished hide [*utzeva*] that one intended to trim** in a precise manner constitutes a practical difference **between** the two opinions. According to **the one who says** that any garment-like item **fit** to become ritually impure must be laundered, **this** hide must be laundered, since it **is also fit** to be susceptible to impurity if he voids his intention. According to **the one who says** that only **an item** already **susceptible to impurity** must be laundered, **this** hide does not require laundering since it **is not susceptible to impurity until he trims it.** This explanation may be corroborated, as **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says: An unfinished hide that** one **intended to trim is ritually pure until he trims it.**

Rava offers a second example: *utzpa d'chashiv aleih lekutzatzah ika beinaihu* — a floor mat, presumably of leather, that the owner intends to trim at the edges. As a floor mat it would objectively be in use and therefore *mekabbel tumah*. But the owner's intention to trim it holds it back from that status. According to Rabbi Yehuda, it is *raui*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, *lav mekabbel tumah ad d'mekutzatz lei* — it will not be *mekabbel tumah* until it is actually trimmed, or until the intention to trim it is abandoned.

### Trimming Intent

A *baraita* supports this position: *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya omer utzpa she-chishav aleih lekutzatzah tehorah ad she-yekutzatzennah* — a floor mat regarding which one has decided to trim remains *tehorah* until the trimming is completed. This confirms that the expressed intention to carry out further physical work holds the object in a state of incompletion, *tumah*-wise, until either the work is done or the intention is retracted.

## Three Criteria

### Spot of Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל נִתַּז עַל מִקְצָת בֶּגֶד – יְהֵא כׇּל הַבֶּגֶד טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר יִזֶּה״ – לֹא אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ אֶלָּא מְקוֹם דָּם בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: A garment **requires laundering** only in the place that the blood was sprayed; but the entire garment does not require laundering. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught:** The verse states: "And when any of its blood shall be sprinkled on a garment." One **might** have thought that even if the blood **sprayed** only **on part of a garment, the entire garment should require laundering.** To counter this, **the** same **verse states:** "That on **which it shall be sprinkled."** This is to be understood: **I told you** that laundering is required **only** in **the place** that the **blood** was sprayed.

The *Gemara* now returns to derive the three criteria enumerated in the *stam mishna*. The first: *yachol nitaz al mikzat beged yehei kol ha-beged taun kibus* — one might have thought that if blood splatters on part of a garment, the entire garment requires laundering. *Talmud lomar asher yizeh* — the verse teaches otherwise. Reading *asher yizeh* as referring to the specific place of the splatter, the *Gemara* concludes: *lo amarti lecha ela mekom dam bilvad* — only the location of the blood requires laundering.

### Tumah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה [וְכוּ׳]. סְתָמָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also teaches: A garment must be laundered only if it is **an item that is fit to become ritually impure,** and only if it is an item fit for laundering. The Gemara observes: Evidently, **the unattributed** portion of the mishna is taught **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** This statement is unlike the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, who holds that only an item that is presently susceptible to impurity requires laundering.

The second criterion, *davar she-hu raui lekabel tumah*, is then noted: *stama k'Rabbi Yehuda*, the *stam mishna* uses the word *raui*, which is the language of fitness rather than actual reception. This aligns the *mishna* with Rabbi Yehuda's position in the debate. The practical significance: in gray-zone cases — objects whose status as *mekabbel tumah* depends on the owner's *machshava* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that physical readiness suffices, while Rabbi Eliezer requires that the *machshava* be present. Where an object is unambiguously in use, no *machshava* is required; it is objectively *mekabbel tumah*. Where an object is unambiguously too raw or too small, no *machshava* can remedy that. The *machshava* operates in the middle ground.

### Laundering Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאוּי לְכִיבּוּס. לְמַעוֹטֵי כְּלִי, דְּבַר גְּרִידָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also teaches: Only an item **fit for laundering** must be laundered. The Gemara observes: This qualification serves **to exclude a vessel** from the requirement of laundering, **as it is suitable for scraping** blood off of it. Laundering is necessary only for material or fabric into which blood is absorbed.

The third criterion: *ra'uy lekibus* — the object must be fit for *kibus*, laundering. This excludes the case where blood splatters on a vessel such as a cup or mug — *l'mautei kli de-bar gerida hu*, for such an object is cleaned by scraping, not laundering. Even though a cup or mug is *mekabbel tumah*, it does not fall under this *halacha* because the method of cleaning it is not *kibus*.

### Consensus Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הַבֶּגֶד וְאֶחָד הַשַּׂק וְאֶחָד הָעוֹר – טְעוּנִין כִּיבּוּס; וְהַכִּיבּוּס בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, וּשְׁבִירַת כְּלִי חֶרֶס בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, וּמְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה בִּכְלִי נְחוֹשֶׁת בִּמְקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ. זֶה חוֹמֶר בְּחַטָּאת מִקׇּדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the garment** mentioned explicitly in the Torah, **and the sackcloth,** and the hide, all of these must be laundered. **And the laundering** must be performed **in a sacred place,** the Temple courtyard, **and the breaking of an earthenware vessel** in which a sin offering was cooked must be performed **in a sacred place, and scouring and rinsing of a copper vessel** in which a sin offering was cooked must be performed **in a sacred place.** With regard to **this** matter, **a stringency** applies **to a sin offering** more **than** it applies to **offerings of the most sacred order.**

The *mishna*'s listing — *echad habeged v'echad hasak v'echad ha-or* — garment, sackcloth, and leather — presents all three as *teunin kibus*, requiring laundering. This consensus, at least at the level of the *stam mishna*, sets the stage for the *Gemara*'s next question: in what sense, exactly, is leather amenable to *kibus*?

## Laundering Leather

### Leather Laundering Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הַבֶּגֶד וְאֶחָד הַשַּׂק כּוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּעוֹר בַּר כִּיבּוּס הוּא?! וּרְמִינְהוּ: הָיְתָה עָלָיו לַשְׁלֶשֶׁת – מְקַנְּחָהּ בִּסְמַרְטוּט. הָיְתָה עַל שֶׁל עוֹר – נוֹתֵן עָלֶיהָ מַיִם עַד שֶׁתִּכְלֶה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **the garment** mentioned explicitly in the Torah, **and the sackcloth,** and the hide, all of these must be laundered. Apparently, **that is to say that a hide,** i.e., leather, **is suitable for laundering. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between that assumption and a mishna that discusses laundering on Shabbat (*Shabbat* 142b): If **there were bird droppings [*lishleshet*] on** the cushion, one **wipes it with a** dry **rag,** but one may not rinse it with water because of the prohibition against laundering. If **it was on** a cushion **of leather, he applies water to it until** the filthy substance **dissolves.** Evidently, cleaning leather with water is not considered laundering.

Having established that leather requires laundering in principle, the *Gemara* now challenges this: *lemeimra de-or bar kibus hu* — are we really saying that the concept of laundering is meaningfully applicable to leather? The *Gemara* raises a tension from the laws of *Shabbos*: there is a prohibition on laundering on *Shabbos*, yet a *mishna* teaches that if a garment has a soiling on it — Tosafot understands this to mean something like animal manure — *mekanacha bismartut*, one wipes it with a cloth; but if the soiling is on a leather garment, *noten aleihem mayim*, one may even use water to remove it, *ad shetichle*, until it is entirely gone. This implies that applying water to leather is not considered *kibus*, not laundering — and therefore should not be prohibited on *Shabbos*. But if it is not *kibus*, why does the *halacha* of *chatat* blood require *kibus* on leather?

### Rav's Shoe Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא רַבָּנַן הָא אֲחֵרִים. דְּתַנְיָא: הַבֶּגֶד וְהַשַּׂק – מְכַבְּסוֹ, הַכְּלִי וְהָעוֹר – מְגָרְרוֹ. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: הַבֶּגֶד וְהַשַּׂק וְהָעוֹר – מְכַבְּסוֹ, וְהַכְּלִי – מְגָרְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This contradiction is **not difficult. That** mishna in tractate *Shabbat* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and **this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **others. As it is taught** in a *baraita* about the blood of a sin offering: If blood sprays onto **a garment or** onto **sackcloth, he launders it;** but if it sprays onto a **vessel or** onto **leather, he scrapes it** off. **Others say:** If it sprays onto **a garment, or** onto **sackcloth, or** onto **leather, he launders it; but** if it sprays onto **a vessel, he scrapes it** off. According to this *baraita*, the Rabbis hold that laundering is not applicable to leather, and the opinion attributed to: Others say, holds that it is applicable.

The *Gemara* sets up the tension as a debate between two *tanna'im*: a *baraita* records that *habeged v'hasak mechabso* — a garment and sackcloth are laundered — while *hakli v'ha-or megarero* — a vessel and leather are scraped. The *tanna kama* thus treats leather like a vessel: scraping rather than laundering. Against this, *Acheirim omrim*: *habeged v'hasak v'ha-or mechabso* — they include leather on the laundering side — *v'hakli megarero* — and confine scraping to other types of vessels. The debate about whether *kibus* applies to leather now has direct consequences both for the *halacha* of *chatat* blood and for *Shabbos*.

### Rav's Shoe Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי: זִימְנִין סַגִּיאִין הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, וְשַׁכְשֵׁיכִי לֵיהּ מְסָאנֵיהּ בְּמַיָּא? כְּמַאן – כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said: Many times I would stand before Rav** on Shabbat **and place water on his** leather **shoes,** which he did not consider laundering on Shabbat? **In accordance with whose** opinion is it? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** in this *baraita*.

The *Gemara* then cites the practice of Rav as reported by Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi: *zimnin sagi'in havina kaimna kammai de-Rav v'shachshi lei masanei bemaya* — many times I stood before Rav and he cleaned his shoes using water on *Shabbos*. *Keman k'Rabbanan* — this aligns with the *tanna kama*, who holds that applying water to leather is not *kibus* and is therefore not prohibited on *Shabbos*.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר, עוֹר לָאו בַּר כִּיבּוּס הוּא?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְהַבֶּגֶד אוֹ הַשְּׁתִי אוֹ הָעֵרֶב אוֹ כׇל כְּלִי הָעוֹר אֲשֶׁר תְּכַבֵּס״! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: קְרָא וּמַתְנִיתִין – בְּרַכִּין, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּקָשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said: And is there anyone who says** that **leather is not suitable for laundering? But isn't it written** with regard to leprosy: **"And the garment, or the warp, or the woof, or any article of leather that you shall wash"** (Leviticus 13:58)? **Rather, Rava said: The verse** that speaks of leprosy **and the mishna** that speaks of the sin offering are ruling **with regard to soft** leather, which is considered subject to laundering. In the *baraita*, **when** the Rabbis and the others **disagree,** it is **with regard to** an item that is made of **hard** leather; as the Rabbis hold that laundering does not apply to hard leather.

Abaye resolves the tension: *lo kashya — ha Rabbanan ha Acheirim*. The debate is not absolute; it depends on the type of leather. *Kara u'matnitin berakin* — both the *pasuk* (in the context of *tzara'at*, where *kol keli ha-or asher techabbes* uses the laundering verb explicitly for leather vessels) and our *mishna* (which includes leather in the laundering obligation for *chatat* blood) are referring to *soft* leather, *rakin*. *Ki pligi bekhashin* — the debate between the *tanna kama* and *Acheirim* concerns *hard* leather. Rav's practice of cleaning his shoes with water on *Shabbos* — *bekhashin v'k'Rabbanan* — reflects hard leather, and follows the *tanna kama* who holds that even for the *halacha* of *chatat*, hard leather is scraped rather than laundered, and on *Shabbos* it presents no problem.

### Kishkush Critical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא דַּאֲמַרִי; נֵיקוּ נֵימָא לֵיהּ לִקְרָא דְּכִי כְתִיבָן בְּרַכִּין כְּתִיבָן?! מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן בִּכְלֵי אֶכְּסָלְגְיָא הַבָּאִים מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא נִיבְעֵי כִּיבּוּס?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava then said:** That **which I said,** that the verse about leprosy relates to soft leather, **is not correct. Shall we stand** and **say of the verse that when** leather articles **are written,** it is only **of soft** leather articles that the Torah **writes?** A verse cannot be constrained in such a manner. **Are we not** also **dealing with articles of hard leather [*aksilgiyya*] that come from overseas, and** yet **the Merciful One says** in the verse that **they require laundering?**

Rava then reopens the analysis. He objects: can one really say there is any authority who holds that the concept of laundering is inapplicable to leather entirely? The verse in *Vayikra* 13:58 states explicitly — *vehabeged o hasheti o ha-erev o kol keli ha-or asher techabbes* — the verb *techabbes* is used for all leather vessels in the context of *tzara'at*. This is a *meforash pasuk*; one cannot simply say *kibus* never applies to leather.

### Soft/Hard Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: צָרַעַת – כֵּיוָן דְּמִגּוּפֵיהּ קָא פָרְחָה, מְחַלְחֲלָא לֵיהּ וּמְשַׁוְּיָא לַהּ רַךְ. אָמַר רָבָא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לִי, הָא קַשְׁיָא לִי:
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** Although the verse also relates to hard leather, this does not mean that all opinions must agree that laundering is always applicable to hard leather. The hard leather in the verse is an exception, because in the case of **leprosy, since** leprosy **sprouts from** within the garment **itself, it loosens it and renders it soft** so that its halakhic status is that of soft leather. **Rava said:** Nevertheless, **if** something poses **a difficulty for me,** according to my opinion that everyone agrees that the *halakha* with regard to laundering applies to soft leather, **this** is what poses **a difficulty for me:**

Rava therefore reformulates: *kara u'matnitin berakin* — both the verse about *tzara'at* and the *mishna* about *chatat* blood are referring to soft leather, where laundering is meaningful. *Ki pligi bekhashin* — the debate is about hard leather. But then Rava retracts this as well: *lav milta hi de'amari* — he rejects his own reformulation. Who is to say, he argues, that the *pasuk* about *tzara'at* is restricted to soft leather? Perhaps it refers to *achsalagya*, a hard leather imported from overseas. One cannot simply read the *pasuk* as limited to one type.

### Kishkush as the Critical Variable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּרִים וּכְסָתוֹת – דְּרַכִּים נִינְהוּ, וּתְנַן: הָיְתָה שֶׁל עוֹר נוֹתֵן עָלֶיהָ מַיִם עַד שֶׁתִּכְלֶה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל כִּיבּוּס דְּלֵית לֵיהּ כִּיסְכּוּס, לָא שְׁמֵיהּ כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Cushions and blankets that are** of **soft** leather, and for which the *halakha* with regard to laundering should be relevant, **and** yet **we learned** about them in the mishna (*Shabbat* 142b): If the filth **was** on a cushion **of leather, he applies water to it until** the filth **dissolves,** which indicates that the *halakha* with regard to laundering is not applicable even to soft leather. **Rather, Rava said:** With regard to **any laundering that does not include rubbing,** it **is not considered laundering.** Consequently, one may apply water to a soft leather cushion, but soft leather remains subject to laundering, so long as there is rubbing.

Rava therefore offers a fundamentally different principle: *kol kibus delit lei kishkush lo shemei kibus* — any *kibus* that lacks *kishkush*, vigorous rubbing, is not called *kibus* at all, at least with respect to leather. Whether the leather is soft or hard, the mere application of water — rinsing — does not constitute laundering. Only if accompanied by this vigorous, friction-based rubbing does it rise to the level of *kibus*. This reframes the entire discussion: the operative variable is not soft versus hard, but the presence or absence of *kishkush*.

### Fabric vs. Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִי הָכִי, בֶּגֶד נָמֵי! בֶּגֶד – שְׁרִיָּיתוֹ זֶהוּ כִּיבּוּסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that placing water upon something is not considered laundering so long as one does not also rub the item, then with regard to a non-leather **garment as well,** one should be allowed to place water upon it on Shabbat. Why does the cited mishna state that one may wipe it only with a dry rag? The Gemara answers: With regard to **a garment, its soaking is its laundering,** and merely placing water on it is forbidden.

The *Gemara* immediately presses: *ei hachi beged nami* — if the requirement for *kishkush* is the criterion, then why can one not put water on a fabric garment that has been soiled, as long as one does not rub? *Beged shriato zehu kibuso* — by garments, soaking alone is already the beginning of the laundering process. The normal method of laundering cloth begins with immersing or soaking it in water, and since that soaking is part of the laundering process, it is itself prohibited on *Shabbos* even before any rubbing occurs. By leather, however, soaking is not the natural first step of cleaning. Therefore, there is no prohibition on *Shabbos* from water alone; the prohibition, if any, attaches only to the *kishkush*.

### Practical Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי: זִימְנִין סַגִּיאִין הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, וְשַׁכְשֵׁיכִי לֵיהּ מְסָאנֵי בְּמַיָּא; שִׁכְשׁוּךְ אִין, אֲבָל כִּיבּוּס לָא – אִי בְּרַכִּין וּכְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל, אִי בְּקָשִׁין וְכַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And that** statement **that Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said: Many times I would stand before Rav** on Shabbat **and place water on his** leather **shoes,** may be explained accordingly. With regard to **placing** water on leather, **yes,** that is permitted, **but** with regard to **laundering,** which includes rubbing, it is **not** permitted. This may be explained as follows: **If** Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi spoke **of soft** leather shoes, then **all agree** that only placing water is permitted. And **if** Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi spoke **of hard** leather shoes, the distinction between placing and rubbing **accords with** the opinion of **the others,** who hold that the requirement of laundering sprayed blood applies even to hard leather.

Even after establishing *kishkush* as the critical variable, the soft-versus-hard distinction retains some relevance. *Ei berakin u'chidivrei hakol* — with soft leather, *kishkush* is prohibited according to all opinions; the *Acheirim* and the *tanna kama* share this view. *Ei bekashin u'ch'Acheirim* — with hard leather, it is only the *Acheirim* who would prohibit even *kishkush*; the *Rabbanan* hold that hard leather never presents a *kibus* problem regardless. So the structure of the ruling is as follows: without *kishkush*, there is no issue with leather of any kind. With *kishkush*, soft leather is problematic according to all; hard leather is the subject of the dispute between *tanna kama* and *Acheirim*, and the *halacha* of *chatat* blood follows the same lines.

### Rav Pappa's Challenge and Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מוּתָּר לְכַבֵּס מִנְעָל בְּשַׁבָּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא, וְהָא אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי: זִימְנִין סַגִּיאִין הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, וְשַׁכְשֵׁיכִי לֵיהּ מְסָאנֵי בְּמַיָּא; שִׁכְשׁוּךְ אִין, אֲבָל כִּיבּוּס לָא! הֲדַר אוֹקֵי רָבָא אָמוֹרָא עֲלֵיהּ וּדְרַשׁ: דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַרְתִּי לִפְנֵיכֶם – טָעוּת הֵם בְּיָדִי; בְּרַם כָּךְ אָמְרוּ: שִׁכְשׁוּךְ מוּתָּר, כִּיבּוּס אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rava taught** in public: It is **permitted to launder a shoe on Shabbat. Rav Pappa said to Rava: But didn't Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi say: Many times I would stand before Rav** on Shabbat **and place water on his** leather **shoes?** Evidently, **placing** water upon leather, **yes,** that is permitted, **but laundering,** which includes rubbing, is **not** permitted. **Rava went back and placed an interpreter before him** so that he could tell the public that he had been wrong, **and taught** in public: The **statements that I said before you** earlier **are my error. Truly,** the Sages **said like this: Placing** water upon shoes is **permitted,** but **laundering** them is **prohibited.**

Rav Pappa challenges Rava: but wait — Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi testified that Rav would *shichshech* his shoes with water on *Shabbos*. Does *shichshech* not imply rubbing? The *Gemara* answers: *shichshech in, avukibus lo* — *shichshech* here means rinsing, not the vigorous *kishkush*. Rav's practice was mere rinsing, which is permitted. The conclusion: *shichshech mutar, kiskus assur* — rinsing is permitted, vigorous rubbing is forbidden. Whether *kiskus* is forbidden even with hard leather remains unresolved in the *Gemara* — the formulation does not specify whether Rava's ruling addresses soft leather (where all agree) or all leather (following *Acheirim*). The practical ruling, therefore, is that one may rinse leather on *Shabbos* and scrape off surface dirt; vigorous rubbing is prohibited, with the question of hard leather remaining under a degree of uncertainty.

## Vessels & Cooking

### Breaking Pottery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הַכִּיבּוּס בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״תְּכַבֵּס בְּמָקוֹם קָדֹשׁ״. שְׁבִירַת כְּלִי חֶרֶס מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ אֲשֶׁר תְּבֻשַּׁל בּוֹ יִשָּׁבֵר״. מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה בִּכְלִי נְחֹשֶׁת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִם בִּכְלִי נְחֹשֶׁת בֻּשָּׁלָה, וּמֹרַק וְשֻׁטַּף בַּמָּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The laundering** must be performed **in a sacred place,** and the breaking of an earthenware vessel must be performed in a sacred place, and the scouring and rinsing of a copper vessel must be performed in a sacred place. **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning a garment on which blood was sprayed, the verse states: **"You shall launder** that on which it shall be sprinkled **in a sacred place"** (Leviticus 6:20). **From where** is the *halakha* with regard to **the breaking of an earthenware vessel** in which a sin offering was cooked derived? **The** subsequent **verse states: "And the earthenware vessel in which it is cooked shall be broken"** (Leviticus 6:21). **From where** is the *halakha* with regard to the **scouring and rinsing of a copper vessel** in which a sin offering was cooked derived? **The verse states** immediately thereafter: **"And if it be cooked in a copper vessel, it shall be scoured, and rinsed in water."**

*Mana hanei milei* — the *Gemara* now derives the other rulings in the *mishna*. *Shviras kli cheres b'makom kadosh*: the source is *u'kli cheres asher tuvushal bo yishaver* — the verse about breaking the earthenware vessel follows immediately in the text after the laundering requirement, and the juxtaposition teaches that just as laundering must occur *b'makom kadosh*, so too must the breaking of the earthenware vessel take place within the *azarah*.

### Metal Vessels

*Mrika v'shtifa b'kli nechoshes b'makom kadosh*: similarly derived from *v'im b'kli nechoshes bushala u'marak v'shutaf bamayim* — the verse about scouring and rinsing the copper vessel, though it does not explicitly mention a holy place, is juxtaposed to the earlier verses that do, and so the requirement of *b'makom kadosh* applies here as well.

### Chatat Uniqueness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* זֶה חוֹמֶר בְּחַטָּאת כּוּ׳. וְתוּ לֵיכָּא?! וְהָאִיכָּא שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמָהּ לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים! בְּחַטָּאוֹת הַחִיצוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **this** matter, **a stringency** applies **to a sin offering** more than it applies to offerings of the most sacred order. The Gemara asks: **And are there no more** *halakhot* specific to a sin offering? **But there is** this *halakha*: **That its blood enters the innermost sanctum** to be sprinkled. The Gemara answers: The mishna is dealing **with external sin offerings,** and this *halakha* applies only to internal sin offerings.

*Zeh chomer b'chatas* — and this *halacha* of the blood is a *chumra* specific to *chatat* over other *kodshei kodashim*. The *halachos* of cooking in vessels apply more broadly, but the blood obligation is uniquely a *chatat* stringency.

## Chatat Stringency

### Stringency List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* שֶׁאִם נִכְנַס דָּמָהּ (לִפְנַי וְ)לִפְנִים – פְּסוּלָה! כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל דָּמִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לַהֵיכָל לְכַפֵּר – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But there is the stricture **that if its blood enters into** the Sanctuary it becomes **disqualified.** The Gemara explains: This mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says: Every** offering's **blood,** not only that of a sin offering, **that enters the Sanctuary to atone** becomes **disqualified;** therefore, this is not a *halakha* specific to a sin offering.

*V'su leka* — is this the only way *chatat* is more stringent? The *Gemara* entertains several candidates. There is the *halacha* that blood of a *chatat* that enters the *heichal* invalidates the offering — is that not a *chumra* of *chatat*? There is the phenomenon of *chatat penimit*, the inner *chatat*, which suggests a category of elevated stringency. There is the fact that a *chatat* atones for sins of *karet*. And there is the requirement of four blood applications, *arba matanos*.

### Rejections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפְּרִין עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת! בְּחַטָּאת דִּשְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But there is the *halakha* **that** external sin offerings **atone for those who are liable to** receive excision, ***karet*,** through unintentional sins. The Gemara explains: The mishna includes an offering that does not have that *halakha*, as its principles also apply **to a sin offering** brought **for hearing the voice,** i.e., for falsely taking an oath that one is unable to testify in another's case. This transgression is not punishable by *karet*.

Each proposed stringency is rejected in turn. The *halacha* of blood entering the *heichal* applies broadly according to Rabbi Akiva: *kol damim she-nichnasu l'heichal l'chaper psulah* — all bloods that enter the *heichal* to atone invalidate the offering, not only *chatat* blood. The existence of *chatat penimit* does not help when we are discussing *chatat chitzonit*, the outer *chatat*. The atonement for *karet* does not apply to all *chatasot*: a *chatat shmiyas hakol*, a *chatat* for failing to testify, does not atone for *karet* at all. And the four applications of blood are not unique to *chatat* according to Rabbi Yishmael, who holds that *kol hadamim te'unim arba matanos* — all bloods require four applications, albeit to the four walls rather than the corners.

### Blood Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנָה אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת! כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל דָּמִים טְעוּנִין אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But there is the stricture **that** the blood of a sin offering **requires four placements** on the altar, unlike other offerings of the most sacred order. The Gemara explains: This mishna is composed **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that **all blood** of offerings **requires four placements,** one **upon** each of the **four corners** of the altar; accordingly, this is not a *halakha* limited to the sin offering.

The *Gemara* then asks about the four *kranos*, the corners of the altar. But the objection is turned: *v'l'ta'ameich* — even on your own reasoning, why single out the corners? There is also the use of one's finger rather than a vessel, *v'ika etzba*; there is the application at the edge of the corner, *v'ika chudah*. The *Gemara* resolves: *ela chad mitrei tlata chumrei nakat* — the *mishna* is not cataloguing all the ways *chatat* is stringent. It is identifying one among two or three: the *halacha* of the blood is *one* of the stringencies of *chatat*, alongside others. The formulation *zeh chomer b'chatas* singles out this *halacha* as distinctive to *chatat*, without claiming it is the *only* distinctive *halacha*.

### Final Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הָאִיכָּא קֶרֶן! הָאִיכָּא אֶצְבַּע! הָאִיכָּא חוּדָּהּ! אֶלָּא חַד מִתְּרֵי תְּלָתָא חוּמְרֵי נָקֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** even **according to your reasoning,** can it be said that there is only one *halakha* that applies to a sin offering but does not apply to other offerings? **Isn't there** the requirement to place a sin offering's **blood** on the **corner** at the top of the altar? **Isn't there** the requirement that a priest place a sin offering's blood on the altar with his **finger? Isn't there** the requirement to place it on **the edge of** the altar? Therefore, it should not be assumed that this is the only *halakha* unique to a sin offering, but **rather** that the mishna simply **cited one of two** or **three stringencies.**
