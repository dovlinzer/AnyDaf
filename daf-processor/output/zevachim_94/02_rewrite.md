# Zevachim 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Leather & Flaying

### Opening: Blood & Launder…

The *daf* begins at the bottom of 93b, continuing the *halacha* concerning blood of a *chatat* that splatters on a garment and must be laundered in the *azarah*. The *mishna* now turns from the question of what type of blood triggers this obligation to the question of what type of material the blood must land on.

### Material Types

The *mishna* introduces the case of *nitas al ha-or ad shelo hufshat* — blood that splatters onto the skin of an animal that has not yet been flayed. In the *Beis HaMikdash*, this is a readily imaginable scenario: an animal has been slaughtered, the blood has been caught in a *kli shareis*, and as one rushes toward the *mizbeach*, some of it splashes onto the hide of the animal still lying there. The implicit assumption is that once the skin has been flayed it is sufficiently analogous to a garment — a *beged* — even though it has not been woven or fashioned into clothing. The question is at what stage, prior to flaying, the skin remains raw material rather than a textile equivalent.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

On this question, the *mishna* records that *eino taun kibus* — before the hide is flayed, there is no obligation to launder. *Mishehufshat* — once it has been flayed — *taun kibus*, laundering is required. This is *divrei Rabbi Yehuda*. This ruling carries an additional dimension beyond merely classifying leather as a *beged*: the very concept of *kibus*, laundering, is normally associated with cloth. The *pasuk* in the *Torah* uses the term *v'yichabes*, and one might have supposed this term simply does not apply to leather. The *mishna*'s ruling establishes that it does.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

*Rabbi Eliezer omer af mishehufshat eino taun kibus* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that even after the hide has been flayed, there is still no obligation to launder. For Rabbi Eliezer, a further stage of processing is apparently required before the leather qualifies. One might add that at the point of flaying, it remains entirely uncertain what use the leather will be put to — it could be tanned, shaped into a vessel, cut into strips, or fashioned into a scroll. That ambiguity, as the *Gemara* will develop, is precisely the basis of the debate.

### Laundering Criteria

The *stam mishna* then continues with three criteria that govern this *halacha* in general. First, *eino taun kibus ela makom hadam* — only the precise spot where the blood landed requires laundering; one need not launder the entire garment. Second, *v'davar she-hu ra'uy lekabel tumah* — the object must be fit to receive *tumah*. A scrap of cloth so small as to be unusable, for example, would not qualify. Third, *v'ra'uy lekibus* — the object must be suitable for laundering. The *mishna* then lists the qualifying materials: *echad habeged v'echad hasak v'echad ha-or* — whether a cloth garment, sackcloth made of goat hair, or leather, *teunin kibus*, all require laundering. *V'hakibus b'makom kadosh* — the laundering itself must take place within the *azarah*.

The *mishna* also anticipates the subsequent topics of the *perek*: *v'shviras kli cheres b'makom kadosh*, that earthenware vessels in which a *chatat* was cooked must be broken — also within the *azarah*, since the absorbed residue of the *chatat* cannot be removed and the vessel cannot leave the *Beis HaMikdash* — and *v'mrika v'shtifa b'kli nechoshes b'makom kadosh*, that copper vessels used to cook a *chatat* must be scoured and rinsed within the holy precincts. *Zeh chomer b'chatas mikodshei kodashim* — and this *halacha* of the blood is more stringent for a *chatat* than for other *korbanot*, since the remaining *halachos* of cooking and vessels apply more broadly, while the blood obligation applies to *chatat* alone.

## Gezeira Shava

### Baraita Derivation

*Mana hanei milei* — what is the Talmudic source for this debate about leather? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan*, the Rabbis taught: *beged* — a garment. *Ein li ela beged* — I have only a garment; *minayin l'rabot or mishe'hufshat*, how do I know to extend the *halacha* to leather after it has been flayed? *Talmud lomar asher yizeh aleha* — the verse adds the phrase "upon which it splatters," expanding beyond the single word *beged*. But then one might have extended it further: *yachol sh'ani m'rabeh b'or ad shelo hufshat*, perhaps even to leather that has not yet been flayed? *Talmud lomar beged* — the word *beged* reins in that expansion. The *Gemara* thus reads the verse as bracketed between *beged* and the broader phrase, a balance of *mi'ut v'ribui*.

### Raui l'Kabbel Tumah

Rabbi Yehuda's reading: *ma beged davar ha-raui l'kabbel tumah, af kol ha-raui l'kabbel tumah* — just as a garment is something *fit* to receive *tumah*, so too anything fit to receive *tumah* is included. The operative term is *raui l'kabbel tumah* — fit for, not yet necessarily in a state of actually receiving *tumah*. This expansion is striking: it moves beyond the category of garment entirely. One might ask whether blood splattering on a table would then trigger an obligation — after all, a table is *mekabbel tumah*. The answer, as the *Gemara* will clarify, lies in the third criterion: the object must also be suitable for *kibus*, laundering, not merely scraping. A table is cleaned by scraping, not washing, so it is excluded on those grounds regardless.

### Mekabbel Tumah

Rabbi Eliezer's reading parallels the structure of Rabbi Yehuda's but employs a subtly different phrase: *ma beged davar m'kabbel tumah, af kol davar m'kabbel tumah* — just as a garment actually receives *tumah*, so too the object must actually be in a state of receiving *tumah*. The term *m'kabbel tumah* — actively receiving — stands in contrast to *raui l'kabbel tumah*, which speaks of fitness or potentiality.

### Raui vs. Mekabbel

This single word difference — *raui* versus *m'kabbel* — is the hinge of the entire debate. Rashi explains: leather after flaying is physically capable of becoming a vessel subject to *tumah*, but it requires one further element to actually be *m'kabbel tumah*, namely the owner's intention — *machshava* — to use it in its current state. If one intends to continue processing it, tanning it, shaping it, or designating it for some other purpose, it has not yet been assigned a use that would confer *tumah*-susceptibility. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *raui* — the physical fitness — suffices, even without the *machshava* being declared. Rabbi Eliezer requires that the *machshava* be present, making the leather an actual *m'kabbel tumah*, not merely a potential one.

A broader question lurks here — Tosafot engages it — about whether raw leather, even after flaying, is genuinely in a state to receive *tumah* before it has undergone any tanning or preparation. The *Gemara*'s framing, as Rashi presents it, assumes that the only thing standing between this leather and *tumah*-susceptibility is the owner's intent: physically, it is already in the relevant zone.

## Machshava Examples

### Cloth Patch Example

*Mai beinaihu* — the *Gemara* asks for concrete examples that distinguish the two positions. Abaye offers: *matlit p'chuta mishalosh ika beinaihu* — a small cloth patch, less than three finger-widths by three finger-widths. Normally an object this small cannot serve any purpose and is therefore not *mekabbel tumah*. However, if one intends to use it as a patch to cover a tear in a garment, that *machshava* renders it functionally significant. According to Rabbi Yehuda — *man d'amar raui* — this patch is *raui*, physically fit to be elevated to *tumah* status if one chooses: *d'i ibaye chashiv aleih*, if one wished, one could designate it. Even without having done so, it belongs in the *raui* category. According to Rabbi Eliezer — *man d'amar davar ha-mekabbel tumah* — since the intent has not yet been expressed, *lav bar kibulei tumah hi*, it is not currently *mekabbel tumah* at all.

### Embroidery Example

Rava adds a further example: *beged shechishav alav litzura ika beinaihu* — a completed garment that the owner has held back from normal use because he intends to have it embroidered. Under ordinary circumstances, a finished garment is unambiguously *mekabbel tumah*. But here the owner's *machshava* is working in the opposite direction — not elevating a marginal object into *tumah*-susceptibility, but withholding a finished object from it. According to Rabbi Yehuda — *raui*, it is physically fit — *d'i ibaye mevatel lei lemachshavtei*, the owner could at any moment abandon the plan to embroider and immediately the garment would be *mekabbel tumah*. That latent fitness is sufficient. According to Rabbi Eliezer, since currently *hashta miha lav bat kibulei tumah*, it is not presently in that state.

### Floor Mat Example

Rava offers a second example: *utzpa d'chashiv aleih lekutzatzah ika beinaihu* — a floor mat, presumably of leather, that the owner intends to trim at the edges. As a floor mat it would objectively be in use and therefore *mekabbel tumah*. But the owner's intention to trim it holds it back from that status. According to Rabbi Yehuda, it is *raui*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, *lav mekabbel tumah ad d'mekutzatz lei* — it will not be *mekabbel tumah* until it is actually trimmed, or until the intention to trim it is abandoned.

### Trimming Intent

A *baraita* supports this position: *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya omer utzpa she-chishav aleih lekutzatzah tehorah ad she-yekutzatzennah* — a floor mat regarding which one has decided to trim remains *tehorah* until the trimming is completed. This confirms that the expressed intention to carry out further physical work holds the object in a state of incompletion, *tumah*-wise, until either the work is done or the intention is retracted.

## Three Criteria

### Spot of Blood

The *Gemara* now returns to derive the three criteria enumerated in the *stam mishna*. The first: *yachol nitaz al mikzat beged yehei kol ha-beged taun kibus* — one might have thought that if blood splatters on part of a garment, the entire garment requires laundering. *Talmud lomar asher yizeh aleiha techabbes* — the verse teaches otherwise. Reading *asher yizeh* as referring to the specific place of the splatter, the *Gemara* concludes: *lo amarti lecha ela mekom dam bilvad* — only the location of the blood requires laundering.

### Tumah Requirement

The second criterion, *davar she-hu raui lekabel tumah*, is then noted: *stama k'Rabbi Yehuda*, the *stam mishna* uses the word *raui*, which is the language of fitness rather than actual reception. This aligns the *mishna* with Rabbi Yehuda's position in the debate. The practical significance: in gray-zone cases — objects whose status as *mekabbel tumah* depends on the owner's *machshava* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that physical readiness suffices, while Rabbi Eliezer requires that the *machshava* be present. Where an object is unambiguously in use, no *machshava* is required; it is objectively *mekabbel tumah*. Where an object is unambiguously too raw or too small, no *machshava* can remedy that. The *machshava* operates in the middle ground.

### Laundering Method

The third criterion: *ra'uy lekibus* — the object must be fit for *kibus*, laundering. This excludes the case where blood splatters on a vessel such as a cup or mug — *l'mautei kli de-bar gerida hu*, for such an object is cleaned by scraping, not laundering. Even though a cup or mug is *mekabbel tumah*, it does not fall under this *halacha* because the method of cleaning it is not *kibus*.

### Consensus Items

The *mishna*'s listing — *echad habeged v'echad hasak v'echad ha-or* — garment, sackcloth, and leather — presents all three as *teunin kibus*, requiring laundering. This consensus, at least at the level of the *stam mishna*, sets the stage for the *Gemara*'s next question: in what sense, exactly, is leather amenable to *kibus*?

## Laundering Leather

### Leather Laundering Debate

Having established that leather requires laundering in principle, the *Gemara* now challenges this: *lemeimra de-or bar kibus hu* — are we really saying that the concept of laundering is meaningfully applicable to leather? The *Gemara* raises a tension from the laws of *Shabbos*: there is a prohibition on laundering on *Shabbos*, yet a *mishna* teaches that if a garment has a soiling on it — Tosafot understands this to mean something like animal manure — *mekanacha bismartut*, one wipes it with a cloth; but if the soiling is on a leather garment, *noten aleihem mayim*, one may even use water to remove it, *ad shetichle*, until it is entirely gone. This implies that applying water to leather is not considered *kibus*, not laundering — and therefore should not be prohibited on *Shabbos*. But if it is not *kibus*, why does the *halacha* of *chatat* blood require *kibus* on leather?

The *Gemara* sets up the tension as a debate between two *tanna'im*: a *baraita* records that *habeged v'hasak mechabso* — a garment and sackcloth are laundered — while *hakli v'ha-or megarero* — a vessel and leather are scraped. The *tanna kama* thus treats leather like a vessel: scraping rather than laundering. Against this, *Acheirim omrim*: *habeged v'hasak v'ha-or mechabso* — they include leather on the laundering side — *v'hakli megarero* — and confine scraping to other types of vessels. The debate about whether *kibus* applies to leather now has direct consequences both for the *halacha* of *chatat* blood and for *Shabbos*.

### Rav's Shoe Example

The *Gemara* then cites the practice of Rav as reported by Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi: *zimnin sagi'in havina kaimna kammai de-Rav v'shachshi lei masanei bemaya* — many times I stood before Rav and he cleaned his shoes using water on *Shabbos*. *Keman k'Rabbanan* — this aligns with the *tanna kama*, who holds that applying water to leather is not *kibus* and is therefore not prohibited on *Shabbos*.

### Abaye's Resolution

Abaye resolves the tension: *lo kashya — ha Rabbanan ha Acheirim*. The debate is not absolute; it depends on the type of leather. *Kara u'matnitin berakin* — both the *pasuk* (in the context of *tzara'at*, where *kol keli ha-or asher techabbes* uses the laundering verb explicitly for leather vessels) and our *mishna* (which includes leather in the laundering obligation for *chatat* blood) are referring to *soft* leather, *rakin*. *Ki pligi bekhashin* — the debate between the *tanna kama* and *Acheirim* concerns *hard* leather. Rav's practice of cleaning his shoes with water on *Shabbos* — *bekhashin v'k'Rabbanan* — reflects hard leather, and follows the *tanna kama* who holds that even for the *halacha* of *chatat*, hard leather is scraped rather than laundered, and on *Shabbos* it presents no problem.

### Kishkush Critical

Rava then reopens the analysis. He objects: can one really say there is any authority who holds that the concept of laundering is inapplicable to leather entirely? The verse in *Vayikra* 13:58 states explicitly — *vehabeged o hasheti o ha-erev o kol keli ha-or asher techabbes* — the verb *techabbes* is used for all leather vessels in the context of *tzara'at*. This is a *meforash pasuk*; one cannot simply say *kibus* never applies to leather.

Rava therefore reformulates: *kara u'matnitin berakin* — both the verse about *tzara'at* and the *mishna* about *chatat* blood are referring to soft leather, where laundering is meaningful. *Ki pligi bekhashin* — the debate is about hard leather. But then Rava retracts this as well: *lav milta hi de'amari* — he rejects his own reformulation. Who is to say, he argues, that the *pasuk* about *tzara'at* is restricted to soft leather? Perhaps it refers to *achsalagya*, a hard leather imported from overseas. One cannot simply read the *pasuk* as limited to one type.

Rava therefore offers a fundamentally different principle: *kol kibus delit lei kishkush lo shemei kibus* — any *kibus* that lacks *kishkush*, vigorous rubbing, is not called *kibus* at all, at least with respect to leather. Whether the leather is soft or hard, the mere application of water — rinsing — does not constitute laundering. Only if accompanied by this vigorous, friction-based rubbing does it rise to the level of *kibus*. This reframes the entire discussion: the operative variable is not soft versus hard, but the presence or absence of *kishkush*.

### Fabric vs. Leather

The *Gemara* immediately presses: *ei hachi beged nami* — if the requirement for *kishkush* is the criterion, then why can one not put water on a fabric garment that has been soiled, as long as one does not rub? *Beged shriato zehu kibuso* — by garments, soaking alone is already the beginning of the laundering process. The normal method of laundering cloth begins with immersing or soaking it in water, and since that soaking is part of the laundering process, it is itself prohibited on *Shabbos* even before any rubbing occurs. By leather, however, soaking is not the natural first step of cleaning. Therefore, there is no prohibition on *Shabbos* from water alone; the prohibition, if any, attaches only to the *kishkush*.

### Soft/Hard Leather

Even after establishing *kishkush* as the critical variable, the soft-versus-hard distinction retains some relevance. *Ei berakin u'chidivrei hakol* — with soft leather, *kishkush* is prohibited according to all opinions; the *Acheirim* and the *tanna kama* share this view. *Ei bekashin u'ch'Acheirim* — with hard leather, it is only the *Acheirim* who would prohibit even *kishkush*; the *Rabbanan* hold that hard leather never presents a *kibus* problem regardless. So the structure of the ruling is as follows: without *kishkush*, there is no issue with leather of any kind. With *kishkush*, soft leather is problematic according to all; hard leather is the subject of the dispute between *tanna kama* and *Acheirim*, and the *halacha* of *chatat* blood follows the same lines.

### Rav Pappa's Challenge

Rav Pappa challenges Rava: but wait — Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi testified that Rav would *shichshech* his shoes with water on *Shabbos*. Does *shichshech* not imply rubbing? The *Gemara* answers: *shichshech in, avukibus lo* — *shichshech* here means rinsing, not the vigorous *kishkush*. Rav's practice was mere rinsing, which is permitted.

### Practical Ruling

The conclusion: *shichshech mutar, kiskus assur* — rinsing is permitted, vigorous rubbing is forbidden. Whether *kiskus* is forbidden even with hard leather remains unresolved in the *Gemara* — the formulation does not specify whether Rava's ruling addresses soft leather (where all agree) or all leather (following *Acheirim*). The practical ruling, therefore, is that one may rinse leather on *Shabbos* and scrape off surface dirt; vigorous rubbing is prohibited, with the question of hard leather remaining under a degree of uncertainty.

## Vessels & Cooking

### Breaking Pottery

*Mana hanei milei* — the *Gemara* now derives the other rulings in the *mishna*. *Shviras kli cheres b'makom kadosh*: the source is *u'kli cheres asher tuvushal bo yishaver* — the verse about breaking the earthenware vessel follows immediately in the text after the laundering requirement, and the juxtaposition teaches that just as laundering must occur *b'makom kadosh*, so too must the breaking of the earthenware vessel take place within the *azarah*.

### Metal Vessels

*Mrika v'shtifa b'kli nechoshes b'makom kadosh*: similarly derived from *v'im b'kli nechoshes bushala u'marak v'shutaf bamayim* — the verse about scouring and rinsing the copper vessel, though it does not explicitly mention a holy place, is juxtaposed to the earlier verses that do, and so the requirement of *b'makom kadosh* applies here as well.

### Chatat Uniqueness

*Zeh chomer b'chatas* — and this *halacha* of the blood is a *chumra* specific to *chatat* over other *kodshei kodashim*. The *halachos* of cooking in vessels apply more broadly, but the blood obligation is uniquely a *chatat* stringency.

## Chatat Stringency

### Stringency List

*V'su leka* — is this the only way *chatat* is more stringent? The *Gemara* entertains several candidates. There is the *halacha* that blood of a *chatat* that enters the *heichal* invalidates the offering — is that not a *chumra* of *chatat*? There is the phenomenon of *chatat penimit*, the inner *chatat*, which suggests a category of elevated stringency. There is the fact that a *chatat* atones for sins of *karet*. And there is the requirement of four blood applications, *arba matanos*.

### Rejections

Each proposed stringency is rejected in turn. The *halacha* of blood entering the *heichal* applies broadly according to Rabbi Akiva: *kol damim she-nichnasu l'heichal l'chaper psulah* — all bloods that enter the *heichal* to atone invalidate the offering, not only *chatat* blood. The existence of *chatat penimit* does not help when we are discussing *chatat chitzonit*, the outer *chatat*. The atonement for *karet* does not apply to all *chatasot*: a *chatat shmiyas hakol*, a *chatat* for failing to testify, does not atone for *karet* at all. And the four applications of blood are not unique to *chatat* according to Rabbi Yishmael, who holds that *kol hadamim te'unim arba matanos* — all bloods require four applications, albeit to the four walls rather than the corners.

### Blood Stringency

The *Gemara* then asks about the four *kranos*, the corners of the altar. But the objection is turned: *v'l'ta'ameich* — even on your own reasoning, why single out the corners? There is also the use of one's finger rather than a vessel, *v'ika etzba*; there is the application at the edge of the corner, *v'ika chudah*. The *Gemara* resolves: *ela chad mitrei tlata chumrei nakat* — the *mishna* is not cataloguing all the ways *chatat* is stringent. It is identifying one among two or three: the *halacha* of the blood is *one* of the stringencies of *chatat*, alongside others. The formulation *zeh chomer b'chatas* singles out this *halacha* as distinctive to *chatat*, without claiming it is the *only* distinctive *halacha*.
