# Sanhedrin 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Murder Parameters

### Hereg Intro

We begin with daf 77, picking up from the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* on 76b opens with *ve-eilu hen ha-harugin* — these are those who are executed. Having completed our treatment of *sekilah* and *sereifah*, we now turn to *hereg*, death by the sword. Only two categories are executed in this manner: *ha-rotzei'ach*, the murderer, and *anshei ir ha-nidachat*, the people of a city that collectively worshipped *avodah zarah*. Over the next several *mishnayot*, the tractate turns to defining the parameters of *retzichah* with considerable precision — much as it did earlier with *ben sorer u-moreh*. *Sanhedrin* is unusual in that it provides this kind of basic definitional work for *aveirot* that might not otherwise be systematically analyzed.

### Direct Strikes

The *mishnah* states: *ha-rotzei'ach she-hikah et rei'eihu be-even o ve-varzel* — if a murderer struck his fellow with either a stone or with iron or some other metal implement, killing him directly through wounding or stabbing, he is *chayav*.

### Holding Underwater

The *mishnah* continues: *kevasho alav le-toch ha-mayim o le-toch ha-esh ve-eino yakhol la'alot mi-sham u-met chayav* — if he held someone under water or in a fire, preventing that person from getting out, even though he did not initially place him there, and the person died, he is liable. This case introduces a key conceptual category: one need not directly strike the victim; actively preventing escape from a lethal situation constitutes an act of murder.

### Pushing Cases

A more counterintuitive case follows. *Dachfo le-toch ha-mayim o le-toch ha-esh* — if he actively pushed him into the water or fire — one might think this is straightforwardly *chayav*, and indeed if the victim cannot escape it would be so, as a *kal va-chomer* from the holding case. Yet the *mishnah* rules: *ve-yakhol la'alot mi-sham u-met patur* — if, at the moment of being pushed, the victim had the ability to escape, and something unexpected prevented him, the one who pushed him is exempt.

This seems remarkable. Even if the pusher intended harm and the victim ultimately died, the pusher is nonetheless exempt. The underlying logic connects to a general requirement: the act must constitute a *shiur mitah* — we must assess, at the moment of the act, whether what was done had the capacity to cause death under normal circumstances. If the act was such that a person could ordinarily have escaped, then what was done is classified as an act of wounding or reckless endangerment, not murder. The unexpected death — due to a cramp, entrapment in unseen seaweed, or some other unforeseen circumstance — does not transform the original act retroactively into an act of murder.

### Animal Agent Cases

The *mishnah* then addresses: *ve-shisah vo et ha-kelev ve-shisah vo et ha-nachash patur* — if he sicked his dog or snake on someone, he is exempt, because this is considered *grama*, indirect causation. Even if the intent was lethal, the attack was carried out through the animal's agency; the perpetrator did not himself directly attack the victim. As the *Gemara* often notes, *patur be-dinei adam ve-chayav be-dinei shamayim* — one may be exempt in a human court yet still liable before Heaven. The *Rambam* in fact refers to such a person as a *rotzei'ach*, a murderer, albeit one who cannot be executed.

A distinct and disputed case follows: *hikish bo et ha-nachash* — if one physically pressed the fangs of a snake directly into the victim's flesh. Here *Rabbi Yehuda* rules *chayav* and *chachamim* rule *patur*. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position seems intuitively compelling, since the person is effectively using the snake as a direct instrument — much like using a blade — rather than merely directing an autonomous agent. Nevertheless, *chachamim* maintain the exemption. The core principle at stake throughout is that *retzichah* requires not merely causation but a specific kind of direct act, and the *halachah* of *nezikin* confirms that causation alone, even in damages cases, is insufficient. Here, in the capital context of murder, that directness requirement is even more stringently applied.

## Shiur & Metal

### Iron No Shiur

The *Gemara* asks: *mipnei mah lo ne'emar yad be-varzel* — why, when the *Torah* speaks of iron, does it not say *yad*, "of the hand"? By contrast, when speaking of stone, the *Torah* says *even yad* — a stone that fills the hand — and from this measure we derive that the stone must be large and weighty enough, under normal assessment, to be capable of causing death. A similar formulation appears for wooden implements. But by *barzel*, metal, the *Torah* simply says *im bi-khli varzel* without the qualifier *yad*. *Shmuel* explains: *she-ha-varzel meimit be-khol shehu* — because iron can kill even in the smallest amount. There is therefore no *shiur* required for metal. A *beraita* confirms this: it was known before the Creator that iron kills in any quantity.

### Iron Distinctions

The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this rule: *ve-hani mili de-barza midza* — this applies specifically when the iron is used for cutting or stabbing. If one strikes someone with a metal vessel rather than piercing with it, then a *shiur* would apply just as it would for a wooden implement. The lethality of iron without any *shiur* is thus predicated on its use as a cutting or penetrating instrument.

## Kovesh vs. Dachaf

### Reisha: Holding

The *Gemara* observes that both the *reisha* and *seifa* of the *mishnah* contain a *chiddush*. The opening case — *kovesh*, holding the victim underwater — teaches us a novel ruling: *af al gav de-lav ihu dachfo*, even though the perpetrator did not himself push the victim into the water. The victim may have entered the water on his own — he was swimming, say — and the murderer simply held him down. *Keivan de-ein yakhol la'alot mi-sham u-met chayav*: since escape was impossible, this is treated as a full act of murder. We do not say this is mere *grama* on the grounds that the person was already in the water; the active prevention of escape is itself the act of killing.

### Seifa: Pushing

The *seifa* is equally novel in the opposite direction: *af al gav de-dachfo* — even though he actively pushed the victim in — *keivan de-yakhol la'alot mi-sham u-met patur*, since at that moment escape was reasonably possible, the act of pushing is not classified as an act of murder, even if the victim ultimately perished. The assessment of whether an act constitutes *retzichah* is made at the moment of the act, not with hindsight. The perpetrator's act must be one that, by its nature, was capable of killing — and that standard is not met when escape was available.

## Metzamtzem Debate

### Shmuel Derasha

Where does the *Gemara* derive that *kovesh* — holding someone in a lethal situation — is considered murder at all? *Shmuel* learns this from the verse: *o ve-eivah*, "or with hatred." The extra word *o* serves *lerabbot et ha-metzamtzem* — to include the one who *metzamtzem*, who confines or restricts the victim's ability to escape. The *Torah* thereby teaches that this act of confinement, even without direct physical attack, constitutes murder.

### Animal Sunstroke

The *Gemara* then presents a case: *hahu gavra de-metzamtzemah le-chayuta de-chavreih be-shimsha* — a person who took his friend's animal and confined it in direct sunlight, preventing it from reaching shade, so that it died of sunstroke. This appears analogous to the *mishnah*'s case of holding a person in a lethal situation. *Ravina mechayev* — *Ravina* rules him liable — while *Rav Acha bereih de-Rava* rules him exempt.

### Rav Acha Response

*Ravina* argues by way of *kal va-chomer*: by *rotzei'ach* — murder — the *Torah* does not equate *shogeg* with *mezid* or *ones* with *ratzon*; it is a narrow, exacting category requiring deliberate intent. Yet even within this strict framework the *Torah* holds the *metzamtzem* liable. By *nezikin*, on the other hand, the *Torah* is far broader — *adam muad le-olam*, a person is always liable even accidentally, even under duress. Surely then, if *metzamtzem* is liable in the narrower domain of murder, it must be liable in the broader domain of damages as well. *Rav Acha bar Rava* disagrees, and his grandson *Mesharshia* explains his grandfather's reasoning: the verse *mot yumat ha-makeh rotzei'ach hu* — "he shall surely be put to death, the slayer is a murderer" — specifically extends *metzamtzem* to the domain of murder through a special derivation, but *benzikin lo chayiv lei metzamtzem*: that extension does not carry over to *nezikin*.

It must be said that this position is difficult to understand from a policy standpoint. One expects the broader domain of damages to impose *at least* as much liability as the narrower domain of capital punishment. The ruling that *metzamtzem* is liable for murder but exempt in damages seems paradoxical. And yet that is the position. *Tosafot* here engages in lengthy analysis of *nezikin* issues — including whether *adam muad le-olam* is truly absolute or admits exceptions, and the comparisons between *nezikin* and *retzichah* with respect to *metzamtzem* — though those discussions are substantially focused on *nezikin* issues in their own right rather than the capital law before us.

## Binding & Starvation

### Starvation Exempt

*Rava* now offers a series of rulings that refine our understanding of when a lethal situation has become sufficiently present to constitute an act of murder. *Kafso u-met be-ra'av patur* — if one bound someone and left him to starve to death, one is exempt. The reason is that the person is not *currently* in the process of being killed. Starvation, unlike drowning or burning, is not an active lethal force already bearing down on the victim. The act of binding, while it sets the conditions for eventual death, is not itself an act of holding the person in the grip of the thing that will kill him. It is *grama* — indirect causation — however morally culpable, and does not rise to the level of *retzichah* subject to capital punishment.

### Heat/Cold Present

*Rava* also rules: *kafso be-chamah u-met, be-tzinah u-met, chayav* — if one bound someone in the sun, or in the cold, and the person died, one is *chayav*. This too requires careful analysis, because ordinarily sun exposure or cold does not kill immediately; a person can be exposed to sun or cold for a period without it being life-threatening. Yet *Rava* rules this constitutes murder. The reasoning is that the lethal force — heat or cold sufficient to kill — is already present and already bearing down upon the victim. When you bind someone and walk away, you have effectively placed them into the grip of that force; you have ensured they cannot escape it. Even though you are no longer physically holding them, you have performed the act of locking them into the lethal situation. The thing that will kill them was there at the moment of the act, and that is what distinguishes this case from starvation.

### Future Exposure

*Rava* qualifies further with the case of *sof chamah lavo, sof tzinah lavo* — one binds a person on a pleasant day, knowing that by the time the sun rises or the frost arrives, the bound person will die from the exposure. Since the lethal force — the killing heat or cold — is not yet present at the moment of the act, this is not murder. One has abandoned the victim to eventual death, which is morally grave, but the act of binding in that moment is not an act of placing the person into the grip of something that is currently killing them.

*Rava* further notes: if one binds a person before a lion, one is exempt; if one binds a person before gnats and the gnats consume the person, one is *chayav*. *Rashi* suggests that by the lion, the victim could still have run away — though this sits uneasily with the broader discussion. *Tosafot* explains more coherently that the lion, though physically present, is not yet attacking; the person is not yet in the midst of being killed. This is analogous to binding someone near a river that will eventually overflow — not the same as holding them underwater. The gnats, by contrast, are already actively attacking the victim at the moment of confinement; the person is *already in* the lethal process and the binder is preventing escape from it.

*Rav Ashi* disagrees and rules that even with gnats, one is exempt, on the grounds that *hani azlei ve-hani atu* — these gnats will go and others will come. Each swarm of gnats is a different entity, so one cannot say that the same lethal agent that was present at the moment of binding is continuously active. Whether gnats in a swamp constitute a continuous natural force — as sunrays do — or a succession of discrete agents is the crux of the disagreement.

### Sealed House Case

*Rav Ashi* presents another case — attributed in the *Gemara* to *Rabbi Zeira* — that clarifies the principle further. If someone brings his friend into a marble house — hermetically sealed — and lights a candle, and the person dies from lack of oxygen as the candle consumes the air, he is *chayav*. *Ta'ama de-adlik lei sharga* — the ruling is predicated on the candle. Without the candle, had the person simply been locked in the sealed house, he would not immediately be in danger; there is ample air to begin with. The harmful process — the depletion of oxygen — would only begin later. Therefore without the candle, locking the person in would be more like the *sof chamah lavo* case, and one would be exempt.

*Tosafot* enters a lengthy discussion here prompted by the principle *isho mishum chitzo* — that one's fire is halachically treated as one's arrows. *Tosafot* initially reasons that if a fire one kindles spreads and kills someone (given warning and intent), that constitutes *retzichah*, since the fire remains the extension of one's initial act — like an arrow in flight. This leads to the further question: if one locks someone in a house knowing that a fire will reach it within the hour, is that also *retzichah*? *Tosafot* acknowledges the tension: that scenario looks much more like *sof chamah lavo* — the lethal agent is not yet present. *Tosafot* ultimately steps back from the position, concluding that the most defensible reading is simply the one our *Gemara* has been developing: the lethal agent must already be present and acting upon the victim at the moment of the confinement for the act to constitute murder.

### Barrel & Roof

The *Gemara* records a dispute between *Rabbah* and *Rabbi Zeira* regarding two cases: *kafa alav gigit* — placing an overturned barrel on someone so they die from suffocation — and *para alav ma'azivah* — tearing off a rooftop so that the person dies from exposure. One rules *chayav* and one rules *patur*, and the *Gemara* attempts to determine who holds which position.

The *Gemara* initially suggests that *Rabbah* is the one who rules *patur*, aligning it with his ruling that one who binds another to starve is exempt — reading both the barrel and rooftop cases as analogous to situations where the lethal force is not immediately present. Yet this is puzzling: when a barrel is placed over someone, the shortage of air begins immediately, making it seem very much like holding a person in the grip of a currently active lethal force. The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing: *belo sharga nami mati hevla be-sha'atei* — even without a candle in the barrel case, the dangerous atmosphere sets in at once. So why is there any debate?

*Tosafot* addresses this by distinguishing between a lethal force that is constant in character and one that *progressively intensifies*. In the case of binding someone in the sun, the same heat bearing down on the person for a prolonged period is what kills — the heat is constant, it is the exposure time that accumulates. That is treated as the person being held in the killing thing from the outset. But in the barrel case, the oxygen depletion grows progressively worse — each moment there is less oxygen than before, so in a sense the lethal agent is not a constant force but an escalating absence. Similarly, *Tosafot* draws a comparison to a person already hungry from *Yom Kippur* who is then bound: the starvation that will kill him is not the same hunger he had at the outset, but a hunger that will continue to intensify. This progressive escalation of harm — rather than a steady lethal force already present at full measure — is what places the barrel case in a category of ongoing debate, and aligns it somewhat with the starvation exemption in *Rabbah*'s view.

## Removing Protection

### Well & Ladder

*Rava* now articulates a series of rulings that probe a different dimension: not confining someone within a lethal situation, but removing the protection or means of escape that stood between the victim and death. *Dachfo le-vor ve-sulam ba-vor* — if one pushed a person into a well that had a ladder in it, so that the act of pushing was not an act of murder (the victim could climb out), and then either a third party or the murderer himself came and removed the ladder — *patur*, exempt. The initial push was not murder because escape was possible. The subsequent removal of the ladder is not itself an act of murder; it is merely the removal of what would have saved the person, which is *grama* rather than a direct lethal act.

One might ask: is this not analogous to holding the person under water? You are, in effect, ensuring they cannot escape. The distinction may lie in two possible directions: one might argue that the murderer did not physically act upon the person but only upon the ladder — and there is a real question about whether it matters that the act was not directed at the victim's body. Or alternatively, the reasoning may be that being in a well will not immediately kill the person; the death will come from starvation, which as *Rava* established is not a currently active lethal force. Either way, removing the ladder is not an act of *retzichah*.

### Arrow & Shield

Similarly, *Rava* rules: *zarak chetz u-tris be-yado* — if one shot an arrow at a person who was holding a shield, the shooting itself was not an act of murder because the shield would have stopped it. If a third party, or the shooter himself, then came and removed the shield before the arrow struck, killing the victim — *patur*. The reasoning: *be-idna de-shadyei mifseik piskei girei* — at the moment the arrow was shot, that act was already neutralized. The act of shooting was not, at that moment, an act of murder. The subsequent removal of the shield is only the removal of a protective object; it is not itself an act of murder. The act of shooting the arrow and the act of removing the shield are two separate things, neither of which individually constitutes *retzichah*.

### Antivenom Case

*Rava* goes further: *zarak bo chetz ve-samanin be-yado* — if one shot an arrow at a person (or, to use a more vivid illustration, injected someone with snake venom) and the victim was holding medicine or antivenom in his own hand, capable of curing himself — and then someone scattered the medicine, or the shooter himself knocked it away — *patur*. The reasoning: *be-idna de-shadyei bei yakhol le-hitrapot havah* — at the moment the wound was inflicted, the victim had the means to heal himself. This is a striking ruling. The arrow or venom, left entirely alone, *would* kill the victim — it is not something that would be neutralized by a shield. Yet because the victim, at the moment of the act, had available to him the means of self-healing, the act is not classified as murder. The assumption that a person will avail himself of a cure means we do not assess the act as lethal at the moment it was committed.

### Marketplace Antivenom

*Rav Ashi* extends this principle further: *afilu samanin ba-shuk* — even if the antivenom was not in the victim's hand but was available for purchase in the marketplace, since the victim had access to it and could have obtained it in time, the act of shooting or poisoning is not classified as murder. The shooter is exempt even in this case.

*Rav Acha bereih de-Rava* then poses a more challenging variant to *Rav Ashi*: what if there was no antivenom available at all — not in hand, not in the marketplace — but then, before the victim died, someone happened to appear and offer antivenom, which the victim declined? Is this a case of murder? Intuitively, the answer seems yes: at the moment of the act, there was no available cure, and the act was thus lethal. Yet the *Gemara* rules: *harei yatza mi-beit din zakai* — the perpetrator was already acquitted, as it were, at the moment the antivenom appeared. The momentary possibility of rescue, even if not realized, is sufficient to retroactively confirm that the act was not definitively lethal. *Rashi* glosses *yatza mi-beit din zakai* as meaning *ein lanu le-chayvah matza lo zekhut* — that we seek every possible ground to acquit, and the appearance of a potential cure provides that ground. However startling this ruling appears, it reflects the consistently narrow definition of what constitutes a direct act of killing.

## Rebound & Energy

### Stone Rebound

*Rava* now introduces a different conceptual question: what happens when the perpetrator's energy is transmitted through an intermediary medium — specifically, through rebound? *Zorek tzur ba-kotel ve-chazar le-achorav ve-harag chayav* — if one throws a rock against a wall and it rebounds and kills someone, and that was one's intention, one is *chayav*. The rebound does not break the chain of the perpetrator's act, because it is still the thrower's energy propelling the rock. Whether the rock proceeds forward or rebounds off a surface — even something more elastic, like a trampoline — the energy that drives it remains the thrower's, and therefore the act of killing remains his.

### Ball Game Rule

A *beraita* confirms this principle through the case of ball players — those who play a game resembling squash, throwing a ball against a wall to rebound it as far as possible. *She-haragu be-meizid neheragin, be-shogeg golin* — if they killed intentionally they are executed; if unintentionally they are exiled. The *Gemara* observes that *be-shogeg golin* is obvious — *galut* does not require as direct or intentional an act; the classic case of the hatchet head flying off suffices for *galut*. The *chiddush* is *be-meizid neheragin*: that killing by intentional rebound constitutes an act of murder subject to the death penalty. One might have thought this was a case of *hatra'at safek*, a doubtful warning, since *mi yeimar de-hadra* — who can be certain the ball will rebound exactly as intended? The *beraita* teaches that when a person has the skill and intention to achieve a specific rebound and successfully does so, that is not considered a doubtful act; it is exactly what they set out to do.

### Player Intent

*Rabbi Tachlifa bar Amra* taught before *Rabbi Abbahu*: if ball players killed within four *amot* of the wall, they are *patur*; beyond four *amot*, *chayav*. *Ravina* posed a question to *Rav Ashi*: what exactly is the case? If the player intended to hit within four *amot* and it did kill within four *amot*, one should be *chayav* even for a very short rebound. And if the player did not intend it, then even a long rebound should be *patur*. *Rav Ashi* answers: *stam mesachakim ba-kadur kama de-azil tefei nicha lehu* — the standard assumption about ball players is that the farther the ball rebounds, the better. They *prefer* longer rebounds. This establishes a legal presumption: absent contrary evidence, we assume the ball player was trying to achieve a rebound of more than four *amot*. Therefore, if the ball rebounded less than four *amot* and killed someone, it did not do what the player was intending, and he is not liable.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* debate whether this entire ruling concerns intentional murder with the death penalty or unintentional killing with *galut*. The logic of the ruling points strongly toward the *shogeg* scenario: in an intentional murder case, the individual player's specific intent would be the only thing that matters — why would we apply a statistical presumption about what ball players in general prefer? If the person was actually trying to kill and succeeded, the presumption about other players is irrelevant. *Tosafot* is bothered by the *shogeg* reading too: the paradigmatic case of *galut* is the hatchet head flying off unpredictably, a far more unintentional act. Why should a ball player who was trying for a long rebound but whose ball killed someone within four *amot* — still a rebound that required skill and intent — be more exempt than the classic *galut* case? *Tosafot* ultimately leaves this question unresolved.

### Parah Rebound

The *Gemara* then connects this principle to a *halachah* of *Parah Adumah*. When the ashes of the *Parah Adumah* are added to the purification water, this act of *kiddush* — sanctification — must be performed through the person's direct act and energy. If the ashes fell onto the person's hand first and then slid into the trough, or struck the side of the vessel and then fell in, the *kiddush* is *pasul*, invalid. Why? If a rebound preserves the actor's energy, as we just established, should this not count?

The *Gemara* distinguishes: *be-shotet* — the ashes landed on the hand and simply rested there, then fell off by gravity. That is not a rebound carrying the original energy; it is a landing followed by a passive drop. *Rashi* and the discussion here make clear that the initial energy was fully absorbed upon contact and no longer propelled the ashes into the water.

A further case is then discussed: a needle resting on a piece of pottery, upon which purification water is sprinkled. If there is doubt whether the water first struck the needle directly or first struck the pottery and then splashed onto the needle — *pasul*. *Rav Chinena bar Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* clarifies: the term in the *Gemara* is not *mitzah* (rebounded) but *matza* (found) — the water was simply *found* on the needle, with no clear knowledge of whether it arrived by direct spray or by some other path. The invalidity is rooted in the unresolved doubt, not in a ruling that a rebound would itself be disqualifying.

## Sluice Gates

### Dam Release

*Rav Papa* introduces a final, significant ruling. *Hai man de-kaftei le-chaverei ve-atyei le-vidka de-maya* — one who binds his friend and brings him before a dam or sluice gate and then opens the gate, releasing the water onto the bound victim. *Girei dideih hu* — those waters are his arrows; he is *chayav*. This is a notable *chiddush*. The perpetrator did not physically strike the victim, did not put his energy into a thrown object, and is not holding the victim underwater. Yet by releasing the stored energy of the dammed water, by removing the barrier that held it back, the initial surge of water that strikes the victim is treated as if the perpetrator himself threw it. Releasing stored potential energy is halachically equivalent to shooting an arrow.

### First vs. Second

However, this principle comes with a critical limitation: *hani mili be-koach rishon, aval be-koach sheni grama be-alma hu* — this applies only to the first force. If it is the second force — a secondary consequence of the release — it is mere *grama* and not *retzichah*. *Rashi* explains the distinction as follows: if the victim is positioned immediately at the sluice gate such that the first thing the released water does is strike him, that is *koach rishon*, the first force, and constitutes the perpetrator's act. If, however, the victim is positioned further away so that the water first strikes the riverbed and then flows onward to reach him, that secondary movement of the water is *koach sheni* — it is the second force, no longer the direct release of stored energy, and constitutes only *grama*.

This raises an interesting question that the *Gemara* leaves open: what if the released water strikes a surface and rebounds directly onto the victim? In the case of a thrown stone, we established that a rebound preserves the thrower's energy and the thrower is *chayav*. But does the same logic apply when the energy was released rather than actively thrown? When one personally throws an object, one's energy is directly invested in it; a rebound merely redirects that same energy. When one opens a sluice gate, the *Gemara* treats the first strike as equivalent to throwing, but it is unclear whether a subsequent rebound of that same water would be treated as *koach rishon* or *koach sheni*. These are the core questions of how *halachah* defines an act — one's energy, one's direct deed, and where the chain of causation becomes indirect — and we will continue to explore them.
