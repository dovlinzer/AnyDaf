# Bava Kamma 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Previous Topics & Setup

### Prior Discussion Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטְלוּ מוֹכְסִין אֶת חֲמוֹרוֹ וְנָתְנוּ לוֹ חֲמוֹר אַחֵר, נָטְלוּ לִסְטִים אֶת כְּסוּתוֹ וְנָתְנוּ לוֹ כְּסוּת אַחֶרֶת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִתְיָיאֲשִׁין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If **customs collectors took one's donkey and gave him a different donkey** that was taken from another Jew in its stead, or if **bandits took his garment and gave him a different garment** that was taken from a Jew in its stead, **these** items **are** now **his because the owners despaired of** retrieving **them** when they were stolen, and they may therefore be acquired by another.

The previous daf centered on two major discussions: *dina d'malchuta dina* and *gezel goy*. The key point the *mishna* focused on was the case of someone who has stolen money in their purse — one is not permitted to make change from such a pouch, either because the original owners have not yet despaired of recovering it (*ye'ush*) and one would thereby be directly handling stolen funds, or because even if the owners have despaired, it is nonetheless unseemly to take directly from the very pouch in which stolen goods are stored.

### Tax Collectors & Bandits

The *mishna* continues with the case of tax collectors. *Natlu mochsin es chamoro* — the tax collectors seized your donkey and, out of a kind of *rachmanus*, exchanged it for another. *Natlu listim es kesuso* — bandits took your garment and gave you another in its place. *Harei elu shelo* — what they gave you, you are permitted to keep, even though it was seized from someone else. The reason: *mipnei shekvar nityashu mehem* — we may assume the original owners have already despaired of recovering it, and there is thus *ye'ush v'shinui reshus*.

It is worth noting that *listim* and *mochsin* are treated as the same category — which implicitly acknowledges that the tax collectors here are themselves acting as *ganavim*. Though they operate with a veneer of professionalism, the *halacha* treats them accordingly.

### Ye'ush Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אִם בָּא לְהַחְזִיר – יַחְזִיר לִבְעָלִים רִאשׁוֹנִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? יֵאוּשׁ כְּדִי קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that the *baraita* means that **if he** wants to act beyond the letter of the law and **comes to return it** voluntarily, he should **return it to the original owners,** but he is not required to return it. **What is the reason** that he is not required to return it? It is because **despair alone effects** legal **acquisition** and the donkey was, therefore, acquired by the Jew when the customs collector gave it to him.

The Rashba reconciles this *mishna* with the earlier prohibition against taking from the stolen-goods pouch by drawing a distinction between *l'chatchila* and *b'diavad*. Although we do presume *ye'ush* once *mochsin* take people's property — since people generally give up hope of recovering what tax collectors seize — taking directly from that pouch *l'chatchila* is nonetheless inappropriate. In the present case, however, there is an element of salvaging something from the situation, and in any case the exchange has already occurred, making it a *b'diavad* scenario in which one may keep what was received.

## Rescue Cases & Ye'ush

### Rescued Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּצִּיל מִן הַנָּהָר אוֹ מִן הַגַּיִיס אוֹ מִן הַלִּסְטִין, אִם נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of **one who salvages** items **from a river, or from a troop [*hagayis*]** of soldiers, **or from bandits, if the owners** of the items **despaired** of retrieving them, **they are his,** i.e., they belong to the one who salvaged them.

The *mishna* shifts to a different category of *ye'ush*, one that blends elements of *aveida* and theft: *hamatzil min hanahar, min hagayis, u'min halistim* — one who rescues an object swept away by a river, seized by troops, or taken by bandits. *Im nityashu hab'alim harei elu shelo* — if the owners despaired of recovery, the rescuer may keep it. This is structurally similar to the earlier case: it is after the fact, there is an element of salvaging, and the combination of *ye'ush v'shinui reshus* is operative. One might ask, however, whether by accepting the item the rescuer is himself completing the *shinui reshus* — becoming a party to removing it from the owner's control. Yet the *mishna* permits it, reasoning that since it is after the fact and something is being rescued, the acquisition stands.

### Presume vs. Establish

A subtle but critical distinction emerges from reading the *mishna* as a whole. The first clause, dealing with *listim* and *mochsin*, appears to *presume* *ye'ush* — we assume it occurred. The second clause, dealing with rivers, troops, and bandits, speaks of *ye'ush* in terms of whether it actually occurred. This will be the *gemara*'s central question: when may *ye'ush* be presumed, and when must it be concretely established?

## Swarm of Bees

### Bees & Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן נְחִיל שֶׁל דְּבוֹרִים, אִם נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה: נֶאֱמֶנֶת אִשָּׁה אוֹ קָטָן לוֹמַר: מִכָּאן יָצָא נְחִיל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And so too,** with regard to **a swarm of bees, if** the owners **despaired** of retrieving the bees, **they are his,** i.e., they belong to the one who found them. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka said: A woman or a minor,** whose testimony is not generally accepted by the court, **is deemed credible to say:** It was **from here that this swarm emerged,** and it therefore belongs to a certain individual.

The *mishna* continues: *vecheyn nachilo shel devorim* — similarly, a swarm of bees. If bees flee from their keeper's apiary and the owner despairs of recovering them, someone else may acquire them. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka: nemenet isha o katan lomar mikan yatza nachil zeh* — a woman or a minor, whose testimony is ordinarily inadmissible, is believed to say that the swarm emerged from a particular person's property. The obvious difficulty is why their testimony should be accepted at all, and the *gemara* will address this below.

### Field Entry Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ לְהַצִּיל אֶת נְחִילוֹ, וְאִם הִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **may walk into another's field** in order **to salvage his** own **swarm** of bees that has relocated there, **and if he damaged** some property in the process, **he must pay** for **what he has damaged.**

The *mishna* also addresses pursuit of the swarm: *umehalech betoch sadeh chaveiro lehatzil et nachilo* — one is permitted to enter a neighbor's field in order to retrieve a swarm that has alighted there. *Ve'im hizik, meshaleim mah shehizik* — if one causes damage in the process of pursuit, one must pay for what was damaged. This is permitted because of the urgency of the situation: the bees are in motion and may escape entirely if not pursued immediately.

### Branch Cutting Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא יָקוֹץ אֶת סוֹכוֹ עַל מְנָת לִיתֵּן אֶת הַדָּמִים. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אַף קוֹצֵץ וְנוֹתֵן אֶת הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the bees settled on a branch of a tree, **he may not cut** off the other's **branch** in order to take the bees, even **on the condition** that he will later **give him the money** for it. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: He may even cut** off the branch **and** later **give** him **the money** for it as compensation.

A more deliberate act is treated differently. *Aval lo yikotz et sacho al menat litein et hadamim* — one may not cut off a branch upon which the swarm has nested, even with the intention of paying for it, without the owner's permission. Cutting the branch is a conscious, deliberate act of *mazik*, qualitatively different from incidental damage caused during hot pursuit.

*Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka* dissents: one may indeed cut the branch and pay for it. The *gemara* will clarify that this is not a general principle permitting deliberate damage to another's property in order to protect one's own — even with the intention to reimburse. Rather, it is a special dispensation for the particular case of a bee swarm. The broader question — whether one may destroy another's lesser-valued property to save one's own more-valuable property — is not resolved by this *mishna*, and the *gemara* treats it as a case-specific ruling.

## Tannaic Ye'ush Debate

### Conflicting Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אִם נָטַל – מַחְזִיר לִבְעָלִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים. קָסָבַר: יֵאוּשׁ כְּדִי לָא קְנֵי, וּמֵעִיקָּרָא בְּאִיסּוּרָא אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that one who is given an item by a customs collector or a bandit may keep the item. It was **taught** in a *baraita*: **If he took** a donkey from the customs collector, **he** must **return** it **to the original owners.** The Gemara explains: The *tanna* of this *baraita* **holds** that **despair alone does not effect** legal **acquisition.** Consequently, the customs collector did not acquire the donkey, **and it initially came into the possession** of the individual to whom the customs collector gave it **illegally,** and he is therefore required to return it to the original owner.

A *braita* appears to contradict the *mishna*: *im natal machzir lib'alim rishonim* — if one took the exchanged donkey, one must return it to the original owners. This seems to say that *ye'ush* alone does not effect acquisition, and that since one was a party to the transition of ownership — accepting the item from the tax collector directly — one has a real obligation to return it.

Rashi explains: *ye'ush kedi shenityashu hab'alim beyad hamoches lo kana hamoches beye'ush lachudo* — the tax collector does not acquire through *ye'ush* alone. And since the recipient's own acceptance was the act that completed the removal of the object from the owner's control, he too bears responsibility.

This seems at odds with everything learned until now — that a third party who receives stolen property after *ye'ush* acquires it fully. The Rashba resolves the tension by reading the *braita* not as a strict legal contradiction but as an expression of *middas chasidus*: the *mishna* states the technical *halacha* — *harei elu shelo*, you legally own it — while the *braita* teaches that the morally appropriate thing to do is to return it to the original owners. This is especially true in the version of the case where the recipient was himself the initiator of the exchange, actively requesting the substitute donkey rather than passively receiving it.

A second version of this teaching (*ve'ika d'amrei*) softens it further: *im ba l'hachzir, yachzir lib'alim harishonim* — if one *wishes* to return it, one should return it to the original owners, not to the tax collector. *Ye'ush kani* — because *ye'ush* alone does effect acquisition, and this is purely a voluntary act. On this reading, the debate within the *braita* may not concern *ye'ush* itself at all, but rather the degree of moral responsibility one bears when one was the direct agent of the *shinui reshus*. One opinion holds that since the recipient completed the transition, there is a genuine moral imperative to return it. The other holds that once *ye'ush* occurred in the hands of the *moches*, one bears no greater responsibility than one who picks up a lost object after the owner has despaired — one may keep it, and returning it is simply an act of piety.

One might ask, as arose in discussion, whether this reflects a broader pattern in the *gemara* whereby the legal standard and the moral sensibility operate on largely independent tracks. That observation is apt: the *gemara* does not routinely derive the legal rule from moral intuition, and the discussion here is a notable example of that divergence.

### Jewish vs. Non-Jewish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּצִּיל מִן הַגּוֹיִם וּמִן הַלִּסְטִים, אִם נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – אִין, סְתָמָא – לָא. לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא גּוֹי, מִשּׁוּם דְּדָיְינִי בְּגֵיתֵי; אֲבָל לִסְטִים יִשְׂרָאֵל, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרִי מֵימָר – מִיָּיאַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this, Rav Ashi said: **They taught** this **only** when the item was stolen by **a gentile** bandit, **because** the gentile court **judges** a case and imposes its verdict **with force,** and therefore it cannot be assumed that the owners despair. **But** if the robbery was committed by **a Jewish bandit, since** Jewish courts merely **pronounce a verbal** decision but do not have the authority to enforce it, the victim **despairs** of recovering his property.

With this backdrop established, the *gemara* turns to the narrower question: when may *ye'ush* be presumed, and when must it be proven? *Amar Rav Assi: lo shanu ela listim oved kochavim* — this presumption of *ye'ush* applies only to non-Jewish bandits. *Aval listim Yisrael, lo* — by a Jewish bandit, there is no presumption of *ye'ush*. Why? *Savar l'machar nakitna lei b'dina* — one believes that a Jewish bandit is more subject to *beis din*, that one can pursue the matter legally, and therefore one does not despair.

### Rav Yosef Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! גּוֹיִם, דְּדָיְינִי בְּגֵיתֵי – לָא מִיָּיַשׁ; יִשְׂרָאֵל, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרִי מֵימָר – מִיָּיַשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this: On the contrary, the opposite is** more **reasonable:** When dealing with **gentiles, who judge** a case and impose their verdicts **with force, he does not despair** because he realizes that the gentile court will enforce the law. By contrast, when dealing with **a Jew, since** Jewish courts merely **pronounce a verbal** decision but do not have the authority to enforce it, the victim **despairs** of recovering his property.

*Masiv leh Rav Yosef: aderaba, ipcha mistabra* — on the contrary, the opposite makes more sense. Regarding non-Jews: *de-dina be-geisei* — non-Jewish courts are more expansive in their willingness to find defendants liable, accepting even a single witness and relying more readily on circumstantial evidence. Precisely because of this, a victim feels he has a real prospect of winning a case in a non-Jewish court — and therefore does not despair. By contrast, *Yisrael, keivan de-amri meimar miya'esh* — when Jews are involved, since enforcement relies only on words and communal pressure without the backing of governmental force, one is *more* readily *miya'esh*. Rashi offers two explanations of the phrase *de-amri meimar*: first, that a *beis din* ruling cannot be physically enforced and depends entirely on the defendant's voluntary compliance; second, that Jewish courts require higher standards of evidence — *mi yeimar ked-ka'amrat*, "who says you're telling the truth?" — making it harder to prove one's claim.

The irony here is striking. What the *gemara* is effectively saying is that people feel greater confidence in obtaining justice — and actually recovering their property — through the secular legal system. This resonates clearly today. The observation cuts in multiple directions: on one hand, a non-Jewish court may enforce its judgment; on the other, its lower evidentiary standards that the *gemara* associates with greater confidence for the plaintiff are the same standards that a Jewish legal system, rooted in rigorous rules of evidence, deliberately refuses to employ. The *gemara*'s plain sense is that the *niglad* feels more likely to win in secular court — and that itself is a *chillul Hashem* dimension worth noting, as the *gemara* identified just the previous daf.

The *gemara* then repositions Rav Assi's distinction. The statement that *ye'ush* only applies to non-Jewish *listim* was not made about the *reisha* of the *mishna* — where we presume *ye'ush* — but about the *seifa*, where *ye'ush* must be established. *Lo shanu ela Akum* — with non-Jewish bandits, the default is *no* presumption of *ye'ush*; one must know that *ye'ush* actually occurred. *Aval listim Yisrael* — by Jewish bandits, since the victim is more readily *miya'esh*, we do presume it.

## Gannav vs. Gazlan

### Keilim Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: עוֹרוֹת שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, מַחְשָׁבָה מְטַמְּאָתָן; וְשֶׁל עַבְּדָן, אֵין מַחְשָׁבָה מְטַמְּאָתָן. שֶׁל גַּזְלָן, אֵין מַחְשָׁבָה מְטַמְּאָתָן; וְשֶׁל גַּנָּב, מַחְשָׁבָה מְטַמְּאָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the discussion with regard to an owner's despair of retrieving a lost or stolen item, the Gemara notes that **we learned** in a mishna **there** (*Kelim* 26:8): With regard to **hides** that are tanned by the **owner** himself, **thought renders them susceptible to ritual impurity.** Hides and leather are susceptible to contracting impurity only if they are in a finished state. If a private individual uses a piece of hide or leather for a certain purpose, e.g., as a cot or a table top, and decides that this will be its fixed purpose, it is considered a finished product and is susceptible to contracting impurity. **But** with regard to hides **belonging to a leatherworker, thought does not render them susceptible to ritual impurity.** Since this individual sells leather to others, when he uses a piece of leather for a household purpose and decides that this will be its fixed purpose, it is not considered a finished state, as he is likely to change his mind and sell the leather to one who will process it further and put it to a different use. The mishna continues: If they are those **of a robber,** his **thought does not render them susceptible to ritual impurity,** because he is not considered the owner of the hide. If the hides are those **of a thief,** who has stolen them from another, the thief's **thought renders them susceptible to ritual impurity.** The difference is that unlike the case of a thief, who steals items stealthily, the identity of a robber, who takes the item openly, is known to the owner, and he harbors hope of finding him and getting the item back. Consequently, he does not despair of recovering his property.

The *gemara* now probes the underpinnings of these presumptions through a *mishna* in *Keilim*. The case involves a piece of leather being processed: if the homeowner — not a professional tanner — decides the leather is sufficiently processed and intends to use it as a mat, *machshavah metama'atan* — his intention is sufficient to render it a *kli* subject to *tumah*. But if the leather belongs to a professional tanner, *ein machshavah metama'atan* — his intention is irrelevant, because the determination belongs to the customer who commissioned the work. Then: *shel gazlan ein machshavah metama'atan* — if a *gazlan* decides to use the stolen leather in a certain way, his *machshavah* does not render it a *kli*, because we presume the owners have *not* yet despaired. The logic is that a *gazlan* acts openly; the victim knows who robbed him and retains hope of recovering the object through legal recourse. Since *ye'ush* has not occurred, the original owners' claim persists, and the *gazlan*'s *machshavah* is legally irrelevant. By contrast: *aval shel ganav, machshavah metama'atan* — a *ganav* steals covertly, and since the victim has no idea who took the object, he despairs immediately. After *ye'ush*, the *ganav*'s effective de facto control is recognized for purposes of *machshavah*.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים – שֶׁל גַּזְלָן מַחְשָׁבָה מְטַמְּאָתָן, שֶׁל גַּנָּב אֵין מַחְשָׁבָה מְטַמְּאָתָן; לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** that **the matters are reversed:** In the case **of a robber,** the robber's **thought renders them susceptible to ritual impurity.** If the hides are those **of a thief, thought does not render them susceptible to ritual impurity, because the owners have not despaired** of recovering them and the thief has not acquired the hide. Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is that a robber, who seizes items brazenly, is a more difficult criminal to apprehend and bring to justice than a thief.

*Rabbi Shimon omer chiluf hadavar* — the exact reverse. *Shel gazlan machshavah metama'atan* — by a *gazlan*, the *machshavah* does count, because Rashi explains that the victim, seeing someone so brazen as to rob openly in broad daylight, concludes immediately that there is no hope of recovery and despairs at once. *Aval shel ganav, ein machshavah metama'atan* — by a *ganav*, the victim has not despaired; a burglar, precisely because he operates in secret, can be caught. People tend to feel that a clandestine thief leaves a pattern and can eventually be apprehended, while an open robber seems untouchable.

### Ulla Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּסְתָם, אֲבָל בְּיָדוּעַ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יֵאוּשׁ קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the scope of the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the first *tanna*. **Ulla says: The dispute** is only with regard **to an unspecified** case, where it is unknown whether or not the owners despaired, **but where it is known** that the owners despaired, **all agree** that their **despair** effects legal **acquisition.**

*Amar Ulla: machlokes b'stam* — this dispute between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon applies only when we do not know the facts. *Aval beyadua* — but when we actually know whether *ye'ush* occurred, *divrei hakol ye'ush koneh* — everyone agrees: established, concrete *ye'ush* effects acquisition. The dispute is purely about the presumptions we make in ambiguous cases.

### Rabbah Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: בְּיָדוּעַ נָמֵי מַחֲלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rabba says:** Even in cases **where it is known** that the owners despaired, there is **also a dispute,** because although the owner may have expressed despair verbally, he may still hope to retrieve the item.

*Rabbah amar: beyadua nami machlokes* — Rabbah maintains that even when we have concrete knowledge that the owner despaired, the dispute remains. Rabbah's reasoning is almost psychological: one can say one has given up hope, but at some deeper level the owner never truly abandons the wish to recover the object. The person whose car is stolen says "I'll never see it again" while still calling the police monthly. For Rabbah, therefore, even a declared *ye'ush* is subject to the same fundamental debate about whether genuine despair ever occurs for a *ganav* or *gazlan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה: לָא תִּיפְלוֹג עֲלֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא, דִּתְנַן בְּמַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ – ״לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים״. טַעְמָא דְּלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים, אֲבָל נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rabba: Do not disagree with Ulla, as** the formulation of the *halakha* that **we learned in the mishna** is **in accordance with his** opinion. The mishna states that according to Rabbi Shimon, thought does not render the hides of a thief susceptible to ritual impurity **because the owners did not despair** of retrieving them, and therefore the hides do not belong to the thief. This indicates that **the reason** the thought of the thief does not render the hides susceptible to ritual impurity **is that the owners did not despair** of retrieving them. **But** if **the owners** had **despaired** of retrieving them, then **these** items would be **his,** and his thoughts would render the hides susceptible to ritual impurity.

*Abaye* challenged Rabbah, arguing that the *mishna* in *Keilim* itself teaches like Ulla: the language *mishelo nisyashu hab'alim* — "because the owners have not despaired" — implies that the absence of known *ye'ush* is the reason, meaning that *if* the owners did in fact despair, everyone agrees it would count. Rabbah responds: the phrase does not mean "because they have not yet despaired"; it means "because *ye'ush* of the owners is inherently meaningless here" — a significant re-reading that interprets the *mishna* as expressing an objective determination rather than a contingent one.

## Multi-Source Analysis

### Tanna Identification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נָטְלוּ מוֹכְסִין חֲמוֹרוֹ כּוּ׳. מַנִּי? אִי רַבָּנַן – קַשְׁיָא גַּזְלָן, אִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – קַשְׁיָא גַּנָּב!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna here that if **customs collectors took one's donkey** and replaced it with a donkey taken from another Jew, or if bandits took his garment and replaced it with a garment taken from another Jew, he may keep these items because the owners despaired of retrieving them when they were stolen. The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is expressed in this mishna? **If** it is in accordance with **the Rabbis,** who hold that the owner despairs only in the case of a thief who steals secretly, it **is difficult,** because the mishna indicates that the victim of **a robber** also despairs of retrieving his property, as in the case of a customs collector. And **if** it is in accordance with **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that the owner despairs only in the case of a robber, it **is difficult,** because the mishna indicates that the victim of **a thief** also despairs of retrieving his property, as in the case of bandits.

The *gemara* now attempts to determine which *tanna* the *mishna* on our daf follows. The difficulty is that the *mishna* presumes *ye'ush* in two cases — the tax collector (*mochsin*), who Rashi identifies as a *gazlan*, and the bandits (*listim*), whom Rashi identifies as a *ganav* (since bandits go into hiding after their act, which is the *gemara*'s basis for assigning them *ganav* status). The *mishna* presumes *ye'ush* in both. But that creates a problem: *ee Rabbanan kashya gazlan* — if it follows the *Rabbanan*, why presume *ye'ush* by a *gazlan*, since they hold there is none? *Ee Rabbi Shimon kashya ganav* — if it follows Rabbi Shimon, why presume *ye'ush* by a *ganav*, since he holds there is none? According to Ulla, one could answer: *hacha nami beyadua vedivrei hakol* — perhaps in both cases *ye'ush* was actually known, not merely presumed, and everyone agrees known *ye'ush* is effective. But this is somewhat strained given that the *mishna* speaks in terms of presumption. According to Rava, the problem is even sharper: even known *ye'ush* remains disputed, so one can never have both a *ganav* and a *gazlan* case covered simultaneously by any single *tanna*.

### Armed Bandits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעוּלָּא, דְּאָמַר: בְּיָדוּעַ – קָנֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי בְּיָדוּעַ, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּה, דְּאָמַר: בְּיָדוּעַ נָמֵי מַחְלוֹקֶת; הָא מַנִּי? לָא רַבָּנַן וְלָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן! בִּלְסְטִים מְזוּיָּין, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Ulla, who says** that all agree that if **it is known** that the owners despaired, the individual in possession of the items **acquires** them, **here too** it is possible to explain that the mishna is discussing a case **where it is known** that the owners despaired, **and all agree** that the recipient of the stolen property may keep it. **But according to Rabba, who says** that even in cases **where it is known** that the owners despaired, there is **also a dispute,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna written? **It is not** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and it is not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** The Gemara answers that the mishna is discussing a case of **an armed bandit,** who is similar to a robber in that he steals using force and aggression. **And it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that the victim of a robber despairs of recovering his property.

The *gemara* resolves: *delistim m'zuyan veRabbi Shimon hi* — the *mishna* is dealing with armed bandits, *listim m'zuyanim*, who operate openly and without concealment. As such, they are categorized as a type of *gazlan*, not a *ganav*. The *mishna* thus presents two varieties of *gazlan* — the tax collector and the armed bandit — and follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *ye'ush* is presumed by a *gazlan*. The *gemara* then objects: *ee hachi hainu gazlan* — if the armed bandit is just another *gazlan*, why list him separately? *Trei gavnei gazlan* — because the *mishna* is illustrating two distinct types of *gazlan*: the institutionalized extortion of the *moches* and the violent seizure of the armed bandit. This distinction clarifies the breadth of the rule without introducing a separate legal category.

### Braita Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַגַּנָּב וְהַגַּזְלָן וְהָאַנָּס – הֶקְדֵּשָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּתְרוּמָתָן תְּרוּמָה, וּמַעְשְׂרוֹתָן מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof with regard to the dispute between Ulla and Rabba. **Come** and **hear** the following *baraita*: With regard to **a thief, a robber, and one who forces** another to sell him something, **their consecrated items are** considered **consecrated, and their *teruma*,** the portion of the produce designated for the priest, **is** considered ***teruma*, and their tithes are** considered **tithes.**

The *gemara* brings a further source: *hagannav vehagazlan veha'anas vehekdesh — hekdesham hekdesh, u'trumasam truma* — whether a *gannav*, a *gazlan*, or an *anas* (one who forces a sale but pays for it), we presume *ye'ush*, and therefore if any of them consecrates the object it is *hekdesh*, and if they designate it *terumah*, that designation takes effect. The pattern repeats: this source presumes *ye'ush* in all cases, which again fits neither the *Rabbanan* alone nor Rabbi Shimon alone. The *gemara*'s answer is once more *delistim m'zuyan veRabbi Shimon hi*. Even though the source explicitly uses the word *gannav*, the *gemara* maintains that it refers to an armed bandit who is halachically a *gazlan*, and the source follows Rabbi Shimon. This is admittedly a forced reading of a source that labels someone as a *gannav* while arguing he is really a *gazlan* — the *gemara* itself acknowledges the strain.

## Rabbi's Position

### Ibbaya Lehu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר, אוֹמֵר אֲנִי: גַּנָּב – כְּגַזְלָן. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כְּגַזְלָן דְּרַבָּנַן קָאָמַר – וְלָא קָנֵי; אוֹ דִלְמָא כְּגַזְלָן דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן קָאָמַר, וּקְנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara examines **the** matter **itself. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that the *halakha* that applies to a thief is **like** the *halakha* that applies to **a robber. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Was Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **saying** that the *halakha* that applies to a thief is **like** the *halakha* that applies to **a robber** according to the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who hold that the owners do not despair of recovering their item, **and** a thief therefore does **not acquire** the items he steals? **Or perhaps he was saying** that the *halakha* that applies to a thief is **like** the *halakha* that applies to **a robber** according to the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that the owners despair of recovering their property, **and** a thief therefore **acquires** the stolen items.

*Gufah Rabbi omer omer ani: gannav kegazlan* — Rabbi (Judah the Prince) explicitly states that a *gannav* has the same status as a *gazlan*. The question then becomes: like which *gazlan*? *Kegazlan derabbanan kamrat, delo kani* — does Rabbi mean like the *Rabbanan*'s *gazlan*, where there is never *ye'ush*? *O dilma kegazlan deRabbi Shimon kamrat, vekani* — or like Rabbi Shimon's *gazlan*, where *ye'ush* is presumed?

The *gemara* raises the *mishna* on our daf as proof: it presumes *ye'ush* in both the *gazlan* and *gannav* cases. *Ee Rabbanan kashya gazlan, ee Rabbi Shimon kashya gannav* — the same structural problem. *Ee amrat bishlama Rabbi kegazlan deRabbi Shimon kamar, vekani — hamani Rabbi hi* — if Rabbi means that *ye'ush* is always presumed, then the *mishna* is explained: it follows Rabbi, and Rabbi holds *ye'ush* is universal. *Ela ee amrat kegazlan derabbanan kamar, velo kani — hamani* — but if Rabbi means there is never *ye'ush*, then this *mishna* follows no *tanna* — not the *Rabbanan*, not Rabbi Shimon, not Rabbi. *Hacha b'mai askinan delistim m'zuyan veRabbi Shimon hi* — once again the *gemara* retreats to the armed bandit answer and attributes the source to Rabbi Shimon. The *gemara* itself signals this is repetitive but necessary to address the question from Rabbi's own position.

### Father's Honor Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַבָּה: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּמַתְנֵי רַבִּי לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרֵיהּ: לֹא דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת מַמָּשׁ; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ פָּרָה וְחוֹרֵשׁ בָּהּ, חֲמוֹר וּמְחַמֵּר אַחֲרָיו – חַיָּיבִין לְהַחְזִיר, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד אֲבִיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rabba: Come** and **hear** a proof from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's own interpretation of the mishna (111b) that states that children who inherit an item that was stolen by their father are obligated to return it to the owner if it is something that serves as a legal guarantee of a loan, e.g., land. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **would teach Rabbi Shimon, his son,** that the mishna is **not** referring only to **something that may actually serve** as a legal **guarantee,** i.e., land. **Rather,** it is referring **even** to **a cow** that **he plows with,** or **a donkey** that **he drives** by directing it from **behind,** which the heirs **are obligated to return because of the honor of their father.**

A cleaner proof emerges from a different *mishna*. Rabbi taught his son, Rabbi Shimon: *lo davar sheyesh bo achrayus mamash* — when the *mishna* earlier in the *perek* said that sons who inherit stolen property must return things that have *achrayus*, this does not literally mean only land. *Ela afilu parah vechoresh bah vechamor acharayiv* — even a cow one is actively plowing with, or a donkey publicly in use and known to be stolen, must be returned — *chayavin l'hachzir mipnei kevod avihem* — because of honor due to their father. The *gemara* draws the inference: *ta'ama mipnei kevod avihem* — the only reason they return it is for the father's honor. *Ha lav kevod avihem, lo* — if not for that consideration, they would not have to return it, meaning they technically *own* it through *ye'ush v'shinui reshus*. And since the opening *mishna* of the *perek* concerns a *gazlan* — *hagezel uma'achil* — this proves that Rabbi holds *ye'ush* is presumed by a *gazlan*, and therefore when he equates *gannav* with *gazlan*, he means like Rabbi Shimon's *gazlan*: *ye'ush* is always presumed. *Shema mina* — this is a solid proof, and the simplest reading of all the *mishna* and *braita* sources is that they follow Rabbi, who presumes *ye'ush* universally by both *gannav* and *gazlan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד אֲבִיהֶן, הָא לָאו כְּבוֹד אֲבִיהֶן – לָא; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: רַבִּי – גַּזְלָן דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן קָאָמַר! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: The **reason** that the heirs must return the stolen item is **due to the honor of their father. But** if it were **not** for **the honor of their father,** they would **not** be required to return it. **Conclude from it** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **was saying** that the *halakha* that applies to a thief is **like** the *halakha* that applies to **a robber** according to the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon.** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that this is so.

## Bees & Testimony (Detail)

### Bees Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי ״וְכֵן״? אֲפִילּוּ נְחִיל שֶׁל דְּבוֹרִים, דְּקִנְיָן דְּרַבָּנַן הוּא. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הַאי, כֵּיוָן דְּרַבָּנַן בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּקָנֵי לֵיהּ – אֲפִילּוּ סְתָמָא נָמֵי מִיָּיַשׁ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: אִם נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – אֵין, אִי לָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This is** what **it is saying:** The *halakha* stated by the mishna applies **even** to **a swarm of bees, which is** the property of the owners via **a rabbinic** form of **acquisition** due to the fact that one cannot effect a legal acquisition of bees by Torah law. It might **enter your mind to say** that in **this** case, **since one acquires** the swarm of bees **only by rabbinic law, even** where the owners' response is **unspecified** it can be assumed that they **despair** of recovering the bees, and the finder may keep them. To counter this, the mishna **teaches us** that **if** it is known that **the owners** of the bees **despaired** of recovering them, **yes,** the finder may keep the bees; **if** they did **not** despair, **no,** he may not keep them.

*Amar Rav Yosef: mai vecheyn* — what does the *mishna* mean when it says "and similarly" regarding the bee swarm? *Afilu nechila shel devorim d'tikun d'rabanan hu* — even a swarm of bees, where the very ownership of bees is only a rabbinic institution, because it is halachically unclear how one can acquire bees through a recognized act of *kinyan* (Tosafot raises this difficulty, noting that picking up the beehive does not obviously transfer possession of the flying bees within). The Rabbis established a form of ownership for bees based on *darkei shalom*, analogous to the Rabbinic ownership of *yonei shovach*, pigeons. Since ownership of bees is only Rabbinic, one might think we simply presume *ye'ush* without requiring it to be established. *Kama shma lan* — the *mishna* teaches us: no. *Meimar tzricha d'ba'ina leh* — even here, one may acquire the swarm only if the owners were actually *miya'esh*; we do not simply presume it.

### Woman Minor Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשָּׁה וְקָטָן בְּנֵי עֵדוּת נִינְהוּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ בְּעָלִים מְרַדְּפִין אַחֲרֵיהֶם, וְאִשָּׁה וְקָטָן מַסִּיחִין לְפִי תּוּמָּם, וְאוֹמֵר: מִכָּאן יָצָא נְחִיל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Are a woman and a minor eligible for testimony?** The *halakha* is that women and minors cannot present testimony in court, so how can Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka rule that they are deemed credible? The Gemara explains that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Here, we are dealing with** a case **where the owners were pursuing** the bees, **and the woman and the minor were speaking offhandedly and saying:** It was **from here that this swarm emerged.** In this context, it can be assumed that the individual identified as the owner is the real owner of the bees, even in the absence of formal testimony in court.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's ruling — that a woman or minor is believed to say the swarm left from a particular property — immediately raises the question: *isha v'katan bnei edut ninhu?* — since when are a woman or minor competent witnesses? Normally their testimony is inadmissible.

One should note in passing that in medieval Ashkenaz, authorities developed mechanisms for accepting women's testimony in commercial matters, framing it not as formal *edut* but as *ne'emanut* — a form of circumstantial reliability. The *gemara* here, however, is working with the standard rules, and the question is genuine.

### Corroboration & Speech

*Hacha b'mai askinan k'gon shehayu ba'alim mirdafin achareihen* — the *gemara* explains that the case involves corroborating evidence: the owners themselves were visibly pursuing the swarm, which is already strong circumstantial evidence of who owns it. *Ve'isha v'katan mesichin l'fi tuman* — and the woman and child are not formally testifying; they are merely chatting innocently, *mesiach l'fi tumo*, talking without awareness that their words are being used as evidence. The combination of the owners' visible pursuit and the innocent, unconsidered speech of bystanders creates a sufficient basis for the presumption — even without formal *edut*. The *gemara* recognizes that *mesiach l'fi tumo* has a particular credibility precisely because the speaker has no agenda: there is no reason to lie when one is simply gossiping about a neighborhood event.

## Mesiach L'fi Tumo Scope

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֵין מֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ כָּשֵׁר – אֶלָּא לְעֵדוּת אִשָּׁה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned a case of offhand remarks, the Gemara states that **Rav Ashi says:** Information gleaned from **one who speaks offhandedly is valid only with regard to testimony** enabling **a woman** to remarry, as the Sages were lenient and decreed that the incidental remarks of certain individuals who are not eligible to serve as witnesses may be relied upon in such a case.

*Amar leh Ravina l'Rav Ashi* — Ravina challenges Rav Ashi. If we say that a woman and minor cannot give testimony, and we rely in the bee case only on *mesiach l'fi tumo* combined with corroborating circumstances — are we saying *mesiach l'fi tumo* never works for women and minors beyond that narrow context? *V'harei nachil shel devorim mesiach l'fi tumo hu* — so is the bee swarm case simply a case of *mesiach l'fi tumo*, and does that establish a broader principle?

### Rav Ashi Response

*Amar Rav Ashi: ein mesiach l'fi tumo kasher ela l'edut isha bilvad* — no. *Mesiach l'fi tumo* is valid only for *edut isha* — the specific category of testimony concerning whether a woman's husband has died, where the Sages were particularly lenient because of *agunah* concerns. Outside that category, *mesiach l'fi tumo* does not function as a general principle for women and minors in testimony. The bee case, then, must rest on a different ground.

### Cohen Status Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ וְאוֹמֵר: ״זְכוּרַנִי כְּשֶׁאֲנִי תִּינוֹק, וּמוּרְכָּבַנִי עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ שֶׁל אַבָּא, וְהוֹצִיאוּנִי מִבֵּית הַסֵּפֶר, וְהִפְשִׁיטוּנִי אֶת כֻּתׇּנְתִּי, וְהִטְבִּילוּנִי לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה לָעֶרֶב״. וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ הָכִי: ״וַחֲבֵירַי בְּדֵילִין מִמֶּנִּי, וְהָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתִי: יוֹחָנָן אוֹכֵל חַלּוֹת״. וְהֶעֱלָהוּ רַבִּי לַכְּהוּנָּה עַל פִּיו!
>
> **Translation:** Ravina continues to press: surely *mesiach l'fi tumo* works in other contexts as well. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** There was **an incident involving a certain person who was speaking offhandedly and saying: I remember when I was a child and I** would still **ride on father's shoulder. And they took me from school, and removed my tunic, and immersed me** in a ritual bath so that I would be able **to partake of *teruma* that evening.** **And Rabbi Ḥanina** would **conclude** his retelling of the incident **as follows:** That individual then went on to say: **And my friends** would **separate** themselves **from me, and they would call me: Yoḥanan, eater of *ḥallot*,** a reference to the portion of dough given to the priests, which has the same status as *teruma*. **And** after hearing this anecdote, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **elevated him to the priesthood** and granted him the right to partake of *teruma* based **on his statement.** Since it is prohibited for non-priests to consume *teruma* by Torah law, it is apparent that offhand remarks may be relied upon even with regard to Torah law, even in cases unrelated to enabling a woman to remarry.

This case is striking for a further reason: the testimony concerns something witnessed and experienced as a child, being reported by an adult. As a rule, one cannot testify in court about events witnessed during childhood — yet here the *mesiach l'fi tumo* framework bypasses even that limitation. Furthermore, it is self-serving testimony in a meaningful sense — and yet it is credited.

### Captive Woman Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אָתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַב חָנָא קַרְטִיגָנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב אַחָה קַרְטִיגָנָא מִשְׁתַּעֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, בְּתִינוֹק אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ, וְאָמַר: ״אֲנִי וְאִמִּי נִשְׁבִּינוּ לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם, יָצָאתִי לִשְׁאוֹב מַיִם – דַּעְתִּי עַל אִמִּי, לִלְקוֹט עֵצִים – דַּעְתִּי עַל אִמִּי״. וְהִשִּׂיאָהּ רַבִּי עַל פִּיו לַכְּהוּנָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And still,** is there **no** other case where offhand remarks made by those who are ineligible to testify in court would be accepted **by Torah law? But when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, didn't **he say** that **Rav Ḥana of Carthage [Kartigna], and some say** that it was **Rav Aḥa of Carthage, related: An incident came before Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and some say** that this **incident came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **with regard to a certain child who was speaking offhandedly and said: My mother and I were captured** and held **among the gentiles.** When **I would go out to draw water, my mind** was **on my mother,** and when I would go out **to gather wood, my mind** was **on my mother.** Since the child was always conscious of his mother, he was aware that she was not violated by her captors. The Gemara concludes the statement of Rav Dimi and explains the challenge: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **married her to a priest** based **on his statement.** Since it is prohibited by Torah law for a priest to marry a woman who has engaged in sexual intercourse with a man forbidden to her by Torah law and with whom she cannot establish a marital bond, e.g., a gentile, it is clear that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi relied on an offhand remark even with regard to a prohibition by Torah law.

*Ki ata Rav Dimi amar Rav Chana Kartigna* — when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that a case came before Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi (or, in another version, before Rabbi himself): a child who was chatting innocently said, "My mother and I were taken captive among non-Jews. When I went out to draw water, my mind was on my mother. When I gathered wood, my mind was on my mother." Implicit in this innocent narrative is that the child was constantly attentive to his mother's wellbeing, never leaving her unguarded. The implication — that she was not violated — was drawn from this, and *vehesi'ah Rebbi al piv likhunah* — Rabbi permitted her to marry a *kohen* on the basis of this testimony. Here too, the person speaking is not the woman herself (whose word would not be considered sufficiently *l'fi tumo* in her own case), but a third party whose innocent speech carries no evident self-interest.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁבוּיָה הֵקֵילּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The Sages **were lenient with regard to a captured woman.** By Torah law, a woman taken captive may be presumed to remain permitted to a priest, and it is the Sages who decreed that she is forbidden due to the concern that she was raped. It is this rabbinic decree that may be disregarded on the basis of offhand remarks.

The *gemara* resolves these cases: *bishvuya hikilu* — the Sages were lenient specifically in cases of a captive woman, and *bitrumah derabbanan* — the case of *terumah* and *kohen* status today involves only Rabbinic implications, since *terumah* in the present era is *d'rabanan*. The conclusion is that *mesiach l'fi tumo* is valid only for *rabbinic* concerns — it does not override *de'oraita* prohibitions.

The *gemara*'s conclusion, however, invites closer scrutiny. The cases discussed involved *mesiach l'fi tumo* as a pure, isolated mechanism. Where there is corroborating evidence, where the speaker is a disinterested party, and where the legal stakes are matters of *dinei mamonot* rather than personal status, the picture may be more nuanced. This distinction carries practical implications, including the long-standing *Ashkenazim* and *Sefardim* dispute over whether one may rely on a non-Jew's *mesiach l'fi tumo* testimony to determine whether forbidden food has been mixed into permitted food — a case involving corroborating factors and the question of whether *ta'am* is a *de'oraita* concern. The *gemara*'s rule that *mesiach l'fi tumo* does not work *de'oraita* is the anchor of one position, while the presence of corroboration and the absence of self-interest are the basis for leniency on the other side.

## Property Damage Rules

### Branch Cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: תְּנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא, שֶׁיְּהֵא יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֶה שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ וְקוֹצֵץ סוֹכוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, לְהַצִּיל אֶת נְחִילוֹ. וְנוֹטֵל דְּמֵי סוֹכוֹ מִתּוֹךְ נְחִילוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But** if the bees settled on a branch of a tree, **he may not cut** off the other's **branch** in order to take the bees, even on the condition that he will later give him the money for it. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: It is a stipulation of the court,** which takes effect even without being confirmed by individuals, **that one will** be permitted to **enter another's field and cut off another's branch** in order **to salvage his** own **swarm** of bees. **And** the owner of the field then **collects the value of his branch from his fellow's swarm.**

*Tanya: Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka omer: tenai beis din hu* — it was taught in a *braita* that Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka explains his father's lenient ruling by appealing to a rabbinic court stipulation. It is not that there is a general principle permitting deliberate damage to another's property to save one's own; rather, the court made a specific stipulation: *she'yehei yored letokh sedeh chaveiro vekotzetz sukho shel chaveiro lehatzil nechilato* — one may enter a neighbor's field and cut the branch on which the swarm rests in order to retrieve it. *Venotel demei sukho mitokh nechilato shel chaveiro* — and the field owner is entitled to be reimbursed from the value of the swarm, meaning from the benefit the bee-keeper obtains.

### Extended Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא, שֶׁיְּהֵא שׁוֹפֵךְ יֵינוֹ וּמַצִּיל אֶת דּוּבְשָׁנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנוֹטֵל דְּמֵי יֵינוֹ מִתּוֹךְ דּוּבְשָׁנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. וּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא, שֶׁיְּהֵא מְפָרֵק אֶת עֵצָיו וְטוֹעֵן פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנוֹטֵל דְּמֵי עֵצָיו מִתּוֹךְ פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is** also **a stipulation of the court that** one who sees another's honey barrel break **should pour out his** own **wine and** then use the empty wine barrel to **salvage the other's honey,** which is more expensive than wine. **And** the owner of the wine then **collects the value of his wine from the other's honey. And it is** also **a stipulation of the court that one** who sees that another's donkey has fallen **should unload his** own **wood** from his own donkey **and load the other's flax,** which is more expensive than wood, in its place. **And** the owner of the wood then **collects the value of his wood from the other's flax.** These stipulations take effect **as** it was **on this condition** that **Joshua apportioned Eretz** Yisrael **to the Jewish people.**

The *braita* does not stop there: *tenai beis din hu* — there is a broader category of such court stipulations. *Sheshofekh yeino umatzil devashno shel chaveiro, venotel demei yeino mitokh devashno shel chaveiro* — if someone's honey is spilling and the only available container is another's barrel of wine, one may empty the wine barrel and catch the honey, and the honey's owner must compensate for the wine. *Umefarek etzav veto'en pishtano shel chaveiro, venotel demei etzav mitokh pishtano shel chaveiro* — if one's donkey is carrying wood and another's donkey, carrying the more valuable flax, is collapsing, one may remove the wood and load the flax, and the flax owner must pay for the wood. *Shekach hitnah Yehoshua l'Yisrael et ha'aretz* — these are part of the implicit stipulations that *Yehoshua* established upon entering the land, conditions to which all members of *Klal Yisrael* are treated as having agreed.

### Voluntary vs. Forced

An important distinction must be drawn within these cases. In the honey and flax scenarios, the person saving the property is voluntarily sacrificing his own — he chooses to pour out his wine, he chooses to offload his wood. That is categorically different from compelling someone else to absorb a loss, even with reimbursement. One who willingly sacrifices a lesser asset to save a greater one and then expects to be paid back is acting within a recognizable framework of implicit agreement. That is not a license for a general rule that one may deliberately damage another's property to protect one's own, and then simply pay for it. Whether these stipulations extend beyond the specific cases listed — wine for honey, wood for flax, branches for bees — and what the precise parameters of *who* may act, under *what* circumstances, and with *whose* consent, remain questions the *gemara* leaves with some degree of openness.
