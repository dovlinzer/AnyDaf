# Yevamot 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chuppah for Psulot

### Intro: Chuppah Status

The daf under discussion opens with a debate introduced by Rami bar Chama concerning whether *chuppah* is halakhically meaningful for women who are *psulot* — women disqualified from marrying a *kohen*, such as a *grushah*. The question of *yesh chuppah li-psulot* has been framed as whether the act of *chuppah* carries legal weight when the woman is forbidden to the husband.

Before engaging the Gemara's argument directly, it is worth clarifying what "meaningful" means in this context. The question is not whether one fulfills the *mitzvah* of *chuppah* for the purposes of *sheva brachot* — a separate issue that arises, for instance, in the case of a *niddah*, who is a woman in a temporary forbidden status that will eventually be permitted. Here we are speaking of a *psulah* in a substantive sense: a *grushah* to a *kohen*, for example, a woman who is categorically disqualified. The halakhic question is whether the act of *chuppah* carries any legal consequence — specifically, whether it is treated as equivalent to *biah*.

The relevance of this question is not primarily whether such a woman may now marry the *kohen*, since she is already disqualified. Rather, the concern is whether she has been invalidated from eating *terumah*. If she is the daughter of a *kohen*, she eats *terumah* by virtue of her father. An act of forbidden intercourse of the kind that concerns *kehunah* would render her a *chalalah*, permanently stripped of that right. The prior discussion in the Gemara established that we speak only of a *bat shalosh shanim v'yom echad* — the halachically significant threshold age — and the question is whether *chuppah* functions like *biah* in that regard.

### Rashi: Chuppah Alone

Rashi's reading of the Gemara is that the entire debate concerns *chuppah* that occurred without any prior *kiddushin*. The couple proceeded directly to *chuppah* without having performed *kiddushin*. This creates two opposing arguments simultaneously.

On one hand, one might be more lenient than in a case of *kiddushin* alone: even if Rabbi Meir holds that *kiddushin* by a *kohen* to a *psulah* disqualifies her from *terumah*, he might agree that *chuppah* without *kiddushin* has no halakhic standing whatsoever and therefore creates no consequence. *Chuppah* that arrives without *kiddushin* perhaps means nothing at all.

On the other hand, one might be stricter than in a case of mere *kiddushin*: even if Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon hold that *kiddushin* alone does not disqualify her — since *kiddushin* is still distant from actual *biah* — they might agree that *chuppah* is different. *Chuppah* is fundamentally about *nisuin* and cohabitation; it exists in the very context of sexual union. Therefore, even those who are lenient about *kiddushin* might concede that *chuppah* disqualifies her, treating it as functionally equivalent to *biah*.

### Tosafot: Chuppah Act

Tosafot finds Rashi's framing difficult. It seems strange, Tosafot argues, to treat *chuppah* completely devoid of *kiddushin* as though it carries any independent halakhic significance. If the act has no halakhic grounding whatsoever, why would it create legal consequences? A couple who entered a room together without any prior *kiddushin* — why should that be treated as tantamount to forbidden *biah*?

Tosafot therefore turns to a Talmudic debate in tractate Kiddushin that considers whether *chuppah* can itself constitute an act of *kiddushin*, parallel to the standard modes of *kesef*, *shtar*, and *biah*. On Tosafot's reading, the Gemara here presupposes this possibility: the *chuppah* is being discussed as itself an act of *kiddushin*, and the question is whether that kind of *kiddushin* — effected through *chuppah* — disqualifies the woman.

Tosafot acknowledges that this reading creates its own circularity. If *chuppah* functions as *kiddushin*, then one is back to the question of *kiddushin* itself, and it is not immediately clear how this advances the debate beyond where Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer already stand. Tosafot also offers an alternative reading according to which the Gemara is asking about a case where there was *kiddushin* followed by *chuppah* but no actual *biah*, and then the husband died or divorced her. The question becomes whether the *chuppah*, having occurred without *biah*, has permanently altered her status — rendering her a *chalalah* — or whether it is only the act of *biah* that causes permanent invalidation.

### Baraita: No Sex Yet

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* in support of the discussion. The *tanna kamma* teaches that women — whether *kesherot* or *psulot* — who married *kohanim*, or who entered *chuppah* without having had sexual relations, eat from the husband's table and eat *terumah*.

The Gemara questions this reading. When the *baraita* says "*nisu*" — they married — surely that implies actual marriage including consummation. How, then, could these *psulot* be eating *terumah*? If they have had actual *biah* that is forbidden from a *kehunah* perspective, they should be disqualified as *chalalot* and permanently barred from *terumah* even from the father's house.

The Gemara resolves this by reading the two phrases — *nisu* and *nichnesu l'chuppah v'lo niv'alu* — as parallel descriptions of the same scenario rather than two distinct categories. The *baraita* must be speaking of women who entered *chuppah* but did not have intercourse. In that case, *psulot* who had *chuppah* without *biah* are still permitted to eat *terumah*. The *tanna kamma* holds *ein chuppah li-psulot*: *chuppah* without *biah* is not treated as equivalent to *biah*, and her status remains unchanged.

### R. Yishmael: Parallel Ac…

Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, takes the opposing position and articulates it by drawing a direct parallel between *chuppah* and *biah*: whatever *biah* would entitle a woman to eat — *chuppah* also entitles her to eat. Whatever *biah* would not entitle her to eat — *chuppah* also does not. In a case where the woman is a *psulah* and the *biah* would not only fail to entitle her to eat *terumah* but would affirmatively disqualify her by making her a *chalalah*, Rashi understands Rabbi Yishmael as saying that *chuppah* operates the same way: it is not merely that she is not entitled to eat in this state, but that the *chuppah* itself has rendered her permanently a *chalalah*.

This represents *yesh chuppah li-psulot* at its most sweeping: even without *biah*, the act of *chuppah* with a *psulah* creates the same permanent disqualification that actual forbidden intercourse would.

The Gemara then asks whether this dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael simply maps onto the existing debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon. It concludes that the mapping is not straightforward. Rabbi Meir might be lenient specifically about *chuppah* without *kiddushin* — since without *kiddushin* there is no halakhic *kinyan* at all — even though he is strict about *kiddushin* alone. And Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon might be stricter about *chuppah* than about *kiddushin* alone — since *chuppah* is, by its very nature, oriented toward *biah* in a way that *kiddushin* is not. The *baraita*'s emphasis on *chuppah* rather than *kiddushin* or *kesef* points toward the *chuppah* itself as the operative legal act, which supports reading the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael as staking out independent positions rather than merely echoing the tannaitic dispute.

Rav Pappa then reports that Rav Sheshet illuminated this very question from a *mishnah*, declaring *yesh chuppah li-psulot*, and promising to demonstrate that this position is already embedded in an existing *mishnah*. This brings the discussion to the *mishnah* in tractate *Sotah*, which will reframe the question in far broader terms.

## Sotah Investigation

### Sotah Prerequisites

The Gemara turns to the *mishnah* in *Sotah* to support the position of *yesh chuppah li-psulot*. That *mishnah* explains the double *amen* spoken by the suspected woman: *amen she-lo satisi arusa v'nesuah, shomeret yavam u-knusah* — I did not stray when I was an *arusah*, when I was a *nesuah*, when I was a *shomeret yavam*, or after *yibum* was performed. The *mishnah* thus indicates that the *kinui* — the formal jealousy warning — and the *stirah* — the seclusion — can operate even in the context of *arusah* and *shomeret yavam*, not only in the standard case of full *nisuin*.

The Gemara immediately asks: what exactly is the case of *arusah* here? If the *kinui* was issued while she was an *arusah* and the *hashkayah* — making her drink the *sotah* water — also occurred while she was an *arusah*, this cannot work. The *mishnah* in *Sotah* itself establishes that an *arusah* and a *shomeret yavam* do not drink and do not collect their *ketubah*. The *sotah* water is administered only when the verse *tachat ishech* — under your husband — applies, meaning the woman must be a full *nesuah*. An *arusah* has not yet reached that status.

### Chuppah No Consummation

The Gemara therefore reconstructs the case: the *kinui* was issued when she was an *arusah*, the *stirah* occurred, and then they proceeded to *chuppah* without having had intercourse — *nichnesah l'chuppah v'lo niv'alah*. The husband can now administer the *sotah* water for what she did during the *erusin* period, because she is now considered a *nesuah* by virtue of the *chuppah*.

But — and here lies the broader legal significance — this is only possible if the *chuppah* itself is meaningful. She was a *sotah* during *erusin*, forbidden to her husband after the *stirah*. The *chuppah* therefore occurred while she was a *psulah* — forbidden to him. If *chuppah* for a *psulah* has no halakhic standing, then she never became a *nesuah* at all, and the *sotah* procedure cannot be performed. The very structure of the *mishnah* thus implies *yesh chuppah li-psulot*.

This is a striking broadening of the question. Earlier the discussion was confined to a narrow question: does *chuppah* create the equivalent of *biah* to invalidate a woman for *terumah*? Now the question has become general: is *chuppah* with a woman who is forbidden to the husband a meaningful act of *nisuin* at all? The *sotah* case demonstrates that *chuppah* in the presence of *issur* can be halakhically operative in the full sense — not merely creating an analogue to *biah*, but conferring the complete status of *nesuah*.

### Sex Requirement Issue

The Gemara now raises a further difficulty from a *baraita* brought by Rav Acha bar Chanina, who came from the south bearing a teaching: *mibal'adei ishech* — the verse "aside from your husband" — applies only when *shechivat ba'al* preceded *shechivat bo'el*: the husband must have had intercourse with the woman before the suspected adulterer. If the adulterer had intercourse with her first, the *sotah* water does not apply. This is derived from the plain sense of the verse — your husband has already been with you, and now another man has been with you as well.

This creates a serious problem for our constructed case. We said the *stirah* and suspected sin occurred when she was an *arusah*. How, then, did they ever proceed to *chuppah*? After *stirah*, the husband is forbidden to have intercourse with a suspected *sotah*. If they had *chuppah* without *biah*, then the condition of *shechivat ba'al* preceding *shechivat bo'el* is never satisfied, and the *sotah* water would not work.

Rami bar Chama proposes: the *arus* had intercourse with her while she was still in her father's house — an act of intimacy during the *erusin* period, before the *kinui* and *stirah* took place. He then performed *kinui*, she had the *stirah*, and now — after *chuppah* but without *biah* — he can make her drink, because the prior *biah* satisfies the condition of *shechivat ba'al*, and his current abstinence ensures he is *menukah me'avon*.

This resolves the case of *arusah*. But the *sotah mishnah* also includes *shomeret yavam*. The Gemara asks: how can one construct a case of *shomeret yavam* in which there has already been *shechivat ba'al*? Once the brother-in-law has intercourse with the *yevamah*, she is no longer a *shomeret yavam* — she is fully his wife.

### Rav: Intent Irrelevant

The Gemara recalls the earlier debate between Rav and Shmuel. Rav holds *kanah l'chol mili* — an act of *biah* with a *yevamah*, even if performed with no intent to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *yibum*, with no intent to create a marital bond, but purely for the sake of gratification, nevertheless acquires her completely and makes her his full wife in every respect. Shmuel disagrees: without the proper intent to effect *yibum*, such intercourse acquires her only for those matters explicitly enumerated in the *parshah* of *yibum* — exempting co-wives, exempting other brothers from *yibum*, creating inheritance rights — but does not confer the full status of *ishto* that we speak of in general *halacha*.

If so, one might think: according to Shmuel, the *yavam* could have had intercourse with the *yevamah* purely for gratification, and since that intercourse does not confer full wife-status, she could still be called a *shomeret yavam* in a meaningful sense. But then the *sotah* water would not work on her either, since for *sotah* purposes one needs the full status of *nesuah*. The circle cannot be squared using Shmuel's position alone.

The Gemara therefore notes that this entire line of reasoning is being advanced in support of Rav — who is the one who holds *yesh chuppah li-psulot*. It is Rav's position we are trying to demonstrate from the *mishnah*. But the *shomeret yavam* case cannot be explained satisfactorily according to Rav, because Rav holds that any act of *biah* with a *yevamah* makes her completely his wife, leaving no room for the category of *shomeret yavam who has already had relations with the yavam*.

### Maamar Changes Status

The Gemara now introduces an answer of remarkable conceptual audacity. According to Beit Shammai, *maamar* — the formal levitical declaration analogous to *kiddushin* — acquires the *yevamah* with the force of a complete *kinyan* and effects a full *arusah* status. More than that, it completely displaces (*hefriach*) the *zikat yibum*. Once *maamar* is performed, she is no longer in the category of *shomeret yavam*; she is now in the category of a standard *arusah*.

The consequence is striking: subsequent intercourse — if performed not for the purpose of *nisuin* or *yibum* but simply as an act of intimacy — does not have the normal halakhic effect that *biah* with a *yevamah* would have. In a standard *yibum* case, any *biah* automatically elevates the woman to *nesuah* status. But according to Beit Shammai, once *maamar* was performed, the *zika* is removed, and the subsequent intercourse is more analogous to *biah* with an *arusah* — meaningful but insufficient to create *nisuin* without the proper intent.

This means the *yavam* can have had intercourse with her while she was still classified as an *arusah*, satisfying the condition of *shechivat ba'al*, and yet she remains in an *arusah*-like status — parallel to the category of *shomeret yavam* — without having been elevated to *nesuah* through that act. Rashi makes this explicit: the *maamar* rendered her a complete *arusah gemura* and severed the *zikat yibum*, such that *biah mezid* — intercourse without the intent of *yibum* — no longer functions as a *yibum* act. She therefore continues to be called *shomeret yavam*, even after intercourse, because the relationship is now defined by the *maamar*-*kiddushin* rather than by the *zika*.

### Status Parallels

The Gemara then asks why the *mishnah* lists *shomeret yavam* and *knusah* as separate categories when — if *yibum* is performed — *knusah* is legally indistinguishable from *nesuah*. The answer is that the *mishnah* is drawing parallel categories: *nesuah* refers to a woman the man himself chose to marry through the ordinary process, while *knusah* refers to a woman who came to him through his brother's death. Similarly, *arusah* and *shomeret yavam* are parallel constructs — *arusah* is the *erusin* he himself initiated, while *shomeret yavam* is the *arusah*-like state created through *maamar* vis-à-vis a woman originally bound to him through *zika*.

The parallel structure of the *mishnah* thus accommodates both pairs without requiring that they be substantively different in every respect, only that they represent distinct relational pathways to an equivalent halakhic status.

### Two Solutions

The Gemara ultimately offers two resolutions to the complex case of *sotah* in the context of *arusah*.

Rashi cites Rav Pappa who invokes a *baraita*: one does not perform *kinui* on an *arusah* for the purposes of making her drink while she is still an *arusah*, but one may perform *kinui* while she is an *arusah* for the purpose of making her drink once she has become a *nesuah*. On this reading, *ein l'hashkotah k'shehi arusah* — she may not drink while an *arusah* — but *aval mekana lah l'hashkotah k'shehi nesuah* — the warning issued during *erusin* can serve as the basis for administering the water after *nisuin*. This resolves the problem by clarifying that the *stirah* occurred when she was already a *nesuah*; it was only the *kinui* that was issued during *erusin*.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a different solution: *al yedei gilgul*. The principle of *gilgul shvua* holds that once a person is properly before the court and obligated to take an oath on some matter, additional sworn statements on other matters can be "rolled into" that oath. This principle — by no means limited to *sotah* — is derived from this very context. Here, it means that once the woman is validly in the *sotah* procedure based on a proper qualifying case, the court can compel her to swear regarding suspected sins that occurred even during the *erusin* period, even if those incidents would not independently have triggered the full *sotah* process. The double *amen* — *amen she-lo satisi arusa* — reflects precisely this: her oath about the *erusin* period is folded into the main *sotah* oath through *gilgul*.

## Maamar & Terumah

### Maamar Scenario

The Gemara now moves to a new case transmitted by Rav Chanina in the name of Rav Yochanan. A *kohen* performs *maamar* with his *yevamah* — his sister-in-law awaiting *yibum* — and there remains another brother who has not yet performed any act. Even where the *maamar*-performing brother is a *kohen* and the *yevamah* is a *bat kohen*, and the marriage is entirely unproblematic on its face, the *maamar* has nonetheless disqualified her from eating *terumah*. She cannot eat *terumah* until the *yibum* is fully consummated.

### Zikat Yibum Problem

The reason is subtle and reveals the layered nature of the *zikat yibum*. On the surface, everything appears in order. She is a *bat kohen*, entitled to eat *terumah* from her father's house. The *kohen* who performed *maamar* is a perfectly kosher husband-in-waiting; she is not a *grushah* or *almanah* forbidden to him. One would expect that either by virtue of her father, or by virtue of the pending *nisuin* with this *kohen*, she should be permitted to eat *terumah*.

However, the other brother — the one who did not perform *maamar* — still retains a *zikat yibum* toward her. He is forbidden, rabbinically, to act on that *zika* once *maamar* has been performed by the first brother. Yet the *zika* itself has not been dissolved. That residual *zika*, the Gemara explains, functions as a quasi-*kiddushin*. It is as if there is a second claim on her, and were that claim ever to be acted upon, it would constitute a *biah asurah* — since the *maamar* has defined her as bound to the first brother.

The Gemara frames this through the lens of Rabbi Meir's principle: *shomeret l'viah psulah lo achlah* — a woman who is "awaiting a forbidden *biah*" does not eat *terumah*. The other brother's *zika*, if consummated, would be forbidden; therefore she is in a state of *shomeret l'viah psulah* and cannot eat. The question then becomes: does this principle apply only when the awaited forbidden *biah* is a biblical prohibition, or also when it is merely rabbinic?

### Chalal Brother Case

The Gemara refines the case further. When Ravin came from the Land of Israel, he transmitted: if the *kohen* performed *maamar* with his *yevamah*, and there is no complicating factor with the other brother — even if the first brother is a *kohen* and she is a *bat kohen* — she may eat *terumah*. The *divrei hakol* position in the simple case is lenient.

However, if the other brother — the one who did not perform *maamar* — is a *chalal*, the situation changes entirely. A *chalal* who has intercourse with a woman causes her to become a *chalalah*, permanently disqualified. The *zika* of this *chalal* brother toward the *yevamah* thus represents a potential *biah psulah d'oraita* — a biblically forbidden act of intercourse that would create a permanent and irremediable disqualification. In this case, even Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon — who are generally lenient — agree that she cannot eat *terumah*.

The structural reason explains why. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon are normally lenient even in cases where the eventual *biah* would be problematic, because: the *kohen* involved could, in another situation, have a woman who eats *terumah* on his behalf — *yesh lo l'haachil b'makom acher*. That conceptual permission attaches to him and flows through to her. But a *chalal* can never have any woman eat *terumah* by virtue of her relationship with him; there is no scenario in which a *chalal*'s wife eats *terumah* on account of him. Therefore the *zika* of the *chalal* brother cannot be "covered" by that principle, and even Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon concede the disqualification.

### Post-Get Status

The question then shifts to what happens after the *maamar*-performing brother gives her a *get*. The *get* in a *yibum* context dissolves the *maamar*, but because a *get* has now been given, it is rabbinically prohibited for either brother to consummate *yibum* — the act of *yibum* after a *get* is rabbinically forbidden. Biblically, the *zika* still exists. So she is now in a state where, if the brother were to act on his *zika*, it would be a *biah* that is rabbinically but not biblically prohibited. Is she permitted to eat *terumah* at this stage?

### Yochanan v. Lakish

Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish disagree.

Rabbi Yochanan says she may eat. Even Rabbi Meir, the strict opinion, applies his principle only where the awaited forbidden *biah* is a biblical prohibition. In this case, the potential *biah* — *yibum* after a *get* — is forbidden only rabbinically. Therefore even Rabbi Meir would agree she may eat.

Resh Lakish says she may not eat. Even Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, the lenient opinion, apply their leniency only where the *kohen* involved could, in some analogous scenario, have a woman eat *terumah* on his behalf — *yesh lo l'haachil b'makom acher*. Here, this brother has given a *get* to this woman. The very nature of his relationship with any woman he might stand in such a relation to is defined by the *get*, and no woman in that category can ever eat *terumah* on account of him. Even if he were married to another woman with whom this same *get*-defined dynamic held, that woman could not eat *terumah* by virtue of him. Therefore even Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon would not permit her to eat.

The Gemara addresses a potential objection: might a woman who receives a *get* from a *kohen* subsequently return to her father's house and eat *terumah* by virtue of her father — thus demonstrating that women connected to a *kohen* through a *get* can eat *terumah* in some fashion? The Gemara rejects this: a woman who returns to her father's house (*chozeret*) has fully separated from the *kohen*'s sphere — *psika minei u'krova l'vei nasha*, the relationship is severed and she reverts to her family. But in the present case the woman is still *agidah beih* — bound to him by the residual *zika* and the *get* — and cannot be treated as analogous to the *chozeret*.

## Kohen Gadol Rules

### Ketanah-Bogeret Timing

The Gemara transitions to the laws of the *kohen gadol* and his marital restrictions, in preparation for the *mishnah* that follows. Rav Chiyya bar Yosef poses a question to Shmuel: a *kohen gadol* performs *kiddushin* with a *ketanah* — a minor girl — and she matures into a *bogeret* while still under his authority, before *chuppah* has occurred. The *kohen gadol* is restricted to marrying a woman who meets specific criteria — she must be a virgin, and according to one interpretation she must not yet be a *bogeret*. The question is: does this restriction apply at the moment of *kiddushin*, or at the moment of *nisuin*?

Shmuel responds by pointing to the *mishnah*: women forbidden to a *kohen* who were divorced or widowed after *nisuin* remain disqualified — *pasul* — while those divorced or widowed after only *erusin* are *kesheirah*. He argues that this demonstrates what matters for the *kohen*'s special disqualifications: it is the *nisuin*, not the *erusin*.

### Widow Exemption

Rav Chiyya bar Yosef pushes back: the *mishnah* speaks of disqualification through *biah*, and it is *biah* that creates the disqualification of *chalalah*. What makes a woman a *chalalah* is the act of forbidden intercourse, which occurs at *nisuin*, not at *kiddushin*. The *mishnah* tells us only which act creates that specific disqualification; it says nothing about the threshold criteria for the *kohen gadol*'s marriage.

The Gemara then introduces another case that directly tests the question: a man performs *kiddushin* with an *almanah* — a widow — while he is a regular *kohen*, and then he is subsequently appointed *kohen gadol*. May he go through with the *chuppah*? The *mishnah* here appears to say yes — *yichnos*, he may marry her — implying that since the *kiddushin* was permissible at the time it was performed, the *nisuin* may proceed even though he is now a *kohen gadol* for whom an *almanah* would normally be forbidden.

Shmuel supports his position from this ruling: since the *kohen gadol* may complete the marriage to an *almanah* when the *kiddushin* preceded his appointment, it seems that the operative moment is *kiddushin*, not *nisuin*.

### One Word Limit

The Gemara challenges this inference. The case of *almanah* is derived from an extra word in the *pasuk*: *yikach isha* — he shall take a woman. From this seemingly superfluous phrase, the Gemara derives that even a *kohen gadol* may complete *nisuin* with an *almanah* when the *kiddushin* predated his appointment. This is a special *limud* for the *almanah* case. By the same token, one could propose that the same *limud* should apply to the *bogeret* case — that the *pasuk*'s word *isha* likewise permits the *kohen gadol* to complete *nisuin* with a *bogeret* when the *kiddushin* was performed while she was still a *naarah*.

The Gemara rejects this: *isha achas v'lo shtayim* — the word *isha* appears once, and from one word only one special case can be derived. The *limud* was already used for *almanah*; it cannot be simultaneously applied to *bogeret*.

But this creates the obvious question: given that both cases seem to be covered by the same verse, why does the *almanah* case receive the benefit of the *limud* while the *bogeret* case does not?

### Status Change Logic

The Gemara answers: *ha ishtani gufa v'ha lo ishtani gufa* — in one case her body changed, and in the other it did not. Tosafot offers a clearer account of the underlying logic. The decisive factor is not the physical change itself, but rather: *who* underwent the change in status?

In the *almanah* case, the woman's own status did not change between *kiddushin* and *nisuin*. She was an *almanah* at the time of *kiddushin* and remains an *almanah* now. It was the man whose status changed — he was a regular *kohen* at *kiddushin* and became a *kohen gadol* afterward. Since her status was permissible when the relationship was established, the *kiddushin* defines the framework, and the *nisuin* proceeds accordingly. The special *limud* from *yikach isha* confirms this reading explicitly for the *almanah* case.

By contrast, in the *bogeret* case, it is the woman whose status changed between *kiddushin* and *nisuin*. She was a *ketanah* or *naarah* at the time of *kiddushin* — fully permissible to the *kohen gadol* — but she became a *bogeret* before *nisuin*. Since the relevant change occurred in her status, the *nisuin* must be evaluated based on who she is now, not who she was at *kiddushin*. The *limud* from *isha* is therefore reserved for the *almanah* case where the woman's status was constant, and the *bogeret* case — where the woman's own status changed — cannot benefit from it.

This distinction shapes a broader principle: when assessing whether the special marital requirements of a *kohen gadol* are satisfied, one evaluates the woman's status at *kiddushin* when the change was on his side, and at *nisuin* when the change was on hers. The discussion of the *kohen gadol*'s special restrictions will continue in the material that follows.
