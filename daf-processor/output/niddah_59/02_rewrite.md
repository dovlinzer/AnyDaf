# Niddah 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tliya & Lice

### Tliya Principle

The daf begins immediately following the *mishna* on 58b, which introduced the principle of *tliya* — the attribution of a stain to an external source — as a basis for ruling a woman *tahor*. The Gemara employs this framework to permit attributing a blood-colored stain to something other than menstrual blood. Even a wound that has healed may be considered a potential source, on the theory that it might reopen and produce blood. This sets the stage for Rabbi Akiva's sweeping statement that the Sages' entire framework of *ketamim* is oriented toward leniency — a principle he grounds in the verse *dam v'lo kesem*, "blood and not a stain," which may indicate that the laws of *niddah* apply only to blood seen emerging directly from the body, not to something discovered as a stain on a garment. This *drasha* stands alongside the position of Shmuel, who grounds the leniency of stains in the absence of a bodily *hargasha* (sensation), and the simpler reading that stains carry an inherent element of doubt about their origin.

### Rabbi Meir & Sycamore

The Gemara notes: *tanina l'ha de-tano rabbanan* — "we have taught in our *mishna* that which the *Baraita* teaches." The *Baraita* records two cases of *tliya* in practice. In the first, Rabbi Meir attributed a stain to *kilor*, a reddish dye or organic compound that a woman had presumably been handling. In the second, Rabbi attributed a stain to the sap of a sycamore tree — *saraf shikma* — which was similarly reddish. In each case, the existence of a plausible external source of reddish matter was sufficient to render the woman *tahor*.

### Butcher Market

The *Baraita* continues: if a woman passed through a butcher's market — *shuk shel tabachim* — where blood was present in the environment, and she subsequently finds a stain, she may attribute it to that blood even if she is uncertain whether any blood actually splattered on her (*safek niteza vah safek lo niteza vah*). The attribution holds as long as she knows she was in the vicinity. However, if she is uncertain even whether she passed through that market (*safek avra safek lo avra*), she is *teme'ah* and cannot rely on the attribution.

### Formal Rules

This distinction reveals that the laws of *tliya* operate according to formal rules rather than simple probabilistic reasoning. One might compare it to the principle of *ein machzikin tumah mimakom lemakom* and *ein machzikin tahara mimakom lemakom* — that *tumah* or *tahara* cannot be transported indirectly from one location to another. So too here: there must be a concrete, known connection between the woman and a potential external source of blood. An abstract possibility — even a statistically plausible one — is insufficient. Once a known source is established and the woman has a direct connection to it, the stain may be attributed there entirely. But a merely theoretical possibility, however reasonable, does not suffice to create the formal link required for *tliya*.

## Gris Size & Lice

### Shimon vs. Sages

The Gemara transitions with the phrase *ha gufa kashya* — the matter itself is difficult — shifting attention to the minimum size requirement for *ketamim*, which is among the most practically significant of all the *kula*'s in this area of *halacha*. A stain smaller than a *gris* — the size of a split bean, roughly equivalent to a penny or nickel — is not a problem at all. The *mishna*'s position on whether a louse must have been killed in order to attribute a stain to it is now examined, and the Gemara identifies the author of the *mishna* as Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel.

The *Baraita* presents a three-way dispute. Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *harga — tola, lo harga — eino tola*. If she killed a louse, she may attribute the stain to louse blood; if she did not, she may not. The implication is that even the tiniest stain is problematic without confirmed louse-killing. The Sages (*chachamim*) hold the opposite extreme: *bein kach u'vein kach tola* — whether or not she killed a louse, she may always attribute any stain to louse blood, with no minimum size specified.

Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel then criticizes both positions in turn. Regarding his own view, he admits: *she'ein lach kol mita u'mita she'ein bah kama tipei dam macholat* — there is no bed that does not have a few tiny spots of blood from lice. If even the tiniest drop cannot be attributed to a louse, a woman will effectively never be *tehorah* to her husband. This is an almost startling instance of a Tanna openly acknowledging the unworkable severity of his own ruling. Regarding the Sages, he argues the inverse: *she'ein lach kol sadin min hasadinim she'ein bo kama tipei dam* — there is no sheet without drops of blood, and if any drop can always be attributed to a louse, a woman will always be *tehorah* even when she should not be. Neither extreme is tenable.

### Rabbi Chanina

Given these twin failures, the *Baraita* concludes: *aval nirim divrei Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus midvarai u'midraihem* — the words of Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus appear more reasonable than either position. Rabbi Chanina establishes a defined threshold: *ad kama hi tola — ad kegris shel pol*, until the size of a split bean. Stains up to this size may be attributed to a louse; stains larger than this may not. Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel himself concedes — *u'ledvarav anu modim* — that this middle position is correct.

The practical background to this ruling is the historical reality that lice were ubiquitous. Tiny spots of blood on bedding and clothing were endemic, and any legal framework that ignored this would be functionally unworkable. Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus' *shiur* reflects this reality: up to the size of a *gris*, one attributes the stain to louse blood; beyond it, one cannot.

The Gemara then revisits the Sages' position. Since they said a woman may *always* attribute a stain to a louse, with no upper limit specified, the Gemara asks what maximum size they would permit. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers: *tola be'pishpesh* — she may attribute a larger stain to a bedbug (*pishpesh*). The implicit reasoning is that a bedbug produces more blood than a louse, and therefore larger stains remain attributable to an insect source. The size ceiling in this case is *ad k'tormas* — the size of a lupine bean, which is considerably larger than the *gris*.

### Bedbug & Tormas

The Sages' position thus allows for attribution up to the size of a *tormas*, a lupine bean substantially larger than the standard *gris*. What the Gemara is revealing is that in an environment where lice and bedbugs were a common reality, the legal framework had to account for a wide range of insect-produced blood stains.

### Bedbug Features

The Gemara digresses to describe the bedbug: *orko k'rochbo* — it is as long as it is wide, meaning it is roughly square or circular in shape. *Tamo k'reycho* — it tastes as it smells. And *bris krutha lo* — a covenant has been made with it, meaning it is part of its nature that *kol ha'molelo m'reyach bo* — anyone who rubs against it will smell it, a sort of built-in protective mechanism.

The Gemara explains why each of these features is legally relevant. The square shape — *orko k'rochbo* — is relevant to *ketamim*: a stain that is roughly circular or square in shape may be attributed to a bedbug. An unusually elongated stain could not. The taste-resembling-smell detail is cited in the context of *terumah*: if someone eating food detects a bedbug-like taste in his mouth, the principle *tamo k'reycho* tells him that because the taste matches the smell, and the smell is recognizable to anyone who has ever rubbed against one (*bris krutha lo*), he may identify it — and he must spit it out.

### Pigs & Attribution

Rav Ashi adds a further case of environmental *tliya*: *ir she'yesh bah chazirim — ein chosheshin l'ksamim* — in a city where pigs are present, one need not be concerned about stains, since pigs are constantly tracking filth and blood throughout the environment, making any stain attributable to that ambient contamination. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak applies this to the town of Dukra, which — according to Rashi — had such an abundance of garbage heaps and butchers' refuse that blood was omnipresent.

*Tosafot* qualifies the ruling, noting that one should not rely on the presence of pigs to be lenient when the stain is found in a location that pigs would never access. If the circumstances make it genuinely possible for pigs to have been the source, one may be lenient; if not, the custom is to be strict. This again demonstrates the thoroughly contextual nature of all the *tliya* rules: it is not a legal fiction but a realistic attribution, and it must be plausible given the actual circumstances.

## Gris Inclusive/Exclusive

### Rav Huna View

The Gemara returns to the precise threshold of the *gris*, posing the question: *ad kama hi tola?* — how far can she attribute it? Rav Huna rules: *kigris — eina tola* — if the stain is exactly the size of a *gris*, she cannot attribute it to a louse. Only a stain *less than* a *gris* may be attributed. Once it reaches that size exactly, it is already a problem.

### Rav Chisda View

Rav Chisda disagrees: *kigris — tola* — even a stain exactly the size of a *gris* may be attributed. Only *yoter mikigris* — more than a *gris* — is a problem.

### Rav Huna Reply

The Gemara suggests that their dispute may reflect a general disagreement over the semantics of the word *ad* — "until" — in rabbinic usage: *ad ve-ad bichlal* (inclusive of the limit) versus *ad velo ad bichlal* (exclusive of the limit). Rav Huna responds that he does not necessarily commit to a universal rule that *ad* is always exclusive. Rather, his principle is: *ve-hacha lechumra ve-hacha lechumra* — wherever ambiguity exists, one interprets toward stringency. Since the leniency in question is the ability to attribute a stain to a louse, interpreting the threshold as exclusive of the *gris* is the stricter reading, and that is therefore the correct one.

### Rav Chisda Reply

Rav Chisda counters with equal nuance. He does not necessarily commit to *ad* always being inclusive. Rather, his position is grounded in the specific ruling of Rabbi Abahu: *kol shiurei chachamim le-hachmir, chutz mi-kigris she-ba-ketamim le-hakel* — all the minimum sizes established by the Sages are interpreted strictly in cases of doubt, with a single exception: the *gris* of *ketamim*, which is interpreted leniently. Therefore, when a stain is exactly the size of a *gris*, Rav Chisda rules it is still permissible, because the *gris* threshold in the context of stains is uniquely oriented toward leniency. This is consistent with Rabbi Akiva's overarching principle that the whole category of stains was designed to find leniency for women within a rabbinic — not biblical — framework.

An important practical dimension should be noted. The woman herself is the one applying the *tliya*: the language of the *mishna* is *yechola litlot* — she *may* attribute it. The attribution is hers to make, not necessarily a rabbi's. This is significant: women are envisioned as active legal actors in this domain, capable of making these determinations for themselves. The beginning of the *masechet* even contains cases where women rule for other women regarding stains. Expertise in this area does not require a male rabbinic authority — it requires trained judgment in the relevant criteria, and any sufficiently trained person may serve in that role.

As for when an expert *must* actually examine a stain: the primary occasions requiring expert eyes are questions of color, which remains relevant regardless of whether the origin is a *bedikah* cloth or an external stain. Stains smaller than the minimum size require no expert consultation. Stains larger than the minimum size, or any discharge from an internal check where size is irrelevant and color alone determines status, are the situations that call for examination by someone trained to assess color reliably.

## Upper/Lower Stains

### Above/Below Belt

The Gemara raises a further challenge to the Rav Huna–Rav Chisda dispute. A *Baraita* describes a case where a woman has drops of blood (*tipei damim*) both above and below her belt. Blood below the waist is presumptively a problem; blood above the waist is not, since it obviously did not come from a uterine source. The *Baraita* rules: *tola ba'elyon ad kegris* — she may attribute the lower stain to the same external source as the upper stain, up to the size of a *gris*.

### Upper Spot Size

The Gemara asks: what does it mean that the attribution holds "up to the size of a *gris*"? One reading is that the lower stain is the *gris* in question: the lower stain is exactly the size of a *gris*, which would otherwise be a problem, but the presence of the upper stain allows her to attribute it to an external source. This would prove that a stain of exactly *gris* size is indeed a problem — supporting Rav Huna. However, the Gemara deflects this: *lo — kegris milmalla* — the *gris* refers to the upper stain, not the lower one. If the upper stain is at least *gris*-sized, then it demonstrably did not come from a louse (since louse stains are by definition smaller than a *gris*), and therefore the woman can argue that some external, non-louse source produced the upper stain — and the same external source can account for the lower stain as well. But if the upper stain is smaller than a *gris*, it is itself attributable to a louse, and a louse stain above the belt cannot help her attribute a different stain below the belt to the same external source.

On this reading, the *Baraita* neither proves nor disproves whether exactly *gris* size is itself a problem in the standard case.

## Squashed Louse

### Stain & Louse

The Gemara now addresses the case of *nimtza kegris va'od* — a stain found on a woman that is slightly larger than a *gris*, the problematic size threshold. Within that stain — or within the additional increment — there is a squashed louse (*retzufa bo bamakholet*). The question: does the visible louse permit her to attribute the stain and be *tahor*?

### R. Chanina vs R. Yannai

Rabbi Chanina rules *tamei*: she is *teme'ah*. His reasoning is *ki taita be-kegris* — it is only up to the size of a *gris* that we permit her to attribute the stain to a louse. A stain of *kegris va'od* — *gris* plus — exceeds that threshold, and the presence of a squashed louse within it does not change the analysis. Implicitly: the entire rationale for attributing a *gris*-sized stain to a louse is that we always *assume* an invisible louse caused it. A visible louse does not alter that logic.

Rabbi Yannai rules *tahor*. His reasoning is elegant: *hani milei hecha d'lo retzufa bamakholet* — the general rule that a *gris* stain is attributable to a louse applies even when there is no visible louse. *Aval hecha d'retzufa bamakholet* — but when there is a visible squashed louse, one can reason that the additional blood (*va'od*) comes from that visible louse, and the underlying *kegris* portion is attributed to the invisible louse that the law always assumes. After removing the louse's contribution, *pash lei kegris* — what remains is exactly a *gris*, which under the standard rule is attributable to a louse and therefore permissible. *Keivan d'be'alma taita* — since normally, even without seeing a louse, we attribute it, *hacha nami taita* — here too, we attribute the remaining portion.

## Handling Materials

### Excess Attribution

Rabbi Yirmiya poses a related *she'ela*: *nit'aska be-kegris ve-nimtza la be-kegris va'od* — a woman handled a certain amount of blood or red matter the size of a *gris*, and was then found to have a stain the size of *gris* plus. Some of the stain may be attributed to what she was handling, but the remainder exceeds that attribution. The question is whether the standard *gris* leniency can still be applied to what remains after removing the attributed portion.

The Gemara notes that this question could be posed according to either Rabbi Chanina or Rabbi Yannai. According to Rabbi Chanina, who said the squashed louse does not help when the stain exceeds a *gris*: perhaps one could distinguish the two cases. With a squashed louse, his argument was that the entire basis for leniency — attributing the stain to a louse — is not undermined by the louse's presence. But when she was handling another material, one might say: remove some of the stain as attributable to that material, and attribute the remaining *kegris* to the invisible louse. *Aval hacha d'nit'aska — taita* — perhaps she can be lenient here even according to Rabbi Chanina. Conversely, according to Rabbi Yannai, who was lenient with a squashed louse: perhaps when the woman was merely *handling* something, without the louse being physically present in the stain, the argument from *mokhach milta* (the louse as visible proof) is unavailable, and she cannot attribute the excess. The question remains unresolved.

### Middle Removal

The Gemara then brings a *Baraita* as a potential resolution: *nit'aska be-adom ein tola bo shachor* — if she was handling red matter, she cannot attribute a black stain to it; and *muat ein tola bi-mrubah* — if she was handling a small amount, she cannot attribute a larger stain to that small amount. This seems to address precisely the case of Rabbi Yirmiya: she handled a *gris*, the stain is *gris* plus, so she cannot attribute the larger stain to the lesser source.

The Gemara, however, clarifies that this *Baraita* addresses a different scenario: *d'nit'aska bi-kgris ve-nimtza lah shnei grisim va'od* — she handled a *gris* and found a stain of two *grisim* plus. Even if she fully attributes the entire *gris* she handled, she is left with a stain still larger than a *gris*. That case is obvious — of course the attribution does not help her. What is the *chiddush*? *Mahu de-teima shachata kegris tzipor sheda be-emtza* — one might have thought that the blood of a bird she was handling, rather than sitting at the edge of the stain, fell in the middle of an otherwise acceptable distribution of smaller stains, connecting two separate sub-*gris* dots into an apparent whole. In that case, one might argue: remove the bird's blood from the middle, and each remaining portion falls below the minimum size. *Ka mashma lan* — the Gemara teaches that one cannot play this game. The attribution does not permit one to surgically remove a portion from the interior of a stain so as to leave two sub-*gris* remnants.

## Color Attribution

### Chicken Blood

Rava introduces a further principle: *nimtza lah min echad tola bo kama minim* — if a woman found one type of coloration on her hands, she may attribute several different-colored stains to the same external source. The Gemara challenges this from the *Baraita*: *nit'aska be-adom ein tola bo shachor* — if she was handling red matter, she cannot attribute a black stain to it. How can Rava say otherwise?

The Gemara answers: *nit'aska shani* — the case of deliberate handling is different. When she *knows* she was handling chicken blood, she knows exactly what color it was, and a stain of a different color cannot be attributed to it. Rava, by contrast, is discussing a case where the woman merely noticed discoloration on her hands — she does not know what she encountered or where she was. The fact that she has an unexplained reddish stain on her hands demonstrates the existence of some unidentified external source, and by extension, other-colored stains may also derive from that same unknown source.

### Rava Position

A second version of Rava's statement is then cited: even when she *knows* she was handling one substance, she may attribute other-colored stains to it. The Gemara again challenges this from the *Baraita*, and resolves that Rava's second version refers specifically to *nit'aska be-tarnigoles* — a case where she was handling a chicken. A chicken contains many different types of blood in different colors. So Rava is not actually saying that handling one color permits attribution of a different color; rather, if the one substance she was handling is itself a source of multiple colors, then finding multiple colors on herself is consistent with that single source.

## Stains Rabbinic

### Stringency Created

The Gemara now returns to Rabbi Akiva's statement that the Sages created the category of *ketamim* in order to be lenient. The Gemara raises an objection: the very creation of *ketamim* as a prohibited category was itself a *chumra* — a stringency. By Torah law, stains are not problematic. The Sages introduced a new rabbinic category that made them problematic. How can Rabbi Akiva say this was done *l'hakel*?

Ravina resolves the difficulty: *lo le-hakel mi-divrei Torah ela le-hachmir mi-divrei Torah* — it was not that the Sages found a way to be lenient relative to what the Torah required; rather, the Sages added a stringency on top of the Torah's baseline. The Torah itself does not prohibit stains; that was always the Sages' understanding of *dam v'lo kesem*, or of the *hargasha* requirement, or of the inherent doubtfulness of stains. Given that understanding, the Sages could have left stains entirely unaddressed. Instead they introduced a rabbinic prohibition. *Ve-kamu mi-manfasham de-rabbanan* — and once they established this rabbinic category, the Sages recognized it as entirely their own creation, and within it, they deliberately built in a structure of leniencies: attribution to external sources, minimum sizes, positional considerations, and so forth.

### Leniencies Within

This understanding illuminates the entire arc of the discussion. The Sages were not quietly undermining a Torah prohibition with creative leniencies; they were legislating a wholly rabbinic domain and, having done so, shaped that domain consciously toward maximum leniency consistent with preserving the integrity of the category. When Rabbi Akiva declares that the Sages spoke *l'hakel*, he means: once the rabbinic category exists, everything within it is oriented toward finding the woman *tahor* wherever possible. The minimum size of a *gris*, the *tliya* to lice or external sources, the attribution to wounds — all of these are not exceptions to a stringent rule, but the intended architecture of a leniency-oriented rabbinic framework.

## Bedikah Cloths

### Circular Elongated

The Gemara discusses a *bedikah* cloth — a cloth used for an internal check — left under a pillow. If a circular stain is found on it, it may be attributed to a louse; if the stain is elongated, it is presumptively the result of the internal check itself. This is the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer be-Rabbi Tzadok. The Gemara asks whether the Sages dispute him, and eventually concludes from the statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — *halakha ke-Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok* — that there were indeed those who disagreed, and that the ruling is in his favor. Importantly, Rabbi Eliezer be-Rabbi Tzadok's ruling is actually lenient in context: even when a *bedikah* cloth is involved, if the stain is circular and the cloth was left overnight, it may be attributed to a louse and the woman remains *tehorah*.

A critical practical distinction emerges: the minimum size of a *gris* applies only to stains, not to *bedikah* cloths. When a woman performs an internal check, any blood found on the cloth — regardless of size — is treated as a *re'iyah*, an actual sighting, to which the leniencies of *ketamim* do not apply. Color continues to matter, but size does not. Conversely, since our contemporary circumstances are far more hygienic, one generally does not attribute a *bedikah* cloth's stain to environmental sources. A clean *bedikah* cloth is treated strictly.

### Combining Dots

The Gemara addresses a case of multiple separate dots: *tipin tipin ein mitztarefin* — separate, unconnected drops do not combine to reach the minimum size. This is a *halacha* of great practical importance. If a stain consists of distinct, disconnected dots, their sizes are not aggregated to meet the *gris* threshold.

### Red Matter Size

An additional refinement concerns color. A stain may contain both red and non-red matter. The *gris* threshold applies not to the stain as a whole but to the red (or black) matter within it. If the stain covers a large area but the red component alone does not reach the minimum size, it is not a problem. Conversely, if separate red dots are connected by a non-red stain into a single continuous unit, the question arises whether the red dots combine. The ruling is that a single connected stain counts as one unit, and one measures the quantity of red matter within it. If sufficient red matter exists within that connected stain to constitute a *gris*, it is a problem.

## Blood in Urine

### Meir Standing

The ninth *perek* opens with the *mishna* of *ha-isha she-osah tzracheha* — the woman who is urinating and sees blood in the urine. Rabbi Meir rules: *im omedes — teme'ah* — if she was standing when she urinated, she is *teme'ah*. Standing is an unusual posture for a woman to urinate, and therefore when blood appears under those conditions, we suspect it came from the uterus rather than from the urinary tract.

### Meir Sitting

Conversely, Rabbi Meir continues: *ve-im yosheves — tehorah* — if she was sitting, which is the natural posture, the blood may be attributed to a urinary tract issue rather than a uterine source, and she is *tehorah*.

### Yossi Always Lenient

Rabbi Yossi takes a more sweeping position: *bein kach u-vein kach tehorah* — whether she was standing or sitting, if blood emerges with the urine, she is *tehorah* regardless. This is because — anatomically — the urinary orifice and the uterine orifice are distinct in women, and therefore blood appearing in urine is not presumptively menstrual blood under any circumstance.

## Man & Woman Bucket

### Shared Bucket

The *mishna* then addresses a second scenario: *ish ve-ishah she-asu tzrachehem le-toch ha-safal* — a man and a woman who both urinated into the same bucket, and blood was subsequently found in the water. Here there is an additional ground for leniency beyond those discussed earlier: even if the blood came from a woman, it may have emerged with the urine and be attributable to a urinary issue; and even if it is genuinely uterine blood, one might argue it came from the man, since men can also bleed. Rabbi Yossi, who ruled leniently even in the single-woman case, rules *metaher* here as well.

### R. Shimon Source

Rabbi Shimon, however, rules *metamei* even in the case of the shared bucket. His reasoning is stated explicitly in the *mishna*: *she-ein derech ha-ish lehotzi dam — ela she-chezkas damim min ha-ishah* — blood does not ordinarily come from a man; therefore, the presumption (*chazaka*) is that blood in this context comes from a woman, and comes from a uterine source. The double doubt — perhaps it is urinary, perhaps it is from the man — does not move Rabbi Shimon, because the *chazaka* of uterine blood from a woman overrides competing possibilities.

## One vs Double Doubt

### Reish Lakish

The Gemara asks: in the case of the shared bucket, where there is a double doubt — perhaps the blood is urinary and perhaps it came from the man — what would Rabbi Meir say? Is he strict only with a single doubt (*safek echad*), or does he remain strict even with a double doubt (*safek sefeika*)? Reish Lakish rules: *hu hu* — it is the same. Rabbi Meir would be strict even when there is also a man, since the *mishna* does not list Rabbi Meir among those who are *metaher* in the shared bucket case. Presumably then, Rabbi Meir remains strict.

The Gemara presses: if Rabbi Meir is strict even with a double doubt, why does the *mishna* even state his position in the simpler case of one woman standing? The more stringent double-doubt case would have been sufficient to convey his position. The answer is: *le-hodia-acha kocho de-Rabbi Yossi* — the *mishna* chose to present Rabbi Meir's one-doubt stringency in order to highlight the power of Rabbi Yossi's leniency. Rabbi Yossi is lenient even when there is only one doubt and no man is present. Emphasizing this case makes his position more striking.

### Leniency Emphasis

The Gemara then asks the reverse: given that Rabbi Yossi is lenient even in the simpler case, why bother stating the shared-bucket case at all? The Gemara responds: *koach de-heteira adif lei* — the Gemara prefers to demonstrate the power of a lenient ruling over the power of a stringent one. To be strict is easy and requires no courage; one simply plays it safe. To rule leniently requires genuine confidence that the ruling is correct and that it is the proper thing to say. When Rabbi Yossi rules leniently even where there is only one woman and one doubt, that is a more powerful demonstration of the principle than the double-doubt case. The Talmud prioritizes exhibiting the reach of *heter*.

### R. Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan holds that Rabbi Meir stated his strict position only in the one-doubt scenario — the single woman standing. *Aval be-safek sefeika — lo amar* — with a double doubt, Rabbi Meir would not rule *teme'ah*. The Gemara asks: if so, why does the *mishna* in the shared-bucket case not explicitly state that Rabbi Meir joins Rabbi Yossi in ruling *tahor*? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *la-idi de-nasab Rabbi Yossi* — since the previous clause had just stated Rabbi Yossi's lenient ruling, the *mishna* simply continued with Rabbi Yossi's name, even though Rabbi Meir agrees in this case. The omission of Rabbi Meir's name is stylistic, not substantive.

## Yossi Consistent

### Yossi L'Chatchila

The Gemara raises a further question about Rabbi Yossi's position. One might have assumed that his leniency applies only *be-di-avad* — after the fact, when the woman has already handled *taharos* and the question is whether to burn the *terumah*. Perhaps *le-chatchila* — prospectively — Rabbi Yossi would advise greater caution. The Gemara answers that the second case — the shared bucket — was included in the *mishna* precisely to preempt this assumption. If Rabbi Yossi is lenient even in a case that could have been the basis for additional stringency, it demonstrates that his leniency is consistent and applies even *le-chatchila*.

### Baraita Support

A *Baraita* is then cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan's position: in the case of the man and woman sharing a bucket, the *Baraita* explicitly states *Rabbi Meir ve-Rabbi Yossi metaherin* — both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi rule the woman *tehorah*. This confirms that Rabbi Meir's stringency is limited to the single-doubt case, and that with a double doubt — the possibility that the blood came from the man — even Rabbi Meir concedes leniency.

## Shimon Strict

### Shimon Sitting

The Gemara now explores the limits of Rabbi Shimon's stringency. We know that Rabbi Shimon is strict in the shared-bucket case, ruling *metamei* despite the double doubt. But was his strict ruling limited to the case where the woman was *standing* — as Rabbi Meir originally required for suspicion of uterine blood — or does he also apply his stringency when the woman was sitting?

### Shimon Scenarios

A *Baraita* resolves this: *yosheves — tola, omedes — eina tola* — sitting, she may attribute; standing, she may not. That is Rabbi Meir. *Bein kach u-vein kach tola* — whether sitting or standing, she may always attribute: that is Rabbi Yossi. *Bein kach u-vein kach eina tola* — whether sitting or standing, she may never attribute: that is Rabbi Shimon. So Rabbi Shimon rules strictly even when she was sitting.

### Chezkas Damim

A final question is posed: if Rabbi Shimon is strict both when she is sitting and when there is a man present — each being one *safek* — would he be lenient when both conditions obtain simultaneously (double doubt)? The Gemara answers from the concluding language of the *mishna* itself: Rabbi Shimon invokes the principle of *chezkas damim min ha-isha* — it is a presumption that blood comes from a woman and from a uterine source, since that is the most common and natural origin of female bleeding. Given this *chazaka*, Rabbi Shimon would be strict regardless of how many competing possibilities exist. *Lo shana omdim ve-lo shana yoshvim* — he does not distinguish between standing and sitting, between a man being present or not.

## Current Halacha

### We Follow Yossi

The *perek* concludes with the ruling of Rav Abba: *ve-chen hora lei Rav Abba le-chalateih — halakha ke-Rabbi Yossi* — the practical *halacha* follows Rabbi Yossi. Blood found in the urine of a woman is not a problem of *niddah*, regardless of her posture and regardless of whether a man is also present. This is the operative ruling, and a woman who finds blood in her urine, under the circumstances described, need not be concerned that she has become a *niddah* as a result.
