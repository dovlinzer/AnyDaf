# Yevamot 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Great-Aunts & Sixteen

### Great-Aunt Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא מִתּוֹסַנְיָיא לְרַב פַּפֵּי: יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אֲבִי הָאָב, וַאֲחוֹת אֲבִי הָאָב, מַהוּ? מִדִּלְמַטָּה עֶרְוָה — לְמַעְלָה נָמֵי גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָא אִיתְפְּלִיג דָּרָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya from Tusneya sent** a message **to Rav Pappi: Let our Master teach us: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **the wife of a father's father's brother and the sister of a father's father?** Did the Sages prohibit these as secondary forbidden relations? The Gemara presents the different sides of the dilemma: Since one generation **below is a forbidden relation,** i.e., a father's sister and the wife of a father's paternal brother, who are both forbidden by Torah law, perhaps the Sages **also decreed** about the women one generation **above. Or, perhaps the generations are separate** and the women in the generation above are not considered to be forbidden relatives.

The sugya opens with a query sent by Rav Mesharshiya of Tusanya to Rav Pappi: *eishet achi avi ha'av v'achot avi ha'av mahu* — what is the status of one's great-aunt, whether she be the sister of one's grandfather or the wife of one's grandfather's brother? Having already worked through the standard list of *shniyot l'arayot* and touched on the question of *yesh lahem hefsek* or *ein lahem hefsek* — whether the secondary prohibitions continue across multiple generations — the *gemara* now turns to flesh out the list further.

To situate the question: the *Torah* itself forbids a man's aunt who is his father's sister (*erva de'oraita*), and equally the wife of his father's paternal brother. These are *arayot* on the level of biblical law. What the *gemara* is now asking concerns the exact same structural relationship, but shifted one generation upward — not one's father's sister but one's grandfather's sister, not one's father's paternal brother's wife but one's grandfather's brother's wife. In other words, the great-aunt.

### Gezeira for Great-Aunts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדִּלְמַטָּה עֶרְוָה — לְמַעְלָה נָמֵי גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָא אִיתְפְּלִיג דָּרָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Since one generation **below is a forbidden relation,** perhaps the Sages **also decreed** about the women one generation **above. Or, perhaps the generations are separate.**

The question is framed as follows: *midelemata ervah, lemala nami gazru bah* — since at the generation directly below this scenario the relationship constitutes a biblical *ervah*, do we say that the Rabbis likewise extended the prohibition one generation upward to the grandfather's generation? Or do we say, *hai palig dara* — the generations have grown too distant, and therefore no such *gezeira* was enacted?

This is somewhat striking, given that the *gemara* elsewhere does not seem particularly troubled by adding additional generations, particularly in the context of *ein lahem hefsek*. Nevertheless, the *gemara* here treats the question as genuinely open.

### Mishna Omission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע ״מָה הֵן שְׁנִיּוֹת״, וְלָא קָחָשֵׁיב לְהוּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ! תְּנָא וְשַׁיַּיר. מַאי שַׁיֵּיר דְּהַאי שַׁיַּיר? שַׁיַּיר שְׁנִיּוֹת דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** from that *baraita*, which taught: **What are** the **secondary** forbidden **relationships** that were prohibited? **And these** women **were not reckoned among them.** The Gemara replies that this is not conclusive. Perhaps the *tanna* of the *baraita* **taught** some examples **and omitted** other examples of secondary forbidden relations; i.e., perhaps the list is not exhaustive. The Gemara asks: **What** else **did he omit that he omitted this? He omitted** the list of **secondary** forbidden **relationships** that were enumerated by **the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya.**

The *gemara* first attempts to resolve the question from the *mishnah* or *baraita* that enumerated the *shniyot*: since great-aunts were not listed there, perhaps they are not forbidden. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: *tana v'shayer* — the list was incomplete, and one cannot draw inferences from omissions. The question then becomes: *mai shayer d'hai shayer* — what else was left off? And the answer is: *shayer shniyot d'bei Rabbi Chiya* — the additional secondary prohibitions of Rabbi Chiya's school, which we will encounter momentarily. Since we know the list is incomplete, the absence of great-aunts proves nothing.

### Ameimar & Sixteen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר אַכְשַׁר בְּאֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אֲבִי אָבִיו וּבַאֲחוֹת אֲבִי אָבִיו. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב הִלֵּל לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְדִידִי חַזְיָא לִי שְׁנִיּוֹת מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא, וּכְתִיבָן שִׁיתַּסְרֵי לְאִיסּוּרָא. מַאי לָאו: תַּמְנֵי דְּמַתְנִיתָא, וְשֵׁית דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְהָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי — הָא שִׁיתַּסְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar permitted the wife of one's father's father's brother and the sister of one's father's father. Rav Hillel said to Rav Ashi,** challenging Ameimar's statement: **I myself saw** a list of **secondary** forbidden **relationships** written by **Mar, son of Rabbana, and it was written** there that **sixteen** women **were forbidden. What, is it not** that these sixteen must be the **eight** cases listed **in the *baraita*, and six from the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya, and these two** cases of the wife of one's father's father's brother and one's father's father's sister, and **this** totals **sixteen?**

Ameimar, in practice, permitted great-aunts — *achshar b'eishet achi avi aviv u'bachot avi aviv*. However, Rav Hillel then reported to Rav Ashi that he had personally seen a list of secondary *arayot* from the house of Mar brei de-Ravna, and that list recorded precisely sixteen prohibitions as forbidden — *u'chtivan shitser l'issura*. This becomes the basis for an attempt to establish that great-aunts are indeed included in the count.

### Sixteen Count

The counting proceeds as follows. The *baraita* discussed the previous day enumerated eight. Rabbi Chiya's school, as we are about to see, adds six more. That yields fourteen. For the total to reach sixteen, the remaining two must be precisely the two forms of great-aunt: one's grandfather's sister and one's grandfather's brother's wife. This would account for the full number of sixteen, and would mean Ameimar's lenient ruling is incorrect.

### Seventeen vs Sixteen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ: שִׁיבְסְרֵי הָוְיָין, דְּהָא אִיכָּא אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי הָאֵם מִן הָאֵם, דְּפָשְׁטִינַן לְאִיסּוּרָא! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי דְּדָמְיָין לַהֲדָדֵי כַּחֲדָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ, וְהָא שִׁיתַּסְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi defended Ameimar's opinion: **And according to your reasoning,** Rav Hillel, that these cases should be included, **there are seventeen** cases, **as there is** the additional case of **the wife of a mother's maternal** half **brother, whose** status **was resolved** earlier, concluding **that she is forbidden.** If it is as you say, then there should be seventeen cases. The Gemara answers on behalf of Rav Hillel: **This** is **not difficult.** **Those two,** the wife of a father's father's brother and the sister of a father's father, **are similar to each other** and **are counted as a single** case, **and** so **there are sixteen.**

The *gemara* objects: *l'ta'amecha, shivsar havyan* — by your reasoning, the count should reach seventeen, not sixteen. The previous day's discussion had concluded that the wife of one's maternal uncle on the purely maternal side — *eishet achi ha'em min ha'em* — is also forbidden, an addition beyond the original eight. If that addition stands, then even without great-aunts the list exceeds fourteen, and adding two forms of great-aunt would yield seventeen.

The *gemara* resolves this: *hanach tarti d'damyan l'hadadi k'chada chashiv lehu* — the two that resemble one another are counted as one. Rashi explains that the two versions of great-aunt — grandfather's sister and grandfather's brother's wife — are both paternal-side great-aunts and are sufficiently similar to be grouped as a single entry. With that adjustment, the arithmetic works out to sixteen.

### Source Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִכׇּל מָקוֹם לְדִידִי חַזְיָין לַן דִּכְתִיבָן לְאִיסּוּרָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, אִי הֲוָה כְּתִיב לְהֶיתֵּירָא, מִי הֲוֵית סָמְכַתְּ עֲלַיְיהוּ? דְּמָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא מִי חֲתִים עֲלַיְיהוּ? הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי דִּכְתִיב לְאִיסּוּרָא — לָאו מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא חֲתִים עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara restates Rav Hillel's challenge to Ameimar's opinion: **But in any case I myself saw them written as prohibited.** Rav Ashi **said to** Rav Hillel: **And according to your reasoning, if** in the list **it was written that they were permitted, would you have relied on that?** Is the signature of **Mar, son of Rabbana, signed on it?** Although you saw the list, you don't know for certain who wrote it. **Now too, that it is written that they are prohibited,** the same holds true; the signature of **Mar, son of Rabbana, is not signed on it,** and so it cannot be relied upon to reject the opinion of Ameimar.

The *gemara* then deflates the entire line of reasoning. Rav Ashi responds to Rav Hillel: even if one saw such a list written, if it had stated the opposite — that great-aunts are *permitted* — would one have relied upon it? After all, the document was not signed by Mar brei de-Ravna himself; its authority is unknown. *De-Mar brei de-Ravna mi chatim aleiyhu* — Mar brei de-Ravna did not affix his signature to it. Just as one would not rely on an unsigned document to be lenient, one cannot rely on it to be strict. The attempt to establish a *halakha* about great-aunts from this source therefore fails, and the question remains unresolved.

## Rabbi Chiya's List

### Torah Two-Generation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁבִּבְנוֹ וְשֶׁבְּבִתּוֹ וְשֶׁבְּבֶן אִשְׁתּוֹ וְשֶׁבְּבַת אִשְׁתּוֹ — שְׁנִיָּה. רְבִיעִי שֶׁבְּחָמִיו וְשֶׁבַּחֲמוֹתוֹ — שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § One of the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: The third** generation **from one's son and one's daughter,** i.e., one's great-grandchildren, **and** the third generation **from his wife's son and his wife's daughter,** i.e., one's wife's great-grandchildren, are all forbidden as **secondary** forbidden **relationships.** So too, **the fourth** generation **from his father-in-law and from his mother-in-law,** i.e., his wife's great-grandmothers, are prohibited as **secondary** forbidden **relationships.**

The *gemara* now turns to the list of Rabbi Chiya's school, which had been invoked in the preceding discussion. To understand what Rabbi Chiya adds, one must first appreciate the baseline. The *Torah* itself already prohibits relationships spanning two generations: a man may not marry a woman and also her granddaughter, and he may not marry his own granddaughter. Similarly, a man may not marry his wife's grandmother. These two-generation prohibitions are *arayot de'oraita*.

### Great-Granddaughters Down

Rabbi Chiya's innovation begins with the ruling *shlishi she-bivno ve-she-bivito* — the third generation downward through one's son or through one's daughter. The key *chiddush* is this: when the *Torah* prohibits one's granddaughter, it forbids the second generation going straight down through daughters — *bat bito* and *bat beno* are both explicitly biblical. If one continued downward through daughters alone, that would simply be an application of *ein lahem hefsek*. But Rabbi Chiya addresses a different case: where the second generation is a son — a grandson — and then the third generation produces a granddaughter. At the second generation, there is no biblical prohibition, since a grandson's relationship to his grandfather does not generate a restriction. Yet Rabbi Chiya teaches that the great-granddaughter — the third generation, even where the intervening link is a grandson — is nevertheless forbidden as a *shnia*.

Rashi makes this precise: *shlishi she-bivno* refers to *bat ben beno* — one's son's son's daughter; and *shlishi she-bivito* refers to *bat ben bito* — one's daughter's son's daughter. In both cases, the second generation is a son (*ben*), breaking the direct daughter-to-daughter chain, yet the third generation great-granddaughter is still rabbinically prohibited. This accounts for two of Rabbi Chiya's six additions.

### Wife's Great-Granddaught…

The next two entries are parallel, but applied to one's wife. The *Torah* prohibits a man from marrying a woman along with her granddaughter — which means, structurally, one may not marry one's wife's granddaughter, whether through the wife's son or through the wife's daughter. Those are *de'oraita* prohibitions. But again, where the second generation is a grandson — the wife's grandson — the biblical prohibition does not attach at that level. Rabbi Chiya extends the prohibition to the wife's great-granddaughter in such a case: *she-be-ven ishto ve-she-be-bat ishto* — whether the great-granddaughter descends through the wife's son or through the wife's daughter, she is rabbinically forbidden. This yields two more entries, for a running total of four.

### Wife's Great-Grandmother

The final two entries of Rabbi Chiya's list extend the prohibitions upward rather than downward: *revi'i she-be-chamiv ve-she-be-chamoto* — the fourth generation ascending through one's father-in-law or through one's mother-in-law. Here too the *Torah* already forbids a man from marrying a woman's grandmother, meaning one's wife's grandmother is biblically forbidden. Going one generation further upward yields one's wife's great-grandmother. Rabbi Chiya teaches that this too is rabbinically prohibited, whether the great-grandmother descends through the wife's paternal line or her maternal line.

The *chiddush* is most significant when the great-grandmother is reached through a grandfather — that is, where the intervening generation is a grandfather rather than a grandmother, at which level there is no independent biblical prohibition — yet even then, the great-grandmother is a *shnia*. Rashi describes these cases specifically in terms of the mother of the mother of the father-in-law (*em em chamiv*) and the mother of the mother of the mother-in-law (*em em chamoto*), though one might equally apply the reasoning to the paternal grandfather's mother.

These final two entries complete Rabbi Chiya's list of six additions: two forms of one's own great-granddaughters, two forms of one's wife's great-granddaughters, and two forms of one's wife's great-grandmothers.

### Generation Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי שְׁנָא לְמַעְלָה דְּקָחָשֵׁיב לָהּ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא לְמַטָּה דְּלָא קָחָשֵׁיב לָהּ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ? לְמַעְלָה, דְאִיסּוּרָא מִכֹּחַ אִשְׁתּוֹ קָא אָתֵי — חָשֵׁיב לַהּ, לְמַטָּה, דְאִיסּוּרָא לָאו מִכֹּחַ אִשְׁתּוֹ קָאָתֵי — לָא קָחָשֵׁיב לַהּ. וְהָא בֶּן אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבַת אִשְׁתּוֹ, דְּאִיסּוּרָא מִכֹּחַ אִשְׁתּוֹ קָאָתֵי, וְלָא חָשֵׁיב לַהּ! אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת לְמַטָּה דִּידֵיהּ וְלָא חַשְּׁבַהּ, תְּנָא נָמֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת לְמַטָּה דִּידַהּ וְלָא חַשְּׁבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: What is different between** the generations **above,** referring to his wife's great-grandmothers, such **that** the Sage **counts his wife** as one of the generations and refers to them as the fourth generation, **and what is different** with regard to the generations **below,** referring to his great-granddaughters, such **that he did not count his wife** and referred to them as only the third generation? The Gemara answers: When counting **above, the prohibition stems from his wife,** as they are not his blood relatives but his wife's. Therefore, **she is counted.** When counting **below,** however, **the prohibition does not stem from his wife,** as they are his own blood relatives, **and so she was not counted.** The Gemara objects: **But** he includes the granddaughter of **his wife's son and his wife's daughter** in his list of those **forbidden due to his wife, yet he does not count her** and refer to this as the fourth generation. The Gemara answers: **Since he** already **taught three generations below himself,** i.e., his own great-granddaughter, **and** there **he did not count** from his wife because it is his own blood relative, **he taught three generations below** his wife **and did not count her.** Instead, he referred to her great-granddaughter also as three generations below to maintain the uniform style of his words.

Ravina posed a question to Rav Ashi about the terminology: *mai shna lemala de-chashiv lei ishto, mai shna lemata de-lo kachashiv lei ishto* — why when counting upward is one's wife counted as generation one (making her great-grandmother "generation four"), while when counting downward one is not counted as generation zero (making one's great-granddaughter "generation three")?

Rav Ashi explains: *lemala, de-issura mikoch ishto ka'ati chashiv lah* — going upward, the prohibition derives entirely from one's relationship to one's wife; she is the anchor of the relationship, and so she is counted as generation one, her mother as two, her grandmother as three, her great-grandmother as four. *Lemata, de-issura lav mikoch ishto ka'ati, lo kachashiv lah* — going downward, one's own great-granddaughter, the prohibition does not stem from the wife; one counts beginning with the first generation after oneself, so son or daughter is generation one, grandchild generation two, great-grandchild generation three.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty: what of one's wife's great-granddaughters? That prohibition also derives from one's relationship to the wife — *de-issura mikoch ishto ka'ati* — so by the same logic, the wife should be counted as generation one, making the great-granddaughter generation four, not three. The *gemara* answers: *aidei de-tani tlat daragei lemata didei* — since when we counted generations below oneself we called it "three" and did not count ourselves, we likewise do not count the wife when going below her, and so her great-granddaughter is called "three" as well, for consistency of formulation.

### Hefsek Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: שְׁנִיּוֹת דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, יֵשׁ לָהֶן הֶפְסֵק אוֹ אֵין לָהֶם הֶפְסֵק? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב: אַרְבַּע נָשִׁים יֵשׁ לָהֶם הֶפְסֵק, וְתוּ לָא. דִּלְמָא כִּי קָאָמַר רַב — לְהַהִיא מַתְנִיתָא. תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁלִישִׁי וּרְבִיעִי. שְׁלִישִׁי וּרְבִיעִי — אִין, טְפֵי — לָא! דִּלְמָא מִשְּׁלִישִׁי וְאֵילָךְ, מֵרְבִיעִי וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana:** Do those **secondary** forbidden **relationships** taught by one of the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya have a conclusion, or do they not have a conclusion?** Are those specified cases alone forbidden, or are all generations above and below also forbidden? **Come** and **hear** that **which Rav said: Four women have a conclusion.** This implies that it is only these four **and no more.** The Gemara rejects this: **Perhaps when Rav said this he was referring** only **to** the relationships listed in **the *baraita*** and not to all cases of secondary relationships. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** from the *baraita* itself, which states: **The third** generation from his son and his daughter **and the fourth** generation from his father-in-law and mother-in-law. This implies that with regard to **the third and the fourth** generations, **yes,** they are forbidden; but **more** than that, **no.** The Gemara rejects this: **Perhaps** this means **from the third onward and from the fourth onward.** The Gemara does not resolve this issue.

Rav Ashi poses the question to Avimi of Hagronya: *shniyot de-Rabbi Chiya yesh lahen hefsek o ein lahen hefsek* — do Rabbi Chiya's additional prohibitions have a cutoff, or do they continue indefinitely? The *gemara* considers a proof from Rav's statement that there are four women whose *shniyot* prohibitions stop — *arba nashim yesh lahen hefsek* — and since Rabbi Chiya's categories were not among those four, perhaps they continue without limit. The *gemara* deflects: perhaps Rav was only speaking about the original *baraita* of eight, and Rabbi Chiya's list may have its own cutoff.

An attempt is then made from Rabbi Chiya's own language: he specified *shlishi* — the third generation below — and *revi'i* — the fourth generation above. These are specific numbers, suggesting the prohibition stops there. The *gemara* again is unconvinced: *dilma mi-shlishi va-eilakh u-me-revi'i va-eilakh* — perhaps "third" means starting from the third generation onward, not limited to it. The question of *hefsek* for Rabbi Chiya's list is thus left without resolution.

## Converts & Shniyot

### Converts Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: חֲזִי מָר הַאי מֵרַבָּנַן דַּאֲתָא מִמַּעְרְבָא וְאָמַר, בְּעוֹ בְּמַעְרְבָא: גָּזְרוּ שְׁנִיּוֹת בְּגֵרִים, אוֹ לֹא גָּזְרוּ שְׁנִיּוֹת בְּגֵרִים?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: Did the Master see this one of the Sages who came from the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **who said: In the West they asked** whether the Sages **issued a decree** prohibiting **secondary** forbidden **relationships for converts, or** whether **they did not issue a decree** prohibiting **secondary** forbidden **relationships for converts?**

A rabbi who came from the west — from *Eretz Yisrael* — reported to Rava, who conveyed the matter to Rav Nachman: *be'ei be-ma'arva* — they posed the following question in *Eretz Yisrael*: *gazru shniyot be-gerim* — did the Rabbis extend the secondary *arayot* prohibitions to converts? The question arises from the principle of *kegar she-nitgayer ke-katan she-nolad dami* — a convert is legally as a newborn child, severed from prior family relationships. But does this principle interact with the rabbinic secondary prohibitions, and if so, how?

### Eretz Yisrael Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה עֶרְוָה גּוּפַהּ, אִי לָאו ״שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ בָּאִין מִקְּדוּשָּׁה חֲמוּרָה לִקְדוּשָּׁה קַלָּה״, לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן — שְׁנִיּוֹת מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman did not answer whether he had seen this Sage, but **said to him: Now, with regard to** all the **forbidden relationships themselves, were it not** for the fact that if converts would find that as Jews they were permitted to enter into relationships that were forbidden to them as gentiles and **would say** that they went from **a state of more stringent sanctity** as gentiles **to a state of lesser sanctity** as Jews, **then the Sages would not have decreed** to prohibit these relationships. Without the rabbinic decree it would have been permitted for a convert to marry even a close female relative, even his twin sister, who also converted. This is because a convert has the legal status of a newborn, with no family ties. **Is it necessary** to state that the Sages did not extend that decree to include **secondary** forbidden **relationships?** The purpose of the rabbinic prohibitions is to protect the Torah prohibitions, but in the case of converts this particular Torah prohibition does not apply.

The ruling that emerges from *Eretz Yisrael* is that *shniyot* do not apply to converts. To explain why, the *gemara* first addresses the more basic question of biblical *arayot* for converts.

### Holiness Concern

Even at the level of biblical *arayot*, a convert is technically no longer related to his pre-conversion family members. A convert and his sister who converted together could, strictly speaking, marry one another. Yet the Rabbis prohibited this. The *gemara* explains: *ee lav d'lo yomru banu mi-kdusha chamura li-kdusha kala* — were it not for the concern that people would say "we moved from a stricter religion to a more lenient one," the Rabbis would not have enacted the prohibition at all. The prohibition of biblical-level *arayot* for converts was thus a targeted decree, motivated entirely by the concern that converts not perceive Judaism as less holy than their religion of origin, in which such unions were also forbidden.

### Shniyot Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: גֵּרִים, הוֹאִיל וַאֲתוֹ לְיָדָן, נֵימָא בְּהוּ מִלְּתָא: אַחִין מִן הָאֵם — לֹא יָעִידוּ, וְאִם הֵעִידוּ עֵדוּתָן עֵדוּת. אַחִין מִן הָאָב — מְעִידִין לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: Since** the issue of **converts** and their relatives **has come to hand** and is the topic of discussion, **let us say a matter** of *halakha*: **Maternal** half **brothers** who both convert **may not testify** together as a pair of witnesses before the court, **but if they did testify, their testimony is valid.** Although blood relatives are invalid as witnesses, converts are considered as though they have no relations. **Paternal** half **brothers** who both convert **may testify** together ***ab initio*.

Given this reasoning, the *gemara* concludes that *shniyot* present no problem: *shniyot miba'ya* — is there even a question? A non-Jew is not bound by the rabbinic *shniyot*. If a convert's great-great-grandmother were to convert along with him, she was never forbidden to him even as a non-Jew. There is therefore no appearance of leniency — no one will say Judaism permits something that his prior religion forbade. Accordingly, *shniyot* are entirely permitted to converts. The concern of *kedusha kala mi-kedusha chamura* does not arise, and the technical principle of *ke-katan she-nolad dami* operates without restriction in this domain.

## Convert Testimony

### Maternal Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אַחִין מִן הָאֵם נָמֵי מְעִידִין לְכַתְּחִלָּה. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵעֲרָיוֹת? עֶרְוָה — לַכֹּל מְסוּרָה, עֵדוּת — לְבֵית דִּין מְסוּרָה, וְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר כְּקָטָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said: Even maternal** half **brothers may also testify** together ***ab initio*.** In **what** way **is** this case **different from forbidden relations,** where a convert may not marry his maternal sister due to rabbinic decree? The *halakhot* of **forbidden relations are handed over to all,** and every individual chooses his own wife. Therefore, the Sages issued a decree to prevent confusion between the status of a convert and that of a born Jew. However, **testimony is handed over to the court,** and the court knows to distinguish between the status of a born Jew and that of a convert. **And** the legal status of **a convert who** just **converted** is **like** that of **a child** just **born,** and all previous family ties become irrelevant.

Since the discussion has turned to converts, Rav Nachman adds a related ruling: *achi min ha'em lo ya'idu* — brothers on the maternal side who have both converted may not testify together *lechatchila*. However, *v'im he'idu, edutan edut* — if they did testify together *bedi'avad*, their testimony is valid, since *ke-katan she-nolad dami* renders them legally unrelated.

### Paternal Brothers

By contrast, *achi min ha'av me'idin lechatchila* — paternal brothers who have both converted may testify together from the outset, without any *lechatchila* restriction.

### Bio Relationship

This distinction is somewhat counterintuitive. Throughout the laws of *arayot*, paternal relationships carry greater legal weight than maternal ones. Why, then, is the maternal bond treated more stringently here for purposes of testimony? The answer lies not in legal weight but in biological visibility. When two people share the same mother, the biological relationship is plain and apparent to all — there is a clear, enduring physical bond. Even after conversion, it is obvious to observers that these two individuals are biological brothers. The concern is therefore that it will appear as though related parties are testifying together, even though *halakhically* they are not.

Paternal brotherhood, by contrast, rests not merely on biology but substantially on the social construct of family membership — being recognized as part of the same father's household. That social construct dissolves upon conversion in a more complete and perceptible way. The *halakhic* severance is therefore more evident to onlookers, and there is less concern that allowing paternal brothers to testify will create a false appearance of impropriety. Rashi notes in this context that among non-Jews, the maternal bond carried particular significance even prior to conversion, which further explains why the *lechatchila* restriction attaches specifically to maternal brothers.

### Court Control

The *gemara* pushes further: *afilu hachi min em nami me'idim lechatchila* — even maternal brothers who have converted may testify together from the outset. This is because testimony is a court-controlled matter, not a private one.

### Arayot Concerns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵעֲרָיוֹת? עֶרְוָה — לַכֹּל מְסוּרָה, עֵדוּת — לְבֵית דִּין מְסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In **what** way **is** this case **different from forbidden relations,** where a convert may not marry his maternal sister due to rabbinic decree? The *halakhot* of **forbidden relations are handed over to all,** and every individual chooses his own wife. **However, testimony is handed over to the court.**

The *gemara* draws a formal distinction: *erva lechol mesura* — marital decisions are made by individuals and are not subject to court supervision. If a convert were permitted to marry his sister, other people might follow suit and believe they could do the same. The prohibition must therefore be maintained as an absolute rule, regardless of whether those particular individuals know they are permitted. Testimony, by contrast, is *edut levei dina mesura* — entirely under the jurisdiction of the *beit din*. The court knows that *kegar she-nitgayer ke-katan she-nolad dami* and that converts are not *halakhically* related. There is therefore no risk of error spreading, and even maternal brothers may testify together *lechatchila*.

This represents a shift from the earlier concern — where the issue was the convert's perception of relative religious stringency — to a new concern about the potential for the public to draw incorrect inferences. The two rationales operate in different domains, which is why the *gemara*'s application differs between *arayot* and testimony.

## Brother & Son

### Any Brother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אָח מִכׇּל מָקוֹם — זוֹקֵק אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו לְיִבּוּם, וְאָחִיו הוּא לְכׇל דָּבָר, חוּץ מִמִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אָח מִן הַשִּׁפְחָה וּמִן הַגּוֹיָה. מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בֵּן מִכׇּל מָקוֹם — פּוֹטֵר אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מִן הַיִּבּוּם, וְחַיָּיב עַל מַכָּתוֹ וְעַל קִלְלָתוֹ, וּבְנוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר, חוּץ מִמִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בֵּן מִן הַשִּׁפְחָה וּמִן הַגּוֹיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **anyone who has a brother of any kind,** that brother **creates a levirate bond** causing his ***yevama* to** be required to **perform levirate marriage** if the first brother dies childless. **And he is his brother in all respects, except for one who has a brother** born **from** a Canaanite **maidservant or from a gentile woman,** as these do not have the legal status of brothers. Similarly, in the case of **anyone who has a child of any kind,** that child **exempts his father's wife from levirate marriage,** since his father did not die childless. **And** that child **is liable** to receive capital punishment **if he strikes** his father **or curses him. And he is his child in all respects, except for whoever has a child** born **from** a Canaanite **maidservant or from a gentile woman,** as these do not have the halakhic status of children.

The *mishnah* states: *mi she-yesh lo ach mikol makom, zokek et eshet achiv l'yibum* — whoever has any type of brother, that brother creates a *yibum* obligation upon the widow in the event of death. The *mishnah* adds that the brother is considered a full brother *l'chol davar* — for all purposes — with one exception: a half-brother born of a non-Jewish woman or a slave-woman is not considered a legal brother and does not create the *yibum* obligation.

### Mamzer Brother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר. פְּשִׁיטָא, אָחִיו הוּא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, לֵילַף אַחְוָה אַחְוָה מִבְּנֵי יַעֲקֹב: מָה לְהַלָּן — כְּשֵׁרִין וְלֹא פְּסוּלִין, אַף כָּאן — כְּשֵׁרִין וְלֹא פְּסוּלִין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: With regard to the statement that a brother **of any kind** causes his *yevama* to be required to perform levirate marriage, **what** additional case does this come **to add? Rav Yehuda said: This adds** the case of **a *mamzer*,** who, notwithstanding his status, is considered a brother. The Gemara wonders: But **isn't** that **obvious? He is his brother.** The Gemara explains: This is necessary **lest you say: Let us derive** a verbal analogy between the word **"brother"** stated in the verse with regard to levirate marriage and **"brother"** stated with regard to **the children of Jacob. Just as there,** Jacob's children **are** of **unflawed** lineage **and not** of **flawed** lineage and are not *mamzerim*, **so too here,** one might think that only brothers of **unflawed** lineage **and not** brothers of **flawed** lineage, i.e., *mamzerim*, obligate the *yevama* in levirate marriage. Therefore, **this teaches us** that a *mamzer* is considered a brother for the purposes of levirate marriage.

The *gemara* asks: *mikol makom la'atuyei mai* — what does the phrase "any type" come to include? Rav Yehuda answers: *la'atuyei mamzer* — it includes even a brother who is a *mamzer*. That is, if one's father had a child through a forbidden union, producing a *mamzer*, that *mamzer* half-brother still creates a *yibum* obligation upon his brother's widow.

One might have thought to exclude the *mamzer* by applying a *gezeira shava* from the brothers of Yaakov — *shneim asar achim anachnu* — reasoning that just as those brothers were all legally fit (*kesharim*), the term "brother" in the context of *yibum* should likewise be restricted to fit (*kasher*) individuals, excluding a *mamzer*. The *gemara* rejects this: since a *mamzer* son exempts his father's wife from *yibum* — as we are about to see — it follows that *mamzer* status has full relevance within the laws of *yibum*, and therefore a *mamzer* brother likewise creates the *yibum* obligation.

### Mamzer Full Brother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? כֵּיוָן דִּלְעִנְיַן יִבּוּם מִיפְטַר נִפְטָר, מִיזְקָק נָמֵי זָקֵיק. וְאָחִיו הוּא לְכׇל דָּבָר. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ וְלִיטָּמֵא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say that** is **indeed** the *halakha*. Perhaps a brother who is a *mamzer* does not obligate his *yevama* in levirate marriage. The Gemara answers: **Since with regard to levirate marriage,** if the husband had a child who was a *mamzer* he would **exempt** the wife from levirate marriage, the husband's brother **also causes a levirate bond** with his *yevama* even if he is a *mamzer*. § The mishna stated that **he is his brother in all respects.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** was this said? The Gemara answers: **To inherit** from **him, and to become ritually impure for him** if he is a priest, as a brother is one of the relations for whom a priest is permitted to become ritually impure by attending his funeral.

The *mishnah* further states that the *mamzer* brother is considered a full brother *l'chol davar* — for all purposes. The *gemara* identifies two concrete applications: *l'yarsho* — a *mamzer* brother inherits from his brother just as any other brother would — and *l'tamei lo* — a *kohen* whose brother is a *mamzer* is still obligated to render himself *tamei* for the burial of that brother.

The second application requires elaboration. One might have thought to exclude it based on the verse *ki im l'she'ero ha-karov eilav* — a *kohen* renders himself *tamei* only for close flesh — and the teaching that *she'ero* refers to one's wife. Since a *kohen* does not render himself *tamei* for a wife who is a *grusha*, one might analogize: just as the *kohen* is not *metamei* for a wife of disqualified status, so too he should not be *metamei* for a brother of disqualified status such as a *mamzer*.

### Kashering Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, אָחִיו הוּא! סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי אִם לִשְׁאֵרוֹ הַקָּרוֹב אֵלָיו״, וְאָמַר מָר: ״שְׁאֵרוֹ״ — זוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ, וּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יִטַּמָּא בַּעַל בְּעַמָּיו לְהֵחַלּוֹ״, יֵשׁ בַּעַל שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא וְיֵשׁ בַּעַל שֶׁאֵין מִטַּמֵּא, הָא כֵּיצַד? מִטַּמֵּא הוּא לְאִשְׁתּוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא לְאִשְׁתּוֹ פְּסוּלָה, הָכִי נָמֵי: מִטַּמֵּא הוּא לְאָח כָּשֵׁר, וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא לְאָח פְּסוּל — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? הָתָם לְאַפּוֹקֵי קָיְימָא, הָכָא אָחִיו הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious,** as **he is his brother?** The Gemara answers: It might **enter your mind to say** that a priest does not become impure for his brother of flawed lineage, **since it is written** with regard to priests: "None shall defile himself for the dead among his people **except for his kin, that is near unto him"** (Leviticus 21:1–2), **and the Master said "his kin"; this is his wife,** and a priest may defile himself for his wife. **But it is written: "He shall not defile himself, being a husband among his people, to profane himself"** (Leviticus 21:4), implying that he may not defile himself for his wife. This apparent contradiction is resolved as follows: **There is a husband who does become impure** for his wife, **and there is a husband who does not become impure. How so? He becomes impure for his wife if she was fit** and was permitted to a priest, **but he does not become impure for his wife** if she was **unfit** to marry a priest. **So too,** one might have thought that a priest **becomes impure for a brother who is** of **unflawed** lineage and not a *mamzer*, **but he does not become impure for a brother** of **flawed** lineage. Therefore, **this comes to teach us** that he does become impure for a brother who is a *mamzer*. The Gemara asks: **Say this also,** that by the same reasoning by which a priest may not become impure for a wife who was unfit for him, he may not become impure for an unfit brother either. Just as he does not become impure for an unfit wife, he should not become impure for an unfit brother? The Gemara answers: The two cases are different, as **there,** with regard to the unfit wife, **she stands to be dismissed,** since according to Torah law unfit women must be divorced. For this reason he does not become impure for her. **Here, he is his brother.**

The *gemara* rejects this analogy: *hatam l'afukei kaima* — in the case of the *grusha*, the reason the *kohen* is not *metamei* for her is not that she is a person of diminished status, but that the relationship itself is one he should be dissolving. She should not be his wife at all; the marital bond is therefore weakened and does not support the obligation of *tumah*. The *mamzer* brother, however, is not a relationship that one should be ending or severing. He is simply always one's brother. The quality of the relationship — as distinct from the personal status of the relative — determines the *tumah* obligation. Since the fraternal relationship is intact and permanent, the *kohen* is fully obligated to render himself *tamei* for his *mamzer* brother.

## Son Exemption

### Any Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בֵּן מִכׇּל מָקוֹם פּוֹטֵר אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו מִן הַיִּבּוּם, וְחַיָּיב עַל מַכָּתוֹ וְעַל קִלְלָתוֹ, וּבְנוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר, חוּץ מִמִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בֵּן מִן הַשִּׁפְחָה וּמִן הַגּוֹיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: With regard to **anyone who has a son of any kind,** that son **exempts** his father's wife from any levirate bond. **And** that child **is liable** to receive capital punishment **if he strikes** his father **or curses him. And he is his child in all respects, except for whoever has a child** born **from** a Canaanite **maidservant or from a gentile woman,** as these do not have the halakhic status of children.

The *mishnah* states: *mi she-yesh lo ben mikol makom poter eshet aviv min ha-yibum* — whoever has any type of son, even a *mamzer*, that son exempts his father's wife from *yibum*. The biblical law of *yibum* applies only when a man dies *without a son*; if any legally recognized son exists, the *yibum* obligation does not arise. The *mishnah* specifies that the exception is again a son born of a non-Jewish woman or slave-woman.

### Mamzer Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם פּוֹטֵר וְכוּ׳. ״מִכׇּל מָקוֹם״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבֵן אֵין לוֹ״ — עַיֵּין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: With regard to **anyone who has a son of any kind,** that son **exempts** his father's wife from any levirate bond. The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the phrase: **Of any kind? Rav Yehuda said: It adds** a child who is **a *mamzer*. What is the reason** that a *mamzer* exempts a woman from any levirate bond? **As the verse states** with regard to levirate marriage: **"And he has no [*ain lo*] child"** (Deuteronomy 25:5). The phrase *ain lo* can be understood as *ayyen alav*, meaning **investigate him.** That is to say, investigate whether he is indeed childless, because any child, even a *mamzer*, exempts the widow from a levirate bond.

Again the *gemara* asks what "any type" comes to include, and again Rav Yehuda answers: *la'atuyei mamzer*. The derivation comes from the phrase *u-ven ein lo* — "and he has no son" — which the *gemara* reads as *ayen alav*, inviting a broader investigation into what constitutes a son for this purpose. By this expansive reading, any person who falls within the category of son exempts the father's wife from *yibum*.

### Daughters & Grandchildren

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיב עַל מַכָּתוֹ. אַמַּאי? קְרִי כָּאן: ״וְנָשִׂיא בְעַמְּךָ לֹא תָאוֹר״, בְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עַמְּךָ! כִּדְאָמַר רַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: בְּעוֹשֶׂה תְּשׁוּבָה, הָכָא נָמֵי כְּשֶׁעָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** It states in the mishna that a child who is a *mamzer* **is liable** to receive punishment **if he strikes** his father or curses him. The Gemara asks: **Why** should he be liable? **Read here** the verse: **"You shall not curse a ruler of your people"** (Exodus 22:27), which is interpreted to imply that the prohibition against cursing applies only **to one who acts according to the deeds of your people.** However, this father must have engaged in sexual relations with a woman who was forbidden to him in order to have a child who is a *mamzer*. Therefore, he did not act according to the deeds of the people. In that case, why should this son be liable to receive punishment for hitting him or cursing him? The Gemara answers: **As Rav Pineḥas said in the name of Rav Pappa** with regard to a different matter: This is referring to **one who performs repentance. Here too,** it is referring to **one who performed repentance** after the *mamzer* was born. He is thereafter considered to be living in accordance with *halakha*, and so his *mamzer* son is liable to receive punishment for cursing him.

The same *drashah* of *ayen alav* yields further expansions. Not only a son but even a daughter exempts the father's wife from *yibum*. Moreover, a grandson or granddaughter — indeed any descendant — also exempts. The *Torah*'s language of "son" is thus read broadly to encompass the full range of descendants, such that so long as any descendant of the deceased exists, the *yibum* obligation is displaced.

### Honor & Teshuva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי בַּר תְּשׁוּבָה הוּא? וְהָתְנַן: שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: אֵיזֶהוּ ״מְעֻוָּות לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקוֹן״ — זֶה הַבָּא עַל הָעֶרְוָה וְהוֹלִיד מִמֶּנָּה מַמְזֵר! הַשְׁתָּה מִיהָא עוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עַמְּךָ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Is he in fact able to repent** after fathering a *mamzer*? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna that **Shimon ben Menasya says: Which is: "That which is crooked cannot be made straight"** (Ecclesiastes 1:15)? **This** is referring to **one who engaged in intercourse with a relative** who is **forbidden** to him **and fathered a *mamzer* with her.** This implies that he has no possibility of achieving total repentance. The Gemara responds: **At least now,** after repenting, **he** is considered as one who **acts according to the deeds of your people.** Although he cannot totally rectify his transgression, his child is liable to receive punishment for cursing or hitting him.

The *mishnah* further states that if the *mamzer* son were to strike or curse his father, he would be liable (*chayav mita* for striking, and the equivalent prohibition for cursing). The *gemara* questions this. The verse *v'nasi be'amcha lo ta'or* is interpreted to mean that the prohibition against cursing — and by extension, striking — applies only to one who acts as part of the community: *be'oseh ma'aseh amcha*. A sinner who has placed himself outside the community would not be protected by this prohibition. Since the *mamzer*'s father committed a grave transgression to produce the *mamzer*, perhaps the father should not qualify as *oseh ma'aseh amcha* and should therefore not be protected.

The *gemara* cites Rav Pinchas in the name of Rav Pappa: *be'oseh teshuvah* — the *mishnah* speaks of a father who has done *teshuvah*. Once the father has repented, he is again within the community, and the son's obligation of honor is restored.

### Oseh Ma'aseh Amcha

This represents a shift from the earlier concern — where the issue was the convert's perception of relative religious stringency — to a new concern about the potential for the public to draw incorrect inferences. The two rationales operate in different domains, which is why the *gemara*'s application differs between *arayot* and testimony.

## Double Transgression

### Two Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ, וְהִיא בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹתוֹ וּמִשּׁוּם בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹתוֹ בִּלְבַד, וְלֹא מִפְּנֵי בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: He who** unwittingly **engages in sexual relations with his sister, who is the daughter of his father's** lawful **wife, is liable** and must bring two sin-offerings. He must bring one sin-offering **because** she is **his sister** and another **because** she is **the daughter of his father's wife,** which is mentioned in a separate verse in the Torah. **Rabbi Yosei ben Yehuda says: He is liable only because** she is **his sister alone, and not because** of the prohibition against engaging in sexual relations with **the daughter of his father's wife.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita*: *ha-ba al achoto ve-hi bat eshet aviv* — one who engages in relations with his sister who is also his father's wife's daughter transgresses a double prohibition, *chayav mishum achoto u-mishum bat eshet aviv* — liable for both "his sister" and "his father's wife's daughter."

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? אָמְרִי, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״עֶרְוַת אֲחוֹתְךָ בַת אָבִיךָ אוֹ בַת אִמֶּךָ״, ״עֶרְוַת בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ מוֹלֶדֶת אָבִיךָ אֲחוֹתְךָ הִיא״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹתוֹ וּמִשּׁוּם בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of** the **Rabbis,** the first *tanna* of the *baraita*? **They said: Since it is written** in the Torah: **"The nakedness of your sister, the daughter of your father, or the daughter of your mother**…you shall not uncover" (Leviticus 18:9), then **why do I** need the verse: **"The nakedness of your father's wife's daughter, begotten of your father, she is your sister**…you shall not uncover" (Leviticus 18:11)? **Conclude from this** that the additional verse serves **to make him liable** separately **due to** the fact that she is **his sister and due to** the fact that she is **his father's wife's daughter.**

The *tanna kamma* holds that in such a case, two distinct prohibitions are violated. Each prohibition — "sister" and "father's wife's daughter" — is an independent biblical *issur*, and when a single act violates both simultaneously, the transgressions are counted separately. This would mean, for instance, that if the act were done *be-shogeg*, two separate *korbanos* would be required, or if warnings were given, two sets of lashes would be administered.

The case requires clarification. A sister who is not the daughter of one's father's wife is possible: she could be the daughter of a woman one's father had a child with but never married. Conversely, a father's wife's daughter who is not one's sister would simply be a stepsister, and stepsister alone — that is, one's father's wife's daughter who is not also one's sister — is not biblically prohibited. The prohibition of "father's wife's daughter" only attaches when she is also one's sister. The question, then, is whether the fact that the father was married to the mother creates a new and additional prohibition on top of the existing prohibition of "sister."

### Rabbi Yose Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲחוֹתְךָ הִיא״. מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹתוֹ אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** says: **The verse states: "She is your sister,"** which comes to limit and emphasize that **you hold him liable due to** the fact that she is **his sister but you do not hold him liable** a second time **due to** the fact that she is also **his father's wife's daughter.**

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda disagrees. He holds that there is only one prohibition: *mishum achoto ata mechayvo ve'ei ata mechayvo mishum bat eshet aviv* — the liability is only for "his sister," and the fact that the father was married to the mother does not create any additional *issur*. The father's marriage to the mother does not transform the sister into a more prohibited relative; she is prohibited only as a sister, full stop.

### Verse Redundancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״אֲחוֹתְךָ הִיא״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְחַיְּיבוֹ עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ בַּת אָבִיו וּבַת אִמּוֹ, לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what do the Rabbis do with this** verse: **"She is your sister"? They require it** in order to hold him liable to receive punishment **for** having relations with **his sister** who is both **the daughter of his father and the daughter of his mother, to say that** the Sages **do not** derive an additional Torah **prohibition by way of a logical derivation,** i.e., an *a fortiori* inference. In other words, although it would seem that since he is liable to receive punishment for having relations with his father's daughter and also for his mother's daughter, this must be all the more true in a case where she is the daughter of both his father and his mother, yet nevertheless, there is no new prohibition here. Therefore, the matter was taught explicitly in the Torah, to teach that the Sages may not derive additional prohibitions by way of *a fortiori* inferences.

The *tanna kamma*'s reasoning proceeds as follows. The *Torah* writes in one verse: *ervat achotecha bat avicha o bat imecha* — the prohibition of one's sister, whether paternal or maternal. In a separate verse, the *Torah* writes: *bat eshet avicha moledes avicha, achotecha hi* — your father's wife's daughter, born of your father, she is your sister. If the sister prohibition already covers all sisters whether the father was married to the mother or not, why did the *Torah* need to write a separate verse about the father's wife's daughter? The redundancy teaches that in the case where the father *is* married to the mother, a second and distinct prohibition applies — the prohibition of "father's wife's daughter" — in addition to the prohibition of "sister." Thus: *shema mina, le-chayvo mishum achoto u-mishum bat eshet aviv*.

### Achosecha Hi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: ״אֲחוֹתְךָ״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וְחוֹמֶר, טָרַח וְכָתַב לַהּ קְרָא — כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how this principle is derived from the text. **As, if you would say: Why do I** need the term **"your sister" that the Merciful One writes** in the Torah, since it is possible to derive this by *a fortiori* inference and it therefore appears redundant? The answer is that the Torah wrote this apparently redundant term to teach the principle that with regard to **a matter that** could **be derived by** means of an ***a fortiori* inference, the verse unnecessarily wrote it** explicitly. **The Merciful One writes** the term **"she is"** in order to teach that just as in this case the Sages may not derive additional prohibitions by way of an *a fortiori* inference, so too, in all other cases they may not derive additional prohibitions that are not stated in the text.

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda reads the second verse differently. The verse states *achotecha hi* — she is your sister. He understands this phrase as restrictive: it tells you that in this case, the liability is as for *your sister*, and no more. The father's marriage to the mother adds nothing to the prohibition; it remains solely the prohibition of sister.

### Full Sister Derivation

The *tanna kamma* then accounts for the phrase *achotecha hi* differently: it is needed to teach that a full sister — one who shares both the same father and same mother — is also prohibited. One might have thought that since the *Torah* specifies the paternal sister in one verse and the maternal sister in another, a sister who is both — a full sister — might somehow fall between the categories and require separate derivation. The phrase *achotecha hi* serves to establish that a full sister is also prohibited, a fact one could not derive by *kal va-chomer* alone, and therefore requires explicit scriptural statement.

### Placement Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֲחוֹתְךָ״, ״הִיא״ לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם ״אֲחוֹתְךָ״ אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו״. וְרַבָּנַן: אַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיב ״אֲחוֹתְךָ״, אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״הִיא״, שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר בְּעָלְמָא מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין,
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** says: **If so,** that the matter was written for this purpose, **then the Merciful One should have written only: Your sister. Why do I** need the phrase **"she is"?** This limits the *halakha* and teaches that for unintentional intercourse with his sister who is also his father's wife's daughter **you hold him liable because** she is **his sister; you do not** additionally **hold him liable because** she is **his father's wife's daughter.** **And** how do **the Rabbis** interpret the additional phrase "she is"? **Although "your sister" is written, it was necessary to write "she is" so that you not say** that **generally** the Sages may derive additional **prohibitions by way of a logical derivation,** i.e., an *a fortiori* inference.

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda responds: if the purpose of *achotecha hi* were simply to establish the prohibition of a full sister, why would the *Torah* place it in the verse dealing specifically with one's father's wife's daughter? If the point were only about full sisters, the phrase should appear in the verse about sisters generally — the verse that begins *ervat achotecha bat avicha o bat imecha*. The fact that *achotecha hi* appears specifically in the verse about the father's wife's daughter indicates that its function is restrictive: it is telling you that even in that case — even where the father is married to the mother — the prohibition remains only that of "sister," and no new prohibition is generated by the marital relationship.

The *tanna kamma* responds in turn: *af al gav de-chtiv achotecha, itzterich le-michtav hi* — even granting that the word *achotecha* on its own could teach the prohibition of a full sister, the additional word *hi* was still necessary. *Shelo tomar be'alma mazhir min hadin* — one might have argued in principle that since the prohibition of full sister could have been derived by *kal va-chomer* from the partial-sister prohibitions, no explicit verse was needed, and that the verse could instead function restrictively. The *Torah* therefore writes *hi* to make clear that the verse does require an explicit statement — and is not merely restrictive — leaving it available to teach the double prohibition.

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda closes: *im kein, lichtov Rachmana achotecha hi be'idach kra* — if all the *Torah* intended with *achotecha hi* was to establish the prohibition of a full sister, it should have written that phrase in the other verse, the general verse about sisters. The fact that it placed *achotecha hi* specifically in the verse about the father's wife's daughter signals that its message is directed at that very case — and the message is that in that case, the liability is *only* for sister, not for any additional prohibition. The debate is left standing, with both positions textually anchored, and the *gemara* will continue from this point in the following session.
