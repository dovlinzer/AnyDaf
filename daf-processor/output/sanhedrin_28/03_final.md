# Sanhedrin 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Family Relations Overview

### Korva & Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ הֵן הַקְּרוֹבִין: אָחִיו, וַאֲחִי אָבִיו, וַאֲחִי אִמּוֹ, וּבַעַל אֲחוֹתוֹ, וּבַעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, וּבַעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, וּבַעַל אִמּוֹ, וְחָמִיו, וְגִיסוֹ. הֵן וּבְנֵיהֶן וְחַתְנֵיהֶן, וְחוֹרְגוֹ לְבַדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are** the ones disqualified from bearing witness or from serving as judges due to their status as **relatives** of one of the litigants or of each other: One's **brother, and his paternal uncle, and his maternal uncle, and his sister's husband, and the husband of his paternal aunt, and the husband of his maternal aunt, and his mother's husband, and his father-in-law, and his brother-in-law,** i.e., the husband of his wife's sister. **They** themselves, all of these people, **and** also **their sons, and their sons-in-law** are considered relatives. **And his stepson alone** is disqualified from bearing witness about him, but not his stepson's sons or sons-in-law.

Today's daf is 28, and it is a challenging one. It is largely an explication of the *mishnah* that concluded yesterday on 27b, which listed those who are *pasul* — disqualified — as witnesses because of *korva*, familial relationship. The *mishnah*'s prime cases are brother and uncle, with "uncle" encompassing several distinct relationships: a father's brother, a father's sister's husband, a mother's brother, and a mother's sister's husband. All of these forms of uncle are drawn together based on the principle of *ishto kigufo* — that a married couple is treated as a single legal unit, so that a relationship through a spouse is treated as though it were a direct blood relationship.

### Genealogical Chart

To map this out in halachic terminology: if Reuven and Shimon are brothers, they stand in a relationship of *rishon berishon* — first-to-first — belonging to the same generation. Reuven's son Chanoch and Shimon's son Nemuel are in a relationship of *sheni besheni* — second-to-second — that is, first cousins. The *mishnah* disqualifies not only the uncles but also their sons, making first cousins *pasul*, and even their sons-in-law. So if Shimon has a daughter Gittel who marries Mordechai, that Mordechai — a cousin by marriage — is likewise disqualified. This extension to in-laws is again grounded in *ishto kigufo*.

### Ishto Kigufo

The principle of *ishto kigufo* operates such that when two spouses are treated as one legal unit, relationships that pass through a wife are counted as though they were direct. So Shimon and Ezra — who are related only because Shimon's wife Leah is the sister of Ezra's wife Rachel — are counted as brothers for the purposes of testimony, even though the two women serve, as it were, as the connecting bridge. The wives do not add additional degrees of removal; because husband and wife collapse into one unit, the connection passes through as though they were directly related.

It is worth noting, as a parenthetical, that the question of whether one applies *ishto kigufo* twice — that is, whether two separate links through marriage compound — does arise in *halacha*. Where the underlying relationship is at the level of brothers, the double application is accepted. Where the relationship is more distant, such as a cousin of a cousin's spouse, one might not extend the principle twice.

### Sheni Besheni

The *mishnah*'s reach, then, is *sheni besheni* — first cousins — including in-laws at that level. This is the basic rule: disqualification extends to first cousins and to those who marry into that degree of relationship. The *mishnah* also mentions *giso* — a brother-in-law — and this category includes even a wife's sister's husband. The two wives in that case function as the connective tissue, and because each husband-wife pair is one unit, Shimon and Ezra become, in legal terms, like brothers.

The *mishnah* also includes *chorgo* — a stepson — but with an important limitation: the disqualification applies to the stepson himself, *chorgo levado*, and does not extend to the stepson's children or son-in-law. That exception will be addressed later.

### Rabbi Yose vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: זוֹ מִשְׁנַת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲבָל מִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה – דּוֹדוֹ וּבֶן דּוֹדוֹ וְכׇל הָרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ, וְכׇל הַקָּרוֹב לוֹ בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: This** aforementioned *halakha* **is Rabbi Akiva's** version of the **mishna. But the initial** version of the **mishna** reads as follows: **His uncle and the son of his uncle, and anyone who is fit to inherit** from **him.** Only paternal relatives, who are fit to inherit from him, are disqualified; maternal relatives, who do not inherit from him, are not disqualified from bearing witness about him or from adjudicating his case. **And** the *halakha* disqualifying a relative from bearing witness or serving as a judge is referring to **anyone who is related to him at the time** of the trial.

A parenthetical but important dispute concerns the theoretical scope of the disqualification. Rabbi Yose notes that the *mishnah*'s teaching, with its inclusion of relationships passing through women, reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva. Because Rabbi Akiva includes, for instance, a mother's brother or a father's sister's husband, the disqualification operates across lines that do not follow the patrilineal chain of inheritance. Rabbi Yose, however, reports an earlier teaching that limits disqualification to those from whom one is *ra'ui liyerusha* — eligible to inherit. The reasoning is that if you testify on behalf of someone and they prevail in a lawsuit worth a great deal, you may ultimately stand to inherit from them, creating a concrete incentive to lie.

This earlier teaching agreed that the outer boundary is uncles and first cousins, but excluded relationships that pass only through women where there is no line of inheritance. One might ask whether "eligible to inherit" means actively standing to inherit right now. The answer is no — it does not require that one would actually inherit at this moment, only that one stands somewhere in the line of potential inheritance. But equally, it is not extended to every theoretically conceivable heir regardless of remoteness; that would be absurd, as in stories of someone being the hundred-and-tenth claimant to a throne and going about eliminating everyone ahead of them.

## Scriptural Derivation

### Fathers & Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״. מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אִם לְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת בַּעֲוֹן בָּנִים וּבָנִים בַּעֲוֹן אָבוֹת, הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ בְּחֶטְאוֹ יוּמָתוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where is this matter,** that relatives are disqualified from bearing witness, derived? The Gemara answers: It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"The fathers shall not be put to death for the children,** neither shall the children be put to death for the fathers; every man shall be put to death for his own sin" (Deuteronomy 24:16). **Why** must **the verse state** this first clause? **If** it is **to teach that the fathers shall not be put to death for the sin of the children, nor** shall **the children** be put to death **for the sin of the fathers,** this is unnecessary, as **it is in any event stated: "Every man shall be put to death for his own sin."**

Having established the *mishnah*'s framework, the *gemara* asks: *mena hani mili* — from where is all of this derived? It cites a *tannaitic* source: *lo yumtu avot al banim* — fathers shall not die on account of sons, and sons shall not die on account of fathers. What is this verse teaching us? Surely we already know, from the continuation of the verse, *ish bechet'o yumatu* — each person dies for their own sin — that we are not punished for the sins of our relatives.

### Punishment vs. Edut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״ – בְּעֵדוּת בָּנִים, ״וּבָנִים לֹא יוּמְתוּ עַל אָבוֹת״ – בְּעֵדוּת אָבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the statement **"The fathers shall not be put to death for the children"** should be interpreted to mean that they shall not be put to death **by the testimony of the children,** and the statement **"Neither shall the children be put to death for the fathers"** should be interpreted to mean that they shall not be put to death **by the testimony of the fathers.**

The *tanna* therefore reads the verse differently: it is not about capital punishment for another's sin, but about the disqualification of testimony. *Lo yumtu avot al banim be'edut banim* — fathers shall not die on account of their sons' testimony, and sons shall not die on account of their fathers' testimony.

### Theology of Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָנִים בַּעֲוֹן אָבוֹת לָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״פֹּקֵד עֲוֹן אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים״! הָתָם, כְּשֶׁאוֹחֲזִין מַעֲשֵׂה אֲבוֹתֵיהֶן בִּידֵיהֶן. כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְאַף בַּעֲוֹנֹת אֲבֹתָם אִתָּם יִמָּקּוּ״ – כְּשֶׁאוֹחֲזִין מַעֲשֵׂה אֲבוֹתֵיהֶם בִּידֵיהֶם. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כְּשֶׁאוֹחֲזִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁאֵין אוֹחֲזִין? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אִישׁ בְּחֶטְאוֹ יוּמָתוּ״, הֲרֵי כְּשֶׁאוֹחֲזִין מַעֲשֵׂה אֲבוֹתֵיהֶן בִּידֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are children not** put to death **for the sin of the fathers? But isn't it written: "Visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children,** and upon the children's children, unto the third and unto the fourth generation" (Exodus 34:7)? The Gemara answers: **There,** the verse is referring to a situation **where** the children **adopt the actions of their ancestors as their own.** If they do not behave like their ancestors they are not punished for their ancestors' sins. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"And also in the iniquities of their fathers shall they pine away with them"** (Leviticus 26:39), is referring to a case **where they adopt the actions of their ancestors as their own.** Do **you say** that it is referring specifically to a case **where they adopt** the actions of their ancestors, **or** perhaps it applies even **where they do not adopt** their ancestors' actions? **When** the verse **states: "Every man shall be put to death for his own sin,"** the Torah explicates that one is not put to death if he did not sin. Therefore, the verse in Leviticus is clearly referring to a case **where they adopt the actions of their ancestors as their own.**

The *gemara* then briefly engages a theological digression. If children do not suffer for parental sins, how does one understand the verse *poked avon avot al banim* — that God visits the iniquity of fathers upon children? The answer: *keshe'ochazin ma'aseh avoteihem biyadeihem* — only when children continue in the sinful ways of their parents. A *beraita* presses further: perhaps even when they do not continue in those ways? The closing phrase *ish bechet'o yumatu* settles it — each person dies only for their own sin, which demonstrates that the divine punishment attaches only when the child has, by their own choice, perpetuated the parental transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְכָשְׁלוּ אִישׁ בְּאָחִיו״ – אִישׁ בַּעֲוֹן אָחִיו, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכּוּלָּן עֲרֵבִים זֶה בָּזֶה. הָתָם, שֶׁהָיָה בְּיָדָם לִמְחוֹת וְלֹא מִיחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** are descendants **not** punished for the sins of their ancestors unless they adopt their behavior? **But isn't it written: "And they shall stumble one upon another"** (Leviticus 26:37)? This verse is homiletically interpreted to mean that the Jewish people shall stumble, **one due to the iniquity of another,** i.e., they are punished for each other's sins, which **teaches that all** Jews are considered **guarantors,** i.e., responsible, **for one another.** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the verse in Leviticus, the reference is to a case **where** others **had the ability to protest** the sin **but they did not protest.** Consequently, they are punished for not protesting, regardless of any familial relationship they may have with the sinner. Where there was no realistic ability to intervene, there is no culpability. This is not merely a metaphysical accounting but reflects a genuine assignment of moral responsibility: you had an obligation and the capacity to fulfill it, and you declined.

### Plural Avot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן אָבוֹת לְבָנִים, וּבָנִים לְאָבוֹת. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אָבוֹת לַהֲדָדֵי. בָּנִים לְבָנִים – מְנָלַן? אִם כֵּן, לִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בֵּן״! מַאי ״בָּנִים״? דַּאֲפִילּוּ בָּנִים לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its discussion of the source for the disqualification of relatives from bearing witness. From the fact that the verse: "The fathers shall not be put to death for the children" (Deuteronomy 24:16), is not phrased in the singular, i.e., A father shall not be put to death for his child, it is derived that not only are a father and child disqualified from bearing witness about one another, but closely related relatives, i.e., brothers, are also disqualified from bearing witness about each other's children. Consequently, **we found** a source for the disqualification of **fathers** from bearing witness **about** their **children** or the children of their brothers, **and** for the disqualification of **children** from bearing witness **about** their **fathers** or their fathers' brothers. **And all the more so,** the related **fathers,** e.g., brothers, are disqualified from bearing witness **about each other,** as they are certainly more closely related to each other than are a nephew and an uncle. But **from where do we** derive that one brother's **children** cannot bear witness **about** the **children** of the other brother? The Gemara answers: **If so,** if the children of one brother can bear witness about the children of the other brother, **let the verse write: The fathers shall not be put to death for the child.** For **what** reason is **"children"** written, in the plural? It is derived from here **that even children** of brothers are disqualified from bearing witness **about each other.**

Returning to the derivation: the verse uses the plural *avot* — fathers — which the *gemara* reads as implying not just a single father and his son, but two fathers who are themselves related, that is, brothers. This licenses extending the disqualification to uncle and nephew: just as a son cannot testify against a father, and a father cannot testify against a son, so too a nephew cannot testify against an uncle. And since if a nephew cannot testify against an uncle, certainly brothers cannot testify against one another.

### Cousins & Third Parties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בָּנִים לַהֲדָדֵי, בָּנִים לְעָלְמָא מְנָלַן? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: סְבָרָא הוּא, כִּדְתַנְיָא: אֵין הָעֵדִים נַעֲשִׂין זוֹמְמִין עַד שֶׁיִּזּוֹמּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בָּנִים לְעָלְמָא כְּשֵׁרִין, נִמְצָא עֵד זוֹמֵם נֶהֱרָג בְּעֵדוּת אָחִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **We found** a source for the *halakha* that the **children** of brothers cannot bear witness **about each other. From where do we** derive that the **children** of brothers cannot bear witness together **about others?** **Rami bar Ḥama says:** This *halakha* **is** based on **logical reasoning,** and is not derived from a verse. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The witnesses are not rendered conspiring** witnesses **unless they are both rendered conspiring** witnesses. **And** therefore, **if it enters your mind** that related **children are fit** to bear witness together **about others, a conspiring witness** can be **found to be executed based on the testimony of his brother,** i.e., his relative. Since a conspiring witness is executed for his testimony only if his co-witness is also rendered a conspiring witness, the testimony of his co-witness, who is a relative, is what causes him to be executed. This is tantamount to relatives bearing witness about each other. Therefore, relatives cannot serve as witnesses together.

How do we extend the disqualification to cousins? The verse could have written simply *lo yumtu avot al ben* — fathers over a son — in the singular. The plural *banim* is therefore extra, teaching that even the *banim lahadadi* — the sons of the respective fathers — cannot testify against one another. That gives us first cousins.

But this raises a further question: *banim le'alma minalan* — how do we know that two cousins together cannot testify against a third party? Rami bar Chama suggests it is *sevara* — logical reasoning — and illustrates why with a case of *edim zomemim*.

## Edim Zomemim Case

### Zomemim Example

The scenario: cousins Chanoch and Nemuel testify against a third person. Subsequently, *hazama* witnesses come forward and establish that both cousins were elsewhere when the events they testified to allegedly occurred. Now both are *edim zomemim* — conspiring witnesses — subject to the penalty the Torah prescribes: *va'asitem lo ka'asher zamam*, you do to them what they intended to do to the accused. If Chanoch is to be executed as a *zomem*, it is partly because of Nemuel's testimony; without Nemuel, Chanoch alone does not constitute a valid pair of witnesses. Thus, in a sense, Nemuel's testimony is bringing about Chanoch's death — and this, argues Rami bar Chama, is the concern behind *banim le'alma*: cousins together testifying against a third party effectively puts each cousin at risk of dying on account of the other's testimony.

### Three Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דִּתְנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין וְאֶחָד מִצְטָרֵף עִמָּהֶן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שָׁלֹשׁ עֵדִיּוֹת, וְהֵן עֵדוּת אַחַת לַהֲזָמָה. נִמְצָא עֵד זוֹמֵם מְשַׁלֵּם מָמוֹן בְּעֵדוּת אָחִיו. אֶלָּא, הֲזָמָה מֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי. הָכָא נָמֵי, הֲזָמָה מֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: But according to your reasoning,** there is a difficulty arising from **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 56b): If one occupied land for three years, this serves in court as proof that he is the legal owner. If **three brothers** testify to his three-year possession of the land, with each one testifying separately about one year, **and one** unrelated individual **joins with** each of the brothers as the second witness, **these are** considered **three** distinct **testimonies** and are therefore accepted by the court. If they were to be considered one testimony it would not be accepted, as brothers may not testify together. **But they are** considered **one testimony for** the purpose of rendering them **conspiring** witnesses. In other words, they are punished only if all six of the witnesses are rendered conspiring witnesses, and the liability is divided among them. Rava states his objection: If so, **a conspiring witness** can be **found** to be **paying money due to the testimony of his brother,** and nevertheless, the brothers' testimony is not disqualified. **Rather,** this is clearly not considered tantamount to brothers bearing witness about each other, as the **rendering** of one as **a conspiring witness comes** not from his co-witness but **from others,** i.e., the witnesses who testify that he had been with them. **Here too,** it cannot be proven logically that relatives are disqualified from bearing witness together, as the **rendering** of one as **a conspiring witness comes from others.**

Rava challenges this reasoning with a *mishnah*: three brothers testify in a *chazakah* case. Reuven, Shimon, and Levi each witnessed one of the three years of undisturbed possession required to establish *chazakah*, while a fourth, unrelated witness Mordechai witnessed all three years. Mordechai and Reuven constitute one valid pair for year one; Mordechai and Shimon for year two; Mordechai and Levi for year three. These are three *separate* testimonies, and there is no problem of brothers testifying together because each brother testified about a *different* year.

Now suppose all three groups are proven *zomemim*. They all pay. But the principle *ein edim na'asim zomemim ad she'yizumu shneihem* — witnesses are not rendered *zomemim* until both are refuted — requires that for the penalty of *zomemim* to apply, *all* groups must be refuted, since the effect of the testimony — establishing three years of *chazakah* — only materializes when all three testimonies are combined. Refuting one group alone dismantles the *chazakah*, but to impose the penalty of *zomemim* and require payment, all must be refuted.

In that case, the three brothers would be paying on account of one another's testimony. Yet this is not prohibited — the brothers' testimonies are distinct and their combined effect is not what makes them liable. It is the *mazimim* who make them pay. Rava therefore rejects Rami bar Chama's argument.

### Hazama Mei'alma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִם כֵּן לִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״וּבֵן עַל אָבוֹת״, אִי נָמֵי ״הֵם עַל אָבוֹת״. מַאי ״וּבָנִים״? אֲפִילּוּ בָּנִים לְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* that relatives are disqualified from bearing witness together is derived from a different source: **If** it is **so** that relatives can bear witness together, **let the verse write: And a child** shall not be put to death **for the fathers, or: They** shall not be put to death **for the fathers.** For **what** reason is **"and the children"** written in the plural? This indicates that related **children** are disqualified from bearing witness **even about others.**

The *gemara* articulates the point explicitly: *ela hazamah m'alma ka'ati* — the refutation comes from external witnesses, not from the co-relative's testimony. The same applies to the original case of the two cousins testifying against a third party: even if they are later found to be *zomemim*, it is the *hazama* from outside that brings about their punishment, not the testimony of one cousin against the other. So the reasoning of Rami bar Chama collapses — the *sevara* does not hold.

### Extra Word Banim

The *gemara* therefore establishes the rule through textual analysis rather than *sevara*. Had the Torah wished only to disqualify testimony between relatives, it could have written the verse more compactly — *uben al avot* or *heim al avot*. The repetition of the full word *uvanim* is therefore read as adding something: *afilu banim le'alma* — even when the relatives are testifying against a third party together, they are disqualified. One simply must accept that the extra word carries this extension.

## Maternal Relations

### Mother's Family

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן קְרוֹבֵי הָאָב, קְרוֹבֵי הָאֵם מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״אָבוֹת״ ״אָבוֹת״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לִקְרוֹבֵי הָאָב, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לִקְרוֹבֵי הָאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the disqualification of **paternal relatives. From where do we** derive the disqualification of **maternal relatives?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states "fathers," "fathers,"** i.e., it states the word **twice.** This repetition is unnecessary, as the verse could have stated: And the children shall not be put to death for them. **If** the superfluous word "fathers" **is not** needed to teach the **matter of paternal relatives,** as this matter was already derived from the verse, **apply it** to the **matter of maternal relatives.**

Up to this point, all of the derivation concerns *krovei ha'av* — the father's side of the family. This makes sense given that the verse speaks of fathers, and that primary family association in *halacha* flows through the father, *mishpachat av*. But the *mishnah* also disqualifies relatives on the mother's side — uncles through the mother, for example. From where is this derived?

The *gemara* answers: *amar kra avot avot trei zimnei* — the word *avot* appears twice in the verse. *Lo yumtu avot al banim uvanim lo yumtu al avot* — fathers shall not die on account of sons, and sons shall not die on account of fathers. The second *avot* is redundant; *im einu inyan lekrovei ha'av, tenei'u inyan lekrovei ha'em* — since we do not need it to teach us about the paternal relatives, apply it to maternal relatives. This is how the mother's family is brought within the scope of the disqualification.

### For & Against

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן לְחוֹבָה, לִזְכוּת מְנָא לַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״יוּמְתוּ״, ״יוּמְתוּ״ – תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי. אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְחוֹבָה, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִזְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the disqualification of relatives **in** cases of **capital law,** as the verse is referring to execution. **From where do we** derive this *halakha* **in** cases of **monetary law?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states:** the term **"shall** not **be put to death," "shall** not **be put to death," twice. If** it **is not** needed to teach the **matter** of testimony **to** the **detriment** of one's relative, as this *halakha* was already derived from the verse, **apply it** to the **matter of** testimony **to** the **benefit** of one's relative.

The verse as read focuses on *lechovah* — testimony against a relative that might lead to that relative's punishment. But relatives may be disqualified in both directions. One might initially assume that the concern is primarily with bias *in favor* of a relative — that one will want to help them. Yet the verse as interpreted here targets the opposite: you cannot testify *against* your relatives in a way that would lead to their death. The *gemara* therefore asks: *ashkachan lechovah, lizchut minalan* — we have found the basis for disqualification against a relative; from where do we derive disqualification in *their favor*? The answer: *amar kra yumtu yumtu trei zimnei* — the word *yumtu* also appears twice. The second usage is redundant if it only teaches about *lechovah*, so *im einu inyan lechovah, tenei'u inyan lizchut* — apply it to *lizchut*, testimony in favor of the relative.

### Equal Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״ – מִשְׁפָּט הַשָּׁוֶה לָכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the disqualification of relatives **in** cases of **capital law,** as the verse is referring to execution. **From where do we** derive this *halakha* **in** cases of **monetary law?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22), which is interpreted to mean: **A law that is equal for you.** In other words, monetary law and capital law essentially follow the same guidelines.

A final step: all of the preceding derivation concerns *dinei nefashot* — capital cases — given the weighty language of the verse about death. One might have argued that in *dinei mamonot* — monetary cases — a lower standard of disqualification should apply, since the stakes are lower and there might be more room for relatives to testify. The *gemara* addresses this with the principle *mishpat echad yihyeh lachem* — one law shall apply to you equally. The same rules of disqualification operate in both *dinei nefashot* and *dinei mamonot*.

## Rav: Shlishi Berishon

### Rav's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֲחִי אַבָּא לֹא יָעִיד לִי, הוּא וּבְנוֹ וַחֲתָנוֹ. אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָעִיד לוֹ, אֲנִי וּבְנִי וַחֲתָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: My paternal uncle will not testify about me,** neither **he, nor his son, nor his son-in-law,** in accordance with the ruling of the mishna. **Furthermore, I will not testify about him,** neither **I, nor my son, nor my son-in-law.**

With the scriptural basis established, the *gemara* turns to the actual scope of the disqualification as articulated by the Amoraim. Rav Acha transmits in the name of Rav: *achi abba lo yaid li* — my father's brother, my uncle, cannot testify for me; *hu uvno vechasno* — he, his son, and his son-in-law. That is: my uncle, my first cousin, and my cousin by marriage — all disqualified. This reflects what the *mishnah* already established.

### Reciprocal

But Rav then extends the principle: *af ani lo a'id lo, ani uveni vechatani* — not only can he not testify for or against me, but I likewise cannot testify for or against him; and not only me, but my children and my son-in-law as well. So it is not simply that I cannot testify against my uncle; my children cannot testify against my uncle either. This creates a *shlishi berishon* relationship — a great-nephew to a great-uncle — which the *mishnah* itself did not mention. The *mishnah* stopped at *sheni besheni*, first cousins. Rav extends to great-uncles and great-nephews.

### Chasno Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? הָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁלִישִׁי בְּרִאשׁוֹן, וַאֲנַן שֵׁנִי בְּשֵׁנִי תְּנַן, שְׁנֵי בְּרִאשׁוֹן תְּנַן, שְׁלִישִׁי בְּרִאשׁוֹן לָא תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why,** for example, can Rav's son not testify about the brother of his father's father? But **it is** the testimony of a member of the **third** generation **with regard to** a related member of the **first** generation, as there is a two-generation difference between them. **And we learned** in the mishna that a member of the **second** generation cannot testify **about** a member of the **second** generation, e.g., one cannot testify about the son of his paternal uncle. **We** also **learned** that a member of the **second** generation cannot testify **about** a member of the **first** generation, e.g., one cannot testify about his uncle. But **we did not learn** that a member of the **third** generation cannot testify **about** a member of the **first** generation.

The *gemara* challenges this. The *mishnah* only discussed *sheni besheni* — or at most *sheni berishon*, uncle to nephew. It did not discuss *shlishi berishon*. How then can Rav read this into the *mishnah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״חֲתָנוֹ״ דְּקָתָנֵי בְּמַתְנִיתִין? חֲתַן בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In the statement **that is taught in the mishna:** They themselves, and their sons, and their sons-in-law are considered relatives, **what** is the ruling of the mishna concerning **his son-in-law** referring to? It is referring to **the son-in-law of his son.** Accordingly, the mishna disqualifies the testimony of a member of the third generation about a member of the first generation.

The *gemara* attempts to support Rav by re-reading the *mishnah*: perhaps when the *mishnah* said *uvno vechasno* — "his son and his son-in-law" — it means not Shimon's own son-in-law but Shimon's son's son-in-law, that is, Chanoch's son-in-law. This would already be a case of a third generation embedded in the *mishnah* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי ״בֶּן בְּנוֹ״! מִילְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּבַעַל כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let** the mishna **teach:** And his son and **the son of his son,** instead of: His son-in-law. This would be a more straightforward manner of conveying the *halakha* with regard to a member of the third generation testifying about a member of the first generation. The Gemara answers: By mentioning his son-in-law, the mishna **teaches us a matter in passing: That** with regard to the different levels of familial relationships, **a husband is like his wife.** Therefore, there is no difference between one's son and one's son-in-law.

But this reading is rejected. If so, the *mishnah* would be discussing a *sheni beshlishi* relationship, which Rav himself never extended to. Moreover, *ella la'olam chasno mamash* — the plain reading of the *mishnah* is that *chasno* means a literal son-in-law, not a grandson's son-in-law.

### Eight Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שְׁמוֹנָה אָבוֹת, שֶׁהֵן עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה – הָנֵי תְּלָתִין וְתַרְתֵּין הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the mishna is referring to the son-in-law of his son, a difficulty is posed by **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** in a *baraita*: **Eight fathers,** i.e., eight principal relatives mentioned in the mishna, are disqualified, **which are twenty-four** including the son and son-in-law of each. If the mishna is referring to one's grandson **these are thirty-two,** as the son, the son-in-law, and the grandson of each are included.

Before resolving the point, the *gemara* notes that Rabbi Chiya teaches: the *mishnah* enumerates eight *avot* — eight principal categories of disqualified relatives — and each of them extends to sons and sons-in-law, yielding 24 people in total. The *gemara* observes that on Rav's reading, incorporating a third generation, the count should rise dramatically — to 32 or even 48. This is clearly not how Rabbi Chiya understands the *mishnah*, which constitutes a further challenge to reading the extra generation into the text.

### True Chasno

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם חֲתָנוֹ מַמָּשׁ. וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״חֲתַן בְּנוֹ״? כֵּיוָן דְּמֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי, כְּדוֹר אַחֵר דָּמֵי. אִי הָכִי, הָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁלִישִׁי בְּשֵׁנִי, וְרַב אַכְשַׁר שְׁלִישִׁי בְּשֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara consequently rejects the explanation that the mishna is referring to the son-in-law of one's son: **Rather,** the mishna is **in fact** referring to **his actual son-in-law. And why does** Rav **refer to him as the son-in-law of his son,** deriving from this case that a member of the third generation cannot testify about a member of the first generation? **Since** one's son-in-law **comes from outside** the family he is considered a more distant relative than his son, **as if** he belongs to **another generation.** The Gemara challenges this: **If that is so,** then the testimony of an individual with regard to the son-in-law of his father **is** equivalent **to** that of a member of the **third** generation **with regard to** a member of the **second** generation. **And Rav** is known to have **deemed** a member of the **third** generation **fit** to bear witness **about** a member of the **second** generation.

The *gemara* therefore resolves the matter as follows. *Ela l'olam chasno mamash* — *chasno* in the *mishnah* genuinely means son-in-law, nothing more. But then why did Rav call it *chatan beno* — "his son's son-in-law"? Because *keivan d'mei-alma ka'ati k'dor acher dami* — since a son-in-law comes from outside the family, connected only through marriage, he is treated as though he were an additional generation removed. In other words, once the *mishnah* stretches far enough to include even a son-in-law — someone with no blood connection — one can see implicit in it the principle that generation-equivalents, even through non-blood links, are included. The *mishnah* is still technically speaking of *sheni besheni*, but Rav reads in it the principle that underlies *shlishi berishon*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֲחִי אַבָּא לֹא יָעִיד לִי – הוּא וּבְנוֹ וַחֲתָנוֹ, כָּךְ בֶּן אֲחִי אַבָּא לֹא יָעִיד לִי – הוּא וּבְנוֹ וַחֲתָנוֹ. וְאַכַּתִּי הָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁלִישִׁי בְּשֵׁנִי, וְרַב אַכְשַׁר שְׁלִישִׁי בְּשֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav stated** his ruling not in accordance with the mishna, but **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar says: Just as my paternal uncle will not testify about me,** neither **he, nor his son, nor his son-in-law, so too, the son of my paternal uncle will not testify about me,** neither **he, nor his son, nor his son-in-law.** Accordingly, one cannot testify about the grandchild of his brother. The Gemara asks: **But still,** according to Rabbi Elazar, the grandson of one's uncle cannot testify about his great-great uncle, which **is** the testimony of a member of the **third** generation **about** a member of the **second** generation; **and Rav deemed** a member of the **third** generation **fit** to bear witness **about** a member of the **second** generation.

The *gemara* then presses: if the *mishnah* is being read to encompass a third generation, should it not also imply *shlishi besheni*? *Ve'Rav achsher shlishi besheni* — Rav explicitly held *shlishi besheni* to be *kasher*. So one cannot use the *mishnah* to derive *shlishi besheni*. The *mishnah* deals with *sheni besheni* only.

The *gemara* therefore repositions Rav's ruling: *ela Rav d'amar k'Rabbi Eliezer* — Rav holds in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer's *beraita*. Rabbi Eliezer taught: just as my father's brother cannot testify against me — he and his son and his son-in-law — so too my father's brother's son, that is, my first cousin, cannot testify against me — he and his son and his son-in-law. This *beraita* explicitly extends the disqualification to the cousin's children and son-in-law, which is *shlishi besheni* — first cousin once removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּוָותֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav holds in accordance with** Rabbi Elazar's opinion **in one** case, i.e., he disqualifies testimony of a member of the third generation with regard to a member of the first generation, **and he disagrees with him in one** case, i.e., he does not disqualify the testimony of a member of the third generation about a member of the second generation.

The *gemara* then identifies the precise relationship between the positions. Rabbi Eliezer goes further than the *mishnah*: he includes *shlishi besheni*. Rav goes further than the *mishnah* but not as far as Rabbi Eliezer: he includes *shlishi berishon* but not *shlishi besheni*. *Rav savar lah kivateh bechada ufalig alei bechada* — Rav agrees with Rabbi Eliezer on one point and disagrees on another. The result is a spectrum: the *mishnah* sets the floor at *sheni besheni*; Rabbi Eliezer extends to *shlishi besheni*; Rav occupies a middle position at *shlishi berishon*.

## Rav's Reasoning

### Extra Vav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא יוּמְתוּ אָבוֹת עַל בָּנִים וּבָנִים״, לְרַבּוֹת דּוֹר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rav?** It is **as the verse states: "The fathers shall not be put to death for the children, and the children** shall not be put to death for the fathers." The phrase "for the children, and the children" is interpreted **to include another generation,** the grandchildren of one's brother; they are also disqualified.

What is Rav's textual basis for his position? The verse reads *lo yumtu avot al banim uvanim* — the conjunction *vav* before *banim* is extra. The verse could have read *lo yumtu avot al banim banim* — using two *banim* without the *vav*. The extra *vav* extends the disqualification to cover not just fathers and their direct sons but the broader set of *banim* — implying great-nephews and great-uncles, the *shlishi berishon* relationship. Rabbi Eliezer derives his further extension from a different reading of *uvanim*, applying the extension principle of *avot* — sons and sons-in-law — to the cousins themselves.

### Ashkenazi vs. Sefardi

The practical *halacha* diverges between communities. Nobody rules like Rabbi Eliezer extending to *shlishi besheni*. But the debate between Rav and the *mishnah*'s baseline produces a *machloket* between the Rishonim. The Rif and the Rambam rule against Rav: *shlishi berishon* is *kasher*, and the *halacha* follows the *mishnah* limiting disqualification to *sheni besheni*. The Ashkenazim, however, rule like Rav: *shlishi berishon* is *pasul*. A useful mnemonic: for Ashkenazim, any relationship whose combined generational count is four or less is *pasul*. Thus *sheni besheni* (2+2=4) and *shlishi berishon* (3+1=4) are both disqualified; *shlishi besheni* (3+2=5) would not be. And all of this extends, in the default case, to include in-laws through *ishto kigufo*, with some exceptions.

## Mother-in-Law Rules

### Achi Chamoti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אֲחִי חֲמוֹתִי לֹא יָעִיד לִי, בֶּן אֲחִי חֲמוֹתִי לֹא יָעִיד לִי, בֶּן אֲחוֹת חֲמוֹתִי לֹא יָעִיד לִי. וּתְנָא תּוּנָא: בַּעַל אֲחוֹתוֹ, וּבַעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, וּבַעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, הֵן וּבְנֵיהֶן וְחַתְנֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says: The brother of my mother-in-law will not testify about me; the son of the brother of my mother-in-law will not testify about me; the son of the sister of my mother-in-law will not testify about me. And the *tanna*** of the mishna also **taught** this *halakha*: **His sister's husband, and the husband of his father's sister, and the husband of his mother's sister,** and his mother's husband, and his father-in-law, and his brother-in-law, **they** themselves, **and their sons, and their sons-in-law.** This describes the same familial relationship from the perspective of the younger generation.

Rav Nachman now applies the *ishto kigufo* principle to a new chain of relationships: *achi chamoti lo yaid li* — my mother-in-law's brother cannot testify for me. The logic is straightforward: applying *ishto kigufo* to my wife makes my wife's mother effectively my mother; her brother is therefore effectively my mother's brother, a classic uncle, who is already disqualified by the *mishnah*.

### Extensions

From there, *ben achi chamoti lo yaid li* — the son of my mother-in-law's brother cannot testify against me, because combining the marital link and the blood link, he is the equivalent of a first cousin. Similarly, *ben achot chamoti lo yaid li* — the son of my mother-in-law's sister cannot testify against me, for the same reason. Whether the link goes through the brother of the mother-in-law or the sister of the mother-in-law, the resulting relationship, once the marital unity is applied, lands at the level of first cousin. This remains *sheni besheni* in substance, even though the *ishto kigufo* link appears at the beginning of the chain rather than at the end.

The *tanna* of our *mishnah* supports this implicitly. The *mishnah* mentions *baal achot aviv* — the husband of the father's sister — and *baal achot imo* — the husband of the mother's sister — as disqualified; and extends the disqualification to their sons and sons-in-law. Reading this from the perspective of the son-in-law rather than the uncle, one arrives at exactly the same relationships Rav Nachman is describing: the mother-in-law's family. Whether one starts with the marriage link or ends with it, the resulting family map is a mirror image of the same structure.

### Father-in-Law

When the same question is posed about the father-in-law's relatives — *achi chamiv* — the answer is identical. Applying *ishto kigufo* to one's wife makes the father-in-law effectively one's father, and his brother effectively one's uncle. All the same extensions apply: the father-in-law's brother's son, the father-in-law's sister's son — all are first cousins by marriage and thus disqualified. The *mishnah*'s language about *achiv, achei aviv, achei immo, hen uveneihem vechataneihem* — his brother, his father's brothers, his mother's brothers, and their sons and sons-in-law — implicitly encompasses all of these cases once the marital unity principle is applied from the other end.

## Stepson Cases

### Stepson's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי עוּלָּא, אִיבְּעִי לַן: אֲחִי חָמִיו מַהוּ? בֶּן אֲחִי חָמִיו מַהוּ? בֶּן אֲחוֹת חָמִיו מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: When we were in Ulla's study hall, we were asked:** With regard to testifying about **the brother of** one's **father-in-law, what is** the *halakha*? With regard to **the son of the brother of** one's **father-in-law, what is** the *halakha*? With regard to **the son of the sister of** one's **father-in-law, what is** the *halakha*?

Rav Ashi relates that while in the house of Ulla, the following question was posed: *mahu she'ya'id adam be'eshet chorgo* — what is the law if a person wishes to testify about his stepson's wife? The *mishnah* had established *chorgo levado* — the stepson only, not further extensions of his family across generations. But the question here is different: not whether the stepson's children are included, but whether the stepson's *wife* is included through *ishto kigufo*. After all, one might argue that since the stepson himself is disqualified — even though without any blood connection — his wife should be subsumed into him by *ishto kigufo*.

### Sura: Ba'al Ke'Ishto

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לַן: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, אָחִיו וַאֲחִי אָבִיו וַאֲחִי אִמּוֹ – הֵן וּבְנֵיהֶן וְחַתְנֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** And Ulla **said to us: You learned** the answer to this question in the mishna: **His brother, and his paternal uncle, and his maternal uncle,** and his mother's husband, and his father-in-law, and his brother-in-law, **they** themselves, **and their sons, and their sons-in-law.** These relationships are the same as those you were asked about, from the perspective of the younger generation.

The scholars of Sura answered: *ba'al ke'ishto* — the husband is treated as one with the wife. Therefore, even though one would not extend the stepson disqualification to his children, one does extend it to his wife, since she and he are a single unit.

### Pumbedita: Isha Ke'Va'al…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא אָמְרִי: אִשָּׁה כְּבַעְלָהּ. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה כְּבַעְלָהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״עֶרְוַת אֲחִי אָבִיךָ לֹא תְגַלֵּה אֶל אִשְׁתּוֹ לֹא תִקְרָב דֹּדָתְךָ הִיא״. וַהֲלֹא אֵשֶׁת דּוֹדוֹ הִיא! מִכְּלָל דְּאִשָּׁה כְּבַעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In Pumbedita they say** a different version of Rav's answer: **A wife is** considered **like her husband.** Therefore, the wife of his stepson is considered like his stepson, and he cannot testify about her. **As Rav Huna says** that **Rav Naḥman says: From where** is it derived **that a wife is** considered **like her husband? As it is written: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of your father's brother; you shall not approach his wife: She is your aunt"** (Leviticus 18:14). **Isn't she the wife of his uncle,** and not his aunt, i.e., his father's sister? It can therefore be concluded, **by inference,** that **a wife is** considered **like her husband.**

The scholars of Pumbedita formulated it in the reverse: *isha ke'va'alah* — the wife is as her husband. The substance is identical; the two academies agree that the stepson's wife is included, differing only in how they express the marital unity principle. Normally one hears the formulation *ishto kigufo*, which subsumes the wife into the husband; here Sura leads with the inverse — the husband is treated as one with his wife — and Pumbedita returns to the more familiar framing.

### Torah Source

The textual support for the entire principle of marital unity comes from Rav Huna in the name of Rav Nachman, citing the verse: *ervat achi avicha lo tegaleh, el ishto lo tikrav, dodat'cha hi* — do not expose the nakedness of your father's brother; do not approach his wife, for she is your aunt. The verse calls the uncle's wife "your aunt," even though she is technically the wife of the uncle, not a blood relative at all. This demonstrates that *isha ke'va'alah* — the wife is treated as her husband — and thus that the uncle's wife is legally subsumed into the uncle's identity for purposes of these prohibitions and disqualifications.

## Stepfather Cases

### Mother's Husband's Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעַל אִמּוֹ, הוּא וּבְנוֹ וַחֲתָנוֹ. בְּנוֹ – הַיְינוּ אָחִיו?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that among the relatives disqualified from testifying are **his mother's husband, he,** i.e., the mother's husband himself, **and his son, and his son-in-law.** The Gemara challenges: **The son of** his mother's husband **is** the same as **his brother,** i.e., his half brother, and the mishna already stated that his brother is disqualified.

The *mishnah* also mentions *baal imo* — the mother's husband, that is, the stepfather — and states that his disqualification extends to *beno vechasno* — his son and his son-in-law. The question arises: which son? If the stepfather had a son with the man's own mother, that son is already the man's half-brother through the mother — and a brother is already listed as disqualified in the *mishnah*. Why then does the *mishnah* need to specify the stepfather's son separately?

### Rabbi Yirmiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לַאֲחִי הָאָח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** Mentioning this case **is necessary only for the** half **brother of the** half **brother,** i.e., the son of his stepfather from another woman. Although they are not biologically related, they are considered relatives.

Rabbi Yirmiyah answers: *lo nitzracha ela le'achi ha'ach* — the *mishnah* needs this specification for the case of a "brother's brother." Concretely: the stepfather Mordechai was previously married to Esther, and has a son Liam with her. Liam shares no blood relationship with Reuven whatsoever — they do not share a father or a mother. But Liam is the brother of Reuven's mother's husband's son — a brother's brother, related only through the fact that they each share a parent with someone who is shared. Is such a person included in the *mishnah*'s disqualification? Rabbi Yirmiyah reads the *mishnah* as saying yes: even this "brother's brother" is *pasul*.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אַכְשַׁר בַּאֲחִי הָאָח. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ הָא דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, כְּלוֹמַר, לָא סְבִירָא לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda deemed** the testimony of **the** half **brother of the** half **brother fit.** The other Sages **said to him: Did you not hear this** statement **of Rabbi Yirmeya,** that the half brother of the half brother is disqualified? Rav Ḥisda **said to them: I did not hear** this, **as if to say: I do not hold** in accordance with this opinion.

Rav Chisda disagrees: *achi ha'ach kasher* — a brother's brother with no shared parent is *kasher* and may testify. When told that Rabbi Yirmiyah reads the *mishnah* as including this case, Rav Chisda responds: *lo shamia li* — which is to say, *lo sevira li* — I do not hold of that interpretation. I do not read the *mishnah* as extending to a case where there is no shared parent whatsoever.

### Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ אָחִיו! תְּנָא אָחִיו מִן הָאָב, וְקָתָנֵי אָחִיו מִן הָאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the question asked above recurs: The son of his mother's husband **is** the same as **his brother.** The Gemara answers: The mishna **taught** the case of **a paternal** half **brother, and** it also **teaches** the case of **a maternal** half **brother.**

If that is Rav Chisda's view, however, one must ask why the *mishnah* bothered to mention the stepfather's son at all, since that son — on the straightforward reading — is simply the man's brother through the mother, already covered. The *gemara* resolves: *tana achi min ha ve'tana achi min hi* — the *tanna* wished to make explicit that both types of brotherhood are included: a brother through the father and a brother through the mother are equally *pasul*. The mention of the stepfather's son in the *mishnah* serves to make clear that the maternal half-brother is just as disqualified as the paternal half-brother.

## Mechutanim

### Fathers of Couple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲבִי חָתָן וַאֲבִי כַּלָּה מְעִידִין זֶה עַל זֶה, וְלָא דָּמוּ לַהֲדָדֵי אֶלָּא כִּי אַכְלָא לְדַנָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says: The father of** the **groom and the father of** the **bride can testify about each other, and** this is because **they are considered to each other only like a lid on a barrel,** which is not part of the barrel, but merely rests on top of it.

Rav Chisda rules: *avi chatan ve'avi kallah me'idim zeh al zeh* — the father of the groom and the father of the bride may testify against one another. On its face this is surprising: one might have thought that through their respective children — who are married to one another — they would be drawn into the orbit of disqualification. After all, each is, in some sense, related to the other through the marriage.

The *gemara* explains: *kechala ledana* — they are like a cover on a pot — and not a tight-fitting cover at that, but a loose one. Their relationship to one another passes entirely through their children; there is no direct kinship. They are not relatives in any meaningful legal sense — they are *mechutanim*, in-laws of in-laws, connected only by the fact that their children married. This link is too attenuated to constitute *korva*, and therefore they remain fully *kasher* to testify against one another.

## Arussa Question

### Rabba's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: מֵעִיד אָדָם לְאִשְׁתּוֹ אֲרוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says: A man can testify about his betrothed wife;** they are considered related only after marriage.

Rabba bar bar Chana rules: *me'id adam le'ishto arusa* — a person may testify about his betrothed wife, his *arusa*, the woman with whom he has contracted *kiddushin* but not yet performed *nisuin*.

### Ravina Qualifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִינַּהּ, אֲבָל לְעַיּוֹלֵי לַהּ – לָא מְהֵימַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says: We said** this *halakha* **only for** testimony that **removes** money **from her** possession; **but for** testimony that **awards her** money, his testimony is **not deemed credible,** as he will ultimately marry her and benefit from the money.

Ravina qualifies: this can only be testimony *against* her interests — *lafuki minah* — because then it also hurts the testifier's own interests, since he will be marrying her and whatever she loses, he effectively loses. But to testify *in her favor* — *le'ayilu lah*, bringing money into the marriage — one would not be believed, since that helps one's own interests directly.

### Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, לָא שְׁנָא לְאַפּוֹקֵי וְלָא שְׁנָא לְעַיּוֹלֵי – לָא מְהֵימַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this opinion: **But** that **is not so.** There **is no difference** whether the testimony **removes** money from her possession, **and** there **is no difference** whether it **awards** her money. In both cases, his testimony is **not deemed credible.**

The *gemara* rejects this distinction entirely: *lo shna lafukei velo shna le'ayilu* — it makes no difference whether the testimony hurts her or helps her; in either case, *lo m'heman* — the husband is not believed. The *arusa* is already his wife for this purpose, regardless of the direction of the testimony.

### She'er Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ? כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא: אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲרוּסָה לֹא אוֹנֵן וְלֹא מִטַּמֵּא לַהּ, וְכֵן הִיא לֹא אוֹנֶנֶת וְלֹא מִטַּמְּאָה לוֹ. מֵתָה – אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ, מֵת הוּא – גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. הָתָם, בִּשְׁאֵרוֹ תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא, אַכַּתִּי לָאו שְׁאֵרוֹ הִיא. הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם אִיקָּרוֹבֵי דַּעְתָּא הוּא, וְהָא אִיקָּרַבָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ לְגַבַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is your thinking** that you deem a man fit to bear witness concerning his betrothed wife? It is **as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ami said in the name of Ulla: One does not** enter **acute mourning** on the day of the death **of his betrothed wife, nor may he become ritually impure to** bury **her** if he is a priest. **Similarly, she does not** enter **acute mourning for him and** is **not** obligated to **become ritually impure to** bury **him.** If **she dies, he does not inherit her** property. If **he dies, she collects** payment of **her marriage contract** if he wrote it at the time of the betrothal, as it is treated like any other monetary document. Evidently, a betrothed woman is not considered related to her husband. That *halakha* does not serve as a proof for the *halakha* of testimony, as **there,** with regard to ritual impurity and the like, **the Merciful One renders it dependent on** the question of whether the woman is **"his kin"** (Leviticus 21:2), and a betrothed woman **is not yet his kin,** as they are not yet married. But **here,** with regard to testimony, **it is due to a sense of affinity** that he is disqualified, **and** one **feels a sense of affinity toward** his betrothed.

What motivated Ravina's distinction? He may have been reasoning from the *halacha* that in several areas, a *beurothed* wife does not yet have the full legal status of a married wife. Rabbi Chiya bar Ammi in the name of Ulla teaches: with respect to an *arusa*, the husband is not an *onen* if she dies and does not contract *tumah* for her; she likewise does not become *onenet* for him, nor does he inherit from her. The one exception is that if he dies, she collects her *ketuva*. These distinctions exist because the *arusa* has not yet become *she'er* — flesh — with her husband. The verse ties all of these obligations to *she'ero hakarov eilav* — his flesh that is close to him — and *she'er* implies full physical union through *nisuin*, not merely the legal bond of *kiddushin*.

In the context of testimony, however, the principle is different. *Hacha mishum ikruvei da'ata hu* — here the concern is *ikruvei da'ata*, closeness of heart, the emotional and relational bond that inclines a person toward another. And this bond clearly exists between a man and his *arusa*; they are already committed to one another, already oriented toward one another's interests. It does not matter whether the concern is that he will help her or that he is so emotionally entangled that he might even testify against her out of complicated feeling — the point is that the connection itself disqualifies.

This leads to an important conceptual observation with which to close. Throughout the daf, one might be tempted to read the disqualifications of *korva* as entirely consequentialist — the concern is always that a relative might be biased, and therefore unreliable. But as the *gemara* earlier indicated, even Moshe and Aharon — whom we would never suspect of dishonest testimony — cannot testify for or against one another. Disqualification based on *korva* is a formal status, not a case-by-case assessment of likely bias. The underlying reasons exist in the background and help explain why the *halacha* was structured this way, but once someone falls within the defined categories, they are *pasul* regardless of any concrete assessment of their reliability in the particular case. The *arusa* discussion brings this tension into the open — here the *gemara* invokes *ikruvei da'ata* as the explicit ground of disqualification — but even here, once the *arusa* falls within the category, the disqualification is absolute, not subject to individual evaluation. Status *psul* operates on the level of category, not character.
