# Hullin 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood & Water Mixtures

### Rabbi Assi: Clear Serum

The daf opens by continuing a discussion from 87b concerning the nature of blood and blood mixtures — specifically, when a substance retains the halachic status of blood. The preceding context had established that as long as a blood mixture still appears red, it functions as a *machshir zera'im*, rendering food susceptible to *tumah*. The Gemara had objected that if blood is mixed with water, the water alone would suffice to *machshir*, so what is the novelty? The answer had been that the case involves blood mixed with rainwater.

Continuing into 88b, Rabbi Assi mi-Naharville states that even the *tzilusah d'dama* — the clear serum of blood — qualifies as a *machshir zera'im*. As Rashi explains, if blood is allowed to sit undisturbed, the denser matter collects toward the center while a clearer, more watery liquid settles at the edges. Rabbi Assi teaches that even this clear component, though it no longer appears deeply red, retains sufficient connection to blood to render food susceptible to *tumah*.

### Rabbi Yirmiyah: No Karet

Rabbi Yirmiyah mi-Difti then states: *ein bo karet* — this component does not carry liability for *karet*. The question is whether this statement addresses the case of blood mixed with water discussed just above, or the immediately preceding line concerning the *tzilusah d'dama*. Rashi is somewhat ambiguous, but Tosafot understands it as bearing on both cases. Tosafot maintains that one incurs *karet* even for the watery component of blood, since it was originally entirely blood and has merely separated into its constituent parts — though only if a *kezayis* of actual blood is present.

This qualification requires attention. Of course, a *kezayis* of blood is ordinarily required for *karet* — so what is the novelty? The *chiddush* seems to lie in the following: if the blood has separated such that only the clear serum remains in the quantity consumed, and the genuinely red, solid component amounts to less than a *kezayis*, one does not incur *karet*. The analogy is straightforward — if one consumes a stew containing *chazir* mixed with vegetables, liability attaches only if one has ingested a *kezayis* of the forbidden substance itself, not merely of the mixture as a whole.

What makes this particularly striking is the potential middle position it implies. The *tzilusah d'dama* may retain enough of the character of blood to function as a *machshir zera'im*, yet lack sufficient density to trigger the prohibition of eating blood or to carry the liability of *karet*. This would be an unusual intermediate status — connected enough to blood for one purpose but not another.

### Tannaim: Blood & Tumah

A *baraita* then teaches that a blood mixture causes *tumah* in an *ohel* — provided a full *revieis* of blood is present. Again, the same analytical question arises: does this concern blood mixed with water, or the watery component that has separated from the blood itself? If it concerns blood mixed with water, the ruling is intuitive — human blood from a corpse causes *tumas ohel* when present in a tent, and naturally a *revieis* of actual blood must be present within the mixture, not merely a *revieis* of the diluted combination. If, however, the statement addresses the *tzilusah d'dama*, it effectively becomes a limiting ruling: only the red, dense component of blood generates *tumas ohel*, while the clear separated liquid — though connected enough to the blood to *machshir zera'im* — is insufficient to transmit *tumah*.

On this reading, the Gemara emerges with a finely calibrated hierarchy: the clear serum of blood carries enough of blood's identity to render food susceptible, but not enough to impose *karet* on the one who consumes it, nor to cause *tumas ohel*.

### Corpse Liquids & Blood

The Gemara now cites a related source. Though the marginal notation indicates *Tosefta*, and Rabbi Shimshon (the *Rash*, one of the *Ba'alei HaTosafot* who composed a commentary on the Mishnah) notes that all manuscripts read *tnan* — suggesting a *mishnah* — the passage in fact appears in no *mishnah* and belongs to the *Tosefta* alone. The Gemara acknowledges this kind of imprecision elsewhere.

The passage states: *kol mashkei meis tehorim chutz midamo* — all liquids of a corpse are *tahor* except for its blood. The blood of a corpse, as just established, causes *tumah* and even *tumas ohel*. The passage continues: *v'chol marei admumit shebo metam'in b'ohel* — any reddish appearance within the liquid also causes *tumah* in the *ohel*. This formulation could mean, consistent with what was said earlier, that a blood-water mixture causes *tumas ohel* as long as it retains a red appearance; or it could mean that even blood that is not deeply red — perhaps the partially separated, more liquidy component — still qualifies as blood for this purpose. All other liquids of the corpse, however, are *tahor* and carry no *tumah* of the corpse.

## Impure Liquids & Tumah

### Corpse Exception

The Gemara now raises a challenge from a different source. A *mishnah* teaches that liquids emerging from a *tamei* person are treated as though that person touched them — rendering such liquids *tamei* themselves. The question posed is whether this principle extends even to liquids emerging from a corpse, which is the gravest source of *tumah*. If so, the teaching that all non-blood liquids of a corpse are *tahor* seems contradicted.

### Tevul Yom Status

Before resolving this tension, the Gemara introduces an important distinction. A *tevul yom* — someone who has immersed in a *mikvah* on that day but whose purification is not yet complete until nightfall — carries the halachic status of a *sheni l'tumah*. The *mishnah* teaches that liquids emerging from a *tevul yom* are treated as though he touched them. Since a *tevul yom* is in transition out of *tumah*, the Rabbis did not extend the rule that a *sheni* who touches liquid elevates it to a *rishon*. Consequently, *v'eilu v'eilu ein metamin* — these liquids do not cause *tumah* at all.

To appreciate this ruling, one must understand the general principle: when a *sheni* touches a liquid, that liquid rises to the level of a *rishon*. This is why the hands, which are rabbinically considered *sheni*, must be dried after *netilat yadayim* — lest the liquid on them become *rishon* and, upon touching food, elevate it as well. It is also the basis for the custom of washing one's hands before dipping *karpas* on Seder night, since the liquid on the *karpas* would otherwise be elevated by contact with *sheni* hands. For the *tevul yom*, however, who stands on the threshold of *taharah*, the Rabbis made no such extension.

For all other *tamei* persons — whether light (*kalin*), such as one who touched a *sheretz* and is thereby a *rishon*, or grave (*chamurim*), such as a *zav* who is an *av hatumah* — liquids emerging from their bodies are treated as though they touched those liquids, rendering the liquids *rishon l'tumah*. The only exception: certain bodily fluids of those whose very *tumah* derives from their bodily emissions — the *zav*, the *zavah*, and the *niddah* — are themselves *avot hatumah*. Specifically, their spit, urine, and semen (based on the verse *v'chi yarok*, "and if he spits," from which the Gemara extends to all fluids that collect within the body before being discharged) are *avot hatumah* in their own right. Blood and tears, by contrast, do not satisfy this criterion even for these individuals — they would be *rishon* but not *av*.

### Zav vs. Corpse Logic

With this background established, the Gemara returns to the apparent contradiction. The *mishnah* teaches that even low-level *tamei* persons (*kalin* — those who touched a *sheretz*) have their liquids treated as *rishon*, and even *chamurim* (the *zav*, an *av hatumah*) generate *tamei* liquids. Yet the corpse — the *avi avot hatumah*, the most severe source of *tumah* — has all non-blood liquids declared *tahor*. The irony is striking: the weightiest source of *tumah* in the Torah generates no *tumah* in its liquids, while a comparatively minor *tamei* person does.

The Gemara resolves this with a sociological observation. A *zav* — Rashi notes — is not visibly distinguishable from any other person. People do not naturally distance themselves from him, do not recoil, and often do not even know he is a *zav*. Given this proximity and the likelihood of inadvertent contact, the Rabbis extended the *tumah* decree to his emitted liquids as well, to prompt greater vigilance. The corpse, by contrast, is known to all and is naturally avoided by anyone concerned with maintaining *taharah*. Since the Rabbis' protective decree was unnecessary for the corpse situation, they made no extension — leaving non-blood corpse liquids *tahor*.

Tosafot raises a further question: why are we speaking of this as rabbinic in the first place? A liquid that emerges from a body is touching that body; should it not become *tamei* biblically through contact? Tosafot offers two answers. The first, admittedly highly technical, posits the case where some kind of conduit was used such that the liquid never actually made surface contact with the corpse's skin. The second answer — more structurally significant — observes that only the canonical seven liquids (*yayin, shemen, dvash, shemen zayit, mayim, dam, tal* — as encapsulated in the mnemonic *yad shachat dam*) formally qualify as liquids in the halachic sense. Tears, urine, and similar bodily fluids are not among them. Since they are not biblically recognized as liquids, even contact with the corpse would not transmit *tumah* to them; hence the *tumah* applicable to them in the case of a *zav* is purely rabbinic in origin.

### Seven Liquids Rule

This leads to a clarifying discussion of the seven liquids themselves. The question is raised why tears, for example, are not subsumed under *mayim* — water — since they clearly have a water component. The answer is that the Gemara's list of seven liquids was not constituted by chemical composition. The very fact that dew (*tal*) required explicit inclusion — despite being entirely water — demonstrates that the category *mayim* was not simply "anything containing water." The seven liquids are a defined, closed category, and fluids such as tears and sweat, whatever their composition, fall outside it.

## Covering Sacrificial Blo…

### Damo Debate

The Gemara now transitions to the final *mishnah* of the perek, which concerns the obligation of *kissuy hadam* — covering blood after *shechita*. A *baraita* is cited: *v'chissahu* — "and he shall cover it" — teaches that the blood which splatters (*hanitaz*) and the blood remaining on the knife must also be covered. Rabbi Yehuda, however, qualifies: *eimasai* — when does this apply? Only when there is no other blood present. If the main pool of blood is available to be covered separately, one is exempt from covering the splattered blood and the blood on the knife. A second *baraita* teaches that one must cover *kol damo* — all the blood, including what splatters on the walls of the slaughterhouse (*agafayim*), which would require scraping or applying earth to the walls themselves.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then articulates a different position: the obligation is conditioned on whether one has covered *dam hanefesh* — the life blood, the blood that flows immediately upon *shechita*. If that blood has been covered, one is exempt from covering the rest, regardless of where it may have landed.

The Gemara presents this as a three-way debate turning on the interpretation of the word *damo* in the biblical verse. The rabbis read *damo* as *kol damo* — all of its blood, with no qualification. Rabbi Yehuda reads *damo* as *afilu miktzas damo* — any portion of its blood suffices, so covering some blood discharges one of any obligation regarding the remainder. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reads *damo* as *damo hameyuchad* — its most distinguished blood, namely the life blood.

### Pesak & Practice

The question of how we rule is debated among the *rishonim*. Ordinarily, when Rabbi Yehuda employs the phrase *eimasai* — "when does this apply?" — the convention is that he is not dissenting but clarifying the position of the *tanna kamma*. Here, however, the Gemara frames it as a genuine three-way disagreement, which complicates the application of that principle. A number of *rishonim*, including the Rambam and what appears to be the dominant position in Tosafot, hold that Rabbi Yehuda is indeed clarifying, and we rule accordingly. The Ra'avad, by contrast, maintains that Rabbi Yehuda represents a dissenting view and we rule with the *chachamim*, requiring all the blood to be covered.

A practical observation emerges: even those who accept Rabbi Yehuda's ruling do not frame it as permission to cover only a token amount and be done. The consistent formulation is that if there is blood one cannot practicably cover, one is exempt from that remainder — but one should still cover as much as possible. Whether at the biblical level Rabbi Yehuda means only that *miktzas* suffices minimally or that he also envisions covering as much as one can, even if not all, is not fully resolved.

In practice, *shechita* as conducted today involves covering the primary flow of blood as it emerges — the *pirkus* stage — and thereafter the knife is cleaned in a sink rather than in earth. One cannot realistically rule like the *chachamim* requiring all blood to be covered; it would be practically impossible in a slaughterhouse setting. The operative ruling therefore follows either Rabbi Yehuda or Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, with Rabbi Yehuda representing the more lenient position since, read literally, any amount of blood covered would suffice.

## Materials for Kissuy

### Granular vs. Thick

The *mishnah* now addresses the question of *bameh mechassin u'bameh ein mechassin* — with what may one cover the blood, and with what may one not? The items with which one may cover include: *zevel hadak* (thin manure), *chol hadak* (thin sand), *sid* (lime or plaster), *charsit* (pottery material), *leveinah* (brick), and *magufah* (barrel seals) — provided the latter two have been ground (*she-ketashan*) into a granular form. One may not cover with thick manure, thick sand, nor with the brick or barrel seal that has not been ground. And one may not simply place a vessel over the blood — *lo yichpeh alav et ha-keli*.

Two criteria emerge from the *mishnah*: the covering material must be granular in character, and it must be of the type from which vegetation can grow. These are conceptually distinct. Rashi emphasizes that the very act of "covering" — *kisui* — requires something granular rather than solid. When one places a vessel or a solid object over something, the covering is removable and external; something granular, by contrast, integrates itself into the surrounding ground and stays. The covering must therefore be like *afar*, earth, not only in what it is made of but in how it covers.

The imagery here is also evocative. When blood is spilled in violence and injustice, leaving it exposed cries out to heaven — *kol d'mei achicha tzo'akim eilai*, the voice of your brother's blood cries out from the earth. When blood is spilled legitimately through *shechita*, the obligation is to cover it — not to expose and display it. There is a meaningful inversion: the crime of murder is to cover up the blood, while the *mitzvah* after valid *shechita* is to cover it properly.

### RSBG: Growth Principle

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel articulates a general principle: *davar she-megadel bo tzmachim mechassin bo* — anything in which vegetation grows may be used as a covering; anything in which vegetation does not grow may not. At first glance, this appears to merely restate or synthesize the specific cases of the *mishnah* rather than add a new criterion. Yet a closer look reveals that "growth" and "granularity" are not identical. A clump of manure or a solid block of soil is also presumably fertile ground — yet it is excluded. The *mishnah* thus seems to require both characteristics simultaneously: the material must be granular in form *and* capable of sustaining plant life.

One perspective raised during the shiur is that the emphasis on growth may carry a deeper resonance: the blood is itself a life-source, and covering it with something that can itself generate life creates a kind of continuum — life returning to life, the ground sustaining new growth from what was once a source of vitality. This contrasts with the purely structural reading that *megadel tzmachim* is simply a proxy for "resembles earth." Both dimensions may be operative.

### Potter's Standard

The Gemara then asks: what precisely is *chol hadak* — thin sand? Rabbah bar bar Chanah said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: any sand that a potter does not need to grind before using in his clay. Conversely, *chol hagas* — thick sand — is sand that the potter must grind before incorporating into the clay.

A parallel formulation is offered: alternatively, the teaching clarifies the *seifa*, the latter clause — defining thick sand as that which requires grinding. The Gemara then asks: what is the practical difference between these two formulations? The answer is a borderline case — sand that can be crumbled by hand (*demifrach parich*) but does not strictly require grinding. Under the definition of thin sand as "not requiring grinding," such sand qualifies as thin, since one need not grind it. Under the definition of thick sand as "requiring grinding," the same sand might qualify as thick, since in some sense it does benefit from grinding. The formulation chosen therefore affects how borderline cases are classified.

## Klal u'Prat Analysis

### Klal Tzarich l'Prat

The Gemara proceeds to a *baraita* that analyzes the verse hermeneutically. The verse says *v'chissahu* — "and he shall cover it" — and then *be'afar* — "with dust." One might think that covering with stones or placing a vessel on top would suffice; therefore, the verse says *be'afar*. But then one might think that only actual *afar* — earth — is permitted. How, then, do we permit thin manure, thin sand, ground stone, ground pottery, thin flax fibers, thin sawdust, lime, brick, and barrel seals that have been ground? The verse says *v'chissahu*, which is inclusive.

Conversely, one might think the inclusive *v'chissahu* extends to thick manure, thick sand, ground metal vessels, unground brick and barrel seals, flour, and bran. Against this, the verse says *be'afar* to exclude them. The operative distinction is: those things that are *min afar* — of the nature of earth, granular in character and capable of sustaining growth — are included; those that are not *min afar* are excluded.

At this point the Gemara raises the obvious hermeneutic challenge: why not apply *klal u'prat* here? *V'chissahu* would be the *klal* — the general inclusive statement — and *be'afar* would be the *prat* — the limiting particular. Under the principle *klal u'prat ein baklal ela mah shebaprat*, one would be restricted to earth alone, excluding all other materials. Why, instead, does the Gemara proceed by *ribuy u'miut* — inclusion followed by limitation, yielding the broader class of earth-similar materials?

Rav Mari answers: because this is a *klal hatzarich liprat* — a *klal* that requires the *prat* to clarify it. The general rule is that a *klal hatzarich liprat* is not subject to the standard *klal u'prat* analysis.

### Rashi & Tosafot

Rashi and Tosafot differ on what *klal hatzarich liprat* means in this context. Rashi explains that the *klal* here — the word *v'chissahu* — does not stand on its own as a clear general statement. The verb itself is ambiguous: "covering" can refer to placing a vessel or blanket over something (a removable, external cover), or it can refer to the granular, earth-like embedding that is actually required here. Since the verb does not independently convey a well-defined class of objects or actions, the *prat* is not functioning as a limiting exception to an otherwise understood *klal* — rather, it is the *prat* that gives the *klal* its meaning in the first place. A *klal* that needs the *prat* for its very intelligibility cannot be processed through the standard *klal u'prat* analysis.

Tosafot offers a simpler interpretation. The word *tzarich* in *klal hatzarich liprat* means that the *prat* — the word *be'afar* — was needed to communicate a separate piece of information: that earth must be placed both beneath and above the blood. Since the *prat* was needed to serve this other purpose, it cannot simultaneously be understood as a purely restrictive term that limits the *klal* to *afar* alone. The *prat* is doing double duty; therefore, it does not function as a standard *prat* in a *klal u'prat* derivation.

In summary: Rashi locates the problem in the ambiguity of the *klal* itself; Tosafot locates it in the *prat* having been drafted for a separate legal point. Both arrive at the conclusion that *klal u'prat* does not apply here.

### Ribuy u'Miut Method

The Gemara notes that one could alternatively frame the interpretation as *ribuy u'miut* — a broad inclusion followed by a narrowing exclusion — rather than *klal u'prat*. The *ribuy* would be the general word *v'chissahu*, and the *miut* would be *be'afar*, yielding anything similar to the narrower term. Since the entire community operates on the reading that this case is *klal u'prat* rather than *ribuy u'miut*, the Gemara works within that framework. Under *ribuy u'miut*, one arrives at anything that shares the relevant properties of *afar*, without being restricted to *afar* alone — which is precisely the conclusion the Gemara reaches through the alternative analysis. The difference between the two methods would manifest in other cases, but here the practical outcome is the same.

## Desert & Sea Cases

### Gold Coin Case

Having established the general framework, the Gemara now addresses extreme circumstances. If one is traveling in the desert and has no *afar* available with which to cover the blood — and the context of the *baraita* already used to establish the exclusion of desert sand makes clear that sand from the desert floor is not valid, since planting into it yields nothing — one may grind a gold coin and use the gold dust as a covering.

This is surprising given what was said earlier: ground metal (*shchikat klei mateches*) was among the items explicitly excluded. How then can gold dust be used?

### Tallit Ashes

The parallel emergency case involves traveling by sea. If one has no *afar* available on board, one may burn one's *tallit* and use the ashes as a covering. This too raises questions given the earlier discussion, since ashes are not obviously organic in all cases, and their capacity to support plant growth is not self-evident.

### Ashes & Gold Dust

The Gemara resolves these cases through textual derivation. Regarding ashes: *bishlama soref taliso umechasen, ashkechan eifer d'ika* — we find that ashes are called *afar*, as the verse says regarding the *parah adumah*, *velakchu latamei me'afar sreifat hachatat* — they shall take for the *tamei* person from "the dust of the burning of the purification-offering." The word *afar* is used explicitly to describe what is in fact ash. Since the Torah calls ash *afar*, it qualifies as a covering material.

Regarding gold: Rav Zeira cites the verse *ve'afrot zahav lo* — "and he had gold dust" — which uses the word *afrot* (dust) in the context of gold. Since the Torah applies the term *afar* to gold dust, gold dust is classified as *afar* for the purpose of *kissuy hadam*. This represents a category of permitted materials that rests not on the physical properties of *afar* — granularity, capacity for growth — but on the Torah's own application of the label *afar* to those substances.

## Shammai v. Hillel

### Core Dispute

A *baraita* records a tannaitic dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai holds: *ein mechassin ela be'afar* — one may cover the blood only with actual earth, nothing else. All the permitted materials discussed above — ground pottery, sand, thin manure, ashes, and the rest — are rejected by Beit Shammai. Only dirt qualifies.

Beit Hillel responds: *matzinu eifer shekarui afar* — we find that ashes are called *afar*, as proven from the verse about *parah adumah* cited above. Beit Shammai counters that that verse says *afar sreifah* — the dust of burning — which is a compound phrase modifying *afar* with the word "burning." It does not prove that plain *afar* in the verse of *kissuy hadam* extends to ashes in general; it merely gives a specific name to a specific substance. Plain *afar*, used without qualification, refers only to earth.

This debate maps neatly onto the two categories that have emerged across the entire discussion: materials that possess the *properties* of *afar* (granularity, capacity for plant growth), and materials to which the Torah *applies the label* of *afar* (gold dust, ashes). Beit Shammai refuses both extensions; Beit Hillel accepts at minimum the extension via label.

### Added Materials

The *mishnah* adds further materials that were subsequently permitted: ground charcoal (*shachor*), eye salve (*kachol*), and the dust that accumulates in a mill (*nikris pisulin*, as Rashi identifies it — though whether this derives from the wheat being ground or from the millstones themselves is uncertain, given that flour was explicitly excluded earlier). Some authorities add even *zarnich*, identified by Rashi as some form of metallic powder and by others as related to antimony or a similar cosmetic substance. The precise logic of these inclusions — and how some metallic substances come to be permitted despite the exclusion of ground metal vessels earlier — is not fully resolved by the Gemara, and the lines of distinction remain, at minimum, not entirely transparent.

## Abraham's Reward

### Avraham's Statement

The Gemara concludes the perek with an *aggadic* statement from Rava. Because Avraham Avinu said to God, during his intercession on behalf of Sodom: *ve'anochi afar va'eifer* — "and I am dust and ashes" — expressing profound humility before the Divine, his descendants were privileged to receive two *mitzvot* that correspond to those very words: *eifer parah* — the ashes of the *parah adumah*, which purify the *tamei* — and *afar sotah* — the earth placed into the water that the *sotah* drinks.

### Why Not Kissuy

The Gemara immediately anticipates the obvious question: given that this entire perek has been devoted to *kissuy hadam* — which itself requires *afar* — why is that *mitzvah* not included in Avraham's reward?

The answer: *hasam hechsher mitzvah ika, hana leka* — there, in the cases of *parah adumah* and *sotah*, there is a genuine benefit that results from performing the *mitzvah*, in addition to the merit of the *mitzvah* itself. The *parah adumah* restores the *tamei* person to *taharah*; the *sotah* process resolves the husband's suspicion and enables the couple to reconcile — *oseh shalom bein ish l'ishto*. Thus those *mitzvot* carry both spiritual credit and concrete, tangible benefit. *Kissuy hadam*, by contrast, is a *mitzvah* whose performance does not produce any such additional benefit — the meat may be eaten regardless of whether the blood is covered. The *mitzvah* is its own reward, without a corresponding consequential benefit; and for that reason, it does not rise to the level of a reward that mirrors Avraham's humility in the same way.

The observation is striking: paradoxically, one accumulates *mitzvah* credit by performing *kissuy hadam*, yet does not receive credit of the same quality as performing *sotah*. The *sotah* process yields no *mitzvah* points for the individuals involved — it is a process of clarification and resolution, not an affirmative act of piety — yet it yields a concrete benefit. The reward of Avraham, it emerges, was specifically in the dimension of *benefit*, not merely *mitzvah performance*. This closes the perek, with the *aggadata* of the final daf to be continued.
