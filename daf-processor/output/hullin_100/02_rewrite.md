# Hullin 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daf 100 Overview

### Completion & Preview

Daf 100 of *Chullin* marks a milestone — one hundred *dapim* completed — and it also closes what has been an extended and intensive treatment of *kashrus* that will not resume until the next *perek*. The framing context throughout has been the prohibition of *gid hanasheh*: what happens when one inadvertently cooks the *gid* together with permitted meat? That question opened a much broader discussion of *ta'am* (taste), *min bemino* and *min beino mino*, and the role of sixty in nullification.

### Previous Topics Review

To situate the present *sugya*, it is worth recalling the terrain already covered. The *Mishnah* addressed how to determine whether forbidden taste has dispersed through a mixture, the distinction between *min bemino* (like and like, as when forbidden meat falls into permitted meat of the same species) and *min beino mino* (unlike items, such as forbidden fish among permitted meat), and the standard of *shishim* — sixty — as the measure by which absorbed taste is rendered insignificant. Today's *daf* concludes that discussion and then returns the *perek* to the laws of *gid hanasheh* proper.

## Bitul Exceptions

### Mishnah vs. Gemara

The *Mishnah* taught that if a *gid hanasheh* was cooked together with other *gidim* and cannot be identified, none of them may be eaten. Similarly, if a piece of *neveilah* or non-kosher fish became mixed with other pieces of permitted meat and cannot be identified, none of the mixture may be consumed. The plain implication of the *Mishnah* is that *bitul* — nullification — does not operate when the forbidden object is a discrete, independent item rather than a dispersed substance. The *Gemara*, however, pushes back forcefully on this reading.

The *Gemara*'s established principle is that *bitul* functions not merely to negate absorbed taste but to negate the thing itself. One might have been willing to concede *bitul* in cases of thorough admixture — as when blood disperses into water or wine, losing its discrete identity in a homogeneous mass — while still arguing that independent pieces retain their individual integrity and therefore cannot be nullified. After all, when one picks up a single chunk from a pile of chunks, one may be picking up the forbidden piece and nothing else. That is precisely the logic that the *Mishnah* appears to endorse.

### Yavash/Lach Types

The *Rishonim* give this distinction its technical terminology. A homogeneous mixture — whether literally liquid or reduced to a uniform mass in a blender — is called *lach belach*, wet with wet. Discrete, identifiable objects that retain their individual form are called *yavash beyavash*, dry with dry. The *Mishnah*'s apparent ruling is that *bitul* does not work for *yavash beyavash*, since each item retains its integrity.

### Three Exceptions

The *Gemara* rejects that reading entirely. It asserts that *bitul* applies even to *yavash beyavash* — even to separate, independent objects — and that the reason the *Mishnah*'s cases are forbidden is not because *bitul* is categorically inapplicable to discrete items, but because those particular cases fall under specific exceptions. The *Gemara* thereby reformulates the *Mishnah*: rather than establishing a general principle that independent objects cannot be nullified, the *Mishnah* is presenting instances of three recognized exceptions to *bitul*: *berya* (a whole, intact object), items *darko limnot* (customarily sold by count), and *chaticha harauya lehitchabed* (a piece of meat of sufficient quality to be served to honored guests).

### Berya Exception

The first exception is *berya*. The *Gemara*, at the very end of 99b, poses the question with remarkable economy: if *bitul* works even for *yavash beyavash*, then *u-livatel berubbah* — let the *gid* be nullified by a majority of other *gidim*! The answer, in two equally spare words, is *beryah shani* — a *berya* is different. The *gid hanasheh* is a whole object: the Torah's prohibition attaches to the *gid* as an entire, intact entity, and it is that wholeness which prevents it from losing its identity. It should be noted that this applies specifically when the entire *gid* is cooked — if only a segment of the *gid* were involved, it would in principle be subject to *bitul*. But a complete *gid* possesses the halachic status of a *berya*, and a *berya* cannot be negated regardless of how many permitted items surround it.

### Sold by Count

The second exception is the category of *darko limnot* — items customarily sold by count. The *Gemara* at the top of 100a turns to the case of a piece of *neveilah* mixed with other pieces of permitted meat: *v'tibatel berov* — let it be nullified by a majority! The *Gemara*'s response here is more complex and introduces a fascinating tannaitic dispute over the text of a *Mishnah* in *Orlah*.

That *Mishnah* in *Orlah* establishes that certain items are not subject to *bitul* because they are sold by number rather than by weight or volume. The logic is that items sold individually — by count — have significance as independent objects, and that significance prevents them from losing their identity. Items sold by weight or in bulk, by contrast, lack that individual significance; what matters is the quantity, not the particular object.

There is, however, a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish over how to read the operative phrase in that *Mishnah*. Does it read *et shedarko limnot shaninu* — "that which is [exclusively or primarily] sold by count" — meaning the category is limited to items for which counting is the standard mode of sale? Or does it read *kol shedarko limnot shaninu* — "anything whose way it is [even occasionally] to be sold by count"? The practical difference is considerable. A piece of meat, for example, is primarily sold by weight; one pays by the pound. Yet one can certainly imagine a butcher selling an individual rib chop by the piece. According to the broader reading — *kol shedarko limnot* — even that occasional practice suffices to give the piece individual significance. According to the narrower reading — *et shedarko limnot* — meat, which is primarily a commodity sold by weight, would not qualify.

The *Gemara* notes that the question has direct bearing on the *Mishnah*'s case of a piece of *neveilah* among permitted meat. If we follow the broader reading, meat might qualify as *darko limnot* since it is sometimes sold by piece. If we follow the narrower reading, meat does not qualify, and we need another reason to explain why the *Mishnah* forbids the entire mixture.

### Chaticha Exception

That other reason is the third exception: *chaticha harauya lehitchabed*. The *Gemara* states: *shani chaticha, ho'il v're'uyah lehitkabed bah lifnei ha'orchim* — a cut of meat is different, because it is the kind of thing one would present before honored guests. A dignified cut of meat carries a social and honorific significance that prevents it from dissolving into anonymity even when surrounded by a majority of permitted pieces. This is the *Gemara*'s answer even according to the opinion that *et shedarko limnot* — that meat does not qualify as an item primarily sold by count: its status as a fitting presentation piece provides an independent basis for retaining its identity and resisting *bitul*.

### Chaticha Parameters

The *Rishonim* engage in considerable discussion about what precisely qualifies as *chaticha harauya lehitchabed* and what falls outside that category. One question concerns the degree of processing required: does a whole side of beef qualify, even though one would not present it before guests in that form? What about an unplucked chicken — clearly unfit to serve as is, but potentially serviceable after processing? Some hold that the potential for the piece to become presentable is sufficient; others insist that the piece must be fit to serve in its current state.

A related question concerns size and form. Some *Rishonim* hold that a very small piece, or meat that has been reduced to ground meat, would fall outside this category, since neither is typically placed before an honored guest. A larger, intact cut, by contrast, would retain this status regardless of whether it happens to be an inexpensive cut. There is also a nuanced textual difference that carries halachic weight: the phrase *chaticha harauya lehitchabed* — meaning "a piece that is fitting to be honored with" — implies that not all pieces meet this standard, only certain ones. The *Rambam*'s formulation, however, treats a standard cut of meat as presumptively *rauya lehitchabed*, such that the ordinary *chaticha* of meat is assumed to fall into this category without requiring case-by-case assessment. The upshot of the *Gemara*'s analysis is this: even *yavash beyavash*, independent discrete objects, are generally subject to *bitul*. The *Mishnah*'s cases involve exceptions — *berya*, *darko limnot*, and *chaticha harauya lehitchabed* — each of which confers upon the forbidden item a significance, whether legal or social, that allows it to resist nullification.

## Four Quadrants

### Chopped Meat Example

Before examining the precise structure of the *bitul* framework, a clarifying case helps ground the analysis. Consider three separate packages of ground hamburger meat: two kosher and one *neveilah*. The packages have been inadvertently mixed together so that they are indistinguishable. Because the meat is ground, it is not *chaticha harauya lehitchabed*; because it is sold by weight, it is not *darko limnot*; and because it is not intact, it is not a *berya*. None of the three exceptions apply. The question is therefore purely one of *bitul* in the ordinary case of *yavash beyavash*.

The *Gemara*'s ruling — and the view of nearly all *Rishonim* — is that a simple *rov*, a majority, suffices in this case. One may eat all three packages. Some *Rishonim* suggest that the three packages should be eaten separately rather than combined, since eating them together would mean knowingly consuming *issur*; but even those authorities agree that one may make three separate portions and eat each in turn, applying *rov* to each one. Indeed, certain *Rishonim* explain *bitul* here in terms of probability: each time one takes a package, the odds favor its being one of the two kosher ones. But the more fundamental understanding is that when a forbidden item is lost within a majority of permitted items, the *Halachah* treats it as having lost its identity entirely — it is subsumed by the majority and ceases, legally, to exist as the forbidden object.

### Quadrant 1: Rov Enough

This case corresponds to what may be envisioned as the upper-left quadrant of a conceptual grid organizing the four possible combinations of the *yavash/lach* and *min bemino/eino mino* variables. The upper-left quadrant is *yavash beyavash* combined with *min bemino* — separate objects of the same species. Here, the items are not cooked together, so there is no dispersal of taste; and because they are of the same kind, even if cooked together there would be no perceptible alien taste. This is the most lenient quadrant: *rov* alone suffices, both biblically and rabbinically. The three packages of hamburger meat are the paradigm case.

### Quadrant 2: Shishim

The upper-right quadrant is *yavash beyavash* combined with *min beino mino* — separate objects of unlike species. The items are still discrete and not mixed together, so the immediate concern of taste dispersal does not arise. However, because they are of unlike kinds, there is a potential concern: were they to be cooked together, the unlike tastes would interact. This rabbinic concern imports a requirement of *shishim* even for the *yavash beyavash* configuration when the items are *min beino mino*.

### Quadrant 4: Taam Keikar

The lower-right quadrant — *lach belach* combined with *min beino mino* — is the most severe. Here, unlike items are fully mixed together in a liquid or homogeneous medium, and the forbidden taste disperses throughout the entire mixture. This is the biblical problem of *ta'am ke'ikar*: taste is treated as equivalent to the substance itself. One needs *shishim* to nullify that taste, and this requirement is biblical in origin.

### Quadrant 3: Shishim

The lower-left quadrant is *lach belach* combined with *min bemino* — like items cooked together in a liquid medium. In this case, since the items are of the same species, the taste of the forbidden substance is indistinguishable from the taste of the permitted ones; there is no alien flavor to detect. Nevertheless, because the mixture is a homogeneous *lach belach*, there is a theoretical concern that taste could disperse, and one cannot be certain it has not. A rabbinic requirement of *shishim* therefore applies even here. This is precisely the discussion that occupied the previous several *dapim*: even meat cooked with meat of the same type requires sixty when they are combined in a *lach belach* mixture.

The one quadrant that is fully rescued — where *rov* alone suffices and there is no *shishim* requirement, even rabbinically — is the upper-left: *yavash beyavash*, *min bemino*. That is the case of the three separate hamburger packages. They are discrete, not homogeneous; and they are the same species. The forbidden item is furthest from the paradigm of problematic taste dispersal, and thus the ordinary principle of *rov* operates without supplementation.

## Q&A on Bitul

### Cooking Together Q

A natural question arises from the *Mishnah* itself, which discusses *chaticha shel dag* — a piece of fish — cooked together with other pieces. Once items are cooked together, do not their juices exude and intermingle? The answer is yes — and indeed that is exactly what the *Mishnah* addresses. The *Mishnah* requires determining whether taste has been imparted, and if one cannot identify the offending piece, the entire mixture is forbidden. The *Gemara*'s discussion of *bitul* via *rov* pertains to a scenario in which the items are not actually cooked together — where the only problem is one of identity, not of taste dispersal. In that case, absent one of the three exceptions, *rov* suffices. But once items are cooked together in a common pot, one moves into the *lach belach* quadrants, where *shishim* applies.

### Monsey Incident

This framework has direct contemporary application. In the Monsey chicken scandal, a butcher was found to have been selling non-kosher chicken alongside kosher product. One of the halachic questions that arose was whether the pots and equipment of consumers required *kashering*. The observation was made — and credited to Rabbi Tendler's ruling — that since the butcher was simultaneously selling a large quantity of certified kosher chicken alongside the non-kosher product, the principle of *bitul* might apply to the absorbed taste in the cookware, particularly given that any taste absorbed over time is *derabbanan* in status, and a day-old absorbed taste (*nat bar nat*) weakens the stringency further. This is not the place to adjudicate that ruling in full, but it illustrates that the principles under discussion here — *bitul* of *ta'am ke'ikar*, proportionality, and the conditions of *bedieved* leniency — are operative in contemporary *psak*.

### Nine Pieces Q

One might ask whether the case of nine separate pieces of meat drawn from nine different sources — the classic case of *rov* discussed elsewhere in the *Gemara* — is conceptually superior to, inferior to, or equivalent to the three packages of hamburger meat. The answer is that the cases are at least as good as each other, and the hamburger package case may actually be stronger. In the nine-pieces case, one must consume each piece separately while consciously relying on *rov* with each selection, and one must ultimately accept that one has, in fact, consumed a piece of *treifa*. In the hamburger case, by contrast, *bitul* operates as a categorical principle: the forbidden item loses its halachic identity within the majority, and one need not reckon with having consumed *issur* at all.

### Separate vs. Mixed

This distinction highlights a conceptual difference between *rov* and *bitul*. When applying *rov* to a sequence of individual choices — as in the nine-pieces case — the logic is probabilistic: each individual selection is most likely from the permitted majority. But the halachic mechanism of *bitul* goes further. It declares that the forbidden item has ceased, as a matter of law, to exist as a forbidden object within the mixture. The permissibility is not merely probabilistic but ontological: the *issur* has been subsumed.

### Penguin Metaphor

The underlying intuition can be captured by an image: imagine a vast ice floe filled with thousands of identical penguins, save for one who is dancing and insisting on his individual identity. Lost in the crowd, that penguin's distinctiveness evaporates — he is absorbed into the collective mass, indistinguishable from any other. So too with a forbidden item in a mixture: once surrounded by a majority of permitted items with which it is indistinguishable, it loses its individual halachic identity. The exceptions — *berya*, *darko limnot*, *chaticha harauya lehitchabed* — are precisely the cases where the forbidden item is, so to speak, the dancing penguin: it possesses some intrinsic significance, legal or social, that enables it to retain its identity even within the crowd.

### Intentional Mix Q

It must be emphasized, however, that all of the above applies only after the fact — *bedieved*. One is not permitted to deliberately create a nullifying mixture in order to render a forbidden substance permissible. This is the principle of *ein mevatlin issur lechatchilah*: one may not intentionally nullify a prohibition. The cases under discussion all arise from accidental admixture; the leniency of *bitul* is a *bedieved* remedy, not a *lechatchilah* strategy.

### Tomato Sauce Q

A practical illustration: suppose one pours tomato sauce into a stew and then discovers that the sauce contains meat as an ingredient and bears no *hechsher*. One calls the rabbi to ask whether the stew is permissible. The rabbi's analysis will center on proportion — how much non-kosher meat was in the sauce relative to the total volume of the stew — and whether the amount of forbidden substance is sufficient to impart taste. If the proportion is small enough, *bitul* applies and the stew is permitted *bedieved*. The consumer may find this counterintuitive — after all, one is effectively eating something that contains *treifa* — but that is precisely the *chiddush* of *bitul*: the *Halachah* declares the forbidden substance to have lost its identity, and therefore one is no longer, in any halachic sense, eating *treifa*.

### Pork & Beans Q

A further question concerns the mechanics of taste transfer without heat. If, for example, one were to serve foods containing non-kosher ingredients on a plate alongside kosher items, but without any heat involved, does taste transfer from one to the other, or from either into the plate? The governing principle is that taste transfers halachically only through heat: *kavush*, soaking, and *irui*, pouring of hot liquid, are their own discussions, but simple adjacency of cold foods does not cause halachic taste transfer. The plate and the kosher items on it would therefore not be rendered non-kosher merely by proximity, absent the application of heat.

## Chatichah Natah

### Raba's Principle

Continuing on the case of *chaticha shel neveilah*, the *Gemara* records an exposition by Raba bar bar Chana: a piece of *neveilah* or non-kosher fish does not render the rest of the pot forbidden until it has imparted taste into the sauce (*rotev*), the residue (*kippa*), and the other pieces together. This reaffirms the earlier point that the measure of *shishim* applies against everything in the pot collectively — the liquid, the vegetables, the sediment at the bottom — and only if the total quantity is insufficient does the mixture become forbidden.

Raba then wished to qualify and sharpen this ruling in a specific case, and had his *amora* — his designated announcer, whose function was to project the *Amora*'s words to the assembled public in the beit midrash, much as the *tanna* (with a lowercase *tav*) served as a living repository and oral transmitter of tannaitic traditions — stand over him and proclaim the following: *keivan she-natan ta'am be-chaticha, chaticha atzma na'aseit neveilah ve-oseret kol ha-chatichot kulam mipnei she-hen minah* — once the non-kosher piece has imparted its taste into one permitted piece, that piece itself becomes *neveilah*, and it in turn renders all the other pieces forbidden, because they are all of the same kind (*min bemino*) and, according to Rav Yehuda, like-and-like is not nullified.

The case Raba has in mind involves two stages: a piece of non-kosher meat — call it N — is first cooked together with a single piece of kosher meat (piece 1), and only afterward are both placed into the larger stew. In the first stage, N imparts its taste to piece 1. The question is what the halachic status of piece 1 now is when it enters the stew.

### Chanan Term

This principle is of such cardinal importance in the laws of *kashrus* that it has acquired its own shorthand in *beit midrash* parlance: *chanan*, an acronym for *chatichah atzmah na'aseit neveilah* (or *chatichah natah neveilah* in some formulations). When students and *poskim* speak of *chanan*, this is the concept: a piece of permitted food that has absorbed the taste of a forbidden substance does not merely become a carrier for that taste — rather, its entire halachic identity is transformed. It becomes, in law, a new unit of *neveilah* in its own right. Piece 1 is no longer simply a kosher piece with traces of forbidden taste; it has become N-prime — a second unit of *neveilah*.

The contrast with *ta'am ke'ikar* is instructive. *Ta'am ke'ikar* establishes that taste is treated as the substance itself, so that absorbed taste renders a vessel or food forbidden. *Chanan* goes a step further: it is not that piece 1 merely carries a trace of forbidden substance, but that piece 1 itself has become forbidden as an independent unit — with the full weight of *neveilah*.

### Need 120 Now

The practical consequence is significant. When N and N-prime are both placed into the stew, one does not need sixty times N alone. One needs sixty times the combined volume of N and N-prime — that is, 120 rather than 60. The forbidden presence in the pot is now two units, not one, and the *bitul* calculation must account for both.

### Rav Safra Q

This generates an immediate objection from Rav Safra to Abaye: *Rav k'man amrah lishmatei* — whose position is Rava following? Evidently Rav Yehuda's, since the ruling that piece 1's becoming N-prime renders all the other pieces forbidden assumes that *min bemino lo batel* — like-and-like is never nullified, which is Rav Yehuda's position. But if that is so, *mai irya ki natan ta'am* — why does any of this depend on whether N first cooked together with piece 1? Even without that preliminary stage, if N falls directly into the stew surrounded by other pieces of meat, Rav Yehuda would say none of them can nullify it — *min bemino lo batel*. The entire exercise of piece 1 becoming N-prime seems superfluous.

### Answer: Original Removed

The *Gemara* answers: *hacha b'mai askinan b'she-kadam v'silko* — the case is one in which the original N was removed from the pot before the stew was assembled. After N cooked with piece 1 and imparted its taste, N itself was discarded. Only N-prime — piece 1, now transformed — was placed into the stew with the other pieces of kosher meat. In this scenario, if piece 1 were merely a carrier for N's taste, one might reason as follows: N is gone; what remains is only absorbed taste, which might be nullified by the surrounding meat and liquid. But because piece 1 has become N-prime — a full unit of *neveilah* — and because the surrounding items are all of the same species (meat), Rav Yehuda's *min bemino lo batel* prevents any nullification. The case for *chanan* is therefore precisely the scenario of preliminary cooking followed by removal of the original forbidden item: without *chanan*, there would be a basis for leniency; with *chanan*, the transformed piece is treated as a full unit of *neveilah* and cannot be nullified by like-kind items.

## Chatichah Natah (II)

### Rava's Formulation

Rava offers a further formulation of the same principle that applies even without the removal of the original N. Even when N remains in the pot alongside the other pieces of meat and the vegetables and liquid, the presence of N-prime matters. According to Rav Yehuda, the pieces of kosher meat are irrelevant to the nullification calculus — *min bemino lo batel* renders them halachically invisible for purposes of negating the *issur*. What remains to negate N and N-prime are the *eino mino* elements: the potatoes, carrots, liquid, and other non-meat components. Rava's point is that once N has transformed piece 1 into N-prime, you now have two units requiring negation by the *eino mino* components, not one. If you had exactly 60 units of potatoes and liquid, that would suffice against N alone but not against N and N-prime together. You now need the *eino mino* components to equal 120, not 60.

### Ashkenazi/Sefardi

Whether this ruling is normative is itself the subject of a major dispute among *Rishonim* and between the Ashkenazi and Sefardi traditions. The *Tosafot* occupy considerable space with the position of Rabbeinu Ephraim and others who hold, for cogent reasons, that this *Gemara* is not the operative ruling — at least outside the context of *basar b'chalav*, where a separate *Gemara* establishes *chanan* independently and more clearly. The practical upshot is a *machlokes* between *Sefardim* and *Ashkenazim*: *Sefardim* are lenient and do not rule in accordance with this *Gemara* in the *neveilah* context; they require only sixty times the original trace amounts of forbidden substance. *Ashkenazim* rule in accordance with this *Gemara* and hold that if the forbidden substance first imparted taste into a separate piece before both entered the pot, one requires sixty times the volume of the combined units — 120 rather than 60.

### Liquid Application

This ruling generates highly practical questions when the forbidden substance is not a solid piece of meat but a liquid product. Suppose a non-kosher canned tomato sauce — which itself contains meat as an ingredient — is poured into a stew. If the meat in the tomato sauce was already *batel* within the can before it ever reached one's kitchen, then even *Ashkenazim* agree: the *chanan* principle does not reactivate a *bitul* that has already taken effect. The question is when the meat in the sauce was not *batel* in the can — for instance, if the non-kosher meat constituted more than 1/60th of the sauce. In that case, according to *Ashkenazim*, one may need to treat the tomato sauce itself as the forbidden object: the relevant question is not merely whether the trace amounts of non-kosher meat are less than 1/60th of the stew, but whether the entire tomato sauce, having absorbed the forbidden taste, must now be treated as N-prime. Whether a liquid product functions as a *chaticha* for purposes of *chanan* — since *chanan* classically applies to a discrete solid piece — is itself disputed, and the parameters are worked out in the *poskim*. But the operative question in any *sha'ilah* involving a non-kosher product mixed into another dish is precisely this: does *chanan* apply, or does one reckon only with the original trace amounts of forbidden substance?

## Gid in Kosher

### Mishnah Rule

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah* governing the laws of *gid hanasheh* proper. The *Mishnah* states: *noheig bitehorah ve'eino noheig bitmeiah* — the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* applies to kosher animals and does not apply to non-kosher animals. The intuitive rationale is that the Torah's prohibition is directed at the *gid* of an animal one is otherwise permitted to eat: the Torah allows the animal, permits its flesh, but reserves the *gid*. If the animal itself is *tamei* — non-kosher — the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* adds nothing meaningful, since one may not eat any part of it in any case.

A related question lurks in the background: does the *gid* itself have taste — *yesh bigidim binoten ta'am* — or is it tasteless? This matters for the question of whether eating the *gid* of a non-kosher animal triggers any prohibition at all. If the *gid* has no taste, one who eats the *gid* of a camel has not violated the prohibition of eating camel, since the *gid* contributes nothing in terms of flavor; and since *gid hanasheh* does not apply to non-kosher animals, one would violate no prohibition whatsoever — just as eating a hoof or horn of a camel violates nothing. If, however, the *gid* does have taste, one who eats it has consumed camel flesh, triggering one set of lashes, while remaining exempt from a second set since the *gid* prohibition does not, according to the *tanna kamma*, extend to non-kosher animals.

### Tanna Kamma/RY

The *tanna kamma* holds that *gid hanasheh* applies exclusively to kosher animals. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *af bitmeiah* — it applies to non-kosher animals as well.

### RY's Argument

Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning: *vahalo mivnei Yaakov ne'esar gid hanasheh ve'adayin behemah tmeiah muteres lahen* — was it not to the children of Yaakov that the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* was given? And at that time, non-kosher animals were still permitted to them. The children of Yaakov were not yet commanded regarding the broader dietary laws that would distinguish kosher from non-kosher animals. Therefore, when the Torah said "do not eat the *gid hanasheh*," it was necessarily speaking of all animals — including those that would later be classified as non-kosher — since at the time there was no distinction. The prohibition therefore extends to non-kosher animals even after the categories of *tahor* and *tamei* were established.

### Har Sinai Response

The *chachamim* respond: *besinai ne'emar* — that verse, *al ken lo yochlu bnei Yisrael*, was given at Har Sinai, not in the time of Yaakov. When Moshe wrote the *Sefer Torah*, he embedded the verse in the narrative context of Yaakov's encounter, *ela shenichtav bimkomo* — because the divine instruction was to record it in its narrative location. But the *mitzvah* itself was commanded to the Jewish people as a nation, at Har Sinai. And *bnei Yisrael* in that verse means the children of Israel as a people — Israelites — not the literal sons of Yaakov the patriarch. Consequently, one cannot draw conclusions about the scope of the prohibition based on what was or was not forbidden in Yaakov's time.

This position is undergirded by an important principle articulated by the *Rambam* in his *Peirush Hamishnayot*: everything that Jews observe today as obligatory has its source in Matan Torah and not in any earlier precedent. The fact that Avraham performed *bris milah* is not the basis for our obligation; the fact that Yaakov's sons abstained from *gid hanasheh* is not the basis for ours. We are commanded as a people who stood at Har Sinai — whatever preceded that event is historically and spiritually significant but is not the source of our halachic obligations.

### Noahide Q

This principle raises a corollary theological question: are Jews also included in the category of *Bnei Noach* and thereby bound by the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* as a separate basis of obligation? The *Rambam*'s answer is clear: no. The reason Jews observe prohibitions such as *eiver min hachai* — tearing a limb from a living animal — is not because they are members of humanity commanded as *Bnei Noach*, but because they were specifically commanded at Har Sinai. The two obligations run in parallel — both *Bnei Noach* and Jews are forbidden to eat *eiver min hachai* — but the source of each group's obligation is distinct. Jews observe what they observe because of their covenant at Sinai, not because of any Noahide framework.

## Multiple Issurim

### Ein Issur Chal

The *Gemara* now examines Rabbi Yehuda's position through the lens of a technical halachic principle: *ein issur chal al issur* — one prohibition cannot take effect upon an object that is already subject to another prohibition. The *Gemara* notes that this principle ought to generate a structural problem for Rabbi Yehuda's view. The question is not merely whether logically the Torah's prohibition of *gid hanasheh* was intended to encompass non-kosher animals, but whether the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* can even take effect on an animal that is already forbidden as a non-kosher species.

### Gid on Camel

Applied specifically: a camel is forbidden as a *tamei* animal. When does the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* take effect? If it takes effect after the prohibition of *tamei* animal is already operative, then *ein issur chal al issur* would seem to block it. One prohibition cannot layer on top of another. How then can Rabbi Yehuda hold that eating a camel's *gid* carries two separate sets of lashes — one for *tamei* and one for *gid hanasheh*?

### No Taste Answer

One proposed answer draws on the question of whether *gidin* have taste. If the *gid* has no taste — *ein bigidin binoten ta'am* — then a person who eats the *gid* of a camel has not technically consumed camel, since tasteless matter is not considered consumed flesh for halachic purposes. In that case, there is no competition between the two prohibitions: the *tamei* prohibition has not been violated (since the tasteless *gid* is not camel flesh), while the *gid hanasheh* prohibition could independently apply. The two prohibitions would occupy distinct domains and would not interfere with one another.

### Refutation: Two Lashes

This proposal is immediately refuted. A *beraita* explicitly records Rabbi Yehuda as ruling that one who eats the *gid hanasheh* of a non-kosher animal — a camel — *mechayev shtayim*, is liable for two sets of lashes: one for eating camel and one for eating *gid hanasheh*. This demonstrates unambiguously that Rabbi Yehuda holds *yesh bigidin binoten ta'am* — the *gid* does have taste and it does constitute a violation of the prohibition on eating camel. The neat resolution through tastelessness is therefore unavailable. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds *poter* — one is entirely exempt — which may reflect either the view that the *gid* is tasteless, or a different analysis of the overlapping prohibitions. But Rabbi Yehuda clearly holds both that the *gid* has taste and that both prohibitions apply simultaneously.

### In Utero Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves the structural problem differently: *kasavar noheig bishilya* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* takes effect already *in utero*, as the fetus forms. In that case, the prohibitions do not arise sequentially — with the *tamei* prohibition established first and the *gid* prohibition attempting to layer on top afterward. Rather, both come into being simultaneously, at the moment the fetus begins to form. If both prohibitions arise at the same moment, *ein issur chal al issur* is not triggered, since that principle applies only when one prohibition precedes the other chronologically.

### Sequential Prohibition

The *Gemara* immediately presses this resolution. A *Mishnah* about *nezirut* is adduced: a *nazir* becomes *tamei* upon contact with a corpse, even one composed of less than a *k'zayis*, if it is an early miscarriage — *shelo nitkashru eivarav begidin*, meaning that the sinews of the fetus had not yet formed. The implication is clear: sinews form later in fetal development, after the basic structure of the fetus has already been established. Therefore, even if the *gid hanasheh* prohibition applies *in utero*, it cannot arise simultaneously with the *tamei*-animal prohibition. The fetus exists first, and is forbidden as a camel from the moment it begins to form in the womb; only weeks later do the sinews form, at which point the *gid hanasheh* prohibition would theoretically come into being. The prohibition of *tamei* therefore precedes the prohibition of *gid hanasheh*, and the problem of *ein issur chal al issur* returns.

### Override via Weight

The *Gemara*'s final resolution: *af al gav de'issur tumah kadim, asi issur gid chayal alai* — although the prohibition of *tamei* animal precedes the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* in time, the *gid hanasheh* prohibition nonetheless takes effect on top of it. The basis is that the *gid hanasheh* prohibition is in a certain sense weightier: *shekein issur noheig bivnei Noach* — its reach extends even to the period before Matan Torah, having been operative for the children of Yaakov before the Torah was given at Sinai. When a later prohibition is more encompassing or more weighty than an earlier one, it can take effect even upon an object already subject to that earlier prohibition. The *Gemara* notes that this reading is consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's own formulation in the *Mishnah*, where he invokes the children of Yaakov as the basis for the prohibition's scope: it is that very pre-Sinaitic antiquity and breadth that gives *gid hanasheh* the halachic weight to override the principle of *ein issur chal al issur* and take effect upon an already-forbidden camel.
