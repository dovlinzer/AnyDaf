# Nazir 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chutz Laretz Dispute

### Intro: Long Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁנָּזַר נְזִירוּת הַרְבֵּה, וְהִשְׁלִים אֶת נְזִירוּתוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּא לָאָרֶץ, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּבֵית הִילֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר בַּתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who vowed many** days of **naziriteship** while outside Eretz Yisrael, **and completed his naziriteship, and afterward came to Eretz** Yisrael, in order to bring the offerings at the end of his naziriteship, **Beit Shammai say:** He must be **a nazirite** for **thirty days,** so that he has observed a term of naziriteship in ritual purity in Eretz Yisrael, **and Beit Hillel say:** He is **a nazirite from the beginning,** that is, he must observe his entire naziriteship again.

The *Mishnah* at the bottom of 19b presents the case of someone who accepted an extended *nezirut* and subsequently arrived in *Eretz Yisrael*. The juxtaposition is deliberate and significant: the preceding *Mishnah* dealt with someone who accepted *nezirut* in a *beit hakevarot*, who then begins a *nezirut de-tahara* upon leaving the cemetery. Here, by contrast, the *tuma* of *Chutz La'aretz* is rabbinic in character, and no one seriously argues that it *soter* — interrupts — the *nezirut* outright. Nevertheless, there is a symbolic dimension: one arrives in *Eretz Yisrael*, the place where the *korbanot* of *nezirut* must ultimately be brought, emerging from what had been deemed an *eretz tmei'ah*. The question is how to respond halachically to that transition.

Beit Shammai holds that the *nazir* must observe an additional symbolic thirty days of *nezirut* upon entering *Eretz Yisrael*. Beit Hillel goes further: it is as though the *nazir* is transitioning from *tuma* to *tahara*, and must now observe the full original duration of the *nezirut* anew — not bringing a *korban* at the midpoint, since the first *nezirut* has not yet concluded, but extending the period so that only at the end of the combined duration does one bring the *korban*.

### Gussha vs Avira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר בַּתְּחִלָּה. לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: אֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים מִשּׁוּם גּוּשָׁהּ גָּזְרוּ עָלֶיהָ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא גָּזְרוּ עָלֶיהָ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The first clause** of the mishna **teaches** that **Beit Shammai say:** He must be **a nazirite** for **thirty days, and Beit Hillel say:** He is **a nazirite from the beginning.** The Gemara suggests a possible explanation of their dispute: **Let us say that they disagree about this,** that **Beit Shammai hold** that when the Sages declared that the **land of the nations** outside of Eretz Yisrael is impure, **they decreed** so **with regard to its earth.** In other words, they decreed that only the earth of the land of the nations is impure, but its airspace remains pure. **and Beit Hillel hold: They decreed with regard to its airspace,** and it is a severe level of ritual impurity, so he must start his naziriteship from the beginning once he arrives in Eretz Yisrael.

The *Gemara* opens by asking what underlies this dispute. *Lema b'ha kamipalgi* — what is the substantive disagreement that produces these two different results?

The *Gemara* proposes that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel differ on the precise nature of the rabbinic decree rendering *Chutz La'aretz tamei*. Beit Shammai holds *mishum gusha gazru aleha* — the decree was directed at the *gusha*, the soil or dirt of *eretz ammim*. The concern is the ground itself, which may contain buried bodies. Beit Hillel holds *mishum avira gazru aleha* — the decree extended even to the very air of *Chutz La'aretz*.

The practical distinction: if someone enters *eretz ammim* suspended above the ground — carried in a box, for instance, without touching the soil — then according to Beit Shammai, who treats only the *gusha* as *tamei*, that person remains *tahor*. According to Beit Hillel, even the *avira* of *Chutz La'aretz* conveys *tuma*, and such a person would be *tamei* regardless.

One might ask how this differs from ordinary *tumat ohel*. Two answers emerge. First, non-Jews may not generate *tumat ohel* even if they are buried there, in which case the concern would only arise through direct contact with the ground. Second, even if *tumat ohel* were a factor, it could theoretically be avoided by certain configurations — a person suspended in a box would, under normal *tumat ohel* logic, be protected from the spread of *tuma* rising through an enclosure — whereas the *avira* decree of Beit Hillel admits of no such escape.

How does this map onto the *machlokes* about the duration of the additional *nezirut*? The most natural reading is one of proportionality: Beit Shammai, who limits the decree to the *gusha* and thus treats the *tuma* of *Chutz La'aretz* with relative restraint, requires only a symbolic thirty days in response. Beit Hillel, who extends the decree even to the air and thus treats the *tuma* with greater weight, requires the *nazir* to redo the entire period, since they afford no credit for *nezirut* observed in so heavily *tamei* an environment.

### Tosafot Girsa

Tosafot, however, is dissatisfied with this reading. Tosafot presses: even if Beit Shammai limits the decree to the *gusha*, the *tuma* is still treated as a form of *tumat meit* — rabbinic, yes, but real. In the case of a *beit hakevarot*, the *nazir* must redo everything upon leaving, even though *tumat ohel* there also does not apply in the identical manner to the air. So why should Beit Shammai's position of only thirty days follow from treating the *gusha* as *tamei*? The analogy to a cemetery, where even a *tuma* of relatively defined scope requires full restart, seems to tell against Beit Shammai's leniency. This is what Tosafot phrases as *d'mai ta'ama d'Beit Shammai* — the logic of Beit Shammai remains unexplained under the standard *girsa*.

Tosafot therefore proposes a reversed *girsa*: Beit Shammai is the one who holds *mishum avira gazru* — that even the air of *Chutz La'aretz* is *tamei* — and Beit Hillel holds *mishum gusha gazru* — only the ground. This reversal is, admittedly, counterintuitive, since one generally expects Beit Shammai to be the stricter school. But Tosafot's logic is as follows: if Beit Shammai extends the decree so far as to declare even the *avira tamei*, it becomes obvious to all observers that this is a purely rabbinic construct — no *tuma* from the Torah is contracted merely by breathing the air of a place. Precisely because everyone recognizes the decree as rabbinic and artificial in character, Beit Shammai requires only thirty symbolic days, so that the rabbinic *tuma* of *Chutz La'aretz* not be confused with a genuine biblical *tumat meit*. Beit Hillel, by contrast, limits the decree to the *gusha*, which mimics the more familiar pattern of *tumat kever* — actual ground containing bodies. Because the decree resembles biblical *tuma* in its parameters, Beit Hillel requires the *nazir* to treat it with full seriousness and redo the entire period.

So Tosafot presents two coherent but opposing frameworks. In the standard *girsa*: the more one extends the reach of the decree (Beit Hillel, *avira*), the more one demands of the *nazir* when leaving *Chutz La'aretz*, since the weight assigned to that *tuma* is greater. In Tosafot's reversed *girsa*: the more one extends the reach of the decree (Beit Shammai, *avira*), the more clearly everyone understands it as a rabbinic fiction, and therefore a lighter response suffices. It is worth noting that Tosafot elsewhere criticizes those who emend a *girsa* on the basis of *svara* alone — and yet Tosafot here does precisely that, which is not without irony.

### Gemara Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִשּׁוּם גּוּשָׁהּ גָּזְרוּ עָלֶיהָ, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי בִּסְתָם נְזִירוּת קָנֵיסְנָא, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: כִּי קָנֵיסְנָא — בִּתְחִילַּת נְזִירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** No, **it may be that everyone** agrees that **they decreed** only **with regard to its earth, and** they disagree merely over the details of the penalty. **Beit Shammai hold** that **we penalize** him **with an unspecified term of naziriteship,** which is thirty days, **and Beit Hillel hold** that **when we penalize,** we require him to return **to the beginning of** his term of **naziriteship,** and he must observe the entire term of naziriteship afresh.

The *Gemara* then steps back from the *gusha/avira* framework entirely and suggests an alternative: *lo, d'chulei alma mishum gusha gazru* — in fact, everyone would agree that the original decree was directed at the *gusha* and that *Chutz La'aretz* functions as a standard rabbinic *tuma* analogous to *tumat meit*. The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel is not about the nature of the decree but about what response to demand of a *nazir* who observed *nezirut* in *Chutz La'aretz*. Beit Shammai holds *b'stam nezirut kansinan* — we impose a single standard *nezirut* as a fine, a symbolic penalty. Beit Hillel holds *k'techilat nezirut kansinan* — we treat it as though the *nezirut* never began and impose the full original duration again.

This second explanation is, as the lecturer notes, largely descriptive rather than explanatory. It tells us what each school requires without illuminating the underlying reasoning. But it at least establishes that the *machlokes* is not fundamentally about the nature of *tumas Chutz La'aretz* but rather about the appropriate rabbinic response to one who has observed *nezirut* in that condition.

### Queen Helena

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּהֵילֵנִי הַמַּלְכָּה שֶׁהָלַךְ בְּנָהּ לְמִלְחָמָה, וְאָמְרָה: אִם יָבוֹא בְּנִי מִן הַמִּלְחָמָה בְּשָׁלוֹם — אֱהֵא נְזִירָה שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים. וּבָא בְּנָהּ מִן הַמִּלְחָמָה, וְהָיְתָה נְזִירָה שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים, וּבְסוֹף שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים עָלְתָה לָאָרֶץ, וְהוֹרוּהָ בֵּית הִלֵּל שֶׁתְּהֵא נְזִירָה עוֹד שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים אֲחֵרוֹת. וּבְסוֹף שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים נִטְמֵאת. וְנִמְצֵאת נְזִירָה עֶשְׂרִים וְאַחַת שָׁנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא הָיְתָה נְזִירָה אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites a related story: **An incident** occurred **with regard to Queen Helene, whose son had gone to war, and she said: If my son will return from war safely, I will be a nazirite** for **seven years. And her son returned** safely **from the war, and she was a nazirite** for **seven years. And at the end of seven years, she ascended to Eretz** Yisrael, **and Beit Hillel instructed her,** in accordance with their opinion, **that she should be a nazirite for an additional seven years. And at the end of** those **seven years she became ritually impure,** and was therefore required to observe yet another seven years of naziriteship, as ritual impurity negates the tally of a nazirite. **And she was found to be a nazirite** for **twenty-one years. Rabbi Yehuda said: She was a nazirite** for **only fourteen years** and not twenty-one.

To probe the practical application of the dispute, the *Gemara* introduces the story of Queen Helena (*Helni HaMalka*). She had been a *nezira* for seven years while in *Chutz La'aretz*, then came to *Eretz Yisrael*. Beit Hillel instructed her to observe an additional seven years. Before that second period concluded, she became *tamei*, which required yet another seven-year cycle, yielding twenty-one years in total. Rabbi Yehuda, however, says she was a *nezira* for only fourteen years.

The *Gemara* asks: how do we get to fourteen? There are two possibilities. Either Rabbi Yehuda holds like Beit Shammai — that upon entering *Eretz Yisrael* one adds only thirty days rather than seven years — and then she became *tamei* before the end, requiring another seven years, producing seven plus thirty days plus seven, which does not yield a clean fourteen. Or Rabbi Yehuda holds like Beit Hillel — seven plus seven — but disputes the claim that she became *tamei* at all.

The *Gemara* demonstrates that the first option is untenable. If Rabbi Yehuda held like Beit Shammai and she did become *tamei*, he would have said fourteen years and thirty days, not simply fourteen years. The round number of fourteen points to a reading where the thirty days play no role — which means he holds like Beit Hillel but disagrees that she became *tamei* during the second period.

A *baraita* adds the view of Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Eliezer, who derives from the verse *zot torat hanazir* that if a *nazir* becomes *tamei* on the very day the *nezirut* was to be completed, the full law of *nezirut* restarts. This principle, applied to the final-day scenario, would yield yet another inconsistency with the twenty-one year count, reinforcing that Rabbi Yehuda's position denies that she became *tamei* during the second period at all.

## Two Pairs Witnesses

### Conflicting Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים מְעִידוֹת אוֹתוֹ, אֵלּוּ מְעִידִים שֶׁנָּזַר שְׁתַּיִם, וְאֵלּוּ מְעִידִים שֶׁנָּזַר חָמֵשׁ, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נֶחְלְקָה הָעֵדוּת — וְאֵין כָּאן נְזִירוּת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ בִּכְלַל חָמֵשׁ שְׁתַּיִם, שֶׁיְּהֵא נָזִיר שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who had two sets of witnesses testifying about him** that he had taken a vow of naziriteship for a certain period, and **these** witnesses **testify that he took a vow of naziriteship** for **two** terms, **and these** witnesses **testify that he took a vow of naziriteship** for **five** terms. **Beit Shammai say: The testimony is divided,** i.e., the testimonies contradict each other, and since the testimonies are in conflict they are both rejected entirely **and there is no naziriteship here** at all. **And Beit Hillel say:** The testimonies are not completely in conflict with each other, as **two** terms **are included in five** terms, and the unanimous testimony, **that he is a nazirite** for **two** terms, is accepted.

The next *Mishnah* introduces a case in which two sets of witnesses offer conflicting testimony about the duration of a man's *nezirut*. One pair testifies that he vowed to be a *nazir* for two years; the other pair testifies that he vowed for five years. The question is what the *beit din* does with this contradictory testimony.

Before turning to the dispute itself, a preliminary observation is worth making. The introduction of witnesses into *nezirut* is striking. Until this point, the entire tractate has been occupied with the *nazir*'s personal relationship to his vow — the precise words he used, his inner intent, the possibility of seeking *hatarah* from a *chakham*. Now the *Gemara* establishes that there is an external juridical dimension: *beit din* has a stake in determining whether someone is a *nazir*, and witnesses can impose that status even against a person's own denial, at least with respect to consequences like *malkot* for violating the prohibitions of *nezirut*.

This raises a genuine question about the relationship between *nezirut* and *nedarim* in the domain of communal enforcement. One might argue that since *nezirut* involves ongoing and publicly observable prohibitions whose violation carries lashes, it falls more squarely within *beit din*'s purview than an ordinary *neder*, even though *neder* violations can in principle also generate lashes.

### Overlap Theory

Beit Shammai holds *nechleka ha'edut* — the testimony is divided and contradictory, and therefore neither pair has established anything. The result is that no *nezirut* is binding at all.

Beit Hillel holds *yesh bichlal chamesh shtayim* — within the claim of five is the claim of two. The pair testifying to five years is not contradicting the pair testifying to two years; they are saying something larger that subsumes what the other pair says. Therefore, on the point of two years, both sets of witnesses are in agreement. The *nazir* must observe two periods of *nezirut*.

Tosafot raises the question of why the subject does not simply admit the vow by his silence when the first pair testifies, given the principle of *shtika kehoda'ah* — silence as admission. Tosafot resolves this with two possible scenarios: either the subject actively denies everything, or both pairs came before the *beit din* simultaneously, in which case the internal contradiction between them means the subject has no reason to respond.

There is also a question about intention: since so much of the law of *nedarim* and *nezirut* concerns the alignment of *piv velibo* — one's words and one's heart — one might wonder whether a *beit din* may compel *nezirut* based on testimony if the subject claims he did not mean the words. The *Gemara* does not address this directly, but the implicit logic of the *Mishnah* seems to be that once witnesses have testified to the words and there is no overriding context that would redefine them, the external court proceeds on the basis of the statement as made.

### Rabbi Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת שְׁתַּיִם, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת חָמֵשׁ, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בִּכְלַל חָמֵשׁ שְׁתַּיִם. עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל כַּת אַחַת, אֶחָד אוֹמֵר שְׁתַּיִם, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר חָמֵשׁ, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נֶחְלְקָה עֵדוּתָן. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ בִּכְלַל חָמֵשׁ שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to** the *halakha* in the case of **two sets of witnesses, one** of whom **says two** terms **and one** of whom **says five** terms, **that two** terms **are included in five** terms, and he must observe two terms of naziriteship. **With regard to what did they disagree?** They disagreed **with regard to one set** of two witnesses, **one** of whom **says two** terms **and one** of whom **says five** terms, and **that Beit Shammai say:** In that case **their testimony is divided,** as the two witnesses are in conflict and there is no testimony here at all. **And Beit Hillel say:** Since **two** terms **are included in five** terms, their testimony is in agreement with regard to two terms, and therefore he must observe two terms.

The *Gemara* notes that the *Mishnah*'s framing of the dispute — as between two competing pairs of witnesses — is not universally accepted. A *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka states that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not actually disagree in the case of two separate pairs of witnesses. In that case, everyone would agree with the principle of *yesh bichlal chamesh shtayim* — the overlap is clear enough, and the *nazir* must observe two periods. The genuine dispute, Rabbi Yishmael argues, concerned a single pair of witnesses whose members contradict each other internally — one witness says two years and the other witness says five years.

In that scenario, Beit Shammai says *nechleka ha'edut* — the testimony is divided, and a pair whose members cannot agree on what they witnessed cannot stand as valid *edut*. Beit Hillel says even here *yesh bichlal chamesh shtayim* applies: the witness who says five implicitly includes the claim of two, so the two witnesses share common ground on the two-year period and combine to form valid testimony for that duration.

This reframing is illuminating. In the case of two separate pairs, the intuitive force of the overlap argument is relatively strong: it is hard to say that a pair testifying to five years *contradicts* a pair testifying to two, since five is simply a larger claim. The disagreement is about whether two pairs saying different things can generate a combined testimony — a higher standard, but not an inherently unreasonable one. In the case of a single internally divided pair, however, the question is sharper: to what extent can two witnesses who disagree with each other be said to be testifying to the same thing and thus join to create unified *edut*? That is where the real debate lies, and Rabbi Yishmael's *baraita* locates the dispute there.

### Discrete Units

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּמוֹנֶה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָמָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי קָאָמַר? אִילֵּימָא: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר חָמֵשׁ וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר שְׁתַּיִם וְלֹא חָמֵשׁ — הָא קָא מַכְחֲשִׁי אַהֲדָדֵי! אֶלָּא: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר אַחַת וּשְׁתַּיִם, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ אַרְבַּע חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** In relation to the explanation that the dispute is with regard to a single pair of witnesses, **Rav said: All concede in** a case of **counting** that the testimonies contradict each other. **Rav Ḥama said to Rav Ḥisda: What is he saying;** what did Rav mean by this? **If we say that** he was referring to a case where **one** witness **says:** He vowed **five** terms **and not two** terms, **and one says:** He vowed **two** terms **and not five** terms, in such a situation **they** clearly **contradict one another,** and neither of the testimonies is reliable. **Rather,** Rav is speaking of **one** witness who **says** that he heard the person in question count out only **one and two** terms when he took a vow of naziriteship, **and one says** that he heard the person in question count out only **three, four,** and **five** terms when he took a vow of naziriteship.

Rav introduces an important qualification: *hakol modim b'moneh* — everyone agrees in the case where the witnesses are counting discrete units. If the first pair testified that the subject said "I will be a *nazir* one time and two times," and the second pair testified that he said "three times, four times, and five times," there is no genuine contradiction between them, and everyone would agree that the testimonies combine. The distinction turns on how the *nezirut* is described. When the subject is reported to have declared a single block — "two years" or "five years" — the two testimonies describe conceptually different units, and the question of overlap is genuinely contested. When the subject is reported to have declared individual discrete units one by one, the testimonies address individual instances and can be seen to agree on the specific units each covers.

Tosafot explicates the case of *moneh* as follows: the first pair said he declared one time and two times; the second pair said he declared three times, four times, and five times. Alternatively, one could construct the case so that the overlap is even more literal — the first pair reports one and two, the second reports one through five — but either way, when the claims are atomized into individual units, the logic of combination is considerably more intuitive than when they are expressed as aggregates.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לְמָה לִי? הַשְׁתָּא יֵשׁ לוֹמַר: חֲמִירְתָּא אָמַר רַב, קִילְּתָא לָא אָמַר?! אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: אֵין הַכְחָשָׁה בְּמוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need for Rav to state **this** at all? **Now that it is** already **said** with regard to **a stringent** case, where there were two sets of witnesses who generally have full credibility yet **Rav said** that the testimony is considered divided, with regard to **a lenient** case, involving a conflict within one pair of witnesses, who cannot each obligate him in naziriteship, would he **not say** that a conflict in their testimony causes it to be disregarded? Of course the testimonies are disregarded if the conflict is within the same set of witnesses. **They say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: **There is no conflicting** testimony **in** a case of **counting,** as since one of them counted a smaller number and the second counted a larger number, they do not conflict at all, as the smaller number is included in the larger one.

The *Gemara*'s exchange between Rav Chama and Rav Chisda clarifies the precise scope of the *moneh* principle. If one witness says five and explicitly not two — that is, he positively excludes the lower number — then the testimonies actively contradict each other and cannot combine. The *moneh* principle applies only where the witnesses enumerate units sequentially and no such exclusion is implied.

## Va'ani Declarations

### Cascading Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״ וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי״, ״וַאֲנִי״ — כּוּלָּם נְזִירִים. הוּתַּר הָרִאשׁוֹן — הוּתְּרוּ כּוּלָּן. הוּתַּר הָאַחֲרוֹן — הָאַחֲרוֹן מוּתָּר וְכוּלָּם אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who said: I am hereby a nazirite, and another heard** this vow **and said: And I,** and a third person added: **And I, they are all nazirites.** If the vow of **the first was dissolved** by a halakhic authority, **they are all dissolved.** However, if the vow of **the last** individual **was dissolved** by a halakhic authority, the vow of **the last** individual alone **is dissolved, and all** the others remain **bound** by their nazirite vows.

The next chapter opens with a *Mishnah* presenting the case of cascading *nezirut* declarations. One person says *hareini nazir* — I am a *nazir* — and his companion hears and says *va'ani* — and I as well. A third person hears the second and says *va'ani*. The *Mishnah* rules that *kulam nezirim* — all of them are *nezirim* — because each piggybacks on the one before.

The *Mishnah* then addresses what happens when the chain is disrupted. If the first person obtains a *heter* — retroactive annulment — they are all released, *hutru kulam*. The logic is that each subsequent person's *va'ani* is not merely an independent declaration of *nezirut* made in a context created by the first. Rather, it functions as *hatfasa* — a transfer of status. The later declarant is saying, in effect: whatever status applies to you applies to me. This is structurally analogous to *ze k'ze* in the laws of *nedarim*, where the *kedusha* of one object is transferred to another. If the first person's *nezirut* never truly took effect — retroactively voided by the *heter* — there was no status to transfer, and the subsequent declarations are vacuous.

Conversely, if the last person in the chain obtains a *heter*, only he is released. The first person's status is independent and self-standing; the last person's annulment has no upstream effect.

### Wife First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״, וְשָׁמְעָה אִשְׁתּוֹ וְאָמְרָה ״וַאֲנִי״ — מֵיפֵר אֶת שֶׁלָּהּ, וְשֶׁלּוֹ קַיָּים. ״הֲרֵינִי נְזִירָה״, וְשָׁמַע בַּעֲלָהּ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי״ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If one said: **I am hereby a nazirite, and his wife heard** him **and said: And I,** he can **nullify her** vow of naziriteship if he so chooses (see Numbers 30:7–16). **But his** vow remains **intact,** as his naziriteship is not dependent on hers. However, if the wife said: **I am hereby a nazirite, and her husband heard and said: And I, he cannot nullify** her vow of naziriteship, as he would thereby be nullifying his own vow, which he made dependent on hers, and he does not have the ability to nullify his own vow.

A related *Mishnah* addresses the husband-wife scenario. A husband declares *hareini nazir*, and his wife, upon hearing, says *va'ani*. In this case, the husband may annul his wife's *nezirut* — which is his prerogative as a husband — and his own *nezirut* remains standing. Her *nezirut* derived from his and is therefore subject to his *hafera*; his does not depend on hers and is unaffected.

Now consider the inverse: the wife declares *hareini nezira*, and the husband, upon hearing, says *va'ani*. The *Mishnah* rules *eino yachol lehafer* — he cannot annul it. There are two ways to understand this ruling. One reading is that annulling his wife's *nezirut* would, as a practical matter, undo his own, since his *va'ani* piggybacked on her status. Since *lo yachel devaro* — a person cannot annul his own vow — this indirect route of annulment is also closed. A second reading, which the *Gemara* favors, is that the moment the husband said *va'ani* in response to his wife's declaration, he performed an act of *kiyum* — ratification. Having ratified the wife's *nezirut*, he can no longer perform *hafera* upon it, since *kiyum* and *hafera* are mutually exclusive. On this reading, the reason he cannot annul is not that it would affect him, but that he has already forfeited the right to annul through the *kiyum* itself.

### Husband First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר וְאַתְּ״, וְאָמְרָה ״אָמֵן״ — מֵיפֵר אֶת שֶׁלָּהּ, וְשֶׁלּוֹ קַיָּים. ״הֲרֵינִי נְזִירָה וְאַתָּה״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If he said to his wife: **I am hereby a nazirite, and you,** i.e., you shall be a nazirite as well, **and she said: Amen,** in acceptance of this vow, **he can nullify her** vow, **and his** vow remains **intact.** However, if the wife said: **I am hereby a nazirite, and you, and he said: Amen, he cannot nullify** her vow.

The *Mishnah* continues with further variations. If a husband says *hareini nazir v'at* — I am a *nazir*, and how about you — and the wife responds *amen*, he may annul her vow while his own remains standing, since her *nezirut* derived from his. If the wife says *hareini nezira v'ata* — I am a *nezira* and you as well — and he responds *amen*, he again cannot annul, for the same reason as above: his *amen* constitutes *kiyum* of her vow. Whether the mechanism is *lo yachel devaro* or *kiyum*, the practical result is the same — *hafera* is unavailable to him.

The question is raised whether, in the case where the husband performed *kiyum* through his *va'ani* or *amen*, he could subsequently seek *hatara* from a *chakham* for his own *va'ani*, thereby undoing his own *kiyum* and then annulling his wife's *nezirut* from beneath. The *Gemara* leaves this as a live question. It is worth noting that the earlier discussion in the tractate of whether a *chakham* can be *matir* a *kiyum* is directly relevant here, though no final resolution is offered at this stage.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ יָתֵיב רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: וְהוּא שֶׁהִתְפִּיסוּ כּוּלָּן בְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר. וְכַמָּה תּוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר — כְּדֵי שְׁאֵלַת שָׁלוֹם, וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי שְׁאֵלַת שָׁלוֹם — כְּדֵי שֶׁאוֹמֵר שָׁלוֹם תַּלְמִיד לָרַב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Reish Lakish sat before Rabbi Yehuda Nesia, and he sat and said** in explanation of the mishna: **And this** *halakha*, that they are all nazirites, applies only **when they all** took a vow by **associating** themselves with the previous vow **within the time required for speaking** a short phrase. The Gemara asks: **And how much** time is included in the measure of: **Within the time required for speaking** a short phrase? The Gemara answers: It is the time necessary **for greeting** someone. The Gemara asks: **And how much is** the time necessary **for greeting** someone? The Gemara answers that it is the time necessary **for a student to say: Peace** upon you, my teacher, **to** his **rabbi.**

Reish Lakish, sitting before Rabbi Yehuda Nesia — the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — articulates a crucial limiting principle: *vehu she-hitpisu kulam betoch kedei dibbur* — the entire sequence of *va'ani* declarations is valid only if all of them occurred within *tokh kedei dibbur* of the first person's statement.

The assumption underlying Reish Lakish's formulation at this stage is that all subsequent declarants are piggybacking directly on person number one — not on the person immediately before them, but on the original *nazir*. This assumption will be revisited, but it drives the *tokh kedei dibbur* requirement: since each later declarant is extending and participating in the first person's speech act, the temporal connection must be to that original act.

This is a significant point. In the classic case of *yad* — where a *nazir* passes before someone who then says *va'ani* — the *va'ani* works because the passing *nazir* creates a context that gives meaning to the word. That is a different mechanism: the context frames the later utterance. Here, however, what is happening is not merely context-creation but actual *hatfasa* — the later declarant is attaching to and extending the earlier one's speech act itself. That is why *tokh kedei dibbur* is required: one can only be said to participate in another's speech act if one does so within the window during which that speech act is still, legally speaking, live.

### Tokh Kedei Dibbur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תּוּב לָא שָׁבְקַתְּ רַוְוחָא לְתַלְמִידָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to** Reish Lakish: **Once again you have provided no advantage to a student** who wishes to associate himself with the naziriteship of another. If the student's rabbi was passing by at the precise moment that his colleague was vowing to be a nazirite, he would have to greet his rabbi first, and therefore he would not be able to respond to his colleague's vow in time.

The *Gemara* asks: how long is *tokh kedei dibbur*? The answer given is *kedei she'eilat shalom* — the time it takes to extend a greeting, as in *shalom alecha*. But then a further question arises: is it the greeting from a student to a teacher — *shalom alecha rebbi* — or from a teacher to a student? The difference is not merely ceremonial. A student greeting a teacher would say something like *shalom alecha rebbi u'mori*, which is a somewhat longer phrase than a teacher's brief *shalom alecha*. Whether *tokh kedei dibbur* spans two words or three words has practical significance for how many people can realistically fit their *va'ani* declarations within that window.

A response is offered to Reish Lakish: *lo shavkat ravcha le-talmidah* — you have not left room for the student. The implication, as Tosafot understands it, is that Reish Lakish's strict application of *tokh kedei dibbur* — measured from the original speaker's statement — creates problems in cases where a natural interruption intervenes, such as a student pausing to greet his arriving *rebbi* before saying *va'ani*. The push-back suggests that the window may be slightly more generous than Reish Lakish implies.

There is in fact a conceptual distinction worth drawing here. The rule of *tokh kedei dibbur* is classically invoked when a *speaker* wishes to retract or amend something they themselves said. In that context, the window is measured from the end of one's own speech. Here, however, a different party is joining in. One might therefore argue that the window for the subsequent declarant need not be identical to the classical *tokh kedei dibbur* of self-correction. Perhaps the second person's *va'ani* merely needs to occur in close proximity to the first person's statement — not necessarily within the exact same three-word window, but immediately in its wake. This would mean that even after the first person's *tokh kedei dibbur* has closed — after their own window for self-correction has elapsed — a second party could still validly say *va'ani* in the brief moment immediately following. The analogy to other cases of context-driven legal acts supports this reading: in *kiddushin*, for instance, if a couple has been discussing marriage at length, the handing of a ring without explicit words can suffice because *asukin b'oto inyan* — they are still engaged in that matter — and this extends well beyond any *tokh kedei dibbur* of the last spoken word. Here, by contrast, we are not merely saying the context gives meaning to the second person's words; we are saying the second person is actively participating in the first person's speech act. That participation must therefore be temporally proximate to the speech act itself, even if the precise measure of that proximity may differ from the self-correction model.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מִי שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״, וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְשָׁהָה כְּדֵי דִבּוּר, וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי״ — הוּא אָסוּר, וַחֲבֵירוֹ מוּתָּר. וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי דִבּוּר — כְּדֵי שְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם תַּלְמִיד לָרַב.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who said: I am hereby a nazirite, and another heard** him **and waited the time required for speaking** a short phrase **and** then **said: And I,** the first person **is bound** by his vow **and the other is permitted,** as too much time passed between their respective vows. **And how much** time is **the time required for speaking?** It is the time necessary **for a student** to **inquire** after **the welfare** of his **rabbi.**

A *baraita* is cited: *mi she'amar hareini nazir v'shama chaveiro v'shaha kedei dibbur v'amar va'ani, hu assur v'chaveiro mutar* — if the companion waited a *kedei dibbur* before saying *va'ani*, only the first person is a *nazir*, not the second. This supports Reish Lakish's position that *tokh kedei dibbur* is the operative standard, not something more generous.

### Piggyback Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ: מִי שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״, וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי״ ״וַאֲנִי״, וְתוּ לָא! תַּנָּא כִּי רוֹכְלָא לִיחְשֹׁיב וְלֵיזִיל?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say that** the mishna **supports** Reish Lakish's opinion? As it is taught: In the case of **one who said: I am hereby a nazirite, and another heard him and said: And I,** and a third person heard him and said: **And I,** they are all nazirites. The mishna mentions: And I, twice **and no more,** which indicates that only two people can associate themselves with the vow of the first one. The reason for this must be because too much time has passed since the first person spoke. The Gemara rejects this argument: This is no proof, as **should the *tanna* have continued reckoning** cases **like a peddler,** who announces his wares over and over again, by repeating: And I, and I, over and over again?

With the *tokh kedei dibbur* framework established, the *Gemara* turns to the question of whose statement each subsequent declarant is piggybacking on. Reish Lakish assumed they all connect back to person number one. But the *Mishnah* describes a scenario with persons two and three saying *va'ani* sequentially, and the *Gemara* asks: *chad batar chad matpis* — does each person grab onto the one immediately before him — *o dilma bekamma matpis* — or do they all grab onto person number one?

The *nafka mina* is considerable. If each person piggybacks on the one immediately before, the chain can theoretically extend indefinitely: person two connects to person one, person three connects to person two, person four connects to person three, and so on, each within *tokh kedei dibbur* of the prior speaker. If they all connect to person number one, the chain is limited to however many people can fit their *va'ani* declarations within *tokh kedei dibbur* of that first statement — only a handful at most.

A further *nafka mina* follows from this regarding the effect of a middle person's *heter*. If each person connects to the one before, then annulling person two releases persons three and four and everyone below, while leaving person one unaffected. If everyone connects to person one, then annulling anyone other than person one affects no one else.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve the question from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* says: *hutar ha-rishon hutru kullan* — if the first is released, all are released. The inference (*ha emtzai lo*) would be that only the first person's annulment has this cascading effect; a middle person's annulment would affect no one. This would support the view that everyone connects to person one. But the *Gemara* deflects: perhaps the *Mishnah* focused on the first person not because he is the only one whose annulment matters, but because using the first person allows one to say *hutru kullan* — all are released — which is the most dramatic formulation. A middle person's annulment would release only those below, making it a less useful teaching vehicle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי חַד וְלַשְׁמְעִינַן הָנֵי! הָכָא נָמֵי, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא הוּתַּר הָרִאשׁוֹן הוּתְּרוּ כּוּלָּן, הוּתַּר הָאַחֲרוֹן — הָאַחֲרוֹן מוּתָּר וְכוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא אֶמְצָעִי, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָתָנֵי ״וַאֲנִי״ ״וַאֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if the *tanna* wishes to be concise, **let him teach** only **one** example, **and teach us these** other cases of others who say: And I, by means of a single example. The Gemara answers: **Indeed,** this is in fact correct, **but because** the *tanna* **teaches in the latter clause:** If the vow of **the first was dissolved** by a halakhic authority then **they are all dissolved,** but if the vow of **the last** individual **was dissolved** by a halakhic authority then the vow of **the last** individual **is dissolved and all** the others remain **bound** by their vow, one may conclude **by inference that there is a middle** person between the first and the last. **And due to that** reason the *tanna* **teaches: And I, and I,** so that the case would include three people, but not because a fourth person is unable to associate himself with the vow of the first in the same manner.

Similarly, the *Mishnah* says: *hutar ha-achron, ha-achron mutar ve-kullan asurin* — if the last person is released, he is permitted and all before him remain forbidden. The inference would be that an intermediate person would affect only those who follow. But the *Gemara* resists: the *Mishnah* may be using *achron* loosely to mean anyone other than the first person.

### Baraita: Sequential

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא בְּהֶדְיָא: הוּתַּר הָרִאשׁוֹן — הוּתְּרוּ כּוּלָּן, הוּתַּר הָאַחֲרוֹן — הָאַחֲרוֹן מוּתָּר וְכוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין, הוּתַּר אֶמְצָעִי — הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַטָּה מוּתָּר, הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַעְלָה — אָסוּר, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חַד בְּחַבְרֵיהּ מִתְּפִיס, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear, as it is taught** in a *baraita* **explicitly:** If **the first** one **is dissolved, they are all dissolved;** if **the last** one **is dissolved, the last** one **is dissolved and they are all bound** by their vows. **If** the **middle** one **is dissolved,** the vows of anyone **from him and after** him **are dissolved;** those who vowed **from him and before** him are **bound** by their vows. One can **learn from this** that **each associates** himself with the vow of **the other** individual. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from this** that it is so.

The resolution comes from an explicit *baraita*: if the first person is released, all are released; if the last is released, only he is released; if a middle person is released, *heimenu u-lemata mutar, heimenu u-lemala assur* — from him downward is permissible, from him upward is forbidden. This explicitly distinguishes the middle person's annulment from the last person's and from the first person's. *Shema mina chad batar chad matpis* — this demonstrates conclusively that each person piggybacks on the one immediately before him, not all on person one.

This conclusion has a natural quality to it. When person three says *va'ani*, the most immediate referent for those words is person two, who just spoke. The chain is sequential. The practical implication — that the chain can extend indefinitely, each link connecting within *tokh kedei dibbur* of the prior link — follows directly.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion also returns us to a question that runs beneath the entire discussion: to what extent is this the default legal interpretation of *va'ani*, and to what extent does it depend on the specific circumstances? One can readily imagine cases where person three was facing person one and clearly intending to connect to that original declaration, not to person two. Whether halacha would honor that stated intent, or whether the sequential *hatfasa* model operates objectively regardless of subjective direction, is a question the *Gemara* does not definitively resolve. As a default in the absence of clarifying context, the rule is *chad batar chad matpis* — each connects to the one immediately before.
