# Taanit 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rains Post-Destruction

### R. Eliezer ben Prata

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ נַעֲשׂוּ גְּשָׁמִים צִימּוּקִין לָעוֹלָם. יֵשׁ שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין, וְיֵשׁ שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ מוּעָטִין. יֵשׁ שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ יוֹרְדִין בִּזְמַנָּן, וְיֵשׁ שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵין גְּשָׁמֶיהָ יוֹרְדִין בִּזְמַנָּן.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar ben Perata said: Since the day that the Temple was destroyed, rain has been meager,** i.e., overall, not enough has fallen **in the world. There are years whose rains are abundant, and there are years whose rains are scarce. There are years whose rains fall in their proper time,** and **there are years whose rains do not fall in their proper time.**

The daf opens with a *baraita*: *amar Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata, miyom shecharav Beit Hamikdash, na'asu geshamim tzimukim* — from the day the *Beit Hamikdash* was destroyed, rains have become shriveled. This is not to say that rains ceased entirely, for the *baraita* immediately qualifies: there are years in which rains are plentiful and years in which they are scarce; years in which the rains fall in their proper time and years in which they do not. The point is not the complete absence of rain but rather a structural diminishment, a qualitative decline across all categories of rainfall since the destruction.

### Servant Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ יוֹרְדִין בִּזְמַנָּן לְמָה הוּא דּוֹמֶה — לְעֶבֶד שֶׁנָּתַן לוֹ רַבּוֹ פַּרְנָסָתוֹ בְּאֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת, נִמְצְאוּת עִיסָּה נֶאֱפֵית כְּתִיקְנָהּ, וְנֶאֱכֶלֶת כְּתִיקְנָהּ. שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵין גְּשָׁמֶיהָ יוֹרְדִין בִּזְמַנָּן לְמָה הוּא דּוֹמֶה — לְעֶבֶד שֶׁנָּתַן לוֹ רַבּוֹ פַּרְנָסָתוֹ בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, נִמְצְאוּת עִיסָּה נֶאֱפֵית שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ, וְנֶאֱכֶלֶת שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a year whose rains fall in their proper time, to what** may **it be compared? To a servant whose master gave him his** weekly **portion on Sunday.** It is thereby **found** that his **dough is baked properly** throughout the week, **and it is eaten properly,** as he has a sufficient amount. Conversely, with regard to **a year whose rains do not fall in their proper time, to what** may **it be compared? To a servant whose master gave him his portion on Shabbat eve,** when there is insufficient time to prepare it fully. It is thereby **found** that his **dough is baked improperly, and it is eaten improperly.**

The *baraita* proceeds with a *mashal*. A year in which the rains come in their proper season — what does it resemble? A servant whose master gives him his weekly provisions on Sunday. The bread is baked in its proper time and eaten in its proper time. Everything unfolds with appropriate preparation and care. A year in which the rains do not come in their proper season resembles a servant who receives his provisions only on *erev shabbat*, at the last possible moment. The bread is not baked properly and is not eaten properly — everything is rushed and inadequate.

One might ask why the *mashal* is necessary. The practical consequences of untimely rain for agriculture are not difficult to understand. The answer is that the *mashal* is not primarily about the agricultural mechanics. It is about relationship. The image of the *eved* and his *rav* resurfaces here — just as it appeared in the first *mishnah* of the tractate, where rain during *Sukkot* is a *siman kelalah* because it is like a master spilling water on a servant who has come to serve him. The point of invoking this imagery is to re-surface the relational dimension: rain is God communicating with us, and these variations in timing and abundance are to be read through the lens of that dynamic between servant and master, between Israel and God.

### Abundant Rain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין לְמָה הוּא דּוֹמֶה — לְעֶבֶד שֶׁנָּתַן לוֹ רַבּוֹ פַּרְנָסָתוֹ בְּבַת אַחַת, נִמְצְאוּ רֵיחַיִם טוֹחֲנוֹת מִן הַכּוֹר מַה שֶּׁטּוֹחֲנוֹת מִן הַקַּב, וְנִמְצְאוּת עִיסָּה אוֹכֶלֶת מִן הַכּוֹר כְּמוֹ [שֶׁ]אוֹכֶלֶת מִן הַקַּב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a year whose rains are abundant, to what** may **it be compared? To a servant whose master gave him his portion** for a long period of time all **at once.** He performs all of his milling at one time, and it is therefore **found** that **the mill grinds** and produces waste **from a *kor*** of produce in the same amount **as it grinds** and produces waste **from** the much smaller ***kav*** of produce. During each milling process, the same amount of flour goes to waste. Consequently, milling a large amount of flour in a single milling process preserves flour. **And** similarly, it is **found** that **dough is diminished from a *kor*, as it diminishes from a *kav*.**

The *baraita* continues: a year of abundant rains — *shanah shegeshameiha merubin* — what does it resemble? A servant whose master gives him all of his provisions at once. Rashi explains that this means the entire year's supply given at one time, not merely a week's worth in advance. The benefit described is one of economy of scale: *nimtzet reicha-im tochenet min hakur mah shetochenet min hakav* — the amount of waste produced when grinding a *kur* (a very large measure) is only equal to the waste produced when grinding a *kav* (a small measure). Similarly with the dough: the waste from a *kur*'s worth of dough equals the waste from a mere *kav*. The same absolute amount is lost, but proportionally it is negligible when the total quantity is large.

This is admittedly a somewhat puzzling *mashal*. Is it not simply obvious that more rain is better? Perhaps the purpose is to illuminate hidden benefits that would otherwise be overlooked. And indeed, another reading of Rashi sharpens the point considerably: when a large amount of rain falls at one time, a certain quantity is always absorbed unproductively into the soil before any of it becomes genuinely beneficial. When a great abundance falls, that absorbed amount is a tiny fraction of the whole. The economy of scale here is not arbitrary — it reflects the genuine physics of water absorption, which provides a real explanatory basis for the *mashal*.

### Soil Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין לְמָה הוּא דּוֹמֶה — לְאָדָם שֶׁמְּגַבֵּל אֶת הַטִּיט, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ מַיִם רַבִּים — מַיִם אֵינָן כָּלִין, וְהַטִּיט מְגוּבָּל יָפֶה. אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ מַיִם מוּעָטִין — מַיִם כָּלִים, וְהַטִּיט אֵינוֹ מִתְגַּבֵּל יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively, when its rains are abundant, to what** may this year **be compared? To a person who kneads clay. If he has a lot of water,** his **water is not used up** and **the clay** will be **well kneaded. If he has** only **a little water,** the **water** will be **used up and the clay will not be well kneaded.**

An alternative explanation — *davar acher* — takes a different approach. *Bizman shegeshameha merubim l'mah hu domeh l'adam shemegabel et hatit* — a year of abundant rain is compared to a person mixing plaster. If he has ample water, the water does not run out and the plaster is mixed properly. If he has only a scarce amount of water, the water runs out and the plaster cannot be mixed well. The need for this second *mashal* is admittedly not entirely clear, particularly since it lacks the *eved*-and-*rav* imagery of the earlier comparisons. Nevertheless, the concrete image of the craftsman and his materials adds another dimension to the idea that abundance carries hidden advantages.

### Scarce Rain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנָה שֶׁגְּשָׁמֶיהָ מוּעָטִין לְמָה הוּא דּוֹמֶה — לְעֶבֶד שֶׁנָּתַן לוֹ רַבּוֹ פַּרְנָסָתוֹ מְעַט מְעַט, נִמְצְאוּ רֵיחַיִים מַה שֶּׁטּוֹחֲנוֹת מִן הַכּוֹר טוֹחֲנוֹת מִן הַקַּב. נִמְצְאוּת עִיסָּה כַּמָּה שֶׁנֶּאֱכֶלֶת מִן הַכּוֹר אוֹכֶלֶת מִן הַקַּב.
>
> **Translation:** In contrast, with regard to **a year whose rains are scarce, to what** may **it be compared? To a servant whose master gave him his portion little by little.** It is thereby **found that** the amount **that the mill** would have **ground from a *kor*** of produce is that which in practice **it grinds** and produces **from** each ***kav*.** It is likewise **found** that the **dough** that would have been **diminished from a *kor*** is the same amount that is **diminished from a *kav*.** In sum, one retains less dough when given his sustenance little by little.

The *baraita* then presents the counterpart: a year of scarce rain, *shanah shegeshameiha muatin*, is like a servant who receives his provisions only bit by bit, *me'at me'at*. Here the relationship between *mashal* and *nimshal* is again not perfectly aligned, since receiving provisions bit by bit implies spreading the same total amount over time rather than receiving a lesser total. Yet the point is the same: when the rains come only in small quantities, the same absolute amount of waste occurs in grinding and in making dough, but now that waste represents a far greater proportion of the whole. The economy of scale works in reverse — the smaller the total, the more punishing every unit of loss becomes.

## Nakdimon Story

### Lease & Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פַּעַם אַחַת עָלוּ כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל לָרֶגֶל לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְלֹא הָיָה לָהֶם מַיִם לִשְׁתּוֹת. הָלַךְ נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן אֵצֶל הֶגְמוֹן אֶחָד, אָמַר לוֹ: הַלְוֵינִי שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲיָינוֹת מַיִם לְעוֹלֵי רְגָלִים, וַאֲנִי אֶתֵּן לָךְ שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה עֵינוֹת מַיִם. וְאִם אֵינִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ — הֲרֵינִי נוֹתֵן לָךְ שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה כִּכַּר כֶּסֶף, וְקָבַע לוֹ זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: **Once all the Jewish people ascended for** the **pilgrimage Festival to Jerusalem and there was not** enough **water for them to drink. Nakdimon ben Guryon,** one of the wealthy citizens of Jerusalem, **went to** a certain gentile **officer [*hegemon*]** and **said to him: Lend me twelve wells of water for the pilgrims, and I will give back to you twelve wells of water. And if I do not give** them **to you, I will give you twelve talents of silver. And** the officer **set him a time** limit for returning the water.

The *baraita* introduces one of the most dramatic narratives in *Masechet Taanit*: *pa'am achat alu kol Yisrael laregel l'Yerushalayim velo haya lahem mayim lishtot* — once, all of Israel came up for the *regel* to Jerusalem and there was no water for them to drink. A certain man — whom we will come to know as Nakdimon ben Guryon — approached a wealthy Gentile and said: *halveini shteim esrei ma'ayanot mayim le'olei regalim* — lend me twelve springs of water for those coming up on pilgrimage. The precise mechanism of "lending" wells is somewhat opaque; presumably it means granting access and use of the water. In return: *ve'ani eten lecha shteim esrei einot mayim* — I will repay you with twelve springs of water. And if I cannot: *hareini noten lecha shteim esrei kikar kasef* — I will give you twelve talents of silver, an enormous and consequential sum.

A time was set for repayment. Rashi notes a difficulty: if these are *ma'ayanot* — natural springs — would they not replenish themselves from underground sources? It is possible these were actually cisterns, a point that will become relevant to the broader meaning of the story.

### Repeated Demands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ הַזְּמַן וְלֹא יֵרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים, בַּשַּׁחֲרִית שָׁלַח לוֹ: שַׁגֵּר לִי אוֹ מַיִם אוֹ מָעוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ. שָׁלַח לוֹ: עֲדַיִין יֵשׁ לִי זְמַן, כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ שֶׁלִּי הוּא. בַּצָּהֳרַיִם שָׁלַח לוֹ: שַׁגֵּר לִי אוֹ מַיִם אוֹ מָעוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ. שָׁלַח לוֹ: עֲדַיִין יֵשׁ לִי שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם. בַּמִּנְחָה שָׁלַח לוֹ: שַׁגֵּר לִי מַיִם אוֹ מָעוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ. שָׁלַח לוֹ: עֲדַיִין יֵשׁ לִי שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם. לִגְלֵג עָלָיו אוֹתוֹ הֶגְמוֹן, אָמַר: כׇּל הַשָּׁנָה כּוּלָּהּ לֹא יָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים, וְעַכְשָׁיו יֵרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים?! נִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ בְּשִׂמְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** When the** set **time arrived and no rain had fallen, in the morning** the official **sent** a message **to** Nakdimon: **Send me either** the **water or** the **coins that you owe me.** Nakdimon **sent** a message **to him: I still have time,** as **the entire day is mine. At noontime** the official again **sent** a message **to him: Send me either** the **water or** the **coins that you owe me.** Nakdimon **sent** a message **to him: I still have** time **left in the day.** In the afternoon he **sent** a message **to him: Send me either** the **water or** the **coins that you owe me.** Nakdimon **sent** a message **to him: I still have** time **left in the day. That officer ridiculed him, saying:** Throughout **the entire year rain has not fallen, and now it will rain? He entered the bathhouse in** a state of **joy,** anticipating the large sum of money he was about to receive.

When the appointed time arrived, it had not rained. The wells were still mostly depleted. Beginning in the morning, the wealthy man sent word: *shagar li o mayim o ma'ot* — send me either the water or the money you owe me. Nakdimon replied: *adayin yesh li zman kol hayom kulo* — I still have the whole day. At midday the man sent again, and Nakdimon replied that he still had time. At *mincha* he sent again, and again Nakdimon replied that there was still time in the day.

At this point, *ligleg alav oto adon* — the wealthy man mocked him. He said: the entire year it has not rained, and now at the last moment of the last day you expect it to rain? He entered the bathhouse in joy — and, as the *Maharsha* notes, the mockery was heightened by the fact that he was using water for bathing while others lacked water for basic needs.

### Nakdimon Prays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁהַהֶגְמוֹן נִכְנַס בְּשִׂמְחָתוֹ לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ, נַקְדִּימוֹן נִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ כְּשֶׁהוּא עָצוּב. נִתְעַטֵּף, וְעָמַד בִּתְפִלָּה. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ שֶׁלֹּא לִכְבוֹדִי עָשִׂיתִי, וְלֹא לִכְבוֹד בֵּית אַבָּא עָשִׂיתִי, אֶלָּא לִכְבוֹדְךָ עָשִׂיתִי, שֶׁיְּהוּ מַיִם מְצוּיִין לְעוֹלֵי רְגָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As the master entered the bathhouse in his joy, Nakdimon entered the Temple in** a state of **sadness. He wrapped himself** in his prayer shawl **and stood in prayer. He said before** God: **Master of the Universe, it is revealed and known before You that I did not act for my own honor, nor did I act for the honor of my father's house. Rather, I acted for Your honor, so that there should be water for the Festival pilgrims.**

While the wealthy man was entering the bathhouse in triumph, *Nakdimon nichnas leBeit Hamikdash* — Nakdimon entered the *Beit Hamikdash* in anguish. *Nit'atef ve'amad bitfila* — he wrapped himself and stood in prayer. He said before God: *galui veyadu'a lefanecha shelo lichvodi asiti velo lichvod beit abba asiti ela lichvodcha asiti* — it is revealed and known before You that I did not do this for my own honor, nor for the honor of my father's house, but for Your honor — *sheyihyu mayim metzuyim le'olei regalim* — so that there would be water available for those coming up on pilgrimage. He was not seeking credit. He was not making a display of his generosity. He simply acted to provide for the *tzibbur* and explicitly set aside any personal motivation.

The contrast with his mode of prayer and with his motivation is significant: he went into the *Beit Hamikdash*, not into a personal circle; he made no demands; he simply placed his case before God and explained the purity of his intent.

### Rain & Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד נִתְקַשְּׁרוּ שָׁמַיִם בְּעָבִים, וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים עַד שֶׁנִּתְמַלְּאוּ שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲיָנוֹת מַיִם וְהוֹתִירוּ. עַד שֶׁיָּצָא הֶגְמוֹן מִבֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ, נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן יָצָא מִבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ. כְּשֶׁפָּגְעוּ זֶה בָּזֶה, אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי דְּמֵי מַיִם יוֹתֵר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ! אָמַר לוֹ: יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁלֹּא הִרְעִישׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת עוֹלָמוֹ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִילְךָ, אֶלָּא עֲדַיִין יֵשׁ לִי פִּתְחוֹן פֶּה עָלֶיךָ, שֶׁאוֹצִיא מִמְּךָ אֶת מְעוֹתַיי, שֶׁכְּבָר שָׁקְעָה חַמָּה, וּגְשָׁמִים — בִּרְשׁוּתִי יָרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately the sky became overcast and rain fell until the twelve cisterns were filled with water, and** there was even more water, so that **they overflowed. As the master left the bathhouse, Nakdimon ben Guryon left the Temple. When they met one another,** Nakdimon **said to him: Give me** the **money you owe me** for **the extra water** you received. The official **said to him: I know that the Holy One, Blessed be He, has shaken His world and caused rain to fall only for you. However, I still maintain a claim against you,** by **which I can** legally **take my coins from you, as** you did not pay me on the agreed date, **for the sun had already set, and** therefore **the rain fell onto my property.**

*Miyad nitkashru shamayim be'avim veyardu geshamim* — immediately the heavens darkened with clouds and rain fell, *ad shenitmal'u shteim esrei ma'ayanot mayim* — until the twelve wells filled up with water, *vehotiru* — and there was even more water beyond that.

As Nakdimon ben Guryon was leaving the *Beit Hamikdash* and the wealthy man was leaving the bathhouse, they encountered one another. Nakdimon said: give me payment for the additional water — the wells filled while they were still under my lease, so all the water that entered them belongs to me. The wealthy man replied: *yodea ani shelo hir'ish Hakadosh Baruch Hu et olamo ela bishvilcha* — I know that God shook the world for your sake alone, and I acknowledge the miracle. But still: *adayin yesh li pitchon peh alecha* — I still have a claim against you. *Shekvar shak'ah chamah ugeshamim birshuti yardu* — the sun had already set when the rain fell, meaning the rain fell into my wells, not yours.

This is a legitimate legal claim: *kinyan chatzer* — acquisition through one's domain — means that whatever falls or enters your property is yours. The water fell into the wealthy man's wells after the day ended, which would make it his water, not Nakdimon's repayment.

### Sun Breaks Through

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזַר וְנִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נִתְעַטֵּף וְעָמַד בִּתְפִלָּה, וְאָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! הוֹדַע שֶׁיֵּשׁ לְךָ אֲהוּבִים בְּעוֹלָמֶךָ! מִיָּד נִתְפַּזְּרוּ הֶעָבִים וְזָרְחָה הַחַמָּה. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמַר לוֹ אוֹתוֹ הֶגְמוֹן: אִילּוּ לֹא נִקְדְּרָה הַחַמָּה, הָיָה לִי פִּתְחוֹן פֶּה עָלֶיךָ שֶׁאוֹצִיא מִמְּךָ מְעוֹתַיי. תָּנָא: לֹא נַקְדִּימוֹן שְׁמוֹ, אֶלָּא בּוּנִי שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ נַקְדִּימוֹן — שֶׁנִּקְדְּרָה חַמָּה בַּעֲבוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Nakdimon **went back and entered the Temple, wrapped himself** in his prayer shawl, **and stood in prayer. He said before** God: **Master of the Universe, let it be known that You have beloved ones in Your world. Immediately, the clouds scattered and the sun shined. At that time, the master said to him: If the sun had not broken through** the clouds, **I would have** had a claim **against you,** by **which I could** have **taken my coins from you.** A Sage **taught: Nakdimon was not his** real **name; rather his name was Buni. And why was** he **called Nakdimon? Because the sun broke through [*nikdera*] for him.**

*Chazar venichnas leBeit Hamikdash* — Nakdimon went back into the *Beit Hamikdash*. He wrapped himself again in prayer and said: *Ribbono shel olam, hoda sheyesh lecha ahuvim be'olamecha* — Master of the Universe, make it known that You have those who are beloved in Your world. *Miyad nitpazeru he'avim vezarcha hachama* — immediately the clouds scattered and the sun shone through. With sunlight visible, it became clear that it was still day when the rain fell — and therefore the repayment was valid.

It is worth noting that the word *Nakdimon* itself derives from this moment: *nikdera hachamah ba'avuro* — the sun pierced through the clouds for his sake. And it should be noted that when Nakdimon prayed the second time, he did not say *yesh lecha ohavim* — those who love You — but *yesh lecha ahuvim* — those whom You love. The prayer is in the plural. This is not the language of a singular, exceptional figure claiming a unique personal relationship with God. It is an appeal to God's love for a broader community of the righteous.

### Nakdimon Triumphs

At that moment the wealthy man conceded: *ilulei nikdera hachamah haya li pitchon peh alecha she'otzi mimcha ma'otai* — had the sun not pierced through the clouds, I would have had a claim against you, but now I cannot. Nakdimon's debt was satisfied and the extra water was his.

This story invites comparison with the earlier narrative of *Choni Hama'agel*. If it had been a full year without significant rain — and the story takes place around *Pesach* time, consistent with what we know from the *Choni* episode — then both stories deal with the same crisis. But the approaches could not be more different. Choni drew a circle, made demands, and operated outside any institutional framework — outside, notably, the *Beit Hamikdash* itself. His powers were singular, personal, and in a certain sense a challenge to the rabbinic establishment. Nakdimon, by contrast, took practical responsibility, made a business arrangement, acted on behalf of the *tzibbur* with no self-promotion, and when he prayed, entered the *Beit Hamikdash*, made no demands, and appealed to God's love for the broader community of the beloved rather than his own special status.

The *hishtadlut* — the practical effort — of Nakdimon is also theologically significant. He did not wait for others to seek him out. He identified a problem and tried to solve it through this-worldly means: a contractual arrangement, a financial guarantee, an attempt to use the existing infrastructure of cisterns or wells. And yet nothing happened until he actually cried out to God. Every element of the story suggests that the practical initiative and the prayerful acknowledgment of dependence on God are not alternatives but partners. The *hishtadlut* without the *tefilla* remained insufficient; the *tefilla* was the culmination and completion of the *hishtadlut*, not a replacement for it.

The question of why *tefilla* should make a difference — does God not already know our circumstances and intentions? — is a question that runs throughout *Masechet Taanit* and indeed throughout all of rabbinic theology. One answer, consistent with Rav Soloveitchik's approach, is that *tefilla* is not primarily about persuading God but about the person's own acknowledgment of dependency. The very act of articulating one's need before God — *galui veyadu'a lefanecha* — is itself a restoration of the relational posture that makes the divine response possible. Until that acknowledgment is made, something is missing not in God's knowledge but in the person's orientation toward God.

There is also a dimension of legal formalism to the story that is striking. Water, like any commodity, is subject to *kinyan*. If rain falls into your cistern, you acquire it through *kinyan chatzer*. The wealthy man's legal claim was legitimate on its face. The miracle did not override the law; rather, it operated within the law's own framework — demonstrating that the sun had not yet set, and therefore that the rain had fallen within the proper timeframe. The supernatural here is in service of the natural and the legal, not in opposition to them.

## Name & Sun Miracles

### Boni Name

A *tanna* teaches: *lo Nakdimon shemo ela Boni shemo* — his name was not originally Nakdimon but Boni. He was called Nakdimon because *nikdera hachamah ba'avuro* — the sun pierced through the clouds on his account.

### Three Sun Delays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה נִקְדְּמָה לָהֶם חַמָּה בַּעֲבוּרָן: מֹשֶׁה, וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְנַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן. בִּשְׁלָמָא נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן — גְּמָרָא. יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — נָמֵי קְרָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּדֹּם הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְיָרֵחַ עָמָד״. אֶלָּא מֹשֶׁה מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **three** people, **the sun broke through** and shone at an irregular time **for their sake: Moses, Joshua, and Nakdimon ben Guryon.** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the case of **Nakdimon ben Guryon** is known by the aforementioned **tradition. The case of Joshua too** is derived from **a verse, as it is written: "And the sun stood still, and the moon stayed** until the people had avenged themselves upon their enemies" (Joshua 10:13). **However, from where do we** derive that the sun shined in a supernatural way for **Moses?**

*Tanu Rabbanan: shlosha nikdema lahem hachamah ba'avuran* — three figures for whom the sun was held back from setting in its proper time: Moshe, Yehoshua, and Nakdimon ben Guryon. Regarding Nakdimon, this is known through *gemara*, through transmitted tradition — that is, the story itself. But the *baraita*'s formulation reframes what happened: it was not merely that clouds parted and sunlight appeared. The sun actually did not set when it should have. This places Nakdimon in the same category as Yehoshua, for whom the verse states *va-yidom ha-shemesh ve-yarei'ach amad*, and as Moshe, for whom a derivation must be found.

### Proofs from Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָתְיָא ״אָחֵל״ ״אָחֵל״, כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״אָחֵל תֵּת פַּחְדְּךָ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״אָחֵל גַּדֶּלְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** It is **derived** by verbal analogy between **"I will begin"** and **"I will begin." Here,** with regard to Moses, **it is written:** "This day **I will begin to put the dread of you** and the fear of you upon the peoples that are under all the whole heaven" (Deuteronomy 2:25). **And there,** with regard to Joshua, **it is written:** "On this day **I will begin to magnify you** in the sight of all Israel, that they may know that just as I was with Moses, so I will be with you" (Joshua 3:7). The repeated use of the phrase "I will begin" indicates that all the miracles performed for Joshua were also performed for Moses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: אָתְיָא ״תֵּת״ ״תֵּת״, כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״אָחֵל תֵּת פַּחְדְּךָ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״בְּיוֹם תֵּת ה׳ אֶת הָאֱמֹרִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said:** The fact that the sun stood still for Moses is **derived** by a different verbal analogy, between the terms **"put"** and **"put." Here,** with regard to Moses, **it is written: "I will begin to put the dread of you"** (Deuteronomy 2:25). **And there,** with regard to Joshua, **is it written:** "Then Joshua spoke to the Lord, **on the day when the Lord put the Amorites** before the children of Israel, and he said in the sight of Israel: Sun, stand still upon Gibeon, and you, moon, in the valley of Aijalon" (Joshua 10:12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר, אָתְיָא מִגּוּפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁמְעוּן שִׁמְעֲךָ וְרָגְזוּ וְחָלוּ מִפָּנֶיךָ״, אֵימָתַי רָגְזוּ וְחָלוּ מִפָּנֶיךָ — בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּקְדְּמָה לוֹ חַמָּה לְמֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This idea is **derived from the verse itself,** as it says with regard to Moses: "This day I will begin to put the dread of you and the fear of you upon the peoples that are under all the whole heaven, **who, when they hear the report of you, shall tremble, and be in anguish due to you"** (Deuteronomy 2:25). **When** did the nations of the world **tremble and** when were they **in anguish due to you? When the sun broke through for Moses.**

For Yehoshua, the proof is explicit in the verse. For Moshe, *minalan* — from where do we know? Three *amoraim* offer different derivations. Rabbi Eliezer finds a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *akhel*: God says to Moshe, *akhel tait pachtecha tachat kol ha-shamayim* — I will begin to place the fear of you under all the heavens — and God says to Yehoshua, *akhel gadlecha* — I will begin to make you great. The same *gedulah* that occurred for Yehoshua therefore occurred for Moshe. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani derives a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *tait*: by Moshe, *akhel tait pachtecha*; by Yehoshua, *be-yom tait Hashem et ha-Emori*. Rabbi Yochanan derives it directly from the verse itself: *asher yishme'un et shimacha ve-ragzu ve-chalu mipanecha* — they will hear your name and tremble and shake before you — implying that the same miraculous demonstration of power that occurred at Yehoshua's battle must have occurred for Moshe as well, though the precise derivation is admittedly less explicit.

What is theologically striking here is the trajectory: Nakdimon acted in the most this-worldly, practical manner available to him, and yet the miracle done on his behalf is now placed alongside the stopping of the sun for Moshe and Yehoshua. The *baraita* is not saying that all people will receive such miracles. But it is affirming that this-worldly, communally oriented, humble leadership can draw down the same divine response as the most dramatic prophetic intervention.

## Rain Cities & Blessings

### Uneven Rain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן עִיר שֶׁלֹּא יָרְדוּ עָלֶיהָ גְּשָׁמִים כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: וּשְׁתֵּיהֶן לִקְלָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **And likewise,** if there is a particular **city upon which it did not rain,** while the surrounding area did receive rain, this is considered a divine curse, as it is written: "And I will cause it to rain on one city, but on one city I will not cause it to rain, one portion will be rained upon, and the portion upon which it did not rain shall wither" (Amos 4:7). **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: And both of** the cities are faced **with a curse,** as one city suffers from drought while the other is afflicted with destructive storms.

Returning to the *mishnah*'s framework concerning cities where rain fell and cities where it did not, a verse is cited: *al ir achat amtir ve'al ir achat lo amtir* — I will bring rain upon one city and not upon another. One would naturally read this as the city receiving rain being blessed and the city not receiving rain being cursed. But Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *ushteihen liklala* — both may in fact be a curse. The precise mechanism is not fully spelled out, but among the explanations offered is that too much rain can itself be a *kelalah*. Rashi adds that when the city without rain rushes to purchase grain from the city that received it, the sudden demand may create shortages even in the place of abundance. And it should be noted that we do not ordinarily pray for *rov geshamim* — excessive rain — as an unambiguous good.

### Nidah Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָיְתָה יְרוּשָׁלִַים לְנִדָּה בֵּינֵיהֶם״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לִבְרָכָה, כְּנִדָּה: מָה נִדָּה יֵשׁ לָהּ הֶיתֵּר — אַף יְרוּשָׁלַיִם יֵשׁ לָהּ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** This statement reverses the plain meaning of a verse. The Gemara provides other interpretations that Rav Yehuda attributed to Rav, which also run contrary to the simple meaning of a verse. **"Jerusalem among them was a like a menstruating woman"** (Lamentations 1:17). **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** Although the simple meaning of this verse is a curse, it can also be understood **as a blessing.** Jerusalem was **like a menstruating woman: Just as a menstruating woman** will become **permitted** to her husband after the conclusion of her days of ritual impurity, **so too, Jerusalem** will be **repaired** from its destruction.

From here the *gemara* presents a series of statements by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav taking verses that appear to be curses over Jerusalem and revealing the blessing concealed within them. The verse *haysa Yerushalayim lenida beineihem* — Jerusalem became like a *nida* among them — is read as a *bracha*. For just as a woman who is a *nida* has a path back to permissibility — *nida yesh la heter* — so too Jerusalem's current state of separation is not permanent. It will return to its former permitted state.

### Widow Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָיְתָה כְּאַלְמָנָה״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, לִבְרָכָה: כְּאַלְמָנָה — וְלֹא אַלְמָנָה מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וְדַעְתּוֹ לַחֲזוֹר עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the verse: **"How she has become like a widow"** (Lamentations 1:1), **Rav Yehuda said:** This too is **for a blessing.** The verse states that Jerusalem is **like a widow, but is not an actual widow. Rather,** Jerusalem is **like a woman whose husband has gone to a country overseas.** Without her husband by her side she is likened to a widow, **and** yet **he intends to return to her.**

Similarly, *haysa k'almana* — she was like a widow — is read as a blessing. Not *almana mamash* — not a true widow — but rather *k'isha shehalach ba'ala lemedinas hayam veda'aso lachzor aleha* — like a woman whose husband has gone overseas with the intention to return. The separation is real and painful, but it is not final.

### Debased Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְגַם אֲנִי נָתַתִּי אֶתְכֶם נִבְזִים וּשְׁפָלִים״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לִבְרָכָה, דְּלָא מוֹקְמִי מִינַּן לָא רֵישֵׁי נַהֲרֵי וְלָא גְּזִירִיפָּטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The same manner of explanation is provided for the verse: **"Therefore I have also made you contemptible and base"** (Malachi 2:9). **Rav Yehuda said:** This too can be interpreted **as a blessing, as** meaning that the nations view us as lowly, but nevertheless, they do not assign us unpleasant jobs. **They do not** appoint **from us either river officials or government officials [*geziripatei*].**

*Vegam ani nasati eschem nivzim ushfalim* — I have made you degraded and lowly — is also read by Rav Yehuda as a *bracha*: because the nations regard Israel as so lowly, *d'la mokmi minan la rashei naharei velo gezirpati* — they do not appoint Jews as tax collectors or as enforcers. These are positions of worldly status but of deep moral hazard — roles that would require oppressing fellow Jews or fellow human beings. The very contempt of the nations becomes a shield against being conscripted into their apparatus of exploitation. There is a historical irony embedded here that would be familiar to any student of Jewish history: the exclusion from land ownership that drove Jews into moneylending and commerce, for instance, was a curse that carried within it certain forms of economic opportunity, however fraught.

### Reed vs Cedar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִכָּה ה׳ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל כַּאֲשֶׁר יָנוּד הַקָּנֶה בַּמַּיִם״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לִבְרָכָה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״נֶאֱמָנִים פִּצְעֵי אוֹהֵב וְנַעְתָּרוֹת נְשִׁיקוֹת שׂוֹנֵא״ — טוֹבָה קְלָלָה שֶׁקִּילְּלַת אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹתֵר מִבְּרָכָה שֶׁבֵּרְכַת בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע.
>
> **Translation:** The prophet Ahijah the Shilonite cursed Israel in the following terms: **"For the Lord will smite Israel as a reed is shaken in the water"** (I Kings 14:15). **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** This too is **for a blessing, as Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Faithful are the wounds of a friend, but the kisses of an enemy are deceitful"** (Proverbs 27:6)? **The curse with which Ahijah the Shilonite cursed the Jewish people is more** effective **than the blessing with which Balaam the wicked blessed them.**

*Vehika Hashem et Yisrael ka'asher yanud hakaneh bamayim* — God will smite Israel as a reed sways in water — is read as a *bracha*. The basis for this reading is a teaching of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonasan, who cites the verse *ne'emanim pitz'ei ohev vena'ataros nishikos soneh* — trustworthy are the wounds of one who loves you, and burdensome are the kisses of one who hates you. Better to be struck by a friend than kissed by an enemy. Therefore: *tova klala shekilel Achiyah HaShiloni* — better is the curse that Achiyah HaShiloni cursed Israel with — the curse of the swaying reed — *yoter mibracha sheberchan Bilam Harasha* — than the blessing with which the wicked Bilam blessed them. For Bilam blessed Israel like a cedar — *kaarasim alei mayim*.

### Reed Properties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי קִלְּלָן בְּקָנֶה, אָמַר לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: ״וְהִכָּה ה׳ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל כַּאֲשֶׁר יָנוּד הַקָּנֶה״, מָה קָנֶה זֶה עוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹם מַיִם, וְגִזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף וְשׇׁרָשָׁיו מְרוּבִּין, וַאֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הָרוּחוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם בָּאוֹת וְנוֹשְׁבוֹת בּוֹ — אֵין מְזִיזוֹת אוֹתוֹ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ, אֶלָּא הוֹלֵךְ וּבָא עִמָּהֶן. דָּמְמוּ הָרוּחוֹת — עָמַד הַקָּנֶה בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan elaborates: **Ahijah the Shilonite cursed** the Jewish people **by** comparing them to **a reed: "For the Lord will smite Israel as a reed is shaken in the water."** Although it seems to be a curse, this verse is actually a blessing. **Just as this reed stands in a place of water, and its shoots replenish** themselves when cut, **and its roots are numerous** for a plant of its size, **and even if all the winds in the world come and blow against it, they cannot move it from its place, rather, it sways with them** until **the winds subside, and the reed** still **stands in its place,** the same applies to the Jewish people. After all the difficulties that they endure, they will ultimately survive and return home.

What is so admirable about the reed, and what is so problematic about the cedar? The reed, the *gemara* explains, stands in a place of water. Its stalk constantly replenishes itself — *vegizo machalif*. It has many and deep roots — *vesharshav merubim*. And crucially: *vaafilu kol ruchot shebaolam baot venoshvot bo ein miziizot oto mimakomo* — even if all the winds of the world come and blow against it, they cannot move it from its place. Rather, *holech uba imahen* — it sways and bends with them — and when the winds subside, *amad hakaneh bimakomo* — the reed is still exactly where it always was. Flexibility and deep rootedness are not opposites. They are the combination that guarantees survival.

### Cedar Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע בֵּירְכָן בְּאֶרֶז, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כָּאֲרָזִים עֲלֵי מָיִם״, מָה אֶרֶז זֶה אֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹם מַיִם, וְאֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, וְאֵין שׇׁרָשָׁיו מְרוּבִּין. אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הָרוּחוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם נוֹשְׁבוֹת בּוֹ — אֵין מְזִיזוֹת אוֹתוֹ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּשְׁבָה בּוֹ רוּחַ דְּרוֹמִית — עוֹקַרְתּוֹ וְהוֹפַכְתּוֹ עַל פָּנָיו. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁזָּכָה קָנֶה לִיטּוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ קוּלְמוֹס לִכְתּוֹב בּוֹ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה נְבִיאִים וּכְתוּבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **However, Balaam the wicked blessed** the Jews **by** comparing them to **a cedar, as it is stated: "As cedars beside the waters"** (Numbers 24:6). **Just as this cedar does not stand in a place of water, and its shoots do not replenish** themselves, **and its roots are not numerous,** Balaam wished that the same should apply to the Jewish people. Furthermore, while it is true that **even** if **all the winds in the world blow** against **it they will not move it from its place, once the southern wind blows** against **it, it uproots** the cedar **and turns it on its face. And not only that, but** the **reed merited** that **a quill [*kulmos*] is taken from it to write with it a Torah scroll, the Prophets, and the Writings.** Evidently, the curse comparing Israel to a reed is better than the blessing likening them to a cedar.

The cedar, by contrast, does not normally grow in a place of abundant water. Its trunk does not regenerate — *ein gizo machalif*. Its roots are not as numerous or as deep. And while ordinary winds cannot move it — this is its apparent strength — *keivan shenashvah ruach dromit okartahu vehofchatohu al panav* — when a strong southern wind comes, it uproots the cedar and overturns it entirely.

And beyond survival: *velo od illa shezachah kaneh litol mimenu kulmos lichtov bo sefer torah neviim uktuvim* — the lowly reed is also the one taken to fashion the quill with which the *Torah*, *Neviim*, and *Ketuvim* are written. Israel's apparent lowliness in the eyes of the nations does not diminish its role as the vessel of *kedushah*. Quite the opposite — it is precisely through its humble, flexible, deeply rooted character that it becomes the instrument through which the sacred is transmitted.

## Soft as Reed

### Maxim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְעוֹלָם יְהֵא אָדָם רַךְ כְּקָנֶה וְאַל יְהֵא קָשֶׁה כְּאֶרֶז. מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מִמִּגְדַּל גְּדוֹר מִבֵּית רַבּוֹ, וְהָיָה רָכוּב עַל חֲמוֹר וּמְטַיֵּיל עַל שְׂפַת נָהָר, וְשָׂמֵחַ שִׂמְחָה גְּדוֹלָה. וְהָיְתָה דַּעְתּוֹ גַּסָּה עָלָיו מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלָּמַד תּוֹרָה הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages** further **taught** in praise of the reed: **A person should always be soft like a reed, and he should not be stiff like a cedar. An incident** occurred in **which Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, came from Migdal Gedor, from his rabbi's house, and he was riding on a donkey and strolling on the bank of the river. And he was very happy, and his head was swollen with pride because he had studied much Torah.**

From this emerges a general rabbinic maxim: *le'olam yehei adam rach kakaneh ve'al yehei kasheh ka'erez* — a person should always be as soft and pliant as a reed and not as hard and unbending as a cedar. The *gemara* immediately grounds this maxim in a famous narrative.

### Rabbi's Pride

*Ma'aseh sheba Rabbi Eliezer* — there is a story of Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon who came from *Migdal Gedor* — and it is worth pausing on those words. *Migdal Gedor* means something like a fortified tower, a castle surrounded by walls. He was coming from his master's house, from an environment of Torah study that was, in a sense, hermetically sealed from the ordinary world. *Vehaya rachuv al chamor umitayal al sefat hanahar* — he was riding on a donkey and strolling leisurely along the riverbank. *Vesamach simchah gedolah vayashet daato gasah alav* — he was feeling intensely self-satisfied, his mind swelled with a sense of his own greatness, *mipnei shelamad torah harbeh* — because he had learned a great deal of Torah.

One might also note who else rides a donkey in the tradition: *Mashiach*, the future redeemer. There may be a subtle suggestion that this rabbi harbored something of a messianic self-regard — an inflated sense of his own cosmic significance.

### The Ugly Man

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ אָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מְכוֹעָר בְּיוֹתֵר. אָמַר לוֹ: שָׁלוֹם עָלֶיךָ רַבִּי! וְלֹא הֶחְזִיר לוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ: רֵיקָה, כַּמָּה מְכוֹעָר אוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ! שֶׁמָּא כׇּל בְּנֵי עִירֶךָ מְכוֹעָרִין כְּמוֹתְךָ? אָמַר לוֹ: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ, אֶלָּא לֵךְ וֶאֱמוֹר לָאוּמָּן שֶׁעֲשָׂאַנִי: ״כַּמָּה מְכוֹעָר כְּלִי זֶה שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **He happened** upon **an exceedingly ugly person,** who **said to him: Greetings to you, my rabbi, but** Rabbi Elazar **did not return** his greeting. Instead, Rabbi Elazar **said to him: Worthless [*reika*]** person, **how ugly is that man. Are all the people of your city as ugly as you?** The man **said to him: I do not know, but you** should **go and say to the Craftsman Who made me: How ugly is the vessel you made.**

*Venizman lo adam echad shehaya mecho'ar beyoter* — a certain man came toward him who was exceedingly ugly in appearance. The man greeted him respectfully: *shalom alecha Rebbe*. The rabbi did not respond. To respond would have implied a kind of social equality, and this man was not, in the rabbi's estimation, worthy of that. His silence was itself a statement of contempt.

*Vetafla amar* — then the rabbi did speak — not to respond to the greeting but to pronounce judgment: *reika, kama mecho'ar oto ha'ish* — you empty person, how disgusting you are. *Shema kol benei ircha mecho'arim kemotecha* — perhaps all the people of your city are as disgusting as you are.

The man replied without anger: *eini yodea, lech ve'emor la'uman she'asani, kama mecho'ar kli zeh she'asita* — I do not know. Go and tell the craftsman who made me: how ugly is this vessel that you have made.

There is much to notice in this response. The man calls himself a *kli* — a vessel. He is not claiming to be beautiful. He is pointing out that the proper address for a complaint about the vessel is to the One who fashioned it. And there is a further irony in the rabbi's opening word: *reika*, meaning empty person. The word *reik* refers to the interior — what is inside. Yet it is the rabbi who is looking only at the outside and making a judgment about the inside. The man's response — *eini yodea* — is also a model of *anavah*, of not presuming to know everything, in sharp contrast to the rabbi who presumed to assess, judge, and dismiss in a single glance.

### Humble Reply

### Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּדַע בְּעַצְמוֹ שֶׁחָטָא, יָרַד מִן הַחֲמוֹר וְנִשְׁתַּטַּח לְפָנָיו, וְאָמַר לוֹ: נַעֲנֵיתִי לְךָ, מְחוֹל לִי! אָמַר לוֹ: אֵינִי מוֹחֵל לְךָ עַד שֶׁתֵּלֵךְ לָאוּמָּן שֶׁעֲשָׂאַנִי וֶאֱמוֹר לוֹ: כַּמָּה מְכוֹעָר כְּלִי זֶה שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ.
>
> **Translation:** When** Rabbi Elazar **realized that he** had **sinned** and insulted this man merely on account of his appearance, **he descended from his donkey and prostrated himself before him, and he said to** the man: **I have sinned against you; forgive me.** The man **said to him: I will not forgive you go until you go to the Craftsman Who made me and say: How ugly is the vessel you made.**

*Vecheivan sheyada be'atzmo shechata* — as soon as he recognized that he had sinned, *yarad min hachamor* — he descended from the donkey. He came down from his elevated position, literally and figuratively. *Venishtate'ach lefanav* — he prostrated himself before the man — *ve'amar lo: na'aniti lecha, mechol li* — I have spoken out of turn to you; forgive me.

The man replied: *eini mochel lecha ad shetelech la'uman she'asani ve'emor lo: kama mecho'ar kli zeh she'asita* — I will not forgive you until you go and say to the craftsman who made me: how ugly is this vessel You have made. A quick prostration and a request for forgiveness do not constitute *teshuvah*. The man is demanding that the rabbi fully internalize what he has done — not merely that he offended a person, but that he spoke against God's creation, against the *uman* who fashioned this *kli*.

### City Welcome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מְטַיֵּיל אַחֲרָיו עַד שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְעִירוֹ. יָצְאוּ בְּנֵי עִירוֹ לִקְרָאתוֹ, וְהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: שָׁלוֹם עָלֶיךָ רַבִּי רַבִּי, מוֹרִי מוֹרִי! אָמַר לָהֶם: לְמִי אַתֶּם קוֹרִין רַבִּי רַבִּי? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לְזֶה שֶׁמְּטַיֵּיל אַחֲרֶיךָ. אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם זֶה רַבִּי — אַל יִרְבּוּ כְּמוֹתוֹ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי מָה? אָמַר לָהֶם: כָּךְ וְכָךְ עָשָׂה לִי. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי כֵּן, מְחוֹל לוֹ, שֶׁאָדָם גָּדוֹל בְּתוֹרָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **He walked behind** the man, trying to appease him, **until they reached** Rabbi Elazar's **city. The people of his city came out to greet him, saying to him: Greetings to you, my rabbi, my rabbi, my master, my master.** The man **said to them: Who are you calling my rabbi, my rabbi? They said to him: To this man, who is walking behind you. He said to them: If this** man **is a rabbi, may there not be many like him among the Jewish people. They asked him: For what** reason do you say this? He **said to them: He did such and such to me. They said to him: Even so,** forgive him, **as he is a great Torah scholar.**

*Yatzu bnei iro likrato* — the townspeople came out to greet him — *vayomru lo: shalom alecha Rebbe Rebbe mori mori* — peace to you, Rebbe Rebbe, our master, our master. At this point, the contrast with the earlier moment becomes illuminating: when this same rabbi was greeted on the road as simply *Rebbe*, he refused to respond. Now we see what he was accustomed to — *Rebbe Rebbe, mori mori*. The single *Rebbe* of the ugly man had seemed to him an insult, an inadequate form of address, further evidence of the man's deficiency.

The insulted man asked: *lemi atem korim Rebbe Rebbe?* — who are you calling Rebbe Rebbe? They pointed to the man walking behind him. He then said: *im ze Rebbe, al yirbu kamoso b'Yisrael* — if this is a teacher, may there not be many like him in Israel. A play on *Rebbe* and *yirbu*. The townspeople asked why. He told them what the rabbi had done. *Amru lo: af al pi chen, mechol lo, she'adam gadol baTorah hu* — nevertheless forgive him, for he is a great person in Torah.

This defense deserves scrutiny. The community does not say he is a *tzaddik* or that he is distinguished in *ma'asim tovim*. They say he is *gadol baTorah*. And in saying so, they may inadvertently be locating the very root of the problem: his entire identity and sense of worth is constructed around Torah greatness, and it is precisely that identity, unchecked by humility, that led to this behavior. Their defense, in other words, names the very thing that generated the failure.

### Conditional Forgiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: בִּשְׁבִילְכֶם הֲרֵינִי מוֹחֵל לוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא רָגִיל לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן. מִיָּד נִכְנַס רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְדָרַשׁ: לְעוֹלָם יְהֵא אָדָם רַךְ כְּקָנֶה וְאַל יְהֵא קָשֶׁה כְּאֶרֶז. וּלְפִיכָךְ זָכָה קָנֶה לִיטּוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ קוּלְמוֹס לִכְתּוֹב בּוֹ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה תְּפִילִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: For your sakes I forgive him, provided that** he accepts upon himself **not to become accustomed to behave like this. Immediately, Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, entered the study hall and taught: A person should always be soft like a reed and he should not be stiff like a cedar,** as one who is proud like a cedar is likely to sin. **And therefore,** due to its gentle qualities, the **reed merited** that **a quill is taken from it to write with it a Torah scroll, phylacteries, and *mezuzot*.**

He agreed to forgive — but not for the rabbi's sake. *Bishvilchem hareni mochel lo* — for your sake I forgive him. And he added a condition: *uvilvad shelo yehei ragul la'asos ken* — as long as he does not accustom himself to doing this.

This formulation is striking. One might have expected: *uvilvad shelo ya'aseh ken* — as long as he does not do this again. But *shelo yehei ragul la'asos ken* — as long as he does not habituate himself to this — suggests something more nuanced. The *Maharsha* reads this as a prescription about practice: do not engage in habits and behaviors that cultivate this kind of arrogance. If you learn Torah while remaining sealed off from ordinary people — riding on your donkey away from the world, coming down from your fortified tower — you will keep producing this attitude. The cure is not merely willpower but a restructuring of the practices that generate the problem.

*Miyad nichnas Rabbi Elazar berabi Shimon* — immediately the rabbi went in and *darash* — he taught publicly: *le'olam yehei adam rach kakaneh ve'al yehei kasheh ka'erez* — a person should always be as soft as a reed and not as hard as a cedar. *Ulfichach zacha kaneh litol mimenu kulmus lichtov bo sefer torah tefillin umezuzot* — and therefore the reed merited that a quill is taken from it to write the *Sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot*.

What is the rabbi saying with this *derasha*? On the most straightforward level, he is incorporating the lesson he has just learned: I should not have been as unbending and hard as a cedar. But there may be additional layers. The ugly man had called himself a *kli* — a vessel. Here the rabbi teaches that a humble, lowly vessel — the reed — becomes the very instrument through which the most sacred writings are produced. And some might also detect a subtle critique embedded in the closing: the man who refused to forgive easily, who was not immediately pliant, might himself be seen as tending toward the cedar. He ultimately did bend — but only under social pressure, and only for the community's sake rather than for the rabbi's. The reed's virtue, by contrast, is that it sways naturally, not only when pushed by communal opinion.

## Buildings & Danger

### Healthy Buildings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן עִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ דֶּבֶר אוֹ מַפּוֹלֶת כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַפּוֹלֶת שֶׁאָמְרוּ, בְּרִיאוֹת וְלֹא רְעוּעוֹת. שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיוֹת לִיפּוֹל, וְלֹא הָרְאוּיוֹת לִיפּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **And likewise,** if a **city is** afflicted **by pestilence or collapsing buildings,** that city fasts and sounds the alarm, and all of its surrounding areas fast but they do not sound the alarm. Rabbi Akiva says: They sound the alarm but they do not fast. **The Sages taught:** These collapsing buildings **to which** the Sages **referred** are those of **sturdy and not dilapidated** walls; they have walls **that are not ready to fall, and not those that are ready to fall.**

The *mishnah* mentions *mapolet* — collapsing buildings — as one of the circumstances that affects neighboring towns. The *baraita* clarifies: *mapolet she'amru beriot ve'lo re'uot* — the falling buildings referred to are strong, healthy buildings, not dilapidated ones. Specifically: buildings *she'einan re'uyot lipol* — that are not fit to fall — as opposed to buildings that were already on the verge of collapse. When structurally sound buildings fall, that indicates something beyond ordinary physical deterioration.

### Redundancy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי נִיהוּ בְּרִיאוֹת, הֵי נִיהוּ שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיוֹת לִיפּוֹל. הֵי נִיהוּ רְעוּעוֹת, הֵי נִיהוּ רְאוּיוֹת לִיפּוֹל! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּנְפַלוּ מֵחֲמַת גּוּבְהַיְיהוּ. אִי נָמֵי, דְּקָיְימָן אַגּוּדָּא דְנַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses puzzlement with regard to the wording of the *baraita*: **What are sound** walls; **what are** walls **that are not ready to fall; what are dilapidated** walls; **what are those that are ready to fall?** The elements in each pair of walls are apparently the same, and the *baraita* is repetitive. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to specify that in the case of walls **that fell due to their height,** i.e., they are sound but also ready to fall, due to their excessive height. **Alternatively,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the walls **were positioned on a riverbank,** as they are likely to fall despite the fact that they are not dilapidated, as the riverbank itself is unstable.

The *gemara* raises an apparent redundancy: is *beriot* not the same as *she'einan re'uyot lipol*, and is *re'uot* not the same as *re'uyot lipol*? Why give two formulations for each category?

The *gemara* resolves: *lo tzericha denafish machamat govhei* — the distinction is necessary for a building that is structurally sound in its materials but is so tall that it is *re'uya lipol* even though it is *lo re'ua* — not dilapidated. Good materials do not guarantee stability if the construction is improperly proportioned. *Inami dekayma aguda denahra* — or a building situated at the edge of a river: the materials may be entirely sound, but the location means that erosion or flooding could cause it to collapse. So there are buildings that are neither crumbling nor rickety that are nonetheless *re'uyot lipol*, and that distinction is what required the two separate formulations.

### Trembling Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הַהִיא אֲשִׁיתָא רְעִיעֲתָא דַּהֲוַאי בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא דְּלָא הֲוָה חָלֵיף רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל תּוּתַהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקַיְימָא בְּאַתְרַהּ תְּלֵיסַר שְׁנִין. יוֹמָא חַד אִיקְּלַע רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְהָתָם, אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב: נֵיתֵי מָר נַקֵּיף! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא צְרִיכְנָא הָאִידָּנָא, דְּאִיכָּא רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה בַּהֲדַן דִּנְפִישָׁ[א] זְכוּתֵיהּ, וְלָא מִסְתְּפֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: This is **like that** dilapidated wall **that was in Neharde'a, under which Rav and Shmuel would not pass, although it stood in place thirteen years. One day Rav Adda bar Ahava happened** to come **there** and walked with them. As they passed the wall, **Shmuel said to Rav: Come, Master, let us circumvent** this wall, so that we do not stand beneath it. Rav **said to him:** It is **not necessary** to do so **today, as Rav Adda bar Ahava is with us, whose merit is great, and** therefore **I am not afraid** of its collapse.

*Ki hahi shita re'ua dehava bi-Nehardea* — there was a trembling, unstable wall in Nehardea — *ve'lo chalfu Rav u-Shmuel tutei* — that Rav and Shmuel refused to walk under it, *af al gav dekayma afartaser shnin* — even though it had been standing for thirteen years. The fact of its survival for thirteen years was not taken as evidence of safety. If the construction is such that it could fall at any moment, then every moment is the moment it might fall, and prior survival does not alter that analysis.

One day, Rav Adda bar Ahava happened to be in Nehardea. When Rav and Shmuel approached the wall in his company, Shmuel suggested they take their usual detour. Rav replied: today we do not need to go around — *de-ika Rav Adda bar Ahava behadan* — Rav Adda bar Ahava is with us, *de-nafish zechusei* — his merits are so great, *ve-lo mistafina* — I am not afraid the wall will fall. The presence of a *tzaddik* of such extraordinary virtue was understood to provide a form of protection beyond what one's own merits could supply.

### Wine Retrieval

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא חַמְרָא בְּהָהוּא בֵּיתָא רְעִיעָא, וּבְעָא לְפַנּוֹיֵיהּ. עַיְּילֵיהּ לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְהָתָם, מַשְׁכֵיהּ בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא עַד דְּפַנְּיֵיהּ. בָּתַר דִּנְפַק נְפַל בֵּיתָא. אַרְגֵּישׁ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, אִיקְּפַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident. **Rav Huna had a certain** quantity of **wine in a certain dilapidated house and he wanted to move it,** but he was afraid that the building would collapse upon his entry. **He brought Rav Adda bar Ahava to there,** to the ramshackle house, and **he dragged** out a discussion with **him** concerning a matter of ***halakha* until they had removed** all the wine. **As soon as they exited, the building collapsed. Rav Adda bar Ahava realized** what had happened **and became angry.**

Rav Huna had wine stored in a dilapidated building — *bei sarya* — that he was afraid to enter. He arranged for Rav Adda bar Ahava to accompany him, drew him into a conversation in Torah learning as they walked, and kept him engaged in that conversation *ad de-panya* — until the wine had been removed from the building. Rav Adda bar Ahava, entirely immersed in Torah, was simply unaware of where he was or how he was being positioned.

The moment they left the building, *nafal beisa* — the house collapsed. *Regash Rav Adda bar Ahava* — Rav Adda bar Ahava sensed that something had been arranged without his knowledge, and *itped* — he was displeased.

### Avoid Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לְעוֹלָם אַל יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם בְּמָקוֹם סַכָּנָה וְיֹאמַר: עוֹשִׂין לִי נֵס, שֶׁמָּא אֵין עוֹשִׂין לוֹ נֵס. וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר עוֹשִׂין לוֹ נֵס — מְנַכִּין לוֹ מִזְּכִיּוֹתָיו. אָמַר רַב חָנָן: מַאי קְרָא — דִּכְתִיב: ״קָטֹנְתִּי מִכֹּל הַחֲסָדִים וּמִכׇּל הָאֱמֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rav Adda bar Ahava **holds in accordance with this** statement, **as Rabbi Yannai said: A person should never stand in a place of danger and say: A miracle will be performed for me,** and I will escape unharmed, **lest a miracle is not performed for him. And if you say** that **a miracle** will be **performed for him, they will deduct it from his merits. Rav Ḥanan said: What is the verse** that alludes to this idea? **As it is written: "I have become small from all the mercies and all the truth** that You have showed Your servant" (Genesis 32:11). In other words, the more benevolence one receives from God, the more his merit is reduced.

His displeasure was grounded in a principle he cited from Rav Yannai: *le'olam al ya'amod adam bi'mkom sakana ve'yomar osin li nes shema ein osin lo nes* — a person should never stand in a place of danger and say: a miracle will be performed for me, because perhaps a miracle will not be performed for him. *Ve'im timtza lomar osin lo nes* — and even if a miracle is performed — *menakin lo mi-zechuyosav* — it is deducted from his store of merits. The verse cited in support is *katonti mi-kol ha-chasadim u-mi-kol ha-emes* — I have been diminished by all the kindnesses and all the truth — understood here as: the receipt of miraculous kindness reduces one's account of merit.

The contrast with the story of Rav Adda bar Ahava's protective presence is instructive. In that earlier case, Rav was not deliberately putting himself in danger and relying on a miracle; he was simply noting that danger was diminished by the presence of a great *tzaddik*. Here, Rav Huna deliberately placed himself — and Rav Adda bar Ahava — in a dangerous location, relying on the latter's merits to provide protection. That is precisely what Rav Yannai's teaching prohibits. One does not engineer reliance on miraculous protection; one avoids danger. And even when a miracle does occur, it comes at a cost.

Rav Adda bar Ahava's unknowing immersion in Torah throughout this episode also presents a quietly striking image: a man of such extraordinary *zechuyot* and such complete absorption in learning that he could be brought into a collapsing building and emerge without awareness — only sensing after the fact that something had been arranged around him. The contrast with Choni Hama'agel, who was acutely aware of his power and wielded it deliberately, could not be more complete.
