## Framework & Setup

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב נַחְמָן וְעוּלָּא וַאֲבִימִי בַּר פַּפִּי, וְיָתֵיב רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַמֵּי גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וְיָתְבִי וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman and Ulla and Avimi bar Pappi were sitting, and Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ami,** who was studying with them, **was sitting among them, and they were sitting and a dilemma was raised before them**

We begin at the bottom of 63b in order to pick up the sugyot that carry us through 64a–b. The material continues the masechet's sustained examination of *yayin nesech* and the complex halachic landscape governing interactions among *Yisrael*, *ger toshav*, and *oved kochavim*.

### Three Discussions

The passage opens with a gathering of rabbis — Rav Nachman, Ulla, and Avimi bar Pappa, joined by Rabbi Chiya bar Ami — who convene to address a series of questions arising at the intersection of these three categories. We encounter three such gatherings in succession, each structured around a distinct halachic scenario. The Gemara will revisit this sitting repeatedly, and in each instance it is Rav Nachman who emerges as the decisor.

### Gemara Structure

The Gemara's repeated use of the word *yativ* — "they were sitting" — is notable as a structural feature. Normally, when a second *yativ* appears naming an additional figure, it signals the arrival of a decision-maker. Here, however, the decisions in all three discussions belong to Rav Nachman, who was already present at the outset. The repetition of *yativ* thus serves to mark the transition from one distinct question to the next, rather than to introduce a new authority.

## Breaking Barrels

### Problem Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂכָרוֹ לִשְׁבּוֹר בְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּרוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ — אָסוּר, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֹּל לְמַעוֹטֵי תִּיפְלָה שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** If one **hired** a person **to break** barrels of **wine** used for **a libation** so the wine will spill out, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **since he has an interest in the preservation of** the barrels until he breaks them, so that he can be paid for breaking them, his wage is **forbidden, or perhaps** should it be reasoned that **any** action that one performs **to reduce impropriety [*tifela*] is permitted,** even if he is paid for the actual breaking?

The first case: a non-Jew hires a Jew to break barrels of *yayin nesech* — real *avodah zarah* wine. On its face, this seems like straightforward prohibited benefit: the Jew is being employed in work that involves *yayin nesech*, and he stands to receive wages from it. But the scenario introduces a complication. The Jew's very livelihood in this case depends on those barrels remaining intact long enough for him to break them. He has a financial interest, in other words, in the preservation — *kiyum* — of the *yayin nesech*.

### Two Approaches

The Gemara frames the question as follows: *mi amrinan kevan d'rotze b'kiyumo assur* — since the Jew desires the preservation of these barrels (so he can be paid to break them), is his wage thereby forbidden? Or, *o dilma kol lemayutei tifla shapir dami* — can we reason that since the ultimate act reduces the *tifla*, the impropriety, the existence of *yayin nesech* in the world, the arrangement is permitted?

### Rav Nachman's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: יִשְׁבּוֹר, וְתָבֹא עָלָיו בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: He may break** them, **and let a blessing come upon him.**

Rav Nachman rules: *yishbor vetavo alav beracha* — let him break the barrels, and may he be blessed for it. The Gemara then seeks a *beraita* to support this ruling, and the support comes from an unexpected direction — the laws of *kilayim*.

### Kilayim Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין עוֹדְרִין עִם הַגּוֹי בְּכִלְאַיִם, אֲבָל עוֹקְרִין עִמּוֹ, כְּדֵי לְמַעֵוטי אֶת הַתִּיפְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not hoe together with a gentile in** a field that contains a forbidden mixture of **diverse kinds,** **but one may uproot** diverse kinds **with him, in order to reduce impropriety.**

The *beraita* states: *ein odrim im oved kochavim bekilayim* — one may not hoe or till a field together with a non-Jew when that field contains *kilayim*. However, continues the *baraita*, at the top of 64a: *aval okrim imo kedei lemi'utei hatiflah* — one may uproot together with him, since the purpose is to reduce the impropriety. The parallel to the barrel-breaking case is clear: just as uprooting *kilayim* reduces something forbidden even though the worker temporarily benefits from its existence, so too breaking barrels of *yayin nesech* is permitted as an act of diminishing *tifla*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: הַמְקַיֵּים בְּכִלְאַיִם לוֹקֶה. דְּתַנְיָא: הַמְנַכֵּשׁ וְהַמְחַפֶּה בְּכִלְאַיִם לוֹקֶה, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages initially **assumed** that in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that not only one who sows or grows diverse kinds, but even **one who maintains diverse kinds, is flogged. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who removes the weeds** interfering with the growth of the plants **or who covers up** the seeds **of diverse kinds** with earth **is flogged. Rabbi Akiva says: Even one who maintains** them instead of actively uprooting them is flogged.

The Gemara pauses to identify whose opinion the *beraita* reflects. *Sevaruha ha meni Rabbi Akiva hi* — they initially assumed this follows Rabbi Akiva, who rules that *hamekayem bekilayim lokeh* — one who merely maintains *kilayim*, without even sowing or tilling, is liable to lashes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״שָׂדְךָ לֹא תִזְרַע כִּלְאָיִם״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זוֹרֵעַ, מְקַיֵּים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא כִּלְאַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Akiva?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "You shall not sow your field with diverse kinds of seed"** (Leviticus 19:19). **I have** derived **only** the case of **one who sows. From where** is it derived that **one who maintains** diverse kinds also receives lashes? **The verse states: "Not…diverse kinds of seed,"** indicating that there should not be diverse kinds in one's field.

This ruling of Rabbi Akiva requires its own exegetical foundation. The Torah in Vayikra 19:19 reads: *et chukotai tishmoru behemat'cha lo tarbia kilayim sadecha lo tizra kilayim uveged kilayim shatnez lo yaaleh alecha*. The plain reading offers three distinct prohibitions: forbidden crossbreeding of animals, forbidden sowing of mixed seeds, and the prohibition of *shatnez*. But Rabbi Akiva reads the verse with a different division: *behemat'cha lo tarbia* — [pause] — *kilayim sadecha lo* — and then *tizra kilayim*. By detaching the word *kilayim* from the animal prohibition and attaching it to the field prohibition, Rabbi Akiva derives an additional restriction: not merely *zorea* (sowing) is forbidden, but *kilayim sadecha lo* — any act of benefiting the *kilayim* field at all.

This is an elegant piece of midrash. It is worth pausing on the word *kilayim* itself. The root *keleh* means prison. As Rav Hirsch notes, the concept captures the idea that certain things imprison one another — when placed together, they deny each other full expression or growth. Whether applied to animal crossbreeding, mixed seeds, or *shatnez* (wool and linen, representing the animal and plant kingdoms respectively), the prohibition captures a spiritual incompatibility, a confinement that results from forced combination.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ לְמַעוֹטֵי תִּיפְלָה — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its support for the opinion of Rav Naḥman: It is prohibited to maintain diverse kinds, **but nevertheless,** if one wishes to maintain diverse kinds temporarily in order **to** be paid for uprooting them, thereby **reducing impropriety,** it is **permitted.** Similarly, it is permitted for one to receive payment for breaking barrels of wine used for a libation.

The *beraita* further specifies: *hamenakesh vehamachape bekilayim lokeh* — one who weeds to promote growth or covers the seeds of *kilayim* is liable. From this Rabbi Akiva extends the prohibition to *hamekayem*, one who supports or maintains the *kilayim* without directly interacting with the soil — for instance, by building a fence around the field.

Now the Gemara raises a difficulty. If this *beraita* reflects Rabbi Akiva's view — that even *kiyum* of *kilayim* is forbidden and punishable — then permitting the worker to temporarily maintain the field for the purpose of getting paid to uproot it seems to contradict Rabbi Akiva rather than reflect him. The Gemara registers this tension.

### Free vs. Paid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָא מַנִּי? רַבָּנַן הִיא. אִי רַבָּנַן, מַאי אִירְיָא עוֹקְרִין? אֲפִילּוּ קַיּוֹמֵי נָמֵי שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּקָא עָבֵיד בְּחִנָּם, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לִיתֵּן לָהֶם מַתְּנַת חִנָּם אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*, which deems it permitted for one to uproot diverse kinds with a gentile? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who permit one to maintain diverse kinds, but prohibit one from maintaining wine used for a libation, so there is no proof from this *baraita* in support of Rav Naḥman's opinion. The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, why** does the *tanna* **specifically** permit **uprooting** with a gentile? **Even maintaining** the diverse kinds is **permitted.** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where he performed** the uprooting **unpaid, and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **giving an unpaid gift to** a gentile is **prohibited.**

The Gemara resolves the difficulty by reframing the case entirely: *hacha bemai askinan? kegon dik'avid chinam* — what are we actually discussing here? A case where the uprooting was performed without any payment at all. And this, the Gemara says, is the view of Rav Yehuda, who holds that *liten lahem matnat chinam assur* — it is forbidden to give a free gift to a non-Jew.

On this reading, the *beraita* is not about someone being paid to uproot *kilayim*. It is about someone who uproots *kilayim* from a non-Jew's field as a *matnat chinam*, for free. Despite the prohibition on giving free benefit to a non-Jew, this gratuitous uprooting is permitted — because it reduces something forbidden.

### Reducing Issur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נִשְׁמַע לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לָאו אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אָסוּר לִיתֵּן לָהֶם מַתְּנַת חִנָּם, אֲבָל לְמַעוֹטֵי תִּיפְלָה שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הַמְקַיֵּים בְּכִלְאַיִם לוֹקֶה, לְמַעוֹטֵי תִּיפְלָה שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. וְתוּ לָא מִידִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reasons that Rav Naḥman's ruling can in any event be proven from this *baraita*: **From** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda we may understand** the *halakha* **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. Doesn't Rabbi Yehuda say** that **it is prohibited to give** a gentile **an unpaid gift, but to reduce impropriety** he holds that it is **permitted** to work with a gentile? The same can be said **according to Rabbi Akiva as well: Although Rabbi Akiva says** that **one who maintains diverse kinds is flogged,** he presumably holds that if the purpose is **to reduce impropriety,** it is **permitted.** The Gemara concludes: **And nothing more** is to be said on this matter.

From Rav Yehuda's ruling, the Gemara derives a broader principle applicable also to Rabbi Akiva: *lechalot issur muttar* — to diminish or eliminate a forbidden thing is permitted. The Gemara states this with elegant economy: does not Rav Yehuda say it is forbidden to give a free gift to a non-Jew? — *aval lemi'utei tifla shapir dami* — and yet, to reduce an impropriety is permitted. *Lerabbi Akiva nami* — the same logic applies to Rabbi Akiva: even though he rules that *hamekayem bekilayim lokeh*, the act of *lemi'utei tifla* remains permitted. *Vetu lo midei* — and there is nothing more to be said.

## Avodat Kochavim Money

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדוּר יָתְבִי וְקָמִבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: דְּמֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד גּוֹי, מַהוּ? מִי תּוֹפֶסֶת דָּמֶיהָ בְּיַד גּוֹי, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman, Ulla, Avimi bar Pappi, and Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami **were sitting again and a dilemma was raised before them:** With regard to **the proceeds from** the sale of an object of **idol worship** that are **in the possession of a gentile,** who sold the object to another, **what is** the *halakha*? **Does** the object of idol worship **transfer** its forbidden status **to the money** that is **in the possession of a gentile,** as it would to money in the possession of a Jew, **or not?**

The same rabbis reconvene — *hador yatvei vekamibayei lehu* — and a second question is placed before them. *Dmei avodat kochavim beyad oved kochavim mahu* — what is the status of money that a non-Jew has received as proceeds from the sale of an object of idol worship? May a Jew accept such money, for instance, in a commercial transaction?

### Rav Nachman Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב נַחְמָן: מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּמֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד גּוֹי מוּתָּרִין
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to them: It stands to reason** that **the proceeds from** the sale of an object of **idol worship in the possession of a gentile are permitted.**

Rav Nachman rules, *mistabra dmei avodat kochavim beyad oved kochavim mutarin* — it seems that such money, in the hands of the non-Jew, is permitted.

### Conversion Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּהָנְהוּ דַּאֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ זַבִּינוּ כֹּל מָה דְּאִית לְכוּ, וְתוּ אִיתְגַּיַּירוּ.
>
> **Translation:** This may be proven **from certain** gentiles **who came before Rabba bar Avuh** to convert. Rabba bar Avuh **said to them: Go sell everything that you have,** including your objects of idol worship, **and** then **come** back to me to **convert.**

The Gemara adduces a case as apparent support: a group of non-Jews came before Rabba bar Avuha seeking to convert. He told them: *zilu zevinu kol mah de'it lechu vetu itgayeru* — go, sell everything you have, and then come and convert. The implication is that they could sell their idolatrous objects, receive money for them, and then enter the Jewish people with those funds — suggesting that *dmei avodat kochavim beyad oved kochavim* is indeed permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר: דְּמֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד גּוֹי מוּתָּרִין. וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּדַעְתֵּיהּ לְאִיגַּיּוֹרֵי — וַדַּאי בַּטְּלַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** he gave this advice? Isn't it **because he maintains** that **the proceeds from** the sale of an object of **idol worship in the possession of a gentile are permitted,** and therefore he suggested they sell the objects of idol worship so they could derive benefit from the money after they converted? The Gemara rejects this proof: **But perhaps it is different there, as since** their **intention is to convert,** they **certainly revoked** the idolatrous status of these objects, and when they sold them they were selling permitted items.

But the Gemara immediately questions whether this constitutes a genuine proof. *Vedilma shani hatam dekeivan deda'ateih le'igyurei vadai batla* — perhaps that case is different, because since their intention was to convert, the status of the *avodat kochavim* was clearly already annulled in their minds before the sale took place. The money they received would thus not be *dmei avodat kochavim* in the ordinary sense. The story of Rabba bar Avuha, therefore, cannot serve as a clean proof for Rav Nachman's general ruling.

### Creditor Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיָה נוֹשֶׁה בְּגוֹי מָנֶה, וּמָכַר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְהֵבִיא לוֹ, יֵין נֶסֶךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ — מוּתָּר, אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״הַמְתֵּן לִי עַד שֶׁאֶמְכּוֹר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְאָבִיא לָךְ״, ״יֵין נֶסֶךְ וְאָבִיא לָךְ״ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** proof may be brought **from here,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: In the case of **a Jew who was a creditor to a gentile** for the amount of **one hundred dinars, and** the gentile **sold** an object of **idol worship and** from the proceeds **brought him** the payment of the debt, or sold **wine** used for **a libation and** from the proceeds **brought him** the payment of the debt, the money is **permitted. But if** the gentile **said to him: Wait for me until I sell** an object of **idol worship and** from the proceeds **I will bring you** the payment of the debt, or: Wait until I sell **wine** used for **a libation and** from the proceeds **I will bring you** the payment of the debt, this money is **forbidden.** This proves that the proceeds of an object of idol worship in the possession of a gentile are permitted.

The Gemara therefore looks elsewhere: *ela me'ha* — rather, from this case. A Jew was a creditor to a non-Jew for one hundred *dinar*. The non-Jew, in order to repay the debt, sold his *avodat kochavim* and brought the proceeds to the Jew — or sold his *yayin nesech* and brought the proceeds. In such a case, the Gemara rules: *mutar* — permitted. However, if the non-Jew said to the Jew: *hamten li ad she'emkor avodat kochavim ve'avi lecha* — wait for me while I go sell my idolatrous object and then bring you the money — that is forbidden.

### Timing Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: סֵיפָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּי רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause** that it is permitted, **and what is different** in **the latter clause** that it is forbidden? **Rav Sheshet said:** In **the latter clause** the proceeds are forbidden **because** the Jew **desires the preservation of** the object of idol worship or wine used for a libation, since he knows that the gentile must sell it in order to repay the debt.

### Rav Sheshet

The Gemara asks: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa?* — what is the difference between the first scenario and the second? Rav Sheshet explains: *seifa mishum dehava leh kerotzeh bekiyumo* — in the second case, where the non-Jew has told the Jew to wait while he sells the *avodat kochavim*, the Jew now has an active interest in the preservation of the idolatrous object. He wants it to remain intact, unharmed, unsold to anyone else, because his repayment depends on it. This is precisely the concern — *rotzeh bekiyumo* — that renders the arrangement forbidden. In the first case, the Jew receives payment without any such awareness or investment in the source of funds, and the money is therefore permitted.

## Inheritance Division

### Inheritance Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: גֵּר וְגוֹי שֶׁיָּרְשׁוּ אֲבִיהֶן גּוֹי, גֵּר יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: ״טוֹל אַתָּה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וַאֲנִי מָעוֹת״, ״טוֹל אַתָּה יֵין נֶסֶךְ וַאֲנִי פֵּירוֹת״; אִם מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לִרְשׁוּת הַגֵּר — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if he desires its preservation in a case like this, is** the money **forbidden? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Demai* 6:10): In the case of **a convert and a gentile who inherited from their gentile father,** the **convert can say to** his gentile brother: **You take** the objects of **idol worship and I** will take the **money,** or: **You take the wine** used for **a libation and I** will take the **produce;** but **if** they make this exchange **after** the property **came into the possession of the convert,** it is **forbidden.**

The rabbis convene a third time. The case before them: a convert and his brother, an idol worshiper, are inheriting from their father who was a non-Jew. The estate includes objects of *avodah zarah*, *yayin nesech*, and ordinary property. The Gemara presents this scenario without any apparent surprise — such mixed families evidently existed, and the Talmud addresses their legal reality directly.

### Division Proposal

The convert proposes a division: *tol ata avodah zarah va'ani maot, tol ata yayin nesech va'ani perot* — you take the idolatrous objects, I'll take the money; you take the *yayin nesech*, I'll take the produce. On its surface this seems workable: each brother receives his share of the estate, and the *ger* avoids taking possession of prohibited items.

### Possession Problem

The Gemara identifies the problem: *im misheba'u lerashut hager assur* — if the prohibited items have already come into the *ger*'s possession, the arrangement is forbidden. Once the *ger* technically acquires the estate jointly with his brother, he has a stake in the *avodah zarah* and *yayin nesech*, and he has an interest in their preservation as the mechanism by which his brother will receive his share and the *ger* will receive his own. The concern of *rotzeh bekiyumo* applies here as well.

### Shards Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא: מַתְנִיתִין בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הַמִּתְחַלֶּקֶת לְפִי שְׁבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Ulla said:** The ruling of the **mishna** is stated **with regard to** an object of **idol worship** whose value **can be distributed among its shards,** i.e., even if it were broken to pieces its value would remain, so the convert does not desire its preservation.

Rav Bar Ula limits the scope of the ruling: *matnitin baavodah zarah hamithaleket lefi shevareha* — this ruling applies specifically to idolatrous objects that can be divided according to their shards or component pieces. If the *avodah zarah* is made of gold, for example, then breaking it apart creates valuable pieces that can be distributed. In such a case, even a fragmented object retains its worth, and the division is meaningful.

### Wine & Shards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, יֵין נֶסֶךְ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בְּחֶרֶס הַדְרְיָינִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This resolution **works out well** in the case of objects of **idol worship,** but with regard to **wine** used for **a libation, what is there to say?** There is no situation in which the convert does not desire the preservation of the wine until the exchange. The Gemara answers: The ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to Hadrianic earthenware** permeated with wine used for a libation. Since the wine can be extracted by soaking the earthenware in water, the convert does not desire that the vessel remain intact.

The Gemara then asks about *yayin nesech*: how does the concept of division by shards apply to wine? One might think liquid cannot be divided by breaking. But the Gemara recalls the case of *cheres Adriani* — the distinctive clay vessels discussed earlier in the masechet, which were not fully fired and were soaked in wine during manufacture. The broken shards of such a vessel, when soaked in water, would release a concentrated wine essence, rendering them of significant value. Thus even *yayin nesech*, in the form of *cheres Adriani*, can have value in shards — and the *ger*'s desire for his own portion might cause him to want those shards preserved.

### Rav Papa's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יִגָּנֵובוּ וְשֶׁלֹּא יֵאָבֵדוּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: יְרוּשַּׁת הַגֵּר קָאָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי יְרוּשַּׁת הַגֵּר, דְּאַקִּילוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַחְזוֹר לְקִלְקוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't he desire its preservation** in the sense **that it should not be stolen or lost? Rather, Rav Pappa said** that there is a different resolution: **You say** there is a difficulty from the case of **the inheritance of a convert? The inheritance of a convert is different, as the Sages were lenient with regard to it,** as a rabbinic **decree, lest he return to his corrupted** ways if it were prohibited for him to inherit property from his father. In the case of one who is not a convert and desires the preservation of an object of idol worship, it is prohibited for him to profit from it.

This raises the question anew: does not the *ger* have an interest in the preservation of the idolatrous estate, given that his own inheritance depends on his brother receiving it? Rav Papa addresses this: *shani yerushat hager deakilu bah rabanan gezeirah shema yakhzor lekilkulo* — the inheritance of the *ger* is a different matter, because the Rabbis were lenient in this domain out of concern that if the *ger* is denied his inheritance, he might return to his former ways. The potential for *kibbul kilkulo* — his spiritual backsliding — justifies a leniency that would not otherwise apply.

### Inheritance vs. Partners…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁיָּרְשׁוּ, אֲבָל נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement,** that a convert and a gentile are permitted to divide up common property that includes objects of idol worship, **said?** This is said with regard to property **that they inherited; but** if **they were partners,** it is **prohibited.**

A *baraita* at the top of 64b confirms and sharpens the ruling: *bamedebarim amurim sheyerashu aval nishtatefu assur* — these leniencies apply only when the *ger* and the *oved kochavim* are heirs; if they are business partners, it is forbidden. The distinction is clear: a halachic inheritance relationship carries leniencies that a commercial partnership does not. A Jew may be a business partner with a non-Jew, but not in a venture that involves deriving benefit from *avodah zarah*.

## Ger Toshav Annulment

### Ger Toshav Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדוּר יְתַבוּ וְקָמִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב מַהוּ שֶׁיְּבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? דְּפָלַח — מְבַטֵּיל, דְּלָא פָּלַח — לָא מְבַטֵּיל, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֹּל דְּבַר מִינֵּיהּ מְבַטֵּיל, וְהַאי בַּר מִינֵיהּ הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara mentions another discussion among Rav Naḥman, Ulla, Avimi bar Pappi, and Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami. **They were sitting again and a dilemma was raised before them:** With regard to **a gentile who resides in Eretz Yisrael and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*],** including the prohibition against engaging in idol worship, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the possibility **that he can revoke** the status of objects of **idol worship?** Is it the case that one **who worships** idols **can revoke** the status of one, but one **who does not worship** them **cannot revoke** the status of one? **Or perhaps** should it be reasoned that **anyone who is of the same kind** as idol worshippers, i.e., a gentile, **can revoke** its status, **and** a *ger toshav* **is of the same kind** as idol worshippers?

The third question posed in this series: *Ger Toshav mahu sheyevatel avodat kochavim?* — can a *ger toshav* annul the status of an object of idol worship? The question sits against the background of an earlier discussion involving Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan about whether a Jew can ever annul *avodah zarah* — and the elaborate concern about a Jew finding such an object in the marketplace, acquiring it through *kinyan*, and thereafter being unable to remove its prohibited status. The *ger toshav* occupies an intermediate category, and his ability to perform *bitul* is genuinely uncertain.

### Worship Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ רַב נַחְמָן: מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּפָלַח — מְבַטֵּיל, דְּלָא פָּלַח — לָא מְבַטֵּיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to them: It stands to reason** that one **who worships** idols **can revoke** the status of one, but one **who does not worship** them **cannot revoke** the status of one.

Rav Nachman articulates the operative principle: *dephalach mevatel delo phalach lo mevatel* — one who worships an idol can annul it; one who does not worship cannot. The question is then where the *ger toshav* falls. One might argue that since he does not actually worship idols — that is his defining characteristic — he lacks the capacity to annul. Alternatively, one might argue: *kol deminei mevatel vehai bar minei hu* — anyone who belongs to the same general category of idol-worshiping nations can annul, and perhaps the *ger toshav* counts as *bar minei*, of the same kind, even if he personally abstains.

### Marketplace Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁמָּצָא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בַּשּׁוּק, עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּאתָה לְיָדוֹ — אוֹמֵר לְגוֹי וּמְבַטְּלָהּ, מִשֶּׁבָּאתָה לְיָדוֹ — אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר לְגוֹי וּמְבַטְּלָהּ; מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: גּוֹי מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, בֵּין עוֹבְדָהּ וּבֵין שֶׁאֵין עוֹבְדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this ruling from a *baraita*: In the case of **a Jew who found** an object of **idol worship in the marketplace, as long as it has not** yet **come into his possession, he can tell a gentile, and** the gentile **can revoke** its idolatrous status. **Once it has come into his possession, he cannot tell a gentile and** have the gentile **revoke** its status. This applies to any gentile, **because** the Sages **said: A gentile can revoke** the status of **his own** object of **idol worship or that of another** gentile, **whether he worships it or whether he does not worship it.**

A challenge is raised from a *baraita*: if a Jew finds an object of idolatry in the marketplace, as long as it has not yet come into his possession, he may ask a non-Jew to annul its status, and it becomes permissible for him to take it. Once it has come into his possession, however, he may no longer do so, because *oved kochavim mevatel avodat kochavim shelo veshel chaveiro* — a non-Jew can annul the idolatrous status of his own object or that of his fellow — and the phrase continues: *bein ovdah uvein she'eino ovdah* — whether he worships it or does not worship it.

### Friend's Idolatry

The phrase *shel chaveiro* — of his fellow — raises its own question: who is this *chaveiro* whose *avodah zarah* the non-Jew can annul? The *baraita*'s language suggests that a non-Jew can cancel the idolatrous status of an object that was not his own. This was discussed in earlier passages as well: a Jew who encounters *avodah zarah* in the street can, before taking possession, request a non-Jew — not necessarily the original worshiper — to annul it, after which the object may be handled freely. The spiritual danger, whatever its precise nature, is thereby neutralized.

### Debate on Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עוֹבְדָהּ״, וּמַאי ״שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹבְדָהּ״? אִילֵּימָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי גּוֹי, הַיְינוּ שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ! אֶלָּא לָאו ״עוֹבְדָהּ״ — גּוֹי, וּמַאי ״שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹבְדָהּ״ — גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב נָמֵי מְבַטֵּל!
>
> **Translation:** **What** is meant by the phrase: **Worships it, and what** is meant by the phrase: **Does not worship it? If we say** both **this and that** are referring to **a gentile, this is** the same as the previous statement in the *baraita*, that a gentile can revoke the status of **his own** object of idol worship **or that of another** gentile, i.e., an object that he worships or one that another gentile worships. **Rather, isn't it** to be understood that the phrase: **Worships it,** is referring to **a gentile? And what** is the meaning of the phrase: **Does not worship it?** It is referring to **a *ger toshav*,** who does not worship any idols. **And learn from it** that **a *ger toshav* can also revoke** the status of objects of idol worship.

The more pressing interpretive question concerns the clause *bein ovdah uvein she'eino ovdah* — between one who worships it and one who does not. This could be understood as referring to the same non-Jewish individual: whether he personally worships this particular idol or does not, he can still annul. Or it might be read as distinguishing between a full *oved kochavim* (*ovdah*) and a *ger toshav* (*she'eino ovdah*), thereby implying that the *ger toshav* too has the capacity for *bitul*. If so, the *baraita* would constitute a challenge to Rav Nachman's principle.

### Non-Jew Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי גּוֹי, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ הַיְינוּ ״שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ״: רֵישָׁא — זֶה וָזֶה לִפְעוֹר, וְזֶה וָזֶה לְמַרְקוּלִיס; סֵיפָא — זֶה לִפְעוֹר וְזֶה לְמַרְקוּלִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation. **No, actually, I will say to you** that **this** phrase **and that** phrase are both referring to **a gentile, and** with regard to that **which you say,** that **this is** the same as the statement concerning **his** object of idol worship **or that of another** gentile, it can be explained as follows: **The first clause** is referring to a case where both gentiles worship the same idol, e.g., **this** one **and that** one both worship **Peor, or this** one **and that** one both worship **Mercury,** and the *baraita* is teaching that one can revoke the status of an idol that belongs to the other. **The latter clause,** which distinguishes between one who worships it and one who does not worship it, is referring to a case where **this** one worships **Peor and that** one worships **Mercury,** indicating that an idolater can revoke the status of an idol that he does not worship at all, but only if he is himself an idolater, as opposed to a *ger toshav*.

The Gemara rejects the reading that would include the *ger toshav*: *le'olam eima lach idi ve'idi oved kochavim* — in all cases, both terms refer to the *oved kochavim*, the non-Jew proper. As for the distinction between *ovdah* and *she'eino ovdah*, the Gemara explains it through two sub-cases. *Reisha* — the first case — refers to *zeh vezeh lePe'or, zeh vezeh leMarkulis*: both the owner and the fellow worship the same deity, whether Pe'or or Markulis. *Seifa* — the second case — refers to *zeh lePe'or vezeh leMarkulis*: the one who annuls worships a different deity than the one associated with the object. Even so, the annulment is valid. The point is entirely about non-Jews of varying cultic affiliations — the *ger toshav* does not enter the picture.

## Ger Toshav Definition

### Multiple Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵיזֶהוּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב? כֹּל שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵרִים שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Who is a *ger toshav*?** It is **anyone who has accepted upon himself before three *ḥaverim*,** i.e., people devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot, especially *halakhot* of ritual purity, *teruma*, and tithes, **not to worship idols.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

Having determined that the *ger toshav* cannot annul *avodah zarah*, the Gemara steps back to define who the *ger toshav* actually is. The *baraita* presents a *machloket* among *tanna'im*, and the range of opinions reflects genuine disagreement about the legal and theological status of this category.

### Rabbi Meir's View

Rabbi Meir defines a *ger toshav* as *kol shekibel alav bifnei shelosha chaverim shelo la'avod avodat kochavim* — anyone who accepted upon himself, in front of three *chaverim* (a quasi-judicial body of punctilious observers), that he will not engage in *avodah zarah*. This is a formal, public commitment, analogous in structure to other acts of legal acceptance before a *beis din*.

### Chachamim's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו שֶׁבַע מִצְווֹת שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם בְּנֵי נֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Anyone who has accepted upon himself** observance of the **seven mitzvot that the descendants of Noah accepted upon themselves** is a *ger toshav*.

The *chachamim* offer a different criterion: *kol shekibel alav sheva mitzvot shekiblu aleihem bnei Noach* — anyone who accepted upon himself the seven *mitzvot* of the *bnei Noach*. This immediately raises a question: if the *ger toshav* is defined simply by his acceptance of the seven Noahide laws, why is he called a *ger toshav* rather than a *ben Noach*? The distinction must lie elsewhere — the *ger toshav* presumably does more than the ordinary *ben Noach*, even on the view of *chachamim*.

### Third Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵלּוּ לֹא בָּאוּ לִכְלַל גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב, אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב? זֶה גֵּר אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת, שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו לְקַיֵּים כׇּל מִצְווֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה חוּץ מֵאִיסּוּר נְבֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Others say: These have not entered the category of *ger toshav*. Rather, who is a *ger toshav*? This is a convert** who **eats** unslaughtered **animal carcasses,** which are not kosher, but **who has accepted upon himself to observe all of the mitzvot that are stated in the Torah except for the prohibition** against eating unslaughtered **carcasses.**

A third view (*acheirim omrim*) holds that the first two opinions do not describe a *ger toshav* at all. The true *ger toshav* is: *zeh ger ochel nevelot shekibel alav lekayem kol mitzvot ha'amurot baTorah chutz mi'issur nevelot* — one who accepted upon himself to observe all the *mitzvot* stated in the Torah, with one exception: the prohibition of *nevelot*, the requirement that meat be slaughtered according to halachic law. He is, in other words, nearly fully observant, living among Jews (*toshav* — a resident, not a transient), and distinguished only by this single leniency for himself.

One might ask: does this not create other complications? If the *ger toshav* observes all Torah law, including *Shabbat*, that raises a well-known concern derived elsewhere in the Talmud that a non-Jew who observes *Shabbat* is liable for a capital offense. The Gemara does not resolve this tension here, but the question merits attention.

### Wine & Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְיַיחֲדִין אֶצְלוֹ יַיִן, וְאֵין מַפְקִידִין אֶצְלוֹ יַיִן, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּעִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל מְיַיחֲדִין אֶצְלוֹ יַיִן וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּעִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ גּוֹיִם, שַׁמְנוֹ כְּיֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Whatever the definition of a *ger toshav*, the following *halakhot* apply to him: **One may leave him alone with wine** briefly without Jewish supervision with no concern that he might use it for a libation, thereby rendering it forbidden to Jews, as he is not an idol worshipper. **But one may not deposit wine with him** for an extended period of time, lest he exchange it with the wine of a gentile, which is forbidden. **And** this applies **even in a town that has a Jewish majority. But one may leave him alone with wine** briefly without Jewish supervision **even in a town with a majority of gentiles. His oil is** treated **like his wine** in terms of its permissibility.

The *baraita* continues to define the practical parameters of the *ger toshav*'s status: *miyachadim etzlo yayin ve'ein mafkidim etzlo yayin* — we may leave wine with him briefly and unsupervised, but we may not entrust wine to him for extended safekeeping. The distinction between *yichud* (a brief, supervised deposit) and *hafkada* (a longer-term entrusting) reflects a calibrated level of trust. We do not suspect the *ger toshav* of actively mixing the wine with *yayin nesech*, but we worry that over time, inattention might lead him to mix it with his own wine, about which he is less careful. Even in a city with a Jewish majority, the brief deposit is permitted; even in a city with a non-Jewish majority, it is permitted. But the long-term deposit is forbidden in either circumstance.

*Shamno keyeino* — his oil is treated like his wine for these purposes: benefit (*hana'ah*) is permitted, but drinking (*shtiyah*) is forbidden.

### Wine Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְנוֹ כְּיֵינוֹ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? שֶׁמֶן מִי קָא הָוֵי יֵין נֶסֶךְ? אֶלָּא, יֵינוֹ כְּשַׁמְנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interjects: **His oil is like his wine?** Can this **enter your mind? Does** the **oil** of a gentile **become,** i.e., assume the status of, **wine** used for **a libation? Rather,** the *baraita* should be emended as follows: **His wine is like his oil.** It is permitted to derive benefit from it, but not to consume it.

The Gemara immediately challenges the formulation: *shemen mi ka havi yayin nesech?* — can oil even become *yayin nesech*? The categories are being conflated. The Gemara corrects the formulation: it is not that his oil is like his wine, but that *yeino keshamno* — his wine is treated like his oil. That is, the wine of the *ger toshav* is permitted for *hana'ah* but forbidden for drinking, just as his oil would be.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁאָר כׇּל דָּבָר הֲרֵי הוּא כְּגוֹי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵינוֹ יֵין נֶסֶךְ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מוּתָּר בִּשְׁתִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And with regard to all other matters,** a *ger toshav* **is** treated **like a gentile. Rabban Shimon says: His wine is** treated like **wine** used for **a libation. And some say** he says: Even **drinking** it is **permitted.**

Rabban Shimon, however, goes further: *yeino yayin nesech* — the wine of the *ger toshav* is full *yayin nesech*. His reasoning reflects a conservative concern: the *ger toshav* is not scrupulous enough for us to trust his wine at any level. Like a vegan restaurant that theoretically keeps no meat on the premises but may not have checked every ingredient — one simply avoids the uncertainty altogether. And at the other end of the spectrum, there is an opinion that holds *mutar bishtiyah* — the wine of the *ger toshav* is even permitted for drinking. He has committed formally, before a *beis din*, to full observance; there is no more reason to doubt his wine than the wine of a Jew.

### Like Oved Kochavim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא: וְלִשְׁאָר כׇּל דְּבָרָיו הֲרֵי הוּא כְּגוֹי. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לָאו דִּמְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה כְּגוֹי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא, לִיתֵּן רְשׁוּת וּלְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments on the *baraita*: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches: And with regard to all other matters,** a *ger toshav* **is** treated **like a gentile. With regard to what *halakha*** is this stated? Is it **not** teaching **that he can revoke** the status of an object of **idol worship as a gentile** can? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: No,** it is stated with regard to **giving** away rights in **a domain or renouncing** rights in **a domain** in the context of the *halakhot* of joining houses in courtyards for Shabbat.

Having surveyed these views, the Gemara returns to the original question. The *baraita* concludes: *velish'ar kol davar harey hu ke'oved kochavim* — for all other matters, the *ger toshav* is treated like an *oved kochavim*. The Gemara asks: *lemai hilchata?* — with regard to what specific halacha? Perhaps the intent is that he is like an *oved kochavim* for the purpose of annulling *avodah zarah* — which would contradict the ruling of Rav Nachman that he cannot annul.

### Reshut Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְתַנְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד מְשַׁמֵּר שַׁבַּתּוֹ בַּשּׁוּק — מְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת, שֶׁאֵין מְשַׁמֵּר שַׁבַּתּוֹ בַּשּׁוּק — אֵין מְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּת וּמְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **An apostate Jew** who nevertheless **observes his Shabbat in the marketplace,** i.e., in public, **can renounce** his rights in a shared **domain** so the other Jews in the domain may carry in it on Shabbat, but an apostate **who does not observe his Shabbat** even **in the marketplace cannot renounce** his rights in **a domain, because** the Sages **said** that only **a Jew can give** away rights in his **domain or renounce** his rights in his **domain,** and this applies in the context of joining houses in courtyards on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְגוֹי — עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to a gentile,** this is not effective **unless** the Jew **leases** his domain in the courtyard.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *lo, litten reshut u'levatel reshut* — no, the comparison to *oved kochavim* is limited to the laws of *reshut*, the domain rules that govern the creation of an *eruv* on *Shabbat*. The relevant *baraita* is cited: a Jewish *mumar* (apostate) who still observes *Shabbat* publicly can relinquish his domain rights for the purpose of establishing a shared *eruv*; one who does not observe *Shabbat* publicly cannot. The principle is that only a Jew — *noten reshut umevatel reshut* — can formally cede domain rights to enable a common *eruv*. A non-Jew cannot do this; he can only lease his domain to a Jew. How then does the *ger toshav* function? *Uve'oved kochavim ad sheyiskor* — for a non-Jew, the *reshut* must be acquired through a lease. The *ger toshav* falls into this same category for *eruv* purposes: despite his elevated status in many respects, he cannot participate in the domain-cession that only Jews may perform.

### Final Status

The conclusion of the discussion: the *ger toshav* cannot annul *avodah zarah*. His resemblance to an *oved kochavim* is limited to the specific domain of *reshut* and *eruv*. With respect to idol worship, Rav Nachman's principle holds: *dephalach mevatel delo phalach lo mevatel* — the one who worships can annul; the one who does not worship cannot. The *ger toshav*, who has formally committed to abstaining from *avodah zarah*, does not worship it, and therefore cannot annul it.
