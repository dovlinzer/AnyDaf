# Niddah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review: Cloth & Blood

### Rabbi Yishmael Debate

The daf opens with a brief review of the preceding discussion on 14b. The sugya had introduced a debate occasioned by Rebbi's praise of Rabbi Chama bar Bisa to Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose — the names of the principals in the original episode having initially been transposed. The underlying halachic question concerned the use of a *bedikah* cloth that had not been pre-checked, a topic directly relevant to the practical instruction given to *kallahs*, where the use of a pre-checked cloth is emphasized. In that dispute, Rabbi Yishmael reported that his father, Rabbi Yose, adopted the more lenient position, while Rebbi held more strictly. The reason Rebbi's view was particularly significant, notwithstanding that Rabbi Yose may have been his *rebbe*, is that Rebbi served as the head of a *yeshiva*, and the Gemara (as noted by Tosafos) reflects this in parallel passages where the relationship between the two appears more complex. The sharpening of ideas that comes from leading a group of learners conferred upon Rebbi a distinctive scholarly standing.

### Blood in Urine

The review continues with the parallel debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yose regarding a woman who urinates and blood appears in the urine. The question, structurally similar to the *bedikah*-cloth debate, is how much evidentiary basis suffices to assume that the blood originated from an external source rather than from a uterine flow that would render her a *niddah*. Rabbi Yose was more prepared to rule that blood in urine is presumptively not *niddah*, while Rabbi Meir held that it depends on the circumstances. This debate has direct contemporary relevance in cases involving medical conditions, where the question of what basis is needed to attribute blood to a non-uterine source is a live halachic concern.

### Blood Status Levels

The range of possible outcomes established by this discussion spans from one extreme — where she is considered a definite *niddah* at the biblical level — to the intermediate category of a *kesem*, a stain that is of rabbinic force, where there is some question about the origin of the blood but she is nonetheless ruled *teme'ah* rabbinically — and finally to the most lenient conclusion of *tahor legamrei*, entirely pure.

---

## Immediate Finding

### Veset Parable

The Gemara now continues from the middle of 14b. The *mishnah* states that if blood is found on her *bedikah* cloth immediately after relations, they are assumed to have had relations while she was a *niddah*, both are *tamei*, and a *chatat* is brought. The Gemara asks: what is the precise definition of "immediately"? A *beraisa* is cited — *eizehu shiur veset* — asking what constitutes the *veset* in this context. The term *veset* is used here in an unusual sense, not referring to an expected menstrual period but to the window of time that qualifies as "immediate." The *beraisa* offers a *mashal*: like an attendant and a witness standing by the doorframe — *betzad ha-mashkof* — where as soon as the attendant exits the witness enters. The two overlap in time without any real gap. That is what is meant by *veset* in this context — the immediately subsequent check after relations.

### Wipe vs. Bedikah

There is a further qualification: this immediacy counts only as a *kinuach* — a wipe — and not as a full *bedikah*. If the woman spent the amount of time required to perform a thorough *bedikah* before inspecting the cloth, that interval already introduces uncertainty as to whether the blood was present at the time of relations. Thus, the definition of "immediate" is both about closeness in time to the act itself and about brevity of the checking process.

### After Immediate

If the cloth is examined at any point after this narrow window — *nimtza al shela achar zman* — the couple is exempt from a *korban*, though both remain *tamei misafek*. A *beraisa* adds that they are in fact liable in an *asham taluy*, a conditional guilt-offering brought when there is genuine doubt whether a transgression was committed. The *tana* of the *mishnah*, who appears to imply total exemption from any *korban*, is then questioned: why no *asham taluy*? The Gemara answers that an *asham taluy* requires an objective basis in which a permitted and prohibited object stood before the person and one was acted upon without knowing which — *chaticha misht'ei chatichos*. Here, at the moment of the alleged transgression, neither party knew she was a *niddah*. The *issur* only became apparent afterward; at the moment itself, there was no clear prohibition standing before them, and therefore there is no basis for an *asham taluy* according to the *mishnah*.

### Three Periods

Working through the sources, the Gemara establishes three temporal periods. The first is "immediate" — checking within a second, cloth already in hand — which yields a definite *chatat*. The second is "after immediate" — perhaps a few seconds, involving the time needed to retrieve a *bedikah* cloth and check — which constitutes a biblical *safek* potentially generating an *asham taluy*, with the husband equally *tamei*. The third period begins from the time it takes to get off the bed and lasts up to twenty-four hours; this period is purely rabbinic. In this third period, the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* applies, and the husband is *tamei* for having been in contact with a woman who is rabbinically *niddah* — rendering him a *rishon* — but is not considered a *boel niddah*, which would make him an *av ha-tumah* for a full seven days.

## Source Conflict

### Rav Chisda Solution

A difficulty now emerges. The *beraisa* cited by Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Tzadok defines the "after" period — *eizehu achar zman* — as the time it takes to extend one's hand under the pillow, retrieve a cloth, and check. However, the *mishnah* had described the "after" period as the time it takes to get off the bed and wash — *k'dei sheteired min ha-mitah v'tadiach et paneha*. Which is it? Is the second period defined by grabbing a cloth, or by getting off the bed?

Rav Chisda resolves this by reading the word *achar* in the *mishnah* as referring to *achar achar* — later than later. That is, when the *mishnah* speaks of "after," it is actually describing the third period, not the second. The second period begins from the moment when the cloth is not already in hand and must be retrieved from under the pillow. The third period begins from the time it takes to get off the bed. The *mishnah*, which describes getting off the bed, is thus the demarcation of the beginning of the third period. The only way the first period — "immediate" — is operative is if she is holding the *bedikah* cloth in her hand even before they separate. This is acknowledged to be essentially hypothetical in practice, resembling closely the *mashal* of the doorframe.

The Gemara then presses Rav Chisda's reading. A different *beraisa* seems to state explicitly that "after" means the time it takes to get off the bed — plainly describing this as the second period. Rav Chisda's response is that this *beraisa* is using a compressed formulation, and what it should be understood to say is: the second period is grabbing the cloth from under the pillow and checking; the third period — which is what the word *achar* in the *mishnah* denotes when it says "getting off the bed" — is the beginning of the purely rabbinic zone. A further textual challenge from a *beraisa* that seems to state *achar zman* explicitly as the time of getting off the bed, apparently placing that within the second period, is resolved similarly: the *mishnah* reads *achar* as *achar achar*, and when it says that the debate of Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* begins "after that time," the referent is precisely the period of getting off the bed — that is the beginning of the third period that is the subject of their dispute.

### Rav Ashi Solution

Rav Ashi offers an alternative approach: the two descriptions — extending a hand under the pillow to retrieve a cloth, and getting off the bed — actually describe the same temporal moment. How can they be equivalent when getting off the bed seems to take longer? Rav Ashi explains: the *beraisa*'s case of extending a hand under the pillow describes a situation where the woman does not already have a cloth in hand. The *mishnah*'s case of getting off the bed describes a situation where she does have a cloth in hand — she is holding it, gets off the bed, and immediately uses it. In the first case, she has to take time to locate and retrieve the cloth; in the second, she is already holding it but must get off the bed. The two activities may plausibly consume the same amount of time, and both therefore describe the same second period.

## Yavneh Teaching

### Rabbi Eliezer Query

At this point the Gemara cites a *beraisa*: *davar zeh sha'al Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Tzadok lifnei chachamim be-Usha* — Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Tzadok posed this very question before the council of sages at Usha. He wanted to know whether there is in fact a second period with biblical consequences, generating an *asham taluy*, and a distinct third period that is purely rabbinic.

### Sages' Uncertainty

The sages at Usha replied that they had not heard — *lo shamanu* — meaning they did not have a ruling to offer. Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Tzadok then asked whether the question was moot for them. Perhaps they ruled like Rabbi Akiva, in which case the distinction between the second and third periods carries little practical significance, since Rabbi Akiva holds that even in the third period the husband has the full *tumah* of a *boel niddah* and is an *av ha-tumah*. If one rules like Rabbi Akiva, the primary *nafka mina* between the second and third periods — the husband's level of *tumah* — disappears. The *beraisa* continues: they replied that they did not rule like Rabbi Akiva. Reconstructing the implicit dialogue, Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Tzadok apparently then offered to supply the distinction himself — what the sages in Yavneh had taught on this matter.

It is worth noting the setting: Usha was where the *beit din* relocated after Yavneh, and it is particularly associated with *takkanot*, especially in matters related to *ketubot*. One might find it surprising that such a technical question about time periods in *niddah* would occupy sages at Usha who were engaged in the project of reconstituting Jewish life after the *churban*. Yet the preservation of *Torah she-be'al peh* in all its dimensions was itself part of that project, even if Usha is more typically associated with pragmatic legislation.

### Yavneh Distinction

Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Tzadok then teaches: the sages at Yavneh ruled as follows. If she had not yet waited the time it takes to get off the bed and wash — *lo shahata k'dei she-tered min ha-mitah v'tadiach et paneha* — that is the second period. They are *tamei safeik* biblically, exempt from a *chatat*, but liable in an *asham taluy*. Once the time required to get off the bed and wash has elapsed, that is the third period — *achar zman hu zeh* — extending up to twenty-four hours or until the previous *bedikah*, whichever is more recent. This third period is entirely rabbinic.

### Husband 3rd Period

In the third, purely rabbinic period: *bo aleha metamei mishum maga* — the husband is *tamei* for having been in contact with a woman who is rabbinically *niddah*, which renders him a *rishon*. *V'eino metamei mishum boel* — but he does not carry the full seven-day *tumah* of one who had relations with a *niddah*, and he is not an *av ha-tumah*. This is the majority position of the *chachamim*.

### Rabbi Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva disputes this. *Af metamei mishum boel* — even in the third period, within the twenty-four-hour rabbinic window, the husband has the full *tumah* of a *boel niddah* and is an *av ha-tumah*. This is the *machlokes* recorded in the *mishnah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda ben Dosa in the name of Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakai goes to the opposite extreme: not only is the husband not a *boel niddah* in this third period, he is not even *tamei* for touching a woman who is rabbinically *niddah*. Were he a *kohen*, he could enter the *heichal* and offer the incense. The Gemara immediately challenges this: even if one holds like Shammai — who rules that a woman's *tumah* does not extend retroactively beyond the moment of the finding (*dayyan sha'atan*), eliminating the third period altogether — the husband was still engaged in relations and is a *ba'al keri*, rendering him *tamei* by virtue of his own seminal emission. The Gemara resolves this by positing that the case involves a situation where he did not complete the act in a way that leads to ejaculation — *be-she-lo gamar bi'ato* — meaning he was not a *ba'al keri*, and holding like Shammai, he would bear no *tumah* at all and could theoretically enter the *Beit Ha-Mikdash*.

## Source Conflict (II)

### Gemara's Kashya

The Gemara now returns to evaluate both proposed resolutions — Rav Chisda's and Rav Ashi's — in light of the explicit *beraisa* from Yavneh, which states unambiguously that the period of getting off the bed is the third period, not the second. For Rav Chisda, this is perfectly consistent: his entire approach rests on the claim that the *mishnah*'s reference to getting off the bed describes the third period, and the Yavneh *beraisa* confirms this exactly. *Bishlama l'Rav Chisda* — this works well: the leniency of the *chachamim* who do not treat the husband as a *boel niddah* is because this is the third, purely rabbinic period.

*Ela l'Rav Ashi* — but according to Rav Ashi, who maintained that getting off the bed and reaching under the pillow for a cloth are descriptions of the same second period, the *beraisa* poses a difficulty. If getting off the bed is the second period, why do the *chachamim* apply their leniency at that point? According to Rav Ashi, that should still be a biblical *safek* with full consequences. The Gemara attempts to rescue Rav Ashi by suggesting that the *beraisa* may be describing a case where she was not holding the cloth, so that getting off the bed combined with retrieving the cloth would push into the third period. But the Gemara rejects this: if the distinction hinged on whether she was *biyada* (cloth in hand) or *einah biyada* (not holding the cloth), the *beraisa* should have said so explicitly. Since it does not, Rav Ashi's equation of the two time-references cannot be sustained. The Gemara concludes: *kashya* — Rav Ashi's approach remains difficult in light of the Yavneh *beraisa*.

The practical upshot is clear regardless of which resolution is accepted for the theoretical *sugya*: by the time it takes to get off the bed, one is definitively in the third, purely rabbinic period. The three periods are: checking immediately with cloth already in hand — definite *chatat*; time elapsed to retrieve a cloth and check — biblical *safek*, possible *asham taluy*; from getting off the bed through twenty-four hours — purely rabbinic, subject to the *machlokes* between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim*.

A question arises about the husband's precise level of *tumah* in the rabbinic period. To clarify: a woman who is *niddah* is an *av ha-tumah*, as is the husband who had relations with her when she was definitely a *niddah*, as well as whatever she sits upon. Anything she merely touches is a *rishon*. In the purely rabbinic third period, having had relations before the *niddah* status was known, the husband is *tamei* rabbinically — a *rishon de-rabbanan* — for having been in contact with a woman who was at most rabbinically *niddah*. A further point of texture: in the *Torah*, the word *niddah* functions as a verbal noun — *betumat niddatah*, referring to the flow itself — and it is only in the *mishnah* and *gemara* that the woman herself is called a *niddah*. This observation bears on how we speak about these *halachot* with appropriate care.

## Stains on Cloth

### Ketem Stringency

The *mishnah* states: *u-modim chachamim l'Rabbi Akiva be-ro'eh ketem* — the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Akiva in the case of a *ketem*. A *ketem* is a stain discovered on cloth or bedding, as distinct from blood found directly on the body. On one hand, a *ketem* is more lenient in one respect: because there is a possibility the blood originated from an external source — a louse, for example — one first attempts to attribute it to such a source, and if that is possible, she is *tehorah legamrei*. If one cannot attribute it elsewhere, she is *teme'ah* rabbinically, not biblically.

### Retroactive Tumah

Yet in other respects a *ketem* generates greater stringency. When a woman sees blood from her body, the timing can be limited — the blood probably appeared very recently, allowing the retroactive *tumah* to be bounded. When a stain is discovered on cloth, there is no such natural limitation: the stain may have been there for a day, two days, a week. The retroactive extension of *tumah* is therefore potentially longer and more serious.

Rav rules: *l'mafrei'a* — the *chachamim*'s agreement with Rabbi Akiva by a *ketem* means going backward — if they had relations during the period the stain retroactively renders her *teme'ah*, the husband is as *tamei* as she is. This is the view of Rabbi Meir, who is strict with *ketamim*. Shmuel disagrees: *mikan u-le-haba* — the *mishnah* refers only to relations that occur after the stain is discovered. The rabbis, who dispute Rabbi Meir, hold that during the retroactive period covered by the *ketem*, the husband who had relations is not rendered as *tamei* as his wife. Resh Lakish aligns with Rav's reading, attributing the *mishnah* to Rabbi Meir; Rabbi Yochanan aligns with Shmuel, reading the *mishnah* as referring to the *rabbanan* going forward only.

### Prospective Tumah

According to Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan, the *mishnah*'s statement that the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Akiva regarding a *ketem* means: if relations occur *after* the stain is discovered, the husband is as *tamei* as the wife. The Gemara asks: *pshita* — is this not obvious? If she is definitely at least rabbinically *teme'ah* and he then has relations with her, of course he is equally *tamei*.

### Stricter Prospective

The Gemara answers that this ruling is not self-evident. One might have thought: since the entire twenty-four-hour retroactive framework is rabbinic, and *ketamim* are themselves rabbinic, perhaps we apply the same leniency prospectively that we apply retroactively. We have already seen that in the third, purely rabbinic twenty-four-hour period, the husband who had relations is not treated as an *av ha-tumah* — he is a *rishon*, not an *av*. One might extend this logic: since *ketamim* are also rabbinic, even going forward the husband should not be rendered as *tamei* as the wife. *Ka mashma lan* — the *mishnah* teaches that this is not so.

The Gemara then presses: why not? Both are rabbinic, so why the asymmetry? The Gemara answers with a vivid image: *ein shor shachut lefanecha* — in the twenty-four-hour retroactive period, there is no slaughtered ox standing before you. During those previous hours, there was no knowledge that she was *teme'ah*; the *tumah* is projected backward into a period when the problem was not apparent. This is why the rabbinic leniency applies — because the problematic status was not manifest at the time. *Hacha* — here, with the *ketem* in front of you, *yesh shor shachut lefanecha* — the problem is staring you in the face. When the problem is manifest and visible, we do not apply the same leniencies, even though the underlying *tumah* category is also rabbinic.

## No Taharot Status

### All Women Tahor

The Gemara now turns to a *mishnah* of considerable practical importance. *Kol ha-nashim b'chezkat taharah le-ba'aleihen* — all women are presumed *tehorah* for their husbands. This establishes a *chazakah* that when there is no specific knowledge otherwise, a woman is presumed not to be *niddah* for the purpose of marital relations.

### No Checking Needed

This *mishnah* connects directly to a theme discussed in earlier dapim: the question of whether women must perform *bedikot* before and after relations when they are not engaged in the broader world of *taharot*. The conclusion reached was that when not living in a context of *tumah v'taharah* observance generally, women should not — and need not — check before and after relations, as such checking would only generate anxiety without any halachic purpose. This *mishnah* provides one of the key textual bases for that conclusion.

One possible reading of *le-ba'aleihen* — "for their husbands" — is significant: women are *b'chezkat taharah* not in the general *taharot* sense, but specifically for the purpose of marital relations. A woman who is *asuka be-taharot* — actively engaged in *taharot* observance — checks regularly and has different obligations. The *mishnah* may thus be establishing that even a woman engaged in *taharot* observance retains a *chezkat taharah* specifically for the purpose of *hetter le-ba'alah* — permissibility for her husband — distinct from any separate *taharot* obligations she may have. Put differently: the checking requirement and the question of whether relations are permitted are independent. A woman may have obligations to check as part of *taharot* observance, but failure to check before or after relations does not itself render her forbidden to her husband.

---

## Returning Husband

### Journey Return

The *mishnah* adds: *ha-ba min ha-derech* — one who returns from a journey — *ishto meshulemet lo b'chezkat taharah* — his wife is available to him with a presumption of *taharah*. This is a significant *chiddush*. If a husband has been away for an extended period — a month or more — one might expect that he would need to ask whether she had her period during his absence, or that she would need to check before they resumed relations. The *mishnah* rules otherwise: the *chezkat taharah* established before he left continues to apply upon his return without any requirement to verify.

---

## No Taharot Status (II)

### Check vs. Hetter

Returning to the *mishnah* of *kol ha-nashim*, a second interpretive layer emerges. The phrase *le-ba'aleihen* — specifically "for their husbands" — may be read as a deliberate contrast: in the world of *taharot*, a woman is not necessarily in a state of *chezkat taharah* because she is subject to checking obligations and careful scrutiny. But *le-ba'aleihen* — for the specific question of marital permissibility — the *chezkat taharah* remains intact. This may be the *mishnah*'s way of saying that the *taharot* checking requirement and the *hetter* for marital relations operate on separate tracks.

---

## Returning Husband (II)

### Wife Would Inform

The Gemara asks why the case of the returning husband needs to be stated separately: *lama li lemisni ha-ba min ha-derech*? The Gemara answers: *salka da'atach amina* — one might have thought that when the husband is in the city, the reason she is presumed *tehorah* is that she knows he is coming home and checks in anticipation — *ramya anafshah u-vadkah* — and if she found herself to be *niddah*, she would have informed him. On this reading, the *chezkat taharah* is not an independent presumption but rather rests on the inference that she would have acted and spoken. When the husband was away for a long trip — *leisa b'masa* — she was not expecting him at any particular moment, had no reason to check, and therefore might not have reported her status. On this reading, the *chezkat taharah* would not apply to the returning husband.

The *mishnah* therefore teaches that this is not the correct reading. The case of the returning husband establishes that *ka mashma lan* — the reason she is presumed *tehorah* is not that she would have checked and reported. The *chezkat taharah* is an independent presumption, grounded in the fact that there is simply no need to check.

### No Checking Reason

Resh Lakish citing Rabbi Yehuda Nesia qualifies this: *vehu she-ba u-matz'ah be-toch yemei onasah* — the *chazakah* applies when he returns within the period of her cycle, before her next expected period has arrived. This is the operative condition: if he returns before the time of her expected *veset*, she remains *b'chezkat taharah*. If he returns after the *veset* time has passed, she would be *assurah*.

---

## Veset Status

### Rav Huna View

Rav Huna refines this further: *lo shanu ela she-ein lah veset* — the *chazakah* of *taharah* upon the husband's return applies specifically when she does not have a fixed *veset*. *Aval yesh lah veset, assurah* — if she has a regular cycle, then upon returning the husband may not presume she is *tehorah*. The Gemara immediately objects: *ipcha mist'abra* — the opposite reasoning seems more intuitive. A woman without a fixed *veset* might have seen her period at any point; a woman with a fixed cycle has a predictable schedule, and if the husband returns before that scheduled day, surely she remains *tehorah*. The Gemara corrects the formulation: Rav Huna's statement must be read as follows — *lo shanu ela she-lo higi'a sha'at vestah* — the *chazakah* applies only when the expected time of her *veset* has not yet arrived. *Aval higi'a sha'at vestah, assurah* — if the husband returns after her *veset* time has passed, she is *assurah*.

Tosafos notes that this does not contradict the previous point: for a woman without a fixed *veset*, there is no specific day that serves as a trigger — and therefore she is never necessarily *assurah* upon her husband's return in the same way. For a woman with a regular cycle, the passing of her expected day is significant. The corollary is that a pseudo-threshold of thirty days functions even for a woman without a fixed *veset*, since most women will have seen by that point.

### Veset Dispute

The Gemara now introduces the pivotal question: *kasavar vesasot d'Oraita* — Rav Huna apparently holds that the observance of *veset* is of biblical force. This is the reason why, once her expected day has passed, she is definitely *assurah*: if *veset* is biblical, the expectation of the period is weighty enough to change her prior *chezkat taharah* and presume she has now become *niddah*. This generates a substantial debate.

The rationale for treating *veset* as biblical is its character as a *chazakah*: once a woman establishes a regular pattern, the expectation that she will see again on that day carries the weight of a presumption. The counterargument is that women's periods do not always remain fixed — *chazakah d'asuya l'ishtanos* — and it may therefore not be sufficiently reliable to rise to the level of a biblical presumption.

A deeper analytical distinction must be drawn here. There are two separate questions that the concept of *veset d'Oraita* or *veset de-rabbanan* implicates. The first is whether the couple is forbidden to have relations during the time when her period is expected, out of concern that she may become *niddah* at any moment. This prohibition — refraining during the time of *veset* — may be arguable as biblical according to most opinions. The second, more contested question is whether the mere arrival and passing of the expected day, without any actual checking, is sufficient to overturn her prior *chezkat taharah* and render her presumptively *niddah*. If *veset* is *d'Oraita* in this strong sense, the passing of the day definitively changes her status. If *veset* is only *de-rabbanan*, the prior *chezkat taharah* is not overridden, and she remains presumptively *tehorah* unless she actually found herself to be *niddah*.

### Rabba Opinion

Rabba bar bar Chana takes the lenient position: *afilu higi'a sha'at vestah, nami muteres* — even if the husband returns after her expected period has passed, she remains permitted. *Kasavar vesasot de-rabbanan* — he holds that *veset* is of rabbinic force only, and therefore does not override the prior *chezkat taharah*. On this view, in the most extreme formulation, even if a husband returns long after the expected period, the *chezkat taharah* established before his departure remains in place, since we cannot say with certainty that she saw.

### Gemara Challenge

The Gemara challenges this position: *vechi l'olam lo tireh* — does one forever maintain that she never had her period? There must be a point at which the accumulated time demands a reassessment. The Gemara implies this challenge but notes that in the historical context, women spent much of their reproductive years pregnant, which substantially reduced the frequency of menstrual cycles and gave the presumption of *taharah* greater statistical weight. Nonetheless, the logical difficulty of maintaining the *chazakah* indefinitely remains.

## Husband Away

### Veset Calculation

Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules: *ishah she-yesh lah veset, ba'alah mechashiv yemei vestah u-va'alah* — if a woman has a fixed cycle and her husband is away, the husband can calculate her *veset* days. If sufficient time has elapsed since her expected period — long enough for the period to have concluded (at least seven days), and for her to have immersed in the *mikvah* — he may assume she is *tehorah* upon his return and resume relations.

A tension is noted in passing: the formulation of the husband calculating and acting upon those calculations, without explicit inquiry to his wife, raises questions about agency and communication in these laws. The assumption embedded in *u-va'alah* — "and [has relations with] her" — upon returning is worth marking, even if it is not directly resolved by the Gemara.

### Mikvah Assumption

The more pressing halachic question is whether the husband can assume not only that she had her period, but that she subsequently immersed in the *mikvah*. The *chezkat taharah* applies when there is no definite *tumah*. But if we know — even rabbinically — that she was *niddah* during the period, can we then assume she went to the *mikvah* without certainty?

### Mikvah Challenge

Rav Shmuel bar Yeva raises the question to Rav Abba: would Rabbi Yochanan apply this even in the case of a young girl — *yaldah d'beziza l'mitvhal*? Rashi understands *yaldah* as referring to a young girl who would be embarrassed to go to the *mikvah* independently, and might only do so if her husband was present and prompting her. If he is away, perhaps one cannot assume she went. Tosafos reads the word differently — not *yaldah* (child) but understanding *beziza* as indicating not embarrassment but fear: a woman who recently gave birth and is afraid to immerse in cold water for health reasons. Would Rabbi Yochanan maintain the assumption of immersion even in such a case?

### Safeik Resolution

The response to this challenge is critical: *atu vadai ra'atah mi amar Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan never claimed that we can assume she immersed when we know definitively that she was *niddah*. *Eima d'amar Rabbi Yochanan: safeik ra'atah, safeik lo ra'atah* — Rabbi Yochanan's ruling applies only in a case where it is itself uncertain whether she had her period at all. This is the crux: Rabbi Yochanan holds *vesasot de-rabbanan*, and therefore even the passing of the expected day does not create a *vadai* that she saw. There is still a *safeik* whether she menstruated. Given that uncertainty, the husband can add a further *safeik* — perhaps she even went to the *mikvah*. Two layers of uncertainty combine: maybe she saw, and even if she did, maybe she immersed. Together, these permit the husband to maintain the *chezkat taharah*.

This resolution is available only because *veset* is treated as *de-rabbanan*. If *veset* were *d'Oraita* and the passing of the expected day created a definite *niddah* status, the husband could not subsequently assume she immersed — for this would be a case of *safeik motzi midei vadai*.

## Safeik Motzi Midei

### Principle Stated

This leads directly to the Gemara's discussion of the principle *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* — a doubt cannot overturn a definite status quo. The scenario under discussion is one where an object or person has a definite problematic status, but there exists the capacity — and indeed the normal expectation — that the problem would have been remedied. Can an almost-certain assumption that the remedy was performed override the definite prior status?

### Chavér Example

The Gemara brings a *beraisa*: *chaveir she-meis v'hinniah megurah male'ah peiros* — a *chaveir*, someone scrupulously observant regarding *terumot u-ma'asrot*, dies and leaves a storage area full of grain. The grain was definitely *tevel* — not yet tithed. Did he tithe it before he died? *Afilu hein b'nei yoman* — even if the grain was harvested that very day, *harei hein b'chezkat metukannim* — it is presumed to have been properly tithed. Here we have a definite prior *tumah* (or more precisely, a definite *tevel* status), and a *safeik* whether the remedy was performed, yet the Gemara seems to allow the *safeik* to override the definite prior status.

### Chavér Reliability

The Gemara resolves this apparent contradiction: *hatam vadai vadai hu* — there, it is not truly a *safeik* at all; it is a *vadai*. *De-amar Rabbi Chanina Chuzah: chazakah al chaveir she-einah motzi mi-tachat yado davar she-eino metukan* — it is a presumption, almost an axiom, that a *chaveir* will not allow untithed produce to leave his possession. This *chazakah* is sufficiently reliable that we elevate the "almost certain" to the level of *vadai*. When human behavioral reliability is so high and so established, the presumption is not a mere *safeik* overriding a *vadai*; it is a *vadai* — a different kind of certainty — overriding the prior status.

### Scheming Option

A final resolution is offered: *v'iba'it eima, safeik b'safeik hu* — alternatively, the case of the *chaveir*'s grain is not even a case of definite *tevel* to begin with. *Ke-d'Rabbi Oshia: ma'arim adam al tvu'ato u-machnisah b'motzlah* — a person may bring grain into storage with its chaff still attached, feeding it to his animals and thereby exempting it from *ma'aser* at the biblical level, since the biblical obligation of *ma'aser* attaches only once the grain is fully processed in the field. If the finishing process occurs inside the house, it is exempt from *d'Oraita* *ma'aser*. We do not know that the *chaveir* did not use this legitimate stratagem. Therefore, the prior status is itself only a *safeik* — perhaps the grain was never definitively *tevel* at the biblical level. When both the prior status and the remedy are each uncertain, combining those uncertainties resolves in favor of permissibility.

The Gemara thus arrives at the following framework as the sugya closes: the principle *ein safeik motzi midei vadai* applies when the original problematic status was a genuine *vadai* and the purported remedy is only a *safeik*. Where the original status can be elevated from *safeik* to *vadai* through the reliable conduct of a *chaveir*, or where the original status itself was never a full *vadai*, the principle does not block a lenient ruling. The unresolved question — to be continued — is what the halacha is when the original status is a true *vadai* and the remedy is a highly probable but not certain *safeik*.
