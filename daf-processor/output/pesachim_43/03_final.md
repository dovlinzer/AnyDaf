# Pesachim 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Chametz Categories

### Intro: Chametz Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כּוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי, שֵׁכָר הַמָּדִי, חוֹמֶץ הָאֲדוֹמִי, וְזֵיתוֹם הַמִּצְרִי, וְקוֹלָן שֶׁל סוֹפְרִים, וַעֲמִילָן שֶׁל טַבָּחִים, וְזוֹמָן שֶׁל צַבָּעִים. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר אַף תַּכְשִׁיטֵי נָשִׁים. זֶה הַכְּלָל כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מִמִּין דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA These are the prohibited items: **Babylonian *kutaḥ*, Median beer, Edomite vinegar, Egyptian *zitom*, bookmakers' glue, bakers' well-worked dough, and dyers' broth.** Rabbi Eliezer **says that women's adornments are also** prohibited. **This is the principle:** One violates these prohibitions on Passover with **anything that is** prepared **from a type of grain.**

The *daf* opens by situating us within a broader classification of non-standard *chametz* — forms that are prohibited on Pesach yet fall short of the full prohibition that incurs *karet*. The *Mishnah* presents two conceptually distinct categories. The first is *chametz betaarovet*: items that are genuine foods, yet contain *chametz* as one ingredient within a larger mixture. The paradigmatic case is *kutach*, a sour-milk-based dip into which moldy bread has been dissolved. Similarly, barley placed into wine to produce vinegar, or into mead to produce beer, generates a fully fermented grain product — but one subsumed within a larger liquid medium. The *chametz* is real; what renders it a lesser category is its role as a constituent within a mixture rather than as a food unto itself.

The second category, which the *Gemara* will shortly label *chametz nuksha*, comprises items that may indeed have a high grain content, yet are not truly edible in the ordinary sense and were not produced for human consumption. The *Mishnah* lists items such as glues and plasters — preparations applied to the skin for cosmetic or depilatory purposes — which may be flour-based yet are not foods in any meaningful sense. Both categories share the same halachic status: there is a negative prohibition (*lav*) against eating them on Pesach, but no *karet*.

It is worth pausing on this point, which the *Gemara* itself will probe at length. That *chametz betaarovet* — real, fully fermented grain dissolved into a mixture — carries only a *lav* and not *karet* is, on its face, striking. The *Rishonim* debate whether this middle category also violates the biblical prohibition of *bal yera'eh bal yimatze* (owning *chametz* over Pesach), though that question lies at the margin of today's discussion.

### Women's Cosmetics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לְפִרְקָן, וְלֹא הִגִּיעוּ לְשָׁנִים, בְּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים — טוֹפְלוֹת אוֹתָן בְּסִיד, בְּנוֹת עֲשִׁירִים — טוֹפְלוֹת אוֹתָן בְּסוֹלֶת, בְּנוֹת מְלָכִים — בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** **who reached** physical **maturity, but had not** yet **reached the age** of majority, and women who sought to remove hair for cosmetic purposes: **They would smear daughters of the poor with lime; they would smear daughters of the wealthy with fine flour; they would smear daughters of kings with *shemen hamor*, as it was stated:** "For so were the days of their anointing filled, **six months with *shemen hamor*"** (Esther 2:12).

The *Mishnah* concludes with a position attributed to *Rabbi Eliezer*: *af takhshitei nashim* — even women's cosmetics. The *Gemara* immediately flags the literal translation, "women's jewelry," as incoherent in this context, and clarifies that the reference is to *tipulei nashim*, women's cosmetic preparations. The context for this is provided by a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, which describes the depilatory practices used by young Jewish women who had reached physical maturity before the socially expected age, presenting unwanted hair growth as a social concern.

Three tiers of practice are described, corresponding to economic class. Poor women (*bnos aniyim*) would apply *sid*, a plaster, to their skin and then peel it away along with the hair. Wealthy women (*bnos ashirim*) would use *soles*, a fine-flour-based paste — a dough preparation, and notably more expensive. The daughters of kings (*bnos melachim*) used *shemen hamor*, an aromatic oil — the same substance, the *Gemara* notes, used by Esther, as attested by the verse describing her six-month preparation with *shemen hamor* and six months with fragrant spices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״שֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר״? רַב הוּנָא בַּר יִרְמְיָה אוֹמֵר: סְטָכַת. רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: שֶׁמֶן זַיִת שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is *shemen hamor*? Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya said: *Setaket*. Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said:** It is **olive oil** extracted from an olive **that** has **not** yet **reached a third** of its growth; the acidic oil is effective as a depilatory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַנְפִּיקְנִין — שֶׁמֶן זַיִת שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁלִישׁ, וְלָמָּה סָכִין אוֹתוֹ? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשִּׁיר אֶת הַשֵּׂיעָר וּמְעַדֵּן אֶת הַבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that ***anpiknin*** is **olive oil** from an olive **that has not reached a third** of its growth. **And why is it spread** on the body? It is **due to** the fact **that it removes [*mashir*] the hair and pampers the skin.**

The *Gemara* inquires into the precise identity of *shemen hamor*. Rav Yehuda bar Yirmyah calls it *satachet*, a substance Rashi acknowledges is uncertain. Rav Yirmyah bar Abba identifies it as olive oil pressed from olives that have not yet reached a third of their ripening (*shemen zayit shelo hevi'a shalish*). A *baraita* confirms that Rabbi Yehuda identifies this with *anpikanon*, which in the tractate Menachot is similarly described as *shemen zayit shelo hevi'a shalish*. Its use was twofold: it causes hair to fall away (*mashir et hase'ar*) and softens the skin (*me'aden et habasar*). The relevance to Pesach is that some of these cosmetic preparations — specifically the dough-based paste used by wealthier women — are flour-based and thus raise the question of *chametz*. Why *Rabbi Eliezer* mentions them separately, and what he is adding beyond what the *Chachamim* already prohibit, is a question left for the subsequent analysis.

### Grain Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מִמִּין דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** This is the principle: One violates these prohibitions on Passover with **anything that is** prepared **from a type of grain.**

Having enumerated specific cases, the *Mishnah* offers a generalization: *ze haklal kol shehu mi-min dagan* — the principle is that anything containing a grain component is problematic on Pesach. This general principle sweeps in everything: the mixtures, the cosmetics, the fermented drinks. Any grain content triggers the relevant prohibition.

### Specifics vs. Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מִין דָּגָן הֲרֵי זֶה עוֹבֵר בְּפֶסַח, לָמָּה מָנוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת אֵלּוּ? כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא רָגִיל בָּהֶן וּבִשְׁמוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua said: Now that we learned** that by possessing **anything that is a type of grain one violates** the prohibition against leaven **during Passover, why did the Sages list these** items individually? They could have simply stated the principle. The *baraita* explains that the Sages provided a list of prohibited foods **so that** one **would become familiar with these** items **and with their names,** so that it would become widely known that these foods contain a small quantity of grain.

With the general principle in hand, a question is posed in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua — himself a *Tanna*, reflecting upon a received teaching: since we have already taught that anything containing grain is forbidden on Pesach (*kol shehu mi-min dagan harei ze over ba-Pesach*), why did the *Chachamim* bother enumerating all of these specific cases (*lamma manu chakhamim et elu*)? The general rule suffices; why list particulars?

The answer given is *kedei sheyehe ragil bahen u-vi-shmoteihen* — in order that one become familiar with them and with their names. Both the general rule and the specific examples serve indispensable functions. The rule supplies the underlying principle that allows one to extend the law to unfamiliar cases; the specifics supply the concrete knowledge needed to navigate particular situations. One without the other is incomplete.

### Kutach Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּהָהוּא בַּר מַעְרְבָא אִיקְּלַע לְבָבֶל, הֲוָה בִּישְׂרָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: קָרִיבוּ לִי מַתְכִּילְתָּא. שְׁמַע דְּקָאָמְרִי: קָרִיבוּ לֵיהּ כּוּתָּח. כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַע כּוּתָּח — פֵּירַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an incident that underscores the significance of familiarity with the names of foods: **As in this** case **of that** man **from the West,** Eretz Yisrael, who **visited Babylonia,** and **had meat with him, he said to** his hosts: **Bring me a dip** with which to eat my bread. **He heard them saying: Bring him *kutaḥ*. Since he heard** the word ***kutaḥ*, he stopped** eating, as he knew that *kutaḥ* contains milk and may not be eaten with meat. This incident underscores that it is advantageous for one to familiarize himself with the names and ingredients of different foods, so that he will be aware of the nature of the food even if he does not recognize it.

To illustrate the practical importance of knowing specific food names and compositions, the *Gemara* provides a narrative vignette. A man from *Eretz Yisrael* arrived in Bavel and, having meat with him, asked his hosts to bring him some dip (*matchilta*). The hosts, not realizing he had meat, began calling out to bring him *kutach*. The moment he heard the word *kutach*, he immediately declined. He knew that *kutach* was a sour-milk-based dip, and having meat with him, he recognized the *kashrut* problem instantly. The lesson is precisely that familiarity with foods by name — knowing what is in them and what they are called — is what allows one to navigate the real world of *kashrut* and Pesach observance. The general principle does not help unless one also knows that *kutach* contains milk.

## Tannaim Attribution

### Tosafot on Specifics

*Tosafot* presses the question of why the *Mishnah* needs all of these specific examples even more sharply. The response that the specifics help one become familiar with food names is not fully satisfying to *Tosafot*, since we have already established — before even reaching the list — that there are two conceptually distinct categories: *taaruvet* (mixture) and *chametz nuksha* (non-standard *chametz* that is not fully fermented or not fully edible). Given that the categories exist and that the general rule covers everything within them, why enumerate so many examples? The answer, *Tosafot* concedes, is that specifics are genuinely helpful even alongside principles — they fill in the contours of the categories in ways that abstractions cannot.

## Middle Chametz Category

### Conceptual Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא דְּחָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבוֹת, וְנוּקְשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיהּ בְּלָאו?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna* who** maintains that both **full-fledged, leavened grain bread in a mixture, and hardened** leaven, **in its pure,** unadulterated form, which is not suitable for consumption, are both included **in a prohibition?**

The *Gemara* now advances to the core analytical question: who is the *tanna* who holds that *chametz dagan gamur al yedei taarovet* — fully fermented *chametz* contained within a mixture — and *nuksha be-einei* — non-standard *chametz* standing alone, not within a mixture — carry a *lav* rather than *karet*? In six words the *Gemara* crystallizes the conceptual distinction between the two categories, providing the framework for everything that follows.

The first category — *chametz dagan gamur al yedei taaruvet* — encompasses genuinely fermented grain that is real *chametz*, but embedded within a larger mixture. The *kutach* with dissolved moldy bread is the clearest case, as is barley fermented within wine or mead. The *chametz* is authentic; what distinguishes it from paradigmatic *chametz* is its mixture-context. The second category — *nuksha be-einei* — stands by itself, not in any mixture, but is "lesser" *chametz* in some other respect: either it has not fully fermented, or it is not truly edible, or both.

### Nuksha Definition

The term *nuksha* is technical and warrants careful unpacking. It appears to embrace two distinct sub-categories, and Rashi's definition helps orient us. Rashi, in his comment approximately fifteen lines into the narrow column, defines *nuksha* as *eino ra'uy le-achila* — not truly fit for eating — and gives as examples the women's cosmetic preparations that appear at the end of the *Mishnah*: the flour-based paste applied as a depilatory is *nuksha* because, while it has grain content, it was not made for food and is not genuinely edible.

Yet as the *Gemara* proceeds, *nuksha* is also applied to dough that has begun fermenting but has not yet completed the fermentation process — a partially risen, partially cracked dough that stands at an intermediate stage between raw dough and fully leavened bread. This too is called *nuksha*. So the category encompasses two distinct phenomena: (a) something not fully edible (the cosmetics and glues), and (b) something not fully fermented (the partially risen dough). It need not be both simultaneously; either condition suffices to place an item in the *nuksha* category. What they share is that neither is fully paradigmatic *chametz* — and on that basis they are assigned the lesser prohibition of a *lav* without *karet*.

One might ask: if something is *eino ra'uy le-achila* entirely, should there be any prohibition at all? The discussion here seems to presuppose that *nuksha* occupies the middle ground of *ra'uy le-achilat kelev* — edible for dogs if not for humans — placing it in the intermediate band that triggers the *lav*.

### Rabbi Meir: Se'or

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: שִׂיאוּר — יִשָּׂרֵף, וְנוֹתְנוֹ לְכַלְבּוֹ, וְהָאוֹכְלוֹ — בְּאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Leavening** dough, i.e., dough that is at the beginning of the leavening process and is presently hardened leaven, must **be burned, or** one **gives it to his dog. And one who eats it** is flogged **with forty** lashes.

The *Gemara* identifies the *tanna* behind the *nuksha* category: Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav attributes the *Mishnah*'s middle category to Rabbi Meir. The evidence comes from a *baraita*. The *baraita* speaks of *se'or* — a technical term for dough in a partially fermented state, after it has begun to expand and crack but before it has completed fermentation. (This should be distinguished from *se'or* meaning sourdough starter, which functions as a leavening agent. Here *se'or* refers to the dough itself at the intermediate stage of fermentation.) What does one do with *se'or*? The *baraita* says: *se'or yisaref* — burn it — or alternatively, *veyitno le-chalbo* — give it to one's dog.

### Burning vs. Dogs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ ״שִׂיאוּר יִשָּׂרֵף״, אַלְמָא אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: וְנוֹתְנוֹ לִפְנֵי כַּלְבּוֹ, אַלְמָא מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה!
>
> **Translation:** Before analyzing the contents of the *baraita*, the Gemara addresses an apparent contradiction within the *baraita*. **This** *baraita* **itself is difficult. You said** that **leavening** dough must **be burned; apparently it is prohibited to** derive **benefit** from hardened leaven. **And then it teaches: Or** one **gives it to his dog; apparently, it is permitted to** derive **benefit** from the leaven.

The *Gemara* immediately identifies an internal contradiction in the *baraita*. Burning is the prescribed disposal method when one may not derive *hana'ah* (benefit) from an item at all. Giving it to one's dog, by contrast, implies that *hana'ah* is permitted — the owner derives benefit by feeding his animal. These two rulings seem flatly inconsistent.

### Meir vs. Yehuda Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: שִׂיאוּר יִשָּׂרֵף — דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְנוֹתְנוֹ לִפְנֵי כַּלְבּוֹ — דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this is** what the *baraita* **is saying: Leavening** dough must **be burned,** i.e., leavening dough as defined **by Rabbi Meir** must be burned **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who maintains that leavening dough is full-fledged leaven. Alternatively, the *baraita* may be explained as referring to leavening dough as defined **by Rabbi Yehuda, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** that it is prohibited to derive benefit from leavening dough. Each *tanna* maintains that it is prohibited to derive benefit from any dough classified as leavening dough by his definition. When the *baraita* says **that one gives it to his dog,** it means: Leavening dough as defined **by Rabbi Meir,** is only hardened leaven **according to Rabbi Yehuda,** and therefore one may feed it to his dog. Rabbi Yehuda maintains that it is permitted to derive benefit from this type of leaven.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאוֹכְלוֹ בְּאַרְבָּעִים — אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the final statement of the *baraita*, that **one who eats** leavening dough is flogged **with forty** lashes, **we have** once again **arrived at** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** Rabbi Meir holds that one who eats this leavening dough, from which it is permitted to derive benefit according to Rabbi Yehuda, is flogged with forty lashes.

The resolution the *Gemara* proposes is elegant. There is a well-known dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda about precisely which stage of fermentation constitutes the threshold at which dough becomes *se'or* and must be treated accordingly. Conceptually: stages one and four are uncontested — stage one is fully unfermented raw dough, stage four is complete *chametz*. The dispute concerns stages two and three, the intermediate phases.

Rabbi Meir holds that the critical threshold is reached at the earlier stage (stage two). Rabbi Yehuda holds it is only at the later stage (stage three). The *baraita*'s seemingly contradictory rulings are actually two distinct positions woven together. *Se'or yisaref* — burn it — reflects what one must do when one's own threshold has been crossed: for Rabbi Meir it is stage two, for Rabbi Yehuda it is stage three. *Veyitno le-chalbo* — give it to the dog — reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view of stage two: at that point Rabbi Yehuda says the dough is not yet *se'or* in the prohibition-triggering sense, so *hana'ah* is permitted, though it cannot be eaten, and Rabbi Yehuda would concede at least that much stringency toward Rabbi Meir's position. The eating-with-forty-lashes clause (*ha-ochlo be-arba'im*) brings us back to Rabbi Meir's stage two, where eating it generates lashes.

### Meir: Nuksha Lav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר נוּקְשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיהּ — בְּלָאו. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** According to this explanation of the *baraita*, **we** have **learned** that **Rabbi Meir** maintains that **hardened** leaven **in its pure,** unadulterated form, is included **in a prohibition,** and one who eats this leaven is flogged. **And all the more so,** one who eats **full-fledged, leavened grain bread in a mixture** is flogged but does not receive *karet*, as he is not eating the leaven in and of itself. Nevertheless, the prohibition against eating leavened bread on Passover applies in that case.

From this analysis the *Gemara* draws the central inference: *shaminan lei le-Rabbi Meir de-nuksha be-inei be-lav* — we hear from Rabbi Meir that *nuksha* by itself, not in any mixture, is a negative prohibition without *karet*. The partially fermented *se'or* is precisely *nuksha*: it has not completed fermentation, it may not be fully palatable, yet it carries enough of a *chametz* character to be forbidden. Rabbi Meir's position on *se'or* demonstrates that he recognizes this middle category.

From there the *Gemara* reasons by inference: if *nuksha* — which is by itself, but is not fully fermented, and is therefore a lesser form of *chametz* — carries a *lav*, then certainly *chametz dagan gamur al yedei taaruvet* — real, fully fermented *chametz* merely embedded in a mixture — would carry at minimum a *lav* as well. The mixture represents, on this reading, a more obvious candidate for prohibition than *nuksha*, since the *chametz* itself is unambiguously real.

## Tannaim Attribution (II)

### Rav Yehuda: Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בְּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna: **These** substances **are included in the prohibition** but are not punishable by *karet*.

The first attribution, then, is that of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: the *Mishnah* reflects Rabbi Meir's view. Rabbi Meir's ruling about *se'or* demonstrates the existence of a *lav*-without-*karet* category for *nuksha*, and the *Mishnah*'s enumeration of both *chametz betaarovet* and *chametz nuksha* can be understood through his framework — with *nuksha* being the more obviously lesser case that anchors the inference to *taaruvet*.

### Rav Nachman: Rabbi Eliez…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: עַל חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר — עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, עַל עֵירוּבוֹ — בְּלָאו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַל חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר — עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, עַל עֵירוּבוֹ — בְּלֹא כְּלוּם. וְשָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּאָמַר חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת — בְּלָאו, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן נוּקְשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** The mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **For** eating **full-fledged, leavened grain bread** one is **punishable** by ***karet*,** whereas **for** eating **a mixture** that contains leaven one is punished merely **for** violating **a prohibition.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. And the Rabbis say: For** eating **full-fledged, leavened grain bread** one is **punishable by *karet*;** however, **for** eating leaven in **its mixture** one is **not** punished **at all. And we learned according to Rabbi Eliezer, who said** that **full-fledged, leavened grain bread in a mixture is** included **in a prohibition, and** that is true **all the more so** with regard to **hardened leaven in its pure,** unadulterated form.

Rav Nachman offers an alternative attribution: the *Mishnah* is Rabbi Eliezer. The evidence is a *baraita* that states explicitly: *chametz dagan gamur onasho karet* — fully fermented *chametz* carries *karet* — *al eruvo belav* — for *chametz* in a mixture, there is a *lav*. This is a stark, direct statement from Rabbi Eliezer that *chametz betaarovet* falls into the middle category of a negative prohibition without *karet*. According to this attribution, the inference runs in the opposite direction: from the mixture (where real *chametz* exists but is embedded in other things) we infer that *nuksha* by itself — which though unencumbered by a mixture is nevertheless lesser *chametz* — is at least as obviously forbidden.

The *Chachamim* in this *baraita* disagree with Rabbi Eliezer and state: *chametz dagan gamur onasho karet, al eruvo belo klum* — for *chametz* in a mixture, one transgresses nothing at all. This, as noted, is a striking position that will require explanation.

### Attribution Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב יְהוּדָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rav Naḥman did not state** his opinion **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Yehuda,** that the mishna is in accordance with Rabbi Meir's ruling?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: דִּילְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר הָתָם אֶלָּא נוּקְשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיהּ, אֲבָל חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rav Naḥman could have **said to you** that the following distinction applies: **Perhaps Rabbi Meir stated** his opinion **only there,** with regard to **hardened** leaven **in its pure,** unadulterated form; **however,** with regard to **full-fledged, leavened grain bread in a mixture, no,** one is punished not with mere lashes but with *karet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב נַחְמָן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rav Yehuda did not state** his opinion **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Naḥman,** that the mishna is in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer's ruling?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם אֶלָּא חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת. אֲבָל נוּקְשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיהּ — לָא אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rav Yehuda could have **said to you: Rabbi Eliezer stated** his opinion **only there,** with regard to **full-fledged, leavened grain bread in a mixture** that it is included in the prohibition. **However,** with regard to **hardened** leaven **in its pure,** unadulterated form, **he did not state** his ruling and perhaps Rabbi Eliezer maintains that it is permitted to eat hardened leaven.

The *Gemara* now lays out why Rav Nachman could not accept Rav Yehuda's attribution and vice versa, and the crux is which direction the *kal va-chomer* (inference from the lesser to the greater) runs more naturally.

Rav Nachman's objection to Rav Yehuda: when did Rabbi Meir say there is a *lav*? Only for *nuksha be-inei* — *nuksha* standing alone by itself. But *chametz dagan gamur al yedei taaruvet* — real *chametz* mixed in other things — Rabbi Meir may well not have extended the prohibition there. The *chametz* is real, yet its dilution within a mixture makes it a less obvious case than *nuksha*, and we cannot assume Rabbi Meir would follow the inference.

Rav Yehuda's objection to Rav Nachman: when did Rabbi Eliezer say there is a *lav*? Only for *chametz dagan gamur al yedei taaruvet* — real *chametz* in a mixture. But *nuksha be-einei* — something that is by itself, yet is not fully fermented or not fully edible — Rabbi Eliezer may not have included, precisely because its deficiency is intrinsic rather than relational. The inference cannot safely be extended.

The resolution implicit in the *Gemara* is that each view holds that one *tanna* — either Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Eliezer — does in fact hold of both categories, but the direction of the *kal va-chomer* determines which attribution is more defensible. Neither attribution can fully resolve the problem, which is why the *Gemara* entertains both.

## Summary & Explanatio (II)

### Small Proportions

Before turning to the biblical derivation, the *Gemara*'s discussion warrants a pause to address the conceptual puzzle underlying the entire analysis. Why should *chametz betaarovet* — real, fully fermented grain within a mixture — carry only a *lav* rather than *karet*, or in the *Chachamim*'s view no biblical prohibition at all? If one eats *kutach* that contains dissolved moldy bread and the *chametz* is not *batel*, surely this is the same as eating *chametz*?

One important explanation advanced by a number of *Rishonim* is grounded in the concept of *kazayit bi-khdei achilat pras*. The standard for incurring lashes requires eating a *kazayit* of the prohibited substance within the time it takes to eat half a loaf (*pras*). The ratio is roughly one to eight or one to nine: if the *chametz* constitutes less than one part in eight of the mixture, and one eats the mixture at a normal pace, one will never have ingested a full *kazayit* of *chametz* within the requisite timeframe. No matter how much *kutach* one consumes, the proportion of *chametz* within it is so small that the quantity of *chametz* actually ingested never reaches the threshold that triggers lashes under the standard prohibition.

This is the first rationale for needing a *new* prohibition — *chametz betaarovet* — distinct from the ordinary prohibition of eating *chametz*. Under ordinary rules, the *chametz* in the mixture would be prohibited through *ta'am ke-ikar* (the principle that the flavor of a prohibited item carries its prohibition), but one would never meet the quantitative threshold for lashes. By creating a separate category called *chametz betaarovet*, the Torah (according to Rabbi Eliezer) adds a *lav* whose violation is complete by eating the mixture qua mixture, regardless of precise proportions. The *Chachamim* disagree and say no such separate prohibition exists, leaving only the *chatzi shiur* issue.

### Bread Identity Lost

The second explanation is more conceptually radical and turns on the unique identity of *chametz* as *lechem* — bread. Unlike other prohibited foods, *chametz* is not merely a substance defined by its chemical composition; it is defined substantially by its *form* as bread. The terms *lechem*, *chametz*, and *matzah* overlap in a way that reveals this: *chametz* is *lechem* in a fermented state, *matzah* is *lechem* in an unfermented state. The identity of *chametz* as a forbidden item is inseparable from its identity as bread.

When *chametz* is dissolved into a *taaruvet* — when the bread is broken down and its form lost — it may cease to be *lechem* in any meaningful sense. What remains in the mixture is fermented grain in solution, the constituent *materials* of bread, but not bread itself. This is categorically different from, say, blood (*dam*) or forbidden fat (*chelev*) in a mixture: those substances retain their identity regardless of form, because their prohibition is material rather than formal. One can meaningfully say "there is blood in this soup" even when the blood is fully dissolved. Can one meaningfully say "there is bread in this soup" when the bread has dissolved beyond recognition? According to this view, the answer is no — the identity of *chametz* as *lechem* dissolves along with its physical form.

On this reading, even if the *chametz* in a mixture constitutes *kazayit bi-khdei achilat pras* by proportion, one still would not incur the ordinary punishment of *karet* for eating *chametz*, because what one is eating is no longer *chametz* in the paradigmatic sense. The need for a separate biblical category of *chametz betaarovet* (according to Rabbi Eliezer) is thus not merely quantitative but ontological: it is a distinct prohibition for a categorically distinct phenomenon.

## Biblical Sources

### Kol Machmetzet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: ״כׇּל מַחְמֶצֶת לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״ — לְרַבּוֹת כּוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי וְשֵׁכָר הַמָּדִי וְחוֹמֶץ הָאֲדוֹמִי וְזֵיתוֹם הַמִּצְרִי. יָכוֹל יְהֵא עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי כׇּל אֹכֵל חָמֵץ וְנִכְרְתָה״, עַל חָמֵץ דָּגָן גָּמוּר — עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְעַל עֵירוּבוֹ — בְּלָאו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **it was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** who said that the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Meir's opinion: **"You shall eat nothing leavened;** in all your dwellings you shall eat *matzot*" (Exodus 12:20). The Sages taught: This verse comes **to include Babylonian *kutaḥ*, Median beer, Edomite vinegar, and Egyptian *zitom*.** I **might** have thought that one who eats any of these items **will be punishable** by ***karet*.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For whoever eats leavened bread…**that soul **shall be cut off** from Israel" (Exodus 12:15), from which the Sages derived: **For** eating **full-fledged, leavened grain bread** one is **punished** with ***karet*, but for** eating **its mixture** one is only in violation **of a prohibition.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the biblical derivations. A *baraita* cites the verse *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu* — "you shall not eat any *machmetzes*" — as the source that extends prohibition to *kutach ha-bavli*, *shechar ha-madi*, *chometz ha-adomi*, and *zeitum ha-mitzri*: all the various forms of *chametz betaarovet*. Something in this verse — whether the word *kol* ("any") or the word *machmetzes* itself — is understood to include these mixture-cases within the prohibition.

### Ki Kol Ochel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר עַל עֵירוּבוֹ בְּלָאו? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, וְאִילּוּ נוּקְשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיהּ לָא קָאָמַר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: נוּקְשֶׁה לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לֵית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the above statement: **Whom did you hear that said that for** eating **a mixture** which contains leaven one is in violation **of a prohibition? It is Rabbi Eliezer. However,** the *baraita* **is not stating** the *halakha* of **hardened** leaven **in its pure,** unadulterated form. This *baraita* lists only items that contain leaven in a mixture, but not other substances whose legal status is that of hardened leaven, e.g., broth, worked dough, and glue. **Learn from this that Rabbi Eliezer is not of** the opinion that **hardened** leaven is prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, עֵירוּבוֹ בְּלָאו מְנָא לֵיהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל מַחְמֶצֶת לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Eliezer, from where does he** derive the *halakha* that leaven in **its mixture** is included **in a prohibition?** The Gemara answers that he derives it **as it is written: "You shall eat nothing leavened."**

Having established that *machmetzes* encompasses *betaarovet*, the *Gemara* immediately asks: perhaps eating *machmetzes* in a mixture incurs *karet* as well? The answer is drawn from a second verse: *ki kol ochel chametz ve-nichretah* — "for whoever eats *chametz* shall be cut off." The punishment of *karet* applies specifically to *chametz*, not to *machmetzes*. *Al chametz dagan gamur anush karet* — for full, paradigmatic *chametz*, one receives *karet*. *Al eruvo* — the term *machmetzes*, meaning "that which has been leavened in connection with something," covers the mixture case — *belav* — and for that the punishment is a *lav*, not *karet*.

The *Gemara* then identifies who holds this position: *man shamat lei de-amar al eruvo belav* — who is it that says a mixture generates a *lav* rather than *karet*? The answer is Rabbi Eliezer.

### Machmetzet: Leavened

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כָּרֵת נָמֵי לְחַיֵּיב, דְּהָא כְּתִיב: ״כִּי כׇּל אֹכֵל מַחְמֶצֶת וְנִכְרְתָה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this derivation: **If so,** if the expression: Anything leavened, includes leaven in a mixture, **let one also be liable to** receive ***karet*** for eating leaven in a mixture. **As it is written:** "Seven days no leaven shall be found in your houses; **for whosoever eats anything [*kol*] leavened,** that soul **shall be cut off** from the people of Israel, whether he is a sojourner or one born in the land" (Exodus 12:19). Apparently, one is punished with *karet* for eating anything that contains leaven.

The word *machmetzes* itself now receives closer attention. Structurally, *machmetzes* appears to be in the *hif'il* form — something that has been leavened through external agency rather than through its own natural fermentation. Paradigmatic *chametz* ferments by itself: flour and water, left to their own devices, will ferment naturally (even without added yeast, in the *Gemara*'s understanding). Something leavened through an external agent (*nischametz mi-chamas davar acher* — leavened because of something else) might not be immediately obvious as *chametz*; the verse *machmetzes ve-nichretah* comes to tell us that even such externally-leavened bread incurs *karet*.

On this reading, the verse containing *machmetzes* with *karet* is not about *betaarovet* at all — it is about *chametz* produced with an external leavening agent (yeast, baking powder, or the like). The prohibition-without-*karet* verse must get its content from somewhere else. The *Gemara* locates it in the word *kol*: *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu* — the word *kol* ("any") in the prohibition verse is what includes *betaarovet*, since that word is absent from the *karet* verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, (״מַחְמֶצֶת״) אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְחַמֵּץ מֵאֵלָיו, מֵחֲמַת דָּבָר אַחֵר מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל ... מַחְמֶצֶת וְנִכְרְתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rabbi Eliezer **needs that** phrase: Anything leavened, in order to derive **for that which was taught** in a *baraita*: From the phrase: Anything **leavened, I have** derived **only** that an item **that became leavened on its own** is prohibited. However, **from where** do I derive that one is punished with *karet* for eating an item that became leavened **due to a different factor? The verse states:** "Whosoever eats **anything [*kol*] leavened…shall be cut off,"** indicating that food that became leavened due to a different factor is considered leavened bread.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, דְּלָאו נָמֵי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the phrase: Anything leavened, is referring to food that became leavened by means of something else, then when this same phrase appears with regard **to** the **prohibition,** I should explain that it **comes for that** purpose **as well.** Consequently, there should be no violation for eating a mixture that contains leaven, as apparently, the phrase: Anything leavened, does not refer to that case at all.

But the *Gemara* then objects: if *machmetzes* means externally-leavened bread in the *karet* verse, then the *lav* verse (*kol machmetzes lo tocheilu*) should also mean externally-leavened bread, not *betaarovet*. The *Gemara* accepts this and revises: Rabbi Eliezer's source for including *betaarovet* in the prohibition is not the word *machmetzes* but the word *kol* — specifically, the *kol* of *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu* — which has no counterpart in the *karet* verse.

### Kol Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִ״כׇּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** is that he derives this *halakha* **from** the term **anything [*kol*].** He does not derive his opinion from the term leavened; he bases his ruling on the inclusive term anything.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״כׇּל״? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **There, too,** in the verse that mentions the punishment of *karet*, **isn't it written:** "For **whosoever [*kol*]** eats anything leavened, that soul shall be cut off"? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Eliezer **requires that** term **to include women** who are also punishable by *karet* for eating leavened bread.

The resolution: in the prohibition verse (*kol machmetzes lo tocheilu*), the word *kol* extends the objects of the prohibition — things even loosely related to *chametz*, including mixtures. In the *karet* verse (*ki kol ochel chametz ve-nichretah*), the word *kol* is positioned differently: it modifies *ochel*, the one who eats. *Ki kol ochel* — "for anyone who eats" — is extending not the objects but the eaters.

### Women & Chametz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשִׁים מִדְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב נָפְקָא, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אָמַר קְרָא ״אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה כִּי יַעֲשׂוּ מִכׇּל חַטֹּאת הָאָדָם״, הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִישׁ לְאִשָּׁה לְכׇל עוֹנָשִׁין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this source necessary to derive this *halakha*? The fact that **women** may not eat leavened bread is **derived from** the statement that **Rav Yehuda** said that **Rav said. As Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, and similarly,** the Sage in **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The verse said:** "Speak to the children of Israel: **A man or woman, when they commit any of the sins of men,** to commit a trespass against the Lord, and that soul shall be guilty" (Numbers 5:6). **The Torah rendered a man equal to a woman for all punishments of the Torah.** Since the punishment of *karet* for eating leavened bread on Passover is included in this general principle, there is no need for a separate source to include women.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nonetheless, it is **necessary** to cite a source that men and women are equal specifically with regard to the punishment of *karet* for eating leavened bread,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״לֹא תֹאכַל עָלָיו חָמֵץ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים תֹּאכַל עָלָיו מַצּוֹת״, כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּקוּם אֱכוֹל מַצָּה — יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּבַל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ, וְהָנֵי נְשֵׁי, הוֹאִיל וְלֵיתַנְהוּ בְּקוּם אֱכוֹל מַצָּה, דְּהָוְיָא לַיהּ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא (הִיא) — אֵימָא בְּבַל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ נָמֵי לֵיתַנְהוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** as **it could enter your mind to say** that **since it is written: "You shall eat no leavened bread with it; seven days you shall eat with it *matzot*"** (Deuteronomy 16:3), one might have thought that **anyone included** in the obligation **to eat *matza* is** also **included** in the **prohibition against eating leavened bread. And these women, since they are excluded** from the obligation **to eat *matza*, as it is a time-bound, positive mitzva** from which they are exempt as a rule, I might **say they are also excluded from the prohibition against eating leavened bread.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that women are also prohibited from eating leavened bread.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאִתְרַבּוּ לְהוּ בְּבַל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ — אִיתְרַבִּי נָמֵי לַאֲכִילַת מַצָּה, כְּרַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר). דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת בַּאֲכִילַת מַצָּה דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹאכַל עָלָיו חָמֵץ וְגוֹ׳״, כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּבַל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ — יֶשְׁנוֹ בַּאֲכִילַת מַצָּה. וְהָנֵי נְשֵׁי נָמֵי, הוֹאִיל וְיׇשְׁנָן בְּבַל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ — יֶשְׁנָן בְּקוּם אֱכוֹל מַצָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And now** that women **have been included in the prohibition against eating leavened bread, they** should **also be included** in the obligation **to eat *matza*,** even though it is a time-bound, positive mitzva, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. As Rabbi Eliezer said: Women are obligated to eat *matza* by Torah** law, **as it is stated: "You shall eat no leavened bread with it;** seven days you shall eat with it *matzot*" (Deuteronomy 16:3). These two commandments are juxtaposed to teach that **anyone included** in **the prohibition against eating leavened bread is** also **included in** the obligation **to eat *matza*. And these women too, since they** are **included** in **the prohibition against eating leavened bread, they are** also **included in** the obligation **to eat *matza*.**

The *Gemara* now explains what the expansion of eaters accomplishes: *le-rabbot et ha-nashim* — to include women in the prohibition. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches, and so too the school of Rabbi Yishmael: the verse *ish o isha ki ya'asu mi-kol chatat ha-adam* equates women and men for all punishments in the Torah. In general, women are equally prohibited from negative commandments without any need for a special verse. Why then does the *karet* verse need *ki kol ochel* to include women in the *chametz* prohibition?

The answer is that we might have excluded women from the *chametz* prohibition based on a specific logical chain. The verse *lo tochal alav chametz, shivat yamim tochal alav matzot* — "do not eat *chametz* on it, for seven days eat *matzot*" — links the prohibition of *chametz* to the obligation to eat *matzah*. The principle derived is: whoever is in the category of *bekum echol matzah* (positively commanded to eat *matzah*) is in the category of *bal tochal chametz* (prohibited from eating *chametz*). Since *matzah* is a positive time-bound commandment (*mitzvat asei she-hazman gerama*), women might be exempt from it. And if they are exempt from *matzah*, they might equally be excluded from the *chametz* prohibition. The verse *ki kol ochel chametz* comes to insist that women are indeed prohibited in *chametz*.

The *Gemara* then reverses the chain: now that we know from the verse that women *are* prohibited in *chametz*, we use the same linkage to include them in the obligation of *matzah*. As Rabbi Elazar teaches: *nashim chayavot ba-achilat matzah devar Torah* — women are biblically obligated in eating *matzah*. The verse *lo tochal alav chametz* links the two: whoever is in *bal tochal chametz* is in *bekum echol matzah*. Since women are prohibited in *chametz*, they are obligated in *matzah*. The verse thus serves both to extend the prohibition of *chametz* to women and, through the linkage, to obligate them in *matzah*.

## Kol Expansion Analysis

### Kol Expands What?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חָזֵית דְּהַאי ״כׇּל״ לְרַבּוֹיֵי נָשִׁים, וּמַפְּקַתְּ עֵירוּבוֹ? אֵימָא: לְרַבּוֹיֵי עֵירוּבוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this derivation: **What did you see** that led you to understand **that** the term **anything [*kol*]** comes to **include women and to exclude** leaven in **its mixture?** On the contrary, **say** that it comes **to include** in the punishment of *karet* one who eats **its mixture.**

Having established that the word *kol* in the *karet* verse expands the eaters to include women, the *Gemara* raises a challenge: what gives us confidence that the *kol* in the *karet* verse is expanding the category of eaters (people) rather than the objects being eaten? Why couldn't *ki kol ochel chametz ve-nichretah* use *kol* to expand *chametz* to include *betaarovet* — and thereby impose *karet* on eating a mixture?

### Chelev Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא, קָאֵי בְּאוֹכְלִין מְרַבֵּה אוֹכְלִין. קָאֵי בְּאוֹכְלִין מְרַבֵּה נֶאֱכָלִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is reasonable** to explain that the verse comes to include women since the verse is **dealing with those who** are obligated in the mitzva not to **eat** leavened bread, **it includes those who eat,** as the verse says: "For anyone who eats leavened bread…shall be cut off." It stands to reason that the expression: Anyone [*kol*] includes additional people who are punishable by *karet*, not additional types of leaven. Would a verse that **is dealing with those who** may not **eat** leaven come to **include** additional types of foods that may not **be eaten?**

To sharpen the challenge, the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* about *chelev* (forbidden fat): *ki kol ochel chelev* — "for whoever eats *chelev*." There, the word *kol* modifies *ochel*, yet the *drashah* derived from it expands not the eaters but the objects: it extends the prohibition from *chelev* of unblemished animals (*temimim*) to *chelev* of blemished animals (*ba'alei mumin*), and ultimately to the *chelev* of entirely unsanctified animals (*chullin*). So there is precedent for a *kol* that grammatically modifies "eaters" yet functionally expands the objects being eaten. Why, then, can we not use *ki kol ochel chametz* to expand the objects of the *chametz* prohibition to include *betaarovet* — and assign *karet* to eating a mixture?

### Context-Dependent Kol

The *Gemara*'s answer is that the word *kol* naturally expands what remains to be expanded given the specific context of the verse. In the *chelev* case, there was no remaining category of eaters that needed inclusion — everyone is obviously included in the prohibition of *chelev*. With nothing left to do in the direction of expanding eaters, the *kol* is put to work expanding the objects (*chelev* of different categories of animals). In the *chametz* case, however, there is a genuine category of eaters that needed to be included — namely, women, who might otherwise have been excluded because of the linkage to *matzah*. Since there is work to be done expanding the eaters, the *kol* is applied there: *kol ochel* includes women. We do not simultaneously apply it to expand the objects to *betaarovet*, because the word has already been fully employed in including women.

The principle is: *kol* expands what the context leaves unreached. Where the eaters are already fully covered, *kol* expands the objects. Where the eaters need inclusion, *kol* covers the eaters.

### Rabbanan: No Betaarovet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חָזֵית דְּהַאי ״כׇּל״ לְרַבּוֹיֵי נָשִׁים, וּמַפְּקַתְּ עֵירוּבוֹ? אֵימָא: לְרַבּוֹיֵי עֵירוּבוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this derivation: **What did you see** that led you to understand **that** the term **anything [*kol*]** comes to **include women and to exclude** leaven in **its mixture?** On the contrary, **say** that it comes **to include** in the punishment of *karet* one who eats **its mixture.**

The *Gemara* now clarifies the *Rabbanan*'s position explicitly. The *Rabbanan* — the *Chachamim* who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer — hold that *betaarovet* carries no biblical prohibition at all. They do not use the word *kol* in the prohibition verse (*kol machmetzes lo tocheilu*) to include *betaarovet*; they use only *ki kol* (the double emphasis in the *karet* verse) to include women. For the *Rabbanan*, the first *kol* does nothing with respect to objects, and the *ki kol* of the *karet* verse suffices to establish women's inclusion in the *chametz* prohibition. The result is that, according to the *Rabbanan*, *chametz betaarovet* has no self-standing biblical prohibition — perhaps only the *chatzi shiur* consideration applies.

### Eliezer: Both Inclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כׇּל״ — לָא דָּרְשִׁי, ״כִּי כׇּל״ — דָּרְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Although **they do not derive** a *halakha* from the term: **Anything, they derive** a *halakha* from the expression: **For anyone [*ki kol*],** in the verse: "For anyone who eats leaven."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֵימָא: ״כׇּל״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנָּשִׁים, ״כִּי כׇּל״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת עֵירוּבוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if indeed the phrase: For anyone, is a more inclusive expression than the simple word anything, then according to **Rabbi Eliezer,** another *halakha* could also be derived from here. **Say** that the phrase: **Anyone** who eats leaven, comes **to include the women,** and the phrase: **For anyone** who eats, comes **to include** leaven in **its mixture.** According to Rabbi Eliezer, then, one would be punishable by *karet* for eating leaven in a mixture.

Rabbi Eliezer's position is correspondingly clarified: he uses the *kol* of *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu* to include *betaarovet* within the prohibition, and uses the *ki kol* of *ki kol ochel chametz ve-nichretah* to include women within the *chametz* prohibition. Each verse thus does separate work, and both categories — *betaarovet* (under a *lav*) and women (equally prohibited) — are derived from the two verses respectively.

### Se'or & Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא ״כִּי כׇּל״ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לָא דָּרֵישׁ, וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׂאֹר בַּל תַּקְטִירוּ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא כּוּלּוֹ, מִקְצָתוֹ מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל״. עֵירוּבוֹ מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי כׇּל״. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּדָרֵישׁ ״כׇּל״ — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְקָא דָּרֵישׁ ״כִּי כׇּל״!
>
> **Translation:** **And lest you say that Rabbi Eliezer does not derive** a *halakha* from the phrase: **For anyone [*ki kol*],** as he does not consider this an inclusive expression, the result would be another contradiction. **Wasn't** it **taught** in another *baraita*: "For no [*ki kol*] leaven nor any honey shall be offered as a burnt-offering before God" (Leviticus 2:11)? Had the verse stated only: **You shall not offer leaven, I would have** derived **nothing other** than the *halakha* that it is prohibited to sacrifice **an entire** piece of leaven. **From where** is it derived that it is also prohibited to sacrifice **part of it? The verse states: "No [*kol*]** leaven," indicating that it is prohibited to sacrifice even part of it. **From where** is it derived that it is prohibited to sacrifice leaven in **a mixture? The verse states: "For no** [***ki kol***] leaven." The Gemara analyzes this statement: **Whom did you hear who derives** *halakhot* from the term: ***Kol*?** It is **Rabbi Eliezer, and** nevertheless, **he derives** additional details from the expression: ***Ki kol*.**

The *Gemara* introduces a further complication from a *baraita* in a different context: the prohibition against offering *se'or* on the *mizbeiach* (*se'or bal taktiru*). The verse states *ki kol se'or ve-khol devash lo taktiru mimenu isheh la-Hashem*. The *baraita* derives from the word *kol* in this verse that even a partial amount of *se'or* is prohibited from being burned on the altar (not only a full *kezayit*), and from the double *ki kol* that even *se'or* mixed with *matzah* in a *minchah*-offering is included in the prohibition. The *Gemara* notes that the *tanna* who is characteristically willing to derive inclusionary *derashot* from *kol* is Rabbi Eliezer — and there, *ki kol* yields two inclusions: partial amounts, and *betaarovet* in the sacrificial context.

### Unsolved Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: This matter remains **difficult,** as no satisfactory explanation has been found for why Rabbi Eliezer does not derive from the expression *ki kol* that leaven in a mixture is also prohibited.

This creates a difficulty for Rabbi Eliezer's position in the eating-context. If Rabbi Eliezer uses *ki kol* to include *betaarovet* in the *korban*-prohibition, why does he not similarly use *ki kol* in the eating-context to impose *karet* on eating *chametz betaarovet*? The *Gemara* concludes: *kashya*. This remains an unresolved problem.

## Summary & Explanatio (II)

### Two Categories Recap

Stepping back, the discussion of *daf* 43 centers on the *Mishnah*'s two major categories of *chametz*-related prohibitions. The first is *chametz betaarovet* — real *chametz* embedded within a mixture, encompassing the great bulk of the practical Pesach concerns: the *kutach*, the fermented beverages, the foods that contain *chametz* as one ingredient among others. The second is *chametz nuksha* — either non-fully fermented dough (like *se'or* at the intermediate stage) or non-fully edible preparations (like the depilatory pastes). Both categories carry a *lav* without *karet* according to those *tannaim* who recognize them, but the tannaitic debate is over which category exists and who is its author.

### Rabbanan: No Prohibition

Perhaps the most surprising finding of the entire discussion is the *Rabbanan*'s position on *chametz betaarovet*: according to them, there is no biblical prohibition at all. One who eats *kutach* containing dissolved moldy bread on Pesach transgresses no *lav* and certainly no *karet*. This is genuinely counterintuitive. Most would assume that eating real *chametz* — even in a mixture — should at minimum be a *lav*, given the principle of *ta'am ke-ikar*. But as discussed, the *Rabbanan* apparently hold that either the quantities involved are insufficient (one will never eat *kazayit bi-khdei achilat pras* of *chametz* from the mixture), or that *chametz* as *lechem* loses its identity when dissolved into a *taaruvet*, or both — and therefore no self-standing prohibition attaches. What remains is at most a *chatzi shiur* concern, which is biblically prohibited but carries no *lav* and no *malkut*.

### Eliezer: Two Rationales

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and holds that *chametz betaarovet* carries a *lav* without *karet*. As discussed, two rationales can explain why he insists on a *lav* while denying *karet*. The first rationale is quantitative: the proportions of *chametz* within the mixture are such that one will never consume *kazayit bi-khdei achilat pras* of actual *chametz*, and therefore the ordinary *karet* mechanism is unavailable. The special *lav* of *chametz betaarovet*, derived from *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu*, creates a distinct prohibition triggered by the status of the mixture as a whole rather than by the quantity of *chametz* within it. The second rationale is ontological: the identity of *chametz* as *lechem* is constitutively form-dependent, and when dissolved into a *taaruvet* that identity is lost. One is eating fermented grain ingredients, but not *lechem chametz*, and therefore *karet* — which punishes the eating of *chametz* in its paradigmatic bread-form — does not apply. The *lav* of *betaarovet* is thus a new and distinct prohibition for a categorically distinct phenomenon. These two rationales are not mutually exclusive, and the discussion continues into the next *daf*.
