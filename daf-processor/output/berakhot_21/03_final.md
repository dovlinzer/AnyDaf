# Berakhot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shema & Brachot Status

### Ba'al Keri Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּעַל קֶרִי — מְהַרְהֵר בְּלִבּוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, לֹא לְפָנֶיהָ וְלֹא לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וְעַל הַמָּזוֹן מְבָרֵךְ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ לְפָנָיו. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְבָרֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Ezra the Scribe decreed that one who is ritually impure because of a seminal emission may not engage in matters of Torah until he has immersed in a ritual bath and purified himself. This *halakha* was accepted over the course of many generations; however, many disputes arose with regard to the Torah matters to which it applies. Regarding this, the mishna says: If the time for the recitation of *Shema* arrived and **one** is impure due to a **seminal emission,** he may **contemplate** *Shema* **in his heart, but neither recites the blessings preceding** *Shema*, **nor the blessings following it. Over food** which, after partaking, one is obligated by Torah law to recite a blessing, **one recites a blessing afterward, but one does not recite a blessing beforehand,** because the blessing recited prior to eating is a requirement by rabbinic law. **And** in all of these instances **Rabbi Yehuda says: He recites a blessing beforehand and thereafter** in both the case of *Shema* and in the case of food.

The discussion on 21a continues in the midst of a broader examination of the *ba'al keri* — one who has experienced a seminal emission — and the question of what he is and is not permitted to recite. A central distinction raised in the *mishnah* concerns the difference between biblical and rabbinic obligations: a *ba'al keri* may recite *shema* and *birkat hamazon*, both of which are biblical in nature, but may not recite the *brachot* that precede *shema* or the *brachot* that precede eating, both of which are rabbinic. This framework — *birkat hatorah* is *de'oraita*, *birkat hamazon* is *de'oraita*, while the *brachot* after *talmud Torah* and before eating are rabbinic — drives the *gemara*'s subsequent analysis.

### Safek Shema/Emes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: סָפֵק קָרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, סָפֵק לֹא קָרָא — אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וְקוֹרֵא. סָפֵק אָמַר ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״, סָפֵק לֹא אָמַר — חוֹזֵר וְאוֹמֵר ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״. מַאי טַעְמָא? — קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע דְּרַבָּנַן. ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** One who is **uncertain whether he recited *Shema* or whether he did not recite** it **does not recite it again.** However, one who is **uncertain whether he recited: True and Firm [*emet veyatziv*],** the blessing that follows *Shema* in the morning, **must recite *emet veyatziv* again. What is the reason** for this? In his opinion, the obligation to recite ***Shema*** is only **by rabbinic law.** His ruling follows the principle that in cases of uncertainty involving rabbinic law, the ruling is lenient and he need not repeat it. However, since ***emet veyatziv*** is primarily a commemoration of the exodus from Egypt, it is a mitzva **by Torah law,** and, in cases of uncertainty involving Torah law, the ruling is stringent and he must repeat it.

The *gemara* records a ruling of Rav Yehuda: if a person is uncertain whether he has recited *shema* — perhaps he was distracted and cannot recall whether he said it — he does not go back and repeat it. However, if he is uncertain whether he has recited *emes v'yatziv*, the *brachot* following *shema* that lead into *shmoneh esrei*, he must go back and recite them. The *gemara* immediately provides the rationale: *krias shema* is *derabbanan*, while *emes v'yatziv* is *de'oraita*. The principle at work is that in cases of *safek* regarding a rabbinic obligation, one need not repeat it, but in cases of *safek* regarding a biblical obligation, one must.

### Shema Derabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: ״וּבְשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָהוּא בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises an objection:** How can you say that the obligation to recite *Shema* is only by rabbinic law when it is explicitly written: "And you shall recite them to your children and speak of them when you sit in your home and when you walk by the way, **when you lie down and when you rise"** (Deuteronomy 6:7)? **Abaye said to him: That** verse **was written with regard to matters of Torah.** One need not interpret the verse in the conventional manner, as obligating the recitation of *Shema*, but rather as referring to the general obligation to study Torah.

This ruling is striking, to say the least. *Emes v'yatziv* — a set of *brachot* composed by the rabbis — is classified as biblical, while *shema* — which appears explicitly in the Torah — is classified as rabbinic? Rav Yosef challenges this immediately, and notably his entire challenge consists of just two words from the *pasuk*: *ubeshochavecha uvekumacha* — "when you lie down and when you rise." What more needs to be said? The *pasuk* seems to mandate *shema* directly.

### Abaye's Resolution

Abaye responds with a bold interpretive move: that *pasuk*, he says, may well be referring to Torah study rather than to the recitation of *shema* specifically. The words *ubeshochavecha uvekumacha* appear in the context of teaching and transmitting Torah — "you shall teach them to your children, speak of them when you sit in your house, when you walk along the way, when you lie down and when you rise." If we did not already have the tradition of reciting *shema*, we might have read those *pesukim* as entirely about *limud hatorah*, about learning and teaching Torah at all times of day. Therefore, Abaye argues, *krias shema* as we know it may indeed be only *derabbanan*, while *emes v'yatziv* carries biblical force by virtue of the commandment to remember *yetzias mitzrayim* each day — *lemaan tizkor yom tzeischa me'eretz mitzrayim kol yemei chayecha*. The third paragraph of *shema*, *parshas tzitzis*, also mentions *mitzrayim*, so either *emes v'yatziv* or that paragraph could independently fulfill the biblical obligation of *zeicher yetzias mitzrayim*.

### Mishnah Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: בַּעַל קֶרִי מְהַרְהֵר בְּלִבּוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ לֹא לְפָנֶיהָ וְלֹא לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וְעַל הַמָּזוֹן מְבָרֵךְ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** From here, the Gemara attempts to resolve this issue by citing proof from the mishna. **We learned** in the mishna: **One who experienced a seminal emission** may **contemplate** *Shema* **in his heart, but neither recites the blessings preceding** *Shema*, **nor the blessings thereafter. Over food** which, after partaking, one is obligated by Torah law to recite a blessing, **one recites a blessing thereafter, but not beforehand.**

The *gemara* then tests this position against the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states that a *ba'al keri* thinks *shema* in his heart but makes no *brachot* before or after it, while for food he makes the *bracha* after eating (which is biblical) but not before (which is rabbinic). From this the *gemara* infers that the *brachot* after *shema* are treated as rabbinic in this context — yet if *emes v'yatziv* is truly biblical, the *ba'al keri* should be obligated to recite it. The *gemara* answers: why would you say *emes v'yatziv* here? Because of the mention of *yetzias mitzrayim*. But in this case, *hada'kereh bekrias shema* — the *ba'al keri* has already recited *parshas tzitzis* in his *shema*, which contains *yetzias mitzrayim*. So the biblical obligation has already been fulfilled through *shema* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — לִבְרוֹךְ לְאַחֲרֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it would enter your mind** that the obligation to recite ***emet veyatziv* is by Torah law, let him recite the blessing after** *Shema*. Since he does not recite the blessing, apparently, he is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא מְבָרֵךְ, אִי מִשּׁוּם יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם — הָא אַדְכַּר לֵיהּ בִּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this: **What is the reason that he recites** *emet veyatziv*? **If** it is **because** it deals primarily with **the exodus from Egypt, wasn't it** already **mentioned in the recitation of *Shema*,** in the portion of the ritual fringes?

### Biblical Scope

This leads to an important qualification of Rav Yehuda's original ruling. The obligation to go back and recite *emes v'yatziv* when in doubt applies specifically when one also does not know whether *shema* was recited. If one knows that *shema* was said — which includes *parshas tzitzis* and its mention of *yetzias mitzrayim* — then there is nothing biblical remaining in *emes v'yatziv* that would require repetition.

One might ask whether this biblical obligation can be fulfilled through any mention of *yetzias mitzrayim*, regardless of the specific text. The *gemara* makes no distinction on this point — any formulation that fulfills *zeicher yetzias mitzrayim* satisfies the biblical requirement, whether it is a quote from the Torah itself or rabbinic liturgy. The text of *emes v'yatziv* per se is not what makes it *de'oraita*; it is the content — the remembrance of the Exodus.

The way Tosafot reads the *gemara*, the case in which one must go back and say *emes v'yatziv* is precisely when one also does not know whether *shema* was recited at all. In that situation, no *de'oraita* obligation has been fulfilled, and one must at minimum recite *emes v'yatziv*. Tosafot further suggests that once a person is already obligated to return and say something, there may be reason to go back and say the full package — *shema* together with *emes v'yatziv* — since the *de'oraita* obligation encompasses both the *yetzias mitzrayim* of the third paragraph and *emes v'yatziv* itself.

### Shema Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא הָא וְלָא לִבְעֵי הָא! קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע עֲדִיפָא, דְּאִית בַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let him say this,** *emet veyatziv*, **and he will not need** to recite **that,** *Shema*. The Gemara responds: While one may commemorate the exodus from Egypt in either *Shema* or *emet veyatziv*, ***Shema* is preferable as it contains two** elements, both a commemoration of the exodus and an acceptance of the yoke of the kingdom of Heaven.

The *gemara* then asks: given that *parshas tzitzis* already carries the biblical content of *yetzias mitzrayim*, why should the *ba'al keri* say *shema* at all? Why not simply have him recite *emes v'yatziv* and fulfill the *de'oraita* that way? The *gemara* answers: *krias shema adifa de'is bah tartei* — *krias shema* is preferable because it contains more. It carries both *yetzias mitzrayim* and *ol malchus shamayim*, the acceptance of the yoke of Heaven. When one must choose, *krias shema* is the richer package. Still, the *gemara* affirms that when one has a *safek* whether *shema* was said, one may begin from *emes v'yatziv*, since once *shema* has been said the entire biblical obligation is covered through it.

## Prayer Obligation Rules

### Rabbi Elazar View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: סָפֵק קָרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָפֵק לֹא קָרָא — חוֹזֵר וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. סָפֵק הִתְפַּלֵּל סָפֵק לֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל — אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמִתְפַּלֵּל. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: וּלְוַאי שֶׁיִּתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said** a different opinion: One who is **uncertain whether he recited *Shema*** or **whether he did not recite** *Shema,* **must recite *Shema* again.** According to his opinion, there is a mitzva by Torah law to recite *Shema*. However, if one is **uncertain whether he prayed or whether he did not pray, he does not pray again,** as the obligation to pray is by rabbinic law. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He must pray again; **if only a person would pray throughout the entire day.**

The *gemara* now introduces Rabbi Elazar, who articulates what most people would instinctively assume. If a person is uncertain whether he recited *shema*, he goes back and recites it — since *shema* is biblical. But if he is uncertain whether he davened, he does not go back and repeat *shmoneh esrei* — because *tefillah* is rabbinic, and there is no obligation to repeat a rabbinic act when uncertain whether it was performed.

### Prayer Status

This position rests on the assumption that *tefillah* — at least in its fixed, structured form — is *derabbanan*. While there is a well-known dispute between the Rambam and Ramban on this point, even the Ramban concedes that a daily obligation to pray a fixed text three times a day is rabbinic. The Ramban holds that a biblical obligation to pray exists only in a moment of *tzarah*, when a person faces affliction and the natural human impulse is to cry out — in that context, one must direct that cry to God. The Rambam, by contrast, maintains a daily biblical obligation to pray, but even he is explicit that the specific structure — three *tefillos* per day, a defined text — is entirely rabbinic. So in either case, the structured *shmoneh esrei* as a repeated act triggers the rule: *safek derabbanan*, no need to repeat.

### Rav Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan introduces a different sensibility: *u'levai she'yitpallel adam kol hayom kulo* — would that a person could daven the entire day. This reflects the view that prayer at any time, even beyond the fixed obligations, has intrinsic value. For Rabbi Yochanan, not only is there no problem praying more than required, there is something admirable in it. Applied to the case of *safek* — where a person is unsure whether they davened *mincha* — Rabbi Yochanan's position would at minimum encourage davening again, and might even make it obligatory: after all, even if you have already davened, why not daven again?

### Safek Prayer Options

This generates a three-way framework for how to handle *safek tefillah*. The first position, reflected in Rabbi Elazar's ruling, treats *tefillah* strictly as a rabbinic obligation: if you have a doubt, don't repeat it. The second position, Rabbi Yochanan's, welcomes or even requires repetition — since davening at any time has value, there is every reason to daven again when uncertain. The third approach — a middle path widely employed in practice — is to daven again but to frame the repetition conditionally: if I have already davened, let this count as a voluntary *tefillat nedavah*; if I have not yet davened, let this fulfill my obligation.

This conditionality raises its own questions. The efficacy of such stipulations — whether one can declare in advance the dual character of an act — is debated among the *poskim*. The broader issue it touches is the nature of *kavana* and *da'at*: whether and how consciously articulated intention can shape the halachic classification of a ritual act. The question bears some similarity to the principle of *breira*, though that topic involves a distinct set of considerations and goes beyond the scope of the present discussion.

The underlying tension, however, is real and important. Those who disagree with Rabbi Yochanan are not saying there is no value to praying throughout the day. They are saying that the structured, fixed *shmoneh esrei* belongs in its designated time and mode, and that using it outside that structure — approaching it as an obligation when it isn't one — conflates two distinct categories of religious expression. Rabbi Yochanan allows the formal text to serve as personal prayer; the opposing view insists that if you want to pray personally, you should pray personally, not hijack the structured form.

## Prayer Interruption Cases

### Wake Mid-Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָיָה עוֹמֵד בִּתְפִלָּה וְנִזְכַּר שֶׁהִתְפַּלֵּל פּוֹסֵק — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶמְצַע בְּרָכָה. אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, בְּעַן מִינֵּיהּ: הָנֵי בְּנֵי בֵי רַב דְּטָעוּ וּמַדְכְּרִי דְּחוֹל בְּשַׁבָּת, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּגְמְרוּ? וַאֲמַר לַן: גּוֹמְרִין כׇּל אוֹתָהּ בְּרָכָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** One **who was standing in prayer and remembered that he** already **prayed must interrupt** his prayer, **even in the middle of a blessing.** The Gemara challenges this: **Is that so? Didn't Rav Naḥman say: When we were in the school of Rabba bar Avuh we raised a dilemma before him: Those students in the school of Rav** who **mistakenly recited a** blessing from the **weekday** *Amida* **on Shabbat, what is** the ruling with regards to **completing** the weekday prayer? **And** Rabba bar Avuh **said to us:** The ruling is that **one must complete that entire blessing.** How then did Rav Yehuda say that one must interrupt his prayer even in the middle of a blessing?

The *gemara* records another ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: if a person is standing in *tefillah* — in the middle of *shmoneh esrei* — and suddenly recalls that he has already davened *mincha* earlier that day, he must stop immediately, *posek va'afilu b'emtza berachah* — even in the middle of a *bracha*. The *gemara* challenges this from a related case. Rav Nachman reports that when he was studying with Rabba bar Avuha, students asked: if someone accidentally began reciting the weekday *shmoneh esrei* on *Shabbat* — going on autopilot through the middle *brachot* — what should they do? Rabba bar Avuha answered: *gomrim kol ota berachah* — they complete whichever *bracha* they are currently in the middle of, and then transition to the *Shabbat* text. This seems to indicate that one does not interrupt mid-*berachah*, contrary to Shmuel's ruling.

### Private vs Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא! הָתָם גַּבְרָא בַּר חִיּוּבָא הוּא, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דְּלָא אַטְרְחוּהוּ מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד שַׁבָּת, אֲבָל הָכָא — הָא צַלִּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can you compare** the two cases? **There,** on Shabbat, **the individual is one who is obligated** and should actually recite all eighteen blessings, **and it is the Sages who did not impose upon him in deference to Shabbat** and instituted an abridged formula. **But here, didn't he** already **pray?** Therefore he can stop, even in the middle of a blessing.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing the two cases. In the *Shabbat* scenario, *hatam gavra bar chiyuva hu* — the person is still obligated to daven *shmoneh esrei*. The rabbis did not impose the full weekday *shmoneh esrei* on *Shabbat*, perhaps because it would be burdensome, or because personal requests are not appropriate for *Shabbat* — but the person remains obligated in *tefillah*. He is simply reciting the wrong text, not an entirely inappropriate act. So the rabbis allow him to finish the current *bracha* and then proceed correctly. But in Shmuel's case, *aval hacha hat'aya hi* — it is simply a mistake. The person has already davened *mincha*. There is no remaining obligation, and continuing the *shmoneh esrei* in this state is entirely out of place. He must stop.

One might ask why the reasoning from Rabbi Yochanan does not apply here: if davening the whole day is valuable, why not let the person finish the *shmoneh esrei* as a voluntary *tefillah*? Both Rashi and Tosafot cite the *Behag* — the early geonic work *Halachot Gedolot* — who distinguishes precisely here. We rule like Rabbi Yochanan in the case of *safek*: if uncertain whether you davened, daven again, because even if you already did, there is value in doing so and the intention was not clearly fixed as obligatory from the outset. But we rule like Shmuel in the case of *vadai*: if you *know* you have already davened and you stand up explicitly to fulfill your obligation, then the act has been defined from the beginning as obligatory structured prayer. Once you realize mid-*shmoneh esrei* that no such obligation exists, you cannot retroactively recast it as voluntary. You are stuck in the genre you entered, and since that genre no longer applies, you must stop.

This is not merely formalism. It touches on something deep about the nature of structured versus personal prayer. The act's meaning is determined not only by its content but by the intention with which it was begun. *Kavana* shapes the act — and once the act is framed as obligatory, one cannot shift it to volitional in the middle. Rabbi Yochanan permits using the formal text as personal prayer, but only when it is approached as personal expression. Blending the two orientations mid-stream is not allowed even by Rabbi Yochanan.

### Home to Shul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הִתְפַּלֵּל וְנִכְנַס לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וּמָצָא צִבּוּר שֶׁמִּתְפַּלְּלִין, אִם יָכוֹל לְחַדֵּשׁ בָּהּ דָּבָר — יַחְזוֹר וְיִתְפַּלֵּל, וְאִם לָאו — אַל יַחְזוֹר וְיִתְפַּלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** One who already **prayed, and** then **enters a synagogue to find a congregation** standing and **praying, if he is able to introduce a new element,** an expression or request, into his prayer, **he may pray again, and if not, he may not pray again.**

Shmuel offers a second ruling: if a person has already davened at home and then arrives at the *beit knesset* to find the congregation in the middle of *shmoneh esrei*, he should not daven again — unless he can add something personal to the prayer. *Im yachol l'chadesh bo davar yachzor v'yitpallel, v'im lav al yachzor v'yitpallel* — if he can add a *chidush davar*, a personal innovation, he may daven again; if not, he should not.

### Chidush Davar

This concept of *chidush davar* is the mechanism by which Shmuel allows the formal text to function as personal prayer. The same authority who said you must stop in the middle of *shmoneh esrei* when you realize you've already davened is now saying: if you can make this prayer genuinely personal — by inserting your own words, adding something that is truly yours — then you may use the formal text again even having already fulfilled your obligation. The *chidush* transforms the act from repetition of an obligation into an expression of personal prayer, which always has value.

The relationship between *chidush davar* and spoken articulation is important. *Hirhurim*, thoughts alone, are insufficient — it must be expressed in some way. But the deeper question of what qualifies as *chidush davar* — whether it must be a new textual addition or whether a wholly different inner orientation suffices — will be the subject of further discussion in the *gemara* later. Rabbi Yochanan holds that approaching the prayer with a personal, non-obligatory mindset is itself sufficient to make it personal. Shmuel insists that something must be done *to* the prayer, something that distinguishes this recitation from the obligatory one. The debate is not about whether personal prayer has value; it is about whether the formal text can ever become personal through mindset alone, or whether it requires an external act of personalization.

One might wonder whether *chidush davar* can take the form of a purely experiential difference — davening the same text in a different setting, with a different *chazzan*, in a different emotional state. This question will be addressed directly later in the *masechet*, where the *gemara* returns to the nature of *chidush davar* and whether inner experience alone can constitute it.

### Two Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן קַמַּיְיתָא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי יָחִיד וְיָחִיד אוֹ צִבּוּר וְצִבּוּר, אֲבָל יָחִיד לְגַבֵּי צִבּוּר כְּמַאן דְּלָא צַלִּי דָּמֵי, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אַתְחֵיל בַּהּ, אֲבָל הָתָם דְּאַתְחֵיל בַּהּ — אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This concept is identical to Shmuel's previous statement regarding one who already prayed that he need not pray again. Nevertheless, both statements are **necessary. If he had taught us the first** *halakha*, we would have said that **applies only** to a case involving **an individual** who prayed **and an individual** who began to repeat the prayer, **or** a case where he prayed as part of **a congregation and** began to repeat it as part of **a congregation; however,** in a case where he initially prayed by himself and subsequently joined the congregation at the venue where it was praying, we might have said that **an individual vis-à-vis the congregation is** considered **as one who has not prayed.** Therefore, **he taught us** that in this case, too, one may not repeat the prayer. **And,** on the other hand, **if he had taught us here** only with regard to one who entered a synagogue, we would have thought that the reason he may not pray again is **because he did not** yet **begin** to recite the prayer, **but there, in the case where he** already **began** to recite the prayer, **say** that this is **not** the case and he may continue to repeat the prayer. Therefore, both statements are **necessary.**

The *gemara* then explains why both of Shmuel's rulings are necessary and neither can be derived from the other. Had we only heard the first case — that one stops mid-*shmoneh esrei* upon remembering one has already davened — we might have thought that rule applies only when both instances are of the same type: private and private, or communal and communal. If the first *tefillah* was private (*yachid*) and now the person finds himself in a communal (*tzibur*) setting, one might have thought the communal experience is categorically different — *ke'man d'la tzalay dami*, it is as if he never davened in terms of that richer experience — and therefore he may daven again as an obligation, not merely as a voluntary act. Shmuel's second ruling teaches us otherwise: even if you davened privately at home and now you arrive at a *minyan*, you do not repeat the *shmoneh esrei* on account of the different setting. The obligation is the obligation; having fulfilled it privately does not entitle you to repeat it communally.

Conversely, had we only been told the second case — do not daven again when you arrive at *shul* having already davened at home — one might have thought the rule is simply: don't start. Once you have not started, don't begin. But if you are already *in the middle* of *shmoneh esrei* when you realize you've already davened, perhaps you should finish, since stopping mid-stream seems disruptive. Shmuel's first ruling teaches that even in the middle, even mid-*berachah*, you stop. Both rulings are necessary precisely because the logic in each direction is plausible.

The upshot of Shmuel's framework is that in the context of obligatory structured prayer, once the obligation is fulfilled you do not repeat it — not when you find yourself mid-prayer, not when you arrive at *shul*, not in any circumstance — unless you transform the act through *chidush davar* into something other than repetition of an obligation.

## Starting Mid-Congregation

### Timing Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַנִּכְנָס לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וּמָצָא צִבּוּר שֶׁמִּתְפַּלְּלִין, אִם יָכוֹל לְהַתְחִיל וְלִגְמוֹר עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַגִּיעַ שְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר לְ״מוֹדִים״ — יִתְפַּלֵּל. וְאִם לָאו — אַל יִתְפַּלֵּל. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: אִם יָכוֹל לְהַתְחִיל וְלִגְמוֹר עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַגִּיעַ שְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר לִ״קְדוּשָּׁה״ — יִתְפַּלֵּל, וְאִם לָאו — אַל יִתְפַּלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: One who** did not yet pray and **enters a synagogue and found that the congregation is** in the midst of **reciting** the *Amida* **prayer, if he is able to begin and complete** his own prayer **before the prayer leader reaches** the blessing of **thanksgiving [*modim*], he should** begin to **pray, and, if not, he should not** begin to **pray. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: If he is able to begin and complete** his prayer **before the prayer leader reaches sanctification [*kedusha*], then he should** begin to **pray. If not, then he should not** begin to **pray.**

The *gemara* now raises a related scenario: a person arrives at the *beit knesset* and finds the congregation already in the middle of *shmoneh esrei*, but he himself has not yet davened. The question is whether he should begin *shmoneh esrei* now, knowing that the congregation may finish before he does, causing him to miss certain communal responses.

### Modim Standard

Rav Yehuda rules: *im yachol lehatchil uligmor ad shelo yagia shliach tzibur l'Modim, yitpallel* — if he can begin and finish his *shmoneh esrei* before the *shliach tzibur* reaches *Modim*, he should daven; if not, he should not. The cutoff, remarkably, is not *kedushah* but *Modim*. It is forbidden to be in the middle of one's own *shmoneh esrei* when the congregation is saying *Modim*.

### Kedusha Standard

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagrees and sets a stricter standard: one must be able to finish before the *shliach tzibur* reaches *kedushah*. According to him, the operative concern is the inability to say *kedushah* with the congregation.

### Private Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִפַּלְגִי — מָר סָבַר יָחִיד אוֹמֵר ״קְדוּשָּׁה״, וּמַר סָבַר אֵין יָחִיד אוֹמֵר ״קְדוּשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The basis for their dispute is that one **Sage,** Rav Huna, **holds: An individual** is permitted to **recite *kedusha*** on his own, so he need not insist on reciting it along with the prayer leader; **and** the other **Sage,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **holds** that **an individual may not recite *kedusha*** alone, and, therefore he is required to complete his prayer before the communal prayer leader reaches *kedusha*.

The *gemara* identifies the underlying dispute: *mar savar yachid omer kedushah, u'mar savar ein yachid omer kedushah* — one authority holds that an individual may recite *kedushah* within his private *shmoneh esrei*, while the other holds that this is not possible. According to the opinion that *kedushah* can be said privately, one need not worry about missing it with the congregation, since one can say it within one's own *shmoneh esrei*. Therefore the only critical cutoff is *Modim*. According to the opinion that *kedushah* requires communal participation and cannot be said privately, missing *kedushah* with the congregation is itself a serious loss, and one should not begin if that is unavoidable.

### Kedusha Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין הַיָּחִיד אוֹמֵר ״קְדוּשָּׁה״ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִקְדַּשְׁתִּי בְּתוֹךְ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁבִּקְדוּשָּׁה לֹא יְהֵא פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, Rav Adda bar Ahava stated,** in accordance with the second opinion: **From where is it derived that an individual may not recite *kedusha*** alone? **As it is stated: "And I shall be hallowed among the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 22:32), **any expression of sanctity may not be** recited in a quorum of **fewer than ten** men.

Rav Adda bar Ahava provides the source for why *kedushah* cannot be said privately: *v'nikdashti b'toch bnei Yisrael* — "I will be sanctified among the children of Israel." Any *davar shebekedushah* — any act of publicly sanctifying God's name — falls under this verse and requires a minimum of ten. This principle is derived through a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *toch* appears here in *Vayikra*, and the same word appears in the episode of Korach — *hibadlu mitoch ha'edah hazot* — where the congregation numbered ten (excluding the two righteous men, Moshe and Aharon). Just as there *toch* refers to ten, so here *toch* refers to ten. And since *kedushah* is paradigmatically a *davar shebekedushah*, it requires a *minyan* of ten.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע? דְּתָנֵי רַבְנַאי אֲחוּהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אָתְיָא ״תּוֹךְ״ ״תּוֹךְ״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְנִקְדַּשְׁתִּי בְּתוֹךְ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״הִבָּדְלוּ מִתּוֹךְ הָעֵדָה הַזֹּאת״, מָה לְהַלָּן עֲשָׂרָה. אַף כָּאן עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How is this inferred** from that verse? The Gemara responds: This must be understood in light of a *baraita*, **which was taught by Rabbenai, the brother** of **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: It is inferred** by means of a verbal analogy [*gezera shava*] between the words **among, among. Here it is written: "And I shall be hallowed among the children of Israel," and there,** regarding Korah's congregation, **it is written "Separate yourselves from among this congregation"** (Numbers 16:21). **Just as there** among connotes **ten, so too here,** among connotes **ten.** The connotation of ten associated with the word among written in the portion of Korah is, in turn, derived by means of another verbal analogy between the word congregation written there and the word congregation written in reference to the ten spies who slandered Eretz Yisrael: "How long shall I bear with this evil congregation?" (Numbers 14:27). Consequently, among the congregation there must be at least ten.

It is worth noting that the *gemara* here says *asarah* — ten — without specifying men. The *rishonim* engage with this question. The Rambam writes that a *minyan* consists of *asarah gedolim bnei chorin* and does not explicitly exclude women. The *Shulchan Aruch* does explicitly exclude women, but that formulation is not in the *gemara* itself. Similarly, Rav Moshe, when asked about counting non-observant Jews for a *minyan*, noted that since we derive the requirement from the *meraglim* — designated by the verse as *ha'edah hara'ah hazot*, the wicked congregation — the *halacha* cannot exclude individuals on the basis of their level of observance.

### Kedusha Text/Liturgy

This raises a subtle but important question: if *kedushah* requires a *minyan* because it is a *davar shebekedushah*, what about instances where one reads the words of *kedushah* as biblical text rather than as liturgy? May an individual read *kadosh kadosh kadosh* when reciting the verses of *Yeshayahu*, or *v'nikdashti b'toch bnei Yisrael* when learning *Vayikra*?

The answer lies in the distinction between reciting text *as text* and performing a liturgical act. The *paytanim* and the *halacha* recognize that the same words can function differently depending on their framing. A parallel case is the recitation of *Hashem Hashem Kel rachum v'chanun* in *Selichot*. When said *betzibur* this functions as a *davar shebekedushah*, but if an individual recites *Selichot* alone, the practice is to read those words with the cantillation (*trop*) appropriate to *Vayikra*, treating them as *pesukim* rather than liturgy, and to conclude the verse through its natural ending — even though the liturgical custom cuts off the recitation earlier. This dual framing — the same words, one as text and one as liturgy — illustrates the distinction.

*Krias shema* is in an analogous position. The experience of *krias shema* is fundamentally that of reading biblical text — *krias shema*, the reading of *shema*. The text is Torah, and reciting it participates in the *mitzvah* of Torah reading and declaration of God's sovereignty. *Kedushah*, by contrast, is a dramatic liturgical reenactment — the community collectively scripting and performing the angelic proclamation of God's holiness. It is not recitation of a text; it is enactment of a scene. That categorical difference is what places *kedushah* firmly in the category of *devarim shebekedushah* requiring a *minyan*, while reading the words of *kadosh kadosh kadosh* as part of *psukim* does not carry the same requirement.

A similar consideration applies to *kiddush*. The opening verses of *kiddush* — *vayhi erev vayehi voker yom hashishi*, *vayechulu hashamayim* — are sung in a traditional *niggun*, but that *niggun* is not the biblical *trop*. The question of whether those verses require the same framing distinctions as other liturgical-textual hybrids is a nuanced one, though in practice it is not treated with the same stringency.

### Modim Significance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת מִפְסָק לָא פָּסֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And, in any case, everyone** agrees that **one may not interrupt** his prayer in order to respond to *kedusha*.

Returning to the question of why *Modim* is specifically the critical threshold — more so, apparently, than *kedushah* even according to Rav Yehuda — the answer is illuminating. One might have assumed the concern with *Modim* is about the text itself: *modim anachnu lach*, the expression of gratitude, should be said in solidarity with the congregation. But Rashi explains that it is not the text of *Modim* that is the problem; it is the physical act of bowing. If the entire congregation is bowing before God and one individual is standing upright, *nireh ke-kofer be-mi she-chaveirav mishtachavim lo* — he appears as if he denies the God before whom his fellows are prostrating themselves. The problem is the visible, embodied experience of communal submission to God, not the words.

### Tosafot Solutions

This reframing opens the door to a practical solution, which Tosafot attributes to Rabbeinu Tam: when the *chazzan* reaches *Modim* and the congregation bows, one who is still in the middle of his own *shmoneh esrei* should simply bow — without saying anything — in solidarity with the congregation. The bowing itself resolves the visual and experiential problem without constituting an interruption of *shmoneh esrei*.

Similarly, for *kedushah*, Rashi suggests that silent listening can achieve *yetzias* for the individual: *shomea k'oneh* — hearing is like responding. If one silently attends to the congregation's *kedushah* while in the middle of one's own *shmoneh esrei*, one has in effect participated in *kedushah* without speaking, and without creating a formal *hefsek* in one's prayer. Tosafot debates whether this *shomea k'oneh* mechanism, when it achieves the level of considered as if spoken, would itself constitute an interruption in *shmoneh esrei*. The conclusion Tosafot reaches is that since one did not actually speak, it does not constitute an interruption, even if it counts as participation.

The practical outcome, then, is as follows. The *gemara* instructs *lechatchila*: do not begin *shmoneh esrei* if you cannot finish in time. But Tosafot provides solutions for cases where one finds oneself mid-*shmoneh esrei* facing these situations. For *Modim*: bow silently and say nothing. For *kedushah*: listen silently and be *yotzei* through *shomea k'oneh*. These are *bedi'avad* solutions, not license to disregard the *gemara*'s directive, but they address the reality that one cannot always time one's prayer perfectly.

## Ba'al Keri Brachot

### Rabbi Yehuda Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מְבָרֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Ezra the Scribe decreed that one who is ritually impure because of a seminal emission may not engage in matters of Torah until he has immersed in a ritual bath and purified himself. This *halakha* was accepted over the course of many generations; however, many disputes arose with regard to the Torah matters to which it applies. Regarding this, the mishna says: If the time for the recitation of *Shema* arrived and **one** is impure due to a **seminal emission,** he may **contemplate** *Shema* **in his heart, but neither recites the blessings preceding** *Shema*, **nor the blessings following it. Over food** which, after partaking, one is obligated by Torah law to recite a blessing, **one recites a blessing afterward, but one does not recite a blessing beforehand,** because the blessing recited prior to eating is a requirement by rabbinic law. **And** in all of these instances **Rabbi Yehuda says: He recites a blessing beforehand and thereafter** in both the case of *Shema* and in the case of food.

The *gemara* returns to the issue of *ba'al keri* with a ruling attributed to Rabbi Yehuda: *mevarech lifnehem v'achareihem* — a *ba'al keri* may recite the *brachot* both before and after *shema*, and presumably also before and after food. Rabbi Yehuda is not troubled by the *ba'al keri* status at all.

### Ba'al Keri Assumption

This immediately raises a question about Rabbi Yehuda's underlying position. The logic of the *mishnah*'s distinctions — where *ba'al keri* is permitted to recite what is biblical but not what is rabbinic — assumes that a *ba'al keri* is restricted from engaging with Torah and God's name at a certain level. If Rabbi Yehuda permits recitation of all *brachot*, he must hold that a *ba'al keri* is not prohibited from *divrei Torah* at all.

### Har Sinai Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל קֶרִי שֶׁאָסוּר בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ וְלִבְנֵי בָנֶיךָ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״יוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַדְתָּ וְגוֹ׳״. מָה לְהַלָּן בַּעֲלֵי קְרָיִין אֲסוּרִין, אַף כָּאן בַּעֲלֵי קְרָיִין אֲסוּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that Rabbi Yehuda holds that one who experienced a seminal emission is permitted** to engage **in matters of Torah? Didn't Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say: From where** in the Torah is it derived **that one who experiences a seminal emission is prohibited from** engaging **in matters of Torah? As it is stated:** "Just take heed and guard your soul diligently lest you forget the things your eyes have seen, and lest they depart from your heart, for all the days of your life, **and you shall impart them to your children and your children's children"** (Deuteronomy 4:9), from which we derive, among other things, the obligation to study Torah. **And, juxtaposed to it,** is the verse: **"The day that you stood** before the Lord your God at Horeb" (Deuteronomy 4:10). This juxtaposition teaches us that **just as below,** at the revelation at Mount Sinai, **those who experienced a seminal emission were prohibited** and were commanded to refrain from relations with their wives and immerse themselves, **so too here,** throughout the generations, **those who experience a seminal emission are prohibited** from engaging in Torah study.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi had provided the source for the prohibition. He derives it from the juxtaposition in *Devarim*: the *pasuk* commands *v'hodatam l'vanecha v'livnei vanecha* — you shall inform your children and your children's children of the Torah — and immediately adjacent is the verse *yom asher amadta lifnei Hashem Elokecha be-Chorev*, the day you stood at Har Sinai. Just as the revelation at *Har Sinai* required those present to refrain from sexual activity — an explicit divine command was issued to maintain that state of separation — so too all engagement with Torah is likened to *Har Sinai*, and therefore a *ba'al keri* should be prohibited. This prohibition extends even to reciting a *bracha*, since any utterance of God's name in a Torah context falls under this framework.

### Smuchim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא דָּרֵישׁ ״סְמוּכִים״, וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲפִילּוּ מַאן דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ סְמוּכִים בְּכָל הַתּוֹרָה, בְּמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה — דָּרֵישׁ. דְּהָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא דָּרֵישׁ ״סְמוּכִין״ בְּכָל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ וּבְמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה — דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that Rabbi Yehuda does not derive homiletic interpretations from juxtaposed** verses, **didn't Rav Yosef** already say: **Even one who does not derive homiletic interpretations from juxtaposed** verses throughout **the entire Torah,** nevertheless, **derives** them **in Deuteronomy [*Mishne Torah*], as Rabbi Yehuda does not derive homiletic interpretations from juxtaposed** verses **throughout the entire Torah and he does derive them in *Mishne Torah*.**

The *gemara* asks: does Rabbi Yehuda not accept the principle of *smuchin* — the halachic significance of juxtaposition? If so, why does he not draw the same conclusion from the juxtaposition of Torah learning with *Har Sinai*?

### Mishneh Torah Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ מְנָא לַן דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ — דְּתַנְיָא, בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר ״מְכַשֵּׁפָה לֹא תְחַיֶּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר ״כׇּל שׁוֹכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה מוֹת יוּמָת״ — סְמָכוֹ עִנְיָן לוֹ, לוֹמַר: מָה שׁוֹכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה בִּסְקִילָה, אַף מְכַשֵּׁפָה נָמֵי בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we derive** that Rabbi Yehuda **does not derive homiletic interpretations from juxtaposed** verses **throughout the entire Torah? As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the punishment of a sorceress, **ben Azzai says: It is stated: "You shall not allow a sorceress to live"** (Exodus 22:17), although the manner of her execution is not specified, **and it is stated: "Whoever lies with a beast shall surely be put to death"** (Exodus 22:18). The fact that the Torah **juxtaposed this matter to that** was **to say: Just as one who lies with a beast** is executed **by stoning** (see Leviticus 20), **so too a sorceress** is executed **by stoning.**

The *gemara* demonstrates that Rabbi Yehuda does indeed employ *smuchin* in *Devarim*, through two examples. First, the case of a woman executed for witchcraft: Ben Azzai derives the form of execution — *sekilah*, stoning — from the juxtaposition of the prohibition against a *mechashefah* with the verse about one who lies with an animal, for whom stoning is explicit. Rabbi Yehuda rejects this derivation, preferring instead a logical argument from the categories of *ov* and *yidoni* — forms of necromancy that explicitly carry the penalty of *sekilah* — to establish the execution of *mechashefah* by *sekilah* as well. This shows that Rabbi Yehuda does *not* employ *smuchin* across the Torah generally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּמָכוֹ עִנְיָן לוֹ, נוֹצִיא לָזֶה לִסְקִילָה? אֶלָּא: אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי בִּכְלַל כׇּל הַמְכַשְּׁפִים הָיוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצְאוּ — לְהַקִּישׁ לָהֶן וְלוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי בִּסְקִילָה — אַף מְכַשֵּׁפָה בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this proof **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: And does** the fact **that** the Torah **juxtaposed this matter to that warrant taking** this person **out to be stoned?** Should he be sentenced to the most severe of the death penalties on that basis **Rather,** the source is: **Mediums and wizards were included among all sorcerers. And why were they singled out** from the rest, in the verse: "And a man or a woman who is a medium or a wizard shall surely be put to death; they shall stone them with stones, their blood is upon them" (Leviticus 20:27)? In order to **draw an analogy to them and say to you: Just as a medium and a wizard** are executed **by stoning, so too is a sorceress** executed **by stoning.**

Second, and to establish that Rabbi Yehuda *does* use *smuchin* in *Devarim*: the law that a man may not marry a woman his father had sexual relations with, even outside of marriage. Rav Gidel in the name of Rav establishes this from the juxtaposition in *Devarim* of *lo yikach ish et eshet aviv v'lo yigaleh knaf aviv* with the passage about a man who lies with a woman and the resulting obligations — *smuchin* in *Devarim*. Rabbi Yehuda accepts this derivation and rules accordingly, prohibiting such a union. This confirms that Rabbi Yehuda does apply *smuchin* as a halachic tool when the juxtaposition occurs in *Devarim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה מְנָא לַן דְּדָרֵישׁ — דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר נוֹשֵׂא אָדָם אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו וּמְפוּתַּת אָבִיו אֲנוּסַת בְּנוֹ וּמְפוּתַּת בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we derive** that Rabbi Yehuda **derives homiletic interpretations** from juxtaposed verses **in *Mishne Torah*? As it was taught** in another *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer said that a man** may **wed** a woman **raped by his father and** one **seduced by his father;** a woman **raped by his son and** one **seduced by his son.** Though one is prohibited by Torah law from marrying the wife of his father or the wife of his son, this prohibition does not apply to a woman raped or seduced by them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹסֵר בַּאֲנוּסַת אָבִיו וּבִמְפוּתַּת אָבִיו. וְאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? — דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יִקַּח אִישׁ אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיו וְלֹא יְגַלֶּה אֶת כְּנַף אָבִיו״. כָּנָף שֶׁרָאָה אָבִיו — לֹא יְגַלֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda prohibits** him from marrying **a woman raped by his father and a woman seduced by his father. And Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: What is the reason for Rabbi Yehuda's** opinion? **As it is written: "A man shall not take his father's wife, and shall not uncover his father's skirt"** (Deuteronomy 23:1). The last expression, "and shall not uncover his father's skirt," implies that: **A skirt that has been seen by his father,** i.e., any woman who has had sexual relations with his father, **may not be uncovered** by his son, i.e., his son may not marry her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּבַאֲנוּסַת אָבִיו כְּתִיב — דִּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״וְנָתַן הָאִישׁ הַשּׁוֹכֵב עִמָּהּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** do we know **that** the verse **is written with regard to a woman raped by his father? As** the previous section, **juxtaposed to it,** deals with the laws of rape: **"And the man who lay with her must give** her father fifty shekels…because he has violated her" (Deuteronomy 22:29).

### Har Sinai Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִין בְּמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה דָּרֵישׁ, וְהָנֵי סְמוּכִין מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְאִידָךְ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד לִבְנוֹ תּוֹרָה מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִלּוּ קִבְּלָהּ מֵהַר חוֹרֵב. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּם לְבָנֶיךָ וְלִבְנֵי בָנֶיךָ״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ ״יוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַדְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בְּחוֹרֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** At any rate, we see that in Deuteronomy, Rabbi Yehuda derives homiletic interpretations from juxtaposed verses. Why does he fail to derive that one who experiences a seminal emission is prohibited from engaging in matters of Torah from the juxtaposition of the verses? **They replied: Indeed, in *Mishne Torah*** Rabbi Yehuda **does derive homiletic interpretations** from the juxtaposition of verses, **but** he requires **these juxtaposed verses** in order **to** derive **another** statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: One who teaches his son Torah, the verse ascribes to him** credit **as if he received** the Torah **from Mount Horeb. As it is stated: "And you shall impart them to your children and your children's children"** (Deuteronomy 4:9) **after which it is written: "The day that you stood before the Lord your God at Horeb."** Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda cannot derive from that same juxtaposition a prohibition banning one who experienced a seminal emission from engaging in matters of Torah.

Given that the *Har Sinai* juxtaposition also appears in *Devarim* — *v'hodatam l'vanecha... yom asher amadta lifnei Hashem Elokecha be-Chorev* — Rabbi Yehuda should, by his own methodology, derive the *ba'al keri* prohibition from it. So why does he permit a *ba'al keri* to say *brachot*?

The *gemara* resolves this: Rabbi Yehuda reads the *smuchin* in *Devarim* here, but does not derive from it the prohibition on *ba'al keri* learning Torah. Instead, he follows what Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says the juxtaposition teaches: *kol hamelamed et beno Torah, ma'aleh alav hakatuv k'ilu kibelah me'Har Chorev* — whoever teaches his child Torah, Scripture regards it as if he himself received the Torah from *Har Chorev*. The juxtaposition of Torah teaching with the day at *Har Sinai* teaches not that Torah study requires the same physical conditions as *Har Sinai*, but rather that teaching Torah to one's children is itself a participation in *matan Torah* — a reliving of the chain of transmission that began at *Sinai*. It is not a statement about bodily state; it is a statement about the power of transmitting Torah from generation to generation.

Rabbi Yehuda therefore uses the *smuchin* of *Devarim* — as his methodology requires — but derives a different lesson from it than Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi does. And since the juxtaposition speaks to the value of teaching Torah and not to the physical prerequisites for Torah study, it yields no prohibition on the *ba'al keri*. The discussion will continue.
