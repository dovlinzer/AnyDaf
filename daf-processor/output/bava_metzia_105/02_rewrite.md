# Bava Metzia 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Iska Structure

### Iska Definition

The *iska* is a mechanism by which one party provides capital to another in a form that generates a return without violating the prohibition against interest. Its structure is carefully designed so that the arrangement does not even appear to be a loan at interest. The capital transferred is treated as two halves: one half is a *pikadon*, held by the working party as a kind of guardianship — it remains, in a legal sense, the lender's property — while the other half constitutes an outright loan, which the worker treats as his own to invest.

The worker goes out and trades with the combined sum. If he incurs a loss, the losses are split evenly between the two parties, reflecting equal partnership in the downside. On the profit side, however, the split is asymmetric: the worker retains two-thirds of any profit and the lender receives only one-third. This asymmetry is essential. Were profits split equally, the arrangement would be *mechzei k'ribbit* — it would look like a loan at interest, with the lender receiving a guaranteed proportional return. By giving the worker a larger share of the profit, the arrangement falls below the threshold of what constitutes the appearance of interest. This structure is a *takanah shel chachamim*: the Sages instituted this form precisely so that capital could be deployed productively without transgressing the prohibition on *ribbit*.

### One/Two Iskot

Rava, at the bottom of 104b, addresses the interplay between the number of ventures and the number of documents. His ruling — *chada iska u-trei shtarei psida d'malveh, trei iskei v-chad shtara psida d'loveh* — is terse, and Rashi expands it at considerable length.

Take the case of *chada iska u-trei shtarei*: a single bundle of goods, say a consignment of textiles (Rashi uses the example of *chabilot shel bgadim*), is split across two separate contracts. One portion is destined for Nehardea, the other for Pumbedita, and each is governed by its own *shtar*. Because they are two separate documents, the two halves are treated entirely independently. Suppose the Nehardea half loses five units and the Pumbedita half gains fifteen. The loss of five is split evenly: the lender bears minus two and a half and the worker bears minus two and a half. The gain of fifteen is split one-third to the lender — plus five — and two-thirds to the worker — plus ten. The lender's net is minus two and a half plus five, yielding a net gain of only two and a half. This is *psida d'malveh*: by treating the two halves separately, the lender comes out worse than he would if they were consolidated.

Now take *trei iskei v-chad shtara*: two separate bundles of goods, but governed by a single contract. If one bundle loses five and the other gains fifteen, they are pooled under the single *shtar*. The net result is a gain of ten, of which one-third — approximately three and a third — goes to the lender. That is *psida d'loveh*: when everything is aggregated, the lender does better and the worker does relatively worse.

Rava's point is simply that the choice of how to structure the documentation has real financial consequences for both parties, and which structure is adopted determines who bears the greater burden when the venture's components produce mixed results.

### Effort & Recovery

Rava adds a further ruling at the top of 105a. If a person accepts an *iska* from a colleague and loses money on it — but then, without informing the lender, exerts himself further and recoups the loss — he cannot later turn to the lender and demand that the lender share retroactively in the earlier losses. The lender's response is decisive: you went back to work and recovered the principal not on my behalf, but so that you would not be called someone who fails at business ventures — *mafsidei iska*. The fact that you replenished the principal on your own effort means that we simply return to the original terms of the agreement. The earlier loss, at the moment it occurred, was a real liability, but because the worker chose to remediate it unilaterally and without disclosure, he cannot now reconstruct a mid-venture loss position and demand the lender share it.

## Partnership Exit

### Cannot Split

Rava next addresses the case of two partners who jointly receive an *iska* from a third party. They invest together, the venture turns profitable, and one partner wishes to end his participation — *lo pelog*. If the other partner responds that he wishes to continue and earn more profit, Rava rules that the dissenting partner has the legal power to prevent a split: *dino hu de-me'akev*. One partner cannot unilaterally exit the joint arrangement simply because a profit has been realized at a given moment.

### Profit Lien

The Gemara then works through the arguments serially. If the partner seeking to exit says: give me my half of the profit — *hav li plaga de-ravcha* — the retort is *ravcha le-karna mishtabed*: the profit is liened to the principal. The profit must remain available to cover any future loss in the principal. Because the *iska* continues, the profit cannot be extracted prematurely.

If he then says: fine, give me my half of the profit and my half of the principal, and we will each go our separate ways — the response is *iska de-hadada mishtabed*: the two halves of the *iska* are mutually obligated. The profit of one half stands ready to cover a loss in the principal of the other half. If they were to split the *iska* into two independent halves, this cross-coverage would be lost.

### Mazla D'bei Trei

Suppose the partner seeking to exit concedes all of this — he accepts that profit is liened to principal and that principal and profit are interdependent — but says: I understand, I will remain responsible for your losses from my profit, and your profit will cover my losses; I simply do not want to be actively involved in the venture anymore. The final argument deployed against him is *mazla d'bei trei adif*: the fortune of a partnership of two is superior to that of either individual working alone. Working together, their combined *mazla* is better than if each were to proceed independently.

Tosafot note that *mazla d'bei trei adif* is in a sense always a winning argument — it could justify preventing any exit under any circumstances. That is precisely why the stronger arguments are presented first: that the profit is liened to the principal (*ravcha meshubad l'karna*), and that the principal and profit are interdependent (*karna mishtabed*). If you separate the *iska* into two independent halves, a loss in one half's principal can no longer be covered by the profit of the other. These are the structurally compelling arguments. The *mazla d'bei trei* argument, by contrast, is astrological in its plain meaning — it is not a psychological point about morale or complementary skills, but a claim about how the celestial alignment of two people working together produces better outcomes than either would achieve alone. One might imagine this as each partner's *mazla* being variable: when one is in a favorable period and the other is not, they even out. Whatever precisely it means, the literal sense is astrological, not managerial. At the end of the day, absent a prior stipulation permitting early exit, a partner cannot unilaterally withdraw from a joint *iska*.

## Field Leasing

### Weeding Obligation

The Mishna at 17:38 introduces a new topic: a person who leases a field from another — *hamakabel sadeh mechavero* — and refuses to weed it. Rashi notes that although the term *mekabel* ordinarily evokes *kablanut* — an arrangement where the lessee pays a share of the produce — the context here, and the later use of the word *chakirkah*, indicates that this is actually a *chakirut* arrangement, where a fixed payment is owed regardless of yield, say a hundred *se'ah*.

The lessee argues: what difference does it make to you whether I weed? I am paying your fixed rent — *mah ichpat lach, ho'il va'ani noten lach chakirkah*. The Mishna rules: *ein shom'in lo* — this argument is not accepted. The reason is that the owner can say: tomorrow you will leave this field and hand it back to me overgrown with weeds. You agreed to work the field, and failing to weed it depreciates the property.

The Gemara then works through a series of counteroffers by the lessee. If the lessee says: I will plow the field after the planting season to make up for not weeding now — the owner replies: *chitei ma'alyata ba'ina*, I want good wheat, and good wheat requires timely weeding. If the lessee says: I will buy you wheat from the market — *zivna lach chitei min shuka* — the owner replies: *chitei d'ara ba'ina*, I want the wheat that grows from my own field. If the lessee says: I will weed just enough of the field to cover the amount I owe you in produce — the owner replies with the image of *kamantzeva deshimra le'arai*: a partially tended field has a bad reputation, and you cannot leave part of my field improperly worked.

### Barza Denafal

The Mishna's underlying rationale — *mipnei shemefaltel lifnei ha'asavim*, that the lessee is leaving the field given over to weeds — might seem to encompass all of these counteroffers. But the Gemara clarifies that what really drives the ruling in each case is a principle captured in the phrase *barza denafal nafal*: once the tap has fallen from the barrel, it has fallen. The image is of a barrel of wine whose spigot has dislodged — what is lost cannot be recovered. If you fail to weed the field at the proper time and leave part of it unweeded, those weeds take root and will regrow the following year, which is precisely what the owner cares about. Even if you subsequently plow or do some partial *nikush*, the damage to future productivity is already done. It is not merely that the field looks bad now; it is that the failure to weed at the right moment causes permanent, compounding harm.

### Kdei Le'amid Kari

The Mishna continues: if a lessee's field fails to produce — *velo asatah* — and yet it produces *kedei le'amid kari*, enough to set up a stack of grain, then the lessee remains obligated to work the field: *chayav letapel bah*. Rabbi Yehuda in the Mishna disputes the threshold: rather than *kdei le'amid kari*, he holds that the operative standard is *kdei nefilah* — enough seed to replant the field the following year. If the field does not even produce enough to reseed itself, there is no obligation to continue working it.

## Measurement Standards

### Contract Language

A *baraita* is introduced that elaborates the lessee's contractual obligations. The standard contract language the lessee writes commits him to a full series of agricultural operations: *ana okim ve'agar ve'ezra ve'echtzor ve'amar ve'adush ve'adrei ve'okim kari kamach* — I will stand, plow, sow, cut, bind, thresh, winnow, and set up a stack before you. The lessee takes his half of the produce for his labor and expenses; the owner comes and takes his half. The *baraita* thus confirms that setting up the *kari* — the stack — is a specific contractual undertaking, and the question of how much grain constitutes a *kari* therefore has direct halachic consequence for whether the lessee has fulfilled his obligation.

### Pitchfork Test

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina defines *kedei le'amid kari* as: enough grain that you could stand a *rachat* — a pitchfork — upright in it. The pitchfork will remain standing when there is a sufficient mass of grain to support it. The Gemara then poses the question: what if the *rachat hayotzet mehai gisa lehai gisa* — what if the pitchfork is visible sticking out from both sides of the grain pile? Is that sufficient?

Rabbi Abahu resolves this by reporting that he heard the answer directly from Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina himself: *kol she'ein kones shelo ro'eh pnei hachamah* — the *kones*, the tined end of the pitchfork, must not be visible to the sky. The grain must fully cover the tined portion. Tosafot and Rabbeinu Chananel offer different readings of this criterion. Rabbeinu Chananel understands the textual variant *mehai gisa u-mehai gisa* as asking not whether any part of the pitchfork is visible, but which end of the pitchfork is in question: does the pitchfork stand handle-down, which requires relatively less grain to support it, or tines-down, which requires considerably more grain packed together to remain standing? Rabbeinu Chananel concludes that the *kones* — the wider, tined end, described as *tzad harachav she'yesh bo beit kibul* — is the relevant end, and accordingly the amount needed is that which covers the tined portion of the pitchfork entirely.

### Levi vs. Yannai

Having established what kind of stack qualifies, the Gemara reports a dispute over the minimum quantity in standard measures. Levi says three *se'ah*; the school of Rabbi Yannai — *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* — says two *se'ah*. Reish Lakish adds a gloss: the *sa'atayim* of which they speak is the net amount — *chutz min hahotza'ah* — after deducting the expenses the lessee incurred in working the field. Whether this net figure also excludes *terumot u'maasrot* is left open, though the inference is that tithes are not included in *hotza'ah* since they are a universal obligation affecting both parties equally.

## Taharot Passages

### Pritzei Zeitim

The Gemara now introduces a sequence of *tnan hatam* passages — rulings drawn from tractates in Seder Taharot, the Order of Purities. The Talmud Bavli has no independent *gemara* on the tractates of Taharot, with the exception of Masekhet Niddah, so these rulings surface here in a different context. Their presence requires explanation, which will be addressed in the following section.

The first: *paritzei zeitim v'ha'anavim, Beit Shammai metamin u-Veit Hillel metaharin* — bad olives and bad grapes. Beit Shammai hold that they are susceptible to *tumah*; Beit Hillel hold that they are not. What are *paritzei zeitim* — bad olives? Rav Huna explains: *risha'ei zeitim* — inferior olives that produce little oil.

The Gemara then asks: how do we know that *paritz* means something bad or inferior? Rav Yosef cites the verse: *u'vnei paritzei amcha yinassu le'ha'amid chazon venichshalu* — the sons of the *paritzim* of your people will try to establish the vision and will stumble. Their failure marks them as *resha'im*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak cites a different verse: *holid ben paritz shofech dam* — he fathers a son, a *paritz*, who is a spiller of blood — likewise a *rasha*. From both verses, *paritz* carries the connotation of wickedness and worthlessness, and by extension *paritzei zeitim* refers to olives that are bad in quality — they do not yield much oil.

How much oil qualifies an olive as *paritz*? Rabbi Elazar says: the olive press produces only four *kabbin* per *kur* of olives. The school of Rabbi Yannai says it produces two *se'ah* — approximately three times the amount. The Gemara resolves this as a non-dispute: *v'lo pligi*. In one locality, the press holds one *kur* of olives; in another, it holds three *kurim*. If you fill a press that holds three *kurim* and get only two *se'ah* of oil, that is the same ratio of poor yield as filling a press that holds one *kur* and getting only four *kabbin*. The two figures correspond to different regional practices for loading the olive press, not to a substantive disagreement about what constitutes a *paritz* olive.

### Weak Trees

The next *tnan hatam*: a pure person and an impure person — a *zav* and a *tahor* — ascend together onto a tree or onto branches whose strength is weak — *ilan she'kocho ra, socha she'kocha ra*. The ruling is *tamei*: the impurity transfers to the pure person. The mechanism, as Rashi explains, is that because the branches are weak, they bend under the combined weight of both individuals — *nichpefet tachteihen*. This bending creates a physical connection between the two, so that the weight of the one above presses upon the one below, allowing the *tumah* to transfer as though the *zav* were pressing directly upon the *tahor*.

What constitutes *ilan she'kocho ra* — a tree whose strength is weak? The school of Rabbi Yannai defines it: *kol she'ein b'ikaro lachok rova* — the root system is so small that there is not enough space within the roots to excavate a *rova*. A tree with such a shallow, narrow root system lacks structural strength, and that is what it means for a tree to be weak.

What constitutes *socha she'kocha ra* — weak upper branches? Reish Lakish defines it: *kol she'nechpeset b'chezyonah* — any branch that you can wrap your hand around and fully cover. A branch so slender that a person's hand encompasses it entirely is one that bends under weight and thus enables the transfer of *tumah*. A large, solid branch, by contrast, physically separates the two individuals on the tree, preventing the contact-based transfer of impurity.

### Beit Hapras

The third *tnan hatam*: one who walks through a *beit hapras* — a field in which a grave has been plowed — upon stones that can be dislodged — *al gabei avanim she'yachol le'hasit*. A *beit hapras* is a field where concern exists that fragments of bone the size of a barley grain — *etzem k'se'orah* — may be scattered throughout. An *etzem k'se'orah* conveys the highest level of *tumat meit* through contact and carrying, though not through *ohel* (overhang). If the stones in such a field shift when one steps on them, and an *etzem k'se'orah* is thereby carried by the force of one's step — *nisa mikocho* — the walker has become impure.

The more difficult case: what if a person is riding atop another person or an animal whose strength is weak — *she'kochan ra* — through a *beit hapras*? The *tumah* transfers to the rider through the weakness of the carrier.

What constitutes a person whose strength is weak in this context? Reish Lakish: *kol she'rchuvav be'arkuvotav nokshot* — one whose knees knock together under the weight of the rider. What constitutes a weak animal? The school of Rabbi Yannai: *kol she'ochma matila gelalim* — an animal so burdened that it excretes droppings while being ridden. That is the sign of an animal whose strength is insufficient, and it is through that weakness that the *tumah* of the *beit hapras* transfers to the rider.

### Prayer/Tefillin

The school of Rabbi Yannai makes one final pronouncement: *litfillah u-litfillin arba'ah kabin* — with respect to prayer and the wearing of *tefillin*, the operative threshold is four *kabbin*. The Gemara unpacks each half of this ruling separately.

With respect to prayer: a *baraita* teaches that if a person is carrying a load on his shoulder and the time for prayer arrives, if the load is less than four *kabbin*, he may shift it to his back and pray in that posture. If it is four *kabbin* or more, he must set it down on the ground before praying.

With respect to *tefillin*: a *baraita* teaches that if a person is carrying a load on his head while wearing *tefillin*, the question is whether the load is pressing on and misshaping the *tefillin*. If it does — *rotzetzot* — it is forbidden. If it does not, it is permitted. The threshold of four *kabbin* is the weight of load under discussion in these rulings.

A further *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Chiyya addresses the case of one who carries *zevel* — refuse or manure — on his head while wearing *tefillin*. He may neither move the *tefillin* to the side of his head, out of the proper position but away from the refuse, nor tie them elsewhere on his body. Both of these are *minhag bizayon* — improper treatment of *tefillin*, since they are placed outside their designated location. Rather, he should move his head *tefillin* to his arm, to the place designated for the arm *tefillin*: that is not considered disrespectful, since it is a proper location for *tefillin*.

A dissenting tradition from the school of Rabbi Sheila holds a stricter position: even the wrapping cloth — *mitpachas* — of the *tefillin* may not be placed on the head on which *tefillin* are being worn. And how small a load triggers this restriction? Abaye says: even a sixteenth of a *kab* or *log* from Pumbedita — an extremely small amount, as Rashi notes.

## Taharot Integration

### Strength Motif

Having worked through the entire sequence of *tnan hatam* passages, it is worth pausing to ask why they appear here at all. Rashi explains that all of them are gathered here because each one is accompanied by an explanatory tradition from *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* — the school of Rabbi Yannai provides the definition or measurement in each case. That is a sufficient reason for the compiler of the *sugya* to bring them together: the thread connecting them is that each mishna arrives with a Yannai-school explanation attached.

There is, however, a more satisfying account of what draws these passages into this context, and it is the one that arises from a careful look at the recurring subject matter. Each of these passages turns on a threshold of strength: the bad olives that are too weak to produce adequate oil; the tree or branch whose structural strength is insufficient to prevent the bending that enables *tumah* transfer; the animal whose strength is so diminished that it betrays itself under load; the person whose knees buckle; the load that is too heavy to carry while praying or wearing *tefillin*. The motif running through all of them is the borderline between adequate and insufficient strength — what is strong enough and what falls short. This is precisely the question that governs the preceding agricultural discussion: how much must a field produce for the lessee to remain obligated? What is the minimum strength, so to speak, of the field's output? The literary structure of the Bavli here follows this thematic coherence, not merely the coincidence of Yannai-school explanations.

David Rozenthal reads this sequence as evidence of something more: he sees in these passages traces of what might be called an incipient *Talmud* on the tractates of Seder Taharot — more formally edited discussions that circulated around those *mishnayot* and found their way into the Bavli when the literary editors (*mesadrei ha-Gemara*) placed them alongside the *mishnayot* they illuminated, sometimes bringing along material that is not strictly relevant to the Bavli's own tractate. On this view, the sequence of *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* passages entered the Bavli already as a set, because those Yannai-school traditions had been transmitted together.

An alternative view is more conservative: perhaps there was no separate Taharot *Talmud* in any formal sense, but simply that the *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* traditions on these *mishnayot* were known and transmitted as a group. When the occasion arose — here, because we were already discussing *d'vei Rabbi Yannai*'s measurement in the context of *kablanut* — they were brought in together. The thematic motif of strength thresholds is the stronger literary explanation for why they appear here; the *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* connection is the more historical one.

## Kdei Nefilah

### Minimum Produce

Returning now to the Mishna's dispute: the *tanna kamma* requires *kedei le'amid kari* — enough to set up a stack — for the lessee to be obligated to continue working the field, a threshold the Gemara established as either two or three *se'ah*. Rabbi Yehuda sets the threshold instead at *kedei nefilah* — the amount of seed needed to replant the field the following year. Below that level, the field's output is so negligible that the lessee is released from his work obligation.

### Land Quality Dispute

The Gemara asks: how much, precisely, is *kedei nefilah*? Rabbi Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — though the text as emended by the *Hagahot ha-Bach* reads *arba'ah kabbin le-khor* — says four *kabbin* per *kur* of field. Rabbi Ami then offers his own independent view: eight *se'ah* per *kur*, twice the amount attributed to Rabbi Yochanan. An elder explains the discrepancy to Rav Chama son of Rabbah bar Avuha: in the days of Rabbi Yochanan the land was fertile — *shemena ar'a*; in the days of Rabbi Ami the land had become exhausted — *kachsha ar'a*. Weaker land required proportionally more seed to achieve the same result, and so Rabbi Ami's figure reflects the agricultural conditions of his own time.

### Scattered Sheaves

A second *tnan hatam* is now introduced, drawn this time from Seder Zraim — specifically from Masekhet Peah. *Ha'ruach shepizra et ha'omrin* — if the wind scatters someone's sheaves, the Gemara rules that we estimate how much *leket* the field would have produced and that amount is given to the poor. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: we do not estimate, but rather give the poor *kedei nefilah* — the amount sufficient to reseed the field. The Gemara then asks of this second source: how much is *kedei nefilah*?

This passage is brought here because *kedei nefilah* requires definition in both contexts — the *kablanut* context of the preceding Mishna and the *peah*-*leket* context of Seder Zraim. The Bavli, which produced no *gemara* on Seder Zraim any more than on Seder Taharot, handles this by bringing the relevant *mishnayot* together at the point where the shared term requires elucidation in both directions.

### Four Kabin

Rav Dimi, reporting in the name of either Rabbi Elazar or Rabbi Yochanan, gives the answer: *arba'ah kabbin le-khor* — four *kabbin* for a *kur* of field. This is consistent with the figure attributed to Rabbi Yochanan earlier, and it is precisely this consistency that led the Vilna Gaon to emend the earlier text. Where the earlier discussion had *arba'ah se'im le-khor*, the Gra corrected it to *arba'ah kabbin*, since a *se'ah* is three times a *kab*, and the discrepancy between the two formulations was otherwise irreconcilable. Whether this emendation is correct remains a matter of the precise volumetric relationship between these measures.

### Hand vs. Animal

Rabbi Yirmeyah poses two questions about the meaning of *arba'ah kabbin le-khor zera*. The first: is this a *kur* of *zera* — a field large enough to require a *kur* of seed — or a *kur* of *tevuah* — a field that produces a *kur* of grain? These are very different field sizes. A field that requires a *kur* of seed to sow is substantially larger than a field that yields a *kur* of grain as its output. Rashi explains: is it a large field, defined by how much seed it needs, or a small field, defined by how much it produces?

The Gemara attempts to answer this question from the same *tnan hatam* source: *arba'ah kabbin le-khor zera* — the explicit phrase *kur zera* resolves the first question in favor of the larger field, the one requiring a *kur* of seed.

But a second question immediately follows: when we speak of seeding a *kur* of field, do we mean seeding by hand — *mapolet yad* — or by animal-drawn seeding — *mapolet shevarim*? This is not a trivial distinction. Hand seeding allows the farmer to apply seed with discrimination — more here, less there — and tends to use somewhat less seed overall. Animal-drawn seeding is automatic and uniform; the mechanism disperses seed at a constant rate regardless of local conditions, and typically uses more seed per unit area. The two methods would yield a meaningfully different quantity for *arba'ah kabbin le-khor*. The Gemara leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.
