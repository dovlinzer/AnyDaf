# Bava Metzia 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Iska Structure

### Iska Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: הַאי עִיסְקָא פַּלְגָא מִלְוָה וּפַלְגָא פִּקָּדוֹן, עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מִילְּתָא דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ לְלֹוֶה וְנִיחָא לֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** A common type of business venture was one where the capital or merchandise was supplied by one person and managed by another, who would receive a share, commonly half of the profits, for his efforts. This arrangement can also be viewed as one where the investor is lending half of the invested capital or merchandise to the manager, with the manager agreeing to supervise the venture in exchange for receiving the loan. In order to avoid violating the prohibition of interest, the investor agrees to accept a greater share of the possible loss, e.g., two-thirds, than of the profits, e.g., one-half. The Sages **of Neharde'a said:** With regard to its halakhic status, **this joint venture** is considered **a half-loan and half-deposit,** as the **Sages formulated** an **enactment that** would be **satisfactory for the borrower,** i.e., the manager, **and** equally **satisfactory for the lender,** i.e., the investor.

The *iska* is a mechanism by which one party provides capital to another in a form that generates a return without violating the prohibition against interest. Its structure is carefully designed so that the arrangement does not even appear to be a loan at interest. The capital transferred is treated as two halves: one half is a *pikadon*, held by the working party as a kind of guardianship — it remains, in a legal sense, the lender's property — while the other half constitutes an outright loan, which the worker treats as his own to invest.

The worker goes out and trades with the combined sum. If he incurs a loss, the losses are split evenly between the two parties, reflecting equal partnership in the downside. On the profit side, however, the split is asymmetric: the worker retains two-thirds of any profit and the lender receives only one-third. This asymmetry is essential. Were profits split equally, the arrangement would be *mechzei k'ribbit* — it would look like a loan at interest, with the lender receiving a guaranteed proportional return. By giving the worker a larger share of the profit, the arrangement falls below the threshold of what constitutes the appearance of interest. This structure is a *takanah shel chachamim*: the Sages instituted this form precisely so that capital could be deployed productively without transgressing the prohibition on *ribbit*.

### One/Two Iskot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: חֲדָא עִיסְקָא וּתְרֵי שְׁטָרֵי – פְּסֵידָא דְמַלְוֶה.
> תְּרֵי עִיסְקֵי וְחַד שְׁטָרָא – פְּסֵידָא דְּלֹוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If two people conducted **one joint venture and** drafted it as separate ventures of equal value in **two** separate **documents,** and they suffered a heavy loss from the venture recorded in one of the documents and had a slight gain from the other, the *halakha* is as follows: The two documents are treated as two separate agreements, and one does not calculate the profits and losses from the two ventures together. Therefore, this will be to **the detriment of the lender.** According to the common arrangement, he will gain half of the profits from one venture and suffer two-thirds of the loss of the other. Conversely, if two people engaged in **two joint ventures and** recorded both in **one document,** this will be to **the detriment of the borrower.** They calculate the profits and losses of the two transactions together, and therefore as long as the profits of one joint venture are greater than the losses of the other, the investor will not have to suffer a loss.

Rava, at the bottom of 104b, addresses the interplay between the number of ventures and the number of documents. His ruling — *chada iska u-trei shtarei psida d'malveh, trei iskei v-chad shtara psida d'loveh* — is terse, and Rashi expands it at considerable length.

Take the case of *chada iska u-trei shtarei*: a single bundle of goods, say a consignment of textiles (Rashi uses the example of *chabilot shel bgadim*), is split across two separate contracts. One portion is destined for Nehardea, the other for Pumbedita, and each is governed by its own *shtar*. Because they are two separate documents, the two halves are treated entirely independently. Suppose the Nehardea half loses five units and the Pumbedita half gains fifteen. The loss of five is split evenly: the lender bears minus two and a half and the worker bears minus two and a half. The gain of fifteen is split one-third to the lender — plus five — and two-thirds to the worker — plus ten. The lender's net is minus two and a half plus five, yielding a net gain of only two and a half. This is *psida d'malveh*: by treating the two halves separately, the lender comes out worse than he would if they were consolidated.

Now take *trei iskei v-chad shtara*: two separate bundles of goods, but governed by a single contract. If one bundle loses five and the other gains fifteen, they are pooled under the single *shtar*. The net result is a gain of ten, of which one-third — approximately three and a third — goes to the lender. That is *psida d'loveh*: when everything is aggregated, the lender does better and the worker does relatively worse.

Rava's point is simply that the choice of how to structure the documentation has real financial consequences for both parties, and which structure is adopted determines who bears the greater burden when the venture's components produce mixed results.

### Effort & Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּקַבֵּל עִיסְקָא מִן חַבְרֵיהּ וּפְסֵיד, טְרַח וּמַלְּיֵיהּ וְלָא אוֹדְעֵיהּ, לָא מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּרִי מֵהַיְאךְ פְּסֵידָא בַּהֲדַאי. מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְהָכִי טְרַחְתְּ וּמַלֵּיתֵיהּ – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיקְרוֹ לָךְ "מַפְסֵיד עִיסְקֵי".
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: This one, who receives** merchandise for **a joint venture from another, and lost** money in the process, and then **made the effort** to **replace** the loss **but did not inform** the investor that he had done so, he **may not** later **say to** the investor: **Bear this** original **loss together with me.** This is **because** the investor can **say to him:** It is **for this** reason **that you made the effort to replace** the loss, **so that you should not be called a loser of ventures.** You wanted to preserve your reputation in order to improve your future business prospects but did not intend to be reimbursed.

Rava adds a further ruling at the top of 105a. If a person accepts an *iska* from a colleague and loses money on it — but then, without informing the lender, exerts himself further and recoups the loss — he cannot later turn to the lender and demand that the lender share retroactively in the earlier losses. The lender's response is decisive: you went back to work and recovered the principal not on my behalf, but so that you would not be called someone who fails at business ventures — *mafsidei iska*. The fact that you replenished the principal on your own effort means that we simply return to the original terms of the agreement. The earlier loss, at the moment it occurred, was a real liability, but because the worker chose to remediate it unilaterally and without disclosure, he cannot now reconstruct a mid-venture loss position and demand the lender share it.

## Partnership Exit

### Cannot Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי בֵּי תְרֵי דְּעָבְדִי עִיסְקָא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי וּרְוַוח, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ חַד לְחַבְרֵיהּ: תָּא לִיפְלוֹג, אִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִידַּךְ: נַרְוַוח טְפֵי – דִּינָא הוּא דִּמְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said:** With regard to **these two** managers **who engaged** in **a joint venture together,** i.e., they both received merchandise together from an investor, **and profited** from it, **and one** of them **said to the other: Come, let** us **divide** the profits and terminate the venture, the *halakha* is as follows: **If the other said to him: Let** us wait and **profit more, the *halakha* is that** the second manager indeed **prevents** the first from executing his request.

Rava next addresses the case of two partners who jointly receive an *iska* from a third party. They invest together, the venture turns profitable, and one partner wishes to end his participation — *lo pelog*. If the other partner responds that he wishes to continue and earn more profit, Rava rules that the dissenting partner has the legal power to prevent a split: *dino hu de-me'akev*. One partner cannot unilaterally exit the joint arrangement simply because a profit has been realized at a given moment.

### Profit Lien

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי פַּלְגָא דְּרַוְוחָא – אָמַר לֵיהּ: רַוְוחָא – לְקַרְנָא מִשְׁתַּעְבֵּד.
> וְאִי אָמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי פַּלְגָא רַוְוחָא וּפַלְגָא קַרְנָא – אָמַר לֵיהּ: עִיסְקָא לַהֲדָדֵי מְשׁוּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if,** instead of requesting the final division of the profits and the termination of the venture, one **said to** the other: At least **give me half the profits,** the latter can **say to him: The profit** is **liened to the principal,** meaning that the profits and the principal are considered a single unit, and we can earn much more if we do not set aside the profits. Rava continues: **And if** one **says to** the other: **Give me half the profits and half the principal,** the latter can **say to him: The** merchandise for **the joint venture** is **liened to both** of us. As we are equal partners in this venture, you cannot force me to divide it.

The Gemara then works through the arguments serially. If the partner seeking to exit says: give me my half of the profit — *hav li plaga de-ravcha* — the retort is *ravcha le-karna mishtabed*: the profit is liened to the principal. The profit must remain available to cover any future loss in the principal. Because the *iska* continues, the profit cannot be extracted prematurely.

If he then says: fine, give me my half of the profit and my half of the principal, and we will each go our separate ways — the response is *iska de-hadada mishtabed*: the two halves of the *iska* are mutually obligated. The profit of one half stands ready to cover a loss in the principal of the other half. If they were to split the *iska* into two independent halves, this cross-coverage would be lost.

### Mazla D'bei Trei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמַר לֵיהּ: נִפְלוֹג רַוְוחָא וְנִפְלוֹג קַרְנָא, וְאִי מָטֵי לָךְ פְּסֵידָא דָּרֵינָא בַּהֲדָךְ – אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מַזָּלָא דְּבֵי תְרֵי עָדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **And if** one **says to** the other: **Let us divide the profits and divide the principal, and if you suffer a loss** as a result, **I will bear** the loss **with you,** his partner can **say to him: No,** I do not desire to do that, since **the luck of two** people is **better.** Consequently, I want to continue working together. In all these cases, the claims of the second manager are accepted.

Suppose the partner seeking to exit concedes all of this — he accepts that profit is liened to principal and that principal and profit are interdependent — but says: I understand, I will remain responsible for your losses from my profit, and your profit will cover my losses; I simply do not want to be actively involved in the venture anymore. The final argument deployed against him is *mazla d'bei trei adif*: the fortune of a partnership of two is superior to that of either individual working alone. Working together, their combined *mazla* is better than if each were to proceed independently.

Tosafot note that *mazla d'bei trei adif* is in a sense always a winning argument — it could justify preventing any exit under any circumstances. That is precisely why the stronger arguments are presented first: that the profit is liened to the principal (*ravcha meshubad l'karna*), and that the principal and profit are interdependent (*karna mishtabed*). If you separate the *iska* into two independent halves, a loss in one half's principal can no longer be covered by the profit of the other. These are the structurally compelling arguments. The *mazla d'bei trei* argument, by contrast, is astrological in its plain meaning — it is not a psychological point about morale or complementary skills, but a claim about how the celestial alignment of two people working together produces better outcomes than either would achieve alone. One might imagine this as each partner's *mazla* being variable: when one is in a favorable period and the other is not, they even out. Whatever precisely it means, the literal sense is astrological, not managerial. At the end of the day, absent a prior stipulation permitting early exit, a partner cannot unilaterally withdraw from a joint *iska*.

## Field Leasing

### Weeding Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְלֹא רָצָה לְנַכֵּשׁ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: מָה אִיכְפַּת לְךָ? הוֹאִיל וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ אֶת חֲכִירָךְ – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: לְמָחָר אַתָּה יוֹצֵא מִמֶּנָּה וּמַעֲלֵת לְפָנַי עֲשָׂבִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who received a field from another** to cultivate **and did not want to weed** it, **and** he then **said to** the owner: **What do you care** if I neglect the land? You will not suffer a loss **since I** will **give you** the amount of produce I owe you for **your** granting me **tenancy,** regardless of the state of the field. Nevertheless, **they do not listen to him.** The reason is **because** the owner of the land **can say to him: Tomorrow you** will **depart from** the field, **and it** will **grow weeds for me,** which will remain there and disrupt the yield of the field for years to come.

The Mishna at 17:38 introduces a new topic: a person who leases a field from another — *hamakabel sadeh mechavero* — and refuses to weed it. Rashi notes that although the term *mekabel* ordinarily evokes *kablanut* — an arrangement where the lessee pays a share of the produce — the context here, and the later use of the word *chakirkah*, indicates that this is actually a *chakirut* arrangement, where a fixed payment is owed regardless of yield, say a hundred *se'ah*.

The lessee argues: what difference does it make to you whether I weed? I am paying your fixed rent — *mah ichpat lach, ho'il va'ani noten lach chakirkah*. The Mishna rules: *ein shom'in lo* — this argument is not accepted. The reason is that the owner can say: tomorrow you will leave this field and hand it back to me overgrown with weeds. You agreed to work the field, and failing to weed it depreciates the property.

### Barza Denafal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִי אָמַר לֵיהּ: לְבָתַר הָכִי כָּרֵיבְנָא לַהּ – אָמַר לֵיהּ: חִטֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא בָּעֵינָא. וְאִי אָמַר לֵיהּ: זָבֵינְנָא לָךְ חִטֵּי מִשּׁוּקָא – אָמַר לֵיהּ: חִטֵּי דְּאַרְעַאי בָּעֵינָא. וְאִי אָמַר לֵיהּ מְנַכֵּישְׁנָא לָךְ שִׁיעוּר מְנָתָיךְ – אָמַר לֵיהּ: קָא מַנְסְבַתְּ שֵׁם רַע לְאַרְעַאי.
> וְהָתְנַן: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲלֵת לְפָנַי עֲשָׂבִים! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: בִּזְרָא דִּנְפַל – נְפַל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **If** the cultivator **said to** the owner: **Afterward,** when I have reaped the field, **I will plow** it and remove the weeds, the owner can **say to him: I want superior wheat,** not wheat that sprouted among weeds. **And if he says to** the owner: **I will buy** good **wheat for you from the market,** the owner can **say to him: I want wheat from my land. And if he says to** the owner: **I will weed for you** according to the **measure of your portion,** but no more, the owner can **say to him: You are giving a bad name to my land,** as everyone will see that it is full of weeds. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that the reason they do not listen to him is: **Because it will grow weeds for me,** indicating that these other claims are not accepted? **Rather,** the explanation must be **because** the owner can **say to him: The seed that fell has fallen.** In other words, even if the cultivator later plows the land and uproots all of the weeds, their seeds remain in the ground and will sprout in the following years.

The Gemara then works through a series of counteroffers by the lessee. If the lessee says: I will plow the field after the planting season to make up for not weeding now — the owner replies: *chitei ma'alyata ba'ina*, I want good wheat, and good wheat requires timely weeding. If the lessee says: I will buy you wheat from the market — *zivna lach chitei min shuka* — the owner replies: *chitei d'ara ba'ina*, I want the wheat that grows from my own field. If the lessee says: I will weed just enough of the field to cover the amount I owe you in produce — the owner replies with the image of *kamantzeva deshimra le'arai*: a partially tended field has a bad reputation, and you cannot leave part of my field improperly worked.

The Mishna's underlying rationale — *mipnei shemefaltel lifnei ha'asavim*, that the lessee is leaving the field given over to weeds — might seem to encompass all of these counteroffers. But the Gemara clarifies that what really drives the ruling in each case is a principle captured in the phrase *barza denafal nafal*: once the tap has fallen from the barrel, it has fallen. The image is of a barrel of wine whose spigot has dislodged — what is lost cannot be recovered. If you fail to weed the field at the proper time and leave part of it unweeded, those weeds take root and will regrow the following year, which is precisely what the owner cares about. Even if you subsequently plow or do some partial *nikush*, the damage to future productivity is already done. It is not merely that the field looks bad now; it is that the failure to weed at the right moment causes permanent, compounding harm.

### Kdei Le'amid Kari

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְלֹא עָשְׂתָה, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהַעֲמִיד כְּרִי – חַיָּיב לִטַּפֵּל בָּהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַאי קִצְבָה בִּכְרִי? אֶלָּא אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ כְּדֵי נְפִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who receives a field from another** to cultivate **and it did not produce** a sufficient crop to cover the expenses of its upkeep, **if it has** enough produce **to form a pile** he is **obligated to take care of it** and give the owner his share. **Rabbi Yehuda says: What fixed** measure is **a pile?** There is no inherent measure of produce that is considered significant, as it all depends on the size of the plot of land in question. **Rather,** the relevant issue is **whether it has** a crop **equivalent to** the measure of seeds for **dropping** in a field in order to sow it.

The Mishna continues: if a lessee's field fails to produce — *velo asatah* — and yet it produces *kedei le'amid kari*, enough to set up a stack of grain, then the lessee remains obligated to work the field: *chayav letapel bah*. Rabbi Yehuda in the Mishna disputes the threshold: rather than *kdei le'amid kari*, he holds that the operative standard is *kdei nefilah* — enough seed to replant the field the following year. If the field does not even produce enough to reseed itself, there is no obligation to continue working it.

## Measurement Standards

### Contract Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְלֹא עָשְׂתָה, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְהַעֲמִיד כְּרִי – חַיָּיב לִטַּפֵּל בָּהּ. שֶׁכָּךְ כּוֹתֵב לוֹ: אֲנָא אֵוקֹים וְאֵנוּר וְאֶזְרַע וְאֶחְצוֹד וַאֲעַמַּר וְאֵדוּשׁ וְאֶידְרֵי וְאוֹקֵים כַּרְיָא קׇדָמָךְ, וְתֵיתֵי אַנְתְּ וְתִיטּוֹל פַּלְגָא, וַאֲנָא בְּעַמְלִי וּבְנַפְקוּת יְדַי פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who receives a field from another** to cultivate **and it did not produce** a sufficient crop, **if it has** enough produce **to form a pile** he is **obligated to take care of it** and provide the owner with his share. This is **because this** is what **he writes to him** in the cultivator's contract: **I will stand and plow and plant and reap and bind and thresh and winnow and establish a pile before you, and you will come and take half, and I, for my work and expenses,** will take the other **half.** Based on this contract, if there is sufficient produce to form a pile, the cultivator must fulfill the terms of the agreement.

A *baraita* is introduced that elaborates the lessee's contractual obligations. The standard contract language the lessee writes commits him to a full series of agricultural operations: *ana okim ve'agar ve'ezra ve'echtzor ve'amar ve'adush ve'adrei ve'okim kari kamach* — I will stand, plow, sow, cut, bind, thresh, winnow, and set up a stack before you. The lessee takes his half of the produce for his labor and expenses; the owner comes and takes his half. The *baraita* thus confirms that setting up the *kari* — the stack — is a specific contractual undertaking, and the question of how much grain constitutes a *kari* therefore has direct halachic consequence for whether the lessee has fulfilled his obligation.

### Pitchfork Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי לְהַעֲמִיד בָּהּ כְּרִי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּדֵי שֶׁתַּעֲמוֹד בּוֹ הָרַחַת. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַחַת הַיּוֹצֵא מֵהַאי גִּיסָא לְהַאי גִּיסָא, מַאי?
> תָּא שְׁמַע: אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין כּוֹנֵס שֶׁלּוֹ רוֹאֶה פְּנֵי הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is the amount of: Enough **to form a pile?** How large must the pile be? **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** Enough **for the winnowing shovel to stand in it.** If the pile is big enough that the shovel can be placed there and stand independently without falling, it is considered a sufficiently large pile. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **a winnowing shovel that protrudes from this side to that side,** i.e., whose edges extend beyond the pile, **what** is the *halakha*? Is this considered a pile in which a winnowing shovel can stand or not? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that which **Rabbi Abbahu said:** This **was explained to me by Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina: Any** pile in **which the blade of** the winnowing shovel **cannot see the face of the sun** because it is covered by the pile is considered a significant one.

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina defines *kedei le'amid kari* as: enough grain that you could stand a *rachat* — a pitchfork — upright in it. The pitchfork will remain standing when there is a sufficient mass of grain to support it. The Gemara then poses the question: what if the *rachat hayotzet mehai gisa lehai gisa* — what if the pitchfork is visible sticking out from both sides of the grain pile? Is that sufficient?

Rabbi Abahu resolves this by reporting that he heard the answer directly from Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina himself: *kol she'ein kones shelo ro'eh pnei hachamah* — the *kones*, the tined end of the pitchfork, must not be visible to the sky. The grain must fully cover the tined portion. Tosafot and Rabbeinu Chananel offer different readings of this criterion. Rabbeinu Chananel understands the textual variant *mehai gisa u-mehai gisa* as asking not whether any part of the pitchfork is visible, but which end of the pitchfork is in question: does the pitchfork stand handle-down, which requires relatively less grain to support it, or tines-down, which requires considerably more grain packed together to remain standing? Rabbeinu Chananel concludes that the *kones* — the wider, tined end, described as *tzad harachav she'yesh bo beit kibul* — is the relevant end, and accordingly the amount needed is that which covers the tined portion of the pitchfork entirely.

### Levi vs. Yannai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: לֵוִי אָמַר שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמְרִי סָאתַיִם. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: סָאתַיִם שֶׁאָמְרוּ חוּץ מִן הַהוֹצָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that the *amora'im* engaged in a dispute concerning this issue: **Levi says:** This pile must be **three *se'a*** in size, while the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say: Two *se'a*. Reish Lakish says: The two *se'a*** of **which they spoke** is **without** deducting **the expenses.** Consequently, if he has paid the expenses and a profit of two *se'a* remains, in that case alone it is considered worthwhile to work the field. But if it cannot produce this amount, the cultivator may neglect the land if he so chooses.

Having established what kind of stack qualifies, the Gemara reports a dispute over the minimum quantity in standard measures. Levi says three *se'ah*; the school of Rabbi Yannai — *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* — says two *se'ah*. Reish Lakish adds a gloss: the *sa'atayim* of which they speak is the net amount — *chutz min hahotza'ah* — after deducting the expenses the lessee incurred in working the field. Whether this net figure also excludes *terumot u'maasrot* is left open, though the inference is that tithes are not included in *hotza'ah* since they are a universal obligation affecting both parties equally.

## Taharot Passages

### Pritzei Zeitim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: פָּרִיצֵי זֵיתִים וַעֲנָבִים, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מְטַמְּאִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מְטַהֲרִין.
> מַאי פָּרִיצֵי זֵיתִים? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רִשְׁעֵי זֵיתִים. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: וּמַאי קְרָאָה – "וּבְנֵי פָּרִיצֵי עַמְּךָ יִנַּשְּׂאוּ לְהַעֲמִיד חֲזוֹן וְנִכְשָׁלוּ". רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מֵהָכָא – "וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן פָּרִץ שֹׁפֵךְ דָּם".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a dispute from a different area of *halakha* that discusses a similar measurement: **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Okatzin* 3:6) concerning the *halakhot* of food impurities: With regard to **unruly olives and grapes, Beit Shammai** hold that they become **susceptible to ritual impurity,** as they are considered food, **and Beit Hillel** hold that they do **not** become **susceptible to ritual impurity** because they are of inferior quality and are unfit for consumption. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **unruly [*peritzei*] olives? Rav Huna said: Wicked olives,** i.e., olives that barely produce any oil. **Rav Yosef said: And what is the verse** from which it is derived? **"Also the children of the wicked [*peritzei*] among your people shall raise themselves up to establish the vision but they shall stumble"** (Daniel 11:14). This verse indicates that the word *peritzei* means wicked. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that the meaning of this word can be derived **from here: "If he beget a son that is a robber [*paritz*], a shedder of blood"** (Ezekiel 18:10).

The Gemara now introduces a sequence of *tnan hatam* passages — rulings drawn from tractates in Seder Taharot, the Order of Purities. The Talmud Bavli has no independent *gemara* on the tractates of Taharot, with the exception of Masekhet Niddah, so these rulings surface here in a different context. Their presence requires explanation, which will be addressed in the following section.

The first: *paritzei zeitim v'ha'anavim, Beit Shammai metamin u-Veit Hillel metaharin* — bad olives and bad grapes. Beit Shammai hold that they are susceptible to *tumah*; Beit Hillel hold that they are not. What are *paritzei zeitim* — bad olives? Rav Huna explains: *risha'ei zeitim* — inferior olives that produce little oil.

The Gemara then asks: how do we know that *paritz* means something bad or inferior? Rav Yosef cites the verse: *u'vnei paritzei amcha yinassu le'ha'amid chazon venichshalu* — the sons of the *paritzim* of your people will try to establish the vision and will stumble. Their failure marks them as *resha'im*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak cites a different verse: *holid ben paritz shofech dam* — he fathers a son, a *paritz*, who is a spiller of blood — likewise a *rasha*. From both verses, *paritz* carries the connotation of wickedness and worthlessness, and by extension *paritzei zeitim* refers to olives that are bad in quality — they do not yield much oil.

### Weak Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה פָּרִיצֵי זֵיתִים? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּין לַקּוֹרָה. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמְרִי: סָאתַיִם לַקּוֹרָה.
> וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְעַיְּילִי כּוֹרָא בְּאוֹלָלָא, הָא בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְעַיְּילִי תְּלָתָא כּוֹרִין בְּאוֹלָלָא.
> תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עָלוּ בְּאִילָן שֶׁכּוֹחוֹ רַע, וּבְסוֹכָה שֶׁכּוֹחָהּ רַע – טָמֵא.
> הֵיכִי דָּמֵי אִילָן שֶׁכּוֹחוֹ רַע? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בְּעִיקָּרוֹ לָחוֹק רוֹבַע. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי סוֹכָה שֶׁכּוֹחָהּ רַע? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל שֶׁנֶּחְבֵּאת בְּחֶזְיוֹנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is the amount of **unruly olives?** When are olives classified as unruly? **Rabbi Elazar says:** They are classified as such if it is possible to extract only **four *kav*** of oil **from** one press of the **beam** when the fruits are brought in together to the olive press. **The** Sages of the **school of Rabbi Yannai say:** They are classified as such if it is possible to extract only **two *se'a*** of oil **from** one press of the **beam.** The Gemara comments: **And** these Sages **do not disagree** with regard to the *halakha* itself, as the difference between their rulings stems from divergent local practices. **This** statement of Rabbi Elazar is referring **to a place where** one ***kor*** is **brought into the press,** from which he must be able to extract four *kav*, whereas **that** *halakha* of the school of Rabbi Yannai is referring **to a place where three *kor*** are **brought into the baskets of the** oil **press.** Since they bring in three times the amount of fruit, it must produce exactly three times as much oil. **The Sages taught:** If a *zav* and a ritually pure person **climbed a tree that has little strength,** which shook as they climbed it, **or** if they climbed onto **a branch that has little strength,** the ritually pure person is **rendered ritually impure.** One of the ways a *zav* imparts impurity is by movement, and here the *zav* is viewed as having moved the pure person. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this **tree that has little strength,** i.e., how is a tree with little strength defined? The Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai say:** It is **any** tree **whose trunk is not** broad enough **that** one can **hollow out** a vessel of **a quarter**-*kav* from it. **What are the circumstances** of **a branch that has little strength? Reish Lakish said:** It is **any** branch concerning **which** its circumference can **be hidden,** i.e., inserted, **in** a person's **fist.** A branch of this size is generally not strong enough to hold two people without shaking.

### Beit Hapras

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַמְהַלֵּךְ בְּבֵית הַפְּרָס, עַל גַּבֵּי אֲבָנִים שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהָסִיט, עַל הָאָדָם וְעַל הַבְּהֵמָה שֶׁכּוֹחָן רַע – טָמֵא.
> הֵיכִי דָּמֵי אָדָם שֶׁכּוֹחוֹ רַע? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל שֶׁרוֹכְבוֹ וְאַרְכֻבּוֹתָיו נוֹקְשׁוֹת. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁכּוֹחָהּ רַע? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כֹּל שֶׁרוֹכְבָהּ וּמְטִילָה גְּלָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Oholot* 18:6): With regard to one **who walks in an area in which uncertainty exists concerning the location of a grave or corpse [*beit haperas*],** if he treads **over stones that he can move** as he walks, raising concerns that he might have moved a bone of a corpse and thereby rendered himself impure, or if he was in that location, **on** the back of **a person or** riding **on an animal that had little strength,** he is **impure,** as he is considered to have moved the impurity himself. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **a person that has little strength? Reish Lakish said: Any** person **whose knees knock** against each other **when** someone **rides upon him. What are the circumstances** of **an animal that has little strength?** The Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai say: Any** animal **that releases excrement** due to strain **when** a person **rides upon it.**

### Prayer/Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לִתְפִלָּה וְלִתְפִילִּין אַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין.
> לִתְפִלָּה מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹשֵׂא מַשּׂאוֹי עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ זְמַן תְּפִלָּה, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין – מַפְשִׁילָן לַאֲחוֹרָיו וּמִתְפַּלֵּל. אַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין – מַנִּיחַ עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל.
> לִתְפִילִּין מַאי – הִיא דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה נוֹשֵׂא מַשּׂאוֹי עַל רֹאשׁוֹ וּתְפִילִּין בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, אִם הָיוּ תְּפִילִּין רוֹצְצוֹת – אָסוּר, וְאִם לָאו – מוּתָּר. בְּאֵיזוֹ מַשּׂאוֹי אָמְרוּ – בְּמַשּׂאוֹי שֶׁל אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּין.
> תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הַמּוֹצִיא זֶבֶל עַל רֹאשׁוֹ וּתְפִילִּין בְּרֹאשׁוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יְסַלְּקֵם לִצְדָדִין, וְלֹא יִקְשְׁרֵם בְּמׇתְנָיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נוֹהֵג בָּהֶן מִנְהַג בִּזָּיוֹן. אֲבָל קוֹשְׁרָם עַל זְרוֹעוֹ בִּמְקוֹם תְּפִילִּין.
> מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרוּ: אֲפִילּוּ מִטְפַּחַת שֶׁלָּהֶן אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחַ עַל הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ תְּפִילִּין. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲפִילּוּ רִבְעָא דְרִבְעָא דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** § As the Gemara has cited the rulings of the school of Rabbi Yannai with regard to measurements, it now cites similar *halakhot* that the Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai state: With regard to prayer and with regard to phylacteries,** the measure is **four *kav*.** The Gemara inquires: **What is** the relevance of this measure **with regard to prayer?** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who carries a load on his shoulder and the time for prayer arrives,** if the load is **less than four *kav*,** he **lowers it behind him** while still holding it **and prays,** as a light load of this size does not interfere with prayer. If the load is **four *kav*, he places it on the ground and prays.** **What is** the relevance of this amount **with regard to phylacteries?** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a man **was carrying a load on his head and** he had **phylacteries on his head, if the phylacteries were being crushed** under the load it is **forbidden** to leave them on his head, **but if** they were **not** being crushed, it is **permitted.** With regard **to which load** did the Sages **state** this *halakha*? They stated it with regard **to a load of four *kav*.** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches:** With regard to one **who removes garbage** by carrying it **on his head and** has **phylacteries on his head, he** may **not move** the phylacteries **to the side** to prevent them from being crushed, **and** likewise he may **not tie** the phylacteries of the head **to his loins because he** thereby **treats them in a manner** of **degradation. But** he may **tie them on his arm in the location** where **the phylacteries** of the hand are placed. The Sages **said in the name of the school of Sheila:** It is **forbidden to place on the head** of one **that has phylacteries on it even the scarf** in which **they** are wrapped. The Gemara asks: **And how much** does Rabbi Sheila permit one to place on his head while wearing phylacteries? **Abaye said: Even** as little as **one-quarter of one-quarter** of the smallest measurement **of Pumbedita** is still forbidden from being placed on one's head.

## Taharot Integration

### Strength Motif

Having worked through the entire sequence of *tnan hatam* passages, it is worth pausing to ask why they appear here at all. Rashi explains that all of them are gathered here because each one is accompanied by an explanatory tradition from *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* — the school of Rabbi Yannai provides the definition or measurement in each case. That is a sufficient reason for the compiler of the *sugya* to bring them together: the thread connecting them is that each mishna arrives with a Yannai-school explanation attached.

There is, however, a more satisfying account of what draws these passages into this context, and it is the one that arises from a careful look at the recurring subject matter. Each of these passages turns on a threshold of strength: the bad olives that are too weak to produce adequate oil; the tree or branch whose structural strength is insufficient to prevent the bending that enables *tumah* transfer; the animal whose strength is so diminished that it betrays itself under load; the person whose knees buckle; the load that is too heavy to carry while praying or wearing *tefillin*. The motif running through all of them is the borderline between adequate and insufficient strength — what is strong enough and what falls short. This is precisely the question that governs the preceding agricultural discussion: how much must a field produce for the lessee to remain obligated? What is the minimum strength, so to speak, of the field's output? The literary structure of the Bavli here follows this thematic coherence, not merely the coincidence of Yannai-school explanations.

David Rozenthal reads this sequence as evidence of something more: he sees in these passages traces of what might be called an incipient *Talmud* on the tractates of Seder Taharot — more formally edited discussions that circulated around those *mishnayot* and found their way into the Bavli when the literary editors (*mesadrei ha-Gemara*) placed them alongside the *mishnayot* they illuminated, sometimes bringing along material that is not strictly relevant to the Bavli's own tractate. On this view, the sequence of *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* passages entered the Bavli already as a set, because those Yannai-school traditions had been transmitted together.

An alternative view is more conservative: perhaps there was no separate Taharot *Talmud* in any formal sense, but simply that the *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* traditions on these *mishnayot* were known and transmitted as a group. When the occasion arose — here, because we were already discussing *d'vei Rabbi Yannai*'s measurement in the context of *kablanut* — they were brought in together. The thematic motif of strength thresholds is the stronger literary explanation for why they appear here; the *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* connection is the more historical one.

## Kdei Nefilah

### Minimum Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַאי קִצְבָה בִּכְרִי? אֶלָּא: אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי נְפִילָה. וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי נְפִילָה? רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרְבַּעה סְאִין לְכוֹר. רַבִּי אַמֵּי דִּילֵיהּ אָמַר: שְׁמוֹנַת סְאִין לְכוֹר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא לְרַב חָמָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ, בִּשְׁנֵי דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה שַׁמִּינָא אַרְעָא, בִּשְׁנֵי דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי הֲוָה כְּחִישָׁא אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Yehuda says: What fixed** measure is **a pile? Rather,** the relevant issue is **whether it has** a crop **equivalent to** the measure of seeds for **dropping** in a field in order to sow it. The Gemara asks: **And how much** is **equivalent to **the measure of seeds for **dropping** in a field in order to sow it? **Rabbi Ami says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: **Four *se'a* for** the amount of land sufficient to grow **a *kor*. Rabbi Ami himself,** though, **says eight *se'a* for** the amount of land sufficient to grow **a *kor*. A certain elder said to Rav Ḥama, son of Rabba bar Avuh: I will explain it to you: In the years of Rabbi Yoḥanan the land was fat,** while **in the years of Rabbi Ami the land was lean,** and it was therefore necessary to double the amount of seed for each unit of land.

Returning now to the Mishna's dispute: the *tanna kamma* requires *kedei le'amid kari* — enough to set up a stack — for the lessee to be obligated to continue working the field, a threshold the Gemara established as either two or three *se'ah*. Rabbi Yehuda sets the threshold instead at *kedei nefilah* — the amount of seed needed to replant the field the following year. Below that level, the field's output is so negligible that the lessee is released from his work obligation.

### Land Quality Dispute

The Gemara asks: how much, precisely, is *kedei nefilah*? Rabbi Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — though the text as emended by the *Hagahot ha-Bach* reads *arba'ah kabbin le-khor* — says four *kabbin* per *kur* of field. Rabbi Ami then offers his own independent view: eight *se'ah* per *kur*, twice the amount attributed to Rabbi Yochanan. An elder explains the discrepancy to Rav Chama son of Rabbah bar Avuha: in the days of Rabbi Yochanan the land was fertile — *shemena ar'a*; in the days of Rabbi Ami the land had become exhausted — *kachsha ar'a*. Weaker land required proportionally more seed to achieve the same result, and so Rabbi Ami's figure reflects the agricultural conditions of his own time.

### Scattered Sheaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הָרוּחַ שֶׁפִּיזְּרָה אֶת הָעוֹמָרִין, אוֹמְדִים אוֹתָהּ כַּמָּה לֶקֶט רְאוּיָה לַעֲשׂוֹת וְנוֹתֵן לַעֲנִיִּים. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לַעֲנִיִּים כְּדֵי נְפִילָה.
> וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי נְפִילָה? כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּין לְכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Pe'a* 5:1): If **the wind scattered the** standing **sheaves** so that it is no longer known which gleanings fell from the sheaves during the harvest and belong to the poor, one **evaluates how many gleanings** it was **fit to produce, and** he **gives** these **to the poor. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He gives to the poor** the amount **equivalent to **the measure of seeds **dropping** in the course of harvesting. The Gemara asks: **And how much** is the amount **equivalent to** the measure of seeds **dropping **in the course of harvesting? **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said** that **Rabbi Elazar** said, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan: Four *kav* for a *kor*.

A second *tnan hatam* is now introduced, drawn this time from Seder Zraim — specifically from Masekhet Peah. *Ha'ruach shepizra et ha'omrin* — if the wind scatters someone's sheaves, the Gemara rules that we estimate how much *leket* the field would have produced and that amount is given to the poor. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: we do not estimate, but rather give the poor *kedei nefilah* — the amount sufficient to reseed the field. The Gemara then asks of this second source: how much is *kedei nefilah*?

This passage is brought here because *kedei nefilah* requires definition in both contexts — the *kablanut* context of the preceding Mishna and the *peah*-*leket* context of Seder Zraim. The Bavli, which produced no *gemara* on Seder Zraim any more than on Seder Taharot, handles this by bringing the relevant *mishnayot* together at the point where the shared term requires elucidation in both directions.

### Four Kabin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְכוֹר זֶרַע, אוֹ לְכוֹר תְּבוּאָה? לְמַפּוֹלֶת יָד, אוֹ לְמַפּוֹלֶת שְׁווֹרִים?
> תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּכִי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וְאָמְרִי לָהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּין לְכוֹר זֶרַע. וַעֲדַיִן תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ: לְמַפּוֹלֶת יָד אוֹ לְמַפּוֹלֶת שְׁווֹרִים? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is the amount **equivalent to** the measure of seeds **dropping **in the course of harvesting? **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said** that **Rabbi Elazar** said, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan: Four *kav* for a *kor*. Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma:** Does this mean **for a** field that requires a ***kor* of seed** to plant it, **or for a *kor* of produce?** And if it is the former, does it refer **to sowing** by **hand or to sowing** by **oxen?** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael **he** said that **Rabbi Avuh said** that **Rabbi Elazar said, and some say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Four *kav* for a** field sown with a ***kor* of seed.** The Gemara comments: **And** the other question should **still raise a dilemma for you:** Does this refer **to sowing** by **hand or to sowing** by **oxen?** No answer was found for this question, and the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Dimi, reporting in the name of either Rabbi Elazar or Rabbi Yochanan, gives the answer: *arba'ah kabbin le-khor* — four *kabbin* for a *kur* of field. This is consistent with the figure attributed to Rabbi Yochanan earlier, and it is precisely this consistency that led the Vilna Gaon to emend the earlier text. Where the earlier discussion had *arba'ah se'im le-khor*, the Gra corrected it to *arba'ah kabbin*, since a *se'ah* is three times a *kab*, and the discrepancy between the two formulations was otherwise irreconcilable. Whether this emendation is correct remains a matter of the precise volumetric relationship between these measures.

### Hand vs. Animal

Rabbi Yirmeyah poses two questions about the meaning of *arba'ah kabbin le-khor zera*. The first: is this a *kur* of *zera* — a field large enough to require a *kur* of seed — or a *kur* of *tevuah* — a field that produces a *kur* of grain? These are very different field sizes. A field that requires a *kur* of seed to sow is substantially larger than a field that yields a *kur* of grain as its output. Rashi explains: is it a large field, defined by how much seed it needs, or a small field, defined by how much it produces?

The Gemara attempts to answer this question from the same *tnan hatam* source: *arba'ah kabbin le-khor zera* — the explicit phrase *kur zera* resolves the first question in favor of the larger field, the one requiring a *kur* of seed.

But a second question immediately follows: when we speak of seeding a *kur* of field, do we mean seeding by hand — *mapolet yad* — or by animal-drawn seeding — *mapolet shevarim*? This is not a trivial distinction. Hand seeding allows the farmer to apply seed with discrimination — more here, less there — and tends to use somewhat less seed overall. Animal-drawn seeding is automatic and uniform; the mechanism disperses seed at a constant rate regardless of local conditions, and typically uses more seed per unit area. The two methods would yield a meaningfully different quantity for *arba'ah kabbin le-khor*. The Gemara leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.
