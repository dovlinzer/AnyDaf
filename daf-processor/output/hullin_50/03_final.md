# Hullin 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chelev Properties

### Chelev Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדִידַן מִיסְתָּם נָמֵי לָא סָתֵים.
>
> **Translation:** **and to us,** the residents of Babylonia, not only is it forbidden but **it also does not seal** a perforation? It cannot be that this fat is forbidden by Torah law if they permit it for consumption. Even though we are stringent with regard to its consumption, we may still maintain that it effectively seals a perforation.

The present daf picks up at the very bottom of 49b. Having moved beyond the discussion of the lung, the Gemara has been treating the subject of the *keivah* — the fourth stomach of the cow — and specifically the *chelev* found upon it: whether that fat constitutes forbidden fat and whether *chelev* is capable of sealing holes. The Gemara's conclusion had been that *chelev* from a kosher animal, being more adhesive and more firmly attached to the underlying organ, does seal holes and resolves *treifa* problems. By contrast, *chelev* that is not from a kosher animal — described by the Gemara according to two criteria, namely that it is encased in a membrane and that it drapes over rather than adheres to the organ — is less sticky and therefore does not seal holes. Tosafot cites one of the *ba'alei tosafot* who assumed that the *chelev* of fowl (*of*) was sufficiently adhesive to solve the problem, though the Gemara itself does not address the case of *of* directly.

### Hat-Shaped Fat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאַקַּשְׁתָּא – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – דְּאַיִּיתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The abomasum is shaped like a bow. The side facing outward is curved like the bow itself, while the side facing inward is flat and straight like the bowstring. With regard to the fat **that is on the bow, everyone agrees,** even the residents of Eretz Yisrael, **that it is forbidden** for consumption. This fat, then, is the *ḥimtza*, which does not seal a perforation according to Rav Naḥman. **When they disagree,** it is with regard to the fat **that is on the bowstring.** The residents of Eretz Yisrael permit it for consumption, while those of Babylonia prohibit it. According to Rav Naḥman, the residents of Babylonia should concede that it is nevertheless an effective seal. This, then, is the *bar ḥimtza*.

The Gemara now continues: *amar Rav Nachman: chelev ha-asui k'kova, eino sotem* — fat that is shaped like a hat does not seal holes. This seems to be the same underlying principle stated in somewhat different terms: a fat that covers or caps an organ rather than clinging to it is, by analogy to a hat sitting atop the head, not truly adhesive and does not seal holes. The implication is that this fat is presumably forbidden fat and therefore lacks the sealing capacity.

### Identifying Hat Fat


The Gemara then asks: *heicha amri lei heicha* — where exactly is this hat-shaped fat found? The answer given is: *amri lei chitey de-karkashah ve-amri lei tirpashah de-libba* — some say it refers to the membranous cords or filaments surrounding the *karkashah* (the rectum and large intestine), and others say it refers to the membrane enveloping the heart. The fat surrounding the heart — the pericardium and its overlying fat — sits upon the heart much like a cap, exactly the shape described. Similarly, the fat running along the *karkashah* has a somewhat sheath-like or draped quality rather than adhering firmly to the organ. Both descriptions share the essential quality of being membrane-encased fat that covers rather than sticks, and both are thus classified as forbidden fat that does not seal holes.

### Chimtza Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, דְּאַיִּיתְרָא – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – דְּאַקַּשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that with regard to the fat **that is on the bowstring, everyone agrees that it is permitted.** This, then, is the *bar ḥimtza*. **When they disagree,** it is with regard to the fat **that is on the bow.** The residents of Eretz Yisrael permit it, but the residents of Babylonia prohibit it. This, then, is the *ḥimtza*.

Rava now draws an inference: *sh'ma minah de-Rav Nachman tartei* — from Rav Nachman's statement we can derive two things. Specifically, with regard to the *chimtza* and the *bar chimtza* — two distinct types of fat on the *keivah* — one seals holes and one does not (*chad satim v'chad lo satim*), since one of them resembles a hat and the other does not. The Gemara then acknowledges: *v'lo yadana hai minaihu* — I do not know which is which. This is itself a somewhat puzzling admission: if the distinction is about one type looking like a hat and the other not, why is it not immediately obvious which is which from mere observation? And why is this particular inference attributed to Rav Nachman, given that the earlier discussion already covered essentially the same ground? These difficulties are noted without a ready resolution.

### Bar Chimtza Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב אַוְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מְקַמְּצִין, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי מִשּׁוּם זָקֵן אֶחָד: מְקַמְּצִין. אָמַר רַב אַוְיָא: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, קְמַצוּ וְהַבוּ לֵיהּ, וַאֲכַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: When it was stated that only the residents of Eretz Yisrael permit the fat on the bowstring for consumption, this means that they would eat it in a manner **like that which Rav Avya says** that **Rabbi Ami says:** One **takes a handful** of the fat that lies above the fat on the bowstring, since this is the fat prohibited by the verse: "And all the fat that is upon the innards" (Leviticus 3:3), and the rest is permitted for consumption. **And so says Rabbi Yannai,** also a resident of Eretz Yisrael, **in the name of a certain elder:** One **takes a handful. Rav Avya said: I was standing before Rabbi Ami.** The people there **took a handful** of the fat on the bowstring **and gave him** the remaining fat, **and he ate** from it.

Two Amoraim, Rav Huna bar Chinena and Rav Huna the son of Rav Nachman, state: *bar chimtza satim, chimtza lo satim* — it is the *bar chimtza* that seals holes, while the *chimtza* does not. Rav Tavut adds a mnemonic: *yafe koach haben mikoach ha'av* — the power of the son exceeds that of the father. This phrase derives from a Mishnah in *shavuot* dealing with the collection of debts: a son, unlike a father, can collect without taking an oath, since one does not presume the son has knowledge of whether payment was made. So here, the *bar chimtza* — the "son" of the *chimtza* — has the greater power, namely the power to seal holes. Yet we still do not know which type of fat is which.

The Gemara then says: *ta shma, d'amar Rav Nachman* — come and hear, for Rav Nachman stated: *inhu michal achlei, v'ladidan mistam nami lo satim* — they eat this fat in *eretz yisrael*, while for us in Bavel they do not, and if they do not eat it in Bavel, it does not seal holes for us either. This opens a discussion about the differing practices between *eretz yisrael* and Bavel regarding the *bar chimtza*.

### Rashi/Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמָּעֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא הֲוָה קָאֵי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קְמוֹץ, הַב לִי דְּאֵיכוֹל. חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָמְחַסֵּם, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּבְלָאָה אַתְּ, גּוֹם שְׁדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The attendant of Rabbi Ḥanina was standing before Rabbi Ḥanina,** a resident of Eretz Yisrael. Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: Take** off **a handful** from that fat on the bowstring and **give me** the rest so **that I will eat** from it. Rabbi Ḥanina **saw that** the attendant **was hesitating,** so **he said to him: You are a Babylonian** and usually consider all of this fat forbidden. If so, **cut** off all the fat and **throw it** away.

There is a significant dispute between Rashi and Tosafot as to how this line should be read. Tosafot reads it as a straightforward declarative: in *eretz yisrael* they eat the *bar chimtza* — which establishes that it is permitted fat, and permitted fat is by definition *sotem* — while in Bavel they do not eat it, and accordingly it does not seal holes. The identification of the *bar chimtza* with the fat debated between *eretz yisrael* and Bavel is thus, on Tosafot's reading, the key point.

Rashi, however, reads the statement with an air of astonishment (*bitmiya*): it is a striking thing that in *eretz yisrael* they go so far as to actually eat this fat. In Bavel we refrain — whether out of stringency or because the question was genuinely uncertain — but Rashi argues that our refraining from eating does not necessarily mean we should deny the *bar chimtza* its property of adhesiveness and sealing capacity. Perhaps the Babylonian practice was a *chumra* or a *minhag* arising from an unresolved *machlokes*, but functionally, even in Bavel, the *bar chimtza* remains *sotem*. This reading allows for a possible divergence between the categories of forbidden/permitted fat and the categories of fat that seals holes.

### Akashta/Yitrah


The Gemara clarifies the physical anatomy. The *keivah* is crescent-shaped — like a bow or a croissant. The outer arc of the crescent is called the *akashta* (the bow), and the inner arc is called the *yitrah* (perhaps connected etymologically with stringing a bow). The fat on the *akashta* — the outer crescent — is universally agreed to be forbidden fat, and accordingly it does not seal holes. The debate concerns only the *yitrah*, the inner crescent.

### EY vs Bavel


The Gemara presents two versions of the dispute. According to one account (*ika d'amri*), the fat of the inner crescent (*yitrah*) is what is eaten in *eretz yisrael* but not in Bavel, and it is this fat — whatever its precise status in Bavel — that is the *bar chimtza* capable of sealing holes. According to the other account, the fat of the inner crescent is universally agreed to be permitted, so that everyone agrees it is *sotem*; the debate between *eretz yisrael* and Bavel concerns only the outer crescent, where *eretz yisrael* goes so far as to permit even that. What is clear in either reading is that the inner crescent is the better-situated fat: at minimum it is contested whether it is forbidden, and at minimum it will be *sotem*; at most it is universally permitted and universally *sotem*.

### Peeling Fat


The Gemara now cites a practice that illuminates the *eretz yisrael* position. *ki ha d'amar rav avya amar rav ami: mikamtsan* — like that which Rav Avya said in the name of Rav Ami: you peel it off — meaning one strips away a thin layer of the fat on the *keivah* to remove any genuinely forbidden fat from the adjacent organs that might have mingled with it, and then proceeds to eat the remainder.

### Peeling Practice


Similarly, *rav yannai mishum chad zakein* — Rav Yannai in the name of a certain elder — says *mikamtsan*: peel it off and eat it. Rav Avya reports: *havina kamei d'Rav Ami, v'kamtsu v'havu leh v'achal* — I was present before Rav Ami, they peeled off the layer and gave him the rest to eat, and he ate it.

### Rabbi Chanina


There is a vivid anecdote involving Rabbi Chanina. Rabbi Chanina — who was from *eretz yisrael* — asked his servant: *kmuts hav li d'eichol* — peel off a layer for me so that I can eat the rest of the fat. When he saw the servant hesitating (*chazya d'hava kamchasam*), Rabbi Chanina said to him: "*Bavlat!* Are you a Babylonian?" — meaning, you follow Babylonian practice and consider this fat forbidden. With remarkable sensitivity, Rabbi Chanina did not insist; he said: *kum shadi* — go ahead, cut it all off and discard it. Rather than compel his servant to handle something the servant considered *treif*, Rabbi Chanina simply abandoned his intention to eat and had the entire piece removed.

This episode vividly illustrates the geographic divergence in practice. Rav Ami, Rav Yannai, and Rabbi Chanina were all from *eretz yisrael* and ate this fat freely (after peeling). The Babylonian servant's instinctive hesitation reflects his own community's *chumra*. Whether the outer or inner crescent is specifically intended in these narratives is not entirely clear from the context, but the *bar chimtza* — the inner crescent fat — is at the very least permitted in *eretz yisrael* and is the fat that, even in Bavel, functions as *sotem*.

## Mucus Fillers

### RSbG Mucus Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בְּנֵי מֵעַיִין שֶׁנִּיקְּבוּ, וְלֵיחָה סוֹתַמְתָּן – כְּשֵׁרָה. מַאי לֵיחָה? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: שִׁירְקָא דִּמְעַיָּיא דְּנָפֵיק אַגַּב דּוּחְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that if the small intestines are perforated, the animal is a *tereifa*. With regard to this, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If the **intestines were perforated but mucus seals** the perforated intestines, the animal is **kosher.** The Gemara asks: **What** is this **mucus? Rav Kahana said:** It is the **mucus of the intestines that comes out under pressure,** when one scrapes with a knife.

Having completed the discussion of fat as a hole-sealer, the Gemara moves to a new category of filler: the organ's own mucus. A *baraita* is cited: *rabban shimon ben gamliel omer: benei mei'in shenikvu, velecha sotman, keshera* — if the inner organs were perforated and a mucus seals them, the animal is *kasher*. This introduces a third type of potential hole-sealer alongside membranes (which the Gemara had ruled insufficient) and *chelev* (which works when adhesive): the idea that a naturally secreted mucus from within the organ itself can serve as a legitimate filler.

### Mucus Definition


The Gemara asks: *mai lecha* — what exactly is this mucus? Rav Kahana explains: *shirkeda demei'aya denafik agav duchka* — it is the mucous secretion of the intestinal wall itself, which emerges through the natural pressure of the organ's functioning. This is not an external substance introduced into the hole, but the organ's own biological discharge, which seeps out under pressure and can occlude the perforation.

### Rabbi Lineage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמִירִי חַבְרַיָּא דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא מֵרַבִּי אַבָּא, וּמַנּוּ? רַבִּי זֵירָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ חַבְרַיָּא דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא, וּמַנּוּ? רַבִּי אַבָּא. אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּטְרֵפָה, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּאֵבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The colleagues of Rabbi Abba learned** a tradition **from Rabbi Abba. And who are they?** The phrase: Colleagues of Rabbi Abba, is referring to **Rabbi Zeira. And some say the colleagues of Rabbi Zeira** learned it **from Rabbi Zeira. And who are they? Rabbi Abba.** This is the tradition: **Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, said** that **this** is what **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel with regard to a *tereifa*, and** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to** the *halakhot* of **mourning.**

The Gemara traces the transmission of this tradition: the colleagues of Rabbi Abba learned this from Rabbi Abba, and others say it is the reverse — the colleagues of Rabbi Zeira learned from Rabbi Zeira, and Rabbi Abba was the colleague who transmitted. The chain leads to a specific attribution: Rabbi Abba the son of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba — named for his grandfather — reporting in the name of his father, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, who said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha keRabban Shimon ben Gamliel betreifa, v'halacha keRabbi Shimon ba'avel*. Rashi notes that the "Rabbi Abba" in this chain is not the same Rabbi Abba as the one from whom the tradition was received, which would be paradoxical; this Rabbi Abba is a different figure, the son of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba.

### Against Mucus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּטְרֵפָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּאֵבֶל, מַאי הִיא? דִּתְנַן: כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, בָּא מִמָּקוֹם קָרוֹב – מוֹנֶה עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel with regard to a *tereifa*; this** is the *halakha* **that we stated** above, that mucus forms an effective seal in the intestines. The *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to mourning; what is** this *halakha*? **As we learned** in a *baraita*: If one learns of the death of an immediate relative after other relatives have already begun their period of mourning, the *halakha* is as follows: During **the entire first three days** of mourning, if a mourner **comes** to the house of mourning **from a nearby place, he counts** his days of mourning **with** the other mourners and completes his mourning with them.

The substance of the tradition attributed to Rabbi Yochanan is twofold: we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding *treifa* (that is, the mucus seals the hole), and we rule like Rabbi Shimon regarding *aveilus* (mourning). The Gemara expands on the second ruling with an extended discussion, but first the overall tradition is stated clearly.

### Aveilus Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמָּקוֹם רָחוֹק – מוֹנֶה לְעַצְמוֹ. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, אֲפִילּוּ בָּא מִמָּקוֹם קָרוֹב – מוֹנֶה לְעַצְמוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי, בָּא מִמָּקוֹם קָרוֹב – מוֹנֶה עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If he came **from a distant place, he counts** seven days **on his own** from the time that he was informed of the death. **From this** point **forward,** i.e., after the first three days, **even if he comes from a nearby place, he counts** seven days **on his own. Rabbi Shimon says: Even on the seventh day,** if **he comes from a nearby place, he counts** and completes his mourning **with** the other mourners.

The ruling regarding *aveilus* pertains to a Mishnah in which the question arises as to when a late-arriving mourner joins the household's *shiva* count. The Mishnah establishes: during the first three days, if the newly arrived mourner comes from *makom karov* — a nearby place, defined as within a day's journey — he counts together with the household (*moneh imahem*) from the day of death, even if he personally only learned of the loss later. If he comes from *makom rachok* — a distant place — he counts *shiva* on his own from the day he finds out. The underlying concept is that the household constitutes a unit: if the mourner could theoretically have heard within time, he is treated as if he did hear and is conceptually part of the mourning household from the outset.

After the first three days, the Mishnah rules that even one arriving from *makom karov* counts *shiva* on his own. Rabbi Shimon, however, extends the joining principle: *afilu be-yom ha-shevi'i, ba mi-makom karov moneh imahem* — even if he arrives on the seventh day and comes from a nearby place, he counts together with the household and his *shiva* concludes with theirs. Tosafot raises the practical question of what it means to arrive on the seventh day, since by the morning of that day the mourners have already risen. Tosafot explains that this refers to arriving before the consolers have departed, since the *shiva* formally concludes only when the consolers leave.

Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that we follow Rabbi Shimon in this matter of *aveilus* is part of the same transmitted tradition. It is said in the name of a certain unnamed individual (*man d'hu* — what's-his-name, as Rashi explains that the *ba'alei ha-gemara* knew the story but had forgotten whose name to attach to it) that he once said: *iski v'eisik v'gamru shema'atei mi-pumei d'mara* — I wish to go up to *eretz yisrael* and hear this ruling directly from the one who taught it. This theme of traveling to verify the *mesorah* and hear things *mi-pumei d'mara* — from the mouth of the master — is a recurring and striking motif in these *dapim*.

### Mesorah Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מַאן דְּהוּא: אִיזְכֵּי וְאֶיסַּק וְאֶגְמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא מִפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּמָרַהּ. כִּי סְלֵיק, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲמַר מָר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּטְרֵפָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אֵין הֲלָכָה אֲמַרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Someone** whose name was not given **said: May I merit to go up** to Eretz Yisrael **and learn this *halakha* from the mouth of its Master. When he went up** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, **he found Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba,** and **said to him:** Is it true that **the Master said** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel with regard to a *tereifa*?** Rabbi Abba **said to him: This** is not true. Rather, **I said** just the opposite, that the ***halakha*** is **not** in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and even if mucus seals the perforation of the intestine, the animal is a *tereifa*.

This unnamed person resolved to ascend to *eretz yisrael* precisely to verify the tradition. The desire to confirm the *mesorah* at its source, rather than rely on reported transmission, reflects how seriously the *amoraim* treated the accuracy of attributed rulings, especially in matters as consequential as *treifos*.

When he arrived in *eretz yisrael*, *ashkechei le-Rabbi Abba breih de-Rabbi Chiya* — he found Rabbi Abba the son of Rabbi Chiya — and asked him directly: did you really say we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding *treifa*, that the mucus seals holes? Rabbi Abba's reply is startling: *ha ein halachah amri* — no, I said we do *not* rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding *treifa*. The version of the tradition that had reached Bavel was simply inverted. The mucus does not seal holes according to the final ruling.

### Final Aveilus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּאָבֵל, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלוּגָתָא נִינְהוּ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר הֲלָכָה, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲלָכָה, וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר אֵין הֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The anonymous man asked Rabbi Abba again: **What** about the other ruling reported in your name, that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to mourning;** is this accurate? Rabbi Abba **said to him: There are conflicting** opinions with regard to this matter, **as it was stated** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that the ***halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **and so says Rabbi Yoḥanan:** That is the ***halakha*. But Rav Naḥman says:** That is **not** the ***halakha*.**

The questioner then asks: what is your ruling regarding Rabbi Shimon in the matter of *aveilus*? Rabbi Abba responds that this is itself subject to debate: *pelugta ninhu* — it is disputed. Rav Chisda rules that we follow Rabbi Shimon (*halachah*), and similarly Rabbi Yochanan himself says *halachah*. But Rav Nachman rules that we do not follow Rabbi Shimon (*ein halachah*). The Gemara then concludes: *v'ein halachah ke-Rabbi Shimon* — and the law does not follow Rabbi Shimon on the *treifa* question — but *v'halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon ba-avel* — we do rule like Rabbi Shimon in the matter of *aveilus*, in accordance with the principle *halacha ke-divrei ha-mekil be-avel* — in matters of mourning, we rule like the more lenient position, as stated by Shmuel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּטְרֵפָה, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּאֵבֶל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּיקֵל בְּאֵבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And** the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel with regard to a *tereifa*, and** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to mourning, in accordance with** the statement of **Shmuel,** who **says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the** more **lenient** authority **in** matters relating to **mourning.**

The result is precise: the *treifa* ruling is rejected — the mucus of the organ does not constitute a valid hole-sealer — while the *aveilus* ruling is affirmed — a mourner arriving from a nearby place even on the seventh day joins the household's count and his *shiva* concludes with theirs. The anonymous concluding voice of the Gemara, associated with the *stam* of the *shas*, presents this as the definitive ruling. The entire episode once again demonstrates how dramatically a transmitted tradition could diverge from what was actually taught, and how the *amoraim* took extraordinary steps to correct such errors.

## Hole Dating

### Comparison Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא: מַקִּיפִים בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya says: One may compare** perforations **in the intestines.** If one is unsure whether a perforation occurred before or after slaughter, one may make an additional perforation and compare the two. If their appearance is similar, one may conclude that the perforation occurred after slaughter, and the animal is kosher.

The Gemara introduces a significant *kula*: *amar Rav Shimi bar Chiya: makifin bifnei me'ayim* — one may make comparison holes in the inner organs. The procedure is as follows: when a hole is found in an organ, if there is reason to suppose — or simply a desire to explore whether — it may have occurred after *shechita* rather than when the animal was alive, one may take the same organ, make a fresh hole in another location, and compare the two side by side. If the existing hole resembles the freshly made hole, one may conclude that the original hole was made after *shechita*, and the animal is *kasher*. If they look different, one must assume the hole antedates *shechita* and the animal is a *treifa*. This is a striking *kula*: notwithstanding all the stringencies regarding holes in organs, the Gemara provides a practical experimental method to attribute a hole to a post-mortem cause and thereby rescue the animal's *kashrut* status.

### Rava's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בְּנֵי מֵעַיִים דַּאֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אַקְּפִינְהוּ וְלָא אִידְּמוֹ. אֲתָא רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא בְּרֵיהּ, מְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּהוּ וְאִידְּמוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּמָּה יְדֵי מַשְׁמִשׁוּ בְּהָנֵי מִקַּמֵּי דְּלֵיתֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּמָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַכִּים בְּרִי בִּטְרֵפוֹת כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There were **certain** perforated **intestines that came before Rava.** Rava made other perforations and **compared them, but they were not similar. Rav Mesharshiyya, his son, came** and **rubbed** the new perforations, **and they were similar.** Rav Mesharshiyya therefore deemed the animal kosher. Rava **said to him: From where** did **you** know to do **this?** Rav Mesharshiyya **said to him:** I reasoned: **How many hands rubbed these** earlier perforations **before they came before** the **Master?** Therefore, I thought that if I handled the new ones, perhaps they would look similar. Rava **said to him: My son is as wise in** matters of ***tereifot* as Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

The Gemara records an incident. Certain inner organs were brought before Rava with holes in them. *akfinhu* — he made a comparison hole — but the two holes did not look similar, and Rava accordingly ruled the animal a *treifa*, concluding that the hole had been made while the animal was alive.

### Handling Effect


At that point, *ata Rav Marshorshie brei* — Rav Marshorshie's son arrived. He took the freshly made comparison hole, *mimashmesh bahu* — rubbed and handled it with his hands — and after that handling, *v'idmi* — it now looked just like the original hole. He then pointed this out to Rava: *kama yadei mimashmeshu behani mikami d'leito l'kamei d'mar* — how many hands must have handled this organ before it was even brought before you? The original hole had been handled extensively before Rava saw it. When he made his comparison hole, it was fresh and untouched, and naturally looked different. Once he handled the comparison hole to simulate what the original had undergone, the two appeared identical — and therefore one could conclude that both holes were made after *shechita*.

### Brilliance Praise


Rava's response was unrestrained admiration: *chakim brei b'treifos k'Rabbi Yochanan* — my son is as brilliant in the laws of *treifos* as Rabbi Yochanan. Rashi explains this either as a general superlative — Rabbi Yochanan being the gold standard of *talmidei chachamim* — or more specifically, since Rabbi Yochanan was known as a particular expert in the laws of *treifos*. The practical consequence was that the animal could now be permitted based on the son's insight.

### Lung Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מַקִּיפִים בָּרֵיאָה. אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּאוֹתָהּ עֲרוּגָה, אֲבָל מֵעֲרוּגָה לַעֲרוּגָה – לָא. וְהִלְכְתָא: אֲפִילּוּ מֵעֲרוּגָה לַעֲרוּגָה, מִדַּקָּה לְדַקָּה, וּמִגַּסָּה לְגַסָּה, אֲבָל לֹא מִגַּסָּה לְדַקָּה, וְלֹא מִדַּקָּה לְגַסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar both say: One may compare** perforations **in the lung** to determine whether they occurred after slaughter. **Rava said: We said** this **only** when the two perforations are **on the same side** of the lungs. **But** one may **not** compare perforations **from** one **side to the** other **side.** The Gemara rules: **And the *halakha*** is: One may compare **even from** one **side to the** other **side,** and even **from a small** animal **to** another **small** animal, **or from a large** animal **to** another **large** animal, **but not from a large** animal **to a small** animal, **and not from a small** animal **to a large** animal.

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar both rule: *makifin b'reia* — the comparison test applies even to the lung. Rashi explains this is noteworthy because even in a place where one would not normally attribute the hole to the *shochet's* handling, the comparison test may still be employed. If the hole in the lung resembles a freshly made hole, one may assume the original hole occurred after *shechita* — perhaps from a knife flying during the butchering process or from some other post-mortem cause — and the animal remains *kasher*.

One might wonder about cases where the hole was made by a needle: would a post-mortem needle hole really look different from a pre-mortem one? The Gemara does not address this directly, but the broader principle seems to be that when there is genuine doubt as to when the damage occurred, the presumption of *chezkat kashrut* operates more forcefully, and reasonable grounds for attributing the hole to a post-mortem cause are sufficient to permit. Where the cause of the hole is established with certainty, on the other hand, the relevant *treifa* rules apply without exception.

### Same Row


Rava introduces a limitation on the comparison test for the lung: *lo amru ela b'osah arugah* — the comparison is valid only within the same row or section of lung tissue. *aval me'arugah l'arugah lo* — one may not make a comparison hole in a different row of the lung and compare it to a hole in this row, since different sections of the organ may have different textures and appearances.

### Cross-Row


However, the *hilchata* — the final ruling — is more lenient: *afilu me'arugah l'arugah*, even from one row to another within the same lung, the comparison is valid. Furthermore, *midaka l'daka u'migasa l'gasa* — one may make the comparison hole in an entirely different animal of the same type: small animals (sheep and goats) may be compared to other small animals, large animals (cattle) to other cattle. One takes a healthy organ from a different animal of the same species, makes a fresh hole in it, and uses that as the comparison point. What is not permitted is *lo migasa l'daka v'lo midaka l'gasa* — one may not compare a hole in a cow's organ to a freshly made hole in a sheep's organ or vice versa.

### Trachea

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מַקִּיפִין בַּקָּנֶה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּאוֹתָהּ חוּלְיָא, אֲבָל מֵחוּלְיָא לְחוּלְיָא – לָא. וְהִלְכְתָא: אֲפִילּוּ מֵחוּלְיָא לְחוּלְיָא, וּמִבַּר חוּלְיָא לְבַר חוּלְיָא, אֲבָל לָא מֵחוּלְיָא לְבַר חוּלְיָא, וְלָא מִבַּר חוּלְיָא לְחוּלְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Abaye and Rava both say: One may compare** severed or perforated areas **in the windpipe. Rav Pappa said: We said** this **only** when the two areas are **in the same segment** of the windpipe. **But** one may **not** compare **from** one **segment to** another **segment.** The Gemara rules: **And the *halakha*** is: One may **even** compare **from** one **segment to** another **segment, and from** one **subsegment,** i.e., a thin segment between the main segments, **to** another **subsegment, but not from a segment to a subsegment, and not from a subsegment to a segment.**

Abaye and Rava both rule: *makifin b'kaneh* — the comparison test applies also to the trachea. This is somewhat more complex to visualize, since the trachea is only rendered *treifa* by a cut through the majority of its circumference, but the same principle applies: if such damage is found, one may make a comparable cut elsewhere and compare. Pappa rules that this comparison is valid only *b'oto chulya* — within the same ring section of the trachea's cartilage. *aval mechulya lechulya lo* — from one ring section to another is not a valid comparison.

The *hilchata*, however, again overrules this restriction: *afilu mechulya lechulya* — even from one ring section to another, *u'mivar chulya levar chulya* — and even from one *bar chulya* to another. The *bar chulya*, as Rashi explains, refers to either the soft membrane between the cartilage rings or the softer posterior portion of the cartilage ring where the hard material gives way to softer tissue. The one restriction that remains is that one may not compare a hole in the hard cartilage to a hole in the soft inter-ring membrane or vice versa — *aval lo mechulya levar chulya velo mivar chulya lechulya* — since these different materials will naturally look different and no valid comparison can be drawn between them.

## Chacholet/Keres

### Chacholet Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי: חַלְחוֹלֶת שֶׁנִּיקְּבָה כְּשֵׁרָה, הוֹאִיל וִירֵיכַיִם מַעֲמִידוֹת אוֹתָהּ. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעַי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְקוֹם הַדֶּבֶק – בְּרוּבּוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם הַדֶּבֶק – בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ze'eiri says:** If **the rectum was perforated,** the animal is **kosher, since the hips hold it up** and seal the perforation. **And** to **how much** of the rectum does this apply? **Rabbi Ilai says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If the rectum was perforated in **the place where** it **is attached** to the hips, even if it was perforated **in its majority,** the animal is kosher. If the perforation was **not in the place where** it **is attached** to the hips, the animal is a *tereifa* if it was perforated **in any amount.**

The Gemara now turns to the *chacholet*, the rectum. *amar zeiri: chacholet shenikva, kesheira* — the rectum that has been perforated is *kasher*. The Mishnah had not enumerated the *chacholet* among the organs whose perforation renders an animal a *treifa*, and Zeiri makes explicit why: *hoil v'yerechayim ma'amidot otah* — since the animal's thighs hold it in place and support it, the functional concern that gives rise to *treifa* status for other perforated organs does not apply here with the same force. Rashi elaborates that the waste matter passing through the rectum is already dry and hard, making it highly unlikely that it would actually exit through a hole in the rectal wall.

### Thigh Support


*Amar Rabbi Ila amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the leniency is calibrated by location. *mekom hadevek berubo* — where the thighs actually adhere to the *chacholet*, even if the majority of the rectal wall is missing or torn, the animal is *kasher*. *shelo bimekom hadevek, bemashehu* — where the thighs do not adhere, even a *mashehu*, the slightest perforation, renders the animal *treifa*.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר: לָאו מִי אָמֵינָא לְכוּ לָא תִּתְלוֹ בֵּיהּ בּוּקֵי סְרִיקִי? הָכִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מְקוֹם הַדֶּבֶק, אֲפִילּוּ נִיטַּל כּוּלּוֹ – כָּשֵׁר, וְהוּא שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּיֵּיר בּוֹ כְּדֵי תְּפִיסָה. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּמְלֵא בַּטְדָּא בְּתוֹרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said this** *halakha* **before Rava in the name of Rav Naḥman.** Rava **said** to them: **Have I not told you not to hang on** Rav Naḥman **empty bottles,** i.e., not to falsely attribute statements to him? **So said Rav Naḥman:** With regard to **the place that** the rectum **is attached** to the hips, **even** if **all of it was removed,** the animal is **kosher. And this** is the *halakha* only **where sufficient** space **remains of it** for a hand to **grip.** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is considered sufficient to grip? **Abaye said: The width of a finger.** This measure is sufficient even **in** the case of a large **bull.**

The Rabbis reported this ruling before Rava in the name of Rav Nachman. Rava pushed back sharply: *lav niamina lechu, lo titlu beih buki sariki* — have I not told you not to attribute empty and mistaken ideas to Rav Nachman? Here again we encounter the recurring Talmudic theme of correcting inaccurate *mesorah*. *hachi amar rav nachman* — this is what Rav Nachman actually said: *mekom hadevek afilu nital kulo kasher* — where the thighs adhere, even if the entire *chacholet* is removed, the animal is *kasher* — provided that *vehu shenishtayer bo kedei tfisa* — enough remains to be grasped, i.e., there is some minimal remnant.

### Finger Width


The Gemara asks: *v'chama* — how much is *kedei tfisa*? Abaye answers: *kemalei bitda betura* — as much as a finger-width in a large ox. Even if only a finger's breadth of the rectal wall remains, the animal retains its *kasher* status. This is a remarkable ruling. One might expect that an animal whose rectum has been almost entirely removed would be a *treifa* on physiological grounds, but the *halacha* does not follow physiological intuition here; the rule of the thighs' support overrides what one might expect.

### Inner Keress

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִי. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הֵעִיד נָתָן בַּר שֵׁילָא רֵישׁ טַבָּחַיָּא דְּצִפּוֹרִי לִפְנֵי רַבִּי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נָתָן: אֵי זוֹ הִיא כָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִי? סַנְיָא דֵּיבֵי. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: סַנְיָא דֵּיבֵי. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר: אִיסְתּוּמְכָא דְּכַרְסָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that if **the internal rumen** was perforated or most of the external rumen was torn, the animal is a *tereifa*. With regard to this, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Natan bar Sheila, chief butcher of Tzippori, testified before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **in the name of Rabbi Natan: Which is the internal rumen?** It is **the cecum. And so said Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa:** It is **the cecum. Rabbi Yishmael said:** It is the **opening [*istumka*] of the rumen.**

The Gemara transitions to the *keres*, the first of the four stomach compartments. The Mishnah had distinguished between the *keres hapnimi* — the inner stomach — and the *keres hachitzon* — the outer stomach. A hole in the inner stomach renders the animal a *treifa* even at the level of *mashehu*, while the outer stomach requires a tear of *rov* (majority). The critical question — generating multiple competing opinions — is what exactly constitutes the inner and outer *keres*.

By way of anatomical orientation: the *keres* is the large first chamber, the rumen, where ingested matter initially enters. From there it passes to the *mesas*, then to the *beis hakosos*, where it is regurgitated and rechewed, and finally to the *keivah*. The *keres* itself is an enormous organ.

### Sanya Divi


*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: Natan bar Shila, the head butcher of Tzipori, testified before Rebbi in the name of Rebbi Natan — *eizehu hi hakeres hapnimi? Sanya Divi* — what is the inner *keres* where even a *mashehu* perforation is a *treifa*? It is *sanya divi*, literally "that which is hated by foxes" — a term understood to refer to the cecum, a separate appendage into which waste matter and indigestible material passes. The cecum earns its epithet because even animals will not eat it, given its contents. It is anatomically distinct from the *keres* proper, though in context it is categorized as the "inner" portion of the stomach complex. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Karcha agrees: *sanya divi*.

### Alt Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָקוֹם צַר יֵשׁ בַּכָּרֵס, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶהוּ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: נְפַל כְּרֵסָא בְּבֵירָא. אָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר רַב עַוָּא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מִן הַמֵּיצַר וּלְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: **There is a narrow place in the rumen,** and this is the internal rumen; all the rest is the external rumen. **But I do not know which** area **is** this narrow place. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: The rumen has fallen in a pit,** i.e., since it is unknown which area is considered the narrow place, a perforation in any part of the rumen renders the animal a *tereifa*. **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Ava says** that **Rav Asi says:** The inner rumen extends **from the narrow place,** where the rumen begins to narrow as it approaches the gullet, **and downward** toward the gullet when the animal is suspended by its legs.

Rabbi Yishmael offers a different definition: *istomcha dekarsa* — the stomach of the *keres*, which in ancient usage likely meant the opening or mouth of the organ, perhaps the juncture where the *keres* opens into the next chamber. Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan says: *makom tzar yesh ba-keres ve-eini yodeia eizehu hu* — there is a narrowing within the *keres* and I do not know exactly where it is, and that narrowing is the inner *keres*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak wryly observes: *nafal karsa be-beira* — your *keres* has fallen into the well — an echo of the expression *nafla pita be-beira*, meaning: what good is a ruling if you cannot identify what it describes? Rav Acha bar Rav Avya, explaining Rav Assi's position, says: *min ha-meitzar ulematah* — from where the narrowing begins and below, that is the inner *keres*.

Rabbi Yaakov bar Nachmani in the name of Shmuel defines the inner *keres* as *makom she-ein bo milat* — the place where there is no felt-like material. The *keres* has a particular texture at one end — likened to felt — and where that texture stops and smooth wall begins, that is the inner *keres*. This corresponds roughly to the area sheltered beneath the full rib cage, in contrast to the more exposed portion. Rabbi Avina, in the name of Geniva, citing Rav, gives yet another definition: a *tefach* within the *keres* near the esophagus is the inner *keres* — a position that seems geographically anomalous relative to all the others, since it locates the "inner" *keres* at the entry end rather than the far end of the organ.

### EY Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הַכָּרֵס כּוּלּוֹ – זוֹ הִיא כָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִית, וְאֵיזֶהוּ כָּרֵס הַחִיצוֹן? בָּשָׂר הַחוֹפֶה אֶת רוּב הַכָּרֵס. רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מַפְרַעְתָּה. מַאי מַפְרַעְתָּה? אָמַר רַב אַוְיָא: הֵיכָא דְּפָרְעִי טַבָּחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they said in the name of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina: The entire rumen is the internal rumen. And** if so, **which is the external rumen?** It is the **flesh that envelops the majority of the rumen** on the underside of the animal. **Rabba bar Rav Huna says:** The internal rumen is the ***mafrata*.** The Gemara asks: **What is the *mafrata*? Rav Avya said:** It is **the place that the butcher exposes** when he opens up the animal, i.e., the underside of the stomach.

The position of *eretz yisrael*, transmitted by Rav Ami in the name of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina, is the most sweeping: *kol ha-keres kulo zo hi keres ha-pnimit* — the entire *keres* in its entirety is the inner *keres*, where even *mashehu* is a *treifa*. If so, what then is the outer *keres* that requires *rov*? *basar ha-chofeh et rov ha-keres* — the flesh that covers the majority of the *keres*. This is not the *keres* itself but the abdominal muscles running underneath it. These muscles extend across the underside of the *keres* — not beneath the entire organ, only beneath the majority of it — and a tear in those muscles requires a *rov* before it constitutes a *treifa*. The *keres* proper, on this view, is entirely inner and maximally stringent; only the external musculature receives the more lenient *rov* standard.

This is a strikingly *machmir* position: it means any hole anywhere in the *keres* is a problem, and the leniency of *rov* applies only to the abdominal muscles outside the *keres* entirely. The location of these abdominal muscles can be seen clearly in butchering context — they run along the underside of the carcass and are visible once the animal is opened.

### Exposure Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא עָבְדִי כְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: כֹּל הָנֵי שְׁמַעְתָּתָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כּוּלְּהוּ שָׁיְיכָן בִּדְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: **In Neharde'a, they practiced in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba bar Rav Huna. Rav Ashi said to Ameimar,** one of the Sages of Neharde'a: **What** about **all these** other **statements,** that the internal rumen is another part of the rumen? Ameimar **said to him: They are all included in** the definition **of** the rumen according to **Rabba bar Rav Huna.** The cecum, the opening of the stomach, and the place with no wool all face the underside of the stomach, i.e., the *mafrata*.

*Amar Rabba bar Rav Huna: mafrata* — the demarcation is where it is torn open. Rav Avia explains: *heicha de-pro atvacha* — where the butchers tear open the animal when they begin the slaughtering and processing. When the carcass is hung and cut down the middle, whatever area is immediately exposed constitutes the inner *keres*, while what remains concealed beneath the full rib cage is the outer *keres*. This criterion aligns naturally with the *milat* definition: the area under the full rib cage, sheltered by the ribs, is not exposed when the animal is opened and corresponds to the area with the felt-like texture.

In Nahardea they ruled according to Rabba bar Rav Huna — that is, the inner *keres* is defined by what is exposed during butchering.

### Superset

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי אֲמַר לֵיהּ? כְּבָר פֵּירְשָׁה רַב אַחָה בַּר עַוָּא. וּדְרַבִּי אֲבִינָא וְדִבְנֵי מַעְרְבָא, מַאי אֲמַר לֵיהּ? הָנֵי וַדַּאי פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi replied: **And what** about the opinion **that Rav Asi says** in the name of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** that the internal rumen is an unknown narrow area? Shouldn't the entire rumen be treated as the internal rumen? Ameimar **said to him: Rav Aḥa bar Ava already explained** that **it** is the area extending from the narrow place until the gullet. This area is also included in the *mafrata*. Rav Ashi asked further: **And what** of the opinion **of Rabbi Avina,** that the internal rumen is the handbreadth near the gullet, **and of the residents of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, that it is the entire rumen? Ameimar **said to him: These** opinions **certainly disagree** with that of Rabba bar Rav Huna, but we do not follow them.

Rav Ashi asked Ameimar — who was from Nahardea — *kol hani shema'ateta de-mai?* — what do you do with all those other opinions? The question seems to express surprise that Nahardea would rule like a single position when the matter was so extensively debated. Ameimar replied: *kulhu shaychan be-de-Rabba bar Rav Huna* — they are all subsumed within Rabba bar Rav Huna's criterion. That is, all the other definitions — the narrowing, the area without felt-like texture, the cecum, and so forth — are included within the area exposed during butchering. The exposure test is the broadest definition of inner *keres*, a superset encompassing all the other definitions, and therefore the most *machmir* position among those that still distinguish inner from outer.

Rav Ashi pushes further: and what about the positions of Rav Assi (narrowing) and Rav Avina (near the esophagus) and the position of *bnei Ma'arava* (entire *keres* plus only the abdominal muscles as outer)? Ameimar concedes: *hani vadai pligi* — those clearly disagree with how we rule. The position of *bnei Ma'arava* in particular — where the entire *keres* is inner and the outer is only the external muscles — is distinct from and more stringent than Rabba bar Rav Huna in one direction, while Rabbi Avina's placement near the esophagus is entirely different. The Nahardean practice does not encompass all opinions but does encompass most.

One might ask whether this diversity of practice implies that there was no uniform standard of *kashrut* throughout the Talmudic period. The question is apt: until the *gemara* established *hilchata* rulings, and those rulings were very late indeed — associated with Rav Ashi and Ameimar — wide regional variation existed. *treifos* are matters that only arise when the situation actually presents itself, so such variation would have had practical consequences only when a particular question came before a given authority. The *gemara* clearly tolerated a range of practice while simultaneously striving toward a definitive ruling.

### Size Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּגְדוֹלָה כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא ״גְּדוֹלָה״ – גְּדוֹלָה מַמָּשׁ, וְלֹא ״קְטַנָּה״ – קְטַנָּה מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁנִּקְרַע בָּהּ טֶפַח וְלָא הָוֵי רוּבָּא – זוֹ הִיא שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: ״בִּגְדוֹלָה טֶפַח״, רוּבָּא וְלָא הָוֵי טֶפַח – זוֹ הִיא שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: ״בִּקְטַנָּה בְּרוּבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to a tear in the external rumen, for **a large** animal, a tear of one handbreadth renders it a *tereifa*, while for a small animal, it is a *tereifa* only if most of it was torn. **Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** The word: **Large,** is **not** referring **literally** to **a large** animal, and the word: **Small,** is **not** referring **literally** to **a small** animal. **Rather,** concerning **any** animal large enough that **its** external rumen can be **torn one handbreadth and** this **does not constitute most** of it, **this is what we learned:** For **a large** animal, a tear of **one handbreadth** renders it a *tereifa*. And concerning any animal small enough that **most** of its external rumen can be torn **and** this does **not constitute one handbreadth, this is what we learned: For a small** animal, it is a *tereifa* only if **most** of it was torn. In other words, any external rumen torn either one handbreadth or in its majority renders the animal a *tereifa*.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer bigdola* — Rabbi Yehuda says: for a large animal, a *tefach* tear in the outer *keres* renders it a *treifa*; for a small animal, the majority (*rov*). Rav Binyamin bar Yephet in the name of Rabbi Elazar explains: "large" and "small" are not absolute terms referring to the physical size of the animal per se. Rather, *lo gdola gdola mamash velo ktana ktana mamash* — "large" means an animal whose outer *keres* is large enough that a *tefach* tear does not yet constitute a majority, so that a *tefach* is the earlier threshold; "small" means an animal whose outer *keres* is small enough that the majority is reached before one achieves a *tefach*. The practical upshot: *rov* or *tefach*, whichever comes first, is the measure of *treifa*. For a large-stomached animal the smaller threshold will be the *tefach*; for a small-stomached animal the smaller threshold will be the *rov*.

### Majority Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוּבָּא וְלָא הָוֵי טֶפַח, פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּהָוְיָא טֶפַח בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: עַד דְּמִיקְּרַע בַּהּ טֶפַח לָא הָוֵי טְרֵפָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If **most** of the external rumen is torn **but** it does **not constitute a handbreadth,** isn't it **obvious** that the animal is rendered a *tereifa*? There is a principle that the majority of an entity is considered like all of it. The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this for a case **where** the tear would **constitute a handbreadth with any amount** more than a majority, i.e., the external rumen is slightly smaller than two handbreadths. **Lest you say** that in such a case, the animal **is not a *tereifa* until a handbreadth of it is torn,** this *halakha* **teaches us** that this is not so. Rather, if the majority of the rumen is torn, the animal is a *tereifa*, even if this constitutes less than a handbreadth.

The Gemara objects: *ruba velo havei tefach, peshita* — if we are dealing with a case where the *rov* is torn and it has not yet reached a *tefach*, is that not obvious? Once we have established that any tear is a problem (which is the premise of these discussions), surely a majority of the organ is problematic. The Gemara answers: *lo tzricha dehavei tefach b'mashehu* — the case that requires teaching is one where a majority has been torn, and the addition of just a tiny bit more (*mashehu*) would bring it to a *tefach*. One might have thought to insist on waiting for the full *tefach* before declaring a *treifa*, and the Mishnah teaches that no — we go by *rov*, and majority suffices. Tosafot questions this *havah aminah* as strange — if majority always matters, why would we ever have thought to demand more? — and offers an alternative reading of the Gemara, though the discussion here stays with Rashi's understanding.

### Sela Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר גְּנִיבָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: נִקְדְּרָה כְּסֶלַע – טְרֵפָה, שֶׁאִם תִּמָּתַח תַּעֲמוֹד עַל הַטֶּפַח. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דִּגְנִיבָא אַמַּבָּרָא דִּנְהַרְדְּעָא – כְּסֶלַע כְּשֵׁרָה, יָתֵר מִכְּסֶלַע טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Geneiva says** that **Rabbi Asi says:** If a round hole was **bored** through the external rumen **as** large as **a *sela*** coin, the animal is **a *tereifa*, since if** a hole of such size **will be stretched, it will reach the** length of a **handbreadth. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said:** This matter **was explained to me** personally **by Geneiva** himself, when we were **on the ferry of Neharde'a:** If the hole is exactly as large **as a *sela*,** the animal is **kosher.** If the size of the hole is **greater than a *sela*,** it is **a *tereifa*.**

*Amar Gneva amar Rav Assi: niktra k'sela, treifa* — if a window-like hole (*niktra* implies a shaped, cut-out perforation rather than a mere puncture) the size of a *sela* coin is made in the outer *keres*, the animal is a *treifa*.

### Circle Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה יָתֵר מִכְּסֶלַע? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּגוֹן דְּעָיְילָן תְּלָת קַשְׁיָיתָא בְּצִיפָּא בְּדוּחְקָא, בְּלָא צִיפָּא בְּרַוְוחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is considered **greater than a *sela*? Rav Yosef said: For example,** if **three** date **pits covered** in remains of the date can fit through the hole **with difficulty,** or if, **without** being **covered** in any remains, they can fit **comfortably,** this is considered a hole larger than a *sela*.

The relationship between the *sela* and the *tefach* standard is then explained. The *tefach* standard for a straight tear means a tear of one handbreadth in a straight line. A circular hole the size of a *sela* — meaning a circle whose circumference equals a *tefach* — is the equivalent, since the circumference of a circle the size of a *sela* is a *tefach*. Therefore such a hole constitutes the required threshold. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba adds that this was explained to him directly in the name of Gneva by the bridge of Nahardea: exactly the size of a *sela* is *kasher*, even slightly larger is *treifa* (*k'sela kesheira, yeter mik'sela treifa*). The practical question is then: how much is "a little bit larger"?

### Date Pit Test


*Amar Rav Yosef: kegon d'ayel tlat kashyata betzipa beducheka* — it is like a hole through which three date pits, with the thin layer of date flesh still attached, can be inserted tightly. If three date pits with their flesh can be pushed through the hole under pressure, the hole is large enough to be *treifa*. Conversely, *b'lo tzipa b'revach* — if three bare date pits without any flesh can pass through easily and with room to spare, that too is a hole of sufficient size to constitute a *treifa*. This concludes the daf.
