# Niddah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zera L'vatala

### Hand Cutting: Law or Cur…

The Gemara on 13b, approximately twenty lines into the page, concludes its extended discussion of *zera l'vatala* with a pointed question about the precise legal weight of the phrase appearing in the Mishnah: that the hand of a man who acts improperly "should be cut off." The Gemara asks directly — *dina tenan o litusa tenan*? Is this literally the law, or is it a curse?

The case for literalism draws on a tradition concerning Rav Huna, who is reported to have actually severed someone's hand. The Gemara in Sanhedrin similarly speaks of one who raises his hand against his fellow as deserving to have it cut off, and there too Rav Huna is said to have carried out such a punishment. The possibility is therefore not entirely without precedent, though it strains credulity.

### Rabbi Tarfon's Teaching

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* to probe the question further. Rabbi Tarfon teaches: a hand that a man brings to his genitals should be cut off; a hand brought as far as the navel should be cut off. The rabbis object to this formulation — if you cut off the hand at the navel, you will cut open his stomach. Rabbi Tarfon responds: *mutav tibakei kreso ve'al yered li've'er shachat* — better that the stomach be rent open than that he descend into the netherworld.

The Gemara then analyzes this exchange. If the teaching is literally legal, then the concern about cutting open the stomach is intelligible — you are actually positioning a blade. But if it is only a curse, what does it mean to worry about the stomach being pierced? There would be no blade. The Gemara therefore leans toward literalism on the basis of this internal logic. But then a further difficulty arises: why would it be important that the hand be cut off specifically at the navel? What is the legal significance of that particular location?

### Figurative Interpretation

The Gemara resolves this by reinterpreting Rabbi Tarfon's statement altogether. What Rabbi Tarfon means is not that the hand should be cut off *at* the navel, but that any man who lowers his hand *below* the navel should be deserving of having it cut off — and even this, the Gemara now reads as figurative, as a severe expression of condemnation rather than a literal surgical penalty.

The subsequent exchange reinforces this reading. The rabbis ask Rabbi Tarfon: what if a man has a splinter or thorn lodged below his navel — a legitimate medical need? Should he really not touch himself there? Rabbi Tarfon answers: he should not. But won't that cause his stomach to burst? — meaning, won't it lead to serious harm? Rabbi Tarfon's reply is again *mutav tibakei kreso* — even a medical risk is preferable to descending into the depths. This is striking, and the Gemara acknowledges as much. We were not inclined to read the original statement literally in the first place, so the conclusion that it is figurative is hardly a great consolation. The discussion yesterday was equally dramatic in its exhortations — better that one's children be considered *mamzeirim* than that a man touch himself, even incidentally. All of these fall under the category of hyperbolic condemnation, *ke'ilu*, designed to express the gravity of the transgression rather than to prescribe a literal legal consequence.

The Gemara stops short of a definitive resolution. The preferred reading is clearly figurative, but the question is left somewhat open. The sense one gets from the Gemara's structure — introducing a possibility and then retreating from it — is that it favors the figurative reading, and the precedent of the verse in Deuteronomy about the woman who grabs a man's genitals, whose "hand" the Gemara treats figuratively, supports this. But it remains ambiguous.

### Religious Weight & Halac…

Beyond the narrow question of literal versus figurative, the broader question of the halachic and religious weight of *zera l'vatala* deserves attention. The Gemara employs a range of vivid rhetorical formulations in this context — one who is wasteful of seed is deserving of a curse, compared to one who sheds blood, described as producing children of the flood. These formulations parallel other Talmudic hyperboles: one who gets angry is *ke'ilu oved avodah zarah*; one who has *ga'avah* is *ke'ilu oved avodah zarah*; one who does not engage in procreation is *ke'ilu shofech damim*. These are not merely empty expressions — they tap into the nature of the underlying concern. Not engaging in *periyah ve'riviyah* is *ke'ilu shofech damim* because one is withholding life from the world. The hyperbole is purposeful, but it remains *ke'ilu*, "as if," and does not generate literal legal consequences.

The technical question of whether the prohibition of *zera l'vatala* is *de'oraita* or *de'rabbanan* is genuinely unresolved among the *poskim*. Since the Gemara invokes the stories of Er and Onan and uses such weighty language, some assume a biblical prohibition. But the Gemara never identifies a *pasuk* that says "you shall not do this," and many *poskim* conclude accordingly that it is only rabbinic. Beyond the technical question, however, the *religious* weight assigned to this prohibition has varied dramatically across Jewish history. Among the Kabbalists and the *Chasidim* this was treated as perhaps the gravest sin a man could commit — the *Zohar* is understood by some to regard it as more severe than illicit relations with a non-Jewish woman. The conceptual underpinning is that semen carries unformed life, and to waste it is to give birth, in effect, to evil spirits, to *shedim*, souls that have not reached their proper destination. If one reads the *viduim* associated with Yom Kippur, the emphasis placed on *zera l'vatala* is striking.

This creates serious tension, particularly in the contemporary context. The Gemara itself advised early marriage — at fifteen or seventeen — precisely because of concerns about *hirhurim* and temptation. With later marriage now the norm, the practical and religious pressures involved become acute. The question also arises, as was noted the previous day, in the context of medical procedures requiring semen collection for fertility purposes — even when the purpose is entirely licit, the anxiety surrounding the act remains substantial. The question of how much of this weight reflects authentic Talmudic sensibility, and how much derives from Christian ascetic influence mediated through the medieval period, has been taken up by scholars such as David Biale in his work on Eros and Jewish culture. The *Bavli* itself tends to be overwhelmingly positive about marital sexuality; the anxiety intensifies among the *Rishonim*, and questions of cultural cross-pollination are legitimate, particularly regarding the *Chasidim*, whose conceptual framework here likely developed in an environment where Christian asceticism was prominent. These questions cannot be resolved here, but they are worth raising as the Gemara closes this topic.

## Women & Diminished Capac…

### Categories & Definitions

The Gemara now turns from *zera l'vatala* to the categories of women whose ability to perform self-examination for *niddah* status cannot be fully relied upon. The Mishnah lists: the *chereshet*, the deaf or deaf-mute woman; the *shotah*, the woman of unsound mind; and the *sumah*, the blind woman — as well as *she'nitrefa da'atah*, a woman whose mental state has become confused.

The structure of the Mishnah's remedy is notable. If such women have *pekichot*, companions of sound mind — *bar da'as* — those companions can arrange and perform the necessary examination on their behalf and ensure they immerse properly, thereby enabling them to eat *terumah* if they are wives or daughters of *kohanim*. As Rashi makes clear, *metaknot otan bodkot otan* — the friends do the actual internal check — and *matbilin otan* — they accompany them to the *mikveh*. These women cannot be trusted to execute any step of the process correctly on their own, but through their friends they can be integrated into the normative framework of *taharah*.

One might note what is conspicuously absent from this list: *ketanot*, minor girls. The Gemara had previously addressed them in the context of *mesuvakot damim* and established that their neighbors check them — a *baraita* on 13b makes this explicit. The omission from this Mishnah is interesting and raises the broader question of whether *bar da'as* is a formal legal category or simply a practical inquiry into whether a particular individual can be trusted to perform the task reliably.

### Intelligent Deaf-Mute

The Gemara challenges the inclusion of the *chereshet* in this category. A *chereshet*, the Gemara asserts, *tibdok le'atzma* — she can check herself. In support, a *baraita* is cited in the name of Rabbi, who reports: "There was a *chereshet* in our neighborhood. Not only did she check herself, but her friends would bring her their questions about stains and spots and she would rule on them." This is a remarkable passage — here we have not only a woman's voice entering a halachic context, but a woman functioning as a neighborhood *posekes*, ruling for others in matters of *niddah*. And she was a *chereshet*.

The implication is clear: a deaf woman is fully of sound mind. Her inability to hear does not affect her legal or cognitive status. The Gemara confirms this explicitly.

### Cheresh Definition

The Gemara then defines the precise category of *cheresh* to which the Mishnah refers. *Hatam be'medabberet ve'eina shoma'at* — the case of the woman who is deaf but can speak falls outside the problematic category entirely, and her halachic status is unaffected. The *chereshet* whose status is diminished is specifically *she'eina medabberet ve'eina shoma'at* — one who can neither speak nor hear. This is grounded in a general Mishnaic principle: whenever the *chachamim* speak of a *cheresh* as a person of diminished legal status, they mean one who can neither hear nor speak.

The reasoning is as follows: if a person cannot hear and cannot speak, the assumption — in the absence of alternative means of communication — was that they cannot meaningfully comprehend their surroundings. The inability to communicate intelligibly was taken as evidence of cognitive inaccessibility. This was the reality in an era without sign language education, without literacy as a common alternative channel. The Gemara does, however, acknowledge a *cheresh ha'medabber mitoch ha'ketav* — a deaf-mute who communicates through writing — as a category that alters the legal status. Nowadays, with sign language and modern education making it evident that deaf individuals are fully intelligent and capable, there is significant discussion among *poskim* about whether such individuals retain the status of *lav bar da'as*. The strong inclination is toward saying they do not. But that is a larger discussion.

### Blind Woman's Role

The Gemara similarly questions the inclusion of the blind woman. Surely she can perform the physical internal examination herself and then show the cloth to her companions for visual inspection. The Gemara accepts this reasoning. Rav Yosef b'Rabbi Chanina states plainly: *sumah einah ba'mishna* — the blind woman does not belong in the Mishnah. Cross her out. The Mishnah apparently included her in error, or perhaps at one stage the understanding was that even the visual check of the cloth was considered part of what she herself had to perform.

### Temporary vs. Permanent

The Mishnah's fourth category — *she'nitrefa da'atah* — raises the question of its relationship to *shotah*. The Gemara distinguishes them clearly. A *shotah* is someone in a permanent state of mental impairment, who is never of sound mind. *She'nitrefa da'atah* refers to someone who is currently in the midst of a temporary state of acute mental confusion — perhaps a psychotic episode or a state of delirium brought on by illness, *machmat choli*. It is a transient condition, not an enduring one. This distinction has practical importance: a woman who is normally of sound mind but is temporarily confused falls into this special category and requires her friends' assistance only during that period.

## Impaired Kohen & Terumah

### Night Eating Protocol

The Gemara now introduces a parallel scenario involving a male: the *kohen shoteh*, an impaired *kohen* who wishes to eat *terumah*. The practical challenge is significant. Such a *kohen* cannot be trusted to monitor his own state of purity, and there is an ever-present concern that he has experienced a seminal emission — *ba'al keri* — rendering him *tamei* and disqualifying him from *terumah*. The Gemara's solution is communal oversight: *matbilin oto* — others immerse him in the *mikveh*; *machilin oto terumah la'erev* — he is fed *terumah* that evening, after sunset, which is when *terumah* becomes permitted following immersion; *umeshammerin oto shelo yishan* — and they watch over him to ensure he does not fall asleep.

The logic is that once he sleeps, there is an automatic presumption of *tum'ah* — *yashan, tamei; lo yashan, tahor*. As long as he has not slept since his immersion, he remains *tahor* and may eat. The comparison to the situation of the women is instructive. The intrusion here is less immediate — there is no physical internal examination — but the assumption of *tum'ah* the moment he sleeps is more automatic. The trade-off is easier remediation: a man requires only immersion and the passage of the day to become *tahor* again, unlike a woman who requires seven clean days.

### Pouch Solutions

Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok offers an alternative: *osin lo kis shel or* — fashion for him a leather pouch or cup worn over the genital area, so that any seminal emission during sleep would be detectable upon waking. This would allow him to sleep without the automatic presumption of *tum'ah*.

### Garment Concerns

The rabbis object: *kol sheken shemevi lidei himum* — a tight-fitting garment will cause sexual arousal, which will increase rather than decrease the likelihood of seminal emission. Moreover, a leather pouch may absorb a small amount of semen without making it detectable, creating a false presumption of *taharah*. The objectors' concern is that even a drop *ke'hardal* — the size of a mustard seed, proverbially infinitesimal — could be absorbed and missed.

Rabbi Eliezer's response, cited by a *tanna*, is to substitute metal for leather: *osin lo kis shel matechet* — a metal vessel would not absorb. Abaye specifies: *uveshel nechoshet* — it should be copper. The reason copper is specified becomes clear from a *baraita* cited in a related context: Rabbi Yehuda, discussing the minimum *shiur* for the sprinkling of *parah adumah* waters on the tips of hyssop, says we look at the hyssop tips *ke'ilu hen shel nechoshet* — as if they were made of copper — meaning non-absorbent. Copper is thus the Gemara's standard for a non-absorbent material, which is precisely what is needed here so that no emission goes undetected.

The practical objection that underlies this whole discussion is then generalized by Rav Pappa: *michnasayim asurim* — underwear is forbidden, because tight-fitting garments cause the very sexual stimulation that leads to *zera l'vatala*.

### Priestly Garment Design

The Gemara immediately raises a counter-objection from the Torah itself: does the verse not say *ve'aseh lahem michnesei vad lechasos besar ervah* — and you shall make for them linen breeches to cover their nakedness? The *kohanim* wore underwear! The resolution comes from a *baraita* describing the priestly garments in detail. *Michnesei kohanim* were designed specifically to avoid any form-fitting quality: they extended from the loins above down to the thighs below, resembling the broad skirts worn by charioteers — large, loose, effectively enormous bloomers or boxer shorts. They had drawstrings rather than elastic or snug waistbands, and crucially they had no opening in front for urination and no fitted opening at the back. The entire design ensured the garment would not press against or stimulate the genital area. It is, in passing, an interesting question whether contemporary underwear meets such a standard.

## Activity & Purity

### Riders & Sailors

The Gemara now addresses the broader question of which physical activities or occupations create heightened concern for *keri*. Abaye opens with a ruling at the top of 14a: *rochvei gemalim asurim le'echol biterumah* — those who ride camels may not eat *terumah*, because the constant rubbing against the camel's back — more pronounced than with horses, and more directly skin-to-surface in the absence of a proper saddle — creates an ongoing concern for seminal emission. A *baraita* states bluntly: *rochvei gemalim kulam resha'im* — camel riders are all wicked, because they are all concerned about *keri*.

The contrast with sailors is striking: *ha'sapanim kulam tzaddikim* — ship sailors are all righteous. But the reason has nothing to do with sexual probity. Sailors, always in danger at sea, are constantly making vows and calling out to God — like the story of Jonah. Their righteousness is a function of existential peril, not of virtue.

### Donkey Riders

Donkey riders occupy an intermediate position: *mehem resha'im umehem tzaddikim* — among them are wicked ones and righteous ones, like people generally. There is no blanket categorization. The Gemara offers two alternative explanations for what distinguishes the righteous donkey riders from the wicked ones. One view holds that it depends on whether they use saddles (*machaf*) or not: a saddle reduces the direct friction that leads to *keri*, so a saddled rider is a *tzaddik*, and an unsaddled one is a *rasha*. Another view holds that the distinction turns on riding position: straddling (*mitartin*, riding split-legged) creates the problematic friction, while riding sidesaddle avoids it. Though sidesaddle is arguably less stable, particularly on steep terrain, for the slow-moving and sure-footed donkey the risk may have been deemed acceptable.

### Back-Sleeping Curse

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi would curse those who sleep *aparkid* — on their back. The Aramaic term is generally understood to mean supine, though some suggest it could mean prone. Either way, the concern is that such a sleeping position increases the risk of an erection and subsequent *keri*. Rashi offers several reasons: lying on the back makes an erection visible, which is itself shameful; lying prone causes genital friction against the bedding; and the lower spine pressing against the bed may somehow stimulate arousal. Additionally, Rashi notes that lying on the back gives the hands easier unconscious access to the genital area, such that even unintentional rubbing during sleep could cause an erection.

### Shema & Position

The Gemara introduces a related but distinct ruling from Rav Yosef: *aparkid lo yikra keri'at shema* — one lying flat on his back should not recite *Shema*, as it is not a posture of respect. The Gemara then carefully distinguishes the two prohibitions. For the sleeping concern, if one is slightly tilted — *ki matzli* — it is sufficient, and sleeping in that position is permissible. For *keri'at shema*, however, even a slight tilt is insufficient; one must be substantially on one's side to recite *Shema* appropriately, unless lying fully on one's side or effectively sitting up.

Rabbi Yochanan is cited as an exception: he would incline slightly and recite *Shema*. The Gemara explains: *de-ba'al basar havah* — Rabbi Yochanan was very heavy, and it was therefore difficult for him to sit upright or turn fully on his side. So he was treated leniently. One who is ill or hospitalized similarly would be treated leniently. The underlying principle is that these rules are not absolute but respond to physical circumstances. The point about the *Shema* and the word *be'shokbekha* — which might seem to imply lying specifically on one's back — is resolved by noting that the ancient reclining posture was more of a lateral inclination than a true supine position.

## Post-Coital Examination

### Two-Cloth Custom

The Gemara transitions to the Mishnah's account of post-coital examination. The Mishnah states: *derech benot Yisrael mishamshos bishnei edim* — the practice of Jewish women is to have relations with two cloths, one for him and one for her, wiping themselves after intercourse in order to check for blood. The *tzenu'ot*, the particularly scrupulous women, would have a third cloth used beforehand to prepare and check prior to intercourse.

This raises a tension internal to the Mishnah itself. The earlier Mishnah stated that women check *keshehen holchot leshamesh et hamittah* — before intercourse — as a requirement. Now the post-coital check is presented as normative practice and the pre-coital check as something only the *tzenu'ot* do. The Gemara reconciles this by understanding that both before and after are required, at least in the context of *asukat betaharot*, where a woman is regularly handling *taharot*, and that the Mishnah's phrasing about *tzenu'ot* refers to something else — perhaps refraining from reusing the same cloth. The tension between what is strictly required and what is commendable remains somewhat unresolved.

### Husband's Cloth

If blood is found on his cloth — the cloth with which the husband wiped himself — *teme'im ve'chayavin be'korban*. They are both definitively *tamei* and obligated to bring a *korban*, because there is no other plausible explanation for how uterine blood came to be on his genital area. They were having relations while she was a *niddah*, which carries the prohibition of *karet*, and both parties are therefore obligated in a *korban*.

The question arises: if she had no reason to believe she was a *niddah* — if her *veset kavua* was not due and she had checked beforehand — why is this a *shogeg* that obligates a *korban* rather than an *ones*? This question touches on a broader principle discussed at greater length in *Krisos*: the obligation of a *korban chatat* is not contingent on moral fault but on the metaphysical reality of a sin having occurred. The Gemara in *Shabbos* illustrates this with the *tinok she'nishba bein ha'goyim*, a person raised among non-Jews who never knew he was Jewish and discovers his identity as an adult. He is still obligated in a *korban* for violations committed in complete innocence, because the sin exists as a fact in the world that requires rectification, and he is the one who committed it. Applied here: even if the couple was entirely without fault, the violation occurred and generates a *korban* obligation.

### Wife's Cloth Timing

If blood is found on her cloth, the halachic consequence depends on timing. If she checks *lehavdil* — immediately, meaning within the time it takes to descend from the bed and wash the pubic area — the presumption is that the blood was present during intercourse, and she is definitively *tamei*, with a *korban* obligation for both. If blood is found *le'achar zeman*, after that brief window, they are *teme'im misafek* — doubtfully *tamei* — and are exempt from the *korban* because the sin is not established with certainty, though both are *tamei* on account of the genuine possibility.

After this brief window passes, the rabbinic *me'et le'et* — the twenty-four hour retroactive presumption — applies. In that range it is rabbinic *tum'ah* only, and crucially *eino metamei et bo'alah* — the husband is not rendered *tamei* in that rabbinic range; only she is. Rabbi Akiva dissents: *af metamei et bo'alah* — he maintains that the husband is *tamei* in all cases.

The Gemara then notes that the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Akiva in one specific scenario: *ro'ah ketem*. When a stain (*ketem*) is found rather than a direct internal finding, the husband is rendered *tamei*. This seems counterintuitive — *ketamim* are generally more lenient, being rabbinic — but the explanation lies in temporal uncertainty. If a woman performs an internal check ten minutes after intercourse and finds blood, it is likely the blood was not present during intercourse, because had it been, she would probably have felt or noticed it internally. But if blood is found on a cloth or bedspread, it could easily have been there much earlier. Moreover, *ketamim* are traced back not twenty-four hours but to the last time the garment or cloth was laundered, which may be considerably longer. So while *ketamim* are more lenient in some respects, they carry greater temporal reach in others, and that broader temporal reach leads to stricter treatment regarding the husband's *tum'ah*.

## Blood Source Determinati…

### Louse Blood Concern

The Gemara now raises a fundamental challenge to the entire framework of examination cloths: *ve'nichush dilma dam ma'acholet hu* — perhaps the blood found on the cloth came from a crushed louse rather than from the woman. Ancient society was not highly hygienic, and lice were a common reality. If so, the presumption of *niddah* status rests on uncertain ground.

### Lice-Free Area

Rav Zeira resolves this: *oto makom baduk hu etzel ma'acholet* — that particular area, the pubic region, is established as free of lice. There are two versions of the underlying reasoning. One holds that lice simply are never found there — it is established as a lice-free zone. The other holds that while it is not impossible for lice to be present, the area is too constricted for them to exist there: *sachif dechika lei lema'acholet* — it is too tight for lice.

The practical difference between these two positions emerges in a specific case: what if a louse is actually found, crushed, on the examination cloth? According to the view that lice simply are never there, the presence of a crushed louse is puzzling — presumably it arrived after the cloth was removed from the body, perhaps landing on it subsequently, and the blood therefore cannot be attributed to it. According to the view that it is merely difficult but not impossible for lice to be present, a crushed louse on the cloth is plausible evidence that a louse was indeed there and was crushed during the examination, and the blood may be attributed to it rather than to menstruation.

### Thigh Blood Case

A further case: *badka be'eid ha'baduk lah* — she checked with a cloth that had been verified as clean, then inadvertently touched the cloth to her thigh, and the following day found blood on her thigh. What is the ruling?

Rav rules: *teme'iah niddah* — she is definitively a *niddah*. The most plausible explanation is that blood from the internal examination transferred from the cloth to the thigh when it was touched there, and therefore the blood originated from her body during the examination. Rabbi Shimi bar Chiyya objects: you previously told us that in such a case she is only *chosheshes* — she is only doubtfully *tamei*, not definitively so.

The Gemara reports that Shmuel and the practice of the *beit midrash* aligned with the ruling of definite *tum'ah* — *teme'iah niddah* — which suggests that the question was not purely theoretical; it must have been a relatively common occurrence to have generated explicit teaching in the study house.

## Unchecked Cloth Debate

### Box Scenario

The Gemara moves to a closely related case: a woman checks with a cloth that had *not* been verified as clean beforehand — *eid she'eino baduk lah* — and then places it in a sealed box. The next day she opens the box and finds blood. The question is whether the blood is attributable to the woman or whether it may have been on the cloth from before the examination, perhaps from a louse.

### Rabbi Chiyya's Shift

Rav Yosef reports: *kol yamav shel Rabbi Chiyya timma* — throughout his life, Rabbi Chiyya ruled in this case that the woman is *tamei*. But *ul'eis ziknuso tihar* — in his old age he softened and ruled *tahor*. The Gemara asks: what exactly was the trajectory? Did he begin by declaring her definitively *tamei* as a *niddah* and end by declaring her completely *tahor*? Or did he begin by treating it as a *ketem* — rabbinic *tum'ah* — and later move to complete leniency?

A *baraita* is cited to resolve the question: in exactly this case — an unchecked cloth left in a box — *Rabbi omer teme'iah mishum niddah ve'Rabbi Chiyya omer teme'iah mishum ketem*. This is contemporaneous testimony. Rebbi holds definite biblical *niddah* status; Rabbi Chiyya holds only rabbinic *ketem* status. Since Rabbi Chiyya and Rebbi are debating as contemporaries and colleagues, this represents Rabbi Chiyya's position at the time of his engagement with Rebbi — presumably his older years, since he would not have argued as a colleague with Rebbi while still a junior student.

### Rebbi's Position

Rebbi's reasoning is then unpacked through a dialogue. Rabbi Chiyya challenges Rebbi: do you not admit that in this case the stain must be at least the size of a *gris* — a lentil bean — before she is *tamei*? That is a law of *ketamim*, not of *niddah*. If you require the size of a *gris* to rule *tamei*, you are yourself treating it as a *ketem*, not as *niddah*. How then do you say *niddah*?

Rebbi responds: yes, I require the size of a *gris* — but only *le'afokei midmei macholes*, to eliminate the possibility that the blood came from a louse. Once it is large enough to exclude louse blood, *vadai migufa asei* — it definitively came from her body. And since it was found on a *bedikah* cloth rather than a sheet or garment, once louse blood is excluded, I place it back into the *niddah* category, not the *ketem* category.

### Middle Category

This produces an interesting intermediate halachic category. Normally a *bedikah* cloth is subject to biblical *niddah* status even for the tiniest drop of blood, while a stain on clothing or bedding is rabbinic and requires the minimum size of a *gris*. Here, because the cloth was not pre-checked, we cannot entirely exclude the possibility of louse blood, and so we import the *gris* requirement from the laws of *ketamim*. But once that threshold is met and louse blood is excluded, the finding is treated as a definitive *bedikah* result — biblical *niddah* — not merely as a *ketem*. The size requirement is borrowed from *ketem* law, but the consequence, once that threshold is cleared, is full *niddah* status. This is a genuine middle category that does not fit neatly into either standard framework.

## Academy Authority

### Praise & Meeting

The Gemara now presents a brief but illuminating narrative. Rebbi was in the habit of praising Rabbi Chama bar Bisa to Rabbi Ishmael b'Rabbi Yosi — speaking of him as an *adam gadol*, a great figure. Upon hearing this praise, Rabbi Ishmael b'Rabbi Yosi said: the next time he comes to you, bring him to me. When Rabbi Chama bar Bisa arrived and was brought before him, Rabbi Ishmael b'Rabbi Yosi said: ask me a question, and I will demonstrate the quality that was praised.

### Father vs. Rebbi

Rabbi Chama bar Bisa posed the very question just discussed: a woman who checks with an unchecked cloth, places it in a box, and finds blood the next day — what is the ruling? Rabbi Ishmael b'Rabbi Yosi responded: shall I tell you what my father, Rabbi Yosi, said — or what Rebbi said? Rabbi Chama bar Bisa replied: tell me what Rebbi said.

At this, Rabbi Ishmael b'Rabbi Yosi expressed surprise: *zehu she'omrim alav adam gadol hu?* — is *this* the great man they speak of? *Eich manihin divrei harav veshomin divrei hatalmid?* — how can one set aside the words of the teacher and ask to hear the student? Rabbi Yosi was Rebbi's teacher; Rabbi Ishmael is thus implicitly criticizing not only Rabbi Chama bar Bisa's choice but also Rebbi himself, who was being praised, for the slight it implies to Rabbi Yosi's authority.

### Academy Learning

The Gemara offers Rabbi Chama bar Bisa's reasoning. He thought: *Rebbi reish mesivta hava ve'shechichi rabanan kamei umachdedin shemaysesei* — Rebbi was the head of the great academy, the rabbis gathered around him constantly, and through ongoing disputation and collective learning, his positions were sharpened and clarified. Even if Rabbi Yosi was his teacher, the intensity of academy learning hones an opinion in ways that individual study cannot replicate. Rebbi's view, forged in the crucible of the *yeshivah*, may thus be more refined than Rabbi Yosi's formulation. This is a striking institutional claim about how authority and expertise are produced — not solely through lineage or the teacher-student relationship, but through the ongoing dialectical environment of the *beit midrash*.

## Rebbi vs. Yosi

### Blood Attribution

The Gemara now specifies the positions at stake. Rabbah bar bar Chana reports: *Rebbi metamei, Rabbi Yosi metaher* — Rebbi rules *tamei* and Rabbi Yosi rules *tahor* in the case of the unchecked cloth found with blood. Rabbi Zeira adds: *keshe'timme Rebbi*, when Rebbi ruled *tamei*, his position was aligned with that of Rabbi Meir; *ve'kishe'tiher Rabbi Yosi le'atzmo tiher*, and when Rabbi Yosi ruled *tahor*, he was following his own consistent position.

### Meir on Urination

A *baraita* is cited to establish the connection. *Ha'ishah she'hayetah osah tzarakheiha ve'ra'atah dam* — a woman who was urinating and saw blood. Rabbi Meir says: *omedet teme'iah, yoshevet tehorah* — if she was standing, she is *tamei* because the blood is likely exiting from the uterus; if she was sitting, she is *tahor* because the anatomical position allows the blood to be attributed to the urinary tract.

### Yosi's Leniency

Rabbi Yosi says: *bein kach u'vein kach tehorah* — regardless of position she is *tahor*, because he is inclined to attribute the blood to a non-uterine source. The pattern is consistent: Rabbi Yosi, in cases of ambiguity about the blood's origin, attributes it to an alternative source and rules leniently. Rabbi Meir, even when an alternative source is available, tends toward attributing the blood to the uterus and ruling strictly.

### Meir's Strictness

The Gemara then addresses an apparent inconsistency. We are told that Rebbi's ruling aligned with Rabbi Meir's — but Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina states that when Rabbi Meir said *tamei* in the urination case, he meant only *mishum ketem* — rabbinic *tum'ah* as a stain — not biblical *niddah*. Yet Rebbi, in the unchecked cloth case, was saying *tamei mishum niddah* — full biblical *niddah*. How do the two align?

Rav Acha son of Rav Ashi raises this objection before Rav Ashi. The response is that the two positions do in fact line up: Rabbi Meir holds that even when there is an alternative plausible source for the blood, the blood is attributed to the uterus and she is *tamei* — and this, when understood correctly, is a claim of biblical *niddah* status, not merely rabbinic *ketem*. This is what aligns with Rebbi's position in the unchecked cloth case. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, holds that when an alternative source exists, we attribute the blood there and she is *tahor* — or at most subject only to the lenient treatment of a stain. The underlying dispute between Rebbi and Rabbi Yosi, and between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi, is thus a unified one: when multiple possible sources for blood exist, does halacha attribute it to the uterus or to the alternative source, and does that attribution carry biblical or only rabbinic weight?
