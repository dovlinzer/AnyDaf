# Yevamot 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Northern Wind & Chatzot

### Wind Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא נְשִׁיב לְהוּ רוּחַ צְפוֹנִית. דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל אוֹתָן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר לֹא נָשְׁבָה לָהֶם רוּחַ צְפוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that it was **because** the **north wind did not blow for them,** and the hot weather was likely to lead to medical complications following the procedure. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **All those forty years that the Jewish people were in the wilderness,** the **north wind did not blow for them.**

The *sugya* of *he'arel* — the laws governing what an *arel* may and may not eat, and particularly the prohibition against eating *terumah* derived from the *Korban Pesach* — continues on 72a. The discussion has been anchored in the narrative from Yehoshua establishing that *Bnei Yisrael* were *areilim* throughout their years in the *midbar*, and the *Gemara* has attributed this to the danger of performing *brit milah* when the northern wind did not blow. Before returning to the *halacha*, the *Gemara* lingers on an *agadata* that creates an apparent tension.

A *tanu rabanan* teaches: *kol osan arba'im shana shehayu Yisrael bamidbar, lo haya yom shelo nashva bo ruach tzephonit* — throughout all forty years that Israel was in the wilderness, there was no day on which the northern wind did not blow. This seems to directly contradict the earlier teaching that the north wind was absent during those years. The resolution is that the wind did blow, but only *bachatzot halayla* — precisely at midnight. It did not blow during the day with sufficient force to make *brit milah* safe, yet it blew at midnight nonetheless.

### Chatzot Eis Ratzon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל אוֹתָן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר, לֹא הָיָה יוֹם שֶׁלֹּא נָשְׁבָה בּוֹ רוּחַ צְפוֹנִית בַּחֲצִי הַלַּיְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בַּחֲצִי הַלַּיְלָה וַה׳ הִכָּה כׇל בְּכוֹר וְגוֹ׳״ מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּעֵת רְצוֹן מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **All those forty years that the Jewish people were in the wilderness there was not a day in which** the **north wind did not blow at midnight, as it is stated: "And it came to pass at midnight, that the Lord smote all the firstborn** in the land of Egypt" (Exodus 12:29). The Gemara asks: **What is the** biblical **derivation?** How is it derived from this verse that speaks of the exodus from Egypt that a north wind blew at midnight during the forty years that the Jewish people wandered in the wilderness? The Gemara answers: **This** comes to **teach us that a time of favor is a** significant **matter.** Since midnight had once been a time of divine favor at the beginning of the exodus from Egypt, it continued to be a time of favor throughout the forty years that the Jewish people sojourned in the wilderness.

The *Gemara* derives this from the verse *vayehi bachatzot halayla v'Hashem hika kol bechor b'eretz Mitzrayim* — "And it was at midnight, and God smote every firstborn in the land of Egypt." The *Gemara* asks: *mai talmuda* — what is the lesson drawn from this verse? *De'eis ratzon milta hi* — we learn that *chatzot* is a time God regards with special favor, an *eis ratzon*.

This is a striking observation. One might have expected that the midnight plague of the firstborn — a catastrophic act of divine judgment — would mark *chatzot* as an *eis din*, a time of judgment. Yet it is interpreted as an *eis ratzon*, a time of divine favor. The logic is that even that fearsome event was ultimately an expression of redemption for Israel. And so even when the north wind could not blow during the day, the corresponding *eis ratzon* on high found its earthly expression in the wind blowing at midnight.

### Tikkun Chatzot

This connects directly to the practice of *Tikkun Chatzot*. The understanding that night is generally an *eis din* while *chatzot* itself is an *eis ratzon* provides its theological foundation. Rashi references the *Gemara* in Berachot, where the northern wind blowing at midnight caused David Hamelech's harp to play, awakening him at *chatzot* for his *slichot* — which is precisely the wind described in this passage.

The question of how people before the modern era managed to rise at midnight without alarm clocks is itself illuminating. One answer may simply be that without artificial lighting, *chatzot* was understood more loosely as the deep of night. A more compelling answer, however, emerges from recent studies on natural human sleep patterns. When subjects are removed from artificial light and modern time structures, they tend to fall asleep at nightfall, sleep approximately four hours, then experience a natural period of wakefulness lasting roughly two hours, before returning to sleep. The suggestion is that *Tikkun Chatzot* was organically aligned with this natural awakening — a period of wakefulness in the middle of the night that was, in pre-modern life, entirely normal and expected.

## Mashoch Status

### Rav Huna's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, מָשׁוּךְ — אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, וּמִדִּבְרֵיהֶם גָּזְרוּ עָלָיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּעָרֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: By Torah law,** if one had been circumcised, but subsequently the residual foreskin was **drawn forward** by itself or manually so that it covered the corona, he may **partake of *teruma*,** as he is considered circumcised. **However, from the words of** the Sages, **they decreed** that he must be circumcised again **because he looks** as if he were **uncircumcised.**

The *Gemara* returns to *halacha*. Rav Huna states: *devar Torah, mashoch ochel biterumah* — biblically speaking, a *mashoch* — someone who underwent *brit milah* but subsequently reversed it through a procedure that stretches the remaining skin to recreate the appearance of a foreskin — is permitted to eat *terumah*. The foreskin was removed; whatever it looks like, he has had a *brit milah*. *Midivreihem gazru alav mipnei shenireh ke'arel* — the Rabbis, however, enacted a decree forbidding him, because he appears to be an *arel*. The prohibition is therefore *derabbanan*, not *deoraita*.

### Braita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מָשׁוּךְ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיָּמוּל! מִדְּרַבָּנַן. וּדְקָאָרֵי לַהּ, מַאי קָאָרֵי לַהּ? הָא ״צָרִיךְ״ קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on the following *baraita*: One whose residual foreskin was **drawn forward** so that it covers the corona **requires** a second **circumcision,** indicating that he is not considered circumcised. The Gemara explains: This requirement is **by rabbinic law,** and by Torah law he is considered circumcised. The Gemara asks: **And** the *amora* **who asked** this question, **why did he ask it** in the first place? The *baraita* merely **teaches** that such an individual **requires** circumcision, and does not indicate that it is a Torah obligation.

A challenge is immediately raised: *meisiv* — a *braita* states that a *mashoch tzarich sheyimal*, a *mashoch* must have a *brit milah* performed. The *Gemara* notes that this says no more than *derabbanan* — it does not prove a biblical prohibition.

*De-ka-remi mai ka-remi lei* — the *Gemara* expresses surprise: why was this even presented as a challenge? The *braita* does not say the *mashoch* is *assur* in *terumah*; it merely says *tzarich*, he must. *Tzarich* is notably soft language, far from the categorical formulation one would expect for a biblical prohibition.

### Language Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָטָעֵי בְּסֵיפָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִמּוֹל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסַּכָּנָה הִיא לוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא הַרְבֵּה מָלוּ בִּימֵי בֶּן כּוֹזֵיבָא וְהוֹלִידוּ בָּנִים וּבָנוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״, אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים, וְאוֹמֵר: ״אֶת בְּרִיתִי הֵפַר״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמָּשׁוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The *amora* who raised the question **erred due to the latter clause** of that same *baraita*, which states: **Rabbi Yehuda says: He should not be circumcised because it would be dangerous for him** to do so. His colleagues **said to him: But weren't** there **many** who had drawn their residual foreskins forward and subsequently **were circumcised** a second time **in the days of ben Koziva,** otherwise known as bar Kokheva, **and they fathered sons and daughters.** Such re-circumcision is necessary, **as it is stated: "He must surely be circumcised [*himmol yimmol*]"** (Genesis 17:13), the double verb form indicating: **Even one hundred times. And** furthermore, **it says: "He has broken My covenant"** (Genesis 17:14), which comes **to include one** whose foreskin was **drawn forward.**

The *Gemara* explains that the challenge arose not from the opening clause but from the *seifa* — the end of the *braita* — which had made it sound as though the obligation was biblical. Rabbi Yehuda says in the *seifa*: *lo yimal mipnei shesacanah hi lo* — such a person should not have a *brit milah* because it is dangerous. Rashi explains that attempting to circumcise skin that has been stretched risks turning the person into a *kerus shofcha*, causing damage to his genitalia and rendering him unable to have children.

The *amru lo* — the response to Rabbi Yehuda — is that many men underwent this procedure during the days of Ben Koziva and subsequently had children, demonstrating it was not categorically dangerous. The *braita* then invokes the verse *himol yimol* — the doubled language — which is taken to mean the obligation repeats even if the *brit milah* must be performed again, up to *afilu me'ah pe'amim*, even one hundred times. A second verse, *et briti heifar* — "he has violated my covenant" — is cited *lerabbot es hamashoch*, to include someone who has reversed his circumcision as having violated the covenant and being obligated to correct it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הַאי ״הִמּוֹל יִמּוֹל״ לְרַבּוֹת צִיצִין הַמְעַכְּבִים אֶת הַמִּילָה — ״תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אֶת בְּרִיתִי הֵפַר״, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמָּשׁוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **What is** the meaning of: **And** furthermore, **it says?** Why was it necessary to cite two verses in support of the same *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The additional verse is necessary, **lest you say** that **this** first verse: **"He shall surely be circumcised,"** comes only **to include the shreds** of flesh **that invalidate the circumcision** if they are not cut and to indicate that they must be removed. If so, **come** and **hear** a second verse: **"He has broken My covenant,"** which comes **to include one** whose foreskin was **drawn forward.**

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ve-omer* — why was the second verse necessary? Because one might say that *himol yimol* addresses only *tzitzin sheme'akvin*, fragments of foreskin that prevent the *milah* from being complete — meaning the doubled language teaches that one must go back and finish the job, not that a reversed *brit milah* requires repetition. The verse *et briti heifar* is therefore needed separately to include the *mashoch*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא סָבַר: מִדְּקָא נָסֵיב לַהּ תַּלְמוּדָא (קְרָא) — דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא. וְלָא הִיא: מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** Now **he,** the *amora* who raised an objection based on the first part of the *baraita*, **thought** that **since** at the end of the *baraita* the *tanna* **brings a derivation** from **a verse,** this *halakha* must be **by Torah law. But** in fact **that is not so.** It is only **by rabbinic law, and the verse** quoted is **a mere support** but not the source of the *halakha*.

The *Gemara* concludes that the Tannaic author (*hashas*) — an unusual term here applying to the author of the *braita* rather than to the *Mishnah* or *Gemara* — quoted these verses, and this gave the impression of a biblical obligation. In reality, *ve-lav hi mi-derabbanan ve-kra asmachta be-alma* — the prohibition is Rabbinic and the verse serves only as an *asmachta*.

### Historical Context

What emerges incidentally from this passage is a window into historical reality. The reference to *bimei Ben Koziva* — the period of Bar Kokhba — establishes that reversal of circumcision was practiced in two distinct historical contexts: the Hellenistic period and the Bar Kokhba period.

In the Hellenistic period, Jewish men who wished to assimilate into Greek culture sometimes underwent this procedure because the Greeks regarded circumcision as a disfigurement of the body. The *gymnasia*, central to Hellenistic civic and religious life, were venues of athletic competition conducted in the nude, making circumcision visible and socially stigmatizing for those seeking to integrate. In the Bar Kokhba period, the motivations may have been different — sometimes external pressures rather than voluntary assimilation. The fact that the *braita* mentions that many who underwent the re-circumcision during that period subsequently had children suggests that the *braita* addresses both those who reversed the reversal willingly and possibly those for whom even the restoration of circumcision may not have been entirely an act of free choice.

## Tumtum Status

### Tumtum Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: טוּמְטוּם — אֵין אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, נָשָׁיו וַעֲבָדָיו — אוֹכְלִין. מָשׁוּךְ, וְנוֹלַד כְּשֶׁהוּא מָהוּל — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִים. אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאֵין אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים. טוּמְטוּם — אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a different source: A priest who is **a *tumtum* may not partake of *teruma*,** but **his wives and slaves may partake** of it. A priest who had been circumcised, but subsequently the residual foreskin was **drawn forward, and** similarly **one who was born circumcised,** i.e., without a foreskin, **may partake** of *teruma*. A priest who is **a hermaphrodite [*androginos*],** possessing both male and female genitals, and was circumcised **may partake of *teruma*,** as whether he is male or female he is entitled to eat *teruma*, **but he may not partake of sacrificial food,** which is permitted only to male priests, as he might not be a male. A priest who is **a *tumtum* may not partake of *teruma* or sacrificial food,** as he might be a male, and since his member is hidden he cannot be circumcised.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge against Rav Huna's ruling from a *braita*: *tumtum eino ochel biterumah, nashav va'avadav ochlin* — a *tumtum*, someone whose genitalia are covered and whose sex is therefore indeterminate, cannot eat *terumah*, but his wives and slaves can eat.

The reason the *tumtum* himself cannot eat *terumah* requires unpacking. If he is the child of a *kohen*, he should presumably be permitted to eat *terumah* regardless of sex. The answer is that if he is male, he is necessarily an *arel*, since his genitalia are covered and he cannot have had a *brit milah*. Therefore, because he might be male — and thus an *arel* — he cannot eat *terumah*.

The *braita* continues: *mashoch*, a *mashoch*, *venolad keshu maul*, or someone born already circumcised — *harei eilu ochlin*, these may eat *terumah*. This appears to be a direct challenge to Rav Huna, since a *mashoch* is declared simply permitted, not merely Rabbinically restricted. Rashi, apparently working with a variant text, suggests reading this not as a *tiyuvta* — a refutation — of Rav Huna, but as a support: the *braita* establishes that a *mashoch* is at least *biblically* permitted, which is precisely what Rav Huna said. In the text as standardly printed, however, the *Gemara* reads it as *tiyuvta deRav Huna tiyuvta*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: מָשׁוּךְ, וְנוֹלַד כְּשֶׁהוּא מָהוּל — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִין, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב הוּנָא! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** this *baraita* **teaches** that a priest whose foreskin was **drawn forward and** one who was **born circumcised may partake** of *teruma*. This would seem to be **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Huna** that a priest whose foreskin was drawn forward may not eat *teruma* at least by rabbinic law. The Gemara concludes: This is in fact **a conclusive refutation** of his opinion.

The *braita* also addresses: *androgynos ochel biterumah* — an *androgynos*, one who has both male and female genitalia, may eat *terumah*. Since he has had a *brit milah* performed on his male anatomy and is the child of a *kohen*, he is not an *arel* and may eat *terumah*. However, *ve'eino ochel bekodshim* — he may not eat *kodshei kodashim*, which are restricted to male *kohanim*, since he might be female or a third category. The *tumtum*, by contrast, cannot eat *terumah* or *kodshim* — he cannot eat *terumah* because he might be a male *arel*, and he cannot eat *kodshim* because he might be female.

### Tumtum Wives Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: טוּמְטוּם — אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, נָשָׁיו וַעֲבָדָיו — אוֹכְלִים. נָשָׁיו לְטוּמְטוּם מְנָא לֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא דְּקַדֵּישׁ, דְּתַנְיָא: טוּמְטוּם שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ — קִדּוּשָׁיו קִדּוּשִׁין, נִתְקַדֵּשׁ — קִדּוּשָׁיו קִדּוּשִׁין,
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above in the *baraita*: A priest who is **a *tumtum* may not partake of *teruma*,** but **his wives [*nashav*] and slaves may partake** of it. The Gemara is puzzled by this teaching: **From where does a *tumtum* have wives?** If he does not have a visible male organ, how can he marry a woman? **If we say that he** merely **betrothed** a woman, **as it is taught** in another *baraita*: If **a *tumtum* betrothed** a woman **his betrothal is** considered **a** valid **betrothal,** as he might be a male, and similarly if he **was betrothed** by a man, **his betrothal is** deemed **a** valid **betrothal** as he might be a female, there is a difficulty.

The *Gemara* then focuses on the line that a *tumtum*'s wives eat. This raises an immediate difficulty: *nashav detumtum mena leh* — how does a *tumtum* have wives? If he performed an act of *kiddushin* with a woman, this leads to the question: *eileima dekideish itata* — if he married a woman. A *braita* teaches: *tumtum shekideish, kidushav kidushin* — if a *tumtum* performs *kiddushin*, it takes effect, because out of doubt we must be concerned that he is male. Similarly, *nitkadesh* — if a man gave a ring to a *tumtum*, it too has binding implications, since the *tumtum* might be female.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּאָמַר לְחוּמְרָא, לְקוּלָּא מִי אָמְרִינַן? סְפֵק אִשָּׁה הוּא, וְאֵין אִשָּׁה מְקַדֶּשֶׁת אִשָּׁה?
>
> **Translation:** One could **say that** the *tanna* **said** that the betrothal of a *tumtum* is valid only **as a stringency,** i.e., out of concern that he might be a male, and therefore the woman cannot leave without a proper bill of divorce. But **should we say** that his betrothal is valid also **as a leniency,** to allow his wife to eat *teruma*? There is an **uncertainty** here that perhaps **he is a woman, and** one **woman cannot betroth** another **woman.**

However, the *Gemara* objects: that stringency applies *lechumra*, to be strict about the consequences — implications for relatives, the need for a *get*, *mamzerut* and so forth. But *lekula mi amrinan* — can we rule leniently and assume the *tumtum* is male such that his "wife" is the wife of a *kohen* and therefore permitted to eat *terumah*? *Safek isha hi* — she might not actually be his wife if he is female, and *ein isha mekaddeshet isha* — a woman cannot marry a woman.

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּשֶׁבֵּיצָיו נִיכָּרוֹת מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The *tanna* is referring to a *tumtum* whose male organ is hidden, but he is speaking of a situation **where his testicles are visible externally.** Since it is evident that he is a male, he can betroth a woman even though he cannot have relations with her.

Abaye resolves the problem by proposing: *kiveitzav mibachutz* — the case in the *braita* involves a *tumtum* whose testicles have descended visibly. This is not a total *tumtum* in the classic sense of complete indeterminacy; his maleness is established through visible anatomy even though a *brit milah* cannot be performed. He is thus a definite male, a definite *arel*, and his wife's status is certain. According to Abaye, the *braita* thus presents two distinct cases: the *reisha* describes this unusual *tumtum* who is a *vadai arel*, while the *seifa* — which repeats that a *tumtum* cannot eat *terumah* or *kodshim* — refers to the ordinary *tumtum* who is a *safek arel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מַאי ״נָשָׁיו״ — אִמּוֹ. אִמּוֹ, פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מוֹלִיד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵין מוֹלִיד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different answer: **What is** meant here by the word *nashav*, which was translated earlier as his wives, but which can also be understood as **his women?** It refers here to the priest's **mother,** who, after her husband the priest has passed away, may continue to eat *teruma* by virtue of her son. The Gemara questions this interpretation of the *baraita*: **His mother?** It **is obvious** that she may eat *teruma* on his account, as he is her offspring by a priest. The Gemara explains: This statement is nevertheless necessary, **lest you say** that only if the priest **is capable of having children** does he **enable** his mother **to eat** *teruma*, but if he **is incapable of having children** he **does not enable** his mother **to eat** *teruma*, and therefore a *tumtum*, who cannot have children, should not enable his mother to eat *teruma*. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that this is not so, as a woman may eat *teruma* by virtue of the child she bore a priest, even if that child is incapable of having children.

Rava disagrees and offers a different reading of *nashav*. *Mai nashav* — what does "his women" mean? *Imo* — his mother. The word *isha* can mean wife or woman, and Rava reads *nashav* as referring not to wives but to the mother of the *tumtum*, who is married to a *kohen* and may eat *terumah* as long as there is a surviving child, regardless of that child's sex.

The *Gemara* objects that this is obvious — *pshita* — and asks what novel point Rava would be making. The *Gemara* answers: *mahu deteima, ei ma hazera molid umachil, she'ein molid eino machil* — one might have thought that only a child capable of procreation enables the mother to eat *terumah*, whereas a child who cannot reproduce does not. Since a *tumtum* was presumed incapable of having children, one might have concluded the mother cannot eat. The *Gemara* teaches otherwise — *kamashma lan*, she may eat regardless. Rava's explanation, the *Gemara* acknowledges, is a stretch, and so is Abaye's; both are reconstructions that do not settle comfortably as *peshat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: טוּמְטוּם — אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּקָדָשִׁים. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְאַבָּיֵי, תְּנָא רֵישָׁא עָרֵל וַדַּאי, וְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא סְפֵק עָרֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof in support of Abaye's opinion from that which is taught in the latter part of the *baraita*: A priest who is **a *tumtum* may not partake of *teruma* or sacrificial food.** There is a difficulty here, as the *halakha* that a *tumtum* may not partake of *teruma* was already taught in the first part of the *baraita*. **Granted, according to Abaye,** the *tanna* **teaches** in the **first clause** of the *baraita* the *halakha* governing a *tumtum* who is **definitely uncircumcised,** i.e., one whose testicles are visible externally, so that he is definitely male but cannot undergo circumcision because his member itself is hidden. **And** then **he teaches** in **the latter clause** of the *baraita* the *halakha* governing a *tumtum* about whom there is **uncertainty** as to whether he is **uncircumcised,** i.e., one whose genitalia are completely hidden, so that he might not even be a male.

The *Gemara* then addresses the later passage of the *braita* — *tumtum eino ochel lo biterumah velo bekodshim* — which repeats that a *tumtum* cannot eat *terumah*. According to Abaye this is elegantly coherent: the *reisha* addressed an unusual *tumtum* who is a *vadai arel*, while the *seifa* addresses the ordinary *tumtum* who is a *safek arel*, demonstrating that even a doubtful case disqualifies. According to Rava, the repetition of *tumtum* serves to explain the *reisha*: why did it originally say a *tumtum* cannot eat *terumah*? Because he might be a *safek arel*, and *arel eino ochel lo biterumah velo bekodshim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרָבָא, טוּמְטוּם דְּסֵיפָא לְמָה לִי? מַאי טוּמְטוּם — עָרֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rava, why do I** need the repetition of the *halakha* governing a ***tumtum*** in **the latter clause?** The *tanna* already stated this *halakha* in the first part of the *baraita*. The Gemara answers: **What is** this ***tumtum*** referred to in the latter clause? It is a man who is definitely **uncircumcised.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא סְפֵק עָרֵל לָא אָכֵיל — וַדַּאי עָרֵל אָכֵיל?! מָה טַעַם קָאָמַר: מָה טַעַם טוּמְטוּם אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּפֵק עָרֵל הוּא, וְעָרֵל אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now,** if a *tumtum*, about whom there is **uncertainty** as to whether he is **uncircumcised, may not partake** of *teruma*, as stated in the first clause of the *baraita*, can it be supposed that a man who is **definitely uncircumcised** may eat *teruma*, so that it was necessary for the *baraita* to teach in the latter clause that he may not do so? The Gemara answers: **He is saying: What is the reason.** The *baraita* should be understood as follows: **What is the reason** that **a *tumtum* may not partake of *teruma*?** It is **because** there is **uncertainty** as to whether he is **uncircumcised, and an uncircumcised** priest **may not partake of *teruma* or sacrificial food.**

### Tumtum Safek Arel

The *Gemara*'s final resolution of the *tumtum* discussion is that the *tumtum* is excluded from *terumah* because he is a *safek arel*. This also explains the relationship between the two parts of the *braita*: the opening statement that a *tumtum* cannot eat *terumah* and the closing statement that underscores this — both are expressions of the same concern that he might be male and therefore an *arel*.

The outstanding question about whether a *mashoch* is Biblically or Rabbinically forbidden remains: the *braita* either wholly contradicts Rav Huna or — per Rashi's reading — supports him. Either way, the *braita* treats *mashoch* as a distinct category, and the language of *tzarich* rather than *assur* continues to suggest Rabbinic rather than Biblical status.

### Transgender Discussion

One might ask whether the *Gemara*'s discussion of *tumtum* — and especially the ruling that *tumtum shekideish kidushav kidushin, nitkadesh kidushav kidushin* — has any bearing on contemporary questions of transgender and intersex identity in *halacha*.

The *androgynos* tends to generate more significant discussion in this context than the *tumtum*. The *androgynos* raises the possibility, contemplated in Tannaic literature, that the *androgynos* is a *biryah bifnei atzmah* — a category unto itself, neither male nor female — which speaks directly to intersex questions and the possibility of recognizing a third halakhic sex category. The *tumtum*, by contrast, is fundamentally a case of *safek* — the sex is one thing or the other, we simply do not know which.

As for the transgender question: the *Gemara*'s framework rests on the assumption that sex is determined by genitalia. Most contemporary *poskim* hold that *halachic* status is determined by birth sex. The more complex question arises when a child is born with ambiguous genitalia requiring surgical determination of sex in infancy — that is, where there is a genuine indeterminate status at birth. In that case there may be more basis to argue that a subsequent determination, through medical intervention, affects *halachic* status. When an individual has a clearly defined birth sex and later transitions, the overwhelming *halachic* consensus is that status is determined by what one is at birth.

## Brit Milah Timing

### Rabbi Elazar Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: מָשׁוּךְ (וְנוֹלַד כְּשֶׁהוּא מָהוּל), וְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר כְּשֶׁהוּא מָהוּל, וְקָטָן שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנּוֹ, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַנִּימּוֹלִים, לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי עֲרָלוֹת — אֵינָן נִימּוֹלִין אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַנּוֹ — אֵין נִימּוֹלִין אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ — נִימּוֹלִין בְּיוֹם וּבַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this amoraic dispute as to whether or not one who had been circumcised but his residual foreskin was drawn forward is considered uncircumcised by Torah law is **parallel to** the following dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it is taught in the *Tosefta* (*Shabbat* 16:7): One whose foreskin was **drawn forward, and** similarly, **one who was born circumcised, and a convert who converted when he was** already **circumcised, and a child whose** appropriate **time** for circumcision already **passed** and he was still uncircumcised, **and all others** who require **circumcision,** which, as the Gemara parenthetically adds, comes **to include one who has two foreskins,** both of which must be removed, **may be circumcised only during the day. Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon says:** If the circumcision is performed **at its** appropriate **time,** i.e., on the eighth day, **they may be circumcised only during the day.** However, if the circumcision is performed **not at its** appropriate **time, they may be circumcised** either **during the day or at night.**

The *Gemara* now explores whether the dispute about a *mashoch*'s status — biblical or Rabbinic — corresponds to a known Tannaic debate. A *braita* lists: a *mashoch*, a convert who was already circumcised at the time of conversion, an infant whose *brit milah* was not performed on day 8, and all other non-standard cases of *milah* — *einam nimolin ela bayom*, all of these must be circumcised specifically during the day.

Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon dissents: *bizmano eno nimol ela bayom, shelo bizmano nimol bayom ubalayla* — the requirement of daytime applies only to the eighth-day *brit milah*; if it is performed later than the prescribed time, it may be done either by day or by night.

### Daytime Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? קָטָן שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנּוֹ מִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **And** how can **you understand** the disagreement in that way? With regard to **a child whose** appropriate **time** for circumcision **has** already **passed, is there anyone who says that** the obligation to circumcise him is only **by rabbinic law?** Even after the eighth day, there is certainly a Torah obligation to circumcise him, and yet the *tanna'im* disagree about this case as well.

The *Gemara* immediately rejects this framing. *Ve-tisbara* — does this make sense? An infant whose *brit milah* is performed after day 8 — *katan she'avar zamano* — is surely a biblical obligation. No one would say *derabbanan*. So the question of day versus night clearly does not track the biblical/Rabbinic distinction, since the list contains both clearly biblical cases and clearly Rabbinic ones.

### Vav Drashah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא: מָשׁוּךְ — דְּרַבָּנַן, וְקָטָן שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנּוֹ — דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר דָּרְשִׁינַן ״וּבַיּוֹם״, וּמָר סָבַר לָא דָּרְשִׁינַן ״וּבַיּוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, everyone** agrees that the obligation to circumcise one whose foreskin was **drawn forward** is **by rabbinic law, and** that the obligation to circumcise **a child whose** appropriate **time** for circumcision **has** already **passed** is **by Torah law. And here they disagree with regard to the following: One Sage holds** that **we expound** the phrase **"and on the day"** in the verse "And on the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised" (Leviticus 12:3). The superfluous word "and" indicates that even if the child was not circumcised on the eighth day, the procedure must still be performed during the day. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon, **holds** that **we do not expound** the phrase **"and on the day,"** and therefore a circumcision must be performed during the day only when it takes place on the eighth day, but afterward it may be performed even at night.

Rather, *dechulei alma mashoch derabbanan* — let us accept that the obligation to re-circumcise a *mashoch* is Rabbinic. And *katan she'avar zamano deoraita*, that the infant after day 8 is biblical. The list contains both, and nevertheless the same question applies to all: does the Torah's requirement of *bayom* apply only to day 8 itself, or does it extend to any later performance?

The debate is resolved to be specifically about a textual *drashah*. The verse states *bayom hashmini yimol basar orlato* — on the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised. The word *uvayom* — with the extra *vav* — is read by one opinion as teaching that even if it is not the eighth day, it must still be *yom*, daytime. The other opinion does not read the *vav* of *uvayom* as bearing this extended meaning.

### Notar Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּיָתֵיב רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְקָדָרֵישׁ: נוֹתָר, בִּזְמַנּוֹ — אֵינוֹ נִשְׂרָף אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ — נִשְׂרָף בֵּין בַּיּוֹם בֵּין בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As in** the case **where Rabbi Yoḥanan was sitting and he expounded: *Notar*,** the flesh of an offering that is left over beyond its allotted time, requires burning. If it is burnt **at its** appropriate **time,** i.e., on the same day that it became *notar*, it **may be burned only during the day.** If it is burnt **not at its** appropriate **time,** it **may be burned either during the day or at night.**

Rabbi Yochanan was sitting and expounding a parallel from the laws of *notar*. The verse *vehanotar bayom hashlishi baesh yisaref* — "and the remainder on the third day shall be burnt in fire" — establishes that the burning of *notar* must begin by day, specifically day 3. Rabbi Yochanan taught: *nosar bizmano eno nisraf ela bayom, shelo bizmano nisraf bayom ubalayla* — when the time first arrives it must be by day, but once the obligation has begun and extends beyond its initial moment, it continues into the night and is not limited to daytime thereafter.

### Rabbi Yochanan Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נִימּוֹל לִשְׁמִינִי שֶׁאֵין נִימּוֹל אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת לְתִשְׁעָה, לַעֲשָׂרָה, לְאַחַד עָשָׂר לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר (מִנַּיִן)? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבַיּוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar raises an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** from the following *baraita*: **I have** derived **only** that a child who **is circumcised on** the **eighth** day **may be circumcised only during the day. From where** do I derive **to include** in this *halakha* a child who is circumcised **on the ninth, tenth, eleventh,** or **twelfth** day? **From where** is it derived that he, too, may be circumcised only during the day? Therefore, **the verse states: "And on the day,"** which teaches that the obligation to circumcise during the day extends beyond the eighth day itself.

This parallel was taken to support the position that *brit milah*, too, begins with a daytime requirement on day 8 but may be performed at night if delayed — like the view of Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon.

Rabbi Elazar then challenged Rabbi Yochanan directly: *ein li ela nimol lishmini she'eno nimol ela bayom* — I know only that the eighth-day *brit milah* must be during the day. *Minayin lerabot letisha le'asara leachad asar velishnem asar* — from where do I know that even if performed on day 9, 10, 11, or 12, it must still be during the day? *Talmud lomar uvayom* — the extra *vav* teaches that even on a later date, it is exclusively daytime.

The days 9 through 12 correspond to real cases. A baby born on *erev Shabbat bein hashmashot* presents a safek, and one must assume the later possibility, pushing the *brit milah* to day 9. If Shabbat itself was *bein hashmashot*, it becomes day 10. A Shabbat followed by *Yom Tov* produces day 11, and two days of *Rosh Hashanah* yield day 12.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ וָאו, וָאו וְהֵי דָּרֵישׁ. אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And even** the Sage **who does not expound** the letter ***vav*,** meaning "and," as superfluous, **expounds** the letters ***vav* and *heh*** when they come together and understands them as alluding to cases not explicitly mentioned in the biblical text. Regarding *notar* the verse states: "And that which remains [*vehanotar*] of the flesh of the offering on the third day shall be burnt with fire" (Leviticus 7:17), where the letters *vav* and *heh* teach that the obligation to burn *notar* during the day extends beyond the third day itself. Rabbi Yoḥanan **was silent,** as he had no response.

Rabbi Elazar added that even the opinion that does not read *uvayom* as extending the daytime requirement must acknowledge the *vav-heh* of *vehanotar* — the longer prefix — which teaches that even when the obligation extends beyond its starting point, it remains tied to daytime. Rabbi Yochanan, confronted with this argument, fell silent. Afterward, when Rabbi Elazar had left, Rabbi Yochanan turned to Resh Lakish and said: *ra'iti le-Ben Pedat she-yoshev ve-doresh ke-Moshe mi-pi ha-Gevurah* — I saw Ben Pedat sitting and expounding like Moshe from the mouth of the Almighty. Whether this was admiration or mild criticism — he was doing what I was doing, but perhaps with greater authority — is not entirely clear.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דִּנְפַק, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: רָאִיתִי לְבֶן פְּדָת שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹרֵשׁ כְּמֹשֶׁה מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: דִּידֵיהּ הִיא? מַתְנִיתָא הִיא. הֵיכָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ? בְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים. נְפַק, תַּנְיַיהּ בִּתְלָתָא יוֹמֵי, וְסַבְרַהּ בִּתְלָתָא יַרְחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **After** Rabbi Elazar **left, Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who was impressed with Rabbi Elazar's exposition, **said to Reish Lakish: I saw that** Rabbi Elazar, **son of Pedat, was sitting and expounding** the Torah **as Moses** had received it directly **from the mouth of the Almighty. Reish Lakish said to him:** Was this exposition **his own? It is a *baraita*.** Rabbi Yoḥanan inquired: **Where is this** *baraita* **taught?** Reish Lakish replied: It is **in *Torat Kohanim*,** otherwise known as *Sifra*, a work of halakhic midrash on the book of Leviticus. Rabbi Yoḥanan **went out** and **learned** the entire *Torat Kohanim* **in three days, and reached** a full **understanding of it in three months.**

Resh Lakish replied: *dideih?* — Do you think Rabbi Elazar composed that *drashah* on his own authority? *Matnitin hi* — it is a *braita*; he was quoting established teaching, not originating it. Rabbi Yochanan asked: *heicha tana leih* — where is this *braita* found? Resh Lakish answered: *be-Toras Kohanim* — in the Sifra.

## Brit Milah Timing Continued

### Toras Kohanim Study

Upon hearing this, Rabbi Yochanan went and immersed himself in *Toras Kohanim*: *tanyah be-tlata yomei* — he mastered the text in three days, *ve-savrah be-tlata yarchei* — and spent three months working through its analysis. This raises a remarkable question: had Rabbi Yochanan truly not previously learned *Toras Kohanim* with sufficient depth? Or had he not appreciated it as he should have? The implication seems to be that until this moment he had not engaged with the Sifra adequately, and the encounter with Rabbi Elazar's precise citation of a *braita* he did not recognize prompted him to undertake that study.

This naturally raises the question of when *Toras Kohanim* was committed to writing. The general assumption has been that any written form of *Torah she-ba'al peh* belongs to the Geonic period or later. However, Shlomo Naeh published a study examining the various and overlapping division systems of *Toras Kohanim* — its dual organization by *prakim* and *parshiyyot*, whose boundaries frequently do not align and seem to have been laid atop one another — and proposed that the length of each unit, across all divisions, falls within a specific range. That range corresponds to the accepted dimensions of scrolls in the Greco-Roman world, where scroll length was governed by practical considerations: too short meant too many scrolls; too long meant too much winding. Naeh argued that these divisions reflect the original written organization of the text, and that *Toras Kohanim* may be the earliest component of *Torah she-ba'al peh* to have been set down in writing — plausibly because the dominance of *Beit HaMikdash* culture preceded the Tannaic era, and the practical requirements of *avodah* demanded written precision. Scholarly opinion on his conclusions is divided, but the argument is considered compelling.

## Arel & Parah

### Rabbi Elazar Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עָרֵל שֶׁהִזָּה — הַזָּאָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. מִידֵי דְּהָוֵה אַטְּבוּל יוֹם, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָסוּר בִּתְרוּמָה, כָּשֵׁר בַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** If **an uncircumcised** priest **sprinkled** the purification waters containing the ashes of a red heifer in order to purify someone who had contracted ritual impurity imparted by a corpse, **his sprinkling is valid, just as it is** in the case of a priest **who immersed himself that day** but does not become completely purified until nightfall. **As, although** such an individual **is prohibited from** eating ***teruma*,** he is **fit for** all the rites connected to the red **heifer.**

The *Gemara* turns to a new ruling: *amar Rabbi Elazar, arel shehizah hazaso kesherah* — an *arel* who performed the *hazaah*, the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* waters, his sprinkling is valid. The question is whether there is any reason to think an *arel* should be disqualified from performing this service.

### Tvul Yom Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִטְבוּל יוֹם — שֶׁכֵּן מוּתָּר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר! אַטּוּ אֲנַן לַאֲכִילָה קָאָמְרִינַן, אֲנַן לִנְגִיעָה קָאָמְרִינַן: וּמָה טְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁאָסוּר בִּנְגִיעָה דִּתְרוּמָה — מוּתָּר בַּפָּרָה, עָרֵל שֶׁמּוּתָּר בִּנְגִיעָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמּוּתָּר בַּפָּרָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If **one who immersed himself that day** is fit for all the rites connected to the red heifer, that is because **he is** at least **permitted to** eat **tithes,** and so he is treated more leniently than one who is uncircumcised, for whom it is prohibited to partake of second tithe. The Gemara answers: **Is that to say** that **we are speaking about eating? We spoke** in reference **to touching,** and the proof was as follows: **If one who immersed himself that day** and **who is forbidden to touch *teruma*,** as he invalidates *teruma* by touch, is nevertheless **permitted** to participate **in** all the rites connected to the red **heifer;** then with regard to one who is **uncircumcised, who is permitted to touch** *teruma* according to all opinions, **is it not right that** he should be **permitted** to participate **in** the rites connected to **the** red **heifer?**

Rabbi Elazar offers a comparison: *mi havei mi-tvul yom* — is an *arel* any worse than a *tvul yom*? The practice of using a *tvul yom* to perform the *parah adumah* service was deliberately instituted by the *chachamim* to refute the *tzedukim*, who insisted the *kohen* performing the *parah* service must have waited until nightfall after immersion. The *chachamim* ensured the service was performed by one who had immersed that day but nightfall had not yet come — demonstrating the *tzedukim* were wrong. A *tvul yom*, who retains some residual *tumah* until nightfall, is *asur biterumah* yet *kasher beparah* — forbidden from eating *terumah* yet valid for the *parah adumah* service.

The *Gemara* challenges this comparison: *mah litvul yom sheken mutar bemaaser* — a *tvul yom* is permitted to eat *maaser*, whereas an *arel* may not be. This will be discussed in subsequent *sugyot*. But the *Gemara* immediately deflects this objection: *atu anachilah kamina* — who said we are talking about eating? *Ana lenegiah kamina* — the relevant category for performing the service is the ability to handle and touch, not the rules of eating.

The proper comparison, then, is a *kal vachomer*: a *tvul yom* who is *asur binegiah biterumah* — forbidden from touching *terumah* — is *mutar beparah*, valid for the *parah adumah* service. An *arel*, who is *mutar binegiah* — permitted to handle *terumah* — surely should be permitted for the *parah adumah* service, *ain din shemutar beparah*. This argument is confirmed by a *braita*: *arel shehizah hazaso kesherah, umaaseh hayah vehichshiru chachamim hazaso* — an *arel* who performed the *hazaah* is valid, and this was actually practiced and the *chachamim* ruled it acceptable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: עָרֵל שֶׁהִזָּה — הַזָּאָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. וּמַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה וְהִכְשִׁירוּ חֲכָמִים הַזָּאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: If **an uncircumcised** priest **sprinkled** the purification waters, **his sprinkling is valid. And an incident occurred** in which such an individual sprinkled the purification waters **and the Sages validated his sprinkling.**

### Arel Cannot Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: טוּמְטוּם שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ — קִידּוּשׁוֹ פָּסוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא סְפֵק עָרֵל, וְעָרֵל פָּסוּל לְקַדֵּשׁ. וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ — קִידּוּשׁוֹ כָּשֵׁר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ — קִדּוּשָׁיו פְּסוּלִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּפֵק אִשָּׁה [הוּא], וְאִשָּׁה פְּסוּלָה מִלְּקַדֵּשׁ. קָתָנֵי מִיהָא: עָרֵל וּסְפֵק עָרֵל פָּסוּל מִלְּקַדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which was taught in the *Tosefta* (*Para* 5:5): If **a *tumtum* sanctified** the purification waters by placing a small amount of ashes from the red heifer into springwater that had been placed into a container for that purpose, **his sanctification is invalid because** there is **uncertainty** as to whether he is **uncircumcised, and an uncircumcised** man **is disqualified from sanctifying** the purification waters. **But** if **a hermaphrodite sanctified** the purification waters, **his sanctification is valid. Rabbi Yehuda** disagrees and **says: Even** if **a hermaphrodite sanctified** the purification waters, **his sanctification is invalid, because** there is **uncertainty** as to whether a hermaphrodite is **a woman, and a woman is disqualified from sanctifying** the purification waters. **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that one who is definitely **uncircumcised, and** even one about whom there is **uncertainty** as to whether he is **uncircumcised, is disqualified from sanctifying** the purification waters.

A challenge is immediately raised: *tumtum shekidesh kiddushav pasul* — here *kiddush* refers not to marriage but to the *kiddush* of the *parah adumah*, the mixing of the ashes with water — a *tumtum* who performed this mixing has rendered it invalid, *mipnei shesafek arel*, because he might be an *arel*, and *arel pasul lekadesh* — an *arel* is invalid for the *kiddush*. This *braita* explicitly states that an *arel* cannot perform the *kiddush*, directly contradicting the ruling that he may perform the *hazaah*. The *Gemara* accepts for the moment that these two acts — *kiddush* and *hazaah* — are treated equivalently.

The *braita* continues: *androgynos shekidesh kiddushav kasher* — an *androgynos* who performed the *kiddush* is valid, because the concern about an *androgynos* is that he might be female, not that he might be an *arel*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *af androgynos shekidesh kiddushav psulin, mipnei shesafek isha hi, ve'isha psulah melekadesh* — an *androgynos* is also invalid, because he might be a woman and a woman is invalid for this service. This frames the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Tanna Kamma* about whether women may perform the *parah adumah* service.

A further *braita* states explicitly: *arel vesafek arel pasul melekadesh* — both a definite and a doubtful *arel* are invalid for the *kiddush* of the *parah adumah*.

### Rav Yosef Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי תַּנָּא — תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הוּא, דִּמְרַבֵּי לֵיהּ לְעָרֵל כְּטָמֵא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״, לְרַבּוֹת הֶעָרֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: This *tanna*** of the *baraita*, who disqualifies one who is uncircumcised from sanctifying the purification waters, **is a *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Akiva, who includes the uncircumcised** in the same *halakha* **as** that which governs **the ritually impure. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Akiva says** that the words "any man" in the verse **"Any man** from the seed of Aaron who is a leper or a *zav* shall not eat of the holy things until he be pure" (Leviticus 22:4), come **to include** one who is **uncircumcised,** and it is prohibited for him as well to partake of consecrated food. And so too, with regard to other matters as well, e.g., sanctifying the purification waters, one who is uncircumcised has the same status as one who is ritually impure.

Rav Yosef offers a resolution: *hai tanna tanna devei Rabbi Akiva hu, demarbei leh le'arel ketamei* — this *tanna* who invalidates the *arel* belongs to the school of Rabbi Akiva, who treats the *arel* as equivalent to a *tamei*.

Rav Yosef's reference is to the beginning of the *perek*, where the source of the prohibition against an *arel* eating *terumah* was disputed. Rabbi Akiva derived it from the double expression *ish ish* in a verse about *tmei'im* — *lerabbot es ha'arel*, to include the *arel* in that verse's prohibition. From this, Rav Yosef draws the stronger conclusion: Rabbi Akiva treated the *arel* not merely as analogous to a *tamei* for purposes of *terumah*, but as equivalent to a *tamei* in terms of personal status across the board. Wherever a *tamei* is personally excluded — not in the sense of transferring *tumah*, but in terms of his own disqualification — an *arel* would be similarly disqualified. That is why Rabbi Akiva's school would invalidate an *arel* from performing the *parah adumah* service.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, וְקַשְׁיָא לִי: לָא לִישְׁתְּמִיט תַּנָּא וְלִיתְנֵי הֶעָרֵל וְהַטָּמֵא, וְלֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: I was sitting** at the time **before Rav Yosef, and I had** the following **difficulty:** If so, according to Rav Yosef's opinion that the uncircumcised and the ritually impure have the same status, should one **not** be able **to find a *tanna* who teaches** the *halakha* of **the uncircumcised and** that of **the ritually impure** together, **and we should say** that this is the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva?** There should be some source that reflects this view.

Rava objects strongly: *hevei yasivina kamei d'Rav Yosef ve-kashyei li* — I sat before Rav Yosef and this troubled me. If there were truly a position of Rabbi Akiva treating the *arel* as a *tamei* in all areas, we should have found some statement in *Shas* that said *ha'arel ve-hatamei* cannot do such-and-such, accompanied by the attribution to Rabbi Akiva. The absence of any such formulation across the entirety of *Shas* argues strongly against this being Rabbi Akiva's position. *Lo ishtami tanna ve-lisni ha'arel ve-hatamei ve-leima Rabbi Akiva hi* — it would not have escaped notice; some *tanna* would have formulated the rule in that way.

### Arel Tamei Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָא קָתָנֵי: הֶעָרֵל וְהַטָּמֵא פְּטוּרִים מִן הָרְאִיָּיה! הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דִּמְאִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is there **not** such a source? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One who is **uncircumcised and** one who is **ritually impure are exempt from** making an **appearance** in the Temple on each of the three pilgrim Festivals. The Gemara refutes this argument: This is no proof, as **there** it can be argued that one who is uncircumcised is exempt from appearing in the Temple **because he is repulsive,** and it is unbefitting that one who is uncircumcised appear in the Temple courtyard, but this does not mean that with regard to other matters as well he is treated like one who is ritually impure.

The *Gemara* responds: *ve-haktani ha'arel ve-hatamei paturim min hare'iyah* — there is a *Mishna* stating that an *arel* and a *tamei* are both exempt from *olat re'iyah*, the pilgrimage offering. Here we have exactly the formulation Rava said was missing — *ha'arel ve-hatamei* — grouped together.

The *Gemara* counters: *chutz mishum d'mais* — an *arel* is excluded from the *Beit HaMikdash* not because he has the status of a *tamei*, but simply because it is unseemly for someone who is an *arel* to present himself in the Temple. The grouping with *tamei* in that *Mishna* is incidental and does not reflect a principled equation of the two categories.

The *Gemara* acknowledges that this response somewhat undercuts Rava's objection — after all, the *Mishna* does say *ha'arel ve-hatamei* together, and the reason given for excluding the *arel* could itself reflect an assumption of equivalence. What remains is the fascinating Tannaic possibility — preserved in the school of Rabbi Akiva — that in terms of personal status, wherever a *tamei* is excluded, an *arel* is excluded as well. Rava's objection does not fully resolve the matter, and the question of whether this was Rabbi Akiva's position continues to resonate through the *sugya*.

## Parah Service Debate

### Disqualified List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ. דְּתַנְיָא: הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִים לְקַדֵּשׁ — חוּץ מֵחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר בַּקָּטָן וּפוֹסֵל בְּאִשָּׁה וּבְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **follow their** usual line of **reasoning** with regard to a hermaphrodite. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **All are fit to sanctify** the purification waters **except for a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor. Rabbi Yehuda deems a minor fit** for the task, **but deems a woman and a hermaphrodite unfit.**

The *Gemara* continues with the broader dispute about who may perform the various functions of the *parah adumah* service, following the principle *ve-azdu le-taimeihu* — Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan* proceed according to their respective reasoning.

A *braita* states: *hakol keshairin lekadesh* — anyone is eligible to perform the *kiddush* of the *parah adumah*, mixing the ashes with water, *chutz mi-cheresh shoteh ve-katan* — with the exception of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor.

### Androgynos Status

Rabbi Yehuda differs: *machshir be-katan u-fosel be-ishah* — he validates the *katan* but invalidates the *ishah*, and by extension an *androgynos* who might be female. The *Rabbanan*, conversely, invalidate the *katan* but validate the *ishah*. An *androgynos* according to the *Rabbanan* is valid — he is excluded only if he might be female, and if his male nature is established his participation is permitted.

### Verses Asifah Kiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלָקְחוּ לַטָּמֵא מֵעֲפַר שְׂרֵיפַת הַחַטָּאת״, הָנָךְ דְּפָסְלִי בַּאֲסִיפָה — פְּסוּלִין בְּקִידּוּשׁ, הָנָךְ דִּכְשֵׁרִין בַּאֲסִיפָה — כְּשֵׁרִים בְּקִידּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reasoning of the Rabbis? As it is written: "And they shall take for the impure of the ashes of the burning of the sin-offering,** and he shall place on it running water in a vessel" (Numbers 19:17). The juxtaposition of the placement of the water to the gathering of the ashes indicates that they are governed by the same *halakha*. Therefore, **those who are unfit for gathering** the ashes **are** likewise **unfit for sanctification,** whereas **those who are fit for gathering** the ashes **are** likewise **fit for sanctification.** Since a woman is fit to gather the ashes of the red heifer, she may also sanctify its waters.

The debate between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Yehuda is rooted in the interpretation of two adjacent verses. One verse states *ve-asaf ish tahor* — "and a pure man shall gather" the ashes. The next verse states *ve-lakhu latamei me'afar sereifat hachatat* — "and they shall take for the *tamei* person from the ashes" — and place it with water. The principle derived is: *hanakh de-pasli be-asifah psulin be-kiddush* — those who are invalid for the gathering of the ashes are invalid for the mixing of the water; *hanakh de-keshairin be-asifah keshairin be-kiddush* — those valid for gathering are valid for mixing.

### Women Included

The *Rabbanan* argue as follows. The word *tahor* in *ve-asaf ish tahor* is understood to include women — there is a *drashah* elsewhere establishing this. Therefore, the *asifah* can be performed by any adult of sound mind, male or female, but not by a *katan* who lacks *da'at*. Since the same persons valid for *asifah* are valid for *kiddush*, a woman is valid for *kiddush* as well. A *katan* is excluded from both.

### Rabbi Yehuda Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמַר לָךְ: אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא ״וְלָקַח״, מַאי ״וְלָקְחוּ״? דַּאֲפִילּוּ הָנָךְ דִּפְסוּלִין הָתָם, כְּשֵׁרִים הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda** could have **said to you: If so,** then **let the verse state: And he shall take. What** is the significance of the shift to the plural form: **"And they shall take"?** It teaches **that even those who are unfit there are fit here.** As the *halakhot* of the two cases are not identical, Rabbi Yehuda deems a minor fit to perform the sanctification.

Rabbi Yehuda counters: *amar lach* — if the intent of *ve-lakhu* were simply to preserve the same eligibility as *ve-asaf*, the verse should have said *ve-lakach* in the singular, parallel to *ve-asaf*. *Mai ve-laku* — why does the verse shift to the plural? This teaches that the category for *kiddush* is actually *broader* than for *asifah*: *de-afilu hanakh de-psulin hatam keshairin hacha* — even those invalid for *asifah*, such as a *katan*, are valid for *kiddush*. Rabbi Yehuda therefore holds that a *katan* may perform *kiddush*.

*Ei hachi, ishah nami?* — But if the plural *ve-laku* expands eligibility to include even those excluded from *asifah*, why is a woman still invalid? The answer comes from the verse continuing: *ve-nasan alav mayim chayim el keli* — "and he shall place living water into a vessel." The verse says *ve-nasan* in the masculine singular, not *ve-nasnah*, implicitly excluding a woman.

### Plural Laku Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן — אִי כְּתִיב: ״וְלָקַח״ ״וְנָתַן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא שָׁקֵיל חַד וְיָהֵיב חַד, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְלָקְחוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how do **the Rabbis** respond to this claim? **If** the verse **was written: And he shall take…and he shall place, I would say** that **one** individual must **take** the ashes **and** the same **one** must also **place** the water on them. **The Merciful One** therefore **writes: "And they shall take,"** indicating that the ritual is valid even when performed by two different individuals.

The *Rabbanan* respond to Rabbi Yehuda's grammatical argument. Had the verse said *ve-lakach ve-nasan* in the singular throughout, one might have concluded that the identical person who gathers must be the one who mixes, and that only one person may perform the entire act. The plural *ve-laku* teaches that multiple people may be involved in the gathering — or that different people may perform different stages. Had it continued *ve-lakhu ve-nasnu*, one might have required two people for the mixing as well. *Ve-lakhu ve-nasan* — the shift from plural to singular — teaches that even if multiple people gathered, a single person may perform the mixing. The grammatical movement from plural to singular is not meant to exclude women but to clarify the flexibility of personnel for each stage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְלָקְחוּ״ ״וְנָתְנוּ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דְּשָׁקְלִי תְּרֵי וְיָהֲבִי תְּרֵי, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְלָקְחוּ״ ״וְנָתַן״ — דַּאֲפִילּוּ שָׁקְלִי תְּרֵי וְיָהֵיב חַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written: And they shall take…and they shall place, I would say that two** people must **take** the ashes **and two** must **place** the water on them, but if these rites are performed by fewer than two people they are invalid. **The Torah** therefore **states: "And they shall take…and he shall place,"** to teach **that even** if **two** people **take** the ashes **and one** person **places** the water on them, the ritual is valid. Since the verse had to be formulated precisely in this manner in order to teach that *halakha*, the words "and he shall place" cannot be understood as coming to exclude a woman.

### Hizah Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִזָּה הַטָּהוֹר עַל הַטָּמֵא״, ״טָהוֹר״ — מִכְּלָל שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא, לִימֵּד עַל טְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁכָּשֵׁר בַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is further stated: **"And the pure person shall sprinkle upon the impure"** (Numbers 19:19). The verse states **"pure";** this indicates **by inference that he is** in some way **ritually impure.** In other words, the verse speaks of one who is pure only in relation to one who is impure. Were this not the case, there would have been no need at all to mention his purity, as it would have been understood that since the red heifer is called a sin-offering, with regard to which purity is paramount, the one performing the ritual must be pure. Of necessity, then, this "pure" individual is not completely pure in all regards. This **teaches that one who immersed himself that day is fit to** perform the rites connected to the red **heifer.**

The *Gemara* concludes with the verse that grounds the initial argument about the *tvul yom* and the *hazaah*: *ve-hizah ha-tahor al ha-tamei* — "and the pure one shall sprinkle upon the impure one." *Tahor miklal she-hu tamei* — this is a remarkable *drashah*: the word *tahor*, pure, implies by contrast that this person had previously been *tamei* and went through a process of *taharah*. Yet the verse's qualification of *tahor* — rather than calling him simply pure — suggests that his *taharah* is not entirely complete. From here we derive that a *tvul yom*, who has immersed but whose *taharah* is not yet concluded until nightfall, is valid for performing the *hazaah* of the *parah adumah*. And from this teaching about the *tvul yom*, the *Gemara* earlier sought to establish that an *arel* could likewise perform the *hazaah* — though that proposal encountered the challenge of the *braita* invalidating an *arel*, attributed to the school of Rabbi Akiva, who equates the personal status of an *arel* with that of a *tamei*.
