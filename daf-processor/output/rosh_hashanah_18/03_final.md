# Rosh Hashanah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Teshuvah & Gezar Din

### Yochanan's Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גְּדוֹלָה תְּשׁוּבָה שֶׁמְּקָרַעַת גְּזַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַשְׁמֵן לֵב הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאׇזְנָיו הַכְבֵּד וְעֵינָיו הָשַׁע פֶּן יִרְאֶה בְעֵינָיו וּבְאׇזְנָיו יִשְׁמָע וּלְבָבוֹ יָבִין וָשָׁב וְרָפָא לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Great is repentance, for it tears up the sentence** issued **against a person, as it is stated: "Make the heart of this people fat, and make their ears heavy, and smear over their eyes; lest they see with their eyes, and hear with their ears, and understand with their heart, and they will return, and be healed"** (Isaiah 6:10), implying that if indeed they return and repent, they will be healed from all their sins.

The daf opens with a statement of Rabbi Yochanan: *gedola teshuva she-mekara'at gzar dino shel adam* — great is *teshuva*, which tears up the *gzar din*, the divine decree against a person. The proof text is from Yeshayahu: *hishmen lev ha-am ha-zeh ve-oznav hachbed ve-einav hasha pen yireh ve-einav u-ve-oznav yishma u-levavo yavin va-shav ve-rafa lo* — the heart of this people has become fat and their ears heavy and their eyes blind, lest they see with their eyes and hear with their ears and their heart understand; and they will do *teshuva*, and they will be healed. The verse appears to demonstrate that *teshuva* works even after a *gzar din* has already been issued.

### Papa's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְדִלְמָא לִפְנֵי גְּזַר דִּין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְרָפָא לוֹ״ כְּתִיב, אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁצָּרִיךְ רְפוּאָה — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה גְּזַר דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But perhaps** this was said **before the sentence** was issued, but after one's sentence has been decreed repentance no longer helps. Abaye **said to him: It is written** here: "And they will return **and be healed"** (Isaiah 6:10). **What requires healing?** An illness does. Consequently, **you must say** that the reference here is to **a sentence** that has already been issued, and even so, after repentance, they will be healed.

Rav Papa immediately challenges this reading: *ve-dilma lifnei gzar din* — who says this verse is talking about after *gzar din*? Perhaps this is speaking about *teshuva* performed before any decree has been issued at all, in which case the verse proves nothing about overturning an existing judgment.

### Healing Proof

The response is pointed: *ve-rafa lo ktiv* — it says "and he will be healed." The Gemara asks: what kind of thing requires healing? *Hevei omer zeh gzar din* — it must be that there was already a decree, something harmful already set in motion, that now requires a cure. The language of healing thus signals the prior existence of a *gzar din*, confirming that *teshuva* can indeed tear it up.

## Meitivei Baraita

### RH to YK Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַשָּׁב בֵּינְתַיִם — מוֹחֲלִין לוֹ, לֹא שָׁב בֵּינְתַיִם — אֲפִילּוּ הֵבִיא כׇּל אֵילֵי נְבָיוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם אֵין מוֹחֲלִין לוֹ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּיָחִיד, הָא — בְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **one repents in the meantime,** between Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur, **he is forgiven;** if **he does not repent in the meantime,** then **even if he** later **brings** as offerings **all the rams of Nebaioth in the world,** which are of the best quality, **they do not forgive him** in the Heavenly court. This implies that once one's sentence has been decreed, even repentance cannot alter it. The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult: Here,** where repentance is effective only before the sentence on Yom Kippur, the *baraita* is referring to repentance **of an individual; there,** where forgiveness is granted even after a sentence is issued, it is referring to **communal** repentance.

A *meitivei* objection is raised. A *baraita* states: *hashav bein Rosh Hashanah l'Yom ha-Kippurim mochalin lo* — one who returns in *teshuva* between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur is forgiven. But if one does not do *teshuva* during that window — *afilu heivi kol eilei nevayot she-ba'olam ein mochalin lo* — even if he brings all the fat rams of the world as sacrifices, he will not be forgiven. The implication is that after the *gzar din* is finalized at Yom Kippur, no reversal is possible, which directly contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's position.

### Yachid/Tzibur Split

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, ha b'yachid ha b'tzibur* — it is not difficult; one source speaks of a *yachid*, an individual, and the other of a *tzibur*, a community. The principle emerging from this resolution is a significant one: *teshuva* can tear up a *gzar din* only for a *tzibur*, not for a *yachid*. After Yom Kippur, the *gzar din* of the individual stands. This is, admittedly, a radical qualification of Rabbi Yochanan's opening statement.

## Rain Decree Case

### Wicked→Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״עֵינֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בָּהּ״ — עִתִּים לְטוֹבָה עִתִּים לְרָעָה. עִתִּים לְטוֹבָה כֵּיצַד? הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִין בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וּפָסְקוּ לָהֶם גְּשָׁמִים מוּעָטִים, לְסוֹף חָזְרוּ בָּהֶן. לְהוֹסִיף עֲלֵיהֶן אִי אֶפְשָׁר — שֶׁכְּבָר נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵרָה, אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מוֹרִידָן בִּזְמַנָּן, עַל הָאָרֶץ הַצְּרִיכָה לָהֶן — הַכֹּל לְפִי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from another *baraita*: The verse states: "A land which the Lord your God cares for; **the eyes of the Lord your God are** always **upon it"** (Deuteronomy 11:12). God's constant providence over Eretz Yisrael is **sometimes for** the **good** and **sometimes for** the **bad. Sometimes for** the **good; how so? If the Jews were wholly wicked on Rosh HaShana, so that** only **a meager** amount of **rain was decreed for them** for the year, but **in the end they repented,** what can be done? **To add** rain **is impossible, as the decree was already issued. Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He, causes** those sparse rains **to fall at the** appropriate **times on land that needs it,** e.g., fields, vineyards, and gardens, **all according to** requirements of **the land,** and those rains are just as effective as a lot of rain.

A second *meitivei* objection now complicates the picture further. The *baraita* describes a scenario where God's inspection of the Land of Israel for good or ill operates throughout the year. The case for good: *harei she-hayu Yisrael resha'im gmurim b'Rosh Hashanah* — suppose Israel were completely wicked on Rosh Hashanah, and it was therefore decreed — *v'pasku lahem gshamim mu'atim* — that they would receive only a small amount of rain. Then, later in the year, *u-lesof chazru bahen* — they reversed themselves, they returned to God. Note the unusual language: the *baraita* uses *chazru*, "they reversed," rather than *asu teshuva*, "they did *teshuva*." The problem is now stark: *l'hosif aleihem i efshar, she-kvar nigzera gzeira* — you cannot add to the decree because the *gezeira* was already issued.

### Timing Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עִתִּים לְרָעָה כֵּיצַד? הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִין בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וּפָסְקוּ עֲלֵיהֶן גְּשָׁמִים מְרוּבִּין, לְסוֹף חָזְרוּ בָּהֶן. לִפְחוֹת מֵהֶן אִי אֶפְשָׁר — שֶׁכְּבָר נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵרָה, אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מוֹרִידָן שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּן, עַל הָאָרֶץ שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Sometimes for bad; how so? If the Jewish people were wholly righteous on Rosh HaShana, so that much rain was decreed for them** for that year, but **in the end they regressed** and sinned, what can be done? **To reduce** the amount of rain **is impossible, as the decree was already issued. Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He, causes** the rain **to fall not at the** appropriate **time,** and **on land that does not need it,** e.g., forests and deserts, and the ample rain is no more beneficial than meager rain.

Yet the *gzar din* is not simply left to operate harshly. The resolution: *ela Hakadosh Baruch Hu moridam bizmanan* — God brings the rain at precisely the right time, *al ha-aretz ha-tzricha lahen* — and upon exactly the lands that need it. Even a small quantity of rain, delivered with perfect timing and precision of location, can suffice. The *gezeira* stands, but through its optimal execution, the *teshuva* has its effect. The decree itself is not nullified, but it is, in a sense, fulfilled in the most beneficial way possible.

### Righteous→Backslide

The reverse scenario: *harei she-hayu Yisrael tzaddikim gmurim b'Rosh Hashanah* — suppose Israel were completely righteous on Rosh Hashanah, and it was decreed — *v'pasku lahem gshamim merubim* — that they would receive an abundant rainfall. Then they *chazru bahen* — they slid back into sin. Again the problem: *l'fachot meihem i efshar, she-kvar nigzera gzeira* — you cannot reduce what was decreed, because the *gezeira* was already issued.

### Bad Timing

Yet here too the system finds a way to register the change in conduct: *ela Hakadosh Baruch Hu moridam she-lo bizmanan* — God sends the rain at the wrong time, when rain does damage rather than benefit, *al aretz she-eina tzricha lahen* — on the land that does not need it. The great quantity of rain is delivered, faithfully fulfilling the decree, but its effects are largely nullified. Once again, the *gzar din* is honored in letter while the people's subsequent behavior has its consequence.

## Tear Up vs Within

### Challenge Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְטוֹבָה מִיהָא, לִיקְרְעֵיהּ לִגְזַר דִּינַיְיהוּ וְלוֹסֵיף לְהוּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאֶפְשָׁר בְּהָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: When it is **for good, however, let Him tear up their sentence and add** to the rain that had originally been decreed for them. Since the *baraita* explains that God does not do so, it implies that an evil sentence cannot be rescinded, even if the entire community fully repents. The Gemara answers: **It is different there, because it is possible** to achieve the same result **in this** manner, by causing the rain to fall at the proper time and in the proper place, without rescinding the original sentence.

The Gemara steps back and poses a sharp question. If, as Rabbi Yochanan teaches, *teshuva* genuinely has the power to *kera'* — to tear up — the *gzar din*, why in the case of the wicked who repented did God not simply tear up the decree of meager rain altogether? Why work within the constraint of the original judgment at all, adjusting only its timing and placement, rather than fully reversing it?

### System Preference

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam de'efshar behachi* — there it is different, because it was possible to achieve the desired result while working within the existing framework. The Gemara affirms, however, that *if* working within the system were not possible — *ai hachi nami* — God would indeed tear up the *gzar din* entirely. The operating principle is thus one of minimal intervention: as long as the decree can be fulfilled in a way that honors the changed circumstances, that approach is preferred. But the capacity to fully void a *gzar din* remains.

## Jonah & Psalms

### Jonah Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״יוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם בׇּאֳנִיּוֹת עוֹשֵׂי מְלָאכָה בְּמַיִם רַבִּים. הֵמָּה רָאוּ מַעֲשֵׂי ה׳ וְגוֹ׳ וַיֹּאמֶר וַיַּעֲמֵד רוּחַ סְעָרָה וַתְּרוֹמֵם גַּלָּיו. יָחוֹגּוּ וְיָנוּעוּ כַּשִּׁכּוֹר וְגוֹ׳ וַיִּצְעֲקוּ אֶל ה׳ בַּצַּר לָהֶם וְגוֹ׳ יוֹדוּ לַה׳ חַסְדּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from a different *baraita*: The psalm states: **"They that go down to the sea in ships, that do business in great waters; these saw the works of the Lord,** and His wonders in the deep"** (Psalms 107:23–24). The psalm continues: **"For He commanded, and raised the stormy wind, which lifted up the waves thereof…They reel to and fro, and stagger like a drunken man,** and are at their wits' end"** (Psalms 107:25–27). It continues further: **"Then they cry to the Lord in their trouble,** and He brings them out of their distresses"** (Psalms 107:28). And finally, **"Let them give thanks to the Lord for His mercy,** and for His wonderful works to the children of men"** (Psalms 107:31).

The Gemara notes that the reading of Sefer Yonah on Yom Kippur provides a clear instance of this very principle in action — God reverses His decree entirely in response to the repentance of a *tzibur*. The imagery of the Book of Yonah is precisely about a divine decree being issued and then revoked, and this is of course a *tzibur*, not a *yachid*, which is consistent with what has been established.

### Inverted Nuns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה לָהֶם סִימָנִיּוֹת כְּאַכִּין וְרַקִּין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, לוֹמַר לָךְ: צָעֲקוּ קוֹדֶם גְּזַר דִּין — נַעֲנִין, צָעֲקוּ לְאַחַר גְּזַר דִּין — אֵינָן נַעֲנִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Markers were inserted here** before each one of verses 23 through 28. These markers, which are the letter *nun* inverted, **are similar** in meaning **to the** words **but and only in the Torah,** to tell us that just as the Torah's words are precise and cannot be changed, so too, these people cannot have their sentence changed. This **tells you** that if **they cried out before the sentence** was issued, **they are answered,** but if **they cried out** only **after the sentence** was issued, **they are not answered.**

The Gemara then brings *ta shema* from the *pesukim* in Tehillim describing those who go down to the sea in ships — *yoredei hayam be'oniyot, osei melacha bemayim rabim* — those who do their work on the great waters. They see the wondrous works of God: *vayomar vaya'amod ruach se'ara vateromeim galav, yachogu veyanu'u kashikor* — a great storm wind arises, the waves mount high, the sailors stagger and reel like drunkards. Then: *vayitz'aku el Hashem batzar lahem* — they cry out to God in their distress. And God saves them. *Yodu l'Hashem chasdo* — they give thanks to God for His kindness.

Here the Gemara makes a striking observation. In the text of these *pesukim* in Tanakh, there appear special typographical markings — inverted *nun* letters — at the beginning of the relevant verses, precisely where the passage appears. The Gemara notes: *aseh lahen simaniyot k'achin ve'rachin she'baTorah* — God made these little signs, similar to the *achin* and *rachin* of the Torah, those words or markers which introduce a qualification or limitation. Rashi relates this to the inverted *nun* found in the passage of *vayehi binsoa* — it is exactly that form of textual indicator. These inverted *nun* letters function, as the Gemara puts it, like an asterisk attached to a grand promise: the verses describe God's magnificent salvation of those who cry out at sea, but the asterisk signals that this promise is not unconditional.

### Asterisk Meaning

The Gemara explains: *tza'aku kodem gezar din na'anin* — if they cried out to God before *gzar din*, they will be answered. *Tza'aku le'achar gezar din einan na'anin* — if they cried out after *gzar din*, they will not be answered. This would seem to cut against the earlier principle that a *tzibur* can always reverse its *gzar din*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי נָמֵי כִּיחִידִין דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is not difficult, for **those** who go down to the sea in ships **are also considered like individuals.** Even if there are many such people, they do not constitute an entire community but only a small defined group within it.

The Gemara responds: *hani nami keyichidim dami* — these sailors, even if many of them are aboard, are regarded as *yechidim*, as individuals, not as a *tzibur* in the relevant sense. They do not constitute a communal body whose collective repentance carries the force needed to overturn a communal decree.

## Akiva's Timing

### Bluriah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלָה בְּלוֹרְיָא הַגִּיּוֹרֶת אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, כְּתִיב בְּתוֹרַתְכֶם: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִשָּׂא פָנִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״יִשָּׂא ה׳ פָּנָיו אֵלֶיךָ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Come** and **hear: Beloreya the convert** once **asked Rabban Gamliel: It is written in your Torah:** "The great, mighty, and awesome God **who favors no one"** (Deuteronomy 10:17), **and** elsewhere **it is written: "The Lord shall show favor to you** and give you peace"** (Numbers 6: 26). How can this contradiction be resolved?

The Gemara now brings a further dispute. *Sha'ala Bluriah hagiyoret* — Bluriah the convert posed a challenge to Rabban Gamliel. She noted an apparent contradiction in the Torah: on the one hand, *asher lo yisa panim* — God does not show favor or partiality; on the other hand, *yisa Hashem panav eilecha* — God will lift His countenance toward you. Is God partial or is He not? It is worth noting that in the *She'iltot* the word *be'Torat'chem* — "in your Torah" — does not appear in this version of the story, since that formulation is typically used when a non-Jew challenges the rabbis, whereas Bluriah was herself a convert; perhaps the question was posed before her conversion.

### Debtor Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְפַּל לַהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַכֹּהֵן. אָמַר לָהּ: אֶמְשׁוֹל לָךָ מָשָׁל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לְאָדָם שֶׁנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה וְקָבַע לוֹ זְמַן בִּפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ בְּחַיֵּי הַמֶּלֶךְ, הִגִּיעַ זְמַן וְלֹא פְּרָעוֹ. בָּא לְפַיֵּיס אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: עֶלְבּוֹנִי מָחוּל לָךְ, לֵךְ וּפַיֵּיס אֶת חֲבֵירְךָ. הָכָא נָמֵי, כָּאן בַּעֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבֵּין אָדָם לְמָקוֹם — כָּאן בַּעֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבֵּין אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei the priest joined** the conversation with **her and said: I will tell you a parable. To what is this matter comparable? To a person who lent his friend one hundred** dinars **and fixed a time** for repayment of the loan **before the king, and** the borrower **took an oath by the life of the king** that he would repay the money. **The time arrived, and he did not repay** the loan. The delinquent borrower **came to appease the king** for not fulfilling the oath that he had sworn by the life of the king, **and** the king **said to him:** For **my insult I forgive you,** but **you must** still **go and appease your friend. Here also** the same is true: **Here,** the verse that states: "The Lord shall show favor to you," is referring to **sins** committed **between man and God,** which God will forgive; **there,** the verse that states: "God favors no one," is referring to **sins** committed **between a person and another,** which God will not forgive until the offender appeases the one he hurt.

Rabbi Yosei HaKohen was designated to respond. He offered a *mashal*: *emshol lakh mashal l'mah hadavar domeh* — to what may this be compared? To a person who lent his friend a hundred *maneh*, and they went before the king, and the borrower swore by the life of the king that he would repay by a certain date. The time arrived and he did not pay. He then came before the king to seek forgiveness. The king said to him: *elboni machul lach* — my personal offense I forgive you; *lech u-payes et chaveircha* — go and appease your friend who is owed the money, for that debt remains outstanding.

### God vs Man

The application: *kan bein adam la-makom kan bein adam la-chavero* — the two verses refer to two different categories of sin. When it comes to sins *bein adam la-makom*, between a person and God, *yisa Hashem panav eilecha* — God lifts His countenance and forgives. But *asher lo yisa panim* — God will not show favor when the sin is *bein adam la-chavero*, between one person and another, and the human victim has not yet been appeased.

### Akiva Resolve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְלִימֵּד: כָּאן קוֹדֶם גְּזַר דִּין, כָּאן לְאַחַר גְּזַר דִּין! הָכָא נָמֵי בְּיָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** This is how the contradiction had at first been resolved, **until Rabbi Akiva came and taught** as follows: **Here** the verse is referring to the time **before** one's **sentence** is issued, when God shows favor and forgives; and **there** the verse is referring to the time **after** the **sentence** has been issued, when He no longer forgives. This implies that after a sentence has been issued, there is no possibility of repentance, which seems to contradict the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan. The Gemara answers: **Here too** it is referring to **an individual,** but a community is granted forgiveness even after its sentence has been issued.

Rabbi Akiva, however, offered a different resolution. He read the two verses as referring not to two categories of sin, but to two temporal stages: *kan kodem gzar din kan le'achar gzar din* — one verse speaks of before *gzar din*, the other of after *gzar din*. Before *gzar din*, God will forgive; after *gzar din*, He will not. The Gemara responds that this still does not contradict the earlier framework: *hacha nami be-yachid* — here too we are speaking of a *yachid*. Rabbi Akiva's rule thus applies to the individual, consistent with what has been established throughout.

## Yachid Reversible

### Twin Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּגְזַר דִּין דְּיָחִיד תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁעָלוּ לַמִּטָּה וְחוֹלְיָין שָׁוֶה, וְכֵן שְׁנַיִם שֶׁעָלוּ לַגַּרְדּוֹם לִידּוֹן וְדִינָן שָׁוֶה. זֶה יָרַד וְזֶה לֹא יָרַד, זֶה נִיצַּל וְזֶה לֹא נִיצַּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The question of whether or not **an individual's sentence** can be rescinded **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: Two** people **take to** their **beds, and their illness is the same, or two** people **ascend to the tribunal [*gardom*] for judgment, and their** potential **sentence is the same;** but **this** one **comes down** from his bed, **while that** one **does not come down from his bed, and this** one **is saved** from death, **while that** one **is not saved.**

Having repeatedly reached the conclusion that after *gzar din* a *yachid* cannot reverse the decree, the Gemara now signals that this is itself a matter of Talmudic dispute. *Ve-gzar din de-yachid tannai hi* — the question of whether the *gzar din* of a *yachid* can be overturned is a *machloket tannaim*, a dispute among the Tannaim. The *baraita* presents two parallel cases: *shnayim she-alu le-mita ve-cholyan shaveh* — two people who fell ill with the same illness, and *shnayim she-alu le-gardom* — two people brought before the court to be judged for a capital crime (*gardom*, from the Latin, refers to the place of execution, something like a gallows). In both cases the two individuals were in identical situations — same illness, same verdict — yet their outcomes diverged: one recovered and one died; one was acquitted and one was executed.

### Meir's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי מָה זֶה יָרַד וְזֶה לֹא יָרַד, זֶה נִיצַּל וְזֶה לֹא נִיצַּל? זֶה הִתְפַּלֵּל וְנַעֲנָה, וְזֶה הִתְפַּלֵּל וְלֹא נַעֲנָה. מִפְּנֵי מָה זֶה נַעֲנָה וְזֶה לֹא נַעֲנָה? זֶה הִתְפַּלֵּל תְּפִלָּה שְׁלֵימָה — נַעֲנָה, וְזֶה לֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל תְּפִלָּה שְׁלֵימָה — לֹא נַעֲנָה
>
> **Translation:** **For what** reason **did this** one recover and **come down** from his bed, **while that** one **did not** recover and **come down from his bed;** and why **was this one saved** from death, **while that one was not saved?** The difference between them is that **this** one **prayed and was answered, while that** one **prayed, but was not answered. And for what** reason **was this** one **answered and that** one **not answered? This** one **prayed a prayer** with his **whole** heart and consequently **was answered, while that** one **did not pray a prayer** with his **whole** heart and therefore **was not answered.**

Rabbi Meir's explanation for this divergence: the difference was not in what happened externally but in the quality of prayer. *Zeh hitpalel tefilla shleima ne'eneh ve-zeh lo hitpalel tefilla shleima lo ne'eneh* — one prayed a complete, full prayer and was answered; the other did not pray a complete prayer and was not answered. This raises the deeper question of why one prayer was answered and another not — Rabbi Meir's answer is the qualitative difference in the *tefilla* itself. It is an attempt to account for the variance in outcome, attributing it to the depth and wholeness of the individual's supplication.

### Elazar Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: כָּאן קוֹדֶם גְּזַר דִּין, כָּאן לְאַחַר גְּזַר דִּין. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: יָפָה צְעָקָה לָאָדָם בֵּין קוֹדֶם גְּזַר דִּין בֵּין לְאַחַר גְּזַר דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** Not so; rather, **here** he prayed **before** his heavenly **sentence** was issued, and so he was answered, whereas **there** the other one prayed **after** his heavenly **sentence** was issued, and therefore he was not answered. **Rabbi Yitzḥak** disagreed and **said: Crying out** to God **is effective for a person, both before** his **sentence** has been issued **and** also **after** his **sentence** has been issued, as even after his sentence has been issued, it can still be rescinded if he repents.

Rabbi Elazar offers an entirely different explanation: *kan kodem gzar din kan le'achar gzar din* — perhaps one of the individuals had already been assigned a *gzar din* by Rosh Hashanah, while the other had not. Their situations looked identical on earth, but in heaven one had an unalterable decree against him and one did not. On this reading, the *gzar din* of the *yachid* cannot be reversed — the variation in outcome simply reflects a difference in what was decreed on Rosh Hashanah, not any differential response to prayer.

### Yitzchak Both Times

Rabbi Yitzchak, however, takes the position we have been looking for: *yafa tze'aka la-adam bein kodem gzar din bein le'achar gzar din* — crying out is beneficial for a person both before *gzar din* and after *gzar din*. Notice that Rabbi Yitzchak uses the language of *tze'aka*, crying out, echoing the earlier context of the sailors at sea and the language of urgent supplication, rather than specifically *teshuva*. His position is that even for a *yachid*, even after a *gzar din* has been issued, there remains the possibility of reversal. He does not resolve the question of why one person survived and another did not — but he establishes the principle that the gate is never fully closed for the individual either.

## Tzibur Gezar Din

### Jeremiah Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּגְזַר דִּין דְּצִבּוּר מִי מִיקְּרַע? וְהָא כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״כַּבְּסִי מֵרָעָה לִבֵּךְ״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי אִם תְּכַבְּסִי בַּנֶּתֶר וְתַרְבִּי לָךְ בּוֹרִית נִכְתָּם עֲוֹנֵךְ לְפָנַי״, מַאי לָאו: כָּאן קוֹדֶם גְּזַר דִּין, כָּאן לְאַחַר גְּזַר דִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can a sentence of a community** really **be torn up** because they have repented? **But one verse says:** "O Jerusalem, **wash your heart from wickedness,** that you may be saved"** (Jeremiah 4:14), **and** elsewhere **it is written: "For though you wash yourself with lye, and use much soap, yet the stain of your iniquity is before Me,** says the Lord God"** (Jeremiah 2:22). **What, is it not** that **here** the verse is referring to the time **before the sentence,** when the heart can still be washed with repentance, whereas **there** the verse is referring to the time **after the sentence,** when washing no longer helps, as the sentence cannot be canceled?

Having now affirmed that for the *yachid* the matter is debated, the Gemara pivots to the *tzibur* — which we have been treating as the straightforward case for reversibility — and asks: *ve-gzar din d'tzibur mi mikra?* — who says the *gzar din* of a *tzibur* can be torn up at all? The proof comes from a pair of verses in Yirmiyahu that appear to contradict one another. One verse says: *kabb'si me'ra'ah libbech* — cleanse your heart from evil, implying that atonement is possible. The other says: *im t'khabb'si baneter v'tarbi lach borit nikhtam avonech l'fanai* — even if you scrub yourself with lye and use abundant soap, your sin remains stained before Me. The second verse seems to say that no amount of purification can remove the sin — suggesting that even for the *tzibur* there is a point of no return.

### Shevu'ah Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְאַחַר גְּזַר דִּין, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּגְזַר דִּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה, כָּאן בִּגְזַר דִּין שֶׁאֵין עִמּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** both **this** verse **and that** verse refer to the time **after the sentence** has been decreed, **and** still it is **not difficult: Here** the verse is referring to **a sentence accompanied by an oath** taken by God not to cancel the sentence, whereas **there** the verse is referring to **a sentence that is not accompanied by** God's **oath** not to cancel the sentence, and so the sentence can in fact be canceled through repentance.

The Gemara resolves: *lo kashya, kan b'gezar din she'yesh immo shevu'ah, kan b'gezar din she'ein immo shevu'ah* — it is not difficult; the two verses refer to different categories of *gzar din*. One speaks of a *gzar din* without a *shevu'ah*, and there the *tzibur* can have its decree torn up. The other speaks of a *gzar din* that is accompanied by a divine oath, a *shevu'ah*, and there even the collective cannot reverse the decree. The Gemara has thus introduced yet another layer in the hierarchy of divine judgment: the question is not only whether a decree has been issued, not only whether it has been sealed, not only whether it applies to an individual or community — but also whether God has bound the decree with an oath.

## Shevu'ah Oath

### Eli Oath Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּדְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַמֵּי. דְּאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַמֵּי. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַב יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִן לִגְזַר דִּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִקְרָע — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״[וְ]לָכֵן נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי לְבֵית עֵלִי אִם יִתְכַּפֵּר עֲוֹן בֵּית עֵלִי בְּזֶבַח וּבְמִנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like** what **Rav Shmuel bar Ami** said, **as Rav Shmuel bar Ami** said, **and some say** that it was **Rav Shmuel bar Naḥmani** who **said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: From where** is it derived **that a sentence accompanied by** God's **oath** not to cancel it **cannot be torn up** or canceled? **As it is stated: "And therefore I have sworn to the house of Eli, that the iniquity of Eli's house will not be purged with sacrifice nor offering forever"** (I Samuel 3:14).

The Gemara now asks: from where do we know that there is such a category as a *gzar din* accompanied by a *shevu'ah* that cannot be torn up? The proof text: *ul'khen nishba'ti l'veit Eli im yitkapper avon beit Eli b'zevach u'v'minchah* — I have sworn concerning the house of Eli that the sin of the house of Eli will not be atoned for by sacrifice or meal-offering. God here explicitly takes an oath that the decree against the house of Eli is irrevocable, at least through the means of sacrifice. This is the source for the category of *gzar din she'yesh immo shevu'ah*.

### Torah Loophole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּזֶבַח וּבַמִּנְחָה אֵינוֹ מִתְכַּפֵּר — אֲבָל מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּתוֹרָה. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּזֶבַח וּמִנְחָה אֵינוֹ מִתְכַּפֵּר — אֲבָל מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּתוֹרָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this verse **Rava said: With sacrifice or offering** the sin of Eli's house **is not atoned, but it can be atoned through Torah** study. **Abaye said: With sacrifice or offering** the sin of Eli's house **is not atoned, but it is atoned through Torah** study **and** the performance of **acts of kindness.**

But the Gemara will not let this stand unchallenged either. Rava says: *b'zevach u'v'minchah eino mitkapper, aval mitkapper b'Torah* — the sin cannot be atoned through sacrifice or meal-offering, as the oath specifies, but it *can* be atoned through Torah study. The oath closes one door but leaves another open — the *shevu'ah* was specific to the mode of *kaparah*, not to the possibility of atonement altogether.

### Chesed Factor

Abaye goes further: *b'zevach u'v'minchah eino mitkapper, aval mitkapper b'Torah u'v'gemilut chasadim* — it can be atoned through Torah and through *gemilut chasadim*, acts of lovingkindness. Abaye adds to Rava's formulation the dimension of ethical engagement with the world beyond the *beit midrash*.

### Eli Descendants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְאַבָּיֵי מִדְּבֵית עֵלִי קָאָתוּ. רַבָּה דַּעֲסַק בַּתּוֹרָה — חֲיָה אַרְבְּעִין שְׁנִין, אַבָּיֵי דַּעֲסַק בְּתוֹרָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים — חֲיָה שִׁיתִּין שְׁנִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Rabba and Abaye came from the house of Eli,** which was subject to the curse that most of its members would die young. **Rabba, who engaged** almost exclusively **in Torah** study, **lived for forty years,** whereas **Abaye, who engaged in** both **Torah** study **and in** the performance of **acts of kindness lived for sixty years.**

The reason this is not merely theoretical becomes apparent immediately: *Rabbah v'Abaye mi'devei Eli ka'atu* — Rabbah and Abaye were both descendants of the house of Eli. The question of whether the curse of Eli's line could be mitigated was, for them, an entirely personal one. It should be noted that there is a question in the *rishonim* whether the first statement should be attributed to Rava or Rabbah — most prefer Rabbah, since Eli was a *kohen* and Rava was not, though Rashi notes that perhaps through his mother's line Rava too descended from Beit Eli, which would raise the interesting question of whether the curse followed the maternal line as well.

### Lifespan Evidence

The Gemara provides a striking demonstration of the differential effect of Torah alone versus Torah together with *gemilut chasadim*. *Rabbah d'asak b'Torah chaya arba'in shanin* — Rabbah, who engaged primarily in Torah learning, lived forty years. *Abaye d'asak b'Torah u'v'gemilut chasadim chaya shitin shanin* — Abaye, who engaged in Torah *and* in *gemilut chasadim*, lived sixty years. The context matters: without any *kaparah* at all, members of Eli's line apparently died far younger than even forty, as the subsequent *baraita* will show. Even forty years represented a partial atonement through Torah; sixty represented a fuller one through Torah combined with active kindness.

Tosafot notes a potential tension here: in *Perek Chelek*, Abaye himself says to Rabbah that the master possesses Torah *and* *gemilut chasadim* — implying that Rabbah too had significant *gemilut chasadim*. Tosafot resolves this by saying that *mikol makom Abaye asik tifei minei* — nevertheless, Abaye was more *oseik* in it than Rabbah was. There is a meaningful distinction between performing acts of kindness and making *gemilut chasadim* a defining commitment and orientation of one's religious life. Abaye had made it such a commitment, and the lifespan differential reflects it.

This is, it should be noted, a remarkably powerful statement emanating from within the rabbinic world itself: the difference between a life lived entirely within the *beit midrash* and a life in which Torah study propels one outward into acts of care for others carries, according to this *gemara*, real and measurable consequence.

## Eli Family Torah

### Death at 18

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִשְׁפָּחָה אַחַת הָיְתָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ מֵתֶיהָ מֵתִין בְּנֵי שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה. בָּאוּ וְהוֹדִיעוּ אֶת רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, אָמַר לָהֶם: שֶׁמָּא מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת עֵלִי אַתֶּם, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״וְכׇל מַרְבִּית בֵּיתְךָ יָמוּתוּ אֲנָשִׁים״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There was a certain family in Jerusalem whose members used to die at the age of eighteen,** and they did not know why. **They came and told Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai** about their situation. **He said to them: Perhaps you** are descended **from the family of Eli, as it is written about them: "And all the increase of your house shall die young men"** (I Samuel 2:33). If indeed this is so, the remedy is as follows:

The Gemara now presents a *baraita* that provides concrete context for the severity of the curse upon Eli's house. *Mishpacha achas haysa bi-Yerushalayim she-hayu meseha mesim bnei yud ches shanah* — there was a family in Jerusalem in which all the young men died at eighteen years of age. The family came and reported this pattern to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai.

### Zakkai Diagnosis

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai asked them: *shema mi-mishpachat Eli atem?* — perhaps you are from the family of Eli? He cited the verse: *ve-chol marbis beischa yamusun anashim* — all the multitude of your house shall die as men in their prime. His response to their plight was direct: *lechu ve-isku ba-Torah va-chyu* — go and engage in Torah study, and you will live.

### Torah Success

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכוּ וְעִסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה וִחְיוּ. הָלְכוּ וְעָסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה וְחָיוּ. וְהָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתָהּ מִשְׁפַּחַת רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן עַל שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Go and engage in Torah** study, in the merit of which **you will live. They went and engaged in Torah** study **and lived. And** people **would call that family** afterward **by the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan** in his honor.

*Halchu ve-isku ba-Torah va-chyu* — they went, they learned Torah, and they lived. *Ve-hayu korin osam mishpachat Yochanan al shemo* — they were thereafter called the family of Yochanan, in his honor. It is worth noting that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai prescribed Torah alone, without explicitly adding *gemilut chasadim* — though this need not be read as a contradiction to Abaye's formulation. One might also observe the broader symbolic resonance: the *korbanos* that once atoned, and whose channel was closed by the oath, have been replaced in the post-*churban* world by Torah study — itself understood as the successor to the sacrificial service. The sin of Beit Eli was rooted in the abuse of priestly power; its remedy is found in the democratizing, non-hierarchical pursuit of *talmud Torah*.

## Tzibur Sealed

### Never Sealed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אִינְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: מִנַּיִין לִגְזַר דִּין שֶׁל צִבּוּר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶחְתָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Inya said in the name of Rav: From where** is it derived **that the sentence of a community is never sealed [*neḥtam*]?**

Rav Shmuel bar Inya, citing Rav, introduces a further principle: *minayin le-gezar din shel tzibur she-eino nechtam?* — from where do we know that the *gzar din* of a *tzibur* is never finally sealed? The implication is remarkable: not only can the communal decree be torn up even after issuance, but it is never truly sealed in the first place — the door always remains open.

## Tzibur Sealed (II)

### Sealed Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵינוֹ נֶחְתָּם?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״נִכְתַּם עֲוֹנֵךְ לְפָנַי״! אֶלָּא: אַף עַל גַּב שֶׁנֶּחְתָּם — נִקְרָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ בְּכׇל קׇרְאֵנוּ אֵלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara immediately asks: **Is never sealed? But isn't it written: "Yet the stain [*nikhtam*] of your iniquity is before Me"** (Jeremiah 2:22), which implies that the sentence of a community is indeed sealed. **Rather,** one must say that the question was as follows: From where is it known with regard to the sentence of a community that **although it is sealed, it can** still **be torn up? As it is stated: "As is the Lord our God whenever we call out to Him"** (Deuteronomy 4:7). This implies that there is always a way to draw close to God.

The Gemara immediately challenges this claim. The very verse cited earlier — *nechtam avonech lefanai* — your sin is sealed before Me, with *nechtam* serving as a play on *nechtam* — seems to imply that there is such a thing as a sealed communal decree. This was, after all, Yirmiyahu's address to the entire people.

### Can Tear Up

The Gemara resolves: *ela af al gav she-nechtam nikra* — even if it is sealed, for a *tzibur* the *gzar din* can still be torn up. The sealing does not carry the same finality for a community as it might for an individual. The *tzibur* retains the capacity to reverse even what has been sealed against it.

### Isaiah Proof

The proof text: *ka-Hashem Elokeinu be-chol koreinu eilav* — like Hashem our God, whenever we call out to Him. The *be-chol* — whenever, at all times — establishes the permanent and unconditional availability of divine responsiveness to communal prayer, even in the face of a sealed decree.

## Yachid Seek Days

### When Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״דִּרְשׁוּ ה׳ בְּהִמָּצְאוֹ״! הָתָם בְּיָחִיד, הָכָא בְּצִבּוּר. בְּיָחִיד, אֵימַת? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֵלּוּ עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים שֶׁבֵּין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "Seek the Lord while He may be found; call upon Him when He is near"** (Isaiah 55:6), which implies that there are times when He is not near and does not answer. The Gemara answers: **There** the verse is referring to **an individual,** to whom God is near only at certain times; **here** the verse is referring to **a community,** to which God is close whenever the people call out to Him. § The Gemara asks: With regard to **an individual, when** is God near to him? **Rabba bar Avuh said: These are the ten days between Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur.**

The Gemara then takes up the verse from Yeshayahu: *dirshu Hashem be-himmatz'o* — seek God when He can be found. The word *be-himmatz'o*, "when He can be found," introduces a temporal qualification: God is not equally available at all moments. For the *tzibur*, as just established, He is available *be-chol koreinu eilav* — at all times. But the verse *dirshu Hashem be-himmatz'o* applies to the *yachid*, for whom divine receptivity is circumscribed by timing.

### Ten Days RH-YK

*Amar Rabbah bar Avuha: elu asarah yamim shebein Rosh Hashanah le-Yom ha-Kippurim* — these are the ten days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. It should be noted that there are not literally ten full days between the two holidays — there are seven or eight, depending on whether Rosh Hashanah is observed for one or two days. The count of ten is arrived at inclusively, counting Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves. These *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah* constitute the window during which even the *yachid* finds God particularly receptive, and during which the *gzar din* of the individual can potentially be reversed.

### Naval Reprieve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי כַּעֲשֶׂרֶת הַיָּמִים וַיִּגֹּף ה׳ אֶת נָבָל״ — עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּנֶגֶד עֶשֶׂר לְגִימוֹת שֶׁנָּתַן נָבָל לְעַבְדֵי דָּוִד. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֵלּוּ עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים שֶׁבֵּין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: The verse states: **"And it came to pass about ten days after that the Lord smote Nabal, and he died"** (I Samuel 25:38). These **ten days, what are they doing here,** i.e., why was there a delay of ten days before Nabal died? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: They correspond to the ten meals that Nabal gave the servants of David** who came to visit him, as out of politeness he allowed David's ten servants to eat, and therefore his punishment was delayed for ten days. **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: These are the ten days between Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur,** during which everyone is given one last opportunity to repent for the sins he committed over the course of the previous year.

A proof for this ten-day framework is adduced from the story of Naval: *vayehi ke'aseret hayamim vayigof Hashem et Naval* — it was after ten days that God smote Naval. The Gemara asks: *yud yemei mai avidtaihu?* — what was the significance of those ten days? Rav Yehuda citing Rav answers: they corresponded to the ten meals Naval had given to David's servants — Rashi understands this not as ten separate occasions but as ten people served at one meal, yet constituting ten discrete acts of hospitality, which earned Naval a ten-day reprieve. Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha, however, offers the answer that ties directly to our discussion: *elu asarah yamim shebein Rosh Hashanah le-Yom ha-Kippurim* — God granted Naval the ten days of the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah* as an opportunity to do *teshuva*, and only after those days had elapsed and he had failed to repent did God bring His judgment upon him.

## RH Procession

### Bnei Maron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה כׇּל בָּאֵי הָעוֹלָם עוֹבְרִין לְפָנָיו כִּבְנֵי מָרוֹן. מַאי ״כִּבְנֵי מָרוֹן״? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ: כִּבְנֵי אִמְּרָנָא. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כְּמַעֲלוֹת בֵּית מָרוֹן. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כַּחֲיָילוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית דָּוִד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **On Rosh HaShana all creatures pass before Him like *benei maron*.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the phrase ***benei maron*?** The Gemara answers: **Here** in Babylonia **they interpreted it** to mean: **Like a flock of sheep [*kivnei imarna*]. Reish Lakish** disagreed and **said: Like the ascent of Beit Maron,** which was very steep; one standing at the summit could discern all those climbing the mountain with a single look. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said** another opinion: **Like the soldiers of the house of** King **David,** who could be surveyed with a single glance.

The Gemara now returns to the *mishna*, which states: *be-Rosh Hashanah kol ba'ei olam overin lefanav kivnei maron* — on Rosh Hashanah all those who enter the world pass before God like *bnei maron*. The phrase *kivnei maron* requires explanation.

### Sheep Counting

In Bavel, the phrase was translated as *kivnei amrana*, and Rashi explains: *kekvasim shemonin otam le'asran, veyotzin zeh achar zeh befetach katan she'ein yecholim latzet k'echad* — like sheep that are counted for the tithe of *ma'aser behema*, passing single file through a narrow opening, one after another, since they cannot exit together. This is the image of God as shepherd passing the flock through individually, counting and assessing each one.

### Mountain Pass

Reish Lakish explains *kivnei maron* differently: *kema'alot Beit Maron* — like those ascending the ascents of Beit Maron, a narrow mountain road where, with a deep valley on either side, travelers must proceed one at a time, with a sheer drop on both sides. ArtScroll and Steinsaltz suggest this may refer specifically to Har Meron in northern Israel, which has just such a steep and dangerous path. This is arguably the most viscerally frightening of the three images: one walks a narrow precipice, a small misstep sending one plunging into the valley below. It captures the sense of life hanging by a thread, the precariousness of one's judgment.

### Soldiers Ready

Rav Yehuda, citing Shmuel, offers the third interpretation: *k'chayalot shel Beit David* — like the soldiers of the house of David. Rashi connects *maron* to *lashon marut v'adnut*, a language of mastery and lordship. But Steinsaltz notes that manuscripts read *kivnumeron* rather than *kivnei maron*, and that the word derives from the Latin *numerus*, borrowed also into Greek as *numeron*, meaning a military unit — *anshei tzava*. Moreover, there exists in Greek the expression of people standing *like a numeron*, as soldiers stand for inspection. This etymology almost certainly underlies Rav Yehuda's reading. The imagery here is one of pride and readiness: soldiers who have prepared themselves, polished their equipment, and stand at attention for inspection. It evokes a very different emotional posture from the other two — not the helplessness of the sheep nor the terror of the precipice, but the dignity of one who has done the work and stands ready to be reviewed.

Each of these three images captures something distinct about the experience of standing in divine judgment on Rosh Hashanah: the trusting helplessness of the sheep before the shepherd; the terrifying fragility of the mountain pass; the prepared, upright bearing of the soldier before his king.

### One Glance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְכוּלָּן נִסְקָרִין בִּסְקִירָה אַחַת. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: ״הַיּוֹצֵר יַחַד לִבָּם הַמֵּבִין אֶל כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם״. מַאי קָאָמַר? אִילֵּימָא הָכִי קָאָמַר: דִּבְרַנְהוּ לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא וּמְיַיחֵד לִבַּיְיהוּ כַּהֲדָדָי — וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּלָאו הָכִי הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַיּוֹצֵר רוֹאֶה יַחַד לִבָּם וּמֵבִין אֶל כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And they are all scanned in a single scan. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: We, too, learn** this in the *baraita*: The verse states: **"He who fashions their hearts alike, who considers all their deeds"** (Psalms 33:15). **What is this** verse **saying? If we say this is what it is saying: That He created everyone and unites** all **their hearts together,** there is a difficulty, **since don't we see that it is not so,** as the hearts of people are not united and are not similar to one another? **Rather, is this not what it is saying: The Creator sees their hearts together and considers all their deeds** with a single scan?

Having described how all of humanity passes in procession before God — individually, one by one — the Gemara immediately adds the counterpoint. *Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: v'chulan niskarin b'skira achat* — and yet all of them are surveyed in a single glance. God beholds every human being individually, yet encompasses them all simultaneously. The judgment is personal and particular, and it is also total and instantaneous.

### Mishnah Support

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak notes that this principle is already implicit in the *mishna* itself, which closes with the verse *hayotzer yachad libam hamevin el kol ma'aseihem* — He who fashions their hearts as one, who understands all their deeds. The Gemara clarifies: the verse does not mean that God unifies all human hearts into a single unity — we observe that the world is manifestly not unified in that way. Rather, *hayotzer yachad libam* means that God sees all hearts as one, *ro'eh yachad libam*, perceiving every individual heart simultaneously, *u-mevin el kol ma'aseihem* — and understands every deed of every person at once. The many are seen individually; the many are also seen together. Both are true.

## Six Rosh Chodesh

### Six Month List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עַל שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים הַשְּׁלוּחִין יוֹצְאִין: עַל נִיסָן מִפְּנֵי הַפֶּסַח, עַל אָב מִפְּנֵי הַתַּעֲנִית, עַל אֱלוּל מִפְּנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, עַל תִּשְׁרֵי מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַמּוֹעֲדוֹת, עַל כִּסְלֵיו מִפְּנֵי חֲנוּכָּה, וְעַל אֲדָר מִפְּנֵי הַפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **In six months** of the year **the messengers go out** from the court in Jerusalem to report throughout Eretz Yisrael and the Diaspora which day was established as the New Moon, the thirtieth or the thirty-first day since the previous New Moon. They go out **in** the month of **Nisan, due to Passover,** so that people will know on which day to celebrate it; **in** the month of **Av, due to the fast** of the Ninth of Av; **in Elul, due to Rosh HaShana,** which begins thirty days after the New Moon of Elul; **in Tishrei, due to the** need to establish the **correct** dates on which to celebrate **the Festivals** of Tishrei, i.e., Yom Kippur and *Sukkot*; **in Kislev, due to Hanukkah; and in Adar, due to Purim.**

The *mishna* continues: *al shisha devarim ha-shuluchim yotzim* — on account of six matters, messengers go out to announce the *rosh chodesh* to distant communities. The six months are: Nisan, on account of Pesach; Av, on account of the fast; Elul, on account of Rosh Hashanah — since one cannot send messengers on Rosh Hashanah itself to announce when Rosh Hashanah is, messengers are sent after *rosh chodesh Elul*, and since Elul is almost always twenty-nine days, communities can calculate Rosh Hashanah accordingly; Tishrei, on account of the regulation of the holidays — after Rosh Hashanah, messengers are sent out to confirm or correct the date of *rosh chodesh Tishrei* so that the subsequent *mo'adim* can be properly observed; Kislev, on account of Chanukah; and Adar, on account of Purim. Shavuot does not require its own messengers because it is calculated from Pesach.

### Temple Pesach 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהָיָה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים — יוֹצְאִין אַף עַל אִיָּיר מִפְּנֵי פֶּסַח קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And when the Temple was standing,** messengers **would also go out in** the month of **Iyyar due to small Passover,** i.e., second *Pesaḥ*, which occurs on the fourteenth of Iyyar. This holiday allowed those who were ritually impure or on a distant journey on the fourteenth of Nisan, and therefore incapable of bringing the Paschal lamb at that time, to bring their Paschal lamb a month later.

The *mishna* adds: *ve-cheshe-haya beit ha-mikdash kayam, yotzim af al Iyar mipnei pesach katan* — when the Temple stood, messengers also went out for Iyar, on account of *Pesach Sheni*, so that people who had been unable to bring the *korban pesach* in Nisan would know the correct date on which to bring it in Iyar. This detail confirms that the *mishna*'s framing is from a post-*churban* perspective — the *pesach* mentioned in the context of Nisan refers to *chag ha-matzot*, not to the *korban pesach*, since the special provision for the Temple period is noted separately.

## Six Rosh Chodesh (II)

### Minor Fasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלִיפְּקוּ נָמֵי אַתַּמּוּז וְטֵבֵת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And** if they go out for the month of Av due to the fast, **let them go out also** in the months of **Tammuz and Tevet,** as they too contain public fast days.

The Gemara asks: *ve-lifok nami ateivet ve-tamuz?* — why not also send out messengers for Tevet and Tammuz, so that communities know when to observe the fasts of *asarah b'Tevet* and *shiva asar b'Tammuz*? This leads into a broader discussion of the halakhic status of the minor fast days.

## Zechariah Fasts

### Four Fasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת צוֹם הָרְבִיעִי וְצוֹם הַחֲמִישִׁי וְצוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי וְצוֹם הָעֲשִׂירִי יִהְיֶה לְבֵית יְהוּדָה לְשָׂשׂוֹן וּלְשִׂמְחָה״. קָרֵי לְהוּ ״צוֹם״, וְקָרֵי לְהוּ ״שָׂשׂוֹן וְשִׂמְחָה״!
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Ḥana bar Bizna said** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Thus said the Lord of hosts: The fast of the fourth month, and the fast of the fifth, and fast of the seventh, and the fast of the tenth, shall become times of joy and gladness,** and cheerful seasons, **to the house of Judah"** (Zechariah 8:19). **It calls them** days of **"fast" and it calls them** "times of **joy and gladness."** How so?

The Gemara cites Rav Chana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida, who asks: what is the meaning of the verse in Zechariah — *ko amar Hashem Tzevaos: tzom ha-revi'i ve-tzom ha-chamishi ve-tzom ha-shvi'i ve-tzom ha-asiri yihiyeh le-veit Yehudah le-sason u-le-simcha* — so says the Lord of Hosts: the fast of the fourth, the fast of the fifth, the fast of the seventh, and the fast of the tenth shall become for the house of Judah days of joy and gladness. The prophecy was delivered in the context of the return from exile and the rebuilding of the Temple, when the people asked whether the fasts they had observed through the period of destruction should continue. The answer was that those fast days would be transformed into days of rejoicing.

But the verse creates an internal tension: *kari lei tzom ve-kari lei sason ve-simcha* — it calls the same days both a fast and a time of joy and gladness. The Gemara treats this as a difficulty requiring resolution.

### Peace Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁלוֹם — יִהְיוּ לְשָׂשׂוֹן וּלְשִׂמְחָה, יֵשׁ שְׁמָד — צוֹם, אֵין שְׁמָד וְאֵין שָׁלוֹם — רָצוּ מִתְעַנִּין, רָצוּ אֵין מִתְעַנִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that **this is what it is saying: When there is peace** in the world and the Temple is standing, these days **will be** times of **joy and gladness;** when **there is persecution** and troubles for the Jewish people, they are days of **fasting;** and when **there is no persecution but** still **no peace,** neither particular troubles nor consolation for Israel, the *halakha* is as follows: If people **wish, they fast,** and if **they wish, they do not fast.** Since there is no absolute obligation to fast, messengers are not sent out for these months.

Rav Pappa resolves it: *biz'man she-yesh shalom, yihyu le-sason u-le-simcha* — when there is true peace, when times are good and the Temple stands, those days are transformed into days of joy. *Yesh gzeiras ha-malchus* — when there is governmental persecution, when the people are actively oppressed, *tzom* — they are observed as fast days. This is the extreme of suffering: the fast days are mandatory expressions of mourning under conditions of oppression.

### Three States

*Ein gzeiras ha-malchus, ein shalom* — when there is neither active oppression nor true peace — in other words, the condition of exile without persecution, roughly describing much of Jewish history in the diaspora — then *ratzu mis'anim ratzu ein mis'anim*: if the community wishes to fast, it may; if not, it is not obligated. The minor fast days in such a middle-ground period are optional. This has genuine halakhic implications: in principle, under conditions that are neither shalom nor governmental persecution, an individual or even a community could elect not to observe the minor fasts.

### Tisha B'Av Special

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב נָמֵי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שָׁאנֵי תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב, הוֹאִיל וְהוּכְפְּלוּ בּוֹ צָרוֹת. דְּאָמַר מָר: בְּתִשְׁעָה בְּאָב חָרַב הַבַּיִת בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה וּבַשְּׁנִיָּה, וְנִלְכְּדָה בֵּיתָר, וְנֶחֶרְשָׁה הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, the Ninth of Av** should **also** be like the other fast days, that sometimes it is observed and sometimes not, depending upon the wishes of the community at the time. Why does the mishna state that messengers go out for the month of Av? **Rav Pappa said: The Ninth of Av is different, since the calamities** that occurred on that day **were multiplied. As the Master said: On the Ninth of Av the Temple was destroyed,** both **the first** one **and the second** one; on this day the city of **Beitar was captured;** and on this day **the city** of Jerusalem **was plowed** over by the enemies of the Jewish people, as a sign that it would never be rebuilt. Consequently, the fast of the Ninth of Av is obligatory, and not optional like the other fasts. Messengers are consequently sent out so that people will know when to fast.

*Ei hachi Tisha B'Av nami?* — if that is so, the same logic should apply to Tisha B'Av, which also appears in the Zechariah verse. The Gemara firmly rejects this: *shani Tisha B'Av ho'il ve-huchplu bo tzaros* — Tisha B'Av is different, because multiple catastrophes accumulated upon it. *De-amar Mar: be-Tisha B'Av charav ha-bayis be-rishona u-ve-shniya, ve-nilkeda Beitar, ve-nechrasha ha-ir* — the Temple was destroyed both the first and second time, Beitar was destroyed, and the city was plowed over. Tosafot notes that while multiple tragedies also befell *shiva asar b'Tammuz*, the weight of Tisha B'Av is qualitatively different — not only does it carry the burden of the same tragedy having occurred twice, but the *churban* itself, the destruction of the Temple, is so fundamental a catastrophe that it places Tisha B'Av in a category of its own. Tisha B'Av is also distinguished in that it begins from the night before, whereas the other fasts do not — though the full elaboration of these distinctions awaits the study of *Maseches Taanis*.

## Fast Days Order

### Month Numbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים הָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דּוֹרֵשׁ, וַאֲנִי אֵין דּוֹרֵשׁ כְּמוֹתוֹ: ״צוֹם הָרְבִיעִי״ — זֶה תִּשְׁעָה בְּתַמּוּז שֶׁבּוֹ הוּבְקְעָה הָעִיר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּרְבִיעִי בְּתִשְׁעָה לַחֹדֶשׁ וַיֶּחֱזַק הָרָעָב בָּעִיר וְלֹא הָיָה לֶחֶם לְעַם הָאָרֶץ וַתִּבָּקַע הָעִיר״. וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״רְבִיעִי״ — רְבִיעִי לֶחֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages disagreed about the fasts alluded to in the words of the prophet, as **it is taught** in a *baraita*. **Rabbi Shimon said: Rabbi Akiva would expound four verses, but I would not expound** the texts **as he did.** One of the disputes relates to the fasts mentioned by Zechariah. Rabbi Akiva would expound the verse as follows: **"The fast of the fourth," this is the ninth of Tammuz, on which the city** of Jerusalem **was breached, as it is stated: "And in the fourth month, on the ninth day of the month, the famine was severe in the city, so that there was no bread for the people of the land. Then the city was breached"** (Jeremiah 52:6–7). **And why does** the prophet **call it** the fast of the **fourth?** Because it is in Tammuz, **the fourth of the months** when counting from Nisan.

A *baraita* is now cited that unpacks the Zechariah verse by identifying each of the four fasts. Rabbi Shimon prefaces the discussion by noting that there were four matters on which Rabbi Akiva offered interpretations with which he himself disagrees.

### Breach Tammuz

Rabbi Shimon now articulates the four disputes. The first concerns which fast is referred to by the term "fast of the fourth month." 

**"Tzom ha-revi'i"** — the fast of the fourth month — is **shiva asar b'Tammuz**, the 17th of Tammuz, the month counted as the fourth from Nisan. **Shebo hufke'ah ha-ir** — that is when the city of Jerusalem was breached. The proof text: **b'tisha lachodesh vayechazak ha-ra'av ba-ir ve-lo hayah lechem l'am ha'aretz, va-tibaka ha-ir** — on the ninth of the month the famine was severe and the city was breached. The verse says the ninth, yet we observe the fast on the 17th. Tosafot explains: in the first Temple the city was indeed breached on the ninth of Tammuz, but in the second Temple it was breached on the 17th, and since it is the second Temple's chronology that governs our practice, the fast is observed on the 17th. However, Tosafot also cites a remarkable passage from the Yerushalmi: the *baraita* there reads the date as the 17th even for the first Temple, and explains that while the verse says the ninth, the correct date was actually the 17th — but such was the turmoil and distress of that moment that the people got the date wrong. *Mitoch tirdasam ta'u b'cheshbonam* — in the midst of their anguish they erred in their reckoning. And the Torah records the date as they believed it to be, even though the actual date was the 17th. This is a striking claim: that the *pasuk* records a mistaken date out of a kind of deference to the perception of those who experienced the catastrophe.

### Burn Av

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צוֹם הַחֲמִישִׁי״ — זֶה תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב, שֶׁבּוֹ נִשְׂרַף בֵּית אֱלֹהֵינוּ. וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״חֲמִישִׁי״ — חֲמִישִׁי לֶחֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **"The fast of the fifth," this is the Ninth of Av, on which the Temple of our Lord was burnt. And why does he call it** the fast of the **fifth?** Because it falls in the **fifth of the months.**

**"Tzom ha-chamishi"** — the fast of the fifth month — is Tisha B'Av: **shebo nisraf beis elokeinu** — the day on which the House of our God was burned.

### Gedaliah 3 Tishrei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי — זֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּתִשְׁרִי, שֶׁבּוֹ נֶהֱרַג גְּדַלְיָה בֶּן אֲחִיקָם. וּמִי הֲרָגוֹ — יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן נְתַנְיָה הֲרָגוֹ, לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁשְּׁקוּלָה מִיתָתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים כִּשְׂרֵיפַת בֵּית אֱלֹהֵינוּ. וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״שְׁבִיעִי״ — שְׁבִיעִי לֶחֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **"The fast of the seventh," this is the third of Tishrei, on which Gedaliah, son of Ahikam, was killed. And who killed him? Ishmael, son of Nethaniah, killed him** (see II Kings 25:25; Jeremiah, chapter 41). The Sages established a fast to commemorate Gedaliah's death **to teach you that the death of the righteous is equivalent to the burning of the Temple of our Lord. And why did** the prophet **call it** the fast of the **seventh?** Because Tishrei is the **seventh of the months.**

**"Tzom ha-shvi'i"** — the fast of the seventh month — is the third of Tishrei: **shebo neherag Gedalyah ben Achikam** — the day on which Gedalyah ben Achikam was murdered by Yishmael ben Nesanyah. The *baraita* draws a pointed lesson: **l'lamedcha she-shkulah misasan shel tzaddikim k'sreifat beis elokeinu** — to teach you that the death of the righteous is as weighty in God's eyes as the burning of the Temple. The assassination of Gedalyah appears in the same list as the destruction of the Temple, and is given equivalent halakhic status. One might note the painful irony: Gedalyah accepted lashon hara about the plot against his life, and was murdered as a result — yet it is he who is called righteous and his death that receives this weight.

### Siege Teves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צוֹם הָעֲשִׂירִי״ — זֶה עֲשָׂרָה בְּטֵבֵת, שֶׁבּוֹ סָמַךְ מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי דְבַר ה׳ אֵלַי בַּשָּׁנָה הַתְּשִׁיעִית בַּחֹדֶשׁ הָעֲשִׂירִי בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ לֵאמֹר. בֶּן אָדָם כְּתׇב לְךָ אֶת שֵׁם הַיּוֹם אֶת עֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה סָמַךְ מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל אֶל יְרוּשָׁלִַים״. וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — עֲשִׂירִי לֶחֳדָשִׁים. וַהֲלֹא הָיָה רָאוּי זֶה לִכְתּוֹב רִאשׁוֹן? וְלָמָּה נִכְתַּב כָּאן — כְּדֵי לְהַסְדִּיר חֳדָשִׁים כְּתִיקְנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **"The fast of the tenth," This is the tenth of Tevet, on which the king of Babylonia laid siege to Jerusalem, as it is stated: "And in the ninth year, in the tenth month, on the tenth day of the month, the word of the Lord came to me, saying: Son of man, write the name of the day, of this same day: The king of Babylonia has laid siege to Jerusalem on this very day"** (Ezekiel 24:1–2). **And why did he call it** the fast of the **tenth?** Because it is in Tevet, which is **the tenth of the months. Wouldn't it have been fitting to write** this fast **first,** as the series of events began with the laying of the siege. **Why was** it **written here** at the end of the list? This was done **in order to list the months in** their **proper** order, as the prophet began with the fourth month and ended with the tenth month. This is the statement of Rabbi Akiva.

**"Tzom ha-asiri"** — the fast of the tenth month — according to Rabbi Akiva is **asarah b'Tevet**, the tenth of Tevet, the month of Tevet being the tenth counting from Nisan. **Shebo samach melech Bavel al Yerushalayim** — that is when the king of Babylon laid siege to Jerusalem, as the verse states: **ben adam ksov lecha et shem hayom hazeh, et esem hayom hazeh, samach melech Bavel al Yerushalayim** — son of man, inscribe for yourself the very name of this day, this very day, on which the king of Babylon drew close to Jerusalem. The question is then asked: why is **asarah b'Tevet** listed last in the verse, as the fourth in the sequence, when chronologically the siege occurred first — before the breaching, before the destruction, before the assassination of Gedalyah? Rabbi Akiva's answer: **k'dei l'hasdir chadashim k'tiknan** — the verse lists the fasts in calendar order of their months, not in chronological order of the events. The sequence goes fourth month, fifth month, seventh month, tenth month — reflecting the month numbers, not the sequence of historical events.

### Report 5 Teves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנִי אֵינִי אוֹמֵר כֵּן, אֶלָּא: ״צוֹם הָעֲשִׂירִי״ — זֶה חֲמִישָּׁה בְּטֵבֵת, שֶׁבּוֹ בָּאת שְׁמוּעָה לַגּוֹלָה שֶׁהוּכְּתָה הָעִיר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בִּשְׁתֵּי עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה בָּעֲשִׂירִי בַּחֲמִשָּׁה לַחֹדֶשׁ לְגָלוּתֵנוּ בָּא אֵלַי הַפָּלִיט מִירוּשָׁלִַם לֵאמֹר הוּכְּתָה הָעִיר״, וְעָשׂוּ יוֹם שְׁמוּעָה כְּיוֹם שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon disagreed and said: **I do not say this, but rather** I expound the verse as follows: **"The fast of the tenth," this is the fifth of Tevet, on which the report reached the Diaspora that the city had been smitten, as it is stated: "And it came to pass in the twelfth year of our exile, in the tenth month, on the fifth day of the month, that one that had escaped out of Jerusalem came to me, saying: The city is smitten"** (Ezekiel 33:21); **and they made the day of the report** of the destruction **like the day of the** actual **burning** and decreed a fast on that day.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with Rabbi Akiva's reading of **tzom ha-asiri**. **Ella tzom ha-asiri zeh chamishah b'Tevet** — the fast of the tenth is not **asarah b'Tevet** at all, but rather the fifth of Tevet. What happened on the fifth of Tevet? **Shebo ba'ah shemuah la-golah she-hukta ha-ir** — that is the day on which the report reached the exile that the city had been destroyed. The proof: **vayehi b'shteim esre shanah b'asiri b'chamishah lachodesh legalutenu, ba elai ha-palit mi-Yerushalayim leimor, hukta ha-ir** — in the twelfth year of our exile, in the tenth month on the fifth of the month, the refugee from Jerusalem came to say that the city had been struck down. The fifth of Tevet was when the exiles — those who had been sent away to Bavel a decade or more before the *churban* — first heard the news of the Temple's destruction. **Va-asu yom shemuah k'yom sereifah** — and the day of hearing the report was treated as equivalent to the day of the burning itself, just as in matters of *aveilut* the moment of receiving tidings of a death carries its own weight.

### Shimon Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִרְאִין דְּבָרַי מִדְּבָרָיו, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר עַל רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן וְעַל אַחֲרוֹן אַחֲרוֹן, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר עַל רִאשׁוֹן אַחֲרוֹן וְעַל אַחֲרוֹן רִאשׁוֹן. אֶלָּא שֶׁהוּא מוֹנֶה לְסֵדֶר חֳדָשִׁים, וַאֲנִי מוֹנֶה לְסֵדֶר פּוּרְעָנִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Shimon added: **And my statement seems** more convincing **than his statement, as I say about the first** fast mentioned by the prophet that it marks the event that took place **first, and about the last** fast that it marks the event that took place **last.** According to Rabbi Shimon, the fasts are listed in accordance with the chronological order of the events. **But he,** Rabbi Akiva, **says about the first** fast mentioned by the prophet that it marks the event that took place **last, and about the last** fast mentioned that it marks the event that took place **first, only that he lists** the fasts **in the order of the months, whereas I list** them also **in the order of the calamities** that they mark.

Rabbi Shimon's logic is elegant: **she-ani omer rishon rishon ve-achron achron** — I maintain that the events are listed in perfect chronological order. The siege came first (**asarah b'Tevet** by Rabbi Akiva's reading), then the breaching of the city (**shiva asar b'Tammuz**), then the destruction of the Temple (**Tisha B'Av**), then the assassination of Gedalyah (**third of Tishrei**), and finally — the report reaching the exile (**fifth of Tevet**). By identifying **tzom ha-asiri** as the fifth of Tevet rather than the tenth, Rabbi Shimon achieves both goals simultaneously: the months remain in ascending numerical order, *and* the historical events are listed in the precise sequence in which they unfolded. **Ve-hu omer al rishon achron ve-al achron rishon** — whereas Rabbi Akiva, in placing the siege last in the list, has the first event appearing at the end and uses the ordering of months alone as his rationale. **Va-ani moneh l'seder pur'anut** — Rabbi Shimon concludes: I list them in the order of the catastrophes themselves. The daf concludes here, and we will resume tomorrow with a full understanding of the fast days that underlie this discussion.
