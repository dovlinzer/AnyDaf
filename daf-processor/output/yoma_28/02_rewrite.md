# Yoma 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wood Arrangement Debate

### Prior Analysis Review

The present daf begins at the bottom of 27b. By way of review: the preceding discussion had occupied itself with interpreting the *pesukim* at the opening of *Vayikra* to demonstrate that *hefshet* and *nituach* — the flaying and dismembering of the sacrificial animal — do not, according to *pshat* of the *pesukim*, require a *kohen*. The *gemara* had identified which specific *pasuk* was superfluous and thus available to convey this teaching.

### Rav Assi Statement

The focus then shifted to a statement of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: if a non-*kohen* (*zar*) arranged the woodpile on the altar, he is *chayav misah*. This is a striking claim on two levels. First, it affirms that a *kohen* is required for this *avodah* — which is at least the *pshat* of the *pasuk* *v'archu bnei Aharon ha-kohanim es ha-netachim*, or more precisely *v'archu eitzim al ha-esh*. Second, and more radically, it treats this *avodah* as among the truly final *avodot* for which a *zar* incurs the death penalty.

### Final vs Interim Avodot

The background to appreciating how bold this claim is lies in a distinction the *gemara* had already been developing: not every *avodah* that a *zar* is forbidden to perform carries the penalty of *misah*. Only an *avodah tamah* — a final, completed act of service — generates that liability. Interim *avodot*, preparatory stages that lead toward a subsequent act, do not. This had been the context for extended discussion of *terumas ha-deshen* — whether it is a final act or an intermediate one, and whether it is characterized as a nighttime or a daytime *avodah*.

### Rav Zeira Challenge

Rav Zeira challenged Rabbi Yochanan's ruling. His objection was that arranging the woodpile is performed at night, and a *zar* should not be invalid — let alone *chayav misah* — for a nighttime *avodah*. The force of this challenge is considerable: not only is the *zar* said to be exempt from *misah*, but Rav Zeira implies the act may be entirely *kesheirah* even if performed by a *zar*, notwithstanding the *pasuk*'s explicit reference to *Bnei Aharon*.

### New Wood Placement

The *gemara* essentially accepted this challenge and responded that the statement of Rabbi Yochanan must be reframed. What incurs *chayav misah* is not the initial arranging of the woodpile but the placement of the two new logs upon the pyre — an act that the *gemara* claimed occurred after daybreak. For the nighttime *avodah* itself, Rav Zeira is correct: there is no *misah*, and perhaps a *zar* may even perform it *lechatchilah*. The *terumas ha-deshen*, though technically performed at night, was recharacterized as a daytime *avodah* — a significant interpretive move that the *gemara* acknowledged as standing in some tension with the plain sense of the *pesukim*.

## Interim vs Final Avodah

### Alternative Challenge

We now arrive at the bottom of 27b where the *gemara* introduces an *ika d'amri* — an alternative version of the *sugya* presenting a differently framed challenge to Rabbi Yochanan's ruling.

### Interim Avodah Question

In this version, Rav Zeira's challenge is not premised on the nighttime character of the *avodah* but on a different objection altogether: *vechi yesh lach avodah she-yesh achareiha avodah pesulah b'zar?* — do you have such a thing as an interim *avodah* that is invalid with a *zar*? The principle the *gemara* draws upon here is that *avodah tamah* — and only *avodah tamah* — renders a *zar chayav misah* (*v'avadtem, avodah tamah*). Arranging the woodpile is an intermediate stage: the wood is being laid in preparation for placing the logs, and the logs themselves are placed in preparation for burning the *evarim*. Since it is an interim *avodah* and not a final one, the *zar* should not be *chayav misah*. Indeed, Rav Zeira pushes further: if it is genuinely interim, perhaps even *pesulah* does not apply at all.

### Rashi Copyover Theory

The *gemara* then proceeds to challenge this framing with a series of questions: *v'harei eivarim u-fedarim* — what about the limbs and the fats? And *v'harei terumas ha-deshen* — what about the ash removal? Rashi, however, makes a sweeping editorial intervention here. He excises this entire passage from his *girsa*, writing explicitly: *lo garsinan le-chulah hach girsa de-eivarim ve-di-trumas ha-deshen, hashta de-amrinan l'gabei* — we do not have this reading, because all of these questions and answers are irrelevant to the present inquiry. The *sugya* at this point is concerned with the question of interim versus final *avodah*. The questions about *eivarim* and *terumas ha-deshen* were relevant in the earlier *sugya* because they addressed the issue of nighttime versus daytime *avodah*. Transposed here, they answer questions no one is asking. Rashi's assessment is that this material was inadvertently copied over from the previous *sugya* — *agav shitpa de-riheta de-girsa* — carried along by the momentum of the recitation. The scribe, or the oral transmitter, had just been through these questions and answers, and without pausing to verify their relevance, allowed them to migrate into the new context.

Tosafot, by contrast, devotes a substantial discussion to vindicating the received text, performing considerable analytical labor to explain how these questions and answers do relate to the new *sugya*'s concerns. For the purposes of this shiur, we follow Rashi's position.

### Night vs Day Redux

With Rashi's caveat in mind, let us read through the *sugya* as it appears in the text, while noting why each question fails to connect to the real issue at hand.

The *gemara* asks *v'harei eivarim u-fedarim* — but what about the limbs and the fats? This question made sense in the earlier *sugya*, where the issue was whether a nighttime *avodah* could render a *zar chayav misah*. There, burning the limbs — which occurs at night — presented a genuine puzzle: how can it be invalid for a *zar* if it is a nighttime *avodah*? And the answer — *sof avodas yemama hi*, it is the conclusion of the daytime *avodah* — was a meaningful response. But in the current *sugya*, which concerns interim versus final *avodah*, this exchange contributes nothing. The *evarim* are unambiguously an *avodah tamah* — they are the terminal act of the sacrificial process — so there is no reason to ask about them in the context of interim *avodot*.

Similarly, the subsequent question about *terumas ha-deshen* is entirely at home in the prior *sugya*: there it was a genuine challenge, since *terumas ha-deshen* is performed at night yet apparently involves *kohen*-specific rules. The response — *techilas avodas yemama hi*, it is the beginning of the daytime *avodah* — addressed that challenge directly. But *terumas ha-deshen* is also not an interim *avodah*; it is itself a complete act, the culmination of the nightly fire process. So the question and answer have nothing to contribute here either. Rabbi Yochanan's statement that one who sanctified his hands for *terumas ha-deshen* need not sanctify again the next morning — *she-kvar kideish mi-techilas avodah* — reinforces that this is the initiating act of the day's service, which is again a response calibrated to the nighttime question, not the interim-versus-final question.

The *gemara* then arrives at *ela kashya* — there is still a difficulty — which in context must refer to the original challenge: how can arranging the woodpile, an interim act, render a *zar chayav misah*?

### Two Logs Final Work

At this point, Rav Ashi reframes the tradition of Rabbi Yochanan: *amar Rav Ashi amar Rabbi Yochanan, zar she-sidar shenei gezeirei eitzim chayav* — a *zar* who places the two logs of wood on the arranged woodpile is *chayav*. The act in question is not the organizing of the woodpile but the laying of the two logs upon it. And the reason is: *ho'il ve-avodah tamah hi* — because this constitutes a final act. The two logs are the completion of preparing the fire; the prior arrangement of the pyre was the intermediate stage, but placing the logs is the conclusion of the wood-fire *avodah*.

One might ask whether this is truly an *avodah tamah* or whether it too is merely preparatory, since the burning of the *evarim* and *fedarim* will follow. Tosafot raises this point, noting that by similar logic one could describe the sprinkling of blood as preparatory to burning the limbs. At minimum, one can argue that placing the two logs is the completion of the *eitzim* process specifically — the woodpile *avodah* as its own discrete unit of service has been completed. Rashi himself had not listed this among the classic *avodot tamot* that render a *zar chayav*, and that very ambiguity is what fuels the debate. Rav Ashi's resolution insists that placing the two logs is, at the level of the woodpile *avodah*, a terminal act — *hashlamas ha-avodah* — and therefore *avodah tamah*.

## Lottery & Final Avodot

### Rava's Lottery Q

Rava now presses the logic of this ruling: *ela me-atah tiba'i payis* — if placing the two logs is indeed an *avodah tamah* rendering a *zar chayav misah*, should it not require its own lottery? The underlying principle Rava is working with is that any significant, terminal *avodah* of the *tamid* service — one for which a *zar* incurs death — ought to be subject to a formal *payis*, a drawing of lots to determine who performs it.

### Braita Lottery Link

The *gemara* responds by citing a *beraita*: *mi she-zachah bi-terumas ha-deshen yizkeh bi-siddur shenei gezeirei eitzim* — the *kohen* who wins the lottery for *terumas ha-deshen* also wins the right to place the two logs. So in practice, the placement of the two logs is subsumed within the *terumas ha-deshen* lottery rather than having its own separate draw. In the parallel discussion from the previous *sugya*, Rav had apparently been unaware of this *beraita*; here the *gemara* simply cites it as a challenge to Rava's expectation.

### Final Avodot Lottery

The *gemara* then defends Rava by clarifying what he actually meant: *yipaisu ba bifnei atzmah* — Rava's question was whether the placement of the two logs should receive its own independent lottery. His reasoning was precisely that since it is an *avodah tamah* and a *zar* is *chayav misah* for it, it deserves a dedicated *payis*. The *gemara* proceeds to articulate Rava's underlying principle: *avodah tamah v'zar chayav aleha misah — payis payis* — anything that is an *avodah tamah* for which a *zar* incurs *misah* warrants its own lottery; whereas *ein zar chayav aleha misah — lo ba'ei payis*, if the *zar* is not subject to *misah*, no dedicated *payis* is needed.

### Lottery Context Q's

This principle is then immediately tested. *V'harei shechitah* — but *shechitah* gets its own lottery, and yet a *zar* is entirely valid to perform *shechitah*. How can *shechitah* be the paradigmatic *payis avodah* if the *zar*-invalidity criterion is the operative one?

The *gemara* responds: *shani shechitah de-chilas avodah d'yama hi* — *shechitah* is exceptional because it is the opening act of the entire daytime *tamid* service, and the *tamid* as a whole involves so many *avodot* for which a *zar* is *chayav misah* that the entire complex receives a unified lottery structure. *Shechitah*, as the initiating act of this complex, is swept into that lottery naturally.

This leaves Rava's question partially unresolved and the principle somewhat difficult to apply cleanly. Tosafot notes the broader oddity: the original lottery for the *tamid* covered thirteen distinct *avodot* in a single *payis*, many of which independently would render a *zar chayav misah*. Why any of them should demand a separate lottery is not obvious. The *sugya* acknowledges the difficulty without fully resolving it.

## Transition to Service

### Final Lottery Rule

The *gemara* draws its conclusion: *avodah tamah ba'ei payis; avodah she-achar avodah lo ba'ei payis* — a final *avodah* requires a lottery; an interim *avodah* that precedes another *avodah* does not. This formulation presents itself as the clear upshot of Rava's question and the *gemara*'s ensuing discussion.

It is worth pausing to appreciate the broader debate these two parallel *sugyot* have been conducting. Yesterday's *sugya* — and the first version of the discussion — insisted that for a *zar* to be *chayav misah*, the *avodah* must be performed by day. The principle driving that analysis was night versus day: a nighttime *avodah* is not subject to *zar*-invalidity, let alone *chayav misah*. That required considerable interpretive strain — claiming that placing the logs happened by day, recharacterizing *terumas ha-deshen* as a daytime *avodah* — all to salvage Rabbi Yochanan's ruling.

The present *sugya*, in its alternative framing, sets aside the night/day question entirely. Here a nighttime *avodah* can render a *zar chayav misah*; the only question is whether it is *avodah tamah*. This *sugya* is actually more permissive in one sense — it does not require the *avodah* to be daytime — but more demanding in another, insisting on the terminal character of the act. The two *sugyot* are in genuine tension, and the accidental textual conflation Rashi identifies has obscured that tension in the received text.

Importantly, Rashi suggests that even the final line of the *sugya* — *amar Mar Zutra ve-isema Rav Ashi, af anan nami tenina* — can be read in service of the present *sugya*'s concerns, albeit in a different way than it served the prior one. The Mishnah's silence regarding any announcement for the time of wood-placement (*v'ilu zman sidur gizrei eitzim lo tani*) suggests that the wood was done at night — which, on the current *sugya*'s logic, means the wood fire is the nighttime component of the *tamid* service, with burning the *evarim* as the daytime component. The placement of the logs thus concludes a distinct nighttime *avodah*, making it an *avodah tamah* in its own right. The *gemara* pushes back that perhaps the wood was really placed by day, just without the formal announcement — but in that case the two *sugyot* reach opposite conclusions about when the wood was placed, which is itself part of what the alternative approaches are debating.

### Daybreak Check

With *hadran lach ba-rishonah*, we enter a new *perek* — and the contrast in difficulty level is immediately welcome.

*Amar lahem ha-memuneh* — the appointed overseer addressed those assembled: *tze'u ur'u im higia zman ha-shechitah* — go out and see whether the time for *shechitah* has arrived. If it had, the lookout — *ha-roeh* — would call out *barkai*, signifying the burst of morning light. According to one formulation in the Mishnah, *Matya ben Shmuel* adds that the *memuneh* would ask: *he'ir pnei kol ha-mizrach ad she-b'Chevron?* — has the eastern sky become light all the way to Chevron? And the lookout would respond in the affirmative.

The question naturally arises: why was it necessary to station someone on a height to make this determination? Could those below not discern for themselves whether it was day? The Mishnah answers this directly.

### Moonlight Story

*She-pa'am achas ala ma'or ha-levana* — there was one occasion when moonlight rose and appeared to the assembled *kohanim* to be the light of dawn from the east. Acting on this mistaken impression, they slaughtered the *tamid* — and were then forced to take it out to *beis ha-sreifah* and destroy it, since it had been *shechted* before daybreak and was therefore *pasul*. This is precisely the scenario the *gemara* earlier characterized as *leis lei takanta* — there is no remedy once the error has been made, since the animal is gone. The lesson was institutionalized: a lookout would be posted at an elevation, capable of distinguishing between the gradual, directional light of dawn and the more diffuse illumination of moonlight.

### KG Mikvah

The Mishnah then turns, somewhat abruptly, to the *Kohen Gadol*: *horidu Kohen Gadol l'beis ha-tvilah* — they brought the *Kohen Gadol* down to the immersion chamber. The transition is striking because the preceding discussion of daybreak determination is a year-round practice, whereas this clearly pertains to *Yom Kippur* specifically. One might read the opening of the *perek* as describing the morning of *Yom Kippur* itself — the daily ritual of verifying daybreak is being introduced as part of the *Yom Kippur* morning sequence, leading directly into the *Kohen Gadol*'s preparation.

It is worth noting in passing that there is a debate among the *rishonim* regarding whether *terumas ha-deshen* on *Yom Kippur* was performed by the *Kohen Gadol* or by an ordinary *kohen*. The answer bears on the relevance of the lottery discussions to *Yom Kippur* specifically: according to some *rishonim* and *acharonim*, the lottery was indeed held on *Yom Kippur* for *terumas ha-deshen*, lending continued relevance to the prior *perek* even in the *Yom Kippur* context.

### Purity Rules

The Mishnah introduces a general principle: *zeh ha-klal haya ba-Mikdash* — this was the standing rule in the *Mikdash*. Anyone who relieved himself (*mesich es raglav*, which Rashi explains as a reference to crouching, the posture of a bowel movement) was *ta'un tvilah* — required to immerse in a *mikvah*. Anyone who urinated was required to perform *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* — the washing of hands and feet from the *kiyor*. The text is not necessarily asserting that the *Kohen Gadol* had a bowel movement; rather, having introduced the *Kohen Gadol*'s immersion, the Mishnah takes the opportunity to articulate the broader framework of purity obligations that governed all *kohanim* in the *Mikdash*, within which the *Kohen Gadol*'s immersion finds its natural context.

## Daybreak Opinions

### Braita Formulas

The *gemara* now cites a *beraita* that preserves multiple opinions regarding the precise formulation of the daybreak announcement. Rabbi Yishmael holds that the lookout would declare *barak barkai* — a doubled expression evoking a flash or burst of light, affirming that dawn has broken. Rabbi Akiva's version is *alah barkai* — the light has risen. Rashi notes that *alah barkai* implies a slightly more advanced stage of daylight than *barak barkai*; the morning flash has not merely appeared but ascended.

### Geographic Tests

Nachuma ben Afakshiyon offers a third formulation: *aparkai b'Chevron* — one would announce that the light had reached Chevron. *Matya ben Shmuel* — identified here as *ha-memuneh al ha-payisot*, the overseer of the lotteries — offers the fullest formulation: *he'ir pnei kol ha-mizrach ad she-b'Chevron?* Has the entire eastern sky lit up all the way to Chevron? The lookout would respond affirmatively. This formulation requires not merely the first appearance of dawn but a broader and more complete illumination, extending southward as far as Chevron. The mention of Chevron, which lies to the south of Jerusalem, presumably reflects how far the widening cone of morning light must reach to qualify as genuine dawn rather than a deceptive pre-dawn glow.

### Workers/Foremen

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira offers the most expansive criterion: *he'ir pnei kol ha-mizrach ad b'Chevron, ve-yatzu kol ha-am ish ish li-melachto* — the entire eastern sky lit up to Chevron and all the people have gone out, each to his work. The *gemara* objects: *iy hachi, naga lei tuvei* — if so, by that point it would be very light indeed. The whole purpose of the daybreak determination is to begin the service as early as possible; waiting until laborers have set out for work would mean a considerable delay. The *gemara* therefore clarifies: *lishkor po'alim ka-amrinan* — we do not mean ordinary workers but rather the labor contractors, the foremen who go out before dawn to hire workers for the day's tasks. These are people who must be on the road exceptionally early, and their departure marks a recognizable early-morning moment that can serve as a daybreak indicator without implying that the sun has fully risen.

## Prayer Times Shift

### Avraham Prayer

At this point the *gemara* introduces a striking thematic shift. We have been discussing the determination of daybreak for the *tamid* service, and now Rav Safra pivots to the question of *tefillah*: *tzluta de-Avraham* — the prayer of Avraham. The reader might expect this to introduce a discussion of *Shacharit*, since Avraham is credited with instituting morning prayer. In fact, however, Rav Safra connects the prayer of Avraham to *Mincha*, specifically to the moment *mi-de-mashchari koslei* — from when the walls begin to grow dark.

### Mincha Timing

The logic is spatial and solar: when the sun stands high and then begins its westward descent, the walls of a structure facing east begin to lose the direct sunlight that had illuminated them through the morning hours. The moment the walls start to darken — *mishcherei koslei* — marks the onset of what can be called "afternoon," and this is when the prayer of Avraham was observed. Interestingly, it is Yitzchak who is classically said to have instituted *Mincha* (*vayeitzei Yitzchak lasuach ba-sadeh lifnot erev*). The resolution, as the *Maharsha* explains, is that Yitzchak inherited and institutionalized a *mesorah* that originated with Avraham; Avraham was the *ikar* of *tefillah* in general, and his influence underlay all three daily prayers.

### Prayer Etymology

The *Maharsha*'s comment also points toward an interesting etymology. The word *shachar* — dawn, and thus the root of *Shacharit* — is cognate with *shachor*, black, darkness. Dawn is not named for brightness but for the receding darkness; it is the moment when black turns to the first hint of light. Similarly, *boker* — morning — derives from *levaker*, to distinguish, reflecting the capacity at daybreak to differentiate between objects that had been indistinguishable in the dark. The *Aruch*, cited in the marginal notes, similarly understands *tzluta de-Avraham* as encompassing the entire *tefillah* tradition of the *Avot*, noting that *Avraham* is invoked specifically because he is the foundational figure of prayer. The text of the *Aruch* reads: *Yitzchak went out to meditate in the field toward evening, from the time the sun entered the west and the walls of the east began to grow dark — and this meditation is the midday prayer as transmitted to him from his father.*

## Avraham Halacha Q

### Zealousness Q

Rav Yosef now interjects: *anami me-Avraham negmor u-nigmor* — are we really going to derive *halacha* from Avraham? Rashi interprets this as follows: Avraham was always characterized by extraordinary *zerizus* — alacrity in performing *mitzvot* — and one cannot establish normative *halacha* from the behavior of an individual who was exceptional in his diligence. Just as we do not learn the ordinary obligation from the conduct of a *zrizut*-driven individual, we cannot establish the standard time for *Mincha* from Avraham's personal practice.

### Aruch View

But the marginal citation of the *Aruch* advances a considerably deeper challenge. The *Aruch* reads Rav Yosef's question as striking at a fundamental principle: *kiyem ha-Torah ad she-lo nitnah* — Avraham fulfilled the Torah before it was given. But precisely because his observance was self-initiated rather than divinely legislated, it cannot serve as a source for *halacha*. *Halacha*, on the *Aruch*'s reading, derives from legislated norm — from *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* and from the practices transmitted through the *nevi'im* within the framework of Torah law. Practices adopted independently before *Matan Torah*, however admirable, carry no normative weight.

This is a profound and consequential position. The *Rambam*, in a well-known passage, seems to take a contrasting view. He describes the progressive accumulation of divine commandments across the generations: Noach received the seven *mitzvot*, Avraham received *bris milah* and instituted *Shacharit*, Yitzchak instituted *Mincha*, Yaakov instituted *Maariv* and *ma'aser*, until the *Torah* was given at Sinai with its full complement of 613 *mitzvot*. The *Rambam*'s framing suggests a continuity: what the *Avot* practiced became, through the eventual revelation of Torah law, part of the normative *halacha* tradition. The question of *bris milah* illustrates the tension well. The foundational *halacha* of circumcision: is its source the commandment to Avraham, or the later *pasuk* in *Vayikra*, *u-vayom ha-shmini yimol b'sar orlato*? On the *Aruch*'s logic, the former cannot be the source of law; only the latter provides the normative foundation.

### Rava Counter

Rava challenges Rav Yosef directly: *tana gamri me-Avraham ve-lo gamrina minei?* — a *tanna* derives *halacha* from Avraham, and you say we cannot? Where does a *tanna* learn from Avraham? The *beraita* teaches: *u-vayom ha-shmini yimol b'sar orlato* — this teaches that any time during the eighth day is valid for *milah*. But we know further that *zerizim makdimim le-mitzvot* — the zealous perform *mitzvot* at the earliest opportunity. And the source for this principle is Avraham: *vayashkem Avraham ba-boker vayachavosh es chamoro* — Avraham rose early in the morning. So even regarding *milah*, the normative practice of performing it as early as possible in the day is derived from Avraham's conduct. Thus, concludes Rava, *tanna*im do learn *halacha* from Avraham, and the *Aruch*-style objection to learning the time of *Mincha* from him cannot stand as a categorical prohibition.

## Mincha Time Debate

### Pesach Shabbat Times

The *gemara* now confronts directly what troubled Rav Yosef. The specific claim was that *Mincha* begins from *mishcherei koslei* — when the walls darken, which would be approximately noon, 12:01 in contemporary terms. The *gemara* adduces a *mishnah* that seems to contradict this: when *erev Pesach* falls on *erev Shabbat*, the *tamid* was *shechted* as early as six-and-a-half hours — *shisha u-mechetza*, that is, 12:30 — and brought to the altar at seven-and-a-half hours, 1:30. This was the earliest possible schedule, pressed earlier than usual due to the need to complete the *korban Pesach* before *Shabbat*. And yet even at maximum urgency, *shechitah* did not begin before 12:30.

### Noon vs 12:30

This raises the question sharply: if *mishcherei koslei* truly marks the valid start of afternoon *avodah* at 12:01, why was the earliest possible *shechitah* of the *tamid* at 12:30? Why not start at 12:01? This is what Rav Yosef found difficult in the claim that Avraham's *Mincha* prayer was at the moment the walls first began to darken. If the halakhic afternoon begins at noon, why does the *korbanot* model consistently point to 12:30?

The *gemara* offers two answers.

### Wall Angle Answer

The first answer: *vedilma kotlei de-Veit ha-Mikdash be-sheish u-mechetza mashchari mishum de-lo mechayvenu tuvei* — perhaps the walls of the *Beit ha-Mikdash* did not actually darken until 12:30, because the walls were not perfectly perpendicular. Rashi explains that the walls were wider at the base and narrowed toward the top, so that even after the sun passed directly overhead, its rays continued to strike the angled wall face and keep it illuminated until 12:30. If this is so, then *mishcherei koslei* really does mean 12:01 in principle; it was simply that in the *Mikdash* specifically, the architectural peculiarity of the walls meant that they did not visibly darken until 12:30. The *halachic* starting point remains noon; the *Mikdash*'s practice of 12:30 reflects its particular physical reality, not a different principle.

### Astrologer Answer

The second answer is of a different character altogether: *shani Avraham de-istagninus gedolah hayetah be-libo* — Avraham was a great astrologer (or, in the alternative formulation, a great sage), and he possessed the knowledge to determine with precision exactly when noon arrived. The ordinary person, lacking that expertise, must take a safety margin and wait until 12:30, when the *koslei* have clearly darkened even in the *Mikdash*. This answer does not claim that the *halachic* standard is 12:01 for everyone; rather, Avraham's practice at 12:01 was possible for him because of his exceptional ability to determine the precise moment, while normative *halacha* requires the more conservative 12:30.

### Avot in Yeshiva

The *gemara* now introduces the second of these two explanations with the formulation *mishum de-zaken ve-yosheiv be-yeshiva hu* — because he was an elder who sat in *yeshiva*. This pivot opens into a rich digression establishing the *Avot* as heads of academies. The relevant teaching comes from Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina: *mi-yimeihem shel avoteinu lo parsha yeshiva me-hem* — from the days of our forefathers, *yeshiva* study was never absent from among them. The chain of evidence runs as follows: in Egypt, *yeshiva* was with them — as it says *lech ve-asafta es ziknei Yisrael*, where *zkenim* denotes not merely the aged but sages. In the wilderness, *yeshiva* was with them — *esfa li shivim ish mi-ziknei Yisrael*. And Avraham Avinu was *zaken ve-yosheiv be-yeshiva* — as the *pasuk* says *ve-Avraham zaken ba ba-yamim*. The *Maharsha* notes the deliberate order of proof: the *gemara* establishes the meaning of *zaken* as "sage" from the wilderness and Egypt contexts, where old age alone is clearly not the criterion for selection, and then retroactively applies that meaning to Avraham.

The chain continues: Yitzchak Avinu — *vayehi ki zakein Yitzchak*; Yaakov Avinu — *ve-einei Yisrael kavedu mi-zoken*. Even Eliezer the servant of Avraham is described as *zaken ve-yosheiv be-yeshiva*, derived from *amar Avraham el avdo zekein beito ha-moshel be-chol asher lo* — the one who had dominion over everything that was his. Rabbi Elazar explains: *she-moshel be-toras rabbo* — he had mastery over the entire Torah of his master. The *Maharsha* observes that this characterization is necessary: since Eliezer could not rule on *halacha* in the presence of Avraham, his *Rebbe muvhak*, the only sense in which he could be described as teaching is that he transmitted Avraham's Torah faithfully to others. Hence the epithet *Dammasek Eliezer*, which Rabbi Elazar reads as an acronym for *doleh u-mashkeh* — he who draws water and gives to drink, drawing from the wellspring of his master's Torah and watering others with it. The *Maharsha* finds an additional hint in the inverted word order *Dammasek Eliezer* rather than *Eliezer Dammasek* — in the manner of *Yiftach ha-Gil'adi* — which signals that an interpretive reading of the name is licensed. There is also a thematic resonance: just as Rivkah was *doleh u-mashkeh* when she drew water for the camels at the well, Eliezer here draws the waters of Torah for others.

## Avot & All Torah

### Kiyam Statement

The discussion of the *Avot* as *talmidei chachamim* and *rashei yeshiva* culminates in Rav's celebrated declaration: *kiyam Avraham Avinu kol ha-Torah kullah* — Avraham our forefather fulfilled the entire Torah.

### Ekev Source

The textual basis is *eikev asher shama Avraham be-koli vayishmor mishmarti mitzvotai chukkotai ve-torotai* — because Avraham hearkened to My voice and kept My charge, My commandments, My statutes, and My laws. The accumulation — *mishmarti*, *mitzvotai*, *chukkotai*, *torotai* — is read as encompassing the totality of Torah obligation. When *Shimi bar Chiya* suggests that perhaps this merely refers to the seven *Noahide* commandments, the *gemara* objects: *ha-ika nami milah* — there is also *milah* to account for. Very well, perhaps it means the seven *mitzvot* and *milah*? But then *mitzvotai ve-torotai lama li* — why does the *pasuk* require all these additional terms? The elaborateness of the formulation can only be read as pointing toward the entire scope of *Torah*.

### Eruvi Tavshilin

Rava (or in the alternative, Rav Ashi) extends this to its logical limit: *kiyam Avraham Avinu afilu eruvei tavshilin* — Avraham fulfilled even *eiruv tavshilin*. The proof text is the plural *torotai* — which the *gemara* reads as *achas Torah she-bichtav ve-achas Torah she-ba'al peh*, one referring to the written Torah and one to the oral Torah. Since *eiruv tavshilin* is a purely rabbinic enactment — a *de-rabbanan* institution designed to preserve appropriate mindfulness of *Shabbat* when cooking on *Yom Tov* in anticipation of it — Avraham's adherence to *Torah she-ba'al peh* is extended even to this category. The *Maharsha* and others suggest that in context the point may be more symbolic than literal, perhaps indexing the entire conceptual category of *de-rabbanan* enactments rather than the specific institution of *eiruv tavshilin*.

### Challenges & Ramban

This *gemara* is, as the *Ramban* in his *Peirush al ha-Torah* notes, deeply challenging to read literally. Yaakov married two sisters — a union explicitly prohibited by Torah law. The *Avot* erected *matzeivot* — pillars — which Torah law subsequently forbids (*asher sanei Hashem Elokecha*). There are numerous places where the conduct of the *Avot* diverges from what the Torah later prescribes. The *Ramban* therefore raises the question of whether the *gemara*'s statement should be taken at face value, and offers various ways of understanding its scope.

This difficulty is also placed in striking dialectical relationship with the *Aruch*'s reading earlier in the *sugya*. There, Rav Yosef's challenge was understood to mean: you cannot derive *halacha* from Avraham because his pre-*Matan Torah* practice, however admirable, lacked the force of divine legislation. The implication was that Avraham's Torah observance, while real, was his own voluntary adoption rather than normative obligation. Now, at the close of the *daf*, Rav asserts the opposite pole: Avraham kept *kol ha-Torah kullah*, including Torah *she-ba'al peh*, including the most elaborate post-Sinaitic rabbinic enactments. The tension between these two positions — one diminishing the normative significance of pre-*Matan Torah* practice, the other maximizing it — forms a fitting and characteristically *talmudic* conclusion to the *daf*, to be resumed on *Shabbat*.
