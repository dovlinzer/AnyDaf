# Kiddushin 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Savlanot & Kiddushin

### Rav Huna Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְסִבְלוֹנוֹת. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְסִבְלוֹנוֹת. אָמַר רַבָּה: וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁלַח סִבְלוֹנוֹת לְאַחַר מִכָּאן – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָתָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא: שֶׁמֵּחֲמַת קִידּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שָׁלַח.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that *amora'im* discussed the following matter. **Rav Huna says: One** must be **concerned about gifts.** If a woman agreed to a betrothal and the prospective husband sent her gifts in the presence of witnesses, one must be concerned about the possibility that he sent them for the sake of betrothal. Therefore, the woman may not become betrothed to another man without first receiving a bill of divorce from this one. **And Rabba similarly says** that **one** must be **concerned about gifts. Rabba says: And we raised an objection against** this ***halakha* of ours,** as the mishna teaches: **Even if he sent gifts later, she is not betrothed. Abaye said to him: There,** in the mishna, the **reason** is **as it teaches: Because he sent** the gifts **on account of the first betrothal,** and since betrothal performed by a minor is of no account, she is not betrothed.

The sugya opens on 51b, approximately fifteen lines from the top, with a teaching introduced by the formula *itmar*: Rav Huna said, *choshishin lesavlanot* — we are concerned that gifts sent after an engagement (*savlanot*) may constitute or indicate an act of *kiddushin*. This teaching must be understood against the backdrop of the preceding *mishnayot*, which dealt extensively with conditional *kiddushin* and the principle of *devarim shebalev einam devarim* — that unexpressed mental reservations do not legally alter what has transpired. The immediately preceding *mishnah* had established that when an original act of *kiddushin* was defective — worth less than a *perutah*, for instance — we do not reinterpret subsequent gifts as a new and valid *kiddushin*. Now, Rav Huna addresses a different scenario.

Rashi's formulation is precise: the case requires that there first be some engagement context — *mi sheshidach be'isha venitratzes*, a man courted a woman and she agreed — and then, *vekadam veshilach savlanot beyadayim*, he went ahead and sent these gifts. In this situation, where no prior defective *kiddushin* preceded the gifts, Rav Huna says we are concerned — *choshishin* — that the *savlanot* themselves may constitute or indicate *kiddushin*.

### Rashi v. Tosafot

There is a fundamental interpretive dispute between Rashi and Tosafot regarding the nature of this concern. According to Rashi, the question is whether the *savlanot* themselves function as an act of *kiddushin* — that is, the man may have intended them to serve as the formal act. According to Tosafot, that is not the question at all; rather, the concern is evidentiary: since it was customary to send such gifts only after *kiddushin* had taken place, the presence of *savlanot* may serve as evidence that a prior, valid *kiddushin* occurred, even if no one witnessed it. These two readings produce meaningfully different legal frameworks, and various passages in the *gemara* appear to support one or the other position.

### Rava & Abaye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר רַבָּה: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא: שֶׁמֵּחֲמַת קִידּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שָׁלַח. הָכָא הוּא דְּטָעֵי. הָא בְּעָלְמָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this discussion. **Rabba says: From where do I say** this *halakha*, that one must be concerned about gifts? I have learned it from **the reason** she is not betrothed, **as** the mishna **teaches: Because he sent** the gifts **on account of the first betrothal.** This indicates that **it is here** that there is no concern for that he betrothed her, **since he erred** and thinks she is already betrothed to him by the first act of betrothal, and that there is no need to betroth her again. **But generally,** where this type of mistake is not possible, the sending of gifts **does** effect **betrothal.**

Rava concurs with Rav Huna: *vechen amar Rava, choshishin lesavlanot*. But Rava immediately anticipates the obvious challenge from the *mishnah*, which states, *af al pi sheshilach savlanot acher mikan eina mekudeshet* — even if he sent *savlanot* after the fact, she is not married. Rava himself raises this and says we must ask about it: *amar Rava umosivinan ashmatin*.

Abaye resolves the tension: *hatam kedetani ta'ama, shemachmat kiddushin rishonim shilach* — in the *mishnah*'s case, the reason the *savlanot* are not *kiddushin* is that they came in the wake of a prior defective *kiddushin* which framed their meaning. The sender was relying on the original (flawed) act; the *savlanot* are understood as consequent to that first attempt. In our case, by contrast, no defective *kiddushin* preceded the gifts, and so either the gifts may signal a valid *kiddushin* took place or may themselves constitute one.

There is a second formulation in the *gemara* — *ika de'amri*, some say — in which Rava himself argues from the *mishnah*'s reasoning to support his own position. The *mishnah* specifies the rationale: *shemachmat kiddushin rishonim shilach*, the gifts were sent on account of the prior *kiddushin*. This implies that the only reason the *savlanot* are harmless in the *mishnah*'s case is because of that framing. But *ha b'almam hava kiddushin* — in a normal case without that framing, they would indeed constitute *kiddushin*. This version reads more congenially with Rashi's position, since it suggests the *savlanot* themselves become an act of *kiddushin* rather than merely serving as evidence of one.

In any event, the operative word throughout is *choshishin* — we are *concerned*, not *certain*. This is a *chashash*, not a definitive ruling. The practical implication is that such a woman would require a *get* as a precaution, but one would not, for example, treat intimacy with another man as definite adultery subject to the severest penalties.

### Cultural Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר מְסַבְּלִי – חָיְשִׁינַן, מְסַבְּלִי וַהֲדַר מְקַדְּשִׁי – לָא חָיְשִׁינַן. מִקׇּדְשֵׁי וַהֲדַר מְסַבְּלִי – פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּרוּבָּא מְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר מְסַבְּלִי, וּמִיעוּטָא מְסַבְּלִי וַהֲדַר מְקַדְּשִׁי. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: נֵיחוּשׁ לְמִיעוּטָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about it;** must one be concerned that the gifts might have been given for the sake of betrothal or not? The Gemara answers: **Rav Pappa says: In a place where** the custom is that men first **betroth** women **and afterward send gifts we are concerned** that he might already have betrothed her or is sending the gifts for the sake of betrothal, since that is the only situation in which gifts are typically sent. But in a place where men first **send gifts and afterward betroth** women **we are not concerned** that the gifts are for betrothal. In a place where **they betroth** women **and afterward send gifts,** it **is obvious** that if a man sends a woman gifts he must already have betrothed her. The Gemara clarifies: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* due to a place **where most** men **betroth** women **and afterward send gifts, and a minority** of men **send gifts and afterward betroth** women. **Lest you say we should be concerned about the minority** and say she is not betrothed, Rav Pappa therefore **teaches us** that the behavior of the minority is not taken into account.

The *gemara* then records: *mai havah alah* — what was the conclusion? Rav Pappa answers that the concern depends on local custom. *B'atra demikadshi vehadar mesabli* — in a place where the practice is to perform *kiddushin* first and only then to send gifts, *chashinan*, we are concerned. If the *savlanot* were sent and there was no known *kiddushin*, the cultural context tells us that such gifts are sent only after *kiddushin*, so their presence creates concern that a *kiddushin* occurred. By contrast, *misavli vehadar mikadshi* — in a place where gifts are sent before the *kiddushin* as the local custom — obviously the gifts create no concern; they are simply the customary pre-betrothal gifts.

The *gemara* adds a nuanced case: *lo tzericha deruba mikadshi vehadar misavli u'miuta misavli vehadar mikadshi* — what if most people in the community first perform *kiddushin* and then send gifts, but a minority does it in reverse? One might think to be lenient by ascribing this particular case to the minority practice. The *gemara* rejects this: since ninety percent of the community first does *kiddushin* and then sends gifts, when these gifts appear without a documented *kiddushin*, we follow the majority and are concerned.

This principle is worth pausing over. The *chashash* applies specifically because the *savlanot* belong to a category of gift that is culturally understood as given after *kiddushin*. This is what Rav Pappa is contributing. The point is not merely that he sent a gift in a general engagement context; it is that he sent a particular kind of gift that in this community is exclusively or predominantly associated with the post-*kiddushin* stage. Only in that specific cultural configuration does the concern arise.

One might wonder whether this applies to the modern engagement ring. As it happens, the engagement ring is paradigmatically *misavli vehadar mikadshi* — it is given before the *kiddushin*, not after it — so it would not raise this concern. The relevant scenario would have to involve a gift that in a given community is the customary token sent after an act of *kiddushin* has already occurred.

## Ketubah Evidence

### Ketubah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא מֵרָבָא: הוּחְזַק שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה בַּשּׁוּק, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּחְזַק שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה בַּשּׁוּק נַחְזִיק בָּהּ כְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a related topic: **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna inquired of Rava:** If a woman's **marriage contract was established,** i.e., seen, by people **in the marketplace, what is** the *halakha*; is she assumed to be married? Rava **said to him: And should we establish her as a married woman because a marriage contract was established in the marketplace?** Since a marriage contract can be written by a man even before betrothal and without the woman's consent, its existence does not prove that she is married.

The *gemara* now records a query from Rav Acha bar Rav Huna to Rava: *huchzak shtar ketubah bashuk* — it became known in the marketplace that a *ketubah* had been written for a certain woman. *Mahu* — does this constitute evidence that *kiddushin* must have occurred?

Rashi explains the case as one where people saw the woman's *ketubah* document. The question is whether the existence of a known *ketubah* is sufficient grounds to treat the woman as *eshet ish*. Tosafot views this as the strongest proof for their reading of the entire *savlanot* discussion — this case is clearly about evidentiary inference rather than about the *ketubah* itself functioning as an act of *kiddushin*.

### Rav Ashi Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר כָּתְבִי כְּתוּבָּה – חָיְישִׁינַן, כָּתְבִי וַהֲדַר מְקַדְּשִׁי – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן. מְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר כָּתְבִי – פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ סָפְרָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא סָפְרָא הוּא דְּאִתְרְמִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about it?** The Gemara answers: **Rav Ashi says: In a place where** men first **betroth** women **and afterward write a marriage contract, we are concerned** that he had betrothed her; but in a place where **they write** the marriage contract first **and afterward betroth** women, **we are not concerned. In a place where they betroth** women **and afterward write** the marriage contract it **is obvious** that there should be a concern that he has betrothed her. The Gemara clarifies: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* due to a place **where a scribe is not commonly available. Lest you say** that he wrote the marriage contract early because there was **a scribe who happened** to be there, and the prospective husband took the opportunity to avail himself of the scribe's services while intending to perform the betrothal later, Rav Ashi therefore **teaches us** that there is no concern that he might have done so since the custom is to first betroth the woman.

Rav Ashi provides the resolution, and the parallel to the *savlanot* discussion is unmistakable: *b'atar d'mikadshi vehadar katvi* — in a place where the custom is to perform *kiddushin* and only then to write the *ketubah*, *chashinan*. The presence of a written *ketubah* creates a concern that she is *mekudeshet*. *Katvi vehadar mikadshi* — where writing the *ketubah* precedes *kiddushin*, as is our practice today, *lo chashinan*. There is no concern.

The *gemara* presses: if it is obvious that in a place where *kiddushin* precedes *ketubah* writing we would be concerned, what is the *chiddush*? The answer: *lo tzericha d'lo shchiach safra* — the case concerns a place where scribes are not readily available. One might say: perhaps this man intended to marry in three months but happened to get the *ketubah* written now while the scribe was in town. *Mah d'teima safra hu d'isrami* — perhaps the scribe just happened to be available and so the *ketubah* was written early. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: since the norm is that writing follows *kiddushin*, the presence of a written *ketubah* creates sufficient concern to treat the woman as a *safek mekudeshet*, even if there is a possible innocent explanation.

## Two Sisters Issue

### Mishna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ אוֹ אִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ כְּאַחַת – אֵינָן מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים וּבָהֶן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְלִיקֵּט אָדָם אֶחָד כַּלְכַּלָּה שֶׁל תְּאֵנִים. וְשֶׁלָּהֶן הָיְתָה, וְשֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵי כּוּלְּכֶם מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת לִי בְּכַלְכַּלָּה זוֹ״, וְקִיבְּלָה אַחַת מֵהֶן עַל יְדֵי כּוּלָּן. וְאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who betroths a woman and her daughter or a woman and her sister in one** act of betrothal, by saying: You are both betrothed to me, **neither** of them **is betrothed. And an incident** occurred **involving five women, and among them** were **two sisters, and one person gathered a basket of figs that were from their** field, **and** the fruit **was of** the **Sabbatical** Year, **and he said: You are hereby all betrothed to me with this basket, and one of them accepted** it **on behalf of all of them. And the Sages said: The sisters are not betrothed.**

The *gemara* transitions to a new *mishnah*. *Ha-mekadesh isha u-vitah* — if a man attempts to betroth a woman and her daughter, *o isha ve-achotah* — or two sisters, *ke-achas* — simultaneously, *einan mekudashot* — neither is married. The scenario is that he gave the money to effect *kiddushin* on behalf of two women at once — perhaps through an agent who accepted on behalf of both — without having identified one as primary.

The *mishnah* then records: *ma'aseh be-chamesh nashim, u-va-hen shtei achayot* — there was a case involving five women, among them two sisters. A man picked a basket of figs, *ve-she-la-hen haytah* — which belonged to them, *ve-shel shevi'it haytah* — and it was *shemitah* produce (a detail we will revisit), *ve-amar harei kulchen mekudashot li be-kalkalah zo* — and he declared, "Behold, all of you are betrothed to me with this basket." One woman accepted the basket on behalf of all of them. *Ve-amru chachamim ein ha-achayot mekudashot* — the Sages ruled that the sisters are not married.

### Torah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אִשָּׁה אֶל אֲחֹתָהּ לֹא תִקָּח לִצְרֹר״, הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ צָרוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ, לֹא יְהֵא לוֹ לִיקּוּחִים אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the *halakha* that betrothal does not take effect if one betroths these two women simultaneously, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rami bar Ḥama said:** It is **as the verse states: "And you shall not take a woman to her sister, to be a rival to her"** (Leviticus 18:18). **The Torah states: When** he betroths them together so **that they** would **become rival wives to one another, he will not be able to take,** i.e., betroth, **even one of them.**

*Menani mili* — from where is this rule derived? Rami bar Chama cites the verse: *isha el achotah lo tikach litzror legalot ervatah aleha be-chayeha* — "A woman to her sister you shall not take, to be a rival, to uncover her nakedness in her lifetime." Rami bar Chama reads this as establishing that at the very moment when they would become *tzarot* — rivals — the *kiddushin* cannot take effect even for one of them: *be-sha'at she-na'asu tzarot zo le-zo lo yiheye lecha likuchin afilu be-achas mehen*.

### Karet Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וְנִכְרְתוּ הַנְּפָשׁוֹת הָעֹשֹׂת מִקֶּרֶם עַמָּם״ – אִי קִידּוּשִׁין לָא תָּפְסִי בַּהּ, כָּרֵת מִי מִחַיַּיב? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: קְרָא בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, וּמַתְנִיתִין כִּדְרַבָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה אֲפִילּוּ בְּבַת אַחַת אֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so,** how would you explain **this, that it is written** at the end of the passage: "Whoever shall do any of these abominations, **even the souls that do them shall be cut off from among their people"** (Leviticus 18:29); **if betrothal does not take effect with her,** what **renders** him **liable to receive excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]?** If he is not betrothed to either woman, and he engages in intercourse with either one, he is not liable for *karet*, as this punishment is incurred for this sin only if he engaged in intercourse with the sister of his wife. **Rather, Rava says: The verse** is discussing a case where he betrothed them **consecutively.** He first betrothed one sister and then engaged in intercourse with the other, which accounts for the punishment of *karet*. **And the mishna** is to be explained **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba says: Any** matters **that cannot** be accomplished **sequentially cannot be** accomplished **even** if one performs them **simultaneously.** Here too, since he is unable to betroth the mother and daughter or the two sisters sequentially, his betrothal of both of them simultaneously does not take effect.

Rava challenges this derivation. The verse continues: *ve-nichretu ha-nefashot ha-osot mi-kerev amam* — those who do this are subject to *karet*. But *karet* for violating the prohibition of a sister's wife applies only if one is actually married to her sister first. If the *kiddushin* never takes effect for either sister, then there is no violation carrying *karet* — you have not married anyone. *Ei kiddushin lo tafsi bah, karet mi mechayav?* The verse must therefore be speaking of the sequential case: you married one sister, do not have relations with the other — and that would incur *karet*. The verse is not addressing simultaneous *kiddushin* at all.

### Rabba's Principle

Rava therefore concludes: *kra bezu achar zo* — the verse addresses the sequential case. So what is the source for the *mishnah*'s rule that simultaneous *kiddushin* of two sisters fails? *Umatnisin keRabbah* — the *mishnah* follows Rabbah's famous principle: *kol she'eino bezu achar zo, afilu bevat achas eino* — anything that cannot work sequentially cannot work simultaneously either.

The logic is elegant. If Reuven is married to Rachel and then attempts to betroth Leah (her sister), it would not take effect — the second *kiddushin* is prohibited because he is already married to the first sister. Therefore, when he attempts to do both at once, since there is no sequential ordering — no way to determine which is "first" and which is "second" — and since for whichever one is treated as "second" the *kiddushin* categorically cannot take effect, the result is that it takes effect for neither.

## Ma'aser Parallels

### Ma'aser Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַמַּרְבֶּה בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר – פֵּירוֹתָיו מְתוּקָּנִים וּמַעְשְׂרוֹתָיו מְקוּלְקָלִין. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה אֲפִילּוּ בְּבַת אַחַת אֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to his** opinion from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who increases tithes,** i.e., he tithes two-tenths instead of one-tenth of his produce, the remainder of **his produce is rendered fit** for consumption, as he tithed it properly, **but the tithes are ruined,** as the additional tithe is neither a tithe nor tithed produce. Since it is unclear which of the two-tenths is the actual tithe and which is not, the entire two-tenths may be treated neither as a tithe nor as tithed produce. **But** according to Rabba's opinion, **why** is this produce rendered fit for consumption? **Let us say** that **any** matters **that cannot** be accomplished **sequentially cannot be** accomplished **even** if one performs them **simultaneously.** Since one cannot designate a tithe of two-tenths sequentially, one-tenth followed by a second tenth, he should be precluded from simultaneously designating two-tenths of his produce as a tithe. Accordingly, it should be considered as though he had not designated any tithe at all, and his produce should not be regarded as tithed.

Abaye challenges Rabbah's principle with a case from the laws of *ma'aser*. The *mishnah* teaches: *hamarbeh bema'aser, peirotsav metukkanim uma'asrotav mekulkalim* — if one separates more than the required tenth as *ma'aser*, his produce is rectified (*metukkan*) but his *ma'aser* is disqualified (*mekulkal*). The case: you have ten units of grain and you separate two units as *ma'aser*, when only one unit was required. One of those two units properly functions as *ma'aser* and fixes the nine remaining units; the other is merely grain from the *tevel* pile, and now the two units of designated *ma'aser* are a mixture of true *ma'aser* and *tevel*.

Abaye's challenge: just as with two sisters, you cannot take *ma'aser* sequentially from the same pile after you have already taken it — the second designation would not work. So when you take both units simultaneously, since one of them cannot function as *ma'aser*, why does the *mishnah* not say that neither does? Why not apply Rabbah's principle and say both units remain *tevel*?

### Ma'aser Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי מַעֲשֵׂר, דְּאִיתֵיהּ לַחֲצָאִים, דְּאִי אָמַר: ״תִּיקְדּוֹשׁ פַּלְגָא דְחִיטְּתָא״ – קָדְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him:** The case of **tithe is different, as** tithe status **takes effect partially,** i.e., on less than a unit of produce. **As, if one said: Let half** of each grain **of wheat be designated** as tithe, he has **designated it.** Just as one can designate an entire grain of wheat as a tithe, he can likewise designate a half a grain. In this case too, when one tithes two-tenths of the produce, the ruling is not that one-tenth is the actual tithe and the other tenth is untithed produce. Instead, half of each grain of the designated portion is tithe, while the other half of each grain is not. Accordingly, the remainder of the produce is tithed, as one-tenth of the total has been designated as first tithe. The portion designated as the tithe is ruined, because it is impossible to identify which part of each grain is designated.

The *gemara* answers: *shani ma'aser d'ita l'chatzayim* — *ma'aser* is different because it can take effect on half of each grain. *D'ei amar tikadesh palga d'chitata, kadsha* — if one were to say that half of each grain kernel should be sanctified, that would work. Therefore, in the case of excess *ma'aser*, there is no need to conceptualize the two units as competing: one can instead understand all two hundred kernels (across both units) as having their *ma'aser* sanctity distributed across the whole — half of every single kernel, across the entire combined pile, is *ma'aser*. Since the sanctity can apply fractionally and uniformly to the whole, there is no question of "which unit is first and which is second." The entire pile is addressed simultaneously as a unified whole.

This is precisely what distinguishes it from the two sisters: you cannot say half of each sister is married to you. The *kiddushin* must attach to one specific person, and since you have not identified which one, and the sanctity cannot distribute fractionally, the paralysis of undetermined ordering means it takes effect for neither.

### Behema & Toda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה, דְּלֵיכָּא לַחֲצָאִין, וְלֵיכָּא בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה. וְאָמַר רָבָא: יָצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם בַּעֲשִׂירִי, וּקְרָאָן ״עֲשִׂירִי״ – עֲשִׂירִי וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְעוֹרָבִים זֶה בָּזֶה. שָׁאנֵי מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה, דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּטָעוּת. דִּתְנַן: קָרָא לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי ״תְּשִׁיעִי״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״ – שְׁלָשְׁתָּן מְקוּדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection to Rabba's opinion: **But isn't there** the case of **animal tithe, which does not take effect partially,** as one cannot consecrate half an animal for his tithe. **And** likewise there is **no** possibility to separate animals **sequentially,** as once one has designated one animal as animal tithe he cannot designate another animal for the same purpose. **And Rava says:** If **two** animals **emerged** from the enclosure together **as the tenth, and he called them** both the **tenth,** the **tenth and eleventh** animals **are intermingled with each other.** One is consecrated with the sanctity of animal tithe while the other remains a peace-offering, but there is no way to determine which is which. The question arises: If the principle that any matters that cannot be accomplished sequentially cannot be accomplished even if one performs them simultaneously is correct, neither animal is consecrated. The Gemara rejects this: **Animal tithe is different, as** two animals **can be** designated as animal tithe one after the other **in** the case of **an error.** Although one cannot designate the tenth and eleventh animals as the animal tithe *ab initio*, if he did so in error they are both consecrated. **As we learned** in a mishna: If one erred and **called the ninth** animal the **tenth,** and erred again **and** called **the tenth** animal the **ninth and the eleventh** animal the **tenth,** all **three** animals **are consecrated.**

The *gemara* then presses with *ma'aser behema* — the animal tithe. Unlike grain *ma'aser*, *ma'aser behema* cannot take effect on half an animal, *d'leita l'chatzayim*. And it also cannot work sequentially: once the tenth animal has been designated *ma'aser*, if an eleventh walks out, it is not *ma'aser*. So if two animals walk out of the gate simultaneously when the tenth was expected, and you call both of them "ten," why does the *gemara* say *asiri ve-achad asar me'uravin zeh be-zeh* — ten and eleven are mixed, meaning one is *ma'aser* and one is not, and we simply do not know which — rather than saying, by Rabbah's principle, neither is *ma'aser*?

The *gemara* answers: *shani ma'aser behema d'ita b'ta'ut* — *ma'aser behema* is different because it can take effect even through error. If you miscounted — calling the ninth animal "ten," or the tenth "nine," the eleventh "ten" — the *mishnah* teaches that all three nonetheless acquire *kedushah*. This is because to some degree *ma'aser behema* is defined by the objective fact of being the tenth animal, not entirely by the owner's conscious designation. Since the sanctity arises somewhat automatically, the fact that you erroneously called two animals "ten" does not eliminate it from both; one of them objectively is the tenth, and therefore *ma'aser behema* attaches to one of them — we simply cannot determine which.

This, too, distinguishes it from the two sisters. There, the *kiddushin* depends entirely on the person's intentional act and his identification of a specific woman; there is no objective fact of "being the tenth" that would cause *kiddushin* to take effect automatically on one of them despite the ambiguity.

### Toda Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי תּוֹדָה, דְּלֵיתַהּ בְּטָעוּת, וְלֵיתַהּ נָמֵי בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, וְאִיתְּמַר: תּוֹדָה שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה עַל שְׁמוֹנִים חַלּוֹת, חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: קָדְשִׁי לַהּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לָא קָדְשִׁי לַהּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection to Rabba's principle. **There is** the case of the forty loaves that accompany **a thanks-offering, which are not** consecrated if they were designated **in error, and are also not** consecrated if two sets of loaves were designated for the same offering **sequentially. And** yet **it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to **a thanks-offering that was slaughtered** accompanied **by eighty loaves,** twice the required amount. **Ḥizkiyya said: Forty of the eighty** loaves **are consecrated,** even though their identity cannot be determined. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Not** even **forty of the eighty** loaves **are consecrated.**

The *gemara* next considers the *korban toda* — the thanksgiving offering — accompanied by forty loaves. *D'leita b'ta'ut* — the *kedushah* of the loaves does not take effect through error; and *v'leisa b'zeh v'zeh* — once you have slaughtered the *toda* and sanctified forty loaves, additional loaves cannot become *kadosh*. So if someone slaughtered a *toda* with eighty loaves rather than forty, this should present the exact same challenge to Rabbah's principle: since the second forty cannot be sanctified once the first forty are done, and since both sets are presented simultaneously with no specified ordering, why should any forty be sanctified?

On this very question, *Chizkiyah* and Rabbi Yochanan dispute. Chizkiyah says: *kadshei lei arbaim mitoch shmonim* — forty of the eighty are sanctified; we simply do not know which forty. Rabbi Yochanan says: *lo kadshei lei arbaim mitoch shmonim* — none of them are sanctified. Rabbi Yochanan is consistent with Rabbah's principle; but Chizkiyah appears to contradict it. How do we explain Chizkiyah?

### Intent Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר: ״קָדְשִׁי לַהּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים״ – קָדְשִׁי, ״לֹא יִקְדְּשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קָדְשִׁי שְׁמוֹנִים״ – לָא קָדְשִׁי. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בִּסְתָם, מָר סָבַר: לְאַחְרָיוּת קָא מִיכַּוַּון. וּמָר סָבַר: לְקׇרְבָּן גָּדוֹל קָא מִיכַּוַּון.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: **Wasn't it stated with regard to this** dispute that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: All,** both Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan, **concede** that **anywhere that** the one bringing the thanks-offering **said:** Let **forty of the eighty** loaves **be consecrated,** the forty **are consecrated;** and in a case where he said: **Forty** loaves **should not be consecrated unless** all **eighty are consecrated,** everyone agrees that **they are not consecrated. They disagree only with regard to** a case where the one bringing the thanks-offering designates eighty loaves **without specification** of how many loaves he wants consecrated. One **Sage,** Ḥizkiyya, **holds:** Although he designated eighty loaves, he wants to consecrate only forty, and when he sets aside eighty loaves, **he** merely **intends to ensure** that he will have forty. He therefore brought the extra loaves so that if the first forty loaves are lost or become ritually impure the second forty will be consecrated in their place. Consequently, the first forty loaves are consecrated. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds:** He **intends** to bring **a large offering** of eighty loaves, and therefore none of the loaves are consecrated.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing cases based on the person's stated or implied intent. *Hakol modim* — everyone agrees that if the person explicitly said "forty of the eighty should be sanctified," *kadshi* — it works. He is not trying to sanctify all eighty; he specified forty, and forty are sanctified — we simply do not know which ones. Similarly, *lo yikdeshu arbaim ela im ken kadshi shmonim* — if he said "I will not sanctify forty unless it works for all eighty," everyone agrees it does not work, since it categorically cannot work for all eighty, and he conditioned the partial sanctification on the whole.

*Lo nechelku ela bistam* — the dispute between Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan concerns only the ambiguous case, where the person said nothing explicit. *Mar savar l'achrayus ka mechaven* — Chizkiyah holds that we assume the person's underlying intent was to sanctify forty and have the other forty as backup, so forty are sanctified. *U'mar savar l'korban gadol ka mechaven* — Rabbi Yochanan holds we assume the person wanted to make an unusually large offering of all eighty, and since that cannot work, none are sanctified.

The upshot for our discussion is clear: the *toda* case does not refute Rabbah's principle in any straightforward way. Where intent is explicit and directed only at forty, everyone agrees forty are sanctified; where intent is directed at all eighty, everyone agrees none are. Only in the ambiguous case — *bistam* — do Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan part ways, and that debate concerns what presumption to apply when the person's mind is simply unknown. And crucially, the assumption of the two-sisters *mishnah* is that he intended both — so the case maps cleanly onto the position that neither is sanctified.

## Ayin Mesurin L'Biah

### Rava's Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי כְּרַבָּה? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִין לְבִיאָה נִינְהוּ. לִדְבָרָיו דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns from its analysis of Rabba's opinion to the original issue: **And why does Rava explain** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba? Let him derive** the *halakha* in accordance with a principle of his own, as elsewhere (9a) he establishes that **betrothal that is not given to consummation** is not betrothal at all, and all of **these** cases **are** in this category. Any betrothal in which it is prohibited for the couple to engage in sexual intercourse is not considered betrothal at all. Following this principle, the ruling of the mishna can be explained as follows: Since both women would be forbidden to the man in the event that they were both betrothed to him, as each would be the relative of his betrothed, the betrothals are not given to consummation. Therefore, they do not take effect at all. The Gemara answers: Rava could have explained the mishna this way, but **he stated** his explanation **in accordance with the statement of Rami bar Ḥama.** Rava should be understood as saying: Your opinion that the ruling of the mishna is derived from a verse is incorrect, as the verse is referring only to one who betroths the two sisters or the mother and daughter sequentially. Instead, you should explain the mishna as referring to one who betroths them at the same time, with the reason being that any matters that cannot be accomplished sequentially cannot be accomplished even if one performs them simultaneously.

Having established Rabbah's principle as the basis for the *mishnah*'s ruling that simultaneous *kiddushin* of two sisters is invalid, the *gemara* now asks: *veRava lama lei leshnuyei keRabbah* — why did Rava need to explain the *mishnah* on the basis of Rabbah's principle at all? Rava himself holds a broader and more fundamental position — *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah* — that a *kiddushin* which cannot ultimately be consummated does not take effect at all. If that is Rava's general principle, then the *mishnah*'s case — where a man does not know which sister he has married and therefore can never permissibly have relations with either — should be invalid not because of the simultaneous nature of the attempt, but simply because any *kiddushin* that can never lead to *bi'ah* is not *kiddushin*.

*Lidvarav deRami bar Chama ka'amar* — the *gemara* answers that Rava was responding within the framework of Rami bar Chama's derivation. Rami bar Chama had attempted to derive the *mishnah*'s rule from a verse, and Rava showed that Rami's verse doesn't support the simultaneous case. Rava then supplied Rabbah's principle as the textual basis for the *mishnah* even according to those who might not accept his own principle of *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah*. In other words, Rava was arguing conditionally: even if you reject my general principle, you can still derive the *mishnah*'s ruling from Rabbah's principle. My own principle provides an additional and independent basis.

### Abaye v. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִין לְבִיאָה, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. רָבָא אָמַר: לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. אָמַר רָבָא: בַּר אֲהִינָא אַסְבְּרַהּ לִי: ״כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ אִשָּׁה וּבְעָלָהּ״, קִידּוּשִׁין הַמְסוּרִין לְבִיאָה – הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין, קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִין לְבִיאָה – לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that *amora'im* had a dispute: With regard to **betrothal that is not given to consummation, Abaye says it is betrothal,** since the prohibition against engaging in sexual intercourse does not affect the betrothal itself. **Rava says: It is not betrothal. Rava says:** The Sage **bar Ahina explained to me** that this *halakha* is derived from the verse: **"When a man takes a woman and engages in sexual intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), as it indicates that **betrothal that is given to** permitted **consummation is betrothal,** whereas **betrothal that is not given to consummation is not betrothal.**

The *gemara* now states the dispute directly. *Itmar: kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah* — a *kiddushin* that cannot be consummated. *Abaye amar: havi kiddushin* — Abaye holds it still takes legal effect. *Rava amar: lo havi kiddushin* — Rava holds it does not.

The paradigm case is the man who attempted *kiddushin* with one of two sisters without specifying which. Since he can never determine which one he married, and since having relations with either one risks violating the prohibition of *issurei arayot* — she might be his wife's sister, or he might be violating the prohibition by having relations with the non-married sister — his *kiddushin* can never permissibly translate into *bi'ah*. Rava says: this means no *kiddushin* took effect at all. Abaye disagrees: the *kiddushin* is in some sense real; we simply do not know to whom it applies, and both women require a *get*.

### Scriptural Basis

Rava grounds his position in a verse. *Rava bar Hina asbra li* — Bar Hina explained the derivation to me: *ki yikach ish isha u'be'alah* — "When a man takes a woman and has relations with her." The verse juxtaposes *yikach* (taking/betrothal) with *u'be'alah* (consummation), implying that a valid *kiddushin* must be one capable of leading to *bi'ah*: *kiddushin hamesurin l'biah havu kiddushin* — only a *kiddushin* that is "given over" to *bi'ah* constitutes *kiddushin*. *Kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah, lo havu kiddushin*.

### Tosafot Nuance

Tosafot raises an obvious difficulty with Rava's principle: according to Rava, should *kiddushin* in cases of *issurei lavin* — prohibitions carrying lashes rather than *karet*, such as a *kohen* marrying a *grushah* or a *kohen gadol* marrying a *widow* — not also fail, since these unions cannot be permissibly consummated? Yet the *gemara* elsewhere is clear that such *kiddushin* takes effect.

To resolve this, Tosafot refines the principle. The operative category of *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah* is not simply any *kiddushin* where *bi'ah* happens to be prohibited. Rather, it is specifically a case where it is the act of *kiddushin itself* that creates the *issur bi'ah*. In the case of two sisters: before any *kiddushin* occurs, there is no prohibition on having relations with either woman. It is only because he performed (or attempted) *kiddushin* with one of them that the other becomes prohibited as his wife's sister. The *kiddushin* generates the very *issur* that makes consummation impossible.

By contrast, in the case of a *kohen* and a *grushah*, the *issur* preexists and is independent of the *kiddushin*. The woman's status as a *grushah* was established before he ever betrothed her; his act of *kiddushin* did not create the prohibition. In such cases, the *kiddushin* takes effect despite the prohibition.

This also clarifies a distinct scenario: suppose a woman might be a man's sister but it cannot be determined — perhaps because her mother's relationship history is unknown. If he were to perform *kiddushin* with her, would that be a *safek kiddushin* or null *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah*? According to Rashi's broader reading, it might be categorized as *einam mesurin l'biah* since he can never permissibly have relations with her. According to Tosafot's refinement, it would be a *safek kiddushin* — because the prohibition on his potential sister preexists and is wholly independent of his act of *kiddushin*. His betrothal did not create the *issur*; it was always there. Therefore Tosafot's principle does not apply, and she would be *safek mekudeshet*.

## Five Women Story

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ אוֹ אִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ כְּאַחַת – אֵינָן מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת. הָא אַחַת מֵאִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ אוֹ מֵאִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאַמַּאי? קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִין לְבִיאָה נִינְהוּ! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to analyze these two opinions. **We learned** in the mishna: In the case of **one who betroths a woman and her daughter or a woman and her sister in one** act of betrothal **neither** of them **is betrothed.** The Gemara analyzes this: **But** if he said he is betrothing only **one of** this pair, i.e., **the woman or her daughter, or** one **of** that pair, i.e., **the woman or her sister,** without specifying which of them, **she is betrothed.** Nevertheless, in practice it is prohibited for him to engage in intercourse with either of them, as it cannot be determined which woman he betrothed and which woman is the relative of his betrothed. **But why** should the betrothal take effect? **These are** each **a betrothal that is not given to consummation,** and should not be valid according to Rava. Shall we say this is **a conclusive refutation** to the opinion **of Rava?**

The *gemara* now tests Rava's principle against the *mishnah* itself. *Tnan: hamkadesh isha u'vitah o isha v'achotah k'achat, einan mekudashot* — both are not married. The *mishnah* implies: *ha achat* — if he married only one, without specifying which, she would be *mekudeshet*. But this seems to contradict Rava directly: such a *kiddushin* cannot lead to *bi'ah* and should therefore be null. *Tiufta d'Rava* — this is a decisive refutation of Rava's position.

The *mishnah*'s concluding case involves five women including two sisters, a basket of figs, *shemitah* produce, and the declaration *harei kulchen mekudashot li be-kalkalah zo* — "All of you are betrothed to me with this basket," accepted by one woman on behalf of all. The Sages ruled that the sisters are not married — implying the other three are.

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים וּבָהֶן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְלִיקֵּט אֶחָד כַּלְכַּלָּה שֶׁל תְּאֵנִים, וְשֶׁלָּהֶן הָיְתָה, וְשֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵי כּוּלְּכֶם מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת לִי בְּכַלְכַּלָּה זוֹ״. וְאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת. אֲחָיוֹת הוּא דְּאֵינָן מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת, הָא נָכְרִיּוֹת – מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rava** could have **said to you** in response: **And according to your reasoning** that a betrothal that is not given to consummation is still betrothal, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: **An incident** occurred **involving five women, and among them** were **two sisters. And one** man **gathered a basket of figs that were from their** field, **and** the fruit **was of the Sabbatical** Year. **And he said: You are hereby all betrothed to me with this basket,** and one of them accepted it on behalf of all of them. **And the Sages said: The sisters are not betrothed.** This indicates that **it is the sisters who are not betrothed, but the unrelated women,** the other three women who are not related to each other, **are betrothed.**

Rava responds: *v'letameich eima seifa* — let us examine the end of the *mishnah* carefully. The case in which the three non-sisters are *safek mekudeshet* is the *mishnah*'s conclusion. But if we take the declaration at face value — *harei kulchen*, all five of you — then this is the case of *at v'chamor lo kana*: if one attempts to transfer ownership jointly to a valid recipient and an invalid one (classically, a person and a fetus not yet born), the entire transaction fails for the valid recipient as well, since the package cannot be divided. Similarly here, if he tried to marry all five and it failed for two of them, why would it work for the other three?

Therefore, Rava argues, the *mishnah*'s end-case must be read as *d'amar lehu achat mikem* — he said "one of you," not "all five of you." He was attempting to betroth one unspecified woman from the group of five. In that case, three of the five could be *safek mekudeshet* — any one of the three non-sisters might be the intended bride — while neither sister qualifies.

### Position Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר ״כּוּלְּכֶם״ – ״קְנִי אַתְּ וַחֲמוֹר״ הוּא, וְ״אַתְּ וַחֲמוֹר״ לֹא קָנָה. אֶלָּא לָאו דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״אַחַת מִכֶּם״ וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת? לְרָבָא קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא, לְאַבָּיֵי קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא. אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, רָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to explain what Rava could have said. **What are the circumstances? If we say that he said: You are all** betrothed to me, this **is** comparable to one who gives something to another and says: **Let you and the donkey acquire** this item. **And** in a situation where someone would say: Let **you and the donkey** acquire it, the recipient has **not acquired** the item. Since the donkey cannot acquire items the person does not acquire it either, because the owner linked the two acquisitions. Here too, since he tried to give the item of the betrothal to all of the women, some of whom, the sisters, he cannot betroth together, the betrothal should not take effect even with the other women despite the fact that they are suitable for him. **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where he said to them: One of you** is betrothed, **and it teaches** that **the sisters are not betrothed,** because with regard to the sisters the betrothal is not given to consummation? The Gemara comments: If so, the **first clause** of the mishna **is difficult for Rava,** while the **last clause is difficult for Abaye.** The Gemara presents a resolution: **Abaye explains** the last clause according **to his** line of **reasoning,** and **Rava explains** the first clause according **to his** line of **reasoning.**

This reading creates an asymmetry between the beginning and end of the *mishnah*. *L'Rava kashya reisha* — for Rava, the beginning of the *mishnah* is difficult: it says *ke-achas*, at the same time, implying the problem is the simultaneity. But according to Rava, even attempting *kiddushin* with one unspecified sister — without simultaneity — should also fail, since it would be *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah*. The beginning of the *mishnah* seems to identify the *ke-achas* as the operative defect.

*V'l'Abaye kashya seifa* — for Abaye, the end of the *mishnah* is difficult. Reading the end as "one of you," Abaye must explain why neither sister would be married even in that case, since according to Abaye, *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah* does take effect — the sisters should at least be *safek mekudeshet*.

### Abaye's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ כְּאַחַת – אֵינָן מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת. הָא אַחַת מֵאִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, מֵאִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאִם אָמַר: ״הָרְאוּיָה לְבִיאָה תִּתְקַדֵּשׁ לִי״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים וּבָהֶן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְלִיקֵּט אֶחָד כַּלְכַּלָּה שֶׁל תְּאֵנִים וְאָמַר: ״הָרְאוּיָה לִי מִכֶּם תִּתְקַדֵּשׁ לִי״, וְאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye explains** it according **to his** line of **reasoning,** as follows: In the case of **one who betroths a woman and her daughter or a woman and her sister in one** act of betrothal **neither** of them **is betrothed. But** if he betrothed **one of** a pair consisting of **a woman and her daughter** or **a woman and her sister** by saying: One of you is betrothed to me, each of them is **betrothed** as a matter of uncertainty and requires a bill of divorce, despite the fact that the betrothal is not given to consummation, in case he is in fact betrothed to the other one. The Gemara continues Abaye's explanation: **And if he** explicitly **said: The one** of you **fit for sexual intercourse shall be betrothed to me, neither** of them **is betrothed,** as he cannot engage in intercourse with either, not knowing which of them is his betrothed and which is his betrothed's relative. **And an incident also** occurred **involving five women, and among them** were **two sisters. And one** man **gathered a basket of figs and said: The one** among you **fit** for intercourse **with me shall be betrothed to me** with this basket, **and the Sages said: The sisters are not betrothed.**

*Abaye metaretz l'taameih, Rava metaretz l'taameih* — each resolves the other's difficulty according to his own position. Abaye explains the *mishnah* as follows: *hamkadesh isha u'vitah o isha v'achotah k'achat, einan mekudashot* — only if he attempted to marry both at once do neither become married. *Achat me'isha u'vitah o me'isha v'achotah, mekudeshet* — if he attempted to marry only one, without specifying which, she is in principle *mekudeshet* (as a *safek*). However, *ve'im amar hara'uya l'vi'ah titkadesh li* — if he explicitly stipulated that the one who is permissible to him should be betrothed, then *eina mekudeshet*, since there is no way to know who qualifies, and this *kiddushin she'eino masur l'vi'ah, d'hava lei kiddushin she'eino masur l'vi'ah*.

For the five women case, Abaye reads the man's statement as implicitly including *hara'uya li mikem titkadesh li* — one of you who is fit for me should be betrothed to me. Since the sisters are not "fit" for him in the sense that there is no *bi'ah* possible, they are excluded, and the ruling that they are not married is explained. This reading is something Abaye inserts into the text because Rava's reading of the *mishnah* forced the case into the "one of you" framework — Abaye simply adds the further specification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אַחַת מֵאִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ אוֹ אַחַת מֵאִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ – נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וַאֲחוֹתָהּ כְּאַחַת, וְאֵינָן מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת. וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּחָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים וּבָהֶם שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְלִיקֵּט אֶחָד כַּלְכַּלָּה שֶׁל תְּאֵנִים וְאָמַר: הֲרֵי כּוּלְּכֶם וְאַחַת מִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת לִי בְּכַלְכַּלָּה זוֹ, וְאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אֲחָיוֹת מְקוּדָּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava explains** according **to his** line of **reasoning:** In the case of **one who betroths one of** a pair consisting of **a woman and her daughter or one of** a pair consisting of **a woman and her sister it becomes as one who simultaneously betrothed a woman and her daughter or a woman and her sister, and they are not betrothed,** since betrothal that is not given to consummation is not considered betrothal. **And an incident also** occurred **involving five women, and among them** were **two sisters. And one** man **gathered a basket of figs and said: All of you, and one of** the **two sisters, are hereby betrothed to me with this basket. And the Sages said: The sisters are not betrothed.** Since the mishna can be read in either of these two ways, nothing can be proven from it with regard to the issue of betrothal that is not given to consummation.

Rava, in turn, explains the beginning of the *mishnah*: *hamkadesh achat me'isha u'vitah o achat me'isha v'achotah, na'aseh k'mi shekidesh isha u'vitah v'isha v'achotah k'achat* — attempting to betroth one unspecified member of a forbidden pair is legally equivalent to attempting to betroth both simultaneously. Even though in reality he only tried to betroth one, the indeterminacy makes it as if he tried to betroth both at once, and therefore neither is married.

## Unspecified Daughter

### Baraita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ סְתָם – אֵין הַבּוֹגְרוֹת בַּכְּלָל. הָא קְטַנּוֹת בַּכְּלָל. וְאַמַּאי? קִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁאֵין מְסוּרִין לְבִיאָה נִינְהוּ, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְּרָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna: In the case of **one who betroths his daughter** to a man **without specification,** i.e., without specifying which daughter he meant, **the grown women are not included** among those who might be betrothed, since he does not have the right to betroth them. The Gemara analyzes this: One can infer that the **minor daughters are included,** and each is betrothed as a matter of uncertainty. **But why** are the minor daughters betrothed? Each of **these** betrothals is a **betrothal that is not given to consummation,** since he did not specify which of his minor daughters he had in mind. Therefore, according to Rava betrothal should not take effect with regard to any of them, **and** this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rava.**

The *gemara* brings a further challenge to Rava from a *baraita*: *hamkadesh et bito stam* — if a man betroths off "my daughter" without specifying which daughter. *Eina bogeres bichlal* — his adult daughters are obviously not included, since a father has no legal authority to betroth an adult daughter on her behalf. *Haketanot bichlal* — the minor daughters, however, are included; the *baraita* implies that one of the minor daughters is *safek mekudeshet*.

This is a direct challenge to Rava. If there are multiple minor daughters and the father did not specify which one he was marrying off, this is structurally identical to the two-sisters case: *kiddushin she'ein mesurin l'vi'ah ninhu* — the groom can never know which daughter he married, and therefore can never permissibly have relations with any of them. *U'bisyufta deRava* — this appears to be a decisive refutation of Rava.

### Rava's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּשֶׁאֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא גְּדוֹלָה וּקְטַנָּה. הָא בּוֹגְרוֹת קָתָנֵי! מַאי בּוֹגְרוֹת – בּוֹגְרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא. אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rava** could have **said to you: With what are we dealing here?** It is **when there is only** one **adult** daughter **and** one **minor** daughter. Consequently, once the adult daughter is removed from consideration, it is clear that the father has betrothed the minor daughter, and the betrothal is given to consummation. The Gemara questions this explanation: **But** that mishna **teaches: Grown women,** in the plural. The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of **grown women? Grown women in general.** In other words, whenever people betroth their daughters without further specification, only the minor daughters, and not the adult daughters, are included. The Gemara asks: **If so,** that there are only two daughters, **what** is the purpose **of stating** this; what novel *halakha* does it teach?

Rava responds: *hacha bemai askinan keshein sham ela gedola u'ktana* — the *baraita* addresses a case where there is only one adult daughter and one minor daughter. In that scenario, it is obvious that the *kiddushin* was accepted for the minor daughter, since the father has no authority over the adult. There is no ambiguity whatsoever; the groom knows exactly whom he married. This is entirely unlike the two-sisters case.

The *gemara* challenges: the *baraita* uses the plural *bogeros* and *ketanot*, implying multiple daughters of each type. Rava deflects: *mai bogeros bogeros de'alma* — the plural "adult daughters" is a general formulation referring to the category of adult daughters that any person might have, not to this particular father having many adult daughters. In this case, we read it as: his one adult daughter is excluded, and his one minor daughter is the one who is *safek mekudeshet*. The *gemara* then asks, *ei hachi mai lemimra* — if there is only one minor daughter, what is the *chiddush*? It is entirely obvious.

### Shaliach Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּשַׁוִּיתֵיהּ שָׁלִיחַ. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כִּי מְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשִׁין אַדַּעְתַּהּ דִּידַהּ קָא מְקַבֵּל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ מִידֵּי דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a situation **where** his adult daughter **designated him** as her **agent** to betroth her. **Lest you say** that **when** he **accepts betrothal** from someone on behalf of his daughter without further specification he **accepts it with her in mind,** and he intended to betroth her rather than his minor daughter, the mishna therefore **teaches us that a person does not set aside something from which he has benefit,** e.g., the betrothal of his minor daughter, where he keeps the betrothal money, in favor of something from which he has no benefit, e.g., the betrothal of his adult daughter, where she keeps the betrothal money.

The *gemara* suggests a more nuanced reading: *hacha bemai askinan deshavisa shaliach* — the *baraita* addresses a case where the adult daughter had appointed her father as her *shaliach*, her agent, to arrange her *kiddushin*. In that case, one might think the father would betroth off the adult daughter first: after all, he is acting as her *shaliach*, and perhaps his acceptance of the *kiddushin* was on her behalf. The ambiguity is real — was this the minor daughter (his primary charge) or the adult daughter (whose *shaliach* he is)? *Mahu d'teima ki mekabel kiddushin adaita dideihu ka mekabel* — one might think he accepted the *kiddushin* with the adult daughter in mind, since she is older and the pressure to see her settled is perhaps greater.

### Priority Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ ״קִידּוּשַׁי לְדִידָךְ״? אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ מִצְוָה דְּרַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ וְעָבֵיד מִצְוָה דְּלָא רַמְיָא עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Aren't we** also **dealing** with a case **where** the adult daughter **said to him:** When you betroth me, **my betrothal** money **is for you?** Accordingly, he derives benefit from her betrothal as well. The Gemara explains: **Even so,** the father would not have intended to betroth his adult daughter, since **a person does not set aside a mitzva that is incumbent upon him,** e.g., the betrothal of his minor daughter, **and perform a mitzva that is not incumbent upon him,** e.g., the betrothal of his adult daughter.

*Ka mashma lan dela shavik inish midi de'is lei hana'ah minei* — the *baraita* teaches us that a man does not set aside something from which he derives benefit in order to do something else first. The father benefits financially from the *kiddushin* of the minor daughter — the *kesef kiddushin* goes to him in that case. He would therefore not prioritize the adult daughter's *kiddushin*, from which he receives no such benefit, over the minor daughter's.

The *gemara* then considers: what if the adult daughter said, "Father, you may keep the *kiddushin* money for yourself"? *Mahu d'teima ki mekabel kiddushin adaita dideihu ka mekabel* — perhaps even then he might betroth the adult daughter first. The *gemara* rejects this: *afilu hachi lo shavik inish mitzvah deramya alei ve'avid mitzvah dela ramya alei* — even so, a person does not set aside a *mitzvah* that is incumbent upon him and perform instead one that is not. The father bears a primary obligatory *mitzvah* to marry off his minor daughters; for his adult daughter, any help he provides is supererogatory. He would therefore always marry off the minor daughter first. The result is that the *baraita* does not challenge Rava — and the debate between Abaye and Rava over *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'biah* will continue into the next session.
