# Bava Batra 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Land Sale & Ona'ah

### Beit Kor Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ – מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל״, פִּיחֵת כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – יְנַכֶּה, הוֹתִיר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – יַחֲזִיר. וְאִם אָמַר: ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״, אֲפִילּוּ פִּיחֵת רוֹבַע לִסְאָה, אוֹ הוֹתִיר רוֹבַע לִסְאָה – הִגִּיעוֹ. יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן, יַעֲשֶׂה חֶשְׁבּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says to another: **I am selling you** a plot of **earth** the size of **a *beit kor*, measured** precisely **with a rope,** and **he gave** him even **the slightest amount less** than what was stipulated, the seller **must deduct** the difference from the purchase price of the field and return money to the buyer. If **he gave** him even **the slightest amount more** than what was stipulated, the buyer **must return** the difference to the seller. **And if** the seller **said** to the buyer that he is selling him a *beit kor* of land **more or less,** then **even if he gave** him **a quarter***-kav* **per *se'a* less** than what was stipulated, **or he gave him a quarter***-kav* **per *se'a* more** that what was stipulated, i.e., he gave him one twenty-fourth more or less than what was required, **it is his.** The sale is valid, since the seller told the buyer in advance that he was not committing himself to precise measurements. If the difference is **greater than that** amount, **he must make a calculation,** and the party that suffered a loss must be compensated.

The Mishnah at the bottom of 103b introduces the case of someone selling a plot of land described as a *beit kor* — a unit of approximately thirty *se'ah* — and the consequences that follow depending on how the sale was characterized. If the seller declares that the sale is *mida bechevel*, measured precisely by the yardstick, then any discrepancy, however small, must be corrected: if the buyer received too little, he receives a price reduction, and if he received too much, he returns the excess, whether land or money. If, however, the seller qualified the sale as *hen chaser hen yeter* — more or less — then a tolerance of one *rova* per *se'ah* is permitted. Since there are six *kav* to a *se'ah* and four *reva'im* to a *kav*, this tolerance works out to 1/24 of the total. So long as the discrepancy falls within that 1/24, both buyer and seller must absorb it without adjustment. Once the discrepancy exceeds that threshold, however, the entire overage — not merely the portion beyond the tolerance — must be made good. The mechanics of how this correction is made, and who bears what prerogative, are taken up in the *gemara* and will be addressed in due course.

### Ona'ah & Bitul Mekach

Before turning to the *gemara*, it is worth pausing on a foundational question: why do the rules of *ona'ah* not simply dispose of this entire discussion? *Ein ona'ah bekarka'ot* — the principle that overcharging does not apply to real property — would seem to immunize land sales from any financial correction. But this principle is not quite what is at stake here. *Ona'ah* is the remedy when you receive exactly what you bargained for in terms of quantity, but the price paid was above the fair market rate. Here, by contrast, the quantity itself is off — the buyer received more or less land than the stated amount. That is not a question of pricing but of delivery, and *ona'ah* does not govern it.

The more pressing question is *bitul mekach* — the voiding of a mistaken transaction. Normally, when you receive something materially different from what you contracted for, the sale is null. Why then does the Mishnah not say that the entire sale is void when the measurement is off? The *Rashbam* explains that by landed property, people do not generally wish to unwind the transaction entirely. They understand that a field may not come out to an exact figure and they remain attached to the underlying object — the field itself — rather than to the precise quantity. Accordingly, the remedy is adjustment, not nullification.

### Tosafot: Measured vs. Unspecified

*Tosafot* takes a different and arguably more precise approach. At the top of 104a, in the *tosafot* beginning *pachot kolshehu yenakeh*, the *Ri* draws a critical distinction between two fundamentally different types of discrepancy. The first is where the seller claimed to have actually measured the land and represented a specific figure, which then turned out to be false — whether through deception or error. In that case, the *Ri* says, we have a genuine *mekach ta'ut*: the buyer was led to believe a precise measurement had been conducted and relied upon it, so when the measurement proves wrong, the transaction is vitiated.

The second case — which is the case of our Mishnah — is where no precise measurement has yet been conducted. The parties are standing in the field or dealing with a known parcel, and the seller says, "I am selling you this field, which is a *beit kor*," or "approximately a *beit kor*." Here, *Tosafot* explains, everyone understands from the outset that the figure has not been rigorously established. The seller is not representing that the land has been surveyed to the inch; he is giving an estimate of a known object. Because imprecision is implicitly acknowledged, the discrepancy does not constitute a *mekach ta'ut*.

This distinction also clarifies what it means when a seller says he is selling *mida bechevel*, precisely. According to *Tosafot*, this does not mean he is asserting that the land has already been measured and found to be exactly a *beit kor*. If he were asserting that and it proved wrong, there would be a *mekach ta'ut*. Rather, *mida bechevel* means: whatever the field turns out to measure, I commit to making it exactly right — any deviation, however slight, will be corrected. The precision is a commitment, not a factual claim about a completed measurement.

A further *Tosafot*, in the *tosafot* beginning *pachas kol shehu yenake*, addresses the question of why the buyer cannot simply demand his money back when the land falls short. The buyer might argue: I wanted a full *beit kor*, not a discounted price for less land. *Tosafot* answers that since the parties were dealing with a specific, known field from the start, and everyone understood that measurement might yield slightly more or less than anticipated, the buyer is not in a position to claim total surprise. The object of the sale was always this particular field; the size was an attribute of it, not the independent subject of the transaction. Because of that, *bitul mekach* does not operate to unwind the sale.

This understanding — that the case presupposes a known, identified field with understood boundaries — is central to the entire sugya and becomes essential when the *gemara* turns to the *stam* case.

## Stam Land Sale

### Stam Case Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״בֵּית כּוֹר״ סְתָמָא, מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ – מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל״,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna considers two cases of selling a *beit kor* of land: First, where the seller said that he is selling a *beit kor* measured precisely with a rope, in which case a refund must be made no matter how small the deviation; and second, where the seller said that he is selling a *beit kor* more or less, in which case the sale is valid as long as the deviation is no more than a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*. In connection with these cases, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one says he is selling **a *beit kor*** of land, and he said this **without** any further **specification, what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a solution from what was taught in the mishna: If one says to another: **I am selling you** a plot of **earth** the size of **a *beit kor*, measured** precisely **with a rope,**

The *gemara* opens with a classic *iba'ya lehu*: what is the *halacha* in a *stam* land sale — that is, where neither *mida bechevel* nor *hen chaser hen yeter* was specified? The seller said simply, "I am selling you a *beit kor*," with no modifier whatsoever. Does the absence of any qualifier mean that the slightest discrepancy must be corrected, as if he had said *mida bechevel*? Or does silence carry the more lenient implication of *hen chaser hen yeter*, allowing the 1/24 tolerance?

### 1/24 Tolerance Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּחוֹת כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – יְנַכֶּה, יָתֵר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – יַחֲזִיר. הָא סְתָמָא – כְּ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״ דָּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** and **he gave** him even **the slightest amount less** that what was stipulated, the seller **must deduct** the difference from the purchase price of the field and return money to the buyer. If he gave him even **the slightest amount more** than what was stipulated, the buyer **must return** the difference to the seller. The reason that even the slightest difference in value must be returned is that the seller specified that he was selling land measured precisely with a rope. **But** had he sold the land **without** further **specification,** it would be **like** he sold it saying that it is a *beit kor* **more or less.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא – וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״; אֲפִילּוּ פִּיחֵת רוֹבַע לִסְאָה אוֹ הוֹתִיר רוֹבַע לִסְאָה – הִגִּיעוֹ. הָא סְתָמָא – כְּמִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל דָּמֵי! אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument by proposing another, completely opposite proof: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **And if** the seller **said to** the buyer that he is selling him a *beit kor* of land **more or less,** then **even if he gave** him **one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a* less** than what was stipulated, **or he gave him one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a* more** than what was stipulated, **it is his** and the sale is valid. One can infer: The reason that the sale is valid is that he specified that he was selling a *beit kor* of land more or less. **But** had he sold the land **without** further **specification,** it would be **like** he sold it saying that he was selling the land **measured** precisely **with a rope. Rather,** since the mishna can be interpreted in two opposite ways, **no** inference is **to be learned from** the mishna.

The *gemara* first attempts to resolve the question from the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah states that if the seller says *mida bechevel*, precision is required; the inference is that had he said nothing — *stam* — it would be treated as *hen chaser hen yeter*. The *gemara* then tests the reverse inference from the second clause: the Mishnah also says that if the seller says *hen chaser hen yeter*, the 1/24 tolerance applies; from which one could infer that *stam* would be treated as *mida bechevel*. Since both inferences are possible, the Mishnah itself yields no definitive answer. The *gemara* therefore says: *ella meha leika lemishma mina* — from this Mishnah we cannot draw a conclusion.

### Stam = Tolerance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״; ״כְּבֵית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״; ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – אֲפִילּוּ פִּיחֵת רוֹבַע לִסְאָה, אוֹ הוֹתִיר רוֹבַע לִסְאָה – הִגִּיעוֹ. אַלְמָא סְתָמָא נָמֵי, כְּ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״ דָּמֵי! הָתָם, פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ – אֵיזֶהוּ בֵּית כּוֹר שֶׁהִיא כְּבֵית כּוֹר? כְּגוֹן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara tries to present another proof: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If the seller said to the buyer: **I am selling you** a plot of **earth** the size of **a *beit kor*;** or he said to him: **I am selling you** a plot of **earth about** the size of **a *beit kor*;** or he said to him: **I am selling you** a *beit kor* **more or less;** then **even if he gave** him **one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a* less** than what was stipulated, **or he gave him one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a* more** than what was stipulated, **it is his** and the sale is valid. One can infer: **Apparently,** selling a *beit kor* of land **without** further **specification** is **also like** selling it **more or less.** The Gemara rejects this proof: **There,** the *tanna* **is explaining** his statement, which should be understood as follows: **When is** the phrase **a *beit kor*** treated like the phrase **about** the size of **a *beit kor*?** In a case **where** the seller **says to** the buyer: I am selling you a *beit kor* **more or less.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: אִם כֵּן, ״אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ ״אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סְתָמָא נָמֵי כְּ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״ דָּמֵי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to this: If that is so,** then **why do I** need to repeat the words: **I am selling you,** three times? The fact that the *baraita* repeats these words in each clause indicates that it is discussing three separate sale transactions, and not that the three clauses are all referring to one case. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** the *baraita* that selling a *beit kor* of land **without** further **specification** is **like** selling it **more or less?** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that this is so.

A *braita* is then cited: "A *beit kor* of soil I am selling you; like a *beit kor* of soil I am selling you; more or less I am selling you." The *braita* states that in any of these three formulations, a tolerance of a *rova* per *se'ah* — 1/24 — applies, and if the discrepancy stays within that tolerance, the sale stands as is. This appears to settle the matter: the first formulation, *beit kor afar ani mocher lach*, is the pure *stam* case with no modifier, and it is treated identically to the explicit *hen chaser hen yeter* formulation.

The *gemara* attempts to resist this reading by suggesting that perhaps the *braita* is not presenting three separate cases but rather one case described cumulatively: "a *beit kor* — that is, like a *beit kor* — that is, more or less." Under this reading, only the combined formulation with all three phrases would carry the *hen chaser hen yeter* tolerance. The *stam* case alone would remain unresolved. Rav Ashi rejects this interpretation forcefully: if the *braita* intended a single combined case, it would not have repeated *ani mocher lach* after each separate phrase. The repetition of the full sale declaration after each formulation demonstrates that each is a standalone case with independent legal force. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *alma stam nami ke-hen chaser hen yeter dami* — even without any modifier, a *stam* land sale carries the 1/24 tolerance. *Shema mina*.

### Field-Specific Context

*Tosafot* adds a qualification that prevents a misreading of this ruling. One might think that because a *stam* sale allows a 1/24 tolerance, a seller could deliberately measure out slightly less than the full *beit kor* and invoke this rule. That is not the case. The entire framework of this Mishnah, as *Tosafot* explains in two places, assumes that the parties are dealing with a specific, known field with established boundaries. The subject of the transaction is the field itself; the stated acreage is a description of it. The tolerance exists because when dealing with a concrete, identified piece of land, everyone understands that measurement will be approximate. It is not a license for the seller to intentionally short-deliver a quantity of land.

If, by contrast, the seller simply said he would sell the buyer some land to be measured out from a larger holding — without a specific identified parcel as the object — then even a *stam* statement of quantity would obligate him to deliver the full amount precisely. In that case, the quantity is not an attribute of a known object but is itself the subject of the transaction, and full delivery is required.

## Seller's Prerogative

### Excess Land Handling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה הוּא מַחֲזִיר לוֹ? מָעוֹת. וְאִם רָצָה, מַחֲזִיר לוֹ קַרְקַע. וְלָמָה אָמְרוּ מַחֲזִיר לוֹ מָעוֹת? לְיַיפּוֹת כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל מוֹכֵר – שֶׁאִם שִׁיֵּיר בַּשָּׂדֶה בֵּית תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין, וּבַגִּינָּה בֵּית חֲצִי קַב; וּכְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – בֵּית רוֹבַע; מַחֲזִיר לוֹ אֶת הַקַּרְקַע. וְלֹא אֶת הָרוֹבַע בִּלְבַד הוּא מַחֲזִיר, אֶלָּא אֶת כָּל הַמּוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** If the buyer received too much land, so that he must now compensate the seller, **what does he return to him?** He returns **money,** i.e., he pays the seller for the surplus land. **And if** the seller so **wishes,** the buyer **returns** the surplus **land to him. Why** then **did** the Sages **say** that **he returns money to him?** They said this **in order to enhance the power of the seller,** and enable him to demand payment for the surplus land, rather than accept its return. **As, if the surplus in the field was an area required for sowing nine *kav*** of seed, **and in a garden an area required for sowing a half-*kav*** of seed, **or, according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva, an area required for sowing a quarter**-*kav* of seed (see 11a), the buyer must **return the land** itself **to** the seller, and the seller cannot demand payment in money. **And** if the surplus is greater than a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*, **it is not only the quarter**-*kav* **that he returns; rather,** he returns **all of the surplus.** Since he is already required to make a refund, the refund must be made in the precise amount.

The Mishnah addresses not only the case of the buyer receiving too little, but also the case where he received too much. When the buyer receives less than expected and the discrepancy exceeds the tolerable amount, the remedy is straightforward: the buyer receives a price reduction proportional to the shortfall. *Tosafot* has already explained why the buyer cannot demand full rescission — because the sale was always of this specific field, and the parties understood that measurement might vary.

The more interesting case is when the buyer receives too much. Here the Mishnah grants the *mocher* — the seller — a prerogative: he may choose either to recover the extra land or to demand the monetary equivalent of that extra land from the buyer. This makes intuitive sense when the excess is a small strip that would be difficult to use independently. What use is a tiny sliver of land? The seller may prefer to be compensated in money rather than take back a strip too small to farm on its own. However, the Mishnah qualifies this: once the excess land is objectively large enough to be a usable plot in its own right — the Mishnah specifies nine *kav* for a wheat field — the seller loses his prerogative. At that point, only land is returned; the seller cannot force the buyer to pay money for an independently usable parcel.

### Mishna vs. Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ מַחֲזִיר לוֹ? מָעוֹת וְכוּ׳. לְיַפּוֹת כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל מוֹכֵר אָמְרִינַן, לְיַפּוֹת כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל לוֹקֵחַ לָא אָמְרִינַן?!
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to a buyer who received too much land and now must compensate the seller, the mishna teaches: **What does he return to him?** He pays him **money,** and if the seller so wishes, the buyer returns the surplus land to him, because the Sages said that the buyer pays money in order to enhance the power of the seller. In other words, the Sages allowed the seller to choose whether to take back the surplus land or to demand payment for it from the buyer, even though this effectively forces the buyer to purchase the surplus land from him. The Gemara asks: Is it correct that **we say** that **the seller's power should be enhanced, and** that **we do not say** that **the buyer's power should be enhanced?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: פִּיחֵת שִׁבְעַת קַבִּין וּמֶחֱצָה לְכוֹר, אוֹ הוֹתִיר שִׁבְעַת קַבִּין וּמֶחֱצָה לְכוֹר – הִגִּיעוֹ. יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן – כּוֹפִין אֶת הַמּוֹכֵר לִמְכּוֹר וְאֶת הַלּוֹקֵחַ לִיקַּח.
>
> **Translation:** But **isn't it taught** otherwise in a *baraita*: **If** the seller **gave** the buyer **seven and a half *kav* per *kor* less** than what was stipulated, which is the equivalent of a quarter*-kav* per *se'a*, as a *kor* is equal to thirty *se'a*, **or he gave him seven and a half *kav* per *kor* more** than what was stipulated, **it is his** and the sale is valid. If the difference is **greater than that** amount, the court **compels the seller to sell and the buyer to buy** the difference. This indicates that we also say that the buyer's power should be enhanced, since if the buyer so wishes, the seller is compelled to sell him land and accept payment for it.

A *braita* appears to contradict the Mishnah's allocation of prerogative entirely to the seller. The *braita* states: if the discrepancy is within 1/24, the sale stands. If it exceeds seven and a half *kav* per *kor* — the 1/24 threshold for a full *kor* — then *kofin et hamocher limkor ve'et halokeach likach*: we force the seller to sell and the buyer to buy. This language seems to grant both sides an independent right to demand that the excess land be incorporated into the buyer's purchase. The seller can be forced to sell even if he wants the land back, and the buyer can be forced to buy even if he would rather return the land. This stands in apparent tension with our Mishnah, which places the choice entirely with the seller.

### Price Adjustment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, כְּגוֹן דַּהֲוָה יַקִּירָא מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְזָל הַשְׁתָּא; דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: אִי אַרְעָא יָהֲבַתְּ לִי – הַב לִי כְּזוּלָא דְּהַשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **There,** the *baraita* is not discussing a case where the buyer wishes to acquire the surplus land, but rather with a case **where** the land **was initially,** at the time of the sale, **expensive, but now it is cheap,** and the seller wants the buyer to pay him for the surplus land according to the initial higher rate. In this case, **we say to** the seller, in the name of the buyer: **If you** wish to **give me land** and receive money, **give me** the land **according to the current, cheaper rate.** This is what the *baraita* is referring to when it states that the seller is compelled to sell.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן לוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ כְּשַׁעַר שֶׁלָּקַח מִמֶּנּוּ! הָתָם, כְּגוֹן דַּהֲוָה זוּלָא מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְיָקְרָא לַהּ הַשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **When he gives him** money in payment for the surplus land, **he gives** it **to him according to the rate at which he had bought** the rest of the land **from him?** The Gemara replies: **There** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the land **was initially cheap but now it is expensive.** If, in such a case, the seller wants the buyer to pay him for the surplus land, he is compelled to sell it to him according to the cheap rate from the time of the original sale.

The *gemara* resolves the tension by explaining that the *braita* is addressing a specific economic scenario: the land was worth more at the time of the original sale and has since declined in value. In this context, "forcing the seller to sell" does not mean overriding his fundamental prerogative — the seller always retains the choice of whether to demand the land or demand money. Rather, it means that *if* the seller chooses to sell the extra land to the buyer rather than taking it back, he is forced to sell it at the current lower market rate, not at the price prevailing when the original transaction was made.

The logic is that the baseline position is that the buyer should return the extra land, since he should never have had it. The extra land becomes the subject of a new transaction only if the seller elects to sell it. Since that transaction takes place now, it is governed by current prices. The seller cannot insist on selling at the original rate merely because that was the context of the initial sale.

The *gemara* then cites a further *braita* which states that when the seller does convey the extra land, it is priced at the rate at which the buyer originally acquired. The *gemara* explains this as the converse situation: the land was cheap at the time of the original sale and has since appreciated. Now the buyer, who must purchase the extra sliver, can legitimately argue that his entire involvement with this land was premised on the lower price, and he cannot be forced to pay today's inflated rate for the overage. In both scenarios — whether land has declined or appreciated — the buyer is entitled to the benefit of the lower price: the original price if land has gone up, and the current price if land has gone down. The Mishnah's principle that the seller holds the prerogative is fully preserved; what the *braita* governs is the price at which any forced sale occurs.

## Prerogative Limits

### Rav Huna: Absolute Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאִם שִׁיֵּיר בַּשָּׂדֶה בֵּית תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין שֶׁאָמְרוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּבִקְעָה גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **As, if the surplus in the field was an area required for the sowing of nine *kav*** of seed, and in a garden an area required for the sowing of a half-*kav* of seed, or, according to the Rabbi Akiva, an area required for the sowing of a quarter-*kav* of seed, the buyer can return the surplus land to the seller, and the seller cannot demand payment in money. **Rav Huna says:** The *halakha* **that was stated** in the mishna, that a surplus in the size of an area required for the sowing of **nine *kav*** of seed can be returned to the seller, applies **even in a large valley** which measures several *kor*. If the surplus is a significant plot of land equal in size to an area required for the sewing of nine *kav* of seed, the buyer can return it to the seller, even if it is less than one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a*, i.e., less than one twenty-fourth the size of the field that was sold.

With the basic framework established, the *gemara* turns to a dispute about the precise point at which the seller loses his prerogative — that is, the threshold at which the excess land is considered independently usable and must be returned rather than monetized. The Mishnah states that for a *sadeh* — a wheat field — this threshold is nine *kav*, and for a *ginah* — a vegetable garden — it is a half *kav*.

Rav Huna holds that these figures represent absolute amounts, not proportional ones. Once the excess reaches nine *kav* in absolute terms, the seller loses his prerogative, regardless of the total size of the field being sold. Even if one is selling an enormous tract — a large *bik'ah gedolah* — the seller cannot argue that because the total acreage is vast, nine *kav* is a proportionally negligible amount that should still fall within his range of discretion. Nine *kav* is nine *kav*: it is an objectively usable plot of land, and the seller can farm it, so he must take it back.

### Rav Nachman: Proportional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: נוֹתֵן שִׁבְעַת קַבִּין וּמֶחֱצָה לְכׇל כּוֹר וָכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman says: He calculates seven and a half *kav* for each and every *kor*,** which is equivalent to one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a*. As long as the surplus does not exceed that ratio, he is not required to return it, even if the surplus is greater than the area required for the sewing of nine *kav*.

Rav Nachman disagrees. He holds that the nine *kav* threshold — and by extension all the thresholds in the Mishnah — applies proportionally, *per kor* of land sold. So if a seller sold two *kor*, the relevant threshold for losing his prerogative doubles to eighteen *kav*. The seller does not lose his prerogative until the per-*kor* equivalent of the excess exceeds nine *kav*. Rav Nachman's formula is *nosei shiva kabin u'mechtza lechol kor ve'kor* — seven and a half *kav* per *kor* as the tolerable overage, and nine *kav* per *kor* as the point at which the prerogative is lost.

This is, frankly, a counterintuitive position. The debate between Rav Huna and Rav Nachman is not merely technical: it reflects a fundamental disagreement about whether the usability standard is absolute or relative. Rav Huna's position has considerable intuitive force — nine *kav* is nine *kav*, usable by any standard. Nevertheless, the *halacha* follows Rav Nachman.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיכָּא מִילְּתָא יַתִּירָא, דְּהָוֵי לְתִשְׁעַת קַבִּין – הָדְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** after this calculation **there is** still **a surplus** in excess of a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*, equal in size to an area required **for** the sowing of **nine *kav*** of seed, the entire surplus must be **returned.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: ״שֶׁאִם שִׁיֵּיר בַּשָּׂדֶה בֵּית תִּשְׁעַת קַבִּין״ – לָאו דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ כּוֹרַיִים? לָא, דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ כּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman:** The mishna teaches **that if the surplus in the field was an area required for the sowing of nine *kav*** of seed, the buyer returns the land to the seller. **Isn't this** the *halakha* even in a case **where he sold him** a field measuring **two *kor*?** This would seem to indicate that even if the surplus does not exceed a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*, as a quarter-*kav* per *se'a* in a field of two *kor* is fifteen *kav*, the sole determining factor is whether or not the surplus is equal in size to an area required for the sowing of nine *kav*. Rav Naḥman rejects this argument: **No,** the case in the mishna is specifically **where he sold him a** field measuring one ***kor*.**

Rava challenges Rav Nachman directly from the language of the Mishnah. The Mishnah states: *im shayer basadeh beit tisha kabin* — if nine *kav* were left over in a field — the seller must return it. Does the Mishnah not seem to be stating an absolute rule, applicable regardless of whether one *kor* or two *kor* was sold? Rava presses: *lav d'zavin lei kora'im?* — is the Mishnah not speaking of a case where two *kor* were sold, yet nine *kav* still triggers the mandatory return?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבַגִּנָּה בֵּית חֲצִי קַב״ – לָאו דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ סָאתַיִם? לָא, דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ סְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava raised a further objection to Rav Naḥman: We learned in the continuation of the mishna that if the surplus **in a garden** was **an area required for the sowing of a half-*kav*** of seed, the buyer returns the land to the seller. **Isn't this** the *halakha* even in a case **where he sold him** a garden measuring **two *se'a*?** Once again, this would seem to indicate that the surplus is returned to the seller, provided that it is equal in size to the minimum measure of a garden, even if the surplus does not exceed one-half of a *kav* per two *se'a*, which is equivalent to one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a*. Rav Naḥman rejects this argument as well: **No,** the case in the mishna is **where he sold him a** garden measuring a ***se'a*,** so that the surplus is proportionately twice as large.

Similarly, the Mishnah states *u'vigenah beit chatzi kav* — in a vegetable garden, a half *kav* triggers mandatory return. Does this not apply even if two *se'ah* of garden were sold, so that the threshold remains a half *kav* in absolute terms rather than doubling?

### Case-Specific Sizes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּכְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, בֵּית רוֹבַע״ – מַאי, לָאו דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ סְאָה? לָא, דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ חֲצִי סְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava raised yet another objection to Rav Naḥman from the next clause in the mishna, which states: **Or, according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** if the surplus in the garden was **an area required for sowing a quarter-*kav* of seed,** the buyer returns the land to the seller. **What, isn't this** the *halakha* even in a case **where he sold him a** garden measuring **a *se'a*?** In that case, the surplus does not exceed one-quarter of a *kav* per *se'a*, and nevertheless the surplus is returned to the seller, provided that it is equal in size to the minimum measure of a garden. Rav Naḥman also rejects this argument: **No,** the case in the mishna is **where he sold him** a garden measuring **a half-*se'a*,** so that the surplus is proportionately twice as large.

The *gemara* deflects each of Rava's challenges in turn. For the first: *lo d'zavin lei kor* — the Mishnah is speaking of the case where exactly one *kor* was sold, and that is why nine *kav* is the threshold. Had two *kor* been sold, the threshold would be eighteen *kav*. For the second: *lo d'zavin lei se'ah* — the Mishnah's half-*kav* threshold for a *ginah* applies to the case where one *se'ah* of garden was sold. Had two *se'ah* been sold, the threshold would be one *kav*.

The *gemara* proceeds similarly with the ruling attributed to Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva holds that a *ginah* is usable even at the size of a *rova kav* — a quarter of a *kav* — so that the seller loses his prerogative when the excess reaches that size. Rava's challenge here parallels the earlier ones: *mai lav d'zavin lei se'ah?* — does this not apply even for a *se'ah*-sized garden sale, meaning the quarter-*kav* is absolute? The *gemara* answers: *lo d'zavin lei chatzi se'ah* — Rabbi Akiva's ruling applies to a sale of half a *se'ah*, scaled accordingly.

This pattern of responses is somewhat strained, and as the lecturer candidly notes, the *gemara* is reading the Mishnah's examples as each applying only to a specific total sale size rather than as absolute thresholds — a reading that serves Rav Nachman's proportional framework but does not emerge naturally from the Mishnah's language.

## Unresolved Cases

### Field-to-Garden Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: שָׂדֶה – וְנַעֲשֵׂית גִּנָּה; גִּנָּה – וְנַעֲשֵׂיתָ שָׂדֶה, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that in the case of a field, the buyer can return the land itself if the surplus was an area required for the sowing of nine *kav* of seed, and in the case of a garden, if the surplus was an area required for the sowing of a half-*kav* of seed. **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** If one sold **a field and it** turned out that the plot was larger than had been stipulated, but before the buyer returned the surplus, the plot **was turned into a garden, or** if it was initially **a garden and it was turned into a field** (see 11a), **what** is the *halakha*? Is the surplus governed by the *halakhot* applying to a field or by those applying to a garden? The Gemara answers: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved, as no answer has been found.

Rav Ashi poses a question left unresolved: *sadeh ve-na'aset ginah, ginah ve-na'aset sadeh mai?* — what if a field became a garden, or a garden became a field, in the interim between the sale and the measurement?

The question arises as follows. Suppose the seller sold what is classified as a *sadeh*, and the excess land was eight *kav* — below the nine-*kav* threshold, so the seller retains his prerogative under the *sadeh* standard. Before the parties got around to formally measuring and resolving the discrepancy, that excess plot underwent a change: a stream began flowing through it, or the buyer planted vegetables, and it became usable as a *ginah*. The threshold for a *ginah* is only a half *kav*, and eight *kav* obviously exceeds that. The buyer can now argue: as a garden, this plot is independently usable at any size, so you no longer have a prerogative — you must take it back.

Does the classification follow the nature of the land at the time of the sale, which would preserve the seller's prerogative? Or does it follow the current state of the land, which would eliminate it? The *gemara* leaves this question standing: *teiku*.

### Contiguous Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אִם הָיָה סָמוּךְ לְשָׂדֵהוּ – אֲפִילּוּ כָּל שֶׁהוּא, מַחֲזִיר לוֹ קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: **If** the field being sold **was adjacent to** another **field** belonging to the seller, **then even** if the surplus was of **a minimal** amount, the buyer can **return the land** itself **to** the seller, and the seller cannot demand payment in money. This is because the seller loses nothing when he receives a small tract of land, as he can cultivate it along with his adjoining field.

A separate ruling, introduced by a *tanna*, addresses the case where the excess land is contiguous to the seller's own remaining property: *im haya samuch lesadehu afilu kol shehu machzir lo karka* — if the extra land is adjacent to the seller's field, even if it is a tiny amount, the buyer always returns the land and the seller can never claim a money payment instead.

The rationale is transparent. The entire basis for the seller's prerogative to demand money rather than land was the uselessness of a small strip to someone who has no adjacent property to integrate it into. But if the excess is contiguous to the seller's own field, it is trivially easy for him to incorporate it — he simply adjusts the boundary line. There is no hardship in taking the land back, and therefore no justification for forcing the buyer to purchase what should never have been included in the sale.

### Physical Obstacles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: בּוֹר, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּפְסִיק? אַמַּת הַמַּיִם, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּפְסִיק? דֶּרֶךְ הָרַבִּים, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּפְסִיק? רִיכְבָּא דְּדִיקְלָא, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּפְסִיק? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises** a set of **dilemmas:** With regard to **a pit** between the surplus in the sold field and the adjoining field belonging to the seller, **what is** the *halakha*: **Should** the pit **be** considered **an interposition** between the two fields? With regard to **a water channel** between the two fields, **what is** the *halakha*: **Should** the water channel **be** considered **an interposition?** With regard to **the public thoroughfare, what is** the *halakha*: **Should** the public thoroughfare **be** considered **an interposition?** With regard to **a row of palm trees, what is** the *halakha*: **Should** a row of palm trees **be** considered **an interposition?** The Gemara states: All these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi then raises a series of related questions, all *teiku*, about what counts as "contiguous" for this purpose. If a *bor* — a well — separates the seller's remaining field from the excess strip, does the well constitute enough of a physical break to defeat the *samuch lesadehu* principle, restoring the seller's prerogative? What about *ammas hamayim* — an irrigation canal running between the plots? Or *derech harabbim* — a public path cutting across? Or *richpa de-dikla* — a copse of palm trees standing in between?

In each of these cases, one could argue that the physical feature makes it impractical to simply fold the excess into the seller's adjacent field, thereby reviving the original rationale for giving the seller a choice. On the other hand, one could argue that the adjacency is still effectively there and the physical feature does not eliminate it. All of these remain unresolved: *teiku*.

## Return of Excess

### Entire Amount Returned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֶת הָרוֹבַע בִּלְבַד מַחֲזִיר לוֹ, אֶלָּא כָּל הַמּוֹתָר. כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא? תָּאנֵי רָבִין בַּר רַב נַחְמָן: לֹא אֶת הַמּוֹתָר בִּלְבַד מַחֲזִיר לוֹ, אֶלָּא אֶת כָּל הָרְבָעִין כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the surplus is greater than a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*, **it is not only the quarter-***kav* **that** the buyer **returns; rather,** he returns **all of the surplus.** Since he is already required to make a refund, the refund must be made in the precise amount. The Gemara raises a question: **Isn't it the opposite** [***kelappei layya***]? The buyer is required to return the surplus even when the quarters of a *kav* remain in his possession. **Ravin bar Rav Naḥman taught** the mishna as follows: **Not only must** the buyer **return the extra** land that is beyond the limit of a quarter*-kav* area per *beit se'a*, **but he must** also **return to him every one of the** extra **quarter-***kav* areas of land that he received beyond the stated area of a *beit kor*. When he is required to return the surplus, he returns not only the surplus, but also all the quarter-*kav* areas over and above what had originally been stipulated to be included in the sale.

The Mishnah's language at this point states: *velo et harova bilvad machzir lo ela et kol hamotar* — and not only the *rova* does he return, but the entire excess. The *gemara* flags this as stated backwards. If the Mishnah means to say that once the discrepancy crosses the tolerance threshold, the entire amount — not merely the excess over the threshold — is returned, it should say: not only the extra beyond the *rova*, but all of it, including the *rova* itself. Saying "not only the *rova*" implies the *rova* is the baseline and something additional beyond it is also returned — which reverses the actual rule.

Ravin bar Rav Nachman therefore emends the reading: *ro'eh hamotar bilvad machzir lo, ela et kol hareva'im kulam* — he does not return only the excess beyond the tolerance; he returns the full amount including that which falls within the tolerance. Once the threshold is crossed, the entire discrepancy is corrected, not merely the marginal amount above the *rova*.

## Contradictory Language

### Second Clause Wins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ, הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״ – בִּטֵּל ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״ ״מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל״. ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר, מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל״ –
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the seller says to the buyer: **I am selling you** a plot of land of a certain size **measured** precisely **with a rope more or less,** thereby attaching to the sale two contradictory stipulations; in this case, the words: **More or less, nullify** the words: **Measured** precisely **with a rope.** Accordingly, if the surplus did not exceed a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*, the sale is valid as is. Similarly, if the seller says to the buyer: I am selling you a plot of land of a certain size **more or less measured** precisely **with a rope,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּטֵּל ״מִדָּה בְּחֶבֶל״ ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵר״; דִּבְרֵי בֶּן נַנָּס.
>
> **Translation:** the words: **Measured** precisely **with a rope, nullify** the words: **More or less,** since the principle is that in all cases, one should attend to the final expression; this is **the statement of ben Nanas.**

The daf concludes with the beginning of a new Mishnah that addresses a scenario not yet covered: what happens when the seller uses contradictory language in a single statement?

The first Mishnah established three cases: *mida bechevel* (precise), *hen chaser hen yeter* (approximate), and *stam* (unmodified, treated like approximate). The new Mishnah takes up what happens when both formulations appear together. If the seller said *mida bechevel ani mocher lach, hen chaser hen yeter* — I am selling you this precisely, more or less — the two clauses directly contradict each other. Likewise if he said *hen chaser hen yeter ani mocher lach, mida bechevel* — more or less, precisely.

Ben Nanas rules that in both cases, the second clause controls: *batel hen chaser hen yeter, mida bechevel* — when "more or less" comes second, it nullifies the "precisely" that preceded it, and the sale is governed as *hen chaser hen yeter*. Conversely, when *mida bechevel* comes second, it nullifies the *hen chaser hen yeter*, and the sale is treated as precise. The principle is that the second, later statement reflects the speaker's final intent, overriding what came before.

The Mishnah presents this as the view of Ben Nanas alone — *divrei Ben Nanas* — without stating a dissenting opinion within the Mishnah itself, though the *gemara* will introduce one. The broader question of how contradictory contractual language is resolved, and whether Ben Nanas's principle generalizes beyond land sales, will be addressed in the discussion that follows.
