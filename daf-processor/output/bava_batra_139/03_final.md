# Bava Batra 139 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Guf & Peirot Transfer

### Father's Harvested vs. U…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאָב תּוֹלֵשׁ וּמַאֲכִיל לְכׇל מִי שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, וּמַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ תָּלוּשׁ – הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁל יוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In continuation of the case discussed in the previous mishna of a father who wrote a document granting his property to his son but reserved the rights to the produce during his lifetime, the mishna states that **the father may detach** produce from the land **and feed** the produce **to whomever he wishes, and what he left detached** at the time of his death **belongs to** all the father's **heirs,** not only to this son.

The daf opens with a *mishnah* at the very bottom of 138b concerning a father who wishes to transfer property to his son while retaining personal benefit from it during his lifetime. The mechanism employed is *guf me-hayom u-peirot le-achar mita*: the son receives title (*guf*) immediately — thereby avoiding the problem of a gift that takes effect only after death — but the father retains the right to all produce (*peirot*) from the field until he dies.

The *mishnah* specifies: *ha-av tolesh u-ma'achil le-chol mi she-yirtzeh* — as long as the father is alive, he may harvest the fruit and distribute it to whomever he pleases; the son has no claim on it. Furthermore, *u-ma she-hiniach talush* — if the father harvested fruit and then died, that already-harvested fruit belongs to *harei hu shel yorshim*, all the heirs collectively, not to this particular son who received the field. The *gemara* draws the implication sharply: *talush* — yes, the father retains what he harvested; *mechubar* — no, fruit still attached to the tree at the time of the father's death passes specifically to this son, not to the general heirs.

This is not entirely intuitive. Since the father holds the rights to *peirot*, one might have expected that even unharvested fruit growing on the tree belongs to him, and only upon his death would it pass — together with everything else — to the general heirs. Yet the *mishnah* implies otherwise.

### Ulla: Son vs. Third Party

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא: שָׁמִין אֶת הַמְחוּבָּרִין לַלּוֹקֵחַ! אָמַר עוּלָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בִּבְנוֹ, כָּאן בְּאַחֵר; הוֹאִיל וְדַעְתּוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קְרוֹבָה אֵצֶל בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ketubot* 8:5) that in a similar case, the court **appraises the** produce still **connected** to the ground **for the purchaser** of the land, who must pay the seller's heirs for it? Accordingly, the connected produce also belongs to the heirs. **Ulla said** that this is **not difficult; here,** in the mishna, the *halakha* is stated **with regard to his son,** who receives the connected produce after the father's death. **There,** in the *baraita*, the *halakha* is stated **with regard to another** person who purchased the land from the son. **Since a person's disposition is favorable toward his son,** the father certainly intended to bequeath the connected produce to his son together with the property itself. When the son sold it to another person, there was no such intention.

A *baraita* at the top of 138a appears to contradict this ruling. The *baraita* addresses a case where the son, while the father is still alive, sells his interest to a third party, and then the father dies. In that scenario, *shamin et ha-mechubarim la-lokeach*: one assesses the value of the fruit still on the tree, and this purchaser — who now steps into the son's position with respect to both title and fruit rights — must pay the heirs for that fruit. This implies that the unharvested fruit belongs to the father (and thus passes to all heirs), in direct tension with our *mishnah*.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction through Ulla: *lo kashya kan be-veno kan be-acher* — there is no difficulty. The two cases differ based on whether the recipient is the son or a stranger. *Da'ato shel adam kerova etzel beno* — a father's disposition (*da'at*) is naturally inclined toward his son.

### Da'ato Kerova: Intent Ma…

Although technically the father retains the fruit rights and the unharvested fruit logically belongs to him, when dealing with his own son, the father's intent is that the son should benefit even from fruit still on the tree. Therefore, in the case of our *mishnah* — where the recipient is the son — the father's rights extend only to fruit he has actually harvested. Unharvested fruit at the time of death passes to the son. When, however, the son sold his interest to a third party, no such paternal closeness (*da'ato kerova*) operates, and the *baraita* applies: the unharvested fruit belongs to the father and passes to all the heirs.

## Sons: Clothes vs. Food

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים – אֵין הַגְּדוֹלִים מִתְפַּרְנְסִין עַל יְדֵי הַקְּטַנִּים, וְלֹא הַקְּטַנִּים נִזּוֹנִין עַל הַגְּדוֹלִים, אֶלָּא חוֹלְקִין בְּשָׁוֶה. נָשְׂאוּ הַגְּדוֹלִים – יִשׂאוּ הַקְּטַנִּים. וְאִם אָמְרוּ קְטַנִּים: הֲרֵי אָנוּ נוֹשְׂאִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁנְּשָׂאתֶם אַתֶּם – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לָהֶם; אֶלָּא מַה שֶׁנָּתַן לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם, נָתַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a person died and **left adult and minor sons, the adults are not provided for by** using funds of **the minors, and the minors are not sustained,** i.e., they do not receive food, **by** using funds **of the adults. Rather, they receive a share** of the inheritance **equally,** and each son sees to his needs from his own share. If **the adults married, the minors marry,** as the Gemara will explain. **But if the minors say: We are marrying in the same manner that you** adults **married** during our father's lifetime, the court **does not listen to them. Rather, whatever their father gave** the adults in his lifetime **he gave** them, and the minors do not have the right to receive more than their share of the inheritance.

The next *mishnah*, at the top of 139a, addresses an entirely different situation: a father dies leaving behind both adult sons (*banim gedolim*) and minor sons (*banim ketanim*). The estate is jointly owned by all of them in equal shares, and division has not yet taken place. The practical question is whether ongoing expenses — clothing and food — can simply be drawn from the estate as a whole, or whether each heir must bear his own costs from his individual portion once division occurs.

The problem is that these two categories of expense fall unevenly across age groups. Minor children generally incur higher food costs relative to their share, while adult sons incur substantially higher clothing costs. If clothing expenses were paid equally from the undivided estate, the younger heirs would effectively subsidize the older sons' wardrobes; if food expenses were paid equally from the estate, the older heirs would be unfairly burdened with the younger children's higher food bills.

The *mishnah* therefore rules: *ein ha-gedolim mitparnasim al yedei ha-ketanim* — the older sons cannot have their clothing costs (*mitparnes* referring here specifically to clothing) paid from the estate at the expense of the younger children — *ve-lo ha-ketanim nizonim al ha-gedolim* — and the younger children cannot have their food costs (*nizonim*) paid from the estate at the expense of the older sons. *Ella cholkim be-shava* — instead, the estate is divided equally and each heir bears his own costs from his portion.

The *mishnah* then turns to wedding expenses: *nasu gedolim yisu ketanim* — if the older sons were married after the father's death and wedding costs were drawn from the estate, then when the younger sons come to marry, they are likewise entitled to draw equivalent wedding costs from the estate. However: *ve-im amru ketanim harei anu nosim ke-derech she-nasatem atem, ein shomin lahem* — if the younger sons say, "we wish to marry just as you did," they are not heeded.

This last clause appears contradictory, and the *gemara* will resolve it. The *mishnah* is addressing a different scenario: the older sons were married while the father was still alive, and the father personally paid for their weddings as a gift. After the father died, the younger sons demand that equivalent amounts be drawn from the estate. The *mishnah* rules they cannot: *ella mah she-natan lahem avihem natan* — what the father gave the older sons was a personal gift from the father, and the estate — now equally owned by all — cannot be tapped to replicate it.

### Rava: Eldest Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי גְּדוֹל אֲחֵי דִּלְבַשׁ וְאִיכַּסִּי מִבֵּיתָא – מַאי דַּעֲבַד עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says:** With regard to **this eldest of the brothers who wears** garments **and covers himself from** the common property of **the house, what he did is done,** and the other brothers cannot subtract from his share of the inheritance as a result.

The *gemara* records a ruling by Rava that qualifies the *mishnah*'s general prohibition: *hai gadol achi de-lavash ve-ichasi mi-beisa, mai de-avid avid* — if the eldest brother who represents the household — the one who appears in court, conducts business on behalf of the estate, and represents the family in public — uses estate funds for his clothing, what he has done stands. His clothing costs are legitimately for the benefit of the entire estate, since his public role requires a certain appearance, and may therefore be borne by the estate rather than his personal share.

However, Rava's formulation — *mai de-avid avid*, "what he did stands" — suggests that even this is only *be-dieved* acceptable. The potential for abuse is obvious: one might claim an extravagant wardrobe is necessary to represent the family's honor. Accordingly, Rava's ruling is not a blanket license but a post-facto validation, and *beis din* may scrutinize whether the expenditure genuinely served the estate's interests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֵין הַגְּדוֹלִים מִתְפַּרְנְסִין עַל הַקְּטַנִּים! מַתְנִיתִין – בִּשְׁרָכָא. שְׁרָכָא – פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: נִיחָא לְהוּ דְּלָא נִינַּוַּול; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **the adults are not provided for by** using funds **of the minors?** The Gemara answers: **The mishna** is referring **to an unemployed person [*bisherakha*],** whereas Rava was referring to the brother who handles the finances of the entire family. Therefore it is in the interest of all the brothers for him to wear appropriate attire. The Gemara asks: If the mishna is referring to an **unemployed person,** isn't it **obvious** that he may not take from the common inheritance of all the brothers? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that it is **preferable for them** that he dress appropriately so **that he will not be repulsive,** thereby dishonoring the family name, the mishna **teaches us** that he does not have the right to take money for clothing from the common inheritance, and if he does so, the brothers can subtract from his share of the inheritance as a result.

The *gemara* then raises an objection: but our *mishnah* explicitly prohibits the older sons from having their clothing paid from the estate! Rava answers: *masnisin be-sircha* — the *mishnah* addresses a ne'er-do-well, someone who is simply lounging about and not representing anyone. The *gemara* presses further: *sircha peshita* — isn't that obvious? Of course such a person cannot claim estate funds for his wardrobe. The *gemara* responds: one might have thought that even then it is to the family's benefit that older sons maintain their appearance, and the ruling is therefore necessary to clarify that it is not. It is worth noting the slightly unusual framing: the *gemara* appears to treat Rava's case as the norm and the *mishnah* as the exception, when intuitively it is the opposite — the default is that clothing costs come from one's own share, and Rava's case of the family representative is the narrow exception.

### Wedding Costs Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלִים לְאַחַר מִיתַת אֲבִיהֶן – יִשְּׂאוּ קְטַנִּים לְאַחַר מִיתַת אֲבִיהֶן. אֲבָל נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלִים בְּחַיֵּי אֲבִיהֶן, וְאָמְרוּ קְטַנִּים לְאַחַר מִיתַת אֲבִיהֶן: ״הֲרֵי אָנוּ נוֹשְׂאִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁנְּשָׂאתֶם אַתֶּם״ – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לָהֶן, אֶלָּא מָה שֶׁנָּתַן לָהֶן אֲבִיהֶן – נָתַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **this** is what **it is saying:** If **the adults married after their father's death** and used the common inheritance to pay for their weddings, **the minors** that **marry after their father's death** may also pay for their weddings from the common inheritance. **But** if **the adults married during their father's lifetime, and the minors said after their father's death: We will marry in the same manner that you married,** the court **does not listen to them. Rather, whatever** sum **their father gave** the adults to pay for their weddings in his lifetime **he gave** them, and the minors do not have the right to demand that same sum from the inheritance.

The *gemara* (Rav Yehuda) clarifies the *mishnah*'s apparently contradictory lines about wedding expenses. *Nasu gedolim achar misat avihem* — if the older sons married after the father's death and took wedding costs from the estate, *yisu ketanim achar misat avihem* — the younger sons may do the same. *Aval nasu gedolim be-chayei avihem* — but if the older sons married during the father's lifetime and the father paid, then when the younger sons seek to claim equivalent sums from the estate after the father's death, *ein shomin lahem* — they are not heeded. What the father gave was the father's gift; it cannot be replicated from what is now a jointly-owned estate.

## Daughters: Same Rules

### Parallel Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִנִּיחַ בָּנוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת וּקְטַנּוֹת – אֵין הַגְּדוֹלוֹת מִתְפַּרְנְסוֹת עַל יְדֵי הַקְּטַנּוֹת, וְלֹא הַקְּטַנּוֹת נִזּוֹנוֹת עַל הַגְּדוֹלוֹת, אֶלָּא חוֹלְקוֹת בְּשָׁוֶה. נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלוֹת – יִשְּׂאוּ קְטַנּוֹת. וְאִם אָמְרוּ קְטַנּוֹת: הֲרֵי אָנוּ נוֹשְׂאוֹת כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁנְּשָׂאתֶם אַתֶּם – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if the father **left adult and minor daughters** but no sons, in which case his daughters inherit the estate, **the adults are not provided for by** using funds **of the minors, and the minors are not sustained by** using funds **of the adults. Rather, they receive a share** of the inheritance **equally,** and each daughter sees to her needs from her share. If **the adult** daughters **married, the minor** daughters **marry,** as the Gemara will explain. **But if the minors say: We are marrying in the same manner that you** adults **married** during our father's lifetime, the court **does not listen to them.**

The *mishnah* then addresses *hiniach banot gedolot u-ketanot* — a father who died leaving older and younger daughters. The identical rules apply: older daughters may not take clothing costs from the estate at the younger daughters' expense; younger daughters may not take food costs from the estate at the older daughters' expense; the estate is divided equally and each daughter bears her own costs. The wedding expense rules parallel those of the sons as well.

### Daughters Supported

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה חוֹמֶר בַּבָּנוֹת מִבַּבָּנִים – שֶׁהַבָּנוֹת נִזּוֹנוֹת עַל הַבָּנִים, וְאֵין נִזּוֹנוֹת עַל הַבָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **This** following *halakha* **is a stringency with regard to daughters'** inheritance **vis-à-vis sons'** inheritance: The *halakha* is **that the daughters are sustained by** using funds **of the sons,** as stipulated in their mother's marriage contract, **but they are not sustained by** using funds **of the** other **daughters.**

The reason the *mishnah* repeats these rules for daughters rather than simply cross-referencing the sons is to introduce the concluding distinction: *ze chomer be-banim mi-be-banot* — here is one way in which the case of sons is more stringent than that of daughters. *She-ha-banot nizonot al ha-banim ve-ein nizonot al ha-banot* — when an estate is inherited by sons, daughters have a right to be supported from it (a condition of the *ketubbah* written by the father to the mother, extending maintenance rights to daughters until they marry or reach maturity). But when daughters are the sole heirs and there are no sons, younger daughters cannot claim their food support from the estate against the older daughters — all divide equally. This heightens the stringency for minor daughters in a daughters-only estate relative to their position in a mixed estate where sons inherit.

## Ba'al: Lokeach or Yoresh?

### Avuha bar Geniva Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ בַּר גְּנִיבָא לְרָבָא, יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ: לָוְותָה וְאָכְלָה, וְעָמְדָה וְנִשֵּׂאת – בַּעַל לוֹקֵחַ הָוֵי, אוֹ יוֹרֵשׁ הָוֵי? לוֹקֵחַ הָוֵי – וּמִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת; אוֹ דִּלְמָא יוֹרֵשׁ הָוֵי – וּמִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה גּוֹבֶה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the father **left adult and minor daughters,** and the adults married, the minors marry. **Avuh bar Geneiva sent** a question **to Rava: Our teacher, instruct us:** If a woman **borrowed** money **and consumed** it, **and** before repaying the debt **she arose and married,** bringing her property into the marriage, what is the *halakha*? **Is the husband** considered **a purchaser** of her property **or is** he considered **an heir** to her property? The halakhic ramification of this dilemma is as follows: **Is he** considered **a purchaser, and** the creditor therefore cannot collect his wife's premarital debt from him, as **one does not collect from the purchasers a loan by oral** agreement? **Or perhaps he is** considered her **heir, and one collects from the heirs a loan by oral** agreement.

Avuha bar Geniva sent a question to Rava: *lavta ve-achla ve-amda ve-nisat, ba'al lokeach havei o yoresh havei?* — a woman borrowed money, incurred debt, then married, bringing property into the marriage. The husband now holds rights to this property as *nichsei milog*: she retains title while he enjoys the usufruct (*peirot*). Her creditors wish to exercise their liens against this property now that it is in the husband's hands. The question is: how do we classify the husband's relationship to *nichsei milog* — is he like a *lokeach* (purchaser) or a *yoresh* (heir)?

The practical stakes are clear. A creditor holding only a *milveh al peh* (oral loan, without a formal *shtar*) cannot exercise liens against a purchaser — we protect innocent buyers who had no notice of the debt. But such a creditor *can* collect from heirs, even without a *shtar*, since an heir received the property without paying for it and cannot claim to be an innocent purchaser. If the husband is treated as a *lokeach*, the wife's pre-marital creditors cannot touch the property. If he is treated as a *yoresh*, they can.

One might note that the question makes more natural sense after the wife's death, when the husband fully inherits — at which point you could ask whether his prior usufruct right transforms his inheritance into something resembling a purchase. While the wife is alive, the husband does not yet hold title, which makes the framing somewhat unusual, as *Tosafot* addresses. But the *gemara* proceeds with the question as posed.

### Raba: Daughters' Marriag…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלוֹת – יִשְּׂאוּ קְטַנּוֹת. מַאי, לָאו נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלוֹת לְבַעַל – יִשְּׂאוּ קְטַנּוֹת מִבַּעַל?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: We learned** the answer to this question in the mishna: If **the adult** daughters **married, the minor** daughters **marry. What, is it not** teaching that if **the adult** daughters **married,** each one of them transferring money from the common inheritance **to** her **husband** to cover the wedding expenses, **the minor** daughters also **marry,** paying for the wedding **from** the money that each elder sister transferred to her **husband?** Clearly, although the money the elder daughters took from the inheritance for their weddings has the status of a loan by oral agreement, their husbands are liable to return it. This proves that the husbands are considered heirs.

Rava replies: *tenina* — the answer is already contained in our *mishnah*. He invokes the rule about daughters' wedding expenses: if the older daughters were married after the father's death using estate funds, the younger daughters may claim equal wedding costs from the estate. Now consider what this actually means. Suppose four daughters — call them Sarah, Rivka, Rachel, and Leah — share the estate. Sarah and Rivka marry, drawing $20,000 each from the estate. They bring their respective shares into their marriages. When Rachel and Leah come to marry, they too are entitled to $20,000 from the estate — meaning they can recover part of that sum from the property Sarah and Rivka brought into *their* marriages, property now in the hands of Sarah's and Rivka's husbands. This is precisely our question: the younger daughters had a lien-like claim against the estate, the older daughters took estate property into their marriages with their husbands, and now the younger daughters exercise that claim against property in the husbands' hands. The fact that they may do so implies the husband is treated as a *yoresh*, against whom liens are enforceable.

### Gemara Disputes Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא; נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלוֹת לְבַעַל – יִשְּׂאוּ קְטַנּוֹת לְבַעַל. אִינִי?! וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: נָשְׂאוּ גְּדוֹלוֹת לְבַעַל – יִשְּׂאוּ קְטַנּוֹת מִבַּעַל! דִּלְמָא שָׁאנֵי פַּרְנָסָה, דְּאִית לַהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the mishna is teaching that if **the adults married** and each one transferred money **to** her **husband, the minors** also **marry,** and each one may transfer money from the common inheritance **to** her **husband** for the wedding expenses. They cannot collect money from what was already transferred by their sisters to their husbands. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** that if **the adults married** and transferred money **to the husband, the minors marry** and collect money **from the husband** of each elder sister? The Gemara answers: Even according to this interpretation, there is no proof from the mishna with regard to the case of a loan by oral agreement. **Perhaps** the provision of wedding **expenses** for the daughters **is different, as it generates publicity.** Since it is well known that a specific portion of a person's estate is liened to the future wedding expenses of his single daughters, it has the status of a loan with a promissory note. Therefore, the younger daughters can collect from the husbands of the elder daughters even if the status of the husbands is that of purchasers.

The *gemara* pushes back: *lo* — perhaps the *mishnah* is not describing such a complicated scenario at all. Perhaps Sarah and Rivka married without the estate having been divided yet, so their husbands never received any estate property. The wedding costs come from the still-undivided estate itself, and the younger daughters' subsequent claims are likewise drawn from those same undivided funds — with no lien being exercised against any husband. In that reading, the *mishnah* teaches nothing about our question. The *gemara* acknowledges the ambiguity and presses on.

*Rabbi Hiyya* then teaches in a *baraita*: *nisat gedola le-ba'al yasia ketana mi-ba'al* — explicitly, when the younger daughters come to collect their wedding costs, they collect from the husbands of the older daughters. This resolves the textual ambiguity and appears to establish decisively that the husband is a *yoresh* against whom liens may be enforced. But the *gemara* deflects again: *dilma shani parnasa de-it lah kala* — perhaps wedding costs (*parnasa* here referring to the marriage expenses) are publicly known. Everyone is aware that Sarah and Rivka's marriages cost $20,000, and therefore everyone — including the husbands — knows that Rachel and Leah hold a corresponding lien. Since the husbands had constructive notice, we allow the lien to be enforced in this case. But the general rule for a creditor whose lien is not equally well-known might be different, and one cannot generalize from the wedding case to ordinary *milveh al peh* debt collection.

### Ravin: Widow Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא, לָאו הַיְינוּ דִּשְׁלַח רָבִין בְּאִגַּרְתֵּיהּ: מִי שֶׁמֵּת, וְהִנִּיחַ אַלְמָנָה וּבַת – אַלְמְנָתוֹ נִזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסָיו. נִשֵּׂאת הַבַּת – אַלְמְנָתוֹ נִזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסָיו. מֵתָה הַבַּת – אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בֶּן אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי [בֶּן חֲנִינָא]: עַל יָדִי הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְאָמְרוּ: אַלְמְנָתוֹ נִזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסָיו. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא יוֹרֵשׁ הָוֵי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אַלְמְנָתוֹ נִזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסָיו; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לוֹקֵחַ הָוֵי, אַמַּאי נִזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסָיו?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava: Is this not** identical to the *halakha* **that Ravin sent in his letter** from Eretz Yisrael? That *halakha* states that with regard to **one who died and left a widow and a daughter, his widow is sustained from his property.** If **the daughter** then **married, the widow is** still **sustained from his property,** although it is now in the possession of the daughter's husband, as she had inherited the property from her father. With regard to a case where **the daughter** then **died** and her husband inherited from her, **Rav Yehuda, son of the sister of Rabbi Yosei ben Ḥanina, says:** This **incident occurred with me, and** the Sages **said** that the late man's **widow is** still **sustained from his property** although it is fully owned by his daughter's husband. Rav Pappa explains: **Granted, if you say** the husband **is** considered **an heir,** it is **due to that** reason that **his** father-in-law's **widow is sustained from his property;** it is still liened to her sustenance. **But if you say** that a husband **is** considered **a purchaser** of his wife's property, **why** is his father-in-law's widow **sustained from his property?** The purchasers of inherited property are not obligated to sustain the widow.

Rav Pappa puts to Rava: is this not precisely what Ravin sent in a letter from Eretz Yisrael? The case Ravin described is: a man died leaving a widow (*almanah*) and a daughter, with no sons. The daughter inherits the estate. The widow (*almanah*) has a right — a condition of the *ketubbah* — to be supported from the estate until she remarries or collects her lump-sum *ketubbah*. *Almanato nizonet mi-nechasav* — the widow draws support from the estate now in the daughter's possession. *Niset ha-bat, almanato nizonet mi-nechasav* — even when the daughter marries and brings the estate property into her own marriage, the widow continues to draw support. *Meta ha-bat* — and if the daughter then dies, transferring the property to the daughter's husband?

Rav Yehuda the nephew of Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina reported: *le-didi hava uvda* — this very case came before me, and the ruling was *almanato nizonet mi-nechasav* — the widow continues to be supported even from the property now in the hands of the son-in-law. The *gemara* draws the inference: *ei amrat bishlama yoresh havei, hainu de-almanato nizonet mi-nechasav* — if the husband is treated as a *yoresh*, it makes perfect sense that the widow's lien persists, just as a creditor can collect from an heir even without a *shtar*. *Ela ei amrat lokeach havei, amai nizonet mi-nechasav?* — but if he is a *lokeach*, why should the widow be able to collect? A *milveh al peh* cannot be enforced against a purchaser. This seems to be a compelling proof that the husband is a *yoresh*.

### Yovel & Terminology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי לָאו דִּשְׁלַח רָבִין, אֲנַן לָא יָדְעִינַן? וְהָא תְּנַן: אֵלּוּ הֵן שֶׁאֵין חוֹזְרִין בַּיּוֹבֵל – הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְהַיּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: If Ravin had not sent** this letter, **would we not have known** that the husband has the status of a purchaser? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna (*Bekhorot* 52b): **These** properties **do not return** to their original owners **in the Jubilee** Year (see Leviticus 25:13–24): The portion of **the firstborn,** i.e., it does not return to the common ownership of the brothers to be shared equally like the rest of the inheritance, but rather remains in the possession of the firstborn; **and one who inherits** the property of **his wife** after her death, i.e., the property is not returned to her family. Evidently, the husband does not have the status of a purchaser, as a purchaser must return the property to its original owner in the Jubilee Year.

Abaye interjects that the proof from Ravin's case was not even necessary. The *mishnah* explicitly lists what does not revert to its original owner at *yovel*: *ha-bechorahshah* — the firstborn's double portion is not considered a purchase and therefore does not revert, and turning to 139b: *ve-ha-yoresh et ishto* — "one who inherits his wife." The *mishnah* itself employs the language of *yerushah* to describe the husband's acquisition of his wife's property upon her death. This is a direct terminological proof that the husband is a *yoresh*.

### Usha Enactment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהַשְׁתָּא דִּשְׁלַח, מִי יָדְעִינַן?! הָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בְּאוּשָׁא הִתְקִינוּ, הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרָה נִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ, וּמֵתָה – הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: And now that** Ravin **sent** the letter, **do we** in fact **know** that the husband has the status of an heir? **Doesn't Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, say: In Usha they instituted** an ordinance that in the case of **a woman who sold her usufruct property,** i.e., property that she alone owns and her husband benefits only from the profits, **in her husband's lifetime and** then **died, the husband repossesses** it **from the purchasers?** Apparently the husband's status is that of a purchaser, as a purchaser can appropriate his purchase from a later purchaser, whereas an heir does not inherit property that the person sold before he died.

At this point, having assembled substantial evidence that the husband is a *yoresh*, Rava complicates the picture. Rabbi Yossi berabi Chanina taught: *be-Usha hitkinu* — at Usha they enacted the following: if a woman sells her *nichsei milog* during her husband's lifetime — she holds title and can technically sell it — and then she dies before her husband, *ha-ba'al motzi mi-yad ha-lakochot* — the husband may reclaim the property from the buyers.

Why is this significant? If the husband were purely a *yoresh*, he could only inherit what remained in the wife's hands at her death. Since she sold the property before dying, he would have nothing to claim — just as an heir cannot recover assets a decedent sold during his lifetime. The only way the husband can reclaim property the wife sold is if his relationship to it is that of a *lokeach* — someone who "purchased" his interest when he married, and whose claim therefore antedates and can override a later sale. The *takana* of Usha thus seems to treat him as a *lokeach*, in tension with all the evidence pointing to *yoresh*.

### Rav Ashi: Context Determ…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בַּעַל – שַׁוְּיוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּיוֹרֵשׁ, וְשַׁוְּיוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּלוֹקֵחַ, וְהֵיכָא דְּטָבָא לֵיהּ עֲבַדוּ לֵיהּ; גַּבֵּי יוֹבֵל – שַׁוְּיוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּיוֹרֵשׁ, מִשּׁוּם פְּסֵידָא דִידֵיהּ. גַּבֵּי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – שַׁוְּיוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּלוֹקֵחַ, מִשּׁוּם פְּסֵידָא דִידֵיהּ. גַּבֵּי דְּרָבִין – מִשּׁוּם פְּסֵידָא דְּאַלְמָנָה, שַׁוְּיוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּיוֹרֵשׁ. וְהָא גַּבֵּי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לְלָקוֹחוֹת, וְשַׁוְּיוּהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּלוֹקֵחַ! הָתָם, אִינְהוּ אַפְסִידוּ אַנַּפְשַׁיְיהוּ – כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בַּעַל, לָא אִיבְּעִי לְהוּ לְמִיזְבַּן מֵאִיתְּתָא דְּיָתְבָא תּוּתֵי גַּבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi says** that **the Sages equated the husband with an heir, and the Sages equated him with a purchaser. And they deemed him** as **whichever is better for him,** based on the circumstances of the case. **With regard to** the **Jubilee** Year, **the Sages equated him with an heir, due to his** potential **loss** were he to be equated with a purchaser, as he would be obligated to return the property to his wife's heirs. **With regard to** the case **of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** where the wife sold the property, **the Sages equated him with a purchaser, due to his** potential **loss** were he to be equated with an heir. **With regard to Ravin's** *halakha*, concerning the sustenance of the husband's father-in-law's widow, **due to the** potential **loss of the widow, the Sages equated him with an heir.** The Gemara asks: **But with regard to** the case **of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, there is a loss to the purchasers,** from whom the husband appropriates the property, **and** nevertheless, **the Sages equated him with a purchaser.** The Gemara answers: **There,** the purchasers **caused their own loss. Since** they knew **that there is a husband, they should not have purchased** property **from a wife living under** the authority of **her husband,** removing it from the husband and his heirs.

Rav Ashi resolves the apparent contradiction by rejecting the premise that there is one uniform answer: *ba'al shaviyuh rabanan ke-yoresh u-shaviyuh rabanan ke-lokeiach* — the Sages treated the husband as a *yoresh* in some contexts and as a *lokeach* in others. The governing principle: *ve-heicha de-tava lei avdu lei* — they applied whichever classification served to prevent undue loss.

Working through the cases: regarding *yovel*, *shaviyuh rabanan ke-yoresh mishum pesida didei* — they classified him as a *yoresh* for his benefit, so that property he received through his wife's estate would not revert at *yovel*, sparing him a highly disruptive loss. Regarding the *takana* of Usha (*gabei de-Rabbi Yosei berabi Chanina*), *shaviyuh rabanan ke-lokeiach mishum pesida didei* — they classified him as a *lokeach* for his benefit, enabling him to reclaim property his wife sold out from under him if she predeceased him. Regarding the case of Ravin — the widow drawing support even after the property passed through the daughter to the son-in-law — *shaviyuh rabanan ke-yoresh mishum pesidat almanah* — they classified him as a *yoresh*, this time for the benefit of the *almanah*, ensuring her support rights remain enforceable and she suffers no loss.

As for the buyers who lose out when the husband reclaims property under the Usha enactment — the *gemara* says *hatam inhu afseidu anafshaihu*: they assumed the risk themselves. *Keivan de-ika ba'al lo iba'i lei le-mizban mi-iteta* — knowing that she was married, they should have understood that if she died before her husband, he would have a claim to the property. They purchased with eyes open and did not pay full price. Their loss is self-inflicted.

The *Rashbam* concludes from this analysis that in the original case posed by Avuha bar Geniva — a wife's pre-marital creditors seeking to enforce their liens against property she brought into the marriage — the husband is treated as a *yoresh* and the creditors may collect, paralleling the widow's right to continued support. The true exception to the *yoresh* classification, motivated by protecting the husband's interests, is the Usha enactment alone.

## Daughters' Support Thres…

### Large Estate Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁמֵּת וְהִנִּיחַ בָּנִים וּבָנוֹת; בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַנְּכָסִים מְרוּבִּין – הַבָּנִים יִירְשׁוּ, וְהַבָּנוֹת יִזּוֹנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who died and left** behind both **sons and daughters, when the estate is large the sons inherit** the estate **and the daughters are provided with sustenance** from it according to the stipulations of the deceased's marriage contract with their mother.

With the conclusion of *Yesh Nochalin*, the *gemara* opens the next *perek*, whose central subject is the support of daughters from an estate that sons inherit. The *mishnah* states: *mi she-met ve-hiniach banim u-vanot* — someone died leaving sons and daughters. *Bi-zman she-nechasim merubim, ha-banim yirshu ve-ha-banot yizonu* — when the estate is large (*nechasim merubim*), the sons inherit and the daughters receive maintenance (*mezonot*) from the estate. This reflects a standard condition of the *ketubbah*: a husband undertakes that upon his death, his daughters will be maintained from his estate until they marry or reach *bagrut* (maturity).

### Small Estate Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְכָסִים מוּעָטִין – הַבָּנוֹת יִזּוֹנוּ, וְהַבָּנִים יִשְׁאֲלוּ עַל הַפְּתָחִים. אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁאֲנִי זָכָר, הִפְסַדְתִּי?! אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי אַדְמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a small estate,** which is insufficient to provide for both the sons and the daughters, **the daughters are provided with sustenance. And** if **the sons,** who receive in this case neither inheritance nor sustenance, have no other means with which to support themselves, **they** go and **request** charity **at the doors. Admon says,** rhetorically: **I lost out** just **because I am male?** Rather, he holds that the sons also receive sustenance. **Rabban Gamliel said: I see** as correct **the statement of Admon.**

*Nechasim muatim* — when the estate is small, the daughters receive their maintenance and the sons must go begging. Admon expresses outrage: just because I am male I am left with nothing? Rabban Gamliel acknowledges the force of Admon's argument, but the normative ruling follows neither of them in any practically operative sense; the *halacha* remains that when the estate suffices only for the daughters' maintenance, the sons receive nothing.

The *mishnah* will be explained to mean not that the girls receive more than their maintenance — they do not — but rather that an estate below the threshold is conceptually under the daughters' control as a fund designated for support, not under the sons' control as inherited property.

### Rav Yehuda Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכַמָּה מְרוּבִּין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּזּוֹנוּ מֵהֶן אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. כִּי אַמְרִיתָא קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בַּר רַבִּי, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּזּוֹנוּ מֵהֶן אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּבְגְּרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And how large** must the estate be in order for the sons to inherit it? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** Large **enough to provide sustenance from it for** both **these,** the sons, **and those,** the daughters, for **twelve months.** Rav Yehuda continues: **When I said this** ruling **before Shmuel, he said: That is the statement of Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **But the Rabbis say:** The estate must be large **enough to provide sustenance from it for** both **these and those until** the time **that** the daughters **reach their majority,** at age twelve and six months, as indicated in the marriage contract.

*Ve-chama merubim* — how much constitutes a "large" estate? Rav Yehuda citing Rav defines it as: *kedei she-yizonu mi-hen elu va-elu shenayim asar chodesh* — enough to support boys and girls alike for twelve months. Even if the girls are years away from reaching *bagrut*, once the estate suffices to cover everyone's expenses for a year, it is considered *nechasim merubim* and passes under the sons' control, with the daughters retaining a lien for their support until maturity.

When Rav Yehuda reported this ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel responded: *zo divrei Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi* — that may be Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi's position. *Aval chakhamim omrim kedei she-yizonu mi-hen elu va-elu ad she-yivgaru* — but the Sages hold that the estate must be sufficient to support everyone — boys and girls — until all the girls reach maturity. Only then is it truly *nechasim merubim*. Anything short of that threshold, even if the estate is quite substantial in absolute terms, is *nechasim muatim*, and the money is treated as a fund designated for support rather than as an inherited estate.

This position is corroborated by the tradition of Ravin (and alternatively attributed to Rabbah bar bar Chana) citing Rabbi Yochanan: *kol she-yizonu mi-hen elu va-elu ad she-yivgaru, hen merubim; pachot mi-kan, harei elu muatim* — if there is enough to support both boys and girls until the girls reach maturity, that is a large estate; anything less is a small estate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי: כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כֹּל שֶׁיִּזּוֹנוּ מֵהֶן אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּבְגְּרוּ – הֵן מְרוּבִּין; פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּעָטִין.
>
> **Translation:** This **was also stated** by other *amora'im*: **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and some say** it was **Rabba bar bar Ḥana** who **says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Any** estate **that** is large enough **to provide sustenance from it for** both **these and those until** the time **that** the daughters **reach their majority is a large** estate; **less than that, this is a small** estate.

### Raba: Before Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לֵיכָּא לְאֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עַד שֶׁיִּבְגְּרוּ, שָׁקְלִי לְהוּ בָּנוֹת לְכוּלְּהוּ?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מוֹצִיאִין לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת לַבָּנוֹת עַד שֶׁיִּבְגְּרוּ, וְהַשְּׁאָר לַבָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if there is not** enough to provide **for these and those until** the daughters **reach their majority,** shall the **daughters take all** of the estate, even if it is more than is required for their sustenance? **Rather, Rava said:** The court **appropriates sustenance for the daughters until they reach their majority, and the remainder** is given **to the sons.**

The *gemara* then presses on the language *elu va-elu* — both groups. If there is $15,000 and the girls need $10,000 until *bagrut* while the boys would need another $10,000, is it really the case, as the *mishnah* seems to say about *nechasim muatim*, that the girls receive everything? *Shakli lehu banot le-chulhu?* — do the girls take all $15,000? That is clearly not right. Rava clarifies: *motzi'in lahen mezonot le-vanot ad she-yivgaru* — you cordon off the $10,000 needed for the girls' support until *bagrut*, *ve-ha-sha'ar le-vanim* — and the remainder, the $5,000, goes to the sons. They need more than that and will have to go begging for the rest, as the *mishnah* stated — but no one ever suggested the girls receive funds beyond their entitlement.

### After Threshold

The conceptual point Rava is drawing out is this. Below the threshold — when the total estate does not exceed what is needed to support everyone until the girls reach maturity — the money is not a *yerushah* in any meaningful sense. It is a designated fund for maintenance. Within that fund, the daughters' claims are prior: their full support is guaranteed first; any residual amount then goes to the sons for whatever portion of their support it covers. No one has "inherited" an estate; everyone is merely receiving subsistence.

Only once the estate exceeds that threshold — when there is more than enough to cover every child's maintenance until the girls mature — do the sons truly inherit. At that point, the daughters hold a lien against the sons' estate for their support until *bagrut*, and the sons control the remainder as legitimate heirs. The distinction, then, is not merely quantitative but conceptual: it determines whether the property is characterized as a maintenance fund (under the daughters' prior claim) or as an inherited estate (under the sons' ownership, subject to the daughters' lien).
