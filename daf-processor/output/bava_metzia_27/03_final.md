# Bava Metzia 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Context

### Intro: Safek Chinuch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְטָלָהּ לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ עַל מְנָת לְגוֹזְלָהּ – עוֹבֵר בְּכוּלָּן: מִשּׁוּם "לֹא תִגְזוֹל", וּמִשּׁוּם "הָשֵׁב תְּשִׁיבֵם", וּמִשּׁוּם "לֹא תוּכַל לְהִתְעַלֵּם". וְאַף עַל גַּב (דַּחֲזָרָה) [דְּאַהְדְּרַהּ] לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ, מַתָּנָה הוּא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ, וְאִיסּוּרָא דַּעֲבַד – עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rava says:** In a case where **one saw a *sela*** coin **that fell** from another, if **he took** the coin **in order to steal it, before** the **despair** of the owner, **he violates all of** the following mitzvot: He is liable **due to** the prohibition: **"You shall not…rob"** (Leviticus 19:13); **and due to** the positive mitzva, stated with regard to found items, of: **"You shall return** them to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1), **and due to** the prohibition, stated with regard to one who finds an item: **"You may not disregard"** (Deuteronomy 22:3). **And even if he returned it after** the **despair** of the owner, it is merely **a gift that he gave him; and the transgression that he performed, he performed,** and he remains in violation of these mitzvot.

We take up the *mishnah* at the bottom of 26b, having just completed an important stretch of *gemara* dealing with *safek chinuch* and the various types of *mitzvot* and *aveirot* implicated when one fails to retrieve an *aveidah*, takes it after *yeush*, or takes it with the intention of stealing. With that framework in place, we now turn to the *mishnah* that follows.

## Store & Money Changer

### Found in Store

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצָא בַּחֲנוּת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. בֵּין הַתֵּיבָה וְלַחֶנְוָנִי – שֶׁל חֶנְוָנִי. לִפְנֵי שׁוּלְחָנִי – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. בֵּין הַכִּסֵּא וְלַשּׁוּלְחָנִי – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל שׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **found** items without a distinguishing mark **in a store, those** items belong **to him,** as, since the store is frequented by the multitudes, the owner despairs of its recovery. If the items were found **between the** storekeeper's **counter and the storekeeper,** the items belong **to the storekeeper;** since his customers do not typically have access to that area, presumably the items are his. If one found coins **before a money changer, those** coins belong **to him.** If the coins were found **between the** money changer's **chair and the money changer, those** coins belong **to the money changer,** because his clients do not typically have access to that area.

The *mishnah* opens: *matsa b'chanut harei elu shelo* — if one finds a lost object in a store, he may keep it. Rashi explains that this refers to a case without a *siman*, for if there were a *siman* it would open the question discussed in connection with Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar regarding a *makom sheharabim metzuyim*. In any case, the point is that the *metziah* in question is something like a coin found on the floor — not an item sitting on a shelf. Since objects in a store are constantly being kicked around and moved, *makom* is not a *siman*, and the finder may keep it.

### Counter vs. Money Changer

The *mishnah* continues: *bein hateivah v'lachanvani* — if money is found between the counter and the storekeeper, one assumes it fell from the storekeeper, from the till. *Lifnei shulchani harei elu shelo* — if found in front of the money changer, loose coins on the floor in front of his table, the finder may keep them.

### Chair & Table

*Bein hakiseh v'lashulchani shel shulchani* — if found between the chair where the money changer sits and the table where he conducts his exchanges, it is presumed to belong to the money changer himself, *harei elu shel shulchani*.

### Money in Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלּוֹקֵחַ פֵּירוֹת מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁשִּׁלַּח לוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ פֵּירוֹת, וּמָצָא בָּהֶן מָעוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. אִם הָיוּ צְרוּרִין – נוֹטֵל וּמַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who purchases produce from another or** in a case **where another sent him produce** as a gift, **and he found coins** intermingled **with** the produce, **those** coins belong to **him. If** the coins **were bundled,** this serves as a distinguishing mark and the finder **takes** the coins **and proclaims** his find.

*Halokeach peirot mechavero o sheshalach lo chavero peirot umatsa bahen ma'ot* — if one purchased fruit from another, or was sent fruit as a gift, and found money inside, *harei elu shelo*, he may keep it. This is striking, since one knows precisely who the fruit came from. The *gemara* will address this.

### Bundled Fruit

However, *im hayu tserurim notel umachriz* — if the money was tied in a bundle, then the knot and the count serve as *simanim*, and one must announce it. One might ask: even without a bundle, why not simply return it to the one who sent the fruit, since presumably there has been no *yeush* — the sender did not even know the money was there, making this a case of *yeush shelo mida'at*? The *gemara* will resolve this.

## Shulchani Scope

### Rabbi Elazar: Table

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֲפִילּוּ מוּנָּחִים עַל גַּבֵּי שׁוּלְחָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one found coins before a money changer, those coins belong to him. **Rabbi Elazar says: Even** if the coins were found **placed upon** the **table** itself they belong to the finder.

The *gemara* opens its analysis with a statement by Rabbi Elazar: *afillu munachim al gabei shulchan* — even if the coins are resting on the table itself, the finder may keep them, provided they are not found in the space directly between the table and the money changer.

### Reisha/Seifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לִפְנֵי שׁוּלְחָנִי – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. הָא, עַל גַּבֵּי שׁוּלְחָן – דְּשׁוּלְחָנִי! אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בֵּין הַכִּסֵּא וְלַשּׁוּלְחָנִי – שֶׁל שׁוּלְחָנִי! הָא, עַל גַּבֵּי שׁוּלְחָן שֶׁלּוֹ. אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **We learned** in the mishna: If one found coins **before a money changer, those** coins **belong to him;** this indicates by inference **that** if they were found **upon the table,** the coins belong **to the money changer.** The Gemara responds: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: If the coins were found **between the** money changer's **chair and the money changer, those** coins belong **to the money changer;** this indicates by inference **that** if they were found **upon the table,** the coins belong **to** the finder. The Gemara concludes: **Rather,** due to the contradictory inferences from the first and the latter clauses, **no** inference is **to be learned from this** mishna.

The *gemara* examines this claim against the *mishnah*. From the *reisha*: *lifnei shulchani harei elu shelo* — "in front of the money changer" you may keep it. The implication is that *al gabei shulchan*, on the table itself, belongs to the money changer — *lashulchani*. But now consider the *seifa*: *bein hakiseh v'lashulchani shel shulchani* — between the chair and the *shulchani*. Here the word *shulchani* in context seems to mean the *shulchan*, the table, not the money changer himself. The implication of the *seifa* would then be the reverse — that money on the table belongs to the finder. The two halves of the *mishnah* thus seem to pull in opposite directions regarding money on the table itself. *Ela meha leka lemishma mina* — from the *mishnah* alone, one cannot infer an answer to that question.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, הָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ. מַאי אִרְיָא דְּתָנֵי "בֵּין הַכִּסֵּא לַשּׁוּלְחָנִי שֶׁל שׁוּלְחָנִי"? לִיתְנֵי "עַל שׁוּלְחָן", אִי נָמֵי: "מָצָא בַּשּׁוּלְחָנוּת" כִּדְקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא "מָצָא בַּחֲנוּת שֶׁלּוֹ". אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֲפִילּוּ מוּנָּחִים עַל גַּבֵּי שׁוּלְחָן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Elazar** himself, **from where** does he derive **this** *halakha* that coins found on the table belong to the finder, given that apparently one cannot infer this ruling from the mishna? **Rava said: The mishna is difficult** for **him: Why** did the *tanna* **teach specifically** that when the coins are found **between the** money changer's **chair and the money changer,** those coins belong **to the money changer? Let** the *tanna* **teach** instead: If the coins were found **on** the **table, or:** If the coins were found **in the money-changing establishment, as** it **is taught** in **the first clause** of the mishna: If one **found** items without a distinguishing mark **in a store,** those items belong **to him. Rather, learn from it** that since the money changer typically places his money in his drawer, **even** if the coins were found **placed upon** the **table** itself **these** coins belong **to him.**

Rava resolves the difficulty by explaining that the formulation of the *mishnah* itself is what motivated Rabbi Elazar's ruling. *Matnitin kashya leh* — the *mishnah*'s language is itself the source of the difficulty. Since the *seifa* says *bein hakiseh v'lashulchani* — between the chair and the table — one could ask: why phrase it this way? Simply say *al hashulchan*, "on the table," and it would be self-evident that between the table and the chair also belongs to the money changer. Alternatively, following the structure of the *reisha* which says *matsa b'chanut*, one might have expected the *seifa* to say something like "found in the money-changing area" (*beshulchanot*), and then contrast that with the case between the chair and the table. Instead, the *mishnah* says *lifnei hashulchani* — "in front of the money changer." This phrasing is therefore being read as inclusive of the table itself, which is after all positioned in front of him. *Ela shema mina afillu munachim al gabei shulchan harei elu shelo* — we learn that anything "in front of" the money changer, including what is on the table, the finder may keep.

### Random vs. Merchant

The practical logic underlying this ruling is straightforward: the money changer sits behind his table. Any money lying on the surface of the table in front of him could have been left there by any number of customers coming to exchange money; it is random. Only money found in the space between the table and where the money changer sits is presumed to be his, since that is where he keeps his own funds, behind the table.

### Methodology

This analysis illustrates a broader methodological point worth noting. The *gemara* characteristically does not articulate legal principles in the abstract; it derives them through *diyuk* — close reading of concrete formulations. It would seem more straightforward to simply ask: what does logic dictate in this case? Yet the *gemara* begins from the text and then extracts the principle. This mirrors the *Torah* itself, which speaks concretely — *ki yigach shor et shor reehu vamet* — and from concrete cases the law is derived. One can observe a transitional stage in the *mishnayot*, which often conclude a series of concrete cases with the formula *zeh haklal, kol she-...* — "this is the rule: whatever is..." — moving from the particular toward the general. Having both the concrete cases and the abstracted rule is, of course, more useful than either alone, since an abstract principle without worked examples leaves unclear exactly how it applies.

### Store Privacy

One might ask whether the store itself constitutes private property capable of effecting acquisition on behalf of the owner — after all, *chatzeiro shel adam konah lo*, a person's courtyard acquires objects on his behalf. The answer is that a store open to the public is *einah mishtameret* — it is not a guarded domain. This does not mean the owner cannot lock it at night; rather, it means that at the moment when people are freely entering and moving through it, the owner cannot effectively prevent others from accessing anything found there. A space that functions as a common thoroughfare is not a *makom mishtamer*, and therefore it does not effect acquisition for the owner as a courtyard would. It would, however, be a different question if the owner were *omed betzad chatzeiro* — standing immediately adjacent — but that is a separate matter.

## Fruit Money: Merchant

### Reish Lakish Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלּוֹקֵחַ פֵּירוֹת מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִן הַתַּגָּר, אֲבָל בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר. וְכֵן תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִן הַתַּגָּר, אֲבָל בְּלוֹקֵחַ מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In the case of **one who purchases produce from another,** and he found coins intermingled with the produce, those coins belong to him. **Reish Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yannai:** The Sages **taught** this **only** **in** the case of one **who purchases** produce **from a merchant,** who acquired the produce from several suppliers and is unable to determine the source of the coins. **But in** the case of one **who purchases** produce **from** a single **owner,** he is **obligated to return** the coins to the seller. **And likewise, the *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall of Rav Naḥman **taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** The Sages **taught** that the coins belong to the buyer **only in** the case of one **who purchases** produce **from a merchant, but in** the case of one **who purchases** produce **from a** single **owner,** he is **obligated to return** the coins to the seller.

The *gemara* now addresses the *mishnah*'s ruling that money found inside purchased fruit belongs to the finder. Reish Lakish, citing Rabbi Yannai, limits this rule: *lo shanu ela belokeach min hataggar* — it applies only when one bought from a merchant, a middleman who in turn purchases from many wholesalers. *Aval belokeach miba'al habayit chayav lehachazir* — if one bought directly from the farmer who grew the produce, one is obligated to return the money found in it. The reasoning is that in the latter case, the source of the money is known — it came from this specific farmer — and since he is presumably not aware of the loss, this is a case of *yeush shelo mida'at*, which does not constitute valid *yeush*. Furthermore, the farmer knows who bought from him and can reasonably expect to get it back. A *tanna* repeated this same ruling before Rav Nachman: it is only when one buys from the merchant that the finder may keep the money, because the merchant sources from many different wholesalers. Even if the money could be traced to one of those wholesalers, one does not know which wholesaler it was, and each of those wholesalers has already despaired — *miyaesh* — reasoning that once the money entered the merchant's supply, mixed with everyone else's grain or produce, they will never recover it. The situation thus parallels finding a *metziah* in the street, where the loser has no expectation of identifying the finder.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: וְכִי בַּעַל הַבַּיִת בְּעַצְמוֹ דָּשָׁן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶיסְמְיַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, תִּתַּרְגַּם מַתְנִיתִין כְּגוֹן שֶׁדָּשָׁן עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַכְּנַעֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to** the *tanna*: **But does** the **owner thresh** the grain **himself?** His workers thresh the grain, and the coins could belong to one of them. The *tanna* **said to** Rav Naḥman: Based on the difficulty you raise, **should I delete it** from the collection of authoritative *baraitot*? Rav Naḥman **said to** the *tanna*: **No. Interpret the *baraita*** as referring to a case **where** the grain **was threshed by his Canaanite slave or maidservant,** and therefore any coins found intermingled with the produce belong to the owner.

Rav Nachman challenges the other side of the ruling. Even if one buys from the farmer directly, why should the finder be obligated to return it? The farmer himself did not do the threshing — he has workers. *Stam peirot* here seems to refer to grain broadly. Since the farmer employs workers, the money found in the grain could equally have belonged to any one of them. There is no *siman* — none of the workers will know who lost it, and they will despair of recovering it. It would then be a classic case of *lo siman*, and the finder should be entitled to keep it. Accordingly, Rav Nachman presses: should this *braisa* simply be rejected as logically untenable?

### Kena'ani Slaves

The response is *lo* — the *braisa* can be reconciled. *Titarchei matnisin kegon shedashana al yedei avdo veshifchato hakenanit* — the case applies specifically where the farmer's labor force consists entirely of Canaanite slaves. Since Canaanite slaves possess nothing independently — all their property belongs to the master — any money found in the grain is by definition the farmer's money, not theirs. If the farmer had employed free workers, the situation would indeed be as Rav Nachman suggests: one would not know which individual lost the money, and since there is no *siman*, the finder would be entitled to keep it. Notably, the resolution does not suggest returning the money to the farmer on the grounds that he will investigate and figure out who lost it — once there is no *siman* and the precise identity of the loser is unknown, the ruling is *harei elu shelo*.

## Garment Paradigm

### Klal U'Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַף הַשִּׂמְלָה הָיְתָה בִּכְלַל כׇּל אֵלּוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצָאת? לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ, לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה שִׂמְלָה מְיוּחֶדֶת, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לָהּ תּוֹבְעִים, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לוֹ תּוֹבְעִים חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna is an excerpt from a halakhic midrash concerning lost items, based on the verse: "You shall not see your brother's ox or his sheep wandering, and disregard them; you shall return them to your brother…And so shall you do with his donkey; and so shall you do with his garment; and so shall you do with every lost item of your brother, which shall be lost from him, and you have found it; you may not disregard it" (Deuteronomy 22:1, 3). **The garment was also** included **in the generalization** that one must return **all of these** items. **And why did it emerge** from the generalization that it should be specified? **To draw an analogy to it and to say to you: What** is notable **about a garment?** It is notable **in that there are distinguishing marks concerning it and it has claimants** asserting ownership, and its finder is obligated to proclaim his find. **So too** with regard to **any item concerning which there are distinguishing marks and it has claimants** asserting ownership, its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find.

We turn now to a *mishnah* that could well have served as the opening of the entire tractate, since it addresses the foundational principles that have been operating throughout: the concept of *siman* and the concept of *yeush*. The *mishnah* reads: *af hasimla hayta baklal* — the garment was already included in the general category. The *pasuk* in *Devarim* states, among other things: *vechein ta'aseh lesimlato vechein ta'aseh lechol aveidat achicha asher tovad mimenu umetzata lo tuchal lehitalem* — you shall do the same for his garment, and for any lost object of your brother that you find; you may not ignore it. Since the *pasuk* already contains the general statement *kol aveidat achicha*, which covers everything, why single out the garment? *Lama yatzat* — *lehakesh lah*, to use the garment as a prototype from which other cases may be derived.

### Simanim & Tov'im

*Ma simla meyuchadet sheyesh bah simanim* — what is distinctive about a garment? Before the era of mass production, a cloak typically had identifying marks — *simanim* — that made it distinguishable. *Veyesh lah tov'im* — and there are claimants: someone who loses a garment is not going to give up on it. It is not *hefker*; the owner will go around asking and searching and will not despair of recovering it. *Af kol davar sheyesh bo simanim veyesh lo tov'im chayav lehachriz* — and so the rule is derived: any lost object that has *simanim* and has claimants actively seeking it must be announced and returned.

This formulation encapsulates the two principles that have run through the entire tractate from its opening. The plain reading of the *Torah* never actually permits keeping a lost object — it says to return everything. The *mishnah*, however, reads *simla* as defining the parameters: the obligation to return applies when both conditions hold. Moreover, there is a meaningful distinction between the two requirements. *Tov'im* — active claimants — points to the principle of *yeush*: you are obligated to return an *aveidah* only so long as the owner has not despaired. *Simanim*, meanwhile, address a distinct question: even granting that the owner has not despaired and is still searching, how does the finder know that the person standing before him is actually the owner? How can *simanim* be sufficient evidence on which to rely?

### Yeiush & Siman

These are two genuinely separate foundations. The *yeush* question concerns the owner's subjective state — whether he has given up hope. The *siman* question concerns the evidentiary basis for the finder's decision to return the object. It is true that the two are often linked: when there is a *siman*, the owner typically does not despair, because he believes he can identify his object and reclaim it. And conversely, when there is no *siman*, the owner may conclude that the finder will have no way of knowing the object is his and will give up hope. But the principles are conceptually independent. We have already encountered cases where a *siman* exists and yet the owner despairs — for instance, in a *makom sheharabim metzuyim* where many people pass through — and cases where there is no *siman* but the owner has not despaired — such as the fruit case, where the identity of the source is known even without a distinguishing mark. The *simla* teaches us both of these principles, and the *gemara* will now explore the extent to which each of them is genuinely derived from the *pasuk*.

## Multiple Examples

### Question Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי "בִּכְלַל כׇּל אֵלּוּ"? אָמַר רָבָא: בִּכְלַל "כׇּל אֲבֵדַת אָחִיךָ". אָמַר רָבָא: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא "שׁוֹר", "חֲמוֹר", "שֶׂה" וְ"שִׂמְלָה"?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: When the mishna says that the garment was included **in the generalization** that one must return **all of these** items, in **what** generalization is it included? **Rava said:** It is included **in the generalization:** "And so shall you do with his donkey; and so shall you do with his garment; and so shall you do with **every lost item of your brother,** which shall be lost from him, and you have found it; you may not disregard it" (Deuteronomy 22:3). **Rava says: Why do I** need all the specific items **that the Merciful One writes** that one must return: **An ox, a donkey, a sheep, and a garment?** One of them would seem to suffice.

The *gemara* asks: *lama li dichtav Rachmana shor chamor seh u-salmah* — why does the *Torah* list all these specific examples — an ox, a donkey, a sheep, and a garment — when the *pasuk* already contains the general clause *kol aveidat achicha*? The answer is *tzrikhi* — each item is necessary, each teaching something that could not be derived from the others alone.

### Direct Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא שִׂמְלָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעֵדִים דְּגוּפַהּ וְסִימָנִין דְּגוּפַהּ. אֲבָל חֲמוֹר בְּעֵדִים דְּאוּכָּף וְסִימָנִין דְּאוּכָּף, אֵימָא לָא מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "חֲמוֹר" דַּאֲפִילּוּ חֲמוֹר בְּסִימָנֵי הָאוּכָּף.
>
> **Translation:** Rava answers: They **are** all **necessary,** as a unique *halakha* is derived from each example. **As, if the Merciful One had written** only **"garment," I would say: This matter,** i.e., the mitzva to return a lost item, **applies** only in a case **where** the owner brings **witnesses** capable of testifying **about** the item **itself or** he describes **distinguishing marks concerning** the item **itself; but** with regard to returning **a donkey** to its owner in a case **where** he brings **witnesses with regard to the saddle or** describes **distinguishing marks concerning the saddle** and not on the donkey, **say** that **we do not return** the donkey **to** the owner. To counter this, **the Merciful One writes: "Donkey,"** from which it is derived **that a donkey** is returned to its owner **even** in a case **where** he describes **distinguishing marks** on **the saddle.**

If the *Torah* had written only *simla* and the general *kol aveidat achicha*, one might have read the obligation narrowly: *hani milei be-edim de-gufa ve-simanim de-gufa* — the obligation to return applies only where the identification is direct, either through witnesses who recognize the object itself or through *simanim* on the object itself. It must be noted that the *gemara* hedges here deliberately, because we have not yet resolved whether *simanim* constitute a *d'oraita* basis for return or only a *d'rabbanan* one. The question — which will emerge explicitly shortly — is whether at the *Torah* level, the only valid basis for returning an object is testimony by third-party witnesses who directly identify the object (*tfisat ayin*), or whether a claimant's own description of a *siman* suffices. With this question open, the *gemara* presents both *edim* and *simanim* as possible means of establishing ownership, while noting that both would be understood, under the narrow reading, as requiring identification of the object directly.

### Donkey & Saddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "שׁוֹר" וָ"שֶׂה" כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? "שׁוֹר" – דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְגִיזַּת זְנָבוֹ, וְ"שֶׂה" – לְגִיזּוֹתָיו. וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא "שׁוֹר", דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְגִיזַּת זְנָבוֹ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן "שֶׂה" לְגִיזּוֹתָיו!
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: With regard to the specific mentions of **"ox" and "sheep" that the Merciful One writes, why do I** need them? Rava answers: From **"ox"** it is derived **that** one must return **even the sheared** wool **of its tail; and** from **"sheep"** it is derived **that** one must return even **its sheared** wool. The Gemara challenges: **And let the Merciful One write** only **"ox,"** from which it is derived **that** one must return **even the sheared** wool **of its tail, and** derive **all the more so that** one must return the more substantial **sheared** wool **of a sheep.**

What, then, does the extra word *chamor* — donkey — teach? *Aval be-chamor be-edim de-ukaf ve-simanim de-ukaf, eima lo mahadrinan leih* — if all one has is testimony or *simanim* pertaining to the saddle, not the donkey itself, one might have thought that is insufficient — perhaps the saddle belongs to a different person than the donkey. *Ktav Rachmana chamor* — the *Torah* writes "donkey" to teach that one may return even the donkey on the basis of *simanim* on the saddle alone. We do not entertain the concern that the saddle found on the animal belongs to someone else; rather, *de-afilu chamor be-simanei ha-ukaf*, one returns the donkey on the strength of identifying marks on its saddle.

### By-Products

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: "חֲמוֹר" דְּבוֹר לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְ"שֶׂה" דַאֲבֵידָה – לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The term **"donkey"** stated **with regard to** damage in the category of **Pit, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** (see Exodus 21:33 and Bava Kamma 54a), **and** the term **"sheep"** stated **with regard to a lost item, according to** the opinion of **everyone,** are **difficult.** There is no explanation for why they are stated.

One might then ask: if we derive from the ox that even the tail sheddings must be returned, do we even need the sheep? Rava himself concedes this point — the sheep might have been derivable from the ox by *kal va-chomer*. He therefore offers a different explanation: *chamor de-vor le-Rabbi Yehuda ve-seh de-aveidah divrei ha-kol* — the word *chamor* by *aveidah* is needed specifically for Rabbi Yehuda's position, and the word *seh* teaches something relevant to *aveidah* according to all opinions. Rabbi Yehuda holds, in the context of *Bava Kamma*, that one is liable for vessels damaged in a pit — *chamor ve-lo kelim* does not serve as an exclusion for him — and so the word *chamor* by lost objects cannot serve the function it would serve according to the *Tanna Kama*. Rabbi Yehuda therefore needs that word for a different purpose. Similarly, the word *seh* must be teaching something that has not yet been covered by the other examples.

### Dung Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִימָא לִגְלָלִים הוּא דַּאֲתָא? גְּלָלִים – אַפְקוֹרֵי מַפְקַר לְהוּ. וְדִילְמָא לְסִימָנִין הוּא דַּאֲתָא, דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לַן: סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אוֹ דְרַבָּנַן. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "שֶׂה", דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּסִימָנִין מַהְדְּרִינַן, וְסִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And say** that the term "sheep" **comes to** teach the obligation to return the animal's **dung?** The Gemara answers: One need not return **dung,** because the owner has **renounced its ownership.** The Gemara suggests: **And perhaps** the term "sheep" **comes to** teach the obligation to return an item based on its owner providing **distinguishing marks, as we raised a dilemma:** Is the *halakha* that an item can be identified using **distinguishing marks by Torah law or** is it **by rabbinic law?** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "Sheep"** in order to teach **that** it is not only through the testimony of witnesses, but **even** based **on distinguishing marks** that **we return** lost items to their owner. Resolve the dilemma **and** conclude that the *halakha* that an item can be identified using **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law.**

What could *seh* be teaching? The *gemara* raises the possibility: *ve-eima le-glalim hu de-ata* — perhaps the word *seh* comes to teach that even the animal's dung must be preserved and returned to the owner, or at least that the finder must sell it and return the proceeds, since dung has value as fertilizer. The *gemara* rejects this: *glalim afkurei mafkir lehu* — people do not expect to reclaim their animal's dung; they effectively renounce ownership of it. This is a striking application of *sevara* — the *gemara* is acknowledging that yes, there is an extra word in the *pasuk* that needs to be accounted for, and technically one could use it to teach an obligation regarding dung. But logic dictates otherwise: no one losing an animal intends to claim back its dung, and the finder who is caring for the animal — and potentially paying costs that will be reimbursed — cannot reasonably be expected to collect and hold the dung. The *Torah*'s words are interpreted in light of what people actually care about, and dung simply does not make the list.

## Simanim Authority

### Sheep & Simanim

If the word *seh* is not being used to teach the dung obligation, perhaps it is available to teach something else — specifically, that *simanim* constitute a valid *d'oraita* basis for returning a lost object. After all, *simla* might not be unambiguous on this point, as we will see. Perhaps *seh* is what actually establishes *simanim de-Oraita*, and the *simla* is reserved purely for the *yeush* principle.

### Iba'ya Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מִדְּקָתָנֵי לְהוּ תַּנָּא, לְסִימָנִין גַּבֵּי שִׂמְלָה, דְּקָתָנֵי: מָה שִׂמְלָה מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לָהּ תּוֹבְעִין – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לוֹ תּוֹבְעִין – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּ"שֶׂה" לָאו לְסִימָנִין הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof. The Sages **say:** One can understand the matter **from** the fact **that the *tanna* teaches** the concept of **distinguishing marks together** with the term **garment.** As **it is taught** in the mishna: **What** is notable **about a garment?** It is notable **in that there are distinguishing marks concerning it and it has claimants** asserting ownership, and its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find. **So too** with regard to **any item concerning which there are distinguishing marks and it has claimants** asserting ownership, its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find. **Conclude from it that** the term **"sheep" does not come to** teach the obligation to return an item based on its owner providing **distinguishing marks.**

*Iba'ya lehu* — the question is left open: are *simanim d'oraita* or *d'rabbanan*? This is distinct from the *yeush* question. Whatever conditions lead an owner to despair may sometimes involve *simanim* and sometimes not, but *yeush* is clearly a *d'oraita* principle. The separate question is whether the evidentiary basis for returning an object — namely, a claimant's description of an identifying mark — is itself rooted in *Torah* law or only in rabbinic enactment. The *gemara* is considering using the word *seh* as the source for *simanim d'oraita*, establishing that even by a category of object where direct identification is not self-evident, *simanim* suffice as the return mechanism.

The *gemara* then says: *amri lo — ha detanya u-tana lesimanin gabi simla* — since the *mishnah* itself already teaches *simanim* in the context of *simla*, it is clear that the author of our *mishnah* does not derive *simanim* from *seh*. Either the *mishnah* genuinely holds *simanim* to be *d'oraita* derived from *simla*, or it holds *simanim* to be *d'rabbanan* and is reading the *simla* passage as a mere *asmachta* — but either way, *seh* is being used for some other *drasha* that we have not yet identified. The question of whether *simanim* are *d'oraita* or *d'rabbanan* therefore remains formally unresolved at this stage of the *sugya*. *Shema mina de-seh lav le-simanim hu de-ata* — it is clear the author of our *mishnah* does not use *seh* as the source for *simanim*.

## Pesuk Details

### Worth Losing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "אֲשֶׁר תֹּאבַד" – פְּרָט לַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: "וּמְצָאתָהּ" – פְּרָט לַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And so shall you do with his donkey; and so shall you do with his garment; and so shall you do with every lost item of your brother, **which shall be lost** from him, and you have found it" (Deuteronomy 22:3). The Rabbis derive that this phrase serves **to exclude a lost item in which there is not the value of one *peruta*,** which due to its negligible value is not considered lost. **Rabbi Yehuda says** that this *halakha* is derived from the conclusion of that verse: "Which shall be lost from him, **and you have found it."** The term "and you have found it" serves **to exclude a lost item in which there is not the value of one *peruta*.**

The *braisa* cites the phrase *asher tovad* — "which is lost" — and derives from it that the obligation of return applies only to something substantial enough to be worth losing. This connects to the discussion earlier regarding a lost object worth less than a *shaveh prutah*.

### Shaveh Prutah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַשְׁמָעוּת דּוֹרְשִׁין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. מָר נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ"אֲשֶׁר תֹּאבַד" וּמָר נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ"וּמְצָאתָהּ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the practical difference **between** the two opinions? Ostensibly, the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda both state the same *halakha*. **Abaye said:** There is no practical difference. Rather, the **interpretation of the meaning** of the verse **is** the difference **between** them. One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **derives it from** the phrase: **"Which shall be lost** from him"; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **derives it from** the term: **"And you have found it."**

Rabbi Yehuda derives the *shaveh prutah* threshold from the word *u-matzata* — "and you found it" — it must be something worth finding. The *Tanna Kama* derives it from *asher tovad* — it must be something worth losing. As Abaye observes: *mashmauta d'krai ika beinaihu* — there is no practical difference between these positions; both conclude that a lost object worth less than a *prutah* carries no return obligation. The disagreement is purely textual — which phrase in the *pasuk* is the vehicle for this *drasha*. But that textual disagreement has downstream consequences, because whichever phrase one uses for this *drasha* is then unavailable to teach something else.

### Ravnai: Jew Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ"אֲשֶׁר תֹּאבַד" הַאי "וּמְצָאתָהּ" מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבְנַאי, דְּאָמַר רַבְנַאי: "וּמְצָאתָהּ" – דַּאֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the first Sage, the Rabbis, **who derives** the *halakha* that one need not return a lost item worth less than one *peruta* **from** the phrase **"which shall be lost** from him," **what does he do with** the term: **"And you have found it?"** The Gemara answers: According to the Rabbis, **that** term **is necessary for** the derivation of the *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbenai. As Rabbenai says** in interpreting the verse: "And so shall you do with every lost item of your brother's, which he has lost, and you have found it" (Deuteronomy 22:3), that the term **"and you have found it" means that** it assumes the status of a found item only **when** it actually **enters his possession.**

For the one who derives *shaveh prutah* from *asher tovad*, what does *u-matzata* teach? It goes to the teaching of Ravnai, who derives from *u-matzata d'ata l'yadei mashma* — "and you found it" implies it has already come into your hand — that even after one has picked up a lost object, the obligation to return it applies only to *aveidat achicha*, a fellow Jew's property. One might have thought that even if one is not obligated to pick up a non-Jew's lost item, having picked it up one might be obligated to return it. Ravnai teaches from *u-matzata* that even in that case, the obligation does not apply.

### Yochanan: River Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ"וּמְצָאתָהּ", הַאי "אֲשֶׁר תֹּאבַד" מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: מִנַּיִין לַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁשְּׁטָפָהּ נָהָר שֶׁהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כֵּן תַּעֲשֶׂה לְכׇל אֲבֵדַת אָחִיךָ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאבַד מִמֶּנּוּ וּמְצָאתָהּ" – מִי שֶׁאֲבוּדָה הֵימֶנּוּ וּמְצוּיָה אֵצֶל כׇּל אָדָם, יָצְתָה זוֹ שֶׁאֲבוּדָה הֵימֶנּוּ וְאֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה אֵצֶל כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** Rabbi Yehuda, **who derives** the *halakha* that one need not return a lost item worth less than one *peruta* from the term: **"And you have found it," what does he do with** the phrase: **"Which has been lost** from him"? The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Yehuda, **that** phrase **is necessary for** the derivation of the *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan. As Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: From where** is it derived **with regard to a lost item that the river swept away that it is permitted** for its finder to keep it? It is derived from this verse, **as it is written:** "And so shall you do with his donkey; and so shall you do with his garment; and **so shall you do with every lost item of your brother, which shall be lost from him, and you have found it"** (Deuteronomy 22:3). The verse states that one must return **that which is lost from him,** the owner, **but is** available to be **found by any person. Excluded** from that obligation is **that which is lost from him and is not** available to be **found by any person;** it is ownerless property and anyone who finds it may keep it.

For the one who derives *shaveh prutah* from *u-matzata*, what does *asher tovad* teach? It goes to the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: *minayin l'aveida she-shatafah nahar she-hi muteret* — how do we know that an object swept away by a river may be kept, even without the owner having despaired? From the phrase *asher tovad mimennu* — "which is lost from him." The complete formula is read as: *ne'evad mimennu u-matzui l'chol adam* — it is lost from him but accessible to others. This defines an *aveidah* subject to the return obligation. *Yatza'ah zu she-avudah heimennu ve-einah metzuyah l'chol adam* — excluded is the case of an object lost from the owner and inaccessible to others as well; that is the river case, where the object is simply gone for everyone. Such an object is not an *aveidah* in the operative sense, and keeping it is permitted.

### Derash Allocation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, הָא דְּרַבְנַאי מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ"וּמְצָאתָהּ". וְאִידַּךְ? "מִמֶּנּוּ" לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And the other** *tanna*, the first *tanna*, who derives from the phrase "which shall be lost from him" that one need not return a lost item worth less than one *peruta*, **from where** do they derive the *halakha* **of Rabbi Yoḥanan that** one need not return an item that is lost from him and is not available to be found by every person? The Gemara answers: He **derives it** from the superfluous term **"from him,"** in the phrase "which shall be lost from him." **And** as **for the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yehuda, **he does not learn** anything from the term **"from him."**

The *gemara* then addresses the cross-allocation of these *derashot*. For the one who uses *u-matzata* to derive *shaveh prutah*, where does Ravnai's teaching come from? From the extra *vav* — *u-matzata* as distinct from *matzata*. For the one who uses *asher tovad* to derive *shaveh prutah*, where does Rabbi Yochanan's teaching of the river case come from? From *mimennu* specifically — the word *asher tovad* yields *shaveh prutah*, while *mimennu* yields the *aveida she-shatafah nahar* principle: lost from him specifically, yet accessible to others. The remaining position — that *mimennu* does not yield a *drasha* — simply does not accept this reading of the word. The *gemara* concludes that Rava offers yet another distinction between these positions — not merely a difference in textual allocation, but a genuine practical difference — though that analysis opens the next stage of the discussion.

## The Dilemma of Simanim

### Simanim Authority Question Raised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אוֹ דְרַבָּנַן? מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised** before the Sages: Is identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks by Torah law or** is it **by rabbinic law?** The Gemara asks: **What difference is** there whether it is by Torah law or by rabbinic law?

### Practical Difference: Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי גֵּט אִשָּׁה בְּסִימָנִים. אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מַהְדְּרִינַן, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן – כִּי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא בְּמָמוֹנָא, אֲבָל בְּאִיסּוּרָא – לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The practical difference is **with regard to returning the bill of divorce of a woman** that was lost by an agent before its delivery, **on the basis of distinguishing marks. If you say** that the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is **by Torah law, we return** the document and allow the agent to transmit it to the woman. **But if you say** that it is **by rabbinic law,** we do not return the document, because **when the Sages institute an ordinance,** it is only **with regard to monetary matters** they have the authority to declare property ownerless; **but with regard to ritual matters, the Sages do not institute an ordinance.** They lack the authority to abrogate the prohibitions by Torah law that are associated with a woman's marital status.

## Attempted Proofs for Simanim Status

### Proof from Mishna: Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אַף הַשִּׂמְלָה הָיְתָה בִּכְלַל כׇּל אֵלּוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצָאת לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ? וְלוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה שִׂמְלָה מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לָהּ תּוֹבְעִין חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ סִימָנִין וְיֵשׁ לוֹ תּוֹבְעִין חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. תַּנָּא: תּוֹבְעִין אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סִימָנִין כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from the mishna: **The garment was also** included **in the generalization** that one must return **all of these** items. **And why did it emerge** from the generalization that is should be specified? **To draw an analogy to it and to say to you: What** is notable **about a garment?** It is notable **in that there are distinguishing marks concerning it and it has claimants** asserting ownership, and its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find. **So too** with regard to **any item concerning which there are distinguishing marks and it has claimants** asserting ownership, its finder is **obligated to proclaim** his find. Clearly, the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law. The Gemara rejects the proof: Perhaps it **was necessary for the *tanna*** to mention only the criterion of **claimants,** and the *tanna* **cited** the criterion **of distinguishing marks for no reason,** as by Torah law distinguishing marks are not a relevant factor.

### Proof from Baraita: Donkey-Saddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: חֲמוֹר בְּסִימָנֵי אוּכָּף. אֵימָא "בְּעֵדֵי אוּכָּף".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from the aforementioned statement: The obligation to return **a donkey** to its owner **on the basis of** the **distinguishing marks of the saddle** is derived based on the mention of the word "donkey" in the verse from Deuteronomy. Clearly, the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law. The Gemara rejects this proof: Emend the *baraita* and **say:** There is an obligation to return the donkey only **on the basis of witnesses** who testify with regard to the identity of the owner based on the fact that **the saddle** belongs to him, and not on the basis of distinguishing marks.

### Proof from Mishna: Gerim and Scrutiny

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: "וְהָיָה עִמְּךָ עַד דְּרֹשׁ אָחִיךָ אֹתוֹ". וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁיִּתְּנֶנּוּ לוֹ קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּדְרְשֶׁנּוּ?! אֶלָּא דׇּרְשֵׁהוּ אִם רַמַּאי הוּא אוֹ אֵינוֹ רַמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a mishna (28b): "And if your brother be not near you, and you know him not, then you shall bring it into your house, **and it shall be with you until your brother claims [*derosh*] it,** and you shall return it to him" (Deuteronomy 22:2). **Would it enter your mind that he would give** the lost item **to him before he claims it?** How can the finder return it if he does not know the identity of the owner? **Rather,** the verb *derosh* is not referring to the claim of the owner; it is referring to the scrutiny performed by the finder. **Scrutinize him [*darshehu*]** to determine **whether** the claimant **is a swindler** or **whether he is not a swindler.** Only then may you return the lost item to him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּסִימָנִין? לֹא, בְּעֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its suggested proof: **What, is it not** that the one who claims the lost item proves that he is not a swindler **on the basis of distinguishing marks** that he provides? Apparently, the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** the determination of whether he is a swindler is **on the basis of** scrutinizing his **witnesses.**

### Proof from Yevamot: Facial Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין מְעִידִין אֶלָּא עַל פַּרְצוּף הַפָּנִים עִם הַחוֹטֶם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ סִימָנִין בְּגוּפוֹ וּבְכֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a mishna (*Yevamot* 120a): **One testifies** that a man died, thereby permitting his wife to remarry, **only** if he can testify **about** seeing **the countenance [*partzuf* ] of the face with the nose,** as this allows one to identify the individual with certainty. **Although there are distinguishing marks on his body and on his garments,** which appear to indicate his identity, they cannot be used to identify the person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סִימָנִין לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! אָמְרִי: גּוּפוֹ – דְּאָרוֹךְ וְגוּץ. כֵּלָיו – דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁאֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its suggested proof: **Conclude from it** that the identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks is not by Torah law.** The Sages **say** in rejecting that proof: The distinguishing marks on **his body** mentioned in the mishna are non-specific distinguishing marks, e.g., **that** he was **tall or short,** and that is the reason that the distinguishing marks are ineffective in determining his identity. The distinguishing marks on **his** garments mentioned in the mishna are ineffective in determining his identity, **as we are concerned about** the possibility of **a loan,** e.g., perhaps the husband loaned his clothes to the deceased.

### Clarifying: Borrowed Items Cannot Vitiate Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁאֵלָה, חֲמוֹר בְּסִימָנֵי אוּכָּף הֵיכִי מַהְדְּרִינַן? אָמְרִי: אוּכָּף, לָא שָׁאיְלִי אִינָשֵׁי אוּכָּפָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַקֵּב לֵיהּ לַחֲמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If we are concerned about** the possibility of **a loan, how do we return a donkey** to its owner **on the basis of** the **distinguishing marks of the saddle;** perhaps it was borrowed? The Sages **say** in response: **People do not** typically **borrow a saddle because** saddles that are not custom fit **wound the donkey.**

### Alternative for Yevamot Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כֵּלָיו בְּחִיוָּרֵי וּבְסוּמְקִי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: The distinguishing marks on **his garments** mentioned in the mishna are non-specific distinguishing marks, e.g., **where** the witness said that they were **white or red,** and that is the reason that the distinguishing marks are ineffective in determining his identity.

### Proof from Bill-of-Divorce Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: מְצָאוֹ קָשׁוּר בְּכִיס אוֹ בְּאַרְנָקִי וּבְטַבַּעַת, אוֹ שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ בֵּין כֵּלָיו, אֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה – כָּשֵׁר. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁאֵלָה, כִּי מְצָאוֹ קָשׁוּר בְּכִיס אַמַּאי כָּשֵׁר? נֵיחוּשׁ לִשְׁאֵלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the previous answer with regard to the concern about the possibility of a loan. **But** there is **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **If** the agent **found** the bill of divorce that he lost **bound to** his **pouch, or** his **purse, or** his signet **ring, or if he found it among his garments, even** if he found it **a long time** after he lost it, the distinguishing marks on those items are sufficient in order to identify the bill of divorce as the one that he lost, and it is **valid. And if it enters your mind** that **we are concerned about** the possibility of **a loan, when he found** the bill of divorce **bound to** his **pouch, why is it valid? Let us be concerned about** the possibility of **a loan** and that perhaps the pouch and the bill of divorce belong to someone else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כִּיס וְאַרְנָקִי וְטַבַּעַת לָא מַשְׁאֲלִי אִינָשֵׁי. כִּיס וְאַרְנָקִי – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַמְּנִי, וְטַבַּעַת – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְזַיֵּיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: There is no concern in this case, as **people do not loan a pouch, a purse, or** a signet **ring** to another person. One does not loan his **pouch and** his **purse** to others **due to** the fact **that it portends** the loss of his good fortune. **And** one does not loan his signet **ring** to others **due to** the fact **that** it could be used to **forge** documents.

### Tannaitic Dispute Over Moles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְתַנָּאֵי: אֵין מְעִידִין עַל הַשּׁוּמָא, וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן מַהֲבַאי אוֹמֵר: מְעִידִין עַל הַשּׁוּמָא. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי? דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: סִימָנִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן מַהֲבַאי סָבַר: סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the dilemma whether the identification of an item on the basis of distinguishing marks is by Torah law or by rabbinic law is **the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: **One does not testify on the basis of a mole** on the body of the deceased to determine the identity of a man who died and permit his wife to remarry. **And Elazar ben Mahavai says: One testifies** to identify the corpse **on the basis of a mole. What, is it not with regard to this** matter **that they disagree; as the first *tanna* holds** that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by rabbinic law** and therefore, testimony concerning those marks cannot dissolve a marriage by Torah law; **and Elazar ben Mahavai holds** that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law.**

### Rava Resolves: Alternative Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא סִימָנִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְהָכָא בְּשׁוּמָא מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ, וּמָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא אֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** That is not necessarily the crux of their dispute, as perhaps **everyone** agrees that identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law, and here,** it is **with regard to** whether one needs to be concerned that **a mole is** often **found on one's contemporary,** i.e., one born under the same constellation, rendering it useless as a means of identification, that **they disagree. One Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **a mole is** often **found on one's contemporary** and therefore it is insufficient as a means of identification; **and one Sage,** Elazar ben Mahavai, **holds that a mole is not** often **found on one's contemporary,** and therefore it is sufficient as a means of identification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שׁוּמָא אֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה בְּבֶן גִּילוֹ, וְהָכָא בְּסִימָנִין הָעֲשׂוּיִם לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: סִימָנִין עֲשׂוּיִם לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה. וּמָר סָבַר: סִימָנִין אֵין עֲשׂוּיִם לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that **everyone** agrees that **a mole is not** often **found on one's contemporary, and here** it is **with regard to** whether the appearance of **distinguishing marks** on the body **is apt to change after death** that **they disagree. One Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the appearance of **distinguishing marks is apt to change after death,** and that consequently they are insufficient as a means of identification; **and one Sage,** Elazar ben Mahavai, **holds** that the appearance of **distinguishing marks is not apt to change after death,** and therefore, they are sufficient as a means of identification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שׁוּמָא אֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְסִימָנִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְהָכָא בְּשׁוּמָא סִימָן מוּבְהָק הוּא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא סִימָן מוּבְהָק הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר: שׁוּמָא לָאו סִימָן מוּבְהָק הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that **everyone** agrees that **a mole is not apt to change after death, and** that the identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks is by rabbinic law, and here** it is **with regard to** whether **a mole is a clear-cut distinguishing mark** that **they disagree. One Sage,** Elazar ben Mahavai, **holds** that **a mole is a clear-cut distinguishing mark** that can be relied upon without hesitation even in matters of Torah law, e.g., dissolving a marriage; **and one Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds that a mole is not a clear-cut distinguishing mark.** Since standard distinguishing marks are sufficient by rabbinic law, a marriage, which is in effect by Torah law, cannot be dissolved on the basis of a mole.

### Rava's Direct Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר סִימָנִין לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, הֵיכִי מַהְדְּרִינַן אֲבֵידְתָּא בְּסִימָנִין? דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ לְמוֹצֵא אֲבֵידָה דְּנַהְדַּר בְּסִימָנִין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּכִי אָבְדָה לֵיהּ לְדִידֵהּ נָמֵי נַהְדְּרוּ לֵיהּ בְּסִימָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: If you say** that the identification of an item on the basis of **distinguishing marks is not by Torah law, how do we return a lost item** to the presumed owner **on the basis of distinguishing marks;** perhaps it will result in the return of property to one who was in fact not the owner? Rava answers: We return the lost item, **as** it is **satisfactory to the finder of a lost item to return** it **on the basis of distinguishing marks,** rather than exercise his right by Torah law to retain it, **so that when** an item **is lost from him** in the future, the finder **will return it to him on the basis of distinguishing marks as well.**

### Rav Safra Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא לְרָבָא: וְכִי אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה טוֹבָה לְעַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra said to Rava: But can a person perform** an act that results in **benefit for himself with property that is not his?** The lost item belongs not to the finder but to the one who lost it. How can the finder waive the right of the true owner to the lost item so that he may recover his own lost item in the future?

### Rava's Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְבַעַל אֲבֵידָה לְמֵיהַב סִימָנִין וּלְמִשְׁקְלֵיהּ. מִידָּע יָדַע דְּעֵדִים לֵית לֵיהּ, וּמֵימָר אָמַר: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא יָדְעִי סִימָנִין מוּבְהָקִים דִּידַהּ, וַאֲנָא יָהֵיבְנָא סִימָנִין מוּבְהָקִים דִּידַהּ וְשָׁקֵלְנָא לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we return the lost item, as it is **satisfactory to the owner of the lost item to** be able to **provide** a description using **distinguishing marks and** on that basis **take** possession of the item. **He knows that he has no witnesses** to testify to his ownership, **and he says: No one** else **knows** the **clear-cut distinguishing marks** that are on the item. **And I will provide** a description using the **clear-cut distinguishing marks, and** based on that information **I will take** possession of the item. Each owner gives his tacit agreement to the return of lost items on the basis of distinguishing marks, based on the belief that he is best able to identify them.
