# Niddah 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dead Bird & Machshav (II)

### Context & Background

Today's *daf* is 51, and we pick up at the bottom of 50b. The discussion has been a digression concerning whether the carcass of a kosher bird — *nevilat of tehorah* — which causes *tumah* when swallowed, requires any preliminary condition before it can itself become *tamei*.

The relevant background principle is as follows. Something that will itself become an *av ha-tumah*, if it is also a type of food, can essentially render itself *tamei* as a *rishon* without needing to come into contact with water or touch anything else — by virtue of its potential to become an *av*. However, for this to occur, the dead bird must be considered food, since the *tumah* it is receiving is the *tumah* of foodstuff. This discussion arose in the context of a broader question: when does *machshava* — intention — define something as food?

The general rule is that *machshava* cannot meaningfully define something as food while that thing is still attached to the ground, or while an animal is still alive, because in those states it cannot yet become *tamei*. The *machshava* cannot be operative where *tumah* is impossible. The proof-case was a bird that required a decision — after its death — that one intended to eat it (or to give it to a non-Jew to eat) before it could become *tamei*. That is the context: *machshava* matters not while something is attached, not while it is alive, but the specific discussion now concerns the role of *machshava* for this dead bird.

### Wine Case

The *Gemara* had, at the close of yesterday's *daf*, framed the debate in somewhat narrower terms: in *zilgay* — small villages — everyone agrees that *machshava* is required, because carcasses of birds are not normally eaten there. In cities, where there is a larger population with broader tastes, everyone agrees that *machshava* is not required. The point of debate falls in the intermediate case: a city, where ordinarily no *machshava* would be needed, but the bird has fallen into a vat of wine — a circumstance in which it is not so obvious that people would still want to eat it. That is where the question of *machshava* becomes relevant.

### Core Debate

This framing, however, does not capture the actual principled dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the Rabbis, and indeed *Tosafot* struggles to understand why the *Gemara* bothered with that framing at all. The core debate is not about whether a bird in a vat of wine is normally eaten. It is a fundamental question of principle: since the carcass of a dead bird is destined to be a source of *tumah*, does it require *machshava* — intention to treat it as food — at all? That is the question the *Gemara* now addresses in earnest.

### Kal Va-Chomer

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri advances a *kal va-chomer*: if this carcass of a dead bird can cause a strict *tumah* — *tumah chamurah* — without intention (for when you swallow it, it is an *av ha-tumah* regardless of whether you planned to eat it or even treated it as food), then certainly it should also be able to cause the lighter *tumah* — *tumah kallah* — namely, to render itself *tamei* as a *rishon*, without requiring intention to treat it as food. Why should *machshava* be needed when the heavier *tumah* proceeds without it?

### Rabbis' Response

The Rabbis respond: *lo* — no. The *Gemara* first presents Rava's reading of their counter-argument, which runs as follows: *lo, im amarta betumah chamurah* — true, the severe *tumah* does not require *machshava*, but that is because *tumah chamurah* has a particular weakness: *einah osah kiyotei bah* — it does not replicate itself in kind. The person who becomes *tamei* by touching or swallowing the *av ha-tumah* is a *rishon*, and that *rishon* cannot make another person *tamei*. By contrast, by *tumat ochlin*, food that is a *rishon* does make other food *tamei* as a *sheni*. So perhaps the lighter *tumah* — since it has this additional power of propagation — requires the added condition of *machshava*.

Abaye immediately objects that this reasoning runs backwards: it is a *kol sheken* in the other direction. *Tumah chamurah* has a weakness — it cannot propagate in kind — yet it proceeds without *machshava*. *Tumah kallah*, which is in some sense weightier in that it does propagate, should therefore all the more so not require *machshava*. Abaye's *kol sheken* effectively dismantles the Rabbis' *hava amina*.

Rav Sheshet then offers the more straightforward reading of the Rabbis' position: the reason the *av ha-tumah* — the severe *tumah* when swallowed — does not require *machshava* is *sheken einah tzerichah hechsher* — because it also does not require *hechsher*. An *av ha-tumah* is intrinsically a source of *tumah* by virtue of what it is; it requires no contact with liquid, no external *tumah* transmitted to it, no defining criteria. Therefore it also requires no *machshava*. By contrast, *tumah kallah* — *tumat ochlin* — does ordinarily require *hechsher*, requires external contact with liquid, and therefore also requires *machshava* to establish the thing's identity as food. The two categories operate under different rules, and no *kal va-chomer* bridges them.

---

## Hechsher Question

### Challenge

The *Gemara* is not fully satisfied. It challenges: does this dead bird actually require *hechsher*? The *Mishnah* states explicitly that among the thirteen things said about the carcass of a dead kosher bird, it *requires machshava* and does *not* require contact with liquid — it does not need *hechsher mayim*. If we have already granted that this particular case of *tumat ochlin* does not require *hechsher*, then by parity of reasoning it should not require *machshava* either. The Rabbis' entire defense — that the two categories have different rules — collapses in this case, where *hechsher* has already been waived.

The *Gemara* initially attempts a technical escape: perhaps the dead bird does not require *hechsher sheretz* — it does not need to conduct *tumah* from an outside source — but it does still require *hechsher mayim*, contact with liquid. And just as it requires liquid to be defined as food, it requires *machshava* to be defined as food.

### Rabbi Yishmael

The *Gemara* immediately undermines this escape. What is the basis for requiring *hechsher sheretz* from some things and not others? The school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches from the verse *al kol zera zerua asher yizarea* — "on every seed that will be planted." The principle derived is: *ma zraim she-ein sofan letamei tumah chamurah tzrichim hechsher* — just as seeds, which will never themselves be a source of *tumah chamurah*, require *hechsher*, so too does anything that will never become a source of *tumah chamurah* require *hechsher*. Conversely, *yatzta nivlat of tahor* — the carcass of a dead kosher bird is excluded, since *she-sofa letamei tumah chamurah* — it will eventually be an *av ha-tumah* when swallowed. Therefore *she-ein tzricha hechsher* — it does not require *hechsher*. And critically, as the verse concerns water — *asher yavo mayim al zera* — this exemption from *hechsher* applies specifically to *hechsher mayim* as well.

The *Gemara*'s point becomes clear: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri can now argue with full force. The Rabbis said the reason *machshava* is required for *tumat ochlin* is that *hechsher* is required. But in this case, neither *hechsher sheretz* nor *hechsher mayim* is required. If both criteria have been waived, *machshava* should be waived as well.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* concludes that this is a powerful argument, pressing hard against the Rabbis' position — and proceeds to Rava's resolution.

## Dead Bird & Machshav (II)

### Rava's Answer

Rava — and some say Rav Pappa — resolves the difficulty as follows: *shum tumah chamurah ba-olam, shum tumah kala ba-olam* — we are not speaking of this specific case, but of the generic categories. The generic category of *av ha-tumah* never requires *hechsher*, never requires external transmission, never requires *machshava*. That is the nature of the category. The generic category of *tumat ochlin* requires definitional criteria: contact with liquid, external transmission, and *machshava* to establish identity as food.

The argument, therefore, is this: even though in this specific case we have waived *hechsher* — because the verse teaches that this particular food is exempt from that requirement — the dead bird remains fundamentally a member of the category of *tumat ochlin*. And the one criterion of that category that has *not* been waived is *machshava*. The exemption from *hechsher* is a specific carve-out; it does not dissolve the bird's membership in the category of *tumat ochlin* or abrogate all the criteria of that category. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, by contrast, holds that if two of the three criteria have been waived, the third has been waived as well — if it is theoretically edible, it becomes *tamei* without any further conditions.

The debate thus comes into sharp relief. The Rabbis maintain: each category has its own *halachot*. A dead carcass is an *av* by virtue of what it is — no *hechsher*, no external contact, no *machshava*. To receive *tumah* as food, you need criteria, including *machshava*, and we have not dispensed with that. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri responds: once you have waived the other criteria for this case, consistency demands that you waive *machshava* as well. If it is edible in principle, whether or not one intended to eat it is irrelevant — it becomes *tamei* without any further conditions and renders itself a *rishon*.

## Ma'aser vs Tumah

### Rava's Claim

The *Gemara* now returns to the larger question that preceded this digression: whether *machshava* operates while something is still growing or still alive. Rava states that Rabbi Yochanan — the *amora*, not Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri — would agree *le-inyan ma'aser* that *machshava chibbur shama machshava*: intention counts as effective even while something is still attached to the ground, for purposes of *ma'aser*. This is the link back to our *Mishnah*: since anything obligated in *ma'aser* is by definition a food and therefore subject to *tumat ochlin*, the question now becomes whether something could be in the category of *ma'aser* — because *machshava* while growing works for *ma'aser* — while not yet being in the category of *tumat ochlin*, because *machshava* while growing does not work for *tumah*. Rava holds that this distinction is possible.

Where does Rava derive this distinction? *Mana amina lah* — from where does he draw this claim?

### Savory & Thyme

Rava cites the *Mishnah*: *ha-sha'ah ve-ha-ezov ve-ha-kornit she-be-chatzer* — savory, hyssop, and thyme growing in one's courtyard, not in a field. The context matters: in a courtyard, there is perhaps greater ambiguity about whether these herbs are being cultivated for human use or not. The *Mishnah* states: *im hayu nishmarim* — if they were being guarded and protected, then they are *chayav* in *ma'aser*.

The reason *nishmar* is stipulated is, Rava reasons, because these herbs may have been planted originally for animal fodder. If one then decides to protect them for human use, that constitutes *machshava* even while they are growing, and this intention makes them subject to *ma'aser* — even though an intention formed while something is still growing would not suffice to render it subject to *tumat ochlin*. This, Rava argues, is proof that *machshava* while growing works for *ma'aser* but not for *tumah*.

### Nishmar Definition

Rav Ashi, however, challenges this reading. He argues that the *Mishnah* need not be read as a case of herbs planted for animals that were later redesignated for humans. *Hachi be-chatzer she-einah mishtameret askinan* — we are speaking of herbs that sprouted on their own in a courtyard that is not enclosed or protected. And *stama l'adam kai* — these spices would normally be assumed to be destined for human use; that is not the question. The question is purely whether they are *nishmar* — guarded — or *hefker* — ownerless. If the courtyard protects the produce, it is *nishmar* and *chayav*; if not, people walk through freely and take whatever grows, and it is *hefker* and *patur*.

On this reading, the *Mishnah* says nothing about *machshava* while growing as a means of shifting produce from animal to human use. The word *nishmar* means only that the produce is not ownerless — it is guarded against random taking. Rav Ashi's reading is, as the lecture notes, clearly the more natural understanding of the *Mishnah*.

### Rav Ashi's Proof

Rav Ashi then turns the tables and brings a proof against Rava from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* states: *kol she-chayav be-ma'aser mitamei tumat ochlin* — anything obligated in *ma'aser* can receive *tumah* as foodstuff. If Rava is correct that *machshava* while growing works for *ma'aser* but not for *tumah*, then we could have produce that is *chayav be-ma'aser* — because the owner's intention while it was growing sufficed for *ma'aser* — while it cannot become *tamei* as foodstuff, because the same intention does not suffice for *tumah*. This would violate the *Mishnah*'s rule.

Rava responds: *kol min she-chayav be-ma'aser mitamei tumat ochlin* — read the *Mishnah* as speaking of categories and species (*min*), not of individual circumstances. Any *species* of food that falls within the category of *ma'aser* is the type of thing that can become *tamei* as foodstuff. This does not mean that at the very moment something becomes *chayav be-ma'aser* it simultaneously receives *tumat ochlin*. In the case Rav Ashi raises, the *machshava* while growing made it *chayav be-ma'aser*, but it cannot become *tamei* until a subsequent intention is formed after harvesting. The *Mishnah* speaks of species that are in both categories, not about particular moments or circumstances where the two obligations coincide exactly.

That this reading of the *Mishnah* as referring to species rather than circumstances is correct is supported, Rava argues, by a *Mishnah* we are about to read: *kol she-chayav be-reishit ha-gez chayav be-matanot* — anything obligated in first-shearings is obligated in the priestly gifts of *zero'a, lechayayim, ve-keiva*. It would make no sense to state a general rule about individual circumstances when, as we shall see, there are circumstances where one obligation applies and the other does not.

Rav Ashi then raises a more direct proof for reading the *Mishnah* as speaking of species. The case of one who declares his vineyard *hefker* at night and then rises early in the morning to harvest it before anyone else: such a person remains *chayav* in *peret*, *olelot*, *shichechah*, and *peah* — all the gifts to the poor — but is *patur* from *ma'aser*. This means we have produce that is *chayav* in *peah* but *patur* from *ma'aser* — in apparent violation of the *Mishnah*'s rule that anything *chayav* in *peah* is *chayav* in *ma'aser*. The only way to resolve this is to read the *Mishnah* as speaking of species: a *species* that is in the category of *peah* is the type of species that is in the category of *ma'aser*, but in this unusual circumstance — where *hefker* intervenes — there can be an instance of one without the other. *Shma minah min katani, shma minah* — we see that the *Mishnah* speaks of categories of species, not individual circumstances. This proof satisfies the *Gemara*, and Rava's position is at least defensible, even if not conclusively proven.

## Category Connections

### Shearing & Gifts

The *Mishnah* now introduces a series of general principles about categories of obligation that track one another, presented in the structure of "anything with A has B, but there are things with B that lack A." We have already seen the formulation *kol she-chayav be-reishit ha-gez chayav be-matanot*: anything obligated in the first-shearings given to the *Kohen* — the obligation when one shears one's sheep to give a portion of the shearing — is also obligated in the priestly gifts at slaughter, namely *zero'a, lechayayim, ve-keiva*. But *yesh she-chayav be-matanot ve-eino chayav be-reishit ha-gez* — there are animals that require the priestly gifts but do not require first-shearings. The example is cows: one gives *zero'a, lechayayim, ve-keiva* from cattle, but one does not give *reishit ha-gez* from cattle, as that obligation applies only to sheep.

### Treifah Exception

There is, however, an apparent exception to the rule that anything obligated in *reishit ha-gez* is obligated in *matanot*: the case of a *treifah* — a mortally ill animal. A sheep that is a *treifah* is still subject to *reishit ha-gez*; shearing is wool, and the animal's halachic deficiency does not affect the shearing obligation. But one does not give *matanot* from a *treifah*, since the gifts derive from slaughter and a *treifah* is not a valid slaughtered animal for this purpose.

This apparent exception is addressed by Ravina, who notes that this case reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon. For Rabbi Shimon, a *treifah* is also *patur* from *reishit ha-gez* — *poter* the *tereifah* from *reishit ha-gez* — so on his view, the *treifah* is not an exception: it has neither obligation. Thus the *Mishnah*'s rule is preserved: anything obligated in *reishit ha-gez* is obligated in *matanot*, according to Rabbi Shimon, without exception.

The broader methodological point is established: when the *Mishnah* states that "anything in category A is in category B," it refers to species and types, not to particular circumstances. Within a given species, there can be individual circumstances where one obligation applies but not the other — as in the case of *hefker* produce obligated in *peah* but exempt from *ma'aser* — but that does not undermine the species-level rule.

## Spices Status

### Dill Question

The *Gemara* now takes up a practical question emerging from the foregoing: what is the status of spices as foodstuffs for the purposes of *ma'aser*, *peah*, and *tumat ochlin*? The chain of reasoning runs as follows. A *Mishnah* in tractate *Peah* records that Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis agree that dill — *shaves* — planted in two or three separate locations is treated as separate units, each requiring its own *peah*. From this *Mishnah* it is evident that dill is subject to *peah*. By the rule of our *Mishnah* — anything *chayav* in *peah* is *chayav* in *ma'aser*, and anything *chayav* in *ma'aser* is subject to *tumat ochlin* — it follows that dill is a food and can become *tamei* as foodstuff.

The principle derived is: *alma kol mili de-avid le-ta'ama mitamei tumat ochlin* — anything made for its flavor and used as a spice is considered food and can become *tamei*. This seems initially straightforward.

### Rabbi Akiva

The *Gemara* then raises a contradiction from a *Mishnah* in which a list of spices — including *koshot*, *chimum*, *roshei besamim*, *ta'ah*, *chilfah*, *pilpelin*, and *chalas charia* — are under discussion. Rabbi Akiva holds that these spices may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money, yet they do not receive *tumat ochlin*. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri challenges Rabbi Akiva: you must be consistent. If these spices can be bought with *ma'aser sheni* money, they are food — so why are they not subject to *tumat ochlin*? And if they are not subject to *tumat ochlin* because they are not food, they should not be purchasable with *ma'aser sheni* money either. The two must be linked.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri concludes: *nimnu ve-gamru* — a vote was taken and it was concluded — *she-ein nilkachin be-kesef ma'aser ve-ein mitam'in tumat ochlin*: spices are not food. They cannot be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money and they do not receive *tumat ochlin*. (Rabbi Akiva, it should be noted, was presumably outvoted on this point, given the rule that *halacha ke-Rabbi Akiva me-chaveiro velo me-chaveirav* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Akiva against a single colleague, not against the majority.)

This creates a problem. We established above that dill is subject to *peah*, and therefore *ma'aser*, and therefore *tumat ochlin*. But dill is a spice, and we are now saying spices are not food and are not *tumat ochlin*. How do we reconcile these?

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda resolves the difficulty: *ki tanya hi be-shaves ha-asuyah le-kemach* — the *Mishnah* classifying dill as *peah* and subject to *tumat ochlin* speaks specifically of dill that is being grown and prepared to serve as a *kemach* — that is, made into a sauce or dip. When dill constitutes the food itself — the identity of the dish is defined by the dill-based sauce — then it is a genuine foodstuff and all the obligations of food apply. But when dill is used merely to season a pot of food, it is only a spice enhancing something else, not a food in its own right, and therefore the obligations of food do not apply.

### Pot Seasoning

This distinction is confirmed by a *Mishnah*: *ha-shaves mishe-nitnah ta'am bi-kderah ein bah mishum trumah ve-einah mitamei tumat ochlin* — once dill has given its flavor into the pot, it is no longer *trumah*-relevant and cannot become *tamei* as foodstuff, because once it has been used as seasoning it loses its independent food identity. This *Mishnah* implies that until it is used as seasoning, dill does have food identity. The two categories — dill-as-sauce and dill-as-seasoning — thus explain the apparent contradiction.

## Category Connections (II)

### Scales & Fins

The *Mishnah* continues with another instance of the A-has-B structure: *kol she-yeish lo kaskeset yeish lo snapir* — anything with scales has fins, and anything with horns has split hooves, though the reverse is not always true. The *Gemara* focuses on the scales-and-fins rule. Since, as a practical matter, *anan akaskeset samchinan* — we rely on scales to determine whether a fish is kosher — one might ask why the Torah needs to mention *snapir* (fins) at all. The answer initially offered is: had the Torah written only *kaskeset*, we would not have known what the word meant; by writing both terms, we know each refers to a distinct feature, and from context we identify *kaskeset* as scales and *snapir* as fins.

The *Gemara* then challenges: we know independently that *kaskeset* means scales, since the verse describing Goliath's armor uses the word *shiryon kaskasim* — scale armor. Now that we admit we already know *kaskeset* means scales, why does the Torah still need to write *snapir*? Rabbi Abahu, and similarly the school of Rabbi Yishmael, respond with the verse: *yagdil Torah ve-ya'adir* — the Torah makes itself great and beautiful by stating what could theoretically be inferred. The *Gemara* leaves this without a more analytic resolution.

### Blessings Principle

The *Mishnah* then presents another general principle in the A-has-B structure, this time concerning *brachot*: *kol ha-ta'un berachah l'acharav ta'un berachah l'fanav* — anything that requires a concluding *berachah* requires an opening *berachah*. There is no category of act — whether food or *mitzvah* — that has an ending *berachah* without a preceding *berachah*. But *yesh she-ta'un berachah l'fanav ve-ein ta'un berachah l'acharav* — there are things that have an opening *berachah* without a concluding one.

### Blessing Categories

The *Gemara* asks what falls into this second category. The examples given depend on which opinions one follows. According to the view that vegetables do not require a *berachah acharonah* — since they are not sufficiently sating — vegetables exemplify the category of things with an opening *berachah* only. According to Rabbi Yitzchak, who does recite a *berachah acharonah* on vegetables (as we do in practice), the example would instead be water: water quenches thirst but is not sating, and on this view one recites an opening *berachah* but not a concluding one. According to Rav Papa, who also recites a *berachah acharonah* on water, the example would be *mitzvot*: one recites *birchat ha-mitzvot* before performing a *mitzvah* but not after. According to those in *Eretz Yisrael* who recite a *berachah* even upon removing their *tefillin* — the *berachah* of *lishmor chukav*, derived from the verse *ve-shamarta et ha-chukah ha-zot*, which some apply to *tefillin* and the ongoing obligation of *shmirat tefillin* — even *mitzvot* have both a beginning and ending *berachah*. On that view, the example of things with only an opening *berachah* would be *reichani* — scents. One recites a *berachah* upon smelling something pleasant, but there is no concluding *berachah* for fragrances. *Tosafot* notes that this category of *tefillin* is unique because of the *pesuk* of *shmirah*; all other *mitzvot* have only an opening *berachah*, and the *Gemara* could have cited any of them — but it sought a broader category.

With this, the entire digression concludes, and tomorrow we return to the standard *hilchos niddah*.

## Spices Status (II)

### Rav Kahana

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this analysis to Rav Kahana, who challenged the formulation. Rav Kahana held that one should not say *be-shaves ha-asuyah le-kemach* — that the case of dill as food is a special circumstance of dill designated for a dip — for this would imply that the default assumption is *stama le-kderah*: that ordinarily dill is assumed destined for the pot and therefore only a spice, not a food, and only in exceptional cases is it treated as food.

Rather, says Rav Kahana: *ela stam shaves le-kemach asuyah* — the standard presumption is that dill is made for a dip. Dill is presumed to be a food by default, and only once it has actually been used to season a pot does it lose its food identity. The proof is from the *Mishnah* itself: *ha-shaves mishe-nitnah ta'am bi-kderah ein bah mishum trumah ve-einah mitamei tumat ochlin* — only *after* it gives flavor to the pot does it cease to be food. This implies that *before* it enters the pot, it retains its food status. Were the default assumption that it is destined for the pot, it would already lack food status even before being used. *Ela lav shma minah stam le-kemach asuyah, shma minah* — we see that the default is food status, not seasoning status.

The practical implications of this question are significant: the status of spices with respect to *peah*, *ma'aser*, *ma'aser sheni*, and *tumat ochlin* all depend on whether, for any given spice, we assume it is *stam le-kemach* or *stam le-kderah*.

## Shemittah Laws

### Bi'ur & Shevi'it

The *Mishnah* presents another pair in the A-has-B structure: *kol she-yeish lo bi'ur yeish lo shevi'it* — anything subject to *bi'ur* — the obligation to remove produce from one's home once it is no longer available to animals in the field — is subject to all the laws of *shevi'it*. The logic is that *bi'ur* is itself one of the laws of *shevi'it*, and if that law applies, the full body of *shevi'it* law applies: one must declare the produce *hefker*, one may not conduct business with *peirot shevi'it*, and so on. But *yesh she-yeish lo shevi'it ve-ein lo bi'ur* — there are things subject to *shevi'it* without being subject to *bi'ur*.

### Plant Roots

The example given of things that have *shevi'it* without *bi'ur* is the roots of two specific plants — *ikar ha-luf ha-shoteh* and *ikar ha-dandanah*. The leaves of these plants are subject to *bi'ur*; the roots are not. The reason is derived from the verse governing *bi'ur*: *u-le-vhemtecha ve-la-chayah asher be-artzecha tihyeh chol tevu'atah le-echol* — as long as the wild animals (*chayah*) in the land have access to the produce in the field, one may continue to feed it to one's domesticated animals (*behemah*) at home. Once it is no longer available to the wild animals in the field, one must remove it from the home. The roots of these particular plants, however, are *lo chalu lehu* — they never become unavailable; they are always accessible to animals in the field at all times. Therefore, the condition triggering *bi'ur* never obtains for these roots. This is not a categorical halachic exemption, but a circumstantial one: *bi'ur* simply never becomes applicable to something that is always available in the wild.
