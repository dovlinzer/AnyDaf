# Niddah 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dead Bird & Machshav (II)

### Context & Background

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכוֹת מַחְשָׁבָה לִכְשֶׁיִּתָּלְשׁוּ, קָסָבַר מַחְשֶׁבֶת חִבּוּר לֹא שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **they require intent** to be used for human consumption **once they are detached** from the ground, in order for them to be susceptible to ritual impurity. Rabbi Yoḥanan rules in this manner because he **holds** that **intent** to designate produce while it is **attached** to the ground **is not considered intent.**

Today's *daf* is 51, and we pick up at the bottom of 50b. The discussion has been a digression concerning whether the carcass of a kosher bird — *nevilat of tehorah* — which causes *tumah* when swallowed, requires any preliminary condition before it can itself become *tamei*.

The relevant background principle is as follows. Something that will itself become an *av ha-tumah*, if it is also a type of food, can essentially render itself *tamei* as a *rishon* without needing to come into contact with water or touch anything else — by virtue of its potential to become an *av*. However, for this to occur, the dead bird must be considered food, since the *tumah* it is receiving is the *tumah* of foodstuff. This discussion arose in the context of a broader question: when does *machshava* — intention — define something as food?

The general rule is that *machshava* cannot meaningfully define something as food while that thing is still attached to the ground, or while an animal is still alive, because in those states it cannot yet become *tamei*. The *machshava* cannot be operative where *tumah* is impossible. The proof-case was a bird that required a decision — after its death — that one intended to eat it (or to give it to a non-Jew to eat) before it could become *tamei*. That is the context: *machshava* matters not while something is attached, not while it is alive, but the specific discussion now concerns the role of *machshava* for this dead bird.

### Wine Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּכְרַכִּים, לְמָה לַהּ מַחְשָׁבָה? וְהָתְנַן: נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְנִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר וְהַחֵלֶב בַּכְּרַכִּים — אֵין צְרִיכִין לֹא מַחְשָׁבָה וְלֹא הֶכְשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the case of a young kosher bird that fell into a winepress: **What** type of situation **are we dealing with? If we say** that we are dealing **with cities,** where there are many people available to consume all sorts of food, including the carcass of a kosher bird, **why does it** require **intent? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:3): **An unslaughtered carcass of a kosher animal in any location,** whether the population is large or small, **and the carcass of a kosher bird or the fat** of a kosher animal found **in cities [*bakerakim*], require neither intent** for human consumption **nor** contact with liquid for them to be rendered **susceptible** to impurity?

The *Gemara* had, at the close of yesterday's *daf*, framed the debate in somewhat narrower terms: in *zilgay* — small villages — everyone agrees that *machshava* is required, because carcasses of birds are not normally eaten there. In cities, where there is a larger population with broader tastes, everyone agrees that *machshava* is not required. The point of debate falls in the intermediate case: a city, where ordinarily no *machshava* would be needed, but the bird has fallen into a vat of wine — a circumstance in which it is not so obvious that people would still want to eat it. That is where the question of *machshava* becomes relevant.

### Core Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, אִם מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה — לֹא תְּטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה קַלָּה שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says** in explanation of his opinion: This *halakha* can be derived by the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **If** the carcass of a kosher bird **transmits severe** ritual **impurity,** i.e., it renders one's garments impure when an olive-bulk of it is in the throat, **without thought,** i.e., even if no one had intent that a person should eat it, should it **not transmit a lenient impurity** of food, by touch alone, likewise **without thought?**

This framing, however, does not capture the actual principled dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the Rabbis, and indeed *Tosafot* struggles to understand why the *Gemara* bothered with that framing at all. The core debate is not about whether a bird in a vat of wine is normally eaten. It is a fundamental question of principle: since the carcass of a dead bird is destined to be a source of *tumah*, does it require *machshava* — intention to treat it as food — at all? That is the question the *Gemara* now addresses in earnest.

### Kal Va-Chomer

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri advances a *kal va-chomer*: if this carcass of a dead bird can cause a strict *tumah* — *tumah chamurah* — without intention (for when you swallow it, it is an *av ha-tumah* regardless of whether you planned to eat it or even treated it as food), then certainly it should also be able to cause the lighter *tumah* — *tumah kallah* — namely, to render itself *tamei* as a *rishon*, without requiring intention to treat it as food. Why should *machshava* be needed when the heavier *tumah* proceeds without it?

### Rabbis' Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָהּ יוֹרֶדֶת לְכָךְ, תֹּאמַר בְּטוּמְאָה קַלָּה, שֶׁכֵּן יוֹרֶדֶת לְכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri: **No,** this is not a valid *a fortiori* inference. **If you said** that there is no requirement of intent **with regard to severe** ritual **impurity, that** is because severe ritual impurity **does not assume** its status **with that** requirement of thought, i.e., intent is not relevant to that type of impurity. **Shall you** also **say** that there is no requirement of thought **with regard to lenient impurity, which does assume** its status **with that** requirement of thought?

The Rabbis respond: *lo* — no. The *Gemara* first presents Rava's reading of their counter-argument, which runs as follows: *lo, im amarta betumah chamurah* — true, the severe *tumah* does not require *machshava*, but that is because *tumah chamurah* has a particular weakness: *einah osah kiyotei bah* — it does not replicate itself in kind. The person who becomes *tamei* by touching or swallowing the *av ha-tumah* is a *rishon*, and that *rishon* cannot make another person *tamei*. By contrast, by *tumat ochlin*, food that is a *rishon* does make other food *tamei* as a *sheni*. So perhaps the lighter *tumah* — since it has this additional power of propagation — requires the added condition of *machshava*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: כֹּל דְּכֵן הוּא, וּמָה טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה דְּקִילָּא, דְּאֵינָהּ עוֹשָׂה כַּיּוֹצֵע בָּהּ — מְטַמְּאָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה, טוּמְאָה קַלָּה דַּחֲמִירָא, דְּעוֹשֶׂה כַּיּוֹצֵע בָּהּ — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: But in that case, **all the more so** impurity as food should apply without thought. **If severe impurity,** i.e., the unique impurity of the carcass of a kosher bird, **which is lenient** in the sense **that it does not render** impure another item **similar to it,** nevertheless **imparts impurity without thought,** i.e., without one knowing what is in his throat, then with regard to **lenient impurity,** i.e., the carcass's impurity as food, **which is** more **severe** in the sense **that it renders** impure another item **similar to it, isn't it logical that it** should **impart impurity without thought?**

Abaye immediately objects that this reasoning runs backwards: it is a *kol sheken* in the other direction. *Tumah chamurah* has a weakness — it cannot propagate in kind — yet it proceeds without *machshava*. *Tumah kallah*, which is in some sense weightier in that it does propagate, should therefore all the more so not require *machshava*. Abaye's *kol sheken* effectively dismantles the Rabbis' *hava amina*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר, תֹּאמַר בְּטוּמְאָה קַלָּה שֶׁצְּרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Sheshet said** that **this** is what the Rabbis, who replied to Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, **are saying: No, if you said** that thought is not required **with regard to severe impurity,** i.e., the carcass of a kosher bird in the throat, **as it does not require** contact with a liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity, **shall you** also **say** that thought is not required **with regard to lenient impurity,** i.e., its impurity as food, **where** it **does require** contact with a liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity?

Rav Sheshet then offers the more straightforward reading of the Rabbis' position: the reason the *av ha-tumah* — the severe *tumah* when swallowed — does not require *machshava* is *sheken einah tzerichah hechsher* — because it also does not require *hechsher*. An *av ha-tumah* is intrinsically a source of *tumah* by virtue of what it is; it requires no contact with liquid, no external *tumah* transmitted to it, no defining criteria. Therefore it also requires no *machshava*. By contrast, *tumah kallah* — *tumat ochlin* — does ordinarily require *hechsher*, requires external contact with liquid, and therefore also requires *machshava* to establish the thing's identity as food. The two categories operate under different rules, and no *kal va-chomer* bridges them.

## Hechsher Question

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר? וְהָתְנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּנִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר: צְרִיכָה מַחְשָׁבָה, וְאֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה אֶלָּא בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה, וְאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does** the carcass of a kosher bird **require** contact with a liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to impurity as food? **But didn't we learn** in a *baraita*: **Three matters were stated with regard to the carcass of a kosher bird:** In order for it to be susceptible to impurity as food, it **requires** a person's **intent** to eat it; **and it transmits impurity** to garments by rendering one who swallows it ritually impure **only** when an olive-bulk of it is **in the throat; and it does not require** contact with a liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity?

The *Gemara* is not fully satisfied. It challenges: does this dead bird actually require *hechsher*? The *Mishnah* states explicitly that among the thirteen things said about the carcass of a dead kosher bird, it *requires machshava* and does *not* require contact with liquid — it does not need *hechsher mayim*. If we have already granted that this particular case of *tumat ochlin* does not require *hechsher*, then by parity of reasoning it should not require *machshava* either. The Rabbis' entire defense — that the two categories have different rules — collapses in this case, where *hechsher* has already been waived.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּהֶכְשֵׁר שֶׁרֶץ לָא בָּעֲיָא, הֶכְשֵׁר מַיִם בָּעֲיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Although it does not require** contact **with a creeping animal** for it to be rendered **susceptible** to impurity, i.e., it is inherently impure, nevertheless **it does require** contact with **water** or another liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* initially attempts a technical escape: perhaps the dead bird does not require *hechsher sheretz* — it does not need to conduct *tumah* from an outside source — but it does still require *hechsher mayim*, contact with liquid. And just as it requires liquid to be defined as food, it requires *machshava* to be defined as food.

### Rabbi Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא הֶכְשֵׁר שֶׁרֶץ דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא, כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? הֶכְשֵׁר מַיִם נָמֵי לָא תִּבְעֵי, כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״עַל כׇּל זֶרַע זֵרוּעַ אֲשֶׁר יִזָּרֵעַ״. מָה זְרָעִים, שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, צְרִיכִין הֶכְשֵׁר, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה — צְרִיכִין הֶכְשֵׁר. יָצְתָה נִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר שֶׁסּוֹפָהּ לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about being rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity by contact with **a creeping animal that it does not require** this contact? This ruling is **in accordance with that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught.** If so, it should **also not require** contact with **water** or another liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity, likewise **in accordance with that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught.** **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states that seeds contract impurity from the carcass of a creeping animal only if they first come into contact with water: "And if any part of their carcass fall **upon any sowing seed that is to be sown,** it is pure. But if water be put upon the seed, and any part of their carcass fall thereon, it is impure unto you" (Leviticus 11:37–38). This passage teaches that as long as water did not fall on the seeds, they are not susceptible to ritual impurity. This *halakha* applies to all items similar to seeds. **Accordingly, just as seeds, which will never transmit impurity severe** enough to transmit to humans, like any food, **require** contact with liquid to be rendered **susceptible** to impurity, **so too, all** items **that will never transmit severe impurity require** contact with liquid to be rendered **susceptible** to impurity. This **excludes the carcass of a kosher bird, which will ultimately transmit impurity severe** enough to be transmitted to a human, i.e., when it is in his throat, and therefore **it does not require** contact with liquid for it to be rendered **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* immediately undermines this escape. What is the basis for requiring *hechsher sheretz* from some things and not others? The school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches from the verse *al kol zera zerua asher yizarea* — "on every seed that will be planted." The principle derived is: *ma zraim she-ein sofan letamei tumah chamurah tzrichim hechsher* — just as seeds, which will never themselves be a source of *tumah chamurah*, require *hechsher*, so too does anything that will never become a source of *tumah chamurah* require *hechsher*. Conversely, *yatzta nivlat of tahor* — the carcass of a dead kosher bird is excluded, since *she-sofa letamei tumah chamurah* — it will eventually be an *av ha-tumah* when swallowed. Therefore *she-ein tzricha hechsher* — it does not require *hechsher*. And critically, as the verse concerns water — *asher yavo mayim al zera* — this exemption from *hechsher* applies specifically to *hechsher mayim* as well.

The *Gemara*'s point becomes clear: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri can now argue with full force. The Rabbis said the reason *machshava* is required for *tumat ochlin* is that *hechsher* is required. But in this case, neither *hechsher sheretz* nor *hechsher mayim* is required. If both criteria have been waived, *machshava* should be waived as well.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* concludes that this is a powerful argument, pressing hard against the Rabbis' position — and proceeds to Rava's resolution.

## Dead Bird & Machshav (II)

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב פָּפָּא: שׁוּם טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה בְּעוֹלָם, שׁוּם טוּמְאָה קַלָּה בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said, and some say** that it was **Rav Pappa** who said: The Rabbis' rejection of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri's *a fortiori* inference is not based on the specific *halakha* of impurity of food imparted by the carcass of a bird. Rather, they are referring to the fact that **in general** there is no item under the common **name of severe impurity** that requires contact with a liquid for it to be rendered susceptible to ritual impurity. By contrast, with regard to the common **name of lenient impurity** as food, **in general** the items in this category require contact with a liquid for them to be rendered susceptible to impurity, despite the fact that the particular case of the carcass of a kosher bird is an exception. Therefore, intent is required for lenient impurity even in the case of a carcass of a kosher bird.

Rava — and some say Rav Pappa — resolves the difficulty as follows: *shum tumah chamurah ba-olam, shum tumah kala ba-olam* — we are not speaking of this specific case, but of the generic categories. The generic category of *av ha-tumah* never requires *hechsher*, never requires external transmission, never requires *machshava*. That is the nature of the category. The generic category of *tumat ochlin* requires definitional criteria: contact with liquid, external transmission, and *machshava* to establish identity as food.

The argument, therefore, is this: even though in this specific case we have waived *hechsher* — because the verse teaches that this particular food is exempt from that requirement — the dead bird remains fundamentally a member of the category of *tumat ochlin*. And the one criterion of that category that has *not* been waived is *machshava*. The exemption from *hechsher* is a specific carve-out; it does not dissolve the bird's membership in the category of *tumat ochlin* or abrogate all the criteria of that category. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, by contrast, holds that if two of the three criteria have been waived, the third has been waived as well — if it is theoretically edible, it becomes *tamei* without any further conditions.

The debate thus comes into sharp relief. The Rabbis maintain: each category has its own *halachot*. A dead carcass is an *av* by virtue of what it is — no *hechsher*, no external contact, no *machshava*. To receive *tumah* as food, you need criteria, including *machshava*, and we have not dispensed with that. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri responds: once you have waived the other criteria for this case, consistency demands that you waive *machshava* as well. If it is edible in principle, whether or not one intended to eat it is irrelevant — it becomes *tamei* without any further conditions and renders itself a *rishon*.

## Ma'aser vs Tumah

### Rava's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְעִנְיַן מַעֲשֵׂר, דְּמַחְשֶׁבֶת חִבּוּר שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה. אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן: הַסֵּיאָה וְהָאֵזוֹב וְהַקּוֹרְנִית שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר, אִם הָיוּ נִשְׁמָרִין — חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says: And Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes with regard to** the obligation to **tithe, that intent** while the produce is **attached** to the ground **is considered intent. Rava said: From where do I say** that **this** is the *halakha*? It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 3:9) that deals with the obligation to tithe: **Savory, hyssop [*veha'ezov*], and thyme [*vehakoranit*],** i.e., various types of hyssop plants, that were growing **in a courtyard,** are eaten by some people although they are not specifically intended for human consumption. Therefore, **if they were protected** by the owners, those owners are **obligated** to separate tithes from them.

The *Gemara* now returns to the larger question that preceded this digression: whether *machshava* operates while something is still growing or still alive. Rava states that Rabbi Yochanan — the *amora*, not Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri — would agree *le-inyan ma'aser* that *machshava chibbur shama machshava*: intention counts as effective even while something is still attached to the ground, for purposes of *ma'aser*. This is the link back to our *Mishnah*: since anything obligated in *ma'aser* is by definition a food and therefore subject to *tumat ochlin*, the question now becomes whether something could be in the category of *ma'aser* — because *machshava* while growing works for *ma'aser* — while not yet being in the category of *tumat ochlin*, because *machshava* while growing does not work for *tumah*. Rava holds that this distinction is possible.

Where does Rava derive this distinction? *Mana amina lah* — from where does he draw this claim?

### Savory & Thyme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּזַרְעִינְהוּ מִתְּחִלָּה לָאָדָם — צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּזַרְעִינְהוּ מִתְּחִלָּה לִבְהֵמָה, וְקָתָנֵי: אִם הָיוּ נִשְׁמָרִין — חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the mishna: **What are the circumstances** of this ruling? **If we say** that it is referring to a case **where one initially planted them for human** consumption, does it **need to be said** that there is an obligation to tithe if they are protected by the owners? It is clear that in such a situation the criteria for the obligation to tithe, i.e., that it is human food which is protected, have been met. **Rather,** is it **not** referring to a case **where one initially planted them** to be fed **to an animal,** and nevertheless the mishna **teaches: If** the owner reconsidered their designation and decided to use them for human consumption and **they were protected** by the owners, the owners are **obligated** to tithe them. This apparently indicates that intent while the produce is attached to the ground is considered intent.

Rava cites the *Mishnah*: *ha-sha'ah ve-ha-ezov ve-ha-kornit she-be-chatzer* — savory, hyssop, and thyme growing in one's courtyard, not in a field. The context matters: in a courtyard, there is perhaps greater ambiguity about whether these herbs are being cultivated for human use or not. The *Mishnah* states: *im hayu nishmarim* — if they were being guarded and protected, then they are *chayav* in *ma'aser*.

The reason *nishmar* is stipulated is, Rava reasons, because these herbs may have been planted originally for animal fodder. If one then decides to protect them for human use, that constitutes *machshava* even while they are growing, and this intention makes them subject to *ma'aser* — even though an intention formed while something is still growing would not suffice to render it subject to *tumat ochlin*. This, Rava argues, is proof that *machshava* while growing works for *ma'aser* but not for *tumah*.

### Nishmar Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָכָא בְּחָצֵר שֶׁעָלוּ מֵאֵילֵיהֶן עָסְקִינַן, וּסְתָמָא לְאָדָם קָיְימִי, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם הֶחָצֵר מְשַׁמֶּרֶת פֵּירוֹתֶיהָ — חַיָּיבִין, וְאִם לָאו — פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that Rava's proof is inconclusive, for the following reason: **Here we are dealing with a courtyard where** these various types of hyssop plants **grew by themselves, and** where they are **unspecified,** they are **intended for human** consumption. Therefore, they meet the first criteria for the obligation to tithe, as they are considered food. **And** the mishna addresses the second requirement, that the food must be protected, and **this** is what it **is saying: If the courtyard protects its produce,** the owners **are obligated** in tithes, **and if not, they are exempt.**

Rav Ashi, however, challenges this reading. He argues that the *Mishnah* need not be read as a case of herbs planted for animals that were later redesignated for humans. *Hachi be-chatzer she-einah mishtameret askinan* — we are speaking of herbs that sprouted on their own in a courtyard that is not enclosed or protected. And *stama l'adam kai* — these spices would normally be assumed to be destined for human use; that is not the question. The question is purely whether they are *nishmar* — guarded — or *hefker* — ownerless. If the courtyard protects the produce, it is *nishmar* and *chayav*; if not, people walk through freely and take whatever grows, and it is *hefker* and *patur*.

On this reading, the *Mishnah* says nothing about *machshava* while growing as a means of shifting produce from animal to human use. The word *nishmar* means only that the produce is not ownerless — it is guarded against random taking. Rav Ashi's reading is, as the lecture notes, clearly the more natural understanding of the *Mishnah*.

### Rav Ashi's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אָשֵׁי: ״כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין בַּמַּעַשְׂרוֹת — מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין״, וְאִם אִיתַאּ, הָא אִיכָּא הָנֵי (דְּקָחַיָּיבִין) [דְּחַיָּיבִין] בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וְאֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises** a further **objection** to Rava's explanation from a mishna (50a): **Any** food from **which** one is **obligated to** separate **tithes becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food. But if it is so,** as Rava claimed, that intent while the produce is attached to the ground is considered intent with respect to the obligation to tithe, despite the fact that it is not considered intent with regard to impurity, then the mishna's blanket statement is inaccurate. Rav Ashi elaborates: After all, **there are these** endives that were initially planted in order to feed to an animal and later the owner reconsidered their designation and decided to use them for human consumption, from **which** one is **obligated to** separate **tithe, but which do not become impure with the ritual impurity of food.**

Rav Ashi then turns the tables and brings a proof against Rava from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* states: *kol she-chayav be-ma'aser mitamei tumat ochlin* — anything obligated in *ma'aser* can receive *tumah* as foodstuff. If Rava is correct that *machshava* while growing works for *ma'aser* but not for *tumah*, then we could have produce that is *chayav be-ma'aser* — because the owner's intention while it was growing sufficed for *ma'aser* — while it cannot become *tamei* as foodstuff, because the same intention does not suffice for *tumah*. This would violate the *Mishnah*'s rule.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר — כׇּל מִין שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response that **this** is what the mishna **is saying: Any type** of food from **which** one is **obligated to** separate **tithe becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food.** In other words, this is referring to types of food, not to all situations. It is possible for a particular circumstance to be an exception to this principle.

Rava responds: *kol min she-chayav be-ma'aser mitamei tumat ochlin* — read the *Mishnah* as speaking of categories and species (*min*), not of individual circumstances. Any *species* of food that falls within the category of *ma'aser* is the type of thing that can become *tamei* as foodstuff. This does not mean that at the very moment something becomes *chayav be-ma'aser* it simultaneously receives *tumat ochlin*. In the case Rav Ashi raises, the *machshava* while growing made it *chayav be-ma'aser*, but it cannot become *tamei* until a subsequent intention is formed after harvesting. The *Mishnah* speaks of species that are in both categories, not about particular moments or circumstances where the two obligations coincide exactly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז — חַיָּיב בַּמַּתָּנוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁחַיָּיב בַּמַּתָּנוֹת וְאֵין חַיָּיב בְּרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that **this too stands to reason,** that the mishna is referring only to types of food, not situations, **from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches** (51b): With regard to **any** animal from **which** the owner is **obligated to** give **the first shearing** of its wool, he is **obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it, i.e., the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, if it was slaughtered. **And there are** animals from **which** one is **obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it, **but** from which he is **not obligated to** give **the first shearing.**

That this reading of the *Mishnah* as referring to species rather than circumstances is correct is supported, Rava argues, by a *Mishnah* we are about to read: *kol she-chayav be-reishit ha-gez chayav be-matanot* — anything obligated in first-shearings is obligated in the priestly gifts of *zero'a, lechayayim, ve-keiva*. It would make no sense to state a general rule about individual circumstances when, as we shall see, there are circumstances where one obligation applies and the other does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, הָאִיכָּא טְרֵפָה, דְּחַיֶּיבֶת בְּרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז וְאֵינָהּ חַיֶּיבֶת בַּמַּתָּנוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so,** that the principles of the mishna are all-inclusive, one can raise the following difficulty: **But there is** the specific case of **an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** from **which** one is **obligated to** give **the first shearing, but one is not obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it, as it may not be eaten. This proves that Rava is correct, that the mishna is not referring to all circumstances, but only to general categories.

Rav Ashi then raises a more direct proof for reading the *Mishnah* as speaking of species. The case of one who declares his vineyard *hefker* at night and then rises early in the morning to harvest it before anyone else: such a person remains *chayav* in *peret*, *olelot*, *shichechah*, and *peah* — all the gifts to the poor — but is *patur* from *ma'aser*. This means we have produce that is *chayav* in *peah* but *patur* from *ma'aser* — in apparent violation of the *Mishnah*'s rule that anything *chayav* in *peah* is *chayav* in *ma'aser*. The only way to resolve this is to read the *Mishnah* as speaking of species: a *species* that is in the category of *peah* is the type of species that is in the category of *ma'aser*, but in this unusual circumstance — where *hefker* intervenes — there can be an instance of one without the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה מֵרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said,** rejecting this proof: It is possible that the mishna is referring to all circumstances, and as for the difficulty with regard to a *tereifa*, one can say that in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as we learned** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon exempts the** owner of **a *tereifa* from** the mitzva of **the first shearing.**

*Shma minah min katani, shma minah* — we see that the *Mishnah* speaks of categories of species, not individual circumstances. This proof satisfies the *Gemara*, and Rava's position is at least defensible, even if not conclusively proven.

## Category Connections

### Shearing & Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז — חַיָּיב בַּמַּתָּנוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁחַיָּיב בַּמַּתָּנוֹת וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **any** animal, i.e., sheep and rams, from **which one is obligated** by Torah law (see Deuteronomy 18:4) **to** give **the first shearing** of its wool to a priest, he **is obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood, i.e., the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, which must be removed from slaughtered animals, taken from it (see Deuteronomy 18:3). **And there are** animals from **which one is obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from them, e.g., cattle and goats, **but** from which **he is not obligated to** give **the first shearing.**

The *Mishnah* now introduces a series of general principles about categories of obligation that track one another, presented in the structure of "anything with A has B, but there are things with B that lack A." We have already seen the formulation *kol she-chayav be-reishit ha-gez chayav be-matanot*: anything obligated in the first-shearings given to the *Kohen* — the obligation when one shears one's sheep to give a portion of the shearing — is also obligated in the priestly gifts at slaughter, namely *zero'a, lechayayim, ve-keiva*. But *yesh she-chayav be-matanot ve-eino chayav be-reishit ha-gez* — there are animals that require the priestly gifts but do not require first-shearings. The example is cows: one gives *zero'a, lechayayim, ve-keiva* from cattle, but one does not give *reishit ha-gez* from cattle, as that obligation applies only to sheep.

### Treifah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, הָאִיכָּא טְרֵפָה, דְּחַיֶּיבֶת בְּרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז וְאֵינָהּ חַיֶּיבֶת בַּמַּתָּנוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so,** that the principles of the mishna are all-inclusive, one can raise the following difficulty: **But there is** the specific case of **an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** from **which** one is **obligated to** give **the first shearing, but one is not obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it, as it may not be eaten. This proves that Rava is correct, that the mishna is not referring to all circumstances, but only to general categories.

There is, however, an apparent exception to the rule that anything obligated in *reishit ha-gez* is obligated in *matanot*: the case of a *treifah* — a mortally ill animal. A sheep that is a *treifah* is still subject to *reishit ha-gez*; shearing is wool, and the animal's halachic deficiency does not affect the shearing obligation. But one does not give *matanot* from a *treifah*, since the gifts derive from slaughter and a *treifah* is not a valid slaughtered animal for this purpose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה מֵרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said,** rejecting this proof: It is possible that the mishna is referring to all circumstances, and as for the difficulty with regard to a *tereifa*, one can say that in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as we learned** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon exempts the** owner of **a *tereifa* from** the mitzva of **the first shearing.**

This apparent exception is addressed by Ravina, who notes that this case reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon. For Rabbi Shimon, a *treifah* is also *patur* from *reishit ha-gez* — *poter* the *tereifah* from *reishit ha-gez* — so on his view, the *treifah* is not an exception: it has neither obligation. Thus the *Mishnah*'s rule is preserved: anything obligated in *reishit ha-gez* is obligated in *matanot*, according to Rabbi Shimon, without exception.

The broader methodological point is established: when the *Mishnah* states that "anything in category A is in category B," it refers to species and types, not to particular circumstances. Within a given species, there can be individual circumstances where one obligation applies but not the other — as in the case of *hefker* produce obligated in *peah* but exempt from *ma'aser* — but that does not undermine the species-level rule.

## Spices Status

### Dill Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּחַיָּיב בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר — מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין, אַלְמָא: כֹּל מִילֵּי דַּעֲבִיד לְטַעְמָא — מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין, דְּהַאי שֶׁבֶת לְטַעְמָא עֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And from** the fact that with regard to dill one is **obligated to** separate **tithe,** it follows that it **becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food.** As the mishna on 50a states: Any food that is obligated in tithes becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food. **Apparently, any item that is prepared** in order **to** add **taste** to food, such as dill, **becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food, as this dill is prepared** in order **to** add **taste** to food.

The *Gemara* now takes up a practical question emerging from the foregoing: what is the status of spices as foodstuffs for the purposes of *ma'aser*, *peah*, and *tumat ochlin*? The chain of reasoning runs as follows. A *Mishnah* in tractate *Peah* records that Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis agree that dill — *shaves* — planted in two or three separate locations is treated as separate units, each requiring its own *peah*. From this *Mishnah* it is evident that dill is subject to *peah*. By the rule of our *Mishnah* — anything *chayav* in *peah* is *chayav* in *ma'aser*, and anything *chayav* in *ma'aser* is subject to *tumat ochlin* — it follows that dill is a food and can become *tamei* as foodstuff.

The principle derived is: *alma kol mili de-avid le-ta'ama mitamei tumat ochlin* — anything made for its flavor and used as a spice is considered food and can become *tamei*. This seems initially straightforward.

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הַקּוֹשְׁטְ, וְהַחִימּוּם, וְרָאשֵׁי בְשָׂמִים, וְהַתִּיאָה, וְהַחִלְתִּית, וְהַפִּלְפְּלִים, וְחַלַּת חָרִיעַ — נִקָּחִין בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this conclusion from a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:5), which deals with the ritual impurity of food: With regard to spices such as **costus, amomum, chief spices, root of crowfoot, asafoetida, peppers, and a cake of safflower,** although their function is merely to add taste to food, they are considered food for the purposes of the following *halakha*: They **may be bought with** second-**tithe money,** which must be taken to Jerusalem and used to purchase food. **But** they are not considered food insofar as **they do not become impure with the ritual impurity of food.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The *Gemara* then raises a contradiction from a *Mishnah* in which a list of spices — including *koshot*, *chimum*, *roshei besamim*, *ta'ah*, *chilfah*, *pilpelin*, and *chalas charia* — are under discussion. Rabbi Akiva holds that these spices may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money, yet they do not receive *tumat ochlin*. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri challenges Rabbi Akiva: you must be consistent. If these spices can be bought with *ma'aser sheni* money, they are food — so why are they not subject to *tumat ochlin*? And if they are not subject to *tumat ochlin* because they are not food, they should not be purchasable with *ma'aser sheni* money either. The two must be linked.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: אִם נִקָּחִין בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין? וְאִם אֵינָן מִטַּמְּאִין, אַף הֵם לֹא יִלָּקְחוּ בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said to** Rabbi Akiva: **If** they are considered food to the extent that **they may be bought with** second-**tithe money, for what** reason are **they not** considered food in terms of **becoming impure with the ritual impurity of food? And if they do not become impure** with the ritual impurity of food because they are not considered food, then **they should also not be bought with** second-**tithe money.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: נִמְנוּ וְגָמְרוּ שֶׁאֵין נִקָּחִין בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said** with regard to this *halakha*: **They counted** the opinions of the Sages, **and they concluded that** these spices **may not be bought with** second-**tithe money, and** they do **not become impure** with the ritual **impurity of food.** This apparently contradicts the previous claim that dill, which is a spice, becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri concludes: *nimnu ve-gamru* — a vote was taken and it was concluded — *she-ein nilkachin be-kesef ma'aser ve-ein mitam'in tumat ochlin*: spices are not food. They cannot be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money and they do not receive *tumat ochlin*. (Rabbi Akiva, it should be noted, was presumably outvoted on this point, given the rule that *halacha ke-Rabbi Akiva me-chaveiro velo me-chaveirav* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Akiva against a single colleague, not against the majority.)

This creates a problem. We established above that dill is subject to *peah*, and therefore *ma'aser*, and therefore *tumat ochlin*. But dill is a spice, and we are now saying spices are not food and are not *tumat ochlin*. How do we reconcile these?

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כִּי תַנְיָא הַהִיא — בְּשֶׁבֶת הָעֲשׂוּיָה לְכָמָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says** the following resolution of the difficulty: **When that** *baraita*, which indicates that dill is considered food and can contract the impurity of food, **is taught,** it is referring **to dill that is prepared for a spice dish [*likhmakh*],** i.e., to be ground and placed in a Babylonian spice, *kutaḥ*, which is used as a dip.

Rav Chisda resolves the difficulty: *ki tanya hi be-shaves ha-asuyah le-kemach* — the *Mishnah* classifying dill as *peah* and subject to *tumat ochlin* speaks specifically of dill that is being grown and prepared to serve as a *kemach* — that is, made into a sauce or dip. When dill constitutes the food itself — the identity of the dish is defined by the dill-based sauce — then it is a genuine foodstuff and all the obligations of food apply. But when dill is used merely to season a pot of food, it is only a spice enhancing something else, not a food in its own right, and therefore the obligations of food do not apply.

### Pot Seasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ סְתָמָא לִקְדֵרָה — כִּי לֹא נָתְנָה נָמֵי סְתָמָא לִקְדֵרָה! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סְתָמָא לְכָמָךְ עֲשׂוּיָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana explains the proof: It can be inferred from this mishna that **until** the dill has **imparted flavor in a pot** of food it **is subject to** the prohibition of a non-priest partaking of ***teruma*, and it can become impure with the ritual impurity of food. And if** it **enters your mind** that **undesignated** dill is intended as an ingredient **in a pot** of food, then **even when** one did **not place** the dill in a pot, the same *halakha* with respect to *teruma* and impurity should apply, as when it is **undesignated** the dill is intended as an ingredient **in a pot** of food. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** the mishna that **undesignated** dill is **prepared for a spice dish?** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** the mishna that this is the case.

This *Mishnah* implies that until it is used as seasoning, dill does have food identity. The two categories — dill-as-sauce and dill-as-seasoning — thus explain the apparent contradiction.

## Category Connections (II)

### Scales & Fins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קַשְׂקֶשֶׂת — יֵשׁ לוֹ סְנַפִּיר, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ סְנַפִּיר וְאֵין לוֹ קַשְׂקֶשֶׂת. כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קַרְנַיִם — יֵשׁ לוֹ טְלָפַיִם, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ טְלָפַיִם וְאֵין לוֹ קַרְנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is written: "Whatever has fins and scales in the waters, in the seas, and in the rivers, you may eat them" (Leviticus 11:9). There is a principle with regard to the signs indicating that fish are kosher: **Any** fish **that has scales has fins; and there are** fish **that have fins but do not have scales.** Similarly, with regard to kosher animals it is written: "Whatever parts the hoof, and is wholly cloven-footed, and chews the cud, among the beasts, that you may eat" (Leviticus 11:3). **Any** animal **that has horns has hooves; and there are** animals **that have hooves but do not have horns.**

The *Mishnah* continues with another instance of the A-has-B structure: *kol she-yeish lo kaskeset yeish lo snapir* — anything with scales has fins, and anything with horns has split hooves, though the reverse is not always true. The *Gemara* focuses on the scales-and-fins rule. Since, as a practical matter, *anan akaskeset samchinan* — we rely on scales to determine whether a fish is kosher — one might ask why the Torah needs to mention *snapir* (fins) at all. The answer initially offered is: had the Torah written only *kaskeset*, we would not have known what the word meant; by writing both terms, we know each refers to a distinct feature, and from context we identify *kaskeset* as scales and *snapir* as fins.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״קַשְׂקֶשֶׂת״ וְלָא בָּעֵי סְנַפִּיר! אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״יַגְדִּיל תּוֹרָה וְיַאְדִּיר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if there is proof that *kaskeset* means scales, the question returns: **Let the Merciful One write** only **"scales" and** then there would be **no need** to write **"fins." Rabbi Abbahu says, and likewise** a Sage of **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught,** that this is in accordance with the verse: "The Lord was pleased, for His righteousness' sake, **to make Torah great and glorious"** (Isaiah 42:21).In this context, this means that it is fitting for the Torah to state all the characteristics of a kosher animal rather than merely state that which is absolutely necessary.

The *Gemara* then challenges: we know independently that *kaskeset* means scales, since the verse describing Goliath's armor uses the word *shiryon kaskasim* — scale armor. Now that we admit we already know *kaskeset* means scales, why does the Torah still need to write *snapir*? Rabbi Abahu, and similarly the school of Rabbi Yishmael, respond with the verse: *yagdil Torah ve-ya'adir* — the Torah makes itself great and beautiful by stating what could theoretically be inferred. The *Gemara* leaves this without a more analytic resolution.

### Blessings Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' כֹּל הַטָּעוּן בְּרָכָה לְאַחֲרָיו — טָעוּן בְּרָכָה לְפָנָיו, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁטָּעוּן בְּרָכָה לְפָנָיו וְאֵין טָעוּן בְּרָכָה לְאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna teaches a generalization that is similar to the previous ones: **Anything that requires a blessing after** one partakes of **it requires a blessing beforehand. And there exist** items **that require a blessing before but** do **not require a blessing thereafter.**

The *Mishnah* then presents another general principle in the A-has-B structure, this time concerning *brachot*: *kol ha-ta'un berachah l'acharav ta'un berachah l'fanav* — anything that requires a concluding *berachah* requires an opening *berachah*. There is no category of act — whether food or *mitzvah* — that has an ending *berachah* without a preceding *berachah*. But *yesh she-ta'un berachah l'fanav ve-ein ta'un berachah l'acharav* — there are things that have an opening *berachah* without a concluding one.

### Blessing Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי יָרָק. וּלְרַבִּי יִצְחָק דִּמְבָרֵךְ אַיָּרָק, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַיָּא. וּלְרַב פָּפָּא דִּמְבָרֵךְ אַמַּיָּא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי מִצְוֹת. וְלִבְנֵי מַעְרְבָא דִּמְבָרְכִי בָּתַר דְּסַלִּיקוּ תְּפִילַּיְיהוּ ״אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לִשְׁמוֹר חוּקָּיו״, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי רֵיחָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that there are items that require a blessing before but not after. The Gemara inquires: **What** case does this *halakha* in the mishna **add?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add** the case of **vegetables,** as one recites a blessing before eating them but not afterward. The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, who** maintains that one **recites a blessing on vegetables** after eating them, **what** case does this *halakha* in the mishna **add?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add** the case of **water,** as one recites a blessing before drinking it but not afterward. **And according to** the opinion of **Rav Pappa, who** rules that **one recites a blessing on water** after drinking it, **what** case does this *halakha* in the mishna serve **to add?** The Gemara answers that the mishna, which does not explicitly mention food, serves **to add mitzvot.** In other words, one recites a blessing before performing a mitzva, e.g., wearing ritual fringes or taking the *lulav* and the like, but one does not recite a blessing after its fulfillment. The Gemara asks: **And according to** the **residents of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **who recite** the following **blessing after they remove their phylacteries: Who sanctified us with His mitzvot and commanded us to keep His laws, what** does this *halakha* in the mishna **come to add?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add** the case of fragrant **spices.**

The *Gemara* asks what falls into this second category. The examples given depend on which opinions one follows. According to the view that vegetables do not require a *berachah acharonah* — since they are not sufficiently sating — vegetables exemplify the category of things with an opening *berachah* only. According to Rabbi Yitzchak, who does recite a *berachah acharonah* on vegetables (as we do in practice), the example would instead be water: water quenches thirst but is not sating, and on this view one recites an opening *berachah* but not a concluding one. According to Rav Papa, who also recites a *berachah acharonah* on water, the example would be *mitzvot*: one recites *birchat ha-mitzvot* before performing a *mitzvah* but not after. According to those in *Eretz Yisrael* who recite a *berachah* even upon removing their *tefillin* — the *berachah* of *lishmor chukav*, derived from the verse *ve-shamarta et ha-chukah ha-zot*, which some apply to *tefillin* and the ongoing obligation of *shmirat tefillin* — even *mitzvot* have both a beginning and ending *berachah*. On that view, the example of things with only an opening *berachah* would be *reichani* — scents. One recites a *berachah* upon smelling something pleasant, but there is no concluding *berachah* for fragrances. *Tosafot* notes that this category of *tefillin* is unique because of the *pesuk* of *shmirah*; all other *mitzvot* have only an opening *berachah*, and the *Gemara* could have cited any of them — but it sought a broader category.

With this, the entire digression concludes, and tomorrow we return to the standard *hilchos niddah*.

## Spices Status (II)

### Rav Kahana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּיה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, (אֲמַר): לָא תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁבֶת הָעֲשׂוּיָה לְכָמָךְ, הָא סְתָמָא לִקְדֵרָה, אֶלָּא סְתָם שֶׁבֶת לְכָמָךְ עֲשׂוּיָה, דִּתְנַן: הַשֶּׁבֶת מִשֶּׁנָּתְנָה טַעַם בַּקְּדֵרָה — אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם תְּרוּמָה, וְאֵינָהּ מִטַּמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I said this *halakha* of** Rav Ḥisda's **before Rav Kahana,** and he commented: **Do not say** that the *baraita* is referring specifically to a case **where** the **dill** was prepared **for a spice dish** from the outset, which would indicate that if dill is **undesignated** then it is intended to be an ingredient **in a pot** of food. **Rather, undesignated dill** is also **prepared for a spice dish. As we learned** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:4): With regard to *teruma* **dill, once it imparted flavor in a pot** of food and was removed from the pot, it **is no** longer **subject to** the prohibition of a non-priest partaking of ***teruma*, and it can no** longer **become impure with the ritual impurity of food.**

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this analysis to Rav Kahana, who challenged the formulation. Rav Kahana held that one should not say *be-shaves ha-asuyah le-kemach* — that the case of dill as food is a special circumstance of dill designated for a dip — for this would imply that the default assumption is *stama le-kderah*: that ordinarily dill is assumed destined for the pot and therefore only a spice, not a food, and only in exceptional cases is it treated as food.

Rather, says Rav Kahana: *ela stam shaves le-kemach asuyah* — the standard presumption is that dill is made for a dip. Dill is presumed to be a food by default, and only once it has actually been used to season a pot does it lose its food identity. The proof is from the *Mishnah* itself: *ha-shaves mishe-nitnah ta'am bi-kderah ein bah mishum trumah ve-einah mitamei tumat ochlin* — only *after* it gives flavor to the pot does it cease to be food. This implies that *before* it enters the pot, it retains its food status. Were the default assumption that it is destined for the pot, it would already lack food status even before being used. *Ela lav shma minah stam le-kemach asuyah, shma minah* — we see that the default is food status, not seasoning status.

The practical implications of this question are significant: the status of spices with respect to *peah*, *ma'aser*, *ma'aser sheni*, and *tumat ochlin* all depend on whether, for any given spice, we assume it is *stam le-kemach* or *stam le-kderah*.

## Shemittah Laws

### Bi'ur & Shevi'it

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בִּיעוּר — יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁבִיעִית, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁבִיעִית — וְאֵין לוֹ בִּיעוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches a similar principle: For **all** Sabbatical-Year produce **to which there** applies the obligation of **eradication** from the house when it ceases to be available to the animals in the field, **there is** the sanctity of **Sabbatical-**Year produce upon **it,** i.e., it may not be used for commerce and is ownerless while it is attached to the ground. **And there** is produce **for which there is** the sanctity of **Sabbatical-**Year produce, **but for** which **there is no** obligation of **eradication** from the house, e.g., produce that is preserved in the ground and does not cease to be available in the field.

The *Mishnah* presents another pair in the A-has-B structure: *kol she-yeish lo bi'ur yeish lo shevi'it* — anything subject to *bi'ur* — the obligation to remove produce from one's home once it is no longer available to animals in the field — is subject to all the laws of *shevi'it*. The logic is that *bi'ur* is itself one of the laws of *shevi'it*, and if that law applies, the full body of *shevi'it* law applies: one must declare the produce *hefker*, one may not conduct business with *peirot shevi'it*, and so on. But *yesh she-yeish lo shevi'it ve-ein lo bi'ur* — there are things subject to *shevi'it* without being subject to *bi'ur*.

### Plant Roots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן עֲלֵה הַלּוּף שׁוֹטֶה וְהַדַּנְדַּנָּה, יֵשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁבִיעִית וְאֵין לוֹ בִּיעוּר, עִיקַּר הַלּוּף שׁוֹטֶה וְעִיקַּר הַדַּנְדַּנָּה. דִּכְתִיב ״וְלִבְהֶמְתְּךָ וְלַחַיָּה אֲשֶׁר בְּאַרְצֶךָ תִּהְיֶה כׇל תְּבוּאָתָהּ לֶאֱכֹל״, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁחַיָּה אוֹכֶלֶת מִן הַשָּׂדֶה — אַתָּה מַאֲכִיל לִבְהֶמְתֶּךָ בַּבַּיִת, כָּלָה לַחַיָּה מִן הַשָּׂדֶה — כַּלֵּה לִבְהֶמְתְּךָ שֶׁבַּבַּיִת, וְהָנֵי לָא כָּלוּ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the sanctity of Sabbatical-Year produce applies to any produce upon which there is an obligation of eradication, but the converse is not necessarily the case. The Gemara cites an example of plants whose various parts illustrate these *halakhot*: Plants **such as the wild arum leaf and the ceterach,** which cease to be available in the field during the rainy season, are subject to eradication and to the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year. Examples of the second *halakha* of the mishna, that **there is** produce **for which there is** the sanctity of **Sabbatical-**Year produce **but** for which **there is no** obligation of **eradication** from the house, include the **root of the wild arum and the root of the ceterach.** The Gemara explains **that it is written** in connection to the Sabbatical Year: **"And for the cattle and the beasts that are in your land, all its produce may be eaten"** (Leviticus 25:7), from which it is derived: **As long as the undomesticated animals eat** a type of produce **from the field, you may feed** that type of produce **to your domesticated animal in the house,** as it still remains in the field. But if that type of produce **has ceased for the undomesticated animals in the field, you must cease** feeding it **to your domesticated animal in the house.** This is the obligation of eradication. **And these,** the root of the wild arum and the ceterach, **have not ceased for undomesticated animals** in the field, and therefore there is no obligation of eradication.

The example given of things that have *shevi'it* without *bi'ur* is the roots of two specific plants — *ikar ha-luf ha-shoteh* and *ikar ha-dandanah*. The leaves of these plants are subject to *bi'ur*; the roots are not. The reason is derived from the verse governing *bi'ur*: *u-le-vhemtecha ve-la-chayah asher be-artzecha tihyeh chol tevu'atah le-echol* — as long as the wild animals (*chayah*) in the land have access to the produce in the field, one may continue to feed it to one's domesticated animals (*behemah*) at home. Once it is no longer available to the wild animals in the field, one must remove it from the home. The roots of these particular plants, however, are *lo chalu lehu* — they never become unavailable; they are always accessible to animals in the field at all times. Therefore, the condition triggering *bi'ur* never obtains for these roots. This is not a categorical halachic exemption, but a circumstantial one: *bi'ur* simply never becomes applicable to something that is always available in the wild.
