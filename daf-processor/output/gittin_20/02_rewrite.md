# Gittin 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blank Paper & Invisible…

### Shmuel's Blank Paper

The daf opens, technically, toward the end of Gittin 19b, roughly fifteen lines before the lines widen on the page. There, *Shmuel* presents a striking ruling: if a man gave his wife a blank piece of paper (*neyar chalak*), declared "this is your *get*," she is considered *megureshet* — divorced. The reason? *Chashishinan shema be'mei milin katava* — we are concerned that he may have written it in *mei milin*, water in which gallnuts had been steeped, a substance that produces a mark initially visible but prone to disappearing entirely.

This is a genuinely shocking proposition. The document is, by all appearances, blank. Why should there be any concern, and on what basis would such a claim be entertained? The *Gemara* does not immediately address those deeper questions; instead it first confronts a textual challenge from a *beraita*.

### Beraita Challenge

*Meitivei* — the *Gemara* raises a difficulty from a *beraita*: a man gives his wife a *get* saying *harei zeh gittech*, and she, in her anger, hurls it into the sea, or into fire, or destroys it in any manner. He then claims it was never a genuine *get* at all — it was a *shtar pasim*, a document he carried to project an appearance of wealth, or a *shtar amanah*, a pre-signed IOU before actual lending — in short, some other kind of document, not a bill of divorce. The *beraita* rules: *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed. Once he declared it a *get*, he cannot retroactively render her forbidden to remarry.

The *Gemara* draws an inference: *ta'ama de'ika ktav* — the reason the *beraita* works is that there *was* writing on the document, and his claim now contradicts that prior declaration. But the implication is sharp: *ha leka ktav* — had the document been blank, the outcome would presumably be different, and we would not consider the wife divorced. If *Shmuel*'s concern were correct — that even a blank document might constitute a *get* — the *beraita* would have taught the case of a blank document, where the *chiddush* would be even greater. The fact that it did not suggests that a blank document carries no such concern.

### Shmuel & Beraita

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *hacha bamai askinan de'badkinan leh be'maya de'nara* — the *beraita* actually deals with a case where the document was subsequently processed with some other liquid that brought out previously invisible writing. When examined with this reagent, it became clear that something had in fact been written. So the *beraita*'s case was never truly blank; it always contained writing, merely invisible, and the processing revealed it.

But this resolution immediately softens the radicalism of *Shmuel*'s ruling. If the invisible writing can be made visible through processing, then we are no longer dealing with a document that is *entirely* blank — we now have actual evidence that something was written. The *Gemara* presses further: *ve'hashta de'pilit mai havei hashta hu de'pilit* — even if the writing emerges now under processing, what does that accomplish? At the moment the *get* was handed over, it was blank. We have established throughout the *mishna*'s discussion that writing must be visible to function as a valid *get*. What does it matter that it becomes visible only now?

The *Gemara* answers: *Shmuel nami chashinan ka'amar* — *Shmuel* too only means that we are *concerned*, not that she is definitively divorced. The concern is that the writing may have been visible at the precise moment of transfer and disappeared only afterward. *Tosafot* clarifies: had witnesses observed the document to be blank at the very moment of handover, there would be no concern at all, not even a *safek megureshet*. The case *Shmuel* contemplates is one where the document was examined only later and found to be blank, with no contemporaneous observation at the moment of transfer.

### Processing Invisible Ink

Building on this, the *Gemara*'s conclusion is that a completely blank document about which nothing further can be demonstrated raises no concern. The *beraita* at most suggested concern when there was writing. But where processing reveals that the document *had* been written in a disappearing ink, then *Shmuel* holds that there is at least a *safek megureshet* — for perhaps the writing was visible when the *get* was handed over. *I pali pali, ve'i lo pali lo*: if the processing reveals the writing, there is a concern; if nothing emerges, there is none.

## Witnesses Reading Get

### Reading Before Handing

The *Gemara* now cites *Ameimar* transmitting in the name of *Mareimar* in the name of *Rav Dimi*: *hani bei trei de'yahavi gita kamaihu tzerichinu lemikrei* — when two witnesses are present at the handing of a *get*, they are required to read it beforehand. This makes abundant sense. According to the view that *edei mesirah karsi* — the witnesses to the delivery are the operative legal witnesses — they must know what it is they are observing, for it is their observation that constitutes the testimony to the *get*'s content. And even according to *edei chasima karsi*, where the signed witnesses are primary, reading beforehand at least addresses the concerns already raised about invisible ink and disputed claims.

### Beraita Without Reading

*Meitivei* — the *Gemara* challenges this from the very same *beraita* cited earlier: a man gives a *get*, the woman destroys it, he claims it was something else, and the rule is *eino ne'eman*. But if witnesses had read the *get* beforehand, how could he possibly make such a claim? *Batar de'karyei mi matzei amar lei hachi* — once they have read it, his assertion that it was a different type of document is instantly refuted. The fact that the *beraita* must teach the ruling at all implies that no such reading had taken place.

### Resolution & Pockets

The *Gemara* resolves this: *lo tzericha de'batar de'karyei ayeilu le'vei yadei ve'afkeih* — the witnesses did read it, but after reading it, the husband put it back into his jacket pocket and withdrew it again before handing it over. His claim is not merely that the document was something else from the start, but that he performed an actual switch — he palmed a different document when he reached into his pocket. The *beraita* therefore teaches *kama mashma lan* that we are not concerned with such a claim, even if technically conceivable. The witnesses read it, they saw it handed over, and his assertion of a switcheroo is not believed.

### Tosafot Lechatchila Deba…

*Tosafot* raises an important question from this sugya. Why did the *Gemara* not simply explain the *beraita* as a *bedieved* case — one where the witnesses had failed to read it, the *get* is nonetheless valid *bedieved*, and the *beraita* therefore must address the consequent claim that it was a different document? The fact that the *Gemara* did not take that route implies that even *bedieved*, if the witnesses did not read the *get*, it is invalid. This is a highly practical question: if witnesses failed to read the *get* at the time of delivery, must one treat the divorce as ineffective even after the fact?

*Tosafot* works through the back and forth without settling it in a simple formula. What the *Gemara* does clearly establish is that the requirement to read is at least *lechatchila*, and there are strong grounds to view it as operative even *bedieved*.

### Reading Before & After

The final line of that *Tosafot* is worth highlighting. *Tosafot* records that the *Ri* had a standard practice: the witnesses would read the *get* both before the delivery and after — before, because the *Gemara* makes clear that the primary obligation is prior to handing, so that at the moment of transfer the witnesses know what they are observing; and after, to foreclose any claim that a switch had been made in the interval between reading and delivery, precisely the concern raised by the *Gemara*'s resolution.

*Tosafot* adds: it is *pshita* — obvious — that even if one holds that reading is necessary *bedieved*, if the witnesses read the document not before but after handing, and the woman returned it to them to examine, and they confirmed it was a *get*, that would suffice. For any evidentiary concern is resolved: there is no possibility of a pocket switch, and they can clearly testify to what was transferred. One might argue it is not so *pashut* — if the legal moment of transfer is the handing itself, and at that instant the witnesses had not yet verified the contents, perhaps their subsequent reading cannot retroactively complete the act. This touches broader questions about combining observations across time, analogous to debates about whether witnesses who observed a ring on a woman's finger after it was placed, having not directly seen the placement, can combine those perceptions. These remain significant questions in the laws of *gittin*.

## Found Document Cases

### Mezuza Case

The *Gemara* now presents a series of found-document cases that build on the earlier discussion. *Hahu gavra de'zarak leh gita le'deveiteih le'veini danei* — a man threw a *get* to his wife, and it landed among stacked barrels of wine in her space. When they went to retrieve it, they found not a *get* but a *mezuza*. The dimensions of a *mezuza* are, apparently, close enough to those of a *get* in this period to raise the question. Is this the very document he threw her, meaning he actually handed her a *mezuza* while claiming it was a *get* — perhaps withdrawing the real *get* from his pocket and substituting the *mezuza*, in light of what we just discussed — or did he genuinely throw a *get* that is now buried deeper in the barrel pile, while the *mezuza* had been there independently?

### Rav Nachman's Logic

*Rav Nachman* rules: *mezuzta be'vei danei lo shechicha* — it is not common to find a *mezuza* among wine barrels. The presence of this *mezuza* is therefore suspicious and must be explained. Since we know a document of that size was thrown, and this is the only such object found, the presumption must be that this *mezuza* is what he actually gave her — in which case she is not divorced.

However, this logic applies only when a single *mezuza* is found. *Aval bitartei u'tlat* — if two or three *mezuzot* are discovered among the barrels, then since some of them were clearly there before the incident, the reasonable assumption is that all of them were pre-existing, and that the document actually thrown was a *get* now unaccounted for.

### Lost Get Reasoning

As for the missing *get* in the two-or-three *mezuzot* case: *ve'gita eimar achbarim shakluho* — the *get* may simply have been dragged off by rats, as documents in such locations often were. The inability to produce the *get* is therefore no bar to treating the wife as divorced, especially in light of what was established earlier: once a *get* is properly given, declared by the husband, and — ideally — read by the witnesses, the absence of the document afterward need not undermine the divorce.

### Sefer Torah Get

The *Gemara* then describes a far more dramatic case. *Hahu gavra de'al le'vei knishta, shakal sefer torah yahav leh le'deveiteih ve'amar lah ha gitich* — a man entered a *beit knesset*, took a *Sefer Torah*, handed it to his wife, and declared it to be her *get*. *Rav Yosef* dismissed the concern that this could constitute a valid *get*, and then enumerated the reasons. Could we be concerned about *mei milin*? Perhaps he wrote a *get* in disappearing ink on the parchment of the *Sefer Torah*, it was visible at transfer, and now is no longer seen. The *Gemara* answers: *mei milin agabi mei milin* — the *klaf* of a *Sefer Torah* is itself treated with *mei milin* during its preparation, and therefore *mei milin* written on it would never be visible to begin with. There is consequently no moment at which such writing could have appeared, and no basis for the concern *Shmuel* raised.

Could the *Sefer Torah* itself serve as a *sefer kritut* by virtue of the very *parasha* containing *ve'chatav lah sefer kritut*? Perhaps the Torah's own text constitutes a bill of severance. The answer: *habe'inan ve'chasav lah lishmah ve'leka* — the *get* must be written *lishmah*, for the sake of this particular woman, and a *Sefer Torah* written by a *sofer* in the normal course of his work was not written with any such intention. Moreover, even if he had paid the *sofer* to write that *parasha* with his wife in mind, *habe'inan shino shemo ve'shemah ve'shem iro ve'shem irah ve'leka* — a valid *get* requires the names of the husband, the wife, and their respective cities, none of which appear in the text of the Torah. *Tosafot* notes that while the *lishmah* requirement in the context of *sota* may be more flexible — since the scraping itself is an integral part of the ritual, perhaps reducing the demands on the *ve'chasav* — the *lishmah* of a *get* is categorically different. It is an expression of the husband's will and intent, not merely a technical condition of the writing, and therefore cannot be satisfied by a generic text written by a *sofer* in the ordinary course.

So *Rav Yosef*'s statement that "there is nothing to worry about" is straightforward in all its components. What, then, is his *chiddush*?

### Mei Milin on Klaf

*She'ein mei milin agabi mei milin* — the *chiddush* is specifically the teaching that a *Sefer Torah*'s *klaf*, having been treated with *mei milin* in preparation, renders any writing in *mei milin* permanently invisible from the outset. This is not obvious. One might have thought that *mei milin* on *mei milin* could still produce some temporary visibility, analogous to *Shmuel*'s concern about disappearing ink on ordinary paper. *Rav Yosef* teaches that this is not the case, and that concern is therefore entirely inapplicable to a *Sefer Torah*.

## Lishmah & Overwriting

### Lishmah Overwriting

Having discussed the *lishmah* requirement in the context of the *Sefer Torah* case, the *Gemara* now turns to a related and practically significant question. *Rav Chisda* states: *get she'kesavo shelo lishmah ve'he'evir alav kolmos lishmah, banu lemachlokes Rabbi Yehuda ve'Rabanan* — if a *get* was written *shelo lishmah*, with all the correct names but without the particular intention toward this husband and wife, and the scribe subsequently passed the pen over the writing again with proper *lishmah* intention — writing directly on top of the existing letters — does that retroactively or effectively confer *lishmah*? *Rav Chisda* says this question is the subject of a *machlokes* between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Rabbanan*.

### God's Name Overwrite

The *Gemara* cites the relevant *beraita*: a *sofer* writing a *Sefer Torah* arrived at the divine name — *Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei* — but in his mind he had *yehudah* in mind. He wrote the letters *Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei* correctly, but his intention was for the word *Yehudah*, not for *shem Hashem*; he simply forgot to add the *dalet*. The name was thus written *shelo lishmah*. *Rabbi Yehuda* says: *ma'avir alav kolmos u'mikadsho* — pass the pen over the name again, this time with proper intention for *shem Hashem*, and this sanctifies it. *Va'chachamim omrim eino shem min hamuvchar* — the Sages say this is not the proper way to write God's name. The context of the ruling suggests this is a *bedieved* dispute, not merely a *lechatchila* preference. *Rav Chisda* holds that the same dispute governs the *lishmah* of a *get*: *Rabbi Yehuda* would permit overwriting to supply the missing *lishmah*, while the *Rabbanan* would reject it.

### Rav Acha Distinction

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* pushes back: *dilma lo hi* — perhaps the two cases are not identical. The *Rabbanan*' rejection of the overwritten divine name may rest on a specific principle: *ad kan lo kamri rabbanan hasam de'be'inan zeh Eli ve'anveihu* — the Sages' stringency in that case derives from the demand for *noi mitzvah*, the glorification of God's name implied by the verse "this is my God and I will glorify Him." Writing the divine name by tracing over an improperly intended prior inscription is simply not dignified enough — the name ends up as a piggyback on a name that was not created with the proper sanctity, and the whole of *shem Hashem* was not formed *lishmah* from the outset. That specific demand of dignity and glorification may not apply to the *lishmah* of a *get*, and therefore *aval hacha lo* — here, perhaps everyone would agree that overwriting with *lishmah* intention is valid.

### Tosafot Missing Component

*Tosafot* notes a tension with an earlier discussion in the sugya. Earlier, the *Gemara* considered whether witnesses could effectively sign a *get* by tracing over letters that already bore their names, written in a light ink, with a darker ink. There, the *Gemara* was hesitant to accept ink-over-ink as constituting a valid signature. Yet here, *Rav Chisda* treats overwriting as at least the position of *Rabbi Yehuda* and perhaps universally valid. How are these consistent?

*Tosafot* distinguishes the two cases as follows. In the case of the *get*'s *lishmah*, the first writing — though physically correct — lacked the essential component of *lishmah*. Because *lishmah* was absent, the first writing in a certain sense did not fully constitute the act of writing required by the *get*. The second writing therefore comes to replace what was deficient with something properly accomplished; it creates something new, even though the letters look physically identical before and after. The earlier case of witness signatures is categorically different: the underlying writing was fully valid in every respect. There was no missing component. The act of writing the signature had already been completed — nothing was lacking. A second tracing of identical letters over a complete prior writing adds nothing, for nothing was absent to be supplied.

One could alternatively frame the distinction thus: in the signature case, the problem was whether the act of writing had occurred at all, and since writing had already occurred, the second pass added no new act. Here, the problem is not whether writing occurred but whether *lishmah* is present — and perhaps *lishmah* can be introduced by an act of rewriting even when the physical substrate already exists, precisely because *lishmah* is not the act of writing itself but a quality that can be inserted into an already-written text.

## Rav Chisda's Concern

### Invalidate All Gittin

The *Gemara* now records a remarkable theoretical statement. *Rav Chisda* says: *yachilna lemipsal lechol gittei de'alma* — I could construct an argument to invalidate every *get* in the world. He presents this as a theoretical observation, not a practical ruling. *Rava* asks: what is the argument?

### Woman's Payment Issue

*Rav Chisda*'s first suggestion: the Torah says the husband should write the *get* — *ve'chasav* — but in practice it is the woman who pays the *sofer*'s fees. If she is the one commissioning and paying the scribe, one might argue the scribe is functionally her agent, not the husband's, undermining the requirement that the husband be the effective author. *Rava* dismisses this: *vedilma aknoiyei aknu lei rabanan* — the *Rabbanan* may have established that even though the wife pays, the fee is legally assigned to the husband, so that the *sofer* is treated as an agent of the husband. The practical arrangement of payment does not determine the halakhic attribution of agency.

### Material Value & Issurei

The second possible argument: the Torah says *ve'natan* — he must *give* it to her — and perhaps this implies that the object given must have monetary value, at least a *perutah*. Yet the *mishna* permits writing a *get* on a leaf or other minimal-value material. Perhaps the *get* is therefore not a proper act of giving.

### Issurei Hana'ah Valid

The *Gemara* rejects this line of reasoning by citing a ruling sent from the West, from *Eretz Yisrael*: *ketavo al issurei hana'ah kasher* — a *get* written on an object from which benefit is forbidden is valid. This is a striking ruling. An object of *issurei hana'ah* has, by definition, no halakhic monetary value — indeed, there is a fundamental debate in the *poskim* as to whether such objects can even be considered property in a meaningful legal sense. Yet the *get* is valid. This demonstrates that *ve'natan* does not require a transfer of monetary ownership at all. The obligation is the physical act of handing the object, *netinat get*, not the conveyance of an item of value. The *get* belongs to a different conceptual framework from ordinary *kinyanim* — acquisition is not the operative act; the physical delivery is.

*Rav Ashi* notes that this principle might already be derivable from the *mishna* itself, which permits writing on an olive leaf. The *Gemara* responds that an olive leaf is still worth something — in aggregate, many such leaves have value, and a single leaf is therefore not literally worthless. An object of *issurei hana'ah* is a categorically more extreme case, and the ruling from *Eretz Yisrael* is therefore a genuine *chiddush*.

### Levi's Announcement

The *Gemara* records a *beraita*: *Rebbi* said that writing a *get* on *issurei hana'ah* is *kasher*. *Levi* went out to publicize this ruling *mishmeih de'Rebbi*, and the people did not praise it — presumably because they were uncomfortable accepting it as the position of a single authority. He then taught it *mishmei d'rabbanan* — as the consensus of the Sages — and it was praised. *Amar hilchata ke'vassai* — from this we derive that this is the operative *halacha*.

*Rashi* understands that we learn the *psak* from *Levi*'s own behavior: his insistence on teaching it as consensus opinion, and his discomfort with its being received as a *da'at yachid*, reflects his own conviction that it was the correct *psak*, and the *Gemara* accepts his determination. *Tosafot* disagrees: we learn from the audience's reaction. When the ruling was presented as a lone opinion they were not opposed to its substance — they simply disliked it being framed as a *da'at yachid*. When *Levi* reframed it as consensus, they accepted it enthusiastically, demonstrating that the community of scholars recognized it as the accepted *halacha*.

## Writing: Ink vs. Carving

### Writing Not Engraving

The *Gemara* now turns to the fundamental requirement of *ketivah* — writing — in the context of a *get*. A *beraita* teaches: *ve'chasav ve'lo chakak* — the verse says "he shall write," not "he shall engrave." *Chikuk* — engraving or carving — is not *ketivah*. The underlying distinction is clear: writing involves applying a substance to a surface to form letters; engraving involves carving away material, neither adding ink nor producing letters through deposition. The mode of creation is fundamentally different.

### Tablets Engraving

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *eved she'yatza bi'ktav she'al gabei tavla u'pinkas yatza le'cheirus* — a slave who received a *shtar shichrur* written on a tablet or *pinkas* goes free. These tablets — typically wood surfaced with wax — were written on with a stylus, a process that is inherently a form of *chakika*, engraving into the wax rather than depositing ink. Yet such a document is valid. Furthermore, the *beraita* specifies that writing on a *kipa* or *anduchtri* — some form of a garment — with sewn or attached letters is *not* valid. *Rashi* understands this as letters that have been cut out separately and affixed to the surface. The problem is not that they are engraved but that they are not integrated into the medium — the letters are merely incidentally attached and can be removed without disturbing the surface itself. This implies that the problem with the garment case is the lack of integration, not the mode of inscription, and therefore that *chakika* per se on a proper surface is acceptable.

### Chok Tocho & Yerechot

*Ulla* in the name of *Rabbi Elazar* resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between two types of carving. *Chak tochot* — carving out the interior space — means that rather than tracing the shape of the letter with the stylus, one removes the material *surrounding* the letter, so that the letter is left standing raised. *Chak yerachot* — carving the legs, i.e., the letter-form itself — means the stylus traces the actual shape of the letter directly, carving into the surface to form the letter's contours.

The critical distinction: in *chak yerachot*, the act of the hand traces the shape of the letter itself, analogously to the act of writing with ink. The instrument is working directly on the letter. In *chak tochot*, the instrument works on the space around the letter; the letter itself is never directly acted upon — it merely remains as what was not carved away. It follows that *chak yerachot* is *ketivah*, while *chak tochot* is not.

This distinction is also operative in laws of *Sefer Torah*. If a drop of ink fell into the interior of a *chet*, creating a solid rectangle rather than two legs joined by a roof, and one fixes it by scraping away the ink from the interior — the *chet* has been created by *chak tochot*, working on the interior space rather than forming the letter itself, and the letter is therefore invalid.

### Tzitz Protruding

The *Gemara* now challenges the ruling that *chak tochot* is invalid. The *tzitz* — the frontplate worn by the *Kohen Gadol* — bore the inscription *Kodesh l'Hashem*, and the *Gemara* states: *lo haya ketavo shoke'a ela bolet ke'dinrei zahav* — the letters were not sunken/engraved into the surface; rather they protruded, like the letters on a gold *dinar*. But the *tzitz* is described with the verb *ve'chatavta alav* — "you shall write upon it" — so the protruding letters must constitute *ketivah*. How? If letters protrude because the material *around* them has been pushed down or carved away, that is *chak tochot*, which we have said is not *ketivah*. And the analogy to *dinrei zahav* — minted gold coins — is itself problematic: coins are produced by pressing the material around the image down into a mold, so that the image remains raised — a paradigmatic case of *chak tochot*.

The *Gemara* answers: *ke'dinrei zahav ve'lo ke'dinrei zahav* — like *dinrei zahav* in that the letters protrude, but not like *dinrei zahav* in method. For coins, *de'ilu hatam tochot* — the protruding letters result from pushing down the surrounding material, which is *chak tochot*. For the *tzitz*, *ve'hacha yerachot* — the protruding letters were formed by engraving the letter-form itself from the *back* of the plate. When one engraves the letter from the reverse side, it causes the corresponding area on the front to protrude. The act of the hand was tracing the shape of the letter — *chak yerachot* — even though the visible result on the front is a raised letter rather than a sunken one. Working from the back, one is still directly forming the letter-shape, which constitutes *ketivah*.

This does raise a natural question: when engraving from the rear, one is working on the letter in reverse, relating to the mirror image of its form. The *Gemara* does not treat this as a disqualification, implicitly holding that working directly on the letter-form — even in reverse from behind — is a more genuine act of *ketivah* than working on the surrounding space from the front.

### Coins Minting

*Ravina* then raises a precise question to *Rav Ashi*: *rushma michratz charitz o kinufay michnaf* — when a metal-pressing die is used to mint a coin, what conceptually is occurring? Does the die push *down* the material around the image — carving out the space, which would be *chak tochot* — with the raised areas simply remaining as they were? Or does it push *in* the material that will become the raised image — pressing the metal into the indentations in the mold, so that one is effectively working on the letter-areas themselves? The conceptual answer matters for how we classify the act.

*Rav Ashi* answers: *michratz charitz* — the die pushes down the surrounding material. The raised letters remain raised not because they were pushed up but because the space around them was pushed down. This is *chak tochot* and, for purposes of *ketivah*, is not considered writing. This is consistent with everything said until now.

### Yerechot is Writing

The *Gemara* then raises the *tzitz* passage once more — the same challenge, the same answer. *Ve'lo ke'dinrei zahav de'ilu hatam migavai ve'hacha mibarai* — coins are made by working the die from the front, pushing down the surroundings; the *tzitz* was worked from the back, tracing the letter-form itself.

The conclusions of the sugya: a *get* does not require ink in the conventional sense — the physical deposit of a marking substance on a surface. Engraving without any liquid substance can constitute *ketivah*, provided the act of engraving directly traces the form of the letters themselves (*chak yerachot*). If instead the space around the letters is what is worked upon, leaving the letter raised by default (*chak tochot*), this is not *ketivah*. And sewing separately-cut letters onto a garment is likewise invalid, because the letters are not integrated into their background medium — they exist independently and are merely attached. The question of whether embroidered letters, sewn into the fabric of a garment by threading through it, would be more analogous to *chak yerachot* — as the needle traces the letter-form directly — or would suffer from the same lack of surface-integration as affixed cut-out letters, remains a question the *Gemara* leaves to further reflection.
