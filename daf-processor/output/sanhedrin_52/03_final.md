# Sanhedrin 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Srefah Hierarchy Deb (II)

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶת אָבִיהָ – בִּרְשׁוּת אָבִיהָ, בִּשְׂרֵיפָה; וְאֶת חָמִיהָ – מֵחָמִיהָ, בִּסְקִילָה; וְכׇל אָדָם – בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **Actually,** Rabbi Eliezer's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, and this** is what he **is saying:** The expression: **With her father,** means **under her father's authority,** i.e., she is still betrothed, and she is executed **by burning** if she commits adultery. **And** the expression: **With her father-in-law,** means that after getting married, she engages in intercourse **with her father-in-law,** and she is therefore executed **by stoning. And** if she commits adultery with **any** other **man** after getting married, she is executed **by strangulation,** like any other married woman.

The daf begins in the middle of 51b, in the context of a *braita* exploring the case of a *bat kohen* — a daughter of a *kohen* — who commits adultery and receives the distinctive punishment of *srefah*. The discussion has centered on determining precisely which cases of a *bat kohen* fall under this ruling: is she *nesu'ah* (fully married), *arusah* (betrothed), or some combination? Three positions have emerged from the preceding discussion. The *chachamim* hold that a *nesu'ah* receives *srefah* because it is weightier than *chenek*, while the *arusah* receives *skila* because *skila* is the weightiest punishment. A second view, associated with Rabbi Shimon, holds that both the *arusah* and the *nesu'ah* receive *srefah*, treating *srefah* as the weightiest of all. Now the *Gemara* introduces a third position: that of Rabbi Yishmael.

### Rabbi Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַת [אִישׁ] כֹּהֵן כִּי תֵחֵל לִזְנוֹת״ – בְּנַעֲרָה וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And the daughter of a priest, when she profanes herself by playing the harlot"** (Leviticus 21:9), **the verse is speaking of a young woman who is betrothed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּנַעֲרָה וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ נְשׂוּאָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִנְאַף אֶת אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ מוֹת יוּמַת הַנֹּאֵף וְהַנּוֹאָפֶת״. הַכֹּל הָיוּ בִּכְלַל ״הַנּוֹאֵף וְהַנּוֹאֶפֶת״. הוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּסְקִילָה וּבַת כֹּהֵן בִּשְׂרֵיפָה. מָה כְּשֶׁהוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב אֶת בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִסְקִילָה – אֲרוּסָה וְלֹא נְשׂוּאָה, אַף כְּשֶׁהוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב בַּת כֹּהֵן לִשְׂרֵיפָה – אֲרוּסָה וְלֹא נְשׂוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Do you say** that it is referring only **to a young woman who is betrothed, or** that **it is** referring **even** to **a married woman? The verse states:** "And **a man who commits adultery** with another man's wife, he who commits adultery **with his neighbor's wife, both the adulterer and the adulteress shall be put to death"** (Leviticus 20:10). **All** adulterers **were included in** the category of: **"The adulterer and the adulteress,"** and were executed by strangulation, until **the verse singled out the** betrothed **daughter of a non-priest** for execution **by stoning and the daughter of a priest** for execution **by burning.** It is therefore derived that **just as when the verse singles out the daughter of a non-priest for stoning** the Torah states that the reference is to a woman who is **betrothed and not married, so too, when the verse singles out the daughter of a priest for burning** it is referring to a woman who is **betrothed and not married.**

One might ask: perhaps the verse refers even to a *nesu'ah*? The *Gemara* responds with a chain of reasoning. The verse *ish asher yinaf et eshet re'ehu — mot yumat hano'ef v'hano'efes* establishes *chenek* as the default punishment for adultery — all adulterers, male and female, were encompassed within this rule. Then the Torah created an exception: a *na'ara m'urasa* receives the weightier punishment of *skila* rather than the default *chenek*. When the Torah subsequently creates another exception for a *bat kohen* — assigning her *srefah* — the logic dictates that this exception, like the prior one, applies only to the analogous case: the *na'ara m'urasa*. All other cases of a *bat kohen* revert to the default of *chenek*, just as all other non-betrothed women revert to *chenek* as well.

### Analogy Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹמְמֶיהָ וּבוֹעֲלָהּ בִּכְלַל ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **The conspiring witnesses** concerning the daughter of a priest, **and** the **paramour of** the daughter of a priest, **are included in** the verse: **"And you shall do to him as he conspired** to do to his brother" (Deuteronomy 19:19).

The *braita* makes this explicit: *k'shem shehotzi hakatuve bas Yisrael b'skila arusah v'lo nesuah* — just as the Torah assigned the weightier punishment of *skila* specifically to an *arusah* and not to a *nesuah*, so too *af k'shem shehotzi hakatuve bat kohen l'srefah arusah v'lo nesuah* — when the Torah assigned the special punishment of *srefah* to a *bat kohen*, it similarly applies only to the *arusah* and not the *nesuah*. This is reinforced by the *pshat* of the verse itself, which says *et aviha hi mechalelet* — "she has desecrated her father" — with the focus on the father suggesting that she is still living in her father's house, which is the condition of a *na'ara m'urasa* and not a *nesuah*. This constitutes a third distinct position regarding who receives *srefah*.

## Zomemim Witnesses

### False Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹעֲלָהּ – מַאי ״כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם״ אִיכָּא? אֶלָּא: זוֹמְמֶיהָ בִּכְלַל מִיתַת בּוֹעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interjects and asks: **What** reason **is there** for the punishment of **"as he conspired"** to be applied with regard to **her paramour? Rather,** the *baraita* should be read: **Her conspiring witnesses are included in the death** penalty **of her paramour,** i.e., they are executed by strangulation, which they sought to impose upon her paramour. They are not executed by burning, which is the death penalty that they sought to impose upon her.

The *braita* continues by addressing the men implicated in the adultery case — specifically the *edim zomemim*, the false witnesses who had sought to frame her — and asking what punishment they would receive.

### Bo'el Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו״, וְלֹא לַאֲחוֹתוֹ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** This is **because it is stated: "And you shall do to him as he conspired to do to his brother,"** which is interpreted to mean: "To his brother," **but not to his sister.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The principle is that the *edim zomemim* would receive not the punishment of the *bat kohen* herself, but the punishment that the *bo'el* would have received — which is *chenek*, the standard punishment for adultery by a non-*kohen* male. The source is the verse *la'asos leachiv* — what the witnesses intended to do to their "brother" — and not to their "sister." It is the punishment designated for the man that serves as the model for the *zomemim*.

### Gender Distinction

This ruling underscores the gender-specific character of *srefah* in this context. As the *Gemara* makes clear: *lechacho* — "for her" — the particular punishment is *srefah*, and that punishment does not transfer to the male participants, whether the *bo'el* or the *edim zomemim*. The *bat kohen*'s *srefah* is categorically her own; neither the man who sinned with her nor the witnesses who sought to convict her share in it.

## Srefah Hierarchy Deb (II)

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אַחַת אֲרוּסָה וְאַחַת נְשׂוּאָה יָצָאת לִשְׂרֵיפָה. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ פְּנוּיָה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״אָבִיהָ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״אָבִיהָ״. מָה לְהַלָּן זְנוּת עִם זִיקַת הַבַּעַל, אַף כָּאן זְנוּת עִם זִיקַת הַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Both the betrothed and the married** daughter of a priest **are singled out for** execution by **burning.** One **might** have thought that **even an unmarried** daughter of a priest who engaged in promiscuous intercourse should be executed by burning. This is incorrect, as **here it is stated: "Her father," and there it is stated,** with regard to a betrothed woman who committed adultery: **"Her father"** (Deuteronomy 22:21). It is derived through a verbal analogy that **just as below,** the reference is to the **promiscuous intercourse** of one who has **a bond to a husband, so too here,** the reference is to the **promiscuous intercourse** of one who has **a bond to a husband.**

Having established Rabbi Yishmael's position — that *srefah* is limited to the *na'ara m'urasa* case — the *Gemara* now turns to Rabbi Akiva's opposing view. Rabbi Akiva states: *achas arusa ve-achas nesua yatze'u le-sreifah* — both the *arusah* and the *nesuah* receive *srefah*. This aligns with the position of Rabbi Shimon, which was noted earlier. Rabbi Akiva then raises a potential further expansion: *yachol afilu pnuya* — perhaps even an unmarried woman receives *srefah*? To exclude this possibility, Rabbi Akiva employs a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *aviah* (her father) appears both in the verse about the *bat kohen* and in the context of the *na'ara m'urasa*, where it is part of the phrase *beis aviah*. Just as there the *na'ara m'urasa* is connected to a man through *zikas ha-ba'al* — the bond of a husband — so too the *bat kohen* subject to *srefah* must have such a bond. The *gezeirah shavah* thus excludes an unmarried woman, while the verse itself still encompasses both the *arusah* and the *nesuah*.

### Extra Vav Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אִי, מָה לְהַלָּן נַעֲרָה וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה, אַף כָּאן נַעֲרָה וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael said to him: If** the *halakha* of a priest's daughter who committed adultery is compared to the *halakha* of a betrothed woman who committed adultery, then it can be inferred that **just as there** the reference is specifically to **a young woman who is betrothed, so too here,** the reference is to **a young woman who is betrothed.** This serves as a proof for the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, which is that only the betrothed daughter of a priest is executed by burning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָחִי, ״בַּת״ ״וּבַת״ אֲנִי דּוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: Yishmael, my brother, I derive** it from the fact that the verse could have stated: **"The daughter** of a priest," but instead states: **"And the daughter** of a priest," with the conjunction "and," that married daughters of priests are also included in this punishment.

Rabbi Yishmael pushes back. He argues: *im ma le-halan na'ara ve-arusa af kan na'ara ve-arusa* — if you are going to learn from the *na'ara m'urasa* case, then the comparison should fully track that case, limiting *srefah* to a *na'ara m'urasa* specifically. Rabbi Akiva responds: *u-vas u-vas ani doresh* — "I expound 'daughter' and 'daughter.'" The verse says *u'vas ish kohen*, with the additional *vav* at the beginning of *u'vas*. Had the verse simply said *bas ish kohen*, Rabbi Akiva would have limited the ruling to the *arusah* by analogy to *beis aviah*. But the extra *vav* of *u'vas* extends the ruling to include the *nesuah* as well.

### Dibra Torah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאַתָּה דּוֹרֵשׁ ״בַּת״ ״וּבַת״ נוֹצִיא זוֹ לִשְׂרֵיפָה? אִם מַשְׁמַע לְהָבִיא אֶת הַנְּשׂוּאָה, הָבֵיא אֶת הַפְּנוּיָה; וְאִם מַשְׁמַע לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַפְּנוּיָה, הוֹצֵא אֶת הַנְּשׂוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: And because you derive** this from the difference between the term **"the daughter"** and the term **"and the daughter," we should take this** married daughter of a priest **out to** be executed by **burning?** This derivation of yours is inconsistent, because **if** the conjunction "and" **indicates the inclusion of the married** daughter of a priest, then it should **include the unmarried** daughter of a priest too. **And if it indicates the exclusion of an unmarried one, exclude the married** one as well.

Rabbi Yishmael's response is one of the most striking passages in this section: *ve-chi mipnei she-atah doresh bas u-vas notzi zo le-sreifah?* — "Just because you expound 'daughter' and 'daughter,' shall we take this woman out to be burned?" This is the beautiful distillation of the broader methodological debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva is famous for deriving legal consequences from every additional letter, every superfluous word. Rabbi Yishmael operates by the principle of *dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of ordinary human speech — and therefore resists hyper-interpretation of minor textual variations. His objection has both logical and rhetorical force: logically, the comparison to the *na'ara m'urasa* case points toward limiting *srefah* to the *arusah*; rhetorically, he is saying that burning a woman on the basis of a single extra *vav* demands a justification far more substantial than Rabbi Akiva has offered.

Rabbi Yishmael presses further. He argues that if one takes the extra *vav* of *u'vas* as a genuinely expansive word — one that broadens the category of women subject to *srefah* beyond the *arusah* — then by the same token it should include even an unmarried woman, a *pnuya*. But if instead we rely on the link to *beis aviah* to exclude the *pnuya*, then by that same logic we should exclude the *nesuah*, since the most direct comparison is to the *na'ara m'urasa*. Rabbi Yishmael is arguing that the two interpretive moves Rabbi Akiva is making are in tension: you cannot use the *vav* to expand the category to include the *nesuah* while simultaneously using the *gezeirah shavah* to exclude the *pnuya*, unless you have principled grounds for each. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אַהֲנִי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמַעוֹטֵי פְּנוּיָה, וְאַהֲנִי ״בַּת״ ״וּבַת״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנְּשׂוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how would **Rabbi Akiva** respond? The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Akiva, the **verbal analogy** between the *halakha* of the daughter of a priest and the *halakha* of a betrothed woman **serves to exclude** the case of **an unmarried** daughter of a priest from execution by burning, **and** the distinction between the terms **"the daughter"** and **"and the daughter" serves to include the** case of **a married woman.**

The *Gemara* resolves this from Rabbi Akiva's perspective: the *gezeirah shavah* of *aviah* does the work of excluding the *pnuya*, and the extra *vav* does the independent work of including the *nesuah*. Each device serves a distinct function.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: מִדְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ ״בַּת״ ״וּבַת״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הֲדַר בֵּיהּ מִגְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael** challenged Rabbi Akiva's opinion because he **thought** that **since** Rabbi Akiva had **said to him** that a married woman is included due to the distinction between **"the daughter"** and **"and the daughter,"** it was possible to **conclude from it** that Rabbi Akiva had **retracted** his derivation **from the verbal analogy,** which would have included a married woman, and instead derived the *halakha* only from the conjunction "and" in the term "and the daughter." But in fact, Rabbi Akiva combined this derivation with the verbal analogy.

However, the *Bavli* frames Rabbi Yishmael's response not as a full-throated disagreement but rather as a possible misreading: *sh'ma mina hadar bei m'gezeirah shavah* — perhaps Rabbi Yishmael thought Rabbi Akiva was abandoning the *gezeirah shavah* by appealing to the extra *vav*, and it was on that basis that he challenged him. Whether or not this framing fully captures the depth of their dispute, it is clear that for Rabbi Yishmael, the *gezeirah shavah* and the overall logical analogy together point toward limiting *srefah* to the *na'ara m'urasa*, and the extra *vav* is not a sufficient counter-argument.

### Vav as Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הַאי ״בַּת״ ״וּבַת״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אָבִין: לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ שֶׁחִלֵּק הַכָּתוּב בִּזְכָרִים בֵּין תְּמִימִים לְבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין, יָכוֹל נַחְלוֹק בִּבְנוֹתֵיהֶן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּת״ ״וּבַת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yishmael, what does he derive from this** distinction between **"the daughter"** and **"and the daughter"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires it for that which the father of Shmuel bar Avin taught** in a *baraita*: **Since we find that the verse differentiates with regard to male** priests **between unblemished** priests **and blemished** priests, as only unblemished priests may perform the Temple service, one **might** have thought **we should differentiate between their daughters** as well, and rule that the daughter of a blemished priest should not be sentenced to execution by burning for adultery. Therefore, instead of stating: **"The daughter," the verse states: "And the daughter,"** to include the daughter of a blemished priest.

This raises an obvious question: if Rabbi Yishmael dismisses the extra *vav* as insufficient to expand the ruling, what does *he* do with it? The *Gemara* asks directly: *hai bas u'vas mai darish bei?* Rabbi Yishmael uses the extra *vav* to address the teaching of Avuha d'Shmuel bar Avin. The concern goes as follows: since we know that male *kohanim* are distinguished between those who are unblemished (*temimim*) and those who have blemishes (*ba'alei mumim*) — the latter being disqualified from performing the *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash* — one might have thought (*yachol nachlok bivnoseihem*) that a similar distinction applies to daughters of *kohanim*, so that a blemished *bat kohen* would not receive *srefah*. The *Gemara* acknowledges this is a strange *hava amina* — why would a physical blemish bear on the adultery penalty? — but the idea is that perhaps a blemish compromises her *kehunah* status in some way, thereby affecting the punishment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מִן ״הֵם מַקְרִיבִם וְהָיוּ קֹדֶשׁ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **Rabbi Akiva** derive this? The Gemara answers: He derives it **from** the verse: "They shall be holy to their God, and not profane the name of their God; for the offerings of the Lord, the bread of their Lord, **they offer; and they shall be holy"** (Leviticus 21:6). From the phrase "And they shall be holy" **it is derived** that all priests are considered holy, even if they cannot bring offerings in the Temple. Consequently, there is no difference in this regard between the daughter of a blemished priest and the daughter of an unblemished priest.

The extra *vav* of *u'vas* answers this: it teaches that the ruling applies to all daughters of *kohanim* equally, blemished or unblemished, and that a blemish is entirely irrelevant to the law of *srefah*. For his part, Rabbi Akiva learns this same point from the principle of *v'hayu kodesh* — "they shall be holy" — which indicates that the *kedushah* of *kohanim* is not compromised by the presence of a blemish; the blemish restricts them only from *avodah*, not from their essential sanctified status. The *vav*, freed from this usage by Rabbi Akiva, is then available to expand *srefah* to the *nesuah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אִי מֵהַהִיא הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי אִינְהוּ, אֲבָל בְּנוֹתֵיהֶן לָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why does **Rabbi Yishmael** not derive this *halakha* from this verse? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael would say: **If** it were to be derived **from that** verse, **I would say** that **this matter** applies with regard to the priests **themselves,** who are considered holy even if they are blemished, **but not** with regard to **their daughters.** Therefore, the term "and the daughter" **teaches us** that the *halakha* with regard to the daughters of blemished priests is just like the *halakha* with regard to the daughters of unblemished priests.

The section concludes by touching on the verse *et aviha hi mechalelet* and what Rabbi Yishmael learns from it. Since Rabbi Yishmael arrives at the limitation to *na'ara m'urasa* by analogy rather than by *gezeirah shavah*, the words *et aviha hi mechalelet* are available for a different teaching. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״אָבִיהָ הִיא מְחַלֶּלֶת״, מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **what does he derive from this** verse: **"She profanes her father"** (Leviticus 21:9), if he does not employ the term "her father" for a verbal analogy concerning a young betrothed woman who committed adultery, as Rabbi Akiva does?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶת אָבִיהָ הִיא מְחַלֶּלֶת״? שֶׁאִם הָיוּ נוֹהֲגִין בּוֹ קוֹדֶשׁ – נוֹהֲגִין בּוֹ חוֹל, כָּבוֹד – נוֹהֲגִין בּוֹ בִּזָּיוֹן. אוֹמְרִין: ״אָרוּר שֶׁזּוֹ יָלַד, אָרוּר שֶׁזּוֹ גִּידֵּל, אָרוּר שֶׁיָּצָא זוֹ מֵחֲלָצָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: He **requires it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir would say: Why** must **the verse state: "She profanes her father"?** To teach **that if** initially **they would treat** her father in **a sacred** manner, now **they treat him** in **a profane** manner. If previously they would treat him with **honor,** they now **treat him** with **degradation. They say: Cursed** is the one **who bore this** daughter, **cursed** is the one **who raised this** daughter, **cursed** is the one **from whose loins this** daughter **emerged.**

A *braita* expounds: if the *bat kohen* commits this act, we are not merely noting the natural shame that falls on her father; rather, the verse instructs the community in how to respond. *Im hayu nogim bo kodesh nogim bo chol* — if the community had been treating him with honor as befitting a *kohen*, they should now treat him with ordinary disregard; *im hayu nogim bo kavod nogim bo bizayon* — if they treated him with distinction, they should now treat him with contempt. The community is meant to say: *arur shezo yaldu, arur shezo gildo, arur sheyatza zo mechalatzav* — cursed is the one who gave birth to her, cursed is the one who raised her, cursed is the one from whose loins she came.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּמַאן קָרֵינַן ״רַשִּׁיעָא בַּר רַשִּׁיעָא״, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַשִּׁיעָא בַּר צַדִּיקָא, כְּמַאן? כְּהַאי תַּנָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: In accordance with whose** opinion **do we call even a wicked person** who is **the son of a righteous person: A wicked person, the son of a wicked person? In accordance with whose** opinion is it? It **is in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*,** Rabbi Meir, who says that a sinful daughter profanes her father, and he too is treated with disgrace.

Rav Ashi notes that an echo of this practice persists: *k'man karinan rashia bar rashia va'afilu l'rashia bar tzadika* — when we call someone a wicked person, we call them "wicked, son of the wicked," even if their parents are in fact righteous. The *Gemara* identifies this as following the view of this *tanna* — Rabbi Meir. The underlying social logic is that communal censure of the parents creates pressure that may deter such conduct from arising in the first place. The tension between that social function and the genuine injustice of shaming parents for the independent choices of their children is real, and not easily resolved.

## Srefah Method

### Initial Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ מִצְוַת הַנִּסְקָלִין. מַאי תַּנָּא דְּקָתָנֵי זוֹ מִצְוַת הַנִּסְקָלִין?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **This** describes **the mitzva of those who are stoned,** i.e., the process of execution by stoning. The Gemara asks: **What** was **taught** in the mishna with regard to **which it teaches** now: **This** describes **the mitzva of those who are stoned?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דִּתְנָא: נִגְמַר הַדִּין מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לְסׇקְלוֹ. בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ שְׁתֵּי קוֹמוֹת. וְאַיְּידֵי דְּקָא בָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵא מִצְוַת הַנִּשְׂרָפִין, תְּנָא נָמֵי זוֹ מִצְוַת הַנִּסְקָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The Mishna states this **because it teaches** in the previous chapter (42b): When **the trial has ended** in a guilty verdict and the condemned man has been sentenced to stoning, **he is taken out to be stoned.** And it continues (45a): **The place of stoning** from which the condemned party is pushed to his death **is** a platform **twice the height** of an ordinary person. **And since** the Mishna **wants to teach** in the next mishna: **The mitzva of those who are burned,** and it will then proceed to describe the processes involved in carrying out the other forms of execution, **it also teaches** in this mishna: **This** describes **the mitzva of those who are stoned,** in order to summarize what was taught up to this point.

Having established who receives *srefah* and the debate surrounding it, the *Gemara* transitions to a new *Mishnah* describing how *srefah* is actually carried out. The transition is introduced with the phrase *zo mitzvat haniskalin* — "this is the procedure of those who are stoned" — which signals the close of the *skila* material and the opening of the *srefah* section. The logical question of why the *Mishnah* was structured this way — describing *skila* first, then interrupting with the list of *arba misos*, rather than presenting the four executions in a single orderly sequence from the outset — is noted but not definitively resolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִצְוַת הַנִּשְׂרָפִין – הָיוּ מְשַׁקְּעִין אוֹתוֹ בַּזֶּבֶל עַד אַרְכוּבּוֹתָיו, וְנוֹתְנִין סוּדָר קָשָׁה לְתוֹךְ הָרַכָּה, וְכוֹרֵךְ עַל צַוָּארוֹ. זֶה מוֹשֵׁךְ אֶצְלוֹ וְזֶה מוֹשֵׁךְ אֶצְלוֹ, עַד שֶׁפּוֹתֵחַ אֶת פִּיו. וּמַדְלִיק אֶת הַפְּתִילָה וְזוֹרְקָהּ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו, וְיוֹרֶדֶת לְתוֹךְ מֵעָיו וְחוֹמֶרֶת אֶת בְּנֵי מֵעָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The mitzva of those who are burned,** i.e., the process of execution by burning, is carried out in the following manner: The executioners **submerge** the condemned one **in dung up to his knees** so he cannot move, **and they place a rough scarf within a soft one,** so his throat will not be wounded, **and wrap** these scarves **around his neck. This** one, i.e., one of the witnesses, **pulls** the scarf **toward himself, and that** one, the other witness, **pulls** it **toward himself, until** the condemned one is forced to **open his mouth,** as he is choking. **And** another person then **lights the wick and throws it into his mouth, and it goes down into his intestines and burns his intestines** and he dies.

The *Mishnah* on *srefah* begins: *hayu mashki'in oso bezevel ad arkovosav* — they would immerse the condemned person in dung up to his knees. This is understood by the *meforshim* not primarily as a form of degradation but as a means of restraining the person and holding him stable. A hard rope (*sudar kasha*) is placed inside a soft scarf (*haraka*). The soft wrapping serves two purposes: it avoids leaving visible marks or lacerations on the neck, and — as the *Yerushalmi* explains — it prevents actual strangulation, since the goal is to force open the mouth, not to kill by *chenek*. The scarf-wrapped rope is then placed around the condemned person's neck: *ze moshech etzlo veze moshech etzlo* — one person on each side pulls in opposite directions, *ad sheposayach pivo* — until the person's mouth is forced open. The molten substance, the *ptilah*, is then lit and cast into his mouth, descending into his innards where it burns them internally.

### Mouth Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הוּא, אִם מֵת בְּיָדָם – לֹא הָיוּ מְקַיְּימִין בּוֹ מִצְוַת שְׂרֵיפָה, אֶלָּא פּוֹתֵחַ אֶת פִּיו בִּצְבָת שֶׁלֹּא בְּטוֹבָתוֹ, וּמַדְלִיק אֶת הַפְּתִילָה וְזוֹרְקָהּ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו, וְיוֹרֶדֶת לְתוֹךְ מֵעָיו וְחוֹמֶרֶת אֶת בְּנֵי מֵעָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: But if this** one who is condemned to death by burning accidentally **died at their hands** by strangulation, **they have not fulfilled the mitzva of** execution by **burning for** this person. **Rather,** the process is carried out in the following manner: One **opens the mouth of** the condemned person **with prongs, against his will, and** one **lights the wick and throws it into his mouth, and it goes down into his intestines and burns his intestines** and he dies.

Rabbi Yehuda raises an objection to this method. If one wraps a rope inside a scarf and pulls on it from both sides, the condemned person will effectively be strangled by the scarf, which is exactly the procedure of *chenek* — strangulation. The result would be that *mitzvas srefah* has not been fulfilled; the person has been executed by *chenek* instead. Rabbi Yehuda therefore proposes an alternative: *posayach es pivo betzavas* — one forces open the mouth using tongs (*tzavas*), *shelo betovaso* — even against the person's resistance. Once the mouth is held open by the tongs, the *ptilah* is lit and cast in, descending and burning the innards in the same fashion as described by the first view.

### Historical Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן צָדוֹק: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבַת כֹּהֵן אַחַת שֶׁזִּינְּתָה, וְהִקִּיפוּהָ חֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת וּשְׂרָפוּהָ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה בָּקִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Tzadok said: An incident** occurred **with regard to a certain priest's daughter who committed adultery, and they wrapped her in bundles of branches and burned her,** contrary to the process described in the mishna. The Sages **said to him:** That court did not act properly; they did so **because the court at that time was not proficient** in *halakha*.

At this point, Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok offers testimony: *ma'aseh b'vas kohen achas she'zinasa* — he witnessed a case of a *bat kohen* who committed adultery, and what was done to her was *v'hekifuha chavilei zmoros u'serafuha* — they surrounded her with bundles of branches and burned her. This is literal external burning, entirely unlike what the *Mishnah* prescribes. The response given to him is that the *beis din* of that time simply did not know what they were doing. This case, as will be discussed further, raises significant questions about the divergence between the prescribed procedure and historical practice.

### Ptilah Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי פְּתִילָה? אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: פְּתִילָה שֶׁל אֲבָר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** kind of **wick** is the mishna referring to? **Rav Mattana says: A wick of lead,** i.e., a long, thin piece of lead in the shape of a wick, which is melted and poured down into the intestines.

The *Mishnah* refers to lighting the *ptilah* and casting it into the mouth. The identity of the *ptilah* is now examined. Rav Masna says: *ptilah shel avar* — it is lead. Rashi explains that this does not mean one lights a piece of lead on fire, since lead does not ignite in that manner. Rather, one melts the lead and pours the molten metal down the throat. The *Yerushalmi* offers two additional possibilities: a mixture of lead and tin, or *neft* — a type of oil or petroleum-derived substance, heated and poured in. All three options produce the same essential result — burning of the internal organs — though they differ in the specific substance used.

## Srefah Sources

### Two Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לַן? אָתְיָא ״שְׂרֵיפָה״ ״שְׂרֵיפָה״ מֵעֲדַת קֹרַח. מָה לְהַלָּן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים, אַף כָּאן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that burning means this kind of death? **It is derived from** a verbal analogy between the **burning** that is described in the context of capital punishment (see Leviticus 21:9) and the **burning** described with regard to **the assembly of Korah,** when they were burned by God (see Numbers 17:4). **Just as there,** with regard to the assembly of Korah, they were killed by **the burning of** the **soul** within the body, **but** the **body** itself remained **intact, so too here,** the condemned one is executed by **the burning** of the **soul, but** the **body** remains **intact.** He is not executed by means of the burning of the body with wood, as in that case the body would be consumed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: אָתְיָא שְׂרֵיפָה שְׂרֵיפָה מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, מָה לְהַלָּן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים – אַף כָּאן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** that there is a different source for this method of burning: It is **derived from** a verbal analogy between the **burning** that is described in this context and the **burning** that is described with regard to the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, **the sons of Aaron** (see Leviticus 10:6). **Just as there,** Nadav and Avihu were killed by **the burning of** the **soul, but** the **body** remained **intact, so too here,** the execution is carried out by **the burning of** the **soul, but** the **body** remains **intact.**

Having established the procedure, the *Gemara* now asks: from where do we know that *srefah* is performed in this manner — burning the inside of the body while leaving the outside intact — rather than by external burning? The answer is: *atya sreifa sreifa me'adas Korach* — one derives the method of *srefah* by means of a *gezeirah shavah* from the case of the congregation of Korach: *mah le'halan sreifas neshamah v'guf kayam af kan sreifas neshamah v'guf kayam* — just as there, the soul was burned while the body remained intact, so too here, *srefah* by the court means burning the inner soul-fire while the body remains. Rabbi Eliezer disputes the source: *atya sreifa sreifa mi'bnei Aharon* — the model should be derived instead from the case of Nadav and Avihu, the sons of Aaron, where the same principle applies: *mah le'halan sreifas neshamah v'guf kayam af kan sreifas neshamah v'guf kayam*.

### Korach Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּיָלֵיף מֵעֲדַת קֹרַח, מְנָא לֵיהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֵת מַחְתּוֹת הַחַטָּאִים הָאֵלֶּה בְּנַפְשֹׁתָם״, שֶׁנִּשְׁמָתָן נִשְׂרֶפֶת וְגוּף קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does the one who derives** that burning means this kind of death **from the assembly of Korah** derive that their bodies were not burned? The Gemara answers: He derives it from that **which is written: "And the firepans of these men who have sinned with their souls"** (Numbers 17:3), which indicates **that** only **their souls were burned, but** their **bodies were intact.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: הָהִיא שְׂרֵיפָה מַמָּשׁ הִיא. וּמַאי ״בְּנַפְשֹׁתָם״? שֶׁנִּתְחַיְּיבוּ שְׂרֵיפָה עַל עִסְקֵי נַפְשׁוֹתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** Sage, who does not derive that burning means this kind of death from here, interpret the deaths of the assembly of Korah? The Gemara answers: **That** burning **was actual burning** of their bodies. **And what** is the meaning of the term: **"With their souls"?** It means **that they were deemed liable** to be killed by **burning due to matters of** sustaining **their souls,** i.e., they sinned because Korah helped them fulfill their bodily desires, and consequently they followed him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״בְּחַנְפֵי לַעֲגֵי מָעוֹג חָרֹק עָלַי שִׁנֵּימוֹ״? בִּשְׁבִיל חֲנוּפָּה שֶׁהֶחְנִיפוּ לְקֹרַח עַל עִסְקֵי לְגִימָה, חָרַק עֲלֵיהֶן שַׂר שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם שִׁנָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** This latter explanation is **in accordance with** the statement **of Reish Lakish. As Reish Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "With the flattering mockeries of spitefulness [*maog*] they gnash at me with their teeth"** (Psalms 35:16)? It means that **because of the flattery** of those people **who flattered Korah over matters of eating,** i.e., because of the food and drink that he would give them, **the minister of Gehenna gnashed his teeth over them,** as they eventually sinned and fell into his hands. The word *maog* is interpreted homiletically here as alluding to *uga*, cake.

How does one know that the burning of Korach's congregation was *sreifas neshamah v'guf kayam* rather than literal external burning? The verse says: *es machtos hachata'im ha'eleh b'nafshosam* — "the fire-pans of these sinners against their souls." Reading *b'nafshosam* as indicating the soul-dimension of the punishment, the *Gemara* derives that it was their inner souls — *nishmasam* — that were burned, while their bodies remained. This is reinforced by Reish Lakish's reading of another verse: *b'chanfei la'agei ma'og charak alai shinemo* — which he interprets as referring to the flattery Korach used by means of food and feasting to seduce the leaders of Israel. *Bishvil chanufah she'hechanifu l'Korach al iskei legimah* — through the flattery associated with eating, *chireka alav sar shel Gehenom shinav* — the officer of *Gehinnom* was given license to sharpen his teeth against them. The word *nefesh* or *nafshosam* carries an appetitive connotation in Semitic languages — it can denote the throat, or one's appetitive desires — and so *b'nafshosam* is read as: they were punished through the very appetitive faculty with which they sinned, having been seduced by Korach's feasts.

Those who reject the Korach model read *b'nafshosam* differently: not as a reference to soul-versus-body, but rather as explaining the *cause* of their punishment — *she'nischayvu sreifa al iskei nafshosam* — they became liable to *srefah* on account of what they did with their appetitive souls. The burning itself was literal and physical.

### Nadav Avihu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּיָלֵיף מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן מְנָא לֵיהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּמֻתוּ לִפְנֵי ה׳״, כְּעֵין מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does the one who derives** that burning means this kind of death **from the sons of Aaron** derive that their bodies were not burned? The Gemara answers: He derives it from that **which is written:** "And fire came out from before the Lord, and devoured them, **and they died before the Lord"** (Leviticus 10:2). The term "and they died" indicates that it was **similar to** a natural **death,** in which the body remains intact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: הָהוּא שְׂרֵיפָה מַמָּשׁ הֲוַאי. וּמַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וַיָּמֻתוּ״? דְּאַתְחֵיל בְּהוּ מִגַּוַּאי, כְּעֵין מִיתָה. דְּתַנְיָא, אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן דּוֹסְתַּאי אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי חוּטִין שֶׁל אֵשׁ יָצְאוּ מִבֵּית קוֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים וְנֶחְלְקוּ לְאַרְבַּע, וְנִכְנְסוּ שְׁנַיִם בְּחוֹטְמוֹ שֶׁל זֶה וּשְׁנַיִם בְּחוֹטְמוֹ שֶׁל זֶה וּשְׂרָפוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** Sage, who does not derive that burning means this kind of death from here, interpret the death of the sons of Aaron? The Gemara answers: **That** burning **was actual burning. And** in that case, **what** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And they died"?** It means **that** the fire **started from within them,** and therefore it was **similar to** a natural **death,** which occurs within the person. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Abba Yosei ben Dostai says: Two threads of fire came out of the Holy of Holies and split into four, and two entered the nostrils of this** one, **and** the other **two** entered **the nostrils of that** one, **and** the threads of fire **burned them.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַתֹּאכַל אוֹתָם״! אוֹתָם, וְלֹא בִּגְדֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "And devoured them [*vatokhal otam*]"?** The usage of the expanded term "*vatokhal otam*," instead of the terser *vatokhlem*, indicates a limitation, i.e., only they were consumed, to the exclusion of their bodies. The Gemara answers: The verse means that the fire devoured **"them," but not their clothes.**

What about the case of Nadav and Avihu? Those who derive from *bnei Aharon* that it was *sreifas neshamah v'guf kayam* point to the verse *vayamutu lifnei Hashem* — "and they died before God" — reading *ke'ein mita*, "like death." In natural death, the body remains intact while the soul departs; therefore the verse's use of *mita* language implies that their bodies were left intact while the fire consumed only their inner being. Those who reject this model read *vayamutu* differently: the fire began on the inside (*de'itchilu behu migavai*) in a death-like fashion, but ultimately consumed their bodies as well. A *braita* cited by Abba Yose ben Dosai provides additional detail: *shnei chutin shel esh yatz'u mibeit kodesh hakodashim v'nechleku le'arba* — two strands of fire emerged from the Holy of Holies and split into four; two entered the nostrils of Nadav and two entered the nostrils of Avihu, *v'sarfu osam* — and burned them. The use of *sarfu osam* — "burned them" — suggests their bodies were ultimately consumed, supporting the view that the external body was destroyed as well. Tosafot notes that the verse *vayisa'um b'chutonotam* — they removed them in their garments — indicates the bodies were still present, and suggests that perhaps at minimum the skeletal remains survived, accounting for the *tumas meis* incurred by those who carried them.

### Human Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מִפָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים, מָה לְהַלָּן שְׂרֵיפָה מַמָּשׁ – אַף כָּאן שְׂרֵיפָה מַמָּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us derive** the correct method of execution by burning **from** the *halakha* of the **bull** offerings **that are burned. Just as there,** the reference is to **actual burning, so too here,** perhaps there should be **actual burning.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מֵאָדָם הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן אָדָם, חוֹטֵא, נְשָׁמָה, פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **one should derive** the *halakha* with regard to capital punishment **from** the death of **a person,** i.e., either from the assembly of Korah or the sons of Aaron, **as** they share common elements: They deal with **a person, a sinner,** and **a soul** that is taken through burning, i.e., the person dies as a result of the burning. Furthermore, the *halakha* of **an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time [*piggul*],** is not relevant in either case, whereas it is relevant to bull offerings that are burned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִפָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן מַכְשִׁיר לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary, one should derive** the *halakha* with regard to capital punishment **from** the **bull** offerings **that are burned, as** both **enable** the fulfillment of a mitzva, whereas the deaths of the assembly of Korah and the sons of Aaron were not mitzvot. Furthermore, both of these are fixed *halakhot* **for** all future **generations,** whereas the deaths of the assembly of Korah and the sons of Aaron were onetime incidents.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ נְפִישִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Those** elements that are shared by capital punishment and the deaths of the assembly of Korah and the sons of Aaron **are more numerous** than the elements that are shared by capital punishment and the bulls that are burned. Therefore, the *halakha* is derived from the assembly of Korah and the sons of Aaron.

Why not derive the method of court *srefah* instead from the *parim hanisrafim* — the bulls that are burned as part of certain *chatat* offerings? In that case, the body of the animal is genuinely burned externally on a pyre. The *Gemara* answers: *mistabra me'adam hava lei lemeilaf* — it is more appropriate to derive the method of executing a human being from cases involving human beings rather than from cases involving animals. The points of similarity between the court *srefah* and the cases of Korach and Nadav v'Avihu are: both involve a person (*adam*); both involve a sinner (*chotei*); and in both cases the act of burning is itself the act of killing — unlike the *parim*, which were already slaughtered before being burned. Additionally, the issue of *pigul* is irrelevant in the human context. The *Gemara* entertains the counter-argument that the *parim* model has its own advantages: both burning a *korban* and court execution are fulfilling a *mitzvah* (*machshir*), and both are practices given *ledoros* — for all future generations — rather than being one-time historical events, as the divine punishments of Korach and Nadav v'Avihu were. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* concludes: *haneich nefishi* — the points of similarity between the human cases are more numerous, and those models take precedence.

### Mercy Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּיָלֵיף מֵעֲדַת קֹרַח, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא יָלֵיף מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן? הָהוּא שְׂרֵיפָה מַמָּשׁ הֲוַאי. וְנֵילַף מִינַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **the one who derives** this *halakha* **from the assembly of Korah, what is the reason he does not derive** it **from the sons of Aaron?** Because in his opinion **that was actual burning. But** if this is the case, **let us derive from** the death of the sons of Aaron that execution by burning should be performed with actual burning; why does he derive from the assembly of Korah that hot lead should be used?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״, בְּרוֹר לוֹ מִיתָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The **verse states: "And you shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), which teaches that even with regard to a condemned prisoner one should **select a good,** i.e., a compassionate, **death for him.** The method of burning described in the mishna is certainly faster and less painful than the burning of the entire body.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּאִיכָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And since there is** the *halakha* **of Rav Naḥman, why do I** need the **verbal analogy?** Even without this proof execution by burning would have been performed with hot lead, as it is a less painful form of death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָאו גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים – לָאו שְׂרֵיפָה הִיא כְּלָל. וְאִי מִשּׁוּם ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״, לַפֵּישׁ לֵיהּ חֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִישְׂרוֹף לַעֲגַל. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Were it not** for the **verbal analogy, I would say** that a death that includes **the burning of** the **soul, but** the **body** itself remains **intact, is not burning at all,** and that it does not fulfill the mitzva of execution by burning. **And if** it is necessary to alleviate the condemned one's pain **due to** the *halakha* of **"And you shall love your neighbor as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18), let** the court **increase for him the bundles of branches, so that he will burn quicker.** Therefore, the verbal analogy **teaches us** that even internal burning is regarded as burning, and once this has been established it is taken into consideration that he must be executed in the least painful way.

Once it is established that there are two valid models — Korach (body intact) and Nadav v'Avihu (body burned, on one reading) — the question arises: the one who derives from Korach that the body was intact, why does he not instead derive from Nadav v'Avihu's case (on the reading that the body was burned) the opposite conclusion? And vice versa: the one who derives from Nadav v'Avihu that the body was intact, why does he not derive from Korach's case (on the reading that the body was burned) the opposite conclusion? Each disputant, in other words, has access to both models — one showing the body intact and one showing the body burned. Why does each choose the model where the body is intact as the paradigm for court *srefah*?

The answer comes from Rav Nachman citing Rabba bar Avuha: the verse *ve'ahavta lerei'acha kamocha* — "love your neighbor as yourself" — generates the principle *beror lo misa yafa* — choose for the condemned person the most pleasant available death. Given two viable models of *srefah*, one must choose the less painful and less disfiguring option. Pouring molten lead down the throat, as horrifying as it sounds, is far preferable to external burning of the body, which would be agonizing and disfiguring in the extreme.

However, the *Gemara* then asks: once we have the principle of *ve'ahavta lerei'acha kamocha* operating, why did we need the *gezeirah shavah* at all? Why not simply derive from *ve'ahavta lerei'acha kamocha* directly that we should use the less painful method? The answer is that without the *gezeirah shavah*, the very possibility of internal burning as a form of *srefah* would never have been considered a valid option. The *pshat* of the verse commanding *srefah* points toward literal external burning. One would simply have tried to fulfill the external burning as quickly as possible — by building a very large fire, for instance — so as to minimize suffering, all within the framework of *ve'ahavta lerei'acha kamocha*. The *gezeirah shavah* is what opens up the option of internal burning as a valid form of *srefah* at all. Once that option is established as halachically legitimate, *ve'ahavta lerei'acha kamocha* then compels us to choose it over external burning. The two principles operate in tandem: the *gezeirah shavah* defines the range of available options; *ve'ahavta lerei'acha kamocha* determines which option within that range must be chosen.

## Theological Themes

### Body Image Approach

Two modern scholars have offered complementary frameworks for understanding the larger themes animating the *arba misos* system. Aaron Shemesh's work focuses on the principle that the human body was created *b'tzelem Elokim* — in the image of God — and that a core organizing theme across all four methods of execution is the minimization of bodily disfigurement. On this reading, the entire elaborated procedure of *srefah* — immersing in dung to hold the person steady, wrapping the rope in a soft cloth to avoid lacerations, pouring molten lead rather than external burning — reflects a sustained effort to carry out the required punishment while preserving the integrity of the body as far as possible. The system's insistence on internal rather than external burning, the various protections built into *skila* (pushing from a height rather than direct stoning, the care taken with the body afterward), and analogous details in the other executions all point, on Shemesh's account, to this unifying concern.

### Divine Death Model

Dvora Steinmetz offers a different framework, centered on the phrase *ke'ein mita* — "like death" — which the *Gemara* uses to describe what the fire accomplished in the case of Nadav and Avihu. Steinmetz asks the foundational question of what authorizes a human community to take another human life at all. Her answer emphasizes the concept of executing God's judgment: the court, in carrying out a death penalty, acts as an instrument of divine will. If that is the case, then the form of death brought about by the court should as closely as possible replicate the form of death that God himself brings about. In natural death — and in the divinely ordained deaths of Korach's assembly and of Nadav and Avihu — the body remains and the soul departs. By insisting on *ke'ein mita*, by modeling court *srefah* on these paradigms of divinely inflicted death, the court aligns its act of execution as closely as possible with divine action. On this reading, what is at stake is not primarily the preservation of the body's image, but the theological legitimacy of the court's authority to act as the executor of divine judgment.

## Nadav Avihu Backstory

### Ambition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְבָר הָיוּ מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן מְהַלְּכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְנָדָב וַאֲבִיהוּא מְהַלְּכִין אַחֲרֵיהֶן, וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֲרֵיהֶן. אָמַר לוֹ נָדָב לַאֲבִיהוּא: אֵימָתַי יָמוּתוּ שְׁנֵי זְקֵנִים הַלָּלוּ וַאֲנִי וְאַתָּה נַנְהִיג אֶת הַדּוֹר? אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הֲנִרְאֶה מִי קוֹבֵר אֶת מִי.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, an aggadic midrash on this subject is quoted: **And** it had **already** happened that **Moses and Aaron were walking on** their **way, and Nadav and Avihu were walking behind them, and the entire Jewish people** were walking **behind them. Nadav said to Avihu: When will** it happen that **these two old men will die and you and I will lead the generation,** as we are their heirs? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to them: We shall see who buries whom.**

Having mentioned Nadav and Avihu in the context of the *srefah* discussion, the *Gemara* offers a digression on the backstory of their deaths. The scene is set: Moses and Aaron walked in front, with Nadav and Avihu behind them, and all of Israel behind them. It was a position of extraordinary honor — only Moses and Aaron preceded them. Yet it was not enough. *Amar lo Nadav l'Avihu: eimasai yamusu shnei zkeinim halalu v'ani v'atah nanhig es hador?* — "When will these two old men die," Nadav said to Avihu, "so that you and I can lead the generation?" God's response: *nireh mi kover es mi* — "We shall see who buries whom."

### Camel Proverb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: נְפִישֵׁי גַּמְלֵי סָבֵי דִּטְעִינִי מַשְׁכֵי דְהוּגְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: This** explains the adage **that people say: Many are the old camels that are loaded with the skins of young camels.**

Rav Pappa cites a popular saying that captures this dynamic: *nefishei gamli sabei d'teini mashkei d'huganei* — there are many old camels carrying on their backs the hides of young camels. The proverb cautions against assuming the young will naturally outlast and replace the old. Rashi notes that a young camel's recklessness and excess energy can lead to its premature death, while the old camel endures. Nadav and Avihu, for all their brilliance and position, did not survive Moses and Aaron.

### Seduction Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: לְמָה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם דּוֹמֶה לִפְנֵי עַם הָאָרֶץ? בַּתְּחִלָּה, דּוֹמֶה לְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל זָהָב. סִיפֵּר הֵימֶנּוּ, דּוֹמֶה לְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל כֶּסֶף. נֶהֱנָה מִמֶּנּוּ, דּוֹמֶה לְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל חֶרֶשׂ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּר, שׁוּב אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **To what is a Torah scholar compared** when he is standing **before an ignoramus? At first,** when he does not know him, the ignoramus considers him to be **like a goblet [*lekiton*] of gold.** Once **he has conversed with him** concerning mundane matters, he considers him to be **like a goblet of silver,** i.e., the stature of the Torah scholar is downgraded in the eyes of the ignoramus. Once the scholar **has received benefit from** the ignoramus, **he** considers him to be **like an earthenware goblet,** which **once broken cannot be fixed.**

The *Gemara* then turns to Korach. Rabbi Elazar opens with a question: to what is a *talmid chacham* compared in the eyes of an *am ha'aretz*? Initially, *domeh l'kiton shel zahav* — he appears like a vessel of gold, something precious and untouchable, evoking deep respect and deference. If the *am ha'aretz* begins to converse with him, *siper heimenu domeh l'kiton shel kesef* — the *talmid chacham* is now like a silver vessel: still valuable, but somewhat diminished. If the *am ha'aretz* then provides the *talmid chacham* with some benefit — a loan of money, an invitation to a Shabbat meal — *domeh l'kiton shel cheres* — he has become a vessel of earthenware. And an earthenware vessel, once broken, *shuv ein lo takanah* — can never be repaired. Once that familiarity crosses a certain threshold, no subsequent effort can restore the original aura of authority and reverence.

This parable explains the strategy Korach employed. By inviting the great figures of Israel — *kru'ei eidah, anshei shem* — to his feast and seducing them through hospitality and flattery, *bishvil chanufah she'hechanifu l'Korach al iskei legimah*, he made them his beneficiaries and eliminated the natural distance that would otherwise have protected them from his influence. Having become recipients of his generosity, they could no longer view him with the critical detachment needed to resist his arguments. The same relationship that stripped the *talmid chacham* of his untouchable golden status stripped these leaders of their judgment.

## Execution Authority

### Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָאתָ אֶל הַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם וְאֶל הַשֹּׁפֵט אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם״ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁיֵּשׁ כֹּהֵן יֵשׁ מִשְׁפָּט, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין כֹּהֵן אֵין מִשְׁפָּט.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from the verse: **"And you shall come to the priests, the Levites, and to the judge that will be in those days"** (Deuteronomy 17:9), that **at a time when there is a priest** serving in the Temple, i.e., when the Temple is built, **there is judgment** of capital cases. By inference, **at a time when there is no priest, there is no judgment** of capital cases.

The *Mishnah* notes the structural prerequisites for legitimate court executions. The *braita* derives from the verse *u'vasa el hakohanim haleviyim v'el hashoftim asher yiheyeh bayamim hahem* — "you shall come to the priests, the Levites, and to the judge who will be in those days" — that *srefah* requires three simultaneous conditions: the presence of a functioning *kohen* class (meaning a *Beis HaMikdash*), and a *Sanhedrin* seated within the *Beis HaMikdash*. *Bizman sheyesh Kohen — yesh mishpat*; *bizman she-ain Kohen — ain mishpat*. Outside these conditions, the formal death penalties cannot be administered.

### Rav Chama Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִימַּרְתָּא בַּת טָלֵי, בַּת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּינְּתָה הֲוַאי. אַקְּפַהּ (רַב) חָמָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה חֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת וְשַׂרְפַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Imrata bat Talei was a priest's daughter who committed adultery. Rav Ḥama bar Toviyya surrounded her** with **bundles of branches and burned her.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: טְעָה בְּתַרְתֵּי, טְעָה בִּדְרַב מַתְנָה, וּטְעָה בִּדְתַנְיָא. ״וּבָאתָ אֶל הַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם וְאֶל הַשֹּׁפֵט אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם״ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁיֵּשׁ כֹּהֵן יֵשׁ מִשְׁפָּט, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין כֹּהֵן אֵין מִשְׁפָּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says:** Rav Ḥama bar Toviyya **erred with regard to two** *halakhot*. **He erred with regard to** the ruling **of Rav Mattana,** i.e., that burning is performed using a wick of lead, **and he erred with regard to that which is taught** in a *baraita*: It is derived from the verse: **"And you shall come to the priests, the Levites, and to the judge that will be in those days"** (Deuteronomy 17:9), that **at a time when there is a priest** serving in the Temple, i.e., when the Temple is built, **there is judgment** of capital cases. By inference, **at a time when there is no priest, there is no judgment** of capital cases.

Against this background, the case of the *Amoraic*-period figure Rav Chama bar Tuvia is deeply troubling. Imrasa bat Tali, a *bat kohen* who had committed adultery, was wrapped by Rav Chama in bundles of branches and burned alive — *akphsah chavilei zemoros v'sarfah*. This was carried out in the *Amoraic* period, long after the *Sanhedrin* had ceased to function and court executions could no longer be administered. Rav Yosef's verdict: *ta'ah b'tarti* — he made two errors. First, he violated the ruling of Rav Masna (which reflects the *Mishnah*) that *srefah* is carried out by pouring molten lead internally, not by external burning. Second, and more fundamentally, he violated the *braita*'s requirement that formal executions can only be carried out *bizman sheyesh Kohen* — when the *Beis HaMikdash* is standing and the *Sanhedrin* is in place. Neither condition obtained in his time. What Rav Chama did was an extra-legal act of execution that bore no resemblance to the *halachically* prescribed procedure, carried out at a time when no such execution was permissible.

### Testimony Dismissal

> **Hebrew/Aramara:** הָכִי אֲמַר לְהוּ, וְהָכִי אַהְדַּרוּ לֵיהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק: זְכוּרַנִי כְּשֶׁהָיִיתִי תִּינוֹק וּמוּרְכָּב עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ שֶׁל אַבָּא, וְהֵבִיאוּ בַּת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּינְּתָה וְהִקִּיפוּהָ חֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת וּשְׂרָפוּהָ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: קָטָן הָיִיתָ, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַקָּטָן. שְׁנֵי מַעֲשִׂים הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did** Rabbi Elazar ben Tzadok **say that to** the Sages, **and did** the Sages **answer him in that manner? But isn't** a different version of the exchange **taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says: I remember when I was a child, and was riding on** my **father's shoulders. And they brought a priest's daughter who had committed adultery, and surrounded her** with **bundles of branches and burned her.** The Sages **said to him: You were a minor** at that time **and one cannot bring proof from** the testimony of **a minor,** as perhaps you did not understand the proceedings properly. The two versions of this exchange do not accord with each other. The Gemara answers: **There were two** separate **incidents,** and Rabbi Elazar ben Tzadok testified with regard to both.

Returning to Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok's testimony in the *Mishnah* — that he witnessed a *bat kohen* who had committed adultery being wrapped in bundles and burned — the *Gemara* notes that the response he received was: *mipnei she'lo hayah beis din shel osah sha'ah baki* — the *beis din* of that time did not know what it was doing. Rav Yosef adds: *beis din shel tzedukim havay* — this was a *Sadducean* court. The *Tzadukim* were known as literalists who read the Torah's *srefah* command as mandating actual external burning. This explains the practice without vindicating it; from the Rabbinic perspective, the *Sadducean* courts were executing in a manner that violated the correct *halakhah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי אֲמַר לְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא? אִילֵימָא הָא קַמַּיְיתָא אֲמַר לְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ כְּשֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל, וְלָא אַשְׁגַּחוּ בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ כְּשֶׁהוּא קָטָן, וְאַשְׁגַּחוּ בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Which** incident **did he tell** the Sages about **first? If we say** that **first he told them** about **this first** incident, i.e., the one that is recounted in the mishna, this is unreasonable; if he first **told** them about the incident that occurred **when he was an adult, and they paid no attention to him,** but rejected his statement by responding that the court was not proficient in *halakha*, **would he tell** them afterward about the incident that occurred **when he was a small child and** think that **they would pay attention to him?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָא אֲמַר לְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא, וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: קָטָן הָיִיתָ, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ כְּשֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל, וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה בָּקִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is clear that **he first told them** about **that** incident, i.e., the one recounted in the *baraita*, **and they said to him: You were a minor,** and one cannot bring proof from the testimony of a minor. **And** then **he told them** about the incident that occurred **when he was an adult, and they said to him:** The court did so **because the court at that time was not proficient** in *halakha*.

### Halakha Divergence

The incident points to a striking divergence. The *halakhah* as laid out in this *Mishnah* and *Gemara* prescribes an elaborate, carefully constrained procedure. Historical practice — whether by *Sadducean* courts during the *Beis HaMikdash* period, or by figures like Rav Chama bar Tuvia in the *Amoraic* period — sometimes involved literal external burning with none of the procedural safeguards described. The Rabbinic tradition consistently dismisses such practices as error, whether due to ignorance of the proper method or to the absence of the prerequisite institutional framework. This gap between the *halakhah* as theorized and execution as historically practiced is one of the notable features of this entire tractate, and scholars have debated at length how much of the *Gemara*'s elaborate procedural system ever actually governed real judicial behavior.

### Two Accounts

The *Gemara* harmonizes the two accounts attributed to Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok. In the *Mishnah*, he speaks as an adult witness and is told the *beis din* did not know what it was doing. In a *baraita*, he recalls the same kind of incident as something he witnessed as a child (*tinok*), riding on his father's shoulders, and is told: *katan hayisa v'ein mevi'in raayah min hakatan* — you were a minor; testimony from a minor cannot be relied upon. These are two distinct stories, two distinct incidents. The *Gemara* then notes that the order in which Rabbi Elazar must have presented them: he could not have first described what he saw as an adult and been dismissed, and then tried to prove the same point with a childhood memory. Had the adult testimony been rejected, appealing to a childhood recollection would have been pointless. The logical order must therefore be: he first described what he saw as a child; was told he was too young; and then offered his adult testimony — which was in turn dismissed by attributing the incident to a *beis din* that simply did not know the correct procedure. Both accounts were dismissed, and the *Gemara*'s picture of *srefah* as described in the *Mishnah* stands as the authoritative normative prescription, regardless of how executions may or may not have actually been carried out in practice.
