# Zevachim 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kol HaTadir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַתָּדִיר מֵחֲבֵירוֹ – קוֹדֵם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ. הַתְּמִידִין קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסָפִין. מוּסְפֵי שַׁבָּת קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ. מוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִלְּבַד עֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר אֲשֶׁר לְעֹלַת הַתָּמִיד תַּעֲשׂוּ אֶת אֵלֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** offering **that is** more **frequent than another precedes the other** offering. Therefore, **the daily offerings precede the additional offerings,** which are sacrificed only on certain days. When Shabbat and the New Moon coincide, the **additional Shabbat offerings precede** the **additional New Moon offerings.** Likewise, the **additional New Moon offerings precede** the **additional New Year offerings.** The mishna cites the source for the principle that the frequent precedes the less frequent: **As it is stated** with regard to the additional offerings of the first day of Passover: **"Besides the burnt offering of the morning, which is for a daily burnt offering, you shall offer these"** (Numbers 28:23). The verse indicates that the daily offering is sacrificed first, and then the additional offerings are sacrificed.

### Rashi Examples

The opening of this *perek* presents a foundational principle of the Temple service: *kol hatadir mechavero kodem* — the more frequent offering takes precedence. The *mishna* immediately applies this to the most obvious case: *hatamidim kodmim lamusafim* — the daily sacrifice (*tamid*), brought every single day, precedes the additional offerings (*musafim*), which occur only on specific days. This ordering reflects the fundamental structure of the *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash*: the morning *tamid* is the first *korban* offered each day, and the afternoon *tamid* is always the last, with virtually every other *korban* sandwiched between them — the sole exception being the *korban Pesach*, which must be brought after the afternoon *tamid*.

Rashi, at the very opening of this *perek*, offers the example of *Shabbat tadira meRosh Chodesh* — *Shabbat* occurs once a week while *Rosh Chodesh* occurs once a month, so *Shabbat* has priority. One might wonder why Rashi chooses *Shabbat* and *Rosh Chodesh* as his illustration, when the *mishna* itself provides what seems a more natural example. The *mishna*'s own case — the *tamid* preceding the *musafim* — is arguably a cleaner illustration of the principle, since the *tamid* is brought literally every single day.

### Tamid Over Musafim

The priority of the *tamid* over the *musafim* reflects the very logic embedded in the term *musaf* itself: the additional offering is, by definition, added on top of the *tamid*. The parallel in *tefillah* is familiar — *mussaf* is recited after *shacharit*, and when *Shabbat* and *Rosh Chodesh* coincide, *Shabbat* is mentioned first in the *mussaf* prayer, precisely because it is the more frequent of the two.

### Musafim Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּסְפֵי שַׁבָּת קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ. מוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה
>
> **Translation:** When Shabbat and the New Moon coincide, the **additional Shabbat offerings precede** the **additional New Moon offerings.** Likewise, the **additional New Moon offerings precede** the **additional New Year offerings.**

The *mishna* continues: *musfei Shabbat kodmin lemusfei Rosh Chodesh* — the *musaf* of *Shabbat* precedes the *musaf* of *Rosh Chodesh*, since *Shabbat* occurs weekly while *Rosh Chodesh* is monthly. And *musfei Rosh Chodesh kodmin lemusfei Rosh Hashanah* — *Rosh Chodesh* precedes *Rosh Hashanah*, since *Rosh Chodesh* recurs every month and *Rosh Hashanah* comes only once a year.

### Parshat Pinchas Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִלְּבַד עֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר אֲשֶׁר לְעֹלַת הַתָּמִיד תַּעֲשׂוּ אֶת אֵלֶּה״
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated** with regard to the additional offerings of the first day of Passover: **"Besides the burnt offering of the morning, which is for a daily burnt offering, you shall offer these"** (Numbers 28:23). The verse indicates that the daily offering is sacrificed first, and then the additional offerings are sacrificed.

The *mishna* cites a scriptural basis for this rule from *Parshat Pinchas*: *milvad olat haboker asher le'olat hatamid ta'asu et eleh* — "besides the morning *olah*, which is the continual *tamid*, you shall do these." The verse describing the *musaf* of *Chag HaMatzot* specifies that the *musaf* is brought in addition to the *tamid*, establishing the *tamid*'s precedence. The principle of *musaf* being supplementary to — and therefore secondary to — the *tamid* is thus embedded in the *Torah*'s own language.

### Menalan Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא לַן? מְנָא לַן?! כִּדְקָאָמַר טַעְמָא: ״מִלְּבַד עֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר״! דִּילְמָא תְּמִידִין הֵן דְּקָדְמִי לְמוּסָפִין – מִשּׁוּם דִּתְדִירִי; מוּסָפִין לְמוּסָפִין מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive the principle that the frequent precedes the less frequent? The Gemara expresses puzzlement at this question: **From where do we** derive this? One must say **that the reason is stated** in the mishna, which says that it is derived from the verse: **"Besides the burnt offering of the morning."** The Gemara explains: If that verse is the only source, it could be claimed that **perhaps it is** only the **daily offerings that precede** the **additional offerings, because they are** far more **frequent,** as they are sacrificed daily. With regard to the precedence of a relatively frequent **additional offering over** a relatively less frequent **additional offering,** the question arises: **From where do we** derive this?

The *gemara* then asks: *menalan?* — where do we derive the rule? The *mishna* already quoted a *pasuk*, so why must the question be asked again? The *gemara* explains: from the words *milvad olat haboker* alone, one might have thought the rule applies only to the relationship between the *tamid* and the *musafim* — that the daily sacrifice always precedes any additional offering simply because it occurs every day. But that would not yet tell us which *musaf* takes precedence when multiple *musafim* apply on the same day — such as on *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh*, *Shabbat Chol HaMoed*, or *Rosh Hashanah*. Hence the *gemara*'s question: *musafim lemusafim mena lan?* — from where do we know the ordering among the *musafim* themselves?

### Rabbi Ila's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כָּאֵלֶּה תַּעֲשׂוּ לַיּוֹם שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ – אֵלֶּה כָּאֵלֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ile'a said** that it is derived from the fact **that the verse states** with regard to the additional offerings of Passover: **"Like these you shall offer daily, for seven days"** (Numbers 28:24). This verse, which immediately follows the one cited previously, indicates that the principle that governs **these** additional offerings shall be **like** the principle governing **those** daily offerings and additional offerings mentioned previously, i.e., the more frequent precedes the less frequent.

Rabbi Ila answers from the word *ka'eleh*. The *pasuk* describing the *musaf* of the seven days of *Pesach* — *ka'eleh ta'asu layom shivat yamim* — says "like these you shall do for each of the seven days." Rabbi Ila derives from the prefix *kaf* ("like") an extension of the *tamid*-before-*musaf* principle into the domain of *musafim* themselves: whichever *musaf* is more frequent takes precedence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ! אִם כֵּן, נִיכְתּוֹב: ״אֵלֶּה תַּעֲשׂוּ לַיּוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But that** verse **is required to** teach **its own** *halakha*, that the additional offerings of the first day of Passover recur on each day of Passover. The Gemara answers: **If so, let** the verse **write: These you shall offer daily.** Since the Torah writes: "Like these," both the *halakha* that these additional offerings are brought on each day of Passover and the principle concerning precedence can be derived from this verse.

The *gemara* challenges: isn't the word *ka'eleh* needed for its own plain meaning — to teach that the *musaf* of each day of *Pesach* is equal to that of the first day? If so, how can it simultaneously be a source for the frequency principle among *musafim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כְּתַב ״אֵלֶּה תַּעֲשׂוּ לַיּוֹם שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֵלֶּה לְשִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים (אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא)! ״לַיּוֹם״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If** the verse **had written** only: **These you shall offer daily for the seven days, I would say** that **these** offerings mentioned in the previous verse are sacrificed in total, over **the seven days.** Therefore, the Torah writes: "Like these," to teach that they are all sacrificed each day. The Gemara counters: That interpretation is not possible, as the phrase: "You shall offer **daily," is written** in the verse, which indicates that these offerings are sacrificed on each of the seven days.

The *gemara* responds: had the *Torah* wished only to convey that the subsequent days equal the first, it could have written simply *eleh ta'asu layom* — "these you shall do each day." The addition of *ka'eleh* ("like these") is what extends the teaching. But then the *gemara* pushes further: if the verse had merely said *eleh ta'asu layom shivat yamim*, one might have thought that the *korbanot* of *Pesach* need not each be brought on a specific day — that the various animals could be distributed across the seven days in any combination. The word *layom* ("for each day") addresses that concern. And if it had said *eleh layom*, one might have thought that specification applied only to the first day of *Pesach*, with the remaining six days left without instruction. The word *ta'asu* ("you shall do") signals that all the offerings must be performed. Taken together, these elements work out the plain meaning, leaving the *kaf* of *ka'eleh* available for Rabbi Ila's derivation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי אֵימָא: אֵלֶּה לַיּוֹם, אֲבָל שְׁאָר יוֹמֵי לָא יָדַעְנָא כַּמָּה! אָמַר קְרָא ״תַּעֲשׂוּ״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל הָעֲשִׂיּוֹת שָׁווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And still,** one can **say** that **these** specific offerings are required **for the** first **day; but** with regard to the **other days, I do not know how many** offerings are to be sacrificed. Therefore, the term "like these" is needed to teach this, and cannot be used for the principle that relatively frequent additional offerings precede relatively less frequent additional offerings. The Gemara answers: That interpretation is also not possible, as **the verse states: "You shall offer [*ta'asu*],"** indicating **that all the** sacrificial **rites [*asiyyot*]** on all the days of Passover **should be equal.** Therefore, the term "like these" is in fact superfluous and can be cited as the source of the principle of precedence.

It is worth noting that the *gemara*'s extensive exegetical labor around this *pasuk* reflects a broader phenomenon: unlike *Sukkot*, where each day of *Chol HaMoed* has its own distinct *musaf* and individual character already expressed in the *Torah*, the days of *Pesach* are largely undifferentiated. The *Torah* devotes one paragraph to *Pesach* and multiple paragraphs to *Sukkot*, and this structural asymmetry carries *halachic* implications discussed in *Arachin* and elsewhere.

### Abaye's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר מִגּוּפַהּ דִּקְרָא: אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״מִלְּבַד עֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר״ וְתִישְׁתּוֹק, ״אֲשֶׁר לְעֹלַת הַתָּמִיד״ לְמָה לִי? לְמֵימַר דְּהָךְ דִּתְדִירָא תִּיקְדּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The application of the principle of precedence to all frequent offerings can be derived **from the verse itself,** cited in the mishna. The reason is that **if so,** that only the daily offering precedes less frequent offerings, **let the verse say** merely: **"Besides the burnt offering of the morning," and remain silent** from the rest of the verse. **Why do I** need the additional phrase: **"Which is for a daily burnt offering"?** This serves **to say that this** offering **that is** more **frequent,** i.e., any more frequent offering, **should precede** any less frequent offering.

Abaye disagrees with Rabbi Ila's convoluted derivation and maintains that the rule can be learned directly from the *gufa d'kra* — the body of the same *pasuk*. If the *Torah* wanted only to teach that the *musaf* is brought in addition to the *tamid*, it would have sufficed to write *milvad olat haboker*. Why add the clause *asher le'olat hatamid*? The superfluous phrase — *asher le'olat hatamid*, "which is the continual *tamid*" — comes to establish the general principle that whatever is more *tadir* takes precedence. Abaye thus reaches the same conclusion through a simpler derivation from the same *parasha*.

It is noteworthy that neither Rabbi Ila nor Abaye is content to treat the frequency-based ordering as a mere procedural rule or logical inference (*sevara*). Both insist on grounding it in a scriptural source, indicating that the principle carries the weight of a *Torah*-based ordinance.

## Kol HaMikudash

### Mishna HaMikudash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַמְקוּדָּשׁ מֵחֲבֵירוֹ – קוֹדֵם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ. דַּם הַחַטָּאת קוֹדֵם לְדַם הָעוֹלָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מְרַצֶּה. אֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה קוֹדְמִין לְאֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כָּלִיל לָאִישִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** offering **that is** more **sacred than another precedes the other** offering. The mishna elaborates: If there is blood of a sin offering and blood of a burnt offering to be presented, **the blood of the sin offering precedes the blood of the burnt offering because it effects acceptance,** i.e., atonement, for severe transgressions punishable by *karet*. Likewise, if there are limbs of a burnt offering and portions of a sin offering to be burned on the altar, the burning of **the limbs of** the **burnt offering precedes the portions of** the **sin offering, because** the burnt offering **is entirely** burned **in the flames** on the altar, whereas only part of the sin offering is burned.

The *mishna* now introduces a second governing principle: *kol hamikudash mechaveiro kodem lachaveiro* — whichever offering possesses greater holiness precedes the other. This rule stands alongside the frequency principle and raises an important question: which principle takes precedence when they conflict? The classic practical application is the question of whether one dons a *tallit* or *tefillin* first. The *tallit* is worn every day of the year and is thus more *tadir*, while *tefillin* bear *Shem Hashem* and are *mikudash mechaveiro*. This question is discussed extensively in *Orach Chayim*, and the common practice — though with variations — is to don the *tallit* first.

### Chatat vs. Olah Blood

The *mishna* begins its application of the *kedusha*-based rule: *dam hachattat kodem ledam ha'olah* — the blood of a *korban chattat* precedes the blood of a *korban olah*, because the *dam* of the *chattat* is the vehicle of *kappara*, the atonement process. With the *chattat*, the focus is on the blood; with the *olah*, the blood serves mainly to permit the *eivarim* — the sectioned limbs — to be brought onto the *mizbeiach*. Conversely, *eivarei olah kodmim le'imurei chattat* — the limbs of the *olah* precede the portions (*imurim*) of the *chattat*, because the *olah* is *kalil la'ishim*, entirely consumed by fire. The *mishna*'s language is precise: it uses *eivarim* for the limbs of the *olah* (since the entire body of the animal is burned) and reserves *imurim* for the *chattat*, referring only to the fats and offal placed on the *mizbeiach*.

### Sanctity Matrix

These two rulings together create a matrix of competing sanctities. The *chattat* and the *olah* each possess a domain in which they outrank the other. The blood of the *chattat* is of higher sanctity — it is *meratzeh*, it brings atonement — while the meat of the *olah* is of higher sanctity, since it is entirely offered to God and none is eaten. This gives rise to a framework with several evaluative dimensions: Who eats the *korban*? Is it *kalil* (entirely burned)? Is it *meratzeh* for a sin? How many *matanot* (blood placements on the *mizbeiach*) does it receive? How many days may it be eaten? Does it require accompanying elements (*nesachim*, *lechem*)? Does it possess *kedusha m'rechem* — inherent sanctity from birth?

### Chatat-Asham-Toda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת קוֹדֶמֶת לְאָשָׁם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדָּמָהּ נִיתָּן לְאַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת עַל הַיְסוֹד. אָשָׁם קוֹדֵם לְתוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים. תּוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר קוֹדְמִים לִשְׁלָמִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן נֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד, וּטְעוּנִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, although both effect atonement, **a sin offering precedes a guilt offering due to** the fact **that its blood is placed on** the **four corners** of the altar and the remnants of its blood are poured **on the base** of the altar, whereas the blood of the guilt offering is sprinkled on only two corners of the altar. **A guilt offering precedes a thanks offering and** the **nazirite's ram due to** the fact **that it is an offering of the most sacred order,** and the others are offerings of lesser sanctity. **A thanks offering and a nazirite's ram precede a peace offering due to** the fact **that they are eaten for one day,** like offerings of the most sacred order, whereas a peace offering is eaten for two days, **and** the thanks offering and nazirite's ram **require loaves** to be brought with them, four types with the thanks offering and two types with the nazirite's ram.

The *mishna* continues: *chattat kodemet le'asham mipnei shedama nittan le'arba karnot al hayesod* — the *chattat* precedes the *asham* because the blood of the *chattat* is applied to all four corners of the *mizbeiach* and poured at its base. The *asham*, by contrast, receives only two applications (which count as four, being placed on two opposite diagonal corners). The *asham*, in turn, precedes the *todah* and the *eil nazir* because the *asham* is *kodshei kodashim*, eaten only by the *kohanim* in the *azarah*, whereas the *todah* and *eil nazir* are *kodashim kalim*.

### Toda-Shelamim

The *todah* and *eil nazir* in turn precede the *shelamim*, because they require *lechem* — accompanying bread offerings — and are eaten only for one day, indicating a higher level of *kedusha* than the *shelamim*, which may be eaten for two days.

### Shelamim-Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְּׁלָמִים קוֹדְמִין לַבְּכוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן טְעוּנִין מַתַּן אַרְבַּע [וּסְמִיכָה] וּנְסָכִים, וּתְנוּפוֹת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק. הַבְּכוֹר קוֹדֵם לַמַּעֲשֵׂר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקְּדוּשָּׁתוֹ מֵרֶחֶם, וְנֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Sacrifice of **the peace offering precedes** sacrifice of **the firstborn** offering **due to** the fact **that** the peace offering **requires placing** the blood on the altar, in the form of two placements that are **four, and placing hands** on the head of the offering, **and libations, and the wavings of** the **breast and the thigh** by the priest and the owner; none of which is required for the firstborn offering. **The firstborn** offering **precedes the** animal **tithe** offering **because it is sanctified from** the **womb,** i.e., unlike the animal tithe offering it does not require consecration, **and it is eaten by the priests,** whereas everyone may partake of the animal tithe offering.

The *shelamim* precedes the *bechor* because the *shelamim* is attended by multiple *mitzvot*: *semichah* (laying of hands), *nesachim* (wine and flour libations), and *tenufah* (waving of the breast and thigh). The *bechor*, while possessing *kedusha m'rechem* — an inherent sanctity from the moment of birth — lacks these associated *mitzvot*. The *bechor* in turn precedes *ma'aser*, again on account of *kedusha m'rechem*, since the *ma'aser* animal's sanctity is entirely dependent on the human act of counting and designation.

### Ma'aser-Ofot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּעֲשֵׂר קוֹדֵם לָעוֹפוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא זֶבַח, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ קוֹדֶשׁ קֳדָשִׁים – דָּמוּ וְאֵימוּרָיו. הָעוֹפוֹת קוֹדְמִין לַמְּנָחוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן מִינֵי דָמִים. מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא קוֹדֶמֶת לְמִנְחַת נְדָבָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא בָּא עַל חֵטְא. חַטַּאת הָעוֹף קוֹדֶמֶת לְעוֹלַת הָעוֹף, וְכֵן בְּהֶקְדֵּישָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The** animal **tithe** offering **precedes bird** offerings **due to** the fact **that it** requires **slaughtering,** whereas the bird's nape is pinched; **and there are** two elements **of** the animal tithe offering that have the status of **offerings of the most sacred order: Its blood** that is presented on the altar **and its portions** that are burned on the altar, whereas with regard to bird offerings only the blood is presented on the altar. **The bird** offerings **precede meal offerings due to** the fact **that they are types** whose **blood** is presented, and atonement is effected by the blood. The **meal offering of a sinner precedes a voluntary meal offering due to** the fact **that it comes** to atone **for a sin.** For the same reason the sacrifice of **the bird sin offering precedes** the sacrifice of **the bird burnt offering, and likewise with regard to its consecration,** the sin offering takes precedence.

The *ma'aser* precedes the bird sacrifices, because mammals (*min zvach*) are of an intrinsically higher sanctity than fowl. And birds precede the *mincha* because they possess blood — *minei damim* — and the quality of blood as the vehicle of *kappara* gives them an advantage over a grain offering, even though a *mincha* can in certain circumstances serve as a *korban chattat*.

### Mincha Distinctions

Within the category of *mincha*, *minchat chote* — a meal offering brought as a *chattat* — precedes *minchat nedava*, a voluntary meal offering, because the former is brought by one who is *ba'al chet*, a sinner requiring atonement. The goal of *kappara* thus confers a higher *kedusha* even within the category of *mincha*. Finally, *chattat ha'of* precedes *olat ha'of*, mirroring the rule for mammalian sacrifices: the atoning function of the *chattat* elevates its blood to a higher sanctity. This applies not only to the order of offering but also to the order of designation: *v'chen behakdesha* — when two birds are set aside together, the *chattat* is designated first, and the second automatically assumes the status of *olah*.

## Levites' Source

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּפַר שֵׁנִי בֶן בָּקָר תִּקַּח לְחַטָּאת״ –
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the placement of the blood of a sin offering precedes the sprinkling of the blood of a burnt offering, whereas the burning of the limbs of a burnt offering precedes the burning of the portions of a sin offering. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: This is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* discussing a verse about the consecration of the Levites: "Then let them take a young bull, and its meal offering, fine flour mingled with oil; **and a second young bull you shall take for a sin offering"** (Numbers 8:8).

The *gemara* opens 89b with the question: *mina hanei mili?* — from where do we derive the rule of *kol hamikudash mechaveiro kodem et chaveiro*?

### Levites' Bulls

The *gemara* cites a *beraita* grounded in the *parshat Behaaloscha* passage describing the consecration of the *Levi'im*. When the *Levi'im* were designated for their special role, they were required to bring two bulls — one as an *olah* and one as a *chattat*. This *korban* is exceptional and without close parallel; it bears some resemblance to the *miluim* sacrifices that inaugurated the *kohanim*, but it is sui generis in important ways, including the fact that it was brought collectively on behalf of all the *Levi'im* — some twenty-two thousand individuals — as what appears to be a single communal *korban chattat*, which raises its own conceptual difficulties.

### Par Sheni Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם בָּא לְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֵן שְׁנַיִם, הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וַעֲשֵׂה אֶת הָאֶחָד חַטָּאת וְאֶת הָאֶחָד עֹלָה״; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּפַר שֵׁנִי בֶן בָּקָר תִּקַּח לְחַטָּאת״? שֶׁיָּכוֹל שֶׁיְּהֵא חַטָּאת קוֹדֶמֶת לְכׇל מַעֲשֵׂה עוֹלָה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּפַר שֵׁנִי בֶן בָּקָר תִּקַּח לְחַטָּאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: **If** this verse **comes to teach that they are two** bulls, this is superfluous, as it **is already stated: "And offer the one for a sin offering and the other for a burnt offering"** (Numbers 8:12). **Why** must **the verse state: "And a second young bull you shall take for a sin offering"? As** one **might** have thought **that** the **sin offering should precede all** the **rites of** the **burnt offering,** therefore **the verse states: "And a second young bull you shall take for a sin offering,"** which indicates that the sin offering actually comes second to the burnt offering.

The *pasuk* states: *par sheni ben bakar tikach l'chatas* — "the second bull you shall take as a *chattat*." The *beraita* asks: if the purpose were merely to tell me that there are two bulls, this was already established by the previous verse — *v'asah et ha'echad chatas v'et ha'echad olah* — "you shall make one a *chattat* and one an *olah*." So what does calling it *par sheni*, "the second bull," come to teach?

The *beraita* reasons: you might have thought — since the *chattat* was mentioned first in the earlier verse — that the *chattat* precedes the *olah* in every respect. By calling the *chattat* the *second* bull, the *Torah* implies otherwise. But then you might reverse course entirely and conclude that the *olah* precedes the *chattat* in every respect, since it was designated first in the numerical sense. The tension between these two verses is the scriptural foundation for our principle.

### Chatat/Olah Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי ״פַּר שֵׁנִי״, יָכוֹל תְּהֵא עוֹלָה קוֹדֶמֶת לְחַטָּאת לְכׇל מַעֲשֶׂיהָ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַעֲשֵׂה אֶת הָאֶחָד חַטָּאת וְאֶת הָאֶחָד עֹלָה״. הָא כֵּיצַד? דַּם חַטָּאת קוֹדֶמֶת לְדַם עוֹלָה – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּרַצָּה; אֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If** the verse had stated only that the sin offering is the **second bull,** one **might** have thought that the **burnt offering precedes** the **sin offering with regard to all its rites.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And offer the one for a sin offering and the other for a burnt offering,"** indicating that the sin offering precedes the burnt offering. **How** can **these** verses be reconciled? The placement of the **blood of** the **sin offering precedes** the sprinkling of the **blood of** the **burnt offering because it effects acceptance,** whereas the burnt offering does not effect atonement. And burning the **limbs of** the **burnt offering** on the altar precedes burning the portions of the sin offering, in fulfillment of the phrase: "And a second young bull you shall take for a sin offering."

The resolution is precisely the two-part rule of our *mishna*: *dam chatas kodem ledam olah mipnei shemeratzeh* — the blood of the *chattat* precedes the blood of the *olah* because it is the vehicle of atonement, and the *imurim* of the *olah* precede the *imurim* of the *chattat* because the *olah* is entirely consumed on the *mizbeiach*. Each of the two sacrifices has a domain in which it holds primacy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? מַתָּנָה קַמַּיְיתָא דִּמְכַפְּרָה – תִּיקְדּוֹם, וְהָנָךְ לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** do all four placements of the blood of the sin offering precede the sprinkling of the blood of the burnt offering? **Let** only the **first placement** of blood, **which effects atonement, take precedence, and those** other placements should **not** come before the sprinkling of the blood of the burnt offering.

A deeper question arises from within this framework: given that only the first *matana* of blood is strictly indispensable for *kappara* (the remaining three being *lechatchila*), why not do just the first *matana* of the *chattat*, then proceed with the entire *avodah* of the *olah* (which is intrinsically more *kadosh* in terms of its body), and only then return to complete the remaining three *matanot* of the *chattat*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָכָא בְּחַטַּאת הַלְוִיִּם עָסְקִינַן; וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּכִי עוֹלָה דָּמֵי, קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא הִיא תִּיקְדּוֹם. בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרִי: הוֹאִיל וְהִתְחִיל בְּמַתָּנוֹת, גּוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** In the verse **here, we are dealing with** the **sin offering** brought by **the Levites** for their consecration. **And even though** it does not effect atonement and **is** therefore **comparable to a burnt offering, the Merciful One states** that the sin offering **precedes** the burnt offering with regard to the presentation of the blood. This indicates that in general, all four placements of the blood of a sin offering precede the sprinkling of the blood of the burnt offering, despite the fact that only the first placement effects atonement. **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they say** there is another answer: **Once** the priest **commenced with the placements** of the blood of the sin offering, he **completes** all of them before sprinkling the blood of the burnt offering.

Ravina answers: the proof is derived from the *chatat haleviim* — a *chattat* that is not brought for any specific sin. This *korban* is so exceptional in character that it barely resembles a standard *chattat*: it carries no genuine *baal chet*, no specific transgression it atones for. Yet the *Torah* still grants it the precedence of a *chattat* over the *olah*. The implication is that the rule is not contingent on the actual act of *kappara* but is rather a categorical designation: the entire *avodah* of the *chattat*, including all four *matanot*, precedes the *olah* as a class. Splitting the *matanot* around the *olah*'s *avodah* would be too confusing and contradicts the blanket rule established by the *Torah*.

The *sages of the west* (*b'maarava amri*) offer a simpler and more practical explanation: *hoil v'hitchil b'matanot gomeir* — once one has begun the blood applications of a given *korban*, one finishes them. It is simply too confusing to interrupt midway. This characteristically direct approach of the *Eretz Yisrael* *Amoraim* resolves the matter without requiring the sophisticated categorical argument that Ravina employs in Babylonia.

## Unresolved Comparisons

### Dam vs. Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: דַּם חַטָּאת וְאֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן קוֹדֵם? דַּם חַטָּאת קוֹדֵם – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּרַצֶּה, אוֹ דִילְמָא אֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה קוֹדְמִין – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן כָּלִיל לָאִישִּׁים?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If there is **blood of a sin offering and limbs of a burnt offering** to be sacrificed, **which of them precedes** the other? Does the **blood of** the **sin offering take precedence, due to** the fact **that it effects acceptance? Or perhaps** the **limbs of** the **burnt offering take precedence, due to** the fact **that they are entirely** burned **in the flames** of the altar.

Having established the basic framework, the *gemara* now probes its boundaries with a series of unresolved questions. The first *ibaia lehu*: *dam chattat ve'evarei olah* — what about the blood of the *chattat* versus the limbs of the *olah*? These are the two holiest components of their respective sacrifices — the very items that define each one's superiority. Which takes precedence?

On one hand, *dam chattat kodem mipnei shemerutze* — the blood of the *chattat* should come first because it effects atonement. On the other hand, *evarei olah kodem mipnei shehein kalil la'ishim* — the limbs of the *olah* should come first because they are entirely consumed by fire on the *mizbeiach*.

### No Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דַּם חַטָּאת קוֹדֵם לְדַם עוֹלָה. לְדַם עוֹלָה הוּא דְּקָדֵים, לְאֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה לָא קָדֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear,** as the mishna teaches that the **blood of a sin offering precedes** the **blood of a burnt offering.** One can infer from this that the blood of the sin offering does not precede all elements of the burnt offering; it is only **with regard to the blood of** the **burnt offering that it takes precedence,** whereas it does **not take precedence with regard to the limbs of** the **burnt offering.**

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishna*. From the *reisha*: *dam chattat kodem ledam olah* — the blood of the *chattat* precedes the blood of the *olah*. One might infer that *ledam olah hu dekadim* — it only precedes the blood of the *olah*, not the limbs; hence the limbs of the *olah* would take precedence even over the blood of the *chattat*. But the *gemara* immediately counters from the *seifa*: *evarei olah kodmin le'imurei chattat* — the limbs of the *olah* precede the fats of the *chattat*. One might read this as: only before the *imurei chattat* does the *olah*'s body take precedence — but not before the blood of the *chattat*. Since both inferences cancel each other out, *ela me'ha lekka lemishma mina* — no conclusive ruling can be drawn from the *mishna* on this question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִסֵּיפָא: אֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה קוֹדְמִין לְאֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת. לְאֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת הוּא דְּקָדְמִי, לְדַם חַטָּאת לָא קָדְמִי! אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **On the contrary,** the opposite conclusion can be inferred **from the latter clause** of the mishna, which teaches that **limbs of a burnt offering precede the portions of** the **sin offering** consumed on the altar. This indicates that **it is** only **the limbs of** the **burnt offering that precede the portions of** the **sin offering** to be burned, **but they do not take precedence with regard to the blood of** the **sin offering.** The Gemara again concludes: **Rather, no** inference is **to be learned from this** mishna concerning this dilemma.

### Olah Blood vs. Chatat Fa…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: דַּם עוֹלָה וְאֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן קוֹדֵם? דַּם עוֹלָה קוֹדֵם – דְּקָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ כָּלִיל; אוֹ דִילְמָא אֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת קוֹדְמִין – (דְּקָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ) דִּמְכַפֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If there is **blood of a burnt offering** to be sprinkled **and portions of a sin offering** to be burned, **which of them precedes** the other? Does **the blood of** the **burnt offering take precedence, as it comes from** an offering that is burned in its **entirety** on the altar? **Or perhaps the portions of** the **sin offering** to be burned **take precedence because they come from** an offering that **effects atonement.**

The second *ibaia lehu* is the mirror image: *dam olah ve'imurei chattat* — what of the blood of the *olah* versus the fats of the *chattat*? Should the blood of the *olah* come first, since it derives from a sacrifice that is *kalil* — entirely burned? Or should the fats of the *chattat* come first, since they derive from a sacrifice that is *mechaper*?

### No Inference (2)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דַּם חַטָּאת קוֹדֵם לְדַם עוֹלָה. דַּם חַטָּאת הוּא דְּקָדֵים לְדַם עוֹלָה, אֲבָל אֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear,** as the mishna teaches that **the blood of** the **sin offering precedes the blood of** the **burnt offering.** One can infer from this that **it** is only **the blood of** the **sin offering that precedes the blood of** the **burnt offering, but the portions** of the **sin offering** to be burned do **not** take precedence.

Again the *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishna* in both directions, and again the two possible inferences precisely cancel each other out. *Ela me'ha lekka lemishma mina* — the *mishna* offers no decisive guidance on this combination either. Both questions are left open. The intuitive sense — that blood, as the primary vehicle of the *avodah*, carries an elevated sanctity over the burning of limbs — would suggest that the blood of the *chattat* precedes even the *imurei* of the *olah* and that the blood of the *olah* may not precede the *imurei chattat*, but the *gemara* does not render a firm conclusion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִסֵּיפָא: אֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה קוֹדְמִין לְאֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת. אֵיבְרֵי עוֹלָה הוּא דְּקָדְמִי לְאֵימוּרֵי חַטָּאת, אֲבָל דָּם עוֹלָה לָא! אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **On the contrary,** the opposite conclusion can be inferred **from the latter clause** of the mishna, which teaches that **the limbs of** the **burnt offering precede** the **portions of** the **sin offering** to be consumed on the altar. This indicates that **it is** only **the limbs of** the **burnt offering that precede the portions of** the **sin offering** to be burned, **but the blood of** the **burnt offering** does **not.** The Gemara again concludes: **Rather, no** inference is **to be learned from this** mishna concerning this dilemma.

## Asham Classification

### Olah vs. Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: דַּם עוֹלָה וְדַם אָשָׁם, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן קוֹדֵם? דַּם עוֹלָה קוֹדֵם – דְּקָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ כָּלִיל; אוֹ דִילְמָא דַּם אָשָׁם קוֹדֵם – (דְּקָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ) דִּמְכַפֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If there is **blood of a burnt offering and blood of a guilt offering** to be sprinkled on the altar, **which of them precedes** the other? Does the **blood of the burnt offering take precedence, as it comes from** an offering that is burned in its **entirety** on the altar? **Or perhaps the blood of a guilt offering takes precedence, as it effects atonement.**

The third *ibaia lehu* introduces a new element: *dam olah vedam asham* — which takes precedence, the blood of the *olah* or the blood of the *asham*? This question opens onto a deeper issue that runs throughout the *masechet*: what is the essential nature of a *korban asham*? Is it fundamentally akin to a *chattat* — an atoning sacrifice whose blood is the central *avodah* — or is it closer to an *olah*, differing from the standard *chattat* in that its blood is applied to only two corners of the *mizbeiach* (counted as four)? The *asham* shares with the *chattat* the quality of being *mechaper*, yet it resembles the *olah* in its blood application. On this classification hangs the answer to which takes precedence.

Should we say *dam olah kodem deka'ati mikoach kalil* — the *olah*'s blood is privileged because it flows from a sacrifice that is entirely consumed? Or *dam asham kodem demichaper* — the blood of the *asham* is privileged because, like the *chattat*, it atones for sin?

### Ranking Dam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: חַטָּאת קוֹדֶם לְאָשָׁם – חַטָּאת הוּא דְּקָדְמָה לֵיהּ לְאָשָׁם, אֲבָל עוֹלָה לָא. מַאי, לָאו דָּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear,** as the mishna states that **a sin offering precedes a guilt offering.** One can infer from this that **it** is only **a sin offering that precedes a guilt offering, but a burnt offering** does **not. What, is** the mishna **not** referring to the sprinkling of the **blood,** which would indicate that the blood of a burnt offering does not precede the blood of a guilt offering?

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishna*. The *mishna* explicitly states *chattat kodemet le'asham* — the *chattat* precedes the *asham*. From this we can reason: *chattat hu dekadmei lei le'asham* — the *chattat* comes before the *asham*, *aval olah lo* — but the *olah* does not. That is, the quality of being *mechaper* places the *asham* above the *olah*, so the ranking for blood is: *chattat*, then *asham*, then *olah*.

### Asham Lower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; אַאֵימוּרִים. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדָּמָהּ נִיתָּן״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No,** it is referring **to the** precedence of the **sacrificial portions** consumed on the altar. The Gemara adds that the language of the mishna **is also precise** in this regard, **as it teaches** that the sin offering takes precedence **due to** the fact **that its blood is placed** on the four corners of the altar, rather than simply teaching: It is placed on the four corners of the altar, as it would have taught had it been speaking of the blood. One can therefore **conclude from** the statement of the mishna that its subject is the sacrificial portions burned on the altar, not the blood.

But wait — why does the *chattat* precede the *asham* if both are *mechaper*? The *gemara* answers by reference to the *matanot*: the *chattat*'s blood is applied to four corners of the *mizbeiach* and at the *yesod*, while the *asham* receives only the two-corner application. The greater number of applications to the *mizbeiach* defines the *chattat* as the higher-sanctity offering. The *asham* is thus one rank below the *chattat* — it is, as the discussion suggests, a lower-*kedusha* version of a *chattat*, sharing the *mechaper* quality but lacking the full complement of *matanot*.

Furthermore, the *gemara* notes that the *mishna*'s language — *mipnei shedama nittan* — is specifically about the blood. This confirms that when comparing the body and limbs of these sacrifices (rather than the blood), the relevant ranking is: *olah* first, then *chattat*, then *asham*.

### Asham Price

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת קוֹדֶם לְאָשָׁם – חַטָּאת הוּא דְּקָדְמָה לֵיהּ לְאָשָׁם, אֲבָל עוֹלָה לָא. מַאי, לָאו דָּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear,** as the mishna states that **a sin offering precedes a guilt offering.** One can infer from this that **it** is only **a sin offering that precedes a guilt offering, but a burnt offering** does **not. What, is** the mishna **not** referring to the sprinkling of the **blood,** which would indicate that the blood of a burnt offering does not precede the blood of a guilt offering?

The *gemara* also considers and rejects a competing argument: perhaps the *asham* should rank above the *chattat* because it has a fixed price (*kitzva*) — certain categories of *asham* require an animal worth at least one *shekel*, making it an expensive, high-quality offering. The *gemara* dismisses this: *ribui d'mizbeiach adif* — what matters most is the degree to which the *mizbeiach* receives blood. The number of *matanot* is the primary indicator of sanctity, overriding the consideration of the *asham*'s monetary value or the quality of the animal.

## Toda-Eil Nazir

### Kodshei vs. Kalim

Having established the ranking among the *chattat*, *asham*, and *olah*, the *gemara* turns to examine the positioning of the *toda* and *eil nazir*. The *mishna* had ruled that the *asham* precedes both the *toda* and the *eil nazir* because the *asham* is *kodshei kodashim* while the *toda* and *eil nazir* are *kodashim kalim*. A bold line separates the two classes: no matter what qualities *kodashim kalim* may possess, the categorical distinction of *kodshei kodashim* — eaten only by *kohanim* in the *azarah* — takes precedence. Qualities such as accompanying bread offerings, which matter greatly for ranking within *kodashim kalim*, cannot elevate a *korban* above this primary classification.

### Toda Over Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר כּוּ׳. אַדְּרַבָּה – שְׁלָמִים קָדְמִי, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּצִיבּוּר כִּבְיָחִיד! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, נֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד עֲדִיפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **A thanks offering and** the **nazirite's ram** precede a peace offering due to the fact that they are eaten for only one day. The Gemara challenges: **On the contrary, peace offerings** should **precede** the thanks offering and the nazirite's ram, **as they are** offered **by the community as well as by the individual.** A communal peace offering is sacrificed on the festival of *Shavuot*, but there is no communal thanks offering or nazirite's ram. The Gemara explains: **Even so,** the fact that the thanks offering and the nazirite's ram **are eaten for** only **one day** is of **greater** importance.

The *mishna* then states that the *toda* and the *eil nazir* precede the *shelamim*. The rationale is twofold: they require *lechem* — accompanying bread — and they are eaten for only one day. Among *kodashim kalim*, the number of days during which the *korban* may be eaten is a primary indicator of sanctity: one day is more *kadosh* than two.

### Shelamim Communal

The *gemara* challenges this ranking: perhaps the *shelamim* should be considered of higher *kedusha* than the *toda* and *eil nazir*? After all, there exists a *shelamim* brought by the *tzibbur* — communally — as on *Shavuot*, where two *kvasim* are offered as communal *shelamim*. One might argue that even an individual's *shelamim* carries an elevated *kedusha* by virtue of the fact that the category includes a communal offering.

### Eating Days Priority

The *gemara* rejects this: *ne'echalin l'yom echad adifei* — the fact that the *toda* and *eil nazir* are eaten for only one day is a more decisive indicator of *kedusha* than the communal dimension of the *shelamim*. The *shelamim* is eaten for two days, and this automatically places it at a lower level. The communal *shelamim* is indeed an exceptional case — like the *chatat haleviim*, it is rare and sui generis — and cannot serve to elevate the ordinary individual *shelamim* above the *toda* or *eil nazir*.

### Toda vs. Eil Nazir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תּוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר – אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן קוֹדֵם? תּוֹדָה קָדְמָה, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנָה אַרְבָּעָה מִינֵי לֶחֶם; אוֹ דִילְמָא אֵיל נָזִיר קוֹדֵם, שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ עִמּוֹ דָּמִים אֲחֵרִים? תָּא שְׁמַע: זוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת לָזוֹ, שֶׁזּוֹ טְעוּנָה אַרְבָּעָה מִינֵי לֶחֶם, וְזוֹ אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה אֶלָּא שְׁנֵי מִינֵי לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If there is **a thanks offering and a nazirite's ram** to be sacrificed, **which of them precedes** the other? Does the **thanks offering take precedence, as it requires four types of loaves,** whereas the nazirite's ram requires only two? **Or perhaps** the **nazirite's ram takes precedence, as there are other** offerings whose **blood** is placed on the altar together **with** the nazirite's ram. A nazirite is required to sacrifice a burnt offering and a sin offering, as well as a ram. The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that explicitly discusses this case: **This** offering **precedes that** offering, **as this** offering, i.e., the thanks offering, **requires four types of loaves, and that** offering, the nazirite's ram, **requires only two types of loaves.**

Having established that the *toda* and *eil nazir* both rank above the *shelamim*, the *gemara* poses an *ibaia lehu*: between the *toda* and the *eil nazir* themselves, which comes first? The *toda*'s defining characteristic is that it requires four types of bread — three of *matzah* and one of *chametz*, ten loaves each. The *eil nazir*'s defining characteristic is that it is accompanied by *damim acherim* — other sacrifices, specifically an *olah* and a *chattat* — in addition to only two types of bread. The question is whether the greater number of bread types or the additional accompanying sacrifices constitutes the higher sanctity.

This question also connects to a *machloket tannaim* in *Masechet Nazir*: may the *nazir* shave — which marks the conclusion of his *nezirut* — immediately upon offering the *eil nazir*, or only after all three of his sacrifices (*olah*, *chattat*, and *eil nazir*) have been brought? That debate reflects the deeper question of whether the three sacrifices of the *nazir* form a unified package or stand as independent offerings.

### Bread Types Decide

The *gemara* resolves the question: *zu kodemes l'zo she'zu te'una arba'ah minei lechem v'zu eina te'una ela shnei minei lechem* — the *toda* precedes the *eil nazir* because it requires four types of bread while the *eil nazir* requires only two. The presence of additional *korbanot* attending the *eil nazir* does not enter the calculation; the defining comparative feature within this pair is the bread, and the *toda* wins on that measure.

## Shelamim-Bechor-Ma'aser

### Shelamim Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁלָמִים קוֹדְמִים לִבְכוֹר כּוּ׳. אַדְּרַבָּה – בְּכוֹר קוֹדֵם, שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ מֵרֶחֶם, וְנֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, מִצְוֹת יְתֵירוֹת עֲדִיפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And the peace offering precedes the firstborn** offering due to the fact that the peace offering requires two placements of the blood on the altar that are four, and the placement of hands on the animal's head, and libations, and the wavings of the breast and thigh. The Gemara challenges: **On the contrary,** the sacrifice of the **firstborn** offering should **precede** the peace offering, **as it is sanctified from** the **womb and it is eaten** only **by the priests,** whereas the peace offering may be eaten by non-priests. The Gemara explains: **Even so,** the fact that **additional mitzvot** are performed in the case of the peace offering is of **greater** importance.

The *mishna* had ruled that the *shelamim* precedes the *bechor*, and the *gemara* now examines the reasoning. The *shelamim* is attended by multiple *mitzvot*: *semichah* (the owner lays hands upon the animal), *nesachim* (wine and flour libations), and *tenufah* (the waving of the breast and thigh). These numerous attendant requirements indicate a higher level of *kedusha*.

### Bechor M'Rechem

The *gemara* challenges this: perhaps the *bechor* should actually precede the *shelamim* — *sheken kedushato m'rechem*, because its *kedusha* is inherent from birth. Every other *korban* (with virtually no exception) acquires its sanctity through a human act of designation; the *bechor* is born *kadosh*. Moreover, the *bechor* is eaten by the *kohanim*, which might further elevate its status. The *gemara* rejects this: *mitzvot yeteiros adifei* — the greater number of associated *mitzvot* is the more decisive indicator of sanctity. The multiplicity of requirements attending the *shelamim* outweighs the *bechor*'s *kedusha m'rechem*.

### Bechor Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבְּכוֹר קוֹדֵם כּוּ׳. אַדְּרַבָּה – מַעֲשֵׂר קוֹדֵם, שֶׁכֵּן מְקַדֵּשׁ לְפָנָיו וּלְאַחֲרָיו! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, קְדוּשָּׁה מֵרֶחֶם עֲדִיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches: **The firstborn** offering **precedes** the animal tithe offering because it is sanctified from the womb and is eaten only by the priests. The Gemara challenges: **On the contrary,** the animal **tithe** offering should **precede** the firstborn offering, **as** if one mistakenly called the ninth or eleventh animal that emerged from the pen the tenth, those animals that came out **before or after** the tenth are also **sanctified.** The Gemara explains: **Even so,** the fact that the firstborn is **sanctified from** the **womb** is of **greater** importance.

The *bechor*, however, does precede the *ma'aser*. Again, *kedusha m'rechem* is the operative principle: the *bechor*'s inherent sanctity from birth gives it priority over the *ma'aser* animal, whose sanctity depends entirely on the human act of counting.

### Ma'aser Power

The *gemara* challenges: perhaps the *ma'aser* should precede the *bechor* — *sheken mikadesh lifanav v'l'acharyav* — because the *ma'aser* has a remarkable property: if one miscounts while passing animals through the sheepfold and calls the ninth animal "ten," or the eleventh animal "ten," all three — the ninth, tenth, and eleventh — acquire the sanctity of *ma'aser*. As Rashi cites from the *mishna*: *kara litshi'i asiri vela'asiri tshi'i v'l'achar asar asiri, shloshtan mekudashim* — all three are sanctified. This ability of *ma'aser* to "sanctify" animals before and after it might be taken as evidence of a uniquely powerful form of *kedusha*. The *gemara* rejects this: *kedusha m'rechem adifa* — being born holy is a superior quality. The *bechor*'s innate sanctity outweighs even this distinctive property of *ma'aser*.

### Mammals Over Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר קוֹדֵם לָעוֹפוֹת כּוּ׳. אַדְּרַבָּה – עוֹפוֹת קָדְמִי, שֶׁכֵּן קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, מִין זֶבַח עָדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: The animal **tithe** offering **precedes bird** offerings due to the fact that it requires slaughtering, whereas the bird's nape is pinched; and furthermore, there are two elements of the animal tithe offering that have the status of offerings of the most sacred order: Its blood, which is presented on the altar, and its portions that are burned on the altar. The Gemara challenges: **On the contrary, bird** offerings should **precede** the animal tithe offering, **as** they are **offerings of the most sacred order,** whether they are burnt offerings or sin offerings. The Gemara explains: **Even so,** the fact that the animal tithe is a **type of** offering that requires **slaughtering** is of **greater** importance.

The *mishna* had placed *ma'aser* above the bird sacrifices on the grounds that mammalian sacrifices (*min zvach*) are intrinsically of a higher sanctity than fowl. This is a categorical distinction that operates independently of whether a particular bird sacrifice is a *chattat* or an *olah*.

The *gemara* challenges: perhaps the bird sacrifices should rank higher — after all, birds are brought as both *chattat* and *olah*, which are *kodshei kodashim*. On this basis one might elevate *ofot* above *ma'aser*. The *gemara* rejects this: *min zvach adif* — the species of the animal is the overriding consideration. Mammalian sacrifices occupy a categorically higher plane than bird sacrifices, regardless of what type of *korban* the bird is designated as. This has a striking implication that emerges from the full structure of the *mishna*: the *chattat* and *olah* of birds, though they share the names of the highest-ranking *korbanot*, actually occupy a very low position in the overall hierarchy of sanctity — dramatically lower than many of the *korbanot* discussed above them. The fact that this disparity did not emerge prominently during the *perek* on bird sacrifices makes it all the more striking here.
