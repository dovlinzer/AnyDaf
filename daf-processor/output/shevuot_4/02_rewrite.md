# Shevuot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Problem

### Mishnah Overview

The *daf* opens eleven lines into Shevuot 4a, at the words *b'mai okimta* — "how did you explain it?" — and the central question concerns the proper understanding of the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* had enumerated four topics that each exhibit the pattern of *shtayim she'hen arba*, "two that is four." The first concerns *tumas mikdash v'kodashav*: one who is *tamei* and enters the *Beit HaMikdash*, or who is *tamei* and eats a *korban*. The basic "two" that everyone agrees triggers liability is unawareness of one's *tumah* status. The "four" arises because those same two forbidden actions — entering the *Mikdash* or eating *kodshim* — can each be committed in a second type of unawareness: not being aware that the place was the *Beit HaMikdash*, or not being aware that the food was *kodshim*. That is the two that is four for *tumah*.

The second case is *shavuah*. The basic "two" are future-oriented oaths: swearing one will eat something and then not eating it, or swearing one will not eat and then eating. The "four" comes from adding past-oriented oaths: declaring "I ate" when one had not, or "I did not eat" when one had. The *mishnah* continues with *tzaraat* and *Shabbat* as additional instances of the same pattern.

### Gemara Problem

The *gemara* identifies a fundamental difficulty: no single *tanna* holds both of these "two that is four" positions simultaneously. Regarding *tumah*, it is only Rabbi Yishmael who maintains that liability extends even to cases where one forgot that the location was the *Mikdash* or that the food was *kodshim*. Rabbi Akiva holds that liability applies only when one forgot that one was *tamei* — not when the forgotten element was the *Mikdash* or the *kodshim* themselves. Thus the first clause of the *mishnah*, the two that is four by *tumah*, can only accord with Rabbi Yishmael. Conversely, regarding *shavuah*, it is only Rabbi Akiva who holds that one brings a *korban* not merely for a future-oriented oath but also for a past-oriented one. Rabbi Yishmael does not extend *korban* liability to past oaths. So the second clause — the two that is four by *shavuah* — can only accord with Rabbi Akiva. The *mishnah* thus appears to be internally inconsistent, combining the positions of two incompatible *tannaim*.

### Malkut Solution

The solution proposed the previous day was as follows: the entire *mishnah* is attributed to Rabbi Yishmael, and the cases it discusses are not about bringing a *korban* at all but about *malkut* — lashes administered for willful transgression. If every case in the *mishnah* involves someone acting intentionally and receiving lashes rather than bringing an atonement offering, then the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael about what triggers *korban* liability becomes entirely irrelevant. Both *tannaim* would agree on the contours of liability when the question is *malkut*. One might ask how this applies to the *shavuah* case, which can involve passivity — swearing one will eat and then simply not eating. The answer given was that Rabbi Yishmael holds one receives *malkut* even for a passive violation. The proposed reading was thus that the entire *mishnah* speaks of willful acts subject to *malkut*.

## Testing Malkut

### Tzaraat Solution

The *gemara* now tests whether the *malkut* framework can accommodate every clause of the *mishnah*. It raises the *tzaraat* case first: *mar'os nega'im mai malkus ika?* — what is the scenario of *malkut* when it comes to the appearances of *tzaraat*? If the *mishnah* were discussing *korban*, Tosafot notes one could understand it as referring to the *korban* brought at the conclusion of the *tzaraat* purification process. But if the *mishnah* is about *malkut*, what negative prohibition does one transgress merely by being afflicted with *tzaraat*?

The *gemara* answers: *bikotzes baharto* — we are speaking of a case where one cuts off one's leprous spot in order to prevent becoming *tamei*. This is explained by reference to the principle of Rabbi Avin in the name of Rabbi Illai: wherever the *Torah* uses the language of *hishamer*, *pen*, or *al* — "guard yourself," "lest," or "do not" — it constitutes a *lo sa'asei*, a negative prohibition. The *Torah* states *hishamer b'nega hatzaraat*, "guard yourself regarding the affliction of *tzaraat*," and *Chazal* understand this to prohibit cutting off one's *tzaraat* spot to avoid *tumah* liability. Violating this prohibition would incur *malkut*, and the two that is four in this case would require knowing which features of one's condition render one *tamei*. Tosafot raises a complication from another *gemara* that suggests *malkut* applies even when one merely risks having *tzaraat* and attempts to prevent the *kohen* from seeing it — which would seem to indicate the prohibition is broader than the *gemara* here implies. Nevertheless, the *tzaraat* case can at least be accommodated under the *malkut* framework.

### Shabbat Issue

The *gemara* then turns to the *Shabbat* case and encounters a more serious obstacle: *yetzios Shabbos mah malkus ika? lav shenitan le'azharas misas beis din hu?* The negative prohibition against performing labor on *Shabbat* is one whose assigned punishment is *misas beis din* — execution by the court. The well-established rule is that any *lav shenitan le'azharas misas beis din* — a negative prohibition whose designated sanction is capital punishment — cannot serve as the basis for *malkut*. If the warning given to the violator mentioned lashes rather than execution, the court cannot impose the death penalty, but neither can it impose lashes, because *malkut* is not the punishment assigned to that prohibition. Accordingly, the willful desecration of *Shabbat* cannot generate *malkut*, and the *malkut* framework appears to break down at this point.

### Rabbi Yishmael

This is precisely why, the *gemara* explains, the solution attributed the *mishnah* to Rabbi Yishmael specifically: *v'mishum hacha kamukmina k'Rabbi Yishmael*. Rabbi Yishmael holds the exceptional position that *lav shenitan le'azharas misas beis din lokin alav* — even a negative prohibition that carries capital punishment can result in *malkut* if the individual was warned about lashes rather than execution. Under Rabbi Yishmael's view, then, one could indeed receive *malkut* for *Shabbat* violation if warned accordingly.

The *gemara* then raises a meta-question: it had originally attributed the *mishnah* to Rabbi Yishmael because of the *tumah* problem — Rabbi Akiva's position on *tumah* does not yield two that is four. So why is the *Shabbat* issue now being presented as the reason for the attribution to Rabbi Yishmael? The *gemara* clarifies: the *mishnah* could not be Rabbi Akiva to begin with because of the *tumah* problem, not because of *Shabbat*. The *Shabbat* issue is simply an additional reason that reinforces the attribution to Rabbi Yishmael. Moreover, now that the framework has shifted from *korban* to *malkut*, the distinction between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael on *tumah* and *shavuah* — which was entirely a dispute about *korban* liability — disappears. Under *malkut*, both *tannaim* would agree, and the entire *mishnah* could be read consistently. The sole reason the *mishnah* must specifically go like Rabbi Yishmael is his unique position on *lav shenitan le'azharas misas beis din*.

The *gemara*, however, is not yet satisfied. It objects: *i hachi, yedios hatra'os mibei lei* — if we are talking about willful violations subject to *malkut*, then the *mishnah* should have spoken of "warnings" (*hatra'os*), not "awarenesses" (*yedios*). The *gemara* offers a strained response — *tani yedios d'hatra'os*, read "awareness" as referring to awareness of the warning — but this is an obviously forced reading, since the term *yedios* is never employed outside the context of inadvertent unawareness. The argument is losing force.

## Back to Korban

### Two That Is Four

The *gemara* now delivers what proves to be a decisive blow against the *malkut* interpretation. *I hachi, shtayim she'hen arba, tartai hu de-avyan* — if we are discussing willful violations subject to *malkut*, then by *tumah* there are only two cases, not four. The only reason one arrives at four is by asking: what did the person forget? Did one forget one's *tumah* status, or did one forget that the location was the *Mikdash*, or that the food was *kodshim*? That fourfold structure emerges only when the cases are organized around categories of forgotten information. But if one is acting willfully, there is nothing forgotten, and the count collapses back to two: entering the *Mikdash* while *tamei*, and eating *kodshim* while *tamei*.

### Oleh Vayyored

Beyond the structural problem, the *gemara* delivers its knockout argument: *ve-su, harei zeh be-oleh ve-yored* — the *mishnah* itself states, at its conclusion, that the transgressor brings a *korban oleh ve-yored*, a sliding-scale offering. The *mishnah* explicitly names a *korban*. The *malkut* interpretation is therefore impossible. This point, the *gemara* implies, could have been stated from the outset.

### Original Problem

With the *malkut* solution entirely dismantled, the *gemara* returns to its original difficulty. The *mishnah* is clearly discussing *korban*, and the problem is now restored in full: the two that is four for *tumah* goes like Rabbi Yishmael, while the two that is four for *shavuah* — encompassing past-oriented oaths — goes like Rabbi Akiva. The two clauses reflect incompatible *tannaic* positions, and the question of how they coexist in a single *mishnah* demands a new answer.

## Rav Yosef

### Rebbi Synthesis

*Ela amar Rav Yosef: Rebbi hi ve-nasiv lah aliba de-tannai.* Rather, says Rav Yosef, the *mishnah* was composed by Rebbi — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, the editor of the *Mishnah* — and he deliberately incorporated the positions of different *tannaim* in different clauses. *Bi-yedi'os nasiv lah ke-Rabbi Yishmael* — for the *tumah* cases, Rebbi adopted the position of Rabbi Yishmael that liability extends to forgetting the *Mikdash* and *kodshim*. *Bi-shvu'os nasiv lah ke-Rabbi Akiva* — for the *shavuah* cases, Rebbi adopted the position of Rabbi Akiva that the *korban* extends even to past-oriented oaths. One can understand why Rebbi would have composed the *mishnah* this way: he wished to collect instances of the pattern "two that is four" — from *Shabbat*, from *nega'im*, from *shavuah*, and from *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* — and chose the most expansive reading available in each domain, even if those readings derived from different *tannaim*. They are not inherently contradictory; there is no logical reason that one must hold the same way across both domains.

### Rav Kahana

Rav Ashi reports that when he presented this analysis of Rav Yosef before Rav Kahana, Rav Kahana objected to it. *Lo teima nasiv lah aliba de-tannai ve-lei lo sevira lei* — do not say that Rebbi is merely compiling the positions of various *tannaim* without personally subscribing to them. That would be a problematic portrait of the *mishnah*'s editor: we cannot simply say Rebbi assembled a *mishnah* from positions he may not personally hold. Rather, *ela Rebbi ta'ameh de-nafshei mefaresh* — Rebbi is expressing his own considered opinions in the *mishnah*. He personally holds the two-that-is-four by *tumah* along the lines of Rabbi Yishmael, and he personally holds the two-that-is-four by *shavuah* along the lines of Rabbi Akiva. The *gemara* will now demonstrate this from textual evidence.

## Proving Tumah

### Two Nelam

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* establishing the foundational principle of *tumas mikdash v'kodashav*: *minayin she'eino chayav ela she-yesh bo yediya ba-techila ve-yediya ba-sof ve-helam bein-tayim* — how do we know that one is not liable unless there was awareness at the outset, awareness at the conclusion, and a period of forgetting in between? Unlike most *shogeg* violations, where one simply was unaware at the time of the act, *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* requires that the person had initially known of their *tumah* status, then forgot, then sinned, and only subsequently became aware again. The source for this requirement is the doubling of the word *ve-nelam* — "and it was hidden" — in the relevant *pesukim*: *ve-nelam mimenu ve-hu tamei* and again *ve-nelam mimenu*. The *talmud lomar* is *venelam venelam shtei pe'amim*, said in the name of Rabbi Akiva.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva's reading of the double *ve-nelam* is that the duplication implies a double awareness — that one must have known and then forgotten, and known again. The two occurrences of *ve-nelam* together require an initial knowledge followed by concealment, and a subsequent knowledge at the end.

### Rebbi Reading

Rebbi disagrees with Rabbi Akiva's derivation, not with its conclusion. *Eino tzarich* — one does not need both instances of *ve-nelam* to establish that requirement. From a single *ve-nelam*, Rebbi infers the necessity of initial awareness: the word *ve-nelam*, "it became hidden from him," implies by its very structure that there was something to become hidden — that is, prior knowledge. Furthermore, the *pasuk* adds *ve-hu yada*, "and he knew," establishing a second moment of awareness at the end. So Rebbi derives both the initial *yediya* and the concluding *yediya* from a single *ve-nelam* together with *ve-hu yada*.

If the first *ve-nelam* already teaches initial awareness and the second *ve-hu yada* teaches final awareness, what purpose does the second *ve-nelam* serve? *Lechayev al helam tumah ve'al helam mikdash* — the Torah is making one liable not only for forgetting one's *tumah* status but also for forgetting that the location was the *Mikdash* or that the food was *kodshim*. The second *ve-nelam* thus extends liability to a second category of hidden information.

### Tumah Conclusion

This is a crucial finding. Rebbi explicitly disagrees with Rabbi Akiva on how to read the double *ve-nelam*, and he uses that second *ve-nelam* to establish a different proposition entirely — that there are two categories of *helam*, two types of concealment that can trigger liability. This is precisely the Rabbi Yishmael position: that one is *chayav* even when it is the *Mikdash* or the *kodshim* that was forgotten, not merely when the *tumah* itself was forgotten. Rebbi, in other words, personally holds the first clause of the *mishnah* — the two that is four by *tumah* — on his own reasoning, not merely as a citation of Rabbi Yishmael.

## Shavuah Logic

### Gap Identified

The *gemara* now acknowledges a gap in the proof: *ashkechan bi-yedi'os d'islei ta'amei de-nafshei* — good, we have demonstrated that Rebbi holds his own view regarding the *tumah* cases. But *shavuos d'leis lei ta'amei de-nafshei minalan?* — when it comes to *shavuah*, we have no text where Rebbi ever explicitly expressed his own view that the *korban* applies to past-oriented oaths as well. The Rabbi Akiva position on *shavuah* depends on a specific hermeneutic approach, and we do not have a direct statement from Rebbi embracing it. How, then, can we say that Rebbi personally holds this second clause of the *mishnah* rather than merely citing Rabbi Akiva?

### Ribui u'Mi'ut

The *gemara* answers: *sevara hu* — it can be derived logically. Why does Rabbi Akiva hold one is *chayav* for a past-oriented *shavuah*? Because *didarash ribui u'mi'ut* — Rabbi Akiva employs the hermeneutic method of *ribui u'mi'ut*, a broadening-and-narrowing interpretive framework. And *Rebbi nami darish ribui u'mi'ut* — Rebbi likewise employs the *ribui u'mi'ut* approach. If Rebbi uses the same interpretive methodology as Rabbi Akiva, he will inevitably arrive at the same conclusion regarding the scope of *shavuah*. To understand why this is compelling, the *gemara* must explain the *ribui u'mi'ut* principle in detail.

## Ribui u'Mi'ut

### Klal u'Prat

The *gemara* explains *ribui u'mi'ut* in contrast to the more familiar method of *klal u'prat*. The principle of *klal u'prat* holds that when the *Torah* states a general proposition and then specifies a particular example, the law is restricted to that specific example. A classic illustration involves the *menorah*: *ve'asita menoras zahav tahor mikshah te'aseh ha-menorah*. The phrase *ve'asita menorah* is general; *zahav tahor* is a specification; *te'aseh ha-menorah* is again general. Under *klal u'prat*, once the *Torah* specifies "pure gold," that particular example becomes the operative category.

### Klal u'Prat u'Klal

When the pattern is *klal u'prat u'klal* — general, specific, general — the final generalization reopens the category but does not return it to full breadth. The rule is *eino dan ela ke'ein ha-prat*: one may include only things similar to the specific example. Applied to the *menorah*, the second general term *te'aseh ha-menorah* broadens beyond gold specifically but only to other metals — things sufficiently like gold. Wood, for instance, would be excluded as too different from the *prat*.

### Broader Approach

*Ribui u'mi'ut* proceeds differently. Under this approach, the initial broad statement and the subsequent narrowing example do not function as *klal* and *prat* in the technical sense; the specific example is understood as a *mi'ut*, a narrowing, not a specification. Because the general term was stated first, one does not conclude from the specific example that only that example applies. Already at the stage of *ribui u'mi'ut*, one has concluded that a wide category is intended, and the specific example merely indicates the boundary in one direction. When the *Torah* then broadens again — *ribui u'mi'ut u'ribui* — the result is a substantially wider category than *klal u'prat u'klal* ever produces. Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, the final broadening returns one only to things similar to the specific example. Under *ribui u'mi'ut u'ribui*, the final broadening reaches everything except what is most unlike the specific. Returning to the *menorah* example: the *ribui u'mi'ut u'ribui* reading would conclude that the *menorah* may be made of virtually any material except pottery, which would be considered wholly different from gold. The *klal u'prat u'klal* reading, by contrast, limits the category to metals.

### Shavuah Applied

This hermeneutic difference has direct bearing on the *shavuah* case. The *Torah* states *ki tishava levate bisefatayim lehara o lehetiv, lechol asher yivate adam bishvuah* — "when one enunciates with one's lips to do harm or to do good, anything that a person enunciates by oath." Under *klal u'prat u'klal*: *ki tishava levate bisefatayim* is the general statement; *lehara o lehetiv* — harm or benefit — is the specific; *lechol asher yivate adam* is the broadening. The *klal u'prat u'klal* result is: any oath similar to harm-or-benefit, which means future-oriented oaths. The category broadens slightly from the specific examples of harm and benefit to include neutral future commitments, but it remains tethered to the future, since *lehara o lehetiv* refers to prospective actions.

Under *ribui u'mi'ut u'ribui*, by contrast, the initial *levate bisefatayim* is the *ribui* — a broad term for any oral utterance. *Lehara o lehetiv* is the *mi'ut*, the narrowing. Already at the stage of *ribui u'mi'ut*, one has abstracted to any future-oriented oath, not merely beneficial or harmful ones. Then *lechol asher yivate adam* functions as the second *ribui*, the final broadening, and now reaches past-oriented oaths as well. The only category excluded — the most dissimilar thing — would be *nishba levatel et ha-mitzvah*, an oath to nullify a commandment, which is a categorically different type of commitment. Thus Rabbi Akiva's *ribui u'mi'ut* approach yields liability for past-oriented *shavuah*, whereas the *klal u'prat u'klal* approach of Rabbi Yishmael yields only future-oriented liability.

## Pidyon HaBen

### Rabbi Yishmael

The *gemara* now demonstrates Rebbi's use of *ribui u'mi'ut* from a concrete case: the redemption of the firstborn son, *pidyon ha-ben*. Rebbi rules *bechol podin bechor adam chutz min ha-shtarot* — one may redeem a firstborn son with anything except promissory notes. A *shtar* has no intrinsic value of its own; it merely represents a debt, and therefore cannot serve as payment for *pidyon ha-ben*. Everything else — including land, slaves, and movable property — is valid according to Rebbi.

### Rabbis

The Rabbis disagree: *bechol podin bechor adam chutz me'avadim u'shtarot ve-karka'ot* — one may not use slaves, land, or promissory notes. Their reasoning follows *klal u'prat u'klal*: *u'fduyav miben chodesh* is the general statement; *be'erkcha kesef chameshet shekalim* — "silver, five *shekalim*" — is the specification, which refers to something that is chattel and possesses intrinsic monetary value; *tifdeh* is the second general term. By *klal u'prat u'klal — eino dan ela ke'ein ha-prat* — one may only include things similar to the specific: *mah ha-prat meforash davar ha-mitaltel ve-gufo mamon* — just as silver is both movable and intrinsically valuable, so too anything redeemed must be movable and intrinsically valuable. This excludes land (*karka*), which is not movable; slaves (*avadim*), which are legally equated with land; and promissory notes (*shtarot*), which, though movable, have no intrinsic value.

Rebbi's *ribui u'mi'ut* reading, by contrast, produces a much broader category. The two general statements — *u'fduyav* and *tifdeh* — work together with the narrowing term *kesef chameshet shekalim*, and the final broadening includes everything except that which is most unlike silver: namely, *shtarot*, which have no intrinsic value whatsoever. Everything else, including land and slaves, remains valid.

## Consistency Challenge

### Ravina Challenge

This proof for Rebbi's *ribui u'mi'ut* approach now faces a challenge. Ravina objects: *ha ve-Rebbi klal u'prat darash* — but look, there is a case where Rebbi explicitly employs *klal u'prat*, not *ribui u'mi'ut*. If Rebbi were a consistent practitioner of *ribui u'mi'ut*, he should apply it everywhere. The case Ravina cites undermines the claim that Rebbi's *pidyon ha-ben* ruling reflects his general hermeneutic approach.

### Awl Case

The case concerns the boring of a slave's ear with an awl at the doorpost, the ritual performed when an *eved ivri* elects to remain in servitude. The *Torah* mentions specifically a *martzea* — an awl. A *baraita* asks: *ein li ela martzea, minayin le-rabos ha-sov ve-ha-sira ve-ha-machat ve-ha-makdeach ve-ha-michtav?* — from where do we know that one may also use a thorn, a sharpened piece of wood, a metal needle, an inscribing tool, or similar instruments? The *talmud lomar* offered is *ve-lakachta* — "and you shall take" — anything that can be taken in the hand. This is the position of Rabbi Yoshiya b'Rebbi Yehuda, who employs *ribui u'mi'ut* and arrives at the broad conclusion that any hand-held instrument suffices.

Rebbi disagrees. He rules that only something made of metal qualifies — *mah martzea meyuchad shel mateches af kol shel mateches* — just as the awl is specifically of metal, so must any substitute be metal. This is a *klal u'prat* derivation: Rebbi takes the specification (*martzea*, specifically metal) as genuinely restrictive and broadens only to other metals. The *gemara* identifies the methodological difference explicitly: *Rebbi darish klal u'prat, ve-Rabbi Yoshiya b'Rebbi Yehuda darish ribui u'mi'ut*. Rebbi's narrow ruling in the awl case reveals that he is in fact a *klal u'prat* man — which directly contradicts the claim that his broader ruling in *pidyon ha-ben* reflects a general commitment to *ribui u'mi'ut*.

## Pidyon HaBen (II)

### Two Generalizations

The *gemara* resolves the internal tension in Rebbi's rulings. *De'alma klallei u'prattei darish* — generally, Rebbi follows *klal u'prat*. The *pidyon ha-ben* case is an exception, and the reason lies not in a general preference for *ribui u'mi'ut* but in a specific structural feature of those *pesukim*. The relevant *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael establishes that when two general statements appear immediately adjacent to one another before the specific term appears, the sequence is no longer treated as *klal u'prat u'klal* but as *ribui u'mi'ut u'ribui*. The *baraita* derives this from the double use of *b'mayim* — "in the water" — in the context of aquatic creatures, where the word appears twice in succession and only then is followed by the more specific *b'yamim u'vanachalim*, "in the seas and rivers." *B'mayim b'mayim shtei pe'amim ein zeh klal u'prat ela ribui u'mi'ut* — two consecutive general terms transform the analysis from *klal u'prat* to *ribui u'mi'ut*. In *pidyon ha-ben*, the two generalizations *u'fduyav miben chodesh* and *tifdeh* appear adjacent to each other, and only then does the specific term *be'erkcha kesef chameshet shekalim* follow. Because of this structural feature, Rebbi applies *ribui u'mi'ut* in that case specifically — not because he is generally a *ribui u'mi'ut* exponent.

## Two Klalim

### Mayim Baraita

The *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches: *b'mayim b'mayim shtei pe'amim ein zeh klal u'prat ela ribui u'mi'ut* — when two general expressions appear before the specific, it is *ribui u'mi'ut*, not *klal u'prat*. The principle is clear: the presence of two adjacent *klalim* before the *prat* signals a fundamentally different hermeneutic mode.

### Amora Dispute

An *amora* dispute is recorded regarding how to handle such a *klal klal u'prat* sequence. Ravina cites a tradition from the scholars of *Eretz Yisrael* — *kol makom she-ata motzei shtei klalos ha-smuchos zeh la-zeh, hatel prat beineihem ve-donam b'klal u'prat* — wherever you find two adjacent general terms, insert the specific between them and analyze the sequence as a standard *klal u'prat u'klal*. This is a competing approach to reading double generalizations. The resulting debate, then, is whether two adjacent *klalim* before the *prat* shift one to *ribui u'mi'ut* (as the school of Rabbi Yishmael holds) or whether the *prat* is simply inserted mentally between them and the whole sequence is treated as *klal u'prat u'klal* (as the *Eretz Yisrael* tradition proposes).

### Pidyon Exception

For Rebbi, the resolution is now clear. His general methodology is *klal u'prat*, as demonstrated by the awl case. *Pidyon ha-ben* is an exception precisely because the structural condition identified by the school of Rabbi Yishmael obtains there: two generalizations appear side by side before the specification. That structural anomaly, and only that, leads Rebbi to apply the broader *ribui u'mi'ut* framework in that specific instance.

## Final Position

### Concession

This resolution, however, creates a new problem for the *gemara*'s argument about *shavuah*. The *gemara* had argued that since Rebbi uses *ribui u'mi'ut*, he necessarily shares Rabbi Akiva's conclusion that *shavuah* liability extends to the past. But now it has been established that Rebbi is fundamentally a *klal u'prat* man who applies *ribui u'mi'ut* only as an exception in *pidyon ha-ben* due to the unusual structure of those *pesukim*. There is no such structural anomaly in the *shavuah pesukim*. Therefore Rebbi, applying *klal u'prat u'klal* to the *shavuah* verses, would arrive at Rabbi Yishmael's conclusion: the *korban* applies only to future-oriented oaths. *Hashta de'amrat Rebbi klallei u'prattei darish, ba'al korchecha kashya shavuos* — now that one has conceded that Rebbi employs *klal u'prat*, one is forced to conclude that Rebbi himself would not hold that a past-oriented *shavuah* triggers *korban* liability.

### Complete Answer

The *gemara* thus arrives at its final resolution: *ela bi-shvu'os nasiv aliba de-Rabbi Akiva, ve-lei lo sevira lei* — regarding *shavuah*, Rebbi includes the two that is four by quoting Rabbi Akiva's broader position, even though he himself does not personally hold that view. Rebbi's own methodology — *klal u'prat* — would yield only future-oriented *shavuah* liability, consistent with Rabbi Yishmael's reading. But in composing the *mishnah*, Rebbi wished to record another instance of the pattern "two that is four," and for that he incorporated Rabbi Akiva's expansive ruling on *shavuah* without personally subscribing to it. The two clauses of the *mishnah* thus reflect, as Rav Yosef originally proposed, different *tannaic* positions — but with an important qualification: in the *tumah* case, Rebbi personally holds the position he records (that of Rabbi Yishmael, as shown from the *baraita*), while in the *shavuah* case, he cites Rabbi Akiva's view for the sake of completeness, without it representing his own *halachic* conclusion.
