# Niddah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Animal Appearance

### Fetus with Animal Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וְעוֹף — וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ מִצּוּרַת אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **We, too, learn** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 46a) that the fetus of a woman that has the form of an animal cannot survive: In the case of a woman **who** had previously **discharged a fetus** with the appearance **similar to** that **of a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or bird** before giving birth to any children, and subsequently she gives birth to a son, the son is considered a firstborn with regard to the *halakhot* of inheritance, but he does not require redemption, as the fetus is considered a full-fledged offspring in that regard. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The son is not exempted from the requirement of redemption from a priest **unless** it follows the birth of a fetus **that takes the form of a person;** otherwise, it is not considered the offspring that "opens the womb" (Exodus 13:2), and the son requires redemption as a firstborn.

The discussion on 24b begins slightly earlier, at 23b, and concerns a fetus that has the appearance of an animal (*behemah*). The Mishnah had raised the question of whether such a fetus, emerging from a human mother, confers upon her the status of a *yoledet* — a woman who has given birth — with all attendant laws of *tumah vetaharah*. Before proceeding further, it is worth clarifying a distinction that bears on the broader context of these laws.

### Pidyon HaBen Context

The Mishnah in Bechorot draws a clear line between two different firstborn frameworks. For the purposes of the double inheritance portion, the firstborn must be born alive; a stillbirth or miscarriage, even a fully developed one, does not count, and the next living son retains *bechor* status for inheritance. For *pidyon haben*, however, the Torah speaks of *peter rechem* — the one who breaches the womb — and here the standard is halachic birth rather than live birth. Accordingly, any miscarriage that constitutes a *halachic* birth, even a stillbirth, means the next son is no longer a *peter rechem* and does not require redemption. The standard for what constitutes such a birth is, practically speaking, the same as the standard governing whether the mother becomes a *yoledet* subject to the laws of *tumah vetaharah* — and that is precisely the question our Gemara is exploring.

It is worth noting that the occasions for *pidyon haben* are in practice quite rare. A girl born first, a prior miscarriage that halachically constituted a birth, and a father or mother who is a *kohen*, *bat kohen*, *levi*, or *bat levi* — all of these eliminate the obligation before we even reach the question of fetal appearance.

### Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה מֵאַבָּיֵי: לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר בְּהֵמָה בִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הוּא — אָדָם בִּמְעֵי בְהֵמָה מַאי? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינֵּיהּ? לְאִשְׁתְּרוֹיֵי בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava asked Abaye: According to Rabbi Meir, who said** that an item that is similar to **an animal in the womb of a woman is** considered **a full-fledged offspring, what** is the *halakha* with regard to **a human** fetus **in the womb of an animal?** The Gemara explains: **What is the** practical **difference** of this inquiry? The difference is **with regard to permitting** the fetus **in consumption.** A full-fledged fetus found inside its slaughtered mother is permitted to be eaten, despite the fact that it was not slaughtered itself.

With that context established, the Gemara presents a question posed by Rav Adda bar Ahava to Abaye. The Mishnah had stated that according to Rabbi Meir, a fetus that looks like an animal (*behemah beme'ei imo*) is nonetheless a real fetus (*velad ma'alya*) for the purposes of childbirth law. The question then arises: what about the reverse scenario?

### Human in Animal Womb

*Adam beme'ei behemah mai?* — what is the law if a human form is found in the womb of a cow? The Gemara immediately narrows the question: *lemai nafka mina* — what is the practical difference? And the answer is striking in its narrowness. The question is not whether such a creature has human status in any broad sense — whether it is subject to commandments, whether killing it constitutes homicide. The Gemara frames the question entirely around *le'ishtaru'ei ba'achilah* — whether it may be eaten. Is this creature to be treated as a calf, slaughtered through a kosher *shechitah* and eaten? Or does its human-like appearance disqualify it from the category of *behemah* entirely?

This restraint is itself revealing. Notably, even Rabbi Meir, who had argued that a fetus looking like an animal inside a human mother is still a *velad*, never suggested that such a fetus *is* an animal in any biological sense. Nor does the Gemara here suggest that a human-appearing creature in an animal's womb is actually human. The far-reaching implications of that claim — is it cannibalism to eat it? — would be unavoidable, and the Gemara says nothing of the sort. The question throughout is how appearance shapes *halachic* status, not biological identity.

### Dove-like Fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מֵהָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וּמָצָא בָּהּ דְּמוּת יוֹנָה — אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But** one can **resolve** the dilemma **from that** statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In the case of **one who slaughters an animal and finds in it** an item that has **the form of a pigeon,** the pigeon **is prohibited for consumption.** Evidently, the ritual slaughter of a pregnant animal renders its fetus permitted to be eaten only if the fetus is of the same species as the mother. Accordingly, if the fetus has the form of a human, it is prohibited for consumption.

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question from a ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: one who slaughters a *behemah* and finds within it a fetus resembling a dove (*demus yonah*) — it is forbidden to eat that fetus. The use of the word *demus* — appearance — is notable; the Gemara shifts easily between saying something *is* a thing and saying it *looks like* a thing, reinforcing the interpretation that we are always speaking about appearance rather than actual species identity. The fetus resembles a dove and is therefore forbidden, presumably because it cannot be considered part of the *behemah* and was not itself subjected to a proper *shechitah*.

### Hoof Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם — לָא פְּרָסוֹת אִיכָּא, וְלָא פַּרְסָה אִיכָּא; הָכָא — נְהִי דִּפְרָסוֹת לֵיכָּא, פַּרְסָה מִיהָא אִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** with regard to a pigeon fetus, **there are no** split **hooves, and there is not** even **a hoof** at all. Since a pigeon is completely different from the slaughtered animal, the fetus is forbidden. By contrast, **here,** in the case of a human fetus, **although there are no** split **hooves, there is at least a hoof,** i.e., solid feet. Therefore, it is possible that the human fetus is permitted for consumption, and the dilemma remains unresolved.

The Gemara, however, rejects this proof. *Hachi hashta* — how can the cases be compared? There, the dove-like fetus has neither split hooves nor even a single hoof — *lo parsos ika v'lo parsah ika*. Here, a human-appearing fetus does have feet, and the Torah's description of a *behemah* uses the word *parsah*. Since the human form possesses something that at least partially corresponds to the descriptors of a *behemah* found in the verse, perhaps it is still sufficiently categorized as part of the *behemah* and may be eaten. The dove-like fetus matches none of those descriptors at all, and so the two cases cannot be equated.

### Chachamim Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Any** discharged entity **that is not** of human form does not render the woman impure.

The Mishnah continues with the position of the *chachamim*: anything that lacks *tzuras adam*, a human appearance, does not constitute a fetus for purposes of childbirth law. It is with this foundation that the Gemara proceeds to examine precisely what constitutes human appearance.

## Facial Features

### Consensus Hybrids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים: גּוּפוֹ תַּיִישׁ וּפָנָיו אָדָם — אָדָם, גּוּפוֹ אָדָם וּפָנָיו תַּיִישׁ — וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says** that **Rav says: All concede** that if a woman discharged an entity **whose body is** that of **a goat and whose face is** that of **a human,** it is considered **a human** offspring, i.e., even the Rabbis rule that the woman is impure in this case. Likewise, if **its body is** that of **a human and its face is** that of **a goat,** Rabbi Meir concedes that **it is nothing,** and the woman is pure.

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba said in the name of Rav: *hakol modim* — everyone agrees in certain cases. If the body is that of a ram and the face is human, the mother is *temei'ah leidah* — both Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* agree. This is a striking point about the centrality of the face: even the *chachamim*, who demand *tzuras adam*, are satisfied when the face appears human, regardless of the body. The face carries the whole determination.

Conversely, if the body is human but the face resembles a ram, *v'lo klum* — everyone agrees there is no fetal status, even according to Rabbi Meir. This might seem surprising. Rabbi Meir had just argued that even a fully animal-appearing fetus can count. Why would a hybrid case fare worse? Tosafot addresses this, noting a distinction between something that wholly resembles an animal and something so thoroughly mixed that it resists categorization in any direction. The hybrid is, in some sense, a harder case than the uniform animal appearance, though the precise logic is not entirely transparent.

The point about the *talya* — a ram — being the chosen example is itself curious. One possibility raised is that it was selected because of its semi-mythological or exotic resonance, much like the *tachash*, serving to highlight the theoretical and classificatory nature of the discussion.

### Mixed Eye Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁפָּנָיו אָדָם, וְנִבְרָא בְּעַיִן אַחַת כִּבְהֵמָה. שֶׁרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר מִצּוּרַת אָדָם, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים כֹּל צוּרַת אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree only** in a case **where its face is** that of **a human, but it was created with** one human eye and **one eye like** that of **an animal. As Rabbi Meir says** that if the offspring has part **of the form of a human** face, even if one eye is not like that of a human, it is considered a human offspring, and the woman is impure. **And the Rabbis say** that it must have **the entire form of a human** face to be considered a human offspring, and otherwise the woman is not impure.

The actual dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* is now defined more precisely. It is not the fully animal-looking case, nor the hybrid with an animal face. *Lo nachleku ela* — they only debate the case where the face otherwise appears human, but one eye looks like an animal's eye while the other looks human. Is that partial deviation from human facial appearance sufficient to strip the fetus of its *velad* status?

Rabbi Meir holds that as long as some human appearance (*mitzuras adam*) is present, it is enough. The *chachamim* require complete human appearance (*kol tzuras adam*) — the face as a whole must look human.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אָמַר לוֹ) [אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן] לְרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: וְהָא אִיפְּכָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: ״כֹּל צוּרַת״, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: ״מִצּוּרַת״! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי תַּנְיָא — תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** One of the Sages **said to Rav Yirmeya bar Abba: But isn't the opposite taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir says** that a woman who discharged is impure if the fetus has **any** part of the **form** of a human face, **and the Rabbis say** that the woman is impure only if the fetus has a recognizable part **of the form of** a human face, e.g., half of a human face? According to this *baraita*, Rabbi Meir does not even require that a significant part of it must look human. In his opinion, even if it has only one human eye or one human cheek and the rest of the face is like that of an animal, the woman is impure. Rav Yirmeya bar Abba **said to** the Sages: **If this** *baraita* **is taught, it is taught,** and I cannot take issue with it. I have my tradition from Rav, and you should rule in accordance with the *baraita* that you received.

A *braita* is then cited that appears to reverse the positions: Rabbi Meir demanding complete appearance and the *chachamim* accepting partial. Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba responds simply: *i tanya tanya* — if that is a received tradition, so be it, even if it differs from his own. These are simply two transmitted versions of the dispute.

### Facial Components

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵצַח, וְהַגְּבִינִים, וְהָעֵינַיִם, וְהַלְּסָתוֹת, וְגַבּוֹת הַזָּקָן — עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּם כְּאֶחָת. רָבָא אָמַר חַסָּא: מֵצַח, וְהַגָּבִין, וְהָעַיִן, וְהַלֶּסֶת, וְגַבַּת הַזָּקָן — עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּם כְּאֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** A woman who discharges an entity is impure only if the entire face of the fetus has a human form. This includes its **forehead, and the eyebrows, and the eyes, and the cheeks, and** its **chin.** The woman is not impure **unless** these facial features **all as one** have the human form. **Rava** says that **Ḥasa says:** It is sufficient for the fetus to have the appearance of a human on one side of its face; its **forehead, and** one **eyebrow, and** one **eye, and** one **cheek, and** its **chin** are enough. The woman is not impure **unless** these facial features **all as one** have the human form.

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba then transmits a further teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan specifying which facial features constitute the *tzuras adam* that must be present: *metzach* — the forehead; *hagebinin* — the eyebrows; *ha'einayim* — the eyes; *halistot* — the cheeks; and *gabbot hazakan* — the chin. According to this view, all of these features, *ad she'kulam ke'echad*, must collectively appear human.

It is worth observing what is notably absent from this list: the nose and, especially, the ears. The ears, one might think, are quite distinctively human — other animals have ears that project differently from the skull and lack the characteristic lobular form. Yet the Gemara does not include the ears. This likely reflects something deeper about how the Talmud understands human identity: what we attach to the *face* as a locus of personhood is not simply what is biologically unique, but what we encounter and recognize when we look at another human being. The forehead, the eyes, the cheeks — these are the features through which we read personality and humanity.

### Rava Half-Features

Rava, citing Chasa, offers a different accounting. One need not have all features on both sides of the face. It suffices to have: *metzach* — the forehead, *hagaban* — one eyebrow, *ayin* — one eye, *leset* — one cheek, and *vegabbat hazakan* — one half of the chin, with the requirement that all of these be present together (*ad she'kulam ke'echad*), but only in their singular forms, not their bilateral pairs.

The Gemara notes that Rava and Rabbi Yochanan are not really disputing a principle but simply aligning with the two earlier opinions. Rabbi Yochanan's view — requiring all features bilaterally — corresponds to the position that demands *kol tzuras adam*. Rava's view — requiring all the features but only one of each paired set — corresponds to the position that *mitzuras adam* suffices.

### One Feature Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: צוּרַת פָּנִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ — אֲפִילּוּ פַּרְצוּף אֶחָד מִן הַפַּרְצוּפִין, חוּץ מִן הָאוֹזֶן. לְמֵימְרָא דְּמֵחַד נָמֵי סַגִּי?
>
> **Translation:** Ḥasa evidently interprets the version of the Rabbis' statement that only part of a human face is required as referring to half of a human face. The Gemara **raises an objection** to this interpretation from a *baraita*: The miscarriage of a fetus with **the form of** a human **face, which** the Rabbis **said** renders the woman impure, includes **even one of the facial features, apart from the ear.** Apparently, **that is to say that** even **if** the fetus has only **one** facial feature of a human, this **is also sufficient** to render the woman impure.

An objection is raised from a *braita*: *tzuras panim she'amru* — when the sages spoke of the appearance of the face as the determining criterion, even *partzuf echad*, a single facial feature, suffices — with the explicit exception of the ear (*chutz min ha'ozen*). This seems to undercut both Rava and Rabbi Yochanan, suggesting that even one feature is sufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי תַּנְיָא הַהִיא לְעַכֵּב תַּנְיָא, וּכְמַאן דְּאָמַר כֹּל צוּרַת, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִצּוּרַת, וּמַאי ״אֶחָד״ — אֶחָד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: When that** *baraita* **is taught, it is taught with regard to** the *halakha* of **rendering** all of the facial features **indispensable** for the fetus to be defined as human, except for the ear. **And** this ruling **is in accordance with the one who says** that the Rabbis require that **the entire form of** the face must be human. **And if you wish, say** that **actually** this ruling **is in accordance with the one who says** that the Rabbis require only a recognizable part **of the form of** a human face. **And what** is the meaning of the claim that it is sufficient for the fetus to have **one** facial feature of a human? It means **one of each** facial feature of which a human has two, i.e., one eye, one eyebrow, and so on.

Abaye resolves this: *ki tanya hahi le'akev tanya* — that *braita* is teaching not the minimum sufficient for *tumah* but rather the minimum whose absence creates a problem. Missing even one feature is disqualifying — not that one feature alone is sufficient. This aligns with the stricter opinion requiring complete facial appearance. Alternatively, the Gemara suggests reading *echad* not as a single feature but as one from each pair — one of each bilateral set is required, consistent with Rava.

### Missing Limb Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: נִבְרָא בְּעַיִן אַחַת וּבְיָרֵךְ אֶחָד — מִן הַצַּד, אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה; בָּאֶמְצַע, אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** In a case where a fetus **was created with one eye or with one thigh,** if the eye is located **to the side** on the middle of the face, or the thigh is located at the side of the hip, where a human eye or thigh is normally located, the fetus is considered human, and **its mother is impure.** If it appears **in the middle** of the face or hip, the fetus is not considered human, and **its mother is pure.**

Rava introduces a related case. If a fetus is born with only one eye and one thigh — not because it resembles an animal, but simply because the other is absent — *min hatzad*, and what is present is in its proper anatomical location, the mother is still *temei'ah*. The single eye is where an eye should be, the single thigh is where a thigh should be. Even a fetus missing paired features, as long as what is there occupies its correct position, carries the status of a *velad*.

### Cyclops Malformation

The situation changes entirely if the eye or the trunk-like limb is located in the center — *be'emtza*. A fetus with a single eye in the middle of its face, like a cyclops, or with a single limb emerging from the center of the torso, *imo tehorah* — the mother is *tehorah*. Despite having human-derived features, the radical displacement from normal human anatomy is sufficient to deprive the fetus of human appearance. What matters is not just the presence of features but their location within the expected human form.

## Viability Standard

### Esophagus Viability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וִשְׁטוֹ נָקוּב — אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה, וִשְׁטוֹ אָטוּם — אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If **its esophagus is punctured,** although the fetus is considered a *tereifa*, i.e., one that has a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months, **its mother is impure.** But if **its esophagus is sealed,** i.e., it is closed at one end, it does not have the status of a human fetus, and therefore **its mother is pure.**

The Gemara now shifts its analytical frame. Until this point, the discussion had centered primarily on *appearance* — does the fetus look sufficiently human? A new criterion is introduced: viability. Even granting that a miscarriage confers *tumah*, this holds only if the fetus was developing along a path that could have led to a viable child. If its internal development was fundamentally incompatible with life, the mother is not *temei'ah*.

Rava teaches: *shta nakuv* — if the esophagus had a perforation, the mother is *temei'ah*; *shta atum* — if the esophagus was completely sealed, the mother is *tehorah*. A sealed esophagus means the fetus was developing in a manner that could never have yielded a viable child. The absence of a functional alimentary opening signals not merely deformity but fundamental non-viability, and such a development does not rise to the level of a halachic birth.

This raises a natural question about how such a determination could practically be made in the absence of modern anatomy — whether by palpation, observation at delivery, or some other means available to the Sages. The Gemara does not address this directly.

The broader principle emerging here is important: the Gemara is not asserting that these fetuses are non-human in any philosophical sense. Rather, it is asking a narrow halachic question — was this fetus developing in a way that constituted *velad*, on the trajectory toward a viable child? The framework of before and after forty days of gestation is relevant: only after forty days does a miscarriage confer *tumah* at all; beyond that threshold, certain internal or external conditions can indicate that even a forty-day-plus fetus was not on a viable developmental path.

### Missing Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת גּוּף אָטוּם — אֵין אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה. וְאֵיזֶהוּ גּוּף אָטוּם? רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּנָּטֵל מִן הַחַי וְיָמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of a woman **who discharges** a fetus that has **a sealed body, its mother is not impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth. And what is a sealed body? Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It is a body which is lacking a limb that **when removed from a living** person would cause him to **die.**

A *braita* addresses miscarriage of a *guf atum* — a body missing certain limbs. Such a miscarriage does not render the mother *temei'ah leidah*, again because the fetus was developing in a manner incompatible with viability. The Gemara then asks: *v'eizehu guf atum?* — what precisely constitutes a *guf atum*? Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi defines it as missing enough that, had the child been alive, it would have died from this deficiency — *kedei sheyinnatel min hachai vayammut*. The question then becomes: how much must be missing?

### Leg Amputation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה יִנָּטֵל מִן הַחַי וְיָמוּת? רַבִּי זַכַּאי אוֹמֵר: עַד הָאַרְכּוּבָּה. רַבִּי יַנַּאי אוֹמֵר: עַד לִנְקָבָיו, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: עַד מְקוֹם טַבּוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And how much** of the lower part of a person's body **when removed from a living** person would cause him to **die,** because one cannot survive such a wound? **Rabbi Zakkai says:** **Until** above **the knee. Rabbi Yannai says: Until his orifices. Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Yehoshua: Until the location of his navel.**

At the top of 24b, Rav Zakkai says: *ad ha'arkuvah* — missing up to what appears to be the knee (more precisely, one of the joints of the animal's hind leg, corresponding roughly to the hock). Rabbi Yannai says: *ad lanekevah* — until the genitalia, meaning the entire thigh is absent, not merely the lower leg. Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Yosei ben Yehoshua, extends this further: *ad mekom tiburo* — even up to the navel.

### Eliezer Already Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין רַבִּי זַכַּאי לְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אִיכָּא בֵינַיְיהוּ ״טְרֵפָה חַיָּה״, מָר סָבַר ״טְרֵפָה חַיָּה״, וּמָר סָבַר ״טְרֵפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה״. בֵּין רַבִּי יַנַּאי לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אִיכָּא בֵינַיְיהוּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: (ניטל) [נִיטְּלָה] יָרֵךְ וְחָלָל שֶׁלָּהּ — נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute between the *amora'im*: The difference **between** the opinion of **Rabbi Zakkai and** that of **Rabbi Yannai** is whether **a *tereifa* can survive** beyond twelve months. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yannai, **holds** that **a *tereifa* can survive** beyond twelve months. Therefore, although one whose legs were removed until above the knee has the status of a *tereifa*, if a woman discharges a fetus of this form she is impure. Only if the fetus lacks legs until his orifices is the woman pure, as such a person cannot survive. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Zakkai, **holds** that **a *tereifa* cannot survive** beyond twelve months. Therefore, even if the fetus lacks legs only from above the knee and not from his orifices, the woman is not impure. The difference **between** the opinion of **Rabbi Yannai and** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who both agree that a *tereifa* can survive, is with regard to a statement **of Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar said:** If the **thigh,** i.e., the hind leg of the animal, **and its recess were removed** from an animal before slaughter, the animal is considered **an unslaughtered carcass;** consequently, it is forbidden in consumption and imparts ritual impurity even while still alive. Rabbi Yannai agrees with the statement of Rabbi Elazar, and accordingly holds that if the lower part of a person's body until his orifices is missing or removed, the person immediately assumes the halakhic status of a corpse. Rabbi Yoḥanan disagrees with Rabbi Elazar and holds that one whose lower part of his body was missing or removed has the status of a corpse only if it is removed until his navel.

The Gemara identifies the underlying dispute between Rav Zakkai and Rabbi Yannai as turning on a question of *treifa* status: *treifah chayah* — can a *treifah* animal survive? Missing the lower leg renders an animal a *treifah*. If one holds that a *treifah* can nonetheless remain alive for some time, then the lower leg alone is insufficient to establish non-viability; one must require the upper thigh to be missing as well. If one holds that a *treifah* cannot survive, then the lower leg already establishes non-viability.

The debate between Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yochanan turns on a statement of Rabbi Eliezer: *nittal yarech vechalal shelah* — if the thigh and its socket (the entire hip joint) are removed, *nevelah* — the animal is considered already dead, not merely a *treifah* but in the category of something from which life has already departed. Missing the thigh thus represents a more extreme threshold than mere *treifah* status. Rabbi Yochanan, who goes even further to the navel, apparently rejects that threshold as sufficient and demands a yet greater degree of absence.

It should be emphasized: this entire discussion has nothing to do with appearance. It is purely a question of viability — was this fetus on a developmental path toward a living child?

### Head Defect Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה, אֲבָל מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה אֲפִילּוּ כֹּל דְּהוּ — טְהוֹרָה. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת אֶת שֶׁגּוּלְגׇּלְתּוֹ אֲטוּמָה — אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** The **dispute** between the *amora'im* is with regard to a fetus that is lacking part of its body **from below** to **above,** i.e., the lower part of his body; **but** if it is lacking part of its body **from above** to **below, even any amount** of its skull, the woman **is pure. And likewise, Rav Giddel says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of a woman **who discharges** a fetus **whose skull is sealed,** i.e., deficient, **its mother is pure.**

Rav Pappa introduces an important asymmetry. The debates just described — Rav Zakkai, Rabbi Yannai, Rabbi Yochanan — all concern deficiencies *milmata lemala*, from the bottom upward. How far from the foot up must be missing before we say the fetus was non-viable? But *milmala lemata* — if something is missing from the head downward — *afilu kol shehu tehorah* — even the slightest deficiency in the head renders the mother *tehorah*. The head is categorically different. Any defect there, however small, is sufficient to establish non-viability.

### Sealed Skull

In keeping with this principle, Rav Giddel in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules: if a woman miscarries a fetus whose skull is sealed — *shegulgolto atuma*, fused or undifferentiated — the mother is *tehorah*. Rashi explains *atuma* here as *chaserah* — lacking, meaning some portion of the skull is absent or improperly formed. The principle of Rav Pappa applies: any cranial deficiency, however partial, disqualifies the fetus from conferring *tumah*.

### Palm Tree Fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין אַפָּקוּתָא דְּדִיקְלָא — אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha*: **And Rav Giddel says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of a woman **who discharges** a fetus that looks **like** the part **of a palm tree** that **branches out,** i.e., the lower part of its body is formless while the upper part has arms and legs coming out of its shoulders like branches, **its mother is pure.**

Rav Giddel also transmits in the name of Rabbi Yochanan a case involving a fetus that resembles *k'min afakta d'dikla* — a palm tree configuration. Rashi describes this as a trunk at the bottom with the limbs emerging from the top, much as a palm tree has its fronds all extending from the crown. The mother in such a case is *tehorah*. The reasoning here appears to rest on viability rather than appearance: such a configuration is presumed incapable of supporting life.

## Facial Deformation

### Smushed Face

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּפֶּלֶת מִי שֶׁפָּנָיו מוּסְמָסִים, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** with regard to a woman **who discharges** a fetus **whose face is mashed** but not completely flattened, that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says its mother is impure,** and **Reish Lakish says its mother is pure.**

The Gemara records a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish regarding a fetus whose face is *musmasin* — smushed. The features are present but pressed down or compressed. Rabbi Yochanan holds the mother is *temei'ah*: the face, however distorted, retains enough human appearance. Resh Lakish holds the mother is *tehorah*: even smushed features do not constitute sufficient *tzuras adam*.

### Challenge Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַמַּפֶּלֶת יָד חֲתוּכָה וְרֶגֶל חֲתוּכָה — אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא מִגּוּף אָטוּם בָּאתָה. וְאִם אִיתָא, לִיתְנֵי: שֶׁמָּא מִגּוּף אָטוּם אוֹ מִמִּי שֶׁפָּנָיו מוּסְמָסִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: In the case of a woman **who discharges a shaped hand,** i.e., a hand whose fingers are discernible, **or a shaped foot, its mother is impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth,** as it certainly came from a full-fledged fetus, **and we are not concerned** that **perhaps it came from** a fetus with **a sealed,** i.e., deficient, **body. And if it is so,** that a fetus with a mashed face does not render its mother impure, **let** the *baraita* **teach:** We are not concerned that **perhaps** it came **from** a fetus with **a sealed body or from one whose face is mashed.**

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Resh Lakish from a *mishnah* dealing with a case where only a detached limb — a cut-off arm or leg — emerges from the miscarriage without the body. The *mishnah* rules the mother is *temei'ah*, and adds: *v'ein chosheshin shema m'guf atum ba* — we need not be concerned that perhaps the rest of the body was a *guf atum*, a malformed non-viable trunk. The implicit assumption is that we treat the body as normal. Rabbi Yochanan presses: if Resh Lakish is right that a smushed face renders the mother *tehorah*, then the *mishnah* should have added another exemption — we also need not worry that the face was smushed. Its silence on that point suggests no such concern exists, i.e., a smushed face is never a problem, which contradicts Resh Lakish's position.

### Resolution Smushed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פַּפֵּי: בְּפָנָיו מוּסְמָסִין — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּטְמֵאָה, כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בְּפָנָיו טוּחוֹת, וְאִיפְּכָא אִיתְּמַר: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappi says: In** a case where **its face is mashed, everyone agrees that** the woman **is impure. When they disagree,** it is **in** a case where **its face is** completely **flat,** i.e., none of its features are discernible; **and the opposite was stated: Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **its mother is pure, and Reish Lakish says** that **its mother is impure.**

Rav Pappi resolves the dispute by redefining its terms. *Musmasin kuli alma lo pligi detmei'ah* — regarding a smushed face, everyone agrees the mother is *temei'ah*. There is no dispute there. The actual debate concerns *panav tuchot* — features that are completely effaced, entirely flattened to the point of erasure, far beyond merely smushed. According to this resolution, Rabbi Yochanan holds the mother is *tehorah* when features are fully effaced; Resh Lakish holds even then the mother is *temei'ah*.

This also dissolves Rabbi Yochanan's challenge to Resh Lakish. The *mishnah*'s silence about the face simply means it need not be mentioned separately: completely effaced facial features are effectively equivalent to a *guf atum*, a body missing limbs. It is the same category — absence of a body part — and is therefore already covered.

## Stringency Leading to Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא נְפוּק לְקִרְיָיתָא, אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דַּאֲבוּהוֹן, אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּלוּם בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לְיֶדְכֶם? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: פָּנִים טוּחוֹת בָּא לְיָדֵינוּ, וְטִימֵּאנוּהָ. אָמַר לָהֶם: צְאוּ וְטַהֲרוּ מָה שֶׁטִּמֵּאתֶם. מַאי דַּעְתַּיְיכוּ, לְחוּמְרָא? חוּמְרָא דְּאָתְיָא לִידֵי קוּלָּא הִיא, דְּקָיָהֲבִיתוּ לַהּ יְמֵי טוֹהַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya went out to the villages** to inspect their father's fields. When **they came** back **to their father, he said to them:** Wasn't **any incident brought to you** for a halakhic ruling? **They said to him:** A case of a woman who discharged a fetus with **a flat face was brought to us, and we deemed her impure** with the impurity of a woman after childbirth. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to them: Go out and deem pure that which you have deemed impure. What was your thinking** when you ruled that she is impure? Did you reason that as the matter is subject to a dispute, one should rule **stringently?** But your ruling **is a stringency that leads to a leniency, as you have given** the woman thirty-three **days of purity** after the birth of a male, following her period of impurity, which are the minimum days of purity established in the Torah for a woman who gave birth.

The sons of Rabbi Chiya went out to a city and returned to their father reporting a case they had encountered: a fetus was born with *panim tuchot* — completely effaced facial features. They had ruled the mother *temei'ah*, taking what they considered the stringent position, going against the opinion of Rabbi Yochanan who would have declared her *tehorah*.

Rabbi Chiya told them plainly: *tze'u v'taharu mah shetimatem* — go back and reverse your ruling. He then identified their error: *l'chumra? chumra d'ati liyadei kula* — you thought you were being strict? This is a *chumra* that generates a *kula*. By declaring the mother a *yoledet*, they triggered not only the initial period of *tum'at leidah* but also the *yemei tohar* — the following thirty-three or sixty-six days during which bleeding is treated as *tohar blood*, a significant leniency. You cannot play it safe by always ruling *tamei* in childbirth cases; the halachic outcome must be evaluated in full. The *halacha* here follows Rabbi Yochanan: with completely effaced features, the mother is *tehorah*.

This episode encodes a broader principle of the utmost practical importance: *chumrot* do not always produce stringent outcomes. Very often an apparent stringency in one dimension of a halachic framework produces unintended and serious leniencies in another, and the posek must account for the full picture.

## Conjoined Twins

### Two Backs Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּפֶּלֶת בְּרִיָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּים וּשְׁנֵי שִׁדְרָאוֹת, אָמַר רַב: בְּאִשָּׁה — אֵינוֹ וָלָד, בִּבְהֵמָה — אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּאִשָּׁה — וָלָד, בִּבְהֵמָה — מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to a woman or female animal **who discharges an entity that has two backs and two spines, Rav says** that **in** the case of the **woman,** her discharged fetus **is not** considered **an offspring,** as it cannot survive, and therefore the woman does not have the ritual impurity caused by childbirth, and **in** the case of the **animal,** its fetus **is prohibited for consumption. And Shmuel says: In** the case of **a woman,** the discharged fetus is considered **an offspring,** and the woman is impure, and **in** the case of **an animal,** the fetus is **permitted for consumption.**

The Gemara now introduces a new case: a creature — whether fetus or born animal — that has *shenei gabin ushtei shidra'ot*, two backs and two spinal cords. The question is whether this constitutes a *velad* for a human mother and whether it is permissible as food if born to an animal. The description raises the image of what we would today call conjoined twins fused at the back, though the precise anatomy the Gemara envisions is not entirely clear — the Gemara elsewhere discusses two heads but does not specify that here.

### Rav Non-Viable

Rav rules: *be'ishah eino valad* — by a woman, this is not considered a fetus; the mother is not *temei'ah leidah*. And *bevehemah asurah ba'achilah* — if born from an animal, it is forbidden to eat. The reasoning is not that it looks like an animal or lacks human features in the conventional sense, but rather that it is so fundamentally malformed as to be non-viable.

### Shmuel Viable

Shmuel disagrees: *be'ishah valad* — by a woman, it is a fetus, and the mother is *temei'ah*; *uvevehemah muteret ba'achilah* — and from an animal it is permitted to eat. Shmuel accepts that even a creature with this significant deformity can be viable, and viability is the operative criterion.

### Rabbi Chanin Bar Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בִּדְרַב חָנִין בַּר אַבָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חָנִין בַּר אַבָּא: ״הַשְּׁסוּעָה״ — בְּרִיָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּין וּשְׁנֵי שִׁדְרָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do** Rav and Shmuel **disagree?** The Gemara answers: They disagree **concerning** the statement **of Rav Ḥanin bar Abba, as Rav Ḥanin bar Abba said:** The verse states: "Nevertheless these you shall not eat of them that only chew the cud, or of them that only have the hoof cloven [*umimafrisei haparsa hashesua*]: The camel, and the hare, and the rock badger" (Deuteronomy 14:7). The apparently superfluous term ***hashesua*** is not a redundant description of the cloven hoof; it is referring to a separate **entity that has two backs and two spines** and therefore looks like an entirely cloven animal.

The underlying dispute, the Gemara explains, turns on the position of Rabbi Chanin bar Abba. Rabbi Chanin bar Abba identifies the *shasu'ah* mentioned in the Torah among the forbidden animals with precisely this creature — one that has two backs and two spines.

### Rav's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: בְּרִיָּה בְּעָלְמָא לֵיתַהּ, וְכִי אַגְמְרֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמֹשֶׁה — בִּמְעֵי אִמָּהּ אַגְמְרֵיהּ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּרֵיהּ בְּעָלְמָא אִיתַאּ, וְכִי אַגְמְרֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמֹשֶׁה — בְּעָלְמָא אַגְמְרֵיהּ, אֲבָל בִּמְעֵי אִמָּהּ — שָׁרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is with regard to this prohibition that Rav and Shmuel disagree. **Rav says** that **there is no** such living **entity in the world, and when the Merciful One taught** this prohibition **to Moses, he taught** it **to** him with regard to a fetus that has two backs and two spines that is found **in the womb of its mother** after slaughter. **And Shmuel says** that **there is** such **an entity in the world, and when the Merciful One taught** this prohibition **to Moses, he taught** it **to him** with regard to a living animal **in the world, but** a fetus that has two backs and two spines **in the womb of its mother is permitted** for consumption.

Rav understands this to mean: Rav says such a creature *biryah be'alma leita* — does not exist as a viable independent creature. The only place such a thing ever appears is *beme'ei imma* — in the mother's womb. Accordingly, when God taught Moshe that the *shasu'ah* is forbidden, it was referring to the creature in the womb; once born, it is moot because it never survives. For the human mother, it is not a *velad*; for the animal it is forbidden only in the womb on account of that *drasha*.

### Priest Blemish Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא לְרַב: רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר, כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּין וּשְׁנֵי שִׁדְרָאוֹת פָּסוּל לַעֲבוֹדָה, אַלְמָא דְּחָיֵי, וְקַשְׁיָא לְרַב! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ach Shimyya, שֶׁשִּׁדְרָתוֹ עֲקוּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya raised an objection to Rav** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says: Any** priest **who has two backs and two spines is disqualified from** the Temple **service,** as he is blemished. **Evidently,** an entity **that** has two backs and two spines **can survive, and** this is **difficult for** the opinion of **Rav.** Rav **said to him: You are** clearly **Shimi,** i.e., you asked well. Yet the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus is not referring to one who literally has two backs and two spines, but rather **to** one **whose spine is crooked** and therefore appears as though he has two spines. One who actually has two backs and two spines cannot survive.

Rav Shimi bar Chiyya challenges Rav from a Mishnah. The Mishnah in the context of priestly blemishes lists *kol she-yesh lo shenei gavin u-shenei shidra'ot* — a *kohen* with two backs and two spinal cords — as disqualified from Temple service on account of a *mum*. The fact that the Mishnah treats this as a disqualifying blemish presupposes that such a *kohen* exists, functions, and serves. This implies viability, directly contradicting Rav.

### Rav's Response

Rav responds dismissively but with substance: *ach Shimyya* — my dear Shimi — *she-shidrato akuma*, that Mishnah is speaking of a *bent* spine, not a genuinely doubled spine. A kohen with severe spinal curvature, appearing as if he has a double back, is disqualified for his appearance, but this is not the biologically impossible case of true double spines. Rav maintains that a genuine two-spine creature is non-viable and the Mishnah does not contradict this.

## Gestation Periods

### Braita on Viable Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: יֵשׁ בְּעוּבָּרִין שֶׁהֵן אֲסוּרִין — בֶּן אַרְבָּעָה לְדַקָּה, בֶּן שְׁמֹנָה לְגַסָּה, הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַטָּה אָסוּר. יָצָא מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּים וּשְׁנֵי שִׁדְרָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: Among discharged animal **fetuses, there are those that are forbidden** in consumption, as they have the halakhic status of carcasses of unslaughtered animals. Specifically, if an animal fetus is **born** in the **fourth** month of pregnancy in the case **of small** domesticated animals, where the pregnancy is normally five months long, or it is **born** in the **eighth** month of pregnancy in the case **of large** livestock, where the pregnancy is normally nine months long, or if the miscarriage occurred **from this** stage of the pregnancy **and earlier,** i.e., if the pregnancy ended before this stage, the animal is **forbidden.** This **excludes one that has two backs and two spines.**

The Gemara now cites a *braita* concerning fetal development and the threshold of viability in animals. For a small animal (*dakka*) such as a sheep or goat, whose normal gestation is five months, any fetus born within four months is non-viable. For a large animal (*gassa*) such as a cow, whose normal gestation is nine months, a fetus born within eight months is non-viable. Below those thresholds, the animal is considered not to have reached a stage of development compatible with survival.

The *braita* adds: *yatza mi she-yesh lo shenei gavin u-shenei shidra'ot* — this excludes the case of two backs and two spines. The question is what this exclusion means and how each Amora reads it.

### Rav's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ. רַב מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: בֶּן אַרְבָּעָה לְדַקָּה, בֶּן שְׁמוֹנֶה לְגַסָּה, הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַטָּה — אָסוּר. בַּמֶּה דְבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? כְּשֶׁיָּצָא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם, אֲבָל בִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹ — שְׁרֵי. יָצָא מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּים וּשְׁנֵי שִׁדְרָאוֹת, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹ נָמֵי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav explains** the *baraita* **according to his** line of **reasoning, and Shmuel explains** the *baraita* **according to his** line of **reasoning. Rav explains** the *baraita* **according to his** line of **reasoning,** as was assumed above: If an animal fetus is **born** in the **fourth** month of pregnancy in the case **of small** domesticated animals, or it is **born** in the **eighth** month of pregnancy in the case **of large** livestock, or if it was born **from this** stage of the pregnancy **and earlier,** the animal is **forbidden. In what** case **is this statement said?** In a case **where** the animal **emerged into the airspace of the world; but** if it was found **in its mother's womb** after its mother was slaughtered, **it is permitted for** consumption. This **excludes** the case of a fetus **that has two backs and two spines, as even** if it is found **in the womb of its mother it is prohibited.**

Rav explains the *baraita* according to his view. The rule that animals born within four or eight months are forbidden applies to those born into the world, *yatza le'avir ha'olam*. But a fetus *bimei immo*, in the mother's womb, is permissible when the mother is slaughtered — a standard rule about fetal animals. The exclusion of the two-spine case means: unlike normal fetuses, the two-spine creature is forbidden *even in the mother's womb*, because it is the *shasu'ah* described by Rabbi Chanin bar Abba, and that prohibition was taught with reference to its in-utero existence. Rav's position thus receives textual support from the *braita*.

### Shmuel's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: בֶּן אַרְבָּעָה לְדַקָּה, בֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה לְגַסָּה — הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַטָּה אָסוּר. בַּמֶּה דְבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּשֶׁלֹּא כָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו, אֲבָל כָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו — מוּתָּר. יָצָא מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּים וּשְׁנֵי שִׁדְרָאוֹת, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּכָלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו — אִם יָצָא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם אָסוּר, בִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹ שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel explains** the *baraita* **according to his** line of **reasoning,** in the following manner: If an animal fetus is **born** in the **fourth** month of pregnancy in the case **of small** domesticated animals, or it is **born** in the **eighth** month of pregnancy in the case **of large** livestock, or if it was born **from this** stage of the pregnancy **and earlier,** the animal is **forbidden. In what** case **is this statement said?** In a case **when** the fetus's **months** of gestation **were not completed; but** in a case when **its months** of gestation **were completed, it is permitted** for consumption even outside the womb. This **excludes** a fetus **that has two backs and two spines, as even in** a case where **its months** of gestation **were completed, if it emerged into the airspace of the world, it is forbidden,** whereas if it is found **in the womb of its mother, it is permitted.**

Shmuel reads the *baraita* entirely differently. The rule that animals born below the gestation threshold are forbidden applies when their months of development are *not* complete — *she-lo kalu lo chodashav*. If they are fully developed, they are permitted even if premature. The exclusion of the two-spine case means: even when its months are fully complete, once born — *im yatza le'avir ha'olam* — it is forbidden, because it falls under the prohibition of *shasu'ah*. But *bimei immo*, as a fetus in the womb, it is permissible, exactly the reverse of Rav's position.

### Application to Human Cases

The practical consequence for the human case: according to Rav, the two-spine creature is non-viable, never exists independently, and is not a *velad* — the mother is not *temei'ah*. According to Shmuel, such a creature can be viable, and if born it is a *velad* — the mother is *temei'ah*.

### Rav Huna Intervention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא סָבַר לְמֶעְבַּד עוֹבָדָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ לְחוּמְרָא? חוּמְרָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי קוּלָּא הוּא, דְּקָיָהֲבַתְּ לַהּ דְּמֵי טוֹהַר. עֲבֵיד מִיהָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב, דְּקַיְימָא לַן הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב בְּאִיסּוּרֵי, בֵּין לְקוּלָּא בֵּין לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba thought to perform an action,** i.e., to issue a ruling, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel,** that a woman who gives birth to a child with two backs and two spines is impure. **Rav Huna said to him: What is your thinking?** That as this matter is subject to a dispute, one should rule **stringently?** Your ruling **is a stringency that leads to a leniency, as you have given** the woman a period of thirty-three days following her period of impurity when any blood that emerges is **blood of purity. In any event,** you should **perform,** i.e., issue your ruling, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav, as we maintain** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav with regard to ritual matters, whether** his opinion leads **to a leniency or to a stringency.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba was inclined to rule like Shmuel in a case that came before him — to declare a woman who miscarried such a creature a *yoledet* and thus *temei'ah*, which would have been the stricter ruling. Rav Huna intervened: *mai da'atach? l'chumra? chumra d'asi lidei kula* — again the same principle. Treating her as a *yoledet* triggers not only the initial *tumah* but the subsequent *yemei tohar*, a significant leniency. Moreover, the standing rule is *halacha keRav be'issurei*, *bein lekula bein lechumra* — in matters of ritual law, we follow Rav whether his ruling is lenient or strict. Rule like Rav: the creature is not viable, and the mother is not a *yoledet*.

## Human Animal Gestation

### Human Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ, אִשָּׁה יוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה וְיוֹלֶדֶת לְשִׁבְעָה, בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה יוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה — יוֹלֶדֶת לְשִׁבְעָה אוֹ לֹא יָלְדָה?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** The Sages **said** that **a woman can give birth** to a viable offspring **after nine** months of pregnancy **or after seven** months of pregnancy; but if a woman gives birth after eight months of pregnancy, the child cannot survive and is stillborn. Similarly, **a large domesticated animal gives birth** to a viable offspring **after nine** months of pregnancy, and if it discharges a fetus after only eight months, the newborn animal cannot survive. With this in mind, Rava asked: Can a large domesticated animal **give birth** to a viable offspring **after seven** months of pregnancy, like a human, **or** can such an animal **not give birth** to a viable offspring after only seven months?

Rava introduces a comparison between the viability standards governing humans and those governing animals. It is established that a human child born in the ninth month is viable, and one born in the seventh month is viable, but one born in the eighth month is not — a physiologically surprising principle that the Gemara nonetheless treats as settled. The question is whether a parallel rule operates for large animals.

### Animal Seven Month Viability

For a large animal such as a cow, we know that a fetus born within eight months — less than the full nine-month gestation — is non-viable. But does the analogy to humans extend further? In humans, both the ninth and seventh months yield viable births. Does this mean a seven-month calf might similarly be viable, in contrast to an eight-month calf?

### Species Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: תָּא שְׁמַע, הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְמַטָּה אָסוּר. מַאי לָאו אַגַּסָּה? לָא, אַדַּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from the aforementioned *baraita*: If an animal discharges **from this** stage of the pregnancy **and earlier,** the fetus is **forbidden** in consumption as an unslaughtered animal carcass. **What, is it not** referring **to large** livestock, which indicates that large livestock do not give birth to a viable offspring after only seven months of pregnancy? The Gemara answers: **No,** the reference is specifically **to small** domesticated animals, which do not give birth to a viable offspring until after five months of pregnancy.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak directs us to the *braita*: *shemona ulemata asur* — eight months and below is forbidden for a cow. The question is whether this statement refers to cows specifically or to sheep, whose threshold is four months.

### Braita Scope Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אַגַּסָּה — אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּבְאִשָּׁה חָיֵי, בִּבְהֵמָה נָמֵי חָיֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא חָיֵי. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אַדַּקָּה אִיתְּמַר, פְּשִׁיטָא! בַּת תְּלָתָא יַרְחֵי מִי קָא חָיֵי?! אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֲמִינָא: כֹּל בְּצִיר תְּרֵי יַרְחֵי חָיֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer: **What is this? Granted, if you say** that the reference is **to large** livestock, it is **necessary** for the *baraita* to state that an animal does not give birth to a viable offspring after less than a complete period of pregnancy, as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that **since in** the case of **a woman** who gives birth after seven months the baby **survives,** it is logical that **in** the case of a large domesticated **animal** that gives birth after seven months the newborn **also survives,** and it is therefore permitted for consumption. Consequently, the *baraita* **teaches us that** such an animal **does not survive.** **But if you say** that the ruling in the *baraita*, that if an animal discharged a fetus before the period of gestation was completed then the fetus is prohibited, **was stated with regard to small** domesticated animals, isn't it **obvious** that if a sheep or goat fetus was discharged at this stage it cannot survive? **Can it survive after** only **three months** of gestation? The Gemara answers that in fact **it is necessary** for the *baraita* to state this *halakha* with regard to small domesticated animals, as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that **any** mammal that is born **two months less** than its complete gestation **survives,** just as a human born at seven months of gestation survives. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that a sheep or goat that is born at three months of gestation cannot survive and is forbidden for consumption.

If the statement refers to cows, it is needed precisely to forestall the analogy to humans: one might have thought that since a seven-month human is viable, a seven-month calf is also viable, and the *braita* corrects this by specifying that for cows, anything less than nine months is problematic. If the statement refers only to sheep, the Gemara asks whether the analogy to seven-month human viability applies to cows after all — and no definitive resolution is offered.

## Mythical Creatures

### Lilith Fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמַּפֶּלֶת דְּמוּת לִילִית, אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, וָלָד הוּא אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ כְּנָפַיִם. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, מַעֲשֶׂה בְּסִימוֹנְיָא בְּאַחַת שֶׁהִפִּילָה דְּמוּת לִילִית, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְאָמְרוּ: וָלָד הוּא אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ כְּנָפַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** In the case of a woman **who discharges** a fetus that has **the form of a lilith,** a female demon with wings and a human face, **its mother is impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth,** as **it is** a viable **offspring, only it has wings. This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei said: An incident** occurred **in Simoni involving a certain** woman **who discharged** a fetus that had **the form of a lilith, and** the **incident was brought before the Sages; and they said** that **it is** a viable **offspring, only it has wings.**

Rav Yehuda transmits in the name of Shmuel: *hamapelet demus Lilith* — if a woman miscarries a fetus resembling *Lilith*, which Rashi describes as a human form with wings, the mother is *temei'ah leidah*. The wings do not cancel the fundamental human appearance. *Vlada hu ela she-yesh lo knafayim* — it is a fetus, it merely also has wings.

### Simuni Case

A *braita* confirms this ruling. Rabbi Yosi reports that a case arose in Simuni in which a woman gave birth to a *Lilith*-like fetus. When it was brought before the sages, they ruled *vlada hu ela she-yesh lo knafayim* — it is a fetus, with the additional feature of wings. The winged form does not displace human identity.

### Snake Fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפֶּלֶת דְּמוּת נָחָשׁ, הוֹרָה חֲנִינָא בֶּן אָחִיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה. הָלַךְ רַבִּי יוֹסֵף וְסִפֵּר דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, שָׁלַח לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הַנְהֵג בֶּן אָחִיךָ וָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** There was a case of a woman **who discharged** an item that had **the form of a snake. Ḥanina,** the **son of Rabbi Yehoshua's brother, ruled** that **its mother is impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth. Rabbi Yosef went and told** this **matter to Rabban Gamliel.** Rabban Gamliel **sent to Rabbi Yehoshua: Take** hold of **your nephew and come** to me, so that I may admonish him for his ruling.

The Gemara turns to a more challenging case: *hamapelet demus nachash* — a fetus resembling a snake. The difficulty here, which was touched upon earlier in the discussion, is that a snake's eyes have a rounded appearance resembling human eyes, while the rest of the body is entirely unlike a human. Does the eye quality carry sufficient weight?

### Chanania Initial Ruling

Chanania ben achi shel Rabbi Yehoshua — Rabbi Yehoshua's nephew — issued a ruling: *imo temei'ah leidah*. The mother is *temei'ah*. The basis of the ruling, as emerges later, was that the eye of the snake-like fetus was round like a human eye, and that was the deciding feature.

### Gamliel Summons

Rabbi Yosi, evidently troubled by this ruling, brought it to Rabban Gamliel's attention. The underlying issue appears to be at least partly one of rabbinic authority: who has the standing to issue an independent halachic ruling of this kind? Rabban Gamliel — who is the *nasi* and whose contentious relationship with Rabbi Yehoshua over questions of rabbinic authority is well documented — sent word to Rabbi Yehoshua: bring your nephew and come before me.

### Daughter-in-law Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהֲלִיכָתָן, יָצְתָה כַּלַּת (רַבִּי) חֲנִינָא לִקְרָאתוֹ, אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין נָחָשׁ מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהּ: אִמּוֹ טְהוֹרָה. אָמְרָה לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא מִשִּׁמְךָ אָמְרָה לִי חֲמוֹתִי אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה? וְאָמַר לָהּ: מֵאֵיזֶה טַעַם? הוֹאִיל וְגַלְגַּל עֵינוֹ עָגוֹל כְּשֶׁל אָדָם. מִתּוֹךְ דְּבָרֶיהָ נִזְכַּר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, שָׁלַח לוֹ לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מִפִּי הוֹרָה חֲנִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **While they were going** to Rabban Gamliel, **Ḥanina's daughter-in-law went out to greet** Rabbi Yehoshua, and **said to him: My teacher, what is** the *halakha* with regard to a woman **who discharges** an item that looks **like a snake?** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to her: Its mother is pure. She said to him: But my mother-in-law said to me in your name** that **its mother is impure** in such a case, **and** that you **said to her: For what reason** is she impure? It is **because the pupil of** a snake **is round like that of a human. Due to her statement, Rabbi Yehoshua remembered** that he had issued such a ruling. He subsequently **sent** a message **to Rabban Gamliel: Ḥanina issued the ruling based on my** own statement.

As they were on their way, Chanania's daughter-in-law came out to meet them. She addressed Chanania and asked: *hamapelet k'min nachash mahu* — what is the law if the fetus resembles a snake? He answered her: *imo tehorah* — the mother is *tehorah*. He had reversed his ruling. She then pressed him: *v'halo shimcha amra li chamoti* — but your wife told me in your name that the mother is *temei'ah*. Chanania asked what reason his wife had given. The daughter-in-law replied: *ho'il v'galgal eino agol k'shel adam* — because the eye of the snake is round like a human eye.

### Yehoshua Support and Vindication

This exchange triggered Rabbi Yehoshua's memory. *Nitkar Rabbi Yehoshua* — Rabbi Yehoshua recalled that he himself had once ruled this way, and that Chanania's ruling had in fact been based on his own authority. He sent word to Rabban Gamliel: *mipi horah Chananya* — Chanania's ruling was given on my authority. He should not be in trouble; he was in fact transmitting his uncle's own ruling, which had simply been forgotten.

Whether Chanania was summoned because the ruling was considered incorrect or because the concern was about a junior scholar issuing independent rulings without authorization is itself an interesting question. The resolution — that the ruling was traceable to Rabbi Yehoshua himself — addresses both concerns simultaneously.

### Abyaye Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן דְּאָמַר מִילְּתָא — לֵימָא בַּהּ טַעְמָא, דְּכִי מַדְכְּרוּ לֵיהּ — מִדְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Conclude from** this incident that **a Torah scholar [*tzurva merabbanan*] who says** a halakhic **matter should say the reason** for his statement, **so that when** his colleagues **remind him** of his reasoning, **he will remember** that ruling, as happened to Rabbi Yehoshua.

Abyaye derives from this episode a general principle: *tzurba me-rabanan d'amar milta leima b'ta'ameh* — when a Torah scholar issues a ruling, he should state his reasoning alongside it. *D'chi madkru lei midkar* — because if he provides the reason, when someone later reminds him of it, he will remember. If only the conclusion is transmitted — *muttar*, *asur* — the scholar may later be entirely unable to account for the ruling. But if the reason accompanies the ruling, a reminder can restore recollection. This is exactly what happened here: the reasoning transmitted by the daughter-in-law — the round eye — restored Rabbi Yehoshua's memory of his own position and vindicated Chanania entirely.
