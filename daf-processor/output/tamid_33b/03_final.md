# Tamid 33b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## High Priest & Blessing

### High Priest Retinue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל נִכְנָס לְהִשְׁתַּחֲוֹת, שְׁלֹשָׁה אוֹחֲזִין בּוֹ: אֶחָד בִּימִינוֹ, וְאֶחָד בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וְאֶחָד בַּאֲבָנִים טוֹבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: 7:1 After the priests concluded sacrificing the daily morning offering, they would enter the Sanctuary to prostrate themselves. **On occasions when the High Priest** would **enter** the Sanctuary **to prostrate** himself, he would enter before the other priests. When the High Priest enters the Sanctuary, **three** priests **hold him** to assist him and support him, in order to distinguish the service of the High Priest from that of the other priests entering the Sanctuary. **One** priest held **his right** hand **and one** priest held **his left** hand, **and one** priest stood behind the High Priest, holding **onto** the two **precious** onyx **stones** located on the shoulders of the High Priest, on the ephod.

The final section of Masechet Tamid opens at 33b by describing the procedure when the *kohen gadol* himself chose to participate in the morning service. The *mishnah* establishes a baseline: at this stage of the *avodah*, the *kohanim* who had performed the work inside the *heichal* — cleaning the *menorah*, the golden *mizbeiach*, and offering the *ketores* — would enter the *heichal* to perform *hishtachavayah*, prostration, before the next phase began. The *mishnah* specifically highlights what occurred when it was the *kohen gadol* who came to prostrate himself.

The text states: when the *kohen gadol* entered to bow, *shlosha ochezim bo* — three men would hold him, one on his right, one on his left, and one holding the precious stones. This retinue was clearly an expression of *kavod*, befitting the dignity of the office. The precise meaning of "holding the precious stones" invites some discussion. Some understand the reference to be the *avnei shoham* on the shoulders of the *ephod*, or perhaps the *choshen*. One possibility is that the stones were carried separately by an attendant walking before the *kohen gadol*, functioning as a ceremonial display of the jewel-encrusted vestments. Another possibility is that the *ephod* itself, being heavy, may have required physical support. The text does not fully resolve this.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁשָּׁמַע הַמְמוּנֶּה קוֹל רַגְלָיו שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהוּא יוֹצֵא, הִגְבִּיהַּ לוֹ אֶת הַפָּרוֹכֶת. נִכְנַס, וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה וְיָצָא. נִכְנְסוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים, הִשְׁתַּחֲווּ וְיָצְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And once the appointed** priest **heard** the **sound** from the **feet of the High Priest,** produced by the bells attached to the bottom of his robe, he knew **that** the High Priest was **emerging** from the Sanctuary, and he **lifted the curtain** suspended at the opening of the Entrance Hall **for him,** to facilitate his exit. After the High Priest **entered and prostrated** himself **and emerged** from the Sanctuary, **his brethren the priests entered, prostrated** themselves, **and emerged** from the Sanctuary.

As the *kohen gadol* was completing his prostration and leaving the *heichal*, one could hear him approaching through the *pe'amonim*, the bells of the *me'il*. The *memunneh* — the supervising official — would then lift the curtain at the entrance between the *ulam* and the outside, allowing the *kohen gadol* to exit. The *memunneh* himself would then enter and prostrate, followed by all the other *kohanim*, who entered and performed their *hishtachavayah* in turn.

### Prostration Sequence

This passage confirms what had been left implicit in the preceding *mishnah*: all the *kohanim* who were serving that day, after the *ketores* was completed, went into the *heichal* to perform *hishtachavayah*. The *meforshim* understand that this was the moment — after the *ketores* offering — when the assembled *kohanim* entered the inner sanctuary not to perform any specific *avodah*, but simply to prostrate themselves before the Divine presence.

This is a theologically significant detail. The *heichal* was not ordinarily a place one entered without purpose; entry was restricted to those performing the *avodah*. Yet once the morning *avodot* were complete, this act of *hishtachavayah* was permitted and indeed practiced. It reflects a dimension of the Temple service that is less transactional than the *korbanot* themselves — a moment of direct personal connection to the *Shechinah*, which was understood to rest particularly over the *aron* in the *kodesh kodashim* and by extension to fill the *heichal*. This is the world of *tefillah* as opposed to *korban*, the personal encounter with God's presence.

### Blessing Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ וְעָמְדוּ עַל מַעֲלוֹת הָאוּלָם. עָמְדוּ הָרִאשׁוֹנִים לִדְרוֹם אֲחֵיהֶם הַכֹּהֲנִים, וַחֲמִשָּׁה כֵּלִים בְּיָדָם: הַטְּנִי בְּיַד אֶחָד, וְהַכּוּז בְּיַד אֶחָד, וְהַמַּחְתָּה בְּיַד אֶחָד, וְהַבָּזֵךְ בְּיַד אֶחָד, וְכַף וְכִסּוּיוֹ בְּיַד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 7:2 After the priests emerged from the Sanctuary, **they came and stood on the** twelve **stairs** before **the Entrance Hall. The first** five priests **stood to** the **south** of **their brethren, the priests,** who had taken the limbs of the daily offering up to the altar. **And** those five priests had **five vessels in their hands: The basket** with the ashes from the inner altar was **in the hands of one** priest; **and the jug** with the ashes from the Candelabrum was **in the hands of one** priest; **and the coal pan** was **in the hands of one** priest; **and the** smaller **vessel,** the bowl that held the incense, was **in the hands of one** priest, who had burned the incense; **and** the **spoon and its cover** were **in the hands of one** priest, the friend or relative of the one who burned the incense.

After completing their *hishtachavayah*, the *kohanim* emerged and took their positions on the steps of the *ulam*. The *mishnah* states: *ba'u ve'amdu al ma'alos ha-ulam* — they came and stood on the steps of the *ulam*. Those who had performed the various *avodot* of the *heichal* — the five *kohanim* who carried the *kelei hamikdash* — stood to the south of the steps, each holding his respective vessel: one held the *teni*, the gold basket with the ashes from the golden *mizbeiach*; one held the *kuz*, the pitcher containing the material cleared from the *menorah*; one held the *machtah*, the gold shovel with the coals; one held the *bazach*, the small gold vessel with the *ketores*; and one held the *kaf* with its cover, the larger container.

The entire assembly of *kohanim* then stood on these steps — the very same steps where, on ordinary occasions, the *leviyim* would stand to sing their *shirah* — and performed *birkat kohanim*, the priestly blessing. There is a striking parallel here to the account in *Shmini*, where *Aharon* exits the *ohel mo'ed* and blesses the people: *vayisa aharon et yadov el ha'am vayivarkhem*. The *chazal* understand that as a reference to *birkat kohanim*, and indeed the structure here mirrors that: the *kohanim* enter the *heichal*, perform their prostration, emerge, and then bless the people. The parallel is not exact — in *Shmini*, *Aharon*'s blessing followed the offering of the *korbanot* before he entered, whereas here it follows the exit from the *heichal* after the *hishtachavayah* — but the resonance is clear and intentional.

By this stage in the service, the *yisraelim* were evidently already gathered in the *azarah*, for the blessing was directed toward them. Precisely when the gates were opened to the public is never stated explicitly in the *mishnah*, a point that will be discussed below.

### Single Blessing Format

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵרְכוּ אֶת הָעָם בְּרָכָה אַחַת. אֶלָּא שֶׁבַּמְּדִינָה אוֹמְרִים אוֹתָהּ שָׁלֹשׁ בְּרָכוֹת, וּבַמִּקְדָּשׁ בְּרָכָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The priests placed their vessels on the ground **and** then **blessed the people,** reciting **one blessing.** The Priestly Benediction was recited outside the Temple as well, **but** in the Temple, it was recited differently, **as in** the rest of **the country** the priests would **recite it** as **three blessings,** and the listeners would answer amen after each blessing. **But in the Temple** they would recite it as **one blessing,** and the listeners would not respond to each blessing. Rather, at the conclusion of the entire Priestly Benediction they would answer: Blessed are You Lord, God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting.

The *mishnah* specifies that the *kohanim* recited *birkat kohanim* as *bracha achat* — a single blessing. This requires explanation, since we ordinarily speak of the priestly blessing as three distinct verses. The answer given is that in the *mikdash*, the three verses were recited continuously, without any *amen* response between them. It was said as an uninterrupted unit, and therefore counted as one. Outside the *mikdash*, by contrast, the blessing is divided into three, with the congregation responding *amen* between each verse — making it, in effect, three blessings.

### Divine Name & Hands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים אֶת הַשֵּׁם כִּכְתָבוֹ, וּבַמְּדִינָה בְּכִנּוּיוֹ. בַּמְּדִינָה – הַכֹּהֲנִים נוֹשְׂאִים אֶת יְדֵיהֶם כְּנֶגֶד כִּתְפֵיהֶם, וּבַמִּקְדָּשׁ – עַל גַּבֵּי רָאשֵׁיהֶן. חוּץ מִכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, שֶׁאֵין מַגְבִּיהַּ אֶת יָדָיו לְמַעְלָה מִן הַצִּיץ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַגְבִּיהַּ אֶת יָדָיו לְמַעְלָה מִן הַצִּיץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשָּׂא אַהֲרֹן אֶת יָדָיו אֶל הָעָם וַיְבָרְכֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **In the Temple,** the priests **would recite the name** of God **as it is written,** with the letters *yod*, *heh*, *vav*, *heh*, whereas **in** the rest of **the country** the priests would recite the name of God **by His appellation,** *alef*, *dalet*, *nun*, *yod*. Furthermore, **in** the rest of **the country,** while reciting the Priestly Benediction **the priests lift their hands opposite their shoulders, and in the Temple** they raise them **above their heads.** That is the *halakha* with regard to all priests in the Temple, **except for** the **High Priest, who does not raise his hands above the frontplate** on his forehead, as the name of God is written on the frontplate. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** the **High Priest** would **raise his hands above the frontplate** while reciting the Priestly Benediction in the Temple, **as it is stated** with regard to the Priestly Benediction recited by Aaron the High Priest: **"And Aaron lifted his hands toward the people and blessed them"** (Leviticus 9:22).

Two further distinctions between the *mikdash* practice and that of the *medinah* — outside the Temple — are noted here. First, *bamikdash hayu omrim et hashem kichtavo*: in the *mikdash*, the *kohanim* pronounced God's name as it is written, using the ineffable four-letter name. Outside the *mikdash*, the name is pronounced by its substitute, as is universal practice today.

Second, there is a distinction in the height at which the hands are raised. Outside the *mikdash*, the *kohanim* raise their hands to shoulder height, *keneged kitfeihem*. In the *mikdash*, they raised their hands above their heads. The reason given is that within the *mikdash*, the *Shechinah* was understood to rest upon the hands of the *kohanim* as they blessed. In such a context, one must not have any part of his body higher than the locus of the Divine presence — and therefore the hands had to be elevated above the head.

There is one exception: the *kohen gadol* did not raise his hands above the *tzitz*, the gold headband bearing the inscription *kodesh l'Hashem* that he wore on his forehead. Since the *tzitz* itself represents the presence of holiness at the top of the head, the *kohen gadol* need not raise his hands above it, as that level already satisfies the requirement. *Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees and holds that even the *kohen gadol* must raise his hands above the *tzitz*, deriving this from the verse *vayisa aharon et yadav el ha'am vayivarkhem* — he lifted his hands and blessed them — implying an unrestricted elevation of the hands.

## Offering Limbs

### Ascending Ramp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל רוֹצֶה לְהַקְטִיר, הָיָה עוֹלֶה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ, וְהַסְּגָן בִּימִינוֹ. הִגִּיעַ לְמַחֲצִית הַכֶּבֶשׁ, אָחַז הַסְּגָן בִּימִינוֹ וְהֶעֱלָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 7:3 The High Priest is entitled to sacrifice any offering brought to the Temple, at his discretion. **When the High Priest wishes to burn** the limbs of the daily offering and the accompanying meal offering, he **would ascend the ramp** to the altar, **and the deputy** High Priest would walk **to his right.** When he **reached half** the height of **the ramp, the Deputy** would **hold his right** hand **and take him up** to the altar at the top of the ramp.

The *mishnah* now describes the final act of the *tamid*: the burning of the limbs that had been placed halfway up the *kevesh*, the ramp of the outer *mizbeiach*, earlier in the morning. When the *kohen gadol* wished to participate in this *haktarah* — the offering of the limbs onto the fire — the procedure was adapted to accommodate him. He would ascend the ramp, and the *sgan*, the deputy *kohen gadol*, would stand at his right. At the midpoint of the ramp, the *sgan* would take hold of the *kohen gadol*'s right hand and assist him the rest of the way up. The text does not shy away from the implication: the *kohen gadol*, often a political appointee unfamiliar with the rigors of the *avodah*, may not have been physically capable of ascending the full length of the ramp unassisted.

### Limb Presentation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהוֹשִׁיט לוֹ הָרִאשׁוֹן הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל, וְסָמַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן, וּזְרָקָן. הוֹשִׁיט הַשֵּׁנִי לָרִאשׁוֹן שְׁתֵּי הַיָּדַיִם, נוֹתְנָן לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְסָמַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן, וּזְרָקָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the first** of the nine priests who took the limbs up to the altar **handed** the High Priest **the head and the hind leg** of the offering, **and** the High Priest **placed his hands upon them and** then **threw them** onto the altar fire. Next **the second** of the nine priests **handed the two forelegs** of the offering **to the first** priest. He **gave them to the High Priest,** who **placed his hands upon them and** then **threw them** onto the altar fire.

Once the *kohen gadol* was positioned at the top, the other *kohanim* — those who had carried the limbs up — would hand them to him one by one. The *mishnah* specifies that the first *kohen* handed him *harosh veharegel*, the head and the foot, and the *kohen gadol* would perform *semikhah* on them — laying his hands upon them — and then throw them onto the fire. This *semikhah* is, as the text itself acknowledges, largely symbolic. Normally, *semikhah* is performed on a living animal prior to slaughter; here it is a gesture of connection and *kavod*, linking the *kohen gadol* to an offering in which he had played no prior role.

### Throwing Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁמַט הַשֵּׁנִי וְהָלַךְ לוֹ. וְכָךְ הָיוּ מוֹשִׁיטִין לוֹ שְׁאָר כׇּל הָאֵבָרִין, וְהוּא סוֹמֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶן וְזוֹרְקָן. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה – הוּא סוֹמֵךְ, וַאֲחֵרִים זוֹרְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** At that point, **the second** priest **slipped away and left. And** in **that** manner the priests **would hand** the High Priest **the rest of all the limbs, and he** would **place his hands upon them and** then **throw them** onto the altar fire. **And when he wishes, he places his hands and others throw** the limbs onto the fire.

After the first *kohen* handed over the head and foot, the second *kohen* — who had carried the two forearms — would hand them to the first *kohen*, who in turn would hand them to the *kohen gadol*. The *kohen gadol* would again perform *semikhah* and throw them onto the fire. The second *kohen* would then depart, and the process would repeat with each subsequent pair of *kohanim*: each one handing his limbs to the *kohen* stationed permanently beside the *kohen gadol*, who then passed them up. This arrangement preserved a sense of dignity and order, sparing the *kohen gadol* from the somewhat unseemly scene of a constant stream of *kohanim* arriving and departing at his side.

If the *kohen gadol* wished, he could limit his personal participation to the *semikhah* alone and allow the *sgan* or another *kohen* to perform the actual throwing onto the fire.

### Altar Circuit & Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לְהַקִּיף אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מֵהֵיכָן הוּא מַתְחִיל – מִקֶּרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית, מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית. נָתְנוּ לוֹ יַיִן לְנַסֵּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** When the High Priest, who was at the top of the ramp on the south side of the altar, **came to circle the altar** to reach the southwestern corner, where he would pour the libation of wine, **from where does he begin?** He begins **from the southeastern corner** and continues to the **northeastern** corner, then to the **northwestern** corner, and ultimately reaches the **southwestern** corner. At that point, the priests **gave him wine to pour.**

Upon completing the offering of the limbs, the *kohen gadol* descended and circumambulated the *mizbeiach*. The general rule *kol pinot she'ata poneh lo yehu ela leyamin* — all turns are made only to the right — means the circuit proceeds counterclockwise as viewed from above. The *kohen gadol* began at the southeast corner, proceeded to the northeast, then to the northwest, and completed the circuit at the southwest corner. It was at the southwest corner that the *nisuch hayayin*, the wine libation, was performed, the wine being poured through the dedicated funnels that channeled it down beneath the *mizbeiach*.

## Signals & Song (II)

### Flag Signals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַסְּגָן עוֹמֵד עַל הַקֶּרֶן, וְהַסּוּדָרִים בְּיָדוֹ. שְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים עוֹמְדִין עַל שֻׁלְחַן הַחֲלָבִים, וּשְׁתֵּי חֲצוֹצְרוֹת בְּיָדָם. תָּקְעוּ וְהֵרִיעוּ וְתָקְעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Deputy stands** at the High Priest's side **at the corner** of the altar **and the cloths** are **in his hand,** so that he can wave them to signal to the Levites to begin singing when the High Priest pours the libation. **Two priests stand at the** marble **table of the fats,** where the limbs and fats were placed before being taken to the altar, **and** there were **two** silver **trumpets in their hands.** These two priests **sounded a *tekia*,** a long continuous blast; they then **sounded a *terua*,** a series of staccato blasts; **and** lastly they **sounded** another ***tekia*** to alert the Levites to prepare to recite the psalm.

The *sgan* stood at the corner holding *sudarim* — flags or handkerchiefs — to be used for signaling. Two *kohanim* stood by the *shulchan hachalavim*, the fat table in the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbeiach*, each holding a *chatzotzrah*, a trumpet. When the *kohen gadol* (or whoever performed the *nisuch*) was about to pour the wine, they would sound *tekiah teruah tekiah*. They then proceeded to stand beside Ben Arza, who was positioned at the *tzeltzel*, some form of percussion instrument — perhaps resembling a triangle or gong. One trumpet-bearer stood on his right and one on his left.

### Trumpets & Alert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחָה לְנַסֵּךְ, וְהֵנִיף הַסְּגָן בַּסּוּדָרִים, וְהִקִּישׁ בֶּן אַרְזָא בַּצִּלְצָל, וְדִבְּרוּ הַלְוִיִּם בַּשִּׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** Then the High Priest **stooped to pour** the libation, **and the Deputy waved the cloths, and ben Arza struck the cymbals, and the Levites recited the psalm** of that day of the week.

As the officiant bent down to pour the wine — the moment of *nisuch hayayin* — the *sgan* waved the flags, signaling that the libation was actually beginning. Ben Arza then struck the *tzeltzel*, and the *leviyim* opened into song: *vedibru haLevi'im beshir*.

### Song with Wine

The *leviyim* were stationed on the *duchan*, the steps of the *ulam* — the same location where the *kohanim* had just stood for *birkat kohanim*. The musical performance combined instrumental accompaniment with vocal singing, and the *mishnah*'s phrasing *dibru beshir* — literally "spoke in song" — emphasizes the primacy of the vocal element. This relates to a broader debate as to whether *ikar shirah bepeh* or *ikar shirah bikli* — whether the essential component of the Levitical *shirah* is the singing or the instrumental accompaniment — but the language here favors the vocal dimension.

A fundamental principle underlies the timing of the *shirah*: *ein shirah ela al hayayin* — there is no song except over wine. The *gemara* derives this from various associations between wine and joy, including the verse in *Shoftim* where the vine declares that its wine *mesameiach levav anashim* — gladdens the heart of men. Because of this principle, the *leviyim* did not sing during the *shechitah* of the *tamid*, or during the *zerikas hadam*, or during the burning of the *evarim*. They waited until the very moment of the *nisuch hayayin*. This same principle underlies the institution of *kiddush* over wine, and more broadly the practice of performing many significant ritual acts *al hakos*, over a cup of wine — a practice that, while not found in the *gemara* itself for occasions such as a *bris milah* or a wedding, was introduced relatively early precisely because of wine's established ritual significance.

### Psalm Divisions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעוּ לַפֶּרֶק, תָּקְעוּ, וְהִשְׁתַּחֲווּ הָעָם. עַל כׇּל פֶּרֶק – תְּקִיעָה, וְעַל כׇּל תְּקִיעָה – הִשְׁתַּחֲוָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Whenever the Levites **reached the** end of one **section** of the psalm, the priests **sounded a *tekia*, and the people** in the courtyard **prostrated** themselves. **At** the end of **each section** there was **a *tekia*, and for every *tekia*** there was **a prostration.**

Each *psalm* — the *shir shel yom*, as we shall see — was divided into three sections. When the *leviyim* completed the first section and arrived at the break, the *kohanim* sounded another *tekiah teruah tekiah*. At each such *tekiah*, the assembled people bowed — *vayishtachavu ha'am*. This happened three times, corresponding to the three sections of the psalm: *al kol perek tekiah*, and *al kol tekiah hishtachavayah*. The intent was evidently to synchronize the completion of the wine pouring with the completion of the singing, so that both acts concluded together.

### Closing Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶהוּ סֵדֶר הַתָּמִיד לַעֲבוֹדַת בֵּית אֱלֹהֵינוּ, יְהִי רָצוֹן שֶׁיִּבָּנֶה בִּמְהֵרָה בְּיָמֵינוּ, אָמֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **That is the procedure for** the sacrifice of **the daily offering in the service of the House of our God; may it be** His **will that it will be speedily rebuilt in our day, amen.**

The *mishnah* concludes with a remarkable closing line: *zeh'u seder hatamid la'avodat beit eloheinu, yehi ratzon sheyibaneh bimeherah beyameinu amen* — "This is the order of the *tamid* for the service of the house of our God; may it be His will that it be rebuilt speedily in our days, amen." The insertion of this prayer indicates that the *mishnah* was composed — or at least this closing was added — after the *churban habayit*. The *gemara* at the beginning of *Middot* will discuss the authorship of these tractates and suggests that the author was someone who had himself witnessed the Temple in his lifetime. This closing line transforms the descriptive *mishnah* into an act of longing and prayer.

## Levitical Songs

### Day One Song

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשִּׁיר שֶׁהָיוּ הַלְוִיִּם אוֹמְרִים בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ: בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״לַה׳ הָאָרֶץ וּמְלוֹאָהּ תֵּבֵל וְיוֹשְׁבֵי בָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 7:4 The following is a list of each daily **psalm that the Levites would recite in the Temple. On the first day** of the week **they would recite** the psalm beginning: "A psalm of David. **The earth is the Lord's and all it contains, the world and all who live in it"** (Psalms, chapter 24).

The final *mishnah* of the tractate records the *shir shel yom* — the psalm recited by the *leviyim* for each day of the week. On the first day of the week, *bayom harishon hayu omrim: L'Hashem ha'aretz umlo'ah, tevel v'yoshvei vah* — Psalm 24. The connection to the first day is transparent: the first day of creation, when God established dominion over the earth, is directly reflected in the psalm's opening declaration that the earth and its fullness belong to God.

### Day Two Song

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשֵּׁנִי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״גָּדוֹל ה׳ וּמְהֻלָּל מְאֹד בְּעִיר אֱלֹהֵינוּ הַר קׇדְשׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **On the second** day **they would recite** the psalm beginning: "A song; a psalm of the sons of Korah. **Great is the Lord and highly to be praised in the city of God, on His sacred mountain"** (Psalms, chapter 48).

On the second day, the *leviyim* recited *Gadol Hashem u'mihullal me'od b'ir Elokeinu har kodsho* — Psalm 48. The connection to the second day — the separation of the upper and lower waters and the formation of the heavens — is less immediately obvious. One approach found in the *Zohar*, cited by *Kehati*, reads the six days of creation as symbolic of six millennia of history, with each thousand-year period corresponding to a day. Under that framework, the second millennium was the age of *Avraham Avinu* — born in the year 1948 from creation — and the pivotal events of *Akeidas Yitzchak* and the choosing of *Har HaMoriah*, the *ir Elokeinu*, the city of God. This renders the mention of *b'ir Elokeinu har kodsho* apt for the second day.

### Days 3-7 Songs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלִישִׁי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים יִשְׁפּוֹט״. בָּרְבִיעִי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״אֵל נְקָמוֹת ה׳ אֵל נְקָמוֹת הוֹפִיעַ״. בַּחֲמִישִׁי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״הַרְנִינוּ לֵאלֹהִים עוּזֵּנוּ הָרִיעוּ לֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״. בַּשִּׁשִּׁי הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״ה׳ מָלָךְ גֵּאוּת לָבֵשׁ לָבֵשׁ וְגוֹמֵר״. בַּשַּׁבָּת הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״מִזְמוֹר שִׁיר לְיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת״ – מִזְמוֹר שִׁיר לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא, לְיוֹם שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ שַׁבָּת וּמְנוּחָה לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **On the third** day **they would recite** the psalm beginning: "A psalm of Asaph. **God stands in the divine assembly; among the judges He delivers judgment"** (Psalms, chapter 82). **On the fourth** day **they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"O Lord God, to Whom vengeance belongs, God to Whom vengeance belongs, shine forth"** (Psalms, chapter 94). **On the fifth** day **they would recite** the psalm beginning: "For the leader; upon the Gittith, a psalm of Asaph. **Sing for joy to God, our strength; shout aloud to the God of Jacob"** (Psalms, chapter 81). **On the sixth** day **they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"The Lord reigns: He is robed in majesty; the Lord is robed,** girded with strength" (Psalms, chapter 93). **On Shabbat they would recite** the psalm beginning: **"A psalm, a song for Shabbat day"** (Psalms, chapter 92). This is interpreted as **a psalm, a song for the future, for the day that will be entirely Shabbat and rest for everlasting life.**

On the third day: *Elokim nitzav ba'adas Eil, b'kerev elokim yishpot* — Psalm 82. The *Zohar* associates the third millennium with *matan Torah*, the settlement of *Eretz Yisrael*, and the eras of the first and second *Beit HaMikdash* — a period when God was fully present and engaged with His people, *Elokim nitzav ba'adas*.

On the fourth day: *Eil nekamot Hashem, Eil nekamot hofi'a* — Psalm 94. The fourth millennium was the period of the *churban habayit* and *galus*, when Divine judgment fell upon the people — hence the theme of retribution and justice.

On the fifth day: *Harninu l'Elokim uzeinu, hari'u l'Elokei Yaakov* — Psalm 81. The *Zohar* connects this to the ongoing *galus*, where in the absence of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the Jewish people's connection to God is maintained through prayer and song — *harninu* — rather than through the *avodah*.

On the sixth day: *Hashem malach ge'us lavesh, lavesh Hashem oz isazar* — Psalm 93. The sixth millennium anticipates the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash* and the ultimate sovereignty of God.

On *Shabbos*: *mizmor shir l'yom hashabas* — Psalm 92. This is both the plain meaning — the song of *Shabbos* — and, according to the *mishnah* itself, something more: *shir l'asid lavo, l'yom shekulo shabbos u'menucha l'chayei ha'olamim* — a song for the era to come, for the day that is entirely *Shabbos* and rest, for eternal life. The *Zohar*'s interpretive framework, which read the six weekday psalms as corresponding to the six millennia of history, here reaches its natural culmination: the seventh psalm is not merely about the seventh day, but about the seventh millennium — the ultimate *menucha*, the era of *olam haba*.

With this, the *mishnah* concludes, and the tractate closes: *hadran alach perek zman shehein gadil, u'selikah masechet Tamid*.
