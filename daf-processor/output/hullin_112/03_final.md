# Hullin 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nat Bar Nat Summary

### Nat Bar Nat Principles

Today's daf is 112, and we resume from where we concluded on Shabbos: the discussion of *nat bar nat* — taste that is two degrees removed. Before proceeding to the new material, a brief summary of the practical *halacha* is in order.

The Gemara's foundational ruling is expressed in the phrase *dagim she'alu b'kara mutar le'ochlan b'kutach* — fish that rose in a *fleishig* plate may nonetheless be eaten with milk. Even if the plate was *fleishig* and the fish were hot, the fish themselves are not considered *fleishig*, because the taste transfer is two degrees removed: from the meat into the plate, and from the plate into the fish. This is called *nat bar nat de-hetera* — two degrees removed where the original substance was itself *heter* — and it remains *heter*.

This is sharply distinguished from *nat bar nat de-issura*. The reason that using a pot that absorbed *chazir* renders food forbidden — even though the *chazir* went into the pot and the pot into the food, making it technically two degrees removed — is that once something carries a status of *issur*, that forbidden status, as it were, retains its "stickiness." It does not dissipate with distance. By contrast, *milchig* and *fleishig* by themselves are *heter*; they carry no forbidden status until they come into contact with the opposing category. Therefore, when the taste is weakened both practically and *halachically* by being two degrees removed from the original, the food retains no problematic status.

A further qualification: *nat bar nat de-hetera* is only *heter* when the second stage of transfer also remains *heter*. This is explicit in a *meforash* braisa: *k'deira she-bishal bah basar assur le-vashel bah chalav* — a pot in which meat was cooked may not be used to cook milk, because at the second stage — from pot to milk — one encounters meat and milk together. The *heter* of *nat bar nat* applies only where the outcome remains entirely within the *heter* realm.

### Parve in Fleishig/Milchig

What then is the practical status of *parve* food cooked in a *fleishig* or *milchig* pot — the familiar case of the "DE" (dairy equipment) designation on kosher products? The Gemara permits it: at that stage of transfer it is already *nat bar nat* and remains *heter*, and one may eat it together with the opposing category.

Yet if this ruling is straightforward, why do manufacturers label products "dairy equipment," and why the widespread stringency? The answer lies in a dispute among *Rishonim*. Based on Rashi and Tosafot, there is a serious question as to whether actual cooking over fire is comparable to the Gemara's case. The Gemara's ruling may have been limited to placing dry, hot fish on a dry plate — not to food cooked over flame, where the intensity of heat may effect a more direct transfer that does not reduce to a second degree. On the other hand, if cooking was done in water, the water may itself introduce a third degree, potentially restoring the leniency. These competing considerations generate the practical framework that follows.

### Ashkenazi vs Sefardi

For *Ashkenazim*, the upshot is: *lechatchila*, one may not cook *parve* food in a *milchig* pot with the intent to mix it with *fleishig*, nor may one *lechatchila* combine the two after the fact. To illustrate: if one wishes to serve spaghetti at a meal with meatballs, one may not cook those noodles in a *milchig* pot *lechatchila* if that combination is the plan. Even if the noodles were cooked without that intent and only afterward does one wish to add meat sauce, the *Ashkenazi* position remains that one should not mix them *lechatchila*, because perhaps cooking over fire does not reduce the transfer to a second degree.

However, if one did not realize and already combined them on the plate — *bedieved* — even *Ashkenazim* would permit eating what was already mixed. *Bedieved*, *nat bar nat de-hetera* is fully acceptable. *Sefardim* are more lenient: they permit the combination even *lechatchila*, provided it was not done with that intent from the outset.

Practically speaking, when advising people in this situation, one can indicate that eating the *parve* food and the *fleishig* at the same meal is acceptable, so long as they are kept separate on the plate and not mixed together. If they were inadvertently mixed, *bedieved* it is permitted. An additional point worth noting: eating food made in *dairy equipment* does not render one *milchig*, and eating food made in *fleishig* equipment does not render one *fleishig* — this is universally agreed upon.

There is also a broader issue with the OU's practice of labeling products as "dairy equipment" entirely. The concern motivating that label is that consumers will not know the *halachos* of dairy equipment and may mix it improperly. But as established above, even if such mixing occurs, it is *bedieved* permitted. There is a general principle in *halacha* that one does not enact *gezeiros* over situations that are in any case *mutar bedieved*. Calling a product "dairy" when it is at most "dairy equipment" goes beyond what the *halacha* requires.

### Ben Yomo Status

All of the above discussion applies when the *keilim* in question are *ben yomo* — used within the past twenty-four hours, so that the absorbed taste within them is still considered "good." When the *keilim* are *eino ben yomo* — more than a day old — the situation is considerably more lenient.

According to the Gra, if the vessel is *eino ben yomo* and the case involves *nat bar nat de-hetera*, one may cook the *parve* food in the *milchig* or *fleishig* pot *lechatchila* even with the intent to serve it at a meal where the opposing category will be present. Thus, if one's largest pot is a *milchig* pot and it is *eino ben yomo*, one may *lechatchila* cook spaghetti in it for a meat meal. The combination of *nat bar nat* with *eino ben yomo* status permits this even at the outset. That concludes the *regel achat* summary of *nat bar nat*.

### OU Gezeira Critique

As noted above, the OU's labeling of products as "dairy" when they are only made on dairy equipment reflects a self-imposed *gezeira*. The concern that consumers will be unaware of the relevant *halachos* is understandable, but it does not justify treating a *bedieved*-permitted product as if it were fully dairy. The principle is well established that where the underlying act is *mutar bedieved*, there is no basis for a rabbinic *gezeira* — and applying one in this case takes matters further than the *halacha* requires.

## Davar Charif Laws

### Radish & Knife Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאַגַּב חוּרְפֵּיהּ בָּלַע, אֲבָל קִישּׁוּת – גָּרֵיר לְבֵי פְסָקֵיהּ וְאָכֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **as due to its sharpness it absorbs** the fat on the knife. **But** if one cut **a cucumber** with the same knife, it does not absorb the fat to the same extent. One may therefore simply **scrape the place of the cut** to remove any fat residue, **and** then one may **eat** the cucumber with *kutaḥ*.

We turn now to a brief standing-on-one-foot summary of the knife and *davar charif* issues, which the Gemara addressed in just two lines but which carry enormous practical consequences. The Gemara's concern is that when one cuts a *tznon* — a radish — with a *fleishig* knife, the radish absorbs the *fleishig* taste from the knife.

### Clean vs Dirty Knives

A threshold question is whether the Gemara assumes a clean or dirty knife. The straightforward *pshat* of the Gemara is that the knives were dirty and greasy. Evidence for this is that the Gemara says that if one alternates between cutting the radish and cutting other items, it is not a problem. If the knife were clean and the issue were purely the power of the *davar charif* to pull out absorbed taste, it would be difficult to understand why cutting other things in between would alleviate the problem — the absorbed taste in the knife's walls would remain regardless. The simplest explanation is that there was grease on the surface of the knife, and cutting other items in between effectively removed that surface grease.

Tosafot reinforces this reading by noting that if even heat — which is a powerful mechanism of taste transfer — does not make *nat bar nat de-hetera* problematic, how can the *tznon* be more powerful than heat? If one cooked a *parve* item in a *fleishig* pot and the result is *nat bar nat de-hetera*, why would cutting with a *fleishig* knife create a greater problem? The implication is that the issue with the knife is the dirty grease on its surface, not the inherent power of the *davar charif* to extract deeply absorbed taste. On a clean knife, there would be no issue at all.

### Stringent Davar Charif

However, there is a competing reading of the Gemara, and it is this more stringent reading that forms the basis of the *Shulchan Aruch*'s ruling. According to this reading, even on a clean knife, the power of a *davar charif* is such that it actively pulls out the absorbed taste from within the knife — and crucially, it does so without heat and without reducing the taste to *nat bar nat*. In other words, the *davar charif* eliminates the "degree of removal" that would otherwise render the transfer a second-stage *heter*. The absorbed *fleishig* taste is pulled directly into the radish at full strength, as if it were a first-degree transfer.

### Combined Stringent Views

The analysis does not stop there. There is an additional Gemara in Avodah Zarah dealing with a particularly sharp substance — *kurtia de-chiltis* — which states that it enhances the taste of fatty foods. When this Gemara is combined with the stringent reading of our Gemara, one arrives at the most severe interpretation: a *davar charif* not only pulls out absorbed taste without heat and without reducing it to *nat bar nat*, but it will do so even from a knife that is *eino ben yomo* — used more than a day ago. The conclusion of this combined reading is that a completely clean *fleishig* knife, last used six weeks ago, renders the radish or onion cut with it fully *fleishig* — despite the knife being clean, despite it being *eino ben yomo*, and despite what would otherwise be the leniency of *nat bar nat de-hetera*.

This is the source of all the major stringencies associated with *davar charif* in practical *halacha*. Whether leniencies are available *bedieved* is a separate discussion, but as these are not the only possible readings of the Gemaras in question, there is room in extreme cases to explore such options.

### Davar Charif Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִילְחֵי דְּלִיפְתָּא – שְׁרֵי, דְּסִילְקָא – אֲסִירִי, וְאִי פְּתַךְ בְּהוּ דְּלִיפְתָּא – שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **turnip stalks** cut with such a knife are **permitted** for consumption with *kutaḥ*. But **chard** cut with such a knife is **prohibited** for consumption with *kutaḥ*, as it absorbs flavor from the knife. **And if one alternated between** cutting chard **and turnip** stalks it is **permitted,** as the turnip stalks nullify the taste of the meat in the knife.

As for which foods qualify as *davar charif*: the Gemara mentions the radish explicitly; onions and garlic are natural additions to the list. Salt is also raised as a candidate, though whether salt operates identically to the classic *davar charif* is subject to debate.

## Salt & Sour Milk Vessels

### Salt Dish Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב דִּימִי מֵרַב נַחְמָן: מַהוּ לְאַנּוֹחֵי כַּדָּא דְמִלְחָא גַּבֵּי כַּדָּא דְכַמְכָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָסוּר. דְּחַלָּא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi inquired of Rav Naḥman: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to placing a jug of salt,** used to salt meat, **alongside a jug of *kamka*,** i.e., *kutaḥ*, a milk dish? Need one be concerned lest some of the *kutaḥ* fall on the salt without his knowledge and ultimately contaminate his meat? Rav Naḥman **said to him: It is prohibited** to place the two jugs next to each other. Rav Dimi further inquired: **What** is the *halakha* with regard to a similar case involving a jug **of vinegar** used to season meat? Need one be concerned lest the *kutaḥ* fall into the vinegar? Rav Naḥman **said to him: It is permitted** to place these two jugs next to each other.

We now arrive at the actual beginning of today's daf, four lines down on 112a. *Ba'a minei Rav Dimi me-Rav Nachman* — Rav Dimi posed a question to Rav Nachman: *mahu lehaniach kada de-milcha gabi kada de-kimcha* — may one place a dish of salt next to a dish of *kutach*? *Kutach* was a sour-milk dip, used in their cuisine as a condiment. The salt in question was not a shaker but an open bowl from which one would pinch or sprinkle salt by hand. The concern is straightforward: both vessels are open, there is a risk of inadvertent cross-transfer, and a drop of the *milchig kutach* landing in the salt dish could then be transferred into *fleishig* food at a subsequent meal.

### Salt Forbidden, Vinegar…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא לְכִי תֵּיכוּל עֲלַהּ כּוֹרָא דְמִלְחָא? מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי – אִיתֵיהּ (איסורא) [לְאִיסּוּרָא] בְּעֵינֵיהּ, וְהַאי – לֵיתֵיהּ (איסורא) [לְאִיסּוּרָא] בְּעֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi asked: **And what is different** about the vinegar? Rav Naḥman responded: **When you** have thought **about it** long enough to **eat a *kor* of salt,** you will know the reason. The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason** then? In **this** case of the salt, **the prohibited** substance **is substantive,** as the traces of *kutaḥ* are discernible and not nullified by the salt. **But** in **that** case of the vinegar, **the prohibited** substance **is not substantive,** since the *kutaḥ* melts away in the vinegar and will no longer impart flavor.

Rav Nachman's response: *amar lei assur* — it is forbidden to place the salt dish beside the *kutach*. Rav Dimi then asked the follow-up: *dechala mai* — what if instead of salt, one has an open dish of vinegar next to the *kutach*? *Amar lei shari* — that is permitted. The difference prompted Rav Dimi to press further, and Rav Nachman, in an idiomatic response that is also a playful allusion to the very subject under discussion, invited him to measure out some salt before he would explain — a phrase meaning, essentially, think a bit harder.

### Retention & Dissolution

The reason is as follows: *hai ita issura be-hai ve-hai leita issura be-hai* — with the salt dish, the *issur* remains present in it, whereas with the vinegar dish, the *issur* does not remain present. The operative principle is one of retention versus dissolution. If a drop of *kutach* falls into the salt dish, it sits on top of or among the salt granules and remains there as a discrete presence. The next time one uses the salt and places it on meat, the *milchig* contamination is carried along with it. By contrast, if a drop of *kutach* falls into a dish of vinegar, it dissolves and disperses throughout the large volume of liquid, becoming *batel* — nullified — in the vast quantity of vinegar. There is no discrete remaining presence to be subsequently transferred. The principle emerging from this passage is that *bitul* operates only when the forbidden substance is genuinely dissolved and integrated, not when it retains its distinct identity within the medium into which it falls.

## Pigeon in Yogurt Case

### Rav Chinana Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בַּר גּוֹזָלָא דִּנְפַל לְכַדָּא דְּכַמְכָּא, שַׁרְיֵיהּ רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִפַּשְׁרוּנְיָא. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן חַכִּים לְמִישְׁרֵי כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא, אִי לָאו רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִפַּשְׁרוּנְיָא? קָסָבַר: כִּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל ״מָלִיחַ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּרוֹתֵחַ״, הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל מֵחֲמַת מִלְחוֹ, אֲבָל הַאי כּוּתָּחָא הֲרֵי נֶאֱכָל מֵחֲמַת מִלְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain young bird that fell into a jug of *kamka*,** i.e., *kutaḥ*. **Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rava of** the city of **Pashronya, permitted** the bird. **Rava said** about this: **Who is wise** enough **to** discern reasons to **permit** the food in difficult **cases like this, if not Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rava of Pashronya? He maintains** that **when Shmuel said** that **a salted** food imparts flavor **like a boiling** food, **that statement** applies only to a food so salty **that it is not eaten due to its salt, but this *kutaḥ*** can still be **eaten due to,** i.e., despite, **its salt.** Therefore, it is as if both foods are cold and unsalted, and they do not impart flavor to one another, provided one rinses the area of contact.

A case is now presented: *hahu bar gozla denafal lakada detimcha* — a pigeon fell into a pitcher of *kutach*, that is, a yogurt-like sour milk dip. Here we have meat and dairy together, but without any apparent heat source to drive the taste transfer. *Sharya Rav Chinana brei deRav miParshunya* — Rav Chinana son of Rav Mishparshunya permitted it. *Amar Rava* — Rava remarked with evident admiration: *man chakim lemishrei ki hai gavna i lav Rav Chinana brei deRav miParshunya* — who would have been clever enough to find the basis for permitting this, if not for Rav Chinana? The implied question is why this case requires any particular ingenuity to permit. The pigeon had been salted, as all meat must be, and the principle *maliach ke-roteach* — salted is like boiled — would seem to trigger taste transfer even without heat. Why then is it permitted?

### Maliach K'Roteach Principle

The answer is that Rav Chinana rules in accordance with a qualification of Shmuel's principle. *Ki amar Shmuel maliach harei hu ke-roteach* — when did Shmuel say that salting is equivalent to boiling? *Hani mili heicha de'eino ne'echal machamat milcho* — only where the food cannot be eaten because of its saltiness. *Aval hai kutcha* — but this *kutach*, *harei ne'echal machamat milcho* — is indeed edible despite its saltiness. It may be salty, but it is not at the threshold level of inedible saltiness that would trigger *maliach ke-roteach*. Therefore, the principle does not apply, and the pigeon is permitted.

The context that produces inedible saltiness is worth clarifying. There are two relevant scenarios: the saltiness of meat that is in the process of being kashered, with heavy salt still resting upon it before rinsing; and in earlier times, the heavy salting used to preserve meat before refrigeration. That is the level of saltiness Shmuel had in mind — salt so heavy that one would never eat the meat in that state.

### Rashi vs Tosafot Saltiness

Rashi and Tosafot disagree on the precise threshold. Rashi maintains that *eino ne'echal machamat milcho* refers to the extreme saltiness of preserved meat — the kind that would be buried in salt for preservation and would only become edible after rinsing. According to Rashi, this level of saltiness is almost never encountered in practice today, making *maliach ke-roteach* a rarely triggered principle. Tosafot disagrees and sets the threshold lower. According to Tosafot, *eino ne'echal machamat milcho* does not require complete inedibility of that extreme degree. Rather, it is sufficient that the food is as salty as *melach kadronit* — the salt used in the kashering process — meaning salty enough that a normal person would not eat the meat in that state without first rinsing off the salt. This is a lower and more common threshold than Rashi's. The practical *halacha* follows Tosafot.

### Ashkenazi Strict Saltiness

The matter does not end with Tosafot's ruling. True to the broader *Ashkenazi* approach of resolving uncertainty through stringency, the *Ashkenazi* position is that since we cannot precisely determine the exact point at which saltiness crosses the threshold of *eino ne'echal machamat milcho*, we apply stringency to all serious saltiness. This does not mean that a trivial pinch of salt used to season food is a concern — that level is not problematic. But once food becomes significantly salty, *Ashkenazim* treat it with the stringency of *maliach ke-roteach* out of concern that it may have crossed the threshold.

### Bahag Salt-Heat Equation

It is worth dwelling further on the underlying equation between salt and heat, because Tosafot frequently cites the *Bahag* — the *Ba'al Halachot Gedolot* — in connection with this principle, and the *Bahag*'s formulation illuminates much of what follows. The *Bahag* addresses a question the Gemara itself never directly answers: how long must meat be salted in order to complete the kashering process? The *Bahag*'s answer draws on Shmuel's equation of *maliach ke-roteach*: since salt works like heat, one salts the meat for as long as it would take for heat to draw out the blood when roasting the meat over an open fire. The comparison is made possible precisely because Shmuel equates the two mechanisms.

Tosafot builds on this to derive the type of saltiness that triggers taste transfer between pieces of food. Since it is the salt used for kashering — salt at the level equivalent to heat — that constitutes the operative degree of saltiness in Shmuel's principle, that same level of saltiness is what can drive taste transfer between adjacent foods. It is the saltiness that is equivalent to the application of heat, and that is why it has the legal effects of heat.

## Blood Extraction Laws

### Roasted vs Raw Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי חַי, אֲבָל צָלִי – בָּעֵי קְלִיפָה, וְאִי אִית בֵּיהּ פִּילֵי – כּוּלֵּיהּ אָסוּר, וְאִי מְתַבַּל בְּתַבְלִין – כּוּלֵּיהּ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And this statement** applies only if the bird is **raw, but** if it is **roasted, it requires peeling** to remove the outer layer, since roasting softens the meat and causes it to absorb more flavor. **And if it has cracks [*pilei*], it is entirely forbidden,** because the milk is absorbed into the cracks. **And if it has been flavored with spices it is** likewise **entirely forbidden,** because the spices soften the meat and render it absorbent.

The Gemara now turns to the topic of blood extraction from meat during the roasting process, continuing the practical *halachic* discussion of the previous daf. *Ve-hani mili chai* — this is true, that the pigeon that fell into the *kutach* is permitted, only when it is raw. *Aval tzli, bei klipa* — but if it was roasted, one must remove an outer shell. Furthermore, *ve-ei is bei pili, kulei assur* — if the roasted piece has crevices and cracks throughout, the entire piece is forbidden.

What does "roasted" mean in this context? One reading is that the meat was roasted and still hot, and the heat drives taste transfer directly into the outer layer — hence the requirement to remove an outer shell, *klipa*. But a second and equally compelling reading of this Gemara is that "roasted" does not mean the meat is currently hot, but rather that once meat has been roasted, it becomes more physically absorbent. This point deserves serious consideration, because it engages the *metzius* — the physical reality — and not merely the legal framework.

### Absorbency Without Heat

Consider a piece of soft, well-cooked *cholent* meat that is no longer hot. If it fell into a pitcher of milk and sat there for six hours, one could not simply dismiss the concern on the grounds that there was no *yad soledes bo* — no heat sufficient to transfer taste. A substance that is highly porous and absorbent will take in liquids even at cold temperatures, not through taste transfer in the technical *halachic* sense of heat-driven absorption, but through physical penetration into its pores and crevices. This is what the Gemara means when it says that roasted meat requires *klipa*: not that the meat is hot, but that the roasting process renders it absorbent enough that even without heat, the outer layer will have drawn in the milk.

This is also distinguished from the principle of *kavush* — soaking — which operates over a twenty-four-hour period. What the Gemara describes here is a shorter-term absorption driven purely by the physical absorbency of the roasted meat, not by the extended immersion required for *kavush* to take effect.

If there are crevices in the meat — as in a very porous, well-cooked piece — the concern extends throughout the entire piece, and it is entirely forbidden. The Gemara adds: *ve'im tavlu betavlin* — if the meat has been seasoned with spices, it is similarly forbidden across the board, even without crevices, because the spices themselves increase the osmotic draw of the meat and cause greater absorption of surrounding liquids. The same osmosis principle that explains how salt draws out blood from meat during kashering explains how heavily spiced or salted meat draws liquid inward when immersed.

### Red Juices Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כִּכָּר שֶׁחָתַךְ עָלֶיהָ בָּשָׂר – אָסוּר לְאׇכְלָהּ, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאַסְמֵיק, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאַבְרְיֵהּ, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאַסְמְכֵיהּ, אֲבָל קְלִישְׁתָּא – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel says: It is prohibited to eat a loaf** of bread **upon which one cut** unsalted roasted **meat,** since the blood expelled from the roasted meat is absorbed in the loaf. The Gemara adds: **And this statement** applies only **if** the meat **is ruddy** from the blood it contains. **And** furthermore, **this statement** applies only **if** so much blood was absorbed in the loaf that **it passed through** from one side of the loaf to the other and was visible from both sides. **And** furthermore, **this statement** applies only **if** the liquid emitted by the roasted meat **is viscous. But** if it is **runny, we have no** problem **with it,** i.e., the loaf is permitted.

The Gemara now addresses one of the most practically significant questions in this area: what is the status of the reddish juices that flow from roasted meat? Are they still blood — and therefore forbidden — or are they merely permissible gravy?

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Shmuel: kichra de-chatich alei bisra assur le-michlei* — if one cuts meat on a piece of bread, that bread is forbidden to eat. The concern is that the act of cutting causes juices to run off the meat and absorb into the bread, and those juices are suspected of being blood that was not fully extracted during the original process of salting or roasting.

A fundamental question immediately arises: if there is still blood in the meat, why is one permitted to eat the meat itself? The answer rests on the principle of *dama de-evarim shelo peirash* — blood that is absorbed within the substance of the meat itself and has not emerged. Blood that is genuinely dissolved within the tissue of the meat — not in a visible vein or organ but absorbed throughout the flesh — is halachically permissible as long as it has not separated and come out. Once it exits the meat, it assumes the status of forbidden blood. This is why one may in principle eat raw meat without kashering it: the blood is entirely within the meat and has not *peirash* — it has not separated. It is also why roasting, in contradistinction to cooking, does not create a problem even if not all blood is extracted: what stays in, stays in and is permissible; what comes out, comes out and must not be eaten. Cooking creates the additional problem that blood that emerges during cooking is re-absorbed back into the meat in a new, legally problematic form.

### Shmuel Forbids Red

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל שָׁדֵי לֵיהּ לְכַלְבֵּיהּ, רַב הוּנָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי שְׁרֵי – לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁרֵי, אִי אֲסִיר – לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֲסִיר! שָׁאנֵי רַב הוּנָא דַּאֲנִינָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ. רָבָא אָכֵיל לֵיהּ, וְקָרֵי לֵיהּ ״חֲמַר בָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel would throw to his dog** such a loaf of bread that he held was prohibited. **Rav Huna** would not eat the loaf himself but **would** rather **give it to his attendant.** The Gemara objects: **Whichever way you** look at it, Rav Huna's behavior is problematic: **If** the loaf **is permitted, it is permitted for everyone,** including Rav Huna himself. And **if it is prohibited, then it is prohibited for everyone,** and he should not give it to his attendant. The Gemara explains: In fact, the loaf is permitted for consumption, and **Rav Huna is different, as he is of delicate constitution** and did not want to eat the loaf himself. The Gemara further relates: **Rava would eat** a loaf of this type, **and he would call** the red liquid **meat wine.**

Shmuel's ruling continues: *ve-hani mili de-asmik* — this is only a concern if the juice that came out is red; *ve-hani mili de-avrai* — and only if it appeared in sufficient quantity to have penetrated through the full thickness of the bread; *ve-hani mili de-afmichi* — and only if the bread is thick, such that the juice has truly soaked all the way through. But *aval klisha* — if the juice is thin and watery, *leit lan bah* — that is not a problem. Thick, red juice that permeates a substantial piece of bread is the scenario Shmuel considers forbidden; thin, dilute liquid is not in the same category.

The Gemara records that when this occurred in practice, Shmuel would throw the juice-soaked bread to his dog — it was not fit for him to eat, but the dog could benefit from it. Rav Huna, by contrast, would give it to his personal attendant, who was Jewish. This prompted the obvious objection: *mima nafshach* — make up your mind. If it is permitted, it is permitted for everyone; if it is forbidden, it is forbidden for everyone. The Gemara resolves this: *shani Rav Huna de-adinin datei* — Rav Huna was simply fastidious, an *istenis*. For him, it was a matter of personal aesthetic preference, not *kashrut*. The bread was entirely kosher; he simply did not wish to eat something stained with red juices. His attendant, who had no such sensibility, was welcome to eat it, and halachically there was nothing wrong with it.

### Rava Permits Red

Rava takes a fundamentally different position from Shmuel: *Rava achil lei* — Rava would eat it, and he called it *chamra de-vasra* — the wine of the meat. In Rava's view, once meat has been properly roasted for the requisite time, whatever red juices emerge afterward are no longer halachically blood; they are simply the natural juices and gravy of roasted meat. The roasting process, when carried out properly, is presumed to have extracted all the blood that is halachically significant. Anything left behind does not carry the status of *dam* — blood — in the legal sense.

Note that this entire discussion concerns roasting specifically, not cooking after salting. If one were to propose that some blood remained after salting and then cooked the meat, the very act of cooking would cause that blood to emerge and then be re-absorbed, creating a genuine problem. Roasting is the only scenario where one can plausibly argue that remaining blood stayed in throughout the process, and therefore the question of whether post-roasting red juices constitute blood is a meaningful one.

The practical ruling follows Rava: after proper roasting, the red juices are permissible. Shmuel's position that even after roasting there may be residual blood that exits under knife pressure is a minority view, though as the Gemara will show, it is internally consistent.

## Catching Juices While Roasting

### Vessel Under Roasting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מַנִּיחִין כְּלִי תַּחַת בָּשָׂר עַד שֶׁיִּכְלֶה כׇּל מַרְאֵה אַדְמוּמִית שֶׁבּוֹ. מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? מָר זוּטְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מִשֶּׁתַּעֲלֶה תִּימְרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says: One may not place a vessel under** roasting **meat** to catch the drippings of fat **until all the ruddiness of** the meat's **appearance has dissipated.** Beforehand, though, one must be concerned that blood will fall with the drippings into the vessel, rendering the mixture and the vessel prohibited. The Gemara asks: **How do we know** when all the meat's redness has disappeared? **Mar Zutra said in the name of Rav Pappa: As soon as its smoke rises,** one can be sure that all the blood has been expelled from the meat.

The discussion now turns to the practical mechanics of collecting meat juices during the roasting process itself, without simultaneously collecting the blood that is being expelled. *Ein manichin kli tachat basar ad she-yichleh kol mareh admumit shebo* — one may not place a vessel beneath meat while it is roasting until all the reddish appearance in the juices has ceased. The concern is that while blood is still flowing out, catching the drippings will collect blood along with the gravy. One must therefore wait until the outflow of red juice has stopped before positioning a vessel to catch the drippings.

### Smoke as Indicator

The question is: *mena yadeinan* — how can one tell when all the redness has ceased? One cannot easily monitor all the individual drips falling from a large piece of meat over a fire. *Mar Zutra in the name of Rav Papa* says: *mishe-ya'aleh timrato* — when the smoke rises. Rashi offers two explanations of this indicator: either the meat itself begins to smoke, signaling that all moisture including blood has been expelled; or alternatively, the embers beneath the meat, which are suppressed from smoking as long as blood drips onto them, begin to smoke again once the dripping stops.

### Rav Ashi's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא תַּתָּאָה מְטָא, עִילָּאָה לָא מְטָא? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא, אֶלָּא מִשְׁדֵּא בֵּיהּ תַּרְתֵּי גַּלְלֵי מִלְחָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to this: But perhaps the underside** of the meat, which is closest to the coals, has been fully **roasted,** but its **upper** part is still **not roasted** and still expels blood at this stage. **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** One who wishes to collect the drippings **has no remedy except** to **place two lumps of salt in it,** i.e., one in the receptacle under the meat and one on top, in the meat itself.

Rav Ashi objects to this criterion: *dilma mitata mata ila lo mata* — perhaps the bottom is fully roasted but the top is not. If one is roasting multiple layers, the smoke indicator might reflect the state of the lower piece near the fire while blood is still flowing from the upper piece that is further from the heat. The smoke rising from the bottom tells us nothing definitive about the top.

### Two Mounds Salt Solution

*Ela amar Rav Ashi: leit leh takanta ela mishdei bah tarti galalei milcha* — the only reliable solution, says Rav Ashi, is to place two mounds of salt in the collection vessel. The principle, striking as it seems to modern sensibilities, is that blood is attracted to the salt and absorbed into it, while the meat juices remain separate and can be collected from around the salt mounds. The blood, being drawn to the salt, is contained within the mounds; what remains is permissible gravy.

### Tosafot Rejects Salt Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשְׁפְּיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Doing this will allow the blood dripping from the meat to stick to the salt on the sides of the vessel, while the fat will float to the top. **And** when emptying the receptacle, he should **tilt it** gently to pour the fat into another vessel without it mixing with the blood.

Tosafot cites the *Bahag* in rejecting the practical applicability of this method. The *Bahag* states explicitly that it must be precisely two mounds: *aval harbeh melach posek koach ha-dam* — if there is more salt than the prescribed amount, it breaks the power of the blood and the mechanism fails, presumably because excess salt disperses the blood throughout the liquid rather than drawing it in and containing it. Too little salt is equally insufficient. Since we are no longer able to determine with confidence the exact measure of two mounds — *ve-ein anu beki'in ba-davar*, we are not expert in this matter — neither more nor less is acceptable, and we therefore cannot rely on this method at all. This is the characteristic *Ashkenazi* formulation: when a precise measure is required and we cannot guarantee it, the entire practice is set aside.

## Blood Extraction Laws (Continued)

### Shmuel's Contradictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כִּכָּר שֶׁחָתַךְ עָלֶיהָ בָּשָׂר אָסוּר לְאׇכְלָהּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאַגַּב דּוּחְקָא דְסַכִּינָא פָּלֵיט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said to Rav Ashi: And did Shmuel** really **say this,** that once the meat's ruddiness has dissipated it is permitted to place a receptacle underneath it? **But didn't Shmuel say: It is prohibited to eat a loaf** of bread **upon which one cut** roasted **meat?** Apparently he holds that meat contains blood even after it has finished roasting. Rav Ashi replied: Shmuel holds that the meat no longer emits blood after its ruddiness has dissipated. And as for his ruling with regard to a loaf of bread, it **is different there, as due to the pressure of the knife** the meat **expels** more blood.

The Gemara now returns to Shmuel's position and confronts an apparent internal contradiction. *U-mi amar Shmuel hachi* — did Shmuel really rule that once the red stops coming out during roasting one may collect the drippings? But surely Shmuel himself was the authority who said that cutting meat on bread produces forbidden juice — implying that even after roasting, blood can still be present in the meat and emerge under the pressure of a knife. How can these two positions coexist? If Shmuel believes that roasted meat may still contain blood that exits under knife pressure, how can he simultaneously permit collecting the post-roasting drippings on the grounds that the red has stopped flowing?

The Gemara resolves this through a critical distinction: *shani hatam de-agav duchka de-sakina palit* — the case of the bread is different, because there it is the mechanical pressure of the knife that forces out blood that was otherwise trapped deep within the meat. The roasting process expels whatever blood is capable of flowing out freely, and once that flow has ceased, the remaining juices are permissible. But there is a separate category of blood that is trapped so deeply within the muscle tissue that it cannot emerge through roasting alone. The forceful compression of the knife at the moment of cutting can expel that residual blood, which roasting never reached. Therefore, Shmuel's two rulings are consistent: the cessation of red flow during roasting indicates that freely flowing blood has stopped, and the remaining drippings may be collected; yet even roasted meat may harbor pockets of blood that will exit only under knife pressure, rendering the resulting juice on the bread forbidden.

This is a characteristically nuanced Talmudic resolution. Shmuel does not hold that roasting fails to extract blood as a general proposition. He holds that roasting extracts blood that is capable of flowing under heat, while knife pressure extracts blood that is not. These are two distinct categories, and each ruling addresses its own category correctly.

## Salting Fish & Birds

### Fish-Bird Salting Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: דָּגִים וְעוֹפוֹת שֶׁמְּלָחָן זֶה עִם זֶה – אֲסוּרִין. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בִּכְלִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְנוּקָּב – אֲפִילּוּ עוֹפוֹת וְעוֹפוֹת נָמֵי אֲסִירִי, אִי בִּכְלִי מְנוּקָּב – אֲפִילּוּ דָּגִים וְעוֹפוֹת נָמֵי שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says:** If **one salted fish and birds together,** the fish **are prohibited** for consumption due to the blood they absorb from the birds. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this ruling? **If** they were placed **in an unperforated vessel,** in which the blood emitted by the birds will pool, then **even** if one salted **a bird and** another **bird** together they should be **prohibited,** as the blood expelled from one bird will be absorbed by the other. And **if** the case involves **a perforated vessel,** out of which the blood can flow, then **even** if **fish** are salted with **birds** the fish should be **permitted.**

The Gemara presents a new case: *dagim ve-ofot she-melachan zeh im zeh, assur* — fish and birds that were salted together are forbidden. The issue is that the blood being extracted from the birds during kashering may transfer into the fish. In general, one may salt multiple pieces of the same type of meat together without concern that blood drawn from one piece will be absorbed into another. The question is why fish and birds are treated differently.

### Tosafot Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בִּכְלִי מְנוּקָּב, וְדָגִים מִשּׁוּם דִּרְפוּ קְרָמַיְיהוּ קָדְמִי וּפָלְטִי, וְעוֹפוֹת קְמִיטִי, בָּתַר דְּנָיְחִי דָּגִים פָּלְיטִי עוֹפוֹת, וַהֲדַר בָּלְעִי מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the ruling is referring **to a perforated vessel. And** the **fish** are prohibited **because their skin is soft,** and therefore when they are salted **they expel** their blood **first, whereas** the skin of **birds** is **hard. After** the **fish finish** expelling their blood, the **birds** continue to **expel** their blood, **and then** the fish **absorb** blood **from them.** If one salts two birds together, they expel their blood simultaneously, and neither will absorb blood from outside while expelling its own.

Tosafot undertakes an extensive analysis of the multiple principles that explain why same-type meats may be kashered together without concern, and then demonstrates why fish constitute an exception.

Several principles are at work when same-type meats are salted together. The first is *aidi d'tarid l'miflat lo bala* — since the meat is occupied with expelling blood, it cannot simultaneously absorb. Because all pieces are actively expelling their own blood under the influence of the salt, none of them is in a state to absorb the blood flowing from the others. A potential weakness in this principle, as Tosafot notes, is that it might not account for the moment when one piece finishes expelling before another. Tosafot responds with a variant formulation: it is sufficient that a piece is busy expelling even its own juices — not just blood — to prevent absorption, and even when a piece finishes expelling its blood, it continues to expel juices for some time, which prevents any incoming blood from taking hold.

A third principle is *dam mishrak sharik* — blood is inherently slippery in quality. It does not adhere to surfaces; it glides off. Even if blood from one piece of meat contacts another, it does not penetrate and adhere, but slides away. This property of blood explains why, even setting aside the question of whether a piece is actively expelling, blood-to-meat contact during salting does not result in actual absorption.

### Vessel Must Have Holes

Before addressing why fish are an exception, the Gemara clarifies the precondition for any of this to work at all: *ei bikli she-eino menukav* — if the salting is done in a vessel without holes, so that all the expelled blood pools at the bottom and the meat sits in it, *afilu ofot ve-ofot nami assuri* — even two birds together are forbidden. All the foregoing leniencies depend on the blood draining away: either through a board set at an angle, or through a vessel with holes in the bottom. If blood is pooling and surrounding the meat, re-absorption is assumed, and same-type meats are equally problematic. The case of *bikli menukav* — a vessel with holes — is therefore the assumed background of any permissible kashering arrangement.

### Fish Absorbency Timing

Fish cannot benefit from the same leniencies as birds salted with birds. The reason is twofold. First, *mishum de-rafu karmaihu* — because the outer surface of fish is very soft and highly absorbent, unlike the surface of bird or animal meat, which has a tighter skin. Second, *kadmi ufalti* — fish complete the expulsion of their own juices before the birds have finished expelling their blood. The birds, *kimiti* — they hold back, meaning their blood comes out more slowly, tightly retained at first. *Batar d'naihu dagim, palti ofot* — only after the fish have gone quiet and finished their own expulsion does the bird blood begin to flow. *Ve-hadar bali mineihu* — and then the fish, no longer occupied with their own expulsion and possessing a highly absorbent outer surface, absorb the bird blood.

It follows that if fish were placed on top and birds on the bottom on a slanted board, the bird blood would drain away from the fish rather than into them, and the problem might be avoided. The Gemara's concern is precisely the scenario where the fish are positioned to receive the outflowing bird blood while they themselves are in an absorbent, passive state.

## Non-Kosher Mixed Salting

### Trefa-Kosher Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מָרִי בַּר רָחֵל אִימְּלַח לֵיהּ בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה בַּהֲדֵי בְּשַׂר טְרֵפָה, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Meat of a slaughtered** animal **was salted for Rav Mari bar Raḥel together with** prohibited **meat of an animal that had a wound that would have caused it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** in a perforated vessel, as meat is generally salted. **He came before Rava** to ask whether the kosher meat was prohibited, as perhaps it would not have absorbed flavor from the non-kosher meat while itself expelling blood.

The Gemara now presents the case of Rav Mari bar Rachel: he had salted pieces of meat together, and one of the pieces turned out to be *trefa* — it was done unwittingly, as when meat is purchased and only afterward is a disqualifying defect discovered in the animal. The kosher pieces had been salted together with the *trefa* piece on the same platform.

### Non-Kosher Juices Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַטְּמֵאִים״ – לֶאֱסוֹר צִירָן, וְרוֹטְבָן, וְקֵיפֶה שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** When the verse states, with regard to forbidden foods: "These are **the unclean** to you" (Leviticus 11:31), the added definite article serves **to prohibit** not only their flesh but also **their juice** emitted by salting, **and their gravy, and their spices** with which they are cooked. Even while the kosher meat is emitting its blood, it still absorbs the juices of the non-kosher meat, which are absorbed more easily than blood.

He brought the question before Rava. *Amar lei Rava: ha-teme'im, le-asor tsiran ve-rotban ve-kippa shelahen* — Rava cited the verse regarding forbidden animals: not only is the meat of a *trefa* forbidden, but its *tsir* — juices — and *rotev* — gravy — and the *kippa* — the residue or goop in the vessel — are equally forbidden. The great *chiddush* here is that the juices of a non-kosher item carry the same legal status as the item itself and are forbidden just as the meat is.

### Blood vs Juice Transfer

This ruling creates a tension with the earlier analysis of same-type salting. If salting two pieces of kosher meat together is permitted because blood does not transfer — either because the meat is busy expelling or because *dam mishrak sharik*, blood is slippery — why should the juices of the *trefa* piece transfer into the kosher piece?

The resolution is implicit in the distinction between blood and juices. The principle of *dam mishrak sharik* applies specifically to blood: blood is slippery by nature and does not adhere to or penetrate surfaces it contacts. Juices — *tsir* and *rotev* — do not share this property. They are stickier and do penetrate. When kosher and *trefa* meat are salted together, it is not the blood of the *trefa* that transfers into the kosher meat — blood would glide off — but the non-blood juices that are expressed along with the blood, which do transfer and are absorbed.

### Taste vs Thing Itself

This leads to a final observation. One might ask: why does the Torah need a specific verse to tell us that the juices of a non-kosher animal are forbidden? If the principle of *ta'am ke-ikar* — taste is equivalent to the substance itself — applies, then presumably any taste of the *trefa* meat absorbed into another food should be forbidden on that basis alone, without need of a special derivation. The answer appears to be that the juices of meat may not carry the full taste profile of the meat itself. If the juice is distinct enough in character from the flesh that *ta'am ke-ikar* would not straightforwardly apply, then a special verse is required to declare the juice halachically equivalent to the meat and equally forbidden. The verse fills the gap that *ta'am ke-ikar* alone might not cover.
