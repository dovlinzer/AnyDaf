# Nedarim 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'ila & Konamot

### Me'ila Paths

Today's *daf* is 36, and we pick up in the middle of 35b. The previous session covered rich and significant ground, in particular the whole issue of *me'ila* by *konamot*: whether the concept of trespass applies when something is forbidden by a vow. *Konam* is especially susceptible to this analysis because it borrows from the language of *korban*, and when the object is forbidden to everyone — not merely to one individual — it resembles consecrated property even more closely. According to at least Rabbi Meir, there is a *me'ila* concept even when the prohibition applies only to particular individuals.

### Me'ila as Gezeilah

A second point from the previous discussion deserves brief appreciation. When we say there is a concept of *me'ila*, there are generally two distinct paths by which a person commits *me'ila*: one is by deriving *hana'ah* — benefit or pleasure — from something that is sanctified, and the other is by appropriating it, taking it for oneself or transferring it to another. The question in the study of *me'ila* is always whether these are two separate paths or whether they ultimately reduce to one. Reb Chaim holds that everything is a form of *gezeilah*: to take benefit from something that is not yours is in essence to steal its use. Alternatively, one could say everything is an expression of *hana'ah*, and that appropriating an object is itself a mode of benefiting from it.

What was particularly striking in the previous session is the application of *me'ila* in the sense of appropriation — not mere benefit — to the realm of *nedarim*. If I declare this *kikar* of bread *konam* to me, and I then give it to a poor person, I have committed *me'ila*, even though I derived no benefit whatsoever from that act. The implication is that a vow, by changing the status of an object, makes it as though it belongs to some entity, such that the very act of taking possession — giving it away, transferring it — constitutes a form of theft. That is a significant *chiddush*, extending the concept of *me'ila* well beyond the ordinary notion of deriving pleasure from forbidden property.

### Defining Indirect Benefit

With that context established, we turn to today's *mishna*, which continues the theme of narrowly defining what counts as *hana'ah*. When I am forbidden from deriving benefit from a particular person, what types of activities performed by that person are considered direct benefit and which are merely indirect? The *mishna* already taught that the person may pay off my creditors — a potentially enormous financial service — and yet that is not considered *hana'ah*. Similarly, that person may return my lost object, since the object was mine to begin with and the person is merely performing a *mitzvah* act.

## Mishna Examples

### Permitted Services

The *mishna* continues with several further examples. *Hatoreim trumato umasrotav leda'ato* — the person may separate my *trumah* and *ma'aser* for me with my knowledge. Who exactly "the person" is and whether they are using their own grain or mine will be addressed in the *gemara*, but the basic question is whether this constitutes *hana'ah*. The *mishna* then states that a *kohen* may bring for me the bird offerings of a *zav*, a *zavah*, a *yoledet*, and the animal offerings of *chataot* and *ashamot*. And further: *umelamdo midrash halachot ve'agadot* — he may also teach me *midrash*, *halachot*, and *aggadot*, even if I am prohibited from receiving benefit from him. However, he may not teach me Scripture — *mikra* — directly; though he may teach *mikra* to my sons and daughters.

### Mitzvos Lo'av L'Henos

The organizing principle here is the concept of *mitzvos lav l'henos nitnu*: *mitzvos* are not considered a form of *hana'ah*. This principle is sometimes framed theologically, with Rashi in several places noting that *mitzvos* are meant to be a burden and a responsibility rather than a benefit — though that framing stands in tension with the well-known statement of Rabbi Chananya ben Akashya that *retza Hakadosh Baruch Hu l'zakos es Yisrael, lifikach hirba lahem Torah u'mitzvos* — God wished to grant merit to Israel and therefore multiplied Torah and *mitzvos* for them. And indeed, whenever one recites *asher kideshanu b'mitzvosav v'tzivanu*, one is presumably affirming that *mitzvos* are an opportunity and a privilege, not merely a burden.

### Torah Knowledge

The *halachic* dimension of the principle, however, is distinct from the theological one. The real point is that *halacha* measures benefit exclusively in physical, real-world terms: financial gain or bodily pleasure. Even the most significant spiritual acquisitions fall outside this definition. Teaching someone *Torah* is a case worth examining carefully, since unlike returning a lost object — which is a service that leaves the recipient exactly where they were — Torah instruction actually imparts something. The recipient now possesses knowledge they did not have before. This is not a mere service; it is a transfer of something substantive. And yet it is not *hana'ah*, because what was transferred is not physical and not monetary.

### Physical vs. Spiritual B…

This same logic applies beyond the context of *mitzvos*. One is forbidden to derive *hana'ah* from a corpse, yet one may use a corpse for anatomical dissection and the study of medicine. The benefit there is enormous — the acquisition of medical skill and knowledge — and yet it is not *halachically* considered *hana'ah*, because it is neither physical pleasure nor monetary gain. The *halacha* operates with a thoroughly this-worldly conception of benefit. Thus when the *mishna* permits returning a lost object, separating *trumos u'maasros*, and bringing *korbanos*, the underlying logic is the same: to the degree that all of these are the performance of a *mitzvah*, the service rendered is not *hana'ah*, because *mitzvah* performance as such is not a legally cognizable form of benefit.

One might raise the question of a case in which the *mitzvah* act also produces a financial saving — for instance, if I am forbidden *hana'ah* from certain candles, and it is Chanukah, and using those candles spares me the cost of purchasing others. In such a case, the benefit I derive is not purely the *mitzvah* itself but a financial saving — and yet the financial saving is only relevant because I needed to perform the *mitzvah*. Whether that constitutes *hana'ah* is a genuine question left open.

## Kohanim's Role

### Agency Question

The *gemara* now poses a fundamental question: *hani kohanei shluchei didan havu o shluchei d'Rachmana havu* — are the *kohanim* in the *Beit HaMikdash* considered to be performing our service, representing *Klal Yisrael*, or are they serving God? This is not merely a philosophical question; it bears directly on our *mishna*. When I bring my *korban*, at what point have I transferred it to God's domain? If the *kohen* represents me and acts on my behalf, then he is doing something for me up until the moment the offering reaches the *Mizbe'ach*. If, on the other hand, the *kohen* is God's representative — part of God's domain in the *Beit HaMikdash*, so to speak — then once I hand over the animal, I have discharged my obligation and the *kohen* is no longer acting on my behalf at all.

The *gemara* notes that elsewhere it concludes that *kohanim* are *shluchei Deshmaya* — God's emissaries — for a compelling structural reason: a person cannot appoint an agent for an act they themselves are not permitted to perform. Since a non-*kohen* cannot perform the *avodah*, the *kohen* cannot logically be seen as that person's agent. He must therefore be understood as God's representative.

### Agency & Benefit

The practical consequence for our topic is as follows. Even if the act of bringing a *korban* is a *mitzvah* and would not in itself constitute *hana'ah*, there is a separate concern: being appointed as someone's direct agent and representative — regardless of the specific task involved — is itself a form of *hana'ah*. If I appoint you to throw a rock into a river, even though throwing a rock into a river is not in itself any benefit, the very act of serving as my appointed *shaliach* and acting on my behalf constitutes *hana'ah*. Therefore, if the *kohen* is my *shliach*, his bringing of a *korban* for me — even though the bringing of a *korban* might otherwise be categorized as a *mitzvah* act — would constitute *hana'ah* by virtue of the agency relationship itself. Hence the *gemara*'s question: how does the *mishna* permit this?

### Mechusrei Kapara Excepti…

The *gemara* proposes an answer from the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* does not say generically that the *kohen* may bring any *korban* for me; it specifically lists the *kinei zavim*, *kinei zavot*, *kinei yoldot*, *chataot*, and *ashamot*. The *gemara* asks: if a *kohen* could bring any *korban* for me without restriction, why would the *mishna* specify only these categories? *Ela mechussar kapara sha'ani* — the case of *mechusrei kapara* is categorically different. These are individuals for whom a *korban* is brought not to atone for a sin in the ordinary sense but to effect a transition from a state of *tumah* to *tahara* — to cleanse and change their ritual status. It is not the person's act of connection to God; it is a ritual procedure performed upon them.

### Rab Yochanan's Rule

This distinction is given a precise formulation by Rabbi Yochanan: *hakol tzrichin da'at chutz mimechusrei kaparah* — all *korbanos* require the awareness and knowledge of the person on whose behalf they are brought, with the sole exception of *mechusrei kapara*. The proof: *sheharei adam meivi korban al banav u-venosav ha-ketanim*, a father can bring a *korban* for his minor children, as derived from the verse *zot toras ha-zav, bein gadol bein katan* — the word *toras* is understood to include both adults and minors. A minor cannot meaningfully appoint an agent or give *da'at*, yet the *korban* is valid. This establishes that the category of *mechusrei kapara* does not require the awareness of the person being cleansed.

## Two Types of Korbanot

### Father's Obligation

The distinction Rabbi Yochanan draws has a deep conceptual basis. The familiar teaching that *korban* derives from *karev* — drawing close to God — captures the nature of most offerings: the *olah*, the *shelamim*, the ordinary *chatat* brought for a sin, all involve the person in a relationship of connection, confession, and atonement. *Chazal* understand the normal process of *teshuva* as involving *viduy*, remorse, and engagement with the act of atonement. In all these cases, the person must be present and aware because the *korban* is an expression of their inner state and their relationship with God.

### Zav & Zavah Proof

From the verse *zot toras ha-zav, bein gadol bein katan*, we learn that for *mechusrei kapara* — a *zav*, a *zavah*, a *yoledet* — a *korban* can be brought even for a minor who cannot give informed consent or appoint a representative. This is a strong proof that *da'at* is not required, since the *halacha* explicitly includes the case of a *katan*.

### Karev vs. Cleansing

The contrast being drawn is between two fundamentally different types of *korban*. One type is an act of *karev* — drawing close — through which the person connects to God in a metaphysical and religious sense. The other type, exemplified by *mechusrei kapara*, functions as an instrument of ritual cleansing and change of status. Just as the sprinkling of the ashes of a *parah adumah* removes *tum'at meis* not through any act of the person but through a ritual applied to them, so too the *korban* of a *mechusrei kapara* — whether for a *zav*, *zavah*, *yoledet*, or *metzora* — does not represent the person's reaching toward God. It is a procedure done to the person, not by the person. Accordingly, it does not require the person's knowledge. They may be completely oblivious to the fact that the *korban* is being brought, and it will still be effective in transitioning them from *tumah* to *tahara*.

This brings the discussion back to the question of *issurei hana'ah*. Even if we accept that *kohanim* are in principle *shluchei didan* — our representatives — in the bringing of *korbanos*, the *mishna* can still permit it in these specific cases because *mechusrei kapara korbanos* by their very nature do not require the person to appoint a representative. The *kohen* is not acting as my *shliach*; the *korban* is simply a ritual being done for my benefit that does not require any act of representation on my part. Therefore, even an individual from whom I am forbidden to receive benefit may arrange for this type of *korban* on my behalf.

It is worth noting that a general *da'at* suffices where *da'at* is required; the person does not need to know precisely which *kohen* is performing the service or at exactly what moment.

## Exception Limits

### Rav Shimi Challenge

Having established the principle, the *gemara* continues to probe its limits. Rav Shimi bar Abba raises a challenge from a *braisa* which states: *im haya Kohen, yizrok alav dam chataso vedam ashamo* — if the person from whom I cannot receive benefit is a *kohen*, he may sprinkle for me the blood of my *chatat* and my *asham*. This sounds like a *kohen* can bring any *korban* for me without restriction, which would suggest that *kohanim* are not my representatives at all and the concern about *hana'ah* through agency never arises.

### Metzora Case

The *gemara* deflects this challenge. The *braisa*'s reference to *chatat* and *asham* need not be understood as referring to all *chataot* and *ashamot*; it may specifically mean *dam chataso shel metzora vedam ashamo shel metzora* — the *chatat* and *asham* brought for the *metzora*, which are themselves cases of *mechusrei kapara*. This is confirmed by the verse *zot tihye toras hametzora, bein gadol bein katan* — here too the word *toras* indicates the inclusion of a minor, reinforcing the same principle. Thus the *braisa* does not establish that a *kohen* can bring any *korban* on my behalf; it remains consistent with the view that the only exception to the *da'at* requirement — and the only case where the agency concern is eliminated — is *mechusrei kapara*.

## Pigul Case

### Pigul Mishna

The *gemara* turns to another attempted proof, this time from the laws of *pigul*. The *mishna* teaches: *hakohanim shepiglu be-mikdash* — when *kohanim* in the *Beit HaMikdash* render a *korban* invalid by forming an intent to consume it beyond its proper time, *b'mezidin chayavin* — if they did so willfully, they are liable for damages, having knowingly destroyed the owner's animal. However, *hashogegin* — if they did so unintentionally, perhaps because they mistook a one-day *korban* for a two-day one — *ella shepigulan pigul*: their *pigul* is nonetheless effective and the *korban* is invalid, even though they are exempt from damages.

### Pigul Logic

The *gemara* asks: why is the *pigul* effective even when unintentional? *Iy amarta bishlama shluchei dish'maya havei* — if the *kohanim* are seen as God's representatives, the answer is straightforward. They are physically performing an act upon the *korban* — slaughtering it, performing the service — and their intent in that moment defines the identity of the act, even if the intent was mistaken. As God's representatives engaged in a physical transaction with the *korban*, their thought can render it *pigul* regardless of whether it was deliberate.

### Pigul Problem

But *ella iy amarta shluchei didan havu* — if the *kohanim* are our representatives, a difficulty arises. When a *shaliach* makes an honest mistake while acting within the scope of the agency, the principal can say: *shlicha shavisach litkuni velo l'avusi* — I appointed you as my agent for my benefit, not to do something that harms me. An agent who inadvertently acts outside the parameters of his appointment — not because he went rogue but because he made an honest error — is arguably not acting within the valid scope of his *shlichus* at that moment. If so, neither the principal nor the agent would have intended for the *pigul* intent to be operative, and the *korban* should not be *pigul*.

### Ran's Solution

The Ran addresses this difficulty. When a *kohen* deliberately forms an intent to eat the *korban* at the wrong time, knowing what he is doing, he has unquestionably gone beyond the scope of his appointment and is acting on his own volition, not as the owner's representative. In that moment he is simply a *kohen* with physical access to the animal, and his unauthorized act damages the owner's property — this is the case of willful *pigul*, and the *kohen* is liable. The problematic case is *beshogeg*: if he is sincerely trying to act within his mandate but makes an honest mistake, can that mistake produce valid *pigul*?

The *gemara* offers a resolution: *amar Yitzchak, shani gabei pigul de'amar kra lo yeichashev lo, mikol makom* — perhaps the verse's additional language "it shall not be considered for him" is itself expanding the effect of *pigul* intent beyond what the ordinary rules of *shlichus* would produce. That is, even if an argument from *shlichus* theory could have led us to conclude that an unintentional *pigul* is invalid, the verse overrides that conclusion and renders it effective regardless. The *gemara* thus leaves the fundamental question unresolved: it remains possible to maintain that *kohanim* are in principle *shluchei didan*, with *pigul beshogeg* being a special case handled by the verse; and the reason the *mishna* permits the *mudar* to receive their service is the narrow, specific exception of *mechusrei kapara*. As noted, other *sugyot* in the *gemara* ultimately conclude that *kohanim* are to be regarded as *shluchei Deshmaya*.

## Husband & Wife

### Chatas Cheilev

The *gemara* now returns to elaborate on Rabbi Yochanan's ruling and explore its limits. The *gemara* proposes: *ela me'ata yavi adam chatat cheilev al chaveiro* — if the principle is that *mechusrei kapara* do not require *da'at*, why not extend this further and say one can bring a *chatat* for a friend without the friend's knowledge? The *chatat cheilev* is the paradigmatic sin offering — a *chatat* brought by one who inadvertently ate forbidden fat (*cheilev*), for which one incurs *karet* and therefore must bring a *korban* if done *beshogeg*.

Rabbi Elazar rules explicitly: *hifrish chatas chelev al chaveiro lo asa klum* — one who separates a *chatat cheilev* on behalf of another person has accomplished nothing. A person must bring their own *korban chatat*; even the act of sanctifying the animal must be done by the person who sinned. This is consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: the only exception to the *da'at* requirement is *mechusrei kapara*.

### Wife's Shoteh State

The *gemara* however points to an apparent tension. We established that a husband can bring *korbanos* for his wife even when she is a *shoteh* — a woman not in possession of her full mental faculties. Since the husband is financially responsible for his wife's *korbanos*, as Rabbi Yehuda teaches — *adam mevi korban ashir al ishto*, a man brings a *korban* at the level of a wealthy person for his wife, calibrated to the husband's finances rather than the wife's — one might think this extends to all her *korbanos*, including a *chatat* for a sin. And if the husband can bring for his wife even when she is a *shoteh*, why can he not bring for any person without their awareness?

It is worth pausing on the particulars of this financial obligation. A wife in that era typically had no independent income, and even if she earned something, the *halakhot* of the *ketuva* entitled the husband to those earnings in exchange for his obligation to provide for her. If she incurred an obligation to bring a *korban oleh veyored* — a sliding-scale offering calibrated to financial means — she would technically be classified as destitute. The *braisa* rules that the scale is determined by the husband's wealth, since he bears the financial responsibility for her *korbanos*. This is the core *chiddush*: *adam mevi korban ashir al ishto*. The *gemara* notes that this obligation is referenced in the language of the *ketuva* or its receipt, though the precise textual source is somewhat opaque.

### Shoteh Timing

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by noting that the case of a *shoteh* wife bringing a *korban* for a sin is in practice impossible. If she ate the forbidden fat while she was a *shoteh*, she bears no halachic liability at all — a *shoteh* does not have the legal standing to transgress. If she ate it while of sound mind and subsequently entered a state of *shoteh*, a different problem arises.

### State Makes Pasul

The ruling of Rabbi Yochanan, cited here via Rabbi Yirmiya citing Rabbi Abahu: *achal chelev vehifrish korban venishtata* — if someone ate forbidden fat, separated a *korban* in response, and then entered a state of *shoteh*, *ve-chazar venishtapa* — and later recovered, *pasul* — the *korban* is invalid. The principle is *ho'il venidcha yidcha*: since the *korban* entered a state during the *shoteh* period during which it could not be brought, it was in a state of being unbringable, and once a *korban* becomes unbringable for any period it is considered *pasul* and cannot subsequently be offered. The person would need to separate a new animal.

The upshot is that one can never practically have a case where a husband brings a sin offering for his *shoteh* wife because of a sin she committed. Therefore, the ruling that a husband brings *korbanos* for his wife — even when she is a *shoteh* — is limited strictly to cases of *mechusrei kapara*, which by their nature do not require *da'at* and are unaffected by the *nidcha* problem. The exception Rabbi Yochanan identified remains narrow and precise: only *mechusrei kapara* operate outside the ordinary *da'at* requirement.

## Pesach Exception

### Pesach Proof

Having established the principle, the *gemara* now addresses an attempted proof that the exception could be extended more broadly. If a father can bring *korbanos* for his minor children — which is how Rabbi Yochanan proved that *mechusrei kapara* does not require *da'at* — why not use that precedent as a model for all *korbanos*, including the *korban Pesach*? One should be able to bring a *korban Pesach* for a friend without the friend's active participation, by analogy to what a father does for his children.

### Pesach Ineffective

Against this, Rabbi Elazar rules explicitly: *hifrish pesach al chaveiro lo asa klum* — one who separates a *korban Pesach* on behalf of another has accomplished nothing. The *korban Pesach* requires active participation; the obligation of *minuyav* — being enrolled in the group — must be fulfilled by each participant. It cannot be arranged for someone by another party without their direct involvement.

### Seh Not D'Oraita

Rabbi Zeira provides the conceptual key: *seh l'veis avos lav d'Oraisa* — the concept of *seh l'veis avot*, the family unit collectively sharing in a single *Pesach* offering, is not a biblical requirement. Biblically, one need not actively bring the *korban* specifically for one's minor children. When a father brings a *korban Pesach* and his minor children eat from it, it is not because he has formally included them in the *shechita* as obligated participants; it is because minors are not themselves obligated in *korban Pesach* and therefore may eat from the father's offering without having been included in the *shechita* on their behalf.

### First Son Case

The proof comes from a *mishna*: *ha'omer l'vanav, harei ani shocheit es hapesach al mi sheya'aleh mikem rishon l'yerushalayim* — a father announces to his sons that he will bring the *korban Pesach* for whichever of them arrives in Jerusalem first. Once the first son enters Jerusalem — his head and the majority of his body crossing the threshold — he acquires his portion. Moreover, *umezakke et achiv immo* — he also conveys a portion to his brothers together with him.

### Why Retroactive Works

The difficulty is obvious: *avbisra kai u-mazki lehu*? The *korban* was already slaughtered — it is already meat — before the other brothers were formally included. If *seh l'veis avot* were biblical, one would need the *shechita* to have been performed with each participant explicitly in mind. How can a retroactive inclusion work once the animal is already slaughtered? The answer is that it works precisely because *seh l'veis avot* is not *d'Oraita* and the minor children do not require the *shechita* to have been performed on their behalf. The father never needed to include them for their sake; they eat from his *Pesach* not because it was brought for them but because they are minors exempt from the obligation and therefore also from the restriction against eating from an offering in which one is not enrolled.

The reason the father made the announcement — to race to Jerusalem — was *kedei le-zarzem be-mitzvot*, to motivate his children to show alacrity in the performance of *mitzvos*. A *braisa* confirms this: *ma'aseh hayah*, in a story that actually occurred, the daughters outpaced the sons, and the daughters were found to be *zerizot* — displaying alacrity — while the sons were *shephelot* — rather less impressive. But nothing about this story suggests the sons were excluded from eating; it merely records that they were the slower ones.

### Minors Eat Pesach

The *gemara*'s conclusion is precise and important. The reason minor children eat from their father's *korban Pesach* is not that the father brings the *korban* on their behalf as obligated participants. They are not obligated. The *shechita* need not be performed with them in mind. They eat because they are minors and the restriction on eating from an offering in which one has not been enrolled does not apply to those who bear no obligation in the first place. Therefore, this case cannot serve as a precedent for bringing a *korban Pesach* on behalf of an adult who is fully obligated and has not been enrolled. The only category for which someone else can truly arrange a *korban* without the person's awareness or active participation — whether that person is a minor, a *shoteh*, or anyone else — remains the specific exception of *mechusrei kapara*, whose *korbanos* function as ritual cleansing rather than as the person's own act of connection and atonement.
