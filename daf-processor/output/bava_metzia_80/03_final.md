# Bava Metzia 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinui - Location Change

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפָּרָה לַחֲרוֹשׁ בָּהָר וְחָרַשׁ בַּבִּקְעָה, אִם נִשְׁבַּר הַקַּנְקַן – פָּטוּר. בַּבִּקְעָה וְחָרַשׁ בָּהָר, אִם נִשְׁבַּר הַקַּנְקַן – חַיָּיב. לָדוּשׁ בְּקִטְנִית וְדָשׁ בִּתְבוּאָה – פָּטוּר, לָדוּשׁ בִּתְבוּאָה וְדָשׁ בְּקִטְנִית – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַקִּטְנִית מַחְלֶקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who rents a cow** and a plow in order **to plow on the mountain but he plowed in the valley, if the plowshare,** the cutting tool on the bottom part of the plow, **breaks,** he is **exempt,** as it was even more likely to break on mountainous terrain. In a case where he rents the cow and a plow to plow **in the valley but he plowed on the mountain, if the plowshare breaks** he is **liable.** If he hired the cow **to thresh legumes but it threshed grain,** and the cow slipped and broke its leg, he is **exempt.** If he hired it **to thresh grain but it threshed legumes he is liable, because legumes are slippery.**

The mishnah opens a discussion of cases involving the rental of an animal together with its equipment and workers, building on the previous mishnah's treatment of a renter who deviated from agreed-upon terms. The new case: *hasocher es haparah lachrosh bahar vecharesh babikah* — one who rents a cow to plow on the mountain but instead plows in the valley.

### Kankan & Workers

Rashi explains that the *kankan* is a peg fastened at the back of the plow that creates the furrow. The system involved two workers provided by the owner of the cow along with the animal and equipment as a package arrangement: one worker leads the animal from the front while the other holds the *kankan* firmly from behind, the forward pull of the cow creating the furrow under pressure from the rear. Both workers are rented out by the owner of the cow as part of a single bundled deal with the renter.

### Easier Place - Exempt

If the renter was supposed to plow on the mountain but instead plowed in the valley, and the *kankan* broke — *im nishbar hakankan patur* — the renter is exempt, because plowing in the valley is presumed to be easier than what was contracted for. By making the job easier than agreed, the renter does not bear liability for the breakage.

### Harder Place - Liable

The converse case follows directly: *ubivikah vecharesh bahar* — if the arrangement was to plow in the valley but the renter took the cow to the mountain instead, which is stonier and more hazardous to equipment, then *im nishbar hakankan chayav* — if the *kankan* breaks, the renter is liable. The deviation to a harder location creates the liability.

### Threshing Cases

The mishnah then addresses threshing cases. *Ladush bekitnis vedash betvuah* — if one hired the cow to thresh legumes but instead used it to thresh grain, the renter is *patur*, presumably because grain is easier to crush. The more interesting case runs in the opposite direction: *ladush betvuah vedash bekitnis*, hired to thresh grain but did it on legumes instead — *chayav*, liable, *mishum dekitnis mechalkes*, because legumes are slippery. The issue here is not hardness but slipperiness: legumes are slicker underfoot for the animal, and if the cow slipped, the renter bears liability for having deviated to a more hazardous condition than was agreed.

## Shared Negligence

### Who Pays?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכָא דְּלָא שַׁנִּי בָּהּ, מַאן מְשַׁלֵּם? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּנָקֵיט פְּרָשָׁא מְשַׁלֵּם. רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי אָמַר: דְּנָקֵיט מָנָא מְשַׁלֵּם. וְהִלְכְתָא: דְּנָקֵיט מָנָא מְשַׁלֵּם. וְאִי דּוּכְתָּא דְּמַחְזְקִי (גּוּנְדְּרֵי) – [גְּרוּנְדֵּי] תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מְשַׁלְּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna discussed the liability of a renter who diverged from the terms of the rental agreement, but it does not teach the *halakha* of liability for broken machinery in a case where the renter did follow the agreement. The Gemara asks: In a case **where** the renter **did not diverge from** their agreement, **who pays? Rav Pappa said:** The one **who holds the goad [*parasha*] pays,** as it can be assumed that he caused the plow to break by not leading it in a straight path. **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said:** The one **who holds the vessel,** the plow itself, **pays. And the *halakha*** is that the one **who holds the vessel pays. And if it was a place** where **rocks are commonly** found **they both pay,** as in this case any small irregularity in the ground where the plow digs a furrow is likely to cause the plow to break.

The Gemara now poses the following question: *heich dulo shani baman meshalem* — where the renter did nothing different from the agreed terms and the *kankan* nevertheless broke, who pays? The renter himself has done nothing wrong; he took the animal and workers as arranged and plowed where he was supposed to. The question, then, is between the owner of the cow and his two workers. The owner contends that his workers were irresponsible: one drove the animal and the other held the peg, and the breaking must have resulted from their shared inattentiveness. Rashi's explanation is that the driver must have allowed the animal to veer slightly off course, and had the worker at the back been paying careful attention, he could have adjusted the *kankan* in time to prevent the break. Each one's carelessness may have contributed.

This raises a genuine difficulty: it is not unlike the problem of co-pilots working at cross-purposes, each partly responsible for a single outcome. The assumption in the Gemara is that there was some degree of shared negligence — not an *ones*, an unavoidable accident — and the question is who among the workers bears primary responsibility. The further question, left largely unexplored by the Gemara, is whether the workers are liable as *mazik* — as tortfeasors — or as *shomrim* — as guardians. The framing of the discussion suggests liability as *mazik*.

### Rav Papa

*Amar Rav Papa, deman deparsha meshalem* — Rav Papa holds that the one in front, the driver who leads the animal, pays. In Rashi's reading, this is the worker who initiated the problem by allowing the animal to drift off its straight course. The deviation at the front is what started the chain of events leading to the break.

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi disagrees: *denakit mana meshalem* — the one who holds the peg at the back pays. His reasoning is that the break occurred at the *kankan* itself, and the worker holding it was in the most immediate position to have prevented the damage. Whatever the driver did wrong, the rear worker had the last opportunity to adjust and failed to do so.

### Halacha Decision

*Ve-hilchata denakit mana meshalem* — the halacha follows Rav Sheshet: the one holding the peg pays. The Gemara does not spell out the full rationale, but the most compelling way to understand it is in terms of proximate negligence. Both workers may have been negligent to some degree, but the most proximate cause of the actual breakage — the one who was in position to have stopped it at the critical moment and did not — is the worker at the back. Even if the driver's deviation was what set things in motion, the rear worker's failure to respond was the most immediate and direct cause of the loss. This is distinct from *grama* — indirect causation — because these workers are actively handling the owner's property; rather, it is a question of which negligence most directly produced the damage.

### Tosafot Challenge

Tosafot raises a sharp question: *ve-tzarich le-iyun amai lo meshalem* — why, in the case where the renter did make a change — say, he took the cow to the mountain instead of the valley, which is a harder and rockier location — don't we still ask one of the workers to pay? After all, the Gemara has just established that the most proximately negligent party pays. Even if the renter violated the terms of the contract by changing the location, the workers are the ones physically handling the equipment. Being more careful — watching for rocks, adjusting pressure — could theoretically have prevented the break even in the harder location. With three potentially negligent parties — the renter who made the *shinui*, the driver who may have veered, and the rear worker who failed to adjust — why does the Gemara focus exclusively on the renter and not ask whether one of the workers should bear liability?

### Tosafot Resolution

Tosafot resolves this with a principle that is both precise and striking. *De-mara de-na masar et ha-parah hu ve-ha-socher et ha-poalim* — the owner of the cow rented out the workers to go with the cow and perform the renter's work. When the workers agreed to be hired out, they understood the agreed-upon terms: *anan lo hitninu imcha ela lachrosh ba-bik'ah* — we contracted only to plow in the valley. *Ve-lo kibalnu shmirat hakankan ba-har ela ba-bik'ah* — our acceptance of responsibility for the *kankan* extended only to the level of care appropriate for valley conditions, not mountain conditions. The workers can therefore say to the owner: our obligation to you was calibrated to the original contract, and we did not accept liability for the degree of care required in harder terrain.

At the same time — and here Tosafot adds something striking — *u-micol makom schirut shelanu lo nifsad*: the workers can still demand their wages. Their reasoning is that when the renter deviated from the agreement, they listened to him and performed the work as he instructed. They fulfilled their role by following instructions and are therefore entitled to be paid for the labor they performed. There is thus an interesting split: the workers are paid for following the renter's instructions, but their liability to the owner is governed by the original terms of the contract, not by the modified ones.

### Rocky Place Case

The Gemara then presents a further case: *v'dukhta d'machzaka gundrei* — a place known to be rocky and full of stones. If both workers went in knowing that the terrain was particularly treacherous, then *tarvay mshalmin* — they both pay. This is surprising. In the earlier case, only the rear worker paid on the grounds of proximate negligence. Why, in the rocky-place case, do both workers pay, if the rear worker is still the most proximate?

### Mutual Watchfulness

Tosafot addresses this directly. Rashi suggests that in the rocky-place case there is doubt (*safek*) about who caused the damage, since in such terrain the driver may bear equal or greater responsibility. But Tosafot rejects this reasoning, pointing out that we do not rule like Sumchus — who would divide liability under conditions of doubt — but like the Rabbanan, who follow the principle of *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'aya*: the burden of proof is on the claimant. If it were merely a doubt, neither worker should pay, since neither can be proven negligent.

Instead, Tosafot argues: *ella nirei le-tosafot d'trvayhu pash'u* — they are both actually negligent. In a place known to be rocky, it is highly likely that the equipment will break easily: *kivun d'machzaka b'gundrei hu nishbar me'od b'kal*. Both workers go in knowing they must exercise extraordinary care. And crucially, *ve-haya l'chol echad litten lev al chaveiro kemo al atzmo u'lehazhiro* — each worker was obligated not only to be maximally careful in his own role but also to watch over his fellow worker and warn him if he saw something going wrong. When the terrain is genuinely dangerous, individual attentiveness is not enough; the situation demands mutual watchfulness. Because the driver failed to watch out for the rear worker as he was obligated to do, he too is negligent — not only for his own actions but for his partner's, having failed to intervene when he should have. The result is shared liability. The principle is powerful: in conditions dangerous enough that collective vigilance is required, a person's negligence extends beyond his own actions to his failure to protect against his co-worker's.

## Defect Disclosure

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמּוֹכֵר פָּרָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: פָּרָה זוֹ נַגְחָנִית הִיא, נַשְׁכָנִית הִיא, בַּעֲטָנִית הִיא, רַבְצָנִית הִיא, וְהָיָה בָּהּ מוּם אֶחָד וּסְנָפוֹ בֵּין הַמּוּמִין – הֲרֵי זֶה מִקַּח טָעוּת. מוּם זֶה וּמוּם אַחֵר – אֵין זֶה מִקַּח טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says,** citing the *Tosefta* (*Bava Batra* 4:6): In a case of **one who sells a cow to another and says to him:** You should know that **this cow** has defects, **it is accustomed to goring, it is accustomed to biting, it is a kicker, it lies down** habitually; **but** in reality **it had** only **one defect and he inserted it among the** list of **defects** that it did not have, **this is a mistaken transaction,** as the buyer saw that it did not have the other defects and therefore did not take seriously any of the defects the seller enumerated, including the one that the cow actually had. But if the seller stated: The animal has **this defect,** i.e., the defect that it in fact has, **and other defects,** without specifying what they were, **this is not a mistaken transaction.**

The Gemara introduces a case from Rabbi Yochanan that at first appears somewhat disconnected from the preceding discussion. *Hamocher para l'chaveiro v'amar lo* — someone sells a cow to his fellow and says: *para zo natchanit hi, nashchanit hi, ba'atnit hi, rabetzanit hi* — this cow is a gorer, a biter, a kicker, and a croucher. He lists four distinct problems. In reality, *vehaya bah mum echad*, only one of those defects was actually present, and *v'sinfo bein hamumim* — he appended the real problem to this list of fictitious ones, concealing it among them.

The seller's intent, as Rashi explains, is to protect himself from future complaints: if he lists every possible problem and the buyer can't see any of them, the buyer assumes the whole list is boilerplate — hollow language designed to foreclose complaints rather than genuine disclosure. The seller is counting on the buyer not taking any of it seriously, while in fact the one real defect is quietly listed among the fabricated ones.

### Mekach Taut

*Harei zeh mekach taut* — this is a mistaken transaction. The halacha is that such a sale constitutes *mekach taut* because the buyer cannot be said to have been genuinely forewarned. The only thing that would prevent *mekach taut* in the case of a serious defect is a real pre-warning that the buyer took seriously and could act upon. A boilerplate list of implausible defects does not constitute such a warning. The buyer looks at the cow, sees no obvious problem, assumes the seller is simply covering himself with standard language, and concludes that the cow is essentially sound. When it turns out the cow has one serious defect that was buried in the list, the buyer can rightly say: you did not warn me in any meaningful sense.

The analogy is instructive. When a seller lists ten problems and the cow visibly has none of them, it is as though the seller said "as is" in the most general and unspecified way imaginable. A general disclaimer of that kind does not put a buyer on notice about a specific, serious defect. The comparison to pharmaceutical advertisements — where a long recitation of side effects may not actually register as meaningful warnings — captures precisely why such a list fails to constitute genuine disclosure.

### Single Defect

The Gemara then draws the critical distinction: *mum zeh umum acher ein zeh mekach taut* — if the seller specifically singled out one defect and mentioned only that one (or that one plus a vague reference to other possible issues), it is not *mekach taut*. When one particular problem is identified clearly and individually, the buyer is put on genuine notice. He should have taken that warning seriously, investigated the specific defect, and made an informed decision. Because he was specifically alerted to the problem, the failure to investigate is on him.

Tosafot adds a further dimension: part of why the boilerplate list fails as pre-warning is that a buyer who checked even one of the listed defects and found it absent could reasonably have concluded that the entire list was fictional. That inference — reasonable in context — is what the seller exploits when he hides the real defect among fake ones. By contrast, when the seller singles out one specific defect, the buyer has no such basis for dismissal and cannot claim he was not genuinely warned.

### Slave Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַמּוֹכֵר שִׁפְחָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: שִׁפְחָה זוֹ שׁוֹטָה הִיא, נִיכְפֵּית הִיא, מְשׁוּעְמֶמֶת הִיא, וְהָיָה בָּהּ מוּם אֶחָד וּסְנָפוֹ בֵּין הַמּוּמִין – הֲרֵי זֶה מִקַּח טָעוּת. מוּם זֶה וּמוּם אַחֵר – אֵין זֶה מִקַּח טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this is also taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Batra* 4:3): With regard to **one who sells a maidservant to another and says to him: This maidservant is an imbecile, she is epileptic, she is crazy [*meshuamemet*]; but** in reality **she had** only **one defect and he inserted it among the** other **defects, this is a mistaken transaction.** But if the seller stated: The maidservant has **this defect,** i.e., the defect that she in fact has, **and other defects,** without specifying what they were, **this is not a mistaken transaction.**

A *baraita* confirms that the same principles apply in the case of selling a female slave. *Tanu rabanan hamocher shifcha lechavero v'amar lo: shifcha zo shota hi, nichpit hi, meshu'amemet hi* — the seller says she is mentally unstable, epileptic, and lethargic or vacant (Rashi and Tosafot offer somewhat different readings of *meshu'amemet*). Again, *vehaya bah mum echad v'snafo bein hamumim* — only one defect was real and it was buried in the list. *Harei zeh mekach taut.* And again: if the seller singled out one specific defect and mentioned others only in passing, *ein zeh mekach taut*, because the buyer was on adequate notice.

### All Defects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָיוּ בָּהּ כׇּל הַמּוּמִין הַלָּלוּ, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָיוּ בָּהּ כׇּל הַמּוּמִין הַלָּלוּ – אֵין זֶה מִקַּח טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** If the animal **had all of these defects, what is** the *halakha* in that case? Can the buyer claim to have thought that the seller was not serious when he mentioned so many problems? **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: We say this** *halakha* **in the name of Rava:** If the animal **had all of these defects, it is not a mistaken transaction,** as he was forthright. The seller is not at fault if the buyer did not believe him.

Rav Acha son of Rava poses a sharp question to Rav Ashi: *hayu vo kol hamumim halalu mahu* — what if the cow actually had all of those defects? The question is real: perhaps even then the buyer doesn't take the list seriously, because who would believe a single animal could have every one of those problems? Surely the list still looks like boilerplate.

### Verification

Rav Mordechai reports in the name of Rava: *hayu vo kol hamumim halalu ein zeh mekach taut* — even in that case, it is not *mekach taut*. The reasoning Tosafot offers is that if every one of the listed defects was genuine, then the buyer could have checked any one of them and verified it firsthand. Having had the opportunity to confirm at least one item on the list, the buyer cannot claim ignorance. The failure to verify is his own. The framework, then, is essentially one of verification: a specific, checkable warning that the buyer chose to ignore does not support a claim of *mekach taut*, even when a long list is involved — provided the defects were real and verifiable.

## Cargo Overage

### Wheat/Barley

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר לְהָבִיא עָלֶיהָ חִטִּין, וְהֵבִיא עָלֶיהָ שְׂעוֹרִין – חַיָּיב. תְּבוּאָה, וְהֵבִיא עָלֶיהָ תֶּבֶן – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַנֶּפַח קָשֶׁה כְּמַשּׂאוֹי. לְהָבִיא לֶתֶךְ חִטִּין, וְהֵבִיא לֶתֶךְ שְׂעוֹרִין – פָּטוּר. וְאִם מוֹסִיף עַל מַשָּׂאוֹ חַיָּיב. וְכַמָּה יוֹסִיף עַל מַשָּׂאוֹ וִיהֵא חַיָּיב? סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: סְאָה לְגָמָל, שְׁלֹשָׁה קַבִּין לַחֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who rents a donkey** in order **to bring wheat on it,** to transport it on its back, **and he brought upon it** an identical weight of **barley,** which is lighter than wheat, and the donkey was injured, he is **liable.** Similarly, if he hired it for transporting **grain, and he brought straw** of the same weight **upon it,** he is **liable, because the** extra **volume is as difficult** for the animal **as the load** itself. If he rented a donkey in order **to bring** on it **a *letekh*,** i.e., a measurement of volume, **of wheat, but he brought a *letekh* of barley,** he is **exempt,** as he brought the same volume of a lighter substance. **And** one **who adds to a load** a greater volume than he stipulated is **liable. And how much** must **he add to the load for him to be liable? Sumakhos says in the name of Rabbi Meir: A *se'a* on a camel** and **three *kav* on a donkey.**

The next mishnah introduces a new set of cases involving deviation from agreed cargo specifications. *Hasocher et hachamor lehavi aleha chitin vehevi aleha seorim* — one who rents a donkey to carry wheat but instead loads it with barley. Barley is lighter than wheat by volume, so if the same weight of barley is loaded, it occupies more space. *Chayav* — the renter is liable. Similarly, *tevuah vehevi aleha teven* — if one said grain but brought straw, which is lighter still — *chayav*. The mishnah states the reason explicitly: *mipnei shehanefach kashe kimasoi* — because increased volume is as problematic as increased weight. The same analysis applies in the reverse direction: if one agreed to bring a volume of barley but substituted grain of the same volume, the weight would be greater and the liability equally clear. The fundamental point is that there are two distinct ways to violate the terms of a cargo agreement — by exceeding the agreed weight, or by exceeding the implied volume — and both create liability.

### Volume = Weight

The principle *shehanefach kashe kimasoi* requires clarification. The question is not merely that volume can be a problem, but whether it is treated as a perfectly equivalent measure to weight or whether it operates differently. The next portion of the mishnah sets up the measurement framework. *Lehavi letach chitim* — if the agreement was to carry a *letach* of wheat, which is fifteen *se'ah*, equivalent to ninety *kav* — and the renter brought instead a *letach* of barley, the renter is *patur*, exempt. Same volume, less weight — no violation. But *ve-im hosif al masao chayav* — if he added weight, he is liable.

To situate the units: a *se'ah* is approximately one and a half liters. A *kav* is one-sixth of a *se'ah*. A *letach* is fifteen *se'ah*, or ninety *kav*. A *kor* is two *letach*, or thirty *se'ah*.

### One-Thirtieth Rule

The mishnah then addresses how much overage is required to trigger liability: *v'chama yosif al masao v'yiyeh chayav* — how much must one add before becoming liable? Sumchus, citing Rabbi Meir, gives the standard: one *se'ah* for a camel, three *kav* for a donkey. Since the *letach* case involves a donkey carrying ninety *kav*, the permissible overage is three *kav* — precisely one-thirtieth of the agreed load. The Gemara generalizes: whatever the acceptable overage for a given carrier, that overage represents one-thirtieth of the normal tolerable load for that carrier. If the overage for a camel is one *se'ah* — six *kav* — then a camel's standard load is one hundred and eighty *kav*. The one-thirtieth rule is thus the universal standard across carriers.

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: ״קָשֶׁה כְּמַשּׂאוֹי״ תְּנַן. רָבָא אָמַר: ״קָשֶׁה לְמַשּׂאוֹי״ תְּנַן. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: ״קָשֶׁה כְּמַשּׂאוֹי״ תְּנַן, נִפְחָא כִּי תִקְלָא, וְאִי מוֹסִיף שְׁלֹשָׁה קַבִּין – חַיָּיב. רָבָא אָמַר: ״קָשֶׁה לְמַשּׂאוֹי״ תְּנַן, תִּקְלָא כִּי תִקְלָא וְנִפְחָא הָוֵי תּוֹסֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed about the precise text of this mishna. **Abaye said** that **we learned** in the mishna: **Difficult as the load. Rava said** that **we learned: Difficult for the load.** The Gemara explains: **Abaye said** that **we learned: Difficult as the load,** with the meaning that **volume is like weight, and** therefore when the volume of the two substances is equal, **if one adds three *kav* he is liable. Rava said** that **we learned: Difficult for the load,** with the meaning that if one **weight is like** the other **weight,** then the difference in **volume is** considered **an addition.** In other words, one is liable if the weight of the barley is the same as that of the wheat, in which case the additional volume is considered to cause damage. Rava holds that if the volume of the two is equal, one is not liable for the additional three *kav* of weight.

The Gemara records a dispute between Abaye and Rava on the precise application of the volume-weight equivalence. *Abaye amar kasha k'masao tnan* — Abaye reads the mishnah's principle as establishing a full equivalence: increased volume is measured exactly as increased weight. Volume is treated as just as real a burden as weight, and the same one-thirtieth overage rule applies regardless of whether the excess is measured in weight or volume. *V'Rava amar kasha l'masao tnan* — Rava reads the mishnah differently: it says only that increased volume makes the burden more difficult, not that volume is measured the same way as weight.

The practical difference is significant. For Rava, as long as the total weight of the cargo is below the agreed limit, there is no liability even if the volume exceeds what the agreement implied. The volume problem only becomes actionable once the weight has reached the agreed threshold — at which point the increased volume on top of the equivalent weight constitutes a violation. For Abaye, volume is independently measured: even if the weight is less than the agreed amount, exceeding the volume by one-thirtieth creates liability, because volume is treated as its own form of burden equivalent to weight.

### When Volume Violates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לְהָבִיא לֶתֶךְ חִטִּין, וְהֵבִיא לֶתֶךְ שְׂעוֹרִין – פָּטוּר. וְאִם הוֹסִיף עַל מַשָּׂאוֹ – חַיָּיב. מַאי לָאו שְׁלֹשֶׁת קַבִּין?! לֹא, סְאָה. וְהָא עֲלַהּ קָתָנֵי: וְכַמָּה יוֹסִיף עַל מַשָּׂאוֹ וִיהֵא חַיָּיב? סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: סְאָה לְגָמָל, שְׁלֹשָׁה קַבִּין לַחֲמוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to cite a proof for one of these opinions. **We learned** in the mishna: If one hired a donkey **to bring a *letekh* of wheat, but he brought a *letekh* of barley,** he is **exempt. And** one **who adds to a load** a greater volume than he stipulated is **liable. What, is it not** correct that this means he did not bring exactly a *letekh* of barley, but added **three *kav*,** which would support the opinion of Abaye? The Gemara refutes this interpretation: **No,** he added a whole ***se'a*.** The Gemara asks: **But** the continuation of the mishna **was taught concerning this** case: **How much** must **he add to the load for him to be liable? Sumakhos says in the name of Rabbi Meir: A *se'a* on a camel** and **three *kav* on a donkey.**

The Gemara tests both positions against the mishnah. The mishnah says: brought a *letach* of wheat but brought a *letach* of barley — *patur*; if he added to the load — *chayav*. The immediate reading is that once you have the same number of bags, the standard three-*kav* overage rule applies. This sounds like Abaye: volume is treated the same as weight, and the ordinary one-thirtieth rule governs. But the Gemara says: *lo, se'ah* — no, three *kav* would not suffice; you would need a full *se'ah* of overage. The implication is that only when the additional volume brings the weight up to the equivalent of the original wheat load — which happens at the six-*kav* mark in the barley-wheat case — does the violation occur. That sounds like Rava.

### Lighter Goods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: הֵיכָא דְּלָא שַׁנִּי חִטִּין וְהֵבִיא חִטִּין, שְׂעוֹרִין וְהֵבִיא שְׂעוֹרִין, כַּמָּה יוֹסִיף עַל מַשָּׂאוֹ וִיהֵא חַיָּיב? סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: סְאָה לְגָמָל שְׁלֹשָׁה קַבִּין לַחֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this** is what the last clause of the mishna **is saying:** In a case **where** the renter **did not diverge from** their agreement, e.g., they stipulated that he would bring **wheat and he brought wheat,** or **barley and he brought barley, how much** must **he add to the load for him to be liable? Sumakhos says in the name of Rabbi Meir: A *se'a* on a camel** and **three *kav* on a donkey.**

The Gemara further tests the matter with the case: *lehavi letach chitim veheivi sheish esreih se'orim* — one agreed to bring a *letach* of wheat (fifteen *se'ah*) and instead brought sixteen *se'ah* of barley. He is liable. But this is a one-fifteenth increment, not a one-thirtieth increment as the standard rule would require. This leniency — requiring a larger overage before triggering liability — suggests that when the cargo is lighter, the renter is given more latitude, consistent with Rava's position that volume alone, short of reaching the weight threshold, does not immediately create a violation. *Ha shelosha kavin patur* — had the overage been only three *kav*, he would be exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לְהָבִיא לֶתֶךְ חִטִּין וְהֵבִיא שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שְׂעוֹרִים – חַיָּיב. הָא שְׁלֹשֶׁת קַבִּין – פָּטוּר! תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַבָּיֵי: בִּמְחִיקָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If one hired a donkey **to bring a *letekh*,** i.e., fifteen *se'a*, **of wheat, but he brought** **sixteen** *se'a* of **barley,** he is **liable.** This indicates that if he added only **three *kav*,** i.e., half a *se'a*, he is **exempt. Abaye interpreted** the *baraita* as referring **to smoothed-over** barley, a precisely measured load, where the volume of the barley was not measured with heaped measuring utensils, but leveled flat. Consequently, it is roughly a *se'a* less than the usual amount.

Abaye responds to this challenge with the word *bimechiktsa*. There is a dispute among commentators about what this means: some read it as referring to an exceptionally light variety of barley, in which case Abaye is allowing that when the goods are particularly light he does not apply the full volume-equals-weight equivalence. Others read *bimechiktsa* as a measurement term relating to how precisely the load was measured — a generous measure versus a flush, level measure — which would account for the apparent discrepancy in the overage numbers without conceding the principle. The Gemara's *perush* of Rashi on this word is noted as particularly interesting, though the full analysis is not pursued. The bottom line of the dispute is clear: Abaye treats volume as fully equivalent to weight for purposes of measuring acceptable overage, while Rava holds that volume is only actionable once the weight threshold has been reached.

## Overage by Carrier

### Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קַב לְכַתָּף, אַדְרִיב לַעֲרֵיבָה. כּוֹר לִסְפִינָה, שְׁלֹשֶׁת כּוֹרִים לְבוּרְנִי גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: A *kav*** is too large an addition **for a porter,** and therefore if the porter is injured by the additional weight, the owner must pay him. **An *adriv*,** half a *kor*, is too large an addition **for a small boat [*areiva*]; a *kor*** is too large an addition **for** a regular **boat; three *kor*** is too large an addition **for a large ship [*burnei*].**

A *baraita* — *tanu rabbanan* — sets out the acceptable overage standards across different types of carriers. One *kav* for a human porter (*katef*), three *kav* for a donkey, one *se'ah* for a camel, one *letach* overage for a small raft or boat (*adriv* — which Rashi explains is another term for *letach*), one *kor* for a medium-sized boat (*sfinah*), and three *korim* for a large ship (*vurni gedola*). In each case, the acceptable overage represents one-thirtieth of the normal carrying capacity for that carrier. Thus: a human porter's standard load is thirty *kav*, a donkey's is ninety *kav*, a camel's is one hundred and eighty *kav*, a standard boat's capacity is thirty *kor*.

### Porter's Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: קַב לְכַתָּף. אִם אִיתָא דְּלָא מָצֵי בֵּיהּ בַּר דַּעַת הוּא, לִשְׁדְּיֵהּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּשֶׁחֲבָטוֹ לְאַלְתַּר. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁלֹּא חֲבָטוֹ לְאַלְתַּר, לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לְאַגְרָא יַתִּירָא. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הוּא סָבוּר חוּלְשָׁא הוּא דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: A *kav*** is too large an addition **for a porter.** The Gemara asks: **If it is so, that he cannot withstand this** load, the porter **is a sensible person; let him throw it off** and avoid injury. **Abaye said:** This is referring **to** a case **where** the load **knocked him down immediately,** before he could remove it from his back. **Rava said: Even if you say** that it is referring to a case **where it did not knock him down immediately** the *baraita* is **not** difficult, as **it is necessary only** with regard **to the extra pay** that he can demand for this addition. **Rav Ashi said:** Even if the porter is a sensible man, perhaps **he thought it was** a momentary **weakness that seized him** and did not realize that the load itself was excessive.

The Gemara raises a sharp question regarding the human porter specifically. When the employer places extra weight on the porter's back — beyond the agreed amount — why can't the porter simply refuse or remove it? Unlike a donkey, a person is an intelligent agent. If he allowed the overage to be placed on him and then suffered injury, one might argue he implicitly accepted the extra load. *Bar da'atu hu, u-shadiye* — he is a rational being; he could have thrown it off. How can he now complain that the overloading caused him harm?

Abaye responds: *beshachavtu l'alter* — if the porter collapsed immediately upon the load being placed on him, there was no opportunity to perceive and reject the overage. The injury was instantaneous, which demonstrates that the load was objectively beyond what he could tolerate and that there was no moment of implicit acceptance.

Rava extends the analysis further: *afilu teima v'shelo chavtu l'alter* — even if the injury did not occur immediately, the argument is not that the porter loses his claim entirely. Rather: *lo tzricha ela l'agra yeseira* — what is established by the implicit acceptance of the extra load is not that the employer is absolved of responsibility for harm, but that the porter is owed extra pay. Just as the porter implicitly agreed to carry the excess, the employer implicitly agreed to pay for it. The two implicit agreements mirror each other.

Rav Ashi offers a third position: *hu savar kusha hu denakit leh* — the porter felt that the load was heavy but did not know that the heaviness was because the employer had exceeded the agreed amount. He assumed he was simply not feeling his strongest that day. Because he did not realize the weight was beyond what he had contracted for, he never actually accepted the overage, and there was no implicit agreement. The employer is fully responsible. This raises the interesting empirical question of whether a one-thirtieth increment in weight is actually perceptible to the person bearing it — and the Gemara leaves this as a genuine ambiguity.

### Boats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוֹר לִסְפִינָה, שְׁלֹשֶׁת כּוֹרִין לְבוּרְנִי גְּדוֹלָה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סְתָם סְפִינוֹת בַּת תְּלָתִין כּוֹרִין. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* teaches: **A *kor*** is too large an addition **for** a regular **boat; three *kor*** is too large an addition **for a large ship. Rav Pappa said: Learn from here** that **unspecified boats** can **bear thirty *kor*,** i.e., this is the volume of a ship's cargo. The reason for this claim is that in all these cases the addition that causes damage is one-thirtieth of the normal load. The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** resulting from this observation? The Gemara answers: The difference is with regard to the *halakhot* **of buying and selling,** i.e., one who purchases a boat of unspecified dimensions should know that this is its expected capacity.

The one-thirtieth rule for boats is confirmed by Rav Pappa's inference: since one *kor* of overage is the acceptable maximum for a standard boat, and the overage represents one-thirtieth of capacity, a standard boat holds thirty *kor*. This has practical commercial relevance for determining what a buyer of a boat can expect as the default carrying capacity.

## Craftsmen Status

### Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הָאוּמָּנִין – שׁוֹמְרֵי שָׂכָר הֵן. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁאָמְרוּ: טוֹל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ וְהָבֵא מָעוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. שְׁמוֹר לִי וְאֶשְׁמוֹר לָךְ – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. שְׁמוֹר לִי, וְאָמַר לוֹ: הַנַּח לְפָנַי – שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. הִלְוָהוּ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הִלְוָהוּ מָעוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, הִלְוָהוּ פֵּירוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: רַשַּׁאי אָדָם לְהַשְׂכִּיר מַשְׁכּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עָנִי לִהְיוֹת פּוֹסֵק וְהוֹלֵךְ עָלָיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All artisans** and laborers who take raw materials to their homes **are** considered **paid bailees** for those items until they return them to the owner. **And** with regard to **all those who said** to the owner: I finished the work, and therefore **take** what is **yours,** i.e., this item, **and bring money** in its stead, from that point on each of them is considered **an unpaid bailee.** If one person says to another: **Safeguard** my property **for me and I will safeguard** your property **for you,** each of them is a **paid bailee,** as each receives the services of the other as payment for his safeguarding. If one says: **Safeguard for me, and** the other **says to him: Place it before me,** the second individual is **an unpaid bailee.** **One who lent to** another based **on collateral** is **a paid bailee** for the collateral. **Rabbi Yehuda says: One who lent** another **money** is **an unpaid bailee** for the collateral, whereas **one who lent** another **produce** is **a paid bailee. Abba Shaul says:** It is **permitted** for **a person to rent out a poor person's collateral** that was given to him for a loan, **so that by setting** a rental price **for it** he will thereby **progressively** reduce the debt, i.e., the lender will subtract the rental money he receives from the amount owed by the borrower, **because this is** considered **like returning a lost item.** The borrower profits from this arrangement, whereas if the lender does not use the collateral in this manner it provides benefit to no one.

The mishnah opens with the ruling: *kol umanin shomer sachar hen* — all craftsmen are categorized as *shomer sachar*, a paid bailee. If one gives a craftsman wood to build a box, and while the wood is in the craftsman's possession it is stolen or lost through circumstances that would exempt a *shomer chinam* but not a *shomer sachar*, the craftsman is liable. The question the Gemara will probe is: why? The craftsman is receiving money, but that money is payment for his labor — an equal exchange. What is the additional benefit — the *sachar* — that elevates him to the status of *shomer sachar* rather than *shomer chinam*?

The mishnah adds several related cases. *Vekulan she'amru tol et shelcha vehavei maot shomer chinam* — but if the craftsman tells the owner that the work is done, come take it and bring payment, then the craftsman becomes a *shomer chinam*. However, the Gemara will clarify: if the craftsman says instead "pay me first and then come take it," he is still holding the item in his capacity as an *uman* and retains the status of *shomer sachar*. Only once he has fully relinquished his leverage — by telling the owner to take the item before payment — does he drop to *shomer chinam*.

*Shemor li ve'eshmor lecha shomer sachar* — if two people agree to watch each other's belongings in turn, each is a *shomer sachar* for the other's property during his watch. Though one might characterize this as mutual favors making each a *shomer chinam*, the exchange of watchfulness is treated as a form of payment: each person is compensating the other with *shmirah*, and the reciprocal arrangement creates the status of *shomer sachar* for both.

*Shemor li ve'amar lo hanach lefanai shomer chinam* — if one asks another to watch something and the other says only "put it down," without explicitly accepting the role of watchman, the latter is a *shomer chinam*. The *chiddush* is that even a response as passive as "put it down" creates some degree of *shmirah* obligation, though only at the level of *shomer chinam*.

*Hamalveh al hamashkon shomer sachar* — one who lends money and holds collateral is a *shomer sachar* on the collateral. The nature of the *sachar* here will become a question. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees on similar cases: *hamalveh maot shomer chinam* — a lender of money who holds collateral is a *shomer chinam*, not *shomer sachar*. But *hamalveh peirot shomer sachar* — a lender of fruit is a *shomer sachar*. The reasoning is that when one lends fresh fruit, the borrower actually does the lender a favor by taking it before it spoils; the lender benefits from the transaction in a way that goes beyond the equal exchange, and this incidental benefit constitutes the *sachar* that elevates the obligation.

Abba Shaul adds a related ruling: *rashai adam lehaskir mashkono shel ani* — one who holds collateral from a poor borrower who is unlikely ever to repay the loan may rent out the collateral and apply the proceeds against the loan debt, effectively repaying the borrower's obligation on his behalf, since this is *kemo meshiv aveidah* — like returning a lost object. This grants the lender significant latitude to use another's property, framed as an act of benefit to the debtor.

### Initial Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? דְּתַנְיָא: שׂוֹכֵר כֵּיצַד מְשַׁלֵּם? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a renter,** whose legal status is not stated explicitly in the Torah, **how does he pay** in the event that a rented article is lost or stolen? **Rabbi Meir says:** He pays **like an unpaid bailee,** i.e., only in cases where the loss of the item was due to his negligence. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** He pays **like a paid bailee,** i.e., even in cases where the loss of the item was not due to his negligence. Skilled laborers are similar to renters, as they take possession of the item to earn a profit from it, and the mishna teaches that skilled laborers are like paid bailees. Consequently, the ruling of the mishna is apparently not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

The Gemara asks: *leima matnitin dela kerabbi Meir* — does the mishnah's ruling that craftsmen are *shomer sachar* necessarily represent a position in tension with Rabbi Meir? The underlying question is whether the category of *shomer sachar* requires a benefit beyond the equal exchange of money for labor.

### Baraita Dispute

A *baraita* records the dispute directly in the context of a renter (*socher*): *socher keitzad meshalem, Rabbi Meir omer keshomer chinam, Rabbi Yehuda omer keshomer sachar*. Rabbi Meir holds that a renter is a *shomer chinam* on the rented object; Rabbi Yehuda holds he is a *shomer sachar*. The Gemara assumes that a craftsman and a renter are analogous in the relevant respects — money changes hands in exchange for use or labor — and therefore asks whether the mishnah's ruling that craftsmen are *shomer sachar* aligns with Rabbi Yehuda but not Rabbi Meir.

### Employment Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, בְּהַהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא שָׁבֵיק כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא וְאָגַיר לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ – הָוֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. אִי הָכִי, שׂוֹכֵר נָמֵי בְּהַהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא שָׁבֵיק כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא וּמוֹגַר לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ – הָוֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim. **You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** and the reason skilled laborers are considered like paid bailees is that **through that benefit** that the worker receives from the fact **that** the owner of the item **leaves** aside **everyone else and hires him, he becomes a paid bailee over** the item. The Gemara challenges this reasoning: **If so,** with regard to **a renter as well,** it can be said that **through that benefit** he receives from the fact **that** the owner **leaves** aside **everyone else and rents to him, he** should **become a paid bailee over** the item.

The Gemara proposes that even according to Rabbi Meir, a craftsman might be *shomer sachar* through a distinct benefit not present in a simple rental. *Behahi hana'ah dekashebik kulei alma ve'agar lei ledidi* — the benefit is that out of all the potential employers available, the owner chose specifically this craftsman for the job, giving him employment. The craftsman receives not merely payment for labor but the value of being selected for work in an economy where such work is not guaranteed. This employment-as-benefit is what makes him a *shomer sachar*. The Gemara then asks whether the same logic would apply to a renter: couldn't we say the owner gave the renter a similar benefit by renting to him specifically rather than to others? *Socher nami, behahi hana'ah dekashebik kulei alma umogar lei ledidi* — and therefore the renter should also be *shomer sachar*? This would eliminate the distinction between craftsman and renter and would make Rabbi Meir's position incoherent. The Gemara leaves this version of the answer in difficulty.

### Extra Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, בְּהַהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ טְפֵי פּוּרְתָּא – הָוֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** in light of this refutation the Gemara suggests a different reason that **you** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir: Through that benefit that** the skilled laborer receives from the fact **that** the owner **gives him a little more** money, **he becomes a paid bailee.** Since it is impossible to calculate the precise sum to which a skilled laborer is entitled, it is assumed that he is slightly overpaid.

The Gemara tries a second explanation: *behahi hana'ah deka'yahiv lei tefi purta* — the craftsman receives a little extra beyond the precise fair-market value of his labor, since in practice people do not measure compensation with perfect precision and tend to round generously. This small surplus constitutes the *sachar* that makes him *shomer sachar*. But the same objection returns: *socher nami, mi lo askinan dekamashvi lei tefi purta* — perhaps the owner also gave the renter a slight discount on the rental rate, in which case there would be the same extra benefit and the renter would equally be *shomer sachar*, collapsing the distinction. This answer also fails to survive scrutiny.

### Leverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׂוֹכֵר נָמֵי! מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּקָא מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ טְפֵי פּוּרְתָּא. אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, בְּהַהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּתָפֵישׂ לֵיהּ אַאַגְרֵיהּ, דְּלָא בָּעֵי לְמֵיעַל וּלְמִיפַּק אַזּוּזֵי – הָוֵי עֲלֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **a renter as well, are we not dealing** even **with a case where** the owner **gives him a little more value** for his money, and yet Rabbi Meir claims that he is considered like an unpaid bailee? **Rather, you** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** for a different reason: **Through that benefit** that the skilled laborer receives from the fact **that he holds** onto the item **so that he is not required to go in and go out for** his **money, he becomes a paid bailee over** the item.

The Gemara settles on a third and final explanation: *behahi hana'ah detafis lei a'agra* — the craftsman's *sachar* is the benefit of holding the completed item as leverage until he receives payment. The craftsman has given his labor and is owed money. The owner has paid nothing yet. The craftsman's ability to retain the box until payment is a genuine benefit — *dela ba'ei lemizlei ulemifak azuzei*, he does not have to chase after the owner for his money — and this benefit, which exists because the craftsman has possession of the owner's property, is what makes him a *shomer sachar* on that property. This is qualitatively different from the rental case: when someone rents out a donkey, the owner retains no similar leverage over the renter, and the renter holds no comparable advantage that would benefit the owner.

### Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, כִּדְמַחְלֵיף רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ וְתָנֵי: שׂוֹכֵר כֵּיצַד מְשַׁלֵּם? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that the mishna can be explained in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir without these explanations, **as Rabba bar Avuh reversed** the opinions **and teaches** that the *baraita* says: With regard to **a renter, how does he pay? Rabbi Meir says: Like a paid bailee; Rabbi Yehuda says: Like an unpaid bailee.**

Having reached this conclusion, the Gemara then notes a fundamental uncertainty: *tanya ipcha Rabba bar bar Chana* — Rabba bar bar Chana reports that the *baraita* is in fact reversed. According to his version, it is Rabbi Meir who holds that a renter is *shomer sachar* and Rabbi Yehuda who holds he is *shomer chinam* — exactly the opposite of what was stated earlier. Since there is a genuine textual dispute about which sage holds which position regarding a renter, we cannot determine with confidence whether the mishnah's ruling on craftsmen aligns with Rabbi Meir, with Rabbi Yehuda, or with neither — and the question *leima matnitin dela kerabbi Meir* is left unresolved. What does emerge clearly, however, is the Gemara's final and settled reason for the craftsman's status as *shomer sachar*: the leverage afforded by holding the owner's property against payment is a genuine, distinct benefit — one that the Gemara finds uniquely persuasive, and one that does not transfer to the rental context in any obvious way.
