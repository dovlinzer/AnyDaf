# Beitzah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Machshirei Ochel Nefesh

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

The daf begins by revisiting the discussion that concluded on 28b regarding the fundamental question of *machshirei ochel nefesh* — preparatory work performed in advance of food preparation on *Yom Tov*. Rabbi Yehuda's position, as distinct from Rav Yehuda the Amora, is that one may perform not only acts directly involved in food preparation — such as lighting a fire or cooking — but even acts that are one stage removed from that process, such as sharpening knives or repairing an oven. Rabbi Yehuda permits these preparatory acts provided they satisfy the criterion of *ee efshar la'asoso me'od yom*: it was not possible to perform them the day before. This is a striking principle, as one normally expects such a limiting condition to apply on a rabbinic level, yet here it appears to operate on a biblical one. If time or circumstances genuinely prevented the act from being done on *erev Yom Tov*, the Torah itself permits it on *Yom Tov*; if it could have been done beforehand, it is biblically forbidden.

The upshot of the *Gemara* is that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda: *machshirei ochel nefesh* that could not have been attended to the day before are permitted. The *Gemara* does hedge with the principle of *halacha ve'ein morin ken* — the ruling exists but one may prefer not to publicize it broadly, lest people come to permit such acts even when they could have been performed beforehand.

### Ee Efshar Principle

The *ee efshar la'asoso me'od yom* criterion thus serves as the biblical threshold for permitting preparatory work on *Yom Tov*. What could not have been addressed on *erev Yom Tov* is permitted; what could have been addressed is forbidden at the Torah level. This makes Rabbi Yehuda's position both expansive and carefully bounded, and it is precisely that boundary which the subsequent analysis explores.

### Ran's Distinction

There is, however, an important qualification that emerges from the *Rishonim*. While Rashi explains that fixing a knife that has become so dull as to be non-functional — as opposed to merely sharpening a blade that has lost some of its edge — is problematic because of excessive *tircha* or *uvda de'chol*, the Ran and other *Rishonim* frame the limitation differently and more precisely. Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, who permits *machshirei ochel nefesh*, one may not engage in *asiyas kli* — creating a vessel entirely from scratch. One cannot say, for instance, that lacking any knives, one will melt metal and pour it into a mold to produce a knife on *Yom Tov*. One may repair something that has become dulled, but one may not manufacture something that has crossed the threshold into the category of being made anew.

The Ran grounds this limitation in the same principle that explains why one may not go out to the field, harvest wheat, grind it, and make flour on *Yom Tov* — or why hunting and catching fish are problematic. Acts that are not situated within the immediate context of kitchen-based cooking, that belong instead to large-scale, long-term, factory-style production, fall outside the scope of *ochel nefesh* as the Torah envisions it. One may grind pepper — a kitchen act — but not grind wheat into flour, which is an industrial process. Similarly, one may sharpen a dulled knife, but taking something that has ceased to function as a knife and reconstituting it from that state is akin to factory production and is therefore not permitted, even according to Rabbi Yehuda.

The cases in the *Gemara* where *shinuyim* are required — using something other than a whetstone — and the cases where the knife is completely non-functional, are precisely the cases that approach the category of *asiyas kli* rather than mere repair.

### Bottle Caps Example

A practical application of this distinction concerns the question of opening bottle caps on *Yom Tov*. There is an opinion that when one first opens a bottle and breaks off the plastic ring at the base of the cap, one has transformed the cap into a *kli* — it was previously only capable of keeping the bottle sealed, but after the ring is broken it can function as a removable closure used on any bottle. On *Shabbos*, there are those who are *machmir* on this basis.

On *Yom Tov*, however, the question takes a different form. Even if one accepts that opening a bottle technically constitutes *asiyas kli*, it is an *asiyas kli* that occurs entirely within the immediate context of accessing food or drink — it is not remotely analogous to the Ran's example of going out to harvest wheat or working in a factory. The Ran's concern is with acts that are structurally and contextually removed from the kitchen and the act of eating; opening a bottle cap is squarely within that immediate context. Furthermore, the *ee efshar* consideration applies: opening a carbonated bottle on *erev Yom Tov* causes the contents to lose their fizz, making it genuinely impossible to prepare it beforehand in the same condition. Thus, even one who is *machmir* on bottle caps on *Shabbos* has good grounds to open them on *Yom Tov*.

## Spit as Muktzeh

### Disgusting Spit

The *Gemara* now returns to a *muktzeh* discussion, which is the natural conclusion of the foregoing section. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: a spit upon which meat was roasted on *Yom Tov* — once the meat has been removed — becomes disgusting and may no longer be handled on *Yom Tov*, since it has taken on the status of *muktzeh machmas miyus*.

Rav Adda bar Abba said in the name of Rav Malkio: what one may do is *shomto* — push or drag it off to a corner. Rashi explains that *shomto* implies not picking it up directly with one's hands but rather kicking or nudging it to the side, although that detail is not entirely explicit in the *Gemara* itself.

### Ravina's Allowance

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi said in the name of Rav Huna: even this much — pushing it to a corner — applies only if there remains a *kezayis* worth of meat still on the spit, so that it has not yet become entirely *muktzeh* on account of the food still adhering to it.

Ravina offers a different view: even if no meat remains on the spit at all, one is permitted to move it. The reason is derived from the analogy of a dangerous object lying in a *reshus ha'rabim* — a sharp or rusty object posing a hazard to passersby may be removed despite its *muktzeh* status. Here too, a sharp metal spit may be moved even in a fully disgusting state because of the potential hazard it poses. One could alternatively have invoked the category of *graf shel re'ei* — objects so repulsive that requiring one to leave them in place would be an unreasonable imposition on the sanctity of the day — though perhaps the spit, however visually unpleasant, does not rise to that level of active offense.

### Malkio vs Malkia

The *Gemara* takes the opportunity to clarify a persistent source of confusion: the distinction between Rav Malkio and Rav Malkia, two Amoraim with nearly identical names, each of whom made three statements that are easily attributed to the wrong one.

Rav Chinana, son of Rav Ika, provides the organizational framework. The ruling concerning the spit — that it may be pushed to a corner — was stated in the name of Rav Malkio. The case of *shifchos* — maidservants — concerns a *Mishnah* in which Rabbi Eliezer rules that even a woman attended by many maidservants should not be entirely exempt from household labor, because complete idleness is harmful; Rav Malkio rules that we follow Rabbi Eliezer in this matter. The case of *gumot* concerns the appearance of the indentations from which pubic hairs grow: even if the hairs themselves are not yet visible, the presence of the indentations suffices to establish adulthood for legal purposes. These three — the spit, the maidservants, and the indentations — belong to Rav Malkio.

The three statements of Rav Malkia concern *blorit* — a form of hair cutting associated with pagan practice, regarding which Rav Malkia rules that one must stop cutting within three fingerbreadths of the affected area; *efer mikleh* — burnt ashes that may not be applied to a wound lest the skin appear tattooed; and *gevinah* — the prohibition on eating cheese produced by non-Jews. These three belong to Rav Malkia.

Rav Papa offers a further mnemonic principle: any statement that involves interpreting or ruling in accordance with a *Mishnah* or *Braisa* (a Tannaitic source) belongs to Rav Malkio, while any purely Amoraic statement belongs to Rav Malkia. The mnemonic is *matnisin malchusa* — the Tannaitic material is the "royalty," the more authoritative stratum. The practical difference between Rav Chinana's and Rav Papa's organizational frameworks concerns the case of *shifchos*, which shifts attribution depending on whether one categorizes it as a Tannaitic ruling or otherwise — but the details of that realignment need not be worked through fully here.

## Purchasing Food

### Price Prohibition

With the *muktzeh* discussion concluded, the *Gemara* transitions to a new *Mishnah* that will occupy the remainder of the third *perek*: the question of how to acquire food on *Yom Tov* when one has not made arrangements beforehand, in a manner that avoids the appearance of commerce and *uvdin de'chol*. The *Mishnah* states: a person may not say to a butcher, "Give me a *dinar*'s worth of meat" — that is, one may not describe the desired quantity in monetary terms. Even without actually exchanging money, framing the transaction in the language of price is too reminiscent of a weekday commercial exchange.

### Slaughter & Divide

What is permitted instead is for the butcher to slaughter the animal and divide the meat among his customers. The *Gemara* asks how this works in practice — how does one determine what portion a customer is owed? The answer is that different communities used established, recognized cuts of meat by name, so that a customer could simply request a standard portion without any explicit negotiation of weight or price. In Sura they would speak of a *tarmasa* or half a *tarmasa*; in Narsh, a *chalka* or half a *chalka*; in Pumbedita, an *uzia* or half an *uzia*; in Nehardea and Mata Mechasya, a *riva* or half a *riva*. Rashi explains these as names for standard cuts of meat — akin to what we might call a flank or rib — though one might also read them as standard sizes or weights, and the Steinsaltz commentary does not clearly resolve the question. In any case, the point is that a recognized, named portion — whether cut or measure — allows the transaction to proceed without explicit monetary framing.

### Meat Cuts

The regional variation in terminology is itself illuminating. The *Gemara*'s catalogue of four different terms across four major Babylonian communities reflects a world in which communal convention, not standardized commerce, governed how food changed hands on *Yom Tov*. The recognized cut was both a social and halakhic solution: it allowed people to obtain what they needed without either weighing it or pricing it.

## Borrowing Flour

### Vessel Types

The next *Mishnah* shifts from the butcher to the neighbor. When one person borrows food from a friend on *Yom Tov* — with an implicit understanding that it will be repaid afterward — how does one determine the quantity without engaging in prohibited *medidah*? The *Mishnah* permits a person to say to a friend: fill up this vessel for me — *malei li kli zeh* — bringing over a container to be filled with flour, oil, or similar. This is permitted. What is not permitted, according to the *tanna kamma*, is specifying a quantity by measure: do not say "a cup of flour" or "three ounces."

### Rabbi Yehuda Strict

Rabbi Yehuda adds a further stringency: even if you do not name a measure, if the vessel you bring over is itself designated as a measuring vessel — a *kli ha-meyuchad le-midda* — you may not fill it up either. The *Gemara* works to identify the precise disagreement between these two positions.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: the *tanna kamma*'s prohibition on measurement means specifically that one may not use a vessel that has already been designated and used for measuring — a measuring cup, as it were. But a vessel that *could* serve as a measuring vessel but has not yet been put to that use — *kli ha-omed le-midda* — may still be filled up, since it has not yet been identified with the function of measuring. Rabbi Yehuda goes further: even a vessel that could eventually serve as a measuring vessel — even without having been formally used for that purpose — may not be filled up on *Yom Tov*, because doing so too clearly defines the exact quantity being borrowed and crosses into the territory of *uvda de'chol* or commercial transaction.

The *Gemara* then observes that this alignment — Rabbi Yehuda strict, the Rabbis lenient — appears to be the reverse of another *Mishnah*, in which Rabbi Yehuda permits weighing meat on a balance scale so long as what is placed on the other side is not a standard weight but some other object such as a hatchet, while the *Chachamim* prohibit using the balance scale at all. There, Rabbi Yehuda is the more lenient and the Rabbis are the stricter.

The *Gemara* resolves this apparent contradiction for both Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis: neither party is simply globally lenient or globally strict. Each takes positions based on the specifics of the case. For Rabbi Yehuda: when someone uses a hatchet on a scale, the act is visually bizarre and clearly not a normal commercial transaction — the use of an obviously non-standard counterweight reframes the entire act so that it does not resemble weekday commerce. But a vessel that could serve as a measuring vessel, even if it has not formally been designated as such, looks sufficiently like a normal transaction that Rabbi Yehuda refuses to permit it. For the Rabbis: the use of a balance scale — regardless of what is placed on the other side — is inherently a business instrument and therefore *uvda de'chol*. But a vessel that has not been formally used for measuring does not carry that same visual association, and therefore the Rabbis permit filling it up.

### Simchas Yom Tov

The *Gemara* frames all these leniencies in allowing food-related transactions on *Yom Tov* under the rubric of *simchas Yom Tov*. This is worth pausing over. The Ramban argues that the Torah's specific allowance for cooking on *Yom Tov* — which is absent on *Shabbos* — is not incidental but rooted in the centrality of *simcha* to *Yom Tov*. *Ve-hayisa ach sameach*: the obligation to rejoice is a distinctively *Yom Tov* obligation, and the Torah therefore permits the preparation of food on *Yom Tov* itself in order to maximize that joy. This stands in contrast to *Shabbos*, where *oneg* — sensory pleasure — is the operative concept, derived from the *Nevi'im* rather than the Torah, and carries less structural weight in this context. One might argue that fresh food, cooked on the day rather than reheated from before, speaks more directly to *oneg* — physical pleasure — than to *simcha* in the elevated sense the Ramban intends. But the Ramban's point may be that the very freedom to cook, the ability to engage actively in preparing for the day's enjoyment, is itself a dimension of *simcha* beyond mere sensory gratification. In any case, the *Gemara* explicitly ties the rabbinic allowances for food-related borrowing and quasi-commercial exchanges to this framework of *simchas Yom Tov*.

## Contradiction Resolution

### Scales Lenience

The contradiction identified above — between Rabbi Yehuda's leniency regarding the balance scale and his strictness regarding measuring vessels — is worth examining further in its own right. In the earlier *Mishnah*, Rabbi Yehuda rules that one may weigh meat on a balance scale, provided the counterweight is not a formal standard weight but an unconventional object such as a hatchet or another vessel. This represents a meaningful leniency in the context of acquiring food on *Yom Tov*, since it gives the customer a mechanism to obtain a roughly precise portion. The Rabbis, by contrast, prohibit use of the scale entirely, on the grounds that the very act of weighing on a scale is quintessentially a commercial act — *uvda de'chol* — regardless of what one uses as a counterweight.

### Vessel Strictness

Yet in the *Mishnah* before us, Rabbi Yehuda is the stricter voice: he prohibits filling even a vessel that has only potential use as a measuring vessel, while the Rabbis permit it so long as the vessel has not been formally designated for measuring. The resolution, as noted, turns not on global dispositions toward leniency or stringency but on case-specific reasoning. For Rabbi Yehuda, the decisive factor in the scales case is that the use of a hatchet on the other side of the scale visually and functionally marks the transaction as something other than ordinary commerce. For him, the appearance of normalcy determines permissibility. In the vessel case, even a vessel not yet formally used for measuring looks sufficiently like a normal measuring vessel that it fails to create the same visual departure from commerce.

### Case-Specific Approach

This brings into focus an important methodological point about how we read *psakim*. It is tempting to characterize a given authority as categorically lenient or categorically strict — and this temptation is especially strong with figures like the *Chazon Ish*, who is often portrayed as a systematic *machmir*. But the reality is that any careful *posek* evaluates each case on its own terms. A given *sugya* may yield a more lenient conclusion from someone commonly regarded as strict, because the specifics of that case, in that *posek*'s reading, simply do not support a prohibition. Rabbi Yehuda's reversals here illustrate precisely this dynamic: the same concern — avoiding commercial-looking transactions on *Yom Tov* — leads him to leniency in one case and stringency in another, depending entirely on whether the specific act in question resembles weekday commerce.

## Rava's Reading

### Mention vs. Vessel

Rava offers an alternative reading of the *Mishnah* that is arguably the more straightforward one. According to Rava, the *tanna kamma*'s prohibition — *aval lo be-midda* — is not about what vessel is used at all. Rather, it is a prohibition on explicitly mentioning a measurement. One may not go to one's neighbor and say, "May I please have half a cup of flour?" or "Could you give me three ounces?" Naming a specific measure is what is forbidden, because it too explicitly labels the exchange as a transaction. However, one *may* bring over one's own measuring cup and ask the neighbor to fill it up — even a vessel clearly designated for measuring — because the act of filling a vessel, without naming the quantity verbally, does not carry the same transactional character.

Rabbi Yehuda, on this reading, is stricter: he prohibits filling even a formally designated measuring vessel, regardless of whether any measurement is mentioned verbally. The mere use of such a vessel is enough to make the exchange appear like a formal commercial transaction.

The *Gemara* then runs through the same contradiction — Rabbi Yehuda lenient by the scale, strict by the vessel — and resolves it in a parallel fashion. For Rabbi Yehuda: the hatchet on the scale removes the commercial character of the weighing entirely, while the use of a designated measuring vessel, even without verbal labeling, preserves it. For the Rabbis: the scale itself, regardless of its counterweight, is a business instrument and thus *uvda de'chol*; the use of a measuring cup to convey flour to a neighbor, on the other hand, is not inherently transactional.

### Wine Example

The Rabbis' reasoning is made explicit in the *Gemara*: *de-avidi inshi de-mekarvei chamra be-mana de-kila ve-shatu* — people routinely bring wine to a friend's house in a measuring vessel and drink it together. The measuring cup, in other words, is not exclusively a commercial instrument; it also serves as an ordinary conveyance. Since people use such vessels in non-commercial social contexts all the time, filling a neighbor's measuring cup with flour on *Yom Tov* does not necessarily signal a formal transaction. But if one says, "I need exactly a cup of flour," one has verbally labeled the quantity and the exchange, making it look unmistakably like a purchase.

## Abba Shaul Story

### Pre-Measuring

The *Mishnah* records the practice of Abba Shaul ben Botnit: he would fill his measuring vessels with wine, oil, and similar goods before *Yom Tov*, so that on *Yom Tov* itself all measuring had already been completed. When customers came to his store, he would hand them pre-measured containers without performing any act of *medidah* on the holiday itself, and without needing to name any quantity. The pre-measurement was a complete solution to the *Yom Tov* concerns about both explicit labeling and prohibited measuring.

The *tanna* then extends this: Abba Shaul would do the same even on *Chol HaMoed*, and the *Gemara* explains — not primarily for reasons of commercial precision but in order to avoid *bittul Beis HaMedrash*. On *Chol HaMoed*, Abba Shaul had more time available for Torah study, as did the people who came to learn with him and pose questions. By pre-filling all the vessels at night, he ensured that during the day he would be free to sit and learn without interruption, just as the stories told of the *Chofetz Chaim* describe a similar discipline of managing a store around the priority of *Torah* study.

### Leftover Accumulation

The *Gemara* now reveals a remarkable consequence of Abba Shaul ben Botnit's practice — one that goes beyond the *Yom Tov* question entirely. When wine is poured into a vessel it initially appears full, but as bubbles settle the level drops slightly and the measure is actually somewhat less than it seemed. If one waits for the wine to settle fully, the measure is more precise, but in a commercial setting one cannot simply leave a customer's vessel sitting while waiting for the liquid to settle. The result is that the measurement appears complete but a small surplus accumulates. Similarly, oil poured into a vessel will slowly continue to drip for an extended period — sometimes for hours — so that if one pours an exact measure and hands it over, a small residue remains in the original vessel. This residue, over time, also accumulates into surplus.

Over the course of a year or more, Abba Shaul ben Botnit and his colleagues found that they had each accumulated hundreds of flasks' worth of leftover wine and oil respectively — he from the air pockets in wine, his colleagues from the slow dripping of oil. The numbers are likely an exaggeration in service of the point, but the phenomenon itself is real. The analogy to the rounding error in banking is apt: each individual instance is trivial, but aggregated across all the transactions it becomes substantial.

### Distribution to Poor

Feeling that this accumulated surplus was not rightfully theirs to keep, they brought it to the officers of the Temple treasury in Jerusalem. The treasurers responded: you are not obligated to do this; people generally forgo (*mochel*) these small discrepancies and you bear no real liability. Abba Shaul and his colleagues replied: that may well be true, but we ourselves are not comfortable keeping it.

The treasurers then said — and the phrase is striking — *ho'il ve-hichmartem al atzmechem*: since you are being stringent upon yourselves, the money should nevertheless not go to the Temple treasury. It should go back to the people. The model invoked is the *halacha* regarding stolen property when the victim cannot be identified: one does not simply donate to *tzedakah* — that does not restore anything to the original owner. Rather, *ya'asem be-tzorchei rabbim* — use it for communal needs, so that the public as a whole benefits, including those from whom the small surpluses were inadvertently withheld. Rav Chisda specifies: the appropriate use is *borot, shichin u'mearot* — digging public wells and cisterns. The lesson is that when one assumes personal stringency about money that may not be fully one's own, the remedy is not personal piety through donation to sacred purposes but communal restitution through public works.

## Animals & Professionals

### Animal Feed

The *Gemara* now addresses the question of measuring on *Yom Tov* in contexts beyond food for people. Rav Chisda, through the medium of Rabana Ukva who delivered the exposition publicly, ruled: one may not measure out barley and place it before one's animal on *Yom Tov*. The prohibition of *medidah* applies even when the measurement is for animal feed, not for human consumption, and even when no transactional context is involved — the prohibition is on the act of measuring itself, which carries an inherent association with commercial activity. However, what is permitted is *koder* — scooping: one may scoop out a *kav* or two *kavayim* and place it before the animal. The distinction Rashi draws is between scooping with the vessel that will also convey the grain to the animal — using the same vessel as both measure and delivery — versus measuring into one vessel and then transferring to a separate container. The former is permitted; it resembles the earlier case of asking a friend to "fill this up for me," where the vessel is used directly rather than as an intermediate measure.

### Professional Cooks

A *nachtom* — a professional cook or baker — is granted a special allowance to measure out spices on *Yom Tov*, *kdei shelo yakdiach tavshilo*: in order not to ruin the dish by over-spicing it. Professional cooking, far more than home cooking, depends on precise measurement of seasonings, and allowing that precision is justified by the *Yom Tov* need for proper food. Rashi notes that *nachtom* should be read broadly enough to include anyone cooking a pot, not exclusively a professional baker, but the language still implies a more formal or competent culinary context. The practical question of whether an ordinary home cook may use tablespoons and teaspoons on *Yom Tov* is not entirely resolved by this passage; the allowance appears to be most clearly established in a professional context.

### Flour & Challah

Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba said in the name of Rav: a woman may measure flour on *Yom Tov* and add it to her dough in order to ensure that when she separates *challah* she does so generously — *be-ayin yafa*. Knowing the precise quantity of flour enables her to separate the correct amount of *challah* with confidence rather than being overly stingy out of uncertainty. Shmuel disagrees: this is not measuring for the direct purpose of food but for the purpose of *challah* separation, and he would not permit it.

The *Gemara* then raises an objection: a *braisa* from the school of Shmuel apparently permits this very act. Abaye resolves the tension: Shmuel's ruling represents the *halacha le-ma'aseh* — the practical, applied ruling — rather than the theoretical *halacha*, and this is another instance of *halacha ve'ein morin ken*. The theoretical position allows measuring flour for *challah* purposes; in practice Shmuel prefers that people refrain. Whether we rule like Rav or like Shmuel on this question remains a live issue.

Tosafot addresses a further dimension of the problem relevant to contemporary practice. Since we no longer give *challah* to the *Kohen* but separate only symbolically and burn it, the size of the separated portion is not practically at stake. However, Tosafot notes that one still needs to know how much flour is in the dough in order to determine: first, whether *challah* must be separated at all; and second, whether a *beracha* is required over the separation. Could one measure the flour on *Yom Tov* to clarify the *beracha* question? Tosafot's conclusion is no — one should simply use either a generous quantity, ensuring clear obligation, or a smaller quantity, ensuring clear exemption. But the question itself reveals how the underlying concern about *medidah* intersects with the requirements of *challah* separation even today.

## Re-Sifting Flour

### Sifting Dispute

The *Gemara* presents a dispute among Tannaim regarding re-sifting flour on *Yom Tov*. The *tanna kamma* prohibits it: sifting belongs to the pre-processing stage of flour production, and doing it again on *Yom Tov* is not a kitchen act in the immediate sense but rather a continuation of the milling and refining process that should have been completed beforehand. Against this, Rabbi Papi and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira both hold that re-sifting is permitted. All opinions agree on one point: if a foreign object — a stone or a wood chip — has fallen into the flour, one may re-sift to remove it.

### Nachman's Rebuttal

A *tanna* taught a *braisa* before Ravina: one may not re-sift flour on *Yom Tov*, but if a stone or wood chip has fallen in, one should pick it out by hand rather than re-sift. Ravina responded: *kol sheken d'assur* — picking it out by hand is actually more problematic than re-sifting it, because picking it out by hand constitutes a more obvious act of *borer* — sorting the unwanted from the wanted. Rashi explains the distinction: when one re-sifts flour that has already been sifted, the visual impression is ambiguous — it is not obvious that anything is being removed, since the flour is already refined. But going in with one's hand and deliberately extracting a foreign object is visually and functionally a clear act of *borer*.

This raises the underlying question of why sifting is not itself *borer* — after all, the whole purpose of sifting is separation. Rashi's answer is that once flour has already been sifted, re-sifting it does not look like a sorting operation in any evident way. The act of *borer* as a prohibited *melacha* on *Yom Tov* involves either a biblical or an *uvda de'chol* concern depending on the context, and the case of re-sifted flour may fall into a more ambiguous zone.

Rava bar Rav Huna Zuta then delivered a public ruling at the gates of Nahardea that re-sifting flour on *Yom Tov* is entirely permitted. Rav Nachman's response was dismissive: go tell this person to take his magnanimity and throw it on the thorns — *shekilo tivotaich ve-shadi achaziri*. Go look around Nahardea and see how many sifters are circulating on *Yom Tov*: everyone knows re-sifting is permitted, and this announcement adds nothing.

### Shinuyim Practice

Despite this general permissibility, the *Gemara* records that certain Amoraic wives performed the re-sifting with a *shinuy* — a modification of the normal method. Rav Yosef's wife re-sifted using the back of the sifter rather than the normal sifting face. Rav Yosef told her plainly: I would like good bread — do it the proper way, without the modification. Rav Ashi's wife re-sifted onto the back of the table rather than into a normal vessel. Rav Ashi noted that his wife, the daughter of Rami bar Chama — who was known as a man of exceptional piety — would not have adopted this practice unless she had seen it done this way in her mother's house. The tradition of mimetic practice, transmitted from mother to daughter, lent legitimacy to the *shinuy*, even as Rav Yosef's comment made clear that such modifications are not obligatory and may even compromise the result.

## Storekeeper Purchase

### Final Mishnah

The final *Mishnah* of the third *perek* addresses one more category of food acquisition on *Yom Tov*: going to a familiar storekeeper. One may say to such a storekeeper: give me a dozen eggs, give me fifty nuts — specifying items by number. Why is this permitted? *She'ken derech ba'al ha-bayit li'heyot moneh be-toch beito* — because people routinely count items in the context of household use, not only in the context of commercial transactions. Saying "I need three eggs for this cake" or "give me ten walnuts" is not inherently commercial language. It is categorically different from naming a weight or a price, which are unambiguously transactional.

### Gemara Expansion

The *Gemara* expands this framework with a *braita* that extends the same principle across a variety of suppliers. One may go to a familiar shepherd and say: give me one kid or one lamb. One may go to a familiar butcher and say: give me a thigh or a shoulder — naming the cut but not the weight. One may go to a familiar fattener of birds and say: give me one pigeon or one dove. One may go to a familiar baker and say: give me one loaf or one cake. And one may go to a familiar storekeeper and say: give me twenty eggs, five nuts, ten quinces or peaches, five pomegranates, one *etrog*. The specificity of the list is itself revealing — particularly the single *etrog*, which suggests that *etrogim* were available from stores during the *Yom Tov* season.

### Numbers Principle

The closing principle is: *u'vilvad she-lo yazkir lo skum midah* — as long as one does not mention a specific measurement or sum. Numbers of discrete objects do not constitute a commercial designation in the way that weights and prices do. Rashi reads this to prohibit explicit mention of weight; Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar reads it to prohibit explicit mention of cost. In either reading, the principle is the same: what makes an exchange look like a formal weekday transaction is the invocation of standardized commercial metrics — weight, volume, money. Counting discrete objects by number does not cross that line. With this principle, the third *perek* concludes.

*Hadran alach Ein Tzadin.*
