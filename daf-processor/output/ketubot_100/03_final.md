# Ketubot 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Widow's Sale & Pricing

### Memorial Remarks

Before turning to the daf, we pause to note the passing of Belda Lindenbaum. Though her funeral was a small, family-only gathering — itself a testament to her values, as it could easily have drawn tens of thousands — her impact on *klal Yisrael* was enormous. Countless women have posted tributes describing how every Torah institution they benefited from was built through her vision and philanthropy. She demonstrated what guided, purposeful generosity can do to reshape the face of the Jewish community. She was 76. May her memory be a blessing.

### Dinar Correction

We pick up with the *mishnah* on 99b, which deals with a widow selling off estate property in order to collect her *ketubah*. Before proceeding, a correction is necessary regarding a point raised in the previous daf. When the *Gemara* spoke of a widow selling something worth a *maneh* and a *dinar* for only a *maneh*, the earlier discussion incorrectly assumed this referred to a golden *dinar* — worth 25 *zuz* — making the discrepancy roughly 20%. Rashi, both in the previous *sugya* and here, makes clear that we are dealing with a silver *dinar*, which is essentially a single *zuz* — representing a difference of approximately 1%. This correction is significant not only for today's daf but for all the preceding discussion about the widow over- or under-selling estate property. The two scenarios the *Gemara* addressed — selling something worth 101 for 100, or selling property worth 101 for 101 — reflect a trivially small, roughly 1% discrepancy. The question of how much latitude to grant a representative must be understood against that backdrop.

## Mishna on Court Sale

### Judges' Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוּם הַדַּיָּינִין שֶׁפִּיחֲתוּ שְׁתוּת אוֹ הוֹסִיפוּ שְׁתוּת — מִכְרָן בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The *halakha* with regard to **the assessment of the judges** of the value of a piece of property in order to sell it is as follows: **Where they decreased** the price by **one-sixth** of its market value **or added one-sixth** to its market value, **their sale is void.**

The *mishnah* introduces a new scenario: *shum ha-dayanim* — the judges themselves assess and sell the estate property. The *mishnah* states: *she-pichesu shtu'sh o hosifu shtu'sh* — if they were off by a *shtu'sh*, the sale is void, *mekhan batel*.

The *shtu'sh* (a sixth) is ordinarily the threshold for *ona'ah* — the laws of overcharging or undercharging that can void a commercial sale. However, there is no *ona'ah* for *karka'ot*, real property. So if an ordinary individual were selling his own land at a loss, the rules of *ona'ah* would simply not apply. The reason the court's sale can be voided is different: the court is not the owner selling on its own behalf, but a representative acting for the orphans. Just as the previous *mishnah* discussed the widow's role as a *shliach* of the orphans, here the court acts in that representative capacity. When a representative steps too far outside the bounds of proper valuation — selling at a discount of more than a sixth — they are understood to have exceeded their authority, and the sale does not stand.

The implicit logic of the *mishnah* is therefore that up to a sixth, the sale remains binding; beyond a sixth, the representatives have stepped outside their authorized role. This stands in notable contrast to the widow's case: given the correction noted above, even a 1% underprice voids her sale. The court, by contrast, is given considerably more latitude.

### Rabban Shimon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִכְרָן קַיָּים. אִם כֵּן — מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה? אֲבָל אִם עָשׂוּ אִגֶּרֶת בִּקּוֹרֶת בֵּינֵיהֶן, אֲפִילּוּ מָכְרוּ שָׁוֶה מָנֶה בְּמָאתַיִם אוֹ שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם בְּמָנֶה — מִכְרָן קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Their sale is valid. If** it were **so** that the sale is void, then **what advantage** is there to **the power of the court** over an ordinary person? **However, if they made a document of inspection,** i.e., an announcement that people should come to inspect the field and bid on the property, then **even if they sold** property **worth one hundred dinars for two hundred** dinars, **or** sold property **worth two hundred** dinars **for one hundred dinars, their sale is valid,** as the transaction was agreed upon and done publicly.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees: *mekhan kayam* — even if they are off by more than a sixth, the sale stands. His reasoning is captured in the phrase *she-im ken mah ko'ach beis din yafeh* — "if so, what power does the court have?" This can be read as a policy argument: if every court decision is subject to reassessment and reversal, the authority of the *beis din* is fundamentally undermined. Alternatively, one can invoke the principle of *hefker beis din hefker* — the court has the institutional authority to define legal realities through its rulings, and its acts are therefore binding by virtue of that authority alone.

A pointed question emerges from *Tosafos*: even if we accept *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s position that the sale is not reversed, must the excess be returned? In ordinary *ona'ah* cases, there are two distinct questions — whether the sale is void, and whether even a binding sale requires returning the overage. *Tosafos* asks the same question here: perhaps the court's action stands as a valid sale, yet the party who received the benefit of the mispricing must still repay the difference. Whether concern for undermining court authority extends even to requiring repayment of the overage is not resolved from the *mishnah* itself, though the plain reading appears to be that the sale is simply final.

### Igeret Bikoret

The *mishnah* concludes with an important addendum, attributed presumably to the *chachamim*: *im asu igeret bikoret beineihem* — if they produced a *writ of assessment* among themselves — then *afilu machru shaveh maneh be-masatayim*, even if they sold something worth 100 for 200, or 200 for 100, *mekhan kayam*, the sale is entirely binding. Even the *chachamim*, who ordinarily require voiding a sale that is off by more than a sixth, agree that when this document was produced, the sale stands regardless of the discrepancy.

The precise meaning of *beineihem* — literally "between them" — is somewhat puzzling. *Tosafos* suggests it might refer to a letter passing between courts, though that does not fit the context naturally. The phrase most likely refers to the members of the *beis din* producing an official written assessment among themselves. But what precisely is this *igeret bikoret*, and why should its existence render even a wildly mispriced sale binding?

### Rashi on Hachrazah

Rashi rejects the reading that the *igeret bikoret* simply means the court commissioned an assessor and wrote up a report. He explains: *u-leshon bikoret she-mevakrin oto bnei adam al yedei ha-hachrazah* — the *bikoret* refers to a process by which the public examines and evaluates the property through public announcement. In other words, the *igeret bikoret* does not mean a written appraisal but rather a public auction. Just as estates in later periods required advertisements and open bidding processes, the *hachrazah* ensures that the widest possible pool of buyers has had the opportunity to evaluate and bid on the property.

The logic follows naturally: if you have done everything humanly possible to achieve market price — made public announcement, held an open process, and let the market speak — then whatever price resulted *is* the market price by definition. Even if it deviates dramatically from a prior estimate, the process itself validates the outcome.

### Land Valuation Question

This raises a sharp question: if it is so difficult to establish an objective value for land, how can we meaningfully speak of a 1% underprice as rendering the widow's sale void? The gap between 100 and 101 *dinarim* seems impossible to detect with any precision, given that land valuation inherently involves a range. The answer, while not fully resolved by the *Gemara*, likely parallels what the *Gemara* will discuss regarding *terumah*: there is an accepted range of reasonable prices, and the widow's sale would only be void if it fell below even the lowest end of that range. This is admittedly speculative, but the need for some such framework is clear. Tellingly, the formal exclusion of land from *ona'ah* (*eino la-karka'ot*) itself reflects the impossibility of setting a precise value — which makes the standard applied to the widow's sale all the more noteworthy.

## Shaliach Classification

### Shaliach Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שָׁלִיחַ, כְּמַאן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **An agent** who mistakenly sold land for less than its value is **like whom?**

The *Gemara* now pivots from the specific cases of the widow and the court to a broader question about the law of agency: *iba'ya lehu, shaliach ke-man?* — into which category does an ordinary *shaliach*, an agent, fall? We now have two clearly defined poles. The *almanah*, the widow, is held to a near-zero tolerance standard: even a 1% underprice voids her sale. The *beis din* is given substantially more room: up to a sixth off, the sale remains valid. When someone is simply appointed as an agent to sell property on another's behalf, which standard applies?

### Rava & R. Shmuel Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שָׁלִיחַ כְּדַיָּינִין. רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר בִּיסְנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּאַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman** said: The *halakha* with regard to the **agent** is **like** the *halakha* pertaining to the **judges. Rav Shmuel bar Bisna** said that **Rav Naḥman said:** The *halakha* with regard to the agent is **like** the *halakha* with regard to **a widow.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שָׁלִיחַ כְּדַיָּינִין. מָה דַיָּינִים לָאו לְדִידְהוּ — אַף שָׁלִיחַ נָמֵי לָאו לְדִידֵיהּ, לְאַפּוֹקֵי אַלְמָנָה דִּלְדִידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman** said: The *halakha* with regard to the **agent** is **like** the *halakha* with regard to the **judges. Just as the judges** have an advantage because they **do not** assess the value of property **for their** own benefit, **so too, the agent also does not** act **for his** own benefit; this is **to the exclusion of a widow** who sells **for her own** benefit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר בִּיסְנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּאַלְמָנָה, מָה אַלְמָנָה יְחִידָה — אַף שָׁלִיחַ יָחִיד, לְאַפּוֹקֵי בֵּית דִּין — דְּרַבִּים נִינְהוּ. וְהִלְכְתָא, שָׁלִיחַ כְּאַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel, son of Bisna,** said that **Rav Naḥman said:** The *halakha* with regard to the agent is **like** the *halakha* with regard to **a widow. Just as a widow** is **an individual, so too the agent** is **an individual.** This is **to the exclusion of the court, which is** composed of **many** people. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is** that with regard to this matter, **an agent is like a widow.**

The *Gemara* records a dispute in the name of Rav Nachman. *Rava amar Rav Nachman: shaliach ke-dayanim* — the *shaliach* is like the court. The parallel reasoning is: *ma dayanim lo le-didhu, af shaliach nami lo le-didei* — just as judges sell for the benefit of others with no personal stake in the outcome, so too an agent is acting for someone else's account with no vested interest of his own. That disinterested posture warrants the same latitude as the court: as long as the sale is not more than a sixth off, it stands.

*Rav Shmuel bar Bisna amar Rav Nachman: ke-almanah* — the *shaliach* is like the widow, and even a trivial underprice voids the sale. The reasoning here: *mah almanah yechidah, af shaliach yechidah* — the widow is an individual, and so is the *shaliach*. The strict standard applied to individuals is the baseline; it is only the *beis din*, as an institutional body of multiple judges — *de-rabbim ninhu* — that earns the relaxed standard. Whether the leniency derives from the collective nature of *beis din* or from its special institutional authority, a single individual acting as agent shares neither of those qualities and must be held to the individual standard.

There is another angle on the widow's case: one might suspect that she has a personal incentive to undersell, whether to conclude the sale quickly or even to arrange a side deal at the buyer's expense. The court has no such incentive. Does the *shaliach* resemble the court in that respect? One could argue he does — he has no personal stake in a lower price. But against that, he is enough self-motivated in accepting the agency that greater precision can be demanded of him. The *Gemara* does not fully adjudicate these competing intuitions, but they frame why the question is genuinely difficult.

### Shaliach Like Widow

The ruling is that the *shaliach* is treated like the widow, and even a tiny underprice voids the sale.

### Paradox of Benefit

This ruling sounds harsh, but there is a striking irony embedded in it that works to the seller's advantage. Precisely because the *shaliach*'s sale is void at even the slightest underprice — and because there is no *ona'ah* for *karka'ot* when one sells for oneself — a seller who routes his land sale through an agent gains a protection unavailable to someone selling directly. If the agent gets even a dollar less than the property's value, the seller can void the transaction entirely. If you sell your own land, no *ona'ah* applies and you have no remedy at all for a below-market sale.

The asymmetry is interesting in the other direction too: if the *shaliach* oversells — gets more than the market value — there is no reason to void the transaction. There is no *ona'ah* for land, and the seller is perfectly happy to receive more. So the rule that the *shaliach* is like the *almanah* only cuts in favor of the seller when the price was too low; there is nothing in the sources suggesting it would void an oversale, which, as noted, may not be true when *beis din* oversells — since *beis din* represents all parties and cannot legitimately impose an unfair burden on the buyer either.

### Why Be a Shaliach?

One might reasonably wonder: given how easily a *shaliach*'s act can be invalidated, why would anyone agree to serve as an agent? And from the buyer's side, how does commerce function if a transaction can always be undone by the seller claiming the agent underpriced? The answer is partly pragmatic: in the ancient world of large landholders, managing every transaction personally was not possible, and agents — much like lawyers handling deals today — were a practical necessity. If a buyer wants security, he needs to deal with someone who genuinely holds full authorization; the law is simply clear about what that authorization entails. The business reality meant that agency relationships were understood and accepted as such.

## Terumah Comparison

### Terumah Range

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ ״צֵא וּתְרוֹם״ — תּוֹרֵם כְּדַעַת בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְאִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת — תּוֹרֵם בְּבֵינוֹנִית אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשִּׁים. פִּיחֵת עֲשָׂרָה אוֹ הוֹסִיף עֲשָׂרָה, תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** that case **different from this** case? **As we learned in a mishna** (*Terumot* 4:4): In the case of **one who says to his agent: Go out and separate** *teruma*, the agent **separates** *teruma* **in accordance with the mind-set of the homeowner. And if he does not know the mind-set of homeowner, he separates an intermediate** measure, i.e., **one-fiftieth** of the produce. If **he subtracted ten** from the denominator and separated one-fortieth **or added ten** to the denominator and separated one-sixtieth of the produce, **his *teruma*** is considered ***teruma*.** If the agent is comparable to a widow, then why isn't the *halakha* that the *teruma* that he has separated is nullified, since he did not act in accordance with the wishes of the homeowner?

The *Gemara* raises a comparison from a different *mishnah* to probe what it means to be "off." The *mishnah* states: if one tells his *shaliach* to go and separate *terumah* on his behalf, the *shaliach* does so according to what he estimates the owner would have wanted. If he does not know the owner's preference, he takes the average — one fiftieth, two percent. *Picheisu asarah o hosifu asarah* — if he took one sixtieth or one fortieth, his *terumah* is still valid. So here we see an individual *shaliach* operating with a recognized range — one fortieth to one sixtieth is all acceptable — rather than being held to an exact figure. How does this square with the ruling that a *shaliach* selling land is like the *almanah*, voided even by a tiny discrepancy?

### Range vs. Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא דְּתוֹרֵם בְּעַיִן רָעָה וְאִיכָּא דְּתוֹרֵם בְּעַיִן יָפָה, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְהָכִי אֲמַדְתָּיךְ. אֲבָל הָכָא טָעוּתָא הוּא, אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא אִיבְּעִי לָךְ לְמִיטְעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There, since there are** those **who separate in a miserly fashion** one-sixtieth, **and there are** those **who separate generously** one-fortieth, the agent can **say to** his employer: **I estimated you** to be generous or miserly. **However, here** there is no logical reason for the mistake made by the agent. **It is** simply **an error** on the part of the agent, and so the owner can **say to him: You ought not to have erred.**

The *Gemara* answers: *ha-tam kivan de-ika de-toreim be-ayin ra'ah ve-ika de-toreim be-ayin yaffah* — in the *terumah* case, there is a known range of legitimate practice. Some people are naturally generous in separating *terumah* and some are characteristically stingy, and both ends of that spectrum are recognized norms. The *shaliach* can be told: I estimated your practice falls at the generous or stingy end of this known range. *Aval hacha ta'usa hi* — but in the case of a sale, we are dealing with an objective error, not a range of practice.

### Market Context

This answer itself raises a challenge: if land valuation is so imprecise as to exclude it entirely from *ona'ah*, in what sense is a slight underprice an "objective error"? The resolution implicit in the *Gemara*'s logic is that one must work with an assessed range of market prices. If an appraiser would say that the property realistically could fetch anywhere between 110 and 140, then the sale is only a *ta'us* — an error — if it falls below the bottom of that range. This parallels the *terumah* case: it is not a question of a single precise number but of whether you have fallen outside any defensible range. The practical problem is that markets move: prices can be cut repeatedly below the assessor's estimate in a slow market, or surge well above it in a hot one. The *Gemara* seems to assume the existence of a relatively stable, determinable range, without which the entire framework becomes difficult to apply.

### Wheat Fields Pricing

The clearest scenario in which such a stable range exists is agricultural land with a clear, commodity-like character. Thousands of acres of wheat fields, for example, have a fairly objective per-unit price that does not fluctuate as wildly as residential or commercial property, where location and other idiosyncratic factors dominate. This probably represents the Talmudic default assumption — land value that, while not perfectly precise, is stable enough to support the notion of an objective error when the price falls sufficiently far outside the expected range.

## Halacha Ruling

### Halacha Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים: וְלֵית לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה? וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְתוֹמִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחְלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי אֲבִיהֶן — בֵּית דִּין מַעֲמִידִין לָהֶן אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, וּבוֹרְרִים לָהֶם חֵלֶק יָפֶה. הִגְדִּילוּ — יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת. וְרַב נַחְמָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: הִגְדִּילוּ — אֵין יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת, אִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna bar Ḥanina said** that **Rav Naḥman said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with the statement of** the **Rabbis** in the mishna. The Gemara asks: **Does Rav Naḥman not** agree with the argument: **What** advantage is there to **the power of the court? Didn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Shmuel said:** In a case of **orphans who came to divide their father's property, the court appoints a steward [*apotropos*] for them and selects for** the orphans **appropriate portions** and divides the property accordingly. Afterward, once the orphans have **matured, they are able to protest** this division of the property. **And Rav Naḥman said his own** statement: Once the orphans have **matured, they are not able to protest, as if** they were able to do **so, what advantage** would there be to **the power of the court?** This proves that Rav Naḥman agrees with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

*Amar Rav Huna bar Chinana amar Rav Nachman: halacha ke-divrei chachamim* — we rule like the *chachamim*, not like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. The court's sale is void when the price diverges from fair value by more than a sixth; it is not always binding simply by virtue of the court's authority.

### Orphans' Division

This ruling immediately generates a tension. Does Rav Nachman not hold the principle of *mah ko'ach beis din yafeh*? For he himself says in the name of *Shmuel*: when minor orphans come to divide their father's estate, *beis din* appoints a separate court-appointed representative — an *apitropus* — for each, and those representatives negotiate and divide the estate on behalf of their respective orphans. *Higdilu, yecholim limchos* — once they reach adulthood, Shmuel holds that they may object to the division.

### Challenge Division?

But Rav Nachman himself disagrees with Shmuel and rules: *higdilu, ein yecholim limchos, im ken mah ko'ach beis din yafeh* — they cannot object once they come of age, because the court-appointed division is binding. If the division could be unraveled years later simply because one of the orphans now prefers a different share, the entire institution of court-appointed guardianship collapses. How then does Rav Nachman simultaneously rule like the *chachamim* — voiding the court sale when it is off by more than a sixth — and insist that a court-appointed division of an estate is permanently binding?

## Error in Law

### Error vs. Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּטְעוֹ, הָא דְּלָא טְעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult.** The case of the mishna was one **where** the judges **erred** in their assessment, and therefore Rav Naḥman said that the sale is void in accordance with the Rabbis. However, the case of the division of property among the orphans is one **where they did not err,** and so he ruled in accordance with the principle of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, that the court is given an advantage and the orphans are not able to protest the division.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּלָא טְעוֹ, בְּמַאי יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת? בְּרוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the case is one **where** the judges **did not err,** with regard **to what could** the orphans **protest?** After all, the judges acted correctly. The Gemara answers: They can protest **with regard to** the **locations;** one of the orphans can contend that he prefers property in a different location than he was given.

*Lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Ha d'ta'ah, ha d'lo ta'ah* — one case involves a genuine error, the other does not. When the court over- or under-assesses property by more than a sixth, that is an objective factual error of significant magnitude, and Rav Nachman would say it is not binding. But when the court appoints representatives to divide an estate and those representatives negotiate a division, there is no comparable objective error — there is at most a question of preference. One orphan might later wish he had received the plot closer to the city rather than the one closer to the river; another might prefer a parcel oriented differently. *B'ruchot* — matters of direction and preference — are not objective errors. For cases of preference, Rav Nachman says the division stands; for large objective errors in valuation, it does not.

The *chachamim*'s position is therefore internally coherent within Rav Nachman's framework: a court decision is binding when it falls within a reasonable range — even if not perfect — but becomes voidable when the error is sufficiently large and objectively demonstrable.

### Rabbi's Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה וְעָשָׂה רַבִּי כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, אָמַר לְפָנָיו פַּרְטָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי פַּרְטָא הַגָּדוֹל: אִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה, וְהֶחְזִיר רַבִּי אֶת הַמַּעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said:** A similar **incident** occurred **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **acted in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis** of the mishna. **Perata, son of Rabbi Elazar ben Perata, grandson of Rabbi Perata the Great, said before him: If** that is the case, **what advantage** is there to **the power of the court** over an ordinary person? **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **reversed** his ruling about **the incident.**

*Ki ata Rav Dimi amar*: when Rav Dimi came, he reported a relevant incident. *Ma'aseh va'asa Rebbe ke-divrei chachamim* — there was a case in which *Rebbe* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) ruled like the *chachamim*, holding the court's sale void because it was off by more than a sixth. *Parta*, the son of Rabbi Elazar ben Parta and grandson of Rabbi Parta the Great, then said before him: *im ken mah ko'ach beis din yafeh* — if that is your ruling, where is the power of the court? *Ve-chazar Rebbe ve-asa ma'aseh* — Rebbe reversed his ruling and allowed the court's sale to stand.

### Two Story Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב דִּימִי מַתְנֵי הָכִי, רַב סָפְרָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי: מַעֲשֶׂה וּבִיקֵּשׁ רַבִּי לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, אָמַר לְפָנָיו פַּרְטָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי פַּרְטָא הַגָּדוֹל: אִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה? לֹא עָשָׂה רַבִּי אֶת הַמַּעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi** would **teach** the incident in **this** way, as described above. **Rav Safra** would **teach** it in **this** slightly altered way: There was **an incident, and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **wished to act in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis** of the mishna. **Perata, son of Rabbi Elazar ben Perata, grandson of Rabbi Perata the Great, said before him: If** that is the case, **what advantage** is there to the **power of the court** over an ordinary person? Consequently, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **did not take action** according to the statement of the Rabbis.

*Ika de'amri*, there is an alternate version. Rav Safra taught it differently: *uvikeish Rebbe la'asot ke-divrei chachamim* — Rebbe was *about* to rule like the *chachamim* when Parta interjected with the *mah ko'ach beis din yafeh* argument, and *lo asa Rebbe et ha-ma'aseh* — Rebbe never issued the ruling in the first place. In this version, there is no reversal, only a ruling withheld.

### Reversal Irony

The first version is particularly striking. The very principle that courts do not reverse their own rulings — *mah ko'ach beis din yafeh* — was invoked to cause Rebbe to reverse *his* ruling. He declined to reverse the court's sale, but he was perfectly willing to reverse his own *pesak*. The irony is impossible to miss.

### Law Always Reversed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה — חוֹזֵר, וּמָר סָבַר: אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** Rav Dimi, **holds** that if one **erred in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** the decision is **revoked. And** one **Sage,** Rav Safra, **holds** that if one erred in this manner the decision **is not revoked.** This is why, in Rav Safra's version, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi changed his mind before issuing his ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה חוֹזֵר, וּמָר סָבַר הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וּמָר סָבַר הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, everyone** agrees that if one **erred in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** the decision **is revoked.** There is no fundamental dispute between them, only a disagreement as to the details of the case. One **Sage holds** that the **incident** occurred **in this** way, **and** one **Sage holds** that the **incident** occurred **in this** way.

*Lema bahai kamifligi?* — perhaps this is what the two versions of the story actually dispute. *Mar savar to'eh bidvar mishnah chozer, u-mar savar eino chozer* — one view holds that when a court errs in a matter of black-letter law, the ruling is reversed; the other holds it is not. The *Gemara* rejects this: *lo de-kuli alma to'eh bidvar mishnah chozer* — everyone agrees that an error in black-letter law (*to'eh bidvar mishnah*) requires reversal. The two versions of the story simply differ on what actually happened historically, not on the underlying *halacha*.

Here the *Gemara* draws out a deep structural parallel. There are two axes of error: error in fact and error in law. On the factual axis, the *mishnah* debates whether a court sale mispriced by more than a sixth must be reversed — the *chachamim* say yes, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says no. On the legal axis, there is a distinction between *to'eh be-shikul ha-da'at* — a judgment call where reasonable authorities might disagree, where the ruling stands even if the judgment was wrong — and *to'eh bidvar mishnah*, a clear error in established law, where everyone agrees reversal is required.

### Authority Concept

The parallel illuminates the conceptual framework underlying *mah ko'ach beis din yafeh*. An agent — whether a *shaliach* or the *beis din* itself — acts within their authority when they operate inside the law and within a reasonable range of factual judgment. The *mah ko'ach beis din yafeh* principle protects their acts within that zone: even mistakes of fact do not undo what they have done, because they were authorized. But when they act outside the law — when they are *to'eh bidvar mishnah* — they are no longer functioning as *beis din* at all. Their authorization extends only to lawful action; unlawful action is not protected by institutional authority.

This creates a precise parallel with the *shaliach* who undersells by a dollar: the moment he steps outside the authorized range, he is no longer acting within his *shlichus*. Correspondingly, *beis din* acts are binding when they stay within their lawful mandate — even with errors in fact — but not when they depart from it. The distinction is not about probability of error or quality of judgment; it is about whether one is acting within or outside the scope of one's authority.

## Widow's Warranty

### Estate Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אַרְמַלְתָּא דְּזַבִּינָה — אַחְרָיוּת אַיַּתְמֵי. וּבֵית דִּין דְּזַבֵּין — אַחְרָיוּת אַיַּתְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yosef said:** In the case of **a widow who sold** property to support herself or as payment of her marriage contract, the property **guarantee** rests **upon the orphans.** Therefore, if she sold liened property that was then seized from the purchasers in payment of a previous debt, the buyers are entitled to be reimbursed from the property of the orphans. **And** so too, if the **court sold** property for the same purpose, the property **guarantee** rests **upon the orphans.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious?** The widow does not sell her own property, but rather she sells property from her husband's estate to pay off his debts to her, and so clearly the guarantee rests on his properties that now belong to the orphans.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה לָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ. כִּי אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ, בֵּי דִינָא — מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In truth, **it was not necessary** for Rav Yosef to mention this with regard to the **widow,** as it is clear that the property guarantee rests upon the orphans. **Where** it **was necessary for** him to mention this *halakha* was with regard to the **court. Lest you say:**

Rav Yosef states: *almanah de-zabina, achrayus ayasmi* — when a widow sells estate property to collect her *ketubah*, the *achrayus* — the legal responsibility for warranty of title — falls on the estate, not on the widow personally. If the property turns out to have been improperly sold, if the original owner appears and reclaims it from the buyer, the buyer's claim for reimbursement goes against the orphans' estate, not against the widow who conducted the sale.

This is intuitive: the widow was selling the estate's property to satisfy the estate's debt to her. She was not selling something she owned. Similarly, *u-veis din de-zabni, achrayus ayasmi* — when *beis din* sells estate property, the warranty obligation likewise rests with the estate. The *Gemara* notes that while the widow's case is obvious, the court's case is not, since one might have thought that buying through a formal court process carries an implicit assurance. When property is sold by court order after extensive public announcement, every potential claimant has had ample opportunity to come forward. Perhaps the buyer implicitly waives his right to warranty by assuming no claim will arise. The *Gemara* rejects this: the buyer's assumption does not rise to an actual waiver, and if a title defect emerges, his claim against the estate stands.

### No Announcement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: בֵּית דִּין שֶׁמָּכְרוּ בְּלֹא הַכְרָזָה — נַעֲשׂוּ כְּמִי שֶׁטָּעוּ בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה וְחוֹזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said in the name of Rav Yosef:** With regard to **a court that sold without an announcement,** it **is considered as** if **they erred in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna and** their decision is **reversed.**

*Ameimar* then states in the name of Rav Yosef: *beis din she-machru belo hachrazah, na'asu ke-mi she-ta'u bidvar mishnah ve-chozrin* — if *beis din* sold estate property without going through the required public announcement process, the sale is treated as if the court committed an error in a matter of law (*to'eh bidvar mishnah*), and it is reversed.

Why is the failure to conduct a *hachrazah* classified as *to'eh bidvar mishnah* rather than as an error in fact? The answer is structural: the *hachrazah* is a procedural requirement, not a factual judgment. A court operating without it is not making a bad assessment of value — it is simply not following the procedure it is authorized to follow. It has stepped outside its lawful mandate, and actions outside that mandate are not protected by *mah ko'ach beis din yafeh*. This is precisely analogous to the principle established above: *beis din*'s authority covers errors within the law, not departures from it.

The *Gemara* notes that the *mishnah* itself states *shum ha-yetomim shloshim yom* — assessing orphans' estate requires a thirty-day public announcement process — and *shum ha-hekdesh shishim yom* — for *hekdesh* property, sixty days — with announcements morning and evening. This is not an optional enhancement but a required procedure. Selling without it is an error in law.

## Public Auction Rules

### Announcement Terms

The *mishnah* mandates two distinct announcement periods: thirty days for orphans' property and sixty days for *hekdesh*, with announcements morning and evening throughout. The logic of the thirty-day and sixty-day windows is to maximize exposure: the longer the window, the larger the pool of potential buyers who will hear of the sale and have the opportunity to evaluate and bid.

### Right Price Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: שׁוּם הַדַּיָּינִין שֶׁפִּחֲתוּ שְׁתוּת אוֹ הוֹתִירוּ שְׁתוּת — מִכְרָן בָּטֵל. הָא שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה — מִכְרָן קַיָּים. מַאי לָאו דְּלָא אַכְרוּז? לָא, בִּדְאַכְרוּז.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raised an objection to** the statement of **Ameimar:** The mishna teaches that the *halakha* with regard to **the assessment** of the value of a piece of property in order to sell it through **the judges** is as follows: In a case **where they decreased** the price by **one-sixth** of its market value **or added one-sixth** to its market value, **their sale is void.** One can see from here that if the judges sold the property **for its value, their sale is valid. What, is** the mishna **not** discussing a case where **no announcement was made?** Ameimar replied: **No,** the case under discussion is one **where they did make an announcement.**

Rav Ashi poses a sharp question to Ameimar: the *mishnah* states that when *beis din* undersells or oversells by a sixth, *machran batel* — the sale is void. *Ha shaveh be-shaveh, machran kayam* — if they got the right price, the sale is valid. Now, if the failure to hold a public announcement renders the sale void regardless of the price achieved, then the *mishnah*'s discussion of correct pricing would be irrelevant — even a perfectly priced sale without a *hachrazah* should be void. Yet the *mishnah* makes clear that a correctly priced sale stands. This implies that the *mishnah* is discussing a case *without* a public announcement, which contradicts the claim that any sale without *hachrazah* is void.

The *Gemara*'s response: *lo, be-d'achriz* — the *mishnah* is actually discussing a case *with* a public announcement. We know this from the end of the *mishnah*, which says that if an *igeret bikoret* — which Rashi identifies with the *hachrazah* process — was conducted, the sale is binding regardless of the price. The *mishnah* thus discusses a situation where public auction was held: the beginning addresses the case where the right process was followed but the price was wrong; the end addresses the case where the right process was followed and the price was also drastically off. In both cases, the process was conducted.

### What Requires Announcement…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּסֵיפָא בִּדְאַכְרוּז הָוֵי, רֵישָׁא בִּדְלָא אַכְרוּז, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אִם עָשׂוּ אִגֶּרֶת בִּקּוֹרֶת, אֲפִילּוּ מָכְרוּ שָׁוֶה מָנֶה בְּמָאתַיִם אוֹ שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם בְּמָנֶה — מִכְרָן קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since **the last clause is** referring to a case **where** the court **made an announcement, the first clause** must be referring to a case **where they did not make announcements, as it teaches in the final clause: If they made a document of inspection** and announced the sale publicly, then **even if they sold** property **worth one hundred dinars for two hundred** dinars **or** property **worth two hundred** dinars **for one hundred dinars, their sale is valid.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם בִּדְלָא אַכְרוּז, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּדְבָרִים שֶׁמַּכְרִיזִין עֲלֵיהֶן, כָּאן בִּדְבָרִים שֶׁאֵין מַכְרִיזִין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this should be understood differently. **Actually,** the first clause of the mishna is referring to a case **where they did not make an announcement, and** this is **not difficult. Here,** Ameimar is referring **to items for which one makes an announcement,** and if this was not done then the sale is void. **There,** the mishna is speaking of **items for which one does not make announcements.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין מַכְרִיזִין עֲלֵיהֶן: הָעֲבָדִים וְהַמִּטַּלְטְלִין וְהַשְּׁטָרוֹת. עֲבָדִים טַעְמָא מַאי? שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיִבְרְחוּ. מִטַּלְטְלִין וּשְׁטָרוֹת — שֶׁמָּא יִגָּנְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the **items for which one does not make an announcement: Slaves, movable property, and contracts.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that **slaves** are sold without an announcement? Slaves are sold without an announcement **lest they hear** that they are about to be sold **and escape.** Why is the sale of **movable property and contracts** also not announced? **Lest they be stolen.**

But this resolution creates its own problem: if the *mishnah* only addresses sales *with* a public announcement, how can it say that a correctly priced sale stands? The *Gemara* resolves this through a distinction among the categories of property being sold.

*Ela le-olam de-lo achruz* — fine, the *mishnah* may indeed address cases without a public announcement. The apparent contradiction disappears once one recognizes that the *hachrazah* requirement applies specifically to land. There are categories of property for which no public auction process is required at all: *ha-avadim ve-ha-metaltelin ve-ha-shtarot* — slaves, chattels, and documents of debt. For slaves, the reason is *shem'ei ve-arki* — they will hear they are about to be sold and flee. For chattels and documents, *shema yignavu* — during the extended public viewing period, items might be stolen. You cannot steal land. The *hachrazah* requirement therefore applies only to land, and the *mishnah*'s ruling that a correctly priced sale stands without formal announcement may simply be addressing a sale of these other categories — categories for which public auction is never required.

If so, why might the widow be collecting her *ketubah* through the sale of non-land property? Ordinarily, a *ketubah* lien applies only to real property. But the *Gemara* acknowledges this is a special case where the lien was extended to other property as well.

### Auction Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: לִכְרָגָא לִמְזוֹנֵי וְלִקְבוּרָה — מְזַבְּנִינַן בְּלָא אַכְרָזְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** When does one not make an announcement? **As** the Sages **of Neharde'a say: For** the purpose of paying **head tax,** and **for** payment to provide for children's **sustenance, and for burial,** the court **sells** property inherited by orphans **without an announcement** because these are pressing needs. There is no time to wait for an announcement.

Beyond the distinction between property types, there are situational exceptions to the *hachrazah* requirement. The *Gemara* cites the practice articulated in Neharde'a: *le-kharga, u-le-mezonei, u-le-kevura* — when there is a need to pay government taxes immediately, when the widow needs funds for food support (*mezonot*), or when burial expenses must be met without delay. In these urgent circumstances, *mazbininan belo akhrazta* — one sells without public auction. Urgency overrides process. This also provides a natural reading of the main *mishnah*: the widow seeking to collect her *ketubah* for living expenses is a paradigmatic case of *mezonot*, and her sale can proceed without the thirty-day announcement, yet still remains binding as long as the price is within the acceptable range.

### Neharde'a Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁמַּכְרִיזִין, כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַכְרִיזִין. דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא עָשׂוּ אִגֶּרֶת בִּקּוֹרֶת בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **here,** Ameimar is referring to **a locale where one makes an announcement,** while **there,** the mishna is referring to **a locale where one does not make an announcement, as Rav Naḥman said: They never made a document of inspection in Neharde'a.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר מִינַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּבְקִיאֵי בְּשׁוּמָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי: לְדִידִי מִיפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָרוּ לְהוּ ״בְּנֵי אָכְלִי נִכְסֵי דְאַכְרָזְתָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** Some of the students **understood from** Rav Naḥman's statement that no announcements were made in Neharde'a **because** the Sages there were all **expert in the appraisal** of an article's value. **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said to them:** This **was explained to me** personally **by Rav Naḥman himself:** It was **because** those who purchase the property **are called: People** who **consume property that was publicly announced.** This disparaging nickname was given because the purchasers were perceived as taking advantage of the distress of others by running to buy the property of someone in trouble. Since decent, honest people did not wish to buy property whose sale had been announced, they stopped making announcements.

Rav Nachman adds a third exception — one based not on property type or urgency, but on locale. *Me-olam lo asu igeret bikoret bi-Neharde'a* — they never conducted the public auction process in Neharde'a. One explanation offered is *de-vaki'ei bi-shuma* — the scholars and residents of Neharde'a were such expert assessors that no public bidding process was necessary to achieve a fair price; their expertise substituted for the market mechanism. A different explanation, attributed directly to Rav Nachman by Rav Yosef bar Minyumi, is more social in character: buyers at public auctions in Neharde'a were called *benei achli nichsei akhtekhasta* — "those who devour the property of the disadvantaged" — and the reputational stigma was severe enough that the practice was simply abandoned. The paradox is that the public announcement, designed to protect orphans by maximizing competition, ended up suppressing it because buyers were reluctant to participate. Neharde'a resolved this by foregoing the process altogether.

## Sales Timing Strategy

### Perishable Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מִטַּלְטְלִין שֶׁל יְתוֹמִים — שָׁמִין אוֹתָן וּמוֹכְרִים אוֹתָן לְאַלְתַּר. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: מוֹכְרִים אוֹתָן לִשְׁוָוקִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Movable property that belongs to orphans is appraised and sold immediately** so that it not deteriorate over time. **Rav Ḥisda** said that **Avimi said:** The movable property **is sold on a market** day, when there are many potential buyers and the items will sell for a proper price.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא — דִּמְיקָרַב שׁוּקָא, הָא — דִּמְרַחַק שׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** they do **not disagree** with each other. Rather, **this** opinion, that the items are sold on a market day, applies **when** the **market** day **is approaching,** so the property is not sold immediately. **That** opinion, that the items are sold immediately, applies **when** the **market** day **is far off.**

The *Gemara* closes with a series of practical cases about how a court-appointed guardian should optimize the sale of orphans' property. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: metaltelin de-yatmi, shamminan le-hu u-mazbini le-hu altar* — chattel belonging to orphans should be assessed and sold immediately. The concern is that such goods are perishable: if value depreciates over time, delay works against the orphans' interests.

Rav Chisda says in the name of Avimi: *mazbininan le-hu le-yomei de-shuka* — you wait for market day. Even if the goods lose some freshness, selling on market day ensures a broader buyer pool, complete sales, and potentially better total returns than selling immediately to a thin local market. *Ve-lo peligi* — these two views do not actually disagree in principle. *Ha de-mikrav shuka, ha de-merachak shuka* — if market day is near, you wait; if it is far off and goods will significantly deteriorate in the interim, you sell at once. The principle in both cases is to maximize the orphans' return within the constraints of the situation.

### Holiday Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ שִׁכְרָא דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא בַּר חִילְקַאי יַתְמָא. שַׁהֲיֵיהּ עַד רִיגְלָא, אָמַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּנָפֵל בֵּיהּ אִיצְצָתָא, מַיְיתֵי זוּזָא חֲרִיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana was in possession of beer** that belonged **to Rav Mesharshiyya bar Ḥilkai,** who was **an orphan. He delayed** selling **it until the Festival.** He explained the rationale for his actions and **said: Although** it is possible **that it may deteriorate [*itzatzta*]** and sour a little, **it** nevertheless **brings** in **money** at the time of the Festival, as it will sell for a higher price and for money, not credit. Consequently, it is in the best interests of Rav Mesharshiyya bar Ḥilkai to hold off and sell the beer at the next Festival.

Rav Kahana had beer belonging to the orphan Rav Mesharshiya bar Chilkai in his care. He waited until the *regel* — the pilgrimage festival — to sell it. *Af gav de-nafal beih itzatza* — even though the beer had begun to turn slightly by the time of the festival, he reasoned *mai zuzei charifa* — at the *regel*, he would sell all of it immediately and collect payment in cash. Rashi adds that selling locally in the interim would have meant extended credit to buyers, whereas festival crowds pay on the spot. The small reduction in quality was therefore more than offset by the certainty and completeness of the cash sale.

### Shipping Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ חַמְרָא דְּרָבִינָא זוּטֵי יַתְמָא בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ. הֲוָה לְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי חַמְרָא, הֲוָה קָמַסֵּיק לֵיהּ לְסִיכְרָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְאַמְטוֹיֵי בַּהֲדַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל, לָא עֲדִיף מִדִּידָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** It is also related that **Ravina was in possession of wine** belonging **to** the **orphan Ravina the younger,** who **was his sister's son. He also had wine of his own,** which **he was taking to Sikhra** to sell. **He came before Rav Ashi** and **said to him: What is** the *halakha* here, am **I** able **to bring** his wine along **with** my wine, or must I wait for a more opportune time to sell it? **He said to him: Go** to Sikhra and take his wine along as well, as his **is no better than yours,** and if you sell your own wine in this manner, it is clear that you think this is the best way to sell, and it is permitted for you to sell his wine in this manner.

Ravina had in his possession wine belonging to his orphaned nephew. He was also transporting his own wine to Sichra by ship and asked Rav Ashi whether he should take the orphan's wine along. The concern was risk: if the ship sank, the orphan's wine would be lost. *Amar lei: zil, lo adif mi-didach* — Rav Ashi told him: go ahead, his wine is no different from yours. If you judge the profit opportunity to be worth the risk for your own wine, then it is a reasonable risk to take with his wine as well. The guardian is not required to be more conservative with the orphan's assets than he is with his own; he must simply exercise the same judgment he would apply to his own affairs.

### Discretion Balance

These cases close a full circle. The daf opened with a framework of sharply limited discretion — a *shaliach* who undersells by even a trivial amount has exceeded his authority and the sale is void. Yet the *Gemara* closes by recognizing that those responsible for orphans' assets must exercise real-world judgment: whether to sell immediately or wait for market day, whether to hold goods until a festival, whether to accept the risk of shipping. The tension between strict accountability and practical discretion defines the role of any fiduciary representative, whether *shaliach*, *apitropus*, or *beis din* itself.
