# Pesachim 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Moldy Bread & Dog Standards

### Moldy Bread Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַפַּת שֶׁעִיפְּשָׁה וְנִפְסְלָה מִלֶּאֱכוֹל לָאָדָם, וְהַכֶּלֶב יָכוֹל לְאוֹכְלָהּ — מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין בִּכְבֵיצָה, וְנִשְׂרֶפֶת עִם הַטְּמֵאָה בַּפֶּסַח. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נָתָן אָמְרוּ: אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **bread that grew moldy and was rendered inedible for consumption by a person, but a dog can eat it,** it **can become impure** with the **ritual impurity of foods in** the measure **of an egg-bulk** in size, as it is still considered food. If the moldy bread was ritually pure *teruma*, **it** may **be burned with ritually impure** *teruma* **on Passover.** Once the bread is no longer fit for human consumption, the prohibition against actively rendering it impure no longer applies. **They said in the name of Rabbi Natan: It cannot become ritually impure.**

The *gemara* on this *daf* opens with a *baraita*: *hapat she'ipsha venifsela me'achol la'adam vehakelev yakhol le'okhla* — bread that has become moldy and is no longer fit for human consumption, yet remains edible by dogs. The *baraita* rules that such bread *metamma tum'at ochlin kibeitza*: if there is at least an egg's volume of it, it retains the status of food for purposes of *tum'at ochlin* and can become *tamei*. Furthermore, *venisrepet im hatme'iah bePesach* — it may be burned together with *terumah teme'iah* on Pesach. Ordinarily one may not burn *terumah tehorah* together with *terumah teme'iah*, lest the pure *terumah* become *tamei* in the process. Here, however, since the bread is no longer fit for human consumption, there is no concern about rendering it *tamei*, and so it may be burned alongside the *terumah teme'iah*.

### Status Stickiness

Before proceeding to the dispute in the *baraita*, it is worth pausing on an important underlying principle — what one might call the stickiness of halakhic status. Once something has acquired a status, it does not relinquish it easily. Just as it is easier never to hire someone than to fire them once they have been hired, so too an object that has once attained the status of food does not lose that status readily. If something was never fit for human consumption, it never acquired food status in the first place and cannot become *tamei* as *ochlin*. But if it was once fit for human consumption, it will retain that status even after humans can no longer eat it, so long as it remains edible at all — even if only by a dog. Only when it becomes completely inedible, unfit even for a dog, does it finally shed its food status.

### Rav Natan vs. Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דִּתְנַן, כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בִּטְהָרוֹת: כׇּל הַמְיוּחָד לְאוֹכֶל אָדָם — טָמֵא עַד שֶׁיִּפָּסֵל מִלֶּאֱכוֹל לְכֶלֶב. כְּמַאן — דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which we learned** in a mishna: The Sages **stated** a **principle with regard to ritual purity: Any** food **that is designated as food for a person** that becomes impure remains **ritually impure until it is rendered unfit to be consumed by a dog.** The Gemara reiterates: **In accordance with whose** opinion is that statement? The Gemara answers: It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan,** as his statement indicates that food designated as food for a person is rendered ritually pure as soon as it becomes unfit to be eaten by a person.

The *baraita* then records a dissenting opinion: *mishum Rav Natan amru einah metamma* — in the name of Rav Natan they said it does not convey *tumah*. According to Rav Natan, once bread is no longer edible by humans, it loses its food status immediately, regardless of whether dogs can still eat it. The *gemara* then connects this dispute to a *mishnah* in *taharot*: *klal amru betaharot* — a general principle was stated concerning *taharot*: *kol hamiyuchad le'okhel adam tamei*, anything designated for human food can become *tamei*, and it retains that status *ad sheyipasel me'achol kelev* — until it is no longer fit even for a dog. The *gemara* concludes: *keman delo keRav Natan* — this *mishnah* does not follow Rav Natan, since Rav Natan holds that human inedibility suffices to remove food status, while the *mishnah* requires it to be inedible even by a dog.

### Chachamim Position

The *halacha* follows the *Chachamim* rather than Rav Natan. The operative standard is therefore inedibility even for a dog. Once something that was formerly edible by humans becomes unfit even for a dog, it is no longer considered food for halakhic purposes.

### Pesach Applications

This principle has wide-ranging practical application, particularly regarding medicines, toothpaste, vitamins, and similar substances on Pesach. The *gemara* establishes that once something is not fit for *achilas kelev* — consumption by a dog — it is no longer in the category of food and poses no problem of ownership on Pesach. There is, however, a position of the Rosh that even when something is not *ra'ui*, if a person actually ingests it, the act of eating grants it subjective importance — a concept known as *achshavei* — and causes it to be treated as food. This raises the question of whether ingesting medicine on Pesach creates a problem through *achshavei*.

The *poskim* generally rule that this is not a concern, because when one takes medicine as medicine rather than as food, the act of ingestion does not constitute *achshavei*. It is not eaten in the manner of eating food, and therefore does not confer food status upon it. The same logic applies to toothpaste and similar products — if they are not fit for *achilas kelev*, they fall outside the category of *chametz* and pose no problem. A well-known anecdote recounts that students of the Rav brought this question to him, citing the standard that toothpaste is not edible even by a dog. To test the point, they placed some toothpaste on a plate before a dog, which proceeded to eat it. They reported this back to the Rav, who replied: who is your *rebbi* — me or the dog?

Vitamins and dietary supplements present a more complex case. Unlike medicines, which are clearly not taken as food, dietary supplements serve a nutritional function and occupy a grey area between food and medicine. While they are not eaten in the manner of normal food, the question of how to categorize them on Pesach is a genuine one, and the relevant discussions among *poskim* should be reviewed. This forum is not the place for a definitive ruling, only for identifying the issues.

Returning to *chametz nuksheh*: the examples discussed earlier — substances that are not fully edible — must still be edible at least by a dog in order to retain food status. If something was originally edible by humans but has degraded, it loses its status entirely once it becomes unfit even for a dog. And there are, it should be noted, even cosmetic products that, in extremis, could be consumed by a dog if not by a human — for example, certain mouthwashes or similar substances — which might still fall under *achilas kelev* and thus retain *chametz* status.

## Areivas HaAvdanim

### Trough & Flour Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲרֵיבַת הָעַבְּדָנִים שֶׁנָּתַן לְתוֹכָהּ קֶמַח, תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים — חַיָּיב לְבַעֵר. קוֹדֶם שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְבַעֵר. אָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן לְתוֹכָהּ עוֹרוֹת, אֲבָל נָתַן לְתוֹכָהּ עוֹרוֹת — אֲפִילּוּ תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה אֵין חַיָּיב לְבַעֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **tanners' bowls into which** one **placed flour** in the production process of leather, if the flour was placed **within three days** of the start of Passover, **one is obligated to remove** it, as it is still considered edible leaven. However, if one added the flour **prior to three days** before Passover, **one is not obligated to remove** the contents of the bowl, as the flour will have already been rendered inedible by the odor of the vessel before the beginning of Passover, and is no longer considered edible. **Rabbi Natan said: In what** case **is this statement said?** In a case **where one did not place** animal **hides into** the bowl; **however,** if **one placed hides into** the bowl, **even** if Passover is **within three** days of when he placed the flour in the bowl, **he is not obligated to remove** the flour. Once the foul-smelling hides are placed in the bowl, the flour is certainly no longer edible.

The *gemara* next presents a related *baraita* dealing with a different path to inedibility. *Areivas ha'avdanim* — the troughs of leather workers — would be used to treat animal hides. As part of the tanning process, a flour-based mixture would be placed in the trough together with the leather. The *baraita* rules: *shenasan letochah kemach* — if flour was placed into such a trough — then *toch sheloshah yamim chayav leva'er*, within three days of Pesach one is obligated to destroy it, because within that window the mixture may still be somewhat edible. However, *kodem sheloshah yamim eino chayav leva'er* — more than three days before Pesach, one need not destroy it, because by then it is no longer edible.

### Rabbi Natan's View

Rabbi Natan qualifies the ruling: *bameh devarim amorim shelo nasan letochah oros* — when is the above *halacha* applicable? Only when the leather hides have not yet been placed in the trough. *Aval nasan letochah oros* — but once the leather has been placed into the mixture — *afilu toch sheloshah eino chayav leva'er* — even within three days of Pesach, one need not destroy it. The presence of the leather renders the entire mixture completely inedible and thus removes any *chametz* concern.

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי נָתָן, אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד וַאֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan** with regard to this issue. Consequently, there is no set time at which the flour is considered spoiled, as it is considered inedible **even one day or even one hour** after hides were added to the bowl.

Rava rules: *halachah keRabbi Natan* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Natan — *afilu yom echad va'afilu sha'ah achas* — even if the leather was placed into the mixture only a day or even an hour before Pesach, it suffices to render the mixture inedible and eliminates any obligation to destroy it. This *baraita* thus reinforces the central principle: once *chametz* becomes inedible, it loses its *chametz* status entirely. The point of contention — whether the threshold is inedibility for humans or inedibility even for dogs — was addressed earlier: we rule like the *Chachamim*, requiring inedibility even for dogs. Certainly where something was once fully edible by humans and has attained complete food status, it requires a more decisive degradation to lose that status.

## Dough in Cracks

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה, אִם מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — חוֹצֵץ, וְאִם רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא כַּעֲרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And similarly, with regard to** the *halakhot* of immersion to purify the bowl from **ritual impurity, if one is particular about** the dough that is stuck in the cracks and he plans to remove it and use it, **it** is a foreign substance that **interposes** between the kneading bowl and the water of the ritual bath, and invalidates the immersion of the bowl, leaving it ritually impure. **And if he wants** the dough **to remain** in place, **its** status **is like** that **of the** kneading **bowl** itself and is not an interposition.

The *gemara* now turns to the end of the *mishnah* concerning dough stuck in the cracks of a kneading trough. The *mishnah* states that the same *halacha* that applies on Pesach — determining whether the dough is considered part of the trough or a separate entity — applies also to the laws of *tumah*: *im makpid alav chotzetz* — if one objects to its presence and wishes to remove it, it constitutes a *chatzitzah*, an obstruction; *v'im rotzeh b'kiyumo harei hu ka'areivah* — if one wishes it to remain, it is treated as part of the trough itself.

### Pesach vs. Year-Round

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם — בְּשִׁיעוּרָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא. הָכָא — בִּקְפִידָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is it comparable?** How can the mishna compare the *halakhot* of leaven on Passover to the *halakhot* of interpositions that invalidate an immersion to purify from ritual impurity? **There,** with regard to leaven, **the matter is contingent upon the measure** of the dough, as an olive-bulk of leaven is prohibited. **Here,** with regard to interpositions that invalidate an immersion, **the matter is contingent upon** whether or not one is **particular** about the presence of the dough. In other words, with regard to interpositions it is the attitude of the owner of the bowl that is the decisive factor, not the quantity of the dough.

The *gemara* raises an immediate difficulty. When discussing *chametz* on Pesach, the *mishnah* employed objective criteria: the determining factor was whether the dough had reached the volume of a *kezayit* — a size-based, objective standard — along with the additional criterion of whether it functioned to reinforce the trough. These are objective criteria tied to the physical significance of the dough. By contrast, *mi dami* — how is this comparable? — the *halacha* of *tumah* employs subjective criteria: *hacha b'kfeida talya milta* — here the matter depends on one's subjective *kfeida*, one's personal preference regarding whether the dough should remain or be removed. These are two entirely different frameworks for determining whether the dough is part of the vessel.

### Rav Yehuda's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אֵימָא: וּלְעִנְיַן הַטּוּמְאָה אֵינוֹ כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Yehuda said: Say** that the mishna should be read: **And with regard to** interpositions that invalidate an immersion to purify from **ritual impurity, it is not so,** as it is not the quantity of the dough but the particularity of the owner that is the decisive factor.

Rav Yehuda addresses this tension. *Eima u'l'inyan tumah eino kein* — he proposes that the *mishnah* does not mean the same ruling applies to *tumah*, but rather that *tumah* raises an analogous question — whether the dough is considered part of the trough or not — but resolved by a different set of criteria, namely the question of whether one is *makpid* or not *makpid*.

### Abaye's Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָא ״וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן צֵירוּף טוּמְאָה בַּפֶּסַח, וּבִשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה אִיכָּא פְּלוּגְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Doesn't** the mishna **teach: And similarly with regard to ritual impurity.** The text of the mishna cannot be so drastically emended merely to resolve a difficulty. **Rather, Abaye said: This is** what the mishna **is saying: And similarly with regard to the combination** of two pieces vis-à-vis **ritual impurity during Passover,** when it depends upon their volume. However, **during the rest of the year there is a distinction** based upon whether the owner is particular about it or not.

Abaye rejects this reading. *V'ha v'chen l'inyan tumah tanya* — the *mishnah* explicitly states that the same applies to *tumah*; one cannot simply reinterpret it as saying something different applies. Abaye therefore proposes a wholesale reinterpretation of the *mishnah*: *ella amar Abaye hachi ka'amar* — the *mishnah* is not discussing *chatzitzah* in the context of immersing the trough in a *mikveh* at all. Rather, it is addressing the question of *tziruf tumah* — the combining of foodstuffs for *tumah* purposes on Pesach — and whether the dough in the crack can be combined with food outside the vessel to meet the minimum threshold of *tum'at ochlin*.

### Apple & Dough Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי, כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה אוֹכָלִין, וְנָגְעוּ בְּהַאי בָּצֵק. בְּפֶסַח, דְּאִיסּוּרוֹ חָשׁוּב — מִצְטָרֵף. בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, דְּבִקְפֵידָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא, אִם מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — מִצְטָרֵף, אִם רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא כַּעֲרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What are the circumstances** of the mishna's case? It is a case **where there is less than an egg-bulk of** ritually impure **food, and it touched this dough** in the bowl, and then it came into contact with ritually pure food. **During Passover,** when **the prohibition** that applies to the dough causes it to be considered **significant** although it is a very small quantity, **it combines** with the first piece of food. Together they are the size of an egg-bulk, which is able to transmit the ritual impurity of foods. However, **during the rest of the year, when** there is no prohibition that imparts this significance to the dough, **the matter is dependent on** the owner's **particularity; if he is particular about it,** i.e., he does not want the dough to be there, it is considered food rather than part of the bowl, and **it combines** with the other piece of food. However, **if one prefers its** continued **presence** in its current location, **it is** considered **like** part of **the kneading bowl** itself, rather than food.

Abaye's case unfolds as follows. Suppose there is less than a *k'beitza* — an egg's volume — of food in or near the trough. A *k'beitza* is the minimum size for food to be significant for *tumah*, to receive or transmit it. Now imagine a small apple — say, nine-tenths of a *k'beitza* — resting near the trough, and a small piece of dough stuck in one of the cracks touching that apple. The question is: is that dough treated as food in its own right, independent of the trough, such that it combines with the apple to produce a full *k'beitza* of food and the apple becomes *tamei*? Or is it treated as part of the trough, in which case touching it is no different from touching the wood of the trough, and the apple does not become *tamei*?

According to Abaye, the *mishnah* is addressing precisely this question. *B'Pesach de'issuro chashuv mitztaref* — on Pesach, where the dough's forbidden status lends it significance, even a *kezayit* is treated as an independent entity. Since a *kezayit* of *chametz* on Pesach must be removed, it has objective halakhic significance and is therefore considered a separate unit of food. Consequently, it combines with the apple, and the apple becomes *tamei*. *Bish'ar yemot hashanah dekfida talya milta* — during the rest of the year, size does not determine the outcome; what matters is whether one is *makpid* about the dough's presence. *Im makpid alav mitztaref* — if one objects to the dough and wishes to remove it, it is treated as an independent food item and combines with the apple. *Im rotzeh b'kiyumo harei hu ka'areivah* — if one wishes it to remain, it is part of the trough and does not combine.

This is a conceptually important distinction. On Pesach, an objective size-based criterion — mirroring the *kezayit* threshold for *chametz* removal — governs whether the dough counts as a separate food unit. During the rest of the year, the criterion is subjective: one's own attitude toward the dough's presence. Abaye is careful to note that the question here is not whether the dough constitutes an obstruction to the vessel's immersion in a *mikveh*, but rather the inverse: whether the dough is sufficiently separate to be treated as an independent food item that can unite with food outside the vessel. Whether the criterion of *asuy l'chazeik* — reinforcing the trough — interacts with these *tziruf* criteria is a question the *gemara* does not fully resolve, but the subjective standard of *rotzeh* or *eino rotzeh* governs during the rest of the year.

### Rava's Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: מִי קָתָנֵי ״מִצְטָרֵף״? וְהָא ״חוֹצֵץ״ קָתָנֵי. אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: וְכֵן לְהַעֲלוֹת טׇהֳרָה לַעֲרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava strongly objects to this: Was** the language **taught** in the mishna: **Combines? Didn't** the mishna **teach** that **it interposes?** Abaye's explanation does not account for this term. **Rather, Rava said** that the mishna should be understood as saying: **And so too** with regard to **purifying the kneading bowl** via immersion.

Rava challenges Abaye's reading: *mi katani mitztaref?* — does the *mishnah* actually speak of *tziruf*, of combining food items? The *mishnah* speaks of *v'hachotzetz katani* — *chatzitzah*, obstruction. The operative concept in the *mishnah* is whether the dough constitutes an obstruction, not whether it combines with something else. Rava therefore offers a different reading: *v'chen l'harot taharah la'areivah* — the *mishnah* is indeed addressing *chatzitzah*, but in the context most naturally associated with that word: immersing the trough in a *mikveh* to purify it. The case is that the trough became *tamei* and one wishes to immerse it. *B'Pesach de'issuro chashuv chotzetz* — on Pesach, where the dough has significance by virtue of its forbidden status, even a *kezayit* constitutes a *chatzitzah* and prevents the *tvilah* from being effective. *Bish'ar yemot hashanah dekfida talya milta* — during the rest of the year, whether the dough is a *chatzitzah* depends on whether one objects to its presence or wishes it to remain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי, כְּגוֹן דְּאִיטַּמִּי הָךְ עֲרֵיבָה, וּבָעֵי לְאַטְבּוֹלֵי. בְּפֶסַח, דְּאִיסּוּרוֹ חָשׁוּב — חוֹצֵץ, וְלָא סָלְקָא לַהּ טְבִילָה. בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה בִּקְפִידָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא. אִי מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — חוֹצֵץ, וְאִם רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא כַּעֲרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What are the circumstances** of the mishna's case? It is a case **where the kneading bowl became ritually impure, and** one **wishes to immerse it. During Passover, when the prohibition** of an olive-bulk of leaven causes it to be considered **significant, it interposes** between the water and the kneading bowl, **and the immersion is ineffective.** However, **during the rest of the year, the matter depends upon** whether or not the owner is **particular** about it. **If he is particular about** the dough and wishes to remove it, **it interposes** between the water and the bowl. However, **if** the owner **desires it to be present, it is** considered **like** part of the **kneading bowl** itself, and it does not interpose between the water and the bowl.

This is, Rava argues, the more natural and straightforward reading of the *mishnah*. The question of *chatzitzah* in the context of *mikveh* immersion is precisely what one would expect when the *mishnah* employs that terminology.

### Excluded Middle

The *mishnah*'s formulation raises a notable question about the excluded middle. The *mishnah* states two poles: *makpid* — one objects and wishes the dough removed — and *rotzeh b'kiyumo* — one affirmatively desires it to remain. What about the vast middle ground in which one simply does not care one way or the other? One neither objects to the dough's presence nor positively wishes it to stay.

This question has significant practical resonance in the broader laws of *chatzitzah*. The standard formulation is *makpid* versus *eino makpid* — once one does not object, does not care whether the substance is present, it does not constitute a *chatzitzah*. Some *poskim*, however, demand a higher threshold: that the substance must be positively desired in place, not merely tolerated, in order to be considered part of the body rather than an obstruction. This distinction is relevant, for instance, to a wedding band that one leaves on not because one actively wants it there but because one fears losing it if removed. In practice, the *lechatchilah* standard for *tevila* demands the removal of all potential *chatzitzot*, even those that by any rigorous analysis clearly do not qualify — precisely because one might be *makpid* about them without realizing it.

### Rav Pappa's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מִי קָתָנֵי ״וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן טׇהֳרָה״? הָא ״לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן לְהוֹרִיד טוּמְאָה לַעֲרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa strongly objects to this: Was** the language **taught** in the mishna: **And similarly with regard to ritual purity? Didn't** the mishna **teach: And similarly with regard to ritual impurity? Rather, Rav Pappa said** the mishna should be understood as saying: **And similarly with regard to the transfer of ritual impurity to the kneading bowl** via this dough.

Rava's reading — that the *mishnah* concerns purification through *mikveh* immersion — is challenged by Rav Pappa: *mi katani v'chen le'inyan taharah?* — does the *mishnah* say the same applies to *taharah*? It does not. The *mishnah* says the same applies to *tumah* — becoming *tamei*, not achieving *taharah*. If the issue were *mikveh* immersion, one would expect the *mishnah* to speak of *taharah*, not *tumah*.

### Sheretz Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי, כְּגוֹן דְּנָגַע שֶׁרֶץ בְּהַאי בָּצֵק. בְּפֶסַח דְּאִיסּוּרוֹ חָשׁוּב — חוֹצֵץ, וְלָא נָחֲתָה לַהּ טוּמְאָה. בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה דְּבִקְפִידָא תַּלְיָא, אִם מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — חוֹצֵץ, אִם רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא כַּעֲרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What are the circumstances** of the mishna's case? It is a case **where** the carcass of **a creeping animal touched this dough. During Passover, when its prohibition** causes the dough to be considered **significant, it interposes** between the bowl and the creeping animal, and **ritual impurity does not descend to the kneading bowl,** i.e., the kneading bowl does not become impure. **During the rest of the year, when it depends** upon whether one is **particular** about the presence of the dough, **if he is particular about it, it interposes** between the bowl and the creeping animal and prevents the bowl from becoming impure. However, **if he desires it to be present, it is** considered **like** it is part of **the kneading bowl** itself. Therefore, the entire bowl becomes ritually impure when the carcass of the creeping animal touches the dough.

Rav Pappa therefore offers his own reading: *v'chen le'inyan lehorid tumah la'areivah* — the question is whether *tumah* descends to the trough itself. The case is that a *sheretz* — a dead creeping insect, a primary source of *tumah* — comes into contact not with the trough directly, but only with the piece of dough lodged in the crack. If the dough is considered part of the trough, then contact with the dough is tantamount to contact with the trough, and the trough becomes *tamei*. If the dough is a *chatzitzah* — an independent unit, not part of the trough — then the *sheretz*'s contact with the dough does not render the trough *tamei*. This is an issue of *tumah* descending to the vessel, exactly as the *mishnah* states. On Pesach, where a *kezayit* of dough has objective significance and must be removed, it is treated as independent of the trough, and so the *sheretz*'s contact with the dough does not render the trough *tamei*. During the rest of the year, the determination depends on whether one is *makpid* or *rotzeh b'kiyumo*. Rav Pappa's reading thus preserves both the *mishnah*'s language of *tumah* and the structure of the *chatzitzah* discussion, while avoiding Abaye's *tziruf* framework and Rava's *taharah* framework.

## Batzek HaCherish

### Hardened Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּצֵק הַחֵרֵשׁ, אִם יֵשׁ כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ שֶׁהֶחְמִיץ — הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Deaf dough** is dough for which it is difficult to determine if it has been leavened. It is comparable to a deaf-mute, who cannot communicate. **If there is** dough **similar to it** in that water was added to both at the same time, **which became leavened,** the deaf dough **is prohibited.** Although it has not shown external signs of becoming leavened, it can be presumed that the deaf dough has also become leavened.

The next *mishnah* introduces *batzek hacherish* — dough that has hardened to such a degree that normal observation of fermentation is impossible. We have until now assumed that the determination of whether dough has become *chametz* is made by observation: one looks for visible expansion, cracks on the surface, and other signs of fermentation. What happens when observation is impossible — when the dough has crusted over so thoroughly that none of these signs can be assessed? The term itself is debated: *Rashi* connects it to *cheresh*, a mute or deaf person — one cannot communicate with it, cannot assess its status — while another explanation derives it from *cheres*, pottery, suggesting the dough has become hard as ceramic.

### Comparison Standard

The *mishnah* provides a practical solution: *im yesh kayotzei bo she-hichmitz harei zeh assur* — if there are comparable doughs that were prepared at the same time and those doughs have fermented, then this hardened dough is presumed to have fermented as well and is forbidden. This marks a significant methodological shift: rather than direct observation of the dough itself, one infers its status from the behavior of analogous doughs prepared under the same conditions and at the same time.

### Eighteen-Minute Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִם אֵין שָׁם כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ מַהוּ? אֲמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ אָדָם מִמִּגְדַּל נוּנַיָּא לִטְבֶרְיָא מִיל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara seeks to clarify the ruling of the mishna: **If there is no** dough **similar to it, what is** the *halakha*? **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** According to the Sages, leavening occurs in the time it takes **a person** to **walk** the distance **from Migdal Nunaya** to **Tiberias,** which is **a *mil*,** two thousand cubits.

The *gemara* then raises the next case: *im ein sham kayotzei bo mahu* — what if there are no comparable doughs available for reference? One has no basis for comparison. The *gemara* answers in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, as transmitted by Rabbi Abba: *kedei she-yeilech adam mi-Migdal Nunia le-Tverya mil* — one goes by the time it takes a person to walk from Migdal Nunia to Tiberias, which is one *mil*. If that amount of time has elapsed since the dough was last worked, one assumes fermentation has occurred.

### Mil Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא מִיל! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְּמִיל כְּמִמִּגְדַּל נוּנַיָּא וְעַד טְבֶרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about this formulation: Why is it necessary to mention the distance between these two places? **Let us say** that leavening begins after the time it takes a person to walk a ***mil*.** The Gemara answers: **This** statement incidentally **teaches us that the length of a *mil*** is the distance **from Migdal Nunaya to Tiberias.**

The *gemara* asks: why give the reference of Migdal Nunia to Tiberias? Why not simply say one *mil*? The answer is that it teaches us the concrete measure of a *mil*: *de-shiur de-mil ke-Migdal Nunia le-Tverya* — the distance from Migdal Nunia to Tiberias defines the *mil* as a unit of measurement. Based on other *gemarot*, the minimum time assumed for walking one *mil* is eighteen minutes. A modern mile takes roughly twenty minutes on flat paved roads, and a *mil*, being approximately one kilometer, should theoretically take somewhat less. The eighteen-minute figure appears to presuppose a slower pace — roughly a half-hour mile — which may reflect variations in terrain, physical condition, or the gait of different walkers, including, as the *gemara* elsewhere notes, a pregnant woman.

What is historically significant here is the move from observation to quantification. The entire *gemara* up to this point has assumed that *chametz* is determined by direct observation of fermentation. This *gemara* introduces a temporal standard — eighteen minutes — as a substitute when observation is unavailable. That standard has subsequently been codified as the definitive outer limit for leaving dough unattended during the Pesach baking process, regardless of whether the original context of *batzek hacherish* fully accounts for all the practical circumstances.

## Four-Mil Standard

### Kneading Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: לְגַבָּל וְלִתְפִלָּה וְלִנְטִילַת יָדַיִם — אַרְבָּעָה מִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: With regard to a kneader,** i.e., one who kneads dough for others and should maintain the ritual purity of the dough; **and** similarly, **with regard to** washing one's hands for **prayer** (*Arukh*), **and with regard to washing hands** before eating, one must search either for a ritual bath to immerse the vessel he is using to knead the dough, or for water to purify his hands, provided that water is accessible within the time it takes to walk **four *mil*,** eight thousand cubits.

The mention of the *mil* as a measure prompts a broader discussion. Rav Abbahu cites Resh Lakish: *legabul u'letfilla u'li'netilat yadayim arba'ah mil* — for kneading dough *betaharah*, for prayer, and for washing hands before eating, the operative standard is four *mil*. The case of kneading dough *betaharah* envisions a traveler who normally prepares his dough in a state of *taharah* but is on the road without access to *tahor* vessels. If a baker who works *betaharah* is located within four *mil* ahead in the direction of travel — approximately seventy-two minutes of walking — one must wait until reaching that baker rather than preparing the dough in a state of *tumah*. If the baker is further away than four *mil*, one need not wait indefinitely and may prepare the dough where one is.

### Prayer Timing

The four-*mil* standard applies similarly to prayer. If one is traveling and it is time to *daven*, but a *beit knesset* lies within four *mil* ahead, one must wait and *daven* there rather than praying on the side of the road. *Rashi* frames this in terms of the significance of the *beit knesset* as a location, while *Tosafot* understands the concern as reaching a *minyan* — if a *minyan* is available within four *mil*, one must wait for it. These two readings reflect a broader question in the *gemara* in Berakhot about the relative halakhic weight of *davening* in a *beit knesset* as opposed to *davening* with a *minyan*.

### Hand Washing

The same four-*mil* standard governs *netilat yadayim* before eating. A traveler who is hungry but has no access to water for washing — the familiar predicament of a long car journey with packed food — must wait for the next opportunity to wash hands if it lies within four *mil* in the direction of travel. If the nearest water source is more than four *mil* away, one need not wait. Since the measure is fundamentally one of time and effort rather than bare distance, the practical application scales with how far one has to go.

### Leather Processing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַיְיבוּ אַמְרַהּ, וְאַרְבְּעָה אֲמַר בַּהּ, וַחֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ עִבּוּד. דִּתְנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעִיבְּדָן, אוֹ שֶׁהִילֵּךְ בָּהֶן כְּדֵי עֲבוֹדָה — טְהוֹרִין, חוּץ מֵעוֹר הָאָדָם. וְכַמָּה כְּדֵי עֲבוֹדָה? אָמַר רַבִּי (אִינְיָיא) אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כְּדֵי הִילּוּךְ אַרְבָּעָה מִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Ayvu said** this *halakha*, **and he said it** about **four** cases, as opposed to the three cases mentioned previously. **And one of them** pertained to the **tanning** of hides, which lasts for the time that it takes a person to walk four *mil*. **As we learned** in a mishna: **And all** types of thin, soft hides, which have the status of flesh with regard to ritual impurity because their texture is similar to flesh, **that were tanned** in order to be made into leather, **or that one trod upon** for as long as necessary **for** the **leatherworking** process, **are ritually pure.** They are considered to be leather and are no longer considered like the flesh of the animal, **except for** the **skin of a human** corpse, which always remains ritually impure. The Gemara asks: **How much** time must one tread upon **a hide for the leatherworking** process? **Rabbi Ayvu said that Rabbi Yannai said:** It is the amount of time it takes to **walk four *mil*.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds that it was actually Aivo, not Rav Abbahu, who transmitted this teaching, and that Aivo cited four matters, not three — the fourth being the *ibud* of hides, the processing of animal skins into leather. This connects back to the earlier *baraita* about *areivas ha'avdanim*. Certain animals have hides that are soft enough to be considered edible and therefore food-like, capable of receiving *tumah* as *ochlin*. Once such a hide is properly processed into leather, it loses its food status and can no longer become *tamei*. The *mishnah* states: *v'chullan she'ibdan o shehilach bahen kedei avodah tehorim* — all such hides, if one processed them chemically or walked on them for the duration required for leather-working, become *tehorim* and are no longer subject to *tum'at ochlin*. *Chutz me'or ha'adam* — with the exception of human skin, which remains exceptional for reasons the *mishnah* does not elaborate. Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yannai specifies: *kedei hiluch arba mil* — the walking required to process the leather is four *mil*.

### Direction Qualifier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְפָנָיו, אֲבָל לְאַחֲרָיו — אֲפִילּוּ מִיל אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר. אָמַר רַב אַחָא, וּמִינַּהּ: מִיל — הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר, הָא פָּחוֹת מִמִּיל — חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: They taught** that one must search for water to wash one's hands before eating or prayer for the amount of time it takes to walk four *mil* **only** when the water **is before him,** in the direction that he is traveling. **However,** when it is **behind him, he need not return even a *mil*. Rav Aḥa said: From this** statement one may infer that **he** need **not return a *mil*, but he must return less than one *mil*** in order to obtain water.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina qualifies the entire four-*mil* standard: *lo shanu ela lefanav* — this applies only when the relevant destination lies ahead in the direction one is already traveling. *U'le'achorav afilu mil eino chozer* — but if one has already passed the location, even a *mil* of backtracking is not required. The obligation is to delay forward progress, not to reverse course. The *gemara* draws an implication from this formulation: *mil hu de'eino chozer, ha'pachos mimil chozer* — the fact that even a *mil* of backtracking is not required implies that less than a *mil* would require backtracking. If the nearest *shul* with a *minyan*, or the nearest rest stop with water, lies less than a *mil* behind, one should in principle turn back.

## Challah on Yom Tov

### The Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מַפְרִישִׁין חַלָּה בְּטוּמְאָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How does one separate *ḥalla* in ritual impurity during the Festival** day of Passover? Ordinarily, one may separate ritually pure *ḥalla* from dough and give it to a priest immediately so that he may eat it. Ritually impure *ḥalla* is unfit for a priest and must be burned, yet it is prohibited to bake or burn anything that is not fit to be eaten during the Festival day. However, it is also prohibited to wait and burn it after the Festival day, since it will become leavened in the meantime.

A new *mishnah* poses a challenging practical problem: *keitzad mafrishim challah be'tumah be'Yom Tov?* — how does one separate *challah* when the dough has become *tamei* on *Yom Tov*? The setting is a household preparing *matzot* on *Yom Tov* — baking on *Yom Tov* is permitted. The dough has become *tamei*, perhaps through contact with a *sheretz* that fell into the kneading trough. One must still be *mafrish challah*, but the *challah teme'iah* cannot be given to a *kohen* to eat, nor can it simply be left out, since it will become *chametz*. The obvious solution — burning it — is also unavailable on *Yom Tov*, because burning *challah teme'iah* is fundamentally an act of destruction rather than a constructive use of fire, even if one incidentally benefits from the fuel. Baking it in the oven is similarly prohibited, since one may not bake on *Yom Tov* food that one cannot eat, and *challah teme'iah* is inedible. The situation thus presents a genuine impasse: one cannot burn it, cannot bake it, and cannot simply leave it.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא תִּקְרָא לָהּ שֵׁם עַד שֶׁתֵּאָפֶה. בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: תָּטִיל בְּצוֹנֵן. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** A woman **should not designate it** as *ḥalla* prior to baking; rather, she should refrain from doing so **until it is baked.** In other words, she should wait until she has baked all of the dough, and there is no risk of it becoming leavened. Only then should she separate *ḥalla* from it. The portion of *ḥalla* may then be kept until after the Festival day, when it may be burned. **Ben Beteira says: She should** separate the *ḥalla* before it is baked, and **place** the dough **in cold** water so that it will not become leavened. **Rabbi Yehoshua said:**

Rabbi Eliezer proposes a solution: *lo tikra lah shem ad shete'afeh* — do not designate any loaf as *challah* until after all the loaves are baked. While the loaves are in the oven, none of them has yet been designated as *challah*, and any one of them is theoretically edible. Therefore, the baking of all the loaves is entirely permissible on *Yom Tov* — one is baking potentially edible food. Once the baking is complete, one designates one loaf as *challah*. At that point, the *challah* is already baked and hard; it is no longer at risk of becoming *chametz*, and no prohibited labor remains to be performed.

### Ben Beteira

Ben Beteira offers a different approach: *tateih betzonen* — submerge the dough in cold water. Cold water prevents fermentation, and so one can set aside the raw *challah* dough in cold water and leave it there until *Yom Tov* is over, at which point it can be dealt with normally. The practical difficulty of this approach in a Pesach context — deliberately leaving raw dough outside the oven — is self-evident.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא זֶה הוּא חָמֵץ שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין עָלָיו בְּבַל יֵרָאֶה וּבְבַל יִמָּצֵא. אֶלָּא: מַפְרִישָׁתָהּ וּמַנִּיחָתָהּ עַד הָעֶרֶב, וְאִם הֶחְמִיצָה — הֶחְמִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is not the leavened bread about which we are warned** with the prohibitions: **It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found.** These prohibitions do not apply because the *ḥalla* does not actually belong to the owner of the dough; it is instead considered to be consecrated property. **Rather, she should separate** the *ḥalla* **and leave it until the evening; and if it becomes leavened,** then **it will become leavened,** but this is of no concern.

Rabbi Yehoshua takes a more expansive position: *lo zehu chametz shemuzeharin alav beval yera'eh uval yimatzeh* — this is not the kind of *chametz* regarding which one is warned by the prohibitions of *bal yera'eh* and *bal yimatzeh*. The *challah* has already been sanctified; it belongs to *kohanim*, even if no specific *kohen* has yet received it. It is not the owner's property. One therefore does not transgress by leaving it in one's possession. *Ella mafrisha u'manicha ad ha'erev* — one separates it, leaves it until the evening, and lets it become *chametz* if it will. *V'im hechmitza hechmitza* — and if it becomes *chametz*, so be it. No one transgresses.

### Tovat Hana'ah Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא בְּטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה מָמוֹן. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה אֵינָהּ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to benefit** of **discretion,** i.e., benefit accrued from the option of giving *ḥalla*, *teruma* and tithes to whichever priest or Levite one chooses. The Sages debated whether this benefit has monetary value and would constitute a form of ownership. **Rabbi Eliezer holds: Benefit** of **discretion is** considered to have **monetary** value. Therefore, one owns the *ḥalla* he separates, and he must be careful to prevent it from becoming leavened. However, **Rabbi Yehoshua holds: Benefit** of **discretion is not** considered to have **monetary** value. One does not own the *ḥalla*, and therefore he may allow it to become leavened.

The *gemara* explores the underlying debate. The disputants appear to disagree about *tovat hana'ah* — the right of the one who separated the *challah* to direct it to a *kohen* of his choosing. Even though the *challah* does not belong to the owner in a full proprietary sense, the owner possesses the right to determine which *kohen* receives it. This right — analogous to a government official who does not personally receive a contract but controls who does — may constitute a form of concrete monetary interest in the object. *Rabbi Eliezer savar tovat hana'ah mamon* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that this right of distribution constitutes monetary ownership, and therefore the owner does transgress *bal yera'eh* with respect to the *challah*. *Rabbi Yehoshua savar tovat hana'ah einah mamon* — Rabbi Yehoshua holds that this right does not rise to the level of monetary ownership, and therefore no transgression occurs.

### Ownership Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא סָבְרִי טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה אֵינָהּ מָמוֹן, וְהָכָא בְּ״הוֹאִיל״ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, וְאִי בָּעֵי אִיתְּשִׁיל עֲלַהּ — מָמוֹנֵיהּ הוּא. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, everyone holds** that **benefit** of **discretion is not** considered to have **monetary** value, **and here they disagree** with regard to the principle of: **Since, etc. As Rabbi Eliezer holds: We say** that **since, if he wants, he can ask** to have his separation of the *ḥalla* voided when he regrets having done so, **it is his property.** Even if one does not actually revoke the status of the *ḥalla*, the fact that the potential for such an action exists indicates that he still maintains a form of ownership of this dough. **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds: We do not say** that **since** he could theoretically revoke the status of the *ḥalla*, the dough is considered his. Consequently, he may leave it until the end of the Festival day without being concerned that it will become leavened.

The *gemara* then proposes an alternative framework for the dispute, one that does not require taking a position in the broader *tovat hana'ah* controversy. *De'kulei alma savri tovat hana'ah einah mamon* — perhaps both opinions agree that *tovat hana'ah* is not monetary ownership. The dispute instead turns on the principle of *ho'il* — theoretical possibility. *Rabbi Eliezer savar amrinan ho'il v'i ba'ei mitshil alah mamona hu* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that since one could theoretically reverse the designation of *challah* — the act of separation, like a vow, can be retroactively annulled — the *challah* is still sufficiently in one's possession. The theoretical ability to reclaim it makes it enough of one's property to trigger *bal yera'eh*. *Rabbi Yehoshua savar lo amrinan ho'il* — Rabbi Yehoshua does not accept this reasoning. Theoretical reversibility does not constitute ownership.

The *gemara* notes that of the two frameworks, *tovat hana'ah* intuitively seems the stronger basis for considering the *challah* one's property — the right to direct the benefit is a concrete, ongoing interest. The reason the *gemara* avoids locking in that framework is that the *tovat hana'ah* question recurs in broader contexts throughout the *gemara*, and the current discussion is better served by the more local principle of *ho'il* and retroactive annulment.

## Cooking on Yom Tov

### Baking for Tomorrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הָאוֹפֶה מִיּוֹם טוֹב לְחוֹל, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: לוֹקֶה, רַבָּה אָמַר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the principle: Since, etc., the Gemara cites a related dispute. **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagree with regard to **one who bakes** bread on **a Festival** day **for** use during **the week. Rav Ḥisda said: He is flogged** because he has desecrated the Festival. **Rabba said: He is not flogged.**

The *gemara* now turns to what is arguably the most conceptually central discussion on the *daf*: the permissibility of cooking or baking on *Yom Tov* for a purpose other than *Yom Tov* itself. *Ha'ofeh miyom tov lechol* — one who bakes on *Yom Tov* explicitly stating that the food is intended for the following weekday.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר לוֹקֶה: לָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל וּמִיקַּלְעִי לֵיהּ אוֹרְחִים — חֲזֵי לֵיהּ״. רַבָּה אָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה: אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **Rav Ḥisda said** that **he is flogged** because **we do not say** that **since guests** may **happen** to visit **him,** the bread is **fit for him** on the Festival day itself. **Rabba said** that **he is not flogged** because **we say** that **since** guests may visit him, the bread is considered to have been baked for use on the Festival day itself. Even if guests do not actually come, he has not desecrated the Festival.

Rav Chisda rules: *lokei* — one receives lashes. The Torah permitted cooking and baking on *Yom Tov* solely for the benefit of *Yom Tov*. If one explicitly intends the food for the following day, the cooking is not *le'tzorech Yom Tov* and the prohibition against *melacha* on *Yom Tov* is violated. The hypothetical argument that guests might arrive and benefit from the food on *Yom Tov* itself does not factor into the analysis: what matters is the actual intention, and the actual intention is for the following day.

### Rabbah

Rabbah rules: *ein lokei* — one does not receive lashes. Rabbah's position is based on the principle of *ho'il* — since (*ho'il*), theoretically, if guests were to arrive, this food could be eaten on *Yom Tov* itself. The food is objectively edible on *Yom Tov*; it is present and available. The cooking therefore has the character of being for *Yom Tov* in an objective sense, even if subjectively one intended it for the following day. *Tosafot* adds a qualification to Rabbah's position: if the baking occurred in the very last moments of *Yom Tov* — so close to nightfall that there was literally no time remaining to benefit from the food on *Yom Tov* — then even Rabbah would concede to Rav Chisda, since there is no objective possibility of benefit on *Yom Tov* itself, not merely a subjective lack of intention.

### Yom Tov to Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה לְרַב חִסְדָּא: לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ לָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, הֵיאַךְ אוֹפִין מִיּוֹם טוֹב לְשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם עֵירוּבֵי תַּבְשִׁילִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said to Rav Ḥisda: According to you, who said that we do not say** the principle: **Since, etc., how** is it permitted to **bake** on **a Festival for Shabbat? He said to him:** One is permitted to bake on a Festival for Shabbat **due to the joining of cooked foods [*eiruv tavshilin*]** instituted by the Sages.

Rabbah then challenges Rav Chisda: *hei ofei miyom tov leShabbat* — according to your position, how is it ever permitted to cook on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*? We know that one may do so with an *Eiruv Tavshilin*, but according to Rav Chisda, any cooking not intended for *Yom Tov* itself should be a biblical violation. How does *Eiruv Tavshilin* resolve a *de'oraita* prohibition?

### Shared Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשּׁוּם עֵירוּבֵי תַּבְשִׁילִין שָׁרֵינַן אִיסּוּרָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא צוֹרְכֵי שַׁבָּת נַעֲשִׂין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּגְזַרוּ בֵּיהּ, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ אוֹפִין מִיּוֹם טוֹב אַף לְחוֹל. וְכֵיוָן דְּאַצְרְכוּהּ רַבָּנַן עֵירוּבֵי תַּבְשִׁילִין — אִית לֵיהּ הֶיכֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba responded: **Due to** the practice of **the joining of cooked foods,** which was instituted by the Sages, **may one permit a Torah prohibition?** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: By Torah** law, whatever one **needs** for **Shabbat** may **be done on a Festival,** and the prohibition against performing labor during the Festival does not apply to preparations for Shabbat. **It was the Sages who decreed** that one may not bake on a Festival for Shabbat, as **a decree lest** people **say** that **one may bake on the Festival even for** use during **the week. And since the Sages required a joining of cooked foods, one has a conspicuous marker** reminding him that baking on the Festival for Shabbat is permitted but baking on the Festival for a weekday is prohibited.

Rav Chisda responds by citing Rav Yitzchak: *mi de'oraita tsorkhei Shabbat na'asin b'Yom Tov* — biblically, cooking on *Yom Tov* for a *Shabbat* that immediately follows is permitted. When *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* are contiguous, they share a single *kedushah*; the *Shabbat* is, in a sense, an extension of the *Yom Tov*, and cooking for it is tantamount to cooking for the *Yom Tov* itself. One could not, by contrast, cook on a *Yom Tov* in Tishri for *Shabbat* in Tishri two weeks later; contiguity is essential. The Rabbis added a prohibition against cooking from *Yom Tov* to *Shabbat* without preparation, as a *gezeirah* lest people conclude that one may also cook from *Yom Tov* for an ordinary weekday — *gezeirah shema yomru ofin miyom tov af lechol*. *Eiruv Tavshilin* was then instituted as a rabbinic mechanism to permit what the Torah allows while providing a *hekeira* — a recognizable signal — that makes clear to all that the general rule forbids cooking from *Yom Tov* for the weekday, and that cooking for *Shabbat* is only possible under specific conditions. The *Eiruv Tavshilin* thus navigates between biblical permission and rabbinic restriction.

### Sick Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: בְּהֵמָה הַמְסוּכֶּנֶת, לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לֶאֱכוֹל הֵימֶנָּה כְּזַיִת צָלִי מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **raised an objection to** Rav Ḥisda's opinion from a *baraita*: If one wishes to slaughter **a dangerously** ill **animal** before it dies on its own and becomes prohibited for consumption, **he may not slaughter** it on a Festival day **unless** there is enough time so that **he can eat an olive-bulk of it roasted while it is still day.**

Rabbah then presses Rav Chisda with another challenge: *behema hamesukhenet* — a sick animal that is about to die. The rule is *lo yishchot ela k'dei sheyochal heimena k'zayit tzali mib'od yom* — one may only slaughter such an animal on *Yom Tov* if there is enough time to roast a *kezayit* of its meat and eat it before the day ends. Roasting is specified because it is the fastest cooking method and requires no *kashering* through salting, since the blood drips out during the roasting process. The implicit rationale for the condition is that the slaughter is only *le'tzorech Yom Tov* — for the purpose of *Yom Tov* — if there is time to actually benefit from it on *Yom Tov*. Rabbah argues that this supports his position: *bishlama ledidi de'amri ho'il* — according to me, who says *ho'il* — since in principle you could eat from it on *Yom Tov*, the slaughter is permitted even if in practice you do not intend to eat the meat. The *mishnah* requires only that there be enough time, not that one actually intend to eat it.

### Financial Loss Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יָכוֹל לֶאֱכוֹל״ — אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא בָּעֵי לְמֵיכַל. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמְרִי ״הוֹאִיל״: ״הוֹאִיל וְאִי בָּעֵי לְמֵיכַל מָצֵי אָכֵיל״ — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי יִשְׁחוֹט. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, דְּאָמְרַתְּ לָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, אַמַּאי יִשְׁחוֹט?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reads this statement precisely: The *baraita* stipulates that the slaughter is permitted if **one can eat** the meat while it is still day, **although he is not required to** actually **eat** the meat. **Granted, according to my** position, **that I said** that one may rely on the principle: **Since, etc.,** this ruling is reasonable. **Since if one wants to eat he may eat, due to that** reason alone **he may slaughter** the animal. **But** according to **you, who** said **that we do not say** the principle of: **Since, etc., why may he slaughter** such an animal?

Rav Chisda objects: how can this be permitted according to his own view? If one is not planning to eat the meat, then the slaughter is not for the purpose of *Yom Tov*, and it should constitute a biblical violation. Financial loss — however significant — does not override a *de'oraita* prohibition. *Umishum hefsed mamon sharina issur d'oraita?* The question is stark.

### Financial Loss Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם הֶפְסֵד מָמוֹנוֹ. וּמִשּׁוּם הֶפְסֵד מָמוֹנוֹ שָׁרֵינַן אִיסּוּרָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, אִין: מִשּׁוּם הֶפְסֵד מָמוֹנוֹ — גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת, וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִכְזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּלֹא שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him:** He is permitted to slaughter the animal **due to** the **monetary loss** that would be incurred by not slaughtering it, and not due to the principle of: Since, etc. Rabba asked rhetorically: **Will we permit a Torah prohibition due to monetary loss? Rav Ḥisda said to him: Yes,** although this factor on its own does not mitigate the prohibition. Rather, **due to** the **monetary loss** that would otherwise be incurred, **one decided to eat an olive-bulk** of the meat, although he does not need to. **And** since **it is impossible** to eat even **an olive-bulk of meat without slaughtering** the animal, one is permitted to slaughter the animal.

Rabbah resolves the difficulty in a way that fully preserves Rav Chisda's framework rather than overriding it. Because the alternative is the complete financial loss of the animal — a thousand-dollar cow dying and becoming *neveilah* — the owner is strongly motivated to eat some of the meat on *Yom Tov*. We therefore tell the owner: you may slaughter the animal *on condition* that you genuinely commit to eating a *kezayit* of it today. *Gamar belibo le'echol kezayit v'i efshar li'kzayit basar belo shechita* — if he sincerely resolves to eat a *kezayit* today, then the slaughter of the whole animal is a necessary precondition for eating that *kezayit*, and the entire *shechita* is considered *le'tzorech Yom Tov*. Financial loss does not override the Torah — it merely provides the practical incentive that makes genuine intention credible and forthcoming. The *de'oraita* prohibition is not overwritten; rather, the owner navigates through it by genuinely forming the requisite intention.

This debate between Rav Chisda and Rabbah — one requiring actual intention for *Yom Tov* benefit, the other permitting *melacha* wherever objective benefit on *Yom Tov* is theoretically possible — is the conceptual pivot around which much of the later *Yom Tov* cooking discussion turns. The *gemara* will continue to develop its implications in the *daf* that follows.
