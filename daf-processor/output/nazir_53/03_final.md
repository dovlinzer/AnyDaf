# Nazir 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Skull & Backbone

### Individual Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעְיָא לְהוּ: רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת מִן שִׁדְרָה וְגוּלְגּוֹלֶת, מַאי? כִּי קָתָנֵי חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת — הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מִשְּׁאָר אֵבָרָיו, אֲבָל מִן שִׁדְרָה וְגוּלְגּוֹלֶת דַּחֲמִירִי — אֲפִילּוּ רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת. אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** A dilemma was raised before the Sages: **With regard to a quarter-*kav* of bones from a spine and a skull, what** is the *halakha*? Does a nazirite shave for them? The Gemara explains the two sides of the dilemma: **When** the mishna **teaches** that one shaves for **a half-*kav* of bones,** does this apply only **when** they **are from his other limbs, but** in the case of bones **from** the **spine and skull, which are stringent,** as they impart ritual impurity on their own, **even a quarter-***kav* **of bones** imparts impurity? **Or perhaps** it **is no different.**

We are in the middle of a discussion concerning the portion of the *mishnah* that focuses on the skull and the backbone — *shidra v'gulgollet*. The first question to establish was whether it is only the two of them combined that are *metamei b'ohel*, or whether each one alone suffices. All the evidence pointed to each alone, and that is the way the Rambam rules.

### Quantity Requirements

The next question concerns cases where one does not have the whole skull or backbone, but has a sufficient quantity of them. We have already learned that a quarter of a *kav* — *revi'it kav* — of the majority of bones by number (*rov minyan*) or by structure (*rov binyan*) causes *tuma b'ohel*, and that double that amount, a half-*kav*, is the threshold for *nazir megaleach*: the *nazir* must shave his head and reverse the entire process. These two cases thus present a different threshold for the *nazir* to shave versus what causes *tuma b'ohel* in the ordinary sense.

The assumption, then, is that the same structure applies to *shidra v'gulgollet*: a *revi'it kav* drawn from the skull or backbone — even if one does not have their entirety — causes *tumas ohel*, and a half-*kav* would be the threshold for *nazir megaleach*.

### Rami bar Chama's Q

Against this assumption, Rami bar Chama raised the following question: since *shidra v'gulgollet* are far weightier than ordinary bones — there are only two of them and each one alone represents the entire body — perhaps even a smaller quantity of them should trigger *nazir megaleach*. That is, perhaps for *nazir megaleach* one does not need the standard half-*kav*; perhaps a *revi'it kav* — the same amount that normally causes *tuma* from ordinary bones — would suffice. This question remains before us as we enter the top of 53a.

## Shammai's Position

### Shammai: One Bone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: עֶצֶם אֶחָד מִן שִׁדְרָה אוֹ מִן גּוּלְגּוֹלֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from the following: **Shammai says** that **one bone from the spine or from the skull** imparts ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* brings a potential proof: *ta shema* — come and hear. Shammai says: *etzem echad min hashidra u'min hagulgollet* — even one bone from the backbone or from the skull causes *tuma* and triggers the *nazir*'s obligation to shave. The question is whether we can extrapolate from Shammai's ruling what the opposing opinion holds.

### Cannot Infer Opposition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי שַׁמַּאי דְּמַחְמִיר טְפֵי. לִיפְשׁוֹט מִינַּהּ: טַעְמָא דְּשַׁמַּאי, דְּמַחְמִיר, הָא לְרַבָּנַן — עַד דְּאִיכָּא חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת! דִּילְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּשַׁמַּאי אֶלָּא בְּעֶצֶם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בְּרוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת — אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Shammai is different, as he is very stringent,** and therefore nothing at all can be inferred from his opinion with regard to that of the Rabbis. The Gemara counters: If so, **let us resolve** the problem **in** the opposite manner: **The reasoning** here is that **of Shammai, who is** particularly **stringent.** From this it may be inferred that, **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** one is not rendered impure and a nazirite is not required to shave **unless there is a half-*kav* of bones** from the spine and skull. The Gemara rejects this claim as well: **Perhaps** the argument is not that extreme after all, and **the Rabbis disagree with Shammai only with regard to** whether **one bone** from the spine or from the skull imparts impurity. **However, with regard to a quarter-***kav* **of bones, even the Rabbis** might **concede** that it renders people and items ritually impure, and a nazirite must shave for it.

The *Gemara* dismisses this attempt: *shani Shammai d'machmir tfei* — Shammai is simply exceptionally stringent. From his stringency in one direction, one cannot infer the precise contours of the opposing view. The opposing position might hold, first, that you need the whole skull and backbone, and second, that if you have a certain quantity, you need a standard half-*kav* — not conceding any reduced threshold for *shidra v'gulgollet*. Just because Shammai is so strict does not mean the other school agrees to his degree of stringency; they might simply apply the ordinary rule of a half-*kav*.

The *Gemara* then attempts to reason in the other direction: perhaps Shammai's strictness implies that the opposing opinion is as lenient as possible — requiring the full half-*kav*. But the *Gemara* rejects this too: *dilma ad kan lo pligi rabanan alei d'Shammai ela b'etzem echad aval b'rov atzamot revi'it kav afilu rabanan modei* — perhaps the Rabbis disagree with Shammai only regarding a single bone being sufficient, but would agree that a *revi'it kav* of the majority of bones suffices. We simply cannot know.

The same point was made previously in a different discussion: from Shammai's position alone, one cannot infer how wide the *machloket* is. Shammai is very strict — even one bone from the skull or backbone. The opposing view might require both skull and backbone together, or might require skull or backbone individually but demand a full half-*kav* of their substance, or might concede that a *revi'it kav* suffices. All remain open. The question of whether, for the *tuma* relevant to the *nazir*, a half-*kav* or a *revi'it kav* from *shidra v'gulgollet* is required therefore remains unresolved. The broader lesson is that from one position in a *machloket* one cannot infer how wide that *machloket* extends.

## Historical Shiurim

### First Elders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: זְקֵנִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, מִקְצָתָן הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת וַחֲצִי לוֹג דָּם — לַכֹּל. רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, וּרְבִיעִית דָּם — לֹא לַכֹּל. וּמִקְצָתָן הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אַף רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת וּרְבִיעִית דָּם — לַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said** that **some of the early Elders would say: A half-*kav* of bones and a half-*log* of blood** impart ritual impurity **in all** forms. Their impurity applies by Torah law, and therefore they impart impurity in a tent. But **a quarter-***kav* **of bones and a quarter-***log* **of blood,** they do **not** impart impurity **in all** forms, i.e., they do not impart impurity in a tent. **And some of** these Elders **would say** that **even a quarter-***kav* **of bones and a quarter-***log* **of blood** impart impurity **in all** forms. This was the dispute of earlier generations.

Having established this unresolved question, the *Gemara* introduces a statement by Rabbi Eliezer that provides historical and conceptual background to the entire doubling of the *shiur* for *nazir megaleach*. The doubling is admittedly quite strange: why should the threshold for the *nazir* shaving be specifically double the amount that causes ordinary *tumas ohel*? If this were a universal rule it would be one thing — but why is it only with bones and blood that the *shiur* for *nazir megaleach* is double the ordinary *shiur* for *tumas ohel*?

Rabbi Eliezer explains: *zekeinim ha-rishonim* — the original early elders — were themselves divided. One group among them held *chatzi kav atzamot ve-chatzi log dam le-kol*: a half-*kav* of bones and a half-*log* of blood was the universal *shiur* for all purposes — *tuma*, *teruma*, *nezirut*, *Pesach*, *kodashim*. This was their single undifferentiated *shiur* for *tumas ohel*. The quarter-*kav* and quarter-*log* played no role in their system.

### Second Elders

A second group among the early elders took the opposite position: *af rova atzamot ve-revi'it dam le-kol* — the quarter-*kav* of bones and the quarter-*log* of blood suffice for everything. They likewise drew no distinction between *nazir*, *oseh Pesach*, *teruma*, and *kodashim*. The debate between these two groups was simply: half-*kav* or quarter-*kav*, half-*log* or quarter-*log* — with no internal differentiation by category. One notes that the existence of this debate already complicates any simple claim that all *shiurim* are *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, a point the *Gemara* itself will address.

### Final Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית דִּין שֶׁלְּאַחֲרֵיהֶם אָמְרוּ: חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת וַחֲצִי לוֹג דָּם — לַכֹּל, רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת וּרְבִיעִית דָּם — לִתְרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים. אֲבָל לֹא לְנָזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח.
>
> **Translation:** The **court that followed them said: A half-*kav* of bones and a half-*log* of blood** impart ritual impurity **in all** forms. **A quarter-***kav* **of bones and a quarter-***log* **of blood** impart impurity only **with regard to *teruma* and offerings,** i.e., the Sages decreed that they impart impurity in a tent to invalidate *teruma* and offerings **but not with regard to a nazirite.** A nazirite is not required to shave or bring offerings for impurity after contact with a quarter-*kav* of bones or a quarter-*log* of blood. **And** similarly, **one who performs** the ritual of **the Paschal offering** may proceed despite the fact that he came into contact with this amount of blood or bones, as the Sages did not apply this decree in cases where one's impurity precludes the performance of a mitzva whose neglect is punishable by *karet*.

The *beit din* that came after them — *beit din shel achareihem* — established the following ruling: *chatzi kav atzamot ve-chatzi log dam le-kol* — a half-*kav* of bones and a half-*log* of blood is the *shiur* for all purposes. This is because if one has double the standard *shiur*, obviously everyone is rendered *tamei* under any category. But — and this is the essential point that must be read alongside the first part — *rova atzamot ve-revi'it dam le-teruma ve-kodashim aval lo le-Nazir ve-oseh Pesach*: the quarter-*kav* of bones and the quarter-*log* of blood constitute *tuma* only with respect to *teruma* and *kodashim*, not for the purposes of the *nazir* (triggering the obligation to shave and bring *korbanot*) and not for the *oseh Pesach* (affecting one's ability to bring the Passover offering).

### Nazirite vs Terumah

The practical upshot is this: if one has only the quarter-*kav* or quarter-*log*, one is considered *tamei met* for the purposes of *teruma* and *kodashim* — one would need to become *tahor* — but this would not destroy one's *nezirut* and force the full shaving and *korban* process. Similarly, it would not prevent one from bringing a *korban Pesach* — though the *Gemara* does not spell out explicitly whether such a person would then be permitted to eat the *korban Pesach*.

The most natural analytical framework for this distinction would be to say that the half-*kav* is the *de-oraita* threshold, while the quarter-*kav* is a *mi-de-rabbanan* stringency — one we apply for *teruma* and *kodashim* but do not apply when the consequences are particularly weighty: we would not on account of a merely *de-rabbanan* status of *tamei* prevent someone from bringing a *korban Pesach*, nor would we force a *nazir* to bring a *korban* he is not truly *de-oraita* obligated to bring. This exact structure was applied earlier on 42b in the context of *tumat chiburin*, where Rav Yannai said: *lo amru tuma be-chiburin ela le-teruma ve-kodashim, aval le-Nazir u-le-oseh Pesach lo*. The *Gemara* there also drew the same line between *teruma* and *kodashim* on one side, and *nazir* and *oseh Pesach* on the other — suggesting a *de-oraita*/*de-rabbanan* framework. However, that is not where our *Gemara* ultimately lands.

## Third Opinion Status

### Not a Compromise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי אֵין הַכְרָעַת שְׁלִישִׁית מַכְרַעַת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about the ruling of *halakha* in this case. **Now** consider, there is a principle that **the decision** of the **third** opinion is **not** considered **a decision.**

The *Gemara* raises a pointed challenge: *michdi ein hachra'ah shelishit machra'at* — a third opinion does not qualify as a compromise position that decides the *halacha*. What does this mean?

When a third opinion occupies the middle ground between two extremes, we might be inclined to rule according to it — we treat it as a *hakhra'ah*, a deciding compromise. But that inclination applies only when the third opinion is genuinely a midpoint within the same conceptual universe as the other two. If the third opinion is not really a compromise but rather introduces an entirely new concept that neither of the first two positions acknowledges as a legitimate axis of distinction, then it is simply a third independent view — not a *hakhra'ah*. In that case, the three opinions are simply three equal positions, and the fact that one appears numerically "in the middle" does not give it any special decisional weight.

Here: the first group of elders said the *shiur* is a half-*kav*, full stop, for everything. The second group said the *shiur* is a quarter-*kav*, full stop, for everything. Neither group drew any distinction between *nazir* and *oseh Pesach* on the one hand and *teruma* and *kodashim* on the other. The third position — the *beit din shel achareihem* — says: for *nazir* and *oseh Pesach* the *shiur* is half-*kav*, for *teruma* and *kodashim* the *shiur* is quarter-*kav*. This is not a compromise; it is an entirely new conceptual category that the first two groups never acknowledged. To use an analogy: if one party insists on living in New York and the other insists on Los Angeles, declaring that they will live in Ohio is not a *hakhra'ah* — it is a completely independent position of its own. Kansas City, by contrast, would be a genuine *hakhra'ah*.

Since both earlier groups spoke in absolute, undifferentiated terms — half-*kav* for everything, or quarter-*kav* for everything — the final *beit din*'s categorical distinction between types of *tuma* purposes is not a compromise between them. It is a third opinion that neither side prepared the conceptual ground for. And precisely because it is not a *hakhra'ah*, the question becomes: on what authority do we adopt it? Why does this third position carry the day?

### Prophetic Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי, מִפִּי שְׁמוּעָה אָמְרוּ: מִפִּי חַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi said:** This ruling was not stated as a compromise. Rather, **they said** it **from tradition, from Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi,** the last of the prophets.

The *Gemara* answers through a statement of Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi: *mipi shemu'ah amru* — this ruling was transmitted as a received tradition. Specifically, it was a tradition going back to *mipi Chagai Zechariah u-Malachi* — Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, the last of the prophets, at the beginning of the Second Temple period. The ruling is not the independent reasoning of the later *beit din*; they had an ancient tradition supporting this very distinction.

The *Gemara* notes — and the Rambam states explicitly — that this is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. Yet it is also worth noting that *mipi shemu'ah* and *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* may not be identical. As the Netziv explains, when the *Gemara* uses the term *halacha*, Rashi often glosses it as *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, but the Netziv argues that it may simply mean an exceedingly ancient tradition whose original rationale has been forgotten — a tradition of great antiquity, but not necessarily one with the formal claim of Sinaitic origin.

One might ask why the tradition is attributed specifically to Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi. The *Gemara* does not explain this attribution. Nor does it fully clarify whether they transmitted this distinction by virtue of their status as *nevi'im* — which would raise complex questions about the role of prophecy in the *halachic* process — or whether they transmitted it simply as the last links in the chain of tradition as members of the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*, in which case the authority is that of transmission rather than of prophetic revelation. These questions are not resolved here. What is clear is that the distinction between *nazir* and *oseh Pesach* on one side and *teruma* and *kodashim* on the other is, in the *Gemara*'s final conclusion, a genuine *ikar halacha* — not merely a rabbinic stringency applied selectively, but a substantive distinction grounded in received tradition.

## Mishnah List Analysis

### Complete List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל אֵלּוּ הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § In its list of sources of impurity for which a nazirite must shave, the mishna taught: **For these** aforementioned sources of ritual impurity **a nazirite shaves.**

The *mishnah* teaches: *al elu nazir megaleach* — over these does the *nazir* shave. The list comprises the backbone, the skull, a limb from a corpse or from a living person bearing sufficient flesh (*basar ka-rauy*), a half-*kav* of bones, a half-*log* of blood, and finally — in a separate category — a bone the size of a barley grain (*etzem ke-se'orah*), which causes *tuma* through touching and carrying (*maga* and *masa*) but not through *ohel*.

### Al Elu Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל אֵלּוּ״ דְּרֵישָׁא לְמַעוֹטֵי עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, דְּעַל מַגָּעוֹ וְעַל מַשָּׂאוֹ — אִין, וְעַל אֲהִילוֹ — לָא. וְ״עַל אֵלּוּ״ דְּסֵיפָא — לְמַעוֹטֵי אֶבֶן הַסָּכוֹכִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that the phrase: **For these, in** the mishna's **first clause,** serves **to exclude a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk. As for touching it and carrying it, yes,** a nazirite shaves, **but for his overlying** it, **no,** he does not shave. **And** the phrase: **For these, in the** mishna's **latter clause,** serves **to exclude an overhanging [*hasekhukhit*] stone.** Although a stone that forms a cover over a corpse imparts impurity in a tent, a nazirite is nevertheless not obligated to shave due to this source of impurity.

The *Gemara* explains that the phrase *al elu* — "over these" — serves an exclusionary function. It comes to exclude the *etzem ke-se'orah* from the *ohel* category: *de'al maga ve'al masa in, ve'al ohel lo* — for touching and carrying, yes; for being under the same *ohel*, no. The *mishnah* is thus organized into two sub-lists: one pertaining to *tumas ohel*, and one pertaining to *tumas maga u-masa*.

What is striking is that the *Gemara* here takes something of a *Rabbi Yishmael*-like approach — *dibra Torah kileshon bnei adam* — to the *mishnah* itself, treating each additional word as carrying halachic weight rather than as ordinary linguistic usage. The *mishnah*'s first *al elu* divides the two internal lists (those causing *tumas ohel* from those causing only *tumas maga*), while the *ve'elu* at the *mishnah*'s close divides it from the *mishnah* that follows on 54a, which lists cases where the *nazir* does *not* shave — including, as we will see, cases such as an overhanging stone (*even ha-sochech*).

## Half-Kav Bones

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת. חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת — אִין, רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת — לָא. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִית בְּהוֹן עֲצָמוֹת כִּשְׂעוֹרָה — תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** § In its list of sources of impurity for which a nazirite must shave, the mishna taught: **And a half-*kav* of bones. A half-*kav* of bones, yes,** a nazirite must shave if he contracts impurity from them; **a quarter-***kav* **of bones, no,** he is not obligated to shave for that. **What are the circumstances? If we say that they contain bones** that are **a barley-grain**-**bulk, let** the *tanna* of the mishna **derive** the *halakha* that it imparts ritual impurity **due to** the fact that it is **a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk.**

The *Gemara* now doubles back to the *mishnah*'s ruling on *chatzi kav atzamot* — a half-*kav* of bones causes *tumas ohel* and *nazir megaleach* — as opposed to *rov atzamot*, the majority of bones, which is not stated here. The *Gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what is the actual scenario? If the bones are all individually the size of a barley grain, then even without a half-*kav*, a single bone suffices — *teipok lei mishum etzem ke-se'orah*, it comes out from the rule of *etzem ke-se'orah*. There is no need for a half-*kav* in that case.

### Dust Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאַקְמַח אַקְמוֹחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the mishna is referring to a situation **where it has been made like flour.** In that case, a half-***kav*** of bones render people and items impure in a tent, although they do not include a bone the volume of a barley-grain-bulk.

The *Gemara* therefore resolves: *ela de-ikmach chamuchei* — the bones must have been ground into dust, pulverized to such a degree that there is not even a single *etzem ke-se'orah* among them. In that case, no individual bone meets the *shiur* for *tumas maga*, yet the aggregate quantity of a half-*kav* still causes *tumas ohel*.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot notes the difficulty here: *tzarich iyun, mai ka parich* — why is the *Gemara* asking this question at all? We are discussing *tumas ohel*, and an *etzem ke-se'orah* does not cause *tumas ohel* regardless. What prompted the *Gemara*'s challenge? Tosafot offers a complicated answer: the *Gemara* understood that the *mishnah*'s ruling on *chatzi kav atzamot* implicitly excludes scenarios involving *less* than a half-*kav* — not merely from causing *tumas ohel*, but from causing *tuma* at all. What scenario would that be? One where the bones have been entirely pulverized to dust. In that condition, less than a half-*kav* of dust would not be *metamei* at all, not even through touching. The half-*kav* threshold, when reduced to dust, is what preserves the *tuma* even without any individual bone meeting the *shiur*.

## Limb w/o Flesh

### Flesh Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל אֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת וְעַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי שֶׁיֵּשׁ עֲלֵיהֶן בָּשָׂר כָּרָאוּי. אֵין עֲלֵיהֶן בָּשָׂר כָּרָאוּי, מַאי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ עֲלֵיהֶן. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that a nazirite shaves **for a limb** severed **from a corpse and for a limb** severed **from a living** person, **upon** either of **which there is a fitting** quantity of **flesh.** The Gemara asks: If **there is not a fitting** quantity of **flesh upon them, what** is the *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The nazirite does not shave for them. Reish Lakish said: The nazirite does shave for them.**

The *mishnah* then turns to *ever min ha-met ve'ever min ha-chai she-yesh alav basar ka-rauy* — a limb from a corpse or from a living person that has sufficient flesh upon it. *Basar ka-rauy* means flesh of an amount sufficient to heal if the limb were reattached to a living person.

The *Gemara* proceeds with what reads as a somewhat unusual reverse order through the *mishnah*, having already addressed the bones, the *rov minyan* and *rov binyan*, and the skull and backbone. Now it turns to the *ever*. Before examining the *ever* with sufficient flesh, the *Gemara* raises a prior question:

### R. Yochanan: No Shaving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ עֲלֵיהֶן, דְּהָא קָתָנֵי בְּרֵישָׁא: עַל אֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת וְעַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי וְכוּ׳ שֶׁיֵּשׁ עֲלֵיהֶן כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר — אִין, אֲבָל אֵין עֲלֵיהֶם — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective opinions. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The nazirite does not shave for them, as** the *tanna* **teaches in the first clause** of the mishna in the list of sources of ritual impurity for which a nazirite must shave: **For a limb** severed **from a corpse and for a limb** severed **from a living** person that contains a fitting quantity of flesh. One can infer from this: **Those upon which there is an olive-bulk of flesh, yes,** he must shave for them, **but** if **there is not** that amount of flesh **upon them, no,** a nazirite need not shave due to them.

*Eiver she-ein alav basar ka-rauy mai* — what is the law when a limb does not have sufficient flesh upon it? The answer is obviously that it does not cause *tumas ohel*. The real question is: does it still cause *tuma* through *maga* — through touching? Could one argue that a limb retains halachic significance as an *ever*, even when lacking sufficient flesh, such that touching it renders the *nazir* obligated to shave?

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *ein ha-nazir megaleach aleihem* — the *nazir* does not shave over them. His reasoning: *de-katani b'reisha* — the first *mishnah* explicitly requires *she-yesh alav ke-zayit basar*, and the implicit reading is that without that amount of flesh, it does not cause *tuma*. Furthermore, if a limb lacking sufficient flesh could cause *tumas maga*, it should have appeared on the *maga* list in the *mishnah*. It is on neither list, and therefore the *nazir* is not *megaleach*.

### Reish Lakish: Tumah via…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: מְגַלֵּחַ. מִדְּלָא קָתָנֵי בְּסֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that **he shaves,** employing the following reasoning: **From** the fact that the mishna **does not teach** the following **in the latter clause,** i.e., the subsequent mishna (54a), in the list of sources of impurity for which a nazirite need not shave: A limb that does not contain a fitting quantity of flesh, one can infer that a nazirite is obligated to shave for a limb of that type.

Reish Lakish disagrees: *megaleach*. He derives this from the fact that the second *mishnah* — the one listing cases where the *nazir* does *not* shave — does not include *ever she-ein alav basar ka-rauy* in its list. Since it is absent from that negative list, Reish Lakish infers that the *nazir does* shave upon touching it — it is a case of *tumas maga*, not *tumas ohel*.

To understand the force of this debate, it is essential to appreciate the structure of the two *mishnayot*. The first *mishnah* (on 49b) lists cases of *nazir megaleach*; its list includes *ever min ha-met ve-ever min ha-chai she-yesh alav basar ka-rauy*, along with the *etzem ke-se'orah* for *maga*. The second *mishnah* (on 54a) lists cases of *ein hanazir megaleach*: places like a field of graves (*beit ha-pras*) and *eretz ha-amim*, and physical categories such as the tombstone (*golel* and *dofek*), *revi'it dam*, and *rov ha-atzamot* — this last corresponding to the *revi'it ha-kav*. Critically, *ever she-ein alav basar ka-rauy* does not appear in the second *mishnah*.

### Implicit vs Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר לָךְ: כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּמַשְׁמַע מִכְּלָלָא, לָא קָתָנֵי בְּסֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said to you,** in response to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish's argument: The fact that the mishna omits this case from the list is not proof, as the *tanna* **does not teach in the latter clause anything that can be understood by inference** from the earlier mishna.

The *Gemara* presses Rabbi Yochanan: if the logic is that anything not appearing in the first list is excluded, why does the second *mishnah* go to the trouble of listing *reva'it atzamot* — the quarter-*kav* of bones — which is already implicitly excluded by the first *mishnah*'s requirement of a half-*kav*? The second *mishnah* apparently does mention things that are already implicit exclusions from the first.

### Implicit Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת, דְּמַשְׁמַע חֲצִי קַב עֲצָמוֹת — אִין, רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת — לָא, וְקָתָנֵי בְּסֵיפָא רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת. הָתָם אִי לָאו רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ עַל מַגָּעוֹ וְעַל מַשָּׂאוֹ לָא, לְהָכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיתְנֵי רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, דְּעַל אֲהִילָן הוּא דְּאֵין הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this claim of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **But** the first clause of the mishna lists the case of **a half-*kav* of bones,** which **indicates: A half-*kav* of bones, yes,** a nazirite must shave for that; **a quarter-***kav* **of bones, no,** he is not obligated to shave for that. **And** yet the *tanna* **teaches in the latter clause** that a nazirite does not shave for **a quarter-***kav* **of bones.** This shows that the next mishna does not rely on the rulings of this mishna. Rather, it lists all the items for which a nazirite need not shave. The Gemara rejects this argument: **There, had** the *tanna* **not** taught **a quarter-***kav* **of bones, I would say** that he need **not** shave **even for touching it or carrying it. For this** reason it **was necessary** for the mishna **to teach** the case of **a quarter-***kav* **of bones,** to indicate that **it is** only **for their** ritual impurity contracted in **a tent** that **a nazirite does not shave.**

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *kol heicha d'mashma miklala lo tana b'seifa* — wherever the exclusion is genuinely implicit in the *klal* of the first *mishnah*, the second *mishnah* does not need to repeat it. The second *mishnah* only mentions something when, without the explicit statement, one might have drawn an erroneous inference.

In the case of *reva'it atzamot*, had the second *mishnah* not stated it explicitly, one might have thought that less than a half-*kav* is not *metamei* even through *maga* or *masa*. The explicit statement therefore comes to clarify: the quarter-*kav* is excluded only from *ohel*, not from *maga*. Even less than a half-*kav* of bones still causes *tuma* through touching. That clarification was necessary and thus warranted explicit mention.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חֲצִי לוֹג דָּם, דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ חֲצִי לוֹג דָּם — אִין, רְבִיעִית דָּם — לָא, וְקָתָנֵי בְּסֵיפָא רְבִיעִית דָּם! הָתָם, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: רְבִיעִית דָּם הַבָּא מִשְּׁנֵי מֵתִים מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **But** the mishna lists **a half-*log* of blood** among those sources of impurity for which a nazirite must shave, **from which you** can **learn** that for **a half-*log* of blood, yes,** he shaves; for **a quarter-***log* **of blood, no,** he does not shave. **And** yet **the latter clause** of the mishna **teaches** that he need not shave for **a quarter-***log* **of blood.** The Gemara answers: It is also necessary to state this *halakha* unambiguously **there, to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, as Rabbi Akiva said: A quarter-***log* **of blood that comes from two corpses renders** people and items **impure in a tent,** whereas the mishna simply states: A quarter-*log*, which indicates that all of the blood comes from a single corpse.

As for the *revi'it dam* appearing in the second *mishnah*, Rabbi Yochanan explains: *l'afukei midivrei Rabbi Akiva* — it is stated there to exclude Rabbi Akiva's persistent position that *revi'it dam* drawn from two corpses causes *tumas ohel*. The *mishnah* wishes to explicitly reject that view and therefore states it. In principle, the second *mishnah* does not need to restate things already implicit in the first.

Returning to *ever she-ein alav basar ka-rauy*: since the first *mishnah* explicitly conditions the *ever* on *she-yesh alav basar ka-rauy*, the implication that a limb without sufficient flesh is excluded is fully embedded in the first *mishnah*. There is no need to repeat it in the second *mishnah*. Thus, the *nazir* does not shave for it.

Reish Lakish, by contrast, reads the two lists differently: the first *mishnah* organizes by modality — *ohel* cases and *maga* cases — and the second *mishnah* enumerates everything that does not trigger *nazir megaleach*. If a scenario is absent from the second *mishnah*, it is *megaleach*. The *she-yesh alav basar ka-rauy* of the first *mishnah* implicitly suggests the case of *she-ein alav basar ka-rauy*, and since that case is not listed in the second *mishnah* among the non-*megaleach* categories, Reish Lakish concludes that it is *megaleach* through *maga*. Rabbi Yochanan's position seems the stronger reading of the *mishnah* as a whole, but as we will see, Reish Lakish draws support from *midrashei halacha*.

## Midrashic Derivations

### Braita Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה בַּחֲלַל חֶרֶב אוֹ בְמֵת״, ״עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה״ — זֶה הַמַּאֲהִיל עַל פְּנֵי הַמֵּת, ״בַּחֲלַל״ — זֶה אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי וְיֵשׁ לוֹ לְהַעֲלוֹת אֲרוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"And whoever in the open field touches one who is slain by the sword, or one who dies on his own,** or a bone of a man, or a grave, shall be unclean seven days" (Numbers 19:16). This verse is expounded by the Sages as follows: **"In the open field"; this** is referring to the *halakha* of **one who overlies a corpse,** even without touching it. **"One who is slain"; this** is referring to **a limb** slain, i.e., severed, **from a living** person, **that contains** enough flesh **for** the limb **to heal.**

The *Gemara* cites a *braita* that provides scriptural grounding for the categories of *tumas ohel* and *tumas maga*. The *pasuk* read for *ohel* is: *ve-chol asher yiga al pnei ha-sadeh u-ve-chalal cherev o ve-meit o ve-etzem adam o ve-kaver* (Bamidbar 19:16). The *Gemara* reads *al pnei ha-sadeh* as referring to *ha-me'ahil* — one who is an *ohel* over these sources of *tuma*. The phrase *al pnei* — literally "upon the face of" — is understood as superfluous given the word *yiga* ("touch"), and from this superfluity the *drashah* derives that the *pasuk* speaks of *tumas ohel*.

### Chalal Cherev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חֶרֶב״ הֲרֵי זֶה כְּחָלָל, ״אוֹ בְּמֵת״ — זֶה אֵבֶר הַנֶחְלָל מִן הַמֵּת, ״אוֹ בְּעֶצֶם אָדָם״ — זֶה רוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, ״אוֹ בְּקֶבֶר״ — זֶה קֶבֶר סָתוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages further derive from the phrase "one who is slain by the sword" that the legal status of a metal **sword** in terms of its degree of ritual impurity **is like** that of **one who is slain,** i.e., a metal implement, e.g., a sword, that was rendered impure through contact with a corpse is impure to the same degree of severity as a corpse itself. **"Or one who dies on his own"; this is a limb that was slain,** i.e., severed, **from a corpse** and is covered with enough flesh that it would heal if he were alive. **"Or a bone of a man"; this is a quarter-***kav* **of bones. "Or a grave"; this is a sealed grave,** which imparts impurity when there is less than a handbreadth between the corpse and its cover.

*Cherev* gives rise to the famous principle *cherev harei hu ke-chalal* — a metal vessel is halachically equivalent to a *chalal*. This means that metal *keilim* that come into contact with a *meit*, or are present in the same *ohel* as a *meit*, acquire the *tuma* of the *meit* itself and become *avi avot ha-tuma* rather than merely *av ha-tuma*. They are not merely secondary sources of *tuma* but are elevated to the primary level of the corpse itself. This principle will be discussed further in the next *daf*.

*O ve-meit* — from *meit* the *braita* derives *eiver ha-nechlal min ha-meit*, a limb that is part of a corpse — that is, *ever min ha-meit*, where the whole body is the *meit* and the limb is extracted from it, as opposed to the *chalal* case where only the limb itself is the "dead thing."

*O ve-etzem adam* — from *etzem adam* the *braita* derives *rov atzamot*, the majority of bones. Though *etzem* sounds like a single bone, the *drashah* understands it as referring to the bone that represents the entire person — *rov atzamot*. The list from this *pasuk* thus covers *eiver min ha-chai*, *eiver min ha-meit*, and *rov atzamot* — most of the *mishnah*'s *ohel* categories — though notably not the blood or *ke-zayit basar* categories as yet.

### Ohel vs Touching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי נְגִיעָה, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, תַּנְיָא: ״(וְכׇל) הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּעֶצֶם אוֹ בֶחָלָל״, ״בַּעֶצֶם״ — זֶה עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, ״אוֹ בֶחָלָל״ — זֶה אֵבֶר הַנֶחְלָל מִן הַחַי וְאֵין בּוֹ לְהַעֲלוֹת אֲרוּכָה,
>
> **Translation:** **While with regard to contact** with these sources of impurity, **Rav Yehuda said** that **it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse **"and** upon **him that touched a bone, or the slain,** or the dead, or the grave" (Numbers 19:18) is expounded as follows: **"A bone"; this is a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk. "Or the slain"; this is a limb slain,** i.e., severed, **from the living, which does not contain** enough flesh **for it to heal.**

The *braita* on *tumas ohel* is then set alongside the parallel *braita* on *tumas maga*, derived from a different *pasuk*: *ve-al ha-nogei'a be-etzem o be-chalal o be-meit o be-kaver* (Bamidbar 19:18). Rav Yehuda introduces this *braita* as a *tena* — a tannaitic source — dealing with touching. The two *beraitot* share much of the same language but serve different halachic functions: the first defines sources of *tumas ohel*; the second defines sources of *tumas maga*.

From *be-etzem* in the touching *pasuk*, the *braita* derives *etzem ke-se'orah* — a bone the size of a barley grain causes *tuma* through touching. This is the distinctive *shiur* for *maga* that does not apply to *ohel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹ בְּמֵת״ — זֶה אֵבֶר הַנֶחְלָל מִן הַמֵּת, ״אוֹ בְקָבֶר״ — אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: זֶה קֶבֶר שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַדִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues its interpretation of the verse. **"Or the dead"; this is a limb slain from a corpse,** which is not covered by enough flesh for it to heal were he alive. **"Or the grave"; Reish Lakish said: This is a grave** that contained a corpse from **before** the time **the command** of these *halakhot* was given to the Jewish people. These graves render people and items ritually impure by contact, but do not carry the *halakhot* of impurity imparted in a tent.

*O ve-kaver* in the *ohel* list gives rise to *kever satum* — a sealed grave. The *halacha* of *kever satum* is that if a body is enclosed in a sealed coffin or burial chamber, the entire enclosure is treated as the *meit* itself: the *tuma* spreads across the full dimensions of the casket and bursts upward (*tumah boka'at ve-olah boka'at ve-yoredet*), passing through the covering and continuing upward, because the sealed grave is treated as an extension of the corpse. This is why overflight of cemeteries is a concern. If the burial chamber were not sealed — lacking full sides — the *tuma* would stop within a *tefach* of the body at the covering above and would not proceed further upward, since the covering would function as an *ohel* to block it.

### RL: Flesh-Deficient Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה הַיְינוּ הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּעֶצֶם! אֶלָּא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי רַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **this limb from a corpse,** which is listed above as imparting ritual impurity by contact, **what are the circumstances** of the case? **If** the limb **contains a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk, this is** included in the *halakha* of **one who touches a bone,** and it was already taught that a bone imparts impurity by contact. **Rather,** it must be **that it does not contain a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk, and even so the Merciful One includes it** in the *halakhot* of impurity, despite the fact that it does not contain enough flesh. This indicates that a nazirite shaves for a limb of this kind, in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish.

The key contribution from the *maga* braita for the Reish Lakish dispute is the following. Where the *ohel* braita derived from *be-chalal* the case of *eiver min ha-chai she-yesh lo le-haalot arucha* — a limb with sufficient flesh — Reish Lakish reads the same word *chalal* in the *maga* braita to yield a different derivation: *zeh eiver ha-nichleh min ha-chai ve-ein bo le-haalot arucha* — a limb from a living person that does *not* have enough flesh to heal. Similarly, *o be-meit* in the *maga* braita yields *eiver ha-nichleh min ha-meit* without sufficient flesh. The two *beraitot* thus present a parallel structure: the *ohel pasuk* gives us *eiver min ha-chai* and *eiver min ha-meit* with sufficient flesh; the *maga pasuk* gives us the same two categories without sufficient flesh — and these latter cause *tuma* only through touching, not through *ohel*. This is the explicit scriptural support for Reish Lakish's position: a limb from a corpse or a living person lacking sufficient flesh is *metamei* through *maga*, even if it does not cause *tumas ohel*.

The parallel structure between the two *beraitot* is also visible in the bone categories: *etzem adam* in the *ohel pasuk* refers to *rov atzamot*, while *be-etzem* in the *maga pasuk* refers to the smaller *etzem ke-se'orah*. So too with *chalal* and *meit* — each appears in both *pesukim* but yields a different level of *tuma* depending on context.

The final word in the *maga pasuk* — *o ve-kaver* — receives a striking interpretation from Reish Lakish: *zeh kever she-lifnei ha-dibbur* — the grave before the giving of the Torah at Sinai. What this mysterious phrase means, and its full halachic significance, will be taken up in the next session.
