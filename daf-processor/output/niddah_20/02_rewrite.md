# Niddah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Colors & Base Red

### Intro: Base Red

The *daf* begins on *amud bet*, picking up approximately fifteen lines from the bottom, at the words *d'chazyei adom*. Having already addressed the questions surrounding the frequency of checking and the fundamental definition of *chomer tumah vetaharah* — including the identification of uterine blood as the relevant category — the *Gemara* turns to the colors of blood that render a woman a *niddah*. As established, although many substances are biologically blood, only those presenting certain colors of red are halachically problematic.

The *Gemara* continues its examination of the *Mishnah*, which identifies four reds (*eideh adom*). The first of these is described as the base red — *kedom hamakah*, like the color of blood that emerges from a wound. Rather than accepting this description as sufficiently precise, the *Gemara* immediately presses: *mai kedom hamakah* — be more specific.

### Rav Yehuda: Slaughter

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: this is *kedom shor shachut*, like the blood that flows when an ox is slaughtered. The *Gemara* immediately raises an obvious question: why not simply say *kedom shechitah*, the blood of the slaughtering itself? That formulation would seem at least equally explicit, if not more so.

### Why Specify Wound

The *Gemara* explains: *ee amar kedom shechitah hava amina kekula shechitah* — had the *Mishnah* said "blood of *shechitah*," one might have assumed this referred to the entire process of slaughter. As the *Gemara* discusses in *Pesachim*, the blood emerging from an animal after *shechitah* passes through different colors at different stages, as the flow continues. Therefore the *Mishnah* specifies *kedom hamakah* — *ketechilat haka'at hasakin*, from the very moment the knife first strikes the throat, meaning the blood that first spurts out. That is the precise shade constituting the base red of the *Mishnah*.

### Alternate Views

*Ulla amar*: the standard is *kedom tzipor chaya*, like the blood of a live bird. The *Gemara* raises a question about the meaning of *chaya* here. Does it mean "alive" — that is, a bird that has been wounded but is still living — as opposed to a slaughtered bird? Or does *chaya* perhaps mean "healthy," as opposed to a lean or weakened bird? The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

*Zeiri amar amar Rabbi Chanina*: the color is *kedom ma'acholet shel rosh*, like the blood of a head louse. The *Gemara* challenges this: in the laws of *telyah* — the principle of attributing stains to external sources — the ruling states that if a woman kills a louse and finds a stain on her garment, she may attribute the stain to that louse and remain *tahor*. The relevant passage refers simply to a louse, not specifically a head louse. If the *Mishnah*'s standard of this red is keyed precisely to the head louse, should not the *telyah* ruling similarly specify that only killing a head louse permits such attribution? The *Gemara* resolves: *lo derosh* — indeed, the *telyah* rule is limited to head lice. The *Gemara* adds, with a measure of candor, that it apparently understood different types of lice to inhabit different parts of the body, with distinct coloration.

*Amar Rabbi Abbahu shem Rabbi Abba*: the color is *kedom etzba ketana shel yad shenikfah vechaysah vechazra venikfah* — like the pinky finger that sustained a wound, healed, and then reopened. That specific blood is the standard. And even this is not sufficient — the *Gemara* further specifies that it must be *ella shebachur shelo nasa isha*, a young man who has never been married, and *ad ben esrim*, only up to age twenty.

### Attribution Principle

The *Gemara* now invokes the attribution principle (*telyah*) in connection with the larger question of what this first red encompasses. The laws of *telyah* permit a woman to attribute a stain found on her garment to another source if there is a reasonable basis for doing so — for instance, if her husband or son had a wound and could have come into contact with her clothing, she may attribute any stain to that source. *Bishlama bivno* — this works straightforwardly in the case of her son, who could in principle be the young, unmarried man satisfying the first red's criterion. *Ella ba'alah* — but how could her husband satisfy that criterion? He is, by definition, married. *Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak*: the case is where they entered the *chuppah* but had not yet consummated the marriage — *shenichnasu lechuppah velo niv'alah*. In such a case, the husband is formally married but the union is unconsummated, so if he has a bleeding finger its blood could be attributed to him as satisfying the criterion. The degree of precision the *Gemara* brings to bear here is striking. Crucially, all of this precision functions as a *leniency*: the more narrowly and precisely the first red is defined, the more colors fall outside it and are consequently permitted.

### Bloodletting Red

*Amar Rav Nachman*: a fifth red — *kedam hakaza*, the blood of bloodletting. The *Gemara* challenges this with a story involving *Maaseh uba lifnei Rabbi Meir be'khilor*: Rabbi Meir attributed a stain to *khilor*, a type of red eye salve, declaring the woman *tehorah*. Similarly, Rabbi attributed a stain to the reddish sap of a sycamore tree. These are cases where stains on a garment are attributed to non-menstrual sources, again invoking the *telyah* principle.

The *Gemara* questions whether these cases of attribution refer to the first red of the *Mishnah*, which would imply that the first red is not as narrowly defined as the preceding discussion suggested — after all, Rabbi Meir was attributing stains to an eye salve or tree sap that apparently matched the color, not to the precisely defined blood of a young man's reopened wound.

### First Red Narrowness

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension: *lav ashar damim* — these cases of attribution may refer to the other three reds, not to the first red of the *Mishnah*. The first red remains very narrowly and precisely defined. The other three reds are also halachically problematic, and stains matching those broader categories may be addressed through the *telyah* principle. This resolution confirms that the first red of the *Mishnah* is an exceptionally narrow, precise shade, while the other reds have somewhat broader application.

## Expert Bloodletting Demo

### Bloodletter Demo

The discussion of *dam hakaza* — bloodletting blood — leads naturally into an episode that illuminates the practical difficulty of color identification. *Amemar, Mar Zutra, ve'Rav Ashi havu yasvi kamei umna* — Amemar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi were sitting before a bloodletter, whether as patients or observers of the procedure. *Shaklei lekarna kama'isa le'Amemar* — the bloodletter took the first horn — the instrument used to collect the initial blood — and showed it to Amemar. *Amar lehu*: Amemar declared to his colleagues that the red of the *Mishnah* looks precisely like this.

### Second Horn Different

*Shaklei leachrisi* — they then examined a second horn from later in the same bloodletting procedure. *Amar lehu*: Amemar said that this red is different — *ishtani* — it has already changed from the first red. This too is only like the first red in character, but is distinguishably not the same shade. The level of precision required is extraordinary.

### Rav Ashi: Can't See

*Amar Rav Ashi*: someone like me, who cannot distinguish between that one and that one — *delo yadana bein hai lehai* — is not qualified to inspect blood. *Lo miba'ei lei lemichzei damai*: I should not be among those who rule on the colors of menstrual blood.

This is a striking moment in the *sugya*. The discussion begins by defining the first red with great narrowness, which functions as a leniency — blood that fails to match so precise a standard is *tahor*. But once precision is demanded at this level, a *posek* who cannot discern these subtle differences cannot rule, and if he is stringent out of uncertainty, the result may be practical stringency rather than leniency. One might ask: if the rabbis themselves struggle to distinguish these shades, are women expected to do so? This question will be revisited. Women who deal with this on a regular basis may develop a genuine experiential expertise — and there are cases already mentioned where women brought blood for ruling to other expert women rather than to rabbis. This dynamic of overlapping expertise and authority is a theme that will only deepen as the *sugya* develops.

## Black Red & Ink

### Charas & Ink

The *Gemara* moves explicitly onto *amud bet* and takes up the second problematic color — the black red. *Amar Rabbah bar Rav Huna*: *shachor kecharas* — black as *charas*, which is a gallnut or an extremely black substance used to produce ink. *Charas she'amru deyo* — the *charas* being referenced is ink itself, specifically its blackest component, which, as the discussion will clarify, settles to the bottom of liquid ink.

*Tanya nami hachi*: a *beraita* confirms this — *shachor kecharas*, and the *charas* that is meant is ink. The *Gemara* immediately asks: why not simply say *deyo*, ink? *Ei amar deyo hava amina kiphachuta d'deyo* — one might have understood the standard to refer to the weaker, lighter portion of the ink, since the sediment settles and the upper layer is less concentrated. *Ka mashma lan*: therefore the formulation specifies *kecharuta d'deyo*, the dense black portion of the ink, not its diluted upper layer. This is also noted as bearing relevance to the *Tosafot* discussion concerning which inks are valid for *Sifrei Torah* — specifically what exactly constitutes *charas* and what its properties must be.

### Liquid vs. Dry

*Ibaia lehu*: is the comparison to ink made when the ink is liquid (*lacha*) or when it has dried and congealed (*yavesha*)? *Ta shma*: come and hear — *Rabbi Ami palli kurta d'deyo*, Rabbi Ami split a kernel of congealed ink and used it for the comparison. This indicates that the standard is set against dry, congealed ink, not liquid ink.

### Darker Blacks Bad

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: *keir kedeyo veche'anav tme'ah* — like black wax (which Rashi identifies as *keir*), or ink itself in its darkest form, or like a black grape — these are shades of black that are *tme'ah*. *Vezu sheshaninu amok mikan tme'ah*: this elaborates on the *Mishnah*'s ruling that anything deeper than the ink-black standard is *tamei*. All the examples given — black wax, ink at full density, black grape — fall at or beyond the problematic threshold.

### Lighter Blacks OK

*Amar Rabbi Elazar*: *kezayis kezefes uche'orev tahor* — like an olive, like tar, or like a raven, these are *tahor*. *Vezu sheshaninu deheh mikan tahor*: this corresponds to the *Mishnah*'s ruling that lighter shades are permitted. One might find it counterintuitive that olive, tar, and raven are all classified as lighter and therefore permitted — all would seem quite dark to the untrained eye. The point is well-taken: just as there are many subtle gradations of color that specialists in any domain train themselves to perceive, so too here the relevant distinctions lie along a spectrum invisible to most people.

## Wine Red & Garments

### Siua Garments

The *Gemara* turns to the third color — the mixed-wine red. *Ulla amar*: the color is *kilvush Siua*, like the garment of Siua, which Rashi identifies as a place-name. The people of that region, apparently, produced garments of a characteristic color that served as a reference point for this particular shade of red.

### Nomad Garment

*Ulla ikla lePumbedita* — Ulla came to Pumbedita and there, *chazyei lehu taia*, he saw a nomad wearing a black garment. He announced to the people of Pumbedita: *shachor detnan kehai* — the black of our *Mishnah* is precisely like the black of that man's garment. What followed was predictable: *mardu minei purtza purtza*, they began tearing small strips from the nomad's garment, though they were scrupulous not to steal it — *veyahavu bei arba me'ah zuzei*, they paid four hundred *zuzei* in total for all the strips they took.

### Royal Garments

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the reference point is *elu kelim haulyarin habaim mimedinas hayam* — these are garments, which Rashi identifies with a place-name and Jastrow associates with royal garments (from a Greek term), specifically those imported from overseas. The black of the *Mishnah* corresponds to this shade. The *Gemara* then raises a challenge: this implies that these *ulyarin* garments are black. Yet there is a well-known teaching of Rabbi Yannai to his children.

### Yannai Shrouds

*Amar lehu Rabbi Yannai le-vanav*: do not bury me in black garments, and do not bury me in white garments. *Shechorim shema ezkeh ve-ehye ke-avel bein ha-chatanim* — if I merit and go to the world of reward, where everyone is dressed in white, I will appear as a mourner among bridegrooms, conspicuously out of place. *Levanim shema lo ezkeh ve-ehye ke-chatan bein ha-avelim* — and if I do not merit and I go to the opposite, wearing white I will appear as a bridegroom among mourners. The concern is always to be dressed appropriately for whatever occasion awaits. What then did Rabbi Yannai wish to be buried in? *Ela bichailim ha'ulaim ha'ba'im mi'mdinas ha'yam* — in these very royal garments from overseas. *Alma lav uchmaninu* — evidently, then, these garments are not black, which contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's use of them as the standard for the black of the *Mishnah*.

The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya* — *ha b'glima*, one reference concerns the cloak, *ha b'phtora*, the other concerns the table garments, the napkins or formal dining wear. Rashi understands *phtora* as table coverings; Jastrow understands it as the formal garments worn specifically at table. In either reading, some category of these garments is indeed black while the outer cloaks are not — and it was specifically those black table garments that Rabbi Yochanan cited as the color standard.

## Checking Methods

### White Cloth

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: *v'kulam ein bodkin ela al gabei matlit levana* — all of the colors are checked only against a white cloth background. This rule may be understood in two complementary ways: stains must appear on a white cloth so as not to have their color obscured by the fabric's own color, and additionally, when making comparative assessments, the background against which one evaluates the stain must be white so as to bring out the color accurately.

### Red for Black

*Amar Rav Yitzchak bar Abudimi*: *v'shachor al gabei adom* — black should be checked against a red background. Since the black color of the *Mishnah* is, as Rabbi Chanina explains, essentially a red that has been "smitten" or darkened, perhaps a red background helps draw out and reveal the underlying redness in the black. It is worth noting that in current practice, blood is not checked against a red background; those who have developed expertise simply examine the stain directly, viewing it against whatever background it appears on, which is typically white cloth.

### Reconciliation

*Amar Rav Yirmiyah mi'Difti*: *v'lo pligi* — these authorities are not truly arguing. *Ha b'shachor ha b'sha'ar damim*: black is checked against a red background; the other reds are checked against a white background. This is a clean resolution.

### Rav Ashi's Q

*Maskif lei Rav Ashi*: if so, then Shmuel should have explicitly excluded black from his general rule — he should have said "all colors against white, except for black." Since Shmuel stated the rule without exception, perhaps he and Rav Yitzchak genuinely disagree. *Ela amar Rav Ashi b'shachor gufei ka mipligi*: they actually debate specifically how the black is to be checked — against a white background or against a red background.

## Variation Limits

### Ulla: Deeper Bad

Having identified five colors — the four reds of the *Mishnah* and the black — the *Gemara* now turns to the question of how much variation around each standard color remains problematic. *Amar Ulla*: *kulam amok mikan tamei deyah mikan tahor* — for all of the colors, a deeper shade is *tamei* and a lighter shade is *tahor*. *K'shachor* — they all follow the same rule as black. The *Gemara* immediately asks: if they all share the rule of black, why was black specifically singled out in the *Mishnah*? The answer: *salka daitach amina* — one might have thought, given that Rabbi Chanina teaches that black is really red that has been "smitten" and darkened, that since you are not looking at the original color but at a transformed one, perhaps even the lighter black shades should be *tamei* out of uncertainty. *Ka mashma lan*: therefore the *Mishnah* specifies that only a particular shade of black is problematic; lighter shades remain *tahor*.

### Measure Utility

*V'Rami bar Abba amar*: an alternative and more stringent view — *v'kulam amok mikan tamei deyah mikan nami tamei* — for all the colors, whether lighter or darker than the standard, it is still *tamei*, with the sole exception of black, where darker is *tamei* but lighter is *tahor*. This raises an obvious and pointed question: if both lighter and darker are *tamei*, what practical purpose does identifying a precise standard color serve? *Ela mai ahani shiur de'rabanan*: of what benefit is the rabbis' measure?

The *Gemara* answers: *l'afokei deyah de'deyah* — to exclude the case of "lighter of lighter," meaning that only the range immediately surrounding the standard color is *tamei*; a color that departs sufficiently far from the standard — what might be called two degrees of removal — is *tahor*. So the precision of the standard color delimits a range, not an infinite spectrum.

A further question surfaces: if all colors are problematic in both directions, why enumerate four separate reds? Why not simply identify the lightest possible problematic red and declare everything darker *tamei*? Two answers are possible. First, the four reds are not necessarily on a linear spectrum — color has dimensions of hue, saturation, and other qualities, so they cannot be arranged on a single line. Second, Rashi explains that just as "lighter of lighter" is *tahor*, so too "darker of darker" is *tahor*, meaning the four reds each define a zone of problematic color along the spectrum, with the regions between them — sufficiently far from any of the four standards — remaining *tahor*.

### Rami: Precise Only

*Ve'ika de'amri, Rami bar Abba amar*: there is an alternative version of Rami bar Abba's position that is, if anything, more lenient — *bekulan amok mikan tahor deyah mikan tahor*, for all colors except black, any deviation, whether lighter or darker, is *tahor*. Only the precise color itself is *tamei*. Black alone follows the stricter rule that darker shades are *tamei*. In this reading, *lehachi mahani shiur derabbanan* — this is why the *Mishnah* identifies the precise standards: so that only those exact shades are prohibited.

### Bar Kappara

*Bar Kappara amar*: all colors follow the rule of black — deeper is *tamei*, lighter is *tahor* — *chutz mizo*, with the single exception of the mixed-wine red, for which both a deeper shade and a lighter shade are *tahor*. Only the precise wine-color itself is *tamei*.

### Wine Red Leniency

*Bar Kappara ayitu lei vedachi* — when blood matching the wine color, or approaching it, was brought to Bar Kappara, whether slightly lighter or slightly deeper, he would declare it *tahor*. *Amar Rabbi Chanina*: how great is this person — *libei kishmatei* or *de'avid kishmatei* — his heart is as his teaching, meaning he actually rules in practice according to his stated position, even when declaring a deeper shade *tahor*, which is the striking *chiddush* of his view.

## Saffron & Earth

### Saffron Middle

The *Gemara* now addresses the fourth and final color of the *Mishnah*: *uchekeren karkom*, like the horn of saffron. It is worth pausing to note what saffron looks like: the flower presents bright yellow petals surrounding deep red stamens — the stamens, which are thread-like and resemble horns, are what is referenced here. These are the most valuable part of the saffron plant, more precious per ounce than gold.

*Tana*: a *beraita* specifies *lach velo yavesh* — the comparison applies only to the moist flower, not a dried one. But then the *Gemara* encounters a series of apparently contradictory *beraitot*. *Tani chada*: one *beraita* says to take the bottom and not the top. *Vetanya idach*: another says the top and not the bottom. *Vetanya idach*: yet another says the top and certainly the bottom. *Vetanya idach*: a fourth says the bottom and certainly the top. Four *beraitot*, each seemingly contradicting the others.

### Reconcile 4 Views

*Amar Abaye*: *tlata darei vetlata tarfei havya* — the saffron flower has three rows and three leaves in each row. *Nakut dara metzia v'tarpa metziata* — take the middle row and the middle leaf. With this key, all four *beraitot* can be reconciled. When a *beraita* says "the top and certainly the bottom," it means the middle is the standard and what is above it is certainly included; when it says "the bottom and certainly the top," it again means the middle and what is above it; when it says "the top and not the bottom," it means the middle but not what is below it — and so on through the various formulations. Each *beraita* is expressing the same practical standard through different ways of triangulating around the middle position.

### Saffron Fresh

*Ki atu lekamei d'Rabbi Abbahu amar lehu*: when this question came before Rabbi Abbahu, he ruled *begushaihu shaninu* — the standard applies only when the saffron is still planted in the ground, in its natural state. A cut flower, even a fresh one, does not serve as the reference.

### Water-Earth Test

*Tanu rabanan*: the final color standard to be examined is *k'mimei adamah*, like water mixed with earth. The *Gemara* gives detailed instructions. *Meiti adamah shemeina mibik'at Beit Kerem* — bring fertile earth from the valley of Beit Kerem. *U'matzip aleiha mayim* — pour water over it. This is the ruling of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Akiva holds the earth must come from the valley of Yodefat; Rabbi Yossi says the valley of Sachni; Rabbi Shimon says even the valley of Ginosar suffices. *V'chayozei bahen* — and anything similar to these is equally acceptable, meaning the requirement is not rigidly tied to one specific location.

### Mixing Procedure

A second *beraita* elaborates on the procedure. Bring fertile earth from Beit Kerem and pour water over it *k'klipat hashum* — in a layer as thin as the peel of a garlic clove. *V'ein shiur lamayim mishum d'ein shiur l'afar*: there is no fixed quantity of water, and correspondingly there is no fixed quantity of earth. Whatever amount of earth one uses, one adds just enough water to cover it with that thin layer.

*V'ein bodkin otan tzelulin ela achurin* — one does not check the color when the water is clear and settled but rather when it is cloudy and mixed with the earth. *Tzalelu v'chazar v'ochran* — if the mixture has separated and cleared, one stirs it again. *U'cheshehu ochran ein ochran beyad ela bichli* — when stirring, one does not use the hand but a vessel.

The *Gemara* then debates what exactly this last rule means. Does it prohibit holding the mixture in one's hand, requiring instead that it be kept in a vessel — while allowing the finger to stir it? Or does it prohibit stirring with the finger, requiring a separate implement to be used for stirring? *Ta shema*: come and hear — *keshehu bodek ein bodek ela bechos*, when checking the color one must check it only in a vessel, not in the hand. The *Gemara* notes this answers the question of the container but not the stirring implement. The question of whether one may stir with the finger remains unresolved: *teiku*.

*Yatvu kamei d'Rabbah bar Rav Huna*: when this discussion came before Rabbah bar Rav Huna, he ruled that the earth comparison is valid *b'komah shaninu* — only in its original place. Once earth is transported, its qualities change and it can no longer serve as the color reference.

### Chanina Bold

*Rabbi Chanina palig kurta d'gargishta u'vadak bah* — Rabbi Chanina split open a clump of earth and examined the stain directly against it, without mixing it with water at all. *Liyteh l'Rabbi Yishmael b'rabbi Yosi b'askara* — he cursed with *askara*, a severe respiratory disease, anyone who would try to replicate this practice. Rashi clarifies: Rabbi Chanina did not curse any particular individual; rather, he warned that only someone with his level of expertise could perform such a check, and anyone else who attempted it should be forewarned that it was beyond their ability. *Rabbi Chanina hu d'chakim* — Rabbi Chanina is uniquely wise in this regard. *Kulei alma lo chakimi* — no one else possesses that discernment.

## Expertise Crisis

### Yochanan Quits

The *Gemara* now turns directly to what might be called the crisis of rabbinic expertise in blood inspection. *Hachi amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the wisdom and expertise of Rabbi Chanina — *chachmuto shel Rabbi Chanina* — was precisely what caused Rabbi Yochanan to cease inspecting blood: *garmah li d'lo achvi dama*. Why? Because Rabbi Yochanan observed that when he would classify something as *tamei*, Rabbi Chanina would rule it *tahor*, and when he would classify something as *tahor*, Rabbi Chanina would rule it *tamei* — *metaminu metaher, metahernu metamei*. From this he concluded that his own judgment was entirely unreliable and he withdrew from inspecting blood.

### Eleazar Continues

*Amar Rabbi Eleazar*: his conclusion was precisely the opposite. *Anvatanuto shel Rabbi Chanina garmah li d'chazai dama* — the humility of Rabbi Chanina was what caused him to continue inspecting blood. Rabbi Chanina, for all his greatness, was still willing to engage with doubtful cases, to take on the responsibility of ruling even in difficult and uncertain circumstances. *U'machnis nafshiah l'safek* — he did not retreat behind false modesty. *V'chazai* — he looked at the blood and ruled. *Ana lo achzei* — how could I, seeing his example, refuse to do so?

### Responsibility Balance

This exchange encapsulates a profound tension that runs through the entire world of *psak halacha*. Rav Moshe Feinstein articulates this tension beautifully in the introduction to his *Iggros Moshe*: the *Gemara* warns that one who rules when unqualified causes tremendous damage — *v'rabim chalalim hipilah*. Yet the *Gemara* equally warns that one who is qualified and yet refuses to rule bears a heavy responsibility for what results from that refusal. The principle is clear: if one genuinely lacks the expertise, one must withdraw. But false humility, when others depend on one's capacity to rule, is itself a form of irresponsibility. The balance is delicate, and it is here expressed with unusual directness.

### Zeira No Science

*Amar Rabbi Zera*: *tiva d'Bavel garmah li d'lo chazai* — the nature of *Bavel* led him not to inspect blood. The meaning is that he lacked fluency in the natural sciences — knowledge of the properties of earth, colors, and related empirical matters. His reasoning: *damina de'tiva lo yadanah, bidama yadanah* — if I cannot master natural science, how can I be expected to master the inspection of blood, which demands an even higher level of precise empirical knowledge? A mastery of the relevant sciences is a prerequisite for entering into blood inspection. Without it, one cannot even sit for the examination.

### Rabba/Rava Case

*Lemimra de'teva tava d'milta* — the *Gemara* reflects that blood inspection is apparently predicated on knowledge of the natural sciences. The case is then cited: the *amora* in question — some *geonim* read *Rabba*, others read *Rava*, and as the *Gemara* will make clear, the *girsa* matters because of the connection to the earlier statement about who claimed ignorance — was *deyada b'teiva*, knowledgeable in science, yet *v'lo yada b'dama*, was nonetheless unable to inspect blood. The *Gemara* draws the *kal vachomer*: if even one who knows the sciences cannot inspect blood, then someone who does not even know the sciences — such as Rabbi Zera — certainly cannot. The *Tosafot* note that it should be *Rabba* rather than *Rava* who claimed ignorance earlier, since it is *Rava* who is here identified as the one knowledgeable in science who nonetheless refused to inspect blood; clearly *Rava* did not say he was ignorant of science.

## Elazar Master

### Why Master

*Ulla ikla l'Pumbedita* — Ulla came to Pumbedita. Blood was brought before him for inspection, but *v'lo chaza* — he refused to rule. He explained: *umah Rabbi Elazar demarei d'ara d'Yisrael hava* — Rabbi Elazar, who is known as the master of the Land of Israel, *ki mikla l'asra d'Rav Yehuda v'lo chaza dama* — when he would travel to the territory of Rav Yehuda, he would refuse to inspect blood out of deference to the local rabbinic authority. *Ana echzei* — should I, a visitor, rule here in someone else's domain?

This raises the question: why is Rabbi Elazar called the master of the Land of Israel, and what is the significance of blood inspection to that title? *V'amai karu lei l'marei d'ara d'Yisrael*: why is he called master of the Land of Israel? The *Gemara* answers through a story. *De-itta d'aisaya l'kamei dama l'kamei d'Rabbi Elazar* — a woman came and brought blood before Rabbi Elazar. *Hava yativ Rabbi Ami kamei* — Rabbi Ami was seated before him in the study hall as the woman brought her stain.

### Smells Desire

*Archei* — Rabbi Elazar smelled the stain. The association with the messianic vision — *v'lo l'marei einav yishpot*, "he shall not judge by what his eyes see" — comes to mind. Whether or not this is the precise intertextual reference, the act of smelling rather than only looking at the stain to determine its nature is itself remarkable. *Amar lah hai dam chimud hu* — he declared to her: this is *dam chimud*, blood of desire, not menstrual blood.

The rabbinic understanding is that a woman experiencing sudden intense sexual longing could produce a blood stain that is not menstrual in origin. This may in part reflect an analogy to male physiology, where arousal can lead to seminal emission, projected onto female experience. What is more, there is a physiological correlate: mid-cycle, at the peak of hormonal activity coinciding with heightened desire, staining can indeed occur — not caused by the desire itself but correlated with the underlying hormonal state. The connection between the moment of longing and the staining is thus real, if not directly causal.

This point is halachically significant in a broader sense. The standing assumption throughout the *sugya* has been that any blood originating from the uterine lining is *dam niddah*, regardless of what caused it to be discharged — whether hormones, physical impact, or *ones*. If *dam chimud* is categorically *tahor*, this implies either that the rabbis understood it to originate from a different anatomical source, not the uterine lining, or — more provocatively — that the cause of the discharge was considered relevant. This would be a notable departure from the general assumption, though the more likely reading is the former: they did not identify it as uterine blood at all.

### Ami Verifies

*Ve-batar de-nafka, atzla Rabbi Ami* — after the woman left, Rabbi Ami went after her to verify Rabbi Elazar's ruling. *Amra lei*: she told him that her husband had been away on a journey — *ba'ali haya ba-derekh* — and she had felt intense longing for him — *ve-chimadeti beih*. The stain had appeared precisely at the moment of that desire. The ruling was confirmed. In this reading, the longing may have been triggered by the hormonal peak underlying mid-cycle staining, consistent with the correlation described above.

### God's Secret

*Karia lei*: Rabbi Ami declared regarding Rabbi Elazar, *Sod Hashem liyreav* — the secret of God is to those who fear Him (*Tehillim* 25:14). Rabbi Elazar's ability to identify *dam chimud* by smell alone elevated him to a station of nearly prophetic expertise. This is not merely a matter of technical color identification; it is an expression of a deeper form of discernment.

It is worth pausing to reflect on the broader symbolic dimension of blood inspection as it is developing in this *sugya*. The function of the *posek* who examines blood parallels in striking ways the role of the *kohen* inspecting *negaim*. The minimum size of a stain mirrors the minimum size of a *nega* — a *gris*. The lighting requirements for inspection parallel one another. And just as the *kohen*'s ruling *determines* the status of the *metzora* rather than merely identifying a pre-existing fact, the rabbinic ruling on blood carries its own constitutive authority. The rabbis, in the post-*Mikdash* era, absorbed the *kohen*'s authoritative role in determining *tumah* and *taharah*. The ability to inspect blood thus became one of the primary expressions of rabbinic learning and authority — which explains why this capability is the very basis of Rabbi Elazar's title as master of the Land of Israel.

## Rava 60 Samples

### Queen Mother Test

*Ifshormuz imei deShormuz Malka* — the queen mother of Shormuz Malka, the non-Jewish ruler of *Bavel*, sent blood to Rava. Whether this was a deliberate test of rabbinic expertise or a genuine request made for her own religious purposes — perhaps Zoroastrian religious law, which was equally concerned with the impurity of menstrual blood, or perhaps, as Rashi suggests, proximity to conversion — the circumstance created an extraordinary occasion. *Hava yativ Rava, Ovadia kamei* — Rava was seated with Rav Ovadia before him. *Oraichei* — Rava smelled the blood. *Amar lei hai dama dechimuda hu* — this too is blood of desire.

### 60 Samples

*Amra lei* — the queen mother's son or daughter responded: *dilma kesuma ba'arubba* — perhaps this is like the blind person in the skylight, who occasionally sees light by chance without truly perceiving. Perhaps Rava simply guessed correctly. *Hadar shadrat lei shittin mane de-minei dama* — she then sent sixty different blood samples to test him.

### 59 Right, 1 Trick

*Ve-kulla amrina* — all sixty he was able to assess and rule upon correctly. *Hu batra* — the last one, however, he could not immediately resolve. *Dam kinei havai* — it was lice blood, a deliberate trick. *Lo yada* — he could not determine what it was. *Isttayem milsa* — then, through what the *Gemara* describes as some form of heavenly assistance, he received an epiphany and realized what had been done. *Ve-shadar lah shikusa de-migdela kemei* — he sent her, as a knowing gift, a louse-comb, signaling that he understood perfectly what the sixty-first sample was.

### Queen's Praise

*Amra*: the queen mother declared, *Yehuda'ei betavne de-libba yasvitu* — the Jews sit in the innermost chambers of the heart. They possess a depth of perception, an almost supernatural expertise. The entire episode stands as a remarkable testament to the heights of rabbinic mastery in blood inspection — and, simultaneously, underscores how extraordinary and perhaps rare such mastery truly was.

## Yehuda Withdraws

### First Drop Withheld

*Amar Rav Yehuda*: against the background of these demonstrations of expertise, Rav Yehuda's own trajectory is instructive. *Meishei havah chazina dama* — I used to inspect blood. Then came the incident that changed everything. *Keivan de-amra li eimei de-Yitzchak beri* — the mother of his son Yitzchak, that is, his own wife, told him — *hai tipla kama lo maisna lehu* — I do not bring them the first drop of blood. *Mishum de'zehima* — because it is smelly, or because she found it objectionable, and she assumed the rabbi would dismiss it as well.

### Smelly Drop

The problem this creates is severe. Rav Yehuda was inspecting blood and rendering rulings, but he now realized he was never being shown the first drop. If the first drop is the most diagnostically significant — or if its absence from the sample means he was never seeing the complete picture — then his rulings were based on incomplete evidence. A woman might in fact be *tme'ah* based on the first drop, yet the only drops he examined were the subsequent ones, which appeared acceptable. *Lo chazina* — I refuse to inspect blood.

### Only Uncertain

However, Rav Yehuda maintained one category in which he would still rule: *bein tamei le'tahor vadai chazina* — where the status was moving between certain *tamei* and certain *tahor*, as in the case of a *yoledes*. A woman who has given birth goes through a period when her blood is *tamei* for either seven or fourteen days and then *tahor* for thirty-three or sixty-six days. When she brings blood after the period of *tumah* has concluded and during the period when blood might still appear, she is not going to be embarrassed about bringing the first drop, because the blood has been present for an extended time and the dynamics that caused her to withhold the first drop in the earlier case simply do not apply. In this context, Rav Yehuda would still rule. The episode is striking not only for what it reveals about Rav Yehuda's scrupulousness but for what it discloses about the dynamics between women bringing blood and rabbis examining it: the rabbi's knowledge is entirely dependent on the completeness and accuracy of what the woman chooses to bring.

## Yaltha Case

### Conflicting Rulings

The final episode of the *daf* involves Yaltha, the famously assertive wife of Rav Nachman. *Yaltha ayisi dama lekamei de'Rabbah bar bar Channah* — Yaltha brought blood to Rabbah bar bar Channah. *Ve'tamei lah* — he ruled it *tamei*. *Hadar ayisi lekamei de'Rabbi Yitzchak brei de'Rav Yehuda* — she then brought the same stain to Rabbi Yitzchak the son of Rav Yehuda. *Ve'dachi lah* — and he ruled it *tahor*.

### Overrule Q

*Heichi avid hachi*: how was this permissible? The *Gemara* does not initially question Yaltha's act of forum shopping — seeking a second opinion after a ruling she did not accept — but rather how Rabbi Yitzchak could have overruled the prior ruling at all. *Ve'hatanya, chakham she'tima ein chaveiro rashai le'taher* — one *posek* who rules *tamei* cannot be overruled by a colleague who rules *tahor*. And by the same principle, a *posek* who rules *asar* cannot have his ruling reversed by a colleague who rules *mutar*. How then did Rabbi Yitzchak rule *tahor*?

### First Ruling Known

*Me'ikara tmu'ei hava metamei lah* — the *Gemara* resolves: in truth, Rabbi Yitzchak had initially intended to rule *tamei* as well, deferring to the prior ruling of Rabbah bar bar Channah. *Keivan de'amrah lei* — but then Yaltha informed him: *kol yoma hava madkhei li ki hai gavna* — Rabbah bar bar Channah used always to rule this type of stain as *tahor* for me. The reason I came to you, she explained, is that *ve'ha'idna hu de'chash be'eineih* — his eyes were currently troubling him. Knowing that Rabbah bar bar Channah was by his own practice inclined to rule this type of stain *tahor*, and that his current ruling of *tamei* was likely an error attributable to his diminished eyesight, Rabbi Yitzchak understood that the operative precedent pointed to *tahor* — and ruled accordingly.

### Eye Problem Hint

This resolution is, as several have noted, highly revealing. Yaltha went to Rabbah bar bar Channah precisely because she knew he had an eye problem, expecting either that he would rule incorrectly or that she would thereby have grounds to seek a second ruling. She appears to have known the answer all along. The entire episode suggests that Yaltha possessed genuine expertise — she understood the status of her own blood — but was compelled to work through the medium of rabbinic rulings. Going to a rabbi with impaired vision was a way of navigating the system toward the ruling she knew to be correct.

### Not to Nachman

One question stands out: why did Yaltha not simply bring the blood to her own husband, Rav Nachman? *Mashma de-lo havya regila l-harot l-Rav Nachman ba'alah* — the *Gemara*'s language implies she was not in the habit of bringing blood to him.

### Tosafot Why

*Tosafot* raises several possible explanations. Perhaps Rav Nachman was not among those qualified to inspect blood — and we have seen throughout this *sugya* that many prominent *amoraim* withdrew from blood inspection. Alternatively, perhaps Rav Nachman preferred not to rule on his own wife's blood. On this point, *Tosafot* notes that the disqualification by personal proximity applies in *negaim* — *kol ha-negaim adam roeh chutz mi-nig'ei atzmo* — a person may examine others' *negaim* but not his own. There is debate about relatives: most authorities permit examining the *negaim* of relatives, but the case of blood raises separate questions since there is no explicit *Mishnaic* parallel. In general, a *posek* may rule for himself and his relatives in matters of *halacha*, but this very issue — whether to inspect one's wife's blood — has its own dynamics. A third possibility *Tosafot* raises: perhaps Rav Nachman would have been inclined toward stringency in ruling on his own household and Yaltha preferred an objective ruling. In that case, she sought out another qualified authority precisely to obtain an unbiased assessment, free of the personal stringency her husband might have applied to himself.

The *sugya* thus closes with a layered picture of rabbinic authority, female knowledge, and the interpersonal dynamics of *psak halacha*. The question of who brings blood to whom, and under what circumstances a second ruling may be sought, is not merely procedural — it opens onto the deeper question of where knowledge actually resides and how authority is legitimately exercised.
