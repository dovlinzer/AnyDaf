# Yoma 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Backup Kohen Gadol

### Baraita Setup

The daf opens on 12b with a *baraita* — cited also in Masechet Horayot — addressing what happens when a *kohen gadol* is rendered unfit for service and a replacement is appointed in his stead, only for the original to regain his fitness. The *baraita* itself is a *Tosefta*, and its original context in Horayot is not specifically about Yom Kippur; Horayot deals broadly with positions of communal authority — king, *kohen gadol*, *Sanhedrin* — and therefore the question it raises is a general one. The *baraita*'s language, *u'minu acher tachtav*, "they appointed another in his place," suggests a scenario where no backup was already waiting in the wings, unlike the Yom Kippur preparation discussed in the *mishnah*. Nevertheless, the scenario is clearly relevant: the *kohen gadol* is incapacitated, someone serves in his place, and then the original recovers. The *baraita* is unequivocal on one point: *rishon chozer la'avodato* — the first *kohen gadol*, once fit again, returns to his position. The question is what becomes of the second.

### Rabbi Meir

*Rabbi Meir* holds that the second *kohen*, having served in the capacity of *kohen gadol*, retains that status for the remainder of his life. In practice this means he is bound by all the *mitzvot* of *kehuna gedola*: he may not marry a widow, he may not allow his hair to grow wild or rend his garments upon the death of a close relative, he does not leave the *Beit HaMikdash* during a bereavement, and he wears all eight priestly garments when performing the *avoda*. What *Rashi* does not explicitly address — and what remains an open and somewhat striking question — is whether he would also be obligated in the daily *mincha* offering that a *kohen gadol* brings each morning and afternoon. Two *kohanim gedolim* each bringing that offering simultaneously would be a peculiar state of affairs.

### Rabbi Yose

*Rabbi Yose* disagrees. He agrees that the first *kohen gadol* returns to his role, but he holds that *sheni eino ra'ui lo l'Kohen Gadol v'lo l'Kohen Hedyot* — the second is fit neither to serve as *kohen gadol* nor as an ordinary *kohen*. He cites a historical precedent: the case of Yosef ben Illem of Tzippori, who served as a stand-in for the *kohen gadol* and was thereafter ruled by the sages to be disqualified from both roles. This is not presented as a new ruling but as applied precedent.

### Eivah & Maalin

The *Gemara* explains the reasoning behind each half of *Rabbi Yose*'s position. Why can he not serve as *kohen gadol*? *Mishum eivah* — because of the enmity it would generate. The entire institution of the *kohen gadol* is premised on there being a singular figure at the apex of the *kehuna*. Having two individuals simultaneously bearing the title of *kohen gadol* creates an intolerable rivalry. Why, then, can he not revert to serving as a *kohen hedyot*? Because of the principle of *maalin bakodesh velo moridin* — once elevated in sanctity, one cannot be demoted. His appointment to the role of *kohen gadol*, even if temporary, constitutes a real elevation that cannot simply be undone.

The practical implication is this: his true *halachic* status remains that of a *kohen gadol*, but he is prevented from actively exercising that status in the *Beit HaMikdash* due to the conflict it would create. This is not a denial of his status; it is a suspension of its expression.

### If He Performs

*Rabbah bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yossi*. And *Rabbi Yose* himself concedes — *u'modeh Rabbi Yossi she'im avar ve'avad avodato kesherah* — that if the second *kohen* went ahead and performed the *avoda* in defiance of this ruling, the *avoda* would be valid *bedi'avad*. This is a significant concession, because if a *kohen hedyot* were to perform the *avoda* in the eight golden garments of the *kohen gadol*, the *avoda* would be invalid. The fact that it is valid here confirms that his underlying status truly is that of a *kohen gadol*. The ruling is only that we do not permit him to exercise it — not that the status is fictional.

*Rav Yehudah* in the name of *Rav* similarly rules *halacha ke-Rabbi Yossi*, and the implication follows that all the *halachot* that apply to a *kohen gadol* — particularly in the context of bereavement, such as the prohibition against *keriah* and allowing one's hair to grow wild — would also apply to him. The debate, it emerges, reduces to a single practical question: may he actively perform the *avoda*? On that point alone, *Rabbi Yose* says no, on account of the conflict it would generate.

### Met Rishon

*Rav Yehudah* in the name of *Rav* adds a second ruling: *u'modeh Rabbi Yossi she'im met rishon she'chozer la'avodato* — if the first *kohen gadol* dies, the second steps into his place as the new *kohen gadol*. The *Gemara* asks: is this not obvious? If we have established that his status is genuinely that of a *kohen gadol*, of course he would succeed to the role upon the first one's death. The *Gemara* answers that one might have thought otherwise — *tzar mechayim*. As long as the first *kohen gadol* is alive and knows that this person is waiting to assume his position the moment he dies, that itself constitutes a kind of rivalry, an ominous presence that shadows him during his lifetime. One might therefore have thought that to prevent even this form of conflict, the second would not be permitted to succeed even upon the first's death. The ruling tells us we are not concerned about that. The succession is legitimate.

This point is directly relevant to the *mishnah*'s discussion of the Yom Kippur backup *kohen gadol*: should the original become permanently incapacitated or die, the stand-in assumes the role in full. Both *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yose* agree that the substitute acquires the personal status of a *kohen gadol*; their dispute concerns only whether he may actively serve in that capacity during the original's lifetime.

## Backup Wife Debate

### Rabbi Yehuda

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* itself, which raises the question of whether a backup wife should also be prepared for the *kohen gadol* before Yom Kippur. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that indeed *af isha acheret matkinin lo* — they should prepare another woman for him as well. The verse states *ve'chiper ba'ado uva'ad beito*, "and he shall atone for himself and for his household," and the *halacha* understands *beito* to require that he be married. Should his wife die on Yom Kippur, he would no longer fulfill that requirement. Therefore *Rabbi Yehuda* argues for a designated backup wife.

### Infinite Regression

The *Chachamim* object: *im ken ein ladavar sof* — if you are going to worry that his wife might die and therefore prepare a backup, you must worry that the backup will die as well, and there is no end to this logic. The *Gemara* takes this objection seriously and asks why the *Rabbanan* themselves are not equally troubled. They are, after all, concerned that the *kohen gadol* might become *tamei* — which is why a backup *kohen gadol* is appointed in the first place. Why not worry about his wife dying as well?

### Tumah Shchicha

The *Rabbanan* respond: *tumah shchicha* — *tumah* is common. Becoming *tamei* through seminal emission, contact with another *tamei* person, and similar causes is a relatively frequent occurrence. *Mitah lo shchicha* — death, by contrast, is not so common. The possibility that this particular person will die on this particular day is remote enough that we are not required to prepare for it.

### Two Deaths

*Rabbi Yehuda* accepts this logic but applies it asymmetrically. He is willing to be concerned about the unlikely event that one person — the *kohen gadol*'s wife — might die. *Lemitah de'chad chayshinan* — that level of concern is reasonable. *Lemitah detartei lo chayshinan* — that both the wife and the backup wife would die is too remote a scenario to warrant concern. The *Rabbanan* disagree.

### Zariz Argument

The *Rabbanan* respond sharply: once you allow yourself to worry about unreasonable possibilities, there is no principled stopping point. If the chance of one woman dying is already low enough to be dismissed as *lo shchicha*, what is the difference between worrying about that and worrying about two deaths? The *Rabbanan* further challenge *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framework by noting that he has no defensible threshold — if he is concerned about unlikely scenarios, he should be concerned about all unlikely scenarios with equal consistency.

### Two Tamei

The *Gemara* then turns the challenge back on the *Rabbanan* themselves: if they are concerned about the *kohen gadol* becoming *tamei*, they should equally be concerned that both the *kohen gadol* and his backup might become *tamei* on Yom Kippur. The *Rabbanan* respond: *Kohen Gadol zariz hu* — the *kohen gadol* is careful and meticulous, so the concern is much diminished. The *Gemara* probes: if he is so careful, why appoint a backup at all? The answer: *keivan de'avdinan lei tzarah* — precisely because there is a competitor waiting in the wings, he will be all the more vigilant. The backup functions not merely as a contingency but as an incentive. This is quite different from death, which no amount of vigilance can prevent. *Tumah* is avoidable; death is not. That is the fundamental asymmetry between the concerns raised by *Rabbi Yehuda* and those of the *Rabbanan*.

## Backup Wife Problem

### Nisu'in Required

Having established that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position has some merit, the *Gemara* now asks how the backup wife arrangement would actually work in practice. The first obstacle: *beito amar Rachmana* — the *Torah* requires *beito*, his household, meaning an actual wife. Simply having another woman designated in the wings is meaningless if he is not married to her. Fine, the *Gemara* says — he does *kiddushin* with her before Yom Kippur, thus making her his wife.

But the *Gemara* presses further: *v'hachama de'lo kanis lah lav beiso hu* — as long as he has not brought her into his home as his wife, she is not yet his *beito*. The *Torah*'s language of *beito* implies not merely *kiddushin* — betrothal — but *nisu'in*, actual cohabitation, the second stage of marriage in which the couple begins to live together as husband and wife. The Torah's own usage elsewhere reflects this distinction: *ki yikach ish isha uva eileiha* — the "taking" of a wife is associated with physical union; *asher eiras isha velo lekacha* — betrothal is distinct from the consummation of the marriage. The *Gemara* understands *bayit* throughout to refer to *nisu'in*, not merely *kiddushin*.

### One Wife Only

Once the *Gemara* establishes that *nisu'in* is required, a second problem emerges immediately. If he has already done *nisu'in* with the backup wife, he now has two wives simultaneously. But the verse says *beito* — his household, singular — *velo bishnei batim*, not two households. This is a striking point: in a society that formally permitted polygamy, the *Gemara* reads the Yom Kippur requirement as implicitly restricting the *kohen gadol* to one wife, at least for purposes of the *avoda*. The idea of a singular *kohen gadol* who represents the apex of the *kehuna* resonates here with the idea of a singular wife who defines his *bayit*. Just as there cannot be two *kohanim gedolim* serving simultaneously — a point the *Gemara* made earlier in connection with *eivah* — there cannot be two *batim* on Yom Kippur.

The *Gemara* suggests that he could simply divorce the backup wife — but if he divorces her, he is back to square one, with only one wife and no contingency if she dies. The *Gemara* therefore proposes a *get* issued *al tenai* — a conditional divorce — as the mechanism for maintaining exactly one wife at all times.

### Rashi Caveat

Before following the *Gemara* through its discussion of conditional *gittin*, it is important to note what *Rashi* says explicitly about this passage. He writes: *v'chol sugya zo ein teirutzim she'ba yecholim la'amod ki yesh lahem teshuvot rabot* — every single suggestion the *Gemara* is about to offer is subject to numerous objections. *Ve'eino ella kemelamdin otanu v'im tomar ken yesh lehashiv ken v'eino meshiv kol teshuvot she'yesh lehashiv* — it is as if the *Gemara* is teaching us by process of elimination, answering one problem at a time to lead us toward the final correct formulation, without stopping to enumerate every possible objection to each intermediate proposal. *Ki be'achat hu mesalko leteretz inyan acher ad she'he'emidah al mechona* — each step is rejected for a single reason, redirecting the inquiry, until the discussion is finally settled.

This caveat is important because, as will become apparent, many of the proposals the *Gemara* entertains are obviously flawed. *Rashi* preempts the reader's impatience: yes, the suggestions are inadequate in more ways than the *Gemara* acknowledges at each step. The point is not to present watertight proposals but to think through the problem systematically.

The difficulty is fundamentally created by the requirement of *nisu'in*. If *kiddushin* alone sufficed, the solution would be straightforward: betroth the backup wife on the condition that *kiddushin* take effect at the moment his first wife dies. The *kiddushin* would be calibrated to activate precisely when needed and remain inert otherwise. There would be no overlap, no period of two simultaneous wives. But since *nisu'in* — the lived reality of establishing a household together — cannot be effected by a condition triggering at a future moment, the couple must actually begin living together before Yom Kippur. And once they have done so, the *Gemara* must grapple with how to ensure that at any given moment during Yom Kippur, he is married to exactly one woman.

### Conditional Get

The mechanism the *Gemara* pursues is a *get al tenai* — a conditional bill of divorce. The goal is to craft conditions such that whichever wife needs to be "available" at any moment is in fact his wife, and whichever does not need to be available is not. The *Gemara* will work through a series of formulations — most of them flawed in obvious ways — before arriving at what it considers a workable arrangement.

## Get Solutions

### Get If Dies

The first proposal: he says to the backup wife, *harei zeh giteich al menat shetamuti* — "here is your *get* on the condition that you die." This is plainly useless: if she dies, she has no need of a *get*, and he would in any case be left with only wife number one. The *Gemara* immediately rejects this.

Second attempt: *harei zeh giteich al menat shelo tamuti* — "here is your *get* if you do not die." The logic here is more coherent: if the backup wife survives Yom Kippur, the *get* takes effect and she is out of the picture, leaving him married to wife number one alone. If she dies, the *get* does not take effect — but she is dead, so that problem resolves itself, and he remains married to wife number one. The *Gemara* notes that this arrangement handles the basic cases: *ilu lo meita nitgarsha lah* — if she lives, she is divorced; *v'im meita, kaima hacha* — if she dies, wife number one remains.

But the *Gemara* then identifies the fatal flaw: *dilma lo meita v'hava lah gita dehai gita u'meita chaverta* — perhaps she does not die and the *get* therefore takes effect divorcing her, but then wife number one dies. In that case, he has no wife at all. The entire point of the backup wife arrangement was to ensure he always has a wife. This formulation fails to cover the scenario it was designed for.

### Get If Doesn't Die

The *Gemara* considers several further permutations. What if the condition is *al menat shetamus achas michem* — "if one of you dies"? This also fails: if neither dies, the condition is not met and he remains married to both; if one dies, the *get* takes effect for the surviving wife — and he has no wife.

The *Gemara* also raises an independent objection grounded in *Rava*'s ruling: *harei zeh giteich al menat shelo tishtei yayin kol yemei chayai* — a *get* conditioned on a restriction that applies for the entire duration of the husband's life is not valid as a *get*, because *kerisut* — the concept of severance — requires that the divorce effect a clean separation during the lives of both parties. A condition that binds them together indefinitely is inconsistent with the nature of a *get*. This rules out any formulation that ties the condition to the death of either party across the full span of a life.

### Midday Overlap

The *Gemara* makes its most significant advance with the formulation: *harei zeh giteich al menat shelo tamus chaverta* — "this is your *get* if wife number one does not die." Now the logic is considerably closer to the goal. If wife number one survives Yom Kippur, the backup's *get* takes effect and he is left married to wife number one alone. If wife number one dies, the condition is not met, the *get* does not take effect, and he remains married to wife number two. The *Gemara* acknowledges this: *ilu meita chaverta megarsha, v'im meita ha, kaima ha* — whichever wife survives, there is exactly one wife at the end of the day.

But a new and subtle problem emerges: *dilma meita chaverta b'palga d'avoda* — what if wife number one dies in the middle of the day? If she dies at noon, then her death means the *get* does not take effect — he remains married to wife number two. But for the first half of the day, both wives were simultaneously his. The *avoda* of the morning was performed with *shnei batim*, two households, in violation of the requirement of *beito*. The retroactive invalidation of the *get* confirms that the first half of the day was problematic. The question of when exactly wife number one dies creates an unavoidable window of overlap.

### Both Conditional

The *Gemara* arrives at the solution of issuing conditional *gittin* to both wives simultaneously. To wife number two, the *kohen gadol* says: *harei zeh giteich al menat shelo tamus chaverta* — "this is your *get* if wife number one does not die." To wife number one, he says: *harei zeh giteich al menat she'ekanes ani l'veit haknesset* — "this is your *get* if I go into a *beit knesset*."

The two conditions work in tandem. If wife number one survives the day, her *get* does not take effect — she remains his wife — and wife number two's *get* does take effect, leaving him with one wife. If wife number one dies during the day, wife number two remains his wife. The residual problem of the midday overlap — if wife number one dies at noon, what about the morning? — is resolved by the trigger mechanism of the *kohen gadol*'s own action. If he sees that wife number one is about to die, he goes into the *beit knesset* (or performs whatever act serves as the designated trigger), thereby activating her *get* retroactively, so that she is considered divorced from the start of that day. The retroactive *get* eliminates the overlap.

The choice of "entering a *beit knesset*" as the trigger is, as the *Gemara* implicitly acknowledges, a somewhat arbitrary substitute for what one would really want to say, which is "if you die" — a condition that cannot be attached to a *get* because of the *kerisut* principle. Some other observable act must be designated instead, one the *kohen gadol* can choose to perform or withhold as circumstances require.

This entire discussion is, as *Rashi* cautioned, more a framework for thinking through the problem than a fully worked-out practical solution. Each step clears one obstacle while leaving others unaddressed, and the *Gemara* is transparent that many objections could be raised that it does not stop to answer.

### Rav Ashi

*Rav Ashi* — and some say *Rav Avira* — now raises a fundamental challenge to the premise that drove the entire discussion. The *Gemara* assumed throughout that the word *bayit* in the verse *ve'chiper ba'ado uva'ad beito* must refer to a single wife, making it impossible for the *kohen gadol* to have two wives simultaneously on Yom Kippur. But does *bayit* really carry this restriction elsewhere in *halacha*?

*Rav Ashi* points to *yibbum*: *shtei yevamot ha'ba'ot mi'bayit echad* — the scenario of two wives from one household is the first *mishnah* of Masechet Yevamot and forms the basis of the entire tractate. The verse governing *yibbum* states *asher lo yivneh et beit achiv* — "that he not build his brother's *bayit*" — yet we never restrict *yibbum* to a man who had only one wife. If *bayit* means "one wife," all of *Yevamot* would be undermined. The *Gemara* responds that *yevimto yevimto* — the word "*yevimto*," his sister-in-law, is written in a form that is understood to include even multiple wives in the obligation of *yibbum*.

*Ravina* — and some say *Rav Shravya* — presses further: if *bayit* implies *nisu'in* rather than *kiddushin*, then *yibbum* should not apply to an *arusah*, a betrothed woman with whom *nisu'in* has not yet taken place. Yet *halacha* clearly holds that an *arusah* is subject to *yibbum*. The *Gemara* answers from the word *ha'chutzah* in the *yibbum* passage, which implies a woman still "outside" her husband's house — i.e., one with whom *nisu'in* has not yet occurred — is nonetheless included in the *parasha* of *yibbum*.

*Rav Ashi*'s challenge is a serious one: the entire edifice of the *Gemara*'s discussion of conditional *gittin* was built on the premise that *beito* means one wife and that *nisu'in* is required. Both premises require special *derashot* to be sustained when tested against other areas of *halacha*. This does not invalidate the readings, but it shows that the *Gemara* is aware of the tension and addresses it directly.

## Onen Status

### Normal Kohen

The *Gemara* introduces a new *baraita*: *Kohen Gadol makriv onen v'eino ochel* — a *kohen gadol* brings offerings even when he is an *onen*, an immediate mourner in the period between a relative's death and burial, but he does not eat from the *korbanot* during that period. This ruling contrasts with the rule for an ordinary *kohen*, who may not perform the *avoda* at all while an *onen*. For a *kohen hedyot*, doing the *avoda* as an *onen* would invalidate the service entirely.

### Kohen Gadol Exception

The basis for the *kohen gadol*'s exception is the verse *u'min ha'Mikdash lo yeitzei* — "and from the *Mikdash* he shall not go out" — which applies in the context of bereavement. The *kohen gadol* is commanded to remain at his post even when a close relative has died. He therefore continues the *avoda* in a state of *aninut*. However, his exemption extends only to the performance of the service itself; he remains prohibited from eating *korbanot* while an *onen*, since the permission flows from the specific requirement that he not abandon his post, not from any broader suspension of *aninut* status.

*Rava* comments on the phrase *kol ha'yom* — "the whole day" — used by *Rabbi Yehuda* in this connection, understanding it initially to mean that we would even compel the *kohen gadol* to leave his home during a bereavement in order to perform the *avoda*. *Abaye* challenges this: *Rabbi Yehuda* himself rules, in the case of an ordinary *kohen* who was in the middle of the *avoda* when he learned that a relative had died, that he must stop immediately — *maniach avodato v'yotzei*. If *Rabbi Yehuda* holds so stringently about the *aninut* invalidating an ordinary *kohen*'s *avoda*, how could he simultaneously hold that for the *kohen gadol* we drag him out of his house and make him perform the *avoda* as an *onen*?

### Gezeira Shema

*Rava* therefore revises his interpretation of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position. When *Rabbi Yehuda* says *kol ha'yom*, he does not mean we compel the *kohen gadol* to perform the *avoda*; rather, he means the opposite. He holds that *rabbinically*, the *kohen gadol* should not perform the *avoda* during the entire day of the death, even though biblically he is permitted and even required to do so. The concern is *gezeira shema yokhal* — a rabbinic precautionary measure lest he come to eat from the *korbanot*. Since performing the *avoda* and eating from the *korbanot* are normally coupled activities, there is a concern that if he performs the *avoda* as an *onen* he might come to eat as well, which would be prohibited. At night, however, *Rabbi Yehuda* agrees he may perform the *avoda*, because biblical *aninut* applies only during the day of death; at night, even if rabbinical *aninut* applies, eating from *korbanot* consumed at night would not constitute a biblical violation. Therefore the rabbinic concern is limited to the daytime.

### Tomorrow's Topic

The connection of this *baraita* to the larger discussion of Yom Kippur will be taken up in the next session. The scenario the *Gemara* is building toward is this: if the *kohen gadol*'s wife dies during the Yom Kippur *avoda* — the very contingency that generated the entire discussion of conditional *gittin* and backup wives — he becomes an *onen* at that moment. Can he continue the *avoda*? Biblically, as established, a *kohen gadol* continues his service even as an *onen*, grounded in *u'min ha'Mikdash lo yeitzei*. But does *Rabbi Yehuda*'s rabbinic concern apply here as well, given the context of Yom Kippur? That question, and its implications for the entire structure of the Yom Kippur service, will be the focus of the next daf.
