# Sukkah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fruit Stems & Schach

### Mekabel Tumah Issue

The discussion on this *daf* begins at the bottom of 13b, in the midst of a broad examination of invalid *schach*. The particular issue at hand concerns items that are *mekabel tumah* — susceptible to becoming *tamei* — and specifically the problem posed by fruit growing from a tree. Since fruit is edible, it qualifies as food and is therefore *mekabel tumah*. This raises an immediate question about the *yad* — the stem or twig connecting the fruit to the branch: does it share the fruit's status, or is it to be treated separately?

The practical stakes for *schach* are significant. If one cuts a branch bearing fruit and uses it as *schach*, the fruit itself is invalid *schach* because it is *mekabel tumah*. The question is whether the stems attached to that fruit are counted among the invalid *schach* as well. The answer turns on whether the stems are halachically considered an appendage of the fruit. Normally, the rule of *yad* — that the stem is treated as part of the fruit — applies only when one is interested in the fruit being connected to it. When cutting for *schach*, by contrast, one is presumably not interested in the fruit at all, which would argue against classifying the stem as a *yad*.

### Stem Status Debate

The counterargument, however, is that even when cutting for *schach*, one may in fact want the fruit attached — not to eat it, but because its weight helps hold the *schach* in place. This tension was debated among the *Amora'im*, and a *baraita* was cited that dealt with precisely this scenario: large palm fronds bearing dates, and whether the stems are counted toward the measure of invalid *schach* when such a frond is placed atop a *sukkah*. The *baraita* appears to encode exactly this disagreement.

The *Gemara* then asks whether there is any way to understand the *baraita* such that all opinions actually agree the stems are never counted when cutting for *schach*. If so, the apparent debate in the *baraita* must be recharacterized.

### Kavana Question

Rav Menashe bar Gadda proposes precisely such a reading. He argues that *kulei alma* — everyone — agrees that if one cuts the branch for the purpose of *schach*, the stem has no status as a *yad* relative to the fruit, and therefore poses no problem of invalid *schach*. The apparent debate in the *baraita*, he argues, does not concern a case where the cutting was done with *schach* in mind from the outset.

### Intent for Eating

Rather, says Rav Menashe bar Gadda, *ki pligi* — where the *tanna'im* disagree — is in a case where the branch was cut initially *le'achila*, for the purpose of eating the fruit. When fruit is cut with eating in mind, the stem is clearly a *yad* — the classic rule applies. The question arises when the owner subsequently changes his mind and decides to use the branch as *schach* instead. Does this new intention retroactively dissolve the status the stem had already acquired as a *yad* to the fruit?

This reframing of the debate transforms it from a question about the nature of *schach* into a question about *kavana*: can a change of intention alter a halachic status that has already been established?

## Machshava & Ma'aseh

### Vessels & Intent

To address this question, the *Gemara* invokes a general principle from the laws of *tumah*, citing the *mishna* at the top of 14a: *kol hakelim yordim liydei tumah bemachshava* — all vessels descend into *tumah* through thought alone. The language of "descending" is striking; the *mishna* means that through one's intention, an object acquires the identity and susceptibility of a vessel.

### Ambiguous Status

The principle applies to objects with an ambiguous status — those whose identity as a *kli* is not yet fixed. Many objects in the world are clearly vessels and therefore automatically *mekabel tumah*, while others are clearly not. But there is an intermediate category: objects whose identity is not objectively determinable and depends on the owner's intent. Rashi illustrates this with the example of a large piece of tanned leather. In its current form it could function as a mat, or it could be cut into strips for shoes. Until the owner's intent clarifies the matter, the object's halachic identity remains unsettled.

Once the owner declares — even without any physical act — that this leather is to be a mat, that intention fixes its identity. It is now a vessel and becomes susceptible to *tumah* through *machshava* alone.

### Action vs. Intent

The second half of the *mishna*'s principle is equally important: *ve'ein olim mitumatan ella beshinuy ma'aseh* — objects cannot lose that status except through a change of action. Once an object's identity has been established — whether through intent alone or through physical action — a new intention is insufficient to dislodge it. Something more concrete is required: an actual physical change to the object itself.

The *Gemara* articulates this principle systematically. *Ma'aseh motzi midei ma'aseh* — an action can reverse a prior action. If one fashioned the leather into the shape of a mat through physical work, a subsequent act of cutting it into a different shape redefines it. An action is also sufficient to override a prior intention. But a new *machshava* — however sincere — cannot override a prior action, and crucially, it cannot even override a prior *machshava*. *Machshava* is powerful enough to resolve an ambiguous identity and fix it, but once identity is established, thought alone cannot undo it.

The underlying logic is that *machshava* operates specifically on things with ambiguous identity. It resolves that ambiguity. But once identity is clear, there is nothing for *machshava* to operate on — the only instrument capable of changing what is now a settled status is a physical act.

### Apply to Stems

Applying this principle to the stem of a fruit, the *Gemara* reasons as follows. In the case Rav Menashe bar Gadda describes — where the branch was cut initially *le'achila* — the stem's status as a *yad* to the fruit was established from the outset. Now the owner changes his mind and decides to use the branch as *schach*. That change of mind is a new *machshava*. But if the principle from *kol hakelim* applies here, a new *machshava* cannot override a status already established by prior *machshava*. So how could the owner's new intent possibly reclassify the stem?

One might distinguish between vessels and stems: perhaps for a *kli*, where a significant identity is at stake, a mere thought cannot reassign it — but the status of a *yad* is not really a fixed identity of the object itself, only a functional question of what it is being used to serve. Since it is a question of function rather than identity, perhaps *machshava* can freely toggle it back and forth — *machshava mashvia lei umachshava mafkia lei*.

The *Gemara* rejects this distinction, however, citing the *mishna* in *Uktzin* dealing with the stems of food, which provides an illuminating parallel case.

## Uktzin Stems

### Stems of Food

The *Gemara* introduces the relevant passage from *Uktzin*: *kol yadot ha'ochlim shebissasan bagoren, tehoros* — all the stems of food that were *bissasan* in the threshing floor are *tahor*, meaning the stems are no longer classified as *yadot* of the food. Rabbi Yossi, however, says they remain *tamei*, indicating that he regards the stems as still part of the food even after *bissasan* in the threshing floor.

### Bissasan Meanings

The key dispute within this *mishna* turns on what *bissasan* means. Two *Amora'im* disagree. Rabbi Elazar says *bissasan* means *hitar agdan* — one undid the bundle. Rabbi Yochanan says *bissasan mamash* — one literally threshed the grain.

To understand why this matters, one must consider the role the stem plays for wheat. The stem — the straw — serves the wheat by allowing it to be bundled into a sheaf, which makes carrying possible. Once the bundle is brought to the threshing floor and the owner undoes it, intending to thresh the grain, the straw no longer serves this carrying function. According to Rabbi Elazar, this undoing of the bundle suffices to change the stem's status: even without physically altering the grain or the straw, the act of unbundling in the threshing floor makes clear that the stem is no longer needed, and it loses its classification as a *yad*.

The parallel to the *schach* case is striking: just as placing the branch on top of the *sukkah* is an act done *with* the object rather than *to* it, so too undoing the bundle is an act done in context of a new purpose without physically changing the object itself. According to Rabbi Elazar, such contextual action is sufficient to dissolve the *yad* status.

### Rabbi Yossi View

According to Rabbi Yochanan, however, *bissasan* means literal threshing — a physical act performed on the grain itself. On this reading, only a direct physical change to the object can reclassify the stem's status. The parallel for *schach* would be that one must physically alter the branch — beating it down, flattening it — before the stem can change its classification.

But if that is so, why does Rabbi Yossi maintain that even after literal threshing, the stem remains a *yad*? The *Gemara* cites Resh Lakish's explanation: *hoil ve'raui le'hofchan be'atara* — since the straw remains fit to be turned over with a pitchfork. Even after one begins threshing the wheat, the straw still serves a functional purpose: it can be used to maneuver the grain with a pitchfork when flipping it to thresh from the other side. Because the straw retains this use, Rabbi Yossi holds that it continues to be a *yad*.

At this point, the *Gemara* quotes a beautiful *derasha* from Rabbi Elazar: why does the Torah use the word *vaye'etar* — etymologically connected to *attar*, pitchfork — to describe Yitzchak's prayer on behalf of his wife? Just as a pitchfork turns the grain from one side to another in the threshing floor, so does the prayer of the righteous turn God's attribute of — the text uses striking language — *middat achzariyut* to *middat rachamanut*, from cruelty to compassion. The imagery of prayer as an implement of reversal, turning over the divine disposition, is powerful.

### Stem Function

Returning to the *schach* case: the *Gemara* now presses on the analogy. If on Rabbi Yochanan's reading one must physically alter the object to change the *yad* status, and the *Gemara* posits that this was done — that one actually did something physical to make the branch into *schach* — why would anyone still consider the stem part of the fruit? If even in the threshing case Rabbi Yossi only maintains the *yad* status because the straw retains a useful function (turning the grain with a pitchfork), what useful function does the stem serve here, after the branch has been physically worked into *schach*?

The *Gemara* answers: *chazya lechisasa lemi'intelei bigila'ei* — it remains fit for use after *Sukkot* ends. The Yom Tov will not last forever. When one dismantles the *sukkah* after *Sukkot*, the branch will still have fruit attached, and the owner — who originally cut the branch with eating in mind — will want to handle the fruit using the stem. Therefore, even in the midst of using it as *schach*, the stem retains its functional connection to the fruit, and its *yad* status is not fully dissolved.

This conclusion provides the key to the entire debate. The question of whether the stem is counted as invalid *schach* or as valid *schach* depends on whether, under the circumstances, the stem still serves the fruit. The underlying point of disagreement — what physical act is required to sever that connection, and whether any act suffices given the stem's anticipated future use — is what the *tanna'im* dispute.

The *Gemara* then returns to the *mishna* of *Uktzin* proper, introducing the discussion with the word *gufa* — signaling that what had been quoted piecemeal in the course of the earlier argument will now be examined as a whole. Rashi notes, in what appears to be a student's gloss, that *gufa* is ordinarily used to reexamine a *baraita* or an amoraic statement, not a *mishna*. The explanation offered is that this *mishna* is from the order of *Taharot*, for which there is no *Gemara* in the world — the *Babylonian Talmud* contains no tractate *Gemara* on *Taharot*. Therefore, the only opportunity to examine and explicate this *mishna* is in the course of a discussion elsewhere, which justifies the unusual use of *gufa* here.

The *mishna* in *Uktzin* is then quoted in full: *kol yadot ha'ochlim shebissasan bagoren, tehoros, v'Rabbi Yossi metamei*. The *Gemara* presents the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar over what *bissasan* means, as described above, and concludes with Resh Lakish's rationale for Rabbi Yossi's position: the straw remains useful as a *yad* even after threshing, because it can be moved with a pitchfork.

## Planks of Wood

### Planks Debate

The *mishna* now moves to a new topic: *mesachin b'nisarin divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — one may use planks of wood as *schach*, according to Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir, however, forbids it. The most obvious reason for Rabbi Meir's position is the *gezeirat tikra* — the rabbinic concern that planks of wood laid across the top of a structure look like a standard roof, and using them as *schach* might blur the distinction between a *sukkah* and an ordinary roofed structure. The *mishna* then adds: if one placed a plank that is four *tefachim* wide upon the *sukkah*, it remains *keshera* — provided one does not sleep beneath it. Rashi explains this is referring to a plank placed adjacent to the wall, where it can be treated as a bent extension of the wall (*dofen akuma*), but since it is invalid *schach* it may not be slept under.

### Rav's View

Rav explains the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir as follows: *machlokes b'nisarin sheyesh bahen arba'ah* — they argue specifically about planks that are four *tefachim* wide. Rabbi Meir holds the *gezeirat tikra*, and therefore invalidates such planks. Rabbi Yehuda does not hold the *gezeirat tikra* at all, and therefore permits even four-*tefach* planks. But below four *tefachim*, *divrei hakol keshera* — all agree the *sukkah* is valid, because no one would mistake such a narrow piece of wood for a roofing plank.

### Shmuel's View

Shmuel disagrees. In his reading, the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir concerns planks *less* than four *tefachim*. Once a plank reaches four *tefachim* in width, *divrei hakol psula* — everyone agrees it is invalid. Every *tanna* concedes the *gezeirat tikra* at that size. The argument is only about smaller planks: Rabbi Meir says even they are problematic; Rabbi Yehuda says they are not.

### Size Significance

Rav Pappa refines the position of Rav. He clarifies that below three *tefachim*, all agree the *sukkah* is valid — the *Gemara* dismisses such a thin piece of wood as *kani b'alma*, nothing more than a stick. Above four *tefachim*, all agree it is problematic. The debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir, on Rav's view, is confined to the range of three to four *tefachim*.

The conceptual stakes are sharpened here. Both positions agree that the *gezeirat tikra* is a real concern at some size threshold; the argument is about which size qualifies. Rabbi Meir holds that three *tefachim* or more is significant, because once something exceeds the range of *lavud* — the halachic principle that gaps of less than three *tefachim* are treated as closed — it constitutes a recognizable width. Rabbi Yehuda holds that below four *tefachim* one does not yet have *shiur makom*, a significant spatial unit unto itself, and therefore the *gezeirat tikra* does not yet apply. It is a striking feature of this analysis that what begins as a pragmatic concern — does this look like a roof? — is resolved not by asking what roofs actually look like, but by applying formal halachic categories: *lavud* and *shiur makom*.

## Combining Planks

### Sheets vs. Planks

The *mishna* teaches that *shnei sadinim mitztarfin* — two sheets combine to create a problematic measure of invalid *schach*. The *Gemara* now asks whether the same combination rule applies to planks. The *mishna* continues: *shnei nesarim ein mitztarfin* — two planks do not combine. Rabbi Meir, however, says they do: *af nesarim k'sadinim*, planks are like sheets.

### Two Planks Case

To understand the combination question, one must clarify what measure the combining is meant to create. Two sheets of invalid *schach*, each less than four *tefachim* wide, placed side by side, combine to produce four *tefachim* of invalid *schach*. Could two planks do the same? This question fits naturally with Shmuel's reading of the debate. If Rabbi Meir holds that planks less than four *tefachim* are already problematic — say, a plank of three *tefachim* — then a second such plank placed alongside the first would combine with it, and the total invalid *schach* would exceed four *tefachim*. Rabbi Yehuda, on this reading, says no such plank is problematic on its own and therefore there is nothing to combine.

On Rav's reading, however, this case is difficult to construct. If Rabbi Meir says planks are only problematic at four *tefachim*, then a single plank already constitutes four *tefachim* of invalid *schach* — there is nothing left to combine toward. And if the plank is less than four *tefachim*, Rabbi Meir himself says it is fine.

### Side Combination

The *Gemara* resolves this difficulty for Rav's position. The combining is not toward a measure of four *tefachim* of invalid *schach* in the center of the *sukkah*, but rather toward *arba amot min hatzad* — four *amot* worth of planks placed along the side. The significance of four *amot* along the side is its relationship to *dofen akuma*: the principle that invalid *schach* adjacent to a wall can be treated as a bent extension of that wall, thereby preserving the *sukkah*'s validity. This leniency applies only when the invalid *schach* extends no more than four *amot* from the wall. If plank after plank is placed along the side and the total exceeds four *amot*, the *dofen akuma* principle can no longer save the *sukkah*. According to Rav, Rabbi Meir's concern about planks combining is precisely this: accumulation along the side until the four-*amot* limit of *dofen akuma* is exceeded.

On this reading, Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that planks are valid *schach*, would say they simply do not combine — because they are not invalid to begin with. The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yehuda could equally have said these planks are all perfectly valid *schach*; his statement that they do not combine is essentially a reactive formulation, stated in counterpoint to Rabbi Meir's claim that they do.

### Baraita Evidence

The *Gemara* then notes that there is a *baraita* that supports Rav and a *baraita* that supports Shmuel. The *baraita* supporting Rav explicitly states: if one used cedar planks less than four *tefachim* wide, *divrei hakol kesheira* — all agree it is valid. Once the plank is four *tefachim* wide, Rabbi Meir invalidates and Rabbi Yehuda validates. This maps precisely onto Rav's position that the debate concerns four *tefachim* and larger, with no debate below that threshold.

## Historical Anecdote

### Persecution Account

Within the *baraita* supporting Rav, Rabbi Yehuda himself offers an account from personal experience to demonstrate that four-*tefach* planks are permitted. He relates: *ma'aseh bi'she'at hassakana* — there was an incident during a time of persecution, when it was dangerous to build a *sukkah* openly. He speaks in the first-person plural, and given that Rabbi Yehuda was a student of Rabbi Akiva, this *she'at hassakana* appears to be something he actually lived through. He describes how they brought planks that were four *tefachim* wide, placed them atop a porch, and dwelt beneath them — to all appearances sitting under an ordinary covered portico, while in fact fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*.

### Sakana Caveat

The *Gemara* immediately deflects the proof: *ein she'at hassakana re'ayah* — conduct during a time of persecution cannot serve as a normative halachic proof. What the rabbis permitted under conditions of mortal danger does not establish what is permissible in ordinary circumstances. The historical note is poignant, but it carries no probative weight.

### Shmuel Baraita

The *baraita* supporting Shmuel presents the opposite ruling: once a plank reaches four *tefachim*, *divrei hakol pesula* — all agree it is invalid. Below four *tefachim*, that is where the debate lies: Rabbi Meir invalidates and Rabbi Yehuda validates.

### Meir's Concession

The *baraita* continues with a significant concession: *u'modeh Rabbi Meir* — even Rabbi Meir agrees that if there is a gap between one plank and the next equal in width to a plank itself, and one fills that gap with valid *schach* — *pesoles goren veyekev*, the term used for valid *schach* — the *sukkah* is valid. The *Gemara* explains the mechanics of this ruling, but the essential point is that interspersing valid *schach* between the planks can rescue the *sukkah* through a combination of halachic principles, including *dofen akuma* applied on both sides. Correspondingly, *u'modeh Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda, who is generally lenient, admits that if a plank four *tefachim* wide is placed on the *sukkah*, even if the *sukkah* remains valid by virtue of *dofen akuma*, one may not sleep beneath that plank — and if one does, one has not fulfilled the obligation.

## Rotating Planks

### Side Rotation

The *Gemara* now raises a new question: *hafkhan al tzideihen* — what if one takes these four-*tefach* planks, which are invalid, and rotates them ninety degrees before placing them on the *sukkah*? Oriented on their side, they are no longer four *tefachim* wide in the way that triggered the *gezeirat tikra*. Does this rotation render them valid *schach*? Three *Amora'im* address this question. Rav Huna says the *sukkah* remains *pesula*. Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna — the latter being Rav Huna's own son — say it is *keshera*.

### Rav Nachman Rules

When Rav Nachman happened to visit Sura, Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna came to him and posed the question. Rav Nachman ruled: *pesula* — it remains invalid. His reasoning: *na'asu k'shfudim shel matechet* — once the rabbis declared these planks invalid, they confer upon them the full status of invalid *schach*, comparable to metal bars. Metal bars are invalid *schach* regardless of how they are oriented, because the invalidation is categorical. The same applies here: the *gezeirat tikra* did not merely forbid a particular configuration; it rendered the planks themselves invalid *schach* in a wholesale manner, such that reorienting them effects no change.

### Huna Vindicated

Rav Huna, upon hearing this, turned to Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna with a pointed remark: had he not told them to rule as he did? They replied that he had never given them his reasoning, and that Rav Nachman had now supplied the rationale they needed to accept the ruling. Rav Huna countered: had they ever asked him for a reason and he had failed to give one? If they wanted a reason, they should have asked.

### Doorway Proof

The *Gemara* then attempts to bring a proof for the position that reorientation does not help. The *baraita* cited enumerates three ways a *sukkah* can be invalidated, one of which is: *shenatan aleha neser sherachav arba'ah tefachim* — if a plank four *tefachim* wide was placed on it. The *baraita* adds: *af al pi shelo hichnis letocha ella shlosha tefachim, pesula* — even if only three of the four *tefachim* extend over the interior of the *sukkah*, it is invalid. The *Gemara* initially reads this as a case where the plank has been rotated: four *tefachim* in one orientation becomes three *tefachim* covering the *sukkah* in the rotated orientation — and still it is invalid.

The *Gemara* rejects this reading, however. The case is not one of rotation. Rather, it describes a plank laid flat in its normal orientation, but positioned so that three *tefachim* extend over the interior of the *sukkah* and one *tefach* extends beyond the wall to the outside. The reason this is nonetheless treated as a four-*tefach* plank is the principle of *psal hayotzei min hasukkah*: any *schach* extending beyond the *sukkah*'s boundary is halachically treated as though it is inside the *sukkah*. If this principle makes a small overhang count toward valid *sukkah* coverage when the *schach* is valid, the same principle causes the overhanging portion of an invalid plank to count toward its full four-*tefach* measurement, even though only three *tefachim* actually lie over the interior.

The net result is that this *baraita* does not prove the case about rotation directly. Nevertheless, the ruling is clear from the earlier amoraic discussion: once *Chazal* invalidated the planks, they are treated as categorically invalid *schach*, and rotation does not help.

## Practical Ruling

### 3-1 Scenario

The *Gemara's* final discussion examines the case of the plank positioned with three *tefachim* inside the *sukkah* and one *tefach* outside. As noted, the principle of *psal hayotzei min hasukkah* treats the overhanging portion as though it, too, is inside, making the full four-*tefach* width operative. This principle — that *schach* extending outside the *sukkah* is treated as if it were inside for purposes of the relevant halachic rules — thus applies symmetrically: if it makes valid *schach* outside count for valid coverage inside, it equally makes invalid *schach* outside count toward the full measure of invalidity inside.

### Extension Rule

The *Gemara* establishes that the extension of *psal* outside the *sukkah* is counted as *sukkah* for halachic purposes in both directions. This resolves the question about the three-in, one-out scenario: the *baraita*'s ruling that such a plank invalidates the *sukkah* as a full four-*tefach* plank follows directly from this principle.

### Tosafot Psak

A practical question remains: the *Gemara* presents two competing *baraitot*, one supporting Rav and one supporting Shmuel. Which interpretation governs the *halacha*? Tosafot conclude that from the plain reading of this *Gemara*, the ruling follows Shmuel's interpretation. Although as a general rule we follow Rav over Shmuel in halachic disputes, Tosafot infer from the *Gemara*'s treatment of the material that here we rule like Shmuel. The practical consequence is that even planks between three and four *tefachim* in width remain within the scope of the debate — and Rabbi Meir's position that they are invalid would need to be taken into account.
