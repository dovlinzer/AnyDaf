# Yoma 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shoveling Coals

### Silver to Gold Transfer

The daf opens in the middle of 44b with the *mishnah*'s discussion of the shoveling of hot coals from the top of the altar — a necessary preliminary to the *ketoret* service of *Yom Kippur*. The *mishnah* states: *bechol yom haya choteh beshel kesef u-me'arah letoch shel zahav* — on every ordinary day the *kohen* would shovel the coals using a silver vessel and then pour them into a gold one. The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for this two-vessel system? The answer is *HaTorah chasa al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah is concerned with the waste of Jewish money. Gold is a soft metal, and using it to shovel directly into the coals would cause rapid deterioration. Therefore, the silver vessel absorbs that wear, while the gold vessel — brought in for the actual service — is preserved.

### Yom Kippur Gold Vessel

On *Yom Kippur*, however, the procedure changes: *vehayom choteh beshel zahav uva haya machnis* — today he shovels directly with the gold vessel and carries it in with that same vessel. The *Gemara* explains: *mishum chulshat Kohen Gadol* — because of the physical weakness of the *Kohen Gadol*, who has been fasting, one does not wish to burden him with unnecessary manipulation of vessels.

### Pesuk-Based Approach

The *Rashash* notes that there is arguably a more straightforward reason rooted in the *pesukim* themselves: the plain sense of the verse implies that the same vessel used to shovel the coals is the one brought into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. This raises a further question about the scope of the *mishnah*'s discussion. *Rashi* understands the *mishnah* as referring specifically to what is distinctive about the *ketoret* of the *Kodesh Kodashim*. But one might read the *mishnah* more broadly — that certain special practices observed on *Yom Kippur* applied even to the regular morning and afternoon *ketoret*, not only to the inner service. The question of whether the single-vessel procedure on *Yom Kippur* reflects a *pesuk*-based requirement or merely an accommodation to the *Kohen Gadol*'s condition bears on which reading of the *mishnah* is correct.

### Quantity Debate

The *mishnah* also records that on a normal day the *kohen* would shovel with a vessel holding four *kabin* and pour into one holding three, whereas on *Yom Kippur* he shoveled directly with the three-*kav* vessel. A *baraita* is then cited teaching that one *kav* of coals would scatter when the transfer was made — consistent with going from four to three — while another *baraita* states that two *kabin* would scatter. The *Gemara* observes: *bishlama hach detani kav* — the position of one extra *kav* scattering corresponds to the *Rabbanan*, who say four is poured into three. But who holds that two would scatter? *Lo Rabbanan ve-lo Rabbi Yosi* — not the *Rabbanan* (four to three) and not *Rabbi Yosi* (six to three, which would yield three extra).

The answer is given in the name of *Rav Shizbi* citing *Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*: he shoveled with four, as the *Rabbanan*, but poured into a vessel holding only two *kabin*, yielding two extra. So we now have three positions: the vessel brought in held two, three, or (according to *Rabbi Yosi*) that you shoveled with six. *Rav Ashi* then resolves the difficulty with *Rabbi Yosi*'s position: *afilu tema Rabbi Yosi* — one can even maintain *Rabbi Yosi*'s view, since the six-*kav* measure was a *se'ah midbarit* — a wilderness measure — whereas the vessel that received the coals held three *kabin Yerushalmi'ot*, the larger Jerusalem measure. Since a *se'ah midbarit* equals five *kabin Yerushalmi'ot*, only two *kabin* would be extra, which accounts for the *baraita* that teaches two scatter.

## Fire Pan Modifications

### Thin Gold & Long Handle

The *mishnah* continues to enumerate the differences between the ordinary daily procedure and that of *Yom Kippur*. A *baraita* elaborates: *bechol yom hayetah guldah avah ve-hayom rakah* — on a normal day the wall of the fire pan was thick, while on *Yom Kippur* it was thin. *U-ve-chol yom hayetah ketzarah ve-hayom arucha* — the handle was short on ordinary days, but long on *Yom Kippur*. The reason: *kedei she-tehei zero'o shel Kohen Gadol mesaya'ato* — so that the *Kohen Gadol*'s arm could assist him in manipulating the vessel. This is in anticipation of the subsequent service in which the *Kohen Gadol*, holding the fire pan in one hand and the incense in the other, must pour the incense using his arms and elbows in a confined space.

### Niashti Ring/Sheath

Another *baraita* records the teaching of *Ben Ha-Segan* — the son of the deputy *Kohen Gadol* — that *bechol yom lo hayah lah niashti ve-hayom hayah lah niashti*: on ordinary days the fire pan did not have a *niashti*, while on *Yom Kippur* it did. The word *niashti* is unusual and lacks a clear parallel in other contexts, so its meaning must be inferred from the commentators.

### Rashi Sound Theory

*Rashi* explains that the *niashti* was a ring (*taba'at*) attached to the vessel that would clatter against the metal as the *Kohen Gadol* walked — *she-mekashkesh u-mashmi'a kol* — producing a sound upon his entry. The conceptual basis is the principle of *ve-nishma kolo be-vo'o*: when the *Kohen Gadol* enters the *heichal*, there should be some audible signal of his approach. On ordinary days this is accomplished by the bells of the *me'il*. On *Yom Kippur*, however, he wears only the *bigdei lavan* — the white garments — which have no bells. *Rashi*'s position is therefore that the ring on the fire pan served as a substitute, at least when the *Kohen Gadol* was carrying it, for the sound that the *me'il* would ordinarily produce.

### Tosafot Leather Sheath

*Tosafot* quotes *Rashi* and then notes that the *Yerushalmi* reads *nartik* rather than *niashti* — and *nartik* denotes a sheath. The *Riva* interprets this as a leather covering wrapped around the handle: *k'ein nartik shel or haya asui saviv yada* — like a pot holder placed around the handle to prevent burning. The question this raises is: why was such protection not needed on ordinary days? The answer offered is that the concern was not the initial carrying of the fire pan into the *Kodesh Kodashim*, which is a matter of minutes, but rather the later retrieval. After the *Kohen Gadol* placed the *ketoret* on the coals, he would go and perform the other *avodos*, and only afterward return to retrieve the fire pan — which by that point had been sitting with burning coals inside for an extended time, rendering the handle dangerously hot. Furthermore, on *Yom Kippur* the gold was thinner and the fire pan had a longer handle, both of which may have affected heat distribution. *Tosafot*'s explanation — that the issue is prolonged exposure during the interval before retrieval — accounts for why this protection was needed specifically on *Yom Kippur* and not on ordinary days.

## Gold Types

### Seven Gold Types

The *mishnah* states that on ordinary days the fire pan was made of *zahav yarok* — yellow-green gold — while on *Yom Kippur* a red gold was used. *Rav Chisda* teaches: *shiva zehavim hem* — there are seven types of gold. He enumerates them as follows. First, plain *zahav* and *zahav tov* — standard gold and good gold — derived from the verse in *Bereishit*: *ve-zahav ha'aretz hahi tov*, which implies that there is ordinary gold and then a superior grade. Third, *zahav Ofir* — gold from the land of *Ofir* — which is presumably distinguished by its origin and attendant quality. Fourth, *zahav mufaz* — gold that resembles *paz*. *Rashi* explains that *paz* shines like a precious stone, *matzhiv k'margalit*. Fifth, *zahav shachut* — literally "drawn" gold — which *Rashi* describes as gold so soft and pliable it can be drawn out like a thread. Sixth, *zahav sagur* — "closed" gold — described as being of such exceptional quality that *besha'ah sheniftach kol hachanuyot nisgaru*: when this gold comes to market, all the other gold shops close, unable to compete. Seventh, *zahav parvayim*.

### Red Gold Symbol

*Zahav parvayim* is explained: *she-domeh l'dam ha-parim* — it resembles the blood of oxen. This is the red gold used for the fire pan on *Yom Kippur*.

### Gold & Kaparah Link

The choice of red gold is not merely a matter of expense or rarity. The *Gemara* elsewhere linked the *ketoret* service on *Yom Kippur* with the blood service: the *Gemara* taught that both the incense and the blood require the clearing of the *heichal* — *bevo'o l'chaper bakodesh* — and that the *ketoret* too is *mechaper*. Given that the *ketoret* and the blood of the *par* and *sa'ir* are thus intertwined in the framework of *kaparah*, it is fitting that the vessel carrying the incense coals is fashioned from a gold whose very color evokes the blood — *she-domeh l'dam ha-parim*. The *baraita* subsequently cited confirms this identification explicitly: *bechol yom hayah zehavo yarok ve-hayom adom* — every day yellow, today red — *ve-hainu zahav parvayim she-domeh l'dam ha-parim*.

### Rav Ashi 5 Types

*Rav Ashi* disagrees with *Rav Chisda*'s enumeration. He maintains: *chamisha hen* — there are only five types, not seven. His position is that each of the five distinct categories itself contains an internal division — a standard quality and a higher quality — *v'chol chad v'chad it bei zahava v'zahava tava*. The seven of *Rav Chisda* thus reduce to five primary types, each with its own gradation.

## Incense Grinding

### Incense Quantity

On ordinary days, the *kohen* would offer half a *maneh* of *ketoret* in the morning and half a *maneh* in the afternoon. On *Yom Kippur*, an additional quantity of incense was used — the double handful (*melo chofnav*) — for the service inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

### Extra Fine Grinding

The *mishnah* further notes that on ordinary days the *ketoret* was ground fine (*dakah*), but on *Yom Kippur* it was ground extra fine (*dakah min ha-dakah*). The *Tanna'im* address this as follows: the verse for *Yom Kippur* states that the incense should be *ketoret dakah* — fine incense. One might ask: this quality of fineness is already required for ordinary incense, as the verse in the context of the regular *ketoret* says *ve-shachakta mimenah hadek* — grind it very fine. Why then does the Torah need to repeat the specification of *dakah* by *Yom Kippur*? The answer is: *ela l'havi dakah min ha-dakah* — to teach that on *Yom Kippur* an extra grinding is performed, producing incense finer than what is ordinarily required.

## Processional Route

### Standard Route

On ordinary days, the *kohanim* would ascend the ramp (*kevesh*) on the east side and descend on the west, following the general rule that all turnings in the *Mikdash* are made toward the right — *kol pinot she-atah poneh lo yehu ela derech yamin l'mizrach* — which yields a counter-clockwise circuit around the altar.

### Middle Path (Rashi)

On *Yom Kippur*, the procedure changes. According to *Rashi*'s *girsa*, the *Kohen Gadol* alone ascended and descended via the middle of the ramp: *oleh b'emtza ve-yored b'emtza*. The reason given is *mishum kevodo shel Kohen Gadol* — for the honor of the *Kohen Gadol*, who is given his own unobstructed path, as it were, straight through the middle.

### Retinue Version

An alternative *girsa* reads not *oleh* (singular) but *olim* (plural): *olim b'emtza ve-yordim b'emtza*. According to this version, a retinue of *kohanim* accompanied the *Kohen Gadol*, ascending the middle of the ramp in front of him and descending in front of him when he came down. This reading transforms the image significantly — rather than a lone figure ascending quietly, the scene becomes one of an escort procession, lending the moment weight and ceremonial grandeur.

### Honor & Significance

Both readings converge on the theme of *kevod Kohen Gadol*, but the retinue version carries a different sensibility. While *Yom Kippur* indeed has the quality of solitude and intimacy — the *Kohen Gadol* alone entering the *Kodesh Kodashim* — from the perspective of those assembled in the *Azarah*, a full processional would communicate the gravity and majesty of what was unfolding in a way that a single figure moving about could not. The *segan*, *rosh beit av*, and several *kohanim* mentioned elsewhere in the *perek* as present may have formed part of this retinue.

## Private Water

### Pitcher vs. Kiyor

On ordinary days, the *Kohen Gadol* would sanctify his hands and feet from the communal *kiyor* — the laver used by all the *kohanim*. On *Yom Kippur*, he did so from a private pitcher designated for him alone.

### Kavodo Rationale

The *Gemara* gives the reason as *mishum kevodo shel Kohen Gadol* — for his honor. This is somewhat striking, since one might have expected the reasoning to center on the *Kohen Gadol*'s physical condition — he must repeatedly immerse in the *mikveh* and wash his hands and feet, and having to descend to the communal *kiyor* each time would be burdensome. Yet the *Gemara* frames it in terms of honor rather than practicality. It is worth noting that there is an opinion in the *mishnah* that even on ordinary days he had a private pitcher, which would certainly be explicable as *kevodo shel Kohen Gadol*.

## Number of Fires

### Minimum 2+1

The *mishnah* now turns to the number of pyres (*ma'arachot*) maintained on the outer altar. All opinions agree on a minimum of two fires on ordinary days, and all agree that one additional fire was added on *Yom Kippur* for the coals used in the *Kodesh Kodashim* service. The dispute is over the precise count on a regular day.

### R. Yehuda 4 Fires

*Rabbi Yehuda* holds that on ordinary days there were two fires: one *ma'aracha gedolah* — the great pyre for burning the limbs and fats of the sacrifices — and one *ma'aracha shniya shel ketoret* — the secondary pyre whose coals were used to burn the daily *ketoret* on the gold altar. On *Yom Kippur*, a third fire is added, bringing the total to three.

### R. Yosi 3+1

*Rabbi Yosi* holds that on ordinary days there were three fires. In addition to the two agreed upon by all, there is a third fire dedicated to *kiyum ha-esh* — maintaining a constant flame — in fulfillment of the verse *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach*. On *Yom Kippur* a fourth is added.

*Rashi* explains *kiyum ha-esh* as follows: if the main fire was not burning vigorously enough, one would take from this dedicated fire to reinforce it. This is not a fire that burns independently for its own sake, but one kept in reserve to ensure the main pyre never falters.

### R. Meir 4+1

*Rabbi Meir* (identified in some versions as *Rebbi*) holds that on ordinary days there were four fires. Beyond the three of *Rabbi Yosi*, there is a fourth fire dedicated to *evarim u-fedarim shelo nit'achlu miba'erev* — limbs and fats from the previous day's sacrifices that were not fully consumed overnight. The reasoning is that the main fire is for the current day's *korbanot*; items left over from the previous day belong on a separate pyre rather than being returned to the main one. Each morning, any unconsumed limbs from the night before would be transferred to this fourth fire. On *Yom Kippur*, a fifth fire is added.

## Two Base Fires

### Main Pyre

Having surveyed the positions, the *Gemara* now derives from the *pesukim* in *Tzav* the basis for the fires that are universally agreed upon. The opening verses of the *Tzav* passage read: *hi ha-olah al mokdah al hamizbe'ach kol ha-layla ad ha-boker* — this is the burnt offering on its pyre on the altar all night until morning. This first reference to *mokdah* is identified as the *ma'aracha gedolah*, the main fire for burning the limbs and fats.

### Incense Pyre

Immediately following, the verse continues: *ve-eish hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — and the fire of the altar shall burn in it. This second mention of fire, which appears redundant, is identified as the *ma'aracha shniya shel ketoret* — the secondary pyre whose coals were taken daily to service the gold altar. Thus, from the very opening of the *Tzav* passage, the *Tanna'im* derive a scriptural basis for two fires.

### Pesuk Mentions

The paragraph in *Tzav* mentions fire multiple times. After the initial two references, it further states *asher tochal ha-esh et ha-olah al hamizbe'ach* — a third reference — and later *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* and *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach lo tichbeh*. These repeated mentions become the raw material for the debate over whether additional fires beyond two are mandated.

## Third Fire Debate

### R. Yosi Kiyum

*Rabbi Yosi* derives a third fire from the subsequent verse: *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — a further mention of fire that is otherwise redundant. This teaches, in his view, a separate fire dedicated to *kiyum ha-esh*, the perpetual maintenance of fire on the altar.

### R. Yehuda Kindling

*Rabbi Yehuda* disputes this derivation. He holds that this verse — *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — does not mandate a third fire but rather teaches the rule of *atzatzot alista*: that when adding kindling to the altar fire, the actual lighting must be performed at the top of the altar itself. One may not, for instance, ignite the kindling at the base of the ramp and carry it up already lit. As the *baraita* states: *minayin le-atzatzot alista she-lo tehei ela be-rosho shel mizbe'ach* — how do we know that the lighting of kindling must be done only at the head of the altar? *Talmud lomar ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — the fire must be kindled *on* it, meaning at the top.

For *Rabbi Yehuda*, then, the verse that *Rabbi Yosi* uses for a third fire is fully accounted for by this teaching about lighting kindling. There is no scriptural basis for a dedicated *kiyum ha-esh* fire, and the two universally-agreed-upon fires are all that exist on ordinary days.

### R. Shimon Challenge

*Rabbi Yehuda* further derives from the verse in *Vayikra* — *ve-nasnu benei Aharon ha-kohen esh al hamizbe'ach* — that the lighting of kindling must be performed by a *kohen kasher* using *klei sharet*. *Rabbi Shimon* challenges the first part of this: *ve-khi ta'aleh al da'atkha she-zar karev legabei mizbe'ach* — does it really require a verse to tell you that a non-*kohen* may not ascend the altar? For *Rabbi Shimon*, it is self-evident that a *zar* cannot go up onto the altar — no scriptural derivation is needed. This is a striking position: even though *shechita* is valid when performed by a non-*kohen* on the floor of the *Azarah*, the act of ascending the altar is categorically off-limits to anyone who is not a *kohen*, and this, says *Rabbi Shimon*, is obvious without a verse.

### R. Shimon Resolution

*Rabbi Shimon* therefore redirects the verse. Rather than teaching *who* performs the kindling, the verse teaches *where*: *ella limed al hatzatat alita she-lo tehei ella be-rosho shel mizbe'ach* — the lighting must be done at the top of the altar. Once we know that the lighting must occur at the top of the altar, the requirement for *kohanim* follows automatically, since the altar is patently their domain.

*Rabbi Yehuda*, by contrast, does not accept that it is obvious a *zar* cannot ascend the altar. He therefore requires the verse of *benei Aharon* to establish that. But does that leave him without a source for the requirement to kindle at the top? No — *Rabbi Yehuda* derived that from the *Tzav* verse, *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo*. In turn, *Rabbi Yosi* used that *Tzav* verse for the third fire of *kiyum ha-esh*, and derived the kindling-at-the-top rule from the *Vayikra* verse — just as *Rabbi Shimon* does.

One might ask: if the verse says one must light the kindling at the top of the altar, what would be the concern about a non-*kohen* lighting it from below? *Hava amina*: one might have supposed that a non-*kohen* could stand on the ground and use a bellows to fan flames upward, thereby feeding the fire from below without actually ascending. The verse therefore comes to teach that any tending or adding to the fire must be done by someone physically present at the top — and consequently by a *kohen*.

## Fourth Fire

### Overnight Remains

The *Gemara* now turns to *Rabbi Meir*'s fourth fire, which is designated for *evarim u-fedarim shelo nit'achlu miba'erev* — limbs and fats that were not fully consumed overnight. The question is from where *Rabbi Meir* derives that such a separate fire exists.

### Return to Main

The *Rabbanan* do not accept the existence of a fourth fire. In their view, limbs that were not consumed overnight are simply returned to the main pyre. A *baraita* describes the practical procedure: in the morning, the *kohen* would arrange any unconsumed limbs to the side, clear and reset the main fire, and then restore the limbs to it. If there was insufficient space atop the altar for the limbs during this process, they could be placed temporarily on the ramp (*kevesh*) or on the *sovev* — the surrounding ledge — until the main fire was ready. The verse *asher tochal ha-esh et ha-olah al hamizbe'ach* — the fire that consumes the burnt offering on the altar — is read by the *Rabbanan* as teaching that this same fire continues to consume whatever remains of the *olah*; no separate fire is needed.

### Separate Fourth

*Rabbi Meir* derives the fourth fire from the extra *vav* in *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo lo tichbeh* — a stylistic addition the *Rabbanan* do not treat as exegetically significant (*ve-Rabbanan vav lo darshi*).

### Pesuk on Olah

As for the verse *asher tochal ha-esh et ha-olah al hamizbe'ach*, which the *Rabbanan* use to teach that the same fire continues consuming the *olah*, *Rabbi Meir* interprets it differently. He reads the emphasis as falling on *ha-olah* — animal sacrifices — rather than on the continuity of the same fire. The teaching is therefore: *ichulei olah atah machzir ve-ee atah machzir ichulei ketoret* — you return the remnants of animal offerings to the fire, but you do not return unconsumed *ketoret* to the gold altar to finish burning. The practical import is that pieces of *ketoret* that fell off or were not fully consumed are not put back; only animal sacrifice portions are returned.

## Fifth Fire YK

### Universal Extra

Notwithstanding all the debate over the precise count on ordinary days, the *Gemara* notes that all opinions agree an additional fire is added on *Yom Kippur*: *mosifin bo bayom* — one extra fire is used on that day for the coals of the *Kodesh Kodashim* service. The question is the scriptural basis for this universal agreement.

### Vav-Hey Extra

The derivation is from the letters of *ve-ha-esh* — specifically, the *vav* and the *heh* that introduce the phrase *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo*. Where the verse could have read simply *esh hamizbe'ach tukad bo*, the additional *vav-heh* signals that under certain circumstances yet another fire is added beyond the baseline. The *Gemara* notes: *v'afilu le-man d'la darish vav, vav-heh darish* — even an opinion that does not ordinarily derive a *halacha* from a single extra *vav* would recognize that an extra *vav* combined with a *heh* is meaningful and comes to teach an additional case. The occasion indicated by this extra fire is *Yom Kippur*, the one day per year on which the coals of the *Kodesh Kodashim* service require their own dedicated pyre.

## Fire Source YK

### Which Altar?

Having established that there is an extra fire on *Yom Kippur*, the *Gemara* turns to a different question: from which altar is the fire taken — for the *Yom Kippur* incense service in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and for the *menorah*? The verse in *Acharei Mot* states *ve-lakach melo ha-machtah gachalei esh me'al hamizbe'ach* — he shall take a full fire pan of coals from the altar — but it does not specify *which* altar. One might have thought the coals are taken from the gold altar, which is closer to where they will be used. Similarly, the fire used to kindle the *menorah* might in principle come from the gold altar. The *Gemara* sets out to determine the correct source.

### Gezeira Shava

The *Gemara* first proposes a *gezeira shava* on the word *esh*: it is stated in the context of the daily *ketoret*, and it is stated in the context of the *machtah* of *Yom Kippur*, and it is stated in the context of the *menorah*. Just as we established that the fire for the daily *ketoret* on the gold altar comes from the outer altar, so too by analogy the fire for the *Yom Kippur* fire pan and the *menorah* comes from the outer altar.

### Closest Source

The *Gemara* immediately entertains a counter-argument: the same linkage by *esh* across these three cases could equally support an opposite conclusion — that they all follow the principle of proximity, *besamuch lo*, each taking fire from the closest available source. Under this reading, the fire for the daily *ketoret* comes from the outer altar not because it must always come from there, but because that is the nearest fire when the *kohen* stands there. For the *menorah* and the *Yom Kippur machtah*, the nearest fire might well be the gold altar.

### Esh Tamid

The *Gemara* resolves the question for the *menorah* from the phrase *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach lo tichbeh* — the constant fire on the altar shall not be extinguished. The word *tamid* in the *Mikdash* is associated with the *menorah*, whose flame is called *ner tamid*. The teaching is: the fire for the *tamid* must come from the outer altar — *lo tehei ela be-rosho shel mizbe'ach*. This establishes that the *menorah* is kindled from the outer altar.

### YK Link

With the *menorah*'s source established, the *Gemara* now links the *Yom Kippur* fire pan to the *menorah* through another *gezeira shava* on *esh*: just as the *menorah* fire comes from the outer altar, so too the *machtah* of *Yom Kippur*. Alternatively, the derivation may proceed through *esh tamid* itself, carrying both the *menorah* and the *Yom Kippur* fire pan together.

### Coals Pesuk

The *Gemara* finally confirms the derivation for *Yom Kippur* directly from the verse: *ve-lakach melo ha-machtah gachalei esh me'al hamizbe'ach — milefnei Hashem* — from before God. The question is posed: *eizehu mizbe'ach she-miktzato lifnei Hashem ve-ein kulo lifnei Hashem* — which altar has a portion that can be described as *lifnei Hashem* and a portion that cannot? The answer is: *hevei omer zeh mizbe'ach ha-chitzon* — it must be the outer altar. The inner gold altar, situated entirely within the *heichal*, stands wholly in the presence of God; one cannot speak of one side being closer to God than another. The outer altar, however, has its western side nearer to the *Ulam* and *Heichal*, and thus more *lifnei Hashem*, while its eastern side is further away. The phrase *me'al hamizbe'ach milefnei Hashem* therefore specifies the outer altar — take the coals from that part of the outer altar which faces toward the Divine Presence. This settles definitively that the fire for the *Yom Kippur ketoret* is taken from the outer altar.
