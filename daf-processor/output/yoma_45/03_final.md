# Yoma 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shoveling Coals

### Silver to Gold Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיָה חוֹתֶה בְּשֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **On every** other **day,** a priest **would scoop up** the coals with a coal pan made **of silver and pour** the coals from there **into** a coal pan **of gold.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** the gold pan was not used to scoop the coals? The Gemara answers: Because **the Torah spared the money of the Jewish people.** Since the pan is worn away with use, it is preferable to use a less expensive silver pan.

The daf opens in the middle of 44b with the *mishnah*'s discussion of the shoveling of hot coals from the top of the altar — a necessary preliminary to the *ketoret* service of *Yom Kippur*. The *mishnah* states: *bechol yom haya choteh beshel kesef u-me'arah letoch shel zahav* — on every ordinary day the *kohen* would shovel the coals using a silver vessel and then pour them into a gold one. The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for this two-vessel system? The answer is *HaTorah chasa al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah is concerned with the waste of Jewish money. Gold is a soft metal, and using it to shovel directly into the coals would cause rapid deterioration. Therefore, the silver vessel absorbs that wear, while the gold vessel — brought in for the actual service — is preserved.

### Yom Kippur Gold Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיּוֹם חוֹתֶה בְּשֶׁל זָהָב וּבָהּ הָיָה מַכְנִיס. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם חוּלְשָׁא דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **But** on **this day,** on Yom Kippur, the High Priest **scoops up with** a coal pan **of gold, and with** that coal pan, **he would bring** the coals **into** the Holy of Holies. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that on Yom Kippur only one pan is used? **Due to the weakness** of the **High Priest.** He has to perform the entire service by himself while fasting; using only one pan minimizes his exertion.

On *Yom Kippur*, however, the procedure changes: *vehayom choteh beshel zahav uva haya machnis* — today he shovels directly with the gold vessel and carries it in with that same vessel. The *Gemara* explains: *mishum chulshat Kohen Gadol* — because of the physical weakness of the *Kohen Gadol*, who has been fasting, one does not wish to burden him with unnecessary manipulation of vessels.

### Pesuk-Based Approach

The *Rashash* notes that there is arguably a more straightforward reason rooted in the *pesukim* themselves: the plain sense of the verse implies that the same vessel used to shovel the coals is the one brought into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. This raises a further question about the scope of the *mishnah*'s discussion. *Rashi* understands the *mishnah* as referring specifically to what is distinctive about the *ketoret* of the *Kodesh Kodashim*. But one might read the *mishnah* more broadly — that certain special practices observed on *Yom Kippur* applied even to the regular morning and afternoon *ketoret*, not only to the inner service. The question of whether the single-vessel procedure on *Yom Kippur* reflects a *pesuk*-based requirement or merely an accommodation to the *Kohen Gadol*'s condition bears on which reading of the *mishnah* is correct.

### Quantity Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם בְּשֶׁל אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּין וְכוּ׳. תָּנָא: נִתְפַּזְּרוּ לוֹ קַב גֶּחָלִים, מְכַבְּדָן לָאַמָּה. תָּנֵי חֲדָא: קַב, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: קַבַּיִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא הָךְ דְּתָנֵי קַב — רַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא הָךְ דְּתָנֵי קַבַּיִים, מַנִּי? לָא רַבָּנַן וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **On every** other **day,** a priest scoops up the coals with a coal pan **of four *kav*** and pours the coals into a coal pan of three *kav*. Rabbi Yosei says: On every other day, a priest scoops with a coal pan of a *se'a*, which is six *kav*, and then pours the coals into a coal pan of three *kav*. **It was taught** in a mishna (*Tamid* 33a): As he poured from a pan of four *kav* to a pan of three *kav*, **a *kav* of coals became scattered, and he swept them into the canal** that passed through the Temple and ran to the Kidron brook. It **was taught** in **one** *baraita*: **A *kav*** of coals was scattered. **And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **Two *kav*** were scattered. The Gemara comments: **Granted, this** *baraita*, which **teaches that** a single ***kav*** was scattered, is understandable. It is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** the first *tanna* of the mishna, who maintain that coals are poured from a coal pan of four *kav* to one of three. **But that** *baraita*, which **teaches** that **two *kav*** of coals were scattered, in accordance **with whose** opinion is it? **It is not** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis and not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** According to Rabbi Yosei, three *kav* of coals would have been scattered.

The *mishnah* also records that on a normal day the *kohen* would shovel with a vessel holding four *kabin* and pour into one holding three, whereas on *Yom Kippur* he shoveled directly with the three-*kav* vessel. A *baraita* is then cited teaching that one *kav* of coals would scatter when the transfer was made — consistent with going from four to three — while another *baraita* states that two *kabin* would scatter. The *Gemara* observes: *bishlama hach detani kav* — the position of one extra *kav* scattering corresponds to the *Rabbanan*, who say four is poured into three. But who holds that two would scatter? *Lo Rabbanan ve-lo Rabbi Yosi* — not the *Rabbanan* (four to three) and not *Rabbi Yosi* (six to three, which would yield three extra).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשֶׁל קַבַּיִים הָיָה מַכְנִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: With** a coal pan **of two *kav* he would bring** the coals **into** the Holy of Holies. If one accepts the opinion of the Rabbis that the coals were scooped with a coal pan of four *kav*, two *kav* were scattered.

The answer is given in the name of *Rav Shizbi* citing *Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*: he shoveled with four, as the *Rabbanan*, but poured into a vessel holding only two *kabin*, yielding two extra. So we now have three positions: the vessel brought in held two, three, or (according to *Rabbi Yosi*) that you shoveled with six. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיָה חוֹתֶה בְּשֶׁל סְאָה מִדְבָּרִית, וּמְעָרֶה לְתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשֶׁת קַבִּים יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: You can even say** that this *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and this is what he is saying: On every** other **day,** a priest **scooped with** a coal pan **of a desert *se'a*, which** is five Jerusalem *kav*, **and** then **he poured** the coals **into a coal pan of three Jerusalem *kav*.** Therefore, two *kav* would be scattered.

*Rav Ashi* then resolves the difficulty with *Rabbi Yosi*'s position: *afilu tema Rabbi Yosi* — one can even maintain *Rabbi Yosi*'s view, since the six-*kav* measure was a *se'ah midbarit* — a wilderness measure — whereas the vessel that received the coals held three *kabin Yerushalmi'ot*, the larger Jerusalem measure. Since a *se'ah midbarit* equals five *kabin Yerushalmi'ot*, only two *kabin* would be extra, which accounts for the *baraita* that teaches two scatter.

## Fire Pan Modifications

### Thin Gold & Long Handle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיְתָה כְּבֵידָה, וְהַיּוֹם קַלָּה. תָּנָא, בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיְתָה גִּלְדָּהּ עָבֶה, וְהַיּוֹם רַךְ. בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיְתָה קְצָרָה, וְהַיּוֹם אֲרוּכָּה. מַאי טַעְמָא — כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא זְרוֹעוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מְסַיַּיעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **On every** other **day,** the coal pan **was heavy, but** on **this day** it was **light. It was taught** in a *baraita*: **On every** other **day its side was thick but** on **this day** it was **soft** and thin. **On every** other **day** its handle **was short but** on **this day it was long. What is the reason? So that the arm of the High Priest could assist him** in carrying the coal pan, i.e., he could support the coal pan by resting it against his arm rather than bear the entire weight in his hand.

The *mishnah* continues to enumerate the differences between the ordinary daily procedure and that of *Yom Kippur*. A *baraita* elaborates: *bechol yom hayetah guldah avah ve-hayom rakah* — on a normal day the wall of the fire pan was thick, while on *Yom Kippur* it was thin. *U-ve-chol yom hayetah ketzarah ve-hayom arucha* — the handle was short on ordinary days, but long on *Yom Kippur*. The reason: *kedei she-tehei zero'o shel Kohen Gadol mesaya'ato* — so that the *Kohen Gadol*'s arm could assist him in manipulating the vessel. This is in anticipation of the subsequent service in which the *Kohen Gadol*, holding the fire pan in one hand and the incense in the other, must pour the incense using his arms and elbows in a confined space.

### Niashti Ring/Sheath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא, בְּכׇל יוֹם לֹא הָיָה לָהּ נִיאַשְׁתִּיק, וְהַיּוֹם הָיָה לָהּ נִיאַשְׁתִּיק. דִּבְרֵי בֶּן הַסְּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught** in a *baraita*: **On every** other **day it did not have a ring, but** on **this day it has a ring** on the end of the handle, which clatters against it and makes a noise in fulfillment of the verse "And the sound thereof shall be heard when he goes in to the Sanctuary" (Exodus 28:35); this is **the statement of the son of the Deputy.**

Another *baraita* records the teaching of *Ben Ha-Segan* — the son of the deputy *Kohen Gadol* — that *bechol yom lo hayah lah niashti ve-hayom hayah lah niashti*: on ordinary days the fire pan did not have a *niashti*, while on *Yom Kippur* it did. The word *niashti* is unusual and lacks a clear parallel in other contexts, so its meaning must be inferred from the commentators.

### Rashi Sound Theory

*Rashi* explains that the *niashti* was a ring (*taba'at*) attached to the vessel that would clatter against the metal as the *Kohen Gadol* walked — *she-mekashkesh u-mashmi'a kol* — producing a sound upon his entry. The conceptual basis is the principle of *ve-nishma kolo be-vo'o*: when the *Kohen Gadol* enters the *heichal*, there should be some audible signal of his approach. On ordinary days this is accomplished by the bells of the *me'il*. On *Yom Kippur*, however, he wears only the *bigdei lavan* — the white garments — which have no bells. *Rashi*'s position is therefore that the ring on the fire pan served as a substitute, at least when the *Kohen Gadol* was carrying it, for the sound that the *me'il* would ordinarily produce.

### Tosafot Leather Sheath

*Tosafot* quotes *Rashi* and then notes that the *Yerushalmi* reads *nartik* rather than *niashti* — and *nartik* denotes a sheath. The *Riva* interprets this as a leather covering wrapped around the handle: *k'ein nartik shel or haya asui saviv yada* — like a pot holder placed around the handle to prevent burning. The question this raises is: why was such protection not needed on ordinary days? The answer offered is that the concern was not the initial carrying of the fire pan into the *Kodesh Kodashim*, which is a matter of minutes, but rather the later retrieval. After the *Kohen Gadol* placed the *ketoret* on the coals, he would go and perform the other *avodos*, and only afterward return to retrieve the fire pan — which by that point had been sitting with burning coals inside for an extended time, rendering the handle dangerously hot. Furthermore, on *Yom Kippur* the gold was thinner and the fire pan had a longer handle, both of which may have affected heat distribution. *Tosafot*'s explanation — that the issue is prolonged exposure during the interval before retrieval — accounts for why this protection was needed specifically on *Yom Kippur* and not on ordinary days.

## Gold Types

### Seven Gold Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיָה זְהָבָהּ יָרוֹק. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שִׁבְעָה זְהָבִים הֵן: זָהָב, וְזָהָב טוֹב, וּזְהַב אוֹפִיר, וְזָהָב מוּפָז, וְזָהָב שָׁחוּט, וְזָהָב סָגוּר, וּזְהַב פַּרְוַיִם. זָהָב וְזָהָב טוֹב, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּזְהַב הָאָרֶץ הַהִוא טוֹב״. זְהַב אוֹפִיר — דְּאָתֵי מֵאוֹפִיר. זָהָב מוּפָז —
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **On every** other **day, it was** of **greenish gold** but on this day it was of a red gold. **Rav Ḥisda said: There are seven** types **of gold** mentioned in the Bible: **Gold, and good gold, and gold of Ophir** (I Kings 10:11), **and glistering gold** (I Kings 10:18), **and *shaḥut* gold** (I Kings 10:17), **and closed gold** (I Kings 10:21), **and *parvayim* gold** (II Chronicles 3:6). The Gemara explains the reason for these names: There is a distinction between **gold and good gold, as it is written** in the verse: **"And the gold of that land is good"** (Genesis 2:12), which indicates the existence of gold of a higher quality. **Gold of Ophir** is gold **that comes from Ophir. Glistering [*mufaz*] gold** is so named

The *mishnah* states that on ordinary days the fire pan was made of *zahav yarok* — yellow-green gold — while on *Yom Kippur* a red gold was used. *Rav Chisda* teaches: *shiva zehavim hem* — there are seven types of gold. He enumerates them as follows. First, plain *zahav* and *zahav tov* — standard gold and good gold — derived from the verse in *Bereishit*: *ve-zahav ha'aretz hahi tov*, which implies that there is ordinary gold and then a superior grade. Third, *zahav Ofir* — gold from the land of *Ofir* — which is presumably distinguished by its origin and attendant quality. Fourth, *zahav mufaz* — gold that resembles *paz*. *Rashi* explains that *paz* shines like a precious stone, *matzhiv k'margalit*. Fifth, *zahav shachut* — literally "drawn" gold — which *Rashi* describes as gold so soft and pliable it can be drawn out like a thread. Sixth, *zahav sagur* — "closed" gold — described as being of such exceptional quality that *besha'ah sheniftach kol hachanuyot nisgaru*: when this gold comes to market, all the other gold shops close, unable to compete. Seventh, *zahav parvayim*.

### Red Gold Symbol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְפָז. זָהָב שָׁחוּט — שֶׁנִּטְוֶה כְּחוּט. זָהָב סָגוּר — בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּפְתָּח כָּל הַחֲנוּיוֹת נִסְגָּרוֹת. זְהַב פַּרְוַיִם — שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְדַם הַפָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **because it resembles the luster of pearls [*paz*]** in the way it glistens. ***Shaḥut* gold** is named as such **because** it is very malleable and **is spun like thread [*shenitve keḥut*].** *Shaḥut* is a contraction of the words *shenitve keḥut*. **Closed gold** is so called **because when** a shop **opens** to sell it, **all the** other **shops close,** as no one is interested in purchasing any other type of gold. ***Parvayim* gold** is so called **because** its redness **resembles the blood of bulls [*parim*].**

*Zahav parvayim* is explained: *she-domeh l'dam ha-parim* — it resembles the blood of oxen. This is the red gold used for the fire pan on *Yom Kippur*.

### Gold & Kaparah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיָה זְהָבָהּ יָרוֹק, וְהַיּוֹם אָדוֹם — וְהַיְינוּ זְהַב פַּרְוַיִם שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְדַם הַפָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § *Baraita*: **On every** other **day** the coal pan was made **of greenish gold, but** on **this day** it was made of **a red** gold, **and this is the *parvayim* gold which resembles the blood of bulls.**

The choice of red gold is not merely a matter of expense or rarity. The *Gemara* elsewhere linked the *ketoret* service on *Yom Kippur* with the blood service: the *Gemara* taught that both the incense and the blood require the clearing of the *heichal* — *bevo'o l'chaper bakodesh* — and that the *ketoret* too is *mechaper*. Given that the *ketoret* and the blood of the *par* and *sa'ir* are thus intertwined in the framework of *kaparah*, it is fitting that the vessel carrying the incense coals is fashioned from a gold whose very color evokes the blood — *she-domeh l'dam ha-parim*. The *baraita* subsequently cited confirms this identification explicitly: *bechol yom hayah zehavo yarok ve-hayom adom* — every day yellow, today red — *ve-hainu zahav parvayim she-domeh l'dam ha-parim*.

### Rav Ashi 5 Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: חֲמִשָּׁה הֵן, וְכׇל חַד וְחַד אִית בֵּיהּ זָהָב וְזָהָב טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: There are** in fact only **five** types of gold, the last five in Rav Ḥisda's list. Gold and good gold are not independent categories; rather, **each and every one** of the types of gold has two varieties: Regular **gold and** a superior variety called **good gold.**

*Rav Ashi* disagrees with *Rav Chisda*'s enumeration. He maintains: *chamisha hen* — there are only five types, not seven. His position is that each of the five distinct categories itself contains an internal division — a standard quality and a higher quality — *v'chol chad v'chad it bei zahava v'zahava tava*. The seven of *Rav Chisda* thus reduce to five primary types, each with its own gradation.

## Incense Grinding

### Incense Quantity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם מַקְרִיב פְּרָס שַׁחֲרִית וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **On every** other **day,** a priest **sacrificed a *peras*,** half of a *maneh*, of incense **in the morning,** and a *peras* in the afternoon, but on this day the High Priest adds an additional handful of incense and burns it in the Holy of Holies.

On ordinary days, the *kohen* would offer half a *maneh* of *ketoret* in the morning and half a *maneh* in the afternoon. On *Yom Kippur*, an additional quantity of incense was used — the double handful (*melo chofnav*) — for the service inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

### Extra Fine Grinding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיְתָה דַּקָּה, וְהַיּוֹם דַּקָּה מִן הַדַּקָּה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״דַּקָּה״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁחַקְתָּ מִמֶּנָּה הָדֵק״! אֶלָּא, לְהָבִיא דַּקָּה מִן הַדַּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **On every** other **day,** the incense **was** ground **fine** as prescribed by the Torah, **but** on **this day it was superfine. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the incense on Yom Kippur that it is **"finely ground** aromatic incense" (Leviticus 16:12). **What** does the **verse** mean to **teach** by this? **Has it not already been stated: "And you shall grind some of it finely"** (Exodus 30:36)? **Rather, it teaches** that on Yom Kippur the incense has to be **superfine.**

The *mishnah* further notes that on ordinary days the *ketoret* was ground fine (*dakah*), but on *Yom Kippur* it was ground extra fine (*dakah min ha-dakah*). The *Tanna'im* address this as follows: the verse for *Yom Kippur* states that the incense should be *ketoret dakah* — fine incense. One might ask: this quality of fineness is already required for ordinary incense, as the verse in the context of the regular *ketoret* says *ve-shachakta mimenah hadek* — grind it very fine. Why then does the Torah need to repeat the specification of *dakah* by *Yom Kippur*? The answer is: *ela l'havi dakah min ha-dakah* — to teach that on *Yom Kippur* an extra grinding is performed, producing incense finer than what is ordinarily required.

## Processional Route

### Standard Route

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם כֹּהֲנִים עוֹלִין בְּמִזְרָחוֹ שֶׁל כֶּבֶשׁ. דְּאָמַר מָר: כׇּל פִּינּוֹת שֶׁאַתָּה פּוֹנֶה לֹא יְהוּ אֶלָּא דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין לַמִּזְרָח.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **On every** other **day, priests ascend on the eastern side of the ramp.** A *baraita* explains the reason for this: **As the Master said: All the turns that you turn should be only to the right,** which, after ascending the altar, means one will turn **to the east** and will mean one will circulate the altar in a counter-clockwise fashion. When they descended, they again turned to the right, which is to the west of the ramp.

On ordinary days, the *kohanim* would ascend the ramp (*kevesh*) on the east side and descend on the west, following the general rule that all turnings in the *Mikdash* are made toward the right — *kol pinot she-atah poneh lo yehu ela derech yamin l'mizrach* — which yields a counter-clockwise circuit around the altar.

### Middle Path (Rashi)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיּוֹם (עוֹלִין) בָּאֶמְצַע (וְיוֹרְדִין) בָּאֶמְצַע. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **But** on **this day** the priests **ascend in the middle** of the ramp **and descend in the middle. What is the reason? Due to the eminence** of the **High Priest** he should not walk on the side but in the middle.

On *Yom Kippur*, the procedure changes. According to *Rashi*'s *girsa*, the *Kohen Gadol* alone ascended and descended via the middle of the ramp: *oleh b'emtza ve-yored b'emtza*. The reason given is *mishum kevodo shel Kohen Gadol* — for the honor of the *Kohen Gadol*, who is given his own unobstructed path, as it were, straight through the middle.

### Retinue Version

An alternative *girsa* reads not *oleh* (singular) but *olim* (plural): *olim b'emtza ve-yordim b'emtza*. According to this version, a retinue of *kohanim* accompanied the *Kohen Gadol*, ascending the middle of the ramp in front of him and descending in front of him when he came down. This reading transforms the image significantly — rather than a lone figure ascending quietly, the scene becomes one of an escort procession, lending the moment weight and ceremonial grandeur.

### Honor & Significance

Both readings converge on the theme of *kevod Kohen Gadol*, but the retinue version carries a different sensibility. While *Yom Kippur* indeed has the quality of solitude and intimacy — the *Kohen Gadol* alone entering the *Kodesh Kodashim* — from the perspective of those assembled in the *Azarah*, a full processional would communicate the gravity and majesty of what was unfolding in a way that a single figure moving about could not. The *segan*, *rosh beit av*, and several *kohanim* mentioned elsewhere in the *perek* as present may have formed part of this retinue.

## Private Water

### Pitcher vs. Kiyor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם כֹּהֵן [גָּדוֹל] מְקַדֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו מִן הַכִּיּוֹר וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § We learned in the mishna that **every** other **day the High Priest sanctifies his hands and his feet from the laver** like the other priests, and on this day he sanctifies them from the golden flask. **What is the reason? Due to the eminence** of the **High Priest.**

On ordinary days, the *Kohen Gadol* would sanctify his hands and feet from the communal *kiyor* — the laver used by all the *kohanim*. On *Yom Kippur*, he did so from a private pitcher designated for him alone.

### Kavodo Rationale

The *Gemara* gives the reason as *mishum kevodo shel Kohen Gadol* — for his honor. This is somewhat striking, since one might have expected the reasoning to center on the *Kohen Gadol*'s physical condition — he must repeatedly immerse in the *mikveh* and wash his hands and feet, and having to descend to the communal *kiyor* each time would be burdensome. Yet the *Gemara* frames it in terms of honor rather than practicality. It is worth noting that there is an opinion in the *mishnah* that even on ordinary days he had a private pitcher, which would certainly be explicable as *kevodo shel Kohen Gadol*.

## Number of Fires

### Minimum 2+1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיוּ שָׁם אַרְבַּע מַעֲרָכוֹת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיוּ שְׁתַּיִם מַעֲרָכוֹת, וְהַיּוֹם שָׁלֹשׁ. אַחַת מַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְאַחַת מַעֲרָכָה שְׁנִיָּה שֶׁל קְטוֹרֶת, וְאַחַת שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **On every** other **day, there were four arrangements** of wood **there,** upon the altar, but on this day there were five. **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **On every** other **day there were two arrangements** of wood on the altar, **but** on **this day** there were **three: One,** the **large arrangement; and one,** the **second arrangement** for coals **for the incense; and one,** the additional arrangement of wood, **which they add on that day** for the incense that is burned in the Holy of Holies. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *mishnah* now turns to the number of pyres (*ma'arachot*) maintained on the outer altar. All opinions agree on a minimum of two fires on ordinary days, and all agree that one additional fire was added on *Yom Kippur* for the coals used in the *Kodesh Kodashim* service. The dispute is over the precise count on a regular day.

### R. Yehuda 4 Fires

*Rabbi Yehuda* holds that on ordinary days there were two fires: one *ma'aracha gedolah* — the great pyre for burning the limbs and fats of the sacrifices — and one *ma'aracha shniya shel ketoret* — the secondary pyre whose coals were used to burn the daily *ketoret* on the gold altar. On *Yom Kippur*, a third fire is added, bringing the total to three.

### R. Yosi 3+1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּכׇל יוֹם שָׁלֹשׁ, וְהַיּוֹם אַרְבַּע. אַחַת שֶׁל מַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְאַחַת מַעֲרָכָה שְׁנִיָּה שֶׁל קְטוֹרֶת, וְאַחַת שֶׁל קִיּוּם הָאֵשׁ, וְאַחַת שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: On every** other **day** there were **three** arrangements, **but** on **this day** there were **four: One, the large arrangement; and one,** the **second arrangement for the incense; and one, for the upkeep of the fire,** so that if the fire of the large arrangement begins to die down, wood from this arrangement may be added to it to raise the flames; **and one,** the additional arrangement of wood **that they add on that day** for the incense that is burned in the Holy of Holies.

*Rabbi Yosei* holds that on ordinary days there were three fires. In addition to the two agreed upon by all, there is a third fire dedicated to *kiyum ha-esh* — maintaining a constant flame — in fulfillment of the verse *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach*. On *Yom Kippur* a fourth is added.

*Rashi* explains *kiyum ha-esh* as follows: if the main fire was not burning vigorously enough, one would take from this dedicated fire to reinforce it. This is not a fire that burns independently for its own sake, but one kept in reserve to ensure the main pyre never falters.

### R. Meir 4+1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי (מֵאִיר) אוֹמֵר: בְּכׇל יוֹם אַרְבַּע, וְהַיּוֹם חָמֵשׁ. אַחַת שֶׁל מַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְאַחַת שֶׁל מַעֲרָכָה שְׁנִיָּה שֶׁל קְטוֹרֶת, וְאַחַת שֶׁל קִיּוּם הָאֵשׁ, וְאַחַת לְאֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַכְּלוּ מִבָּעֶרֶב, וְאַחַת שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: On every** other **day there were four** arrangements of wood on the altar **but** on **this day** there were **five: One,** the **large arrangement; and one,** the **second arrangement for the incense; and one, for the upkeep of the fire;** and **one, for** burning **the limbs and fats that were not** fully **consumed** on the altar **the** previous **evening; and one,** the additional arrangement of wood **that they add on that day** for the incense that is burned in the Holy of Holies.

*Rabbi Meir* (identified in some versions as *Rebbi*) holds that on ordinary days there were four fires. Beyond the three of *Rabbi Yosi*, there is a fourth fire dedicated to *evarim u-fedarim shelo nit'achlu miba'erev* — limbs and fats from the previous day's sacrifices that were not fully consumed overnight. The reasoning is that the main fire is for the current day's *korbanot*; items left over from the previous day belong on a separate pyre rather than being returned to the main one. Each morning, any unconsumed limbs from the night before would be transferred to this fourth fire. On *Yom Kippur*, a fifth fire is added.

## Two Base Fires

### Main Pyre

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת תַּרְתֵּי אִית לְהוּ, מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״הִיא הָעוֹלָה עַל מוֹקְדָה עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה״ — זוֹ מַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה. ״וְאֵשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ תּוּקַד בּוֹ״ — זוֹ מַעֲרָכָה שְׁנִיָּה שֶׁל קְטוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the different opinions: **At any rate, everyone has** at least **two** arrangements in their calculations. **From where do we** derive this? **The verse states: "It is the burnt-offering on the flame on the altar all night"** (Leviticus 6:2); **this** is referring to **the large arrangement.** It states further: **"And the fire of the altar shall be kept burning thereby"** (Leviticus 6:2), **this** additional mention of a fire is referring to **the second arrangement,** which is **for the incense.

Having surveyed the positions, the *Gemara* now derives from the *pesukim* in *Tzav* the basis for the fires that are universally agreed upon. The opening verses of the *Tzav* passage read: *hi ha-olah al mokdah al hamizbe'ach kol ha-layla ad ha-boker* — this is the burnt offering on its pyre on the altar all night until morning. This first reference to *mokdah* is identified as the *ma'aracha gedolah*, the main fire for burning the limbs and fats.

### Incense Pyre

Immediately following, the verse continues: *ve-eish hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — and the fire of the altar shall burn in it. This second mention of fire, which appears redundant, is identified as the *ma'aracha shniya shel ketoret* — the secondary pyre whose coals were taken daily to service the gold altar. Thus, from the very opening of the *Tzav* passage, the *Tanna'im* derive a scriptural basis for two fires.

### Pesuk Mentions

The paragraph in *Tzav* mentions fire multiple times. After the initial two references, it further states *asher tochal ha-esh et ha-olah al hamizbe'ach* — a third reference — and later *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* and *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach lo tichbeh*. These repeated mentions become the raw material for the debate over whether additional fires beyond two are mandated.

## Third Fire Debate

### R. Yosi Kiyum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, קִיּוּם הָאֵשׁ מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ: מִ״וְּהָאֵשׁ עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ תּוּקַד בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where does Rabbi Yosei** learn about the additional arrangement **for the upkeep of the fire? He derives it** from the verse: **"And the fire upon the altar shall be kept burning thereby"** (Leviticus 6:5), which mentions fire for the third time.

*Rabbi Yosei* derives a third fire from the subsequent verse: *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — a further mention of fire that is otherwise redundant. This teaches, in his view, a separate fire dedicated to *kiyum ha-esh*, the perpetual maintenance of fire on the altar.

### R. Yehuda Kindling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הָהוּא, לְהַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא הוּא דַּאֲתָא. דְּתַנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לְהַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָאֵשׁ עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ תּוּקַד בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that normally there are only two arrangements, explain this third mention of a fire? **That** additional mention **comes to** teach about **the kindling** of the **thin wood chips,** which were used to ignite the fires on the altar, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda would say: From where** is it derived **that the kindling of the wood chips should** be done **only at the top of the altar,** rather than setting them alight at the bottom of the altar and carrying them up? **The verse states: "And the fire upon the altar shall be kept burning thereby"** (Leviticus 6:5), which indicates that the fire that is brought there has to be lit on the altar itself.

*Rabbi Yehuda* disputes this derivation. He holds that this verse — *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — does not mandate a third fire but rather teaches the rule of *atzatzot alista*: that when adding kindling to the altar fire, the actual lighting must be performed at the top of the altar itself. One may not, for instance, ignite the kindling at the base of the ramp and carry it up already lit. As the *baraita* states: *minayin le-atzatzot alista she-lo tehei ela be-rosho shel mizbe'ach* — how do we know that the lighting of kindling must be done only at the head of the altar? *Talmud lomar ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo* — the fire must be kindled *on* it, meaning at the top.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁזָּר קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ? אֶלָּא, לִימֵּד עַל הַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said to him:** There is no need for a verse to teach that a priest must kindle the chips, **for could it enter your mind that a non-priest could approach the altar? Rather,** this verse **teaches** about the **kindling of the wood chips, that they may be lit only at the top of the altar.**

For *Rabbi Yehuda*, then, the verse that *Rabbi Yosi* uses for a third fire is fully accounted for by this teaching about lighting kindling. There is no scriptural basis for a dedicated *kiyum ha-esh* fire, and the two universally-agreed-upon fires are all that exist on ordinary days.

*Rabbi Shimon* challenges the first part of this: *ve-khi ta'aleh al da'atkha she-zar karev legabei mizbe'ach* — does it really require a verse to tell you that a non-*kohen* may not ascend the altar? For *Rabbi Shimon*, it is self-evident that a *zar* cannot go up onto the altar — no scriptural derivation is needed. This is a striking position: even though *shechita* is valid when performed by a non-*kohen* on the floor of the *Azarah*, the act of ascending the altar is categorically off-limits to anyone who is not a *kohen*, and this, says *Rabbi Shimon*, is obvious without a verse.

### R. Shimon Resolution

*Rabbi Shimon* therefore redirects the verse. Rather than teaching *who* performs the kindling, the verse teaches *where*: *ella limed al hatzatat alita she-lo tehei ella be-rosho shel mizbe'ach* — the lighting must be done at the top of the altar. Once we know that the lighting must occur at the top of the altar, the requirement for *kohanim* follows automatically, since the altar is patently their domain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתְנוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן אֵשׁ עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ — לִימֵּד עַל הַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּכֹהֵן כָּשֵׁר וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁזָּר קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ? אֶלָּא, לִימֵּד עַל הַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** does **Rabbi Yosei** derive that the **kindling of the wood chips** should be at the top of the altar? **He derives it from the** same **place** that **Rabbi Shimon derives it. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And the sons of Aaron the priest shall put fire upon the altar"** (Leviticus 1:7), which **teaches about the kindling of** the **wood chips that it may be** done **only by a fit priest and** one who is robed **in** the priestly **vestments of service;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon said to him:** There is no need for a verse to teach that a priest must kindle the chips, **for could it enter your mind that a non-priest could approach the altar? Rather,** this verse **teaches** about the **kindling of the wood chips, that they may be lit only at the top of the altar.**

*Rabbi Yehuda*, by contrast, does not accept that it is obvious a *zar* cannot ascend the altar. He therefore requires the verse of *benei Aharon* to establish that. But does that leave him without a source for the requirement to kindle at the top? No — *Rabbi Yehuda* derived that from the *Tzav* verse, *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo*. In turn, *Rabbi Yosi* used that *Tzav* verse for the third fire of *kiyum ha-esh*, and derived the kindling-at-the-top rule from the *Vayikra* verse — just as *Rabbi Shimon* does.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קָאֵי אַאַרְעָא וְעָבֵיד בְּמַפּוּחָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what would **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to Rabbi Shimon's reasoning? **If** this *halakha* was derived **from there, I would have said** a non-priest could light the fire on the altar by **standing on the ground** below **and using a bellows** to fan the flames on the top of the altar. Therefore, this verse **teaches us** that in all circumstances the person kindling the fire must be a priest.

One might ask: if the verse says one must light the kindling at the top of the altar, what would be the concern about a non-*kohen* lighting it from below? *Hava amina*: one might have supposed that a non-*kohen* could stand on the ground and use a bellows to fan flames upward, thereby feeding the fire from below without actually ascending. The verse therefore comes to teach that any tending or adding to the fire must be done by someone physically present at the top — and consequently by a *kohen*.

### R. Shimon Challenge

The discussion above covers *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position and how *Rabbi Shimon* challenges him, and also includes *Rabbi Yosei*'s position and how the various parties explain their sources for the rules they derive.

## Fourth Fire

### Overnight Remains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַכְּלוּ מִבָּעֶרֶב, מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ: מִ״וְּאֵשׁ״. וְרַבָּנַן — וָאו לָא דָּרְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where does Rabbi Meir** learn about an additional arrangement for the **limbs and the fats that were not** fully **consumed** on the altar **the** previous **evening? He derives it** from the phrase **"and the fire."** The apparently superfluous word "and" alludes to the existence of an additional arrangement. **And the Rabbis,** i.e., Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Yehuda, who disagree, **do not expound** the word **"and."**

The *Gemara* now turns to *Rabbi Meir*'s fourth fire, which is designated for *evarim u-fedarim shelo nit'achlu miba'erev* — limbs and fats that were not fully consumed overnight. The question is from where *Rabbi Meir* derives that such a separate fire exists.

### Return to Main

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, אֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַכְּלוּ מִבָּעֶרֶב מַאי עָבֵיד לְהוּ? מַהְדַּר לְהוּ לְמַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה. דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִין לְאֵיבָרִים וּפְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַכְּלוּ מִבָּעֶרֶב שֶׁסּוֹדְרָן עַל גַּבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְאִם אֵין מַחֲזִיקָן, שֶׁסּוֹדְרָן עַל הַכֶּבֶשׁ אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי סוֹבֵב, עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה מַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְסוֹדְרָן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל הָאֵשׁ אֶת הָעוֹלָה עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to **the Rabbis, what do they do with the limbs and the fats that were not** fully **consumed** on the altar **the** previous **evening?** Where are they burned? A priest **returns them to the large arrangement,** where the process of their burning is completed. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that **for limbs and fats that were not** fully **consumed** on the altar **the** previous **evening, that they are arranged upon the altar, and if** the space on the altar **cannot hold them, that they are arranged upon the ramp or upon the ledge** that protrudes from the altar, **until the large arrangement is made** the following day **and** then **they are arranged** upon it? **The verse states: "That which the fire will consume of the burnt-offering, on the altar"** (Leviticus 6:3). This is taken to mean that those items that the fire already consumed are once again placed upon the altar.

The *Rabbanan* do not accept the existence of a fourth fire. In their view, limbs that were not consumed overnight are simply returned to the main pyre. A *baraita* describes the practical procedure: in the morning, the *kohen* would arrange any unconsumed limbs to the side, clear and reset the main fire, and then restore the limbs to it. If there was insufficient space atop the altar for the limbs during this process, they could be placed temporarily on the ramp (*kevesh*) or on the *sovev* — the surrounding ledge — until the main fire was ready. The verse *asher tochal ha-esh et ha-olah al hamizbe'ach* — the fire that consumes the burnt offering on the altar — is read by the *Rabbanan* as teaching that this same fire continues to consume whatever remains of the *olah*; no separate fire is needed.

### Separate Fourth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: עִיכּוּלֵי עוֹלָה אַתָּה מַחֲזִיר, וְאִי אַתָּה מַחֲזִיר עִיכּוּלֵי קְטוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **Rabbi Meir** expound this verse? He derives from it that if **parts of a burnt-offering** that were already partially **consumed** on the altar fell off the altar, **you should return them** to continue burning; **but you do not return incense** that was **consumed** and fell off of the inner incense altar.

*Rabbi Meir* derives the fourth fire from the extra *vav* in *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo lo tichbeh* — a stylistic addition the *Rabbanan* do not treat as exegetically significant (*ve-Rabbanan vav lo darshi*).

### Pesuk on Olah

As for the verse *asher tochal ha-esh et ha-olah al hamizbe'ach*, which the *Rabbanan* use to teach that the same fire continues consuming the *olah*, *Rabbi Meir* interprets it differently. He reads the emphasis as falling on *ha-olah* — animal sacrifices — rather than on the continuity of the same fire. The teaching is therefore: *ichulei olah atah machzir ve-ee atah machzir ichulei ketoret* — you return the remnants of animal offerings to the fire, but you do not return unconsumed *ketoret* to the gold altar to finish burning. The practical import is that pieces of *ketoret* that fell off or were not fully consumed are not put back; only animal sacrifice portions are returned.

## Fifth Fire YK

### Universal Extra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת מוֹסִיפִין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם אִית לְהוּ. מָנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ: מִ״וְּהָאֵשׁ״. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ וָאו, וָאו הֵא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **At any rate, everyone** assumes there is an additional arrangement of wood **that they add on that day,** i.e., Yom Kippur, for the incense that is burned in the Holy of Holies. **From where** do **they** derive this? **They derive it** from the verse **"And the fire** upon the altar" (Leviticus 6:5). The apparently superfluous words "and the" allude to an additional fire. **And even one who does not** generally **expound** the word **"and" does expound** the phrase **"and the."**

Notwithstanding all the debate over the precise count on ordinary days, the *Gemara* notes that all opinions agree an additional fire is added on *Yom Kippur*: *mosifin bo bayom* — one extra fire is used on that day for the coals of the *Kodesh Kodashim* service. The question is the scriptural basis for this universal agreement.

### Vav-Hey Extra

The derivation is from the letters of *ve-ha-esh* — specifically, the *vav* and the *heh* that introduce the phrase *ve-ha-esh al hamizbe'ach tukad bo*. Where the verse could have read simply *esh hamizbe'ach tukad bo*, the additional *vav-heh* signals that under certain circumstances yet another fire is added beyond the baseline. The *Gemara* notes: *v'afilu le-man d'la darish vav, vav-heh darish* — even an opinion that does not ordinarily derive a *halacha* from a single extra *vav* would recognize that an extra *vav* combined with a *heh* is meaningful and comes to teach an additional case. The occasion indicated by this extra fire is *Yom Kippur*, the one day per year on which the coals of the *Kodesh Kodashim* service require their own dedicated pyre.

## Fire Source YK

### Which Altar?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשׁ מַחְתָּה וּמְנוֹרָה מִנַּיִין? וְדִין הוּא: נֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בִּקְטוֹרֶת, וְנֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בְּמַחְתָּה וּמְנוֹרָה — מָה לְהַלָּן עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, אַף כָּאן עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that coals of **fire** that are taken with **a coal pan** for the incense on Yom Kippur **and** for the fire **for** lighting **the candelabrum** must also be from the outer altar? **It is a logical derivation. "Fire" is stated with regard to the** daily **incense** offering, **and "fire" is stated with regard to the** incense **coal pan and the candelabrum. Just as there,** in the case of the daily incense offering, the fire is taken from **upon the outer altar, so too** here, in the case of the incense coal pan and the candelabrum, the fire should be taken from **upon the outer altar.**

Having established that there is an extra fire on *Yom Kippur*, the *Gemara* turns to a different question: from which altar is the fire taken — for the *Yom Kippur* incense service in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and for the *menorah*? The verse in *Acharei Mot* states *ve-lakach melo ha-machtah gachalei esh me'al hamizbe'ach* — he shall take a full fire pan of coals from the altar — but it does not specify *which* altar. One might have thought the coals are taken from the gold altar, which is closer to where they will be used. Similarly, the fire used to kindle the *menorah* might in principle come from the gold altar. The *Gemara* sets out to determine the correct source.

### Gezeira Shava

The *Gemara* first proposes a *gezeira shava* on the word *esh*: it is stated in the context of the daily *ketoret*, and it is stated in the context of the *machtah* of *Yom Kippur*, and it is stated in the context of the *menorah*. Just as we established that the fire for the daily *ketoret* on the gold altar comes from the outer altar, so too by analogy the fire for the *Yom Kippur* fire pan and the *menorah* comes from the outer altar.

### Closest Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בִּקְטוֹרֶת, וְנֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בְּמַחְתָּה וּמְנוֹרָה — מָה לְהַלָּן בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ, אַף מַחְתָּה וּמְנוֹרָה בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If the matter is derived through reasoning, it is also possible to posit an alternative argument: **Or,** alternatively, one could **go this way. "Fire" is stated with regard to the** daily **incense** offering, **and "fire" is stated with regard to the** incense **coal pan and the candelabrum. Just as there,** in the case of the daily incense offering, the fire is taken from a place **near to it,** i.e., the outer altar, **so too,** in the case of the incense **coal pan and the candelabrum,** the fire should be taken from a place **near to it,** i.e., the inner altar.

The *Gemara* immediately entertains a counter-argument: the same linkage by *esh* across these three cases could equally support an opposite conclusion — that they all follow the principle of proximity, *besamuch lo*, each taking fire from the closest available source. Under this reading, the fire for the daily *ketoret* comes from the outer altar not because it must always come from there, but because that is the nearest fire when the *kohen* stands there. For the *menorah* and the *Yom Kippur machtah*, the nearest fire might well be the gold altar.

### Esh Tamid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֵשׁ תָּמִיד תּוּקַד עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ לֹא תִכְבֶּה״ — אֵשׁ תָּמִיד שֶׁאָמַרְתִּי לָךְ, לֹא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Since there are two equally logical derivations, a verse is required to teach the *halakha*. **The verse states: "A perpetual fire shall be kept burning on the altar, it shall not go out"** (Leviticus 6:6). The **"perpetual fire" that I told you,** i.e., the fire of the candelabrum, about which the Torah states "a lamp to burn continually" (Exodus 27:20), **should** be lit **only** from a fire that is **upon the top of the outer altar.**

The *Gemara* resolves the question for the *menorah* from the phrase *esh tamid tukad al hamizbe'ach lo tichbeh* — the constant fire on the altar shall not be extinguished. The word *tamid* in the *Mikdash* is associated with the *menorah*, whose flame is called *ner tamid*. The teaching is: the fire for the *tamid* must come from the outer altar — *lo tehei ela be-rosho shel mizbe'ach*. This establishes that the *menorah* is kindled from the outer altar.

### YK Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ אֵשׁ לַמְּנוֹרָה, אֵשׁ לַמַּחְתָּה מִנַּיִין? וְדִין הוּא: נֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בְּמַחְתָּה, וְנֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בִּמְנוֹרָה — מָה לְהַלָּן עַל גַּבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, אַף כָּאן עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **We** have therefore **learned** the source for the **fire for** the **candelabrum. From where** do we derive the same *halakha* for the **fire for the** incense **coal pan** on Yom Kippur? **It is a logical derivation. "Fire" is stated with regard to** the incense **coal pan** on Yom Kippur, **and "fire" is stated with regard to** the **candelabrum. Just as there,** in the case of the candelabrum, the fire is taken from **upon the outer altar, so too, here,** in the case of the incense coal pan, the fire should be taken from **upon the outer altar.**

With the *menorah*'s source established, the *Gemara* now links the *Yom Kippur* fire pan to the *menorah* through another *gezeira shava* on *esh*: just as the *menorah* fire comes from the outer altar, so too the *machtah* of *Yom Kippur*. Alternatively, the derivation may proceed through *esh tamid* itself, carrying both the *menorah* and the *Yom Kippur* fire pan together.

### Coals Pesuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בִּקְטוֹרֶת, וְנֶאֶמְרָה אֵשׁ בְּמַחְתָּה — מָה לְהַלָּן בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ, אַף כָּאן בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלָקַח מְלֹא הַמַּחְתָּה גַּחֲלֵי אֵשׁ מֵעַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳״, אֵיזֶהוּ מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁמִּקְצָתוֹ לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְאֵין כּוּלּוֹ לִפְנֵי ה׳ — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** If the matter is derived through reasoning, it is also possible to posit an alternative argument: **Or,** alternatively, one could **go this way: "Fire" is stated with regard to the** daily **incense** offering, **and "fire" is stated with regard to the** incense **coal pan** on Yom Kippur. **Just as there,** in the case of the daily incense offering, the fire is taken from a place **near to it,** i.e., the outer altar, **so too, here,** in the case of fire for the incense coal pan, the fire should be taken from a place **near to it,** i.e., the inner altar. Since there are two equally logical derivations, a verse is required to teach the *halakha*: **The verse states: "And he shall take a pan full of coals of fire from upon the altar from before the Lord"** (Leviticus 16:12). The description of the altar being "from before the Lord" suggests it is not entirely before the Lord. **Which altar** is only **partially before the Lord, but not all of it is before the Lord,** i.e., part of it lies directly parallel to the Sanctuary, but part of it does not? **You must say** that **this is the outer altar.** Only the western side of it lies parallel to the entrance to the Sanctuary. In contrast, the inner altar is entirely within the Sanctuary and so is considered entirely before the Lord.

The *Gemara* finally confirms the derivation for *Yom Kippur* directly from the verse: *ve-lakach melo ha-machtah gachalei esh me'al hamizbe'ach — milefnei Hashem* — from before God. The question is posed: *eizehu mizbe'ach she-miktzato lifnei Hashem ve-ein kulo lifnei Hashem* — which altar has a portion that can be described as *lifnei Hashem* and a portion that cannot? The answer is: *hevei omer zeh mizbe'ach ha-chitzon* — it must be the outer altar. The inner gold altar, situated entirely within the *heichal*, stands wholly in the presence of God; one cannot speak of one side being closer to God than another. The outer altar, however, has its western side nearer to the *Ulam* and *Heichal*, and thus more *lifnei Hashem*, while its eastern side is further away. The phrase *me'al hamizbe'ach milefnei Hashem* therefore specifies the outer altar — take the coals from that part of the outer altar which faces toward the Divine Presence. This settles definitively that the fire for the *Yom Kippur ketoret* is taken from the outer altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב: ״מֵעַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב: ״מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳״, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מֵעַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי ״מִזְבֵּחַ״ — מִזְבֵּחַ פְּנִימִי, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דַּוְקָא מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳,
>
> **Translation:** **And it is necessary to write "from upon the altar" and it is necessary to write "from before God" because if the Merciful One had written** only **"from upon the altar" I would have said: What altar** is the verse referring to? The **inner altar.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "from before God." And if the Merciful One had written** only **"from before God," I would have said** it means **specifically from** the part of the altar that lies **before God,** i.e., from the northwestern corner, which lies directly parallel to the entrance of the Sanctuary;

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מֵהַאי גִּיסָא וּמֵהַאי גִּיסָא אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **but from this side or from that side** of the altar, both of which are not directly in front of the Sanctuary's entrance, I would **say** that **no,** the fire may not be taken from there. Therefore, it is **necessary** to write both terms in order to teach this *halakha*.
