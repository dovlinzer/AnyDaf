# Avodah Zarah 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Avodah Zarah

### Calendar & Nezikin

*Masekhet Avodah Zarah* is the last large tractate in *Nezikin*, and with its commencement we find ourselves drawing close to the conclusion of that entire order.

### Subject Matter

Contrary to what one might expect from its name, *Avodah Zarah* is not primarily about the act of worshipping idols. The first half of the tractate is devoted to a different question altogether: how do we negotiate the larger world around us, especially given that this world is not only non-Jewish but also, in the rabbinic period, a pagan world? That is certainly the focus of the first two chapters.

### Terminology & Censorship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לִפְנַי אֵידֵיהֶן שֶׁל גּוֹיִם שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים — אָסוּר לָשֵׂאת וְלָתֵת עִמָּהֶם, לְהַשְׁאִילָן וְלִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן, לְהַלְווֹתָן וְלִלְווֹת מֵהֶן, לְפוֹרְעָן וְלִפְרוֹעַ מֵהֶן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמֵּיצֵר הוּא לוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמֵּיצֵר הוּא עַכְשָׁיו, שָׂמֵחַ הוּא לְאַחַר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: On the **three days before the festivals of gentiles** the following actions are prohibited, as they would bring joy to the gentile, who would subsequently give thanks to his object of idol worship on his festival: It is **prohibited to engage in business with them; to lend** items **to them or to borrow** items **from them; to lend** money **to them or to borrow** money **from them;** and **to repay** debts owed to **them or to collect repayment** of debts **from them. Rabbi Yehuda says: One may collect repayment** of debts **from them because this causes** the gentile **distress.** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yehuda: **Even though he is distressed now,** when he repays the money, **he is happy afterward** that he is relieved of the debt, and therefore there is concern that he will give thanks to his object of idol worship on his festival.

The opening *mishna* begins with the phrase *lifnei edeihem*, "before their holidays," and the original text used the word *goyim* — simply, non-Jews — or *nochri*, meaning foreigner. If one were to ask a typical *yeshiva* student what the standard term in the *gemara* is for non-Jews, he would likely answer *akum*, an abbreviation of *ovdei kochavim u'mazalot*, worshippers of stars and constellations. But the original language of the *mishna*, *gemara*, and *baraita* uses *goyim* or *nochri*, neither of which implies idolatry.

What happened was that in the Middle Ages, Jewish apostates who had converted to Christianity informed the Church about passages in the *Talmud* that expressed negative attitudes toward non-Jews. This led the Church in various places to confiscate, censor, and even burn the *Talmud*. In response, Jews engaged in a process of self-censorship, replacing *goy* and *nochri* with *akum* — so they could claim to the Church that these passages referred only to ancient pagans, not to contemporary Christians. Some passages were removed entirely, such as the discussions of Jesus in *Sanhedrin*. And when a source is particularly sensitive, the substituted term is not even *akum* but *Knani* — a Canaanite — allowing the claim that the reference concerns only those ancient peoples from thousands of years ago. One therefore knows that a passage is especially pointed when the term used is *Knani*, as in the famous question of *gezel Knani*, theft from a Canaanite, which in the original would have been *gezel goy*.

### Goyim vs. Idolaters

It is important to recognize that not all non-Jews are idolaters. Even if the vast majority were in the rabbinic period, the concern is not always rooted in their status as idolaters; sometimes it simply arises from the fact that they are non-Jews. So while there is not a perfect one-to-one correspondence between *goy* and *oved avodah zarah*, there was in the rabbinic world a high degree of practical overlap. The very first *mishna*, *lifnei edeihem shel goyim*, illustrates this: the prohibition on certain interactions before their holidays derives precisely from the connection to idolatry, even though the word used was simply *goyim*. Nevertheless, the *gemara* itself at times acknowledges non-Jews who are not idolaters and not pagans, and that distinction matters throughout the tractate.

### Tractate Structure

The tractate can be divided thematically into three broad sections. The first two chapters deal with how one navigates the larger Gentile pagan world — whether one is indirectly supporting or participating in pagan practices, or being endangered physically or religiously through certain types of interactions. The middle chapter deals with idolatry more directly: what to do when one encounters objects that may have been used in pagan worship — an idol discarded in a marketplace, or a bowl with an image of a deity on it. Was it an object of worship, or merely a decorative item? The final two chapters focus intensively on one particular domain of concern: wine. The question of *yayin nesech*, wine touched by a non-Jew under various circumstances, occupies the last two chapters in considerable detail.

The tractate is genuinely fascinating on multiple levels. Historically, it provides a remarkable window into the world of the rabbis — the references to Roman pagan practices, the texture of Greco-Roman life, bathhouses and marketplaces and legal institutions. Halakhically, it is enormously practical, given that so many of its topics — wine, cooked foods, cheese, milk, and other products — remain relevant. And philosophically, it raises enduring questions about how Jews are to perceive the outside world, how much separation is required, how much integration is possible, and what assumptions we bring to our understanding of non-Jewish society.

### Significance & Sources

Because of its profound practical dimensions, *Avodah Zarah* attracted the attention of a great many *rishonim* whose commentaries are not found on more purely theoretical tractates like *Sanhedrin* or *Shavuot*. The abundance of *rishonim* on this *masekhet* reflects the ongoing relevance of its questions to Jewish communities across the medieval world.

### Yerushalmi & Dina

One might ask whether the *Yerushalmi* presents a different orientation to these questions than the *Bavli*. This is a genuinely important question. There are issues in the tractate that bear directly on the Jewish relationship to the land of Israel — questions about protecting Jewish economic self-sufficiency and Jewish presence in the land — which have a particular character in the Palestinian context. More broadly, the experience of Jews in *Bavel* was very different from that of Jews in Roman Palestine. In *Bavel*, Jews generally enjoyed a welcoming and supportive government; in the land of Israel, the Roman occupation carried with it a long history of oppression, and the Romans were experienced not as hosts but as occupiers of the Jews' own land.

The *Mishnah* and *baraita*, as Palestinian documents, reflect that Israeli perspective. How the *Bavli* then recasts this material is worth examining closely. One clear example of the difference emerges elsewhere: the principle of *dina de'malchuta dina* — the law of the kingdom is the law — is articulated in the *Bavli* but never appears in the *Yerushalmi*. The *Mishnah* treats tax collectors as presumptive thieves; the *Bavli* pushes back, arguing that they operate under government license and their collections are therefore legally sanctioned. These are not small differences; they reflect fundamentally different postures toward the non-Jewish political order. Whether a similarly clear divergence emerges specifically within *Avodah Zarah* is a question worth keeping in view as we proceed.

## Mishna: Business Ban

### Mishna Text

The *mishna* opens: *lifnei edeihem shel goyim* — before the holidays of the non-Jews — *shlosha yamim* — three days before — it is *asur lasset velatet imahem*, forbidden to engage in commerce with them, buying or selling; *ul'hashilan*, to lend them objects; *ulish'ol mehem*, to borrow objects from them; *ul'halvotam*, to lend them money; *ulilovot mehem*, to borrow money from them; *ul'faran*, to repay a debt one owes them; *ulifroa mehem*, or to collect a debt they owe you.

### R. Yehuda Dispute

Rabbi Yehuda disputes one item: *nifrain mehem*, one may collect debts from them during this period, *mipnei shemeitzer hu lo* — because having to pay is an anguish to the non-Jew. The act of paying causes him distress, not joy, and therefore it cannot serve as a source of the problem that the *mishna* is concerned with.

### Response to R. Yehuda

The Sages respond: *af al pi shemeitzer hu achshav same'ach hu le'achar zman* — even though he feels anguish in the moment of payment, afterward he feels relief and even satisfaction. The debt is discharged; he is free of an obligation. Whatever momentary pain the payment causes, it ultimately generates a sense of happiness.

### Rashi's Reason

From this exchange one can infer that something causing joy is the source of the problem. But why should causing a non-Jew joy be prohibited in connection with their holiday? Rashi provides a direct explanation: *vechulan mishum de'azil u'modeh la'avoda kochavim beyom eydo* — all of these transactions are forbidden because they cause the non-Jew happiness, and when that non-Jew arrives at his holiday one, two, or three days later, he will celebrate with greater joy and will give thanks to his god. The Jew's business deal, or loan, or act of repayment becomes the occasion for an expression of gratitude to Zeus or whichever deity — "Thank you for this good fortune." The Jew has thus indirectly contributed to an act of *avodah zarah*.

### Aiding Idolatry

Why is that a problem? The *gemara*'s fundamental answer is that non-Jews are bound by the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*, the Seven Noahide Laws, which include a prohibition against *avodah zarah*. By providing the occasion for their idolatry, one is in some sense abetting a transgression. Even if this does not rise to the level of *lifnei iver* — placing a stumbling block before the blind, which technically requires that the other party could not have sinned without your assistance — there remains a serious concern about aiding and abetting another person's sin.

### Three Frameworks

There are in fact three distinct ways of understanding the prohibition, and they are worth distinguishing carefully. The first and narrowest framework, the one that the *gemara* itself emphasizes, is *lifnei iver* or something adjacent to it: the non-Jew will sin, and I have helped make that sin possible. From this perspective, if non-Jews were not prohibited against idolatry, there would be no problem with our business dealings whatsoever. The second framework shifts the focus: it is not that I am concerned about their sin per se, but that through this transaction I become in some way connected to their act of *avodah zarah* myself. I am not transgressing, but I am participating — the way someone who stands in a kitchen chatting and tasting food while another person cooks on *Shabbos* is not violating *Shabbos* but is in some sense implicated in what is happening around him. The third framework is in many ways the most straightforward and the most directly intuitive: when a major pagan holiday approaches, all the surrounding commerce and excitement and social activity is bound up with that holiday. By participating in that commerce — even in transactions that have no direct connection to the worship itself — one is becoming part of the cultural fabric of an idolatrous event.

### Culture vs. Sin

This third framework is the *pshat*, the plain explanation. Consider what it means to go shopping in the days before a major holiday: even if one is buying perfectly ordinary goods, one is participating in the season, contributing to the excitement, and embedding oneself in a culture organized around that holiday. The question of borrowing money is particularly illuminating: if the non-Jew lends money to the Jew, he is not being aided in his idolatry in any direct sense. Yet that transaction too is prohibited. Why? Because the issue is not whether one is enabling their worship but whether one is becoming part of the broader social and commercial energy surrounding it.

Interestingly, the *gemara* does not explicitly articulate this third explanation. But it is, in many respects, the most compelling account of why borrowing — not just lending — should be caught by the prohibition.

### Non-Idolater Recognition

One important caveat must be kept in view throughout. The *gemara* itself acknowledges the existence of non-Jews who are not idolaters. The full logic of *lifnei iver* and of concern about connecting to *avodah zarah* applies only where actual idolatrous worship is at stake. Where non-Jews are not practitioners of idolatry, the concern about their thanksgiving to pagan gods simply does not arise, and the limits of these principles must be respected accordingly.

## Tosafot: Sunday

### Tosafot Q

A major practical question occupied the *rishonim* of medieval Christian Europe: the principal market day was Sunday, and Christianity was widely regarded as *avodah zarah*. How could Jews do business on Sunday, which was the Christian Sabbath and day of worship, when the *mishna* explicitly prohibits commerce before and on the holidays of non-Jews? This was not an ancient theoretical problem — it was an ongoing reality that Jews faced every week. *Tosafot* frames the question directly: *al ma samkhu ha'olam lasset ulasset beyom eid ovdei kokhavim imahem* — on what basis did the entire community rely when conducting business with them on their holiday?

### Saint Days

*Tosafot* notes one preliminary distinction: *nihi de-rov eideihem min ha-kedoshim hem* — most Christian holidays are saint days. A saint day commemorates a holy person, not a god, and does not involve worship of a deity in the same sense. Nevertheless, *Tosafot* acknowledges that every week there is one day — Sunday — that is a genuine religious observance, and the question therefore remains fully in force.

### Eivah Answer

The first answer *Tosafot* offers is *de-shari mishum eivah*: it is permitted in order to avoid engendering *eivah*, hatred on the part of non-Jews. If Jews refused to participate in the Sunday market, they might provoke hostility from their neighbors. This explanation reflects a particular way of perceiving the surrounding society — as one in which antisemitism is always potentially just beneath the surface, and in which Jewish behavior must be calibrated to avoid triggering it.

### Eivah Objection

*Tosafot* himself, however, is not satisfied with this answer. The *eivah* concern is intelligible when a non-Jew approaches a Jew directly and the Jew refuses to transact with him — that refusal is visible and can provoke resentment. But if Jews simply do not appear in the market on Sunday, why would the non-Jews even notice, let alone be offended? There is no obvious mechanism by which their absence generates *eivah*. Other *rishonim* do argue that if the entire Jewish community stops frequenting the market, the non-Jewish merchants notice the loss of business and become genuinely hostile. But *Tosafot* finds this response unpersuasive.

### Darchei Shalom

An alternative framing, the flip side of *eivah*, is *darchei shalom* — the ways of peace. Rather than being motivated by fear of non-Jewish hostility, one participates in Sunday commerce as a positive expression of cultivating good relations with one's neighbors. This is not about avoiding a danger; it is about affirming a value. Living among non-Jews requires fostering interactions that are genuinely constructive and neighborly.

### Machanif Explanation

*Tosafot* then cites a source from the *Yerushalmi* that refines this further. The *Yerushalmi* distinguishes between two cases: where the non-Jew is a stranger, one is not permitted to do business with him; but where one knows the non-Jew, it is permitted *mipnei she-hu k'machanif* — because it constitutes a form of flattery, or more precisely, a form of polite social engagement. The *Yerushalmi* adds a striking illustration: if one enters a city and finds the inhabitants in the midst of a celebration, one may join in the rejoicing — *she-eino ela k'machanif* — because to refuse would be socially inappropriate.

### Politeness Frame

What does *machanif* mean here? One way to read it is simply as a form of *darchei shalom* — maintaining congenial relations. But there is a more precise understanding: when one gives a neighbor a gift on their holiday or participates in a social gathering, the act is understood by all parties as neighborliness and basic decency, not as religious endorsement or participation in worship. The social context makes clear that one is simply being a good neighbor. Just as one might give candy to children who come to one's door on Halloween — which is quite different from taking one's own children to participate in the practice — there are acts that are read as politeness rather than as religious participation. When you join a celebration already in progress, to refuse pointedly is rude; to accept a drink or a greeting is simply human. *Machanif* in this sense means being appropriately polite and socially engaged, not betraying one's values. Of course, there is a line — one would not sing the songs of their worship — but the *Yerushalmi* is pointing to a range of ordinary social behavior that falls on the permissible side.

### Rabbeinu Tam

*Rabbeinu Tam* offers a different and more technical approach. He argues that the core prohibition of the *mishna* applies specifically to selling objects that the non-Jew could use directly in his acts of pagan worship. Selling goods that cannot serve as materials for worship is simply not covered by the prohibition. This provides a clean explanation for why ordinary commerce on Sunday — selling cloth, grain, household goods — does not violate the *mishna*.

### Money Fungibility

But *Rabbeinu Tam* faces an immediate challenge. If money is the one thing non-Jews actually do offer in their worship — the collection plate passed around in church — then any financial transaction with them might seem to be providing them with their offering material. He resolves this by invoking the fungibility of money: just because a Jew sold something to a non-Jew and the non-Jew now has money does not mean that those specific coins are the ones that will end up in the collection plate. The non-Jew had money before and will have money regardless. There is no traceable causal link between the Jew's transaction and the specific funds used in worship.

### Religious Loans

There is, however, a case where caution is warranted: *umihu nakhon hu lehachmir keshebah oved kokhavim ve'omer halveini ma'ot leshikri* — when a non-Jew explicitly comes and says, lend me money for my religious observance. In this case, the purpose of the loan is stated, and the Jew is directly funding a religious purpose. *Tosafot* notes that in the actual historical context of medieval Jewry, this could arise when a prominent ecclesiastical figure — identified by the pejorative term *operier* — sought to borrow funds for some religious undertaking, perhaps even a crusade. In such a case, lending should be avoided.

### Supporting Clergy

*Rabbeinu Chananel*, however, disagrees even here. He argues that even when money is given to priests and monks — *galachim* and *khumarim* — this does not constitute a direct act of worship. The money goes to support the practitioners, to pay for their food and lodging, not to be used in the actual ritual acts themselves. Supporting the personnel of a religious institution is sufficiently indirect that it does not cross into *avodah zarah*. *Tosafot* is thus navigating a genuinely complicated medieval reality: Jews were heavily involved in money-lending, and the question of whether one could lend to churches and monasteries was live and practical.

### Christians Not Idolaters

The most striking and theologically significant answer *Tosafot* offers is preserved toward the end of this discussion. Rather than arguing that the prohibition does not technically apply, or that various practical considerations override it, *Tosafot* entertains the possibility that we need to reconsider how we categorize Christians themselves. The key passage reads: *kimla beghavaihu delo palchu le'avodat kochavim* — we know that the Christians among us do not actually worship *avodah zarah* in any meaningful sense, even if their religion, considered as a system, can be characterized as *avodah zarah*. As a precedent, *Tosafot* cites the famous case of Rav Yehuda sending a gift to a non-Jewish acquaintance on his holiday, justified because *kimli beghavei delo palach le'avodat kochavim* — he knew this individual did not actually worship *avodah zarah*.

### Religion vs. Persons

This is one of the most consequential passages in all of the *rishonim* on how to think about Jews' relationship to the Christian world. *Tosafot* here drives a wedge between Christianity as a religious system and individual Christians as persons. The religion may be classifiable as *avodah zarah*; the people practicing it may not be. The concept *Tosafot* deploys — articulated more explicitly elsewhere — is *minhag avoteihem bi'yedeihem*: they are doing what they were raised to do, following ancestral custom, not engaging in a sincere and deliberate act of pagan worship. The religion is inherited rather than chosen, practiced by rote rather than out of genuine theological conviction.

There is admittedly something patronizing about this framing. It says, in effect: I can lower the barriers between us by deciding that even though you take your religion very seriously, you are not really a sincere worshipper of a foreign god — you are just going through motions you absorbed from childhood. In the context of medieval Christendom, there is an additional irony: the people who were least enthusiastic about the Church's religion were often those in whom the older paganism was still alive. Yet for *Tosafot*, this framing served a crucial function. It allowed for substantially increased interaction with the Christian population by separating the halakhic status of Christianity as a religion from the halakhic status of Christians as individuals — without requiring *Tosafot* to make the arguably more radical claim that Christianity itself is not *avodah zarah*.

### Interest Distinction

*Tosafot* in the following passage addresses the lending question from yet another angle. *Rabbeinu Tam* notes that the *mishna*'s prohibition on lending applies specifically to lending without interest — *davka bechinam*. But lending *beribis* — with interest — is permitted even in the days leading up to their holidays, *mishum demitzta'er tuva* — because being charged interest causes the borrower considerable anguish and irritation, not joy. Since the entire concern is that commerce generates the non-Jew's happiness and thereby contributes to his joyous thanksgiving on the holiday, a transaction that causes him financial pain rather than pleasure does not trigger the prohibition.

### Debt Collection

Similarly, the question of collecting outstanding debts during their holiday period is treated leniently by *Tosafot*. The *gemara* rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha that where there is no document — and one risks losing the money if collection is delayed — one may collect *mipnei she-hu k'matzil miyadam*, because one is in effect rescuing one's own funds from potential loss. *Tosafot* suggests that perhaps even where a document does exist, collection may still be permitted, since there is always the concern that the debtor will not have funds available at a later date. The combined effect of these discussions is that virtually all the commercial activities that medieval Jewish communities actually engaged in — buying, selling, lending, collecting — could be accommodated through one or another of *Tosafot*'s frameworks.

## Edeihem Dispute

### Aleph-Ayin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד תָּנֵי ״אֵידֵיהֶן״, וְחַד תָּנֵי ״עֵידֵיהֶן״. מַאן דְּתָנֵי ״אֵידֵיהֶן״ לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ, וּמַאן דְּתָנֵי ״עֵידֵיהֶן״ לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav and Shmuel** disagree with regard to the correct version of the text of the mishna. **One teaches** the term meaning: Their festivals, as ***eideihen*,** spelled with an *alef* as the first letter, **and one teaches *eideihen*** with an *ayin* as the first letter. The Gemara comments: **The one who teaches *eideihen*** with an *alef* **is not mistaken, and the one who teaches *eideihen*** with an *ayin* **is not mistaken,** as there is support for each version of the term.

The *gemara* now returns to the very word *edeihem* — "their holidays" — with which the *mishna* opens, and reports a debate between Rav and Shmuel. *Chad tani aideihen* — one of them reads the word with an *alef* — *vechad tani eideihen* — and the other reads it with an *ayin*. *Man detani aideihen lo mishtabesh u'man detani eideihen lo mishtabesh* — neither is making a mistake. Both readings are correct and carry distinct meaning.

### Destruction Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּתָנֵי ״אֵידֵיהֶן״ לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי קָרוֹב יוֹם אֵידָם״, וּמַאן דְּתָנֵי ״עֵידֵיהֶן״ לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״יִתְּנוּ עֵדֵיהֶם וְיִצְדָּקוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The one who teaches *eideihen*** with an *alef* **is not mistaken, as it is written: "For the day of their calamity [*eidam*] is at hand"** (Deuteronomy 32:35), and the future downfall mentioned in the verse is partly due to the festivals of idol worshippers. The term there is spelled with an *alef*. **And** likewise, **the one who teaches *eideihen*** with an *ayin* **is not mistaken, as it is written: "Let them bring their witnesses [*eideihem*], that they may be justified"** (Isaiah 43:9), i.e., the festivals will serve as witnesses against gentile sinners, proving that they engaged in idol worship. The term there is spelled with an *ayin*.

The *alef* reading derives *edeihem* from the word for disaster or destruction. The supporting verse is *ki karov yom eidam* — for the day of their downfall is near — drawn from Deuteronomy 32:35, where *eid* signifies calamity. On this reading, the *mishna* is covertly referring to the non-Jews' holidays as the days of their ruin. This is an example of a practice — using a derogatory term to describe something while appearing to use a neutral one — born of the psychological needs of an oppressed minority community. Jews living in a pagan Roman world that appeared to be flourishing and dominant needed ways to maintain hope and perspective; using the word *eid* to mean both "holiday" and "destruction" was one such rhetorical strategy. The same impulse can be seen in other examples: *beit tiflotam* rather than *beit tefillotam* — the house of their nonsense rather than the house of their prayers — or *Nittel Nacht*, which is not a Hebrew coinage meaning the night from which sanctity has been removed, but simply the German *Nativity Night*, the night of the birth.

### Testimony Reading

The *ayin* reading derives *edeihem* from *eidut*, testimony. The verse is *yitnu edeihem ve-yitzdaku* — let them give their testimony and be vindicated. On this reading, the idols of the pagans will one day be called upon to testify on behalf of their worshippers — but their testimony will in fact condemn those worshippers, exposing the reality that they engaged in *avodah zarah*. Their very holidays are thus moments that generate the evidence against them.

### Mitzvot Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״יִתְּנוּ עֵדֵיהֶם וְיִצְדָּקוּ״ בְּאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם כְּתִיב? הָא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּתִיב, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל מִצְוֹת שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה בָּאוֹת וּמְעִידוֹת לָהֶם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִתְּנוּ עֵדֵיהֶם וְיִצְדָּקוּ״ — אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, ״יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיֹאמְרוּ אֱמֶת״ — אֵלּוּ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is this** verse: **"Let them bring their witnesses that they may be justified," written with regard to the nations of the world? Isn't it written with regard to the Jewish people? As Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: All the mitzvot that the Jews perform in this world** will **come and bear witness for them in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Let them bring their witnesses that they may be justified." These are the Jews,** as their good deeds bear witness for them and demonstrate their righteousness. When the verse states: **"And let them hear, and say: It is truth"** (Isaiah 43:9), **these are the nations of the world,** who will admit to the righteousness of the Jews.

The *gemara* then notes that the verse *yitnu edeihem ve-yitzdaku* does not straightforwardly refer to idolaters; it would seem to be addressed to *Yisrael*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi resolves this: *kol mitzvot she-Yisrael osim ba-olam ha-zeh ba'ot u-ma'idot lahem le-olam ha-ba* — every *mitzvah* that the Jewish people perform in this world will come and testify on their behalf in the world to come. On this reading, *edeihem* — their testimony — refers to the *mitzvot* themselves, which function as witnesses for the righteous. The end of the verse, *ve-yishme'u ve-yomru emet* — they will hear and say, this is true — refers to the nations, who will be compelled to acknowledge the righteousness of the Jewish way of life even against their own interest.

### Rav Huna Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מַאן דְּאָמַר ״עֵידֵיהֶן״ — מֵהָכָא: ״יֹצְרֵי פֶסֶל כֻּלָּם תֹּהוּ וַחֲמוּדֵיהֶם בַּל יוֹעִילוּ וְעֵדֵיהֶם הֵמָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: The one who says** that the correct word is ***eideihen*** with an *ayin* derived this use of the term **from here: "They that fashion a graven image are all of them vanity, and their delectable things shall not profit; and their own witnesses [*eideihem*]** see not, nor know" (Isaiah 44:9). This demonstrates that the objects of idol worship will serve as witnesses against their worshippers.

Finally, Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua offers an alternative verse as the source for the *alef* reading: *yotzrei fesel kullam tohu, va-hamudeihem bal yo'ilu, ve-edeihem heima* — the fashioners of idols are all vanity, their cherished things will not help them, and it is they — the idols — that are their testimony. The idols they worship will not benefit them; indeed, those very idols will stand as testimony against them, confirming their idolatry. This is the same essential idea as the previous *ayin* reading, grounded in a different verse from Isaiah.

## Judgment Midrash (II)

### Future Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: לְעָתִיד לָבֹא מֵבִיא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וּמַנִּיחוֹ בְּחֵיקוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר: לְמִי שֶׁעָסַק בָּהּ יָבֹא וְיִטּוֹל שְׂכָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites homiletic interpretations of the verse that was discussed earlier: "All the nations are gathered together, and let the peoples be assembled; who among them can declare this, and announce to us former matters? Let them bring their witnesses, that they may be justified; and let them hear, and say: It is truth" (Isaiah 43:9). **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa taught, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Simlai** who taught: **In the future, the Holy One, Blessed be He,** will **bring a Torah scroll and place it in His lap and say:** Anyone **who engaged in its** study **should come and take his reward.**

Rabbi Hanina bar Papa — and some say Rabbi Simlai — expounded: *la-atid la-vo*, in the time to come, the Holy One Blessed Be He will bring a *sefer Torah* and place it in His bosom, and will declare: whoever occupied themselves with this, let them come and receive their reward. The scene being constructed is an eschatological judgment in which the *Torah* itself serves as the criterion for reward — and the surprising development is that the nations of the world rush forward to claim that they too deserve a share in that reward.

### Nations Gather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד מִתְקַבְּצִין וּבָאִין אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם בְּעִרְבּוּבְיָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל הַגּוֹיִם נִקְבְּצוּ יַחְדָּו וְגוֹ׳״, אֹמֵר לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אַל תִּכָּנְסוּ לְפָנַי בְּעִרְבּוּבְיָא, אֶלָּא תִּכָּנֵס כׇּל אוּמָּה וְאוּמָּהּ וְסוֹפְרֶיהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיֵאָסְפוּ לְאֻמִּים״, וְאֵין ״לְאוֹם״ אֶלָּא מַלְכוּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְאֹם מִלְּאֹם יֶאֱמָץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately, the nations of the world** will **gather** together **and come intermingled** with each other, **as it is stated: "All the nations are gathered together** and let the peoples be assembled." **The Holy One, Blessed be He,** will **say to them: Do not enter before Me intermingled; rather, let each and every nation enter with their scholars, as it is stated: "And let the peoples [*le'umim*] be assembled"** (Isaiah 43:9); **and** the term ***le'om*** means **nothing other than kingdom, as it is stated: "And the one kingdom [*ule'om*] shall be stronger than the other kingdom [*mile'om*]"** (Genesis 25:23).

*Misgabshin bahem dukhturin ve-irvuvya* — all the nations come crowding together in a great commotion, everyone rushing to claim their portion, as the verse says, *kol hagoyim nikbetzu yakhdav* — all the nations are gathered together. God says to them: *al tikanesu lefanai be-irvuvya* — do not come before Me all mixed together. *Ela tikanes kol uma va-uma ve-sofreha* — let each nation come separately with its own sages and learned leaders. The verse supports this: *ve-ye'asfu le-ummim*, and nations gather — *ve-ein le-um ela malkhut*, and *le-um* denotes a national identity, as in the verse *u-le'om mi-le'om ye-ematz*, one nation strives against another. What is remarkable here is the implicit insistence that nations not be painted with a single brush. There is diversity among the nations; they are not all the same, and they deserve to be evaluated distinctly.

### Clarity Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא עִרְבּוּבְיָא קַמֵּי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא? אֶלָּא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיעַרְבְּבוּ אִינְהוּ [בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי], דְּלִישְׁמְעוּ מַאי דְּאָמַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** it possible for **there** to be **intermingling before the Holy One, Blessed be He,** that it should be necessary for each nation to stand and be addressed separately? **Rather,** the nations are instructed to stand separately **so that they will not become intermingled with each other** in order **that they will** each **hear what He says to them.**

The *gemara* interrupts with a question: *u-mi ika irvuvya kamei hakadosh barukh hu* — is there any confusion before the Holy One Blessed Be He? Surely God can sort out all the nations simultaneously. The answer: *ela ki heikhi dela le-irvevu inhu bahadi hadadi* — the separation is not for God's sake but for the nations' sake, so that each nation can hear clearly and attend to what is being said to them. The arrangement serves clarity of communication, not any epistemic need on God's part.

### Rome First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [מִיָּד] נִכְנְסָה לְפָנָיו מַלְכוּת רוֹמִי תְּחִלָּה. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲשִׁיבָא. וּמְנָלַן דַּחֲשִׁיבָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְתֵאכֻל כׇּל אַרְעָא וּתְדוּשִׁנַּהּ וְתַדְּקִינַּהּ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ רוֹמִי חַיֶּיבֶת שֶׁטִּבְעָהּ יָצָא בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately, the Roman Empire enters first before Him.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the Roman Empire enters first? It is **because** the Roman Empire is the most **important** of all of the nations. **And from where do we** derive **that it is** the most **important? As it is written** in the book of Daniel with regard to the fourth empire that will rule over the world: **"And it shall devour the whole earth, and shall tread it down, and break it in pieces"** (Daniel 7:23), and **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This** empire that will devour the earth is **the wicked Roman** Empire, **whose name spread throughout the world.**

*Miyad nikhnesah lefanav malkhut romi tekhila* — immediately the Roman kingdom enters first. The *gemara* asks: why Rome? *Mishum de-khashiva* — because it is the most important. The source is the vision of Daniel, in which the fourth beast *ve-tokhal kol ar-a ve-tedushena ve-tedukena* — ate the whole earth, trampled it and crushed it. Rabbi Yochanan identifies this beast with wicked Rome, *she-tiv'ah yatza be-khol ha-olam* — whose imprint and whose power went out throughout the entire world.

### King First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּמַאן דַּחֲשִׁיב עָיֵיל בְּרֵישָׁא? כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מֶלֶךְ וְצִבּוּר — מֶלֶךְ נִכְנָס תְּחִלָּה לְדִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַעֲשׂוֹת מִשְׁפַּט עַבְדּוֹ וּמִשְׁפַּט עַמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״. וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְמֵיתַב מַלְכָּא מֵאַבָּרַאי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִיפּוֹשׁ חֲרוֹן אַף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that whoever is** more **important enters first?** This is **in accordance with** a statement **of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda says:** When **a king and a community** are brought before God for judgment, the **king enters for judgment first, as it is stated: "That He make the judgment of His servant and the judgment of His people Israel,** as every day shall require" (I Kings 8:59). **And what is the reason** that it is important for the king to enter first? **If you wish, say** that it is **not proper conduct for the king to stand outside** and wait for the trial of his subjects to end. **And if you wish, say** instead that the king is brought in first so that he may be judged **before** God's **anger intensifies** due to the sins of the community.

The *gemara* then asks: on what basis does the most important nation enter first? This is derived from a principle articulated by Rav Chisda: *melekh ve-tzibur, melekh nikhnas tekhila la-din* — when a king and his people come to be judged, the king enters first, as the verse states, *la'asot mishpat avdo u-mishpat amo Yisrael* — to execute judgment for His servant and for His people Israel, listing the king's judgment before the people's. Two reasons are offered for why the king goes first. The first: *lav orakhe ar-a le-meitev malka me-avrai* — it is not respectful for a king to be kept waiting outside. The second, somewhat more striking: *mikamei de-lifosh kharon af* — before God's anger builds. Since God is reviewing an endless parade of sins, to enter first, before the full weight of divine frustration accumulates, might be advantageous. The application of this reasoning to wicked Rome is admittedly somewhat strained — it is borrowed from a different context concerning the judgment of a Jewish king alongside his people — but it serves the homiletical purpose.

## Judgment Midrash (II)

### Rome's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: בְּמַאי עֲסַקְתֶּם? אוֹמְרִים לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הַרְבֵּה שְׁוָוקִים תִּקַנְּינוּ, הַרְבֵּה מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת עָשִׂינוּ, הַרְבֵּה כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב הִרְבֵּינוּ, וְכוּלָּם לֹא עָשִׂינוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְעַסְּקוּ בְּתוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its narration of the future judgment. First, the members of the Roman Empire enter. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, says to them: With what did you occupy yourselves? They say before Him** in response: **Master of the Universe, we have established many marketplaces, we have built many bathhouses,** and **we have increased much silver and gold. And we did all** of this **only for** the sake of **the Jewish people, so that they would** be free to **engage in Torah** study.

God asks Rome: *bemai asaktam* — what have you accomplished? Rome responds: *ribbono shel olam* — Master of the Universe — we have done great things. *Harbe shvakim tiknannu* — we established many marketplaces. *Harbe merkhata'ot asinu* — we built many bathhouses. *Harbe kesef ve-zahav hirbbenu* — we multiplied gold and silver, creating wealth and prosperity. *Ve-khulam lo asinu ela bishvil Yisrael* — and all of it we did for the sake of Israel, *kedei she-ya'asku ba-Torah* — so that they should be able to engage in Torah study. The implication: Rome created the conditions — the economic infrastructure, the social amenities, the accumulated wealth — that enabled Torah civilization to flourish. And for this they claim reward.

### God Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: שׁוֹטִים שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם, כׇּל מַה שֶּׁעֲשִׂיתֶם לְצוֹרֶךְ עַצְמְכֶם עֲשִׂיתֶם, תִּקַּנְתֶּם שְׁוָוקִים לְהוֹשִׁיב בָּהֶן זוֹנוֹת, מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת לְעַדֵּן בָּהֶן עַצְמְכֶם, כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁלִּי הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִי הַכֶּסֶף וְלִי הַזָּהָב נְאֻם ה׳ צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, says to them: Fools of the world!** Are you attempting to deceive Me? **Everything that you did, you did for your own needs. You established marketplaces to place prostitutes in them;** you built **bathhouses for your own enjoyment;** and as for the **silver and gold** that you claim to have increased, **it is Mine, as it is stated: "Mine is the silver, and Mine the gold, said the Lord of hosts"** (Haggai 2:8).

God's response is devastating: *shotim she-ba-olam* — idiots! *Kol mah she-asitem le-tzorech atzmekhem asitem* — everything you did, you did for your own sake. *Tikantem shvakim le-hoshiv bahen zonot* — you built your marketplaces to house your prostitutes. *Merkhata'ot le-aden bahem atzmekhem* — your bathhouses were for your own indulgence. *Kesef ve-zahav sheli hu* — and as for all that gold and silver, it is Mine: *she-ne'emar li ha-kesef ve-li ha-zahav ne'um Hashem tzeva'ot* — as it is written, the silver is Mine and the gold is Mine, declares the Lord of Hosts.

### Torah Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלוּם יֵשׁ בָּכֶם מַגִּיד זֹאת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִי בָכֶם יַגִּיד זֹאת״, וְאֵין ״זֹאת״ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אֲשֶׁר שָׂם מֹשֶׁה״. מִיָּד יָצְאוּ בְּפַחֵי נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Is there no one among you who can declare** that they have studied **this** Torah? This is the meaning of the continuation of the verse from Isaiah, **as it is stated: "Who among them can declare this?"** (Isaiah 43:9). **And "this"** is referring to **nothing other than the Torah, as it is stated: "And this is the Torah that Moses set** before the children of Israel" (Deuteronomy 4:44), and whoever did not engage in its study does not receive reward. **Immediately,** the members of the Roman Empire **leave disappointed.**

*Klum yesh bakhem magid zot* — is there anyone among you who can say *zot*? *Ve-ein zot ela Torah* — and *zot* refers to nothing other than *Torah*, as in the verse *ve-zot ha-Torah asher sam Moshe* — and this is the *Torah* that Moses placed. None of them actually engaged with *Torah*, and none of them truly did what they did for Israel's sake. *Miyad yatze'u be-fakhi nefesh* — they immediately departed despondent.

### Verse Interpretation

The *gemara* pauses to address the verse *yitnu edeihem ve-yitzdaku* more carefully. Is it referring to non-Jews — whose testimony condemns them — or to Jews? The answer, already sketched above in the discussion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, is that the verse operates on two levels simultaneously. The first part of the verse — *yitnu edeihem ve-yitzdaku*, they shall present their testimony and be vindicated — refers to the Jewish people, whose *mitzvot* testify on their behalf. The second part — *ve-yishme'u ve-yomru emet*, they will hear and say, this is true — refers to the nations, who acknowledge the truth even as they are being judged against.

## Persia & Nations

### Persia Enter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָאת מַלְכוּת רוֹמִי, וְנִכְנְסָה מַלְכוּת פָּרַס אַחֲרֶיהָ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא חֲשִׁיבָא בָּתְרַהּ. וּמְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲרוּ חֵיוָא אָחֳרִי תִּנְיָנָא דָּמְיָא לְדֹב״, וְתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵלּוּ פַּרְסִיִּים, שֶׁאוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין כְּדוֹב, וּמְסוּרְבָּלִין [בָּשָׂר] כְּדוֹב, וּמְגַדְּלִין שֵׂעָר כְּדוֹב, וְאֵין לָהֶם מְנוּחָה כְּדוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Roman Empire leaves, and the Persian Empire enters after it. What is the reason** that the Persian Empire enters second? The reason is **that after** the Roman Empire it is the next most **important. And from where do we** derive this? **As it is written** in Daniel's vision: **"And behold another beast, a second, like a bear"** (Daniel 7:5). **And Rav Yosef teaches: These are the Persians,** who are compared to a bear, **as they eat and drink** copious amounts **as** does **a bear, and they are fleshy like a bear, and they grow** their **hair** long **as** does **a bear, and they never rest, like a bear,** which is constantly on the move from one place to another.

*Ve-nikhnesah malkhut paras akhareha* — the Persian kingdom enters after Rome. The *gemara* asks: why Persia next? Because it is next in importance. The source again is Daniel's vision: *va-aru khivah akherei tinyana* — and behold, a second beast appeared, *damya le-dov* — resembling a bear. Rav Yosef expounds: this refers to the Persians, *she-okhlin ve-shosin ke-dov* — who eat and drink like a bear, *u-mesubbalin be-vasar ke-dov* — who are fleshy like a bear, *u-megadlin se'ar ke-dov* — who are hairy like a bear, *ve-ein lahem menuha ke-dov* — and who are never at rest, like a bear. This is not a flattering portrait.

### Persia Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֹמֵר לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: בְּמַאי עֲסַקְתֶּם? אוֹמְרִים לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הַרְבֵּה גְּשָׁרִים גָּשַׁרְנוּ, הַרְבֵּה כְּרַכִּים כָּבַשְׁנוּ, הַרְבֵּה מִלְחָמוֹת עָשִׂינוּ, וְכוּלָּם לֹא עָשִׂינוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְעַסְּקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, says to them: With what did you occupy yourselves? They say before Him** in response: **Master of the Universe, we have built many bridges, we have conquered many cities,** and **we have fought many wars. And we did all** of this **only for** the sake of **the Jewish people, so that they would engage in Torah** study.

God asks Persia: *bemai asaktam* — what have you done? *Harbe gisharim gisharnnu* — we built many bridges. *Harbe krakhim kavashnu* — we conquered many cities. *Harbe milkhamot asinu* — we waged many wars. *Ve-khulam lo asinu ela bishvil Yisrael, kedei she-yit'aseku ba-Torah* — and all of it was for the sake of Israel, so that they might engage in *Torah*.

### Persia Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: כׇּל מַה שֶּׁעֲשִׂיתֶם לְצוֹרֶךְ עַצְמְכֶם עֲשִׂיתֶם, תִּקַּנְתֶּם גְּשָׁרִים לִיטּוֹל מֵהֶם מֶכֶס, כְּרַכִּים לַעֲשׂוֹת בָּהֶם אַנְגַּרְיָא, מִלְחָמוֹת אֲנִי עָשִׂיתִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה״, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ בָּכֶם מַגִּיד זֹאת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִי בָכֶם יַגִּיד זֹאת״, וְאֵין ״זֹאת״ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אֲשֶׁר שָׂם מֹשֶׁה״, מִיָּד יָצְאוּ מִלְּפָנָיו בְּפַחֵי נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, says to them: Everything that you did, you did for your own needs. You established bridges to collect taxes from** all who pass over **them.** You conquered **cities to use** their residents for **forced labor [*angareya*];** and with regard to fighting the **wars, I wage** wars, and your success is from Me, **as it is stated: "The Lord is a man of war"** (Exodus 15:3). **Is there no one among you who can declare** that they have studied **this** Torah? **As it is stated: "Who among them can declare this"** (Isaiah 43:9), **and "this"** is referring to **nothing other than the Torah, as it is stated: "And this is the Torah that Moses set"** (Deuteronomy 4:44). **Immediately,** the members of the Persian Empire **leave from before Him disappointed.**

God rejects this: *kol mah she-asitem le-tzorech atzmekhem asitem* — you did it all for yourselves. *Gishartem gisharim litol mehem mekhes* — you built bridges to collect tolls. *Krakhim la'asot bahem angariya* — you conquered cities in order to conscript their populations into royal service. *Milkhamot ani asiti* — as for your wars, those are Mine: *she-ne'emar Hashem ish milkhama* — as it is written, the Lord is a Man of War. And again: *klum yesh bakhem magid zot? Ve-ein zot ela Torah* — none of you can claim any genuine relationship to *Torah*. *Miyad yatze'u milfanav be-fakhi nefesh* — they too left despondent.

### Persia Advantage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דַּחֲזָת מַלְכוּת פָּרַס לְמַלְכוּת רוֹמִי דְּלָא מַהְנְיָא וְלָא מִידֵּי, מַאי טַעְמָא עָיְילָא? אָמְרִי: אִינְהוּ סָתְרִי בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ וַאֲנַן בָּנִינַן, וְכֵן לְכׇל אוּמָּה וְאוּמַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But once the Persian Empire sees that** everything said by **the Roman Empire is completely ineffective, what is the reason** that they **come** forward? The Gemara answers: They believe that their claims will be more effective, as **they say:** The Romans **destroyed the** Second **Temple, and we** had **built** it, as the Second Temple was constructed under the auspices and with the encouragement of Cyrus, the king of Persia. The Gemara adds: **And likewise,** a similar exchange occurred **with each and every nation.**

The *gemara* asks: Persia watched Rome enter and fail to establish any valid claim. Why then did Persia think it would fare better? The answer: *amri inhu satrei beit ha-mikdash* — Rome destroyed the *Beit Hamikdash*; that is a decisive strike against them. *Va-anan banina* — but we, the Persians, allowed the Jews to return to their land and rebuild the *Beit Hamikdash*. Persia therefore believed it had a meaningful moral advantage over Rome, one that might carry weight in the divine judgment.

### Other Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דַּחֲזוֹ לְקַמָּאֵי דְּלָא מַהֲנֵי וְלָא מִידֵּי, מַאי טַעְמָא עָיְילִי? סָבְרִי: הָנָךְ אִישְׁתַּעְבַּדוּ בְּהוּ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, וַאֲנַן לָא שַׁעְבַּדְנוּ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. מַאי שְׁנָא הָנֵי דַּחֲשִׁיבִי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָנֵי דְּלָא חֲשִׁיבִי לְהוּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּהָנָךְ מָשְׁכִי בְּמַלְכוּתַיְיהוּ עַד דְּאָתֵי מְשִׁיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But once** the other nations **see that** everything said by **the first ones,** Rome and Persia, **is completely ineffective, what is the reason** that they **come** forward? The Gemara answers that **they think: Those** Empires **subjugated the Jewish people, but we did not subjugate the Jewish people.** The Gemara further asks: **What is different** about **these,** Rome and Persia, **which were singled out** explicitly, **and what is different** about **those** other empires that come afterward, **which were not singled out** and mentioned by name? It is **because** with regard to **these,** Rome and Persia, **their kingship extends until the coming of the Messiah.**

The same pattern then repeats for every nation: *ve-khen be-khol uma va-uma* — and so it is with each and every nation. Yet the *gemara* raises a further question: once all the other nations saw that even Rome and Persia, with all their historical significance and all their purported contributions, gained nothing — why did they persist and enter as well? The answer: *savri hanei ishtabdu be-Yisrael va-anan lo ishtabdanu be-Yisrael* — at least Rome and Persia actually subjugated Israel and had a direct historical relationship with the Jewish people. The other nations — those that never subjugated Israel, never had contact with her — thought that their very distance might work in their favor. Perhaps they could claim that unlike the great empires, they had never oppressed the Jews. So they entered with a different kind of argument.

### Significance

The *gemara* then asks a structural question: why does the *midrash* focus specifically on Rome and Persia and not enumerate all the other nations? *Mishum de-hanei mashkhei be-malkhusaihu ad de-ati meshikha* — because Rome and Persia maintain their dominion until the coming of the *Mashiakh*. They are the nations of enduring eschatological significance; the others, however historically real, do not bear on the ultimate end of history in the same way.

### Conclusion

What this entire *midrash* accomplishes — and it is worth stepping back to see this clearly — is a sustained meditation on a question that faced every Jew living in the Roman and later Christian world: how do we evaluate the accomplishments of the civilization around us? There is a genuine claim to be made. Rome built roads, bathhouses, legal institutions, and an economy; Persian policy permitted the return to Zion and the rebuilding of the Temple. Jews benefited from these things. Should they feel grateful? The *midrash* is constructed precisely to foreclose that gratitude without dismissing the underlying question. It places the claim in the mouths of Rome and Persia themselves, then rejects it on two grounds: first, they did everything for their own self-interest, not for Israel's benefit; and second, all the wealth and power they accumulated ultimately belongs to God anyway — *li ha-kesef ve-li ha-zahav*. Whatever good came from their civilization was not their achievement to credit; it was divine provision flowing through them. The *midrash* thus produces a particular way of seeing the world — one in which no gratitude is owed to the great powers, no sense of indebtedness to the civilization that surrounds and often oppresses the Jewish people. Whether the *gemara* elsewhere presents a more generous view is a question we will keep open as we continue.
