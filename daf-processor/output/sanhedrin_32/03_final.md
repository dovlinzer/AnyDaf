# Sanhedrin 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek Intro & Structure

### Opening & Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְאֶחָד דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת בִּדְרִישָׁה וּבַחֲקִירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Both** cases of **monetary law and** cases of **capital law are equal with regard to** the requirement for **inquiry and interrogation** of the witnesses, **as it is stated: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22), meaning that all legal procedures must be uniform.

Today's *daf* is 32, and with it we begin the fourth *perek*, *Echad Dinei Mamonot*. The first three *prakim* laid essential groundwork: the first *perek* established which cases require a court of three, of twenty-three, or of seventy-one — reflecting the fundamental distinction between civil and capital cases. The second *perek* digressed to address the special status of the *Kohen Gadol* and the king. The third *perek* returned to the core questions of what constitutes a valid court, how judges are defined, what renders witnesses valid or invalid, and at what stages a litigant may still bring proofs to overturn a decision. There it was established that in monetary cases a majority of one suffices to reach a verdict, and the framework for when evidence may be brought to reverse a judgment was laid out. These serve as the building blocks for the present *perek*.

### Drishah V'Chakirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה בֵּין דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת לְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת? דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה. דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – פּוֹתְחִין בֵּין לִזְכוּת בֵּין לְחוֹבָה, וְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – פּוֹתְחִין לִזְכוּת וְאֵין פּוֹתְחִין לְחוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having stated the essential similarity between the two, the mishna enumerates the differences between them. **What** are the differences **between** cases of **monetary law and** cases of **capital law?** Cases of **monetary law** are judged **by** a court of **three** judges, **and** cases of **capital law** are judged **by** a court of **twenty-three** judges. In cases of **monetary law,** the court **opens** the deliberations **either** with a claim **to exempt** the accused, **or** with a claim **to find** him **liable. And** in cases of **capital law,** the court **opens** the deliberations with a claim **to acquit** the accused, **but** it **does not open** the deliberations with a claim **to find** him **liable.**

The *perek* opens with the principle that *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* share certain fundamental requirements. Specifically: *echad dinei mamonot v'echad dinei nefashot b'drishah uv'chakirah* — whether in civil or capital cases, both require *drishah v'chakirah*. *Drishah* refers to the type of questioning that establishes the precise facts of what occurred and verifies that the witnesses are telling a consistent story. *Chakirah* refers to the seven specific interrogative questions — six concerning when the event occurred and one concerning where — which create the legal basis for a finding of *edim zomemim*. If other witnesses come forward and testify that the original witnesses could not have been present at the stated place and time — because they were elsewhere in the company of these new witnesses — the original witnesses are disqualified as *edim zomemim*. Both *drishah* and *chakirah* are required in *dinei nefashot* and, according to the *Mishnah*, in *dinei mamonot* as well. The scriptural basis is *mishpat echad yihiyeh lachem* — there shall be one standard of justice, whether the case is capital or civil.

### Key Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – מַטִּין עַל פִּי אֶחָד, בֵּין לִזְכוּת בֵּין לְחוֹבָה, וְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – מַטִּין עַל פִּי אֶחָד לִזְכוּת, וְעַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם לְחוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law,** the court **directs,** i.e., issues, the ruling **based on** a majority of **one** judge, **either to exempt, or to find liable. But** in cases of **capital law,** the court **directs** the judgment **based on** a majority of **one** judge **to acquit and based on** a majority of **two** judges **to find liable.**

Notwithstanding this shared foundation, the *Mishnah* proceeds to enumerate the differences between *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot*. The first difference already noted in the first *perek* is that civil cases require a court of three while capital cases require twenty-three. This distinction in court size does not constitute a violation of *mishpat echad*; the underlying rules remain the same.

The more substantive differences are as follows. With respect to how deliberations are opened: in *dinei mamonot*, the court may open by arguing either toward innocence or toward guilt — *potchin bein l'zechut bein l'chovah*. In *dinei nefashot*, however, the court may only open by arguing toward the accused's innocence — *potchin l'zechut v'ein potchin l'chovah*. The reason is the principle of *v'hitzilu ha'edah*: the court bears a special mandate to exert extraordinary effort to protect a possibly innocent life from wrongful capital punishment.

*Tosafot* makes a further observation: some of these distinctions can only apply to *dinei nefashot* in the first place, because in monetary cases there is no pure category of innocence or guilt. One party's innocence is the other party's loss; one party's liability is the other's gain. The concepts of *zechut* and *chovah* in monetary cases are always relative to the two competing parties, making an absolute *potchin l'zechut* structurally impossible. In *dinei nefashot*, by contrast, there is a straightforward distinction: arguments toward the accused's guilt constitute *chovah*, and arguments toward innocence constitute *zechut*.

### V'Hitzilu Ha'edah

The *Mishnah* continues: with respect to majority requirements, *dinei mamonot* may be decided by a simple majority of one — *matin al pi echad* — whether the verdict favors or disfavors either litigant. In *dinei nefashot*, a majority of one suffices to acquit, but a majority of two is required to convict. Since in capital cases there is a true *chovah* — the punishment of the accused — the *Torah* requires a heightened threshold before that verdict may be rendered.

### American Law Comparison

This structure invites comparison to American law, though the differences are significant. In American criminal proceedings, the standard of proof itself already differs: civil cases operate on a preponderance of the evidence, while criminal cases require proof beyond a reasonable doubt — a distinction that has no precise parallel in the *Mishnah*'s framework. More strikingly, American law in both capital and criminal cases requires a unanimous jury — not merely a majority of two — and in most civil jurisdictions unanimity is similarly required, though certain jurisdictions permit majority verdicts. The *Talmudic* framework, which imposes a mere majority of two for capital convictions, thus diverges markedly from contemporary practice, even while sharing the underlying intuition that the threshold for finding someone guilty of the gravest offenses must be meaningfully higher.

### Case Reversal Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – מַחְזִירִין בֵּין לִזְכוּת בֵּין לְחוֹבָה, דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – מַחְזִירִין לִזְכוּת וְאֵין מַחְזִירִין לְחוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law,** the court **brings** the accused **back** to be judged again if new evidence arises, **either** with a claim **to exempt** the accused, **or** with a claim **to find** him **liable.** In cases of **capital law,** the court **brings** the accused **back** to be judged again with a claim **to acquit** him, **but** the court **does not bring** him back to be judged with a claim **to find** him **liable.**

With respect to reversibility of verdicts: in *dinei mamonot*, the court may revisit a case and reverse its decision in either direction — *machzirin bein l'zechut bein l'chovah* — whether new evidence or a recognized error would benefit or harm either party. Since hurting one side always benefits the other, a reversal is necessarily both *zechut* and *chovah* simultaneously.

In *dinei nefashot*, however, a verdict may only be reversed to find the accused innocent — *machzirin l'zechut v'ein machzirin l'chovah*. Once someone has been acquitted, no new evidence may be brought to reinstate their guilt. This is analogous to the principle against double jeopardy in American law, which similarly bars retrial of an acquitted defendant.

### Who Can Argue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – הַכֹּל מְלַמְּדִין זְכוּת וְחוֹבָה, דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – הַכֹּל מְלַמְּדִין זְכוּת, וְאֵין הַכֹּל מְלַמְּדִין חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law, all** those present at the trial **may teach** a reason to **exempt** a litigant **or** to find him **liable.** In cases of **capital law, all** those present at the trial **may teach** a reason to **acquit** the accused, **but not all** present **may teach** a reason to find him **liable.** Only the judges can teach a reason to find him liable.

In *dinei mamonot*, anyone present — including students and non-judges, the *talmidim* who sit in the court as observers or apprentices — may argue on either side of the case. In *dinei nefashot*, students and non-judges may voice arguments in defense of the accused — *hakol melamdim zechut* — but arguments toward guilt may only come from the judges themselves — *v'ein hakol melamdim chovah*.

### Argument Switching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – הַמְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה מְלַמֵּד זְכוּת, וְהַמְלַמֵּד זְכוּת מְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה. דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – הַמְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה מְלַמֵּד זְכוּת, אֲבָל הַמְלַמֵּד זְכוּת אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר וּלְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law, one who** initially **teaches** a reason to find the accused **liable may** then **teach** a reason to **exempt** him, **and one who** initially **teaches** a reason to **exempt** him **may** then **teach** a reason to find him **liable.** In cases of **capital law, one who** initially **teaches** a reason to find him **liable may** then **teach** a reason to **acquit, but one who** initially **teaches** a reason to **acquit may not return and teach** a reason to find him **liable.**

The *Mishnah* addresses the ability of judges to switch their argumentative position during deliberations. In *dinei mamonot*, any judge who has argued on one side may freely switch to arguing the opposite position. In *dinei nefashot*, if a judge has been arguing toward the accused's guilt, he may change his position and argue for innocence. However, if a judge has already argued toward the accused's innocence, he may not subsequently argue toward guilt. *Rashi* clarifies that this restriction applies only to the deliberative arguments, not to the final vote: a judge who argued for innocence may ultimately vote for guilt if that is his sincere conclusion. The logic, however, is that since the judge has already publicly invested himself in the argument for innocence, that commitment will press him to search even more rigorously for grounds on which to exonerate the accused — and that pressure is precisely what *dinei nefashot* is designed to create.

### Timing Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם, וְגוֹמְרִין בַּלַּיְלָה. דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם, וְגוֹמְרִין בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law,** the court **judges during the daytime, and may conclude** the deliberations and issue the ruling even **at night.** In cases of **capital law,** the court **judges during the daytime, and concludes** the deliberations and issues the ruling only **in the daytime.**

Regarding timing: *dinei mamonot* may be opened during the day and concluded at night — that is, deliberations begun during daylight hours may be finalized on the following evening. *Dinei nefashot*, however, must not only begin during the day but the *gemar din*, the final pronouncement of the verdict, must also occur during the day. While it may take place on a subsequent day, it may never be rendered at night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – גּוֹמְרִין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם, בֵּין לִזְכוּת בֵּין לְחוֹבָה. דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – גּוֹמְרִין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם לִזְכוּת, וּבַיּוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו לְחוֹבָה. לְפִיכָךְ, אֵין דָּנִין לֹא בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת וְלֹא בְּעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law,** the court may **conclude** the deliberations and issue the ruling even **on that same day, whether to exempt** the accused **or to find** him **liable.** In cases of **capital law,** the court may **conclude** the deliberations and issue the ruling even **on that same day to acquit** the accused, but must wait until **the following day to** find him **liable. Therefore,** since capital cases might continue for two days, the court **does not judge** cases of capital law on certain days, **neither on the eve of Shabbat nor the eve of a Festival.**

Furthermore, in *dinei mamonot* the entire proceeding may be completed in a single day regardless of the outcome. In *dinei nefashot*, a verdict of innocence may be rendered on the same day the case opened, but a verdict of guilt may not. If the court is inclined toward conviction, judgment must be deferred until the following day — giving the judges time to sleep on the matter, reflect further, and avoid any rush to condemn a defendant in a capital case.

### Holiday Restrictions

It follows from the above that a capital case may not be initiated on *Erev Shabbat* or *Erev Yom Tov*. Since a guilty verdict requires a second day of deliberation, and *din* cannot be conducted on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*, beginning a case on *Erev Shabbat* would force an unacceptable delay in rendering judgment. Such delay constitutes *inuy hadin* — the wrongful prolongation of a judgment — and is therefore prohibited.

### Tahor V'Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, הַטְּמָאוֹת וְהַטְּהָרוֹת – מַתְחִילִין מִן הַגָּדוֹל. דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – מַתְחִילִין מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** In cases of **monetary law,** and likewise in **the** cases of **ritual impurity and purity,** the judges **commence** expressing their opinions **from the greatest** of the judges. In cases of **capital law,** the judges **commence** issuing their opinions **from the side,** where the least significant judges sit.

The *Mishnah* then addresses the order of arguments among judges. In *dinei mamonot* — and here, interestingly, the *Mishnah* adds cases of *tumah v'taharah*, which raises the question of why a matter of *tumah v'taharah* would come before a *beit din* rather than simply being referred to a rabbi for a *psak*. One would have to construct a case in which there was an attendant *malkus* or *mamonot* dimension — perhaps someone claimed that another rendered his grain *tamei* and thus owes compensation. The exact case is not spelled out, but in any event the principle is that deliberations begin with the senior judge, since in non-capital contexts there is no concern that the elder's view will suppress genuine debate.

In *dinei nefashot*, however, deliberations begin from one of the more junior judges — *matchilin min hatzad* — precisely because if a senior judge were to argue first for conviction, his voice might dominate and chill the deliberations. The *Mishnah* ensures that real debate and vigorous advocacy for innocence have every opportunity to unfold before the most authoritative opinions are heard.

It is worth noting that *malkus* cases have not been explicitly addressed. The *Mishnah* mentions twenty-three as the size of the court only for genuine *dinei nefashot*. It would appear that cases that fall between pure *mamonot* and *nefashot* — including *malkus* — are in many respects treated in the *mamonot* category, though this will require further analysis as the *Gemara* proceeds.

### Judge Eligibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְאֵין הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, אֶלָּא כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים הַמַּשִּׂיאִין לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **All are fit to judge** cases of **monetary law. But not all are fit to judge** cases of **capital law; only priests, Levites, and Israelites who** are of sufficiently fit lineage to **marry** their daughters **to** members of **the priesthood** are fit to judge cases of capital law.

Finally, the *Mishnah* addresses who is qualified to serve as a judge. Anyone — notwithstanding various disqualifications such as family relationship, *avadim*, and the like, and setting aside the broader question of whether women may serve — may sit as a judge in *dinei mamonot* regardless of lineage. In *dinei nefashot*, however, only *Kohanim*, *Levi'im*, and *Yisraelim* of sufficiently established *yichus* to be fit for marriage into *kehunah* may serve as judges. *Rashi* notes the practical implication: a *mamzer* may serve as a judge in *dinei mamonot* but not in *dinei nefashot*.

## Drishah Challenge

### Initial Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת מִי בָּעֵינַן דְּרִישָׁה וַחֲקִירָה? וּרְמִינְהוּ: שְׁטָר שֶׁזְּמַנּוֹ כָּתוּב בְּאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן בַּשְּׁמִיטָּה, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים וְאָמְרוּ: ״הֵיאַךְ אַתֶּם מְעִידִין עַל שְׁטָר זֶה? וַהֲלֹא בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי!״ שְׁטָר כָּשֵׁר וְעֵדָיו כְּשֵׁרִין. חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא אִיחֲרוּהוּ וּכְתָבוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that cases of monetary law require inquiry and interrogation of the witnesses. The Gemara asks: **Do we require inquiry and interrogation** in cases of **monetary law? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Makkot* 1:2): With regard to a promissory **note whose date is written on the first of Nisan of the Sabbatical** Year, **and witnesses came and said** to the signatory witnesses: **How** is it that **you are testifying concerning this** promissory **note? But** is it **not** so that **on such and such day** on which the promissory note was written **you were with us in such and such place?** The promissory **note** is nevertheless **valid, and its witnesses** remain **fit** to bear witness. The reason they are not proven to have signed the promissory note falsely is that **we are concerned** that **perhaps they delayed and wrote it,** i.e., the loan was given on an earlier date, and the promissory note was postdated.

With the *Mishnah* before us, the *Gemara* immediately raises a challenge: *dinei mamonot mi ba'inan drishah v'chakirah?* Is it really true that monetary cases require *drishah v'chakirah*? Those familiar with earlier sections of the *masechet* will recall that this is, at the very least, not straightforwardly true. The *Gemara* now probes how to demonstrate this, and from which source.

### Postdated Document

The *Gemara* introduces a *Tosefta* as its challenge. Consider the following case: a *shtar* bears a date of the first of Nisan in a *Shemitah* year. The end of that year will annul outstanding loans. Witnesses then come forward in what appears to be a textbook case of *hazamah* — they claim that the witnesses who signed the document could not have done so on the stated date, because on that very day the signatories were in an entirely different location, in the company of these new witnesses.

The *halacha* in this case is that the *shtar* remains valid and the original witnesses retain their credibility — *shtar kasher v'adav kisherim*. Why? Because *chayishinan shema icharu v'chasavu* — we entertain the possibility that the document was post-dated. Postdating a loan document is entirely permissible; it means that the liens associated with the loan will not take effect until the later date specified in the document. The loan itself may have been extended in an earlier year, with the document dated forward to allow the borrower more time before liens could be collected — and to give the approaching *Shemitah* less opportunity to annul the debt. Because postdating is permitted, the date inscribed in the *shtar* cannot be taken as definitive proof of when the transaction occurred, and *hazamah* is therefore not available as a mechanism in this case.

### Postdate Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בָּעֵינַן דְּרִישָׁה וַחֲקִירָה, הֵיכִי חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא אִיחֲרוּהוּ וּכְתָבוּהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the contradiction: **And if it enters your mind** to say that **we require inquiry and interrogation** in cases of monetary law, **how are we concerned** that **perhaps they delayed and wrote it?** The signatory witnesses would be asked on which day they signed the promissory note, and when that does not accord with what is written in the promissory note, their testimony would be disregarded.

The *Gemara* now articulates the inference: if *dinei mamonot* truly required *drishah v'chakirah* — and specifically *chakirah*, the rigorous establishment of place and time — then postdating would never be permissible in the first place, because the permissibility of postdating presupposes that the time stated in a document need not be taken as authoritative. The existence of postdating as a legitimate practice thus demonstrates that *chakirah* is not operative in *dinei mamonot*, at least as it relates to documents.

### Mishnah Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ מַתְנִיתִין: שִׁטְרֵי חוֹב הַמּוּקְדָּמִין פְּסוּלִים, וְהַמְאוּחָרִים כְּשֵׁרִין. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בָּעֵינַן דְּרִישָׁה וַחֲקִירָה, מְאוּחָרִין אַמַּאי כְּשֵׁרִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **And according to your reasoning** that we do not require inquiry and interrogation in cases of monetary law, **the mishna** elsewhere **should pose a difficulty for you** as well. As *mishnayot* are more authoritative than *baraitot*, it is preferable to raise a contradiction between two *mishnayot* than to raise a contradiction from a *baraita* to a mishna. The mishna teaches (*Shevi'it* 10:5): **Antedated promissory notes** are **not valid, but postdated** promissory notes are **valid. And if it enters your mind** to say **we require inquiry and interrogation** in cases of monetary law and that if there is a contradiction the testimony is not accepted, **why** are **postdated** promissory notes **valid?** The witnesses' testimony does not accord with what is written in the document.

The *Gemara* responds to this proof by noting that the challenge could have been posed even more simply, directly from the *Mishnah* itself, without resorting to the *Tosefta*. For the *Mishnah* states the general rule: *shtarei chov hamukdamim psulim hamucharim ksheirim* — predated loan documents are invalid, because they create liens earlier than the transaction actually took place; postdated loan documents, however, are valid. If you truly required *drishah v'chakirah* and insisted on firmly establishing the time of a transaction, how could a postdated document — one in which the stated date is by design not the actual date — ever be valid?

The *Gemara* then defends the choice of the *Tosefta* over the *Mishnah* as the stronger proof. The *Mishnah* merely permits postdating in the abstract. The *Tosefta*, however, goes further: it constructs a case in which postdating is permitted even in a situation where it is distinctly unusual and would ordinarily raise eyebrows — namely, a document dated to the first of Nisan of a *Shemitah* year, a time when virtually no one would lend money, since the end of that very year will annul the loan. One might have thought that in such a peculiar case, where postdating is so unlikely, we would treat the stated date as genuine and allow *hazamah* to operate. The *Tosefta* teaches that even in such a case — *afilu hachi*, even so — since *Shemitah* annuls only at the year's end, the document remains valid, and we still do not treat the date as authoritative enough to support *hazamah*. The *Tosefta* thus demonstrates that the irrelevance of the date in a *shtar* is not merely a practical accommodation but a thoroughgoing principle: even in the most unlikely of postdating scenarios, the time is not treated as firm.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, קַשְׁיָא. סִימָן: חרפ״ש.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its question: **In any case,** the contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita* is **difficult.** The Gemara presents **a mnemonic** device for the following discussion, with each letter representing the name of a Sage who suggests an answer: ***Ḥet*, *reish*, *peh*, *shin*.**

The *Gemara* concludes: *mikol makom kashya* — in any event the difficulty stands. The practice of postdating, particularly in such an extreme case, demonstrates that *chakirah* simply does not function in *dinei mamonot* in the way the *Mishnah* seems to imply. And while one might have tried to limit this conclusion to *shtarot* specifically — since a *shtar* is processed differently from live oral testimony before the *beit din* — the *Gemara* does not accept that limiting move. The exception is more general.

### Rabbi Chanina Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, אֶחָד דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְאֶחָד דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת בִּדְרִישָׁה וּבַחֲקִירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״. וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת לָא בָּעֵינַן דְּרִישָׁה וַחֲקִירָה? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּנְעוֹל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לֹוִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the first answer. **Rabbi Ḥanina says: By Torah law, both** cases of **monetary law and** cases of **capital law** are equal **with regard to inquiry and interrogation** of witnesses, **as it is stated: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22). **And what is the reason** the Sages **said** that in cases of **monetary law we do not need inquiry and interrogation?** It is an ordinance instituted by the Sages **so as not to lock the door in the face of** potential **borrowers.** The Sages were concerned that intensive examination of the witnesses would often result in contradictory testimony and render it difficult for lenders to collect their debts. This could lead to people refraining from lending money.

Rabbi Chanina resolves the tension as follows: *dvar Torah echad dinei mamonot v'echad dinei nefashot b'drishah v'chakirah* — at the level of *d'Oraita*, the *Torah* indeed imposes the same requirement of *drishah v'chakirah* on both civil and capital cases, as the *Mishnah* states, grounded in the verse *mishpat echad yihiyeh lachem*. The *Mishnah* is accurately describing the *d'Oraita* framework.

### Lo Tin'ol Daltot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so** that the Sages removed the requirement for inquiry and interrogation in cases of monetary law,

The question then becomes: why did the Sages waive *drishah v'chakirah* in *dinei mamonot* in practice? The answer is *kedei shelo tin'ol daltot bifnei lovin* — so as not to lock the door before borrowers. In the *Torah*'s economic system, lending money is already an unattractive proposition: one may not charge interest, the borrower may deny the debt, collection requires litigation, and *Shemitah* threatens to annul the loan entirely. To impose additionally the full rigors of *drishah v'chakirah* — making it harder to collect in court — would be to discourage lending altogether and thereby harm the very borrowers the system seeks to protect. The Sages therefore waived these requirements to keep the lending market viable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָעוּ – לֹא יְשַׁלְּמוּ? כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁתִּנְעוֹל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לוֹוִין!
>
> **Translation:** then if the judges **erred they should not** need to **pay** the party they wronged, as they can claim that they were prevented from examining the witnesses effectively. The Gemara answers: If that were to be the *halakha*, **all the more so that** this **would lock the door in the face of** potential **borrowers.** If people know that the courts are not responsible for an error in judgment, they will not be willing to lend money.

The *Gemara* then raises an objection: if the basis for the waiver is *lo tin'ol daltot bifnei lovin*, could the judges of a *beit din* that errs in a monetary case use the streamlined process as a defense — arguing that since *drishah v'chakirah* was not required of them, they cannot be held liable for the mistake? The *Gemara* rejects this: *kol shekein she-tin'ol daltot bifnei lovin* — if judges were freed from liability for errors precisely because *drishah v'chakirah* was waived, the result would be even more chilling for lenders, who would have no recourse if the court wrongly decided against them. The protections for lenders must therefore remain intact.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מַתְנִיתִין דְּהָכָא בְּדִינֵי קְנָסוֹת, וְאִידַּךְ בְּהוֹדָאוֹת וְהַלְוָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The ruling of **the mishna here,** that cases of monetary law require inquiry and interrogation, is stated **with regard to laws of fines,** not standard cases of monetary law. **And the other** sources, i.e., the mishna in tractate *Shevi'it* and the *baraita*, which do not require inquiry and interrogation, are stated **with regard to** cases of **admissions and loans,** in which there is cause to relax the procedures of deliberation, as explained.

*Rava* offers a different resolution. He accepts that in practice *drishah v'chakirah* has been waived for standard monetary cases. Our *Mishnah*, however, which states that *dinei mamonot* do require *drishah v'chakirah*, is not speaking of ordinary loans and admissions — *hoda'ot v'halva'ot* — but rather of *dinei knasot*: monetary fines imposed as punishment. Fines occupy a different category from ordinary civil claims; they function more like sanctions than like contractual remedies, and their punitive character brings them closer to *malkus* and *misah* than to ordinary commerce. Accordingly, the *Torah*'s requirement of *drishah v'chakirah* was never waived for *dinei knasot*, even as it was relaxed for routine monetary cases.

### Reish Lakish Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּהוֹדָאָה וְהַלְוָאָה. כָּאן – בְּדִין מְרוּמֶּה, כָּאן – בְּדִין שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְרוּמֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: This and that,** i.e., both the mishna here and the other sources, are stated **with regard to** cases of **an admission and a loan.** The distinction between them is that the mishna **here,** which rules that cases of monetary law require inquiry and interrogation, is stated **with regard to** a possibly **fraudulent trial,** where the court suspects that one party is attempting to defraud the other party and have witnesses offer false testimony on his own behalf. **There,** in the *baraita* and in the mishna in tractate *Shevi'it*, which do not require inquiry and interrogation, the ruling is stated **with regard to a trial that** does **not** appear **fraudulent.**

*Rav Pappa* agrees that the waiver applies to ordinary *hoda'ot* and *halva'ot*, and that the *Mishnah* is therefore to be understood along one of the lines already proposed. He then cites *Reish Lakish*, who raises a different principle from a different scriptural source. The verse *betzedek tishpot amitecha* — judge your neighbor with righteousness — stands alongside the emphatic *tzedek tzedek tirdof* — justice, justice shall you pursue. Why the repetition? *Reish Lakish* resolves it: *kan b'din merumah kan b'din she-eino merumah* — the first formulation addresses an ordinary case, while the doubled emphasis of *tzedek tzedek tirdof* addresses a case where the judge suspects that something fraudulent is taking place. *Tosafot* notes that if fraud is definitively established but cannot be identified, the court should simply refuse to adjudicate the case. But where fraud is merely suspected, the court must impose *drishah v'chakirah* with particular rigor, pressing hard to uncover the deception. Thus the *Mishnah*'s statement that *dinei mamonot* require *drishah v'chakirah* may refer specifically to suspicious cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״בְּצֶדֶק תִּשְׁפֹּט עֲמִיתֶךָ״, וּכְתִיב ״צֶדֶק צֶדֶק תִּרְדֹּף״. הָא כֵּיצַד? כָּאן – בְּדִין מְרוּמֶּה, כָּאן – בְּדִין שֶׁאֵין מְרוּמֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** This distinction is **in accordance with** the statement **of Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish raises a contradiction** between two verses: It **is written** in one verse: **"In justice shall you judge your neighbor"** (Leviticus 19:15), **and** it **is written** in another verse: **"Justice, justice, shall you follow"** (Deuteronomy 16:20), with the repetition indicating that it is not enough to merely judge with justice. He continues: **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? **Here,** this latter verse is stated **with regard to** a possibly **fraudulent trial,** where the court must take extra care to judge with justice; and **there,** that former verse is stated **with regard to a trial that** does **not** appear **fraudulent.**

### Rav Ashi Tzedek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מַתְנִיתִין כִּדְשַׁנִּין, קְרָאֵי אֶחָד לְדִין וְאֶחָד לִפְשָׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** The ruling of **the mishna here,** that cases of monetary law require inquiry and interrogation, is **as we answered,** i.e., in accordance with any one of the answers offered by the other *amora'im*. And those **verses** were not stated with regard to fraudulent trials; rather, **one** is stated **with regard to judgment,** in which the court must pursue justice extensively, **and one** is stated **with regard to compromise.**

*Rav Ashi* adds a further layer. He accepts that the *Mishnah*'s *drishah v'chakirah* requirement is addressed by one of the foregoing answers. But he observes that the doubled language of *tzedek tzedek tirdof* carries an additional teaching beyond the suspicious-case principle of *Reish Lakish*: *echad l'din v'echad l'pshara* — one instance of *tzedek* refers to formal adjudication, and the second refers to compromise. The verse is teaching that the same standard of justice and rigor that governs a court ruling must also govern a *pshara* — a negotiated compromise. Even when the parties agree to settle rather than receive a formal verdict, the arrangement must be constructed with fairness and genuine moral seriousness, not merely with whatever is expedient.

## Ships & Camels

### Ships Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״צֶדֶק צֶדֶק תִּרְדֹּף״ – אֶחָד לְדִין וְאֶחָד לִפְשָׁרָה. כֵּיצַד? שְׁתֵּי סְפִינוֹת עוֹבְרוֹת בַּנָּהָר וּפָגְעוּ זֶה בָּזֶה, אִם עוֹבְרוֹת שְׁתֵּיהֶן – שְׁתֵּיהֶן טוֹבְעוֹת, בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹבְרוֹת. וְכֵן שְׁנֵי גְּמַלִּים שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹלִים בְּמַעֲלוֹת בֵּית חוֹרוֹן וּפָגְעוּ זֶה בָּזֶה, אִם עָלוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן – שְׁנֵיהֶן נוֹפְלִין, בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – שְׁנֵיהֶן עוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"Justice, justice, shall you follow," one** mention of "justice" is stated **with regard to judgment and one** is stated **with regard to compromise. How so?** Where there are **two boats traveling on the river and they encounter each other, if both of them** attempt to **pass, both of them sink,** as the river is not wide enough for both to pass. If they pass **one after the other, both of them pass. And similarly,** where there are **two camels who were ascending the ascent of Beit Ḥoron,** where there is a narrow steep path, **and they encounter each other, if both of them** attempt to **ascend, both of them fall.** If they ascend **one after the other, both of them ascend.**

A *Baraita* is cited to illustrate the principle of *tzedek* in *pshara*. Two ships are traveling through a body of water and arrive simultaneously at a narrow strait. If both attempt to pass at once, both will sink. If they take turns — one going first — both can pass safely.

### Camels Case

The parallel case: two camel-drivers are ascending the narrow mountain path of *Beit Choron* and encounter one another face to face. If both try to proceed simultaneously, both will fall. If one yields and they go one at a time, both reach their destination.

### Priority Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? טְעוּנָה וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה – תִּידָּחֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה מִפְּנֵי טְעוּנָה. קְרוֹבָה וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ קְרוֹבָה – תִּידָּחֶה קְרוֹבָה מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינָהּ קְרוֹבָה. הָיוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן קְרוֹבוֹת, שְׁתֵּיהֶן רְחוֹקוֹת – הָטֵל פְּשָׁרָה בֵּינֵיהֶן, וּמֵעֲלוֹת שָׂכָר זוֹ לָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does one decide which of them should go first? If there is one boat that is **laden and** one boat **that is not laden,** the needs of the one **that is not laden should be overridden due to** the needs of the one **that is laden.** If there is one boat that is **close** to its destination **and** one boat **that is not close** to its destination, the needs of the one that is **close should be overridden due to** the needs of the one **that is not close.** If **both of them were close** to their destinations, or **both of them were far** from their destinations, **impose a compromise between them** to decide which goes first, **and** the owners of the boats **pay a fee to one other,** i.e., the owners of the first boat compensate the owner of the boat that waits, for any loss incurred.

The *Baraita* then establishes the principles for determining who goes first. If one vessel or camel is laden with goods and the other is not, the laden one takes priority — *tidacha she-eina te'una mipnei te'una* — because the economic cost of delay is greater for the party carrying cargo. If one is far from its destination and the other is close, the one that has a longer journey ahead takes priority — *tidacha krova mipnei she-eina krova* — because making the farther party wait imposes a greater burden. These are not arbitrary choices; they reflect a principled assessment of relative need and cost.

### Compromise Payment

If the two are equal in all relevant respects — both close, or both far, both laden, or both unladen — then a *pshara* is arranged between them. But the *Baraita* adds a critical element: even within the *pshara*, fairness demands that the party who goes first compensate the party who yields — *ma'aleh s'char zu l'zo*. The one who benefits from going first pays for that privilege. How the precise amount is calculated, or what happens when both parties wish to pay for the right to proceed first, is left open — but the underlying principle is clear. Even compromise must be structured, principled, and fair. This is what *tzedek tzedek tirdof* demands even in the domain of *pshara*.

## Best Courts Pursuit

### Excellent Courts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״צֶדֶק צֶדֶק תִּרְדֹּף״ – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה, אַחַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְלוֹד, אַחַר רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי לִבְרוּר חַיִל.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"Justice, justice, shall you follow."** This teaches that one should **follow the best,** most prestigious, **court** of the generation. For example, follow **after Rabbi Eliezer to Lod, after Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai to Beror Ḥayil.**

The *Gemara* offers yet another reading of *tzedek tzedek tirdof*, this one addressed not to courts but to litigants. *Halach achar beit din yafe* — seek out the finest court available to you. The verse is read as a mandate for parties to a dispute to pursue not merely the nearest or most convenient judicial forum, but the best one, even if that requires traveling.

### Sages' Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: קוֹל רֵיחַיִם בְּבוּרְנִי – שְׁבוּעַ הַבֵּן, שְׁבוּעַ הַבֵּן. אוֹר הַנֵּר בִּבְרוּר חַיִל – מִשְׁתֶּה שָׁם, מִשְׁתֶּה שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught:** When the gentile authorities issued decrees outlawing observance of the mitzvot, members of Jewish communities devised clandestine ways of indicating observance of mitzvot to each other. For example: If one produces **the sound of a millstone in** the city called **Burni,** this is tantamount to announcing: **Week of the son, week of the son,** i.e., there will be a circumcision. If one displays the **light of a lamp in** the city called **Beror Ḥayil,** this is tantamount to announcing: There is a wedding **feast there,** there is a wedding **feast there.**

The *Gemara* illustrates this by listing the great sages of the era and their locations: *achar Rabbi Eliezer l'Lod* — follow Rabbi Eliezer to Lod; *achar Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai l'Bror Chayil* — follow Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai to Bror Chayil. Make the journey to wherever the greatest scholars have established their courts.

## Brit Milah Customs

### Mill Sound Signal

The mention of Bror Chayil occasions a *Baraita* that digresses into local customs of that region and its neighbor Borni. The *Baraita* teaches: *kol recheiyim b'Borni shavua ben* — the sound of a mill grinding in Borni is a signal that a *brit milah* is taking place. *Rashi* explains that *shavua ben* was a phrase used to refer to a *brit milah*, derived from the fact that it occurs at the end of seven days. *Rashi* further suggests that this was a period of persecution, and the sound of the mill served as a covert announcement that a *brit milah* was being performed.

### Candle Lighting

The parallel custom for Bror Chayil: *ha-ner b'Bror Chayil mishteh sham* — if candles are seen burning in Bror Chayil, it signals that a celebration is taking place. *Rashi* indicates that this, too, refers to a *brit milah* rather than a wedding, though *mishteh* can refer to either.

### Candles & Sheet

*Tosafot* raises an obvious question: since candles burn at night in general, how does one know that the candles in Bror Chayil specifically signal a celebration? Perhaps it refers to a conspicuous number of candles, or to candles kindled during the day. *Tosafot* then draws a normative conclusion from this passage: *mikan nahagu lehadlik ner b'milah* — from here derives the custom of lighting candles at a *brit milah*. *Tosafot* also notes an attendant custom: *umah shenahagu lifros hamapal hapetach* — the practice of hanging a sheet or cloth over the doorway at a *brit milah*, which derives from a tradition that in earlier times in Jerusalem, when a cloth was spread over the entrance of a home, it signaled an open house — a *seudat mitzvah* to which guests were invited to enter. Both customs — candle-lighting and the hanging of the cloth — are thus traced by *Tosafot* to this passage.

## Best Courts Pursuit (II)

### More Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״צֶדֶק צֶדֶק תִּרְדֹּף״ – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר חֲכָמִים לִישִׁיבָה. אַחַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְלוֹד, אַחַר רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי לִבְרוּר חַיִל, אַחַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לִפְקִיעִין, אַחַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְיַבְנֶה, אַחַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לִבְנֵי בְרָק, אַחַר רַבִּי מַתְיָא לְרוֹמִי, אַחַר רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן לְסִיכְנִי, אַחַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְצִיפּוֹרִי, אַחַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה לִנְצִיבִין, אַחַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לַגּוֹלָה, אַחַר רַבִּי לְבֵית שְׁעָרִים, אַחַר חֲכָמִים לְלִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"Justice, justice, shall you follow."** This teaches that one should **follow the Sages to the academy** where they are found. For example, follow **after Rabbi Eliezer to Lod, after Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai to Beror Ḥayil, after Rabbi Yehoshua to Peki'in, after Rabban Gamliel to Yavne, after Rabbi Akiva to Bnei Brak, after Rabbi Matya to Rome [*Romi*], after Rabbi Ḥananya ben Teradyon to Sikhnei, after Rabbi Yosei to Tzippori, after Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira to Netzivin, after Rabbi Yehoshua to the exile [*gola*],** i.e., Babylonia, **after Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **to Beit She'arim,** and **after the Sages** in the time of the Temple **to the Chamber of Hewn Stone.**

The *Gemara* continues the list of sages and their locations, now introducing a second *Baraita* that elaborates further. In addition to Rabbi Eliezer in Lod and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai in Bror Chayil, the list includes Rabbi Yehoshua in *Peki'in*, Rabban Gamliel in Yavneh, Rabbi Akiva in Bnei Brak, *achar Rabi Matya le-Romi* — Rabbi Matya in Rome (a striking reminder that the community of great Torah scholarship extended even to Rome), Rabbi Chananyah ben Teradyon in Sichni, Rabbi Yosi in Tzippori, and *achar Rabi Yehudah ben Beteira le-Netzivin* — Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira in Netzivin. The list also includes, in some versions, Rabbi Chanina in *Golah*, which *Rashi* identifies as Pumbedita, and Rabbi [Yehudah HaNasi] in Beit Shearim — which one can visit in Israel today, where significant archaeological remains have been excavated.

### Lishkat HaGazit

The list culminates with the ultimate venue: *achar chachamim le-Lishkat HaGazit* — follow the great Sages to the *Lishkat HaGazit*, the Chamber of Hewn Stone, seat of the *Sanhedrin HaGedolah*. The word *tirdof* is thus read with full literalness: not merely pursue justice as an abstraction, but physically run, travel, relocate to wherever the greatest justice can be obtained.

## Opening in Defense

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת פּוֹתְחִין כּוּ׳. הֵיכִי אָמְרִינַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ: מִי יֵימַר כִּדְקָאָמְרִיתוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in cases of **monetary law,** the court **opens** the deliberations either with a claim to exempt the accused, or with a claim to find him liable. In cases of capital law, the court opens the deliberations with a claim to acquit the accused, but does not open the deliberations with a claim to find him liable. The Gemara asks: **How do we say** this opening stage of the deliberations? In other words, with what claim does the court begin deliberating? **Rav Yehuda said: We say this to** the witnesses: **Who says** that the event occurred **as you said?** Perhaps you erred?

Having established that in *dinei nefashot* one opens the deliberations in defense of the accused — *potchin l'zechut* — the *Gemara* now confronts a practical difficulty. What exactly does this mean when the witnesses have just testified and their testimony appears clear and damning? How does one "open for innocence" in the face of seemingly unambiguous evidence of guilt?

### Ulla's Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ עוּלָּא: וְהָא חָסְמִינַן לְהוּ? וְלִיחַסְמוּ! מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, מַסִּיעִין אֶת הָעֵדִים מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּיטָּרֵף דַּעְתָּן וְיַחְזְרוּ בָּהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said to him: But** by confronting the witnesses in this manner, **we silence them.** The witnesses will think that the court suspects them of lying, and they will not testify. Rav Yehuda said to him: **And let them be silenced. Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:1): **Rabbi Shimon ben Eliezer says:** In cases of capital law, the court **brings the witnesses from** one **place to** another **place in order to confuse them so that they will retract** their testimony if they are lying.

*Ulla* objects: *ve-achasminhu lehu?* Would this not silence the witnesses? Once their testimony has been accepted, is it appropriate to challenge it so explicitly? *Rashi* explains that we greet even accepted testimony with a note of skepticism.

### Direct vs Indirect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם מִמֵּילָא קָא מִידְּחוּ, הָכָא קָא דָחֵינַן לְהוּ בְּיָדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Are** the *halakhot* **comparable? There,** where Rabbi Shimon ben Eliezer says to bring the witnesses from place to place, the witnesses **are repressed by themselves,** whereas **here, we repress them by** direct **action,** and that the court should not do.

The *Gemara* responds to this concern by distinguishing between two types of deterrence. The practice cited elsewhere — of moving witnesses from room to room before their testimony in capital cases in order to unsettle them and cause them to have second thoughts — achieves its effect *mimila*, indirectly. The witnesses themselves decide to withdraw; no direct pressure is applied. What *Rav Yehudah* proposes, by contrast, is a direct challenge to the witnesses — *kadchinan lehu beyadayim* — and that crosses a line. Once the court begins actively impugning the witnesses through direct confrontation, it has moved from protecting the accused to undermining the judicial process itself.

### Raba's Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר עוּלָּא: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן, ״יֵשׁ לְךָ עֵדִים לַהֲזִימָּם?״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: וְכִי פּוֹתְחִין בִּזְכוּתוֹ שֶׁל זֶה שֶׁהִיא חוֹבָתוֹ שֶׁל זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ulla says: We say this** to the accused: **Do you have witnesses to determine** that the witnesses who testified against you are **conspiring witnesses? Rabba said to him: But do we open** the deliberations **with** a claim to **acquit** the accused **that is** to **the liability of this** one, i.e., the witnesses? This claim can lead to the witnesses incurring liability for their testimony.

*Rava* then proposes a different approach to *potchin l'zechut*: instead of challenging the witnesses, we address the accused and say, *yesh lacha edim lehaziman?* — perhaps you have witnesses who can establish that the testifying witnesses are *edim zomemim*, that is, that they were elsewhere at the time they claim to have witnessed the act, and thus could not have seen what they claimed to see. This appears to open genuinely in the accused's defense without directly impugning the witnesses who have already testified.

### Hazamah Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הָוְיָא חוֹבָתוֹ? וְהָתְנַן: אֵין עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיִּגָּמֵר הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rabba's assumption: **But is** this to **the liability of** the witnesses? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Makkot* 5b): **Conspiring witnesses are not killed** for their testimony **until the verdict** of the one concerning whom they testified **is issued?** Therefore, if they will be shown to be conspiring witnesses at this early stage of the proceedings, they will not be liable.

*Rava*'s proposal is immediately challenged. The difficulty is this: by planting the idea of *hazamah* in the accused's mind before *gemar din*, we may be inadvertently creating a scenario in which the accused waits until after *gemar din* before producing *edim zomemim*. The *halacha* is that *edim zomemim* are not executed until after *gemar din* — *ein edim zomemim neheragim ad sheyigmar hadin*. If the accused produces *edim zomemim* after the verdict is already final, the witnesses will be subject to death, and we will have, through our opening statement, contributed to that outcome. We will have opened in defense of the accused in a way that brings *chovah* — serious liability — upon the witnesses who testified.

### Raba Refined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי אָמֵינָא: אִילּוּ שָׁתֵיק הַאי עַד דְּמִיגְמָר דִּינֵיהּ, וּמַיְיתִי עֵדִים וּמַזֵּים לְהוּ, הָוְיָא לֵיהּ חוֹבָתוֹ שֶׁל זֶה. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ, ״יֵשׁ לְךָ עֵדִים לְהַכְחִישָׁן?״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara restates Rabba's objection: **This** is what **I say: If** the accused **would be silent until his verdict is issued and** then **brings witnesses and** the court **determines them** to be **conspiring** witnesses, it will be found that the statement of the court **is** to **the liability of this** one, i.e., the witnesses. **Rather, Rabba says: We say to** the accused: **Do you have witnesses to contradict them?** If the first witnesses are contradicted as to the facts of the case, no one is liable.

*Rava* refines his proposal in response: the court should say instead, *yesh lacha edim lehach-chishan?* — not do you have witnesses to make the testifiers *edim zomemim*, but simply do you have witnesses who can *contradict* what they said. Contradicting witnesses do not create *hazamah*; *hazamah* requires the specific claim that the testifying witnesses were elsewhere at the time in question. Contradicting witnesses merely offer a competing account of the facts. This achieves the goal of opening in the accused's defense — inviting him to produce exculpatory evidence — without creating any risk of *chovah* for the original witnesses.

### Anchoring Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: ״מִדִּבְרֵיכֶם נִזְדַּכָּה פְּלוֹנִי״. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ, ״אִי לָא קְטַלְתְּ לָא תִּדְחַל״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״כׇּל מִי שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לוֹ זְכוּת יָבֹא וִילַמֵּד עָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** We say to the witnesses: **Based on your statements, so-and-so is acquitted.** The court issues a *pro forma* declaration that it is possible to find a reason to acquit based on the testimony of the witnesses, and then they begin the deliberations. **Abaye and Rava both say: We say to** the accused: For example, **if you did not kill** anyone, **do not fear** the consequences of these proceedings, as you will be acquitted. **Rav Ashi says:** The court announces: **Whoever knows** of a reason **to acquit** the accused **should come and teach** this reason **concerning him.**

*Rav Kahana* takes a different approach, less focused on adducing counter-evidence and more focused on how the court frames its own deliberations. He says: *m'divreichem nizdaka ploni* — after the witnesses testify, the very first statement the court makes is: "Based on everything that has been said, it appears that the accused is innocent." This flies in the face of the testimony just received, but the effect is powerful: by anchoring the deliberation in that direction, the court shapes the subsequent conversation to think in terms of innocence first. The psychological phenomenon of anchoring — the power of an initial framing to condition all subsequent analysis — is operative here. Whoever frames the opening position exercises disproportionate influence over where the discussion goes.

### Reassurance Method

*Abaye* and *Rava* both propose yet another method: *amrinan lei i lo katalt lo tidchal* — we say to the accused, "If you did not kill anyone, do not be afraid." This seems at first glance to be less a legal technique than a pastoral reassurance. But it serves a dual function. First, it puts into the court's own consciousness the genuine possibility that the accused is innocent, and by articulating that possibility directly, the judges begin to internalize it in their deliberations. Second, an accused person who feels presumed guilty may comport himself in ways that inadvertently influence the judges against him; by reassuring him that innocence means no cause for fear, we level the psychological playing field.

### Public Announcement

*Rav Ashi* proposes the most practical approach: *kol mi sheyodeia lo zechut yavo v'yilamed alav* — make a public announcement in the court inviting anyone who possesses exonerating evidence, testimony, or relevant facts to come forward. This directly and concretely opens the possibility of vindication. Even if no one comes forward, the announcement itself orients the deliberations toward the possibility of innocence and signals to all present that the court is actively seeking grounds for acquittal.

### Sotah Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״אִם לֹא שָׁכַב אִישׁ אוֹתָךְ וְאִם לֹא שָׂטִית וְגוֹ׳״ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the explanation **of Abaye and Rava. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The priest administering the *sota* rite to the *sota* says to her: **"If no man has lain with you and if you have not gone astray** to impurity while under your husband, you shall be free from this water of bitterness that causes the curse. But if you have gone astray while under your husband…" (Numbers 5:19–20). The priest first states the scenario in which the woman is innocent of adultery.

A *Baraita* is then cited in support of *Abaye* and *Rava*'s method, drawn surprisingly from the context of *sotah*. The verse states: *im lo shachav ish otach v'im lo satit tumah tachat ishech hinaki mi'mei hamarim hamarerim* — if no man has lain with you, and you have not gone astray under your husband, be cleared by the bitter waters. Only after this statement of innocence does the verse address the possibility of guilt: *v'at ki satit* — but if you did stray, then the consequences follow. From the structure of the *sotah* procedure — itself a remarkable case study in the intersection of judicial and ritual process — the *Gemara* derives the principle that even in a capital-adjacent proceeding, one opens by articulating the possibility of innocence before turning to the possibility of guilt. *Mikan shepotchin b'dinei nefashot techilah l'zechut* — from here we learn that in capital cases, the deliberations must always open with a statement favoring the accused's innocence.
