# Yevamot 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiddushei Ketanah & Yibum

### Scenario Setup

The daf opens at the bottom of 109b, with the Mishnah's discussion of *Rabban Gamliel*'s ruling. The case involves a man who contracted *kiddushei ketanah* — a marriage to a minor girl that was effected not through her father, and therefore binding only *derabbanan* — and whose brother subsequently died, leaving him with an obligation of *yibum derabbanan* toward his sister-in-law. To make the scenario concrete: Reuven is married to Rachel through *kiddushei ketanah* (a *derabbanan* marriage), and his brother dies leaving Leah as the *yevamah*. The complication is that Leah is Rachel's sister — i.e., *achot ishto* — and is therefore an *ervah* who normally cannot be subject to *yibum*. But is this *ervah* relationship truly operative here, given that the marriage to Rachel is only *derabbanan*?

### Rabban Gamliel's View

*Rabbi Eliezer*'s position is that one should teach the minor girl to perform *mi'un* — the minor's right to refuse and exit the marriage — thereby dissolving the *derabbanan* bond and permitting *yibum* with Leah. *Rabban Gamliel* takes a more nuanced stance: if the girl does *mi'un*, well and good; if not, one should simply wait until she matures. Once she becomes an adult, she becomes his wife *de'oraita*, and at that point Leah, as her sister, becomes a genuine *ervah* and may walk away entirely — without *chalitzah*, and without *yibum*.

### The Core Problem

The difficulty with *Rabban Gamliel*'s position is immediate. The obligation of *yibum* begins at the moment the brother dies. At that moment, Rachel is only a *derabbanan* wife, and thus Leah is only a *derabbanan ervah* — not a *de'oraita ervah*. Given that the *mitzvah* of *yibum* had already taken hold when the *ervah* was merely *derabbanan*, how can Leah walk away entirely simply because Rachel later becomes an adult? Perhaps one should argue that since the *zikah* (the *yibum* bond) was already in force when the *ervah* was only rabbinic, the *mitzvah* should persist — and at minimum, *chalitzah* might be required.

### Rav Elazar Asks

*Rabbi Elazar* posed this question directly to *Rav*: what exactly is *Rabban Gamliel*'s reasoning?

### Suspended Kiddushin

The first possibility *Rav* considers is that *Rabban Gamliel* holds *kiddushei ketanah* are *teluyim* — suspended or held in abeyance. The Aramaic phrase used is evocative: *kiddusheha mitla talu*, and *chi gadlah gadli bahadah* — the *kiddushin* "grows up with her." According to this view, when she contracts *kiddushin* as a minor, the act is not simply a *derabbanan* institution with no *de'oraita* dimension. Rather, the *de'oraita* marriage is latent within the original act and automatically comes into force when she reaches adulthood. There is no new act of *kiddushin* required; the original act, held in suspension, simply activates. On this reading, even retroactively, Rachel was in some sense already his wife when the *yibum* obligation began — and therefore Leah was already an *ervah* in some meaningful sense from the outset.

### New Kiddushin Theory

The second possibility is that *Rabban Gamliel* holds an entirely different view: the original *kiddushei ketanah* carry no *de'oraita* weight whatsoever. When Rachel becomes an adult, it is not that the old *kiddushin* activates — rather, a new act of *kiddushin* is created. This raises a separate question within *yibum* law: if a man has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* with a woman and then, without having performed either *yibum* or *chalitzah*, proceeds to marry her sister, mother, or daughter — creating an *ervah* situation after the *zikah* has already begun — does that *ervah* status dissolve the *zikah*? Can one simply generate an *ervah* after the fact and thereby neutralize the *yibum* obligation? *Rabban Gamliel* apparently holds yes: even if the *ervah* is created after the *zikah* began, she walks away — *nifterah yevamah ve-halchah lah*. On this reading, the crucial question then becomes: when does the new *kiddushin* with Rachel take effect? Since there is no ring or document, only *bi'ah* (intercourse) could create a new act of *kiddushin*. Hence the formulation: *ei baal in, ei lo baal lo* — if they have had *bi'ah*, yes; if not, no.

Two distinct analytical threads thus emerge: a *yibum* question about whether an *ervah* created after the *zikah* began dissolves it, and a *ketanah* question about what precisely happens when a minor girl grows up — whether the original *kiddushin* automatically activates, or whether a new act of *kiddushin* is required. The *Gemara* proceeds to focus on the latter.

### Rav Sheshet Objects

*Rav Sheshet* dismisses *Rav*'s framing sharply, suggesting that *Rav* must have been dozing off when he said it — a turn of phrase *Rav Sheshet* apparently favored. His basis is a *baraita* that, in his view, settles the matter clearly.

### Baraita on Teluyim

The *baraita* states: *hamekadesh et ha-ketanah, kiddusheha teluyim* — one who betroths a minor, her *kiddushin* is suspended. *Rav Sheshet* reads this as unambiguous support for the position that once she grows up, the *kiddushin* automatically takes effect — *gadlah gadli bahadah* — without requiring any new act of *bi'ah*.

### Rav Avin's Response

*Rav Avin bar Rav Nachman* responds that *mitalyasa ve-kayma* — "in abeyance and pending" — does not necessarily mean the *kiddushin* will automatically crystallize upon adulthood. Rather, it means the situation remains unresolved: *ei baal in, ei lo baal lo*. The *kiddushin* will take effect only if *bi'ah* occurs.

### Woman's Rights

The reasoning offered is that the woman retains her own set of prerogatives parallel to his. He has advantages she lacks — the power to divorce her unilaterally — but she has advantages he lacks: the right to perform *mi'un* and exit the marriage on her own initiative. As long as she has not had relations with him as an adult, she has not yet relinquished that right, and the *kiddushin* has not yet resolved into something definitive.

### Core Question

The core question thus crystallizes: is *kiddushei ketanah* automatically operative upon her reaching adulthood, or is a new act of *bi'ah* required to effectuate it? This is not merely a technical question about the minor's marriage — it ramifies into major areas of practical *halacha*.

---

## Rav vs. Shmuel/Sheshet

### Rav's Position

*Rav* holds that a new act of *bi'ah* is required. When a *ketanah* grows up, the original *kiddushin* does not automatically become operative *de'oraita*; only if they subsequently have intercourse as adults does the marriage become fully binding at a Torah level. *Rav Sheshet* disagrees: the *kiddushin* activates automatically, without any new act.

### Challenge to Rav

The *Gemara* now challenges *Rav*'s position. A *ketanah* who did not perform *mi'un* grew up, and then accepted *kiddushin* from a second man. What is the ruling?

### Rav Implied

*Rav* rules that she does not need a *get* from the second man — the second *kiddushin* was ineffective. *Shmuel* rules that she does need a *get* from the second man. The implication of *Rav*'s ruling seems to be that when she becomes an adult she is already *mekudeshet* to the first husband — automatically, even without intercourse — which appears to contradict his stated position that *bi'ah* is required.

### Gemara's Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: the case where *Rav* says she does not need a *get* from the second man is specifically the case where she had already had *bi'ah* with the first husband after reaching adulthood. The second man's *kiddushin* failed because she was already fully *mekudeshet* *de'oraita* to the first husband — via that act of *bi'ah*. *Rav*'s position is therefore internally consistent: *bi'ah* is indeed required, and it had occurred in this case.

### Shmuel's Position

But the *Gemara* then presses: if she had *bi'ah* with the first husband as an adult, why does *Shmuel* say she still needs a *get* from the second man? How could the second *kiddushin* possibly be effective? *Shmuel*'s answer is the crucial formulation: *kol ha-bo'el al da'at kiddushin ha-rishonim hu bo'el* — anyone who has intercourse does so within the framework of the original *kiddushin*. According to *Shmuel*, even when the adult woman has intercourse with her husband, that act is not a new, independent *kiddushin*; it is a continuation of the original *kiddushei ketanah*. Since the original *kiddushin* was only *derabbanan*, the subsequent intercourse — framed entirely by that context — never creates a new *de'oraita kiddushin*. Therefore, there is no *de'oraita* marriage, and when the second man gives *kiddushin*, it could potentially take effect.

### Three-Way Debate

The three-way structure of the debate is now clear. *Rav Sheshet* holds that upon reaching adulthood, the *kiddushei ketanah* automatically activates *de'oraita* — no intercourse required. *Rav* holds that intercourse after adulthood creates a new act of *kiddushin de'oraita*, independent of and superseding the original rabbinic framework. *Shmuel* holds that even post-adolescent intercourse does not create a new *kiddushin*, because all intercourse is understood within the context of the original *kiddushin* — and since that original *kiddushin* was *derabbanan*, the couple never achieves a *de'oraita* marital status unless they explicitly reframe the relationship.

This three-way debate is not merely of theoretical interest. It bears directly on the practical reasoning of major twentieth-century *poskim*.

### Rav Moshe Approach

*Rav Moshe Feinstein*'s well-known approach to cases of *agunah* and potential *mamzerut* frequently relied on invalidating the original *kiddushin* — by identifying a *pesul* in the witnesses, for instance — and thereby establishing that no valid marriage ever took place. A standing objection to this approach is that even if the original *kiddushin* were defective, the couple lived together for years and had repeated intercourse: why would each act of intercourse not itself constitute a new *ma'aseh kiddushin*? *Rav Moshe* answered by invoking precisely *Shmuel*'s principle: *kol ha-bo'el al da'at kiddushin ha-rishonim hu bo'el*. The subsequent intercourse was not intended as an independent act of *kiddushin*; it was understood by both parties as occurring within the framework of what they believed was their already-established marriage. Since no one had in mind to create a new *kiddushin*, none was created. The difference from the *ketanah* case is that there the original *kiddushin* was merely *derabbanan*, while in *Rav Moshe*'s cases the original *kiddushin* was attempted *de'oraita* but was *pasul*; the principle, however, operates analogously.

### Henkin's Disagreement

*Rav Yosef Eliyahu Henkin* disagreed with *Rav Moshe*'s application of this principle. His argument was that *kiddushei bi'ah* — betrothal effected through intercourse — does not depend on subjective intent. The objective reality of sustained marital cohabitation, of a man and woman living together as husband and wife and engaging in intercourse within that context, itself creates *kiddushin*, regardless of whether either party consciously intends the act as a new *kinyan*. The *de'oraita* framework, in his view, is activated by the reality of the marital relationship, not merely by articulated intent. On this reading, *Rav Moshe*'s leniency would not hold, since the years of cohabitation would themselves constitute valid *kiddushin*. One can see this disagreement as corresponding, at a theoretical level, to the debate between *Rav* and *Shmuel*: *Rav* might hold that the objective act of *bi'ah* in a marital context creates *kiddushin* even without explicit intent, while *Shmuel* would say the act is always colored by the original framing.

## Tnai Case & Limits

### Kiddushin B'Tnai

The *Gemara* notes that *Rav* and *Shmuel* have already debated this same underlying question in a different context: *kidesh al tnai ve-chanas stam* — a man performed *kiddushin* with an explicit condition (*tnai*), and then the couple began living together without reiterating the condition. They continued to live as husband and wife and to have intercourse, but the *tnai* was never formally addressed again.

### Rav on Tnai

*Rav* rules that a *get* is required. Since the husband has moved forward into married life with her, he has implicitly waived (*achulei achlah*) his original *tnai*. The act of living together and having intercourse overrides the earlier conditional framework and creates a new, unconditional marital reality.

### Shmuel on Tnai

*Shmuel* rules that no *get* is needed. All subsequent intercourse is understood as occurring within the framework of the original *kiddushin* — including its *tnai*. *Kol ha-bo'el al da'at kiddushin ha-rishonim hu bo'el*. Since the *tnai* was never fulfilled or waived explicitly, it remains operative, and the marriage was never fully effectuated.

### Gemara's Reasoning

The *Gemara* then asks: if so, why does the debate need to be stated in both cases — the *ketanah* case and the *tnai* case? Perhaps one could derive the other. The *Gemara* explains that the two cases are not fully parallel. In the *tnai* case, the *tnai* is explicit and the husband is directly confronted with it; when he subsequently has intercourse, one can reasonably infer that he is consciously setting the *tnai* aside. Therefore, *Rav*'s position — that the *bi'ah* overrides the earlier context — is most naturally at home in the *tnai* case.

### Possible Agreement

By contrast, in the *ketanah* case, there is no explicit problem confronting the husband. He married a young girl, they grew up together, and nothing in his lived experience signals that a new act of *kiddushin* is needed. He simply does not perceive a problem. Therefore, *Rav* might in fact agree with *Shmuel* in the *ketanah* case — holding that since the husband has no awareness that a new *de'oraita* framework is called for, the *bi'ah* does not generate one. Conversely, in the *tnai* case, *Shmuel* might agree with *Rav* — since the problem is so explicit that the husband's continued *bi'ah* could be construed as a conscious act of waiver. Both cases need to be stated to prevent either such inference.

## Challenge & Bei Korsaia

### Objection to Rav

The *Gemara* raises a further challenge to *Rav*'s stated position: does *Rav* really hold that the *ketanah*'s *kiddushin* only becomes *de'oraita* upon subsequent *bi'ah*? A story from *Narash* appears to cut the other way.

### Abduction Story

The case: a girl who had been married as a *ketanah* grew up, and was being conveyed in the *bei korsaia* — a special seat or conveyance used to bring the bride to the *chuppah* — when another man appeared and abducted her, apparently accepting her *kiddushin* on the spot. In other words, she had become an adult, had not yet had *bi'ah* with the first husband, and the second man performed an act of *kiddushin* before the *chuppah* took place.

### Rav Huna/Chananel

*Rav Huna* and *Rav Chananel*, both students of *Rav*, were present in *Narash* at the time, and ruled that she does not need a *get* from the second man — the second *kiddushin* was simply ineffective.

### Implication

The apparent implication is that she was already fully *mekudeshet* to the first husband the moment she became an adult — automatically, without *bi'ah* — consistent with *Rav Sheshet*'s position, not *Rav*'s.

### Rav Papa's Explanation

*Rav Papa* resolves the difficulty by noting a local custom: in *Narash*, the couple has *bi'ah* before the ceremonial chariot ride, not after. The *chuppah* and consummation preceded the *bei korsaia* procession. Therefore the ruling of *Rav Huna* and *Rav Chananel* was issued in a case where *bi'ah* with the first husband had already occurred — fully consistent with *Rav*'s position.

### Rav Ashi's Approach

*Rav Ashi* takes a different approach entirely, accepting the case more straightforwardly: the second *kiddushin* was indeed performed before any *bi'ah* with the first husband, and *de'oraita* she was not yet fully married to him. Nevertheless, the second man's *kiddushin* did not take effect — not because the first marriage had activated, but because *hu asa shelo ke-hogen*: he acted in a grossly improper manner by intercepting and abducting the bride on her way to the *chuppah*.

### Hafka'as Kiddushin

Since he acted in violation of basic ethical norms, the Rabbis responded in kind — *anan nami na'avid leh shelo ke-hogen*: we too will deal with him outside the normal rules. They voided (*afkinhu*) his *kiddushin*. The *Gemara* raises the question: what if the second man performed *kiddushei bi'ah* rather than *kiddushei kesef*? If he gave money, one might argue the Rabbis declared it *hefker* and the *kiddushin* collapsed. But if he had intercourse — what can the Rabbis do? The answer: *shavyuh rabbanan le-vi'alaso beilas zenus* — the Rabbis declared his act of intercourse to be *beilas zenus*, devoid of *kiddushin* significance. They refused to recognize it as a marital act at all.

---

## Hafka'as Kiddushin

### Case-Specific Nature

This episode of *hafka'as kiddushin* is striking precisely because it is entirely case-specific. The more familiar instances of *hafka'as kiddushin* operate within pre-defined rabbinic rules: the Rabbis set up a system in advance, and if a person violates the parameters of that system, the *kiddushin* is automatically void — for example, if a husband attempts to annul a *get* in the absence of the agent (*shelo bifnei ha-shali'ah*), the Rabbis simply refused to recognize the transmission as valid. All of this flows from the principle *kol d'mekadesh ada'ata d'rabbanan mekadesh*: every act of *kiddushin* is implicitly performed within and subject to the rabbinic framework, and the Rabbis therefore retain the authority to void it when their rules are violated.

The *bei korsaia* case is different. Here, the man is not working within the rabbinic system at all — he is brazenly circumventing it. Invoking *ada'ata d'rabbanan* to void his *kiddushin* would be difficult, since he clearly had no intention of operating within rabbinic norms.

## Ruling & Implication (II)

### Reiteration

The practical ruling that emerges from the entire preceding discussion: *Rav Yosef* declares that the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Eliezer* — one teaches the *ketanah* to perform *mi'un*. The Amora *Rabbi Elazar* similarly states: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer*, affirming that in the scenario involving sisters, the correct practical course is to have the minor perform *mi'un* rather than wait for her to mature.

## Hafka'as Kiddushin (II)

### Tosafot's Doubt

*Tosafot* raises an explicit question about the mechanism underlying *hafka'as kiddushin* in the *bei korsaia* case. The formulation *lefichach asu lo shelo ke-hogen ve-afki'inhu kiddushin minei* is analyzed: the *Ri* was uncertain whether the voiding of the *kiddushin* is based on the standard principle — *kol ha-mekadesh ada'ata d'rabbanan mekadesh* — or whether it operates on the broader, more dramatic rabbinic power: *yesh koach b'yad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah*. The basis for the doubt is precisely that this case does not carry the standard formula seen elsewhere; the phrase *ada'ata d'rabbanan* is conspicuously absent.

### Distinction

The resolution turns on distinguishing between categories of *hafka'as kiddushin*. Most cases in which the Rabbis void a *kiddushin* involve scenarios that were anticipated and defined in advance within the rabbinic legal architecture. The rules are known at the outset, the parties operate within a defined framework, and when those rules are transgressed the *kiddushin* is dissolved as a function of the system itself — this is the *ada'ata d'rabbanan* logic. The *bei korsaia* case stands apart: here the man is acting entirely outside the system, violating basic ethical norms in the very act of *kiddushin*, not exploiting a loophole within it.

### Two Categories

This yields two categories of *hafka'as kiddushin*: categorical *hafka'as kiddushin*, where the rules are defined in advance and the voiding flows automatically from the system (*ada'ata d'rabbanan*); and ad hoc *hafka'as kiddushin*, where the man acts outside the system entirely, and the Rabbis intervene on a case-specific basis to void what he has done.

### Rabbinical Override

For the ad hoc category, the operative principle is *yesh koach b'yad chachamim la'akor davar min ha-Torah* — the Rabbis possess the authority to override Torah law in specific circumstances. This is a more powerful and more exceptional invocation, not something deployed routinely.

### Application

Applied to the *bei korsaia* story: the second man's act of *kiddushin* was not voided because he violated an anticipated rabbinic rule, but because the Rabbis refused entirely to recognize an act of *kiddushin* performed through abduction and moral outrage. Critically, the improper conduct was the very act of *kiddushin* itself — not some later misconduct. This is an important limiting principle: *hafka'as kiddushin* applied to the act of *kiddushin* as the transgression, not as a response to subsequent bad behavior.

### Conclusion

The *sugyot* thus align clearly: the *bei korsaia* case is the only instance in the *Gemara* in which the language *shelo ke-hogen* appears in the context of *hafka'as kiddushin*, and it is the only instance that does not invoke *kol d'mekadesh ada'ata d'rabbanan mekadesh*. All other cases of *hafka'as kiddushin* operate within the categorical rabbinic framework and rest on the *ada'ata* principle. This one case alone operates outside the system and requires recourse to the Rabbis' broader power to override Torah law.

## Two Wives Rules

### Two Katanot

The Mishnah presents the case: a man was married to two minor orphans — *shnei yetomot ketanot*, understood by *Rashi* here as non-sisters — and died. The *Gemara* affirms that *bi'ah* or *chalitzah* of the surviving brother with one of the *yevamot* releases the other. Since both women entered their marriages as *ketanot*, both occupy the same *derabbanan* status vis-à-vis the deceased husband, and the rules of *yibum* operate symmetrically between them. Acting on one fully exempts the other.

### Two Chereshot

The same principle applies if both wives are deaf-mutes (*chereshot*). A *chereshet* cannot enter a *de'oraita* marriage, but *derabbanan* the Rabbis instituted valid marriage for her. Since both wives are at the same *derabbanan* level, one releasing the other through *bi'ah* or *chalitzah* works here as well.

### Pikachat & Choreshet

The Mishnah then distinguishes a case in which one wife is a *pikachat* — a hearing, fully capable adult — and the other is a *chereshet*. Although both are the deceased's wives, they are not of equivalent standing. The *pikachat*'s marriage was *de'oraita*; the *chereshet*'s was *derabbanan*. They cannot be treated symmetrically.

### Pikachat Releases

*Bi'at ha-pikachat poteret ha-chereshet*: if the brother performs *yibum* or *chalitzah* with the *pikachat*, the *chereshet* is released. Acting on the stronger, *de'oraita* bond discharges the entirety of the obligation.

### D'oraita vs. D'rabbanan

The reverse, however, does not hold: *ein bi'at ha-chereshet poteret ha-pikachat*. Acting at the *derabbanan* level cannot dissolve a *de'oraita zikah*. The *derabbanan* has no traction on the *de'oraita* obligation; performing *yibum* or *chalitzah* with the wife of lesser standing leaves the *pikachat*'s bond fully intact.

### Gedolah & Ketanah

The same asymmetry applies to the case of a *gedolah* and a *ketanah*: the adult's *de'oraita* marriage overrides the minor's *derabbanan* status. Acting on the *gedolah* releases the *ketanah*, but acting on the *ketanah* does not release the *gedolah*.

### Chalitzah Problem

The Mishnah's reference to *chalitzah* as an option in the case of two *chereshot* immediately prompts a difficulty: how can a *chereshet* perform *chalitzah*?

---

## Choreshet & Chalitzah

### Question on Chalitzah

The *Gemara* asks directly: *chereshet bat chalitzah hi*? Is a *chereshet* even eligible to perform *chalitzah*?

### Not Bat Chalitzah

A *chereshet* cannot perform *chalitzah* — the act requires specific capacities, including the recitation of verses, that a deaf-mute cannot fulfill.

### Invalid Chalitzah

A *baraita* confirms: *chalitzah* performed by a deaf man (*cheresh*), or by a *chereshet*, or with a *katan* (minor male), is *pesulah* — invalid. This appears to categorically bar *chalitzah* by a *chereshet*.

### Rav Gidel

*Rav Gidel* resolves the difficulty by reinterpreting the Mishnah's language: when the Mishnah speaks of the two *chereshot* case and mentions *bi'ah* or *chalitzah*, it meant only *bi'ah* — the *chalitzah* clause applies only to the two *ketanot* case, where *chalitzah* is at least theoretically discussable. For *chereshot*, only *bi'ah* is on the table.

### Rava's Position

*Rava* disagrees. He maintains that *chalitzah* is indeed possible in the *chereshot* case, and the key lies in a distinction about the nature of the original marriage.

### Distinction

*Rava* introduces the distinction: *chereshet me'ikara* — a woman who was deaf from birth and entered her marriage as a *chereshet* — versus *pikachat she-nishtareshet* — a woman who was hearing at the time of marriage and later became deaf. These two cases are fundamentally different.

### Always Choreshet

If she was always a *chereshet*, her entire marital relationship exists within the *derabbanan* world. The Rabbis admitted her to marriage as a *chereshet*, and they can correspondingly admit her to *chalitzah* as a *chereshet*: *ki heichi de'al hachi nefik* — just as she entered, so she exits. The same rabbinic framework that enabled the marriage enables the dissolution.

### Became Deaf

If, however, she married as a hearing woman and her marriage was fully *de'oraita*, the subsequent *chalitzah* must also meet *de'oraita* standards. A *chereshet* cannot provide *de'oraita chalitzah* — she cannot recite the required verses. The rabbinic dispensation for her entry into marriage cannot retroactively downgrade the *de'oraita* exit requirement.

---

## Abaye's Challenge

### Abaye Challenges

*Abaye* challenges *Rava*'s distinction. Is it truly the case that a *chereshet me'ikara* — a woman who was always deaf — can perform *chalitzah* on the grounds that her marriage was *derabbanan*?

### Two Brothers Setup

The *Gemara* presents the relevant Mishnah from the upcoming *perek*: two brothers, one hearing and one deaf (*cheresh*), married to two unrelated women, one hearing and one deaf-mute (*chereshet*). The marriages are matched: the hearing brother to the hearing woman, the deaf brother to the deaf woman.

### Deaf Brother Dies

If the deaf brother — married to the *chereshet* — dies, what does the hearing brother do with respect to the *chereshet*? He has a *mitzvah* of *yibum derabbanan* with her. The Mishnah states: *koneis* — he does *yibum* — but *chalitzah* is not an option.

### Question

This is *Abaye*'s question: if the marriage between the deaf brother and the deaf woman was only *derabbanan*, why cannot the hearing brother do *chalitzah*? According to *Rava*'s reasoning, since it was a *derabbanan* marriage, *chalitzah* should also be possible on a *derabbanan* level.

### Attempted Resolution

One might attempt to resolve *Abaye*'s challenge by suggesting that the *chereshet* in this Mishnah was not deaf from birth — perhaps she married the deaf brother while hearing, and later became deaf. If so, the marriage was *de'oraita*, and the inability to perform *chalitzah* is expected.

### Hearing Brother Dies

*Abaye* builds his case further from the Mishnah's second scenario: if the hearing brother — married to the hearing woman — dies, what does the deaf brother do? *Koneis* — he does *yibum* with the hearing *yevamah* — and he cannot give a *get* to divorce his own wife, the *chereshet*. Why? Because his marriage to the hearing *yevamah* through *yibum* creates a *de'oraita* bond (the act of *bi'ah* in a *yibum* context effectuates a *de'oraita* marriage), and he cannot have two wives where one might compromise the other.

### Divorce Logic

But crucially: earlier in the Mishnah it emerged that the deaf brother *can* divorce his *chereshet* wife — *motzi et ishto be-get*. If he can divorce her, it must be that he married her as a deaf man — because a man who was hearing at marriage and subsequently became deaf cannot give a *get* (*nischaresh hu lo yotzi le'olam*). Therefore, the deaf brother must have been deaf from the beginning.

### Conclusion

Since the deaf brother was deaf from the beginning, and he was married to the *chereshet*, the reasonable inference is that she too was deaf from the beginning — *chereshet me'ikara*. Her marriage to the deceased was *derabbanan*. Yet the Mishnah states explicitly that *chalitzah* is not an option — *koneis, ein choletz lo*. This directly refutes *Rava*'s position that a *chereshet me'ikara* can perform *chalitzah*.

---

## Sisters Cases

### Sisters Scenario

A further Mishnah sharpens the difficulty. Two hearing brothers are married to two sisters: one sister is hearing, the other is a *chereshet*.

### Hearing Dies

If the hearing brother — married to the hearing sister — dies, the hearing *yevamah* cannot be taken in *yibum* by the surviving hearing brother because she is his wife's sister — *achot ishto*, a full *de'oraita ervah*.

### Deaf Dies

If the deaf brother — married to the deaf sister — dies, the deaf *yevamah* cannot perform *chalitzah* because she is a *chereshet*.

### The Problem

The surviving hearing brother is thus caught: he cannot do *yibum* with the *chereshet yevamah* because she is his wife's sister — *achot ishto*. He cannot do *chalitzah* with her because she is a *chereshet*. The only thing he can do is divorce his hearing wife — but even that does not help, since after divorcing her, the *chereshet* becomes *achot gerushato*, his divorced wife's sister, which is equally an *ervah*.

### Agunah Result

The *chereshet yevamah* is thus permanently trapped — an *agunah* within the *derabbanan* framework. She cannot be released through *yibum*, cannot be released through *chalitzah*, and has no recourse. This is a genuine and troubling result of the rabbinic institution of marriage for the *chereshet*.

---

## Resolution Attempts

### Divorce Proof

The *Gemara* returns to the proof from the divorce case. Since the deaf brother can divorce his *chereshet* wife, he must have married her as a *cheresh* — because a man who became deaf after a hearing marriage cannot give a *get*.

### Deaf Me'ikara

Therefore, the deaf woman in this Mishnah must have been a *chereshet me'ikara* — deaf from the beginning of her marriage. Her marriage was entirely *derabbanan*.

### Yet Chalitzah Forbidden

And yet — despite the marriage having been entirely *derabbanan* — *chalitzah* is not an option for her. This is the problem for *Rava*'s position.

### Explicit Mishnah

The Mishnah is explicit: *koneis, ein choletz lo* — he does *yibum* with her, he does not perform *chalitzah*. This formulation applies even in the scenario where the marriage was *derabbanan* from the outset.

### Gemara Silent

The *Gemara* does not offer a satisfying resolution to *Abaye*'s challenge. *Ishtik* — silence. The question stands unanswered at this point, and the discussion carries over into the next daf.

### Final Conclusion

What has been established, however, is the following: a *chereshet* cannot perform *chalitzah* — even when her original marriage was contracted as a *chereshet* and was therefore only *derabbanan* in character. *Rava*'s elegant distinction — that *chereshet me'ikara* can perform *chalitzah* because she entered and exits within the same *derabbanan* framework — is challenged by the explicit ruling of the Mishnah, which *Abaye* has demonstrated must refer to a *chereshet me'ikara* case. The question of how to reconcile *Rava*'s reasoning with the Mishnah's ruling will await resolution as the daf continues.
