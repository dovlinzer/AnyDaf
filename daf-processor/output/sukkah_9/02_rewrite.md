# Sukkah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis Shammai vs. Beis Hi…

### Old Sukkah Definition

The ninth daf of Masechta Sukkah opens as a direct continuation of the preceding discussion, which concluded with the concept of *sukkah ganvach* — an acronym encompassing sukkos made by non-Jews (*goyim*), women (*nashim*), and animals (*behemah*). The ruling established there is that such a *sukkah* is *kosher*: no particular intent is required for the *sukkah* as an object of *mitzvah* to be valid. The sole requirement pertaining to the *sechach* is that it must have been placed there for the purpose of shade (*tzel*).

The *mishnah* on daf 9a continues this theme by presenting a dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel regarding what is called a *sukkah yeshanah* — an old *sukkah*. This refers not merely to a *sukkah* that was built for *Sukkos* and left standing, but more precisely to a structure that was built months earlier for some entirely other purpose. Beis Shammai declares such a *sukkah* invalid (*poslin*), while Beis Hillel declares it valid (*machshirin*), consistent with their general position that a *sukkah* need not be constructed with any thought of the holiday in mind.

The *mishnah* then defines what constitutes an "old" *sukkah*: any structure built more than thirty days before *Sukkos* (*kodem lachag shloshim yom*). Rashi explains that within thirty days of the *chag*, a person naturally begins studying the laws of the upcoming holiday, and therefore even an unstated intent may be presumed. Alternatively, one might argue that the timing itself confers the designation: a Jew who constructs a hut within thirty days of *Sukkos* has, by that very fact, built something that is objectively a *sukkas hachag*, regardless of his explicit thoughts at the time.

The *mishnah* closes with a further qualification: *aval im asah l'shem chag* — but if one built the *sukkah* explicitly for the sake of the holiday, *afilu mi-techilas ha-shanah kesherah* — even if it was built from the very beginning of the year, it is valid. "Beginning of the year" here does not mean a mere fifteen days prior; it means even a full year earlier. The operative factor for Beis Shammai, then, is that the *sukkah* must be identifiable as a *sukkas hachag*, a *sukkah* for the *yom tov*. This identity can be established in one of two ways: either through explicit intent at the time of construction, in which case even a year's advance is acceptable, or through the objective circumstance of having been built within thirty days of the holiday, in which case no conscious intent is needed.

Beis Hillel, as we saw in the previous daf, requires none of this. A *sukkah* built by non-Jews, built to shelter animals, or constructed with no connection whatsoever to the holiday remains fully valid for the *mitzvah*, provided only that it was built to provide shade.

It is worth noting what Tosafos adds here from the Yerushalmi. Tosafos on the words *sukkah yeshanah* cites a Yerushalmi that rules: *tzarich l'chadesh bah davar* — even according to Beis Hillel, if one wishes to use an old *sukkah* not built for the purpose of the *chag*, one must do something new. The *chavrayah* in the Yerushalmi say one must add a *tefach* of new *sechach* for the sake of *Sukkos*; Rabbi Yosi says even a minimal addition suffices (*kol she-hu*), though he qualifies that this minimal strip must extend across the entire length or width of the *sukkah*.

The Yerushalmi is thus pulling back from the full permissiveness of Beis Hillel as recorded in the Bavli. The Bavli's position is that a *sukkah* completely lacking any identity as a *yom tov sukkah* is nonetheless valid according to Beis Hillel, with no requirement to add or designate anything. The Yerushalmi introduces the notion that even according to Beis Hillel some token act of designation is appropriate — or perhaps even strictly required. This raises a genuine practical *halachic* question: how much weight do we give this Yerushalmi *l'halacha*? Consider, for example, a traveler who encounters a gazebo with a thatched roof in a park where no Jews reside. In principle, with appropriate application of *gud asik* or similar constructs regarding the walls, such a structure might technically qualify as a *sukkah*. Yet no Jew will be adding new *sechach* to designate it for the holiday. Whether the Yerushalmi's requirement is strictly mandated or merely a preferable practice — given that the Bavli makes no such demand — remains an open question *l'halacha*.

### Beis Shammai's Intent

The *gemara* proceeds to ask: *mai ta'amayhu d'Beis Shammai* — what is the textual basis for Beis Shammai's position that a *sukkah* must be built with the *chag* in mind? The *gemara* cites the verse: *chag ha-sukkos shivat yamim l'Hashem* — the festival of *Sukkos*, seven days, to God. Just as earlier in the *masechta* the word *sukkos* in a verse was read not merely as the name of the holiday but as a statement about the object itself, so too here. Reading the verse as *sukkah l'Hashem* — a *sukkah* for God — and reading *chag ha-sukkos* as requiring a *sukkas hachag*, Beis Shammai derives that the *sukkah* must be constructed as a *sukkah* for the *yom tov*. It cannot be a neutral hut; it must be identifiable as a *sukkas hachag*. *Sukkah asuyah l'shem chag ba'inan* — we require a *sukkah* that is made for the sake of the *chag*.

### Beis Hillel's Position

Beis Hillel, however, requires this verse for an entirely different derivation. The *gemara* states that Beis Hillel uses the verse for what Rav Sheshet taught in the name of Rabbi Akiva: *minayin l'atzei sukkah she'asurin kol shivah* — from where do we know that the wood of a *sukkah* is forbidden for personal benefit throughout all seven days?

The principle invoked here is ordinarily called *huktzah l'mitzvato* — an object designated for a *mitzvah* may not be diverted to another use. One may not, for instance, take one's *esrog* during *Sukkos* and process it into jam, nor may one remove oil designated for the *menorah* for other purposes. The verse *chag ha-sukkos shivat yamim l'Hashem* establishes that the *sukkah* is consecrated to God and therefore forbidden for personal benefit throughout the seven days.

A *baraisa* elaborates on this with the striking formulation of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: *k'shem she-chal shem shamayim al ha-chagigah, kach chal shem shamayim al ha-sukkah* — just as God's name descends upon the *korban chagigah* (which is sanctified to God), so too God's name descends upon the *sukkah*. The reading here focuses not on the word *sukkos* but on the word *chag*: since *chag* is often read by *Chazal* as a reference to the *korban chagigah*, the juxtaposition of *chag* and *sukkos* implies that just as the *chagigah* is sanctified, so too the *sukkah* is sanctified. This is the use Beis Hillel makes of the verse — not to require that the *sukkah* be designated as a *sukkas hachag*, but to establish its sanctified status once it is in use.

There is something striking about this position. Beis Hillel maintains on one hand that a *sukkah* requires no designation whatsoever as a *yom tov sukkah* — any hut, built by anyone, for any purpose, suffices — yet on the other hand holds that once it is actually being used as a *sukkah*, the structure becomes almost *kadosh*, sanctified like a *korban*. One might ask: does this mean that every hut in the world becomes forbidden for benefit on *Sukkos* because in theory it could serve as a *sukkah*? Presumably not. The sanctification takes effect when the structure is actually used as a *sukkas hachag* — when one enters it and uses it for the *mitzvah* — not merely because it is theoretically eligible. Still, the irony is apparent: Beis Hillel, the more lenient voice on the question of designation, articulates an almost more potent sanctification of the *sukkah* once it enters use.

### Verse Interpretation

The *gemara* then asks: does Beis Shammai not also need the verse for the *chagigah* derivation — to establish that the *sukkah* is off-limits for personal benefit? The *gemara* answers: *ein hachi nami* — indeed, Beis Shammai does need it for that purpose as well. The word *chag* tells us the *sukkah* is like a *korban chagigah* and is forbidden for benefit. But then the *gemara* asks: if so, from where does Beis Shammai derive the requirement that the *sukkah* be designated for the *chag*? The answer is that Beis Shammai relies on a second verse: *chag ha-sukkos ta'aseh lecha shivat yamim* — the festival of *Sukkos* you shall make for yourself, seven days. Here again the juxtaposition of *chag* and *sukkah* is significant. Rashi reads the verse in a way that intermingles the terms — *sukkos ta'aseh lecha* for the *chag* — deriving that the *sukkah* one makes must be made for the sake of the *chag*. *Sukka asuyah l'shem chag ba'inan.*

### Chol HaMoed Construction

Beis Hillel, however, reads the second verse differently. The emphasis for Beis Hillel falls not on the juxtaposition of *chag* and *sukkah* as a requirement of designation, but on the phrase *shivat yamim* — seven days. This teaches that one may construct a *sukkah* at any point during the seven days, including *chol ha-moed*. *Ha-hu mibayei lei l'oseh sukkah b'chol ha-moed* — Beis Hillel uses the verse to permit building a *sukkah* even during the intermediate days of the festival.

The *gemara* notes that Beis Shammai, by contrast, holds like Rabbi Eliezer, who maintains that one may not build a *sukkah* on *chol ha-moed*. Rabbi Eliezer's broader position — which we will encounter later in the *masechta* — is that a *sukkah* must truly function as one's home for the entire seven days, requiring two full meals per day throughout the week. A derivative of this is that one cannot build the *sukkah* on *chol ha-moed*, since that would mean one has not dwelt in it for all seven days. Saying that Beis Shammai aligns with Rabbi Eliezer is not a surprising *chiddush*, since Rabbi Eliezer is generally understood to be of the school of Beis Shammai.

What emerges from this analysis is a coherent cluster of positions around Beis Shammai: the *sukkah* must be built before the holiday, it must be designated as a *sukkas hachag*, and it must stand for all seven days. Each requirement points in the same direction — toward a *sukkah* that is intentional, serious, and fully identified as a permanent-for-the-week substitute for the home. Beis Hillel is radically liberal by contrast: any hut, built by anyone, for any reason, erected even mid-festival, fulfills the obligation.

### Chal Shem Shamayim

It is worth pausing on the concept of *chal shem shamayim al ha-sukkah* before proceeding. There is an important difference between this formulation and the standard rabbinic concept of *huktzah l'mitzvato*. The ordinary notion of *huktzah l'mitzvato* is generally understood to be rabbinic in origin, and its operative logic is essentially: do not divert the object away from its designated use for the *mitzvah*. Under that framework, what is forbidden is removing the object from its *mitzvah* function — taking the *esrog* to make jam, for instance, since one still needs it for the *mitzvah* of *lulav* and *esrog*.

The language of *chal shem shamayim*, by contrast, is grounded in *pesukim* and sounds far more absolute — an almost biblical-level sanctification. This has led a major strand of *rishonim* to understand that *sukkah* occupies a distinctive category: not merely the standard rabbinic *huktzah l'mitzvato*, but a heightened, biblical concept unique to *sukkah*, which renders the *sechach* (and perhaps the entire structure) more categorically off-limits.

This raises the question posed by a student: if the walls and *sechach* are forbidden for benefit, how can one sit inside the *sukkah* at all? The answer must be that the forbidden benefit is not the natural use of the *sukkah* as a dwelling — the shade, the shelter, the very purpose for which one sits inside. The prohibition must be defined more narrowly: deriving benefit in a manner that redirects the object away from its function, or benefit that is wholly extraneous to the *sukkah*'s role as a *sukkah*. Whether that is the correct limiting principle remains part of the broader discussion, but the point stands that *chal shem shamayim* cannot mean that sitting under *sechach* is itself forbidden.

There is also a fascinating interpretive dimension to why *sukkah* specifically would carry this stronger formulation. The *sechach*, as has been noted in this *masechta*, evokes the *ananei kavod* — the clouds of glory that accompanied Israel in the wilderness. If the *sechach* represents the divine cloud, the manifestation of God's presence that hovered over Israel, then *chal shem shamayim al ha-sukkah* is not merely a legal formula — it captures something deeply real about what the *sechach* symbolizes. The name of Heaven literally descends upon the *sechach* because the *sechach* represents the very medium through which God's presence rested upon the people.

There remains a further debate among the *rishonim* as to whether this concept of *chal shem shamayim* applies only to the *sechach* or extends to the walls of the *sukkah* as well. Given that the primary vehicle of the *sukkah* as a dwelling — and its primary symbolic resonance — is the *sechach*, there is a strong argument to confine the heightened sanctification to the *sechach* alone.

## Leshmah Principle

### Tzitzis Leshmah

The discussion now turns to a broader question raised by *Abaye*: does Beis Hillel not hold of the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav? What is that ruling?

Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Rav that if one made *tzitzis* from *kotztzim*, *nimim*, or *gardim* — the natural fringe threads, loose uncut stitching, or hanging weave at the edge of a garment — the *tzitzis* is invalid (*pesulah*). If, however, one used *sisin* — ordinary strings intentionally inserted into the garment for the purpose of serving as *tzitzis* — it is valid (*kesherah*). The critical factor is not the material but the intent: the strings must be placed in the garment specifically to serve as *tzitzis*.

When Rav Yehuda later reported this ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel went further: *af min ha-sisin nami pesulah* — even *sisin* is invalid, because *de-ba'inan teviyah lishmah* — we require that the spinning itself be done for the sake of *tzitzis*. This is the practical significance of the notation on commercially produced *tzitzis* strings: *tavui lishmah* — spun for the sake of the *mitzvah*. One may not simply take a ball of ordinary wool and cut strings from it; the material itself must have been processed for the purpose of *tzitzis*.

This produces a dispute between Rav and Shmuel: Rav holds that as long as the strings are placed in the garment for the sake of *tzitzis*, the underlying material need not have been designated beforehand; Shmuel holds that even the spinning must be done *lishmah*. But critically, both agree that some act of designation — at minimum, the insertion of the strings into the garment — must be performed *lishmah*.

### Sukkah Exception

The *gemara* raises the challenge: given this universal principle that objects of *mitzvah* must be made *lishmah*, why does Beis Hillel permit a *sukkah* that was built with no such intent? Should we not require *sukkah asuyah lishmah*?

The *gemara* answers: *shani hatam de-amar kra — gedilim ta'aseh lach* — it is different there, because the verse says "you shall make twisted threads for yourself." The word *lach* — "for yourself" — is read as *l'shem hovcha*, for the sake of your obligation, meaning the *tzitzis* must be made intentionally for the purpose of fulfilling the *mitzvah*. This is a particular *halachic* demand of *tzitzis*, not a universal requirement.

The *gemara* immediately notes that *sukkos* also contains a comparable expression: *chag ha-sukkos ta'aseh lecha* — the festival of *Sukkos* you shall make for yourself. Does *lecha* not equally imply *lishmah*? The *gemara* responds: *hahu mibayei lei l'miutei gezulah* — that *lecha* is needed to exclude a stolen *sukkah*. One cannot fulfill the obligation with a *sukkah* one does not own. The *lecha* teaches ownership, not intent.

The *gemara* anticipates the rejoinder: by *tzitzis* as well, *lach* could be read as requiring ownership rather than intent. To this the *gemara* answers that *tzitzis* has an additional verse — *ve-asu lahem*, they shall make for themselves — which already establishes the ownership requirement (*mishelahem*). Therefore the *lach* in the *tzitzis* verse is freed up to teach the *lishmah* requirement, whereas no such doubling exists by *sukkah*.

### Mitzvah vs. Kedusha

What emerges from this analysis is an important distinction. The general rule, according to Beis Hillel, is that objects used for a *mitzvah* do not require *lishmah*. The *lulav*, the *esrog*, the *shofar* — there is no requirement that they be fashioned with specific intent for the *mitzvah*. The *lishmah* requirement is a specific *halachic* demand that applies in particular contexts by virtue of a special verse.

It is important, however, to distinguish objects of *mitzvah* from objects of *kedusha*. *Tefillin* and a *Sefer Torah* require *lishmah* for the *klaf* and the writing — but these are not merely functional objects used to perform a *mitzvah*; they are objects that actually bear God's names and possess intrinsic sanctity. The *lishmah* requirement for objects of *kedusha* is of a categorically different character. The debate here concerns only objects of *mitzvah* in the functional sense — and there the basic rule, per Beis Hillel, is that *lishmah* is not required.

Beis Shammai's position remains that *sukkah* does require a form of designation — not *lishmah* in the technical sense, but that it be identifiable as a *sukkas hachag*. Whether Beis Shammai would extend a general *lishmah* requirement to other objects of *mitzvah* such as the *shofar* or the *lulav* is not definitively established by the *gemara* here, but at minimum they hold that a *sukkah* must bear the identity of a *yom tov sukkah*.

### Ownership Issues

The *gemara*'s discussion of the *lecha* and *lahem* verses also clarifies the question of ownership more broadly. By *sukkah*, the ruling is that one may use a borrowed *sukkah* and make a *berachah* in it. One visits a friend's *sukkah* for a meal and fulfills the *mitzvah* without requiring formal transfer of ownership. By *tzitzis*, by contrast, there is a real problem with using a borrowed garment's *tzitzis*, which is why a synagogue that provides a *tallis* for congregants typically arranges some form of notional transfer of ownership to the person using it, in order to allow a *berachah* to be recited. This difference flows from the respective *pesukim* and what they require by way of ownership.

One might ask: why does the Torah need to tell us that a stolen *sukkah* is invalid? Is it not already covered by the general principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — a *mitzvah* performed by means of a transgression? This question will be addressed in full when the *masechta* reaches the relevant *sugya* in the third *perek*, in the context of *lulav* and *esrog*.

## Sukkah Under Tree

### Tree Prohibition

The *mishnah* presents its next case: *ha-oseh sukkato tachas ha-ilan* — one who constructs his *sukkah* under a tree. Such a *sukkah* is invalid — *k'ilu asah b'toch ha-bayis* — it is as if he built it inside the house. A tree constitutes *pasul sechach* because it remains attached to the ground. Were its branches to be severed and placed as *sechach*, they would be entirely valid; but while still attached and living, they are *pasul*. The question that now presents itself is: what is the status of valid *sechach* (*kosher s'chach*) positioned underneath *pasul sechach*?

### Sukkah Tachas Sukkah

The *mishnah* continues with an adjacent case: *sukkah al gabei sukkah* — a double-decker *sukkah*, one *sukkah* constructed directly above another, with both composed of valid *sechach*. The ruling is: *ha-elyonah kesherah ve-ha-tachtonah pesulah* — the upper *sukkah* is valid, the lower one is invalid. This seems counterintuitive: if both use *kosher sechach*, why should the lower one be invalid? The *gemara* derives from a verse the principle of *sukkah tachas sukkah* — one may be under only a single *sukkah*, not beneath two layers of *sukkah*. The upper *sukkah* is under only its own roof; the lower one is beneath two layers of *sukkah*, which renders it invalid.

If the *sukkah tachas sukkah* principle applies even when both layers are *kosher sechach*, it applies all the more so when the upper layer is *pasul sechach*, such as a tree. In the case of a tree, not only is there a double-roof problem, but the upper layer is itself invalid.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Exception

Rabbi Yehuda introduces a qualification: *im ein diyurin ba-elyonah, ha-tachtonah kesherah* — if no one is actually dwelling in the upper *sukkah*, the lower one is valid. Rabbi Yehuda accepts the principle of *sukkah tachas sukkah* in theory, but holds that the upper structure only acquires the functional identity of a *sukkah* when people are actually living in it. If the upper layer of *sechach* is not being used as a *sukkah* — even if it is theoretically usable as one — it does not generate the *sukkah tachas sukkah* problem. It is then simply a double layer of *sechach*, and the lower *sukkah* remains valid.

This is a striking position. A structure of very sturdy, tightly woven *sechach* — thick enough to bear human weight above it — can still be considered *kosher sechach* and not a permanent roof, provided it retains enough porosity for rain to penetrate. Tosafos establishes this criterion: what makes a roof permanent is not that it is immovable or structurally solid, but that it does not allow rain through. A *sechach* that is dense enough to support a person standing on it yet still permits rain to pass through remains valid *sechach* — a remarkable conclusion. The principle of *sukkah tachas sukkah*, then, turns not on the structural properties of the upper layer but on whether it is actually functioning as a *sukkah* in the lived sense.

## Rava's Shade Analysis

### Shade-Sunlight Ratio

The *gemara* opens its analysis of the tree case with a ruling from Rava: *lo shanu ela b'ilan she-tzilato meruba mi-chamato* — the *mishnah*'s invalidation of a *sukkah* under a tree applies only when the tree provides more shade than sunlight over the area beneath it. In that case, the tree is functioning as a roof — a second, invalid roof positioned above the valid *sukkah*. Given that *sukkah tachas sukkah* invalidates the lower *sukkah* even when both layers are *kosher*, it certainly applies when the upper layer is *pasul*. But *aval chamato meruba mi-tzilato — kesherah*: if more sunlight comes through than shade, the tree is not functioning as a roof, and the *sukkah* beneath it is valid.

Rava derives this from the *mishnah*'s own language. Rather than simply declaring the *sukkah* under a tree invalid (*pesulah*), the *mishnah* offers the metaphor: *k'ilu asah b'toch ha-bayis* — it is like having built it inside the house. This formulation is not merely rhetorical flourish. The *gemara* reads it as a limiting principle: *ilan dumya de-bayis* — a tree is problematic only when it resembles a house. Just as a house is characterized by its roof providing more shade than sunlight — that is what makes it a genuine covering — a tree functions as an invalidating roof only when it similarly provides more shade than sunlight over the area of the *sukkah*.

### Schach Combination

The *gemara*, however, does not allow Rava's ruling to stand unchallenged. Even if the tree provides only a minority of shade — even if it is not functioning as a full roof — there remains a problem: *ha kamitztaref schach pasul bahadi schach kasher* — the *pasul sechach* of the tree is combining with the *kosher sechach* of the *sukkah*. The problematic branches overhanging the *sukkah*, even if they constitute only a minority of the shade, are still present and interacting with the valid *sechach* below.

This point generates one of the most practically significant debates in the *rishonim* regarding *sukkah* construction. There are two fundamentally different ways to understand what "combining" means here, and they have major implications *l'halacha*.

The first reading — which tends to govern stringent practice — is that any area of *sechach* directly beneath an overhanging branch is itself invalidated. The reasoning would be that the shade in that precise area is being produced, at least in part, by the *pasul sechach* of the tree above. The valid *sechach* below it in that zone is effectively negated. This is why people who build a *sukkah* near a tree must look directly upward: any column of space directly beneath an overhanging branch may lack valid *sechach*, and if enough of the *sukkah*'s area is so affected, the *sukkah* becomes problematic — not only for sitting under those areas, but potentially even for the structural integrity of the *sukkah*'s *sechach* coverage. If the affected area is large enough, it may compromise the wall count of the *sukkah* as well.

The second reading — associated with Rashi and certain other *rishonim* — is considerably more lenient. On this view, the *gemara* does not say that the *pasul sechach* invalidates the *kosher sechach* below it; it says only that the *pasul sechach* is combining with the *kosher sechach*. The only problem, according to this reading, is that one may not use the overhanging branches to supplement the shade of the *sukkah*. If one has sufficient *kosher sechach* on one's own, the overhanging branches are simply irrelevant. Under this reading, as long as one does not need the tree branches to supply additional shade — a non-issue in the era of pre-packaged rolls of *sechach* — a tree with only a minor overhang poses no practical problem for the *sukkah* beneath it.

These are two very different readings of the *gemara*, and the practical consequences are substantial.

### Mixed Schach Solution

The *gemara* proposes a solution to the problem of combining *pasul sechach* with *kosher sechach*: *amar Rav Pappa b'she-chavtan* — Rav Pappa says the case is one where the branches have been beaten down and mixed together with the *kosher sechach*.

The image is of a pliable tree whose overhanging branches have been bent down and intermingled with the valid *sechach* laid on the *sukkah*'s roof. Rather than the *pasul sechach* sitting distinctly above the *kosher sechach*, the two are now genuinely mixed together into a single mass. In such a case, the *halacha* treats the combined unit as a whole and defines its identity by its majority. If the *kosher sechach* constitutes the majority of the mixture, the entire mass is valid.

This is a fascinating *halachic* concept. It is not identical to the classic notion of *bittul* — the nullification of a prohibited substance within a mixture of food. In the food context, if one has identifiable pieces of *treif* mixed with *kosher* food, mere numerical majority does not suffice for *bittul*; the prohibited substance must be truly dissolved into the mixture. Here too, if the *pasul sechach* and the *kosher sechach* were laid side by side in clearly distinct, alternating strips — a metal bar next to a bamboo rod — the *pasul* would not be *batel* even if the *kosher* is the majority. The operative principle is that the mixture must be a genuine, undifferentiated whole. When it is, the entire unit's identity is determined by the character of its majority ingredient.

This concept finds a parallel in other areas of *halacha*: for example, when determining whether a dough is considered bread for purposes of *challah* or *matzah*, when the dough is a true mixture of wheat and another grain, one looks at the overall character of the dough as determined by the majority ingredient.

### Mixed Schach Halacha

The *gemara* asks: if the solution is simply that the branches were mixed together with the *kosher sechach*, what is the *chiddush*? This should be entirely obvious. The *gemara* answers: one might have thought that even when mixed, we should prohibit the *sukkah* as a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic safeguard — lest people assume that mixing is unnecessary and attempt to use *pasul sechach* without mixing it in. The ruling teaches *d'lo gazrinan* — we do not impose such a *gezeirah*. When the mixing has been properly done, the *sukkah* is valid, and we are not concerned that this will lead to laxity.

### Vines and Gourds

The *gemara* then notes that this principle is already taught in another *mishnah*: *hidli aleha et ha-gefen ve-et ha-dala'at ve-et ha-kissom* — if one bent over the *sukkah* a grapevine, a gourd plant, or *kissom* (some other pliable plant), *v'sichech al gaban* — and placed *sechach* on top of them — *pesulah*. The *sukkah* is invalid. However, *im hayah sichuch harbeh mehem* — if the *kosher sechach* was more than the vine and gourd, *o she-katzatztan* — or if one actually severed the plants from their roots — *kesherah*, it is valid. The case of cutting the plants and thereby removing their *pasul* status involves a separate principle that will be addressed later (*ta'aseh v'lo min he-asuy*). The significant *chiddush* here is the first clause: if the *kosher sechach* is the majority and is mixed in with the vine and gourd, the *sukkah* is valid despite the presence of the *pasul sechach*.

This *mishnah* thus already establishes that when *pasul sechach* is genuinely mixed with *kosher sechach* and the *kosher* is the majority, the *sukkah* is valid — and that we do not prohibit the mixture out of concern that one will fail to mix properly.

### Bediavad vs. Lechatila

The *gemara* raises one further refinement. One might have thought that the validity of a mixed *sukkah* applies only *b'diavad* — after the fact, if one has already constructed the *sukkah* in this manner, we will tell him it is valid and he need not dismantle it. But *l'chatchila* — as an initial matter, if one asks what to do — the proper answer should be to cut down the branches or move the *sukkah*. The *gemara* rules *kamashma lan* that even *l'chatchila* one may mix the *pasul sechach* with the *kosher sechach* to resolve the problem. The mixing is a fully acceptable initial solution, not merely a *b'diavad* concession.

### Branch Overhang Impact

The discussion concludes by returning to the fundamental ambiguity in the *gemara*. Rava's ruling that a tree which provides only a minority of shade does not invalidate the *sukkah* is now complicated by the recognition that even minority-shade overhanging branches pose a problem through the combination of *pasul* and *kosher sechach*. The *gemara*'s response — that Rav Pappa's solution of mixing resolves the issue — in turn prompts the question of whether that solution is obvious. And the resolution is that it is a *chiddush* either in terms of avoiding a *gezeirah* or in terms of permitting the practice *l'chatchila*.

What remains genuinely contested — and this is the live *halachic* question — is which of the two interpretations of "combining" governs in practice. Under the reading that overhanging *pasul sechach* invalidates the *kosher sechach* directly below it, a tree with even modest overhang can create zones of invalid *sechach* within one's *sukkah*, with potentially significant consequences for both the obligation to dwell beneath *kosher sechach* and the structural requirements of the *sukkah*'s roof. Under the more lenient reading, as long as one is not relying on the tree branches to supply shade, their presence is simply immaterial. The *gemara* itself is not entirely explicit, and this ambiguity is what drives the divergence in *halachic* practice.
