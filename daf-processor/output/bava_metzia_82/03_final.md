# Bava Metzia 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Creditor & Collateral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְוָהוּ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? דְּתַנְיָא: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן וְאָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – יִשָּׁבַע וְיִטּוֹל מְעוֹתָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One who lent to** another based **on collateral** is **a paid bailee** for the collateral. The Gemara comments: **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who lends to another** based **on collateral and the collateral was lost,** the lender **takes an oath** that he was not negligent in his safeguarding, **and** then **he** may **take his money** that he lent him. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer,** who apparently maintains that the lender took the collateral as proof of the loan, and therefore he is considered an unpaid bailee, who is liable for negligence unless he takes an oath.

### Mishna: Shomer Sachar

The *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* rules that the creditor holding collateral is a *shomer sachar* — a paid watchman. This classification carries meaningful consequences. A *shomer sachar* is exempt only in cases of *ones*, a genuine accident entirely beyond his control. He remains liable for theft and loss (*geneiva va'aveida*). The *mishna*'s baseline, then, is that the creditor-as-collateral-holder bears a heightened responsibility, comparable to someone who receives payment for watching an object.

### R. Yehuda: Money/Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם הִלְוִיתַנִי אֶלָּא עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן? אָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – אָבְדוּ מְעוֹתֶיךָ. אֲבָל הִלְוָהוּ אֶלֶף זוּז בִּשְׁטָר וְהִנִּיחַ לוֹ מַשְׁכּוֹן עֲלֵיהֶם, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל: אָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – אָבְדוּ מְעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Akiva says** that the borrower can **say to him: Didn't you lend to me only** based **on the collateral?** If **the collateral is lost, your money is lost.** In other words, the collateral was taken as security for the debt. **But** if **he lent him one thousand dinars by means of** a promissory **note and** the borrower **left him collateral against** the money, **everyone agrees** that if **the collateral is lost, his money is lost.** In this case it cannot be claimed that the collateral was held as proof of the debt, as there is a document attesting to the debt. Consequently, it was evidently taken as security corresponding to the loan, which means that if the collateral is lost, the lender loses his money.

Rabbi Yehuda dissents from this baseline and offers a distinction. When the loan involved money, the creditor receives nothing from the arrangement. He lent money at risk, earns no interest, and derives no material benefit from holding the collateral. In such a case, Rabbi Yehuda holds the creditor to be only a *shomer chinam* — an unpaid watchman — who is exempt upon swearing he was not negligent. However, if the loan involved fruit, the situation changes. If I lend you fresh apples and will receive fresh apples in return, I benefit because I am spared the deterioration I would have suffered had the fruit sat in my possession. In that case, Rabbi Yehuda agrees that the creditor is a *shomer sachar*. This is an eminently reasonable position. The difficulty lies with the *tanna kamma*, who treats all collateral-holding creditors as *shomrei sachar* regardless of the nature of the loan, and the *gemara* will now attempt to clarify why.

## R. Eliezer v. R. Akiva

### R. Eliezer: Shomer Chinam

The *gemara* raises the question: is our *mishna* consistent with Rabbi Eliezer? A *baraita* is cited: *hamilveh chaveiro al hamashkon* — one who lends to his friend on collateral, and the collateral is lost. Rabbi Eliezer rules *yishava v'yitol me'otav* — the creditor takes an oath that he was not negligent and still collects his money. In other words, Rabbi Eliezer holds the creditor to be no more than a *shomer chinam*, fully exempt upon taking the requisite oath. This squarely contradicts the *tanna kamma* of our *mishna*, who holds the creditor to be a *shomer sachar*.

### R. Akiva: Debt Collection

Rabbi Akiva disputes Rabbi Eliezer sharply. He frames the creditor's argument from the borrower's perspective: *klum hilvisani ela al hamashkon* — you only agreed to lend me the money because I gave you my collateral. If the collateral is lost, the debt is lost with it. *Avad hamashkon avdu me'otecha* — the collateral is gone, and so is your money. Rabbi Akiva's framing is notably different from the *mishna*'s language. He does not speak in terms of a *shomer sachar* receiving payment for his watchmanship. Rather, he is articulating a structural claim: the collateral was placed as a vehicle through which the debt could be collected. If the creditor can no longer return the collateral, it is as though the debt has been satisfied thereby.

### With Shtar (Document)

The *baraita* then presents an agreed-upon case. If a thousand *zuz* were lent *bishtar* — with a written document bearing witnesses — and collateral was additionally placed, then *divrei hakol*, all agree: *avad hamashkon avdu me'otav*, the loss of the collateral extinguishes the debt. Why does the existence of a *shtar* change things? Because the document itself already serves as the memory record of the loan — both parties can never forget that the debt exists and is documented. Since the *shtar* handles the memory function, the only reason to additionally take a physical *mashkon* must be to secure payment from it. Thus even Rabbi Eliezer concedes that in the case of a *shtar*, the collateral was clearly intended as a source of collection, and its loss therefore cancels the debt.

## Rashi: Zichron vs. Collateral

### R. Eliezer: Zichron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַפִּילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – שֶׁמִּשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ, כָּאן – שֶׁמִּשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** it is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the lender **took his collateral at the time of his loan,** and therefore the collateral served as proof of the loan, whereas **there,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the lender **took his collateral** later, **not at the time of his loan,** to enhance his ability to collect payment. In this latter case, the collateral is clearly security for the money, and therefore he is considered a paid bailee.

According to Rashi, the fundamental disagreement between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva turns on why collateral was taken at all. Rabbi Eliezer understands that in an ordinary loan without a *shtar*, the purpose of the *mashkon* is *zichron devarim* — a memory aid. I take your iPhone so that neither of us forgets the transaction. When you come to repay me, seeing the iPhone in my possession reminds both of us that this loan exists. On this reading, the *mashkon* is essentially an object belonging to you that I happen to be holding. Since I receive no benefit from the arrangement, I am a *shomer chinam* and liable only for negligence. This is entirely coherent and, as Rashi reads it, consistent with the case of a *shtar*: once a document exists, the *zichron* function is redundant, so we must assume the collateral was taken for collection purposes.

### R. Akiva: Payment

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds that collateral is always taken for the purpose of collection — *klum hilvisani ela al hamashkon*. You agreed to lend me money precisely because you held my property as a guarantee that you could be made whole. If the collateral is gone, the loan is gone. This is worth emphasizing: Rabbi Akiva is not invoking the framework of *shomer sachar* per se. He is not saying that the creditor receives a benefit and therefore bears the heightened responsibility of a paid watchman. Rather, the logic is structural: the *mashkon* was designated to stand in for the debt, and when it can no longer be returned, it is deemed to have gone toward satisfying that debt.

The *mishna*, which frames the creditor as a *shomer sachar*, employs a categorically different conceptual framework — and this tension will drive the *gemara*'s analysis. Importantly, there is an additional implication of Rabbi Akiva's position. If the collateral was held as a payment vehicle, then even if the *mashkon* is destroyed by an act entirely beyond the creditor's control — struck by lightning — the logic of *avad hamashkon avdu me'otecha* should apply just as forcefully. It is not that the creditor was a bad watchman; it is that the arrangement itself means the collateral's loss erases the debt, regardless of cause.

## Reconcile Mishna

### Timing: At vs. After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי, הִלְוָהוּ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – שֶׁהִלְוָהוּ מָעוֹת, כָּאן – שֶׁהִלְוָהוּ פֵּירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But don't** both **this and that,** the mishna and the *baraita*, **teach** the same phrase: **One who lent to another** based **on collateral,** which indicates that the collateral was given at the time of the loan? **Rather,** the Gemara proposes a different resolution: It is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he lent him money,** whereas **there,** the mishna is referring to a situation **where he lent him produce.** Since produce will spoil, the lender benefits from the deal, as he will receive fresher produce in return. Therefore, he is considered a paid bailee for the collateral.

The *gemara* now attempts to reconcile the *mishna*'s ruling — that the creditor is a *shomer sachar* — with Rabbi Eliezer's position, which treats the creditor as a *shomer chinam*. The proposed resolution is a temporal distinction: *kan she-mishkeno be-sha'at halva'ato, kan she-mishkeno shelo be-sha'at halva'ato* — in one case the collateral was taken at the time of the loan, and in the other it was taken afterward. Rabbi Eliezer, in the *baraita*, is addressing a case where the *mashkon* was taken at the moment the loan was made. There, one might reasonably understand the collateral as serving a *zichron devarim* purpose, since it was part of the original transaction. But when collateral is taken *after* the loan is already established, the implication is entirely different. At that later stage, the creditor is anxious about repayment and takes the *mashkon* specifically to have something to collect from. In that case, even Rabbi Eliezer would agree the collateral functions as a collection vehicle — and therefore, our *mishna*, which holds the creditor to heightened liability, could be addressing collateral taken after the loan was made.

### Reject Timing

The *gemara* rejects this resolution. *Ve-ha idi ve-idi hilveh al ha-mashkon tanya* — both the *mishna* and the *baraita* use the same language, *hilveh al ha-mashkon*, "he lent on the collateral," which implies that the collateral was given at the time of the loan in both cases. There is no linguistic basis to assign one source to an early-collateral scenario and the other to a late-collateral scenario. The proposed timing distinction therefore cannot be sustained.

### Money vs. Fruit

The *gemara* attempts a second reconciliation. Perhaps the *baraita* of Rabbi Eliezer deals with a monetary loan — where the creditor receives no benefit and is thus a *shomer chinam* — while our *mishna* deals with a loan of fruit, where, as Rabbi Yehuda noted, the creditor does receive a benefit. If so, the *mishna*'s ruling that the creditor is a *shomer sachar* could be reconciled with Rabbi Eliezer, since Rabbi Eliezer might agree that a fruit-loan produces benefit sufficient to create *shomer sachar* status.

### Reject Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הִלְוָהוּ מָעוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, הִלְוָהוּ פֵּירוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. מִכְלָל דִּלְתַנָּא קַמָּא לָא שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the mishna **teaches** that **Rabbi Yehuda says: One who lent** another **money** is **an unpaid bailee,** whereas **one who lent** another **produce** is **a paid bailee, by inference** you can conclude that according to **the first *tanna* there is no difference** between one who lends money and one who lends produce. If so, the proposed resolution does not fit the text.

The *gemara* promptly dismantles this reading. *Mi-dekattani seifa Rabbi Yehuda omer* — the *mishna* itself contains Rabbi Yehuda's clause, which explicitly distinguishes between a monetary loan (*shomer chinam*) and a fruit loan (*shomer sachar*). If Rabbi Yehuda felt the need to introduce this distinction as a *chiddush*, it follows necessarily — *mi-khlal de-Tanna Kamma lo shani lei* — that the *tanna kamma* did not make this distinction. The *tanna kamma* holds you are a *shomer sachar* whether the loan was of money or fruit. Therefore, one cannot read the *tanna kamma* as restricting itself to fruit loans.

The *gemara* does attempt one further maneuver: perhaps the entire *mishna* is Rabbi Yehuda's view, and the opening ruling represents not an independent *tanna kamma* but rather Rabbi Yehuda himself, with his subsequent clause as a clarification rather than a dispute. This reading would require us to understand the *mishna* as *chisura me-chasra* — missing words — and reconstruct it as: the creditor is a *shomer sachar* when he lent fruit; but if he lent money, he is a *shomer chinam*, as Rabbi Yehuda teaches. The *gemara* notes, however, that this leaves the *mishna* inconsistent with Rabbi Akiva, and a *stam mishna* is attributed to Rabbi Meir — a student of Rabbi Akiva — making it very difficult to accept that the *mishna* runs contrary to Rabbi Akiva's position.

### Conclusion: Not R. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הִלְוָהוּ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁהִלְוָהוּ פֵּירוֹת, אֲבָל הִלְוָהוּ מָעוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. שֶׁרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הִלְוָהוּ מָעוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, הִלְוָהוּ פֵּירוֹת – שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. אִי הָכִי, קָמָה לַהּ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **The entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** the mishna **is incomplete and this is what it is teaching: One who lent to** another based **on collateral is a paid bailee. In what** case **is this statement said? When he lent him produce. But** if **he lent him money,** he is **an unpaid bailee. As Rabbi Yehuda says: One who lent** another **money** is **an unpaid bailee** with regard to the collateral, whereas **one who lent produce** is **a paid bailee. If that is so,** it turns out that **the mishna is established not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** This is problematic, as most of the Sages of the mishna were Rabbi Akiva's students, and anonymous *mishnayot* are generally presumed to follow his rulings. **Rather, it is clear** that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.**

The *gemara* concludes: *ela michvarta, matnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Eliezer* — the straightforward conclusion is that our *mishna* is simply not in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer. The *mishna* holds the creditor to be a *shomer sachar*; Rabbi Eliezer holds the creditor to be a *shomer chinam*; and no acceptable reading can harmonize the two. This does not mean the *mishna* necessarily follows Rabbi Akiva's exact logic either. As noted, Rabbi Akiva's argument — that loss of the *mashkon* erases the debt — is not the same as saying the creditor is a *shomer sachar* in the technical sense. The *mishna*'s use of the *shomer sachar* framework will require its own explanation.

## Shmuel: Disproportionate

### Disproportionate Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּדְלָא שָׁוֵי מַשְׁכּוֹן שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי, וּבְדִשְׁמוּאֵל קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דְּאוֹזְפֵיהּ אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי לְחַבְרֵיהּ וְאַנַּח לֵיהּ קַתָּא דְמַגְּלָא עִילָּוַיְיהוּ, אֲבַד קַתָּא דְמַגְּלָא – אֲבַדוּ אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer applies only **in** a case **where** the **collateral is not equal** to **the monetary value** of the loan, **and they disagree with regard to** a statement **of Shmuel. As Shmuel says:** With regard to **one who lends one thousand dinars to another and** the borrower **puts before** the lender as collateral **for** the loan **the handle of a sickle,** which is worth only a small fraction of the loan, nevertheless, if **the sickle is lost, the thousand dinars are lost.** The Gemara is suggesting that Rabbi Akiva would agree with this ruling, whereas Rabbi Eliezer would disagree with it.

The *gemara* now explores a different way of understanding the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva. Perhaps their debate is specifically about a case of disproportionate collateral — where the *mashkon* is not worth the full value of the loan. I lend you a thousand dollars and you give me, as collateral, an Apple Watch worth only three hundred dollars. In such a case, the *gemara* suggests, the two opinions may be debating the extent to which disproportionate collateral can be said to represent the loan.

### Shmuel's Position

This leads the *gemara* to cite Shmuel's striking ruling. *Hai man d'ozif alfa zuzai l'chavrei* — someone who lent a thousand *zuz* to his friend, and the borrower left as collateral *kta d'magli* — a sickle handle, a trivially small and cheap object. Shmuel rules: *avad kta d'magli avdu alfa zuzei* — if the sickle handle is lost, the entire thousand *zuz* is lost with it. This is a remarkably extreme position. Shmuel holds that the collateral represents the entire loan, however disproportionate the values. The entire sum is considered secured against even a negligible *mashkon*, and if that *mashkon* is gone, so is the debt. The *gemara* considers whether Rabbi Akiva's position might align with Shmuel — that even disproportionate collateral stands for the whole loan — while Rabbi Eliezer would hold that a disproportionate *mashkon* can serve only as *zichron devarim* and therefore leaves the creditor as a *shomer chinam*, entirely exempt.

### Reject Extreme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בִּדְלָא שָׁוֵי מַשְׁכּוֹן שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לֵית לְהוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, וְהָכָה בִּדְשָׁוֵי שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי, וְקָא מִיפַּלְגִי בִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If** the case is one **where** the **collateral is not equal** to **the amount of money** constituting the loan, **everyone holds** that the *halakha* is **not** in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel. But here** the dispute is referring **to** a situation **where** the collateral does **equal the amount of the money** constituting the loan, **and they disagree with regard to** a statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak.**

The *gemara* rejects this framing. When collateral is disproportionate, *kol alma leis lei di-Shmuel* — everyone would reject Shmuel's position. No one holds that a sickle handle can wipe out a thousand-*zuz* debt. The most that could be argued in such a case is that the *mashkon* goes toward partial payment — but not that it represents the entire sum. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the debate of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva must be situated in a case where the collateral is equal in value to the loan, and the question is what principle governs such a situation.

## R. Yitzchak: Ownership

### Scripture Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״. אִם אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן, צְדָקָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? מִכָּאן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that a creditor acquires collateral** given to him and is considered its owner as long as the item is in his possession? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "You shall return the pledge to him when the sun goes down that he may sleep in his garment, and bless you; **and it shall be a righteousness for you** before the Lord your God" (Exodus 24:13). Rabbi Yitzḥak infers: **If** the creditor **does not acquire the collateral,** then **from where is the righteousness** involved in returning it? In this case, the creditor would not be giving up anything of his own. **From here** it is derived **that a creditor acquires the collateral.**

Having established that the debate concerns proportionate collateral, the *gemara* introduces a ruling of Rabbi Yitzchak that might explain the underlying disagreement. Rabbi Yitzchak asks: *minayin l'baal chov she-koneh mashkon* — what is the scriptural source establishing that a creditor takes actual ownership of the collateral? He derives it from the verse *ul'cha tihyeh tzedakah* — "and it shall be *tzedakah* for you," referring to the practice of returning a day-garment to a poor borrower each morning and a night-garment each evening. The verse describes this act as *tzedakah*. The rabbinic usage of *tzedakah* connotes charity — giving something of one's own to another. If the creditor were merely returning something that always remained the borrower's property, there would be no *tzedakah* in the act; he would simply be handing back what was never his. For the act to qualify as *tzedakah*, it must be that the creditor has taken legal possession of the garment and is therefore giving of his own property when he returns it each day. *Mikan l'baal chov she-koneh mashkon* — from here we learn that the creditor acquires ownership of the collateral.

### Ownership Effect

The significance of Rabbi Yitzchak's principle for our discussion is considerable. If the creditor actually owns the *mashkon*, then the question of what *type* of watchman he is becomes secondary. The watchman framework — *shomer chinam* vs. *shomer sachar* — applies when one is guarding an object that belongs to someone else. But if the creditor owns the collateral, the framework shifts entirely: the object is his, and if it is destroyed, that is his loss. He then bears an obligation to return an equivalent item when the debt is paid — and this creates a form of strict liability (*chiyuv onsin*) that has nothing to do with his status as a watchman.

### Limit: Not at Loan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בְּמִשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ, אֲבָל מִשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ – מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **And** can **you understand** it that way? You can **say that Rabbi Yitzḥak stated** this *halakha* in a case **where** he **took his collateral not at the time of his loan** but at a later stage, in order to collect his debt. **But did** Rabbi Yitzḥak **say** this ruling in a situation where he **took his collateral at the time of his loan?**

The *gemara* challenges whether Rabbi Yitzchak's principle can serve as the basis of the Rabbi Eliezer–Rabbi Akiva dispute. *Eimar de-amar Rabbi Yitzchak bemishkono shelo be-sha'at halva'ato* — Rabbi Yitzchak's principle, that the creditor takes ownership, was stated only for the case where the collateral was taken *after* the loan was already made. As understood by *chazal*, the scriptural passage about returning the garment deals with post-loan collateral — collateral seized by the creditor as a subsequent collection measure. Rabbi Yitzchak never asserted his ownership principle regarding collateral taken *at the time of the loan*, which is precisely the scenario at the center of the Rabbi Eliezer–Rabbi Akiva debate. Accordingly, Rabbi Yitzchak's principle cannot explain what those two *tanna'im* are disputing.

### All Agree?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק. וְהָכָא בְּמִשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ, וּבְשׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵידָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. דְּאִיתְּמַר: שׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵידָה, רַבָּה אָמַר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** if **he took his collateral not at the time of his loan, everyone holds** in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Yitzḥak. And here** it is discussing a case **where he took his collateral at the time of his loan, and** Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva **disagree with regard to** the case of **a bailee of a lost item. As it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed concerning the responsibility of **the bailee for a lost item.** If someone found a lost item and it is subsequently lost or stolen from him, what responsibility does he bear toward the owner? **Rabba said:** This individual is considered to be **like an unpaid bailee. Rav Yosef said:** He is **like a paid bailee.**

The *gemara* acknowledges this limitation and recalibrates. *Bemishkono shelo be-sha'at halva'ato* — when collateral was taken after the loan — *kol alma is lehu de-Rabbi Yitzchak*: everyone may well agree to Rabbi Yitzchak that the creditor takes ownership. That question is settled, or at least not contested between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva. The debate we are trying to explain concerns collateral given at the time of the loan. We are therefore back to the need to explain what, precisely, divides Rabbi Eliezer from Rabbi Akiva in that context — and the *gemara* will now pivot to a new and ultimately more satisfying framework.

## Shomer Aviday

### Aviday Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא, בְּשׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵידָה – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, וְהָכָא בְּמִלְוֶה צָרִיךְ לְמַשְׁכּוֹן קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the opinion **of Rav Yosef is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** There is no question that Rabba's opinion is the subject of a dispute between *tanna'im*, as Rabbi Akiva's opinion cannot be reconciled with his ruling: If one who takes collateral for his loan is considered a paid bailee, the same certainly applies to one who goes out of his way to safeguard a lost item. The Gemara is asking whether there is any way to explain Rav Yosef's ruling in accordance with the opinions of both *tanna'im*, or if he must accept that Rabbi Eliezer disputes his opinion. The Gemara responds: **No,** it is possible **with regard to a bailee for a lost item** that **everyone holds** in accordance with the opinion **of Rav Yosef,** even Rabbi Eliezer. **And here,** the dispute concerns a different issue.

The *gemara* introduces a striking parallel. The same conceptual question that divides Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva regarding a creditor and his *mashkon* is mirrored in a debate about a *shomer aveidah* — someone who finds a lost object and holds it pending its return to the owner. In that case too, one is in possession of another's property without having been formally hired or compensated. What is the status of such a finder? Is he a *shomer chinam*, liable only for negligence, or a *shomer sachar*, liable also for theft and loss?

### Rabba v. Rav Yosef

This is a recorded dispute between Rabbah and Rav Yosef. *Shomer aveidah*: Rabbah says he is like a *shomer chinam*; Rav Yosef says he is like a *shomer sachar*. The question that immediately presents itself is: what *sachar* — what payment or benefit — could the finder of a lost object possibly receive? He was not hired; he receives nothing material.

### Mitzvah Benefit

Rashi explains that the *sachar* is *sechar mitzvah* — but this should not be understood crudely as "earning points in heaven," since *mitzvos lav lehenos nitnu*, the performance of a *mitzvah* is not normally considered *hana'ah*, benefit, in a halachic-monetary sense. Rather, the relevant principle is *osek ba-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* — one who is engaged in performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from other *mitzvos* during that time. If I am busy caring for a found object — bringing it inside, airing it out, tending to it — and a poor person comes to the door asking for bread, I am not obligated to stop and give charity, because I am occupied with the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah*. That exemption from other obligations constitutes a genuine benefit — a form of *hana'ah* — which, according to Rav Yosef, is sufficient to classify the finder as a *shomer sachar*.

### Mitzvah Collateral

This framework now applies directly to the creditor holding a *mashkon*. Lending money — particularly to a poor person — is itself a *mitzvah*. The lender risks his capital, forgoes interest, and extends himself for the borrower's benefit. As a result, the principle of *osek ba-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* applies: while engaged in this act of lending, he is exempt from other *mitzvos* that might otherwise be demanded of him. If a different poor person comes and asks for a charitable gift while this lender is in the midst of making a loan, he may decline. That exemption is the benefit that would make him a *shomer sachar* — a position consistent with Rabbi Akiva's ruling and with the *tanna kamma* of our *mishna*.

## Creditor's Need

### Using Collateral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: מִצְוָה קָא עָבֵיד שֶׁהִלְוָהוּ, וְהָוֵי שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו מִצְוָה קָא עָבֵיד, שֶׁלַּהֲנָאָתוֹ מִתְכַּוֵּין, וְהָוֵי שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds that he is performing a mitzva** in **that he lent to him, and** therefore **he is** considered **a paid bailee. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds** that **he is not performing a mitzva, as his intention is** to lend **for his own benefit. And** consequently **he is** considered **an unpaid bailee** for the collateral.

The *gemara* now arrives at what it presents as the actual crux of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva. *Vehacha be-malveh tzarich le-mashkon kamifligi* — the debate is specifically about a case where the creditor has a personal need for the *mashkon*. Rashi illustrates: suppose you come to borrow money during harvest season, and you happen to possess a sickle. I, the lender, have no sickle and could really use one. So I agree to lend you the money, with the understanding that you will leave your sickle as collateral. I now have the use of the sickle throughout the harvest season. The loan enables me to obtain something I need. The question is whether this self-interested dimension of the transaction undermines the *mitzvah* character of the loan.

### R. Akiva: Still Mitzvah

Rabbi Akiva holds that even when the creditor benefits materially from holding the collateral, the *mitzvah* of lending remains intact. *Mar savar mitzvah ka'avid she-hilve'o* — he maintains that one is performing a *mitzvah* by lending to him. The principle of *mitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* applies: even when motivation is mixed, the act is still a *mitzvah*. Accordingly, *osek ba-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* still operates, the creditor retains the benefit of exemption from other obligations, and he therefore qualifies as a *shomer sachar*.

### R. Eliezer: No Mitzvah

Rabbi Eliezer holds the opposite. When the creditor's motivation is self-interest — *le-hana'ato mitkavein*, he intends the loan for his own benefit — this undermines the characterization of the act as a *mitzvah*. He is not performing the *mitzvah* of lending; he is effectively using the borrower as a vehicle to obtain something he wants. Since the act loses its *mitzvah* character, *osek ba-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* no longer applies. The creditor receives no *hana'ah* of the relevant sort, and he therefore falls to the status of *shomer chinam*. This is a pointed position: it suggests that ulterior intent, even when accompanying a technically valid act, can strip that act of its *mitzvah* standing for purposes of halachic consequence — a conceptually rich and somewhat counterintuitive result.

With this, the *gemara* has finally identified a framework that explains the dispute in terms consistent with the *mishna*'s language of *shomer sachar* versus *shomer chinam*. The earlier attempts — whether framing the debate around the *mashkon* as payment for the debt, around Shmuel's position on disproportionate collateral, or around Rabbi Yitzchak's ownership principle — were each rejected or shown to be inapplicable to the case at hand. The *shomer aveidah* parallel, and the underlying question of whether *osek ba-mitzvah* status is compromised by self-interest, provides the conceptual ground on which Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva actually stand.

## Moving Barrel Case

### Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּעֲבִיר חָבִית מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם וּשְׁבָרָהּ, בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – יִשָּׁבַע. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: זֶה וְזֶה יִשָּׁבַע, וְתָמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יְכוֹלִין זֶה וָזֶה לִישָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was transporting a barrel from one place to another and he broke it, whether** he was **an unpaid bailee or a paid bailee,** if **he takes an oath** that he was not negligent he is exempt from payment. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Both **this** one, an unpaid bailee, **and that** one, a paid bailee, must **take an oath** to exempt themselves from payment, **but I wonder whether** both **this** one **and that** one **can take an oath.** In other words, this is the *halakha* that I heard from my teachers, but I do not understand their ruling.

A new *mishna* opens, continuing the general theme of custodians and their liability for objects in their care. The case is this: *hamavir chavit mimakom le-makom* — someone who is moving a wine barrel from one place to another — *ve-shavra* — and breaks it. To be precise, the *mishna* is not describing a hired mover in the simple sense, but rather a *shomer* — a watchman — who was entrusted with the barrel and who, as part of his custodial duties, had occasion to move it. He had license to do so. While in the act of moving it, the barrel broke — perhaps it slipped from his hands, perhaps he tripped.

### Tanna Kamma

The *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* rules: *bein shomer chinam bein shomer sachar yishava* — whether he is an unpaid watchman or a paid watchman, he takes an oath and is exempt. The *gemara* identifies the *tanna kamma* as Rabbi Meir, consistent with the principle that an unattributed *mishna* reflects Rabbi Meir's view. This ruling is surprising. One could understand why a *shomer chinam* would be exempt after taking an oath — swearing he was not negligent is precisely the mechanism that exonerates an unpaid watchman. But a *shomer sachar* is exempt only from genuine *ones*, an unforeseeable accident. That a barrel slips or that someone trips while carrying it seems to fall well within the range of mishaps a paid watchman is expected to prevent. Yet Rabbi Meir allows even the *shomer sachar* to take an oath and walk away. Implicitly, his position must be that this type of accidental breakage is classified as *ones* — a genuine accident — and therefore falls within the *shomer sachar*'s zone of exemption.

### R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּעֲבִיר חָבִית לַחֲבֵירוֹ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם וּשְׁבָרָהּ, בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – יִשָּׁבַע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – יִשָּׁבַע, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר – יְשַׁלֵּם. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: זֶה וְזֶה יִשָּׁבַע, וְתָמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יְכוֹלִין זֶה וָזֶה לִישָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who was transporting a barrel for another** person **from one place to another and he broke it, whether** he was **an unpaid bailee or a paid bailee,** if **he takes an oath** that he was not negligent, he is exempt from payment. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: An unpaid bailee takes an oath** and does not pay, but **a paid bailee pays. Rabbi Eliezer says: This** one **and that** one **take an oath, and I wonder whether** both **this one and that one can take an oath.**

Rabbi Yehuda states the position that strikes most readers as most logical: *shomer chinam yishava, nosei sachar yeshalem* — the unpaid watchman takes an oath and is exempt; the paid watchman pays. Rabbi Yehuda's implicit reasoning is that tripping or dropping a barrel is neither gross negligence nor a true *ones*. It sits in the middle: not so culpable as to make even a *shomer chinam* liable, but not so unforeseeable as to excuse a *shomer sachar*. Each watchman is therefore governed by his own framework in the expected way.

### R. Eliezer Astounded

Rabbi Eliezer in the *mishna* stakes out a different position. He says: *zeh va-zeh yishava* — I also hold the tradition that both take an oath. *Ve-tmiani im yacholin zeh va-zeh lishava* — and I am astounded that both of them can do so. His amazement seems directed primarily at the *shomer sachar*: how can one who broke the barrel by tripping or dropping it simply swear and walk away? This does not look like *ones* at all. But the phrase *zeh va-zeh* — "this one and that one" — suggests his skepticism extends even to the *shomer chinam*: perhaps even an unpaid watchman should be considered negligent in such a scenario, in which case his oath would be unavailing. Rabbi Eliezer thus expresses that he has received the tradition of Rabbi Meir, but finds it deeply difficult to accept.

## Negligence Debate

### R. Meir ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר נִתְקַל לָאו פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא? וְהָתַנְיָא: נִשְׁבְּרָה כַּדּוֹ וְלֹא סִילְּקָהּ נָפְלָה גְּמַלּוֹ וְלֹא הֶעֱמִידָהּ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב בְּהֶיזֵּיקָן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: פָּטוּר מִדִּינֵי אָדָם וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. וְקַיְימָא לַן דִּבְנִתְקַל פּוֹשֵׁעַ פְּלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes these opinions. Is that **to say** that **Rabbi Meir holds** that **one who stumbles is not** considered **negligent,** but the victim of an accident? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If **one's pitcher broke** in a public place **and he did not remove it** from there, or if **his camel fell and he did not stand it up, Rabbi Meir renders him liable for the damage they** caused? **And the Rabbis say** that he is **exempt according to human laws but** is **liable according to the laws of Heaven.** Although the court cannot impose liability, nevertheless he is morally culpable. **And we maintain that they disagree with regard to** the question of whether **one who stumbles is negligent.** This indicates that Rabbi Meir holds that one who stumbles is considered negligent.

The *gemara* opens the analysis of this *mishna* by confirming what was already stated in the *baraita*: the *tanna kamma* is Rabbi Meir. A *stam mishna* is Rabbi Meir. The *gemara* now turns to interrogate his position.

### Contradiction

If Rabbi Meir holds that the *shomer* who trips and breaks the barrel is exempt — indeed, that the breakage is tantamount to *ones* — this seems to conflict with a well-known position of Rabbi Meir elsewhere. In a separate *baraita*, the *gemara* cites: one who is walking in the public domain and his pitcher falls and breaks, and he does not remove the shards — *nishbera kado ve-lo silko*; or whose camel falls and he does not stand it back up — *nafla gmalo ve-lo he'emido* — and another person is subsequently injured by these hazards: *Rabbi Meir mechayeiv benizkan* — Rabbi Meir holds the original person liable for damages. The *chachamim* disagree: they exempt him from human courts, holding him liable only in the heavenly court, because they do not regard the failure to clear the hazard quickly as actionable negligence. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, treats the person as fully negligent.

The *gemara* notes the halachic conclusion reached in Bava Kamma from this passage: *de-biniskal poshea pligi* — the debate turns on whether someone who trips — or whose object slips — is considered negligent. Rabbi Meir in Bava Kamma holds that *niskal poshea* — one who trips is negligent. How then can Rabbi Meir in our *mishna* treat the watchman who trips and breaks the barrel as an *ones*? The two positions of Rabbi Meir appear directly contradictory.

### Two R. Meirs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תִּבְרַהּ, מִי שֶׁשָּׁנָה זוֹ לֹא שָׁנָה זוֹ. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְמֵימַר: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – יִשָּׁבַע, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר – יְשַׁלֵּם. הַאי כִּי דִינֵיהּ וְהַאי כִּי דִינֵיהּ. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמֵימַר: אִין, גְּמָרָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. וּמִיהוּ תָּמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יְכוֹלִין זֶה וָזֶה לִישָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: Break** the mishna, as he **who taught this did not teach that,** i.e., there are two traditions with regard to Rabbi Meir's opinion. **And Rabbi Yehuda came to say** a different ruling: **An unpaid bailee takes an oath** and does not pay while **a paid bailee pays, this one in accordance with his law and that one in accordance with his law,** as an unpaid bailee is exempt from liability for theft and loss, while a paid bailee is liable in those cases and exempt only in cases of circumstances beyond his control. **And Rabbi Eliezer came to say: Yes,** there is **a tradition** that **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** as I learned from my teachers, **but I wonder whether** both **this** one **and that** one **can take an oath.**

Rav Elazar resolves this with a blunt declaration: *tavrah* — break it in half. *Mi she-shana zo lo shana zo* — whoever taught this one did not teach that one. There are, in effect, two distinct traditions attributed to Rabbi Meir that cannot be harmonized. One version of Rabbi Meir holds that tripping constitutes *ones*, making even the *shomer sachar* exempt upon oath; the other holds that tripping constitutes negligence, rendering the person fully liable for resulting damages. These two positions represent extremes, and Rav Elazar concludes that they derive from two different textual sources that happen to share the same attribution.

### R. Yehuda Reason

Rabbi Yehuda's position, by contrast, is coherent on its own terms and requires no such division. He holds that tripping is neither pure *ones* nor full *peshiah* — negligence. It occupies an intermediate status. This means that a *shomer chinam*, whose liability threshold is *peshiah*, is properly exonerated by oath. But a *shomer sachar*, whose threshold is *ones*, must pay, because what occurred was not a true *ones*. Rabbi Yehuda applies the graduated *shomer* categories exactly as they are designed to function.

### R. Eliezer Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם מִשְׁתְּבַע דְּלָא פְּשַׁע בַּהּ: אֶלָּא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר אַמַּאי מִשְׁתְּבַע? כִּי לָא פְּשַׁע נָמֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי בָּעֵי. וַאֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם נָמֵי, הָתִינַח בִּמְקוֹם מִדְרוֹן. שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם מִדְרוֹן, מִי מָצֵי מִשְׁתְּבַע דְּלָא פְּשַׁע בַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, an unpaid bailee takes an oath that he was not negligent with regard to** the barrel, as required by Torah law, **but why does a paid bailee take an oath?** Even **if he was not negligent he is still required to pay,** as he is obligated to pay for theft and loss. **And even** with regard to **an unpaid bailee,** this **works out well** if the barrel broke **on an inclined plane [*midron*],** as the accident occurred due to the difficulty of transporting it, but if it broke **not on an inclined plane** but under different circumstances, **how can he take an oath** that **he was not negligent** with it? Evidently his negligence caused the accident.

Rabbi Eliezer's position, as expressed in both *mishna* and *baraita*, is to hold the tradition of Rabbi Meir — *zeh va-zeh yishava* — while openly questioning its logic. *Bishlama shomer chinam mishtava d'lo pasha bah* — I can understand how a *shomer chinam* takes an oath of non-negligence. *Ela shomer sachar amai mishtava* — but why should a *shomer sachar* be permitted to swear and be exempt? *Ki lo pasha nami shalumei ba'ei* — even if he was not negligent, he should still be liable, since the event was not an *ones*. And then Rabbi Eliezer extends his skepticism further: *ve-afilu shomer chinam nami* — even a *shomer chinam* gives me pause. *Ha-tenach be-makom midron* — if the watchman was descending an incline, one might understand how the barrel broke without negligence. But *shelo be-makom midron mi matzi mishtava d'lo pasha* — if he was walking on level ground, can he truly swear he was not at fault? Perhaps *niskal poshea* — a person who trips on flat ground is negligent by definition.

Rabbi Eliezer's posture is thus one of principled unease: he transmits the tradition faithfully, but refuses to endorse its logic. He is troubled that Rabbi Meir's position — that both the *shomer chinam* and the *shomer sachar* can exonerate themselves by oath — runs against the straightforward analysis of what tripping and dropping a barrel actually represent. The *gemara* indicates that the resolution of this difficulty — why Rabbi Meir would treat accidental breakage during transport as *ones* — will emerge in the following *daf*.
