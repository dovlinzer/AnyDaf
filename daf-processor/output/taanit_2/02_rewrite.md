# Taanit 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Taanit Theme

### Masechet Overview

With *Masechet Taanit* we begin a sequence of smaller tractates — *Taanit*, *Megillah*, *Moed Katan*, and *Chagigah* — bundled together in this single volume of *Seder Moed*. As its title indicates, the tractate focuses on fasting. When we first think of fasting, our minds go naturally to what might be called the historical fasts: the fixed days commemorating tragedies of the past, already reflected in the *nevi'im* when the returning exiles ask whether they should continue to observe *Tsom Ha-chamishi*, *Tsom Ha-shivi'i*, and *Tsom Ha-asiri*. The *navi* responds that with the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash*, those fast days will be transformed into days of rejoicing. We touched on some of this in *Masechet Rosh Hashanah* during the discussion of *Megillat Taanit*. But the historical fasts are very much a secondary concern in this tractate. The primary subject is fasting — and, more broadly, religious response — to tragedy or impending tragedy, specifically centered on the absence of rain.

### Rain & Tragedy

This focus becomes clearer when one recalls the *tefilla* of *Shlomo* at the dedication of the *Beit HaMikdash* (*Melachim Aleph*, Perek 8). There *Shlomo* describes the central reasons people will turn to God in prayer: *beheatzer hashamayim velo yihye matar* — when the heavens are shut and there is no rain; *raav ki yihye baaretz*, *dever*, *shidafon*, *yerakon*, *arbe*, *chasil* — famine, pestilence, blight, locust, infestation. The consistent refrain is *veata tishma hashamayim vsalachta*: You will hear from the heavens and forgive. In an agricultural society, the most recurring source of existential precariousness — the thing that makes your life feel uncertain and drives you toward God — is what is happening with the crops, and above all whether it will rain.

In this light, *Masechet Taanit* can be seen in some ways as a counterpart to *Masechet Berachot*. *Masechet Berachot* concerns fixed liturgy: the *Shema*, the *Shemoneh Esrei*, the regular rhythms of prayer that respond to the natural cadences of day and night. To the extent that *Berachot* addresses more immediate religious responses — to sudden events, to changes in circumstance — it does so at the very end, in the *birchot hashevach*: seeing lightning, seeing trees blossom, hearing good news and saying *she-he-che-yanu*, hearing bad news and saying *Baruch Dayan HaEmet*. *Masechet Taanit*, by contrast, is fundamentally about religious engagement with the world — seeing God's presence, or feeling God's absence, in the circumstances of one's life, and knowing how to respond.

### Religious Engagement

This tractate is therefore less about ritual structure and more about that thoroughgoing religious responsiveness: calling out to God when things go wrong, praying for greater divine presence in the world, seeking rain as a expression of one's entire relationship with the Creator. More than almost any other tractate, *Taanit* deals with what it means to inhabit a religious mindset attuned to circumstances — to see the world through the lens of God's involvement, whether manifest or withheld.

### Q: Why Fasting?

This raises a pointed question: if the tractate is ultimately about religious response to tragedy and the absence of rain, why is *taanit* — fasting — the dominant theme? After all, one might imagine several religious responses to calamity: prayer, *teshuva*, acts of charity. Yet the tractate is named for the fast. And that framing shapes our entire perception of what the religious response to difficulty looks like. This is a question worth carrying throughout the tractate rather than answering prematurely.

### Sin & Forgiveness

A crucial observation emerges from the refrain in *Shlomo*'s prayer: *vsalachta* — and You shall forgive. Tragedy, in the biblical framework, is conceptualized as punishment for human failing. The response to calamity therefore involves not merely petition but the acknowledgment of sin and the need for forgiveness. This connects the fast to *teshuva* and, by extension, to *Yom Kippur*: fasting as a form of self-affliction that signals repentance, perhaps even a kind of self-imposed suffering that stands in place of the punishment deserved. The connection between agricultural productivity and *mitzvot* observance — already anticipated by the *Shema* itself — undergirds this entire framework, as will become clear on the very first *daf*.

## Mishna: Gevurot Geshamim

### Meamatai Opening

The first *mishna* opens with *me'ematai mazkirim gevurot geshamim* — from when do we begin mentioning the *gevurot geshamim*, the power of the rain? The specific insertion in question is *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem*, the phrase we add to the second *bracha* of *Shemoneh Esrei*. Notably, no tragedy has yet entered the picture. This first *mishna* describes a normal year, a year where everything is expected to proceed in its proper course. No anxiety, no drought, no impending calamity — simply the question of when, in the course of the liturgical calendar, one begins acknowledging God as the one who brings rain.

### R. Eliezer v. R. Yehoshua

*Rabbi Eliezer* says one begins on *yom tov harishon shel chag* — the first day of the *chag*, meaning *Sukkot*, since *chag* without qualification refers to *Sukkot*. *Rabbi Yehoshua* says only from *yom tov ha'acharon shel chag* — the last day of the *chag*. This likely means after *Sukkot* has concluded, beginning on *Shemini Atzeret*, though the precise moment will be clarified later.

### Rain as Curse

*Rabbi Yehoshua* objects to *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position: *ho'il ve'ein hageshamim ela siman kelala bachag* — rain on *Sukkot* is nothing but a sign of curse. The source for this is the well-known *mishna* in *Sukkah* comparing rain on *Sukkot* to a master's servant who comes to pour water, only to have the master throw the jug back at him. We are trying to dwell in the *sukkah* and perform the *mitzvah*, and rain drives us out — it is therefore a sign of divine displeasure. Given this, why would one begin reciting *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* during *Sukkot*, when rain itself is unwanted?

### Hazkarah v. Sheolah

*Rabbi Eliezer* responds: *af ani lo amarti lish'ol ela lehazkir* — I never said to begin *asking* for rain; I said only to begin *mentioning* it. The distinction is between *hazkarah* — acknowledging God's power to bring rain — and *she'ela* — the actual petition that it rain now. *Mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* is a form of praise, not a request. One is not petitioning God for rain during *Sukkot*; one is simply acknowledging, within the *bracha* of *gevurot*, that God is the master of wind and rain.

### Ein Shoalin

The *mishna* briefly introduces a further distinction: *ein sho'alin et hageshamim ela samuch lagashamim* — one does not actually request rain (*veten tal umatar*) until close to the time when rain is genuinely expected. This insertion appears somewhat out of place in the flow of the *mishna*, but its purpose is clarificatory: since the debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* turned on whether *hazkarah* implies *she'ela*, the *mishna* makes clear that the petition itself — *veten tal umatar* — is an entirely separate matter that occurs even later. The *mishna* will expand on this in a subsequent passage; it is mentioned here only to sharpen the terms of the current debate.

### R. Yehuda Ruling

*Rabbi Yehuda* then articulates a precise structural ruling: *ha'over lifnei hateiva beyom tov ha'acharon shel chag* — the *shliach tzibbur* on the last day of *chag* (i.e., *Shemini Atzeret*) — *ha'acharon mazkir, harishon eino mazkir*: the *ba'al musaf* recites *mashiv haruach*, while the *ba'al shacharit* does not. Conversely, on *yom tov harishon shel Pesach*, *harishon mazkir, ha'acharon eino mazkir*: the *ba'al shacharit* still recites it, but the *ba'al musaf* stops. This is precisely our practice today: *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* is said from *musaf* of *Shemini Atzeret* through *shacharit* of the first day of *Pesach*.

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is subtly different from *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s. *Rabbi Yehoshua* had said one begins after the last day of *Sukkot*, which might mean the night of *Shemini Atzeret* or *shacharit* of *Shemini Atzeret*. *Rabbi Yehuda* imposes a much cleaner symmetry: the period runs precisely from *musaf* of *Shemini Atzeret* to — but not including — *musaf* of *Pesach*. Why *musaf* specifically will be addressed in the *Gemara*.

## Gevurot Geshamim Sources

### Textual Grounding

The *Gemara* opens with a structural question mirroring the famous opening of *Masechet Berachot*: *Tanna heicha kai diktani me'eimatai?* — from what frame of reference is the *Tanna* asking *when* we begin the *hazkarah*? The question presupposes that we already know we are obligated to recite it in the first place. Where is that obligation established?

The *Gemara* answers that the *Tanna* is departing from the *mishna* in *Berachot* which lists various insertions made in *Shemoneh Esrei* — among them, *mazkirin gevurot geshamim bitchiyat hameitim* (the mention of rain is in the second *bracha*, that of *techiyat hameitim*) and *shoalin bevirkat hashanim* (the request for rain is in the ninth *bracha*, that of the years). But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: if so, why not address the timing there, in *Berachot*, rather than waiting until now? The *Gemara* responds: *Tanna me'Rosh Hashanah salik* — the *Tanna* is departing from *Masechet Rosh Hashanah*, which immediately preceded this tractate. There we taught *uvechag nidonim al hamayim* — on *Sukkot* we are judged regarding water. Having just taught that *Sukkot* is the time of divine judgment over water, it is natural to proceed directly to: from when do we begin mentioning rain in prayer?

This leads to a further question: if the frame of reference is *Rosh Hashanah* and the *mishna* of *nidonim al hamayim*, why not simply say *me'ematai mazkirin al hageshamim* — from when do we mention the rain? What is the significance of the word *gevurot*? *Mai gevurot?* What do the "powers" of rain mean?

It is worth noting an interesting bibliographic aside here. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* explain the reference to *Rosh Hashanah* by saying it is in the same *seder* — *Seder Moed* — as opposed to *Berachot*, which is in *Seder Zeraim*. They do not emphasize the more obvious point, which is that *Rosh Hashanah* immediately preceded *Taanit* in the *Daf Yomi* cycle. This raises a genuine historical question about whether the ordering of *Rosh Hashanah, Yoma, Sukkah* — reorganized to follow the chronological sequence of the holidays — was already in place during the time of the *rishonim*, or whether the original order of the *mishna* was different. It is a bibliographic curiosity worth noting, though not one that can be resolved here.

### Pessukim Chain

*Rabbi Yochanan* answers: *mipnei she'yordin bigvurah* — rain descends through God's *gevurah*, God's might. He derives this through a chain of *pessukim*. The verse in *Iyov* describes God as *oseh gedolot ve'ein cheker* — doing great things beyond comprehension — and immediately follows with *hanoten matar al pnei aretz*, giving rain upon the face of the earth. The connection between rain and *ein cheker* (beyond comprehension) is thereby established. Then *Rabba bar Shila* links this via a *gezerah shavah* on the word *cheker* to *Yeshayahu*, which in the context of creation describes God as *lo yi'af velo yiga ein cheker litvunato* — unfathomable in wisdom. That same context then connects to a verse in *Tehillim* that, speaking of *briat ha'olam*, says *machin harim bekocho ne'ezar bigvura* — God establishes the mountains with His strength, girded in *gevurah*. The chain thus runs: rain is linked through *ein cheker* to creation, and creation is linked through its context to *gevurah*.

### Gevurot & Creation

It is admittedly a convoluted derivation. But the underlying point is theologically significant. By linking *geshamim* to *gevurah* through the context of *maaseh bereishit*, the *Gemara* frames the mention of rain not primarily as a prelude to petition but as an acknowledgment of God's creative power. When we say *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem*, we are not merely warming up to ask for rain — we are recognizing that the rain belongs to the same order as the creation of the world itself. Rain, like creation, is beyond human comprehension; it is an act of divine *gevurah* directly manifest in the world.

### Tosafot: Why Gevurot

*Tosafot* presses the point further: there are other terms in these very *pessukim* — *koach*, *ne'ezar* — so why specifically *gevurah*? Why is it called *gevurot geshamim* rather than, say, *koach geshamim*? *Tosafot* acknowledges the convoluted chain of derivations but then, in the final two lines of the *dibbur*, offers what is clearly the more compelling answer: *mishum dehabracha matchala bigvurah* — because the *bracha* itself begins with *atah gibor*. The phrase *gevurot geshamim* is appropriate because the mention of rain occurs within the *bracha* of *gevurot*, the second *bracha* of *Shemoneh Esrei*, which begins *Atah gibor le'olam Hashem*. While *Tosafot* introduces this as *ee nami* — an alternative — it is universally understood to be the more substantive explanation, and the *Gemara*'s elaborate *pessukim*-chain serves to demonstrate that applying the term *gevurah* to rain is conceptually apt, even if the proximate reason for the name is the placement within that *bracha*.

The debate over the *Gemara*'s frame of reference — is it responding to the *mishna* in *Berachot* or the *mishna* in *Rosh Hashanah*? — is itself connected to the underlying dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua*. If *mashiv haruach* is anchored in *Berachot*, where *Havdala* in *chonen hadaat* is simply the appropriate insertion for the occasion, then the mention of rain is similarly a contextually appropriate insertion — a piece of *shevach* suited to the season — with no inherent connection to petitioning for rain. This is *Rabbi Eliezer*'s orientation. If, however, the frame of reference is *Rosh Hashanah* and *nidonim al hamayim*, then the mention of *mashiv haruach* is the beginning of a movement toward *ten tal umatar*, a lead-in to the petition — which is *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s orientation.

### Avodah Shebalev

A *beraita* is now cited to establish why rain belongs in prayer at all. The verse in *Devarim* reads: *le'ahava et Hashem Elokeichem ule'avdo bechol levavchem* — to love God and to serve Him with all your heart. The *beraita* asks: *eizehu avodah shebalev?* — what is the service of the heart? *Hevei omer zo tefilla* — it is prayer. This is a foundational statement: prayer is defined as *avodah shebalev*, the service of the heart. Immediately following that verse, the Torah states: *venatati matar artzechem be'ito yoreh umalkosh* — I will give rain in its time, the early rain and the late rain. The juxtaposition establishes the connection: serving God with the heart through prayer is directly tied to God's granting of rain.

### Mitzvot & Agriculture

The significance of this connection runs deeper than a textual hint. Rain in *Eretz Yisrael* is explicitly conditioned on *mitzvot* observance throughout the *Torah*, and the *Shema* itself makes precisely this point. If you truly believe that the rain — and therefore your entire livelihood and sustenance — depends on your faithfulness to God's commandments, then prayer is not merely reciting words at appointed times. It is the ongoing expression of that consciousness of dependency, of the recognition that God is directly involved in the provision of life's most basic necessities.

The contrast with *Mitzrayim* is illuminating. Egypt is watered by the Nile, a predictable, naturalistic process. *Eretz Yisrael* depends on rain from the sky — rain that comes from above, that is unpredictable, that is explicitly described in the *Torah* as *einei Hashem Elokecha bah mereshit hashanah v'ad acharit shanah* — God's eyes are upon it from the beginning of the year to the end. This creates a fundamentally different religious consciousness. When your sustenance literally descends from the heavens and depends on your relationship with God, the acknowledgment of that dependency becomes woven into the very texture of daily life. The mention of rain in prayer is therefore not a minor liturgical detail but the expression of a pervasive and thoroughgoing religious mindset.

## Maftechot: Divine Keys

### R. Yochanan: 3 Keys

*Rabbi Yochanan* states: *gimmel maftechot beyado shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu shelo nimsru beyad shaliach* — there are three keys held directly in God's hands that were not entrusted to any intermediary. These are: *mafteach shel geshamim* (the key of rain), *mafteach shel chaya* (the key of childbirth), and *mafteach shel techiyat hameitim* (the key of resurrection). It is worth noting, as a general observation, that the first *perek* of *Taanit* features a disproportionate number of *Amora'im* from *Eretz Yisrael*, *Rabbi Yochanan* prominent among them — a reflection, perhaps, of the acuteness with which the reality of rain dependence was felt there.

### Key of Rain

For the key of rain: *yiftach Hashem lecha et otzaro hatov et hashamayim latet matar artzecha be'ito* — God will open for you His good storehouse, the heavens, to give rain to your land in its time.

### Key of Childbirth

For the key of childbirth: *vayizkor Elokim et Rachel vayishma eleiha Elokim vayiftach et rachmah* — God remembered Rachel, God heard her, and opened her womb.

### Key of Resurrection

For the key of resurrection: *viyadatem ki ani Hashem b'fitchi et kivroteichem* — you shall know that I am God when I open your graves.

### Direct Control

Two features of these prooftexts are worth highlighting. First, in all three cases the operative verb is a form of *pticha* — opening — which is precisely the root of *mafteach*, key. Second, and more significantly, in each verse God's name is immediately juxtaposed with the act of opening, with no intermediary. This is why, for example, the verse about *Leah* whose womb was opened is not used despite occurring earlier in the narrative: in that verse, God's name and the act of opening are not immediately adjacent. The insistence on direct juxtaposition makes the theological point emphatically: these three things are under God's exclusive, unmediated control. There is no *shaliach*, no natural mechanism standing between God and the act. God holds the key directly.

The message for our religious life is profound. It would be easy — and is perhaps a common inclination today — to think of rain in meteorological terms, as the product of atmospheric conditions, with God perhaps involved at some remove. The *Gemara* resists this entirely. *Einei Hashem Elokecha bah* — God's eyes are directly upon it. When rain comes, it is God opening the heavens. When it does not, the absence is equally direct. Your prayer and your relationship with God have a direct and unmediated impact on this outcome. The key is in God's hands, but — as one reading puts it — the key to the key is in human hands: *Rachel* cries out and God opens; you observe the *mitzvot* and God brings rain. It is ultimately our direct relationship with God that determines the outcome.

### Parnassah & Geshamim

A tradition from *Maarava* (the *Amora'im* of *Eretz Yisrael*) adds a fourth key: *mafteach shel parnassah* — the key of livelihood more generally — citing the verse *pose'ach et yadecha umasbia l'chol chai ratzon*. The *Gemara* asks why *Rabbi Yochanan* did not include this. The answer: *geshamim hainu parnassah* — rain simply is *parnassah* in an agricultural society. For *Rabbi Yochanan*, the concrete image of rain is more powerful precisely because it is more specific. To abstract it to *parnassah* in general loses something. The verse about *parnassah* also does not have God's name immediately juxtaposed with the act of opening, which weakens the proof for direct, unmediated divine control.

### Life & Resurrection

The grouping of rain, childbirth, and resurrection carries its own theological weight. All three concern the giving and sustaining of life. Rain gives life to the crops; childbirth brings new life into the world; resurrection restores life to those who have died. By placing rain alongside these two other forms of direct divine life-giving, the *Gemara* frames *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* not merely as a weather petition but as a recognition of God's ongoing role as *mechayeh meitim* — the One who gives life in all its forms. The rain that falls from heaven, the womb that opens, and the grave that releases are all expressions of the same divine power.

### Kimelman: Rain Metaphor

This connection between rain and *techiyat hameitim* also appears in the *bracha* of *Atah gibor* itself, where *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* is embedded within the *bracha* most associated with resurrection: *mechayeh meitim Atah rav l'hoshia*, *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem*, *mechalkel chayyim bechesed mechayeh meitim berachamim rabbim*. Reuven Kimelman has suggested a striking interpretation of this juxtaposition. Rather than seeing rain as a secondary element appended to a *bracha* about resurrection, he argues that the insertion of *geshamim* functions to make *techiyat hameitim* itself more credible and more concrete. Consider: the world appears dead in drought. Seeds are buried in the ground, invisible and inert. Rain descends, and what was buried in the earth rises to life. In the observed, daily reality of agricultural existence, one witnesses something very much like resurrection. By anchoring *techiyat hameitim* to this experiential reality, the *bracha* renders the abstract doctrine tangible. Rain is not merely placed alongside resurrection; it is what makes resurrection believable.

## Timing of Hazkarah

### Source of Timing

Having established the textual and theological basis for *gevurot geshamim*, the *Gemara* now inquires into *Rabbi Eliezer*'s specific position regarding the timing of *hazkarah*. The question is: what is his source for beginning on the first day of *Sukkot*? The *Gemara* considers two possibilities: did he derive it from *lulav*, or from *nisuch hamayim* — the water libation performed on *Sukkot*? The practical difference matters, because if derived from *lulav*, *hazkarah* begins in the daytime on the first day of *Sukkot*; if derived from *nisuch hamayim*, it might begin the night before.

### Beraita Sources

The *Gemara* cites a *beraita* that resolves the question. *Rabbi Eliezer omer mishaat netilat lulav* — from the time one takes the *lulav*. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer mishaat hanachato* — from the time one puts it down, i.e., only after *Sukkot* concludes, beginning with *Shemini Atzeret*. The *beraita* thus confirms that *Rabbi Eliezer*'s source is indeed *lulav*, not *nisuch hamayim*.

### Lulav Symbolism

*Rabbi Eliezer* explains his reasoning: *hu ve'arba'ah minim hallalu einam ba'im ella lirtzot al hamayim* — the *lulav* and the four species come specifically as an act of supplication regarding water. The four species are plants that depend on water; by taking them, we are implicitly acknowledging the world's dependence on water and praying for God's provision of it. *Keshem she'arba'ah minim hallalu i efshar bahem belo mayim, kach i efshar la'olam belo mayim* — just as these species cannot exist without water, so the world cannot exist without water. If the very act of taking *lulav* is a prayer for rain, then one should begin saying *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* from that moment.

### Yehoshua Counter

*Rabbi Yehoshua* counters: *vehalo geshamim bachag einan ella siman klalah* — rain on *Sukkot* is nothing but a sign of curse. One cannot begin mentioning the coming of rain when rain at that moment would itself be a misfortune.

### Eliezer Response

*Rabbi Eliezer* responds, as in the *mishna*: *af ani lo amarti lish'ol ella lehazkir* — I am speaking of *hazkarah*, not *she'ela*. The analogy he invokes is striking: *ukeshem shetechiyat hameitim mazkir kol hashanah kullah ve'einah ella bizmanah, kach mazkir gevurot geshamim kol hashanah ve'einan ella bizmanan* — just as we mention *techiyat hameitim* throughout the entire year even though it will only occur at the appointed time, so too we may mention the *gevurot geshamim* throughout the year even though the rain itself comes only in its season. The mention of something is not the same as requesting it.

### Rainy Season Obligation

From this reasoning, *Rabbi Eliezer* draws a further consequence: if one wishes to recite *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* throughout the entire year, one may. The *hazkarah* is a form of *shevach* that is not inherently limited to any season. However, one is obligated to recite it once the rainy season begins — that is, from the first day of *Sukkot*. *Tosafot* makes this point explicit: *Rabbi Eliezer* distinguishes between the theoretical permissibility of year-round *hazkarah* and the seasonal obligation. The obligation arises when it is now the appropriate time for rain; the permission to mention it at other times demonstrates that *hazkarah* is of a fundamentally different character than *she'ela*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Link

*Rabbi* (i.e., *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi*) then offers a position in implicit support of *Rabbi Yehoshua*: *mishat she-poskin she'ila, kach mafsik lehazkira* — at the moment one ceases asking for rain (after the first day of *Pesach*), one must also cease the *hazkarah*. This means that if you stop *mashiv haruach* when you stop *ten tal umatar*, then the two are inextricably linked — the *hazkarah* is not an independent *shevach* that can be recited at will, but is bound to the *she'ela*. Similarly, if the *hazkarah* can only continue as long as the *she'ela* is relevant, it also cannot begin before the *she'ela* is appropriate.

*Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* offers a variation within *Rabbi Eliezer*'s general framework: he places the beginning of *hazkarah* on the second day of *Sukkot*, because he holds that *nisuch hamayim* — which he views as the source — began on the second day. *Rabbi Akiva* similarly ties *hazkarah* to *nisuch hamayim* but places its beginning on the sixth day of *Sukkot*.

### Shevach v. Bakasha

The debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* (as elaborated by *Rabbi*) thus resolves into a fundamental philosophical question about the nature of *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem*. Is it a general *shevach* — a praise of God's power that, like the mention of *techiyat hameitim*, is conceptually unconstrained by the calendar? Or is it a *bakasha* in liturgical form, inseparably tied to the request of *ten tal umatar* and therefore governed entirely by the same seasonal constraints? *Rabbi Eliezer* holds the former; *Rabbi Yehoshua* and *Rabbi* hold the latter.

## Validating Eliezer

### Techiyat Analogy

The *Gemara* pauses to assess *Rabbi Eliezer*'s analogy: *shafir ka'amar lei Rabbi Eliezer le-Rabbi Yehoshua* — *Rabbi Eliezer* made a good argument to *Rabbi Yehoshua*. If we mention *techiyat hameitim* the whole year even though it will only come in its proper time, why should *mashiv haruach* be different? The analogy appears sound.

### Rain Timing Problem

Nevertheless, the *Gemara* reconstructs *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s response: *bishlama techiyat hameitim mazkir de-chol yoma zmanei hu* — it is understandable that we mention *techiyat hameitim* every day, because every day would be an appropriate day for it to occur; we would welcome it at any time. Rain, however, is categorically different: *kol eimat de-ati zmanaihu?* — is there truly no bad time for rain to come? The premise of the analogy fails.

### Yatzah Nisan

The *Gemara* marshals a *mishna* from elsewhere in the tractate: *yatza Nisan ve-yardu geshamim siman klala hein* — if *Nisan* has passed and rain falls, it is a sign of curse. The supporting verse — *halo katzir chitim hayom* — underscores that rain at the time of wheat harvest is categorically inappropriate. Unlike *techiyat hameitim*, which would be welcome on any day, rain outside its season is not merely untimely but harmful. The analogy therefore cannot hold, and *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s position is vindicated: *hazkarah* of rain, unlike that of *techiyat hameitim*, is season-dependent, and one should not mention it during *Sukkot* if rain at that time would itself be a curse.

## Religious Rhythms

### Non-Holiday Period

A final observation bears directly on the meaning of *mashiv haruach* within the larger structure of the liturgical year. Rabbi Dov Berkowitz notes a striking pattern: *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* is said only during what might be called the "dead time" of the year — the months when there are no *yomim tovim*. It appears at the very conclusion of *Shemini Atzeret* and runs through the beginning of *Pesach*, occupying precisely the stretch of the calendar with no major holidays.

### Human Activity Period

The period from *Nisan* through *Sukkot* is the period of active human endeavor in the agricultural cycle. You are out in the fields — plowing, planting, tending, harvesting. This is the time of human initiative and physical labor.

### Chagim Structure

The *chagim* of this period serve to take that human activity and direct it toward God. *Pesach* arrives at the time of the barley harvest; *Shavuot* at the wheat harvest, the time of *bikkurim*; *Sukkot* at the ingathering of all the harvest. The *mitzvot* of these holidays — *bikkurim*, *lulav*, *simcha lefanei Hashem Elokecha* — are structures for taking the products of human labor and acknowledging them as gifts of God. You share the harvest with the *ani*, the *ger*, and the *yatom*; you take the *lulav* and *etrog* and rejoice before God. Human endeavor is transposed into religious expression.

### Waiting & Dependency

From *Sukkot* through *Pesach*, the human role is fundamentally different. The planting has been done; now one waits. The outcome is entirely beyond human control. Whether the crops will grow depends on whether it rains, and rain is, as the *Gemara* has established, held directly in God's hands.

### Passive Reliance

*Mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* is the liturgical expression of this waiting, this passive reliance. It is recited precisely during the months when there is nothing the farmer can do but pray. The religious posture of this period is one of dependency, of acknowledging that the result lies entirely with God. Prayer — *avodah shebalev* — is the appropriate act when human activity cannot determine the outcome.

### Seasonal Religiosity

What emerges is a picture of two distinct religious modes corresponding to two distinct phases of the agricultural year. During the *chagim*, the religious task is to take active human endeavor and consecrate it — to prevent the labors of the field from being understood as purely human achievements, and to channel them toward acknowledgment of and gratitude to God. During the months of *mashiv haruach*, the religious task is different: to inhabit a posture of conscious dependency, of waiting and prayer, of recognizing that the outcome of all that labor now rests with God alone. The *hazkarah* of *geshamim* is therefore not merely a seasonal liturgical insertion. It is the marker of an entire religious orientation — one that the structure of the year itself is designed to cultivate.
