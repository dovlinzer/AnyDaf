# Zevachim 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul & Matirin

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **any** item **that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar, one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

Daf 44a picks up approximately ten lines into the page, at the break in the text, continuing the *mishna* that identifies which parts of the various *korbanot* are subject to *piggul* and which are not.

### Matir Concept

The *mishna* states: *kol sheyesh lo matirin bein le'adam bein la'mizbe'ach, chayavin alav mishum piggul* — anything that has something else serving as its *matir*, meaning the element that renders it permitted, is subject to *piggul*. The paradigmatic case is an animal sacrifice: the blood is the *matir*, because once the blood is applied to the *mizbe'ach*, the meat and the *emurim* become permissible — either for human consumption or for burning on the altar. The blood makes the remainder of the *korban* valid.

### Piggul Not on Matir

*Piggul* does not apply to the *matir* itself. Even when a *korban* is rendered *piggul*, one who eats the blood does not incur *kares*. It is specifically the parts of the *korban* that have a *matir* — the meat and the *emurim* — to which *piggul* applies. The *matir* itself, because it is the enabling element rather than the element enabled, stands outside the scope of this prohibition.

## Deriving Scope

### Shelamim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוֹ אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא כַּיּוֹצֵא בִּשְׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים מְיוּחָדִים – נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד, אַף כֹּל נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד;
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Or perhaps** the *halakha* of *piggul* **extends only to** an offering that is **similar to peace offerings: Just as peace offerings are notable** in that **they are eaten for two days and one night, so too,** the *halakha* of *piggul* applies to **any** offering that is **eaten for two days and one night.**

The *Gemara* introduces a *baraita* that traces the derivation of *piggul*'s scope from the biblical text. The *pesukim* concerning *piggul* speak specifically about a *shelamim*, so the opening question is whether *piggul* applies only to *shelamim*. The verse in question is Vayikra 7:18: *ve'im he'achol ye'achel mibasar zevach shelamav bayom hashlishi lo yeiratzeh hamakriv oto lo yeichashev lo piggul yihyeh vehanefesh haochelet mimenu avonah tisa*. Perhaps *piggul* is limited to offerings similar to *shelamim* — specifically, those eaten for two days and an intervening night. The *baraita* then asks how we extend *piggul* beyond that narrow category.

### Basar & Zevach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱכָל לְיוֹם וָלַיְלָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִבְּשַׂר״ – כֹּל (שֶׁשִּׁירִין) [שֶׁשְּׁיָרָיו] נֶאֱכָלִין. עוֹלָה שֶׁאֵין שְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֶבַח״.
>
> **Translation:** But as for an offering that **is eaten** only **for one day,** i.e., the day the offering is sacrificed, **and** the following **night,** e.g., a sin offering, guilt offering, and firstborn offering, **from where** is it derived that the *halakha* of *piggul* applies to this offering as well? The verse states: **"Of the flesh** of the sacrifice of his peace offering," which teaches that the status of *piggul* can apply to **any** offering **whose remainder** of meat **is eaten** after its sacrificial portions have been offered on the altar. The *baraita* asks: With regard to **a burnt offering, whose remainder** of meat **is not eaten,** as it is burned in its entirety on the altar, **from where** is this *halakha* derived? **The verse states: "Sacrifice,"** which includes any offering that is slaughtered.

The first extension comes from the word *mibasar*: the phrase *mibasar zevach shelamav* is parsed so that the word *basar* — meat — functions as a broadening term. Anything that has edible remains (*she'ayarim ne'echalim*) is included. But what of the *olah*, which has no meat that is eaten? For that, the *baraita* derives from the word *zevach*: the term *mibasar zevach shelamav* contains both *basar*, covering offerings with edible remnants, and *zevach*, covering all animal sacrifices including those wholly consumed. Together, these two words extend *piggul* to all animal *korbanot*.

### Kedoshim Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת הָעוֹפוֹת וְהַמְּנָחוֹת, עַד שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר הֵם מַקְדִּישִׁים לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **From where** is it derived **to include bird** offerings, e.g., doves or pigeons, which are not slaughtered but whose napes of their necks are pinched, **and meal offerings, until I include** even the ***log* of oil** that accompanies the guilt offering **of** a recovered **leper? The verse states** with regard to the consumption of consecrated food in a state of ritual purity: "That they separate themselves from the sacred items of the children of Israel, **which they consecrate to Me,** and that they do not profane My holy name" (Leviticus 22:2).

The *baraita* then asks how we know to include birds, *menachot*, and the *log shemen* of the *metzora* — a far broader category. The source is *asher hem makdishim li* (Vayikra 22), which speaks of anything they sanctify. The difficulty is that this verse concerns *tumah*, not *piggul*. The *baraita* resolves this through a double *gezeira shava*: the word *chilul* links *nosar* to the *tumah* verse, and the word *avon* links *piggul* to *nosar*. Since the scope of *kodashim* by *tumah* encompasses all *korbanot*, and *nosar* shares that scope, and *piggul* is equated to *nosar*, it follows that *piggul* likewise applies to the full range of sanctified items.

### Derivation through Verbal Analogies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָתֵי נוֹתָר ״חִילּוּל״–״חִילּוּל״ מִטּוּמְאָה, וְאָתֵי פִּיגּוּל ״עָוֹן״–״עָוֹן״ מִנּוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* clarifies this derivation: The *halakha* that the prohibition of ***notar*** applies to all these offerings is **derived** through a verbal analogy of **profanation** in the context of *notar*: "And if anything remains until the third day, it shall be burned in fire…and anyone who eats it shall bear his iniquity, because he has profaned the sacred item of the Lord" (Leviticus 19:6–8), and **profanation** stated in the verse discussing **ritual impurity:** "And that they do not profane My holy name." **And** the *halakha* that ***piggul*** applies to all these offerings is subsequently **derived** through a verbal analogy of **"iniquity"** in the context of *piggul* and **"iniquity"** stated in the verse discussing ***notar*.** With regard to *piggul*, the verse states: "It shall be *piggul*, and the soul that eats of it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 7:18), and with regard to *notar*, it is stated in the aforementioned verse: "And anyone who eats it shall bear his iniquity."

### Shelamim Emphasis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵאַחַר שֶׁסּוֹפוֹ לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר שְׁלָמִים מֵעַתָּה? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה שְׁלָמִים מְיוּחָדִים – שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן מַתִּירִין בֵּין לָאָדָם בֵּין לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין בֵּין לָאָדָם בֵּין לַמִּזְבֵּחַ – חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **And since** the verse **eventually includes all items,** even meal offerings and the *log* of oil of a leper, **now** one can ask: **Why does** the verse **state** *piggul* specifically with regard to **peace offerings?** The *baraita* answers: This serves **to tell you** that the offering must be similar to peace offerings in the following way: **Just as peace offerings are notable** in **that they have permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar, so too,** with regard to **any** item **that has permitting factors, either for a person or for the altar, one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

If the Torah ultimately includes everything through these expansions, why does it mention *shelamim* at all? The *baraita* answers: *lomar lecha* — to tell you, the mention of *shelamim* is not to limit the scope to *shelamim* alone, but to identify a structural principle.

### Limiting Principle

The principle derived from *shelamim* is this: *piggul* applies only to something that has *matirin*, whether for human consumption or for the altar — *mah shelamim meyuchadim sheyesh lahem mattirim bein la'adam uvein la'mizbe'ach, af kol sheyesh lo mattirim bein la'adam uvein la'mizbe'ach chayavin alav mishum piggul*. The word *shelamim* does not limit the scope to those *korbanot* resembling *shelamim* in their eating period; rather, it defines the structural requirement: there must be two components, a *matir* and that which the *matir* permits. Anything satisfying this structure, across all categories of *kodashim*, is subject to *piggul*.

### Excluded Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָעוֹלָה – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְעוֹרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים. עוֹלַת הָעוֹף – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ. חַטַּאת הָעוֹף – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים. פָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים – דָּמָם מַתִּיר אֶת אֵימוּרֵיהֶן לִיקְרַב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* specifies: With regard to **the burnt offering, its blood renders its flesh permitted to** be burned on **the altar and its hide** to be used **by the priests.** With regard to **the bird burnt offering, its blood renders its flesh** and its skin **permitted to** be burned on **the altar.** With regard to **the bird sin offering, its blood renders its flesh permitted for** consumption by **the priests.** With regard to **bulls that are burned,** e.g., the bull sacrificed for an unwitting communal transgression, **and goats that are burned,** e.g., the goat sacrificed for an unwitting communal sin of idol worship, **their blood renders their sacrificial portions permitted to be sacrificed** on the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ, וְאֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה, וְהַקְּטֹרֶת, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, וּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים, וְהַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And I exclude,** via the analogy to peace offerings, **the handful** of a meal offering, **the frankincense, the incense, the meal offering of priests, the meal offering of** the **anointed priest, the meal offering** brought with the **libations** that accompany animal offerings, **and the blood.** All these do not have an item that renders them permitted either for a person or for the altar.

The *baraita* then lists what this principle excludes: the *kometz*, the *levonah*, the *ketoret*, the *minchat kohanim*, and the *minchat kohen mashiach*. These are things that are themselves the *matir* for something else, or that are wholly consumed with no remainder dependent on them. *Tosafot* raises the interesting question of whether the *kometz* is the *matir* for the *levonah* or vice versa — but in any case, both are *matirin* themselves. Similarly, the *ketoret* burned on the inner altar, the free-will *mincha* of any *kohen* that is entirely burned, and the obligatory daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol* all fall into this excluded category. The *dam* — the blood — is likewise excluded, as it is the *matir* itself.

### Nesachim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: נִסְכֵי בְהֵמָה – חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדַּם הַזֶּבַח מַתִּירָן לִיקָרֵב. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא אָדָם מֵבִיא אֶת זְבָחָיו הַיּוֹם, וּנְסָכִין מִיכָּן עַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים! אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי, אֶלָּא בְּבָאִין עִם הַזֶּבַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **As it is taught** in another *baraita*: With regard to the **libations of an animal** offering, **one is liable for** consuming **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*, as the blood of the offering renders them permitted to be offered** on the altar; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Rabbis **said to him: But a person** may **bring his offerings today and** the accompanying **libations from now until** even **ten days** later. Evidently, then, the blood of the offering does not render the libations permitted. Rabbi Meir **said to them: I too spoke only about** libations that **come** to be sacrificed together **with the offering.** If so, the *baraita* under discussion represents two conflicting opinions.

The *baraita* also excludes *minchat nesachim* — the flour, oil, and wine accompanying a *korban*. The rationale is that *nesachim* can be brought independently, even days after the animal sacrifice, and therefore they function as their own *matir*. The *mishna* records a parallel debate about *nesachim* that will be explored further: when brought together with the *korban*, is the blood of the *korban* their *matir*, or are they considered *matir es atzman* even in that circumstance?

## Rabbi Shimon View

### Outer Mizbe'ach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה שְׁלָמִים מְיוּחָדִין – שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו, אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָן עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל. יָצְאוּ פָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים, הוֹאִיל שֶׁאֵין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן כִּשְׁלָמִים – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **Rabbi Shimon says** that the fact that the verse specifies peace offerings as the standard case of *piggul* teaches: **Just as peace offerings are notable** in **that they have** a permitting factor that is sacrificed **on the external altar,** i.e., their blood, **and one is liable for** eating **them** due to violation of the prohibition of *piggul*, **so too,** with regard to **any** item **that** has a permitting factor that **is** sacrificed **on the external altar, one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.** This serves to **exclude bulls that are burned and goats that are burned: Since** their blood is **not** presented **on the external altar like peace offerings, one is not liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*.**

Rabbi Shimon introduces an additional criterion derived from the comparison to *shelamim*: not only must there be *matirin*, but the *matir* must be applied to the outer *mizbe'ach*, just as it is by *shelamim*. He states: *mah shelamim meyuchadim she-yeshnam al mizbe'ach ha-chizon ve-chayavin alav, af kol she-yeshnam al mizbe'ach ha-chizon chayavin alav mishum piggul*.

### Parim Exclusion

By this reasoning, *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* are categorically excluded from *piggul*. Although their *emurim* are burned on the outer *mizbe'ach*, the blood — the *matir* — is applied to the inner altar. Since the *matir* does not go on the outer *mizbe'ach* as it does by *shelamim*, Rabbi Shimon rules there is no *piggul* liability for these offerings at all: *yatz'u parim hanisrafim use'irim hanisrafim ho'il ve-ein al mizbe'ach ha-chizon ke-shelamim ein chayavin alav mishum piggul*. This ending of the *baraita* is nearly word-for-word identical to our *mishna*, and the parallel is instructive — the *mishna* codifies the conclusion while the *baraita* exposes the derivation.

## Bechor & K-P-K

### Bechor Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר [מָר]: כַּיּוֹצֵא בִּשְׁלָמִים. מַאי נִיהוּ? בְּכוֹר, דְּנֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד. בְּמַאי אָתֵי? אִי בְּמָה מָצִינוּ – אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said:** Perhaps the *halakha* of *piggul* extends only to an offering that is **similar to peace offerings: What is this** offering that is similar to a peace offering but not included in the category of peace offerings? The Gemara answers: The reference is to **a firstborn** offering, **which is eaten for two days and one night,** as is a peace offering. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **By what** hermeneutical principle **is** the *halakha* of the firstborn offering **derived? If** it is **by** the hermeneutical principle of: **What do we find** with regard to, a principle of inductive reasoning involving a comparison between cases that include similar details, i.e., since the peace offering and firstborn offering are similar with regard to the time designated for their eating, *piggul* status should apply to each, this **can be refuted.**

The *Gemara* now examines the opening move of the *baraita* more closely. The *baraita* raised the possibility that *piggul* applies only to offerings eaten for two days and an intervening night, like *shelamim*. The *Gemara* asks: what offering could possibly fit that description other than a *shelamim* itself? The answer: *bechor* — the firstborn animal, which is also eaten for two days and one night and is in many ways treated similarly to a *shelamim*, though it is technically a distinct category of *korban*. So the narrow reading of the *baraita* would limit *piggul* to *shelamim* and *bechor* alone.

### K-P-K Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִשְׁלָמִים – שֶׁהֵן טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים, וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the refutation: **What** is notable **about peace offerings?** They are notable in **that they require placing hands** on the head of the offering, **libations, and waving of the breast and thigh.** None of these apply in the case of a firstborn offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵ״אִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* that *piggul* status applies to a firstborn offering is derived through the hermeneutical principle of a generalization and a detail and a generalization, **from** the following verse: **"And if any be at all eaten** of the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings on the third day, it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 7:18). The terms: "And if any" and "be at all eaten" are generalizations, while the words: "The flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offering" constitute a detail. According to the hermeneutical principle of a generalization and a detail and a generalization, in such a case one includes any item that is similar to the detail, and therefore one includes the firstborn offering.

The *Gemara* then asks: if one wanted to broaden beyond just *bechor* through a *binyan av* or *mah mazinu* analogy, could that work? No — because *shelamim* have distinctive requirements not shared by other *korbanot*: the laying on of hands (*semicha*), *nesachim*, and the waving of the breast and thigh (*tenufas chaze ve-shok*). Since *shelamim* are not sufficiently similar to other offerings, an analogical extension would be vulnerable to refutation. Instead, the *baraita* relies on the *klal u-prat u-klal* principle applied to the verse *im he'achol ye'achel mibasar zevach shelamav*: *he'achol ye'achel* is a *klal*, *zevach shelamav* is a *prat*, and the closing form of the verse provides a second *klal* — thus broadening the scope to anything similar in kind to the *prat*.

### Juxtaposed Klalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי תְּרֵי כְּלָלֵי דִּסְמִיכִי אַהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ! אָמַר רָבָא, כִּדְאָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁנֵי כְּלָלוֹת הַסְּמוּכִים זֶה לָזֶה, הַטֵּל פְּרָט בֵּינֵיהֶם וְדוּנֵם בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **These two** phrases **are generalizations that are adjacent to one another,** which means that this is not an instance of a generalization and a detail and a generalization, as they are not in that order. **Rava said:** The hermeneutical principle applies even in this case, **as they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: In **every place that you find two generalizations that are adjacent to one another, cast** the **detail** that is written afterward **between them, and interpret them in** the manner of **a generalization and a detail** and a generalization. Consequently, this verse is considered to state a generalization and a detail and a generalization.

One might object: both *klalim* — the doubled language *he'achol ye'achel* — appear together before the *prat* of *zevach shelamav*, not framing it on both sides in the classic *klal u-prat u-klal* structure. The *Gemara* answers by citing the principle stated by Rava in the name of the sages of *Eretz Yisrael*: *kol makom she-ata moza shnei klalim ha-smuchim ze la-ze, hatel prat beineihem ve-donehu be-klal u-prat u-klal* — wherever you find two *klalim* adjacent to each other with a *prat* immediately following, you may rearrange them and apply the *klal u-prat u-klal* rule. The double language of *he'achol ye'achel* is thus legitimately read as two *klalim* enclosing the *prat* of *shelamim*, enabling the standard *klal u-prat u-klal* derivation that broadens *piggul* beyond *shelamim*.

## Log Shemen Debate

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע. הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי מִנְחַת נְסָכִים וְהַדָּם – אֲתָאן לְרַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* teaches: **Until I include** even **the *log* of oil of a leper.** The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling? The Gemara answers that **it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the ***log* of oil of the leper, one is liable for** consuming **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** if the guilt offering that this oil accompanied became *piggul*; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Say the latter clause: And I exclude the meal offering** brought with the **libations** that accompany animal offerings, **and the blood,** as they do not have a permitting factor. Here **we arrive at** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who dispute the ruling of Rabbi Meir.

The *Gemara* identifies which Tanna authored the *baraita* cited earlier — specifically the portion that includes *log shemen shel metzora* within the scope of *piggul*. The *Gemara* states: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi*. A separate *baraita* confirms this: *log shemen shel metzora she-chayavin alav mishum piggul divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that *piggul* applies to the *log shemen* of the *metzora*. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that the blood of the *asham* accompanying the *metzora* serves as the *matir* for the oil, just as the blood of an animal sacrifice is the *matir* for the meat. Correspondingly, Rabbi Meir applies the same logic to *nesachim*: *nischei behemah chayavin aleihem mishum piggul mipnei she-dam ha-zevach matiran likarev* — since the blood of the animal is the *matir* that permits them to be offered, *piggul* applies to them as well.

### Rabbis Counter

The rabbis challenge Rabbi Meir on both counts. With respect to *nesachim*, they demonstrate that since a person may bring his animal sacrifice today and his *nesachim* up to ten days later, the blood of the *korban* cannot be the *matir* for the *nesachim* — they stand independently. Rabbi Meir acknowledges this: *af ani lo amarti ela be-ba'im im ha-zevach* — he limited his ruling to *nesachim* that are brought together with the *korban*. The rabbis, however, maintain that even when brought together, the fact that they could come separately means they are considered *matir es atzman* in all circumstances, and are therefore excluded from *piggul*. By the same logic, the rabbis argue, the *log shemen* should likewise be excluded.

This creates a problem for the *baraita*: it appears to include *log shemen* in *piggul* while excluding *minchat nesachim*. Since Rabbi Meir includes both and the rabbis exclude both, neither position can account for the *baraita*'s split verdict.

### Rav Yosef Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע – מַתְּנוֹתָיו שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ; וּמִדְּמַתְּנוֹתָיו שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ – מַתְּנוֹתָיו מְפַגְּלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling, that even the *log* of oil of a leper is included in the prohibition of *piggul*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who** agrees with the Rabbis that the libations of an animal offering are not permitted by the blood of the offering. And Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **says** with regard to the ***log* of oil of a leper** that it is not the blood of the guilt offering that renders it permitted; rather, the **placements of its** oil "before the Lord" (Leviticus 14:16) **render** the remainder of the oil **permitted** to be eaten by the priests. **And from** the fact **that** the **placements of its** oil **render** the oil **permitted,** by the same token the **placements of its** oil **render it *piggul*,** i.e., if the oil was placed with the intent that the priests should consume its remainder on the following day, one who consumes the oil is liable for consuming *piggul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, עַד שֶׁיִּזְרוֹק הַדָּם. נִזְרַק הַדָּם – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the source for Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the ***log* of oil of a leper,** one who derives benefit **from it** is liable for **misusing** consecrated property if he derives benefit from it at any point after it has been consecrated in a service vessel, **until the blood** of the leper's guilt offering **is sprinkled.** At this stage the oil is permitted to the priests, and therefore the prohibition against misusing property consecrated to the Temple no longer applies to it. Once **the blood has been sprinkled, one may not derive benefit** from the oil *ab initio*, by rabbinic law, as it must still be placed on the leper's right ear, thumb, and big toe. **But** if one derived benefit from it, he is **not** liable for **misuse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין, עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן מַתְּנוֹתָיו. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן מַתַּן שֶׁבַע וּמַתַּן בְּהוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** One who derives benefit from the oil is liable for **misusing** consecrated property **until** the priest **places its** own **placements,** i.e., until the oil is sprinkled seven times toward the Sanctuary, as these sprinklings render the remainder of the oil permitted to the priests. **And** the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **agree that consumption** of the *log* of oil is **prohibited until** the priest **places the seven placements,** i.e., sprinklings, of oil toward the Sanctuary, **and** performs the **placing** of the oil **on the** leper's **thumb and big toe.**

Rav Yosef resolves the difficulty by attributing the *baraita* to a third opinion: *ha mani Rabbi hi* — this is the position of *Rabbi* (Yehuda HaNasi). Rav Yosef explains that Rabbi holds *log shemen shel metzora matnosav sharu leih* — the sprinkling actions (*matanos*) of the *log shemen* are what render the remainder of the oil permissible. This means the *log shemen* itself has two components: the sprinkling (which functions as a *matir*) and the remaining oil (which the sprinkling permits). On this view, even when the *log shemen* is brought separately without any accompanying *korban*, it retains an internal *matir*-and-remainder structure.

The textual support for this position comes from a dispute about *meilah*. The rabbis hold: *mo'alin bo ad she-yizarek ha-dam* — the *log shemen* retains *meilah* status until the blood of the *asham* is sprinkled; once the blood is applied, *lo nehenin ve-lo mo'alin* — it may not be used for benefit but no longer carries *meilah*. Rabbi, however, holds: *mo'alin ad she-yiten matnosav* — *meilah* applies even after the blood is sprinkled, until the oil itself is sprinkled. Since the act of sprinkling the oil removes the *meilah* status, it follows that the sprinkling serves as a *matir* for the remaining oil. Accordingly, Rabbi holds that even when the *log shemen* comes separately, the oil has its own internal *matir* structure — the sprinkling — distinct from anything the blood provides.

This allows Rabbi to split the vote in a way neither Rabbi Meir nor the rabbis do: like the rabbis, Rabbi holds that *nesachim*, having no internal two-part structure, are excluded from *piggul*; but unlike the rabbis, Rabbi holds that the *log shemen* does have an internal *matir* — the sprinkling — and is therefore subject to *piggul*. This is a striking resolution.

### R. Yirmiah Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, אָמַר: גַּבְרָא רַבָּא כְּרַב יוֹסֵף, לֵימָא כִּי הָא מִילְּתָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **They said this** statement **before Rabbi Yirmeya** in Eretz Yisrael, whereupon **he said:** Would **a great man such as Rav Yosef say such a matter,** that the sprinkling of the oil renders the rest of the oil *piggul*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי לוֹג [הַבָּא] בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – דִּלְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מַתְּנוֹתָיו שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ, וְלָא מְפַגְּלִין לֵיהּ! דְּתַנְיָא: לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדָּם מַתִּירוֹ לַבְּהוֹנוֹת. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** But there is the case of the ***log* of oil that is brought by itself,** i.e., where the leper brings it after he has sacrificed his offerings. There **everyone** agrees that **its placements render** the remainder of the oil **permitted to** the priests, **and** yet **they do not render** the oil ***piggul*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the ***log* of oil of a leper, one is liable for** consuming **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*, as** the **blood** of the offering **renders it permitted to** be placed on **the thumb and big toe** of the leper. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: וַהֲלֹא אָדָם מֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ עַכְשָׁיו, וְלוֹג מִיכָּן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים! אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי, אֶלָּא בְּבָא עִם הָאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: But a person** may **bring his guilt offering today and** the accompanying ***log*** of oil **from now until** even **ten days** later. Rabbi Meir **said to them: I too spoke only about** a *log* of oil that **comes with the guilt offering.** This indicates that even according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, it is the blood of the offering that renders the oil *piggul*.

When this answer of Rav Yosef was reported before Rabbi Yirmiah, his reaction was sharp: *gavra rabba k'Rav Yosef leima ki hai milta?* — would so great a scholar as Rav Yosef give such an answer? The objection is this: if the rationale for including *log shemen* in *piggul* is that the sprinkling of the oil is its *matir*, then even when the *log* is brought alone — completely separately from the *korban*, with no blood of an *asham* in the picture — the same reasoning should apply. The sprinkling still functions as the *matir* of the remaining oil in that case, since everyone agrees *matnosav sharu leih* — the sprinkling is what permits it even then. Yet the *baraita* unambiguously states that there is no *piggul* for *log shemen* when brought alone: *lo shemen shel metzora chayavin alav mishum piggul mipnei she-dam matiro lebehonot divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir says that when it comes with the *korban*, the blood permits it; and when asked about bringing them separately, he concedes *afilu lo amarti ela be-va'im im ha-asham* — I only said so when it comes with the *asham*. So even Rabbi Meir acknowledges there is no *piggul* when the *log* comes alone.

But that is precisely the problem with Rav Yosef's answer. When the *log* comes alone, the sprinkling is still the *matir* for the remaining oil — yet no one says *piggul* applies in that case. If the existence of an internal *matir* through the sprinkling were sufficient to create *piggul*, it should apply when the *log* comes alone as well. The failure of *piggul* to apply in that case suggests that the mere fact of the sprinkling being a *matir* does not suffice. The distinguishing factor, it seems, is that the *log* brought alone does not function as a genuine *korban* in the full sense — notably, none of the *log shemen* is placed directly on the *mizbe'ach*.

### Abaye Reconcile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וּסְמִי מִיכָּן נְסָכִים. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם לָא תִּסְמֵי; וְתָנָא לוֹג הַבָּא עִם הָאָשָׁם – וְהוּא הַדִּין לִנְסָכִים הַבָּאִים עִם הַזֶּבַח, וַהֲדַר תָּנָא נְסָכִים הַבָּאִים בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן – וְהוּא הַדִּין לְלוֹג הַבָּא לִפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yirmeya says: Actually,** the aforementioned *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and omit** the case of the meal offering brought with the **libations** that accompany animal offerings **from** the list of items that are not subject to the *halakha* of *piggul*. **Abaye says: Actually, do not omit** this item from the list, and the *baraita* can be explained in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir as follows: The *tanna* first **taught** the *halakha* with regard to the ***log*** of oil **that comes with the** leper's **guilt offering, and the same is true of libations that come with** an animal **offering,** as according to Rabbi Meir *piggul* status applies to both of these. **And then** the *tanna* **taught** that *piggul* does not apply to **libations that come by themselves, and the same is true of a *log*** of oil **that comes by itself.**

Rabbi Yirmiah's preferred answer is to take the *baraita* as straightforwardly representing Rabbi Meir's position and simply to delete the exclusion of *nesachim* from the list: *ela amar Rabbi Yirmiah le'olam Rabbi Meir hi, ve-sami mikan nesachim* — erase that line. On this reading, the *baraita* is entirely consistent with Rabbi Meir: both *log shemen* and *nesachim* are included in *piggul* when brought with the *korban*.

Abaye, however, objects to any emendation: *le'olam lo tismi* — do not erase anything. Abaye instead proposes a reread of the *baraita*'s structure. The *baraita* is not drawing a distinction between *log shemen* (included in *piggul*) and *nesachim* (excluded). Rather, it is drawing a distinction between coming *with* the *korban* and coming *without* it. When it says there is *piggul* by the *log*, it means the *log* brought together with the *asham* — and the same applies to *nesachim* brought together with their *korban*. When it says there is no *piggul* by *nesachim*, it means *nesachim* brought separately — and the same applies to *log* brought separately. The *baraita* illustrates two cases of the same Tannaitic position: with the *korban*, the blood functions as *matir* and *piggul* applies; without the *korban*, there is no *matir* from the blood and no *piggul* — whether the item in question is *nesachim* or *log shemen*.

## Priestly Rights

### Chatas Ha-of

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטַּאת הָעוֹף – דָּמָהּ מַתִּיר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנֵי לֵוִי:
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **the bird burnt offering, its blood renders its meat** and its skin **permitted to** be eaten by **the priests.** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter,** that the meat of a bird burnt offering is eaten by the priests, derived? The Gemara cites a *baraita* **that Levi teaches,** with regard to a verse that discusses the gifts that are to be presented to the priests: "This shall be yours of the most sacred items, from the fire: Every offering of theirs, every meal offering of theirs, and every sin offering of theirs, and every guilt offering of theirs, which they shall render to Me, shall be most sacred for you and for your sons" (Numbers 18:9).

The *Gemara* notes that *chatat ha'of dama matir besarah la-kohanim* — the blood of the bird *chatat* renders its meat permitted to the *kohanim* — and asks: from where do we know that *kohanim* are entitled to eat the bird *chatat*? The Torah does not state this explicitly. The source is a *baraita* taught by Levi, derived from the concluding section of Parashat Korach. Rashi notes the aptness of the context: the challenge of Korach to the *kehunah* occasioned a comprehensive reaffirmation of all priestly entitlements at the end of that *parasha*. The verse (Bamidbar 18:9) states: *zeh yihyeh lecha mikodesh hakodashim min ha'esh — kol korbanam le-chol minchasam u-le-chol chatasam u-le-chol ashamam asher yashivu li kodesh kodashim lecha hu u-le-vanecha*. The *Gemara* proceeds to derive a series of priestly entitlements from the expansive language of this verse.

### Log in Kol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כׇּל קׇרְבָּנָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״מִן הָאֵשׁ״ כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי לָאו מוֹתָר מִן הָאֵשׁ הוּא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* clarifies what is included by the term "every" in each of these clauses. The verse states: **"Every offering of theirs,"** which serves **to include** the ***log* of oil of a leper;** it too is given to the priests. The Gemara explains why it is necessary to derive from the verse that the oil is a gift to the priesthood: It might **enter your mind to say** that the oil should not be included, as **the Merciful One writes** in this same verse: **"From the fire," and this** *log* of oil, notwithstanding its status as an offering, **is not reserved from the fire.** Only an item concerning which part of it is brought to the altar can be said to be reserved from the fire, and none of the oil is brought to the altar. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** by the phrase: "Every offering of theirs," that the oil goes to the priests.

The word *kol korbanam* — "all their sacrifices" — is read as including the *log shemen shel metzora*. One might have thought the *log shemen* is excluded because *min ha'esh* — "from the fire" — implies that what the *kohanim* receive must be something placed on the *mizbe'ach*, and none of the *log shemen* goes on the *mizbe'ach* directly. The Torah therefore writes *kol korbanam* to include anything that is *nikrav* — brought near — and Rashi points out that the verse in the *metzora* passage explicitly uses the language *ve-hikriv oto le-asham ve-et log ha-shemen*, applying the root of *korban* to the oil itself.

### Minchas Omer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכׇל מִנְחָתָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת מִנְחַת עוֹמֶר וּמִנְחַת הַקְּנָאוֹת. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אוֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״ – וּמִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר לְהַתִּיר אָתְיָא, וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת לְבָרֵר עָוֹן קָאָתְיָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase: **"Every meal offering of theirs,"** serves **to include** the ***omer* meal offering,** brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan, **and the meal offering of jealousy,** brought by a *sota*. The Gemara elaborates: It might **enter your mind to say** that as the verse states with regard to the consumption of sacrificial food by the priests: **"And they shall eat those items with which atonement is achieved"** (Exodus 29:33), only those foods that facilitate atonement are given to the priests. **And** this would exclude the *omer* meal offering and the meal offering of jealousy, as **the *omer* meal offering comes to permit** the consumption of the new crop, **and the meal offering of jealousy comes to clarify** the **transgression** of the *sota*, as part of the ordeal undergone by the woman. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** by the phrase: "Every meal offering of theirs," that even these meal offerings are eaten by the priests.

The phrase *le-chol minchasam* — "all their meal offerings" — is read to include *minchat ha'omer* and *minchat ha-kena'ot* (the *sotah mincha*). One might have excluded these because the eating of the *mincha* is normally justified by its being *asher kupar bahem* — something that achieves atonement — whereas *minchat ha'omer* comes to permit the new grain (*le-hatir*) and *minchat kena'ot* comes to clarify whether the woman sinned (*le-varer avon ka-atya*). Since neither achieves classic *kapara*, one might have thought the *kohanim* have no entitlement to them. The Torah therefore writes *le-chol minchasam* to include them.

### Chatasam Detail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּלְכׇל חַטָּאתָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת חַטַּאת הָעוֹף. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: נְבֵילָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase: **"And every sin offering of theirs,"** serves **to include the bird sin offering;** its meat too is given to the priests. The Gemara explains: It might **enter your mind to say** that this meat should not be eaten at all, as **it is** an unslaughtered **animal carcass,** since the bird is killed by pinching its nape (see Leviticus 5:8) rather than by slaughtering, which is the manner of ritual slaughter of non-sacred birds. Therefore, the phrase: "And every sin offering of theirs," teaches that the bird sin offering is eaten by the priests.

The phrase *le-chol chatasam* includes *chatat ha'of* — the bird *sin-offering*. This is the case the *Gemara* began with. One might have excluded it on the grounds that it is technically a *neveilah*, since it is not slaughtered through a conventional *shechita* but through *melikat ha-oreph* — breaking the bird's neck from the back. The Torah nonetheless includes it within the *kohanim*'s entitlement.

### Asham Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכׇל אֲשָׁמָם״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לְהַכְשִׁיר קָאָתוּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase: **"Every guilt offering of theirs,"** serves **to include the guilt offering of a nazirite** who contracted ritual impurity, **and the guilt offering of a leper.** The Gemara elaborates: It might **enter your mind to say** that these should not be given to the priests to eat, as **they come to qualify** these individuals, rather than to atone. The guilt offering of a nazirite renders him fit to restart his term of naziriteship, and the guilt offering of a leper qualifies him to eat sacrificial food, whereas the verse states: "And they shall eat those items with which atonement is achieved" (Exodus 29:33). Therefore, the verse **teaches us** by the phrase: "Every guilt offering of theirs," that these offerings as well are eaten by the priests.

The phrase *le-chol ashamam* — "all their guilt offerings" — is read to include *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*. The *Gemara* notes that the entitlement to *asham metzora* is actually stated explicitly elsewhere; the function of *le-chol ashamam* here is therefore to equate *asham nazir* to *asham metzora*. The basis for possible exclusion: both are brought not for classical atonement but to change the person's halachic status — the *asham nazir* reinstates a new *nezirut* (the actual purification from *tumah* being accomplished through the *parah adumah*), and the *asham metzora* is part of the purification process. Since these are not standard *kappara* offerings, one might have thought the *kohanim* have no eating entitlement. The verse teaches otherwise.

### Gezel Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ! אֶלָּא לְרַבּוֹת אֲשַׁם נָזִיר כַּאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **It is explicitly written with regard to the guilt offering of a leper** that it is consumed by the priests: "For as the sin offering is the priest's, so is the guilt offering" (Leviticus 14:13). Why, then, is this derivation necessary? **Rather,** the *baraita* means to say that the phrase: "Every guilt offering of theirs," serves **to include the guilt offering of a nazirite,** teaching that it is **like the guilt offering of a leper,** in that both are eaten by the priests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר יָשִׁיבוּ״ – זֶה גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר. ״לְךָ הוּא״ – שֶׁלְּךָ יִהְיֶה, אֲפִילּוּ לְקַדֵּשׁ בּוֹ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to the phrase **"which they shall render** to Me," **this is** referring to an item **stolen** from **a convert.** One who robs a convert who then dies with no heirs must give the stolen item and an additional one-fifth to the priests. Finally, the term **"for you"** teaches that **it shall be yours, even to betroth a woman with it,** i.e., these gifts are considered the priest's property in all regards.

Finally, the phrase *asher yashivu li* — "that which they return to Me" — is identified as referring to *gezel ha-ger*: the principal and added fifth that must be returned when one steals from a convert who subsequently dies without heirs. Since a convert who dies without descendants has no one to receive restitution, the money goes to the *kohanim*. The *Gemara* emphasizes that this money is fully owned by the *kohanim* — *le-kohen hu she-yehei afilu ka-rishbasa isha* — it may even be used for *kiddushin*. This stands in sharp contrast to the meat of a *chatat* or other sacrificial portions, which the *kohanim* eat but do not own. As the *Gemara* elsewhere formulates it, the *kohanim* eating sacrificial meat are eating from *mishulchan gavoa* — God's table. The host permits them to partake; the food remains His. Accordingly, if a *kohen* were to use the meat of a *chatat* as the instrument of *kiddushin*, it would not take effect. The money of *gezel ha-ger*, by contrast, belongs fully to the *kohanim* and may be used freely.

## R. Eliezer Parim (II)

### Outside Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: פִּיגֵּל בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ – פִּיגֵּל. בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים – לֹא פִּיגֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** § According to the first *tanna* of the mishna, the bulls that are burned and the goats that are burned, the blood of which is presented on the inner altar, are subject to *piggul*, whereas Rabbi Shimon rules that they are not subject to *piggul*. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that there is a third opinion concerning the matter: **Rabbi Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili:** If in his service of the bulls that are burned or the goats that are burned the priest had an intention that can **render** the offering ***piggul* with regard to a matter that is performed outside** the Sanctuary, i.e., in the Temple courtyard, **he has rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** If his intention was **with regard to a matter that is performed inside** the Sanctuary or the Holy of Holies, **he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* containing the position of Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yose HaGlili: *piggul b'davar hane'aseh ba-chutz* — *piggul* in the context of *parim hanisrafim* applies only when both the *avoda* being performed and the improper thought concern acts and elements of the *chutz*, meaning the *azara*, the courtyard — as opposed to the *pnim*, the inner *heichal*. This is a more nuanced position than that of Rabbi Shimon, who categorically excludes *parim hanisrafim* from *piggul* altogether. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *piggul* does apply to *parim hanisrafim*, but only under a specific structural condition.

### Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? הָיָה עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט לְהַזּוֹת מִדָּמוֹ לְמָחָר״ – לֹא פִּיגֵּל, שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה בַּחוּץ בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים לֹא פִּיגֵּל. הָיָה עוֹמֵד בִּפְנִים, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי מַזֶּה עַל מְנָת לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרִים וְלִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר״ – לֹא פִּיגֵּל, שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה בִּפְנִים בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* elaborates: **How so?** If **he was standing outside** when slaughtering the animal, **and said: I hereby slaughter** the animal with the intention of **sprinkling its blood tomorrow** inside the Sanctuary, **he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** The reason is **that** in the case of **an intention outside with regard to a matter that is performed inside, one has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** Likewise, if he **was standing inside** when sprinkling, **and said: I hereby sprinkle** the blood of the sin offering **in order to burn** its **sacrificial portions** on the external altar **and to pour out** its **remainder** on the base of the altar **tomorrow, he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*, as** this is **an intention inside with regard to a matter that is performed outside.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָיָה עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט לִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר״, אוֹ ״לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרִים לְמָחָר״ – פִּיגֵּל, שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה בַּחוּץ בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **he was standing outside, and said: I hereby slaughter** the animal with the intention of **pouring out** the **remainder** of its blood **tomorrow, or to burn** its **sacrificial portions tomorrow, he has rendered** the offering ***piggul*, as** this is **an intention outside with regard to a matter that is performed outside.**

The *baraita* provides a series of cases. If a *kohen* was standing in the *azara* performing *shechita* and declared that he was slaughtering in order to sprinkle blood on the inner altar tomorrow — *lo pigel*. Although the *avoda* being performed (the *shechita*) is in the *chutz*, the improper thought concerns an act of the *pnim* (the inner sprinkling). Since the thought is about something done inside, no *piggul* results.

Conversely, if the *kohen* was standing in the *heichal* performing the inner blood sprinkling and declared that he intends to burn the *emurim* or pour the *shirayim* tomorrow — again, *lo piggul*. The improper thought concerns outer acts, but the *avoda* being performed is an inner act. The two elements must match: both the act and the thought must concern the *chutz*.

The positive case: if the *kohen* was standing in the *azara* performing *shechita* and declared that he intends to pour the *shirayim* tomorrow or to burn the *emurim* tomorrow — *pigel*, *shemachshava ba-chutz bidavar na'aseh ba-chutz*. Here the *avoda* is outer (*shechita* in the *azara*) and the improper thought concerns outer acts (the pouring of *shirayim* and the burning of *emurim* on the outer *mizbe'ach*). Both conditions are satisfied and *piggul* results.

One nuance worth noting: whether the pouring of *shirayim* itself counts as a qualifying outer *avoda* depends on whether *shirayim* are *me'akev* — indispensable — for *chataot ha-penimiyot*; if they are not *me'akev*, they do not constitute a *piggul*-generating *avoda*. But at minimum, the *shechita* itself is performed outside, as are *kabalah* and *holachah*, giving multiple outer *avodos* through which *piggul* could be generated during the processing of a *par hanisraf*.

### Makish Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי קְרָא? ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יוּרַם מִשּׁוֹר זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים״ – וְכִי מָה לָמַדְנוּ מִשּׁוֹר זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים מֵעַתָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: What is the verse** from which this is derived? The verse states with regard to the sacrificial portions of a bull for an unwitting sin of the anointed priest, which is one of the bulls that are burned: **"As it is taken off from the bull of the peace offering"** (Leviticus 4:10). **But what, then, do we learn from the bull of a peace offering?** Everything that is specified with regard to a peace offering is stated with regard to this bull as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַקִּישׁ פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ לְשׁוֹר זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים; מָה שׁוֹר זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים – עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ מַעֲשָׂיו וּמַחְשְׁבוֹתָיו עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, אַף פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ – עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ מַחְשְׁבוֹתָיו וּמַעֲשָׂיו עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse **juxtaposes the bull** for an unwitting sin **of** the **anointed priest with the bull of the peace offering: Just as the bull of the peace offering** is not rendered *piggul* **unless** the priest's **actions and intentions** relate to the service performed **on the external altar,** as that is where it is offered, **so too, the bull** for an unwitting sin **of** the **anointed priest** is not rendered *piggul* **unless** the priest's **intentions and actions** relate to the service performed **on the external altar.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asks: what is the scriptural basis for this limiting condition? The answer lies in the verse by the *par kohen hamashiach*, one of the *parim hanisrafim*: *ka'asher yuram mi-shor zevach hashelamim* — the *emurim* are to be treated just as the *emurim* of a *shelamim*. This verse is puzzling: the *Torah* already listed all the *emurim* of the *par* in detail. After enumerating them, why does it add "just like the *shelamim*"? There is nothing left to teach about the *emurim* themselves. Therefore, the comparison must be doing something different. The *baraita* reads it as a *makish* — an analogy — connecting the *par kohen hamashiach* to the *shor zevach hashelamim*: just as by a *shelamim* the laws of *piggul* apply only when both the acts (*ma'asim*) and the thoughts (*machshavot*) concern the outer *mizbe'ach*, so too by the *par kohen hamashiach* — and by extension all *parim hanisrafim* — *piggul* applies only when both the *avoda* being performed and the wrongful intention concern acts on the outer *mizbe'ach*.

## R. Eliezer Parim (II)

### Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says** his ruling **in the name of Rabbi Yosei.**

Rav Nachman rules in the name of Rabba bar Avuha in the name of Rav: *halacha ke-Rabbi Eliezer she-amru mishum Rabbi Yosef* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Eliezer. *Parim hanisrafim* are not categorically exempt from *piggul*, as Rabbi Shimon held; rather, *piggul* applies to them when both the *avoda* being performed and the improper intention concern matters of the outer *azara*.

## Pesak Theory

### Messianic Era

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא לִמְשִׁיחָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Does one issue **a *halakha* for the messianic** period, when the Temple will be rebuilt?

Rava immediately objects: *hilchesa le-mashiacha?* Is this a ruling on a law that can only become practically relevant in the messianic era, when the Temple is rebuilt? What is the force of pronouncing a definitive *halacha* on such a matter?

### Torah Study Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, כֹּל שְׁחִיטַת קֳדָשִׁים לָא לִתְנֵי – הִלְכְתָא לִמְשִׁיחָא הוּא! אֶלָּא דְּרוֹשׁ וְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר; הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּרוֹשׁ וְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If that is so,** that such *halakhot* are not taught, **let** the *tanna* **not teach all** the *halakhot* of **the slaughter of sacrificial** animals, i.e., tractate *Zevaḥim*, as **it is** entirely **a *halakha* for the messianic** period. **Rather,** one studies these *halakhot* due to the principle of: **Study** Torah **and receive reward,** i.e., one is rewarded for the study of Torah regardless of its practical applicability. **Here too, study** Torah **and receive reward.**

Abaye responds: *ela me'atah kol shechitas kodashim lo lisni?* Should we abandon the study of the entire tractate of *Zevachim*? None of it is currently applicable. *Ela derosh ve-kabel sachar* — study it and receive the reward; *hachi nami derosh ve-kabel sachar* — here too, study it and receive the reward. Torah study is intrinsically valuable regardless of immediate practical application.

### Relevance Concern

Rava refines his objection: *halacha kamina* — what troubles me is not the study of these laws but the specific act of rendering a formal *psak halacha*. Issuing a definitive ruling implies practical relevance. When one says "the *halacha* is like Rabbi Eliezer," the natural implication is that this determination matters — that someone might rely on it or be affected by it. If there is no context in which it could ever be applied, what does it mean to issue such a ruling? The resolution offered — that one is simply studying and receiving reward — does not entirely satisfy, because it does not explain the *phrasing* of the *halacha* as a formal ruling rather than as a theoretical position.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* addresses this tension by cataloguing a number of places in the *Gemara* where *halacha le-mashiacha*-type rulings are issued on matters exclusive to *mikdash* practice, without the *Gemara* raising any objection. *Tosafot* observes that in most of those cases, a practical application can be identified that makes the ruling relevant even today. For the handful of cases where no such application can be found, *Tosafot* offers a distinct resolution: perhaps the particular concern in our passage is not merely that the law is Temple-era, but that *piggul* by definition only arises when a *kohen* has acted wrongly — has harbored an improper intention. Issuing a *psak halacha* about a law that presupposes transgression, which is itself applicable only in the time of the Temple, invites a sharper objection than ruling on a straightforward Temple-era procedure. That combination — inapplicable in practice and arising only through wrongdoing — may be what Rava finds difficult to justify as a formal *psak*, even if the study itself remains entirely worthwhile.
