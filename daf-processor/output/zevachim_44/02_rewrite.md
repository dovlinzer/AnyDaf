# Zevachim 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul & Matirin

### Daf Overview

Daf 44a picks up approximately ten lines into the page, at the break in the text, continuing the *mishna* that identifies which parts of the various *korbanot* are subject to *piggul* and which are not.

### Matir Concept

The *mishna* states: *kol sheyesh lo matirin bein le'adam bein la'mizbe'ach, chayavin alav mishum piggul* — anything that has something else serving as its *matir*, meaning the element that renders it permitted, is subject to *piggul*. The paradigmatic case is an animal sacrifice: the blood is the *matir*, because once the blood is applied to the *mizbe'ach*, the meat and the *emurim* become permissible — either for human consumption or for burning on the altar. The blood makes the remainder of the *korban* valid.

### Piggul Not on Matir

*Piggul* does not apply to the *matir* itself. Even when a *korban* is rendered *piggul*, one who eats the blood does not incur *kares*. It is specifically the parts of the *korban* that have a *matir* — the meat and the *emurim* — to which *piggul* applies. The *matir* itself, because it is the enabling element rather than the element enabled, stands outside the scope of this prohibition.

## Deriving Scope

### Shelamim Question

The *Gemara* introduces a *baraita* that traces the derivation of *piggul*'s scope from the biblical text. The *pesukim* concerning *piggul* speak specifically about a *shelamim*, so the opening question is whether *piggul* applies only to *shelamim*. The verse in question is Vayikra 7:18: *ve'im he'achol ye'achel mibasar zevach shelamav bayom hashlishi lo yeiratzeh hamakriv oto lo yeichashev lo piggul yihyeh vehanefesh haochelet mimenu avonah tisa*. Perhaps *piggul* is limited to offerings similar to *shelamim* — specifically, those eaten for two days and an intervening night. The *baraita* then asks how we extend *piggul* beyond that narrow category.

### Basar & Zevach

The first extension comes from the word *mibasar*: the phrase *mibasar zevach shelamav* is parsed so that the word *basar* — meat — functions as a broadening term. Anything that has edible remains (*she'ayarim ne'echalim*) is included. But what of the *olah*, which has no meat that is eaten? For that, the *baraita* derives from the word *zevach*: the term *mibasar zevach shelamav* contains both *basar*, covering offerings with edible remnants, and *zevach*, covering all animal sacrifices including those wholly consumed. Together, these two words extend *piggul* to all animal *korbanot*.

### Kedoshim Inclusion

The *baraita* then asks how we know to include birds, *menachot*, and the *log shemen* of the *metzora* — a far broader category. The source is *asher hem makdishim li* (Vayikra 22), which speaks of anything they sanctify. The difficulty is that this verse concerns *tumah*, not *piggul*. The *baraita* resolves this through a double *gezeira shava*: the word *chilul* links *nosar* to the *tumah* verse, and the word *avon* links *piggul* to *nosar*. Since the scope of *kodashim* by *tumah* encompasses all *korbanot*, and *nosar* shares that scope, and *piggul* is equated to *nosar*, it follows that *piggul* likewise applies to the full range of sanctified items.

### Shelamim Emphasis

If the Torah ultimately includes everything through these expansions, why does it mention *shelamim* at all? The *baraita* answers: *lomar lecha* — to tell you, the mention of *shelamim* is not to limit the scope to *shelamim* alone, but to identify a structural principle.

### Limiting Principle

The principle derived from *shelamim* is this: *piggul* applies only to something that has *matirin*, whether for human consumption or for the altar — *mah shelamim meyuchadim sheyesh lahem mattirim bein la'adam uvein la'mizbe'ach, af kol sheyesh lo mattirim bein la'adam uvein la'mizbe'ach chayavin alav mishum piggul*. The word *shelamim* does not limit the scope to those *korbanot* resembling *shelamim* in their eating period; rather, it defines the structural requirement: there must be two components, a *matir* and that which the *matir* permits. Anything satisfying this structure, across all categories of *kodashim*, is subject to *piggul*.

### Excluded Items

The *baraita* then lists what this principle excludes: the *kometz*, the *levonah*, the *ketoret*, the *minchat kohanim*, and the *minchat kohen mashiach*. These are things that are themselves the *matir* for something else, or that are wholly consumed with no remainder dependent on them. *Tosafot* raises the interesting question of whether the *kometz* is the *matir* for the *levonah* or vice versa — but in any case, both are *matirin* themselves. Similarly, the *ketoret* burned on the inner altar, the free-will *mincha* of any *kohen* that is entirely burned, and the obligatory daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol* all fall into this excluded category. The *dam* — the blood — is likewise excluded, as it is the *matir* itself.

### Nesachim Debate

The *baraita* also excludes *minchat nesachim* — the flour, oil, and wine accompanying a *korban*. The rationale is that *nesachim* can be brought independently, even days after the animal sacrifice, and therefore they function as their own *matir*. The *mishna* records a parallel debate about *nesachim* that will be explored further: when brought together with the *korban*, is the blood of the *korban* their *matir*, or are they considered *matir es atzman* even in that circumstance?

## Rabbi Shimon View

### Outer Mizbe'ach

Rabbi Shimon introduces an additional criterion derived from the comparison to *shelamim*: not only must there be *matirin*, but the *matir* must be applied to the outer *mizbe'ach*, just as it is by *shelamim*. He states: *mah shelamim meyuchadim she-yeshnam al mizbe'ach ha-chizon ve-chayavin alav, af kol she-yeshnam al mizbe'ach ha-chizon chayavin alav mishum piggul*.

### Parim Exclusion

By this reasoning, *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* are categorically excluded from *piggul*. Although their *emurim* are burned on the outer *mizbe'ach*, the blood — the *matir* — is applied to the inner altar. Since the *matir* does not go on the outer *mizbe'ach* as it does by *shelamim*, Rabbi Shimon rules there is no *piggul* liability for these offerings at all: *yatz'u parim hanisrafim use'irim hanisrafim ho'il ve-ein al mizbe'ach ha-chizon ke-shelamim ein chayavin alav mishum piggul*. This ending of the *baraita* is nearly word-for-word identical to our *mishna*, and the parallel is instructive — the *mishna* codifies the conclusion while the *baraita* exposes the derivation.

## Bechor & K-P-K

### Bechor Query

The *Gemara* now examines the opening move of the *baraita* more closely. The *baraita* raised the possibility that *piggul* applies only to offerings eaten for two days and an intervening night, like *shelamim*. The *Gemara* asks: what offering could possibly fit that description other than a *shelamim* itself? The answer: *bechor* — the firstborn animal, which is also eaten for two days and one night and is in many ways treated similarly to a *shelamim*, though it is technically a distinct category of *korban*. So the narrow reading of the *baraita* would limit *piggul* to *shelamim* and *bechor* alone.

### K-P-K Rule

The *Gemara* then asks: if one wanted to broaden beyond just *bechor* through a *binyan av* or *mah mazinu* analogy, could that work? No — because *shelamim* have distinctive requirements not shared by other *korbanot*: the laying on of hands (*semicha*), *nesachim*, and the waving of the breast and thigh (*tenufas chaze ve-shok*). Since *shelamim* are not sufficiently similar to other offerings, an analogical extension would be vulnerable to refutation. Instead, the *baraita* relies on the *klal u-prat u-klal* principle applied to the verse *im he'achol ye'achel mibasar zevach shelamav*: *he'achol ye'achel* is a *klal*, *zevach shelamav* is a *prat*, and the closing form of the verse provides a second *klal* — thus broadening the scope to anything similar in kind to the *prat*.

### Juxtaposed Klalim

One might object: both *klalim* — the doubled language *he'achol ye'achel* — appear together before the *prat* of *zevach shelamav*, not framing it on both sides in the classic *klal u-prat u-klal* structure. The *Gemara* answers by citing the principle stated by Rava in the name of the sages of *Eretz Yisrael*: *kol makom she-ata moza shnei klalim ha-smuchim ze la-ze, hatel prat beineihem ve-donehu be-klal u-prat u-klal* — wherever you find two *klalim* adjacent to each other with a *prat* immediately following, you may rearrange them and apply the *klal u-prat u-klal* rule. The double language of *he'achol ye'achel* is thus legitimately read as two *klalim* enclosing the *prat* of *shelamim*, enabling the standard *klal u-prat u-klal* derivation that broadens *piggul* beyond *shelamim*.

## Log Shemen Debate

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *Gemara* identifies which Tanna authored the *baraita* cited earlier — specifically the portion that includes *log shemen shel metzora* within the scope of *piggul*. The *Gemara* states: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi*. A separate *baraita* confirms this: *log shemen shel metzora she-chayavin alav mishum piggul divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that *piggul* applies to the *log shemen* of the *metzora*. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that the blood of the *asham* accompanying the *metzora* serves as the *matir* for the oil, just as the blood of an animal sacrifice is the *matir* for the meat. Correspondingly, Rabbi Meir applies the same logic to *nesachim*: *nischei behemah chayavin aleihem mishum piggul mipnei she-dam ha-zevach matiran likarev* — since the blood of the animal is the *matir* that permits them to be offered, *piggul* applies to them as well.

### Rabbis Counter

The rabbis challenge Rabbi Meir on both counts. With respect to *nesachim*, they demonstrate that since a person may bring his animal sacrifice today and his *nesachim* up to ten days later, the blood of the *korban* cannot be the *matir* for the *nesachim* — they stand independently. Rabbi Meir acknowledges this: *af ani lo amarti ela be-ba'im im ha-zevach* — he limited his ruling to *nesachim* that are brought together with the *korban*. The rabbis, however, maintain that even when brought together, the fact that they could come separately means they are considered *matir es atzman* in all circumstances, and are therefore excluded from *piggul*. By the same logic, the rabbis argue, the *log shemen* should likewise be excluded.

This creates a problem for the *baraita*: it appears to include *log shemen* in *piggul* while excluding *minchat nesachim*. Since Rabbi Meir includes both and the rabbis exclude both, neither position can account for the *baraita*'s split verdict.

### Rav Yosef Answer

Rav Yosef resolves the difficulty by attributing the *baraita* to a third opinion: *ha mani Rabbi hi* — this is the position of *Rabbi* (Yehuda HaNasi). Rav Yosef explains that Rabbi holds *log shemen shel metzora matnosav sharu leih* — the sprinkling actions (*matanos*) of the *log shemen* are what render the remainder of the oil permissible. This means the *log shemen* itself has two components: the sprinkling (which functions as a *matir*) and the remaining oil (which the sprinkling permits). On this view, even when the *log shemen* is brought separately without any accompanying *korban*, it retains an internal *matir*-and-remainder structure.

The textual support for this position comes from a dispute about *meilah*. The rabbis hold: *mo'alin bo ad she-yizarek ha-dam* — the *log shemen* retains *meilah* status until the blood of the *asham* is sprinkled; once the blood is applied, *lo nehenin ve-lo mo'alin* — it may not be used for benefit but no longer carries *meilah*. Rabbi, however, holds: *mo'alin ad she-yiten matnosav* — *meilah* applies even after the blood is sprinkled, until the oil itself is sprinkled. Since the act of sprinkling the oil removes the *meilah* status, it follows that the sprinkling serves as a *matir* for the remaining oil. Accordingly, Rabbi holds that even when the *log shemen* comes separately, the oil has its own internal *matir* structure — the sprinkling — distinct from anything the blood provides.

This allows Rabbi to split the vote in a way neither Rabbi Meir nor the rabbis do: like the rabbis, Rabbi holds that *nesachim*, having no internal two-part structure, are excluded from *piggul*; but unlike the rabbis, Rabbi holds that the *log shemen* does have an internal *matir* — the sprinkling — and is therefore subject to *piggul*. This is a striking resolution.

### R. Yirmiah Object

When this answer of Rav Yosef was reported before Rabbi Yirmiah, his reaction was sharp: *gavra rabba k'Rav Yosef leima ki hai milta?* — would so great a scholar as Rav Yosef give such an answer? The objection is this: if the rationale for including *log shemen* in *piggul* is that the sprinkling of the oil is its *matir*, then even when the *log* is brought alone — completely separately from the *korban*, with no blood of an *asham* in the picture — the same reasoning should apply. The sprinkling still functions as the *matir* of the remaining oil in that case, since everyone agrees *matnosav sharu leih* — the sprinkling is what permits it even then. Yet the *baraita* unambiguously states that there is no *piggul* for *log shemen* when brought alone: *lo shemen shel metzora chayavin alav mishum piggul mipnei she-dam matiro lebehonot divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir says that when it comes with the *korban*, the blood permits it; and when asked about bringing them separately, he concedes *afilu lo amarti ela be-va'im im ha-asham* — I only said so when it comes with the *asham*. So even Rabbi Meir acknowledges there is no *piggul* when the *log* comes alone.

But that is precisely the problem with Rav Yosef's answer. When the *log* comes alone, the sprinkling is still the *matir* for the remaining oil — yet no one says *piggul* applies in that case. If the existence of an internal *matir* through the sprinkling were sufficient to create *piggul*, it should apply when the *log* comes alone as well. The failure of *piggul* to apply in that case suggests that the mere fact of the sprinkling being a *matir* does not suffice. The distinguishing factor, it seems, is that the *log* brought alone does not function as a genuine *korban* in the full sense — notably, none of the *log shemen* is placed directly on the *mizbe'ach*.

### Abaye Reconcile

Rabbi Yirmiah's preferred answer is to take the *baraita* as straightforwardly representing Rabbi Meir's position and simply to delete the exclusion of *nesachim* from the list: *ela amar Rabbi Yirmiah le'olam Rabbi Meir hi, ve-sami mikan nesachim* — erase that line. On this reading, the *baraita* is entirely consistent with Rabbi Meir: both *log shemen* and *nesachim* are included in *piggul* when brought with the *korban*.

Abaye, however, objects to any emendation: *le'olam lo tismi* — do not erase anything. Abaye instead proposes a reread of the *baraita*'s structure. The *baraita* is not drawing a distinction between *log shemen* (included in *piggul*) and *nesachim* (excluded). Rather, it is drawing a distinction between coming *with* the *korban* and coming *without* it. When it says there is *piggul* by the *log*, it means the *log* brought together with the *asham* — and the same applies to *nesachim* brought together with their *korban*. When it says there is no *piggul* by *nesachim*, it means *nesachim* brought separately — and the same applies to *log* brought separately. The *baraita* illustrates two cases of the same Tannaitic position: with the *korban*, the blood functions as *matir* and *piggul* applies; without the *korban*, there is no *matir* from the blood and no *piggul* — whether the item in question is *nesachim* or *log shemen*.

## Priestly Rights

### Chatas Ha-of

The *Gemara* notes that *chatat ha'of dama matir besarah la-kohanim* — the blood of the bird *chatat* renders its meat permitted to the *kohanim* — and asks: from where do we know that *kohanim* are entitled to eat the bird *chatat*? The Torah does not state this explicitly. The source is a *baraita* taught by Levi, derived from the concluding section of Parashat Korach. Rashi notes the aptness of the context: the challenge of Korach to the *kehunah* occasioned a comprehensive reaffirmation of all priestly entitlements at the end of that *parasha*. The verse (Bamidbar 18:9) states: *zeh yihyeh lecha mikodesh hakodashim min ha'esh — kol korbanam le-chol minchasam u-le-chol chatasam u-le-chol ashamam asher yashivu li kodesh kodashim lecha hu u-le-vanecha*. The *Gemara* proceeds to derive a series of priestly entitlements from the expansive language of this verse.

### Log in Kol

The word *kol korbanam* — "all their sacrifices" — is read as including the *log shemen shel metzora*. One might have thought the *log shemen* is excluded because *min ha'esh* — "from the fire" — implies that what the *kohanim* receive must be something placed on the *mizbe'ach*, and none of the *log shemen* goes on the *mizbe'ach* directly. The Torah therefore writes *kol korbanam* to include anything that is *nikrav* — brought near — and Rashi points out that the verse in the *metzora* passage explicitly uses the language *ve-hikriv oto le-asham ve-et log ha-shemen*, applying the root of *korban* to the oil itself.

### Minchas Omer

The phrase *le-chol minchasam* — "all their meal offerings" — is read to include *minchat ha'omer* and *minchat ha-kena'ot* (the *sotah mincha*). One might have excluded these because the eating of the *mincha* is normally justified by its being *asher kupar bahem* — something that achieves atonement — whereas *minchat ha'omer* comes to permit the new grain (*le-hatir*) and *minchat kena'ot* comes to clarify whether the woman sinned (*le-varer avon ka-atya*). Since neither achieves classic *kapara*, one might have thought the *kohanim* have no entitlement to them. The Torah therefore writes *le-chol minchasam* to include them.

### Chatasam Detail

The phrase *le-chol chatasam* includes *chatat ha'of* — the bird *sin-offering*. This is the case the *Gemara* began with. One might have excluded it on the grounds that it is technically a *neveilah*, since it is not slaughtered through a conventional *shechita* but through *melikat ha-oreph* — breaking the bird's neck from the back. The Torah nonetheless includes it within the *kohanim*'s entitlement.

### Asham Types

The phrase *le-chol ashamam* — "all their guilt offerings" — is read to include *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*. The *Gemara* notes that the entitlement to *asham metzora* is actually stated explicitly elsewhere; the function of *le-chol ashamam* here is therefore to equate *asham nazir* to *asham metzora*. The basis for possible exclusion: both are brought not for classical atonement but to change the person's halachic status — the *asham nazir* reinstates a new *nezirut* (the actual purification from *tumah* being accomplished through the *parah adumah*), and the *asham metzora* is part of the purification process. Since these are not standard *kappara* offerings, one might have thought the *kohanim* have no eating entitlement. The verse teaches otherwise.

### Gezel Ger

Finally, the phrase *asher yashivu li* — "that which they return to Me" — is identified as referring to *gezel ha-ger*: the principal and added fifth that must be returned when one steals from a convert who subsequently dies without heirs. Since a convert who dies without descendants has no one to receive restitution, the money goes to the *kohanim*. The *Gemara* emphasizes that this money is fully owned by the *kohanim* — *le-kohen hu she-yehei afilu ka-rishbasa isha* — it may even be used for *kiddushin*. This stands in sharp contrast to the meat of a *chatat* or other sacrificial portions, which the *kohanim* eat but do not own. As the *Gemara* elsewhere formulates it, the *kohanim* eating sacrificial meat are eating from *mishulchan gavoa* — God's table. The host permits them to partake; the food remains His. Accordingly, if a *kohen* were to use the meat of a *chatat* as the instrument of *kiddushin*, it would not take effect. The money of *gezel ha-ger*, by contrast, belongs fully to the *kohanim* and may be used freely.

## R. Eliezer Parim

### Outside Acts

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* containing the position of Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yose HaGlili: *piggul b'davar hane'aseh ba-chutz* — *piggul* in the context of *parim hanisrafim* applies only when both the *avoda* being performed and the improper thought concern acts and elements of the *chutz*, meaning the *azara*, the courtyard — as opposed to the *pnim*, the inner *heichal*. This is a more nuanced position than that of Rabbi Shimon, who categorically excludes *parim hanisrafim* from *piggul* altogether. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *piggul* does apply to *parim hanisrafim*, but only under a specific structural condition.

### Examples

The *baraita* provides a series of cases. If a *kohen* was standing in the *azara* performing *shechita* and declared that he was slaughtering in order to sprinkle blood on the inner altar tomorrow — *lo pigel*. Although the *avoda* being performed (the *shechita*) is in the *chutz*, the improper thought concerns an act of the *pnim* (the inner sprinkling). Since the thought is about something done inside, no *piggul* results.

Conversely, if the *kohen* was standing in the *heichal* performing the inner blood sprinkling and declared that he intends to burn the *emurim* or pour the *shirayim* tomorrow — again, *lo piggul*. The improper thought concerns outer acts, but the *avoda* being performed is an inner act. The two elements must match: both the act and the thought must concern the *chutz*.

The positive case: if the *kohen* was standing in the *azara* performing *shechita* and declared that he intends to pour the *shirayim* tomorrow or to burn the *emurim* tomorrow — *pigel*, *shemachshava ba-chutz bidavar na'aseh ba-chutz*. Here the *avoda* is outer (*shechita* in the *azara*) and the improper thought concerns outer acts (the pouring of *shirayim* and the burning of *emurim* on the outer *mizbe'ach*). Both conditions are satisfied and *piggul* results.

One nuance worth noting: whether the pouring of *shirayim* itself counts as a qualifying outer *avoda* depends on whether *shirayim* are *me'akev* — indispensable — for *chataot ha-penimiyot*; if they are not *me'akev*, they do not constitute a *piggul*-generating *avoda*. But at minimum, the *shechita* itself is performed outside, as are *kabalah* and *holachah*, giving multiple outer *avodos* through which *piggul* could be generated during the processing of a *par hanisraf*.

### Makish Source

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asks: what is the scriptural basis for this limiting condition? The answer lies in the verse by the *par kohen hamashiach*, one of the *parim hanisrafim*: *ka'asher yuram mi-shor zevach hashelamim* — the *emurim* are to be treated just as the *emurim* of a *shelamim*. This verse is puzzling: the *Torah* already listed all the *emurim* of the *par* in detail. After enumerating them, why does it add "just like the *shelamim*"? There is nothing left to teach about the *emurim* themselves. Therefore, the comparison must be doing something different. The *baraita* reads it as a *makish* — an analogy — connecting the *par kohen hamashiach* to the *shor zevach hashelamim*: just as by a *shelamim* the laws of *piggul* apply only when both the acts (*ma'asim*) and the thoughts (*machshavot*) concern the outer *mizbe'ach*, so too by the *par kohen hamashiach* — and by extension all *parim hanisrafim* — *piggul* applies only when both the *avoda* being performed and the wrongful intention concern acts on the outer *mizbe'ach*.

## R. Eliezer Parim (II)

### Halacha

Rav Nachman rules in the name of Rabba bar Avuha in the name of Rav: *halacha ke-Rabbi Eliezer she-amru mishum Rabbi Yose* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Eliezer. *Parim hanisrafim* are not categorically exempt from *piggul*, as Rabbi Shimon held; rather, *piggul* applies to them when both the *avoda* being performed and the improper intention concern matters of the outer *azara*.

## Pesak Theory

### Messianic Era

Rava immediately objects: *hilchesa le-mashiacha?* Is this a ruling on a law that can only become practically relevant in the messianic era, when the Temple is rebuilt? What is the force of pronouncing a definitive *halacha* on such a matter?

### Torah Study Value

Abaye responds: *ela me'atah kol shechitas kodashim lo lisni?* Should we abandon the study of the entire tractate of *Zevachim*? None of it is currently applicable. *Ela derosh ve-kabel sachar* — study it and receive the reward; *hachi nami derosh ve-kabel sachar* — here too, study it and receive the reward. Torah study is intrinsically valuable regardless of immediate practical application.

### Relevance Concern

Rava refines his objection: *halacha kamina* — what troubles me is not the study of these laws but the specific act of rendering a formal *psak halacha*. Issuing a definitive ruling implies practical relevance. When one says "the *halacha* is like Rabbi Eliezer," the natural implication is that this determination matters — that someone might rely on it or be affected by it. If there is no context in which it could ever be applied, what does it mean to issue such a ruling? The resolution offered — that one is simply studying and receiving reward — does not entirely satisfy, because it does not explain the *phrasing* of the *halacha* as a formal ruling rather than as a theoretical position.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* addresses this tension by cataloguing a number of places in the *Gemara* where *halacha le-mashiacha*-type rulings are issued on matters exclusive to *mikdash* practice, without the *Gemara* raising any objection. *Tosafot* observes that in most of those cases, a practical application can be identified that makes the ruling relevant even today. For the handful of cases where no such application can be found, *Tosafot* offers a distinct resolution: perhaps the particular concern in our passage is not merely that the law is Temple-era, but that *piggul* by definition only arises when a *kohen* has acted wrongly — has harbored an improper intention. Issuing a *psak halacha* about a law that presupposes transgression, which is itself applicable only in the time of the Temple, invites a sharper objection than ruling on a straightforward Temple-era procedure. That combination — inapplicable in practice and arising only through wrongdoing — may be what Rava finds difficult to justify as a formal *psak*, even if the study itself remains entirely worthwhile.
