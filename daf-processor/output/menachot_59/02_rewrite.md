# Menachot 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oil & Frankincense

### Four Categories

The *mishnah* at the top of 59a opens in the middle of the *perek*'s broader discussion of the physical constitution of *menachot*. The previous *mishnah* had established that all *menachot* are kneaded with lukewarm water and that one must take great care to prevent them from becoming *chametz*. Our *mishnah* now turns to features that distinguish one *mincha* from another, beginning with the requirements of oil and frankincense. Four categories are enumerated: *menachot* requiring both oil and frankincense, those requiring oil but not frankincense, those requiring frankincense but not oil, and those requiring neither.

### Require Both

The *mishnah* identifies the following as requiring both oil and frankincense: *minchat hasolet*, *hamachavat*, *hamarcheshet*, *hachalot*, and *harikikin* — that is, all the standard voluntary *menachot* described at the opening of Vayikra, whether of plain flour, a pan, a deep pan, loaves, or wafers. To these the *mishnah* adds *minchat kohanim* — a *mincha* brought by a *kohen*, which shares the same physical composition as a standard *mincha* even though the entire offering is burnt on the *mizbeach* rather than having a *komets* separated. Similarly included are *minchat kohen mashiach*, the *chavitin* brought by the anointed *kohen* in two halves daily; *minchat ovdei kochavim*, since non-Jews may bring *korbanot*; *minchat nashim*, since nothing in the *pesukim* suggests any difference between a *korban* brought by a man or a woman; and *minchat ha'omer*, the *mincha* of the *omer*, which likewise requires both oil and frankincense.

### Nesachim Category

By contrast, *minchat nesachim* — the flour-and-oil *mincha* accompanying an animal *korban*, together with the wine libation — requires oil but not frankincense. The reasoning, as the *Gemara* will develop, is that the *minchat nesachim* is not an independent offering but rather an accompaniment to the animal *korban*, and the *pesukim* describing it mention only flour and oil, never frankincense.

### Lechem Hapanim

The *lechem hapanim*, the showbread arranged on the *shulchan* in the *Heichal*, falls into the inverse category: it requires frankincense but not oil. The bread itself is made of plain flour and water. The frankincense, placed in two small vessels on the *shulchan*, is what is offered on the *mizbeach*, while the bread itself is eaten by the *kohanim*. That this most prominent of Temple fixtures contains no oil is striking, and the *Gemara* will address it directly.

### Neither Oil/Frankinc.

The final category — neither oil nor frankincense — comprises *shtei halechem*, *minchat chotei*, and *minchat kenaot*. The *shtei halechem* of Shavuot is made of flour and water and, unlike the *lechem hapanim*, has no frankincense offered alongside it; the *kivsei atzeret* are offered separately, and since the *shtei halechem* itself is *chametz*, nothing directly attached to it is burnt on the *mizbeach*. The *minchat chotei*, the *mincha* variant within the *korban oleh ve'yored* for certain sins, explicitly lacks both oil and frankincense, since these beautifying and fragrant additions would be incongruous with an offering of atonement. And the *minchat kenaot*, brought on behalf of a woman suspected of adultery, is likewise explicitly stripped of both, as the Torah states: *lo yiten aleha shemen v'lo yasim aleha levonah ki minchat kenaot hi minchat zikaron mazkeres avon* — "he shall place on it neither oil nor frankincense, for it is a *minchat kenaot*, a *mincha* of remembrance, recalling sin."

## Rav Papa's Ruling

### Rav Papa Intro

The *Gemara* opens this section with a statement of Rav Papa: *kol heicha d'tnan esser tnan* — "whenever we state [a type of *mincha*] in the *mishnah*, we mean ten [*chalos*]." The point is that each type of *mincha* mentioned in the *mishnah* is brought in units of ten.

### Two Explanations

Rashi offers two distinct explanations of this remark. The first is straightforward: when the *mishnah* enumerates types of *menachot* — *machavas*, *marcheshes*, *chalos*, *rekikin* — it means that each type is brought as ten units. The second explanation is more ingenious: count the *menachot* listed in the *mishnah* as requiring both oil and frankincense, and you arrive at exactly ten. *Solet* is one; *machavas*, *marcheshes*, *chalos*, *rekikin* bring the total to five; *minchat kohanim* is six, *minchat kohen mashiach* seven, *minchat ovdei kochavim* eight, *minchat nashim* nine, and *minchat ha'omer* ten.

### Vs. Rabbi Shimon

Either way, Rav Papa's statement is directed against the position of Rabbi Shimon. A later *mishnah* records a dispute about the *minchat ma'afeh tanur*, the oven-baked *mincha*. The Torah there mentions both *chalos matzos belulos bashemen* and *rekikei matzos meshuchim bashemen*, presenting both loaves and wafers. Rabbi Shimon reads this as permitting a mixture: one could bring half *chalos* and half *rekikin*, or any proportional combination including an equal split of five and five. The *Hachamim* disagree, holding that the Torah presents two discrete options — either all ten are *chalos* or all ten are *rekikin* — with no mixing permitted. According to Rabbi Shimon, there would be an eleventh valid category: a *mincha* comprising five *chalos* and five *rekikin*. Rav Papa's ruling — whether understood as "ten of each type" or "ten types total" — thus establishes that our *mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Shimon.

## Oil Derivation

### Pasuk ונתת שמן

The *Gemara* now turns to the Talmudic derivation underlying the categories established by the *mishnah*, beginning with the requirement of oil. The *baraita* cites the *pasuk* in Vayikra 2:14-15, which describes the *minchat ha'omer*: *v'nasata aleha shemen v'samta aleha levonah mincha hi* — "you shall place on it oil and put on it frankincense, it is a *mincha*." From the word *aleha* — "on it" — the *Gemara* derives: oil on *this* and not on the *lechem hapanim*. The *lechem hapanim* is nowhere explicitly told to receive oil, and the word *aleha* functions as a limiting term, confining the oil requirement to the *menachot* under immediate discussion.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

One might, however, have reasoned otherwise. The *Gemara* voices a *kal vachomer*: if the *minchat nesachim*, which does not require frankincense, nevertheless requires oil, then the *lechem hapanim*, which does require frankincense and is therefore in some sense "weightier," should certainly require oil as well. If requirements are assumed to stack linearly — that a *mincha* possessing one element should possess the other as well — then the *lechem hapanim*, which already has the frankincense the *minchat nesachim* lacks, ought also to have oil.

### Talmud Lomar

The *Talmud lomar* rejects this inference: *aleha shemen* — on *it*, oil, and not on the *lechem hapanim*. The word *aleha* forecloses the *kal vachomer* by insisting that the requirement remains where it is stated and does not travel to contexts where it is not. The *lechem hapanim* does not mention oil; it does not receive oil.

## Frankincense Derivation

### Pasuk לבונה

The same *pasuk* then yields the parallel derivation for frankincense: *v'nasata aleha levonah* — "you shall place on it frankincense." Here *aleha* likewise functions as a limiting term: frankincense goes on *this* — the *omer* and the *menachot* explicitly mentioned — and not on the *minchat nesachim*, which nowhere mentions frankincense. The *Gemara* states: *aleha levonah v'lo le-minchat nesachim levonah* — the frankincense requirement stays with the *menachot* where it is spoken, and may not be extended to those where it is silent.

### Kal Vachomer (Frankinc.)

Again, a *kal vachomer* might have suggested otherwise. We have just learned from a *pasuk* that the *lechem hapanim* receives no oil and yet receives frankincense. If the *lechem hapanim*, which lacks oil, still gets frankincense, then the *minchat nesachim*, which does have oil and is therefore in some sense more substantial, should certainly receive frankincense as well. The logic is the same as before: if the lesser has the feature, the greater should too.

### Limit Frankincense

The *Talmud lomar* again closes this off: *aleha levonah v'lo le-minchat nesachim levonah*. Keep the frankincense requirement where the Torah placed it; do not extend it through inference to the *minchat nesachim*. The *Gemara* then turns to the final word of the *pasuk*, *mincha hi* — "it is a *mincha*." The word *mincha* functions inclusively, extending the oil and frankincense requirement to the *mincha* brought on the eighth day of the *miluim*, which was not otherwise mentioned. The word *hi* ("it") then functions as a limitation, excluding the *shtei halechem*, for which neither oil nor frankincense was mentioned. The principle throughout is consistent: read the *pesukim* as they stand. Where oil is not mentioned — as by the *lechem hapanim* — do not add it. Where frankincense is not mentioned — as by the *minchat nesachim* — do not add it. Where neither is mentioned — as by the *shtei halechem* — add neither.

## Minchat Kohanim

### Question Posed

Having established these derivations, the *Gemara* begins to interrogate them. If *aleha shemen* is a limiting term that excludes the *lechem hapanim* from receiving oil, why should it not equally exclude the *minchat kohanim*? After all, the Torah does not explicitly say oil by the *minchat kohanim* either. If the *pasuk* is being read as an exclusion, perhaps it is the *minchat kohanim* that should be on the outside.

### Include Position

The *Gemara* answers: *mistabra minchat kohanim hava lei le-ribuy* — it is more reasonable to assume that the *minchat kohanim* falls within the included group of standard *menachot* and that the *lechem hapanim* is what stands apart. The question is which side of the line each offering belongs on, and the *Gemara* now marshals the evidence.

### Shared Features

A series of similarities between *minchat kohanim* and the standard *menachot* is adduced. First, *isaron*: both a standard *mincha* and a *minchat kohen* use an *isaron* of *solet*, whereas the *lechem hapanim* uses a substantially larger measure. Second, *kli*: both are prepared using a *kli sharet*, a sacred vessel, whereas the *lechem hapanim* is sanctified in the oven or on the *shulchan*. Third, *chutz*: both the standard *menachot* and the *minchat kohen* are performed in the *azarah*, outside the *Heichal* proper, whereas the *lechem hapanim* is placed inside the *Heichal* itself. Fourth, *tzura*: both become invalid (*pasul*) by the following morning, losing their valid form overnight — the *lechem hapanim* by contrast remains on the *shulchan* throughout the entire week. Fifth, *hagasha*: both a standard *mincha* and a *minchat kohen* undergo *hagasha*, the touching of the *mincha* to the *mizbeach*, which is not done for the *lechem hapanim*. Sixth, *ishim*: some part of both goes onto the *mizbeach* fire — the *komets* of a standard *mincha* and the entire *minchat kohen* — whereas of the *lechem hapanim* itself nothing is placed on the *mizbeach*; only the *levona* from its accompanying vessels is burnt.

### Counter Position

The *Gemara* pushes back: *adaraba*, the opposite. Perhaps it is the *lechem hapanim* that should be included with the standard *menachot*, and the *minchat kohanim* that should stand apart. On a different set of criteria, the *lechem hapanim* resembles the *minchat ha'omer* — the very *mincha* whose *pasuk* is under discussion — more closely than the *minchat kohanim* does.

### LH Similarities

Several points of comparison are enumerated. First, *tzibur*: the *lechem hapanim* is a *korban tzibur*, a communal offering, as is the *minchat ha'omer*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always a *korban yachid*, an individual's offering. Second, *chova*: the *lechem hapanim* is obligatory, as are the *omer* and other fixed *menachot*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always a voluntary, free-will offering. Third, *tumah*: the *lechem hapanim* and the communal *menachot* are subject to the principle of *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* — communal obligations override impurity and may be offered even in a state of *tumah* — whereas the *minchat kohanim*, being a *yachid* offering and always voluntary, would never override *tumah*. Fourth, *achilah*: the *lechem hapanim* is eaten by the *kohanim*, as are the portions of standard *menachot* not burnt on the *mizbeach*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is entirely burnt. Fifth, *pigul*: the *lechem hapanim* and standard *menachot* can become *pigul*, since they have a *matir* — the *komets* in the case of a standard *mincha*, and the *levona* in the case of the *lechem hapanim* — whereas the *minchat kohanim*, being entirely burnt with no remaining portion, has no *matir* and therefore cannot become *pigul*. Sixth, *Shabbat*: the *omer* and other obligatory communal *menachot* override *Shabbat*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* does not.

### Nefesh Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves the impasse by identifying *nefesh* as the decisive criterion. The opening *pasuk* of the *mincha* laws in Vayikra 2:1 reads: *v'nefesh ki takriv korban mincha l'Hashem solet yihiyeh korbano v'yatzak aleha shemen v'natan aleha levona* — "when a soul brings a *mincha* offering to Hashem, it shall be of *solet*, and he shall pour oil on it and place frankincense on it." The word *nefesh* — "soul," denoting an individual — frames the entire discussion of oil and frankincense as applying to personal, individual offerings. Since the *minchat kohanim* is always the *korban* of a private individual, it falls naturally within this framework. The *lechem hapanim*, a communal offering brought by the *tzibur*, stands outside this framework, and that is why it does not receive oil. Rashi further notes that even beyond the *pasuk* of the *omer*, which has been the textual focus, the very first *pasuk* of the *mincha* laws leads with *nefesh* and immediately prescribes oil and frankincense, making clear that oil and frankincense are the hallmarks of the individual *mincha*. The *minchat kohanim* is included; the *lechem hapanim* is excluded.

## Minchat Nesachim

### Question Posed

The *Gemara* now applies the same structure to the frankincense derivation. If *aleha levonah* is limiting and excludes the *minchat nesachim* from receiving frankincense, why should it not instead exclude the *minchat kohanim*? The *minchat kohanim* also does not have frankincense explicitly mentioned in its own *parasha*. Perhaps it is the *minchat kohanim* that should be on the outside.

### Exclude Position

The *Gemara* responds: *mistabra minchat kohanim hava lei le-ribuy* — it is more reasonable to keep the *minchat kohanim* inside the standard group, and to read the *pasuk* as excluding the *minchat nesachim*. The evidence is again marshaled comparatively.

### MK Differences

A series of ways in which the *minchat kohanim* resembles standard *menachot* — and the *minchat nesachim* does not — is presented. *Isaron*: the *minchat kohanim* uses one *isaron* of flour, as does a standard *mincha*, whereas the *minchat nesachim* varies in quantity depending on the animal it accompanies. *Balul*: the *minchat kohanim* is described as mixed with oil (*belulah bashemen*), the same language used for standard *menachot*, whereas the *minchat nesachim* mentions oil but not this mixing formulation. *B'log*: the *minchat kohanim* uses only a *log* of oil, as do standard *menachot*, whereas the *minchat nesachim* requires multiple *logim* depending on the animal. *Mugash*: the *minchat kohanim* undergoes *hagasha*, the presentation to the *mizbeach*, as do standard *menachot*, whereas *hagasha* is not performed for the *minchat nesachim*. *Biglal atzmo*: the *minchat kohanim* is brought in its own right as an independent offering, whereas the *minchat nesachim* is brought only as an accompaniment to a primary animal *korban*.

### MN Similarities

The *Gemara* counters: *adaraba*, one could argue that the *minchat nesachim* should be on the inside, and it is the *minchat kohanim* that should be excluded.

### MN Characteristics

*Tzibur*: the *minchat nesachim* accompanies communal *korbanot* such as the *korban tamid* and *musaf*, just as the *omer* is a communal *korban*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always a private offering. *Chova*: the *minchat nesachim* accompanying obligatory *korbanot* is itself obligatory, just as the *omer* is obligatory, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always voluntary. *Tumah*: since the *minchat nesachim* accompanies *korbanot* that can be brought in a state of *tumah* when the community is impure, the *nesachim* themselves can be brought in that state — something never applicable to the *minchat kohanim*. *Shabbat*: the *minchat nesachim* is brought on *Shabbat* when it accompanies the *korban tamid* or *musaf*; the *minchat kohanim* is never brought on *Shabbat*.

### Nefesh Resolution

The resolution is the same as before: *mistabra nefesh*. The opening *pesukim* of the *mincha* laws frame oil and frankincense as characteristics of the personal, individual *nefesh* offering. Any *korban* that is personal — including the *minchat kohanim* — is thus naturally included in the requirement of oil and frankincense. What is excluded are offerings that fall outside that personal framework: the *minchat nesachim*, which is not an independent *nefesh* offering but an accompaniment, and the communal *shtei halechem*, *lechem hapanim*, and similar offerings. The various *pasuk* words — *aleha*, *aleha*, *hi* — simply reinforce: do not extend what is not written, and read each *mincha* according to its own *pesukim*.

## Shtei Halechem

### Question Posed

The *Gemara* applies the identical framework a third time, now regarding *shtei halechem*. If *hi* is coming to exclude *shtei halechem* from receiving oil and frankincense, why should it not instead exclude the *minchat kohanim*? Once again, the *minchat kohanim* does not have oil and frankincense explicitly stated in its own *parasha*.

### Exclude Position

The *Gemara* answers: *mistabra minchat kohanim hava lei le-ribuy* — it is more reasonable to include the *minchat kohanim* with the standard *menachot* and to read the exclusion as applying to the *shtei halechem*. The *minchat kohanim* bears numerous characteristics that align it with standard *menachot* rather than with *shtei halechem*.

### MK Differences

*Isaron*: the *minchat kohanim* and standard *menachot* are each brought as an *isaron* of flour, whereas the *shtei halechem* is not measured in *esronim* in the same way. *Kli*: standard *menachot* and *minchat kohanim* are kneaded or prepared in a *kli sharet*, whereas the *shtei halechem* — like the *lechem hapanim* — is sanctified in the *tanur*, and indeed it is *pasul* if kneaded in a *kli sharet*. *Matza*: all standard *menachot* are *matza*, and the *minchat kohanim* is likewise *matza*, whereas the *shtei halechem* is explicitly *chametz*. *Ve'etzem*: the *minchat kohanim* and standard *menachot* are each brought independently, *biglal atzmo*, on their own account, whereas the *shtei halechem* comes together with the *kivsei atzeret*. *Hagasha*: standard *menachot* and *minchat kohanim* undergo *hagasha*, whereas this is not performed for the *shtei halechem*. *Ishim*: some part of both a standard *mincha* and a *minchat kohanim* — whether the *komets* or the entire offering — is placed on the *mizbeach* fire, whereas nothing of the *shtei halechem* itself goes on the *mizbeach*.

### SH Similarities

The *Gemara* again counters: *adaraba*, one could argue that the *shtei halechem* is in fact the offering most similar to the *omer* — the very *mincha* whose *pasuk* anchors this entire derivation — and should therefore be included.

### SH Characteristics

A lengthy and detailed enumeration follows. *Tzibur*: the *shtei halechem* is a *korban tzibur*, as is the *omer*. *Chova*: both are obligatory. *Docheh tumah* and *docheh Shabbat*: both override impurity and *Shabbat* by virtue of being obligatory communal offerings. *Achilah*: both the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* are eaten — standard *menachot* have a portion eaten by the *kohanim* — whereas the *minchat kohanim* is entirely burnt. *Pigul*: both the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* have a *matir* and can therefore become *pigul*; the *minchat kohanim*, being entirely burnt, cannot. *Tnufa*: both the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* are waved (*tenufa*), whereas ordinary *menachot* are not waved, and the *minchat kohanim* is not waved. *Ba'aretz*: both must come from grain grown in *Eretz Yisrael*. *Bizman*: both come at a fixed, specific time — the *omer* on Pesach, the *shtei halechem* on Shavuot. *Chadash*: both come from the new grain. Indeed, the *shtei halechem* and the *minchat ha'omer* are genuinely parallel offerings: one from barley, one from wheat; one permitting the new grain to the populace outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the other permitting the new grain to be brought as an offering within it. The *Gemara* notes that the list of similarities here is actually more numerous (*v'hani nefishin*) than in the previous cases.

### Nefesh Final

Nevertheless, the conclusion is the same: *mistabra nefesh*. Despite the remarkable convergence between *shtei halechem* and *minchat ha'omer*, *nefesh* is the decisive factor. Oil and frankincense are the markers of the individual, personal *mincha* — the offering of a *nefesh*. The *shtei halechem* is not a *nefesh* offering; it is communal, and frankincense and oil are not mentioned in its *parasha*. Therefore it does not receive them.

The governing principle of this entire sugya is thus made fully explicit: the various words *aleha*, *aleha*, and *hi* throughout the *pasuk* are each saying the same thing — do not go beyond what the Torah states. Where oil is not mentioned, do not add it; where frankincense is not mentioned, do not add it; where neither is mentioned, add neither. The only reason the *Gemara* raises the question of *minchat kohanim* at all — in each of the three iterations — is that the *minchat kohanim* also lacks explicit mention of oil and frankincense in its own *parasha*, and one might therefore have thought it should be excluded along with *lechem hapanim*, *minchat nesachim*, and *shtei halechem*. The answer in every case is *nefesh*: the *minchat kohanim* is the offering of an individual *nefesh* and is therefore naturally drawn into the group of personal *menachot* that receive oil and frankincense, while the non-*nefesh* offerings remain outside.
