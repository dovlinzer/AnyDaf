# Menachot 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oil & Frankincense

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יֵשׁ טְעוּנוֹת שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה, שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא לְבוֹנָה, לְבוֹנָה וְלֹא שֶׁמֶן, לֹא לְבוֹנָה וְלֹא שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There are** four types of meal offerings: Those **that require** both **oil and frankincense,** those that require **oil but not frankincense,** those that require **frankincense but not oil,** and those that require **neither frankincense nor oil.**

The *mishnah* at the top of 59a opens in the middle of the *perek*'s broader discussion of the physical constitution of *menachot*. The previous *mishnah* had established that all *menachot* are kneaded with lukewarm water and that one must take great care to prevent them from becoming *chametz*. Our *mishnah* now turns to features that distinguish one *mincha* from another, beginning with the requirements of oil and frankincense. Four categories are enumerated: *menachot* requiring both oil and frankincense, those requiring oil but not frankincense, those requiring frankincense but not oil, and those requiring neither.

### Require Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ טְעוּנוֹת שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה: מִנְחַת הַסּוֹלֶת, וְהַמַּחֲבַת, וְהַמַּרְחֶשֶׁת, וְהַחַלּוֹת, וְהָרְקִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: **And these** are the meal offerings that **require** both **oil and frankincense: The fine-flour meal offering,** as it is stated: "And he shall pour oil upon it, and put frankincense thereon" (Leviticus 2:1); **the** meal offering prepared in **a pan** (see Leviticus 2:5–6); **the** meal offering prepared in **a deep pan** (see Leviticus 2:7–10); **and the** meal offering baked in an oven, which can be brought in the form of **loaves or** in the form of **wafers** (see Leviticus 2:4).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, מִנְחַת גּוֹי, מִנְחַת נָשִׁים, מִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Additional meal offerings that require both oil and frankincense are **the meal offering of priests; the meal offering of the anointed priest,** i.e., the griddle-cake offering brought by the High Priest every day, half in the morning and half in the evening; **the meal offering of a gentile; a meal offering** brought **by women;** and **the *omer* meal offering** (see Leviticus 23:15).

The *mishnah* identifies the following as requiring both oil and frankincense: *minchat hasolet*, *hamachavat*, *hamarcheshet*, *hachalot*, and *harikikin* — that is, all the standard voluntary *menachot* described at the opening of Vayikra, whether of plain flour, a pan, a deep pan, loaves, or wafers. To these the *mishnah* adds *minchat kohanim* — a *mincha* brought by a *kohen*, which shares the same physical composition as a standard *mincha* even though the entire offering is burnt on the *mizbeach* rather than having a *komets* separated. Similarly included are *minchat kohen mashiach*, the *chavitin* brought by the anointed *kohen* in two halves daily; *minchat ovdei kochavim*, since non-Jews may bring *korbanot*; *minchat nashim*, since nothing in the *pesukim* suggests any difference between a *korban* brought by a man or a woman; and *minchat ha'omer*, the *mincha* of the *omer*, which likewise requires both oil and frankincense.

### Nesachim Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחַת נְסָכִים טְעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן, וְאֵין טְעוּנָה לְבוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The meal offering** brought with **libations** that accompany burnt offerings and peace offerings **requires oil but does not require frankincense.**

By contrast, *minchat nesachim* — the flour-and-oil *mincha* accompanying an animal *korban*, together with the wine libation — requires oil but not frankincense. The reasoning, as the *Gemara* will develop, is that the *minchat nesachim* is not an independent offering but rather an accompaniment to the animal *korban*, and the *pesukim* describing it mention only flour and oil, never frankincense.

### Lechem Hapanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים טָעוּן לְבוֹנָה, וְאֵין טָעוּן שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The shewbread requires frankincense but does not require oil.**

The *lechem hapanim*, the showbread arranged on the *shulchan* in the *Heichal*, falls into the inverse category: it requires frankincense but not oil. The bread itself is made of plain flour and water. The frankincense, placed in two small vessels on the *shulchan*, is what is offered on the *mizbeach*, while the bread itself is eaten by the *kohanim*. That this most prominent of Temple fixtures contains no oil is striking, and the *Gemara* will address it directly.

### Neither Oil/Frankinc.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא, וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת – אֵין טְעוּנִין לֹא שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא לְבוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The two loaves** brought on the festival of *Shavuot* (see Leviticus 23:17), **the meal offering of a sinner, and the meal offering of jealousy** brought by a *sota* **require neither oil nor frankincense.** The two loaves do not require oil or frankincense because these additions are not mentioned with regard to it. The meal offering of a sinner does not require them, as it is written: "He shall not put oil upon it, neither shall he give any frankincense upon it; for it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 5:11). With regard to the meal offering brought by a *sota*, it is similarly written: "He shall pour no oil upon it, nor give frankincense upon it, for it is a meal offering of jealousy, a meal offering of memorial, bringing iniquity to remembrance" (Numbers 5:15).

The final category — neither oil nor frankincense — comprises *shtei halechem*, *minchat chotei*, and *minchat kenaot*. The *shtei halechem* of Shavuot is made of flour and water and, unlike the *lechem hapanim*, has no frankincense offered alongside it; the *kivsei atzeret* are offered separately, and since the *shtei halechem* itself is *chametz*, nothing directly attached to it is burnt on the *mizbeach*. The *minchat chotei*, the *mincha* variant within the *korban oleh ve'yored* for certain sins, explicitly lacks both oil and frankincense, since these beautifying and fragrant additions would be incongruous with an offering of atonement. And the *minchat kenaot*, brought on behalf of a woman suspected of adultery, is likewise explicitly stripped of both, as the Torah states: *lo yiten aleha shemen v'lo yasim aleha levonah ki minchat kenaot hi minchat zikaron mazkeres avon* — "he shall place on it neither oil nor frankincense, for it is a *minchat kenaot*, a *mincha* of remembrance, recalling sin."

## Rav Papa's Ruling

### Rav Papa Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כֹּל הֵיכָא דִּתְנַן עֶשֶׂר – תְּנַן, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין יָבִיא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Pappa stated** a principle with regard to all the *mishnayot* in tractate *Menaḥot*: **Anywhere that we learned** in a mishna that one brings a meal offering, **we learned** that one must bring **ten** items of the same type, either loaves or wafers. The Gemara explains: This statement of Rav Pappa serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, who says:** One who takes a vow to bring a meal offering baked in an oven must bring ten items. If he wishes, he may bring ten loaves or ten wafers, and if he wishes **he may bring half** of them as **loaves and** the other **half** as **wafers.** Rav Pappa **teaches us** that the *tanna* of the mishna maintains **that** one may **not** do so; all ten must be of the same type.

The *Gemara* opens this section with a statement of Rav Papa: *kol heicha d'tnan esser tnan* — "whenever we state [a type of *mincha*] in the *mishnah*, we mean ten [*chalos*]." The point is that each type of *mincha* mentioned in the *mishnah* is brought in units of ten.

### Two Explanations

Rashi offers two distinct explanations of this remark. The first is straightforward: when the *mishnah* enumerates types of *menachot* — *machavas*, *marcheshes*, *chalos*, *rekikin* — it means that each type is brought as ten units. The second explanation is more ingenious: count the *menachot* listed in the *mishnah* as requiring both oil and frankincense, and you arrive at exactly ten. *Solet* is one; *machavas*, *marcheshes*, *chalos*, *rekikin* bring the total to five; *minchat kohanim* is six, *minchat kohen mashiach* seven, *minchat ovdei kochavim* eight, *minchat nashim* nine, and *minchat ha'omer* ten.

### Vs. Rabbi Shimon

Either way, Rav Papa's statement is directed against the position of Rabbi Shimon. A later *mishnah* records a dispute about the *minchat ma'afeh tanur*, the oven-baked *mincha*. The Torah there mentions both *chalos matzos belulos bashemen* and *rekikei matzos meshuchim bashemen*, presenting both loaves and wafers. Rabbi Shimon reads this as permitting a mixture: one could bring half *chalos* and half *rekikin*, or any proportional combination including an equal split of five and five. The *Hachamim* disagree, holding that the Torah presents two discrete options — either all ten are *chalos* or all ten are *rekikin* — with no mixing permitted. According to Rabbi Shimon, there would be an eleventh valid category: a *mincha* comprising five *chalos* and five *rekikin*. Rav Papa's ruling — whether understood as "ten of each type" or "ten types total" — thus establishes that our *mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Shimon.

## Oil Derivation

### Pasuk ונתת שמן

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנָתַתָּ עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן״ – וְלֹא עַל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the *omer* meal offering requires both oil and frankincense. With regard to this meal offering, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall put oil upon it** and lay frankincense upon it; it is a meal offering" (Leviticus 2:15). From this it can be inferred: One must put oil specifically "upon it," **but** one does **not** place **oil upon the shewbread.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the Talmudic derivation underlying the categories established by the *mishnah*, beginning with the requirement of oil. The *baraita* cites the *pasuk* in Vayikra 2:14-15, which describes the *minchat ha'omer*: *v'nasata aleha shemen v'samta aleha levonah mincha hi* — "you shall place on it oil and put on it frankincense, it is a *mincha*." From the word *aleha* — "on it" — the *Gemara* derives: oil on *this* and not on the *lechem hapanim*. The *lechem hapanim* is nowhere explicitly told to receive oil, and the word *aleha* functions as a limiting term, confining the oil requirement to the *menachot* under immediate discussion.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל – וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה מִנְחַת נְסָכִים שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לְבוֹנָה טְעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן, לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁטָּעוּן לְבוֹנָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטָּעוּן שֶׁמֶן!
>
> **Translation:** **As** one **might** have thought: Could **this not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference,** to conclude that the shewbread should require oil? The *baraita* explains: **And if the meal offering** brought with **libations** that accompany burnt offerings and peace offerings, **which does not require frankincense,** nevertheless **requires oil,** then with regard to **the shewbread,** for which the *halakha* is more stringent in **that** it **requires frankincense, is it not logical that** it should also **require oil?**

One might, however, have reasoned otherwise. The *Gemara* voices a *kal vachomer*: if the *minchat nesachim*, which does not require frankincense, nevertheless requires oil, then the *lechem hapanim*, which does require frankincense and is therefore in some sense "weightier," should certainly require oil as well. If requirements are assumed to stack linearly — that a *mincha* possessing one element should possess the other as well — then the *lechem hapanim*, which already has the frankincense the *minchat nesachim* lacks, ought also to have oil.

### Talmud Lomar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עָלֶיהָ״ – עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן, וְלֹא עַל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states "upon it,"** which indicates that one places **oil upon it,** the *omer* meal offering, **but** one does **not** place **oil upon the shewbread.**

The *Talmud lomar* rejects this inference: *aleha shemen* — on *it*, oil, and not on the *lechem hapanim*. The word *aleha* forecloses the *kal vachomer* by insisting that the requirement remains where it is stated and does not travel to contexts where it is not. The *lechem hapanim* does not mention oil; it does not receive oil.

## Frankincense Derivation

### Pasuk לבונה

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְשַׂמְתָּ עָלֶיהָ לְבֹנָה״ – עָלֶיהָ לְבוֹנָה, וְלֹא עַל מִנְחַת נְסָכִים לְבוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the phrase **"and lay frankincense upon it"** indicates that one must place **frankincense "upon it," but** one does **not** place **frankincense upon the meal offering** brought with **libations.**

The same *pasuk* then yields the parallel derivation for frankincense: *v'nasata aleha levonah* — "you shall place on it frankincense." Here *aleha* likewise functions as a limiting term: frankincense goes on *this* — the *omer* and the *menachot* explicitly mentioned — and not on the *minchat nesachim*, which nowhere mentions frankincense. The *Gemara* states: *aleha levonah v'lo le-minchat nesachim levonah* — the frankincense requirement stays with the *menachot* where it is spoken, and may not be extended to those where it is silent.

### Kal Vachomer (Frankinc.)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁאֵינוֹ טָעוּן שֶׁמֶן – טָעוּן לְבוֹנָה, מִנְחַת נְסָכִים שֶׁטְּעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטְּעוּנָה לְבוֹנָה?
>
> **Translation:** **As** one **might** have thought: Could **this not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference? And if the shewbread,** for **which** the *halakha* is more lenient than the meal offering brought with libations in that it **does not require oil,** nevertheless **requires frankincense,** then concerning **the meal offering** brought with **libations, which** does **require oil, is it not logical that** it should also **require frankincense?**

Again, a *kal vachomer* might have suggested otherwise. We have just learned from a *pasuk* that the *lechem hapanim* receives no oil and yet receives frankincense. If the *lechem hapanim*, which lacks oil, still gets frankincense, then the *minchat nesachim*, which does have oil and is therefore in some sense more substantial, should certainly receive frankincense as well. The logic is the same as before: if the lesser has the feature, the greater should too.

### Limit Frankincense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עָלֶיהָ״ – עָלֶיהָ לְבוֹנָה, וְלֹא עַל מִנְחַת נְסָכִים לְבוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states "upon it,"** to indicate that one places **frankincense upon it,** the *omer* meal offering, **but** one does **not** place **frankincense upon the meal offering** brought with **libations.**

The *Talmud lomar* again closes this off: *aleha levonah v'lo le-minchat nesachim levonah*. Keep the frankincense requirement where the Torah placed it; do not extend it through inference to the *minchat nesachim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִנְחָה״ – לְרַבּוֹת מִנְחַת שְׁמִינִי לִלְבוֹנָה, ״הִיא״ – לְהוֹצִיא שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, שֶׁלֹּא יִטְעֲנוּ לֹא שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא לְבוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the phrase "it is a meal offering," the term **"meal offering"** serves **to include** in the obligation of frankincense the **meal offering of the eighth** day of the inauguration of the Tabernacle in the wilderness. With regard to that offering the verse states: "And a meal offering mixed with oil" (Leviticus 9:4), but it does not mention frankincense. Therefore, the term "meal offering" written in the context of the *omer* meal offering serves to apply the requirement **of frankincense** to the meal offering of the eighth day of inauguration. And the term **"it is"** in the same phrase serves **to exclude the two loaves** sacrificed on *Shavuot*, to indicate **that they will require neither oil nor frankincense.**

The *Gemara* then turns to the final word of the *pasuk*, *mincha hi* — "it is a *mincha*." The word *mincha* functions inclusively, extending the oil and frankincense requirement to the *mincha* brought on the eighth day of the *miluim*, which was not otherwise mentioned. The word *hi* ("it") then functions as a limitation, excluding the *shtei halechem*, for which neither oil nor frankincense was mentioned. The principle throughout is consistent: read the *pesukim* as they stand. Where oil is not mentioned — as by the *lechem hapanim* — do not add it. Where frankincense is not mentioned — as by the *minchat nesachim* — do not add it. Where neither is mentioned — as by the *shtei halechem* — add neither.

## Minchat Kohanim

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן״ – וְלֹא עַל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁמֶן. אֵימָא: ״עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן״ – וְלֹא עַל מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁמֶן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **The Master said** that the phrase: "And you shall put **oil upon it,"** teaches that one places oil upon the *omer* meal offering, **but** one does **not** place **oil on the shewbread.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: Why does the *baraita* conclude that this verse excludes the shewbread? One can **say** instead: **"Upon it"** you shall place **oil, but** one does **not** place **oil on the meal offering of priests.**

Having established these derivations, the *Gemara* begins to interrogate them. If *aleha shemen* is a limiting term that excludes the *lechem hapanim* from receiving oil, why should it not equally exclude the *minchat kohanim*? After all, the Torah does not explicitly say oil by the *minchat kohanim* either. If the *pasuk* is being read as an exclusion, perhaps it is the *minchat kohanim* that should be on the outside.

### Include Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן עִשָּׂרוֹן, כְּלִי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **the meal offering of priests should be included** in the requirement of oil, **as** the meal offering of priests is similar in many ways to the *omer* meal offering that is the subject of the verse. The Gemara details the points of similarity between the two types of meal offerings: Both are prepared from **a tenth** of an ephah of flour, whereas each of the twelve loaves of the shewbread is prepared from two-tenths of an ephah. Furthermore, both are kneaded and consecrated in a service **vessel,** whereas the shewbread is not consecrated in a service vessel but rather is baked in an oven in the Temple courtyard.

The *Gemara* answers: *mistabra minchat kohanim hava lei le-ribuy* — it is more reasonable to assume that the *minchat kohanim* falls within the included group of standard *menachot* and that the *lechem hapanim* is what stands apart. The question is which side of the line each offering belongs on, and the *Gemara* now marshals the evidence.

### Shared Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ, וְצוּרָה, הַגָּשָׁה,
>
> **Translation:** Thirdly, both the meal offering of priests and the *omer* meal offering are sacrificed **outside** the Sanctuary on the outer altar, whereas the shewbread is placed on the Table inside the Sanctuary. **And** in both cases the *halakha* of a change in **form** applies, i.e., if they were left overnight without being sacrificed they are disqualified, whereas the shewbread is left on the Table for a week. Furthermore, in both cases there is the obligation of **bringing** the meal offering **near** to the lower part of the altar, at the southwest corner, an obligation that does not apply to the shewbread.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִישִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** finally, some portion of both the meal offering of priests and the *omer* meal offering is placed in the **fire,** as the handful of the *omer* meal offering is sacrificed, while the entire meal offering of a priest is burned on the altar. By contrast, the shewbread is not sacrificed on the altar at all. In total, there are therefore six points of similarity between the meal offering of priests and the *omer* meal offering, all of which are not shared by the shewbread. Consequently, the requirement of oil stated with regard to the *omer* meal offering should also apply to the meal offering of priests, not to the shewbread.

A series of similarities between *minchat kohanim* and the standard *menachot* is adduced. First, *isaron*: both a standard *mincha* and a *minchat kohen* use an *isaron* of *solet*, whereas the *lechem hapanim* uses a substantially larger measure. Second, *kli*: both are prepared using a *kli sharet*, a sacred vessel, whereas the *lechem hapanim* is sanctified in the oven or on the *shulchan*. Third, *chutz*: both the standard *menachot* and the *minchat kohen* are performed in the *azarah*, outside the *Heichal* proper, whereas the *lechem hapanim* is placed inside the *Heichal* itself. Fourth, *tzura*: both become invalid (*pasul*) by the following morning, losing their valid form overnight — the *lechem hapanim* by contrast remains on the *shulchan* throughout the entire week. Fifth, *hagasha*: both a standard *mincha* and a *minchat kohen* undergo *hagasha*, the touching of the *mincha* to the *mizbeach*, which is not done for the *lechem hapanim*. Sixth, *ishim*: some part of both goes onto the *mizbeach* fire — the *komets* of a standard *mincha* and the entire *minchat kohen* — whereas of the *lechem hapanim* itself nothing is placed on the *mizbeach*; only the *levona* from its accompanying vessels is burnt.

### Counter Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן צִיבּוּרָא, חוֹבָה, טַמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **On the contrary [*adderabba*],** it stands to reason that **the shewbread,** not the meal offering of priests, **should be included** in the requirement of oil, **as** the shewbread has points of similarity with the *omer* meal offering. The Gemara elaborates: The shewbread and the *omer* meal offering are **communal** meal offerings, whereas the meal offering of priests is an individual meal offering. Furthermore, they are both **obligatory** offerings, whereas the meal offering of priests is voluntary. Additionally, both can sometimes be sacrificed in a state of **ritual impurity,** as the prohibition against performing the Temple service in a state of impurity is disregarded in cases involving the public. Like the offering of an individual, the meal offering of priests is not brought in a state of impurity.

The *Gemara* pushes back: *adaraba*, the opposite. Perhaps it is the *lechem hapanim* that should be included with the standard *menachot*, and the *minchat kohanim* that should stand apart. On a different set of criteria, the *lechem hapanim* resembles the *minchat ha'omer* — the very *mincha* whose *pasuk* is under discussion — more closely than the *minchat kohanim* does.

### LH Similarities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָכֵיל פִּיגּוּלָא, בְּשַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Also, there is the *halakha* **that** both the shewbread and the *omer* meal offering are **eaten** by priests, while the meal offering of priests is entirely burned on the altar. Furthermore, the *halakha* of **an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time [*piggul*]** applies to both the shewbread and the *omer* meal offering, but not to the meal offering of priests. And finally, both the shewbread and the *omer* meal offering are brought even **on Shabbat,** as they are communal offerings, whereas the meal offering of priests is not sacrificed on Shabbat. Accordingly, there are also six points of similarity between the shewbread and the *omer* meal offering. Why, then, isn't the verse interpreted as including the shewbread in the requirement of oil, and excluding the meal offering of priests?

Several points of comparison are enumerated. First, *tzibur*: the *lechem hapanim* is a *korban tzibur*, a communal offering, as is the *minchat ha'omer*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always a *korban yachid*, an individual's offering. Second, *chova*: the *lechem hapanim* is obligatory, as are the *omer* and other fixed *menachot*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always a voluntary, free-will offering. Third, *tumah*: the *lechem hapanim* and the communal *menachot* are subject to the principle of *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* — communal obligations override impurity and may be offered even in a state of *tumah* — whereas the *minchat kohanim*, being a *yachid* offering and always voluntary, would never override *tumah*. Fourth, *achilah*: the *lechem hapanim* is eaten by the *kohanim*, as are the portions of standard *menachot* not burnt on the *mizbeach*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is entirely burnt. Fifth, *pigul*: the *lechem hapanim* and standard *menachot* can become *pigul*, since they have a *matir* — the *komets* in the case of a standard *mincha*, and the *levona* in the case of the *lechem hapanim* — whereas the *minchat kohanim*, being entirely burnt with no remaining portion, has no *matir* and therefore cannot become *pigul*. Sixth, *Shabbat*: the *omer* and other obligatory communal *menachot* override *Shabbat*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* does not.

### Nefesh Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא ״נֶפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Even so, **it stands to reason** that one should include the meal offering of priests, as in the same passage that deals with the *omer* meal offering the verse states: "And when **anyone** brings a meal offering to the Lord, his offering shall be of fine flour" (Leviticus 2:1). This verse includes all meal offerings of individuals in the *halakhot* of meal offerings stated in this chapter, including the meal offering of priests.

The *Gemara* resolves the impasse by identifying *nefesh* as the decisive criterion. The opening *pasuk* of the *mincha* laws in Vayikra 2:1 reads: *v'nefesh ki takriv korban mincha l'Hashem solet yihiyeh korbano v'yatzak aleha shemen v'natan aleha levona* — "when a soul brings a *mincha* offering to Hashem, it shall be of *solet*, and he shall pour oil on it and place frankincense on it." The word *nefesh* — "soul," denoting an individual — frames the entire discussion of oil and frankincense as applying to personal, individual offerings. Since the *minchat kohanim* is always the *korban* of a private individual, it falls naturally within this framework. The *lechem hapanim*, a communal offering brought by the *tzibur*, stands outside this framework, and that is why it does not receive oil. Rashi further notes that even beyond the *pasuk* of the *omer*, which has been the textual focus, the very first *pasuk* of the *mincha* laws leads with *nefesh* and immediately prescribes oil and frankincense, making clear that oil and frankincense are the hallmarks of the individual *mincha*. The *minchat kohanim* is included; the *lechem hapanim* is excluded.

## Minchat Nesachim

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״עָלֶיהָ לְבֹנָה״ – וְלֹא עַל מִנְחַת נְסָכִים לְבוֹנָה. אֵימָא: ״עָלֶיהָ לְבֹנָה״ – וְלֹא עַל מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים לְבוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said:** The phrase: "And lay **frankincense upon it"** (Leviticus 2:15), teaches that one must place frankincense upon the *omer* meal offering, **but** one does **not** place **frankincense upon the meal offering** brought with **libations.** The Gemara asks: Why does the *baraita* state that this verse excludes the meal offering brought with libations? One can **say:** One places **frankincense upon** the *omer* meal offering, **but** one does **not** place **frankincense upon the meal offering of priests.**

The *Gemara* now applies the same structure to the frankincense derivation. If *aleha levonah* is limiting and excludes the *minchat nesachim* from receiving frankincense, why should it not instead exclude the *minchat kohanim*? The *minchat kohanim* also does not have frankincense explicitly mentioned in its own *parasha*. Perhaps it is the *minchat kohanim* that should be on the outside.

### Exclude Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן עִשָּׂרוֹן בָּלוּל בְּלוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **the meal offering of priests should be included** in the requirement of frankincense, **as** the meal offering of priests is similar in many respects to the *omer* meal offering. The Gemara elaborates: Both are prepared from **a tenth** of an ephah of flour, whereas the libations that accompany meal offerings come in various amounts, depending on the type of animal offering they accompany. Furthermore, in both cases the flour is **mixed with a *log*** of oil, whereas in the case of the meal offering brought with libations, the amount of oil mixed with the flour depends on the type of animal offering it accompanies.

The *Gemara* responds: *mistabra minchat kohanim hava lei le-ribuy* — it is more reasonable to keep the *minchat kohanim* inside the standard group, and to read the *pasuk* as excluding the *minchat nesachim*. The evidence is again marshaled comparatively.

### MK Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּגָּשׁ, בִּגְלַל עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, both the meal offering of priests and the *omer* meal offering are **brought near** to the altar, a ritual that is not performed with the meal offering brought with libations. And finally, both are sacrificed **due to themselves,** i.e., they do not accompany any other offering, whereas meal offerings brought with libations accompany animal offerings. There are therefore four points of similarity between the meal offering of priests and the *omer* meal offering that do not apply to the meal offering brought with libations.

A series of ways in which the *minchat kohanim* resembles standard *menachot* — and the *minchat nesachim* does not — is presented. *Isaron*: the *minchat kohanim* uses one *isaron* of flour, as does a standard *mincha*, whereas the *minchat nesachim* varies in quantity depending on the animal it accompanies. *Balul*: the *minchat kohanim* is described as mixed with oil (*belulah bashemen*), the same language used for standard *menachot*, whereas the *minchat nesachim* mentions oil but not this mixing formulation. *B'log*: the *minchat kohanim* uses only a *log* of oil, as do standard *menachot*, whereas the *minchat nesachim* requires multiple *logim* depending on the animal. *Mugash*: the *minchat kohanim* undergoes *hagasha*, the presentation to the *mizbeach*, as do standard *menachot*, whereas *hagasha* is not performed for the *minchat nesachim*. *Biglal atzmo*: the *minchat kohanim* is brought in its own right as an independent offering, whereas the *minchat nesachim* is brought only as an accompaniment to a primary animal *korban*.

### MN Similarities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִנְחַת נְסָכִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן צִיבּוּרָא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **On the contrary,** it stands to reason that **the meal offering** brought with **libations** and not the meal offering of priests **should be included** in the requirement of frankincense. This is **because** the meal offering brought with libations is similar to the *omer* meal offering **in that** both the meal offering brought with libations and the *omer* meal offering are **communal** meal offerings, whereas the meal offering of priests is that of an individual.

The *Gemara* counters: *adaraba*, one could argue that the *minchat nesachim* should be on the inside, and it is the *minchat kohanim* that should be excluded.

### MN Characteristics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹבָה, וְאִיטַּמִּי. בְּשַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, both the meal offering brought with libations and the *omer* meal offering are **obligatory** offerings, while the meal offering of priests is a gift offering. **And** both may sometimes be sacrificed in a state of **ritual impurity,** as the prohibition against performing the Temple service in a state of impurity is disregarded in cases involving the public; whereas the meal offering of priests must be brought in a state of purity because it is an offering of an individual. Finally, both are brought even **on Shabbat,** whereas the meal offering of priests may not be brought on Shabbat. Accordingly, as there are also four points of similarity between the meal offering brought with libations and the *omer* meal offering, one can ask why the meal offering brought with libations is not included in the requirement of frankincense.

*Tzibur*: the *minchat nesachim* accompanies communal *korbanot* such as the *korban tamid* and *musaf*, just as the *omer* is a communal *korban*, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always a private offering. *Chova*: the *minchat nesachim* accompanying obligatory *korbanot* is itself obligatory, just as the *omer* is obligatory, whereas the *minchat kohanim* is always voluntary. *Tumah*: since the *minchat nesachim* accompanies *korbanot* that can be brought in a state of *tumah* when the community is impure, the *nesachim* themselves can be brought in that state — something never applicable to the *minchat kohanim*. *Shabbat*: the *minchat nesachim* is brought on *Shabbat* when it accompanies the *korban tamid* or *musaf*; the *minchat kohanim* is never brought on *Shabbat*.

### Nefesh Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא ״נֶפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, **it stands to reason** that one should include the meal offering of priests in the requirement of oil and frankincense, as in the passage discussing the *omer* meal offering the verse states: **"Anyone."** This verse is referring to all meal offerings of individuals, including meal offerings of priests.

The resolution is the same as before: *mistabra nefesh*. The opening *pesukim* of the *mincha* laws frame oil and frankincense as characteristics of the personal, individual *nefesh* offering. Any *korban* that is personal — including the *minchat kohanim* — is thus naturally included in the requirement of oil and frankincense. What is excluded are offerings that fall outside that personal framework: the *minchat nesachim*, which is not an independent *nefesh* offering but an accompaniment, and the communal *shtei halechem*, *lechem hapanim*, and similar offerings. The various *pasuk* words — *aleha*, *aleha*, *hi* — simply reinforce: do not extend what is not written, and read each *mincha* according to its own *pesukim*.

## Shtei Halechem

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִנְחָה״ – לְרַבּוֹת מִנְחַת שְׁמִינִי לִלְבוֹנָה, וְאֵימָא – לְהוֹצִיא? הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבּוֹת – שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that the term: **"Meal offering"** (Leviticus 2:15), serves **to include** the **meal offering of the eighth** day of the inauguration of the Tabernacle **in** the requirement of **frankincense.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** one can **say** that this term serves **to exclude** the meal offering of the eighth day from the requirement of frankincense. The Gemara is puzzled by this suggestion: **What is this** suggestion? **Granted, if you say** that the verse serves **to include** the meal offering of the eighth day of inauguration in the requirement of frankincense, it works out **well.** This is because the only reason one could know that the requirement of frankincense applies would be that the *halakha* of this meal offering, which was brought on one occasion, is derived from the *halakha* of a meal offering that is brought in all generations.

The *Gemara* applies the identical framework a third time, now regarding *shtei halechem*. If *hi* is coming to exclude *shtei halechem* from receiving oil and frankincense, why should it not instead exclude the *minchat kohanim*? Once again, the *minchat kohanim* does not have oil and frankincense explicitly stated in its own *parasha*.

### Exclude Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְהוֹצִיא – לְמָה לִי? שָׁעָה מִדּוֹרוֹת לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that the verse serves **to exclude** the meal offering of the eighth day of the inauguration of the Tabernacle from the requirement of frankincense, **why do I** need a verse for this purpose? There is a principle that **we do not learn** the requirements of **transitory** offerings **from** the requirement of offerings of later **generations.** In other words, even without this verse one would not have thought that the requirement of frankincense applies to the meal offering of the eighth day, so there is no need for the verse to exclude this possibility.

The *Gemara* answers: *mistabra minchat kohanim hava lei le-ribuy* — it is more reasonable to include the *minchat kohanim* with the standard *menachot* and to read the exclusion as applying to the *shtei halechem*. The *minchat kohanim* bears numerous characteristics that align it with standard *menachot* rather than with *shtei halechem*.

### MK Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִיא״ – לְהוֹצִיא שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, שֶׁלֹּא יִטְעֲנוּ לֹא שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא לְבוֹנָה. וְאֵימָא לְהוֹצִיא מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים? מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן עִשָּׂרוֹן,
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that the term: **"It is"** (Leviticus 2:15), serves **to exclude the two loaves** sacrificed on *Shavuot*, to indicate **that they will require neither oil nor frankincense.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** one can **say** that this serves **to exclude the meal offering of priests** from the requirements of oil and frankincense. The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **the meal offering of priests should be included** in these requirements, while the two loaves should be excluded, **as** the meal offering of priests is similar to the *omer* meal offering in several respects that do not apply to the two loaves. The Gemara elaborates: Both are prepared from **a tenth** of an ephah of flour, unlike the two loaves, which are prepared from two-tenths.

*Isaron*: the *minchat kohanim* and standard *menachot* are each brought as an *isaron* of flour, whereas the *shtei halechem* is not measured in *esronim* in the same way. *Kli*: standard *menachot* and *minchat kohanim* are kneaded or prepared in a *kli sharet*, whereas the *shtei halechem* — like the *lechem hapanim* — is sanctified in the *tanur*, and indeed it is *pasul* if kneaded in a *kli sharet*. *Matza*: all standard *menachot* are *matza*, and the *minchat kohanim* is likewise *matza*, whereas the *shtei halechem* is explicitly *chametz*. *Ve'etzem*: the *minchat kohanim* and standard *menachot* are each brought independently, *biglal atzmo*, on their own account, whereas the *shtei halechem* comes together with the *kivsei atzeret*. *Hagasha*: standard *menachot* and *minchat kohanim* undergo *hagasha*, whereas this is not performed for the *shtei halechem*. *Ishim*: some part of both a standard *mincha* and a *minchat kohanim* — whether the *komets* or the entire offering — is placed on the *mizbeach* fire, whereas nothing of the *shtei halechem* itself goes on the *mizbeach*.

### SH Similarities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **On the contrary,**

The *Gemara* again counters: *adaraba*, one could argue that the *shtei halechem* is in fact the offering most similar to the *omer* — the very *mincha* whose *pasuk* anchors this entire derivation — and should therefore be included.

### SH Characteristics

The following segment appears at the beginning of Menachot 59b:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן: צִיבּוּר, חוֹבָה, טַמְיָא, דַּאֲכַל, פִּיגּוּלָא, בְּשַׁבְּתָא, מַתִּיר, תְּנוּפָה, בָּאָרֶץ, בִּזְמַן חָדָשׁ, וְהָנֵי נְפִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** it stands to reason that **the two loaves,** not the meal offering of priests, **should be included** in the requirement of oil and frankincense, **as** the two loaves have points of similarity with the *omer* meal offering. The Gemara elaborates: The two loaves and the *omer* meal offering are **communal** meal offerings, whereas the meal offering of priests is a meal offering of an individual. Both are **obligatory** offerings, whereas the meal offering of priests is a gift offering. Both are sometimes sacrificed in a state of **ritual impurity,** while the meal offering of priests may not be. Also, they are similar in **that** priests **eat** the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering, whereas the meal offering of priests is entirely burned upon the altar. Furthermore, the *halakha* of ***piggul*** applies to the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering, but not to the meal offering of priests. And these offerings are sacrificed even **on Shabbat,** whereas the meal offering of priests is not. Additionally, both the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering **render** other items **permitted,** as the *omer* meal offering renders permitted the consumption of the new crop and the two loaves render permitted the sacrifice of meal offerings from the new crop; whereas the meal offering of priests does not render anything permitted. And both cases include the requirement of **waving,** while the meal offering of priests is not waved. In addition, the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering must come from the produce **of Eretz** Yisrael, whereas the meal offering of priests may consist of produce from outside Eretz Yisrael. Also, the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering are sacrificed **at** a fixed **time,** as the *omer* meal offering is brought on the day after the first Festival day of Passover and the two loaves are sacrificed on *Shavuot*. By contrast, there is no fixed time for a meal offering of priests. Finally, the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering must come from the **new** crop, whereas the meal offering of priests may be brought from the old crop. **And these** eleven points of similarity between the two loaves and the *omer* meal offering **are** more **numerous** than the six points of similarity between the meal offerings of priests and the *omer* meal offering.

A lengthy and detailed enumeration follows. *Tzibur*: the *shtei halechem* is a *korban tzibur*, as is the *omer*. *Chova*: both are obligatory. *Docheh tumah* and *docheh Shabbat*: both override impurity and *Shabbat* by virtue of being obligatory communal offerings. *Achilah*: both the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* are eaten — standard *menachot* have a portion eaten by the *kohanim* — whereas the *minchat kohanim* is entirely burnt. *Pigul*: both the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* have a *matir* and can therefore become *pigul*; the *minchat kohanim*, being entirely burnt, cannot. *Tnufa*: both the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* are waved (*tenufa*), whereas ordinary *menachot* are not waved, and the *minchat kohanim* is not waved. *Ba'aretz*: both must come from grain grown in *Eretz Yisrael*. *Bizman*: both come at a fixed, specific time — the *omer* on Pesach, the *shtei halechem* on Shavuot. *Chadash*: both come from the new grain. Indeed, the *shtei halechem* and the *minchat ha'omer* are genuinely parallel offerings: one from barley, one from wheat; one permitting the new grain to the populace outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the other permitting the new grain to be brought as an offering within it. The *Gemara* notes that the list of similarities here is actually more numerous (*v'hani nefishin*) than in the previous cases.

### Nefesh Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא ״נֶפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, **it stands to reason** that one should include the meal offering of priests in the requirement of oil and frankincense, as in the passage discussing the *omer* meal offering the verse states: **"Anyone."** This verse is referring to all meal offerings of individuals, including meal offerings of priests.

Nevertheless, the conclusion is the same: *mistabra nefesh*. Despite the remarkable convergence between *shtei halechem* and *minchat ha'omer*, *nefesh* is the decisive factor. Oil and frankincense are the markers of the individual, personal *mincha* — the offering of a *nefesh*. The *shtei halechem* is not a *nefesh* offering; it is communal, and frankincense and oil are not mentioned in its *parasha*. Therefore it does not receive them.

The governing principle of this entire sugya is thus made fully explicit: the various words *aleha*, *aleha*, and *hi* throughout the *pasuk* are each saying the same thing — do not go beyond what the Torah states. Where oil is not mentioned, do not add it; where frankincense is not mentioned, do not add it; where neither is mentioned, add neither. The only reason the *Gemara* raises the question of *minchat kohanim* at all — in each of the three iterations — is that the *minchat kohanim* also lacks explicit mention of oil and frankincense in its own *parasha*, and one might therefore have thought it should be excluded along with *lechem hapanim*, *minchat nesachim*, and *shtei halechem*. The answer in every case is *nefesh*: the *minchat kohanim* is the offering of an individual *nefesh* and is therefore naturally drawn into the group of personal *menachot* that receive oil and frankincense, while the non-*nefesh* offerings remain outside.
