# Sotah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shimshon narrative

### Vayivka intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיְהִי כִּי הֵצִיקָה לּוֹ בִדְבָרֶיהָ כׇּל הַיָּמִים וַתְּאַלְצֵהוּ״. מַאי ״וַתְּאַלְצֵהוּ״? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר בִּיאָה נִשְׁמְטָה מִתַּחְתָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** "And it came to pass, when she pressed him daily with her words, and urged him, that his soul was vexed unto death" (Judges 16:16). The Gemara asks: What is the meaning of "and urged him?" How did she do so? **Rabbi Yitzḥak of the school of Rabbi Ami says: At the moment** immediately before his **completion of** the act of **intercourse, she slipped away from beneath him.**

The daf picks up at the bottom of 10b, in the midst of the story of Shimshon — a narrative that the Gemara introduces as part of its broader *mida k'neged mida* expansion from the *sotah* theme, though notably the focus here shifts to a man's sexual sins rather than a woman's. The Gemara quotes the verse *Vayivka Elokim et hamachtesh asher balechi* — God cracked open the hollow within the jawbone, from which water flowed to relieve Shimshon's thirst.

### Lust & punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי אַחֲרֵי כֵן וַיֶּאֱהַב אִשָּׁה בְּנַחַל שֹׂרֵק וּשְׁמָהּ דְּלִילָה״. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אִילְמָלֵא לֹא נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״דְּלִילָה״ — רְאוּיָה הָיְתָה שֶׁתִּקָּרֵא דְּלִילָה: דִּילְדְּלָה אֶת כֹּחוֹ, דִּילְדְּלָה אֶת לִבּוֹ, דִּילְדְּלָה אֶת מַעֲשָׂיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And it came to pass afterward, that he loved a woman in the valley of Sorek, whose name was Delilah** (Judges 16:4). **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** Even **if she had not been called** by the **name Delilah, it would have been fitting that she be called Delilah,** for **she weakened [*dildela*] his strength, she weakened his heart,** and **she weakened his deeds,** thereby decreasing his merits.

Rav Yitzchak of the school of Rabbi Ami comments: *hu ivah l'davar tamei* — he lusted after an impure thing, namely the non-Jewish women he pursued — *lefichach nitlu chayav b'davar tamei* — therefore his very life was made dependent on an impure thing, the jaw of a donkey from which the water emerged.

### Dan prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתָּחֵל רוּחַ ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: חָלְתָה נְבוּאָתוֹ שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְהִי דָן נָחָשׁ עֲלֵי דֶרֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the spirit of the Lord began** to move him in Mahaneh Dan, between Zorah and Eshtaol" (Judges 13:25). **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: The prophecy of Jacob our forefather** concerning the tribe of Dan **took effect** through Samson, a member of the tribe of Dan, **as it is written: "Dan shall be a serpent in the way,** a horned snake in the path, that bites the horse's heels, so that his rider falls backward" (Genesis 49:17).

The verse *Vatachel ruach Hashem l'pa'amo b'machane Dan* — the spirit of God began to ring out in the camp of Dan — prompts Rabbi Chama the son of Rabbi Chanina to observe: *chalah nevuaso shel Yaakov Avinu* — the prophecy of Yaakov Avinu took effect here. The reference is to *yehi Dan nachash alei derech*, that Dan would be a serpent upon the road. Now at last, with Shimshon arising from the tribe of Dan, that ancient blessing begins to manifest.

### Shechina as bell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְפַעֲמוֹ בְּמַחֲנֵה דָן״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׁכִינָה מְקַשְׁקֶשֶׁת לְפָנָיו כְּזוֹג. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״לְפַעֲמוֹ בְּמַחֲנֵה דָן״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״פַּעֲמֹן וְרִמֹּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **"To move him [*lefa'amo*] in Mahaneh Dan." Rabbi Yitzḥak of the school of Rabbi Ami says:** This **teaches that the Divine Presence jangled before him,** inspiring him, **like a bell [*zog*],** as **it is written here: "To move him [*lefa'amo*] in Mahaneh Dan," and it is written there** with regard to the clothing of the High Priest: **"A bell [*pa'amon*] and a pomegranate"** (Exodus 39:26).

Rav Yitzchak of the school of Rabbi Ami adds a second teaching on *l'pa'amo*: *melamed she'haysa Shechina mekashkeshes lifanav k'zug* — the *shechina* was knocking before him like a bell. The verbal parallel is drawn between *l'pa'amo* here and *pa'amon v'rimon* — a bell and a pomegranate — as the *shechina* rang out, driving him forward between Tzor'ah and Eshta'ol.

### Mountains crushed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בֵּין צׇרְעָה וּבֵין אֶשְׁתָּאוֹל״, אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: צׇרְעָה וְאֶשְׁתָּאוֹל שְׁנֵי הָרִים גְּדוֹלִים הָיוּ, וַעֲקָרָן שִׁמְשׁוֹן וּטְחָנָן זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Between Zorah and Eshtaol." Rabbi Asi says: Zorah and Eshtaol were two large mountains, and Samson uprooted them and ground them one against the other.**

Rabbi Assi teaches that Tzor'ah and Eshta'ol were *shnei harim gedolim* — two enormous mountains — *v'akran Shimshon v'tachnan zeh bazeh* — and Shimshon uprooted them both and ground them together with his great strength. This is offered as an elaboration on the magnitude of that same divine spirit resting upon him.

### Ahimeleach oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהוּא יָחֵל לְהוֹשִׁיעַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל״, אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הוּחַל שְׁבוּעָתוֹ שֶׁל אֲבִימֶלֶךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם תִּשְׁקֹר לִי וּלְנִינִי וּלְנֶכְדִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** Samson's parents were being told that **the oath of Abimelech,** king of the Philistines, **was negated, as it is written** that Abimelech said to our forefather Abraham: "Now therefore swear unto me here by God **that you will not deal falsely with me, nor with my son, nor with my son's son;** but according to the kindness that I have done unto you, you shall do to me, and to the land wherein you have sojourned" (Genesis 21:23). The oath of the descendants of Abraham was no longer binding since the Philistines broke their oath by subjugating the Jewish people.

The Gemara then doubles back to the verse *v'hu yachel l'hoshia et Yisrael* — he will begin to bring salvation to Israel. Rabbi Chama the son of Rabbi Chanina reads the word *yachel* here not as *chalah* — taking effect — but as *chullin*, in the sense of *lo yachel devaro* from *Nedarim*: the oath of Avimelech has been voided. The reference is to Avimelech's oath — *im tishkor li u'lnini u'l'nechdi* — that Israel would not betray him or his children or grandchildren. Rashi explains that since the Philistines had already violated that covenant by oppressing Israel, the oath was annulled — *batlah l'shon chabalah lefi shehem avru al shvuas techila* — and Shimshon was therefore free to attack them.

### Sexual blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּגְדַּל הַנַּעַר וַיְבָרְכֵהוּ ה׳״. בַּמֶּה בֵּרְכוֹ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, שֶׁבֵּרְכוֹ בְּאַמָּתוֹ: אַמָּתוֹ כִּבְנֵי אָדָם, וְזַרְעוֹ כְּנַחַל שׁוֹטֵף.
>
> **Translation:** "And the woman bore a son, and called his name Samson; **and the child grew, and the Lord blessed him**" (Judges 13:24). The Gemara asks: **With what did He bless him? Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It means **that He blessed him with regard to his penis,** that despite his youth **his penis** should function **like** that of physically mature **men, and** that **his seed** should be **like an overflowing river.**

The verse *vayigdal hana'ar vayivarchehu Hashem* — the lad grew and God blessed him — raises the question: with what did God bless him? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *shebarcho b'amaso* — with his sexual organ. The Gemara immediately clarifies: not that it was unusually large, but *amaso k'vnei adam v'zaro k'nachal shotef* — its form was normal, but his seed was like a rushing river. Rashi explains: *l'mai shenisava nisbarech* — he was blessed in the very thing he desired.

What is striking about this teaching is that the verse in question precedes the episodes of Shimshon's pursuit of women. The Gemara's reading implies that the desire came first, then the blessing in response to it, and then the acting upon it — a sequence that anticipates the larger theme the Gemara will develop shortly: that individuals are blessed with extraordinary capacities which, if not governed properly, ultimately become their undoing. The connection to the message of *sotah* may also be relevant here — the desire for certain things does not justify pursuing them without restraint.

### Foxes & torches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּלְכֹּד שִׁמְשׁוֹן וַיִּלְכֹּד שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת שׁוּעָלִים״. מַאי שְׁנָא שׁוּעָלִים? אָמַר רַבִּי אַיְּבוּ בַּר נַגְדִּי אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אָמַר שִׁמְשׁוֹן, יָבֹא מִי שֶׁחוֹזֵר לַאֲחוֹרָיו, וְיִפָּרַע מִפְּלִשְׁתִּים שֶׁחָזְרוּ בִּשְׁבוּעָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **"And Samson went and caught three hundred foxes,** and took torches, and turned tail to tail, and put a torch in the midst between every two tails" (Judges 15:4). The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **foxes** than any other animal, that he chose them for this purpose? **Rabbi Aivu bar Nagdi says** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: Samson said: Let** the animal **that goes in reverse** when it tries to escape, i.e., the fox, **come and exact punishment from the Philistines, who reneged on their oath** that Abimelech swore to Abraham.

The Gemara then jumps back to an earlier episode: *vayelech Shimshon vayilkod shalosh meot shualim* — Shimshon caught three hundred foxes. Why specifically foxes? Rabbi Abbahu bar Rabbi Nagdi, citing Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba, explains that Shimshon said: *yavo mi shechozer le'achorav* — let the creature that walks backwards on its own path come to take vengeance on the Philistines, who similarly reversed themselves on their oath. The fox, which retreats backwards rather than turning to run, becomes the fitting symbol of those who betrayed the covenant.

### Shimshon's width

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הֶחָסִיד: בֵּין כְּתֵיפָיו שֶׁל שִׁמְשׁוֹן שִׁשִּׁים אַמָּה הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁכַּב שִׁמְשׁוֹן עַד חֲצִי הַלַּיְלָה וַיָּקׇם בַּחֲצִי הַלַּיְלָה וַיֶּאֱחֹז בְּדַלְתוֹת שַׁעַר הָעִיר וּבִשְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזוֹת וַיִּסָּעֵם עִם הַבְּרִיחַ וַיָּשֶׂם עַל כְּתֵיפָיו״, וּגְמִירִי דְּאֵין דַּלְתוֹת עַזָּה פְּחוּתוֹת מִשִּׁשִּׁים אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon the Pious said:** The width **between the shoulders of Samson was sixty cubits, as it is stated: "And Samson lay till midnight, and arose at midnight, and grabbed hold of the doors of the gate of the city, and the two posts, and plucked them up, bar and all, and put them upon his shoulders,** and carried them up to the top of the mountain that is before Hebron" (Judges 16:3). The verse indicates that the width of the gate of the city of Gaza was equal to the width of Samson's shoulders, **and it is learned** as a tradition **that doors** of the gate **of Gaza were no less than sixty cubits** wide.

A *Braitah* is cited in the name of Rabbi Shimon HaChasid: the distance across Shimshon's shoulders was *shishim amah* — sixty cubits.

### Gates on back

The source for this measurement is derived from the later episode where Shimshon, having slept through half the night in Gaza, arose at midnight, seized the doors and lintels and bolt of the city gate, and carried them on his shoulders. A received tradition states that the gates of Azza were never less than sixty *amah* across — and he bore them all on his back.

### Grinding metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי טוֹחֵן בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין טְחִינָה אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן עֲבֵירָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״תִּטְחַן לְאַחֵר אִשְׁתִּי״. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הֵבִיא לוֹ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ לְבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּתְעַבֵּר הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Samson's capture, the verse states: "And the Philistines laid hold on him, and put out his eyes; and they brought him down to Gaza, and bound him with fetters of brass; **and he did grind in the prison-house**" (Judges 16:21). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Grinding is nothing other** than **a language of a transgression** of sexual intercourse, **and so** the verse **says: "Then let my wife grind unto another man"** (Job 31:10). This **teaches that each and every** Philistine man **brought his wife to the prison in order that she should be impregnated by** Samson.

The verse *vayehi tochen b'veit ha'asurim* — he was grinding in the prison house — is interpreted by Rabbi Yochanan: *ein tchinah ela lashon aveirah* — *tchinah*, grinding, is here a euphemism for sexual intercourse. He supports this from the verse in Job: *titchan l'acher ishti* — then another man will grind after my wife. Proceeding from this, Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *melamed shekol echad ve'echad hevi lo et ishto l'veit ha'asurim k'dei shetita'ber heimenu* — every man brought his wife to the prison so that she might conceive from Shimshon, using him essentially as a means of obtaining powerful offspring.

The irony within the context of *masechet Sotah* is sharp. Throughout the tractate, the framing has been one of a woman suspected of adultery, a jealous husband bringing her before the *beit hamikdash* for judgment. Here the image is inverted: it is the husband himself who is forcing his wife into sexual contact with another man. The woman is treated entirely as an object subject to her husband's will. This only deepens the tractate's implicit critique of a legal framework that places the full burden of suspicion on the woman while leaving the man's conduct largely unexamined. Rashi, notably circumspect, writes only: *Raboteinu peirshu mah shepeirshu* — the Sages explained what they explained — declining to elaborate.

### Desire fulfilled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: קַמֵּי דְּשָׁתֵי חַמְרָא — חַמְרָא, קַמֵּי רָפוֹקָא (גְּרִידְיָא — דּוּבְלָא).
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: This** is an example of the folk saying **that people say: Before a wine drinker,** bring **wine; before one who digs in the ground,** bring **figs.** So too, Samson, who married Philistine women, was brought more Philistine women while in prison.

Rav Pappa adds a proverbial gloss: *hainu d'amri inshi* — this is what people say — *kamei d'shatei chamra chamra*, before one who drinks wine, you bring wine; *kamei d'rafuka, gerida duvla*, before a farmer you bring a basket of vegetables. People receive what they are drawn to. The point, as applied to Shimshon, is that even when such a desire does a person no ultimate good, it is what they get — because it is what they sought.

## Reciprocal adultery

### Mida k'neged mida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַמְזַנֶּה — אִשְׁתּוֹ (מְזַנֶּנֶת) [מְזַנָּה] עָלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם נִפְתָּה לִבִּי עַל אִשָּׁה וְעַל פֶּתַח רֵעִי אָרָבְתִּי״, וּכְתִיב: ״תִּטְחַן לְאַחֵר אִשְׁתִּי וְעָלֶיהָ יִכְרְעוּן אֲחֵרִין״, וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אִיהוּ בֵּי קָארֵי, וְאִיתְּתֵיהּ בֵּי בוּצִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **anyone who commits adultery, his wife commits adultery against him, as it is stated: "If my heart has been enticed unto a woman, and I have lain in wait at my neighbor's door"** (Job 31:9), **and it is written: "Then let my wife grind unto another man and may strangers kneel over her"** (Job 31:10). **And this** explains the folk saying **that people say: He is** found **among the pumpkins [*karei*] and his wife among the zucchinis [*butzinei*],** which are similar types of vegetables. In other words, she acts the same way that he does.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *kol hamizaneh ishto mezaneh alav* — any man who commits acts of *znut*, his wife will do the same against him. This is a striking statement in the context of a tractate formally concerned with the wife's guilt. The Gemara does not limit the concept of sexual sin to its narrow *halachic* definition. Earlier, the Gemara noted that Shimshon's moral failing began not merely when he married non-Jewish women but when he went to prostitutes — a *musar* framing that goes well beyond the technical *halacha*. Similarly here, *hamizaneh* presumably refers not only to sleeping with another man's wife (which would be *halachic* adultery) but to sleeping around more broadly. The passage thus meaningfully shifts the lens: if a man's wife is sinning, one must ask what role he played in creating that dynamic.

### Job proofs

The Gemara brings a proof from the book of Job: *im niftah libi al ishah v'al pesach re'ei aravti* — if my heart has been seduced toward a woman, and I lay in wait at the door of my neighbor — then *titchan l'acher ishti* — another man will grind after my wife, and *v'aleha yichre'un acharin* — men will prostrate themselves after her. Here the adultery is indeed technical — it concerns another man's wife — but the parallel and the principle are clear: one who chases after his neighbor's wife will have men chasing after his own.

### Gourd & gander

The Gemara closes this section with a popular saying: *ihu bevei kari v'it'teih bevei butzini* — he eats a small gourd while his wife eats a large one. The image captures the *mida k'neged mida* dynamic colloquially: what you do will be done to you in kind.

## Righteous judge

### Shimshon judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שִׁמְשׁוֹן דָּן אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל כַּאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דָּן יָדִין עַמּוֹ כְּאַחַד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Samson judged the Jewish people as their Father in Heaven** does, with complete justice, **as it is stated: "Dan shall judge his people, as one** of the tribes of Israel" (Genesis 49:16), which is interpreted to mean that Samson, from the tribe of Dan, judges his people just as God, Who is "One."

Returning to Shimshon, Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *Shimshon dan et Yisrael k'avihem she-bashamayim* — Shimshon judged Israel like their Father in Heaven. This is derived from *Dan yadin amo ke-achad* — like the One, like God. The verse's reference to the tribe of Dan is read as a reference to Shimshon specifically. The context matters: the Gemara is simultaneously depicting Shimshon's lust and his extraordinary righteousness as a judge — a portrait of genuine human complexity, resisting the easy categorization of a figure as wholly righteous or wholly wicked.

### Shimshon & God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שִׁמְשׁוֹן עַל שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נִקְרָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי שֶׁמֶשׁ וּמָגֵן ה׳ אֱלֹהִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Samson [*Shimshon*] is called by the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "For the Lord God is a sun [*shemesh*] and a shield"** (Psalms 84:12).

Rabbi Yochanan goes further: *Shimshon al shemo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu nikra* — the very name Shimshon was a reference to God. The proof is from *ki shemesh u-magen Hashem Elokim* — for God is a sun and a shield. The name שמשון shares its root with *shemesh*, a protector and shield.

### Name clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לֹא יִמָּחֶה! אֶלָּא: מֵעֵין שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵגֵין עַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ — אַף שִׁמְשׁוֹן מֵגֵין בְּדוֹרוֹ עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** If that is so, then his name **should not be erased** just like other sanctified names are not erased. **Rather,** he is not called by the name of God but his name **is akin to the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** for **just as the Holy One, Blessed be He, protects the entire world, so too Samson, in his generation, protected all the Jewish people.**

This raises an immediate question: if the name שמשון is a divine name, may it be erased? The Gemara resolves: *ela me-ein shemo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — it is not a proper name of God but a descriptor, a name *me-ein*, in the manner of. Tosafot sharpens the analysis: names like *chanun* and *rachum* and even *shalom* are not among the seven divine names that may not be erased, because they are descriptions rather than proper names. The word *shemesh* — a shield, a protector — similarly functions as a descriptor. Many proper names incorporate divine descriptors: *Yeshayahu*, *Eliezer*, and others contain a divine element without constituting a *shem kodesh* in the formal sense. The answer of *me-ein shemo* in the Gemara therefore resolves the question: the name may be erased because it is a descriptor, not a proper name.

### Eternal protection

The Gemara concludes the point: *ma HaKadosh Baruch Hu magen al kol ha-olam kulo, af Shimshon magen be-doro al Yisrael* — just as God protects the entire world eternally, so Shimshon was the protector of Israel in his generation. The contrast is telling: God's protection is universal and unending; Shimshon's was particular and temporal — but within those limits, it partook of something of the divine quality.

### Bilam & Shimshon legs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּלְעָם חִיגֵּר בְּרַגְלוֹ אַחַת הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיֵּלֶךְ שֶׁפִי״. שִׁמְשׁוֹן חִיגֵּר בִּשְׁתֵּי רַגְלָיו הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁפִיפֹן עֲלֵי אֹרַח״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Balaam was lame in one of his legs, as it is stated** with regard to him: **"And he went, limping [*shefi*]"** (Numbers 23:3). **Samson was lame in** both of **his two legs, as it is stated** that when Jacob mentioned the tribe of Dan in the prophecy that pertained to Samson, he referred to him as: "Dan shall be a serpent in the way, **a horned snake [*shefifon*] in the path"** (Genesis 49:17), which is double *shefi*, i.e., doubly lame.

The Gemara then adds that Rabbi Yochanan also said Bilam was lame in one leg — *va-yelech shefi* contains an Aramaic word suggesting dislocation — while Shimshon was lame in both legs: *shefifon alei orach*, the doubled form. This is notable: Shimshon, for all his extraordinary physical power, carried a physical disability. The Gemara here refuses to flatten him into a simple hero. He could be the greatest judge of his generation, physically powerful beyond all human measure, morally serious in his conduct of the people — and simultaneously lustful, a pursuer of women, and physically lame. That complexity is itself part of what makes this *aggadata* so rich.

## Five blessed ones

### Five intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה נִבְרְאוּ מֵעֵין דּוּגְמָא שֶׁל מַעְלָה, וְכוּלָּן לָקוּ בָּהֶן. שִׁמְשׁוֹן בְּכֹחוֹ, שָׁאוּל בְּצַוָּארוֹ, אַבְשָׁלוֹם בִּשְׂעָרוֹ, צִדְקִיָּה בְּעֵינָיו, אָסָא בְּרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Five** individuals **were created** with a characteristic that is **akin to a representation of the** One on **High, and they were all stricken by that** characteristic. **Samson** was glorified **in his strength, Saul in his neck** (see I Samuel 9:2), **Absalom in his hair, Zedekiah in his eyes,** and **Asa in his feet.**

The Gemara introduces a *braitah*: *chamisha nivre'u me-ein dugma shel ma'ala* — five were created bearing something resembling — with deliberate distancing language — the image of what exists above. That is, five individuals possessed qualities so extraordinary that they partook of something almost divine. *Ve-chulam laku bahen* — and for all of them, those very qualities became their undoing.

### Shimshon strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁמְשׁוֹן בְּכֹחוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּסַר כֹּחוֹ מֵעָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Samson** was stricken **by his strength,** which led to his demise, **as it is written:** "And she made him sleep upon her knees; and she called for a man and had the seven locks of his head shaved off; and she began to afflict him, **and his strength went from him**" (Judges 16:19).

Shimshon in his strength — *Shimshon be-kocho*. The verse cited is *vayaser kocho me-alav* — his strength was taken from him at the end. The moral logic here is somewhat different from the earlier *mida k'neged mida* formulation: it is not precisely that Shimshon's strength was his sin and therefore led to punishment of the same kind. Rather, the thing he was most extraordinarily gifted with was ultimately the thing that did not save him — it was taken. The strength was divine in quality; ultimately it was not his to keep.

### Shaul's neck

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאוּל בְּצַוָּארוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח שָׁאוּל אֶת הַחֶרֶב וַיִּפֹּל עָלֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Saul** was smitten **in his neck, as it is written:** "Then said Saul to his armor-bearer: Draw your sword, and thrust me through with it; lest these uncircumcised come and thrust me through, and make a mock of me. But his armor-bearer would not; for he was sore afraid. **Therefore, Saul took his sword and fell upon it**" (I Samuel 31:4); he fell with his neck upon the sword.

Shaul in his neck — *Shaul be-tzavaro* — because he stood *mi-shichmo va-ma'ala* above everyone else. The verse brought is *vayikach Shaul et hacherev vayipel aleha* — he fell upon his sword. Rashi suggests that the typical mode of execution by sword is decapitation, connecting the manner of his death to the neck. As with Shimshon, however, the connection is not that his height caused pride or sin — the moral message here is not about a fatal flaw in the usual sense. The Gemara's point seems to be rather that however blessed a person is, however divine the quality, it is not ultimately theirs. Nothing exempts them from their fate.

### Absalom's hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבְשָׁלוֹם בִּשְׂעָרוֹ, כִּדְבָעֵינַן לְמֵימַר קַמַּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Absalom** was stricken **in his hair, as we will state later.**

Avshalom in his hair — *Avshalom be-se'aro* — and this is the case that is most explicitly a *mida k'neged mida*, as the Gemara notes briefly, deferring a fuller treatment to a later discussion.

### Zidkiyahu eyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִדְקִיָּה בְּעֵינָיו, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת עֵינֵי צִדְקִיָּהוּ עִוֵּר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Zedekiah** was stricken **in his eyes, as it is written:** "And they slew the sons of Zedekiah before his eyes, **and put out the eyes of Zedekiah,** and bound him in fetters, and carried him to Babylon" (II Kings 25:7).

Tzidkiyahu in his eyes — *Tzidkiyahu be-einav* — and the verse is direct: *v'et einei Tzidkiyahu iver* — his eyes were blinded. Rashi acknowledges that there is no explicit verse praising the beauty of Tzidkiyahu's eyes, and concludes it must have been a received tradition.

### Asa gout

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָסָא בְּרַגְלָיו, דִּכְתִיב: ״רַק לְעֵת זִקְנָתוֹ חָלָה אֶת רַגְלָיו״, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁאֲחָזַתּוּ פָּדַגְרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן לְרַב נַחְמָן: הֵיכִי דָּמֵי פָּדַגְרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּמַחַט בִּבְשַׂר הַחַי. מְנָא יָדַע? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מֵיחַשׁ הֲוָה חָשׁ בֵּיהּ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מֵרַבֵּיהּ שְׁמַע לֵיהּ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: ״סוֹד ה׳ לִירֵאָיו וּבְרִיתוֹ לְהוֹדִיעָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Asa** was stricken **in his feet, as it is written:** "Now the rest of all the acts of Asa, and all his might, and all that he did, and the cities that he built, are they not written in the book of the chronicles of the kings of Judah? **But in the time of his old age he was diseased in his feet**" (I Kings 15:23). **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** This indicates **that gout [*padagra*] grabbed hold of him. Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, said to Rav Naḥman: What are the circumstances** of **gout?** What pain does it involve? **He said to him:** It feels **like a needle** inserted **into living flesh.** The Gemara asks: **From where did he know this?** The Gemara answers: **Some say** that he himself **suffered from this** condition, **and some say** that **he heard it from his teacher, and some say** that he knew it through divine inspiration, as it stated: **"The secret of the Lord is with them that fear Him; and His covenant, to make them know it"** (Psalms 25:14).

Asa in his legs — *Asa be-raglav* — the verse being *rak le-et ziknaso chala et raglav* — in his old age his legs became ill. Again, no explicit verse establishes that his legs were a special blessing; that too must have been tradition.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav that the disease which afflicted his legs was *podagra* — gout. Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman asked his father: what does *podagra* feel like? He replied: *kemachata be-vasar hachai* — like a needle in raw flesh. Samuel Johnson described it as a rat gnawing at one's toe. The Gemara then asks how Rav Nachman knew this. Three answers are offered: some say he had experienced it himself; some say he received it as a tradition from his teacher; and some say *sod Hashem lirei'av u'vrito le-hodiam* — God's secret is with those who fear Him — an inspired knowledge, reminiscent of a similar discussion elsewhere regarding the *shir habia*.

### Asa draft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶעֱנַשׁ אָסָא — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעָשָׂה אַנְגַּרְיָא בְּתַלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַמֶּלֶךְ אָסָא הִשְׁמִיעַ אֶת כׇּל יְהוּדָה אֵין נָקִי״. מַאי ״אֵין נָקִי״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ חָתָן מֵחֶדְרוֹ וְכַלָּה מֵחוּפָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: For what** reason was **Asa punished** in his feet? **Because he made Torah scholars perform forced labor [*angarya*], as it is stated: "Then King Asa made a proclamation unto all Judah; none was exempted;** and they carried away the stones of Ramah and the timber thereof, with which Baasa had built, and King Asa built with them Geba of Benjamin and Mizpah" (I Kings 15:22). The superfluous expression "unto all" indicates that the proclamation was issued to **everyone, including** Torah scholars. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the next phrase in the verse: **"None was exempted [*ein naki*]"? Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** This includes **even a bridegroom from his chamber and a bride from her wedding canopy,** as the verse states with regard to a bridegroom: "He shall be free [*naki*] for his house one year" (Deuteronomy 24:5).

Rava asks: why was Asa punished? Since the punishment is not straightforwardly a *mida k'neged mida* — it is not that his legs caused sin — why did he suffer? *Mipnei she'asa angarya be-talmidei chachamim* — because he conscripted Torah scholars into military service. The verse is *ve-hamelech Asa hishmia et kol Yehuda ein naki* — King Asa mobilized all of Judah, none exempt. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: not only Torah scholars, but even *chatan me-chedro ve-chala me-chupata* — a groom from his chamber and a bride from her wedding canopy, whom the Torah itself exempts. The *Aruch* adds: *ve-chol sheken talmidei chachamim* — and certainly Torah scholars. This Gemara is, needless to say, extensively cited in contemporary debates over the drafting of *kollel* students in Israel.

## Timnah geography

### Timnah verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וַיֵּרֶד שִׁמְשׁוֹן תִּמְנָתָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״הִנֵּה חָמִיךְ עֹלֶה תִמְנָתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is written** with regard to Samson: **"And Samson went down to Timnah,** and saw a woman in Timnah of the daughters of the Philistines" (Judges 14:1), **and it is written** in the Torah passage concerning the incident of Judah and Tamar: "And it was told to Tamar, saying: **Behold, your father-in-law is going up to Timnah** to sheer his sheep" (Genesis 38:13).

The Gemara notes an apparent contradiction in the Torah: one verse says *vayered Shimshon Timnata* — Shimshon went *down* to Timnah — while another verse says *hinei chamich oleh Timnata* — your father-in-law is going *up* to Timnah. Does one ascend or descend to Timnah?

### Moral reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שִׁמְשׁוֹן שֶׁנִּתְגַּנָּה בָּהּ — כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ יְרִידָה. יְהוּדָה שֶׁנִּתְעַלָּה בָּהּ — כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ עֲלִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** These terms do not refer to the manner of traveling to Timnah but are used figuratively. Concerning **Samson, who was disgraced there** in Timnah, the term indicating **descent is written with regard to his** journey. Concerning **Judah, who was elevated there,** the term indicating **ascent is written with regard to his** journey.

Rabbi Elazar resolves the contrast not geographically but morally: *Shimshon shenisganeh bah, ksiv beih yeridah* — for Shimshon, who was morally degraded by the experience, the language of descent is used. *Yehudah shenisaleh bah, ksiv beih aliyah* — for Yehudah, who was ultimately elevated by the experience — through his confession and his role in the messianic line — the language of ascent is used. The same place, two contrasting spiritual trajectories.

### Single vs. two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: שְׁתֵּי תִּמְנָאוֹת הָיוּ, חֲדָא בִּירִידָה, וַחֲדָא בַּעֲלִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** differently: **There were two** cities named **Timnah, one** was reached **by descent** into a valley, **and one** was reached **by ascent.**

Two further positions are offered. Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani says *shtei Timnos hava* — there were actually two separate places called Timnah, one topographically lower and one higher, each appearing in a different verse. This may well be *pshat*: the use of *aliyah* and *yeridah* in biblical Hebrew is not always strictly geographic, as *aliyah la-aretz* makes plain.

### Timnah approach variations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: חֲדָא תִּמְנָה הֲוַאי, דְּאָתֵי מֵהַאי גִּיסָא — יְרִידָה, וּדְאָתֵי מֵהַאי גִּיסָא — עֲלִיָּה. כְּגוֹן וַרְדּוֹנְיָא וּבֵי בָארֵי וְשׁוּקָא דְנַרֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** differently: **There was one Timnah,** and it was located on the slope of a mountain. One **who came from this side** reached it by **descent, and** one **who came from that side** reached it by **ascent.** The Gemara presents examples of such cities: **For example: Vardonia, and Bei Varei, and the market of Neresh.**

Rav Pappa maintains that there was only *chada Timnah* — a single Timnah — and resolves the contradiction simply: *de-asi me-hai gisa yeridah u-de-asi me-hai gisa aliyah* — depending on from which direction one approached, the road went either up or down. He compares this to several known villages — Vardunya, Bei Bari, and Shuka de-Narsh — each situated on a hillside such that one approach descends and another ascends. The single-Timnah reading carries its own literary power: one place, two stories of men and women and sexual encounter, one degrading and one elevating.

## Tamar crossroads

### Pesach Einayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּשֶׁב בְּפֶתַח עֵינַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states with regard to Tamar: "And she put off from her the garments of her widowhood, and covered herself with her veil, and wrapped herself, **and sat in the entrance of Enaim [*befetaḥ einayim*]**, which is by the way to Timnah; for she saw that Shelah was grown up, and she was not given unto him to wife" (Genesis 38:14).

The Gemara turns to Tamar and the phrase *vatashev be-pesach einayim* — she sat at the opening of Enaim, or at the crossroads. The phrase *pesach einayim* receives multiple interpretations.

### Abraham's tent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָלְכָה וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ בְּפִתְחוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, מָקוֹם שֶׁכׇּל עֵינַיִם צוֹפוֹת לִרְאוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Alexandri says:** This **teaches that she went and she sat at the entrance** of the home **of Abraham our forefather, a place that all eyes hope to see it,** as she was certain that Judah would pass there.

Rabbi Alexandri teaches: *melamed she-halcha ve-yashva be-fischo shel Avraham Avinu* — she went and sat at the doorway of Avraham Avinu — the place toward which all eyes turn, *makom she-kol einayim tzofot li-roso*, because Avraham kept *pesach ha-ohel* and was the paradigmatic welcoming host. It is a *drash*, of course; the geographical connection between Timnah and Avraham's tent remains unexplained.

### Enaim place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חָנִין אָמַר רַב: מָקוֹם הוּא שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ עֵינַיִם, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״תַּפּוּחַ וְהָעֵינָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanin** says that **Rav says:** It is **a place called Enaim, and similarly** the verse **states** in the list of cities in Eretz Yisrael in the portion of Judah: **"Tappuah and Enam"** (Joshua 15:34).

Rabbi Chanina in the name of Rav says: *makom hu she-shemo Enaim* — it is simply a place name, as confirmed by the verse *Tapuach ve-haEnam*, where Enam is listed as a city.

### Eyes to words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: שֶׁנָּתְנָה עֵינַיִם לִדְבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: She provided eyes [*einayim*] for her statements,** i.e., with her words she provided an opening [*petaḥ*] for Judah to solicit her.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani offers a third reading: *she-natna einayim li-dvareha* — she gave eyes to her words, meaning she positioned herself so as to enable Yehudah to act upon his desire. The precise implication of this reading will become clearer as the encounter unfolds.

## Yehudah's questions

### Non-Jewish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁתְּבָעָהּ אָמַר לָהּ: שֶׁמָּא נׇכְרִית אַתְּ? אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: גִּיּוֹרֶת אֲנִי. שֶׁמָּא אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ אַתְּ? אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: פְּנוּיָה אֲנִי. שֶׁמָּא קִיבֵּל בִּךְ אָבִיךָ קִידּוּשִׁין? אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: יְתוֹמָה אֲנִי. שֶׁמָּא טְמֵאָה אַתְּ? אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: טְהוֹרָה אֲנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Judah **solicited her** to engage in sexual intercourse with him, **he** first attempted to verify her status and **said to her:** Are **you perhaps are a gentile? She said to him: I am a convert.** He asked: **Perhaps you are a married woman? She said to him: I am an unmarried woman.** He asked: **Perhaps your father accepted betrothal for you** and you are unaware of it? **She said to him: I am an orphan.** He asked: **Maybe you are impure? She said to him: I am pure.**

As Yehudah approaches and requests to sleep with her, the Gemara presents a remarkable sequence of questions. First: *shem nachris at?* — perhaps you are a non-Jewish woman and I am forbidden from you? She replies: *giyoret ani* — I am a convert, do not worry.

### Married status

He asked: *shem eshet ish at?* — perhaps you are a married woman? She replies: *pnuyah ani* — I am unattached.

### Father married

He asked: *shem kibel bach avich kiddushin?* — perhaps your father betrothed you while you were still a minor? She replies: *yetomah ani* — I am an orphan, and a convert at that — she would have no father to have arranged such a betrothal.

### Niddah status

He asked: *shem tme'ah at?* — perhaps you are in a state of *niddah*? She replies: *tehorah ani* — I am pure.

### Rashi truth

Rashi attempts to read each of Tamar's answers as technically true. She is indeed a *giyoret* by virtue of her marriage into Yehudah's family. She is indeed *pnuyah* in the sense the Gemara will explore. She is indeed an orphan, and her answer regarding *kiddushin* by her father serves a further *halachic* purpose for Rashi: since she was married not by her father but by her mother — and since the Rabbis had not yet legislated that maternal arrangements carry full *halachic* validity — she was not truly Yehudah's daughter-in-law in the strict sense, which is why Yehudah was not transgressing *arayot* by lying with her. One might immediately object that this undermines Tamar's entire purpose: if she was never really his daughter-in-law, then the *yibum* claim that motivated her entire scheme collapses. This tension is real.

### Family levirate

The Ramban addresses this directly. He argues that in the ancient world, before the Torah formalized *yibum* as a duty incumbent on the brother specifically, the obligation was understood more broadly as a family responsibility — anyone in the *mishpacha* could fulfill it. One sees a trace of this in the story of Rut and Boaz, where Boaz is not the dead man's brother yet the entire framework of redemption and quasi-*yibum* applies. What Tamar was doing, on this reading, was asserting her claim to *yibum* from the patriarch of the family itself — a claim that made perfect sense within the pre-Sinaitic understanding of the institution. The questions about *niddah* and *kiddushin* reflect the Gemara reading its own *halachic* categories back onto that earlier world, but the essential structure of the story remains coherent.

## Abraham eshel

### Eshel meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּטַּע אֶשֶׁל בִּבְאֵר שָׁבַע״, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָשָׂה פַּרְדֵּס, וְנָטַע בּוֹ כׇּל מִינֵי מְגָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses Abraham's house: It is written: **"And he planted an *eshel* in Beersheba,** and called there on the name of the Lord, the Everlasting God" (Genesis 21:33). **Reish Lakish says:** This **teaches that** Abraham **made an orchard and planted in it all kinds of sweet things.**

Having mentioned Avraham's doorway in connection with Tamar, the Gemara pauses to discuss the verse *vayita eshel be-Be'er Sheva* — and he planted an *eshel* in Be'er Sheva. What is an *eshel*? A dispute is offered: one opinion says *pardes* — a garden orchard — *she-nata bo kol minei migadim*, planted with all manner of delicacies; another says *pundak* — an inn. The word *vayita* presents a difficulty for the inn interpretation, since one plants a tree, not a building. But the Gemara resolves this from a parallel usage: *vayita ohalei apadano* — he pitched his palatial tents — where the same verb means to erect a structure.

### Eshel acrostic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, חַד אָמַר: פַּרְדֵּס, וְחַד אָמַר: פּוּנְדָּק. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּרְדֵּס, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּטַּע״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פּוּנְדָּק, מַאי ״וַיִּטַּע״? כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וַיִּטַּע אׇהֳלֵי אַפַּדְנוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The tanna'im **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya** disagree as to the meaning of the word "*eshel*." **One said** that it means **an orchard [*pardes*], and one said** that it means **an inn [*pundak*].** The Gemara continues: **Granted, according to the one who said** that it means **an orchard, this is what is written: "And he planted,"** and this is suitable language for an orchard. **But according to the one who said** that he opened **an inn, what is** the meaning of the phrase **"and he planted"?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And he shall plant [*vayitta*] the tents of his palace** between the seas and the beauteous holy mountain; and he shall come to his end, and none shall help him" (Daniel 11:45), indicating that the word vayitta, and he planted, is also used to indicate pitching tents.

Reish Lakish teaches: *al tikrei vayikra, ela vayakrei* — do not read it as "he called out," but as "he caused others to call out." The word *eshel* is further read as an *acrostic*: *achila, shtiya, levaya* — eating, drinking, and being accompanied on one's way.

### Abraham method

Avraham's method of *kiruv* was as follows: he welcomed travelers in, fed them and gave them drink.

### Blessing redirect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקְרָא שָׁם בְּשֵׁם ה׳ אֵל עוֹלָם״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״וַיִּקְרָא״, אֶלָּא ״וַיַּקְרִיא״. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִקְרִיא אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ לִשְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּפֶה כׇּל עוֹבֵר וָשָׁב. כֵּיצַד? לְאַחַר שֶׁאָכְלוּ וְשָׁתוּ, עָמְדוּ לְבָרְכוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶם: וְכִי מִשֶּׁלִּי אֲכַלְתֶּם? מִשֶּׁל אֱלֹהֵי עוֹלָם אֲכַלְתֶּם! הוֹדוּ וְשַׁבְּחוּ וּבָרְכוּ לְמִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The verse there states: "And he planted an *eshel* in Beersheba, **and called there [*vayyikra*] on the name of the Lord, the Everlasting God**" (Genesis 21:33). **Reish Lakish said: Do not read** this word literally as **"*vayyikra*,"** and he called, but **rather** as ***vayyakri*, and he caused** others **to call.** This **teaches that Abraham our forefather caused the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He, to be called** out **in the mouth of all passersby. How so? After** the guests of Abraham **ate and drank, they arose to bless him. He said to them: But did you eat from** what is **mine?** Rather, **you ate from** the food of **the God of the world.** Therefore, **you** should **thank and praise and bless the One Who spoke the world into being.**

When the guests wished to thank him for the hospitality, he redirected them: *v'chi misheli achaltem? mishel Elohei olam achaltem* — you did not eat my food; you ate the food of the God of the world. *Hodu ve-shabchu u-varchu l'mi she-amar ve-haya ha-olam* — give thanks and praise and bless the One who spoke the world into being. That is the deeper meaning of *vayikra b'shem Hashem El Olam* — he caused God's name to be on the lips of all who passed by. The practical lesson is timeless: you bring people in with hospitality, and through that opening you direct their gratitude upward.

## Mistaken identity

### Covered face

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּרְאֶהָ יְהוּדָה וַיַּחְשְׁבֶהָ לְזוֹנָה כִּי כִסְּתָה פָּנֶיהָ״. מִשּׁוּם דְּכִסְּתָה פָּנֶיהָ, חַשְּׁבַהּ לְזוֹנָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of the incident of Judah and Tamar. It is written: **"When Judah saw her, he thought her to be a prostitute, for she had covered her face"** (Genesis 38:15). The Gemara asks: **Because she had covered her face he thought her to be a prostitute?** Prostitutes usually uncover their faces in order to attract men.

The Torah says *vayachsheveha l'zonah ki kistah paneha* — he thought her to be a prostitute because she had covered her face. The Gemara is puzzled: *mishum d'kistah paneha chashvah l'zonah?* — is it from *covering* her face that he concluded she was a prostitute? This seems to assume a social context in which a brazen woman is uncovered and a modest woman is veiled. But in that case, a covered face should suggest modesty, not *znut*.

### Veil meaning

The puzzle reflects a genuine cultural tension. In some societies modesty demands veiling; in others, the veil serves the opposite function — it enables anonymity and thus facilitates illicit encounters. A person entirely concealed cannot be identified, which may be precisely the point for certain purposes. The question the Gemara is wrestling with is which convention operates here.

### Recognition failure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁכִּסְּתָה פָּנֶיהָ בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** The verse means **that** Tamar **covered her face in the home of her father-in-law,** Judah. Therefore, he did not recognize her when her face was uncovered.

Rabbi Elazar resolves it differently: *she-kistah paneha b'veit chamia* — she had always covered her face in Yehudah's household. Therefore he did not recognize her. He had never seen her clearly as his daughter-in-law because she was so modest in his presence — and since he had never seen her face, he could not now identify her. The implication is not that the veil signaled *znut* but rather that the veil prevented recognition, given her habitual modesty in his home.

### Modest bride reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל כַּלָּה שֶׁהִיא צְנוּעָה בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ — זוֹכָה וְיוֹצְאִין מִמֶּנָּה מְלָכִים וּנְבִיאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Any daughter-in-law who is modest in the house of her father-in-law merits that kings and prophets emerge from her.**

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *kol kallah she-tznuah b'veit chamia zochah v'yotzim mimena melachim u-nevi'im* — any bride who is modest in her father-in-law's house merits that kings and prophets descend from her. The principle is intuitive: a married woman, living under the same roof as her father-in-law, takes particular care to maintain her *tzniut* precisely because there is another man present who is not her husband. That careful modesty carries with it a great reward.

## Tamar proof

### Amotz lineage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן — מִתָּמָר. נְבִיאִים — דִּכְתִיב: ״חֲזוֹן יְשַׁעְיָהוּ בֶן אָמוֹץ״. מְלָכִים — מִדָּוִד. וְאָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: דָּבָר זֶה מָסוֹרֶת בְּיָדֵינוּ מֵאֲבוֹתֵינוּ: אָמוֹץ וַאֲמַצְיָה — אַחִים הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive this? **From Tamar. Prophets** emerged from her, **as it is written: "The vision of Isaiah, the son of Amoz"** (Isaiah 1:1). **Kings** emerged from her, as seen **from David. And Rabbi Levi says: This matter is a tradition** that **we** received **from our ancestors: Amoz,** father of Isaiah, **and Amaziah,** king of Judea, **were brothers.**

The Gemara asks: *menalan?* — from where do we know this is so? *Mi-Tamar* — from Tamar herself. For the prophets, the Gemara cites *chazon Yeshayahu ben Amotz*. Rabbi Levi adds: *davar zeh masoret b'yadeinu me-avoteinu* — we have this as a received tradition — *Amotz ve-Amatzia achim havu* — Amotz and Amatzia were brothers.

### Isaiah descent

Since Amatzia was *melech Yehuda* — king of Judah — Yeshayahu's father Amotz was of the royal family, descended from David, and thus from Yehudah and Tamar. From Tamar therefore came both kings — through David — and prophets — through Yeshayahu. Her modesty in Yehudah's house was the seed of that extraordinary legacy.

## Public shame

### Burning threat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִיא מוּצֵאת״. ״הִיא מִיתּוֹצֵאת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ סִימָנֶיהָ בָּא סַמָּאֵל וְרִיחֲקָן, בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וְקֵירְבָן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse describes Tamar's court hearing: **"When she was brought forth [*mutzet*],** she sent to her father-in-law, saying: By the man whose these are, am I with child" (Genesis 38:25). The Gemara comments: **It should have** stated: **When she was *mitutzet*.** The word *mutzet* also carries the implication of being found. What then, is taught by the use of that term? **Rabbi Elazar says: After her signs,** which she was using to prove that she was impregnated by Judah, **were brought out,** the evil angel **Samael came and distanced them** from each other in an attempt to prevent Judah's admission and Tamar's survival, which would enable the birth of King David. The angel **Gabriel** then **came and moved** the signs **closer** again.

The Torah says *hi mutzeit* — she was being taken out to be burned. Rashi reads *mutzeit* as meaning she was discovered, i.e., found and apprehended. Rabbi Elazar teaches: *l'achar she-nimtze'u simanim* — after the evidence — the signet, cord, and staff — had been found, she was being brought to judgment with them.

At that moment, *ba Samael* — the angel Samael came. Samael, associated with Esau and the forces of impurity, *ba ve-richakon* — came and distanced those items from her. The motivation is clear: Samael does not want this story to succeed, because from Tamar and Yehudah will ultimately come David and the messianic line — which spells the undoing of Esau's dominion. He therefore tried to remove the evidence that would save her.

*Ba Gavriel ve-kirvan* — Gavriel came and restored them to her. This is the deeper reading of *hi mutzeit*: the items were found again, restored by divine intervention, enabling her to present her case.

### Gabriel restore

The Gemara connects this to the Psalm title *la-menatze'ach al yonat eilem rechokim l'David* — a *michtam* of David. Rabbi Yochanan interprets: *mi-sha'ah she-nitrachaku simanim, na'aseit k'yonah ilemet* — at the moment the evidence was distanced from her, she became like a mute dove, with nothing to say in court and facing execution. David, who descended from Tamar, is the *michtam* — the one who emerged from that moment of divine rescue.

### Fire principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹחַ לוֹ לָאָדָם שֶׁיַּפִּיל עַצְמוֹ לְתוֹךְ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, וְאַל יַלְבִּין פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים. מְנָלַן — מִתָּמָר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is more amenable for a person to throw himself into a fiery furnace** if faced with the choice of publicly embarrassing another or remaining silent even if it leads to being burned, **and not humiliate another in public. From where do we** derive this? **From Tamar,** as she was prepared to be burned if Judah did not confess, rather than humiliate him in public.

The same chain of authorities — Rav Zutra bar Tuvia in the name of Rav, and some say Rabbi Chanin bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida, and some say Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai — teaches: *noach lo le-adam she-yapil atzmo le-toch kivshan ha-esh ve-al yalbin pnei chaveiro be-rabbim* — it is better for a person to throw himself into a burning furnace than to shame his fellow in public.

The proof is from Tamar: rather than directly confront Yehudah and shame him, she sent the evidence obliquely and allowed him to come forward himself. The *peshat* dimension is compelling on its own terms: a direct confrontation would likely have been denied or deflected; by giving him the opening, she allowed him to own the truth. But the *midrash* draws from this the deeper principle about the gravity of public shaming — *libun panim*, whitening the face, draining the blood from someone's cheeks — which is so severe that one should be prepared for personal suffering rather than inflict it on another.

### David humility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״לִמְנַצֵּחַ עַל יוֹנַת אֵלֶם רְחוֹקִים לְדָוִד מִכְתָּם״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנִּתְרַחֲקוּ סִימָנֶיהָ נַעֲשֵׂית כְּיוֹנָה אִילֶּמֶת. ״לְדָוִד מִכְתָּם״: שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנָּה דָּוִד, שֶׁהָיָה מָךְ וְתָם לַכֹּל. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״מִכְתָּם״ — שֶׁהָיְתָה מַכָּתוֹ תַּמָּה, שֶׁנּוֹלַד כְּשֶׁהוּא מָהוּל. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״מִכְתָּם״ — כְּשֵׁם שֶׁבְּקַטְנוּתוֹ הִקְטִין עַצְמוֹ אֵצֶל מִי שֶׁגָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, כָּךְ בִּגְדוּלָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This is as it is written: "For the leader, upon *yonat eilem reḥokim*, a psalm [*mikhtam*] of David"** (Psalms 56:1). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** the verse means: **From the moment that her signs were distanced [*reḥokim*], she became like a mute dove [*yona illemet*].** And the phrase **"a psalm [*mikhtam*] of David"** means: The one **from whom David emerged, as he was modest [*makh*] and flawless [*tam*] with everyone. Alternatively, *mikhtam*** indicates **that *makkato*,** the place on his body that would have required wounding [*makka*], **was complete [*tama*],** i.e., **that** David **was born circumcised. Alternatively, *mikhtam*** indicates that **just as in his youth** David **made himself small in front of one who was greater than him** in order **to learn Torah** from that person, **so too, when he became great** and was crowned king, he still behaved in this manner, so that his modesty, *makh*, was complete, *tam*, all of his life.

The connection between David's *michtam* and Tamar's modesty is thus sustained: the quality that saved Tamar — her *tzniyut* in Yehudah's house — becomes an inheritance for the Davidic line, expressed as the humility and purity that characterize David himself.

## Yehudah admits

### Recognition items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֶּר נָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: "And she said: **Discern, please,** whose are these, the signet, and the cords, and the staff" (Genesis 38:25).

*Haker na* — do you recognize these? Yehudah is presented with the signet, cord, and staff.

### Haker circularity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בְּ״הַכֶּר״ בִּישֵּׂר לְאָבִיו — בְּ״הַכֶּר״ בִּישְּׂרוּהוּ. בְּ״הַכֶּר״ בִּישֵּׂר: ״הַכֶּר נָא הַכְּתֹנֶת בִּנְךָ הִיא״. בְּ״הַכֶּר״ בִּישְּׂרוּהוּ: ״הַכֶּר נָא לְמִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: With** use of the word **discern** Judah **informed his father** that Joseph was lost, and also **with** use of the word **discern they informed** Judah about the signs. The Gemara explains: **With** the word **discern he informed** Jacob his father when he brought him the coat of Joseph and said to his father: "And they sent the coat of many colors, and they brought it to their father; and said: This have we found. **Discern now whether it is your son's coat** or not" (Genesis 37:32). **With** the word **discern they informed him:** "And she said: **Discern, please, whose are** these."

Rabbi Chama the son of Rabbi Chanina observes: *be-haker biser le-aviv* — with the word *haker* he informed his father. The brothers had earlier sent Yosef's coat dipped in blood to Yaakov, saying *haker na ha-ketonet bincha hi im lo* — recognize now, is this your son's coat? And immediately after that episode, the Torah records *va-yered Yehudah me-eit echav* — Yehudah went down from his brothers — and the story with Tamar begins. The same word *haker* now returns to measure out Yehudah's reckoning.

### Jacob echo

The literary and moral parallel is precise. By Yaakov, the sons kept their distance and presented the evidence indirectly — they let the father recognize the coat himself rather than confront him with what they had done. Here too, Tamar does not confront Yehudah directly but sends the evidence and lets him recognize it. In both cases, the person given the *haker* moment is allowed to arrive at the truth through their own recognition.

### Teshuva opening

This structure is what makes Yehudah's *teshuva* genuine. Had Tamar accused him directly, the dynamic would have been defensive — denial, deflection, dismissal. By presenting the evidence and giving him an opening, she allowed him to come forward and claim the truth himself, to own it. That is the moment of authentic *teshuva*.

### Recognition theme

The *mida k'neged mida* dimension here reaches further back still. The pattern of *vayaker* and *haker* runs from Yaakov and Esav — where Yaakov used the *gedei izim* and Yitzchak *lo hikiro* — through the brothers using *gedei izim* dipped in blood to make Yaakov recognize something false — through Yehudah's reckoning here — and further still to *vayaker Yosef et echav v'hem lo hikiruhu* by the Yosef story. The word *haker* is a thread stitched through the entire family narrative, and it is always about recognition, concealment, and the moment of moral accounting.

## Yehudah's confession

### Tzaddka statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּכֵּר יְהוּדָה וַיֹּאמֶר צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And Judah acknowledged them, and said: She is more righteous than I;** forasmuch as I gave her not to Shelah my son" (Genesis 38:26).

*Vayaker Yehudah vayomer tzadka mimeni* — Yehudah recognized and said: she is more righteous than I.

### Joseph vs Yehudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַב חָנִין בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: יוֹסֵף שֶׁקִּדֵּשׁ שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בַּסֵּתֶר — זָכָה וְהוֹסִיפוּ לוֹ אוֹת אַחַת מִשְּׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״עֵדוּת בִּיהוֹסֵף שָׂמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **This is** the same **as Rav Ḥanin bar Bizna says** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida says: Joseph, who sanctified the name of Heaven in private** by not committing adultery with the wife of Potiphar, **merited that one letter from the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He, was added to his** name, **as it is written: "He appointed it in Joseph [*bihosef* ] for a testimony in his name,** when He went forth against the land of Egypt" (Psalms 81:6). In this verse the name Joseph is written with an additional letter *heh*, found in the ineffable name of God.

Rabbi Chanin bar Bizna said in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida: Yosef, who sanctified God's name *ba-seter* — in private — by withstanding the advances of Potifar's wife, *zacha ve-hosifu lo ot achat mishmo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — merited that one letter of God's name was added to his: *eydut bi-Yehosef shemo*, the extra *heh* making his name יהוסף. Yehudah, who sanctified God's name *be-farhesia* — in public — by openly acknowledging his sin, *zacha ve-nikra kulo al shemo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — merited that his entire name contains the letters of God's name: יהודה contains a *yud*, a *heh*, a *vav*, and a *heh*.

### Bat kol promise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה שֶׁקִּדֵּשׁ שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — זָכָה וְנִקְרָא כּוּלּוֹ עַל שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוֹדָה וְאָמַר ״צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי״, יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: אַתָּה הִצַּלְתָּ תָּמָר וּשְׁנֵי בָּנֶיהָ מִן הָאוּר — חַיֶּיךָ שֶׁאֲנִי מַצִּיל בִּזְכוּתְךָ שְׁלֹשָׁה מִבָּנֶיךָ מִן הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** He continues: **Judah, who sanctified the name of Heaven in public, merited that his entire** name **is called by the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** for all the letters of the ineffable name of God are included within the name of Judah, with the addition of the letter *dalet*. **When he confessed and said: "She is more righteous than I," a Divine Voice went forth and said: You saved Tamar and her two children** in her womb **from** being burned by **the fire.** By **your life,** i.e., **in your merit, I will save three of your children from the fire.**

At the moment Yehudah said *tzadka mimeni*, a *bat kol* went forth: *ata hitzalta Tamar u-shnei baneha min ha-or* — you have saved Tamar and her two children from the fire.

### Three names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן נִינְהוּ — חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** And **who are they? Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah** (see Daniel, chapter 3).

*Chayecha she-ani matzil bi-zchutcha gimel mi-banecha min ha-or* — in your merit I will save three of your descendants from the fire. *Man ninhu? Chananya, Mishael, ve-Azarya* — those thrown into Nevuchadnezzar's furnace and saved.

### Divine origin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי״. מְנָא יָדַע? יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: מִמֶּנִּי יָצְאוּ כְּבוּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Judah said: **"She is more righteous than I [*mimmenni*]."** The word "*mimmenni*" can also be understood as "from me," with Judah thereby admitting that he is the father. The Gemara asks: **From where did he know** that it was in fact from him that Tamar was pregnant? The Gemara answers: **A Divine Voice went forth and said: From Me these hidden** matters **emerged,** and this woman will be the mother of royalty, which requires that Judah be the father.

The Gemara then reads *tzadka mimeni* not only as "she is more righteous than I" but as a divine utterance: *mimeni yatzu kevushim* — from Me went forth these hidden decrees. The entire chain of events, the *bat kol* declares, was God's design.

## Union resolution

### Separation read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא יָסַף עוֹד לְדַעְתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The same verse continues: **"And he knew her [*leda'atah*] again no more [*velo yasaf*],"** seemingly indicating that Judah did not engage in sexual intercourse with Tamar again.

*Ve-lo yasaf od le-da'atah* — he no longer knew her. The plain reading is that after this episode Yehudah and Tamar had no further sexual contact.

### Marriage alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל סָבָא חֲמוּהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַמֵּי: כֵּיוָן שֶׁיְּדָעָהּ — שׁוּב לֹא פָּסַק מִמֶּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel the Elder, father-in-law of Rav Shmuel bar Ami, says in the name of Rav Shmuel bar Ami:** The verse actually means that **once he knew** of **her** that her intentions were for the sake of Heaven, **he did not desist from** engaging in sexual intercourse with **her again,** as **it is written here:**

Shmuel Saba, the father-in-law of Rav Shmuel bar Ami, citing Rav Shmuel bar Ami himself, offers a different interpretation: *keivan she-yad'ah* — once he knew her, reading *da'atah* not as sexual knowledge but as recognition of her righteousness and her *l'shem shamayim* motivation, *shuv lo pasak mimena* — he did not cease from her. They began to live together as husband and wife.

### Textual variant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְלֹא יָסַף עוֹד לְדַעְתָּה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״קוֹל גָּדוֹל וְלֹא יָסָף״.
>
> **Translation:** **"*Velo yasaf od leda'atah*," and it is written there** at the giving of the Torah: "These words the Lord spoke unto all your assembly in the mount out of the midst of the fire, of the cloud, and of the thick darkness, **with a great voice and it went on no more [*velo yasaf*]"** (Deuteronomy 5:18), which is interpreted to mean: A great voice that did not cease.

The proof is a *gezeirah shavah*: *ve-lo yasaf od le-da'atah* is read in parallel with *kol gadol ve-lo yasaf* — the great voice that did not cease. Just as that voice continued without interruption, so too the relationship continued. The word *yasaf* in both contexts is read as cessation that did not occur.

### Happy ending

This alternative ending is remarkable in the context of a tractate about sexual sin and its consequences. Here the narrative that began with lust and moral failure — Yehudah mistaking his daughter-in-law for a prostitute — resolves into a proper marital union. Because he did the right thing, because he admitted, the story ends not in separation and shame but in legitimate relationship. The framework of *mida k'neged mida* is fulfilled in the positive direction: genuine *teshuva* and honest reckoning restore what was broken.

## Zonah vs kedesha

### Cult prostitute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיִּשְׁלַח אֶת הַשְּׂעִיר בְּיַד אִישׁ עִתִּי. דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנְנִי שׁוֹלֵחַ אֶת אַשְׁמָתִי וְגוֹ׳״ — וּדִרְשׁוּ רַבָּנַן אֵימָא בַּמִּדְיָן. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁהִשְׁמִיעַ קוֹל בַּמִּדְיָן — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע בְּנֵי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת דִּבְרֵי הַמִּדְיָנִית״. וְהַיְינוּ דַּהֲוַאי לְצִמִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is a continuation of the discussion on the incident of Judah and Tamar.

When Yehudah wished to send payment, he dispatched his friend the Adullamite, who was told to ask for *ha-kedeshah hi be-Einayim al ha-derech* — the *kedeshah* who sat by the road at Enaim. *Kedeshah* technically refers not to an ordinary prostitute but to a cult prostitute — a woman who served in pagan temples as part of idolatrous practice, a phenomenon attested widely across the ancient Near East.

### Face-saving

Robert Alter notes the discrepancy: when Yehudah first encountered her, the Torah says he thought her a *zonah* — an ordinary prostitute. But when he sends his friend to make payment, he instructs him to ask for a *kedeshah*. Alter's insight is that Yehudah was attempting to save face among the local population. Asking a neighbor to help locate a *zonah* you had slept with is frankly embarrassing. Asking for a *kedeshah* carries a different connotation — something more exotic, perhaps quasi-religious — and sounds more dignified than having to admit you visited a common streetwalker. It is, in short, a face-saving euphemism.

### No cult here

The neighbors' response is pointed: *lo hayta bizeh kedeshah* — there has been no *kedeshah* here. *Zonahs* we may know about; *kedeshas* we do not. The very attempt at euphemism fails, and the word the neighbors use brings Yehudah right back to where he started.
