## Opening & Intro

### Intro & Schedule

We are presently in the middle of Nedarim 53a, having moved somewhat ahead in the learning around Tisha B'Av. With that scheduling note aside, we turn directly to the substance of the *daf*.

### Wine, Oil, Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיַּיִן — מוּתָּר בְּיֵין תַּפּוּחִים. מִן הַשֶּׁמֶן — מוּתָּר בְּשֶׁמֶן שׁוּמְשְׁמִין. מִן הַדְּבַשׁ — מוּתָּר בִּדְבַשׁ תְּמָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who vows** that **wine** is forbidden **to** him **is permitted** to partake of **apple wine,** i.e., cider, as the unspecified term wine refers only to grape wine. One who vows that **oil is** forbidden **to** him is **permitted** to partake of **sesame oil,** as the unspecified term oil refers only to olive oil. One who vows that **honey** is forbidden to him is **permitted** to eat **date honey,** as the unspecified term honey refers only to bee honey.

The *mishnah* midway down 53a opens with a sequence of specific exclusions from *nedarim*. *Hanoder min ha-yayin mutar b'yein tapuchim* — one who vows against wine is still permitted to drink apple wine, something akin to hard cider. The *mishnah* then continues: *min ha-shemen* — one who vows against oil — *mutar b'shemen shumshumin*, is permitted to use sesame oil, since oil, in the primary sense, means olive oil. And *min ha-dvash* — one who vows against honey — *mutar b'dvash tmarim*, is permitted to use date syrup, since *dvash* in the language of the *mishnah* already refers to bee honey, *dvash dvorim*.

### Tapuach & Charoset

The word *tapuach* merits a brief note. In Tanakh, *tapuach* may not refer to what we think of as an apple at all; it may denote some variety of citrus fruit, possibly an *etrog* or something related. This has practical implications, for instance regarding the preparation of *charoset*, since the *gemara* requires that *charoset* include *tapuchim*. Extensive research has been done on the range of ingredients — spices, wine, fruit — that appear in *charoset* traditions across Jewish communities, and the botanical identity of *tapuach* bears directly on that question.

## Lashon Bnei Adam

### Usage vs. Definition

The overarching principle animating these *mishnayot* is *lashon bnei adam* — common, everyday speech. One might suppose that these exclusions rest on technical botanical or culinary definitions: apple wine is not truly wine, sesame oil is not truly oil. But the *bavli*'s understanding, at least, is different. The debate is not about technical taxonomy but about what a person actually means when he utters a particular word. The power of human speech to create the *neder* is bounded by what the speaker intends, which in turn is determined by ordinary usage.

The *mishnah*'s cases make this clear. When a person says *dvash* today, no one — even in the most health-conscious of communities — intends agave nectar or maple syrup. Everyone understands *dvash* to mean bee honey. The meaning of *dvash* in Tanakh is a separate question; there it likely referred in many contexts to fruit syrup rather than a bee product. But by *lashon chachamim*, by the time of the *mishnah*, *dvash* had come to mean bee honey, and that is the *lashon bnei adam* that governs the *neder*.

### Vinegar & Sour Foods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַחוֹמֶץ — מוּתָּר בְּחוֹמֶץ סִתְוָנִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** One who vows that **vinegar** is forbidden to him is **permitted** to partake of **vinegar of late grapes,** as vinegar is typically made from wine.

The *mishnah* continues: *min ha-chometz mutar b'chometz stavniyot* — one who vows against vinegar is still permitted to use the vinegar produced from the winter grapes discussed in the previous *mishnah*. These are grapes left on the vine until winter, either unripened or overripened, which yield a sour condiment used in cooking. Since this is not the standard vinegar a person has in mind when he uses the word *chometz*, it falls outside the scope of the *neder*.

### Leeks & Plants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַכְּרֵישִׁין — מוּתָּר בְּקַפְלוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** One who vows that **leeks** are forbidden to him is **permitted** to eat ***kaflutot*,** a type of leek.

*Min ha-krishim mutar ba-kappalotot* — one who vows against *krishim* (leeks) is permitted to eat *kappalotot* (a different variety of leek). Many of the plants that appear throughout *mishnayot* — the *luf* plant in *Masechet Shevi'it* being a notable example — require specialized study to identify. There are dedicated works on the flora and fauna of the Talmud, and they are indispensable for understanding such passages with precision.

### Field vs. Garden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַיָּרָק — מוּתָּר בְּיַרְקוֹת הַשָּׂדֶה, שֶׁהוּא שֵׁם לָווּיי.
>
> **Translation:** One who vows that **vegetables** are forbidden to him is **permitted** to eat wild **field vegetables, as this** type of vegetable has **a modifier** and is not referred to by the unspecified term vegetable.

The *mishnah* then addresses an important distinction: *hanoder min ha-yerek mutar b'yarkot ha-sadeh* — one who vows against *yerek* (vegetables) is permitted to eat field vegetables, *yarkot ha-sadeh*, because the *neder* is understood to apply only to garden vegetables, *yarkot ginah*, the ones cultivated and sold in markets. The reason given is *she-hu shem levai* — field vegetables carry a distinctive, qualifying name. The concept of *shem levai* appears in other areas of *halacha* as well; for instance, the *mishnah* in *Parah* rules that the *ezov* used for the *parah adumah* must be the plain variety, not one that carries a qualifying name such as *ezov yavan* or *ezov kochli*.

One may ask whether the distinction between garden and field vegetables turns on species — whether certain species are inherently wild and uncultivated — or on the actual condition of the plant, whether a particular specimen happened to grow wild even if its species has been domesticated. This remains an interesting open question.

## Oil & Rov

### Oil in EY & Bavel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַשֶּׁמֶן, בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל — מוּתָּר בְּשֶׁמֶן שׁוּמְשְׁמִין, וְאָסוּר בְּשֶׁמֶן זַיִת. וּבְבָבֶל — אָסוּר בְּשֶׁמֶן שׁוּמְשְׁמִין, וּמוּתָּר בְּשֶׁמֶן זַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who vows** that **oil** is forbidden **to** him, if he is **in Eretz Yisrael** he is **permitted to** eat **sesame oil and** is **prohibited from** eating **olive oil,** as in Eretz Yisrael the unspecified term oil refers to olive oil. **And** if he took the vow **in Babylonia, sesame oil** is **forbidden** to him, as oil in Babylonia was generally made from sesame seeds, **and** it is **permitted** for him **to** eat **olive oil,** which was rarely used there.

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* (introduced with *tanya*) from the *Tosefta* that elaborates on the *mishnah*'s ruling about oil. *Ha-noder min ha-shemen b'eretz Yisrael* — one who vows against oil in the Land of Israel — *mutar b'shemen shumshumin ve'asur b'shemen zayit*: he is permitted to use sesame oil but prohibited from olive oil, because in Eretz Yisrael, where olive trees are plentiful, olive oil is the default oil. *U'v'Bavel asur b'shemen shumshumin u'mutar b'shemen zayit* — in Bavel, where sesame is the primary oil crop, the *neder* against oil prohibits sesame oil and permits olive oil.

### Mixed Locales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁמִּסְתַּפְּקִין מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה — אָסוּר בָּזֶה וּבָזֶה.
> פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּרוּבָּא מִן חַד מִסְתַּפְּקִין. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אֵיזִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן סָפֵק אִיסּוּרָא לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** If he takes the vow in **a locale where** people **use** both **this** type of oil **and that** type, **he is prohibited from** eating both **this** type **and that.** The Gemara asks with regard to the last statement: **Isn't** it **obvious** that he is prohibited from eating both types of oil? The statement seems superfluous. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is not superfluous. It is **necessary** only to teach that this is the *halakha* even **where most** people **use** only **one** type of oil. **Lest you say: I should follow the majority** and permit the other kind of oil, the *baraita* **teaches us** that **an uncertain** Torah **prohibition** is treated **stringently.**

The *baraita* then addresses a place where both oils are in use: *asur ba-zeh u'va-zeh* — both are forbidden. This seems obvious — *pshita*! The *gemara* responds: *la tzricha* — no, we need this ruling. *D'rov mi-chad mistapkin mahu* — what is the law in a locale where the majority of oil use comes from one type? One might have thought we should follow the *rov* and only prohibit the majority oil. The *baraita* therefore teaches us that even in such a case, both are forbidden, because *safek issura l'chumra* — in a case of doubt regarding a prohibition, we rule stringently.

This raises a deeper question about the nature of this *rov*. The *Beis Yosef* (in *Siman* 208 on the *Tur*) notes a crucial distinction. The standard application of *rov* is a case where the majority of the population uses one oil exclusively and a minority uses the other exclusively. But what if everyone uses both oils, simply in different proportions? In that scenario, no one truly thinks of one oil as *the* oil to the exclusion of the other. As the *Beis Yosef* points out, when everyone in a locale uses both, the *rov* argument loses its force, because *b'lashon bnei adam* everyone there understands that "oil" refers to both. The *baraita*'s statement that *asur ba-zeh u'va-zeh* may therefore be directed at a case where some people use only one and others use only the other — with a majority using one — and the ruling comes to teach that even the minority oil is included, because *safek issura l'chumra*.

### Safek Deoraita

This application of *safek issura l'chumra* to *nedarim* raises a broader question: would we not simply apply *safek deoraita l'chumra* here? This gets into a significant dispute among *rishonim*. The *Rambam*, most prominently in *Hilchos Tumas Meis* but also elsewhere, maintains that the principle of *safek deoraita l'chumra* is itself rabbinic in origin. According to the *Rambam*, on a *deoraita* level every *safek* is technically permitted, since one cannot establish the prohibition conclusively. The one exception the *Rambam* acknowledges is the *asham talui*, which one brings for doubts about certain *aveiros* — and even there the *Rambam* reasons carefully. The *Rashba*, by contrast, holds that *safek deoraita l'chumra* is itself a *din deoraita*, built into the structure of Torah law itself.

Against the backdrop of this dispute, the specific invocation of *safek issura l'chumra* for *nedarim* raises a further point. Even according to the *Rashba*, who makes *safek deoraita l'chumra* a biblical principle, one might have asked: why should a self-imposed *neder* — an *issur* that the individual created through his own speech — receive the same treatment? That requires its own *halacha*. And according to the *Rambam*, who holds the general principle is only rabbinic, perhaps *nedarim* could be treated with special stringency even at a *deoraita* level, given that a person has personally undertaken the obligation through his own words.

### Speech & Authority

This points to a broader dimension of *nedarim*: the authority of human speech to create prohibition must, in some measure, connect the speaker to the *Hakadosh Baruch Hu*. When a person vows, he is in some way placed in a position analogous to divine legislation — his words carry binding force. That parallel may be precisely why the *safek* in *nedarim* is treated with comparable gravity to a *safek* in Torah-ordained law. A proof for *safek deoraita l'chumra* as an explicit Torah principle may also be derivable from *Parshas Ki Seitzei*, where the verse concerning cutting down trees during a siege seems to indicate that a tree of doubtful status — neither clearly a fruit tree nor clearly otherwise — may not be cut down, which would constitute a direct textual grounding for treating *safek* in *deoraita* matters stringently.

## Shemita & Vegetables

### Shemita Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיָּרָק, בִּשְׁאָר שְׁנֵי שָׁבוּעַ — אָסוּר בְּיַרְקוֹת הַגִּינָּה, וּמוּתָּר בְּיַרְקוֹת הַשָּׂדֶה. וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית — אָסוּר בְּיַרְקוֹת הַשָּׂדֶה, וּמוּתָּר בְּיַרְקוֹת הַגִּינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to **one who vows** that **vegetables** are forbidden **to** him, if he takes the vow **during the** first six **years of the seven-year** Sabbatical **cycle,** he is **prohibited from** eating **garden vegetables and permitted to** eat **field vegetables. But** if he takes the vow **during the Sabbatical Year,** he is **prohibited from** eating **field vegetables,** which are commonly eaten in the Sabbatical Year, **and** he is **permitted to** eat **garden vegetables,** which are rarely consumed during that period, as it is prohibited to work the land.

The *mishnah* continues: *hanoder min ha-yerek* — one who vows against vegetables — *b'she'ar shnei shavua asur b'yarkot ha-ginah u'mutar b'yarkot ha-sadeh* — during the six ordinary years of the seven-year cycle, the *neder* applies to garden vegetables and not to wild field vegetables, since the latter carry a *shem levai* and were presumably not in the person's mind. *U'va-shvi'it asur b'yarkot ha-sadeh u'mutar b'yarkot ha-ginah* — but during a *shemita* year, the ruling reverses: the *neder* applies to wild field vegetables, not to garden vegetables. Why? Because during *shemita*, one cannot ordinarily be cultivating annual garden vegetables; it would be impermissible to maintain such a garden in most configurations. So the vegetables one actually means when saying *yerek* during *shemita* are the wild, field varieties.

An interesting question arises: if one vowed against *yerek* in the third year, does the meaning of that vow shift when the *shemita* year arrives, reflecting the changed *lashon bnei adam* of that time? Or does the *mishnah* apply only to a *neder* taken during the *shemita* year itself? The answer is not clear.

### Imports & Abbahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מְבִיאִין יָרָק מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ. אֲבָל בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁמְּבִיאִין יָרָק מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel:** **They taught** that if he takes the vow in the Sabbatical Year he is permitted to eat garden vegetables **only** if he is **in a place where** people **do not bring vegetables from outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael. **However, in a place where** people **bring vegetables from outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael, where the *halakhot* of the Sabbatical Year do not apply, **to Eretz** Yisrael, he is **prohibited** from eating garden vegetables as well, as they are widely available, and therefore included in the unspecified word vegetable.

Rabbi Abbahu, quoting Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel, qualifies the ruling: *lo shanu ela bimkom she'ein mevi'in yerek mi-chutz la'aretz* — this applies only to a place where vegetables are not imported from outside Eretz Yisrael. If there is no active import trade, then whatever vegetables are available during *shemita* must be the permissible field vegetables, and the *neder* is understood accordingly. But *aval bimkom she-mevi'in yerek mi-chutz la'aretz asur* — in a place where vegetables are imported from outside Eretz Yisrael, garden vegetables remain available even during *shemita*, and therefore the *neder* would apply to those as well.

The *gemara* notes that this is *ka-tanai* — analogous to a Tannaic dispute — and records the debate: one *tanna* holds that one may not import vegetables into Eretz Yisrael from outside at all (not only during *shemita* but at any time), while Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel permits the importation.

### Kedushat Shvi'it

Before moving on, it is worth noting that eating fruits and vegetables with *kedushat shvi'it* is not itself prohibited. On the contrary, the *Rambam* holds that there is a *mitzvah* to eat *peirot shvi'it*, derived from the phrase *lachem l'ochlah*. The various *heterim* employed by the *rabbanut* or other agencies to allow *shvi'it* produce into the marketplace are a separate and complex topic, but the underlying point is that one should not be anxious about eating such produce — that is precisely what it is there for. The *Rav Kook* reportedly suggested that one should wash one's hands before eating *kedushat shvi'it* fruits, not necessarily with a *bracha*, but as an expression of deference to the *kedusha* of those fruits, which carry the holiness of the Land of Israel.

### Tumat Met & Soil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: אֵין מְבִיאִין יָרָק מִן חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ, רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מְבִיאִין יָרָק מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ. מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְבִיאִין? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִשּׁוּם גּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that this distinction is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** cited in a *baraita*: **One may not bring vegetables from outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael. **Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel says: One may bring vegetables from outside** of **Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the one who said** that **one may not bring** vegetables from outside of Eretz Yisrael? **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** It is **because a clod** of earth might be brought with the vegetables to Eretz Yisrael.

What is the reason for the prohibition on importing vegetables into Eretz Yisrael according to the first opinion? *Amar Rabbi Yirmiya mishum gush* — Rabbi Yirmiya explains it on account of a clod of soil. Vegetables brought from outside Eretz Yisrael may carry soil from *chutz la'aretz*, and the soil of foreign lands carries a rabbinic *tumah* known as *eretz ha'amim* — *tumat meit m'drabbanan* — because of the concern that the soil of those lands may be mixed with fragments of bone from bodies buried throughout the terrain. Since one cannot know, the Rabbis applied a blanket *tumah* to the soil of lands outside Eretz Yisrael. This concern would be relevant primarily for those who maintain *chaverim* standards of purity.

## Vegetable Categories

### Cabbage & Asparagus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַכְּרוּב — אָסוּר בְּאִיסְפַּרְגוֹס, מִן הָאִיסְפַּרְגוֹס — מוּתָּר בִּכְרוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who vows that **cabbage** is forbidden **to** him **is prohibited from** eating ***ispargus*,** as that is a type of cabbage. However, one who vows that ***ispargus*** is forbidden **to** him is **permitted to** eat **cabbage.**

The *mishnah* continues with additional cases. *Min ha-keruv assur ba-isparagus* — one who vows against cabbage (*keruv*) is also prohibited from eating *isparagus*, which appears to be a subcategory of cabbage. The Steinsaltz edition describes *isparagus* as an energy drink prepared from a leafy green vegetable, possibly kale — a characterization that reflects the culinary discourse of our own era. Other *sugyot* in *shas* suggest it may be a prepared drink of some kind, though the precise preparation is unclear. In any case, *isparagus* is a sub-category of *keruv*, and therefore a *neder* against the more inclusive term encompasses it. *Min ha-isparagus mutar ba-keruv* — but if one vowed only against *isparagus*, the *neder* does not extend to cabbage generally, since *isparagus* is the more specific subcategory.

### Etymology & Modern

The word *keruv* also means the *keruvim*, the angelic figures on the *aron*. Our ancestors did not place two golden cabbages on the *aron*, so one wonders what the two words share. One possibility is that the way a cabbage's leaves fold tightly over each other resembles the way the *keruvim*'s wings enclosed their bodies — the same quality of folding inward and covering. In modern Hebrew, *keruvit* means cauliflower, while Brussels sprouts, being simply small cabbages, are called *keruv katan*. Whether the etymological connection between *keruv* the vegetable and *keruvim* the angels is biblical or post-biblical is itself an interesting question, as *keruv* in the sense of cabbage may not appear in Tanakh.

### Grits & Mikpeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַגְּרִיסִין — אָסוּר בְּמִקְפָּה, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר. מִן הַמִּקְפָּה — מוּתָּר בִּגְרִיסִין. מִן הַמִּקְפָּה — אָסוּר בִּשּׁוּם, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר. מִן הַשּׁוּם — מוּתָּר בְּמִקְפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** One who vows that **pounded beans** are forbidden **to** him **is prohibited from** eating pounded bean **stew [*mikpa*]. However, Rabbi Yosei** rules that he is **permitted** to eat it. One who vows that pounded bean **stew** is forbidden **to** him is **permitted to** eat **pounded beans** according to all opinions. One who vows that **stew** is forbidden **to** him **is prohibited from** eating the **garlic** of the stew. **However, Rabbi Yosei** rules that he is **permitted** to eat the garlic. One who vows that **garlic** is forbidden **to** him **is permitted to** eat **stew.**

*Min ha-grisin assur ba-mikpeh* — one who vows against *grisin* (some kind of coarsely ground grain or bean, prepared into a thick porridge — roughly analogous to grits) is also prohibited from eating *mikpeh*, a specific prepared dish, perhaps a bean soup or grain porridge. *Mikpeh* appears elsewhere in *shas* in *Masechet Sukkah*, where the question of *yardu geshamim* (rain falling into the *sukkah*) is resolved when *mi-she-tisrach ha-mikpeh* — when the *mikpeh* is ruined by the rain. *Mikpeh* is more inclusive than plain *grisin*; it is a dish made with *grisin* as a principal ingredient.

### Garlic & Mikpeh

> [This is the same blockquote as the previous section for "Grits & Mikpeh," which continues with the next ruling]

*Min ha-mikpeh assur ba-shum* — one who vows against *mikpeh* is even prohibited from eating garlic, because garlic was evidently a principal ingredient in *mikpeh*. When a person says *mikpeh*, the garlic is so central to that dish that even plain garlic falls within his intent. *Ve-Rabbi Yose matir* — Rabbi Yose disagrees and permits it, since he does not consider garlic to be encompassed by the word *mikpeh*.

This entire sequence of cases turns on the principle of *lashon bnei adam*. We are not living in the era of the *mishnah* and cannot know with certainty what each term evoked for its speakers. That is precisely why contemporary *poskim* who receive *shailot* about *nedarim* — and people do take *nedarim* inadvertently or with less than full appreciation of their severity — must carefully investigate what the person actually meant and whether grounds exist for *hatarah*, *petach*, or *charata*.

*Min ha-shum mutar ba-mikpeh* — but if one vowed only against garlic, he is permitted to eat *mikpeh*, because *mikpeh* is a complete dish in its own right, not reducible to garlic alone.

### Lentils & Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים — אָסוּר בַּאֲשִׁישִׁים, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר. מִן הָאֲשִׁישִׁים — מוּתָּר בַּעֲדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** One who vows that **lentils** are forbidden **to** him **is prohibited from** eating ***ashishim*,** a dish made from lentils. However, **Rabbi Yosei permits** it. It is agreed by all opinions that one who vows that ***ashishim*** is forbidden **to** him **is permitted to** eat **lentils.**

*Min ha-adashim assur ba-ashishim* — one who vows against lentils is prohibited from eating *ashishim*, a kind of lentil cake or bread made from lentil flour, because lentil bread is included in the person's intent when he says *adashim*. *Rabbi Yose matir* — Rabbi Yose permits it, holding that *ashishim* carries its own distinct name and is therefore a separate food. *Min ha-ashishim mutar ba-adashim* — but one who vows against *ashishim* specifically is permitted to eat plain lentils, since *ashishim* is the more specific term.

### Wheat Singular/Plural

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חִטָּה חִטִּין שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — אָסוּר בָּהֶן בֵּין קֶמַח בֵּין הַפַּת. ״גְּרִיס גְּרִיסִין שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — אָסוּר בָּהֶן בֵּין חַיִּים בֵּין מְבוּשָּׁלִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״קֻוֽנָּם גְּרִיס אוֹ חִטָּה שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — מוּתָּר לָכוֹס חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: ***Ḥitta*,** wheat in singular form, or ***ḥittim*,** wheat in plural form, are *konam* for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** them, he is **prohibited from** eating wheat, **whether** as **flour or bread.** If one says: **Pounded bean** or **pounded beans** are *konam* for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** them, he is **prohibited from** eating **them, whether raw or cooked. Rabbi Yehuda says** that if one says: **Pounded bean or *ḥitta* is *konam*** for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** them, he is **permitted to chew** them **raw,** as that is not the normal way to eat them, and therefore was not included in the intention of the vow.

The *mishnah* then discusses *chita chitin she-eini to'eim* — one who says "wheat I will not taste" — *asur bahen bein kemach bein ha-pat*: it is prohibited whether as flour or as baked bread. The word *chitin* in *mishnaic* Hebrew refers both to the grain and to wheat bread, since bread colloquially took its name from its primary ingredient. An interesting question arises about raw flour: since no one eats raw flour, one might argue a person never had raw flour in mind when saying *chitin*. Yesterday's *daf* touched on a related *machloket* about foods that are inedible in their natural state.

A *baraita* introduced with *tanya* records Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel's view:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: ״חִטָּה שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — אָסוּר לֶאֱפוֹת וּמוּתָּר לָכוֹס. ״חִטִּים שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — אָסוּר לָכוֹס וּמוּתָּר לֶאֱפוֹת. ״חִטָּה חִטִּין שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — אָסוּר בֵּין לָכוֹס בֵּין לֶאֱפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel says** that if one vows: ***Ḥitta*** is *konam* for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** it, he is **prohibited from** eating **baked** wheat, i.e., baked wheat bread, **but** he is **permitted to chew** wheat grains. If one says: ***Ḥittim*** are *konam* for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** them, he is **prohibited from chewing** wheat grains **but** is **permitted to bake** them, as this term is referring to grains of wheat. If one says: ***Ḥitta*** or ***ḥittin*** are *konam* for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** them ,he is **prohibited both from chewing** wheat grains and **from baking** wheat bread.

*chita she-eini to'eim* — if one says "wheat" in the singular — *asur le'afot* — it is prohibited to bake it into bread — *u'mutar lachos* — but permitted to chew raw kernels. Because *chita* in the singular refers to *pat shel chita*, a loaf of bread from wheat flour. But *chitin she-eini to'eim* — if one uses the plural form *chitin* — *assur lachos u'mutar la'afot* — it is prohibited to chew on raw kernels but permitted to bake, because *chitin* refers to the wheat grains themselves.

Rabbi Yehuda had earlier made a similar distinction between the singular *gris* and the plural *grisin*. The same logic applies: *gris she-eini to'eim assur levashel u'mutar lachos* — a vow against a single *gris* prohibits preparing it as porridge but permits chewing the raw grain; *grisin she-eini to'eim assur lachos u'mutar levashel* — a vow against the plural *grisin* prohibits chewing and permits boiling.

### Mishna Methods

This raises an important methodological question about how to read *mishnah*. When a later *tanna* states a position that overlaps with an earlier one, is he disagreeing, or is he clarifying what the earlier *tanna* already meant? The tendency to minimize *machloket* as much as possible — reading later opinions as specifications or elaborations rather than as genuine disagreements — is a recognizable approach, and one that the Artscroll edition tends to favor. Alternatively, one can read the editorial history of the *mishnah* as genuinely incorporating later opinions that diverge from earlier ones, which is not a defect but simply how law develops. Neither approach is obviously wrong; they reflect different methodological commitments about how *mishnah* was composed and what its editors intended.

## Perek 7: Yerek

### Gourds & Yerek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיָּרָק — מוּתָּר בַּדִּלּוּעִין, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: For **one who vows** that **vegetables** are forbidden **to** him, it **is permitted** for him **to** eat **gourds,** as people typically do not include gourds in the category of vegetables; **and Rabbi Akiva prohibits** him from eating gourds.

*Hadran alach ha-noder min ha-mevushal* — we have completed the sixth *perek* of *Nedarim* and begin the seventh. The seventh *perek* opens: *ha-noder min ha-yerek mutar b'diluim* — one who vows against *yerek* (vegetables) is permitted to eat *delu'im* (gourds, squash, pumpkins). One possible explanation: *yerek* may literally mean *greens*, referring specifically to leafy or green-colored produce, and *delu'im*, which come in shades of orange, yellow, and other colors, do not fit that description. Just as we speak of "getting some greens" and do not mean tomatoes or orange bell peppers, *yerek* may exclude gourds on coloristic or categorial grounds.

### Shaliach Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא אוֹמֵר אָדָם לִשְׁלוּחוֹ ״קַח לִי יָרָק״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא מָצָאתִי אֶלָּא דִּלּוּעִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Akiva: But doesn't a person say to his agent: Purchase vegetables for me, and** the agent, after failing to find vegetables, returns with gourds and **says: I found only gourds?** This indicates that gourds are not considered vegetables.

*Ve-Rabbi Akiva oser* — Rabbi Akiva, however, prohibits *delu'im* as well. The *chakhamim* respond: *va-halo amar adam l'shlucho kach li yerek ve-hu omer lo matzasi ella diluim* — is it not the case that if a person sends a messenger (*shaliach*) to buy vegetables, and the *shaliach* returns saying "I'm sorry, they didn't have any *yerek*, they only had *delu'im*," the *meshale'ach* would be dissatisfied? The *shaliach* came back empty-handed because he concluded that gourds are not *yerek*. Does this not prove that *delu'im* are not included in *yerek*?

### Legumes & Merchants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: כֵּן הַדָּבָר, אוֹ שֶׁמָּא אוֹמֵר הוּא ״לֹא מָצָאתִי אֶלָּא קִיטְנִית״? אֶלָּא, שֶׁהַדִּלּוּעִין בִּכְלַל יָרָק, וְקִיטְנִית אֵינוֹ בִּכְלַל יָרָק.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to them: The matter is so,** and that proves my opinion; **or perhaps,** does the agent return and **say: I found only legumes? Rather,** it is apparent **that gourds are included in** the category of **vegetables,** although they differ from other vegetables, and therefore, the agent purchases gourds and explains that he found only gourds. **And legumes are not included in** the category of **vegetables,** and that is why the agent dispatched to purchase vegetables would not purchase legumes at all.

Rabbi Akiva responds: *kein ha-davar* — is that really what would happen? *O shema omer hu lo matzati ella kitnit* — would the *shaliach* not rather say, "They didn't have anything except beans"? That is, the *shaliach* would return empty-handed when there were only *kitniyot* available — because *kitniyot* are clearly not *yerek* — but if *delu'im* were available, the *shaliach* would simply buy them and bring them back. *Ela she-ha-diluim bichlal yerek ve-kitniyot eino bichlal yerek* — gourds are included in the category of *yerek*, while beans are not. According to Rabbi Akiva, then, the *chakhamim*'s scenario is not realistic; a sensible *shaliach* would bring back the gourds. It is only beans that would provoke the response "they didn't have vegetables."

### Kitniyot Category

This brings us to the question of what *kitniyot* means as a category. We are familiar with the prevalent *Ashkenazi minhag* — and also the custom of some North African communities — to avoid certain foods on *Pesach*, including rice, various legumes, mustard, and possibly sesame. The word *kitniyot* reflects a broad taxonomic category already present in *mishnaic* law. The *Rambam* in *Hilchos Kilayim* recognizes a tripartite division of cultivated plants: *tevuah* (grain), *yerakot* (vegetables), and *kitniyot* — a broad intermediate category with its own *halachot* for *kilayim* and other areas. The *Ashkenazi Pesach minhag* of *kitniyot* does not necessarily encompass every plant in this rabbinic category, though the question of how broadly to apply it has generated ongoing debate. The rhetorical moves made by those who question the *minhag* — pointing to coffee beans or cocoa beans — reflect genuine engagement with where the category's boundaries lie.

### Ful Hamitzri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָסוּר בְּפוֹל הַמִּצְרִי לַח, וּמוּתָּר בַּיָּבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** for one who vows that vegetables are forbidden to him, it **is prohibited to** eat **the fresh cowpea**, which is considered a vegetable, **and** it **is permitted to** eat **dry** cowpea, which is not a vegetable.

The *mishnah* concludes the *perek* with: *ve-assur b'ful ha-mitzri lach u'mutar b'yavesh* — one who vows against *yerek* is prohibited from eating fresh Egyptian beans (*ful ha-mitzri*) but permitted to eat them dried. The *ful* in Arabic-speaking cuisine of North Africa is a familiar staple, and this is almost certainly the same food. When fresh, *ful ha-mitzri* is a vegetable; when dried and hard, it becomes a *kitnit*. Green beans provide a useful analogy: fresh off the plant, with the pod still bright green and tender, they are a vegetable. Fully dried, the beans inside the pod become a legume.

This distinction has practical *halachic* ramifications: many families are willing to eat fresh green beans on *Pesach* even while observing the *minhag* of *kitniyot*, since the fresh bean in its pod is a vegetable, not a dried legume. The same transition point applies to peas — fresh pea pods are a vegetable; split peas are a legume. Fresh corn on the cob occupies a similar conceptual space: fresh, it reads as a vegetable; dried and ground, it approaches the status of a grain. The moment of transition from fresh vegetable to dried grain or legume is itself a meaningful taxonomic and *halachic* threshold.

## Gemara: Shaliach Role

### Yarkei Kedeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיָּרָק כּוּ׳. וְהָא מִן יָרָק נְדַר! אָמַר עוּלָּא: בְּאוֹמֵר ״יַרְקֵי קְדֵרָה עָלַי״, וְדִילְמָא יָרָק הַנֶּאֱכָל בִּקְדֵרָה קָאָמַר? בְּאוֹמֵר ״יָרָק הַמִּתְבַּשֵּׁל בִּקְדֵרָה עָלַי״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna: For **one who vows** that **vegetables** are forbidden **to** him, it is permitted to eat gourds, and Rabbi Akiva prohibits him from eating gourds. The Gemara questions Rabbi Akiva's ruling: **But** how can his vow include gourds, which are fruits and not vegetables; **didn't he vow** to refrain **from** eating **vegetables? Ulla said:** The mishna is referring **to one who said: Vegetables** cooked **in a pot** are forbidden **to me.** Gourds are included in the category of vegetables cooked in a pot. The Gemara asks: **And** if that is what he said, **perhaps he is saying: A vegetable that is eaten in a pot,** i.e., a vegetable that is added to flavor the food cooked in the pot, is forbidden to me? The Gemara answers: The mishna is referring **to one who said: A vegetable that is cooked in a pot** is forbidden **to me,** a statement that can include gourds.

The *gemara* now enters into the dispute between the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Akiva more analytically. A *meitivei* presses: if one who vows *mimini yerek* — from types of vegetables — is permitted, what is the exact content of the *neder* and what does it tell us about the dispute? *Amar Ulla be'omer yarkei kedeira alai* — Ulla explains: we are dealing with a case where the person said not simply *yerek* but *yarkei kedeira* — pot vegetables — are forbidden to me. This introduces a specific subcategory. In this framing, the *neder* targets pot vegetables specifically, leaving open the possibility of sending the *shaliach* to purchase a different kind of *yerek* that does not go in a pot. The dispute between the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Akiva would then concern whether *delu'im* qualify as *yarkei kedeira* or not.

### Cooked in Pot

The *gemara* challenges Ulla's reading: *ve-dilma yerek ha-ne'echal ba-kedeira ka'amar* — perhaps the person said a vegetable *eaten* from a pot, or as the alternate formulation has it, *yerek ha-mitbashel ba-kedeira*, a vegetable *cooked* in a pot? In that case, it is not a named subcategory but a universal description — any vegetable cooked in a pot — and that would encompass essentially all vegetables. Under this reading, Ulla cannot use the *yarkei kedeira* framing to explain why a *shaliach* might be sent to purchase a different kind of *yerek*: if all cooked vegetables are included, there is no remainder for the *shaliach* to retrieve.

### Messenger Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דִּצְרִיךְ שְׁלִיחָא לְאִמְּלוֹכֵי עֲלַהּ, לָאו מִינֵיהּ הוּא. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְּמִימְּלִיךְ שְׁלִיחָא עֲלַהּ — מִינֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis **disagree?** The Gemara explains that **the Rabbis maintain: Any item with regard to which an agent must consult** the person who dispatched him before purchasing it, **is not** considered the same **type.** Since the agent must ask whether he can purchase gourds, apparently they are not a vegetable. **And Rabbi Akiva maintains: Any item with regard to which an agent** must **consult is** considered the same **type.** With regard to food of a different type, he does not consult.

With Ulla's resolution set aside, the *gemara* proceeds directly to the underlying dispute: *u'v'mai kamiflagi?* — what exactly do the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Akiva disagree about? *Rabbanan savri: kol milta de-tzarikh shaliach le-imlukhei aleih, lav mineihu* — the *rabbanan* hold that if the *meshale'ach* would consider dismissing the *shaliach* for having made the purchase, that demonstrates it was not what the *meshale'ach* meant. When we say a person would send back the *shaliach* for bringing *delu'im* when asked for *yerek*, that proves *delu'im* are not *yerek*. The standard of interpretation is the *meshale'ach*'s intent and expectation.

*Ve-Rabbi Akiva savar: kol milta de-mamlikh shaliach aleih, mineihu* — Rabbi Akiva holds that whatever falls within the *shaliach*'s range of reasonable judgment is included in the category. If the *shaliach* could plausibly have concluded that *delu'im* fulfill the request for *yerek*, then *delu'im* are indeed *yerek*. The *meshale'ach*'s subjective displeasure is not the determining factor; what matters is whether the *shaliach* had a reasonable basis for his decision.

### Shaliach Extension

This disagreement reflects a deeper question about the nature of *shelichut*. Is a *shaliach* an extension of the *meshale'ach* — *shaliach mi-din yad* — in which case the *shaliach* has no independent standing and every decision he makes is evaluated purely by reference to what the *meshale'ach* would have wanted? Under this model, the *rabbanan*'s position is entirely natural: the *shaliach* is only an arm of the *meshale'ach*, and the *meshale'ach*'s intent is the sole criterion. Alternatively, is the *shaliach* a newly empowered halakhic agent — someone to whom the *meshale'ach* has granted genuine authority to act? Under that model, the *shaliach* retains a measure of autonomous judgment, and the question shifts to what a reasonable agent in that role would determine.

### Shlucho Kemoso

This second model, in its most radical form, implies that what the *shaliach* decides *is* the *meshale'ach*'s will — legally, by definition. When the *meshale'ach* empowers a *shaliach* with full *shelichut*, he transfers his *ratzon* to the *shaliach*: whatever the *shaliach* does, the *meshale'ach* is bound by it, not because the *shaliach* guessed correctly, but because the *meshale'ach* empowered the *shaliach* to stand in his place. *Shlucho shel adam ke-moso* — a person's agent is as the person himself. This principle generates many further questions across *shas*, including whether *shaliach na'aseh eid* and whether *shaliach oseh shaliach*, and it remains a central topic in the *lomdishe sfarim* on *shelichut* broadly. We will continue from approximately the midpoint of 54a tomorrow morning, which will be *Rosh Chodesh Av*.
