# Bekhorot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mumim for Kohanim

### Opening & Scope

Daf 43 opens a new *perek*, *Mumim Ellu*, transitioning from the earlier discussion of blemishes in animals to the parallel category of blemishes in *kohanim*. The connection is organic: while the *masechta* is primarily concerned with the *bechor*, the issue of *mumim* naturally bridges to the priestly service. Although this is not strictly about the *bechor* in the narrow sense, the conceptual framework — what constitutes a disqualifying blemish — applies across both domains, and the Mishnah treats them together.

### Kohanim-Specific Blemish…

The Mishnah introduces a list of blemishes that are additional to those shared with animals — conditions that disqualify a *kohen* but have no exact parallel in the laws governing animal offerings. These include distinctive head shapes referred to as *kilyon*, *liftan*, and *makban*; deformities of the neck known as *shakut* and *shamut*; a condition called *skifat* affecting the back of the skull; and *ba'alei chatoteret*, those with a hunchback or dorsal growth. The Mishnah then addresses *kireach* — baldness — and records a dispute concerning whether a hunchback is disqualifying.

### Fixed vs. Transitory

The Mishnah opens with the principle that *mumim elu*, the blemishes enumerated in the previous *perek*, whether *kevi'in* (fixed) or *overin* (transitory), *poslin ba'adam* — disqualify a *kohen* from performing the *avodah*. The conceptual basis is clear: just as a *bechor* with a transitory blemish cannot be offered as a *korban* in the *Beis HaMikdash* at that moment, so too a *kohen* with even a transitory blemish may not serve. The parallel holds on both sides of the equation.

### Head Shape Blemishes

The Gemara provides anatomical descriptions of each unusual head shape. *Kilyon* — likely derived from the root meaning "to come to a point" — describes a triangular, pointed head, essentially the shape of an inverted cone. *Liftan*, the turnip, is the inverse: wide at the top and pointed at the bottom, a description that carries rather unflattering connotations. *Makban* — which shares its root with the *Maccabees*, from *makevet*, a hammer — describes what might be called a hammerhead configuration: a large skull, narrow neck, with the cranium protruding markedly both in front and behind, not unlike a hammerhead shark.

### More Physical Deformities

The condition *shakut* refers to a head that is slanted in the front, or alternatively, to a person whose neck is so short that the head appears buried or sunk between the shoulder blades. The Gemara clarifies *shakut* and *shamut* as two neck-related deformities: *shakut* is a neck that appears hidden, depressed; *shamut* is the opposite — thin and narrow, so that the head appears to protrude outward from the neck on both sides. *Skifat* describes a back of the head that is flattened or cut off, lacking the normal curvature; the Gemara invokes the folk expression *shakil pisa*, "a piece was removed," to capture how it appears. The *ba'al chatoteret* is the hunchback — the word *chatoteret* being the same used for a camel's hump — and it is this category that generates a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages.

### Baldness Debate

The Mishnah declares *kireach pasul* — a bald *kohen* is disqualified. What is the definition of *kireach*? The Mishnah says: *kol she'ein lo shita shel sei'ar mukefet me'ozen l'ozen* — one who lacks even a single row of hair encircling the head from ear to ear. The Gemara, as will be seen, will subject this definition to careful scrutiny, differentiating among various patterns of baldness and concluding that only particularly bizarre manifestations are problematic, not normal baldness.

### Visibility in Service

A natural question arises: many of these conditions — misshapen heads, hunchbacks — would have been partially obscured during *avodah*. *Kohanim* wore the *mitznefet*, and priestly garments covered much of the body. One might therefore wonder whether the concern is primarily visual — about what the people assembled in the *Azarah* see — or whether these are intrinsic disqualifications independent of visibility.

This question proves consequential. The Gemara, as will emerge, distinguishes sharply between a genuine *mum*, a condition that *machil avoda* (desecrates the service), and the categories of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon* and *marit ha'ayin*, which are lesser in halakhic force. The fact that many of the conditions in this Mishnah are inherently visual — head shapes, baldness, a visible dorsal growth — suggests that visibility is indeed a factor. Yet the Gemara does not limit these disqualifications to visible locations alone, which deepens the question. For now, the observation is worth carrying forward: are the *kohanim*-specific disqualifications limited to what would be seen by the people bringing *korbanot*, or do they extend beyond that?

One broader framework for understanding the entire category is that the *kohen* in the *Beis HaMikdash* occupies a role analogous in certain respects to the *keilim* of the *Mikdash* — not merely a functionary but a sanctified instrument of the service. The *Rambam*, in discussing the laws of *kohanim*, speaks of *klei hamikdash vehamishartim bo*, treating the priests and the vessels together. In *Zevachim*, the *kohen* must serve barefoot, his feet touching the floor of the *Azarah*, so that he connects to the *kedusha* of the *Beis HaMikdash*. Seen through this lens, the aesthetic demands placed upon *kohanim* reflect the same principle as the aesthetic demands placed upon the *menorah* and the *mizbe'ach* — objects used in the service must conform to a certain standard of wholeness and appearance. This framing is perhaps more satisfying than alternative explanations, and it will be developed further when the Gemara addresses the category of *nitraba bemitzvot*.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Why Derive Lists

The Gemara opens with a structural question: why should we assume that the full list of *mumim* applying to animals also applies to *kohanim*, and vice versa? The *pesukim* in *Emor* list specific blemishes for animals brought as *korbanot*, and separately list blemishes that disqualify *kohanim*. There is significant overlap, but the lists are not identical. Why not treat each list as discrete and exhaustive for its own domain?

### Textual Gaps

The Gemara identifies the specific asymmetries. *Yabbelet* — a type of wart or skin growth — is written in the list of *mumim* for animals but is not mentioned in the Torah's list of blemishes for humans. Conversely, *dak* and *tavlul* — conditions involving the eye, specifically a line penetrating the pupil that mixes white and colored areas — appear in the Torah's list for humans but are not mentioned for animals. If the lists are distinct, *yabbelet* would not apply to *kohanim*, and the eye condition would not apply to animals.

### Garav & Yalefet Link

The Gemara resolves this through a *gezeira shava*. A *baraita* teaches: since *garav* and *yalefet* — two types of skin conditions — appear in both the human list and the animal list, this shared terminology creates a verbal link allowing us to apply each list to the other. The *gezeira shava* on *garav–garav* and *yalefet–yalefet* permits us to learn: whatever is written regarding blemishes for humans applies equally to animals, and whatever is written for animals applies to humans.

### Mufne Requirement

The Gemara raises the standard requirement for a valid *gezeira shava*: the linking words must be *mufne* — superfluous in their own context, freed from the need to carry independent meaning, so that they are available for the hermeneutical link. If the words were not superfluous, the *gezeira shava* would be subject to logical challenge. The Gemara demonstrates that *garav* is indeed superfluous, because the Torah could have written only *yalefet* and derived *garav* through a *kal v'chomer*: if the less severe skin condition (*yalefet*) constitutes a *mum*, certainly the more severe and visually repugnant *garav* does. Since the Torah wrote *garav* explicitly despite this *kal v'chomer*, it must be to render the word available for the *gezeira shava*.

### Asymmetric Learning

With the *gezeira shava* established, the Gemara explains why one could not simply reason by logic from one list to the other — which would have made the *gezeira shava* unnecessary. The reason is that logical inference is blocked in both directions, and because the *gezeira shava* must be *mufne*, the superfluous writing is justified.

### Animals on Altar

Why can one not learn humans from animals? *Sheken hu atzmo karev legabei mizbe'ach* — because the animal itself is placed upon the *mizbe'ach*. The aesthetics demanded of something offered on the altar may be held to a higher standard than what is demanded of a person who performs a service. Therefore, a longer or stricter list of *mumim* for animals might be justified by that distinctive quality, and one cannot straightforwardly apply the animal list to humans.

### Nitraba Bemitzvot

Conversely, why can one not learn animals from humans? *Sheken nitraba bemitzvot* — because a human being has more *mitzvot*. An animal has no *mitzvot* at all. This phrase requires interpretation, and on the surface it is challenging: the abundance of *mitzvot* does not obviously explain why a *kohen*'s appearance should be held to a stricter standard.

### Kodushat Kohanim

The Gemara later explains this through the concept of *kedushato meruba* — a greater intrinsic *kedusha*. Rashi understands this as a general statement about human sanctity through *mitzvot*. However, the commentary *Reishis Bikurim* offers an interpretation that is both more contextually precise and more theologically satisfying: *nitraba bemitzvot* refers specifically to the *mitzvot* of the *kehuna* — the priestly service in the *Beis HaMikdash*. On this reading, the heightened standard applied to *kohanim* flows from their specific sacred role as *meshartim*, servants, in the *Mikdash*, not from a generic statement about human beings and *mitzvot*. This reading aligns with the framework described above — the *kohen* as a sanctified instrument of the service, analogous to the *keilim* of the *Mikdash* — and limits the aesthetic concern appropriately to the *Beis HaMikdash* context. It would be a mistake to extrapolate from these *halachot* any general principle about the worth of persons with physical differences.

Importantly, these disqualifications do not prevent a *kohen* from receiving *terumah* or sharing in the *korbanot*. They exclusively limit actual participation in the *avodah*. This narrowness itself is significant.

### Why Repeat Words

The Gemara then asks a more refined question: granted that *garav* and *yalefet* must appear in both contexts to enable the *gezeira shava*, why was it necessary for the Torah to repeat the entire list in each context? One might imagine the Torah writing the full shared list only once, the distinctive *mumim* of each category separately, and then writing *garav* and *yalefet* in both places as the linking mechanism — without repeating everything.

The Gemara considers the various permutations. If the Torah wrote the full list only by humans and the linking words by animals: there are *mumim* distinctive to animals, such as *kalut* (fused hooves) and *chalut* (problems with the teeth roots), that have no parallel in humans — they could not have been included in a human-only list and then applied to animals. If the Torah wrote the full list only by animals and linked to humans: there are conditions distinctive to humans, such as *giben* (eyebrow deformity — one eyebrow or no eyebrows, which animals do not have) and *charum* (a flat or absent nasal bridge between the eyes, a condition that does not present in animals because their noses are positioned below their eyes), which would have been omitted.

What if the Torah wrote the shared conditions only once, wrote each distinctive list separately, and used *garav* and *yalefet* as the link? This is exactly the question posed — and the answer comes from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *kol parsha shene'emrah v'nishnis, lo nishnis ela bishvil davar shenischadesh bah* — any *parasha* that is stated and then repeated is repeated only for the sake of one new thing it introduces. The Torah does not need to justify every word of a repeated passage. If the Torah needed to introduce even one distinctive element in that passage, it would naturally restate the broader context without the reader being required to find independent significance in every repeated item. This is the school of *dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of human language — associated with Rabbi Yishmael, as distinct from Rabbi Akiva's principle of *omnisignificance*, which would require every repeated letter and word to carry independent meaning.

The two schools represent a genuine and enduring divide in hermeneutics. Rabbi Akiva is *doresh kotzei osiyot* — he draws meaning from the very crowns atop the letters. Rabbi Yishmael counters: *afilu atah doresh bas u'vas kol hayom kulo eini shomeia lach* — even if you expound extra *vavs* all day, I will not accept it. It is worth noting that the *baraita* we recite each morning as part of the liturgy is Rabbi Yishmael's — the thirteen hermeneutical principles — yet that *baraita* introduces the *Sifra*, the halakhic midrash on *Vayikra*, which follows Rabbi Akiva's school. The relationship between the two approaches is not always a clean opposition.

Nachum Ish Gamzu, who had developed the approach of drawing meaning from the particle *et* — the same technique picked up by Rabbi Akiva — ultimately ceased applying it in one instance, stating *et Hashem elokecha tira peiresh*. Rabbi Akiva then took up that very exposition: *et Hashem elokecha l'rabot talmidei chachamim* — and indeed Nachum Ish Gamzu was Rabbi Akiva's own teacher. Rabbi Akiva in this sense inherited and extended the interpretive mantle.

### Rava's Question

The Gemara now turns to a separate but related question, posed by Rava. If through the *gezeira shava* we learn all the lists from one another, why did the Torah need to write the word *mum* in all three separate contexts — by *adam* (the *kohen*), by *kodshim* (other offerings), and by *bechor*? Given that the categories are linked, could the Torah not have written that a *mum* is disqualifying in only one place and derived the others through inference?

### Three Categories

Rava's answer is that each of the three categories is distinctive in a way that blocks inference from the others. *Mum ba'adam* — the disqualification of *kohanim* — might be thought distinctive *sheken nitraba bemitzvot*, by virtue of the particular priestly role. Since the *kohen*'s status is distinctive, one could not infer the disqualification of animals from humans. Conversely, animals placed on the *mizbe'ach* — *sheken atzma karev legabei mizbe'ach* — might be thought subject to a higher aesthetic standard, blocking inference from animals to humans. *Bechor* is distinctive because its *kedusha* attaches *meirechem*, from the moment of birth, and it is offered on the *mizbe'ach*; perhaps the demand is greatest there. *Kodshim* (other offerings) may have a greater *kedusha* in a different sense: as Rashi explains, the category of *kodshim* is broader, applying to both male and female animals across many types, whereas *bechor* is narrower; moreover, a *bechor* with a *mum* may be eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, while other *korbanot* may not — indicating that the *kedusha* of ordinary *kodshim* in certain respects exceeds that of *bechor*.

The Gemara systematically considers whether any two of the three might suffice to establish the third. Each attempt fails because the paired categories share a feature the third lacks: *adam* and *kodshim* share *kedusha* that is in some sense intrinsic or broad; *bechor* and *kodshim* both go on the *mizbe'ach*; *adam* and *bechor* are both *kadosh* from birth in their respective ways. In each case, the third category could argue it is distinctive. *Tzrichi* — all three mentions are necessary.

The lesson of *Reishis Bikurim* applies here as well: when the Gemara speaks of *nitraba bemitzvot* as the distinctive feature of the human/priestly category, it is referring specifically to the *mitzvot* of service in the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the entire discussion of aesthetics and *mumim* is bounded by and makes sense within that particular sacred context. These concerns do not translate beyond the *Mikdash* setting. Whether a *ba'al mum* is disqualified on a *bamah* (private altar) during the period when *bamot* were permitted is a related question that warrants separate investigation, but the logic of the aesthetics concern suggests it would not apply there.

## She'eino Shaveh Category

### Rav Yochanan Drasha

Having established the *gezeira shava* that links the *mumim* lists, the Gemara now turns to the categories of blemishes unique to *kohanim* — the *yeser aleihem*, those conditions listed by the Mishnah that go beyond what applies to animals. The foundation for this expanded category is a *drasha* of Rabbi Yochanan. The verse in *Emor* reads: *kol ish asher bo mum mizerah Aharon lo yikrav lehakriv lechem elohav* — any man who has a blemish from the seed of Aaron shall not draw near to offer. Rabbi Yochanan reads this as: *ish sheshaveh bezera shel Aharon* — it must be a man who is similar to the typical members of Aaron's seed, the normative population of *kohanim*. The disqualification is not limited to those with a specific catalogued *mum*; it extends to anyone who does not fall within what we might call the normal distribution of the priestly community.

### Mum vs. She'eino

This is a striking reading. It moves the disqualification from the specific and catalogued to the statistical and distributional — not a formal blemish, but a significant deviation from the norm. The Gemara asks the obvious question: what is the practical halakhic difference between calling something a *mum* and placing it in this new category of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*?

### Aseh Not Lo Ta'aseh

The answer is *mishum achulei avoda* — the difference lies in whether the *avodah* performed is retroactively invalidated. A *kohen* with a genuine *mum* who performs the *avodah* has *mum bo velo yechalel* — the verse establishes that performing the service constitutes *chillul*, desecration of the service, which means the *avodah* is *pasul*. By contrast, *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon lo machil avoda* — this category does not desecrate the *avodah* if the *kohen* nonetheless performs it. The prohibition is real, but it does not rise to the level of a *lo ta'aseh*. Rather, the positive framing of the verse — implying that only one who is *shaveh bezera Aharon* should serve — creates an *aseh*, a positive commandment, to have only appropriately similar *kohanim* perform the service. As Rashi explains: *hashaveh bezera Aharon havei kohen veya'avod, veshe'eino hashaveh lo ya'avod* — the verse implicitly says the similar one should serve, and the dissimilar one should not. Violating this is an *aseh*, not a *lo ta'aseh*, and does not retroactively invalidate.

### Marit Ha'ayin Defined

The Gemara then introduces a third category: *marit ha'ayin*. This is a term familiar from other halakhic contexts — for instance, the prohibition against leaving non-dairy creamer out alongside a *fleishig* meal because of how it appears to observers. Here, *marit ha'ayin* refers to conditions of a *kohen*'s appearance that are visually distracting to the people in the *Azarah* who observe him during the *avodah*, but which do not constitute a *mum* and do not fall within the category of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. The example brought in the next Mishnah is eyelashes that have fallen out. This is visually unusual and distracting, but it is natural, not necessarily far outside the normal distribution, and certainly not a classical *mum*. The Mishnah explicitly labels such cases *mishum marit ha'ayin*.

### Three Categories

The Gemara thus lays out three precisely defined halakhic categories. First, a genuine *mum*: this *machil avoda*, invalidates the service retroactively, and it is a *lo ta'aseh* for the *kohen* to perform service in such a state. Second, *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*: this does not invalidate the *avodah* retroactively; it is only an *aseh*, a positive command, not to serve in this condition — a biblically grounded prohibition of lesser force. Third, *marit ha'ayin*: purely rabbinic in origin, not a Torah prohibition at all, and similarly does not invalidate the *avodah*. These three categories are conceptually clean and halakhically differentiated, and they will govern the analysis of each specific condition in the Mishnah.

## Specific Blemishes

### Kilyon (Pointy)

The Gemara describes *kilyon* as *d'dami reishei l'achla* — the head resembles the cover of a barrel, which is to say it comes to a point at the top, producing a triangular cranial profile.

### Lifta (Turnip)

*Liftan* is *d'dami reishei l'gadgalida d'lifta* — the head resembles the bulb of a turnip: wide at the top and tapering to a narrow point at the bottom.

### Makban (Hammer)

*Makban* — the hammerhead — is described as *d'dami reishei l'makaban*: the skull is large and protrudes markedly from both front and back, with the neck positioned centrally so that the cranium juts forward and backward like a hammerhead. The Gemara adds: *v'tzavaro omed b'emtza rosho* — the neck stands at the midpoint of the skull's width, which accentuates the protrusion in both directions.

### Neck Deformities

*Shakut* and *shamut* are two contrasting neck conditions. *Shakut* means buried or hidden: *d'chazya mechaba* — the neck is so short that the head appears to sit sunk between the shoulder blades, with no visible separation. *Shamut* is the opposite: *d'arich v'shamut* — the neck is elongated and thin, causing the head to appear to protrude outward and unsupported. The Gemara notes that *she'rosho shakut milfanav* — the head is slanted in the front — as the initial description of *shakut*, alongside the neck interpretation.

### Skifat (Flat Back)

*Skifat* describes the back of the skull as flattened or truncated — *me'achorav* — lacking the normal rounded curvature. The Gemara invokes the folk expression *shakil pisa*: it looks as though a piece was removed from the back of the head.

### Hunchback Debate

Regarding *ba'alei chatoteret*, the Gemara clarifies the terms of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages. *D'it bei etzem kulei alma lo pligi d'pasul* — if the dorsal growth contains bone, everyone agrees the *kohen* is disqualified.

### RY vs. Chachamim

The dispute is only when *d'leit bei etzem* — there is no bone in the growth. One view holds that this condition nonetheless places the *kohen* outside the range of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. The other view — *kakurei d'bishra b'alma hu* — says it amounts to nothing more than a lump of flesh, and one need not regard it as meaningful.

### Bone Question

The question of whether a bony structure is required for a condition to constitute a *mum* or a *she'eino shaveh* problem reflects a deeper issue: not every departure from the average is halakhically significant. The objection "it is just a piece of flesh" implies that some criterion beyond mere visual difference must be met — whether it is the presence of a structural anomaly (as evidenced by bone), a condition that is rare enough to be meaningfully outside the normal distribution, or something else. Crucially, having bone or not is not simply about aesthetics — if it were only about appearance, the presence of bone would be irrelevant. This suggests that the criteria here are not reducible to a single principle of visual distraction.

### Baldness Specifics

The Gemara addresses the *kireach* (baldness) ruling and notes that Rava qualifies it significantly. The concern is not with common baldness, which is a natural variation, but with forms of baldness that are particularly unusual.

### Baldness Girsaot

There are multiple *girsaot* of Rava's statement, and the differences are consequential. One reading: the problem is when a *kohen* has no hair in the back of the head but has hair in the front — an extremely unusual pattern, the reverse of ordinary baldness. If he has hair in the back, even without hair in the front (which is common), he is *kasher*. Rashi reads the text differently: if a *kohen* has a complete ring of hair all the way around the base of the skull but is completely bald on top — the "monk's tonsure" configuration — that is the disqualifying pattern, because it is bizarre. If the baldness is in front only, or if there is hair both in front and back, this is also flagged in certain versions of the text. The *Shita Mekubetzet* records yet another *girsa*: according to one version of Rava's statement, total absence of hair is *pasul*; according to another, it is *kasher*.

### Natural Baldness OK

The operative principle across all the *girsaot* is consistent: normal baldness — the ordinary progression of male hair loss, even to a considerable degree — is not a disqualifying condition. Only baldness that presents in a pattern so aberrant as to be genuinely outside the normal range constitutes a problem. A *kohen* who is simply bald in the common manner is *kasher*. The concern is with the truly bizarre, such as complete absence of all hair or a ring pattern with total baldness on top.

## RY on Specific Cases

### RY's List

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *harkarchanim vehananasim vehasavleganim* — *kohanim* who are bald in a disqualifying pattern, *kohanim* who are dwarfs, and *kohanim* with *savleganim* (a permanent condition of tearing or watery eyes) are *pesulim lefi she'eino shaveh bezaro shel Aharon* — disqualified because they do not fall within the normal distribution of Aaron's seed. Notably, Rabbi Yochanan frames all three as *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*, not as *mumim*.

### Watery Eyes

The Gemara questions whether Rabbi Yochanan's ruling was even necessary. *Karchanim* — unusual baldness — is addressed in our Mishnah. *Nanasim* — dwarfs — appear in a subsequent Mishnah. Why does Rabbi Yochanan need to enumerate them? The Gemara responds that the essential novelty is *savleganim* — watery eyes — which is not mentioned in any Mishnah. Rabbi Yochanan is teaching us that this condition too falls into the category of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*.

### Gemara Challenge

But the Gemara pushes further. Even for *karchanim* and *nanasim*, which are in the Mishnah, one might have thought — having now defined three distinct categories — that the Mishnah classified them as problems of *marit ha'ayin* rather than *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. Rabbi Yochanan therefore teaches that they belong to the middle category, not the lowest.

### Eyelashes Example

The Gemara, however, challenges this reasoning: the Mishnah itself, when it intends to mark something as *marit ha'ayin*, does so explicitly. The case of *venishru risei einav* — fallen eyelashes — is accompanied by the Mishnah's statement *pasul mipnei marit ha'ayin*. Since the Mishnah labels *marit ha'ayin* explicitly when it applies, one ought to be able to infer that anything not so labeled is not in that category — making Rabbi Yochanan's clarification unnecessary.

### Marit Ha'ayin Scope

The Gemara responds with a parsing question: when the Mishnah uses the phrase *mishum marit ha'ayin*, how much of the preceding list does it cover? If the phrase is stated at the end of a longer enumeration, one might read it as applying retroactively to everything listed before it. In that case, the *kohanim*-specific conditions listed earlier in the Mishnah might all be classified as *marit ha'ayin*, even without explicit labeling on each one. Rabbi Yochanan's teaching would then be necessary to clarify that *karchanim*, *nanasim*, and *savleganim* are *she'eino shaveh*, not mere *marit ha'ayin*.

### Baraita RY

The Gemara then reveals the full stakes: there is a *baraita* that explicitly rules these three cases — *karchanim*, *nanasim*, and *savleganim* — as *kesherim* altogether, with the only concern being *marit ha'ayin*. This *baraita* is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda, who learns from the verse *hakohanim* an inclusion of bald *kohanim* as valid. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the baldness and similar conditions are not a *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon* problem at all — at most, they raise a concern of *marit ha'ayin*.

### Pesak vs. Baraita

The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yochanan is ruling *against* the *baraita* of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yochanan's teaching is not merely an elaboration of what the Mishnah states; it is a *pesak* that rejects the lenient view of Rabbi Yehuda. According to Rabbi Yochanan, *karchanim*, *nanasim*, and *savleganim* are disqualified as *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon* — a biblical *aseh* — and not merely on the rabbinic ground of *marit ha'ayin*. This resolution is consistent with the three-tier framework: the Mishnah's usage of *marit ha'ayin*, when it appears more than once, signals that it applies only to the cases immediately adjacent to each occurrence; everything else defaults to *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. And Rabbi Yochanan establishes that the halakha follows this stricter categorization rather than the *baraita*'s more permissive one.
