# Bekhorot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mumim for Kohanim

### Opening & Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מוּמִין אֵלּוּ, בֵּין קְבוּעִין בֵּין עוֹבְרִים — פּוֹסְלִין בָּאָדָם, וְיָתֵר עֲלֵיהֶן: הַכִּילוֹן, וְהַלַּפְתָּן, וְהַמַּקָּבָן, וְשֶׁרֹאשׁוֹ שָׁקוּט, וּסְקִיפַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Concerning **these blemishes** which were taught with regard to an animal, **whether** they are **permanent or transient, they** also **disqualify in** the case of **a person,** i.e., they disqualify a priest from performing the Temple service. **And in addition to those** blemishes, there are other blemishes that apply only to a priest: **One whose** head **is pointed,** narrow above and wide below; **and one whose** head **is turnip-like,** wide above and narrow below; **and one whose** head **is hammer-like,** with his forehead protruding; **and** one **whose head** has **an indentation; and** one **wherein the back of his head protrudes.**

Daf 43 opens a new *perek*, *Mumim Ellu*, transitioning from the earlier discussion of blemishes in animals to the parallel category of blemishes in *kohanim*. The connection is organic: while the *masechta* is primarily concerned with the *bechor*, the issue of *mumim* naturally bridges to the priestly service. Although this is not strictly about the *bechor* in the narrow sense, the conceptual framework — what constitutes a disqualifying blemish — applies across both domains, and the Mishnah treats them together.

### Kohanim-Specific Blemish…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲלֵי חֲטָרוֹת — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹסְלִין. הַקֵּרֵחַ פָּסוּל. אֵיזֶהוּ קֵרֵחַ? כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ שִׁיטָה שֶׁל שֵׂעָר מוּקֶּפֶת מֵאוֹזֶן לְאוֹזֶן, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **those with humped** backs, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** them **fit** for service **and the Rabbis deem** them **disqualified. The *kere'aḥ* is disqualified** from performing the Temple service. **What is a *kere'aḥ*?** It is **anyone who does not have a row of hair encircling** his head **from ear to ear. If he has** a row of hair from ear to ear, **that** person is **fit** for service.

The Mishnah introduces a list of blemishes that are additional to those shared with animals — conditions that disqualify a *kohen* but have no exact parallel in the laws governing animal offerings. These include distinctive head shapes referred to as *kilyon*, *liftan*, and *makban*; deformities of the neck known as *shakut* and *shamut*; a condition called *skifat* affecting the back of the skull; and *ba'alei chatoteret*, those with a hunchback or dorsal growth. The Mishnah then addresses *kireach* — baldness — and records a dispute concerning whether a hunchback is disqualifying.

### Fixed vs. Transitory

The Mishnah opens with the principle that *mumim elu*, the blemishes enumerated in the previous *perek*, whether *kevi'in* (fixed) or *overin* (transitory), *poslin ba'adam* — disqualify a *kohen* from performing the *avodah*. The conceptual basis is clear: just as a *bechor* with a transitory blemish cannot be offered as a *korban* in the *Beis HaMikdash* at that moment, so too a *kohen* with even a transitory blemish may not serve. The parallel holds on both sides of the equation.

### Head Shape Blemishes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּילוֹן — דְּדָמֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ לְאַכְלָא, ״לַפְתָּא״ — דְּדָמֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ לְגַדְגָּלִידָא דְּלִיפְתָּא, תָּנָא: וְצַוָּארוֹ עוֹמֵד בְּאֶמְצַע רֹאשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara clarifies the blemishes that the mishna lists as disqualifying a priest: **One whose** head **is pointed [*kilon*],** this is one **whose head resembles the cover of a barrel,** narrow above and wide below. **One whose** head **is turnip-like,** this is one **whose head resembles the top of a turnip,** wide above and narrow below. The Sages **taught** in a *baraita* another detail relating to this blemish: **And his neck stands in the middle of his head,** i.e., his head protrudes equally in all directions around his neck, whereas a typical person's neck is situated at the back of his head.

The Gemara provides anatomical descriptions of each unusual head shape. *Kilyon* — likely derived from the root meaning "to come to a point" — describes a triangular, pointed head, essentially the shape of an inverted cone. *Liftan*, the turnip, is the inverse: wide at the top and pointed at the bottom, a description that carries rather unflattering connotations. *Makban* — which shares its root with the *Maccabees*, from *makevet*, a hammer — describes what might be called a hammerhead configuration: a large skull, narrow neck, with the cranium protruding markedly both in front and behind, not unlike a hammerhead shark.

### More Physical Deformities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַקָּבָן — דְּדָמֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ לְמַקָּבָן, ״וְשֶׁרֹאשׁוֹ שָׁקוּט״ — מִלְּפָנָיו, ״וּסְקִיפָת״ — מֵאֲחוֹרָיו, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: שְׁקִיל פִּיסָּא. תָּנָא: וְצַוָּארוֹ שָׁקוּט וְשָׁמוּט. ״שָׁקוּט״ — דַּחֲבִיס מִחְבָּס, ״שָׁמוּט״ — דַּאֲרִיךְ וּשְׁמִיט.
>
> **Translation:** **One whose** head **is hammer-like [*makkavan*],** this is one **whose head resembles a hammer,** with his forehead protruding. **And** one **whose** head is **indented [*shakut*],** this is one with a depression **in the front of his** head. **And** one **wherein the back of his head protrudes [*sekifat*]** is someone for whom it appears a piece **from the back of his** head has been removed, **as people say: A piece has been taken [*shakil pisa*].** *Sekifat* is understood to be an abbreviation of that term. The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **And** a priest **whose neck is short, or** one whose neck **is narrow,** is disqualified from performing the Temple service as well. A priest with **a short** neck is one **whose** neck **is hidden** and is not visible between his shoulders. A priest with **a narrow** neck is one **whose** neck **is long and narrow.**

The condition *shakut* refers to a head that is slanted in the front, or alternatively, to a person whose neck is so short that the head appears buried or sunk between the shoulder blades. The Gemara clarifies *shakut* and *shamut* as two neck-related deformities: *shakut* is a neck that appears hidden, depressed; *shamut* is the opposite — thin and narrow, so that the head appears to protrude outward from the neck on both sides. *Skifat* describes a back of the head that is flattened or cut off, lacking the normal curvature; the Gemara invokes the folk expression *shakil pisa*, "a piece was removed," to capture how it appears. The *ba'al chatoteret* is the hunchback — the word *chatoteret* being the same used for a camel's hump — and it is this category that generates a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages.

### Baldness Debate

The Mishnah declares *kireach pasul* — a bald *kohen* is disqualified. What is the definition of *kireach*? The Mishnah says: *kol she'ein lo shita shel sei'ar mukefet me'ozen l'ozen* — one who lacks even a single row of hair encircling the head from ear to ear. The Gemara, as will be seen, will subject this definition to careful scrutiny, differentiating among various patterns of baldness and concluding that only particularly bizarre manifestations are problematic, not normal baldness.

### Visibility in Service

A natural question arises: many of these conditions — misshapen heads, hunchbacks — would have been partially obscured during *avodah*. *Kohanim* wore the *mitznefet*, and priestly garments covered much of the body. One might therefore wonder whether the concern is primarily visual — about what the people assembled in the *Azarah* see — or whether these are intrinsic disqualifications independent of visibility.

This question proves consequential. The Gemara, as will emerge, distinguishes sharply between a genuine *mum*, a condition that *machil avoda* (desecrates the service), and the categories of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon* and *marit ha'ayin*, which are lesser in halakhic force. The fact that many of the conditions in this Mishnah are inherently visual — head shapes, baldness, a visible dorsal growth — suggests that visibility is indeed a factor. Yet the Gemara does not limit these disqualifications to visible locations alone, which deepens the question. For now, the observation is worth carrying forward: are the *kohanim*-specific disqualifications limited to what would be seen by the people bringing *korbanot*, or do they extend beyond that?

One broader framework for understanding the entire category is that the *kohen* in the *Beis HaMikdash* occupies a role analogous in certain respects to the *keilim* of the *Mikdash* — not merely a functionary but a sanctified instrument of the service. The *Rambam*, in discussing the laws of *kohanim*, speaks of *klei hamikdash vehamishartim bo*, treating the priests and the vessels together. In *Zevachim*, the *kohen* must serve barefoot, his feet touching the floor of the *Azarah*, so that he connects to the *kedusha* of the *Beis HaMikdash*. Seen through this lens, the aesthetic demands placed upon *kohanim* reflect the same principle as the aesthetic demands placed upon the *menorah* and the *mizbe'ach* — objects used in the service must conform to a certain standard of wholeness and appearance. This framing is perhaps more satisfying than alternative explanations, and it will be developed further when the Gemara addresses the category of *nitraba bemitzvot*.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Why Derive Lists

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? וְהָאִיכָּא יַבֶּלֶת, דְּלָא כְּתִיב בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּאָדָם! וְתוּ: דַּק תְּבַלּוּל, דְּלָא כְּתִיבִי בִּבְהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why** does the mishna say that all the blemishes that disqualify an animal disqualify a priest as well? **But there is** the blemish of **a wart, which is not written in the Torah with regard to a person** but is written with regard to an animal (see Leviticus 22:22). **And furthermore,** there are also the blemishes of **a cataract** and **a *tevallul*, which** appear in the Torah with regard to a person but **are not written** in the Torah **with regard to an animal** (see Leviticus 21:20). Why, then, does the mishna on 38a teach that such blemishes disqualify an animal?

The Gemara opens with a structural question: why should we assume that the full list of *mumim* applying to animals also applies to *kohanim*, and vice versa? The *pesukim* in *Emor* list specific blemishes for animals brought as *korbanot*, and separately list blemishes that disqualify *kohanim*. There is significant overlap, but the lists are not identical. Why not treat each list as discrete and exhaustive for its own domain?

### Textual Gaps

The Gemara identifies the specific asymmetries. *Yabbelet* — a type of wart or skin growth — is written in the list of *mumim* for animals but is not mentioned in the Torah's list of blemishes for humans. Conversely, *dak* and *tavlul* — conditions involving the eye, specifically a line penetrating the pupil that mixes white and colored areas — appear in the Torah's list for humans but are not mentioned for animals. If the lists are distinct, *yabbelet* would not apply to *kohanim*, and the eye condition would not apply to animals.

### Garav & Yalefet Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵילָף יָלְפִי מֵהֲדָדֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: בָּאָדָם לֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ יַבֶּלֶת, בַּבְּהֵמָה לֹא נֶאֱמַר דַּק תְּבַלּוּל, מִנַּיִן לִיתֵּן אֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה וְאֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״גָּרָב״ ״גָּרָב״, ״יַלֶּפֶת״ ״יַלֶּפֶת״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: These blemishes **are derived from one another, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **In** the case of **a person,** i.e., a priest, **it is not stated of him** that **a wart** disqualifies him from Temple service, and **in** the case of **an animal, it is not stated** that **a cataract** or **a *tevallul*** disqualify it from being sacrificed. **From where** is it derived that one **applies that which is said of this** case **to that** case, **and that which is said of this** case **to that** case? **The verse states: "Scabbed"** and **"scabbed," "scurvy"** and **"scurvy,"** both with regard to a person and an animal (Leviticus 21:20; 22:22), which serves **to** draw **a verbal analogy** from the verses.

The Gemara resolves this through a *gezeira shava*. A *baraita* teaches: since *garav* and *yalefet* — two types of skin conditions — appear in both the human list and the animal list, this shared terminology creates a verbal link allowing us to apply each list to the other. The *gezeira shava* on *garav–garav* and *yalefet–yalefet* permits us to learn: whatever is written regarding blemishes for humans applies equally to animals, and whatever is written for animals applies to humans.

### Mufne Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַפְנֵי, דְּאִי לָא מַפְנֵי, אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: אָדָם מִבְּהֵמָה לָא יָלֵיף, שֶׁכֵּן הִיא עַצְמָהּ קְרֵיבָה לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ; בְּהֵמָה מֵאָדָם לָא יָלְפָא, שֶׁכֵּן נִתְרַבָּה בְּמִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: These verses must be **free,** i.e., they are superfluous and are stated only for the purpose of the verbal analogy. This means that the verbal analogy cannot be refuted by logical reasoning, whereas if the verses are not free one can refute the analogy if there is a significant difference between the two cases. **As, if they are not free,** the verbal analogy **can be refuted** as follows: One **cannot derive** the disqualifications of **a person** for performing the Temple service **from** those of **an animal,** as the case of an animal can be considered more stringent **because** the animal **itself is sacrificed upon the altar.** Similarly, one **cannot derive** the disqualifications of **an animal from** those of **a person,** as one can claim that the *halakha* with regard to a person should be more stringent, **since** a person **was included in** the performance of **mitzvot.**

The Gemara raises the standard requirement for a valid *gezeira shava*: the linking words must be *mufne* — superfluous in their own context, freed from the need to carry independent meaning, so that they are available for the hermeneutical link. If the words were not superfluous, the *gezeira shava* would be subject to logical challenge. The Gemara demonstrates that *garav* is indeed superfluous, because the Torah could have written only *yalefet* and derived *garav* through a *kal v'chomer*: if the less severe skin condition (*yalefet*) constitutes a *mum*, certainly the more severe and visually repugnant *garav* does. Since the Torah wrote *garav* explicitly despite this *kal v'chomer*, it must be to render the word available for the *gezeira shava*.

### Asymmetric Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאיֵי, אַפְנוֹיֵי מַפְנֵי, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״יַלֶּפֶת״ וְלָא בָּעֵי ״גָּרָב״, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה יַלֶּפֶת דְּלָא מְאִיסָא הָוֵי מוּמָא, גָּרָב דִּמְאִיס לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? ״גָּרָב״ ״גָּרָב״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Indeed,** the terms **are free.** Accordingly, since these disqualifications are derived from a verbal analogy they must be accepted as though they were written explicitly in the Torah. The Gemara proves these verses are free: **Let the Merciful One write** that **scurvy** disqualifies a person and an animal, **and** it would be **unnecessary** for the Merciful One to write that **a scab** disqualifies them, as **I would say: And if scurvy, which is not repulsive, is** considered a disqualifying **blemish,** then in the case of **a scab, which is repulsive,** is it **not all the more so** a disqualifying blemish? If so, then with regard to **"scabbed"** and **"scabbed," which the Merciful One writes** with regard to a person and an animal, **why do I** need them? Evidently, they are **to** be considered **free** for the verbal analogy.

With the *gezeira shava* established, the Gemara explains why one could not simply reason by logic from one list to the other — which would have made the *gezeira shava* unnecessary. The reason is that logical inference is blocked in both directions, and because the *gezeira shava* must be *mufne*, the superfluous writing is justified.

### Animals on Altar

Why can one not learn humans from animals? *Sheken hu atzmo karev legabei mizbe'ach* — because the animal itself is placed upon the *mizbe'ach*. The aesthetics demanded of something offered on the altar may be held to a higher standard than what is demanded of a person who performs a service. Therefore, a longer or stricter list of *mumim* for animals might be justified by that distinctive quality, and one cannot straightforwardly apply the animal list to humans.

### Nitraba Bemitzvot

Conversely, why can one not learn animals from humans? *Sheken nitraba bemitzvot* — because a human being has more *mitzvot*. An animal has no *mitzvot* at all. This phrase requires interpretation, and on the surface it is challenging: the abundance of *mitzvot* does not obviously explain why a *kohen*'s appearance should be held to a stricter standard.

### Kodushat Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתְּבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא כּוּלְּהוּ בְּחַד, וְגָרָב וְיַלֶּפֶת הָכָא וְהָכָא, וְלֵילְפוּ מֵחֲדָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if animals and priests are disqualified by the same blemishes, **let the Merciful One write all the** disqualifying blemishes **with regard to one** of them, either a person or an animal, **and** let the Merciful One write that **a scab and scurvy** disqualify both **here and here,** for the purpose of the verbal analogy, **and let** all the other blemishes **be derived** in one case **from** the other **one,** through the verbal analogy.

The Gemara later explains this through the concept of *kedushato meruba* — a greater intrinsic *kedusha*. Rashi understands this as a general statement about human sanctity through *mitzvot*. However, the commentary *Reishis Bikurim* offers an interpretation that is both more contextually precise and more theologically satisfying: *nitraba bemitzvot* refers specifically to the *mitzvot* of the *kehuna* — the priestly service in the *Beis HaMikdash*. On this reading, the heightened standard applied to *kohanim* flows from their specific sacred role as *meshartim*, servants, in the *Mikdash*, not from a generic statement about human beings and *mitzvot*. This reading aligns with the framework described above — the *kohen* as a sanctified instrument of the service, analogous to the *keilim* of the *Mikdash* — and limits the aesthetic concern appropriately to the *Beis HaMikdash* context. It would be a mistake to extrapolate from these *halachot* any general principle about the worth of persons with physical differences.

Importantly, these disqualifications do not prevent a *kohen* from receiving *terumah* or sharing in the *korbanot*. They exclusively limit actual participation in the *avodah*. This narrowness itself is significant.

### Why Repeat Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהֵיָיא בְּהֵי לִיכְתֹּב רַחֲמָנָא? אִי כְּתַב בְּאָדָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כֹּל דְּפָסוּל בְּאָדָם פָּסוּל בִּבְהֵמָה, קָלוּט וְחוּטִין דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ בְּאָדָם, בִּבְהֵמָה נָמֵי לָא לִיפַּסְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **To which** is this referring, i.e., **in which** case **should the Merciful One write** all the blemishes? **If** the Merciful One had **written** all of them **with regard to a person** then **I would say: All** blemishes **that disqualify in** the case of **a person** likewise **disqualify in** the case of **an animal.** If so, then with regard to **closed** hooves **and** damaged **gums, which are not** written **with regard to a person** as they are not relevant because a person has no hooves and his gums are covered with teeth, I would say: **In** the case of **an animal as well they should not disqualify.**

The Gemara then asks a more refined question: granted that *garav* and *yalefet* must appear in both contexts to enable the *gezeira shava*, why was it necessary for the Torah to repeat the entire list in each context? One might imagine the Torah writing the full shared list only once, the distinctive *mumim* of each category separately, and then writing *garav* and *yalefet* in both places as the linking mechanism — without repeating everything.

The Gemara considers the various permutations. If the Torah wrote the full list only by humans and the linking words by animals: there are *mumim* distinctive to animals, such as *kalut* (fused hooves) and *chalut* (problems with the teeth roots), that have no parallel in humans — they could not have been included in a human-only list and then applied to animals. If the Torah wrote the full list only by animals and linked to humans: there are conditions distinctive to humans, such as *giben* (eyebrow deformity — one eyebrow or no eyebrows, which animals do not have) and *charum* (a flat or absent nasal bridge between the eyes, a condition that does not present in animals because their noses are positioned below their eyes), which would have been omitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתְּבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא כּוּלְּהוּ בְּחַד, וְהָנָךְ דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ בְּאִידַּךְ — לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּבְהֵמָה, וְהָנָךְ דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ בְּאִידַּךְ — לִכְתּוֹב בְּאָדָם, וְגָרָב וְיַלֶּפֶת — הָכָא וְהָכָא, וְלֵילְפִי מֵהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** even so, why is it necessary for the Torah to delineate all the common blemishes? **Let the Merciful One write all of them in** the verses discussing **one** of them, either an animal or a person, **and** as for **those that are not** relevant **for the other,** i.e., those blemishes that apply only in the case of an animal, **let the Merciful One write with regard to an animal, and** as for **those that are not** relevant **for the other,** i.e., those blemishes relevant only to a person, **let** the Merciful One **write with regard to a person. And** let the Torah write the blemishes of **a scab and scurvy** both **here and there,** for the purpose of the verbal analogy, **and let** the fact that their common blemishes disqualify in both cases **be derived from one another.**

What if the Torah wrote the shared conditions only once, wrote each distinctive list separately, and used *garav* and *yalefet* as the link? This is exactly the question posed — and the answer comes from the school of Rabbi Yishmael:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כְּתַנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כׇּל פָּרָשָׁה שֶׁנֶּאֱמְרָה וְנִשְׁנֵית — לֹא נִשְׁנֵית אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּתְחַדֵּשׁ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reason the Torah repeats their common blemishes is **in accordance with** that which **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught, as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Every passage** in the Torah **that was stated and repeated was repeated only for the novel element introduced therein.** It is the style of the Torah to repeat a passage even in order to teach only one additional *halakha*. Consequently, with regard to the blemishes of a person and an animal, their common blemishes were repeated on account of those blemishes that apply only to an animal or a person.

The Torah does not need to justify every word of a repeated passage. If the Torah needed to introduce even one distinctive element in that passage, it would naturally restate the broader context without the reader being required to find independent significance in every repeated item. This is the school of *dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of human language — associated with Rabbi Yishmael, as distinct from Rabbi Akiva's principle of *omnisignificance*, which would require every repeated letter and word to carry independent meaning.

The two schools represent a genuine and enduring divide in hermeneutics. Rabbi Akiva is *doresh kotzei osiyot* — he draws meaning from the very crowns atop the letters. Rabbi Yishmael counters: *afilu atah doresh bas u'vas kol hayom kulo eini shomeia lach* — even if you expound extra *vavs* all day, I will not accept it. It is worth noting that the *baraita* we recite each morning as part of the liturgy is Rabbi Yishmael's — the thirteen hermeneutical principles — yet that *baraita* introduces the *Sifra*, the halakhic midrash on *Vayikra*, which follows Rabbi Akiva's school. The relationship between the two approaches is not always a clean opposition.

Nachum Ish Gamzu, who had developed the approach of drawing meaning from the particle *et* — the same technique picked up by Rabbi Akiva — ultimately ceased applying it in one instance, stating *et Hashem elokecha tira peiresh*. Rabbi Akiva then took up that very exposition: *et Hashem elokecha l'rabot talmidei chachamim* — and indeed Nachum Ish Gamzu was Rabbi Akiva's own teacher. Rabbi Akiva in this sense inherited and extended the interpretive mantle.

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא מוּם בְּאָדָם, מוּם בְּקָדָשִׁים, מוּם בִּבְכוֹר?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says: Why do I** need **the Merciful One to write** that **a blemish** causes disqualification from Temple service **in** the case of **a person,** i.e., a priest (see Leviticus 21:17–23), and that **a blemish** causes disqualification **in** the case of **sacrificial** animals (see Leviticus 22:20–25), and that **a blemish** causes disqualification for sacrifice **in** the case of **a firstborn** animal (see Deuteronomy 15:21)?

The Gemara now turns to a separate but related question, posed by Rava. If through the *gezeira shava* we learn all the lists from one another, why did the Torah need to write the word *mum* in all three separate contexts — by *adam* (the *kohen*), by *kodshim* (other offerings), and by *bechor*? Given that the categories are linked, could the Torah not have written that a *mum* is disqualifying in only one place and derived the others through inference?

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא מוּם בְּאָדָם, שֶׁכֵּן נִתְרַבָּה בְּמִצְווֹת. אָדָם מִבְּכוֹר בְּהֵמָה לָא אָתֵי, שֶׁכֵּן הוּא עַצְמוֹ קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava answers: They are all **necessary. As, had the Merciful One written** only that **a blemish** causes disqualification **in** the case of **a person,** one might suggest that blemishes cause disqualification only in that case, **since** a person **was included in** the performance of **mitzvot.** Similarly, had the Merciful One written only that blemishes disqualify a firstborn animal from being sacrificed, it could be claimed that one **cannot derive** the disqualification of **a person** on account of blemishes **from** the disqualification of **a firstborn animal, since** an animal has an added aspect of stringency in that **it itself is sacrificed upon the altar.**

Rava's answer is that each of the three categories is distinctive in a way that blocks inference from the others. *Mum ba'adam* — the disqualification of *kohanim* — might be thought distinctive *sheken nitraba bemitzvot*, by virtue of the particular priestly role. Since the *kohen*'s status is distinctive, one could not infer the disqualification of animals from humans. Conversely, animals placed on the *mizbe'ach* — *sheken atzma karev legabei mizbe'ach* — might be thought subject to a higher aesthetic standard, blocking inference from animals to humans.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים מִבְּכוֹר לָא אָתֵי, שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ מֵרֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, one **cannot derive** the disqualifications of **sacrificial** animals **from** those of **a firstborn** animal, **since** the **sanctity of** a firstborn animal is **from the womb,** i.e., from birth, whereas the sanctity of other offerings applies only upon consecration by a person, and they are therefore of a lesser sanctity.

*Bechor* is distinctive because its *kedusha* attaches *meirechem*, from the moment of birth, and it is offered on the *mizbe'ach*; perhaps the demand is greatest there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם מִקֳּדָשִׁים לָא אָתֵי, שֶׁכֵּן הֵן עַצְמָן קְרֵיבִין. בְּכוֹר מִקֳּדָשִׁים לָא אָתֵי, שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ מְרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, had the Merciful One written only that sacrificial animals are disqualified due to blemishes, it could be claimed that one **cannot derive** the disqualifications of **a person from** that of **sacrificial** animals, **as** the offerings **themselves are sacrificed.** Similarly, one **cannot derive** the disqualifications of **a firstborn** animal **from** those of **sacrificial** animals, **as the sanctity** of other offerings **is greater** than that of a firstborn animal because there are many types of other offerings, whereas a firstborn animal has only one type of sanctification. Therefore, it was necessary for the Merciful One to specify that blemishes disqualify in each case.

*Kodshim* (other offerings) may have a greater *kedusha* in a different sense: as Rashi explains, the category of *kodshim* is broader, applying to both male and female animals across many types, whereas *bechor* is narrower; moreover, a *bechor* with a *mum* may be eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, while other *korbanot* may not — indicating that the *kedusha* of ordinary *kodshim* in certain respects exceeds that of *bechor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא, תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי? מֵהֵי תֵּיתֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: Granted, the disqualification of **one** of these cases **cannot be derived from** only a single other **one.** But **let one** of them **be derived from** the other **two.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **From which** two **should** the other one **be derived?**

The Gemara systematically considers whether any two of the three might suffice to establish the third. Each attempt fails because the paired categories share a feature the third lacks: *adam* and *kodshim* share *kedusha* that is in some sense intrinsic or broad; *bechor* and *kodshim* both go on the *mizbe'ach*; *adam* and *bechor* are both *kadosh* from birth in their respective ways. In each case, the third category could argue it is distinctive. *Tzrichi* — all three mentions are necessary.

The lesson of *Reishis Bikurim* applies here as well: when the Gemara speaks of *nitraba bemitzvot* as the distinctive feature of the human/priestly category, it is referring specifically to the *mitzvot* of service in the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the entire discussion of aesthetics and *mumim* is bounded by and makes sense within that particular sacred context. These concerns do not translate beyond the *Mikdash* setting. Whether a *ba'al mum* is disqualified on a *bamah* (private altar) during the period when *bamot* were permitted is a related question that warrants separate investigation, but the logic of the aesthetics concern suggests it would not apply there.

## She'eino Shaveh Category

### Rav Yochanan Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר עֲלֵיהֶן בְּאָדָם. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כֹּל אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר בּוֹ מוּם מִזֶּרַע אַהֲרֹן״, אִישׁ שֶׁשָּׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **in addition to those** blemishes that disqualify an animal, there are other blemishes that apply **to a person** despite the fact that they are not actual defects. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** These *halakhot* are derived from **that** which **the verse states: "No man among the seed of Aaron the priest who has a blemish** shall come forward to sacrifice the offerings of the Lord made by fire" (Leviticus 21:21). The verse indicates that the priest must be **a man who is equal among the seed of Aaron,** i.e., one whose appearance is similar to the other priests.

Having established the *gezeira shava* that links the *mumim* lists, the Gemara now turns to the categories of blemishes unique to *kohanim* — the *yeser aleihem*, those conditions listed by the Mishnah that go beyond what applies to animals. The foundation for this expanded category is a *drasha* of Rabbi Yochanan. The verse in *Emor* reads: *kol ish asher bo mum mizerah Aharon lo yikrav lehakriv lechem elohav* — any man who has a blemish from the seed of Aaron shall not draw near to offer. Rabbi Yochanan reads this as: *ish sheshaveh bezera shel Aharon* — it must be a man who is similar to the typical members of Aaron's seed, the normative population of *kohanim*. The disqualification is not limited to those with a specific catalogued *mum*; it extends to anyone who does not fall within what we might call the normal distribution of the priestly community.

### Mum vs. She'eino

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין מוּמָא לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן? אִיכָּא מִשּׁוּם אַחוֹלֵי עֲבוֹדָה: מוּמָא מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״מוּם בּוֹ וְלֹא יְחַלֵּל״, שֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן לָא מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service due to a full-fledged **blemish** that disqualifies both people and animals, and a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service because **he is not equal among the seed of Aaron?** The Gemara responds: **There is** a difference between them **with regard to profaning** the Temple **service.** A priest with a full-fledged **blemish** who performs rites in the Temple **profanes** the **service** he has performed, **as it is written: "He has a blemish; that he not profane** My Sanctuaries" (Leviticus 21:23). A priest **who is not equal among the seed of Aaron** does **not profane** the **service** he performs.

This is a striking reading. It moves the disqualification from the specific and catalogued to the statistical and distributional — not a formal blemish, but a significant deviation from the norm. The Gemara asks the obvious question: what is the practical halakhic difference between calling something a *mum* and placing it in this new category of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*?

### Aseh Not Lo Ta'aseh

The answer is *mishum achulei avoda* — the difference lies in whether the *avodah* performed is retroactively invalidated.

### Marit Ha'ayin Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן, וּמִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **What** difference **is there between** a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service because **he is not equal among the seed of Aaron,** e.g., one whose head is pointed, **and** a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service **due to the** mere **appearance of** a blemish, such as one whose eyelashes have fallen out, as stated in the *mishnayot* further on in this chapter? The Gemara responds: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to the violation of the prohibition, which is stated as **a positive mitzva,** i.e., that priests who are equal shall serve in the Temple. A priest who is not equal among the seed of Aaron who performs the Temple service transgresses this prohibition. By contrast, a priest who is disqualified due to the appearance of a blemish is disqualified by rabbinic law, and he does not transgress a Torah prohibition by performing the Temple service.

The Gemara then introduces a third category: *marit ha'ayin*. This is a term familiar from other halakhic contexts — for instance, the prohibition against leaving non-dairy creamer out alongside a *fleishig* meal because of how it appears to observers. Here, *marit ha'ayin* refers to conditions of a *kohen*'s appearance that are visually distracting to the people in the *Azarah* who observe him during the *avodah*, but which do not constitute a *mum* and do not fall within the category of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. The example brought in the next Mishnah is eyelashes that have fallen out. This is visually unusual and distracting, but it is natural, not necessarily far outside the normal distribution, and certainly not a classical *mum*. The Mishnah explicitly labels such cases *mishum marit ha'ayin*.

### Three Categories

The Gemara thus lays out three precisely defined halakhic categories. First, a genuine *mum*: this *machil avoda*, invalidates the service retroactively, and it is a *lo ta'aseh* for the *kohen* to perform service in such a state. Second, *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*: this does not invalidate the *avodah* retroactively; it is only an *aseh*, a positive command, not to serve in this condition — a biblically grounded prohibition of lesser force. Third, *marit ha'ayin*: purely rabbinic in origin, not a Torah prohibition at all, and similarly does not invalidate the *avodah*. These three categories are conceptually clean and halakhically differentiated, and they will govern the analysis of each specific condition in the Mishnah.

## Specific Blemishes

### Kilyon (Pointy)

The Gemara describes *kilyon* as *d'dami reishei l'achla* — the head resembles the cover of a barrel, which is to say it comes to a point at the top, producing a triangular cranial profile.

### Lifta (Turnip)

*Liftan* is *d'dami reishei l'gadgalida d'lifta* — the head resembles the bulb of a turnip: wide at the top and tapering to a narrow point at the bottom.

### Makban (Hammer)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַקָּבָן — דְּדָמֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ לְמַקָּבָן, וְצַוָּארוֹ עוֹמֵד בְּאֶמְצַע רֹאשׁוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **One whose** head **is hammer-like [*makkavan*],** this is one **whose head resembles a hammer,** with his forehead protruding. ... **And his neck stands in the middle of his head,** i.e., his head protrudes equally in all directions around his neck, whereas a typical person's neck is situated at the back of his head.

*Makban* — the hammerhead — is described as *d'dami reishei l'makaban*: the skull is large and protrudes markedly from both front and back, with the neck positioned centrally so that the cranium juts forward and backward like a hammerhead. The Gemara adds: *v'tzavaro omed b'emtza rosho* — the neck stands at the midpoint of the skull's width, which accentuates the protrusion in both directions.

### Neck Deformities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וְצַוָּארוֹ שָׁקוּט וְשָׁמוּט. ״שָׁקוּט״ — דַּחֲבִיס מִחְבָּס, ״שָׁמוּט״ — דַּאֲרִיךְ וּשְׁמִיט.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **And** a priest **whose neck is short, or** one whose neck **is narrow,** is disqualified from performing the Temple service as well. A priest with **a short** neck is one **whose** neck **is hidden** and is not visible between his shoulders. A priest with **a narrow** neck is one **whose** neck **is long and narrow.**

*Shakut* and *shamut* are two contrasting neck conditions. *Shakut* means buried or hidden: *d'chazya mechaba* — the neck is so short that the head appears to sit sunk between the shoulder blades, with no visible separation. *Shamut* is the opposite: *d'arich v'shamut* — the neck is elongated and thin, causing the head to appear to protrude outward and unsupported. The Gemara notes that *she'rosho shakut milfanav* — the head is slanted in the front — as the initial description of *shakut*, alongside the neck interpretation.

### Skifat (Flat Back)

*Skifat* describes the back of the skull as flattened or truncated — *me'achorav* — lacking the normal rounded curvature. The Gemara invokes the folk expression *shakil pisa*: it looks as though a piece was removed from the back of the head.

### Hunchback Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲלֵי חֲטָרוֹת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כּוּ׳. דְּאִית בֵּיהּ עֶצֶם, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּפָסְלָא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי — דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ עֶצֶם: מָר סָבַר — הָא אֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן, וּמָר סָבַר — קִרְקוּרָא דְּבִישְׂרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And** with regard to **those with humped** backs, **Rabbi Yehuda** deems them fit for service and the Rabbis deem them disqualified. The Gemara elaborates: In a case **where there is a bone in** his hump, **everyone agrees that** the priest is **disqualified** for Temple service. **They disagree when there is no bone.** One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **this** person **is not equal among the seed of Aaron** and is therefore disqualified from performing the Temple service; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **it is merely a piece of flesh** and is not considered a blemish.

Regarding *ba'alei chatoteret*, the Gemara clarifies the terms of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages. *D'it bei etzem kulei alma lo pligi d'pasul* — if the dorsal growth contains bone, everyone agrees the *kohen* is disqualified.

### RY vs. Chachamim

The dispute is only when *d'leit bei etzem* — there is no bone in the growth. One view holds that this condition nonetheless places the *kohen* outside the range of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. The other view — *kakurei d'bishra b'alma hu* — says it amounts to nothing more than a lump of flesh, and one need not regard it as meaningful.

### Bone Question

The question of whether a bony structure is required for a condition to constitute a *mum* or a *she'eino shaveh* problem reflects a deeper issue: not every departure from the average is halakhically significant. The objection "it is just a piece of flesh" implies that some criterion beyond mere visual difference must be met — whether it is the presence of a structural anomaly (as evidenced by bone), a condition that is rare enough to be meaningfully outside the normal distribution, or something else. Crucially, having bone or not is not simply about aesthetics — if it were only about appearance, the presence of bone would be irrelevant. This suggests that the criteria here are not reducible to a single principle of visual distraction.

### Baldness Specifics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקֵּרֵחַ פָּסוּל. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לוֹ לְאַחֲרָיו וְיֵשׁ לוֹ לְפָנָיו, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ לְאַחֲרָיו — כָּשֵׁר, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵין לוֹ לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **the *kere'aḥ*,** i.e., anyone who does not have a row of hair encircling his head from ear to ear, **is disqualified** from performing the Temple service. **Rava says:** The Sages **taught** that a *kere'aḥ* is disqualified **only if he does not have** a row of hair **in the back** of **his** head **but he has** a row of hair **in the front. But** if **he has** a row of hair both in the front and **in the back,** he is **fit** for performing the Temple service. **And all the more so, when he has** hair **in the back but he does not have in the front** he is fit for performing the Temple service, as this is typical of older men.

The Gemara addresses the *kireach* (baldness) ruling and notes that Rava qualifies it significantly. The concern is not with common baldness, which is a natural variation, but with forms of baldness that are particularly unusual.

### Baldness Girsaot

There are multiple *girsaot* of Rava's statement, and the differences are consequential. One reading: the problem is when a *kohen* has no hair in the back of the head but has hair in the front — an extremely unusual pattern, the reverse of ordinary baldness. If he has hair in the back, even without hair in the front (which is common), he is *kasher*. Rashi reads the text differently: if a *kohen* has a complete ring of hair all the way around the base of the skull but is completely bald on top — the "monk's tonsure" configuration — that is the disqualifying pattern, because it is bizarre. If the baldness is in front only, or if there is hair both in front and back, this is also flagged in certain versions of the text. The *Shita Mekubetzet* records yet another *girsa*: according to one version of Rava's statement, total absence of hair is *pasul*; according to another, it is *kasher*.

### Natural Baldness OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי אַסֵּיפָא, וְאִם יֵשׁ לוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵין לוֹ לְפָנָיו, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ בֵּין לְאַחֲרָיו בֵּין לְפָנָיו — פָּסוּל, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְפָנָיו וְאֵין לוֹ לְאַחֲרָיו, וּדְאֵין לוֹ כְּלָל — דְּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are those who teach** the statement of Rava **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna: **And if he has** a row of hair, **that** person is **fit** for performing the Temple service. **Rava says:** The Sages **taught** that such a person is fit **only when he has** a row of hair **in the back but he does not have in the front. But** if **he has** a row of hair **both in the back and in the front,** he is **disqualified** from performing the Temple service. **And all the more so,** one **who has** a row of hair **in the front and does not have in the back, or** one **who does not have** any hair **at all,** is **disqualified.**

The operative principle across all the *girsaot* is consistent: normal baldness — the ordinary progression of male hair loss, even to a considerable degree — is not a disqualifying condition. Only baldness that presents in a pattern so aberrant as to be genuinely outside the normal range constitutes a problem. A *kohen* who is simply bald in the common manner is *kasher*. The concern is with the truly bizarre, such as complete absence of all hair or a ring pattern with total baldness on top.

## RY on Specific Cases

### RY's List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַקַּרְחָנִין וְהַנַּנָּסִין וְהַזַּבְלְגָנִין — פְּסוּלִין, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן. קַרְחָנִין — תְּנֵינָא! נַנָּסִין — תְּנֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The bald** priests, **and the** priests who are **dwarves, and the** priests with **leaky** eyes, are **disqualified** from performing the Temple service, **since they are not equal among the seed of Aaron.** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary for Rabbi Yoḥanan to state this? With regard to **bald** priests, **we learn** in the mishna that they are disqualified. Similarly, with regard to priests who are **dwarves, we learn** in a mishna (45b) that they are disqualified.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *harkarchanim vehananasim vehasavleganim* — *kohanim* who are bald in a disqualifying pattern, *kohanim* who are dwarfs, and *kohanim* with *savleganim* (a permanent condition of tearing or watery eyes) are *pesulim lefi she'eino shaveh bezaro shel Aharon* — disqualified because they do not fall within the normal distribution of Aaron's seed. Notably, Rabbi Yochanan frames all three as *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*, not as *mumim*.

### Watery Eyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַבְלְגָנִין אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן, וּמַהוּ דְּתֵימָא מִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It was necessary for him to teach us** this statement with regard to priests with **leaky** eyes, as the *halakha* with regard to them does not appear in any mishna. **And** furthermore, it was necessary for him to reiterate the *halakha* with regard to bald priests and dwarves **lest you say** they are disqualified only by rabbinic law, **due to the appearance** of a blemish. Rabbi Yochanan therefore teaches that they are disqualified by Torah law, as they are not equal among the seed of Aaron.

The Gemara questions whether Rabbi Yochanan's ruling was even necessary. *Karchanim* — unusual baldness — is addressed in our Mishnah. *Nanasim* — dwarfs — appear in a subsequent Mishnah. Why does Rabbi Yochanan need to enumerate them? The Gemara responds that the essential novelty is *savleganim* — watery eyes — which is not mentioned in any Mishnah. Rabbi Yochanan is teaching us that this condition too falls into the category of *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*.

### Gemara Challenge

But the Gemara pushes further. Even for *karchanim* and *nanasim*, which are in the Mishnah, one might have thought — having now defined three distinct categories — that the Mishnah classified them as problems of *marit ha'ayin* rather than *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. Rabbi Yochanan therefore teaches that they belong to the middle category, not the lowest.

### Eyelashes Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן (אִיתְנָא) [מִיתְנָא] קָתָנֵי, וְשֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ רִיסֵי עֵינָיו פָּסוּל מִפְּנֵי מַרְאִית הָעַיִן! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: תָּנֵי חֲדָא, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But anywhere that** a priest is deemed disqualified **due to the appearance** of a blemish, the ***tanna* teaches** this explicitly, e.g., in the mishna on this *amud*: **And one whose eyelashes have fallen out is disqualified** from performing the Temple service, **due to the appearance** of a blemish. The Gemara responds: Rabbi Yochanan's statement is necessary, **lest you say** the *tanna* **taught** explicitly in **one** instance that a priest is disqualified due to the appearance of a blemish **but the same is true with regard to all of** those listed after the first clause of the mishna, starting from the case of priests with humped backs. Therefore, Rabbi Yochanan teaches that this is not the *halakha*.

The Gemara, however, challenges this reasoning: the Mishnah itself, when it intends to mark something as *marit ha'ayin*, does so explicitly. The case of *venishru risei einav* — fallen eyelashes — is accompanied by the Mishnah's statement *pasul mipnei marit ha'ayin*. Since the Mishnah labels *marit ha'ayin* explicitly when it applies, one ought to be able to infer that anything not so labeled is not in that category — making Rabbi Yochanan's clarification unnecessary.

### Marit Ha'ayin Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מִיהְדָּר וּמִיתְנָא, קָא הָדַר וְתָנֵי: וְשֶׁנִּיטְּלוּ שִׁינָּיו פָּסוּל מִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן! אֶלָּא, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא, דְּתַנְיָא: הַקַּרְחָנִין וְהַנַּנָּסִין וְהַזַּבְלְגָנִין כְּשֵׁרִים, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ פָּסוּל אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: **But anywhere that there is** a priest disqualified due to the appearance of a blemish, the *tanna* **repeats and teaches** explicitly that this is the reason. As the *tanna* **repeats and teaches** (44a): **And one whose teeth fell out is disqualified due to the appearance** of a blemish. **Rather,** the reason Rabbi Yochanan reiterates the *halakha* with regard to a bald priest and a dwarf is **to exclude that which is taught** in a *baraita*, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The bald** priests, **the dwarves, and the** priests with **leaky** eyes **are fit** for performing the Temple service by Torah law, **and** the Sages **said** they are **disqualified only due to the appearance** of a blemish. Rabbi Yochanan therefore teaches that they are disqualified by Torah law, as they are not equal among the seed of Aaron.

The Gemara responds with a parsing question: when the Mishnah uses the phrase *mishum marit ha'ayin*, how much of the preceding list does it cover? If the phrase is stated at the end of a longer enumeration, one might read it as applying retroactively to everything listed before it. In that case, the *kohanim*-specific conditions listed earlier in the Mishnah might all be classified as *marit ha'ayin*, even without explicit labeling on each one. Rabbi Yochanan's teaching would then be necessary to clarify that *karchanim*, *nanasim*, and *savleganim* are *she'eino shaveh*, not mere *marit ha'ayin*.

### Baraita RY

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן הַאי תַּנָּא? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״הַכֹּהֲנִים״ — לְרַבּוֹת הַקַּרְחָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Who is this *tanna*** who says that such priests are fit by Torah law? **It is Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** When the verse states: "And Aaron's sons, **the priests,** shall lay the pieces" (Leviticus 1:8), this serves **to include the bald** priests in the performance of the Temple service.

The Gemara then reveals the full stakes: there is a *baraita* that explicitly rules these three cases — *karchanim*, *nanasim*, and *savleganim* — as *kesherim* altogether, with the only concern being *marit ha'ayin*. This *baraita* is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda, who learns from the verse *hakohanim* an inclusion of bald *kohanim* as valid. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the baldness and similar conditions are not a *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon* problem at all — at most, they raise a concern of *marit ha'ayin*.

### Pesak vs. Baraita

The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yochanan is ruling *against* the *baraita* of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yochanan's teaching is not merely an elaboration of what the Mishnah states; it is a *pesak* that rejects the lenient view of Rabbi Yehuda. According to Rabbi Yochanan, *karchanim*, *nanasim*, and *savleganim* are disqualified as *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon* — a biblical *aseh* — and not merely on the rabbinic ground of *marit ha'ayin*. This resolution is consistent with the three-tier framework: the Mishnah's usage of *marit ha'ayin*, when it appears more than once, signals that it applies only to the cases immediately adjacent to each occurrence; everything else defaults to *she'eino shaveh bezera shel Aharon*. And Rabbi Yochanan establishes that the halakha follows this stricter categorization rather than the *baraita*'s more permissive one.
