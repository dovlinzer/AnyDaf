# Sukkah 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lulav vs. Sukkah Blessin…

### Rav Yehuda's Position

The daf opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *lulav shiva ve-sukkah yom echad* — the *lulav* is blessed all seven days, while the *sukkah* receives a *bracha* on only one day. This is not a statement about when one is obligated to sit in the *sukkah*, but about when one recites the *bracha*. The counter-intuitive element is this: one blesses the *lulav* every day of the seven, including days that are presumably not biblical, yet one blesses the *sukkah* only once, despite the *sukkah* being a biblical *mitzvah* every day of the week. What is the underlying logic?

### Discrete vs. Continuous

The conceptual distinction that animates Rav Yehuda's position concerns whether a *mitzvah* consists of discrete acts or a single continuous process. We tend to think of sitting in a *sukkah* as a series of individual acts — this meal, that nap — each requiring its own *bracha*. But in truth, the nature of the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* is expressed in the verse *basukkot teshvu shivat yamim*: dwelling in the *sukkah* for the entire week. When one initially enters the *sukkah* and begins that dwelling, the *bracha* is recited; for the remainder of the week, each return is simply a continuation of the same uninterrupted act of residence.

The *Gemara* articulates this precisely. The reason *lulav* receives a *bracha* every day is that *lulav de-mafsik leilot mi-yamim* — the nights divide the days; the *mitzvah* of *lulav* does not apply at night, and therefore each day constitutes a distinct, separate act. *Kol yoma mitzvah bifnei atzmah hu* — every day stands on its own. By contrast, *sukkah de-lo mafsik leilot mi-yamim* — for *sukkah*, the nights do not interrupt the days. *Kulu shiva ke-chad yoma arichta damu* — all seven days are like one long day, one unbroken process of dwelling. The logic is elegant and reflects the genuine phenomenology of what it means to live in a *sukkah* for a week.

### Rabba bar Bar Chana

Rabba bar Bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states the precise opposite: *sukkah shiva ve-lulav yom echad* — seven days for *sukkah*, one day for *lulav*. His reasoning operates on an entirely different axis. *Sukkah de-oraita shiva* — every day of *sukkah* is a biblical *mitzvah*, and therefore warrants its own *bracha* each day. *Lulav de-rabbanan sagi lei be-chad yoma* — *lulav* is rabbinic after the first day, and one day suffices. More precisely: the first day is the only biblically mandated day, and the *bracha* of *asher kidishanu be-mitzvotav* belongs only to the biblical obligation. If one missed the first day of *lulav*, one would presumably not recite the *bracha* on the second day at all. The phrase *sagi lei be-chad yoma* thus means not that one day is sufficient as a matter of convenience, but that only one day is truly entitled to the *bracha*.

For this position, the argument about continuous process is unpersuasive — every day one continues to perform the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*, every day one ought to acknowledge it afresh with a *bracha*.

### Ravin's View

When Ravin arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he brought a different version of Rabbi Yochanan's position: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh sheva* — both *lulav* and *sukkah* receive *brachot* all seven days. For *lulav*, the rabbinic character of the subsequent days does not preclude a *bracha* — each day remains distinct. For *sukkah*, the biblical obligation every day demands daily acknowledgment regardless of the theoretical continuity of the process.

For the sake of completeness, there exists a logical fourth possibility — one *bracha* once for the entire week for both *sukkah* (because it is a continuous process) and *lulav* (because only the first day is biblical). This position, however, does not appear in the *Gemara*.

### Rav Yosef's Ruling

Rav Yosef concludes: *nechot hadin de-Rabba bar Bar Chana be-yadecha* — take hold firmly of the position of Rabba bar Bar Chana. *De-kulei Amoraim kaymi ke-vateih* — all the *Amoraim* agree with it, at least with respect to *sukkah*. The consensus, he claims, is seven days for *sukkah* and one day for *lulav*.

It bears noting that this is not our actual *psak*. Our practice treats *sukkah* not as a single continuous act with a *bracha* once daily, but as a series of discrete acts — each *shiur* of eating in the *sukkah* generates its own *bracha*. This effectively transforms the *sukkah* from something conceptually resembling an uninterrupted process into something more like the *lulav* model of distinct acts. Where does this come from? That will emerge as the *sugya* develops.

## Shehecheyanu on Sukkot

### Tosefta on Lulav

The *Gemara* cites a *Tosefta*: one who constructs a *lulav* for personal use — binding the *hadasim* and *aravot* together in preparation for the *mitzvah* — recites *baruch Shehecheyanu ve-kiyimanu ve-higianu lazman hazeh*. This is an important *halacha* that many are unaware of in practice.

### Two Aspects

There are two distinct aspects of *Shehecheyanu* on *Yamim Tovim*. The first is the *Shehecheyanu* of the sanctity of the day itself — *kedushat hayom* — recited at *Kiddush* as the *Yom Tov* begins. The second is the *Shehecheyanu* recited upon encountering a new periodic *mitzvah*, such as taking the *lulav* for the first time in the season. These are conceptually independent. The *Shehecheyanu* in *Kiddush* responds to the arrival of the *Yom Tov*; the *Shehecheyanu* on the *mitzvah* responds to one's initial engagement with that *mitzvah*.

### When Shehecheyanu Applies

This distinction explains a curious asymmetry. On the second night of *Sukkot* one does recite *Shehecheyanu* in *Kiddush*, but one does not recite *Shehecheyanu* when taking the *lulav* on the second day. Why the difference?

The *Shehecheyanu* of *kedushat hayom* must be recited at the actual moment of the day's sanctity — when the day begins. On the second night of *Yom Tov*, operating under *sfeika d'yoma*, one reasons: if yesterday was not truly *Yom Tov*, then yesterday's *Shehecheyanu* was meaningless, and today is the first real day of *Yom Tov*, demanding its own *Shehecheyanu*. Hence the second-night *Shehecheyanu* is recited again.

The *Shehecheyanu* upon a *mitzvah*, however, operates differently. It marks the encounter with a new periodic *mitzvah*, and that encounter can begin at various stages of engagement — not necessarily at the moment of performance. One may recite *Shehecheyanu* on a new garment when purchasing it, or when wearing it for the first time; the blessing responds to any meaningful stage of initial contact. Therefore, even if yesterday were not truly *Yom Tov*, the act of taking the *lulav* yesterday — or even the act of constructing and preparing it — already constituted an encounter with the *mitzvah*, and that *Shehecheyanu* holds. Today, even if it were the first actual performance of the *mitzvah*, the *Shehecheyanu* was already covered. One does not recite it again.

This is why the *Tosefta* says the ideal time to recite *Shehecheyanu* on the *lulav* is when constructing it — upon first genuine engagement. Similarly, *ha'oseh sukkah le'atzmo omer Shehecheyanu* — one who builds the *sukkah* for personal use recites *Shehecheyanu* at that moment. This is the principle underlying the *Tosefta*.

The *Shehecheyanu* recited over *Kiddush* on *Sukkot* night thus does double duty — it covers both the arrival of the *Yom Tov* and the initial engagement with the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*. This double function has practical implications for the ordering of *brachot* on the first and second nights, though a full treatment of that topic is a discussion unto itself.

## Baraita Contradiction

### Baraita vs. Amoraim

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* that creates a sharp tension. The *baraita* states: one who constructs a *lulav* recites *Shehecheyanu*; when taking it to fulfill the *mitzvah*, one recites *al netilat lulav*. And further: *af al pi shebeirach alav yom rishon, chozer umevarech kol shivah* — even though one blessed the *lulav* on the first day, one returns and blesses it all seven days. For *sukkah*: one who builds it recites *Shehecheyanu*, and when first entering recites *asher kidishanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu leishev basukkah*. But then: *keivan shebeirach yom rishon, shuv eino mevarech* — once one has blessed it on the first day, one never blesses it again.

This is the exact inversion of the earlier *Amoraic* statement endorsed by Rav Yosef: seven days for *lulav* and one day for *sukkah*, rather than one for *lulav* and seven for *sukkah*. The *Gemara* therefore declares: *kashya lulav a'lulav, kashya sukkah a'sukkah* — there is a contradiction on both *lulav* and *sukkah*.

### Beit HaMikdash Context

The contradiction regarding *lulav* is readily resolved. *Bishlama lulav a'lulav lo kashya* — for *lulav*, there is no real difficulty: *kan bizman she-Beit HaMikdash kayam, kan bizman she-ein Beit HaMikdash kayam*. The *baraita* that requires a *bracha* all seven days may reflect the context of the *Beit HaMikdash*, when *lulav* was a biblical obligation every day. Rashi adds that this specifically addresses one who went up to the *Beit HaMikdash* and performed the *mitzvah* there each day. In that context, every day is biblical and warrants its own *bracha*. The earlier *Amoraic* position that only one day receives a *bracha* assumes the post-Temple situation, in which only the first day is biblical. These are simply two different historical contexts.

### Tannaitic Debate

The contradiction regarding *sukkah* is more fundamental and cannot be resolved by appeal to historical context. *Ella sukkah a'sukkah kashya* — the *baraita* says one *bracha* at the beginning; the *Amoraic* consensus says seven days. The *Gemara* responds: *tannai hi* — this is in fact a *Tannaitic* dispute. What the *Amoraim* debated reflects an underlying disagreement between *Tannaim*, the traces of which appear in the *baraita* on *tefillin* to which the *Gemara* now turns.

## Tefillin Analogy

### Rabbi's View

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* on *tefillin*: *kol zman she-menichan mevarech aleihem, divrei Rabbi* — whenever one puts on *tefillin*, one recites a *bracha*, even if one puts them on multiple times in a single day. Rabbi's position is that each act of donning *tefillin* is a discrete act requiring its own *bracha*, even within the same continuous day.

### Chachamim's View

The *Chachamim* disagree: *eino mevarech ella shacharit bilvad* — one recites the *bracha* only once in the morning. Their reasoning is that wearing *tefillin* is a continuous process throughout the day; it is not a series of distinct acts but a single extended act of fulfilling the *mitzvah*. Taking them off briefly and putting them back on does not generate a new *bracha* because the process has not ended — it has merely been interrupted.

This dispute maps directly onto the question of *sukkah*. The *Chachamim*'s position — once for the whole process — is the basis for the view that *sukkah* receives a single *bracha* at the start of the seven days. The entire week is one uninterrupted process, and the initial *bracha* covers it. Rabbi's position — that even within a continuous process there are multiple moments that generate a new *bracha* — supports the view that *sukkah* too receives multiple *brachot* across the week.

What precisely constitutes those multiple opportunities? Rashi, reading the earlier *Gemara* discussion simply, understands it to mean once per day — each day is somehow a distinct enough unit even within the ongoing process. Tosafot, drawing the comparison to *tefillin* more tightly, understands it as every discrete act of re-entering the *sukkah* for a purposeful act — eating or sleeping — just as each distinct act of donning *tefillin* generates a new *bracha*. This reading of Tosafot is in fact our practice.

### Abaye vs. Rava

*Itmar* — it was stated. Abaye rules *hilcheta ke-Rabbi* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi, and therefore for *tefillin* one blesses each time it is donned anew, and for *sukkah* one blesses multiple times throughout the week. Rava rules *hilcheta ke-Rabbanan* — the *halacha* follows the *Chachamim*: once for the process of *tefillin* per day, and perhaps once for the entire week of *sukkah*.

### Rava's Inconsistency

Rav Mari, son of Shmuel's daughter, reports: *chazina lei le-Rava de-lo avid kishmatei* — I observed that Rava did not act in accordance with his own stated position. Rava's practice was to rise early, go to the bathroom, come out, wash his hands, put on *tefillin*, and recite a *bracha*. Later in the day, when he needed to use the bathroom again, he would remove his *tefillin*, enter the bathroom, come out, wash his hands, re-don his *tefillin*, and recite a *bracha* again. Thus Rava himself recited multiple *brachot* on *tefillin* in a single day.

One might argue this is a special case. When *tefillin* are removed to enter the bathroom — a space where one is prohibited from wearing them — that prohibition constitutes an absolute break in the process. It is not merely an interruption; it is a mandated discontinuity. The same logic would not apply to simply removing *tefillin* temporarily and replacing them without entering a prohibited space, nor would it necessarily apply to *sukkah*, where there is never a moment one is forbidden from being inside. However, since Rava had categorically said *hilcheta ke-Rabbanan* — once and no more — and yet in practice recited a second *bracha*, the observation stands: even Rava conceded that "once per day" does not always hold. The upshot is *ve-anan nami ke-Rabbi avidna* — we too follow Rabbi's position, not the *Rabbanan*. By *tefillin*, one recites a *bracha* each time it is newly donned, and *umi-varchim kol shiva* — similarly for *sukkah*, one recites a *bracha* throughout all seven days.

The *Gemara* also cites the practice of the students of Rav Ashi, who would make a *bracha* every time they touched their *tefillin*, even while wearing them continuously. This is the most striking parallel to *sukkah*: even without removing and replacing the *tefillin*, a conscious re-engagement with the *mitzvah* prompted a new *bracha*. One can see how the *sukkah*, in which the process is never actually interrupted, might similarly admit of multiple *brachot* whenever one returns to the *sukkah* with renewed purposeful intent.

## Final Sukkah Ruling

### Anu Nami k'Rabbi

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is that we follow Rabbi's position with respect to *tefillin*, and by the parallel logic, we recite a *bracha* on *sukkah* throughout the seven days — *umi-varchim kol shiva*. The phrase *kol shiva* — all seven — is understood differently by Rashi and Tosafot.

### Rashi's Interpretation

Rashi reads *kol shiva* simply: once per day, every day. Each day is treated as a renewed encounter with the *mitzvah*, and each morning one recites the *bracha* afresh. This is an acknowledgment that even a continuous process benefits from daily re-labeling — a way of ensuring that the *mitzvah* remains present in one's conscious experience each day rather than fading into the background of routine.

### Tosafot's Interpretation

Tosafot's reading maps the comparison to *tefillin* more precisely. *Lulav eino mevarech ella pa'am achat ba-yom* — for *lulav*, one blesses only once per day, since shaking the *lulav* is a single act completed at one moment. *Aval sukkah kol eimat de-nichnas lah* — but for *sukkah*, every time one enters it for a purposeful act — eating, sitting, sleeping — *ve-afilu eser pe'amim ba-yom mevarech al kol achat ve-achat* — one blesses each such act, even ten times in a day.

This precisely mirrors the *tefillin* framework: just as each discrete act of donning *tefillin* generates a *bracha*, each discrete act of entering the *sukkah* for fulfillment of the *mitzvah* generates a *bracha*. And this is our practice. In this way, what is conceptually a continuous seven-day process has been transformed — via the *tefillin* analogy — into a series of discrete acts, each with its own *bracha*.

One additional point deserves mention. The *sukkah* is, in a genuine sense, more continuous than *tefillin*. With *tefillin*, there are real moments of non-wearing: one is forbidden from wearing *tefillin* into a bathroom, and one is not permitted to sleep in them. With *sukkah*, there is no such interruption. Even when one is not physically inside the *sukkah*, one can still be said to be "living" in the *sukkah* — just as a person lives in their home without being physically present in it at every moment. The idea that the entire week constitutes one uninterrupted act of dwelling has real conceptual power. Nevertheless, following the *tefillin* model, the *Gemara* concludes that multiple *brachot* are appropriate, and that is what we do.

## Blessings on Rabbinic Mi…

### All Seven Days

The *Gemara* returns to the *lulav* question with a new set of statements. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel says: *Mitzvat Lulav kol shiva* — the *mitzvah* of *lulav* is all seven days. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: *yom rishon Mitzvat Lulav, mi-kan va-eilah Mitzvat zekenim* — the first day is the Torah's *mitzvah* of *lulav*; thereafter it is a rabbinic institution. The question is not whether one is obligated to take the *lulav* on those subsequent days — that is agreed — but whether one recites the *bracha* of *asher kidishanu be-mitzvotav* on days that are rabbinic in character.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

Rav Yehuda's statement that *Mitzvat Lulav kol shiva* should be understood in this context to mean: one recites the *bracha* all seven days. The association of making a *bracha* with "fully owning" the identity of an act as a *mitzvah* is striking and recurring. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds that only the first day, which is biblical, warrants the *bracha*; the remaining days are rabbinic and receive no *bracha*.

Rabbi Yitzchak appears to say *kol yoma Mitzvat zekenim* — every day is rabbinic, and no *bracha* is made. The *Gemara* immediately challenges: does he mean even the first day is only rabbinic? That is a remarkable position, seemingly denying a biblical obligation in *lulav* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *Gemara* reinterprets: *bar mi-yom rishon* — he means all the days after the first are rabbinic. But then this is indistinguishable from the position of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and the *Gemara* accepts this reconciliation. What the simple reading of Rabbi Yitzchak's statement may suggest — that he considered *lulav* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* to be entirely rabbinic — is a striking position that the *Gemara* is compelled to reinterpret.

### Chanukah Example

Rav is then cited as also holding that the *mitzvah* of *lulav* is all seven days — meaning he recites the *bracha* all seven days. The *Gemara* demonstrates this from a statement of Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav: *ha-madlik ner shel Chanukah tzarich levarech* — one who lights the *Chanukah* candle must recite a *bracha*. This demonstrates that Rav has no objection to reciting *asher kidishanu be-mitzvotav* on a rabbinic *mitzvah*.

The *Gemara* adds the ruling of Rabbi Yirmiyah: *ha-ro'eh ner shel Chanukah tzarich levarech* — even one who merely sees *Chanukah* candles and did not light them recites a *bracha*. What *bracha*? *She'asah nissim*. For *Chanukah* and *Purim*, the *bracha* of *she'asah nissim* is appropriate because these *mitzvot* are centered on publicizing the miracle — *pirsumei nisa*. The person who sees the lit candles is the recipient of that publicized miracle and therefore recites the *bracha* as a participant in that act of *pirsumei nisa*.

The *Gemara* then records the precise text and structure of the *Chanukah brachot*. On the first night, the lighter recites three *brachot*: *l'hadlik ner shel Chanukah*, *she'asah nissim*, and *Shehecheyanu*. The one who merely sees recites two: *she'asah nissim* and *Shehecheyanu* — for *Shehecheyanu* marks one's encounter with the *mitzvah*, and this encounter occurs even without performing the act of lighting. From the second night onward, the lighter recites two: *l'hadlik* and *she'asah nissim*, dropping *Shehecheyanu*; the observer recites one: *she'asah nissim* alone.

### Rabbinic Authority

Now that the *Gemara* has established that a *bracha* is recited on *Chanukah* candles, the obvious question arises: *ve-heichan tzivanu* — where exactly were we commanded to light? This is the foundational question for any *bracha* recited on a rabbinic *mitzvah*.

Two sources are offered. *Mi-lo tasur* — from the verse prohibiting deviation from the rulings of the *Beit Din ha-Gadol*, the *kohanim* and *shofet* at the place God will choose. We are biblically mandated to follow rabbinic authority, and therefore we may say *ve-tzivanu* even regarding rabbinic enactments. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an alternative: *sha'al avicha ve-yageidcha* — ask your father and he will tell you; follow the traditions and rulings of prior generations.

Why seek a second *pasuk* beyond *lo tasur*? Because *lo tasur* addresses the rabbis' interpretive function — their role in determining how biblical *mitzvot* are to be understood and applied. That is the classic domain of *lo tasur*: go to the sages when there is a question about how to observe the Torah's laws, and do not deviate from their ruling. When it comes to the rabbis establishing entirely new practices — like *Chanukah* — that is arguably outside *lo tasur*'s stated scope. *Sha'al avicha ve-yageidcha* speaks to a broader mandate: simply follow what your elders have established, even in their creative and legislative capacity, not only in their interpretive one.

The *Gemara* does not raise this question for *Purim*, because *Purim* followed a different path: the effort to include *Megillat Esther* within *Tanach* — as expressed in the *midrash* of Esther's request *kitvuni le-dorot* — meant that once the *Megillah* was canonized, reading it could be said to be commanded by God directly through *Tanach*, not merely through rabbinic enactment. The question of *ve-heichan tzivanu* thus resolved itself for *Purim* through canonization. For *Chanukah*, where no such claim was available, the *Gemara* had to address the authority question directly through *lo tasur* and *sha'al avicha*.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explicitly teaches this ruling regarding *lulav* as well — not merely as an inference from the *Chanukah* precedent, but directly: Rav stated that *Mitzvat Lulav kol shiva*, meaning one recites the *bracha* all seven days, including the rabbinic days of *Chol HaMoed*.

## Practical Application

### Pre-Built Sukkah

The *Gemara* returns to the practical *halacha* of *brachot* on *sukkah*. The *baraita* states: one who builds a *sukkah* recites *Shehecheyanu*; when entering for the first time to fulfill the *mitzvah*, one recites *asher kidishanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu leishev basukkah*. But what of a person whose *sukkah* was already standing — *hayetah asuyah ve-omedet* — pre-built from before the holiday? There was no act of construction to serve as the occasion for *Shehecheyanu*.

The ruling is: *im yachol lechadesh bah davar* — if one can add something new to the *sukkah* — new decorations, additional *sechach* — do so, and use that new engagement as the occasion for *Shehecheyanu*. If not, *ve-im lav*, then when one enters the *sukkah* to perform the *mitzvah* for the first time, one recites both *brachot* — the *bracha* of *leishev ba-sukkah* and the *Shehecheyanu* — together.

### Integration Into Kiddush

Rav Assi reports: *chazina lei le-Rav Kahana de-kamar lehu le-kulhu akasa de-kiddush*a — I saw Rav Kahana recite all the *brachot* over the *Kiddush* cup. This is our practice.

It is instructive to observe the stages through which the *Gemara* moves. Ideally: recite *Shehecheyanu* when constructing the *sukkah*. Did not do so? Add something new to the *sukkah* and recite it then. Did not do so? Recite it upon first entering the *sukkah*. Did not do so? Recite it over *Kiddush*, where it does double duty — for the *Yom Tov* and for the *sukkah* simultaneously. That last stage is where we have settled in practice, and Rav Kahana's usage suggests this is not merely *bedi'eved* but a considered and complete fulfillment. Reciting *Shehecheyanu* in *Kiddush* before one has actually performed the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* is permissible because, as established earlier, *Shehecheyanu* on a *mitzvah* can be recited at the stage of first engagement, not only at the moment of performance.

## Multiple Mitzvot

### Multiple Mitzvot Options

The *Gemara* broadens the discussion of *brachot* to the case of multiple *mitzvot* performed simultaneously. A *baraita* addresses the scenario in which several *mitzvot* are before a person at once — Rashi offers the example of taking the *lulav*, sitting in the *sukkah*, putting on *tefillin*, and putting on *tzitzit*. One option is to recite a single comprehensive *bracha*: *baruch asher kidishanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu al ha-mitzvot* — a single blessing covering all the obligations at once.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *mevarech al kol achat ve-achat bifnei atzmah* — one must recite a separate *bracha* for each *mitzvah* individually.

### Halacha Ruling

Rabbi Zeira — and some attribute this to Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa — rules that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda: each *mitzvah* receives its own individual *bracha*.

### Theological Basis

What is the reasoning behind Rabbi Yehuda's position? The *Gemara* cites the verse *baruch Hashem yom yom* — blessed is God day by day. One might ask: does this mean one blesses God during the day but not at night? The *Gemara* responds: *bechol yom va-yom ten lo me'ein birchotav* — each day, acknowledge God in a manner that reflects the distinct character and gifts of that day. On *Rosh Chodesh* one mentions *Rosh Chodesh*; on *Chanukah* one mentions *Chanukah*. Each day carries its own unique quality, and one's acknowledgment of God should reflect that particularity.

The same principle applies to multiple *mitzvot*: *bechol davar va-davar ten lo me'ein birchotav* — for each distinct *mitzvah*, acknowledge God in a manner befitting that specific blessing. The *mitzvah* of *sukkah* represents a distinct and particular way God has sanctified us, different from the *mitzvah* of *lulav*, different from the *mitzvah* of *tallit*, different from the *mitzvah* of *tefillin*. To lump them into a single *bracha* diminishes the distinctive character of each one, both theologically — in terms of how we acknowledge God's unique blessings — and experientially — in terms of how we relate to each *mitzvah* as its own special bond.

## Fullness and Emptiness

### The Parable

The same sage — Rabbi Zeira, or some say Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa — offers a striking teaching on the difference between human ways and divine ways. *Bo re'eh shelo ke-middat ha-kadosh baruch hu middat basar va-dam* — come and see that God's ways are not the ways of human beings. In the realm of human experience, *kli reikan machazik* — an empty vessel can hold new content; *malei eino machazik* — a full vessel cannot. This is the normal logic of physical containment. But with God, the reverse obtains: *malei machazik* — a full vessel holds more; *reikan eino machazik* — an empty vessel cannot hold.

### Im Shamoa Tishma

The proof-text: *ve-hayah im shamoa tishma* — if you have truly listened. The conditional structure is read as: *im shamoa* — if you have already listened before, *tishma* — then you will be able to hear more.

### Disposition and Attention

Rashi explains: one who has trained oneself in the disposition of paying attention and listening has developed a capacity that enables continued and deeper learning. The more one has listened before, the more one is capable of listening going forward. Conversely, *ve-im lav* — if one has not cultivated this disposition, future listening will be fruitless: *lo tishma* — you will not succeed in hearing again.

### Learning in Context

One may also understand this principle in terms of the association of ideas. When a learner encounters a new concept and already possesses an existing network of related knowledge, the new idea finds connections and anchors itself within that network. It is held more securely, understood more deeply, because it does not arrive in an empty space but into a context of meaning. If the mind is empty of prior learning, there are no connections to be made, and the new material passes through without taking hold. This is not merely an ancient insight; it reflects what contemporary educational theory recognizes as constructivist learning — that understanding is built by connecting new knowledge to prior knowledge.

### Review and Continuity

The *Gemara* adds a *davar acher* — another reading of the same verse — drawing on the following phrase: *ve-im yifneh levavecha ve-lo tishma* — and if your heart turns away and you do not hear. Reading this alongside the first clause: *im shamoa ba-yashan tishma ba-chadash* — if you have reviewed the old material, you will be able to hear the new. The act of *chazarah* — review — keeps prior learning active and available, creating the cognitive and dispositional conditions under which new learning can be absorbed. But *ve-im yifneh levavecha ve-lo tishma* — if you abandon review, if your heart turns away, *shuv lo tishma* — you will not be able to learn anew.

The deeper point of the parable is that God, in making the human mind, fashioned a vessel whose capacity increases with use and filling. The human being is a container unlike any made by human hands — the more that is placed within it, the more it can receive. It is God's handiwork, and this is how He has made us: *malei machazik, reikan eino machazik*.
