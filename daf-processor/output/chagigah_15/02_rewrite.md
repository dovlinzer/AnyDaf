# Chagigah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Merkavah Transmission

### Yochanan & Elazar

The daf opens by picking up the threads of the *maaseh merkavah* discussions that had been unfolding across the previous material. We had seen Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai and Rabbi Elazar ben Arach engaged in these exchanges, and similarly Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Yossi HaKohen. The earlier narrative had culminated in an extraordinary vision at Har Sinai — the concept of *vayochelu vayishtu*, the eating and drinking at the mountain, the being called up to an encounter with the divine presence. The *gemara* now pauses to interrogate whether the full picture of this transmission has been accurately represented.

### Four Generations

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* — found approximately ten lines down in the wide-line text — in which Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda teaches that there were three *hartza'ot*, meaning three formal presentations or expositions of *maaseh merkavah*, each in a *rav-talmid* relationship. Rabbi Yehoshua presented *maaseh merkavah* before Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai. Rabbi Akiva — who, it bears noting, was a student of Rabbi Yehoshua — then presented before Rabbi Yehoshua. And Chananya ben Chachinai presented before Rabbi Akiva. The chain thus runs across four generations, each disciple expounding these matters before his master.

### Elazar Absent

This immediately raises a question: what of Rabbi Elazar ben Arach, who figures so prominently in the earlier narrative? Why is he not counted in this chain? The *gemara* answers: *d'artzi ve-artzu kamei kachashiv* — the list counts only those who both presented and had it presented before them. The point of the chain is a continuous *rav-talmid* tradition. Rabbi Elazar ben Arach may well have expounded *maaseh merkavah* before Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai, but he never had a student who then carried the tradition forward and presented it back to him. The chain, therefore, does not run through him: *d'artzi velo artzu kamei, lo kachashiv*.

This is consistent with a broader and melancholy fact about Rabbi Elazar ben Arach. In *Pirkei Avot* he is described as *ma'ayan hamitgaber*, an ever-strengthening spring, and Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai was dazzled by his independent mastery of *maaseh merkavah*. Yet we have almost no surviving traditions in his name in the *talmud* or elsewhere. The story attached to this silence is that he relocated to a city where *talmidei chachamim* were few — apparently drawn by the comfort of its famous bathhouses — assuming that given his stature, scholars would follow him. They did not. The principle *hevei goleh limkom Torah* — one must pursue Torah, it will not pursue you — proved itself, and Rabbi Elazar ben Arach's learning withered. His near-absence from the rabbinic record is thus entirely of a piece with his exclusion from this chain of *maaseh merkavah* transmission.

### Chananya Counted

This resolution, however, creates an apparent inconsistency. Chananya ben Chachinai, the last figure in the list, presented before Rabbi Akiva — but nobody is recorded as having presented *maaseh merkavah* before him. Why is he counted if he, like Rabbi Elazar ben Arach, is missing one side of the *rav-talmid* relationship? The *gemara* answers directly: *d'artzi miya kamei man d'artzi* — he at least presented before Rabbi Akiva, who had himself received the tradition from Rabbi Yehoshua. That is sufficient to place Chananya within the chain, because the chain is reckoned from the top down — A presented to B, B to C, C to D — and Chananya occupies the terminal position. Rabbi Elazar ben Arach, by contrast, stands entirely outside the chain: he never served as the link through which the tradition passed onward.

## Pardes Entry

### Four Names

With the *maaseh merkavah* chain established, the *gemara* now introduces one of the most celebrated and discussed passages in all of rabbinic literature. *Tanu Rabbanan*: four entered the *pardes* — a term universally understood to refer to some form of heavenly ascent, whether as mystical study or as literal ascent to the supernal realm. The four are identified: Ben Azzai, Ben Zoma, Acher — that is, Elisha ben Avuyah — and Rabbi Akiva.

### Marble Stones

Before they entered, Rabbi Akiva issued a warning: *keshe'atem magi'im eitzel avnei shayish tahor* — when you arrive at the stones of pure marble, do not say "water, water," for what appears to be water is not water at all, and the one who speaks falsehood will not be established before God. Of all the possible warnings one might imagine being given before a heavenly ascent, this is a striking one: the danger of misidentifying what one sees and thereby uttering something false in the presence of the divine.

### Acher Destroyed

Each of the four entered and met a different fate. Acher — Elisha ben Avuyah — *kitzetz b'netiyot*: he cut down the young shoots. The image is deliberately chosen. One has entered a garden, and the most destructive act imaginable in a garden is to cut down the young growths before they can flourish. Whatever he encountered within the *pardes*, the result was devastation rather than enlightenment.

### Akiva Success

Rabbi Akiva alone *yatza b'shalom* — he exited in peace, the only one of the four to emerge without harm.

### Chananel Vision

Before turning to the individual fates, it is worth pausing over a remarkable passage in Rabbeinu Chananel, who draws on the *Aruch* and on the *heichalot* literature to explain what the *pardes* actually was. Rabbeinu Chananel writes: *kinnu'uhu Pardes* — they called it *pardes*, *mei'ein Gan Eden shehu genuzah l'tzaddikim* — because it is like the *Gan Eden* hidden away for the righteous. More specifically, *kach oto makom hu makom ba'Aravot* — that place is the place in the *Aravot*, the highest of the heavens, where the souls of the righteous are gathered, as was discussed in the earlier *gemara*. And this, he says, is spelled out in the *heichalot* literature.

### Heichalot Practice

What Rabbeinu Chananel then describes is a complete portrait of the theurgic mystical practice underlying the *pardes* narrative. The sages who were worthy of this level would pray, would cleanse themselves from all impurity, would fast, immerse in the *mikvah*, purify themselves, and then *v'hayu mishtamshim b'shemot* — would make use of divine names. Through these preparations they would achieve visions: *tzofim b'heichalos* — they would peer into the heavenly *heichalot*, the divine chambers, seeing how the angelic watches stood guard over the divine temple, one chamber opening into the next.

Rabbi Akiva's warning, in this framework, is recast. Rabbeinu Chananel writes that he told them: when you are prepared to see *be'uvnusa dliba* — in your mind's eye, as one might translate that phrase — when you reach the marble stones, do not say "water, water," because there is no water there at all; it is only *dmus baolam nires*, a kind of vision. The *heichalot rabbasi* explains the source of the optical illusion: the guardians of the divine chamber pour out what appear to be enormous waves of water from the marble entrance, yet there is not even a single drop of water there — only the radiant glow of the pure marble stones. Anyone who asks "what is this water?" is simply pushed away, *nehdaf*, expelled.

Crucially, Rabbeinu Chananel is explicit that the sages did not literally ascend to heaven: *v'einam olim bashamayim ela tzofim v'ro'im be'uvnusa dliba* — they saw it in their mind's eye, *ke'adam haroeh u'mehabit mitoch aspaklaria she'eina me'ira* — like someone seeing through a cloudy lens. This is of course the classical distinction: all the *nevi'im* saw through a cloudy lens; only Moshe saw through a clear one.

In this framework, Ben Azzai's fate is explained as follows: he added further divine names in order to achieve a vision through a clear lens rather than a cloudy one — *kdei lehabit be'aspaklaria me'ira* — and he died as a result. Ben Zoma used divine names beyond what he could handle and *nitrefah da'ato* — his mind became disordered. And Acher cut down the *netiyot* in the garden. This association of the *pardes* story with the *heichalot* literature is illuminating precisely because when people think of Jewish mysticism, they tend to think of the *Zohar* and of descriptive literature — but the *heichalot* material preserves something different: a theurgic practice, a structured discipline of fasting, immersion, the use of divine names, and induced mystical vision. That is what Rabbeinu Chananel identifies as the background of this narrative.

One question that arises is whether *mayim* here functions as a metaphor for *Torah*, as it so often does in rabbinic literature. The resonance is not obviously present in this particular context, and the imagery of water in the *pardes* story remains genuinely elusive.

### Ben Azzai Dies

Turning to the individual fates: *Ben Azzai heitzitz vamet* — Ben Azzai peered and died. The verse applied to him is *yakar be'einei Hashem hamavta l'chasidav* — dear in the eyes of God is the death of His righteous ones — which pointedly frames him as among God's *chasidim* rather than treating his death as straightforward punishment. The Maharsha attempts to explain that Ben Azzai's soul achieved direct communion with the divine to such a degree that the body simply could not survive. Yet the formulation *heitzitz vamet* — he peered and he died — carries an ambivalence that is difficult to resolve entirely. It sounds as though something went wrong, even as the prooftext insists on his righteousness.

### Rambam Theory

Rambam reads the entire *pardes* narrative in a strikingly different register. For him, the subject is not mystical ascent at all but rather the intellectual study of theology — what he calls *ma'aseh merkavah* in the sense of metaphysics. He writes that if one proceeds properly — not claiming demonstration where none exists, not hastily rejecting propositions whose negation has not been demonstrated, and not attempting to apprehend what lies beyond one's apprehension — one will achieve human perfection at the level of Rabbi Akiva, who *nichnas b'shalom v'yatza b'shalom*. But if one overreaches intellectually, one joins Elisha Acher: not merely imperfect but "most deficient among the deficient," overcome by delusive imaginings and an inclination toward evil. The image of honey is similarly rationalized: just as excess honey upsets the stomach and causes vomiting, so intellectual apprehension, if not arrested at its proper limit, may be perverted into a defect. This is an elegant reading, but it should be stated plainly: it is not *peshat*.

### Ben Zoma Injured

*Ben Zoma heitzitz v'nifga* — Ben Zoma peered and was injured. The verse applied to him is *dvash matzata echol dayeka, pen tisba'enu v'hikeito* — if you have found honey, eat only what is sufficient for you, lest you sate yourself and vomit it back. He went beyond what he was capable of absorbing, and the result was injury — commonly understood, as it is even before Milton Steinberg made it famous, as mental disorder. Unlike Ben Azzai, whose framing in the verse suggests a kind of tragic sanctity, the verse applied to Ben Zoma carries a note of censure: he overextended, and he suffered for it.

## Ben Zoma After

### Castrated Dog

The *gemara* now presents two halakhic discussions attributed to Ben Zoma, and their placement here is not immediately obvious. They asked Ben Zoma: *ma hu l'sarsei kalba* — is it permitted to castrate a dog? Since a dog is entirely outside the category of *korbanot*, one might have thought that the concerns surrounding castration — which are typically tied to sacrificial fitness — would not apply. Ben Zoma answers from the verse: *u'v'artzachem lo ta'asu* — the verse concerning castrated animals that may not be brought as sacrifices concludes with a general prohibition, "do not do this in your land," which Ben Zoma reads as extending the prohibition to any animal, not only those eligible for sacrifice. This formulation, as is well known, creates substantial halakhic difficulty for the contemporary practice of neutering pets.

### Virgin Pregnant

The second question put to Ben Zoma: *besulah she'ibra mahu l'Kohen Gadol* — if a virgin has conceived, what is her status with respect to marriage to a *Kohen Gadol*, who is required to marry a *besulah*? The question turns on whether it is possible to be simultaneously pregnant and still a virgin in the halakhic sense. The *gemara* raises Shmuel's ruling that a man can have intercourse with a virgin multiple times without rupturing the hymen — but answers that this is uncommon, *lo shchiach*. If so, the most plausible explanation for a woman who is pregnant but whose hymen is intact is not that she had intercourse — since that would be an uncommon scenario — but rather *shema be'ambati ibra*: she may have conceived in a bathhouse, having immersed in water into which a man had previously ejaculated.

The *gemara* then challenges this: does Shmuel not also rule that semen which does not exit *kechetz* — with the force of an arrow — cannot cause conception? The answer is that the requirement of *yoreh kechetz* applies to the moment the semen exits the man; once it has done so in that fashion, it retains its potency even if it subsequently enters the woman's body through the water of a bathhouse.

Setting aside the obvious scientific difficulties, this *gemara* becomes the classical locus for later discussions of artificial insemination, as it establishes the conceptual possibility of conception without direct sexual contact and raises questions of paternity that are addressed elsewhere in the literature. The comparison to the *midrash* about Ben Sira's conception is relevant here as well.

### Christian Context

What this discussion is doing in the immediate context of the *pardes* narrative is genuinely puzzling. One possibility is that it is here to establish that however Ben Zoma's encounter with the *pardes* affected him, he retained the capacity for coherent halakhic reasoning. His breakdown was specific to the mystical domain, as we will see shortly. But another possibility — and it is worth raising even if it cannot be definitively established — is that these discussions are colored by the question of Christian influence. The notion of a virgin who is truly pregnant, and Ben Zoma's insistence that the most plausible explanation maintains her virginity intact, resonates uncomfortably with Christian nativity claims. Indeed, a scholarly article has identified other Ben Zoma texts that may reflect contact with proto-Christian or proto-Gospel traditions. If that reading is correct, then even the question about the castrated dog may carry hidden resonances. The difficulty is that the halakhic content of both discussions is entirely straightforward, which makes it hard to press the point.

On the specific halakhic question: Ben Zoma's position is that the *be'ulah/besulah* distinction turns on the rupturing of the hymen, and his answer is that if the hymen is intact, she should be treated as a *besulah* regardless of the pregnancy, because the most logical explanation that accounts for both facts — intact hymen plus pregnancy — is the bathhouse scenario rather than intercourse. His final position, in other words, is that she genuinely is a virgin who genuinely is pregnant.

## Temple Mount Vision

### Yehoshua Meeting

The *gemara* now presents the story of Ben Zoma's encounter with Rabbi Yehoshua on Har HaBayit. Rabbi Yehoshua was standing on an elevated step on the Temple Mount — which is itself a fascinating detail, raising questions about the *tumah* status of those present there — when he saw Ben Zoma. Ben Zoma did not rise in his presence. This was a significant slight: Rabbi Yehoshua was a figure of the prior generation, very much a *rebbe* in relation to Ben Zoma. Rabbi Yehoshua asked him: *me-ayin u-le-ayin Ben Zoma* — where are you coming from and where are you going? Your head is so far elsewhere that you didn't even see me standing here. One might also hear in this question an echo of *me-ayin bata u-le-an attah holekh*, the classic call to self-reckoning.

### Three Fingerbreadths

Ben Zoma's answer reveals exactly where his mind was. He said: *tzofeh hayiti bein mayim ha-elyonim la-mayim ha-tachtonim* — I was peering between the supernal waters and the lower waters, and there is only three fingerbreadths separating them. His proof: *v'ruach Elohim merachefet al pnei ha-mayim, k'yonah she-merachefet al baneha v'eina noga'at* — the spirit of God hovered over the waters like a dove hovering over its young without touching them — indicating that the divine spirit, which he associates with the supernal waters, was only the barest distance above the lower waters. Three fingerbreadths. Here, too, the water theme from the *pardes* narrative resurfaces.

### Still Outside

Rabbi Yehoshua's response to his students was devastating in its brevity: *adayin Ben Zoma mibachutz* — Ben Zoma is still outside. He has not arrived. Whether this means his mystical understanding is off-base, or that he is verging on something quasi-heretical, or simply that his mind has not properly returned from wherever it has gone, the verdict is clear: his head is entirely in the clouds, and what he is saying in the mystical domain is not right.

### Water Separation

The *gemara* then analyzes the substance of Ben Zoma's error. The verse he cited — *v'ruach Elohim merachefet al pnei ha-mayim* — is from day one of creation. But the separation of the upper and lower waters, *v'yehi mavdil bein mayim la-mayim*, occurred on the second day. Ben Zoma was deriving the current distance between the supernal and lower waters from a verse that describes conditions before the *havdalah* of day two even took place. That is why his reasoning is off.

As for the actual distance between the upper and lower waters, the *amoraim* offer a range of opinions. Rav Acha bar Yaakov says it is *k'mlo nima* — a hair's breadth. The rabbis say it is like the planks of a bridge — the wooden planks laid right next to one another, with only the smallest gap between them. Mar Zutra, or according to some Rav Assi, says it is like two garments spread directly against one another. And some say it is like two cups placed rim to rim, one inverted onto the other. In each case, the separation is vanishingly small — far from Ben Zoma's three fingerbreadths, and in the case of the actual cosmological question, perhaps more significant still is that he was measuring from the wrong day of creation entirely.

The *gemara* closes this discussion with a flourish. The original story ended simply with *adayin Ben Zoma bachutz* — a powerful, stark conclusion. The subsequent analytical elaboration about the correct cosmological picture is a later *gemara* insertion, and while it adds technical precision, it does somewhat dilute the force of that extraordinary ending.

## Acher Heresy

### Metatron Sitting

The *gemara* now turns to Acher — Elisha ben Avuyah. The original tannaitic source said only that he *kitzetz b'netiyot* — cut down the young shoots — and cited the verse *al titten et picha lachati et besarecha*, do not let your mouth bring guilt upon yourself. What that sin was, the original source left unspecified. The Aramaic passage that follows is a later *gemara* elaboration, providing the specific content of his heresy. He saw *Metatron*, the great angel who serves in the innermost divine chamber, and Metatron had been granted permission *lemisav* — to sit — and to write down the merits of the Jewish people. This is worth pausing over: Metatron is not simply a messenger angel but a figure of extraordinary elevation, associated in this literature with what may be a kind of divine manifestation or at least a uniquely proximate divine presence.

### Two Powers

When Acher saw Metatron seated, he drew upon a tradition: there is no sitting in the divine presence, no competition, no back of the head — since angels have faces on all four sides — and no weariness. The tradition holds that none of these things exist in heaven. Yet here was Metatron, sitting. Acher drew the terrible conclusion: *shema chas v'shalom dvei reshuyos hen* — perhaps, God forbid, there are two competing divine powers. The sight of what appeared to be a second enthroned being led him into the foundational heresy: a dualist theology, a second authority alongside God.

### Merits Erased

The heavenly response was immediate. Metatron was expelled from that inner chamber and lashed with sixty tongues of flame. Rabbeinu Chananel and others are quick to clarify that Metatron did nothing wrong and is not being punished in any meaningful sense; the expulsion and lashing were designed precisely to demonstrate to Acher that Metatron held no independent authority — God alone was sovereign. A question was put to Metatron: why, when you saw Acher, did you not rise before him? Again, not because Metatron owed Acher deference, but because had he risen it might have forestalled the heretical conclusion. The incident underscores that no being in heaven exercises independent power.

Subsequently, permission was given to Metatron to erase the merits of Acher — a detail that carries its own poignancy given that we are close to Rosh Hashanah, when the imagery of writing and erasing merits is especially vivid.

### Except Acher

With the *gemara's* Metatron elaboration complete, the original tannaitic story resumes. A *bat kol* went forth and declared: *shuvu banim shovavim, chutz me'Acher* — return, wayward children — except for Acher. The heavenly proclamation of universal *teshuvah* explicitly excluded him.

### Hedonistic Fall

Acher's response to this, as elaborated by the *gemara*, was the logic of the man who has concluded he has nothing to lose: *ho'il ve'itred hahu gavra me'hahu alma* — since this person has been cast out from the world to come — *leipuk leih behai alma* — he might as well extract whatever benefit he can from this world. *Nafak Acher letarbut ra'ah* — Acher went out toward bad behavior. The word *tarbut* is itself interesting: in modern Hebrew it means culture, and in this context it likely implies an embrace of the Hellenistic or Roman hedonistic way of life.

### Prostitute Turnip

The *gemara* illustrates the depth of his fall with a scene. He found a prostitute and approached her. She recognized him: are you not Elisha ben Avuyah, the great sage? To demonstrate that he was no longer bound by anything, he reached into a planted bed — it was *Shabbos* — pulled out a turnip, and handed it to her. She said: that must be someone else. He was not who she thought. No, it was Acher: for whom the desecration of *Shabbos* had become a trivial, unremarkable act.

## Acher & Meir

### Opposites

The narrative now shifts to a series of exchanges between Acher and Rabbi Meir — exchanges that are moving precisely because they occur after Acher has *yatza letarbut ra'ah*, yet Rabbi Meir continues to engage him in Torah discourse. This mirrors structurally what the *gemara* did with Ben Zoma: presenting halakhic and aggadic discussions in the immediate aftermath of the account of his spiritual injury. After going off the *derech*, Acher asked Rabbi Meir: what is meant by the verse *gam et zeh lumat zeh asah ha'Elokim* — this opposite this God has made?

Rabbi Meir offered one interpretation: everything God created, He created with its opposite. He made mountains, He made valleys. He made seas, He made rivers.

### Akiva Version

Acher replied that Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Meir's own *rebbe*, did not explain the verse that way. The objection is pointed: mountains and rivers are not really opposites in any meaningful sense. Rabbi Akiva's version was more penetrating: *bara tzaddikim bara resha'im, bara Gan Eden bara Gehennom* — He created the righteous and the wicked; He created *Gan Eden* and *Gehennom*. The true opposites are spiritual and eschatological.

### Double Portions

This leads to an elaborate teaching. Every person, Rabbi Akiva maintained, has two portions: one in *Gan Eden* and one in *Gehennom*.

### Double Consequence

If a *tzaddik* merits it, he takes his own portion in *Gan Eden* plus the forfeited portions of the wicked — he inherits double in the world of reward. Conversely, the *rasha* who is found guilty takes his own portion in *Gehennom* plus the forfeited portions of the *tzaddikim* — he suffers double in the world of punishment. Rav Mesharshya supplies the prooftexts: regarding the righteous, the verse says *lachem b'artza mishneh yirashu* — in their land they shall inherit double; regarding the wicked, *umishneh shivaron shivram* — double of destruction is their downfall. One student raised a perceptive point: for this economy to balance perfectly, the world would need to contain an equal number of righteous and wicked, otherwise there would be unclaimed portions. The *zeh lumat zeh* symmetry itself implies this.

It is worth noting what is implicit in this teaching as addressed to Acher. On the one hand, the double punishment for the *rasha* is sobering. On the other hand, as another student observed, the teaching opens with the premise that every person — including, presumably, Acher — has a *chelek b'Gan Eden* waiting for him. That portion is always there. The question is only which portion one ultimately claims.

### Gold Glass Vessels

Acher sent another question to Rabbi Meir: what is meant by *lo ya'archenah zahav u'zechuchis, u'temurasah kli paz* — it shall not be compared to gold or glass, and its exchange is vessels of fine gold? Rabbi Meir answered: these refer to the words of Torah, which are as difficult to acquire as gold and *paz*, and as easy to lose as glassware. The contrast was vivid: precious metals must be smelted and recast; glass, once broken, is simply destroyed. Torah is precisely that fragile.

### Vessels Fixable

But again, Acher reports, that is not how Rabbi Akiva explained the verse. Rabbi Akiva's reading: *kli zahav u'kli zechuchis af al pi shenishberu yesh lahem takanah* — both gold vessels and glass vessels, even when broken, can be repaired. Gold can be smelted and recast; glass, famously, can be melted and reformed. So too *af talmid chacham af al pi shesarach yesh lo takanah* — even a *talmid chacham* who has gone rotten still has a possibility of repair.

The tension between the two teachings attributed to Rabbi Akiva — the double punishment for the *rasha* in one, and the *yesh lo takanah* in the other — is real. Held together, they may be read as a complementary message: the consequences of persistence in wickedness are severe and doubled, but return remains possible; the vessel is not beyond repair.

### Teshuvah Appeal

Rabbi Meir drew the practical conclusion directly: *af atah chozer becha* — you too, return. Do *teshuvah*. Acher's reply was devastating: *kvar shamati me'achorei hapargod, shuvu banim shovavim chutz me'Acher* — I have already heard from behind the divine curtain: "return, wayward children — except for Acher." He had internalized the *bat kol* as a final, irrevocable verdict.

One can read this in several ways. Theologically, it raises the question of whether the *bat kol* was a genuine divine pronouncement or a kind of test — a challenge to demonstrate precisely the quality of *teshuvah* that transcends even divine discouragement, that says "I will prove that wrong." But one can also read it in a deeply human register: Acher had simply given up on himself. He had convinced himself he was a lost cause, and no argument from Rabbi Meir could dislodge that conviction. The tragedy of the person who believes themselves beyond redemption is a recognizable one. The basic rabbinic theology is that *teshuvah* is available to anyone — and so the heavenly message, whether taken as literal or psychological, is either a test or a self-imposed prison. Perhaps both.

## Horseride Quest

### Shabbat Horseride

A later story, again in Aramaic and therefore a *gemara* elaboration rather than part of the original tannaitic text: Acher was riding on horseback on *Shabbos*, and Rabbi Meir was walking behind him, learning Torah from his mouth as he went. The image is almost surreal — the *rasha* on a horse, violating *Shabbos*, while his devoted student walks alongside trying to extract Torah from him.

### Boundary Warning

Acher interrupted the exchange: *Meir chazor l'achorecha* — Meir, turn back. He had measured by the horse's steps that they were approaching the *techum Shabbos* boundary, and he did not want Rabbi Meir to inadvertently violate it on his account. Rabbi Meir seized on the turn of phrase: *af atah chazor becha* — you too, turn back. Do *teshuvah*. Acher replied with what had become his standard answer: haven't I already told you — *kvar shamati me'achorei hapargod* — everybody can return except Acher.

### Thirteen Synagogues

Rabbi Meir would not accept this. He physically took hold of Acher — *takfei* — and dragged him into a *beis midrash*. There he employed the practice of asking a child to recite whatever verse he was currently learning, treating the child's answer as a kind of *siman*, a message from heaven — similar in spirit to the *goral ha-Gra*, the practice of opening a *Tanakh* at random and reading the verse one's finger falls on as a divine communication. He asked a child: *pesok li pesukach* — tell me your verse. The verse was *ein shalom amar Hashem l'resha'im* — there is no peace, says God, to the wicked. Not helpful.

He brought Acher to another *beis knesses* and asked another child. The verse: *ki im techabsi baneser ve'tarbi lach boris, nichtam avoneich lefanai* — even if you cleanse yourself with lye and multiply the soap, your sin is still stained before Me.

### Condemning Verses

He continued through *beis knesses* after *beis knesses*, and the pattern repeated. Every child, every verse, yielded a similar answer. The *gemara* records that Rabbi Meir brought Acher to thirteen *batei knesses* in all — *kulu pasku lei k'hai gavna* — and all of them produced verses of a similar condemning character. The last: *ve'l'rasha amar Elokim mah lecha l'saper chukai* — and to the wicked God says: what are you doing reciting My statutes? Not only is *teshuvah* apparently foreclosed; even the occupation of Torah study is being challenged.

### Violent Response

The final child in the final *beis knesses* had a speech impediment — *havah megamgem b'lishneih*, he had difficulty enunciating. When he recited the verse *ve'l'rasha amar Elokim*, his lisp made it sound like *ve'l'Elisha amar Elokim* — to Elisha, God says: what are you doing reciting My statutes? Acher heard his own name in the child's stumbling words, and the effect was shattering.

### Dismemberment

Two versions follow. According to one: Acher had a knife in his hand and he dismembered the child, sending the pieces to each of the thirteen synagogues — a horrifying echo of the *Pilegesh b'Givah*. According to the other: he said merely that had he a knife in his hand, he would have done so.

This detail — that Acher may have murdered and dismembered a child — is not the version of the story that typically circulates. The Acher narrative as popularly known concerns intellectual heresy and the tragedy of a great mind lost to apostasy. But the *gemara* here makes him a killer. This serves a purpose in the narrative economy: it becomes easier to account for how a figure of such obvious greatness could be so thoroughly cast out, and it makes the later ambivalence about his redemption both more dramatic and more comprehensible. The sin was not merely intellectual; it reached the depths of violence. Yet still, as we are about to see, the story does not end there.

## Heavenly Judgment

### Dilemma

*Ki nach nafshieh d'Acher* — when Acher died, a heavenly dilemma arose. On the one hand, *lo m'dainin lei daini* — he should not be judged and punished — *mishum d'asak b'Oraita* — because he engaged deeply in Torah and achieved tremendous levels of learning. On the other hand, *v'lo l'olma d'atei lo atei* — he should not receive the world to come — *mishum d'chata* — because of his sins. The heavens, as it were, could not decide what to do with him.

### Meir Plea

Rabbi Meir proposed a resolution: *mutav d'lidainei v'lyatei l'olma d'atei* — it would be better that he should be judged for his sins and then receive the world to come. Let him undergo the purifying fire of *Gehennom* and then ascend. Rabbi Meir expressed this as a personal aspiration: when will I die, so that I can rise and argue his case in heaven, and cause smoke to rise from his grave — the smoke signifying that he is being judged in *Gehennom*, which is the necessary precondition for eventual admission to *Olam Ha-Ba*.

### Grave Smoke

*Ki nach nafshieh d'Rabbi Meir* — when Rabbi Meir died, *salik kutra mikivrieh d'Acher* — smoke indeed rose from the grave of Acher. The sign appeared.

### Yochanan Object

Rabbi Yochanan, however, was troubled by this solution. *Gevurah l'mikli rabieh?* — Is this an achievement, to burn one's own *rebbe*? He did not consider it praiseworthy that Rabbi Meir had engineered a solution that involved causing his teacher to burn in *Gehennom*.

### Yochanan Promise

Rabbi Yochanan framed the deeper tragedy: *chad havei beinana v'lo matzinan l'atzulei* — one was among us and we could not save him when he was alive. This is the sorrowful irony. When Acher was alive, when intervention was still possible, the sages could not reach him. *Ee nakteih b'yad man marmi leih man* — if I had taken hold of his hand to bring him up, who would have taken him from me? Rabbi Yochanan believed he could have saved Acher in life, but no one managed it. So he declared: when will I die, and I will cause the smoke to cease from his grave.

### Guardian Overcome

*Ki nach nafshieh d'Rabbi Yochanan* — when Rabbi Yochanan died, *pasak kutra mikivrieh d'Acher* — the smoke ceased from the grave of Acher. The eulogizer at Rabbi Yochanan's funeral captured it: *afilu shomer hapetach lo amad l'fanecha rabbeinu* — even the guardian of the gate of *Gehennom* could not stand before you, our teacher. Rabbi Yochanan had descended into *Gehennom*, overcome its guardian, found Acher, taken hold of his hand, and brought him up.

### Heretic Redeemed

This is, by any measure, a startling conclusion. A man who had committed heresy of the most fundamental kind — who had embraced dualism, abandoned observance, desecrated *Shabbos*, and according to one tradition murdered a child — was in the end brought to *Gan Eden* by Rabbi Yochanan. The *gemara* does not resolve this; it presents it as the outcome. The ambivalence toward Acher that runs through the entire narrative — the continued loyalty of Rabbi Meir, the heavenly dilemma about his judgment, the competing efforts of Meir and Yochanan over his posthumous fate — finds its resolution not in condemnation but in rescue.

One might compare this to the ambivalence that arose a thousand years later in certain communities around Shabbetai Tzvi: figures whose spiritual gifts were undeniable even as their sin was catastrophic, and around whose fate after death similar debates circulated about who might rescue the soul from *Gehennom* and bring it to *Gan Eden*. The pattern of recognizing terrible sin while acknowledging spiritual greatness, and the refusal to simply close the book, runs deep. And it is worth noting, as one student observed, that for all his transgressions, Acher was protective of Rabbi Meir — warning him not to cross the *Shabbos* boundary, not wanting his path to contaminate his student. He became a destroyer in many senses, but not an active recruiter for his way of life.
