# Kiddushin 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Original Money vs. Servi…

### Yi'ud Timing

Daf 20 continues the discussion of *eved ivri*, picking up in the middle of 19b. The central question concerns which money constitutes the *kesef kiddushin* for *yi'ud*: is it the original purchase price, or is it whatever value of service remains at the time the master decides to perform *yi'ud*? One practical difference is whether *yi'ud* can be performed even when less than a *perutah*'s worth of service remains. The *gemara* addresses this through a series of *braitot* that examine the related question of when, precisely, the *kiddushin* process begins.

If the original money is the *kesef kiddushin* — even though that characterization is only confirmed retroactively when the master chooses to perform *yi'ud* — the question arises: what happens if another man attempts to betroth the girl during the interval between purchase and *yi'ud*? The *braita* rules that the second man's *kiddushin* would not take effect if the master ultimately proceeds with *yi'ud*. The transaction is treated as though the master had declared at the moment of purchase, *"at mekudeshet li me'achshav"* — you are betrothed to me from now — with completion deferred to the moment of *yi'ud*. Even though no such declaration was made, and even though the master had not yet made up his mind, the structure functions like a *me'achshav u-le'achar zman*, locking out any intervening *kiddushin*. This is a significant *chiddush* in understanding how the *kesef kiddushin* operates when it is identified with the original purchase money.

### Father's Marriage

A second *braita* addresses a related scenario. A man sells his daughter as an *amah ivriyah* and then, before the master has had the opportunity to perform *yi'ud*, the father goes and betroths her to another man — to Shimon. The *braita* states: *sichek ba-adon u-mekudeshet lashani* — he laughs in the face of the master, and she is betrothed to Shimon. This ruling, that Shimon's *kiddushin* does take effect, is attributed to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda.

### Kesef Kiddushin Dispute

The reasoning follows directly from Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda's position on the nature of the *kesef kiddushin*. In his view, the *kesef kiddushin* for *yi'ud* is not the original purchase money but rather the remainder of the service — the value of the labor still owed. Since no *kiddushin* process has yet commenced under this view, the father retains the ability to betroth his daughter to another man, and that betrothal is fully binding.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, rule that the master may still perform *yi'ud* — *im ratza leyayeid, meyayeid* — because they hold that the original money serves as *kesef kiddushin*, and retroactively, if *yi'ud* is performed, that original money renders the earlier *kiddushin* as though it had been made *me'achshav*. Shimon's *kiddushin* therefore does not take hold. If the master does not perform *yi'ud*, she would be betrothed to Shimon. The entire question is thus *tluim ve-omdim* — suspended and contingent on the master's eventual decision.

### Mashal Comparison

The *gemara* then introduces a *mashal*: the situation is comparable to a man who says to a woman, *harei at mekudeshet li le'achar shloshim yom* — you are betrothed to me after thirty days — without the addition of *me'achshav*. If another man betroths her during those thirty days, she is betrothed to the second man, because the first man's declaration contains no operative *me'achshav* to lock out intervening *kiddushin*.

The *gemara* asks: *mashal le-man* — whose position does this *mashal* illustrate? It cannot be the *rabbanan*, since they allow the master to still perform *yi'ud* and thereby override Shimon's *kiddushin*. Rav Acha the son of Rava therefore concludes that the *mashal* illustrates the position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda.

The *gemara* then clarifies the added value of this *mashal*. One might have thought: even according to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, since the master has the power to perform *yi'ud* at any moment he chooses — unlike the thirty-day case where a fixed interval makes it clear that nothing is operative — perhaps the ever-present possibility of *yi'ud* should be sufficient to lock out Shimon even without a *me'achshav*. The *mashal* comes to teach that we do not say this. According to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, since the *kesef kiddushin* is only the remainder of the service and no *kiddushin* has yet taken effect, the father may betroth her to Shimon and it is binding — notwithstanding the fact that *yi'ud* could have been performed at any moment.

The two positions thus produce symmetric but opposite results: according to the *chachamim*, using the original money as *kesef kiddushin* is sufficient to function as *me'achshav* even without an explicit declaration, blocking Shimon's *kiddushin*. According to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, even the open-ended and ever-present possibility of *yi'ud* does not block Shimon, because until *yi'ud* is actually performed, nothing has begun.

## Stipulation vs. Yi'ud

### No Yi'ud Stipulation

A third *braita* raises yet another configuration. A man sells his daughter as an *amah ivriyah* and stipulates with the master at the time of sale: *al menat shelo leyayeid* — on the condition that there will be no *yi'ud*. She is sold for service only; the master is not to marry her. The question is whether such a stipulation can validly exclude *yi'ud* from the institution of *amah ivriyah*.

### Meir vs. Rabbanan

Rabbi Meir rules that the stipulation is upheld — *niskayem ha-tnai*. The *rabbanan* disagree: if the master subsequently wishes to perform *yi'ud*, he may do so — *im ratza leyayeid, meyayeid* — because the stipulation has no force.

### Void Stipulation

The *rabbanan*'s reasoning is grounded in the principle of *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah*: one who stipulates against the way the Torah has defined an institution — *kol ha-matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah, tna'o batel* — the stipulation is void.

### Marriage Obligations

The *gemara* then raises a challenge to Rabbi Meir's position from a *braita* dealing with marriage. If a man betroths a woman on condition that he has no obligations of *she'er, kesut, ve'onah* — sustenance, clothing, and marital relations — the ruling is that the *kiddushin* takes effect but the stipulation is void: *harei zo mekudeshet ve-tna'o batel*. This too is the position of Rabbi Meir. If a man fundamentally intends to perform *kiddushin*, the *kiddushin* takes effect. But he cannot alter the Torah-defined parameters of the institution. It is a package; if you enter it, you accept the whole of it. How then can Rabbi Meir permit the stipulation against *yi'ud*?

### Yehuda's Position

Before resolving the tension regarding Rabbi Meir, the *gemara* notes the intermediate position of Rabbi Yehuda: *be-davar she-be-mamon, tna'o kayam* — a stipulation against a monetary obligation is valid. Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes between *onah*, which is a personal obligation and cannot be stipulated away, and the obligations of *she'er* and *kesut*, which are financial in nature. Since financial obligations are the sort of thing a person can waive — they are akin to a debt — the Torah leaves them subject to the parties' agreement.

This is analogous to the law of a *shomer*: the Torah sets default liability rules for a *shomer chinam* and a *shomer sachar*, but a person may stipulate to expand or contract those defaults, precisely because they are monetary. One way to understand this is that the Torah's rules for a *shomer* represent presumed contractual defaults — they reflect what most people intend absent explicit agreement — and explicit contrary terms simply override those defaults. Applied to marriage, Rabbi Yehuda reasons that while the personal obligation of *onah* cannot be contracted away, the financial obligations of maintenance and clothing can be.

Rabbi Meir takes the stricter view: even monetary stipulations against Torah-defined institutional obligations are void. The challenge, then, is how Rabbi Meir can simultaneously hold that a stipulation against *yi'ud* is valid in the *amah ivriyah* case. The answer lies in the position of Chizkiyah, addressed in the following section.

## Le'Amah Derasha

### Chizkiyah's Derasha

The *gemara* introduces Chizkiyah's teaching to resolve the apparent inconsistency in Rabbi Meir's position. The Torah uses the phrase *ki yimkor ish et bito le'amah* — when a man sells his daughter as a maidservant. The word *le'amah* is superfluous, and Chizkiyah derives from it: *pe'amim she'eino mochrah ela le'amah bilvad* — there are circumstances under which the sale is purely for service, without any accompanying right of *yi'ud*. The Torah thus itself contemplates a version of the institution in which *yi'ud* is absent. This is not, in Chizkiyah's reading, a stipulation that overrides the Torah; it is a distinct institutional form that the Torah itself authorizes.

This resolves the tension in Rabbi Meir's position. Rabbi Meir does not allow parties to modify an institution by stipulating against the Torah's definition. But Chizkiyah teaches that the Torah itself provides for two forms of the *amah ivriyah* arrangement: one that includes the potential for *yi'ud*, and one — *le'amah bilvad* — that does not. Rabbi Meir therefore holds that the stipulation works not because the parties have overridden the Torah, but because the Torah itself validates this alternative form.

### Pasuk Allocation

The *rabbanan* do not accept Chizkiyah's reading. They understand the institution as a single package — *amah ivriyah* always comes with the right of *yi'ud* — and therefore a stipulation against *yi'ud* is void. What, then, do the *rabbanan* do with the word *le'amah*? The *gemara* explains that they require it for a different teaching: *melamed she-mochrah li-pesulim* — it teaches that a father may sell his daughter to someone who is *pasul* for marriage, meaning someone whom the Torah prohibits her from marrying at the level of a *lav*, such as a *kohen* who would be marrying a *gerushah*, or a *mamzer*.

This is a significant ruling: even though the sale into *amah ivriyah* is predicated on the possibility of *yi'ud*, and *yi'ud* with a *pasul* would be forbidden, the Torah nonetheless permits the sale. The institution is not dissolved by the fact that *yi'ud* is practically barred. The prohibition gets in the way of exercising the right, but it does not change the institutional structure. This is precisely the distinction the *rabbanan* draw: you cannot stipulate to change the institution, but circumstances can prevent the exercise of a right without voiding the institution itself.

The *rabbanan* understand the two cases as follows. When a father sells his daughter to a *pasul* with no stipulation, the institution is intact; *yi'ud* simply cannot be exercised. When a father stipulates against *yi'ud* with someone who is not a *pasul*, he is attempting to alter the institution — and that the *rabbanan* reject. For Rabbi Meir, the calculus runs in the opposite direction, as will become clear.

### Eliezer's Reading

Rabbi Eliezer offers a different allocation of the *pesukim*. He argues that there is no need for the word *le'amah* to teach the permissibility of selling to a *pasul*, because that can already be derived from another verse: *im ra'ah be'einei adoneha asher lo ye'adah* — if it is evil in the eyes of the master such that he does not perform *yi'ud*. Rabbi Eliezer reads *im ra'ah* as referring to a halachically problematic marriage — *she-ra'ah be-nisu'eha*, that there is something wrong with the marriage. The verse is thus already describing the case of a *pasul*, where the master cannot perform *yi'ud* because the marriage would be forbidden, and yet the sale has taken effect. From this verse alone one learns that a sale to a *pasul* is valid. The word *le'amah* is therefore available for a larger *chiddush*: *melamed she-mochrah li-krovim* — even a sale to a relative, where *yi'ud* would not merely be forbidden but would be halachically ineffective (*kiddushin* not being *tofes* at all among *arayot*), the sale is nonetheless valid.

### Kal VaChomer

The *gemara* asks: why is a *pasuk* needed to permit the sale to *pesulim*? A *kal va-chomer* would seem to suffice: if *kiddushin* itself takes effect *be-di'avad* even when it involves an *issur lav* — as in the case of a *kohen* who marries a *gerushah* — then certainly a sale, which does not yet constitute *kiddushin*, should be valid. The *gemara* responds that the *kal va-chomer* fails because it is not symmetric. The father's power in the context of marriage is broader than his power in the context of sale: a father can betroth his daughter even when she is a *na'arah*, whereas he can only sell her when she is a *ketanah*. Since his power in selling is more limited, one might have thought he cannot sell her to a *pasul*. The verse *le'amah* therefore explicitly teaches that he can.

### Institution vs. Fact

The final point in this section concerns the question of *krovim* and the mirror-image positions of Rabbi Meir and the *rabbanan*. The *rabbanan* hold that *krovim* — relatives for whom *kiddushin* would be entirely ineffective — are excluded, and a sale to them is not valid. But a sale to a *pasul* — where *kiddushin* would be *tofes be-di'avad* — is permitted, because the institution remains intact; only the exercise of *yi'ud* is blocked by circumstance. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, permits even a sale to *krovim*, reading *im ra'ah be'einei adoneha* to cover that case as well.

For Rabbi Meir, the picture is striking and, as the *gemara* acknowledges, quite strange. Rabbi Meir uses *le'amah* to teach that a father may stipulate against *yi'ud*, producing an institutional form of *amah ivriyah* that is purely for service. He uses *im ra'ah be'einei adoneha* to teach the permissibility of selling to a *pasul*, mirroring where Rabbi Eliezer learns *krovim*. But Rabbi Meir holds like the *rabbanan* that a sale to *krovim* is invalid.

The asymmetry is genuinely puzzling. Rabbi Meir permits the parties to define the institution without *yi'ud* through stipulation, yet holds that the institution cannot validly exist when *yi'ud* is simply impossible due to the *karov* relationship. The *rabbanan* are internally consistent in the opposite direction: they prohibit the stipulation because the institution is indivisible, yet permit the sale to *krovim* because the institution is intact and only the circumstances prevent *yi'ud*. Rabbi Meir's position — that deliberate exclusion works but factual impossibility does not — is, as the *gemara* itself notes, quite difficult to understand intuitively.

## Father vs. Son

### Father/Son Rule

The *gemara* now addresses a specific *braita* that states: one may sell his daughter to her grandfather (the father of the seller) but may not sell her to her brother (the son of the seller). A second *braita* states that one may not sell to either. The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: the second *braita*, which prohibits both, follows the *rabbanan* who hold that *krovim* are excluded categorically. The first *braita* — which permits the sale to the grandfather but not to the brother — requires an explanation of whose view it represents.

### Yi'ud Possibility

The *gemara* concludes: *le'olam ke-Rabbanan* — the first *braita* also follows the *rabbanan*. But the *rabbanan* concede in the case of the grandfather — *modu Rabbanan heicha de-ika tzad yi'ud* — because there, while the master himself (the grandfather) cannot perform *yi'ud* with his own granddaughter, his son can. If the seller has a brother — that is, another son of the grandfather who is not a *karov* to the girl — *yi'ud* remains structurally possible. The institution is therefore intact, and the sale is valid.

This reasoning prompts further questions. What if the grandfather has no other son, or no son old enough to perform *yi'ud* within the six-year term? Does the structural availability of *yi'ud* in the abstract suffice, or must *yi'ud* be practically possible in the specific circumstances? The *gemara* does not resolve this, and it remains an open question.

## Yovel Context

### Rambam Yovel

At this point, a fundamental contextual question arises: when did the laws of *eved ivri* actually apply? The Rambam (*Hilchot Shemitah ve-Yovel*, chapter 10, *halacha* 9) rules explicitly: *be-zman she-ha-yovel noheig, noheig din eved ivri* — the institution of *eved ivri* is operative only when the *yovel* is operative. When the *yovel* does not function, none of these laws apply. The *yovel* ceased to be operative already before the end of the First Temple period, from the time of the Assyrian exile under Sancheriv, when the northern tribes were displaced and the requirement of having all the tribes settled in the land — a precondition for *yovel* — was no longer met. Accordingly, by the latter part of the First Temple era, the laws of *eved ivri* were no longer practically applicable. The extended discussion of the *gemara* is thus theoretical in its halachic application, though not diminished in its moral and conceptual weight.

## Treatment Rights

### Begapo Yavo

The *gemara* now turns to a new *braita* examining the personal rights of the *eved ivri*. The verse states: *im be-gapo yavo, be-gapo yeitzei* — if he comes in by his body alone, he leaves by his body alone. The *gemara* will explore what this means in practice.

### Eliezer ben Yaakov

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov reformulates the principle as: *yechidi nichnas, yechidi yeitzei* — he enters alone, he exits alone. The *gemara* asks what distinguishes this formulation from the first.

### Rava's Reading

Rava explains *be-gapo yavo, be-gapo yeitzei*: if he enters whole in body, he leaves whole in body. The verse teaches *she-eino yeitzei be-roshei evarim* — that he does not go free on account of loss of limb, as a *eved kena'ani* would. If a master knocks out the eye or tooth of a *eved kena'ani*, the slave goes free. The *eved ivri* does not obtain freedom on this basis. This might appear to disadvantage him, but in fact it works in his favor: the master must pay the full range of injury compensation applicable to a free person — *tza'ar, sheves, ripuy, boshet* — and that payment may well be sufficient for the *eved ivri* to purchase his own freedom outright, leaving him with money to spare. The verse clarifies that bodily injury is compensated but does not of itself terminate the servitude.

### Abaye's Question

Abaye challenges Rava: is this teaching not already derived from the verse *lo yeitzei ke-tzeit avadim* — he shall not go free as slaves go free — which was studied earlier? Why does this verse need to add anything?

### Injury Compensation

The *gemara* answers: if we had only the earlier verse, one might have thought that the rule works differently for the *eved ivri* — that he both goes free and receives compensation for the injury. The verse *be-gapo yavo be-gapo yeitzei* therefore teaches definitively that bodily injury does not serve as a basis for freedom at all. He is compensated, but he does not go free on that account.

## Wife & Children

### Shifcha Provision

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — *yechidi nichnas, yechidi yeitzei*, he enters alone and exits alone. If the *eved ivri* enters with a wife and children, the master provides him with a *shifcha kena'anis* to produce slave children from the union. If he enters without a wife and children, the master does not provide a *shifcha kena'anis*. The provision of a *shifcha* is thus conditioned on the *eved* already being a married man when he enters service.

Viewed from the perspective of the wife, this arrangement is deeply uncomfortable: a husband who has sold himself into servitude due to poverty is now, as part of that servitude, given another woman. The *gemara* does not address this perspective directly. There is also the implicit suggestion that this arrangement carries within it a quality of *chotei niskar* — the wrongdoer benefiting from his transgression — in that the very poverty that forced the man into servitude now enables him a form of license he did not previously have. This too the *gemara* leaves unresolved.

## Redemption Price

### Value Increased

A new *braita* takes up the question of the redemption price when an *eved ivri* seeks to buy back his freedom before the end of his term. *Nimkar be-maneh ve-hishbi'ach ve-amad ma'asayim* — he sold himself for a hundred and his value rose to two hundred. How do we calculate the price of redemption? *Minayin she-mechashvinan lo ela maneh* — how do we know we use the original purchase price? The verse states *mi-kesef miknato* — from the money of his purchase. The redemption is calculated against the original price, prorated for the remaining years.

### Value Decreased

The converse case: *nimkar be-ma'asayim ve-hichsif ve-amad maneh* — he was sold for two hundred but his value declined to a hundred. *Minayin she-ein mechashvinan lo ela maneh* — how do we know we use the current, lower price? The verse states *ke-fi shanav yashiv et ge'ulato* — according to his years he shall return his redemption. This verse directs us to calculate based on what the remaining years are currently worth.

### Lower Price

The principle that emerges is that we always apply whichever of the two verses yields the lower redemption price — *lekula*. If the original purchase price is lower (because his value rose), we use the original. If the current market value is lower (because his value fell), we use the current. The question of why we consistently apply *lekula* rather than *lechumra* — always taking the higher price — is addressed in the following section.

---

## Lekula Principle

### Abaye's Boast

The question of why we read the *pesukim* toward leniency is prompted by a famous exchange. Abaye, in a moment of scholarly confidence, declared: *hareini ke-ven Azzai be-shukei Tiveria* — I am like Ben Azzai in the marketplaces of Tiberias. Ben Azzai was renowned as a towering figure of learning, and Abaye was effectively issuing an open challenge: ask me anything. One of the *rabbanan* immediately responded with the question: the two *pesukim* can be read either *lekula* — always using the lower price — or *lechumra* — always using the higher. What determines that we read them *lekula*?

### Torah's Concern

The *gemara* answers: *mide de-hava Rachmana legabei* — we know that the Torah expresses concern for the welfare of the *eved ivri*. Given this orientation, we assume the Torah intends the reading that is more favorable to him.

### Ki Tov Lo Imach

The source for the Torah's concern is a *braita* that many will know from earlier learning: *ki tov lo imach* — it should be good for him with you. The verse mandates that the *eved ivri* be treated as an equal. *Imach be-ma'achal* — equal in food; *imach be-mishteh* — equal in drink. The master may not eat fine white bread while the slave eats bran bread; may not drink aged wine while the slave drinks new wine; may not sleep on soft cushions while the slave sleeps on straw. The accommodations must be equivalent. From this the *gemara* derives: *kol ha-koneh eved ivri ke-koneh adon le-atzmo* — whoever purchases a *eved ivri* is as if he has purchased a master for himself.

### Buying Master

This teaching carries within it a subtle note of censure directed at the buyer: be aware of what you are taking on. The person you are purchasing is not someone you may treat with contempt or deprive of dignity. He must have what you have.

### Tosafot Pillow

*Tosafot* raises a challenge: the formulation says *adon* — master, not peer. How can he be merely equivalent? *Tosafot* resolves this with a passage from the Yerushalmi. Suppose the master has only one pillow. If he sleeps on it himself, he is not fulfilling *ki tov lo imach* — he is treating himself better than the *eved*. But if he neither sleeps on it himself nor gives it to the *eved*, that is *midat Sedom* — the attribute of Sodom: causing harm to another without any benefit to oneself. Since he is not using the pillow, there is no justification for depriving the *eved* of it. Therefore, he must give it to the *eved*. The result is that the *eved* ends up with better treatment — and that is why the *gemara* calls it *ke-koneh adon le-atzmo*: in the final reckoning, the *eved* may actually come out ahead.

## Treatment vs. Price

### Strict Redemption

The *gemara* entertains a counterargument. Perhaps the Torah's concern for the *eved ivri*'s welfare, expressed through *ki tov lo imach*, applies only to his treatment while in servitude — *ki heichi de-lo litzta'er lei*, so as not to cause him suffering. But when it comes to the redemption price, perhaps we should be strict — *nachmir alav* — and require him to pay the higher amount.

### Sin Leads to Slavery

What would motivate applying stringency at the redemption stage? The *gemara* invokes a teaching of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina: *bo u-re'eh kama kasha avka shel shevi'it* — come and see how grave is even the dust of *shevi'it* transgression. A person who engages in commerce with *shevi'it* produce — not even the primary prohibition, merely a lighter *issur aseh* — sets himself on a path of decline. Because he pursued profit by disregarding *halacha*, the consequence unfolds measure for measure.

### Downward Spiral

The teaching traces the descent through the *parshiyot* of *Behar*: he sells his movable property, as the verse states *bi-shnat ha-yovel tashuvu ish la-achuzato*, juxtaposed to *ve-chi timkeru mimkar la-amitecha*. Then he sells his fields — *ki yamuch achicha u-machar me-achuzato*. Then his home — *ve-chi yimkor beit moshav ir choma*. The *gemara* then introduces, from *Mishpatim*, the sale of his daughter — *ve-chi yimkor ish et bito le-amah* — even though that *parshah* is not found in *Behar*. The *gemara* explains that a person in this position would sooner sell his daughter than borrow with interest, because the two remaining options are selling his daughter or taking an interest-bearing loan. Finally, having sold everything, he sells himself — *ve-chi yamuch achicha ve-nimkar lach* — and if he still has not repented, he may wind up sold to a non-Jew, and ultimately to the service of a pagan temple, *nimkar la-avodat kochavim atzmah*.

### Awareness Stages

The *gemara* notes a shift in language between the stages. After the sale of movable property, it says *lo hirgish* — he was not sensitive, he did not perceive that his poverty resulted from his *aveirah*. After the subsequent stages, the language shifts to *lo ba l'yado* — the awareness did not come to him. Why the difference?

### Repeated Sin

The *gemara* cites Rav Huna: *kivan she-avar adam aveirah ve-shanah bah, hutrah lo* — once a person has committed a transgression and repeated it, it becomes permitted to him. The *gemara* immediately clarifies: not literally permitted, but *na'asit lo ke-heter* — it becomes psychologically as if permitted. The first time, there was a reasonable expectation that the person would have been *murgash* — sensitive, aware — and would have recognized the causal connection between his transgression and his punishment. He was not. By the time he has descended further, the expectation of awareness is far lower; it is no longer surprising that awareness did not come to him. The phrasing of *lo ba l'yado* reflects this diminished expectation — of course it did not come to him; he is already so far gone.

## Daughter vs. Interest

### Economic Logic

The *gemara* explains why a man in this spiral would choose to sell his daughter before taking a loan with *ribbit*. When a daughter is sold, the debt she represents decreases over time — *barta migre'ah ve-nafkah* — as she works off her term of service, her redemption price diminishes. The trajectory is toward resolution. A loan with interest, by contrast, only compounds — *ha mosifa ve-azla* — the debt grows continuously. The practical calculation therefore favors selling the daughter over borrowing with interest, even though morally neither reflects well on the person's situation.

This is not a moral endorsement. Rather, it is an economic observation embedded within a trajectory of failure and sin. The inclusion of the daughter's sale in this sequence — drawn from *Mishpatim* into the context of *Behar* — itself conveys the gravity of the situation. That a man should be reduced to selling his daughter is presented not neutrally but as a marker of how far one can fall through the progressive neglect of *mitzvot*.

## Redemption Mandate

### Stone After Fall

Returning to the question of whether we should be strict on the redemption price because of the *chet* that brought the *eved* to this position: perhaps, since this person has descended all the way to the service of a pagan temple, we should simply seal his fate — *eima lidchi even achar ha-nofel*, let us throw a stone after the one who has already fallen. This idiom evokes the image of *sekilah* — pushing someone off a cliff and then throwing a stone on top of them — and is the Talmud's way of articulating the impulse to give up on someone who has fallen so far.

### Acharei Nimkar

The *gemara* rejects this: *talmud lomar, acharei nimkar ge'ulah tihyeh lo, echad me-echav yig'alenu* — after he is sold, redemption shall be his; one of his kinsmen shall redeem him. Even at this lowest point, the Torah mandates our responsibility to redeem him. The obligation does not expire with his degradation.

### Mandate Conclusion

Given this strong redemptive mandate, the *gemara* concludes, it follows that the Torah intends to make redemption as accessible as possible — and therefore the lower of the two prices applies. A Torah that commands us to redeem him must intend that the price of redemption not be set at the maximum. The principle of *lekula* is thus not merely a formal reading of competing *pesukim*; it flows from the Torah's fundamental orientation toward enabling the *eved ivri*'s return to freedom.

## Two Psukim

### Value Increased

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a direct derivation from the *pesukim* themselves: *im od rabot ba-shanim, le-fihem yashiv ge'ulato mi-kesef miknato* — if there are many years, he shall return his redemption according to them from the money of his purchase. The word *rabot* — many or increased — refers not to the number of years remaining but to the increase in his value *over the years*. If his value has increased during the years of his service — *nitraba kaspo ba-shanim* — we use *mi-kesef miknato*, the original purchase price, which is the lower amount.

### Value Decreased

Conversely, *ve-im me'at nish'ar ba-shanim* — if his value has decreased over the years — *ke-fi shanav yashiv et ge'ulato*, the redemption follows his current value, which is the lower amount. In both cases, the operative *pasuk* directs us to the figure that is more favorable to the *eved*. The two *pesukim* thus work in tandem: one covers the case of rising value, the other covers falling value, and in each case the result is the lower price.

### Pshat Rejected

The *gemara* considers an alternative reading: perhaps the *pesukim* simply refer to how many years remain in the six-year term. If many years remain, use the purchase price; if few years remain, use the current price. The *gemara* rejects this as the correct reading. If the Torah meant only *shanim* — years — it would have written simply *shanim*. The use of the phrase *ba-shanim* — in the years, over the course of the years — indicates a change in value *over time*, not merely the quantity of remaining years. *Nitraba kaspa ba-shanim* — his value increased over the years — or *nitmeit kaspa ba-shanim* — his value decreased over the years — is what is being indicated by the distinctive phrasing.

### K'Sinai Derasha

Rav Yosef closes the discussion with a remarkable accolade: *darshinhu Rav Nachman le-hani kra'ei ke-Sinai* — Rav Nachman interpreted these *pesukim* as if the interpretation came directly from Sinai. It is a fitting conclusion to a discussion that began with Abaye's boast of being like Ben Azzai in the marketplaces of Tiberias. In the event, it is Rav Nachman — not Abaye — who produces the decisive reading of the *pesukim*, and not a word of Abaye's brilliant answer appears in the *gemara*'s resolution.

## Moral Synthesis

### Moral Tensions

The *gemara* on this *daf* presents the institution of *eved ivri* within a carefully constructed moral frame that holds two orientations in tension simultaneously. On one hand, there is censure. The teaching of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina traces a downward spiral from the violation of *shemitah* law — not even the primary prohibition, but its secondary dust — through the progressive loss of property, dignity, and autonomy. Each stage is marked by the failure to perceive, or later even to be capable of perceiving, that sin is the cause of the descent. Selling one's daughter appears in this sequence as one of the lowest points before the final degradation of selling oneself, and the moral weight of that inclusion is unmistakable.

On the other hand, the Torah's response to this situation is not abandonment or additional punishment. The mandate *acharei nimkar ge'ulah tihyeh lo* — after he is sold, redemption shall be his — asserts that even at the bottom of the pit, the obligation of kinship and community toward the fallen person does not expire. And the principle of *ki tov lo imach* — that the *eved ivri* must be treated as an equal, fed the same food, given the same drink, afforded the same shelter, and in the edge case described by *Tosafot*, actually given priority — transforms the institution from one of domination into something closer to its opposite. *Kol ha-koneh eved ivri ke-koneh adon le-atzmo*: the buyer takes on a master, not a servant.

These two strands — the moral censure embedded in the path that leads to servitude, and the radical demands of dignity and ease of redemption placed upon the master — do not resolve into a single clean principle. The *gemara* holds them together. The *eved ivri* has, in the fullest sense, brought this upon himself through repeated, unrepentant transgression. And yet the Torah's response is not to make his bondage harsher or his redemption harder, but to surround him with obligations of equal treatment and to mandate his redemption at the lowest possible price. The institution of *eved ivri* thus encodes within its very laws a kind of redemptive compassion — a social and halachic structure that acknowledges moral failure while refusing to use that failure as license for cruelty or indifference.
