# Shevuot 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel's Collateral

### Shmuel's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַרֵישָׁא. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב: אַרֵישָׁא. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** In response, **Shmuel says:** This statement relates **to** the case in **the first clause** of the mishna, where the debtor is the defendant. **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav similarly says** that it relates **to the first clause. And Rabbi Yoḥanan similarly says** that it relates **to the first clause.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רֵישָׁא? סֵיפָא דְּרֵישָׁא: ״סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו וְשֶׁקֶל הָיָה שָׁוֶה״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו וּשְׁלֹשָׁה דִּינָרִין הָיָה שָׁוֶה״ – חַיָּיב. דִּשְׁבוּעָה גַּבֵּי לֹוֶה הִיא, וְשַׁקְלוּהָ רַבָּנַן מִלֹּוֶה וְשַׁדְיוּהָ אַמַּלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** did the *amora'im* mean by: **The first clause?** The Gemara answers: They were not referring to the very first *halakha* in the mishna, but rather to **the latter part of the first clause:** There is a case of a creditor who claims: **I lent you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and it was worth a shekel. And the other** individual, the debtor, **says: That is not** the case; **rather, you lent me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth three dinars,** i.e., three-quarters of a *sela*. In this case, he is **liable** to take an oath. **As** in this case, **the oath** should in principle be taken **by the debtor,** since he is the one who admitted to a part of the creditor's claim, **but the Sages removed** the obligation to take an oath **from the debtor and imposed it on the creditor,** deeming him liable to take an oath that the collateral was not worth more than a shekel.

The daf opens, roughly fifteen lines into 43b, at the words *amar Shmuel*. The background is the *mishnah* already studied, which discussed a creditor who held a borrower's collateral (*mashkon*) and then lost it. The *mishnah*'s implicit assumption was straightforward: the creditor must deduct the value of the lost *mashkon* from the outstanding debt, and he owes the borrower compensation equal to that value. Against this backdrop, Shmuel introduces a strikingly different conceptual framework for thinking about *mashkon* altogether.

### Broom Handle Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דְּאוֹזְפֵיהּ אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי לְחַבְרֵיהּ, וּמַשְׁכֵּן לֵיהּ קַתָּא דְּמַגָּלָא; אֲבַד קַתָּא דְּמַגָּלָא – אֲבַד אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי. אֲבָל תַּרְתֵּי קַתָּאתֵי – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel says:** With regard to **one who lent one thousand dinars to another and took from him the handle of a sickle as collateral,** if **the handle of the sickle is lost,** the creditor has **lost the** entire sum of **one thousand dinars,** even though the lost collateral was worth less. **But** if he took **two handles** as collateral and only one of them was lost, the creditor does **not** lose the entire debt; he loses only the value of the handle that he lost.

Shmuel states: *hai man de'ofei alfa zuza lechavrei umashken lei kata de'magla* — a person who lends a thousand *zuz* to his friend and receives in return a scythe handle as collateral — *avad kata de'magla, avad alfa zuzei*: if he loses that scythe handle, he has lost his thousand dollars. The *mashkon*, in Shmuel's view, is not held because of its intrinsic monetary value; it is not there so the creditor can recoup the debt by selling it. Rather, the implicit understanding of the arrangement is an exchange: you return the handle, I return your money; I return your money, you return the handle. The flip side of that exchange logic is that if the creditor cannot return the handle, the borrower need not repay the debt.

### Rashi's Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תַּרְתֵּי קַתָּאתֵי: אֲבַד חֲדָא – אֲבַד חֲמֵשׁ מְאָה, אֲבַד אִידַּךְ – אֲבַד כּוּלֵּיהּ. אֲבָל קַתָּא וּנְסָכָא – לָא. נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ קַתָּא וּנְסָכָא: אֲבַד נְסָכָא – אֲבַד פַּלְגָא, אֲבַד קַתָּא – אֲבַד כּוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman says: Even** if he took **two handles** and only **one** of them was **lost,** he has **lost five hundred** dinars, i.e., half the debt. If **the other** one was then also **lost,** he has **lost the entire** debt. **But** if he took **a handle and a piece of silver** as collateral and then lost the handle, he has **not** lost half the debt, as he presumably relied on the silver, not the handle, for payment. The Sages **of Neharde'a say: Even** if he took **a handle and a piece of silver,** and **the piece of silver was lost,** he has **lost half** the debt. If **the handle** was then **lost,** he has **lost the entire** debt.

Rashi explains that the *mashkon* functions as a one-to-one exchange object. The creditor holds onto it with the understanding that returning the object and receiving repayment are two sides of the same act. This is, admittedly, a puzzling arrangement from the creditor's perspective — why would any *malveh* agree to terms under which a worthless broom handle represents his entire debt? The answer is that one does not have to agree to this arrangement. The question, to be examined shortly, is whether this is the default or whether it requires explicit stipulation.

Rashi continues: *aval tartei katati* — if there were two scythe handles — *lo*: Shmuel's principle would not apply in the same way. Rashi's explanation is that the exchange logic operates as a one-to-one correspondence. If one handle is lost, perhaps the exchange relationship still inheres in the remaining handle; the creditor pays for the lost one, and the surviving handle continues to serve as the symbolic exchange object.

### One vs. Two Objects

The question naturally arises whether losing one of two handles means the debtor is discharged from half the debt. Rashi's reasoning, at least as applied to two identical objects, is that the exchange relationship might be preserved partially by the surviving object. One might have thought that the logic of Shmuel simply does not apply when the *mashkon* consists of multiple objects — that the whole premise of the exchange breaks down, and we revert to strict valuation. But that is not where the Gemara takes us.

### Multiple Collaterals

Rav Nachman goes further: *afilu tartei katati* — even with two handles — *avad chada avad chamesh me'ot, avad idach avad kulei*: lose one handle and you lose 500; lose the other and you lose the remaining thousand. With two identical objects, the debt is divided equally between them. *Aval kata venaska lo* — but if the collateral consists of a handle and a silver ingot, then this equal division would not apply. Rashi explains that the silver ingot clearly exists not as a symbolic exchange object but as something of genuine value that the creditor intends to collect from if the debt goes unpaid. The silver ingot is therefore outside the Shmuel framework entirely; if it is lost, the creditor simply reimburses the borrower for its actual value. The broom handle, however, may still function as the exchange object.

The *Nehardaei* go even further still, ruling that even a handle and a silver ingot — *afilu katva venaskla* — follow equal division: lose the ingot and you lose 500, lose the handle and you lose 500, regardless of the disparity in their actual values.

Tosafot, however, reads the case differently. Because of what follows in the Gemara, Tosafot maintains that Shmuel's framework applies even to objects of real value, and that *kata venaska lo* means not that the silver ingot is excluded from the framework, but that when the two objects are of unequal value, the debt is divided between them *proportionally* rather than equally. If the silver ingot represents ninety percent of the combined value of the collateral and the handle represents ten percent, then losing the ingot forfeits $900 of the debt and losing the handle forfeits $100.

### Shmuel vs. Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו וְשֶׁקֶל הָיָה שָׁוֶה״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו וּשְׁלֹשָׁה דִּינָרִין הָיָה שָׁוֶה״ – חַיָּיב. לֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״הָא קַבֵּילְתֵּיהּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges Shmuel's opinion based on the mishna. **We learned** in the mishna: There is a case of a creditor who claims: **I lent you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and it was worth a shekel. And the other** individual, the debtor, **says: That is not** the case; **rather, you lent me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth three dinars,** i.e., three-quarters of a *sela*. In this case, the debtor is **liable** to take an oath. According to Shmuel's opinion that if the collateral is lost, the debt is canceled, **let** the debtor **say to him: You have** already **received** repayment of the debt by means of the collateral.

The Gemara now confronts the obvious difficulty: the *mishnah* assumes that when a creditor loses a *mashkon*, he deducts its value from the debt — not that the entire debt is extinguished. The *mishnah*'s case explicitly contemplates a dispute over the value of the *mashkon* and the resulting *shevuat modeh bemiktzat*. Under Shmuel's framework, the moment the *mashkon* is lost, the entire debt disappears, rendering the *mishnah*'s detailed analysis of partial admissions and oaths incomprehensible. If the *mashkon* — regardless of its value — represents the full debt, then Gloria, upon learning her watch was lost, owes nothing, takes no oath, and the entire litigation framework of the *mishnah* collapses.

The Gemara resolves this with a distinction: *masnisin b'defarish, Shmuel b'delo parish* — the *mishnah* speaks of a case where the terms were made explicit, while Shmuel speaks of a case where they were not. The question is which direction this distinction runs.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין בִּדְפָרֵישׁ, שְׁמוּאֵל בִּדְלָא פָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* in **the mishna is with regard to** a case **where** the creditor **stated explicitly** that he is taking the collateral only to assure payment of the value of the item, and not as full repayment. Therefore, since there is a dispute with regard to the collateral's monetary value, the two parties must litigate this matter. **Shmuel,** by contrast, was referring **to** a case **where** the creditor **did not state explicitly** whether he was taking the collateral to cover only its monetary value or the entire debt. In that case, it is presumed that he took it to cover the entire debt.

According to Rashi, the default — when nothing is said — is Shmuel's arrangement: the *mashkon* embodies the full debt, and if lost, the entire debt is forfeited. The *mishnah* represents the case where the parties explicitly stipulated otherwise, excluding the Shmuel model. According to Tosafot, the default is the *mishnah*'s model — the *mashkon* is worth only its actual value — and Shmuel's framework applies only when explicitly stipulated. Tosafot's reading is the more intuitive one: it is far more natural to treat collateral as being worth what it is worth, with the Shmuel arrangement as an exceptional explicit deal.

### Asmachta Analysis

Tosafot takes the analysis further. Even when the Shmuel arrangement is explicitly stipulated, Tosafot does not view it as a legal *concept* of what a *mashkon* is — that is, not as an inherent legal definition according to which the *mashkon* fully embodies the debt. Rather, it is simply a side deal: the parties agree that if the creditor loses the collateral, the debt is forfeited. This is the structure of an *asmachta* — a conditional penalty clause — similar to a case where one stipulates that failure to plow a field results in a ten-thousand-dollar penalty. Such deals are enforceable when properly made, but they represent agreed-upon conditions, not conceptual redefinitions of what collateral is. Rashi, by contrast, for whom the Shmuel model is the default when nothing is said, appears to view the *mashkon*-as-full-debt-embodiment as a genuine conceptual category, not merely a side deal.

## R. Eliezer vs. R. Akiva

### Tannaitic Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן וְאָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן – יִשָּׁבַע וְיִטּוֹל אֶת מְעוֹתָיו. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר, יָכוֹל הוּא שֶׁיֹּאמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם הִלְוִיתַנִי – אֶלָּא עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן; אָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, אָבְדוּ מְעוֹתֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that Shmuel's ruling is **the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is stated in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who lends** money **to another on** the basis of **collateral, and the collateral was lost, he must take an oath** that it was in fact lost **and** may then **take his money;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Akiva says** that the debtor **can say to** the creditor: **Didn't you lend me** the money **only on** the basis of **the collateral?** Since **the collateral was lost, your money is lost** as well.

The Gemara now asks whether Shmuel's position reflects a broader *tannaitic* dispute. The relevant *baraita* concerns a case where a creditor lent money against a *mashkon* and the *mashkon* was subsequently lost: *hamalveh et chavero al hamashkon ve'avad hamashkon*.

### R. Eliezer Chinam

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *yishava v'yitol me'otav* — the creditor takes an oath that he was not negligent and collects his entire debt. This is a position not even contemplated in the *mishnah*. Rabbi Eliezer treats the creditor as a *shomer chinam* — an unpaid guardian — who bears no liability for loss that occurs without negligence. The creditor loses the *mashkon*, takes an exculpatory oath, and is entitled to collect the full debt as if nothing had happened to the collateral.

### R. Akiva Full Debt

Rabbi Akiva rules the polar opposite: *yachol she'yomar lo klum hilvitani ela al hamashkon* — the borrower may say, "You only lent me money in order to hold my collateral. *Avad hamashkon avdu me'otecha* — you lost the *mashkon*, you lost your money." This is recognizably aligned with Shmuel's position. The *mashkon* is the full embodiment of the debt; its loss extinguishes the debt.

### With Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַמַּלְוֶה אֶלֶף זוּז בִּשְׁטָר, וְהִנִּיחַ מַשְׁכּוֹן בְּיָדוֹ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל: אָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן אָבְדוּ מְעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **one who lends** another person **one thousand dinars with** a promissory **note, and** in addition to the note, the debtor **left collateral in** the creditor's **possession, all agree** that since **the collateral was lost, his money is lost** as well. Since he had a promissory note as proof of the loan, the collateral was clearly taken as potential repayment.

An additional clause appears: if the loan was accompanied by a written *shtar* (*b'shtar*) and the creditor held collateral, then *divrei hakol avad hamashkon avdu ma'osav* — all agree that losing the *mashkon* means losing the money. This is surprising: one might have thought that having a *shtar* — which concretizes and formalizes the debt — would make it *harder*, not easier, to extinguish the obligation. Rashi's implicit explanation (to be developed below) is that precisely because the creditor already had a *shtar*, taking additional collateral signals that the collateral was intended as the exchange object for repayment, not merely as security.

### Equal Value Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּשָׁוֵי שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי – מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אֶלָּא לָאו בִּדְלָא שָׁוֵי שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי – וּבְדִשְׁמוּאֵל קָא מִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** under which Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva disagree? **If** it is a case **where** the collateral was **worth the amount of money** that he lent to him, **what is the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that although the collateral was lost, the debtor is still liable to pay? **Rather, is it not** a case **where** the collateral was **not worth the amount of money** that he lent him? **And** accordingly, **they disagree with regard to** the ruling of **Shmuel.** Rabbi Akiva holds in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel, that if the collateral is lost the entire debt is canceled, and Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with this opinion.

The Gemara analyzes the structure of the *tannaitic* dispute. The first question is: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the *mashkon* was worth the full value of the debt, *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer* — why would Rabbi Eliezer say the creditor can simply take an oath and collect? The Gemara responds: *ela lav d'lo shavei shiur zuzei* — it must be that the *mashkon* is not worth the full value of the debt. Only then can Rabbi Eliezer's position be understood on its own terms.

### Shomer Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; בִּדְלָא שָׁוֵי – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לֵית לְהוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל; וְהָכָא בִּדְשָׁוֵי שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי, וּבִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, in** a case **where** the collateral was **not worth** the value of the loan **everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is **not** in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel. But here** the dispute is **with regard to** a case **where** the collateral was **worth the amount of money** that he lent him, **and they disagree with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yitzḥak. As Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that a creditor acquires collateral** given to him and is considered its owner as long as the item is in his possession? **As it is stated** with regard to a creditor who returns the collateral to the debtor for his personal use: **"And it shall be a righteousness for you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13).

The Gemara proposes that the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva actually concerns *shomer* status. The underlying logic turns on whether a creditor holding a *mashkon* receives any benefit from doing so. Generally, a *shomer chinam* — who receives nothing from the arrangement — is liable only for negligence, while a *shomer sachar* — who receives compensation of some kind — bears the additional liability of theft and loss. If the *mashkon* is worth the full value of the debt, the creditor clearly benefits: he has collateral against which he can collect if the borrower defaults. That would make him a *shomer sachar*, and losing the *mashkon* would require reimbursement. If, however, the *mashkon* is of negligible value, the creditor derives little practical benefit, and the argument for *shomer chinam* status becomes plausible.

The Gemara ultimately shifts the frame: *u'vi-Rabbi Yitzchak kamifligi* — the debate of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva concerns the principle enunciated by Rabbi Yitzchak, to which we now turn.

## Kinyan Mashkon

### Kinyan Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן, צְדָקָה מִנַּיִן לוֹ? מִכָּאן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak infers: **If** the creditor **does not acquire** the **collateral,** then **from where is his righteousness?** If the collateral is not his, the creditor would not be giving up anything of his own. **From here** it is derived **that a creditor acquires** the **collateral.** Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with this opinion, maintaining that the creditor has the halakhic status of an unpaid bailee with respect to the collateral, so that he is exempt from paying restitution if it is stolen or lost. He can therefore collect the entire debt from the debtor. Rabbi Akiva agrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yitzḥak, and holds that since the creditor acquires the collateral, he bears financial responsibility to pay its value in the event of its loss. Therefore, he must deduct the value of the collateral when collecting payment of the debt.

Rabbi Yitzchak asks: *minayin l'baal chov she-koneh mashkon* — from where do we derive that a creditor acquires (*koneh*) the *mashkon*? That is, does the creditor actually take formal possession of the collateral, such that it is legally considered his?

### Tzedakah Verse

The source is the verse *u'lecha tihyeh tzedakah* — "and it will be *tzedakah* to you." The context is the Torah's law requiring the creditor, in certain circumstances, to return the collateral every evening (the debtor's nighttime garment, for example) or every morning. The Gemara reads *tzedakah* here not merely as "righteousness" but in its rabbinic sense of charity or giving from one's own resources. The question is: if the creditor is merely returning the borrower's own property, wherein lies the *tzedakah*? There is no charitable act in returning what was never yours. The inference is therefore that when the borrower hands over the *mashkon*, it becomes the creditor's property — *kinyan* is effected — and when the creditor returns it each morning, he is giving away something that is legally his. That constitutes *tzedakah*.

Tosafot adds a nuance in the tiny note *tzedakah minayin*: *d'lo mikrei tzedakah ela im ken mechaser mamona* — it is only called *tzedakah* if the act actually causes the giver a monetary loss. Returning property that is genuinely one's own, with an obligation to provide it, does involve such a loss, which is what makes the act qualify as *tzedakah* in the full sense.

A brief digression is warranted here. The concept of *tzedakah* in the Torah is often misconceived as equivalent to outright gifting. The Torah's primary modality for supporting those in need is the interest-free loan — *im kesef talveh et ami* and related *mitzvot* — rather than the outright gift. The question of whether lending money to a poor person constitutes *tzedakah* in the formal legal sense is a genuine conceptual issue: on the one hand, the lender is giving his own money; on the other hand, he expects it back, so the loss is only contingent. The Rambam famously rules that enabling employment — a form of transaction that involves no loss at all — constitutes the highest form of *tzedakah*, which suggests that the Rambam understands *tzedakah* in broad enough terms to include arrangements that do not necessarily entail an outright financial loss.

### Enhanced Obligation

If the creditor truly takes *kinyan* of the *mashkon*, his level of responsibility is significantly elevated. The *mashkon* is no longer "her watch" — it is his watch, subject to his full obligation to return it. If something happens to it in the interim, he cannot disclaim responsibility by saying it was not his property. The obligation runs against him personally.

### Post-Loan Taking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? וְתִיסְבְּרָא?! אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק – שֶׁמִּשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָה; מִשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָה מִי אָמַר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **shall we say** that the opinion **of Rabbi Yitzḥak is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*?** The Gemara answers: **And** how can **you understand** that? **Say that Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that a creditor acquires the collateral in a case **where he took the collateral from him not at the time of the loan,** but afterward, in order to induce him to return the loan, as described in the context of the aforementioned verse. But in a case where the creditor **took the collateral from him at the time of the loan, does** Rabbi Yitzḥak **say** that he has acquired it?

The Gemara now introduces an important limitation: *eima d'amar Rabbi Yitzchak b'mashkono shelo b'sha'at halva'ah* — Rabbi Yitzchak's principle of *kinyan* applies only when the creditor took the collateral *after* the loan was made, not at the time of the loan itself. The logic is conceptually transparent: when a debtor, who has not repaid, is subsequently subject to a *beit din*-authorized entry into his home to seize collateral, that act of seizure closely resembles actual collection of the debt. The creditor is not merely receiving a token — he is actively recovering what is owed. In that context, the language of *kinyan* makes intuitive sense. But when collateral is tendered simultaneously with the loan, the borrower is simply providing security for a debt that has not yet matured; the notion that the creditor thereby "takes possession" in any meaningful proprietary sense is far less natural.

The consequence for the *tannaitic* debate is that Rabbi Yitzchak's *kinyan* principle does not govern the case of collateral given at the time of the loan. We therefore need a different framework to understand why Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva disagree in the at-the-time-of-loan scenario.

### Shomer Avedah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק. וְהָכָא – בְּמִשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ, וּבְשׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵדָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי; דְּאִיתְּמַר: שׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵדָה – רַבָּה אָמַר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** in a case where **he took the collateral from him not at the time of the loan, everyone agrees that** the opinion **of Rabbi Yitzḥak is accepted, and here,** the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva is **with regard to** a case where **he took the collateral from him at the time of the loan. And they disagree with regard to** the halakhic status of **one who is safeguarding a lost item** that he found but has not yet returned. **As it was stated:** With regard to **one who is safeguarding a lost item, Rabba says** that his halakhic status is **like** that of **an unpaid bailee,** who is exempt from paying restitution in the event that the item is lost or stolen,

The Gemara resolves: *b'mashkono shelo b'sha'at halva'ah kulei alma lo peligi d'it lei d'Rabbi Yitzchak* — when collateral is taken after the loan, everyone agrees with Rabbi Yitzchak; the creditor has *kinyan* and bears full responsibility for loss. *V'hacha b'mashkono b'sha'at halva'ato* — the dispute concerns collateral tendered at the time of the loan. And the operative question in that case is framed as the *shomer avedah* debate, to which we now turn.

## Shomer Avedah Debate

### Rabbah vs. Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **and Rav Yosef says** that it is **like** that of **a paid bailee,** as while performing the mitzva of taking care of a lost item he is exempt from performing other mitzvot that involve the outlay of money, and the money that he saves is tantamount to payment received. Similarly, since safeguarding collateral is part of the mitzva to lend money to the needy, one who does so is exempt during that time from performing other mitzvot that involve the outlay of money. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer's ruling, that even if the collateral was lost the creditor collects the entire debt, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabba, and Rabbi Akiva's ruling, that the creditor loses the value of the collateral, is in accordance with the opinion of Rav Yosef.

The Gemara cites a directly relevant dispute: *shomer avedah* — a person who finds a lost object and tends it while seeking its rightful owner — *Rabbah amar k'shomer chinam, Rav Yosef amar k'shomer sachar dami*. Is such a guardian a *shomer chinam* or a *shomer sachar*? The relevance to our case is that the creditor holding collateral at the time of the loan is similarly situated: he is performing some kind of custodial function, but whether he receives meaningful compensation for it is contested.

### Collateral as Mitzvah

The parallel is now made explicit. The question of whether a creditor holding a *mashkon* is a *shomer chinam* or *shomer sachar* tracks the *shomer avedah* debate precisely because, in both cases, the guardian may be said to be performing a *mitzvah*.

### Mitzvah Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא; בְּשׁוֹמֵר אֲבֵידָה – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף; וְהָכָא בְּמַלְוֶה צָרִיךְ לְמַשְׁכּוֹן קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר מִצְוָה קָא עָבֵיד, וּמָר סָבַר לָאו מִצְוָה קָא עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **shall we say** that the opinion **of Rav Yosef is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*?** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, with regard to one who is safeguarding a lost item, everyone accepts** the opinion **of Rav Yosef. And here they disagree with regard to** a case where **the creditor needs the collateral** and uses it with the permission of the debtor, deducting a usage fee from the debt. One **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that **he is** still **performing a mitzva** by safeguarding it, and is therefore exempt from performing other mitzvot during that time. Consequently, he has the status of a paid bailee. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds** that since he derives benefit from the collateral, he is safeguarding it for his own benefit, and **is not performing a mitzva.** Therefore, he has the status of an unpaid bailee.

Rashi identifies the relevant *mitzvah* benefit: *schar mitzvah, u'malveh al hamashkon nami mitzvah avid* — the creditor earns *mitzvah* credit for having extended the loan, and this *mitzvah* provides a tangible practical benefit. The practical benefit is *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah*: one who is engaged in performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from other *mitzvah* obligations at that moment. So if the creditor is holding the *mashkon* as part of the larger enterprise of having extended an interest-free loan — itself a *mitzvah* of the Torah — and a poor person approaches him requesting charity, he may decline on the grounds that he is already occupied with a *mitzvah*. This exemption from the *tzedakah* obligation constitutes the "salary" that elevates him to *shomer sachar* status, according to Rav Yosef.

One might wonder whether this theoretical benefit — the possibility of being exempted from a *tzedakah* request that may never materialize — constitutes meaningful compensation. That is precisely what makes the question debatable.

### Loan vs. Holding

The Gemara goes on to distinguish between the *mitzvah* of lending and the act of holding the *mashkon*. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva may hinge not on whether lending money is a *mitzvah* — that is explicit in the Torah — but on whether the holding of the *mashkon* itself partakes of the *mitzvah* character of the loan. One view is that taking a *mashkon* transforms the transaction: rather than a purely altruistic loan in which only the borrower benefits, the creditor now has a stake in the object, a practical self-interest. This makes the arrangement more business-like and less *mitzvah*-like, and the creditor is no longer receiving "compensation" in the *mitzvah* sense. According to this view, the creditor is a *shomer chinam*: Rabbi Eliezer's position.

The alternative view separates the two components cleanly: the loan was a *mitzvah*, and the custodial function of holding the *mashkon* partakes of that *mitzvah* character. The creditor is therefore a *shomer sachar*: Rabbi Akiva's position.

### Using Collateral

The Gemara addresses one additional scenario briefly passed over above. The case of *malveh tzakich lemashkon* — where the creditor actively uses the *mashkon* to facilitate repayment of the debt, for example by using the borrower's hoe and crediting rental fees against the outstanding balance. (This arrangement requires the creditor to pay rental fees to avoid *ribbit*.) The question in this scenario is whether holding and using the *mashkon* in this way constitutes a *mitzvah*. The Gemara notes: *keivan d'tzakich lemashkon ein kan mitzvah* — since the creditor is deriving direct benefit from the *mashkon*, the *mitzvah* framing may not apply, and the arrangement is more purely transactional. According to this view, the creditor would be a *shomer chinam*, bearing less liability precisely because the personal benefit he derives strips the arrangement of its *mitzvah* quality — a somewhat counterintuitive result that the Gemara acknowledges.

## Shmuel & Shemitah

### Shemitah Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן וְנִכְנְסָה שְׁמִיטָּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה אֶלָּא פְּלַג – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט. דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה מַשְׁכּוֹנוֹ כְּנֶגֶד חוֹבוֹ – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט, וְאִם לָאו – מְשַׁמֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes discussion of Shmuel's statement that if the creditor loses the collateral, the debt is canceled. **Let us say** that Shmuel's ruling is **the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is stated in a *baraita* with regard to the *halakha* that the Sabbatical Year does not abrogate debts for which collateral was taken: If **one lends** money **to another on** the basis of **collateral and the Sabbatical Year commences,** then **even if** the collateral **is worth only half** the value of the loan, the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** the debt; this is **the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says: If** the value of the **collateral was commensurate with his debt,** the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** the debt, **but if** it was **not** commensurate with the debt, the Sabbatical Year **abrogates** the debt.

The Gemara now revisits Shmuel's framework in a different legal context, asking whether the *tannaitic* dispute in the *mishnah* concerning *shemitah* reflects a debate about Shmuel's principle. The *mishnah* states: *hamalveh et chavero al hamashkon venichnesah shemitah* — a person lent money against a *mashkon* and the *shemitah* year arrived. Normally, *shemitah* annuls outstanding loans — the creditor may no longer collect. But a loan secured by a *mashkon* may be different, because holding the *mashkon* is in some sense already a form of collection.

### Whole Debt Protected

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *af al pi she'eino shaveh ella palga eino meshammet* — even if the *mashkon* is worth only half the debt, *shemitah* does not annul the loan at all. The implication is that the entire hundred dollars is protected, even though the *mashkon* is worth only fifty.

### Partial Annulment

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi rules: *im hayah mashkon k'neged chovo eino m'shameit* — if the *mashkon* is worth the full value of the debt, *shemitah* does not annul it. *V'im lav m'shameit* — but if not, then *shemitah* does annul the loan. The implication is that the unrepresented portion of the debt — the fifty dollars not covered by the watch's value — is annulled.

### Full Embodiment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט״ דְּקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אִילֵימָא כְּנֶגְדּוֹ – מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא סָבַר: כְּנֶגְדּוֹ נָמֵי מְשַׁמֵּט?! אֶלָּא אַמַּאי תָּפֵיס מַשְׁכּוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the statement: The Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** the debt, **which the first *tanna*,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **says? If we say** it means that the Sabbatical Year does not abrogate that part of the debt that is **commensurate with** the collateral, but it does abrogate the rest, this indicates **by inference that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds** that the Sabbatical Year **abrogates** the entire debt, including the part **commensurate with** the collateral **as well. But** if this is so, **why did** the creditor **seize collateral** from the debtor at all?

The Gemara now asks: *u'vid'Shmuel kamifligi* — is this *tannaitic* debate actually a debate about Shmuel's principle? Under Shmuel's view, the *mashkon* fully embodies the entire debt regardless of its face value. A fifty-dollar watch, in Shmuel's framework, represents the full hundred-dollar debt. If that is true, then holding the watch means that the entire debt is "in hand," and *shemitah* cannot annul any of it — that would be Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position. The opposing view holds that the watch only represents its actual value of fifty dollars, and therefore only that portion of the debt is protected; the remaining fifty dollars is annulled — that would be Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's position.

### Unrepresented Portion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלּוֹ – וּבְדִשְׁמוּאֵל קָא מִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that the Sabbatical Year does not abrogate the debt at all, and the debtor is liable to pay an amount **commensurate with** the **entire** debt? **And** accordingly, **they disagree with regard to Shmuel's** principle that collateral is considered equivalent to the entire debt, even if it is worth less than the debt.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading. It cannot be that the debate concerns whether *shemitah* annuls even the portion of the debt represented by the *mashkon* itself: *ella amai tafis mashkon* — why would anyone hold the *mashkon* if not precisely to protect that portion of the debt from *shemitah*? Everyone must agree that the value represented by the *mashkon* is not annulled. The debate, then, is more narrow: it concerns only the *remainder* of the debt, the fifty dollars not covered by the *mashkon*'s value.

### Reminder Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; לְעוֹלָם כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: כְּנֶגְדּוֹ; וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא סָבַר: כְּנֶגְדּוֹ נָמֵי מְשַׁמֵּט. וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ: לְמַאי תָּפֵיס לֵיהּ מַשְׁכּוֹן? לְזִכְרוֹן דְּבָרִים בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, actually,** the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that the Sabbatical Year does not abrogate the debt is referring only to the part of the debt **commensurate with** the collateral, **and they disagree with regard to that** part of the debt. **The first *tanna*,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that the Sabbatical Year does not abrogate the part of the debt **commensurate with** the collateral, **and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds** that it **abrogates** the part of the debt **commensurate with** the collateral **as well. And as for** the question **you said: For what** purpose **did he seize collateral from him** at all, the answer is that he takes it **merely as a reminder,** to increase the likelihood that the debt will be paid.

The Gemara concludes with a third position. *L'zikharon devarim b'alma* — the *mashkon* is merely a reminder, nothing more. According to this view, holding a *mashkon* worth fifty dollars on a hundred-dollar debt does not mean the creditor has "seized" any portion of the debt at all. The watch is simply there to remind both parties that the debt exists. Since no part of the debt has been effectively collected, *shemitah* may annul the entire loan — including even the portion nominally corresponding to the *mashkon*'s value. This represents the most extreme position in the other direction from Shmuel.

## Summary & Oaths

### Three Models

Having traversed this terrain, the Gemara's discussion can be organized around three distinct conceptual models of what a *mashkon* is.

### Model 1: Full

Shmuel's model: the *mashkon* fully embodies the entire debt. Regardless of the *mashkon*'s actual monetary value, its presence means that the creditor holds the entire debt in hand. Losing the *mashkon* means losing the entire debt. Holding the *mashkon* during *shemitah* protects the entire debt from annulment.

### Model 2: Value

The middle position: the *mashkon* represents precisely the portion of the debt equal to its market value. Losing a fifty-dollar *mashkon* on a hundred-dollar debt means losing fifty dollars. During *shemitah*, only the fifty dollars represented by the *mashkon* is protected; the remainder is annulled.

### Model 3: Reminder

The most restrictive view: when the *mashkon* is worth less than the full debt, it does not represent *any* portion of the debt. It is merely a mnemonic device — a reminder to both parties. For *shemitah* purposes, the entire debt remains subject to annulment, as though no *mashkon* were held at all. (The creditor may still owe restitution if he loses the object itself, but that is a separate question of bailment law.)

### Gemara Summary

These three models map neatly onto the debates encountered throughout the *sugya*: the debate between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi about *shemitah*; the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva about creditor liability; and the question of whether a creditor is a *shomer chinam* or *shomer sachar*. The debate of Rabbah and Rav Yosef about *shomer avedah* status provides the underlying conceptual foundation for how performing a *mitzvah* might or might not constitute compensation sufficient to elevate one's guardian status.

### All Oaths Topic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַנִּשְׁבָּעִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה – נִשְׁבָּעִין וְלֹא מְשַׁלְּמִין. וְאֵלּוּ נִשְׁבָּעִין וְנוֹטְלִין: הַשָּׂכִיר, וְהַנִּגְזָל, וְהַנֶּחְבָּל, וְשֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה, וְחֶנְוָנִי עַל פִּנְקָסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All** those **who take an oath that** is legislated **by the Torah take an oath and do not pay.** By Torah law, one takes an oath only in order to exempt himself from a monetary claim. **And these** litigants **take** a rabbinically instituted **oath and receive** possession of the disputed funds or property, i.e., their claim is upheld by means of the oath, even though they are not in possession of the property in question: **A hired worker** who claims that he has not received his wages; **and one who was robbed** and sues the person who robbed him; **and one who was injured,** who claims compensation from the person who injured him; **and one whose opposing** litigant is **suspect with regard to the** taking of an **oath.** When a person suspected of taking false oaths is liable to take an oath in order to exempt himself, the claimant takes an oath instead and receives payment. **And a storekeeper** relying **on his ledger** also takes an oath and is paid.

The *mishnah* now opens a new chapter with the words *kol hanishba'in shebaTorah* — a formulation that marks a transition from the specific cases of *mashkon* and creditor liability to a broader taxonomy of oaths in Jewish law.

## Torah vs. Rabbinic

### Torah Oaths

The *mishnah* establishes a fundamental structural principle: *nishba'in v'lo meshalmin* — all oaths imposed by the Torah result in the swearer taking the oath and thereby *not* paying. Conversely, there is a rabbinic category of *nishba'in v'notlin* — oaths after which the swearer *collects*. The distinction is critical. Torah-mandated oaths function as a defensive mechanism: they exculpate or neutralize a claim. Rabbinic oaths, by contrast, are an offensive mechanism: they validate a claim and authorize collection.

### Modeh B'Miktzat

The first of the three Torah oaths is *modeh b'miktzat*: partial admission. If a defendant admits to owing part of a claimed debt but denies the remainder, the Torah requires him to swear regarding the denied portion — and upon doing so, he is exempt from paying it.

### Ed Echad

The second Torah oath: *ed echad* — a single witness. When one witness testifies that the defendant owes money, the defendant is required to take an oath denying the claim. After taking the oath, he is not obligated to pay.

### Shvuat Shomrim

The third Torah oath: *shvuat hashomrim* — the guardian's oath. A guardian (*shomer*) who claims that the entrusted object was lost or stolen without negligence on his part takes an oath to that effect and is thereby exempted from liability.

### Rabbinic Oaths

In contrast to these three defensive Torah oaths, the *mishnah* enumerates several rabbinic oaths that entitle the swearer to *collect*. The rabbis instituted these oaths in specific cases where existing circumstances create a strong presumption in favor of the claimant — strong enough that, while falling short of the evidentiary standard required for ordinary monetary adjudication (*hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah*), they justify granting the claimant an oath as a means of validating the claim.

### Presumption

The animating principle is that in these specific cases, the claimant is most likely telling the truth. The rabbis therefore devised a mechanism — the oath — to translate that probabilistic presumption into an actionable legal result, authorizing collection on the basis of the oath alone.

## Oath Collection

### Employee Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשָּׂכִיר – כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״תֵּן לִי שְׂכָרִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״; הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״נָתַתִּי״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא נָטַלְתִּי״ – הוּא נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא שָׁם מִקְצָת הוֹדָאָה. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״תֵּן לִי שְׂכָרִי חֲמִשִּׁים דִּינָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הִתְקַבַּלְתָּ דִּינַר זָהָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does this *halakha* apply to **the hired worker?** The case is where one **says to** his employer: **Give me my wages that are** still **in your possession.** The employer **says: I** already **gave** them to you. **And that** worker **says: I have not received** them. In such a case, the worker **takes an oath** that he has not received his wages, **and he receives** payment from his employer. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This oath cannot be administered **unless there is partial admission** on the part of the employer. **How so?** The case is where the worker **said to him: Give me my wages, fifty** silver **dinars, which are** still **in your possession. And** the employer **says: You have** already **received one golden dinar,** which is worth twenty-five silver dinars. Since the employer has admitted that he owes part of the sum, the worker takes an oath and is paid the whole sum.

The first case of rabbinic *nishba'in v'notlin* is the *sachir* — the hired worker or employee. The scenario: David says to Dov, "Pay me my wages, which you owe me." Dov responds, "I already paid you." David says, "I never received it."

### Employee Collects

*Hu nishba v'notel* — David takes the oath and collects. The Gemara's rationale is not a moral judgment against employers. Rather, it reflects an epistemic asymmetry: the employer is occupied with numerous employees and cannot reliably track each payment. It is far more likely that he does not clearly remember whether he paid David or not. David, by contrast, has only one employer and knows with certainty whether he was paid. Accordingly, David's knowledge is more reliable, and the oath is placed with him.

### R. Yehuda Reversal

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with the baseline rule. He holds that David can take the offensive oath and collect *only* if there is first a partial admission from the employer — *ad she-tehei miktzat hoda'ah*. The example: David claims fifty *dinar*; Dov says, "I already paid you a gold *dinara*," worth twenty-five silver *dinarim*, leaving only twenty-five still owed. That creates a *modeh b'miktzat* situation. Ordinarily, the defendant — Dov — would take the *modeh b'miktzat* oath and be exempt from paying the disputed remainder. But Rabbi Yehuda says: in these cases, rather than allowing the defendant to neutralize the claim with his oath, we *transfer* the oath to the claimant — David — who then takes it and collects. Rabbi Yehuda's consistent position across all three cases in the *mishnah* is that the rabbinic offensive oath requires a pre-existing *modeh b'miktzat* dynamic on the defendant's side; the oath is then reversed and given to the plaintiff.

### Robbery Victim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִגְזָל – כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ שֶׁנִּכְנַס לְבֵיתוֹ לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת; הוּא אוֹמֵר ״כֶּלְיִי נָטַלְתָּ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא נָטַלְתִּי״ – הוּא נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא שָׁם מִקְצָת הוֹדָאָה. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ ״שְׁנֵי כֵּלִים נָטַלְתָּ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא נָטַלְתִּי אֶלָּא אֶחָד״.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does this *halakha* apply to **one who was robbed?** The case is where witnesses **testified about** the defendant **that he entered** the claimant's **house to seize collateral from him without** the **authority** to do so. The claimant **said: You took items** that **belong to me. And** the defendant **said: I did not take** them. The claimant **takes an oath and receives** payment of his claim. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This oath cannot be administered **unless there is partial admission** on the part of the defendant. **How so?** The case is where the claimant **said to him: You took two items. And he says: I took only one.** Since the defendant admits that he took one item from the house, the claimant takes an oath and receives payment of his whole claim.

The second case is *nigzal* — the robbery victim. *Hayu me'idim oto she-nichnas l'veito l'mashkeno shelo birshut* — witnesses testify that they saw the defendant enter the claimant's home to seize collateral without proper *beit din* authorization. That unauthorized entry is established by testimony.

### Victim Collects

David, the victim, claims: "You took my possessions." Dov denies it. Since Dov's unauthorized entry into the home is established, creating credible grounds to suspect he took something, *hu nishba v'notel* — David may take an oath specifying what was taken and collect accordingly. Although no one witnessed the actual taking, the circumstantial evidence — established unauthorized entry — is sufficient, in the rabbinic framework, to authorize the oath-based collection.

### R. Yehuda Partial

Rabbi Yehuda again requires a partial admission to trigger this mechanism. The example: David says two items were taken; Dov admits to taking one but denies taking the other. That admission creates the *modeh b'miktzat* predicate, and the oath is transferred to David, who swears to the second item and collects.

### Assault Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְבָּל – כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁנִּכְנַס תַּחַת יָדוֹ שָׁלֵם וְיָצָא חָבוּל; וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״חָבַלְתָּ בִּי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא חָבַלְתִּי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא שָׁם מִקְצָת הוֹדָאָה. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״חָבַלְתָּ בִּי שְׁתַּיִם״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא חָבַלְתִּי בְּךָ אֶלָּא אֶחָת״.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does this *halakha* apply to **one who was injured?** The case is where witnesses **testified about** the injured person **that he entered into the domain of** the defendant **whole but left injured, and** the claimant **said to** the defendant: **You injured me. And** the defendant **says: I did not injure** you. The injured party **takes an oath and receives** compensation. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This oath cannot be administered **unless there is partial admission. How so?** The case is where the claimant **said to** the defendant: **You injured me twice. And the other says: I injured you only once.** In such a case, the injured party takes an oath that he was injured twice and receives compensation for both injuries.

The third case is *nechbal* — the assault victim. *Hayu me'idim oto she-nichnas tachat yado shaleim* — witnesses testify that David entered Dov's domain in good health. *V'yatzah chavul* — he left injured, bearing visible wounds. David claims: "You beat me." Dov denies it.

### Assault Collects

*Harei zeh nishba v'notel* — David takes an oath and collects. The circumstantial evidence — entering healthy, leaving injured — is sufficient in the civil context to establish the presumption authorizing the oath. This falls short of the standard for criminal prosecution, and it would also be insufficient as formal testimony to establish *chiyuv* in ordinary adjudication. But for the rabbinic oath mechanism, the evident before-and-after is enough to extend to David the benefit of the oath.

### R. Yehuda Assault

Rabbi Yehuda once again requires the *modeh b'miktzat* predicate. The example: David says "You broke both my arms"; Dov admits to one but denies the other. The partial admission activates the reversed oath, and David swears to the second injury and collects.

## Gold-Silver Ratio

### Mishnah Ratio

The *mishnah*'s formulation in Rabbi Yehuda's example of the employee case uses specific currency figures: David claims fifty silver *dinarim*, and Dov says he already paid a gold *dinara*. The implicit arithmetic of the *mishnah* is that a gold *dinara* is worth twenty-five silver *dinarim*.

### Tosafot Object

Tosafot flags this as problematic. In Tosafot's own time, gold was worth only approximately ten times as much as silver by weight — not twenty-five times. How, then, does the *mishnah*'s arithmetic work?

### Rabbeinu Tam

Rabbeinu Tam expresses puzzlement at the magnitude of the historical change: *eich nishtanu kol kach* — how did things change so drastically? Even in Talmudic times, Rabbeinu Tam suggests, gold was only approximately twelve times the value of silver by weight.

### Coin Thickness

Tosafot resolves the discrepancy by pointing to the physical dimensions of the coins themselves. A gold coin was twice as thick as a silver coin. If gold is twelve times the value of silver by weight, and a gold coin is twice as thick — and therefore twice as heavy — as a silver coin, the gold coin would be worth approximately twenty-four to twenty-five silver coins, which matches the *mishnah*'s implicit ratio.

### Historical Ratios

This resolution, while creative, touches on a broader phenomenon worth noting. Gold-to-silver ratios have in fact fluctuated dramatically across different historical periods. The ratio of roughly 10:1 familiar to the *rishonim* is by no means universal or fixed; historical data show ratios ranging widely depending on time and place. Tosafot, however, appears not to have been aware of the extent to which such fluctuation was possible, and hence the difficulty posed by the *mishnah*'s figures seemed to require the coin-thickness explanation rather than a simple appeal to historical variation in commodity prices.
