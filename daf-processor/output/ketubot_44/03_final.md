# Ketubot 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketuva Collection Rights

### Mishna Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְאָרֵס אֶת בִּתּוֹ וְגֵרְשָׁהּ, אֵירְסָהּ וְנִתְאַרְמְלָה — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ. הִשִּׂיאָהּ וְגֵרְשָׁהּ, הִשִּׂיאָהּ וְנִתְאַרְמְלָה — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ שֶׁלָּהּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל אָב. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם מִשֶּׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ, אֵין לְאָבִיהָ רְשׁוּת בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who betroths his** minor **daughter** to a man, **and** the man subsequently **divorces her,** and her father then **betroths her** to another, **and she is widowed,** the payment specified in **her marriage contract,** even from her second husband, **is his,** i.e., it belongs to the father. However, if her father **married her** off **and** her husband **divorced her,** and her father then **married her** to another man **and she was widowed,** even the payment specified in **her marriage contract** from her first marriage **is hers. Rabbi Yehuda says** that the payment specified in **the first** marriage contract belongs **to** the **father. They said to him: If** it **was after he married her** off, even the first time, **her father no** longer **has authority over her.**

The daf begins in the middle of 43b, approximately four lines after the new *gemara* begins. The *mishna* addresses the question of who collects a woman's *ketuva* depending on the stage of marriage at which she is divorced. If she is divorced from *erusin*, the father collects, because the daughter remains under his legal authority (*reshut av*) throughout *erusin*, and he retains the right to remarry her after such a divorce. If the divorce follows *nisuin*, she herself collects — with one important exception articulated by Rabbi Yehuda.

### R. Yehuda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל אָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the payment specified in **the first** marriage contract belongs **to** the **father.**

The *gemara* directs its attention to Rabbi Yehuda's position: *harishona shel av* — even the payment from the first *nisuin* goes to the father, provided the father arranged the *erusin* when his daughter was a *ketana*. The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — what is his reasoning?

### Rabba & Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הוֹאִיל וּמִשְּׁעַת אֵירוּסִין זָכָה בָּהֶן הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: Since the father is entitled to** the payments of the marriage contract **from the time of** her **betrothal,** when the first husband obligated himself to pay her marriage contract, as the girl was under her father's authority at the time, the father receives the money. Although the money is actually paid only after the girl was married and divorced, at which time she is under her own jurisdiction, her father acquired his right to the marriage contract from the time of her betrothal, when she was under his authority.

Rabba and Rav Yosef both explain: *hoil u-mishe'as erusin zachah bahen ha-av* — since the *ketuva* obligations commenced at the time of *erusin*, and at that moment the father would have been the payee had a divorce occurred, those obligations remain payable to the father even after *nisuin*. The argument is straightforward: the *ketuva* debt originated at a time when the father was the entitled party, and therefore he remains so even once *nisuin* takes place. It is a reasonably coherent position — yet the *gemara* will challenge it.

## Bogeret Between Erusin

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל אָב. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בִּמְאָרֵס אֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה וּבָגְרָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִשֵּׂאת, שֶׁאֵין לְאָבִיהָ רְשׁוּת בָּהּ. אַמַּאי? הָכָא נָמֵי לֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִשְּׁעַת אֵירוּסִין זָכָה בָּהֶן הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the payment specified in **the first** marriage contract belongs **to** her **father. And Rabbi Yehuda concedes** in the case **of one who betroths his daughter when she is a minor, and she matures and subsequently marries, that her father no** longer **has authority over her** once she becomes an adult, and he does not retain his rights to her marriage contract. According to the above explanation, **why** does Rabbi Yehuda agree in that case? **Here too, let him say: Since the father is entitled to** the payment of her marriage contract **from the time of** her **betrothal,** he receives the payment even if she married after she reached majority.

Rava raises a difficulty from the following *braita*: Rabbi Yehuda himself concedes that if the father arranged *erusin* when the daughter was a *ketana*, but she became a *bogeret* before *nisuin*, and then the *nisuin* took place — *she'ein l'aviha reshut bah* — the father no longer has any authority over her, and therefore the *ketuva* from this *nisuin* is not payable to him.

The challenge is sharp. If Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning is that *erusin* established the father as the payee and that status persists, then even her becoming a *bogeret* between *erusin* and *nisuin* should make no difference — he was the payee, and nothing should change that. Why, then, does Rabbi Yehuda concede this case?

To sharpen the point further: imagine the *erusin* occurs at a certain time, establishing the *ketuva* obligation with the father as payee. The daughter subsequently becomes a *bogeret*, exits her father's authority, and then *nisuin* takes place. According to Rabbi Yehuda's stated logic — that the obligation began under the father's authority and therefore remains his — her transition to *bogeret* status ought to be irrelevant. The original payee was the father; that should not change. Yet Rabbi Yehuda concedes it does.

### Unwritten vs Written

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי אִתְּמַר, הָכִי אִתְּמַר: רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הוֹאִיל וּבִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְתָּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** this statement **was stated, it was stated as follows: Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: Since** the amounts of the marriage contract **are written under his authority,** as the marriage contract is drafted right before the marriage, at which point the girl was still under her father's jurisdiction, he is therefore entitled to the money. This explains why the ruling is different if she reached majority before her marriage. In this case, the marriage contract was written when she was no longer under her father's jurisdiction, and therefore her father is not entitled to the payment of her marriage contract.

The resolution turns on a crucial distinction between the unwritten and written dimensions of the *ketuva* obligation. The obligation to pay a *ketuva* exists even before any document is written — it is a *takanas chachamim*, a Rabbinic enactment that takes effect from *erusin*. However, just before the *chuppah*, an actual *ketuva* document is written. This writing is not merely a record of the pre-existing obligation; it *redefines* and recreates the obligation. The written *ketuva* generates new obligations distinct from the earlier unwritten ones.

### Formalist Approach

With this distinction in hand, Rabba and Rav Yosef restate Rabbi Yehuda's position more precisely: *hoil u-vireshuso nichtavin* — the determining factor is not when the *erusin* occurred, but under whose authority the *ketuva* is written. If the *ketuva* is written while she is still a *ketana* under her father's control — as typically happens just before the *chuppah*, at the *tish* — then those written obligations are created while she is in his *reshut*, and accordingly the father becomes the payee. In the scenario where she becomes a *bogeret* before *nisuin*, the *ketuva* is written after she has already exited her father's authority. At that moment she is her own person, and the newly created written obligations belong to her.

This is a notably formalist approach. Rabbi Yehuda is saying: we can only define the obligation in terms of who holds legal authority at the time the *ketuva* is written. Whatever the social purpose of the *ketuva* — and it is admittedly strange, since the *ketuva*'s entire function is to protect her after divorce or widowhood, at a time when the father will no longer take her back — Rabbi Yehuda maps the payee onto the legal reality at the moment the document is created. If she is eleven years old and the *nisuin* occurs at a very young age, the *ketuva* gets paid to the father, even though the purpose of the *ketuva* is entirely oriented toward protecting her. It is a peculiar result, but Rabbi Yehuda's framework is a consistent one: the writing of the *ketuva* recreates and defines the obligation, and that definition is fixed by the legal authority in force at the moment of writing.

The broader implication, accepted even by Rabbi Yehuda, is that the writing of the *ketuva* before *nisuin* genuinely recreates the obligations rather than merely recording existing ones. This has direct consequences for the lien question the *gemara* is about to raise.

## Liens & Dating

### When Liens Exist

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִיגְבָּא, מֵאֵימַת גָּבְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And** now that it has been established that even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, the rights to a marriage contract are determined based upon the time of marriage and not the time of betrothal, despite the fact that its sum is fixed at that time, the Gemara asks: **From when** does the husband's property become liened to ensure **collection** of his wife's marriage contract? Does the monetary claim take effect at the time of betrothal, so that there is a lien on any property he owned at that time, or is there a lien only on property that the husband owned at the time of marriage?

The *gemara* now poses the question *u-migba mei-eimat gavya* — from what date does the *ketuva* carry liens against the husband's estate? The *mishna* assumes that *ketuva* obligations are in force already from *erusin*. If *erusin* occurred in 2010 and *nisuin* — with the writing of the *ketuva* — occurred in 2015, and she comes to collect in 2030, against which properties does she have lien rights? Does her lien reach back to 2010 and encompass property the husband sold between 2010 and 2015, or does it begin only from 2015, when the *ketuva* was written?

This question is sharpened by the preceding discussion: if the writing of the *ketuva* at *nisuin* recreates and redefines the obligations, one might argue that the liens are equally reset — the old liens dissolved and new ones created at the time of writing. On the other hand, one could argue that the core obligation, which existed from *erusin* as an unwritten *takanas chachamim*, carries liens that were never interrupted.

### Rav Huna's Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מָנֶה מָאתַיִם מִן הָאֵירוּסִין, וְתוֹסֶפֶת מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין. וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** With regard to the **one hundred dinars** for a non-virgin and the **two hundred** for a virgin, the basic sums of a marriage contract instituted by the Sages, she has a lien on her husband's property **from the** time of the **betrothal,** as she was already entitled to the basic sum of her marriage contract from that day, **but** with regard to the **addition,** i.e., additional sums of money stipulated by the husband himself, the wife's lien on his property takes effect only **from** the time of **the marriage. And Rav Asi said:** The lien for **both this and that** take effect only **from the** time of their **marriage.**

Rav Huna takes a middle position. He distinguishes between the *ikar ketuva* — the core *ketuva* of 200 *zuz* (or the standard amount), which is obligated even without being written — and the *tosefet*, the additional amount the husband voluntarily adds at the time of writing. The *ikar ketuva* exists from *erusin*, and so its liens continue from *erusin* as well. The *tosefet*, however, is something that arises only at the time of the written *ketuva* and is associated specifically with *nisuin*; therefore its liens begin only from *nisuin*. Rav Huna in effect says: even though the writing recreates the obligations in some sense, the lien attached to the underlying debt that existed from *erusin* survives.

### Rav Assi's Position

Rav Assi takes the more sweeping position: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh min ha-nisu'in* — both the *ikar ketuva* and the *tosefet* carry liens only from *nisuin*. Once we accept that the writing of the *ketuva* recreates the obligations, it follows naturally that all liens are reset and begin from the time of that writing. This is the more internally consistent view given the framework established earlier.

## Competing Ketubos

### Two Documents Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב הוּנָא הָכִי? וְהָאִתְּמַר: הוֹצִיאָה עָלָיו שְׁתֵּי כְּתוּבּוֹת, אַחַת שֶׁל מָאתַיִם, וְאַחַת שֶׁל שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת. וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בָּאתָה לִגְבּוֹת מָאתַיִם — גּוֹבָה מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן, שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת — גּוֹבָה מִזְּמַן שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rav Huna** actually **say this,** that a woman has a lien on her husband's property from the time of betrothal with regard to payment of the one hundred or two hundred dinars that constitute the main sum of her marriage contract? **But wasn't it stated** that *amora'im* discussed the case of a woman who **produced against** her husband, upon their divorce, **two marriage contracts,** written at different times, **one of two hundred** dinars **and** the other **one of three hundred? And Rav Huna said:** Since she can claim only one marriage contract, if **she came to collect** the first sum of **two hundred** dinars, **she** can **collect** that amount even **from** property her husband sold after **the first** point in **time,** when this marriage contract was written, and if she wishes to collect the one worth **three hundred** dinars, **she** can **collect from** property her husband sold after **the second** point in **time.**

The *gemara* now asks whether Rav Huna's position is self-consistent, by confronting it with another ruling attributed to him. The case: a woman who divorces produces two *ketubot* — one dated 2015 for 200 *zuz*, and one dated 2017 for 300 *zuz*. Rav Huna rules that if she wishes to collect the 200, she may do so using the earlier date, and her liens extend back to 2015; if she wishes to collect the 300, she may do so, but her liens run only from 2017.

### Addition Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, תִּיגְבֵּי מָאתַיִם מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן, וּמֵאָה מִזְּמַן שֵׁנִי. וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תִּיגְבֵּי חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת כּוּלָּם? מָאתַיִם מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן, תְּלָת מְאָה מִזְּמַן שֵׁנִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: **And if it is so,** that Rav Huna maintains that the lien for the basic sum and the addition can be ascribed to different dates, **let her collect two hundred** dinars **from** property sold after **the first** point in **time,** as she was already entitled to the basic sum of her marriage contract from that day, **and** she can collect **the** additional **one hundred** dinars **from** property sold after **the second** point in **time. And** even **according to your reasoning, let her collect the entire five hundred** dinars, the sum of both marriage contracts. She should be able to collect **two hundred from** property sold after **the first** point in **time** and **three hundred from** property sold after **the second** stipulated **time.**

The *gemara* challenges this ruling. If Rav Huna is willing to split the *ketuva* obligations in the *erusin*/*nisuin* case — allowing the 200 *zuz* core to carry liens from *erusin* while the *tosefet* carries liens only from *nisuin* — why does he not apply the same logic here? The 300 *ketuva* should simply be read as the original 200 plus an additional 100. Under Rav Huna's own reasoning, the woman ought to be entitled to collect 200 from the earlier lien date and 100 from the later one. And pushing the logic further: why not even read it as a total of 500 — the full 200 from the first document and the full 300 from the second, each with its own lien date?

### Explicit Language Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מַאי טַעְמָא לָא גָּבְיָא? כֵּיוָן דְּלָא כָּתַב לָהּ: ״צְבֵיתִי וְאוֹסֵיפִית לָךְ תְּלָת מְאָה אַמָּאתַיִם״. הָכִי קָאָמַר לַהּ: אִי מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן גָּבְיַאתְּ — גְּבַאי מָאתַיִם, אִי מִזְּמַן שֵׁנִי גָּבְיַאתְּ — גְּבַאי תְּלָת מְאָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is the reason** that **she may not collect** the entire sum of **five hundred** dinars? **Since** he did **not write to her** in the second marriage contract: **I chose** to **add to** the payment of **your** marriage contract, and therefore I am writing a contract for **three hundred** dinars in addition **to** the first **two hundred** dinars, it is apparent that **this is what he** meant to **say to her** by writing a second marriage contract: **If you collect from** property sold after **the first** point in **time, you may collect two hundred** dinars; **if you collect from** property sold after **the second** point in **time, you may collect three hundred** dinars.

The *gemara* provides its answer: *keivan d'lo kasav lah* — since he did not write in the second *ketuva* the explicit language, *z'visi v'osifis lach slas me'ah a-masayim* — "I have desired and I have added to you 300 upon the existing 200" — we cannot read the second document as an addition to the first. If he had intended to supplement the first, he had language available to say so clearly. Similarly, if the 300 were meant to represent the original 200 plus 100 more, he should have written: *osifis lach me'ah a-masayim* — "I am adding 100 to the 200." He did not write either of these formulations.

### Competing Alternatives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא גָּבְיָא, מִדְּלָא כְּתַב לַהּ ״אוֹסֵיפִית לִךְ מֵאָה אַמָּאתַיִם״, אַחוֹלֵי אַחְלֵיהּ לְשִׁעְבּוּדָא קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too, this is the reasoning** for the ruling **that she does not collect** the additional one hundred dinars from the second stipulated time, **as he did not write to her** in the second marriage contract: **I added one hundred** dinars **to your** original marriage contract **of two hundred** dinars. Evidently, he did not add to the existing marriage contract. Rather, **she forgave** her rights **to the first** marriage contract, including the **lien** on his property from the date it was written, in order to accept the second marriage document.

Since no such language appears, the two documents are read not as cumulative or as B incorporating A, but as separate and competing alternatives: *i mi-zman rishon gavis, gavis masayim; i mi-zman sheni gavis, gavis slas me'ah* — if you choose to collect from the first date, you collect 200; if you choose to collect from the second date, you collect 300. They are alternatives. She picks one. We do not aggregate them, and we do not read the later one as reinterpreting or supplementing the earlier one.

The rule that flows from this is that in the ordinary *ketuva* case — where the husband at *erusin* incurs an unwritten base obligation of 200, and then at *nisuin* writes a *ketuva* that explicitly adds a *tosefet* on top of the core amount — the structure is different. There the *ketuva* document explicitly layers the *tosefet* onto the *ikar*, so that we do treat them as a layered obligation: the 200 carries liens from *erusin*, the *tosefet* from *nisuin*. But where no such explicit language exists, competing documents are read as alternatives, not as cumulative obligations. Furthermore, the *gemara* notes — *acholi achlei l'shibuda kama* — if she chooses to use the later document, she has implicitly waived her earlier liens. The second *ketuva* represents an entirely new obligation of 300, not the first obligation reconstituted.

## Supersession Doctrine

### Rav Nachman's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִי בָּעֲיָא — בְּהַאי גָּבְיָא, אִי בָּעֲיָא — בְּהַאי גָּבְיָא. לֵימָא פְּלִיגָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן? דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת הַיּוֹצְאִין בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה — בִּיטֵּל שֵׁנִי אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § After clarifying Rav Huna's opinion, the Gemara turns its attention to a more general issue, connected to his last statement. **The Master,** i.e., Rav Huna, **said,** as indicated in the above discussion, that **if she wishes** she can **collect** the sum specified in **this** marriage contract, and **if she wishes** she can **collect** the sum specified in **that** marriage contract. The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that this opinion **disagrees** with **that** of **Rav Naḥman? As Rav Naḥman said:** With regard to **two documents** that pertain to the same issue and **that are produced one after the other,** e.g., a pair of documents that ascribe the transfer of ownership over a particular field to different times, the **second,** later document **nullifies the first.** Here too, the second marriage contract should negate the first one entirely.

At this point the *gemara* introduces a broader principle through Rav Nachman. The case now shifts from two *ketubot* to two *shtarot* — documents of title transfer on the same piece of property. If Michael holds one document stating that a co-op was sold to him in 2012, and a second document stating the same co-op was sold to him in 2015, when did the sale occur? And more practically: from what date do Michael's liens on the seller's estate begin?

The reason the date matters is this: when a seller transfers property, he implicitly undertakes to reimburse the buyer if any creditor of the seller later takes the property away from the buyer by virtue of a pre-existing lien. The buyer therefore acquires lien rights on the seller's estate from the date of purchase — so that if a creditor seizes the property, the buyer can collect from the seller's remaining assets. An earlier date means broader access to those assets, because it extends the buyer's lien further back in time.

Rav Nachman rules: *bitel sheni es ha-rishon* — the second document supersedes the first. The buyer, by accepting the second document, has implicitly forfeited the earlier lien. His liens run only from 2015. The logic is that since the second document provides no additional benefit over the first — it is the same property, at a later date — the only thing the buyer could have been signaling by accepting it is a willingness to treat the first document as though it never happened. It is a worse deal at a later date with no compensating advantage. Accepting it therefore functions as a waiver of the rights established by the earlier document.

### Rav Pappa's Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו מִי אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּמוֹדֶה רַב נַחְמָן דְּאִי אוֹסֵיף בֵּיהּ דִּיקְלָא, לְתוֹסֶפֶת כַּתְבֵיהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, הָא אוֹסֵיף לַהּ מִידֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **Was it not stated with regard to** this *halakha* of Rav Naḥman that **Rav Pappa said: And Rav Naḥman concedes that if he added** so much as **a palm tree in** the second document, this shows that **he wrote it as an addition,** and therefore the second document does not cancel the first, but adds to its sum? **Here too, he added something for her,** as the sum of money specified in the second marriage contract is larger than that specified in the first.

Rav Pappa qualifies Rav Nachman's rule: *u-modei Rav Nachman d'i hosif bah dikla l'tosefes kasveih* — Rav Nachman would concede that if the second document includes something additional — the classic example is a palm tree added to the property transferred — then supersession does not apply. When the later document conveys something the earlier one did not, the buyer is not simply accepting a worse version of the first; he has a genuine tradeoff. The later document carries a later lien date but confers an additional benefit. In that case, the two documents stand as competing alternatives, and the buyer may choose which to invoke.

### Sale vs Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת הַיּוֹצְאִין בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה — בִּיטֵּל שֵׁנִי אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹן. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּמוֹדֶה רַב נַחְמָן דְּאִי אוֹסֵיף בֵּיהּ דִּיקְלָא — לְתוֹסֶפֶת כַּתְבֵיהּ. פְּשִׁיטָא רִאשׁוֹן בְּמֶכֶר וְשֵׁנִי בְּמַתָּנָה, לְיַפּוֹת כֹּחוֹ הוּא דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ — מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן רִאשׁוֹן בְּמַתָּנָה וְשֵׁנִי בְּמֶכֶר, דְּאָמְרִינַן מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַעַל חוֹב הוּא דִּכְתַב כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the Gemara has mentioned the statement of Rav Naḥman, it discusses this matter **itself: Rav Naḥman said:** With regard to **two documents that are produced one after the other,** the **second nullifies the first. Rav Pappa said: And Rav Naḥman concedes that if he added a palm tree to it, he wrote it as an addition.** The Gemara analyzes this *halakha* in detail. It **is obvious** that if the **first** document was a document **of a sale, and the second** stated that the same field was given **as a gift,** the second document does not negate the first, as **he wrote** the additional document of a gift **to improve the rights** of the recipient **due to the *halakha*** of **one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. **And all the more so,** this is the *halakha* if he wrote the **first** document **as a gift and the second** in the form **of a sale, as we say** it was **due to the *halakha*** of **a creditor that he wrote** it in **this way.** Out of concern that his creditor might come and snatch the field from the recipient and leave him without redress, he writes a document of sale for the recipient, so that he can return and collect this sum from him.

The *gemara* extends the analysis: *pshita* — it is obvious — that if the first document framed the transfer as a sale and the second as a gift (*rishon be-mecher ve-sheni be-matanah*), the later document does provide a benefit, and choice is available. The benefit derives from *dina de-bar metzra* — the neighbor's right of first refusal. When property is sold, the neighboring owner has a halachic claim to be offered the property before a stranger is approached; but when property is given as a gift, no such claim applies. A person may give a gift to whomever he chooses. So if the second document frames the transfer as a gift, Michael acquires protection against the neighbor's claim under *dina de-bar metzra* — a distinct advantage not available under the first document. The two documents therefore offer different benefits, and Michael may choose between them.

(It is worth noting that in practice, the *dina de-bar metzra* has indeed been applied halachically in modern times: the Heschel School board, when selling its 89th Street building, offered B'nai Jeshurun on 88th Street — a former owner of the building and its neighbor — the right of first refusal, and B'nai Jeshurun ultimately acquired the property.)

The *gemara* adds *ve-chol she-ken* — certainly the reverse also holds: if the first document was a gift and the second was a sale (*rishon be-matanah ve-sheni be-mecher*), the sale document is also the more beneficial in its own way, because a buyer who paid money can claim reimbursement if a creditor seizes the property, whereas a recipient of a gift has no such recourse. In each permutation, the later document carries a distinct advantage, and so Michael retains the right to choose.

One might ask: if the property was already sold in 2012, how can a 2015 document frame it as a gift? The answer is that this is an acknowledged legal fiction, permissible because both parties consent. In theory, Michael could have returned the property to the seller and the seller could then have given it as a gift. Since both parties agree to the framing, halacha allows it to be treated as such. It is, as acknowledged, a somewhat extraordinary construction, but a permissible one within the framework of *mamon*.

### Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי שְׁנֵיהֶם בְּמֶכֶר שְׁנֵיהֶם בְּמַתָּנָה בִּיטֵּל שֵׁנִי אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹן. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַפְרָם אָמַר: אֵימַר אוֹדוֹיֵי אוֹדִי לֵיהּ. רַב אַחָא אָמַר: אֵימַר אַחוֹלֵי אַחְלֵיהּ לְשִׁיעְבּוּדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav Naḥman meant that **if both of them** were documents **of sale** or **both of them** were documents **of gifts,** the **second nullifies the first.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? *Amora'im* argued over this matter. **Rafram said: Say** that the recipient of the field **admitted to him** that the first document was invalid, e.g., it was forged, and he therefore wrote a second, valid document. **Rav Aḥa said: Say** that the recipient **forgave him his lien** from the date of the first document. Consequently, if the seller's creditors collect this field as payment for the debt owed to them, which necessitates the seller reimbursing the buyer for the purchase price of the field, the buyer has a lien only on property owned by the seller from the time of the second document.

Where the second document adds no benefit — both documents are, say, bills of sale for the same property on the same terms — supersession applies in full: *bitel sheni es ha-rishon*. Rav Pappa explains the mechanism: *eimar oduye odi leih* — Rav Pappa attributes this to what functions as an implicit admission (*hoda'as ba'al din*) on Michael's part. By accepting the 2015 document, he is treated as admitting that the 2012 sale never really took effect — that the true date of transfer was 2015. The legal fiction of *hoda'as ba'al din k'me'ah edim dami* — an admission by a party in interest carries the weight of a hundred witnesses — creates this result even if it is factually inaccurate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: אוֹרוֹעֵי סָהֲדֵי. וּלְשַׁלּוֹמֵי פֵּירֵי וּלְטַסְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** these two explanations? The Gemara explains: **The** practical difference **between them** involves several cases. First, there is the issue of whether this serves to **impair** the reliability of the **witnesses:** According to Rafram, who assumes that the first document was of questionable validity, the witnesses who signed on that document are likewise under suspicion, and therefore their testimony and signature in other cases are of questionable value. **And** there is also a difference with regard **to paying** for the **produce** of the property between the dates specified in the two documents. According to Rafram, the transfer of ownership did not take place at the date specified in the first document. Consequently, the recipient of the field must compensate the original owner for the field's produce that he consumed between the two dates. According to Rav Aḥa, the transfer of ownership took place at the time specified in the first document. **And** finally, there is a difference **with regard to** the payment of **the land tax [*taska*].** If the first document was invalid, the previous owner must pay all taxes due during the period between the two documents.

Rav Acha offers a narrower reading: Michael need not be read as denying that the 2012 transaction occurred. Rather, he has simply *waived his liens* — *acholi achlei l'shibudeh* — from the earlier date. The 2012 sale happened; he merely gives up the lien rights that attached from that date.

The practical difference between these two positions — *nafka mina* — is substantial. First, concerning witnesses: if we accept that Michael is effectively denying the 2012 event ever occurred, then he is implicitly treating those witnesses as false. As *Tosafot* explains, this would not impugn those witnesses in any general sense, but it would affect Michael's own rights under any other document he holds bearing the same witnesses' signatures — since he cannot selectively invalidate their testimony only when convenient. Second, concerning the fruits of the property (*lishlumei peiri*): if Michael is merely waiving liens, he retains the benefit of whatever he derived from the property during those three years — he owned it, he ate its fruit. If however the entire 2012 transaction is treated as a fiction that never happened, the seller could demand back-payment for three years of use. Third, regarding taxes (*arnona*): whoever is deemed to have owned the property during those years bears the tax liability for that period. Under the waiver reading, Michael owned it and owes the taxes; under the denial reading, the seller is deemed to have owned it and the tax falls on him.

The obviously more proportionate and factually honest reading is the narrower one — that Michael waives his earlier liens. To treat his acceptance of a later-dated document as a denial of the very witnesses who signed the earlier one, with cascading consequences for all his other documents bearing those signatures, is a remarkably far-reaching implication of what appears to be a relatively modest act.

## Ketuva Application

### Summary & Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ דִּכְתוּבָּה? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָנֶה מָאתַיִם מִן הָאֵירוּסִין, וְתוֹסֶפֶת מִן הַנִּישּׂוּאִין. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה מִן הַנִּישּׂוּאִין. וְהִלְכְתָא: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה מִן הַנִּישּׂוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The above discussion came in the wake of the dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Asi with regard to whether the lien on a husband's property to ensure payment of his wife's marriage contract applies from the time of betrothal or the time of marriage. The Gemara returns to that issue. **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter of **a marriage contract?** The Gemara responds: **Come** and **hear** the following ruling, **as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon:** The lien on his property with regard to the **one hundred dinars** or **two hundred** dinars that comprise the basic sum of a marriage contract applies **from** the time of **the betrothal, and the** lien with regard to the **additional** sum applies **from** the time of **the marriage. And the Rabbis say:** The lien with regard to **both this and that** takes effect only **from** the time of **the marriage.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that with regard to **both this and that,** the lien takes effect **from** the time of **the marriage,** in accordance with the majority opinion of the Rabbis.

The *gemara* now returns to the original *ketuva* lien question and reaches its conclusion. *Tosafot* address the issue at this point. The position of Rav Huna — splitting between the *ikar ketuva* with liens from *erusin* and the *tosefet* with liens from *nisuin* — is contrasted with the position of the *chachamim*. The *chachamim* rule: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh min ha-nisu'in* — both the core *ketuva* and the *tosefet* carry liens only from *nisuin*. And the *halacha* follows the *chachamim*: when the *ketuva* is written at the time of *nisuin*, the old liens from *erusin* do not survive, and all lien rights are anchored to the moment of the *ketuva*'s writing. Whatever obligations existed at the time of *erusin* do not continue to generate liens into the period following *nisuin*.

## Motzi Shem Ra Intro

### Torah Case Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגִּיּוֹרֶת שֶׁנִּתְגַּיְּירָה בִּתָּהּ עִמָּהּ וְזִינְּתָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּחֶנֶק, אֵין לָהּ לֹא פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב, וְלֹא מֵאָה סֶלַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a female **convert whose daughter converted with her and** later, as a young woman, the daughter **engaged in licentious sexual relations** when she was betrothed, **she is** executed **by strangulation,** not stoning, the method of execution that would be employed had she been born Jewish. **She has neither** the *halakha* of being executed at the **entrance to** her **father's house,** as in the case of a woman who was born Jewish who committed this crime, **nor** does she receive **one hundred *sela*** if her husband defames her by falsely claiming that she had committed adultery. The reason is that the verses state "Israel" (Deuteronomy 22:19, 21) with regard to these *halakhot*, indicating that these *halakhot* apply only to those born as Jews.

With the *ketuva* discussion concluded, the *gemara* pivots to the next *mishna*, which opens a new subject — *motzi shem ra*. It is worth appreciating how these topics connect. The *ketuva* as a Rabbinic institution traces its origins to the Torah's cases involving young girls and marriage-related payments. The *ones* and *mefateh* cases established the 50 *shekel* fine — itself rooted in the societal reality of *mohar*, the bride-price — and that fine became the template for the *ketuva*'s base amount. Now the *gemara* turns to the second Torah case in this constellation: the *motzi shem ra*, where after the wedding night a husband falsely claims his wife was not a virgin.

The Torah's framework for *motzi shem ra* is as follows. If a husband makes this claim and it proves true — if she in fact committed adultery as a *na'arah betulah me'orasah* — she is executed by *sekilah* at *petach beit aviha*, the entrance of her father's house. The Torah specifies: *ki astah nevalah b'Yisrael* — because she committed an outrage in Israel. The location — the father's house — and the mode of execution — *sekilah* rather than the *chenek* that normally applies to adultery — are the distinctive features of this case. If, on the other hand, the husband lied, he receives *malkus* and a fine of *me'ah kesef* — 100 silver pieces, double the standard *ketuva* amount of 50 — which is given *l'avi ha-na'arah*, to the girl's father. This doubling is no accident: he attempted to have her killed and to deprive her of her *ketuva* (equivalent to 50 *kesef*), and so he pays double. He also loses the right ever to divorce her.

### Giyoret Exception

Rather than beginning with the standard case, the *mishna* opens with an unusual exception: *hagiores she-nisgayra bita ima* — a convert woman who converted together with her daughter, whose daughter then committed adultery as a *na'arah me'orasah*.

### Conceived Pre-Conv

The first scenario: the daughter was conceived before the mother converted and was also born before the conversion. In this case none of the special laws of *motzi shem ra* apply to her at all. She did not begin life as a *bat Yisrael*; the Torah's formulation *ki astah nevalah b'Yisrael* excludes her.

### Born Post-Conv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה הוֹרָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְדוּשָּׁה וְלֵידָתָהּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה — הֲרֵי זוֹ בִּסְקִילָה, וְאֵין לָהּ לֹא פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב, וְלֹא מֵאָה סֶלַע. הָיְתָה הוֹרָתָהּ וְלֵידָתָהּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, הֲרֵי הִיא כְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכׇל דְּבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** However, if the daughter's **conception occurred** when her mother **was not** yet **in** a state of **sanctity,** i.e., when she was still a gentile, **but her birth** took place when her mother was **in** a state of **sanctity,** as her mother converted during her pregnancy, **this** daughter is punishable **by stoning** if she committed adultery as a betrothed young woman. However, **she has neither** the *halakha* of being executed at the **entrance to** her **father's house, nor** the right to **one hundred *sela*** if it turns out that her husband defamed her. If **her conception and birth occurred** when her mother **was in** a state of **sanctity,** i.e., after she converted, **she is like a** regular **Jewish woman in all matters.**

The second scenario: the daughter was conceived before the mother converted, but was born after the conversion — *horasa shelo bi-kedusha ve-lidasa bi-kedusha*. In this case she is executed by *sekilah* — because she was born as a Jew, and *b'Yisrael* is satisfied — but *ein lah petach beit av*, there is no father's house to bring her to, and *ein lah me'ah sela*, there is no fine of one hundred *sela*. She has no legal father, so those elements of the *motzi shem ra* framework simply do not apply. She receives *sekilah*, but without the full ritual specified in the Torah.

The third scenario: both conception and birth occurred after the mother's conversion. In this case *harei hi ke-vat Yisrael le-chol devareha* — she is treated in every respect as a daughter of Israel, and all the normal laws apply.

### Father Deceased

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ לָהּ אָב וְאֵין לָהּ פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב, יֵשׁ לָהּ פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב וְאֵין לָהּ אָב — הֲרֵי זוֹ בִּסְקִילָה. לֹא נֶאֱמַר פֶּתַח בֵּית אָב אֶלָּא לְמִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** If a young woman who is betrothed commits adultery and **she has a father but does not have an entrance to** her **father's house,** i.e., if her father does not possess a house of his own, or if **she has an entrance to** her **father's house but does not have a father,** as he has passed away, **she is** nevertheless executed **via stoning, as** the requirement that she is to be executed at the **entrance to her father's house is stated only for a mitzva** but it is not an indispensable requirement.

The fourth case introduced by the *mishna*: a daughter who does have a legal father, but whose father has died — she is an orphan. The *mishna* states *harei zeh bi-sekilah* — she receives *sekilah*. The *mishna* notes that *lo ne'emar petach beit av ela l'mitzvah* — the requirement of the father's house is only a *mitzvah* (an ideal, not an absolute requirement), and so its absence does not block the execution. What the *mishna* does not clarify, however, is whether the *me'ah kesef* fine applies in the case of an orphan. That question is left open.

### Torah's Law

To summarize the Torah's law as it applies to the standard case: the subject is a *na'arah betulah me'orasah l'ish* — a young woman, a virgin, betrothed to a man. He claims *lo nimtze'u vetulim la-na'arah* — signs of virginity were not found in her. If the claim is true and she committed adultery during her *erusin*, *ve-hotzi'u et ha-na'arah el petach beit aviha u-sekaluha* — she is brought to the entrance of her father's house and stoned. The punishment is *sekilah* rather than *chenek* because the Torah treats this as a more severe form of adultery — she was still under her father's roof, still in the period of *erusin*. If the husband lied — *moti shem ra* — he receives *malkus* and pays *me'ah kesef*, double the *ketuva* value of fifty *kesef*, given to the girl's father.

The fine of *me'ah kesef* is explicitly structured as *kefel* — a doubling. He was trying both to kill her and to cause her to lose her *ketuva* entitlement of fifty *kesef*; therefore he pays double that amount in fine.

### Orphan's Fine

The *mishna* has told us that an orphan girl receives *sekilah* but has left us without a ruling on whether the *me'ah kesef* fine applies in her case. Since the Torah says the fine is given *l'avi ha-na'arah* — to the girl's father — and she has no father, it is unclear whether the fine exists at all, and if so to whom it would be paid. This open question — whether an orphaned daughter receives the *me'ah kesef* — is what the *gemara* will take up in the following discussion.
