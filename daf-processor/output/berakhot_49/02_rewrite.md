# Berakhot 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## HaTov VeHaMeitiv

### Debate Setup

The *gemara* on 49a opens with a dispute between Abba Yosi ben Dostai and the Rabbis concerning the blessing of *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*, the fourth blessing of *birkat ha-mazon*. The *gemara* introduces the debate with the words *v'Rabbanan*, appearing approximately twelve lines down on 49a. One authority holds that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* requires *malchus* — meaning the full formula of *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam* — while the other holds it does not.

### Malchus Requirement

It is important to clarify what is meant here by *malchus*: this is not *malchus beit David*, the kingship of the House of David, which we encountered in connection with *boneh Yerushalayim*, but rather *malchus shamayim* — the phrase *melech ha-olam* in the opening of a *bracha*. The question is whether *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* must be introduced with *Elokeinu melech ha-olam* or whether a shorter formula suffices.

The *gemara* explains the logic of each position. The view that *malchus* is required holds that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is *d'Rabbanan* — a rabbinic addition — and therefore stands as an independent *bracha*, not organically connected to the earlier blessings of *birkat ha-mazon*. As an independent *bracha*, it requires its own *Elokeinu melech ha-olam*. The view that *malchus* is not required holds that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is *d'Oraisa* — that is, it was part of the original structure of *benching* — and is therefore *samuch l'chavertah*, attached to and following from the preceding *brachot*.

### Samuch L'Chavertah

This is the operative literary principle: a *bracha samuch l'chavertah* — one juxtaposed to and continuous with a preceding *bracha* — does not require its own *Elokeinu melech ha-olam*. The *malchus* invoked in the opening *bracha* carries forward through the entire chain. This is familiar from *shemoneh esrei*, where *baruch atah Hashem rofe cholei amo Yisrael*, *baruch atah Hashem go'el Yisrael*, and all subsequent *brachot* carry no *melech ha-olam*, because they all link back to the opening *bracha* in which *malchus* was established. The theme of *malchus* is present by implication; to repeat it would not be *l'vatalah* because it is understood as continuous. The principle, then, is that when a *bracha* was originally established as part of a sequence, the *malchus* of the opening *bracha* is considered to carry through; when a *bracha* stands independently, it requires its own.

### Application to Benching

An instructive parallel is *birkat ha-Torah*. The first *bracha* opens with *Elokeinu melech ha-olam* and closes with *ha-melamed Torah l'amo Yisrael*. The subsequent *bracha*, beginning with a fresh *baruch*, is treated as a new independent *bracha* and therefore opens again with *Elokeinu melech ha-olam asher bachar banu*, closing with *baruch atah Hashem nosein ha-Torah*. It is not treated as *samuch l'chavertah*, and accordingly requires its own *malchus*. The same logic applies throughout *benching*: the three core *brachot* form an original and continuous sequence; *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*, if understood as a later rabbinic appendage, falls outside that sequence and must stand on its own.

## Boneh Yerushalayim

### Chatimah Debate

The *gemara* turns to a *baraita*: *mah hu chosem b'vinyan Yerushalayim* — what is the closing formula, the *chatimah*, of the blessing of *boneh Yerushalayim*? The third blessing opens with *racheim Hashem Elokeinu al Yisrael amecha v'al Yerushalayim irecha*. The question is how it should end. Rabbi Yosi the son of Rabbi Yehuda says one ends with *moshia Yisrael* — savior of Israel. This is a striking position, because it was mentioned earlier that one who says *moshia Yisrael* is an ignoramus. Yet here a named *tanna* endorses precisely that formula, which confirms that this *nusach* had genuine traction and could not simply be dismissed.

### Both Themes

The *gemara* challenges: does he mean *moshia Yisrael* to the exclusion of *boneh Yerushalayim*? It answers: *ella eima af moshia Yisrael* — even *moshia Yisrael*. This formulation can be read either as permitting both *moshia Yisrael u-voneh Yerushalayim* together, or as allowing either formula. Rashi reads it as meaning that one may choose either, with both *boneh Yerushalayim* and *moshia Yisrael* being acceptable options. The *gemara*'s own tendency, as we have seen, favors the explicit *boneh Yerushalayim*.

The emphasis on *moshia Yisrael* may reflect a post-*churban* sensibility: from the perspective of *galut*, the restoration of the Jewish people may feel more urgent and immediate than the rebuilding of Jerusalem. A parallel can be drawn to the *bracha* recited after reading *Megilat Esther* — *ha'el ha-moshia* — which is similarly diaspora-oriented in its framing. In *shemoneh esrei* as well, the sequence runs from *mekabetz nidchei Yisrael* to *boneh Yerushalayim* and then to *matzmiach keren yeshuah*, which refers to the house of David — these themes are clearly interconnected.

### One Theme Rule

Rabbah bar Rav Huna once came to the house of the *Reish Galuta*, opened with one theme and closed with two — presumably saying both *moshia Yisrael u-voneh Yerushalayim* in the *chatimah*. Rav Chisda challenged him: is it such a small matter to end with two themes? For we have a *baraita* in which Rabbi says *ein chotmin b'shtayim* — one does not close a *chatimah* with two themes. The underlying principle is *ein osin mitzvos chavilos chavilos* — one does not bundle *mitzvos* together; each deserves its own proper focus.

It is worth noting that the blessing itself opens with multiple themes — *racheim... al Yisrael amecha v'al Yerushalayim irecha* — yet closes with only one. The opening may be expansive, but the *chatimah* must be singular. This creates the problem: if the blessing addresses both the salvation of Israel and the building of Jerusalem, which theme should the closing formula reflect?

### Exceptions

The *gemara* tests the rule. Levi challenged Rabbi with examples that appear to use two themes in a *chatimah*: *al ha-aretz v'al ha-mazon*, *al ha-aretz v'al ha-peirot*, *mekadesh Yisrael v'ha-zmanim*, *mekadesh Yisrael v'roshei chodashim*. Rabbi answered each: *aretz d'mapka mazon* — the land that brings forth the food; *aretz d'mapka peirot* — the land that brings forth the fruit; *Yisrael d'kadshinhu l'zmanim* — Israel who sanctifies the times by establishing the calendar. In each case, what appears as two themes resolves into one: one entity, one act, one attribution to God.

The case of *mekadesh ha-Shabbat v'Yisrael v'ha-zmanim*, which combines *Shabbat*, Israel, and the *moadim*, cannot be similarly resolved. Here the *gemara* says simply *chutz mi-zo* — this is an exception — and explains *hacha chada hi, hatam tarti*: here it is one, there it is two. Rashi explains that the formula means simply that God sanctifies — *hamekadesh* — and that single verb applies alike to *Shabbat*, to Israel, and to the *moadim*. It is one divine act of sanctification applied to multiple objects. By contrast, *moshia Yisrael u-voneh Yerushalayim* posits two distinct divine acts — redemption and construction — and is therefore genuinely *tarti*, two themes.

The objection was raised that *moshia Yisrael* and *boneh Yerushalayim* could similarly be treated as a single theme — the salvation of Israel leads to the building of Jerusalem, just as the land bringing forth food is one concept. But as the *gemara* makes clear, this analogy does not hold: in the other cases, one is ascribing a single action to God, with the second element being an effect or instrument of the first. Here, one is ascribing two distinct actions to God — saving Israel and building Jerusalem — making them genuinely two themes that should not be bundled.

The *gemara* closes this discussion with the principle: *lefi she-ein osin mitzvos chavilos chavilos* — one does not bundle *mitzvos* together.

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet offers a practical resolution. The *chatimah* should mirror the opening theme. If one opened with *racheim al amcha Yisrael* — emphasizing the salvation of the people — one closes with *moshia Yisrael*. If one opened with *racheim al Yerushalayim* — emphasizing the city — one closes with *boneh Yerushalayim*. This is a notable position: it allows for the possibility that the entire *bracha*, from opening to closing, focuses not on Jerusalem but solely on the redemption of Israel, without any mention of *binyan Yerushalayim* in the *chatimah*. Given the *gemara*'s insistence that the land's goodness is bound up with the presence of the *Beit Ha-Mikdash*, this is a striking leniency.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman disagrees. Even if one opened with *racheim al Yisrael* — even when the opening theme is the people's salvation — one must still close with *boneh Yerushalayim*. This is our *nusach*. The basis is the verse: *boneh Yerushalayim Hashem nidchei Yisrael yekaneis* — God builds Jerusalem and gathers in the dispersed of Israel.

### Geulah Themes

The *gemara* interprets this verse to mean: *eimatai boneh Yerushalayim Hashem? Bizman she-nidchei Yisrael yekaneis* — when does God build Jerusalem? When He gathers in the dispersed of Israel. On this reading, the building of Jerusalem is the culmination of the ingathering of exiles; one opens with the salvation of Israel and closes with *boneh Yerushalayim* because the latter is the ultimate goal to which the former leads.

Rashi, however, reads the verse differently — that *boneh Yerushalayim* comes first, and *nidchei Yisrael yekaneis* follows from it. On Rashi's reading, the restoration of Jerusalem is not the culmination but the beginning of the process of Israel's salvation. The *chatimah* of *boneh Yerushalayim* is therefore connected to the opening theme of *moshia Yisrael* because the building of Jerusalem is what initiates the *tishu'at Yisrael*. This is not an easy reading of the *gemara*'s plain sense, which suggests the ingathering precedes the building. But Rashi's approach allows the *chatimah* to be seen as *me'ein ha-ptichah* — thematically continuous with the opening — even as the precise sequence of events is understood differently.

The connection between these themes and *birkat ha-mazon*'s agricultural context is suggestive. The imagery of planting runs through the biblical theology of Israel's relationship to its land — *zera Yisrael* — and the verb *matzmiach* appears in *shemoneh esrei* in *matzmiach keren yeshuah* regarding *malchut beit David*, and also in *mechalkel chayim be-chesed mechayyeh meitim matzmiach yeshuah*, where it appears without explicit reference to David. These are themes the *Chachamim* may have been building upon, though the *gemara* does not make the connection explicit.

The double *chatimah* of *Tisha B'Av* — *menachem Tziyon u-voneh Yerushalayim* — raises the question of *ein chotmin b'shtayim* in a different context. The answer, along the lines of what the *gemara* offers here, would be that *menachem Tziyon* is accomplished *through* the *boneh Yerushalayim*, rendering them sufficiently connected to be treated as one theme. More broadly, the *gemara* here treats the requirement that the *chatimah* mirror the *ptichah* with some flexibility — the connection can be one of thematic continuation rather than verbal identity. The stronger general principle tends to be that the *chatimah* should mirror the line immediately preceding it, which is why the insertions of *ya'aleh v'yavo* and *retzei* are placed just before the final line *u-veneh Yerushalayim ir ha-kodesh bimherah ve-yameinu*, so that the *bracha* closes naturally into *boneh Yerushalayim*.

## Rav Chisda Incident

### Missing Elements

The *gemara* now relates a significant incident. Rav Zeira said to Rav Chisda: come and teach us some *mishnayot*. Rav Chisda replied: *birchat mezona lo gamrina* — I don't even know *benching*, and you want me to teach you *mishnayot*? Rav Zeira pressed: what do you mean? Rav Chisda explained that he had once eaten at the house of the *Reish Galuta*, had led *birkat ha-mazon*, and Rav Sheshet raised himself against him like a serpent poised to strike — a dramatic expression of fury and censure. The reason: Rav Chisda had omitted *brit*, *Torah*, and *malchus* from the *benching*. Here *malchus* refers not to *melech ha-olam* but to *malchus beit David*, which we established earlier must be mentioned in *boneh Yerushalayim*. Together, *brit*, *Torah*, and *malchus beit David* are required elements of the third blessing, and Rav Chisda had not mentioned any of them.

### Rav Chananel

Rav Chisda explained that he had acted in accordance with the position of Rav Chananel citing Rav: *lo amar brit Torah u-malchut yatza* — if one did not say *brit*, *Torah*, or *malchus*, one has nevertheless fulfilled the obligation.

### Universal Application

The reasoning offered is striking: *brit* does not apply to women, who are not obligated in *brit milah*; *Torah* and *malchus* do not apply to women and slaves, who are not obligated in *talmud Torah* in the classical formulation, and for whom the laws of kingship do not apply in the same way. Since these elements are not universally applicable to all those obligated in *birkat ha-mazon*, they cannot be considered indispensable to the text. The implication is that a required component of a *bracha* must be one that is meaningful and applicable to every person obligated in that *bracha*.

One of the *Rishonim* — understood to be the Rashba — goes further and says that Rav Chisda intentionally adopted a text that was universal, applicable to all, rather than reciting a formula that excluded women and slaves. On this reading, it is not merely that he ruled these elements were not *me'akev*, but that he positively sought a *nusach* that would serve all people equally. This explains why Rav Chisda did not simply say the fuller text as a preferred option even if its omission is not fatal — because he wanted his *benching* to be universally valid in form, not only in effect.

It is worth noting that our standard text says *al brisecha she-chasamta bi-vsareinu*, a formulation that explicitly references *brit milah*. Some manuscripts — including versions noted in the *Gra*'s textual tradition — do not include the words *she-chasamta bi-vsareinu*, reading only *al brisecha*, which would allow women to be included in the general covenant with God without the specific reference to circumcision. The *gemara* itself, while it begins with *brit* in a general sense, quickly connects it to *yud-gimel britot* at the top of the *daf*, referring to *brit milah*, and states explicitly that the requirement does not apply to women — *einam b'nashim* — which strongly implies that the text containing *she-chasamta bi-vsareinu* was at least understood to refer to *brit milah*. Whether the *exact* text included that phrase is a separate question the *gemara* does not resolve.

This discussion connects to the broader debate raised approximately a month earlier in the *masechta* about the level of women's obligation in *birkat ha-mazon* — whether they are obligated *d'Oraisa* or only *d'Rabbanan*. Rashi there points to this very *gemara*, noting that the exclusion of women from *brit milah* and from receiving a portion in the land may bear on the question of their biblical obligation. The *gemara* never spells this out definitively, but the connections are there in the background. The question of *gerim* is also raised — *Tosafot* discusses whether converts, who similarly lack ancestral portions in the land, stand in a different position.

When it comes to the precise legal effect of omitting these elements, it is also worth noting that even when the *gemara* says *lo yatza*, this sometimes functions rhetorically rather than as a definitive ruling of non-fulfillment, and even a literal ruling of *lo yatza* may mean only *d'Rabbanan* and not *d'Oraisa*.

### Challenge

Rav Zeira responded with a pointed challenge: *v'at shavkat kol hanei tannai v'amorai v'pashkat k'Rav* — you abandoned all the *tannaim* and *amoraim* who required these elements, in order to follow the single opinion of Rav? This is precisely what had enraged Rav Sheshet: Rav Chisda had adopted a minority position against a broad consensus of earlier authorities who held that these elements were required and that their omission rendered the *benching* invalid.

## Double Malchus

### Malchus Rule

The *gemara* now returns to the question of *malchus* in *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* — here *malchus* in the sense of *melech ha-olam* — consistent with the earlier conclusion that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is an independent *bracha* and therefore requires *Elokeinu melech ha-olam*. The *gemara* invokes the principle already stated by Rabbi Yochanan: *kol bracha she-ein bah malchus lo shema bracha* — any *bracha* without *malchus* is not properly a *bracha*. But this principle was already stated once by Rabbi Yochanan. What then is the novel teaching here?

### Double Malchus Theory

Rav Zeira proposes: *lomar she-tzricha shtei malchuyot* — to say that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* requires two expressions of *malchus*. One for itself — *chada didei* — and one in place of *boneh Yerushalayim* — *chada d'Vanei Yerushalayim* — since *boneh Yerushalayim* closes with *baruch atah Hashem boneh Yerushalayim* or *be-rachamav boneh Yerushalayim*, without *melech ha-olam*. Since that *malchus* was missing in *boneh Yerushalayim*, it is compensated for in *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*, which contains *ha-melech ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv la-kol*. This is reminiscent of Tuesday in the creation narrative receiving two *ki tov* because Monday had none.

### Objection

An objection is raised: if we are compensating for a missing *malchus*, should we not also compensate for *birkat ha-aretz*, which similarly omits *melech ha-olam*? That would require three expressions of *malchus* in *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* — one for itself, one for *boneh Yerushalayim*, and one for *birkat ha-aretz*.

### Response

The *gemara* responds: *birkat ha-aretz* requires no such compensation because *mishum d'havya leh bracha semucha l'chavertah* — it is a *bracha samuch l'chavertah*, attached to the opening *bracha* which carried *malchus*. It was never independently required to have its own *malchus*, so there is nothing to compensate for. Extending the logic: *boneh Yerushalayim* is also *samuch l'chavertah*, and by the same reasoning should require no compensation either. The *gemara* accepts this: *hu ha-din d'afilu Bonei Yerushalayim nami lo ba'ei* — fine, even *boneh Yerushalayim* did not strictly require *malchus*.

### Rav Papa

Rav Papa then reframes the entire matter. The two expressions of *malchus* in *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* are not a compensation for missing *malchus* in earlier *brachot*. Rather, *tzricha shtei malchuyot l'var mididei* — in addition to the *malchus* for itself, there are two more within the *bracha*. The text of *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* already contains *ha-melech ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv la-kol*, and additionally *el avinu malkenu* — so we have *melech* for *boneh Yerushalayim* and *malkeinu* for *birkat ha-aretz*, paying back the missing *malchus* of both earlier *brachot* simultaneously. This is a structurally elegant resolution that ties all four *brachot* together.

## Forgotten Insertions

### Shabbat Insertion

Rav Zeira was sitting behind Rav Giddel, who was sitting before Rav Huna. Rav Giddel was relaying teachings from Rav Huna, serving as an intermediary. He taught: if one forgot to mention *Shabbat* in *boneh Yerushalayim*, one recites a separate *bracha*: *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam asher natan shabbasos li-mnucha l'amo Yisrael b'ahava l'os u-le-vrit, baruch atah Hashem mekadesh ha-Shabbat*. This *bracha* stands alone with its own *Elokeinu melech ha-olam* because it is not said *samuch* to the rest of the *benching*; it is being inserted after the fact and must therefore stand independently. Note that the *chatimah* — *mekadesh ha-Shabbat* — does not repeat *Elokeinu melech ha-olam*, consistent with the rule that a closing formula need not do so. When Rav Zeira asked who had said this, Rav Giddel replied: Rav. This *bracha* is printed in some *siddurim* and benchers, though unfortunately many benchers omit it.

### Yom Tov Insertion

Rav Giddel continued: if one forgot *Yom Tov*, one recites: *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam asher natan yamim tovim l'amo Yisrael le-simcha u-le-zikaron, baruch atah Hashem mekadesh Yisrael ve-ha-zmanim*. Again said in the name of Rav. The *nusach* in our benchers differs slightly: it mentions the specific *Yom Tov* by name, says *le-sason u-le-simcha* rather than *le-simcha u-le-zikaron*, and does not include *zikaron*. These are textual variants between our received *nusach* and the *gemara*'s formulation.

### Rosh Chodesh Insertion

Rav Giddel then taught: if one forgot *Rosh Chodesh*, one says *baruch she-natan rashei chodashim l'amo Yisrael le-zikaron*. Here, unlike *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, the formula has no *simcha* theme — only *zikaron*. Rav Zeira adds that he was uncertain on several points: whether the *bracha* included *simcha*, whether it included a *chatimah* at all — since a short *bracha* does not necessarily require a closing formula, just as *al pri ha-etz* and *borei nefashot* have no *chatimah* — and whether Rav Giddel said this in his own name or in the name of Rav. The *nusach* we have for *Rosh Chodesh* is: *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam asher natan rashei chodashim l'amo Yisrael le-zikaron* — no *chatimah*, no *simcha* — which happens to correspond exactly to what was said, without resolution of the uncertainties.

## Chozer L'Rosh

### Nachman's Ruling

Giddel bar Minyumi was present before Rav Nachman when Rav Nachman himself forgot — apparently omitting *ya'aleh v'yavo* or *retzei* — and returned to the very beginning of *benching*, *hadar le-reisha*. When asked why, Rav Nachman cited a ruling: *ta'ah chozer le-rosh* — if one erred, one returns to the beginning. This was said in the name of Rav — the same Rav in whose name the special *bracha* was just taught. An apparent contradiction.

### Before HaTov VeHaMeitiv

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction with a qualification attributed to Minyumi bar Chalifa in the name of Rav: *lo shanu ela she-lo patach b'ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* — the ruling to say the special *bracha* applies only if one has not yet begun *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*. *Aval patach b'ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv, chozer le-rosh* — if one has already begun *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*, one must return to the beginning of *benching*.

This is because *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* marks the end of the core *benching*. Once one has crossed into that *bracha*, one has left the essential framework of *birkat ha-mazon*. The insertion of *retzei* or *ya'aleh v'yavo* belongs within *boneh Yerushalayim*; once one has moved definitively beyond it, there is no way to retroactively correct the omission without repeating the whole. The question of what constitutes "beginning" *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is significant: according to the *Acharonim*, saying *ha'el avinu* — the first substantive words of that *bracha* — crosses the threshold. If one has said only *baruch atah Hashem Elokeinu melech ha-olam*, one may pivot and end with *asher natan shabbasos li-mnucha* rather than continuing into *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*.

### When to Repeat

The analogy to *shemoneh esrei* is instructive. If one forgets *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-geshem* or recites it incorrectly, one does not return only to that *bracha* but to the very beginning of *shemoneh esrei*. The first three *brachot* function as a unified structure; an error within that unit requires restarting the whole. Similarly in *benching*, the three core *brachot* are a unit; if the required insertion in *boneh Yerushalayim* was omitted and one has already moved past that structure, the entire unit must be repeated. Note that this is an entirely rabbinic requirement — biblically, the precise *nusach* and the obligation to mention special days are rabbinic; the biblical requirement is satisfied by any expression of gratitude for food. The rabbinic structure, however, is what governs most of our practical ritual life.

## Rosh Chodesh Rule

### Shmuel's Teaching

The *gemara* cites a ruling of Shmuel, transmitted through a chain of Rav Nachman: if one forgot *Rosh Chodesh* in *tefillah* — in *shemoneh esrei* — one must repeat. But if one forgot it in *birkat ha-mazon*, one does not repeat.

### Reasoning

When Rav Amram was asked by Rav Avin for the reason behind this distinction, he replied that he too had been puzzled and had asked Rav Nachman, who told him that he had not heard the reason from Shmuel himself. They therefore worked it out independently: *tefillah de-chova hi* — prayer is an obligation; one is required to *daven* today regardless. The proper *nusach* for today includes the *Rosh Chodesh* insertion; if one omitted it, one has not fulfilled today's obligatory prayer and must repeat. *Birkat mezona de'i ba'ei achil, i ba'ei lo achil* — *birkat ha-mazon*, by contrast, arises only when one eats; no one was obligated to eat today specifically. Since the meal itself was optional, the *benching* associated with it is not *chova* in the same way, and the omission of *Rosh Chodesh* does not require repetition.

### Distinction

The *gemara* then challenges: by that logic, on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, where one is obligated to have a *seudah*, the *benching* should function like *tefillah* and require repetition if the *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* theme was omitted. The answer is yes — and this is precisely the ruling of Rav: *ta'ah chozer le-rosh*, one returns to the beginning, qualified only by the caveat that one has not yet begun *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*. The two positions attributed to Rav are therefore not contradictory: the special *bracha* suffices on days where there is no *chiyuv seudah*; on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, where the meal is obligatory, repeating the *benching* is required when the special insertion was forgotten.

This also explains why *al ha-nissim* on *Purim* and *Chanukah* is not *me'akev*. On *Purim* there is a *mishteh*, but the question of whether it constitutes a fully obligatory *seudah* involving bread is debated, and in any case the obligation is not of the same character. On *Chanukah* there is no *chiyuv seudah* at all. Since neither day obligates a meal in the same way *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* do, the *benching* for those days does not carry the same *chiyuv*, and the omission of *al ha-nissim* does not require repetition.

The question arises about *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh*: one is obligated to eat on *Shabbat*, and if one forgot *Rosh Chodesh* in *benching* on *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh*, must one go back? The *halacha* is that one does not — which is somewhat surprising given that the same reasoning which would not apply to *Rosh Chodesh* alone should perhaps not change simply because it coincides with *Shabbat*. This distinction between *Rosh Chodesh* falling on *Shabbat* and the parallel question in *shemoneh esrei* warrants further analysis — *tzarich iyun*.

## Minimum Amount

### Textual Basis

The *gemara* returns to the question of how much one must eat to be obligated in *zimmun* and *birkat ha-mazon*. The underlying debate turns on how to read the verse *v'achalta v'savata u-verachta* — you shall eat and be satisfied and bless. Rabbi Meir reads *v'achalta* as referring to eating and *v'savata* as referring to drinking — not full satiation but an accompaniment to eating that completes the act. On this reading, any significant act of eating triggers the obligation. Rabbi Yehuda reads *v'achalta v'savata* together as describing an eating that itself produces satiation — *achila she-yesh ba savia* — an eating that is inherently filling.

### Meir vs Yehuda

From this, in the context of the *mishnah* here, Rabbi Meir holds that a *kezayit* — an olive's volume — is sufficient for *birkat ha-mazon* and to join a *zimmun*, while Rabbi Yehuda requires a *kebeitzah* — an egg's volume. These measurements stand in an approximately two-to-three ratio, with a *kezayit* at roughly three and a half fluid ounces and a *kebeitzah* at six to ten ounces.

### Parallel Cases

However, the *gemara* notes that in the *mishnah* in *Pesachim* — which discusses when one must return to Jerusalem to burn *basar kodesh* that was inadvertently taken outside the city, and when one may burn it where one stands — the positions appear reversed. Rabbi Meir there says one returns for a *kebeitzah*, and Rabbi Yehuda says one returns for a *kezayit*. This reversal creates an apparent contradiction with the positions in our *mishnah*.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves it simply: *mukhlefet ha-shitah* — the names are transposed; simply switch them. Abaye rejects this approach. He says: *le'olam lo teipoch* — do not reverse them. The positions are internally consistent; the two debates are simply about different things.

### Meir's Reasoning

In the context of *birkat ha-mazon* and *zimmun*, the *amoraim* explain that the dispute turns on the biblical text. Rabbi Meir holds *v'achalta zo achila, v'savata zo shtiya* — the verse addresses eating accompanied by drinking, and an act of eating is measured at a *kezayit*, the standard *shiur* for eating. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the verse requires an eating that produces satiation, which is the measure of a *kebeitzah*.

In the context of the *Pesachim* case — when to return for meat or *chametz* — the dispute is not about biblical texts but about analogy. Rabbi Meir holds *chazarato k'tumato* — returning for the meat is analogous to the *shiur* for *tumah*, and *tumah* from food requires a *kebeitzah*. Therefore one returns for a *kebeitzah*.

### Yehuda's Reasoning

Rabbi Yehuda holds *chazarato k'issuro* — returning is analogous to the *issur* of eating invalid *kodshim*, and the *shiur* for that *issur* is a *kezayit*. Therefore one returns for a *kezayit*. The cases of *chametz* and *kodshim* track together under one or the other model. It may be that each *tanna* took a different case as the paradigm — one anchoring the analysis in *tumah*, the other in *issur* — and then applied it to both.

It should be noted that the framing of this *gemara* is striking: it sounds as though the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda over *kezayit* and *kebeitzah* in *birkat ha-mazon* may be a biblical-level dispute. Other *gemarot* have suggested that the biblical obligation requires actual satiation, with *kezayit* and *kebeitzah* being rabbinic *shiurim*. Here, however, the *gemara* derives these measures directly from *v'achalta v'savata*, suggesting that even a *kezayit* or *kebeitzah* could create a biblical obligation. On Rabbi Meir's reading, the emphasis is on the *act* of eating rather than on being filled — any eating of a *kezayit* is halakhically significant. On Rabbi Yehuda's reading, the requirement is for an eating that has the *potential* to be filling — even if it did not actually satiate in this instance, the volume of a *kebeitzah* is inherently the kind of eating that could.

## Zimmun Text

### Three People

The *mishnah* now sets out the text of *zimmun* — the formal invitation to join in *birkat ha-mazon* — according to the number of participants. With three people, the leader says *nevarech she-achalnu mishelo* — let us bless the One from Whose food we have eaten. If the leader himself is one of the three, he says *nevarech*, including himself in the group. If there are three others and he is an additional participant, he may say *barchu* — bless — calling upon the group without needing to include himself.

### Ten People

With ten people, *Elokeinu* is added: *nevarech Elokeinu she-achalnu mishelo*. The addition of God's name reflects the heightened sanctity of a larger assembly. The same distinction between *nevarech* and *barchu* applies when the leader is or is not counted among the ten.

### Hundred People

At a hundred, *Hashem* is added: *nevarech Hashem Elokeinu she-achalnu mishelo*. The divine name is explicitly invoked as the assembly grows.

### Thousand People

At a thousand, one says *nevarech l'Hashem Elokeinu Elokei Yisrael* — God of Israel is added, reflecting the sense that so large a gathering of Israel represents the people as a whole in its collective identity.

### Ten Thousand

At ten thousand, the full formula is: *nevarech l'Hashem Elokeinu Elokei Yisrael Elokei Tzevaot Yoshev Ha-Keruvim al ha-mazon she-achalanu mishelo* — God of Hosts Who dwells upon the cherubim, for the food we ate from His table. The addition of *yoshev ha-keruvim* — a phrase that appears earlier in the *masechta* in the context of the *Kohen Gadol*'s proclamation in the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* — brings the language of the inner sanctum into the *zimmun* of a vast multitude, as if the *Shechinah* is fully present in so great a gathering of Israel. The *mishnah* notes that *echad asara v'echad asara ribo* — the text is the same whether it is ten or ten myriads, which we will see is the subject of dispute.

### Response Formula

Whatever formula the leader uses, the assembled respond in kind, mirroring the exact text but replacing *nevarech* with *baruch*: if the leader says *nevarech Elokeinu*, they answer *baruch Elokeinu*; if he says *nevarech Hashem Elokeinu Elokei Yisrael Elokei Tzevaot Yoshev Ha-Keruvim al ha-mazon she-achalanu mishelo*, they respond with the full parallel text, beginning *baruch Hashem Elokeinu* and so forth through to the end.

## Yose vs Akiva

### Yose Position

Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds that the text of *zimmun* varies according to the size of the assembly — *lefi rov ha-kahal hen mivarchin* — deriving this from the verse *bi-mkhelot barchu Elohim mi-mekor Yisrael*: the congregational praise of God scales with the crowd. The expansive formulas of the *mishnah* — adding *Elokeinu*, then *Hashem*, then *Elokei Yisrael*, then *Elokei Tzevaot Yoshev Ha-Keruvim* — all reflect the position of Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

### Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. Just as in *shul* the text does not change based on whether ten or ten thousand are present — one simply says *barchu et Hashem* — so too in *zimmun*, once the threshold is reached, the text remains constant regardless of the numbers. This is a principled rejection of the graduated formula; the sanctity of the assembly does not escalate indefinitely through textual additions.

### Identification

The *gemara* identifies these as the same positions debated earlier in the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* as we have it contains two strands: one set of teachings giving different formulas for different crowd sizes, and one stating that the formula is the same regardless. These are now identified as representing, respectively, the position of Rabbi Yose HaGlili and the position of Rabbi Akiva.

### Ishmael Clarification

Rabbi Ishmael adds a clarification on the *shul* model that Rabbi Akiva invokes: the correct formula in synagogue is not merely *barchu et Hashem* but *barchu et Hashem ha-mivorach* — the full formula we recite today when called to the Torah and at *barchu* in *shacharit* and *maariv*. This is the precise *nusach* of the *beit ha-knesset*, and Rabbi Akiva's analogy to synagogue practice therefore implies that fixed formula as the standard.
