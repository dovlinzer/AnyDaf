# Shevuot 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yedi'ot HaTumah

### Mishnah Setup

The Gemara opens with the phrase *gufa*, indicating a return to the core matter. The Mishnah has established that one is liable for a *korban* only where there exists *yedi'ah batchilah* — knowledge at the outset — and *yedi'ah basof* — knowledge at the conclusion — with an *he'elam*, a lapse of awareness, in between. This framework applies to the laws of *tumah*: one must have had some awareness regarding *tumah*, then entered a state of forgetfulness or ignorance, and subsequently regained awareness. The two axes of this structure concern either forgetting that one is *tamei* or forgetting that the place entered is the *Mikdash* — or in the related context of *kodesh*, forgetting the sanctity of the food one is consuming. These are the two or four permutations that the Mishnah generates.

### R. Akiva's View

The Gemara asks: from where is this principle derived? The answer is *talmud lomar vene'elam vene'elam shtei pe'amim* — the Torah uses the word *vene'elam* twice, and Rabbi Akiva derives from this doubling that two distinct *yedi'ot* are required. The repetition of the term is not incidental; it is the exegetical anchor for the two-knowledge requirement.

### R. Yishmael Challenge

Rabbi Yishmael takes a different approach. He maintains that one need not derive the two *yedi'ot* from the doubled expression, since even the single use of *vene'elam* implies prior knowledge — *miklal sheyada*. The logic is linguistic: *vene'elam* denotes something that became hidden or concealed, which presupposes that it was previously known. The verse in context further states *vehu yada ve'asham* — "and he knew and became guilty" — indicating an awareness that follows the forgetting. Rabbi Yishmael thus holds that a single occurrence of *vene'elam* already implies two states of knowledge, rendering the second appearance of *vene'elam* available for a different derivation: to create liability separately for *he'elam tumah* and for *he'elam mikdash*, two distinct categories of lapse.

### Rava's Sotah Proof

The Gemara challenges the premise that *vene'elam* inherently means "knew and then forgot." Rava raises a difficulty from the laws of *sotah*. The verse concerning the *sotah* uses the same root: *vehi ne'elmah mei'einei ishah* — "and she was hidden from her husband's eyes." If *vene'elam* always implies prior knowledge followed by forgetting, then in the *sotah* case, one would be forced to say the husband once knew of the infidelity, then forgot, and only then proceeded to bring his wife for the *sotah* procedure. This is obviously untenable: the entire premise of the *sotah* process is that the husband *does not know* whether his wife was unfaithful. The *sotah* procedure is not a post-hoc ritual for confirmed knowledge; it is a mechanism for resolving genuine doubt.

### Sotah Analysis

Abaye raises a further difficulty from within the *sotah* laws themselves, citing the principle *venikah ha'ish me'avon veha'ishah hi tisa et avonah* — the husband is absolved of sin and the woman bears her own guilt. A Baraita teaches: *bizman sheha'ish menukeh me'avon hamayim bodkin et ishto* — the bitter waters work only when the husband himself is free of sin. If the husband had known of the adultery, covered it up, and then submitted his wife to the ordeal, this very act of concealment would constitute an *avon* on his part — and the waters would not work. The entire efficacy of the *sotah* procedure is contingent on the husband's innocence, which would be forfeit if he had knowledge and then "forgot." Thus, *vene'elam* in that context cannot possibly mean prior knowledge followed by forgetting.

### Torah Examples

Additional instances from Torah further undermine the reading of *vene'elam* as implying prior knowledge. The verse describes divine wisdom as *ne'elamet mei'einei kol chai u-me'of hashamayim nisterah* — hidden from all living creatures and concealed from the birds of the sky. If *vene'elam* required prior knowledge, this would imply that someone once possessed full understanding of divine wisdom and then forgot it. But the very next verse states *lo yada enosh erchah* — no human has ever known its true measure. The *chukim* of the Torah cannot be fully grasped; the notion that some person once knew them and then forgot is impossible. These examples demonstrate conclusively that *vene'elam* need not imply prior knowledge; it can simply mean that something was, and remains, concealed.

### Abaye's Solution

Abaye therefore proposes a reinterpretation of what "knowledge" means in this context. He invokes the principle *yediat beit rabo shemah yediah* — the knowledge one absorbs in one's teacher's house, meaning the knowledge acquired in childhood education, counts as the relevant *yediah*. This is not the knowledge of a specific incident — not a conscious, moment-to-moment awareness that one has become *tamei* — but rather a general conceptual familiarity with the laws of *tumah v'taharah*.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi elaborates on this distinction in illuminating detail. The phrase *yediat beit rabo she-lamad b'veit rabo she-hanogea b'tumah tamei* means that a person learned as a child — particularly in an era like that of Chizkiyahu, when Torah knowledge was widespread — that contact with a source of *tumah* renders one *tamei*. Additionally, *vehu yada she-hirgish k'she-naga*: he was aware, at least on some level, that he had touched something. So it is not that he was entirely oblivious. What he did not do is *hitbonenah tumah b'libo she-nitma* — he did not mentally put the pieces together and consciously register: I touched that *sheretz*, that *sheretz* is *tamei*, therefore I am now *tamei*. He possessed, in effect, a kind of subconscious or background knowledge — the conceptual framework was there, the triggering incident was there, but the conscious synthesis never occurred. It is something like knowing the speed limit and being aware that traffic is moving quickly, but not actively registering in the moment that one is speeding.

### Rav Pappa Q

Rav Pappa presses Abaye with a question arising from the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah had enumerated different permutations, one of which involves a case where there is *ein bah yediah batchilah* — no knowledge at the outset — and *yesh bah yediah basof* — knowledge at the conclusion. If Abaye's principle is that *yediah* means general conceptual knowledge acquired in one's education, how can there be a case of no initial knowledge? Surely everyone knows the basic laws of *tumah* — that contact with impure objects renders one *tamei*. Rav Pappa formulates his challenge precisely: *mi leika d'lo yada yediat beit rabo d'leit lei yediat beit rabo?* Is there truly someone who lacks even this background level of knowledge?

### Tinok Shenishba

The answer is *mashkachat lah b'tinok shenishba l'vein hanachrim* — yes, one can find such a case in the *tinok shenishba*, the Jewish child taken captive and raised among non-Jews. Literally this refers to a Jewish infant kidnapped and raised without any Jewish education or exposure to Torah law. In the world of the Talmud, such kidnappings were not uncommon. The concept has been significantly expanded in later halachic discourse to encompass any Jew who grew up entirely without Jewish education — raised in an assimilated household, with no exposure to *halachah* — who would simply not know why, for instance, one cannot turn on a light on *Shabbat*. For such a person, the very conceptual framework that Abaye identifies as the necessary *yediah* is absent. In that case, the Mishnah's permutation of *ein bah yediah batchilah* is perfectly applicable: there was genuinely no prior knowledge of any kind, and only later, through some form of education or exposure, does the person acquire *yediah basof*.

### Historical Waves

This discussion prompted a broader reflection on the variation in Jewish knowledge and observance across history. The Gemara elsewhere notes that at the time of Chizkiyahu, Torah knowledge was so pervasive that even the youngest child knew the *halachot* of *tumah v'taharah*, and a survey from one end of *Eretz Yisrael* to the other found universal familiarity — the image of *meHodu v'ad Kush* applied internally to Jewish learning. Yet there were other periods of sharp decline, when large segments of the population had little knowledge of basic observance, drawn instead toward idolatry. The *Beit Sheni*, for all its outward grandeur, was ultimately destroyed precisely because of such failures. At the same time, other Gemaras note moments of extraordinary scholarly flourishing — the famous comparison where Rav Yehuda had only to remove his shoe during a drought before rain came, while in later generations great learning seemed to coexist with diminished spiritual potency. Knowledge and righteousness, it emerges, do not always track together, and they both move in waves throughout Jewish history. There is, the discussion implies, nothing new under the sun: there have always been more observant and less observant Jews, the content and locus of observance simply shifting from one era to the next.

## Yetziot HaShabbat

### Mishnah Setup

The Gemara now turns to the Mishnah's second item: *yetziot HaShabbat*. The familiar formulation from Tractate Shabbat reads: *yitziot HaShabbat shtayim she'hen arba bifnim u'shtayim she'hen arba bachutz* — the exits of Shabbat are two that are four inside, and two that are four outside. The immediate arithmetical consequence of this formulation is striking: the Shabbat Mishnah enumerates not four but eight cases in total. This stands in contrast to our Mishnah in Shevuot, which states only *shtayim she'hen arba* — two that are four — with no mention of a second set. The Gemara will work to explain this discrepancy.

### Three Components

Before proceeding to the individual cases, it is essential to establish what constitutes a prohibited act of transfer on *Shabbat*. Three components are required for full liability *mi'd'Oraita*: *akirah* (lifting — removing an object from its resting place), *ha'avarah* (transference across the boundary between domains), and *hanachah* (placing down — depositing the object in the new domain). All three must be performed by a single person, acting in the usual manner. If the labor is divided between two people, neither is *chayav*; each has performed only part of the prohibited act.

The relevant domains are *reshut hayachid* — the private domain — and *reshut harabim* — the public domain. A third category, *makom patur*, also exists and plays a role in more complex traversals, but the core prohibition involves transfer between the private and public domains.

Regarding the two that are four in our Mishnah: Rashi explains that *shtayim she'hen arba mi'divreihem hosifu shtayim le'asur lechatchila im asah patur* — the two that derive from rabbinic enactment are *patur aval assur*: one is exempt from a *korban* but the act is still prohibited. So two cases carry full Torah-level liability and two additional cases are rabbinically prohibited, accounting for the "four" of the formulation.

### Case 1: Ani Outside

The Mishnah in Shabbat begins: *he'ani omed bachutz u'ba'al habayit bifnim* — the poor man stands outside and the homeowner is inside, this being the standard configuration. The first case: *pashat he'ani et yado lifnim venatan letoch yado shel ba'al habayit* — the poor man extends his hand into the interior domain and places an object into the hand of the homeowner. In this case, the *ani* has performed both the *akirah* (lifting the object from its place in the public domain) and the *hanachah* (depositing it in the homeowner's hand in the private domain). The *ani* is therefore *chayav*. The homeowner has done nothing that carries liability.

### Case 2: Ani Takes

The second case, presented as an alternative: *o she'natal mitocha vehotzi* — or the *ani* reaches his hand into the interior, takes an object from it, and withdraws. Here too the *ani* has performed both the *akirah* — lifting the object from the homeowner's domain — and the *hanachah* — coming to rest with it in his own domain outside. Again, the *ani* has done everything; the *ani* is *chayav*.

### Case 3: Baal Habayit Out

The third case: *pashat ba'al habayit et yado lachutz venatan letoch yado shel ani* — the homeowner extends his hand outside and places an object into the outstretched hand of the poor man. Here the homeowner has performed the *akirah* from within and the *hanachah* into the public domain. The *ba'al habayit* is *chayav*; the *ani* is *patur*.

### Case 4: Ani Takes Out

Fourth: *o she'natal mitocha vehichnis* — or the homeowner, with his hand extended outside, takes an object from the *ani*'s hand and brings it back inside. Again, the homeowner has performed the complete prohibited act — *akirah* from the public domain and *hanachah* within his own private domain. The *ba'al habayit* is *chayav*; the *ani* is *patur*.

### Cases 5-8: Divided Labor

The Mishnah then turns to the cases of divided labor. *Pashat he'ani et yado lifnim venatal ba'al habayit mitocha* — the *ani* extends his hand inside, but the homeowner takes the object from his hand. Or: *she'natan lo letochah vehotzi* — the homeowner places something into the *ani*'s extended hand, and the *ani* withdraws. In both of these sub-cases, *shnehem peturim* — both are exempt — because the *akirah* was performed by one party and the *hanachah* by the other. Similarly mirrored are the cases where the homeowner extends his hand outside: *pashat ba'al habayit yado lachutz venatal ani mitochah* — the *ani* takes from the homeowner's extended hand; or *she'natan letochah vehichnis* — the *ani* deposits an object into the homeowner's extended hand and the homeowner draws it back inside. Again, *shnehem peturim*. These four cases of divided labor — *patur aval assur* — complete the set of eight.

### Gemara Q

Having reviewed the Mishnah in Shabbat, the Gemara poses its question about our Mishnah in Shevuot: *mai shna hacha detani shtayim she'hen arba vetu lo* — why does Shevuot state only "two that are four" without the additional "inside" and "outside"? Why does Shabbat say *shtayim she'hen arba bifnim u'shtayim she'hen arba bachutz*, while Shevuot contents itself with the shortened formulation?

### Preliminary Answer

A preliminary answer is offered: *hatam de-ikar Shabbat hu tani avot ve-toldot, hacha delav ikar Shabbat hu avot tani ve-toldot lo tani* — there, in Tractate Shabbat, the subject matter is Shabbat itself, and so both *avot* and *toldot*, both the primary cases and their derivatives, are fully elaborated. Here, in Shevuot, Shabbat is not the central topic; only the *avot* are taught and the *toldot* are not. So the truncated formulation is appropriate to the secondary context.

### Rav Pappa Object

The Gemara is unsatisfied with this. Rav Pappa presses: if *yetziot* refers to the cases of actual *hotza'ah* — the acts of removal — then there are really only two such cases, not four. If the formula "two that are four" is supposed to enumerate only the cases where one is *chayav*, then the inside and outside distinction would need to be mentioned in order to reach four *chiyuvim*. And if the "four" includes cases of *patur*, then one cannot say this Mishnah discusses only *chiyuvim*. The formulation is internally inconsistent. The question, in short, is: *chiyyubi mai ninhu yitziot? Yitziot tartin havyen* — if *yetziot* means acts of taking out, there are really only two of them.

### Yetziah Broad Use

The resolution requires expanding the semantic range of the word *yetziah*. Even in the Mishnah in Shabbat, where the text says *yitziot HaShabbat* and then describes both *yetziah* (removal from private to public) and *hachnasah* (bringing in from public to private), the term *yetziah* is used to encompass both. The Mishnah counts *hachnasot* as *yetziot* because it is using *yetziah* in a broad sense — denoting any transfer between domains, regardless of direction. This is what allows the formulation "two that are four" to hold: two general categories of transfer, each with two concrete cases, yielding four total *chiyuvim*.

### Baraita Source

Support for this usage is drawn from a Baraita listing the thirty-nine *avot melachot*, where one of the categories is stated as *hamotzi mereshut lereshut chayav* — one who transfers from domain to domain is liable. The formulation *mereshut lereshut* is conspicuously general: it does not say *mi-reshut hayachid li-reshut harabim* but simply "from one domain to another." This breadth is intentional. If the prohibition applied only to removing something from the private to the public domain, the Baraita would have specified that direction. The open formulation indicates that bringing something *in* — from public to private — is equally prohibited and equally subsumed under the term *hotza'ah*.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi states this principle explicitly: *tanna hachnasa nami yetziah kari leh* — the Tanna calls *hachnasah* also a *yetziah*. The proof is the Baraita just cited: *mi lo askinan dekama'yel leh vekakari leh hotza'ah* — are we not dealing there also with cases where one is bringing an object *in*, and yet the Baraita calls it *hotza'ah*? Clearly the term *hotza'ah* or *yetziah* is being used to denote any act of transfer between domains, not merely removal in one direction. The better conceptual term would perhaps be *ha'avarah* — conveyance or transfer — but the Mishnah and Baraita use *yetziah* to encompass both directions. What unifies them is the structure of the prohibited act: an *akirah* from one domain and a *hanachah* in the other, performed by the same person. Whether one calls the result a *yetziah* or *hachnasah* depends only on which direction the object travels; the underlying legal structure is identical.

### Raba: Reshuyot

Rava offers a still different resolution to the linguistic difficulty. Rather than expanding the meaning of *yetziah* to include *hachnasah*, Rava proposes that *yetziot* in the Mishnah does not mean "acts of removal" at all, but *reshuyot* — domains. *Reshuyot Shabbat shtayim* — the relevant domains of Shabbat are two. The two general categories are the two domains, *reshut hayachid* and *reshut harabim*, and the various permutations of transfer between them generate the four (or eight) specific cases. On this reading, the Mishnah is not claiming that there are two types of *yetziah*; it is rather orienting the entire discussion around the two domains that define the prohibition.

## Mar'ot Nega'im

### Four Marks Intro

The Mishnah's third item is *mar'ot nega'im* — the appearances of *tzara'at* lesions — which are described as two that are four. This topic is less familiar to most contemporary learners than *Shabbat*, and a brief orientation to the relevant laws is essential. The Torah in Parashat Tazria introduces the condition with the words: *adam ki yihyeh be'or besaro se'eit o sapachat o baheret* — a person who has on the skin of his flesh a *se'eit*, or a *sapachat*, or a *baheret*. Three terms are listed, but the halachic tradition identifies four distinct shades that trigger the laws of *tzara'at*: *baheret*, *se'eit*, *sid heichal*, and *krum habeitza*. The term *sapachat* in the verse does not name a fourth primary shade but rather denotes a secondary or derivative shade attached to one of the primaries — but which primary or primaries it attaches to is, as we shall see, precisely what is disputed.

### Visual Order

The four shades are arranged in order of decreasing whiteness. *Baheret* is the most intensely white — the Mishnah compares it to snow, and the word itself relates to the Hebrew *bahir*, meaning bright or brilliant. *Se'eit* is the white of clean wool — slightly less dazzling than snow but clearly white. *Sid heichal* is the white of the plaster used to coat the walls of the *Heichal*, the Temple sanctuary — a lime-white, somewhat more muted. *Krum habeitza* is the color of the membrane found just beneath the eggshell — not the shell itself but the thin translucent film inside it, which presents a faint, delicate white. This is the palest of the four and sits at the bottom of the scale.

### Sapachat Ambiguity

The Mishnah states *mar'ot nega'im shnayim she'hem arba* and proceeds to explain: *baheret azah kasheleg*, and *u'shniyah lah kesid haheichal* — *baheret* is intense as snow, and secondary to it is *sid heichal*. Then *se'eit ketzemer lavan*, and *u'shniyah lah kekrum habeitza* — *se'eit* is like white wool, and secondary to it is *krum habeitza*. This formulation pairs each of the two primary shades with one secondary shade: *baheret* with *sid heichal*, and *se'eit* with *krum habeitza*.

The ambiguity arises from the word *sapachat* in the original verse. Read one way, *sapachat* refers to a secondary shade attaching specifically to *baheret*, making it a modifier for *baheret* alone. Read another way, it could indicate that each of the two named primaries — *se'eit* and *baheret* — has its own *sapachat*, its own secondary shade. The practical stakes of this ambiguity concern the law of *mitztaref* — combination. Two differently shaded patches of *tzara'at* can combine to meet the minimum required size for a diagnosis of *nega tzara'at* only if they belong to the same halachic category, or to a parent-derivative relationship within a category. The question of which shades can combine with which therefore turns entirely on how the categories and their *sapachot* are structured.

### R. Akiva Setup

Rabbi Akiva holds that *baheret* and *se'eit* are the two *avot* — the two primary categories — and that *sid heichal* and *krum habeitza* are the two *toldot*, the derivative or secondary shades. Crucially, Rabbi Akiva assigns both *toldot* to *se'eit* alone. That is: *se'eit* is the *av* from which *sid heichal* and *krum habeitza* both derive, while *baheret* stands alone as the other *av* with no *toldah* of its own. On Rabbi Akiva's scheme, the structure is: *baheret* (primary, no derivative); *se'eit* (primary, with *sid heichal* and *krum habeitza* as its two secondary shades). This is a 1 and 2 with two sub-variants under 2: call it 1, 2, 2A, and 2B.

### Hanina Challenge

Hanina raises a sharp challenge to Rabbi Akiva's position, specifically as it bears on the law of *mitztaref*. If we accept Rabbi Akiva's ordering and then try to ask how the shades combine: can *sid heichal* combine with *baheret*? No, because there are two levels between them — *se'eit* intervenes, and one cannot jump two levels in the chain of parent-derivative relationships. Can *sid heichal* combine with *se'eit*? No, because though *sid heichal* is indeed closer to *se'eit* in the ordering, *se'eit* is *se'eit*'s *av* — but *sid* is a *toldah* and *se'eit* is an *av*, and a *toldah* cannot combine with anything except members of its own sub-group. On Rabbi Akiva's reading, then, *sid heichal* is left isolated: it cannot combine with *baheret* (too far up), and it cannot combine with *se'eit* in a way that would trigger the combined minimum.

### Combination Rules

The rules governing combination are: a secondary shade can combine only with its parent category or with shades that share the same parent. This means that patches of different *toldot* stemming from the same *av* can combine with one another, and a *toldah* can combine with its own *av*, but a *toldah* cannot jump over its parent to combine with a more distant shade. The structure of the combination rules thus follows precisely the structure of the category assignments, which is why the assignment of *sid heichal* as a *toldah* of *se'eit* has such significant practical consequences.

### Sapachat Resolution

The difficulty regarding *krum habeitza* is somewhat more tractable. Although *krum habeitza* sits below *se'eit* in the ordering, the verse *u'le-se'eit u'le-sapachat* — "and to *se'eit* and to *sapachat*" — indicates that *sapachat* is subsidiary to *se'eit*, *sapahat tfelah le-se'eit*. And, crucially, the Torah explicitly designates this relationship even though *krum habeitza* is lower in shade intensity. The verse's pairing of *se'eit* and *sapachat* provides the authorization for *krum habeitza* to function as *se'eit*'s derivative and to combine accordingly. *Sid heichal*, by contrast, lacks an analogous explicit authorization under Rabbi Akiva's system, and the problem remains unresolved for it.

### Non-Akiva View

The Gemara's conclusion is therefore: *mechuvarata lo ke-Rabbi Akiva* — the plainest understanding of the Mishnah's formulation does not accord with Rabbi Akiva's view. The Mishnah as we have it, which pairs *baheret* with *sid heichal* as its secondary shade and *se'eit* with *krum habeitza* as its secondary shade, reflects a different classification entirely — one in which each of the two *avot* has exactly one *toldah*. In that scheme: *baheret* is primary (1) with *sid heichal* as its derivative (1B), and *se'eit* is primary (2) with *krum habeitza* as its derivative (2B). This clean two-by-two structure is what yields the formulation "two that are four" — two *avot* each with one *toldah* — and it avoids the combinatorial problems that beset Rabbi Akiva's arrangement. The Mishnah's language, then, represents a Tannaitic view other than Rabbi Akiva's, and the discussion will continue from this point.
