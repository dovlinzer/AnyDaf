# Beitzah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Egg Completion & Mekach…

### Rav's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: בֵּיצָה, עִם יְצִיאָתָהּ נִגְמְרָה. מַאי ״עִם יְצִיאָתָהּ נִגְמְרָה״? אִילֵּימָא, עִם יְצִיאָתָהּ נִגְמְרָה וּמוּתֶּרֶת לְאׇכְלָה בְּחָלָב — הָא בִּמְעֵי אִמָּהּ אֲסוּרָה לְאׇכְלָה בְּחָלָב? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתַּרְנְגוֹלֶת וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֵּיצִים גְּמוּרוֹת — מוּתָּרוֹת לְאָכְלָן בְּחָלָב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: An egg is fully** formed **upon its emergence;** i.e., it is not considered an egg until it is laid. The Gemara inquires: **What is** the meaning of the statement: An egg **is fully formed upon its emergence?** To which issue is Rava referring? **If we say** he meant an egg **is fully formed** and called an egg only **upon its emergence, and** at this stage **it is permitted to eat it with milk,** this indicates that while an egg is still **inside its mother,** even if it is fully formed, it is considered meat and it is **prohibited to eat it with milk. But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who slaughters a chicken and finds fully** formed **eggs inside it, it is permitted to eat them with milk?**

The *daf* opens at the very bottom of 8b, continuing the interpretation of Rav's statement that an egg is only complete when it comes out — that is, only after a fertilized egg has been laid does it possess the potential to hatch a chick. An egg removed from inside a *shechted* hen, even if fertilized, cannot hatch. The *Gemara* asks *l'mai nafka mina* — what practical *halachic* difference does this make? The answer: it matters for *mekach ta'ut*, transactions in which a sale may be voided due to error.

### Pachya Eggs & Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְפָחֲיָא לְמַאן? יְהַבּוּ לֵיהּ בֵּיעֵי דִשְׁחוּטָה. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִקָּח טָעוּת הוּא, וְהָדַר. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הַאי לַאֲכִילָה קָא בָּעֵי לְהוּ, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר דְּפָחֲיָא — מִשּׁוּם דִּצְרִיבָן, לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְמִיתְּבָא לֵיהּ בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **of a live** chicken, **who has?** He sought to purchase eggs of this kind. **They gave him eggs of a slaughtered** chicken. **He came before Rabbi Ami,** claiming he had been cheated. Rabbi Ami **said to** the sellers: This **is a mistaken transaction, and it is rescinded;** the sale is void. The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that the transaction is void, as he specified exactly what he wanted. The Gemara answers: The ruling is necessary, **lest you say** that **this** individual **wants them for food** rather than for chicks, **and that which he said,** that he is looking for eggs **of a live** chicken, he said only **because they are hard-shelled,** mature eggs. **What is the practical difference,** i.e., what is this man claiming from the seller according to this rejected interpretation? He is merely demanding **to refund him the difference** in value **between the two** types of eggs. Rabbi Ami therefore **teaches us** that the sale involved a fundamental error, as the eggs of a slaughtered chicken are unfit for incubation. The transaction is therefore void.

The *Gemara* presents a case: someone went to the marketplace asking *d'pachya l'man* — who has *pachya* eggs, that is, laid eggs? *Rashi* explains that *pachya* refers to eggs from a hen that cooed while laying. The merchant sold him eggs taken from inside a *shechted* hen without disclosing this. The buyer later came before *Rabi Ami*, who ruled that this was a *mekach ta'us* — a sale in error — and reversed the transaction.

The *Gemara* objects: this seems obvious — if he explicitly asked for laid eggs and received something else, of course it is a *mekach ta'us*. The *Gemara* responds: one might have thought the buyer merely wanted the eggs for eating, and his request for laid eggs was simply because *mishum d'shafra* — a laid egg represents a further degree of completion. If so, this would not be a fundamental error voiding the entire sale, but only an overcharge — entitling the buyer to the *beini beinei*, the price differential between the two types of eggs. The ruling teaches us that we assume a buyer asking for laid eggs intends to hatch chicks, since only a laid egg can produce a chick (per Rav's statement). This constitutes a fundamental mismatch between what was sought and what was sold, rendering the entire transaction void.

This raises a broader conceptual point: not every error in a sale automatically voids the whole transaction. The distinction turns on whether the mistake goes to the heart of the buyer's purpose. Consider a case where someone buys a car and requests a specific accessories package, but receives a different one — that may entitle him to a price adjustment or a replacement, but it does not necessarily void the entire sale. The reason is that his core *da'as* — his underlying intent — was still to buy the car. Here, by contrast, if the buyer's fundamental purpose was to raise chicks, and the eggs he received cannot possibly hatch, the entire transaction was misconceived from the outset. One might ask how much of this depends on what a reasonable person in the marketplace would have understood the request to mean, as opposed to the buyer's actual private intent. The answer is that we generally require the intent to be reasonably self-evident from context; otherwise, we are dealing with *devarim she-belev*, and monetary matters are not resolved by unexpressed intentions alone.

### Dichrei Eggs Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: בֵּיעֵי דְּדִכְרָא לְמַאן! בֵּיעֵי דְּדִכְרָא לְמַאן! יְהַבּוּ לֵיהּ בֵּיעֵי דְּסָפְנָא מֵאַרְעָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִקָּח טָעוּת הוּא, וַהֲדַר. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הַאי לַאֲכִילָה קָא בָּעֵי לְהוּ, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר דְּדִכְרָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּשַׁמִּינָן טְפֵי, לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְמִיתְּבָא לֵיהּ בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: **A certain person said to** vendors: Does **anyone** have **eggs of** a chicken that has had relations with **a rooster?** Does **anyone** have **eggs of a rooster? They gave him eggs** that a hen had **absorbed from the ground,** i.e., which had not been fertilized by a rooster. **He came before Rabbi Ami** claiming that he had been cheated. Rabbi Ami **said to them:** This **is a mistaken transaction, and it is rescinded.** The Gemara again asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case. The Gemara explains: The ruling is necessary, **lest you say** that **this** person **wants** the eggs **for food, and that which he said,** that he wants eggs **of a rooster,** he said only **because they are fatter. What is the practical difference;** i.e., what is this man claiming from the seller according to this rejected interpretation? He is merely demanding **that they should refund him the difference** in value **between the two** types. Rav Ami therefore **teaches us** that this is not the case; rather, the sale is void.

A parallel case follows. Someone went around the marketplace asking *bei dichrei l'man* — who has eggs from a male, meaning eggs fertilized by a rooster? The merchant sold him *bei d'safna me-ara* — eggs produced by friction from the ground, meaning unfertilized eggs laid without a male. He came before *Rabi Ami*, who ruled: *mekach ta'us ve-hadar* — it is a sale in error and is reversed.

Again the *Gemara* objects: *pshita* — this is obvious. He asked for fertilized eggs and received unfertilized ones. The *Gemara* responds: one might have thought he wanted fertilized eggs for eating — perhaps because *mishum d'shaminan tvei*, they are fattier and of higher quality. If so, this is merely a quality difference warranting the *beini beinei* price adjustment, not a full reversal. The ruling teaches that if someone asks specifically for fertilized eggs, we assume he wants them for the purpose of hatching chicks — which is the fundamental and exclusive reason to insist on fertilization — and therefore the entire transaction is voided.

The *Gemara* then introduces a second answer (*ve-i ba'it eima*), transitioning away from the *mekach ta'us* discussion entirely.

## Completion Defined

### Rubah vs. Kulah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי עִם יְצִיאָתָהּ נִגְמְרָה — עִם יְצִיאַת רוּבָּהּ נִגְמְרָה, וְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֵּיצָה שֶׁיָּצְאָה רוּבָּהּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב וְחָזְרָה — מוּתֶּרֶת לְאׇכְלָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another explanation of Rav's statement. **And if you wish, say** instead: **What is** the meaning of the claim: **An egg is fully formed upon its emergence?** It means that **it is fully formed with the emergence of most of it, in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Yoḥanan. As Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is taught in a *baraita* that in the case of **an egg, most of which emerged** from the chicken **on a Festival eve, and** the egg **returned** inside the mother and was finally laid on the Festival itself, it **is permitted to eat** this egg **on the Festival.** Since most of the egg had emerged before the Festival began, it is considered to have been laid the day before.

The second interpretation of Rav's phrase *im yetziata nigmera* — "when it comes out, it is complete" — concerns the precise moment of birth for the *halachot* of an egg born on *Yom Tov*. According to one view, *im yetziat ruban nigmera*: the egg is considered complete — i.e., born — once the majority of it has emerged, even if it has not fully exited.

### Yochanan's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמַר: מַאי עִם יְצִיאָתָהּ נִגְמְרָה — עִם יְצִיאַת כּוּלָּהּ נִגְמְרָה. עִם יְצִיאַת כּוּלָּהּ — אִין, אֲבָל רוּבָּהּ — לָא. וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** the following explanation: **What is** the meaning of the expression: **Fully formed upon its emergence?** It means that **it is fully formed upon the emergence of all of it.** The Gemara infers: **Upon the emergence of all of it, yes,** it is fully formed at this stage; **however,** if only **most of it** came out the day before, **no,** it is not considered fully formed. **And** this reading serves **to exclude** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** In any case, Rav's statement can correspond to this statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The relevance of the *rubah* interpretation is established through the ruling of *Rabi Yochanan*: an egg whose majority emerged before *Yom Tov* and then retreated is considered already born from *erev Yom Tov* and is permitted for consumption on *Yom Tov*. According to the *rubah* interpretation, Rav's statement supports *Rabi Yochanan* — the egg is complete once its majority has emerged. The opposing *kulah* view is presented *u-le-afkuei me-de-Rabbi Yochanan* — specifically to reject *Rabi Yochanan*'s position, holding that full emergence is required before the egg is considered born.

Of the two interpretations of Rav's statement, this second one — concerning the moment of birth — is probably more likely to represent what Rav was addressing, since it speaks directly to the central *sugya* of when an egg is forbidden on *Yom Tov*, rather than to the ancillary question of *mekach ta'us*.

## Eggs in Slaughtered Hen

### Complete Eggs Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתַּרְנְגוֹלֶת וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֵּיצִים גְּמוּרוֹת — מוּתָּרוֹת לְאָכְלָן בְּחָלָב. רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיוּ מְעוֹרוֹת בְּגִידִין — אֲסוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the halakhic status of eggs found inside a slaughtered chicken, the Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself:** In the case of **one who slaughters a chicken and finds inside it fully formed eggs, it is permitted to eat** these eggs **with milk. Rabbi Ya'akov says: If** the eggs **were** still **attached by sinews, it is prohibited** to eat them with milk, as they are considered meat.

The *Gemara* now transitions back to a matter raised earlier in the discussion: when eggs found inside a *shechted* hen are considered part of the hen's meat, and when they are regarded as separate — which determines their status regarding *basar be-chalav*. The *Gemara* states: *ha-shocheit et ha-tarnegolet u-matza bah beitzim gemurot* — one who slaughters a hen and finds complete eggs inside her. *Tosafot* notes that the correct *girsa* likely refers to near-complete rather than fully complete eggs, as the subsequent discussion implies. *Rashi* explains that these eggs lack a hardened shell. In such a case, *mutarot le-echolan be-chalav* — they may be eaten with milk, as they are not considered the flesh of the hen.

*Rabi Yaakov* disagrees: if the eggs were still *me'urot be-gidim* — intertwined with the sinews — they are forbidden. At that stage, he holds, they remain essentially part of the hen's body and therefore carry the status of meat for the prohibition of *basar be-chalav*.

### Braita on Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹכֵל מִנִּבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר, מִן הַשְּׁלָל שֶׁל בֵּיצִים, מִן הָעֲצָמוֹת, וּמִן הַגִּידִין, וּמִן הַבָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּתְלַשׁ מִן הַחַי — טָהוֹר. מִן הָאֶשְׁכּוֹל שֶׁל בֵּיצִים, מִן הַקּוּרְקְבָן וּבְנֵי מֵעַיִין, אוֹ שֶׁהִמְחָה אֶת הַחֵלֶב וּגְמָעוֹ — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who **taught this** *halakha* **that the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who eats** one of the following parts **of the unslaughtered carcass of a kosher bird: From a cluster of eggs** that are still attached to it by sinews, or **from** its **bones, or from the sinews, or from meat that has been detached from a live** animal, is **ritually pure** because none of these are considered part of the meat of the bird, and therefore they do not impart the ritual impurity of an animal carcass. However, if one ate **from the ovary of** its **eggs,** which contains very small eggs that do not possess any of the regular characteristics of eggs, or if he took a piece **of the craw or the intestines, or if he melted the fat** of a dead bird **and swallowed it,** he is **ritually impure** from the impurity imparted by the unslaughtered carcass of a bird.

The *Gemara* asks who is the author of a *braita* concerning the *tum'ah* generated by eating from a *neveilah* of a kosher bird. The *halacha* is that one becomes *tamei* by swallowing a *kebeitzah* of the bird's flesh. The *braita* enumerates what counts as flesh for this purpose and what does not. On the permissive side: *ha-shalal shel beitzim* — the cluster of developing eggs — as well as bones, sinews, and flesh torn from the bird while it was still alive. On the stringent side: *ha-eshkol shel beitzim* — the ovary sac — along with the crop, the intestines, and liquefied fat, all of which render one *tamei*.

The distinction between *shalal* and *eshkol* is as follows: the *eshkol* is the ovary sac itself, while the *shalal* refers to the cluster of eggs that have separated from the sac and are developing, though still within the body. Since the *braita* rules that eating from the egg cluster does not generate *tum'ah*, the *Gemara* identifies this position as incompatible with *Rabi Yaakov*, who holds those same eggs are considered meat when intertwined with sinews.

### Authorship Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא מִן הַשְּׁלָל שֶׁל בֵּיצִים טָהוֹר? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב, הָאָמַר: אִם הָיוּ מְעוֹרוֹת בְּגִידִין — אֲסוּרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Who is the *tanna*** who **taught** that if one ate **from a cluster of eggs he is pure,** which indicates that eggs still attached by sinews to the chicken are not considered part of the meat of the bird? **Rav Yosef said:** This ruling is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov. For if** you say it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov, didn't he say: If** the eggs **were attached by sinews** it is **prohibited** to eat them with milk, indicating that he considers these eggs meat of the chicken.

*Rav Yosef* states: *delo k'Rabbi Yaakov* — the *braita* is not in accordance with *Rabi Yaakov*, since *Rabi Yaakov* holds that eggs intertwined with sinews are forbidden, implying he treats them as meat. The *braita*, which categorically excludes the egg cluster from the *tum'ah*-generating flesh of the bird, cannot reflect his view.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב הָתָם אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן אִסּוּרָא, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה — לָא. וְכִי תֵּימָא לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה נָמֵי נִגְזוֹר — אַפּוֹשֵׁי טוּמְאָה הוּא, וְאַפּוֹשֵׁי טוּמְאָה מִדְּרַבָּנַן לָא מַפְּשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **From where** do you draw this conclusion? **Perhaps Rabbi Ya'akov stated** that these eggs are part of the chicken **only there, with regard to** the **prohibition** against eating the eggs with milk; **however,** perhaps **with regard to ritual impurity** he did **not** say that these eggs are considered part of the chicken. **And if you say** that **with regard to** ritual **impurity let us also issue a decree** and be stringent in a case of uncertainty and therefore rule that these attached eggs should be considered part of the chicken, **this** would serve to **proliferate impurity, and we do not proliferate impurity** that is **by rabbinic law.** One does not declare an item ritually impure by rabbinic law merely because uncertainty has arisen with regard to its status.

*Abaye* challenges *Rav Yosef*: *minai* — how do you know this? Perhaps *Rabi Yaakov*'s stringency applies exclusively *le-inyan issura* — to the prohibition of *basar be-chalav* — and not to the laws of *tum'ah*. On a fundamental level, *Rabi Yaakov* may agree that these eggs are not technically meat; his ruling may be an additional stringency specific to *basar be-chalav*, where there are well-known extensions of the prohibition beyond the strictly technical.

The *Gemara* then raises an obvious follow-up: if we are willing to be stringent for *basar be-chalav*, why not apply the same stringency to *tum'ah*? The answer is *apushi tum'ah hu* — that would expand the scope of ritual impurity. *Apushi tum'ah mi-de-rabanan lo mapshinan* — we do not rabbinically extend *tum'ah* beyond its established parameters when there is no compelling reason to do so. *Rashi* acknowledges that this principle does not prevent all rabbinic extensions of *tum'ah* — there are many such *gezeirot* — but the point is that here there is no sufficient justification to do so, and at some point one must say enough.

The upshot of *Abaye*'s position is that the *braita* could in theory be consistent with *Rabi Yaakov*: he may be strict for *basar be-chalav* rabbinically, while agreeing that for *tum'ah* the egg cluster does not qualify as meat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מַאן תַּנָּא מִן הָאֶשְׁכּוֹל שֶׁל בֵּיצִים טָמֵא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אִם הָיוּ מְעוֹרוֹת בְּגִידִין אֲסוּרוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִמַּאי דְּאֶשְׁכּוֹל, מֵהָנָךְ דְּתַלְיָא בְּאֶשְׁכּוֹל? דִּלְמָא אֶשְׁכּוֹל גּוּפֵיהּ. וְכִי תֵּימָא: אֶשְׁכּוֹל גּוּפֵיהּ מַאי לְמֵימְרָא! מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקּוּרְקְבָן וּבְנֵי מֵעַיִין, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּבָשָׂר נִינְהוּ, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא אִינָשֵׁי דְּלָא אָכְלִי — אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן, הָכָא נָמֵי: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא אִינָשֵׁי דְּלָא אָכְלִי — אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** a different version of this discussion, according to which the question is: **Who is the *tanna*** who **taught** that if one eats **from the ovary of** its **eggs he is ritually impure? Rav Yosef said: It is Rabbi Ya'akov, who said: If** the eggs **were attached by sinews, they are prohibited. Abaye said to him: From where** do you know **that** the term **ovary** means: **From these** eggs **that are hanging from the ovary; perhaps** it is referring to the **ovary itself,** the part of the flesh of the bird where the eggs develop? **And if you say:** If it is referring to **the ovary itself, what** is the purpose **of stating** this? It is obvious that the ovary itself is meat. One can answer as follows: **Just as it is in** the case of **the craw and the intestines, that even though they are meat** according to all opinions, **since there are people who do not eat** them, **it was necessary to teach us** that they have the status of meat; **here, too,** with regard to an ovary, **since there are people who do not eat** it, **it was necessary to teach us** that it may not be eaten with milk. Therefore, this argument does not prove that this version of the discussion is incorrect.

A second version of the exchange (*ve-ika de-amri*) reverses the framing: rather than asking who authored the permissive ruling about *shalal*, it asks who authored the stringent ruling about *eshkol*. *Rav Yosef* suggests it is *Rabi Yaakov*, since the *eshkol* in the *braita* refers to eggs that have emerged from the sac but remain suspended within it — the very scenario *Rabi Yaakov* calls forbidden. *Abaye* again challenges: perhaps *eshkol* refers simply to the ovary sac itself, not to the eggs growing outside of it. And the *braita*'s novelty in mentioning the ovary sac is comparable to its novelty in mentioning the crop and intestines — these are all inner organs that some people do not eat and one might think are not considered *ochel* sufficient to generate *tum'ah*. The *braita* teaches that despite being unusual foods, they are considered part of the bird's flesh. This reading of the *braita* would place it in opposition to *Rabi Yaakov*, not in support of him.

## Birth Timing & Mating

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם — נוֹלָד בַּיּוֹם. כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה — נוֹלָד בַּלַּיְלָה. כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה — נוֹלָד בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה. כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם נוֹלָד בַּיּוֹם — זוֹ תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת. כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה נוֹלָד בַּלַּיְלָה — זוֹ עֲטַלֵּף. כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה — אָדָם וְכׇל דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Any** species **whose intercourse** occurs only **in the** hours of the **day** is **born** only **by day; any** species **whose intercourse occurs** only **at night is born** only **by night; any** species **whose intercourse occurs either by day or by night is born either by day or by night.** The Gemara elaborates: **Any** species **whose intercourse occurs by day is born by day, this** is referring to **a chicken. Any** species **whose intercourse occurs by night is born by night, this is a bat. Any** species **whose intercourse occurs either by day or by night,** this means **a human** being **and all that are similar to him.**

A *braita* is introduced establishing a general biological principle: *kol she-tashmisho ba-yom, nolad ba-yom* — any species that mates during the day gives birth during the day; *kol she-tashmisho ba-lailah, nolad ba-lailah* — any species that mates at night gives birth at night; and *kol she-tashmisho bein ba-yom u-vein ba-lailah, nolad bein ba-yom u-vein ba-lailah* — any species that mates both by day and by night gives birth in both.

### Hens & Daytime Birth

The *Gemara* identifies examples: the hen is a diurnal species — *kol she-tashmisho ba-yom nolad ba-yom, zu tarnigolas* — and therefore only lays eggs during the day. The bat is nocturnal — *kol she-tashmisho ba-lailah nolad ba-lailah, zu atalef*. Humans and all species similar to them mate and give birth both by day and by night.

## Coop Egg Case

### Rav Mari Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם נוֹלָד בַּיּוֹם — זוֹ תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְכִדְרַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: בָּדַק בְּקִנָּה שֶׁל תַּרְנְגוֹלִין מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב וְלֹא מָצָא בָּהּ בֵּיצָה, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמָצָא בָהּ בֵּיצָה — מוּתֶּרֶת. וַהֲלֹא בָּדַק! אֵימַר לֹא בָּדַק יָפֶה יָפֶה. וַאֲפִילּוּ בָּדַק יָפֶה, אֵימַר: יָצְתָה רוּבָּהּ וְחָזְרָה הֲוַאי, וְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: Any** species **whose intercourse occurs by day is born by day, this is a chicken.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the **practical** halakhic **difference** of this statement? The Gemara answers: The halakhic difference is with regard **to that which Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana** said, **as Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana said:** If one **examined a chicken's nest on a Festival eve and did not find an egg in it, and the following day,** on the Festival, **he rose early and found an egg in it,** the egg is **permitted,** as it can be assumed it was not laid that night. The Gemara asks: **But didn't he examine** the nest before the Festival and fail to find an egg there? If so, the egg must have been laid on the Festival. The Gemara answers: **Say** that **he did not examine very carefully. And even** if **he did examine carefully,** you can **say** that **most of** the egg **emerged** on the eve of the Festival **and returned** inside its mother, and this ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.** This *baraita* shows that the *halakha* does not take into account the possibility that a chicken could lay an egg at night.

The *halachic* relevance of hens laying only during the day emerges from a ruling of *Rav Mari the son of Rav Kahana*. He teaches: *badak be-kana shel tarnigolas me-erev Yom Tov ve-lo matza bah beitza* — if one checked the hen's roost right before *Yom Tov* and found no egg, and the next morning woke up early and found an egg, *mutteres* — it is permitted. The reasoning: since we found the egg before daybreak, one would normally assume it was laid at night — which would mean it was born on *Yom Tov* and would be problematic. But since hens cannot lay eggs at night, one of two explanations must apply: either one did not check well enough (*lo badak shapir*) and there was already an egg present, or — even if one checked carefully — one must say *eimur yatza rubah ve-chazra hava*: the majority of the egg had already emerged before *Yom Tov* and then retreated, so by *Rabi Yochanan*'s ruling it was already considered born before *Yom Tov* began.

This is a striking instance of the pre-empirical orientation of Talmudic reasoning: rather than accepting the apparent evidence that the egg was laid at night, the *Gemara* prefers to invoke the authoritative principle that hens do not lay at night and constructs an alternative explanation — however unusual — to preserve consistency with that principle.

### Rooster Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל אָמַר רַב: בָּדַק בְּקִנָּה שֶׁל תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב וְלֹא מָצָא בָּהּ בֵּיצָה, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמָצָא בָהּ בֵּיצָה — אֲסוּרָה! הָתָם בִּדְסָפְנָא מֵאַרְעָא. אִי הָכִי דְּרַב מָרִי נָמֵי אֵימָא מֵאַרְעָא סָפְנָא? בִּדְאִיכָּא זָכָר בַּהֲדַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul say** that **Rav said:** If **one examined a chicken's nest on a Festival eve and did not find an egg in it, and the following day he rose early and found an egg in it, it is prohibited?** This indicates that a chicken might indeed lay an egg at night. The Gemara answers: **There** it is referring **to** an egg **that** the chicken **absorbed from the earth,** i.e., one that was not formed by male fertilization. An egg that is not produced by intercourse can be laid at night as well. The Gemara challenges this: **If so,** in **Rav Mari's** case **too,** you can **say that** the chicken **absorbed from the earth** and laid the egg during the night of the Festival. How, then, could Rav Mari permit the egg? The Gemara answers: There it is referring **to** a case **where there is a rooster with it.** The Gemara asks: Even **where there is a rooster with it,** one can **also say that it absorbed from the earth** rather than from the rooster.

The *Gemara* raises an apparent contradiction from a statement of *Rav Yosef bar Shaul amar Rav*: if one checked the roost right before *Yom Tov* and found no egg, and then found one the next morning before daybreak, *asura* — it is forbidden, because it was evidently laid overnight. The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hatam be-dasfna me-ara* — that case involves an unfertilized egg. The principle that hens do not lay at night applies only to fertilized eggs, since the underlying rule links birth timing to mating. An unfertilized egg, having no connection to daytime mating, may be laid at any time, including at night. It is therefore possible for an egg found before daybreak to have been laid overnight if it is unfertilized.

The *Gemara* now objects to *Rav Mari*'s leniency: if we are worried about the possibility of an unfertilized egg, then even in his case one should assume the egg might have been laid at night. The response: *de-ika zachar ba-hada* — there is a rooster in the vicinity, which means we presume the egg is fertilized and therefore could not have been laid at night.

The *Gemara* pushes back: even with a rooster present, perhaps the hen laid an unfertilized egg. This leads to a new principle.

### Ravina's Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, גְּמִירִי: כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא זָכָר — לָא סָפְנָא מֵאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Ravina said:** It is **learned** as a tradition that **anywhere that a male is present,** a chicken **does not absorb from the earth.**

*Ravina* provides a tradition — *gemiri* — that wherever a rooster is present, a hen will not lay unfertilized eggs: *kol heicha de-ika zachar lo safna me-ara*. Whether this means the rooster will mate with every available hen, or that the hen's biology responds to the rooster's presence such that it will not lay without fertilization, the tradition establishes the principle categorically.

### Rooster Voice Range

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב גַּמָּדָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּשָׁמְעָה קָלֵיהּ בִּימָמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Until where** exactly is it considered to have a male with it; How near must a rooster be for this principle to come into effect? **Rav Gamda said in the name of Rav:** The male must be **any place** **where** the hen can **hear its voice by day,** when sounds cannot be heard as easily as at night. If the chicken is close enough to hear the rooster's call by day, the rooster is considered close enough to the chicken that the latter will produce eggs only through fertilization by the male.

How close must the rooster be for this principle to apply? *Rav Gamda in the name of Rav* teaches: as far as the rooster's voice can be heard *bi-ymama* — during the daytime. The qualifier "daytime" is significant: since hens mate and lay during the day, and sounds carry less far in the day than at night, the relevant question is whether the hen can hear the rooster's crowing during daytime hours. If it can, it will not lay unfertilized eggs; if it cannot, it might.

### Sixty Houses Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד רַב מָרִי עוֹבָדָא עַד שִׁתִּין בָּתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Mari took action** in accordance with this opinion, when he found an egg on a Festival morning after he had looked in the nest the day before and failed to find an egg there. He examined **up to** a distance of **sixty houses** from the chicken, and although he did not find a rooster within this area, he assumed there was one further away and permitted the egg.

*Rav Mari* issued a practical ruling in a case where the rooster was sixty houses away, determining that this was still within hearing range and therefore the hen would not lay an unfertilized egg. Consequently, any egg found in such a coop could be presumed fertilized and therefore could not have been laid at night — lending support to the conclusion that it was laid before *Yom Tov*.

### Rooster Obstacles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיכָּא נַהֲרָא — לָא עָבְרָא. וְאִי אִיכָּא מַבָּרָא — עָבְרָא. וְאִי אִיכָּא מִיצְרָא — לָא עָבְרָא. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וְעָבְרָא אַמִּיצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And if** there is **a river** between the rooster and the chicken, the chicken **does not cross** the river; **but if there is a bridge,** it **crosses** the river. **And if there is** only **a rope bridge** suspended across the river, the chicken will **not cross** on the rope. The Gemara comments: Nevertheless, **an incident** occurred in which a chicken **crossed over on a rope bridge.** However, one may not rely on this possibility.

The *Gemara* then addresses physical obstacles that might prevent a rooster from crossing to reach a hen. If there is a river between them, *lo avra* — the rooster will not cross. If there is a bridge, *avra* — it will cross. If there is a thin plank — *metzra*, which *Rashi* describes as a narrow board one would traverse while holding a thin rope — then *lo avra*, it will not cross. Yet the *Gemara* reports an actual incident in which a rooster did cross a *metzra*. The discussion here evokes the *Gemara*'s observation elsewhere that *ein apotropos le-arayos* — no barrier can be fully trusted to prevent mating — so that even significant obstacles may not deter a rooster.

### Safek Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא — בִּדְסָפְנָא מֵאַרְעָא, מַאי אִירְיָא בָּדַק? כִּי לָא בָּדַק נָמֵי! כִּי לָא בָּדַק, אֵימָא מֵאֶתְמוֹל הֲוַאי. אִי הָכִי כִּי בָּדַק נָמֵי, אֵימָא: יָצְתָה רוּבָּהּ וְחָזְרָה הִיא, וְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן! דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **In what** manner **did you establish** this *halakha* of Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul, who prohibits eating an egg discovered in a nest on a Festival morning after the previous day's examination revealed nothing? You said that it is referring to a case **in** which the chicken **absorbed from the earth.** If so, **why** discuss **specifically** a situation in which **one examined** the nest beforehand? The same *halakha* should apply **even when one did not examine** the nest beforehand. The Gemara answers: In a case **where he did not examine** the nest, one could **say** that the egg **was** laid **yesterday,** and there is no reason to prohibit its use. Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul therefore specified that he checked the day before and is certain that the egg was not laid prior to the Festival. The Gemara challenges this: **If so, when he examined** the nest **as well, say:** Perhaps this **is** a case in which **most of** the egg **emerged and returned, and** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan?** The Gemara answers: The case discussed **by Rabbi Yoḥanan,** where most of the egg emerged from the chicken and returned, **is uncommon,** and therefore it is assumed not to have occurred.

The *Gemara* then circles back to resolve a conceptual difficulty. Returning to the scenario where one checked before *Yom Tov* and found no egg but then found one the next morning: the entire discussion had assumed the egg's unfertilized status as the problematic case. But the *Gemara* now asks: *mai irya badak* — why is it significant that one checked right before *Yom Tov*? Even if one had not checked, one could simply assume the egg might have been laid from *erev Yom Tov*. The ruling: *ki lo badak, eima mei'etmol havei* — if one did not check before *Yom Tov*, and found an egg the next morning, one may be lenient and assume it was laid before *Yom Tov*. Since the prohibition on a *Yom Tov*-born egg is rabbinic, *safek de-rabbanan l'kula* — in cases of doubt regarding rabbinic prohibitions, we rule leniently.

The *Gemara* asks: if that is so, even when one did check and found nothing, one should still be lenient — perhaps the egg had been laid with its majority emerged and then retreated, as per *Rabi Yochanan*. The *Gemara* answers: *d'Rabbi Yochanan lo shchiach* — that scenario is uncommon. So if one checked carefully and found nothing, the possibility of *Rabi Yochanan*'s scenario is too remote to rely upon. But if one did not check at all, and there is a genuine possibility the egg was already laid from before *Yom Tov*, the doubt is real and one may rule leniently.

This yields a practically important ruling: if one finds an egg in a coop on *Yom Tov* morning and there is no certainty that it was laid overnight, it is *muttar* — unless one checked right before *Yom Tov* and confirmed the coop was empty.

### Tosafot's Caveat

*Tosafot* raises a significant question on this leniency. The egg in question is a *davar she-yeish lo matirin* — something that will become permissible on its own after *Yom Tov*. The general principle of *davar she-yeish lo matirin* holds that we do not apply certain leniencies — such as *bittul* in a mixture — to objects that will become permitted later; why not wait? If so, should *safek de-rabbanan l'kula* not apply here either, and one should instead wait until after *Yom Tov* to eat the egg?

*Tosafot* answers: *shani hacha* — this case is different, because in any event it is more common for a hen to lay during the day than at night, so even without the specific analysis about fertilization and roosters, the statistical likelihood favors the egg having been laid before *Yom Tov*. The simple sense of the *Gemara*, however, is that the leniency of *safek de-rabbanan l'kula* applies straightforwardly here: the prohibition is rabbinic and the doubt is genuine. *Tosafot*'s caveat reminds us of the earlier discussion of *davar she-yeish lo matirin* — the question of whether that principle extends to block other forms of leniency beyond *bittul* — but the *Gemara* itself appears to permit the egg in the straightforward doubt scenario without invoking that concern.

## Chametz & Se'or

### Shammai's Amounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׂאוֹר בְּכַזַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Beit Shammai say:** The measure that determines liability for **leaven** is **an olive-bulk.** However, the measure for leavened bread is greater, a large date-bulk. According to Beit Hillel, the measure in both cases is an olive-bulk.

The *Gemara* now proceeds to the second of the three *Mishnah* disputes between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* recorded in this *masechta*: the minimum quantities of *chametz* and *se'or* on *Pesach*. *Se'or* is sourdough — not itself edible in normal circumstances, but used as a fermenting agent to leaven dough. The Torah prohibits both *chametz* and *se'or*. *Beit Shammai* holds that *se'or* carries its prohibition at the size of a *kezayit*, while *chametz* carries its prohibition only at the larger size of a *kakosevet* — approximately the volume of a pressed date.

### Shammai's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא חָמֵץ, וְלָא בָּעֵי שְׂאוֹר, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה חָמֵץ שֶׁאֵין חִמּוּצוֹ קָשֶׁה — בִּכְזַיִת, שְׂאוֹר שֶׁחִמּוּצוֹ קָשֶׁה — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. שְׂאוֹר דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? לוֹמַר לְךָ: שִׁיעוּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה לֹא כְּשִׁיעוּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai?** The Gemara explains: **If** it is **so** that the measures are equal, **let the Merciful One write** only the prohibition of **leavened bread, and it would not be necessary** to write **leaven, and I would say** an *a fortiori* inference: **If** the measure that determines liability for **leavened bread, whose leavening is not** as **extensive,** is **an olive-bulk,** then **leaven, whose leavening is extensive, all the more so** should the measure that determines liability be an olive-bulk. If so, **why do I** need the explicit reference to **leaven that the Merciful One wrote** in the verse: "Seven days there shall be no leaven found in your houses" (Exodus 12:19)? It must be **to tell you** that **the measure of this,** leavened bread, **is not equal to the measure of that,** leaven. Rather, the measure that determines liability for leaven is smaller.

The *Gemara* asks for *Beit Shammai*'s reasoning. Their position rests on the logic that since *se'or* has more powerful leavening ability — *chimutzo kasheh* — one might assume it would be more stringently forbidden. If so, why did the Torah need to prohibit *se'or* at all? Had it only said *chametz*, one could have derived *se'or* a fortiori: if ordinary bread, whose leavening power is weak, is forbidden, certainly sourdough, whose leavening power is strong, is forbidden. The very fact that the Torah explicitly mentions *se'or* separately must be *l'malfatcha* — to teach something new: namely, that their *shiurim* are not identical. Since the baseline measure for eating-related prohibitions is a *kezayit*, that minimum applies to the more powerful *se'or*. For *chametz*, which is less potent as a leavening agent, *Rashi* explains that the next logical size — the *kakosevet*, which is the measure that provides some minimal satiation (as established in the context of *Yom Kippur*) — applies.

### Hillel's Amounts

### Hillel's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל: צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא שְׂאוֹר, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּחִמּוּצוֹ קָשֶׁה, אֲבָל חָמֵץ, דְּאֵין חִמּוּצוֹ קָשֶׁה — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא חָמֵץ, מִשּׁוּם דְּרָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה. אֲבָל שְׂאוֹר, שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Beit Hillel** maintain that both the mention of both leaven and leavened bread **are necessary. As if the Merciful One had written** only **leaven, I would have said** its measure that determines liability is an olive-bulk **because its leavening is extensive. However,** with regard to **leavened bread,** whose **leavening is not extensive, say no,** that is not the measure. Therefore, it is **necessary** to mention leavened bread as well. **And** conversely, **if the Merciful One had written** only **leavened bread,** I would have said that the measure that determines liability for leavened bread is an olive-bulk, **because it is fit for consumption** on its own; **however, leaven, which is not fit for consumption** on its own but only when used as a leavening agent for dough, **say no,** its measure is not like that of leavened bread. Therefore, it is **necessary** to mention both cases.

*Beit Hillel* holds that both *chametz* and *se'or* are governed by the same measure: a *kezayit*.

*Beit Hillel* reasons that both *chametz* and *se'or* needed to be stated explicitly because each has a stringent dimension not possessed by the other: *se'or* is stringent because of its powerful leavening capacity, while *chametz* is stringent because it is *ra'ui le-achila* — fit for ordinary human consumption. Neither can be derived from the other. *Se'or* cannot be derived from *chametz* alone, since one might have thought that since *se'or* is not normally eaten, it would not be prohibited. *Chametz* cannot be derived from *se'or* alone, since one might have thought the prohibition stems specifically from the capacity to leaven, which *chametz* lacks to the same degree. Since each has its own independent basis for stringency, both must be stated, and both are governed by the same *shiur* of a *kezayit*.

### Eating vs. Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי לֵית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: פְּתַח הַכָּתוּב בִּשְׂאוֹר וְסִיֵּים בְּחָמֵץ, לוֹמַר לְךָ: זֶהוּ שְׂאוֹר זֶהוּ חָמֵץ! לְעִנְיַן אֲכִילָה כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְלִיגִי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי לְעִנְיַן בִּיעוּר. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: לָא יָלְפִינַן בִּיעוּר מֵאֲכִילָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: יָלְפִינַן בִּיעוּר מֵאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Beit Shammai, don't they hold** in accordance with **Rabbi Zeira's** opinion? **As Rabbi Zeira said,** the verse states: "Seven days there shall be no leaven found in your houses, for whoever eats that which is leavened, that soul shall be cut off from the assembly of Israel" (Exodus 12:19). **The verse begins with leaven and ends with leavened bread to say to you** that **leaven is equivalent to leavened bread.** How, then, can Beit Shammai maintain that the two are prohibited by different measures? The Gemara answers: Indeed, **with regard to** the measure that determines liability for **eating, everyone agrees** that the same measure applies to leavened bread and leaven. **When they disagree** it is **with regard to** the **removal** of leavened bread from one's house. **Beit Shammai hold** that **we do not derive** the *halakha* of **removal from** that of **eating.** Rather, with regard to the obligation of removal, different measures apply to leavened bread and leaven. **And Beit Hillel hold** that **we derive** the *halakha* of **removal from** the measure that determines liability for **eating.**

The *Gemara* introduces the position of *Rabi Zeira*: one verse opens with *se'or* and closes with *chametz* — *shivat yamim se'or lo yimatzei be-vateichem ki kol ochel machmetzet* — equating them *le-halacha*. The *Gemara* then clarifies the scope of the dispute: *l'inyan achila, kulei alma lo pligi* — regarding eating, nobody disagrees. All agree that one transgresses upon eating a *kezayit* of either. *Ki pligi l'inyan biyur* — the dispute concerns *biyur*, the prohibition of owning or possessing *chametz* on *Pesach*. *Beit Shammai savri lo yalpinan biyur me-achila* — *Beit Shammai* holds that we do not derive the *shiur* for the prohibition of ownership from the *shiur* of eating. Consequently, although eating a *kezayit* of either would be a transgression, owning *chametz* only becomes prohibited when the amount reaches a *kakosevet*. *Beit Hillel* holds *yalpinan biyur me-achila*: we learn the *shiur* for ownership from the *shiur* for eating, and both are a *kezayit*. This reading is confirmed by *Rabi Yose bar Hanina* and by a *braita* that cites the verse *ve-lo yera-eh lecha chametz ve-lo yera-eh lecha se'or* — the doubling of the prohibition teaches that this is precisely the point of the dispute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת לְעִנְיַן בִּיעוּר, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן אֲכִילָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל זֶה וְזֶה בִּכְזַיִת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״וְלֹא יֵרָאֶה לְךָ (שְׂאוֹר) וְלֹא יֵרָאֶה לְךָ (חָמֵץ)״. זֶהוּ מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁבֵּין בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל. שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׂאוֹר בִּכְזַיִת וְחָמֵץ בִּכְכוֹתֶבֶת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וְזֶה בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** That opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*. **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** The **dispute** between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel was stated **with regard to** the **removal** of leavened bread; however, **with regard to** the measure that determines liability for **eating, everyone agrees** that the measure for both **this and that** is **an olive-bulk.** The Gemara further explains: **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*. The verse states: **"And no leavened bread shall be seen with you, and no leaven shall be found in all your borders"** (Exodus 13:7). **This** obligation to remove leavened bread **is** subject to **a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, as Beit Shammai say:** The measure that determines liability for removal of **leaven** is **an olive-bulk, and** the measure for leavened bread is **a large date-bulk, and Beit Hillel say:** The measure for **both this and that** is **an olive-bulk.** This *baraita* indicates that the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel is with regard to the obligation to remove leavened bread, not liability for eating it.

### Ownership Concept

*Beit Shammai*'s position opens an interesting conceptual window onto the nature of the prohibition of owning *chametz*. There are two possible understandings of why the Torah prohibits possessing *chametz* in one's home. The first is that it serves as a safeguard against eating: since we eat *chametz* throughout the year and it is not inherently associated in our minds with *issur*, we might forget ourselves and eat it. Owning it therefore risks violation, and the Torah preemptively prohibits possession. Under this understanding, the prohibition of ownership is functionally derivative of the prohibition of eating — it is the Torah's own *seyag*. The second understanding is that ownership of *chametz* is itself antithetical to the identity of the day as *Chag ha-Matzot*: the entire theme of the festival is *matzah* and the departure from Egypt, which was so rapid that the dough did not have time to leaven. On this view, the presence of *chametz* in one's domain contradicts the essence of the day, quite apart from any risk of eating.

*Beit Shammai*'s position — that the *shiur* for *biyur* is larger than the *shiur* for eating — seems more compatible with the second understanding. If the entire basis of the ownership prohibition were simply "lest you come to eat it," then a *kezayit* of *chametz* — the exact amount whose consumption would be a transgression — should trigger the ownership prohibition as well, since one could pop it in one's mouth at any moment. The fact that *Beit Shammai* requires a *kakosevet* for the ownership violation, despite agreeing that eating a *kezayit* is forbidden, suggests that the prohibition of ownership is not simply a derivative of the eating concern but reflects something independent — perhaps the thematic incompatibility of *chametz* with the day — and therefore requires a more substantial presence to be triggered.

## Blood Coverage on Yom Tov

### Lechatchila vs. Di-eved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט חַיָּה וָעוֹף בְּיוֹם טוֹב וְכוּ׳. הַשּׁוֹחֵט, דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט. מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר יִשְׁחוֹט! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט וִיכַסֶּה קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel also dispute whether or not **one who slaughters an undomesticated animal or a bird on a Festival,** without having prepared earth the day before with which to cover the blood after the slaughter, may dig out earth on the Festival itself to cover the blood. The Gemara analyzes the wording of the mishna: **One who slaughters** an animal; this indicates that **after the fact, yes,** one may dig out dirt, but ***ab initio*, no,** even Beit Shammai agree that one may not slaughter an undomesticated animal or a bird if there is no prepared dirt. Otherwise, the mishna would have said: One may slaughter an undomesticated animal or a bird on a Festival. **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **And Beit Hillel say** that one **may not slaughter** it. This proves **by inference that the first *tanna*,** Beit Shammai, **holds** that one **may slaughter** it even *ab initio*. The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult,** as Beit Hillel **said** and meant the following: **One may not slaughter** an animal **and cover** the blood. They are emphasizing that one may not cover the blood even if he slaughtered an animal, whereas Beit Shammai maintain that if one slaughtered he may cover the blood *ab initio*.

The *Gemara* now addresses the third and final dispute in the *Mishnah*: the question of covering the blood of a *chayah* or *of* — a wild animal or bird — when *shechita* is performed on *Yom Tov*. The *mitzvah* of *kisuy ha-dam* requires the blood to be covered with earth above and below. The *Mishnah* frames the question using the phrase *ha-shochet*, which could be read either as referring to someone who has already slaughtered (*di-eved*) or to someone who is asking prospectively whether he may slaughter (*lechatchila*).

The *Gemara* identifies a textual problem. On one hand, the opening of the *Mishnah* sounds like a *di-eved* scenario — what does one do after the fact? On the other hand, *Beit Hillel*'s response — *lo yishchot* — is a prohibition against slaughtering, which can only make sense as a *lechatchila* ruling. Furthermore, at the end of the *Mishnah*, both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree that *im shachat, she-yachpor be-deker ve-yichaseh* — if one already slaughtered, one may dig and cover — making clear that *di-eved* is agreed upon by both. Therefore the dispute must be *lechatchila*.

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וּמוֹדִים שֶׁאִם שָׁחַט, שֶׁיַּחְפּוֹר בַּדֶּקֶר וִיכַסֶּה. מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָאו דִּיעֲבַד הוּא! אָמַר רַבָּה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט שֶׁבָּא לִימָּלֵךְ — כֵּיצַד אוֹמֵר לוֹ? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: שְׁחוֹט, חֲפוֹר וְכַסֵּה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיָה לוֹ עָפָר מוּכָן מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **And** Beit Hillel **concede that if he** already **slaughtered, then he may dig with a shovel and cover** the blood, which indicates that Beit Hillel were not emphasizing the covering of the blood. This proves **by inference that** the **first clause** of the mishna **is not** referring to one's action **after the fact,** but to the *halakha ab initio*. **Rabba said** that **this is what** the mishna **is saying.** The phrase: One who slaughters an animal, is not a broad directive on how to act, but rather it means: If there is **one who slaughters** animals **who comes to consult** a Sage on how to proceed, **what** should the Sage **say to him? Beit Shammai say** that the Sage **says to him: Slaughter** it *ab initio*, **dig, and cover** the blood. **And Beit Hillel say: He may slaughter** the animal **only if he had earth** that was **prepared while it was still day.**

*Rava* explains the *Mishnah* as follows: *ha-shochet she-ba l'himalekh keitzad* — the *Mishnah* addresses a slaughterer who comes to ask for practical instruction: what is he to do? *Beit Shammai* tells him: *shechot, chapor ve-chaseh* — slaughter, then dig, then cover. *Beit Hillel* tells him: *lo yishchot ela im kein haya lo afar muchan mi-be'od yom* — do not slaughter unless you already had dirt prepared before *Yom Tov*. The question throughout is therefore *lechatchila*. *Beit Shammai* permits proceeding with the slaughter and covering even without pre-prepared dirt; *Beit Hillel* prohibits the slaughter entirely unless dirt was prepared beforehand.

### Rav Yosef's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט שֶׁבָּא לְהִמָּלֵךְ, כֵּיצַד אוֹמֵר לוֹ? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: לֵךְ חֲפוֹר, שְׁחוֹט וְכַסֵּה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיָה לוֹ עָפָר מוּכָן מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef** provided a slightly different explanation of the mishna and **said** that **this is what** the mishna **is saying:** With regard to **one who slaughters animals who comes to consult, what does** the Sage **say to him? Beit Shammai say** that **he says to him: Go dig, slaughter** the animal, **and cover** the blood. **And Beit Hillel say: He may slaughter** the animal, **only if he had earth** that was **prepared while it was still day.**

*Rav Yosef* presents the same basic ruling but with a subtle difference: according to *Beit Shammai*, he formulates the instruction as *lech chapor, shechot ve-chaseh* — go dig first, then slaughter, then cover — rather than *Rava*'s formulation of slaughter first and dig afterward.

### Rav Zeira's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: לֵימָא מָר וְרַבָּה בִּדְרַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב קָא מִפַּלְגִיתוּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּתֵּן עָפָר לְמַטָּה וְעָפָר לְמַעְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁפַךְ אֶת דָּמוֹ וְכִסָּהוּ בֶּעָפָר״. ״עָפָר״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״בֶּעָפָר״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַשּׁוֹחֵט צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּתֵּן עָפָר לְמַטָּה וְעָפָר לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Let us say** that **the Master,** i.e., Rav Yosef, **and Rabba dispute** the following statement **that Rabbi Zeira** said that **Rav said, as Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said: One who slaughters** an undomesticated animal or bird is obligated to perform the mitzva of covering the blood, and therefore **he must place earth beneath** the blood **and earth above** it,** as it is stated: "And he shall pour out its blood and cover it in earth"** (Leviticus 17:13). It **is not stated:** Cover it with **earth, but "in earth,"** indicating that the blood must be concealed inside the earth. The verse thereby **teaches that one who slaughters** an animal **must place earth beneath and earth above** the blood.

*Abaye* identifies what he believes to be the underlying disagreement between *Rava* and *Rav Yosef*. He suggests they are arguing *bid-Rabbi Zeira amar Rav*: *Rav Zeira* rules in the name of *Rav* that when performing *shechita* on a *chayah* or *of*, one must place earth both below the blood and above it, not just on top. The source is the verse *ve-shaphach et damo ve-chisahu be-afar* — the blood is to be covered *be-afar*, "with earth," implying that the blood must be *within* the earth, surrounded above and below. This is a somewhat unexpected reading of the verse, yet it is the established *halacha* of *Rav Zeira*.

*Abaye* suggests that *Rav Yosef* — who instructs *dig first, then slaughter* — holds of *Rav Zeira*: one must lay a bed of loose earth before slaughtering so that blood falls onto it, and then cover from above afterward. *Rava*, who instructs *slaughter first, then dig*, apparently does not require pre-existing loose earth below, meaning he does not hold of *Rav Zeira*.

### Pre-existing Dirt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בֵּין לְדִידִי בֵּין לְרַבָּה אִית לַן דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִפַּלְגִינַן, רַבָּה סָבַר: אִי אִיכָּא עָפָר לְמַטָּה — אִין, אִי לָא — לָא. חָיְישִׁינַן דִּלְמָא מִמְּלִיךְ וְלָא שָׁחֵיט. וּלְדִידִי (אַדְּרַבָּה) הָא עֲדִיפָא, דְּאִי לָא שָׁרֵית לֵיהּ — אָתֵי לְאִמְּנוֹעֵי מִשִּׂמְחַת יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: This is not so; rather, **both** according **to me and** according **to Rabba we are** of the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Zeira, and here we disagree with regard to this** matter: **Rabba holds** that **if there is** prepared **earth beneath, yes,** in that case one may slaughter an animal, but **if there is no** earth prepared beneath, **no,** he may not slaughter it at all. Why not? Rabba says: **We are concerned** that **perhaps one will reconsider and not slaughter** it at all, and he will have dug a hole on a Festival unnecessarily. **And according to my** opinion, **on the contrary: This** situation, in which he is permitted to dig first, **is preferable, since if you do not permit him** to dig in all cases for the purpose of slaughter, **he will** be unable to eat meat and will **refrain from rejoicing on the Festival.**

*Rav Yosef* rejects *Abaye*'s diagnosis: *bein l'didi bein l'Rava it lan d'Rabbi Zeira* — both he and *Rava* hold of *Rav Zeira* and require earth below the blood as well as above. The difference between their formulations of *Beit Shammai*'s instruction reflects a different dispute entirely.

*Rava* holds *ika afar le-mata in, i lo lo*: even according to *Beit Shammai*, who permits the slaughter *lechatchila* on *Yom Tov*, this is only permitted if loose earth already exists below — that is, if the physical precondition of *Rav Zeira*'s requirement is already in place before the question arises. If there is no pre-existing loose earth and one would need to dig in order to create the lower layer, *Rava* rules that *Beit Shammai* would not permit the slaughter at all. The concern is *dilma mimrekh ve-lo shakhat* — that the person might dig the lower layer, then change his mind and decide not to slaughter, and the digging on *Yom Tov* will have been performed for nothing. Rather than risk a purposeless violation, *Rava* requires that the earth already be present.

### Yosef's Leniency

*Rav Yosef* disagrees: he would instruct the person to go ahead, dig the lower layer, then slaughter, then cover — accepting the risk that the slaughter might ultimately not take place. His reasoning is *adraba ha-adifa*: this is actually preferable, because insisting on pre-existing loose earth makes it harder for people to perform *shechita* on *Yom Tov* when they wish to, thereby depriving them of *simchat Yom Tov*. Better to allow the digging and accept the theoretical risk of abandonment than to impose a practical barrier to the joyous consumption of fresh meat on the festival.

It should be noted that this entire dispute between *Rava* and *Rav Yosef* is framed within *Beit Shammai*'s position, which permits the slaughter *lechatchila*. The practical *halacha* follows *Beit Hillel*, who requires that earth be prepared before *Yom Tov* as a precondition for slaughtering at all. The question of what happens if one slaughtered without preparation — the *di-eved* scenario where all agree one may dig and cover — will be addressed in the continuation of the *sugya*.
