# Kiddushin 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Na'arah Me'orasah

### Skilah vs. Chenek

The discussion picks up toward the bottom of 9b, where the Gemara had been analyzing the circumstances under which a man who rapes a *na'arah me'orasah* — a betrothed virgin, meaning a woman over whom *kiddushin* has taken effect but who remains physically a virgin — is punished by *skilah*, as opposed to the punishment for adultery generally, which is *chenek*. Both are capital punishments, and one ends up dead either way; nonetheless, *chazal* understood *skilah* to be the more severe of the two. That evaluative distinction is itself rooted in a broader discussion in Sanhedrin, where the principle of *ve'ahavta l're'acha kamocha* — in its formulation of *bror lo misah yafah*, selecting a fitting or pleasant form of death even for one being executed — shapes the entire architecture of the different death penalties and how damaging each is to the human body.

### Ten Rapists Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ עָלֶיהָ עֲשָׂרָה אֲנָשִׁים, וַעֲדַיִין הִיא בְּתוּלָה – כּוּלָּן בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר, אוֹמֵר אֲנִי: הָרִאשׁוֹן בִּסְקִילָה, וְכוּלָּן בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** If **ten men engaged in sexual intercourse with** a betrothed young woman, **and she is still a virgin,** as they engaged in anal intercourse with her, **they are all** punished **by stoning. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that **the first** one is punished **by stoning,** as he engaged in intercourse with a virgin young woman, **but all** the others are punished **by strangulation.** Once the first man engages in intercourse with her she is no longer considered a virgin, even if he engaged in anal intercourse with her.

The *braita* under discussion had stated: *ba'u aleha asarah anashim ve'adayin hi besulah* — ten men could rape the same woman and she would still retain the status of *besulah*. This is because the *braita* is discussing anal intercourse, which does not have the same consequences as vaginal intercourse; specifically, she remains a *besulah* afterwards, and therefore all ten rapists would be subject to *skilah* rather than *chenek*. This is a matter of dispute between *Rebbi* and the *rabbanan*.

The Gemara then asks: *ve'Rebbi hai svara minaleh* — from where does *Rebbi* derive his position? The answer is that the *pasuk* referring to marriage uses the word *u've'alah*. Had the *Torah* wished to convey only one idea, it could have simply written *u'vaal*. The additional *heh* allows *Rebbi* to derive two separate *derashos* from a single word.

The Gemara then introduces *Rava*, who uses that same *pasuk* for a different *drasha* entirely: *kiddushin hamesurin l'viah havei kiddushin* — *kiddushin* for which a sexual relationship would subsequently be permitted constitutes valid *kiddushin*. But *kiddushin she'ein mesurin l'viah* — *kiddushin* effected at a moment when a sexual relationship between the parties would be prohibited — does not constitute valid *kiddushin*. *Rava* derives this principle from *ki yikach ish ishah u've'alah*.

## Kiddushin Mesurin

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אוֹ בְעָלָהּ״, מַאי ״וּבְעָלָהּ״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כּוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** that a woman cannot be betrothed through intercourse, **let the verse write:** When a man takes a woman **or engages in intercourse with her. What is** indicated by the phrase: **"And engages in sexual intercourse with her"?** One can **learn from** the verse **all** these *halakhot*, that intercourse is a valid mode of betrothing a woman but not acquiring a maidservant, and betrothal is effective only when it is given to consummation.

*Rava*'s principle, as stated in the Gemara, is: *kiddushin hamesurin l'viah havei kiddushin, kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'viah lo havei kiddushin*. The *pasuk* could have said *o va'alah* — either take *kiddushin* or have a sexual relationship — but because it says *u've'alah*, using the conjunction "and both," we derive that the two are necessarily linked. This is the *shema mina kulu hu*.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Turning ahead to 51a, there is a significant *machlokes* between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* regarding how to understand the category of *kiddushin she'ein mesurin l'viah*. *Rashi*'s position is that the principle refers to any and all of the *arayos* — the prohibited sexual relationships. If one were to attempt *kiddushin* with a sister, grandmother, or any other *ervah*, such *kiddushin* would be entirely ineffective. This connects to the broader principle that for certain categories of *arayos*, the relationship is governed by *ein kiddushin tofsin* — the *kiddushin* does not take hold at all. Indeed, according to at least some *tannaim*, this is precisely the criterion that defines *mamzerut*: the *ervah* must be so severe that *kiddushin* would not function between the parties.

### Tosafot's Reading

*Tosafot*, however, disagrees fundamentally. *Tosafot* holds that *kiddushin she'ein mesurin l'viah* does not refer to *arayos* at all. *Tosafot* understands the principle as addressing the distinction between two categories of prohibited relationships within *halacha*. There are *chayvei lavin* — for example, a *kohen* marrying a *zonah*, or a *yisrael* marrying a *mamzeret* — where the prohibition itself is not affected by marital status in the sense that the same prohibition existed independently before any *kiddushin* took place.

*Tosafot*'s own formulation makes this explicit: *hatkadshi li b'kiddushin... she'issur habiah al yedei kiddushin* — the case *Tosafot* has in mind is one where the *issur biah* comes into being *because of* the *kiddushin* itself. For instance, if a man attempts to marry one of two sisters without specifying which one, the act of *kiddushin* with respect to one sister immediately creates a prohibition regarding the other, leaving him in a situation where neither is permitted to him due to *safek*.

### Yom Kippur Case

The *Ran* on the first *daf* of the *Rif* in *Kesuvos* raises a penetrating difficulty from the opening *mishnah* of *Maseches Yoma*, which states that a spare wife is prepared for the *Kohen Gadol* in the event that his wife dies on *Yom Kippur*, since the *Kohen Gadol* is required to be married on that day. The difficulty: one cannot perform *kiddushin* on *Yom Kippur*, because sexual relations are forbidden on *Yom Kippur*, and therefore any such *kiddushin* would constitute *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'viah* — and hence be invalid. The *Ran* is compelled to address this problem. Part of the answer must involve the fact that *kiddushin* need only be *reuyim l'viah* — suited to a sexual relationship — in a prospective sense; *motzoei Yom Kippur*, the 11th of *Tishrei*, sexual relations would immediately become permitted. But the question remains at least an interesting one, and the *Ran* must work through it carefully.

## Na'arah Me'orasah (II)

### Rebbi's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי לְעִנְיַן קְנָס דְּכוּלְּהוּ מְשַׁלְּמִי. מַאי שְׁנָא מִקְּטָלָא? שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּמֵת הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר שָׁכַב עִמָּהּ לְבַדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes with regard to** the **fine** of a rapist and a seducer **that they all pay** this fine, and they are not exempt merely because their acts of intercourse were with a non-virgin. The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** this case **different from** the **death** penalty? With respect to the death penalty they are punished as though they engaged in intercourse with a betrothed non-virgin, and yet concerning the fine, they pay as though they engaged in intercourse with a virgin. The Gemara answers: It **is different there,** with regard to capital punishment, **as the verse states: "Then the man only who lay with her shall die"** (Deuteronomy 22:25), which indicates that only one man is executed for having sexual intercourse with a virgin, and no more.

Returning to the *sugya* of *na'arah me'orasah*: the *braita* quoted the *tanna kamma* as holding that all ten rapists would be subject to *skilah*, since she remains a *besulah* throughout. *Rebbi* then adds his own position: *omer ani harishon b'skilah ve'kulum b'chenek* — it is only the first rapist who receives *skilah*; all the rest receive *chenek*. *Rebbi*'s reasoning is that the first rapist is the one who would have effected *kiddushei biah* with her, as it were — he is the one who acts in the position of the husband — and the remaining rapists are not. It is worth noting that the phrase *omer ani* — "I say" — appears in rabbinic literature to be used exclusively by *Rebbi*, and several *acharonim* have observed that this locution reflects a kind of personal humility: even as the author of the *Mishnah*, *Rebbi* presents his own positions as personal opinions rather than authoritative rulings, a characteristic notably paralleled in the accounts of his humility at the end of *Maseches Sotah*.

The Gemara then works through how *Rebbi* and the *rabbanan* each handle the available *pesukim*. The *pasuk* by *na'arah hame'orasah* uses the word *be'ulas ba'al*. *Rebbi* learns from this: *ba'al oseh ota be'ulah shelo k'darkah, ve'ein acher oseh ota be'ulah shelo k'darkah* — a woman's husband renders her a *be'ulah* through anal intercourse, but if another man anally rapes her, that does not render her a *be'ulah*. She therefore retains her *besulah* status, but the punishment for the subsequent rapists is *chenek* rather than *skilah*, since by *Rebbi*'s reading only the first rapist stands in the position of the one who would have married her by *kiddushei biah*.

### Zeira's Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן הַאי ״לְבַדּוֹ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, ״וּמֵתוּ גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״וּמֵת הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר שָׁכַב עִמָּהּ לְבַדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, what do they do with this** term **"only"?** The Gemara answers: **They require it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*. The verse states: **"Then they shall both of them die"** (Deuteronomy 22:22). This indicates that this is not the case **unless** the two of them **are equal,** i.e., both deserve punishment. But if one of them cannot be punished, e.g., if he is a minor, the other is not executed either. This is **the statement of Rabbi Oshaya. Rabbi Yonatan says** that the verse: **"Then the man only who lay with her shall die,"** indicates that in some cases only one of them is liable to receive the death penalty.

*Rabbi Zeira* then clarifies the scope of the *machlokes*: *modeh Rebbi l'inyan knas d'kulu meshalmi* — *Rebbi* agrees with the *rabbanan* that all ten rapists pay the fifty-shekel *knas*. The *machlokes* is exclusively about the form of the capital punishment. What is the basis for distinguishing the *knas* from the death penalty? The *pasuk* states *u'meis ha'ish asher shachav imah l'vado* — the man who slept with her, he alone will die. *Rebbi* uses *l'vado* to exclude the remaining nine rapists from *skilah*, limiting it to the first. As for the *rabbanan*, they use *l'vado* differently — for the *drasha* taught in a *braita*: *u'meisu gam shneihem*, requiring that both parties be equivalent, which is the position of *Rabbi Yoshiyah*; while *Rabbi Yonasan* derives his position from *u'meis ha'ish asher shachav imah l'vado* in a different way. Nonetheless, *Rebbi* is able to accommodate all the relevant *derashos* within his reading, since, as the Gemara notes, the *pasuk* could have written *be'ulas ish*; the fact that it writes *be'ulas ba'al* allows *Rebbi* to derive two distinct *halachos* — *shema mina tartei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הַאי סְבָרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״בְּעוּלַת אִישׁ״, מַאי ״בְּעוּלַת בַּעַל״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan, from where does he** derive **this conclusion** that only a woman's husband can render her a non-virgin through intercourse in an atypical manner, if he uses this verse to derive the mode of betrothal by means of sexual intercourse? The Gemara answers: **If so,** that this verse is teaching only one *halakha*, **let the verse write:** If a man be found lying with **a *beulat ish*,** literally, a woman who engaged in sexual intercourse with a man. **What** is indicated by the term **"*beulat ba'al*,"** literally, who engaged in sexual intercourse with a husband? **Learn two** *halakhot* **from it:** First, that one can betroth a woman through intercourse, and second, that only a woman's husband can render her a non-virgin by intercourse in an atypical manner.

## Techilat vs. Sof Biah

### Opening Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תְּחִילַּת בִּיאָה קוֹנָה אוֹ סוֹף בִּיאָה קוֹנָה?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Since intercourse is not a momentary act but has different stages, does **the beginning of intercourse effect acquisition or** does **the end of intercourse effect acquisition?** When exactly is the moment of betrothal?

The Gemara on 10a opens with a new *ibaya*: *techilat biah konah o sof biah konah?* — does the initial penetration of a sexual act effect *kinyan kiddushin* in the context of *kiddushei biah*, or does the completion of the act do so?

### Practical Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֶעֱרָה בָּהּ, וּפָשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְקִבְּלָה קִדּוּשִׁין מֵאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The **practical difference** resulting from this question is in a case **where** one **engaged in** only **the initial stage of intercourse with her and** in the meantime **she reached her hand out and accepted betrothal from another** man. If the beginning of sexual intercourse effects acquisition, the other man's betrothal is meaningless. If the end of sexual intercourse effects acquisition, she is betrothed to the other man.

The practical *nafka mina* is as follows: suppose a man initiated intercourse with a woman but did not complete the act, and they then separated, whereupon she accepted *kiddushin* from a different man. Which *kiddushin* takes effect? The answer depends entirely on which stage of the sexual act creates the *kinyan kiddushin*.

### Tosafot's Approaches

*Tosafot* here confronts the need to reconcile this Gemara's question with a Gemara in *Yevamos* where it appears obvious that initial penetration — *ha'ara'ah* — is what counts as a sexual act throughout *halacha*. *Tosafot* cites four distinct approaches.

The *Rashba* (of Shantz) holds that when we say *ha'ara'ah* effects a *kinyan*, that is specifically in a case where only *ha'ara'ah* occurred and nothing more. If the act was completed, then the operative *da'at* is directed toward the completion. The *Rivam* takes a different approach, holding that the husband's *da'at* is determinative: he may direct his intent toward whichever stage of the act he chooses, and that stage is what creates the *kinyan*. *Rabbeinu Tam* argues that once the Torah has included penetration within the halachic category of a sexual act, the term *bi'ah* wherever it appears actually refers to *ha'ara'ah*. Accordingly, the Gemara's question about *techilat biah* versus *sof biah* is not asking about the beginning versus the end of the full sexual act, but rather about the beginning versus the end of *ha'ara'ah* itself — the beginning of the beginning, as it were, or the end of the beginning. *Rabbeinu Nissim Gaon* — who was from North Africa and was not technically a *Gaon* in the institutional sense — distinguishes according to context: in *Yevamos*, the discussion concerns *yibbum*, where the *yevamah* is already *zekukah ve'omedet* — already legally bound — and therefore a lower threshold of sexual activity suffices to complete the legal transaction, much as *chuppah* would. In *kiddushei biah*, however, where the legal act being accomplished carries greater weight, the *da'at* is properly directed only toward *gmar biah*.

### Kohen Gadol Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל דְּקָא קָנֵי בְּתוּלָה בְּבִיאָה, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** there is a difference with regard **to a High Priest who acquires a virgin through sexual intercourse.** If only the end of intercourse effects acquisition, she is no longer a virgin at the time of the betrothal, which would mean that a High Priest cannot acquire a woman through intercourse, as it is prohibited for him to marry a non-virgin (Leviticus 21:14). **What,** then, is the *halakha*?

A further *nafka mina* concerns the *Kohen Gadol*, who has a *mitzvas aseh* to marry a *besulah*. If the initial penetration renders a woman a *be'ulah* — as the Gemara in *Yevamos* indicates — and yet the *kinyan kiddushin* is only created at the completion of the act, it would follow that it is impossible for a *Kohen Gadol* to ever effect *kiddushei biah* with his wife. The act would have transformed her status before the *kinyan* took hold. It should be noted that already in the *amoraic* period, *kiddushei biah* was prohibited as a matter of practice; but the theoretical question remains significant for understanding the *Kohen Gadol*'s obligation.

### Amemar's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: כׇּל הַבּוֹעֵל – דַּעְתּוֹ עַל גְּמַר בִּיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said in the name of Rava: Anyone who engages in sexual intercourse has the completion of** the act of **intercourse in mind,** not the beginning. Therefore, the acquisition is complete only when the act has been completed.

*Amemar* resolves the *ibaya* by transmitting a ruling in the name of *Rava*: *kol habo'el da'ato al gmar biah* — whenever a man engages in sexual intercourse, his intent is always directed toward the completion of the act. This is an established *chazakah*. The *kinyan kiddushin* is therefore created only at the end of the act, not at the initial penetration. This is the point that requires reconciliation with the Gemara in *Yevamos* — the task that the four approaches in *Tosafot* each undertake in their own way.

## Biah Nisuin Osa

### Central Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בִּיאָה נִשּׂוּאִין עוֹשָׂה, אוֹ אֵירוּסִין עוֹשָׂה?
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does the **sexual intercourse** that is performed to effect betrothal **effect marriage** at the same time that it effects betrothal, **or** does it **effect** only **betrothal?**

A further *ibaya* follows: *biah nisuin oseh o eirusin oseh?* — does *kiddushei biah* accomplish both stages of marriage in a single act, or does it effect only the first stage, *kiddushin* (equivalent to *eirusin*)? With *kiddushei kessef* and *kiddushei shtar*, the *kessef* or *shtar* effects the first stage, and a separate process of *chuppah* is required to accomplish *nisuin*. The question here is whether *kiddushei biah* might bypass this two-stage structure entirely, reaching *nisuin* directly.

### Three Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ – לְיוֹרְשָׁהּ, וְלִיטַמֵּא לָהּ, וּלְהָפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ נִשּׂוּאִין עוֹשָׂה – יוֹרְשָׁהּ וּמִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ וּמֵיפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵירוּסִין עוֹשָׂה – אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ וְאֵינוֹ מִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ וְאֵינוֹ מֵיפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The **practical difference** that arises from this question concerns all cases where it matters whether a woman is betrothed or married. For example, one issue is whether the act allows him **to inherit** property from **her.** If he betroths her through intercourse, does he inherit her property when she dies as he would if they were married? **And** if he is a priest, is he required **to become ritually impure to** bury **her,** as he must do for his wife? **And** similarly, does a betrothal through intercourse allow him **to nullify her vows** by himself, without her father's participation? **If you say** that intercourse **effects marriage, he inherits** property from **her, and he becomes impure to** bury **her, and he nullifies her vows** alone. **And if you say** that **it effects** only **betrothal, he does not inherit** property from **her, and he does not become ritually impure to** bury **her, and he cannot nullify her vows** alone.

The *nafka mina* is spelled out in three practical areas. First, *yerushah*: a husband inherits his wife's property only once they have reached *nisuin*. Second, *tumah*: if the husband is a *kohen*, he may become *tamei* to participate in her burial only if they have reached *nisuin*. Third, *hafaras nedarim*: the husband's unilateral power to annul his wife's vows belongs to him alone only at the stage of *nisuin*; during *eirusin*, it is a combined power shared by both the father and the husband. As the Gemara states: *im amarta nisuin oseh, yorshah u'mitamei lah u'meifer nedareiah*; and *im amarta eirusin oseh, eino yorshah ve'eino mitamei lah ve'eino meifer nedareiah*.

### Abaye's Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאָב זַכַּאי בְּבִתּוֹ בְּקִדּוּשֶׁיהָ בְּכֶסֶף בִּשְׁטָר וּבְבִיאָה, וְזַכַּאי בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ וּבְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּבַהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ, וּמְקַבֵּל אֶת גִּיטָּהּ, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת בְּחַיֶּיהָ. נִישֵּׂאת – יָתֵר עָלָיו הַבַּעַל שֶׁאוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת בְּחַיֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from the following mishna (*Ketubot* 46b): **A father has authority** over **his daughter with regard to her betrothal,** whether it is **through money, through a document, or through sexual intercourse.** Likewise, a father **has a right to items she** has **found, and to her earnings, and to** effect **the nullification of her vows,** i.e., a father may nullify his daughter's vows. **And he accepts her bill of divorce** on her behalf if she is divorced from betrothal before she becomes a grown woman. **And** although he inherits her property when she dies, e.g., property she inherited from her mother's family, **he does not enjoy the profits** of her property **during her lifetime.** If the daughter **married, the husband has more** rights and obligations **than** her father had before the marriage, **as he enjoys the profits** of her property **during her lifetime.**

*Abaye* brings a *braita* in support: *ha'av zakai bevito bekiddusheha bechessef bishtar u'veviah* — the father has rights to his daughter's *kiddushin* effected by *kessef*, *shtar*, or *biah* — and then lists his various entitlements: rights to objects she finds, to the work of her hands, to the annulment of her vows, to the receipt of her *get*, and the fact that he does not receive the produce of her independently owned property during her lifetime. The *braita* then continues: *nisseit, yeter alav habaal she'ochel peirot bechayyeha* — once she marries, the husband has greater rights than the father did, specifically including the right to the produce of the wife's property during her lifetime. When a woman brings real estate into a marriage, the husband holds certain rights to the produce of that property during the marriage.

### Two Stages Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי ״בִּיאָה״, וְקָתָנֵי ״נִישֵּׂאת״! כִּי קָתָנֵי ״נִישֵּׂאת״ – אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna **teaches** that a man has rights to his daughter's betrothal through **sexual intercourse, and** it subsequently **teaches:** If the daughter **married.** This indicates that intercourse effects only betrothal. The Gemara rejects this claim: **When it teaches:** If the daughter **married,** that is referring **to the other** modes mentioned here, i.e., betrothal by means of money or a document. In these cases there is a difference between betrothal and marriage, while it is possible that betrothal through intercourse effects marriage as well.

The *braita* thus mentions *biah* as one stage and then refers separately to *nisseit* as a subsequent stage — presenting *biah* and *nisuin* as two distinct moments. This appears to constitute a direct challenge to the position that *biah nisuin oseh*, since if *biah* already accomplishes *nisuin*, there would be no need for a distinct *nisseit*.

### Defense of Biah Nisuin

The Gemara deflects this challenge by arguing: even according to the position that *biah nisuin oseh*, one could read the *braita*'s reference to *nisseit* as applying only to cases of *kiddushei kessef* or *kiddushei shtar*, where *chuppah* is still required to reach *nisuin*. For *kiddushei biah*, *nisuin* would be accomplished immediately and automatically. The *braita* thus fits either position, and is not a decisive proof against *biah nisuin oseh*.

## Age of Three Braita

### Age Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִיאָה, וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ יָבָם – קְנָאָהּ, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וּמְטַמְּאָה אֶת בּוֹעֲלָהּ
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita*: A girl who is **three years and one day old can be betrothed through intercourse, and if** her ***yavam* engaged in intercourse with her, he has acquired her. And** if she is married, **one is liable** if he engages in intercourse **with her, due to** her status as **a married woman. And** if she is impure as a menstruating woman, **she renders one who engages in intercourse with her ritually impure** for seven days.

*Rava* now brings another *braita*: *bat shalosh shanim veyom echad mitkadeshet beviah* — from the age of three years and one day, a girl's sexual act carries halachic consequence. As *Rashi* notes, below this age the act is treated as *kenoten etzba ba'ayin* — like putting a finger in the eye — meaning it does not register as a sexual act at all in halachic terms. The age of three is thus an arbitrary but fixed halachic threshold. It should be acknowledged that this topic intersects with serious questions regarding child abuse and how *halacha* addresses such violations from multiple angles — a point that is not uniquely a modern concern, as *Tosafot* itself engages with it here.

### Yibum Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב תַּחְתּוֹן כְּעֶלְיוֹן,
>
> **Translation:** And he **renders impure the object upon which he lies like the upper one.** One who engages in sexual intercourse with a menstruating woman renders the objects upon which he lies ritually impure even if he has no direct contact with them.

The *braita* continues: *ve'im ba aleha yavam kna'ah* — if her husband has died and she falls before the *yavam* (brother-in-law), the *yavam* can effect *yibbum* through sexual intercourse with her. Furthermore, *vachayavim aleha mishum eshet ish* — anyone who thereafter has relations with her violates the prohibition of *eshet ish*, adultery.

### Bo'el Niddah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נִישֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִכׇּל עֲרָיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה – מוּמָתִין עַל יָדָהּ, וְהִיא פְּטוּרָה. וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִן הַפְּסוּלִין – פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if she marries a priest she may partake of *teruma*** from that point onward. **And if one of those with whom sexual relations are forbidden by the Torah** (see Leviticus, chapter 18) **engages in intercourse with her, he is put to death due to** his sin with **her, and she is exempt** from punishment as a minor. **And if someone of unfit** lineage, i.e., a man who would disqualify her from marrying a priest if he engaged in sexual intercourse with her, **engages in intercourse with her, he has disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood.** This concludes the *baraita*.

The *braita* then states: *u'metama bo'alah letame mishkav tachton ke'elyon* — whoever has relations with her during a state of *niddah* is subject to the *tumah* of *bo'el niddah*. One might ask how a girl of three could be in a state of *niddah*; various hormonal conditions can produce vaginal bleeding even in very young girls, and the age of puberty has varied considerably across historical periods.

The *halacha* of *tumah* transfer in this context deserves attention. The *Torah* prescribes not only a prohibition on having relations during *niddah*, but also a transfer of *tumah* from the *niddah* to the *bo'el*. A *niddah* who sits upon couches — even a great many in succession, as in the image of the Princess and the Pea — renders all of them *mishkav hazav* or *mishkav haniddah*, transferring *tumah* that can itself defile people and vessels, *letame adam vechelim*. The *bo'el niddah*, however, becomes an *av hatumah* through a different mechanism: if he sits on something, the item does not become *mishkav elyon* in the full expansive sense, but rather only *mishkav tachton* — transferred by contact rather than through the extended notion of sitting atop multiple layers. This is discussed at length throughout *Seder Taharot*. The key distinction is that *tumah* normally descends in level: an *av hatumah* touching something renders it *rishon letumah*, and so on downward. Here, however, there is something closer to a horizontal transfer: the *niddah* and the *bo'el niddah* both stand as *av hatumah*, but the scope of what each can subsequently defile differs — she can render things *av hatumah* through *mishkav*, while he can only produce *vlad hatumah*.

### Kohen Marriage

The *braita* continues: *ve'im nisset le'kohen tochal biterumah* — if she reaches *nisuin* with a *kohen*, she may eat *terumah*. And if a man who is an *ervah* to her has relations with her, *mumatim al yadah* — he is executed on her account — while *vehi petura*, she being a minor is exempt from any punishment. Furthermore, *im ba'ala echad min hapesulim, pesulah min hakehunah* — if she has relations with someone who disqualifies her from marrying a *kohen*, that disqualification takes effect; after the age of three, the act counts as a sexual act, so the status of *nivelah lapasul* applies and bars her from future marriage to a *kohen*.

### Rava's Two Stages

Just as in *Abaye*'s *braita*, *Rava*'s *braita* here also appears to distinguish between the stage of *biah* and the subsequent stage of *nisseit* as two separate moments — *katani biah vekatani nisset*. This appears again to challenge the position that *biah nisuin oseh*. The Gemara deflects once more, however: the clause *nisseit le'kohen* need not be read as a distinct second stage separate from *biah*; it can be read as saying that if the *nisuin* accomplished through *biah* was to a *kohen*, then she may eat *terumah*. In other words, the *braita* can be harmonized with *biah nisuin oseh* by reading *nisseit* not as a temporally subsequent act but as a description of the character of the *nisuin* already effected. Both *Abaye* and *Rava* have brought *beraisos* that seem to distinguish between *biah* and *niset*, and the Gemara finds a way to deflect each challenge — evidently working to sustain the position of *biah nisuin oseh*.

## Bag-Bag Debate

### Opening Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וּכְבָר שָׁלַח יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בַּג בַּג אֵצֶל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה לִנְצִיבִין: שָׁמַעְתִּי עָלֶיךָ שֶׁאַתָּה אוֹמֵר אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה. שָׁלַח לוֹ: וְאַתָּה אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן? מוּחְזְקַנִי בְּךָ שֶׁאַתָּה בָּקִי בְּחַדְרֵי תוֹרָה, לִדְרוֹשׁ בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר אִי אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **And Yoḥanan ben Bag Bag already sent** a message **to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira in** the city of **Netzivin: I heard about you that you say** that **the daughter of a non-priest betrothed** to a priest **may partake of *teruma*.** Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira **sent** back this reply **to him: And do you not say so? I know about you that you are an expert in** many **areas of Torah; do you not know how to teach** *halakhot* **based on an *a fortiori*** inference?

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* of a different character: *uchvar shalach Yochanan ben Bag-Bag etzel Yehuda ben Beteira le-Nitzivin* — *Rabbi Yochanan ben Bag-Bag* sent a message to *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* in *Nitzivin*. *Ben Bag-Bag* had heard that *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* issued a ruling that *arusa bat Yisrael ochelet biterumah* — a *Yisrael* woman who has undergone *kiddushin* with a *kohen* may eat *terumah* already at the stage of *eirusin*. *Ben Bag-Bag* responds: I did not think you held that way. He then adds: *muchzakani becha she'ata baki bechadrei Torah lidrosh kal vachomer* — I considered you deeply learned in the inner chambers of Torah and capable of constructing a *kal vachomer*. This comment raises an interesting point: a *kal vachomer* is an argument of logic, yet *ben Bag-Bag* frames it as requiring fluency in *halacha*. The reason appears to be that one must know the full range of sources and parameters of the system to know where and how a *kal vachomer* may properly be applied.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית, שֶׁאֵין בִּיאָתָהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה – כַּסְפָּהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה, זוֹ, שֶׁבִּיאָתָהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁכַּסְפָּהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה? אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁתִּכָּנֵס לַחוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *a fortiori* inference is as follows: **If** in the case of **a Canaanite maidservant, whose sexual intercourse** with a priest **does not allow her to partake of *teruma*,** as she is not betrothed to him, **her money,** i.e., if a priest acquires her through money, **allows her to partake of *teruma*, is it not logical** with regard to **this** betrothed woman, **whose sexual intercourse** with a priest **allows her to partake of *teruma*,** that **her** betrothal **money** from the priest should **allow her to partake of *teruma*?** If this is indeed the case, the daughter of a non-priest betrothed to a priest should be permitted to partake of *teruma*. **But what can I do, as the Sages said:** By rabbinic law, **the daughter of a non-priest betrothed** to a priest **may not partake of *teruma* until she enters the** wedding **canopy.**

*Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*'s *kal vachomer* runs as follows. A *shifcha kna'anit* — a Canaanite female slave — whose sexual intercourse with her master (which is itself prohibited) does not allow her to eat *terumah*, nevertheless may eat *terumah* by virtue of the monetary purchase (*kessef*) that acquired her. A married woman, whose *biah* does effect her entitlement to eat *terumah* — is it not logically compelling that her *kessef kiddushin* should also entitle her to eat *terumah*? The reasoning is: if *kessef* works for a *shifcha* even where *biah* does not, then certainly *kessef* should work for a wife where *biah* does. This is the *kal vachomer*.

### Rabbinic Decree

*Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* then anticipates the objection: *aval ma'ase, she'harei amru chachamim ein arusa bat Yisrael ochelet biterumah ad she'tikanes lechuppah* — but in practice, the rabbis ruled that a *bat Yisrael* who is *me'orasah* may not eat *terumah* until after *chuppah*. This is a *gezerah derabbanan*. The reason for this rabbinic decree, established a few *dafim* earlier, is the concern that during *eirusin*, when she still lives in her parents' home, she may share the *terumah* with her *Yisrael* family members, who would then come to eat *terumah* impermissibly. A question arises regarding a *bat kohen* marrying into a *kohen* family, where *terumah* is permitted regardless — whether the *gezerah* applies in such a case is worth consideration, though it is not resolved here.

### Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בְּבִיאָה שֶׁעַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה, וְכֶסֶף שֶׁעַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה – בְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ מֵיכָל אָכְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of the comparison between money and sexual intercourse? **If** it is referring to a case **of sexual intercourse by means of** a wedding **canopy,** i.e., after they are married, **and** betrothal through **money** where the marriage is then completed **by means of** a wedding **canopy, in both** cases **she may partake of** *teruma* even by rabbinic law, as she is a full-fledged married woman.

*Yochanan ben Bag-Bag* then challenges the *kal vachomer* itself. He asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If you are talking about *biah* accompanied by *chuppah* compared to *kessef* accompanied by *chuppah*, then of course she eats *terumah* in both cases because she is already *nesuah*. If you are talking about *biah* with *chuppah* versus *kessef* without *chuppah*, then you are comparing two stages of marriage against one — *hacha tarti vehacha chada* — and it is not surprising that they produce different outcomes. The *kal vachomer* lacks logical force because the two cases are not genuinely comparable.

### Reformulated Kal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, בְּבִיאָה שֶׁעַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה, וְכֶסֶף שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה – הָכָא תַּרְתֵּי, וְהָכָא חֲדָא! אֶלָּא לָאו בְּבִיאָה שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה, וְכֶסֶף שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Or rather** one could say that this comparison is referring to **sexual intercourse by means of** a wedding **canopy, and** betrothal through **money not by means of** a wedding **canopy.** If so, the *a fortiori* inference is not valid, as **here,** in the case of intercourse, there are **two** stages of acquisition, intercourse followed by the wedding canopy, **and here,** in the case of money, there is only **one** stage. **Rather, is it not** correct to say that the comparison between money and intercourse is referring **to sexual intercourse not by means of** a wedding **canopy and** also **money not by means of** a wedding **canopy?**

The Gemara therefore reformulates *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*'s *kal vachomer*: the argument should be understood as comparing *biah* without *chuppah* to *kessef* without *chuppah*. This formulation is significant, because it represents a support for the position of *biah nisuin oseh*: if *biah* without *chuppah* is taken as clearly sufficient to allow *terumah* consumption — to the extent that it forms the stronger side of the *kal vachomer* — this only makes sense if *biah* accomplishes *nisuin* directly, rendering it simply stronger than *kessef*. But if *biah* only accomplishes *eirusin*, then both *kessef* and *biah* would be equivalent in effect, and one could not constitute a self-evident premise from which to derive the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא נִשּׂוּאִין עוֹשָׂה – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דַּאֲלִימָא לַהּ בִּיאָה מִכֶּסֶף. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ קִדּוּשִׁין עוֹשָׂה, מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דִּמְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that intercourse by itself **effects marriage,** it is **due to that** reason that it **is obvious to** Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira **that** the mode of **sexual intercourse is stronger than money,** and allows her to partake of *teruma*. **But if you say** that sexual intercourse **effects** only **betrothal, what is different here,** in the case of betrothal through intercourse, **that it is obvious to him** that it allows her to partake of *teruma*, **and what is different here,** in the case of betrothal through money, **that he is uncertain** and must derive the matter through an *a fortiori* inference? In both cases she is only betrothed.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* then offers an alternative reading: we may after all be speaking of *biah* accompanied by *chuppah* versus *kessef* without *chuppah*. The objection of *hacha tarti vehacha chada* can be answered by restructuring the *kal vachomer* as follows. A *shifcha kna'anit*, for whom *biah* — even with *chuppah* — does not allow her to eat *terumah*, nonetheless may eat *terumah* by virtue of *kessef* alone, without *chuppah*. A wife, for whom *biah* together with *chuppah* does work to allow her to eat *terumah* — should not *kessef* alone, without *chuppah*, also work? The asymmetry between *shifcha* and *wife* now drives the argument.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ בְּבִיאָה שֶׁעַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה וְכֶסֶף שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה. וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: ״הָכָא תַּרְתֵּי וְהָכָא חֲדָא״ – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִיהָא אִיתֵיהּ;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Actually, I will say to you** that he is speaking **about sexual intercourse by means of** a wedding **canopy and money not by means of** a wedding **canopy. And** as for **that which you said** in opposition to this *a fortiori* inference: **Here** there are **two** stages of acquisition, **and here** there is only **one** stage. **In any event, there is** a valid ***a fortiori*** inference.

This reformulated *kal vachomer* is noted to rest on the assumption that the role of *kessef* in purchasing a slave and the role of *kessef kiddushin* in marriage are analogous. The Gemara implicitly highlights the limits of that analogy: the *kinyan* of acquiring a slave is a one-stage process, while marriage is a two-stage process, and therefore *kessef* in the two contexts plays fundamentally different roles.

The ultimate conclusion is that *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*'s *kal vachomer*, however reformulated, is met by the answer that the rabbis instituted a *gezerah* preventing a *me'orasah bat Yisrael* from eating *terumah* until *chuppah* — and the entire discussion ultimately serves to establish that there is no need to read *biah nisuin oseh* into the sources. The *kal vachomer* of *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*, as understood through *Ravina*'s reading, concerned not a *de'oraisa* question but a *derabbanan* one: *me'd'oraisa*, *mifshet peshita lei d'achla* — on the level of *de'oraisa*, it was obvious to *Ravina* that a *me'orasah* could eat *terumah*. The entire exchange between *ben Bag-Bag* and *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* was therefore about the *gezerah* of the rabbis, specifically whether a *me'orasah* need not worry about *simpon*.

## Simpon Concern

### Two Concerns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּאָכְלָה, וּמִדְּרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּשְׁלַח לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that **by Torah law, it is obvious to** Yoḥanan ben Bag Bag **that** she **may partake of** *teruma* also when she is betrothed to a priest through money alone, as she is acquired with his money. **And** the question **that he sent him** is referring to the *halakha* **by rabbinic law.**

The Gemara now clarifies what *Ravina* understood to be the real content of *ben Bag-Bag*'s message to *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*. What *ben Bag-Bag* had actually heard was that *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* rules that *arusa bat Yisrael ochelet biterumah velo chayshinan lesimpon* — that she may eat *terumah* and we do not worry about *simpon*. The question is accordingly not about the *de'oraisa* entitlement to eat *terumah*, which is uncontested, but about whether either of the two rabbinic concerns — sharing with family members, or *simpon* — should prevent her from eating *terumah* already at the *eirusin* stage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: שָׁמַעְתִּי עָלֶיךָ שֶׁאַתָּה אוֹמֵר אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, וְלָא חָיְשַׁתְּ לְסִימְפּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina elaborates: **And this is** the question **that he sent to him: I heard about you that you say** that in practice **the daughter of a non-priest betrothed** to a priest **may partake of *teruma*, and we are not concerned about an annulling factor [*simfon*],** i.e., that he might find some blemish in her through which the betrothal would be considered retroactively invalid. If so, she would have eaten *teruma* when she was not permitted to do so. And it was with regard to this issue that Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira **sent to him: And do you not say this? I know about you that you are an expert in** many **areas of Torah; do you not know how to teach** *halakhot* **based on an *a fortiori*** inference?

*Simpon*, as *Rashi* defines it — *kol davar hamevatel davar karui simpon* — refers to anything that would retroactively undermine a prior act. In context: *mishum simpon, shema yimatzei bah mum venimtzu kiddushei taut* — there is a concern that after the woman begins eating *terumah*, the husband may discover a *mum* (a physical defect) that he was unaware of, making the entire *kiddushin* a *kiddushei taut*, a mistaken betrothal. If the *kiddushin* is retroactively annulled, she will have been a *bat Yisrael* consuming *terumah* illegitimately. The *simpon* concern is therefore about the risk of retroactive undoing. It should be noted that the question of *kiddushei taut* based on discovered defects — particularly psychological and behavioral ones — sits at the very forefront of contemporary *agunah* discussions.

### Slave Defects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית שֶׁאֵין בִּיאָתָהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה – כַּסְפָּהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה, וְלָא חָיְשִׁינַן לְסִימְפּוֹן, זוֹ שֶׁבִּיאָתָהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁכַּסְפָּהּ מַאֲכִילָתָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה, וְלֹא נֵיחוּשׁ לְסִימְפּוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The *a fortiori* inference is as follows: **If a Canaanite maidservant, whose** engaging in **intercourse** with a priest **does not allow her to partake of *teruma*,** and yet **her** receiving purchase **money allows her to partake of *teruma*, and we are not concerned about *simfon*,** i.e., that the Canaanite maidservant may have some physical blemish that nullifies her purchase retroactively, **is it not logical** with regard to **this** betrothed woman, **whose intercourse** with a priest **allows her to partake of *teruma*, that her** receiving betrothal **money** from the priest should **allow her to partake of *teruma*** even before entering the wedding canopy, **and we should not be concerned about *simfon*?**

The Gemara now addresses why the *simpon* concern does not apply to a slave transaction. The reason involves the nature of slave acquisition and the kinds of *mumim* that might be discovered. If the defect is visible — *mumim shebagaluy* — the master already saw it when he made the purchase: *haka chazi lei*. If it is a hidden defect on a covered part of the body, that too is not a problem in the context of acquiring a slave — *mai nafka lei minei, limalacha kaba'ei lei velo ichpat lei* — because the owner purchased the slave for labor, whether in the home or in the field, and a physical defect on a hidden part of the body is irrelevant to the slave's capacity to work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא גַּנָּב אוֹ קוּבְיוּסְטוּס – הִגִּיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** And even if this slave **is discovered** to be **a thief or a gambler [*kuvyustus*],** which are considered hidden blemishes that affect his work, **it has come to him,** i.e., the slave is acquired by the one who purchased him and the transaction is non-refundable.

### Slave vs. Wife

A further distinction: for a wife, the concern of *simpon* exists because marriage is a two-stage process in which the husband has not yet taken full possession of the relationship; the possibility of retroactive annulment is live. For a slave, by contrast, the acquisition is complete in one stage and there is no analogous opening for retroactive undoing based on hidden defects of a physical nature.

### Ganav Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אָמְרַתְּ, לִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין אוֹ (נִכְתַּב) [מוּכְתָּב] לַמַּלְכוּת? הָנְהוּ קָלָא אִית לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The reason is that the buyer should have suspected behavior of this kind, which is common among slaves, and therefore he suffers the loss. **What do you say?** Perhaps he discovered that the slave is **an armed bandit** and subject to be killed by the government for this, **or sentenced** to death **by the government** for another reason, and is sought by the authorities. This is not a valid claim, since **these** matters **generate publicity,** and therefore he is assumed to have taken the risk into consideration.

The Gemara does introduce one category of slave defect that remains a concern: *nimtza ganav o kubyustos hegi'o* — if the slave turns out to be a thief or a *kubyustos* (a kidnapper or violent criminal), the purchase stands and the buyer must accept it. Why? *Eino din, listim mezuyan* — if he is an armed robber, *nichtav lamalchut, kol it leihu* — if he has been prosecuted by the government, his criminal record is already publicly known. This means the buyer had access to that information and cannot claim it was hidden.

This raises the question, however, about *petty* theft — a *ganav* in the simple sense of a kleptomaniac or ordinary thief. A behavioral or psychological defect of this kind is not obviously well-known, and the *braita*'s formulation of *nimtza ganav* might encompass it. Indeed, *Rashi* in his explanation — *nimtza ganav eino batel bekach, deha kayma lan hamocher eved lachaveiro venimtzu ganav o kubyustos higi'o* — ties this to the *braita*'s ruling. The implication is that for a *ganav* in the more ordinary sense, there may still be an opening to treat such a behavioral defect as a *mum* that would warrant concern — one would need to thread the needle carefully with the *baraisa*.

## Rambam-Ra'avad

### Rambam Position

Turning back to 10a, there is an important *machlokes* between the *Rambam* and the *Ra'avad* regarding the role of anal intercourse in the law of rape and the payment of *knas*. The *Tosafot Rid HaZaken* — which in *Maseches Kiddushin* is most likely the work of *Rav Avraham Min HaHar*, whose primary project in this tractate was defending the *Rambam* against his critics, a project characteristic of the Provençal *chachamim* — addresses this directly. In the narrow-lined commentary, it states: *dehaRambam katav bena'arah bivtulah perek alef she'eino meshalem knas ela kedarkah* — the *Rambam* rules in *Hilchos Na'arah Besulah* (chapter 1) that the *knas* of fifty *shekel* applies only to vaginal rape, not to anal rape.

### Ra'avad Objection

The *Ra'avad* objects strenuously, arguing that this Gemara appears to fully equate anal and vaginal intercourse in the context of rape. The *Tosafot Rid HaZaken* attempts to explain the *Rambam*'s position and reconcile it with the other *Gemaras* in *Ketubot*, but the difficulty is genuine. Underlying the *Rambam*'s position is a conceptual question about what the *Torah* construes as the crime of rape — and in particular, the role that the woman's status as a *besulah* plays in that crime. Since only the husband makes her a *be'ulah* through anal intercourse, while any other man's anal act does not alter her *besulah* status at all, the *Rambam* may be reading the crime as essentially tied to the vaginal violation that transforms her *besulah* status. The *Tosafot Rid HaZaken* works through these considerations in the course of defending the *Rambam*'s *shita* against the *Ra'avad*'s challenge.
