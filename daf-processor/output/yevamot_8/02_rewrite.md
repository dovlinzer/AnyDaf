# Yevamot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yibum & Arayot

### Review: Yibum Override

The daf begins approximately fifteen lines down, at the words *amar lei Rav Acha mi-Difti*. To recall where we left off: after many dappin establishing why one might have thought that the *mitzvah* of *yibum* would apply even in a case of *arayot*, the Gemara worked through the entire framework of *asei docheh lo ta'asei*, ultimately concluding that this principle could not serve as a valid basis, since it does not apply where *karet* is at stake.

The Gemara then arrived at a different *hava amina* entirely. The very case of *yibum* is itself a case of *arayot* — namely, *eshet ach*, one's brother's wife — and this fact might have provided a basis for assuming that *yibum* could apply even in the presence of other *arayot*. The reasoning: either through the principle of *keivan de-ishtari ishtari* — once something is permitted to enable a particular outcome, other related obstacles should similarly be set aside — or through the logic that since a verse links all the *arayot* to one another, what is true of one may be true of the rest.

It is on this basis that we require the verse containing the word *aleha*. That verse is in the prohibition of the wife's sister: *ve-ishah el achotah lo tikach litzror legalot ervatah aleha be-chayeha* — do not take a woman alongside her sister as a rival wife, to uncover her nakedness upon her, during her lifetime. The word *aleha* links this verse to *yevamah yavo aleha*, the operative phrase of *yibum*, thereby establishing that the prohibition of *achos ishah* applies even in the context of *yibum*.

### Rav Acha's Question

Rav Acha mi-Difti raised a pointed question to Ravina: *mikdi kol arayot itka'u le-eshet ach ve-itka'u le-achos ishah* — the verse links all *arayot* to one another, and we now have two *arayot* in play: *eshet ach*, which tells us that *yibum* is operative, and *achos ishah*, which tells us, via *aleha*, that *yibum* does not apply. Given this equipoise, why do we assume that *achos ishah* sets the rule? Why not link all other *arayot* to *eshet ach* and conclude that *yibum* applies in all such cases, with *achos ishah* standing as the sole exception?

### Two-Prohibition Rule

The Gemara offers two responses. The first is simply a matter of interpretive default: *le-kula u-le-chumra, le-chumra makshinan* — when one has a choice between linking toward leniency or stringency, one defaults to stringency.

The second answer is conceptually richer: *hacha terei issurei ve-hacha chad issura, trei mi-trei yalfanan*. Any other *ervah* arising in a *yibum* context carries two distinct prohibitions — the standing prohibition of *eshet ach* that always applies to a brother's wife, plus the additional prohibition specific to that *ervah* — daughter-in-law, mother-in-law, or whichever it might be. *Achos ishah* in a *yibum* context is exactly analogous: it too carries the prohibition of *eshet ach* combined with the prohibition of *achos ishah*. The simple case of *yibum* — *eshet ach* alone — carries only one prohibition. It is therefore far more apt to derive the rule for other *arayot* in a *yibum* context from *achos ishah*, which like them carries two prohibitions, than from the isolated case of *eshet ach* with its single prohibition. One does not derive a two-prohibition case from a one-prohibition case: *terei mi-terei yalfanan, aval me-chad lo yalfanan*.

### Rava: No Verse Needed

Rava now stakes out a more radical position: *erva gufah lo tzericha kra de-ein asei docheh lo ta'asei she-yesh bo karet*. The prohibition of *ervah* requires no verse at all, because it would never have occurred to anyone that *yibum* — a positive commandment — could override a *lo ta'asei* that carries *karet*. The principle of *asei docheh lo ta'asei* simply does not operate in the presence of *karet*, and so no *hava amina* exists in the first place.

One might immediately object: but we just established a stronger *hava amina*, the argument of *keivan de-ishtari ishtari* — once one prohibition has been set aside to enable *yibum*, perhaps another should be as well. Tosafot addresses this directly. First, Rava may simply not hold of Ulla's principle of *keivan de-ishtari ishtari* at all. But Tosafot goes further and offers a more precise conceptual distinction: the case where *keivan de-ishtari ishtari* operates — the classic example involving a *ba'al keri* and a *metzora* — involves a single shared *issur*, namely a *tamei* person entering the *Beis HaMikdash*. Regardless of the source of one's *tumah*, the prohibition being waived is one and the same. By contrast, in our case, the Torah waives the prohibition of *eshet ach* to enable *yibum*, but the prohibition of *achos ishah* is an entirely separate *issur* with its own name and status. Allowing one *issur ervah* does not entail allowing a different *issur ervah*. The two prohibitions are not merely different degrees of intensity of one thing; they are categorically distinct. Even if one accepts *keivan de-ishtari ishtari* in principle, it would not apply across categorically distinct *issurim*.

Rava's conclusion, then, is that one never had a genuine *hava amina* that *yibum* could override *ervah*. What, then, does the verse accomplish? *Ki itztarich kra le-maseir tzarah* — the verse was needed not to exclude *ervah*, which we knew, but to establish the law of the *tzarah*, the co-wife. This is precisely the *chiddush* that our Mishnah opens with: *chamesh esrei nashim potrot tzaroteihen* — fifteen women exempt their co-wives. The Mishnah highlights the exemption of the *tzarah*, not the exemption of the *ervah* herself, because the latter is taken for granted.

The mechanism works as follows: the word *aleha* links the verse to *yibum*, placing the verse in the context of *yibum*. Once the verse is speaking about a *yibum* context, it is positioned to address the *litzror* clause — that not only does the *ervah* herself not undergo *yibum*, but her co-wife does not either.

## Rava: Tzarah is Key

### Gemara Objects

The Gemara raises an objection to Rava's reading. If *ervah* requires no verse, why does the *braisa* itself appear to be proving precisely that point? The *braisa* uses the *aleha* linkage to conclude that *yibum* does not apply in the case of *achos ishah*, and only then proceeds to ask: *ein li ela hi — tzarasa minayin?* The *braisa*'s very structure seems to treat the *ervah* exclusion as the primary *chiddush* requiring derivation, with the *tzarah* exclusion following as a secondary consequence.

### Rava Responds

Rava's answer is that the *braisa* is not trying to prove the *ervah* case on its merits. It establishes the *ervah* context only as a necessary step toward proving the case of *tzarah*. The *braisa* must first get the verse talking about *yibum* and *ervah* — not because we needed proof of the *ervah* rule, which we could have derived ourselves — but in order to introduce the *litzror* clause and derive from it the law of the *tzarah*.

The objection is then extended: the *braisa* does not stop with *achos ishah*. It then asks how we know the rule applies to all other *arayot*, and derives this through a *mah matzinu* — reasoning by analogy from *achos ishah* to other *arayot*. And similarly, it asks how we know that the *tzarot* of those other *arayot* are also excluded, and again uses analogy. The Gemara suggests that this expanded structure also seems to prove the *arayot* exclusion as a primary *chiddush*. Rava's response is the same: the entire progression through *achos ishah* and the other *arayot* serves only to set up the corresponding *tzarah* cases. At each stage, the *braisa* is moving toward the law of *tzarah*; the *ervah* cases are vehicles, not destinations.

### Original Braisa Cited

To ground this discussion, it is worth recalling the *braisa* as it appears on 3b. The text reads: *isha el achosah lo tikach litzror* — the verse against marrying a wife's sister. Then: *aleha* — *mah talmud lomar?* What does this teach? The verse is juxtaposed with *yevamah yavo aleha* — the operative language of *yibum* — through a *gezeirah shavah* on the shared word *aleha*. The *braisa* concludes that this prohibition applies *bimkom mitzvah*, in a context of *yibum*, and the Torah says: *lo sikach*. It then asks: *ein li ela hi, tzaratah minayin?* — I have derived only her; from where do I derive the co-wife? The answer: *talmud lomar litzror*. And then: *ein li ela tzaratah, tzaras tzaratah minayin?* — from where do I derive the co-wife of the co-wife? The answer: the doubled form *litzror* rather than the simple *latzor*.

The *braisa* then extends to all other *arayot* through a *mah matzinu*: just as *achos ishah* is a *ervah* for which one is liable, so too all other *arayot* are excluded from *yibum*, and so too their co-wives.

Rava reads the entire structure as oriented toward *tzarah*. The *braisa* needed to get *achos ishah* into a *yibum* context in order to generate the *litzror* derivation; it needed to extend the *arayot* framework in order to cover the *tzarot* of those other *arayot*. None of it was proving something we didn't already know about *ervah* itself.

### Rabbi's Drasha

The Gemara now introduces a competing tradition: *Rabbi omer* — Rabbi derives the same laws from different verses. The relevant verses in the *yibum* passage read: *velakachah lo leishah veyibmah* — and he shall take her to be his wife and perform *yibum* with her. Rabbi notes that the Torah could have written *velakach* and *veyibam* — and he shall take, and he shall perform *yibum* — without the pronominal suffix. Instead it writes *velakachah* and *veyibmah* — her specifically. The feminine suffix, Rabbi argues, is exclusionary: it is *her* he takes and performs *yibum* with, not others in certain related situations. Specifically: one *heh* excludes the *ervah* herself from *yibum*, and the other excludes the *tzaras ervah*.

The reading thus yields: *velakachah* — her but not an *ervah*; *veyibmah* — her but not the co-wife of an *ervah*. This is an entirely different *drasha* from the *achos ishah* — *aleha* derivation. The weakness of Rabbi's *drasha* is readily apparent: there is nothing in *velakachah* or *veyibmah* that hints specifically at *ervah* or *tzaras ervah*; one must import those concepts entirely from without.

### Two Drashas

Rava reads Rabbi's *drasha* through his own lens. Since Rava holds that *ervah* needs no verse, he reinterprets the two suffixed forms accordingly. *Velakachah* means: her, and not the *tzarah* — there is no *yibum* for the co-wife of an *ervah*. *Veyibmah* specifies that this exclusion operates *bimkom yibum*, in a *yibum* context. The practical upshot of this second specification is critical: it tells us that the concept of *tzarah* as a problem is limited to the *yibum* context. Outside of *yibum*, the co-wife of a woman forbidden to me as an *ervah* is not forbidden to me by virtue of being her *tzarah*.

Thus, according to Rava, Rabbi's two words together teach: no *yibum* for a *tzarah* in a *yibum* context — and the implication that outside a *yibum* context, the *tzarah* relationship creates no prohibition. The *ervah* herself, meanwhile, we already knew was excluded without any verse.

### Rav Ashi Observes

Rav Ashi adds that Rava's reading is implicit in the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah opens: *chamesh esrei nashim potrot tzaroteihen* — fifteen women exempt their *tzarot*. Crucially, the Mishnah does not say "fifteen women are themselves exempt *and* exempt their *tzarot*." It simply states that they exempt their *tzarot*, taking their own exemption entirely for granted. *Shema mina* — this confirms that the *chiddush* of the Mishnah is the law of *tzarah*, not the law of *ervah*.

## Tzarah Logic Problem

### Challenge to Rava

Having established that the verse is needed for *tzarah* rather than *ervah*, the Gemara poses a symmetrical challenge: *tzarah nami lo tiba'i kra* — perhaps *tzarah* likewise needs no verse. Just as *ervah* needs no verse because *asei* cannot override *lo ta'asei she-yesh bo karet*, perhaps the same is true of *tzarah*. If *tzarah* is itself an *issur* carrying *karet*, one should have been able to derive the same conclusion without a special derivation.

### Rashi Explains

Rashi clarifies the Gemara's reasoning. Without linking the verse to a *yibum* context, one would have read the verse straightforwardly: the prohibition of *achos ishah* and her *tzarah* apply as a general *issur ervah* in all cases. And since *karet* is stated at the end of the *arayot* section — *ve-nichresu* — one would have concluded that *tzaras ervah* is independently forbidden with *karet* in all circumstances, not only in a *yibum* context. Therefore, just as *ervah* itself needs no verse since *asei* cannot override a *karet*-bearing prohibition, so too *tzarah* should need no verse on the same grounds.

The Gemara's question, then, is: what work does the *drasha* actually do?

### Tzarah Contexts

To appreciate the answer, it is essential to distinguish two scenarios. In one scenario — *bimkom mitzvah* — the *tzarah* arises within a *yibum* context. For example: Reuven is married to Rachel and Dvorah, and Shimon is married to Leah. Reuven and Shimon are brothers, and Rachel and Leah are sisters. When Shimon dies childless, Reuven cannot perform *yibum* with Leah, his wife's sister. Since there is no *mitzvat yibum* with Leah, Dvorah — who is Reuven's co-wife to Leah, his brother's wife — also has no *mitzvat yibum*. Once the *mitzvah* falls away, Dvorah reverts to the default status of *eshet ach* with no operative *mitzvah*, and she becomes forbidden. This is the law of *tzaras ervah bimkom mitzvah*.

In the second scenario — *shelo bimkom mitzvah* — a different man, Gideon, is married to Rachel and Dvorah, with no brother-sister relationship to Reuven's wife. When Gideon dies, Reuven cannot marry Rachel because she is his wife's sister. But Dvorah? She is permitted. She was a co-wife to someone who was an *ervah* to Reuven, but the context of *yibum* is entirely absent. The concept of *tzaras ervah* as an exclusion from *yibum* simply does not apply here.

### Rami bar Chama Objects

Rami bar Chama now poses a sharp question to Rava: *eima ha-ervah gufah shelo bimkom mitzvah tishteri* — if *tzarah* is permitted outside the *yibum* context, why not say the same about *ervah* itself? If the verse's role is to limit the *tzarah* prohibition to a *yibum* context, perhaps all *arayot* prohibitions are similarly limited to *yibum* contexts. Outside of *yibum*, perhaps the wife's sister and all other *arayot* should be permitted.

### Rava's Answer

The Gemara immediately rejects the suggestion regarding *ervah* itself: *lav kal va-chomer hu?* — is this not a *kal va-chomer*? If *ervah* is forbidden even *bimkom mitzvah*, all the more so is she forbidden *shelo bimkom mitzvah*. Rava responds to the parallel question about *tzarah*: *tatim alecha kra* — the verse bears you down. The verse says *be-chayeha* — during her lifetime. This phrase establishes a sweeping, time-bound prohibition of *achos ishah* in all contexts: as long as one's wife is alive, the sister is categorically forbidden regardless of circumstance. The word *aleha* in the verse then specifically links this prohibition to *yibum*, limiting the *tzarah* exclusion to that context. *Bimkom aleha hu de-asira* — it is in the *yibum* context that the *tzarah* is also forbidden; *shelo bimkom aleha sharya* — outside that context, the *tzarah* is permitted.

## Verse Contradiction

### Rav Huna: Trei Krai

At this point the Gemara identifies a deeper textual problem. Rav Huna bar Tachlifa, citing Rava, observes: *trei krai ktivi* — the verse of *achos ishah* contains, in effect, two distinct textual sections that appear to contradict one another.

Before examining the contradiction, the Gemara notes that *be-chayeha* is needed for a more fundamental *halacha*: to tell us that the prohibition of *achos ishah* applies even after the first wife has been divorced, not only during marriage. One might have inferred from the phrase *ishah el achosah* — a woman alongside her sister — that the prohibition depends on the co-wife relationship being active, and that upon divorce the sister becomes permissible. Therefore, *be-chayeha* teaches *kol she-be-chayeha*: as long as the first wife is alive, the prohibition stands, even after divorce.

### Reconciling Verse Halves

The first half of the verse reads: *ve-ishah el achotah lo tikach litzror* — and a woman alongside her sister you shall not take as a rival. The word *litzror* implies *both* the sister and her *tzarah* are forbidden, since it references the co-wife relationship. The second half reads: *legalot ervatah aleha be-chayeha* — to uncover her nakedness; *ervatah* is singular, implying only *she* is forbidden, with no mention of a *tzarah*.

These two halves appear in tension. *Trei krai ktivi* — there are two messages embedded in one verse.

### Two Cases Defined

The resolution is: *bimkom mitzvah shteihem asurot* — in the *yibum* context, both she and the *tzarah* are forbidden. *Shelo bimkom mitzvah, hi asurah ve-tzaratah muteret* — outside the *yibum* context, she alone is forbidden and the *tzarah* is permitted. The first half of the verse — she and her *tzarah* are forbidden — speaks to the *yibum* context; the second half — singular *ervatah*, only she — speaks to the non-*yibum* context.

### Reversing the Rule

The Gemara challenges: *ipcha ana* — why not reverse the assignment? Why not say that *bimkom mitzvah* only she is forbidden while the *tzarah* is permitted, and *shelo bimkom mitzvah* both are forbidden? This alternative is not entirely unreasonable: one could argue that *tzaras ervah* is a general *ervah* status applying broadly, but within a *yibum* context the *tzarah* prohibition is overridden to enable *yibum* — just as the *eshet ach* prohibition is overridden.

### Reversal Fails

The Gemara rejects the reversal: *im kein lo yomar aleha* — if that were correct, the verse would not need the word *aleha* at all. Without *aleha*, one would naturally read the two halves of the verse as follows: the first prohibits both her and the *tzarah*, the second prohibits only her. To reconcile them, the logical inference would be to assign the more permissive reading to the *yibum* context — that is, only she is forbidden in *yibum* and the *tzarah* is not — in order to enable *yibum*. Without *aleha*, one would have arrived at the reversed rule as the most natural reading. The Torah therefore writes *aleha*, explicitly linking the verse to *yibum*, to signal that the assignment is the *opposite* of what we would have inferred: in the *yibum* context, both are forbidden. The *legalot ervatah* — singular — refers to the non-*yibum* case, where only she is forbidden.

### Rav Ashi: Permit?

Rav Ashi poses a final alternative reading to Rav Kahana: *mi-mai de-hai aleha le-issura, dilma le-hetera* — how do we know that *aleha* comes to restrict, i.e., to forbid the *tzarah* in the *yibum* context? Perhaps *aleha* comes to permit. On this reading, *ve-ishah el achotah lo tikach litzror* establishes the general prohibition against both the sister and her *tzarah*, while *legalot ervatah aleha be-chayeha* comes to say: but *bimkom aleha* — in the context of *yibum* — perhaps both are permitted, with *yibum* overriding both prohibitions. *Aval bimkom aleha shtehen mutarot*.

### Final Resolution

The Gemara dispatches this reading: *im ken legalot ervah de-chada heichi mashkachat lah?* If *aleha* permits everything in the *yibum* context, there would never be a case where only the singular *ervatah* is at issue. On Rav Ashi's proposed reading, the possibilities are: outside *yibum*, both are forbidden; inside *yibum*, both are permitted. But *legalot ervatah* — the singular — requires a case where only she, and not the *tzarah*, is forbidden. That case is the non-*yibum* scenario, and it must remain: *hi asurah ve-tzaratah muteret*. The singular *ervatah* cannot find its referent if both cases are symmetric. The reversal therefore fails, and the original resolution stands.

To summarize Rava's ultimate position: *ervah* is excluded from *yibum* without requiring any verse, since *asei* cannot override *lo ta'asei she-yesh bo karet*, and no serious *hava amina* ever existed. The real *chiddush* introduced by the verse — through *aleha* linking to *yibum*, and through *litzror* — is the law of *tzarah*. And the verse must do double duty: it must both establish that *tzaras ervah* is excluded from *yibum bimkom mitzvah*, and clarify that *tzaras ervah* is *not* a general *issur ervah* extending to non-*yibum* contexts. That limiting function — *shelo bimkom mitzvah sharya tzarah* — is what the word *aleha* accomplishes. For Rabbi, the same twin conclusions are derived from *velakachah* and *veyibmah*, with Rava reading those suffixed forms as pointing exclusively to *tzarah*, and the *veyibmah* specifying that the exclusion operates within a *yibum* context.
