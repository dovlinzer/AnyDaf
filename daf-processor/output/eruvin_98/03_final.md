# Eruvin 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ogdo BeYado & Porch

### Principle Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיָה קוֹרֵא בְּסֵפֶר עַל הָאִיסְקוּפָּה וְנִתְגַּלְגֵּל הַסֵּפֶר מִיָּדוֹ — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who **was reading** a sacred **book** in scroll form on Shabbat **on** an elevated, wide **threshold, and the book rolled from his hand** into the public domain, **he** may **roll it** back **to himself,** since one of its ends remains in his hand.

As we enter the final week of *Masechet Eruvin*, the last *perek* represents a significant thematic shift — away from the laws of *eruvin* proper and toward the broader laws of carrying in *reshut hayachid*, along with special exceptions for *kitvei kodesh*. This parallels the end of *Masechet Shabbat*, where we encountered special dispensations for one who was caught outside the *techum* with a *lulav*.

The principle that anchors the opening discussion is known as *ogdo beyado* — literally, "the knot is in his hand." The idea is that if one holds one end of a long object while the other end extends into a different domain, one has not performed a complete act of *hotza'ah*, because the entire object did not begin fully within one domain and transfer fully into another. The whole object is physically one unit; the papers or parchment cannot be divided. Accordingly, even if a *sefer* rolls from a *reshut hayachid* into a *reshut harabim*, and the reader is still holding one end, the act of rolling it back would at most constitute a rabbinic violation. The animating question, then, is whether we permit such a rabbinic violation for the sake of protecting *kitvei kodesh*.

### Porch vs Rooftop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה קוֹרֵא בְּרֹאשׁ הַגָּג וְנִתְגַּלְגֵּל הַסֵּפֶר מִיָּדוֹ, עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לַעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. מִשֶּׁהִגִּיעַ לַעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — הוֹפְכוֹ עַל הַכְּתָב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵין מְסוּלָּק מִן הָאָרֶץ אֶלָּא כִּמְלֹא מַחַט — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בָּאָרֶץ עַצְמוֹ — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת עוֹמֵד בִּפְנֵי כִּתְבֵי הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** If **he was reading on top the roof,** which is a full-fledged private domain, **and the book rolled from his hand, as long as** the edge of the book **did not reach** within **ten handbreadths** above the public domain, the book is still in its own domain, and he may **roll it** back **to himself.** However, **once the book has reached** within **ten handbreadths** above the public domain, it is prohibited to roll the book back to oneself. In that case, **he** may only **turn it** over **onto the** side with **writing,** so that the writing of the book will be facedown and not exposed and degraded. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if the scroll **is removed only a needle breadth from the ground, he rolls it** back **to himself. Rabbi Shimon says: Even if** the scroll is **on the ground itself, he rolls it** back **to himself, as you have nothing** that was instituted **as a rabbinic decree** to enhance the character of Shabbat as a day of **rest** that **stands** as an impediment **before** the rescue of **sacred writings.**

The *Mishnah* addresses two distinct scenarios. In the case of the porch (*iskopa*), the ruling is unqualified: if one is reading on a porch and a *sefer* rolls away, one may roll it back without restriction. In the case of the rooftop, however, the *Mishnah* introduces a three-way debate that turns on how close the fallen end of the scroll is to the ground of the *reshut harabim*.

The first opinion holds that once the scroll enters the airspace of *reshut harabim* — that is, once it descends within ten *tefachim* of the ground — one may not roll it back. At that point, even a rabbinic violation is impermissible, and one must instead turn the scroll so that the writing faces the wall, protecting it from the elements. Rabbi Yehuda is more lenient: even if the scroll is only a hair's breadth (*melo hachut*) off the ground, one may roll it back — the *Gemara*'s version of the measurement is *melo hachut*, a string's breadth, rather than the needle's breadth sometimes cited. As long as the scroll has not actually touched the ground, Rabbi Yehuda permits rolling it back. Rabbi Shimon is the most permissive: even if the scroll has hit the ground, one may roll it back, since the entire prohibition is rabbinic, and rabbinic prohibitions yield before the need to protect *kitvei kodesh*.

This immediately raises a structural question: why is the porch case stated without debate, as a simple ruling, while the rooftop case generates a three-way *machloket*?

### Reconciling Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת אֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד בִּפְנֵי כִּתְבֵי הַקּוֹדֶשׁ. אֵימָא סֵיפָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵין מְסוּלָּק מִן הָאָרֶץ אֶלָּא מְלֹא הַחוּט גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בָּאָרֶץ עַצְמָהּ גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ — רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מְצִיעֲתָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אִין, רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מְצִיעֲתָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **who is** the *tanna* of the mishna? **It is Rabbi Shimon, who said: Anything that** is prohibited on Shabbat and its prohibition is not by Torah law, but rather **is due to** a **rabbinic decree** issued to enhance the character of Shabbat as a day of rest does **not stand** as an impediment **before** the rescue of **sacred writings.** But if it is the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **say the latter clause** of the mishna as follows: **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even if** the scroll **is removed only a needle breadth from the ground, he rolls it** back **to himself;** and **Rabbi Shimon says: Even if** the scroll is **on the ground itself, he rolls it** back **to himself.** Is it possible that the *tanna* cited in **the first clause** of the mishna is Rabbi Shimon, as claimed above, while it is explicitly stated that **the last clause** represents the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** and yet its **middle clause** reflects the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? Rav Yehuda said: Yes,** that is the correct, albeit unconventional, explanation. **The first and last clauses** are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** while **the middle clause** of the mishna reflects the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *Gemara* (top of 98a) opens by analyzing the porch case: *hai iskopa heichi dami* — what exactly are we dealing with? If the porch is a *reshut hayachid* — ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide — and the *reshut harabim* lies before it, and we are told without qualification that one may roll the scroll back, this implies we have no concern even if the scroll has made contact with the *reshut harabim*. As the *Gemara* notes: *velo gazrinan dilma nafal ve'ati le'atuyei* — we are not concerned that once he rolls it back up, the scroll might fall entirely and he will then go out and retrieve it from the domain. Given that conclusion, the unqualified porch ruling corresponds precisely to Rabbi Shimon's position in the rooftop debate: even if the scroll rests in the *reshut harabim*, it may be retrieved because the entire *issur* is rabbinic, and rabbinic prohibitions do not stand in the way of protecting *kitvei kodesh*.

The *Gemara* asks: *mani* — whose opinion is this? *Rabbi Shimon hee* — it is Rabbi Shimon, *d'amar kol davar shehu mishum shvus ein omeid bifnei kisvei hakodesh*, who holds that anything that is merely a *shvus* — a rabbinic prohibition — will not stand in the way of protecting holy scriptures.

This creates a structural puzzle: *reisha v'seifa Rabbi Shimon, metziata Rabbi Yehuda* — the opening case (the porch) follows Rabbi Shimon, the closing position (in the rooftop debate) is Rabbi Shimon, yet the middle of the *mishnah* presents the debate including Rabbi Yehuda's view. How can the *mishnah* proceed in this manner? Rav Yehuda answers: this is not, in fact, a problem. The porch case was stated as an unattributed *stam* position reflecting Rabbi Shimon's view; the rooftop debate then records the range of opinions, the last of which is Rabbi Shimon — the same view as the opening case. The difference between porch and rooftop is not substantive; it is merely a matter of literary presentation.

## Karmelis vs Reshut

### Rabba's Trampled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: הָכָא בְּאִיסְקוּפָּה הַנִּדְרֶסֶת עָסְקִינַן, וּמִשּׁוּם בִּזְיוֹן כִּתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ שְׁרוֹ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that the mishna may be understood differently. **Here, we are dealing with a threshold that is trodden upon** by the public, **and due to** the potential **degradation of the sacred writings the Sages permitted** one to violate the rabbinic decree. It would be disgraceful if people were to trample over sacred writings.

Rabba, however, is dissatisfied with this answer. He prefers to establish a *substantive* distinction between the porch and the rooftop. He says: *hacha b'iskopa hanidreset askinan* — we are dealing with a porch that has heavy foot traffic. Although such a porch may technically be a *reshut hayachid*, the constant trampling creates a situation where the scroll, once fallen, is in genuine danger of being trodden upon. In that case, *umishum bizayon kisvei hakodesh sharu rabbanan* — because of the concern for the degradation (*bizayon*) of holy scriptures, the *rabanan* permit overriding even a rabbinic prohibition. According to Rabba, this leniency would be agreed upon by all opinions, since the concern is not merely an abstract rabbinic violation but an imminent *bizayon* to *kitvei kodesh*. The porch case is therefore not specifically a ruling of Rabbi Shimon; it is a consensus position grounded in the special urgency of preventing physical disrespect to sacred texts.

### Abaye's Four Amot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: תּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע — הוֹפְכוֹ עַל הַכְּתָב. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּאִיסְקוּפָּה נִדְרֶסֶת עָסְקִינַן, מָה לִי תּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת מָה לִי חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to his** explanation: It was taught that if the scroll rolled **within four cubits, he rolls it** back **to himself;** if it rolled **beyond four cubits, he turns it over onto** its **writing. And if you say we are dealing with a threshold that is trodden on** by the public, **what** difference is there **to me** whether it remained **within four cubits** and **what** difference is there **to me** if it rolled **beyond four cubits?** Since the prohibition is a rabbinic decree, not a Torah prohibition, why isn't one permitted to move the scroll in both cases to prevent the degradation of the sacred writings?

Abaye challenges Rabba's explanation. There is a *braisa* which refines the porch case: if the scroll rolled within four *amot*, one may roll it back (*golelo etzlo*); if it rolled further than four *amot*, one turns it face-down (*hofcho al hakasv*). Abaye's challenge is as follows: *v'ee amarta b'iskopa hanidreset askinan* — if we are dealing with a trampled porch, and the entire leniency is grounded in the urgency of preventing *bizayon* to *kitvei kodesh*, then *ma li toch arba amos ma li chutz l'arba amos* — what difference does it make whether it is within or beyond four *amot*? If a rabbinic prohibition is overridden because of the danger of degradation, it should be overridden regardless of distance. The four-*amot* distinction is therefore inexplicable on Rabba's terms.

### No Torah Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּאִיסְקוּפָּה כַּרְמְלִית עָסְקִינַן, וּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים עוֹבֶרֶת לְפָנֶיהָ. תּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דְּאִי נָפֵיל וּמַיְיתִי לֵיהּ לָא אָתֵי לִידֵי חִיּוּב חַטָּאת — שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן. חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דְּאִי מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ אָתֵי לִידֵי חִיּוּב חַטָּאת — לָא שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: Here, we are dealing with a threshold** that is **a *karmelit*,** as the threshold is four handbreadths wide but is less than ten handbreadths high. Furthermore, on one side of the *karmelit* there is a private domain, **and a public domain passes before it.** The reason for the different rulings is as follows: If the scroll rolled **within four cubits,** even **if** the entire scroll **falls** out of the one's hand **and he brings it** back, **he cannot incur liability** to bring **a sin-offering,** as the prohibition against carrying from a public domain to a *karmelit* is a rabbinic decree. Consequently, **the Sages permitted him** to roll it back to himself, as there is no danger of transgressing a Torah prohibition. However, if it rolled **beyond four cubits, so that if he brings it** back **he incurs liability** to bring **a sin-offering,** as carrying an object four cubits in the public domain is prohibited by Torah law, **the Sages did not permit him** to roll it back. In this case, if he forgot and carried the scroll instead of rolling it, he would be violating a severe prohibition.

Abaye therefore offers his own explanation: *hacha b'iskopa karmelis askinan v'reshut harabim overet l'faneha* — we are dealing not with a *reshut hayachid* but with a porch that is less than ten *tefachim* high, making it a *karmelit*, and a *reshut harabim* runs before it. This is the key distinction. Because the porch is a *karmelit*, when the scroll rolls from the porch into the *reshut harabim*, the violation involved in rolling it back is doubly attenuated. First, it is only rabbinic because of *ogdo beyado* — one is holding one end of the scroll. Second, it is only rabbinic because one is moving an object from a *karmelit* to a *reshut harabim*, which is itself only a *derabbanan*. The *issur* is thus a *derabbanan* layered upon a *derabbanan* — what one might call a *d'rabbanan* of a *d'rabbanan* — and it is this compounded attenuation that explains the porch ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת נָמֵי נִגְזַר דִּילְמָא מְעַיֵּיל מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד. וְכִי תֵּימָא כֵּיוָן דְּמַפְסֶקֶת כַּרְמְלִית לֵית לַן בַּהּ, וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: הַמַּעֲבִיר חֵפֶץ מִתְּחִלַּת אַרְבַּע לְסוֹף אַרְבַּע, וְהֶעֱבִירוֹ דֶּרֶךְ עָלָיו — חַיָּיב. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בְּאִיסְקוּפָּה אֲרוּכָּה, אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי מִידְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, let us likewise issue a decree** in the case where the scroll remained **within four cubits, lest he bring** the scroll **in from the public domain to the private domain,** i.e., to his house. **And lest you say: Since a *karmelit* separates** the public and private domains **we have no** problem **with it,** as nothing is directly carried from one domain to the other, **didn't Rava say: One who carries an object from the beginning of four** cubits **to the end of four** cubits in the public domain, **and he carried it by way of** the airspace **above his head,** he **is liable, even though** the object remained more than ten handbreadths off the ground and passed from the beginning to the end of four cubits by way of an exempt zone? Here, too, one should be liable for carrying the scroll from the public domain to a private domain by way of a *karmelit*. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with an extended threshold.** Consequently, **in the meantime,** while he is carrying the scroll along the length of the threshold, **he will remember** not to bring it into the private domain.

### Carrying Concern

The *Gemara* raises a further objection: *toch arba amos nami nigzar dilma maisi me'reshut harabim l'reshut hayachid* — even within four *amot*, should we not be concerned that one will pick up the scroll from the *reshut harabim* and carry it all the way into the house, which is a *reshut hayachid*? After all, most porches stand in front of a house, and one would naturally bring a fallen book inside. If that is a real concern, then even within four *amot* there is a risk of a Torah violation — not by carrying in the *reshut harabim*, but by transitioning from *reshut harabim* through the *karmelit* and into the house.

The *Gemara* first suggests that perhaps the *karmelit* interposes as a break: *keivan d'mafseka karmelit leit lan bah* — since the *karmelit* separates the *reshut harabim* from the house, we are not concerned, because one would have to consciously execute two separate transitions rather than one fluid act of carrying. The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from a ruling of Rava: *hamavir chaifetz mitchillat arba l'sof arba v'he'eviro derech alav chayav* — if one takes an object and carries it from the beginning of four *amot* to the end of four *amot*, passing it over a neutral zone along the way, one is still liable. Rashi explains this ruling as addressing a case where one carried the object above ten *tefachim* — through airspace that is technically not *reshut harabim* — and yet remained liable. Thus, passing through a neutral zone such as a *karmelit* does not break the act of *hotza'ah*. If one picked up the scroll from the *reshut harabim* and deposited it in the house, one would be liable, regardless of having passed through the *karmelit*.

### Long Porch Memory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם בְּאִסְקוּפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֲרוּכָּה, וּסְתָם כִּתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ עַיּוֹנֵי מְעַיֵּין בְּהוּ וּמַנַּח לְהוּ. וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא מְעַיֵּין בְּהוּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְעָיֵיל לְהוּ בְּהֶדְיָא לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד. הָא מַנִּי בֶּן עַזַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מְהַלֵּךְ כְּעוֹמֵד דָּמֵי. וְדִילְמָא זָרֵיק לְהוּ מִזְרָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֶה בֶּן עַזַּאי בְּזוֹרֵק. אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר אַהֲבָה: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת אֵין מְזָרְקִין כִּתְבֵי הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually,** it is referring **to a threshold that is not extended;** however, **ordinarily one peruses sacred writings and** then **puts them** in their place. Consequently, there is no concern that he might pass directly from the public to the private domain, as he will pause on the threshold to read the scroll. The Gemara asks: According to this explanation too, **let us be concerned lest he** pause to **peruse** the scroll **in the public domain, and** subsequently **carry it directly into the private domain** without pausing in the *karmelit*. The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** mishna? **It is** the opinion of **Ben Azzai, who said** that **walking is considered like standing.** Consequently, one who passes through the *karmelit* is considered to have paused and stood there. Therefore, the object was not transferred directly from the public domain to the private domain, as he paused in the *karmelit*. The Gemara asks: **But** what of the concern **lest he throw** the scrolls inside, rather than carry them by hand, **as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Ben Azzai concedes** that **one who throws** an object from the public domain to a private domain by way of an exempt domain is liable? The Gemara answers that **Rav Aḥa bar Ahava said: That is to say** that **one does not throw sacred writings,** as this is demeaning to them. Consequently, there is no concern that one might throw the scrolls rather than carry them by hand.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty by offering two alternatives. The first: *hacha b'askupa arucha* — we are dealing with a very long porch, oriented not along the *reshut harabim* but extending deeply away from it, like a pier. If the scroll fell near the edge of such a long porch, and one went down to retrieve it, by the time one had walked all the way back through the length of the porch to reach the house, *adeihachi v'hachi midkar* — in the meantime, one would remember that one is carrying on *Shabbat* and would set the scroll down on the porch rather than continuing into the house. The length of the porch itself serves as a cognitive buffer that makes the concern of inadvertently carrying into the house sufficiently remote.

The second alternative: *ee ba'it eima de-ulam b'shipufa she'eina arucha, v'stam kitvei kodesh iyunei me'aynei behu u'manach lehu* — even if the porch is not particularly long, one must consider what the person was doing on the porch in the first place: reading. The natural thing for someone who retrieves a scroll from the *reshut harabim* and places it back on the porch is to resume reading, not to carry it inside. Once the scroll is back on the porch, the reader's own engagement with the text provides the stop — one sits down and continues reading, rather than walking inside. The concern that one might carry it the entire way into the house is therefore treated as sufficiently attenuated.

The overall resolution, then, is that the porch case operates on a distinct principle: it is a *karmelit*, yielding a doubly attenuated *d'rabbanan*, and within four *amot* there is no realistic concern of reaching a Torah violation. For that reason, everyone — even Rabbi Yehuda, who disputes Rabbi Shimon in the rooftop case — would agree that rolling the scroll back is permitted. Outside of four *amot*, however, the risk of inadvertently incurring a *de'oraita* violation reasserts itself, and the *rabanan* do not allow it.

## Rooftop Proximity

### Suspension Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹפְכוֹ עַל הַכְּתָב? וְהָא לָא נָח! [אָמַר רָבָא:] בְּכוֹתֶל מְשׁוּפָּע.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: Once the scroll has reached within ten handbreadths above the public domain, it is prohibited to roll it back to oneself, and **one turns it over onto the writing.** The Gemara asks: **But** why is this prohibited? Since the scroll **did not** come to **rest** in the public domain, rolling it back to oneself should not be prohibited. **Rava said:** This teaching is referring **to an inclined wall.** Although the scroll did not reach the ground, it came to rest within the confines of the public domain.

Returning now to the rooftop case — which is a *reshut hayachid* — the *Gemara* asks: *v'ha lo nach* — but the scroll hasn't come to rest! When the scroll tumbles from a rooftop and hangs suspended in midair, with the reader holding one end at the top and the other end dangling, there is no *hanacha* — no resting — in the *reshut harabim*. Without *hanacha*, there is no act of carrying at all, not even a rabbinic one. If so, there should be no issue with rolling it back regardless of whether it is within or beyond ten *tefachim*. Why does the *mishnah* establish distinctions based on proximity to the ground?

This is the *Gemara*'s opening challenge on the rooftop case: if the scroll is merely suspended — not resting on anything — then the debate about ten *tefachim*, a hair's breadth, or touching the ground seems to address a non-problem, since a suspended object has neither *akirah* nor *hanacha* in any meaningful sense.

### Slanted Wall

Rava answers: *amar Rava b'kosel meshupa* — the *mishnah* is dealing with a slanted or angled wall, meaning a wall with a protrusion or ledge jutting out at some point along its face. When the scroll falls from the roof, it does not hang freely; it lands on this ledge and comes to rest. The ten *tefachim* question then becomes live: if the ledge on which it rests is within the ten-*tefachim* airspace of *reshut harabim*, the scroll is considered to have come to rest within *reshut harabim*, making rolling it back a genuine rabbinic act — which is precisely the question the *mishnah* debates. If the scroll were simply suspended with no resting point, there would be no prohibition to discuss.

### Two Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי:] בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לְמַתְנִיתִין? בְּכוֹתֶל מְשׁוּפָּע, אֵימָא סֵיפָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵינוֹ מְסוּלָּק מִן הָאָרֶץ אֶלָּא מְלֹא הַחוּט — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, וְהָא נָח לֵיהּ! חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּכוֹתֶל מְשׁוּפָּע, אֲבָל בְּכוֹתֶל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשׁוּפָּע, לְמַעְלָה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, לְמַטָּה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה — הוֹפְכוֹ עַל הַכְּתָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: In what** manner **did you establish** that **the mishna** is referring **to** the case of **an inclined wall? Say the latter clause** of the mishna as follows: **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if the scroll **is removed only a needle breadth from the ground, he rolls it** back **to himself. But didn't** the scroll come to **rest** in the public domain? It shouldn't matter whether or not the scroll is in contact with the ground. The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and is teaching the following: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **in** the case of **an inclined wall. However,** **with regard to a wall that is not inclined,** the following distinction applies: If the end of the scroll is **three** handbreadths **above** the ground, **he** may **roll it** back **to himself;** but if it is **below three** handbreadths from the ground, it is considered as though the scroll is on the ground, and **he must** therefore **turn it** facedown **onto the writing.**

Abaye challenges Rava's interpretation: *bemai okimta l'masechtein b'kotel meshupa* — if the entire *mishnah* operates on the assumption of a slanted wall where the scroll actually rests, then look at Rabbi Yehuda's position: *afilu eino mesulak min ha'aretz ela melo chut golelo etzlo* — even if it is only a hair's breadth off the ground, one may roll it back. Now, how can there be a platform or ledge a hair's breadth off the ground? The *Gemara* grants that such a scenario might exist in principle, but then pushes further: if the scroll is resting on a platform that is a hair's breadth above the ground of *reshut harabim*, it is effectively *within* three *tefachim* of the ground and governed by the principle of *lavud* — it is halachically as though it is resting on the ground itself. Given that, how does Rabbi Yehuda permit rolling it back?

The *Gemara* responds: *chasorei mechasra v'hachi katani* — the *mishnah* is actually incomplete as stated, and must be read as containing two sub-cases rather than three opinions about a single scenario. *Bameh devarim amurim* — in what circumstances do we apply the rule of ten *tefachim*? When the wall is slanted (*kotel meshupa*) and the scroll actually landed on a protrusion — in that case, if the protrusion is within ten *tefachim*, the scroll has genuinely rested in the airspace of *reshut harabim*, and whether one may roll it back is the subject of debate. But *aval b'kotel she'eino meshupa* — if the wall is straight and the scroll is simply suspended in midair, then the calculation shifts entirely: the relevant threshold is three *tefachim*, not ten.

### R. Yehuda Rest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר אֲפִילּוּ אֵינוֹ מְסוּלָּק מִן הָאָרֶץ וְכוּ׳. דְּבָעֵינַן הַנָּחָה עַל גַּבֵּי מַשֶּׁהוּ. וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה לְרַבָּנַן צָרִיךְ הַנָּחָה, לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ?! אֶלָּא: כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּכוֹתֶל מְשׁוּפָּע, אֲבָל בְּכוֹתֶל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשׁוּפָּע — אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. שֶׁרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵינוֹ מְסוּלָּק מִן הָאָרֶץ אֶלָּא מְלֹא הַחוּט — גּוֹלְלוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּבָעֵינַן הַנָּחָה עַל גַּבֵּי מַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if the scroll **is removed only** a needle breadth **from the ground,** one rolls it back to himself. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yehuda maintains **that** in order for an object to be deemed at rest, **we require** that it **rest atop something.** Consequently, a scroll that is not actually touching the ground is not considered resting and may be rolled back. The Gemara asks: **But** consider **that** which **Rava said** with regard to an object located **within three** handbreadths of the ground. Rava said that according **to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Akiva and claim that something in the air is not considered to be at rest, nevertheless, for one to incur liability **it is necessary** for the object to **rest** on a surface. **Let us say** that **he stated his *halakha* in accordance with** only one of **the *tanna'im*,** but not in accordance with all of them. This is an unacceptable conclusion. **Rather,** the Gemara rejects the previous explanation in favor of the following one: The mishna **is all** in accordance with **Rabbi Yehuda, and it is incomplete and is teaching** the following: **In what** case **is this statement** that once the end of the scroll is within ten handbreadths of the ground it may not be rolled back **said?** It was said in the case of **an inclined wall. But with regard to a wall that is not inclined, even** if the end of the scroll is **less than three handbreadths** from the ground, **one** may **roll it** back **to himself, as Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if the scroll **is removed only a needle breadth from the ground, he rolls it** back **to himself.** **What is the reason** for this ruling? The reasoning is that for an object to be considered at rest, **we require** that it **rest atop some** surface. Consequently, if the scroll is not actually touching the ground, it is not considered at rest, even if it is less than three handbreadths from the ground.

With this reconstruction in place, what is Rabbi Yehuda's position and what is its rationale? The *Gemara* explains: *de'ba'inan hanacha al gabei mashehu* — Rabbi Yehuda requires an actual physical resting on something, however minimal. Even if the scroll is within three *tefachim* of the ground — which would normally be treated as *lavud* and halachically equivalent to resting on the ground — that is not sufficient for Rabbi Yehuda. He holds that unless the scroll is actually in physical contact with a surface, there is no *hanacha* and therefore no *issur* even in the rabbinic sense. Accordingly, as long as the scroll remains a hair's breadth above the ground, it has not physically rested, and one may roll it back. Only once it touches the ground and achieves actual *hanacha* does the restriction engage — and even then, Rabbi Shimon disagrees, as will be seen.

The *Gemara* then notes that Rava himself had articulated a similar principle in a different context: *keluta betoch shelosha l'rabbanan tzarich hanacha* — even according to the *rabbanan* in the debate surrounding *keluta k'mi she'huncha* (a concept going back to the beginning of *Masechet Shabbat*), an object within three *tefachim* of a surface still requires actual physical placement to constitute a *hanacha*. The *Gemara* asks whether Rabbi Yehuda's position in the *mishnah* is essentially a *tannaitic* parallel to Rava's *amoraic* ruling — *leima k'tannai amrah l'shma'atetah* — and whether Rava and the tannaim are debating the same underlying question.

### Final Positions

The *Gemara* ultimately consolidates the positions as follows. Remove the intermediate "within three *tefachim*" scenario from the debate of the suspended scroll — *ella kulei Rabbi Yehuda hi*, it is all Rabbi Yehuda's framework. The *mishnah* reconstructed contains two cases:

First, when the wall is slanted (*kotel meshupa*) and the scroll rests on a ledge: the relevant threshold is ten *tefachim* — that is the airspace of *reshut harabim* — and the first opinion holds that once the scroll is within that airspace and at rest, one may not roll it back.

Second, when the wall is straight and the scroll is suspended in midair: *afilu pacho mishlosha tefachim golelo etzlo* — even within three *tefachim*, Rabbi Yehuda says one may roll it back, because *be'inan hanacha al gabei mashehu* — physical resting on something is required, and mere suspension, even within three *tefachim*, does not constitute *hanacha*. The hair's-breadth formulation expresses this principle: as long as the scroll has not actually touched the ground, it has not rested, and rolling it back involves no *issur*.

Rabbi Shimon then stands as the final opinion: even if the scroll has physically touched the ground and achieved actual *hanacha* in *reshut harabim*, the act of rolling it back is only rabbinic — because *ogdo beyado* — and rabbinic prohibitions yield to protect *kitvei kodesh*. The two primary opinions in the suspended scenario thus debate the threshold of *hanacha*: does mere proximity within three *tefachim* constitute *hanacha* sufficient to prohibit rolling back (the first position), or is actual physical contact with a surface required (Rabbi Yehuda)? Rabbi Shimon does not enter this debate about *hanacha* thresholds — he simply holds that even actual *hanacha* does not prohibit, given the rabbinic nature of the *issur* and the imperative to honor *kitvei kodesh*.

## Window Ledge

### Fragile Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זִיז שֶׁלִּפְנֵי חַלּוֹן — נוֹתְנִין עָלָיו וְנוֹטְלִין מִמֶּנּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. גְּמָ׳ הַאי זִיז דְּמַפֵּיק לְהֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּמַפֵּיק לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — לֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא נָפֵיל, וְאָתֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי. אֶלָּא דְּמַפֵּיק לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם דְּמַפֵּיק לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּמַאי ״נוֹתְנִין עָלָיו״ דְּקָתָנֵי, כֵּלִים הַנִּשְׁבָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a ledge in front of a window,** that is ten handbreadths high and four handbreadths wide, **one** may **place** objects **upon it or remove** them **from it on Shabbat** via the window. GEMARA: The Gemara clarifies: **This ledge, to where does it protrude? If you say** that the ledge **protrudes into a public domain,** one should be prohibited to place an object on it, as **we should be concerned lest** the object **fall and he** will forget and **come to bring it in** from the public domain to a private domain. **Rather,** it must be **that** the ledge **protrudes into a private domain;** but if so, it is **obvious** that it is permitted to place objects on it and to remove them. **Abaye said: Actually,** the mishna is dealing with a case **where it protrudes into a public domain, and what** is the meaning of **that** which **it teaches: One** may **place** objects **upon it?** This refers to **fragile utensils,** which would break instantly if they fell. Consequently, there is no concern that one might then bring them in from the public domain to the private domain.

A new *mishnah* introduces the case of *ziz she'lifnei chalon* — a ledge or protrusion extending outward from a window. The *mishnah* states that one may place objects on it and remove objects from it on *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* immediately asks: *hai ziz demafik leheicha* — into what domain does this ledge extend? If it juts out over *reshut harabim*, we should be concerned — *leichush shema nafal v'ati l'asuyei* — that an object placed on the ledge will fall to the ground below, and the owner will go out to retrieve it, thereby carrying from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid*. On the other hand, if the ledge extends over a *reshut hayachid* such as a private courtyard (*chatzer*), then the entire ruling is trivially obvious — *peshita* — and requires no elaboration.

Abaye resolves the tension: *le'olam demafik l'reshut harabim* — indeed, the ledge extends over *reshut harabim*, but the *mishnah*'s permissive ruling is limited. When it says one may place objects on the ledge, this refers specifically to *keilim hanishbarim* — fragile vessels, things that would break upon falling. If an object would break upon landing in *reshut harabim*, one will not bother going down to pick it up, so the concern of retrieval is eliminated. Sturdy objects, by contrast, may not be placed on the ledge, since if they fell one would likely retrieve them.

### Permitted Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: זִיז שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַחַלּוֹן הַיּוֹצֵא לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — נוֹתְנִין עָלָיו קְעָרוֹת וְכוֹסוֹת, קִיתוֹנִיּוֹת וּצְלוֹחִיּוֹת. וּמִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּכׇל הַכּוֹתֶל עַד עֲשָׂרָה הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים. וְאִם יֵשׁ זִיז אֶחָד לְמַטָּה מִמֶּנּוּ — מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ, וּבָעֶלְיוֹן אֵין מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד חַלּוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a ledge in front of a window that protrudes into a public domain, one** may **place on it bowls, cups, small cups [*kitoniyot*], and saucers.** All of these utensils are made of fragile glass or earthenware, which supports Abaye's opinion. The *baraita* continues: **And one** may **use** this ledge along the **entire** length of **the wall,** if the ledge spans its length, whether in close proximity to the window or removed from it, **until the lower ten** handbreadths of the wall, but not if the ledge is lower than that. **And if there is one** other **ledge below it** but still ten handbreadths above the ground, **one** may **use** the lower ledge along the entire length of the wall; **but with regard to the upper** ledge, **one** may **use it only opposite his window.**

A *braisa* confirms and elaborates this position: *ziz she'lifnei chalon hayotzei l'reshut harabim noten alav katarot v'khossot, kitoniyot v'tzelochiyor* — on a ledge extending over *reshut harabim*, one may place plates, cups, pitchers, and similar vessels. Rashi understands these items as implicitly being breakable — the *braisa* is listing characteristic fragile objects, consistent with Abaye's interpretation. The *braisa* also adds: *u'mishtamesh b'chol hakotel* — one may also make use of the entire wall surface, including holes or recesses within it, for storage or placement purposes. However, this use of the wall is limited to those portions above ten *tefachim*; anything done within the ten *tefachim* airspace of *reshut harabim* constitutes a problematic transfer from *reshut hayachid* into *reshut harabim* airspace.

### Ledge Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי זִיז הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה — מְקוֹם פְּטוּר הוּא, וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּנֶגֶד חַלּוֹנוֹ נָמֵי לָא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ. וְאִי אִית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה — בְּכוּלֵּי הַכּוֹתֶל לִישְׁתַּמֵּשׁ. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּחְתּוֹן דְּאִית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה, וְעֶלְיוֹן לֵית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה וְחַלּוֹן מַשְׁלִימָתוֹ לְאַרְבָּעָה. כְּנֶגֶד חַלּוֹן — מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ, דְּחוֹרֵי חַלּוֹן הוּא. דְּהַאי גִּיסָא וּדְהַאי גִּיסָא — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** upper **ledge, what are its circumstances? If it is not four** handbreadths deep, although **it is an exempt domain** with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, which means it does not pose a problem in itself, **one should** nonetheless **not** be permitted to **use** it **even opposite his window,** as anything placed on this narrow ledge is likely to fall. Consequently, it is as though he has thrown the object directly to the ground. **And if it is four** handbreadths deep, **let him use** the ledge along the **entire** length of the ledge along **the wall.** **Abaye said:** We are dealing with a case where **the lower** ledge **is four** handbreadths deep **and the upper** ledge **is not four** handbreadths deep, but the **windowsill** on the inside **completes it to** form a surface **four** handbreadths deep. Accordingly, **one** may **use** the part of the upper ledge **opposite the window, as it is** considered **an extension of the window,** but the parts **to this side or to that side** of the window are **prohibited,** as they are less than four handbreadths deep.

The *braisa* then introduces an additional distinction that requires explanation: *v'im yesh ziz echad lemata heimenu mishtamesh bo* — if there is a second, lower ledge beneath the first, one may use it. *U've'elyon ein mishtamesh bo ela kineged chalono* — but the upper ledge may only be used in the space directly opposite one's window, not along its entire length. This asymmetry is puzzling. Why should a lower ledge be usable anywhere, while an upper ledge is restricted to the area facing the window?

The *Gemara* presses: *hai ziz heichi dami* — what are the dimensions of this *ziz*? If it does not have four *tefachim* of width, then even directly opposite the window one should not use it, since objects placed on so narrow a ledge will inevitably fall. Conversely, if it has four *tefachim*, then one should be able to use it anywhere along the wall without restriction.

Abaye offers the resolution: *tachton d'it bei arba'ah* — the lower ledge that may be used anywhere is one that has four *tefachim* of width, making it a stable surface. Objects placed there are unlikely to fall, so there is no concern of retrieval from *reshut harabim*. *Elyon leit bei arba'ah v'chalon mashlimo l'asarah* — the upper ledge, however, has less than four *tefachim* of width, but the window itself adjoins it and provides the additional depth needed to create a functional resting surface. *Keneged chalon mishtamesh* — opposite the window, where the ledge is stabilized by the window opening that completes its width, one may use it; *dehai gisa u'dehai gisa asur* — but on either side of the window, where the ledge stands alone without the window's supplementary width, it is too narrow to be relied upon, and objects placed there are liable to fall. The apparent rule about upper versus lower ledges is therefore not really about height at all — it is about width. A narrow ledge is usable only where the window stabilizes it; a wide ledge may be used anywhere along the wall.

## Cross-Domain Motion

### Standing & Moving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עוֹמֵד אָדָם בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּמְטַלְטֵל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּמְטַלְטֵל בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יוֹצִיא חוּץ מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person** may **stand in a private domain and move** objects that are **in a public domain,** as there is no concern that he might mistakenly bring them into the private domain. Similarly, one may stand **in a public domain and move** objects **in a private domain, provided that he does not carry** them **beyond four cubits** in the public domain, which is prohibited on Shabbat.

The next *mishnah* moves from special cases involving *kitvei kodesh* and architectural features to a broader analysis of *hotza'ah* — specifically, what it means to move an object across domains when the person doing so is not fully within either domain. *Omed adam b'reshut hayachid u'metaltel b'reshut harabim* — a person may stand in *reshut hayachid* and move objects within *reshut harabim*. Likewise, one may stand in *reshut harabim* and move objects within *reshut hayachid*. The caveat is *u'bilvad shelo yotzi chutz m'arba amot* — one must not move the object more than four *amot* within the *reshut harabim*, since that would replicate the full prohibited act of carrying, regardless of where one's body is standing.

This is a striking ruling. Throughout *Masechet Eruvin* and *Masechet Shabbat*, the *rabanan* erected numerous rabbinic fences against situations far less likely to lead to a *de'oraita* violation. Yet here, where the person is standing in *reshut hayachid* and actively moving an object in *reshut harabim* — a scenario that, intuitively, seems quite likely to result in the object being brought back into *reshut hayachid* — the *mishnah* permits it without restriction beyond the four-*amot* limit. The ruling is indeed a *chiddush*, and the *Gemara* will take it up further.

### Bodily Waste

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְיַשְׁתִּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְיַשְׁתִּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וְכֵן לֹא יָרוֹק. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף מִשֶּׁנִּתְלַשׁ רוּקּוֹ בְּפִיו, לֹא יְהַלֵּךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַד שֶׁיָּרוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** However, **a person** may **not stand in a private domain and urinate into a public domain,** nor may one stand **in a public domain and urinate into a private domain. And likewise, one** may **not spit** in such a manner that the spittle passes from a private domain to a public domain or vice versa. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even once** a person's **spittle is gathered in his mouth, he** may **not walk four cubits** in the public domain **until he spits** it out, for he would be carrying the accumulated spittle in his mouth, which is akin to carrying any other object.

The *mishnah* then introduces a related prohibition: *lo ya'amod adam b'reshut hayachid v'yashtin b'reshut harabim* — a person may not stand in *reshut hayachid* and urinate into *reshut harabim*, nor stand in *reshut harabim* and urinate into *reshut hayachid*. Similarly, *v'chein lo yarok* — one may not spit across domains. The logic is straightforward: the liquid or spittle originates in one domain and travels to another, making the bodily function itself an act of cross-domain transfer. In an era without indoor plumbing, when urination in the street and spitting were common, these prohibitions had immediate practical application.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a further dimension specifically regarding spitting: *af mishenitlash ruko befiv* — from the moment that the spittle becomes distinctly separated within one's mouth, one is halachically treated as carrying it. The phenomenon is experiential: when one gathers saliva in preparation to spit, the spit feels like a discrete object, no longer simply part of the mouth's moisture. At that point, says Rabbi Yehuda, *lo yehalech arba amot ad sheyirok* — one may not walk four *amot* without spitting, because one would be carrying a liquid for that distance. Moreover, one may not spit into a *reshut hayachid*. The only permissible option is to spit directly in front of oneself, where one stands. This ruling has a parallel on *Yom Kippur*, where the question of whether separated saliva in the mouth may be swallowed raises analogous concerns about what counts as a distinct object within the body.

### Text Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: לֹא יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּמְטַלְטֵל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁבְקַתְּ רַבָּנַן וְעָבְדַתְּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר?! הוּא סָבַר: מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְלָא הִיא: סֵיפָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥinnana bar Shelamiya would teach** this mishna **to Ḥiyya bar Rav before Rav** as follows: **A person** may **not stand in a private domain and move** objects that are **in a public domain.** Rav **said to him:** Have **you abandoned** the majority opinion of **the Rabbis and followed** the solitary dissenting opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who is stringent in this regard? The Gemara explains: Rav Ḥinnana **maintains that from** the fact that **the latter clause** of the mishna, was taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** it can be inferred that **the first clause** was **likewise** taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. But** in fact **that is not so: The latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, while the first clause** is in accordance with that of **the Rabbis.**

The *Gemara* records a textual variant. Rabbi Chinana bar Shelmaya taught this *mishnah* to Chiya bar Rav in the presence of Rav, but his version read *lo ya'amod adam b'reshut hayachid v'yashtin b'reshut harabim* — a prohibition — rather than the *mishnah*'s permissive framing of the opening case (standing in one domain and moving in another). In other words, his version of the earlier *mishnah* about moving objects appeared to prohibit what our *mishnah* permits.

Rav reacted: *shavkta rabbanan v'avdata k'Rabbi Meir* — you have abandoned the view of the *rabanan* and are acting like Rabbi Meir. The implication is that Rabbi Chinana bar Shelmaya's version of the earlier *mishnah* tracked Rabbi Meir's stricter position, which will appear in a subsequent *mishnah* explicitly prohibiting cross-domain movement.

### Rabbanan vs R. Meir

The *Gemara* explains Rabbi Chinana bar Shelmaya's reasoning: *mid'seifa Rabbi Meir, reisha nami Rabbi Meir* — since the end of the *mishnah* presents Rabbi Meir's view prohibiting these acts, he assumed the opening of the *mishnah* was simply the same opinion restated. He therefore read the whole *mishnah* as Rabbi Meir's position.

The *Gemara* corrects this: *v'lo hi* — this is not so. *Seifa Rabbi Meir, reisha rabbanan* — the end of the *mishnah* belongs to Rabbi Meir, but the opening belongs to the *rabanan*. They represent a genuine *machloket*, not a repetition. Our *stam mishnah* — the unattributed ruling permitting a person to stand in one domain and move objects in the other — follows the *rabanan*, while Rabbi Meir's more stringent position appears explicitly as a named opinion in the later *mishnah*. The two are not to be harmonized; they reflect a real dispute between the *rabanan* and Rabbi Meir.
