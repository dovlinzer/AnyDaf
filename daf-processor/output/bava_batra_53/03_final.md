# Bava Batra 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wall & Gate חזקה

### Na'al, Gadar, Paratz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמַחְזִיק, אֲבָל בְּנוֹתֵן מַתָּנָה וְהָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ וְהַמַּחְזִיק בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר – דִּלְמִקְנֵי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא; נָעַל, גָּדַר, פָּרַץ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה. תָּנֵי רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּקִדּוּשִׁין דְּבֵי לֵוִי: נָעַל, גָּדַר, פָּרַץ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא בְּפָנָיו – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **In what** case **is this statement,** that one establishes the presumption of ownership after profiting from the property for a certain duration, **said?** It is said **in** a case of one who **has** mere **possession** of the property, which does in some cases serve as proof of ownership. **But in** a case of one who **gives a gift, or** there are **brothers who divided** their inheritance, **or** there is **one who takes possession of** the **property of a convert** who died without heirs, **where** the function of possessing the item is **only to acquire** it and not to establish the presumption of ownership, if one **locked** the door of the property, or **fenced** it or **breached** its fence even **a bit, this is** considered **taking possession** of the property. **Rav Hoshaya teaches in** the *baraita* of tractate ***Kiddushin*** that was taught in the **school of Levi:** If one **locked** the door of the property, or **fenced** it or **breached** its fence even **a bit,** if this was done **in** the **presence** of the seller, **this is** considered **taking possession** of the property.

Today's daf is 53, and we are in the midst of discussing a type of *chazakah* that is more akin to the *chazakah* discussed in Masechet Kiddushin and Masechet Bava Batra — namely, *chazakah* as a *kinyan*. Yesterday we quoted a *baraita* from the Tosefta of Kiddushin: *tani Rav Oshaya be'Kiddushin d'vei Levi*. This *chazakah* is not the kind used to bring evidence in a contested ownership dispute; it is rather one of the specific, concrete acts by which title to a field is transferred. Land is acquired through *kessef*, *shtar*, or *chazakah*: the buyer pays money, the seller writes and hands over a *shtar* of sale, or the buyer goes onto the land and performs an act that demonstrates ownership. That last mode — *chazakah* — has been defined as *na'al, gadar u'faratz*: locking a gate, building a wall, or making an opening. The key point, as we have been emphasizing, is that this is a single, concrete act — not three years of cultivating the land and eating its *peirot* — and it must be an act that improves the land rather than merely deriving benefit from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכַמָּה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא? כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גָּדַר גָּדֵר וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה, וּפָרַץ פִּרְצָה כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס וְיֵצֵא בָּהּ – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that taking possession can be performed by building a fence or breaching a fence even a bit. The Gemara clarifies: **And how much** is the measure of **a bit?** It is **in accordance with** the statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says:** If one had previously **built a fence, and** now **completed it to** a height of **ten** handbreadths, which is the height of a halakhically significant barrier; **or** similarly, if one had previously **breached a breach,** and now expanded it **in order** that it be large enough **that** a person can **enter and exit through it, this is** considered **taking possession.**

The *gemara* earlier established that these acts need only be performed *kol shehu* — a small amount suffices. To clarify what that means in practice, the *gemara* invokes the formulation of Shmuel: *gader geder vehishlimo l'asarah* — if you completed a wall that was already standing, raising it to ten *tefachim*, or *paratz pirtza kedai sheyikanes ve'yetzei bah* — if you widened an opening so that one can pass through it comfortably — *harei zeh chazakah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי גָּדֵר הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא הֲווֹ סָלְקִי לַהּ, וְהַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי לָא סָלְקִי לַהּ – מַאי עֲבַד? וְאֶלָּא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲווֹ סָלְקִי לַהּ, וְהַשְׁתָּא לָא סָלְקִי לַהּ – טוּבָא עֲבַד! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲווֹ סָלְקִי לַהּ בְּרַוְוחָא, וְהַשְׁתָּא קָא סָלְקִי לַהּ בְּדוּחְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **this fence? If we say that initially** one could **not climb over it** to enter the field, **and now too** one still could **not climb over it, what did he accomplish?** Nothing has changed through his completing the height of the fence. **And alternatively,** if it was such **that initially** one **could climb over it** to enter the field, **and now** one could **not climb over it,** he has **accomplished a great deal,** and the mishna should not have referred to this addition as: A bit. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary to** state this ruling if the height of the fence was such **that initially** one **could climb over it with ease, and now** one **could climb over it** only **with effort.**

But the *gemara* immediately asks: what was the condition of the wall beforehand? If it was already high enough that people could not vault it, and after your addition it remains equally impassable — *mai avad?* You accomplished nothing practical. Conversely, if before your addition people could easily go over it and afterward they cannot — *tuv avad!* — that is a major transformation, not a *kol shehu*. The *gemara* resolves: *lo tzericha d'meikara havi salki lah be'rivcha, ve'hashta ka'salki lah be'duchka* — before your addition people could vault it easily, and afterward only with difficulty. You have made a real improvement without having effected a complete qualitative transformation. The improvement need not be radical — quantitative progress suffices — but it cannot be an act that accomplishes nothing whatsoever.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי פִּרְצָה – הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲווֹ עָיְילִי בַּהּ, וְהַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי עָיְילִי בַּהּ – מַאי עֲבַד? וְאֶלָּא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא הֲווֹ עָיְילִי בַּהּ, וְהַשְׁתָּא קָא עָיְילִי בַּהּ – טוּבָא עֲבַד! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲווֹ עָיְילִי בַּהּ בְּדוּחְקָא, וְהַשְׁתָּא עָיְילִי בַּהּ בְּרַוְוחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara similarly asks: **What are the circumstances** of **this breach? If we say that initially,** one **could enter** the field **through it, and now too** one **could enter** the field **through it, what did** he **accomplish?** Nothing has changed through his expanding the breach? **And alternatively,** if it was such **that initially** one **could not enter** the field **through it, and now** one **could enter** the field **through it,** he has **accomplished a great deal,** and the mishna should not have referred to this as: A bit. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this ruling if the size of the breach was such **that initially** one **could enter** the field **through it with effort, and now** one **could enter** the field **through it with ease.**

The *gemara* then applies the same analysis symmetrically to the opening. If it was already wide enough for people to pass through and you merely widened it further without adding functionality — *mai avad?* If it was too narrow to be usable at all and you made it functional — *tuv avad*, not a *kol shehu*. The resolution is again the middle case: *d'meikara havi ayeili bah be'duchka, ve'hashta ayeili bah be'rivcha* — before it was passable only with difficulty, and now it is comfortable. The symmetry between the two cases is elegant and deliberate: both *gadar* and *paratz* require a real but not necessarily dramatic improvement.

### Lock Gate Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נָתָן צְרוֹר וְהוֹעִיל, נָטַל צְרוֹר וְהוֹעִיל – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה. מַאי ״נָתַן״ וּמַאי ״נָטַל״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **placed a stone and** it **helps** to serve some objective, or if one **removed a stone and** it **helps** to serve some objective, **this** act **is** considered **taking possession.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **placed, and what** is the meaning of **removed?**

The *gemara* now records a statement of Rav Asi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *natan tzeror veho'il, natal tzeror veho'il* — if you added a stone to a wall and thereby accomplished something beneficial, or removed a stone and thereby accomplished something beneficial — *harei zo chazakah*. The operative word is *ho'il*: it is not sufficient to merely do something; the act must be meaningful.

At this point it is worth returning to a debate that bears directly on what *ho'il* requires — the disagreement between the Rashbam and Tosafot regarding *na'al*, locking a gate. The Rashbam holds that simply locking the gate is insufficient; what is required is the installation of a lock. Merely closing the gate, he argues, is *mavriach ari mi-nichsei chaveiro* — it is like chasing a lion away from your friend's field. The act is interpreted as nothing more than protecting the property from some imminent harm, which is something any passerby might do out of neighborly concern, not an act that expresses ownership.

Tosafot disagree. They argue that locking a gate is not comparable to *mavriach ari*, and their reasoning is instructive. The relevant line in Tosafot reads: *ve'lo dami ne'ilat delet le-mavriach ari, aval hacha she-no'el bifnei kol adam ve'ein mani'ach adam leikanes, muchach milta she-habayit hu shelo ve'kani be-ne'ila le-chud* — locking a door in front of all people and admitting no one demonstrates that the house belongs to him, and therefore locking alone acquires. Tosafot's point is less about whether the harm is imminent or remote, and more about the positive character of the act. What an owner does when he leaves his property is lock it. Locking the door is the paradigmatic act of responsible ownership; it is not the act of a concerned passerby. As long as the act manifests the behavior of an owner, it constitutes *chazakah*.

This suggests a broader framework with two interrelated components. One is the question of imminence: when damage is imminent and one rushes to prevent it, the act is naturally read as emergency intervention, not as ownership behavior. The other is the question of improvement: *chazakah* may require not merely preventing damage but actually improving the property. Preventing a scratch is not the same as giving a wax job. On this reading, *mavriach ari* is a problem not only because any bypasser might do it, but because preventing damage does not constitute *shivcha* — an improvement of the land. Whether the concern is about the character of the act or the requirement for actual improvement, both lines of reasoning point toward the same conclusion: the act must be one that expresses genuine, ongoing ownership behavior directed toward the property's betterment.

### Natan/Natal Tzror

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא נָתַן צְרוֹר – וּסְכַר מַיָּא מִינַּהּ, נָטַל צְרוֹר – וְאַפֵּיק מַיָּא מִינַּהּ; הַאי מַבְרִיחַ אֲרִי מִנִּכְסֵי חֲבֵרוֹ הוּא! אֶלָּא נָתַן צְרוֹר – דְּצַמֵּד לַהּ מַיָּא, נָטַל צְרוֹר – וְאַרְוַח לַהּ מַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that he **placed a stone** in the fence **and stopped the water from** flooding the field, or he **removed a stone** from the fence **and** thereby fashioned an opening that **released water** that had been flooding the field, **this** is analogous to one who **chases away a lion from another's property.** In other words, these acts prevent damage to the field, which one is obligated to prevent even in the case of the property of another, and accordingly, they do not constitute a demonstration of ownership. **Rather,** it means that he **placed a stone that connected water to** the field and irrigated it, or he **removed a stone and enhanced** the flow of **water to it.**

Returning to the case of adding or removing a stone, the *gemara* asks: *mai natan u'mai natal?* What exactly are the scenarios? The *gemara* first considers: *natan tzeror ve-sachar maya mineh* — you placed a stone in a gap in the wall and thereby prevented floodwater from entering the field. That would seem to be a significant benefit. Or conversely, *natal tzeror ve-afik maya mineh* — you removed a stone and allowed floodwater that was pooling in the field to escape, preventing the ground from becoming submerged.

The *gemara* rejects both: *hai mavriach ari mi-nichsei chaveiro hu*. These are cases of preventing damage — chasing the lion away. One way to understand this is that the act, given its context of imminent flooding, will be interpreted as emergency intervention that any decent person would perform for a neighbor's property, not as an act expressing ownership. Another way to understand it is that preventing damage is simply not the same as improving the land — and *chazakah* requires genuine improvement.

### Mavriach Ari

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁתֵּי שָׂדוֹת וּמֶצֶר אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶן, הֶחְזִיק בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן לִקְנוֹתָהּ – קְנָאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another statement of the same *amora* with regard to taking possession. **And Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If there were **two fields with one boundary between them,** and one **took possession of one of** the fields in order **to acquire it,** he has **acquired it.**

The *gemara* then provides the cases where adding or removing a stone does constitute *chazakah*: *natan tzeror vetzamid la-maya* — you placed a stone in a gap in the wall to keep rainwater in the field, preventing it from running off so that the field continues to be watered. Or *natal tzeror ve'arvach la-maya* — you removed a stone to allow water flowing nearby to enter and irrigate the field. In both cases, the act is directed at improving the field by watering it, not at preventing imminent damage. This is an act of caring for the land, of actively improving it — and therefore it constitutes *chazakah*.

Note the structural elegance: the same physical act — adding or removing a stone — can be either *mavriach ari* or genuine *chazakah* depending entirely on the circumstances and what the actor is trying to accomplish. The external act is identical; what changes is whether the water is flowing in or out, and what the actor's purpose is relative to the land's wellbeing.

## Fields & Boundaries

### Two Fields, Metzar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִקְנוֹת אוֹתָהּ וְאֶת חֲבֶרְתָּהּ – אוֹתָהּ קָנָה, חֲבֶרְתָּהּ לֹא קָנָה. לִקְנוֹת אֶת חֲבֶרְתָּהּ – אַף אוֹתָהּ לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** If his intention was **to acquire it and** also acquire **the other** field, he has **acquired** the first field, but has **not acquired the other** field, since the fields are separated by a boundary. If he took possession of one field in order **to acquire** only **the other** field, **he has not acquired even that** field of which he took possession, since his intention when taking possession was to acquire the other field, and one does not acquire an item without the intention to do so.

Rav Asi further states in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *shtei sadot u'metzar echad beineihem* — two adjacent fields with a single boundary marker between them. This marker might be a slightly raised strip of earth, a line of plaster, or some other demarcation running along the boundary. If one performed a *chazakah* on one of them in order to acquire it — *kanah* — one acquires that field. If one intended the *chazakah* to work for both — *liknot otah v'et chavertah* — one acquires only the field on which the act was performed. And if one performed the *chazakah* on field A while intending it to work only for field B — *af otah lo kanah* — one acquires nothing at all, not even field A.

This last ruling raises an important point about intent in *kinyan*. Even if one performed an objectively valid act of *chazakah* on field A, if one's intention was directed entirely at field B, that act does not function as a *kinyan* on field A. The analogy is to one who unknowingly lifts a *hefker* object — say, someone unknowingly places a *hefker* item in one's bag and one picks up the bag. Despite having performed the physical act of *hagbahah*, the absence of intent means no *kinyan* was effected. Similarly here, performing *chazakah* on field A while directing one's intention toward field B means the act does not serve as a *kinyan* on field A.

This raises an obvious question. The *gemara* in Kiddushin establishes that if a seller conveys two fields to a buyer — one nearby and one in a distant location — and the buyer performs *chazakah* on one, the buyer acquires both. Why should that work, when here, with two fields separated only by a thin boundary marker, a *chazakah* on one cannot work for the other?

There are two principal answers offered by the *Rishonim*, and there is a practical difference between them. The first answer is that in the Kiddushin case the buyer has already paid for both fields. Payment, or at minimum the buyer's obligation to pay, is what links the two fields together in a single transaction, so that a *chazakah* on one acts for both. In our case, by contrast, where the land is *hefker* — say, the estate of a *ger* who died without heirs — there is no monetary transaction to bind the two plots together. The second answer invokes the principle of *da'at acheret maknah oto*: a third party's intention can unite separate items. When a seller intends to convey both fields, that external intention combines them so that a *chazakah* on one reaches both. When land is *hefker*, however, the acquirer acts unilaterally — there is no other party whose *da'at* links the two plots. Without money and without another party's unifying intent, only the objective reality governs, and the boundary marker defines them as two separate units.

The practical *nafka mina* between these two approaches concerns a gift. If one is trying to give another person two fields as a gift, there is *da'at acheret* — the donor's intention to convey both — but no money. Would that suffice to allow a *chazakah* on one to reach the other? This is a genuine dispute among the *Rishonim*. What is clear and uncontested is that in the case of *hefker*, where neither money nor another party's unifying intent is present, the *chazakah* can only work on the field on which it was performed. Even a line of demarcation that is physically minimal — a small raised mound of earth — is sufficient to define two contiguous plots as legally distinct units.

### Chazakah on Metzar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: הֶחְזִיק בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן לִקְנוֹת אוֹתָהּ, וְאֶת הַמֶּצֶר, וְאֶת חֲבֶרְתָּהּ – מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: מֶצֶר דְּאַרְעָא חַד הוּא, וְקָנֵי; אוֹ דִּלְמָא הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if one **took possession of one of** the fields in order **to acquire it, and the boundary, and the other** field, all together? **Do we say** that the **boundary of the land is one,** i.e., these two fields are joined by means of their common boundary, **and** therefore he has **acquired** all of them? **Or perhaps this** field **stands alone and that** field **stands alone.** The Gemara notes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Zeira then poses a further question: *hechzik be'achas mehen liknot otah ve'et hametzar ve'et chavertah mahu?* Suppose one performed a *chazakah* on field A and declared one's intention to acquire not merely A and B, but A, the boundary marker, and B together. The reasoning behind this attempt is that by including the boundary marker itself in one's intended acquisition, one reframes the entire expanse as a single unit — not two fields divided by a line, but one continuous plot with a small mound in the middle. Perhaps the *metzar* with the fields is all one thing: *metzar de'ara chad hu*. An alternative formulation makes the point even more sharply: *hai metzar dehai ara u'dehai ara hu* — this boundary belongs to this field and to that field, so including it in one's intent allows everything to be viewed as unified.

Or perhaps not: *o dilma lo, hai lechud ka'i v'hai lechud ka'i* — each remains a separate unit, the boundary line is itself a separate entity, and no matter how broadly one frames one's intention, the three units — field A, the *metzar*, and field B — remain legally distinct. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הֶחְזִיק בַּמֶּצֶר לִקְנוֹת שְׁתֵּיהֶן, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן הַאי מֶצֶר – אַפְסֵרָא דְאַרְעָא הוּא, וְקָנֵי; אוֹ דִּלְמָא הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if one **took possession of the boundary** between the two fields in order **to acquire both of** the fields? **Do we say** that the legal status of **this boundary** is that of **the halter of the land and he acquires** the fields, just as one acquires an animal through the acquisition of its halter? **Or perhaps** this field **stands alone and that** field **stands alone,** as the boundary is not connected to the field in the same manner that a halter is connected to an animal. The Gemara notes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Elazar then poses a related but distinct question: *hechzik bametzar liknot shteihen mahu?* What if one performed the *chazakah* directly on the boundary marker itself? One might reason that since the boundary marker is connected to both A and B — it serves and divides both — performing *chazakah* on it is like taking hold of both fields simultaneously. The *gemara* uses the image of an *afsara* — a bridle — attached to a horse: grabbing the bridle acquires the horse because the bridle serves, controls, and represents the horse. Similarly, the boundary marker serves both fields and might be thought to represent both.

Or perhaps the opposite logic prevails: the very purpose of the *metzar* is to divide. Its function is separation, not unification. Therefore, performing *chazakah* on it cannot serve to bring the fields together; it only reinforces their distinctness. According to the Rashbam's understanding, the *metzar* is not even part of field A or field B; it is a third independent unit, and field A, the boundary, and field B are each legally distinct from one another. This question, too, is left unresolved.

### Inner/Outer Houses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: שְׁנֵי בָתִּים זֶה לִפְנִים מִזֶּה, הֶחְזִיק בַּחִיצוֹן לִקְנוֹתוֹ – קְנָאוֹ. לִקְנוֹת אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת הַפְּנִימִי – חִיצוֹן קָנָה, פְּנִימִי לֹא קָנָה. לִקְנוֹת אֶת הַפְּנִימִי – אַף חִיצוֹן נָמֵי לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** If there were **two houses** in a courtyard, **this** one situated **within** the courtyard relative **to that** one, and one **took possession of the outer** house in order **to acquire it, he has acquired it.** If his intention was **to acquire it and** also acquire **the inner** house, **he has acquired the outer** house, but has **not acquired the inner** house. If he took possession of the outer house in order to **acquire the inner** house alone, he **has not acquired even the outer** house.

Rav Nachman states in the name of Rabba bar Avuha: *shtei batim zeh lifnim mizeh* — two houses, one behind the other, where the inner house can only be accessed by passing through the outer house; there is no independent entrance to the inner house from the street.

The rulings follow the same structural logic as the fields, with one significant difference. If one performed *chazakah* on the outer house to acquire it — *kanah*. If one performed *chazakah* on the outer house intending to acquire both — *hachitzon kanah, hapnimi lo kanah* — one acquires only the outer. If one performed *chazakah* on the outer house intending to acquire only the inner — *af hachitzon lo kanah* — one acquires nothing. So far, this is identical to the field case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחְזִיק בַּפְּנִימִי לִקְנוֹתוֹ – קְנָאוֹ. לִקְנוֹת אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת הַחִיצוֹן – קָנָה שְׁנֵיהֶן. לִקְנוֹת אֶת הַחִיצוֹן – אַף פְּנִימִי לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** If he **took possession of the inner** house in order **to acquire it,** he **has acquired it.** If his intention was **to acquire it and** also acquire **the outer house,** he **has acquired both of them.** Since the residents of the inner house possess the right to pass through the outer house in order to enter and exit the courtyard, the outer house is viewed as an extension of the inner house. If he took possession of the inner house in order **to acquire** only **the outer** house, he has **not acquired even the inner** house, since he did not take possession of the property that he intended to acquire.

The divergence comes with the inner house. If one performs *chazakah* on the inner house to acquire it — *kanah*. If one performs *chazakah* on the inner house intending to acquire both — *kanah shneihem* — one acquires both. This is the *chiddush*. Why should *chazakah* on the inner house also reach the outer, when *chazakah* on the outer house cannot reach the inner? The reasoning is that access to the inner house necessarily requires passage through the outer. The outer house is, in a functional sense, an extension of the inner — a corridor or antechamber that the inner depends upon. Since the inner cannot exist or be used independently of the outer, the outer is seen as attached to and part of the inner. The outer, by contrast, is fully self-sufficient; it exists and functions entirely independently of the inner house. Therefore, *chazakah* on the outer house can reach only the outer.

It is worth pausing to note the conceptual assumption embedded here. One might have thought that an entire structure — a building with multiple rooms or floors — should be treated as a single unit, such that *chazakah* anywhere within it reaches the whole. What the *gemara* reveals is that a wall and a door, creating distinct interior spaces, are sufficient to define legally separate units. The *chiddush* cuts in both directions: separate rooms within a single structure can be treated as distinct for purposes of *chazakah*, and yet the functional dependency of one room upon another can, in specific configurations, unify them.

The Rif, Rambam, and certain other *Rishonim* have a different *girsa* of this *gemara* in which the inner/outer house case follows the field case exactly — *chazakah* on one cannot work for the other regardless of direction. Our version of the *gemara*, which makes the inner house exceptional, is the more analytically interesting reading, and the reasoning from functional dependency provides its logical foundation.

## House Without Doors

### Building Palterin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הַבּוֹנֶה פַּלְטֵרִין גְּדוֹלִים בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהֶעֱמִיד לָהֶן דְּלָתוֹת – קָנָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? קַמָּא – לִבְנֵי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דַּאֲפֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of taking possession of ownerless property. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** With regard to **one who builds large palaces [*palterin*] on the property of a convert** who died without heirs, **and another came and placed doors** upon **them,** the latter **has acquired** the property. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? **The first,** i.e., the one who built the palaces, **merely turned over bricks,** i.e., building an incomplete house is not sufficient to take possession of the property.

Rav Nachman states in the name of Rabba bar Avuha: *haboneh palterin gedolim benichsei hager* — one who builds a large mansion on the estate of a *ger* who died without heirs. The land is *hefker*. Rather than performing the conventional acts of *chazakah* — fencing the land, opening an aperture — this person constructs an entire house on the vacant lot. One might assume this is an exceptionally strong *chazakah*: the property value has been radically improved.

### Adding Doors

Then another person comes and installs the doors — *uva acher vehe'emid lahen dlatot* — and it is this second person who acquires the field. The builder of the mansion acquires nothing. The one who merely hung the doors acquires everything, including the structure, though the builder is presumably entitled to reimbursement for his labor. The reason the second person acquires through the property follows from the principle that when one builds on another's land — even *hefker* land — the structure attaches to the land, and title follows from the *kinyan* on the land itself.

The *gemara* explains: *kama, livnei be'alma hu de'apich* — the first person merely turned over some bricks. But this explanation is, on its face, unsatisfying. Building an entire mansion is a transformative improvement to the property. Why is it not a *chazakah*?

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot engages this question systematically. First, Tosafot addresses the suggestion that the builder performed a conventional act of *chazakah* — digging the foundation. Tosafot replies: *keshebana yesod, lo mahani midi, ella bekarka haomed lacharisha* — digging a foundation counts as an act of turning the earth only when the land is suited for plowing and planting. Here, the purpose is construction, not cultivation.

But a deeper question confronts Tosafot: a case appears later in the *gemara* where a person finds a mansion already standing on *hefker* land and performs *siyud* — plastering a small section, or making a minor engraving — and thereby acquires the entire property. How can plastering a wall be a *chazakah* when building a whole house is not?

Tosafot's answer is precise: *dehatam mashpiach benichsei hager deinu paltarin* — in the case of the already-standing mansion, the property is the house, and the act of plastering improves the house. But in the case where one builds the mansion from scratch, *mai nichsei hager? hu karka* — the *hefker* property is the land, not the house. And the land has not been improved: *she'ein rauyah ledur sham kol zeman she'ein dlatot* — the property is not fit for habitation as long as there are no doors.

The logic is not about property value — that has obviously increased dramatically — but about functional improvement. A house without doors cannot be lived in. No one takes up permanent residence in a doorless structure. Therefore, despite the enormous investment of construction, the land has not yet been functionally improved. The property cannot yet fulfill its purpose as a dwelling. Only when doors are installed does the house become habitable, and only then has the land been genuinely improved in a functional sense.

Tosafot anticipates the obvious objection: even without doors, one can take shelter in the structure from sun or rain — *vayeish bah gag ve'rauy lehikanes bah mipnei hachamma u'migsham* — so some functional benefit does exist. Tosafot responds: *dechol makom lo chazi ledira keva kol zeman she'ein bah dlatot* — without doors, the structure is not suited for permanent habitation, and it is permanent habitation that constitutes the relevant functional improvement for purposes of *kinyan*.

Tosafot then adds a striking observation: *adarabba kilkel, deme'ikara chazi lizeri'ah, ve'hashta lo chazi lizeri'ah* — in fact, the builder has made things worse. Before the mansion was built, the land was suitable for planting and cultivation. Now, with a building standing on it, it can no longer be farmed. The act of construction has actually *reduced* one form of utility — agricultural use — while the use it was meant to create, residential use, is not yet operative. Narrowly defined, the builder has not improved the land; he has rendered it temporarily unusable for any purpose.

## Plastering Floors

### Siyud on Palterin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמּוֹצֵא פַּלְטֵרִין בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר, וְסָד בָּהֶן סִיּוּד אֶחָד אוֹ כִּיּוּר אֶחָד – קְנָאָן. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אַמָּה. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וּכְנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: One who finds palaces** built **on the property of a convert** who died without heirs **and plastered them** with **one** application of **plaster or** tiled them with **one tile, has acquired them.** The Gemara asks: **And how much,** i.e., what is the minimum area that must be plastered or tiled? **Rav Yosef said:** A square **cubit. Rav Ḥisda said: And** he acquires it in this manner only if it was plastered or tiled **opposite the entrance,** where it can be easily seen.

Rav Dimi bar Yosef states in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *hamotzei paltarin benichsei hager vesad bahen siyud echad* — one who finds a mansion already standing on *hefker* land — the estate of a deceased *ger* — and plasters a single section of it, *kna'an* — acquires it. As Tosafot already noted in the previous context, this case is the contrast to building a house: there, the property is the land; here, the property is the mansion, and plastering improves the mansion.

### Amah & Keneged Hapetach

Two further conditions are specified. Rav Yosef states: *amah* — the plaster or engraving must be at least an *amah* in extent; anything smaller is too trivial to qualify as a genuine improvement. Rav Chisda adds: *uchneged hapetach* — the plastering must be in a visible location. The Rashbam understands this to mean that when one opens the door, the plastered area is immediately visible — facing the entrance. If the improvement is in a more hidden location, a larger and more substantial act would be required. The underlying principle is consistent with everything established in this daf: a *chazakah* must represent a substantive, perceptible improvement, not a trivial or invisible one.

## Benefit vs. Improvement

### Matzia Matza'ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: הַאי מִילְּתָא אֲמַר לַן רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, וְאַנְהֲרִינְהוּ עַיְנִין מִמַּתְנִיתָא: הַמַּצִּיעַ מַצָּעוֹת בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר – קָנָה. ״וְאַנְהֲרִינְהוּ עַיְנִין מִמַּתְנִיתָא״ – מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: כֵּיצַד בַּחֲזָקָה? נָעַל לוֹ מִנְעָלוֹ, אוֹ הִתִּיר לוֹ מִנְעָלוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁהוֹלִיךְ כֵּלָיו אַחֲרָיו לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ, וְהִפְשִׁיטוֹ, וְהִרְחִיצוֹ, סָכוֹ, גֵּרְדוֹ, וְהִלְבִּישׁוֹ, וְהִנְעִילוֹ, וְהִגְבִּיהוֹ – קְנָאוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לֹא תְּהֵא חֲזָקָה גְּדוֹלָה מֵהַגְבָּהָה – שֶׁהַגְבָּהָה קוֹנָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said: Rav Sheshet said this statement to us, and he enlightened our eyes from a *baraita*** that alludes to the same matter. He said: **One who spreads** out **mattresses on the property of a convert** who died without heirs **has acquired** it. **And** that which I said, that **he enlightened our eyes from a *baraita*, what is it? As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Kiddushin* 1:5): **How** does one acquire a Canaanite slave **through taking possession?** If the slave **placed** one's **shoe for him, or untied** his **shoe for him, or** if it occurred **that he carried his garments after him to the bathhouse, or undresses him, or bathes him,** or **anoints him,** or **scrubs** the oil off **him, or dresses him, or puts on his shoes, or lifts him,** one **acquires** the slave. **Rabbi Shimon said:** The acquisition generated by taking **possession should not be** considered **greater than** the acquisition generated by **lifting, as lifting acquires** property **in any situation.**

Rav Amram then cites a teaching that Rav Sheshet shared with them — a teaching that, in Rav Amram's words, *ve'anharnu einin*, lit up their eyes — and which Rav Sheshet subsequently showed was implicit in a *baraita*. The teaching: *hamatzia matza'ot benichsei hager* — one who spreads mattresses on *hefker* land and sleeps there has acquired it, *kana*.

This is surprising given everything established up to this point. The entire framework of *chazakah* as *kinyan* has been built around the principle of improving the land. Sleeping on the land with a mattress does not improve it. Some *Rishonim* note that sleeping directly on the bare ground without a mattress would not suffice either — it is not a normal manner of benefiting from land. But spreading a mattress and sleeping constitutes a normal, comfortable manner of enjoying the property directly. Nevertheless, by the logic of improvement, it should not work.

### Slave Chazakah

The source that illuminated this is a *baraita* from Kiddushin concerning the *chazakah* of slaves. Slaves, like land, are acquired through *kessef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*. What constitutes the *chazakah* of a slave? The *baraita* lists: *na'alo min'alo* — if the slave tied the master's shoe; *o hitir lo min'alo* — or untied it; *o sheholich kelav acharav l'veit hamirchatz* — or carried the master's bathing equipment to the bathhouse; *hifshito* — undressed him; *hirtzo* — bathed him; *sacho* — anointed him with oil; *gardo* — scraped him; *vehilbisho* — dressed him; *vehin'ilo* — put on his shoe; *vehigbiho* — or lifted him up. Any of these constitutes *kana* — acquisition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לֹא תְּהֵא חֲזָקָה גְּדוֹלָה מֵהַגְבָּהָה – שֶׁהַגְבָּהָה קוֹנָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said:** Acquisition generated through taking **possession should not be greater than** acquisition generated through **lifting, as lifting acquires** property **in any situation.**

Rabbi Shimon adds: *lo tehei chazakah gedolah mehagbahah, shehagbahah konah bechol makom* — the *chazakah* of a slave should not be more effective than *hagbahah*, which works everywhere. The *gemara* clarifies Rabbi Shimon's intent: implicit in the *tanna kamma* is that if the master were to lift the slave — *higbia rabbo lo* — that would not constitute *kinyan hagbahah* on the slave; only *kinyan chazakah* applies to slaves. Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds that *hagbahah* on a slave should also work. The debate about *hagbahah* of a slave is set aside; what interests us here is the nature of *chazakah* on a slave.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: הִגְבִּיהוֹ לְרַבּוֹ – קְנָאוֹ, הִגְבִּיַּ רַבּוֹ לוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לֹא תְּהֵא חֲזָקָה גְּדוֹלָה מֵהַגְבָּהָה – שֶׁהַגְבָּהָה קוֹנָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this last statement, the Gemara asks: **What is** Rabbi Shimon **saying** here, as the first *tanna* also said that a slave can be acquired by lifting? The Gemara explains: **This** is what he **is saying:** The first *tanna* holds that if **he lifted his master,** the master **acquires him,** as he is performing labor for the master, but if **his master lifted him,** the master **does not acquire him,** as the slave has not performed labor on his behalf. With regard to this *halakha*, **Rabbi Shimon said:** Acquisition generated through taking **possession should not be greater than** acquisition generated through **lifting, as lifting acquires** property **in any situation.** Consequently, one can acquire a slave even by lifting him.

### Benefit Framework

The *chiddush* that Rav Sheshet derived from this *baraita* is significant. None of the listed acts of slave *chazakah* involves improving the slave. The master is not giving the slave a haircut or a manicure — he is not doing anything to enhance the slave's condition. On the contrary, the slave is improving the master: tying his shoes, bathing him, carrying his belongings. The master is deriving benefit from the slave, not improving him.

This compels a broadening of the definition of *chazakah*. *Chazakah* is not limited to acts of improvement; it also encompasses deriving direct benefit from the thing itself. This makes *chazakah* somewhat more similar to the *chazakah* of three years — the evidence of ownership derived from harvesting the land's produce — in that benefit, not only physical transformation, can establish acquisition. But this expanded definition must be carefully delimited, or it collapses the distinction entirely.

The key constraint is directness of interaction. Spreading a mattress and sleeping on the land is a direct interaction with the land itself — one's body directly benefits from the ground. But picking apples from a tree is not sufficiently direct: the land is below, the tree grows from it, the fruit hangs from the tree, and the benefit is realized when the harvested fruit is consumed. Too many intermediary steps intervene between the land and the benefit. Similarly, with the slave: all of the listed acts involve the slave's body directly serving the master's body — tying his shoe, bathing him, lifting him. By contrast, if one directed a *hefker* slave to write a letter, the benefit (a completed letter) is indirect, mediated by the slave's labor producing an object. Likewise, sending the slave to harvest one's field does not constitute *chazakah* because the benefit is indirect and removed.

The principle that emerges is: *chazakah* as *kinyan* encompasses not only physical improvement of the object but also direct bodily benefit derived from the object itself, provided the interaction is immediate rather than mediated through an intervening act or product. Playing a game on a vacant field, for instance, would meet this standard — the body directly benefits from the ground. Harvesting fruit from the land does not, because the act of reaping and the act of benefiting are distinct and separated.

## Intent & Indirectness

### Shada Lifta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בִּירָאָה אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דִּשְׁדָא לִיפְתָּא בֵּי פִילֵי דְּאַרְעָא דְגֵר – לָא הָוֵי חֲזָקָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? בְּעִידָּנָא דִּשְׁדָא – לָא הָוֵי שְׁבָחָא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּקָא שָׁבַח – מִמֵּילָא קָא שָׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya Bira'a says** that **Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **this one** **who threw turnip** seeds **in the crevices [*filei*] of the land of a convert** who died without heirs, it **is not** sufficient to **take possession. What is the reason** for this? **As at the time that** he **threw** the seeds **there was no enhancement** to the value of the field. **Now that** the turnips have grown and the value of the field **is enhanced, it is enhanced by itself.**

Rav Yirmeya Bira'a states in the name of Rav Yehuda: *hai man d'shada lifta bei pilei d'ara d'ger* — one who drops seeds into a crevice on *hefker* land — *lo havi chazakah*, it does not constitute *chazakah*. The reasoning: *be'idna d'shada lo havi shivcha* — at the moment of dropping the seeds, there has been no improvement to the land. The seeds are merely sitting in a crevice; the land itself has not been altered.

One might object: *hashta d'kasmach* — eventually the seeds will sprout and grow, and the land will then contain growing produce and will have been improved. The *gemara* responds: *mimila kasmach* — that happens on its own. The improvement that eventually results is not produced by the act itself; it is produced by nature acting upon the seeds over time. The act of dropping seeds into a crevice — without plowing, covering, or watering — has not yet transformed the land in any way. The eventual improvement is too indirect and too removed from the initial act to be attributed to it. This is consistent with the principle of directness established throughout the daf: the act of *chazakah* must itself constitute the improvement or the direct benefit, not merely set in motion a sequence that may eventually lead to improvement.

### Pruning Dikla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דְּפָשַׁח דִּיקְלָא; אַדַּעְתָּא דְּדִיקְלָא – קָנֵי, אַדַּעְתָּא דְחֵיוָתָא – לָא קָנֵי. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? שְׁקַל מֵהַאי גִּיסָא וּמֵהַאי גִּיסָא – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּדִיקְלָא. כּוֹלָּא מֵחַד גִּיסָא – אַדַּעְתָּא דְחֵיוָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a series of rulings with regard to taking possession of land. **Shmuel says:** With regard to **this one who cuts** the branches of **a palm tree,** if he had **in mind** the acquisition **of the palm tree,** he **has acquired** it; but if he had **in mind** the benefit **of the animals,** to feed his animals the branches, he **has not acquired** it. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which it can be known what he had in mind? The Gemara answers: If he **took** branches **from this side and from that side,** he had **in mind** the acquisition **of the palm tree,** as this assists the growth of the tree; but if the branches that he took were **all from one side,** he had **in mind** the benefit **of the animals.**

Shmuel concludes with one further case: *hai man d'pashach dikla ada'ata d'dikla* — one who prunes a palm tree with the intent of improving the tree, *kani* — acquires it. This is a valid act of *chazakah*: one performed a physical act that improves the object. But *ada'ata d'chiyusa* — if one pruned the palm tree not to improve it but to obtain the branches for one's animals — *lo kani*, it does not work.

This introduces a further dimension: intent. Even an act that objectively results in improvement to the object does not constitute *chazakah* if the actor's intention was not directed toward improving the object. The pruning itself may be physically identical in both cases, and the tree may be equally improved in both cases, yet the absence of improvement-directed intent disqualifies the act.

How does one determine which intent governed? *Shakal mehai gisa u'mehai gisa* — if the pruning is distributed evenly from both sides of the tree, this indicates concern for the tree's health and welfare, and we interpret the act as *ada'ata d'dikla* — directed toward the tree — even if one's ultimate purpose was also to obtain branches. The act of pruning evenly, which is good for the tree, demonstrates that one was acting as a steward of the tree. *Kula m'chad gisa* — if one took all the branches from one side only, which is harmful to the tree though perhaps more convenient for one's purposes, this signals that the tree's wellbeing was not the primary concern, and the act is characterized as *ada'ata d'chiyusa*, with the consequence that it does not constitute *chazakah*.

The lesson is that it is not sufficient for an act to result in improvement — it must be performed as an act of improvement. *Chazakah* requires not only the right external act but the right internal orientation: one must be acting as an owner who is improving and caring for the property, not as a user who happens to leave the property somewhat better than he found it. This theme — that intent and the quality of the act matter alongside its objective consequences — will continue to be explored in the cases that follow on the next daf.
