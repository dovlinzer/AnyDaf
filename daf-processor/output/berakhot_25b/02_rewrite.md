# Berakhot 25b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'al Keri Mikvah

### Mishnah Ba'al Keri

The *amud* opens with a *mishnah* concerning the *ba'al keri* — a man who has had a seminal emission — who is ordinarily unable to recite *shema*. The *mishnah* states that if he has the opportunity to go to the *mikvah*, emerge, dry himself, dress, and complete all of this before sunrise, he should do so. If not, he should remain in the *mikvah* and recite *shema* there, using the water as a covering for his body. The implication of the *mishnah*, then, is that the deadline for reciting *shema* is *hanetz hachamah*, sunrise.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

The *gemara* opens by asking: *leima tanna stam keRebbi Eliezer* — does the anonymous *mishnah* reflect the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *shema* may only be recited until *hanetz hachamah*, until sunrise? The *gemara* deflects this: *afilu teima Rebbi Yehoshua* — one could even say the *mishnah* reflects the view of Rabbi Yehoshua, who permits *shema* until three hours into the day. The reason the *mishnah* emphasizes sunrise is not to establish the final deadline but to capture the *vatikin* ideal. As Rabbi Yochanan states: *vatikin hayu gomrim otah im hanetz hachamah* — the *vatikin*, the scrupulous and ancient ones, would finish *shema* precisely at the moment of sunrise so that they could begin *shemoneh esrei* exactly at that point. The *mishnah*'s emphasis on sunrise is thus not a ceiling but an ideal: even if one is not disqualified by going past sunrise, achieving *shema* at that moment is the most praiseworthy *zman*.

### Heart Seeing Nakedness

The *gemara* then turns to a practical problem with reciting *shema* in the *mikvah*: *veHare livo roeh et haErvah* — but the heart sees the nakedness. The water serves as a covering for the body from the neck down, but there is nothing dividing the chest from the genital area. This is grounded in the verse *velo yera'eh becha ervat davar* — no nakedness shall be seen among you — which the *gemara* reads as prohibiting any exposure of *ervah* in the context of reciting *berakhot*, *tefillah*, and the like.

The concern, as Rashi explains, is that the upper torso of the body is not normally exposed to the genital area, and therefore when nothing divides them — as when a person is simply draped in a straight garment with no belt — there is a problem of *libo roeh et haErvah*. This principle has direct practical application: one may not *daven* or recite *berakhot* when the chest and the genital area are under the same uncinched garment without any divider. This is, incidentally, the halakhic basis for the *gartel*.

### Dirty Water Solution

The *gemara* resolves the problem of *libo roeh et haErvah* in the *mikvah* by quoting Rabbi Elazar, or as another version has it, Rabbi Acha bar Abba bar Acha in the name of Rabbeinu: *bemayim achurim shanu* — the *mishnah* is speaking of dirty, murky water. Such water, the *gemara* explains, is *dedamu ke'ara semichta* — it resembles thick earth. When one is submerged up to the neck in such opaque water, it is as though every part of the body is clothed. There is no sense of one area being exposed to another; the murky water functions like a garment covering the entire submerged body.

### Clear Water Debate

A *braisa* then addresses the case of *mayim tzulim*, clear water. It rules that one may sit in clear water up to the neck and recite *shema*. A minority view adds that one should agitate the water with one's foot — stirring up the mud from the floor — so as to make the water sufficiently opaque. The *gemara* then questions the first opinion: does it not face the problem of *libo roeh et haErvah*? The answer is that the first *tanna* holds *libo roeh et haErvah mutar* — he does not consider this a halakhic problem. According to his view, the prohibition of *ervah* is defined solely by external visibility: as long as the nakedness is not visible to others, there is no violation, regardless of what is exposed within the garment or water.

### Heart vs Heel

The *gemara* pushes further: if the first *tanna* permits *libo roeh*, what about *harei ikvo roeh et haErvah* — the heel also sees the nakedness? Rashi explains that the underlying concern is that parts of the body not normally exposed to one another must be covered. Yet the *gemara* resolves this symmetrically: just as *libo roeh mutar* according to the first *tanna*, so too *akvo roeh mutar* — the heel being exposed to the *ervah* is likewise no concern, as long as the nakedness is not visible to the outside.

The *halakhah* that emerges, and the one we rule by, is that *akvo roeh et haErvah* is not a problem. The only operative concern is *libo roeh et haErvah* — the chest and the genital area must have some divider between them. In practical terms today, underwear with an elastic band provides that division; the *gemara*'s case assumes a straight, uncinched garment with no waist separation.

### Abaye Rava Debate

The *gemara* then raises the question of *noge'a* — touching. If the heel is not merely exposed to the *ervah* but is physically touching it, is there a problem? The *gemara* recalls the earlier discussion about two people under a blanket, where touching (as opposed to merely seeing) was its own concern. Abaye says it is forbidden; Rava says it is permitted.

However, Rav Zvid transmits the debate differently from Rabbi Chinana son of Rav Ika, who presents the following version: when it comes to actual touching — *noge'a* — even Rava agrees it is forbidden, *kulei alma assur*. The debate between Abaye and Rava applies only to the case of *roeh* — exposure without touching: Abaye says even that is forbidden, Rava says it is permitted.

### Torah Not Angels

Rava's reasoning is encapsulated in the famous principle: *lo nitna Torah lemalachei hasharet* — the Torah was not given to the ministering angels. As Rashi explains, angels have no *ervah*; they can afford such scrupulousness. But human beings, *aval anu shebasarenu yesh lanu ervah ve'i efshar lanu lehishamer mikol zeh* — we have an *ervah* and it is impossible for us to guard against all of this. To demand that one never *daven* or recite *berakhot* in conditions where *akvo roeh et haErvah* applies — particularly in an era before underwear was commonplace, or in the common *gemara* case of being naked under a blanket on a cold night — would be an unreasonable burden.

The final *halakhah*: *noge'a assur*, touching with another body part is forbidden, in accordance with Rava; *roeh mutar*, mere exposure of the heel to the *ervah* is permitted. The *libo roeh* concern, however, remains operative and requires a divider at the waist.

## Tzoa Ervah Issues

### Tzoa Covered Nakedness

The *gemara* introduces a new case from Rava: *tzoa be'ashashit mutar likrot krias shema k'negda* — if excrement is placed beneath a glass container, one may recite *shema* in its presence, even though the excrement is visible through the glass. However, *ervah be'ashashit asur likrot krias shema k'negda* — nakedness covered by a transparent covering remains forbidden for reciting *shema*.

### Covering Distinction

The *gemara* explains the distinction. The prohibition of *tzoa* is grounded in the verse *vechisita et tze'atecha* — cover your excrement — which speaks of covering as the operative concept. Once the *tzoa* is covered, even beneath a transparent vessel, the requirement of covering has been fulfilled. Therefore it is permitted. The *gemara* raises the question of whether visibility should matter, given the prior discussion about not having excrement in one's field of vision — but that earlier concern applied only to uncovered excrement. Once it is covered, visibility is no longer the criterion.

*Ervah*, by contrast, is governed by the verse *velo yera'eh becha ervat davar* — it shall not be *seen*. A transparent covering still allows it to be seen, and therefore the prohibition remains. The *gemara* is at pains here to distinguish two different textual bases: covering for *tzoa*, seeing for *ervah*, and a transparent vessel satisfies the former but not the latter.

### Spit Nullification

Abaye introduces a practical dispensation for a small amount of *tzoa*: *mevatlah berok* — one may spit on it, with the spit overwhelming the small quantity of excrement and effectively nullifying its presence. Rava adds that the spit must be *rok avah*, thick spit, for this to work.

### Excrement Pit Covered

Rava further rules: *tzoa begumah meniach sandalo aleha vekoreh krias shema* — if excrement is in a small indentation or pit in the ground, one may cover it with one's shoe and then recite *shema*, since placing the shoe over it constitutes a valid covering. This is again presuming the absence of any smell problem.

### Stuck Excrement Teiku

Mar son of Ravina raises the question that would have been familiar to anyone who has walked a city street: *tzoa sheduvuka besandalo mahu* — what is the law if excrement is stuck to the bottom of one's sandal? Perhaps the act of standing on it presses it against the ground in a manner analogous to covering; perhaps not. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Rav Yehuda Nakedness

### Naked Non-Jew Issue

Rav Yehuda rules: a naked non-Jew (*nochri*) — it should be noted that the term *akum* appearing in the printed text is a result of censorship; the original reading is *nochri* — renders *krias shema* forbidden in his presence. The *gemara* immediately challenges this.

### Jew Forbidden Too

The *gemara* asks: *mai irya akum afilu Yisrael nami* — why single out a non-Jew? Even a Jew is forbidden. The *gemara* answers: for a Jew, the prohibition is obvious; the case of a non-Jew needs to be stated because one might have thought otherwise. Specifically, one might argue — based on the verse in Yechezkel that compares the flesh of certain people to *besar chamorim*, the flesh of donkeys — that a non-Jew's nakedness is halakhically equivalent to an animal's nakedness, which does not constitute *ervah*. Therefore Rav Yehuda must tell us that this is not so: *ikru erva*, they are also considered *ervah*, as demonstrated by the narrative of Noah in which the nakedness of a non-Jewish figure is explicitly described using that term.

### Non-Jew Comments

This *gemara* belongs to a category of Talmudic passages that make deeply uncomfortable statements about non-Jews. This is one of the reasons passages like this underwent self-censorship in the medieval and early modern periods, with terms like *nochri* replaced by *akum*. The claim is grounded in a prooftext — the verse from Yechezkel — and the *gemara*'s use of it is a *havah amina*, a rejected argument, rather than a ruling. Yet the very framing invites serious reflection.

### Teaching Responsibility

Every major religious tradition contains passages that express hostility or dehumanizing attitudes toward outsiders, often composed under conditions of persecution and communal siege. The Jewish community was no exception. The critical question, however, is not simply that such passages exist — it is what we do with them. The *talmud* is a multi-vocal text, and it contains not only passages like this but many that affirm the fundamental dignity and humanity of all people. The burden falls on the teacher and the posek to determine which voices carry halakhic authority and which reflect a historically conditioned margin.

The concern is not abstract: when inflammatory passages are transmitted uncritically, as authoritative lines dictating practical attitudes, they shape the psyche and behavior of those who receive them. The *torah she'be'al peh* is unique in that it records virtually every current of feeling and thought that passed through the tradition — but this freedom comes with a corresponding responsibility on the part of those who teach it to contextualize, bracket, and assign authority appropriately.

## Smelly Waters Urine

### Smelly Water Ban

The *gemara* rules that one may not cover oneself with *mei masra* — smelly water — or with the water used to soak flax, which is also malodorous, for the purpose of reciting *shema*. Even if one were to pour additional water into such a vessel, the smell would not be neutralized; these waters are categorically excluded from use.

### Urine Dilution

Urine (*mei raglayim*), however, occupies a different category. The *gemara* had treated urine with somewhat less stringency in earlier discussions, and here it permits using urine if one first adds water to it: *ad sheyatil letochan mayim*.

### Water Amount Debate

A *braisa* asks how much water must be added. One opinion says *kol shehu* — even the smallest amount suffices. Rav Zakai says one needs a *revi'is*, approximately three and a half ounces.

### Timing Water Addition

Rav Nachman introduces a distinction based on the timing of when the water is added. If the water is added *levasof* — after the urine has already been collected — the debate between *kol shehu* and *revi'is* applies. But if the water was already in the vessel *betchilah*, before any urine was added, then even Rav Zakai would agree that *kol shehu* suffices. Rashi explains this using the logic of *bitul*: as each drop of urine falls into a vessel already containing water, it is immediately nullified by the water already present.

Rav Yosef reverses the framework: the debate between *kol shehu* and *revi'is* applies at the beginning, when water is added before urination. Once the urine is already collected, however, everyone agrees that *revi'is* is required. Rav Yosef acted in accordance with Rav Zakai's position — instructing his attendant to bring him a *revi'is* of water before he urinated, so he could then *daven*.

### Chamber Pots Prohibited

A *braisa* rules that a *garef shel re'i* — a chamber pot used for excrement — and an *avit shel mei raglayim* — a vessel used for urine — are both forbidden in the presence of *krias shema*, even if they have been completely emptied. The reason is twofold: they are designated for that purpose, and they have absorbed smells over time. Similarly, urine itself requires dilution with water before one may recite *shema* in its presence, with the same debate between *kol shehu* and Rav Zakai's *revi'is* applying once more.

## Distance Bathrooms

### Excrement Bed Position

The *mishnah* is now read in the context of excrement near a bed — the setting in which *shema* would often be recited. The *gemara* distinguishes between the chamber pots being *lifnei hamita*, in front of the bed (in front of the person reciting), and *leachar hamita*, behind the bed (behind the person).

### Gamliel Position

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that if the chamber pot is behind the bed, one may recite *shema* immediately; if it is in front, one may not recite. He adds that distancing oneself *arba amos* — four cubits — from the excrement suffices. The *gemara* probes whether his position is: (a) behind the bed requires no action whatsoever, while in front requires four *amos*; or (b) behind the bed requires four *amos*, while in front is entirely impermissible.

### Eliezer Room Rule

Rabbi Eliezer takes the more stringent position: even a room one hundred *amos* across does not afford sufficient distance. One may not recite *shema* in the same room as these vessels until they have either been removed from the room entirely or placed *tachat hamita* — under the bed — which functions as a form of burial.

### Braisot Resolution

A second *braisa* is then cited in which the positions of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appear in reverse from the first *braisa*. The *gemara* initially wonders which *braisa* has the names recorded correctly. It determines that the second *braisa* must be the one with the transposed names, and resolves the conflict by reversing the attributions there. The reasoning: the position that the entire room is equivalent to four *amos* is independently verified as belonging to Rabbi Shimon bar Elazar, corroborating the first *braisa*'s attribution. Rashi notes that he does not know where else in *Shas* this independent verification is to be found — and indeed, Rabbi Akiva Eiger in his *Gilyon HaShas* lists approximately fifteen instances in *Shas* where Rashi writes *lo yadati heichan hi*, acknowledging that a *gemara*'s cross-reference cannot be located elsewhere.

### Room vs Outdoor

The conceptual underpinning of the *halakhah* is as follows: outdoors, a person's personal space is defined as four *amos* in any direction; beyond that, something is no longer considered to be within one's domain. Indoors, however, the entire room constitutes one unified space — *kulei beisa k'arba amos dami* — and therefore the distance of four *amos* is insufficient. One must either remove the *tzoa* from the room or place it under the bed. The final *halakhah* is in accordance with Rabbi Shimon bar Elazar. Outdoors, four *amos* suffices; indoors, the entire room is treated as though it were within four *amos*.

## Bed Height Domains

### Bed Height Question

Rav Yosef posed a question to Rav Huna regarding the height of the bed above the floor when a chamber pot is placed beneath it. Rav Huna stated that it was obvious to him that if the bed is less than three *tefachim* from the ground — approximately nine inches — the principle of *lavud* applies: the gap is so small that it is legally as though there is no gap, and the chamber pot is considered buried. Similarly, it was clear that if the bed is ten *tefachim* or more from the ground, the space beneath constitutes its own independent domain — *reshus achres* — and placing the vessel there is not considered burial at all. The question was: what about the range of three to ten *tefachim*?

### Rava Bed Ruling

Rava rules definitively on the endpoints: less than three *tefachim* is like *lavud* and is considered burial; ten *tefachim* constitutes a separate domain and is not. These rulings are clear and not in dispute.

### Three Ten Range

The middle range — from three to ten *tefachim* — remains unresolved. This was precisely the question Rav Yosef posed to Rav Huna, and neither provided a definitive answer.

### Halacha Practice

The practical *halakhah* remains as established: within the same room, the entire room is one domain and four *amos* does not suffice; outdoors, four *amos* is the operative distance.

## Torah Tefillin Bedroom

### Bridal Chamber Torah

Rav Acha married off his son to the daughter of the house of Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel and Marta. On the wedding night, the couple was brought to the bridal chamber, but *lo havi mistayei milta* — things were not succeeding: they were unable to consummate the marriage. Rav Acha followed to investigate and discovered that a *sefer Torah* had been stored in the bridal chamber — apparently in the home of the bride's father, where a room had been set aside, the father's place of storage for the *sefer Torah*. Rav Acha told the father-in-law that had he not come to check, he would have endangered his son — both spiritually and, the *gemara* implies, perhaps even as divine intervention preventing the consummation of an act that would have been a transgression.

### Torah Double Vessel

A *braisa* establishes the underlying *halakhah*: a room that contains a *sefer Torah* or *tefillin* is forbidden for marital relations (*shimmush hamita*) until the sacred objects are either removed from the room or placed in a double vessel — *kli besoch kli*, one container inside of another.

### Non-Standard Covering

Abaye qualifies: the double vessel requirement is not satisfied if both vessels are the normal coverings of the object. At least one of the two containers must be something other than the standard pouch or wrapping in which the object is normally kept. The reasoning is that the double vessel is meant to convey that the sacred object is truly put away and not present; if one uses only the normal coverings, even stacking them ten deep, the object is still effectively "dressed" in its ordinary state and is considered present.

### Standard Coverings Fail

Rava articulates this principle explicitly: *afilu asarah mani k'chad mani dami* — even ten coverings, if they are all the standard coverings of the object, are considered legally equivalent to a single covering. However, a combination such as wrapping a *sefer* in a cloak (*glima*) and placing it in a book chest (*kamtra*) — where the cloak is not the *sefer*'s ordinary covering — constitutes a valid double vessel.

### Partition Solution

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers an alternative: rather than double-wrapping, one may make a *mechitzah* — a partition — ten *tefachim* high between oneself and the *sefer Torah*. This effectively creates a separate domain for the *sefer Torah* and permits marital relations on one's side of the partition.

### Mar Zutra Story

Mar Zutra visited the home of Rav Ashi and entered the room of Rav Ashi's son, Mar bar Rav Ashi, who was then living there with his wife. He noticed that a *sefer Torah* had been placed in the room behind a *mechitzah* of ten *tefachim*. Mar Zutra challenged this: *keman*, like whom are you ruling — like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi? And even Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi permitted the *mechitzah* solution only *deleis lei beita achrina* — when one has no other room available. Since Rav Ashi's household surely had other rooms, the *sefer Torah* should have been moved there rather than remaining in the room behind a partition. Mar bar Rav Ashi acknowledged the point: *lav adatai* — he had not thought of it.

### Books Distinction

Tosafot draws a distinction regarding the degree of *kedushah* involved. The strict requirements of double vessels and non-standard coverings apply specifically to a *sefer Torah* and *tefillin*, which possess the highest level of *kedushah*. Tosafot adds that even a handwritten *chumash* should be treated with similar stringency. For *shar sefarim*, other books, a single covering suffices. In practical terms today, this distinction becomes relevant for those living in smaller apartments where *sefarim* must be stored in the bedroom: one covering is sufficient for ordinary books, while *tefillin* require the full double-vessel treatment.

## Excrement Direction

### Four Amot Behind

Rav Sechora in the name of Rav Huna rules: *lo shanu ela l'achorav* — the rule of four *amos* applies only when the *tzoa* is behind you. Four *amos* of distance to the rear is sufficient to permit recitation of *shema*.

### Field of Vision

When the *tzoa* is in front of you, a different standard applies: *marchik melo einav* — one must distance oneself until it is entirely out of one's field of vision. The same principle applies, and with even greater stringency, to *shemoneh esrei*.

### Bathroom Standing

The *gemara* cites Rav Afram bar Pappa in the name of Rav Chisda: a person may stand in front of a *beit hakisei* — a bathroom — and *daven*, apparently even though the bathroom would be within one's field of vision. How is this consistent with the rule just stated?

### Unused Bathroom

The *gemara* resolves this: *hacha b'mai askinan b'veit hakisei she'ein bo tzoa* — we are dealing with a bathroom that contains no excrement, one that has never actually been used, or from which excrement has been entirely removed. A bathroom without excrement does not carry the same status. However, the *gemara* immediately challenges this resolution as well, citing Rav Yosef: did we not already learn that a *beit hakisei* is problematic even if it contains no *tzoa* at present, because of its designated function and absorbed smells?

### Designated Unused

The *gemara* refines the position: the case of Rav Chisda must refer to a bathroom that has not merely been emptied but has never been used at all. A bathroom that has been used acquires a permanent status regardless of its current cleanliness. One that has merely been designated but never used remains ambiguous. On that precise point, Ravina raises a question — *hizmino l'veit hakisei mahu*, if a space has been designated as a bathroom but not yet used, does designation alone confer the prohibited status? The *gemara* further clarifies that Ravina's question pertains specifically to whether one may *daven* on the very spot designated for the bathroom, not merely in front of it. If one is simply facing the designated area from the outside, that is presumably fine even if the designation matters for standing on the spot. The question remains unresolved.

## Persian Bathrooms

### Sealed Design

Rava introduces a ruling of immediate practical relevance: *hani bei kissai deParsai*, the Persian-style bathrooms — even though excrement is present — are considered *kistumim damu*, as though they are sealed. Rashi explains: the Persian design featured an opening set at an angle, *veshpua*, such that waste would roll along the slope and fall into a pit below — not directly beneath where the user was seated. As a result, there were no lingering smells in the user's immediate space.

### Modern Toilets

This ruling has direct application to contemporary bathrooms. Modern flush toilets operate on an essentially analogous principle: waste and odor are immediately removed by the flushing mechanism, leaving no residue in the immediate space. On this basis, poskim have ruled that ordinary household bathrooms do not carry the halakhic status of *beit hakisei* as discussed in the *gemara*.

### Public Restrooms

The exception, however, is public restrooms. Due to the volume of use and the persistent smells that accumulate in such spaces, they do retain a problematic status and cannot be treated as equivalent to the Persian-style design.

## Women Seminal Issue

### Zav Niddah Keri

A new *mishnah* is introduced, serving to broaden the earlier discussion of *ba'al keri* to include women and those already in a state of *tumah*. The *mishnah* addresses: a *zav* — a man with an irregular bodily discharge — who then has a seminal emission; a *niddah* who expels *zera* — a woman who had intercourse before becoming a *niddah* and who now expels the semen after her period has begun; and *hamishameshet sheratah niddah* — a woman who had intercourse and then became a *niddah* after the *zera* was expelled. All of these cases require *tevilah* — immersion — not to address the other *tumah* (which the *mikvah* will not resolve), but specifically in order to be permitted to *daven* and recite *shema*, which is the ongoing concern of the *ba'al keri* legislation. Rabbi Yehuda exempts: once a person is already in a state of *tumah* from which *tevilah* would be ineffective, there is no purpose in immersing for the *keri* aspect either.

### Ba'al Keri Zav

The *gemara* raises a question regarding the order of events. When does Rabbi Yehuda's exemption apply? The *mishnah*'s first case — a *zav* who then saw *keri* — seems clear: the person was already a *zav* when the seminal emission occurred, and *tevilah* would not have helped from the outset. But what about the reverse case: a *ba'al keri* who subsequently becomes a *zav*? At the time of the seminal emission, he was indeed obligated to go to the *mikvah*. Does that original obligation persist even after he acquires *zav* status, or does the new *tumah* render the *tevilah* pointless and therefore exempt him?

### Woman Intercourse

The *gemara* finds the answer through the parallel case of the woman: *hamishameshet sheratah niddah* — the woman who had intercourse and then became a *niddah*. In this case, the *zera* was expelled first (while she was still permitted) and she became a *niddah* only afterward. This is directly analogous to a *ba'al keri* who subsequently becomes a *zav*: the *keri* / *zera* event came first, the disqualifying *tumah* came second. The *mishnah* states that she requires *tevilah*, and Rabbi Yehuda exempts here too.

### Rabbi Chiyya Explicit

Rabbi Chiyya taught this rule explicitly: *ba'al keri shera'ah zivah tzarich tevilah* — if a man was first a *ba'al keri* and subsequently became a *zav*, he requires immersion according to the *Chachamim*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Exempt

Rabbi Yehuda exempts across the board: once a person carries the status of *zav*, immersion cannot help for that *tumah*, and therefore he exempts the *ba'al keri* dimension as well, regardless of which status came first. The proof from the woman's case confirms that Rabbi Yehuda's exemption is not contingent on the order of events.

## Transition Shema

### Three Chapters End

With this *mishnah* and its *gemara*, the third *perek* of *Berakhot* concludes. The first three *perakim* of the *masechta* have addressed *krias shema* from every angle: its times, its recitation, the conditions of bodily purity required, the spatial requirements regarding *tzoa*, *ervah*, and smells, and the edge cases created by overlapping states of *tumah*.

### Fourth Chapter Start

The fourth *perek* now begins. It shifts the focus from *shema* to *shemoneh esrei* — from the recitation of the *Torah*-text that constitutes *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim* to the *amidah* that is the core act of Jewish prayer. The next several *dapim* will be devoted to the times, conditions, and conceptual foundations of *tefillah*.

---

## Prayer Times

### Shacharit Times

The opening *mishnah* of the fourth *perek* establishes the times for each of the prayers. *Tefilat hashachar* — the morning prayer, *Shacharit* — may be recited until midday. Rabbi Yehuda says it extends only until four hours into the day. Rabbi Yehuda operates from a *korban* model: the morning communal *tamid* was offered only until four hours, and therefore *tefillah*, which corresponds to the *tamid*, should likewise conclude by that time.

### Mincha Times

*Tefilat hamincha* — the afternoon prayer — may be recited until nightfall. Rabbi Yehuda says only until *plag hamincha*, which works out to approximately one hour and a quarter before nightfall. Again, Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning follows the *korban* model: the afternoon *tamid* was not offered all the way until nightfall but was concluded earlier.

### Maariv Musaf

The evening prayer — *tefillat ha'erev* — has *ein lah keva*, no fixed limit, and may be recited throughout the night. *Musaf* may be recited *kol hayom*, the entire day. Rabbi Yehuda restricts *Musaf* to until seven hours into the day — one hour past noon. Tosafot notes a linguistic distinction: for *Mincha* the *mishnah* says *ad ha'erev*, "until evening," while for *Musaf* it says *kol hayom*, "the whole day." The difference is that *Musaf* may genuinely be recited at any point during the day — even in the morning, before *Mincha* — and therefore the phrasing "all day" is more apt.

## Vatikin Ideal

### Sunrise Mitzvah

The *gemara* on the fourth *perek* opens with a tension: a *braisa* states that the *mitzvah* of *tefillah* is specifically at *hanetz hachamah*, sunrise — *mitzvatah im hanetz hachamah* — so that one may juxtapose *geulah* to *tefillah* and begin *davening* at the very start of the day. This seems to imply that the ideal time, or even the required time, for *shacharit* is sunrise, not the entire morning until noon.

### Ideal vs Halacha

The *gemara* resolves this: *ki tanya hahi l'Vatikin* — that *braisa* describes the practice of the *vatikin*, the scrupulously pious. It is an ideal, not a *halakhic* limit. The operative *halakhah* is that *shacharit* is valid until noon.

### Vatikin Timing

Rabbi Yochanan articulates the *vatikin* practice precisely: *vatikin hayu gomrim otah im hanetz hachamah* — the *vatikin* would complete *shema* exactly at the moment of sunrise, so that they could move directly into *shemoneh esrei* at that precise moment. The thrust of the *vatikin* ideal is thus to place *shemoneh esrei* — not *shema* — at sunrise. *Shema* is completed just before sunrise so that *shemoneh esrei* follows immediately at *hanetz*.

## Makeup Prayer

### Missed Maariv

Rabbi Yochanan is cited to the effect that if one forgot and failed to *daven Ma'ariv*, one may recite two *shemoneh esrei* the following morning — one for *Shacharit* and one as a makeup for the missed *Ma'ariv*. Similarly, if one missed *Shacharit*, one may recite two *shemoneh esrei* at *Mincha*. The *gemara* is thus introducing the concept of *tashlumin* — makeup prayer — which has no analogue elsewhere in *halakhah*. One does not, for instance, take the *lulav* twice the next day if one missed waving it; yet for *tefillah* such a makeup is apparently available.

### Noon Cutoff

The *gemara* refines the application: up until midday, one who prays receives *s'char tefilla bizmana* — the credit for having prayed in the proper time. After midday, one still receives the credit for *tefillah* itself but loses the additional merit of *tefilla bizmana* — prayer at the right time. This framing initially suggests that *davening* past noon is not a formal "makeup" but simply a late fulfillment, still within the extended period of the morning prayer. The *gemara* will move toward a more structured makeup model.

### Mincha Makeup

The *gemara* raises the case not addressed among those discussed: if one missed *Mincha*, can one recite two *shemoneh esrei* at *Ma'ariv* — one for *Ma'ariv* and one as a makeup for the missed *Mincha*?

### Korban vs Rachamim

This question crystallizes the central theological tension of the entire *perek*. If *tefillah* operates on the model of *korban*, then missing the time is decisive in one direction but not another. Sacrifices offered on the same halakhic day can be made up later in that day — which explains why a missed *Ma'ariv* can be compensated the next morning, since *erev* and *boker* constitute *yom echad*, one halakhic day, as the verse states *vayehi erev vayehi boker yom echad*. But a *korban* from one day cannot be brought the next day: *avar yomo batel korbano*. If *Mincha* belongs to the afternoon and *Ma'ariv* belongs to the following halakhic day, the *korban* model would prohibit any makeup.

The alternative: *kivan detzloota rachamei hi* — since prayer is fundamentally about *rachamim*, about the unrestricted pouring out of the heart to God, one might argue that *kol eima detzabei matzli* — one may pray at any time — and the entire framework of time limits and makeups is secondary to the essential nature of prayer as human communication with the divine.

### Scripture Proof

A *braisa* is cited that appears to deny any makeup for *tefillah*: *me'uvat lo yuchal litkon* — that which is perverted cannot be straightened; *vecheisaron lo yuchal lehimanot* — and that which is lacking cannot be counted. The *braisa* applies the first half of the verse to one who missed *krias shema* of the evening or morning, or who missed *Ma'ariv* or *Shacharit* — these cannot be remedied. The second half is applied to one who was absent when a group gathered for a *mitzvah*; that absence cannot be retrospectively included. This appears to categorically reject the possibility of makeup prayer.

### Willful Neglect

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the contradiction: the *braisa*'s prohibition applies specifically to one who *bittlo bemeizid* — who willfully neglected the prayer. Rav Ashi adds that the *braisa*'s own language is precise: it uses the word *bittul*, annulment or negation, rather than *ta'ah*, erring inadvertently — *shema mina*, this itself demonstrates the distinction. One who deliberately skips *tefillah* forfeits any makeup. One who genuinely forgot or erred may make it up in the following *tefillah*.

### Dialectic Resolution

The *halakhah* of makeup prayer thus embodies a genuine synthesis of the two models. Pure *korban* logic would permit no makeup across days and no flexibility whatsoever. Pure *rachamim* logic would impose no time limits at all and render the concept of "making up" meaningless. The actual *halakhah* lives in the tension between them. *Tefillah* carries the formal structure of *korbanot*: fixed times, the possibility of losing the merit of *tefilla bizmana*, and the impossibility of making up a willfully missed prayer. But the *rachamei* foundation of prayer — its nature as the unrestricted cry of the human heart to God — provides the halakhic basis for makeup prayer when a genuine inadvertent error has occurred, even across the boundary between one prayer time and the next. The times matter, but they do not utterly exhaust the meaning of *tefillah*.
