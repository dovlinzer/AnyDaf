# Berakhot 25b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'al Keri Mikvah

### Mishnah Ba'al Keri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָרַד לִטְבּוֹל, אִם יָכוֹל לַעֲלוֹת וּלְהִתְכַּסּוֹת וּלְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ וּלִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע קֹדֶם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה — עָלָה וּמִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ וְקוֹרֵא. וְאִם לָאו — יִתְכַּסֶּה בְּמַיִם וְיִקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one who experienced a seminal emission descended to immerse himself, and he is able to ascend, dress, and use the water** as a covering **and recite *Shema* before sunrise, he ascends, uses** the water **as a covering, and recites.** If he is unable to do so, **he covers himself in the water and recites.**

The *amud* opens with a *mishnah* concerning the *ba'al keri* — a man who has had a seminal emission — who is ordinarily unable to recite *shema*. The *mishnah* states that if he has the opportunity to go to the *mikvah*, emerge, dry himself, dress, and complete all of this before sunrise, he should do so. If not, he should remain in the *mikvah* and recite *shema* there, using the water as a covering for his body. The implication of the *mishnah*, then, is that the deadline for reciting *shema* is *hanetz hachamah*, sunrise.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תַּנָּא סְתָמָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּאָמַר עַד הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה? אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְדִלְמָא כְּוָתִיקִין. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וָתִיקִין הָיוּ גּוֹמְרִים אוֹתָהּ עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us say that** the anonymous *mishnah* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said:** One may recite *Shema* **until sunrise.** The Gemara immediately rejects this assumption: **Even if you say** that the *mishnah* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** who disagrees with Rabbi Eliezer and holds that one may recite the morning *Shema* until the third hour of the day, **and perhaps** the *halakha* in the *mishnah* was directed toward those whose practice was **in accordance with** the custom of the ***vatikin*,** pious individuals who were scrupulous in their performance of mitzvot, with regard to whom **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The *vatikin* would conclude** the recitation of *Shema* **with sunrise.**

The *gemara* opens by asking: *leima tanna stam keRebbi Eliezer* — does the anonymous *mishnah* reflect the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *shema* may only be recited until *hanetz hachamah*, until sunrise? The *gemara* deflects this: *afilu teima Rebbi Yehoshua* — one could even say the *mishnah* reflects the view of Rabbi Yehoshua, who permits *shema* until three hours into the day. The reason the *mishnah* emphasizes sunrise is not to establish the final deadline but to capture the *vatikin* ideal. As Rabbi Yochanan states: *vatikin hayu gomrim otah im hanetz hachamah* — the *vatikin*, the scrupulous and ancient ones, would finish *shema* precisely at the moment of sunrise so that they could begin *shemoneh esrei* exactly at that point. The *mishnah*'s emphasis on sunrise is thus not a ceiling but an ideal: even if one is not disqualified by going past sunrise, achieving *shema* at that moment is the most praiseworthy *zman*.

### Heart Seeing Nakedness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו — יִתְכַּסֶּה בְּמַיִם וְיִקְרָא. וַהֲרֵי לִבּוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה?
>
> **Translation:** **And if** one calculates that he will **not** be able to ascend and cover himself with a garment in time to recite *Shema*, he should **cover himself in the water and recite** *Shema* there. The Gemara asks: How can one recite *Shema* with his head above water? **His heart sees** his **nakedness** as there is no barrier between them.

The *gemara* then turns to a practical problem with reciting *shema* in the *mikvah*: *veHare livo roeh et haErvah* — but the heart sees the nakedness. The water serves as a covering for the body from the neck down, but there is nothing dividing the chest from the genital area. This is grounded in the verse *velo yera'eh becha ervat davar* — no nakedness shall be seen among you — which the *gemara* reads as prohibiting any exposure of *ervah* in the context of reciting *berakhot*, *tefillah*, and the like.

The concern, as Rashi explains, is that the upper torso of the body is not normally exposed to the genital area, and therefore when nothing divides them — as when a person is simply draped in a straight garment with no belt — there is a problem of *libo roeh et haErvah*. This principle has direct practical application: one may not *daven* or recite *berakhot* when the chest and the genital area are under the same uncinched garment without any divider. This is, incidentally, the halakhic basis for the *gartel*.

### Dirty Water Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וְאִי תֵּימָא רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא בַּר אַחָא מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ: בְּמַיִם עֲכוּרִין שָׁנוּ, דְּדָמוּ כְּאַרְעָא סְמִיכְתָּא, שֶׁלֹּא יִרְאֶה לִבּוֹ עֶרְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Regarding this **Rabbi Elazar said, and some say** it was **Rabbi Aḥa bar Abba bar Aḥa in the name of Rabbeinu,** Rav: **This was taught with regard to murky water, which is** considered to be **like solid earth.** Therefore, it constitutes a barrier so that **his heart does not see** his **nakedness.**

The *gemara* resolves the problem of *libo roeh et haErvah* in the *mikvah* by quoting Rabbi Elazar, or as another version has it, Rabbi Acha bar Abba bar Acha in the name of Rabbeinu: *bemayim achurim shanu* — the *mishnah* is speaking of dirty, murky water. Such water, the *gemara* explains, is *dedamu ke'ara semichta* — it resembles thick earth. When one is submerged up to the neck in such opaque water, it is as though every part of the body is clothed. There is no sense of one area being exposed to another; the murky water functions like a garment covering the entire submerged body.

### Clear Water Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַיִם צְלוּלִין — ישֵׁב בָּהֶן עַד צַוָּארוֹ וְקוֹרֵא. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים עוֹכְרָן בְּרַגְלוֹ. וְתָנָא קַמָּא, וַהֲרֵי לִבּוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה! קָסָבַר לִבּוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה — מוּתָּר. וַהֲרֵי עֲקֵבוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה! קָסָבַר עֲקֵבוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one was in **clear water, he should sit in it up to his neck and recite *Shema*. And some say: He sullies** the water **with his foot.** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the first *tanna* doesn't his heart see** his **nakedness** through the clear water? The Gemara replies: **He holds** that even if **his heart sees** his **nakedness, it is permitted** to recite *Shema*. The Gemara continues and asks: **But** in the clear water, **doesn't his heel see** his **nakedness?** The Gemara replies: Here too, the first *tanna* **holds** that in a case where **his heel sees** his **nakedness** it is **permitted.**

A *braisa* then addresses the case of *mayim tzulim*, clear water. It rules that one may sit in clear water up to the neck and recite *shema*. A minority view adds that one should agitate the water with one's foot — stirring up the mud from the floor — so as to make the water sufficiently opaque. The *gemara* then questions the first opinion: does it not face the problem of *libo roeh et haErvah*? The answer is that the first *tanna* holds *libo roeh et haErvah mutar* — he does not consider this a halakhic problem. According to his view, the prohibition of *ervah* is defined solely by external visibility: as long as the nakedness is not visible to others, there is no violation, regardless of what is exposed within the garment or water.

### Heart vs Heel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: עֲקֵבוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָעֶרְוָה מוּתָּר, נוֹגֵעַ — אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אָסוּר, וְרָבָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר. רַב זְבִיד מַתְנִי לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא הָכִי. רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא מַתְנִי לַהּ הָכִי: נוֹגֵעַ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר, רוֹאֶה — אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אָסוּר, רָבָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר: לֹא נִתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת. וְהִלְכְתָא נוֹגֵעַ — אָסוּר, רוֹאֶה — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** If **one's heel sees** his **nakedness it is permitted.** However, what is the *halakha* in a case where his heel **touches** his nakedness? May one in that circumstance recite *Shema* or not? **Abaye said: It is prohibited, and Rava said: It is permitted.** The Gemara notes: **Rav Zevid taught this *halakha* in that manner. Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rav Ika, taught it as follows:** In a case where his heel **touches** his nakedness, **everyone agrees that it is prohibited.** Their dispute is with regard to a case where his heel **sees** his nakedness. **Abaye said: It is prohibited, and Rava said: It is permitted; the Torah was not given to the ministering angels,** and a person, who, as opposed to a ministering angel, has genitals, cannot avoid this. **And the *halakha*** is that if his heel **touches** his nakedness **it is prohibited,** but if it merely **sees** his nakedness, **it is permitted.**

The *gemara* pushes further: if the first *tanna* permits *libo roeh*, what about *harei ikvo roeh et haErvah* — the heel also sees the nakedness? Rashi explains that the underlying concern is that parts of the body not normally exposed to one another must be covered. Yet the *gemara* resolves this symmetrically: just as *libo roeh mutar* according to the first *tanna*, so too *akvo roeh mutar* — the heel being exposed to the *ervah* is likewise no concern, as long as the nakedness is not visible to the outside.

The *halakhah* that emerges, and the one we rule by, is that *akvo roeh et haErvah* is not a problem. The only operative concern is *libo roeh et haErvah* — the chest and the genital area must have some divider between them. In practical terms today, underwear with an elastic band provides that division; the *gemara*'s case assumes a straight, uncinched garment with no waist separation.

### Abaye Rava Debate

The *gemara* then raises the question of *noge'a* — touching. If the heel is not merely exposed to the *ervah* but is physically touching it, is there a problem? The *gemara* recalls the earlier discussion about two people under a blanket, where touching (as opposed to merely seeing) was its own concern. Abaye says it is forbidden; Rava says it is permitted.

However, Rav Zvid transmits the debate differently from Rabbi Chinana son of Rav Ika, who presents the following version: when it comes to actual touching — *noge'a* — even Rava agrees it is forbidden, *kulei alma assur*. The debate between Abaye and Rava applies only to the case of *roeh* — exposure without touching: Abaye says even that is forbidden, Rava says it is permitted.

### Torah Not Angels

Rava's reasoning is encapsulated in the famous principle: *lo nitna Torah lemalachei hasharet* — the Torah was not given to the ministering angels. As Rashi explains, angels have no *ervah*; they can afford such scrupulousness. But human beings, *aval anu shebasarenu yesh lanu ervah ve'i efshar lanu lehishamer mikol zeh* — we have an *ervah* and it is impossible for us to guard against all of this. To demand that one never *daven* or recite *berakhot* in conditions where *akvo roeh et haErvah* applies — particularly in an era before underwear was commonplace, or in the common *gemara* case of being naked under a blanket on a cold night — would be an unreasonable burden.

The final *halakhah*: *noge'a assur*, touching with another body part is forbidden, in accordance with Rava; *roeh mutar*, mere exposure of the heel to the *ervah* is permitted. The *libo roeh* concern, however, remains operative and requires a divider at the waist.

## Tzoa Ervah Issues

### Tzoa Covered Nakedness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: צוֹאָה בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית, מוּתָּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדָּהּ. עֶרְוָה בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית, אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדָּהּ. צוֹאָה בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית מוּתָּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדָּהּ דְּצוֹאָה בְּכִיסּוּי תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא, וְהָא מִיכַּסְּיָא. עֶרְוָה בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדָּהּ, ״וְלֹא יִרְאֶה בְךָ עֶרְוַת דָּבָר״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא. וְהָא קָמִיתְחַזְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Opposite feces** covered only **by** a **lantern**-like covering, which is transparent, **it is permitted to recite *Shema*.** But **opposite nakedness** covered only **by** a **lantern-**like covering, **it is prohibited to recite *Shema*. Opposite feces in a lantern, it is permitted to recite *Shema*** because with regard to **feces,** the ability to recite *Shema* **is contingent upon covering,** as it is said: "And cover your excrement" (Deuteronomy 23:14), **and** although it is visible, **it is covered.** On the other hand, **opposite nakedness** covered only **by** a **lantern-**like covering, **it is prohibited to recite *Shema*; the Torah said: "And no indecent thing shall be seen in you"** (Deuteronomy 23:15), **and** here **it is seen.**

The *gemara* introduces a new case from Rava: *tzoa be'ashashit mutar likrot krias shema k'negda* — if excrement is placed beneath a glass container, one may recite *shema* in its presence, even though the excrement is visible through the glass. However, *ervah be'ashashit asur likrot krias shema k'negda* — nakedness covered by a transparent covering remains forbidden for reciting *shema*.

### Covering Distinction

The *gemara* explains the distinction. The prohibition of *tzoa* is grounded in the verse *vechisita et tze'atecha* — cover your excrement — which speaks of covering as the operative concept. Once the *tzoa* is covered, even beneath a transparent vessel, the requirement of covering has been fulfilled. Therefore it is permitted. The *gemara* raises the question of whether visibility should matter, given the prior discussion about not having excrement in one's field of vision — but that earlier concern applied only to uncovered excrement. Once it is covered, visibility is no longer the criterion.

*Ervah*, by contrast, is governed by the verse *velo yera'eh becha ervat davar* — it shall not be *seen*. A transparent covering still allows it to be seen, and therefore the prohibition remains. The *gemara* is at pains here to distinguish two different textual bases: covering for *tzoa*, seeing for *ervah*, and a transparent vessel satisfies the former but not the latter.

### Spit Nullification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: צוֹאָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, מְבַטְּלָהּ בְּרוֹק. אָמַר רָבָא: וּבְרוֹק עָבֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: A small amount of feces may be nullified with spittle,** and as long as it is covered, it is permitted to recite *Shema*. **Rava said:** This applies specifically when it is **thick spittle.**

Abaye introduces a practical dispensation for a small amount of *tzoa*: *mevatlah berok* — one may spit on it, with the spit overwhelming the small quantity of excrement and effectively nullifying its presence. Rava adds that the spit must be *rok avah*, thick spit, for this to work.

### Excrement Pit Covered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: צוֹאָה בְּגוּמָּא — מַנִּיחַ סַנְדָּלוֹ עָלֶיהָ, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Feces in a hole** in the ground, **he places his sandal over** the hole to cover **it and recites *Shema*.**

Rava further rules: *tzoa begumah meniach sandalo aleha vekoreh krias shema* — if excrement is in a small indentation or pit in the ground, one may cover it with one's shoe and then recite *shema*, since placing the shoe over it constitutes a valid covering. This is again presuming the absence of any smell problem.

### Stuck Excrement Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: צוֹאָה דְּבוּקָה בְּסַנְדָּלוֹ — מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Ravina, raised a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* in a case where **feces is stuck to his sandal?** Perhaps he would be considered filthy in that case? **Let** this dilemma **stand** unresolved.

Mar son of Ravina raises the question that would have been familiar to anyone who has walked a city street: *tzoa sheduvuka besandalo mahu* — what is the law if excrement is stuck to the bottom of one's sandal? Perhaps the act of standing on it presses it against the ground in a manner analogous to covering; perhaps not. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Rav Yehuda Nakedness

### Naked Non-Jew Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: גּוֹי עָרוֹם אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: Opposite a naked gentile, it is forbidden to recite *Shema*.**

Rav Yehuda rules: a naked non-Jew (*nochri*) — it should be noted that the term *akum* appearing in the printed text is a result of censorship; the original reading is *nochri* — renders *krias shema* forbidden in his presence. The *gemara* immediately challenges this.

### Jew Forbidden Too

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא גּוֹי? אֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי! יִשְׂרָאֵל פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּאָסוּר, אֶלָּא גּוֹי אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ מַהוּ דְתֵימָא, הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״אֲשֶׁר בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹרִים בְּשָׂרָם״, אֵימָא כַּחֲמוֹר בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאִינְהוּ נָמֵי אִיקְּרוּ עֶרְוָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעֶרְוַת אֲבִיהֶם לֹא רָאוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why did** Rav Yehuda discuss **particularly** the case of **a gentile? Even with regard to a Jew** it is **also** prohibited. The Gemara replies: Opposite the nakedness of **a Jew, it is obvious** that it is **prohibited; however,** opposite the nakedness of **a gentile, it was necessary for him** to say. **Lest you say** that **since it is written about** gentiles: **"Their flesh is the flesh of donkeys"** (Ezekiel 23:20), **say** that his nakedness **is like** that of **a mere donkey** and does not constitute nakedness. Rav Yehuda **taught us that their** nakedness **is also considered nakedness, as it is written** regarding the sons of Noah: **"And their father's nakedness they did not see"** (Genesis 9:23). Although Noah predated Abraham and was consequently not Jewish, his nakedness is mentioned.

The *gemara* asks: *mai irya akum afilu Yisrael nami* — why single out a non-Jew? Even a Jew is forbidden. The *gemara* answers: for a Jew, the prohibition is obvious; the case of a non-Jew needs to be stated because one might have thought otherwise. Specifically, one might argue — based on the verse in Yechezkel that compares the flesh of certain people to *besar chamorim*, the flesh of donkeys — that a non-Jew's nakedness is halakhically equivalent to an animal's nakedness, which does not constitute *ervah*. Therefore Rav Yehuda must tell us that this is not so: *ikru erva*, they are also considered *ervah*, as demonstrated by the narrative of Noah in which the nakedness of a non-Jewish figure is explicitly described using that term.

### Non-Jew Comments

This *gemara* belongs to a category of Talmudic passages that make deeply uncomfortable statements about non-Jews. This is one of the reasons passages like this underwent self-censorship in the medieval and early modern periods, with terms like *nochri* replaced by *akum*. The claim is grounded in a prooftext — the verse from Yechezkel — and the *gemara*'s use of it is a *havah amina*, a rejected argument, rather than a ruling. Yet the very framing invites serious reflection.

### Teaching Responsibility

Every major religious tradition contains passages that express hostility or dehumanizing attitudes toward outsiders, often composed under conditions of persecution and communal siege. The Jewish community was no exception. The critical question, however, is not simply that such passages exist — it is what we do with them. The *talmud* is a multi-vocal text, and it contains not only passages like this but many that affirm the fundamental dignity and humanity of all people. The burden falls on the teacher and the posek to determine which voices carry halakhic authority and which reflect a historically conditioned margin.

The concern is not abstract: when inflammatory passages are transmitted uncritically, as authoritative lines dictating practical attitudes, they shape the psyche and behavior of those who receive them. The *torah she'be'al peh* is unique in that it records virtually every current of feeling and thought that passed through the tradition — but this freedom comes with a corresponding responsibility on the part of those who teach it to contextualize, bracket, and assign authority appropriately.

## Smelly Waters Urine

### Smelly Water Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִתְכַּסֶּה לֹא בַּמַּיִם הָרָעִים וְלֹא בְּמֵי הַמִּשְׁרָה עַד שֶׁיָּטִיל לְתוֹכָן מַיִם. וְכַמָּה מַיָּא רָמֵי וְאָזֵיל? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר לֹא יִתְכַּסֶּה לֹא בְּמַיִם הָרָעִים וְלֹא בְּמֵי הַמִּשְׁרָה כְּלָל. וּמֵי רַגְלַיִם עַד שֶׁיָּטִיל לְתוֹכָן מַיִם, וְיִקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** And we learned in the mishna: **And** one who needs to recite *Shema* **may not cover himself with either foul water or water** in which flax was **soaked until he pours** other **water into it.** The Gemara asks: **How much water does he continue to pour** in order to render them a permissible covering. If he is covering himself in water in which flax was soaked, it must be a considerable amount of water, requiring at least an equally considerable amount of water to neutralize it. **Rather, this is what it says: One may neither cover himself with foul water nor water** in which flax was **soaked at all; and urine,** which is considered repugnant, **until he adds** clean **water to it, and** only then **he may** recite *Shema*.

The *gemara* rules that one may not cover oneself with *mei masra* — smelly water — or with the water used to soak flax, which is also malodorous, for the purpose of reciting *shema*. Even if one were to pour additional water into such a vessel, the smell would not be neutralized; these waters are categorically excluded from use.

### Urine Dilution

Urine (*mei raglayim*), however, occupies a different category. The *gemara* had treated urine with somewhat less stringency in earlier discussions, and here it permits using urine if one first adds water to it: *ad sheyatil letochan mayim*.

### Water Amount Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כַּמָּה יָטִיל לְתוֹכָן מַיִם — כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. רַבִּי זַכַּאי אוֹמֵר: רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a related disagreement in a *baraita*: **How much water must one add** in order **to** nullify urine? **Any quantity** is sufficient. **Rabbi Zakkai says:** One must add a **quarter** of a *log*.

A *braisa* asks how much water must be added. One opinion says *kol shehu* — even the smallest amount suffices. Rav Zakai says one needs a *revi'is*, approximately three and a half ounces.

### Timing Water Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַחֲלוֹקֶת לְבַסּוֹף. אֲבָל בַּתְּחִילָּה, כׇּל שֶׁהֵן. וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מַחֲלוֹקֶת לְכַתְּחִילָּה. אֲבָל לְבַסּוֹף דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל רְבִיעִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: אַיְּיתִי לִי רְבִיעֵיתָא דְמַיָּא, כְּרַבִּי זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** This **dispute** is with regard to a case where the urine is already in a vessel, and **afterward** one seeks to nullify it. **However,** if the clean water was in a vessel **at the beginning,** before the urine, each drop of urine is nullified as it enters the vessel and therefore **any amount** of clean water in the vessel is sufficient. **And Rav Yosef said:** This **dispute** is with regard to the amount of water necessary to have in the vessel **at the beginning,** before the urine. **However, afterward, everyone agrees** that **a quarter** of a *log* is required. The Gemara relates: **Rav Yosef said to his servant** at the beginning: **Bring me a quarter** of a *log* **of water, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zakkai.**

Rav Nachman introduces a distinction based on the timing of when the water is added. If the water is added *levasof* — after the urine has already been collected — the debate between *kol shehu* and *revi'is* applies. But if the water was already in the vessel *betchilah*, before any urine was added, then even Rav Zakai would agree that *kol shehu* suffices. Rashi explains this using the logic of *bitul*: as each drop of urine falls into a vessel already containing water, it is immediately nullified by the water already present.

Rav Yosef reverses the framework: the debate between *kol shehu* and *revi'is* applies at the beginning, when water is added before urination. Once the urine is already collected, however, everyone agrees that *revi'is* is required. Rav Yosef acted in accordance with Rav Zakai's position — instructing his attendant to bring him a *revi'is* of water before he urinated, so he could then *daven*.

### Chamber Pots Prohibited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גְּרָף שֶׁל רְעִי וְעָבִיט שֶׁל מֵי רַגְלַיִם — אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדָּן, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן כְּלוּם. וּמֵי רַגְלַיִם עַצְמָן — עַד שֶׁיָּטִיל לְתוֹכָן מַיִם. וְכַמָּה יָטִיל לְתוֹכָן מַיִם — כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. רַבִּי זַכַּאי אוֹמֵר: רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** an elaboration of this point in the *Tosefta*: **Opposite a chamber pot** used for **excrement or urine, it is prohibited to recite *Shema*, even if there is nothing in it,** as it is always considered filthy. **Opposite urine itself,** one may not recite *Shema* **until he pours water into it. And how much water must he pour into it? Any quantity. Rabbi Zakkai says: A quarter** of a *log*.

A *braisa* rules that a *garef shel re'i* — a chamber pot used for excrement — and an *avit shel mei raglayim* — a vessel used for urine — are both forbidden in the presence of *krias shema*, even if they have been completely emptied. The reason is twofold: they are designated for that purpose, and they have absorbed smells over time. Similarly, urine itself requires dilution with water before one may recite *shema* in its presence, with the same debate between *kol shehu* and Rav Zakai's *revi'is* applying once more.

## Distance Bathrooms

### Excrement Bed Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גְּרָף שֶׁל רְעִי וְעָבִיט שֶׁל מֵי רַגְלַיִם — אָסוּר לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע כְּנֶגְדָּן, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן כְּלוּם. בֵּין לִפְנֵי הַמִּטָּה בֵּין לְאַחַר הַמִּטָּה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לְאַחַר הַמִּטָּה — קוֹרֵא, לִפְנֵי הַמִּטָּה — אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא, אֲבָל מַרְחִיק הוּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְקוֹרֵא. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בֵּית מֵאָה אַמָּה — לֹא יִקְרָא עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֵם אוֹ שֶׁיַּנִּיחֵם תַּחַת הַמִּטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** an elaboration of this point in the *Tosefta*: **Opposite a chamber pot** used for **excrement or urine, it is prohibited to recite *Shema*, even if there is nothing in it,** as it is always considered filthy. That is the ruling **both** when it is **before the bed and** when it is **behind the bed. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** When it is **behind the bed, one may recite** *Shema*, but when it is **before the bed, one may not recite** *Shema*, **but he** must **distance himself four cubits and** only then **recite** *Shema*. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar** is even more strict, **saying: Even** in **a house one hundred cubits** in size, **one may not recite** *Shema* **until he removes it or places it beneath the bed.**

The *mishnah* is now read in the context of excrement near a bed — the setting in which *shema* would often be recited. The *gemara* distinguishes between the chamber pots being *lifnei hamita*, in front of the bed (in front of the person reciting), and *leachar hamita*, behind the bed (behind the person).

### Gamliel Position

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that if the chamber pot is behind the bed, one may recite *shema* immediately; if it is in front, one may not recite. He adds that distancing oneself *arba amos* — four cubits — from the excrement suffices. The *gemara* probes whether his position is: (a) behind the bed requires no action whatsoever, while in front requires four *amos*; or (b) behind the bed requires four *amos*, while in front is entirely impermissible.

### Eliezer Room Rule

Rabbi Eliezer takes the more stringent position: even a room one hundred *amos* across does not afford sufficient distance. One may not recite *shema* in the same room as these vessels until they have either been removed from the room entirely or placed *tachat hamita* — under the bed — which functions as a form of burial.

### Braisot Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵיכִי קָאָמַר, אַחַר הַמִּטָּה — קוֹרֵא מִיָּד, לִפְנֵי הַמִּטָּה — מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְקוֹרֵא, אוֹ דִילְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר: לְאַחַר הַמִּטָּה — מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְקוֹרֵא, לִפְנֵי הַמִּטָּה — אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא כְּלָל. תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַחַר הַמִּטָּה — קוֹרֵא מִיָּד, לִפְנֵי הַמִּטָּה — מַרְחִיק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בֵּית מֵאָה אַמָּה — לֹא יִקְרָא עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֵם אוֹ שֶׁיַּנִּיחֵם תַּחַת הַמִּטָּה. בַּעְיָין אִיפְּשִׁיטָא לַן, מַתְנְיָיתָא קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי: אֵיפוֹךְ בָּתְרָיְיתָא. מָה חָזֵית דְּאָפְכַתְּ בָּתְרָיְיתָא? אֵיפוֹךְ קַמַּיְיתָא! מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר כּוּלֵּיהּ בַּיִת כְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דָּמֵי — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** A dilemma was raised: **How does** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **state** this *halakha*? What did he mean? Did he mean that if the chamber pot is **behind the bed, he recites** *Shema* **immediately; before the bed, he distances himself four cubits and recites? Or perhaps he states the following:** If the chamber pot is **behind the bed, he distances himself four cubits and then recites** *Shema,* but if it is **before the bed he may not recite** *Shema* **at all?** In order to resolve this dilemma, the Gemara cites proof. **Come** and **hear** that **it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If it is **behind the bed he recites** *Shema* **immediately; before the bed, he distances himself four cubits. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even** in **a** large **house of one hundred cubits one may not recite** *Shema* **until he removes it or places it beneath the bed.** The Gemara notes: **Our dilemma has been resolved,** but **the *baraitot* contradict each other.** The statements made in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in one *baraita* were made in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in the other. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: **Reverse** the **latter** *baraita* and say that the names of the *tanna'im* were attached to the wrong opinions. This solution is difficult: **What did you see** that led you to **reverse** the **latter** *baraita*? **Reverse the first** one. The Gemara proves that the latter *baraita* should be reversed in accordance with the opinions expressed by these Sages in general. **Who did you hear** that **said that an entire house is considered like four cubits? It is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** who expressed that opinion in the *halakhot* of *eiruv*. Consequently, it is reasonable to posit that this would also be his opinion with regard to these *halakhot*, and the *baraita* was reversed accordingly.

A second *braisa* is then cited in which the positions of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appear in reverse from the first *braisa*. The *gemara* initially wonders which *braisa* has the names recorded correctly. It determines that the second *braisa* must be the one with the transposed names, and resolves the conflict by reversing the attributions there. The reasoning: the position that the entire room is equivalent to four *amos* is independently verified as belonging to Rabbi Shimon bar Elazar, corroborating the first *braisa*'s attribution. Rashi notes that he does not know where else in *Shas* this independent verification is to be found — and indeed, Rabbi Akiva Eiger in his *Gilyon HaShas* lists approximately fifteen instances in *Shas* where Rashi writes *lo yadati heichan hi*, acknowledging that a *gemara*'s cross-reference cannot be located elsewhere.

### Room vs Outdoor

The conceptual underpinning of the *halakhah* is as follows: outdoors, a person's personal space is defined as four *amos* in any direction; beyond that, something is no longer considered to be within one's domain. Indoors, however, the entire room constitutes one unified space — *kulei beisa k'arba amos dami* — and therefore the distance of four *amos* is insufficient. One must either remove the *tzoa* from the room or place it under the bed. The final *halakhah* is in accordance with Rabbi Shimon bar Elazar. Outdoors, four *amos* suffices; indoors, the entire room is treated as though it were within four *amos*.

## Bed Height Domains

### Bed Height Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּעַאי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: מִטָּה פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה פְּשִׁיטָא לִי דִּכְלָבוּד דָּמֵי. שְׁלֹשָׁה, אַרְבָּעָה, חֲמִישָּׁה, שִׁשָּׁה, שִׁבְעָה, שְׁמֹנָה, תִּשְׁעָה, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָדַעְנָא. עֲשָׂרָה וַדַּאי לָא מִיבְּעֵי לִי. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ דְּלָא אִיבַּעְיָא לְךָ, כׇּל עֲשָׂרָה רְשׁוּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: I raised a dilemma before Rav Huna: It is obvious to me that a bed** under which there is a space of **less than three** handbreadths **is considered connected [*lavud*]** to the ground as if the void beneath it does not exist, as *halakha* considers a void of less than three handbreadths as sealed. What, then, is the dilemma? **What is** the *halakha* if that space is **three, four, five, six, seven, eight** or **nine** handbreadths? **He said to him: I do not know.** However, with regard to a space greater than **ten** handbreadths **I certainly have no dilemma,** as it is clear that this space is considered a separate domain. **Abaye said** to him: **You did well that you did not have a dilemma,** as the *halakha* is that **any** space **ten** handbreadths high **is a separate domain.**

Rav Yosef posed a question to Rav Huna regarding the height of the bed above the floor when a chamber pot is placed beneath it. Rav Huna stated that it was obvious to him that if the bed is less than three *tefachim* from the ground — approximately nine inches — the principle of *lavud* applies: the gap is so small that it is legally as though there is no gap, and the chamber pot is considered buried. Similarly, it was clear that if the bed is ten *tefachim* or more from the ground, the space beneath constitutes its own independent domain — *reshus achres* — and placing the vessel there is not considered burial at all. The question was: what about the range of three to ten *tefachim*?

### Rava Bed Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי. עֲשָׂרָה — רְשׁוּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** summarized and **said: The *halakha* is that less than three** handbreadths **is considered connected** and it is permitted to recite *Shema*. **Ten** handbreadths **is a separate domain.**

Rava rules definitively on the endpoints: less than three *tefachim* is like *lavud* and is considered burial; ten *tefachim* constitutes a separate domain and is not. These rulings are clear and not in dispute.

### Three Ten Range

The middle range — from three to ten *tefachim* — remains unresolved. This was precisely the question Rav Yosef posed to Rav Huna, and neither provided a definitive answer.

### Halacha Practice

The practical *halakhah* remains as established: within the same room, the entire room is one domain and four *amos* does not suffice; outdoors, four *amos* is the operative distance.

## Torah Tefillin Bedroom

### Bridal Chamber Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחַאי אִיעַסַּק לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ בֵּי רַב יִצְחָק בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר מָרְתָא, עַיְּילֵיהּ לְחוּפָּה, וְלָא הֲוָה מִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִילְּתָא. אֲזַל בָּתְרֵיהּ לְעַיּוֹנֵי, חֲזָא סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה דְּמַנְּחָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֵיכוּ הַשְׁתָּא לָא אֲתַאי סַכֵּנְתּוּן לִבְרִי. דְּתַנְיָא: בַּיִת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה אוֹ תְּפִילִּין אָסוּר לְשַׁמֵּשׁ בּוֹ אֶת הַמִּטָּה עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֵם אוֹ שֶׁיַּנִּיחֵם כְּלִי בְּתוֹךְ כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Aḥai arranged for his son** to marry into **the family** of **Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel bar Marta. He** led **him to enter the wedding canopy** for the wedding ceremony, **but he was unsuccessful** in his attempts to consummate the marriage. Rav Aḥai **followed him to examine** possible causes of the problem and **he saw a Torah scroll placed** there. **He said to them: Had I not come now, you would have endangered** the life of **my son. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **In a room in which there is a Torah scroll or phylacteries, it is forbidden to engage in conjugal relations until he takes them out** of the room **or places them in a vessel inside a** second **vessel.**

Rav Acha married off his son to the daughter of the house of Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel and Marta. On the wedding night, the couple was brought to the bridal chamber, but *lo havi mistayei milta* — things were not succeeding: they were unable to consummate the marriage. Rav Acha followed to investigate and discovered that a *sefer Torah* had been stored in the bridal chamber — apparently in the home of the bride's father, where a room had been set aside, the father's place of storage for the *sefer Torah*. Rav Acha told the father-in-law that had he not come to check, he would have endangered his son — both spiritually and, the *gemara* implies, perhaps even as divine intervention preventing the consummation of an act that would have been a transgression.

### Torah Double Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּכְלִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ כִּלְיָין, אֲבָל בִּכְלִי שֶׁהוּא כִּלְיָין — אֲפִילּוּ עֲשָׂרָה מָאנֵי כְּחַד מָאנָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: They only taught** that a vessel inside a second vessel is sufficient **when the vessel is not their,** the Torah scroll's or the phylacteries', regular **vessel. But a vessel that is their** regular **vessel, even ten vessels are considered as one vessel,** and the Torah or phylacteries must be covered in another vessel not typically used for that purpose.

A *braisa* establishes the underlying *halakhah*: a room that contains a *sefer Torah* or *tefillin* is forbidden for marital relations (*shimmush hamita*) until the sacred objects are either removed from the room or placed in a double vessel — *kli besoch kli*, one container inside of another.

### Non-Standard Covering

Abaye qualifies: the double vessel requirement is not satisfied if both vessels are the normal coverings of the object. At least one of the two containers must be something other than the standard pouch or wrapping in which the object is normally kept. The reasoning is that the double vessel is meant to convey that the sacred object is truly put away and not present; if one uses only the normal coverings, even stacking them ten deep, the object is still effectively "dressed" in its ordinary state and is considered present.

### Standard Coverings Fail

A *braisa* is introduced continuing from the previous passage. (The text continues in Berakhot 26a with further halakhot about partition solutions and historical narratives.)

## Excrement Direction

### Four Amot Behind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב סְחוֹרָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לַאֲחוֹרָיו, אֲבָל לְפָנָיו — מַרְחִיק מְלֹא עֵינָיו, וְכֵן לִתְפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Seḥora said** that **Rav Huna said: They only taught** that it is sufficient to distance oneself four cubits when the feces are **behind him, but** if they are **before him he must distance himself** to the point that it is **no longer within his range of vision;** and the *halakha* **is the same** for **prayer.**

Rav Sechora in the name of Rav Huna rules: *lo shanu ela l'achorav* — the rule of four *amos* applies only when the *tzoa* is behind you. Four *amos* of distance to the rear is sufficient to permit recitation of *shema*.

### Field of Vision

When the *tzoa* is in front of you, a different standard applies: *marchik melo einav* — one must distance oneself until it is entirely out of one's field of vision. The same principle applies, and with even greater stringency, to *shemoneh esrei*.

### Bathroom Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: עוֹמֵד אָדָם כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא וּמִתְפַּלֵּל! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ צוֹאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **Is that so? Didn't Rafram bar Pappa say** that **Rav Ḥisda said: One may stand opposite a bathroom and pray.** The Gemara resolves this contradiction: **With what are we dealing here? With a bathroom that has no feces,** and therefore there is no need to distance himself to that extent.

The *gemara* cites Rav Afram bar Pappa in the name of Rav Chisda: a person may stand in front of a *beit hakisei* — a bathroom — and *daven*, apparently even though the bathroom would be within one's field of vision. How is this consistent with the rule just stated?

### Unused Bathroom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חֲנִינָא: בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא שֶׁאָמְרוּ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ צוֹאָה וּבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ שֶׁאָמְרוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אָדָם! אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּחַדְתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks again: **Is that so? Didn't Rav Yosef bar Ḥanina say: The bathroom to which** the Sages **referred** in all of the *halakhot* of distancing oneself was **even** one **in which there were no feces, and the bathhouse to which** the Sages **referred** in all of the *halakhot* of uttering sacred matters, was **even** one **in which there was no** naked **person. Rather, with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with a new** structure, built as a bathroom but not yet used for that purpose.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this resolution as well, citing Rav Yosef: did we not already learn that a *beit hakisei* is problematic even if it contains no *tzoa* at present, because of its designated function and absorbed smells?

### Designated Unused

The *gemara* refines the position: the case of Rav Chisda must refer to a bathroom that has not merely been emptied but has never been used at all. A bathroom that has been used acquires a permanent status regardless of its current cleanliness. One that has merely been designated but never used remains ambiguous. On that precise point, Ravina raises a question — *hizmino l'veit hakisei mahu*, if a space has been designated as a bathroom but not yet used, does designation alone confer the prohibited status? The *gemara* further clarifies that Ravina's question pertains specifically to whether one may *daven* on the very spot designated for the bathroom, not merely in front of it. If one is simply facing the designated area from the outside, that is presumably fine even if the designation matters for standing on the spot. The question remains unresolved.

## Persian Bathrooms

### Sealed Design

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי בָּתֵּי כִּסָּאֵי דְּפָרְסָאֵי אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית בְּהוּ צוֹאָה — כִּסְתוּמִין דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: These Persian bathrooms, even though they contain feces, they are considered as sealed,** as they are constructed on an incline so the feces will roll out of the bathroom underground.

Rava introduces a ruling of immediate practical relevance: *hani bei kissai deParsai*, the Persian-style bathrooms — even though excrement is present — are considered *kistumim damu*, as though they are sealed. Rashi explains: the Persian design featured an opening set at an angle, *veshpua*, such that waste would roll along the slope and fall into a pit below — not directly beneath where the user was seated. As a result, there were no lingering smells in the user's immediate space.

### Modern Toilets

This ruling has direct application to contemporary bathrooms. Modern flush toilets operate on an essentially analogous principle: waste and odor are immediately removed by the flushing mechanism, leaving no residue in the immediate space. On this basis, poskim have ruled that ordinary household bathrooms do not carry the halakhic status of *beit hakisei* as discussed in the *gemara*.

### Public Restrooms

The exception, however, is public restrooms. Due to the volume of use and the persistent smells that accumulate in such spaces, they do retain a problematic status and cannot be treated as equivalent to the Persian-style design.

## Women Seminal Issue

### Zav Niddah Keri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זָב שֶׁרָאָה קֶרִי, וְנִדָּה שֶׁפָּלְטָה שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, וְהַמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת שֶׁרָאֲתָה נִדָּה — צְרִיכִין טְבִילָה. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Continuing the earlier discussion of the *halakhot* of immersion for Torah study and prayer for one who experienced a seminal emission, the mishna discusses a case where individuals who were already impure with a severe form of ritual impurity are exposed to the impurity of a seminal emission as well. They are required to immerse themselves and purify themselves of the impurity of the seminal emission even though they remain impure due to the more severe impurity. Consequently, even a ***zav*,** whose impurity lasts at least seven days, **who experienced a seminal emission,** for which, were he not a *zav*, he would be impure for only one day; **a menstruating woman who discharged semen,** despite the fact that she is already impure with a severe impurity unaffected by her immersion; **and** a woman **who engaged in conjugal relations** with her husband **and** later **saw menstrual blood,** all **require immersion. And Rabbi Yehuda exempts** them from immersion.

A new *mishnah* is introduced, serving to broaden the earlier discussion of *ba'al keri* to include women and those already in a state of *tumah*. The *mishnah* addresses: a *zav* — a man with an irregular bodily discharge — who then has a seminal emission; a *niddah* who expels *zera* — a woman who had intercourse before becoming a *niddah* and who now expels the semen after her period has begun; and *hamishameshet sheratah niddah* — a woman who had intercourse and then became a *niddah* after the *zera* was expelled. All of these cases require *tevilah* — immersion — not to address the other *tumah* (which the *mikvah* will not resolve), but specifically in order to be permitted to *daven* and recite *shema*, which is the ongoing concern of the *ba'al keri* legislation. Rabbi Yehuda exempts: once a person is already in a state of *tumah* from which *tevilah* would be ineffective, there is no purpose in immersing for the *keri* aspect either.

### Ba'al Keri Zav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּעַל קֶרִי, שֶׁרָאָה זִיבָה לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַהוּ? כִּי פָּטַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָתָם בְּזָב שֶׁרָאָה קֶרִי, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָאו בַּר טְבִילָה הוּא. אֲבָל בַּעַל קֶרִי שֶׁרָאָה זִיבָה, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא בַּר טְבִילָה הוּא מְחַיַּיב, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the students of the yeshiva: **One who experienced a seminal emission** and was therefore required to immerse himself, who later **saw a discharge** that rendered him a ***zav*; according to Rabbi Yehuda, what is his legal status?** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: **When,** in our mishna, **Rabbi Yehuda exempted a *zav* who saw a seminal emission** from immersion, that **was because from the outset he was not fit for immersion,** as the immersion would not be effective in purifying him from the impurity of a *zav*; **however, one who experienced a seminal emission,** who later **saw a discharge** that rendered him a ***zav*,** who **was fit for immersion** and only later became impure with the severe impurity of a *zav*, would Rabbi Yehuda **require** immersion? **Or perhaps there is no difference** and he is exempt from immersion in both cases?

The *gemara* raises a question regarding the order of events. When does Rabbi Yehuda's exemption apply? The *mishnah*'s first case — a *zav* who then saw *keri* — seems clear: the person was already a *zav* when the seminal emission occurred, and *tevilah* would not have helped from the outset. But what about the reverse case: a *ba'al keri* who subsequently becomes a *zav*? At the time of the seminal emission, he was indeed obligated to go to the *mikvah*. Does that original obligation persist even after he acquires *zav* status, or does the new *tumah* render the *tevilah* pointless and therefore exempt him?

### Woman Intercourse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע הַמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת וְרָאֲתָה נִדָּה צְרִיכָה טְבִילָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר. וְהָא מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת וְרָאֲתָה נִדָּה כְּבַעַל קֶרִי שֶׁרָאָה זִיבָה דָּמְיָא, וְקָא פָּטַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּהֶדְיָא: בַּעַל קֶרִי שֶׁרָאָה זִיבָה צָרִיךְ טְבִילָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** In order to resolve this dilemma, **come** and **hear** the last case of the mishna: A woman **who engaged in conjugal relations** with her husband **and** later **saw menstrual blood requires immersion. And Rabbi Yehuda exempts** them from immersion. **Isn't the** woman **who engaged in conjugal relations** with her husband **and** later **saw menstrual blood like one who experienced a seminal emission,** who later **saw a discharge** that rendered him a ***zav*,** as in both cases there is a less severe ritual impurity followed by a more severe impurity; **and** nevertheless, **Rabbi Yehuda exempts. Conclude from this** that Rabbi Yehuda does not distinguish between the cases. And indeed, **Rabbi Ḥiyya explicitly taught: One who experienced a seminal emission** who later **saw a discharge** that rendered him a ***zav* requires immersion, and Rabbi Yehuda exempts.**

The *gemara* finds the answer through the parallel case of the woman: *hamishameshet sheratah niddah* — the woman who had intercourse and then became a *niddah*. In this case, the *zera* was expelled first (while she was still permitted) and she became a *niddah* only afterward. This is directly analogous to a *ba'al keri* who subsequently becomes a *zav*: the *keri* / *zera* event came first, the disqualifying *tumah* came second. The *mishnah* states that she requires *tevilah*, and Rabbi Yehuda exempts here too.

### Rabbi Chiyya Explicit

Rabbi Chiyya taught this rule explicitly: *ba'al keri shera'ah zivah tzarich tevilah* — if a man was first a *ba'al keri* and subsequently became a *zav*, he requires immersion according to the *Chachamim*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Exempt

Rabbi Yehuda exempts across the board: once a person carries the status of *zav*, immersion cannot help for that *tumah*, and therefore he exempts the *ba'al keri* dimension as well, regardless of which status came first. The proof from the woman's case confirms that Rabbi Yehuda's exemption is not contingent on the order of events.

## Transition Shema

### Three Chapters End

With this *mishnah* and its *gemara*, the third *perek* of *Berakhot* concludes. The first three *perakim* of the *masechta* have addressed *krias shema* from every angle: its times, its recitation, the conditions of bodily purity required, the spatial requirements regarding *tzoa*, *ervah*, and smells, and the edge cases created by overlapping states of *tumah*.

### Fourth Chapter Start

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּפִלַּת הַשַּׁחַר עַד חֲצוֹת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד אַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת. תְּפִלַּת הַמִּנְחָה עַד הָעֶרֶב, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד פְּלַג הַמִּנְחָה. תְּפִלַּת הָעֶרֶב אֵין לָהּ קֶבַע. וְשֶׁל מוּסָפִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁבַע שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna determines the times beyond which the different prayers may not be recited. According to the Rabbis, **the morning prayer** may be recited **until noon. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It may be recited only **until four hours** after sunrise. According to the Rabbis, **the afternoon prayer** may be recited **until the evening. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It may be recited only **until the midpoint of the afternoon [*pelag haminḥa*],** i.e., the midpoint of the period that begins with the sacrifice of the daily afternoon offering and ends at nightfall, which is the end of the afternoon. **The evening prayer** may be recited throughout the night and **is not fixed** to a specific hour. According to the Rabbis, **the additional prayer** may be recited **all day. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It may be recited only **until seven hours** after sunrise.

The fourth *perek* now begins. It shifts the focus from *shema* to *shemoneh esrei* — from the recitation of the *Torah*-text that constitutes *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim* to the *amidah* that is the core act of Jewish prayer. The next several *dapim* will be devoted to the times, conditions, and conceptual foundations of *tefillah*.

## Prayer Times

### Shacharit Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: מִצְוָתָהּ עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּסְמוֹךְ גְּאוּלָּה לִתְפִלָּה, וְנִמְצָא מִתְפַּלֵּל בַּיּוֹם! כִּי תַּנְיָא הַהִיא לְוָתִיקִין. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וָתִיקִין הָיוּ גּוֹמְרִים אוֹתָהּ עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that the morning prayer may be recited only until a few hours into the day. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** based on what was taught in a *baraita*: **The mitzva** is to recite the morning *Shema* **with sunrise** so that **he will juxtapose redemption,** which is mentioned in the blessings following *Shema*, **to** the *Amida* **prayer,** which is recited immediately after sunrise, and **find** himself **praying in the daytime.** Clearly, the time to recite the morning prayer is immediately after sunrise. The Gemara responds: This *baraita* does not establish a binding *halakha*. Rather, it **taught that** rule specifically **with regard to those who are scrupulous in fulfillment of mitzvot [*vatikin*]. As Rabbi Yoḥanan said: *Vatikin* would finish** reciting the morning *Shema* **with sunrise,** but those who are not *vatikin* may recite their prayers later.

The opening *mishnah* of the fourth *perek* establishes the times for each of the prayers. *Tefilat hashachar* — the morning prayer, *Shacharit* — may be recited until midday. Rabbi Yehuda says it extends only until four hours into the day. Rabbi Yehuda operates from a *korban* model: the morning communal *tamid* was offered only until four hours, and therefore *tefillah*, which corresponds to the *tamid*, should likewise conclude by that time.

### Mincha Times

*Tefilat hamincha* — the afternoon prayer — may be recited until nightfall. Rabbi Yehuda says only until *plag hamincha*, which works out to approximately one hour and a quarter before nightfall. Again, Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning follows the *korban* model: the afternoon *tamid* was not offered all the way until nightfall but was concluded earlier.

### Maariv Musaf

The evening prayer — *tefillat ha'erev* — has *ein lah keva*, no fixed limit, and may be recited throughout the night. *Musaf* may be recited *kol hayom*, the entire day. Rabbi Yehuda restricts *Musaf* to until seven hours into the day — one hour past noon. Tosafot notes a linguistic distinction: for *Mincha* the *mishnah* says *ad ha'erev*, "until evening," while for *Musaf* it says *kol hayom*, "the whole day." The difference is that *Musaf* may genuinely be recited at any point during the day — even in the morning, before *Mincha* — and therefore the phrasing "all day" is more apt.

## Vatikin Ideal

### Sunrise Mitzvah

The *gemara* on the fourth *perek* opens with a tension: a *braisa* states that the *mitzvah* of *tefillah* is specifically at *hanetz hachamah*, sunrise — *mitzvatah im hanetz hachamah* — so that one may juxtapose *geulah* to *tefillah* and begin *davening* at the very start of the day. This seems to imply that the ideal time, or even the required time, for *shacharit* is sunrise, not the entire morning until noon.

### Ideal vs Halacha

The *gemara* resolves this: *ki tanya hahi l'Vatikin* — that *braisa* describes the practice of the *vatikin*, the scrupulously pious. It is an ideal, not a *halakhic* limit. The operative *halakhah* is that *shacharit* is valid until noon.

### Vatikin Timing

Rabbi Yochanan articulates the *vatikin* practice precisely: *vatikin hayu gomrim otah im hanetz hachamah* — the *vatikin* would complete *shema* exactly at the moment of sunrise, so that they could move directly into *shemoneh esrei* at that precise moment. The thrust of the *vatikin* ideal is thus to place *shemoneh esrei* — not *shema* — at sunrise. *Shema* is completed just before sunrise so that *shemoneh esrei* follows immediately at *hanetz*.

## Makeup Prayer

### Missed Maariv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עַד חֲצוֹת וְתוּ לָא, וְהָאָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: טָעָה וְלֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל עַרְבִית — מִתְפַּלֵּל בְּשַׁחֲרִית שְׁתַּיִם, שַׁחֲרִית — מִתְפַּלֵּל בְּמִנְחָה שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does everyone** hold that one may recite the morning prayer only **until noon and no later? Didn't Rav Mari, son of Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba, say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who erred and did not recite the evening prayer, prays in the morning prayer two** *Amida* prayers; one who erred and did not recite **the morning prayer, prays in the afternoon prayer two** *Amida* prayers? Apparently, the morning prayer may be recited until the evening, at least in the event that he forgot to recite it in the morning.

Rabbi Yochanan is cited to the effect that if one forgot and failed to *daven Ma'ariv*, one may recite two *shemoneh esrei* the following morning — one for *Shacharit* and one as a makeup for the missed *Ma'ariv*. Similarly, if one missed *Shacharit*, one may recite two *shemoneh esrei* at *Mincha*. The *gemara* is thus introducing the concept of *tashlumin* — makeup prayer — which has no analogue elsewhere in *halakhah*. One does not, for instance, take the *lulav* twice the next day if one missed waving it; yet for *tefillah* such a makeup is apparently available.

### Noon Cutoff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא מְצַלֵּי וְאָזֵיל, עַד חֲצוֹת, יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ שְׂכַר תְּפִלָּה בִּזְמַנָּהּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, שְׂכַר תְּפִלָּה — יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ, שְׂכַר תְּפִלָּה בִּזְמַנָּהּ — לָא יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Indeed, **one may continue praying for the entire day.** However, if he prayed **until noon, they give him a reward for** reciting the **prayer at its** appointed **time.** If he prayed **from there on, they give him a reward for** reciting the **prayer. They do not give him a reward for** reciting the **prayer at its** appointed **time.**

The *gemara* refines the application: up until midday, one who prays receives *s'char tefilla bizmana* — the credit for having prayed in the proper time. After midday, one still receives the credit for *tefillah* itself but loses the additional merit of *tefilla bizmana* — prayer at the right time. This framing initially suggests that *davening* past noon is not a formal "makeup" but simply a late fulfillment, still within the extended period of the morning prayer. The *gemara* will move toward a more structured makeup model.

### Mincha Makeup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: טָעָה וְלֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל מִנְחָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּתְפַּלֵּל עַרְבִית שְׁתַּיִם? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר, טָעָה וְלֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל עַרְבִית מִתְפַּלֵּל שַׁחֲרִית שְׁתַּיִם, מִשּׁוּם דְּחַד יוֹמָא הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר יוֹם אֶחָד״, אֲבָל הָכָא תְּפִלָּה בִּמְקוֹם קׇרְבָּן הִיא, וְכֵיוָן דְּעָבַר יוֹמוֹ בָּטַל קׇרְבָּנוֹ. אוֹ דִילְמָא כֵּיוָן דִּצְלוֹתָא רַחֲמֵי הִיא, כׇּל אֵימַת דְּבָעֵי מְצַלֵּי וְאָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of one who forgot to pray and seeks to compensate for the prayer that he missed, **a dilemma was raised before them** in the study hall: **One who erred and did not recite the afternoon prayer, what is** the ruling? May he **recite** in **the evening prayer two** *Amida* prayers? The Gemara articulates the sides of the dilemma: **If you say** that **one who erred and did not pray the evening prayer prays in the morning prayer two** *Amida* prayers, perhaps that is **because** the evening and the morning are both part of **one day, as it is written: "And there was evening and there was morning, one day"** (Genesis 1:5); the evening and the following morning constitute a single unit. **But here,** in the case under discussion, perhaps **prayer is in place of sacrifice.** Since in the case of sacrifice we say, **since its day passed, its sacrifice is invalid** and there is no way to compensate for the missed opportunity, the same should be true for prayer. **Or, perhaps, since prayer is supplication, any time that one wishes, he may continue to pray?**

The *gemara* raises the case not addressed among those discussed: if one missed *Mincha*, can one recite two *shemoneh esrei* at *Ma'ariv* — one for *Ma'ariv* and one as a makeup for the missed *Mincha*?

### Korban vs Rachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: טָעָה וְלֹא הִתְפַּלֵּל מִנְחָה — מִתְפַּלֵּל עַרְבִית שְׁתַּיִם, וְאֵין בָּזֶה מִשּׁוּם דְּעָבַר יוֹמוֹ בָּטַל קׇרְבָּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come and hear** a resolution to this dilemma from that **which Rav Huna bar Yehuda said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who erred and did not recite the afternoon prayer, prays in the evening prayer two** *Amida* prayers **and there is no element of: Its day passed, its sacrifice is invalid.**

This question crystallizes the central theological tension of the entire *perek*. If *tefillah* operates on the model of *korban*, then missing the time is decisive in one direction but not another. Sacrifices offered on the same halakhic day can be made up later in that day — which explains why a missed *Ma'ariv* can be compensated the next morning, since *erev* and *boker* constitute *yom echad*, one halakhic day, as the verse states *vayehi erev vayehi boker yom echad*. But a *korban* from one day cannot be brought the next day: *avar yomo batel korbano*. If *Mincha* belongs to the afternoon and *Ma'ariv* belongs to the following halakhic day, the *korban* model would prohibit any makeup.

The alternative: *kivan detzloota rachamei hi* — since prayer is fundamentally about *rachamim*, about the unrestricted pouring out of the heart to God, one might argue that *kol eima detzabei matzli* — one may pray at any time — and the entire framework of time limits and makeups is secondary to the essential nature of prayer as human communication with the divine.

### Scripture Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי. ״מְעֻוָּת לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקֹן וְחֶסְרוֹן לֹא יוּכַל לְהִמָּנוֹת״: ״מְעֻוָּת לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקֹן״ — זֶה שֶׁבִּטֵּל קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שֶׁל עַרְבִית וּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שֶׁל שַׁחֲרִית, אוֹ תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית אוֹ תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל שַׁחֲרִית. ״וְחֶסְרוֹן לֹא יוּכַל לְהִמָּנוֹת״ — זֶה שֶׁנִּמְנוּ חֲבֵירָיו לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, וְלֹא נִמְנָה עִמָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the possibility to compensate for a prayer that he failed to recite at its appointed time, the Gemara **raises an objection** based on what was taught in a *baraita*. The meaning of the verse: **"That which is crooked cannot be made straight; and that which is wanting cannot be numbered"** (Ecclesiastes 1:15), is as follows: **That which is crooked cannot be made straight** refers to **one who omitted the evening *Shema* and the morning *Shema,* or the evening prayer, or the morning prayer. And that which is wanting cannot be numbered [*lehimanot*]** refers to **one whose friends reached a consensus [*nimnu*]** to perform **a mitzva and he was not part of their consensus [*nimnu*]** and, consequently, he missed his opportunity to join them in performance of the mitzva. This *baraita* clearly states that there is no way to compensate for a missed prayer.

A *braisa* is cited that appears to deny any makeup for *tefillah*: *me'uvat lo yuchal litkon* — that which is perverted cannot be straightened; *vecheisaron lo yuchal lehimanot* — and that which is lacking cannot be counted. The *braisa* applies the first half of the verse to one who missed *krias shema* of the evening or morning, or who missed *Ma'ariv* or *Shacharit* — these cannot be remedied. The second half is applied to one who was absent when a group gathered for a *mitzvah*; that absence cannot be retrospectively included. This appears to categorically reject the possibility of makeup prayer.

### Willful Neglect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — שֶׁבִּטֵּל בְּמֵזִיד. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״בִּטֵּל״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״טָעָה״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** To resolve this difficulty, **Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: With what are we dealing here** in this *baraita*? We are dealing with a case where one **intentionally failed** to recite the prayer. Only then he has no remedy. However, one who failed to pray due to error can compensate for the missed prayer by reciting the next prayer twice. **Rav Ashi said:** The language of the *baraita* **is also precise as it teaches omitted and did not teach erred.** This indicates that the *halakha* is different in the case of error. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this.**

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the contradiction: the *braisa*'s prohibition applies specifically to one who *bittlo bemeizid* — who willfully neglected the prayer. Rav Ashi adds that the *braisa*'s own language is precise: it uses the word *bittul*, annulment or negation, rather than *ta'ah*, erring inadvertently — *shema mina*, this itself demonstrates the distinction. One who deliberately skips *tefillah* forfeits any makeup. One who genuinely forgot or erred may make it up in the following *tefillah*.

### Dialectic Resolution

The *halakhah* of makeup prayer thus embodies a genuine synthesis of the two models. Pure *korban* logic would permit no makeup across days and no flexibility whatsoever. Pure *rachamim* logic would impose no time limits at all and render the concept of "making up" meaningless. The actual *halakhah* lives in the tension between them. *Tefillah* carries the formal structure of *korbanot*: fixed times, the possibility of losing the merit of *tefilla bizmana*, and the impossibility of making up a willfully missed prayer. But the *rachamei* foundation of prayer — its nature as the unrestricted cry of the human heart to God — provides the halakhic basis for makeup prayer when a genuine inadvertent error has occurred, even across the boundary between one prayer time and the next. The times matter, but they do not utterly exhaust the meaning of *tefillah*.
