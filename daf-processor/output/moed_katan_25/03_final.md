# Moed Katan 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shavuos & Regel

### Shavuos Post-Churban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי עֲנָנִי בַּר שָׂשׂוֹן אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה: יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי עֲצֶרֶת וַעֲצֶרֶת הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר. שְׁמַע רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְאִיקְּפַד, אֲמַר: אַטּוּ דִּידֵיהּ הִיא? דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Anani bar Sason taught at the entrance to the house of the *Nasi*:** If one suffered the loss of a close relative shortly before the festival of *Shavuot*, his **one day** of mourning **before *Shavuot* and *Shavuot*** itself together count as **fourteen** days. The day before the Festival counts as seven full days of mourning because the Festival interrupts the seven-day period of mourning, and the Festival itself is counted as a seven-day Festival. Even though it is a one-day Festival, it is counted as seven days because it has the same *halakha* as the other Festivals. After *Shavuot*, then, only sixteen days are left to complete the thirty-day mourning period. **Rabbi Ami heard** this **and became angry** with him. **He said: Is that to say** that it is **his own** opinion? **It is** the opinion **that Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Oshaya** said.

The daf begins in the middle of 24b, resuming the discussion of how a *regel* interrupts *avelus*. The preceding material had treated the sensitive distinctions between individual mourning obligations and the more public, communal dimensions of mourning — obligations that only attach once a person has been recognized as a member of the community. We now return to the question of whether *Shavuos*, post-*Churban*, retains the status of a *regel* for purposes of interrupting *shiva* and *shloshim*. The broader ruling is that *Shavuos*, *Rosh Hashanah*, and *Yom Kippur* are all treated as *regalim* capable of being *mafsik* the *avelus*.

### Rav Annani Darshan

Three lines into the wide-margin text, the Gemara records: *darash Rav Annani bar Sasson a-pitcha de-vei Nasi* — Rav Annani bar Sasson expounded the following ruling on the doorstep of the *Beis HaNasi*: *yom echad lifnei Atzeres ve-Atzeres harei kan arba asar* — one day before *Shavuos* and *Shavuos* itself count as fourteen days. The logic proceeds as follows: when *Shavuos* arrives, the *shiva* ends. Then the *chag* of *Shavuos*, as a *regel*, is *oleh* for the *shloshim*. The striking *halacha* here is that *Shavuos*, by virtue of the concept of *tashlumin* and the broader idea of a *regel* carrying the weight of seven, counts as seven days — even though only one day of *Yom Tov* was actually observed. The person emerging from this sequence therefore has only sixteen days of *shloshim* observance remaining.

### Ami & Sheshes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַשׁ רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אַקִּילְעָא דְּרֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא: יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי עֲצֶרֶת וַעֲצֶרֶת — הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר. שְׁמַע רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אִיקְּפַד, אָמַר: אַטּוּ דִּידֵיהּ הִיא? דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** Similarly **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa taught in the pavilion [*kila*] of the Exilarch:** If one's relative died shortly before the festival of *Shavuot*, his **one day** of mourning **before *Shavuot* and *Shavuot*** itself count as **fourteen** days. **Rav Sheshet heard and became angry.** He **said: Is that to say** that it is **his own** opinion? **It is** the opinion **that Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Oshaya** said.

*Shama Rabbi Ami ve-ikpid* — Rabbi Ami heard and was upset. His objection was not that the *halacha* was incorrect, but that it was being presented as an independent teaching. *Atu didei?* — Is this your own ruling? *Rabbi Elazar amar Rabbi Oshaya!* — it belongs to Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya. The same scene then repeats: *darash Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha a-pitcha de-kila d'Reish Galusa* — Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha expounded the identical ruling in the pavilion of the Reish Galusa — *yom echad lifnei Atzeres ve-Atzeres harei kan arba asar* — and Rav Sheshes likewise heard and was likewise upset, making the same attribution: *Rabbi Elazar amar Rabbi Oshaya*.

### Shavuos Tashlumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מִנַּיִן לָעֲצֶרֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ תַּשְׁלוּמִין כׇּל שִׁבְעָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּחַג הַמַּצּוֹת וּבְחַג הַשָּׁבוּעוֹת״, מָה חַג הַמַּצּוֹת יֵשׁ לָהּ תַּשְׁלוּמִין כׇּל שִׁבְעָה — אַף חַג הַשָּׁבוּעוֹת יֵשׁ לָהּ תַּשְׁלוּמִין כׇּל שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** In both of these cases, a Sage presented an opinion as their own, without attributing it to the original Sage who stated it, **for Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Oshaya said: From where** is it derived **that the *Shavuot*** offerings **have redress all seven** subsequent **days?** That is to say, if someone failed to bring the Festival offering on the Festival itself, he has six more days to bring it. **As it is stated:** "Three times a year shall all your males appear before the Lord your God in the place that He shall choose; **on the festival of Passover, and on the festival of *Shavuot*,** and on the festival of *Sukkot*" (Deuteronomy 16:16). **Just as** the offerings of **the festival of Passover have redress all seven** days, as Passover is seven days long, **so too** the offerings of **the festival of *Shavuot* have redress all seven** days, during the week following *Shavuot*. From this we learn that *Shavuot* is treated like a seven-day Festival for all halakhic purposes.

The underlying source attributed to Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya is now cited: *minayin she-Shavuos yesh lah tashlumin kol shiva?* — from where do we derive that *Shavuos* has *tashlumin* for all seven days? *She-ne'emar: be-chag ha-matzos u-ve-chag ha-Shavuos u-ve-chag ha-Sukkos* — just as *Pesach* is a seven-day festival during which one may make up missed *korbanos* for all seven days, so too *Shavuos* has *tashlumin kol shiva*. The six days following *Shavuos* have a special halakhic status as days within which a *korban chagiga* may be brought in compensation.

What is not immediately obvious, and what invites closer scrutiny, is the derivational connection between this teaching and the *halacha* of *avelus*. The teaching of Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya establishes *tashlumin* for *Shavuos* in the context of *korbanos*, not mourning. That one day of *Shavuos* should carry the weight of seven days for purposes of *shloshim* counting is not a direct derivation from this source. At most, it seems, the later ruling is implicit in or derivative of the earlier one. This makes the indignation of Rabbi Ami and Rav Sheshes somewhat puzzling: if the connection is derivative rather than explicit, why is it so important to attribute it to Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya? One possible answer is that one should at minimum have acknowledged the source upon which the ruling rests; another is that perhaps Rabbi Elazar in fact stated both teachings and only the underlying *halacha* has been quoted here. The matter remains unresolved.

### Rosh Hashanah Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְבְּרֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרַב אַוְיָא סָבָא וּדְרַשׁ: יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה — הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר. אָמַר רָבִינָא, הִלְכָּךְ: יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי הֶחָג, וְחַג, וּשְׁמִינִי שֶׁלּוֹ — הֲרֵי כָּאן עֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa granted permission to Rav Avya the Elder** to speak **and he taught** the following in public: If one's relative died shortly before Rosh HaShana, his **one day** of mourning **before Rosh HaShana and Rosh HaShana** itself together count as **fourteen** days, because Rosh HaShana is treated like a seven-day Festival. **Ravina said: Therefore,** if one's relative died shortly before *Sukkot*, his **one day** of mourning **before the Festival** of *Sukkot*, **and the Festival** of *Sukkot* itself, which is seven days, **and its Eighth** day of Assembly, which is considered to be a separate Festival, count as **twenty-one** days, and he must observe only nine more days of mourning to complete the thirty-day period of mourning.

*Ata Rav Pappa legabi Avia Sava ve-darash* — Rav Pappa brought along Rav Avia Sava (who presumably served as his *maggid* to announce the teaching) and expounded: *yom echad lifnei Rosh Hashanah ve-Rosh Hashanah harei kan arba'ah asar* — one day before *Rosh Hashanah* and *Rosh Hashanah* together count as fourteen days. This is more striking than the ruling regarding *Shavuos*, because *Rosh Hashanah* has no concept of *tashlumin kol shiva*. *Rosh Hashanah* is *yoma arichta*, a single long day — but even granting that, it does not by itself produce the quantity of seven days.

### Succos & Shemini

Ravina then extends the logic to its furthest application: *hilkach yom echad lifnei ha-chag ve-chag u-shemini shelo harei kan esrim ve-echad yom* — one day before *Sukkos*, the seven days of *Sukkos*, and *Shemini Atzeres* together account for twenty-one days, leaving only nine days of *shloshim* to be observed thereafter. The accounting runs as follows: one day before *Sukkos* terminates the *shiva*, removing seven days from the *shloshim*; the seven days of *Sukkos* itself, as a *regel* that is *oleh* for the *shloshim*, accounts for another seven; and then *Shemini Atzeres*, as a single-day *Yom Tov* that — on the principle being discussed — counts as seven days toward the *shloshim*, contributes the final seven. Total: twenty-one days, with nine days of *shloshim* remaining.

### Chaviva Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אִיקְּלַע לְסוּרָא דִפְרָת, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חֲבִיבָא מִסּוּרָא דִפְרָת לְרָבִינָא: אָמַר מָר, יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה — הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא, מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הוּא דְּאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **Ravina happened** to come **to** the city of **Sura on the Euphrates** River. **Rav Ḥaviva from Sura on the Euphrates said to Ravina:** Did the **Master say** that **one day** of mourning **before Rosh HaShana and Rosh HaShana** itself count **as fourteen** days? **He said to him: I said that it stands to reason** that this is so **according to Rabban Gamliel,** who maintains that Rosh HaShana is treated like a pilgrim Festival. But I did not rule in accordance with this opinion.

Rav Chaviva from Sura by the Euphrates raised a challenge to Ravina, citing the teaching that one day before *Rosh Hashanah* and *Rosh Hashanah* itself amount to fourteen days — meaning that a single-day *Yom Tov* counts as seven days. This is, as he implies, a striking and counterintuitive position. Ravina responded: *ana mistabra ke-Rabban Gamliel hu de-amina* — I was quoted imprecisely. What I said was that it stands to reason according to Rabban Gamliel that *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur* are *mafsik* the *avelus* — I never stated the further ruling that *Rosh Hashanah* itself counts as a full seven days.

### Tosafos Analysis

Tosafos raises the pointed question of what practical purpose this ruling regarding *Rosh Hashanah* serves. The *halacha* that *Shavuos* counts as fourteen days, or that *Sukkos* together with *Shemini Atzeres* accounts for twenty-one days — these rulings have genuine practical consequences. But who needs the ruling that *Rosh Hashanah* accounts for fourteen days? Ten days after *Rosh Hashanah* comes *Yom Kippur*, which will in any case terminate the *shloshim*. Whatever additional days *Rosh Hashanah* might subtract from the *shloshim* are therefore irrelevant. Similarly, a parallel ruling regarding *Yom Kippur* would be rendered moot by the arrival of *Sukkos*. Tosafos labors to find a resolution but reaches no clearly satisfying answer. Indeed, examining the *Shulchan Aruch* confirms that no practical *halacha* is recorded to the effect that *Rosh Hashanah* counts as fourteen days. The *Shulchan Aruch* states simply that if one observes a bit of *avelus* before *Rosh Hashanah*, *Rosh Hashanah* ends the *shiva* and *Yom Kippur* ends the *shloshim*; or if one observes a bit before *Yom Kippur*, *Yom Kippur* ends the *shiva* and *Sukkos* ends the *shloshim*. The strange *halacha* of a non-*regel* counting as seven days is thus effectively moot in practice — which leaves open the question of why the Gemara bothered to state it at all.

## Keriah & Relatives

### Mishnah Ein Korin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין קוֹרְעִין וְלֹא חוֹלְצִין וְאֵין מַבְרִין אֶלָּא קְרוֹבָיו שֶׁל מֵת, וְאֵין מַבְרִין אֶלָּא עַל מִטָּה זְקוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Mourners **do not rend** their garments during the intermediate days of a Festival **and do not remove** their garments from their shoulders. **And** others **do not provide them with a meal [*mavrin*]** after the burial, **except for close relatives of the deceased. And** the consolers **provide the** first **meal** after the burial **only** while the mourner is sitting **on an upright bed,** and not on one that is overturned.

The Gemara now arrives at the next *Mishnah*. It is worth pausing to orient ourselves: the entire foregoing discussion of *avelus* — which has occupied a full week of study — has been an explication of a single *mishnah* about *regalim* and *Shabbos* being *mafsik*. The tractate is, of course, *Moed Katan*, whose primary subject is *Chol HaMoed*, and we now reach one of the final two *mishnayot* in the chapter.

The *Mishnah* states: *ein korin v'lo choltzin* — one does not perform *keriah* (the tearing of the garment upon hearing of a death) and does not perform *chalitzah* — here meaning not the *chalitzah* of *Hilchos Yevamos* but the uncovering of the shoulder in mourning, as we have seen discussed previously. *V'ein mavrin* — and one does not serve the *seudat havra'ah*, the meal provided to the mourner immediately after returning from the cemetery, which halachically must come from others' food rather than the *avel*'s own. *Ela kerovo shel meis* — except for the immediate relatives of the deceased.

The question this immediately raises is: why does the *Mishnah* need to specify these particular practices? If it means to say simply that one does not observe *shiva* or *shloshim* except as a relative, why single out *keriah*, *chalitzah*, and *seudat havra'ah*? The answer, on a straightforward *peshat* reading, is that these three practices — unlike *shiva* and *shloshim*, which are multi-day ongoing observances — are immediate expressions of grief: they are performed at the moment of tragedy, not sustained over days. There was evidently a practice for non-relatives to perform them upon the death of great sages or community leaders, as we will shortly see confirmed by a *braita*. The *Mishnah* therefore needed to address this phenomenon explicitly and restrict these practices to relatives.

### General vs. Chol Hamoed

However, the question of what the *Mishnah* is actually addressing is complicated by a second, very different way of reading it. The *Yerushalmi*, followed by most *Rishonim*, understands the *Mishnah* as speaking specifically about *Chol HaMoed*. On this reading, the *Mishnah* is not making a general statement about *avelus* throughout the year; it is specifying what practices are permitted during *Chol HaMoed* for the immediate relatives of someone who died during that period. We have already established that the full observance of *shiva* does not apply during *Chol HaMoed*; but these immediate expressions — *keriah*, *chalitzah*, and *seudat havra'ah* — would still be performed by the *krovim* even on *Chol HaMoed*, whereas no one else would observe them. This is a perfectly natural reading given the context of *Moed Katan*, whose subject is *Chol HaMoed*. After a sustained immersion in *hilchos avelus*, it is easy to lose sight of the fact that the *Mishnah* itself is situated within a tractate about *Chol HaMoed*, and the transition back to that context is fully coherent on this reading.

The Gemara, as we will see, does not engage with the *Chol HaMoed* reading; it appears to treat the *Mishnah* as a general statement and proceeds accordingly.

### Seudas Havraah

The *Mishnah* adds: *v'ein mavrin ela al mitah zekufah* — one does not serve the *seudat havra'ah* except on an upright couch, not on an overturned one. The term *mitah* here should be understood not as a sleeping bed but as a kind of divan or couch upon which people would recline when eating — far more relevant to the question of dining posture than to sleeping arrangements.

### Two Readings

This clause generates two sharply divergent interpretations. Rabbeinu Gershom, who reads the *Mishnah* as a general statement unconnected to *Chol HaMoed*, understands the two occurrences of *mavrin* in the *Mishnah* as referring to two different subjects: the first means the *avel* receiving the *seudat havra'ah*, and the second means the person serving it. On this reading, the *avel* is reclining on an overturned couch as part of his mourning practice, while the person feeding him — who is not an *avel* — should sit on an upright couch, not an overturned one.

The Ra'avad, following the *Yerushalmi* and the *Rosh*, reads the *Mishnah* as specifically about *Chol HaMoed* and rejects the idea of a distinction between the *avel*'s couch and the feeder's couch. On the contrary, they maintain that the person providing the *seudat havra'ah* would actually sit together with the *avel* for that meal — and if there was sufficient intimacy and closeness between them, they would recline together. On *Chol HaMoed*, however, the overturning of couches is a *davar she-be-farhesya* — a public, visible act of mourning — that is not performed. Hence the *Mishnah* specifies that even those relatives who do perform the *seudat havra'ah* on *Chol HaMoed* will do so on an upright couch, not an overturned one.

The two readings thus diverge in both their subject and their substance: one is a general *halacha* about the etiquette of serving the *seudat havra'ah* at any time of year, while the other is a specific *Chol HaMoed* ruling about which visible mourning practices are and are not suspended.

## Chacham Exception

### Braita Chacham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וַאֲפִילּוּ חָכָם? וְהָתַנְיָא: חָכָם שֶׁמֵּת — הַכֹּל קְרוֹבָיו. הַכֹּל קְרוֹבָיו סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא: הַכֹּל כִּקְרוֹבָיו: הַכֹּל קוֹרְעִין עָלָיו, וְהַכֹּל חוֹלְצִין עָלָיו, וְהַכֹּל מַבְרִין עָלָיו בָּרְחָבָה! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּלָאו חָכָם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that only the relatives of the deceased rend their clothes. The Gemara asks: **And** is this the case **even** if the deceased was a Torah **Sage? But isn't it taught** otherwise in a *baraita*: When a Torah **scholar dies, everyone is his relative.** The Gemara clarifies: **Does it enter your mind** to say that **everyone is his relative? Rather,** this *baraita* should be understood as follows: **Everyone is** considered to be **like his relative** in the sense that **everyone rends his** garment in anguish **over him, and everyone bares** his shoulder **over him** in mourning, **and everyone eats the mourner's meal over him in the public square** as mourners do. The death of a Torah scholar is a personal loss for every Jew. So why is the mishna limited to only relatives? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach this *halakha* in a case **where** the deceased **is not a** Torah **scholar.**

The Gemara does not accept the possibility that the *Mishnah* might be arguing against the well-established practice of performing *keriah* for great sages and communal leaders. The practice was too firmly attested — both in other *Tannaic* sources and in the narratives we have already encountered — for the Gemara to read the *Mishnah* as abolishing it. Instead, the Gemara brackets that apparent implication and asks: *v'afilu chacham?* — would one not perform *keriah* even for a *chacham*, even if one were not his relative?

The answer comes from a *braita*: *chacham she-meis, hakol krovo* — when a *chacham* dies, everyone is his relative. The Gemara immediately clarifies: surely one does not mean literally that everyone is his relative — *ela hakol k'krovo* — everyone is *like* his relative.

### Keriah Chacham

The *braita* continues: *hakol kor'in alav* — everyone must perform *keriah*; *v'hakol choltzin alav* — everyone must uncover the shoulder; *v'hakol mavrin alav ba-rachava* — everyone participates in the *avelus* meal in the public thoroughfare. The logic of these three practices — *keriah*, *chalitzah*, and *havra'ah* — being extended to the entire community becomes clear when we understand their character. Unlike *shiva*, which is a sustained, multi-day observance, these are immediate expressions of the tragedy. When a great sage dies, the entire community performs *keriah*, participates in the procession to the cemetery, and then comes together for the meal of mourning in the public domain. After that, the *krovim* continue with *shiva* while the broader community returns to normal life. The *Mishnah*, on this reading, was never intended to prohibit these practices for great sages; it addresses the ordinary case of a private individual.

### Adam Kasher Theology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָדָם כָּשֵׁר הוּא — חַיּוֹבֵי מִיחַיַּיב לְמִיקְרַע. דְּתַנְיָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה בָּנָיו וּבְנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָדָם מֵתִים כְּשֶׁהֵן קְטַנִּים — כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּבְכֶּה וְיִתְאַבֵּל עַל אָדָם כָּשֵׁר. יִבְכֶּה וְיִתְאַבֵּל?! עֶרְבוֹנָא קָא שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ?! אֶלָּא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא בָּכָה וְהִתְאַבֵּל עַל אָדָם כָּשֵׁר. שֶׁכׇּל הַבּוֹכֶה וּמִתְאַבֵּל עַל אָדָם כָּשֵׁר — מוֹחֲלִין לוֹ עַל כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹתָיו בִּשְׁבִיל כָּבוֹד שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ! דְּלָאו אָדָם כָּשֵׁר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if** the deceased **was an upright person** who feared Heaven and performed good deeds, then aren't all those present at his death **obligated to rend** their garments over his death? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **For what** reason **do a person's sons and daughters die when they are young?** They die **so that he will cry and mourn** over the death of **an upright person.** The Gemara questions the formulation: They die so that **he will cry and mourn? Is security,** i.e., his children, **taken from him** in advance to ensure that in the future he will mourn over the death of an upright person? **Rather** the *baraita* means as follows: His children died **because he did not cry or mourn over an upright person** who died. **As** with regard to **anyone who cries and mourns over an upright person** who died, **they forgive him** for **all his transgressions because of the honor he accorded** to the deceased. If this is the case, one also rends his clothes over an upright person. The Gemara answers: Rather, the mishna is referring only to one **who** was **not an upright person.**

The Gemara presses further: even if the deceased is not a *chacham*, if he is an *adam kasher* — an upright, morally exemplary individual, someone who may have no official standing in the community but who is recognized as a paragon of ethical conduct — is one *chayav* to perform *keriah*? The Gemara cites a *braita* that addresses this in the starkest possible terms: *mipnei mah metim banav u-vnotav shel adam k'she-heim ktanim?* — why does a person's sons and daughters die when they are still young? *K'dei she-yivkeh v'yitabel al adam kasher* — so that he should cry and mourn for an upstanding individual when that person dies.

The Gemara is visibly troubled by this teaching, even on its own terms. *Arvona kashkil mineih?* — You are taking a collateral from him? The idea that a person is punished in advance so that he will do the right thing in the future strains credulity even as logic. The Gemara therefore pivots: *ella mipnei she-lo bacha v'yitabel al adam kasher* — it is rather because the person already failed to mourn appropriately for a worthy individual who died in the past. The punishment is thus retrospective, not prospective.

This remains deeply troubling theologically. The ideas of *poked avon avot al banim* and the broader framework in which children's deaths are understood as punishment for parental failures reflect a worldview that is, frankly, extremely disturbing. Part of the context is a society in which child mortality was devastatingly common, and the *braita* represents an attempt — however difficult for us — to find some framework of meaning within that reality. It is also worth noting that if one must suffer for failing to honor the morally great, the logic may be that such a failure reveals a deep confusion about what is genuinely valuable in the world. A *chacham* has clear communal status; everyone knows who he is. An *adam kasher* who is an ordinary layperson — perhaps an accountant, recognized by no official title — has only his intrinsic moral worth. Failing to acknowledge the gravity of his death may reveal that one has not internalized what truly matters. That said, the harshness of the statement cannot be softened away.

### Forgiveness Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכׇּל הַבּוֹכֶה וּמִתְאַבֵּל עַל אָדָם כָּשֵׁר — מוֹחֲלִין לוֹ עַל כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹתָיו בִּשְׁבִיל כָּבוֹד שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **anyone who cries and mourns over an upright person** who died, **they forgive him** for **all his transgressions because of the honor he accorded** to the deceased.

The *braita* then offers a more positive formulation: *shekol ha-boche u-misabel al adam kasher mochalin lo al kol avonasav* — whoever cries and mourns for an *adam kasher*, all of his sins are forgiven, *bishvil kavod she-asa lo* — on account of the honor shown to him. The theological implication is the mirror image of the punishment: acknowledging the true worth of a morally great person, regardless of his social standing, itself constitutes a recognition of genuine values — and that recognition earns forgiveness.

The Gemara then returns to the practical question of reconciliation between the *Mishnah* and the *braita*: the *Mishnah*, it concludes, speaks to the ordinary case — someone who is not a *chacham* and not an *adam kasher*. The practice of *keriah* and *chalitzah* for such a person, even a close friend or a somewhat distant relative, is limited to actual *krovim*. The exceptional cases of a *chacham* and an *adam kasher* fall outside the *Mishnah*'s intended scope.

### Keriah at Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּקָאֵי הָתָם בִּשְׁעַת יְצִיאַת נְשָׁמָה חַיּוֹבֵי מִיחַיַּיב! דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הָעוֹמֵד עַל הַמֵּת בִּשְׁעַת יְצִיאַת נְשָׁמָה — חַיָּיב לִקְרוֹעַ. לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה — לְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁנִּשְׂרַף, שֶׁחַיָּיב לִקְרוֹעַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But **if one was standing there at the time of the soul's departure,** i.e., at the time of death, he **is** also **obligated** to rend his clothes. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: One who stands over the deceased at the time of the soul's departure is obligated to rend** his clothes. **To what may this be likened? To a Torah scroll that is burned,** for which anyone present **is obligated to rend** his clothes.

The Gemara identifies yet another case that obligates *keriah* regardless of the relationship to the deceased: *d'kai hasam b'sha'as yetzias neshama* — one who is physically present at the moment the soul departs. A *braita* in the name of Rav Shimon ben Elazar teaches: *ha-omed al ha-meis b'sha'as yetzias neshama chayav likroa* — one who stands over the dying person at the actual moment of death is obligated to perform *keriah*. *L'ma ze domeh? L'sefer Torah she-nisraf* — to what is this comparable? To witnessing a *sefer Torah* being burned. At the moment of death, any person — by virtue of the soul that departs — is like a *sefer Torah*; the witness to that moment is obligated to express the tragedy through *keriah*. The *Mishnah*'s restriction to *krovim* thus applies specifically when one was not present at the moment of death and the deceased is neither a *chacham* nor an *adam kasher*.

### Rav Safra Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב סָפְרָא, לָא קְרַעוּ רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ, אָמְרִי: לָא גָּמְרִינַן מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי תַּנְיָא ״הָרַב שֶׁמֵּת״? ״חָכָם שֶׁמֵּת״ תַּנְיָא. וְעוֹד: כֹּל יוֹמָא שְׁמַעְתָּתֵיהּ בְּפוּמִּין בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא. סְבוּר: מָה דַּהֲוָה הֲוָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי, תְּנֵינָא: חָכָם, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בְּהֶסְפֵּד — חַיָּיבִין לִקְרוֹעַ. סְבוּר לְמִיקְרַע לְאַלְתַּר, אָמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי, תַּנְיָא: חָכָם כְּבוֹדוֹ בְּהֶסְפֵּידוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that **when Rav Safra passed away the** other **Sages did not rend** their garments **over him. They said: We did not learn from him,** as he did not disseminate his Torah knowledge to the public. **Abaye** berated them and **said to them: Is it taught** in the *baraita*: If one's **teacher died? It is taught:** If a Torah **scholar died,** and Rav Safra was certainly a Torah scholar. **And furthermore, every day his teachings are in our mouths in the study hall,** so that even if we did not learn directly from him, we should still be considered his students. **They** then **thought that what was** done **was** done, and it was now too late for them to rend their garments. **Abaye said to them: We learned:** With regard to a Torah **scholar, as long as they are engaged in eulogizing** him, then people **are obligated to rend** their garments, even after the time of his death. **They** then **thought to rend** their garments **immediately. Abaye said to them: It is taught** in a *baraita*: **A** Torah **scholar's honor is at** the time of **his eulogy,** and so you should wait until the time of the eulogy before rending your garments.

The Gemara illustrates these principles through a narrative. When Rav Safra died, the rabbinic students did not perform *keriah* for him. Their reasoning: *lav gaminei minei* — he was not our *Rebbe*; we did not study directly under him. Abaye challenged them: *mi tanya ha-rav she-meis?* — does the *braita* say "your *rav*"? It says *chacham she-meis* — a *sage* who dies, which requires no personal relationship. Moreover, Abaye added, even on their own terms: *kol yoma shmatetei b'funin b'vei medrasha* — his teachings are in our mouths every day in the *beis medrasha*. The students then concluded that since they had missed the moment, *mah de-hava hava* — what was was; the time for *keriah* had passed. Abaye corrected them again.

### Hesped Timing

The *braita* states: *chacham kol zman she-oskin be-hesped chayavin likro'a* — for a *chacham*, as long as the *hesped* is still being delivered, one remains obligated to perform *keriah*. This makes deep sense once one appreciates the function of a *hesped* in the rabbinic world: the eulogy was not merely a biographical tribute but was designed to stir profound emotion, to move people to cry and wail and feel the full weight of the loss. *Keriah* performed during the *hesped* is therefore not a belated afterthought but the expression of being genuinely overwhelmed — and that is precisely the moment of *kavod* for the deceased.

The students, hearing this, concluded that since further eulogies were scheduled for later in the day, they could perform *keriah* immediately in anticipation. Abaye corrected them a third time: *chacham kevodo be-hespedo* — the greatest *kavod* for a *chacham* is to perform *keriah* at the actual moment of the *hesped* itself, not in advance of it and not disconnected from it. The proper time is the moment when the eulogy is actively being delivered and one is moved by it — not a calculated performance outside that context.

## Rav Huna Funeral

### Sefer Torah Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, סְבוּר לְאוֹתוֹבֵי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה אַפּוּרְיֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִסְדָּא: מִילְּתָא דִּבְחַיֵּיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, הַשְׁתָּא לֵיקוּם לֵיהּ לֶיעְבַּד לֵיהּ? דְּאָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא: אֲנָא חֲזֵיתֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא דְּבָעֵי לְמֵיתַב אַפּוּרְיֵיהּ, וַהֲוָה מַנַּח סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה עֲלֵיהּ, וְכַף כַּדָּא אַאַרְעָא וְאוֹתֵיב סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה עִילָּוֵיהּ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: אָסוּר לֵישֵׁב עַל גַּבֵּי מִטָּה שֶׁסֵּפֶר תּוֹרָה מוּנָּח עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another incident: **When Rav Huna died they thought to place a Torah scroll on his bier,** as was commonly done after the death of a Torah scholar, as if to say that the deceased fulfilled everything written in the scroll. **Rav Ḥisda said to them:** This is **a practice that he did not hold with during his lifetime; now should we stand up and do** it **for him** when he is dead? **As Rav Taḥlifa said: I** myself **saw Rav Huna, who wished to sit on his bed, and there was a Torah scroll placed on it. And he turned a jug over and placed the Torah scroll on it** so that he could then sit on the bed. **Apparently he holds** that **it is prohibited to sit on a bed upon which a Torah scroll lies.** Therefore, it would be inappropriate to lay a Torah scroll next to his body after he died.

The Gemara now opens an extended narrative about the death and burial of Rav Huna. Upon his death, the community wished to place a *sefer Torah* in his coffin — or rather on the bed on which he would be carried — to accompany him on the procession to the cemetery. This was not a plan to bury the *sefer Torah*; the intention was that he would be carried alongside it, evoking the declaration *kiyam zeh mah she-kasuv be-zeh* — this person fulfilled everything written in this scroll. The practice of accompanying a great sage's body with a *sefer Torah* as a statement about his embodiment of *Torah* was known in other contexts.

### Torah Prohibition

Rav Chisda objected: *milta d'be-chayim lo sevira lei, hashta lekum ve-liyavid lei?* — something that in his lifetime he would not have done, you now want to do to him? The evidence comes from Rav Tachlifa, who reported: *ana chaziteih l'Rav Huna deba'i le-misav apurya* — I once saw Rav Huna wish to sit on a couch on which a *sefer Torah* was resting. Rather than simply move the *sefer Torah* aside and sit down, he took a vessel, overturned it on the floor, and placed the *sefer Torah* on top of it as a kind of makeshift stand. The inference: *kasavar assur lishev al gabi mita she-sefer Torah munach aleiha* — Rav Huna held that one may not sit on a couch upon which a *sefer Torah* rests. To now have him lie in a coffin alongside a *sefer Torah* would violate the very principle he had lived by.

### Doorway Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא הֲוָה נָפֵיק פּוּרְיָא מִבָּבָא, סְבוּר לְשַׁלְשׁוֹלֵי דֶּרֶךְ גַּגִּין. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִסְדָּא, הָא גְּמִירְנָא מִינֵּיהּ: חָכָם, כְּבוֹדוֹ דֶּרֶךְ פֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** When they tried to remove his corpse from his house for the burial, **the bier would not** fit **through the** narrow **door. They** then **thought to lower** it **from the roof. Rav Ḥisda said to them: This I learned from him,** Rav Huna himself: **A scholar's honor** is for him to be taken out **through the** main **opening,** and not in any other manner.

The next difficulty: *lo havah nafik purya mi-baba* — the bed could not be taken out through the doorway. The king-size bed on which Rav Huna lay simply would not fit. The community considered hoisting it up through the roof — carrying it out through a window and across the rooftop, or lowering it from there.

### Petach Requirement

Rav Chisda intervened again: *gamirna minei — chacham kevodo derech petach* — I have learned from him that the proper and honorable way to remove a *chacham* is through the *petach*, the doorway, not through any window or roof. The *kavod ha-meis* demands that the body exit through the front door.

### Narrower Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר לְאַשְׁנוֹיֵי מִפּוּרְיָא לְפוּרְיָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִסְדָּא, הָכִי גְּמִירְנָא מִינֵּיהּ: חָכָם כְּבוֹדוֹ בְּמִטָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לְחָכָם שֶׁכְּבוֹדוֹ בְּמִטָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְכִּיבוּ אֶת אֲרוֹן הָאֱלֹהִים אֶל עֲגָלָה חֲדָשָׁה״. פְּרוּס בָּבָא וְאַפְּקוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They** then **thought to move him from** his **bier to** a narrower **bier** so that it would fit through the door. But **Rav Ḥisda said to them: I learned from him,** Rav Huna himself, **as follows: A scholar's honor is** for him to be taken out **on the first bier. As Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: From where** is it derived that **a scholar's honor is** for him to be taken out **on the first bier? As it is stated: "And they set the Ark of God upon a new cart"** (II Samuel 6:3). When taking the Ark to Jerusalem, King David had it placed back on the cart upon which it had been returned by the Philistines, and a Torah scholar is considered to be similar to the Ark of the Covenant. When they saw that there was nothing else that they could do, **they broke the doorway and took him out** through it.

The next proposed solution: *savru la-shunoyei mi-purya le-purya* — transfer him from the large bed to a narrower one that would fit through the doorway. The logic is intuitive: if the obstacle is the width of the bed, find a narrower bed, and the body can be carried out through the door with full dignity.

### First Bed Honor

Rav Chisda objected a second time: *hachi gamirna minei — chacham kevodo be-mitah rishonah* — I have learned from him that the *kavod* of a *chacham* lies in being carried on the first bed, the original bed upon which he died or was first laid. Moving the body to a second, substitute piece of furniture would itself be a diminishment of honor.

### Aron Wagon Source

The source for this principle is a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *minayin le-chacham she-kevodo be-mitah rishonah? She-ne'emar: vayarkivu et aron ha-Elohim al agalah chadashah* — where is it derived that a *chacham*'s honor lies in being carried on the first conveyance? From the verse that describes the *aron* of God being placed on a wagon — understood here as the same wagon on which it had originally been sent back by the *Pelishtim*, not a newly fashioned one. The *aron* was not transferred to a new wagon; it was returned on the same vehicle that had carried it from the beginning. The parallel to the *chacham* resting on his *mitah rishonah* thus draws on the same principle applied to the *aron* itself — a powerful comparison, and one that complements the earlier association of a *chacham* with a *sefer Torah*.

It is worth noting the distinction between this *halacha* and the previous one about the *petach*: the requirement to exit through the doorway appears to apply to *meis* generally, while the *mitah rishonah* rule is stated specifically with respect to a *chacham*.

### Demolish Door

With no permissible alternative, the solution was simply: *poretz bava ve-afkuhu* — they demolished the doorway and took him out. The original bed, the proper exit through the *petach*, and the *kavod* of Rav Huna were all preserved — at the cost of the door.

## Hesped Journey

### Abba Eulogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּתַח עֲלֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: רָאוּי הָיָה רַבֵּינוּ שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה עָלָיו שְׁכִינָה, אֶלָּא שֶׁבָּבֶל גָּרְמָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba opened** his eulogy for **him: Our Rabbi was worthy that the Divine Presence should rest upon him, except** for the fact **that Babylonia caused it** not to rest. In other words, it was only because he lived in Babylonia and not in Eretz Yisrael that the Divine Presence did not rest upon him.

With Rav Huna's body now being transported to *Eretz Yisrael* for burial, the Gemara turns to the eulogies delivered over him. *Patach alei Rabbi Abba* — Rabbi Abba opened with the following: *rauy haya Rabbeinu she-tishreh alav Shechina* — our teacher was worthy of having the *Shechina* rest upon him, *ela she-Bavel garma lei* — except that being in *Bavel* prevented it, for the *Shechina* rests only on those in *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Yechezkel Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב נַחְמָן בַּר חִסְדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר חִסְדָּא: ״הָיֹה הָיָה דְבַר ה׳ אֶל יְחֶזְקֵאל בֶּן בּוּזִי הַכֹּהֵן בְּאֶרֶץ כַּשְׂדִּים״! טְפַח לֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ בְּסַנְדָּלֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו אָמֵינָא לָךְ לָא תִּיטְרוֹד עָלְמָא? מַאי ״הָיָה״ — שֶׁהָיָה כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Ḥisda raised an objection** against this, **and some say** that it was **Rav Ḥanan bar Ḥisda:** Is it not stated: **"The word of the Lord came [*hayo haya*] to Ezekiel the priest, son of Buzi, in the land of the Chaldeans"** (Ezekiel 1:3), thereby implying that a prophet can prophesy outside of Eretz Yisrael? **His father tapped him with his sandal** on his foot, thereby hinting to him that he should be quiet. **He said to him: Have I not told you not to trouble everyone** with questions in the middle of a eulogy? The Gemara answers the question: **What** is the meaning of the doubling of the word **"came [*hayo haya*]"?** It implies **that it had already come** before, i.e., that Ezekiel had already begun to prophesy in Eretz Yisrael, and his prophecy in Babylonia was merely a continuation of that prophecy.

*Metiv Rav Nachman bar Chisda* — and some say *Rav Chana bar Chisda* — immediately challenged: the verse states *haya haya devar Hashem el Yechezkel ben Buzi ha-Kohen be-eretz Kasdim* — the word of God came to Yechezkel in the land of *Kasdim*, meaning *Bavel*. Yechezkel received *nevua* in *Bavel*. How then can one say the *Shechina* does not rest outside *Eretz Yisrael*?

### No Questions

His father struck him on the sandal in response and said: *lav amina lach lo tatrid alma?* — have I not told you not to bother people, not to disturb the occasion? *Mah haya she-haya kvar* — what was, was. The implicit message is clear: one does not raise *kushyas* in the middle of a *hesped*. Rabbeinu Gershom offers an additional resolution on the merits: Yechezkel had begun his *nevua* in *Eretz Yisrael*, and having established that connection, *she-haya kvar*, he was able to continue receiving prophecy even in *Bavel*. But this is offered more as a *teretz* than as a demonstrated reading of the text.

### Ami Assi Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַסְּקוּהּ לְהָתָם, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי וּלְרַבִּי אַסִּי: רַב הוּנָא אָתֵי. אֲמַרוּ: כִּי הֲוֵינַן הָתָם לָא הֲוָה לַן לְדַלּוֹיֵי רֵישִׁין מִינֵּיהּ. הַשְׁתָּא אָתֵינַן הָכָא — אֲתָא בָּתְרִין? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֲרוֹנוֹ בָּא. רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי נְפוּק, רַבִּי אִילָא וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא לָא נְפוּק. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַבִּי אִילָא נְפַק, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא לָא נְפַק.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that **when they took** Rav Huna **there,** to Eretz Yisrael, for burial **they said to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi: Rav Huna has come,** and they misunderstood and thought that he was still alive. **They said: When we were there,** in Babylonia, we **did not have strength to lift our heads before him. Now** that **we have come here, has he come after us?** **They said to them: His coffin has come. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi went out** to meet his funeral procession. **Rabbi Ila and Rabbi Ḥanina did not go out. Some say** that **Rabbi Ila went out,** but **Rabbi Ḥanina did not go out.**

When the procession arrived in *Eretz Yisrael*, word was sent to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi: *Rav Huna ati* — Rav Huna is coming. Their response was immediate alarm: *ki havinnan tam, lo hava lan le-mizkpei reshin mineih* — when we were there in *Bavel*, we could not lift our heads in his presence. He was so imposing, so towering in his *Torah*, that they lived in a kind of constant awe. *Ha-shta ateinan hacha, ata batran?* — Now that we have come here, he is coming after us?

### Coffin Clarified

They were informed: *arona hu de-ati* — it is his coffin that is coming. He is dead. Upon clarification, Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi went out to participate in the funeral procession.

### Transport Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּנְפַק, מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: אָרוֹן הָעוֹבֵר מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם — עוֹמְדִים עָלָיו בְּשׁוּרָה וְאוֹמְרִים עָלָיו בִּרְכַּת אֲבֵלִים וְתַנְחוּמֵי אֲבֵלִים. דְּלָא נְפַק, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּתַנְיָא: אָרוֹן הָעוֹבֵר מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם — אֵין עוֹמְדִים עָלָיו בְּשׁוּרָה וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים עָלָיו בִּרְכַּת אֲבֵלִים וְתַנְחוּמֵי אֲבֵלִים. קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — שֶׁשִּׁלְדּוֹ קַיֶּימֶת, כָּאן — בְּשֶׁאֵין שִׁלְדּוֹ קַיֶּימֶת. וְרַב הוּנָא שִׁלְדּוֹ קַיֶּימֶת הֲוָה? דְּלָא נְפַק, לָא סַיְּימוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Those who **went out, what is** the **reason** that they went? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: When **a coffin is passing from place to place,** the people **stand in a line** to show respect **for** the deceased, **and they recite the mourners' blessing and the consolation of the mourners over it.** Those **who did not go out, what is the reason** that they did not? **As it is taught** in another *baraita*: When a **coffin is passing from place to place, they do not stand in a line** to show respect **for** the deceased, **and they do not recite the mourners' blessing or** the **consolation of the mourners for him.** If so, **these** two tannaitic statements **contradict each other.** The Gemara answers: **It is not difficult: Here,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where the skeleton** of the deceased **is** still **intact,** and the mourning practices must be observed. **And there** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where the skeleton** of the deceased **is no longer intact,** and it is not necessary to observe the customs of mourning. **And Rav Huna's skeleton was** still **intact.** The reason that the **one** Sage **did not go out** was that **they did not confirm for him** that the skeleton was still intact.

Rabbi Ila and Rabbi Chanina did not go out. A variant tradition records that Rabbi Ila went while Rabbi Chanina did not. This distinction — who went out and who did not — has been preserved across two millennia. The reason those who went out did so: a *braita* teaches that when a coffin is transported from place to place, the community in the new location *stands in the shura*, recites *birkas avelim*, and offers *tanchumei avelim* — the full funeral observances are repeated at each stop. Therefore, going out to participate was appropriate and obligatory.

### Skeleton Status

Those who did not go out relied on a different *braita*: *aron ha-over mi-makom le-makom ein omdim alav be-shura* — when a coffin is transported from place to place, one does not stand in formation, does not recite *birkas avelim*, and does not offer *tanchumei avelim*. The two *braitot* appear to contradict each other. The Gemara resolves: *lo kashya — kan she-shildo kayemet, kan she-ein shildo kayemet* — the distinction depends on whether the skeleton is still intact. If the skeleton remains whole, the body still bears the character of a *meis* in a full sense, and the funeral observances are repeated at the new location. If the skeleton has already decomposed into disconnected bones, the full ritual is not repeated. *Ve-shilda de-Rav Huna kayemes hava* — Rav Huna's skeleton was still intact. Those who did not go out were simply not aware of this fact; they had not been informed, and therefore did not know that circumstances warranted going out.

## Cave Burial

### Cave Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הֵיכָא נַינְּחֵיהּ? רַב הוּנָא רִיבֵּץ תּוֹרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא רִיבֵּץ תּוֹרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of Eretz Yisrael **said: Where shall we bury him?** They concluded: **Rav Huna disseminated Torah to** the people of **Israel, and** similarly **Rabbi Ḥiyya disseminated Torah to** the people of **Israel;** therefore, it is appropriate to bury Rav Huna next to Rabbi Ḥiyya.

With Rav Huna's body now in *Eretz Yisrael*, the question arose: *heicha ninchei?* — where should he be buried? The reasoning offered: *Rav Huna reibitz Torah b'Yisrael, Rav Chiya reibitz Torah b'Yisrael* — Rav Huna disseminated *Torah* in Israel, and Rabbi Chiya disseminated *Torah* in Israel. Rabbi Chiya's contribution was legendary: he famously declared that he would ensure *Torah* would never be lost from Israel, and he implemented a remarkable system — producing *sifrei Torah*, distributing them, and establishing a structure in which students became teachers and teachers empowered new teachers, creating a self-sustaining chain of transmission. Since both men had contributed to the broad dissemination of *Torah* across Israel, Rav Huna should be buried next to Rabbi Chiya.

### Chaga Volunteer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן מְעַיֵּיל לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חַגָּא: אֲנָא מְעַיֵּילְנָא לֵיהּ, דְּאוֹקֵמְתֵּיהּ לְתַלְמוּדַאי כִּי הֲוֵינָא בַּר תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי שְׁנִין וְלָא חֲזֵי לִי קֶרִי, וּמְשַׁמַּע לִי(ה) קַמֵּיהּ, וִידַעִי בְּעוֹבָדֵיהּ, דְּיוֹמָא חַד אִתְהֲפִיכָא לֵיהּ רְצוּעָה דִתְפִילִּין וִיתֵיב עֲלַהּ אַרְבְּעִין תַּעֲנִיָּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** They asked: **Who will take him in** to Rabbi Ḥiyya's burial cave, as few are fit to enter it? **Rav Ḥagga said to them: I will take him into** the cave, **for I presented my studies** before him **when I was** just **eighteen, never having experienced a seminal emission. And** so too **I attended to him and knew his** great **deeds.** For example, **one day one of the straps of his phylacteries turned around,** the unpainted side being turned outward, **and he observed forty fasts for this,** as he had acted negligently, allowing the black side to face inward.

*Amar lehu Rav Chaga: ana ma'ayilna lei* — Rav Chaga volunteered to bring the body into the cave where Rabbi Chiya was buried.

### Chaga Credentials

Rav Chaga offered his credentials: *d'ukmaseih le-talmudei ki havina bar tamnei srei shanin* — he had established his learning — presenting the full corpus of what he had studied before his teacher — when he was just eighteen years old; *v'lo chazai li keri* — and he never experienced a seminal emission throughout that period; *u-meshamshi lei kamei* — and he served as Rav Huna's personal attendant; *ve-yada'ina b'uvdeih* — and he knows his deeds intimately. He then illustrated: one day the strap of Rav Huna's *tefillin* became reversed, with the white side exposed and the black turned inward. Rav Huna thereupon fasted forty fasts in penance. Rav Chaga was thereby making two claims simultaneously: that his own virtue would permit him safe passage into the protected cave, and that his intimate relationship with Rav Huna made him the fitting person for this task.

### Sons Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַיְּילֵיהּ, הֲוָה גָּנֵי יְהוּדָה מִיַּמִּינֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ וְחִזְקִיָּה מִשְּׂמָאלֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ יְהוּדָה לְחִזְקִיָּה: קוּם מִדּוּכְתִּיךְ, דְּלָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא דְּקָאֵים רַב הוּנָא. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאֵים — קָם בַּהֲדֵיהּ עַמּוּדָא דְנוּרָא. חַזְיֵיהּ רַב חַגָּא, אִיבְּעִית, זַקְפֵיהּ לַאֲרוֹנֵיהּ וּנְפַק אֲתָא. וְהַאי דְּלָא אִיעֲנַשׁ (ענש), מִשּׁוּם דְּזַקְפֵיהּ לַאֲרוֹנֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥagga **took him in.** The body of Rabbi Ḥiyya's son **Yehuda lay** buried **to the right of his father, and** the body of his other son **Ḥizkiyya** lay **to his left.** The spirit of **Yehuda said to** the spirit of **Ḥizkiyya: Rise from your place, as** it is **not proper conduct** to remain lying when the body of **Rav Huna is standing here. When** Ḥizkiyya's corpse stood **up, a pillar of fire rose with him.** When **Rabbi Ḥagga saw this, he was frightened** by what he saw, and so **he stood up** Rav Huna's **coffin and went away.** The Gemara comments: **And he was not punished** or harmed by this pillar of fire **because he set up Rav Huna's coffin** as protection for himself.

Inside the cave, Rabbi Chiya's two sons, Yehuda and Chizkiya, were buried on either side of their father — one to his right, one to his left. Yehuda said to Chizkiya: *kum mi-duchach* — rise from your place. According to Rabbeinu Chananel's text, the more readily intelligible version, the formulation is: *d'lav orach ara d'ka'i Rav Huna mi-bara'i* — it is not appropriate that Rav Huna should remain lying. Room needed to be made.

### Fire Pillar

As Chizkiya rose, *kama bahadei amuda de-nura* — a pillar of fire rose together with him. Rav Chaga saw this pillar of fire emerging from within the cave, was seized with terror, stood the *aron* of Rav Huna upright, and fled. The *aron* was thus left unburied.

### Punishment Question

The Gemara observes: *v'hai d'lo i-anish — mishum d'zakfei l'arona de-Rav Huna* — the reason Rav Chaga was not punished was because he had stood up the *aron*. But this explanation is deeply puzzling. Rav Chaga had been chosen precisely for his virtue and his closeness to Rav Huna. His flight left the body unburied. How does standing the *aron* upright — apparently using it as a shield between himself and the fire before fleeing — constitute a sufficient justification for his not being punished? The text is very difficult, and satisfying analysis is hard to find.

Rabbeinu Chananel's version offers a different reading: *hai d'lo i-anish d'vei nesi'ah* — those of the *Beis HaNasi*'s household were not punished, a reference to the special merit and protection associated with Rav Huna's lineage from the *Nasi*. This formulation at least shifts the ground of the explanation, but it does not fully account for what Rav Chaga did and why it was treated as acceptable. The question, for now, is left open.
