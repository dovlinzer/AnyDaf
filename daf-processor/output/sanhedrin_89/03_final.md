# Sanhedrin 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zaken Mamre Definition

### Chomer Sofrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חוֹמֶר בְּדִבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים מִבְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה. הָאוֹמֵר: ״אֵין תְּפִילִּין״, כְּדֵי לַעֲבוֹר עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה – פָּטוּר. ״חָמֵשׁ טוֹטָפוֹת״, לְהוֹסִיף עַל דִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the rulings of the rebellious elder the mishna states: There is greater **stringency with regard to** traditional **rabbinic interpretations** of the Torah **than with regard to matters of Torah.** If **one states:** There is **no** mitzva to don **phylacteries,** and his intention is **in order to** have others **violate matters of Torah,** he is **exempt** from punishment as a rebellious elder. One who disputes matters written explicitly in the Torah is not considered an elder and a Torah scholar, and therefore does not assume the status of a rebellious elder. If, however, he disputed a matter based on rabbinic tradition, e.g., he stated that there should be **five compartments** in the phylacteries of the head, in order **to add** an extra compartment **to** the four established according to traditional **rabbinic interpretations** of the Torah, he is **liable.**

The *daf* opens with the *mishna* found at the bottom of 88b, approximately ten lines from the end, which introduces the principle of *chomer bedivrei sofrim midivrei torah* — that rabbinic interpretation can carry greater weight than what is stated explicitly in the verses themselves. The context is the *zaken mamre*, the rebellious elder.

### Tefillin Examples

The *mishna* illustrates this principle through the laws of *tefillin*. If a *zaken mamre* were to declare that there is no such thing as *tefillin* at all — thereby denying something stated explicitly in the *psukim* of the *Torah* — he would be *patur*, exempt from the *chiyuv* of a *zaken mamre*. The reason is that such a statement does not constitute a *hora'ah*: the *zaken mamre* is defined by his positioning himself as a *posek* in defiance of the *Beis Din*, and a flat denial of explicit *Torah* law does not rise to the level of a competing legal ruling.

However, if he were to claim that the *shel rosh* requires not four *batim* but five, then he is *chayav*. This position engages with the halachic question of what constitutes *tefillin* — accepting the institution while disputing its parameters — and that constitutes a true *hora'ah*. It is thus counterintuitive but correct: one is more liable for deviating on a matter whose details derive from rabbinic interpretation than for denying something stated outright in the text.

This *mishna* reflects the opinion of *Rebbi Yehuda*, who derives from the phrase *al pi hatorah asher yorucha* that the *zaken mamre* can be liable for any matter that has both a *Torah* basis (*al pi hatorah*) and a dimension requiring interpretation (*asher yorucha*). This is the broader view, as distinct from *Rebbi Meir*, who requires that the matter be one for which an ordinary person would incur *kares* and *chatas*.

### Mosif Gore'a Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁעִיקָּרוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וּפֵירוּשׁוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף, וְאִם הוֹסִיף גּוֹרֵעַ. וְאֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא תְּפִילִּין, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says: One is liable only for** issuing a ruling with regard to **a matter whose essence,** whose basic obligation, **is from matters of Torah and whose explanation is from** traditional **rabbinic interpretations** of the Torah **and which includes** the possibility **to add** to it, **and if one added** to it, **one compromises** his fulfillment of the mitzva and does not satisfy his obligation. **And we have only** the mitzva to don **phylacteries** that meets those criteria. **And** Rabbi Oshaya's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda,** who says: A rebellious elder is liable only for a matter whose essence is from matters of Torah and whose explanation is from traditional rabbinic interpretations of the Torah.

*Rebbi Elazar* then cites *Rebbi Oshaya* with a considerably narrower definition. He states: *eino chayav ela al davar she'ikaro midivrei torah uperusho midivrei sofrim veyesh bo lehosif ve'im hosif gore'a* — one is only liable as a *zaken mamre* for something whose root is in the *Torah*, whose elaboration derives from rabbinic interpretation, and where there is the possibility of addition such that adding actually subtracts. The *gemara* concludes: *va'anu ein lanu ela tefillin* — the only example that fits all these criteria is *tefillin*.

This formulation is narrower than the *mishna* and is said to align with *Rebbi Yehuda*. It introduces the *mosif gore'a* principle: not merely that one added to a *mitzvah* object, but that by adding, one rendered the original invalid. As will be seen, this is a demanding criterion that eliminates most potential cases.

### Lulav/Tzitzit Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא לוּלָב, דְּעִיקָּרוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וּפֵירוּשׁוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף, וְאִם הוֹסִיף – גּוֹרֵעַ? בְּלוּלָב, מַאי סְבִירָא לַן? אִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּלוּלָב אֵין צָרִיךְ אֶגֶד, הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי. וְאִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּצָרִיךְ אֶגֶד, גָּרוּעַ וְעוֹמֵד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't there** the mitzva of ***lulav*** and the other species that one takes on the festival of *Sukkot*, **whose essence is from matters of Torah, and whose explanation is from** traditional **rabbinic interpretations** that establish the identity and the number of the four species enumerated in the Torah, **and which includes** the possibility **to add** other species to it, **and if one added** to it, **one compromises** his fulfillment of the mitzva and does not satisfy his obligation? The Gemara rejects this possibility: That is not the case, as **with regard to** the mitzva of ***lulav*, what do we hold? If we hold that** fundamentally **a *lulav* does not require binding** of the species together in order to fulfill the mitzva, then adding an additional species is inconsequential, as **these** species with which he fulfills the mitzva **stand alone and that** additional species **stands alone.** It is as though he were holding the species of the mitzva and an additional unrelated item that does not affect fulfillment of the mitzva. **And if we hold** that **a *lulav* requires binding** of the four species together in order to fulfill the mitzva, fulfillment of the mitzva **is already compromised** from the outset. The rebellious elder is liable only when the object of the mitzva was as it should be and the addition compromised that object and disqualifies it. In this case, the object was never as it should be.

The *gemara* challenges *Rebbi Elazar's* restriction by proposing additional candidates. Regarding *lulav*: its root is in the *Torah*, its details are rabbinic elaboration, and adding an extra *min* might seem to invalidate the *arba minim*. But the *gemara* responds that the answer depends on whether one holds that the *lulav* requires binding (*egged*). If no binding is required, then each species stands independently (*hai lechudei kai vehai lechudei kai*), and adding an extra species does not redefine the bundle and does not invalidate it. If binding is required, then a bundle that included an extra species would be *garu ve'omed* — invalid from the very moment of its construction. There would never be a moment when a valid bundle existed only to be subsequently invalidated by the addition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא צִיצִית, דְּעִיקָּרוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וּפֵירוּשׁוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף, וְאִם הוֹסִיף – גּוֹרֵעַ? בְּצִיצִית, מַאי סְבִירָא לַן? אִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּקֶשֶׁר הָעֶלְיוֹן לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי. וְאִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּקֶשֶׁר הָעֶלְיוֹן דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, גָּרוּעַ וְעוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't there** the mitzva of **ritual fringes, whose essence is from matters of Torah, and whose explanation is from** traditional **rabbinic interpretations** that establish the number of fringes enumerated in the Torah and the number of threads in each fringe, **and which includes** the possibility **to add** fringes or threads to it, **and if one added** to it, **one compromises** his fulfillment of the mitzva and does not satisfy his obligation? The Gemara rejects this possibility: That is not the case, as **with regard to ritual fringes, what do we hold? If we hold that the upper knot is not** mandated **by Torah law,** and one fulfills his obligation by placing the threads on the corner of the garment, **these** threads with which he fulfills the mitzva **are independent and that** additional thread **is independent** and does not compromise fulfillment of the mitzva. The additional string is not considered as joined to the required strings. **And if we hold that the upper knot** is mandated **by Torah law,** then fulfillment of the mitzva **is already compromised** from the outset when the additional string is tied together with the other threads.

The same logic applies to *tzitzit*. Adding an extra string might seem to invalidate the *tzitzit*, but it depends on whether the *kesher elyon* — the top knot — is *de'oraisa*. If it is not (a minority view), the extra string stands independently and does not invalidate. If it is *de'oraisa*, which is indeed the accepted *pesak*, then the extra string creates an invalid object from the outset — *garu ve'omed* — rather than invalidating something previously valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּפִילִּין נָמֵי? אִי עֲבַד אַרְבְּעָה בָּתֵּי, וְאַיְיתִי אַחֲרִינָא וְאַנַּח גַּבַּיְיהוּ – הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי. וְאִי עֲבַד חַמְשָׁה בָּתֵּי – גָּרוּעַ וְעוֹמֵד הוּא! הָאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בַּיִת חִיצוֹן שֶׁאֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הָאֲוִיר – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** the status of **phylacteries** is **also** the same. **If one crafts** phylacteries of the head with **four compartments, and** he then **brings another** compartment **and places** it **alongside them, these** compartments with which he fulfills the mitzva **stand alone and that** additional compartment **stands alone** and does not compromise the fulfillment of the mitzva. **And if one crafts** phylacteries of the head with **five compartments,** the fulfillment of the mitzva **is already compromised** from the outset. The Gemara answers: **But doesn't Rabbi Zeira say:** If there is an obstruction which creates **an outer compartment** of the phylacteries of the head **that is not exposed to the air,** the phylacteries of the head are **unfit** for use in the fulfillment of the mitzva? Therefore, adding a fifth compartment to the four compartments of the phylacteries of the head always compromises the fulfillment of the mitzva.

The *gemara* then presses the same challenge against *tefillin*: if one begins with four valid *batim* and attaches a fifth as a separate unit, the fifth stands independently and does not invalidate the four. If one constructs five *batim* as a single unit from the start, then again the object is *garu ve'omed* — never valid to begin with. The difficulty, then, is: how is *tefillin* any different from *lulav* or *tzitzit*?

*Rav Zeira* resolves the problem: *bayis chitzon she'eino ro'eh es ha'avir pasul* — the outer compartment of the *shel rosh* must be exposed to the open air, and if it is not, it is invalid. When a fifth *bayis* is added to what was a valid set of four, even if the fifth stands as a separate unit and does not redefine the whole as a set of five, it nonetheless invalidates the outer compartment of the original four by covering it and preventing its exposure to the air. This is the unique mechanism by which *tefillin* satisfies the *mosif gore'a* criterion: there is a valid object to begin with, and the act of adding genuinely invalidates it — not by redefining the unit, but through this distinct *pesul* of the *bayis chitzon*.

It should be noted that no *psukim* are brought in the *gemara* to explain why *Rebbi Elazar* requires this *mosif gore'a* criterion as the essential definition of *zaken mamre*. The derivation remains analytically interesting but without explicit scriptural grounding in the text as presented.

## Execution Procedures

### Jurisdiction Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ לֹא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁבְּעִירוֹ, וְלֹא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁבְּיַבְנֶה, אֶלָּא מַעֲלִין אוֹתוֹ לְבֵית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. וּמְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתוֹ עַד הָרֶגֶל, וּמְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכׇל הָעָם יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיִרָאוּ וְלֹא יְזִידוּן עוֹד״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין מְעַנִּין אֶת דִּינוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, אֶלָּא מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּד, וְכוֹתְבִין וְשׁוֹלְחִין שְׁלוּחִין בְּכׇל הַמְּקוֹמוֹת: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מִתְחַיֵּיב מִיתָה בְּבֵית דִּין״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One does not execute** the rebellious elder, **neither in the court that is in his city, nor in the court that is in Yavne,** although that was the seat of the Sanhedrin after the destruction of the Second Temple. **Rather, one takes him up to the High Court in Jerusalem. And they guard him** in incarceration **until the pilgrimage Festival, and** the court **executes him during the pilgrimage Festival, as it is stated: "And all the nation shall hear, and fear, and no longer sin intentionally"** (Deuteronomy 17:13); this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda says: One does not delay** administering **justice** to **this** individual. **Rather,** the court **executes him immediately, and** the judges **write** reports **and dispatch agents to all the places,** informing them: **So-and-so is liable** to be punished with the **court**-imposed **death** penalty for disobeying the court.

The next *mishna* continues the laws of the *zaken mamre* and turns to the question of where and when he is executed. The *mishna* states: *ein memisin oso lo beveis din shebe'iro velo beveis din shebeyavneh* — he is not executed either in his local *Beis Din* or in the *Beis Din* of *Yavneh*. *Rashi* and *Tosafos* both raise the obvious difficulty: why is *Yavneh* mentioned at all? The *Beis Din* of *Yavneh* rose to prominence precisely after the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed, yet the *zaken mamre* requires the *Beis Din* in the *Beis HaMikdash* to have jurisdiction. The *mishna*'s mention of *Yavneh* thus indicates that even a major court sitting outside the *Beis HaMikdash* cannot execute the *zaken mamre*.

### Beis Din HaGadol

Execution can only occur under the authority of the *Beis Din HaGadol she'birushalayim*. This is not only the venue of the *gmar din* but also the place of execution itself. It is worth noting, however, that the *Rambam* makes explicit that the actual adjudication — the determination of liability — can be conducted in the *zaken mamre's* local *Beis Din*. He is then brought to *Yerushalayim* for execution. Notably, the opening *mishna* of *Sanhedrin* does not list the judgment of the *zaken mamre* among the matters requiring a *Beis Din* of seventy-one; the elevated venue is required specifically for the execution, not the trial itself.

### Regel vs. Immediate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ לֹא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁבְּעִירוֹ וְלֹא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁבְּיַבְנֶה, אֶלָּא מַעֲלִין אוֹתוֹ לְבֵית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם וּמְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתוֹ עַד הָרֶגֶל, וּמְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכׇל הָעָם יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיִרָאוּ״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר ״יִרְאוּ וְיִירָאוּ״? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיִירָאוּ״! לָמָּה מְעַנִּין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל זֶה? אֶלָּא, מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּד, וְכוֹתְבִין וְשׁוֹלְחִין בְּכׇל מָקוֹם: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי נִתְחַיֵּיב מִיתָה בְּבֵית דִּין״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:3): **One does not execute** the rebellious elder, **neither in the court that is in his city, nor in the court that is in Yavne. Rather, one takes him up to the High Court that is in Jerusalem. And they guard him** in incarceration **until the pilgrimage Festival, and** the court **executes him during the pilgrimage Festival, as it is stated: "And all the nation shall hear, and fear"** (Deuteronomy 17:13); this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda said to** Rabbi Akiva: **Is it stated:** And all the nation **shall see and fear,** which would indicate that the entire nation must actually witness his execution? **But isn't it stated only:** "And all the nation **shall hear and fear,"** indicating that merely informing the people of the execution is sufficient? **Why delay** administering **justice** to **this** individual? **Rather,** the court **executes him immediately, and** the judges **write** reports **and dispatch agents to all the places** informing them: **So-and-so was** sentenced and found **liable** to be punished with **execution in court.**

The *mishna* records a dispute regarding timing. According to the *Tanna Kamma* in the name of *Rebbi Akiva*: *umeshamrin oso ad haregel umemisin oso baregel* — one waits until the *regel* and executes him then, as the *pasuk* states: *vechol ha'am yishme'u veyira'u velo yezidun od* — all the people will hear and fear and no longer act presumptuously. The rationale for waiting is that the *regel*, when all of Israel gathers in *Yerushalayim*, maximizes the deterrent effect and reinforces the authority of the *Beis Din HaGadol*.

*Rebbi Yehuda* disagrees: *ein me'anin es dino shel ze* — one does not delay and cause *inui hadin*, the suffering of prolonged judgment, once the *gmar din* has been rendered. According to *Rebbi Yehuda*, execution is immediate, and the requirement of public awareness — *veyishme'u veyira'u* — is satisfied by sending written notifications throughout all locations: *ish ploni nischayev misa beveis din*. *Rebbi Yehuda* presses the textual point: the *pasuk* does not say *yir'u* — they will see — but *yishme'u* — they will hear. Hearing about the execution through dispatched messages is sufficient; there is no need for the physical gathering of the *regel*.

The consequence of *Rebbi Yehuda's* position regarding venue is also noteworthy: since the trial occurs in the local *Beis Din* and execution follows immediately upon the *gmar din*, execution would take place locally rather than in the *Beis Din HaGadol*. The elevation to *Yerushalayim* was driven by the logic of waiting for the *regel*; once that is removed, execution proceeds where the judgment was rendered.

### Four Akhraza Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה צְרִיכִין הַכְרָזָה – הַמֵּסִית, וּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, וְזָקֵן מַמְרֵא, וְעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין. בְּכוּלְּהוּ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ ״וְכׇל הָעָם״ וְ״כׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל״. בְּעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין כְּתִיב ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָרִים״? דְּלָא כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חֲזוּ לְסָהֲדוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There are **four** transgressors condemned to be executed whose verdicts **require a proclamation** to inform the public: **One who instigates** others to engage in idol worship, **and the stubborn and rebellious son, and the rebellious elder, and conspiring witnesses. In all of these** cases, **it is written concerning them: "And all the nation"** (Deuteronomy 17:13), or: **"And all Israel"** (Deuteronomy 13:12, 21:21), except **with regard to conspiring witnesses,** where **it is written: "And those who remain** shall hear and fear" (Deuteronomy 19:20), **as not everyone is fit for** providing **testimony.** Therefore, rather than saying that all of the nation or all of Israel must be informed, only those remaining who are not disqualified from providing testimony are those who must heed the verse: "Hear and fear and shall henceforth commit no more such evil in the midst of you" (Deuteronomy 19:20).

The *gemara* introduces a *baraisa*: *arba'ah tzerichin akhraza* — four categories of individuals require a public proclamation throughout all of Israel upon their execution, as derived from the formulation *vechol Yisrael yishme'u veyira'u*. These four are: *ha-meisis* (one who seduces others to *avodah zarah*), *ben sorer u-moreh*, *zaken mamre*, and *edim zomemin*. In each case, some form of the phrase *veyishme'u veyira'u* appears in the relevant *psukim*. The one variation is by *edim zomemin*, where the *pasuk* reads *vehanish'arim yishme'u veyira'u* — only the remaining will hear and fear — because the *gemara* explains: *de-lav kol amma chazu le-sahaduso*, not everyone is qualified to serve as a witness, and so the deterrent effect is directed specifically at those who can.

### Institution Protection

*Ramban*, in his commentary on the *Torah*, offers a striking insight that illuminates the entire list. In each of these four cases, he observes, the individual is being executed not simply because his act alone deserves the death penalty, but in order to protect critical institutions. The *ben sorer u-moreh* is executed to protect the institution of parental authority. The *edim zomemin* are executed to protect the integrity of the courts — even when the person they falsely accused was not actually put to death. The *zaken mamre* is executed to protect the decisory authority of the *Beis Din* — its power not merely to adjudicate but to interpret and rule. And the *meisis* is executed to protect divine authority, even if nobody actually worshipped *avodah zarah* as a result.

This is why in each case the *Torah* insists on *veyishme'u veyira'u*: the deterrent must be public and pervasive, because what is at stake is not merely one individual's sin but the integrity of the foundational structures that sustain Jewish life. It also explains why the punishment can appear disproportionate to the concrete harm caused — the harm being protected against is institutional, not individual.

## Navi HaSheker

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נְבִיא הַשֶּׁקֶר הַמִּתְנַבֵּא מַה שֶּׁלֹּא שָׁמַע, וּמַה שֶּׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר לוֹ – מִיתָתוֹ בִּידֵי אָדָם. אֲבָל הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת נְבוּאָתוֹ, וְהַמְוַותֵּר עַל דִּבְרֵי נָבִיא, וְנָבִיא שֶׁעָבַר עַל דִּבְרֵי עַצְמוֹ – מִיתָתוֹ בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָנֹכִי אֶדְרֹשׁ מֵעִמּוֹ״. הַמִּתְנַבֵּא בְּשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאוֹמֵר: ״כָּךְ אָמְרָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה״, אֲפִילּוּ כִּוֵּין אֶת הַהֲלָכָה לְטַמֵּא אֶת הַטָּמֵא וּלְטַהֵר אֶת הַטָּהוֹר. הַבָּא עַל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִרְשׁוּת הַבַּעַל לְנִשּׂוּאִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִבְעֲלָה, הַבָּא עָלֶיהָ הֲרֵי זֶה בְּחֶנֶק. וְזוֹמְמֵי בַּת כֹּהֵן וּבוֹעֲלָהּ, שֶׁכׇּל הַמּוּזַמִּין מַקְדִּימִין לְאוֹתָהּ מִיתָה, חוּץ מִזּוֹמְמֵי בַּת כֹּהֵן וּבוֹעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The false prophet** mentioned in the Torah includes **one who prophesies that which he did not hear** from God **and** one who prophesies **that which was not said to him,** even if it was said to another prophet. In those cases, **his execution is at the hand of man,** through strangulation imposed by the court. **But** with regard to **one who suppresses his prophecy** because he does not want to share it with the public, **and one who** contemptuously **forgoes the statement of a prophet** and refuses to heed it, **and a prophet who violated his own statement** and failed to perform that which he was commanded to do, **his death is at the hand of Heaven, as it is stated:** "And it shall come to pass, that whosoever shall not hearken unto My words that he shall speak in My name, **I will exact it of him"** (Deuteronomy 18:19). **One who prophesies in the name of idol worship and says: This** is what the **idol said, even** if **he approximated the** correct ***halakha*** in the name of the idol **to deem ritually impure that which is ritually impure and to deem ritually pure that which is ritually pure,** is executed by strangulation. In the case of **one who engages in intercourse with a married woman once she entered her husband's domain for** the purposes of **marriage, even if** the marriage was not yet consummated, as **she did not** yet **engage in intercourse** with him, **one who engages in intercourse with her is** executed **by strangulation.** **And conspiring** witnesses who testified that **the daughter of a priest** committed adultery are executed by strangulation, even though were she guilty, she would be executed by burning. **As all** those **who are rendered conspiring witnesses are led to** their deaths via **the same** mode of **execution** with which they conspired to have their victim executed, **except for conspiring** witnesses who testified that **the daughter of a priest and her paramour** committed adultery. In that case, although the priest's daughter who commits adultery is executed by burning, the conspiring witnesses who sought to have her executed are executed by strangulation, as is the paramour whom they also conspired to have executed.

The *mishna* now moves to the next category of *chenek*: the *navi ha-sheker*, the false prophet. Four types are identified. First: one who prophesies something he never heard — he fabricates a prophecy entirely. Second: one who prophesies something that was communicated to another *navi* but not to him — technically also *mah shelo shama*, since he himself received no such prophecy, but the *mishna* specifies this case because one might have thought that since it was a genuine divine communication to someone, it carries some legitimacy. The *pasuk* on which these are based is: *ach ha-navi asher yazid ledaber davar bishmi es asher lo tzivisiv ledaber* — the prophet who presumptuously speaks in my name what I did not command him to speak. Both categories fall under *mitat beis din*, execution by human agency.

Three other categories are left to divine judgment — *misatan bidei shamayim*. These are: one who suppresses his own prophecy (*ha-koveish nevuato*), one who violates the word of a *navi* (*ha-mavater al divrei navi*), and a *navi* who violates his own prophecy (*navi she'avar al divrei atzmo*). The derivation is from the *pasuk*: *ve-ha'ish asher lo yishma el devarai* — the man who does not hearken to my words — with the *gemara* reading it both ways: *lo yishma*, he does not obey, and *lo yashmia*, he does not cause them to be heard. Both acts — disobeying the *navi* and failing to transmit the prophecy — are addressed by the same verse, and the consequence is *anochi edrosh me'imo* — I, God, will seek it from him, indicating *mitat shamayim*.

The *mishna* then lists two additional categories of *chenek* that do not belong to the *navi ha-sheker* laws. One who prophesies in the name of *avodah zarah* — even if the content he delivers happens to align with correct *halacha* — is also subject to human execution. And one who commits adultery with a married woman (*haba al eishet ish*) is executed by *chenek* once the woman has formally entered the *reshut* of the husband for *nisuin*, even if the marriage has not yet been consummated. The special case of a *bat kohen* who commits adultery requires *sreifah* for her, while the adulterer still receives *chenek*, and *edim zomemin* who attempted to convict her receive *chenek* as well, since it is written *hi ba'esh tisaref* — only *she* is burned, excluding all the men involved, who revert to the baseline punishment of adultery.

### Scriptural Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה מִיתָתָן בִּידֵי אָדָם, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מִיתָתָן בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. הַמִּתְנַבֵּא מַה שֶּׁלֹּא שָׁמַע, וּמַה שֶּׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר לוֹ, וְהַמִּתְנַבֵּא בְּשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – מִיתָתָן בִּידֵי אָדָם. הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת נְבוּאָתוֹ, וְהַמְוַותֵּר עַל דִּבְרֵי נָבִיא, וְנָבִיא שֶׁעָבַר עַל דִּבְרֵי עַצְמוֹ – מִיתָתָן בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אַךְ הַנָּבִיא אֲשֶׁר יָזִיד לְדַבֵּר דָּבָר בִּשְׁמִי״. זֶה הַמִּתְנַבֵּא מַה שֶּׁלֹּא שָׁמַע. ״וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא צִוִּיתִיו״ – הָא לַחֲבֵירוֹ צִוִּיתִיו, זֶה הַמִּתְנַבֵּא מַה שֶּׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר לוֹ. ״וַאֲשֶׁר יְדַבֵּר בְּשֵׁם אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים״ – זֶה הַמִּתְנַבֵּא בְּשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וּכְתִיב: ״וּמֵת הַנָּבִיא הַהוּא״. וְכׇל מִיתָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּתוֹרָה סְתָם – אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא חֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* about the punishment of those who sin concerning prophecy: With regard to **three** of them, **their execution is at the hand of man, and** with regard to **three** of them, **their execution is at the hand of Heaven.** In the case of **one who prophesies that which he did not hear** from God, **and** one who prophesies **that which was not stated to him, and one who prophesies in the name of idol worship, their execution is at the hand of man.** In the case of **one who suppresses his prophecy, and one who** contemptuously **forgoes the statement of a prophet, and a prophet who violated his own statement, their execution is at the hand of Heaven.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says** that it is derived from a verse, **as the verse states:** "But the prophet who shall sin intentionally to speak a word in My name that I have not commanded him to speak, or who shall speak in the name of other gods, that prophet shall die" (Deuteronomy 18:20). The Gemara analyzes the verse: **"But the prophet who shall sin intentionally to speak a word in My name"; this** is a reference to **one who prophesies that which he did not hear.** The verse then states: **"That I have not commanded him,"** from which it is inferred: **But I did command another. This** is a reference to **one who prophesies that which was not stated to him. "Or who shall speak in the name of other gods"; this** is a reference to **one who prophesies in the name of idol worship. And it is written** with regard to all of these: **"That prophet shall die." And every death stated in the Torah without specification** is referring to **nothing other than strangulation.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת נְבוּאָתוֹ, וְהַמְוַותֵּר עַל דִּבְרֵי נָבִיא, וְנָבִיא שֶׁעָבַר עַל דִּבְרֵי עַצְמוֹ – מִיתָתָן בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִשְׁמַע״. קְרִי בֵּיהּ: ״לֹא יַשְׁמִיעַ״, וּקְרִי בֵּיהּ: ״לֹא יִשְׁמַע אֶל דְּבָרַי״. וּכְתִיב: ״אָנֹכִי אֶדְרֹשׁ מֵעִמּוֹ״ – בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **One who suppresses his prophecy, and one who** contemptuously **forgoes the statement of a prophet, and a prophet who violated his own statement, their execution is at the hand of Heaven, as it is written:** "And it shall come to pass, that **whosoever shall not hearken [*yishma*] unto My words** that he shall speak in My name, I will exact from him" (Deuteronomy 18:19). The reference is to one who forgoes the statement of a prophet. **Read into** the verse: Who **shall not voice [*yashmia*];** the reference is to one who suppresses the prophecy. **And read into** the verse: **Shall not heed [*yishama*];** the reference is to one who violated his own statement. **And it is written** with regard to all of these: **"I will exact from him,"** meaning he is liable to receive death **at the hand of Heaven.**

The *baraisa* then maps each category to its scriptural source, tracking closely with the *gemara's* exposition of the same verse. The phrase *et asher lo tzivisiv ledaber* — what I did not command him — is read to mean: I did not command *him*, though I may have commanded a colleague. *Lachaveiro tzivitiv* — I commanded his friend — establishes the second category of one who transmits another's prophecy as his own. The phrase *va'asher yedaber beshem elohim acherim* covers the case of prophesying in the name of *avodah zarah*. And the conclusion *umeis hanavi hahu* — that *navi* shall die — is a *stam misah* in the *Torah*, which by convention means *chenek*.

### Tzidkiyah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמִּתְנַבֵּא מַה שֶּׁלֹּא שָׁמַע, כְּגוֹן צִדְקִיָּה בֶן כְּנַעֲנָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַשׂ לוֹ צִדְקִיָּהוּ בֶן כְּנַעֲנָה קַרְנֵי בַרְזֶל״. מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד? רוּחַ נָבוֹת אַטְעִיתֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ מִי יְפַתֶּה אֶת אַחְאָב וְיַעַל וְיִפֹּל בְּרָמֹת גִּלְעָד״. ״וַיֵּצֵא הָרוּחַ וַיַּעֲמֹד לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַיֹּאמֶר אֲנִי אֲפַתֶּנּוּ״. ״וַיֹּאמֶר תְּפַתֶּה וְגַם תּוּכָל צֵא וַעֲשֵׂה כֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna lists among those liable to be executed as a false prophet **one who prophesies that which** he **did not hear.** The Gemara comments: **For example, Zedekiah, son of Chenaanah,** who prophesied that Ahab should wage war against the kingdom of Aram and would be successful, **as it is written: "And Zedekiah, son of Chenaanah, made for him horns of iron,** and said: So says the Lord: With these shall you gore the Arameans, until they are consumed" (II Chronicles 18:10). The Gemara asks: **What was** Zedekiah, son of Chenaanah, **to do?** Why was he held responsible? After all, **the spirit of Naboth misled him, as it is written: "And the Lord said: Who shall entice Ahab, that he may go up and fall at Ramoth Gilead…and there came forth the spirit, and stood before the Lord, and said: I will entice him. And He said…You shall entice him, and shall prevail also; go forth, and do so"** (I Kings 22:20–22).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מַאי ״צֵא״? צֵא מִמְּחִיצָתִי. מַאי רוּחַ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רוּחוֹ שֶׁל נָבוֹת הַיִּזְרְעֵאלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: What** is the meaning of the apparently unnecessary term: **"Go forth"?** Could the Lord not have sufficed with telling the spirit: Do so? The term means: **Go forth from My proximity.** Since the spirit volunteered to engage in deceit, it was no longer fit to stand in proximity to God. **What** is the identity of the **spirit** that undertook to entice Ahab? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It was **the spirit of Naboth the Jezreelite,** who was eager to take revenge upon Ahab.

The *gemara* identifies *Tzidkiyah ben Kena'anah* as the paradigmatic case of one who prophesies what he never heard. As described in *Tanach*, he fashioned iron horns and declared *ko tenagach et Aram* — so shall you gore Aram — presenting this as divine prophecy to *Achav*. The difficulty is immediate: *mai hava leh le-me'evad*? How can *Tzidkiyah* be held liable if he apparently received some vision or spirit that led him to believe he had a genuine prophecy?

The *gemara* provides the context. God asked which spirit would seduce *Achav* to go up and fall in battle at *Ramot Gil'ad*. A spirit came forward and said *ani afatenu* — I will seduce him. God said *tzei va'aseh chen* — go and do so. *Rav Yehuda* explains the word *tzei*: *tzei me-mechitzati* — depart from my domain — indicating that while God permitted this false spirit to act, it was not acting on God's behalf. *Rebbi Yochanan* identifies this spirit as *ruach Navot ha-Yizre'eli*, the spirit of *Navot* whom *Achav* had murdered, now returning to bring about *Achav's* downfall in battle.

The problem, then, is one of culpability: *Tzidkiyah* believed himself to be a genuine recipient of divine prophecy. How can he be punished?

### Signon Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵידַק, כִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: סִיגְנוֹן אֶחָד עוֹלֶה לְכַמָּה נְבִיאִים, וְאֵין שְׁנֵי נְבִיאִים מִתְנַבְּאִים בְּסִיגְנוֹן אֶחָד. עוֹבַדְיָה אָמַר: ״זְדוֹן לִבְּךָ הִשִּׁיאֶךָ״. יִרְמְיָה אָמַר: ״תִּפְלַצְתְּךָ הִשִּׁיא אֹתָךְ זְדוֹן לִבֶּךָ״. וְהָנֵי, מִדְּקָאָמְרִי כּוּלְּהוּ כַּהֲדָדֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא כְּלוּם קָאָמְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It was** incumbent **upon** Zedekiah **to discern** between actual prophecies and false prophecies, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** A prophetic vision relating to **one** and the same **subject matter [*sigenon*] may appear to several prophets, but two prophets do not prophesy** employing **one** and the same **style** of expression. An example of identical content expressed in different styles is the prophecy where **Obadiah said: "The pride of your heart has beguiled you"** (Obadiah 1:3), and **Jeremiah said** a similar message employing slightly different language: **"Your terribleness has deceived you, even the pride of your heart"** (Jeremiah 49:16). **And** with regard to **these** false prophets, **from** the fact **that** in their case **all** the prophets **are saying** their prophecies **like each other,** i.e., employing an identical style, **conclude from it** that **they are saying nothing** of substance and that it is a false prophecy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא לָא הֲוָה יָדַע לֵיהּ לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק? יְהוֹשָׁפָט הֲוָה הָתָם, וְקָאָמַר לְהוּ. דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוֹשָׁפָט הַאֵין פֹּה נָבִיא עוֹד לַה׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אִיכָּא כֹּל הָנֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִבֵּית אֲבִי אַבָּא: סִיגְנוֹן אֶחָד עוֹלֶה לְכַמָּה נְבִיאִים, וְאֵין שְׁנֵי נְבִיאִים מִתְנַבְּאִים בְּסִיגְנוֹן אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Perhaps** Zedekiah, son of Chenaanah, **did not know this** statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak,** and therefore should not have been held responsible? The Gemara answers: **Jehoshaphat,** king of Judea, **was there, and he said to them** that they were false prophets, **as it is written: "But Jehoshaphat said: Is there not here besides a prophet of the Lord,** that we might inquire of him?" (I Kings 22:7). Ahab **said to him: But aren't there all these** prophets here? Why seek others? Jehoshaphat **said to** Ahab: **This is** the tradition that **I received from the house of the father of my father,** the house of David: A prophetic vision relating to **one** and the same **subject matter may appear to several prophets, but two prophets do not prophesy** employing **one** and the same **style** of expression. They are false prophets, as they employed the same language when stating their prophecy.

The *gemara* answers that *Tzidkiyah* should have attended to the teaching of *Rebbi Yitzchak*: *signon echad oleh le-chama nevi'im ve-ein shenei nevi'im mitnab'im be-signon echad* — a single theme or content may be given to many prophets, but two prophets never prophesy in precisely the same language. Each *navi* receives a prophecy refracted through his own personality and intellect, and the expression is always uniquely his own. This is demonstrated from the comparison of *Ovadia* and *Yirmiyah*, who each prophesied the theme of *zdon libcha hishi'acha* — the brazenness of your heart has misled you — but with different precise formulations.

In the episode of *Achav*, all the prophets were delivering their prophecy in exactly the same words. This linguistic uniformity should itself have been a signal that what they were saying was not genuine prophecy. The *gemara* questions the anachronism — how would *Tzidkiyah* have known *Rebbi Yitzchak's* teaching? — and answers that *Yehoshaphat* was present and was already articulating this very principle. As the *pasuk* records, *Yehoshaphat* asked *ha'ein po navi la-Hashem od* — is there not another true prophet of God here? — and when challenged, he explained: *kach mekubbani mibeis avi abba, signon echad oleh le-chama nevi'im ve-ein shenei nevi'im mitnab'im be-signon echad*. This was his received tradition from his ancestral house.

One might still ask whether this logical awareness is sufficient to establish *mezid* — intentional wrongdoing — for one who had a genuine experiential sense of prophetic reception. The *gemara* does not resolve this tension cleanly, but the implication appears to be that since *Tzidkiyah* should have recognized, on the basis of the known principle, that the identical language of all the prophets was a disqualifying sign, he is held to have spoken knowingly and is thus liable.

### Chananya Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמִּתְנַבֵּא מַה שֶּׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר לוֹ: כְּגוֹן חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עַזּוּר, דְּקָאֵי יִרְמְיָה בְּשׁוּק הָעֶלְיוֹן וְקָאָמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ [צְבָאוֹת] הִנְנִי שֹׁבֵר אֶת קֶשֶׁת עֵילָם״. נָשָׂא חֲנַנְיָה קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמוֹ: מָה עֵילָם שֶׁלֹּא בָּא אֶלָּא לַעֲזוֹר אֶת בָּבֶל אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא ״הִנְנִי שֹׁבֵר אֶת קֶשֶׁת עֵילָם״, כַּשְׂדִּים עַצְמָן עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אֲתָא אִיהוּ בְּשׁוּק הַתַּחְתּוֹן אֲמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ וְגוֹ׳ שָׁבַרְתִּי אֶת עֹל מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: הַאי לַחֲבֵירוֹ נָמֵי לֹא נֶאֱמַר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיתְיְהִיב קַל וָחוֹמֶר לְמִידְרַשׁ, כְּמַאן דְּאִיתְּמַר לֵיהּ דָּמֵי. הוּא נִיהוּ דְּלֹא נֶאֱמַר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna lists among those liable to be executed as a false prophet **one who prophesies that which was not stated to him.** The Gemara comments: **For example, Hananiah, son of Azzur, as Jeremiah was standing in the upper market** in Jerusalem **and saying: "So says the Lord of hosts: Behold, I will break the bow of Elam,** the chief of their might" (Jeremiah 49:35). **Hananiah raised an *a fortiori*** inference **himself: If,** with regard to **Elam, which came only to assist Babylonia, the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: "Behold, I will break the bow of Elam," all the more so** are **the Chaldeans,** i.e., the Babylonians, **themselves,** destined to fall. Hananiah **came to the lower market** and **said: "So speaks the Lord** of hosts, the God of Israel, saying: **I have broken the yoke of the king of Babylon"** (Jeremiah 28:2). As a result, Hananiah, son of Azzur, was killed at the hand of Heaven (Jeremiah 28:16). **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** Referring to this case as one who prophesies that which was not said to him is not precise, as not only was it not said to Hananiah, **neither was this** prophecy **stated to another** prophet. Abaye **said to him: Since** this matter **can be inferred** by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference, **it is like** a prophecy **that was said to** Jeremiah, as inferring matters *a fortiori* is a legitimate manner of derivation. It is Hananiah who misrepresented the prophecy, **as it was not stated to him,** i.e., to Hananiah, and then he presented the prophecy as though he heard it from God.

The case of *mah shelo ne'emar lo* — prophesying something told to another — is illustrated by *Chananya ben Azur*. *Yirmiyah* had stood in the upper marketplace and declared in the name of God: *hineni shover es keshes Elam* — I will break the bow of Elam — one of the nations that had joined *Bavel* in its campaign against Israel. *Chananya* then proceeded to the lower marketplace and declared, on his own account: *koh amar Hashem shavarti es ol melech Bavel* — so says God, I have broken the yoke of the king of *Bavel*.

*Rav Pappa* challenges *Abaye*: this does not quite fit the category, because what *Yirmiyah* heard was about *Elam*, not about *Bavel*. So *Chananya* was not simply transmitting *Yirmiyah's* prophecy — he made a separate claim that was not said to *Yirmiyah* either. *Abaye* responds: since the *kal vachomer* was fully available — if God will break the bow of *Elam*, which came only to assist *Bavel*, then how much more so will God break *Bavel* itself — *keivan d'isyahiv lei kal vachomer le-midrash, ke-man de'is'mar lei dami*. It is as though it were explicitly said to *Yirmiyah*. Accordingly, *Chananya's* error lies not in constructing an unreasonable inference, but in presenting his own logical derivation as though it were a received divine prophecy — and that is precisely what makes him a *navi sheker*.

### Suppression Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת נְבוּאָתוֹ: כְּגוֹן יוֹנָה בֶּן אֲמִיתַּי. וְהַמְוַותֵּר עַל דִּבְרֵי נָבִיא: כְּגוֹן תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת נְבוּאָתוֹ לוֹקֶה. אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מַאן דְּאָכֵיל תַּמְרֵי בְּאַרְבְּלָא לָקֵי? מַאן מַתְרֵי בֵּיהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: חַבְרֵיהּ נְבִיאֵי. מְנָא יָדְעִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה ה׳ [אֱלֹהִים] דָּבָר כִּי אִם גָּלָה סוֹדוֹ״. וְדִילְמָא הֲדַרוּ בֵּיהּ? אִם אִיתָא דְּהָדְרִי בֵּיהּ, אוֹדוֹעֵי הֲווֹ מוֹדְעִי לְכֻלְּהוּ נְבִיאֵי. וְהָא יוֹנָה, דַּהֲדַרוּ בֵּיהּ וְלָא אוֹדְעוּהּ? יוֹנָה מֵעִיקָּרָא ״נִינְוֵה נֶהְפָּכֶת״ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ. אִיהוּ לָא יְדַע אִי לְטוֹבָה אִי לְרָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna lists among those liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven **one who suppresses his prophecy.** The Gemara comments: **For example, Jonah, son of Amittai** (Jonah 1:1–3). The mishna also lists: **And one who forgoes the statement of a prophet.** The Gemara comments: **For example,** § **A *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Ḥisda: One who suppresses his prophecy is flogged.** Rav Ḥisda **said** a parable **to him: Is one who eats dates in a sieve flogged?** How can he be flogged? **Who forewarns him** if he eats dates infested with worms that no one sees? Likewise, as no one knows whether the prophet received a prophecy, how can he be forewarned? **Abaye said: His fellow prophets** forewarn him. The Gemara asks: **From where do they know** that he received a prophecy? **Abaye says:** They know, **as it is written: "For the Lord God will do nothing, unless He reveals His counsel** to His servants the prophets" (Amos 3:7). The Gemara challenges: **And perhaps** the heavenly court **reconsidered with regard to** the prophecy, and the prophet is no longer commanded to disseminate the prophecy. The Gemara answers: **If it is so that they reconsidered with regard to** the prophecy, the heavenly court **would have informed all the prophets.** The Gemara challenges: **But** in the case of **Jonah, they reconsidered it and did not inform them** that the people of Nineveh had repented for their sins and were therefore spared the foretold destruction. The Gemara explains: In the case of **Jonah, from the outset,** the heavenly court **told him** to say: **"Nineveh will be overturned"** (Jonah 3:4). Still, **he did not know if** the sentence would be **for the good,** as their corruption would be overturned through repentance, **or** if it would be **for the bad,** as the city would be overturned through destruction. Therefore, the prophecy was never revoked, but simply fulfilled in accordance with one of its possible interpretations.

The *gemara* addresses the case of *ha-koveish nevuato* — suppressing prophecy — and cites *Yonah ben Amittai* as the example, who fled rather than deliver his prophecy. A *Tanna* who studied before *Rav Chisda* stated that one who suppresses his prophecy incurs *malkus*. *Rav Chisda* pressed back with a practical objection: *man de'achal tamre be-arba lo lakei* — who could possibly issue a warning? How can one be forewarned and thus liable for *malkus* for an act that is both passive and entirely private in its occurrence?

*Abaye* resolves this: *chavrei nevi'ei* — his fellow prophets would know, because *ki lo ya'aseh Hashem Elokim davar ki im gila sodo el avadav ha-nevi'im* — God does not act without first revealing His secret to His servants the prophets. Thus, a colleague who received the same awareness of the prophecy could, in principle, issue a forewarning. The *gemara* raises a further question: perhaps God had retracted the prophecy, which would explain why it was not delivered. The response is that if a retraction had occurred, all the fellow prophets who knew of the original prophecy would also have been informed of its retraction. The case of *Yonah* is then raised as a seeming counterexample — *Yonah* remained outside *Ninveh* to see what would happen even after the people repented, suggesting he was not informed of the shift. The *gemara* answers that the original prophecy *ninveh nehepaches* — *Ninveh* will be overturned — was inherently ambiguous. *Yonah* did not know whether it meant the city would be destroyed or the city's conduct would be overturned in repentance. There was no retraction; rather, there was a clarification of the original prophecy's meaning.

### Michah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְוַותֵּר עַל דִּבְרֵי נָבִיא: כְּגוֹן חַבְרֵיהּ דְּמִיכָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאִישׁ אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי הַנְּבִיאִים אָמַר אֶל רֵעֵהוּ בִּדְבַר ה׳ הַכֵּינִי נָא, וַיְמָאֵן הָאִישׁ לְהַכּוֹתוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ יַעַן אֲשֶׁר לֹא שָׁמַעְתָּ [וְגוֹ׳]״.
>
> **Translation:** **And one who forgoes the statement of a prophet.** The Gemara comments: **For example,** the colleague of the prophet **Micah,** son of Imla (see II Chronicles 18:7–8), **as it is written: "And a certain man of the sons of the prophets said unto his colleague by the word of the Lord: Strike me, please. And the man refused to strike him"** (I Kings 20:35). **And it is written: "Then he said to him: Because you have not listened** to the voice of the Lord, behold, as soon as you leave me, a lion shall slay you. And as soon as he left from him, a lion found him; and slew him" (I Kings 20:36).

The paradigmatic case of *ha-mavater al divrei navi* — one who violates the word of a prophet — is drawn from the episode in *Tanach* where *ish echad mibnei ha-nevi'im* asked his fellow to strike him. The *gemara* understands this figure to be *Micha*: he sought to appear beaten so that he could deliver a message to *Achav* with appropriate dramatic weight. His colleague refused, even though a *navi* had directed him. The *pasuk* records that this man was subsequently killed by a lion — an expression of divine punishment for failing to obey the word of a prophet.

### Ido HaNavi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנָבִיא שֶׁעָבַר עַל דִּבְרֵי עַצְמוֹ: כְּגוֹן עִדּוֹ הַנָּבִיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי כֵן צִוָּה אֹתִי״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ גַּם אֲנִי נָבִיא כָּמוֹךָ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּשׇׁב אִתּוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ וַיִּמְצָאֵהוּ אַרְיֵה״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna lists among those liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven: **And a prophet who violated his own statement.** The Gemara comments: **For example, Iddo the prophet,** who, according to tradition, prophesied the punishment of Jeroboam in Bethel, **as it is written:** "I will neither eat bread nor drink water in this place, **for it so was commanded me** by the word of the Lord" (I Kings 13:8–9). **And it is written: "And he said to him: I too am a prophet like you;** and an angel spoke unto me by the word of the Lord, saying: Bring him back with you into your house, that he may eat bread and drink water" (I Kings 13:18). **And it is written: "And he went back with him,** and ate bread in his house, and drank water" (I Kings 13:19). **And it is written** that he died at the hand of Heaven: **"And he went, and a lion met him** by the way, and killed him" (I Kings 13:24).

The case of *navi she'avar al divrei atzmo* — a prophet who violated his own words — is *Ido HaNavi*. He had declared by divine command that he would neither eat bread nor drink water, and an individual claiming also to be a prophet told him: *gam ani navi kamocha* — I too am a prophet like you — and instructed him that it was permissible to eat and drink. Despite knowing what God had commanded him, *Ido* listened to this other man and ate. The *pasuk* records that he too was found and killed by a lion on his journey home. The lion is the instrument of divine punishment — *bidei shamayim* — in both this case and the case of *Micha's* colleague, while *Yonah*, who suppressed his prophecy, was swallowed by a fish before ultimately being spared.

### Proving Navi Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְוַותֵּר עַל דִּבְרֵי נָבִיא, מְנָא יָדַע דְּאִיעֲנַשׁ? דִּיהַב לֵיהּ אוֹת. וְהָא מִיכָה, דְּלָא יְהַב לֵיהּ אוֹת, וְאִיעֲנַשׁ? הֵיכָא דְּמוּחְזָק שָׁאנֵי. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, אַבְרָהָם בְּהַר הַמּוֹרִיָּה, הֵיכִי שְׁמַע לֵיהּ יִצְחָק? אֵלִיָּהוּ בְּהַר הַכַּרְמֶל, הֵיכִי סָמְכִי עֲלֵיהּ וְעָבְדִי שְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ? אֶלָּא הֵיכָא דְּמוּחְזָק שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In the case of **one who forgoes the statement of a prophet, from where does he know** that the one prophesying is actually a prophet and **that he** will be **punished** for failing to comply with the prophecy? The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **where** the prophet **provides him** with **a sign** indicating that he is a prophet. The Gemara asks: **But** in the case of **Micah, who did not provide him** with **a sign, and** yet he was **punished,** how could he have known that Micah was a prophet? A case **where** he has already **assumed** the **presumptive status** of a prophet **is different,** and no sign is necessary. The Gemara continues: **Since if you do not say so,** and claim that even the prophecy of one established as a prophet requires a sign, then in the case of **Abraham at Mount Moriah, how did Isaac listen to him** and submit to being slaughtered as an offering? Likewise, in the case of **Elijah at Mount Carmel, how did** the people **rely upon him and** enable him to **engage** in the sacrifice of animals **slaughtered outside** the Temple, which is prohibited? **Rather,** perforce, a case **where** he has already **assumed** the **presumptive status** of a prophet **is different.**

The *gemara* transitions to a broader and striking question: how does one establish that a person is genuinely a *navi*, such that violating his words carries the halachic consequence of *mitat shamayim*? The *gemara* suggests initially that the answer lies in signs (*os*) — the *Torah* indicates that a prophet who gives a sign that is fulfilled can be treated as genuine. But the case of *Micha's* colleague poses a problem: *Micha* told the man to strike him without providing any sign to prove his prophetic status. How was the man then obligated to listen?

The *gemara* answers: *heicha de-muchzak shani* — where one is already established as a *navi*, the rules are different. If a person is *muchzak* as a prophet, he need not produce a sign for each individual instruction; his prior status carries the obligation.

*Rashi* explains that *muchzak* here means that the person is known to be a *tzaddik* and a *navi emes* — righteous and a true prophet. How does one acquire that status? *Rashi* implies that it comes from a track record of predictions that proved accurate, even if these do not meet the technical definition of a wondrous sign. *Rambam* engages similarly with the question of how character and prior prophetic performance combine to establish *muchzak* status, though the precise threshold remains open to interpretation.

The *gemara* then reinforces the principle with two famous cases. *Ve-ei lo teima hachi, Avraham be-Har HaMoriah heicha shama lei Yitzchak* — if not for the *muchzak* principle, how did *Yitzchak* consent to being slaughtered? *Avraham* had not produced a sign in any conventional sense. Yet *Avraham's* standing as someone in whom divine communication was manifest was sufficient for *Yitzchak* to accept his father's instruction. Similarly, *Eliyahu be-Har HaKarmel* directed the people to bring a *korban* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, an act that would otherwise constitute a severe *issur de'oraisa* of *shechutei chutz*. They listened because *Eliyahu* was *muchzak* as a prophet, and a *navi muchzak* may instruct a temporary deviation from a prohibition. The *gemara* concludes: *ela heicha de-muchzak shani* — it is the established status of the prophet that creates the obligation to listen, even absent a contemporaneous sign.

## Akeidah Narrative

### Satan's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי אַחַר הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה וְהָאֱלֹהִים נִסָּה אֶת אַבְרָהָם״, אַחַר מַאי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן זִימְרָא: אַחַר דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל שָׂטָן, דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּגְדַּל הַיֶּלֶד וַיִּגָּמַל וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר שָׂטָן לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, זָקֵן זֶה חֲנַנְתּוֹ לְמֵאָה שָׁנָה פְּרִי בֶטֶן, מִכׇּל סְעוּדָה שֶׁעָשָׂה לֹא הָיָה לוֹ תּוֹר אֶחָד אוֹ גּוֹזָל אֶחָד לְהַקְרִיב לְפָנֶיךָ? אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם עָשָׂה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל בְּנוֹ? אִם אֲנִי אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״זְבַח אֶת בִּנְךָ לְפָנַי״ מִיָּד זוֹבְחוֹ. מִיָּד ״וְהָאֱלֹהִים נִסָּה אֶת אַבְרָהָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the binding of Isaac, the Gemara elaborates: It is written: **"And it came to pass after these matters [*hadevarim*] that God tried Abraham"** (Genesis 22:1). The Gemara asks: **After what** matters? How does the binding of Isaac relate to the preceding events? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Zimra:** This means **after the statement [*devarav*] of Satan, as it is written: "And the child grew, and was weaned,** and Abraham prepared a great feast on the day that Isaac was weaned" (Genesis 21:8). **Satan said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, this old man, you favored him with a product of the womb,** i.e., a child, **at one hundred years** of age. **From the entire feast that he prepared, did he not have** even **one dove or one pigeon to sacrifice before You** as a thanks-offering? God **said to** Satan: **Did** Abraham **prepare** the feast for any reason **but for his son? If I say to him: Sacrifice your son before Me,** he would **immediately slaughter him. Immediately,** after these matters, the verse states: **"And God tried Abraham."**

Having mentioned *Avraham* at *Har HaMoriah*, the *gemara* digresses to explore the *Akeidah* narrative through *midrashic* elaboration. *Rebbi Yochanan* in the name of *Rebbi Yosi ben Zimra* asks: what were the *devarim* — "things" — that preceded the divine test of *Avraham*? The verse states *vayehi achar ha-devarim ha-eileh ve-ha-Elokim nissah es Avraham* — and it was after these things that God tested *Avraham*. The answer: *achar devarav shel Satan* — after the words of the *Satan*.

The background is the feast *Avraham* made upon the weaning of *Yitzchak*. The *Satan* approached God and objected: this elderly man, granted the fruit of his womb after a hundred years, made a great celebration — but from the entire feast did not bring even a single turtledove or young bird as a *korban* before God. Is this how he shows gratitude? God replied: *kelum asah ela bishvil beno* — everything he does is for the sake of his son. If I were to ask him to sacrifice his son, *mi-yad zovcho* — he would agree immediately. And immediately: *ve-ha-Elokim nissah es Avraham*.

### Mashal Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר קַח נָא אֶת בִּנְךָ״. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר אַבָּא: אֵין ״נָא״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן בַּקָּשָׁה. מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁעָמְדוּ עָלָיו מִלְחָמוֹת הַרְבֵּה, וְהָיָה לוֹ גִּבּוֹר אֶחָד וְנִצְּחָן. לְיָמִים עָמְדָה עָלָיו מִלְחָמָה חֲזָקָה. אָמַר לוֹ: בְּבַקָּשָׁה מִמְּךָ, עֲמוֹד לִי בְּמִלְחָמָה זוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ רִאשׁוֹנוֹת אֵין בָּהֶם מַמָּשׁ. אַף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אָמַר לְאַבְרָהָם: נִיסֵּיתִיךָ בְּכַמָּה נִסְיוֹנוֹת וְעָמַדְתָּ בְּכֻלָּן. עַכְשָׁיו עֲמוֹד לִי בְּנִסָּיוֹן זֶה, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ אֵין מַמָּשׁ בָּרִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah continues: **"And He said: Take, please [*na*], your son"** (Genesis 22:2). **Rabbi Shimon bar Abba says:** The word ***na* is nothing other than an expression of entreaty.** Why did God request rather than command that Abraham take his son? The Gemara cites **a parable of a flesh-and-blood king who confronted many wars. And he had one warrior** fighting for him, **and he overcame** his enemies. **Over time,** there was **a fierce war confronting him.** The king **said to** his warrior: **I entreat you, stand** firm **for me in this war,** so **that** others **will not say: There is no substance in the first** victories, and you are not a true warrior. Likewise, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, also said to Abraham: I have tried you with several ordeals, and you have withstood them all. Now, stand** firm **in this ordeal for Me, so that** others **will not say: There is no substance in the first** ordeals.

*Rebbi Shimon ben Abba* explains the word *na* in the phrase *kach na et bincha* — please take your son. The word *na* is a language of entreaty, of request. He offers a *mashal*: a human king who had one great warrior who helped him prevail in many wars. When a particularly difficult battle arose, the king said to him: "Please, stand with me in this battle" — not because the general had ever failed, but so that no one could say the previous victories were easy and meant little. God similarly said to *Avraham*: "I have tested you with many tests and you have withstood them all. Now please, stand with me in this one, so that no one will say the earlier tests had no real substance."

### God's Command

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶת בִּנְךָ״. שְׁנֵי בָּנִים יֵשׁ לִי. ״אֶת יְחִידְךָ״. זֶה יָחִיד לְאִמּוֹ וְזֶה יָחִיד לְאִמּוֹ. ״אֲשֶׁר אָהַבְתָּ״. תַּרְוַיְיהוּ רָחֵימְנָא לְהוּ. ״אֶת יִצְחָק״. וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּטָּרֵף דַּעְתּוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** God said to Abraham: "Please take your son, your only, whom you love, Isaac" (Genesis 22:2). When God said: **"Your son,"** Abraham said: **I have two sons.** When God said: **"Your only,"** Abraham said: **This** son **is an only** son **to his mother, and that** son **is an only** son **to his mother.** When God said: **"Whom you love,"** Abraham said: **I love both of them.** Then God said: **"Isaac." And why** did God prolong His command to **that extent?** Why did He not say Isaac's name from the outset? God did so, **so that** Abraham's **mind would not be confused** by the trauma.

The command unfolds incrementally. *Kach et bincha* — take your son. *Avraham* replied: I have two sons — *Yishmael* and *Yitzchak*. *Et yechidecha* — your only son. But each is unique to his mother, *ze yachid le'immo veze yachid le'immo*. *Asher ahavta* — the one you love. *Teravyehu rachmina aleihon* — I love both of them. The beauty of this exchange, as the *midrash* presents it, reveals something profound about *Avraham's* character: even after being commanded to sacrifice *Yitzchak*, *Avraham* does not distance himself emotionally from *Yishmael*. He acknowledges his full love for both sons. Finally, God says *et Yitzchak* — *Yitzchak*.

### Incremental Disclosure

Why the drawn-out exchange? *Kedai shelo titaref da'ato alav* — so that *Avraham* not be overwhelmed and disoriented by the shock of the command all at once. The gradual disclosure allows *Avraham* time to internally acclimate to what is being asked of him, so that he can proceed with full composure rather than in a state of psychological collapse.

### Satan's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְדָמוֹ שָׂטָן לַדֶּרֶךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: ״הֲנִסָּה דָבָר אֵלֶיךָ תִּלְאֶה... הִנֵּה יִסַּרְתָּ רַבִּים וְיָדַיִם רָפוֹת תְּחַזֵּק. כּוֹשֵׁל יְקִימוּן מִלֶּיךָ... כִּי עַתָּה תָּבוֹא אֵלֶיךָ וַתֵּלֶא״. אָמַר לוֹ: ״אֲנִי בְּתֻמִּי אֵלֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Satan preceded** Abraham **to the path** that he took to bind his son and **said to him: "If one ventures a word to you, will you be weary…you have instructed many, and you have strengthened the weak hands. Your words have upheld him that was falling…but now it comes upon you, and you are weary"** (Job 4:2–5). Do you now regret what you are doing? Abraham **said to him** in response: "And **I will walk with my integrity"** (Psalms 26:11).

As *Avraham* set out toward *Har Moriah* with *Yitzchak*, the *Satan* moved to intercept him. He cited verses from *Iyov*: *hanissa davar eleicha tileh* — will a matter be tested against you and you will grow weary? *Hinneh yissarta rabbim ve-yadayim rafot techazzek* — behold you have instructed many and strengthened weak hands. *Koshel yekimun milecha* — your words have lifted up those who would stumble. *Ki atta tavo eleicha ve-tileh* — now it has come upon you and you are weary.

In context, these are words spoken to *Iyov* by a companion, urging him not to lose faith after having strengthened so many others. The *midrash* reads the *Satan* as deploying these verses as a challenge to *Avraham*: you have been the pillar of faith for so many, strengthening others — and now God tests you with this terrible demand? After all you have sacrificed and built? The *Satan* may also be suggesting that the *Akeidah* will undermine the very ethical and spiritual edifice that *Avraham* has spent his life constructing.

### Abraham's Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שָׂטָן לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא... אָמַר לוֹ: כָּךְ עוֹנְשׁוֹ שֶׁל בַּדַּאי, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ אָמַר אֱמֶת אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Satan **said to** Abraham: Perhaps that is so. However, **this is the punishment of the liar, that even** if **he speaks the truth,** others **do not listen to him.**

*Avraham* replied: *ani be-tummi elech* — I walk in my integrity. His response is the inverse of *Iyov's*: where *Iyov* demanded justification, *Avraham* accepted without question. God has asked; he will do it. Doubt may exist, the logic may be elusive, but his commitment is absolute.

### Satan's Deception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: ״הֲלֹא יִרְאָתְךָ כִּסְלָתֶךָ״. אָמַר לוֹ: ״זְכׇר נָא מִי הוּא נָקִי אָבָד״. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא דְּלָא קָא שָׁמַיע לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְאֵלַי דָּבָר יְגֻנָּב״. כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַפַּרְגּוֹד: ״הַשֶּׂה לְעוֹלָה״ וְאֵין יִצְחָק לְעוֹלָה. אָמַר לוֹ: כָּךְ עוֹנְשׁוֹ שֶׁל בַּדַּאי, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ אָמַר אֱמֶת אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Satan **said to** Abraham: **"Is not your fear of God your foolishness?"** (Job 4:6). In other words, your fear will culminate in the slaughter of your son. Abraham **said to him: "Remember, please, whoever perished, being innocent"** (Job 4:7). God is righteous and His pronouncements are just. **Once** Satan **saw that** Abraham **was not heeding him, he said to him: "Now a word was secretly brought to me,** and my ear received a whisper thereof" (Job 4:12). **This** is what **I heard from behind the** heavenly **curtain [*pargod*],** which demarcates between God and the ministering angels: **The sheep is to** be sacrificed as **a burnt-offering, and Isaac is not to** be sacrificed as **a burnt-offering.** Abraham **said to him:** Perhaps that is so. However, **this is the punishment of the liar, that even** if **he speaks the truth,** others **do not listen to him.** Therefore, I do not believe you and will fulfill that which I was commanded to perform.

When the *Satan* saw that this approach had not deterred *Avraham*, he changed tactics. He said: *ve'elai davar yugnav* — a thing was stolen to me, I received a kind of secret. *Kach shamati me'achorei ha-pargod* — this is what I heard from behind the heavenly curtain: *hasseh le'olah ve'ein Yitzchak le'olah* — it is a sheep that will be brought as the *olah*, and *Yitzchak* will not actually be sacrificed. The *Satan's* intent here was apparently to deflate the value of the test — if *Avraham* proceeded believing that at the end there would be no actual sacrifice of *Yitzchak*, his compliance would not constitute the same level of *emunah* and sacrifice.

*Avraham* responded: *kach onsho shel badai, afilu omer emet ein shom'in lo* — this is the punishment of a liar: even when he speaks the truth, no one believes him. I know what God asked of me; I will not be swayed by what you claim to have overheard. *Nechama Leibowitz* observes, in a related vein, that the *Satan* in this and similar *midrashim* — including one in which the *Satan* warns *Avraham* that the world will call him a murderer — may be understood as an externalization of *Avraham's* own inner turmoil and conflict. The *midrash* does not simply portray an untroubled march to *Har Moriah*, but rather gives form to the genuine anguish and struggle that any such test would involve. Indeed, without some measure of internal resistance, the concept of a test would be empty.

### Ishmael's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי לֵוִי אָמַר: אַחַר דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל יִשְׁמָעֵאל לְיִצְחָק. אָמַר לוֹ יִשְׁמָעֵאל לְיִצְחָק: אֲנִי גָּדוֹל מִמְּךָ בְּמִצְווֹת, שֶׁאַתָּה מַלְתָּ בֶּן שְׁמֹנַת יָמִים, וַאֲנִי בֶּן שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה. אָמַר לוֹ: וּבְאֵבֶר אֶחָד אַתָּה מְגָרֶה בִּי? אִם אוֹמֵר לִי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא ״זְבַח עַצְמְךָ לְפָנַי״, אֲנִי זוֹבֵחַ. מִיָּד: ״וְהָאֱלֹהִים נִסָּה אֶת אַבְרָהָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternative explanation of the verse: "And it came to pass after these matters that God tried Abraham" (Genesis 22:1). **Rabbi Levi says:** This means **after the statement of Ishmael to Isaac,** during an exchange between them described in the verse: "And Sarah saw the son of Hagar…mocking" (Genesis 21:9). **Ishmael said to Isaac: I am greater than you in** the fulfillment of **mitzvot, as you were circumcised** at the **age of eight days,** without your knowledge and without your consent, **and I** was circumcised at the **age of thirteen years,** with both my knowledge and my consent. Isaac **said to** Ishmael: **And do you provoke me with one organ? If the Holy One, Blessed be He,** were to **say to me: Sacrifice yourself before Me, I** would **sacrifice** myself. **Immediately, God tried Abraham,** to confirm that Isaac was sincere in his offer to give his life.

*Rebbi Levi* offers an alternative reading of *vayehi achar ha-devarim ha-eileh*. In his view, it refers not to the words of the *Satan* but to *achar devarav shel Yishmael* — after the words of *Yishmael* to *Yitzchak*. On the occasion of the celebration, *Yishmael* said to *Yitzchak*: *ani gadol mimcha ba-mitzvot* — I am greater than you in *mitzvot*, for *ata malta ben shmonat yamim* — you underwent your *bris milah* as an eight-day-old infant, while *va'ani ben shalosh esre shana* — I underwent it at thirteen, when I was fully aware and could have refused. *Yitzchak* replied, you think one limb is such an accomplishment? *Im omer li Ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu zaveach atzmecha lefanai* — if God were to say to me, offer yourself before Me, *ani zoveach* — I would do it. And immediately: *ve-ha-Elokim nissah es Avraham*.

What is particularly compelling about this version is that it draws *Yitzchak* into the center of the narrative. The *Akeidah* is not merely a test of *Avraham*; it is also a fulfillment of *Yitzchak's* own declaration of readiness and faith. *Yitzchak* is not a passive subject but a willing participant, and the *midrash* establishes that his willingness was not merely coerced compliance but an expression of genuine commitment to divine service.
