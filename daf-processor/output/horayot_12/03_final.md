# Horayot 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Status

### Par Offering Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵיזֶהוּ הַמָּשִׁיחַ? הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, לֹא הַמְרוּבֶּה בִּבְגָדִים. אֵין בֵּין כֹּהֵן הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה לִמְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים, אֶלָּא פַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And who is the anointed** priest? It is the High Priest **who is anointed with the anointing oil, not the** High Priest consecrated by donning **multiple garments,** i.e., one who served after the anointing oil had been sequestered, toward the end of the First Temple period. **The difference between** a High **Priest anointed with the anointing oil and** one consecrated by donning **multiple garments** unique to the High Priest **is only** that the latter does not bring the **bull that comes for** the transgression of **any of the mitzvot.**

The daf begins at the bottom of 11b, in the middle of a *mishnah* discussing the *kohen hamashiach*'s obligation to bring a *par* — the special bull offering — when he sins based on his own *psak*. This obligation applies specifically when the *kohen gadol* has been anointed with the *shemen hamishcha*. The one area of difference between the oil-anointed *kohen gadol* and the *kohen gadol merubeh begadim* — one who attains that status by wearing the special eight vestments rather than through anointing — is precisely this *par* offering. In all other respects, the *mishnah* holds, the two are equivalent.

The *gemara* has also been discussing the broader use of the *shemen hamishcha*. The oil was used to anoint each successive *kohen gadol*, even when the incoming *kohen gadol* was the son of the outgoing one. By contrast, it was not used to anoint the son of a king, except in cases where the succession was disputed among competing claimants.

### Kings vs. Kohanim

There are two distinct reasons why the *shemen hamishcha* is always employed for the *kohen gadol* but only conditionally for kings. First, the institution of the *kehunah gedolah* is not fundamentally a *yerusha* — an inheritance — in the way that kingship is. While there may be a preference for the son of the *kohen gadol*, that preference does not carry the automatic legal force of inheritance. Second, the *shemen hamishcha* was originally created to anoint the vessels of the *mishkan*, and the *kohen gadol* — and indeed the *kohanim* generally — were themselves counted among those sacred vessels. This gives the oil a natural, organic connection to the *kehunah*, whereas its use for kings requires a more specific license.

### Kingship Yerusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעַן יַאֲרִיךְ יָמִים עַל מַמְלַכְתּוֹ הוּא וּבָנָיו בְּקֶרֶב יִשְׂרָאֵל. זֶה וָזֶה שָׁוִים בַּעֲבוֹדַת יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וּמְצֻוִּוים עַל הַבְּתוּלָה, וַאֲסוּרִים עַל הָאַלְמָנָה, וְאֵינָם מִטַּמְּאִים בִּקְרוֹבֵיהֶם, וְלֹא פּוֹרְעִים, וְלֹא פּוֹרְמִים, וּמַחְזִירִין הָרוֹצֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** so that his reign should be prolonged, he and his sons in the midst of Israel. Both this High Priest currently serving and that former High Priest are equal with regard to performing the rest of the Yom Kippur service, and they are both commanded with regard to marrying a virgin (see Leviticus 21:13), and it is prohibited for both to marry a widow (see Leviticus 21:14), and they may not render themselves impure with impurity imparted by a corpse even in the event that one of their relatives dies (see Leviticus 21:11), and they may not grow their hair long and they may not rend their garments as expressions of mourning (see Leviticus 21:10), and when they die they restore the unwitting murderer to his home from the city of refuge (see Numbers 35:25).

The *gemara* now turns to the inherited character of kingship. The verse states: *lema'an yaarich yamim al mamlachto hu uvanav bekerev Yisrael* — so that his reign should be prolonged, he and his sons in the midst of Israel. This verse establishes that kingship is fundamentally a *yerusha*, passing from father to son through the generations.

The *gemara* asks: where do we know that when there *is* a dispute among the king's sons, anointing is required? And more pointedly — cannot the king simply designate which of his sons he chooses as his heir, rendering *meshicha* unnecessary? The *gemara* invokes *Rav Papa*'s reading of the same verse: *hu uvanav bekerev Yisrael* — when there is peace and unity in Israel, *bizman sheshalom beYisrael*, then the king's designation of his heir suffices even without *meshicha*. But the question of when *meshicha* is required — and whether the *shemen hamishcha* may licitly be used for this purpose at all — remains to be resolved by appeal to historical precedent.

### Firstborn Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אַף יֵהוּא בֶּן נִמְשִׁי לֹא נִמְשַׁח אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי מַחְלוּקְתּוֹ שֶׁל יוֹרָם. וּמִשּׁוּם מַחְלוּקְתּוֹ שֶׁל יוֹרָם בֶּן אַחְאָב נִמְעוֹל בְּשֶׁמֶן? כִּדְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בַּאֲפַרְסְמָא דַּכְיָא, הָכִי נָמֵי בַּאֲפַרְסְמָא דַּכְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught: Even Jehu, son of Nimshi,** king of Israel, **was anointed only due to the challenge of Joram** (see II Kings 9:1–14). And **due to the challenge of Joram, son of Ahab, shall we misuse consecrated anointing oil** and anoint a king of Israel, who does not require anointing? The **answer: As Rav Pappa said,** they anointed him **with pure balsam** oil, not with anointing oil. **So too,** with regard to Jehu, they anointed him **with pure balsam** oil, not with anointing oil.

A *beraita* is cited: *af Yehu ben Nimshi lo nimshach ela mipnei machlokto shel Yoram* — Yehu was anointed solely on account of the dispute with Yoram. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: but Yehu was the *bechor*, the firstborn son — why would anointing be necessary if the succession was naturally his? The implicit principle at work seems to be: if the king's natural heir, the *bechor*, succeeds him and nobody disputes this, there is no need for *meshicha*. If the heir is not the *bechor* but the king has designated him and the succession is uncontested, that too suffices. Only when the heir is not the *bechor* and the succession is actively disputed does the need for *meshicha* arise.

The *gemara* responds: *chasorei mechasra vehachi katani* — the *beraita* is incomplete and must be read as follows. There is a prior, unstated premise: *malchei beit David meshuchin* — the kings of the house of David are anointed; *malchei Yisrael ein meshuchin* — the kings of Israel are not anointed. Where does this come from? Rav derives it from the verse God spoke to Shmuel: *kum moshchehu ki zeh hu* — arise and anoint him, for this is the one. *Zeh ta'un meshicha ve'ein acher ta'un meshicha* — this one, and his descendants when necessary, require anointing, and no others.

With that premise established, the *beraita*'s statement about Yehu becomes intelligible: even though Yehu was the *bechor*, since he was from *malchei Yisrael* — who have no license to use the *shemen hamishcha* — one might think no anointing would occur even when disputed. The *beraita* comes to tell us that even for *malchei Yisrael*, when there is a genuine dispute, some form of anointing ritual is performed. However, as the *gemara* clarifies, they could not use the actual *shemen hamishcha*. As Rav Papa will state, this was done *ba'afarsema dachya* — with clean, fragrant oil — a substitute ritual that evokes *meshicha* without deploying the sanctified oil itself.

### Judah vs. Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶת יְהוֹאָחָז מִפְּנֵי יְהוֹיָקִים, שֶׁהָיָה גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים. וּמִי קַשִּׁישׁ מִינֵּהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ הַבְּכוֹר יוֹחָנָן הַשֵּׁנִי יְהוֹיָקִים הַשְּׁלִישִׁי צִדְקִיָּהוּ הָרְבִיעִי שַׁלּוּם״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוּא שַׁלּוּם הוּא צִדְקִיָּהוּ, הוּא יוֹחָנָן הוּא יְהוֹאָחָז. לְעוֹלָם יְהוֹיָקִים קַשִּׁישׁ, וּמַאי ״בְּכוֹר״? בְּכוֹר לַמַּלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **And** they anointed **Jehoahaz due to Jehoiakim, who was two years older than he** was. The Gemara asks: **And was** Jehoiakim **older than** Jehoahaz? **But isn't it written: "And the sons of Josiah: The firstborn Johanan, the second Jehoiakim, the third Zedekiah, the fourth Shallum"** (I Chronicles 3:15), **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is Shallum, he is Zedekiah;** these are two names for one person. Likewise, **he is Johanan, he is Jehoahaz,** who is mentioned in the book of Kings. Since Jehoahaz was the eldest, why was it necessary to anoint him? The Gemara answers: **Actually, Jehoiakim** was **older** than Jehoahaz. **And what** is the meaning of the term **"firstborn"** written with regard to Jehoahaz? It means that his status was like that of **a firstborn** in terms of ascent **to the kingship.**

The resulting *halacha* as the *gemara* understands it turns on two interlocking criteria. First, it must involve *malchei beit David*; only they have the license to use the actual *shemen hamishcha*. Second, even within *malchei beit David*, *meshicha* is required only when the succession deviates from the natural *bechor* order and the deviation is disputed. When succession is uncontested and goes to the firstborn, the *yerusha* is self-executing and no additional ritual is necessary.

The *gemara* then raises a further challenge: the *beraita* states that *v'es Yehoachaz mipnei Yehoyakim she'haya gadol mimenu sheish shanim* — Yehoachaz was anointed because Yehoyakim was six years older. Yehoachaz was thus the younger son, yet the people installed him first. That is why *meshicha* was required — here it is *malchei beit David*, not the *bechor*, and the succession was contested.

### Yehoachaz Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָלְכִי זוּטְרֵי מִקַּמֵּי קַשִּׁישֵׁי? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶת הַמַּמְלָכָה נָתַן לִיהוֹרָם כִּי הוּא הַבְּכוֹר״! יְהוֹרָם מְמַלֵּא מְקוֹם אֲבוֹתָיו הֲוָה, יְהוֹיָקִים לָאו מְמַלֵּא מְקוֹם אֲבוֹתָיו הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do younger** sons **rule before elder** sons? **But isn't it written: "And the kingdom he gave to Jehoram, because he was the firstborn"** (II Chronicles 21:3). The Gemara answers: **Jehoram was a surrogate for his ancestors** as he was suited to serve as king, so since he was firstborn, he ascended to the throne. **Jehoiakim was not a surrogate for his ancestors;** he was not suited to serve as king. Therefore, his brother ascended to the throne before him.

The *gemara* then scrutinizes whether Yehoyakim was truly older than Yehoachaz. The verse in Divrei HaYamim lists the sons of Yoshiyahu: the *bechor* Yochanan, the second Yehoyakim. So Yehoyakim appears to be the second son — how is he older than Yehoachaz? Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *hu Yochanan hu Yehoachaz* — Yochanan *is* Yehoachaz. He was the firstborn by birth, but the *beraita* calls him *bechor l'malchus* — first in the order of kingship — not first in the order of birth. Yehoyakim was older biologically, but Yehoachaz was made king first.

The *gemara* presses further: is it really possible for a younger son to be made king before the older? The verse states: *v'es hamamlacha nasan l'Yehoram ki hu habechor* — the kingdom was given to Yehoram because he was the firstborn. So it seems the eldest son has an exclusive claim! The *gemara* answers: *Yehoram memalei makom avosav hava; Yehoyakim lo memalei makom avosav hava* — Yehoram was fit to fill the place of his fathers, whereas Yehoyakim was not. When the firstborn is deserving, he receives the kingship automatically. When there is a more deserving candidate among the younger sons, the kingship may pass to him.

### David & Solomon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הוּא שַׁלּוּם הוּא צִדְקִיָּהוּ, הוּא יוֹחָנָן הוּא יְהוֹאָחָז. וְהָא חַד חַד קָא חָשֵׁיב, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַשְּׁלִישִׁי הָרְבִיעִי״! מַאי ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ – שְׁלִישִׁי לַבָּנִים, וּמַאי ״רְבִיעִי״ – רְבִיעִי לַמַּלְכוּת. דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא מְלַךְ יְהוֹאָחָז, וּלְבַסּוֹף יְהוֹיָקִים, וּלְבַסּוֹף יְכׇנְיָה, וּלְבַסּוֹף צִדְקִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: He is Shallum, he is Zedekiah; he is Johanan, he is Jehoahaz.** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the verse **enumerate them individually, as it is written: "The third** Zedekiah, **the fourth** Shallum," indicating that they are two people? The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of **third?** It means the **third among the sons. And what** is the meaning of **fourth?** It means the **fourth** to ascend **to the kingship.** How so? **Initially, Jehoahaz reigned, and ultimately,** after him, **Jehoiakim, and ultimately,** after him, **Jeconiah,** son of Jehoiakim, **and ultimately,** after him, **Zedekiah,** who was fourth to the kingship.

This principle draws directly on the foundational cases: Shlomo was not the oldest son of David, yet David designated him as heir. The *gemara* notes that the king's power to designate is embedded in the concept of *yerusha* — one can choose among one's heirs — but when that choice is contested, the anointing ritual is what validates it.

The *gemara* also identifies the remaining sons of Yoshiyahu. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *hu Shalum hu Tzidkiyahu* — Shalum and Tzidkiyahu are the same person. The verse calls him the third son and the fourth. The *gemara* resolves this: *shlishi l'vanim* — he was third in birth order — *revi'i l'malchus* — but fourth in the order of succession. The succession ran: Yehoachaz first, then Yehoyakim, then Yechanyah (not mentioned in the verse's list of sons), and finally Tzidkiyahu. As for Tzidkiyahu's name — his original name was Masanya, and the king of Bavel renamed him *Tzidkiyahu* with a pointed meaning: *Ya yatzdik alecha es hadin im timrod bi* — God should execute justice against you if you rebel against me as the previous kings did. The verse then records: *v'gam b'melech Nevuchadnetzar marad* — and he too rebelled, despite his sworn oath.

## Shemen Hamishcha

### Oil Hidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הֲוָה שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה? וְהָתַנְיָא: מִשֶּׁנִּגְנַז אָרוֹן, נִגְנַז שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה וְצִנְצֶנֶת הַמָּן וּמַקְלוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן שְׁקֵדֶיהָ וּפְרָחֶיהָ וְאַרְגַּז שֶׁשָּׁלְחוּ פְּלִשְׁתִּים דּוֹרוֹן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵת כְּלֵי הַזָּהָב אֲשֶׁר הֲשֵׁבֹתֶם לוֹ אָשָׁם תָּשִׂימוּ בָאַרְגַּז מִצִּדּוֹ וְשִׁלַּחְתֶּם אֹתוֹ וְהָלָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the fact that Jehoahaz was anointed: **And was there anointing oil** during the days of Jehoahaz? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **When the Ark of the Covenant was sequestered, the anointing oil, and the jar of *manna*** (see Exodus 16:33), **and Aaron's staff** with **its almonds and blossoms** (see Numbers 17:23), **and the chest that the Philistines sent** as **a gift to Israel,** were all **sequestered** with it, **as it is stated:** "And you shall take the Ark of the Lord, and lay it upon the cart, **and put the vessels of gold that you return Him as a guilt-offering in a chest by its side and send it away that it may go"** (I Samuel 6:8).

The *gemara* turns to a striking historical question. Was the *shemen hamishcha* even available to anoint the later kings of *beit David* — Yehoachaz and others — given everything we know about its fate? A *beraita* teaches: *mishenignaz Aron, nignaz shemen hamishcha* — from the time the Ark was hidden, the *shemen hamishcha* was hidden along with it. Together with the Ark were secreted: the jar of *man*, the staff of Aharon (with its blossoms and flowers), and the chest sent by the Pelishtim as an offering to Israel. The *gemara* cites the verse from Shmuel Aleph in which the Pelishti priests instruct their leaders to place the golden vessels *ba'argaz mitsido* — in a chest alongside the Ark — and since they specified it must remain beside the Ark, the chest was thereafter kept with the Ark permanently, including when it was hidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי גְּנָזוֹ? יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה גְּנָזוֹ, שֶׁרָאָה שֶׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״יוֹלֵךְ ה׳ אֹתְךָ וְאֶת מַלְכְּךָ וְגוֹ׳״, צִוָּה וּגְנָזוּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לַלְוִיִּם הַמְּבִינִים לְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל הַקְּדוֹשִׁים לַה׳ תְּנוּ אֶת אֲרוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ בַּבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה שְׁלֹמֹה בֶן דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין לָכֶם מַשָּׂא בַּכָּתֵף עַתָּה עִבְדוּ אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם וְאֵת עַמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And who sequestered** the Ark? **Josiah, king of Judea, sequestered it, as he saw that it is written in the Torah** in the portion of rebuke: **"The Lord will lead you, and your king** whom you shall establish over you, unto a nation that you have not known" (Deuteronomy 28:36). **He commanded and** the people **sequestered them, as it is stated: "And he said to the Levites who taught all Israel, and who were sacred unto the Lord: Place the sacred Ark in the room that Solomon, son of David, king of Israel built; there shall be no more burden upon your shoulders. Now serve the Lord your God and His people Israel"** (II Chronicles 35:3).

Who hid the Ark? *Yoshiyahu Melech Yehuda ganzo* — Yoshiyahu king of Judah hid it. He foresaw, based on the verse in the Torah — *yolech Hashem otcha ve'et malchecha asher takim alecha el goy asher lo yadata* — that the people and their king would be exiled to a foreign nation. Not wishing the Ark to fall into enemy hands, he commanded that it be hidden.

The proof text cited is Yoshiyahu's command to the Levites: *tnu et aron hakodesh babayit asher bana Shlomo ben David Melech Yisrael; ein lachem masa bakatef* — place the holy Ark in the house that Shlomo built; you will no longer carry it on your shoulders. This seems puzzling — wasn't the Ark already in the Temple? The *gemara* interprets the verse as Yoshiyahu speaking prophetically about the future: once the Ark is taken into exile in Bavel, you will never have the opportunity to carry it back on your shoulders as it was once carried through the wilderness into Eretz Yisrael. Therefore, hide it now.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָתְיָא ״שָׁם״ ״שָׁם״, אַתְיָא ״מִשְׁמֶרֶת״ ״מִשְׁמֶרֶת״, אָתְיָא ״דּוֹרוֹת״ ״דּוֹרוֹת״! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בַּאֲפַרְסְמָא דַּכְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says: One derives** a verbal analogy between the term: **There,** written with regard to the Ark (see Exodus 29:43), and the term: **There,** written with regard to the jar of *manna* (see Exodus 16:33); and between the term: **Keepsake,** written with regard to the jar of *manna* (see Exodus 16:33), and the term: **Keepsake,** written with regard to Aaron's staff (see Numbers 17:25–26); and between the term: **Generations,** written with regard to the jar of *manna* (see Exodus 16:33), and the term: **Generations,** written with regard to the anointing oil (see Exodus 30:31). By means of these verbal analogies it is derived that all of these items were sequestered. **Rav Pappa said:** They anointed Jehoahaz **with pure balsam** oil, not with anointing oil.

Rabbi Elazar provides the exegetical links (*gezeiros shavos*) that tie all the hidden items together. The word *sham* links the Ark to the *man* (the Ark: *venoadeti lcha sham*; the *man*: *veten shama melo ha'omer man*). The word *mishmeret* links the *man* to the *shemen hamishcha* (the *man*: *lifnei Hashem lemishmeret ledoroteichem*; the oil: *yihyeh zeh li ledoroteichem*). The word *mishmeret* links the *man* to Aharon's staff as well. And the Pelishti chest was always beside the Ark by the instruction of the Pelishti priests themselves.

### Anointing Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מוֹשְׁחִין אֶת הַמְּלָכִים? כְּמִין נֵזֶר. וְאֶת הַכֹּהֲנִים? כְּמִין כִּי. מַאי ״כְּמִין כִּי״? אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר גַּדָּא: כְּמִין כָּף יְוָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: How does one anoint the kings?** One smears the oil in a manner that is **similar to** the form of **a crown** around his head. **And** how does one anoint **the priests?** One smears the oil in a shape **like** the Greek letter **chi.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **Like** the Greek letter **chi? Rav Menashya bar Gadda said: Like** the **Greek** equivalent of the Hebrew letter ***kaf*.

This answers the question of how later kings were anointed: Rav Papa clarifies that when the *shemen hamishcha* was unavailable — whether because the succession involved *malchei Yisrael* to whom no license for the actual oil applied, or because the oil had been hidden in the time of Yoshiyahu — a substitute was used. They anointed *ba'afarsema dachya* — with clean, fragrant oil — a parallel ritual that preserved the symbolism of *meshicha* without using the sanctified oil itself.

Having established that, the *gemara* returns to the mechanics of anointing. A *beraita* teaches: *keitzad moshchin et hamelachim? k'min nezer* — how do you anoint kings? In the form of a crown — that is, you draw the oil around the head like a crown. And for *kohanim*, *k'min chi* — in the form of the Greek letter chi. Rav Menashia bar Gadda explains: like a Greek *chaf*, which takes the form of an X. Rashi understands this as resembling a *chet* — beginning from between the eyes, going straight up and over the head and back, forming an arch.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: בַּתְּחִלָּה מוֹצְקִין שֶׁמֶן עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שֶׁמֶן בֵּין רִיסֵי עֵינָיו. וְתַנְיָא אַחֲרִיתִי: בַּתְּחִלָּה נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שֶׁמֶן בֵּין רִיסֵי עֵינָיו, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מוֹצְקִים לוֹ שֶׁמֶן עַל רֹאשׁוֹ! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: מְשִׁיחָה עֲדִיפָא, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: יְצִיקָה עֲדִיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **one** *baraita*: **Initially, they pour oil on** the priest's **head, and thereafter, they place oil for him between the lashes of his eyes. And it is taught** in **a different** *baraita*: **Initially, they place oil for him between the lashes of his eyes, and thereafter, they pour oil on his head.** The Gemara explains: **It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. Some say: Anointing** with oil between his eyes **is preferable** and takes precedence, **and some say: Pouring** oil on his head **is preferable** and takes precedence.

Two *baraitot* dispute the order of the two acts — *yetzika* (pouring the oil on the head) and *meshicha* (the manual application of oil between the eyebrows). One *beraita* holds that pouring comes first and the manual application follows; the other reverses the order. The *gemara* records a further dispute: some hold that whichever act comes first is the more important one, and on that view *meshicha* is superior because the *k'lei shareit* were sanctified through *meshicha* alone, without any pouring; others hold that *yetzika* is the superior act. The proof text for those who consider *yetzika* primary: *vayitzok mishmen hamishcha al rosh Aharon vayimshach oto l'kadsho* — he poured the oil on Aaron's head and then anointed him to sanctify him. The proof text for those who consider *meshicha* primary: the *k'lei shareit* were sanctified by *meshicha* alone, demonstrating that *meshicha* is the essential act. The *gemara* struggles to reconcile each position with the *pasuk*, producing a notably strained reading on each side.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר יְצִיקָה עֲדִיפָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּצֹק מִשֶּׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה עַל רֹאשׁ אַהֲרֹן וַיִּמְשַׁח אֹתוֹ לְקַדְּשׁוֹ״. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מְשִׁיחָה עֲדִיפָא, מַאי טַעְמָא? קָסָבַר: שֶׁכֵּן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא אֵצֶל כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת. וְהָכְתִיב ״וַיִּצֹק״, וּבַסּוֹף ״וַיִּמְשַׁח״! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מַאי טַעַם ״וַיִּצֹק״? מִשּׁוּם דְּ״וַיִּמְשַׁח״.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the one who said** that **pouring** oil on his head **is preferable?** It is **as it is written: "And he poured from the anointing oil upon Aaron's head and anointed him to sanctify him"** (Leviticus 8:12), indicating that pouring precedes anointing. **And the one who said** that **anointing is preferable** and takes precedence, **what is the reason** for his opinion? **He holds:** Anointing takes precedence **as** that is what **you find with regard to service vessels** (see Numbers 7:1). They were anointed, but the anointing oil was not poured on them. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written** with regard to the priests: **"And he poured," and ultimately: "And anointed"?** The Gemara answers: **This** is what the verse **is saying: What is the reason** that **he poured** the oil? It is **due to** the fact **that he** had already **anointed** them. Anointing is the primary component of the process.

### Aaron's Beard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כַּשֶּׁמֶן הַטּוֹב [וְגוֹ׳] יֹרֵד עַל הַזָּקָן זְקַן אַהֲרֹן וְגוֹ׳״ – כְּמִין שְׁנֵי טִפֵּי מַרְגָּלִיּוֹת הָיוּ תְּלוּיוֹת לְאַהֲרֹן בִּזְקָנוֹ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, תָּנָא: כְּשֶׁהוּא מְסַפֵּר, עוֹלוֹת וְיוֹשְׁבוֹת לוֹ בְּעִיקַּר זְקָנוֹ, וְעַל דָּבָר זֶה הָיָה מֹשֶׁה דּוֹאֵג, אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא חַס וְשָׁלוֹם מָעַלְתִּי בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה? יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״כְּשֶׁמֶן הַטּוֹב וְגוֹ׳ כְּטַל חֶרְמוֹן״, מָה טַל חֶרְמוֹן אֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה, אַף שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה שֶׁבִּזְקַן אַהֲרֹן אֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "It is like the precious oil upon the head coming down upon the beard, Aaron's beard,** that comes down upon the collar of his garments" (Psalms 133:2). **Two drops** of anointing oil, **shaped like pearls, were suspended for Aaron from his beard. Rav Pappa said** that it is **taught: When** Aaron would **speak** and his beard would move, those drops would miraculously **rise and settle on the roots of his beard** so that they would not fall. **Moses was concerned about this matter. He said: Perhaps, Heaven forfend, I misused** the consecrated **anointing oil** and poured more than necessary, as two additional drops remain? **A Divine Voice emerged and said: "It is like the precious oil** upon the head coming down upon the beard, Aaron's beard, that comes down upon the collar of his garments. **Like the dew of Hermon"** (Psalms 133:2–3). This analogy teaches: **Just as there is no misuse of the dew of Hermon,** which is not consecrated, **so too,** with regard to **the anointing oil that is on Aaron's beard, there is no misuse** of consecrated property.

The *gemara* cites the celebrated *beraita* about the drops of oil on Aharon's beard. The verse in Tehillim speaks of oil descending on the beard — *k'shemen hatov yored al hazakan zakan Aharon* — and the *gemara* reads this as a description of the *shemen hamishcha*: two pearl-like drops of oil remained suspended on the tips of Aharon's beard after the anointing. Rav Papa adds: when Aharon was speaking, the drops would rise up to the root of the beard — they would not drip away during conversation.

This phenomenon troubled Moshe. He feared: *shema chas v'shalom ma'alti b'shemen hamishcha* — perhaps, God forbid, I misused the *shemen hamishcha*. Rashi explains that Moshe worried he had accidentally derived personal benefit from the oil during the anointing. A *bas kol* went forth and declared: *k'shemen hatov* — it is the good oil, there is no cause for concern. The *pasuk* continues: *k'tal Chermon* — like the dew of the Chermon — just as dew involves no *me'ilah*, so too the *shemen hamishcha* on Aharon's beard carries no *me'ilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַעֲדַיִין הָיָה אַהֲרֹן דּוֹאֵג, אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא מֹשֶׁה לֹא מָעַל, אֲבָל אֲנִי מָעַלְתִּי? יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: ״הִנֵּה מַה טּוֹב וּמַה נָּעִים שֶׁבֶת אַחִים גַּם יָחַד״, מָה מֹשֶׁה לֹא מָעַל, אַף אַתָּה לֹא מָעַלְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And still Aaron was concerned. He said: Perhaps Moses did not misuse** consecrated property; **but** perhaps **I misused** consecrated property, as the additional oil is on my beard and I enjoy it. **A Divine Voice emerged and said: "Behold, how good and how pleasant it is for brothers to dwell together in unity"** (Psalms 133:1). **Just as** your brother **Moses did not misuse** consecrated property, **so too, you did not misuse** consecrated property.

Aharon, however, remained anxious: perhaps Moshe had not misused the oil, but he himself had. A second *bas kol* responded with the opening of that same *perek* in Tehillim: *hineh mah tov umah naim shevet achim gam yachad* — just as Moshe had not transgressed, neither had Aharon.

### Spring Anointing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מוֹשְׁחִים אֶת הַמְּלָכִים אֶלָּא עַל הַמַּעְיָין, כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּמָּשֵׁךְ מַלְכוּתָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ לָהֶם קְחוּ עִמָּכֶם אֶת עַבְדֵי אֲדֹנֵיכֶם [וְגוֹ׳] וְהוֹרַדְתֶּם אֹתוֹ אֶל גִּחוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One anoints the kings only upon a spring,** as an omen, **so that their kingdom will continue** like a spring, **as it is stated** with regard to the coronation of Solomon before the death of David: **"And the king said unto them: Take with you the servants of your lord,** and let Solomon my son ride upon my own mule, **and bring him down to Gihon.** And let Tzadok the priest and Nathan the prophet anoint him there king over Israel and sound the shofar and say: Long live King Solomon" (I Kings 1:33–34).

The *gemara* records a further teaching: *ein moshchin es hamelachim ela al hamayan* — kings are anointed only beside a spring or flowing water. The symbolism: just as a spring flows perpetually and is not stagnant, so too the king's reign should be long-lived and unceasing. The proof text is the account of Shlomo's anointing, where David commanded: *v'horadetem oto el Gichon* — bring him down to the Gichon spring, and there anoint him.

## Symbols & Omens

### Omens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי לִידַּע אִי מַסֵּיק שַׁתֵּיהּ אִי לָא, נִיתְלֵי שְׁרָגָא בַּעֲשָׂרָה יוֹמֵי דְּבֵין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים בְּבֵיתָא דְּלָא נָשֵׁיב זִיקָא. אִי מָשֵׁיךְ נְהוֹרֵיהּ – נִידַּע דְּמַסֵּיק שַׁתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: This** person **who seeks to know if he will complete his year or if** he will **not,** i.e., whether or not he will remain alive in the coming year, **let him light a lamp, during the ten days that are between Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur, in a house in which wind does not blow. If its light continues** to burn, **he knows that he** will **complete his year.**

This discussion of symbolic action leads the *gemara* into a broader digression on the power of *simanim* — symbolic signs and omens — and the related but distinct category of forecasting the future. Rabbi Ami says: if someone wishes to know whether his year will be prosperous, let him light a candle during the ten days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur in a room with no wind; if the flame burns down fully without being extinguished, he will know his year will be successful.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּבָעֵי לְמִיעְבַּד בְּעִיסְקָא, וּבָעֵי לְמִידַּע אִי מַצְלַח אִי לָא מַצְלַח, לִירַבֵּי תַּרְנְגוֹלָא. אִי שָׁמֵין וְשָׁפַר, מַצְלַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And one who seeks to conduct a business venture and wishes to know if he will succeed** or **if** he will **not succeed, let him raise a rooster. If** the rooster **grows fat and healthy, he will succeed.**

If someone wishes to know whether a business venture will succeed, let him raise a chicken: if it grows fat and healthy, this bodes well for his investment. If someone wishes to know whether he will return safely from a journey — in an era when travel was genuinely perilous — let him go to his friend's house and look for the reflection of a reflection. If he sees it, he will return home.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי לְמִיפַּק [לְאוֹרְחָא] וּבָעֵי לְמִידַּע אִי (חָזַר) [הָדַר] וְאָתֵי לְבֵיתָא, אִי לָא – נֵיקוּם בְּבֵיתָא דְּחַבָּרָא, אִי חָזֵי בָּבוּאָה דְבָבוּאָה לִידַּע דְּהָדַר וְאָתֵי לְבֵיתֵאּ. וְלָאו מִלְּתָא הִיא, דִּלְמָא חָלְשָׁא דַּעְתֵּיהּ, וּמִיתְּרַע מַזָּלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One who seeks to embark on a journey and wishes to know if he** will **return and come to** his **home** or **if** he will **not, let him go to a dark [*daḥavara*] house. If he sees the shadow of a shadow he shall know that he** will **return and come home.** The Sages reject this: This omen **is not** a significant **matter. Perhaps he will be disheartened** if the omen fails to appear, **and his fortune will suffer** and it is this that causes him to fail.

However, the *gemara* cautions: this last omen is not a reliable thing — *v'lav milsa hi*. The concern is psychological. Seeing an unfavorable sign may cause the person to despair — *dilma chalsha da'atei umistara mazlei* — and that despondency itself may become self-fulfilling, causing failure that would not otherwise have occurred. The *gemara* is not merely dismissing the omen's predictive value; it is acknowledging that the frame of mind produced by such signs has real consequences for outcomes. Positive signs can inspire effort and confidence; negative signs can undermine both.

### Rosh Hashanah Foods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ סִימָנָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, [לְעוֹלָם] יְהֵא רָגִיל לְמִיחְזֵי בְּרֵישׁ שַׁתָּא קָרָא וְרוּבְּיָא, כַּרָּתֵי וְסִילְקָא וְתַמְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Now that you said** that **an omen is** a significant **matter, a person should always be accustomed to seeing** these **on Rosh HaShana: Squash, and fenugreek, leeks, and chard, and dates,** as each of these grows quickly and serves as a positive omen for one's actions during the coming year.

Abaye draws out the positive side of this insight: *hashta da'amrat simana milta hi* — now that you say that symbolic action has genuine substance, you should always endeavor on Rosh Hashanah to eat certain foods as auspicious signs: *kara, rubya, karsi, silka, vtamrei* — gourd, fenugreek, leek, beet, and dates. Rashi explains that these are things that ripen or flourish quickly, and thus serve as symbols of a prosperous and fruitful year, in the spirit of the aspiration to be *l'rosh v'lo l'zanav* — a head and not a tail.

### River Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא לִבְרֵיהּ: כִּי בָּעֵיתוּ מֵיעַל וּמִיגְמַרי קַמֵּי רַבַּיְיכוּ, גְּרֻסוּ מַתְנִיתָא וְעַלּוּ לְקַמֵּי רַבַּיְיכוּ. וְכִי יָתְבִיתוּ קַמֵּיהּ, חֲזוֹ לְפוּמֵּיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיוּ עֵינֶיךָ רֹאוֹת אֶת מוֹרֶיךָ״. וְכִי גָרְסִיתוּ, גְּרֻסוּ עַל נַהֲרָא דְּמַיָּא, דְּכִי הֵיכִי דְּמָשְׁכִן מַיָּא מׇשְׁכָן שְׁמַעְתָּתַיְיכוּ. וְתִיבוּ אַקִּילְקְלֵי דְּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא, וְלָא תִּיבוּ אַפַּדְנֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – טָב גִּלְדָּנָא סַרְיָא [דְּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא לְמֵיכַל], מִכּוּתָּחָא דְּרָמֵי כֵּיפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya said to his son: When you seek to enter and study before your teacher, study the *baraita*** first, **and** only then **enter before your teacher. And when you are sitting before him, look to his mouth, as it is written: "And your eyes shall see your teacher"** (Isaiah 30:20). **And when you study, study adjacent to a river of water; just as the water flows, your studies will flow** unimpeded. He added: **And** it is preferable for you to **sit on the rubbish heaps [*akilkelei*] of Mata Meḥasya, and do not sit in the palaces [*appadnei*] of Pumbedita. Better to eat the rotten fish [*gildana*] of Mata Meḥasya than** to eat ***kutḥa*, which displaces rocks,** a metaphor for how potent it is.

Rav Mesharshia addressed practical advice to his sons on how to approach Torah study. When preparing to go learn *gemara* before one's *rebbe*, one must first master the underlying text: *girsu matnita* — study and memorize the *mishnah* and *beraita* thoroughly, and only then enter the *beit midrash* for the analytical discussion. When sitting before your *rebbe*, *chazu l'fumei* — watch his mouth as he speaks. This is both practically and symbolically significant. Rabbi Meir famously said that had he seen the face of his *rebbe* rather than the back, he would have attained even greater mastery. There is an inspirational and emotional dimension to seeing your *rebbe* directly, and an additional resonance in watching the lips: Torah is *Torah she-be-al peh*, the oral tradition, and the continuity of that tradition is embodied in the living words passing from mouth to ear.

When memorizing your texts, do so *al nahara* — beside a river: *de-chi hichi de-mashkin maya, mashkin shamaisaichu* — just as the waters of the river flow continuously, so too may your learning flow. This evokes the earlier theme of the spring beside which kings are anointed — the same symbolism of perpetual, living movement applies to the perpetual flow of *Torah she-be-al peh*.

Rav Mesharshia adds a further practical counsel: better to sit on the refuse heaps of Masa Machasia — a city of great Torah scholarship — than to occupy a seat of honor in Pumbedisa, if Pumbedisa cannot match the richness of Torah environment. The quality of the Torah community one inhabits matters more than the comfort or prestige of one's position within it.

### Horn vs. Cruse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״רָמָה קַרְנִי בֵּאלֹהָי״, ״רָמָה קַרְנִי״ וְלֹא רָמָה פַּכִּי, דָּוִד וּשְׁלֹמֹה שֶׁנִּמְשְׁחוּ בְּקֶרֶן – נִמְשְׁכָה מַלְכוּתָן, שָׁאוּל וְיֵהוּא שֶׁנִּמְשְׁחוּ בְּפַךְ – לֹא נִמְשְׁכָה מַלְכוּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **"My horn is exalted in my God"** (I Samuel 2:1). The Gemara infers: **My horn is exalted, and my jug is not exalted. David and Solomon were anointed with** oil from **a horn.** This was a good omen for them and **their reign endured. Saul and Jehu were anointed with** oil from **a jug and their reign did not endure.**

The *gemara* returns to the symbolism of the anointing vessel itself. The verse in Shmuel states: *rama karni beilohai, rama karni v'lo rama pachi* — my horn is raised to my God; my horn is raised, and not my flask. The *gemara* draws a sharp historical distinction. David and Shlomo were anointed from a *keren* — a horn, like a cornucopia — and their reigns were long and enduring. Shaul and Yehu were anointed from a *pach* — a cruse or flask — and their reigns were foreshortened. The vessel itself serves as a *siman*: the open, flowing horn symbolizes abundance and continuity; the closed flask suggests limitation and brevity.

## Par Korban

### No Par for Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מָשִׁיחַ״ – יָכוֹל מֶלֶךְ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֹּהֵן״. אִי כֹּהֵן, יָכוֹל מְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מָשִׁיחַ״. אִי מָשִׁיחַ, יָכוֹל מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ״, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מָשִׁיחַ עַל גַּבָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "Anointed"** is written in the verse (Leviticus 6:15). One **might** have thought that the reference is to **a king.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Priest." If** the reference is to **a priest,** one **might** have thought that the reference is to a priest consecrated by donning **multiple garments.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Anointed." If** the reference is to one who is **anointed,** one **might** have thought that the reference is even to a priest **anointed for war.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And the anointed priest,"** indicating **that there is no anointed** priest **over him;** rather, he is the highest-ranking priest.

With the discussion of *simanim* and anointing concluded, the *gemara* returns to the core question of the *par* offering. A *beraita* is cited: *mashiach — yachol melech?* When the Torah speaks of the *kohen hamashiach* bringing the *par*, might this refer to a king, who is also anointed? The answer: *talmud lomar kohen* — the text specifies *kohen*, excluding kings. But even within the category of *kohen*, the question arises: perhaps *meshicha* is sufficient to identify the *kohen gadol*, and thus even the *kohen gadol merubeh begadim* — who has no anointing — would be included? The text adds *moshuach* — anointed — excluding him. And might the *kohen mashiach milchama* — the kohen anointed for war, who also receives oil — be included? The text says *v'hakohen hamashiach* — with the definite article, implying *ha-miyuman she-bimshuchim* — the preeminent among those anointed, the one above whom no other stands: namely, the *kohen gadol* proper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע? כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא: ״הַיָּרֵךְ״ – הַמְיוּמֶּנֶת שֶׁבַּיָּרֵךְ, הָכָא נָמֵי: ״הַמָּשִׁיחַ״ – הַמְיוּמָּן שֶׁבַּמְּשׁוּחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** is this **inferred?** The Gemara answers: It is **as Rava said** with regard to the term **"the thigh"** in the verse: "The sciatic nerve that is on the hollow of the thigh" (Genesis 32:33); the reference is to **the stronger of the thighs. Here too,** where the verse states: **"The anointed,"** the reference is to **the** most **distinguished of** those **anointed,** i.e., the High Priest.

The *gemara* also asks: why did we even consider that the *par* might refer to a king? The answer: one might have thought that since kings commit both the error of acting *and* the error of issuing a ruling — the combined condition required for the *kohen gadol*'s special *par* — perhaps a king bringing a *par* for this combined error is logically possible. The *gemara* preempts this: a king who sins brings a *sa'ir* — a goat — under the *parsha* of *nasi*. There is no scenario in which a king brings a *par*. Hence the *gemara* confirms that the entire *parsha* of the special *par* belongs exclusively to the *kohen gadol*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״מָשִׁיחַ״ – יָכוֹל מֶלֶךְ. מֶלֶךְ פַּר הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי? שָׂעִיר הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי! אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: עַל שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה יָבִיא שָׂעִיר, עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר יָבִיא פַּר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **The Master said: "Anointed"** is written in the verse. One **might** have thought that the reference is to **a king.** The Gemara asks: **Is it a bull that a king brings** for a sin-offering? **It is a male goat that he brings,** as the Torah states explicitly, later in that passage. The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** for the *tanna* to say this, as it may **enter your mind to say:** It is **for the unwitting** performance **of an action** for which all people are liable to bring a sin-offering that a king **shall bring** a male goat as his offering; but **for absence** of awareness **of the matter** with the unwitting performance of an action, a king **shall bring a bull.** Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that it is only the High Priest who brings a bull.

### Par Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין בֵּין מָשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה כּוּ׳. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָא תַּנְיָא: מְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים מֵבִיא פַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The difference between** a High **Priest anointed with the anointing oil** and one consecrated by donning multiple garments is only that the latter does not bring the bull that comes for the transgression of any of the mitzvot. The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as, if it was** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: A priest who is consecrated by donning **multiple garments brings a bull that comes for** the transgression of **any of the mitzvot;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir, but the Rabbis did not concede** that point **to him.**

The *mishnah* states: *ein bein mashiach b'shemen hamishcha l'merubeh begadim ela par haba al kol hamitzvot* — the only difference between the oil-anointed *kohen gadol* and the vestments-elevated *kohen gadol* is the *par* offering brought for transgression of the entire body of law. The *gemara* notes: *matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi Meir* — our *mishnah* is not in accordance with Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Meir Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? דְּתַנְיָא: ״מָשִׁיחַ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מָשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, מְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"Anointed"** is written in the verse. **I have** derived **only a priest anointed with the anointing oil. From where** do I derive the *halakha* of a priest who is consecrated by donning **multiple garments? The verse states: "The anointed priest,"** from which it is derived that anyone who is appointed as the High Priest is included, even if he was not anointed.

A *beraita* records Rabbi Meir's position: *merubeh begadim mevi par al kol hamitzvot* — even a *kohen gadol* elevated through vestments alone brings the *par*. The sages disagree. The *gemara* traces Rabbi Meir's reasoning: from the phrase *haKohen HaMashiach* he derives, by some form of inclusion (*ribbui*), that even the *merubeh begadim* is encompassed. The sages read the same phrase as limiting the *par* to the oil-anointed *kohen gadol* alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתֵּיהּ? כְּרַבָּנַן,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with which** opinion **did you interpret** the mishna? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* then looks at the *seifa* — the latter clause of the *mishnah*: *ein bein Kohen meshamesh l'Kohen she'avar ella par Yom HaKippurim v'asirit ha'eifah* — the only difference between the currently serving *kohen gadol* and the one who previously served but was replaced is who brings the Yom Kippur *par* and the daily *minchas chavitin*. In all other respects, the former *kohen gadol* retains the full status of a *kohen gadol*. This position, the *gemara* establishes, is the view of Rabbi Meir. The *mishnah* thus appears to open with a *non-Rabbi Meir* ruling and conclude with a *Rabbi Meir* ruling.

## Past KG Status

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֵין בֵּין כֹּהֵן מְשַׁמֵּשׁ לְכֹהֵן שֶׁעָבַר אֶלָּא פַּר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים וַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה. אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר. דְּתַנְיָא: אֵירַע בּוֹ פְּסוּל בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל וְעָבַר וּמִינּוּ כֹּהֵן אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו – הָרִאשׁוֹן חוֹזֵר לַעֲבוֹדָתוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי כׇּל מִצְוֹת כְּהוּנָּה עָלָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. [רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: רִאשׁוֹן חוֹזֵר לַעֲבוֹדָתוֹ, וְשֵׁנִי אֵינוֹ רָאוּי לֹא לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל וְלֹא לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט].
>
> **Translation:** **Say the latter clause: The difference between** a High **Priest** currently **serving** in that capacity **and a former** High **Priest is only** with regard to the **bull** brought by the High Priest on **Yom Kippur and the tenth of an ephah** meal-offering; but with regard to all other matters the two are equal. In this clause **we arrive at** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If temporary **disqualification befell the High Priest and he left** his position **and they appointed another priest in his stead,** after the cause of his disqualification passes, **the first** priest **returns to his service** as High Priest. **And** with regard to **the second** priest, **all of the mitzvot of the** High **Priesthood** are still incumbent **upon him;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yosei says: The first returns to his service, and the second is unfit** to serve in either position; he may serve **neither as a High Priest nor as an ordinary priest.**

Rabbi Meir holds that a *kohen gadol* who served in that capacity and was subsequently replaced retains nearly all the obligations and privileges of the *kehunah gedolah*. *Kol mitzvot kehunah alav* — all the laws of the *kehunah gedolah* remain upon him. The only exceptions are the Yom Kippur *par* and the daily *minchas chavitin*, which belong exclusively to the currently serving *kohen gadol*.

### Rabbi Yossi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּיוֹסֵף בֶּן אִילֵּים מִצִּפּוֹרִי שֶׁאֵירַע בּוֹ פְּסוּל בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְעָבַר, וּמִינּוּ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, וְלֹא הֱנִיחוּהוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים לִהְיוֹת לֹא כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וְלֹא כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט. כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה, כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט מִשּׁוּם מַעֲלִין בַּקֹּדֶשׁ וְאֵין מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. **Rabbi Yosei said:** There was **an incident involving** the priest **Yosef ben Eilim of Tzippori: When disqualification befell a High Priest and he left** his position, the priests **appointed another,** Yosef ben Eilim, **in his stead. And** after the cause of the disqualification passed, **his brethren the priests did not allow** Yosef ben Eilim **to** serve, **neither** as **a High Priest nor** as **an ordinary priest.** Neither as **a High Priest, due to enmity,** jealousy, and bitterness that would be engendered if there were two High Priests with equal standing in the Temple. Nor as **an ordinary priest, because** the principle is: **One elevates** to a higher level **in** matters of **sanctity and one does not downgrade.** Once he has served as a High Priest he cannot be restored to the position of an ordinary priest.

Rabbi Yosei takes the opposite view: *harishon chozer l'avodato* — the original *kohen gadol* resumes his service — *v'hasheni eino ra'uy lo l'Kohen Gadol v'lo l'Kohen Hedyot* — and the one who served in his place is no longer fit to serve as either *kohen gadol* or as a regular *kohen*. He cannot serve as *kohen gadol* because of *eivah* — the jealousy and resentment that would arise from having another figure capable of performing virtually all the same functions. And he cannot revert to the status of a *kohen hedyot* because of the principle of *ma'alin bakodesh v'ein moridin* — we elevate in holiness and do not diminish.

### Yosef ben Eilim

The *beraita* illustrates his position with the historical case of Yosef ben Eilim of Tzipori. When the *kohen gadol* became incapacitated on Yom Kippur, Yosef ben Eilim was appointed in his place and performed the Yom Kippur service. After Yom Kippur, the case came before the sages. They ruled: the original *kohen gadol* returns to his position; Yosef ben Eilim, the second, is no longer fit to serve as either *kohen gadol* or *kohen hedyot*, for the two reasons Rabbi Yosei articulates.

## Par Korban (II)

### Mishnah Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אִין, רֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: רַבִּי הִיא, וְנָסֵיב לַהּ אַלִּיבָּא דְתַנָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Can it be that **the first clause** of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Meir, **and the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir? Rav Ḥisda said: Indeed, the first clause** of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis and the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. Rav Yosef said:** The entire mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and he formulates** the mishna **according to** different ***tanna'im*.** It is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis with regard to a High Priest consecrated by donning multiple garments, and in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir with regard to a former High Priest.

The *gemara* has now established that the *mishnah*'s first clause (limiting the *par* to the oil-anointed *kohen gadol*) is not Rabbi Meir's position, while the second clause (a past *kohen gadol* retains virtually full status except for two things) is Rabbi Meir's position. How can a single *mishnah* reflect two incompatible opinions?

Rav Chisda answers: *ltirutza, reisha Rabbanan u-seifa Rabbi Meir* — the *mishnah* is indeed composed of two separate teachings that were stitched together by Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi. The first clause represents the rabbinic position against Rabbi Meir; the second clause represents Rabbi Meir's own position. This is not without precedent — Rebbe drew on multiple *tanna* sources when editing the *mishnah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The entire mishna **is** stating the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, and he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir with regard to one** matter **and disagrees with him with regard to one** matter.

Rav Yosef disagrees. He does not wish to attribute the *mishnah* to two incompatible sources set side by side. Rather, the entire *mishnah* represents Rebbe's own considered position — *nasiv lei aliba d'tannai* — he selects the non-Rabbi Meir view on the first issue and the Rabbi Meir view on the second issue, producing a coherent hybrid ruling.

Rava offers a third answer: *Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *mishnah* reflects the view of Rabbi Shimon, who agrees with Rabbi Meir on one point (that a past *kohen gadol* retains nearly full status) and disagrees with him on another (that the *par* is limited to the oil-anointed *kohen gadol*). How do we identify the *beraita* as Rabbi Shimon's? Because one of its listed distinctions — that a *kohen gadol* is exempt from the *korban oleh v'yored* for *tumat mikdash* — was taught in an earlier *daf* specifically in the name of Rabbi Shimon. This identification anchors the entire *beraita* to Rabbi Shimon and resolves the attribution of the *mishnah*.

## KG vs. Regular Kohen

### Seven Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט אֵלּוּ הֵם: פַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת, וּפַר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה, וְלֹא פּוֹרֵעַ וְלֹא פּוֹרֵם. אֲבָל הוּא פּוֹרֵם מִלְּמַטָּה וְהַהֶדְיוֹט מִלְמַעְלָה. וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא לִקְרוֹבִים, וּמוּזְהָר עַל הַבְּתוּלָה, וְאָסוּר בָּאַלְמָנָה, וּמַחְזִיר אֶת הָרוֹצֵחַ,
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **These are matters** with regard to **which** there are differences **between a High Priest and an ordinary priest:** The High Priest brings **the bull that comes for any of the mitzvot, and the bull of Yom Kippur, and the** daily **tenth of an ephah** meal-offering. **And he may not grow** his hair **long and may not rend** his garments as expressions of mourning; **but** he **rends** his garment **from below** in an inconspicuous manner, **and the ordinary** priest rends his garment **from above,** in the typical manner. **And** the High Priest **may not render himself impure** with impurity imparted by a corpse even **in** the event that one of his **relatives** dies, **and** he is **warned to** marry **a virgin, and** it is **prohibited** for him **to** marry **a widow, and** when he dies **he restores the** unwitting **murderer** to his home from the city of refuge.

Having established that the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Shimon's view — consistent with Rabbi Meir in one area and diverging in another — the *gemara* now examines the *beraita* that enumerates the full range of differences between a *kohen gadol* and a *kohen hedyot*. The *beraita* lists the distinctions systematically, and several of these warrant closer discussion.

### Mourning Laws

The *kohen gadol* may not perform the standard signs of mourning when an immediate relative dies. *V'lo poreiah v'lo porem* — he does not let his hair grow wild and does not rend his garments at the neckline. However, he does perform a modified form of rending: *porem milimata* — he tears from the bottom of his garment, rather than the top. A *kohen hedyot* who rends does so at the top, near the neck. The *kohen gadol* likewise may not become *tamei* even for his closest relatives — mother, father, son, or daughter — even in the moment of their death.

### Marriage Laws

The *kohen gadol* is *muzhar al habesulah* — he has an obligation to marry a virgin. The word *muzhar* is notable: it connotes a positive commandment rather than a strict prohibition. And he is *asur b'almanah* — prohibited from marrying a widow, a restriction that does not apply to the ordinary *kohen*, who may marry a widow but not a *grushah*.

### Murderer & Vestments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל [וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק]. מַקְרִיב חֵלֶק בָּרֹאשׁ, וְנוֹטֵל חֵלֶק בָּרֹאשׁ, וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ בִּשְׁמוֹנָה כֵּלִים. וְכׇל עֲבוֹדַת יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים אֵינָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה אֶלָּא בּוֹ, וּפָטוּר עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** the High Priest **sacrifices** offerings even as **an acute mourner** on the day that a close relative dies, **but he may not partake** of the offerings on that day **and he does not receive a share** of those offerings. **He sacrifices a portion at the head** of the priests, i.e., whenever he chooses, **and takes a portion at the head,** i.e., he takes a portion from any offering that he chooses. **And he performs** the Temple service **wearing eight** priestly **garments, and the entire Yom Kippur service is valid only** when performed **by him, and he is exempt from** bringing a sliding-scale offering for the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods.

Two further distinctions: *umachazir es harotze'ach* — when the *kohen gadol* dies, a murderer who has been confined to an *ir miklat* (city of refuge) is released and may return home. And the *kohen gadol* wears eight vestments, while the *kohen hedyot* wears only four.

### Eating in Mourning

A High Priest continues to bring *korbanot* even on the day he suffers an immediate bereavement — *u'makriv onen* — yet he may not eat from them — *v'eino ochel*. This is directly connected to the episode in this week's *parsha* where Aharon, following the deaths of his sons, does not eat the *korban chatas*, and Moshe accepts his explanation. The *gemara* explicitly ties Aharon's conduct to this *halacha*: look at Rashi on the verse *He-achalti chatas hayom hayita be'einai Hashem* — the connection to an *onen*'s obligation is precisely there.

### Tumat Mikdash

A final distinctive law: *upoter tumat mikdash vekodashav* — the *kohen gadol* does not bring the sliding-scale *korban oleh v'yored* for the sin of *tumat mikdash*, i.e., entering the *mikdash* or eating from *kodashim* in a state of *tumah*. This exemption was taught in the name of Rabbi Shimon in an earlier *daf*, and it is precisely this teaching that allows the *gemara* to identify the entire *beraita* as Rabbi Shimon's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן נוֹהֲגִים בִּמְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים חוּץ מִפַּר הַמֵּבִיא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת, וְכוּלָּן נוֹהֲגִים בְּמָשִׁיחַ שֶׁעָבַר חוּץ מִפַּר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים וַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה, וְכוּלָּן אֵין נוֹהֲגִים בִּמְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה חוּץ מֵחֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים הָאֲמוּרִים בַּפָּרָשָׁה: לֹא פּוֹרֵעַ וְלֹא פּוֹרֵם, וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא לִקְרוֹבִים, וּמוּזְהָר עַל הַבְּתוּלָה, וְאָסוּר בָּאַלְמָנָה, וּמַחֲזִיר אֶת הָרוֹצֵחַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And all** these *halakhot* **are in effect with regard to** the High Priest who is consecrated by donning **multiple garments, except for the bull the** High Priest **brings for all the mitzvot. And all** these *halakhot* **are in effect with regard to a former anointed** High Priest, **except for the bull of Yom Kippur and the tenth of an ephah. And all** these *halakhot* **are not in effect with regard to** a priest **anointed for war, except for the five matters stated in the portion** where the *halakhot* of the High Priest are enumerated (see Leviticus, chapter 21): **He may not grow** his hair **long and may not rend** his garments, **and he may not render himself impure** with impurity imparted by a corpse even **in** the event that one of his **relatives** dies, **and he is warned to** marry **a virgin, and** it is **prohibited** for him **to** marry **a widow, and** when he dies **he restores the** unwitting **murderer** to his home from the city of refuge; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say: He does not restore the** unwitting **murderer** to his home.

The *beraita* concludes with two summary statements. *V'chulan nohegin bimruveh begadim* — all of these laws apply equally to a *kohen gadol* who attained his status through vestments rather than anointing, *chutz mipara mevi'ah al kol hamitzvot* — except for the *par* brought for universal transgression. And *v'chulan nohegin bimshiach she'avar* — all of these laws apply equally to a past *kohen gadol* who no longer serves — *chutz mipara Yom HaKippurim v'asitis haeifah* — except for the Yom Kippur *par* and the daily *minchas chavitin*. These two summary clauses correspond precisely to the two clauses of the *mishnah*, and since we can identify this *beraita* as Rabbi Shimon's, Rava's resolution stands.

## KG Milchama

### Identifying War Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מֵחֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים הָאֲמוּרִים בַּפָּרָשָׁה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו״ – זֶה כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, ״אֲשֶׁר יוּצַק עַל רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה״ – זֶה מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה, ״וּמִלֵּא אֶת יָדוֹ לִלְבֹּשׁ אֶת הַבְּגָדִים״ – זֶה מְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים. עַל כּוּלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״רֹאשׁוֹ לֹא יִפְרָע וּבְגָדָיו לֹא יִפְרֹם וְעַל כׇּל נַפְשֹׁת מֵת לֹא יָבֹא״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: And all these *halakhot* are not in effect with regard to a priest anointed for war, **except for the five matters stated in the portion** where the *halakhot* of the High Priest are enumerated. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: It is **as the Sages taught: "And the priest who is greater than his brethren"** (Leviticus 21:10); **this is a High Priest. "Upon whose head the anointing oil is poured"; this is** the priest **anointed for war. "And who is consecrated to don the garments"; this is** the High Priest who is consecrated by donning **multiple garments. With regard to all of them,** the verse **states: "He shall neither let the hair of his head grow, nor rend his garments, neither shall he come upon any dead body"** (Leviticus 21:10–11).

The *beraita* also addresses the *kohen mashiach milchama* — the kohen anointed to accompany Israel into war. *V'chulan einam nohegin ela bimshiach milchama* — none of the above special statuses apply to the *kohen mashiach milchama*, except for five matters stated at the opening of *Parshas Emor*. Where does the *gemara* derive the inclusion of the *mashiach milchama* and the *merubeh begadim* within the *parsha's* opening address? From the verse: *v'hakohen hagadol me'echav* — and the *kohen* great among his brothers. This is the oil-anointed *kohen gadol*. *Asher yutzak al rosho shemen hamishcha* — upon whose head the anointing oil was poured — this, read expansively, includes the *mashiach milchama*, who also receives oil. *Umilei et yado lilbosh et habegadim* — who has consecrated his hands by wearing the vestments — this includes the *merubeh begadim*. Once the verse includes someone who wears the vestments but is not anointed, the reference to anointing must be doing something else — namely, including the *mashiach milchama*.

### Five Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהוּ כּוּלָּן מַקְרִיבִין אוֹנְנִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי נֵזֶר שֶׁמֶן מִשְׁחַת אֱלֹהָיו עָלָיו״ – ״עָלָיו״ וְלֹא עַל חֲבֵירוֹ. וְאַחַר שֶׁחָלְקוּ הַכָּתוּב, יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא מְצֻוֶּוה עַל הַבְּתוּלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **all of** these priests **sacrifice** offerings as **acute mourners.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For the consecration of the anointing oil of his God is upon him"** (Leviticus 21:12), from which it is derived: **"Upon him,"** the High Priest, **but not upon another** priest. **And after the verse differentiated** the priest anointed for war, one **might** have thought **that he would not be commanded to** marry **a virgin,** a mitzva that appears in the following verse. Therefore, **the verse states** with the letter *vav* as a prefix: **"And he** shall take a wife in her virginity" (Leviticus 21:13), which serves to include the priest anointed for war.

And then the *pasuk* continues: *al kulan hu omer rosho lo yifra uvegadav lo yifrom ve'al kol nafshot met lo yavo le'aviv ule'imo lo yitama* — for all of them it says: he shall not let his hair grow wild, shall not rend his garments, shall not become *tamei* to any corpse, not even for his father or mother. These prohibitions apply to all three categories: oil-anointed *kohen gadol*, *merubeh begadim*, and *mashiach milchama*. The *beraita* records that the list of five laws (*lo poreiah v'lo porem, eino mitamei likrovim, metzuvah al habesulah, asur b'almanah*) applies to the *mashiach milchama*, with the return of the murderer from the *ir miklat* as a sixth item debated by Rabbi Yehudah and the sages.

### Exclusion Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״וְהוּא אִשָּׁה בִבְתוּלֶיהָ יִקָּח״, אַחַר שֶׁחִלֵּק הַכָּתוּב, רִיבָּה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁעָבַר מֵחֲמַת קִרְיוֹ, מֵחֲמַת מוּמִין מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to the verse **"And he shall take a wife in her virginity." Once the verse had distinguished** between a priest anointed for war and a High Priest in terms of bringing offerings as an acute mourner, it **included** the priest anointed for war with regard to the *halakhot* that follow; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: I have** derived **only** that the High Priest **who stepped down** for a brief period **due to his seminal emission** is commanded to marry a virgin. **From where** do I derive that a High Priest who stepped down **due to blemishes,** who will remain disqualified, is commanded to marry a virgin? Therefore, **the verse states: "And he,"** to include a High Priest who stepped down due to blemishes. According to Rabbi Akiva, there is no source available to include the priest anointed for war.

However, the *gemara* raises a question: might the *mashiach milchama* also continue performing the Temple service even when bereaved, as the *kohen gadol* does? The verse *umin hamikdash lo yetze* — he shall not leave the *mikdash* — implies that he should remain and continue working. The *gemara* answers: the verse continues *ki nezer shemen mishchat Elohav alav* — because the anointing oil of God is upon him — *alav v'lo al chaveiro* — upon him and not upon one similar to him. The *mashiach milchama* is "similar" in that he too receives oil, but the obligation to remain and serve in the *mikdash* when bereaved applies only to the full *kohen gadol*. The *mashiach milchama* is thus excluded from this particular status by the limiting force of *alav*.

But if the *mashiach milchama* is excluded from the verse *ki nezer shemen mishchat Elohav alav*, one might think he is also excluded from the very next verse — *v'hu isha b'visuleha yikach* — the obligation to marry only a virgin. The *gemara* answers: the *vav* of *v'hu* comes to re-include him. After the exclusion, the *vav* restores the obligation of marrying a virgin to the *mashiach milchama* as well.

This interpretation is itself a point of dispute between *tannaim*. Rabbi Yishmael reads the *vav* of *v'hu* as including the *mashiach milchama* — after the *pasuk* excluded him from the *mikdash* obligation, it includes him back in for the marriage restriction. Rabbi Akiva reads the *vav* differently: *ein li ela she'avar machmas kirya; avar machmas mum minayin?* — I know only that a *kohen gadol* who was removed because of an impurity retains his status; how do I know that one removed because of a physical blemish also retains that status? Rabbi Akiva uses the *vav* of *v'hu* to include a *blemished former kohen gadol* — extending his marital obligations to him — rather than to re-include the *mashiach milchama*.

## Leprous KG

### Leprosy Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁנִּצְטָרַע, מַהוּ בְּאַלְמָנָה? מִידְחָא דָּחֵי, אוֹ מִיפְטָר פָּטַר? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman:** In the case of **an anointed** High Priest **who was afflicted with leprosy, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to** marrying **a widow?** Is he temporarily **disqualified** from service, i.e., does he remain a High Priest and is it therefore prohibited for him to marry a widow? **Or** is he totally **absolved** of his status as High Priest and therefore it is permitted for him to marry a widow? The answer **was not available to him.**

The *daf* concludes with a question posed by Rava to Rav Nachman. *Mashiach shenitzara mahu b'almanah?* — if a *kohen gadol* contracted *tzara'as* and thereby became incapacitated from Temple service, may he now marry a widow? The question turns on how we understand the mechanism of his restriction. *Midcha dachi* — did the status of *kohen gadol* categorically push away the category of *almanah* from him, such that it is a permanent bar? Or is it *miptar patar* — merely a contextual exemption, an ongoing prohibition that exists only so long as he actively holds the *kehunah gedolah*? If the latter, once he is no longer serving as *kohen gadol* — due to *tzara'as* — the prohibition would lapse and he could marry a widow. Rav Nachman did not have the answer.

### Blemish Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִימְנִין הָוֵי יָתֵיב רַב פָּפָּא וְקָמִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן לְרַב פָּפָּא, תְּנֵינָא: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁעָבַר מֵחֲמַת קִרְיוֹ, עָבַר מֵחֲמַת מוּמִין מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהוּא״. קָם נַשְּׁקֵיהּ בְּרֵישֵׁיהּ וִיהַיב לֵיהּ בְּרַתֵּיה.
>
> **Translation:** On another **occasion, Rav Pappa was sitting and he raised** the same **dilemma. Huna, son of Rav Naḥman, said to Rav Pappa: We learn** in a *baraita*: **I have** derived **only** that the High Priest **who stepped down** for a brief period **due to his seminal emission** is commanded to marry a virgin. **From where** do I derive that a High Priest who stepped down **due to blemishes,** who will remain disqualified, is commanded to marry a virgin? **The verse states: "And he."** Leprosy is an example of a blemish, so it is prohibited for a High priest afflicted with leprosy to marry a widow. When Rav Pappa heard this *baraita*, **he arose** and **kissed him on his head and gave him his daughter** to marry, due to his appreciation for his expertise in Torah study.

On another occasion, Rav Papa was raising the same question. Huna the son of Rav Nachman addressed him: *tenina* — we have already learned the answer in a *beraita*. The *beraita* states: *ein li ela she'avar machmas kirya; avar machmas mum minayin? Talmud lomar v'hu.* I would have known only that a *kohen gadol* who was removed because of impurity retains his *kohen gadol* obligations — including the restriction against marrying a widow. How do I know that one removed because of a *mum* — a physical blemish — retains those same obligations? The verse teaches it with the word *v'hu*. Since the *beraita* establishes that even a blemished, non-serving *kohen gadol* retains his marital restrictions, the same reasoning applies to one incapacitated by *tzara'as*: the restriction against marrying a widow remains in force. The status of *kohen gadol* is a categorical displacement, not merely a situational exemption.

### Rav Papa Accept

Rav Papa accepted the ruling. He rose, kissed Huna on the head, and — since the discussion had turned to marriage — gave his daughter to him in marriage. The *daf* concludes on this note.
