# Menachot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kosher Food Principle

### Non-Kosher Food Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״מִן הַבָּקָר״ לְמַטָּה, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, אֶלָּא לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is derived from a passage in the Torah that discusses burnt offerings: "You shall bring your offering from the cattle, even from the herd or from the flock" (Leviticus 1:2), that certain animals are prohibited for sacrifice upon the altar. **When it states later,** in the next verse: "If his offering is a burnt offering **of the herd"** (Leviticus 1:3), this is difficult, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** this, as it was already written earlier. **Rather,** this serves **to exclude an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*]** from being brought as an offering.

The daf picks up at the bottom of amud bet with a discussion that was largely absent from Pesachim: the question of what qualifies as valid food for a *korban*, rooted in the *pasuk* in Yechezkel known as *mimashkei Yisrael*, from which we derive that *korbanot* must be brought exclusively from *kosher* food. The Gemara opens a *braita* that entertains the possibility — however remote — that one might have brought a *korban* from a *treifa*. The Gemara's structure is clear: one could have constructed a *kal va-chomer* ruling that a *treifa* is unacceptable, but if some counter-argument existed, we would then require an explicit *pasuk* to settle the matter. The Gemara's task, accordingly, is to surface whatever argument might have supported the use of a *treifa* — and in doing so, it maps out the full range of exceptions to the principle that *korbanot* must come from *kosher* food.

### Omer Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁמּוּתֶּרֶת לַהֶדְיוֹט – אֲסוּרָה לַגָּבוֹהַּ, טְרֵיפָה שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַהֶדְיוֹט – אֵין דִּין שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַגָּבוֹהַּ? חֵלֶב וָדָם יוֹכִיחוּ, שֶׁאֲסוּרִין לַהֶדְיוֹט וּמוּתָּרִין לַגָּבוֹהַּ!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* questions the need for this derivation: **But could this not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference? And if a blemished** animal, **which is permitted to an ordinary person [*lehedyot*]** for consumption, is nevertheless **prohibited** as an offering **for the Most High** (see Leviticus 22:19), then certainly with regard to **a *tereifa*, which is forbidden to an ordinary person** for consumption (see Exodus 22:30), **is it not logical that it is prohibited for the Most High?** The *baraita* responds: **Fat [*ḥelev*] and blood prove** that this *a fortiori* inference is not valid, **as they are forbidden to an ordinary person and** yet **they are permitted for the Most High.**

The Gemara asks: *mai im hashavta?* — what could one have argued to think that a *treifa* might be acceptable? Rav answers: *minchat omer tochiach* — the *omer* offering proves it. Every year the *omer* is brought while the new grain (*chadash*) is still not yet permitted for general consumption. At the moment of its offering, a layperson cannot eat from it — *she'assur le-hedyot* — yet it is valid as a *mincha* — *u-mutar le-gavoha*. This might seem to demonstrate that a *korban* can be brought from something otherwise forbidden.

The Gemara rejects this immediately: *mah le-minchat omer she-ken materet* — the *minchat omer* is distinguishable because it is not merely an instance of a forbidden item being brought; it is the very mechanism by which the prohibition on *chadash* is lifted. The *omer* makes itself, and other things, permissible. That exceptional causal power sets it apart, and one cannot derive from it a general license to bring *korbanot* from forbidden food.

### Shemitah & Omer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשְּׁבִיעִית – שְׁבִיעִית נָמֵי, שֶׁכֵּן מַתֶּרֶת סְפִיחִין בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: סְפִיחִין אֲסוּרִים בִּשְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Although the *omer* meal offering generally renders the new crop permitted, the *omer* brought **during a Sabbatical Year** does not render the crop permitted, as it is prohibited to plant during the Sabbatical Year, and consequently there is no new crop for the *omer* offering to permit. The Gemara counters this suggestion: The *omer* meal offering brought during **a Sabbatical Year also** renders something permitted, **as it permits produce that grew without being purposely planted [*sefiḥin*] during the Sabbatical Year.** The Gemara responds: Nevertheless, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that ***sefiḥin* are prohibited during the Sabbatical Year,** the *omer* meal offering brought during a Sabbatical Year does not render the new crop permitted for consumption, and yet it is permitted for the Most High. A verse is therefore necessary to derive that a *tereifa* may not be sacrificed.

The Gemara pivots: what about *shemitah* year grain? If, following the period of *bi'ur*, the grain is no longer fully permissible, and the *omer* is brought from such grain, does that not show that a *korban* can come from restricted food?

Rashi notes that we are discussing the period after *bi'ur*, when the grain is no longer available to animals in the field. Tosafot raises a pointed difficulty: the period of *bi'ur* for grain would occur around *Pesach* time, precisely when the grain is only just ripening — how can it already be past *bi'ur*? One might suggest we are dealing with the year following *shemitah*, yet that too creates complications, since the new crop would not inherently carry *shemitah* restrictions. The Gemara's formulation is admittedly compressed.

The Gemara responds: *shemitah* grain at least makes *sevichim* — self-grown produce — permissible. But the Gemara raises a further objection from the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *sevichim* in *shemitah* are forbidden even when they grow on their own. The practical concern is that allowing people to eat self-grown produce would create a pretext to claim all harvested grain grew spontaneously. Here the Gemara treats this prohibition as *d'oraita*, and therefore concludes that according to Rabbi Akiva, even *shemitah* grain brought for the *omer* is not fully permissible — meaning we would have an example of a *korban* brought from something genuinely restricted.

### Treifa Self-Permit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נָמֵי לִפְרוֹךְ, מָה לְמִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁכֵּן מַתֶּרֶת חָדָשׁ בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Abba said to Rav Ashi: According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva as well, let us refute** the statement of Rav, as **what** is notable **about the *omer* meal offering?** It is notable **in that** the *omer* **permits** the **new** crop for consumption **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, where the prohibitions of the Sabbatical Year do not apply.

This reasoning eliminates the *omer* as proof that *korbanot* can come from forbidden food. The *omer* remains a unique case because of its inherent permissive power, not simply an instance of bringing something forbidden.

Then comes an almost dizzying counterpoint:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: חָדָשׁ בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, שֶׁכֵּן בָּאָה לְהַתִּיר לָאו שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And even according to the one who says** that the consumption of produce from the **new** crop grown **outside of Eretz** Yisrael is **not** prohibited **by Torah law,** Rav's statement can be refuted in another manner: What is notable about the *omer* meal offering? It is notable **in that** the *omer* **comes to permit a prohibition** that applies to a substance **that** was previously **within it,** i.e., the burning upon the altar of a handful from the *omer* meal offering renders the remainder of the meal offering permitted to the priests, whereas a *tereifa* is entirely forbidden.

Rav Acha mi-Difti to Ravina asks — if so, let a *treifa* itself be brought and let it permit its own prohibition. This is a peculiar suggestion, and Tosafot openly admits he does not understand it. The *omer* permits *chadash*, which is at least an explicit function established by the Torah. But what prohibition would a *treifa korban* unlock? Rashi suggests the *hava amina* was that bringing the *treifa* would render its own flesh permitted afterward, but why one would have assumed this is unclear. The final word of the last Tosafot on this point states plainly: *ve-halo eino matir issur shevi'it* — and so too we cannot say a *treifa korban* would permit the *treifa* prohibition. Tosafot does not resolve the question, and the lecturer declines to improve upon Tosafot.

The Gemara concludes this thread with the definitive framing:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מָה לְמִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁכֵּן מִצְוָתָהּ בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one can **refute** the statement of Rav **like this: What** is notable **about the *omer* meal offering?** It is notable **in that its mitzva is in this** manner, i.e., the Torah requires the *omer* meal offering to be brought from the new crop in order to permit the new crop for consumption. By contrast, there is no mitzva to sacrifice specifically a *tereifa*.

*mah le-minchat omer she-ken mitzvata be-khach* — the *minchat omer* is exceptional because the Torah itself designated this as its nature. Where the Torah explicitly declares that this is how the *korban* is to be brought — from *chadash*, from *chelev*, from whatever — that is not evidence for a general rule allowing *korbanot* from forbidden things. The exceptions exist precisely because the Torah mandated them. Without such explicit mandate, no permission follows.

### Incense Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מְפַטֵּם הַקְּטֹרֶת יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאָסוּר לַהֶדְיוֹט וּמוּתָּר לַגָּבוֹהַּ. מְפַטֵּם גַּבְרָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said** that this is the response alluded to at the end of the *baraita*: The *halakha* that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice must be derived from a verse **because it can be said** that **the one who prepares the incense proves** that the *halakha* concerning a *tereifa* may not be derived by the *a fortiori* inference, **as** this is **prohibited to an ordinary person, and** is nevertheless **permitted for the Most High.** The Gemara questions the terminology of Reish Lakish: But **the one who prepares** the incense **is a person.** How can it be said that a person is prohibited to an ordinary person?

Resh Lakish offers a different argument for why one might have thought *treifa* is acceptable: *mefatem ha-ktoret yokhiach* — the preparation of the incense proves it. The making of *ketoret* is forbidden to a layperson — *she'assur le-hedyot* — yet permitted for sacred use — *u-mutar le-gavoha*.

The Gemara initially objects that the incense-maker is a person, not an object being brought as a *korban*. The issue at hand is whether the *object* being offered can be forbidden. The Gemara refines: the *pitum ha-ktoret* itself — the finished incense compound — is what is *assur le-hedyot* and *mutar le-gavoha*. Indeed, the Torah states *ish asher ya'aseh chamoh*, *ve'ish asher yikach chamoh*, *va'asher ya'aseh mimenah lehariach bah ve-nichrat me'amav* — one who makes or smells such incense for private use is subject to *karet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, פִּטּוּם הַקְּטֹרֶת יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאָסוּר לַהֶדְיוֹט וּמוּתָּר לַגָּבוֹהַּ. מָה לְפִטּוּם הַקְּטֹרֶת, שֶׁכֵּן מִצְוָתוֹ בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Reish Lakish meant that **the preparation of the incense proves** it, **as** it is **prohibited** to prepare the incense mixture for use **by an ordinary person** (see Exodus 30:37), **and** yet it is **permitted** to do so **for the Most High.** The Gemara refutes this claim: **What** is notable **about preparation of the incense?** It is notable **in that its mitzva is in this** manner. By contrast, there is no mitzva to sacrifice specifically a *tereifa*.

Still, the Gemara dismisses this example with the now-familiar response: *mah le-pitum ha-ktoret — she-ken mitzvata be-khach*. The *ketoret* is not forbidden food that is being brought despite its prohibition; it is something whose very prohibition for private use derives from the fact that it was designated for God. The logic inverts: the reason a layperson cannot make or smell it is because it belongs to divine service. This is structurally different from a *treifa*, which is forbidden for wholly independent reasons.

### Shabbat for God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא אָמַר, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: שַׁבָּת תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַהֶדְיוֹט וּמוּתֶּרֶת לַגָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Ravina, said** that this is the response of the *baraita*: The *halakha* that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice must be derived from the verse, **because it can be said** that **Shabbat proves** that the *halakha* concerning a *tereifa* cannot be derived by the *a fortiori* inference, **as it is prohibited for an ordinary person** to perform labor on Shabbat, **and** yet the labor involved in the Temple service is **permitted** on Shabbat **for the Most High.** Without the verse, one might similarly conclude that a *tereifa* is permitted for the Most High despite the fact that it is prohibited for consumption.

The son of Ravina raises a broader case: *Shabbat tochiach* — Shabbat proves the point. One may not violate Shabbat for lay purposes — *assur le-hedyot* — yet the Temple service overrides Shabbat — *mutar le-gavoha*. Here the argument shifts from the nature of forbidden objects to the nature of forbidden actions: if an action is prohibited for a layperson yet permitted for divine service, does that not demonstrate that restrictions applying to ordinary life do not apply to *korbanot*?

The Gemara counters:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְשַׁבָּת, שֶׁכֵּן הוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ אֵצֶל הֶדְיוֹט בְּמִילָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **What** is notable **about Shabbat?** It is notable **in that the general prohibition** against labor on Shabbat **was permitted with regard to an ordinary person in** the case of **circumcision,** as the mitzva of circumcision must be performed in its proper time, even on Shabbat, despite the fact that the act of circumcision is generally prohibited on Shabbat.

Shabbat already has exceptions for laypeople, such as the *mitzvah* of *milah*. The Gemara then immediately asks: is *milah* really for lay purposes? *Milah mitzvah hi* — it is a divine commandment. So the counterexample collapses; we have never actually shown that Shabbat is violated for purely private laypeople's needs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ מִילָה צוֹרֶךְ הֶדְיוֹט הוּא? מִילָה מִצְוָה הִיא! אֶלָּא, מָה לְשַׁבָּת שֶׁכֵּן מִצְוָתָהּ בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say** that **circumcision is** considered **a requirement of an ordinary person,** whose performance was exempted from the general prohibition against labor on Shabbat for one's private needs? **Circumcision is a mitzva. Rather,** the statement of Mar, son of Ravina, can be refuted like this: **What** is notable **about Shabbat?** It is notable **in that its mitzva is in this** manner, i.e., the Torah requires that offerings be brought on Shabbat. By contrast, there is no mitzva to sacrifice specifically a *tereifa*.

The final response is once again *mah le-Shabbat she-ken mitzvata be-khach* — the Torah explicitly required *korbanot* that override Shabbat. That is not evidence of a general rule; it is another instance of Torah-sanctioned exception.

### Shatnez in Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, כִּלְאַיִם תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֲסוּרִין לַהֶדְיוֹט וּמוּתָּרִין לַגָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Abba said** that this is the response mentioned in the *baraita*: The *halakha* that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice must be derived from a verse **because it can be said** that the prohibition against **diverse kinds proves** that the *halakha* of a *tereifa* cannot be derived from the *a fortiori* inference, **as it is prohibited for an ordinary person** to wear garments sewn from a mixture of diverse kinds (Deuteronomy 22:11), **and** yet such garments are **permitted for the Most High,** as the belt of the priestly vestments was fashioned from a mixture of diverse kinds.

Rav Adda bar Ahava introduces the case of *kilayim*: the prohibition of *shatnez* — wool and linen mixtures — is forbidden to a layperson, yet the priestly garments, specifically the *avnet*, contain *shatnez*. One who studies *ta'amei ha-mitzvot* might even suggest that the very reason *shatnez* is forbidden to laypeople is its association with sacred priestly garments — a logic that, the lecturer notes, would extend to *techeiles* as well.

The Gemara responds:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְכִלְאַיִם, שֶׁכֵּן הוּתְּרוּ מִכְּלָלָן אֵצֶל הֶדְיוֹט בְּצִיצִית? אַטּוּ צִיצִית צוֹרֶךְ הֶדְיוֹט הִיא? מִצְוָה הִיא! אֶלָּא
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **What** is notable **about** the prohibition against **diverse kinds?** It is notable **in that** the **general prohibition** against wearing a garment sewn from diverse kinds **was permitted in** the case of **an ordinary person with regard to ritual fringes,** as a string of sky-blue wool must be placed on a four-cornered garment even if that garment is made from linen. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say** that **ritual fringes** are considered **a requirement of an ordinary person,** whose performance was exempted from the general prohibition concerning diverse kinds with regard to one's private needs? Placing ritual fringes on a garment **is a mitzva. Rather,** the claim of Rav Adda bar Abba can be refuted like this:

*kilayim* is not absolutely forbidden to laypeople, because one who wears a linen garment must attach wool *tzitzit*, including a *techeiles* string, which must be wool. The Torah's juxtaposition of *lo silbash shatnez tzemer u-fishtim yachdav* and *gedilim ta'aseh lach* teaches that when necessary, *shatnez* is permitted in *tzitzit*.

The Gemara pushes back: *atu tzitzit tzorech hedyot hi?* — is *tzitzit* really a lay purpose? *Mitzvah!* It is a divine obligation. The Gemara answers: *mah le-kilayim she-ken mitzvato be-khach* — the Torah explicitly authorized this.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְכִלְאַיִם שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is notable **about diverse kinds?** It is notable **in that its mitzva is in this** manner, since the belt of the priestly vestments must be sewn from diverse kinds. By contrast, there is no mitzva to sacrifice specifically a *tereifa*.

A natural question follows: is it not better to wear cotton strings than to commit *kilayim*? The answer in the Gemara's world is that *tzitzit* is not a special ritual garment one deliberately acquires; it is an obligation attached to whatever four-cornered garment one happens to own. Once one has a linen garment, one has a *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*, and there is no way to fulfill it with linen on linen if the *techeiles* must be wool. No comparable compulsion ever forces one to bring a *korban* from a *treifa*; one can always redeem or replace the animal.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, לִיהְדַּר דִּינָא וְתֵיתֵי בַּ״מָּה הַצַּד״ – מָה לִמְלִיקָה שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ, חֵלֶב וָדָם יוֹכִיחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said:** The *halakha* that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice must be derived from the verse, **because it can be said: Let** this claim **be derived by** analogy from **the common element** of two sources, as follows: With regard to the question of the *baraita*: **What** is notable **about pinching?** It is notable **in that its sanctity prohibits it,** one can respond: **Fat and blood prove** that this consideration is not enough to reject the *a fortiori* inference, as these are prohibited before they are sanctified and are nevertheless permitted for the Most High.

Having surveyed multiple exceptions — each of which can be individually distinguished — Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi raises the possibility of a *ma ha-tzad ha-shaveh*, a common-denominator argument. Even if no single case alone proves the rule, perhaps from their combination one can derive that forbidden things may be brought as *korbanot*.

### Baal Mum Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְחֵלֶב וָדָם, שֶׁכֵּן בָּאִים מִכְּלַל הֶיתֵּר – מְלִיקָה תּוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the question: **What** is notable **about fat and blood?** They are notable **in that they come from** an item that is **generally permitted,** i.e., the animal from which they come is itself permitted for consumption, one can reply: **Pinching proves** that this consideration is insufficient for a rejection of the *a fortiori* inference, as a bird that was killed by pinching is entirely prohibited for eating, and yet a pinched bird is permitted to the altar.

The Gemara constructs the argument as follows. *Melika* of a bird *olah* demonstrates that a *korban* can be brought from something which is technically a *neveilah* — yet that case can be distinguished: *she-ken kedushatoh asartoh*, its prohibition arose from its own sanctity. *Chelev ve-dam* show that forbidden parts of an otherwise *kosher* animal can be offered — yet that too is distinguishable: *ba'u mi-kelal heter*, the rest of the animal was always permitted. Neither case's distinctive characteristic applies to the other. Therefore *lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh ve-lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh* — their reasons are not interchangeable. The *tzad ha-shaveh* — the common denominator — is that in both cases something forbidden to a layperson is permitted for sacred use: *she'asurin le-hedyot u-mutarin le-gavoha*. So too, one might argue, a *treifa* — forbidden to a layperson — should be permissible as a *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁאֲסוּרִין לַהֶדְיוֹט וּמוּתָּרִין לַגָּבוֹהַּ, אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא טְרֵפָה – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַהֶדְיוֹט תְּהֵא מוּתֶּרֶת לַגָּבוֹהַּ, מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁכֵּן מִצְוָתָהּ בְּכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheisha concludes: **And** accordingly, **the inference has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case; **their common element** is **that they are prohibited** for consumption **to an ordinary person and are** nevertheless **permitted for the Most High.** Therefore, **I will also bring** the case of **a *tereifa*** and say: **Even though is it prohibited** for consumption **to an ordinary person, it should be permitted for the Most High.** It is therefore necessary to derive from a verse that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice. The Gemara rejects this: **What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable in **that** with regard to fat and blood, and pinching, in both cases **its mitzva** is performed **in this** manner.

The Gemara responds: *mah le-ha-tzad ha-shaveh she-ba-hen she-ken mitzvatan be-khach* — the universal feature shared by all the exceptions is that the Torah explicitly designated each one as mandated. That is the one true common denominator, not the surface feature of being forbidden to laypeople. This response closes the *ma ha-tzad* argument and reinforces the need for a *pasuk* to exclude *treifa*.

### Yotzei Dofen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַקְרִיבִין כִּקְרֵיבִין, וּמוּתָּר לַהֶדְיוֹט וְאָסוּר לַגָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa Sava said to Rav Ashi:** But an animal **born by caesarean section proves** that this *a fortiori* inference cannot be rejected based on that consideration, **as with regard to it** the Torah **did not render those who sacrifice like that which is sacrificed,** since an animal born by caesarean section is unfit for sacrifice whereas a priest born in such a manner may perform the Temple service. **And** yet an animal born by caesarean section is **permitted** for consumption **to an ordinary person and prohibited for the Most High.** If so, one cannot reject the *a fortiori* inference because with regard to blemishes the Torah rendered those who sacrifice like that which is sacrificed. Why then is a verse necessary in order to derive that a *tereifa* is unfit?

Rav Ashi then reframes the entire discussion from a different angle. He notes that the original *kal va-chomer* reasoning — which aimed to show a *treifa* is forbidden — was itself vulnerable to challenge. The *kal va-chomer* proceeded from *baal mum*: since a blemished animal may be eaten by a layperson yet is invalid as a *korban*, one can argue *a fortiori* that a *treifa* — which a layperson may not eat — should certainly be invalid. But Rav Ashi observes that *baal mum* is a more encompassing disqualification than *treifa*: not only is a blemished animal invalid as a *korban*, but a *kohen* who has a *mum* is also disqualified from performing the service — *assur ba-makriv ki-kerev*. Perhaps the reason a *baal mum* animal is invalid is specifically this amplified scope, and one cannot derive from it that a *treifa* — which presents no such disqualification at the level of the *kohen* — would be invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַקְרִיבִין כִּקְרֵיבִין, וּמוּתָּר לַהֶדְיוֹט וְאָסוּר לַגָּבוֹהַּ. מָה לְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, שֶׁכֵּן אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi responds: **What** is notable **about** an animal **born by caesarean section?** It is notable **in that** such an animal **is not sanctified with firstborn** status, whereas a firstborn animal that was born as a *tereifa* is sanctified. Accordingly, without the verse one might have concluded that a *tereifa* may be sacrificed.

Rav Acha the elder responds to Rav Ashi with the case of *yotzei dofen* — an animal born by Caesarean section. Such an animal is permitted for a layperson to eat — *mutar le-hedyot* — yet cannot be brought as a *korban* — *assur le-gavoha*. Moreover, a *kohen* who was himself born by Caesarean is still a valid *kohen*; the disqualification operates only at the level of the animal. This asymmetry — an animal perfectly acceptable to a layperson, yet rejected by the altar — seems to establish a *kal va-chomer* against *treifa* even more powerfully than *baal mum*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעַל מוּם יוֹכִיחַ, מָה לְבַעַל מוּם שֶׁכֵּן עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַקְרִיבִין כִּקְרֵיבִין, יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן יוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa Sava answers: **A blemished** animal **proves** that this is not the decisive consideration, as it does become sanctified with the sanctity of a firstborn, and it too is permitted for consumption and prohibited for the Most High. And if you say: **What** is notable **about a blemished** animal? It is notable **in that with regard to it** the Torah **rendered those who sacrifice like that which is sacrificed,** one can respond: An animal **born by caesarean section proves** that this consideration is not decisive, as a priest born by caesarean section may perform the Temple service.

The Gemara counters: *ma li yotzei dofen she-ken eino kadosh be-vichora* — a *yotzei dofen* has a more severe invalidity in another domain: it is not sanctified as a firstborn. The verse states *asher yivaled*, which *Chazal* interpret to mean a natural vaginal birth, excluding Caesarean delivery. A *treifa*, by contrast, is still sanctified as a firstborn if born naturally and then becomes *treifa* later, and a *kohen* who is *treifa* remains a valid *kohen*. So while a *yotzei dofen* animal has no *kedushat bechor* at all, a *treifa* animal has no such compounded invalidity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁמּוּתָּרִין לַהֶדְיוֹט וַאֲסוּרִים לַגָּבוֹהַּ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן טְרֵפָה שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַהֶדְיוֹט תְּהֵא אֲסוּרָה לַגָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa Sava concludes: **And** therefore, **the inference has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case; **their common element** is **that they are permitted** for consumption **to an ordinary person and prohibited for the Most High. And all the more so a *tereifa*, which is prohibited to an ordinary person, should be prohibited for the Most High.** If so, the derivation from a verse is unnecessary.

The Gemara then cycles through the now-familiar *chazer ha-din* — the back-and-forth between *baal mum* and *yotzei dofen*, each illuminating what the other lacks, until the *tzad ha-shaveh* is reached: both are *mutar le-hedyot ve-assur le-gavoha*. If even things that a layperson may eat are forbidden on the *mizbe'ach*, then certainly a *treifa*, which a layperson cannot eat, should be forbidden as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתְּרוּ מִכְּלָלָן, תֹּאמַר בִּטְרֵיפָה שֶׁהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi refutes the proof of Rav Aḥa Sava: **What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable **in that their general prohibition was not permitted,** since blemished animals and those born by caesarean section are never permitted for sacrifice. **Will you say** that the same applies **to a *tereifa*, whose general prohibition was permitted,** as will be explained? Accordingly, it is necessary to derive from the verse that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice.

At this point the Gemara raises a sharp counter: *mah le-ha-tzad ha-shaveh she-ba-hen she-ken lo hutru mi-khlalan* — the distinguishing feature common to *baal mum* and *yotzei dofen* is that they are never, under any circumstances, a valid *korban*. But *tomar be-treifa she-hutrah mi-khlalah* — a *treifa*, one might argue, does sometimes get an exception. The Gemara will now investigate what that exception could be.

### Treifa Hidden Flaw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: טְרֵיפָה שֶׁהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ מַאי הִיא? אִילֵימָא מְלִיקָה דְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף לַגָּבוֹהַּ, בַּעַל מוּם נָמֵי בְּעוֹפוֹת אִשְׁתְּרוֹיֵי אִשְׁתְּרִי, תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בַּבְּהֵמָה, וְאֵין תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בָּעוֹפוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** This ***tereifa* whose general prohibition was permitted, what is it,** i.e., to what case is this referring? **If we say** that it is referring to the **pinching of a bird burnt offering for the Most High,** whereby the bird is initially rendered a *tereifa* at the start of the pinching process, and nevertheless it is sacrificed upon the altar, then the same may be said of **a blemished** animal **as well.** As **with regard to birds** it is **permitted** to sacrifice a blemished bird. This is in accordance with the *halakha* that the requirement that an offering must be **unblemished and male** applies **to animal** offerings, **but there is no** requirement that an offering must be **unblemished and male in** the case of **bird** offerings.

Rav Acha son of Rava asks: what is this exception for *treifa*? Is it the *melika* of the bird *olah*? In that procedure, one first breaks the spinal cord — rendering the bird a *treifa* — before actually completing the killing by severing the trachea or esophagus. So a *treifa* state precedes the completed act, yet the *korban* is valid, and the *kohanim* may eat the *chatas ha-of*.

The Gemara objects to the *olat ha-of* example: birds are generally treated more leniently with respect to *korbanot*. The Torah's requirements of *tamut* and *zachrut* — unblemished and male — apply only to mammals. *Ein tamut ve-zachrut be-of* — for birds, these standards are not required. One cannot derive leniency for a mammal *korban* from leniences specific to birds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מְלִיקָה דְּחַטַּאת הָעוֹף לַכֹּהֲנִים – כֹּהֲנִים מִשּׁוּלְחַן גָּבוֹהַּ קָא זָכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this permitting the general prohibition found in the context of a *tereifa* is referring to the *halakha* that the **pinching of a bird sin offering** renders it permitted **to the priests** for consumption despite the fact that it was not slaughtered by cutting its neck with a knife. This claim can be refuted as well, as the **priests receive** their portion **from the table of the Most High,** i.e., they may partake of the sin offering only because it was permitted for sacrifice upon the altar. Accordingly, there is no difference between a *tereifa* due to pinching and a blemished bird, as both were released from their general prohibition in this regard, since both are permitted for sacrifice upon the altar and both are therefore permitted for consumption by the priests. Consequently, the *a fortiori* inference remains valid, and the verse is unnecessary.

But there is another dimension to the bird case: not only is the *olat ha-of* brought in a *treifa* state, but the *kohanim* eat the *chatas ha-of* even though it is a *treifa*. This would appear to represent an instance where the *treifa* prohibition is lifted for ordinary consumption — could that not justify bringing an animal *korban* from a *treifa*?

The Gemara responds: *kohanim mi-shulchan gavoha ka zachu* — when *kohanim* eat *kodashim*, they are eating from God's table. Their consumption is not lay eating; it is part of the sacred process of the *Mikdash*. Since birds are treated leniently as *korbanot* in general, the *kohanim*'s eating of the *treifa* bird is subsumed within that same leniency. No conclusion can be drawn from birds about mammals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא פָּרֵיךְ הָכִי: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁכֵּן מוּמָן נִיכָּר, תֹּאמַר בִּטְרֵיפָה שֶׁכֵּן אֵין מוּמָהּ נִיכָּר! מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, refute** the *a fortiori* inference **like this: What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable **in that** with regard to both a blemished animal and one born by caesarean section **their blemish is noticeable,** as a blemished animal is visibly blemished and it is well known when an animal is born by caesarean section. **Will you say** that they can serve as the source of the *halakha* **of a *tereifa*, whose blemish is not** necessarily **noticeable? Due to that** reason, **the verse:** "Of the herd" (Leviticus 1:3), **was necessary,** to teach that a *tereifa* is unfit for sacrifice.

With the *treifa* exception deflated, the Gemara arrives at what will be the definitive answer. The final counter to the *tzad ha-shaveh* argument from *baal mum* and *yotzei dofen* is: *mah le-ha-tzad ha-shaveh she-ba-hen she-ken muman nikar* — both *baal mum* and *yotzei dofen* have one crucial feature in common: their disqualification is evident. The *mum* is visible; the *yotzei dofen* status, while not physiologically apparent, was known in the community — Tosafot notes that at the time such births were rare and well-documented.

*Tomar be-treifa she-ken ein mumo nikar* — but a *treifa* involves an internal injury that is entirely unknown and unknowable until after slaughter. No one can see it. No one knows about it. Perhaps what truly matters for the *mizbe'ach* is not the binary of *kosher* versus non-*kosher* in the abstract, but rather whether the disqualification is externally perceptible. In the world of the *Mikdash*, aesthetics and visible integrity carry real halachic weight — the entire institution of *baal mum* is oriented around what is discernible. So perhaps a *treifa*, whose flaw is hidden from all human eyes until the animal is slaughtered, should be accepted.

This, the Gemara concludes, is the genuine *hava amina* — and *mishum hachi itzterich kra*, this is precisely why we needed an explicit *pasuk* to exclude *treifa*. The logical arguments alone were insufficient to rule it out.

### Need for Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּטְרֵיפָה מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: ״מִמַּשְׁקֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – מִן הַמּוּתָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** § After trying to prove why a derivation from a verse is necessary, the Gemara questions the very source provided by the *baraita* on 5b for the disqualification of a *tereifa*, i.e., the verse: "Of the herd" (Leviticus 1:3). **But** is the *halakha* that **a *tereifa*** is unfit for sacrifice **derived from here? It is derived from there,** i.e., from the verse: **"From the well-watered pastures of Israel"** (Ezekiel 45:15), from which it is derived that an offering may be brought only **from** that **which is permitted to the Jewish people.**

Having established why we need a *pasuk* to exclude *treifa*, the Gemara immediately raises a further question: *treifa me-hacha nafka?* — do we actually learn the exclusion of *treifa* from this *pasuk* in the Torah? Perhaps we learn it from the *pasuk* of *mimashkei Yisrael* in Yechezkel, which establishes that *korbanot* must be from what is permitted to Israel.

The Gemara acknowledges the point. As a matter of strict logic, one might wonder why we need any *pasuk* in the Torah at all, given the principle derived from Yechezkel. The answer, as the Gemara often treats such *pesukim* from *Nevi'im*, is that the verse in Yechezkel functions as a *giluy milta* — a disclosure or confirmation of a received tradition — rather than as an independent halakhic source. And as Tosafot explains, *mimashkei Yisrael* is the readily available shorthand reference for the entire body of reasoning establishing that non-*kosher* items are invalid as *korbanot*.

### Three Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲבוֹר תַּחַת הַשָּׁבֶט״ – נָפְקָא, פְּרָט לַטְּרֵיפָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹבֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Alternatively, this *halakha* can be **derived from** a verse discussing animal tithe offerings: **"Whatever passes under the rod,** the tenth shall be holy for the Lord" (Leviticus 27:32). This teaches that all animals may be sacrificed as the animal tithe, **excluding a *tereifa*, as it does not pass** under the rod on account of its weakness, and the Sages derived from this the *halakha* that a *tereifa* is unfit for any type of offering.

The Gemara then explains why no single *pasuk* is sufficient and why three are needed. The first is *mimashkei Yisrael* — the principle that *korbanot* must come from what is permitted to Israel. The second is derived from *mikol asher ya'avor tachat ha-shevet* — the verse about *ma'aser behema*, which describes animals that "walk under the rod" as one counts them. A *treifa* is not considered an animal that walks under the rod, since *treifot* are sometimes sickly and may die prematurely; they lack the image of vigorous health. From this we extend the exclusion of *treifa* to all *korbanot*. The third is *min ha-bakar*, which will address a scenario the other two do not cover.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי מִ״מַּשְׁקֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְמַעוֹטֵי הֵיכָא דְּלֹא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, דֻּמְיָא דְּעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, אֲבָל הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר אֵימָא תִּתַּכְשַׁר, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כׇּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲבוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: All of these verses **are necessary, because if** the disqualification of a *tereifa* was derived **from** the verse **"the well-watered pastures of Israel," I would say** that this verse serves **to exclude** a *tereifa* only in a case **where it did not have a period of fitness,** e.g., if it was born a *tereifa* and was therefore never fit for sacrifice. This is **similar to** the case of **the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*] and diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard,** whose disqualification is derived from this verse. **But** with regard to a *tereifa* that **had a period of fitness,** one might **say** that **it should be fit.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "Whatever passes** under the rod," to teach that all animals that do not pass under the rod are unfit for sacrifice, even if they were once fit.

Had we only the *pasuk* of *mimashkei Yisrael*, one might have thought it excludes only something that was *never* kosher — comparable to *orla* or *kilayei ha-kerem*, items forbidden from the very moment of their formation. *Aval hayta lah sha'at ha-kosher* — but if the animal had a period of permissibility before becoming a *treifa*, perhaps that earlier kosher status would allow it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כׇּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲבוֹר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְמַעוֹטֵי הֵיכָא דְּנִטְרְפָה וּלְבַסּוֹף הִקְדִּישָׁהּ, דֻּמְיָא דְּמַעֲשֵׂר. אֲבָל הִקְדִּישָׁהּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִטְרְפָה, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּאַקְדְּשַׁהּ הֲוָה חַזְיָא, אֵימָא תִּתַּכְשַׁר. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מִן הַבָּקָר״, צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And if the Merciful One had written** only: **"Whatever passes** under the rod," **I would say** that this verse serves **to exclude** a *tereifa* only **where it was rendered a *tereifa* and** its owner **subsequently sanctified it.** This is **similar to** the case of animal **tithes,** as this verse is teaching that a *tereifa* cannot be subsequently sanctified as a tithe. **But** if the owner **sanctified it and it was subsequently rendered a *tereifa*,** which means **that at the time when it was sanctified it was fit,** one might **say** that **it should be fit.** Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote: "Of the herd,"** to teach that even an animal that became a *tereifa* after it was already sanctified is unfit for sacrifice. Accordingly, all three verses **are necessary.**

Had we only the *pasuk* of *mikol asher ya'avor tachat ha-shevet*, one might have thought it excludes only a case where the animal became *treifa* before being consecrated — *d'be'idna d'nitreifa, lo akdasha* — since at the moment it was made *treifa* it had no sanctity. But *hikdishah u-levasof nitreifa* — if one first sanctified the animal and it subsequently became *treifa* — then *d'be'idna d'akdeshah chazya hava*, at the moment of consecration it was fully fit. Perhaps the subsequent *treifa* status would not undermine its validity.

Therefore the Gemara concludes: *katav Rachmana min ha-bakar* — the third *pasuk* is needed to rule out even this last case. *Tzerichi* — all three are necessary. Together they establish, comprehensively, that whenever an animal reaches the *mizbe'ach* in a non-*kosher* state — whether it was always *treifa*, whether it became *treifa* before consecration, or whether it became *treifa* after consecration — it cannot serve as a valid *korban*. As Tosafot summarizes, *mimashkei Yisrael* serves as the encompassing rubric to which all of this reasoning is attributed.

## Mincha Invalidities

### Mincha Invalidities List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וְאֶחָד כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת, שֶׁקְּמָצָן זָר, אוֹנֵן, טְבוּל יוֹם, מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים, מְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים, שֶׁלֹּא רָחַץ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו, עָרֵל, טָמֵא, יוֹשֵׁב, עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבֵּי כֵלִים, עַל גַּבֵּי בְהֵמָה, עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלֵי חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Both the meal offering of a sinner and all** other **meal offerings** with regard to **which** the one who **removed their handful** was **a non-priest,** or a priest who was **an acute mourner,** i.e., whose relative died and was not yet buried, or a priest who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and was waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed, or a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments,** or a priest **who had not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete the purification process, or a priest **who did not wash his hands and feet** from the water in the Basin prior to performing the Temple service, or **an uncircumcised** priest, or **a ritually impure** priest, or a priest who removed the handful while **sitting,** or while **standing** not on the floor of the Temple but **upon vessels,** or **upon an animal,** or **upon the feet of another** person; in all these cases the meal offerings are **unfit** for sacrifice.

With the principle of *kosher* food for *korbanot* fully established, the Gemara moves to the next *Mishnah*, which addresses a different dimension of invalidity: not the nature of the food being offered, but the identity and status of the one performing the service. The *Mishnah* draws a parallel to a similar discussion in Zevachim, but with a tighter focus — whereas Zevachim opened at length with the issue of *shelo lishmah*, here we proceed directly to the various categories of individuals whose performance of *kamitza* invalidates the *mincha*.

### Zar & Kamitza

The *Mishnah* states that whether it is a *minchat chotei* or any other *mincha*, if any of the following individuals performed the *kamitza*, the *mincha* is invalid: a *zar* — a non-*kohen*. This warrants pause. We know from *shechita* that a *zar* may perform the slaughter of an animal *korban* — *shechita* is *kasher be-zar*. Yet *kamitza* — the handful-taking from the *mincha*, which is the functional analog of *shechita* — is not *kasher be-zar*.

Two explanations present themselves. The first is that *kamitza* and *melika*, unlike *shechita*, are performed with the *kohen*'s bare hands, which function in the manner of a *kli shares* — a sacred vessel. Since the hands of a non-*kohen* cannot serve in that capacity, the act is invalid. *Shechita*, by contrast, is done with a knife, and at least according to some *rishonim*, it is the knife — a *kli shares* — that is the operative instrument. The second explanation, following Tosafot, is that *shechita lav avodah hi* — *shechita* is not exclusively a *Mikdash* ritual, since one slaughters animals for lay purposes as well. *Kamitza* and *melika*, by contrast, exist only as specific *Mikdash* rites, and therefore require a *kohen*.

### Disqualified Kohanim

Beyond the *zar*, the *Mishnah* enumerates a series of *kohanim* who are themselves disqualified from performing *kamitza*. An *onen* — a *kohen* who has suffered the death of a close relative on that day — is disqualified. A *tvul yom* — one who has immersed in a *mikveh* but has not yet waited for nightfall to complete his purification — is similarly invalid, despite having already immersed. A *mechusar begadim* — a *kohen* not wearing the full and proper *bigdei kehuna* — renders the service invalid. A *mechusar kippurim* — one who has completed the purification process in every respect except for the final *korban*, such as a *zav* whose only remaining step is the offering — is likewise disqualified. The *Mishnah* also includes one who has not performed the required washing of hands and feet (*rechitzat yadayim ve-raglayim*), an *arel* — an uncircumcised *kohen*, even one who cannot be circumcised due to a medical condition — and one who is *tamei*.

Additionally, a *kohen* who performs *kamitza* while *yoshev* — seated — is disqualified, as the verse *la'amod le-shareit* requires the service to be performed standing. And one who stands elevated on a vessel, on an animal, or even on the foot of a fellow *kohen* — *al gabei raglei chaveiro* — is invalid. This last disqualification reflects the requirement that the *kohen*'s feet make direct contact with the floor of the *Mikdash*. The sanctity of the *Mikdash* is transmitted through that physical connection; any barrier between the *kohen*'s bare feet and the *Mikdash* floor severs that link and invalidates the service.

### Left Hand Kamitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמַץ בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל, בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: יַחְזִיר וְיַחֲזוֹר וְיִקְמוֹץ בְּיָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** If the priest **removed the handful with** his **left** hand the meal offering is **unfit. Ben Beteira says: He must return** the handful to the vessel that contains the meal offering **and again remove the handful,** this time **with** his **right** hand.

The *Mishnah* then states independently: *kamatz be-smol — pasul*. If the *kohen* took the *kamitza* with his left hand, it is invalid. Ben Beteira disagrees and says: *yachzir ve-yachzor ve-yikmotz be-yamin* — he should return the flour to the vessel and take the *kamitza* again with his right hand.

The simple reading is that Ben Beteira's dispute is limited specifically to the left-hand case, not to the entire preceding list. Two textual indicators support this: first, the left-hand case is presented as a separate and independent statement, after the list already concluded with *pasul*. Second, Ben Beteira does not say the act is *kasher* — he says one should redo it with the right hand. This distinction may carry conceptual weight. The cases on the list represent genuine *psul avodah* — a fundamentally flawed performance of the service that renders it *pasul* in the strong sense. Left-hand *kamitza*, by contrast, might be understood not as a *pasul* act but as a *non-act* — something so technically off that it does not rise to the level of a recognized service at all, and therefore can simply be disregarded and redone.

The Brisker analytical framework of distinguishing between something labeled *pasul* and something that simply is not *kasher* is relevant here. Ben Beteira may be holding that left-hand *kamitza* falls into a kind of neutral zone: not a positively disqualifying act, but not a valid act either — and therefore reversible. The Gemara, however, will generalize Ben Beteira's position to include the entire list, not only the left-hand case.

One might ask: if the argument is reversibility, several of the cases on the list — such as standing on a colleague's foot — are equally easy to reverse. Both that case and left-hand *kamitza* are minor technical failures. The response may be that the *halacha* draws a line differently than intuition might suggest: standing off the *Mikdash* floor may represent a disconnection from *kedusha* that makes the act substantively invalid, whereas using the wrong limb while otherwise fully qualified and properly standing may be a technical deficiency that does not rise to *psul*. These are interesting distinctions, though admittedly speculative.

### Pebbles in Kometz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמַץ וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ צְרוֹר, אוֹ גַרְגֵּר מֶלַח, אוֹ קוֹרֶט שֶׁל לְבוֹנָה – פָּסוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: הַקּוֹמֶץ הַיָּתֵר וְהֶחָסֵר – פָּסוּל. וְאֵיזֶהוּ הַיָּתֵר – שֶׁקְּמָצוֹ מְבוֹרָץ, וְחָסֵר – שֶׁקְּמָצוֹ בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If a priest **removed the handful** of flour, **and a stone, a grain of salt, or a pinch [*koret*] of frankincense emerged in his hand,** the meal offering is **unfit due to** the fact **that** the Sages **said: The handful that is outsized or that is lacking** is **unfit.** The existence of one of these foreign items in the handful means that the requisite measure of flour is lacking. **And which is the outsized** handful? It is one **where he removed the handful overflowing [*mevoratz*]** in a manner in which his fingers do not hold the flour. **And** which is the **lacking** handful? It is one **where he removed the handful with the tips of his fingers.**

The *Mishnah* turns to the practical mechanics of *kamitza* itself. If, while taking the *kometz*, a small pebble — *tzror* — or a grain of salt — *gargar melach* — or a small piece of the *levona* entered the hand, the *kamitza* is *pasul*. The reason is connected to the general rule: *ha-kometz ha-yater ve-ha-chaser pasul* — if the *kometz* contains too much or too little, it is invalid. A pebble or salt crystal displaces flour and makes the *kometz* deficient — *chaser* — in its content.

What constitutes *yater* — excess? *Shekematzto mevorat* — the *kometz* is overflowing, bursting beyond the hand, whether above, below, or through the fingers. What constitutes *chaser* — deficiency? *Shekematzto be-roshei etzbe'otav* — if the *kohen* merely used his fingertips, scooping only a small pinch rather than a full, properly measured handful.

### Mincha Sinner Phrase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמִתְנָא ״אֶחָד מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וְאֶחָד כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת״? לִיתְנֵי ״כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁקְּמָצָן זָר וְאוֹנֵן״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna **to teach: Both the meal offering of a sinner and all** other **meal offerings? Let it teach: All the meal offerings** with regard to **which** the one who **removed their handful** was **a non-priest or an acute mourner.** Why does the mishna single out the case of the meal offering of a sinner?

The Gemara asks: why does the *Mishnah* need to say both *minchat chotei* and *kol ha-minchos*? One might simply have said "all *minchos*," and that alone would have included the *minchat chotei*. The Gemara answers: *le-Rabbi Shimon itzterich* — this formulation was necessary on account of Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Shimon & Beauty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִיצְטְרִיךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בְּדִין הוּא שֶׁתְּהֵא מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא טְעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא חוֹטֵא נִשְׂכָּר, וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה? שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא קׇרְבָּנוֹ מְהוּדָּר, וּבַדִּין הוּא שֶׁתְּהֵא חַטַּאת חֵלֶב טְעוּנָה נְסָכִים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It was necessary** for the mishna to teach this *halakha* in this manner **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: By right the meal offering of a sinner should require oil and frankincense** like other meal offerings, **so that** the **sinner will not profit. And for what** reason does **it not require** oil and frankincense? So **that his offering will not be of superior quality. And** likewise, **by right** the **sin offering of** forbidden **fat,** i.e., the offering brought by one who unwittingly ate the forbidden fat of a domesticated animal, **should require libations**

The *Mishnah*'s phrasing is required because of a position articulated by Rabbi Shimon in a *baraita*. Rabbi Shimon reasons as follows: *be-din hu she-tehei minchat chotei teu'na shemen u-levona* — strictly speaking, a *minchat chotei* should require oil and *levona*, just like other *minchos*, because *shelo yehei chotei niscar* — the sinner should not benefit by being allowed to bring a cheaper offering. Why then does the *minchat chotei* not require *shemen u-levona*? *Shelo yehei korbano mehudar* — so that the *korban* should not be beautiful and aesthetically impressive. The sin offering should be diminished in appearance, reflecting the gravity of the sin.

By the same token, Rabbi Shimon reasons that a *chatat chelev* should require *nesachim* — the accompanying libations of oil, flour, and wine that accompany *olos* and *shelamim* — so that the sinner does not get off easy. Again, the reason it does not is *shelo yehei korbano mehudar*.

Tosafot raises a difficulty: many *korbanot* have fixed costs, so it is not clear that omitting *shemen u-levona* actually saves the sinner money. Tosafot addresses this more specifically in the context of the *chatas* than the *mincha*, but the question remains open to discussion.

What matters for the *Mishnah*'s formulation is this: since Rabbi Shimon holds that the governing concern for the *minchat chotei* is *shelo yehei korbano mehudar*, one might have thought that even the various *psulim* listed in the *Mishnah* — performed by a *zar*, an *onen*, a *tamei* — should be tolerated or even welcomed in the case of a *minchat chotei*, since the worse and less presentable the performance, the more it aligns with the principle of *shelo yehei korbano mehudar*. The *Mishnah* therefore explicitly says *echad minchat chotei ve-echad kol ha-minchos* — even the *minchat chotei* is subject to the same invalidities.

### Zevachim Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָתָם נָמֵי לִיתְנֵי: אֶחָד חַטַּאת חֵלֶב וְאֶחָד כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ דָּמָן זָר וְאוֹנֵן, וְלֵימָא: לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִצְטְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **there as well,** i.e., with regard to slaughtered offerings, **let** the mishna (*Zevaḥim* 15b) **teach: Both the sin offering of** forbidden **fat and all the slaughtered offerings** with regard to **which** the one who **collected their blood** was **a non-priest or** a priest who is **an acute mourner,** are disqualified. **And let us say** that **it was necessary** to teach the mishna in this manner **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** to emphasize that although the sin offering is not of superior quality, in that it does not require that libations be brought with it, nevertheless it is disqualified if its blood was collected by one unfit for Temple service. Why then does that mishna teach simply: All the slaughtered offerings with regard to which the one who collected their blood was a non-priest are disqualified?

The Gemara presses: if that is the reason, why did the corresponding *Mishnah* in Zevachim not also specify *echad chatat chelev ve-echad kol ha-zevachim*? One could equally have argued that Rabbi Shimon would permit *psulim* in the *chatat* of sin!

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּתְנָא לֵיהּ ״כׇּל״ וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״חוּץ״ – כּוּלְּהוּ מַשְׁמַע, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּתְנָא ״כׇּל״ וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״חוּץ״ – כּוּלְּהוּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its question: **Apparently, since** the *tanna* **teaches** that mishna with the term: **All, and he does not teach: Except, all** offerings **are indicated** by the general disqualification, and there is no need to emphasize the *halakha* with regard to a sin offering, even in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. **Here too,** with regard to meal offerings, **since** the *tanna* **teaches** the mishna with the term: **All, and he does not teach: Except,** this means that **all of them are indicated,** even the meal offering of a sinner. Why then does the mishna specifically mention the meal offering of a sinner?

The Gemara answers: in Zevachim, the *Mishnah* said *kol ha-zevachim* — "all animal offerings" — without exception. When a *Mishnah* says *kol* and lists no exclusions, *kol* is understood comprehensively, leaving no room to infer that any category is treated differently. Here in Menachot, the situation is different. The opening of this very chapter distinguished between a *minchat chotei* and other *minchos* with respect to *shelo lishmah*, creating the impression that the two categories might be treated differently throughout. Given that prior distinction, one might have read the list of invalidities as applying only to regular *minchos* and not to the *minchat chotei*, lest the sinner benefit from a *mehudar* offering. Therefore the *Mishnah* had to explicitly state that both categories are equally subject to these invalidities.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאוֹקֵימְנָא לְרֵישָׁא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, סֵיפָא נָמֵי דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **It was necessary** to teach the mishna in this manner as it might **enter your mind to say: Since I have established that the first clause,** i.e., the mishna on 2a, is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** with regard to **the last clause as well,** i.e., the mishna here, it may be concluded **that** it is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** the *halakha* in this manner, to emphasize that it is in accordance with his opinion.

The Gemara then notes a further wrinkle. The first *Mishnah* of the chapter — which distinguished *minchat chotei* from other *minchos* in the context of *shelo lishmah* — was established as not following Rabbi Shimon. If so, one might conclude that this entire chapter does not represent Rabbi Shimon's view, and that the present *Mishnah* too is not Rabbi Shimon. In that case, the concern about *shelo yehei korbano mehudar* would not apply, and no special reason exists to allow *psulim* in the *minchat chotei*. Accordingly, saying *kol ha-minchos* alone should have been sufficient, without explicitly naming the *minchat chotei*.

The Gemara responds: *ka mashma lan* — nevertheless it was necessary to state it explicitly, lest one think the *seifa* of the *Mishnah* is also not like Rabbi Shimon and therefore does not address the concern that Rabbi Shimon would allow *psulim* by a *minchat chotei*. The explicit mention ensures clarity.

With this discussion concluded, the Gemara turns to the more central question of whether an invalid *kamitza* can be undone and repeated — the question of reversal that underlies Ben Beteira's position and opens the next sustained analysis.

### Invalid Kamitza Can Be Redone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: זָר שֶׁקָּמַץ – יַחְזִיר, וְהָא אֲנַן ״פָּסַל״ תְּנַן? מַאי ״פָּסַל״? פָּסַל עַד שֶׁיַּחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says:** In the case of **a non-priest who removed a handful, he should return** the handful to the meal offering. The Gemara challenges: **But we learned** in the mishna that a non-priest **disqualified** the meal offering by removing a handful from it. The Gemara responds: **What** does the mishna mean when it says: **Disqualified?** It means that the non-priest has **disqualified** the meal offering **until** such time as **he returns** the handful to the meal offering, whereupon a priest fit for the Temple service should again remove a handful from the meal offering and sacrifice it.

Rav interprets the *Mishnah* to mean that an invalid *kamitza* can be returned and redone. The Mishnah's language of *pasul* — disqualified — should be understood as "disqualified until it is rectified by return and redo." This reading opens a window onto Ben Beteira's opinion and explains how all the individuals listed in the *Mishnah* might, according to Ben Beteira, be remediable rather than absolutely disqualifying.
