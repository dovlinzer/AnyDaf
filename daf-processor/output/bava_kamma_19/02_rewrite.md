# Bava Kamma 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzroros & Ha'ada

### Review of Questions

The daf opens by picking up from the bottom of 18b, where the Gemara has been occupied with a series of conceptual questions raised by Rava concerning *tzroros* — the halachic category of pebbles or similar objects scattered by an animal's feet. The Gemara seems almost deliberately committed to leaving these questions unresolved, more invested in the richness of the conceptual territory than in arriving at practical conclusions.

It is worth reviewing the questions that have accumulated. One question concerns the definition of the animal's body and its *koach*: if a force is set in motion, rolls down a hill, and then causes damage, how do we categorize that indirect damage? Another question — raised by Rav Ashi — is what Sumchus would hold in a case two degrees removed. Sumchus does not accept the standard rabbinic framework of *tzroros* as paying half; but if the damage is *koach kocho*, two full degrees removed from the animal, would even Sumchus agree that this constitutes indirect force deserving only half-damages? Or would he maintain that two degrees removed is still considered equivalent to the animal's own *koach*, warranting full payment? This question opens a further one that the Gemara does not explore: what would the *rabbanan* hold about two degrees removed? Would they say the owner is entirely *patur*? Or perhaps a quarter of damages?

The central question under discussion at this point concerns *ha'ada* — warning. With *keren*, an animal that gores is *tam* and pays half until it has done so three times and been warned, at which point it becomes *mu'ad* and the owner pays full. The reason for the progression there is that the initial half-payment reflects the unexpected and not-fully-negligent nature of the act. That rationale does not apply to *tzroros*, where the half-payment reflects the *indirect* nature of the damage rather than unexpectedness. Nevertheless, the Gemara entertains the possibility that *tzroros* structurally follows the pattern of *keren*, such that warning three times (*ha'ada*) could raise the obligation from half to full.

### Sound as Kocho

The Gemara cites a *braita* taught by Rami bar Yechezkel: a chicken that inserts its head into the opening of a glass vessel and produces a sound that shatters it pays *nezek shalem* — full damages. This is a striking case, because the sound emanating from the animal is not the direct body of the animal. Just as we have seen that an animal's bodily discharge is not considered the animal's direct body — making it a matter of *kocho* — so too a sound produced by an animal is, in the most literal sense, a disembodied *koach*. Yet the *braita* assigns full payment.

A related teaching from Rav Yosef: a horse that neighs or a donkey that brays and by doing so shatters vessels pays only *chatzi nezek* — half damages.

The apparent contradiction between these two rulings prompts the Gemara to ask whether the distinction is that the chicken had done this three times — meaning it had been warned three times (*ha'ada*) and therefore the payment rises to full. If so, the debate between the two rulings would map onto the question of *yesh ha'ada l'tzroros* versus *ein ha'ada l'tzroros*: does warning an animal that repeatedly causes *tzroros*-type damage escalate the payment to full?

### Ha'ada for Tzroros

The Gemara immediately pushes back: *lo*, no — this is not a case of warning at all. Both cases involve a single incident, and what they represent is simply the standard dispute between Sumchus and the *rabbanan* applied to the category of sound as *kocho*. The one who rules full payment holds like Sumchus, who maintains that *tzroros* pays full; the one who rules half payment holds like the *rabbanan*.

### Sumchus Debate

But this interpretation raises a difficulty: *ve-ha meshuneh hu* — is this not an unusual act? Even according to Sumchus, an unusual act (*shinui*) should reduce the payment to half. Sticking one's head into a glass vessel and shattering it with the force of one's cry seems highly abnormal. The Gemara answers: *lo, de-ika bezrani* — there were seeds inside the vessel. It is entirely natural for a chicken to insert its head into a container where seeds are present. Since the *act* of inserting the head is natural, it is not considered *meshuneh* — even though in this particular instance the reverberating sound happened to shatter the vessel. This reflects a principle worth noting: *meshuneh* is measured by how natural the initiating act is, not by whether it commonly produces this particular consequence. As long as the animal is doing something it naturally does, the fact that the result was unusual does not render the act a *shinui*.

The upshot is that the question of *ha'ada b'tzroros* remains entirely unresolved.

## Shinui + Tzroros

### Shinui Question

Rav Ashi now poses a related question: *yesh shinui l'tzroros l'veyaneshek o ein shinui l'tzroros l'veyaneshek* — if an animal performs an unusual act (*shinui*) that causes *tzroros*, does that combination reduce the payment to a quarter?

The question is best understood within the larger architecture of *nezek* liability. According to the *rabbanan*, ordinary *tzroros* pays half — it is a form of *regel* liability, reduced by its indirect nature. Separately, an unusual act (*meshuneh* or *shinui*) pays half, treated as a form of *keren* because the animal acted against its nature. What happens when both features are present simultaneously? The logic would be: you would have paid half because of *tzroros*, but since it was a *shinui*, you pay half of what you would otherwise have paid — yielding a quarter. Conversely, one might begin from the *shinui* baseline of half and reduce by half for *tzroros*, reaching the same quarter.

The question is essentially: do we compound the two reductions multiplicatively? If *tzroros* functions as a *knas* — a penalty of sorts, arbitrary in its structure — then perhaps we can. If it is a genuine *mamon* calculation expressing proportional responsibility, the arithmetic of half-of-a-half demands explanation.

It is notable that the Gemara does not raise the parallel question of *tzroros d'keren* — whether an animal with *kavanato l'hazik* (intention to damage) that incidentally scatters pebbles would create *tzroros* liability on the *keren* track. This omission is itself informative: the Gemara seems to treat *meshuneh* as still operating within the *regel* framework, even if it is assigned a half-payment associated with *keren*. Because the initiating act is fundamentally one of walking or normal behavior done unusually — rather than a genuine act of intentional goring — the soil for the *tzroros* question remains *regel*, making the compound question of *shinui* plus *tzroros* a meaningful one within that domain.

### Rava's Implications

The Gemara suggests resolving Rav Ashi's question from Rava's prior question. Rava had asked: *yesh ha'ada l'tzroros o ein ha'ada l'tzroros* — does warning escalate *tzroros* from half to full? If so, the Gemara reasons, this implies that Rava already presupposed an answer to Rav Ashi's question. Specifically: if Rava is asking whether *ha'ada* brings *meshuneh* cases of *tzroros* all the way up to full, then Rava must have assumed that without *ha'ada*, a *meshuneh tzroros* is already at half — not a quarter. This would resolve Rav Ashi's question in the direction of *ein shinui l'tzroros*: no, the *shinui* does not reduce the payment to a quarter.

Rashi and Tosafos grapple with precisely how these two questions interact, and the Gemara seems to assume that Rava's question about *ha'ada* encompasses both the ordinary *tzroros* case and the *meshuneh tzroros* case together.

### Conditional Logic

The Gemara deflects this inference, however: *dilma Rava im timtza lomar ka-amar* — perhaps Rava is speaking conditionally. His question about *ha'ada* is framed as a second-level question contingent on the first: *im timtza lomar ein shinui* — if we can assume that even a *meshuneh* case of *tzroros* remains at half (i.e., if Rav Ashi's question resolves one way), then Rava's further question is whether *ha'ada* raises that half to full. Rava is not presupposing the answer to Rav Ashi; he is stacking conditional hypotheticals. The question of whether *shinui* plus *tzroros* yields a quarter therefore remains entirely open — another *teiku*.

## Koach Kocho

### Degrees of Removal

Rav Ashi raises yet another question, one that was already introduced in the previous session: *koach kocho l'Sumchus — k'kocho dami o lo?* For Sumchus, who insists that ordinary *tzroros* pays full, how does he treat a case two full degrees of separation removed from the animal's body? Does he have a received tradition (*halacha*) establishing the half-payment of *tzroros* only for *koach* — one degree removed — while treating *koach kocho* (two degrees removed) as equivalent to the animal's own force, warranting full payment? Or does he perhaps lack such a tradition entirely, in which case even *koach kocho* might pay full under his view?

### Unresolved Teiku

The Gemara answers: *teiku* — unresolved. An additional dimension that the Gemara leaves entirely unexplored, but which presents itself naturally, is what the *rabbanan* would say about *koach kocho*. Would they hold the owner liable for a quarter? For nothing at all? At some degree of removal — three, four, five steps — the damage surely becomes pure *grama*, entirely indirect causation for which there is no liability. The Gemara seems to assume the possibilities of full or half, yet never raises the possibility of zero, which is intellectually surprising. Presumably there exists a threshold beyond which even Sumchus would concede that indirection has become so attenuated as to eliminate liability entirely — but where that threshold lies is never mapped.

## Mishna Reading

### Mishna Cases

The Gemara now returns to the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* reads: *haysa meva'etes* — if an animal was kicking — *o shehaysa tzroros menatzin mitachas ragleiha v'shibra et hakelim meshalem chatzi nezek* — or if pebbles were scattering from under its feet and it broke vessels, it pays half damages. This combining of two cases in a single clause creates an interpretive question that the Gemara now takes up.

### Two Readings

*Ibayah lehu: heichi ka-amar?* — how does one read this *Mishnah*? The first possible reading: these are two entirely independent scenarios. Scenario one — *haysa meva'etes v'hizika biv'itah* — the animal was kicking and directly broke something by kicking, a case of *keren* paying half. Scenario two — a separate situation of ordinary *tzroros* from normal walking, paying half according to the *rabbanan*.

The second possible reading: the *Mishnah* is not presenting two independent scenarios. Rather, it is presenting kicking (direct break) and then *tzroros machmas bi'uta* — pebbles scattered as a result of the kicking. In this reading, both cases involve the act of kicking; the second case is *tzroros* caused by the kicking itself.

Now, why would *tzroros* caused by kicking pay only half? If one reads this as the *rabbanan*, then it must be ordinary *tzroros* of *regel* — independent of the kicking — that pays half, while kicking itself also pays half as *keren*. But if the second clause is *tzroros* arising from the kicking, then paying half would mean that *tzroros* of an act of *keren* is treated as ordinary *tzroros* — no different from *tzroros* of *regel*. That would imply no quarter applies, resolving Rav Ashi's question. Alternatively, reading the second clause as *tzroros de'keren* paying half could imply that normal *tzroros* (of *regel*) in fact pays full — as Sumchus holds — and the *Mishnah* reflects Sumchus's position: only because it is *tzroros* of kicking does it get reduced to half.

### Final Section

The Gemara turns to the *sof* — the concluding passage of the *Mishnah*: *darsa al hakli v'shibarto v'nafal hashever al kli acher v'shibro — al harishon meshalem nezek shalem v'al ha'acharon meshalem chatzi nezek*. The animal trampled directly on a vessel and broke it; the shard then flew and broke a second vessel. For the first it pays full; for the second, half. This is a straightforward application of the *rabbanan*: direct damage is full; indirect (*tzroros*) is half.

The Gemara raises the possibility of reading this passage according to Sumchus instead: *rishon* and *sheni* do not refer to the two vessels, but to the first and second objects hit by the flying shards — the first hit by the shard pays full (*koach*), the second pays half (*koach kocho*). This would make the *Mishnah* Sumchus's position on two degrees of removal. But to read the *Mishnah* this way, one would have to resolve Rav Ashi's unresolved question about Sumchus and *koach kocho* — which the Gemara is committed not to resolve.

### Rav Ashi's Reading

The Gemara therefore concludes: *Rav Ashi k'rabbanan moki lei* — Rav Ashi reads the *Mishnah* according to the *rabbanan*. The first case is *gufo* (the animal's direct body causing damage) and the second is *koach* (the shard). This is the straightforward reading the Gemara had originally assumed: direct trampling pays full; the flying shard pays half.

### Tzroros of Kicking

Returning to the question of how to read the earlier clause of the *Mishnah*, Rav Ashi himself reopens the ambiguity. His question is now explicit: *haysa meva'etes v'hizika biv'yuta, tzroros k'orchaya chatzi nezek* — if the *Mishnah* is read as: it was kicking and directly broke (paying half), and *tzroros* through normal walking pays half, that is one reading. But *ha machmas biyuta revi'a nezek* — what if the *tzroros* arose from the kicking itself? Then it would be *tzroros* compounded with *keren*, and a quarter would seem to apply.

This reading would resolve the question of *shinui* plus *tzroros* by showing that *tzroros* of *keren* does yield a quarter — *yesh shinui l'tzroros*. Alternatively: *o dilma* — perhaps the *Mishnah* is deliberately including *tzroros* of kicking and still says half, thereby teaching that combining *tzroros* with *keren* does not reduce to a quarter — *ein shinui l'tzroros*. The *Mishnah* remains susceptible to both readings, and the conclusion is once again: *teiku*.

Tosafos makes a sharp observation about the layered structure of this entire passage. The original *ibayah* — the question of how to read the *Mishnah* — was raised without spelling out what the practical difference would be. The Gemara then developed two possible sets of implications: (1) does the *Mishnah* reflect Sumchus or the *rabbanan*, and (2) does it teach *yesh shinui l'tzroros* or *ein shinui l'tzroros*? As Tosafos points out: *hashta hosif mida'ato* — the Gemara built the implications onto an initial underdetermined question, with Rav Ashi ultimately explicating what was really at stake. What presents itself as a sequence of discrete disputes is in fact a single question developed through accumulating interpretive layers — a phenomenon the *rishonim* were plainly aware of.

One student raised the question of why the Gemara is willing to entertain the possibility that the *stam mishna* is attributed to Sumchus — a minority view — rather than simply assuming it reflects the majority. The response is that this entire *sugya* is unusually committed to keeping possibilities open rather than closing them, perhaps because the practical *halacha l'ma'aseh* cases of *tzroros* were relatively rare, making the conceptual architecture more compelling than the drive toward resolution. The Gemara consistently finds the questions more interesting than the answers.

Another observation: throughout this analysis, the Gemara is thoroughly category-oriented. It never steps back to ask what the underlying logic of each category demands — why someone whose animal is kicking and scatters pebbles should or should not be held to the same standard as someone whose animal is walking normally. The occasional glimpse of that logic (as with *ha'ada l'tzroros* — where one might argue conceptually that warning should be irrelevant because the reduction in payment reflects indirection, not surprise) is never pursued. The Gemara organizes everything by formal classification, leaving the logic implicit or unexplored.

## Tzroros Contexts

### Inevitable Scatter

Rava bar Rav Chama asked Rabbi Ami — and some attribute the questioner as Rav Chisda, and some as Rabbi Chiya bar Abba. The case: an animal was walking in a place where it was impossible for any animal to traverse without causing pebbles to scatter — *haysa mehelechet bimkom she-ee efshar lah ela im kein menatza* — and then it kicked, causing pebbles to fly and damage. *Ma hu?*

The question is whether this compounding of inevitability and *shinui* changes the analysis. The fact that the pebbles were going to scatter regardless might suggest we treat this as ordinary *tzroros* of *regel*, with no *shinui* — in which case it pays half. On the other hand, in this particular instance the pebble was dislodged specifically through the act of kicking — *mi-koach bi'uta ka-menatza* — and that specific causal mechanism was a *shinui*.

### Incidental Shinui

The question is how to characterize an act when the *shinui* is entirely incidental to the outcome: had the animal walked normally, the pebble would have scattered anyway. But the pebble in fact scattered because it was kicked. One might also consider a variation where the *shinui* is not fully incidental — where, for instance, a normal walk would have projected the pebble ten feet while the kick propelled it twenty, with damage occurring at fifteen feet. In that case, the *shinui* was causally necessary for the damage to occur where it did. The Gemara's case seems to contemplate pure incidentality. *Teiku* — the question of how to classify an incidental *shinui* in *tzroros* remains unresolved.

### Public to Private

Rabbi Yirmiyah asked Rabbi Zeira: *haysa mehelechet be-reshus ha-rabim v'hitiza v'hizika — mahu?* An animal was walking in the public domain, caused pebbles to scatter, and the damage occurred. Is it liable? The question is how to classify *tzroros* for purposes of the *reshus* exemption: do we compare it to *keren* — which is *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim* — because it pays half? Or is it fundamentally a *toldah* of *regel*, and like *regel* is *patur* in *reshus ha-rabim*?

This is, one might note, in some respects a more fundamental question than whether *tzroros* is paid *mi-gufo* or from cash — which occupied much of the prior session. The *mi-gufo* question concerns the manner of payment; this question concerns whether any payment is owed at all.

There is also a version of this question that includes the word *u-ve'atah* — that the animal kicked — transforming the *tzroros* into *tzroros de-keren*, and raising the prior unaddressed question of whether such cases follow the *keren* track (and would be *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim*) or the *regel* track (and would be *patur*).

### Regel Category

Rabbi Zeira answered: *mistavra tolda d'regel hu* — it is logically evident that *tzroros* is a *toldah* of *regel*. The logic is not fully unpacked, but the intuition is clear: the mere fact that *tzroros* pays half does not transform its fundamental character. Pebbles scattered by an animal's feet are an extension of the animal's normal movement — *regel* — and the reduction to half reflects the indirect nature of the damage, not a reclassification as *keren*. *Tzroros* therefore remains *patur* in *reshus ha-rabim*, at least when the pebbles and the damage both occur in the public domain.

An interesting implication follows from this reasoning: perhaps *tzroros* originating in an act of *shinui* or even of *keren* might still be categorized as *regel* by its very nature. The argument would be that *tzroros* — incidental scatter — is inherently a form of *regel* regardless of the act that triggered it. Unless the animal is consciously aiming the pebble to strike a specific target (which would be *kavanato l'hazik*), the incidental dislodging of a stone is always structurally equivalent to the incidental damage of *regel*. This reading would mean that even *tzroros de-keren* is *regel* and would be *patur* in *reshus ha-rabim* — a stronger and more elegant formulation of Rabbi Zeira's conclusion.

Rashi's discussion of the defecation case from the previous session is relevant here: he observes that *tzroros* of *shen* is still classified as *regel*. The act of eating generates the animal's pleasure — that is *shen*; but the incidental trajectory of a piece of fruit dislodged during eating is not where the animal derives pleasure. The category of *tzroros* is defined not by the act that precipitates it but by the character of the harm: incidental, indirect, not the source of the animal's benefit. On this view, all *tzroros*, wherever it originates, is *regel*.

The subsequent question: *hitiza be-reshus ha-rabim v'hizika be-reshus ha-yachid* — what if the pebble was dislodged in the public domain but landed in the *nizak*'s private domain and caused damage there? The initial response of Rabbi Zeira was *akira ein kan, hanacha yesh kan* — if there was no problem in the uprooting, there is no problem in the landing, so one is *patur*. The question exposes the two competing rationales for the *reshus ha-rabim* exemption.

On one view, the exemption derives from the owner's *right* to use *reshus ha-rabim*: since I have a right to be here with my animal, I cannot be held liable for what it does naturally in the space I am permitted to occupy. Under this rationale, it should not matter where the pebble ultimately lands — my right to walk in *reshus ha-rabim* exempts me from the consequences of normal animal behavior whether those consequences materialize in the public or private domain.

On the competing view, the exemption reflects the *damagee's* contributory negligence: one who places objects in *reshus ha-rabim* assumes the risk of damage from animals legitimately present there. Under this rationale, when the pebble lands in *reshus ha-yachid*, the damagee has *not* been negligent — he placed his vessel in his own domain and had every right to expect it to be safe. In that case, the animal owner should be *chayav*.

This is a genuine *nafka mina* between the two rationales, and the Gemara proceeds to bring *Tannaitic* evidence that bears on it.

The Gemara cites a *braita*: an animal walking and causing *tzroros* is *chayav* whether the damage occurs in *reshus ha-yachid* or *reshus ha-rabim*. At face value, this establishes that *tzroros* in *reshus ha-rabim* is *chayav* — which would make it structurally like *keren*, not *regel*. The Gemara deflects: *hitiza be-reshus ha-rabim v'hizika be-reshus ha-yachid* — when the *braita* says *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim*, it means the kicking happened in *reshus ha-rabim* but the damage landed in *reshus ha-yachid*. Under this reading, the *braita* is consistent with *tzroros* being *patur* when both act and damage are in *reshus ha-rabim*.

This same move is required to interpret the *sof ha-Mishnah*: the *Mishnah* says that for the flying shard, one is *patur* on the first (the vessel directly trampled — *regel* in *reshus ha-rabim*) and *chayav* on the second (the vessel struck by the shard). If the pebble reached into *reshus ha-yachid*, that would explain the *chiyuv*. The irony is striking: the animal is *patur* for the vessel it stepped on directly but *chayav* for the vessel struck by the flying shard — because the former is *regel* in *reshus ha-rabim* (exempt) and the latter is *tzroros* reaching *reshus ha-yachid* (liable).

Rabbi Yochanan's statement — *ein chatzi nezek chaluk, lo l'reshus ha-yachid v'lo l'reshus ha-rabim* — half-damages applies equally without distinction as to domain — is likewise deflected: perhaps Rabbi Yochanan was speaking only about *keren*, not *tzroros*, ruling against those who would have said *keren* is *patur* or pays full in one domain or the other.

The Gemara concedes in each instance that while the *peshat* of these sources seems to say *tzroros* is *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim* outright, the deflection to *hitiza be-reshus ha-rabim v'hizika be-reshus ha-yachid* is at least viable. Even that minimalist concession, however, makes a meaningful statement: it establishes that even when the animal's act was in *reshus ha-rabim*, if the pebble lands in the *nizak*'s private domain, the owner is liable. This points toward the contributory-negligence rationale as the operative logic of *reshus ha-rabim*: once the damage occurs in a domain where the *nizak* had no responsibility to guard his property, the *nizak's* negligence drops out and the animal owner's liability is restored.

## Reshus Harabbim

### Tail Wagging

The Gemara shifts to a case presented by Rav Yehuda Nesia and Rabbi Oshaya, sitting together on a porch: *kishkeshah bi-znava mahu* — if an animal was wagging its tail and broke something, is it *chayav*?

### Policy Override

The response: *v'chi yochezena bi-znava v'yeileich?* — are you supposed to hold onto its tail as you walk it? This answer invokes the *freedom-of-movement* rationale for exemption in *reshus ha-rabim*: we cannot hold an owner liable for an animal's natural movements in a space the owner is permitted to use, or the practical ability to bring animals into public spaces would be destroyed.

### Natural Acts

The Gemara pursues the implication: if the tail-wagging is a natural act, why ask the question at all? *Keren lav orchaya — ha orchaya* — keren is not the natural behavior of animals, while this is. This generates the arresting irony at the heart of the *reshus ha-rabim* laws: *keren* — which is normally the *more* severe category, precisely because the animal acted against type — is *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim*, while *shen va-regel* — the *less* severe categories, natural acts — are *patur* there. The very naturalness that usually indicates greater negligence becomes, in *reshus ha-rabim*, the reason for exemption. Because it is natural for an animal to walk and eat and move in a normal way, and because society requires that we allow animals to do these things in public spaces, we cannot impose liability for them there. We can impose liability for *keren*, because holding an owner responsible for his animal's violent eruptions does not meaningfully constrain his freedom to use the public domain — no one anticipates their animal goring, and the prospect of liability for goring will not cause an owner to hesitate before bringing an animal to *reshus ha-rabim*. But liability for walking, eating, and tail-wagging would make the public domain effectively unusable for anyone with livestock.

### Vigorous Wagging

The Gemara refines the question: *kishkeshah yeseirah kamibaiya lei* — what if it was wagging its tail *unusually vigorously*? Now we are in the territory of *meshuneh* — an abnormal act. This raises a pointed question about how *meshuneh* interacts with the *reshus ha-rabim* exemption. *Meshuneh* has been categorized as *keren* — it is precisely because the act is abnormal that it carries a half-payment liability akin to *keren*. If we follow the formal categorization, then a *meshuneh* act in *reshus ha-rabim* should pay, as *keren* pays there.

And the policy logic also supports this: the *reshus ha-rabim* exemption exists because we cannot constrain normal animal behavior. Unusual behavior, by definition, is not what we must accommodate in order to allow ordinary use of the public domain. Liability for *meshuneh* acts in *reshus ha-rabim* should not cause any reasonable owner to hesitate before bringing an animal there, because no reasonable owner expects abnormal behavior. The case for exempting *meshuneh* in *reshus ha-rabim* is therefore substantially weaker than the case for exempting normal *regel*.

### Two Rationales

Yet the two competing rationales for *reshus ha-rabim* liability converge here and diverge in instructive ways. The policy rationale — *freedom of movement* — says we exempt the owner because we must allow him to use the public domain without being crushed by liability for natural animal behavior. Under this rationale, *meshuneh* should be *chayav* because we do not need to protect the owner from liability for unusual behavior in order to preserve his practical freedom. The contributory-negligence rationale — which assigns exemption because the *nizak* took the risk of being in *reshus ha-rabim* — might extend even to unusual acts, since anyone in *reshus ha-rabim* arguably assumes some risk of abnormal animal behavior as well. These two rationales were already in tension in the *tzroros* discussion: the *tzroros* analysis pointed toward contributory negligence as the operative logic, while the tail-wagging discussion points toward policy. The Gemara does not synthesize them; it presents them side by side, and a careful reader finds both at work.

### Zoological Case

Rav Eina poses a final, remarkable question: *kishkeshah bi-amatah mahu* — if an animal was, in a sense, displaying its male member and thereby struck and broke something, what is the *halacha*? The question is whether this should be categorized as *keren* on the grounds that *keren de-it lei yetzra takfa leh* — just as *keren* is driven by the animal's aggressive impulse, so too this behavior might be attributed to something like a *yetzer* that overcame the animal. Under that categorization it would be *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim*. Or perhaps *keren* requires specifically *kavanato l'hazik* — intention to damage — which is absent here. The Gemara concludes: *teiku*. The question of whether an animalistic impulse short of intent to harm suffices to classify behavior as *keren* — and therefore as *chayav* in *reshus ha-rabim* — is left open.

## Bor Liability

### Self-Tangled

The *Mishnah* discusses a case where a bucket becomes tangled in a chicken's leg and the chicken, moving about, causes the bucket to break something. Rav Huna establishes: *lo shanu ela she-nikshar me-elav* — the rule (that the owner pays half-damages for *tzroros*) applies only when the bucket tangled on its own, through the chicken's natural movement.

### Intentional Tying

*Aval kasharo adam chayav* — but if a person deliberately tied the bucket to the chicken's leg, the person who tied it is liable. The mechanism here shifts from *tzroros* to *bor*: the chicken is now carrying what functions as a *bor* — an obstacle hazard — and the person who created that hazard is liable as the maker of a *bor* when someone trips over it or is otherwise damaged by the moving bucket.

### Negligent Placement

This raises the question: if the bucket was ownerless (*hefker*) and it became tangled on its own, who is liable? *I de-atzne'ei* — if the bucket's owner stored it safely, the owner is *anus* (coerced by circumstances beyond control) and *patur*. *Ve-i lo atzne'ei* — if the owner left it where it could foreseeably be disturbed by an animal, the owner is *poshe'a* — negligent — and has created a *bor*. *Ela chayav ba'al ha-tarnagol* — if neither of these fits, the owner of the chicken would be liable. But immediately: *mai shana kulei nezek de-lo* — why would the chicken's owner not pay full damages? Because of the principle *ish bor ve-lo shor bor*: the verse (*Shemos* 21:33) establishes that *bor* liability attaches to a person who digs a pit, not to an animal. The owner of the chicken therefore cannot be held liable as a *bor*-maker even if his chicken transported the hazard.

### Bor Mitgalgel

The Gemara clarifies: Rav Huna's statement about the tied bucket creating *bor* liability is not a comment on the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah*'s case is *addi aduyei* — the chicken threw or launched the bucket through motion, making this a case of *kocho* and *tzroros*, for which the chicken's owner pays half. Rav Huna's teaching is an independent ruling about a *bor mitgalgel* — a *bor* that travels, moved by the feet of people or animals. The governing principle: if one was negligent in the original placement of an object, and it is then moved by an animal to another location where it causes damage, one remains liable as the creator of the traveling *bor*. The animal's intervention does not break the chain of liability to the original negligent party, because *ish bor ve-lo shor bor* means only that the animal owner acquires no *bor* liability — it does not dissolve the original owner's responsibility.

### Bucket Owner Category

Tosafos raises the final and most elegant question of the *sugya*: suppose the bucket's owner was negligent — he left it where he knew an animal could kick it — and the animal did kick it, and the consequent motion caused damage. The owner of the animal pays half for *tzroros*. But what does the negligent bucket-owner pay, and under what category? It is not *shor* — the damage was not done by his animal. It is not *bor* in the classic sense — the vessel was not stationary in a pit. What category applies when a person's negligent placement of an object allows another force (an animal) to mobilize it as an instrument of harm?

Tosafos answers: this would be *esh* — the category of fire liability. Fire's defining feature in *halacha* is that one sets a process in motion that then causes damage through the agency of another force — wind, terrain, or circumstance. When a person negligently places an object where it is foreseeable that an animal will kick it and cause damage, that person has similarly set a damaging process in motion through an intermediate agent. The liability therefore falls under *esh*. The result: the animal's owner pays half for *tzroros*, and the negligent bucket-owner pays the remainder under the rubric of *esh*. This is a question the Gemara itself does not fully develop, but it is precisely the kind of boundary case that the entire *sugya* has been orbiting — where the four canonical categories of *arba'ah avos nezikin* meet, overlap, and require creative application.
